# Bava Metzia 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Donkey Rental Intro

### Previous Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ שֶׁנַּעֲשֵׂית אַנְגַּרְיָא, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ. אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּאַנְגַּרְיָא חוֹזֶרֶת. אֲבָל אַנְגַּרְיָא שֶׁאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת, חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ חֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Or** if the donkey **was seized for public service,** the owner can **say to** the renter: **That which is yours is before you. Rav says: They taught** this *halakha* **only with regard to temporary public service,** i.e., the government confiscates the animal for a certain period of time, after which they return it. **But** in a case of **permanent public service,** where they confiscated the animal permanently, the owner is **obligated to provide** the renter with another **donkey.**

The daf opens on 78b, approximately five lines before the text widens, with a new *mishnah* that shifts the discussion from workers to rentals. The *mishnah* addresses the case of one who rents a donkey, and its opening concern is liability when the renter uses the animal outside the terms of the agreement.

### Liability Framework

Two distinct frameworks govern this liability. The first is the principle of *techlilato bipshiah vesofo beones*: when a person begins with negligence, he bears responsibility for all that follows, even if the ultimate harm was an *ones* — an unforeseeable accident — since there is a presumption that the accident was an outgrowth of the original negligence. That presumption can, however, be rebutted if the harm was clearly unrelated to the initial negligence, in which case the renter would be exempt. The second framework, associated with Rabbi Meir, is *kol hamavir al daato shel baal habayit nikra gazlan* — one who violates the owner's terms is considered a thief. Under this approach, the renter's liability is not grounded in negligence at all but in the fact that by exceeding the terms of the rental he has effectively assumed full possessory responsibility, making him liable for anything that goes wrong regardless of any causal connection.

### Techlilato Bipshiah

The distinction between these two approaches is significant. Under *techlilato bipshiah vesofo beones*, liability is a consequence of the presumed causal chain from the initial negligence, and a sufficiently remote or unrelated harm could break that chain. Under Rabbi Meir's approach, the liability is total and unconditional from the moment the renter deviates from the agreement. These two frameworks set the conceptual backdrop for the cases that follow.

## Minor Defects

### Vehivrika/Avzeket

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר וְהִבְרִיקָה, מַאי ״וְהִבְרִיקָה״? הָכָא תַּרְגִּימוּ ״נְהוֹרִיתָא״. רָבָא אָמַר: אַבְזְקָת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches with regard to **one who rents a donkey and it became ill [*vehivrika*],** that the one who rented it to him bears no responsibility. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the term ***vehivrika*? Here,** in Babylonia, **they interpreted** it as **light in the eye,** a white spot that forms on the pupil of the donkey's eye that impairs its vision. **Rava said** that it is referring to **worm rot [*avzeket*],** a disease of worms in the donkey's foot.

The *mishnah* continues with a new set of cases concerning defects that arise in the course of the rental. *Vehivrika* — translated in Babylonia as *nahurisa*, a term connoting light or brightness — refers, according to Rashi, to a condition affecting the iris of the eye, a kind of vision impairment. The donkey is not fully functional, but it can still carry burdens. It is, in the language of a familiar analogy, a dented car that remains drivable. Rava adds *avzeket*, which Rashi describes as a type of worm that has invaded the animal's foot or hoof. Again, the donkey remains usable, even if impaired.

### Harei Shelcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר וְהִבְרִיקָה, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches with regard to **one who rents a donkey and it became ill, [the owner] can say to [the renter]: That which is yours is before you.**

In both of these cases, the *mishnah* rules that the owner need not replace the animal. He may say *harei shelcha lefanecha* — here it is before you, take it as it is. The donkey is still serviceable, and the owner's contractual obligation does not extend to providing a defect-free animal so long as the animal can still perform its function.

### King's Worm Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: אַבְזְקָת בְּמֵילָתָא דְּמַלְכָּא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: בְּמַאי? בְּטַלֵּי כְסַף, אוֹ בְּטַלֵּי דְהַב? אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּטַלֵּי כְסַף אֲמַר, וְקַטְלוּהּ. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּטַלֵּי דְהַב אֲמַר, וְשַׁבְקוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the term worm rot, the Gemara relates: There was **a certain** individual **who said to** others: I saw **worm rot on the king's clothing. They said to him: On what** did you see it? **On silver,** i.e., linen, **items or on gold,** i.e., woolen, **items? There are** those **who say** that **he said** he had seen it **on silver items, and they killed him.** Since worms are not found on linen garments, they realized that he must have been lying. **There are** those **who say** that **he said** he had seen it **on gold items, and** therefore **they let him be.**

A tangential but memorable anecdote is attached to the mention of *avzeket*. The Gemara relates that a certain man warned the royal court: *avzeket bemilsa demalka* — there is a worm eating away at the king's clothing. When asked which garments — *betali dekesaf*, the silver ones (which Rashi explains refers to linen, whose color resembles silver), or *betali dedehav*, the gold ones (wool, dyed a golden shade) — one tradition holds he said the silver ones and was executed, because the worm in question does not in fact eat linen, exposing his claim as a lie that impugned the king's honor. Another tradition holds he said the gold ones and was left alone, since that at least was plausible.

## Angarya Cases

### Angarya Hozeret

The *mishnah* then addresses a more dramatic disruption: *angarya* — seizure of the animal by agents of the king for royal use. Here too, the *mishnah* rules that the owner may say *harei shelcha lefanecha*, and need not provide a replacement. This seems surprising, since unlike a visual impairment or a worm, the seized animal is entirely unavailable to the renter.

### Rav's Position

Rav resolves this by drawing a distinction. *Lo shanu ella be'angarya hozeret* — the ruling that no replacement is required applies only to *angarya hozeret*, a temporary seizure after which the animal will be returned. If the seizure is *angarya she'eino hozeret*, a permanent taking with no return, then the owner is obligated to provide a replacement, just as he would if the donkey had died or been incapacitated.

### Shmuel Derech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בֵּין אַנְגַּרְיָא חוֹזֶרֶת בֵּין אַנְגַּרְיָא שֶׁאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת, אִם בְּדֶרֶךְ הֲלִיכָה נִיטְּלָה – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ. וְאִם לָאו בְּדֶרֶךְ הֲלִיכָתָהּ נִיטְּלָה – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ חֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: Whether** the donkey was seized to perform **a permanent public service or a temporary public service, if it was seized** and then taken in the direction in **which it** was **walking,** i.e., the government officials led the animal in the direction it was already going, the owner can **say to** the renter: **That which is yours is before you.** The common practice was that when an animal was confiscated by government officials, the one who had been leading it would follow them until the officials found another animal. At this point, the officials would take the animal they just found and release the one they had previously seized. Therefore, the owner can insist that the renter follow the officials, who in any event are going in the direction he wishes to go, in order to retrieve the animal he had rented. **But if it was seized** and then taken **not** in the direction **in which it** was **walking,** but in a different direction, the owner is **obligated to provide** the renter with another **donkey.**

Shmuel takes a different approach entirely. According to Shmuel, the distinction is not between temporary and permanent seizure, but between the circumstances under which the seizure occurred. *Im be'derech halicha nitla* — if the donkey was seized as it was traveling along the route — the owner may say *harei shelcha lefanecha*, even if the animal will never be returned. *Ve'im lav be'derech halichata nitla* — if it was not seized along its route of travel — the owner must replace it, even if the seizure is only temporary.

Rashi describes the scenario underlying this distinction as one in which royal troops would press a donkey into service for a leg of their journey, release it when they encountered the next available animal, and so move from victim to victim. On this reading, the question of *derech halichata* resolves into whether the seizure occurred in the direction the renter was already heading, since the donkey would eventually be returned once the troops found their next draft animal.

### Tosafot Circumstances

Tosafot offers what appears to be a more intuitive reading. Rather than focusing on the physical direction of travel, the distinction turns on the circumstances under which the seizure occurred. If the royal agents simply came upon the donkey as it was traveling on the road, the renter happened to be in the wrong place at the wrong time — something akin to his own ill fortune or *mazal* — and therefore the loss is, in some sense, attributable to him. The owner can say: you happened to be on that road at that time; that is your bad luck, not my responsibility. But if the agents went house to house searching specifically for a suitable animal, the seizure has nothing to do with any choice or circumstance attributable to the renter, and it is a pure *ones*. In that case, even if the animal will never return, the owner must replace it.

This reading produces a striking result in Shmuel's position: if the seizure is entirely the renter's "fault" in this attenuated sense of bad fortune, the owner need not replace the animal even if it is gone permanently. Conversely, if the seizure is a pure *ones* with no connection to any act or circumstance of the renter, the owner must replace it even if the animal will be back in two days. The criterion is not duration but attribution.

## Baraita Reconciliation

### Meitivei Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר וְהִבְרִיקָה אוֹ שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּטְּתָה, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ. מֵתָה אוֹ שֶׁנַּעֲשֵׂית אַנְגַּרְיָא – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ חֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **one who rents a donkey and it became ill or went mad,** the owner can **say to** the renter: **That which is yours is before you. If it died or was seized for public service,** the owner is **obligated to provide** the renter with another **donkey.**

The Gemara now raises a challenge from a *baraita*. The *baraita* states that if the rented donkey died or was seized by the king — *meisa o shenastasa angarya* — the owner is obligated to replace it. This *baraita* flatly contradicts the *mishnah*, which permits the owner to say *harei shelcha lefanecha* in the case of *angarya*.

### Rav Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּאַנְגַּרְיָא חוֹזֶרֶת, כָּאן בְּאַנְגַּרְיָא שֶׁאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rav** this is **not difficult,** as he can explain that **here,** in the mishna, it is referring **to permanent public service,** and therefore the owner does not have to provide the renter with another donkey, whereas **there,** in the *baraita*, it is referring **to temporary public service.** Consequently, the owner must give the renter another donkey.

For Rav, the resolution is straightforward: the *mishnah* speaks of *angarya hozeret* and the *baraita* speaks of *angarya she'eino hozeret*. Each source addresses a different category, and there is no genuine contradiction.

### Shmuel Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל קַשְׁיָא! וְכִי תֵּימָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן שֶׁבְּדֶרֶךְ הֲלִיכָתָהּ נִיטְּלָה, כָּאן שֶׁלֹּא בְּדֶרֶךְ הֲלִיכָתָהּ נִיטְּלָה. הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אִם בְּדֶרֶךְ הֲלִיכָתָהּ נִיטְּלָה, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ, וְאִם לָאו – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ חֲמוֹר. מִכְּלָל דִּלְתַנָּא קַמָּא לָא שָׁאנֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to** the opinion of **Shmuel** it is **difficult. And if you would say** that **according to** the opinion of **Shmuel too,** it is **not difficult,** as he could resolve the apparent contradiction in a similar manner by explaining that **here,** in the mishna, it is referring to a case **where it was seized** and then taken in the direction in **which it** was **walking, and there,** in the *baraita*, it is referring to a case **where it was seized** and then taken **not** in the direction in **which it** was **walking** but in a different direction, this resolution is not possible. This resolution cannot be accepted, because **from** the fact **that** the *baraita* **teaches in the latter clause** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: If it was seized** and then taken in the direction in **which it** was **walking** the owner can **say to** the renter: **That which is yours is before you, but if not** the owner is **obligated to provide** the renter with another **donkey, by inference** one can learn that **according to** the opinion of **the first *tanna*** there **is no difference** whether or not the animal was seized and then taken in the direction in which it was walking. If so, the *baraita* is difficult to reconcile with Shmuel's opinion.

For Shmuel, however, the resolution is more complex. Shmuel can propose that the *mishnah* addresses a case where the donkey was seized *be'derech hilucha* — in the course of the renter's own travel, attributable to his circumstances — while the *baraita* addresses a case where it was seized *shelo be'derech hilucha*, a pure *ones* for which the owner bears responsibility. The Gemara objects, however, that the closing clause of the *baraita* itself invokes precisely this distinction, and attributes it specifically to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. If the *baraita*'s conclusion already belongs to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, then the opening clause of the *baraita* — which mandates replacement — must reflect the position of the *Tanna Kamma*, who apparently requires replacement in all cases regardless of circumstances, posing a difficulty for Shmuel.

### Shimon ben Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ שְׁמוּאֵל: לָאו מִי אִיכָּא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר דְּקָאֵי כְּווֹתִי? אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, וְחַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר וְהִבְרִיקָה אוֹ נִשְׁתַּטֵּית, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ. מֵתָה אוֹ שֶׁנַּעֲשֵׂית אַנְגַּרְיָא – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ חֲמוֹר. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁלֹּא בְּדֶרֶךְ הֲלִיכָתָהּ נִיטְּלָה, אֲבָל נִיטְּלָה בְּדֶרֶךְ הֲלִיכָתָהּ – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Shmuel** could **say to you: Isn't there** the ruling of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who holds in accordance with my** opinion? **I spoke in accordance with** the ruling of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. If you wish, say** instead that the **entire** *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, and** it **is incomplete, and this is what it is teaching:** With regard to **one who rents a donkey and it became ill or went mad,** the owner can **say to** the renter: **That which is yours is before you.** If **it died or was seized for public service,** the owner is **obligated to provide** the renter with another **donkey. In what** case **is this statement said? When it is seized** and then taken **not** in the direction in **which it** was **walking** but in a different direction, **but** if **it was seized** and then taken in the direction in **which it** was **walking,** the owner can **say to** the renter: **That which is yours is before you.**

The Gemara offers Shmuel a response: *lav miklal d'Rabi Shimon ben Elazar d'ka'ei k'vasi* — is Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's opinion not worth having as a source? Shmuel aligns himself with Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who he regards as correct. The Gemara then offers a further resolution: *kulah Rabi Shimon ben Elazar hi* — the entire *baraita* is the position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, and the final clause is not a dispute with the earlier ruling but a clarification of it. The *baraita*, properly reconstructed, reads as follows: if the donkey developed an eye condition or went somewhat mad (*nishtata*), the owner says *harei shelcha lefanecha*; if it died or was seized, he must replace it — but this applies only when the seizure occurred *shelo be'derech hilucha*, not through any circumstance of the renter. If it was seized *be'derech hilucha*, the owner may again say *harei shelcha lefanecha*. This is the consistent teaching of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar throughout.

## Riding Purpose

### Riding Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דִּשְׁכִיחַ לְאֹגוֹרֵי – תַּרְעוֹמֶת מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ? אִי דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ לְאֹגוֹרֵי – אַגְרָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתַּב לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** this is referring to a place **where** donkeys **are available for rent, what** is the **purpose of** this **grievance?** He does not have any cause for grievance in this case, as he can simply rent another donkey and has not lost anything. **If** it is a place **where** donkeys **are not available for rent,** should he be **required to give** the owner **his fee?** After all, the donkey failed to perform the task for which it was rented.

The Gemara now raises a further difficulty. Elsewhere, a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar states: *hasocher et hachamor lirkov aleha* — if one rents a donkey specifically for riding — *vehivrika o shenishtata*, and it develops the same eye condition or goes somewhat mad, *chayav le'ha'amid lo chamor*, the owner must replace it. But this contradicts the *mishnah*'s ruling that *vehivrika* does not require replacement. How can the whole earlier *baraita* be attributed to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar if his own statement here requires replacement for *vehivrika*?

### Safety Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: לִרְכּוֹב עָלֶיהָ שָׁאנֵי. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וּכְלֵי זְכוּכִית – כְּלִרְכּוֹב עָלֶיהָ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said** that this is not a contradiction. The case of one who takes a donkey **to ride on is different,** as he requires a donkey that walks steadily. A sick donkey is no better than a dead one for this purpose. **Rav Pappa said: And** acquiring a donkey so that it should carry **glass vessels is considered** to be **like riding on it,** as glass vessels also require steady walking to prevent the load from falling.

Raba bar Rav Huna resolves the apparent contradiction: *lirkov aleha shani* — riding is different. Rashi explains that when a person intends to ride the donkey, even a mild impairment becomes unacceptable. A donkey with compromised vision or erratic behavior poses a genuine danger to the rider — it might stumble on a bridge or throw the rider into a pit. When the donkey is used only to carry cargo, the worst outcome of a stumble is that the cargo falls and needs to be retrieved. But a rider cannot accept that risk. Therefore, when the rental is specifically for riding, even a defect that would otherwise be tolerated renders the animal unusable, and the owner must replace it.

### Glassware

Rav Pappa extends the logic of this ruling: *uchelei zechochis ke'irchavo alav dami* — transporting glassware is equivalent to riding, in the sense that an impaired or erratic animal creates an unacceptable risk of total loss. Just as a rider cannot tolerate the danger posed by a compromised animal, a shipper of fragile goods cannot accept the risk that a stumble will shatter everything. In both cases, the standard for usability is stricter than for ordinary cargo.

## Death Mid-Journey

### Half Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר לִרְכּוֹב עָלֶיהָ וּמֵתָה לוֹ בַּחֲצִי הַדֶּרֶךְ, נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ שֶׁל חֲצִי הַדֶּרֶךְ, וְאֵין לוֹ עָלָיו אֶלָּא תַּרְעוֹמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** In the case of **one who rents a donkey to ride on it and it died halfway through the journey,** the renter **gives** the owner **his fee for half of the journey, but** the renter **has nothing but a grievance against** the owner. He has no legal claim against the owner over the fact that he now has to go to great trouble to find another donkey.

The Gemara now introduces a new and consequential category of cases. Raba bar Huna citing Rav states: *hasocher et hachamor lirkov aleha umeisa lo bachatzi haderech* — if one rents a donkey for riding and it dies in the middle of the journey — *noten lo secharo shel chatzi haderech*, the renter pays the owner for half the journey. And *ein lo alav ela teroumes* — the renter's only recourse is a complaint; he cannot demand a replacement.

This ruling is striking. The *mishnah* had established that when a donkey dies, the owner must replace it. Here, the renter pays half and receives only the right to complain — no replacement is forthcoming. The Gemara asks: *heichi dami* — what are the circumstances that make this ruling coherent?

If replacement animals are readily available at the midpoint, the renter has no real complaint: take the remaining payment and rent another animal. If no replacement is available, how can the owner simply collect half the fee and walk away from his obligation to provide a donkey for the full journey?

### Tosafot Reinterpret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִם דִּשְׁכִיחַ לְאֹגוֹרֵי – תַּרְעוֹמֶת מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ? אִם דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ לְאֹגוֹרֵי – אַגְרָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתַּב לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** this is referring to a place **where** donkeys **are available for rent, what** is the **purpose of** this **grievance?** He does not have any cause for grievance in this case, as he can simply rent another donkey and has not lost anything. **If** it is a place **where** donkeys **are not available for rent,** should he be **required to give** the owner **his fee?** After all, the donkey failed to perform the task for which it was rented.

Tosafot grapples with this difficulty honestly. The simple reading of the Gemara — that the owner collects half and owes nothing more — is hard to justify when the renter never wanted to stop in the middle. Tosafot therefore reinterprets the case. When the Gemara says that replacement animals are not available *at the same price*, it means they are not available at the original rental rate. But at a slightly higher rate, the renter can find another animal. Since the owner brought him halfway, and the additional cost for the second half of the journey is only marginally higher, the owner is not required to absorb that difference. The renter was brought halfway, he can complete the journey, and the modest extra expense is absorbed as part of the *ones* that neither party caused. Tosafot acknowledges that this interpretation is an imposition on the plain text, but notes that the alternative — demanding nothing from the renter and giving the owner nothing — seems equally untenable when nobody is at fault. The question of who bears the burden of unforeseeable misfortune is at the heart of this case.

### Mid-Route Sales

Tosafot offers an alternative reading as well: the case may involve a renter who is a merchant transporting wares for sale, and the midpoint market — South Carolina, as it were — turns out to be a perfectly viable market for selling those wares. Since the renter can sell his goods where he is and has not in fact suffered a commercially meaningful loss, the owner can say: I brought you to a place where you can do your business, and the claim that you required a specific destination is overstated. On this reading, the *teroumes* — the complaint — is real, but the harm is genuinely limited.

## Specified Donkey

### Stam vs. Zeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא: בְּשֶׁאֵין בְּדָמֶיהָ לִיקַּח. אִם יֵשׁ בְּדָמֶיהָ לִשְׂכּוֹר – יִשְׂכּוֹר, רַב לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב: לָא מְכַלֵּינַן קַרְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* only in a case **where there is not** enough **money** to be gained **from** the sale of its carcass **to purchase** another donkey. The Gemara asks: Even so, **if there is** enough **money** to be gained **from** the sale of its carcass **to hire** another donkey, **let him rent** another donkey and continue along his way. What grounds are there for grievance? The Gemara explains that **Rav** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rav said: One may not fully deplete the principal** in order to pay rental fees. Consequently, the renter has no right to sell the carcass, so as not to deplete the entire monetary value of the donkey.

The Gemara now introduces a critical distinction that is not explicit in the *mishnah* but proves essential to understanding it: the difference between *chamor stam* — renting an unspecified donkey — and *chamor zeh* — renting this particular donkey. If the owner rented Michael a donkey without specification, and the animal dies mid-journey, the owner owes Michael a replacement, period. He contracted to provide a donkey for the week and must honor that commitment. If, however, he specified *chamor zeh*, the analysis becomes more nuanced.

Even in the case of *chamor zeh*, the renter retains some rights. Rashi notes that the owner may still have an obligation that flows from this donkey, in the sense that if the carcass can be sold and the proceeds used to purchase a replacement animal, that purchase represents a continuation of the original rental relationship — the new donkey stands in for the old one, and the renter is entitled to use it for the remainder of the week. The question then becomes: what if the proceeds are insufficient to buy a replacement outright? Could they at least be used to rent a donkey for the remaining time?

### Rav Principal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִתְּמַר: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר וּמֵתָה לוֹ בַּחֲצִי הַדֶּרֶךְ. אָמַר רַב: אִם יֵשׁ בְּדָמֶיהָ לִיקַּח – יִקַּח, לִשְׂכּוֹר – אַל יִשְׂכּוֹר. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אַף לִשְׂכּוֹר – יִשְׂכּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it was stated** in a dispute between *amora'im* on this issue: With regard to **one who rents a donkey and it died halfway through the journey, Rav says: If there is** enough **money** to be gained **from** the sale of its carcass **to purchase** another donkey, the renter **may purchase** one, but if there is enough money only **to rent** another donkey, **he may not rent** one. **Shmuel says: Even** if **there is** enough money to be gained from the sale of its carcass only **to rent** another donkey, **he may rent** one.

The Gemara introduces the debate between Rav and Shmuel in this context. Rav holds *lo mechalinan karna* — we do not consume the principal. Rav's position is that the owner's obligation, when he contracted for *chamor zeh*, is to allow the renter to use his assets, not to actually dissipate those assets. If the donkey dies and its carcass is worth enough to buy a replacement, Rav requires the owner to buy a replacement: the owner reinvests the asset, Michael uses it for the rest of the week, and at the end of the week the owner still has his donkey. The principal is preserved even while its use is extended. But if the money from the carcass is not sufficient to purchase another donkey outright, Rav says it cannot be used to rent one. Renting would mean taking the hundred-dollar carcass value, paying it out in rental fees, and having nothing left — that is consuming the principal. The owner's responsibility to the renter does not extend to an actual diminution of his assets.

Rashi makes this explicit: when the *mishnah* says the owner must replace the animal, this refers to a case where the donkey dies while still at the owner's premises, and the owner digs into his own pocket, adds his own money to the carcass proceeds, and buys a new donkey. This is not cutting into the principal because at the end of the week the owner has a donkey worth its full value. He has lost nothing of his capital base. What he cannot be required to do is take the carcass proceeds and have them evaporate in rental fees, leaving him with nothing.

### Shmuel Rental

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַב סָבַר: לָא מְכַלֵּינַן קַרְנָא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל סָבַר: מְכַלֵּינַן קַרְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: **Rav holds** that **one may not fully deplete the principal,** and as the carcass now constitutes the principal value of the donkey, one may not use the proceeds of its sale to pay rental fees, as nothing will be left to return to the owner. **And Shmuel holds** that **one may fully deplete the principal.**

Shmuel disagrees: *mechalinan karna*. According to Shmuel, when a renter has a contractual right to the use of an animal for the week, that right is enforceable even at some cost to the owner's principal. If the proceeds of the dead donkey are sufficient to rent a replacement for the remaining days of the rental, that rental is required — even though the money is "consumed" rather than reinvested in a lasting asset. The renter's right to usage for the contracted period is robust enough to justify this. The debate between Rav and Shmuel is therefore not merely about this one case, but about a fundamental question: to what extent does a bailor-type relationship obligate the owner to actively protect the beneficiary's usage rights even at the expense of his own capital?

## Tree Seizure

### Yavesh Ilan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, יָבֵשׁ הָאִילָן אוֹ נִקְצַץ – שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִין בּוֹ. כֵּיצַד יַעֲשֶׂה? יִלָּקַח בּוֹ קַרְקַע, וְהוּא אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת. וְהָא הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּכִי מָטֵי יוֹבֵל קָא הָדְרָא אַרְעָא לְמָרַהּ, וְקָא כָלְיָא קַרְנָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav's opinion from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Arakhin* 5:1), which discusses the case of a borrower who gives his lender a tree as a security for a loan so that the lender may eat an amount of its fruit up to the value of the loan. If **the tree dried up or was cut down, it is prohibited for both** the borrower and lender to use the body of the tree. **What should be done? Land should be purchased with** the proceeds from the sale of what is left of the tree, **and** the lender **enjoys the profits** of that land. The Gemara explains the objection: **And** yet **here, once** the **Jubilee** Year **arrives,** that purchased **land will return to its** original **owner.** It will not remain with the borrower after the loan has been repaid. **And** it will turn out that **the principal is fully depleted,** as the borrower will be left with nothing.

The Gemara now draws an analogy to a case involving a *mashkona* — a form of loan collateral — to test the Rav-Shmuel debate in a parallel context. The structure of the *mashkona* arrangement is that Gloria, the creditor, takes possession of a tree on the debtor's property and receives all the fruit — all the profits — from that tree for the duration of the loan, as a mechanism that addresses (in whatever way was addressed in the earlier *perek*) the *ribbis* concern. She owns the tree's yield; he still owns the tree itself. This is structurally identical to the donkey rental: he owns the donkey, Michael has the right to use it for the week. Now, during the loan period, *yavesh ilan v'niktzatz* — the tree dries up and is cut down. Gloria has lost her source of income for the year. What happens?

### Land Purchase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּזַבֵּין לֵיהּ לְשִׁתִּין שְׁנִין. דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: מִנַּיִן לַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֵהוּ לְשִׁשִּׁים שָׁנָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת בַּיּוֹבֵל – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָאָרֶץ לֹא תִמָּכֵר לִצְמִיתוּת״. מִי שֶׁאֵין שָׁם יוֹבֵל – נִצְמֶתֶת, יֵשׁ שָׁם יוֹבֵל – אֵינָהּ נִצְמֶתֶת. יָצְתָה זוֹ, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין שָׁם יוֹבֵל – אֵינָהּ נִצְמֶתֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where he purchased** the field **for sixty years. As Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav Ketina says: From where** is it derived with regard **to one who sells his field for sixty years** or any fixed length of time, **that it does not return** to its original owner **in** the **Jubilee** Year? **As it is stated: "And the land shall not be sold in perpetuity"** (Leviticus 25:23). This prohibition applies to land **which,** if **there** were **no Jubilee** Year, **would be** sold **in perpetuity,** but as **there is a Jubilee** Year it **is not** sold **in perpetuity.** That **excludes this** case, where the field is sold for a fixed length of time, **as even though there is no Jubilee** Year **it is not** sold **in perpetuity.**

The most obvious resolution would be to sell the wood and give Gloria the proceeds. But *shneichem assurim bo* — neither party can unilaterally do this. The owner cannot sell the wood and hand over the proceeds because Gloria might argue that continued fruit production would have yielded more. Gloria cannot sell the wood against the owner's will because that would terminate his asset. Instead, the Gemara instructs: *yilkach bo karka* — sell the wood and use the proceeds to purchase a plot of land, from which Gloria then eats the fruit, *v'hu ochel peiros*. This preserves the structure of the arrangement: the owner's principal takes a new form (land instead of tree), and Gloria continues to benefit from it. This appears to support Rav's position that we do not consume the principal — we find a way to reinvest it.

### Yovel Impact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, לְכִי מָטוּ שִׁיתִּין שְׁנִין – קָא הָדְרָא אַרְעָא לְמָרַהּ, וְקָא כָלְיָא קַרְנָא! אֶלָּא, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין הַיּוֹבֵל נוֹהֵג. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַיּוֹבֵל נוֹהֵג וּמְכַלֵּינַן קַרְנָא, נִצְלְחֵיהּ לְצִיבֵי וְנִשְׁקְלֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Ultimately, when** the end of those **sixty years arrives, the land will return to its owner, and** at that point **the principal will be fully depleted.** The Gemara answers: **Rather, with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with** a period **when** the *halakhot* of **the Jubilee** Year **are not in effect,** as one can purchase land in perpetuity when the Jubilee Year is not observed. The Gemara adds support for this explanation: **This too stands to reason, as, if it enters your mind** that this *halakha* is referring to a period **when** the *halakhot* of **the Jubilee** Year **are in effect and** the ruling is that **the principal may be fully depleted,** why undertake a complicated transaction? **Let** the lender simply **cut** the tree **into branches** for kindling, **and take it** and sell it right away.

The Gemara complicates this picture. If we assume *yovel* — the Jubilee — is in effect, then when the purchased land reaches its *yovel*, it reverts to its original owner, Jenna. The money paid for it is gone, and the owner has lost his investment. So buying land and having it revert at *yovel* does cut into the principal after all. If the *baraita* is nonetheless prepared to mandate this arrangement, it appears that we *are* prepared to consume the principal in service of Gloria's rights — which would support Shmuel.

### Sixty-Year Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא – לָא קַשְׁיָא: זִמְנִין דְּשָׁלְמוּ שְׁנֵי מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא מִקַּמֵּי יוֹבֵל. אִי נָמֵי דְּמָטוּ לֵיהּ זוּזֵי וּפָרֵיק לַהּ אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ שְׁנִין מִקַּמֵּי יוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **If** the challenge to Rav's opinion is **due to that** *baraita*, it is **not difficult.** There may be **times that the years** during which the lender is entitled **to the collateral are completed before** the arrival of the **Jubilee** Year, in which case the land would revert to the borrower for a certain amount of time, and therefore the principal is not necessarily fully depleted. **Alternatively,** it is possible **that money comes into** the borrower's possession **and he redeems** the field **four** or **five years before** the arrival of the **Jubilee** Year. In this case the principal will not be fully depleted, as he repays the loan before the land is returned.

But the Gemara resolves this: *hacha b'mai askinan d'zavneh l'shishim shani* — the case involves a stipulated sixty-year purchase, which avoids the *yovel* reversion. The basis for this is the ruling of Rav Chisda citing Rav Katina: *minayin l'mocher sadeh l'shishim shana she'eina chozeres b'yovel* — how do we know that land sold for sixty years does not revert at *yovel*? From the verse *ve'ha'aretz lo timacher l'tzmitut* — the land shall not be sold into perpetuity. The *yovel* corrects sales into perpetuity, but a sixty-year sale was never a sale in perpetuity, so the *yovel* mechanism does not apply. The sixty-year purchase therefore preserves the principal — the owner retains a durable asset — and the *baraita* is consistent with Rav's principle of *lo mechalinan karna*.

The Gemara raises the objection that even after sixty years the land reverts to Jenna, so eventually the investment is lost. The Gemara's final resolution is *b'zman she'ein yovel noheig* — a time when *yovel* does not apply, which was in fact the situation during the Second *Beit Hamikdash* period and certainly thereafter. The question of *yovel* turns out to have been somewhat academic. What the *baraita* ultimately demonstrates is that even in this case, the structure chosen — reinvesting proceeds in a new productive asset — reflects the principle that we protect the principal and do not simply let it dissolve into payments. The *baraita* thus aligns with Rav's position.

The Gemara also addresses why the parties cannot simply sell the wood and hand the cash to Gloria rather than going through the inconvenience of buying land. Even in a world where some degree of principal consumption might be tolerated, the arrangement that best preserves the owner's interests is preferred. Buying land, even land that will eventually revert, preserves the possibility that the owner will pay off the loan and recover the land before *yovel*, or that he will derive the land's value himself for the remaining years. Selling the wood outright and handing the cash to Gloria leaves the owner with nothing whatsoever. So even within a framework that is prepared to countenance some erosion of principal, we choose the option that minimizes it.

## Boat Sinking

### Boat Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַסְּפִינָה וְטָבְעָה לָהּ בַּחֲצִי הַדֶּרֶךְ, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אִם נָתַן – לֹא יִטּוֹל, וְאִם לֹא נָתַן – לֹא יִתֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who rents a boat, and it sunk halfway through the journey, Rabbi Natan says: If** the renter already **gave** money for the boat, **he does not receive** a refund, **but if he has not** yet **given** money **he does not give** it after the boat has sunk.

The Gemara now transitions from donkeys to boats, presenting a structurally parallel set of cases. The *mishnah*-type scenario is: *hasocher et hasefina vetava lah bachatzi haderech* — one rents a boat and it sinks halfway to the destination. The renter's cargo is lost. The owner's boat is gone. And now the question is: what does the renter owe the owner for the portion of the journey that was completed?

This case differs from the donkey rental in one notable respect. Renting a donkey for the week is a time-based rental; renting a boat for a journey is a task-based engagement — the payment is for completing a delivery, not for a period of usage. This makes the question of partial payment more acute.

### Rav Nosson

Rav Nosson states a principle: *natan lo yitol, ve'im lo natan lo yiten* — if the renter already paid, he cannot recover the payment; if he has not yet paid, he need not pay. This appears to be an application of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraaya*: whoever holds the money has the presumptive claim, and neither party can compel the other to move.

### Specificity Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בִּסְפִינָה זוֹ וְיַיִן סְתָם, אִם נָתַן אַמַּאי לֹא יִטּוֹל? נֵימָא לֵיהּ: הַב לִי סְפִינְתָּא, דַּאֲנָא מַיְיתֵינָא חַמְרָא. אֶלָּא בִּסְפִינָה סְתָם וְיַיִן זֶה, אִם לֹא נָתַן אַמַּאי לֹא יִתֵּן? נֵימָא לֵיהּ: הַב לִי הָהוּא חַמְרָא וַאֲנָא מַיְיתֵינָא סְפִינְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this contract? **If we say** that the owner stated he was renting him **this** particular **boat and** the renter said that he was transporting **unspecified wine,** then even **if he gave** money, **why should he not receive** a refund? **Let him say to** the owner: **Give me the boat** so **that I can transport wine,** and if you cannot do so, refund the rental fee so that I can rent another boat. **Rather,** one could say that this is referring to a situation where the owner said he would rent him **an unspecified boat, and** the renter said that he would transport **this** particular **wine.** But in that case, even **if he did not give** money, **why should he not give** the owner the rental fee? **Let** the owner **say to him: Give me that wine and I will bring a boat.** Since that particular wine is gone, the renter cannot comply with his request, and therefore he should have to pay the rental fee.

The Gemara immediately asks: *heichi dami* — under what circumstances does this ruling apply? If the boat owner said *sefinah zo* — this boat — and the cargo owner said *yayin stam* — wine generally — then the boat owner is the one who cannot fulfill his end of the agreement, since the specific boat is gone. The cargo owner could in theory provide more wine; he is not bound to *this* wine. The cargo owner could therefore demand: give me another boat and I will supply the wine, and I refuse to pay you since you cannot live up to your terms. Similarly, if it is reversed — the cargo owner specified *yayin zeh*, this wine, while the boat owner said *sefinah stam* — then the cargo owner is the party who cannot fulfill his obligations. The boat owner is in principle able to find another boat. The cargo owner, having specified *this* wine, cannot produce it from the ocean floor. In that case, the cargo owner owes the boat owner, since it is the cargo owner's inability to perform that excuses the remainder of the journey.

### Both Specified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ אֶלָּא בִּסְפִינָה זוֹ וְיַיִן זֶה. אֲבָל בִּסְפִינָה סְתָם וְיַיִן סְתָם – חוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: You find** the correct application of Rabbi Natan's ruling **only in** a case where the two parties stipulated **this** specific **boat and that** specific **wine.** Since neither party can fulfill his part of the agreement, the money remains where it is. **But** if they stipulated **an unspecified boat and unspecified wine,** as they can both complete the agreement, **they divide** the rental fee, i.e., the renter pays half.

Rav Pappa resolves the most interesting scenarios. If both parties specified — *sefinah zo veyayin zeh* — then each can point to the other's impossibility of performance. Neither can compel the other, and neither can recover from the other. This is a clean application of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraaya*: the money stays where it is, whether paid or unpaid. Each party can deflect the other's claim by pointing to the other's inability to perform.

The case of both *stam* — neither party specified — is equally interesting. In that case, both parties are theoretically capable of continuing: the boat owner can find another boat, the cargo owner can supply more wine. But in practice, neither is likely to want to. Since in principle the agreement could be honored by both sides, the situation resolves to a split: the renter pays for the portion of the journey completed. If the boat owner demands full payment, the cargo owner can say: fine, produce your boat and I will produce my wine. If the cargo owner wants to avoid paying anything, the boat owner can make the same rejoinder. Since neither genuinely wants to reconstitute the arrangement, they split — the cargo owner pays for what was delivered, no more and no less.

## Voluntary Abandon

### Mid-Point Unload

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַסְּפִינָה וּפְרָקָהּ לָהּ בַּחֲצִי הַדֶּרֶךְ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ שֶׁל חֲצִי הַדֶּרֶךְ, וְאֵין לוֹ עָלָיו אֶלָּא תַּרְעוֹמֶת. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּקָא מַשְׁכַּח לְאֹגוֹרַהּ – אַמַּאי אִית לֵיהּ תַּרְעוֹמֶת? וְאִי דְּלָא קָא מַשְׁכַּח לְאֹגוֹרַהּ – כּוּלֵּיהּ אַגְרַהּ בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who rents a boat and unloads it [*uferakah*] halfway through the journey,** the renter **gives** the owner **his** rental **fee for half the journey, and** the owner of the boat **has nothing but a grievance against him.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If we say that** this is referring to a situation where the owner **can find** someone else **to whom he can rent out** the boat, **why does he have** grounds for **a grievance? And if it** is referring to a case **where he cannot find** another **to whom he can rent it out,** the renter **should be required to pay the full** rental **fee,** as he reneged on his commitment to rent the boat for the entire trip.

A related case: *hasocher et hasefinah uparkah bachatzi haderech* — the renter unloads his cargo midway through the journey voluntarily, without any compelling reason. Again, *noten lo secharo shel chatzi haderech, ve'ein lo alav ela teroumes* — the renter pays for half, and the boat owner's grievance is limited to a complaint.

### Other Customers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּקָא מַשְׁכַּח לְאֹגוֹרַהּ, אֶלָּא אַמַּאי אִית לֵיהּ תַּרְעוֹמֶת – מִשּׁוּם רַפְסְתָא דִסְפִינְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** this is referring to a case **where** the owner **can find** someone else **to whom he can rent out** the boat. **But why does he have** cause for a **grievance? Because of the wear and tear on the boat** due to the additional loading and unloading of cargo, which was not taken into account in their agreement.

Once again the Gemara asks: *heichi dami*? If the boat owner can easily find other customers in the intermediate port, why does he even have a complaint? He simply loads new cargo and continues to Florida. And if he cannot find other customers, should the renter not owe him the full fee for depriving him of the entire journey's earnings?

The Gemara resolves this: the ruling applies where the boat owner can find other customers — and that is precisely why the renter pays only half. The complaint is not about lost income from the remaining journey.

### Shinui Da'at

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, טַעַנְתָּא מְעַלַּיְיתָא הִוא וּמָמוֹנָא אִית לֵיהּ גַּבֵּיהּ. אֶלָּא מַאי פְּרָקָהּ – דְּפַרְקֵהּ לְטוּעְנֵיהּ בְּגַוַּיְהּ. אֶלָּא מַאי תַּרְעוֹמֶת – מִשּׁוּם שִׁינּוּי דַּעְתָּא. אִי נָמֵי לְאַשְׁלָא יַתִּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, that is a proper** legal **claim, and** the owner of the boat **has** not merely a grievance against the renter, but cause for claiming **monetary restitution from him. Rather, what is** the meaning of the term ***perakah*?** This means **that** the renter **unloaded** more of **his own cargo into** the boat halfway through the journey. Accordingly, the *baraita* is ruling that the renter must pay a fee for the additional cargo only for the second half of the journey. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **what is** the **grievance?** Why should the owner object to this arrangement at all? The Gemara explains that the grievance is **due to the change from** the renter's prior **intention,** as they had not agreed upon the addition of this additional cargo when they performed the transaction. **Alternatively,** the grievance is because **of the extra rope** that was necessary to secure the additional cargo.

So what is the complaint? The Gemara offers two answers. First, *mishum rifsata d'sefinata* — wear and tear on the boat. Unloading and reloading causes physical depreciation; switching customers mid-route imposes costs that a continuous journey would not. The Gemara then circles back: if that is so, shouldn't the renter owe actual compensation for the depreciation, not merely suffer a *teroumes*? The Gemara therefore refines the scenario: *parka l'te'unei d'gavei* — the cargo was unloaded in a manner that caused no actual wear and tear. With that ruled out, the complaint is *mishum shinui da'ata* — the disruption of the boat owner's expectations regarding his customer. He contracted with the renter, knew the renter's temperament and requirements, and now must deal with an unknown customer for the second half of the journey — a source of genuine uncertainty and displeasure. Alternatively, *l'ashlei yeteira* — he may need to purchase additional rigging or ropes for a new cargo load, rather than drawing from his home supply, adding an unanticipated expense. Either of these grounds the complaint without rising to the level of enforceable monetary liability.

## Cargo Rights

### Food Portions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר לִרְכּוֹב עָלֶיהָ – שׂוֹכֵר מַנִּיחַ עָלֶיהָ כְּסוּתוֹ וּלְגִנוֹתוֹ וּמְזוֹנוֹת שֶׁל אוֹתָּה הַדֶּרֶךְ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – חַמָּר מְעַכֵּב עָלָיו. חַמָּר מַנִּיחַ עָלָיו שְׂעוֹרִים וְתֶבֶן וּמְזוֹנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – שׂוֹכֵר מְעַכֵּב עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who rents a donkey to ride on it,** the **renter** may **place on it his garment, his water jug, and food for that journey. Beyond those** items, the **donkey driver,** who would take the renter on the journey, may **prevent him** from placing anything on the animal by saying that he does not wish to further burden the donkey. The **donkey driver** may **place on it barley and hay** for the donkey **and his own food for that** first **day** alone. **Beyond those** items, the **renter** may **prevent him** from placing anything on the animal.

The Gemara presents a new *baraita* governing what a renter may load onto a donkey hired for riding. *Sokher maniach aleha ksuto v'lignuto u'mezonot shel oto haderech* — the renter may place on the donkey his garment, his water bottle, and provisions for the entire journey. Beyond this, the donkey's owner (*hammar*) can object to any additional loading, since the rental was for riding, not for carrying. The *hammar*, for his part, may load *se'orim v'teven u'mezonotav shel oto hayom* — barley, straw, and one day's worth of food for the donkey. The renter can object to the *hammar* loading more than a day's worth of provisions, since excess weight slows the animal.

The Gemara notes the asymmetry: the renter gets the whole journey's worth of provisions; the *hammar* gets only one day's.

### Employment Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דִּשְׁכִיחַ לְמִזְבַּן – חַמָּר נָמֵי לִיעַכֵּב, וְאִי דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ לְמִזְבַּן – שׂוֹכֵר נָמֵי לָא לִיעַכֵּב! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא צְרִיכָא דִּשְׁכִיחַ לְמִטְרַח וּלְמִזְבַּן מֵאַוּוֹנָא לְאַוּוֹנָא, חַמָּר דַּרְכֵּיהּ לְמִטְרַח וּלְמִזְבַּן. שׂוֹכֵר לָאו דַּרְכֵּיהּ לְמִטְרַח וּלְמִזְבַּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** this is referring to a situation **where** food **is available for purchase, the donkey driver** should **also** be able **to prevent** the renter from bringing food for the entire journey, **and if** it is a case **where** food **is not available for purchase,** the **renter** should **also not** be able **to prevent** the donkey driver from loading on the donkey his own food for the entire journey. **Rav Pappa said: No,** the ruling of the *baraita* is **necessary** in a situation **where** food **is available** for one who **goes to the trouble to purchase** it **from** one **station [*me'avna*] to** the next **station.** Since it is **the manner of a donkey driver to go to** the **trouble to purchase** food, he may load the animal only with food for that day, whereas it is **not the manner of** the **renter to go to the trouble to purchase** food, and therefore he may take food with him for the entire journey.

The Gemara notes the asymmetry: the renter gets the whole journey's worth of provisions; the *hammar* gets only one day's.

Rav Pappa answers: *lo tzricha d'shchiach l'mitrach u'lmizban me'avna l'avna* — the case is where food can be purchased at stops, but only with effort and hustling. The norm of behavior differs for these two parties. The *hammar* is a worker — *darkei l'mitrach u'lmizban*, it is his way to work hard and hustle. He is expected to manage the logistics. The renter, as the employing party, is not expected to run around acquiring food at every stop. The differential in what each may carry reflects their differential status in the arrangement.

## Gender/Pregnancy

### Gender Clause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר לִרְכּוֹב עָלֶיהָ אִישׁ – לֹא תִּרְכַּב עָלֶיהָ אִשָּׁה. אִשָּׁה – רוֹכֵב עָלֶיהָ אִישׁ. וְאִשָּׁה, בֵּין גְּדוֹלָה וּבֵין קְטַנָּה, אֲפִילּוּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת, וַאֲפִילּוּ מְנִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who rents a donkey with** the understanding that **a man will ride upon it, a woman may not ride upon it.** If he rented it **with** the understanding that **a woman** will ride upon it, **a man** may **ride upon it. And** if he rented it **with** the understanding that **a woman** will ride upon it, any female may ride upon it, **whether** she is **an adult woman or a minor girl.** And **even a pregnant** woman, despite her additional weight, **and even a nursing woman** who takes the child with her may ride upon it.

The *baraita* continues: if one rents a donkey and specifies that *ish* — a man — will ride it, a woman may not ride. The Gemara does not elaborate on the precise reason for this, but it is presumably related either to riding posture or to some differential in how the animal responds, as Rashi and Tosafot do not address the underlying rationale here.

Conversely, if the rental specifies that a woman will ride, then the donkey is available for any woman — *bein gedolah bein ketanah*, whether adult or minor, *afilu meuberes va'afilu menika* — even if she is pregnant or nursing. Pregnancy and nursing might be thought to add weight beyond what was contemplated, but the ruling treats all women as within the class contracted for.

### Akarsa Tikla

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא מְנִיקָה אָמְרַתְּ, מְעוּבֶּרֶת מִיבַּעְיָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מְעוּבֶּרֶת וְהִיא מְנִיקָה קָאָמַר. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בִּינִיתָא אַכְּרֵסַהּ תָּקְלָה. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now** that **you said** that the owner cannot prevent even **a nursing woman** from riding upon the donkey, despite the fact that this involves the weight of two people, **is it necessary** to say that **a pregnant woman** may ride upon the donkey? **Rav Pappa said:** The *tanna* **spoke** of **a pregnant woman who is** also **nursing,** as there is additional weight. **Abaye said: You** can **learn from** the fact that a pregnant woman is considered heavier than the average woman **that the weight of a fish [*binita*] is in its belly,** i.e., weight increases according to the size of its belly. The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **difference** resulting from Abaye's statement with regard to a fish? The Gemara explains: It is **with regard to** the *halakhot* of **buying and selling,** so that one can know how to evaluate the weight of a fish, and calculate its value accordingly.

The Gemara asks: if a nursing woman — who carries a separate infant — is within the permitted class, surely a pregnant woman, whose additional weight is entirely internal to her body, is obvious. Why enumerate pregnant women at all? The Gemara responds: *meuberes vehi menika* — the ruling encompasses even a woman who is simultaneously pregnant and nursing, a condition of compounded weight. Abaye notes a further implication: *shema mina akarsa tikla* — we learn from this that weight is concentrated in the stomach (*tikla* referring to the balance point). The practical application, as Rashi explains, is for commerce: when purchasing fish, one should insist that the fish be gutted before weighing, since the stomach contents add disproportionate weight and can distort the transaction.

### Tosafot Weight

Tosafot reads the Gemara differently and arrives at the opposite conclusion. Tosafot suggests that the Gemara's underlying premise is that a woman carrying a nursing infant actually weighs *less* than a pregnant woman, because the infant bears some of its own weight — the mother supports the child partially, but the child also rests independently. On this reading, *akarsa tikla* means that it is specifically the pregnant woman's belly-weight that is the decisive factor: since a pregnant woman's additional mass is entirely carried by her stomach and cannot be partially self-supported as an infant can, the pregnant woman may weigh more than the same woman nursing her child. This represents an inversion of the plain reading of the Gemara, as the *maggid shiur* acknowledges, and the physiological claim is itself debatable. What is clear is that Tosafot is engaged with a different causal logic than Abaye, understanding the comparison between pregnant and nursing riders as a weighting argument rather than a simple accumulation of persons.
