# Niddah 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Vestot Overview

### Veset Haguf/Hayamim Revi…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֵין הָאִשָּׁה קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת עַד שֶׁתִּקְבָּעֶנָּה שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים, וְאֵינָהּ מִטַּהֶרֶת מִן הַוֶּסֶת עַד שֶׁתֵּעָקֵר מִמֶּנָּה שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **as a woman establishes** a fixed menstrual **cycle only after she establishes it three times. And** a woman **is purified from the** existing fixed menstrual **cycle,** in the sense that intercourse is permitted on that day, **only when she has been displaced from** that day **three times.**

We pick up at the bottom of 63b, in the midst of the ongoing discussion of *vestot*. By way of review: the *veset haguf* refers to bodily experiences — such as cramps or other physical sensations — that indicate a woman is approaching her period. The *veset hayamim* refers to a cyclical pattern based on a fixed number of days. Interestingly, the *veset haguf* was considered more precise and, in certain respects, more significant: whereas a *veset haguf* required three repetitions to be established, it was seen as a sharper indicator of the body's state. By contrast, for a *veset hayamim*, there is no marital relations permitted for the entire day-unit — whether daytime or nighttime — during which the woman is expected to see.

### Day 15 to Day 20 Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה יוֹם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר, וְשִׁינְּתָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים — זֶה וָזֶה אֲסוּרִין. שִׁינְּתָה פַּעֲמַיִם לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים — זֶה וָזֶה אֲסוּרִין. שִׁינְּתָה שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים — הוּתַּר חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר, וְקָבְעָה לָהּ יוֹם עֶשְׂרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **If the woman was accustomed to see** the flow of blood on the **fifteenth day and she deviated** from the norm **to see** the flow of blood **on** the **twentieth day,** then on both **this** day, the fifteenth, **and that** day, the twentieth, it is **prohibited** for her to engage in intercourse. **If she deviated** from the norm **twice,** then on both **this** day, the fifteenth, **and that** day, the twentieth, it is likewise **prohibited** for her to engage in intercourse. **If she deviated** from the norm **to see** the flow of blood **on** the **twentieth day three times, it becomes permitted** for her to engage in intercourse on the **fifteenth, and she** has **established the twentieth day for herself** as the day of her fixed menstrual cycle.

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 63b presents the following scenario: a woman regularly sees on day 15. As the *gemara* earlier noted, this likely refers either to day 15 from her immersion in the *mikvah* — which would yield a 22-day cycle and is more physiologically plausible — or, as we will see when the *gemara* develops the point, to the 15th day of the Hebrew month. For now the discussion proceeds in terms of cycle length.

One month, her cycle shifts to day 20. The following month, she is forbidden on both day 15 and day 20. Day 15 remains prohibited because it is her established pattern, and an established *veset* is not uprooted by a single deviation. Day 20 is prohibited because it was the most recent month's occurrence and might be the beginning of a new pattern asserting itself. In the intervening days she is permitted. If the following month she returns to day 15, she need not worry about day 20 at all, since that single deviation did not recur.

The rule is this: a *veset* requires three consecutive occurrences to become established; an established *veset* requires three consecutive deviations to be uprooted. A one- or two-time deviation generates concern for the immediately following month, but if that month passes without the deviation recurring, the concern lapses — it is uprooted after just a single non-occurrence.

If the new pattern asserts itself three consecutive times, it replaces the old one. Until that point, the old *veset* never disappears; the woman simply worries about both. Once the new pattern is fully established three times, the old one is supplanted.

### Three Times Rule

The *mishnah* states explicitly: *she-ein ishah kova'at lah veset ad she-tikba'enah shalosh pe'amim* — a woman does not establish a *veset* unless she establishes it three times; and *ve-einah mitaheret min ha-veset ad she-te'aker mimennah shalosh pe'amim* — she does not become free of the *veset* until it is uprooted three times.

One question worth holding in mind as we approach the *gemara* is what "uprooted three times" actually means. Suppose a woman with an established day-15 cycle sees on day 20, day 20, and then day 19 — three months without seeing on day 15. Is that sufficient to uproot the *veset*? Or must the old pattern actually be *replaced* by a new, established one? The plain language — *te'aker mimennah gimel pe'amim* — sounds as though mere non-occurrence three times should suffice, without any requirement of replacement. But this remains a genuine question as we proceed into the *gemara* and the *rishonim*.

## Rav vs. Shmuel Dilug

### 15, 16, 17 Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאֲתָה יוֹם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר לְחֹדֶשׁ זֶה, וְיוֹם שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר לְחֹדֶשׁ זֶה, וְיוֹם שִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר לְחֹדֶשׁ זֶה — רַב אָמַר: קָבְעָה לָהּ וֶסֶת לְדִילּוּג, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עַד שֶׁתְּשַׁלֵּשׁ בְּדִילּוּג.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** If a woman **saw** menstrual blood on the **fifteenth of this month, and** on the **sixteenth of** the **month** after **that, and** on the **seventeenth of** the **month** after **that, Rav says: She has** thereby **determined her** menstrual **cycle by skipping,** i.e., as a month and a day. **And Shmuel says:** Her menstrual cycle is not determined **until she skips** a day **three times.** According to Shmuel, as the cycle is established in this case not by the date itself but by the pattern of one additional day every month, the pattern is established only when this occurs three consecutive times, i.e., when she menstruates on the fourth occasion.

The *gemara* at the top of 64a introduces the following case: a woman saw on day 15 of one month, day 16 of the next, and day 17 of the month after that — three successive months, each one day later than the previous. This is referred to as a *veset ha-dilug*, a "jumping" or plus-one pattern. The question is whether this constitutes an established *veset ha-dilug*.

The complication is that while we have three occurrences, only two of them exhibited the actual plus-one jump: from 15 to 16, and from 16 to 17. The first month's occurrence — day 15 — was simply day 15; it did not itself jump from anything. So while three months of data exist and a pattern is discernible, the defining characteristic of the pattern — the progressive increment — was only manifested twice.

### Rav's Position

*Rav* holds that she has established a *veset ha-dilug*. The day-15 occurrence counts as the first of the three required instances, even though it did not itself exhibit a jump. It forms part of the overall pattern, and three occurrences have occurred.

### Shmuel's Position

*Shmuel* disagrees. Since the day-15 occurrence was not itself a plus-one event, it cannot be counted among the three required instances. She would need a fourth month — say, day 18 — so that the jump would have occurred three times independently: 15→16, 16→17, 17→18. Only then would the pattern be established according to *Shmuel*.

### Veset HaChodesh

Before proceeding to the proofs, an important structural point raised by *Tosafot* deserves attention. Notice that the *gemara* frames the case as: she saw on day 15 *of this month*, day 16 *of this month*, day 17 *of this month* — *le-chodesh zeh*. Why does the language invoke the day of the month rather than a gap between occurrences?

*Tosafot* points out that if we were talking about gaps, an additional data point would be required to establish even the starting gap. To know that the gap was 15 days, you need two sightings; to know the next gap was 16 days, you need a third sighting; the fourth sighting establishes a gap of 17 days. So to have three gap-measurements, you would need four sightings — not three. The fact that the *gemara* speaks of three sightings generating the pattern implies that we are not talking about gaps at all, but about the specific day within the Hebrew month. The woman always sees on the 15th, then the 16th, then the 17th of the calendar month.

*Tosafot* further notes that this holds even if the months vary in length — one full (*malei*) and one deficient (*chaser*) — because it is the *keviut ha-chodesh*, the fixing of the month, that matters, not the raw interval between sightings.

This raises a natural question: what is the mechanism by which the day of the Hebrew month could influence a woman's physiology? The *Ra'ah* — a student of the *Ramban* — suggests that the *gemara* does not mean the calendar month at all, but rather the *molad*, the actual lunar cycle. Thus "15 days into the month" would mean 15 days from the new moon, wherever that falls. This has a certain scientific resonance, as the connection between female cycles and the lunar cycle is well documented. However, the *Ra'ah*'s reading does require departing from the plain meaning of *le-chodesh*. The *Rashba*, following *Tosafot* more closely, accepts that the Hebrew calendar month itself can have a metaphysical-physiological impact — and indeed, there are reported cases of women whose periods consistently align with *Rosh Chodesh* regardless of the calendar month's length.

### Three Types of Vestot

This discussion brings into view three primary categories of *veset* in the *gemara*. The *veset haguf* is based on bodily sensations. The *veset hayamim* is based on the gap of days between bleedings — the *haflaga*. And the *veset ha-chodesh* is based on the specific day within the Hebrew month, independent of the time elapsed since the previous sighting. These are distinct categories, and the *veset ha-chodesh* is in some respects the most puzzling, as it seems least directly linked to any obvious physiological predictor.

The *gemara* attempts to resolve the Rav-Shmuel debate by suggesting that perhaps they are simply reprising the famous *machloket* between *Rabbi* and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* over the number of instances required to establish a *chazakah*. The *baraita* is cited: a woman who married and her first husband died, then a second husband died — *Rabbi* holds that two occurrences suffice to establish a pattern of concern; *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* holds that a third marriage is permitted, and only from a fourth does the pattern become a prohibition. The *gemara*, however, rejects this identification: all parties here agree with *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* that three instances are required. The debate between *Rav* and *Shmuel* is specifically about whether day 15 — which establishes the starting point but does not itself exhibit the jump — counts among those three instances.

## Baraita 15-18 Case

### Baraita Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה יוֹם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר, וְשִׁינְּתָה לְיוֹם שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר — זֶה וָזֶה אֲסוּרִין. שִׁינְּתָה לְיוֹם שִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר — הוּתַּר שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר, וְנֶאֱסַר חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר וְשִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר. שִׁינְּתָה לְיוֹם שְׁמוֹנָה עָשָׂר — הוּתְּרוּ כּוּלָּן, וְאֵין אָסוּר אֶלָּא מִשְּׁמוֹנָה עָשָׂר וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** If a woman **was accustomed to see** a flow of blood on the **fifteenth day** of the month, **and deviated** and instead experienced bleeding **on the sixteenth day** of the month, on both **this** day, the fifteenth, **and that** day, the sixteenth, it is **prohibited** for her to engage in intercourse with her husband. If she then **deviated** and experienced bleeding **on the seventeenth day** of the following month, **the sixteenth** day becomes **permitted, and the fifteenth and seventeenth** days of the following month **are prohibited.** If she subsequently **deviated** and experienced bleeding **on** the **eighteenth day** of the following month, **all** the days **are permitted.** The fifteenth is permitted because she has now experienced bleeding three times on a different day, while the other days are permitted because she experienced bleeding only once on each day. **And** it is **prohibited** for her to engage in intercourse in the following month **only from** the **eighteenth** day **and onward,** i.e., on the nineteenth day, since she has now established a pattern of bleeding after one month and one day.

The *gemara* now challenges *Rav* from a *baraita*. The *baraita* presents a woman who regularly saw on day 15. She then sees on day 16 — so both 15 and 16 are now prohibited for the following month. The next month she sees on day 17: day 16 is released (since it only occurred once and then failed to recur), while both 15 and 17 are now the concern. The month after that she sees on day 18: *hutru kulam ve-ein asurah ela mi-shmonah asar va-eilach* — all prior days are released, and from day 18 onward is the new concern.

Note what just happened to day 15: three months running she did not see on day 15 (she saw on 16, then 17, then 18), and it is now released without having been explicitly replaced by a new fixed *veset*. This is a significant finding, as it directly addresses the question raised at the outset: *Rashi* makes clear that *me-shmonah asar va-eilach* means that from day 18 a new plus-one pattern has now been established, so the concern going forward is for day 19 the next month, then 20, and so on.

### Kashya to Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא לְרַב! אָמַר לְךָ רַב: לְמוּדָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** This is **difficult for Rav,** as he maintains that twice is sufficient to establish a pattern, whereas this *baraita* requires three instances. **Rav** could have **said to you** in response: Since she **was accustomed** to experience bleeding on the fifteenth **it is different.** In that particular case the fifteenth does not count as the beginning of a new pattern because it was already her regular established time.

This *baraita* raises a difficulty for *Rav*. According to *Rav*, day 15 can count as the first instance of a pattern, so when the woman has seen on days 15, 16, and 17, that should already constitute three instances of a *veset ha-dilug* — and one would expect concern for day 18 to begin at that point. Why does the *baraita* only raise the concern starting from day 18 itself?

One might ask the same question of *Shmuel*: even according to *Shmuel*, once the woman has seen on 15, 16, 17, and 18, you have three genuine plus-one jumps, so the new pattern is established. The *gemara* assumes that the statement *me-shmonah asar va-eilach* already incorporates this — meaning *Shmuel*'s position is consistent with the *baraita* by reading it as: four sightings (15, 16, 17, 18) providing three jumps, with day 18 as the new starting point. The difficulty falls specifically on *Rav*.

### Limuda Shani

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְקָאָרֵי לַהּ, מַאי קָאָרֵי לַהּ? לְמוּדָה אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דִּלְמוּדָה וַעֲקַרְתֵּיהּ — בִּתְרֵי זִימְנֵי עָקְרָה לֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at Shmuel's question: **And he who asked [*udeka'arei*] it, why did he ask it?** The *baraita* clearly stresses the fact that the woman began with an established fixed menstrual cycle that was not part of the pattern. Shmuel could answer: **It was necessary** for the *baraita* to teach the case where she **was accustomed** to experience bleeding on a specific day, **lest you say** that **since she was accustomed** to experience bleeding on a specific day **and displaced** that pattern by bleeding on a different day, it should be sufficient that **with two times she displaces it.** For this reason, the *baraita* **teaches us** that an established pattern is displaced only after three occasions where she experiences bleeding on a different day. If it is not established, one displacement is enough.

*Rav* responds: *limuda shani* — a case involving an established prior pattern is different. When a woman has been seeing on day 15 for many months, that day-15 occurrence is not an open, uncommitted data point that can be recruited into a new pattern. It belongs to the old established *veset*. Therefore, when she sees on day 16 and then 17, one cannot look back and say: "ah, 15, 16, 17 — a new pattern." The 15 was the old pattern; the new pattern begins only with the first deviation. Thus the relevant sequence is 16, 17, 18 — three instances of the jumping pattern — and only once all three have occurred does the new *veset ha-dilug* come into effect.

This is the distinction *Rav* draws: if a woman begins seeing for the first time and the initial three months yield days 15, 16, and 17, then day 15 counts toward the new pattern, as it has no prior identity as an established *veset*. But if day 15 was already a *veset kavua*, it cannot simultaneously serve as the opening of a new, different pattern.

### Uprooting vs. Replacement

The *gemara* asks: why was it necessary for the *baraita* to use the case of an established pattern to convey *Rav*'s principle? Could it not have made a simpler point? The *gemara* responds: one might have thought that since the woman had a well-entrenched established pattern, perhaps that established pattern could be uprooted by two deviations rather than three — given how strongly it was fixed, perhaps its displacement requires less strict rules once the deviations begin. Therefore the *baraita* teaches that even an established *veset* requires three consecutive non-occurrences to be uprooted.

The deeper question, however, remains unresolved: has day 15 here been displaced because three months elapsed without it occurring, or because a new pattern (16, 17, 18) actively replaced it? The *gemara* explicitly leaves this open. Even after this discussion it is not definitively clear whether a *veset kavua* can be uprooted by mere non-occurrence three times, or whether actual replacement by a new pattern is required. Whatever answer one proposes here, it is a *machloket rishonim*. Anything one might think to ask here is a *machloket rishonim*.

## Rav Pappa Rules

### One Time Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֵין הָאִשָּׁה קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת עַד שֶׁתִּקְבָּעֶנָּה וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא לְמִקְבְּעַהּ, אֲבָל לְמֵיחַשׁ לַהּ בַּחֲדָא זִימְנָא חָיְישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **As a woman establishes** a fixed menstrual **cycle only after she establishes it** three times. **Rav Pappa says,** in explanation: **We said** that she must experience bleeding three times **only with regard to establishing** a fixed menstrual cycle, **but with regard to** being **concerned** for a pattern, even **after one time she must be concerned** that this might be the beginning of a fixed pattern.

*Rav Pappa* articulates a clarification: when we say three times is required — whether to establish or to uproot — that applies only to *establishing* a fixed pattern or *uprooting* one. But for mere concern — *le-michash lah* — a single occurrence is sufficient. This is the principle underlying all the earlier cases: when a woman with an established day-15 cycle sees once on day 20, she is concerned about both days the following month even though nothing has been established yet.

### Zavah Period Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי — הֵיכָא דְּקָיְימָא בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי נִדָּתָהּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא קָיְימָא בְּתוֹךְ יְמֵי נִדָּתָהּ — אֵימָא לָא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If** this *halakha* was derived only **from there, I would say** that **this statement** applies in a case **where** she experiences bleeding on the twentieth day while **she is standing in her days** suitable for **menstruation,** i.e., the days following the eleven days after her last period, when she is most likely to menstruate. **But** in a case **where she is not standing in her days** suitable for **menstruation,** but rather during the eleven days when uterine bleeding would render her a *zava*, **I would say** that she does **not** need to be concerned that she might experience bleeding again at that time. Therefore, Rav Pappa **teaches us** that even during these eleven days she must be concerned.

The *gemara* challenges: is this not already obvious from our *mishnah*? The *mishnah* itself says that a woman who regularly sees on day 15 and once sees on day 20 must worry about both 15 and 20 the following month — so a single occurrence is evidently sufficient for concern. *Rav Pappa* would seem to be restating what the *mishnah* already teaches.

The *gemara* answers that *Rav Pappa*'s contribution is not redundant. One might have thought the following: when the *mishnah* says a single occurrence generates concern, it applies only when that single occurrence fell within the woman's *niddah* period — that is, more than 18 days after her previous sighting, in the range where uterine bleeding is expected. If the single occurrence fell instead within the *zavah* period (between days 8 and 18 from the previous sighting), one might have said: that is a completely anomalous occurrence, not characteristic of a menstrual pattern at all, and therefore no concern need be generated for the following month. *Rav Pappa* teaches otherwise: even a single sighting during the *zavah* period generates concern for the following month.

It should be noted that this is distinct from the rule of *ein ketamim mishum veset* — the principle that stains, even *tamei* stains, do not create *veset* concerns. *Rav Pappa*'s ruling applies to a genuine flow, not a mere stain. A stain, even if it renders the woman a *niddah*, does not generate *veset* concerns for the future.

### Unestablished Uprooting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינָהּ מִטַּהֶרֶת מִן הַוֶּסֶת וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דִּקְבַעְתֵּיהּ תְּלָתָא זִימְנֵי, דִּצְרִיכִי תְּלָתָא זִימְנֵי לְמִעְקְרֵיהּ, אֲבָל תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי — בַּחֲדָא זִימְנָא מִיעֲקַר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** a woman **is purified from the** existing fixed menstrual **cycle,** in the sense that intercourse is permitted on that day, **only** when she has been displaced from that day three times. **Rav Pappa says,** in explanation: **We said** this **only** in the case of a set cycle **that she established** by **three times.** With regard to such a cycle, we said **that** a different **three times are necessary to displace** that cycle. **But** with regard to a cycle that is established with only **two times, it is displaced by one time** of bleeding on a different day.

*Rav Pappa* adds a further point: the three-times uprooting requirement applies only when the *veset* was itself fully established through three occurrences. If the *veset* was established by fewer than three occurrences — say, two times — then a single non-occurrence is sufficient to eliminate that concern entirely.

Again the *gemara* asks: is this not obvious from the *mishnah*? The *mishnah* says a *veset* is only established after three times, implying that anything short of three is not yet a *veset kavua* and needs less to be dismissed. *Rav Pappa* responds: one might reasonably have assumed a proportionality principle — one occurrence requires one non-occurrence to uproot, two occurrences require two, three require three. *Rav Pappa* teaches that this is not so: anything short of three established occurrences is eliminated by a single non-occurrence. The parallel in the laws of *zavah* is instructive: a woman who sees one or two days during the *zavah* period needs only one clean day, while only three days of *zavah* bleeding triggers the full seven-day requirement.

## Day 20-30 Case

### 20 to 30 Switch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה יוֹם עֶשְׂרִים, וְשִׁינְּתָה לַיּוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים — זֶה וָזֶה אֲסוּרִין. הִגִּיעַ יוֹם עֶשְׂרִים וְלֹא רָאֲתָה — מוּתֶּרֶת לְשַׁמֵּשׁ עַד יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים, וְחוֹשֶׁשֶׁת לְיוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Pappa:** If a woman **was accustomed to see** the flow of blood on the **twentieth day** after her previous flow, **and she deviated** from the norm to experience bleeding **on** the **thirtieth day,** on both **this** day, the twentieth, **and that** day, the thirtieth, it is **prohibited** for her to engage in intercourse. If the **twentieth day arrived,** counting from the previous flow, which had occurred on day thirty, **and she did not see** a flow of blood, **it is permitted for her to engage** in intercourse **until** the **thirtieth day** after the previous flow, **and she** must be **concerned on** the **thirtieth day.**

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* that *Rav Pappa* cited in support of his position. A woman regularly saw on day 20 — her established *veset kavua*. One month she sees instead on day 30.

### Four Month Cycle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים וְרָאֲתָה, הִגִּיעַ יוֹם עֶשְׂרִים וְלֹא רָאֲתָה, וְהִגִּיעַ יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים וְלֹא רָאֲתָה, וְהִגִּיעַ יוֹם עֶשְׂרִים וְרָאֲתָה — הוּתַּר יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים, וְנֶאֱסַר יוֹם עֶשְׂרִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאוֹרַח בִּזְמַנּוֹ בָּא.
>
> **Translation:** If the **thirtieth day arrived and she saw** a flow of blood, and then the **twentieth day** after that **arrived and she did not see** a flow of blood, **and** the **thirtieth day** from her previous flow **arrived and she did not see** blood on that day but saw on a later day, **and** then the **twentieth day** after her previous flow **arrived and she saw** a flow of blood, the **thirtieth day becomes permitted,** as she experienced bleeding after an interval of thirty days only twice. This is in accordance with the opinion of Rav Pappa that a cycle of bleeding that has occurred twice is displaced by a single time of not bleeding on that day. **And** she is **prohibited** on the **twentieth day, because** it is the **manner** of women that their blood flow **comes at its** usual **time.**

The *baraita* traces four successive months. Month one: she sees on day 30 only, not on day 20. Both 20 and 30 are now concerns for month two. Month two arrives: she does not see on day 20, but she does see on day 30. Now day 30 has occurred twice in a row. Both 20 and 30 remain concerns for month three. Month three arrives: she sees neither on day 20 nor on day 30. Then month four arrives — and she sees on day 20.

### Reassertion Principle

The ruling: *hutrah yom sheloshim* — day 30 is released — *ve-na'aseh yom esrim*, and day 20 reasserts itself as the governing *veset*. The reason given is *mipnei she-oreach bi-zmano ba* — because the regular visitor arrived at its expected time. The established *veset* of day 20, with all its historical weight, reasserted itself, and this sufficed to dismiss the day-30 pattern even though it had occurred twice.

This *baraita* is adduced in support of *Rav Pappa*'s principle that a pattern established fewer than three times is uprooted by a single contrary occurrence. Day 30 occurred twice but was dismissed by a single return of day 20.

### Remaining Questions

However, this proof is not without difficulty. The case is not a clean test of *Rav Pappa*'s principle for several reasons. First, month three was a total miss — neither 20 nor 30 occurred — which itself may have begun eroding the day-30 pattern before day 20 returned in month four. Second, and more importantly, what dismissed day 30 was not a neutral non-occurrence but the powerful reassertion of the *veset kavua* of day 20. One might argue that it is specifically the weight of the long-established *veset* that drove day 30 off the table. Would day 30 have been so clearly dismissed if month four had instead been day 28 — something that negated day 30 but without the force of the prior established pattern?

This also touches again on the unresolved question about uprooting. Month three produced no sighting at all. Does that constitute one of the steps toward uprooting day 20's established *veset*? It seems not, since day 20 reasserts cleanly in month four. But does that mean three missed months are insufficient to uproot? Or is this a special case because the absence was followed immediately by the original *veset*'s own reassertion? The *gemara* leaves all of this open, and as noted — anything one might think to ask here is a *machloket rishonim*.

The general tendency among the *rishonim* is that a *veset kavua* must be *replaced* — not merely missed — for uprooting to be effective. Three months of non-occurrence without a competing new pattern is not sufficient. But this too remains debated.

## Dam Betulim Intro

### Women as Vines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשִׁים בִּבְתוּלֵיהֶם כִּגְפָנִים, יֵשׁ גֶּפֶן שֶׁיֵּינָהּ אָדוֹם, וְיֵשׁ גֶּפֶן שֶׁיֵּינָהּ שָׁחוֹר, וְיֵשׁ גֶּפֶן שֶׁיֵּינָהּ מְרוּבֶּה, וְיֵשׁ גֶּפֶן שֶׁיֵּינָהּ מוּעָט. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל גֶּפֶן יֵשׁ בָּהּ יַיִן, וְשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ יַיִן — הֲרֵי זֶה דּוֹרְקְטִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Women, with regard to** the blood that flows when **their hymens** are ruptured, **are like grapevines: There is a vine that** produces **wine** that **is red, and there is a vine that** produces **wine** that **is black; and there is a vine that** produces **wine in abundance, and there is a vine that** produces only **a meager** amount of **wine. Rabbi Yehuda says: In every vine, there are** grapes fit to produce **wine, but** any vine in **which there are no** grapes fit to produce **wine, this is a dry vine [*durkati*].** Likewise, any woman who experiences bleeding is capable of giving birth, whereas one who does not experience bleeding is like a dry vine, unable to give birth.

With the discussion of *vestot* concluded, the *gemara* transitions to a *mishnah* that properly belongs to the opening of the next *perek*, though it is placed here. The *mishnah* states: *nashim bi-vtuleihen ke-gefanim* — women in terms of their hymens are like vines. Just as some vines yield red wine and others black, some produce abundant juice and others very little, so too women vary in the extent and duration of their hymenal bleeding. *Rabbi Yehuda* adds that every vine has wine — a woman without any hymenal bleeding is like the *dorkati*, a dried-out, unproductive vine — a term the *gemara* etymologizes from the Aramaic *dor katua*, "a severed generation," meaning a lineage that will not continue.

The purpose of this *mishnah* in context is to set up the discussion that follows: when determining how long after first intercourse bleeding may be attributed to the hymen, one must bear in mind that women vary considerably. Some bleed more than others, and for longer.

## Wound Healing Signs

### Shmuel's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁנּוֹחֶרֶת. כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לִי: נְחִירָה זוֹ אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מָה הִיא, אֶלָּא כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהָרוֹק מָצוּי בְּתוֹךְ הַפֶּה מֵחֲמַת תַּשְׁמִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Beit Hillel say: The blood is attributed to the torn hymen **until the wound heals.** The Gemara clarifies: **Until when** can the blood be attributed to the torn hymen? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: All the time that she is *noḥeret*.** Rav Yehuda continues: **When** I subsequently **said this** *halakha* **before Shmuel, he said to me: This *noḥeret*, I do not know what it is,** nor do I know what Rav means by it. **Rather, all the time that the saliva is in** her **mouth due to sexual intercourse,** she may attribute the blood to the torn hymen. Shmuel is using a euphemism, i.e., as long as there is blood in her vagina resulting from sexual intercourse.

*Shmuel*'s criterion for determining whether the hymenal wound is still unhealed is straightforward: as long as there is bleeding during intercourse — *kol zman she-ha-rok matzri be-toch ha-peh machmat tashmish*, "as long as the saliva is still in the mouth because of sex" (a metaphoric expression) — one may consider the wound unhealed and the bleeding attributable to the hymen. *Tosafot* further notes that bleeding occurring during the following day, even outside the context of intercourse, may also be attributed to the hymenal bleeding that occurred during sex the night before.

### Rav's Stricter Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְחִירָה דְּקָאָמַר רַב, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַב — עוֹמֶדֶת וְרוֹאָה, יוֹשֶׁבֶת וְאֵינָהּ רוֹאָה — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁלֹּא חָיְתָה הַמַּכָּה. עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע וְרוֹאָה, עַל גַּבֵּי כָּרִים וּכְסָתוֹת וְאֵינָהּ רוֹאָה — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁלֹּא חָיְתָה הַמַּכָּה. עַל גַּבֵּי כּוּלָּם וְרוֹאָה, עַל גַּבֵּי כּוּלָּם וְאֵינָהּ רוֹאָה — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁחָיְתָה הַמַּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: This ***noḥeret* that Rav says; what is it like?** What did he mean? **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak said: It was explained to me by Rav** as follows: If the young girl **stands** up **and sees** blood, but she **sits and does not see** blood, **it is known that the wound has not** yet **healed,** and the blood is still attributed to the torn hymen. Similarly, if she sits **on the ground and sees** blood, but she sits **on cushions and blankets and does not see** blood, **it is known that the wound has not** yet **healed** and she may attribute the blood to the torn hymen, as the blood flows due to the strain of sitting on the ground. But if she sometimes sits **on all of them,** i.e., the ground, cushions, and blankets, **and sees** blood, and on other occasions she sits **on all of them and does not see** blood, **it is known that the wound has healed,** and this blood must now be menstrual blood.

*Rav*'s criterion is stricter. *Rav Yehuda* reported that when he stated *Rav*'s view in the presence of *Shmuel*, *Shmuel* said he did not know what *Rav* meant by the term *nocheret* (literally, something like "dripping" or "pierced"). The explanation attributed to *Rav* — transmitted by *Shmuel* citing *Rav Yitzchak* — is positional: if the woman stands and sees blood, but sits and does not, then the wound is demonstrably unhealed. Similarly, if she sits on hard ground and bleeds, but sits on soft cushions and does not. The idea is that the physical discomfort of certain positions continues to irritate the unhealed wound, and the differential bleeding pattern is evidence that the blood originates from the hymenal site.

### Consistency Test

If, however, the bleeding occurs consistently regardless of position — she bleeds in all postures and on all surfaces, or equally fails to bleed in any — then *biyadua she-chayah ha-makkah*: one must presume the wound has healed. The reasoning is that consistent, position-independent bleeding is more characteristic of uterine blood than of wound-based bleeding; if it came from the wound, one would expect positional variation. *Tosafot* clarifies that in such a case it is not that the wound is definitively healed, but that the evidence of ongoing wounding is absent, and one must therefore be concerned that the healing has occurred and subsequent bleeding is of uterine origin.

*Rav*'s criterion is thus considerably more demanding than *Shmuel*'s: beyond the fact of bleeding during intercourse, *Rav* requires that positional variation be demonstrable before attributing the blood to the hymenal wound.

## Dam Betulim Intro…

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַשְּׂאוֹר יָפֶה לָעִיסָּה, כָּךְ דָּמִים יָפִין לָאִשָּׁה. תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כׇּל אִשָּׁה שֶׁדָּמֶיהָ מְרוּבִּין — בָּנֶיהָ מְרוּבִּין.
>
> **Translation:** As **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches: Just as leaven is good for dough, so too, blood is good for a woman.** It was likewise **taught in the name of Rabbi Meir: Any woman whose blood is plentiful, her children are plentiful.**

*Rabbi Chiya* taught: just as sourdough is beneficial for dough — causing it to rise — so blood is beneficial for a woman, understood here as a reference to fertility. And the teaching in the name of *Rabbi Meir*: a woman who has abundant blood has abundant children. The *gemara* understands this as connecting menstrual blood to fertility — the assumption being that regular, robust menstrual flow is an indicator of reproductive health. The connection the *gemara* appears to draw to hymenal bleeding is striking and somewhat difficult scientifically, but the *mishnah*'s comparison of women to vines is meant to contextualize the wide variation in the quantity and duration of *dam betulim*.

## Pubescent, No Period

### Rav vs. Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ לִרְאוֹת וְנִיסֵּת, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹתְנִין לָהּ לַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עַד מוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת (ארבע) [אַרְבָּעָה] לֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of a young woman **whose time to see** a menstrual flow **has arrived** but she has not yet begun to menstruate, **and she married** and engaged in intercourse and her hymen was torn, **Beit Shammai say:** The Sages **give her the first night** during which the blood is attributed to the torn hymen. Thereafter, any blood is assumed to be menstrual blood. **And Beit Hillel say:** The blood is attributed to the torn hymen **until the conclusion of Shabbat,** and she may engage in intercourse with her husband for **four nights,** as it was customary for a virgin to marry on Wednesday.

The *mishnah* describes the category of a girl who has reached the age of puberty (*higia zmanah*) but has not yet had her period, and who then marries. *Beit Hillel* grants her four nights during which any hymenal bleeding is not attributed to uterine bleeding. The *gemara* asks: does this mean four literal nights, or does it mean four *onot* — four halachic time-units, each of which may be either a daytime or nighttime period?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: שִׁימְּשָׁה בַּיָּמִים — רַב אָמַר: לֹא הִפְסִידָה לֵילוֹת, וְלֵוִי אָמַר: הִפְסִידָה לֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is a dispute between *amora'im* with regard to the following case: If **she engaged in intercourse** with her husband **during the daytime,** in addition to engaging in intercourse at night, **Rav says: She has not lost** her **nights,** and the blood is still attributed to the torn hymen for four nights. **Levi says: She has lost** her **nights,** since she has engaged in intercourse twice during the daytime and twice at night, and therefore she has already used up the equivalent of four nights.

*Rav* holds that if she has relations during the daytime, she does not lose her nighttime allocations — the four nights referred to in the *mishnah* mean the period spanning four 24-hour days, and any intercourse and bleeding within that window is attributed to the hymen. *Levi* disagrees: she loses the nights. According to *Levi*, *arba leilot* means *arba onot* — four units of time — and once those four units have been used, subsequent bleeding is no longer attributed to the hymen, regardless of how much calendar time remains.

### Shabbat Permissibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב, לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵא ״אַרְבָּעָה לֵילוֹת״? אוֹרַח אַרְעָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּדַרְכָּהּ דְּבִיאָה בַּלֵּילוֹת. וּלְלֵוִי, לִיתְנֵי ״אַרְבָּעָה לֵילוֹת״, ״עַד מוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת״ לְמָה לִי? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דִּשְׁרֵי לְמִבְעַל לְכַתְּחִלָּה בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rav, why do I** need the mishna **to teach: Four nights?** The mishna should have stated four days, which would include both days and nights. Rav would respond that the mishna **teaches us proper conduct, as it is proper that intercourse** should be performed only **at night.** The Gemara suggests: **And according to** the opinion of **Levi, let** the mishna **teach** only: **Four nights. Why do I** need the mishna to specify: **Until the conclusion of Shabbat?** Levi would answer that **this teaches us that it is permitted to engage in intercourse for the first** time **on Shabbat.** Since the custom was for a virgin to marry on Wednesday, which means that one of the four first nights is Shabbat, it is permitted to engage in intercourse on that night, despite the fact that it may cause her to bleed.

The *mishnah* says "until *Motzaei Shabbat*" — since in the time of the *mishnah*, women typically married on Wednesday, and the first intercourse was Wednesday night. Thus the four nights would be Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, and *Shabbat* nights, concluding at *Motzaei Shabbat*.

According to *Rav*, this phrasing of "until *Motzaei Shabbat*" is simply the natural way to express four days. But according to *Levi*, why say "until *Motzaei Shabbat*" instead of simply "four *onot*"? The *gemara* answers: *de-shari le-mivaal le-chatchilah be-Shabbat* — the *mishnah* comes to emphasize that one may have relations on *Shabbat* even in this context, where such relations may continue to cause hymenal bleeding. This connects to the broader discussion in *Ketubot* about whether initial intercourse — with its attendant hymenal rupture and bleeding — is permissible on *Shabbat*. Here, where the hymen has already been partially opened in prior nights, the question is whether continued relations on *Shabbat* that will cause further bleeding and possibly pain are permissible. The answer is yes, and this is supported by *Shmuel*'s principle:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: פִּרְצָה דְּחוּקָה מוּתָּר לִיכָּנֵס בָּהּ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמַּשִּׁיר צְרוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that Levi's ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel, as Shmuel said: It is permitted to enter into a narrow opening** in a wall **on Shabbat, and** this is the *halakha* **even though** doing so **causes pebbles to fall** from the wall. Similarly, although engaging in intercourse might cause a wound and bleeding, it is permitted on Shabbat.

*Pirtza dechuka mutar leikanes bah be-Shabbat af al pi she-meshir tzerurot* — a tight opening may be entered on *Shabbat* even though doing so dislodges some pebbles. The metaphor, applied here to continued relations during the four-night period, permits the act even though it causes ongoing hymenal bleeding.

## Dam Betulim Intro……

### Blood & Fertility

*Rabbi Chiya*'s teaching that blood is beneficial for a woman, like sourdough for dough, and the teaching in the name of *Rabbi Meir* that a woman with abundant blood has abundant children, are the *gemara*'s broader theological-medical context for the *dam betulim* discussion. The *gemara* understands the *mishnah*'s vine metaphor — and *Rabbi Yehuda*'s insistence that all vines have wine — as asserting that the presence or abundance of hymenal blood is an indicator of fertility and vitality. A woman who lacks any *dam betulim* entirely is like the *dorkati*, the barren vine.

## Prepubescent Girl

### Four Nights/Healed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינוֹקֶת שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ לִרְאוֹת וְנִיסֵּת — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹתְנִין לָהּ אַרְבַּע לֵילוֹת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁתִּחְיֶה הַמַּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a young girl whose time to see** a menstrual flow, i.e., the age of puberty, **has not** yet **arrived, and she married** and engaged in intercourse and her hymen was torn, **Beit Shammai say:** The Sages **give her four nights** after intercourse during which the blood is attributed to the torn hymen and she remains ritually pure. Thereafter, any blood is assumed to be menstrual blood and renders her impure. **And Beit Hillel say:** The blood is attributed to the torn hymen **until the wound heals.**

For a *tinoket* — a prepubescent girl who has not yet reached the age of menstruation — *Beit Shammai* holds that she is given four nights within which hymenal bleeding may be attributed to the wound and not to uterine bleeding. *Beit Hillel* is more lenient still: she is given until the wound heals, *ad she-tichyeh ha-makkah*, with no fixed nighttime limit. The rationale for the greater leniency is that since she is prepubescent, there is far less reason to suspect uterine bleeding; the default presumption strongly favors the hymenal wound as the source.

### Age Over History

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: וַאֲפִילּוּ רָאֲתָה. מִמַּאי? מִדְּקָא מְפַלֵּיג בְּסֵיפָא בֵּין רָאֲתָה בֵּין בְּשֶׁלֹּא רָאֲתָה, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא — לָא שְׁנָא הָכִי וְלָא שְׁנָא הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna first addresses the case of a young girl who has not yet reached puberty. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: And** this *halakha* applies to her **even** if **she has seen** menstrual blood. He explains his reasoning: **From where** do I derive this? I derive it **from** the fact **that** the *tanna* **distinguishes in the latter clause** of the mishna **between** a young girl who has **seen** menstrual blood **and** a young girl who has **not seen** menstrual blood. **By inference,** in **the first clause** of the mishna the *halakha* **is no different** in **this** case, where the young girl has experienced menstrual bleeding, **and** it **is no different** in **that** case, where she has not yet experienced menstrual bleeding.

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* clarifies that the *tinoket* category is determined by age, not by menstrual history. Even if a prepubescent girl has had some anomalous prior bleeding, her status as a *tinoket* is unaffected. This is derived from the structure of the *mishnah*: the later clause, discussing a girl who has reached puberty, specifies two sub-cases — whether or not she has had prior menstrual bleeding — thereby implying by contrast that in the earlier clause, concerning the prepubescent girl, this distinction is irrelevant. The *baraita* confirms this:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁתִּחְיֶה הַמַּכָּה, בֵּין רָאֲתָה בֵּין לֹא רָאֲתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This** explanation of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Beit Hillel say:** With regard to a young girl who has not yet reached puberty, the blood she emits is attributed to the torn hymen **until the wound heals,** regardless of **whether she has seen** menstrual blood beforehand or **whether she has not** yet **seen** menstrual blood.

## Already Menstruated

### Beit Shammai Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאֲתָה וְעוֹדָהּ בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹתְנִין לָהּ בְּעִילַת מִצְוָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה (כּוּלָּהּ) [שֶׁלָּהּ].
>
> **Translation:** In the case of a young woman who **saw** menstrual blood before marriage while **she was still in her father's house, Beit Shammai say:** The Sages **give her** permission to engage only in **relations** that consummate a marriage, which are **a mitzva,** after which she is ritually impure due to the blood. **And Beit Hillel say:** The husband and wife may engage even in several acts of intercourse, as any blood seen **throughout the entire night** is attributed to the torn hymen.

For a woman who has already menstruated prior to marriage — and thus cannot claim the full benefit of the doubt afforded to prepubescent girls or to girls who have merely reached puberty without yet having their period — *Beit Shammai* permits only *beilat mitzvah*, the first act of intercourse. The designation *beilat mitzvah* is noteworthy; the term *mitzvah* here reflects the halachic obligation associated with the initial consummation of marriage. Any bleeding during or attributable to that first act may be attributed to the hymen; thereafter, any bleeding raises the concern of uterine blood.

### Beit Hillel Rule

*Beit Hillel* is, as always, one degree more lenient: the entire first night is permitted. Even if intercourse occurs multiple times during that first night, and bleeding occurs at any point during that night, it may all be attributed to hymenal bleeding. Bleeding from the second night onward, however, is no longer presumptively hymenal. The *gemara* notes that there is a question — addressed in an earlier passage and implicit here — of how far beyond the act of intercourse itself the attribution may extend. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* suggest that bleeding occurring during the following day, even without intercourse, may in some circumstances still be attributed to the wound caused the previous night.

## Pubescent, No Period…

### One vs. Four Nights

To summarize the three-way typology of the *mishnah*: for the prepubescent girl (*tinoket*), *Beit Shammai* gives four nights and *Beit Hillel* gives until the wound heals. For the girl who has reached puberty but not yet had her period, *Beit Shammai* gives one night and *Beit Hillel* gives four nights — specifically framed as "until *Motzaei Shabbat*," given the convention that marriage occurred on Wednesday. The pattern is consistent: *Beit Hillel* is one category more lenient than *Beit Shammai* across all three cases. The reasoning throughout is calibrated to how strongly the presumption of hymenal, rather than uterine, bleeding can be maintained given the woman's age and prior menstrual history.
