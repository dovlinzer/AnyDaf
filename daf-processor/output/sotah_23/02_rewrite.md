# Sotah 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zimri & Pinchas

### Zimri & Pinchas

The daf opens by returning to the close of the *aggadata* passage that concluded the previous amud. The Gemara had been exploring the phenomenon of women who appear one way and are in reality another — the *isha perusha* who presents outward piety while concealing inner corruption. But as the discussion developed, the Gemara broadened its scope considerably, recognizing this as a wider human phenomenon that applies to men no less than to women. This broadening itself is worth noting: even within a tractate devoted to the *sotah*, the Gemara is attuned to the question of whether we are examining something specific to women's sin or something universal in human nature.

### Broader Phenomenon

The final line of the *aggadata* before the *mishna* is worth savoring for the precision with which it captures a particular personality type — one that has since become a stock figure in Rabbinic literature: *ma'aseihem kema'asei Zimri u'mevakshim sachar kePinchas* — they act like Zimri yet expect to be rewarded like Pinchas. The figure is not merely about duplicity before others, the hypocrite who performs righteousness publicly while sinning in private. At a deeper level, it describes people who have somehow convinced themselves of their own righteousness, who have internalized the self-image of Pinchas while their conduct mirrors Zimri.

### Zenut & Kina

The pairing of Zimri and Pinchas is especially resonant in this context. Zimri sins through public sexual transgression with a Midianite woman, while Pinchas is the *kanai*, the zealot, whose defining act is *kina* — zealotry. The concept of *kina* runs through the Torah on multiple levels. On one plane it describes the jealous husband; on another it describes God's own demand for exclusive fidelity: *El kana hu*, God is a jealous God. The metaphor of Israel's worship of other gods is framed throughout the Torah as *zenut* — *ve'zanah acharei elohei nechar ha'aretz* — a betrayal of the covenantal relationship with God, depicted as a kind of spiritual adultery. Pinchas acts *asher kina l'elohav*, as one who is jealous on God's behalf, because Zimri's act was simultaneously an act of physical *zenut* and a form of abandonment of God through entanglement with the deities of Midian. The intersection of the metaphorical *zenut* and the literal *zenut* is striking, and it also reinforces that this passage is not exclusively about women — it is about men, about the fundamental human capacity for self-deception in matters of fidelity and devotion.

## Zechut Toleh Debate

### R. Shimon: No Zechut

The *mishna* now opens with a striking position: *Rabbi Shimon omer ein zechut tola b'mayim hamarim* — Rabbi Shimon flatly denies that merit ever caused the bitter waters to suspend their judgment. It is worth pausing on the epistemological strangeness of this debate. Generations after the *Beit HaMikdash*, the Tannaim are debating what actually occurred when a woman drank the *mayim hamarim*. No doubt all parties could observe that sometimes a woman drank the water and nothing happened. The question is how to interpret that fact: does it mean she was genuinely innocent, or that her *zechut* suspended the judgment? The observable facts may have been shared; the dispute is about what those facts mean.

### Public Trust Problem

Rabbi Shimon's argument against *zechut tola* is primarily a policy argument. *Ve'im atah omer hazechut tola* — if you were to say that merit can suspend the effects of the waters, then *madhe ata et hamayim bifnei kol hanashim* — you undermine the waters' credibility in the eyes of all women. The *Sifrei* formulates this as *makhshikh gavno*, you cause it to become blurry, to lose its potency in the public's perception. The image is almost that of muddying the water itself. Two distinct harms follow. First, people will see a woman emerge unaffected and say: I know this woman committed adultery, and yet nothing happened — the water does not work. Second, the innocent women suffer: *motzi shem ra al hatehorot*. Women who were genuinely innocent and whose innocence was confirmed by the waters will now be suspected retroactively — people will say, *tme'iot hayu*, they were really guilty, *ela shetaltah lahen hazchut*, it is just that their merit suspended the punishment. For Rabbi Shimon, this double injury to the institution's integrity proves it cannot be so.

One might further note the practical problem raised in this connection: if *zechut tola* were operative, how would a husband ever know that he could resume marital relations with his wife? You might answer that he works with the *chazakah* — what has been observed provides a presumptive basis for conduct. But once you introduce the possibility of *zechut tola*, you have complicated that presumption considerably, and the question of when the husband and wife may resume their relationship becomes genuinely difficult.

### Rebbi: Zechut Works

Rebbi disagrees. *Hazechut tola bamayim hamererim* — merit does suspend the judgment of the bitter waters. But Rebbi's position is more nuanced than simply affirming *zechut tola* without qualification. He adds: *ve'einah yoledet ve'einah meshabbachat* — even when merit suspends the negative judgment, the positive effect of the waters is not realized either. The waters, had she been innocent, would have brought enhanced fertility — *ve'niketah ve'nizre'ah zara*. If she is guilty but her *zechut* intervenes, she escapes the physical destruction, but she does not receive the blessing. She does not bear children.

### Observable Signs

Rebbi's answer to Rabbi Shimon's concern is built on observability. If a woman drank the waters and afterward bore healthy children and her body flourished, people can see she was innocent. If a woman drank and received no blessing of children, people will say: the *pasuk* promises *ve'niketah ve'nizre'ah zara* — and she has not been *nizre'ah zara*. We can infer that her *zechut* must have been operative. Rebbi goes further still: *ela mitnavnevet ve'holekhet* — not only does she fail to bear children, but she actually deteriorates. And *sof hi meta be'otah mitah* — ultimately she dies from it, in the same recognizable way, only stretched over a longer period. So the public will see a clear spectrum: women who drank and flourished, whose innocence is confirmed, and women who drank and deteriorated, whose guilt is evident even if the timeline is extended. This, Rebbi argues, is a reasonable answer to Rabbi Shimon's concerns about the water's reputation. What is striking throughout this entire discussion is how the Tannaim construct the reality of what *must* have happened based on what the halacha and policy require — an argument from necessity rather than from direct observation.

## Mincha Tamei

### Before Sanctification

The *mishna* now transitions, somewhat abruptly, back to the *mincha* of the *sotah*. The assumed order of the *sotah* procedure is: the *mincha* is brought and offered, and then she drinks the water — *ve'achar yashkeh et hamayim*. The *mishna* doubles back, however, to address what happens if the *mincha* becomes *tamei* at different stages of the process. If the *mincha* became *tamei* before it was placed into the *kli sharet*, it is treated like any other *mincha* in that pre-sanctified state. At that stage it has only *kedushat peh* — a sanctity conferred through verbal designation — and such sanctity can be transferred: the *mincha* can be redeemed for money and a new *mincha* brought in its place.

### After Sanctification

Once the *mincha* has been sanctified by the *kli sharet*, however, it acquires *kedushat haguf* — an intrinsic, constitutive sanctity that defines its very identity rather than being merely instilled upon it. Once something attains *kedushat haguf*, it cannot be redeemed and cannot be removed from the *azarah*. If it subsequently becomes *tamei*, it must be burned within the *azarah*. This is not a law specific to the *minchat sotah*; it is the universal rule for all *menachot*. The *mishna* establishes this baseline in order to set up the more specific cases that follow.

## Mincha Burned

### Intro: Mincha Burned

Having established when an ordinary *mincha* must be burned on account of *tum'ah*, the *mishna* now introduces a related but distinct category: *ve'elu sheminchoteihem nisrafot* — the following women are those whose *mincha* is burned even though it has not become *tamei*. The *minchat sotah* has a set of unique circumstances under which the offering is rendered without purpose, and in those cases, since the *mincha* has already been sanctified in the vessel and cannot be redeemed, it must be burned. The fire is not on the *mizbe'ach* — it is on the ash pile within the *azarah*, where *pesulim* are burned.

### Confession

The first case: *ha'omeret tme'ah ani lach* — if the woman confesses her guilt. She is not subject to execution under these circumstances, since there were no witnesses and no formal warning, but her confession renders the entire *sotah* procedure moot. There is no longer any purpose for the *mincha*. Since it has been sanctified, it cannot be redeemed, so it is burned, and she is divorced without receiving her *kesubah*.

### Witnesses

The second case: witnesses come forward and testify to her guilt. Even without a formal confession, the existence of witness testimony eliminates the need for the ordeal. Again the *mincha* is burned, and she is divorced without her *kesubah*.

### Refuses to Drink

The third case: *ha'omeret eini shota* — she refuses to drink. This is a kind of *nolo contendere*: she neither admits guilt nor contests the charges, but she refuses to submit to the process. The result is that she is divorced without her *kesubah*, since she is the one obstructing the mechanism by which her innocence could be established. The husband remains in a state of prohibition relative to her, and since she is preventing resolution, she bears the consequence. The *mincha* is burned.

### Husband's Relations

The fourth case is distinctive in that the fault lies with the husband: *ve-she-ba'alah ba aleha baderech* — the husband had sexual relations with her after she became a *sotah* and before the ordeal was completed. The Torah conditions the efficacy of the waters on the husband being *nikah me'avon* — free from sin in this matter. If he is not, the waters will not work. Once it is known that this condition has been violated, there is no point in proceeding with the *mincha* or the ordeal, and the *mincha* is not brought. In this case, since it is the husband's conduct that has nullified the process, she would presumably receive her *kesubah* — though the *mishna* notes this as a matter to be examined further.

## Wife of Kohen

### Shared Ownership

The *mishna* now arrives at an entirely different category of *nisrafot*: *ve-chol hanesu'ot le-kohanim minchateihem nisrafot* — all women married to *kohanim* have their *mincha* burned. This burning is of a different character from the burning of the *pesul* cases above. The rule of *minchat kohen kalil tihyeh lo te'achel* — the *mincha* of a *kohen* must be entirely consumed, burned on the *mizbe'ach* — applies here. The question is why the wife of a *kohen* should have her *mincha* treated as a *minchat kohen*.

The answer is that the husband is considered a part-owner of the *mincha*. Rashi explains this in relation to the broader rule that a husband is obligated to pay for his wife's obligatory *korbanot* — a principle derived from the Gemara in Nazir. The husband foots the bill; she is the *mitkapeir*, the one for whom the atonement is being made and on whose behalf the offering is brought. Tosafot raises the question of whether this applies equally to a *minchat nedava*, a voluntary offering, since the financial obligation of the husband is specifically tied to obligatory *korbanot*. There, perhaps, he does not bear the cost even if, technically, *kinyan isha kana ba'alah* — the husband's acquisition of his wife entails some rights in her property. But for the *minchat sotah* specifically, the case is especially strong: *ve-hevi ha'ish et ishto* — the Torah frames the entire process as the husband bringing his wife. It says *ve-hevi et korbanah aleha* — he is the one who brings the *korban* on her behalf. More than any other case where the husband merely writes the check, here the Torah explicitly casts him as the active agent bringing the offering for her. Consequently, it is both her *mincha* and his *mincha*, and since it is his *mincha*, the rule of *minchat kohen* applies and the remainder may not be eaten.

### Rashi: Beis Hadeshen

Rashi, however, reads the *mishna*'s term *nisrafot* differently. In his commentary, Rashi states: *ve'afillu karav hakometz ke-hilchato ve-lo nitme'u nisrafim shirayim be-veit hadeshen* — even though the *kometz* was offered properly and nothing became *tamei*, the *shirayim* are burned in the *beis hadeshen*. On Rashi's reading, the burning of the remainder is not a burning on the *mizbe'ach* as a true *minchat kohen* would be, but rather a burning on the ash pile within the *azarah*, the same place where *pesulim* are burned. This anticipates the Gemara's discussion of two competing positions: is the remainder burned on the *mizbe'ach* as befits a genuine *minchat kohen*, or is it burned in the *beis hadeshen* because this *mincha* is a hybrid of two statuses? The *beis hadeshen*, it should be noted, is located within the *azarah* itself — it is not *chutz la-machaneh* — but it is distinct from the *mizbe'ach*.

## Gender Differences

### Kohenet Status

The *mishna* then pivots to a broader discussion, introduced by the contrast between the wife of a *kohen* and a *kohenet* — a woman who is herself a daughter of a *kohen* — who marries an Israelite. *Ve-hakohenet she-niset le-Yisrael minchatah ne'echelet* — her *mincha* is eaten in the normal fashion, because she carries no *kohen* status herself and her husband is not a *kohen*, so the *mincha* is treated as a standard *minchat Yisrael*. The *kometz* is burned on the *mizbe'ach* and the *shirayim* are eaten by the *kohanim*.

It is worth noting the terminology here. Throughout the Talmud, a woman who is a daughter of a *kohen* is almost always referred to as *bat kohen*. The usage of *kohenet* is relatively unusual. It is striking that a *mishna* which calls her *kohenet* proceeds to demonstrate that she has virtually no *kohen* status in practice. The identity of the tribe, particularly with respect to priestly lineage, flows through the male line. A woman's tribal identity is defined by the man she marries, not the family she was born into. This is reflected in law throughout: a *bat kohen* who marries an Israelite loses access to *terumah*; a woman who marries a *kohen* gains access to it. The discussion of the *mishna* is organized around a question: *mah bein kohen le-kohenet* — what is the difference between a *kohen* and a *kohenet*?

### Chalalah

The *mishna* states: *kohenet mitchalelet, ve-kohen eino mitchalel* — a *kohenet* can have her status compromised; a *kohen* cannot. The concept of *chalalah* refers to a woman whose priestly fitness has been violated, rendering her forbidden to a *kohen*. This status does not belong only to daughters of *kohanim* — any woman, whether *bat kohen* or *bat Yisrael*, who engages in certain categories of forbidden sexual acts becomes *pesulah le-kohen*. For example, an *almanah* who has relations with a *kohen gadol* becomes a *chalalah* and is forbidden even to a *kohen hedyot*. The act of sex is what determines her status; the precise scope of the husband's transgression — whether his violation requires *kiddushin* or obtains even without it — is a *machloket rishonim*, with the Rambam holding that the *kohen* only transgresses in the context of *kiddushin* while other *rishonim* disagree.

What is remarkable, and what the *mishna* draws attention to, is the asymmetry. A *kohen* who commits any sexual sin under the sun does not lose his *kehuna* status through his own action. His sanctity is not penetrable from without. He can be born a *chalal* — if he is the product of a forbidden union — but a born *kohen*'s status cannot be forfeited through his own conduct. There are *halachot* of social censure — a *kohen* currently married to a woman he is forbidden to marry is not permitted to *duchan*, for example — but these are social mechanisms of pressure, not a change in fundamental status. The disparity here mirrors a broader asymmetry: for a married woman, sexual infidelity constitutes adultery and violates her personal status; for a married man, sexual relations with an unmarried woman are not technically adultery in the same sense, and his personal status is not affected. The woman can be rendered *tamei* and forbidden; the man's status persists unchanged. This is reflected as well in the *kehuna* context: the woman's priestly fitness can be violated; the man's cannot.

### Tumah Differences

The *mishna* continues: *kohenet mitame'ah le-meitim, ve-ein kohen mitame'ah le-meitim* — a daughter of a *kohen* is permitted to become *tamei* through contact with the dead; a male *kohen* is not. The source for this distinction is *emor el ha-kohanim benei Aharon* — the prohibition of *tum'at meit* is addressed to *benei Aharon*, the sons of Aaron, *ve-lo benot Aharon* — not the daughters of Aaron. The *Tosefta* also notes the related case, conspicuous in its absence from the *mishna*, that a *kohen* serves on the *mizbe'ach* — *kohen omed u-makriv al gabei ha-mizbe'ach* — which plainly does not apply to a *kohenet*.

### Eating Offerings

The *mishna* concludes: *kohen ochel be-kodshei kodashim, ve-ein kohenet ochelet be-kodshei kodashim* — a *kohen* eats from the *kodshei kodashim* such as *chatat* and *asham* within the *azarah*, but a *kohenet* does not. The source is *kol zachar bi-vnei Aharon yochlenu* — all males among the sons of Aaron shall eat from it. The Torah is explicit.

### Summary: Kohenet

The irony of this entire *mishna* is profound. It designates the woman as *kohenet* — a term that implies an affirmative priestly identity — yet enumerates exclusively cases where she lacks any distinct status on account of her father being a *kohen*. Every item in the list demonstrates that she is, in terms of *halachic* identity, simply a woman, with no privileges and no restrictions that differ from those of an ordinary *Yisraelit*. Even the *chalalah* status, which might seem like a priestly restriction, applies to any Jewish woman. The *kohenet* of this *mishna* is, functionally, a *bat kohen* with no operative *kohen* status of her own.

## Hybrid Mincha

### The Problem

The Gemara now examines the central legal puzzle embedded in the *mishna*: what exactly happens to the *minchat sotah* of a woman married to a *kohen*? The Gemara frames it as follows. *Kol hanesu'ot li-khehunah minchatam nisrafet* — all women married to *kohanim* have their *mincha* burned, regardless of whether the woman is a *kohenet*, a *leviyah*, or an *Israelite*. The reason is *mipnei she-yesh lo chelek bah* — because the husband has a share in it. Insofar as it is his *mincha*, *lo te'achel* — it may not be eaten. But then: *ve-ein olah kalil, mipnei she-yesh lah chelek bah* — you cannot simply offer the whole thing on the *mizbe'ach* as a single unit, because she has a share in it. A true *minchat kohen* is offered whole, without the separation of *kmitzah*; but here, because the woman is not a *kohen*, you must take a *kmitzah*, as would be done for any *minchat Yisrael*. So the *mishna* resolves it: *ela ha-kometz karev be-atzmo ve-ha-shirayim kreivim be-atzmam* — the *kometz* is offered by itself, and the remainder is offered by itself. You perform the *kmitzah* in deference to its identity as a *minchat Yisrael*, and you burn the remainder in deference to its identity as a *minchat kohen*. The difficulty, however, is immediately apparent: once part of the offering has been placed on the *mizbe'ach* — namely the *kometz* — is it permitted to place the remainder on the *mizbe'ach* as well? The *pasuk* teaches: *ki kol se'or ve-chol devash lo taktiru mimenu isheh la-Hashem*, and the *drasha* from *mimenu* is that once part of a *korban* has been offered on the *mizbe'ach*, the remainder is forbidden to be placed there. This is a general principle: even the uneaten meat of a *korban* that is not an *olah* — the innards are offered on the *mizbe'ach*, but the remaining flesh that is designated for eating is forbidden to go up on the *mizbe'ach*.

### R. Eliezer: Eitzim

The Gemara answers in the name of Yehudah the son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi: *de-masik lehu le-shem eitzim* — you burn the remainder by designating it as firewood rather than as a *korban*. The *mizbe'ach* always requires fuel; you are not offering the remainder as a sacrifice but as combustible material to feed the fire. This is the position of Rabbi Eliezer, as found in a *baraita*: Rabbi Eliezer reads the phrase *lerei'ach nicho'ach* as a limiting qualifier. The prohibition is *lo ya'alu lerei'ach nicho'ach* — you may not offer it up as a pleasing fragrance, as a *korban*. But you may place it on the *mizbe'ach le-shem eitzim* — as firewood, which is not the same act as bringing a *korban*. The context for this *sevara* is a dispute about what to do when pieces of *korbanot* have become mixed together — say, limbs from an *olah* and limbs from a *chatat*, and it is no longer possible to determine which is which. According to Rabbi Eliezer, you may put all of them on the *mizbe'ach* and designate the *chatat* limbs as firewood; according to the Rabbis, you may not do this — you must allow them to become *pasul* and then burn them, passively, without putting them on the *mizbe'ach* at all.

### Tosafot's Question

Rashi, in his commentary on this passage, speaks of a *tenai* — a conditional designation — that one makes when burning the remainder: if the *kometz* was not truly a proper *kmitzah* and the whole thing should be treated as a *minchat kohen*, then this burning is valid as an offering; and if it is a *minchat Yisrael* and the *shirayim* are truly *shirayim*, then these are to be regarded as firewood. Tosafot objects to this framing. A *tenai* presupposes a *safek* — an uncertainty as to which of two possibilities obtains. By the legs of the mixed lambs, you genuinely do not know whether a given limb came from the *olah* or the *chatat*, and so a conditional designation makes sense. But here there is no uncertainty. This *mincha* is simultaneously a *minchat kohen* and a *minchat Yisrael* — both statuses coexist in it at the same time. It is not a question of which it is; it is both. The situation is analogous to an *androgynos* who is both male and female, or to a *chatzi eved ve-chatzi ben chorin* — the half-slave, half-free individual, familiar from discussions in *Minchat Chinuch*. You cannot make a *tenai* between two simultaneous realities. Tosafot's resolution is that you simply accept the best possible compromise: the remainder is burned, it is not eaten, and it is not offered as a proper *korban* — it occupies an intermediate status that satisfies neither identity fully. As the *shiur* puts it, a compromise is not when everyone is happy; a compromise is when everyone is equally unhappy.

One further question arises from this discussion: when burning *le-shem eitzim*, does the act require the same standards of *haktarah* that a proper offering does? Does it need to occur by day? Must there be proper *kavana*? Tosafot, citing the *Yerushalmi*, explores whether the requirement of intent for *haktarah* applies when the material is being burned as firewood rather than as a *korban*. The implicit answer seems to be that if you are genuinely burning it *le-shem eitzim*, the technical requirements of *haktarah* do not apply — it is not being brought as a *korban* in any formal sense.

### R. Elazar b.R. Shimon

But the Gemara has proceeded on the assumption of Rabbi Eliezer's position. The Rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Eliezer — who hold that you cannot designate the mixed limbs as firewood — would be unable to use that solution here. The Gemara therefore pivots to the ruling of Rabbi Elazar beRebbi Shimon, found in a *baraita*: *ha-kometz karev le-atzmo ve-ha-shirayim mitpazrin al beit ha-deshen* — the *kometz* is offered on the *mizbe'ach* on its own, and the remainder is scattered on the *beis hadeshen*. This is precisely the reading Rashi adopted for the *mishna*.

The case Rabbi Elazar beRebbi Shimon was originally addressing is a third scenario: a *kohen* who brings a *minchat chotei* — the meal offering that serves as the poverty-tier substitute in an *oleh ve-yored* obligation. When the Torah describes this offering it says *ve-hayeta la-kohen ke-minchah* — it shall be for the *kohen* like a *mincha*. The question is: like which *mincha*? Rabbi Elazar beRebbi Shimon argues that the word *ke-minchah* — like a *mincha*, not like *his* *mincha* — means it is to be treated like a standard *minchat Yisrael* in one respect: a *kmitzah* is taken. The word is *ke-minchah*, not *ke-minchat kohen*. However, since it is still a *minchat kohen* in some sense, the *shirayim* cannot be eaten. So he reaches the same intermediate resolution: *kmitzah* is taken, the *kometz* is offered, and the remainder cannot be eaten and cannot be properly offered, so it is burned in the *beis hadeshen*. The Rabbis, by contrast, hold that *ke-minchah* means it is fully like a *minchat kohen* and the whole thing is burned on the *mizbe'ach*. Their disagreement with Rabbi Elazar beRebbi Shimon is specifically about this case, which they regard as a straightforward *minchat kohen*, not a hybrid.

### Rabbanan Agree

The Gemara then makes a crucial concession: *ve-afillu Rabbanan lo paligi alei de-Rabbi Elazar beRebbi Shimon ella be-Minchat Chotei shel Kohanim de-vat akrava hi* — even the Rabbis only disagree with Rabbi Elazar beRebbi Shimon in the case of the *minchat chotei* of a *kohen*, which they regard as a genuine *minchat kohen* that should be fully offered. But in the case of the wife of a *kohen*, where the *mincha* is genuinely a hybrid — simultaneously carrying the status of a *minchat kohen* and a *minchat Yisrael* — *afillu Rabbanan modu* — even the Rabbis concede that you cannot eat the *shirayim* and cannot properly offer them on the *mizbe'ach*. You take the *kmitzah*, you offer it, and the remainder is burned in the *beis hadeshen*. This is the position that Rashi had already adopted in the *mishna*, and which now receives its Talmudic grounding.

## Gender Differences (II)

### General M/F Diffs

The *mishna* transitions again, now moving from the specific *kohen*/*kohenet* dichotomy to a general enumeration of differences between men and women across a range of *halachic* contexts. The Gemara supplies the *pesukim* underlying each distinction.

### Metzora Hair

The *mishna* states: *ha'ish poreia u-forem* — a man who is a *metzora* must let his hair grow wild and rend his garments. A woman does not. The Gemara derives this from the *pasuk*. The *Torah* begins the laws of *tzara'at* with *ish ish ki yihyeh be-or besaro* — it opens with *ish*, man. One might have thought that women are entirely exempt from *tzara'at* laws. The *pasuk* then says *ve-ha-tzarua asher bo ha-nega* — using a general term for the *metzora* without specifying gender — to teach that a woman too can be a *metzora* and is subject to *tum'at tzara'at*. The *hava amina* from *ish* is not that women are exempt from *tum'ah*, but that some of the active behavioral obligations incumbent upon a *metzora* — letting the hair grow wild, rending the garments — apply specifically to men by virtue of that initial *ish*.

### Nazirite Vow

*Ha'ish madir et beno be-Nazir* — a man can make his son a *Nazir*, even without the son's consent or explicit agreement, *ve-ein ha-ishah maderet et benah be-Nazir* — but a woman cannot impose a *nazirite* vow on her son. The Gemara cites Rabbi Yochanan: *halacha hi be-Nazir* — this is a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* with respect to the laws of *nezirut*. Reish Lakish had offered a different rationale, rooting the distinction in the laws of *chinuch* — the obligation to educate a child in *mitzvot* — and holding that *chinuch* falls specifically on fathers with respect to sons but not on mothers with respect to daughters. But that was Reish Lakish's reading; the Gemara here follows Rabbi Yochanan and grounds the law in *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*.

### Nazirite Inheritance

*Ha'ish megalei'ach al nezirut aviv* — a man who himself became a *Nazir* after his father was a *Nazir* may, under certain circumstances, make use of his father's remaining *korbanot* for his own *nezirut*, *ve-ein ha-ishah megale'ichat al nezirut aviha* — but this does not apply to a woman. Again Rabbi Yochanan: *halacha hi be-Nazir* — this too is *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*.

### Father Marries Daughter

*Ha'ish mekadesh et bito* — a father can arrange the *kiddushin* of his daughter, giving her in marriage, *ve-ein ha-ishah mekadeshet et bitah* — a mother cannot. The source is the *pasuk*: *et bitti natati la'ish ha-zeh* — "I gave my daughter to this man" — the father speaking. This right belongs to the father *de-oraita*; *de-rabbanan* a mechanism was created for the mother as well, but the Biblical institution is paternal.

### Sale to Slavery

*Ha'ish mocher et bito* — a father can sell his daughter as a slave in certain circumstances of poverty; *ve-ein ha-ishah mocheret et bitah* — a mother cannot. The source is *ki yimkor ish et bito* — the Torah specifies *ish*, the father.

### Stoning Execution

*Ha'ish niskal arom* — a man who is stoned is stoned while naked; *ve-ein ha-ishah niskelet arumah* — a woman is not. The Gemara derives this from *ve-ragmu oto kol ha-edah* — they shall stone him, and *oto* is taken to mean him in his own state, without his garments. The Gemara preempts the possible misreading: *oto* cannot mean to exclude women from capital punishment altogether, since the Torah elsewhere explicitly says *ve-hotzeita et ha-ish ha-hu o et ha-ishah ha-hi el she'arecha* — you shall bring out that man or that woman to your gates, and execute them. Rather, *oto* teaches *oso be-lo chesuto ve-lo osah be-lo chesusah* — him without his clothes, but not her without hers.

### Hanging

*Ha'ish nitlah* — after execution by stoning, a man's body is hung; *ve-ein ha-ishah nitlit* — not a woman. The source is *ve-talita oto al ha-eitz* — and you shall hang him, *oto ve-lo otah*. This raises the perennial question in the *Torah*'s legislative language: since the default gender of Hebrew is masculine, when does the masculine pronoun limit the law to men, and when is it inclusive? The *gemara* consistently addresses this on a case-by-case basis, and the masculine genitive is not always read as exclusionary. In these cases, it is.

### Sale for Theft

*Ha'ish nimkar bi-gnivato ve-ein ishah nimkeret bi-gneivatah* — a man who steals and cannot repay is sold into slavery by *beit din*, but a woman is not. The *pasuk* says *ve-nimkar bi-gnivato* — the word *bi-gnivato*, using the masculine possessive, is the basis: *bi-gnivato ve-lo bi-gneivatah* — for his theft, not for her theft. The *beit din* has no authority to sell a woman in satisfaction of her theft debt.

The *Tosefta* in chapter 2, *halacha* 8, supplements this list considerably: a *kohen* serves on the *mizbe'ach*, which a *kohenet* does not; the father retains rights to his minor daughter's found objects, her earnings, and the power to annul her vows; a man is obligated in *mitzvot asei she-ha-zman geramah* while a woman is not; a man transgresses *bal takif* and *bal tashchit* — shaving the sidelocks and beard — and the prohibition of *tum'at meitim* for a *kohen*; only a boy can be a *ben sorer u-moreh*; a man can be sold into slavery more than once, and a woman cannot be; a man undergoes *retziah* — ear-piercing to mark perpetual slavery — and a woman does not; and various other distinctions related to the laws of *eved ivri*. The *mishna*'s list, while substantial, is thus only a selection from a fuller catalogue of male-female *halachic* differences.
