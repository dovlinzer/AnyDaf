# Gittin 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shlichus & Na'arah

### Father as Shaliach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לִשְׁמוֹר אֶת גִּיטָּהּ, אֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לְהִתְגָּרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** there is another principle: **Any** female **who is unable to safeguard her bill of divorce is unable to be divorced.**

The daf begins in the middle of 64b with the phrase *v'chol she'eina yachola lishmor gita* — any girl who cannot watch her own *get* cannot be divorced. To situate this discussion, we must recall the context: the Gemara is exploring the concept of *shlichus*, specifically the automatic or permanent *shaliach* that the Torah assigns to a father to represent his minor daughter. The Mishnah addresses the case of a *na'arah me'orasah* — a girl between twelve and twelve-and-a-half years old who is *me'orasah* but not yet *nisuah*. Since she has not yet begun living with her husband, she has not been emancipated from her father's authority. In this intermediate stage, she can receive her own *get* and represent herself, and simultaneously her father can receive the *get* on her behalf, given that he retains legal control over her and is entitled to her benefits during this period.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קְטַנָּה הַיּוֹדַעַת לִשְׁמוֹר אֶת גִּיטָּהּ – מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת, וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ יוֹדַעַת לִשְׁמוֹר אֶת גִּיטָּהּ – אֵינָהּ מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת. וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא קְטַנָּה יוֹדַעַת לִשְׁמוֹר אֶת גִּיטָּהּ? כֹּל שֶׁמְּשַׁמֶּרֶת גִּיטָּהּ וְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A minor girl who knows** how **to safeguard her bill of divorce can be divorced, and** one **who does not know** how **to safeguard her bill of divorce cannot be divorced. And which is** the **minor girl** who **knows** how **to safeguard her bill of divorce?** It is **any** minor girl **who safeguards her bill of divorce and something else.**

When the girl is a *ketanah* — a minor below the age of twelve — Rashi holds that only her father may receive the *get* on her behalf. Tosafot disagree sharply: even as a *ketanah*, the *get* may always be given to the girl herself. Since she is a passive recipient in the transaction, no *da'as* on her part is required. She always represents herself in this sense.

The practical debate between Rashi and Tosafot becomes sharpest when we examine the Mishnah's statement about *v'chol she'eina yachola lishmor gita*. Rashi reads this as a requirement of status: even if the *get* is given to the father, if the girl herself is so young that she would not understand that she has been divorced, the divorce is ineffective. For Rashi, it is not merely about the physical act of giving the *get* — there must be some comprehension on the part of the girl that she has achieved the status of being divorced.

### Understanding Get

Tosafot's reading is more compelling and will guide the analysis going forward. For Tosafot, *v'chol she'eina yachola lishmor gita* addresses solely the act of giving the *get* to the girl herself: if she does not understand what she has received, the act of delivery to her is meaningless. But if the *get* is given to the father, even a one-day-old infant can be divorced — just as a one-day-old infant can be married through her father's agency. If a girl can acquire the status of marriage at any age through her father, she can equivalently acquire the status of divorce through her father. The only constraint is whether giving the *get* to the girl herself constitutes a valid act of reception. Tosafot thus explains the Mishnah as speaking about cases where the *get* is delivered directly to the girl.

Under this reading, the phrase emphasizes whether she understands the physical object she has been given and appreciates its significance. It is not about the status of being divorced — that can happen regardless — it is about whether giving the *get* to her constitutes a legally meaningful delivery.

### Ketanah Yoda'at

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הָכִי קָאָמַר: כֹּל שֶׁמְּשַׁמֶּרֶת דָּבָר אַחֵר מֵחֲמַת גִּיטָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *tanna* **saying** in the statement: Who safeguards her bill of divorce and something else? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **this is** what **he is saying: Any** minor **who safeguards something else due to her bill of divorce,** i.e., she lost her bill of divorce itself but she guards something else that she mistook for her bill of divorce.

Rabbi Yochanan clarifies this cryptic phrase: *kol she'meshameres davar acher machmat gita* — she watches something else on account of her *get*. Rashi explains that even if she loses her *get*, she has held onto another object in its place — a book, for instance — because she understood the *get* was something important. She grasps that it is a significant item even if she substitutes another object for it.

### Distinguishing Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ: הָא שׁוֹטָה בְּעָלְמָא הִיא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: כֹּל שֶׁמַּבְחֶנֶת בֵּין גִּיטָּהּ לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ objects to this: That** minor girl **is merely an imbecile,** as she keeps something else in lieu of her bill of divorce. **Rather, Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ says in the name of Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika:** The reference is to **any** girl **who distinguishes between her bill of divorce and something else,** and safeguards her bill of divorce and does not safeguard less significant items.

Rav Huna bar Manoach rejects Rashi's reading forcefully: *ha shotah b'alma hi* — such a girl is essentially an imbecile! If she confuses a book with her *get* merely because she knows the *get* is important, that is hardly a relevant standard of knowledge. Accordingly, Rav Huna bar Manoach, citing Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika, reverses the reading: *kol she'mavchenes bein gita v'davar acher* — the girl who understands the *difference* between her *get* and something else. This means that if someone says to her, "Give me the *get*, I'll give you a different piece of paper in exchange," she refuses, insisting that her *get* is important and she must hold onto it specifically. It is not merely that she can watch the *get* as a physical object; she understands its unique importance and will not accept a substitute. This is the level of knowledge required for a valid delivery of the *get* to her directly.

## Minor Development

### Tzror & Egoz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: צְרוֹר וְזוֹרְקוֹ, אֱגוֹז וְנוֹטְלוֹ – זוֹכֶה לְעַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין זוֹכֶה לַאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos minors, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rabbi Asi says:** If one gives a child **a pebble and he throws it** away and one gives him **a nut and he takes it,** he is capable of distinguishing between items of value and worthless items, and **he acquires** property **for himself but does not acquire** property **on** behalf of **others.**

Having established that intellectual development is a determining factor in whether a *get* given to a minor constitutes a valid act, the Gemara transitions to a broader discussion of levels of intellectual development in minors as they pertain to transactions and acquisitions generally. The first stage is described by the phrase *tzror v'zorko egoz v'notlo*: if a child finds a pebble and throws it away, but finds a nut and keeps it, the child has demonstrated an understanding of differential value. The pebble is worthless to him; the nut is something he wants to hold. He recognizes that not all objects are equivalent.

### Self vs. Others

At this first stage, the Gemara rules: *zocheh l'atzmo v'ein zocheh l'acherim* — the minor can acquire for himself but not on behalf of others. Rabbinically, once a child understands the difference between a pebble and a nut — a fairly young age — he can take possession of an object given to him. This is a rabbinic institution; a minor cannot take *de'oraita* possession until adulthood. Nevertheless, the Rabbis recognized this capacity and validated it. The rationale is that when someone gives the child an object, the adult is supplying the necessary *da'as* — the requisite intent to transfer ownership. The child need not himself be the fully competent party who initiates the transaction. He merely needs to do something that physically constitutes a meaningful act of taking — holding the object with some sense of what is occurring. The giver is the one effectuating the transfer. By analogy to *get*: the husband is the one effecting the divorce, but the recipient still needs to perform something that constitutes a meaningful reception.

At this stage, however, the minor cannot take possession on behalf of someone else. That requires a substantially higher level of *da'as*.

### Da'as Acheret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֵפֶץ – וּמַחְזִירוֹ לְאַחַר שָׁעָה, זוֹכֶה בֵּין לְעַצְמוֹ וּבֵין לַאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** If the child develops to the extent that he is given **an item and he returns it** to its owners **later,** because he understands the concept of ownership, **he acquires** property **both for himself and on** behalf of **others.**

The case of *da'as acheret makneh oso* — another party providing the requisite intent — is relevant here. When you give the minor an object, you are the source of *da'as*. But for the minor to acquire on behalf of a third party, the minor would need to function as a conduit through which your intent reaches that third party. The minor would need to hold not only his own understanding but to represent another's interests — something beyond his current capacity.

## Minor Development (II)

### Object Permanence

The Gemara introduces a second and more advanced stage: *chefetz u'machziro l'achar sha'ah* — if you give the minor an object and an hour later ask him to return it, and he can retrieve it for you. This reflects something akin to object permanence: the child retains a mental representation of the object beyond the immediate present moment. He does not forget about it; he can locate it after an interval of time. This suggests a more developed cognitive capacity than the simple immediate recognition of the first stage.

At this stage, the Gemara rules: *zocheh bein l'atzmo bein l'acherim* — the minor can acquire both for himself and on behalf of others. Because the child has demonstrated an understanding that extends beyond the here-and-now, there is enough comprehension to serve as a vehicle through which another person takes possession when that other person is supplying the *da'as*.

### De'oraita Status

The question arises whether this capacity operates on a *de'oraita* level. Tosafot raises the issue: since it is the adult providing the *da'as* — similar to the *get* scenario, where the husband is the one effecting the divorce — perhaps even these rabbinic capacities of the minor could function on a *de'oraita* level. In general, however, the assumption is that all of this operates rabbinically. This is debated among the Rishonim. As will be seen, the Gemara's subsequent discussion will illuminate what the default assumption is and why.

## Rava's Gimmel Midot

### Stage Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לִי: דָּא וְדָא אַחַת הִיא. מַאי ״דָּא וְדָא אַחַת הִיא״? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה – זוֹכֶה לְעַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין זוֹכֶה לַאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda continued. **When I stated** Rabbi Asi's ruling **before Shmuel he said to me: This and that are one.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **This and that are one? Rav Ḥisda says:** With regard to **both this** child **and that** child, each **acquires** property **for himself but does not acquire** property **on** behalf of **others,** and there is no distinction in this regard between the developmental stages of a minor.

The Gemara records that when this analysis was presented before Shmuel, he responded: *da veda achat hi* — this and that are the same *halacha*. Rav Chisda explains: *echad zeh ve'echad zeh zocheh l'atzmo ve'ein zocheh l'acherim* — in both the first stage and the second stage, the minor can only acquire for himself; neither stage permits acquiring on behalf of others. Even the capacity to return an object after an interval does not elevate the minor to the status where his taking possession on behalf of a third party is meaningful, according to Shmuel as understood by Rav Chisda.

## Possession & Shituf

### Rav Chanina Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב חִינָּנָא וַורְדָּאן: כֵּיצַד מִשְׁתַּתְּפִין בְּמָבוֹי? מַנִּיחַ אֶת הֶחָבִית, וְאוֹמֵר: ״הֲרֵי זוֹ לְכׇל בְּנֵי הַמָּבוֹי״, וּמְזַכֶּה לָהֶם עַל יְדֵי בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים, וְעַל יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הָעִבְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥinnana of Vardania raises an objection** based on what was taught in a mishna (*Eiruvin* 79b): **How does one merge** the courtyards that open **into the alleyway** on behalf of all the residents of the alleyway? **He places a barrel** filled with his wine **and says: This is for all the residents of the alleyway. And he** may **confer possession** of the wine **to** the other residents of the alleyway **by means of his adult son or daughter, or by means of his Hebrew slave or maidservant.**

Rav Chanina of Vardan challenges this ruling from a *baraita* concerning *shituf mavoi* — the joint acquisition used to create an *eiruv* for a shared alleyway.

### Shituf Mavo'ot

The *baraita* asks: *keitzad mishtatfin bemavoi* — how does one perform *shituf* of a *mavoi*? The answer is that one takes a barrel of wine (or another food), declares it to be for all the residents of the *mavoi*, and transfers possession of it to everyone through an intermediary. Specifically, the *baraita* lists the valid intermediaries: through one's adult son or daughter (*bno u'vito hagedolim*), or through one's Hebrew male or female slave (*avdo v'shifchato ha'ivrim*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי שִׁפְחָה – הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאַתְיָא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, מַאי בָּעֲיָא גַּבֵּיהּ? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלָא אַתְיָא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, וְקָתָנֵי זוֹכָה לַאֲחֵרִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **this maidservant? If she developed two** pubic **hairs,** indicating that she reached majority, **what** is she **doing with** the owner of the barrel? A Hebrew maidservant is emancipated when she reaches puberty. **Rather, is** the reference here **not** to a case **where she did not** yet **develop two** pubic **hairs,** and she is a minor, **and it is taught** that she can **acquire on** behalf of **others?** Apparently, a minor can acquire property on behalf of other people.

The Gemara immediately questions the case of the Hebrew female servant: *hai shifcha heichi dami* — what is the case of this maidservant? If she has already developed *shtei se'arot* — the two pubic hairs that mark puberty, typically around age twelve — she should already have been freed, since that is when a *shifchah ivriyah* goes free. It must therefore be that she does not yet have those signs of maturity — meaning she is a minor. Yet the *baraita* states that she can take possession on behalf of all the residents of the *mavoi*. This directly contradicts the position that a minor cannot acquire on behalf of others, even at the more advanced stage of *chefetz u'machziro*.

This challenge applies against Shmuel's understanding that *da veda achat hi* — that both stages only permit acquisition for oneself. Here is a minor girl taking possession for others.

### Derabbanan Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי שִׁיתּוּפֵי מְבוֹאוֹת – דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: The **merging of alleyways is different,** as it is an ordinance **by rabbinic law.** The Sages ruled leniently with regard to this rabbinic ordinance and allowed a minor to acquire property on behalf of others.

The Gemara answers on behalf of Rav Chisda: *shani shitufei mavo'os derabbanan* — *shituf mavoi* is a rabbinic institution, and therefore the rules that apply to it need not mirror the *de'oraita* rules for acquisition. Because it is entirely a rabbinic creation, the Rabbis may permit a minor to serve as the acquiring agent even for others, in a context where the underlying institution itself has no *de'oraita* analog.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אִישְׁתִּיק וַורְדָּאן. מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר? כֹּל דְּתַקּוּן רַבָּנַן – כְּעֵין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא תַּקּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** Rav Ḥinnana of **Vardonia was silent** and had no response. The Gemara asks: **What could he have said** in response? The Gemara answers that he could have responded: **All** ordinances **that the Sages instituted,** **they instituted parallel to Torah law,** and they did not innovate novel halakhic models.

Rav Chisda reports that Rav Chanina of Vardan was silenced — *ishtik Vardan* — by this response. Yet the Gemara wonders what he should have said, implying his original question had force. His response could have been: *kol detikkun rabbanan ke'ein d'oraisa tikkun* — everything the Rabbis enacted is patterned after *de'oraita* law. If the Rabbis permitted it in *shituf mavoi*, it is because in general minors can acquire for others, and the Rabbis were following that principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ – כִּי אָמְרִינַן: כָּל דְּתַקּוּן רַבָּנַן כְּעֵין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא תַּקּוּן – בְּמִילְּתָא דְּאִית לַהּ עִיקָּר מִן הַתּוֹרָה, אֲבָל מִילְּתָא דְּלֵית לַהּ עִיקָּר מִן הַתּוֹרָה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, Rav Ḥinnana of Vardonia, why was he silent? He holds that **when we say: All** ordinances **that the Sages instituted, they instituted parallel to Torah law,** it is **with regard to a matter that is rooted in the Torah,** and upon which the Sages instituted an ordinance. **However, with regard to a matter that is not rooted in the Torah,** e.g., the *halakhot* of joining courtyards and merging alleyways, **no,** they did not institute the ordinances parallel to Torah law.

The Gemara then articulates the counter-response that would defeat this rejoinder: *ke'ein d'oraisa tikkun* applies only when the rabbinic institution has a *de'oraita* root — *bemilta de'is lah ikar min haTorah*. When a rabbinic institution is built from whole cloth — *milta deleis lah ikar min haTorah* — as *shituf mavo'os* is, we cannot infer from it that the Rabbis were tracking a *de'oraita* principle. The case of, say, chicken and milk being rabbinic is modeled after the *de'oraita* prohibition of meat and milk; one can infer from the rabbinic extension that it tracks the original. But *shituf mavo'os* has no *de'oraita* analog at all, so we cannot use it as proof for *de'oraita* capacity.

Crucially, this entire exchange implies something significant: the premise throughout the Gemara seems to be that when an adult provides the *da'as* and gives an object to a minor, the minor can take possession even on a *de'oraita* level. The dispute is specifically about whether the minor can then serve as an agent for a third party. This point is not as obvious as it might appear, and it is noted as being debated among the Rishonim.

### Ma'aser Pidyon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב אַוְיָא: מַעֲרִימִין עַל מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי. כֵּיצַד? אוֹמֵר אָדָם לִבְנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ הַגְּדוֹלִים; לְעַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הָעִבְרִים: ״הֵא לָכֶם מָעוֹת הַלָּלוּ, וּפְדוּ בָּהֶן מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי זֶה״, וְאוֹכְלוֹ בְּלֹא חוֹמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Avya raises** another **objection** to Shmuel's opinion, according to Rav Ḥisda's explanations, that a minor cannot acquire property on behalf of others, based on a mishna in tractate *Ma'aser Sheni* (4:4): **One may employ artifice** to exempt himself from the obligation to add one-fifth to the sum when redeeming **second tithe,** which the owner of the tithe is required to add. **How so? A person says to his adult son or daughter,** or **to his Hebrew slave or his maidservant: Here you are,** take **money and redeem second tithe with it.** After they redeem the second tithe, they give it to their father or master **and he eats it without** adding **one-fifth.**

The Gemara introduces a second challenge, this time concerning *ma'aser sheni*. The context involves a practice the Gemara terms *ma'arimin al ma'aser sheni* — "scheming" with respect to *ma'aser sheni*. When a person redeems his own *ma'aser sheni* onto coins, he is required to add a *chomesh* — the additional fifth (which in practice equals 25%, since the additional quarter becomes a fifth of the total). This obligation to add a *chomesh* applies only when the owner himself redeems his own *ma'aser sheni*. If, however, a different person redeems it — using his own money — no *chomesh* is required.

The *baraita* describes the permitted maneuver: one says to his adult son or daughter, or to his Hebrew male or female slave, *hei lachem ma'ot halalu* — here, I am giving you this money as a gift. It is now yours. You then use it to redeem my *ma'aser sheni*. Since they are now the owners of the money, and they are the ones performing the redemption, no *chomesh* is required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי שִׁפְחָה הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאַתְיָא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, מַאי בָּעֲיָא גַּבֵּיהּ? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלָא אַתְיָא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, בְּמַעֲשֵׂר בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה – דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **this maidservant? If she developed two** pubic **hairs,** indicating that she reached majority, **what** is she **doing with** the owner of the produce? A Hebrew maidservant is emancipated when she reaches puberty. **Rather, is** the reference here **not** to a case **where she did not** yet **develop two** pubic **hairs?** Apparently, a minor can also acquire property on behalf of others. The Gemara rejects this proof: **With what are we dealing here?** It is **with tithes today,** which is in effect **by rabbinic law,** and the Sages ruled leniently in matters of rabbinic law.

The Gemara again asks: *hai shifcha heichi dami* — what is the case of this Hebrew maidservant? If she has already reached adulthood, she is no longer a *shifchah*. She must therefore be a minor. And yet here she is performing an act of *pidyon ma'aser sheni* — redeeming *ma'aser sheni* — which is not merely an act of taking possession on behalf of others; it is an independent legal act. This is an even stronger challenge.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה מִי אִיכָּא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין עֶבֶד עִבְרִי נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַיּוֹבֵל נוֹהֵג! אֶלָּא בְּעָצִיץ שֶׁאֵינוֹ נָקוּב – דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is there a Hebrew maidservant today? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The provision of **a Hebrew slave is in practice only during a period when the Jubilee** Year **is in practice.** Therefore, there have been no Hebrew slaves or maidservants since observance of the Jubilee Year ceased, before the destruction of the First Temple. **Rather,** it must be that the mishna is referring to a case where the produce grew **in an unperforated pot,** which one is obligated to tithe **by rabbinic law.**

Tosafot raises a pointed difficulty: in what sense does any analogy to acquisition on behalf of others — *zocheh l'acherim* — apply here? When we discussed acquiring for others, the model was that an adult provides the *da'as* and the minor serves as a conduit. But here, the minor herself is the active party. She owns the money (which was given to her) and she herself is performing the *pidyon*. There is no *da'as acheret makneh* at work — no external adult is supplying the intent. She is fully the agent of the transaction. Moreover, Tosafot notes, in general one requires adult status to perform *pidyon ma'aser sheni*. How, then, can a minor do it at all?

### Hafla'ah Theory

Tosafot resolves this through the principle of *mufla samuch l'ish* — the halacha that in the year before a minor reaches *bar mitzvah* or *bat mitzvah* (a twelve-year-old girl or a thirteen-year-old boy), his or her verbal declarations that change status — *nedarim* and *hekdesh* — take effect. This is a *de'oraita* principle. Now, how do we characterize *pidyon ma'aser sheni*? Is it a transaction — a *kinyan* — in which the owner of the *ma'aser sheni* acquires the coins in exchange for the food? Or is it more like *hafla'ah* — a verbal declaration that transfers a status? One might argue that the minor is not "buying" anything; she is merely transferring the *kedusha* from the fruit to the coin. It is not a *kinyan* of an object she did not previously own; it is a transfer of a sanctified status from one substrate to another. If we view *pidyon* as analogous to *hekdesh*, then just as a minor in the year before *bar/bat mitzvah* can consecrate objects through verbal declaration, she might also be able to transfer the sanctity of *ma'aser sheni* from fruit to coin.

The connection to the broader discussion of minors acquiring on behalf of others remains somewhat unclear even according to Tosafot — but the Gemara plainly treats the case as relevant.

The Gemara's response to the challenge is, once again: *hacha bemai askinan b'ma'aser b'zman hazeh d'rabbanan* — this is discussing *ma'aser sheni* in our era, which is only rabbinically obligatory. And the institution of the Hebrew maidservant (*amah ivriyah*) no longer applies at all in the present day, since *ein eved ivri noheg ella b'zman she'hayovel noheg* — the entire institution of Hebrew slaves operates only when *Yovel* is observed, and *Yovel* requires the Jewish people to be settled in the Land. So neither of these cases establishes that a minor can perform such acts when *de'oraita* stakes are involved. The Gemara thus successfully brackets each challenge as involving only rabbinic law, leaving open the question of the *de'oraita* position.

## Rava's Gimmel Midot (II)

### Stage One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, שָׁלֹשׁ מִדּוֹת בְּקָטָן: צְרוֹר וְזוֹרְקוֹ, אֱגוֹז וְנוֹטְלוֹ – זוֹכֶה לְעַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין זוֹכֶה לַאֲחֵרִים; וּכְנֶגְדָּן בִּקְטַנָּה – מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת לְמֵיאוּן.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the capacity of minors to acquire property, **Rava says** that there are **three stages in** the development of **a minor:** With regard to a minor who is given **a pebble and he throws it** away but when given **a nut he takes it, he acquires** property **for himself but does not acquire** property **on** behalf of **others. And with regard to a minor girl with the corresponding** stage of intellectual development, after the death of her father she can be **betrothed** by her mother and her brother by rabbinic law, and can opt out of that betrothal **through refusal.**

With the preceding analyses as background, the Gemara now presents Rava's famous tripartite summary: *amar Rava gimmel middos b'katan* — Rava said there are three stages in the development of a minor's legal capacities. The first corresponds to *tzror v'zorko egoz v'notlo*: once a child recognizes that a pebble is worthless but a nut has value, he can take possession for himself — *zocheh l'atzmo v'ein zocheh l'acherim*.

### Girl's Stage One

Rava's formulation then turns to the female parallel: *v'knegedan b'ktana* — the corresponding stage for a girl. The male stage involves a child's recognition of differential value among objects. For a girl, the corresponding legal institution is *miskadeshes l'miyun* — she can be betrothed for the purpose of *miyun*. This requires explanation. By Torah law, only a father may betroth his minor daughter; if the father is deceased, the Torah provides no mechanism. The Rabbis enacted that when there is no father, the mother and brothers may arrange the girl's betrothal. Because this is a rabbinic institution, the girl may subsequently annul it through *miyun* — an informal verbal rejection, without the need for a *get*. At what age can this take effect? The girl must be old enough that if she were given the *kesef kiddushin*, she would understand that she received something of value. Rashi notes this is the capacity to preserve *kiddushin* the same way she preserves any other object of value — *kol tinokes she'eina yechola lishmor kiddusheha* — if she cannot protect the money of her betrothal, the betrothal enacted by the mother and brothers is not operative even rabbinically. Once she can, it is effective, and since it is only rabbinic, she retains the right of *miyun* to walk away.

Rashi goes further and says that at this stage, not only can the mother and brothers betroth her — if she is a complete orphan, she herself can accept the *kiddushin*. This is a remarkable position, though understandable in light of the desperate social realities that Tosafot elsewhere describes: the frequency of death and tragedy in medieval life was such that children required protection through betrothal arrangements as early as possible.

### Pautot Age

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַפָּעוֹטוֹת – מִקָּחָן מִקָּח וּמִמְכָּרָן מִמְכָּר בְּמִטַּלְטְלִין; וּכְנֶגְדָּן בִּקְטַנָּה – מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת בְּקִידּוּשֵׁי אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** At the next stage of development are young **children** aged approximately six through eight, whose **purchase is a purchase and whose sale is a sale, with regard to movable property. And with regard to a minor girl with the corresponding** stage of development, **she is divorced** by receipt of her bill of divorce, even if it is **from betrothal by her father,** which is by Torah law.

The second stage in Rava's scheme corresponds to *hapautot* — the age range of roughly six to nine years old — at which Rabbinic law permits minors to engage in buying and selling of movable goods (*mikach u'mimkar b'mitaltelin*, not land). The Gemara says this is permitted *kedei chayeihem* — to enable them to manage basic daily life. For a girl, the corresponding legal capacity is *miskadeshes b'kiddushei aviha* — she is old enough that if her father betroth her, it is a fully valid *de'oraita* betrothal. And crucially, this is the age that is the threshold for *yoda'at lishmor et gita* — knowing how to watch her *get*. The distinction here is illuminating: *yoda'at lishmor et kiddusheha* — knowing how to watch money of *kiddushin* — requires only that she recognizes physical value. But *yoda'at lishmor et gita* requires that she understand the *get* as an abstract symbol of a changed legal status. The *get* does not have intrinsic monetary value; its significance is conceptual. Therefore, understanding a *get* requires a more developed level of cognition than understanding a coin. The *pautot* age is when this higher threshold is reached.

### Stage Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעוּ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים – נִדְרֵיהֶן נֶדֶר וְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן הֶקְדֵּשׁ; וּכְנֶגְדָּן בִּקְטַנָּה – חוֹלֶצֶת. וְלִמְכּוֹר בְּנִכְסֵי אָבִיו – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The third stage of development is when **they have reached the age of vows,** when **their vows are** valid **vows and their consecration is** valid **consecration. And with regard to a minor girl with the corresponding** stage of development, **she performs *ḥalitza*** to free herself from her levirate bond. **And** with regard **to selling his father's** landed **property,** a minor cannot sell it **until he will** reach the **age of twenty.**

The third stage is *higi'u l'onas nedarim* — when minors reach the year before *bar* or *bat mitzvah* (a twelve-year-old girl, a thirteen-year-old boy). At this point, *nidra neder hekdesha hekdesh* — their vows take effect and their acts of *hekdesh* are binding. For a girl, the corresponding capacity is *choletzes* — she may perform *chalitza*. Tosafot notes that there is a Talmudic opinion that a woman performing *chalitza* need not be an adult, even if the man must be; this stage follows that view.

Rava adds a special rule regarding the sale of land inherited from one's father: *ul'makor b'nichsei aviv ad sheyehei ben esrim* — even after a boy reaches *bar mitzvah* at thirteen and is fully an adult, the Rabbis did not recognize the sale of inherited land as valid until he reached twenty. The concern was that a young man of thirteen, even legally an adult, lacked the life experience and judgment necessary to appreciate the full consequences of selling real property. Given that selling land was among the most consequential financial transactions possible, the Rabbis delayed full competence for this specific act until age twenty, regardless of *bar mitzvah* status.

## Minor Shaliach

### Location Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ קְטַנָּה שֶׁאָמְרָה: ״הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי״ – אֵינוֹ גֵּט עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדָהּ; לְפִיכָךְ, אִם רָצָה הַבַּעַל לַחֲזוֹר – יַחְזוֹר. שֶׁאֵין קָטָן עוֹשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a minor girl who said** to an agent: **Receive my bill of divorce for me, it is not** a valid **bill of divorce until the bill of divorce reaches her possession. Therefore, if the husband seeks to retract** his decision before his wife receives the bill of divorce, **he can retract** it, **as a minor does not designate an agent.** Consequently, the agent is not an agent for receipt, and the divorce does not take effect when the husband hands the document to the agent. The agent is an agent for delivery, and the divorce takes effect when the bill of divorce enters the wife's possession.

The Mishnah presents the case of someone who says to his *shaliach*: *ten get l'ishti b'makom ploni* — give this *get* to my wife in a specific location — and the *shaliach* delivers it to her in a different location. The *get* is invalid, because the husband appointed him a *shaliach* specifically for that place, and outside that place the appointment does not hold.

### No Shaliach Power

The Mishnah then states: *ketanah she'amra hitkabel li giti eino get ad sheyagiya l'yadah* — a *ketanah* who said "accept my *get* for me" — this is not a valid *get* until it reaches her hand directly. The reason is that a minor cannot appoint a *shaliach*. If she attempts to appoint someone to receive her *get*, her attempt is legally ineffective. What happens instead is that when the husband gives the *get* to the agent, the husband has implicitly made that agent a *shaliach* for delivery (*shaliach holachah*). The *get* will only take effect when the agent delivers it to her hand. Since she cannot appoint a *shaliach*, the agent was never her representative — and the husband can retract his delivery *shaliach* at any point before the *get* reaches the wife. As the Mishnah states: *she'ein katan oseh shaliach* — a minor cannot create a *shaliach*.

### Father Appoints

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ אָבִיהָ: ״צֵא וְהִתְקַבֵּל לְבִתִּי גִּיטָּהּ״, אִם רָצָה לַחֲזוֹר – לֹא יַחְזוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if **her father said to** the agent: **Go out and receive my daughter's bill of divorce on** her behalf, then **if** the husband **seeks to retract** his decision, **he cannot retract** it. As a father can receive the bill of divorce on behalf of his minor daughter, he can designate an agent for receipt, and the divorce takes effect when the husband hands the document to the agent.

If, however, the father says to an agent, *tzei v'hitkabel l'biti gita* — go and accept the *get* for my daughter — the father's appointment is valid. The father, who does legally represent his minor daughter, can appoint a *shaliach* to receive the *get* on her behalf. Once the husband gives the *get* to this agent, *im ratza lachazor lo yachazor* — the husband may not retract, because the father has validly appointed the agent as the daughter's representative for *kabbalah* (reception), and the *get* takes effect upon delivery to the agent.

## Place Conditions (III)

### Mar'eh vs. Hakpid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר: ״תֵּן גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״, וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר – פָּסוּל. ״הֲרֵי הִיא בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״, וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who says** to an agent: **Give this bill of divorce to my wife in such and such a place,** if the agent deviated **and gave it to her in another place** the divorce is **invalid.** However, if he said to the agent: Give this bill of divorce to my wife, **she is in such and such a place,** without explicitly instructing the agent to give her the document there, **and he gave it to her in another place** the divorce is **valid.**

The Mishnah distinguishes between two types of location specifications in the context of *shlichus* for a *get*. When the husband says to his *shaliach*, *ten get l'ishti b'makom ploni* — give the *get* to my wife in a specific place — and the agent delivers it in a different place, the *get* is invalid. The husband specified the place, and we read that specification as a *hakpadah* — a genuine restriction on the terms of the *shlichus*. By contrast, if the husband says *harei hi b'makom ploni* — "she can be found at such-and-such a place" — and the agent delivers it elsewhere, the *get* is valid. Here the husband was merely offering logistical guidance — pointing the *shaliach* toward where to find his wife — not conditioning the *get* on a specific location.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁאָמְרָה: ״הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״, וְקִיבְּלוֹ לָהּ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר – פָּסוּל; רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַכְשִׁיר. ״הָבֵא לִי גִּיטִּי מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״, וֶהֱבִיאוֹ לָהּ מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the woman who** when designating her agent for receipt **said** to her agent: **Receive my bill of divorce for me in such and such a place, and he received it for her in another place,** the divorce is **invalid;** and **Rabbi Elazar deems** it **valid.** If she said to him: **Bring me my bill of divorce from such and such a place, and he brought it for her from another place,** it is **valid.** Because he is an agent for delivery, the woman is not particular where he receives the bill of divorce, as the divorce takes effect only when the bill of divorce reaches her possession.

The Mishnah then addresses the parallel case where the wife specifies a location: *ha'isha she'amra hitkabel li giti b'makom ploni* — a woman who said "accept my *get* for me in a specific place." If the agent receives it elsewhere, according to the *Rabbanam* the *get* is invalid. Rabbi Eliezer, however, validates the *get*. The disagreement concerns how to read the woman's specification of a place.

### Rashi's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא דְּלָא פְּלִיג, וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא דִּפְלִיג? אִיהוּ, דְּמִדַּעְתֵּיהּ מְגָרֵשׁ – קָפֵיד, אִיהִי, דִּבְעַל כֻּרְחַהּ מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת – מַרְאָה מָקוֹם הִיא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Elazar, what is different** in **the first clause,** where the agent deviated from the husband's instructions and delivered the bill of divorce in a different place, **where he does not disagree** with the unattributed opinion of the first *tanna* that the divorce is invalid, **and what is different** in **the latter clause,** where the agent deviated from the wife's instructions and received the bill of divorce in a different place, **where he disagrees** with the unattributed opinion of the first *tanna* and deems the divorce valid? The Gemara answers: **He,** the husband, **who divorces** his wife **of his own volition, insists** that the divorce be effected in a certain place. However, **she,** the wife, **who is divorced** even **against her will,** is in no position to insist with regard to the manner in which the divorce will be effected, and **is** merely **indicating a place for him** to give her the bill of divorce.

The Gemara asks: why does Rabbi Eliezer rule differently when it is the wife who specifies a location, compared to when it is the husband? The answer articulates the fundamental asymmetry: *ihu d'midaiteih megareish, kapid* — the husband, who is initiating the divorce entirely of his own will, can genuinely restrict the terms under which his *shaliach* operates. He can say: I only want to divorce her here. *Ihee d'al karchah migareshet* — she is being divorced against her will and cannot control the circumstances. Therefore, when she names a location, she is merely being *mareh makom* — pointing the *shaliach* toward where he can find her husband — not imposing a genuine condition.

Rashi elaborates the precise logic. Since the wife is appointing a *shaliach* and must presumably want the *get* to happen (otherwise, why appoint anyone?), she cannot realistically condition her appointment on a specific location, because the husband may simply refuse to cooperate there. The husband is the one with ultimate control over whether and where the *get* is given. Once we presume that the wife wants to be divorced — which her appointment of a *shaliach* demonstrates — we conclude that any location she names is guidance, not restriction. She cannot genuinely restrict it to one location because she cannot compel the husband to comply. Therefore, the *Rabbanam* read her specification as *hakpadah* while Rabbi Eliezer reads it as *mareh makom*.

This analysis is worth pausing over. One might argue that this is precisely the moment when a woman has actual agency: she is the one appointing the *shaliach*, and she can define the terms of her appointment. If she says "only accept it here," why should we ignore that because she is generally disempowered in *gittin*? Rashi's answer is practical: given that the husband controls whether the *get* happens at all, any condition she imposes is hollow — he can always simply decline to give the *get* in that location. Once she has made a *shaliach* and shown she wants the divorce, we presume she will accept it wherever it is offered.

One might add that in a post-*Cherem d'Rabbeinu Gershom* world, where a wife cannot be divorced against her will, these assumptions might shift — the fundamental asymmetry that drives Rashi's logic is no longer as stark.

## Hulkach vs. Kabbalah

### Husband's Hulkach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הָבֵא לִי גִּיטִּי״ – אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדָהּ. ״הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי״, אֲסוּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: An Israelite woman married to a priest partakes of *teruma*. If she says to an agent: **Bring me my bill of divorce,** designating him as an agent for delivery, **she** continues to **partake of *teruma* until the bill of divorce reaches her possession.** However, if she says: **Receive my bill of divorce for me,** thereby designating him as an agent for receipt, it is **immediately prohibited** for her **to partake of *teruma*.**

The distinction between *shaliach l'holachah* and *shaliach l'kabbalah* is clarified through the case introduced in the Mishnah. When the husband says to an agent *holech* — convey this *get* to my wife — he has made the agent a *shaliach* for delivery (*shaliach holachah*). The *get* does not take effect until it reaches the wife's hand.

### Wife's Hulkach

When the wife says to an agent *havei li giti* — bring me my *get* — she is not appointing him as a *shaliach* for *kabbalah* (reception) on her behalf. Rather, as the Gemara understands it through the principle of *holech k'zachi*, the wife is effectively making the agent a *shaliach holachah* for the husband — asking him to go and serve as the husband's delivery agent. The *get* does not take effect until it reaches her hands.

### Terumah Status

This distinction has an immediate practical consequence: if the wife is married to a *kohen*, she eats *terumah* while she is married and must stop once she is divorced. When she says *havei li giti*, since the agent is a *shaliach holachah* and she is not divorced until the *get* reaches her, she continues eating *terumah* until the *get* arrives — *ochelet b'terumah ad sheyagiya get l'yadah*.

## Place Conditions (III)

### Woman's Place

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ – אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ גֵּט לְאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹסֵר מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the woman said to the agent: **Receive my bill of divorce for me in such and such a place,** then even if he received it elsewhere, **she** continues to **partake of *teruma* until the bill of divorce reaches that place. Rabbi Elazar prohibits** her from partaking of *teruma* **immediately.**

However, when she says *hitkabel li giti*, appointing the agent as a *shaliach l'kabbalah*, the moment the husband gives the *get* to the agent she is effectively divorced (since reception by her designated agent equals reception by her). Since she does not know when that moment will arrive, she must immediately stop eating *terumah* — *assur l'echol b'terumah miyad*.

## Place Conditions (III)…

### Mixed Places

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְגִיטָּא מִיהָא הָוֵי? וְהָאָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא, לָא הָוֵי גִּיטָּא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאָמְרָה לֵיהּ: ״הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטָּא בְּמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא, וְזִימְנִין דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לֵיהּ בְּבָבֶל״; וְהָכִי קָאָמְרָה לֵיהּ: מִשְׁקָל – כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לֵיהּ, שִׁקְלֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ; גִּיטָּא לָא הָוֵי – עַד דְּמָטֵית לְמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא.
>
> **Translation:** In this mishna, the first *tanna* apparently states that if the agent for receipt received the bill of divorce in a place other than the place designated by the woman for receipt, the bill of divorce is valid when the agent brings it to the designated place. The Gemara asks: **And in any event, is it** a valid **bill of divorce? But didn't you say** in **the first clause,** i.e., in the previous mishna, that if the agent received the bill of divorce in another place, **it is not** a valid **bill of divorce?** The Gemara answers: **No,** this *halakha* is **necessary** with regard to a case **where she said to him: Receive the bill of divorce for me in** the city of **Mata Meḥasya, and sometimes you** can **find him in** the city of **Babylon. And this is what she is saying:** When **taking** the bill of divorce, **anywhere that you find him, take it from him,** However, it **is not a** valid **bill of divorce until you reach Mata Meḥasya.**

The Mishnah states that if the wife says *hitkabel li giti b'makom ploni* — accept my *get* for me in a specific place — she may eat *terumah* until the *get* arrives at that place (*ochelet b'terumah ad sheyagiya get l'oto makom*). This phrasing is striking: it says until the *get* arrives at that place, not until the *shaliach* arrives at that place. The Gemara works to clarify this.

The Gemara explains that the Mishnah is describing a compound case: she says to the *shaliach*, "Accept my *get* for me in *Mata Machasia*, but sometimes you will find my husband in *Bavel*." In other words, she named a primary location but acknowledged that the husband could sometimes be found elsewhere. In such a case, the Gemara understands her to be saying: wherever you find my husband, take the *get* from him — but I do not want it to take effect until you arrive at the named location. The husband may give the *get* to the *shaliach* anywhere, but the *get*'s effect is suspended until the *shaliach* reaches *Mata Machasia*.

### Tosafot Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹסֵר מִיָּד. פְּשִׁיטָא, דְּהָא מַרְאָה מָקוֹם הִיא לוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if the woman said to the agent: Receive my bill of divorce for me in such and such a place, **Rabbi Elazar prohibits** her from partaking of *teruma* **immediately.** The Gemara asks: That **is obvious, as** she **is** merely **indicating a place for him** to receive the bill of divorce and not stipulating that the divorce is contingent upon receipt of the document in that place.

This is a genuinely strange reading, as Tosafot immediately observes. The mechanics are puzzling: the husband hands the *get* to the *shaliach* in *Bavel*, but the *get* does not yet take effect. So in what capacity does the *shaliach* hold the *get* while traveling to *Mata Machasia*? He cannot be the wife's *shaliach l'kabbalah*, since the *get* has already been received but not yet taken effect. Is he then the husband's *shaliach l'holachah*? But the husband gave it to him without making him a *shaliach* for delivery. Tosafot tries to resolve this by suggesting that since the wife stipulated this condition, and the husband plays into it, there is some sense in which the husband effectively delays the operation of the *get*. But how the wife can impose this delay — given that the *da'as* for the *get*'s effect belongs to the husband — remains mechanically unclear.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״זִיל לְמִזְרָח, דְּאִיתֵיהּ בְּמִזְרָח״; וְקָא אָזֵל לְמַעֲרָב. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, בְּמַעֲרָב – הָא לֵיתֵיהּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דִּילְמָא בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאָזֵיל מֵיגָס גָּאֵיס בֵּיהּ, וִיהַב לֵיהּ גִּיטָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** Rabbi Elazar's ruling is **necessary** in a case **where she said to him: Go to the east, as** my husband **is in the east, and** the agent **went to the west. Lest you say** that since the husband **is** certainly **not in the west** and the agent will not find him there, the bill of divorce will certainly not take effect until later, Rabbi Elazar **teaches us** that **perhaps while he was going** west, the agent happened to **encounter** the husband, **and** the husband **gave the bill of divorce to** the agent.

Rabbi Eliezer rules *oser miyad* — she must stop eating *terumah* immediately — because in his view, when a woman names a location it is merely *mareh makom*: guidance for finding the husband, not a genuine restriction. Since the *get* could be given anywhere at any time, she must stop eating *terumah* from the moment she appoints the *shaliach*. The Gemara adds a refinement: even if the wife told the *shaliach* "go east, you'll find him in the east," and he went west instead, Rabbi Eliezer would still require her to stop eating immediately — because *dilma bahadi deka'azal u'mati gabei*, perhaps as he walked out the door he happened to run into the husband and the *get* was given. Since there is no *halachically* enforceable restriction on where it takes place, there is always a theoretical possibility that it happened immediately.

## Eiruv Analogy

### Eiruv Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ ״עָרֵב לִי בִּתְמָרִים״, וְעֵירַב לוֹ בִּגְרוֹגְרוֹת; ״בִּגְרוֹגְרוֹת״, וְעֵירַב לוֹ בִּתְמָרִים; תָּנֵי חֲדָא: עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related *halakha*. With regard to **one who says to his agent: Establish an *eiruv*** of Shabbat boundaries **on my** behalf **with dates, and he established an *eiruv* on his** behalf **with dried figs,** or if said to his agent: Establish an *eiruv* on my behalf **with dried figs, and he established an *eiruv* on his** behalf **with dates,** it **is taught** in **one** *baraita*: **His *eiruv* is** a valid ***eiruv*. And** it **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **His *eiruv* is not** a valid ***eiruv*.**

The Gemara now applies the debate between the *Rabbanam* and Rabbi Eliezer beyond *gittin*, to the domain of *eiruv*. The Mishnah-level rule for *eiruv* involves designating specific food for the purpose. If a person instructs his *shaliach*, "Make my *eiruv* with dates," and the *shaliach* makes it with figs instead, or vice versa, two *beraitot* conflict: one says the *eiruv* is valid, the other says it is not. Rabba resolves this: *ha rabbanan ha Rabbi Elazar* — one *baraita* follows the *Rabbanam*, who read instructions as a *hakpadah* (and thus the *eiruv* with the wrong food is invalid), and the other follows Rabbi Eliezer, who reads instructions as *mareh makom* (and thus the *eiruv* is valid regardless).

### Rava's Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא רַבָּנַן, הָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. הָא רַבָּנַן – דְּאָמְרִי: קְפִידָא; הָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: מַרְאָה מָקוֹם הִיא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** This is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, in which it is taught that it is not a valid *eiruv*, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** and **that** *baraita*, in which it is taught that it is a valid *eiruv*, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar.** He explains: **This** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say:** When one gives instructions to his agent, there is **insistence** on his part that the agent implement those instructions without deviation. Failure to do so revokes his designation as his agent. And **that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** who **says: She is** merely **indicating a place for him** to receive the bill of divorce and not stipulating that the divorce is contingent on receipt of the document in that place. In the *baraita* as well, he was not particular as to what food should be used to establish the *eiruv*.

This suggests that the dispute between the *Rabbanam* and Rabbi Eliezer is not limited to the specific case of a woman appointing a *shaliach* for her *get*, but reflects a general jurisprudential disagreement: when a person specifies a particular detail in giving instructions to a *shaliach*, do we presume that specification is a genuine restriction (*hakpadah*) or merely helpful guidance (*mareh makom*)? The *Rabbanam* default to *hakpadah*; Rabbi Eliezer defaults to *mareh makom* based on contextual reasonableness.

### Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: הָא וְהָא רַבָּנַן; כָּאן בְּשֶׁלּוֹ, כָּאן בְּשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef said:** Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who say: When one gives instructions to his agent, there is insistence on his part that the agent implement those instructions without deviation. However, not all deviations are equal. **Here,** where the *baraita* rules that it is a valid *eiruv*, the reference is to a case where the one who designated the agent instructed him to establish the joining of the courtyard **with his** dates or dried figs and the agent deviated and established the *eiruv* with the other type of fruit, but it belonged to the one issuing the instructions. **There,** where the *baraita* rules that it is not a valid *eiruv*, the reference is to a case where the one who designated the agent instructed him to establish the joining of the courtyard **with** the dates or dried figs **of another,** and the agent deviated and established the *eiruv* with the other type of fruit belonging to that other person. The *eiruv* is not valid because that other person authorized use of only a specific type of fruit.

Rav Yosef offers an alternative resolution that avoids invoking the Rabbi Eliezer/*Rabbanam* dispute. He says both conflicting *beraitot* could represent the *Rabbanam*: *kan b'shelo kan b'shel chaveiro* — here we are dealing with the *shaliach*'s own food, and there we are dealing with the sender's food. If I tell a *shaliach* "make an *eiruv* with dates" and I am providing the dates, I am *makpid* about what is used — it is my property and I can restrict it. If, however, the *shaliach* is providing the food from his own stores, then even the *Rabbanam* would say I cannot genuinely be *makpid* about what he uses — I have no right to dictate which of his foods he employs.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא, הָאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ: ״עָרֵב לִי בְּמִגְדָּל״ – וְעֵירַב לוֹ בְּשׁוֹבָךְ, ״בְּשׁוֹבָךְ״ – וְעֵירַב לוֹ בְּמִגְדָּל; דְּתַנְיָא חֲדָא: עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב; הָתָם – מַאי שֶׁלּוֹ וְשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** to Rav Yosef: **However, that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who says to his agent: Establish a joining** of Shabbat boundaries **on my** behalf **in a tower, and he established the *eiruv* in a dovecote,** or he said to the agent: **Establish a joining** of Shabbat boundaries **on my** behalf **in a dovecote, and he established the *eiruv* in a tower,** it is **taught** in **one** *baraita*: **His joining** of Shabbat boundaries **is** a valid ***eiruv*. And** it **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **His joining** of Shabbat boundaries **is not** a valid ***eiruv*. There, what** distinction between **his** fruit **and** fruit **of another is there?**

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef's framework from another *baraita*: the case where someone instructs his *shaliach* to make an *eiruv* in a tower (*migdal*) and the *shaliach* makes it in a pigeon coop (*shovach*), or the reverse. Again, one *baraita* validates the *eiruv* and one does not. If Rav Yosef's resolution is based on whose food it is, what does that have to do with whether the *eiruv* was made in the tower or the coop? The location of the deposit is not about ownership of food.

### Food Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי, אִיכָּא פֵּירֵי דְמִגְדָּל וּפֵירֵי דְשׁוֹבָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There too, there is** a distinction between **fruit of the tower and fruit of the dovecote.** In these *baraitot* the instruction did not relate to the location of the placement of the *eiruv*; rather, the instruction was related to the location of the fruit to be used in establishing the *eiruv*. In one *baraita*, the produce in both locations belongs to the one who designated the agent; in the other *baraita*, the produce in both locations belongs to another.

Rav Yosef responds: in this case too, the reference to the *migdal* or *shovach* is not about the physical location of the *eiruv* deposit — it is about the *source* of the food. The *migdal* and the *shovach* are storage locations, and specifying one or the other indicates which stores of food the instruction refers to. "Make the *eiruv* from the tower" means "use the food stored in the tower," and "make it from the pigeon coop" means "use the food stored there." Thus the case is really still about whose food and which specific stores are being designated. If the *shaliach* uses the food from the wrong location, the question is whether the *ba'al* was genuinely restricting which of his stores should be used. Once understood this way, Rav Yosef's framework holds: when it is the sender's food, specification indicates *hakpadah*; when it is the *shaliach*'s food, the *Rabbanam* themselves concede that no *hakpadah* is imposed.

Abaye's own preferred resolution, by contrast, is simpler: the two *beraitot* reflect the straightforward disagreement between the *Rabbanam* and Rabbi Eliezer as general disputants over the presumption of *hakpadah* versus *mareh makom*, without any need to distinguish between whose food is involved.
