# Meilah 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs. Rabbi Yochanan

### Intro: Me'ilah & Terumas

The daf opens at the top of 9b with a fundamental *machlokes* between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan concerning the laws of *me'ilah* as they apply to the ashes of offerings burned on the *mizbeiach*.

### Mishnah: Until Beis Hade…

The simple reading of the *mishnah* is that *me'ilah* applies to things burned on the *mizbeiach* until they are taken out to the *beis hadeshen*. Until that point, the *mitzvah* is considered still incomplete.

### Rav: After Terumas

Rav holds that once *terumas hadeshen* has been performed — the symbolic morning removal of a scoop of ash placed beside the ramp of the altar — there is no longer *me'ilah*. At that moment, the *mitzvah* is considered fulfilled, and the ashes are no longer subject to the laws of *me'ilah*.

### R. Yochanan: Persists Un…

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. He emphasizes that the full *avodah* of removing the ashes entirely outside the *mikdash* — *hotza'at hadeshen* — remains to be done. Since this removal is performed in *bigdei kehunah* and constitutes its own *avodah*, the *mitzvot* associated with the burned offerings are not yet complete even after *terumas hadeshen*. Accordingly, *me'ilah* persists until the ashes are actually taken out.

Both authorities do agree, however, that before *terumas hadeshen* there is unquestionably *me'ilah* on the ashes. The debate is whether *terumas hadeshen* alone suffices to end *me'ilah*, or whether *me'ilah* persists all the way until *hotza'at hadeshen*.

The *gemara* at the top of 9b opens with a *meiseiv* — a challenge — drawn from a *mishnah* in *Zevachim*. That *mishnah* teaches that certain items placed on the *mizbeiach* along with the skin — such as horns or hair — may remain even though they do not belong there on their own; and similarly, if a coal (*gachelet*) that was on the *mizbeiach* fell off, one does not put it back. Pseudo-Rashi clarifies that we are speaking of ordinary firewood, not the burned *imurim* of a *korban*. The implication of *na'aseh mitzvaso* as it applies to ordinary firewood is set aside, and the *gemara* focuses on the operative principle.

The *gemara* then infers: *ha al gabei mizbeiach yachazir* — if the coal is still on the *mizbeiach* but has moved from the fire to another part of the *mizbeiach* where there is no fire, one does return it to the fire. This is the *tosefta*'s ruling. The question is how we know we are dealing with a situation after *terumas hadeshen* has already been performed. Rashi addresses this by explaining that we are speaking of material that came from the main central pile of ashes — the large mound in the middle of the *mizbeiach* — into which the ashes from the surrounding fires would be swept only after *terumas hadeshen*. Tosafot takes a different approach, as will be seen below.

The challenge to Rav is clear: *bishlama le-Rabbi Yochanan nicha* — according to Rabbi Yochanan, since the *mitzvah* of *hotza'at hadeshen* remains to be done, it is perfectly understandable why one moves the coal back into the proper place. But according to Rav, who holds that after *terumas hadeshen* there is no longer *me'ilah* and the *mitzvah* is complete, why should it matter whether the coal is returned or not?

## Coal vs. Ashes Analysis

### Tosefta: Coal Falling Off

The *tosefta* teaches that if a coal fell off the *mizbeiach*, one does not put it back. But if the coal merely moved to a different location on the *mizbeiach* — a place without fire — one does return it to the fire. This ruling demands explanation, particularly according to Rav, who holds that after *terumas hadeshen* the matter is complete. Why does one bother to move a coal back to the fire if the *mitzvah* is already fulfilled?

### Rav: Coal Has Mashasha

Rav answers: *lo, shani gachelet de-it bah mashasha* — a coal is different because it still has *mashasha*, substance. It has not yet fully burned to ashes. When Rav says the *mitzvah* is complete after *terumas hadeshen*, he means specifically with respect to what has already become pure ashes. A coal, by contrast, still has combustion remaining; it has not yet finished becoming ashes. Therefore, even according to Rav, the *mitzvah* is not yet complete as long as the material remains in the state of a coal, and that is why one returns it to the fire.

### R. Yochanan: Coal vs. As…

The *gemara* then presents an alternative version of the argument, *ika de-amri*, running the inference the other direction. On this reading, the *tosefta*'s reason for returning the coal to the fire is precisely because it still has *mashasha* — substance still to be burned. But pure ashes, which have no *mashasha* left, even if they are still on the *mizbeiach*, one would not bother returning them to any particular place.

This formulation favors Rav — *bishlama le-Rav nicha* — because the only reason one cares about the coal is that it still has substance and has not yet turned to ashes. But it poses a difficulty for Rabbi Yochanan — *ela le-Rav Yochanan kashya* — since according to him one should care about even pure ashes, as they still need to be taken out to the *beis hadeshen*. Why should the *tosefta* suggest that ashes are treated as irrelevant?

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *hu hadin de-afilu efer* — the same law would indeed apply even to ashes, and one would return ashes as well. The reason the *tosefta* specifically mentions a *gachelet* is to teach you that even something that still has *mashasha*, and still has burning left to be done, nevertheless once it has fallen off the *mizbeiach* — *ki paka me-al gabei mizbeiach lo yachazir* — one does not return it. The novelty is not that ashes are excluded, but that even a coal, despite having residual combustion, is not replaced once it falls off.

### Tosafot: Potential for U…

At this point Tosafot raises an important question about the very premise of the debate. Why does everyone agree that before *terumas hadeshen* there is *me'ilah*? After all, one does not need to use these specific ashes for *terumas hadeshen* — any ashes from the pile will do. Tosafot frames it differently from the straightforward reading: what it means for the ashes to still have a *mitzvah* to be done is not that *these* particular ashes must be used in *terumas hadeshen*, but that they remain in the category of ashes that have the *potential* to be used for *terumas hadeshen*. As long as they have that potential, they are not yet *na'aseh mitzvaso*.

This framing also resolves the question of how we know the *tosefta* is speaking after *terumas hadeshen*. According to Tosafot, what matters is not when *terumas hadeshen* was actually performed, but whether the material is currently in a position where the *mitzvah* could be applied to it. When a coal or ash has moved to a random part of the *mizbeiach* where ashes are not normally gathered, it is no longer part of the collection to which *terumas hadeshen* is normally done, and therefore it has temporarily left the category of *na'aseh mitzvaso*. This is why one returns it — not merely for convenience, but to restore it to its proper status. One might ask whether this means it is even more emphatically *not na'aseh mitzvaso* if it has moved away from where the *mitzvah* applies — and that is indeed Tosafot's point: one returns it precisely because it needs to be back in the functional position of the *mitzvah*.

The broader takeaway from this section is Rav's position that *na'aseh mitzvaso* by burned items requires not just the performance of *terumas hadeshen* but the full reduction to ash. As long as something remains a coal rather than pure ash, the *mitzvah* is not yet complete — and that is a distinct criterion that operates even within Rav's view.

## Me'ilah Repayment Models

### Benefit from Money

The *gemara* shifts to a new topic: the question of *me'ilah* repayment. When one benefits from a *korban* or from funds designated for a *korban*, what exactly must one give back?

The straightforward case is benefit from money set aside for a *korban*. If the money has not yet been used to purchase the *korban*, the answer is simple: add the repayment plus the additional fifth to the original sum and use it to bring or enhance the *korban*. The *gemara* distinguishes between *kodshei kodashim* and *kodshim kalim*, and between benefit before *zerikah* and benefit from *imurim* after *zerikah*, as cases where *me'ilah* still applies.

### Rav: Becomes Nedavah Olah

The *gemara* introduces an *itmar* — a recorded dispute among Amoraim. If one benefited from the meat of *kodshei kodashim* before *zerikat damim*, or from the *imurim* of *kodshim kalim* after *zerikat damim* — in both cases, material that was exclusively for the *mizbeiach* and not permitted for human benefit — what does one do?

Rav says: *mah she-neheneh yipelu le-nedavah* — whatever one benefited, the equivalent value goes toward a *nedavah*, that is, a communal *olat tzibbur*. One buys an *olah* with the money. The idea is that since the benefit came from something destined entirely for the *mizbeiach*, the repayment should similarly go entirely to the *mizbeiach*, and the closest approximation is an *olah*, which is entirely burned.

### Levi: Entirely for Altar

Levi disagrees. He says one should bring *davar she-kulo la-mizbeiach* — something that goes completely to the altar, more completely than even an *olah*, since an *olah* still has the *or*, the hide, which goes to the *kohanim*.

A *baraita* supports Levi: those who learn before the sages say one brings something that is entirely for the altar — and the *gemara* asks, what is that? The answer is *ketoret*, the incense offering, which is entirely burned. Tosafot notes that the phrase "those who learn before the sages" is associated elsewhere in the *gemara* in *Sanhedrin* with Levi, who would learn before *Rebbe*. This is unusual, since Levi is not quoted in the *mishnah* or *baraitot*, but Tosafot acknowledges that in certain places this phrase does refer to him.

It is worth noting the conceptual asymmetry here. In the case of *imurei kodshim kalim* after *zerikah*, the material taken would indeed have gone to the *mizbeiach*, so returning something entirely for the *mizbeiach* makes intuitive sense. But in the case of *basar kodshei kodashim* before *zerikah*, the meat would ultimately have been eaten by the *kohanim*, not placed on the *mizbeiach*. One has not taken something that would have been burned. Yet the same repayment — an *olah* or *ketoret* — applies. The *chiddush* is that even when the benefit came from meat that was destined for the *kohanim*, one must give back something entirely for the *mizbeiach*. One does not simply pay the *kohanim* for a meal; one must give back to *hekdesh*, and the vehicle for that return is something wholly consumed on the altar.

The case of *eitzim* — wood donated as a *korban* — is raised as a possible parallel, since it too could be considered entirely burned. Whether Levi would include such a donation in his category remains a question, as wood is a less classic case of a *korban* than *ketoret* or an animal *mincha*.

### Contradiction: Sea vs. O…

A difficulty emerges from the *baraita*: an internal contradiction within the same passage. The *baraita* states that if one benefited from the money of a *chatat* or *asham* before the *korban* was offered — meaning before it was purchased — one adds the fifth and uses the money to bring the *chatat* or *asham*. But if the *chatat* was already offered (*karav chatato*), the money is thrown into the *Yam HaMelach*, the Dead Sea. The *baraita* then also states categorically that whenever one benefits from a *korban* of the *mizbeiach*, the repayment goes as a *nedavat tzibbur* — an *olah* — with no qualification about whether the original *korban* was already offered.

The contradiction: in the *chatat* case, if the *korban* was already brought, the money is thrown into the sea. In the categorical rule, it always becomes an *olah*. Why the discrepancy?

### Resolution: Two Models

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction by identifying two different opinions within the *baraita*. The first clause — throw the money into the *Yam HaMelach* — follows Rabbi Shimon, who holds that any *chatat* whose owners have been atoned for through another means must die; the money is treated analogously. The final clause — bring the repayment as a *nedavah olah* — follows the Rabbanan, who hold that in such cases the money should be used for a communal *olah*.

Rava adds a further distinction. The rule of throwing money into the sea applies specifically when one became aware of the obligation before the *korban* was offered. At that point, conceptually, one had an obligation to set aside *chatat* money, and when the *chatat* was subsequently offered for another reason, the money takes on the status of a *chatat she-niskepru ba'aleha ba-acher* — a *chatat* whose owners have already been atoned for through a different offering — and must be discarded accordingly. But if one only became aware of the obligation after the *chatat* had already been brought, then the obligation to repay never had *chatat* money at its core; there was no moment when *chatat* money was being set aside. In that case, everyone agrees the money goes as an *olah*.

Underlying Rava's distinction is a deeper structural point. There are, in fact, two models for *me'ilah* repayment. The first model holds that one gives back the same type of *korban* from which one benefited: if you took from a *chatat*, you give back a *chatat*; if you took from an *asham*, you give back an *asham*. The money thrown into the sea is a consequence of this model — when you cannot bring a replacement *chatat* because atonement was already achieved another way, you follow the laws of what happens to surplus *chatat* funds. The second model holds that the repayment is always an *olah* or something entirely for the *mizbeiach*, because what one took was sanctified material and the return must be sanctified material entirely consumed by the altar.

The resolution suggests that these two models are not entirely at odds. Even under the "give back what you took" model, most cases resolve into an *olah* — because a leftover *asham* becomes an *olah*, a leftover *chatat* (under the Rabbanan) becomes an *olah*, and something taken from *shelamim imurim* must be repaid by an *olah* since one cannot give back a *shelamim* when only the portion destined for the *mizbeiach* was used. The one exception is the *chatat* case under Rabbi Shimon, which ends with the money thrown into the sea rather than an *olah*. The *olah* model is therefore essentially correct in nearly all cases, but its application tracks the "give back what you took" logic rather than constituting an independent principle.

## Tamei After Zerikah

### Rava: Kodshei Kodashim

Rava states: everyone agrees that if one benefited from the meat of *kodshei kodashim* that had become *tamei*, or from *imurim* before *zerikah*, or from the *imurim* of *kodshim kalim* after they had been placed on the fire, one is *patur* — exempt from *me'ilah*. The *gemara* immediately says *peshita* — this is obvious. But in fact, the statement is far from obvious.

Consider the case of *basar kodshei kodashim she-nitma*. If the meat became *tamei* before *zerikah*, then the *zerikah* was never a *matir* for this meat, and our very first *mishnah* in the first *perek* established that if a problem occurs before *zerikah* and *zerikah* never validates it, the *issur me'ilah* remains. So why should one be *patur*?

### Tosafot: After Zerikah

Tosafot resolves the difficulty by explaining that Rava is speaking specifically about meat that became *tamei* **after** *zerikat hadam*. On this reading, the meat had been rendered permitted to the *kohanim* through *zerikah*, and then subsequently became *tamei*. *Kodshim she-nifsalu* — sanctified items that have been invalidated — must be burned, and one might have thought that since there is now a *mitzvah* of *srefah* to burn this meat, perhaps it is not yet *na'aseh mitzvaso* and *me'ilah* would re-apply. Rava teaches that even so, there is no *me'ilah*. The *hiddush* is that once the meat was rendered *mutar la-kohanim* through valid *zerikah*, it has departed from the category of *kodshei Hashem*, and the subsequent *mitzvah* to burn it due to *tumah* does not bring it back into the domain of *me'ilah*.

This reading makes the *peshita* understandable as applying to the first part of the statement — the *kodshei kodashim* meat — while the true novelty lies in the second part: the *imurei kodshim kalim* after they are placed on the fire.

### Kodshei vs. Kodshim Kalim

On the *kodshim kalim* question, one might have thought that since the *imurim* are on the *mizbeiach* and there is still a *mitzvah* to turn them and ensure they are fully burned to ashes, they are not yet *na'aseh mitzvaso*, and *me'ilah* still applies. The *gemara* teaches that this is not the case — once they are placed on the fire, they are no longer subject to *me'ilah*, even though full combustion has not yet occurred.

This asymmetry with *kodshei kodashim* — where *me'ilah* persists until the ashes are fully complete and processed — reflects a fundamental structural difference. *Kodshei kodashim* is conceptually *kulo la-Hashem*; the entire animal belongs to the Divine realm, and even the meat eaten by the *kohanim* is considered *mi-shulchan gavoah*. Accordingly, the *mitzvah* of the ashes, *terumas hadeshen*, and *hotza'at hadeshen* all bear upon the status of *me'ilah*, because this is an offering that is maximally *kodshei Hashem*. *Kodshim kalim*, by contrast, only attain *me'ilah* status in the narrow window of the *imurim* after *zerikah*. Having struggled to enter the *me'ilah* category at all, they exit it at the earliest point — as soon as the *imurim* are placed on the fire and the basic act of offering has commenced.

Tosafot raises an additional possibility: perhaps the obligation of *hotza'at hadeshen* — and by implication the *na'aseh mitzvaso* question that prolongs *me'ilah* — applies only to *kodshei kodashim* and not to *kodshim kalim* at all. The *pesukim* regarding *terumas hadeshen* are written in the context of the *olah*, a *kodshei kodashim* offering. If there is fundamentally no *mitzvah* of ash removal for *kodshim kalim*, then there is no *na'aseh mitzvaso* concern to sustain *me'ilah* once the *imurim* have been placed on the fire.

## Me'ilah Repayment Mode…

### When Aware: Critical Tim…

Rava's statement about *me'ilah* repayment establishes that the critical variable is not merely whether the *korban* has been offered, but specifically when the person became aware of his obligation. If one became aware before the *korban* was offered, one has conceptually incurred an obligation to set aside *chatat* money, and when the *chatat* is subsequently offered through other means, that conceptual obligation takes on the status of *chatat she-niskepru ba'aleha ba-acher* — a *chatat* whose owners achieved atonement through a different offering. Under Rabbi Shimon, such a *chatat* must die, and so the money follows that rule and goes to the *Yam HaMelach*.

But if one only became aware after the *korban* had already been brought, then from the moment of awareness onward there is no possibility of setting aside *chatat* money in any meaningful sense — the *chatat* is already gone. Therefore, the rule of *ein mafrishin le-chatchilah le-ibud* — one does not set aside money to be discarded — applies. In this case, everyone agrees the money goes toward a *nedavah olah*.

It is worth adding that this distinction reinforces the structural observation made earlier: the model of "give back what you took" generates the possibility of money being thrown into the sea only when the obligation had already crystallized as *chatat* money before the offering was brought. When one benefits directly from a *korban* rather than from money — and the *korban* is present and intact — there is no moment at which *chatat* money is conceptually set aside, and everyone agrees the repayment takes the form of an *olah* or something wholly for the *mizbeiach*.

## Wholly Burned Items

### Me'ilah: From Sanctifica…

The *mishnah* now turns to a new category: items that are burned entirely on the *mizbeiach* — the *kometz*, the *levonah*, the *ketoret*, *minchat kohanim*, *minchat kohen mashiach*, and *minchat nesachim*. For all of these, *me'ilah* begins from the moment they are sanctified — *me'ilan mi-she-yukdashu*. This is their initial *kedushat me'ilah*.

### Stages of Sanctity

Once these items are sanctified in a vessel — *kiddeshan bi-kli* — they attain *kedushat haguf* and are susceptible to all the consequences of full sanctity. At this stage they become *huchshar bi-kli* — capable of being rendered invalid through contact with a *tevul yom* or a *mechusar kippurim*, and *linah* applies. The *mishnah* then states: *ve-chayavin aleihen mishum notar u-mishum tamei* — one is liable for *notar* and *tumah* with regard to them. These items are fully subject to all the *issur* categories that apply to *kodashim*.

However, the *mishnah* adds: *u-figul ein bah* — there is no *piggul* for these items. This exceptional ruling requires explanation.

### General Principle: Matir…

The *mishnah* articulates the underlying principle as a *klal*: *kol she-yesh lo matirin* — anything that has a *matir*, meaning a final act performed on or with the *korban* that renders the remaining portions permitted (whether for eating or for being placed on the *mizbeiach*), *ein chayavin mishum piggul lo zeh ve-lo zeh ad she-yikrevu matirin* — neither the food portion nor the altar portion incurs *piggul* liability until the *matir* has been performed. *Zerikah* is the paradigmatic *matir* for most *korbanot*: the sprinkling of blood makes the meat permissible to the *kohanim* and allows the *imurim* to be placed on the *mizbeiach*. Where such a *matir* exists, *piggul*, *notar*, and *tumah* liability for the remaining portions all wait until the *matir* is done.

*Ve-chol she-ein lo matirin* — but anything that has no such *matir*, where the entire item is placed directly on the *mizbeiach* and there is no further act that renders anything permitted — *chayav aleihen mishum notar ve-tamei*, one is liable for *notar* and *tumah*, but *piggul ein bo* — there is still no *piggul*. The reason is that *piggul* by definition requires an act of *ritzuy*, an act through which the *korban* is validated and rendered *ratzu*, accepted, before the *piggul* thought can then apply to what remains. When the entire item goes on the *mizbeiach* at once and there is nothing "remaining" in the ordinary sense, the mechanism of *piggul* cannot operate. Even an *olah*, which is entirely burned, has *zerikah* as its *matir*, which precedes the placing of the limbs on the *mizbeiach* — and so an *olah* can be subject to *piggul*. But items like *kometz*, *levonah*, and *ketoret*, where the sole act is placing the item on the *mizbeiach* itself with no preceding *matir*, cannot generate *piggul*.

### Proof: Asher Yikrav

The *gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the scriptural source for these two principles? A *baraita* is cited. One might have thought that *tumah* liability for eating *kodashim* applies only to items that have a *matir* — that is, only to things that function like a normal *korban* with *zerikah ha-dam* — via a *kal va-chomer*: *piggul*, which is very stringent (it applies with a single awareness, requires a fixed *korban*, and has no exceptions), nevertheless applies only to things with *matirin*. Should not *tumah*, which is more lenient (requiring two awarenesses, having a sliding-scale *korban* in the form of *oleh ve-yored*, and admitting exceptions in cases of *tzibbur*), certainly be restricted to things with *matirin* as well?

The *Torah* teaches otherwise. The verse *asher yikrav mi-kol zarechem el ha-kodashim* — addressing anyone who approaches the *kodashim* in a state of impurity — is understood to speak of *all* categories of *kodashim*. *Bechol ha-kodashim ha-katuv medaber* — the verse applies across the board. Therefore *tumah* liability applies even to things that have no *matir*, such as *levonah* or *minchat kohen*.

The verse's phrasing is then exploited in the opposite direction. One might think: since the verse broadens *tumah* to all *kodashim*, does *tumah* apply even before the *matir* is performed in cases where a *matir* does exist? Perhaps eating *kodshei kodashim* meat while *tamei* before *zerikah* incurs liability. The *gemara* answers from the same phrase. Rabbi Eliezer raises the point that the verse uses the language of *yikrav* — "who approaches" — rather than "who eats," even though *karet* for eating *kodashim* be-*tumah* applies to eating, not merely approaching. This usage is deliberate: *asher yikrav* teaches that liability applies only after the *matirin* have been *karev* — after the validating act has been performed. Before the *matir*, even if the item is formally *kodesh*, no *karet* liability for eating in *tumah* applies.

### Without Matirin: Vessel…

Rabbi Eliezer's principle is then formulated as the general rule: *kol davar she-yesh lo matirin, eino chayav ad she-yikrevu matirin* — anything with a *matir* incurs *tumah*, *notar*, and *piggul* liability only after the *matir* is performed. *Ve-chol davar she-ein lo matirin, eino chayav ad she-yikadesh bi-kli* — anything without a *matir* incurs these liabilities as soon as it is sanctified in the vessel. Since there is no subsequent act that will render it permitted, and since the entire item will go on the *mizbeiach* directly, the moment of vessel sanctification is the operative threshold for *tumah* and *notar*. There is, again, no *piggul* in either case for items without *matirin*, since *piggul* requires the structure of a validating act followed by something remaining.

The *gemara* thus provides the scriptural foundation for the entire framework of the *mishnah*: the stages of *kedushat haguf*, vessel sanctification, *matirin*, and their respective consequences for *me'ilah*, *tumah*, *notar*, and *piggul*. With this, the *perek* concludes.

A final note on the reading of Rava's earlier statement about *basar kodshei kodashim she-nitma*: Rashi's *girsa* reads *kodem zerikah*, and Rashi appears to suggest a reading reminiscent of Rabbi Akiva's principle that once meat is entirely unfit — unable to be placed on the *mizbeiach* and unable to be eaten — there is no *me'ilah*. Tosafot strongly contests this, noting that Rabbi Akiva stated his principle specifically with regard to *yotzei* and not to other *pesulim*, and that the *gemara*'s response of *peshita* would be unintelligible under Rashi's reading as applied to Rava's case. The far cleaner reading, as Tosafot argues, is that Rava is speaking throughout of meat that became *tamei* after *zerikah* — and on that reading, the statement is coherent, the *peshita* is appropriate for the first clause, and the *chiddush* lies in the law of *imurei kodshim kalim* once they have been placed on the fire.
