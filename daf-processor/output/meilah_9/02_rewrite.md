# Meilah 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs. R. Yochanan

### Machlokes Overview

The daf opens with a fundamental *machlokes* between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan concerning the law of *me'ilah* — the prohibition against deriving benefit from consecrated property — as it applies to the ashes of burnt offerings. The simple reading of the Mishnah is that *me'ilah* applies to things burnt on the *mizbeiach* until they are taken out to the *beis hadeshen*, since until that point the *mitzvah* has not yet been fully completed. However, Rav holds that once *terumas hadeshen* — the symbolic morning scooping of ash — has been performed, *me'ilah* no longer applies. Rabbi Yochanan emphasizes the importance not only of the original *terumas hadeshen* but also of the *hotza'at hadeshen*, the full removal of ashes to the area outside the *mikdash*, which is itself an *avodah* performed in *bigdei kehunah*. According to Rabbi Yochanan, until that final stage is completed, the *mitzvah* is not yet finished and *me'ilah* therefore remains in force.

### Zevachim Mishnah

The Gemara opens with a *meiseiv* — a challenge — from a Mishnah in Zevachim. The Mishnah states: *vechulan shepaku mei'agabi hamizbeiach lo yachazir* — if various items fell off the *mizbeiach*, they are not returned. This general rule extends to the case of a *gachelet*, a burning coal: *vechan gacheles shepaku mei'agabi hamizbeiach lo yachazir* — a coal that fell off the *mizbeiach* is not put back. As Rashi notes, this refers not to the burnt innards of a *korban* but to ordinary firewood that has turned into charcoal.

The Mishnah continues: *ha'agabi mizbeiach yachazir* — if, however, the coal was still on the *mizbeiach* and merely moved from the fire to another spot on the *mizbeiach* where there was no fire, it is returned to the fire. The question this raises is: why would one bother returning a coal that has already turned to ash and over which *terumas hadeshen* has presumably already been performed? The very act of returning it suggests that the *mitzvah* has not yet been completed.

The layout of the *mizbeiach* is relevant here. There was a large *ma'aracha gedola* — the main fire — along with a secondary fire (*ma'aracha sheniya*) for the *ketoret*, and a central pile of ashes. Rashi's approach to establishing that we are dealing with a situation after *terumas hadeshen* is somewhat complicated, and his reading depends on identifying the ash in question as coming from the central mound. This is because items actively turning to coal on the main fire would be obviously subject to *me'ilah*, as *terumas hadeshen* would not yet have been done. Rashi therefore establishes that we are dealing with a coal drawn from the central ashes — material over which *terumas hadeshen* has already been performed.

### Coal vs. Ash

The Gemara now applies this Mishnah to the debate. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the ruling is straightforward: even after *terumas hadeshen*, there are still specific *mitzvot* to be performed — namely, *hotza'at hadeshen* — and therefore one would naturally ensure that items are properly situated. However, for Rav, who holds that *me'ilah* ends at *terumas hadeshen*, the question becomes sharp: *ela leRav kashya* — why should it matter at all whether the coal is returned to the fire? Once *terumas hadeshen* has been done, Rav holds the *mitzvah* is complete; the coal's location would seem irrelevant.

The Gemara answers on Rav's behalf: *lo, shani gachelet d'it bah mashasha* — a coal is different because it still has substance. Rav's position is that *me'ilah* ends when the material has fully become ash. A *gachelet* — a burning coal — still has burning left to do; it has not yet become pure ash. Therefore, even according to Rav, it has not yet reached the stage of *na'aseh mitzvaso* and must be returned to the fire so that it can complete its combustion and become ash.

An alternative version of the argument presents the inference in the opposite direction: the only reason the ruling mentions returning the coal at all is precisely because it still has substance — *ta'ama mishum gachelet d'it bah mashasha* — but pure ash that has no further substance would not be returned even if it remained on the *mizbeiach*. This formulation presents the ruling as evidence for Rav's position: only a *gachelet* matters, not already-complete ash.

### R. Yochanan's Reply

Rabbi Yochanan, faced with the inference that favors Rav, responds: *hu hadin d'afilu efer* — the same rule would apply even to ash; the Mishnah chose to speak of a coal not to exclude ash but to teach that even something still possessing substance and still capable of further burning — a *gachelet* — does not need to be returned once it has fallen off the *mizbeiach*. The coal case thus represents the stringent end of the spectrum: if even a coal need not be returned once it has fallen off, certainly ash need not be. This formulation resolves the challenge by interpreting the Mishnah's case as extending, rather than limiting, the category of what is left off the *mizbeiach*. It remains somewhat unclear why falling off the *mizbeiach* should categorically remove the obligation to return an otherwise valid item, but the Gemara does not press this point further, accepting it as a given.

### Tosafot on Terumas

A deeper conceptual question underlies the entire discussion: what exactly does it mean that there is still a *mitzvah* of *terumas hadeshen* to be performed on these ashes? Since *terumas hadeshen* can be performed using any of the ashes and does not require these particular ashes specifically, in what sense is the *mitzvah* "their" *mitzvah*?

The approach offered previously was that these ashes must at minimum be part of the pile from which *terumas hadeshen* is taken, and therefore retain a connection to the *mitzvah* so long as they remain in that pile. Tosafot, however, frames it differently. According to Tosafot, the relevant criterion is not whether *terumas hadeshen* has been performed, but whether the ashes are in a position where they have the *potential* to be used for *terumas hadeshen*. When the coal or ash moves to some part of the *mizbeiach* where the ashes are not normally gathered for *terumas hadeshen*, it is no longer in a position where that *mitzvah* can be applied to it. Therefore, it is not in a state of *na'aseh mitzvaso* — but paradoxically, because the *mitzvah* can no longer be performed on it in its current location, one must move it back. According to Rav, this reasoning applies as long as the item is still a *gachelet* with substance; once it is pure ash it is considered complete. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the *mitzvah* of *hotza'at hadeshen* remains to be done regardless, so the coal or ash retains its significance until that final stage.

## Me'ilah Restitution

### Benefit Before/After

The Gemara now pivots to a different but related question: when one has committed *me'ilah* from a *korban*, not by stealing its monetary value but by deriving personal *hana'ah* from the animal itself — what does restitution look like? If I took money set aside for a *korban*, I simply return the money plus the added fifth. But if the *korban* was already an animal and I rode on it, or benefited from the part designated for the *mizbeiach*, to what do I owe restitution and how do I make it?

The Gemara introduces two categories: one who derived benefit from the meat of *kodshei kodashim* before *zerikat dam*, and one who derived benefit from the *imurin* — the designated altar portions — of *kodshim kalim* after *zerikat dam*. In both cases, the person has benefited from something that was wholly destined for the *mizbeiach* or otherwise unavailable for human use, and the question is how to restore it.

### Rav: Olat Nedavah

Rav rules: *mah she'neheneh yipelu lindavah* — the monetary value of what one benefited from is contributed toward a communal *nedavah*, that is, an *olat tzibur*, a communal burnt offering. This is an approximation: the *olah* goes entirely on the *mizbeiach*, and so it serves as the closest substitute for what was taken, which was also destined for the altar.

### Levi: Ketoret

Levi goes further. He holds: *yavi davar shekulo lamizbeach* — one should bring something that goes *entirely* onto the *mizbeiach*. An *olah* does not fully qualify, because the skin (*or*) remains and is not consumed on the altar. What is entirely consumed? The *ketoret* — the incense offering. A *baraita* cited in support of Levi states: *yavi davar shekulo lamizbeach* — bring something that is entirely for the altar — and identifies this as the *minchat kohanim*, which is fully burnt. The *halomedin lifnei chachamim*, identified in a Gemara in Sanhedrin as Levi who would study before Rebbe, said: bring *ketoret*, since the incense is entirely consumed. Since the *kohanim* must actually perform the bringing of *ketoret*, one does not bring it personally but rather contributes the funds to the *tzibur* for that purpose.

Tosafot notes a difficulty: if this phrase (*halomedin lifnei chachamim*) refers to Levi, it is unusual that this appears as a *tanya kevasai d'Levi* — as though a *baraita* is confirming Levi's view — when it would simply be Levi himself speaking. Moreover, Levi is a post-Tannaitic figure and never appears in the Mishnah or standard *beraitot*. Tosafot therefore concludes that the phrase refers to someone else, though his precise identity remains uncertain.

An important conceptual point: while the idea of substituting an entirely-altar offering makes obvious sense for the case of the *imurei kodshim kalim* — which were themselves destined entirely for the altar — it is a significant *chiddush* that the same restitution applies to *basar kodshei kodashim*, the meat of *kodshei kodashim*, which would never have gone onto the *mizbeiach* at all but would have been eaten by *kohanim*. Nevertheless, since it cannot be restored in kind, the best available substitute is an offering that is entirely consumed.

### Money Restitution

The Gemara now takes up the case where what was misappropriated was not the animal itself but money designated for a *korban*. The cases of *chatat* and *asham* money are addressed. If one benefited from money designated for a *chatat* and the *chatat* has not yet been purchased, one simply adds the fifth and uses the combined sum to purchase the *chatat*: *yosif veyavi chatato*. Similarly for *asham* money: *yosif veyavi ashamo*.

The more complex case arises when the *chatat* has already been brought. If one became aware of the misappropriation before the *chatat* was offered, then at that moment an obligation to set aside restitution funds crystallized. Subsequently, a *chatat* was brought — but not from this person's funds. The restitution money now resembles a *chatat she'niskapru ba'aleha be'acher* — a *chatat* whose owners were atoned for by means of another offering. The *halacha* for such a *chatat* is that it must die. By analogy, the money goes to *yam hamelach*: *yolich l'yam hamelach* — it is cast into the Dead Sea.

If, however, the *asham* was brought and a second one exists, the second becomes an *olah*. Therefore, if benefit was derived from *asham* money and the *asham* was subsequently brought: *yipelu lindavah* — the money goes toward a communal *olah*.

### Restitution Principles

The Mishnah then articulates the overarching principles: *kol korbonos hamizbeach l'mizbeach* — whatever was taken from something destined for the altar goes back to the altar; *korbonos bedek habayis l'bedek habayis* — what was taken from Temple treasury funds is restored to the Temple treasury; *korbonos tzibur lindvas tzibur* — what was taken from a communal offering is restored as a communal *nedavah*, that is, as a burnt offering.

This last principle overlaps with Rav's ruling from earlier. The Mishnah states that whatever one derived benefit from a *korban* — including *basar kodshei kodashim* which would never have gone on the altar — is restored through an *olat nedavah*. This is stated without qualification: *lo shena d'ikaper ba'aleha v'lo shena d'lo ikaper* — it does not matter whether the original *korban* was brought or not.

### Contradiction

This creates an apparent internal contradiction in the sources. The earlier ruling said that if the *chatat* was brought before the person became aware, the money is thrown into the Dead Sea. The Mishnah's general principle says that whenever one derives benefit from a *korban*, the money goes to a *nedavah* — with no qualification regarding whether the original *korban* was brought. Why then, in the *chatat* case, do we throw the money into the Dead Sea?

### Rav Gbiha: Reisha/Seifa

The Gemara resolves this by noting that the *reisha* and *seifa* represent two different opinions. The *reisha* — which prescribes casting the money into the Dead Sea — follows Rabbi Shimon, who holds that any *chatat* whose owners have been atoned for by means of another offering must die. Applied to the money, this means the funds take on the character of such a *chatat* and must be disposed of accordingly. The *seifa* — which directs that the money go toward a communal *olah* — follows the Rabbis, who hold that a *chatat* in such circumstances is brought as an *olah* rather than destroyed. Thus, the two rulings do not contradict each other; they represent the positions of Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis respectively.

Rav Gbiha from Bei Katil reports to Rav Ashi that Abaye said the same: *reisha* Rabbi Shimon, *seifa* Rabbanan.

## Status Changes

### Tamei After Zerikat

Rava now makes a broad statement: everyone agrees that if one derived benefit from the meat of *kodshei kodashim* that became *tamei*, or from *imurei kodshim kalim* after they were placed on the fire (*she'he'elam*), one is exempt from *me'ilah*. The Gemara responds: *peshita* — this is obvious. But this response is surprising, because at least one of these cases is far from obvious.

Consider the meat of *kodshei kodashim* that became *tamei*. If *zerikah* was performed while the meat was already *tamei*, our very first Mishnah in this tractate established that *me'ilah* still applies — the *zerikah* was not *matir* because the meat was *pasul*. So why does Rava say everyone agrees there is no *me'ilah*? And for the *imurei kodshim kalim* that are already burning on the *mizbeiach* but have not yet turned to ash, the discussion about *na'aseh mitzvaso* would suggest the *mitzvah* is not yet complete. Why would everyone agree there is no *me'ilah*?

### Tosafot on Status

Rashi (or Pseudo-Rashi) reads Rava's statement as discussing the case of *basar kodshei kodashim* that became *tamei* before *zerikah* — and understands it as a variant of Rabbi Akiva's principle that once *zerikah* has occurred, even with a *pasul* situation, there is no *me'ilah* on the meat. However, Tosafot strongly disputes this reading. Tosafot argues that Rabbi Akiva's ruling was stated specifically for the case of *yotzei* — an animal that left the *azarah* — and was never extended to other *pesulim*. Moreover, the Gemara would never have said *peshita* with respect to such a reading of Rava.

Tosafot therefore understands Rava as discussing the case where the meat became *tamei* after *zerikat hadam*. Here the reasoning is cleaner: once *zerikah* is performed validly, the meat is released to the *kohanim* for eating, at which point it exits the category of *kodshei Hashem*. If it then becomes *tamei*, there is indeed a *mitzvah* of *srefah* — it must be burned — but that obligation does not draw the meat back into the realm of *me'ilah*. Something can be *assur* without being subject to *me'ilah*: *kodshim kalim* meat, for instance, is forbidden before *zerikah* yet carries no *me'ilah*.

The *chiddush* then lies in the second case: *imurei kodshim kalim* that were placed on the altar fire. One might have thought that since the *mitzvah* requires the *imurim* to be fully consumed — there is a *mitzvah lehapuchei betzinora*, to turn them over and ensure they burn completely — the *mitzvah* is not yet *na'aseh* until they become ash, and therefore *me'ilah* still applies. Rava teaches that this is not so — once placed on the fire and burning, both Rav and Rabbi Yochanan agree that *me'ilah* is gone.

### Gemara Consensus

The Gemara explains: *mahu de-teima*, one might have thought that since *basar kodshei kodashim she'nitma ais lei mitzvat srefah l'kohanim* — there is still a *mitzvah* of burning — and *emurei kodshim kalim ika mitzvah le'hapuchei betzinora* — there is still a *mitzvah* to turn them over on the fire — one would say the *mitzvah* is not complete and *me'ilah* remains. Rava therefore teaches that in both cases, everyone agrees there is no *me'ilah*. The Gemara's *peshita* is directed at the idea that once the meat has exited *kedushat Hashem*, even residual obligations do not bring it back into *me'ilah*; the *chiddush* is that the same applies to *imurei kodshim kalim* on the altar fire.

### Kedusha Levels

This leads to a broader conceptual question: why is it that *kodshim kalim* carry no *me'ilah* on their meat at all, even before *zerikah*, when the meat is clearly forbidden? The answer lies in the nature of the consecration. *Kodshei kodashim* are conceived as *kulo la-Hashem* — entirely for Heaven — so that even the portions eaten by *kohanim* are, as it were, *mishulchan gavoah*, from the table of the Most High. The entire animal, conceptually, belongs to Heaven, and therefore *me'ilah* applies throughout. *Kodshim kalim*, by contrast, are not conceived as *kulo la-Hashem* from the outset. The animal itself is partially the owner's, and only after *zerikah* do the *imurim* crystallize as belonging wholly to the *mizbeiach*, triggering *me'ilah* on them.

This difference in the underlying level of *kedushah* explains a further point: why, once *imurei kodshim kalim* begin burning on the *mizbeiach*, do both Rav and Rabbi Yochanan agree there is no *me'ilah*, even though they dispute whether *na'aseh mitzvaso* requires the ashes to be removed for the case of the burnt *olah*? Tosafot suggests an additional explanation: the *mitzvah* of *terumas hadeshen* and *hotza'at hadeshen* — with their attendant *na'aseh mitzvaso* implications — may be derived from *pesukim* that speak specifically about the *olah*, and therefore may not apply at all to *kodshim kalim*. If so, once the *imurim* of *kodshim kalim* begin burning, there is no remaining *mitzvah* whose non-completion would keep *me'ilah* in force.

## Me'ilah Restitution…

### Rava: Timing of Awareness

Returning to the contradiction between throwing money into the Dead Sea and bringing an *olah*, Rava offers a precise resolution. The rule of *yolich l'yam hamelach* — casting the money into the Dead Sea — applies only when the person became aware of the misappropriation before the *chatat* was brought (*d'ishtemoda lei kammai kapara*). In that case, at the moment of awareness, the obligation to set aside *chatat* funds crystallized. Since that virtual *chatat* existed, when a different *chatat* was subsequently brought, the money took on the status of *chatat she'niskapru ba'aleha be'acher*, and must be disposed of accordingly.

However, if the person became aware only after the *chatat* was already brought (*l'achar kapara*), then there is no *me'ilah* obligation of the ים המלח variety. The Gemara states: *ein mafrishin l'chatchilah l'ibud* — one does not set aside funds with the aim of throwing them away. Since at the moment of awareness there was already no *chatat* that could be brought — the atonement had already been accomplished by other means — the money never assumed the character of a *chatat* awaiting a parallel. Therefore the funds go toward a communal *olah* rather than being cast into the Dead Sea.

The deeper principle is that the status of *chatat she'niskapru ba'aleha be'acher* requires that the displaced *chatat* virtually existed in advance of the original offering. If the obligation only came into being after the original offering, no such status can be created retroactively.

This resolution also illuminates the broader contradiction between the two models of restitution — give back an *olah* versus give back what you took. When what was misappropriated was money, there is scope for saying "restore what you took" — meaning replace the *chatat* money with *chatat* money — and then, if circumstances change, apply the rules that govern *chatat* funds. But when one derived *hana'ah* directly from the *korban* itself, the animal is still there; there is no concept of "giving back a *chatat*" because the *chatat* exists as a physical reality. In that case, both opinions agree the restitution takes the form of an *olah* — or, according to Levi, something even more fully consumed — which is *kulo lamizbeach*. The principle of giving back what was taken ultimately resolves, in almost every scenario, to bringing an *olah*, since that is the only substitute that adequately replaces what was designated for the altar.

## Matirin Rule

### Fully Burned Items

The Mishnah now opens a new chapter and introduces a distinct set of cases: the *kometz* and *levonah* separated from a *minchah*, the *ketoret* brought daily, the *minchat kohanim*, and the *minchat kohen mashiach*, as well as *minchat nesachim* — the flour component accompanying animal offerings. All of these share a common feature: they are entirely consumed on the *mizbeiach*, with nothing remaining for human consumption. The Mishnah states that *me'ilah* applies to these items from the moment of their consecration.

### Three Stages

The Mishnah then identifies a second stage of *kedushah* for these items: *kiddeshan bikli* — once they are sanctified through placement in a sacred vessel, they achieve *kedushat haguf*. From this point, they are susceptible to *tumah* in even its more subtle forms (*tevel yom*, *mechusar kippurim*), and the prohibition of *linah* — overnight invalidation — begins to apply.

The standard structure of a *korban* has three stages: consecration, at which point *me'ilah* applies; the moment of *kedushat haguf* when *tumah* susceptibility and *linah* begin; and the moment the *matir* — the permitting act, typically *zerikat hadam* — is performed, after which *piggul*, *notar*, and *tumah* of eating apply to whatever remains. For items that are entirely consumed on the *mizbeiach*, there is no third stage distinct from the second, because there is no portion that is released for eating or other use after a *matir* is performed. Therefore the Mishnah states: *ve'chayavin aleihen mishum notar u'mishum tamei* — one is liable for *notar* and *tamei* — but *piggul ein bah* — there is no *piggul*.

### Matirin Principle

The Mishnah articulates the underlying rule that governs all of these cases: *kol she'yesh lo matirin* — anything that has a *matir*, meaning an act performed on the *mizbeiach* that releases the remainder of the *korban* for its intended use (such as *zerikat hadam* for most *korbanot*, or the burning of the *kometz* for a *minchah*) — *ein chayavin mishum piggul lo zeh velo zeh ad sheyikrev hamatir* — neither the offering nor the released portion becomes subject to *piggul* until the *matir* has been offered.

Conversely, *kol she'ein lo matirin* — anything that has no *matir*, meaning it goes directly onto the *mizbeiach* without a separate act releasing another portion — *kivan shekidesh bikli* — from the moment it is sanctified in the vessel — one is liable for *notar* and *tamei*, but *piggul ein bo* — there is no *piggul*. The reason there is no *piggul* in this class of cases is that *piggul* requires an act of *ritzuy* — an act that makes the offering valid and releases the remainder. Without a *matir*, there is no such act, no moment at which one could form an invalid intent that would render the offering *piggul*. Since *piggul* is defined by the concept of *lo yeratze* — the offering is not accepted because of a disqualifying intention during one of the service acts — it cannot apply where there are no such service acts that release a remainder.

### Asher Yikrav

The Gemara seeks to ground these principles in *pesukim*. The question arises: perhaps the prohibition of *tumah* — eating *kodashim* while *tamei* — applies only to things that have a *matir*, that is, normal *korbanot* with *zerikat hadam*? A *kal vachomer* supports this: *piggul*, which is the more severe prohibition (applying with only a single moment of awareness, carrying a fixed *korban*, and admitting no exceptions), applies only to things with a *matir*. Should not *tumah*, which is the lesser prohibition (requiring two moments of awareness, carrying an *olah v'yored*, and overridden for *tzibur*), certainly be restricted to the same class?

The Gemara responds by citing a *pasuk*: *emor aleihem ledoroteichem kol ish asher yikrav mikol zarechem* — you shall tell them for their generations that any person who approaches from all your offspring [who is *tamei* shall not approach the sacred things]. The phrase *kol asher yikrav* — all who approach — is understood as *bekhol hakodashim hakatuv medaber* — the *pasuk* speaks of all *kodashim*, even those without a *matir*. Therefore the prohibition of *tumah* applies across the board.

### Rabbi Eliezer

The Gemara then raises the converse question: perhaps *tumah* applies even before the *matir* is performed, for items that do have a *matir*? That is, if one eats the meat of a *korban* while *tamei* before *zerikat hadam*, perhaps one is liable? The Gemara cites *talmud lomar asher yikrav* — the same phrase *asher yikrav* — to teach that liability only begins after the act of *hakravah*, after the *matir* has been brought.

Rabbi Eliezer sharpens this derivation. He notes that the straightforward reading of the *pasuk* — "anyone who approaches the sacred things" — would seem to apply to one who merely *touches* the sacred item while *tamei*. But one is not liable for *kares* by touching *kodashim* while *tamei*; the *kares* applies to eating. Why then does the Torah use the language of *yikrav* — approaching — when speaking about the prohibition of eating *kodashim betumah*? To derive the following rule: *kol davar she'yesh lo matirin eino chayav ad sheyikrevu matirin* — for anything that has a *matir*, one is not liable unless and until the *matir* has been offered. And *kol davar she'ein lo matirin* — for anything without a *matir* — *eino chayav ad sheyikadesh bikli* — liability begins when it is sanctified in the vessel.

This is the organizing principle of the Mishnah. At sanctification, *me'ilah* begins. At *kedushat haguf* — for most *korbanot* this is *shechitah*, for these entirely-consumed items it is sanctification in the vessel — *tumah* susceptibility, *linah*, and related disqualifications begin. And when the *matir* is performed, *piggul*, *notar*, and the prohibition of eating *betumah* fully crystallize. For items without a *matir*, that final stage collapses into the second, and *piggul* never applies at all.

*Hadran lach Chatat Ha'of.*
