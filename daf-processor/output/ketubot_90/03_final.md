# Ketubot 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Multiple Documents

### Two Gets, Two Ketubos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנֵי גִיטִּין וּשְׁתֵּי כְתוּבּוֹת, גּוֹבָה שְׁתֵּי כְתוּבּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a woman had **two bills of divorce and two marriage contracts** as a result of her divorce and remarriage to the same man, the fact that she is in possession of these documents proves that she was never paid for her first marriage contract, and she **collects two marriage contracts.**

The final *mishnah* of the ninth *perek* opens with a case involving multiple documents: *shnei gittin u-shtei ketubot* — a woman produces two *gittin* and two *ketubot*, staggered in sequence. The pattern is a *ketubah* from one date, a *get* from a later date, then a second *ketubah* and a second *get*, indicating two marriages followed by two divorces with the same husband. In such a case, the *mishnah* rules *gova shtei ketubot* — she may collect two *ketubot*.

### Remarriage & New Ketubah

This ruling requires explanation, because as we have seen in earlier discussion, remarriage to the same husband does not automatically generate a new *ketubah* obligation. The default assumption is that the original *ketubah* continues as the basis of the second marriage. However, if the husband proactively chose to write a new *ketubah*, he has created a distinct, second obligation — and in that case, both are binding. The staggered pattern of the documents, with two *ketubot* and two *gittin*, demonstrates precisely that he did so.

### Two Ketubos, One Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי כְתוּבּוֹת וְגֵט אֶחָד, אוֹ כְּתוּבָּה וּשְׁנֵי גִטִּין, אוֹ כְּתוּבָּה וְגֵט וּמִיתָה — אֵינָהּ גּוֹבָה אֶלָּא כְּתוּבָּה אַחַת. שֶׁהַמְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְהֶחְזִירָהּ — עַל מְנָת כְּתוּבָּה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה מַחְזִירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If she was in possession of **two marriage contracts and** only **one bill of divorce; or** if she had one **marriage contract and two bills of divorce; or** if she had **a marriage contract, a bill of divorce, and** witnesses to her husband's **death** after their remarriage, **she collects** payment of **only one marriage contract.** This is because there is a presumption **that one who divorces his wife and remarries her, remarries her with the intention** of using her **first marriage contract,** and she agrees that she collects payment of only the original document. This is the presumption, unless he wrote another marriage contract for her.

The *mishnah* then addresses three additional configurations, each of which limits her to collecting only a single *ketubah*. The cases are: two *ketubot* and one *get*; one *ketubah* and two *gittin*; and one *ketubah*, a *get*, and the husband's subsequent death. The last case is self-evident — she was divorced, then her husband died, and she cannot collect again as a widow. The case of one *ketubah* and two *gittin* illustrates the default principle most clearly: even though the second *get* is evidence that he remarried her, the absence of a second *ketubah* means the remarriage was conducted *al menas ketubah ha-rishonah* — on the basis of the original *ketubah* — and no new obligation was created.

The case of two *ketubot* and one *get* is the one the *mishnah* identifies as needing explanation. One might have thought that both documents are simultaneously binding — perhaps the husband intended to accumulate additional obligations. Indeed, a reader familiar with earlier discussion will recall a scenario in which a woman possessed two *ketubot* and was permitted to collect from both. The present *mishnah* rules *eino gova ela ketubah achas* — she may collect only one — because the presumption is that the second *ketubah* was written in anticipation of a remarriage following a divorce that may or may not be documented here. But the question immediately arises: which *ketubah* may she use? The earlier one carries earlier liens and therefore broader collection rights; the later one might carry a higher monetary amount. The *mishnah*'s silence on which one suggests that the choice belongs to her — a point the *Gemara* will address directly.

### Sequential Documents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִי בָּעֲיָא בְּהַאי — גָּבְיָא, אִי בָּעֲיָא בְּהַאי — גָּבְיָא? לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת הַיּוֹצְאִין בָּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, בִּיטֵּל שֵׁנִי אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that if she had two marriage contracts and one bill of divorce, she can collect only one marriage contract. However, it does not specify which marriage contract she can claim. Does this mean that **if** she **desires, she can collect** payment of the marriage contract **with this one, and if** she **desires, she can collect** payment **with that one?** In that case, if she prefers she can use the document that promises the larger sum, and if she prefers to use the marriage contract with the earlier date in order to be able to collect property that her husband had sold to others between the dates on the two documents, she may collect with that one. The Gemara asks: If that is the case, **let us say that it is a conclusive refutation of** a statement that **Rav Naḥman** said that **Shmuel said, as Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said:** If there are **two documents that are issued one after the other,** each recording the same transaction of a sale or a gift and they are separated by a few days, it is assumed that the **second** document **cancels the first** one. Why not say in this case as well that the second marriage contract voids the first one?

The *Gemara* raises the possibility that this choice contradicts the ruling of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *shnei shtaros ha-yotzin zeh achar zeh, bitel sheni es ha-rishon* — when two documents emerge sequentially and clearly refer to the same underlying transaction, the second voids the first. The clearest example is two bills of sale for the same plot of land. Since one cannot purchase the same property twice, the later document supplants the earlier one, and the buyer may not rely on the earlier dating. The practical significance of the earlier date lies in the scope of liens: because the seller accepts responsibility if a prior creditor forecloses on the property, the buyer with earlier liens can recover from the seller's property of that earlier period. A later document narrows that window. Similarly, if a disputed claim of ownership arises, proof of an earlier acquisition date carries independent value.

The apparent contradiction with our *mishnah* is thus: if a woman possesses two *ketubot* for the same marriage, the second should void the first, leaving her only the second. Yet the *mishnah* implies she may choose between the two.

### Waiver Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וּמוֹדֵה רַב נַחְמָן דְּאִי אוֹסֵיף בֵּיהּ דִּיקְלָא, לְתוֹסֶפֶת כַּתְבֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Was it not stated with regard to** the *halakha* Rav Naḥman quoted in the name of Shmuel that **Rav Pappa said: And Rav Naḥman concedes that if he added to** the transaction detailed in the second document **a palm tree** that was not mentioned in the first document, this shows that he did not intend to cancel the first document. Rather, **he wrote** the second document **as an addition** to the first document.

Rashi explains the underlying logic of Rav Nachman's principle: no one compelled the holder to accept the second document. A second bill of sale generally weakens the buyer's position — it establishes ownership from a later date, with correspondingly later liens. By voluntarily accepting it, the buyer implicitly declares that he waives whatever rights and protections he had under the earlier document. The acceptance of the later, less advantageous document constitutes a form of *mechilah* — waiver — of the benefits conferred by the earlier one.

### Added Benefits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי בִּדְאוֹסֵיף לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Here too,** the Gemara is dealing **with** a case **when he added** an additional sum **for her** in the second marriage contract. This proves that he wanted to add to the first marriage contract, and not to void it.

Rav Papa then states the critical qualification: *umodu Abaye v'Rava d'ei hosif bah dikla l'tosefes kesavah* — Abaye and Rava concede that if the second document added even one additional palm tree, one additional parcel, or any incremental benefit beyond what the first document included, then acceptance of the second document does not constitute a waiver of the first. The reasoning is direct: if the second document is clearly and entirely inferior to the first in every respect, then accepting it can only be explained as a willing surrender of the first. But if the second document contains any dimension in which it exceeds the first, then the holder has a rational basis for accepting it that does not imply waiver — he is preserving his freedom to choose whichever instrument better serves his interests in a given situation. Acceptance of the second document under those circumstances signals choice, not surrender.

### Choice of Ketubos

The application to our *mishnah* follows directly. Rav Papa concludes: *hacha nami bid'osif lah* — here too, the second *ketubah* must have included some added amount, even a single additional monetary unit, beyond the first. This is what allows the woman to retain the option of choosing between the two. When the second *ketubah* is not uniformly inferior — because it carries a higher face value, even if it comes with later liens — she is not presumed to have waived the first by accepting it, and she retains the ability to elect whichever is more advantageous in the circumstances. The *Gemara* notes that a double, cumulative obligation — both *ketubot* simultaneously binding — would require explicit language in the second document expressing precisely that intent. Absent such language, the result is an *or*, not an *and*: she collects from one or the other, at her election.

### Get vs. Ketubah Dating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הוֹצִיאָה גֵּט וּכְתוּבָּה וּמִיתָה, אִם גֵּט קוֹדֵם לַכְּתוּבָּה — גּוֹבָה שְׁתֵּי כְּתוּבּוֹת. כְּתוּבָּה קוֹדֶמֶת לַגֵּט — אֵינָהּ גּוֹבָה אֶלָּא כְּתוּבָּה אַחַת, שֶׁהַמְּגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְהֶחְזִירָהּ — עַל מְנָת כְּתוּבָּה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה הֶחְזִירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If **she produced a bill of divorce, a marriage contract, and** witnesses to her husband's **death,** **if** the date of the **bill of divorce precedes** the date **of the marriage contract, she** collects payment for her **two marriage contracts.** She is entitled to the first marriage contract by virtue of the bill of divorce. She is entitled to the second one because she has shown that it was written for her when they remarried. If the date of the **marriage contract precedes** the date **of the bill of divorce, she collects** payment of **only one marriage contract.** This is because it is presumed **that one who divorces his wife and remarries her, remarries her with the intention** of using her **first marriage contract,** unless there is a reason to say otherwise.

A related *braita* addresses the case in which a woman produces a *get*, a *ketubah*, and relies on the husband's subsequent death to claim as a widow. The rule is as follows: *im get kodem l'kesuvah, gova shtei ketubot* — if the *get* is dated before the *ketubah*, she collects two *ketubot*, because the later *ketubah* proves that he chose to obligate himself anew in a second marriage. If, however, the *ketubah* is dated before the *get* — *eina gova ela ketubah achas* — she collects only one, because even with clear evidence of divorce and remarriage, the absence of a new *ketubah* means the remarriage was on the basis of the original document alone.

## Minor's Ketubah

### Katan Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ קָטָן שֶׁהִשִּׂיאוֹ אָבִיו — כְּתוּבָּתָהּ קַיֶּימֶת, שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן קִיְּימָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a minor who was married** off **by his father,** the wife's **marriage contract** that the minor wrote **is valid** even after the husband comes of age. He cannot excuse himself by saying that it was drafted when he was a minor, **as** it is **on this condition,** the terms of this marriage contract, that **he maintained her** as his wife upon his maturity.

The *mishnah* now introduces a new case: *katan she-hisio aviv* — a minor boy who was married off by his father. This is noteworthy because the earlier discussion throughout the tractate has focused predominantly on the father arranging the marriage of a minor girl. The *mishnah* contemplates actual *nisuim* — full cohabitation — even when the boy is a *katan*. It is worth reiterating what the *Gemara* elsewhere makes clear: even in the context of *kiddushei ketanah*, the *Gemara* stated that actual *nisuim* — the couple living together — would not occur until the girl was at least a *na'arah*, twelve and a half years of age. Nevertheless, the case of a minor boy is presented here as a recognized, if unusual, reality.

### Ketubah Continuity

This ruling requires explanation, because as we have seen in earlier discussion, remarriage to the same husband does not automatically generate a new *ketubah* obligation. The default assumption is that the original *ketubah* continues as the basis of the second marriage. However, if the husband proactively chose to write a new *ketubah*, he has created a distinct, second obligation — and in that case, both are binding. The staggered pattern of the documents, with two *ketubot* and two *gittin*, demonstrates precisely that he did so.

The rule is *kesubasah kayamet* — her *ketubah* remains in force. Although the minor boy lacked the legal capacity to obligate himself in a *ketubah* at the time it was written, once he reaches adulthood and continues to maintain the marriage, it is understood that he does so *she-al menas kein kiymah* — on the basis of that original commitment — and the *ketubah* accordingly becomes binding upon him. The continuation of the marriage as an adult is itself the act that ratifies and assumes the earlier obligation.

## Ger & Conversion

### Non-Jewish Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיְּירָה אִשְׁתּוֹ עִמּוֹ — כְּתוּבָּתָהּ קַיֶּימֶת, שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן קִיְּימָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, in the case of **a convert whose wife converted with him,** the **marriage contract** that she had as a gentile **is valid, for on this condition he maintained her** as his wife.

The final *mishnah* of the *perek* presents a case of striking historical interest buried within a legal ruling: *ger she-nisgayerah ishto imo* — a man and his wife who both convert to Judaism together. Before their conversion, they had a marriage document drawn up according to the practices of their non-Jewish society. The *mishnah* notes, almost in passing, that non-Jews did indeed produce documents at marriage that functioned similarly to a *ketubah* — recording the dowry brought by the wife, and specifying the lump sum she would be entitled to in the event of divorce or the husband's death. This is not described as *pasak lah ketubah kedas Moshe v'Yisrael*, but the parallel institution is evident.

After their conversion, the couple remains married without rewriting a *ketubah*. When the husband later dies, the *mishnah* rules *kesubasah kayamet* — her *ketubah* obligation continues — because by remaining married after conversion the husband implicitly maintained the terms of the original agreement, based on the understanding that their earlier obligations carry forward.

### Base Amount Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מָנֶה מָאתַיִם, אֲבָל תּוֹסֶפֶת — אֵין לָהּ. וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תּוֹסֶפֶת יֵשׁ לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna said: They taught** that the wife of a minor or convert receives payment **only** with regard to the main sum of **one hundred dinars** or **two hundred** dinars. **However, she does not have** the right to receive the **additional** sum that he wrote in her marriage contract, because this document is not legally binding, as it was written by a minor. She receives the main sum only as a result of an ordinance instituted by the Sages. **And Rav Yehuda said: She has even** the **additional** sum.

Rav Huna offers a reading of the *mishnah* that, as the *Gemara* notes, departs substantially from the plain meaning: *lo shanu ela maneh u-matayim* — the ruling applies only to the base *ketubah* amount. His position is that whatever additional amount (*tosefes*) appeared in the pre-conversion document does not carry over into a binding Jewish *ketubah* obligation. Only the standard statutory amounts — two hundred *zuz* for a *besulah*, one hundred for an *almanah* — remain in effect.

### Full Amount

Rav Yehuda disagrees: *afilu tosefes yesh lah* — even the additional amount she was promised continues to be binding. This appears to be the straightforward reading of the *mishnah*, which uses the unqualified language *kesubasah kayamet* — her *ketubah* stands — without any limitation to the base amount.

### Braita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: חִידְּשׁוּ — נוֹטֶלֶת מַה שֶּׁחִידְּשׁוּ. חִידְּשׁוּ אִין, לֹא חִידְּשׁוּ לָא! אֵימָא: אַף מַה שֶּׁחִידְּשׁוּ. וְהָא לָא תָּנֵי הָכִי: חִידְּשׁוּ — נוֹטֶלֶת מַה שֶּׁחִידְּשׁוּ, לֹא חִידְּשׁוּ — בְּתוּלָה גּוֹבָה מָאתַיִם, וְאַלְמָנָה מָנֶה! תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרַב יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against the opinion of Rav Yehuda from a *baraita*: If a minor who came of age or a gentile who converted then **introduced** an additional sum to the marriage contract, **she takes** the additional sum **that they introduced.** The Gemara infers: **Yes,** the woman receives **what they introduced.** However, if **they did not introduce** an additional sum, she does **not** collect, even if it was written in the original marriage contract. The Gemara refutes this proof: **Say** that this means that she takes **even that which they introduced,** in addition to the entire amount of her original marriage contract. The Gemara asks: **But** the *tanna* **did not teach this,** and the continuation of the *baraita* states the opposite: If **they introduced** a new sum, **she takes** the additional sum **that which they introduced.** If **they did not introduce** a new sum, **a virgin collects two hundred** dinars **and a widow one hundred dinars.** She does not collect the additional sum listed in the marriage document. This provides **a conclusive refutation** of the statement **of Rav Yehuda,** whose opinion is rejected.

A *braita* is then cited as a challenge to Rav Yehuda. The *braita* states: *chidshu* — if after conversion or after the minor boy came of age they *innovated*, meaning they added a new amount to the *ketubah* — *noteles mah she-chidshu*, she is entitled to collect what they newly added. The *Gemara* initially attempts to read this as meaning she receives both the additional amount and the original full amount from before. But the *braita* continues explicitly: *lo chidshu — besulah gova matayim ve-almanah maneh* — if they did not add a new amount, she receives only the base statutory amount. This is unambiguous: the *braita* contradicts Rav Yehuda directly. *Tiyuvta de-Rav Yehuda tiyuvta* — it is a complete refutation of his position.

### Gemara Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה מַתְנִיתִין אַטְעֵיתֵיהּ, הוּא סָבַר: ״כְּתוּבָּתָהּ קַיֶּימֶת״ — אַכּוּלַּהּ מִילְּתָא קָאֵי. וְלָא הִיא, אַעִיקַּר כְּתוּבָּה קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav Yehuda was misled by** the language of **the mishna** and reached an incorrect conclusion. **He thought** that the phrase: **Her marriage contract is valid, is referring to the entire matter,** the entire sum of the marriage contract. **But that is not so,** as it **is referring** only **to the main** sum of the marriage contract that was established by the Sages, and not to any additional sum.

The *Gemara* explains that Rav Yehuda was misled by the language of the *mishnah* itself: *hu savar ketubatah kayamet akula milta* — he read *kesubasah kayamet* as meaning the entirety of the *ketubah* stands. In fact, as the *Gemara* clarifies, it means only that the base *ketubah* obligation stands: *akula milta* refers merely to the base amount.

### Tosafos Innovation

*Tosafos* presses the question further: if the ruling is only that she receives the base statutory amount, what is the *chiddush* of the *mishnah*? Any Jewish wife, even without a prior document of any kind, is entitled to the base amount by Rabbinic enactment. Why do we need the *mishnah* to tell us that a convert whose wife converted with him gets the standard hundred or two hundred *zuz*?

*Tosafos* offers a brilliant resolution. The *chiddush*, they explain, lies in the determination of which base amount she is entitled to. The *mishnah* is teaching: *de-ashmianu heicha de-nisa besulah, af al gav de-chasha gadla ve-nitgayera ke-she-hi be'ulah, afilu hachi yesh lah ketubah matayim* — we are taught about a case where they married when she was a *besulah*, and now after conversion she is a *be'ulah*. The question is whether she receives two hundred *zuz* as a *besulah* or one hundred as an *almanah*. The *mishnah* teaches that since they were first married when she was a *besulah*, that original context defines the marriage, and she therefore receives the full two hundred *zuz* — even though by the time of conversion she is no longer a *besulah*. The same logic applies in the case of the *katan*: the woman might now be a *be'ulah* at the time the obligation becomes binding, yet because the original marriage was to a *besulah*, that classification governs.

One sharp-eyed observer noted that *Tosafos*' reading carries a significant subtext: by assuming that the woman is still characterized as a *besulah* based on the context of the original non-Jewish marriage, the *mishnah* is implicitly attributing to that non-Jewish couple a standard of *tzniut* that one might not readily assume. The *chiddush* cuts against any casual assumption about the moral comportment of non-Jewish society.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָדְרַן עֲלָךְ הַכּוֹתֵב לְאִשְׁתּוֹ
>
> **Translation:** May we return to you chapter "One who writes to his wife."

The *Gemara* concludes: *Hadran alach kotev le-ishto* — and with that, the ninth *perek* comes to a close.

## Estate Claims

### Wife Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁהָיָה נָשׂוּי שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, וָמֵת — הָרִאשׁוֹנָה קוֹדֶמֶת לַשְּׁנִיָּה. וְיוֹרְשֵׁי הָרִאשׁוֹנָה קוֹדְמִין לְיוֹרְשֵׁי שְׁנִיָּה. נָשָׂא אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וָמֵתָה, נָשָׂא שְׁנִיָּה וּמֵת הוּא — שְׁנִיָּה וְיוֹרְשֶׁיהָ קוֹדְמִין לְיוֹרְשֵׁי הָרִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who was married to two women and died, the first** woman he married **precedes the second** in collecting the payment specified in her marriage contract if there are insufficient funds to pay both, because her document is dated earlier. So too, if the wives died after their husband before they received payment for their marriage contracts, **the heirs of the first** wife **precede the heirs of the second** wife in collecting these payments. If **he married a first** woman **and she** subsequently **died,** and he then **married a second** woman **and he** subsequently **died, the second** wife **and her heirs precede the heirs of the first** wife. This is because the marriage contract of the second wife is considered a debt that the estate of the deceased is required to pay, whereas the claim of the heirs of the first wife is based on the stipulation in the marriage contract that male children inherit their mother's marriage contract. Heirs receive their share of the estate only from property that remains after all debts have been settled.

The tenth *perek* opens with a new *mishnah*: *mi she-hayah nasui shtei nashim* — a man married to two wives who then died. The *mishnah* addresses the competing claims against the estate. *Ha-rishonah kodemes la-shniyah* — the first wife has priority over the second. The two wives are positioned as creditors against the estate, and the earlier creditor has the right to collect first. If the estate is insufficient, the second wife receives only what remains.

The same principle extends to the heirs: *ve-yorshei ha-rishonah kodmin le-yorshei ha-shniyah* — the heirs of the first wife take priority over the heirs of the second. The *mishnah* then describes a more complex scenario: *nasa es ha-rishonah u-mesah, nasa shniyah u-mes hu* — he married the first wife and she died; he then married the second wife and he died. In this case, *shniyah ve-yorsheha kodmin le-yorshei rishonah* — the second wife and her heirs come first, before the heirs of the first wife.

The reasoning reflects the distinction between debt and inheritance. The second wife (*Leah*, in our example) is a creditor of the estate: her husband died before her, and her *ketubah* obligation — say ten thousand and two hundred *zuz* — was never discharged. She or her heirs come to collect that sum as a debt owed to her. The heirs of the first wife (*Rachel*), by contrast, are not creditors; they are coming by virtue of the principle of *kesubas benin dichrin* — the rabbinic institution by which the male sons of a wife who predeceased their father take out their mother's *ketubah* amount from the estate as an inheritance before the estate is divided generally. Since creditors take precedence over heirs, Leah and her heirs collect first, and only thereafter do Rachel's heirs receive their special inheritance.

### Creditor Priority & Seizure Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִדְּקָתָנֵי ״הָרִאשׁוֹנָה קוֹדֶמֶת לַשְּׁנִיָּה״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״הָרִאשׁוֹנָה יֵשׁ לָהּ וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה אֵין לָהּ״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִי קָדְמָה שְׁנִיָּה וְתָפְסָה — לָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינַּהּ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בַּעַל חוֹב מְאוּחָר שֶׁקָּדַם וְגָבָה — מַה שֶּׁגָּבָה גָּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From** the fact **that it teaches: The first** woman he married **precedes the second** in collecting the payment of her marriage contract, **and it does not teach** simply that **the first** woman **has** the right to receive payment of her marriage contract **and the second does not have** that right, the mishna thereby teaches **by inference that if the second preceded** the first **and seized** property in payment of her marriage contract, **we do not expropriate it from her,** because her rights to the property are not completely canceled. The Gemara suggests: **Learn from** the mishna the following principle: In the case of **a creditor** holding a promissory note dated **later** than the notes of other creditors **who preceded** the other creditors **and collected** his debt, **whatever he collected, he has collected,** and it is not expropriated from him even if the debtor does not have the means to pay back all his creditors.

The *Gemara* examines the precise language of the *mishnah* — *ha-rishonah kodemes la-shniyah*, the first comes before the second — and asks whether this phrasing carries implications about what happens when the second wife does not wait her turn. The *Gemara* draws an inference: since the *mishnah* uses the language of *priority* rather than saying outright that the second wife has no claim at all, it may be implying that if the second wife seized the property before the first, she is permitted to keep it. From this the *Gemara* formulates a broader principle: *ba'al chov me'uchar she-kadam ve-gava, mah she-gava gava* — a later creditor who jumped ahead and seized property is entitled to retain what was seized.

### Tosafos Objection & Social Policy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: מָה שֶּׁגָּבָה — לֹא גָּבָה, וּמַאי ״קוֹדֶמֶת״ — לִגְמָרֵי קָתָנֵי, כְּדִתְנַן: בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **Actually, I will say to you** that **what he collected, he has not collected,** i.e., he must restore the property to the debtor so that the latter can pay the other creditors. **And what** does the mishna mean when it teaches that the first wife **precedes** the second? It **teaches** that the first wife **completely** precedes the second and is granted exclusive rights to collect the payment of her marriage contract. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 115a): **A son precedes a daughter** in matters of inheritance. Were she to come first and take part of the inheritance, it would not become hers; the son completely precedes her, so that in cases where there is a male heir, the daughter receives nothing. The same understanding of the word precedes applies in this matter as well.

*Tosafos* raises a powerful objection to this principle: *teima mah sevara hi zo, di-lo yehei ba'al chov ela ke-loke'ah* — what is the logic here? Let the later creditor be treated no differently than a purchaser. And we know that if someone purchased property *after* the earlier creditor's lien was established, the earlier creditor may foreclose on that property and take it even from the buyer who paid full value. If a creditor with earlier liens can take property from a bona fide purchaser, why should he not be able to take it from a later creditor who seized it? The later creditor's claim is, if anything, weaker than that of an innocent purchaser.

*Tosafos* quotes the *Rashbam*'s answer: *ba'al chov me'uchar adif mi-loke'ah* — in this specific context, the later creditor who seizes actually has a stronger position than a purchaser would, because of a social policy consideration. The principle is *shelo tin'ol delet bifnei lovin* — we must not close the door before borrowers. If we were to hold that a later creditor who seizes property must always yield to an earlier creditor regardless of circumstances, the result would be that no one would lend money to a person who already has outstanding debts. By preserving at least the possibility that a later creditor who acts quickly may retain what he seizes, we keep open the incentive for continued lending.

*Tosafos* then grapples with whether this answer is fully satisfying: if a later creditor can seize and keep, would that not instead discourage the *earlier* creditor from lending, knowing that someone behind him in line might race ahead? The response is that each potential lender assumes he will be the one to act first. But *Tosafos* acknowledges the underlying tension honestly: the policy involves a genuine unfairness, and the question of where to draw the line — between protecting prior lienholders and keeping credit markets functioning — is genuinely difficult. What is notable is that the *Rashbam* explicitly adds *kesubas isha mishum china* — the wife's *ketubah* is included in this policy — indicating that the same principle extends to the competing claims of two wives against a single estate.

### No Permanent Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מִדְּלָא קָתָנֵי ״אִם קָדְמָה שְׁנִיָּה וְתָפְסָה אֵין מוֹצִיאִין מִיָּדָהּ״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִי קָדְמָה שְׁנִיָּה וְתָפְסָה — מַפְּקִינַן מִינַּהּ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בַּעַל חוֹב מְאוּחָר שֶׁקָּדַם וְגָבָה — מַה שֶּׁגָּבָה לֹא גָּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that the discussion was as follows: **From the fact that it does not teach: If the second** wife **preceded** the first wife **and seized** property **it is not expropriated from her,** it proves **by inference that if the second** wife **preceded** the first **and seized** property as payment for her marriage contract, **we do appropriate it from her.** The Gemara suggests: **Learn from** the mishna the following rule: In the case of **a creditor** holding a promissory note dated **later** than the notes of other creditors **who preceded** the other creditors **and collected** his debt, **whatever he collected, he has not collected,** i.e., it is expropriated from him.

The *Gemara* ultimately declines to resolve the question from the language of the *mishnah* in either direction. One reading argues that *kodemes* — comes first — means fully excludes, and the second wife has no claim at all; if she were to seize property first, it would be taken from her. Another reading argues from the same word that she merely has a lower priority but is not entirely excluded, and seizure by the later party is valid. Each direction of inference is tested and found inconclusive, as the *Gemara* shows that the *mishnah*'s language can be explained away in both readings. The question of whether *mah she-gava gava* is resolved one way or the other thus remains without a definitive proof from this *mishnah*.

## Benin Dichrin

### Classic Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשָׂא אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וָמֵתָה, נָשָׂא שְׁנִיָּה וּמֵת הוּא — שְׁנִיָּה וְיוֹרְשֶׁיהָ קוֹדְמִין לְיוֹרְשֵׁי הָרִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **he married a first** woman **and she** subsequently **died,** and he then **married a second** woman **and he** subsequently **died, the second** wife **and her heirs precede the heirs of the first** wife. This is because the marriage contract of the second wife is considered a debt that the estate of the deceased is required to pay, whereas the claim of the heirs of the first wife is based on the stipulation in the marriage contract that male children inherit their mother's marriage contract. Heirs receive their share of the estate only from property that remains after all debts have been settled.

The *mishnah* then sets out the classic scenario: *nasa es ha-rishonah u-mesah, nasa shniyah u-mes hu* — he married the first wife (*Rachel*) and she died; he married the second wife (*Leah*) and then he died. To give the facts concrete form: Rachel brought a large dowry and received a *tosefes* commensurate with her family's wealth, so that her *ketubah* totaled twenty thousand *zuz* (the base amount plus fifteen thousand in dowry plus an additional *tosefes* sum). Leah's *ketubah* totaled ten thousand and two hundred *zuz*.

### Principle Defined & Three Inferences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ נָשָׂא אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַחַת בְּחַיָּיו וְאַחַת בְּמוֹתוֹ יֵשׁ לָהֶן כְּתוּבַּת בְּנִין דִּכְרִין, וְלָא חָיְשִׁינַן לְאִינְּצוֹיֵי. מִמַּאי — מִדְּקָתָנֵי: שְׁנִיָּה וְיוֹרְשֶׁיהָ קוֹדְמִים לְיוֹרְשֵׁי רִאשׁוֹנָה, מִיקְדָּם הוּא דְּקָדְמִי, הָא אִיכָּא — שָׁקְלִי. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כְּתוּבָּה נַעֲשֵׂית מוֹתָר לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ. מִמַּאי? מִדְּלָא קָתָנֵי ״אִם יֵשׁ שָׁם מוֹתַר דִּינָר״. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כְּתוּבַּת בְּנִין דִּכְרִין לָא טָרְפָה מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין טָרְפָה מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי — לֵיתוֹ בְּנֵי רִאשׁוֹנָה וְלִטְרְפִינְהוּ לִבְנֵי שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: If **he married the first** woman, etc. The Gemara notes: **Conclude three** conclusions **from this** statement: **Conclude from it** that if **one** of the man's wives died **in his lifetime and** the other **one** died following **his death,** then the sons of the first wife **are entitled to** collect the **marriage contract concerning male children and we are not concerned** that this would lead to **quarreling.** The Gemara asks: **From where** is it known that this is correct? **From** the fact **that it teaches:** The **second** wife **and her heirs precede the heirs of the first** wife, it can be inferred that **they precede** the heirs of the first, **but** if **there are** enough funds in the estate for all the claims against it, then the children of the first wife do **take** their share of the dowry. **The second point one can conclude from it is** that **one marriage contract becomes surplus for the other.** The Sages ruled that each son may claim his mother's marriage settlement only when the value of the estate exceeds the sum total of the marriage contracts by at least one dinar, so that the biblical laws of inheritance can be fulfilled. Since the marriage settlement collected by the heirs of the second wife is considered a debt owed by the estate, this sum is considered to have been paid equally by all the heirs. The biblical laws of inheritance have thereby been fulfilled, and the sons of the first wife can claim the marriage contract concerning male children even if nothing will be left in the estate after they have collected their payment. **And conclude from it** a third point, that when one collects the payment for **the marriage contract concerning male children, he cannot seize liened** property that his father sold to others, as one can when collecting a debt. **As, if it should enter your mind that it can be repossessed from liened** property, then **let the sons of the first** wife **come and repossess** land already claimed **by the sons of the second** wife as payment for their mother's marriage contract, since the land the sons of the second wife took was previously liened, due to the marriage contract of the first wife. Rather, the children of the first wife are viewed not as creditors but as heirs, who cannot repossess property sold by their father.

When Rachel died, her husband inherited her estate. But when Reuven subsequently died, Rachel's sons by him come forward not as ordinary heirs but under the principle of *kesubas benin dichrin*: the male heirs of a wife who predeceased her husband are entitled to take out the full amount of their mother's *ketubah* from the estate as their inheritance, before the remainder of the estate is divided equally among all heirs. This ensures that the sons of the wife with the larger dowry receive the benefit of what their mother brought into the marriage, rather than seeing it absorbed into an undifferentiated pool to be shared with the children of another wife.

At the same time, Leah — or, if she has since died, her heirs — comes to collect her *ketubah* of ten thousand and two hundred *zuz* as a *creditor*. She was widowed when Reuven died, and her *ketubah* is an unpaid debt against the estate. As a creditor, Leah's claim is satisfied before any portion of the estate passes to heirs. Rachel's children, by contrast, are receiving their mother's share as an *inheritance*, not as a debt.

The *mishnah*'s ruling — that Leah's heirs come before Rachel's heirs — thus reflects the fundamental rule that a creditor's claim is satisfied prior to any division among heirs.

The *Gemara* proceeds to draw three inferences from this *mishnah*: *shema mina telat*.

### Inference One

The first inference: *shema mina achas be-chayav ve-achas be-moso, ve-yesh lahen kesubas benin dichrin* — even when one set of heirs derives their claim from a wife who died during the husband's lifetime and the other set derives their claim from a death that occurred at or after his death, *kesubas benin dichrin* still applies to the former group, and we are not concerned about fights — *velo chaishinan le-intzuyei*.

This inference requires explanation. In the *classic* formulation of *kesubas benin dichrin*, both wives predeceased the husband, and all heirs are on an equal footing: Rachel's boys take their mother's chunk, Leah's boys take their mother's chunk, and whatever remains is divided. In that symmetric case, while one set may receive more than the other, both are operating under the same institutional framework and neither has grounds to object in principle.

Here the situation is asymmetric. Leah is coming as a *creditor* — her ten thousand *zuz* is a debt. Rachel's children, by contrast, are coming as *heirs* under a *rabbinic* institution. Leah's children might credibly object: our mother collected her debt, which is fine — but why do you get a special Rabbinic inheritance before the rest of the estate is divided? Our mother didn't die before father, so we don't get *kesubas benin dichrin*; you get a special chunk and we get nothing extra. That seems unfair, and one might fear that this perceived unfairness would generate conflict and litigation — *intzuyei* — severe enough that the Rabbis would have declined to apply *kesubas benin dichrin* in such a case.

The *Gemara* rules that we do not have that concern. Rachel's children receive their *kesubas benin dichrin* even when Leah's heirs are coming as creditors. The asymmetry in the basis of the respective claims does not deprive the *benin dichrin* of their entitlement.

### Ketubah as Leftover & Mosar Dinar Rule

The second inference: *kesubah na'asis mosar le-chaverta* — a *ketubah* collection is considered as though it were the "leftover" (*mosar*) for the other *ketubah*. This requires context.

The standard rule of *kesubas benin dichrin* includes a critical limitation: the institution applies only when there remains in the estate at least one *dinar* (*mosar dinar*) beyond what is disbursed under *benin dichrin*, available for division according to the ordinary rules of Biblical inheritance. This *mosar dinar* need not be large — a symbolic amount above a *perutah* suffices — but it must exist. The rationale is that *kesubas benin dichrin* is a Rabbinic enactment, and the Rabbis did not intend to override *yerusha de-Oraisa* entirely: if applying the Rabbinic institution would leave nothing whatsoever for a division according to Biblical rules, the Rabbinic institution is set aside and the estate is divided equally among all heirs. Rachel's children, who had a large *ketubah* behind them, would in that case receive far less — but the Biblical entitlement to an undivided inheritance takes precedence.

The question the second inference addresses is this: suppose the entire estate is, say, twenty thousand *zuz*. Rachel's children want their twenty thousand as *benin dichrin*, and Leah's heirs come and take ten thousand as creditors. After Leah's heirs collect, only ten thousand remains. Is there a *mosar dinar* to support *kesubas benin dichrin* for Rachel's children, or have they been left with nothing for Biblical division?

The *Gemara* reasons that the very payment of Leah's *ketubah* debt *is* the functional equivalent of the *mosar dinar*. When a creditor holds a lien against an estate, the estate's assets conceptually include whatever is needed to satisfy that debt. Even if after the creditor is paid there is nothing left for equal division, the estate *had* money that was deployed to pay the creditor — and that payment is as if the money existed as surplus, was used to discharge a legitimate obligation of the estate, and thus satisfies the requirement that something existed beyond the *benin dichrin* entitlements. The critical formulation is: if, absent any creditors, the estate would have had just slightly more than the *benin dichrin* claims, then the fact that a creditor absorbed that surplus does not deprive Rachel's children of *kesubas benin dichrin*, because the surplus was there — it simply went to pay a legitimate debt.

The *Gemara* supports this by noting that the *mishnah* does not condition its ruling on the presence of a *mosar dinar* beyond the *benin dichrin* distributions. Since it does not mention this requirement, it must be that the creditor payment itself satisfies it.

### Inference Three

The third inference: *kesubas benin dichrin lo tarfei mi-meshabdei* — heirs collecting under *kesubas benin dichrin* do not collect from encumbered property, i.e., property that had already been sold or pledged. This distinguishes their status from that of creditors, who *do* hold liens against the debtor's prior encumbered assets and may foreclose on property sold after the lien was established.

The *Gemara* derives this from the structure of the *mishnah*. If Rachel's children were collecting as creditors — as the delayed collection of their mother's unpaid *ketubah* — then, because Rachel's *ketubah* predates Leah's *ketubah* in time, Rachel's children would have first priority even over Leah's children. A creditor with earlier liens collects before a creditor with later liens. But the *mishnah* rules that Leah's children come *first*. The only way to reconcile this is to conclude that Rachel's children are not creditors at all — they are *heirs*, receiving under the Rabbinic institution of *kesubas benin dichrin*. Since they are heirs rather than creditors, they cannot reach encumbered property, and when creditors (like Leah's heirs) compete with heirs, the creditors win.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ אַחַת בְּחַיָּיו וְאַחַת בְּמוֹתוֹ אֵין לָהֶן כְּתוּבַּת בְּנִין דִּכְרִין, וּמַאי ״קוֹדְמִין״ — לְנַחֲלָה קָתָנֵי. וְכִי תֵּימָא: ״יוֹרְשֵׁי הָרִאשׁוֹנָה״ לְמָה לִי? אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא ״שְׁנִיָּה וְיוֹרְשֶׁיהָ״, תְּנָא נָמֵי ״לְיוֹרְשֵׁי הָרִאשׁוֹנָה״. וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ: כְּתוּבָּה נַעֲשֵׂית מוֹתָר לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ — דִּלְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: אֵין כְּתוּבָּה נַעֲשֵׂית מוֹתָר לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ, וְהָכָא הוּא דְּאִיכָּא מוֹתַר דִּינָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi objects to** two of the three conclusions stated above: **From where** is it known that all of this is correct? **Perhaps I** could **actually say to you** that if **one** wife died **in his lifetime and one** died following **his death,** then **no one is entitled to** collect the **marriage contract concerning male children. And what does** the mishna mean when it says **precede?** It does not mean that if there are enough assets remaining, the sons of the first wife receive the sum of their mother's marriage settlement. Rather, **it is teaching** that after the sons of the second wife receive the sum of their mother's marriage settlement, the sons from both marriages **inherit** equal shares of the remaining estate. **And if you would say** that if the mishna is referring to the inheritance of the remainder of the estate, **why do I** need the mishna to mention **the heirs of the first** wife; since it is teaching a *halakha* concerning their inheritance from their father and not their inheritance from their mother, why refer to them as the heirs of the first wife? One could reply that **since it taught: The second** wife **and her heirs,** the mishna **also taught** the parallel phrase: **The heirs of the first** wife, but no halakhic conclusions should be drawn from this. **And concerning what you said** that **one marriage contract becomes surplus for the other,** this too can be rejected: **Perhaps I** could **actually say to you** that **one marriage contract does not become surplus for the other, and** that the case under discussion **here is where there is a surplus of** an additional **dinar,** and the reason why it was not explicitly mentioned is because it is not the subject of our mishna.

Rav Ashi challenges all three inferences. His opening point: *dilma l'olam eima lach, achas be-chayav ve-achas be-moso, ein chan kesubas benin dichrin* — perhaps I would say that in a case where one wife's claim arises from events during the husband's lifetime and the other's arises from events at his death, there is in fact *no* application of *kesubas benin dichrin* at all. The first inference would then be invalid.

And if so, *u-mai kodmin, l'nachala katani* — what does the *mishnah*'s statement that Leah's heirs "come first" mean? Perhaps it means only that they come first in terms of the *nachala*, the inheritance — i.e., they collect their creditor's share first, and then the remaining estate is divided equally among all heirs. Rachel's children would receive their proportional share of what remains, but not any special *kesubas benin dichrin* entitlement above and beyond that. The word *yorshei rishonah* — the heirs of the first wife — appears in the *mishnah* only because the earlier clause mentioned Leah's heirs (*shniyah ve-yorsheha*), and the *mishnah* used parallel language for Rachel's children without meaning to indicate that they have any special status.

Rav Ashi then challenges the second inference as well. The claim was that a creditor payment counts as the *mosar dinar* to support *kesubas benin dichrin*. Rav Ashi says: *dilma l'olam eima lach, ein kesubah na'asis mosar le-chaverta* — perhaps the *ketubah* payment to one wife does not serve as the *mosar* for the other's *benin dichrin* entitlement. You would need a genuine *mosar dinar* — an actual dollar remaining in the estate after all *benin dichrin* entitlements are distributed — and if that dollar was consumed by the creditor, it is gone. Perhaps the *mishnah* is simply operating in a case where there *was* a *mosar dinar* remaining after Leah collected and Rachel's children received their share — and the *mishnah* simply did not spell this out because it was assumed to be present in the background facts.

In sum, Rav Ashi argues that the two inferences that strengthen Rachel's children's position — that *kesubas benin dichrin* applies even in this asymmetric scenario, and that a creditor payment counts as the *mosar dinar* — are not compelled by the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* could be read as assuming, without stating explicitly, that the conditions for those inferences are met, rather than proving that those conditions are unnecessary. The *peshut* of the *mishnah* still favors the first reading, but Rav Ashi's point is that neither inference is *muchrach* — forced by the text — and the matter will require further analysis.
