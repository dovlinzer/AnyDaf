# Makkot 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Roads to Arei Miklat

### Tanya: Pathways

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקְלָט״ הָיָה כָּתוּב עַל פָּרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים, כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּכִּיר הָרוֹצֵחַ וְיִפְנֶה לְשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **Refuge was written on** signs at every **crossroads** marking the path to a city of refuge, **so that the** unintentional **murderer would identify** the route to the city of refuge **and turn to** go **there.**

The daf opens at the very last line of 11a with a *tanya* touching on the infrastructure surrounding the *arei miklat*. The *mishnah* had spoken of paths interconnecting the various cities of refuge — but what is truly needed is not merely roads running between one *ir miklat* and another, but roads leading from every point in the land toward these cities. All roads, in other words, should lead to the *arei miklat*.

The *tanu rabbanan* that follows makes this concrete.

### R. Elazar b. Yaakov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מַאי קְרָא? ״תָּכִין לְךָ הַדֶּרֶךְ״ – עֲשֵׂה [לְךָ] הֲכָנָה לַדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said: What is the verse** from which this is derived? **"Prepare for you the road"** (Deuteronomy 19:3), meaning: **Perform for you preparation of the road.**

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov teaches: *miklat haya katuv al haparashat drachim* — the word *miklat* was written at every crossroads throughout the country. Whenever a traveler arrived at a fork in the road, a sign indicated which way to turn in order to reach the nearest city of refuge. The stated rationale is *kedai sheyakir rotzeach viyifneh lishama* — so that the killer would be able to see and turn in the right direction. It is worth pausing on the language: the person who killed unintentionally is nonetheless consistently referred to as *rotzeach*, a striking usage that runs throughout this entire section of the *gemara*.

### Tachin Lecha Haderech

Rav Kahana then asks: *mai kra?* — what is the scriptural basis for this obligation to signpost the roads? The answer is *tachin lecha haderech* — "prepare the way for yourself" (Deuteronomy 19:3). The *derasha* reads this as: *aseh lecha hachanah laderech*, make the way prepared and direct so that the fleeing killer can reach the city with ease. This verse speaks not only to the physical maintenance of roads but to every form of advance preparation that facilitates the journey.

### Rav Chama Petichta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא פָּתַח לַהּ פִּתְחָא לְהַאי פָּרַשְׁתָּא מֵהָכָא: ״טוֹב וְיָשָׁר ה׳ עַל כֵּן יוֹרֶה חַטָּאִים בַּדָּרֶךְ״ – אִם לַחַטָּאִים יוֹרֶה, קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַצַּדִּיקִים.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos that *halakha*, the Gemara cites that **Rav Ḥama bar Ḥanina introduced this portion** with regard to the *halakhot* of exile **with an introduction from here: "Good and upright is God; therefore He directs sinners along the way"** (Psalms 25:8). He said: **If He directs sinners** by commanding the placing of signs directing them to the city of refuge, it may be inferred ***a fortiori*** that He will assist and direct **the righteous** along the path of righteousness.

Rav Chama bar Chanina would open his *petichta* — his homiletical introduction — to the *parasha* of *arei miklat* from the verse *tov veyashar Hashem al ken yoreh chataim baderech*: "God is good and just, and therefore He will show sinners the path" (Psalms 25:8). The classical form of Rabbinic *midrash*, particularly as one encounters it throughout *Bereishit Rabbah* and *Shemot Rabbah*, works by beginning from a verse that appears distant from the topic and then weaving one's way toward the core passage — much as one might open a *derashah* on *Parshat Toldot* with a verse from *Mishlei* and then gradually connect it to the narrative of Avraham and Yitzchak.

Here the connection is pointed: the person heading to the *ir miklat* has sinned — for the death was not a pure accident, since some negligence was involved, and this is why the *gemara* calls him *rotzeach*. And yet God desires to create the paths that will protect him and help him arrive safely. The *drasha* concludes: *im lachataim yoreh, kal vachomer latzadikim* — if God guides sinners on the path, how much more so the righteous.

### Reish Lakish Petichta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ פָּתַח לַהּ פִּתְחָא לְהַאי פָּרַשְׁתָּא מֵהָכָא: ״וַאֲשֶׁר לֹא צָדָה וְהָאֱלֹהִים אִנָּה לְיָדוֹ וְגוֹ׳״, ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יֹאמַר מְשַׁל הַקַּדְמֹנִי מֵרְשָׁעִים יֵצֵא רֶשַׁע וְגוֹ׳״,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish introduced this portion with an introduction from here:** It is stated with regard to an unintentional murderer: **"And one who did not lie in wait, but God caused it to come to his hand,** and I will appoint you a place where he may flee" (Exodus 21:13). Now this is puzzling. Why would God cause one to sin in this manner? The verse states: **"As the ancient parable says: From the wicked comes forth wickedness"** (I Samuel 24:13). Evil incidents befall those who have already sinned.

Reish Lakish would open his *petichta* differently. Rather than beginning from a distant verse, he jumps directly into the *parasha* itself: *va'asher lo tzadah veha'elokim inah leyado* — "and he did not intend it, yet God delivered him into his hand" (Exodus 21:13). He then weaves in the verse *ka'asher yomar hamashal hakadmoni meirsha'im yeitzei resha* — "as the ancient proverb says, from wicked people comes evil."

### Divine Causation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמָּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר – בִּשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁהָרְגוּ אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ, אֶחָד הָרַג בְּשׁוֹגֵג וְאֶחָד הָרַג בְּמֵזִיד, לָזֶה אֵין עֵדִים וְלָזֶה אֵין עֵדִים. הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַזְמִינָן לְפוּנְדָּק אֶחָד, זֶה שֶׁהָרַג בְּמֵזִיד יוֹשֵׁב תַּחַת הַסּוּלָּם, וְזֶה שֶׁהָרַג בְּשׁוֹגֵג יוֹרֵד בַּסּוּלָּם, וְנֹפֵל עָלָיו וְהֹרְגוֹ, זֶה שֶׁהָרַג בְּמֵזִיד – נֶהֱרָג, וְזֶה שֶׁהָרַג בְּשׁוֹגֵג – גּוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish explains: In this light, the verse "But God caused it to come to his hand" may be understood. **With regard to what** scenario **is the verse speaking?** It is **with regard to two people who killed a person,** where **one killed unintentionally** while **the other killed intentionally. For this** person **there are no witnesses** to his action, **and for that** person **there are no witnesses** to his action; therefore, neither received the appropriate punishment of exile and execution, respectively. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, summons them to one inn. This** person **who killed intentionally sits beneath a ladder, and that** person **who killed unintentionally descends the ladder, and** he **falls upon him and kills him.** There were witnesses to that incident and therefore, **that** person **who killed intentionally is killed, and that** person **who killed unintentionally is exiled,** each receiving what he deserved.

The difficulty that Reish Lakish identifies is one of profound theological weight. The verse *va'asher lo tzadah veha'elokim inah leyado* seems to say that God caused this death to happen — that the killer was merely God's *kli*, His instrument. If so, why should he be exiled at all?

The answer lies in the other verse: *meirshaim yeitzei resha* — evil comes from wicked people. Even if God is orchestrating the events, this person was already culpable in some respect, and the exile is the appropriate consequence for that culpability.

The *gemara* explains the scenario: *bishnei bnei adam shehargu et hanefesh* — two people who killed: one intentionally (*bemeizid*) and one unintentionally (*bishogeg*), and neither had witnesses. Both escaped legal consequence. So what does God do? *Hakadosh Baruch Hu mazminam lefundak echad* — He arranges that they both end up at the same inn. The intentional killer sits beneath a ladder; the unintentional killer descends the ladder and falls on him, killing him. Justice is served on both accounts: *zeh sheharag bemeizid neherag* — the one who murdered deliberately is now killed as he deserved, and *zeh sheharag bishogeg goleh* — the one who killed accidentally is now sent into exile as he deserved. God orchestrated the outcome, but the result maps precisely onto what justice required for each person from the start.

A student pointed out that the verse *meirshaim yeitzei resha* appears in the context of the story of David and Saul — specifically, David's declaration that he would not kill Saul even when he had the opportunity. That context, centered on restraint from murder, makes the verse especially apt here.

### God's Guidance Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִן הַתּוֹרָה וּמִן הַנְּבִיאִים וּמִן הַכְּתוּבִים – בַּדֶּרֶךְ שֶׁאָדָם רוֹצֶה לֵילֵךְ בָּהּ – מוֹלִיכִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the path upon which God leads people, the Gemara cites a statement that **Rabba bar Rav Huna says** that **Rav Huna says, and some say** it was a statement that **Rav Huna says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: From the Torah, from the Prophets, and from the Writings** one learns that **along the path a person wishes to proceed, one leads** and assists **him.**

Rabbah bar Rav Huna said in the name of Rav Huna — and some say Rav Huna said it in the name of Rav Elazar — that from the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings we learn: *bederech she'adam rotzeh lalechet bah molichin oto* — in the path that a person wishes to walk, Heaven leads him.

## Sources for Choice

### Bilaam Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַתּוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תֵלֵךְ עִמָּהֶם״, וּכְתִיב: ״קוּם לֵךְ אִתָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** One learns this **from the Torah, as it is written** that initially God said to Balaam with regard to the contingent dispatched by Balak: **"You shall not go with them"** (Numbers 22:12). After Balaam implored Him and indicated his desire to go with them, **it is written: "Arise, go with them"** (Numbers 22:20).

From the Torah: by Bilaam, God first says *lo telech imahem* — do not go with them. But Bilaam keeps pressing, and eventually God says *kum lech itam* — arise and go with them. Because he persisted, God accommodated his desire. This is the principle of *bederech she'adam rotzeh lalechet*: if you insist on going, God will not stand in your way.

### Isaiah Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַנְּבִיאִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲנִי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ מְלַמֶּדְךָ לְהוֹעִיל מַדְרִיכְךָ בְּדֶרֶךְ (זוּ) תֵּלֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** One learns this **from the Prophets, as it is written: "I am the Lord your God, Who teaches you for your profit, Who leads you on the path that you go"** (Isaiah 48:17), indicating that along the path that one seeks to go, God will direct him.

From the Prophets: *ani Hashem Elohecha melamedcha leho'il madrichcha bederech telech* — "I am the Lord your God who teaches you for your benefit, who guides you in the path that you will walk" (Isaiah 48:17). The verse is read not as "the path you *should* walk" but as "the path you *will* walk" — the path you have already chosen. Even if that path leads toward wrongdoing, God will guide you along it. A student raised the question of *lifnei iver* — does facilitating another's sin not itself constitute a prohibition? The response is that the principle here is primarily one of *non-interference*: God does not compel a person against his chosen course.

### Proverbs Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַכְּתוּבִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם לַלֵּצִים הוּא יָלִיץ וְלַעֲנָוִים יִתֶּן חֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** One learns this **from the Writings, as it is written: "If one seeks the cynics, He will cause him to join the cynics, but to the humble He will give grace"** (Proverbs 3:34), indicating that if one chooses cynicism God will direct him there and if he opts for humility God will grant him grace.

From the Writings: *im laleitzim hu yalitz vela'anavim yitein chein* — "if with mockers, He will mock, but to the humble He grants grace" (Proverbs 3:34). Rashi understands *im laleitzim hu yalitz* to mean: if someone chooses to be a mocker, to pursue wrongdoing, God will make it possible — *ein me'akvin beyado*, He will not obstruct him.

The broader thematic point is clear and ties back to the opening of the daf: *tachin lecha haderech* — preparing the road to the *arei miklat* is part of this same divine principle of enabling a person to pursue the path he has chosen, here the path toward refuge and life. But the same divine dynamic operates in the inverse direction as well: God will not block the path of one who chooses wickedness.

## Protection En Route

### Rav Huna's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: רוֹצֵחַ שֶׁגָּלָה לְעִיר מִקְלָט וּמְצָאוֹ גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם וַהֲרָגוֹ – פָּטוּר. קָסָבַר: ״וְלוֹ אֵין מִשְׁפַּט מָוֶת״ – בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם הוּא דִּכְתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes its discussion of the *halakhot* of exile. **Rav Huna says:** In the case of an unintentional **murderer who was exiled to a city of refuge, and the blood redeemer found him** on the way **and killed him,** he is **exempt.** The Gemara notes: **Rav Huna holds** that the verse: "Lest the blood redeemer pursue the murderer…and strike him fatally…**and for him there is no sentence of death,** as he did not hate him from before" (Deuteronomy 19:6), **is written with regard to the blood redeemer,** teaching that the blood redeemer is not liable to be executed for killing the murderer.

Rav Huna states: *rotzeach shegalah le'ir miklat umatz'o goel hadam vehargo, patur* — if a manslayer is fleeing to the *ir miklat* and the *goel hadam* intercepts and kills him along the way, the *goel hadam* is exempt. The manslayer is not legally protected until he actually arrives within the city.

The reasoning is based on the verse *pen yirdof goel hadam acharei harotzeach ki yeicham levavo vehisigo vehargo baderech velo ein mishpat mavet* — lest the *goel hadam* pursue the killer, and when his heart is hot overtake him and kill him on the road, and there would be no judgment of death (Deuteronomy 19:6). Rav Huna reads *velo ein mishpat mavet* as referring to the *goel hadam*: if he kills the fleeing man before he reaches the city, the *goel hadam* bears no capital liability. This is precisely why the Torah commands *tachin lecha haderech* — you must prepare the road and ensure the manslayer can reach the *ir miklat* as quickly as possible, because until he arrives he is exposed.

### Textual Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״וְלוֹ אֵין מִשְׁפַּט מָוֶת״ – בָּרוֹצֵחַ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בָּרוֹצֵחַ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהוּא לֹא שֹׂנֵא לוֹ מִתְּמוֹל שִׁלְשׁוֹם״ – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר בָּרוֹצֵחַ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav Huna from a *baraita*: **"And for him there is no sentence of death"; the verse is speaking with regard to the** unintentional **murderer,** teaching that the unintentional murderer is not liable to be executed. That is why the Jewish people were commanded to establish cities of refuge to protect him. The *baraita* proceeds to prove that the verse is written with regard to the murderer. **Do you say** that it is speaking **with regard to the** unintentional **murderer, or** is it speaking **only with regard to the blood redeemer? When it states** in an earlier verse: **"And he did not hate him from before"** (Deuteronomy 19:4), it is clear that the reference is to the unintentional murderer, and therefore, **you must say** that in the phrase: "And for him there is no sentence of death," **the verse is speaking with regard to the** unintentional **murderer.**

A *meitivei* is raised from a *braita*: *velo ein mishpat mavet, berotzeach hakatuv medaber* — this verse is talking about the killer himself, not the *goel hadam*. The *braita* argues: you might think it refers to the *goel hadam*, but when the verse adds *ki lo sonei hu lo mitmol shilshom* — "for he did not hate him previously" — that phrase clearly describes the killer, establishing that his act was unintentional. Therefore *velo ein mishpat mavet* in the prior clause is also about the killer, meaning he himself does not deserve the death penalty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְלוֹ אֵין מִשְׁפַּט מָוֶת״ – בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בָּרוֹצֵחַ? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי לֹא שׂוֹנֵא הוּא לוֹ מִתְּמוֹל שִׁלְשׁוֹם״ – הֲרֵי רוֹצֵחַ אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים: ״וְלוֹ אֵין מִשְׁפַּט מָוֶת״? בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Huna **states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **that** following ***tanna*, as it is taught** in another *baraita*: **"And for him there is no sentence of death"; the verse is speaking with regard to the blood redeemer.** The *baraita* clarifies: **Do you say** that it is speaking **with regard to the blood redeemer, or** is it speaking **only with regard to** the unintentional **murderer? When it states: "As he did not hate him from before,"** the unintentional **murderer** is already **stated,** as that phrase certainly is referring to him. **How do I realize** the meaning of the verse: **"And for him there is no sentence of death"?** It is **with regard to the blood redeemer** that **the verse is speaking.**

The *gemara* responds that Rav Huna's position aligns with a different *tanna*, *ki hai tanna*, who holds that *velo ein mishpat mavet* does refer to the *goel hadam*. According to that *tanna*, the verse is read as follows: since *ki lo sonei hu lo mitmol shilshom* is established as referring to the killer (his lack of prior hatred demonstrating the unintentional nature of the act), the *velo ein mishpat mavet* clause becomes redundant if it too refers to the killer — and is therefore read instead as establishing the exemption of the *goel hadam*.

A student offered an additional textual observation: in Deuteronomy 19:4, *velo sonei lo mitmol shilshom* appears in a verse that is already explicitly framed around the *rotzeach*, making it natural to attribute that phrase to the killer throughout. The counterargument is that precisely because that first mention is already established, the second appearance of *lo sonei lo* can carry a distinct reference — and that permits reading *velo ein mishpat mavet* as directed at the *goel hadam* rather than at the killer.

This is a critically important debate: does the manslayer enjoy legal protection while en route, or is he entirely exposed until the moment he passes through the city gate? The *peshat* of the *pesukim* — as the lecturer notes — most plausibly supports the latter reading: the Torah establishes the *ir miklat* specifically because the manslayer has no protection until he gets there, which is why preparing the roads matters so much.

### Two Sages Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מוֹסְרִין לוֹ שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁמָּא יַהַרְגֶנּוּ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וִידַבְּרוּ אֵלָיו. מַאי לָאו דְּמַתְרוּ בֵּיהּ, דְּאִי קָטֵיל בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof concerning Rav Huna's ruling from the mishna. **We learned** in the mishna: **And they would provide** the unintentional murderer fleeing to a city of refuge with **two Torah scholars,** due to the concern that **perhaps** the blood redeemer **will** seek to **kill him in transit, and** in that case **they will talk to** the blood redeemer. The Gemara asks: **What, is it not that** the Torah scholars **forewarn him that if he kills** the unintentional murderer **he** would be **liable to be executed?** That contradicts Rav Huna's opinion that a blood redeemer who kills the unintentional murderer is exempt.

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah*: *mosrin lo shnei talmidei chachamim shema yehargenu baderech veyedabru elav* — two Torah scholars are assigned to accompany the manslayer, lest the *goel hadam* kill him on the way, and they speak to the *goel hadam*. The question arises: what do they say? If they are *talmidei chachamim*, presumably they speak *halacha* — they inform the *goel hadam* that the man is protected and that killing him would render the *goel hadam* liable to the death penalty. That reading would imply that the manslayer is indeed legally protected during transit. Alternatively, the two sages are there not to deliver a *halakhic* warning but simply to persuade.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, כִּדְתַנְיָא: וִידַבְּרוּ אֵלָיו – דְּבָרִים הָרְאוּיִים לוֹ. אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: אַל תִּנְהַג בּוֹ מִנְהַג שׁוֹפְכֵי דָמִים, בִּשְׁגָגָה בָּא מַעֲשֶׂה לְיָדוֹ. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: הוּא מְדַבֵּר עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזֶה דְּבַר הָרֹצֵחַ״. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הַרְבֵּה שְׁלִיחוּת עוֹשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** the statement of the Torah scholars to the blood redeemer can be explained **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **And they will speak to him** about **matters appropriate to him. They say to** the blood redeemer: **Do not accord him treatment** appropriate for **murderers,** as it was **unintentionally** that **he came to be involved in the incident. Rabbi Meir says:** The unintentional murderer too **speaks [*medabber*] on his own behalf** to dissuade the blood redeemer, **as it is stated: "And this is the matter [*devar*] of the murderer,** who shall flee there and live" (Deuteronomy 19:4), indicating that the murderer himself apologizes and speaks to the blood redeemer. The Sages **said to** Rabbi Meir: **Many** matters **are performed** more effectively through **agency.**

A *braita* supports the persuasion reading: *veyedabru elav devarim re'uyim lo* — they speak to him things that are fitting for him to hear. *Omrim lo: al tinhag bo minhag shofchei damim, bishgaga ba leyado* — they say: do not treat this man as a murderer; what happened was an accident. Of course, as one might observe, such words are a difficult sell when the *goel hadam* is running in the heat of passion. The role of *talmidei chachamim* may be that their authority and their manner of speaking lend a certain weight that could not otherwise be achieved.

### Intermediary Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: בִּשְׁגָגָה בָּא מַעֲשֶׂה לְיָדוֹ. פְּשִׁיטָא! דְּאִי בְּמֵזִיד, בַּר גָּלוּת הוּא? אִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master says** in the *baraita*: It was **unintentionally** that **he came to be involved in the incident.** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious? As, if it were intentionally** that he killed a person, **is he liable to be exiled?** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** even intentional murderers flee to a city of refuge on occasion.

Rabbi Meir holds: *hu medaber al yedei atzmo* — the manslayer himself may speak in his own defense, citing the verse *veze devar harotzeach* (Deuteronomy 19:4). The Rabbis respond: *harbeh shelichut osef* — a messenger or intermediary can accomplish far more than one can accomplish on one's own. When a person is trying to argue for his own life against the man whose relative he killed, the chances of success are considerably lower than if trained scholars speak on his behalf.

The *gemara* then asks: *peshita* — is it not obvious that the statement *bishgaga ba miyado* (it was an accident) needs to be said? After all, *deveizid bar galuta hu* — if it were intentional, surely this man would not be running to the *ir miklat*. The *gemara* answers: not necessarily obvious, because in fact everyone runs to the *ir miklat* initially — including those who killed intentionally. The two sages are therefore not merely persuading but also informing. The *goel hadam* genuinely may not know whether the fleeing person is *shogeg* or *meizid*.

## Court Procedures

### Initial Refuge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּתְּחִלָּה, אֶחָד שׁוֹגֵג וְאֶחָד מֵזִיד מַקְדִּימִין לְעָרֵי מִקְלָט, וּבֵית דִּין שׁוֹלְחִין וּמְבִיאִין אוֹתָם מִשָּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And** so **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: Initially, either** one who killed another **unintentionally or** one who killed another **intentionally** would **hurry** and flee **to the cities of refuge, and the court** in his city would **send** for him **and** would **bring him from there** to stand trial.

Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda teaches: *bit'chilla, echad shogeg ve'echad meizid makdimin le'ir miklat* — initially, both the unintentional killer and the intentional murderer are sent to the *ir miklat*. The city functions as a kind of holding place pending trial.

### Court Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב מִיתָה – הֲרָגוּהוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁלְחוּ זִקְנֵי עִירוֹ וְלָקְחוּ אֹתוֹ מִשָּׁם וְנָתְנוּ אֹתוֹ בְּיַד גֹּאֵל הַדָּם וָמֵת״. מִי שֶׁלֹּא נִתְחַיֵּיב – פְּטָרוּהוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִצִּילוּ הָעֵדָה אֶת הָרֹצֵחַ מִיַּד גֹּאֵל הַדָּם״. מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב גָּלוּת – מַחְזִירִין אוֹתוֹ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵשִׁיבוּ אֹתוֹ הָעֵדָה אֶל עִיר מִקְלָטוֹ אֲשֶׁר נָס שָׁמָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: With regard to **one who was** found **liable** to receive the **death** penalty for intentional murder, after the trial the court **would execute him, as it is stated: "And the elders of his city shall send and take him from there and deliver him into the hands of the blood redeemer and he shall die"** (Deuteronomy 19:12). And with regard to **one who was not** found **liable** to receive the **death** penalty, e.g., if they deemed that it was due to circumstances beyond his control, **they freed him, as it is stated: "And the congregation shall rescue the murderer from the hands of the blood redeemer"** (Numbers 35:25). With regard to **one who was** found **liable to be exiled,** the court would **restore him to his place** in the city of refuge, **as it is stated:** "And the congregation shall judge between the murderer and the blood redeemer…**and the congregation shall restore him to his city of refuge, that he fled there"** (Numbers 35:24–25).

Once there, *beit din sholchim umevi'im otam misham* — the court then asserts control, summoning the accused from the city of refuge. *Mi she'nitchayav mita haragu* — one found guilty of intentional murder is executed, as the verse states: *ve'shalchu ziknei iro velakchu oto misham venatnu oto beyad goel hadam vamet*. *Mi she'lo nitchayav patruhu* — one found entirely innocent is freed, as in *vehitzilu ha'eda et harotzeach miyad goel hadam*. And *mi she'nitchayav galut machzirin oto limkomo* — one convicted of *shogeg* manslaughter is returned to the *ir miklat*, as in *veheshivu oto ha'eda el ir miklato asher nas shamma*.

This framework also clarifies the role of the *talmidei chachamim* accompanying the fleeing man. Even before the trial, they may speak to the *goel hadam* based on the presumption of what is known at that moment — that the circumstances appear consistent with *shogeg*. A student raised the question of whether the *talmidei chachamim* would similarly protect even the *meizid* runner on the grounds that the court has not yet rendered its verdict and vigilante action should be avoided in all events. The answer is that while the *braita* frames their speech as conveying *shogeg*, there is reason to suppose the protective function extends more broadly — the court, not the *goel hadam*, should be the arbiter.

### Rabbi's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: מֵעַצְמָן הֵן גּוֹלִים. כִּסְבוּרִין הֵן אֶחָד שׁוֹגֵג וְאֶחָד מֵזִיד – קוֹלְטוֹת, וְהֵן אֵינָן יוֹדְעִין שֶׁבַּשּׁוֹגֵג קוֹלְטוֹת, בְּמֵזִיד אֵינָן קוֹלְטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The Torah does not command intentional murderers to flee to a city of refuge; rather, the Torah is cognizant of the fact that in practice, intentional murderers **would exile** themselves **on their own,** as **they thought** that they would be **admitted** to these cities, which would provide refuge for **both unintentional and intentional** murderers, **and they do not know that** only those who murder **unintentionally are admitted** to these cities, but those who murder **intentionally are not admitted.**

Rabbi (Rebbe) disagrees with Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda. He holds: *me'atzman hen golim* — when the *meizid* killer is found in the *ir miklat*, he went there on his own initiative, not under *beit din* supervision. *Soverin hen echad shogeg ve'echad meizid kolelet* — those who killed intentionally mistakenly believed the *ir miklat* protects everyone. *Ve'einan yodim she'bashogeg kolelet uvemeizid einah kolelet* — but in fact the city protects only the *shogeg*, not the *meizid*. According to Rabbi, *beit din* would not dispatch a *meizid* to the *ir miklat*; they would retain him locally. The *peshat* of the *pesukim* that describe *beit din* recalling someone from the *ir miklat* for execution describes a person who went there of his own accord, believing himself protected.

This generates a further question: if the *ir miklat* does not protect a *meizid* killer, can the *goel hadam* enter the city and kill him with impunity? And if the trial has not yet been held, how can anyone — including the *goel hadam* — know with certainty whether the person is *shogeg* or *meizid* before the verdict is rendered? The *gemara* does not resolve this entirely, and the lecturer acknowledges the strangeness of a situation in which the *rotzeach* might be killed before having the opportunity to defend himself.

## City Requirements

### Murderers Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עִיר שֶׁרוּבָּהּ רוֹצְחִים – אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְדִבֶּר בְּאׇזְנֵי זִקְנֵי הָעִיר הַהִיא אֶת דְּבָרָיו״ – וְלֹא שֶׁהוּשְׁווּ דִּבְרֵיהֶן לִדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Elazar says:** An unintentional murderer **is not admitted to a city** of refuge **whose majority** consists of unintentional **murderers, as it is stated** with regard to an unintentional murderer who fled to a city of refuge: **"And he shall speak his matters in the ears of the elders of that city"** (Joshua 20:4), indicating that there is some novel element in the matters that he seeks to convey to the elders of the town, **but not when their matters are equal to his matters,** as those elders made the same statements when they arrived at the city of refuge as unintentional murderers.

Rabbi Elazar rules: *ir she'rubah rotzechim einah koletet* — a city in which the majority of inhabitants are themselves people who killed *bishogeg* cannot function as an *ir miklat*. The city of refuge is not merely a geographical location but a community. The presence of an ordinary, non-fugitive population is what confers upon the city its status and gives meaning to its function as a sanctuary. The *ir miklat* works because the manslayer is a protected minority within a normal civic community.

The source: *vediber be'oznei ziknei ha'ir ha'hi et devarav* — in Yehoshua, the text describes the manslayer speaking in the ears of the elders of that city. The elders and the townspeople must be categorically distinct from the manslayer; *lo she'hushu devav l'dvarav* — their situation cannot be equated with his.

### Elders Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ זְקֵנִים אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת, דְּבָעֵינַן ״זִקְנֵי הָעִיר״ וְלֵיכָּא. אִיתְּמַר: עִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ זְקֵנִים, רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי – חַד אוֹמֵר: קוֹלֶטֶת, וְחַד אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת – בָּעֵינַן ״זִקְנֵי הָעִיר״, וְלֵיכָּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר קוֹלֶטֶת – מִצְוָה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar says:** An unintentional murderer **is not admitted to a city in which there are no elders, as we require** the fulfillment of the verse: "And he shall speak in the ears of **the elders of the city"** (Joshua 20:4), **and there are none. It was stated: A city in which there are no elders** is the subject of a dispute between **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi. One says:** An unintentional murderer **is admitted** there, **and one says:** An unintentional murderer **is not admitted** there. The Gemara explains: **According to the one who says** that an unintentional murderer **is not admitted to a city in which there are no elders,** his reasoning is due to the fact that **we require** the presence of **the elders of the city and there are none. According to the one who says** that an unintentional murderer **is admitted** there, his reasoning is that he holds that speaking to the elders is **merely a mitzva** *ab initio*, but it does not affect the city's status as a city of refuge.

Rabbi Elazar further rules: *ir she'ein bah zekeinim einah koletet* — a city without elders cannot function as an *ir miklat*. The source is the same passage in Sefer Yehoshua, which makes the presence of *ziknei ha'ir* an explicit element of the *ir miklat* framework.

### Elders Debate

This ruling is itself subject to dispute. Rav Ami and Rav Assi debate: *chad amar kolelet* — one holds the city still provides refuge even without elders, *ve'chad amar einah kolelet* — the other holds it does not. According to the view that it does not: *beinan ziknei ha'ir ve'leika* — the verse demands elders and they are absent. According to the view that it does: *mitzva be'alma* — the mention of elders in the verse is describing the ideal arrangement, not an indispensable condition.

### Ben Sorer Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ זְקֵנִים – רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי, חַד אָמַר: נַעֲשֶׂה בָּהּ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין נַעֲשֶׂה בָּהּ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין נַעֲשֶׂה בָּהּ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה – בָּעֵינַן ״זִקְנֵי עִירוֹ״, וְלֵיכָּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר נַעֲשֶׂה בָּהּ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה – מִצְוָה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And a city in which there are no elders** is the subject of another dispute between **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi. One says:** One **can become a wayward and rebellious son in it. And one says:** One **cannot become a wayward and rebellious son in it.** The Gemara explains: **According to the one who says** that one **cannot become wayward and rebellious son in it,** it is due to the fact that **we require** the presence of the elders of the city, as it is written: "And his father and mother shall take hold of him and bring him out to **the elders of his city** and the gate of his place" (Deuteronomy 21:19), **and there are none. According to the one who says** that one **can become a wayward and rebellious son in it,** the presence of the elders is **merely a mitzva** *ab initio*.

The same dispute between Rav Ami and Rav Assi is applied to the case of a city without elders in the context of the *ben sorer umoreh*. One holds: *na'aseh bah ben sorer umoreh* — a rebellious son can be prosecuted in such a city. The other holds he cannot: *beinan ziknei iro ve'leika* — the verse requires that the parents bring the child to the elders of his city, and if they are absent, the proceeding cannot be held. The view that it can proceed holds: *mitzva be'alma* — this is the preferred procedure, but if no local *beit din* exists, one may take the child to the *beit din* of the next town.

### Egla Arufah Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ זְקֵנִים, רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי – חַד אָמַר: מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה – בָּעֵינַן ״זִקְנֵי הָעִיר הַהִיא״, וְלֵיכָּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה – מִצְוָה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And a city in which there are no elders** is the subject of another dispute between **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi. One says:** If a corpse was discovered proximate to that city, the inhabitants of the city **bring a heifer whose neck is broken. And one says:** The inhabitants of the city **do not bring a heifer whose neck is broken.** The Gemara explains: **According to the one who says** that the inhabitants of the city **do not bring a heifer whose neck is broken,** it is due to the fact that **we require** the presence of the elders of the city, as it is written: "And **the elders of that city** shall bring the calf down to a rough valley" (Deuteronomy 21:4), **and there are no** elders. **According to the one who says** that the inhabitants of the city **bring a heifer whose neck is broken,** the presence of the elders is **merely a mitzva** *ab initio*.

The same framework applies to *eglah arufah* — the rite performed when a corpse is found outside a city and responsibility must be assumed. One of Rav Ami and Rav Assi holds: *mevi'ah eglah arufah* — the ritual may be performed even in a city without elders. The other holds: *einah mevi'ah* — it cannot. The view that it cannot: *beinan ziknei ha'ir ha'hi ve'leika* — the verse explicitly requires the elders of that city, and this requirement is particularly compelling here, since the entire logic of the ritual is that the *city itself* takes communal responsibility for the death. Unlike the *ben sorer umoreh* case, one cannot simply transfer the responsibility to the elders of a neighboring town, because the ritual is about this city's particular accountability.

### Tosafot Resolution

Tosafot raises a difficulty. There is a *gemara* elsewhere that rules, when there are multiple surrounding towns and one is closer but lacks elders while a farther one has elders, the *eglah arufah* is performed by the town with elders. This implies that elders are indeed a *sine qua non* for the rite. How then can anyone hold *mitzva be'alma* — that it is merely preferred?

Tosafot resolves the contradiction as follows: when there is a choice between a town with elders and one without, the rite is assigned to the one with elders — even if it is not the geographically nearest town. But if *none* of the surrounding towns has elders, then the rite proceeds without them. The *mitzva be'alma* position, properly understood, holds that elders are ideal but not absolutely essential when no alternative exists.

## Scripture's Tone

### Harsh Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה נֶאֶמְרָה פָּרָשַׁת רוֹצְחִים בְּלָשׁוֹן עַזָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְדַבֵּר ה׳ אֶל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לֵאמֹר דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לֵאמֹר תְּנוּ לָכֶם אֶת עָרֵי הַמִּקְלָט אֲשֶׁר דִּבַּרְתִּי אֲלֵיכֶם וְגוֹ׳״?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina says: For what** reason **was the portion** discussing **murderers stated** **with harsh language, as it is written: "And the Lord spoke [*vayedabber*] to Joshua saying: Speak [*dabber*] to the children of Israel, saying: Assign you the cities of refuge of which I spoke [*dibbarti*] to you** by means of Moses" (Joshua 20:1–2).

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina poses a question: *mipnei mah ne'emar parashat rotzchim bileshon azah* — why does the passage in Sefer Yehoshua concerning the *rotzeach* and the *arei miklat* employ particularly harsh language? Specifically, while elsewhere in Sefer Yehoshua God speaks to Yehoshua with the word *vayomer*, here the text repeatedly uses *vayidaber* — a term that *chazal* consistently understood as conveying a sterner, more severe register of speech. The word *daber* even sounds harder, with its emphatic consonants.

### Torah Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Why does the Torah repeatedly employ a term of *dibbur*, connoting harsh speech, as opposed to the term of *amira*, connoting neutral speech? It is **due to** the fact **that** the cities of refuge **are** a mitzva **of the Torah,** and therefore they warrant emphasis.

Rav Huna's initial answer: *mipnei she'hen shel Torah* — because these laws originate in the Torah itself. The repeated use of *vayidaber* and *dibarti* in this passage evokes the classic Sinaitic formula *vayidaber Hashem el Moshe lemor*, signaling that what Yehoshua is now implementing is the fulfillment of a weighty divine command from Sinai. The *vayidaber* language marks this as a moment of supreme gravity.

### Language Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּכׇל דִּיבּוּר לָשׁוֹן קָשָׁה? אִין, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״דִּבֶּר הָאִישׁ אֲדֹנֵי הָאָרֶץ אִתָּנוּ קָשׁוֹת״. וְהָתַנְיָא: ״נִדְבְּרוּ״, אֵין ״נִדְבְּרוּ״ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן נַחַת, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״יַדְבֵּר עַמִּים תַּחְתֵּינוּ״! ״דִּבֶּר״ לְחוּד, ״יַדְבֵּר״ לְחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that all** instances of **speaking [*dibbur*]** indicate **harsh language?** The Gemara answers: **Yes, as it is written** with regard to Joseph's brothers: **"The man, the lord of the land, spoke [*dibber*] harshly to us"** (Genesis 42:30). The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "Then **they** who feared the Lord **spoke [*nidberu*]** with one another" (Malachi 3:16), that the term **"they spoke" is nothing other than a term of gentleness, and likewise,** the same is true of the verse which **states: "He subdues [*yadber*] peoples under us"** (Psalms 47:4), meaning that God will calmly and gently conduct the nations under the influence of the Jewish people? The Gemara answers: The meaning of ***dibber* is discrete** and the meaning of ***yadber* is discrete.** There is a difference between the two conjugations of the same root.

The *gemara* challenges: is it really true that every instance of *dibur* carries a harsh connotation? Several counter-examples are adduced. *Diber ha'ish adonei ha'aretz itanu kashot* — there, Yosef "spoke harshly" to his brothers and *dibur* is indeed harsh. But *az nidberu yirei Hashem* — "then those who feared God spoke to one another" — this is a pleasant, warm exchange, not a harsh one. And *yadber amim tachtenu* — this *yadber* connotes leading or guiding nations, again not harsh.

The *gemara* resolves: *dibur lachud, yadber lachud* — the *dikduk* distinguishes between *dibur* (speech, which leans harsh) and *yadber* (to lead or guide, which can be pleasant). These are distinct usages of the same root.

### Disagreement

The debate then pivots: *peliga Rabbi Yehuda veRabbanan*. One opinion holds that the harshness of the language reflects the fact that these *mitzvot* are of Torah origin and carry special weight; the other holds that God was actually displeased with Yehoshua for tarrying in implementing the commandment to designate the cities.

### Joshua Writing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבָּנַן, חַד אוֹמֵר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשִּׁיהָם. וְחַד אוֹמֵר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes the discussion of the harsh language employed in the portion discussing murderers in the book of Joshua. **Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis disagree with regard to this** matter. **One says** harsh language was employed **because** Joshua **delayed** fulfilling the mitzva of designating cities of refuge, **and one says** it is **because** the cities of refuge **are** a mitzva **of the Torah,** and therefore they warrant emphasis.

Since the discussion now centers on Sefer Yehoshua, the *gemara* connects to another question. The verse *vayichtov Yehoshua et hadevarim ha'eleh bisefer Torat Elohim* — "and Yehoshua wrote these words in the Book of the Torah of God" — raises the question of what exactly Yehoshua wrote in the *sefer Torah*.

### Anger View

One opinion holds that the harsh *vayidaber* language reflects God's displeasure — Yehoshua was slow in fulfilling the commandment of establishing the cities of refuge, and God's speech to him was therefore stern. This anger view does not sit easily alongside the plain reading of the Yehoshua narrative, but it fits a pattern in Rabbinic literature of holding leaders to an elevated standard of alacrity in fulfilling *mitzvot*.

### Eight Verses Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּכְתֹּב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֶת הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת אֱלֹהִים״, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה – חַד אוֹמֵר: שְׁמֹנָה פְּסוּקִים, וְחַד אוֹמֵר: עָרֵי מִקְלָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an additional dispute with regard to the portion of the cities of refuge in the book of Joshua. It is written: **"And Joshua wrote these matters in the scroll of the Torah of God"** (Joshua 24:26). **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya disagree with regard to this** matter. **One says:** The reference is to the final **eight verses** in the Torah that record the death of Moses and were recorded by Joshua in the scroll of the Torah, in addition to the rest of the Torah that was written by Moses (see *Bava Batra* 15a). **And one says:** The reference is to the portion of the **cities of refuge** that appears in the book of Joshua.

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemya debate the question of Yehoshua's writing. One holds: *shemona pesukim* — Yehoshua wrote the final eight verses of the Torah, the *pesukim* at the end of Devarim that describe the death of Moshe. This connects to the famous debate in Bava Batra about who wrote those verses, with one opinion there attributing them to Yehoshua. The verse in Yehoshua referring to *sefer Torat Elohim* would then be a reference to the *sefer Torah* of Moshe — meaning Yehoshua wrote the closing verses of Moshe's Torah. The word *hadevarim* in that verse additionally evokes *Sefer Devarim*, giving the identification added resonance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁמֹנָה פְּסוּקִים, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת אֱלֹהִים״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר עָרֵי מִקְלָט, מַאי ״בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת אֱלֹהִים״? הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״וַיִּכְתּוֹב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּסִפְרוֹ אֶת הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה הַכְּתוּבִים בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת אֱלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses these two opinions: **Granted, according to the one who says that** the reference is to the final **eight verses** in the Torah, **that is** the reason **that it is written:** "And Joshua wrote these matters **in the scroll of the Torah of God,"** as he wrote those verses and they were included in the Torah. **But according to the one who says** that the reference is to the portion of the **cities of refuge** in the book of Joshua, **what** is the meaning of the phrase **"in the scroll of the Torah of God"?** They appear in the book of Joshua, not in the Torah. The Gemara answers: **This** is what the verse **is saying: And Joshua wrote in his book these matters that are** also **written in the scroll of the Torah of God.**

The other opinion holds: *parshat arei miklat* — what Yehoshua wrote was the section dealing with the *arei miklat*, which appears in Sefer Yehoshua itself. According to Rashi, *sefer Torat Elohim* in this verse refers not to Moshe's Torah but to Sefer Yehoshua, which is itself a book of divine teaching. On this reading, Yehoshua recorded in his own book the fulfillment of what had been commanded in the Torah — he wrote the *arei miklat* passage as the implementation of what God had commanded through Moshe.

The *gemara* notes: *bishlama le'man de'amar shemona pesukim*, the identification of *sefer Torat Elohim* as Moshe's Torah is straightforward. But *le'man de'amar arei miklat*, one must explain: *hachi ka'amar vayichtov Yehoshua besifro et hadevarim ha'eleh hakevuvim bisefer Torat Elohim* — Yehoshua wrote in his own book those matters that are recorded in the Torah of God. The *sefer Torat Elohim* is Moshe's Torah, and Yehoshua's book records the fulfillment of its commands.

## Binding Materials

### Flax vs. Sinews

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵפֶר שֶׁתְּפָרוֹ בְּפִשְׁתָּן, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – חַד אוֹמֵר: כָּשֵׁר, וְחַד אוֹמֵר: פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to cite another dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and one of the Sages in which it is not clear which opinion is attributable to which Sage. In the case of a Torah **scroll where one sewed its** sheets **with linen** threads, **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir disagree with regard to this** matter. **One says:** The Torah scroll is **fit** for use, **and one says:** The Torah scroll is **unfit** for use.

A further debate is introduced: *sefer she'tefaro be'pishtan* — what is the status of a *sefer Torah* in which the parchment sections have been sewn together with flax rather than with *gidin* — sinews? Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir disagree: one holds it is *kasher*, the other *pasul*.

### R. Yehuda Pasul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פָּסוּל, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְמַעַן תִּהְיֶה תּוֹרַת ה׳ בְּפִיךָ״, וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לִתְפִילִּין: מָה תְּפִילִּין הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי לְתוֹפְרָן בְּגִידִין – אַף כֹּל לְתׇפְרָן בְּגִידִין. וְאִידַּךְ: כִּי אִיתַּקַּשׁ – לַמּוּתָּר בְּפִיךָ, לְהִלְכוֹתָיו לָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **According to the one who says** that the Torah scroll is **unfit** for use, the reason is **as it is written** with regard to phylacteries: "And it shall be for you a sign on your hand and a memorial between your eyes, **in order that the Torah of God shall be in your mouth"** (Exodus 13:9). **And** in this verse **the entire Torah is juxtaposed** and likened **to phylacteries: Just as** with regard to **phylacteries,** there is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai to sew them with sinews, so too,** with regard to **all** sheets of the Torah scroll, there is a requirement **to sew them with sinews. And the other** Sage holds: **When** the Torah scroll **is juxtaposed** and likened to phylacteries, it is only **with regard to** the principle that the sheets of the Torah scroll may be prepared only from a species of animal **that is permitted to your mouth,** i.e., that it is permitted for a Jew to eat; but with regard **to its** other ***halakhot*, it is not juxtaposed** and likened to phylacteries.

The view that it is *pasul* derives from the verse *le'maan tihye Torat Hashem be'ficha* (Exodus 13:9). The *gemara* uses this verse to connect *tefillin* and *sefer Torah*: it is a *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai* that *tefillin* must be sewn with *gidin*, and the same verse equates *tefillin* with *sefer Torah*. *Vehikish kol haTorah kullah letefillin* — just as *tefillin* require *gidin*, so too does a *sefer Torah*.

### Limited Derivation

The opposing view holds: *ki hikish le'le'maan tihye Torat Hashem be'ficha, lehilchotav lo hikish* — the *hekesh* between *tefillin* and *sefer Torah* is limited. The verse connects them only for the *derasha* of *beficha* — namely, that the *klaf* must come from the hide of a permitted animal, one that is *mutar beficha*, fit for consumption. For other *halachot* of *tefillin*, including the requirement of *gidin*, no *hekesh* is made to *sefer Torah*. A *sefer Torah* therefore does not require sinew binding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: חֲזֵינַן לְהוּ לִתְפִילִּין דְּבֵי חֲבִיבִי דִּתְפִירִי בְּכִיתָּנָא, וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: I saw that the phylacteries of the house of my uncle,** Rabbi Ḥiyya, **were sewn with linen. But the *halakha* is not in accordance with his** opinion; phylacteries may be sewn only with sinews.

Rav Chazina adds a brief practical note: *chazeina lehu letefillin d'bei chavivi* — he saw the *tefillin* in the home of his beloved, referring to Rabbi Chiya, *detfiri bechitna* — that they were sewn with flax. He then notes: *ve'leis hilcheta kevasei* — the *halacha* does not follow this practice for *tefillin*. The ruling is stated simply and the *gemara* moves on.

## High Priest Death

### Mishnah Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶחָד מָשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה, וְאֶחָד הַמְרוּבֶּה בִּבְגָדִים, וְאֶחָד שֶׁעָבַר מִמְּשִׁיחוּתוֹ – מַחֲזִירִין אֶת הָרוֹצֵחַ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה מַחֲזִיר אֶת הָרוֹצֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Torah states that an unintentional murderer is required to remain in the city of refuge to which he fled until the death of the High Priest. The mishna elaborates: With regard to High Priests, who were appointed in several different manners, **one anointed with the anointing oil,** which was the method through which High Priests were consecrated until the oil was sequestered toward the end of the First Temple period; **and one** consecrated by donning **multiple garments,** the eight vestments unique to the High Priest, which was the practice during the Second Temple period; **and one** who received a temporary appointment due to the unfitness of the serving High Priest, **who departed from his anointment** with the restoration of the serving High Priest to active service, their deaths **facilitate** the **return of the murderer** from the city of refuge to his home. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** the death of a priest **anointed for war** to address the soldiers (see Deuteronomy 20:1–7) **facilitates** the **return of the murderer.**

The *mishnah* now enumerates the categories of *kohen gadol* whose death releases the manslayer from the *ir miklat*: *echad mashuach beshemen hamishcha* — one anointed with the oil of anointment, as was practiced in the first *Beit HaMikdash*; *ve'echad hamerubeh bigvadim* — one who attained the status of *kohen gadol* through investiture in the eight garments alone, a practice that became necessary toward the end of the first Temple period when the *shemen hamishcha* was no longer available; and *ve'echad she'avar mimishichuto* — even a former *kohen gadol*, one who had served in that capacity but was temporarily displaced — for example, because a *pul* rendered him unfit to serve on *Yom Kippur* and a replacement served in his place. Even such a retired *kohen gadol*, his death would free the manslayer.

### Mothers' Care

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ אִימּוֹתֵיהֶן שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים מְסַפְּקוֹת לָהֶן מִחְיָה וּכְסוּת, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִתְפַּלְּלוּ עַל בְּנֵיהֶם שֶׁיָּמוּתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Therefore, the mothers of** High **Priests** would **provide** those exiled to cities of refuge with **sustenance and garments so that they would not pray that their sons would die.** The more comfortable their lives in the city of refuge, the less urgency they would feel to leave, and the less likely it would be that they would pray for the death of the High Priests.

Because the death of any sitting *kohen gadol* releases the manslayer, *imoseihem shel kohanim mesapkos lahen michya uchsus* — the mothers of the *kohanim gedolim* would send generous care packages to the manslayers confined in the *arei miklat*. The motivation: *kedei shelo yispallelu al beneihem sheyamusu* — so that the manslayers would not pray for their sons to die. If you have been receiving food and clothing from this man's mother, you might feel some gratitude toward her son and refrain from wishing him dead. Or equally, once the *kohen gadol* dies and the manslayer is free to leave, the care packages stop — and perhaps the manslayer would rather remain in comfortable exile than lose his benefactress.

### Curse Efficacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָשַׁב בָּהּ עַד מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי בְעִיר מִקְלָטוֹ יֵשֵׁב עַד מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאַחֲרֵי מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that the death of these High Priests facilitates the return of the murderer, derived? **Rav Kahana said** they are derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "And he shall dwell there until the death of the High Priest** who was anointed with the sacred oil" (Numbers 35:25), **and it is written: "For in his city of refuge he shall dwell until the death of the High Priest"** (Numbers 35:28), **and it is written: "And after the death of the High Priest** the murderer shall return to his ancestral land" (Numbers 35:28).

But this raises a question: why should the prayer of these manslayers have any effect? The verse states: *ke'tzipor lanud kederor la'of, ken kilat chinam lo tavo* — like a bird that flutters and a swallow that flies free, so a causeless curse will not land (Proverbs 26:2). If there is no deserving basis for it, why should God answer a prayer calling for the death of an innocent *kohen gadol*?

### Culpability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* טַעְמָא דְּלָא מְצַלּוּ, הָא מְצַלּוּ – מָיְיתִי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״כַּצִּפּוֹר לָנוּד כַּדְּרוֹר לָעוּף כֵּן קִלְלַת חִנָּם לֹא תָבֹא״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: מִפִּירְקֵיהּ דְּרָבָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי, שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶן לְבַקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל דּוֹרָן וְלֹא בִּקְּשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **The reason** that the High Priest will not die **is that they do not pray; but if they prayed** for the death of the High Priest, would he **die? But isn't it written: "As the wandering sparrow, as the flying swallow, so a curse that is baseless shall come home"** (Proverbs 26:2)? Why does the mishna express concern over a baseless curse? **A certain elder said to him: I heard in the lecture** delivered **by Rava** that it is not a baseless curse, as the High Priests share the blame for the unintentional murders performed by these people, **as they should have pleaded for mercy for their generation,** that no murder should transpire, even unintentionally, **and they did not plead.**

An elder answered: *amar hahu saba mipirkei d'Rava shmia li* — I heard this from the teaching of Rava. *She'haya lahem levakesh rachamim al doram velo bikshu* — the *kohanim gedolim* bear a share of culpability: they were obligated to pray for their generation, to intercede before God so that such tragedies of accidental death would not occur — and they did not do so. More broadly, the *kohen gadol* as spiritual leader bears responsibility for the moral and physical welfare of his people. If he failed in that responsibility, the deaths that occurred under his watch are not entirely beyond his accountability, and a prayer calling for his death is therefore not entirely without basis.

This principle runs deep. Leadership confers not only honor but responsibility for what happens within one's domain.

### Prayer Variant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּתְפַּלְּלוּ עַל בְּנֵיהֶם שֶׁלֹּא יָמוּתוּ. טַעְמָא דִּמְצַלּוּ, הָא לָא מְצַלּוּ – מָיְיתִי? מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֶעְבַּד? הָכָא אָמְרִינַן: טוֹבִיָּה חֲטָא וְזִיגּוּד מִנַּגַּיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And some teach** a variant reading of the mishna: Therefore, the mothers of High Priests would provide those exiled to cities of refuge with sustenance and garments, **so that** those exiled **would pray that their sons will not die.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** that the High Priests will not die **is that they pray, but if they did not pray** for the High Priest not to die, would the High Priest **die? What could** the High Priest **have done** to prevent the unintentional murder? **Here,** in Babylonia, **we say** an adage to describe a situation of that sort: **Toviyya sinned and Zigud is flogged.**

Some manuscripts record the text of the *mishnah* differently: not that the mothers sought to prevent prayer for the *kohen gadol's* death, but *kedei she'yitzalu aleihem shelo yamutu* — they sent care packages so that the manslayers would actively pray for the *kohen gadol* to live. But the *gemara* objects: on this reading, the implication would be that if the manslayers did *not* pray for the *kohen gadol's* life, *amai meiti* — why should the *kohen gadol* die? *Mah hava lei lemi'evad* — what wrong had he done to deserve death? The *gemara* responds with the Babylonian formulation of the irony: *Tuvia chata veZigud minged?* — Tuvia sinned and Zigud gets punished? Here the parallel is: the *rotzeach* killed and the *kohen gadol* deserves death? The Eretz Yisrael formulation of the same irony: *Shechem nasiv uMavgai gazir?* — Shechem took Dinah and the men of his city had to undergo circumcision?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָתָם אָמְרִי: שְׁכֶם נָסֵיב וּמִבְגַּאי גָּזַיר.
>
> **Translation:** **There,** in Eretz Yisrael, **they say** a different adage with the same application: **Shechem married** a woman **and Mavgai circumcised** himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: מִפִּירְקֵיהּ דְּרָבָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי, שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶן לְבַקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל דּוֹרָן וְלֹא בִּקְּשׁוּ. כִּי הָא דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַכְלֵיהּ אַרְיָא בְּרָחוֹק תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, וְלָא אִישְׁתַּעִי אֵלִיָּהוּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain elder said to him: I heard in the lecture** delivered **by Rava** that the High Priests share the blame, **as they should have pleaded for mercy for their generation and they did not plead.** Consequently, they required the exiles to pray on their own behalf. The Gemara illustrates the concept of the responsibility held by the spiritual leadership: This is **like** in **this** incident **where a certain man was eaten by a lion at a distance of three parasangs from** the place of residence of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and Elijah** the prophet **did not speak with him** for **three days** because of his failure to pray that an incident of this kind would not transpire in his place of residence.

The *gemara* resolves even this version with the same teaching of Rava: *she'haya lahem levakesh rachamim al doram velo bikshu* — the *kohanim gedolim* themselves bear responsibility for not having prayed sufficiently for their generation. It is not unjust that they require the prayers of the manslayers to preserve their lives; they forfeited a degree of their own protection by failing in their pastoral duty. This is presented as even weightier than before: not merely that the curse might be answered, but that the *kohanim gedolim* begin with a measure of deserving — and need prayer to counteract it.

## Justice Excursus

### Sage's Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: קִלְלַת חָכָם – אֲפִילּוּ בְּחִנָּם הִיא בָּאָה. מְנָלַן? מֵאֲחִיתוֹפֶל, שֶׁבְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכָּרָה דָּוִד שִׁיתִין, קְפָא תְּהוֹמָא, בְּעָא לְמִישְׁטְפָא לְעָלְמָא. אֲמַר: מַהוּ לִכְתּוֹב שֵׁם אַחַסְפָּא וּמִישְׁדֵּא בִּתְהוֹמָא דְּלֵיקוּ אַדּוּכְתֵּיהּ? לֵיכָּא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ מִידֵּי. אֲמַר: כׇּל הַיּוֹדֵעַ דָּבָר זֶה וְאֵינוֹ אוֹמְרוֹ – יֵחָנֵק בִּגְרוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos curses that are realized, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **the curse of a Sage, even** if it is **baseless,** i.e., based on a mistaken premise, **it** nevertheless **comes** to fruition and affects the object of the curse. **From where do we** derive this? It is derived **from** this incident involving **Ahithophel. When David dug the drainpipes** in preparation for building the Temple, the waters of **the depths rose** and **sought to inundate the world.** David **said: What is** the *halakha*? Is it permitted **to write** the sacred **name on an earthenware shard and throw** it **into the depths,** so **that** the water will subside and **stand in its place? There was no one who said anything to him.** David **said: Anyone who knows** the answer to **this matter and does not say it shall be strangled.**

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *klalat chacham afilu bechinam hi ba'ah* — a curse issued by a Torah scholar takes effect even if it is undeserved, even a *klalat chinam*. The authority of a *chacham* is such that his words carry independent force regardless of whether the underlying deserving exists.

### Ahitophel Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* נָשָׂא אֲחִיתוֹפֶל קַל וָחוֹמֶר בְּעַצְמוֹ, אָמַר: וּמָה לַעֲשׂוֹת שָׁלוֹם בֵּין אִישׁ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, אָמְרָה הַתּוֹרָה: שְׁמִי שֶׁנִּכְתַּב בִּקְדוּשָּׁה יִמָּחֶה עַל הַמַּיִם, לְכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁרֵי. כְּתַב שֵׁם אַחַסְפָּא, שְׁדָא אַתְּהוֹמָא, נְחַת וְקָם אַדּוּכְתֵּיהּ. וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי כְּתִיב: ״וַאֲחִיתֹפֶל רָאָה כִּי לֹא נֶעֶשְׂתָה עֲצָתוֹ וַיַּחֲבֹשׁ אֶת הַחֲמוֹר וַיָּקׇם וַיֵּלֶךְ אֶל בֵּיתוֹ (וְ)אֶל עִירוֹ וַיְצַו אֶל בֵּיתוֹ וַיֵּחָנַק וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Then **Ahithophel raised an *a fortiori*** inference **on his own** and **said: And if** in order **to make peace between a man and his wife** in the case of a *sota*, when the husband suspects his wife of having committed adultery, **the Torah says: My name that was written in sanctity shall be erased on the water,** then, in order **to establish peace for the whole world in its entirety, is it not all the more so** permitted? Ahithophel **said to** David: **It is permitted.** David **wrote** the sacred **name on an earthenware shard** and **cast it into the depths,** and the water in the depths **subsided and stood in its place.** **And even so it is written** that during the rebellion of Absalom: **"And Ahithophel saw that his counsel was not taken, and he saddled his donkey and he arose and went to his house, to his town, and he commanded his household and strangled himself"** (II Samuel 17:23).

The source is Ahitophel. When David was digging the foundations of the *Beit HaMikdash*, he dug so deep that he tapped into the primordial depths — the *tehom* — and the waters began to rise, threatening to flood the world. David asked whether anyone knew how to stop this, and declared: *kol hayodeia davar zeh ve'eino omro yechanek bigrono* — whoever knows the answer and does not say it, let him choke in his throat.

Ahitophel reasoned through a *kal vachomer*: if to make peace between husband and wife, the Torah permits God's name — written in sanctity — to be dissolved in water (*sotah*), then certainly to save the entire world, God's name can be written on pottery and cast into the depths to seal the waters. *Amar leih shari* — it is permitted. He wrote the divine name, cast the shard into the depths, and the waters receded.

Now, the condition in David's declaration was *if* someone knows and *does not say*. Ahitophel did say — in fact, he may not even have known the answer until the pressure of David's words caused him to work it out. The curse was therefore a *klalat chinam* — it should not have applied to Ahitophel at all. And yet: *ve'Ahitophel ra'ah ki lo ne'estah atzato... vayechanek* — Ahitophel saw that his counsel was not followed in the matter of the succession, went home, gave final instructions, and died by choking — precisely the death David's words had threatened. The *klalat chacham* took effect despite being causeless.

### Eli Samuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: קִלְלַת חָכָם – אֲפִילּוּ עַל תְּנַאי הִיא בָּאָה. מְנָלַן? מֵעֵלִי, דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ [עֵלִי] לִשְׁמוּאֵל: ״כֹּה יַעֲשֶׂה לְּךָ אֱלֹהִים וְכֹה יוֹסִיף אִם תְּכַחֵד מִמֶּנִּי דָּבָר״. וְאַף עַל גַּב דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּגֶּד לוֹ שְׁמוּאֵל אֶת כׇּל הַדְּבָרִים וְלֹא כִחֵד מִמֶּנּוּ״, [וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי] כְּתִיב: ״וְלֹא הָלְכוּ בָנָיו בִּדְרָכָיו וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a similar statement: **Rabbi Abbahu says:** With regard to **the curse of a Sage, even** if it is stated **conditionally, it comes** to realization. **From where do we** derive this? It is derived **from** an incident involving **Eli** the High Priest, **as Eli said to Samuel,** after the latter had received a prophetic vision with regard to Eli, that his sons do not follow his path: **"Therefore may God do to you, and more also, if you hide any matter from me** of all the matters that He spoke unto you" (I Samuel 3:17). **And even though it is written** immediately thereafter: **"And Samuel told him all the matters, and did not hide from him"** (I Samuel 3:18), **it is written** at the time of Samuel's death: **"And his sons did not follow in his ways"** (I Samuel 8:3), indicating that God did to Samuel as he prophesied with regard to Eli, and his own sons did not follow his path. Despite the fact that Eli stated the curse conditionally, Samuel was affected by the curse.

Rabbi Abbahu adds: *klalat chacham afilu al tnai hi ba'ah* — a sage's curse takes effect even when stated conditionally. The source is Eli and Shmuel. Eli said to Shmuel: *koh ya'aseh lecha Elokim vecho yosif im techahed mimeni davar* — so should God do to you and add to it, if you conceal anything from me. Shmuel did not conceal; he told Eli everything. The condition was fulfilled — or rather, the condition for the curse's activation was *not* met, since Shmuel told the truth. The curse should have been null.

And yet the verse records: *velo halchu vanav bidrachav* — Shmuel's own children did not walk in his ways. They took bribes, precisely as Eli's sons had abused their priestly authority. The implication is that Eli's words — *koh ya'aseh lecha Elokim* — carried the force of a curse: *do to you what was done to me*. Even though Shmuel satisfied the stated condition, the inner logic of the conditional curse was fulfilled: Shmuel too would suffer the heartbreak of wayward sons.

### Tosafot Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: נִידּוּי עַל תְּנַאי – צָרִיךְ הֲפָרָה. מְנָלַן? מִיְּהוּדָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם לֹא הֲבִיאֹתִיו אֵלֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״יְחִי רְאוּבֵן וְאַל יָמֹת, וְגוֹ׳ וְזֹאת לִיהוּדָה״?
>
> **Translation:** Apropos declarations that take effect even if they were stated conditionally and the condition was not fulfilled, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Ostracism** that was declared **conditionally requires nullification,** even though the condition was not fulfilled. **From where do we** derive this? It is derived **from Judah, as it is written** with regard to his request that his father allow the brothers to take Benjamin to Egypt: **"If I do not bring him to you…**I would have sinned to you for all days" (Genesis 43:9), i.e., I will remain ostracized as a sinner. **And Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Let Reuben live and not die"** (Deuteronomy 33:6), followed immediately by the verse: **"And this for Judah"** (Deuteronomy 33:7)? Why was the blessing of Judah linked to that of Reuben?

Tosafot raises the challenge: in the Middle Ages, rabbinic leaders regularly used conditional *cherem* — *nidui al tnai* — as an enforcement mechanism. If you do not comply, you are in *cherem*. But if even a conditional curse of a *chacham* takes effect regardless of whether conditions are met, has not everyone upon whom such a *cherem* was declared been placed in a state requiring *hafarah*?

### Medieval Herem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כׇּל אוֹתָן אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר, עַצְמוֹתָיו שֶׁל יְהוּדָה הָיוּ מְגוּלְגָּלִין בָּאָרוֹן, עַד שֶׁעָמַד מֹשֶׁה וּבִקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Throughout those forty years that** the children of **Israel were in the wilderness, Judah's bones were rattling in the coffin,** detached from one another, because the ostracism that he declared upon himself remained in effect, **until Moses stood and entreated** God to have **mercy upon him.**

Tosafot distinguishes: the cases of David and Eli both involved conditions over which the subject had no full control. Ahitophel genuinely may not have known the answer until the pressure brought it forth; Shmuel told the truth, yet the outcome was beyond his control. Because the condition lay outside the person's power to determine, the curse took effect from the outset.

But where the condition involves something fully within the person's control — *kegon shemandit adam shelo ya'aseh hadavar*, where a person is told: if you do such-and-such you will be in *cherem* — the *nidui* does not take hold from the outset. *Dekivan devyado hu, mis'at hanidui anu yodim bevadai shelo bedato la'asot hadavar* — since we can presume that a person does not intend to violate and thereby enter *cherem*, the *cherem* does not take effect unless and until he actually violates the condition. This distinction was critical for medieval rabbinic authorities, for whom *cherem al tnai* was a primary instrument of communal governance.

### Lion Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּי הָא דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַכְלֵיהּ אַרְיָא בְּרָחוֹק תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, וְלָא אִישְׁתַּעִי אֵלִיָּהוּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara illustrates the concept of the responsibility held by the spiritual leadership: This is **like** in **this** incident **where a certain man was eaten by a lion at a distance of three parasangs from** the place of residence of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and Elijah** the prophet **did not speak with him** for **three days** because of his failure to pray that an incident of this kind would not transpire in his place of residence.

A narrative illustrates the principle of leadership responsibility introduced earlier. A certain man was attacked by a lion within three *parsei* of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — within what might be called his zone of communal authority. As a result, Eliyahu, who had been in the habit of speaking to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi regularly, did not speak to him for three days. The implication is that the attack occurred within a community for which Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi bore some degree of responsibility. Had he taken sufficient measures — teaching his community greater vigilance, implementing protections — perhaps this would not have happened.

### Smoke Detectors

This principle finds a contemporary resonance: a rabbi who on *Yom Kippur* delivered his central *derashah* on the obligation to ensure working batteries in smoke detectors was articulating precisely this point. The spiritual leader of a community bears responsibility for the physical safety of those in his care. If something preventable occurs within his community, questions may rightfully be asked of him.

### Judah's Bones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מִי גָּרַם לִרְאוּבֵן שֶׁיּוֹדֶה – יְהוּדָה, ״וְזֹאת לִיהוּדָה... שְׁמַע ה׳ קוֹל יְהוּדָה״. עָאל אִיבְרֵיהּ לְשָׁפָא, לָא הֲוָה קָא מְעַיְּילִי לֵיהּ לִמְתִיבְתָּא דְּרָקִיעַ, ״וְאֶל עַמּוֹ תְּבִיאֶנּוּ״. לָא הֲוָה קָא יָדַע לְמִישְׁקַל וּמִיטְרַח בִּשְׁמַעְתָּא בַּהֲדֵי רַבָּנַן, ״יָדָיו רַב לוֹ״. לָא הֲוָה יָדַע לְפָרוֹקֵי קוּשְׁיָא, ״וְעֵזֶר מִצָּרָיו תִּהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** Moses **said before Him: Master of the Universe, who caused Reuben to confess** his sin with Bilhah? It was **Judah.** Judah's confession to his sin with Tamar led Reuben to confess to his own sin. Moses continued: **"And this is for Judah…hear God, the voice of Judah"** (Deuteronomy 33:7). At that point **his limbs entered** their designated **place [*leshafa*]** and no longer rattled, but the Heavenly court still **would not** allow **him** to **enter the heavenly academy.** Moses continued: **"And bring him to his people"** (Deuteronomy 33:7), so that he may join the other righteous people in Heaven. That request was also granted, but Judah **did not know** how **to engage in the give-and-take of *halakha* with the Sages** in the heavenly academy. Moses continued: **"His hands shall contend for him"** (Deuteronomy 33:7). That request was also granted, but Judah **did not know** how **to resolve** any **difficulty** raised to reject his opinion until Moses prayed: **"And You shall be a help against his adversaries"** (Deuteronomy 33:7).

The *gemara* returns to the question of conditional curses by examining the case of Yehuda. When Yehuda pledged *im lo hevi'otiv eilecha vehatzagtiv lefanecha vechatati le'avi kol hayamim* — if I do not bring Binyamin back and present him before you I will be a sinner to my father all my days — this is read as a form of self-imposed *cherem*: acceptance of outcast status should the condition not be fulfilled.

Did Yehuda fulfill it? In a sense he did — Binyamin came safely to Yaakov in Mitzrayim, even if not directly "presented" before him as Yehuda had promised. Yet the *gemara* shows that the self-curse took effect nonetheless: *atzmotav shel Yehuda hayu megulgalim ba'aron* — Yehuda's bones rolled within the *aron* throughout the forty years in the desert, a sign that his soul found no rest.

At Moshe's prayer, the resolution came in stages. *Yihi Reuven ve'al yamot* — Moshe prayed for Reuven's eternal life, for Reuven had admitted his sin with Bilhah, and it was Yehuda's own public confession that had prompted Reuven's. *Vezot liYehuda* — Moshe then turned to Yehuda: is this what Yehuda deserves? *Shema Hashem kol Yehuda* — God heard and the bones reconnected, *alu eivrav laShafe*, forming a proper skeleton. But Yehuda's soul still could not enter the heavenly *yeshiva* — *ve'lo havu ka'ma'ylei lei bimetivta derakia* — until *ve'el amo tevi'enu* brought him in. Even then, *lo hava yada limishkal umitarach bishemata* — he could not argue well in Talmudic discourse with the other souls. *Yadav rav lo* — his hands were strengthened, and he could debate. Yet *lo hava yada lifrokei kushya* — he could not resolve difficulties. *Ve'eizer mitzarav tihye* — finally he received help even in that.

The case of Yehuda explains why *nidui al tnai* in uncontrollable circumstances takes hold from the outset: Yosef might have detained Binyamin; Binyamin might have been lost or stolen along the way. Because Yehuda's ability to fulfill the condition was not entirely in his hands, the self-imposed *cherem* attached immediately.

## Release Conditions

### Judgment Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִשֶּׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ מֵת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל – הֲרֵי זֶה אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה. אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ מֵת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל וּמִינּוּ אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו וּלְאַחַר מִכֵּן נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ – חוֹזֵר בְּמִיתָתוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁנִי. נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ בְּלֹא כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְהַהוֹרֵג כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁהָרַג – אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִשָּׁם לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If, **after** the unintentional murderer's **verdict was decided** and he was sentenced to exile, **the High Priest died,** he **is not exiled,** as the death of the High Priest exempts him from exile. **If** it was **before his verdict was decided** that **the High Priest died and they appointed another in his place, and thereafter his verdict was decided, he returns** from exile **with the death of the second** High Priest. If **the verdict of** a murderer **was decided** at a time **when** there was **no High Priest, and** likewise in the cases of **one who** unintentionally **killed a High Priest and** in the case of **a High Priest who killed** unintentionally, the unintentional murderer **never leaves** the city of refuge.

The *mishnah* now addresses the determinative moment: which *kohen gadol's* death frees the manslayer? The answer is the *kohen gadol* who was in office at the time of the manslayer's *gmar din* — the conclusion of his trial, when *beit din* issued its sentence of exile to the *ir miklat*.

### No HP at Judgment

If the *ir miklat* does not protect a *meizid* killer, can the *goel hadam* enter the city and kill him with impunity? And if the trial has not yet been held, how can anyone — including the *goel hadam* — know with certainty whether the person is *shogeg* or *meizid* before the verdict is rendered? The *gemara* does not resolve this entirely, and the lecturer acknowledges the strangeness of a situation in which the *rotzeach* might be killed before having the opportunity to defend himself.

### HP Succession

If the *kohen gadol* died during the proceedings, before *gmar din*, and a new *kohen gadol* was appointed in his place, and only thereafter was the *gmar din* rendered — *chozer bemitat hasheni* — the manslayer's release is contingent on the death of the second *kohen gadol*, the one in office at the moment of *gmar din*.

### Kills HP

If the manslayer himself killed a *kohen gadol*, he is never released, because the *kohen gadol* whose death would theoretically free him is the very person he killed, and there can be no mechanism for release built around that.

### No Departure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא לֹא לְעֵדוּת מִצְוָה, וְלֹא לְעֵדוּת מָמוֹן, וְלֹא לְעֵדוּת נְפָשׁוֹת. וַאֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל צְרִיכִים לוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ שַׂר צָבָא יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּיוֹאָב בֶּן צְרוּיָה אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִשָּׁם לְעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר נָס שָׁמָּה״ – שָׁם תְּהֵא דִּירָתוֹ, שָׁם תְּהֵא מִיתָתוֹ, שָׁם תְּהֵא קְבוּרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one who is exiled may **not leave** the city at all, **either for testimony** relating to **a mitzva, or for testimony** relating to **monetary matters, or for testimony** relating to **capital** matters. **And even** if **the Jewish people require** his services, **and even** if he is **the general of the army of Israel like Joab ben Zeruiah, he never leaves** the city of refuge, **as it is stated:** "And the congregation shall restore him to his city of refuge, **that he fled there"** (Numbers 35:25), from which it is derived: **There shall be his dwelling, there shall be his death, there shall be his burial.**

While confined to the *ir miklat*, the manslayer may not leave under any circumstances. *Lo le'edut mitzvah* — not even to testify in fulfillment of a *mitzvah*; *ve'lo le'edut mamon* — not for monetary testimony; *ve'lo le'edut nefashot* — not even for capital testimony. *Ve'afilu Sar Tzva Yisrael keYoav ben Zeruiah* — even if he is the commander of Israel's armies and they are at war and need him. This last point raises obvious questions about *pikuach nefesh* — how can a negative prohibition override the security of the nation? The *gemara* grounds the rule in the verse *asher nas shamma*: *sham tehei dirato, sham tehei mitato, sham tehei kevurato* — there shall be his dwelling, there his death (if the *kohen gadol* does not die first), there his burial.

### Territorial Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהָעִיר קוֹלֶטֶת – כָּךְ תְּחוּמָהּ קוֹלֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Just as** an unintentional murderer **is admitted** to **the city** of refuge, **so is he admitted** to **its outskirts, located** within the Shabbat **boundary.**

The protection of the *ir miklat* extends not merely to its physical walls but to its surrounding *tchum* — the two-thousand-*amah* boundary that defines the Sabbath domain, *tchum Shabbat*. *Keshem she'ir kolelet kach tchum kolet* — just as the city itself provides refuge, so too does its full perimeter.

## Outside Boundaries

### R. Yose: Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוֹצֵחַ שֶׁיֵּצֵא חוּץ לַתְּחוּם וּמְצָאוֹ גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: מִצְוָה בְּיַד גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם, וּרְשׁוּת בְּיַד כׇּל אָדָם. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: רְשׁוּת בְּיַד גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם, וְכׇל אָדָם חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Just as** an unintentional murderer **is admitted** to **the city** of refuge, **so is he admitted** to **its outskirts, located** within the Shabbat **boundary.** Once he entered the outskirts of the city, the blood redeemer may not kill him. In a case **where a murderer emerged beyond the** Shabbat **boundary** of the city of refuge **and the blood redeemer found him** there, **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says:** It is **a mitzva for the blood redeemer** to kill him, **and** it is **optional for every** other **person** to do so. **Rabbi Akiva says:** It is **optional for the blood redeemer, and every** other **person is liable for** killing **him.**

*Ve'rotzeach sheyatza chutz latchum umatz'o goel hadam* — if the manslayer has left the *tchum* of the *ir miklat* and the *goel hadam* encounters him: Rabbi Yose HaGelili holds *mitzvah beyad goel hadam* — it is a *mitzvah* for the *goel hadam* to kill him. Furthermore, *reshut beyad kol adam* — any ordinary person is permitted to kill him.

### R. Akiva: Reshut

Rabbi Akiva holds: *reshut beyad goel hadam* — the *goel hadam* is permitted but not obligated; there is no *mitzvah* here. *Ve'chol adam chayavin alav* — on one reading of the final clause, any other person who kills him is liable for murder; on an alternate *girsa*, *ein chayavin alav* — they are not liable, but neither do they have any affirmative permission.

### Ethical Problem

The ethical difficulty of Rabbi Yose HaGelili's position is striking. This man killed *bishogeg* — unintentionally. He has served his exile. Why should leaving the *ir miklat's tchum* make him deserving of death, and why should that killing constitute a *mitzvah*? One way to read Rabbi Yose is less as a statement about the man's culpability and more as a mechanism of enforcement: the severity of the consequence is what keeps the manslayer within the city. If leaving carried no penalty, the institution of *ir miklat* would have no teeth.

Rabbi Akiva's position is more consistent with the spirit of the *pesukim*, which seem at every turn to be trying to *protect* the manslayer rather than to deliver him into further danger. The Torah tolerates the residual element of vigilante justice embodied by the *goel hadam* — it cannot entirely legislate away the grief and rage of a bereaved family — but it does not endorse it as a *mitzvah*. On Rabbi Akiva's reading, the Torah channels and limits this impulse without sanctifying it. Whether anyone who kills the manslayer outside the *tchum* would bear murder liability remains, on some versions of the text, an open question — itself a reflection of how profoundly difficult it is to assign full moral responsibility in these cases.
