# Ketubot 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Head Covering

### Opening & Context

Today's learning is dedicated to the memory of those murdered in the *Shoah*. We transition from a *sugya* that dealt with the norms of married women's behavior — specifically the question of head covering — to the next *mishnah*, which opens a discussion of enormous contemporary relevance to *agunah* solutions and conditional marriage.

### Head Covering Review

The previous *mishnah*'s treatment of head covering was notably distinct from the discussion in *Berachot*, which addresses hair as a form of *ervah*. In the *mishnah* under that discussion, the concern is not absolute: it applies specifically to married women, only in public contexts, and bears on the question of whether a woman is drawing undue attention to herself. The *mishnah* emphasizes the head rather than the hair per se, and the *gemara* there raises whether the operative standard is *dat Yehudit* — the norms of Jewish women, which might be subject to change as societal norms evolve — or *dat Moshe*, which may carry biblical weight and may or may not be similarly amenable to change. These remain live and important questions.

### Cond. Marriage Intro

We now turn to the *mishnah* at the bottom of 73b, which addresses *kiddushin al tnai* — conditional betrothal — and which will draw us into one of the most significant *sugyot* for discussions of *agunah* and possible *agunah* solutions in contemporary *halachah*.

### No Vows Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמִּקְדָּשׁ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים, וְנִמְצְאוּ עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים — אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, כְּנָסָהּ סְתָם וְנִמְצְאוּ עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים — תֵּצֵא שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who betroths a woman on condition that there are no vows** incumbent **upon her, and** it was subsequently **discovered** that there are **vows** incumbent **upon her, she is not betrothed.** This is because if the condition is not fulfilled, the betrothal is nullified. If **he married her without specification and** it was subsequently **discovered** that **vows** were incumbent **upon her, she** may be **divorced without** payment of her **marriage contract,** since he discovered a deficiency about which she had not initially informed him. However, this does not invalidate the betrothal, since he did not make any explicit condition.

The *mishnah* begins: *HaMekadesh et haIsha al menat she'ein aleha nedarim* — a man betroths a woman on the condition that she has no binding vows upon her. *V'nimtze'u aleha nedarim* — and it is subsequently discovered that she does in fact have vows. *Eina mekudeshet* — the betrothal is void. Since the *kiddushin* was made conditional and the condition was not met, the *kiddushin* never took effect. This is straightforward: he verbalized the condition explicitly.

### No Condition, Vows

The *mishnah* then continues with a second clause: *kinusah stam* — he brought her in, meaning he performed *nisu'in*, the stage of full marriage that is the focus of our *masechet*, without articulating any condition. *V'nimtze'u aleha nedarim* — and vows are discovered. *Teitze shelo biKetuvah* — she is divorced without receiving her *ketubah*.

The *gemara* will debate how to read this second clause. The most natural reading of the *mishnah* as a whole seems to be that the second clause builds on the first: the man had already stipulated at the time of *kiddushin* that he does not want a wife with vows, and then he proceeded to the *nisu'in* without re-articulating that condition. Because he had verbalized the *tnai* earlier, it is evident he cares about the matter — hence he may divorce her without a *ketubah*. Yet she may still require a *get*, since the *nisu'in* itself was performed without the condition being reiterated. Alternatively, one could read the second clause as entirely independent: a man simply married a woman with no condition stated at any stage, and the discovery of *nedarim* is, by itself, a sufficient basis for divorce without *ketubah*. This ambiguity is precisely what drives the subsequent *gemara*.

### Blemishes Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ מוּמִין וְנִמְצְאוּ בָּהּ מוּמִין — אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. כְּנָסָהּ סְתָם וְנִמְצְאוּ בָּהּ מוּמִין — תֵּצֵא שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְתוּבָּה. כֹּל הַמּוּמִין הַפּוֹסְלִין בַּכֹּהֲנִים — פּוֹסְלִין בְּנָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** If he betrothed her **on condition that she has no blemishes, and** it was subsequently **discovered** that **she** did have **blemishes, she is not betrothed.** But if **he married her without specification, and** it was subsequently **discovered** that **she** had **blemishes, she** may be **divorced without** payment of her **marriage contract.** The mishna clarifies what qualifies as a blemish: **All of the blemishes** that are listed in tractate *Bekhorot* involving significant physical deformities **that disqualify priests** from service similarly **disqualify** betrothal of **women,** as a mistaken transaction.

The *mishnah* continues with a parallel case: *al menat she'ein ba mumin* — he marries on the condition that she has no blemishes. The condition is not met. Again, *kiddushin al tnai*, condition unmet, *kiddushin* void. Then *kinusah stam* — the *nisu'in* proceeds without re-articulation — and blemishes are discovered. *Teitze shelo biKetuvah*. The structure precisely parallels the vows case.

### Define Mumim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּתְנַן נָמֵי גַּבֵּי קִדּוּשִׁין כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא! הָכָא — כְּתוּבּוֹת אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, תַּנָּא קִדּוּשִׁין אַטּוּ כְּתוּבּוֹת. הָתָם — קִדּוּשִׁין אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, תְּנָא כְּתוּבּוֹת אַטּוּ קִדּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments: **And we learned** a mishna (*Kiddushin* 50a) **also concerning betrothal** just **like this case.** The mishna there is essentially identical to the mishna here, so why must it be repeated? The Gemara explains: **Here, it was necessary for** the *tanna* to mention these *halakhot* in the context of **marriage contracts,** which is the topic of this tractate. Therefore, **he taught** the *halakha* of **betrothal due to** the *halakha* of **marriage contracts. There,** in *Kiddushin*, **it was necessary for him** to mention the *halakha* of **betrothal,** so **he taught** about **marriage contracts due to betrothal.**

The *mishnah* adds: *kol haMumim haposlim baKohanim poslim baNashim* — whatever blemishes disqualify a *kohen* from Temple service disqualify a woman in this context. This is the operative standard if the husband did not specify which kind of blemish he had in mind. The *gemara* notes that the same *mishnah* appears in *masechet Kiddushin*, where its purpose is to establish the laws of conditional *kiddushin*, while here in *masechet Ketubot* the teaching is needed to address the *ketubah* consequences — specifically that she may be divorced without receiving one. The *mishnah* is thus needed in each tractate for a different focal point: *kiddushin* is the subject there; *ketubah* is the subject here.

## Which Vows Matter

### Inui Nefesh Vows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: בְּאֵלּוּ נְדָרִים אָמְרוּ — שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאכַל בָּשָׂר, וְשֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁתֶּה יַיִן, וְשֶׁלֹּא תִּתְקַשֵּׁט בְּבִגְדֵי צִבְעוֹנִים. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: בְּאֵלּוּ נְדָרִים אָמְרוּ — דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן עִינּוּי נֶפֶשׁ: שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאכַל בָּשָׂר, וְשֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁתֶּה יַיִן, וְשֶׁלֹּא תִּתְקַשֵּׁט בְּבִגְדֵי צִבְעוֹנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: These are the vows they spoke about** in the mishna that are considered grounds for divorce without payment of the marriage contract: A vow that **she will not eat meat or that she will not drink wine or that she will not adorn herself with colored garments. That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **These are the vows they spoke about: Matters that involve affliction,** such as **that she will not eat meat, or that she will not drink wine, or that she will not adorn herself with colored garments.**

The *gemara* turns to define which category of vows is meant by the *mishnah*. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Yosi ben Yehotzadak*: *be'elu nedarim amru* — when the *mishnah* speaks of vows, it is not referring to all vows indiscriminately. Only certain categories are meant. A *baraita* confirms this: *be'elu nedarim amru — dvarim sheyesh bahen inui nefesh* — the *mishnah* refers specifically to vows that involve affliction of the person, *inui nefesh*.

### Affliction Examples

The examples given are: *shelo tochal basar* — that she may not eat meat; *shelo tishteh yayin* — that she may not drink wine; *shelo titkashet bivigdei tzevonin* — that she may not adorn herself with colored garments. These are the same categories the *gemara* earlier identified as constituting *inui nefesh* according to one *tanna*. The implication is that lesser, more trivial vows would not rise to the threshold of being a disqualifying factor.

### Explicit vs Implied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָוֵי בַּהּ רַב פָּפָּא: אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַרֵישָׁא: כֵּיוָן דְּקָא קָפֵיד — אֲפִילּוּ כֹּל מִילֵּי נָמֵי! אֶלָּא אַסֵּיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa discussed it: To which** statement in the mishna is this referring? **If we say** it is referring **to the first clause** of the mishna, where one betroths a woman on condition that there are no vows incumbent upon her, then **since he** demonstrated that he **is particular** about vows, **even** vows concerning **any** other **matters,** including insignificant ones, should **also** be included. Since he stipulated a condition and it was not fulfilled, the betrothal is invalid. **Rather,** one must conclude that it is referring **to the latter clause** of the mishna, about one who marries a woman without stipulation and then discovers that vows were incumbent upon her. In such a case the mishna says she may be divorced without payment of her marriage contract. However, it does not say this for all vows, but only for vows concerning matters of significant affliction.

*Ba'ei mineih Rav Pappa*: on which clause of the *mishnah* does Rabbi Yochanan's limitation apply? *Ilima areisha* — if it applies to the first clause, where the man explicitly stipulated "on condition of no vows," then since he expressed an explicit concern, surely any vow, however trivial, ought to matter. The threshold of *inui nefesh* seems out of place there. *Ela asifa* — it must therefore apply to the second clause, where no condition was stated. In that case, since nothing was articulated, the *mishnah* can only be referring to vows of meaningful consequence.

### Reasonable Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם אַרֵישָׁא, וּמִידִּי דְּקָפְדִי בַּהּ אִינָשֵׁי — הֲוָה קְפִידֵיהּ קְפִידָא. מִידֵּי דְּלָא קָפְדִי בַּהּ אִינָשֵׁי — לָא הָוֵי קְפִידֵיהּ קְפִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Actually,** one can explain that it is referring **to the first clause** of the mishna, where he stipulates that the marriage is conditional on the assumption that she has no vows incumbent upon her, and that the point is that for a vow concerning **a matter about which people are** ordinarily **particular, his insistence is** considered legitimate **insistence,** and is effective to invalidate the betrothal. But with regard to a vow concerning **a matter about which people are** generally **not particular, his insistence is not** considered **insistence,** and such a vow is not considered a violation of the condition. Consequently, the betrothal is valid.

*Rav Ashi amar*: Rav Ashi disagrees with this framing and applies the limitation even to the first clause — even where the man explicitly said "no vows." *Mihu d'kafid inshi havei kfeida* — what counts as a legitimate concern is determined by what people normally care about. If he said "no vows" without further specification, we interpret that statement to encompass only vows that normal people would genuinely object to. *Mihu d'la kafdi inshi* — things that people ordinarily disregard are not included, even if the man used broad language. Of course, if he is explicit and specific — "I don't want any vow whatsoever, no matter how trivial" — then he may indeed make such a condition binding. A person may set any stipulation he chooses. But where he speaks in general terms, we do not read the condition to extend beyond what a person would reasonably mean. One might ask why *inui nefesh* is the relevant measure here, since the concept designates an affliction of *her* body — why should that be the operative criterion for *his* concern? The most natural answer is that what significantly affects her wellbeing and daily life also shapes the texture of the shared marital relationship in ways that matter to him as well.

## Rav v. Shmuel

### Condition Persistence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קִידְּשָׁה עַל תְּנַאי וּכְנָסָהּ סְתָם, רַב אָמַר: צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that the Sages had a dispute concerning the following question: If he **betrothed her conditionally,** such as that she had no vows incumbent upon her, **and he** subsequently **married her without specification,** and then it was discovered that the condition had not been fulfilled, **Rav said:** Although he may divorce her without payment of her marriage contract, the betrothal is not nullified, and therefore **she requires a bill of divorce from him. And Shmuel said:** The betrothal was invalid from the outset, and therefore **she does not require a bill of divorce from him.**

*Itmar*: the *gemara* now introduces the central dispute that emerges from this *mishnah*, and which has profound ramifications for *halachah*. The case: *kidsha al tnai* — the *kiddushin* was made with an explicit condition — *v'kansah stam* — and the *nisu'in* was performed without reiterating that condition. What is the *halachah*? Does the *tnai* made at the time of *kiddushin* continue to operate at the time of *nisu'in*, even though it was not repeated?

### Rav's Position

*Rav amar*: *tzricha heimenu get*. She requires a *get* from him. Once the *nisu'in* has taken place — even though the *tnai* was not repeated — she is considered married. The *tnai* made at the time of *kiddushin* does not persist through the *nisu'in* to void the marriage.

### Shmuel's Position

*Shmuel amar*: *eina tzricha heimenu get*. She does not require a *get*. Since the *tnai* was articulated at the time of *kiddushin*, if the *kiddushin* never took effect because the condition was not satisfied, then the subsequent act of *nisu'in* and cohabitation does not create a marriage. In Shmuel's view, marital relations are meaningful only insofar as they rest upon an antecedent valid *kiddushin*. Without a binding *kiddushin* as the foundation, the act of *be'ilah* is simply *be'ilah* — it is not an independent act of *kiddushin*.

### Ain Adam Oseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא תֵּימָא טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַב כֵּיוָן שֶׁכְּנָסָהּ סְתָם אַחוֹלֵי אַחֲלֵיהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ, אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְרַב: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה בְּעִילָתוֹ בְּעִילַת זְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Do not say** that **Rav's reason** for requiring a bill of divorce is that **since he married her without specification,** this indicates that **he waived his condition** entirely, and therefore he must give her the payment of her marriage contract if he divorces her. **Rather, Rav's reason** is **because a person does not** intentionally **engage in licentious sexual intercourse.** He is aware that the initial betrothal may possibly be nullified, rendering sexual intercourse licentious. Therefore, when he marries her, he does so with the intention that the consummation of the marriage serves as unconditional betrothal. However, as he does not entirely waive his condition, if it becomes clear that the condition was not fulfilled, she may be divorced without receiving payment of her marriage contract.

*Amar Abaye*: Abaye clarifies that Rav's reasoning is not, as one might suppose, that by proceeding to *nisu'in* without reiterating the *tnai*, the husband implicitly waived it — thereby retroactively validating the original act of *kiddushin*. That would be a remarkable notion: that a waiver occurring a year later could retroactively render the original giving of the ring binding. Abaye dismisses that reading. *Ela ta'ama deRav* — Rav's reason is *she'ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* — because a person does not wish his act of marital intercourse to be an act of fornication. The original *kiddushin* was not binding because the *tnai* was unmet. But the act of *be'ilah* itself is one of the recognized modes of effecting *kiddushin*, and we presume that within the context of a *chuppah* and the entire marital framework, the man intends this act to be a marital one. Because he would not want it to be an illicit act, we conclude that he wishes the intercourse itself to constitute *kiddushei bi'ah*.

### Marital Intent

This reasoning raises a deeper question: does the principle of *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* mean that the man actively intends the act of *be'ilah* to be an act of *kiddushin* — in which case *kavvanah* would be necessary — or does it mean that in a marital context *kiddushei bi'ah* operates automatically, irrespective of explicit intent? Some *Acharonim* take the latter position, that *kiddushei bi'ah* within a marital context requires no specific *kavvanah*. The practical concern with such a reading is significant, however: it would mean that any couple living together in an established marital arrangement, even without having contracted a halachically valid *kiddushin*, would be considered married and would require a *get* — a result with potentially sweeping implications for *agunah* cases and the like.

The *gemara* proceeds: once the *nisu'in* is performed and the couple cohabits, Rav holds they are married not because the original *kiddushin* has been retroactively validated, but because the act of *be'ilah* itself constitutes a new, independent act of *kiddushei bi'ah*. There is accordingly a *nafka mina* between these two theories: when precisely is the marriage considered to have taken effect? If it is from the time of the original *kiddushin*, one set of consequences follows; if from the time of *be'ilah*, another set applies. For example, if the woman had relations with another man between the *kiddushin* and the *nisu'in*, the question of when they are deemed married is directly material. In the *gemara*'s framing, *kiddushei bi'ah* and *nisu'in* here occur together at the time of *be'ilah*, with the earlier *kiddushin* not having been the operative act.

## Minor Girl Case

### Ktanah Growing Up

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא פְּלִיגִי בַהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא, דְּאִתְּמַר: קְטַנָּה שֶׁלֹּא מֵיאֲנָה, וְהִגְדִּילָה, עָמְדָה וְנִישֵּׂאת — רַב אָמַר: אֵין צְרִיכָה גֵּט מִשֵּׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But they** already **disagree about this** fundamental issue of whether it may be assumed that a person does not intentionally engage in licentious sexual intercourse **one** other **time. As it is stated:** With regard to **a minor girl** whose mother or brother married her off, and **who did not refuse** her husband, despite having the right to do so, **and** when **she became an adult she arose and married** someone else, **Rav said: She does not require a bill of divorce from the second one.** Since she did not refuse the first husband while still a minor, and presumably he later engaged in sexual intercourse with her when she became an adult, and since the assumption is that he does not intentionally engage in licentious relations, the first marriage is binding and the second is meaningless.

The *gemara* observes that Rav and Shmuel have already disputed this issue once before in an analogous context. *Ktanah shemi'anah v'higdilah*: the case of a minor girl who was married off by her mother or brothers — as is permitted when she has no father — and who did not exercise her right of *mi'un* (refusal) while she was still a minor. She then became an adult and continued to live with her husband. The original *kiddushin* effected by her mother or brothers had only rabbinic force; it was not a fully binding *kiddushin deOraita*.

### Rav on Intercourse

*Amda venisset* — suppose she then goes and marries a second man. Rav holds: *ein tzricha get misheini* — she does not require a *get* from the second man. Why? Because by continuing to cohabit with the first husband after reaching adulthood, she effected a new, binding *kiddushei bi'ah* with him. The continued marital intercourse in an established marital context is itself a valid act of *kiddushin*. Her subsequent "marriage" to the second man was therefore invalid from the outset, since she was already married. Consequently, the second *kiddushin* simply does not take effect and no *get* is required from that man.

### Shmuel on Original

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: צְרִיכָה גֵּט מִשֵּׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: She requires a bill of divorce from the second one.** Her first husband did not engage in sexual intercourse with the intention that it serve as a new betrothal, rather he intended to continue the relationship established between them when she was a minor. Therefore, she is not considered to be his wife and the second marriage is binding.

Shmuel, by contrast, holds: *tzricha get misheini*. In his view, the continued marital cohabitation does not independently create *kiddushin* where none previously existed. Since the original *kiddushin* by her mother and brothers was not fully binding, and she never executed a new, independent act of *kiddushin* with her first husband as an adult, she was not halachically married to him when she contracted the second marriage. The second *kiddushin* is therefore valid, and if she now wishes to leave the second man she requires a *get* from him.

The parallel to the *tnai* case is evident. In both cases, the original *kiddushin* was deficient — in the *tnai* case because the condition was unmet; in the *ktanah* case because her original *kiddushin* lacked full *deOraita* force. The question in both cases is whether continued cohabitation independently generates a binding marriage. Rav says yes; Shmuel says no, everything depends on whether the foundational *kiddushin* was valid.

### Two Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אִתְּמַר בְּהָהִיא: בְּהָהִיא קָאָמַר רַב מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיכָּא תְּנָאָה, אֲבָל בְּהָא דְּאִיכָּא תְּנָאָה — אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל. וְאִי אִתְּמַר בְּהָא: בְּהָא קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, אֲבָל בְּהָךְ — אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַב, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: It is **necessary** to state the dispute in both instances. **For if it was stated** only **in that** case of the minor who did not refuse, one could say that **in that** case **Rav stated** his opinion **because there is no condition** attached to the betrothal. Consequently, when she becomes an adult, he engages in sexual intercourse with intent to betroth her, as he recognizes that the initial betrothal was ineffective. **But in this** case, **where there is a condition** and it is unfulfilled, one could **say that he concedes to Shmuel** that he did not intend to betroth her through intercourse, and she does not require a bill of divorce. **And** conversely, **if it was stated** only **in this** case concerning betrothal, it could be argued that **in this** case **Shmuel said** that the betrothal is not valid when he engages in sexual intercourse with her, since she violated a condition, **but in that** case of the minor who did not refuse, one could **say** that **he concedes to Rav.** Therefore, it is **necessary** to state the argument explicitly in both cases.

The *gemara* asks: since it is the same underlying dispute, why must it be stated in both cases? The *gemara* answers: *tzricha*. Each case is needed because it presents a scenario where one might have thought that even Rav would concede to Shmuel.

Had we only the *tnai* case, one might have said: that is where Rav says continued cohabitation is binding — *because* there is no explicit *tnai* in force at the time of *nisu'in*. But in the *tnai* case, where the man explicitly articulated "only on condition of no vows," one might think that even Rav concedes that the explicit *tnai* continues to frame the relationship, and that continued cohabitation does not override it. We therefore need the *tnai* case to show that even there, Rav holds the *be'ilah* creates *kiddushin*. This, in fact, is a larger *chiddush* on Rav's part: where a *tnai* was explicitly stated, there is greater reason to think it should persist and frame all subsequent relations, and yet Rav holds that the continued marital *be'ilah* nonetheless creates *kiddushin* unconditionally.

Conversely, had we only the *ktanah* case — where no *tnai* was ever articulated — one might have thought that Shmuel, who says continued cohabitation is not binding, agrees only there because nothing at all was ever said. But in the *tnai* case, where at least something was verbalized (even if the condition ultimately was not met), perhaps Shmuel would agree with Rav that continued living together creates *kiddushin*. We therefore need both cases to fully establish the scope of their dispute.

## Agunah Solutions

### Ein Tnai Principle

Our *amud* is brief, owing in part to the substantial *Tosafot* that occupies much of the page. The *Tosafot* engages with a statement found in another *gemara*: *ein tnai benis'uin* — conditions cannot operate at the *nisu'in* stage. This principle is of great moment for understanding whether conditional marriage can ever serve as a solution to the *agunah* problem.

From our *gemara* itself, the conclusion seems to be that it is specifically because the *tnai* was not reiterated at the time of *nisu'in* that Rav holds the *be'ilah* creates *kiddushin* unconditionally. One might reason, accordingly, that had he explicitly reiterated the *tnai* before the *chuppah* — "everything that follows, including our living together, remains conditional on her having no *nedarim*" — then perhaps even Rav would agree the *tnai* remains operative. But this other *gemara*, with its categorical *ein tnai benis'uin*, suggests that in principle, no matter what is said, once the couple begins living together they are married. There is no room for conditions at the *nisu'in* stage.

### Tosafot on Agunah

*Tosafot* directly engages this issue in the context of the *agunah* problem. The prospect of a solution through conditional *kiddushin* — making the original betrothal conditional on circumstances that would prevent an *agunah* situation — has been debated by *poskim* going back some 150 years, notably among the rabbis of France. The idea is straightforward: if the *kiddushin* is made conditional at the outset — for example, on condition that the husband will always give a *get* if the marriage breaks down, or on condition that some future circumstance does or does not come to pass — then if those conditions are not met, the *kiddushin* is retroactively void and no *get* is required.

### Explicit Tnai Option

One approach discussed by *Tosafot* is whether a sufficiently explicit and carefully framed *tnai* at the time of *kiddushin* might nonetheless operate effectively. From our *gemara*, the limiting factor appears to be the *be'ilah* at the time of *nisu'in*, which in Rav's view creates an unconditional *kiddushin* regardless of what came before — grounded in *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut*. But as *Tosafot* notes, the principle of *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* operates *mistama* — as a default assumption in the absence of explicit statement. If the parties explicitly and unambiguously declare that their living together and cohabiting is itself still conditional, one could argue they have effectively acknowledged the possibility of *beilat zenut* and are consenting to the arrangement. In that case, perhaps the assumption no longer applies and the *tnai* could remain operative. The question then becomes whether the categorical *ein tnai benis'uin* from the other *gemara* would foreclose even this.

### Secular Marriage Impact

A further avenue discussed by later *poskim* is that the principle of *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* was formulated in a social context where religious marriage was the only form of recognized marriage. In contemporary reality, where civil marriage exists and is recognized by the state and by society at large, the couple may not perceive their cohabitation as *beilat zenut* even if the halachic *kiddushin* were conditional or void. If there is civil marriage in place, the marital setting is defined not only by the religious *kiddushin*, and therefore the presumption that the man would not want his *be'ilah* to be *zenut* may operate differently. This provides an additional basis to argue that a properly structured *tnai* might remain effective in contemporary circumstances — at least as a matter of theoretical *lamdanut*, even if the *halachah lema'aseh* remains contested.

## Mishnah Reconcile

### Two Clauses Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: כְּנָסָהּ סְתָם וְנִמְצְאוּ עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים — תֵּצֵא שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְתוּבָּה. כְּתוּבָּה הוּא דְּלָא בָּעֲיָא, הָא גִּיטָּא בָּעֲיָא. מַאי לָאו, קִידְּשָׁה עַל תְּנַאי וּכְנָסָהּ סְתָם, תְּיוּבְתָּא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses proofs for both sides of this dispute: **We learned** in the mishna: If **he married her without specification, and it was discovered that vows were incumbent upon her, she** may be **divorced without** receiving payment of her **marriage contract.** The Gemara infers: **She does not require** or receive payment of her **marriage contract, but she does require a bill of divorce. What, is it not** speaking about the case discussed in the first clause, namely that **he betrothed her conditionally and** then **married her without specification?** If so, the mishna is **a conclusive refutation of Shmuel's** opinion.

The *gemara* now examines how our *mishnah* relates to the debate of Rav and Shmuel. The second clause of the *mishnah* — *kinusah stam v'nimtze'u aleha nedarim, teitze shelo biKetuvah* — appears on its simple reading to be building on the first clause. That is, the *kiddushin* was already conditional, the *nisu'in* was done without repeating the *tnai*, vows are discovered, and she must be divorced. *Teitze* implies she requires a *get*. *Mai lav bid'katrei tnai* — is this not then explicitly a case where the *tnai* was stated at *kiddushin* and she still requires a *get* after *nisu'in*? That would be a clear contradiction of Shmuel.

### Rav Reads Stam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, קִידְּשָׁה סְתָם, וּכְנָסָהּ סְתָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **No,** the case there is referring to where **he betrothed her without specification and** then **married her without specification.**

Rav resolves this by insisting that the second clause is an entirely independent case. When the *mishnah* says *kinusah stam* — he brought her in without condition — it means that nothing was said at either the *kiddushin* or the *nisu'in* stage. It is a simple case: a man married a woman, and after *nisu'in* vows are discovered. In that case, a woman with *nedarim* constitutes grounds for divorce, and though no *tnai* was ever stated, the man is not required to pay the *ketubah* — a woman with binding vows is precisely the kind of situation he cannot have been expected to knowingly accept. She requires a *get* because of course there was *nisu'in*, but the *ketubah* is forfeited.

### Shmuel Expects Chiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל, קִידְּשָׁה עַל תְּנַאי וּכְנָסָהּ סְתָם — הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא בָּעֲיָא גִּיטָּא? אַדְּתָנֵי הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים וְנִמְצְאוּ עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, לִיתְנֵי: כְּנָסָהּ סְתָם וְנִמְצְאוּ עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים — אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן הָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to this explanation, if **he betrothed her conditionally and** then **married her without specification,** is it **indeed** the case **that she does not require a bill of divorce?** If so, **instead of teaching** in the mishna that if **one betroths a woman on condition that there are no vows** incumbent **upon her** to fulfill, **and it was** subsequently **discovered** that there are **vows** that are incumbent **upon her, she is not betrothed, let** the mishna **teach** instead a more novel *halakha*: If he betrothed her with a condition and **married her without specification and** then **it was discovered** that **vows were** incumbent **upon her, she is not betrothed. And** from this one can derive that **all the more so** the same *halakha* would apply in **this** case, where he betrothed her conditionally but did not repeat the condition when marrying her.

Shmuel's interpretation of the *mishnah* proceeds differently. He argues that the *mishnah* would be more instructive if it presented the case where the *tnai* was made at *kiddushin* and was followed by *nisu'in*. After all, the obvious *chiddush* from Shmuel's position would be: even if there was *nisu'in* after the conditional *kiddushin*, she is still not married if the condition went unmet — *eina mekudeshet*. Why does the *mishnah* tell us only what happens before *nisu'in*, the obvious case? It should have told us the more striking ruling: that even after *nisu'in*, the original *tnai* renders her *lo mekudeshet* with no *get* required. The *gemara* responds on Shmuel's behalf.

### Resolution: Any Stage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי קָאָמַר: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים וּכְנָסָהּ סְתָם, וְנִמְצְאוּ עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים — אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. קִידְּשָׁה סְתָם וּכְנָסָהּ סְתָם — תֵּצֵא שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That is also what he is saying,** and one should understand the mishna this way: **One who betroths a woman on condition that there are no vows** incumbent **upon her** to fulfill, **and** then **marries her without specification, and it is** then **discovered** that **vows were** incumbent **upon her, she is not betrothed.** If **he betrothed her without specification and married her without specification, she** requires a bill of divorce but may be **divorced without** receiving payment of her **marriage contract.**

The *gemara* resolves: *hachi nami ka'amar* — that is indeed what the *mishnah* is saying. The *mishnah*'s first clause, which states that where *kiddushin* was conditional and the condition was not met she is *eina mekudeshet*, is not limited to the case where *nisu'in* has not yet taken place. The *mishnah* speaks to the general principle: if the *kiddushin* was made conditional and the condition was not satisfied, she is not *mekudeshet* — regardless of whether *nisu'in* subsequently occurred. The second clause then addresses an independent scenario: where no *tnai* was ever articulated, she requires a *get* but forfeits her *ketubah*.

## Vows Without Tnai

### Why Reject Vows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתוּבָּה הוּא דְּלָא בָּעֲיָא, הָא גִּיטָּא — בָּעֲיָא. וּמַאי שְׁנָא כְּתוּבָּה דְּלָא בָּעֲיָא — דְּאָמַר: אִי אֶפְשִׁי בְּאִשָּׁה נַדְרָנִית, אִי הָכִי גֵּט נָמֵי לָא תִּיבְּעֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about this *halakha* according to Shmuel: The mishna says that **she does not require** or receive payment of her **marriage contract, but** one can infer that **she does require a bill of divorce. And what is different** about **a marriage contract that she does not require** payment? **Because he says: I do not want a vowing wife,** and therefore the marriage is considered a mistaken transaction. **If so, she should also not require a bill of divorce.** Since he is clearly particular about this, shouldn't the betrothal also be considered a mistaken transaction?

The *gemara* presses further on the second clause — the case where no *tnai* was stated. If the man never said anything, why should we assume he objects to vows sufficiently to justify his withholding the *ketubah*?

### Taos Analysis

This raises the possibility that a woman with vows should be regarded as *kiddushei ta'os* — a mistaken betrothal. If men universally object to vowed wives, then perhaps the *kiddushin* itself is void from the outset, and she should not even need a *get*. *D'amar* — since we say *ee efshi b'isha nadranit*, I cannot accept a wife who takes vows, why does this not void the marriage entirely? *Get nami lo tiba'ei* — why should a *get* be required at all if it is a clear case of *kiddushei ta'os*?

### Safek Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: צְרִיכָה גֵּט מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: צְרִיכָה גֵּט מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם. רָבָא אָמַר: תַּנָּא סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ, גַּבֵּי מָמוֹנָא לְקוּלָּא, גַּבֵּי אִיסּוּרָא לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: She requires a bill of divorce from the words of** the Sages, i.e., by rabbinic law. Although by Torah law the betrothal is in fact invalid, the Sages declared that since he did not explicitly stipulate the condition, she requires a bill of divorce. **And similarly, Rav Ḥisda said: She requires a bill of divorce from the words of** the Sages. **Rava said** a different explanation: The ***tanna* is uncertain** about the status of the betrothal in this case. **Concerning monetary matters,** one **should be lenient.** Therefore, she cannot extract money from the husband for the marriage contract based on the principle that in monetary cases the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. But **concerning prohibitions** such as adultery, one **must be stringent,** and she therefore requires a bill of divorce.

*Amar Rabba*: it is not a clear-cut *kiddushei ta'os*. Rather, it is a *safek* — a genuine uncertainty as to whether the *kiddushin* was valid. We are not certain whether a man's objection to *nedarim* is so universal and absolute that we can declare the *kiddushin* void without any prior stipulation. *Sfuki maspka lei*. *V'chen amar Rav Chisda tzricha get midivrehem.* Rabba and Rav Chisda both rule that a *get* is required, though the nature of that requirement is now qualified by the *safek*.

### Issura-Mamona Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּטָעוּת שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים. אֲבָל בְּטָעוּת אִשָּׁה אַחַת — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: The dispute** between Rav and Shmuel **is in** the case of **an error** concerning **two women.** The same man married one woman on condition that she had no vows incumbent upon her and then married another woman without specification. Rav and Shmuel disagree whether the condition he explicitly stated for the first woman should be seen as proof that he is particular about this with regard to the second one as well, to the point that the betrothal is invalid if the condition was not fulfilled. **But in** the case of **an error** concerning **one woman,** where he betroths her with a condition, marries her without specification, and subsequently discovered that the condition was not fulfilled, **all agree,** including Rav, **that she does not require a bill of divorce from him.**

The practical consequence of this *safek* is a well-known halachic split between *mamona* and *issura*. *Lgabei mamona l'kula* — on the monetary question of whether the *ketubah* must be paid, we rule leniently: since we are uncertain whether the *kiddushin* was binding, she bears the burden of proof, and *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* — the one seeking to extract money must prove entitlement. She cannot collect the *ketubah*. *Gabi issura lachumra* — on the question of personal status, however, where the concern is *safek eshet ish*, we are stringent: she requires a *get* because the *kiddushin* may have been fully valid.

## Intermediate Position

### No Middle Ground

What is striking here — and the *gemara* does not resolve it — is that the *gemara* does not recognize an intermediate category of *hakpadah*. One might have thought there exists a middle ground: not quite *kiddushei ta'os* (which would void the *kiddushin* entirely), but enough of an objection that the man is entitled to divorce her without paying the *ketubah*. The *kiddushin* would be valid and binding, yet her failing to disclose the *nedarim* would constitute a kind of fault on her part sufficient to justify divorcing her without *ketubah*.

### Warranty Analogy

Consider a parallel from commerce: if a person purchases a car and discovers a major defect, we might say it is a *mekach ta'os* — the entire transaction is void. But if the defect is minor — the CD player does not work — it is not a *mekach ta'os*, yet the buyer is still entitled to compensation or repair. There is a middle tier. By analogy, one could have imagined a case of *nedarim* where the man cannot say "we were never married," yet can legitimately say "this was not what I agreed to, and I am entitled to end the marriage without paying the *ketubah*."

### Gemara's Limits

The *gemara* does not pursue this middle path. Instead, it limits itself to two options: either it is a *safek kiddushei ta'os* — in which case we split the ruling between *mamona* and *issura* — or it is a *get midivrehem*, a rabbinically required *get*, without entitlement to *ketubah*. Notably, earlier in the *masechet*, the *gemara* did grapple with an analogous middle category: the case where a man married a woman believing her to be a *betulah* and discovering she was not. There, the *gemara* entertained the possibility of a binding *kiddushin* combined with reduced *ketubah* entitlement. It is therefore genuinely puzzling that here the *gemara* does not deploy similar reasoning, and no explicit resolution is offered for this apparent gap in the analysis.

## Rava Limits Debate

### Two Women Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּטָעוּת שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים. אֲבָל בְּטָעוּת אִשָּׁה אַחַת — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: The dispute** between Rav and Shmuel **is in** the case of **an error** concerning **two women.** The same man married one woman on condition that she had no vows incumbent upon her and then married another woman without specification. Rav and Shmuel disagree whether the condition he explicitly stated for the first woman should be seen as proof that he is particular about this with regard to the second one as well, to the point that the betrothal is invalid if the condition was not fulfilled. **But in** the case of **an error** concerning **one woman,** where he betroths her with a condition, marries her without specification, and subsequently discovered that the condition was not fulfilled, **all agree,** including Rav, **that she does not require a bill of divorce from him.**

*Amar Rava*: the *gemara* now presents Rava's attempt to narrow the scope of the Rav-Shmuel debate. *Machlokes b'ta'os shtei nashim* — the dispute applies only in the case of two women. That is: a man made a *tnai* with one woman, that *tnai* was not met or the *kiddushin* was invalid, and he then went ahead and married a different woman without stating any condition. The question is whether the *tnai* he made with the first woman is somehow presumed to apply to his marriage with the second woman as well. In that peculiar scenario, Rav and Shmuel disagree.

### One Woman Consensus

*Aval b'isha achat divrei hakol eina tzricha heimenu get* — but in the straightforward case of one woman, where he made a *tnai* with her at *kiddushin* and proceeded to *nisu'in* with her, everyone agrees the *tnai* remains operative. If the condition was not met, she does not need a *get*, because the *tnai* at *kiddushin* naturally carries through to the *nisu'in* stage. Rava's reading thus fundamentally limits Rav's position: Rav agrees that a properly framed *tnai* at *kiddushin* binds the entire subsequent relationship, including the *be'ilah*.

### Abaye Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּטָעוּת אִשָּׁה אַחַת הִיא, וְקָמוֹתְבִינַן תְּיוּבְתָּא מִינַּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But the mishna** discusses **an error** concerning **one woman,** as a second woman is not mentioned, **and we raise an objection from it** against Shmuel, implying that Rav and Shmuel have a dispute in this case as well. How, then, can you say that dispute is in the case of an error concerning two women?

*Amar Abaye*: Abaye challenges this immediately. *V'ha matnisin b'isha achas hi* — our *mishnah* deals with one woman, and we have just used it to challenge Shmuel's position. This presupposes that Rav and Shmuel's debate is relevant to the single-woman case. If Rava is correct that both Rav and Shmuel agree in the one-woman case, why would the *mishnah* be cited as a challenge to Shmuel at all? Rashi notes that an implicit consensus seems to have formed in the course of the *gemara* that the debate is indeed applicable to the one-woman case, precisely because we challenged Shmuel from our *mishnah* — which involves only one woman.

### Rava Refines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִתְּמַר הָכִי אִתְּמַר, אָמַר רַבָּה: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּטָעוּת אִשָּׁה אַחַת כְּעֵין שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים. אֲבָל בְּטָעוּת אִשָּׁה אַחַת גְּרֵידְתָּא — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the previous explanation must be retracted and instead the Gemara says: **If** the above statement of Rabba **was said, it was said as follows: Rabba said** that **the dispute** between Rav and Shmuel **is with regard to** a case of **an error** concerning **one woman similar to** an error concerning **two women.** He betrothed one woman conditionally and then divorced her, and subsequently married her again without specification. In this case, Rav and Shmuel dispute whether the assumption is that he intended to nullify the initial condition with the second betrothal or whether he married her the second time based upon the conditions of the first betrothal. **But in** the case of a simple **error only** concerning **one woman,** where he betroths her with a condition, marries her without specification, and then discovers that the condition was not fulfilled, **all agree** that **she does not require a bill of divorce from him.**

Rava accordingly refines his position: *machlokes b'isha achas k'ein shtei nashim* — the debate applies even in the case of one woman, but only when the circumstances resemble those of two women. The case Rava now envisions, according to Rashi, is something like this: a man betrothed a woman with a *tnai*, they had a troubled relationship, he then divorced her before *nisu'in*, and subsequently they reconciled and contracted a new *kiddushin* and *nisu'in* without re-stating the *tnai*. Does the *tnai* from the first *kiddushin* carry over into the second? Rav says it does not — the new *nisu'in* without re-articulation is a fresh, unconditional marriage. Shmuel says the *tnai* from the first round persists.

It is admittedly a strange reading of Rav and Shmuel, and Rava is clearly pressing to say that in an ordinary case — one woman, one *kiddushin*, one *nisu'in* — a *tnai* at *kiddushin* remains operative through *nisu'in* and the *be'ilah*, and this is universally agreed. The extraordinary *chiddush* of Rav, in Rava's reading, is limited to these peculiar edge cases.

## Invalid K. + Sex

### Baraita Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: קִידְּשָׁהּ בְּטָעוּת, וּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְכֵן קָטָן שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׁלַח סִבְלוֹנוֹת לְאַחַר מִיכֵּן — אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, שֶׁמֵּחֲמַת קִדּוּשִׁין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים שָׁלַח. וְאִם בָּעֲלוּ — קָנוּ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר: אִם בָּעֲלוּ לֹא קָנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rabba's statement from a *baraita*: In cases where the man **betrothed her in error, or** he betrothed her with an item worth **less than the value of a *peruta*, and similarly** in a case where there was **a minor boy who betrothed** a woman, **even if** the man **later sent presents [*sivlonot*]** to the bride after he became an adult, **she is not betrothed, because he sent** them **on the basis of the original betrothal. And if** the men in any of these cases **engaged in sexual intercourse** with the woman after betrothal at the appropriate time, **they have acquired** the women as their wives, since they presumably intended the intercourse to serve as betrothal. **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael:** Even **if they engage in sexual intercourse, they have not acquired** the women as wives, since they presumably engaged in these relations based upon the earlier betrothal, which was invalid.

*Aisaivei Abaye* — Abaye challenges from a *baraita*: if a man performed *kiddushin* with a woman in error — *b'ta'us* — or with an object worth less than a *perutah*, such that the *kiddushin* was invalid; or a minor who betrothed — all cases where the *kiddushin* was not binding. *Af al pi sheshalach sivlonos l'achar mikan* — even if after these events he sent gifts (*sivlonos*) to her and her family in the customary manner between *kiddushin* and *nisu'in*, this does not constitute a new act of *kiddushin*. *Shemachmat kiddushin harishon shalach* — because the gifts were sent in the framework of the original (invalid) *kiddushin* and the social custom surrounding it, not with intent to effect a new *kiddushin*.

### Tannaim Dispute

*V'im ba'alu* — but if they subsequently had intercourse (after, for example, the minor became an adult): *kanu* — they are now married, according to one opinion. *Mishum Rabbi Yishmael omer*: *im ba'alu lo kanu* — the act of intercourse does not render them married. This is, the *gemara* observes, the precise dispute of Rav and Shmuel transposed to the level of *Tannaim*: an original *kiddushin* that was not binding, followed by cohabitation — does the *be'ilah* independently create *kiddushin*?

### Same as Rav-Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, דְּכִי טָעוּת אִשָּׁה אַחַת הִיא, וּפְלִיגִי. מַאי לָאו טָעוּת נְדָרִים?
>
> **Translation:** Noting that the first case in the *baraita* is where a man betrothed a woman in error, Abaye asks: **But here** in this *baraita*, **where** the first case **is an error** concerning **one woman, and** the first *tanna* and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda **disagree; what, is it not that** the intention of the phrase: **Betrothed** her in error, is referring to an **error** in the condition with regard to **vows,** where he mistakenly believed she didn't have any vows incumbent upon her? This would constitute a refutation of Rabba's statement that all agree that in a case of an error concerning one woman she is not betrothed.

*Abaye* identifies this *baraita* dispute as identical in structure to the Rav-Shmuel debate. The case in the *baraita* is one of a deficient *kiddushin* followed by *be'ilah*; the question of whether *be'ilah* independently creates *kiddushin* is exactly what Rav and Shmuel argue about.

### Taos v. Tnai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, טָעוּת פָּחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. פָּחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ: קִידְּשָׁהּ בְּטָעוּת וּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה! פָּרוֹשֵׁי קָא מְפָרֵשׁ: קִידְּשָׁהּ בְּטָעוּת כֵּיצַד? כְּגוֹן שֶׁקִּידְּשָׁהּ בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** here it is referring to a halakhic **error,** that he betrothed a woman with an item worth **less than the value of a *peruta*.** The Gemara asks: Concerning the case where the betrothal is with an item worth **less than the value of a *peruta*,** the *baraita* **teaches it explicitly** as a separate case with the following words: If **he betrothed her in error, or** if he betrothed her with an item worth **less than the value of a *peruta*.** The Gemara answers: **It is explaining** what it stated earlier: **What is** the case of one who **betrothed her in error? For example, when he betrothed her with** an item worth **less than the value of a *peruta*,** as he did not engage in sexual intercourse with her based on such a betrothal.

*Rabba* objects: there is a meaningful distinction between *ta'us* and *tnai*. The *baraita* is dealing with a case of genuine error — *ta'us* — where the parties did not realize the original *kiddushin* was invalid. But a case of *tnai*, an explicit condition, is different. When there is an explicit *tnai*, the parties know exactly why the *kiddushin* might not be binding. That explicit framing arguably shapes all subsequent relations as well. Rabba thus maintains that even Rav, who holds that *be'ilah* creates *kiddushin* in a *ta'us* case, would agree that an explicit *tnai* frames all subsequent cohabitation — and that therefore the *baraita* deals with something different from our central case.

## Taos Definition

### Knowledge Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין קִדּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְגָמַר וּבָעַל לְשֵׁם קִדּוּשִׁין. וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין קִדּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְכִי קָא בָעַל, אַדַּעְתָּא דְּקִדּוּשִׁין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים בָּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that **a person knows that betrothal does not take effect with** an item worth **less than the value of a *peruta*. Consequently,** he **decides to engage in sexual intercourse for the purpose of betrothal** and therefore acquires the woman as his wife through these relations. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, **holds** that **a person does not know that betrothal does not take effect with** an item worth **less than the value of a *peruta*, and when he** later **engages in sexual intercourse, he does so on the basis of the original betrothal,** so no new betrothal takes place.

The *gemara* proceeds to clarify the internal logic of the *baraita*'s dispute. One view: *adam yodeia she'ein kiddushin tofsin b'pachot mishaveh pruta* — a person knows that *kiddushin* effected with less than a *perutah* (or by a minor) is invalid. Therefore, when they subsequently have intercourse, *v'gamar u'ba'al l'shem kiddushin* — they do so with conscious intent to create a new, valid *kiddushin*. On this reading, the *be'ilah* is an intentional, knowing act of *kiddushin*. Hence it is binding.

### Ignorance Approach

The opposing view: *ein adam yodeia she'ein kiddushin tofsin b'pachot mishaveh pruta* — people are generally not aware that *kiddushin* with less than a *perutah* is legally defective. They believe themselves to be already validly married. *V'chi ka ba'al adaata d'kiddushin harishonim ba'al* — and when they subsequently cohabit, they do so entirely in reliance on and within the framework of the original *kiddushin*. Since the original *kiddushin* was invalid, the *be'ilah* does not constitute a new act of *kiddushin* — it is framed by the original (defective) *kiddushin* and has no independent effect.

### Condition Frames All

The conceptual distinction Rabba draws between *ta'us* and *tnai* now becomes clearer: in the *ta'us* case, the question is one of knowledge — did the parties know or not know the original *kiddushin* was invalid? In the *tnai* case, that question is irrelevant. When a man explicitly says "I am marrying you only on condition of X," that explicit condition announces itself as the frame for everything that follows, regardless of knowledge. All subsequent cohabitation is colored by that declaration. The man did not make the condition because he was confused or mistaken; he made it deliberately, and its explicit character means it continues to define the relationship even through the *nisu'in* and *be'ilah*. Rabba therefore holds that the *baraita* — which deals with *ta'us*, ignorance of invalidity — cannot resolve the question of *tnai*.

## Father Consent Case

### Father Consent Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״הֲרֵינִי בּוֹעֲלִיךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּרְצֶה אַבָּא״, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא רָצָה הָאָב — מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: רָצָה הָאָב מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, לֹא רָצָה הָאָב אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye again **raised an objection to** Rabba's statement from a *baraita*: If a man said to a woman: **I am engaging in sexual intercourse with you** for the purpose of betrothal **on condition that** my **father will desire** our betrothal, and then he married her without specification, **although the father did not desire** it, **she is** nevertheless **betrothed** through this act of intercourse. **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon:** If **the father desires** it, **she is betrothed,** and if **the father does not desire** it, **she is not betrothed,** since he engaged in intercourse with her based on the initial condition.

*Aisaivei* — the *gemara* raises one further *baraita*. A man says to a woman: *hareini bo'alaich al menat sheyirtzeh abba* — "I am having intercourse with you on condition that your father consents to the marriage." The intercourse is thus explicitly made conditional on the father's agreement.

### Silence Suffices

One opinion holds that despite the non-fulfillment of the stated condition — *af al pi shelo ratza ha'av*, even if the father did not explicitly agree — she is nonetheless *mekudeshet*, married. How so? Because the phrase "on condition that the father agrees" is interpreted to mean *al menat sheyishtok ha'av* — on condition that the father does not object. If the father was silent and raised no objection, that silence constitutes sufficient "agreement," and the condition is deemed met.

### Explicit Agreement

*Mishum Rabbi Yehuda omer*: if the father agreed, *mekudeshet*; if the father did not agree, *eina mekudeshet*. Rabbi Yehuda requires genuine, positive agreement. The condition means what it says: the father must affirmatively consent.

### Parallels Rav-Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, דְּכִי טָעוּת אִשָּׁה אַחַת דָּמֵי, וּפְלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** **But here it is similar to a mistake** concerning **one woman, and they disagree** whether the betrothal is valid.

*V'hacha bachada itteta dami* — and this case, the *gemara* notes, involves one woman with an explicit condition followed by *be'ilah*. And yet there is a *machlokes*: one *Tanna* holds that *be'ilah* renders them married even if the condition was not met, while Rabbi Yehuda holds that the explicit *tnai* continues to control. The *gemara* identifies this as paralleling the Rav-Shmuel dispute: even in the single-woman case, with an explicit *tnai*, followed by *be'ilah*, there is a dispute as to whether the *be'ilah* overrides the *tnai* or the *tnai* frames the *be'ilah*. This challenges Rava's claim that the single-woman case is universally agreed.

The *gemara* qualifies this: the *baraita* dispute may actually hinge not on whether *be'ilah* overrides the *tnai*, but on what the *tnai* means — does "if the father agrees" mean non-objection or affirmative consent? On that reading, both opinions in the *baraita* agree that if the condition is genuinely unmet, she would not be *mekudeshet*; they disagree only about what constitutes fulfillment of the condition.

### Rava's Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּרְצֶה הָאָב״ — עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּשְׁתּוֹק הָאָב, וְהָא שָׁתֵיק לֵיהּ. וּמָר סָבַר ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיֹּאמַר אַבָּא ׳הֵן׳״, וְהָא לָא אָמַר אַבָּא ׳הֵן׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **There, they disagree about this:** One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds that: On condition that** my **father will desire** it, means: **On condition that** my **father is silent. Consequently,** if his father does not protest, the betrothal is valid, **and he was** indeed **silent about it. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, **holds** that it means: **On condition that** my **father says yes, and he did not say yes.** Therefore, the dispute is about the significance of the father's silence in this case.

Rava's ultimate contention, drawing this discussion together, is that when a *tnai* is explicit and clearly stated, it should always continue to frame the outcome — regardless of whether *be'ilah* subsequently takes place. The *be'ilah* does not override an explicit *tnai*; rather, the *tnai* colors all that follows. Where it appears that Rav holds otherwise — that *be'ilah* creates *kiddushin* unconditionally — Rava reads Rav as addressing a different configuration: cases where the situation is not simply one woman with one explicit *tnai*, but something more unusual that resembles the dynamic of two separate women. In the standard case of explicit conditional *kiddushin* followed by *nisu'in* with one woman, Rava maintains, the *tnai* ought to remain operative through all subsequent stages of the relationship. The discussion of this point continues in the next *daf*.
