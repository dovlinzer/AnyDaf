# Menachot 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzitz Meratzeh

### Mishnah: Tamei/Yotzei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִטְמָא הַקּוֹמֶץ וְהִקְרִיבוֹ – הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה, יָצָא וְהִקְרִיבוֹ – אֵין הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה, שֶׁהַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל הַטָּמֵא וְאֵינוֹ מְרַצֶּה עַל הַיּוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **the handful became ritually impure and** despite this the priest **sacrificed it, the frontplate** worn by the High Priest **effects acceptance** of the meal offering, and the remainder is eaten by the priests. If the handful **left** its designated area **and** despite this the priest then **sacrificed it, the frontplate does not effect acceptance.** The reason is **that the frontplate effects acceptance for** offerings sacrificed when **ritually impure and does not effect acceptance for** offerings **that leave** their designated areas.

The Mishnah that opens our discussion introduces a fundamental rule governing the *tzitz*, the golden headplate worn by the *Kohen Gadol*. The Mishnah presents two contrasting cases. In the first, the *kometz* — the handful of flour taken from a *mincha* offering, which serves as the operative element validating the sacrifice — became *tamei* and was nevertheless offered on the *mizbeach*. The ruling is *hatzitz meratzeh*: the *tzitz* renders it acceptable. Despite the *tumah*, the *korban* is valid *b'dieved*.

In the second case, the *kometz* was taken outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, thereby acquiring the status of *yotzei*, and was then offered on the *mizbeach*. Here the ruling reverses: *ein hatzitz meratzeh*. The *tzitz* does not render the *korban* acceptable.

### Tzitz Mechanism

The Mishnah thus establishes a clear asymmetry: the *tzitz* forgives the problem of *tumah* — or more precisely, it alleviates the *tumah* status in a way that preserves the *korban*'s validity — but it does not do so for the disqualification of *yotzei*. It is important to note from the outset what *meratzeh* does and does not mean. The *tzitz* does not in any moral sense absolve the sin; rather, it wipes away the invalidating consequence of the *tumah* with respect to the *korban*'s status. The *korban* becomes *leratzon*, acceptable before God, despite the *tumah* that affected the *kometz* or, in an analogous case, the blood of an animal sacrifice.

## Braita: Tzitz Power

### V'nasa Aharon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְנָשָׂא אַהֲרֹן אֶת עֲוֹן הַקֳּדָשִׁים״ – וְכִי אֵיזֶה עָוֹן הוּא נוֹשֵׂא? אִם תֹּאמַר עֲוֹן פִּיגּוּל – הֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יֵחָשֵׁב״! אִם תֹּאמַר עֲוֹן נוֹתָר – הֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יֵרָצֶה״!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It is written with regard to the frontplate: "And it shall be upon Aaron's forehead, **and Aaron shall bear the sin committed with the sacred items,** which the children of Israel shall hallow, even all their sacred gifts; and it shall be always upon his forehead, that they may be accepted before the Lord" (Exodus 28:38). The Sages expounded: **But which sin does he bear? If you say** he atones for **the sin of *piggul*,** it **is already stated: "It shall not be credited** to him" (Leviticus 7:18). **If you say** he atones for **the sin of *notar*,** it **is already stated** in the same verse: **"It shall not be accepted."**

The Gemara opens with a *braita* deriving the scope of the *tzitz*'s power from the verse *v'nasa Aharon es avon hakodashim* — "and Aaron shall bear the sin of the sacred offerings" (Shemos 28:38). The *tzitz* is the instrument through which Aaron carries, and thereby removes, that sin. But this immediately raises the question: which sin, precisely, does the *tzitz* carry?

### Not Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אֵינוֹ נוֹשֵׂא אֶלָּא עֲוֹן טוּמְאָה, שֶׁהוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ בְּצִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Evidently, the High Priest wearing the frontplate **bears only the sin of impurity** in the offering of an individual. The frontplate is understood to atone for the sin of sacrificing an impure offering, **as its general prohibition was permitted** in certain circumstances, specifically **in** the case of the **community,** since in a situation where the entire community is impure it is permitted to sacrifice impure communal offerings *ab initio*.

The *braita* methodically tests each candidate. Could the *tzitz* cover the sin of *pigul* — a sacrifice rendered invalid through the improper intention to offer or eat it outside its designated time? No. The Torah in that context explicitly states *lo yeichashev* — it shall not be reckoned, categorically invalidated. Where the Torah has spoken an absolute negation, the *tzitz* cannot override it.

### Not Nosar

Could the *tzitz* cover *nosar*, the sin of meat left over beyond its permitted time? Again, no. There the Torah states *lo yeiraize* — it shall not be acceptable. *Tosafos* helpfully notes that the *braita* cannot literally mean the disqualification of *nosar* itself, since the *tzitz* functions to validate the *korban* at the time of the *avodah*, and *nosar* is a problem that arises only afterward. Rather, *Tosafos* understands the *braita* as addressing the intent to eat at the wrong time, analogous to *pigul*. In any case, any form of wrong thought — wrong time, wrong place, or other disqualifying intent — is explicitly excluded by the Torah's own language.

### Tumah Hutra

What remains? *Ha eino nosei ela avon tumah shehutra miklala b'tzibbur* — the *tzitz* carries only the sin of *tumah*, which is itself in certain circumstances overridden or permitted. The principle is *tumah hutra b'tzibbur*: when a communal sacrifice must be brought and the *kohanim* or the offering are *tamei*, the Torah itself permits proceeding. Rabbeinu Tam adds a further dimension: the end of the verse reads *l'ratzon lahem lifnei Hashem* — it shall be *leratzon*, acceptable, before God. This phrasing implies that the *tzitz* can only confer *ratzon* on something that in other circumstances already admits of *ratzon*. Since *tumah* is sometimes overridden and the offering proceeds acceptably, *tumah* is the category that the *tzitz* addresses. This is also why the operative term throughout is *meratzeh* — making it *leratzon*.

## Rav Zeira Challenge

### Yotzei Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: אֵימָא עֲוֹן יוֹצֵא, שֶׁהוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ בְּבָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira objects to this:** Why not **say** that the frontplate atones for the **sin** of sacrificing offerings that **leave** the courtyard and are thereby disqualified, **as its general prohibition was permitted** in certain circumstances, specifically **in** the case of an offering sacrificed on a private **altar** during the period after the Jewish people had entered Eretz Yisrael and before there was an established location for the Tabernacle?

Rav Zeira challenges the *braita*'s conclusion. Perhaps the *tzitz* covers not *tumah* but *yotzei* — the very disqualification the Mishnah contrasted with *tumah*. His reasoning: *shehutar miklalo b'vamos* — during the era when *bamot*, private altars, were permitted, there was no category of *yotzei* at all. When there is no *Beis Hamikdash*, offering outside its boundaries is not only not prohibited but is precisely how sacrifices are brought. Since the Torah in that historical context permits what we otherwise call *yotzei*, one might argue that *yotzei* too "admits of *ratzon*" in other circumstances, and therefore qualifies as the sin the *tzitz* covers.

The question is admittedly a subtle one. During the era of *bamot*, there is no *halacha* of *yotzei* — nothing is taken "out," because there is no defined sacred space from which one can exit. Still, the underlying logic of the question is that since the Torah has not always treated offerings outside a central sanctuary as categorically impermissible, the *psul* of *yotzei* might be considered lighter and thus subject to the *tzitz*'s power.

### Abaye: Lifnei Hashem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לְרָצוֹן לָהֶם לִפְנֵי ה׳״, עָוֹן דְּלִפְנֵי ה׳ – אִין, עָוֹן דְּיוֹצֵא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: The verse states** with regard to the frontplate: "And it shall be always upon his forehead, **that they may be accepted before the Lord"** (Exodus 28:38), teaching that in the case of **a sin** whose general prohibition is permitted **before the Lord,** i.e., in the Temple, **yes,** the frontplate atones for it. But in the case of the **sin** of offerings **that leave** the courtyard, whose general prohibition is not permitted before the Lord, the frontplate does **not** atone for it.

Abaye rejects this proposal by returning to the verse's phrasing. *L'ratzon lahem lifnei Hashem* — the *ratzon* must be achieved *lifnei Hashem*, before God, in the *Beis Hamikdash*. The sin that the *tzitz* addresses must be one that arises *lifnei Hashem*, within the sacred precincts. The disqualification of *yotzei*, by contrast, is defined precisely by the fact that the offering left that space — the problematic act occurred outside, not inside. Accordingly, the verse cannot be read as empowering the *tzitz* to address it.

## Rabbi Ila Challenge

### Smol Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי אִילְעָא: אֵימָא עֲוֹן שְׂמֹאל, שֶׁהוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ile'a objects to this:** Why not **say** that the frontplate atones for the **sin** of performing the service using one's **left** hand instead of one's right, **as its general prohibition was permitted** in certain circumstances, specifically **in** the case of **Yom Kippur,** when the High Priest carries the spoon bearing the incense into the Holy of Holies with his left hand?

Rabbi Ila proposes another candidate. Perhaps the *tzitz* covers the *avon* of *smol* — the disqualification that arises when a *kohen* performs the *avodah* with his left hand rather than his right. Such an *avodah* is invalid. And yet, Rabbi Ila notes, this too admits of an override: *shehutar miklalo b'Yom HaKippurim*. On Yom Kippur, when the *kohen gadol* carries the fire pan in his right hand and the incense pan in his left, the *avodah* with the left hand is not only permitted but required. Since the left hand has been accepted in that context, perhaps *smol* too qualifies as an *avon* that the *tzitz* can address.

### Yom Kippur Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אָמַר קְרָא ״עָוֹן״ – עָוֹן שֶׁהָיָה בּוֹ וּדְחִיתִיו, לְאַפּוֹקֵי יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים דְּהֶכְשֵׁירוֹ בִּשְׂמֹאל הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: The verse states:** "And Aaron shall bear **the sin** committed with the sacred items" (Exodus 28:38), to say that the frontplate atones for **a sin that was** committed **with** the offering **and I deferred it.** This serves **to exclude** the spoon bearing the incense of **Yom Kippur,** where there is no sin that was deferred, **since its proper** performance **is** for the High Priest to hold it **with** his **left** hand, as he must hold both the coal pan and the spoon of incense.

Abaye again provides the decisive rebuttal. *Avon avon shehaya bo vedachtay* — the *tzitz* addresses something that is inherently problematic but is being overridden or pushed aside in other circumstances. *Tumah hutra b'tzibbur* exemplifies this: *tumah* is always an *avon*, always a problem, but under communal necessity the Torah overrides it. The left hand on Yom Kippur is categorically different. On that day, performing the *avodah* with the left hand is not a problem being overridden; it is precisely how the Torah prescribes that the *avodah* must be done. It is not an *avon* at all in that context. There is therefore no parallel with *tumah*, and the *tzitz* cannot address *smol*.

## Avon Hakodashim

### Kodshim vs. Makdishim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: ״עֲוֹן הַקֳּדָשִׁים״, וְלֹא ״עֲוֹן הַמַּקְדִּישִׁין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The frontplate does not atone for the sin of sacrificing an offering with the left hand for a different reason: The verse states: "And Aaron shall bear **the sin committed with the sacred items [*hakodashim*],"** demonstrating that the frontplate atones for a sin inherent in the offering itself, **and not** for **a sin** committed by **those who bring the offering [*hamakdishin*].**

Rav Ashi offers a different and, as he reads it, more straightforward derivation from the verse itself. The verse says *v'nasa es avon hakodashim* — the sin of *hakodashim*, of the sacred objects themselves. This is a remarkable precision. The *tzitz* addresses only *avon hakodashim* — a defect inherent in the sacrificial object — not *avon hamakdishim* — a defect introduced by the action of those who offer it.

This immediately eliminates the case of *smol* without requiring Abaye's argument. If a *kohen* performs the *avodah* with his left hand, the problem lies entirely with his action, not with the sacrifice itself. The *kodashim* — the animal, the blood — bear no intrinsic defect. The problem is entirely in the *makdishim*, the officiating *kohen*. The verse's plain language therefore excludes any *psul avodah* from the *tzitz*'s scope. The *tzitz* addresses only something wrong with the *korban* itself, such as *tumah* affecting the blood or the *kometz*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סִימָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב סִימָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְאֵימָא עֲוֹן בַּעַל מוּם שֶׁהוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ בְּעוֹפוֹת, דְּאָמַר מָר: תַּמּוּת וְזַכְרוּת בִּבְהֵמָה, וְאֵין תַּמּוּת וְזַכְרוּת בְּעוֹפוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sima, son of Rav Idi, said to Rav Ashi; and some say** that it was **Rav Sima, son of Rav Ashi,** who said **to Rav Ashi: But** why not **say** that the frontplate atones for **the sin of a blemished animal** that is sacrificed, **as its general prohibition was permitted** in certain circumstances, specifically **in** the case of **birds? As the Master says:** The *halakha* that an offering must be **unblemished and** the *halakha* that a burnt offering must be **male** are taught **with regard to animal** offerings, **but** there is **no** requirement that an offering must be **unblemished and male with regard to birds.**

Rav Simi son of Rav Idi (and some say, Rav Simi son of Rav Ashi) then pressed Rav Ashi: perhaps the *tzitz* covers the case of a *baal mum* — a blemished animal — which is also a defect of the *kodashim* themselves. And where do we find that *baal mum* "admits of *ratzon*" in some circumstances? With birds. The Torah requires *tamim* (unblemished) and *zachar* (male) only for animal offerings — *tamus vezachros bivehema* — but for bird offerings, the Torah does not require either. So there is a sense in which *mum* is not universally an *avon* of the sacrifice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עָלֶיךָ אָמַר קְרָא, ״לֹא יֵרָצֶה״, ״כִּי לֹא לְרָצוֹן יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: With regard to your** claim, **the verse states** about blemished animals: **"It shall not be accepted"** (Leviticus 22:23), and: "But whatsoever has a blemish that you shall not bring; **for it shall not be acceptable for you"** (Leviticus 22:20), teaching that in no case are blemished animals accepted as offerings, even due to the frontplate.

Rav Ashi responds: the Torah itself preempts this argument. In the context of *baal mum*, the verse states *lo yeratzeh* and *ki lo leratzon yihiyeh lachem* — it shall not be acceptable, the precise opposite of *leratzon*. Just as *pigul* and *nosar* were eliminated by explicit Torah negations, *baal mum* is eliminated the same way. What remains, definitively, is *tumah* — and only *tumah* — as the *avon* the *tzitz* is empowered to address.

## Shogeg/Mezid Distinction

### Dam Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דָּם שֶׁנִּטְמָא וּזְרָקוֹ בְּשׁוֹגֵג – הוּרְצָה, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא הוּרְצָה. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּיָחִיד, אֲבָל בְּצִיבּוּר, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד – הוּרְצָה, וּבְגוֹי, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד, בֵּין בְּאוֹנֶס בֵּין בְּרָצוֹן –
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **blood** of an offering **that became impure and** a priest **sprinkled it** on the altar, if he did so **unwittingly,** the offering is **accepted** and effects atonement for the owner of the offering. If he sprinkled the blood **intentionally,** the offering is **not accepted. In what** case **is this statement said? In** the case of the offering of **an individual. But in** the case of **a communal** offering, **whether** he sprinkled the blood **unwittingly** or **whether** he did so **intentionally,** the offering is **accepted. And in** the case of an offering of **a gentile** where the blood of the offering became impure, **whether** the priest sprinkled the blood **unwittingly** or **whether** he did so **intentionally, whether** he did so **due to circumstances beyond his control** or **whether** he did so **willingly,**

A second *braita* introduces an important qualification. *Dam shenitma v'zarko beshogeg hurtzah, b'meizid lo hurtzah* — blood that became *tamei* and was then sprinkled: if done *b'shogeg*, unintentionally — either because the *kohen* was unaware of the *tumah* or did not realize it was prohibited to proceed — the *tzitz* is *meratzeh* and the *korban* is valid. If done *b'meizid*, intentionally, the *tzitz* is not *meratzeh*.

This requires clarification. The Gemara will make clear that the *shogeg/meizid* distinction here is not a matter of *d'oraita* law — the *tzitz* in principle operates regardless of intent. Rather, the exclusion of the *meizid* case is a Rabbinic *knas*, a fine: we do not wish the sinner to benefit from his violation (*choteh niskar*), so the Rabbis imposed the penalty of invalidating the *korban* when the *tumah* was knowingly disregarded. The *korban* must then be brought again.

### Yachid vs. Tzibbur

The *braita* then qualifies the qualification. The *braita* addresses when a *tamei* person incurs *kares* for eating *basar kodshim*. One might have thought: if a *tamei* person ate meat of a *korban* before *zerikas damim* — before the blood was sprinkled on the *mizbeach* — he would be liable for *tumah*. Perhaps the verse's prohibition applies even in that case.

The *braita* rejects this. The verse says *kol tahor yochal basar* — only one who is *tahor* may eat the meat — and then describes the *tamei* person who eats from *zevach hashelamim* as incurring *kares*. The *braita* derives: the operative category is *hanitur letahorim* — meat that has been made permissible for *tahorim*. Only meat that has reached that state of permissibility is subject to the *kares* prohibition for a *tamei* person eating it. Before *zerikas damim*, the meat is not yet permitted even to *tahorim*; it therefore does not fall within the verse's prohibition, and a *tamei* person who eats it does not incur *kares* for *tumah* (though it remains *assur* and may implicate *me'ilah*).

### Oved Kochavim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הוּרְצָה.
>
> **Translation:** the offering is **not accepted,** as the verse states with regard to the frontplate: "That it may be accepted for them before the Lord" (Exodus 28:38), with the term "for them" teaching that this applies only for Jews, not for gentiles.

A non-Jew (*oved kochavim*) may bring a *korban*, and the *braita* addresses that case as well. *Bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid bein b'ones bein b'ratzon, lo hurtzah* — whether unintentional or intentional, whether under compulsion or willfully, the *tzitz* does not render a non-Jew's *korban* acceptable when there is a problem of *tumah*. This is understood as a *d'oraita* limitation: the verse says *l'ratzon lahem*, "for them" — specifically for *Bnei Yisrael*.

One might wonder: why does it matter whether a non-Jew's *korban* is *hurtzah*? Two practical implications present themselves. First, a non-Jew may have taken a vow and depends on the *korban* to fulfill it; whether the *korban* is valid bears on whether that obligation is discharged. Second, and more tangibly, the *kohanim* may not eat the meat unless the *korban* was valid. Since a non-Jew can sanctify a *korban*, the question of validity determines whether the meat is permitted.

## Braitot Conflict

### Meat/Chelev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ורְמִינְהִי: עַל מָה הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה? עַל הַדָּם וְעַל הַבָּשָׂר וְעַל הַחֵלֶב שֶׁנִּטְמָא, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד, בֵּין בְּאוֹנֶס בֵּין בְּרָצוֹן, בֵּין בְּיָחִיד בֵּין בְּצִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita*: **For what does the frontplate** worn by the High Priest **effect acceptance?** It effects acceptance **for the blood, for the flesh, and for the fat** of an offering **that became impure** in the Temple, **whether** they were rendered impure **unwittingly or intentionally, whether due to circumstances beyond one's control or willfully, whether in** the case of the offering of **an individual or in** the case of **a communal** offering. In contrast to the statement of the previous *baraita*, this *baraita* teaches that the frontplate does effect acceptance in the case of an individual offering for blood that became impure and was sprinkled intentionally.

The Gemara now introduces a further *braita* that complicates the picture. *Al mah tzitz meratzeh? Al hadam, v'al habasar, v'al hachelev shenitma* — what does the *tzitz* make acceptable? Blood, meat, and fat that became *tamei*. This is a significant expansion. The validity of a *korban* hinges on the blood (*dam*) or, in the case of a *mincha*, the *kometz* — not on the meat or the fat (*chelev*). The *chelev* is burnt on the *mizbeach* and the meat is consumed by the owners, but neither is what makes the *korban* valid. To say that the *tzitz* is *meratzeh* for meat and *chelev* suggests that its operation extends beyond validating the *korban* itself to somehow alleviating the *avon* associated with those elements. This is a conceptually new dimension of the *tzitz*'s power, to which we will return.

Moreover, this *braita* says the *tzitz* works *bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid bein b'ones bein b'ratzon, bein b'yachid bein b'tzibbur* — in all cases, with no distinction for individuals between *shogeg* and *meizid*.

### Contradiction Emerges

The contradiction is stark. The first *braita* restricts the *tzitz*'s effectiveness to *shogeg* for individuals, while this second *braita* applies it in all cases without distinction.

### Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הָא רַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין תּוֹרְמִין מִן הַטָּמֵא עַל הַטָּהוֹר, וְאִם תָּרַם בְּשׁוֹגֵג – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה, בְּמֵזִיד – אֵין תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which teaches that the frontplate effects acceptance for impure blood of an individual offering that was sprinkled intentionally, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** whereas **that** *baraita*, which teaches that the frontplate does not effect acceptance, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not separate *teruma* from ritually impure** produce **for ritually pure** produce. **And if he separated *teruma*** from impure produce **unwittingly, his *teruma* is** considered ***teruma*,** but if he did so **intentionally,** the Sages penalize him and **his *teruma* is not *teruma*. Rabbi Yosei says: Whether** he did so **unwittingly or intentionally, his *teruma* is *teruma*.** Like the ruling found in the second *baraita*, Rabbi Yosei does not distinguish between a case where one acted unwittingly and where one acted intentionally.

Rav Yosef proposes the straightforward resolution: the two *braitot* reflect a *machloket*. One represents the position of *Rabbanan* who impose a *knas* for *meizid*; the other represents Rabbi Yosi, who does not. The analogy for this debate is the *terumah* case.

---

## Terumah Parallel

### Terumah Min Tamei

The *braita* used to establish this pattern: one may not separate *terumah* from *tamei* grain to cover *tahor* grain — that is, one should not take *terumah* from the *tamei* pile when the *tahor* pile is available, thereby giving the *kohen* *terumah teme'ah* when *terumah tehorah* was possible. *V'im taram beshogeg terumato terumah, b'meizid ein terumato terumah* — if one did so *b'shogeg*, the *terumah* takes effect; if *b'meizid*, Rabbinically it does not. This is the *knas* for intentional violation.

### Rabbi Yosi: Mezid OK

Rabbi Yosi dissents: *bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid terumato terumah* — even if done intentionally, the *terumah* takes retroactive effect. Rabbi Yosi does not impose the *knas*. The structural parallel is clear: a case involving *kodesh* in some sense (*terumah*), a problem of *tumah*, and a debate over whether *meizid* is penalized. Rav Yosef therefore identifies the *braita* that permits *meizid* with Rabbi Yosi, and the *braita* that restricts to *shogeg* with the *Rabbanan*.

## Attribution Debate

### Rabbi Yosi/Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי דְּלָא קָנֵיס, דִּמְרַצֵּה צִיץ עַל אֲכִילוֹת מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל אֲכִילוֹת, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵין הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל אֲכִילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: You can **say that you heard** that **Rabbi Yosei** holds **that** the Sages **do not penalize** him. **Did you hear him** say, as the *baraita* teaches, **that** the **frontplate effects acceptance for** the impurity of the portions of offerings that are to be **eaten? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: The frontplate effects acceptance for** the impurity of the portions of offerings that are to be **eaten,** and **Rabbi Yosei says: The frontplate does not effect acceptance for** the impurity of portions of offerings that are to be **eaten?**

The Gemara scrutinizes this attribution carefully. Even granting that Rabbi Yosi's *terumah* position aligns with not penalizing *meizid*, the second *braita* says two things: it works even *b'meizid*, and it works for meat and *chelev*. Attributing the *braita* to Rabbi Yosi would therefore require Rabbi Yosi to hold both positions. Does Rabbi Yosi indeed say that the *tzitz* is *meratzeh* for *achilos*, for the things being consumed?

A named *braita* speaks directly to this question. *Rabbi Eliezer omer: hatzitz meratzeh al ha'achilos* — Rabbi Eliezer says the *tzitz* is *meratzeh* for *achilos*. *Rabbi Yosi omer: ein hatzitz meratzeh al ha'achilos* — Rabbi Yosi says it is not. The explicit attribution is thus exactly the reverse of what Rav Yosef's attribution would require: it is Rabbi Eliezer who extends the *tzitz* to meat, and Rabbi Yosi who restricts it.

---

## Ipoch: Reverse Names

### Proposed Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיפוֹךְ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל אֲכִילוֹת, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל אֲכִילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Reverse** the opinions, so that **Rabbi Eliezer says: The frontplate does not effect acceptance for** the impurity of the portions of offerings that are to be **eaten,** and **Rabbi Yosei says: The frontplate does effect acceptance for** the impurity of portions of offerings that are to be **eaten.**

The Gemara suggests: *ipoch* — reverse the names. Given that this is an oral tradition and names can become transposed, perhaps it was Rabbi Yosi who said the *tzitz* works for *achilos* and Rabbi Eliezer who said it does not.

If reversed, the second *braita* — which says the *tzitz* works for blood, meat, and fat without regard to intent — could consistently be attributed to Rabbi Yosi: he neither penalizes *meizid* nor restricts the *tzitz* to the *korban*-validating elements.

---

## Rav Sheshet Refutes

### Cannot Reverse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: וּמִי מָצֵית אָפְכַתְּ לַהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל בָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּטְמָא לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet objects to this: And are you able to reverse** the opinions and say that according to Rabbi Eliezer the frontplate does not effect acceptance for the impurity of portions that are to be eaten? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought **that** one who partakes of **impure sacrificial meat,** i.e., one who partakes of the meat while in a state of ritual impurity, **before the sprinkling of the blood** takes place, is **liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.**

Rav Sheshet objects that the reversal is impossible. An independent source establishes that it is indeed Rabbi Yosi who holds that the *tzitz* does not work for *achilos* — so the original attribution cannot be reversed. The names as transmitted are accurate: Rabbi Eliezer extends the *tzitz* to meat; Rabbi Yosi does not.

## Tamei Eats Meat

### Yachol Basar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל טָהוֹר יֹאכַל בָּשָׂר״, ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכַל בָּשָׂר מִזֶּבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים אֲשֶׁר לַה׳ וְטֻמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו וְנִכְרְתָה״, הַנִּיתָּר לִטְהוֹרִין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה,
>
> **Translation:** To counter this, **the verse states: "Every one that is ritually pure may eat of it"** (Leviticus 7:19), and immediately afterward the verse states: **"But the soul that eats of the meat of the sacrifice of peace offerings, that belong to the Lord, having his impurity upon him,** that soul **shall be cut off** from his people" (Leviticus 7:20). The juxtaposition of these verses teaches that if one who is impure partakes of **that which has become permitted to those who are ritually pure,** he is **liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.**

At this point the Gemara introduces a *braita* on what might appear to be a different topic, but which will prove essential to identifying the authorship of the disputed *braitot*. The *braita* addresses when a *tamei* person incurs *kares* for eating *basar kodshim*. One might have thought: if a *tamei* person ate meat of a *korban* before *zerikas damim* — before the blood was sprinkled on the *mizbeach* — he would be liable for *tumah*. Perhaps the verse's prohibition applies even in that case.

The *braita* rejects this. The verse says *kol tahor yochal basar* — only one who is *tahor* may eat the meat — and then describes the *tamei* person who eats from *zevach hashelamim* as incurring *kares*. The *braita* derives: the operative category is *hanitir letahorim* — meat that has been made permissible for *tahorim*. Only meat that has reached that state of permissibility is subject to the *kares* prohibition for a *tamei* person eating it. Before *zerikas damim*, the meat is not yet permitted even to *tahorim*; it therefore does not fall within the verse's prohibition, and a *tamei* person who eats it does not incur *kares* for *tumah* (though it remains *assur* and may implicate *me'ilah*).

### Sha'at Kosher Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיתָּר לִטְהוֹרִין, אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if one who is impure partakes of **that which is not permitted to those who are pure,** he is **not liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.** Since it is not permitted to eat the sacrificial meat before the sprinkling of the blood, one who partakes of it at that point is not liable to receive *karet* for eating it while ritually impure.

The *braita* then refines the principle. One might ask: does "permitted to *tahorim*" mean currently permitted, or does it mean that the meat once had a moment of permissibility — a *sha'at kosher*? This distinction matters for cases like *lan* (meat left overnight, becoming *nosar*) and *yotzei* (meat taken outside the *Beis Hamikdash*). In those cases, the blood was sprinkled and the meat had a moment when *tahorim* could eat it, but subsequently became forbidden. A *tamei* person who eats such meat at that later stage: is he liable?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא: נֶאֱכָל לִטְהוֹרִין – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל לִטְהוֹרִין – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה, אוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֶת הַלָּן וְאֶת הַיּוֹצֵא, שֶׁאֵינָן נֶאֱכָלִין לִטְהוֹרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Or** perhaps, **is** the verse teaching **only** that if one who is impure partakes of that which **is eaten by those who are ritually pure,** he is **liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure; but** in a case **where** he partakes of **that which is not eaten by those who are ritually pure,** he is **not liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure** despite the fact that its blood has already been sprinkled? **I would** then **exclude** sacrificial meat **that** was **left overnight and** meat **that leaves** the Temple courtyard, **which are not** permitted to be **eaten by those who are ritually pure,** and I would derive that one who is impure who partakes of them is not liable for eating them.

The *braita* includes an expansive term — *asher la'Hashem riba* — to extend liability to such cases. Even though the meat is currently not permitted to *tahorim* (because it is *lan* or *yotzei*), since it once had a *sha'at kosher* when the blood was thrown, a *tamei* person who eats it incurs *kares*. The *braita* simultaneously limits: *mizevach hashelamim* serves as a narrowing term. The Gemara then frames the rule: include cases that had a *sha'at kosher* (*lan* and *yotzei*); exclude cases that never had one (*pigul*, which was disqualified from the very moment of the *machshava*).

### Tamei at Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לַה׳״ – רִיבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "That belong to the Lord,"** which teaches that the verse **included** leftover meat and meat that leaves the Temple courtyard in the prohibition, and one who partakes of them while impure is liable for partaking of them.

A further question arises within the *braita*. What if the meat became *tamei* before *zerikas damim* and was then eaten after *zerikas damim*? At the moment of *zerikas damim*, the meat was *tamei* — it was never *nitir l'tahorim* in the straightforward sense, because *tahorim* could not eat it at that moment. Yet the *braita* implies that a *tamei* person who eats such meat is nonetheless liable. How?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אֶת הַפִּיגּוּלִין וְאֶת הַנּוֹתָרוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought **that I include** in the prohibition **the** meat that was rendered ***piggul*** through one's intention of consuming it after its designated time **and the** meat that was rendered ***notar*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת אֵלּוּ וּלְהוֹצִיא אֶת אֵלּוּ? אַחַר שֶׁרִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב וּמִיעֵט, אָמַרְתָּ: מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי אֶת אֵלּוּ שֶׁהָיְתָה לָהֶן שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֶת אֵלּוּ שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לָהֶן שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **And what did you see to include these,** i.e., leftover meat and meat that leaves the courtyard, **and to exclude those,** i.e., *piggul*? It answers: **After the verse included** some offerings **and excluded** others, **you** should **say** the following: **I include these,** the leftover meat and the meat that leaves the Temple courtyard, **as they had a period of fitness** after their blood was sprinkled, before they were rendered unfit by being left over or by leaving the Temple courtyard. **And I exclude those,** *piggul*, **as they never had a period of fitness,** as they were already unfit when the blood was sprinkled.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תֹּאמַר: בָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּטְמָא לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים וַאֲכָלוֹ לְאַחַר זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, מִפְּנֵי מָה חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **And if you say** that if that is the case, then with regard to sacrificial **meat that became impure before the sprinkling of the blood, and** one who was impure **ate it after the sprinkling of the blood, for what** reason is he **liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition against partaking of the meat while **ritually impure** if it never had a period of fitness? The answer is that he is liable **because the frontplate effects acceptance** and the sprinkling is valid.

*Mipnei shehatzitz meratzeh* — because the *tzitz* is *meratzeh*. This is the pivotal statement. The *tzitz*'s *meratzeh* on the meat means that we treat the meat as though the *tumah* has been alleviated at the level of status. Even though no *tahor* person could literally eat the meat at the moment of *zerikas damim* (since it was *tamei*), the *tzitz* retroactively alleviates that *tumah* status, and we regard the meat as having had a *sha'at kosher*. Consequently, it falls within the category of *nitir l'tehorim*, and a *tamei* person who eats it is liable.

## Meratzeh Meaning

### Braita Implies Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְמָא – אִין, יוֹצֵא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** It arises from this *baraita* that if the offering **became impure,** then **yes,** the frontplate effects acceptance; but in the case of sacrificial meat that **leaves** the Temple courtyard, the frontplate **does not** effect acceptance, and therefore it was never considered to have a period of fitness.

The Gemara draws the immediate implication: this *braita*, by invoking *tzitz meratzeh* in the context of meat, demonstrates that the *tzitz* indeed operates on *achilos* — the consumable elements — not only on the *dam* or *kometz* that validates the *korban*.

### Only Tumah Here

At the same time, the *braita*'s discussion is framed exclusively around *tumah*. It does not extend the *tzitz*'s *meratzeh* to *yotzei*. The case of meat that was taken outside the *Beis Hamikdash* and had its *sha'at kosher* is handled without invoking the *tzitz* — liability derives from the *sha'at kosher* analysis alone. The *tzitz meratzeh* is invoked only for the *tumah* scenario. This is fully consistent with the Mishnah's ruling and with the *braita*'s derivation limiting the *tzitz* to *avon tumah*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Auth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: ״אֵין זְרִיקָה מוֹעֶלֶת לַיּוֹצֵא״? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְקָתָנֵי דִּמְרַצֵּה צִיץ עַל אֲכִילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet now states his question: **Who did you hear who says** that the **sprinkling** of the blood is **not effective** in the case of sacrificial meat that **leaves** the Temple courtyard? This is the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** as seen in tractate *Me'ila* (6b), **and** yet although this *baraita* is then clearly in accordance with his opinion, it **teaches that** the **frontplate effects acceptance for** the impurity of the portions of offerings that are to be **eaten.** Therefore, this too is the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, and the opinions in the *baraita* cited above should not be reversed.

The Gemara now observes that this *braita* — which says the *tzitz* works for *achilos* and simultaneously limits it to *tumah* (not *yotzei*) — must be attributed to Rabbi Eliezer. The reasoning: a position that the *tzitz* does not apply to *yotzei* is explicitly associated with Rabbi Eliezer in the context of *me'ilah* (*ein zerika mo'eles l'yotzei — Rabbi Eliezer*). The *braita* before us adopts precisely this view: *chad mishum tumah v'chad mishum yotzei lo* — the *tzitz* addresses *tumah*, not *yotzei*. And the *braita* also says the *tzitz* works on *achilos*. Therefore, this *braita* is Rabbi Eliezer, and it confirms that Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *tzitz* is *meratzeh* on meat.

This makes the reversal (*ipoch*) proposed earlier impossible. The *braita* naming Rabbi Eliezer as holding *tzitz meratzeh al ha'achilos* is confirmed by this independent source.

## Rav Chisda: RE

### First Braita RE

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ḥisda said:** It is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which teaches that the frontplate effects acceptance for the impurity of the portions of offerings that are to be eaten, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** whereas **that** *baraita*, which teaches that the frontplate does not effect acceptance, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

Rav Chisda now proposes a full resolution. The *braita* at the top of the *amud* — which says the *tzitz* works for blood, meat, and fat without distinction between *shogeg* and *meizid* — is Rabbi Eliezer. This is consistent with the demonstration that Rabbi Eliezer holds *tzitz meratzeh al ha'achilos*.

### RE: Terumah Mezid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דִּמְרַצֵּה צִיץ עַל אֲכִילוֹת, דְּלָא קָנֵיס מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? אִין, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: You can **say that you heard** that **Rabbi Eliezer** holds **that** the **frontplate effects acceptance for** the impurity of the portions of offerings that are to be **eaten,** but **did you hear him** say **that** the Sages **did not penalize** one who acted willfully? The Gemara answers: **Yes. Just as you heard** that **Rabbi Yosei** holds with regard to *teruma* that one who separated impure produce on behalf of pure produce is not penalized, **you heard** that **Rabbi Eliezer** holds the same. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: Whether** one acted **unwittingly or intentionally, his *teruma* is *teruma*.**

Furthermore, Rav Chisda points out, there is a *braita* in which Rabbi Eliezer says exactly what Rabbi Yosi said regarding *terumah*: *Rabbi Eliezer omer bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid terumato terumah* — even if done intentionally, the *terumah* takes effect. Rabbi Eliezer too does not impose the *knas* for *meizid* in the *terumah* case.

### Infer: Korban Mezid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בִּתְרוּמָה דְּקִילָּא, בְּקָדָשִׁים דַּחֲמִירִי מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? אִם כֵּן, הָא אַמַּאן תִּרְמְיַיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: You can **say that you heard Rabbi Eliezer** state this *halakha* **with regard to *teruma*, which** is **lenient,** but **did you hear him** say this **with regard to consecrated** items, **which** are more **severe?** The Gemara answers: **If** it is **so** that Rabbi Eliezer does not hold the same opinion with regard to consecrated items, **to whom will you attribute this** *baraita* that rules that the Sages did not penalize one who acted willfully? Rather, it must be that this is the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer.

Therefore, Rav Chisda concludes, Rabbi Eliezer consistently holds: *b'meizid* is not penalized. The *braita* that says the *tzitz* works even *b'meizid* is Rabbi Eliezer — he holds both that the *tzitz* extends to *achilos* and that *meizid* does not disqualify. The *braita* that restricts to *shogeg* is *Rabbanan*, who impose the *knas*.

---

## Challenge Rav Chisda

### Kalah/Chamimah

The Gemara presses: when Rabbi Eliezer says *b'meizid* does not forfeit the *terumah*, that is by *terumah*, which is halachically lighter (*terumah dekalah*). How can we automatically extend this to *korbanot*, which are weightier (*kadashim dechamimei*)? Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer would be stricter in the *korban* context and would agree with the *Rabbanan* that *meizid* forfeits the *tzitz*'s benefit.

### Braita Proof

Rav Chisda's answer is characteristically practical: we have a *braita* before us that explicitly says the *tzitz* works even *b'meizid* in the context of *korbanot*. We need to find its author. The attribution to Rabbi Eliezer is the most coherent, given his explicit position that the *tzitz* works on *achilos* (confirmed by the independent *braita*) and his established *terumah* ruling. One can theoretically distinguish *terumah* from *korbanot*, but one must still account for the *braita* — and Rabbi Eliezer fits it best.

---

## Ravina: Occur vs. Act

### Tumah Occurred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: טוּמְאָתוֹ, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד – הוּרְצָה; זְרִיקָתוֹ, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – הוּרְצָה, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא הוּרְצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** that the contradiction between the two *baraitot* should be resolved as follows: With regard to the circumstances of the contraction of **its ritual impurity,** regardless of whether the blood was rendered impure **unwittingly or intentionally,** the frontplate effects acceptance for the impurity and the offering is **accepted,** as the second *baraita* teaches. By contrast, with regard to **the sprinkling of** the blood, if it was **unwittingly** sprinkled after becoming ritually impure, meaning that the priest was unaware that it was impure, then the offering is **accepted,** but if it was **intentionally** sprinkled after becoming impure, it is **not accepted,** as the first *baraita* teaches.

Ravina proposes an elegant alternative that dissolves the contradiction between the two *braitot* without requiring a *machloket* at all. His key is a close reading of each *braita*'s precise language. The second *braita* — the one at the top of the *amud* — says *shenitma bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid*, "that became *tamei*, whether *b'shogeg* or *b'meizid*." The subject of the *shogeg/meizid* qualification is the *tumah* occurring — how the *tumah* came about. On this dimension, says Ravina, the *tzitz* works regardless of intent: whether the object became *tamei* accidentally or deliberately, the *tzitz* is still *meratzeh*.

The first *braita*, by contrast, says *dam shenitma v'zarko beshogeg hurtzah, b'meizid lo hurtzah* — "blood that became *tamei* and was sprinkled; *b'shogeg*, it works; *b'meizid*, it does not." Ravina reads the *shogeg/meizid* qualification here as attaching to the *zerika* — the act of sprinkling. The *zerika* must be *b'shogeg*; if the *kohen* knowingly and intentionally performed the *zerika* with *tamei* blood, the *tzitz* does not work.

The two *braitot* thus address different variables and are entirely consistent. The *tumah* may occur *b'meizid* without consequence for the *tzitz* — but the *zerika* must be *b'shogeg*. No *machloket* is required.

## Rav Sheila: Reverse

### Zrika Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב שֵׁילָא אָמַר: זְרִיקָתוֹ, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד – הוּרְצָה. טוּמְאָתוֹ, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – הוּרְצָה, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא הוּרְצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Sheila said** the opposite resolution: With regard to **the sprinkling of** the blood, **whether** it was performed **unwittingly or intentionally,** the offering is **accepted.** By contrast, with regard to the circumstances of the contraction of **its ritual impurity,** if it was rendered impure **unwittingly** the offering is **accepted,** and if it was rendered impure **intentionally** it is **not accepted.**

Rav Sheila presents the precise inverse of Ravina's reading. The *zerika*, says Rav Sheila, may be *b'meizid* without disqualifying the *tzitz* — the act of sprinkling is where leniency applies. It is the *tumah*'s occurrence that must be *b'shogeg*: if the object became *tamei* intentionally, the *tzitz* does not work. Thus the first *braita*'s *shogeg/meizid* distinction applies to how the *tumah* occurred, and the second *braita*'s permissiveness applies to the *zerika*.

## Test Rav Sheila

### Language Check

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב שֵׁילָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: שֶׁנִּטְמָא בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד – הָכִי קָאָמַר: נִטְמָא בְּשׁוֹגֵג וּזְרָקוֹ, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And according to Rav Sheila,** concerning that **which is taught** in the second *baraita*, that the frontplate effects acceptance for blood **that was rendered impure** regardless of **whether** it happened **unwittingly or intentionally, this** is what **it is saying:** If the blood **was rendered impure unwittingly and one sprinkled** its blood, **whether** it was sprinkled **unwittingly or intentionally,** it is accepted.

The Gemara now tests Rav Sheila's reading against the actual language of the *braitot*. The second *braita* — the one that applies *bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid* without restriction — uses the word *shenitma*: "that became *tamei*, whether *b'shogeg* or *b'meizid*." This language speaks directly to how the *tumah* occurred. That is precisely what Ravina says: the *tumah* occurring can be *b'meizid* and the *tzitz* still works. This supports Ravina and undermines Rav Sheila, for whom that *braita* should be about the *zerika*, not the *tumah*.

Rav Sheila is forced to read the *braita* very differently: *shenitma* ("became *tamei*") together with *v'zarko* ("and sprinkled") must be parsed as a single compound unit — the entire event, *tumah* plus *zerika*, is the subject of *bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid*. Rav Sheila would then read: "(if it) became *tamei* and was sprinkled (*shenitma beshogeg*) — whether *b'shogeg* or *b'meizid*" — meaning the *v'zarko* can be *b'meizid* so long as the *nitma* was *b'shogeg*. This is a forced and strained parsing.

Ravina's reading, by contrast, follows the natural structure of the *braita* straightforwardly: *shenitma* (became *tamei*) is qualified by *bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid* — the *tumah*'s occurrence may be either. The *zerika* is addressed in the first *braita*, where *beshogeg hurtzah, b'meizid lo hurtzah* attaches naturally to the act of sprinkling.

---

## Conclusion & Recap

### Ravina Wins

Ravina's reading is the most linguistically consistent with both *braitot*. The language of each *braita* maps cleanly onto the variable Ravina identifies: the second *braita* speaks to how *tumah* occurred, the first to how the *zerika* was performed. No debate between *Tannaim* is required; both *braitot* represent a single coherent position.

### Meratzeh: Status

A deeper point emerges from the discussion as a whole. *Tzitz meratzeh* does not mean moral forgiveness, nor does it mean that the problematic act becomes retroactively permitted. It means the alleviation of a *halachic status*. When the *tzitz* is *meratzeh* for *tumah*, it removes the disqualifying *tumah* status that would otherwise invalidate the *korban* or the offering's subsequent consequences. Similarly, when the *tzitz* is *meratzeh* for the meat, it treats the meat's *tumah* status as though alleviated — not permitting anyone to eat it, but affecting how the law regards its status for other *halachic* purposes.

### Implications

This understanding has concrete *halachic* implications. As we saw from the *braita* about a *tamei* person eating *basar kodshim*: the *tzitz*'s alleviation of the meat's *tumah* status is what allows us to regard the meat as having had a *sha'at kosher*, thereby making a *tamei* person who eats it liable for *kares*. Far from benefiting the offender, the *tzitz meratzeh* on the meat can increase liability. Similarly, the question of whether the *tzitz* is *meratzeh* affects the *me'ilah* status of the meat — if the *tzitz* alleviates the *tumah* and the meat is treated as valid *kodesh*, the laws of *me'ilah* may apply differently than if the *tumah* remains fully in force. The *tzitz* thus operates as a mechanism that adjusts *halachic* status, with consequences that ripple through multiple areas of the laws of *kodashim*.
