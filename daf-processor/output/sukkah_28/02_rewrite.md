# Sukkah 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reb Eliezer Sukkah Rules

### 14 Meals in Sukkah

We pick up at the very top of 27b with the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who maintains that one must eat fourteen meals in a *sukkah* — two each day across all seven days of *Sukkos*. Related to this, Rabbi Eliezer holds that one does not fulfill the obligation with a *sukkah* built on *Chol HaMoed*, nor by visiting a friend's *sukkah*. The same *sukkah* must be used for all seven days, establishing it as a genuine substitute for one's home. This is an exceptionally demanding position, rooted in the idea that the *sukkah* must function as a true replacement dwelling.

### Collapsed Sukkah Rebuilt

There is, however, one concession. The *Gemara* cites the ruling: *umodim she'im nafla shechozer uvona bechol hamoed* — all agree that if one's *sukkah* collapsed, he may rebuild it on *Chol HaMoed* and it is treated as a continuation of the original *sukkah*. The *Gemara* notes that this might seem obvious, but the *chiddush* is that one might have thought the newly rebuilt structure constitutes a separate *sukkah*, disqualified because it was not standing for all seven days. The ruling teaches us that this is not so.

### Verse-Based Reasoning

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Eliezer* — what is the reason behind Rabbi Eliezer's position? The answer is that the verse states *chag hasukkos ta'ase lecha shivas yamim* — you shall make it for yourself, seven days. This implies the *sukkah* must be a seven-day structure. The Rabbis respond that the verse simply means: make a *sukkah* for the *yom tov*, without requiring that it stand for the entire seven days.

A *baraisa* then spells out Rabbi Eliezer's position further: just as one does not fulfill the obligation of *lulav* on the first day with a borrowed *lulav* — since the verse says *ulakachtem lachem*, you shall take for yourselves, meaning from what is yours — so too, one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* in another person's *sukkah*, since the verse says *ta'ase lecha*, it must be yours. The underlying principle seems unified: to qualify as a substitute house, the *sukkah* must be personally yours and continuously so throughout all seven days.

### Tosafos Ownership

*Tosafos* raises a pointed question against Rabbi Eliezer's position: if the *sukkah* must belong to the individual — *mishelcha* — does this mean that every member of a household requires his own separate *sukkah*? Would two people ever be able to fulfill the obligation together in a single *sukkah*? *Tosafos* suggests that perhaps the concept of *teshvu k'ein taduru* — dwelling in a *sukkah* as one dwells in a home — permits household members to share one *sukkah*, since that reflects the natural structure of a household. But beyond family members, partnership with a neighbor would be disqualified. This means, according to Rabbi Eliezer, that one could not *sukkah*-hop: all fourteen meals must be in one's own household *sukkah*.

### Rabbis' Counter-Verse

The Rabbis disagree: *af al pi she'omru ein adam yotzei yedei chovato b'yom tov harishon b'lulavo shel chaveiro, aval yotzei yedei chovato b'sukkato shel chaveiro* — although one cannot fulfill the *lulav* obligation on the first day with a borrowed *lulav*, one may nonetheless fulfill the *sukkah* obligation in another's *sukkah*. Their proof: *kol ha'ezrach b'Yisrael yeshvu basukkos* — all citizens of Israel shall dwell in *sukkos*. From this the *Gemara* derives that all of Israel could potentially dwell in a single *sukkah*, which implies that private ownership is not required; partnership or borrowing suffices.

*Rashi* proposes that perhaps all Israel owns it jointly — but *Tosafos* presses that this only strengthens the contrast with Rabbi Eliezer, who requires genuine individual ownership. The Rabbis hold that once the verse makes clear that partnership is acceptable, borrowing another's *sukkah* is likewise permitted.

The *Gemara* then asks what the Rabbis do with the word *lecha* — "yours" — in the verse. Their answer: it excludes a stolen *sukkah*. A borrowed *sukkah* is permitted by inference from *kol ha'ezrach*. And what does Rabbi Eliezer do with *kol ha'ezrach*, given that he does not permit sharing? He applies it to a novel case: a convert who converted *bein hashmashos* during *Sukkos*, or a minor who came of age *bein hashmashos*. Since Rabbi Eliezer generally requires all seven days, one might have thought these individuals could never fulfill the obligation. The verse teaches that since no other option was available to them, they fulfill the *mitzvah* with whatever portion of the *chag* remains.

The Rabbis respond that since they already hold a *sukkah* built on *Chol HaMoed* is valid, they have no need for a special verse to address this case — it is already subsumed within their general position.

## Learning Dedication Stor…

### No Holiday Visits

The *Gemara* relates a story: Rabbi Ilai once traveled to greet his teacher Rabbi Eliezer in Lod on the *regel*. Rabbi Eliezer said to him: *Ilai eincha mishovsei ha'regel* — Ilai, you are not among those who stay home on the festival. The remark implies disapproval. The *Gemara* notes that even if this was not *Sukkos*, and even setting aside the problem of Rabbi Eliezer's own view that one must be in his own *sukkah* all seven days, Rabbi Eliezer objected to the very act of traveling to visit one's teacher during the *regel*.

### Praise for Staying Home

Why? Because *she'hayah Rabbi Eliezer omer: meshabeach ani et ha'atzlanim she'ein yotzin mibateihem baragel* — Rabbi Eliezer used to say: I praise the lazy people who do not leave their homes on the *regel*. He does not actually admire laziness, of course; the point is more like a stopped clock being right twice a day. Even if one stays home for the wrong reasons, the result — remaining at home during *yom tov* — is the right outcome.

### Household Rejoicing

The basis for this is the verse *v'samachta b'chagecha* — you shall rejoice on your festival — together with the formulation *v'samachta atah u'veisecha*, you and your household. The *mitzvah* of *simchas ha'regel* obligates a man to celebrate the festival together with his wife. This was already a live tension in earlier generations, as men would travel to visit their rebbes or undertake other pilgrimages on *yom tov*, much as people today travel on *Rosh Hashana* to Uman or elsewhere. Rabbi Eliezer's position was that the *mitzvah* begins at home, with one's family.

The *Gemara* raises a counterpoint: Rabbi Yitzchak cites a proof that one is actually *obligated* to greet one's teacher on the *regel*, derived from the story of the *Isha HaShunamis*, whose husband asks: *madua at holechet elav hayom lo chodesh velo Shabbos* — why are you going today? It is neither *Rosh Chodesh* nor *Shabbos*. The implication is that on *Rosh Chodesh* and *Shabbos* one does go to greet a holy man. Rabbi Yitzchak derives from this that on the *regel* as well there is an obligation of *hakbalas penei rabbo*. The *Gemara* resolves the tension: there is no contradiction. If one goes and returns the same day, that is compatible with fulfilling *simchas ha'regel* with one's household. If one leaves and does not return the same day — abandoning one's family for the entire festival — that violates the obligation of *v'samachta atah u'veisecha*.

This discussion opens a broader reflection. The very language *makbil penei rabbo baragel* echoes the *aliya la'regel* — the pilgrimage to the Temple. Post-*churban*, the pilgrimage to encounter the divine is replaced, in some sense, by the act of going to be in the presence of the holy man, the *penei rabbo*. And that stands in tension with the domestic reality of the *regel*, the obligation to experience it within one's household. Rabbi Eliezer weighed in favor of the domestic dimension; the *Gemara* accommodates both, provided the visit is completed within the same day.

## Sheet on Sukkah Story

### Heard from Rebbe Only

The *Gemara* then relates a second *ma'aseh*: Rabbi Eliezer spent a *Shabbos* in the Upper Galilee, in the *sukkah* of Yochanan bar Rabbi Ilai in Caesarea — or, according to some versions, Caesareon. The sun began beating down on the *sukkah*, and his host, concerned for Rabbi Eliezer's comfort, asked: *mahu she'efros aleha sadin* — what is the ruling regarding spreading a sheet over the *sukkah* to provide shade?

Rabbi Eliezer did not answer. Instead, he deflected the question entirely, remarking that there was not one tribe of Israel that had not produced a *shofet* during the period of the *Shoftim*. The sun continued advancing. When it reached the midpoint of the *sukkah*, the question was raised again, and again Rabbi Eliezer deflected — this time noting that every tribe had produced *neviim*. When the sun finally reached Rabbi Eliezer's feet, Yochanan bar Rabbi Ilai had enough; he took the sheet and spread it over the *sukkah* himself. At that point, Rabbi Eliezer *hifshil talito lachorav veyatza* — put on his cloak and walked out of the *sukkah*.

The *Gemara* explains: *lo mipnei she'hifligo bidvarim* — he was not evading the question out of disrespect or unwillingness to teach. Rather, *mipnei shelo amar davar shelo shama mipi rabbo le'olam* — because he had never in his life said anything he had not heard from his teacher. Since his teacher had never ruled on the question of spreading a sheet over a *sukkah* on *Shabbos*, he refused to issue a ruling. He would not say yes, he would not say no, and so he deflected until the situation resolved itself by his walking out.

The irony is not lost: the man who objected to visiting one's teacher on *yom tov* is the very same man who never uttered a ruling he had not received from his teacher.

### Tent-Making Analysis

The *Gemara* then tries to resolve the underlying *halacha* from Rabbi Eliezer's other rulings. Is spreading a sheet over a *sukkah* on *Shabbos* a problem of *oseh ohel arai* — creating a temporary canopy? The sheet was clearly temporary and was being spread over an existing covered structure, so the question is whether even this modest addition constitutes a prohibited *ohel*. The *Gemara* attempts to derive a position from Rabbi Eliezer's ruling in a related case.

### Window Stopper Case

The *Gemara* cites a *mishna*: *pkak hachalon* — the case of a window stopper. If a block is used to fill and seal a window, is inserting it on *Shabbos* problematic because it effectively adds to the wall? Rabbi Eliezer rules: *bizman she'kashur vetalui, pokekin bo* — if the stopper is tied to a string and suspended off the ground, one may stop up the window, since it is already effectively attached, like a hinged element of the wall. But if it is not suspended — even if it is on a string but dragging on the ground — *ein pokekin bo*, one may not use it, because placing it constitutes creating something new. The *chachamim* rule leniently in both cases.

From this it would seem that Rabbi Eliezer should have answered the sheet question directly, presumably prohibiting it as he prohibited the unsuspended window stopper. The *Gemara* explains why he nonetheless refrained: *hatam hu de'mevatel, hacha lo mevatel*. In the window-stopper case, the stopper is being left in place and becomes integrated into the wall. The sheet over the *sukkah* is removed almost immediately once the sun moves; it is far more transitory. Rabbi Eliezer may have reasoned that this case was distinguishable and therefore sufficiently uncertain that he could not rule without a direct tradition from his teacher.

## Learning Dedication St…

### 30 Sukkah Laws

The *Gemara* transitions to another *ma'aseh* involving Rabbi Eliezer. He spent a *Shabbos* — perhaps the same *Shabbos*, perhaps a different one — in the Upper Galilee, and the people there posed thirty *halachos* to him regarding the laws of *sukkah*. The *Gemara* is at the top of 28a. Of the thirty, Rabbi Eliezer said *shamati* — I have a received tradition — for twelve. For the remaining eighteen, he said *lo shamati* — I have no tradition. Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehuda records the inverse: eighteen he had heard and twelve he had not.

When the people challenged him — *kol devarecha einam ela mipi hashemua*, your entire learning amounts to nothing but transmitted traditions, where are your own views? — Rabbi Eliezer responded: *hizkaktuni lomar davar shelo shamati mipi rabosai?* Are you forcing me to say something I never heard from my teachers?

### Always in Beis Midrash

He then offered a defense that was not a justification of limitation but of completeness. *Miyami lo kidmani adam b'veis hamidrash* — in all my days, no one ever preceded me to the *beis midrash*. I was always first. *Velo yashanti b'veis hamidrash lo shinat keva velo shinat arai* — I never slept in the *beis midrash*, not a deep sleep and not even a light nap. *Velo hinachti adam b'veis hamidrash veyatzasi* — I never left the *beis midrash* while anyone else was still there; I was the last to leave. *Velo shachti sichas chulin* — I never engaged in idle conversation. *Velo amarti davar shelo shamati mipi rabbi me'olam* — and I never said anything I had not heard from my teacher.

The *Maharsha*, echoing *Rashi*, explains why Rabbi Eliezer begins with those first points before arriving at the last. The point is not merely that he transmitted faithfully, but that he had ensured total, exhaustive absorption of his teacher's teaching. He was there before anyone else, he stayed later than anyone else, he never wasted a moment. If something is missing from his repertoire, it is because his teacher never taught it — not because Rabbi Eliezer failed to be present for it.

This formulation reflects a distinctive and coherent vision of *Torah she'be'al peh*. Rabbi Eliezer's approach was *masorah* — tradition as the supreme mode of Torah transmission. He would not speculate. He would not derive on his own. He was a faithful, complete conduit. This stands in pointed contrast to the approach of Rabbi Akiva, who was the great embodiment of the creative interpretive tradition, using human reasoning and textual analysis to derive new *halachos* and new understandings. In the famous *aggados* involving the two — especially the deathbed encounter — that contrast emerges with particular sharpness.

There is also a striking internal parallel in Rabbi Eliezer's own character. His insistence that the *sukkah* must be the same *sukkah* all seven days, that one must remain planted in it with total consistency, that one does not travel around even to visit his teacher on *yom tov* — this mirrors his approach to the *beis midrash*: plant yourself, stay, do not move, do not wander. The constancy he demanded of the *sukkah* is the constancy he embodied in his own life of learning.

### Yochanan Ben Zakkai

The *Gemara* then records the *middos* of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, who was Rabbi Eliezer's teacher. *Miyamav lo sach sichas chulin* — he never spoke idle words. *Velo halach arba amos b'lo Torah u'vlo tefillin* — he did not walk four *amos* without Torah and without *tefillin*. The *Maharsha* notes that *tefillin* itself is an embodiment of Torah. *Velo kidmo adam b'veis hamidrash* — no one preceded him to the *beis midrash*. *Velo yashan b'veis hamidrash lo shinat keva velo shinat arai* — he never slept there, not deeply and not lightly. *Velo hirher b'mevo'os hametunofos* — even though his mind was constantly engaged in Torah, when walking through filthy alleyways, he was able to restrain his thoughts and not think Torah there. *Velo hiniach adam b'veis hamidrash veyatza* — he never left the *beis midrash* while others remained. *Velo matza'o adam yoshev vedomeh* — no one ever caught him sitting in silence, doing nothing. *Ela yoshev veshoneh* — he was always sitting and learning.

Then a detail that seems out of place in this list: *velo pasach adam deles letalmidav ela hu b'atzmo* — he himself opened the door for his students, not relying on anyone else to do so. The *Gemara* suggests the resonance: just as the Torah says God is *Elohei ha'elohim* and then immediately describes his care for the orphan and the widow, so here too, alongside his towering greatness, he performed this small act of service for his students. The *Maharsha* adds that there may have been competition among students jostling at the door; Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai wanted to ensure that every student could enter without obstruction — recalling the contrast with the story of Hillel, who was once barred at the door by a guard.

*Velo amar higia et la'amod mi'beis hamidrash* — he never told others it was time to leave the *beis midrash*, except on *Erev Pesach* and *Erev Yom Kippur*, where *mitzvos* demanded it: on *Erev Pesach*, because of the *Korban Pesach* and the obligation to eat the *Seder* while children are awake; on *Erev Yom Kippur*, because of the *mitzvah* to eat. The *Gemara* then notes: *v'chein hayah Rabbi Eliezer talmido noheig acharyav* — Rabbi Eliezer, his student, followed the same practice. The *middos* listed about Rabbi Eliezer earlier were a subset of those practiced by his teacher.

### Yonatan ben Uziel

The *Gemara* now introduces a broader context. *Shemonim talmidim hayu lo l'Hillel haZaken* — Hillel had eighty students. Thirty of them were at such a level that the *Shechinah* could have rested upon them as it did upon Moshe. Thirty were worthy of having the sun stand still for them as it did for Yehoshua. Twenty were *beinonim*. *Gadol shebakulam Yonasan ben Uziel* — the greatest of all was Yonasan ben Uziel, to whom the *Targum* on *Nevi'im* is ascribed. *Katan shebakulam Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* — and the smallest of all of them was Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai.

This is a striking *Gemara*. All of the remarkable qualities we have just catalogued in Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, and he was the *least* of Hillel's eighty disciples. The *Gemara* naturally notes the implication: *gadol shebakulam al achas kamah v'chamah* — how incomparably greater must Yonasan ben Uziel have been.

Of Yonasan ben Uziel: *shebesha'ah she'yosheiv v'oseik baTorah, kol of she'poreiach alav miyad nisraf* — when he would sit and learn Torah, any bird that flew overhead was burned. *Tosafos* explains that his learning had the character of the original *matan Torah*, in which fire accompanied the giving of the Torah at Sinai; the fiery angels hovering above him to participate in his learning consumed any bird that passed over.

The contrast between Yonasan ben Uziel and Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai invites reflection on what is listed as the totality of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's mastery: *Mikra, Mishnah, Gemara, halachos, aggados, dikdukei Torah, dikdukei Soferim, kal v'chomer, gezeirah shavah, tekufos, gematrios, sichas malachei hashareis, sichas sheidim, sichas dekalim, meshalos kovsim, meshalos shualim, davar gadol v'davar katan*. What is the *davar gadol*? *Ma'aseh Merkavah*. What is the *davar katan*? *Havayos d'Abaye v'Rava* — the analytical discussions of the Talmud. The verse it fulfills: *lehancheil ohavai yesh, v'otzroseihem amalei* — to bequeath to those who love me substance, and fill their storehouses.

This passage bears directly on the earlier question about the two paradigms of *Torah she'be'al peh*. One might have assumed that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai — who, like his student Rabbi Eliezer, said he never uttered something not heard from his teachers — was purely a *masorah* figure. But the list of what he mastered does not support that reading. *Gemara, kal v'chomer, gezeirah shavah, havayos d'Abaye v'Rava* — these are not passive transmissions. They are analytical enterprises, requiring the exercise of human reasoning. One cannot derive a *kal v'chomer* or analyze a *gezeirah shavah* purely by receiving and repeating a tradition.

The resolution appears to lie in the complexity of the *rebbe* himself. Different students of a great figure absorb different dimensions of his teaching. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was not simply a conduit; his *takkanos*, his decision to negotiate with the Romans, his reorientation of Jewish life after the *churban* toward *Torah* and *Yavneh* — none of this is the work of a pure *masorah* figure. He embodied both the fidelity of tradition and the creativity of human interpretation. Rabbi Eliezer, his student, absorbed one dimension of that — the *masorah* — and made it the totality of his approach.

This is illuminated by the earlier story of Hillel. When Hillel proved from thirty different derivations that the *Korban Pesach* overrides *Shabbos*, the *Bnei Batera* would not accept it. Only when he said he had a *masorah* from Shemaya and Avtalyon did they capitulate. Hillel's entire career represented the argument that creative interpretation is a legitimate mode of Torah transmission — but he met with resistance. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, his student, carried both modes. Rabbi Eliezer, student of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, appears to have inherited only one.

The *Rambam*'s approach is also relevant here. He uses this very *Gemara* — that *ma'aseh Merkavah* is *davar gadol* and *havayos d'Abaye v'Rava* is *davar katan* — to argue that the proper trajectory of learning is to master *halacha* through *Gemara* study and then devote one's higher energies to philosophy and theology. The *davar katan* is not demeaned; it is the path that gives access to the *davar gadol*. For the *Rambam*, the ultimate goal is the theological and philosophical endeavor, which is accessible to all — even if *ma'aseh Merkavah* in its deepest form is not.

## Exemptions Categories

### Exempt Groups

The *Gemara* turns to a new *Mishnah*: *nashim va'avadim u'ktanim peturim min hasukkah* — women, slaves, and minors are exempt from *sukkah*. This is the standard application of the principle that women are exempt from positive time-bound *mitzvos*. The *Mishnah* then adds: *katan she'eino tzarich le'imo, chayav basukkah* — a minor who no longer needs his mother is obligated in *sukkah*.

### Shammai Stringency

The *Mishnah* records a story: *veyalda kalato shel Shammai haZaken* — Shammai's daughter-in-law gave birth, and Shammai was so stringent that he knocked a hole in the plaster of the ceiling and placed *sechach* directly over the cradle, so that the newborn infant would be in a *sukkah*. He used the principle of *dofen akuma* — the bending wall — to extend the *sukkah* coverage to the area of the crib. This is acknowledged as beyond what the *halacha* requires; even by Shammai's own logic, this was *lechumra* beyond the threshold of the *chinuch* obligation, since the infant was one day old.

### Verse Exclusions

The *Gemara* asks: *mena hani mili* — from where are these exemptions derived? The *baraisa* teaches: the verse states *kol ha'ezrach b'Yisrael yeshvu basukkos* — all *ezrach* among Israel shall dwell in *sukkos*. The word *ezrach* alone would include both men and women. The added letter *hei* — *ha'ezrach* — comes, the *baraisa* says, to exclude women: *lehotzi et hanashim*. Yet the word *kol* — "all" — comes to include minors: *lerabos et haktanim*.

This immediately raises a difficulty. A different *baraisa* states the opposite: in the context of *Yom Kippur*, the verse says *ha'ezrach veha'ger* — and from *ha'ezrach* we include women in the obligation to fast. This implies that *ezrach* alone means men, and *ha'ezrach* is expansive and inclusive. So which is it: does *ha'ezrach* include or exclude women?

Rava responds: *hilchasa ninhu* — both rulings are actually *halachos*, traditional teachings, and the verses are merely *asmachtos*, rabbinic supports. The *Gemara* then presses further: if they are both traditions, and both are linked to verses, why were the verses needed at all? In the case of *sukkah*, the exemption of women follows automatically from the general rule of *mitzvas asei shehazman gerama nashim peturos* — women are exempt from positive time-bound commandments. And in the case of *Yom Kippur*, the obligation of women follows automatically from Rav Yehuda's teaching in the name of Rav: *hishva hakasuv isha le'ish lechol onashin shebatorah* — the Torah equates men and women with respect to all punishments, and since *Yom Kippur* fasting carries the punishment of *kares*, women are equally bound.

### Yom Kippur Comparison

The *Gemara* thus asks why we need either the *masorah* or the verse in each case. Abaye answers: in truth, the exemption of women from *sukkah* is the *halacha*, and the verse is its *asmachta*. But why was a special *halachah* needed to exempt women, given the general principle? Because there was reason to think otherwise.

First: the verse says *teshvu* — you shall dwell — interpreted as *ke'ein taduru*, like your normal dwelling. One's normal dwelling involves both husband and wife. One might therefore have concluded that a *sukkah*, to function as a genuine domestic substitute, must obligate women as well. Hence the special *halachah* is needed to teach that despite this logic, the general rule of positive time-bound exemption applies.

Rava adds a second reason. The *Gemara* derives the obligation to dwell in the *sukkah* on the first night by a *gezeirah shavah* linking the fifteenth of *Tishrei* to the fifteenth of *Nisan*. Since women are obligated in *matzah* on the first night of *Pesach* — because the obligation is tied to the prohibition of *chametz*, which binds everyone — one might have argued that for the first night of *sukkah* as well, women should be obligated. The *halachah* is needed to exclude this inference and confirm that women remain exempt even on the first night.

Turning to *Yom Kippur*: the verse is needed not for the primary obligation to fast, which follows from the general rule. It is needed for the *tosefet inuy* — the additional period of fasting beyond the day itself. Since this extra period carries no punishment and is not independently a negative prohibition, one might have thought women are exempt from it. The verse teaches that since women are obligated in the primary fast, they are equally obligated in the added portion.

The *Rashash* raises an interesting question: why does the *Gemara* not similarly derive that women are obligated in *tosefet* regarding *melacha* — the requirement to cease work slightly before *Yom Kippur* begins? He suggests this may be a basis for the *Rambam*'s position that the biblical obligation of *tosefet* applies to *Yom Kippur* only, and there only regarding the *inuy*, not the prohibition of *melacha*. A further debate among the *acharonim* is whether women are obligated in the *mitzvah* of eating on *Erev Yom Kippur*, given that the obligation to eat on *Erev Yom Kippur* is linked to the obligation to fast — much as they are obligated in both the primary fast and the *tosefet*.

As for the verse *ha'ezrach* in the context of *sukkah*: since women are excluded by *halachah*, and minors are included only rabbinically, what biblical function does *ha'ezrach* serve? The *Gemara* answers: *lerabos et hagerim* — to include converts. One might have thought that *ha'ezrach b'Yisrael* refers only to native-born Jews. The verse teaches that converts are equally obligated.

### Minors Chinuch Age

Returning to the exemption of minors: the verse *kol* is interpreted as including them, but the *Gemara* clarifies that a minor's obligation in *sukkah* is rabbinic, not biblical — it is the *mitzvah* of *chinuch*, training children in the performance of *mitzvos*. The verse serves as a mere *asmachta*.

The *Mishnah* establishes the threshold as *katan she'eino tzarich le'imo* — a minor who no longer needs his mother. The *Gemara* asks: what precisely does this mean? The school of Rabbi Yannai answers: *kol she'nifneh ve'ein imo mekancheto* — any minor who uses the bathroom without requiring his mother to clean him up afterward. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish defines it differently: *kol she'ne'or misheinaso ve'eino kore Ima* — any minor who wakes in the night without calling for his mother. The *Gemara* gently objects: even grown people sometimes call out that way. The formulation is refined: one who wakes and does not cry *Ima Ima Ima Ima* — repeatedly, until the mother comes.

This definition is worth pausing over. The age of *chinuch* is not fixed across all *mitzvos*; as *Tosafos* explains, it depends on the specific *mitzvah*. Can the child shake a *lulav*? Then *chinuch* for *lulav* begins. Can he wear *tzitzis* without ruining them? Then that obligation of *chinuch* begins. Can he walk on his own? Then *chinuch* for *aliyah la'regel* is relevant. The definition here — independence from the mother — is not arbitrary. The obligation of *sukkah* is to dwell in the *sukkah* as one dwells in a house. A child who is entirely dependent on his mother for basic bodily functions is not truly the one *dwelling* in a place; he is being tended to there. Only when a minor achieves a meaningful degree of independence — the capacity to be truly present as a self-sufficient person — does it become meaningful to say that he is dwelling in the *sukkah*. This mirrors the broader principle that a sick person or a *mitzta'er* may be physically present in a *sukkah* and yet not be considered as truly dwelling in it.

The *Mishnah*'s story about Shammai is then reconciled: the *Gemara* explains that the *Mishnah* is incomplete in its present form. The full reading is: Shammai applied his stringency even beyond the age of *chinuch*, all the way to a newborn. Hence the story of breaking open the ceiling to place *sechach* over the infant's crib. That was Shammai's exceptional stringency — *lechumra* even beyond what the *halacha* requires — and it provides no contradiction to the rule that the *chinuch* obligation begins only when the minor achieves independence from his mother.
