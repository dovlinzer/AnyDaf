# Sukkah 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reb Eliezer Sukkah Rules

### 14 Meals in Sukkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין יוֹצְאִין מִסּוּכָּה לְסוּכָּה, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין סוּכָּה בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יוֹצְאִין מִסּוּכָּה לְסוּכָּה, וְעוֹשִׂין סוּכָּה בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד. וְשָׁוִין, שֶׁאִם נָפְלָה, שֶׁחוֹזֵר וּבוֹנָהּ בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **One** may **not depart from** one ***sukka* to** another ***sukka*;** he must reside in the same *sukka* for the entire Festival. **And one** may **not establish a *sukka* during the intermediate days of the Festival** if he failed to do so before the Festival. **And the Rabbis say: One** may **depart from** one ***sukka* to** another ***sukka*, and one** may **establish a *sukka* on the intermediate days of the Festival. And they** all, even Rabbi Eliezer, **agree that if** a *sukka* that one constructed before the Festival **collapsed, he** may **rebuild it during the intermediate days of the Festival.**

We pick up at the very top of 27b with the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who maintains that one must eat fourteen meals in a *sukkah* — two each day across all seven days of *Sukkos*. Related to this, Rabbi Eliezer holds that one does not fulfill the obligation with a *sukkah* built on *Chol HaMoed*, nor by visiting a friend's *sukkah*. The same *sukkah* must be used for all seven days, establishing it as a genuine substitute for one's home. This is an exceptionally demanding position, rooted in the idea that the *sukkah* must function as a true replacement dwelling.

### Collapsed Sukkah Rebuilt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁוִין, שֶׁאִם נָפְלָה, שֶׁחוֹזֵר וּבוֹנֶה אוֹתָהּ בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד. פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא הַאי אַחֲרִיתִי הִיא וְאֵינָהּ לְשִׁבְעָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the *baraita*: **And they agree that if** a *sukka* that one constructed before the Festival **collapsed, he may rebuild it during the intermediate days of the Festival.** The Gemara asks: That is **obvious;** why would it be prohibited? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that according to Rabbi Eliezer **this** rebuilt *sukka* **is** considered **a different one and is not** a *sukka* established for **seven** days, therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that Rabbi Eliezer agrees that it is considered to be the same *sukka*.

There is, however, one concession. The *Gemara* cites the ruling: *umodim she'im nafla shechozer uvona bechol hamoed* — all agree that if one's *sukkah* collapsed, he may rebuild it on *Chol HaMoed* and it is treated as a continuation of the original *sukkah*. The *Gemara* notes that this might seem obvious, but the *chiddush* is that one might have thought the newly rebuilt structure constitutes a separate *sukkah*, disqualified because it was not standing for all seven days. The ruling teaches us that this is not so.

### Verse-Based Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? אָמַר קְרָא: ״חַג הַסּוּכּוֹת תַּעֲשֶׂה לְךָ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״, עֲשֵׂה סוּכָּה הָרְאוּיָה לְשִׁבְעָה. וְרַבָּנַן? הָכִי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: עֲשֵׂה סוּכָּה בֶּחָג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer** that it is prohibited to move from one *sukka* to another during the Festival? The Gemara explains it is as **the verse says: "You shall prepare for yourself the festival of *Sukkot* for seven days"** (Deuteronomy 16:13); this is interpreted to mean: **Establish a *sukka* that is suitable for seven** days. It is considered a *sukka* suitable for the mitzva only if it is established for seven days. The Gemara asks: **And** how do **the Rabbis** interpret this verse? The Gemara answers: In their opinion, **this** is what the **Merciful One is saying:** If one did not establish a *sukka* on the eve of the Festival, he should **establish a *sukka* during the Festival.** The obligation to establish a *sukka* is in effect all seven days of the Festival.

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Eliezer* — what is the reason behind Rabbi Eliezer's position? The answer is that the verse states *chag hasukkos ta'ase lecha shivas yamim* — you shall make it for yourself, seven days. This implies the *sukkah* must be a seven-day structure. The Rabbis respond that the verse simply means: make a *sukkah* for the *yom tov*, without requiring that it stand for the entire seven days.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בַּיּוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג בְּלוּלָבוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּלְקַחְתֶּם לָכֶם בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן פְּרִי עֵץ הָדָר כַּפּוֹת תְּמָרִים״ — מִשֶּׁלָּכֶם, כָּךְ אֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּסוּכָּתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״חַג הַסּוּכּוֹת תַּעֲשֶׂה לְךָ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״ — מִשֶּׁלְּךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in another *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: Just as a person does not fulfill his obligation on the first day of the Festival with the *lulav* of another, as it is written: "And you shall take for yourselves on the first day the fruit of a beautiful tree, branches of a date palm"** (Leviticus 23:40), and the Sages derive from the phrase: Shall take for yourselves, that it must be taken **from your own** and not from that of someone else, **so too, a person does not fulfill his obligation with the *sukka* of another, as it is written: "You shall prepare for yourself the festival of *Sukkot* for seven days"** (Deuteronomy 16:13), and the Sages derive from the term "for yourself" that it must be taken **from your own.**

A *baraisa* then spells out Rabbi Eliezer's position further: just as one does not fulfill the obligation of *lulav* on the first day with a borrowed *lulav* — since the verse says *ulakachtem lachem*, you shall take for yourselves, meaning from what is yours — so too, one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* in another person's *sukkah*, since the verse says *ta'ase lecha*, it must be yours. The underlying principle seems unified: to qualify as a substitute house, the *sukkah* must be personally yours and continuously so throughout all seven days.

### Tosafos Ownership

*Tosafos* raises a pointed question against Rabbi Eliezer's position: if the *sukkah* must belong to the individual — *mishelcha* — does this mean that every member of a household requires his own separate *sukkah*? Would two people ever be able to fulfill the obligation together in a single *sukkah*? *Tosafos* suggests that perhaps the concept of *teshvu k'ein taduru* — dwelling in a *sukkah* as one dwells in a home — permits household members to share one *sukkah*, since that reflects the natural structure of a household. But beyond family members, partnership with a neighbor would be disqualified. This means, according to Rabbi Eliezer, that one could not *sukkah*-hop: all fourteen meals must be in one's own household *sukkah*.

### Rabbis' Counter-Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ אֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן בְּלוּלָבוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּסוּכָּתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״כׇּל הָאֶזְרָח בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל יֵשְׁבוּ בַּסּוּכּוֹת״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל רְאוּיִם לֵישֵׁב בְּסוּכָּה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: Although they said** that **a person does not fulfill his obligation on the first day of the Festival with the *lulav* of another, he fulfills his obligation with the *sukka* of another, as it is written: "All the homeborn in Israel shall reside in *sukkot*"** (Leviticus 23:42). This **teaches that all of the Jewish people are fit to reside in one *sukka*.** If the value of one *sukka* were divided among all the Jewish people, no individual would have a *peruta* stake in it; therefore, no individual could be considered even a part-owner of the *sukka*. The only way the entire Jewish people could fulfill the mitzva in one *sukka* is by residing in a communal *sukka* that does not belong to any of them. Apparently, there is no obligation to reside specifically in one's own *sukka*.

The Rabbis disagree: *af al pi she'omru ein adam yotzei yedei chovato b'yom tov harishon b'lulavo shel chaveiro, aval yotzei yedei chovato b'sukkato shel chaveiro* — although one cannot fulfill the *lulav* obligation on the first day with a borrowed *lulav*, one may nonetheless fulfill the *sukkah* obligation in another's *sukkah*. Their proof: *kol ha'ezrach b'Yisrael yeshvu basukkos* — all citizens of Israel shall dwell in *sukkos*. From this the *Gemara* derives that all of Israel could potentially dwell in a single *sukkah*, which implies that private ownership is not required; partnership or borrowing suffices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״לְךָ״, מַאי דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמַעוֹטֵי גְּזוּלָה, אֲבָל שְׁאוּלָה כְּתִיב: ״כׇּל הָאֶזְרָח״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis,** who do not derive that one is obligated to reside in his own *sukka*, **what do they derive from this** term **"for yourself"?** The Gemara answers: **They require** that term **to exclude a stolen** *sukka*. One does not fulfill his obligation with a stolen *sukka*. **However,** with regard to **a borrowed** *sukka*, **it is written: "All the homeborn,"** to teach that every Jew can fulfill the mitzva in a *sukka* borrowed from the community.

*Rashi* proposes that perhaps all Israel owns it jointly — but *Tosafos* presses that this only strengthens the contrast with Rabbi Eliezer, who requires genuine individual ownership. The Rabbis hold that once the verse makes clear that partnership is acceptable, borrowing another's *sukkah* is likewise permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הַאי ״כׇּל הָאֶזְרָח״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגֵר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר בֵּינְתַיִם וְקָטָן שֶׁנִּתְגַּדֵּל בֵּינְתַיִם. וְרַבָּנַן? כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמְרוּ: עוֹשִׂין סוּכָּה בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד, לָא אִצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Eliezer, what does he do with this** term: **"All the homeborn"?** The Gemara answers: **He requires** that term **to** derive that **a convert who converted in the interim,** during *Sukkot*, **and a minor who reached majority in the interim,** whose obligation began during the Festival, are obligated to fulfill the mitzva of residing in a *sukka*. The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the Rabbis,** from where are these *halakhot* derived? The Gemara answers: **Once** the Sages **said** that **one** may **establish a *sukka* during the intermediate days of the Festival,** an additional **verse** is **not necessary** to derive the obligation of the convert and the minor who reached majority.

The *Gemara* then asks what the Rabbis do with the word *lecha* — "yours" — in the verse. Their answer: it excludes a stolen *sukkah*. A borrowed *sukkah* is permitted by inference from *kol ha'ezrach*. And what does Rabbi Eliezer do with *kol ha'ezrach*, given that he does not permit sharing? He applies it to a novel case: a convert who converted *bein hashmashos* during *Sukkos*, or a minor who came of age *bein hashmashos*. Since Rabbi Eliezer generally requires all seven days, one might have thought these individuals could never fulfill the obligation. The verse teaches that since no other option was available to them, they fulfill the *mitzvah* with whatever portion of the *chag* remains.

The Rabbis respond that since they already hold a *sukkah* built on *Chol HaMoed* is valid, they have no need for a special verse to address this case — it is already subsumed within their general position.

## Learning Dedication (II)

### No Holiday Visits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי אִלְעַאי שֶׁהָלַךְ לְהַקְבִּיל פְּנֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר רַבּוֹ בְּלוֹד בָּרֶגֶל. אָמַר לוֹ: אִלְעַאי! אֵינְךָ מִשּׁוֹבְתֵי הָרֶגֶל. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מְשַׁבֵּחַ אֲנִי אֶת הָעַצְלָנִין, שֶׁאֵין יוֹצְאִין מִבָּתֵּיהֶן בָּרֶגֶל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשָׂמַחְתָּ אַתָּה וּבֵיתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** There was **an incident involving Rabbi Elai, who went** on *Sukkot* eve **to greet his teacher Rabbi Eliezer in Lod on** the first day of **the Festival. He said to him: Elai, you are not among** those who **stay** home **on the Festival** and therefore you have not fulfilled the mitzva of the Festival, **as Rabbi Eliezer would say: I praise the lazy, who,** although they act no differently than they do the entire year, are praiseworthy because they **do not leave their houses on the Festival, as it is written: "You shall rejoice, you and your household"** (Deuteronomy 14:26). The term "your household" is interpreted as referring to one's wife. One who is not home cannot rejoice with his wife.

The *Gemara* relates a story: Rabbi Ilai once traveled to greet his teacher Rabbi Eliezer in Lod on the *regel*. Rabbi Eliezer said to him: *Ilai eincha mishovsei ha'regel* — Ilai, you are not among those who stay home on the festival. The remark implies disapproval. The *Gemara* notes that even if this was not *Sukkos*, and even setting aside the problem of Rabbi Eliezer's own view that one must be in his own *sukkah* all seven days, Rabbi Eliezer objected to the very act of traveling to visit one's teacher during the *regel*.

### Praise for Staying Home

Why? Because *she'hayah Rabbi Eliezer omer: meshabeach ani et ha'atzlanim she'ein yotzin mibateihem baragel* — Rabbi Eliezer used to say: I praise the lazy people who do not leave their homes on the *regel*. He does not actually admire laziness, of course; the point is more like a stopped clock being right twice a day. Even if one stays home for the wrong reasons, the result — remaining at home during *yom tov* — is the right outcome.

### Household Rejoicing

The basis for this is the verse *v'samachta b'chagecha* — you shall rejoice on your festival — together with the formulation *v'samachta atah u'veisecha*, you and your household. The *mitzvah* of *simchas ha'regel* obligates a man to celebrate the festival together with his wife. This was already a live tension in earlier generations, as men would travel to visit their rebbes or undertake other pilgrimages on *yom tov*, much as people today travel on *Rosh Hashana* to Uman or elsewhere. Rabbi Eliezer's position was that the *mitzvah* begins at home, with one's family.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִין שֶׁחַיָּיב לְהַקְבִּיל פְּנֵי רַבּוֹ בָּרֶגֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מַדּוּעַ אַתְּ הוֹלֶכֶת אֵלָיו הַיּוֹם לֹא חֹדֶשׁ וְלֹא שַׁבָּת״, מִכְּלָל דִּבְחֹדֶשׁ וְשַׁבָּת מִיחַיַּיב אִינִישׁ לְאַקְבּוֹלֵי אַפֵּי רַבֵּיהּ! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — דְּאָזֵיל וְאָתֵי בְּיוֹמֵיהּ, הָא — דְּאָזֵיל וְלָא אָתֵי בְּיוֹמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? Didn't Rabbi Yitzḥak say: From where** is it derived **that** one **is obligated to greet his teacher on the Festival?** It is **as it is stated** that the husband of the Shunamite woman asked his wife: **"Why are you going to him today? It is neither the New Moon nor Shabbat"** (II Kings 4:23). This proves **by inference that on the New Moon and Shabbat a person is obligated to greet his teacher.** The Gemara answers that this is **not difficult: This** statement of Rabbi Yitzḥak that one is obligated to go and greet his teacher is referring to a case **where he goes and returns on** the same **day** and can rejoice with his wife at night; **and this** statement of Rabbi Eliezer that one should stay home is referring to a case **where he goes and does not return on** the same **day** and cannot rejoice with his wife at night.

The *Gemara* raises a counterpoint: Rabbi Yitzchak cites a proof that one is actually *obligated* to greet one's teacher on the *regel*, derived from the story of the *Isha HaShunamis*, whose husband asks: *madua at holechet elav hayom lo chodesh velo Shabbos* — why are you going today? It is neither *Rosh Chodesh* nor *Shabbos*. The implication is that on *Rosh Chodesh* and *Shabbos* one does go to greet a holy man. Rabbi Yitzchak derives from this that on the *regel* as well there is an obligation of *hakbalas penei rabbo*. The *Gemara* resolves the tension: there is no contradiction. If one goes and returns the same day, that is compatible with fulfilling *simchas ha'regel* with one's household. If one leaves and does not return the same day — abandoning one's family for the entire festival — that violates the obligation of *v'samachta atah u'veisecha*.

This discussion opens a broader reflection. The very language *makbil penei rabbo baragel* echoes the *aliya la'regel* — the pilgrimage to the Temple. Post-*churban*, the pilgrimage to encounter the divine is replaced, in some sense, by the act of going to be in the presence of the holy man, the *penei rabbo*. And that stands in tension with the domestic reality of the *regel*, the obligation to experience it within one's household. Rabbi Eliezer weighed in favor of the domestic dimension; the *Gemara* accommodates both, provided the visit is completed within the same day.

## Sheet on Sukkah Story

### Heard from Rebbe Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שֶׁשָּׁבַת בַּגָּלִיל הָעֶלְיוֹן בְּסוּכָּתוֹ שֶׁל יוֹחָנָן בְּרַבִּי אִלְעַאי בְּקֵיסָרִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּקֵיסָרְיוֹן, וְהִגִּיעַ חַמָּה לַסּוּכָּה. אָמַר לוֹ: מַהוּ שֶׁאֶפְרוֹשׂ עָלֶיהָ סָדִין?
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** There was **an incident** involving **Rabbi Eliezer, who stayed in the Upper Galilee in the *sukka* of Yoḥanan, son of Rabbi Elai, in Caesarea; and some say that** it did not occur in Caesarea but **in Caesarion. And** the **sun reached** a point over the roofing **of the *sukka*,** rendering it uncomfortable to remain in the *sukka*. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: What is** the *halakha*; may **I spread a sheet over** the roofing?

The *Gemara* then relates a second *ma'aseh*: Rabbi Eliezer spent a *Shabbos* in the Upper Galilee, in the *sukkah* of Yochanan bar Rabbi Ilai in Caesarea — or, according to some versions, Caesareon. The sun began beating down on the *sukkah*, and his host, concerned for Rabbi Eliezer's comfort, asked: *mahu she'efros aleha sadin* — what is the ruling regarding spreading a sheet over the *sukkah* to provide shade?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ חַמָּה לַחֲצִי הַסּוּכָּה, אָמַר לוֹ: מַהוּ שֶׁאֶפְרוֹשׂ עָלֶיהָ סָדִין? אָמַר לוֹ: אֵין לְךָ כׇּל שֵׁבֶט וְשֵׁבֶט מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא יָצְאוּ מִמֶּנּוּ נְבִיאִים. שֵׁבֶט יְהוּדָה וּבִנְיָמִין הֶעֱמִידוּ מְלָכִים עַל פִּי נְבִיאִים. הִגִּיעַ חַמָּה לְמַרְגְּלוֹתָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, נָטַל יוֹחָנָן סָדִין וּפֵירַשׂ עָלֶיהָ, הִפְשִׁיל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר טַלִּיתוֹ לַאֲחוֹרָיו וְיָצָא. לֹא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִפְלִיגוֹ בִּדְבָרִים, אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא אָמַר דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא שָׁמַע מִפִּי רַבּוֹ לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** In the meantime, the **sun reached** directly over the **midpoint** of the roofing **of the *sukka*.** Once again, Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: What is** the *halakha*; may **I spread a sheet over it?** Rabbi Eliezer again evaded the question and **said to him: There is no tribe of Israel from which prophets did not emerge.** And **the tribes of Judah and Benjamin** were unique because **they established kings according to prophets,** as Saul and David were anointed by the prophet Samuel. At that point, the light of the **sun reached the feet of Rabbi Eliezer. Yoḥanan took a sheet and spread it over** the *sukka*. **Rabbi Eliezer slung his cloak** over his shoulder **behind him and emerged** from the *sukka* because he did not want to permit doing so. The Gemara comments: Rabbi Eliezer conducted himself in that manner **not because he was** seeking **to** avoid answering by **diverting his** attention **with** his **words, but because** Rabbi Eliezer **never said a matter that he did not hear from his teacher.**

Rabbi Eliezer did not answer. Instead, he deflected the question entirely, remarking that there was not one tribe of Israel that had not produced a *shofet* during the period of the *Shoftim*. The sun continued advancing. When it reached the midpoint of the *sukkah*, the question was raised again, and again Rabbi Eliezer deflected — this time noting that every tribe had produced *neviim*. When the sun finally reached Rabbi Eliezer's feet, Yochanan bar Rabbi Ilai had enough; he took the sheet and spread it over the *sukkah* himself. At that point, Rabbi Eliezer *hifshil talito lachorav veyatza* — put on his cloak and walked out of the *sukkah*.

The *Gemara* explains: *lo mipnei she'hifligo bidvarim* — he was not evading the question out of disrespect or unwillingness to teach. Rather, *mipnei shelo amar davar shelo shama mipi rabbo le'olam* — because he had never in his life said anything he had not heard from his teacher. Since his teacher had never ruled on the question of spreading a sheet over a *sukkah* on *Shabbos*, he refused to issue a ruling. He would not say yes, he would not say no, and so he deflected until the situation resolved itself by his walking out.

The irony is not lost: the man who objected to visiting one's teacher on *yom tov* is the very same man who never uttered a ruling he had not received from his teacher.

### Tent-Making Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מִדִּידֵיהּ דִּתְנַן: פְּקַק הַחַלּוֹן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּזְמַן שֶׁקָּשׁוּר וְתָלוּי — פּוֹקְקִין בּוֹ, וְאִם לָאו — אֵין פּוֹקְקִין בּוֹ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ פּוֹקְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **resolve** the matter and conclude that it is not permitted **from his own** opinion, **as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **a window shutter** on Shabbat, **Rabbi Eliezer says: When it is tied** to **and hanging** from the window, i.e., it is not touching the ground, **one may shutter** the window **with it,** because that is not considered building; **and if not,** i.e., if it is touching the ground, **one may not shutter** the window **with it. And the Rabbis say: Both** in **this** case **and** in **that** case **one may shutter with it.** From the fact that if it is not hanging from the window, Rabbi Eliezer prohibits shuttering the window, he also prohibits adding to a temporary tent.

The *Gemara* then tries to resolve the underlying *halacha* from Rabbi Eliezer's other rulings. Is spreading a sheet over a *sukkah* on *Shabbos* a problem of *oseh ohel arai* — creating a temporary canopy? The sheet was clearly temporary and was being spread over an existing covered structure, so the question is whether even this modest addition constitutes a prohibited *ohel*. The *Gemara* attempts to derive a position from Rabbi Eliezer's ruling in a related case.

### Window Stopper Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם הוּא דִּמְבַטֵּל, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָא מְבַטֵּל — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: There is a difference between the case of the shutter and the case of the sheet. **There,** in the case of the shutter, **where he negates** it by shuttering the window, it is considered part of the building and it is therefore prohibited. **However, here,** in the case of the sheet, **where he does not negate** it, as he plans on removing it, **no,** it is not necessarily prohibited.

The *Gemara* cites a *mishna*: *pkak hachalon* — the case of a window stopper. If a block is used to fill and seal a window, is inserting it on *Shabbos* problematic because it effectively adds to the wall? Rabbi Eliezer rules: *bizman she'kashur vetalui, pokekin bo* — if the stopper is tied to a string and suspended off the ground, one may stop up the window, since it is already effectively attached, like a hinged element of the wall. But if it is not suspended — even if it is on a string but dragging on the ground — *ein pokekin bo*, one may not use it, because placing it constitutes creating something new. The *chachamim* rule leniently in both cases.

From this it would seem that Rabbi Eliezer should have answered the sheet question directly, presumably prohibiting it as he prohibited the unsuspended window stopper. The *Gemara* explains why he nonetheless refrained: *hatam hu de'mevatel, hacha lo mevatel*. In the window-stopper case, the stopper is being left in place and becomes integrated into the wall. The sheet over the *sukkah* is removed almost immediately once the sun moves; it is far more transitory. Rabbi Eliezer may have reasoned that this case was distinguishable and therefore sufficiently uncertain that he could not rule without a direct tradition from his teacher.

## Learning Dedication (II)

### 30 Sukkah Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שֶׁשָּׁבַת בַּגָּלִיל הָעֶלְיוֹן, וּשְׁאָלוּהוּ שְׁלֹשִׁים הֲלָכוֹת בְּהִלְכוֹת סוּכָּה, שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה אָמַר לָהֶם: שָׁמַעְתִּי, שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה אָמַר לָהֶם: לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, חִילּוּף הַדְּבָרִים: שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה אָמַר לָהֶם: שָׁמַעְתִּי, שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה אָמַר לָהֶם: לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident. **The Sages taught:** There was **an incident involving Rabbi Eliezer, who stayed in the Upper Galilee, and** the people there **asked him thirty *halakhot* in the *halakhot* of *sukka*.** In response to **twelve, he said to them: I heard** an answer from my teachers, and he related what he heard. In response to the other **eighteen, he said to them: I did not hear** an answer. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** It was **the reverse of these matters.** In response to **eighteen he said to them: I heard** an answer; in response to the other **twelve he said to them: I did not hear** an answer.

The *Gemara* transitions to another *ma'aseh* involving Rabbi Eliezer. He spent a *Shabbos* — perhaps the same *Shabbos*, perhaps a different one — in the Upper Galilee, and the people there posed thirty *halachos* to him regarding the laws of *sukkah*. The *Gemara* is at the top of 28a. Of the thirty, Rabbi Eliezer said *shamati* — I have a received tradition — for twelve. For the remaining eighteen, he said *lo shamati* — I have no tradition. Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehuda records the inverse: eighteen he had heard and twelve he had not.

### Always in Beis Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: כׇּל דְּבָרֶיךָ אֵינָן אֶלָּא מִפִּי הַשְּׁמוּעָה! אָמַר לָהֶם: הִזְקַקְתּוּנִי לוֹמַר דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא שָׁמַעְתִּי מִפִּי רַבּוֹתַי. מִיָּמַי לֹא קְדָמַנִי אָדָם בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, וְלֹא יָשַׁנְתִּי בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ לֹא שֵׁינַת קֶבַע וְלֹא שֵׁינַת עֲרַאי, וְלֹא הִנַּחְתִּי אָדָם בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וְיָצָאתִי, וְלֹא שַׂחְתִּי שִׂיחַת חוּלִּין, וְלֹא אָמַרְתִּי דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא שָׁמַעְתִּי מִפִּי רַבִּי מֵעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: Are all the matters** that **you** know **only from what** you **heard?** Don't you say any matters on your own? **He said to them:** Now **you forced me to say a matter that I did not hear from my teachers,** as I must describe my character traits and the manner in which I conduct myself. **In** all **my days, no person ever preceded me into the study hall,** as I am always first to arrive; **and I never slept in the study hall, neither substantial sleep nor a brief nap; and I never left anyone in the study hall and exited,** as I was always last to leave; **and I never engaged in idle conversation;** rather, I discussed only necessary matters or matters of Torah; **and I never said anything that I did not hear from my teacher.** That is why he did not answer those questions that his teacher did not address.

When the people challenged him — *kol devarecha einam ela mipi hashemua*, your entire learning amounts to nothing but transmitted traditions, where are your own views? — Rabbi Eliezer responded: *hizkaktuni lomar davar shelo shamati mipi rabosai?* Are you forcing me to say something I never heard from my teachers?

He then offered a defense that was not a justification of limitation but of completeness. *Miyami lo kidmani adam b'veis hamidrash* — in all my days, no one ever preceded me to the *beis midrash*. I was always first. *Velo yashanti b'veis hamidrash lo shinat keva velo shinat arai* — I never slept in the *beis midrash*, not a deep sleep and not even a light nap. *Velo hinachti adam b'veis hamidrash veyatzasi* — I never left the *beis midrash* while anyone else was still there; I was the last to leave. *Velo shachti sichas chulin* — I never engaged in idle conversation. *Velo amarti davar shelo shamati mipi rabbi me'olam* — and I never said anything I had not heard from my teacher.

The *Maharsha*, echoing *Rashi*, explains why Rabbi Eliezer begins with those first points before arriving at the last. The point is not merely that he transmitted faithfully, but that he had ensured total, exhaustive absorption of his teacher's teaching. He was there before anyone else, he stayed later than anyone else, he never wasted a moment. If something is missing from his repertoire, it is because his teacher never taught it — not because Rabbi Eliezer failed to be present for it.

This formulation reflects a distinctive and coherent vision of *Torah she'be'al peh*. Rabbi Eliezer's approach was *masorah* — tradition as the supreme mode of Torah transmission. He would not speculate. He would not derive on his own. He was a faithful, complete conduit. This stands in pointed contrast to the approach of Rabbi Akiva, who was the great embodiment of the creative interpretive tradition, using human reasoning and textual analysis to derive new *halachos* and new understandings. In the famous *aggados* involving the two — especially the deathbed encounter — that contrast emerges with particular sharpness.

There is also a striking internal parallel in Rabbi Eliezer's own character. His insistence that the *sukkah* must be the same *sukkah* all seven days, that one must remain planted in it with total consistency, that one does not travel around even to visit his teacher on *yom tov* — this mirrors his approach to the *beis midrash*: plant yourself, stay, do not move, do not wander. The constancy he demanded of the *sukkah* is the constancy he embodied in his own life of learning.

### Yochanan Ben Zakkai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: מִיָּמָיו לֹא שָׂח שִׂיחַת חוּלִּין, וְלֹא הָלַךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בְּלָא תּוֹרָה וּבְלֹא תְּפִילִּין, וְלֹא קְדָמוֹ אָדָם בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, וְלֹא יָשַׁן בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ לֹא שֵׁינַת קֶבַע וְלֹא שֵׁינַת עֲרַאי, וְלֹא הִרְהֵר בִּמְבוֹאוֹת הַמְטוּנָּפוֹת, וְלֹא הִנִּיחַ אָדָם בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וְיָצָא, וְלֹא מְצָאוֹ אָדָם יוֹשֵׁב וְדוֹמֵם אֶלָּא יוֹשֵׁב וְשׁוֹנֶה, וְלֹא פָּתַח אָדָם דֶּלֶת לְתַלְמִידָיו, אֶלָּא הוּא בְּעַצְמוֹ, וְלֹא אָמַר דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא שָׁמַע מִפִּי רַבּוֹ מֵעוֹלָם, וְלֹא אָמַר: הִגִּיעַ עֵת לַעֲמוֹד מִבֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, חוּץ מֵעַרְבֵי פְסָחִים, וְעַרְבֵי יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים. וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר תַּלְמִידוֹ נוֹהֵג אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the character traits of Rabbi Eliezer, the Gemara cites character traits of his teacher. The Sages **said about Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai,** the teacher of Rabbi Eliezer: **In** all **his days he never engaged in idle conversation; and he never walked four cubits without** engaging in **Torah** study **and without** donning **phylacteries; and no person ever preceded him into the study hall; and he never slept in the study hall, neither substantial sleep nor a brief nap; and he never contemplated** matters of Torah **in alleyways filthy** with human excrement, as doing so is a display of contempt for the Torah; **and he never left anyone in the study hall and exited; and no person ever found him sitting and silent,** i.e., inactive; **rather, he** was always **sitting and studying; and only he opened the door for his students,** disregarding his own eminent standing; **and he never said anything that he did not hear from his teacher; and he never said** to his students that **the time has arrived to arise** and leave **the study hall except on Passover eves,** when they were obligated to sacrifice the Paschal lamb, and **Yom Kippur eves,** when there is a mitzva to eat and drink abundantly. **And Rabbi Eliezer, his student, accustomed** himself to model his conduct **after his** example.

The *Gemara* then records the *middos* of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, who was Rabbi Eliezer's teacher. *Miyamav lo sach sichas chulin* — he never spoke idle words. *Velo halach arba amos b'lo Torah u'vlo tefillin* — he did not walk four *amos* without Torah and without *tefillin*. The *Maharsha* notes that *tefillin* itself is an embodiment of Torah. *Velo kidmo adam b'veis hamidrash* — no one preceded him to the *beis midrash*. *Velo yashan b'veis hamidrash lo shinat keva velo shinat arai* — he never slept there, not deeply and not lightly. *Velo hirher b'mevo'os hametunofos* — even though his mind was constantly engaged in Torah, when walking through filthy alleyways, he was able to restrain his thoughts and not think Torah there. *Velo hiniach adam b'veis hamidrash veyatza* — he never left the *beis midrash* while others remained. *Velo matza'o adam yoshev vedomeh* — no one ever caught him sitting in silence, doing nothing. *Ela yoshev veshoneh* — he was always sitting and learning.

Then a detail that seems out of place in this list: *velo pasach adam deles letalmidav ela hu b'atzmo* — he himself opened the door for his students, not relying on anyone else to do so. The *Gemara* suggests the resonance: just as the Torah says God is *Elohei ha'elohim* and then immediately describes his care for the orphan and the widow, so here too, alongside his towering greatness, he performed this small act of service for his students. The *Maharsha* adds that there may have been competition among students jostling at the door; Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai wanted to ensure that every student could enter without obstruction — recalling the contrast with the story of Hillel, who was once barred at the door by a guard.

*Velo amar higia et la'amod mi'beis hamidrash* — he never told others it was time to leave the *beis midrash*, except on *Erev Pesach* and *Erev Yom Kippur*, where *mitzvos* demanded it: on *Erev Pesach*, because of the *Korban Pesach* and the obligation to eat the *Seder* while children are awake; on *Erev Yom Kippur*, because of the *mitzvah* to eat. The *Gemara* then notes: *v'chein hayah Rabbi Eliezer talmido noheig acharyav* — Rabbi Eliezer, his student, followed the same practice. The *middos* listed about Rabbi Eliezer earlier were a subset of those practiced by his teacher.

### Yonatan ben Uziel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁמוֹנִים תַּלְמִידִים הָיוּ לוֹ לְהִלֵּל הַזָּקֵן. שְׁלֹשִׁים מֵהֶן רְאוּיִם שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה עֲלֵיהֶן שְׁכִינָה כְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, וּשְׁלֹשִׁים מֵהֶן רְאוּיִם שֶׁתַּעֲמוֹד לָהֶם חַמָּה כִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן. עֶשְׂרִים בֵּינוֹנִים. גָּדוֹל שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן — יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל, קָטָן שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן — רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to praise the Sages. **The Sages taught: Hillel the Elder had eighty students. Thirty of them** were sufficiently **worthy that the Divine Presence** should **rest upon them as** it did upon **Moses our teacher, and thirty of them** were sufficiently **worthy that the sun** should **stand still for them as** it did for **Joshua bin Nun, and twenty** were on an **intermediate** level between the other two. **The greatest of all** the students was **Yonatan ben Uzziel, and the youngest of them** was **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a broader context. *Shemonim talmidim hayu lo l'Hillel haZaken* — Hillel had eighty students. Thirty of them were at such a level that the *Shechinah* could have rested upon them as it did upon Moshe. Thirty were worthy of having the sun stand still for them as it did for Yehoshua. Twenty were *beinonim*. *Gadol shebakulam Yonasan ben Uziel* — the greatest of all was Yonasan ben Uziel, to whom the *Targum* on *Nevi'im* is ascribed. *Katan shebakulam Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* — and the smallest of all of them was Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai.

This is a striking *Gemara*. All of the remarkable qualities we have just catalogued in Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, and he was the *least* of Hillel's eighty disciples. The *Gemara* naturally notes the implication: *gadol shebakulam al achas kamah v'chamah* — how incomparably greater must Yonasan ben Uziel have been.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי שֶׁלֹּא הִנִּיחַ מִקְרָא וּמִשְׁנָה, גְּמָרָא הֲלָכוֹת וְאַגָּדוֹת; דִּקְדּוּקֵי תוֹרָה וְדִקְִדּוּקֵי סוֹפְרִים; קַלִּים וַחֲמוּרִים וּגְזֵרוֹת שָׁווֹת; תְּקוּפוֹת וְגִימַטְרִיָּאוֹת; שִׂיחַת מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת וְשִׂיחַת שֵׁדִים וְשִׂיחַת דְּקָלִים; מִשְׁלוֹת כּוֹבְסִין, מִשְׁלוֹת שׁוּעָלִים; דָּבָר גָּדוֹל וְדָבָר קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: The Sages **said about Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai** that **he did not neglect Bible; Mishna; Gemara; *halakhot* and *aggadot*; minutiae of the Torah and minutiae of the scribes;** the hermeneutical principles of the Torah with regard to ***a fortiori* inferences and verbal analogies;** the calculation of the calendrical **seasons;** and **numerology [*gimmatreyaot*].** In addition, he did not neglect esoteric matters, including **the conversation of ministering angels; the conversation of demons, and the conversation of palm trees; parables of launderers,** which are folk tales that can be used to explain the Torah; **parables of foxes;** and more generally, **a great matter and a small matter.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר גָּדוֹל — מַעֲשֵׂה מֶרְכָּבָה. דָּבָר קָטָן — הֲוָיוֹת דְּאַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא. לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְהַנְחִיל אוֹהֲבַי יֵשׁ וְאוֹצְרוֹתֵיהֶם אֲמַלֵּא״. וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁקָּטָן שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן כָּךְ, גָּדוֹל שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה — כׇּל עוֹף שֶׁפּוֹרֵחַ עָלָיו מִיָּד נִשְׂרָף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **A great matter** is referring to the secrets of the **Design of the** Divine **Chariot,** the conduct of the transcendent universe. **A small matter** is, for example, *halakhot* that were ultimately formulated in the framework of **the disputes of Abaye and Rava.** He did not neglect any of these disciplines so as **to fulfill that which is stated: "That I may cause those that love me to inherit substance and that I may fill their treasuries"** (Proverbs 8:21), as Rabban Yoḥanan was filled with the disciplines of Torah and wisdom. **And if the youngest of them was so** prolific, **the greatest of them** was **all the more so** prolific. The Gemara relates that the Sages **said of Yonatan ben Uzziel,** the greatest of Hillel's students, **that when he sat and was engaged in Torah** study, the sanctity that he generated was so intense that **any bird that flew over him was immediately incinerated.**

Of Yonasan ben Uziel: *shebesha'ah she'yosheiv v'oseik baTorah, kol of she'poreiach alav miyad nisraf* — when he would sit and learn Torah, any bird that flew overhead was burned. *Tosafos* explains that his learning had the character of the original *matan Torah*, in which fire accompanied the giving of the Torah at Sinai; the fiery angels hovering above him to participate in his learning consumed any bird that passed over.

The contrast between Yonasan ben Uziel and Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai invites reflection on what is listed as the totality of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's mastery: *Mikra, Mishnah, Gemara, halachos, aggados, dikdukei Torah, dikdukei Soferim, kal v'chomer, gezeirah shavah, tekufos, gematrios, sichas malachei hashareis, sichas sheidim, sichas dekalim, meshalos kovsim, meshalos shualim, davar gadol v'davar katan*. What is the *davar gadol*? *Ma'aseh Merkavah*. What is the *davar katan*? *Havayos d'Abaye v'Rava* — the analytical discussions of the Talmud. The verse it fulfills: *lehanchiel ohavai yesh, v'otzroseihem amalei* — to bequeath to those who love me substance, and fill their storehouses.

This passage bears directly on the earlier question about the two paradigms of *Torah she'be'al peh*. One might have assumed that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai — who, like his student Rabbi Eliezer, said he never uttered something not heard from his teachers — was purely a *masorah* figure. But the list of what he mastered does not support that reading. *Gemara, kal v'chomer, gezeirah shavah, havayos d'Abaye v'Rava* — these are not passive transmissions. They are analytical enterprises, requiring the exercise of human reasoning. One cannot derive a *kal v'chomer* or analyze a *gezeirah shavah* purely by receiving and repeating a tradition.

The resolution appears to lie in the complexity of the *rebbe* himself. Different students of a great figure absorb different dimensions of his teaching. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was not simply a conduit; his *takkanos*, his decision to negotiate with the Romans, his reorientation of Jewish life after the *churban* toward *Torah* and *Yavneh* — none of this is the work of a pure *masorah* figure. He embodied both the fidelity of tradition and the creativity of human interpretation. Rabbi Eliezer, his student, absorbed one dimension of that — the *masorah* — and made it the totality of his approach.

This is illuminated by the earlier story of Hillel. When Hillel proved from thirty different derivations that the *Korban Pesach* overrides *Shabbos*, the *Bnei Batera* would not accept it. Only when he said he had a *masorah* from Shemaya and Avtalyon did they capitulate. Hillel's entire career represented the argument that creative interpretation is a legitimate mode of Torah transmission — but he met with resistance. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, his student, carried both modes. Rabbi Eliezer, student of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, appears to have inherited only one.

The *Rambam*'s approach is also relevant here. He uses this very *Gemara* — that *ma'aseh Merkavah* is *davar gadol* and *havayos d'Abaye v'Rava* is *davar katan* — to argue that the proper trajectory of learning is to master *halacha* through *Gemara* study and then devote one's higher energies to philosophy and theology. The *davar katan* is not demeaned; it is the path that gives access to the *davar gadol*. For the *Rambam*, the ultimate goal is the theological and philosophical endeavor, which is accessible to all — even if *ma'aseh Merkavah* in its deepest form is not.

## Exemptions Categories

### Exempt Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהָיָה רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בַּסּוּכָּה וְשׁוּלְחָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי פּוֹסְלִין וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one whose head and most of his** body **were in the *sukka* and his table was in the house, Beit Shammai deem it unfit, and Beit Hillel deem it fit.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשִׁים וַעֲבָדִים וּקְטַנִּים פְּטוּרִין מִן הַסּוּכָּה. קָטָן שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ — חַיָּיב בְּסוּכָּה. מַעֲשֶׂה וְיָלְדָה כַּלָּתוֹ שֶׁל שַׁמַּאי הַזָּקֵן, וּפִיחֵת אֶת הַמַּעֲזִיבָה וְסִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּטָּה בִּשְׁבִיל קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Women, slaves, and minors are exempt from the** mitzva of ***sukka*. A minor who does not need his mother** any longer **is obligated** in the mitzva. There was **an incident where the daughter-in-law of Shammai the Elder gave birth** just before *Sukkot*, and Shammai **removed the** coat of **plaster** from the roof, leaving the beams, **and roofed** with the beams **over the bed for the** newborn **minor.**

The *Gemara* turns to a new *Mishnah*: *nashim va'avadim u'ktanim peturim min hasukkah* — women, slaves, and minors are exempt from *sukkah*. This is the standard application of the principle that women are exempt from positive time-bound *mitzvos*. The *Mishnah* then adds: *katan she'eino tzarich le'imo, chayav basukkah* — a minor who no longer needs his mother is obligated in *sukkah*.

### Shammai Stringency

The *Mishnah* records a story: *veyalda kalato shel Shammai haZaken* — Shammai's daughter-in-law gave birth, and Shammai was so stringent that he knocked a hole in the plaster of the ceiling and placed *sechach* directly over the cradle, so that the newborn infant would be in a *sukkah*. He used the principle of *dofen akuma* — the bending wall — to extend the *sukkah* coverage to the area of the crib. This is acknowledged as beyond what the *halacha* requires; even by Shammai's own logic, this was *lechumra* beyond the threshold of the *chinuch* obligation, since the infant was one day old.

### Verse Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶזְרָח״ — זֶה אֶזְרָח, ״הָאֶזְרָח״ — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַנָּשִׁים, ״כׇּל״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַקְּטַנִּים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the *halakha* that women, slaves, and minors are exempt from the mitzva of *sukka*, the Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers that it is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* that it is stated: "All the homeborn in Israel shall reside in *sukkot*" (Leviticus 23:42). Had the verse stated only: **Homeborn,** it would have been derived **that** any **homeborn** member of the Jewish people is obligated to observe this mitzva. However, the term with the addition of the definite article: **"The homeborn,"** indicates that only certain homeborn members are obligated, i.e., men, **to the exclusion of the women.** The word "all" in the phrase: **"All** the homeborn," comes **to include the minors** capable of performing this mitzva.

The *Gemara* asks: *mena hani mili* — from where are these exemptions derived? The *baraisa* teaches: the verse states *kol ha'ezrach b'Yisrael yeshvu basukkos* — all *ezrach* among Israel shall dwell in *sukkos*. The word *ezrach* alone would include both men and women. The added letter *hei* — *ha'ezrach* — comes, the *baraisa* says, to exclude women: *lehotzi et hanashim*. Yet the word *kol* — "all" — comes to include minors: *lerabos et haktanim*.

This immediately raises a difficulty. A different *baraisa* states the opposite: in the context of *Yom Kippur*, the verse says *ha'ezrach veha'ger* — and from *ha'ezrach* we include women in the obligation to fast. This implies that *ezrach* alone means men, and *ha'ezrach* is expansive and inclusive. So which is it: does *ha'ezrach* include or exclude women?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״הָאֶזְרָח״ — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַנָּשִׁים. לְמֵימְרָא דְּ״אֶזְרָח״, בֵּין נָשִׁים בֵּין גַּבְרֵי מַשְׁמַע? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״הָאֶזְרָח״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַנָּשִׁים הָאֶזְרָחִיּוֹת, שֶׁחַיָּיבוֹת בְּעִינּוּי. אַלְמָא ״אֶזְרָח״ — גַּבְרֵי מַשְׁמַע! אָמַר רַבָּה: הִלְכְתָא נִינְהוּ וְאַסְמְכִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן אַקְּרָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master said: "The homeborn"** is **to the exclusion of women. Is that to say that** the term **homeborn** without the definite article **indicates both men and women? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to Yom Kippur that it is stated: "And it shall be a statute forever unto you: In the seventh month, on the tenth day of the month, you shall afflict your souls and shall do no manner of work, the homeborn, or the stranger that sojourns among you" (Leviticus 16:29). And the term **"the homeborn"** in that verse comes **to include homeborn women, who are obligated in** the mitzva of **affliction** on Yom Kippur. In that case, the definite article comes to include women. Therefore, **apparently,** the term homeborn, without the definite article, **indicates** only **men. Rabba said: They are** each a ***halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and the Sages** merely **supported them with verses** as a mnemonic device. Therefore, it is not surprising that the derivations are contradictory.

Rava responds: *hilchasa ninhu* — both rulings are actually *halachos*, traditional teachings, and the verses are merely *asmachtos*, rabbinic supports. The *Gemara* then presses further: if they are both traditions, and both are linked to verses, why were the verses needed at all? In the case of *sukkah*, the exemption of women follows automatically from the general rule of *mitzvas asei shehazman gerama nashim peturos* — women are exempt from positive time-bound commandments. And in the case of *Yom Kippur*, the obligation of women follows automatically from Rav Yehuda's teaching in the name of Rav: *hishva hakasuv isha le'ish lechol onashin shebatorah* — the Torah equates men and women with respect to all punishments, and since *Yom Kippur* fasting carries the punishment of *kares*, women are equally bound.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵי קְרָא וְהֵי הִלְכְתָא? וְתוּ: קְרָא לְמָה לִי, הִלְכְתָא לְמָה לִי? הָא סוּכָּה מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא, וְכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Which** of them **is** derived from **the verse and which is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai and merely supported by a verse? **And furthermore, why do I** need **the verse and why do I** need **the *halakha*? Isn't *sukka* a positive, time-bound mitzva, and** the principle is that **women are exempt from all positive, time-bound mitzvot?** There is no need for a special derivation to exempt women from the mitzva of *sukka*.

The *Gemara* thus asks why we need either the *masorah* or the verse in each case. Abaye answers: in truth, the exemption of women from *sukkah* is the *halacha*, and the verse is its *asmachta*. But why was a special *halachah* needed to exempt women, given the general principle? Because there was reason to think otherwise.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, מִדְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב נָפְקָא. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר קְרָא: ״אִישׁ אוֹ אִשָּׁה״,
>
> **Translation:** And there is no need for a derivation with regard to their obligation to fast on **Yom Kippur,** as that can be **derived from** that **which Rav Yehuda** said that **Rav said, as Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said, and it was likewise taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: The verse says:** "When **a man or woman** shall commit any sin that a person commits, to commit a trespass against the Lord, and that soul be guilty" (Numbers 5:6).

First: the verse says *teshvu* — you shall dwell — interpreted as *ke'ein taduru*, like your normal dwelling. One's normal dwelling involves both husband and wife. One might therefore have concluded that a *sukkah*, to function as a genuine domestic substitute, must obligate women as well. Hence the special *halachah* is needed to teach that despite this logic, the general rule of positive time-bound exemption applies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ לְכׇל עוֹנָשִׁין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם סוּכָּה הִלְכְתָא, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא ״תֵּשְׁבוּ״ — כְּעֵין תָּדוּרוּ, מָה דִּירָה — אִישׁ וְאִשְׁתּוֹ, אַף סוּכָּה — אִישׁ וְאִשְׁתּוֹ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse equated a woman to a man** with regard **to all punishments** and prohibitions **in the Torah.** The mitzvot of Yom Kippur include prohibitions, as well as the punishment of *karet*. Why, then, was this additional derivation necessary? **Abaye said: Actually, *sukka* is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai. Nevertheless, it **was necessary** to teach that a woman is exempt from the mitzva of *sukka*, as **it might enter your mind to say: "Shall you reside"** (Leviticus 23:42) indicates that you reside in the *sukka* **as you dwell; just as dwelling** is typically performed by a **man and his wife, so too,** the mitzva of ***sukka*** is performed by both **a man and his wife.** Therefore, **it teaches us** that women are exempt.

Rava adds a second reason. The *Gemara* derives the obligation to dwell in the *sukkah* on the first night by a *gezeirah shavah* linking the fifteenth of *Tishrei* to the fifteenth of *Nisan*. Since women are obligated in *matzah* on the first night of *Pesach* — because the obligation is tied to the prohibition of *chametz*, which binds everyone — one might have argued that for the first night of *sukkah* as well, women should be obligated. The *halachah* is needed to exclude this inference and confirm that women remain exempt even on the first night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא יָלֵיף ״חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר״ ״חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר״ מֵחַג הַמַּצּוֹת. מָה לְהַלָּן — נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת, אַף כָּאן — נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** a different reason: A *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai **was necessary** to teach that a woman is exempt from the mitzva of *sukka*, as **it might enter your mind to say: Derive** a verbal analogy with regard to *Sukkot*, about which it is written: "On the **fifteenth** day of this seventh month is the festival of *Sukkot*" (Leviticus 23:34), **from** Passover, about which it is written: "And on the **fifteenth** day of the same month is the **festival of *matzot*"** (Leviticus 23:6). **Just as there, women are obligated** to eat *matza* on Passover even though it is a time-bound mitzva, **so too here,** with regard to the mitzva of *sukka*, **women are obligated.** Therefore, the *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai **teaches us** that they are exempt.

Turning to *Yom Kippur*: the verse is needed not for the primary obligation to fast, which follows from the general rule. It is needed for the *tosefet inuy* — the additional period of fasting beyond the day itself. Since this extra period carries no punishment and is not independently a negative prohibition, one might have thought women are exempt from it. The verse teaches that since women are obligated in the primary fast, they are equally obligated in the added portion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ סוּכָּה הִלְכְתָא, קָרָא לְמָה לִי? לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַגֵּרִים. סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא: ״הָאֶזְרָח בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא — וְלֹא אֶת הַגֵּרִים, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And now that you said** that women's exemption from the mitzva of *sukka* is a ***halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **why do I** need the definite article stated in the **verse** in the term "the homeborn"? The Gemara answers: This verse comes **to include converts,** as **it might enter your mind to say** that **the Merciful One says: "The homeborn in Israel,"** indicating that only homeborn Jews are included **and not the converts.** Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that converts are also obligated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מִדְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב נָפְקָא. לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְתוֹסֶפֶת עִינּוּי. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּמִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא לְתוֹסֶפֶת עִינּוּי מֵעוֹנֶשׁ וּמֵאַזְהָרָה, לֹא נִתְחַיְּיבוּ נָשִׁים כְּלָל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: The obligation of women to fast on **Yom Kippur is derived from** the statement **that Rav Yehuda** said that **Rav said.** In that case, why do I need the definite article in the term: The homeborn? The Gemara answers: That phrase **was needed only to** include women in the **extension** of the period **of affliction** on Yom Kippur eve, as **it might enter your mind to say: Since the Merciful One excludes** one who violates the obligation to afflict himself during **the extension** of the period **of affliction from** the **punishment** of *karet* **and** from the Torah **prohibition, women should not be obligated** to observe that period **at all.** Their obligation to observe Yom Kippur is based on the principle: The verse equated a woman to a man with regard to all punishments and prohibitions in the Torah. Since there is neither punishment nor Torah prohibition during that period, women should be exempt. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that since they are obligated to observe Yom Kippur, they are obligated to observe the extension of Yom Kippur as well.

The *Rashash* raises an interesting question: why does the *Gemara* not similarly derive that women are obligated in the *tosefet* regarding *melacha* — the requirement to cease work slightly before *Yom Kippur* begins? He suggests this may be a basis for the *Rambam*'s position that the biblical obligation of *tosefet* applies to *Yom Kippur* only, and there only regarding the *inuy*, not the prohibition of *melacha*. A further debate among the *acharonim* is whether women are obligated in the *mitzvah* of eating on *Erev Yom Kippur*, given that the obligation to eat on *Erev Yom Kippur* is linked to the obligation to fast — much as they are obligated in both the primary fast and the *tosefet*.

As for the verse *ha'ezrach* in the context of *sukkah*: since women are excluded by *halachah*, and minors are included only rabbinically, what biblical function does *ha'ezrach* serve? The *Gemara* answers: *lerabos et hagerim* — to include converts. One might have thought that *ha'ezrach b'Yisrael* refers only to native-born Jews. The verse teaches that converts are equally obligated.

### Minors Chinuch Age

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״כׇּל״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַקְּטַנִּים. וְהָתְנַן: נָשִׁים וַעֲבָדִים וּקְטַנִּים פְּטוּרִין מִן הַסּוּכָּה! לָא קַשְׁיָא כָּאן — בְּקָטָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְחִינּוּךְ, כָּאן — בְּקָטָן שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ לְחִינּוּךְ. קָטָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְחִינּוּךְ מִדְּרַבָּנַן הוּא! מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָא — אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *baraita*: **"All** the homeborn" comes **to include the minors** capable of performing this mitzva. The Gemara asks: **Didn't we learn** in the mishna: **Women and slaves and minors are exempt from the** mitzva of ***sukka*?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** in the *baraita* where it is taught that minors are included, it is referring to **a minor who reached** the age of **training,** whose parents are commanded to train him in the performance of mitzvot and to accustom him to fulfill them. **Here,** in the mishna where it stated that the minor is exempt, it is referring to **a minor** who **did not** yet **reach** the age of **training.** The Gemara asks: The obligation of **a minor who reached** the age of **training** to perform mitzvot **is by rabbinic law,** and therefore it is not derived from a verse. The Gemara answers: Indeed, the obligation of the minor is **by rabbinic law** as part of his training, **and the verse** is **a mere support** alluding to that obligation.

Returning to the exemption of minors: the verse *kol* is interpreted as including them, but the *Gemara* clarifies that a minor's obligation in *sukkah* is rabbinic, not biblical — it is the *mitzvah* of *chinuch*, training children in the performance of *mitzvos*. The verse serves as a mere *asmachta*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָטָן שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ כּוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי קָטָן שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כֹּל שֶׁנִּפְנֶה וְאֵין אִמּוֹ מְקַנַּחְתּוֹ. רַבִּי (שִׁמְעוֹן) אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁנֵּעוֹר מִשְּׁנָתוֹ וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא ״אִמָּא, [אִמָּא]!״ גְּדוֹלִים נָמֵי קָרוּ? אֶלָּא: (אֵימָא) כָּל שֶׁנֵּעוֹר וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא: ״אִמָּא, אִמָּא!״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **A minor who does not need his mother** any longer **is obligated** in the mitzva of *sukka*. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **a minor who does not need his mother? In the school of Rabbi Yannai they said:** This is referring to **any** child **who defecates and his mother does not** need to **wipe him. Rabbi Shimon says:** It is **any** child **who awakens from his sleep and does not call: Mother, mother.** The Gemara asks: **Older** children **also call** for their mother when they arise; what, then, is the criterion? The Gemara answers: **Rather, say** that **any** child **who awakens and does not call: Mother, mother,** repeatedly until his mother comes is characterized as one who does not need his mother. An older child will cry once. However, if his mother does not come, he will tend to himself.

The *Mishnah* establishes the threshold as *katan she'eino tzarich le'imo* — a minor who no longer needs his mother. The *Gemara* asks: what precisely does this mean? The school of Rabbi Yannai answers: *kol she'nifneh ve'ein imo mekancheto* — any minor who uses the bathroom without requiring his mother to clean him up afterward. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish defines it differently: *kol she'ne'or misheinaso ve'eino kore Ima* — any minor who wakes in the night without calling for his mother. The *Gemara* gently objects: even grown people sometimes call out that way. The formulation is refined: one who wakes and does not cry *Ima Ima Ima Ima* — repeatedly, until the mother comes.

This definition is worth pausing over. The age of *chinuch* is not fixed across all *mitzvos*; as *Tosafos* explains, it depends on the specific *mitzvah*. Can the child shake a *lulav*? Then *chinuch* for *lulav* begins. Can he wear *tzitzis* without ruining them? Then that obligation of *chinuch* begins. Can he walk on his own? Then *chinuch* for *aliyah la'regel* is relevant. The definition here — independence from the mother — is not arbitrary. The obligation of *sukkah* is to dwell in the *sukkah* as one dwells in a house. A child who is entirely dependent on his mother for basic bodily functions is not truly the one *dwelling* in a place; he is being tended to there. Only when a minor achieves a meaningful degree of independence — the capacity to be truly present as a self-sufficient person — does it become meaningful to say that he is dwelling in the *sukkah*. This mirrors the broader principle that a sick person or a *mitzta'er* may be physically present in a *sukkah* and yet not be considered as truly dwelling in it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה וְיָלְדָה כַּלָּתוֹ כּוּ׳. מַעֲשֶׂה לִסְתּוֹר? חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: וְשַׁמַּאי מַחְמִיר, וּמַעֲשֶׂה נָמֵי וְיָלְדָה כַּלָּתוֹ שֶׁל שַׁמַּאי הַזָּקֵן, וּפִחֵת אֶת הַמַּעֲזִיבָה וְסִיכֵּךְ עַל הַמִּטָּה בִּשְׁבִיל הַקָּטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna relates: There was **an incident where the daughter-in-law of** Shammai the Elder **gave birth** and he removed part of the roof so the baby would be in a *sukka*. The Gemara asks: Does the mishna cite **an incident to contradict** the preceding *halakha* that minors that are not independent are exempt from the mitzva of *sukka*? The Gemara answers: The mishna is **incomplete, and it teaches the following: And Shammai is stringent** even with very small children; **and there was also an incident and the daughter-in-law of Shammai the Elder gave birth** and Shammai **removed the** coat of **plaster** from the roof and left the beams **and roofed** with the beams **over the bed for the** newborn **minor.**

The *Mishnah*'s story about Shammai is then reconciled: the *Gemara* explains that the *Mishnah* is incomplete in its present form. The full reading is: Shammai applied his stringency even beyond the age of *chinuch*, all the way to a newborn. Hence the story of breaking open the ceiling to place *sechach* over the infant's crib. That was Shammai's exceptional stringency — *lechumra* even beyond what the *halacha* requires — and it provides no contradiction to the rule that the *chinuch* obligation begins only when the minor achieves independence from his mother.
