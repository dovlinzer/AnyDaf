# Nazir 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Talking Cow Mishna

### Cow's Declaration

Daf 10 opens with what will likely be one of the most memorable *mishnayot* encountered in the course of learning *Nazir*. The *Mishnah* presents the following scenario: a man reports that a cow declared, *hareini nezira im omedet ani* — "I will be a *nazira* if I stand up." Or alternatively, that a door declared, *hareini nezira im niftach ani* — "I will be a *nezira* if I am opened." The man comes before us reporting, in apparent seriousness, that he witnessed these declarations by a cow and a door.

### Beis Shammai/Hillel

*Beis Shammai* rules that the man is a *nazir* — not the cow or the door, but the man himself is a *nazir*, presumably contingent on whether the stated condition was fulfilled. *Beis Hillel* rules that he is not a *nazir*. The *Gemara* will clarify the precise nature of this dispute.

### R. Yehuda Position

*Rabbi Yehuda* adds a qualification: even when *Beis Shammai* said the man is a *nazir*, they did not mean he becomes a full *nazir*. Rather, their intent was only that whoever says *harei para zo alai korban im omedet hi* — "this cow is upon me as a *korban* if it stands" — the cow becomes forbidden to him. This is analogous to the earlier *Mishnah* where a man says he is a *nazir* from dates (*develah*): *Rabbi Yehuda* held there that the dates become forbidden to him rather than that he actually assumes full *nezirut*. So too here, the cow becomes forbidden, not that a full *nezirut* takes effect. How this reasoning extends to a talking door is left somewhat open, but presumably the door would become forbidden to him in some analogous sense.

The natural reaction to all of this is: of all the things one might ask about this *Mishnah*, the debate over whether this constitutes *nezirut* or a *korban*-like prohibition seems almost beside the point. What on earth is going on with a cow that declares itself a *nezira*?

---

## Gemara Response

### Rami bar Chama

The *Gemara* asks the obvious question: *para mi ka mishtaya* — since when does a cow talk? *Rami bar Chama* proposes the following reading: *kegon shehayesa para revutza lefanav* — we are dealing with a case in which a cow was crouched down before the man. He observes it and says, *kisvura para zo eina omedet* — this cow must be thinking that it is not going to be able to stand up. It appears so thoroughly crouched that one would say it has given up on rising.

### Meat Nazirut

With that as the backdrop, *Rami bar Chama* explains the man's actual statement as *hareini nazir mibesara im amda me'aleha* — I will be a *nazir* from its flesh if it stands up on its own. And then the cow did in fact stand up on its own. The condition was thus met. This immediately reduces the *Mishnah* to a parallel of the preceding one, since the question now becomes: what does it mean to declare oneself a *nazir* from the meat of a cow?

### Debate Repetition

The *Gemara* notes that *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* simply follow their respective positions — *ve'azlu Beis Shammai leshitasan veBeis Hillel leshitasan* — the same positions they took in the earlier *Mishnah* regarding dates and *develah*. *Beis Shammai* holds that if someone says he is a *nazir* from dates or from meat, he is indeed a *nazir*, because we look past the odd object named and recognize the underlying intent to accept *nezirut*. *Beis Hillel* holds that such a declaration is meaningless, since one cannot be a *nazir* "from" meat any more than one can be a *nazir* "from" dates, and so he is not a *nazir* at all. The immediate question the *Gemara* raises is: why must this debate be repeated? *Beis Shammai* already stated this position once in the earlier *Mishnah*.

## Two/Three Times

### Multiple Cases

*Rava* answers: *trei telas* — two or three times we are shown variations of the same principle. And so teaches *Rabbi Chiya*, and so says *Rabbi Yoshiya* as well: there are multiple illustrations of the same debate, and that is intentional.

### Dates/Grapes

But the *Gemara* goes further and insists that each case is necessary — *u'tzricha*. Had the *Mishnah* only taught the case of *grogros u'develah*, dates and dried figs, one might have thought that *Beis Shammai*'s ruling applies only there. Why? *Mishum demichalfin ba'anavim* — because dates and grapes are genuinely similar. They are both smallish, darkish fruits. It is not entirely unreasonable for someone to confuse the two, to think that what the *Torah* prohibits to a *nazir* under the category of grapes somehow extends to or includes dates. And so *Beis Shammai* could be saying: you clearly wanted to be a *nazir* and simply got confused about which fruit was meant, and therefore your declaration takes effect.

### Meat Distinction

But meat and grapes — *lo michalef* — nobody confuses those. So if the *Mishnah* only taught the case of meat, one might assume the opposite: that the case of dates would not work for *Beis Shammai*, since the confusion between dates and grapes is more plausible and *Beis Shammai* might be relying on that confusion to impose *nezirut*. The case of meat would then need its own logic.

### Wine/Meat Link

That logic runs as follows: *bevishra vechamra* — wine and meat go together. A good meal, a life of sensual indulgence, is classically represented by meat and wine. The institution of *nezirut* is understood by many to represent a form of asceticism, a withdrawal from physical pleasure. It is therefore not at all unreasonable to suppose that when a person says "I am a *nazir* from meat," they are thinking: a *nazir* abstains from the pleasures of meat and wine together. As the *Gemara* notes, *de'amar mar*, there is a teaching that an *onen* — a mourner in the acute stage of mourning — is forbidden from wine and meat. These two are linked as the classic expression of physical indulgence. Indeed, it is worth pausing over this association, because the prohibition of wine for a *nazir* is not solely about hedonism. The *pasuk* in *Yirmiyahu* — *vatashku es hanezirim yayin ve'al hanevi'im tzivisem leimor lo tinavu* — speaks of giving the *nezirim* wine to drink and commanding the prophets not to prophesy, suggesting that wine is problematic not merely because of physical pleasure but because it clouds the mind and distracts from devotion to God. Nonetheless, the popular association of *nezirut* with abstaining from both wine and meat is understandable, and *Beis Shammai* might be saying: when you declared yourself a *nazir* from meat, you were really expressing a desire to be a *nazir* in the full sense. Had the *Mishnah* only told us the meat case, however, I might have thought the dates case is too far-fetched for *Beis Shammai* to accept — dates have no association with *nezirut*.

### Door Example

And then there is the door. Had the *Mishnah* only given us the cases of dates and meat, one might have concluded that *Beis Hillel* concedes in those cases — that even *Beis Hillel* agrees that when there is some basis for the confusion or association, the declaration works. The door case eliminates that possibility. By including a case of complete absurdity — *aval deles*, but a door — the *Mishnah* shows that *Beis Shammai* maintains its position even when the appended object is total nonsense, and *Beis Hillel* maintains its position even in cases where the association might seem coherent. All three cases together establish the full scope of the dispute.

## Tosafot Solutions

### Mapik Hey

Having understood the *Gemara*'s reduction of the *Mishnah* to a straightforward debate about declaring oneself a *nazir* from meat or from a door, *Tosafot* now attempts the harder task: making sense of the actual language of the *Mishnah* on its own terms. The *Mishnah* says *amra para zu* — this cow said — and then quotes the cow as saying *hareini nezira im omedet ani*. How does one read those words in a way that produces the *Gemara*'s conclusion?

*Tosafot*'s first approach invokes anthropomorphism. The idea is that we project human intention onto an animal. The cow is crouched and straining — it *looks* as though it is telling us something. Just as the verse in *Yonah* says *veha'oniyah chishvah lehishaver* — the ship *thought* it would break — the ship of course was not literally thinking; the language is anthropomorphic, conveying that the ship was on the verge of breaking. So too here, when the *Mishnah* says the cow *said* it would be a *nezira*, it means the cow's posture and behavior are *telling* us that it cannot stand.

### Grammar Fix

That handles the first part of the *Mishnah*. But the second part presents a grammatical challenge. The word *nezira* is feminine. How does a man declare himself a feminine *nezira*? *Tosafot* offers a brilliant grammatical observation: the *hei* at the end of *nezira* is a *mapik hei* — a suffix meaning "from it." So *hareini nezira* does not mean "I will be a *nezira*" in the feminine sense; rather, it means *hareini nazir heimena* — I will be a *nazir* from it, from the cow. This transforms the declaration into exactly what the *Gemara* requires: a statement that the man will be a *nazir* from the cow's meat if it stands. The only remaining difficulty is the word *ani* at the end — *im omedet ani*, "if I stand" — which does not fit naturally, since it is the cow that would be standing, not the man. *Tosafot* acknowledges this difficulty but regards the rest of the reading as compelling despite that loose end.

### Literal Approach

*Tosafot*'s second approach takes the *Mishnah* more at face value. According to this reading, the man is genuinely reporting a story — he came and told us: I saw this cow, and it appeared to say that it would be a *nezira* if it stood up. The *Mishnah* records this as a straightforward narrative of what the man reported.

### Story/Meaning

Since what the man reports is complete nonsense — a cow cannot be a *nezira* — we interpret the telling of this absurd story as a *de facto* declaration of *nezirut* on the man's part. As *Tosafot* puts it: *heichah kesheadam mesaper lefaneinu kach bedaito lekayem alav sheyehe nazir mivesara* — when a person comes and tells us this strange story about a cow, what he is really communicating is that he intends to accept upon himself *nezirut* from the cow's meat. The story about the cow is the vehicle; the real content is his declaration of *nezirut*.

One might note that according to this second approach, the primary *chiddush* of the *Mishnah* shifts. It is no longer simply another illustration of the *Beis Shammai*/*Beis Hillel* debate about declaring *nezirut* from non-grape objects. Rather, the novel ruling would be that we interpret an absurd story about a cow as a declaration of *nezirut* altogether — that we translate bizarre narrative into legally operative speech. The *Gemara*'s focus on the *Beis Shammai*/*Beis Hillel* debate then becomes a secondary point following from that primary *chiddush*.

## Rava's Version

### Rava Objection

*Rava* now raises an objection to the entire framework of *Rami bar Chama*. *Mi ktani im amda me'aleha* — does the *Mishnah* actually say "if it stood up on its own"? It does not. And if that is the *Gemara*'s complaint, one might ask: of all the liberties one could take with a *Mishnah* about a talking cow, why is the insertion of the words "on its own" the sticking point? *Tosafot* essentially reads *Rava*'s objection as a more general dissatisfaction with the entire reading — a way of saying that the explanation of *Rami bar Chama* simply does not reflect what is actually happening in the *Mishnah*, and the missing words "on its own" are the marker of that problem.

### Inverted Condition

*Rava* then offers his own reframing. The cow is still crouched down in front of the man, but *Rava* changes the nature of the condition. Rather than the man saying *im amda* — if it stands — *Rava* reads the condition as *im lo amda* — if it does not stand. The man declares *hareini nazir miyayin* — I will be a standard *nazir* from wine — *im lo amda* — if it does not stand. And then the cow stood up on its own — *ve'amda me'aleha*.

One might immediately note that *Rava* himself is inserting a word — *lo* — that does not appear in the *Mishnah*, having just criticized *Rami bar Chama* for inserting the phrase "on its own." *Tosafot* acknowledges this asymmetry but suggests that a missing *lo* is a more tolerable emendation than a missing qualifying phrase, though the reasoning is admittedly strained.

### Ability to Raise

The dispute between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* now takes on an entirely different character. The man said he would be a *nazir* if the cow does not stand. The cow did stand. So why should he be a *nazir* at all? *Beis Shammai savri tursa de-hai gavra mishum okmei biyadei hu* — *Beis Shammai* holds that the man's focus, his *tursa*, was on whether he himself would be able to get the cow to stand. His statement "if it does not stand" was not a neutral observation about the cow's posture; it was a wager or a declaration of intent: "If I cannot get it to stand, I will be a *nazir*." The cow ultimately stood up on its own, but the man was not the one who caused it to stand. The condition — *im lo ukma biyadei*, if I cannot raise it — was therefore met, and he is a *nazir*.

*Tosafot* asks: where does the *Gemara* derive this reading of the man's psychology? The answer, *Tosafot* explains, is that the *Gemara* is trying to understand why a person would ever make such a condition. It makes sense if the man was trying to demonstrate his own ability to get the cow up — essentially making a deal: "I bet I can get it to stand; if I can't, I'll be a *nazir*." That is an intelligible psychological motivation. The cow's crouching is what prompts this, and the man's declaration is really about his own agency in resolving the situation.

### Standing Fact

*Beis Hillel* disagrees about the *tursa*. *U-Beis Hillel savri turpei d'hai gavra mishum terviahu* — *Beis Hillel* holds that the man's focus was simply on the crouching. He was upset that the cow was crouched down, and his declaration was: "If this cow doesn't get up, I'll be a *nazir*." It does not matter who or what caused it to stand — the relevant fact is simply whether the cow ended up on its feet. *Ha kama* — it stood. The condition was met in the most straightforward sense, and he is not a *nazir*.

## Resolution

### Rabbi Yehuda Limit

*Rava*'s reframing now runs into difficulty with *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position. If, as *Rava* explains, the man explicitly declared *hareini nazir miyayin* — I will be a *nazir* from wine — then why does *Rabbi Yehuda* limit even *Beis Shammai*'s ruling to saying that merely the cow becomes forbidden to him? If the man said outright that he would be a *nazir* from wine, there is no basis for *Rabbi Yehuda* to reduce the outcome to a mere prohibition on the cow.

### Must Be Meat

The *Gemara* resolves this by reverting to the formulation of *Rami bar Chama*: the case must be one in which the man said *hareini nazir mibesara* — I will be a *nazir* from its meat — not from wine. We return, in other words, to the same debate we have seen before: the man declared *nezirut* from a non-standard object — meat — and the three-way dispute of *Beis Shammai*, *Beis Hillel*, and *Rabbi Yehuda* plays out exactly as it did in the case of dates. *Beis Shammai* holds that he is a *nazir*, *Beis Hillel* holds that he is not, and *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that the meat becomes forbidden to him. The *tna'i* — the condition about the cow standing — is now layered on top of this debate.

### Concession

The *Gemara* then stages an exchange between the two schools. *Beis Shammai* addresses *Beis Hillel*: even according to your own logic, concede to us — *odu lan mei'ha* — that in this case the man's *tursa* was on the crouching, and the cow stood, so the condition was not met, and he should not be a *nazir* even according to you. *Beis Shammai* is saying: whatever you think about *nezirut* from meat in general, at least agree that in this specific case the condition was fulfilled — the cow stood — and therefore *Beis Hillel* should not impose *nezirut* here.

### Hillel Counter

*Beis Hillel* responds: *lav turpei d'hai gavra mishum okmei biyadei* — no, we read the man's focus as being on *his ability* to get the cow to stand, not merely on whether it stood. He was not the one who got it to stand, so the condition — from *Beis Hillel*'s perspective — was in fact not met, and the man is not a *nazir*. *Beis Shammai* in turn insists: *turpei d'hai gavra mishum okmei biyadei v'halo ukme* — his focus was indeed on whether he could get it to stand, and he did not, so the condition was met and he is a *nazir*.

## Simple Reading

### Core Claim

Stepping back, the simplest way to characterize what the *Gemara* has done is this: the talking cow is, in the end, a distraction. Whatever elaborate story the *Mishnah* constructs about a cow declaring *nezirut* from its crouched position, the operative legal content reduces to a man saying *hareini nazir mibesara* — I will be a *nazir* from its meat — with some kind of condition attached about whether the cow stands.

### Why Illustrations

The multiple illustrations — dates, meat, door, and now the cow — serve the purpose identified earlier: to demonstrate that the dispute between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* applies across the full range of cases, from the marginally understandable to the completely absurd.

### Parallel Structure

The *Mishnah* of the talking cow thus runs in exact parallel to the earlier *Mishnah* about dates. *Beis Shammai* says the man is a *nazir*, *Beis Hillel* says he is not, and *Rabbi Yehuda* says the cow — or the dates, or the door — is forbidden to him. *Rava*'s version adds one further layer: a debate about whether the condition embedded in the declaration was actually met, turning on whether the man's intent was simply that the cow would stand, or specifically that he himself would succeed in raising it. What the talking cow and the talking door have to do with this precise dispute about the nature of a conditional vow remains, to say the least, an interesting question — but there it is, laid out in the *Gemara* of *Nazir* daf 10.
