# Megillah 5b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Purim on Shabbat

### Megillah & Shabbat Q&A

Before resuming the *sugia*, two questions from the previous session warrant brief attention. The first concerned why the concern that one might carry the *megillah* through a public domain — the rationale behind not reading the *megillah* on *Shabbat* — applies even in walled cities. Despite considerable research, no source could be found that directly addresses this question. The *gemara* does acknowledge the existence of a *reshut ha-rabim* even within walled cities, and the silence of the *rishonim* and *acharonim* on this specific point is itself striking.

### Mishloach Manot Timing

The second question concerned the timing of *mishloach manot* and the *seudah* when the *megillah* is read early not merely because of a Monday-Thursday calendar adjustment, but because the fifteenth falls on *Shabbat*. Here the initial position holds: *mishloach manot* travels together with the *seudah*. The underlying principle is that *mishloach manot* is organically connected to the *seudah*, and the two are not separated.

### Jerusalem Practice

When the fifteenth of Adar falls on *Shabbat*, the current practice in Jerusalem is as follows: *mikra megillah* is performed on the fourteenth (Friday), the *seudah* and *mishloach manot* are both observed on Sunday, and the only observance that takes place on the fifteenth itself — on *Shabbat* — is *al ha-nissim*. The day is called *meshulash* precisely because its observances are distributed across three days, though in practice only *al ha-nissim* falls on the fifteenth proper. The reason given for moving the *seudah* to Sunday is not the concern about carrying *mishloach manot* on *Shabbat*, but rather that a *seudah* of *simchat Purim* ought to be distinct from *oneg Shabbat* and should not compete with it.

### Achronim Debate

There is, however, a genuine debate among the *acharonim* on this matter. Some argue strongly, on the basis of the Bavli, that *simcha* is *bo ba-yom* — that the *seudah* belongs on the fifteenth itself — and therefore that both the *seudah* and *mishloach manot* should be observed on *Shabbat*. This position openly acknowledges the difficulty raised earlier: if we are concerned about carrying when reading the *megillah*, why are we not equally concerned about carrying *mishloach manot*? Yet this view holds nonetheless. The two positions are thus: either the *seudah* and *mishloach manot* fall on *Shabbat* itself, or they fall on Sunday with only *al ha-nissim* remaining on *Shabbat*. Jerusalem practice follows the latter.

## Rabbi Plants on Purim

### Gemara's Question

The *gemara* now resumes from the story involving Rabbi, one element of which is that Rabbi planted something on Purim. This leads directly into the question of whether, in addition to *mikra megillah*, *matanot la-evyonim*, the *seudah*, and *mishloach manot*, there is an obligation to refrain from *melacha* on Purim — not in the manner of *Shabbat*, but perhaps analogous to *Chol Ha-Moed*, where *melacha* is restricted but not entirely prohibited. The *gemara* frames the question simply: *v'Rabbi heichi nata b'Purim?* — how did Rabbi plant something on Purim?

### Baraita on Purim

Rav Yosef cites a *baraita* in response. The *baraita* derives its principles from the verse *la'asot otam yemei mishteh ve-simcha ve-yom tov u-mishloach manot ish l're'eihu* — to make these days of feasting and rejoicing, of *yom tov*, and of sending gifts to one another. The *baraita* reads each element as prohibiting something that would undermine it: *simcha* teaches that *hesped* is forbidden on Purim; *mishteh* teaches that fasting is forbidden; and *yom tov* teaches that *melacha* is forbidden. It is worth noting the pattern: each element is translated not into an affirmative obligation but into something one may *not* do. According to this *baraita*, performing *melacha* on Purim is prohibited.

### Melacha Prohibition

The *gemara* thus accepts the premise that *melacha* is forbidden on Purim, and proceeds to explain Rabbi's planting on that basis.

### Unwalled City Explanation

The first explanation offered is that Rabbi was from an unwalled city, where Purim is observed on the fourteenth. When he planted, he planted on the fifteenth — which was not *his* Purim — and there was therefore no prohibition in force.

### Tiberia Walled

The *gemara* challenges this: Rabbi was from Tiberias, *ve-Tverya mukefes chomah mi-yemot Yehoshua bin Nun hi* — and Tiberias is a walled city from the time of Joshua ben Nun. Consequently, Tiberias observes the fifteenth. The explanation is then revised: Rabbi planted on the fourteenth, which was not his day of observance. This too the *gemara* considers satisfactory.

### Chizkiya's Doubt

But the *gemara* pushes further. The revised explanation assumes that it was obvious to Rabbi that Tiberias had the status of a walled city from Joshua's time. Yet this very assumption is called into question: Chizkiya read the *megillah* in Tiberias on both the fourteenth and the fifteenth because he was uncertain — *misafka lei* — whether Tiberias had been surrounded by a wall from the time of Joshua. When such a doubt arises, the practice is to read on both days; the *bracha*, however, is recited on the fourteenth, since the fourteenth is the norm, and the *megillah* itself, in the phrase *al kein ha-perazim ha-yoshvim*, speaks explicitly of the reading of the fourteenth without even explicitly prescribing what the non-*perazim* do.

The *gemara* resolves the tension simply: Chizkiya had a genuine doubt; Rabbi did not. Rabbi was certain that Tiberias was a walled city and therefore observed the fifteenth as his Purim, planting on the fourteenth without any prohibition.

## Melacha Status

### Non-Purim Day

Having established that Rabbi planted on what was not his Purim day, the *gemara* raises a broader question: even granting that Rabbi's day of obligation was the fifteenth, does the fourteenth carry no status at all for residents of walled cities? Is there truly no restriction on *melacha* on the non-Purim day for such a person?

### Megillas Taanis

The *gemara* invokes *Megillas Taanis*, which lists the minor holidays on which *hesped* and *ta'anit* are forbidden. *Megillas Taanis* states *es yom arba-asar ve-es yom chamisha-asar* — both the fourteenth and the fifteenth — calling them *yemei Purim inun*, the days of Purim: *de-lo le-misped be-hon*, that one does not eulogize on those days and does not fast. As we learned extensively in *Masechet Ta'anit*, while most of *Megillas Taanis* no longer applies — *batla Megillas Taanis* — Purim and Chanukah remain in force with respect to the prohibitions of *hesped* and *ta'anit*.

### Rava's Ruling

Rava asks: why does *Megillas Taanis* need to mention the days of Purim at all, since they are already mentioned in the *megillah* itself? He answers: *lo nitzrecha ella le-asur de-zeh be-zeh ve-de-zeh be-zeh* — the only reason *Megillas Taanis* repeats both dates is to establish that the fourteenth and fifteenth form a package. Regardless of which city a person is from, both days are days on which *hesped* and *ta'anit* are forbidden. Just as we do not say *tachanun* on *Shushan Purim*, both days carry this minimal status.

### Own Day Only

The *gemara* might then assume that if both days carry the prohibition of *hesped* and *ta'anit*, perhaps both should also carry a prohibition of *melacha*. The *gemara* rejects this: *hani mili be-hesped u-ve-ta'anit* — the package deal applies only to *hesped* and *ta'anit*. When it comes to *melacha*, *yom echad u-tu lo* — only on one's own day of observance does any restriction on *melacha* apply; the other day carries none.

### Rav Curses Planter

The *gemara* then cites a striking story. Rav saw a man planting flax on Purim, cursed him, and the flax never sprouted. This seems to demonstrate that *melacha* is seriously forbidden on Purim. The *gemara* answers: that was the man's own Purim day — *bar yomei hava* — not the other day. And with that, the *gemara* appears to maintain a firm prohibition of *melacha* on one's own day.

Before moving to the next stage of the discussion, the *tosafot* here raise a remarkable challenge worth pausing over. *Tosafot* quotes a *teshuvah* of Rashi in which Rashi asks: *ve-khi mintan koach li-yemei Megillat Esther lidchos aveilus?* — since when does the *megillah* have the authority to override *aveilus* by prohibiting *hesped*? This is a penetrating question. *Megillas Taanis* is full of such prohibitions even for far more minor occasions, and we have emphasized throughout the enormous authority that *Chazal* ascribed to the *megillah*. Yet Rashi, motivated by the evident practice of his time — *ve-hachazina de-choi amma nahagu bo heter*, people were in fact delivering *hespedim* on Purim — problematizes the *gemara*'s ruling entirely. Rashi concludes in that *teshuvah* that the prohibition of *hesped* on Purim amounts to nothing more than not falling on one's face — that is, not reciting *tachanun* — because Purim is *yom simcha* rather than *yom tzara*. It does not actually prohibit a formal *hesped*. This is a striking position, essentially dismantling the very ruling we have just studied. And if it applies to Purim, it presumably applies to Chanukah as well.

## Melacha Not Obligatory

### Rabba's Position

The entire foregoing discussion proceeded on the premise that *melacha* is genuinely forbidden on Purim. Rabba the son of Rav now overturns that premise entirely. He states: *hesped ve-ta'anit kibilu aleihu* — the Jews ultimately accepted upon themselves the prohibition of *hesped* and *ta'anit*; *melacha lo kibilu aleihu* — but they never accepted the prohibition of *melacha*. The restriction on *melacha* was simply never taken on as a binding obligation.

### Verses Analysis

The *gemara* provides the textual basis for this claim. Initially, when Mordechai and Esther sent word, the formulation included *yom tov*: *la'asot otam yemei mishte ve-simcha ve-yom tov u-mishloach manot ish le-re'eihu*. The Jewish people were asked to make these days into a *yom tov*.

### Acceptance Limited

But when the Jews confirmed their acceptance, the formulation changed. The verse reads: *kiyemu ve-kiblu ha-Yehudim aleihem ve-al zaram lihyot osim et yemei ha-Purim ha-eleh la'asot otam yemei mishte ve-simcha* — and the words *ve-yom tov* do not appear. The Jews accepted *mishte ve-simcha* — feasting and rejoicing — but they did not accept the *yom tov* status that entails the prohibition of *melacha*. Purim, in the end, is not *assur be-melacha*.

This point runs far deeper than a technical question about *melacha*. The entire *sugia* of *megillah* is shadowed by questions about the authority to create new holidays. The *megillah* itself was a late addition to *Sifrei Tanach*, and the authority of its verses to obligate various practices has been a recurring theme. In that light, it is significant that both Purim and Chanukah — the two rabbinic holidays — do not carry the full weight of *yom tov*. One says *al ha-nissim*, one lights candles, one reads the *megillah*; but the day does not become a *yom tov* in the full sense. That reflects something essential about the character of these days, not merely a technicality. To create a full *yom tov* would have required a different level of authority entirely.

### Minhag vs. Halacha

This raises the practical question: if *melacha* is permitted on Purim, why did Rav curse the man who planted? And if people generally did not do *melacha* on Purim, what was the status of that practice? The *gemara* answers: *devarim ha-mutarim va-acherim nahagu bahen issur* — these are things that are technically permitted but which others have taken on as a custom of stringency. In Rav's locale, there was an established *minhag* not to perform *melacha* on Purim, and the man was violating that *minhag*. As for Rabbi, in his locale no such *minhag* existed — or alternatively, Rabbi was demonstrating by his action that the restriction is only a *minhag* and not a binding *halacha*.

### Simcha Planting

A further possibility is also offered: *le-olam nahug* — perhaps Rabbi's locale did observe the *minhag*. In that case, why did Rabbi plant? Because *Rabbi netiya shel simcha nata* — what Rabbi planted was a *netiya shel simcha*, a planting associated with joy. The *minhag* of not performing *melacha* on Purim is not modeled on the *yom tov* prohibition of *melacha*. It is rooted, rather, in the idea that one should not distract oneself from the *simcha* of the day. A *melacha* that is itself an expression of *simcha* — that contributes to rather than detracts from joy — falls outside the scope of this *minhag* entirely. Rabbi could therefore plant without violating anything, because his planting was itself an act of *simcha*.

This distinction is important for understanding the character of the *minhag*. Even within the framework of the *minhag*, we are not treating Purim as a quasi-*yom tov*. The *minhag* is not "avoid *melacha* because this day is like a *yom tov*"; it is "avoid *melacha* because it distracts from *simcha*." That is a fundamentally different rationale, and it produces a fundamentally different boundary.

### Simcha Work Types

The *gemara* brings a parallel from the opposite direction — from the realm of *aveilus* — to substantiate the concept of *netiya shel simcha*. The *mishna* in *Ta'anit* teaches that if the public fast days passed without the community being answered, one curtails *massa u-mattan* (commercial activity), *binyan*, *netiya*, *erusin*, and *nisu'in*. A *baraita* then specifies that the *binyan* referred to is *binyan shel simcha* — joyous construction — and the *netiya* is *netiya shel simcha* — joyous planting. *Eizo hi binyan shel simcha?* — what is joyous construction? Building a *beit chatnut* for one's son, that is, a wedding house, a deeply celebratory undertaking. *Eizo hi netiya shel simcha?* — what is joyous planting? A royal garden, an *avroneki shel melachim*, something planted for pleasure and majesty rather than for commerce.

The halachic category of *netiya shel simcha* thus has independent standing in *halacha*: it is singled out for prohibition when one enters an *aveilus*-like mode because it is the paradigmatic act of joy. By the same logic, it is precisely the type of *melacha* that remains permitted under the Purim *minhag* — because the *minhag* is about not abandoning *simcha*, and a *netiya shel simcha* is *simcha* itself. Even within the *minhag*, we do not treat Purim as a *yom tov*; we treat the *minhag* as an expression of the day's *simcha*, and work that embodies *simcha* is therefore not only permitted but fitting.

## Walled Cities ID

### Tiberia Walls

The *gemara* now returns to the earlier statement about Chizkiya reading the *megillah* in Tiberias on both the fourteenth and the fifteenth due to his uncertainty about its status. The *gemara* asks: how could there be any genuine doubt about whether Tiberias was a walled city from the time of Joshua? The verse in *Yehoshua* lists among the fortified cities of Naftali: *Tzidim, Tzer, Chamat, Rakas, ve-Chinneres*. And as we will establish momentarily, *Rakas* is Tiberias. The verse identifies it explicitly as a walled city. How then was Chizkiya uncertain?

The *gemara* answers: *mishum de-chad gisa shura de-yama hava* — on one side, Tiberias is bounded not by a wall but by the water. It is not fully enclosed by walls; one side is open to the Sea of Galilee. That is the source of the doubt.

### Walled Definition

But if one side is unenclosed, why is there even a doubt? The *gemara* pursues this: if one side has no wall, surely it simply does not qualify — *vadai lav chomah hi*. And indeed a *baraita* addressing the laws of houses in walled cities for *yovel* — *asher lo chomah saviv* — specifies that Tiberias does not qualify for that category, because *yamma chomatah*, the sea serves as its wall. The *baraita* requires *saviv*, full encirclement. So for *battei arei chomah* there would be no doubt: Tiberias does not qualify.

Yet for *mikra megillah*, the *gemara* says, there *is* a doubt: *ki ka mesafka lei le-inyan mikra Megillah*. This introduces a critical point: the criteria for *ir chomah* in *megillah* law may not be identical to those borrowed from *battei arei chomah*. Much is certainly borrowed from that pre-existing category, as *Tosafot* discusses, but the borrowing may not be total.

### Megillah Definition

Why might *megillah* law define the category differently? Rashi's approach here is illuminating. The *pesukim* in the *megillah* do not define walled cities directly; they define *perazim* — unwalled villages. The negative is defined, not the positive. This asymmetry matters: the opposite of a defined category is not simply another defined category but rather everything that is *not* that category. A city that is partially walled, like Tiberias, is not a *peruza* — an open, unwalled settlement — but may also not satisfy a strict definition of *ir chomah*. Depending on which end one defines, these intermediate cases fall differently.

### Exposed vs. Protected

This leads the *gemara* to articulate two possible understandings of what distinguishes *perazim* from *mukkafin*. The first: *mishum de-hani miglu ve-hani lo miglu* — the distinction is between being exposed and being concealed from view. On this reading, *perazim* are settlements that lie open and visible. If so, Tiberias, which is exposed on its water-side, might fall into the *perazim* category — *ve-hani nami mighalya*, it too is exposed. The second possibility: *mishum de-hani mignu ve-hani lo mignu* — the distinction is about security and protection, whether a city is defended or not. On this reading, Tiberias, which has walls on three sides and the sea on the fourth, is genuinely defended — *ve-ha nami mignu* — and therefore might qualify as a walled city.

Because neither criterion produces a clear answer for Tiberias, Chizkiya had a genuine doubt. The *gemara* adds a broader consideration offered by Ramban, who raises the question of why two separate holidays were created at all — the fourteenth for unwalled cities and the fifteenth for walled ones. Ramban argues, based on a close reading of the final chapters of the *megillah*, that those in walled cities initially felt the miracle less directly, since they were better defended and less exposed to the threat. This would explain why *mukkafin* status was tied to defensibility — *mignu* — and why residents of walled cities may have initially resisted accepting Purim altogether. This thesis has significant difficulties, but it anchors itself precisely in this *gemara*'s question about whether the relevant criterion is exposure or protection.

## Biblical City ID

### Rav Ashi Case

The *gemara* now turns to a related case. Rav Ashi read the *megillah* in Hutzo on both the fourteenth and the fifteenth — *maspka lei im mukefet chomah mi-yemot Yehoshua bin Nun hi o lo* — uncertain whether it was walled from Joshua's time. Alternatively, some report that Rav Ashi had no such uncertainty and stated definitively that this Hutzo of Binyamin was indeed walled from Joshua's time, in which case the account of him reading on both days may refer to a different Hutzo altogether.

### RY Identifications

Rabbi Yochanan introduces an identification that he had made in his youth and later confirmed was correct. He reports: *ki havina talya*, when I was just a boy, *amina milta dishailna le-savaya*, I said something and asked the elders, *ve-hishtakach kevasi*, and it was found that I was right. His identifications concern the cities listed in *Yehoshua* as walled: *Chamat zu Tverya* — Chamat is Tiberias; *Rakas zu Tzipori* — Rakas is Tzipori; *Kinneret zu Ginnosar* — Kinneret is Ginosar.

### City Identities

The etymologies are explained. *Chamat* is Tiberias because of the *chamei Tverya*, the hot springs of Tiberias — the name reflects the city's most famous natural feature. *Rakas* is Tzipori because *midalya ke-rikta de-nahara* — Tzipori sits atop a mountain, elevated like the bank of a river above the river's bed. *Kinneret* is Ginosar because *dematiki peiraheha ke-kala de-kinri* — its fruits are as sweet as the sound of a harp; some also note the harp-like shape of the lake itself as a possible basis for the name.

### Eulogy Evidence

Rava now asks: how can anyone claim that *Rakas* is not Tiberias? The eulogies delivered in Tiberias make this identification unmistakably clear. When a great sage died, the *hesped* delivered there was: *gadol hu be-Sheshach ve-shem lo be-Rakas* — he was great in *Sheshach* (a name in *Tanach* for Bavel) and he made a name for himself in *Rakas*. When the *aron* arrived in Tiberias for burial, the eulogy proclaimed: *ohavei seridim, yoshvei Rakas, tze'u ve-kiblu harugei omek* — lovers of the remnant, dwellers of Rakas, go out and receive those slain in the depths, *omek* referring to Bavel's low-lying terrain. And when Rabbi Zeira died — born in Bavel, raised to greatness in *Eretz Yisrael*, and residing in Tiberias — the *hesped* opened: *eretz Shinar hara ve-yalda, eretz tzvi gidla sha'ashu'eha, oy na la amra Rakas ki avda kli chemdatah* — woe says Rakas, for it has lost its precious vessel. Rakas is unambiguously Tiberias.

### Rabba Disputes

Rava therefore disputes Rabbi Yochanan's identification. He accepts that *Rakas* is Tiberias, but rejects the claim that *Chamat* is Tiberias. Instead, *Chamat zu chamei Gader* — Chamat is the hot springs of Gader. And since *Rakas* is Tiberias, not Tzipori, the identification of *Rakas* with Tzipori must be abandoned.

For the etymology of *Rakas* and Tiberias, Rava offers two explanations. Rabbi Yirmi holds that *Rakas* is simply the city's proper name, carrying no symbolic meaning, and that *Tiberias* is the additional name given to it. Why was it called *Tverya*? *She-yoshevet be-tibura shel Eretz Yisrael* — because it sits at the navel, the center, of the Land of Israel. Rava himself holds similarly that *Rakas* is the original name, while *Tverya* was given because *she-tova re'iyata* — its appearance is good, it has a fine and distinguished reputation.

### Kitron & Tzipori

With *Rakas* now identified as Tiberias rather than Tzipori, the *gemara* must locate Tzipori elsewhere. Ze'iri provides the identification: *Kitron zu Tzipori* — Kitron is Tzipori. The etymology of the name Tzipori is that *she-yoshevet be-rosho shel tzippor* — the city sits atop a mountain like a bird perched on high.

### Zebulun Complaint

But the *gemara* immediately challenges this. Kitron appears in *Shoftim*: *Zvulun lo horish et yoshvei Kitron ve-et yoshvei Nahalol* — Zebulun did not dispossess the inhabitants of Kitron. Kitron was thus in the portion of Zebulun. If Kitron were Tzipori, Zebulun would have had Tzipori in its *chelek* from the outset. Yet Zebulun is known to have complained bitterly about its inheritance.

The *gemara* recalls the relevant passage. The verse in *Shirat Devorah* — *Zvulun am charaf nafsho lamut* — is read here not as simple praise of Zebulun's courage but as evidence of Zebulun's complaint, since the verse continues *ve-Naftali al meromei sadeh*, pointing to Naftali's fields. Zebulun said before the Holy One: *Ribono shel Olam*, to my brother Naftali You gave fields and vineyards; to me You gave mountains and hills. To him You gave productive land; to me, You gave seas and rivers. God responded: *kulam tzrichin lach* — everyone will need you. Through the *chalazon* — the sea-creature that produces the blue dye for *techelet* — and through the hidden treasures of the sand (*shefunei temunei chol*), Zebulun would thrive. Rav Yosef explains: *sefunei* is the *chalazon*; *temunei* is *tarit* (tuna); *chol* is the fine sand from which precious white *levanah* glass is produced. Zebulun's concern about protection from theft is addressed with the assurance that *sham yizbechu zivchei tzedek* — any who take from Zebulun without payment will find their commerce fail.

If Zebulun had possessed Tzipori — *ve-ha Tzipori milta de-adifa tuva* — it would have had no basis for complaint. Reish Lakish testifies: *le-didi chazi li zavat chalav u-devash de-Tzipori* — I myself saw the flowing of milk and honey of Tzipori, extending sixteen *mil* by sixteen *mil*. Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan reports that the milk-and-honey region of all of *Eretz Yisrael* stretched twenty-two *parsa* in length and six *parsa* in width, a vast area, and Zebulun's share exceeded what any single tribe should have in terms of *zavat chalav u-devash*. How then could they complain?

The *gemara* resolves this: *afilu hakhi sadot u-chramim adifa lei* — despite everything Zebulun possessed, what it fixed its gaze on was the one thing it lacked: fields and vineyards. This is precisely what *ve-Naftali al meromei sadeh* captures — it was Naftali's fields that Zebulun could not stop comparing its portion to. *Shema mina* — and this allows us to accept the identification of Kitron with Tzipori, since Zebulun's complaint is vindicated by the realization that Tzipori, for all its splendor, did not offer what Zebulun actually wanted.

## Rome & Jerusalem

### Ekron=Caesarea

Rabbi Abbahu opens a new set of identifications anchored in a verse from *Tzephaniah*: *ve-Ekron te-aker* — Ekron will be uprooted. *Zu Keisari bat Edom* — this is Caesarea, daughter of Edom, the Roman outpost situated *bein ha-cholot*, among the coastal sands. *She-hi hayeta yated teku'a le-Yisrael bi-yemei Yevanim* — it was a thorn, or rather a peg, driven into Israel's side in the days of the Greeks, that is, before the rise of Rome proper, in the era of the Hellenistic kingdoms. When the Hasmoneans prevailed and presumably seized Caesarea, *hayu korim otah achidat migdal shir* — they renamed it the conquered tower of *Shir*.

### Destruction Promise

Rabbi Yosei bar Chanina turns to another verse: *ve-hasiroti damav mi-piv ve-shikutzav mi-bein shinav ve-nishar gam hu l'Eloheinu* — I will remove the blood from their mouth and the abomination from their teeth, and they will remain for our God. *Ve-hasiroti damav mi-piv* — this refers to *beit bamya shelahen*, their altars of sacrifice. *Ve-shikutzav mi-bein shinav* — this refers to *beit galya shelahen*; some understand this as another idolatrous installation, others as their oracular sites, from *legalot*, to reveal. And *ve-nishar gam hu l'Eloheinu* — what will remain, what will endure? *Elu batei knesiyot u-vatei midrashot she-be-Edom* — the synagogues and houses of study in the Roman Empire. Everything else will be uprooted; these will survive. The promise of reversal is clear: what is good in the empire will remain and be reclaimed; what is corrupt will be destroyed.

### Theaters to Study

Rabbi Yitzchak adds to this theme, identifying *Leshem* as Pamyas (Banias), and Rabbi Abbahu returns to *Ekron te-aker*: *zu Keisari bat Edom*, Caesarea, daughter of Edom, *she-hayita metropolis shel melachim* — it was a metropolis of kings. Some understand this to mean that kings were raised there; others, that kings were appointed from its population.

The verse continues: *ve-haya ke-aluf bi-Yehuda ve-Ekron ke-Yevusi*. This is read as: *elu teratiyot ve-kirkasiyot she-be-Edom* — these are the theaters and circuses of Rome. *She-atidim sarei Yehuda le-lamed ba-hem Torah ba-rabbim* — the great auditoriums of Rome are destined to become places where the princes of Judah teach Torah in public. *Yevusi*, Rashi explains, is Jerusalem, since Jerusalem was originally the city of the Yevusites. So Ekron — Caesarea — will become like the Yevusi city, like Jerusalem: its theaters and circuses will be transformed into *batei midrash*.

### Caesarea & Jerusalem

The *gemara* distills the essential dynamic into a stark formulation: *im yomar lecha adam charvu shteihem* — if someone tells you both Caesarea and Jerusalem are destroyed, *al ta'amen* — do not believe it. *Yashvu shteihem* — both are flourishing, *al ta'amen* — do not believe that either. But if someone says *charva Keisari ve-yashva Yerushalayim*, Caesarea is destroyed and Jerusalem is settled — or *charva Yerushalayim ve-yashva Keisari*, Jerusalem is destroyed and Caesarea is settled — *ta'amen*, believe it. *She-ne'emar imalei ha-charava* — as the verse says, I will fill the desolate one. If one is filled, the other is desolate; if that one is filled, the first becomes desolate. These two cities cannot both thrive simultaneously; their fates are locked in an inverse relationship.

### Jacob & Esau

This relationship is, at its deepest level, the relationship of Jacob and Esau — *Yaakov ve-Esav* — whose eternal conflict is encoded in the verse *u-leom mi-leom ye'ematz*, one nation will be stronger than the other. Rome and Israel, Caesarea and Jerusalem, are the historical instantiation of that primal conflict. One is always ascendant; the other, not. They cannot both flourish together.

The significance of this *aggadata* at this point in the *masechta* is worth pausing over. Purim is, in a certain sense, the holiday of persistent subjugation. The *gemara* elsewhere observes that we do not say *Hallel* on Purim because *akati avdei Achashveirosh anan* — we remain servants of Achashveirosh. Unlike Chanukah, which marked a genuine historical transformation — the restoration of Jewish self-governance — Purim marked only the removal of an immediate threat, while the underlying historical condition of exile and subjugation remained unchanged. These closing *drashot* about Rome, Caesarea, and Jerusalem thus speak precisely to that condition. They acknowledge that the Jews remain under the yoke of Rome, and they look forward — through biblical verses and rabbinic reading — to the reversal that is promised. In doing so, they give voice to the poignant dimension of Purim: a holiday of survival, not yet of redemption; a day of *simcha*, but not yet of *geula*.
