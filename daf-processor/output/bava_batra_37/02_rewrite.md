# Bava Batra 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chazakah: Years vs. Harv…

### Opening & Previous Summa…

We pick up at the bottom of 36b, having just concluded a discussion about whether *nir* — upturning and tilling the soil — constitutes a valid *chazakah*. More significantly, the *Gemara* there established that the positions of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva in the *Mishnah* — permitting partial years at the beginning and end of the three-year period — represent only those two views. Rav and Shmuel clarified that the *Chachamim* require three full calendar years, while Shmuel adds that three full harvests may suffice, which could in principle be achieved in less than three years, provided the harvests themselves are complete.

### Rashbam's Two Models

Before moving forward, it is worth examining one important passage in the Rashbam that illuminates the conceptual stakes of this dispute. Approximately twenty lines down in the narrow-column commentary, in the entry beginning with the word *shanim*, the Rashbam writes: *zu divrei Rabbi Yishmael ve-Rabbi Akiva*, that this refers to the positions of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva — for whom the *chazakah* of a field is three years but not necessarily day-for-day, being eighteen months according to Rabbi Yishmael and fourteen according to Rabbi Akiva. But the *Chachamim*, in the entry beginning with *chachamim*, require three full years in every matter, measured day-for-day.

The Rashbam then connects this dispute to the two foundational models of *chazakah* introduced at the opening of the *perek*. Recall that one model treats *chazakah* as a status-creating mechanism: doing something three times establishes a presumptive identity or position, analogous to the *shor mu'ad*. On this view, it is the act of doing something three times — three harvests — that generates the legal status, and whether these span full calendar years is secondary. The second model, by contrast, grounds *chazakah* in the practical reality of lost documents: after three years, one has a legitimate claim that one's deed of ownership has been lost or discarded, because a person is not expected to safeguard a *shtar* indefinitely. On this view, the operative measure is calendar time, since the question is how long a person remains vigilant about preserving documents — and that is a function of years, not harvests.

The Rashbam thus proposes a tidy correspondence: those who measure *chazakah* by acts (three harvests, even partial years) align with the status-creation model, while those who require three full calendar years align with the lost-document model. The two conceptual approaches map onto two distinct ways of measuring time.

### Nimukei Yosef's Critique

The Nimukei Yosef pushes back against this framework. He argues that throughout the *Gemara*, both considerations have been operating together and are not independent variables. It is not mere calendar time that renders a person less vigilant about preserving a *shtar*; what produces that relaxation is the combination of one's own visible, owner-like conduct over an extended period together with the original owner's unbroken silence in the face of that conduct. The absence of protest is itself evidence that the claim will not be pursued, and that is what gradually diminishes one's motivation to safeguard the deed.

On this reading, the lost-document rationale does not reduce to a fixed quantity of calendar time. It is intertwined with the owner's silence and with the character of one's acts — acts that should have provoked a response but did not. The two conceptual models are therefore not cleanly separable, and the Rashbam's one-to-one correspondence between models and measurement standards does not hold. Whether *chazakah* is measured in harvests or in years is not simply a function of which underlying theory one adopts; it also turns on the nature of the possessor's conduct and the owner's reaction to it.

## Thirty Trees Example

### Thirty Trees Case

With this background in place, the *Gemara* on 37a introduces a teaching of Abaye. The *Gemara* had just reviewed Rabbi Yishmael's position that in the case of an orchard, three different harvests within a single year — figs, grapes, and olives, for instance — can each count as one of the three required acts, so that *chazakah* is achieved within a single calendar year. *Amar Abaye*, says Abaye: *miderabbi Yishmael nishma lerabanan* — from Rabbi Yishmael's teaching we can derive something relevant even according to the *Rabbanan*, who reject his leniency and insist on three full years.

The case is as follows: thirty trees are planted across a field at the proper density of ten trees per *beit se'ah* — a plot of fifty by fifty cubits. This ratio is recognized as the standard, non-overcrowded distribution for an orchard. Each year for three consecutive years, the possessor harvests ten of the thirty trees, rotating through different groups of ten, so that over three years all thirty are harvested — but no single year sees a harvest of all of them.

The *Gemara* rules: *harei zo chazakah* — this constitutes a valid *chazakah*. How does Rabbi Yishmael's teaching support this? *Lav mi amar Rabbi Yishmael chad peira havei chazakah l'chulhu peiri* — did not Rabbi Yishmael say that harvesting one type of fruit counts as a *chazakah* for all the fruit of the field? In Rabbi Yishmael's case, the grapes are planted only in one portion of the field, the olives in another, and the figs in a third; yet harvesting the grapes alone represents a *chazakah* for the entire orchard. So too here: even though we do not follow Rabbi Yishmael's leniency regarding the number of years required, we accept his underlying premise that harvesting from a portion of the trees — when they are properly interspersed throughout the field — constitutes a *chazakah* representing the entirety. *Hanei havei chazakah lahanei v'hanei havei chazakah lahanei* — what is done to some counts for the others, and vice versa.

### When Fruits Unavailable

The *Gemara* immediately qualifies this ruling. *V'hanei mili heicha d'lo afiki* — this applies only where the other trees have not yet produced their fruit. The logic is that when the ten trees being harvested are the only ones currently bearing fruit, the possessor's selective harvesting is not a conscious decision to ignore the rest; it simply reflects what is available. *Aval afiki v'lo achlinhu lo havei chazakah* — but if the other trees have already produced their fruit and the possessor is not harvesting them, he is effectively abandoning them, and that abandonment undermines the *chazakah*. The thirty-tree principle functions only when each year's harvest corresponds to what is naturally available that season, mirroring the logic of Rabbi Yishmael's case where each harvest type is in season at its own time.

### Trees Interspersed

The *Gemara* adds a second condition: *vehu debazei bizuyei* — the trees must be genuinely interspersed and scattered across the entire field. The term *bizuyei*, which the Rashbam explains as *lashon beziah*, connoting splitting or dispersal, requires that the ten trees harvested each year be distributed throughout the whole plot rather than clustered in their own section.

The reason is intuitive. If the thirty trees are arranged in three separate clusters — ten in the northern third, ten in the middle, ten in the southern third — then harvesting the northern cluster is a *chazakah* only on that portion of the field. It demonstrates no connection to the rest. But if trees of different ripening cycles are intermingled across the entire area, so that harvesting any ten of them requires ranging across the whole field, then that act of harvesting visibly represents dominion over the entire plot.

The Rashbam notes, somewhat reluctantly, that this interspersed model presupposes that vines, olive trees, and figs are planted among one another throughout the same field — an arrangement that raises concerns of *kilayim* and is in any case highly inefficient from a practical farming standpoint. The *Gemara* is nonetheless committed to this condition, because without it the principle collapses: a harvest confined to one section of a field cannot establish *chazakah* over the rest.

This condition also connects directly to the broader question of when a *chazakah* on trees extends to the land beneath and around them. If the trees are distributed at the proper density across a defined area, that ratio creates what might be called an orchard identity — the land and the trees form an integrated unit, and working the trees represents dominion over the associated land. But if trees are scattered sparsely across a very large space, or densely packed into a small one, the organic connection between tree and land is severed, and the *chazakah* on the trees does not carry over to the ground.

## Trees & Land Split

### Rav Zevid Position

The *Gemara* now presents the following scenario. A plot of land with trees — originally owned by a single person — has been in the possession of two different individuals simultaneously for three years: one person, call him David, has been harvesting the fruit of the trees, while another, call her Jenna, has been working the land itself, perhaps cultivating a vegetable garden or otherwise making use of the soil. Each has maintained this conduct for the full three-year period. What does each one's *chazakah* establish?

*Amar Rav Zevid: zeh kanah ilanot v'zeh kanah karka* — David has acquired the trees, and Jenna has acquired the land. Before unpacking the ruling, it is worth noting a significant interpretive dispute between the Rashbam and Tosafot about the very nature of this case. The Rashbam reads it not as a *chazakah* scenario at all but as a straightforward sale or gift: the original owner explicitly gave David the trees and Jenna the land, each performed an act of acquisition, and the question is simply what each transaction included. Tosafot, by contrast, reads the case as a genuine *chazakah* dispute — David and Jenna each maintained possession for three years and now claim ownership — and takes the word *kanah* to mean that each one's *chazakah* establishes the respective ownership. The legal question, however, ultimately converges for both readings: when ownership of an orchard is split between a person who takes the trees and a person who takes the land, what does each party actually receive?

Rav Zevid's position, read through Tosafot's lens, is that the two *chazakot* are entirely severable. David's three years of harvesting the trees establishes ownership of the trees and nothing more — no ownership of the land beneath or around them. Jenna's three years of working the land establishes ownership of the land, with no claim to the trees above it. The two estates are fully separated.

*Matkif lah Rav Pappa* — Rav Pappa challenges this conclusion. *Im ken ein lo lebaal ilanot bakarka klum* — if you are saying that the tree-owner has absolutely no ownership interest in the land, then *leima leh baal karka lebaal ilanot: akor ilnach shekol vezil* — Jenna could simply tell David: uproot your trees, take them, and leave. This is a *reductio ad absurdum*. The Rashbam and Tosafot agree that Rav Pappa is not necessarily claiming that Jenna could evict David from the moment the ruling is made; rather, he is saying that if David has no land rights whatsoever, then as soon as his trees died or were cut down, he would have no right to replant them — he would be left with nothing. This outcome strikes Rav Pappa as untenable: any meaningful ownership of trees must carry with it some ownership of the land that sustains them.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

*Ela amar Rav Pappa: zeh kanah ilanot v'chatzi karka, v'zeh kanah chatzi karka* — rather, says Rav Pappa, David has acquired the trees together with half the plot of land, while Jenna has acquired only the other half. Once we recognize that David's *chazakah* on the trees represents some claim to the land, and Jenna's *chazakah* encompasses the entire plot, the two competing claims on the land are treated as equal, yielding a half-and-half division.

The Rashbam, noting that this seems to swing sharply in the opposite direction, suggests that the figure of half is *lav davka* — not to be taken with strict literalism. David does not necessarily own exactly half of the full acreage; he owns the immediate area beneath and around the trees that is necessary for their survival. The word "half" may be an approximation or a way of saying that his claim on the land is substantial and cannot be reduced to zero, rather than a precise arithmetical division.

### One vs. Two Buyers

Having established the competing positions of Rav Zevid and Rav Pappa in the two-buyer scenario, the *Gemara* now introduces the classic single-seller framework to illuminate why two buyers operates differently. The relevant principles are *mocher b'ayin yafah hu mocher* — a seller sells with a generous eye, the position of Rabbi Akiva — and *mocher b'ayin ra'ah hu mocher* — a seller sells with a narrow, self-interested eye, the position of the *Chachamim*.

These principles come into play when the terms of a sale are ambiguous. If I sell Jenna my land but retain the trees, how much land do I keep for myself around those trees? If I sell David my trees but retain the land, does David receive any of the land that underlies or surrounds the trees? These questions are shaped by whether we presume I sold generously or held back as much as I could for myself.

What makes the two-buyer case categorically different is that when I have fully divested myself of the entire property — giving the trees to one person and the land to another — there is no retained self-interest to invoke. The question is no longer how much I held back for myself; it is how I divided what I gave away entirely. In that setting, the *ein yafah* and *ein ra'ah* framework, which presupposes a seller acting in his own interest versus another's, becomes conceptually inapplicable. The analysis shifts to a different set of interpretive assumptions about how an equal partition between two recipients should be understood, which is why the *Gemara* treats the two-buyer case as governed by distinct principles from the outset.

## Seller Keeps Trees

### Trees Retained Case

The *Gemara* now turns to the scenario of a single seller and a single buyer, beginning with the case of *machar karka v'shayar ilanot* — the seller sells the land but retains the trees for himself. The *Gemara* states that this is *pishita* — obvious: *yesh lo karka*, the seller retains possession of some land that goes along with those trees. The Rashbam clarifies that this does not mean he retains the entire plot; he retains the land immediately around and beneath the trees that is necessary for their continued existence. Even if he retained only one or two trees while selling the land, since he is the original owner who has explicitly held back the trees, we presume that a reasonable amount of adjacent land is included in that retention.

### Trees vs. Wells

What makes this case obvious, and why does it differ from the classic case of the well? The *Gemara* explains: *ואפילו l'Rabbi Akiva d'amar mocher b'ayin yafah hu mocher* — even according to Rabbi Akiva, who ordinarily presumes that a seller acts generously and does not hold back more than he explicitly specified. The paradigmatic case for Rabbi Akiva's principle involves a well. If I sell my entire plot to Jenna but retain ownership of the well, and then I wish to walk to my well, Jenna can say: I did not sell you a path, go work that out for yourself. According to Rabbi Akiva, by selling with a generous eye I am assumed to have given Jenna everything right up to the edge of the well, retaining no access easement for myself.

Why does this same reasoning not strip away the land around the retained trees? The *Gemara* answers: *hani mili gabei bor v'dos d'lo machshi b'ara* — this applies specifically to a well or cistern, because a well does not damage or impair the surrounding land. Jenna can plant right up to the edge of the well; the soil is fully usable. Therefore, to grant myself any space around the well would genuinely encroach on what Jenna is entitled to, and since I am selling generously I give her every usable inch.

*Aval ilanos d'kamachshi b'ara* — but trees are different, because the roots of a tree do damage and impair the adjacent land. The soil immediately surrounding a tree, where the roots spread, is not fully available for Jenna's cultivation in any case. There are therefore two reasons why it is assumed that the seller retained the land around his trees: first, that land is not genuinely useful to Jenna since the roots impair it; and second, to give Jenna title to that land would actually undermine the seller's ownership of the trees themselves, since Jenna could then invoke the laws of *nizkei shecheinim* and demand that the encroaching roots be removed. *Shiyurei shiyer* — therefore, even according to Rabbi Akiva, the seller is presumed to have retained the land immediately around the trees. *D'lav hachi leima leh: uki ilnach vezil* — for otherwise Jenna could tell him: raise up your tree and be gone.

## Seller Sells Trees

### Buyer Gets Land?

The *Gemara* now addresses the reverse case: *machar ilanot v'shayar karka lefanav* — I sell David the trees and retain the land for myself. Here the outcome is explicitly a *plugta d'Rabbi Akiva v'Rabbanan* — a direct dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*.

According to Rabbi Akiva, who rules *mocher b'ayin yafah hu mocher*, the sale of trees is read generously: the buyer receives not only the trees themselves but some portion of the land beneath or around them. This aligns with the principle that a sale is construed to include what is reasonably necessary for the meaningful use of what was sold. The Rashbam and Tosafot note that this interacts with the established rule about two versus three trees — three trees sold together normally carry a plot of land; two do not. Here the discussion may concern two trees, where Rabbi Akiva nevertheless grants at least the land directly beneath them.

According to the *Rabbanan*, who rule *mocher b'ayin ra'ah hu mocher*, the sale is read in maximally self-interested terms: the seller gives only what was explicitly named. David receives the trees and nothing more. The seller retains every inch of land, including the ground immediately beneath the trunks.

### Two Buyers Analysis

The *Gemara* then asks: how does Rav Zevid — who ruled that in the two-buyer case David has only trees and no land — coexist with Rabbi Akiva's principle that a seller of trees is presumed to give some land along with them? And conversely, how does Rav Pappa — who ruled that David gets trees plus half the land in the two-buyer case — coexist with the *Rabbanan*'s principle that a seller gives no land with trees?

The answer, as the *Gemara* spells out, is that the one-buyer and two-buyer scenarios operate under entirely different frameworks. *Hanei milei gabei shenei lekuchot* — the ruling of each Amora applies specifically in the two-buyer context, and for different reasons.

In Rav Zevid's case: when there are two buyers, Jenna can say to David: *ki heichi delididi leit li be'ilanot, ledidach nami leit lach be'ara* — just as I received no trees, you received no land. The division was explicit. The original owner gave one item to each party, and to read any land into David's portion is to take from what was given to Jenna. This is not a question of generosity; it is a question of how a clear bilateral division is to be interpreted, and the symmetry of the split controls.

In Rav Pappa's case: David can respond to Jenna by saying, *k'heichi delidach zavin b'ayin yafah, ledidi nami zavin b'ayin yafah* — just as you received something of lasting, permanent value (land, which endures indefinitely), so too should I be assumed to have received something of lasting value. The Rashbam explains that land, unlike movable property, carries an aura of permanence: what you receive in land, you hold forever, and your children after you. Since the seller gave Jenna something of this permanent character, it is reasonable to assume he also gave David something permanent — which means some land to accompany the trees, lest David's trees eventually die and leave him with nothing.

*Aval hacha mocher b'ayin ra'ah mocher* — but if it is simply me selling trees to David and retaining the land myself, then the *Rabbanan* say I retain the maximum for myself. There is no bilateral division to consider; there is only a seller acting in his own interest.

The critical insight is that the *ein yafah* and *ein ra'ah* framework presupposes a seller with retained self-interest. When a seller fully divests himself of a property by splitting it between two buyers, self-interest is no longer operative, and the question becomes instead how a complete division is to be construed equitably. Rav Zevid reads the explicit split as controlling, producing clean separation. Rav Pappa reads the spirit of a complete divestiture as requiring that each recipient receive something of enduring value, producing a partial land grant to the tree-buyer.

## Packed Trees Nursery

### Packed Trees Rule

The *Gemara* now shifts to a different problem. *Amri Naharda'ei: achlan retzupin ein lo chazakah* — the sages of Nahardea ruled that if one harvested trees that were packed in — *retzufin*, densely planted beyond the normal ratio — there is no *chazakah* on the land.

The term *retzufin* here does not mean that the harvests were continuous over consecutive years, though the word could carry that connotation. Rather, as the Rashbam explains, it refers to trees planted at a density exceeding ten per *beit se'ah* — more tightly packed than the recognized standard for a functioning orchard. Why should this density prevent *chazakah* on the land?

The Rashbam's explanation is that such a densely packed arrangement is not a permanent orchard setup. When trees are planted in very close proximity to one another, it reflects a nursery situation: saplings are grown tightly together as a temporary holding arrangement, after which they will be uprooted and replanted at proper spacing elsewhere. Because the trees are understood to be temporary occupants of the land rather than its long-term residents, the relationship between trees and land lacks the organic permanence that normally allows a *chazakah* on trees to imply ownership of the associated ground. A *chazakah* on the trees may still exist — the possessor's harvesting is meaningful with respect to the trees themselves — but it does not carry over to establish *chazakah* on the land beneath and around them, because that land is not understood to be the permanent home of those trees.

Rava challenges this ruling: *ela me'ata, hai mishra d'aspasta — be-mai kani lah?* If tightly packed, nursery-style planting prevents *chazakah* on the land, then what about a plot of alfalfa, which is also typically grown densely and replanted? How could one ever establish *chazakah* on such land? Rava therefore reformulates: *ela amar Rava, achlan retzupin ein lo karka* — harvesting packed trees does not establish *chazakah* on the *land*, but the *chazakah* on the trees themselves remains valid. The Rashbam himself notes that the analogy to alfalfa is imperfect: alfalfa naturally grows that way (*darko* to grow densely), whereas trees planted at excessive density are in an abnormal, temporary state. In any event, the bottom line is that the *chazakah* attaches to the trees but not to the land, because the trees' temporary relationship with the ground severs the inferential link between possessing trees and owning the soil.

### Dense Vineyard Debate

*Amar Rav Zeira ke-tannai* — Rav Zeira observes that this dispute is foreshadowed in a *tannaitic* disagreement. The case is *kerem shenitua pachos me'arba amos* — a vineyard in which the rows of vines are spaced less than four *amos* apart, which is the standard minimum spacing for a proper vineyard. *Rabbi Shimon omer: eino kerem* — Rabbi Shimon rules that such a densely planted arrangement does not qualify as a vineyard at all.

The practical stakes of this classification are significant. Tosafot notes two consequences: first, the laws of *kilayim* apply more stringently to a vineyard than to isolated vines; second, the exemption from military service for one who has planted a vineyard and not yet *chillel* it (*asher nata kerem v'lo chilalo*) would not apply if the arrangement does not qualify as a vineyard. Rabbi Shimon's view, like Nahardea's ruling on packed trees, treats a planting that will require uprooting and redistribution as not yet constituting the permanent arrangement it purports to be.

*V'Chachamim omrim: harei zeh kerem* — the *Chachamim* disagree and rule that even an overcrowded vineyard is considered a vineyard. Their reasoning: *ro'im es ha'emtza'im k'ilu einam* — we regard the middle vines, the ones that would need to be removed, as if they do not exist. On this view, because a portion of the vines will remain permanently in place, the vineyard retains its legal identity and its connection to the land. Correspondingly, the *chazakah* on those remaining vines would extend to the land as well.

Rav Zeira's point is that the debate between Nahardea's ruling and an alternative position maps precisely onto this *tannaitic* dispute: Rabbi Shimon's view supports Nahardea's ruling that a temporary, overcrowded arrangement cannot establish *chazakah* on land, while the *Chachamim*'s view — treating the permanent portion of the planting as determinative — would support recognizing *chazakah* on the land even in the dense-planting case.

## Palm Tree Sale

### Palm Tree Depths

*Amri Naharda'ei: hai man d'zavin dikla l'chaverei* — the sages of Nahardea further ruled that one who sells a palm tree to another person conveys to the buyer rights extending *mishipulei ad tehoma* — from the surface of the ground down to the very depths of the earth. The buyer acquires not merely the tree that stands above ground but all rights running vertically downward, all the way to the bottom.

### Ownership vs. Rights

This ruling immediately raises the question of how it relates to the broader debate about *ein yafah* and *ein ra'ah*. Does the buyer of a palm tree own the land beneath it, or not? If the *Chachamim* say a seller gives no land with trees, how can Nahardea say the buyer receives rights to the depths?

Two interpretations are offered. The first is that *mishipulei ad tehoma* reflects genuine ownership: the buyer of the palm tree owns the column of earth running directly beneath it, all the way down. On this reading, Nahardea sides with Rabbi Akiva's principle of *mocher b'ayin yafah*, and the ruling is a specific application of that generosity — the sale of a palm tree carries with it ownership of the subterranean earth beneath it.

The second interpretation holds that even if the buyer does not own the land in the full proprietary sense, he possesses rights that prevent the seller from tunneling underneath the tree — even a hundred *amos* underground. As long as the palm tree stands, the buyer's rights of use and enjoyment extend downward without limit, and the seller may not undermine those rights by excavating beneath the tree's root system. On this view, *mishipulei ad tehoma* describes the scope of the buyer's enforceable rights, not necessarily the extent of his title.
