# Hullin 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Spinal Cord

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁבְּרָה הַשִּׁדְרָה, תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חוּט הַשִּׁדְרָה שֶׁנִּפְסַק בְּרוּבּוֹ – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי; רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ נִיקַּב. הוֹרָה רַבִּי כְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: If **the spinal column was broken** and its cord was cut, the animal is a *tereifa*. With regard to this **the Sages taught:** If **the spinal cord was cut in its majority,** the animal is a *tereifa*. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Ya'akov says:** It is a *tereifa* **even** if the spinal cord was only **perforated.** The Gemara notes: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **ruled in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov** that even a small perforation of the spinal cord renders the animal a *tereifa*. **Rav Huna says:** The ***halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov.**

The daf under discussion is 45b, picking up six lines down in the wide lines at the phrase *nishbar hashidra* — "the spine is broken." We are working through the various *trefot*, having dealt with the heart the previous day. The *mishna* lists the spine and the spinal cord together, but the *gemara* makes clear that it is really the spinal cord that is the operative concern. If the spine itself is broken but the spinal cord remains intact, that is not a *treifa*; the spine is essentially irrelevant. The entire discussion therefore centers on the spinal cord.

### Moach vs. Oro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה רוּבּוֹ? רַב אָמַר: רוֹב עוֹרוֹ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: רוֹב מוֹחוֹ. מַאן דְּאָמַר רוֹב מוֹחוֹ – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן רוֹב עוֹרוֹ, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רוֹב עוֹרוֹ – רוֹב מוֹחוֹ מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר נִיוְלִי אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: רוּבּוֹ שֶׁאָמְרוּ – רוֹב עוֹרוֹ, מוֹחַ זֶה – לֹא מַעֲלֶה וְלֹא מוֹרִיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much is** considered **a majority of** the spinal cord to render the animal a *tereifa*? **Rav says: A majority of** the circumference of **its** surrounding membranes, the **meninges. And some say a majority of the nerve tissue of** the spinal cord itself. In other words, even if the meninges are intact, the animal is a *tereifa* if a majority of the nerve tissue is cut. The Gemara notes: According to **the one who says** that the animal is a *tereifa* if **a majority of the nerve tissue** is severed, **all the more so** if **a majority of the meninges** is cut, since if the meninges, the protective membranes, are damaged, the nerve tissue itself will certainly be damaged soon thereafter. But **according to the one who says** that a tear in **a majority of the meninges** renders it a *tereifa*, **what** is the *halakha* in a case where only **a majority of the nerve tissue** was cut? Perhaps the intact membranes will keep the damage from spreading. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof: **As Nayyoli says** that **Rav Huna says: The majority of** the spinal cord, **which they said** renders the animal a *tereifa* if cut, is **the majority of** the **meninges.** But **this nerve tissue makes no difference.**

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita*: *chut hashidra shenipsak derubo* — if the majority of the spinal cord is severed, it is a *treifa*. That is the view of Rabbi. Rabbi Yaakov holds more stringently: even a hole in the spinal cord suffices to render it a *treifa*. The *gemara* notes, strikingly, that when an actual case came before Rabbi himself, he ruled like Rabbi Yaakov — more strictly than his own recorded position in the *mishna*. This is a remarkable observation, since the principle of *maaseh rav* — that a scholar's practical ruling carries even greater weight than his theoretical position — would ordinarily reinforce Rabbi's strict ruling. Yet the *gemara* ultimately rejects that practical precedent in favor of Rabbi's stated *mishna* position.

Why Rabbi would rule against his own *mishna* in practice is genuinely difficult to explain. What is worth noting, however, is how frequently this *perek* presents not merely theoretical debates but actual narratives of *psak* — cases that came before sages, how they ruled, what they sent for further examination, how they proceeded differently in practice than in theory. It is no coincidence that this is the traditional tractate studied for *semicha*; the intersection of methodology and practice of *halachic* decision-making is on vivid display throughout.

Returning to the substantive question: the *baraita* speaks of the majority of the spinal cord being severed, but what exactly constitutes the *moach* and the *oro* of the spinal cord? The spinal cord is not a simple structure. It consists of multiple layers: an outer protective layer, a gap, a web-like inner layer, another gap, and finally the *dura mater*, which adheres directly to the nerve itself and is quite thin. The *gemara*'s term *moach* — literally marrow — refers to some portion of this inner content, perhaps the *dura mater* or the second protective layer, while *oro* refers to the outer skin of the cord.

### Majority Definition


With this anatomical picture in mind, the *gemara* records a debate. *rav amar rov oro* — Rav holds that a *treifa* requires the majority of the outer skin of the spinal cord to be severed. The alternative position, *amrei lah rov mocho*, holds that a *treifa* requires the majority of the inner content to be severed — meaning one must cut deeply enough to reach and sever the majority of the *moach*, not merely the outer layer. If the *moach* is the *dura mater*, then severing its majority is tantamount to severing the nerve itself. If it is the second protective layer, then the debate is about how deep one must cut before the animal becomes a *treifa*, with the outer-skin position holding that once the outer protection is compromised, the cord is ultimately doomed.

### Rava's Logic


Rava draws an important logical inference from this dispute. According to the opinion that requires *rov mocho* — severing the majority of the inner content — it follows *kal v'chomer* that severing the majority of the outer skin would certainly also render it a *treifa*: if the inner content matters even though the outer skin still protects it, surely the outer skin itself matters. This may seem counterintuitive — one would expect that cutting deeper, closer to the nerve, would be the more serious concern — but the *gemara* reasons that an intact outer skin provides meaningful protection and enhances the animal's survival prospects. Thus severing the outer skin, which eliminates that protection, is at least as serious as severing the inner content with the outer still present.

The converse question then arises: according to the opinion that only requires *rov oro* — severing the majority of the outer skin — what is the status if only the *moach* is severed? The *gemara* brings a ruling: *amar Rav Huna*, the majority that the sages spoke of refers to the majority of the *oro*, the outer skin. *moach zeh lo maaleh velo morid* — the inner *moach* makes no difference either way.

### Rav Huna Ruling


This ruling of Rav Huna is itself somewhat surprising. One might have expected the inner content — which is directly protective of the nerve — to matter most, analogous to how marrow is the living material within a bone. The *gemara* may be reasoning along the lines of how bone structure works: the bone itself gives structural integrity and the marrow, while functionally important in its own right, does not bear on the integrity of the bone as bone. So too, perhaps, the *gemara* understood the outer skin of the spinal cord as providing the essential structural function, while the inner material, however physiologically significant, does not determine whether the cord will hold together or collapse. The phrasing *lo maaleh velo morid* — it neither adds nor detracts — does seem extreme if one considers what the inner layers actually do, but this is where the *halacha* lands. The broader point, frequently encountered in this *perek*, is that the determinations of *chazal* regarding *trefot* are not negotiated on scientific grounds by later generations; they are received and applied as authoritative.

### Dissolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: נִתְמָרֵךְ – פָּסוּל, נִתְמַסְמֵס – פָּסוּל. אֵיזוֹהִי הַמְרָכָה וְאֵיזוֹהִי הֲמַסְמָסָה? הַמְרָכָה – כֹּל שֶׁנִּשְׁפָּךְ כְּקִיתוֹן, (מסמסה) [הֲמַסְמָסָה] – כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** If the spinal cord **was liquefied [*nitmareikh*],** the animal is **unfit** for consumption. Even if it **softened [*nitmasmeis*],** the animal is **unfit,** i.e., a *tereifa*. **What is liquefaction, and what is softening? Liquefaction** is **any** case in **which** the nerve tissue becomes liquid, and if the membrane is punctured it can be **poured** out **like** water from **a jug. Softening** is **any** case in **which** the nerve tissue **cannot stand** upright on its own and sags when it is not being supported.

A new question arises: what if the *moach* is not severed but rather dissolved? Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *nitmare'ach pasul, nitmasmes pasul* — if the inner content is dissolved in either of two distinct ways, the animal is invalid. *nitmare'ach* refers to a more complete liquefaction — according to Rashi, it becomes like water. *nitmasmes* refers to something less complete, perhaps best described as having a jelly-like consistency: the material has lost its firmness but has not fully liquefied.

### Types of Dissolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי כׇּבְדוֹ, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ. בֵּי רַב אָמְרִי: נִתְמַסְמֵס – פָּסוּל, נִתְמַזְמֵז – כָּשֵׁר. מֵיתִיבִי: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁנִּתְמַזְמֵז מוֹחָה – טְרֵפָה! הָהִיא נִתְמַסְמֵס אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** In a case where the spinal cord became unusually heavy such that it **cannot stand** upright **due to its weight,** but not due to softening or melting, **what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara responds: The question **shall stand** unresolved. **In the study hall they say:** If the spinal cord **softened** due to disease, the animal is **unfit** for consumption. But if some of its tissue softened and **was emptied** from the spinal cord, the animal remains **kosher.** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: An animal whose nerve tissue was emptied [*nitmazmez*]** from the spinal cord **is a *tereifa*.** The Gemara responds: **That** version of the *baraita* is incorrect. In fact, the word **softened [*nitmasmes*] was stated,** not the word emptied.

The *gemara* sharpens these definitions. *hamramtses* — the more liquefied form — means that it would spill out like a pitcher if cut open; the insides are fully liquid. *hamemasmes* means it cannot stand upright: the cord loses its rigidity; held in the hand, it would simply flop over. Both of these render the animal *pasul*. A further question is posed by Rav Yirmiya: what if the cord cannot stand not because its inner content has jellied but because it is somehow top-heavy and therefore unsteady despite retaining some degree of rigidity? The *gemara* leaves this in *teiku* — unresolved.

The house of Rav adds a further distinction: *nitmasmesa pasul*, but *nitmazmeza kasher* — a third, lesser category, described as *nitmazmeza*, is still valid. Rashi understands this to mean that some of the inner content has been displaced or lost, but the material has neither been severed, fully liquefied, nor fully jellied. That lesser disruption does not render the cord invalid.

A *baraita* is then cited that appears to challenge this, as Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar states that an animal whose *moach* has been *nitmazmeiz* is *treifa*. The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by emending the reading: the correct *girsa* in that *baraita* is *nitmasmes*, not *nitmazmeiz*, and thus there is no contradiction.

### Levi Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי, וְהָא לֵוִי הֲוָה יָתֵיב בֵּי מַסּוּתָא, חַזְיֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּטַרְיֵיהּ לְרֵישֵׁיהּ, אֲמַר: נִתְמַזְמֵז מוֹחֵיהּ דְּדֵין, לָאו דְּלָא חָיֵי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא, לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹלִיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so,** that an animal whose nerve tissue has dissolved is kosher? **But didn't** it happen that **Levi was sitting in the bathhouse,** where **he saw a certain man who banged his head** severely, whereupon **he said: This** man's **nerve tissue has** softened and **been emptied?** Is it **not** that Levi meant **that** the man **cannot live?** If so, the softening and emptying of the nerve tissue should render an animal a *tereifa*. **Abaye said: No,** he intended **to say that** the man **cannot reproduce,** since head trauma might lead to infertility.

A further challenge to the leniency of *nitmazmeza* is raised from a narrative involving Levi. Levi was sitting in a bathhouse when he witnessed a man strike his head violently. Levi declared: *nitmazmeiz mocha dedein* — this man's brain has been *nitmazmeiz*. The implication of the story, as the *gemara* reads it, is that this is a serious, life-threatening condition. If *nitmazmeiz* of the brain portends death, why should *nitmazmeiz* of the spinal cord's inner content be treated as harmless?

### Abbaye's Position


Abbaye resolves this by explaining that Levi's remark does not mean the man will die but rather that he will no longer be able to father children. Rashi, citing a physiological belief of the *gemara*, explains that the spinal cord branches downward through the thigh area and one branch enters the reproductive organ, affecting virility. A disruption of the brain or spinal cord *moach* — even without being fatal — could therefore impair fertility. Thus Levi's exclamation in the bathhouse concerned the man's reproductive capacity, not his life expectancy, and this does not contradict the ruling that *nitmazmeza* does not render the animal a *treifa*.

It is worth pausing on the broader theoretical difficulty lurking behind this entire discussion. The category of *treifa* is traditionally understood in terms of *treifa eina chayah* — a *treifa* cannot live. Yet an animal with a severed spinal cord would in many cases be paralyzed rather than dead; a domesticated animal that is fed by its owner might survive for a considerable time. This difficulty is never explicitly resolved in the *gemara*. Similarly left unaddressed is the noted parallel with yesterday's discussion, where the *gemara* described branches of the trachea feeding into the heart and liver — reflecting an ancient physiological model in which the heart functions as an organ of respiration rather than as a circulatory pump. That understanding is directly relevant to how certain *rishonim* and later authorities cited *gemara* passages in discussions of the definition of death and the significance of circulation — though all such analysis proceeds from the *gemara*'s own framework.

## Extent of Spinal Cord

### Bein HaParshiyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד הֵיכָן חוּט הַשִּׁדְרָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עַד בֵּין הַפָּרָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Until where** does the **spinal cord** extend downward, such that if it is cut above that point the animal is a *tereifa*? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Until** the point **between the branches** of the spinal cord that split off behind the thighs.

Having established the law of the spinal cord, the *gemara* asks: *ad heichan* — until where along the spine does the rule apply? This is particularly pressing given the suggestion that the spinal cord branches off and connects to other parts of the body at lower points. After a certain level, the cord presumably diminishes in criticality. Where is that lower boundary?

### Shmuel's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב דִּימִי בַּר יִצְחָק הֲוָה קָא בָּעֵי לְמֵיזַל לְבֵי חוֹזָאֵי, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לַיחְוֵי לִי מָר בֵּין הַפָּרָשׁוֹת הֵיכָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי לִי גְּדִי וְאַחְוִי לָךְ. אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ גְּדִי שָׁמֵן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּלִיעָן טְפֵי וְלָא יְדִיעַ. אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ כָּחוּשׁ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּלִיטָן טְפֵי וְלָא יְדִיעַ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תָּא אַגְמְרָךְ גְּמָרָא, הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עַד אַחַת – טְרֵפָה, שְׁלִישִׁית – כְּשֵׁרָה, שְׁנִיָּה – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts: **Rav Dimi bar Yitzḥak wanted to go to Bei Ḥozai. He came before Rabbi Yehuda** and **said to him: Master, show me, where** is the area **between the branches** to which Shmuel referred? Rabbi Yehuda **said to him: Go bring me a kid and I will show you.** Rav Dimi bar Yitzḥak **brought him** a choice **kid** with much **fat.** Rabbi Yehuda **said to him:** The location **is buried very** deep in the fat **and** is **not recognizable. He brought** Rabbi Yehuda **a lean** kid. Rabbi Yehuda **said to him:** The bones **jut out very** far, **and** the location is **not recognizable.** Rabbi Yehuda **said to him: Come, I will teach you a tradition** without showing you: **This** is what **Shmuel said:** There are three successive points around the same bone behind the pelvis at which branches diverge from the spinal cord. If the spinal cord is cut anywhere **until the first** gap between these branches, the animal is **a *tereifa*.** If it is severed anywhere after **the third** gap, i.e., after the third branch, the animal is **kosher.** If it was severed within the **second** gap, i.e., between these areas, **I do not know** the *halakha*.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *ad bein haparshiyot* — until the *bein haparshiyot*. Rashi offers three possible explanations of this term, preferring the following: after the main ribcage ends, smaller ribs continue branching off from the vertebrae down the lower spine. The *bein haparshiyot* refers to the area of these smaller ribs — specifically the spaces between the points where these mini-ribs branch off from the spine. Shmuel's tradition then adds detail: until the first such branching point it is still unambiguously *treifa*; from the third branching point onward it is unambiguously *kesheira*; and the second branching point is uncertain.

### Rav Dimi Story


The *gemara* relates a telling story. Rav Dimi bar Yitzchak wished to travel to Be Khuzai, a district in Babylonia. He came before Rav Yehuda — presumably to take his leave. Before letting him go, Rav Yehuda asked: *lechevi li mar bein haparshiyot heicha* — show me where the *bein haparshiyot* is located. The subtext, as the story reveals, may well have been a test of whether Rav Dimi bar Yitzchak was truly ready to leave his teacher's presence.

Rav Dimi replied: bring me a goat and I will show you. A fat goat was brought. Rav Yehuda said: *bliya tefei* — it is too fatty; the relevant area is absorbed and concealed and cannot be clearly identified. A skinny goat was then brought. Rav Yehuda said: *belit tefei* — it is too protruding; the hip bone obscures the location. (Rashi alternatively suggests the animal was still alive in this second case, which would make the difficulty more understandable.) Unable to demonstrate the location satisfactorily either way, Rav Dimi bar Yitzchak did not pass the test. Rav Yehuda then said: come, let me teach you the tradition — *hachi amar Shmuel*: until the first one it is *treifa*, at the third it is *kesheira*, and at the second I do not know.

### Inclusive Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: עַד וְעַד בַּכְּלָל, אוֹ דִּלְמָא עַד וְלֹא עַד בַּכְּלָל? בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר, עַד וְלֹא עַד בַּכְּלָל, פִּי פָרָשָׁה מַהוּ? בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר, עַד וְעַד בַּכְּלָל, פָּרָשָׁה עַצְמָהּ מַהוּ? תָּא שְׁמַע: הַפָּרָשָׁה תִּידּוֹן כְּבָשָׂר. מַאי לָאו פָּרָשָׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה? לָא, שְׁלִישִׁית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, raises a dilemma:** When Shmuel says that the animal is certainly a *tereifa* if the spinal cord is cut anywhere **until** the first gap, does he mean **until and including** the first gap, in which case if it is cut within the first gap the animal is a *tereifa*? **Or perhaps** he means **until and not including** the length of the gap itself? **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma: If you say** that Shmuel means **until and not including** the length of the first gap, and if the spinal cord is cut in the first gap the *halakha* is unknown, then if the spinal cord was cut in **the mouth of** the first **branch,** exactly where it branches off, **what is** the *halakha*? Is it considered to be within the first gap, in which case the *halakha* is unknown? Or is it considered before the gap, in which case a tear in the mouth renders the animal a *tereifa*? **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma: If you say** that Shmuel means **until and including the measure** of the first gap, and if the spinal cord is cut in the first gap the animal is a *tereifa*, then if the first **branch itself,** i.e., the first pair of branching nerves, was cut from the spinal cord, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **The branch** of the spine that was cut **shall be considered as** normal **flesh,** not as the spine. Therefore, it does not render the animal a *tereifa*, unlike the spinal cord itself. **What, is it not** referring even to the **first or second branch?** The Gemara responds: **No,** the *baraita* is referring only to the **third** branch. But if either of the first two pairs of nerves that branch off from the spinal cord are cut, the animal is a *tereifa*.

The *gemara* then asks a series of increasingly precise questions. Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua asks: when Shmuel says "until the first," does he mean *ad ve'ad bichlal* — up to and including the first branching point — or *ad velo ad bichlal* — up to but not including it? Although the practical stakes might seem modest given that the entire zone up through the second point is at minimum a *safek treifa*, the question is still posed.

Rav Pappa then asks: *im timtzi lomar ad velo ad bichlal* — if you conclude that the first point itself is not included in the *vadai treifa* zone, what is the status of *pi parasha* — the very opening of that space? We are now debating a matter of perhaps a centimeter or less.

Rabbi Yirmiya's question goes in the other direction: *im timtzi lomar ad ve'ad bichlal* — if you conclude that the first point is included in the *vadai treifa* zone — what is the status of the *parasha atzma*, the separation itself? On the first reading of Rashi, this refers to the actual small rib bones that branch off. But on a more satisfying reading, it refers to the *space* between the first and second branching points. If the first point is *vadai treifa* and the second is *safek*, is the intervening space treated as *vadai* or as *safek*?

### Bones Insignificant


The *gemara* addresses whether the bones of the mini-ribs themselves matter. A ruling is cited: *haparasha kidon k'basar* — the separation itself is treated like flesh, meaning the bones have no intrinsic significance; only the spinal cord matters. The *gemara* initially reads this as applying to the first and second separations, but then concludes it refers to the third — at the third point the bones, if they break, are irrelevant. The unresolved questions about the first and second points, including what happens in the intermediate spaces, remain in *teiku*. As throughout this section, the question of why the spinal cord constitutes a *treifa* at all — given that its severance causes paralysis rather than death — is noted but left unanswered.

## Spinal Cord in Birds

### Rabbi Yannai vs. Reish L…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹפָא, רַבִּי יַנַּאי אוֹמֵר: לְמַטָּה מִן הָאֲגַפַּיִם, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: עַד בֵּין אֲגַפַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to a bird,** the Sages disagreed as to the lower boundary of the spinal cord, above which if it is cut, the bird will be rendered a *tereifa*. **Rabbi Yannai says:** The spinal cord extends until **below the** level of the **wings. And Reish Lakish says: Until** the point **between the wings,** not below them.

The *gemara* turns to birds: until where along the spine does the *treifa* of the spinal cord apply in a bird? Rabbi Yannai says *l'mata min ha'agapayim* — below the wings, meaning the concern extends throughout the region where the wings are attached, all the way to where the wings end. Reish Lakish says *ad bein ha'agapayim* — until between the wings, meaning only until where the wings begin to be attached; from that point downward, it is no longer a concern. This is a substantial disagreement. Tosafot raises the further question of whether "below the wings" means the bottom of where the wings attach to the body or the bottom of where the wings lie against the body — which would extend the zone considerably lower.

### Ulla & Rav Pazzi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּבֶן פַּזִּי, וְאַיְיתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ עוֹפָא, וּבְדַק עַד בֵּין אֲגַפַּיִם, וּשְׁלַח לֵיהּ דְּבֵי נְשִׂיאָה וְקָם וַאֲזַל, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא אִי מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא צְרִיךְ לְמִבְדַּק טְפֵי, אִי מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד נְשִׂיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says: I was standing before** Rabbi Shimon **ben Pazi, and they brought before him a bird** whose spine had been injured, **and he checked until** the point **between the wings. And** at that point a messenger **of the house of the *Nasi* sent for him, and he got up and went, and I do not know if** he left **because one does not need to check beyond** that point, **or** if **due to the honor of the *Nasi*** he left without completing the inspection.

Ulla relates: I was present before Rav Pazzi when a bird with a question about possible *treifa* was brought before him. He examined the bird for a severing, checking from the neck downward until the space where the wings begin. At that point, a messenger arrived from the house of the *nasi* and Rav Pazzi left to respond, having never rendered an explicit ruling on the bird. Ulla reflects: I do not know whether Rav Pazzi stopped because he considered his examination complete — meaning one need not check beyond where the wings begin, and the bird was effectively declared kosher by his departure — or whether he stopped only because of the obligation to respond immediately to the *nasi*, intending to return and finish the examination.

This story has an additional point of interest. If Rav Pazzi was actively checking the bird, it implies there was some reason to suspect a severance — perhaps the bird fell, or was found in a compromised condition. This is relevant to the broader question of which defects require routine examination and which do not. The question of what triggered the examination here is left open, though it seems plausible that some incident had given reason for concern.

## Liver

### Liver Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיטְּלָה הַכָּבֵד וְכוּ׳. הָא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנָּה כְּלוּם – כְּשֵׁרָה, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא הָוֵי כְּזַיִת, וְהָתְנַן: נִיטַּל הַכָּבֵד וְנִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנָּה כְּזַיִת – כְּשֵׁרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: If **the liver was removed** and nothing remained of it, the animal is a *tereifa*. The Gemara asks: It follows, **therefore,** that if **anything remained of it,** the animal is **kosher, even if** the remaining piece **does not constitute an olive-bulk. But didn't we learn** in a mishna on 54a: If **the liver was removed and an olive-bulk of it remained,** it is **kosher?** One can infer that if less remained, it is a *tereifa*.

The *gemara* now addresses the liver. The *mishna* states that if the liver is removed and nothing remains, the animal is *treifa* — the implication being that if even a small amount (*mashehu*) remains, it is kosher. A later *mishna*, however, states explicitly: *nital hakaved venishtayer mimenu kezayit kesheira* — if a *kezayit* of liver remains, it is kosher. These two *mishnayot* contradict each other: does one need a *kezayit* or does any minimal amount suffice?

### Two Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא, הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי, כִּי הָא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא זָרֵיק לַהּ, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי מְטַבֵּיל לַהּ, וְסִימָנָיךְ: ״עֲשִׁירִין מְקַמְּצִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This is **not difficult. This** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya,** while **that** mishna later in the chapter is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. This is **like that** incident in which an amount smaller than an olive-bulk remained of the liver, and **Rabbi Ḥiyya discarded it,** as he holds that the animal is a *tereifa*, **but Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **dipped it** in a seasoning and ate it. **And your mnemonic** to remember which Sage maintained which opinion is: **The rich are stingy.** Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, was wealthy, but he nevertheless did not allow the meat to go to waste.

Rav Yosef resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya* — it is not difficult; the two *mishnayot* reflect different opinions. One corresponds to Rabbi Chiyya and one to Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi — that is, to the son of Rabbi, who was Rabbi's own child, while Rabbi Chiyya was Rabbi's nephew.

The *gemara* illuminates the dispute through a telling detail about their eating habits. Rabbi Chiyya would simply discard the liver; he did not eat it. Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi would dip the liver and eat it — treating it as something of an appetizer. A mnemonic for remembering who held which view: *ashirim mekamtzin* — wealthy people are frugal. Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi, being from the *nasi*'s household and correspondingly wealthy, did not want to waste anything, including the liver. Rabbi Chiyya, not wealthy, had no interest in the liver.

What does eating practice have to do with *halachic* status? Rashi explains that vital organs — those recognized as central to the life force of the animal — were eaten for their perceived health properties. Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi, who ate the liver, evidently regarded it as a genuinely vital organ and therefore demanded a *kezayit* for it to continue its essential function. Rabbi Chiyya, having no interest in the liver, clearly did not view it as a vital life force, and therefore held that any minimal amount (*mashehu*) suffices — or, as Rashi goes further to explain, the minimal amount is needed not even to perform the liver's own function but merely to cover the point where the liver connects to the gallbladder, preventing an exposed hole there. On this reading, Rabbi Chiyya essentially denies that the liver itself plays a critical life-sustaining role.

The *gemara* had already indicated in a previous passage, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, that the *halacha* requires a *kezayit*, and the present discussion confirms that we rule in accordance with the second *mishna* — corresponding to Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi.

### Troop Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא פּוּלְמוּסָא דַּאֲתָא לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, עֲרַקוּ רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף, פְּגַע בְּהוּ רַבִּי זֵירָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: עָרוֹקָאֵי, כְּזַיִת שֶׁאָמְרוּ בִּמְקוֹם מָרָה. רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר: בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהִיא חַיָּה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הִלְכָּךְ, בָּעֵינַן כְּזַיִת בִּמְקוֹם מָרָה, וּבָעֵינַן כְּזַיִת בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהִיא חַיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **a certain** royal **army came to Pumbedita,** and **Rabba and Rav Yosef fled** the city, whereupon **Rabbi Zeira met them.** Rabbi Zeira **said to them: Refugees,** hear this *halakha*: The **olive-bulk that** the Sages **said** must remain of the liver so the animal will remain kosher must be **in the place** where the liver connects to the **gallbladder. Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** The olive-bulk must be **in the place that** the liver **lives,** i.e., is connected to the other organs, under the right kidney. **Rav Pappa said: Therefore,** in order to satisfy both opinions, **we require an olive-bulk in the place of the gallbladder, and we** also **require an olive-bulk in the place that it lives.** Otherwise, the animal is a *tereifa*.

A *gemara* narrative sets the context. A certain army troop (*pulmasa*) came to Pumbedisa. Concerned about conscription or other dangers, Rabba and Rav Yosef fled. Rabbi Zeira, who had connections with the authorities and could move about freely, encountered them and called them *arukai* — deserters — a somewhat pointed remark given his own liberty of movement. It is in this context that Rabbi Zeira then states the ruling: *kezayit she'amru bimkom mara* — the *kezayit* that the sages required must be located specifically where the gallbladder is.

Another authority, Afah bar Ahva, says *bimkom shehi chayah* — where the animal lives. This somewhat unusual phrase is explained by Rashi as referring either to the lobe of the liver — the distinctive flap-like section visible in a picture of a liver — or to the location where the liver is embedded between or beneath the kidneys.

### Rav Pappa Ruling


Rav Pappa rules: *hilchach ba'inan kezayit bimkom mara uba'inan kezayit bimkom shehi chayah* — since we cannot decide between the two opinions, we require a *kezayit* in both locations: both where the gallbladder is and where the liver is embedded. This is a practical *chumra* born of unresolved dispute.

### Kezayit Form

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מִתְלַקֵּט, מַהוּ? כִּרְצוּעָה, מַהוּ? בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: כְּזַיִת מְרוּדָּד, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If the olive-bulk that remained was not all in one piece, but rather small pieces that could be **gathered** together to form the requisite measure, **what is** the *halakha*? Alternatively, if it was long and thin **like a strip, what is** the *halakha*? **Rav Ashi raises a dilemma:** If the **olive-bulk** was **flat,** broad but thinner than an olive, **what is** the *halakha*? To all these questions the Gemara responds: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rabbi Yirmiya raises a series of questions about the form the *kezayit* must take. *milaket mahu* — what if the required amount is not concentrated in one place but consists of scattered bits here and there that together combine to a *kezayit*? *keritua mahu* — what if it is stretched into a long thin strip, connected but lacking any concentrated mass? Rav Ashi adds: *kezayit merudad mahu* — what if it is present in the required volume but spread as an extremely thin, flat film? Technically there is a *kezayit* of material, but there is no thickness to it; can it perform any function? The *gemara* leaves all these in *teiku*.

A final query comes from Rav Zerika, who asks Rav Ami: *niddaldela kaved ume'ura betarpesheih mahu* — what if the liver has been loosened and separated from its proper position but is still being held in place by its surrounding membrane? Rav Ami replies that he has no idea what this question is about — meaning it is not a recognized halachic category at all. As long as the liver is still there in its location — whether measured from the gallbladder or from where it lives — it counts. The fact that it has become loosened is of no significance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי זְרִיקָא מֵרַבִּי אַמֵּי: נִדַּלְדְּלָה כָּבֵד וּמְעוֹרָה בְּטַרְפְּשֶׁיהָ, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּלְדּוּל זֶה אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מַהוּ, אִי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּמְקוֹם מָרָה – הָא אִיכָּא, אִי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהִיא חַיָּה – הָא אִיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zerika asked Rabbi Ami:** If the **liver was detached** from the other organs in numerous places **and** was **attached** only **to its membranes [*betarpasheha*],** but the liver itself remained intact, **what** is the *halakha*? Rabbi Ami **said to him:** With regard to the *halakha*, **I do not know what** the significance is of **this detachment** that you mentioned. **If** you rule **according to the one who says** that an olive-bulk must remain **in the place of the gallbladder, that is** the case here. And **if** you rule **according to the one who says** that it must be **in the place that it lives, that is** the case here as well. Consequently, there is no doubt that the animal is kosher.

## Conclusion

### Adhesions Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרֵיאָה שֶׁנִּיקְּבָה, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל וְרַב אַסִּי דְאָמְרִי: קְרָמָא עִילָּאָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: קְרָמָא תַּתָּאָה. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: וְסִימָנָיךְ כִּיתּוּנָא דְּוַרְדָּא דְּמַנְּחָא בֵּיהּ רֵיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: **The lung that was perforated** renders the animal a *tereifa*. The Gemara quotes **Rav and Shmuel and Rav Asi, who** all **say:** The mishna is referring specifically to the **outer membrane. And some say** that they said that the mishna is referring specifically to the **inner membrane. Rav Yosef bar Minyumi said** that **Rav Naḥman said: And your mnemonic** should be: The **red robe in which the lung rests.** The inner membrane is red, while the outer membrane is white, and according to Rav Naḥman, the mishna is referring to the inner membrane.

Having completed the discussion of the liver, the shiur turns to the lungs — the most practically significant organ in the laws of *treifot*, because the lungs are the site of the most frequently encountered problems, centered above all on *sirchos*, adhesions. The term *glatt* — now familiar in common usage — literally means smooth, referring to lungs free of adhesions. Adhesions are fibrous connections that link the lung either to the inner wall of the ribcage or to adjacent lobes of the lung itself.

### Why Adhesions Matter


Why are adhesions a problem? The dominant explanation, adopted by Rashi and the subject of a major *tosafot* that will be encountered when the daf is turned, is that an adhesion is a sign that there was previously a hole in the lung and the adhesion has formed over it, concealing it. The concern is thus that a hole exists beneath the adhesion. Tosafot also entertains the possibility that the adhesion might not reflect a pre-existing hole but could itself cause a hole to develop — but this alternative is bracketed, and the working assumption throughout is that an adhesion signals a covered-up perforation.

In practice, *glatt* does not mean that the animal has absolutely no adhesions — virtually no animal's lungs are entirely free of them — but rather that it falls within a more stringent threshold of permissible adhesions, accepting only very thin adhesions that peel away easily. Non-*Glatt* practice tolerates a broader range of adhesions, relying on testing methods — including the water test and the blowing test — to verify whether a hole exists beneath an adhesion. Those tests and the full details of the adhesion laws will be taken up in the next session.

## Lungs - Holes

### Lung Membranes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא: אִי אִינְּקִיב עִילָּאָה וְלָא אִינְּקִיב תַּתָּאָה – תַּתָּאָה מֵגֵין, כִּדְרַבָּה, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: הַאי רֵיאָה דְּאִגְּלִיד כַּאֲהִינָא סוּמָּקָא – כְּשֵׁרָה. אִינְּקִיב תַּתָּאָה וְלָא אִינְּקִיב עִילָּאָה – מַגֵּין אוֹ לָא מַגֵּין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It is **obvious** that **if the outer** membrane **is perforated but the inner** membrane **is not perforated, the inner** membrane **protects** the lung, and the animal is kosher. This is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabba, as Rabba says: This** animal with **a lung whose** outer membrane **was removed,** so that it looks **like a red date,** is **kosher;** all the more so is that the case if it was only perforated. But if **the inner** membrane **was perforated but the outer** membrane **was not perforated,** does the outer membrane **protect** the lung? **Or** does it **not protect** it, in which case the animal is a *tereifa*?

The *gemara* opens the discussion of lung *trefot* with a fundamental question: *ha-reiah she-nikvah* — a lung that has been pierced. Three *amora'im* — Rav, Shmuel, and Rav Assi — weigh in, some saying the *treifa* requires that the *kruma ila'ah*, the outer membrane, be pierced, while others say the *kruma tata'ah*, the inner membrane. The structure of the lung involves multiple layers: an outer wall, a gap between the outer and inner walls, and an inner wall that is closer to the hollow of the lung itself. The debate concerns which layer must be pierced before the animal becomes a *treifa*.

Unlike the membrane surrounding the brain, the lung's membranes were not explicitly discussed in the *mishna*. Yet the *gemara* here does not require the piercing to reach all the way to the hollow interior of the lung — even a hole in one of the outer membranes can suffice, depending on which opinion is followed.

### Red Cloak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָמַר: לָא מַגֵּין, וְחַד אָמַר: מַגֵּין. וְהִלְכְתָא מַגֵּין, כִּדְרַב יוֹסֵף, דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַאי רֵיאָה דְּאָוְושָׁא, אִי יָדְעִינַן הֵיכָא אָוְושָׁא – מוֹתְבִינַן עֲלַהּ גַּדְפָּא אוֹ רוּקָּא אוֹ גִילָא, אִי מְבַצְבְּצָא – טְרֵפָה, וְאִי לָא – כְּשֵׁרָה. וְאִי לָא יָדְעִינַן הֵיכָא אָוְושָׁא – מַיְיתִינַן מְשִׁיכְלְתָא דְּמַיָּא פָּשׁוֹרֵי וּמוֹתְבִינַן לַהּ בְּגַוַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree with regard to** this question. **One says:** It **does not protect** the lung; **and one says:** It **protects** the lung. **And the *halakha* is** that it **protects** the lung, **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Yosef. As Rav Yosef says:** With regard to **this lung that emits a sound** when inflated, **if we know** from **where it emits a sound, we set a feather, or saliva, or straw on** that point. **If** the saliva **bubbles** when the lung is inflated, the animal is **a *tereifa*,** since this proves that the lung is perforated through both membranes. **And if not,** the animal is **kosher. And if we do not know** from **where it emits a sound, we bring a basin of tepid water and set** the lung **inside it.**

Rav Yosef bar Miyumi said in the name of Rav Nachman, with a mnemonic to aid recall: *ke-chituna de-varda de-mancha bei reiya* — like a red cloak that lies within the lung. This is a description of the inner membrane surrounding the lung, which has a distinctly reddish appearance. The ruling embedded in the mnemonic is that it is the inner membrane — this red cloak — that is the decisive layer. A hole that penetrates only the outer layer is not sufficient to render the animal *treifa*; the inner, red membrane must be pierced. This parallels the *gemara*'s earlier discussion of the membrane around the brain, where similarly the inner membrane was the determining layer.

### Peeled Lung


This ruling is confirmed and amplified by Rava: *hai reiya de-iglid de-atya sumka* — if a lung has been peeled of its outer membrane and the result looks like a red date, it is nonetheless *kesheira*. The red appearance is precisely that inner membrane which remains intact and continues to protect the lung. The removal of the entire outer membrane, while certainly a significant alteration to the lung's surface, does not compromise the inner membrane and therefore does not constitute a *treifa*.

### Inner vs. Outer


This raises a converse question. We have established that if the outer membrane is pierced but the inner is not, the inner protects — that is the clear implication of the ruling about the red cloak. But what if it is the inner membrane that is pierced while the outer remains intact? Does the outer protect even when the inner has a hole?

Rav Acha and Ravina debate this. One holds that the outer membrane does not protect if the inner has been breached; the other holds that it does. The *halacha* is that the outer membrane does protect: *hilchata magin*. Thus both membranes must be pierced for the animal to be *treifa* — a hole only in the inner membrane, with the outer intact, is not sufficient.

### Avsha Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּחַמִּימֵי לָא – דְּכָוְוצִי, בְּקָרִירֵי לָא – דִּמְטָרְשִׁי, אֶלָּא מוֹתְבִינַן בְּפָשׁוֹרֵי וְנָפְחִינַן לַהּ. אִי מְבַצְבְּצָא – טְרֵפָה, וְאִי לָא – כְּשֵׁרָה. תַּתָּאָה אִינְּקִיב, עִילָּאָה לָא אִינְּקִיב, וְהַאי דְּאָוְושָׁא – זִיקָא דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** One **cannot** place it **in hot water, as it causes** the lung **to contract,** closing the perforation. And one **cannot** place it **in cold water, as it hardens** the lung and may cause it to crack. **Rather, we set it in tepid water and inflate it. If** the water **bubbles,** the animal is **a *tereifa*. And if not,** the animal is **kosher,** since it is apparent that only **the inner** membrane **is perforated** and **the outer** membrane **is not perforated, and the** fact **that it emits a sound is** due to **the air** moving in the space **between** the two membranes. Evidently, Rav Yosef holds that if only the inner membrane is perforated, the animal is kosher.

Rav Yosef states: *hai reiya de-avsha* — if a lung makes a whistling sound. This refers to a testing procedure: after removing the lung from the animal, one blows into it as one would inflate a balloon, and listens for escaping air. If a whistling sound is heard, a hole is present somewhere. The question is then how to locate it.

If one knows where the sound is coming from, the procedure is: place a feather (*gadfa*), spittle (*roka*), or a thin straw (*gila*) over that spot and blow again. If the feather flutters or the spittle bubbles, air is escaping through that point to the outside, and the lung is *treifa*. If there is no movement, the lung is *kesheira* despite the sound.

If one does not know where the sound originates, one cannot apply the spot-test. In that case, the procedure is the water test.

### Water Test


*Meisinei mitikla d'maya, pshurei* — one takes a vessel filled with lukewarm water, inflates the lung, and submerges it. Specifically, the water must be lukewarm: not hot (*bachamimei lo*), because hot water causes the tissue to constrict and would compress a hole shut, concealing it; and not cold (*bekarimei lo*), because cold water hardens the tissue and might actually cause a hole to be created artificially. Lukewarm water alone preserves the lung's natural state. Once the inflated lung is submerged, one blows further and observes: if air bubbles emerge, the lung is *treifa*; if no bubbles appear, it is *kesheira*.

### Sound Explanation


But if no air is actually escaping to the outside, why was there any sound at all? The *gemara* explains: *tata inkiv ila lo inkiv* — the inner membrane was pierced but the outer was not. The air escapes from the hollow of the lung through the hole in the inner membrane and enters the space between the inner and outer walls. That trapped air is what produces the whistling sound. But because the outer membrane is intact, the air goes no further, and the lung is still *kesheira* — consistent with the ruling that both membranes must be pierced.

This analysis implies a structural picture in which the two membranes function as a genuine double wall, with a real space between them. A hole in the inner wall allows air to seep into that interstitial space, producing an audible sound; a hole in the outer wall is necessary to allow that air to escape entirely to the exterior. Only when both walls are compromised is the animal a *treifa*. One might also ask: what if there is a hole in the inner membrane in one location and a hole in the outer membrane in a different location? That question is not explicitly addressed, though the emphasis on requiring that air escape all the way to the outside suggests the concern is with continuity of the penetration.

## Lung Defects

### Lung Redness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי רֵיאָה דְּאִיגְּלִיד כַּאֲהִינָא סוּמָּקָא – כְּשֵׁרָה. וְאָמַר רָבָא: רֵיאָה שֶׁהֶאֱדִימָה מִקְצָתָהּ – כְּשֵׁרָה, כּוּלָּהּ – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara turns to **the** matter **itself** mentioned above: **Rava says: This** animal with **a lung whose** outer **membrane was removed** so that it looks **like a red date** is **kosher. And Rava says:** With regard to **a lung** whose outer membrane **reddened** due to bleeding of the lung, if only **part of it** turned red, then the animal is **kosher,** but if **all of it** turned red, the animal is **a *tereifa*.**

Rava states: *reia she-hidima miktzata kashira, kula treifa* — if a lung shows redness (*hidima*) from a wound in part of it, it remains *kesheira*; if the entire lung is reddened, it is *treifa*. The redness here is not the natural red of the inner membrane, as discussed earlier, but the redness of a wound — blood collecting beneath the surface from broken blood vessels, without having escaped to the exterior, much like a bruise or contusion.

### Ravina Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: מִקְצָתָהּ מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָדְרָא בָּרְיָא, כּוּלָּהּ נָמֵי הָדְרָא בָּרְיָא! מִי לָא תַּנְיָא: וּשְׁאָר שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים, עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא מֵהֶם דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rava: What is the reason** that the animal is kosher if only **part of** the outer membrane reddened? Presumably, it is **because** the animal eventually **recovers** from the wound. But even if **all of it** turned red **as well,** the animal **recovers. Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If one injures one of the eight impure creeping creatures mentioned in the Torah, one is liable for desecrating Shabbat; **but** if one injures **other repugnant creatures and creeping animals,** one is not liable **until blood leaves their** body as a result of the injury, even if their skin reddens as a result of internal bleeding?

Ravina challenges Rava: if part of the lung being reddened is *kesheira* because *d'hadar barya* — it will heal — why should the entire lung being reddened be any different? Surely the entire lung can also heal. He supports this with a *baraita* about the laws of *shabbat*.

### Shabbat Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: לִשְׁמוֹנָה שְׁרָצִים מְדַמֵּינַן לַהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: נִצְרַר הַדָּם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא יָצָא. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ מִקְצָתָהּ נָמֵי! אֶלָּא, לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: We compare** the lung **to** one of the **eight creeping creatures, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **the blood collected** under the skin of one of the eight creeping creatures, **even if it did not leave** the body, one is liable for desecrating Shabbat, because the blood will not be reabsorbed into the body and the wound is permanent; that is difficult: **If so,** then there is still no reason to distinguish between part of the lung and all of it, and **even** if only **part of** the lung turned red, the animal should be rendered a *tereifa* **as well,** because the blood will not be reabsorbed into the body. **Rather, there is no difference** between the reddening of part or all of the lung.

One of the *melachot* of *shabbat* is causing blood to flow. This applies even when the blood does not exit the body — if blood collects beneath the skin as a result of a wound (*nitzrar ha-dam af al pi she-lo yatza*), a prohibited act has been performed. The *gemara* distinguishes between different categories of animals: large animals certainly have real skin such that subcutaneous blood collection counts as a *melacha*. Regarding the eight rodents (*sheratzim*) enumerated in the Torah as sources of *tumah*, they too have real skin and subcutaneous reddening counts. Regarding other small creatures, they lack what is considered real skin and the reddening does not constitute a *melacha* unless the blood actually exits.

Ravina argues from this: if the lung's reddening is to be treated like the eight *sheratzim* — as real subcutaneous wounding that does not naturally heal — then even a partial reddening should be *treifa*. And if it is to be treated like the other creatures — as insignificant because the wound will heal — then even a complete reddening should be *kesheira*. Either way, the distinction between partial and complete reddening seems incoherent.

The *gemara* responds: *ela lo shna* — in fact there is no difference. Rashi is inclined toward the lenient reading: either way it is *kesheira*. Tosafot, however, cites Rabbeinu Tam who says either way it is *treifa*. Interestingly, this is one of several cases in this *perek* where Rashi tends toward leniency and Rabbeinu Tam toward stringency. Tosafot notes that Rabbeinu Gershom's *hilchos trefos* aligns with one reading, and that Rabbeinu Tam himself authored a work called *hilchos gedolos* on this subject — indicating how early the practice began of composing specialized handbooks on the laws of *kashrus*, stretching back at least to Rabbeinu Gershom's time. On the specific question of *hidima*, Rashi and Rabbeinu Tam coincide in ruling *kesheira*, though they diverge in other cases.

### Dried Lung

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: רֵיאָה שֶׁיָּבְשָׁה מִקְצָתָהּ – טְרֵפָה. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב פַּפִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: כְּדַי שֶׁתִּפָּרֵךְ בְּצִפּוֹרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** If **part of the lung was dried,** the animal is **a *tereifa*. And how much** must the lung be dried so as to render the animal a *tereifa*? **Rav Pappi says in the name of Rava:** It must be **so** dry **that it can be crumbled with a fingernail.**

Rava further states: *reia she-yavsha miktzata treifa* — a lung of which part has dried out is *treifa*, even if only part. How dry must it be to qualify? Rav Pappa reports in the name of Rava: *kedei she-tiparech be-tziporen* — so dry that it would crumble if snapped with a fingernail. This is an advanced degree of desiccation — extremely brittle.

### Rabbi Yossi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן, כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם? דְּתַנְיָא: אֵיזוֹ הִיא יְבֵשָׁה – שֶׁאִם תִּינָּקֵב וְאֵינָהּ מוֹצִיאָה טִיפַּת דָּם; רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם אוֹמֵר: יְבֵשָׁה כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא נִפְרֶכֶת בְּצִפּוֹרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this statement? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam? As it is taught** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 37a): If a kosher firstborn animal has a blemish, such as a dried ear, it may be slaughtered outside the Temple. **What is a dried** ear? It is any ear **that if perforated does not bleed a drop. Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam says:** It must be **so dry that it can be crumbled with a fingernail.**

The *gemara* notes: *ke-man* — whose opinion does this follow? It corresponds to Rabbi Yossi ben HaMeshullam. The comparison arises from a parallel debate in the laws of *bechor*, the firstborn animal. To establish a *mum* in a firstborn — a physical defect that exempts it from being brought as a *korban* and permits the *kohen* to eat it outside the *beit haMikdash* — the *mishna* discusses a dried limb, particularly a dried ear. What counts as dried? *nitnakev ve-eino motzi tipat dam* — if pinpricked and no blood emerges, it is considered dry, according to the Sages. Rabbi Yossi ben HaMeshullam is more stringent: *yeveshah kedei she-tehei niphrechet be-tziporen* — it must be brittle enough to crumble when snapped with a fingernail.

The *gemara* initially implies that it imports the more lenient standard (no blood emerges) into the lung case as well, then compares it to Rabbi Yossi ben HaMeshullam's crumble standard.

### Redness Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, גַּבֵּי אוֹזֶן בְּכוֹר דְּקָא שָׁלֵיט בֵּיהּ זִיקָא – לָא הָדְרָא בָּרְיָא, אֲבָל רֵיאָה דְּלָא קָא שָׁלֵיט בַּהּ אַוֵּירָא – הָדְרָא בָּרְיָא. וְאָמַר רָבָא: הַאי רֵיאָה דְּקָיְימָא גִּילְדֵּי גִּילְדֵּי, אוּכָּמֵי אוּכָּמֵי, חֶזְוָותָא חֶזְוָותָא – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **You** may **even say** that the statement is in accordance with the opinion of the **Rabbis. With regard to the ear of a firstborn, which is exposed to the wind,** the Rabbis hold that the animal **would not recover** if it is so dry that it does not bleed. **But** with regard to the **lung, which is not exposed to the wind,** the animal **would recover** unless it is so dry that it can be crumbled with a fingernail. **And Rava says: This lung that stands** before us **covered** in **scabs,** or covered in **black** marks, or covered in sores with different **appearances,** is nevertheless **kosher.**

The *gemara* provides the resolution: *ela lo shna* — the two cases are ultimately not to be distinguished. The lung being *kesheira* even when all of it is reddened is because the redness indicates that healing can occur; the blood is collecting but not escaping. Whether the reddening is partial or total does not change this.

### Ear vs. Lung


The reason the dried-lung standard is pegged to crumbling rather than the lower threshold of no blood emerging upon pinprick is explained by the *gemara* through a structural distinction. *gabei ozen bechor d'ka shalit bei zika* — the ear of a firstborn is exposed to the open air. Because it is exposed, once blood ceases to flow through it (*lo hadra bari*), it will not recover. Hence the lower threshold — no blood emerging — suffices to declare it a *mum*. *aval reia d'lo ka shalit bah avira* — the lung, however, is protected inside the body and is not exposed to the air. Even if blood has ceased flowing through a dried section, the protected environment allows it to recover, so long as it has not yet reached the extreme state of crumbling. This is why only the crumbling standard, consistent with Rabbi Yossi ben HaMeshullam, establishes *treifa* for the lung.

### Discolorations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אֲהִינֵי סוּמָּקָא דִּיבַשׁ גִּילְדֵּי, סִימָן). גּוּפָא, אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי רֵיאָה דְּקָיְימָא גִּילְדֵּי גִּילְדֵּי, אוּכָּמֵי אוּכָּמֵי, חֶזְוָותָא חֶזְוָותָא – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides **a mnemonic** to remember the following statements of Rava with regard to the lung: **Dates, red, that dried, scabbed.** ... The Gemara turns to **the** matter **itself** mentioned above: **Rava says: This lung that stands** before us **covered** in **scabs,** or covered in **black** marks, or covered in sores with different **appearances,** is nevertheless **kosher.**

Rava states: *hai reia de-kaima gildi gildi ve-uchmi uchmi* — a lung that presents with what looks like peeling flakes in multiple places, or with black spots in multiple places, or with other color variations (*chazusa chazusa*) in multiple places — is nonetheless *kesheira*. These types of surface discoloration, while potentially alarming in appearance, do not render the lung a *treifa*. Later discussions in the *gemara* will address other types of discoloration that do present problems, and *rishonim* distinguish carefully between different categories; but the forms enumerated here are ruled permissible.

### Blisters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: אֵין מַקִּיפִין בְּבוּעֵי. וְאָמַר רָבָא: הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי אוּנֵּי דִּסְרִיכָן לַהֲדָדֵי, לֵית לְהוּ בְּדִיקָה. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא כְּסִדְרָן, אֲבָל כְּסִדְרָן – הַיְינוּ רְבִיתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar says in the name of Rava: One may not compare cysts** of the lungs. If one finds a perforated cyst on the lung, but does not know whether it was perforated before or after slaughter, he may not perform a test by perforating another cyst in order to compare their appearances. **And Rava says: These two lobes** of the lung **that adhere to one another** by thin strands **have no** need for **inspection,** since it is certain that these adhesions arose due to a perforation of the lung, rendering the animal a *tereifa*. The Gemara adds: **And we said** this *halakha* **only** with regard to adhesions **that are out of order,** where a lobe adhered to a non-adjacent lobe. **But** with regard to adhesions that are **in order, that is their** normal manner of **growth,** and the animal is kosher.

Amimar said in the name of Rava: *ein makiphin b'vuei* — one may not make comparison-testing when it comes to *buei*, blisters or boils. To understand this, one must know that a standard testing method for an ambiguous hole — one where it is unclear whether it pre-dates *shechita* (and would thus be a *treifa*) or was caused afterward during handling — is comparison: if there is another hole nearby that is known to have been made after *shechita*, the two holes are compared for size, color, and appearance. If they match, the doubtful hole is presumed to be post-*shechita* and the animal is *kesheira*.

Amimar rules that this comparison method does not apply in areas where blisters (*buei*) are present. Blisters alter the appearance of the surrounding tissue in ways that make meaningful comparison impossible; the normal visual cues are distorted. Therefore, if there is a doubtful hole in a blister area, one cannot test it by comparison and must assume it arose before *shechita*. The animal is presumed to be *treifa*. The details of this testing method and its application to adhesions will be developed further in the subsequent session.

## Conclusion (II)

### Session End

The shiur concludes at the threshold of the most consequential topic of this entire section: the laws of *sirchos*, adhesions. Despite covering an extensive range of issues — the spinal cord and its *moach*, the geographic extent of the *treifa* in both animals and birds, the liver and its required measure, and the multiple defects of the lungs including holes, redness, desiccation, discoloration, and blisters — the *gemara*'s treatment of adhesions, which are the primary practical issue in the *kashrut* of meat and the foundation of the distinction between *glatt* and non-*Glatt*, remains to be examined in full. That discussion, including the testing of adhesions by inflation and water immersion and the conditions under which an adhesion can be dismissed or must be treated as evidence of an underlying hole, will be taken up at the next session.
