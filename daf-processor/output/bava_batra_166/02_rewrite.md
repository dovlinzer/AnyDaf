# Bava Batra 166 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zahav bi-Dinarin

### Beraita Setup

Today's *daf* is 166, and we pick up with *zahav bi-dinarin*. The *Mishnah* had established several baseline rules for monetary documents: if a *shtar* contains contradictory amounts, one follows the lesser; if it specifies a denomination without a number — "borrowed *sla'im*" or "borrowed *zuzim*" — the minimum of two is assumed. Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* now takes up a *Beraita* that presents a more striking puzzle.

The *Beraita* begins at the bottom of 165b with *tanu rabanan*: if someone writes *amar kesef* — literally "silver" — the minimum is one *dinar* of silver, and the *Gemara* had already explained why the word is interpreted as a coin rather than an ingot. If the document says *kesef dinarin* or *dinarin kesef*, a minimum of two *dinarin* is assumed. But then comes the surprising ruling: if the document says *kesef bi-dinarin* — silver *in dinarin* — the obligation is *ein pachos mi-shenei dinarei zahav kesef*, not less than two *dinarei* of gold worth of silver. The use of the letter *bet* somehow flips the interpretation to the opposite metal. And the converse holds as well: *zahav bi-dinarei* is interpreted as gold worth two silver *dinarei*.

So the *Gemara* on the top of 166a engages this directly. Why, when the document says gold in *dinarin*, do we interpret *dinarei* as silver? Perhaps, the *Gemara* suggests, *ve-eima dahava prichah bi-trei dinarei dahava kama* — perhaps it means slivers of gold worth two gold *dinarei*, remaining entirely within the gold context?

### Abaye's Principle

*Amar Abaye*: *yad ba'al ha-shtar al ha-tachtona* — the burden of proof rests on the holder of the document, the one seeking to extract payment. Therefore, we interpret the ambiguous *bi-dinarei* to mean *dinarei* of silver, the lesser-value denomination. That much is straightforward. But this answer immediately invites a counter-question: if the principle of *yad ba'al ha-shtar al ha-tachtona* always pushes toward the lesser amount, then when someone writes *kesef bi-dinarei* — silver in *dinarei* — we should similarly interpret it as silver in silver *dinarei*, since silver is the lesser metal. Why then does the *Beraita* rule that *kesef bi-dinarin* means silver worth two gold *dinarei*?

The *Gemara* sharpens this in the parenthetical: *reisha de-katani kesef bi-dinarin ein pachos mi-shenei dinarei zahav kesef amai?* — why, when you say silver in *dinarin*, do we rule it means silver worth two gold *dinarei*? Perhaps, the *Gemara* says, it means *eima kaspa nascha bi-trei dinarei kaspa kama* — ingots or slivers of silver worth two silver *dinarei*. If we're going to apply *yad ba'al ha-shtar al ha-tachtona* to push toward the lesser metal, we should certainly do so when starting from silver. The answer Abaye offered for the gold case seems to work against the silver case, and vice versa.

### Rav Ashi's Distinction

*Amar Rav Ashi*: *reisha de-katani dinarei, seifa de-katani dinarin* — the *reisha* uses the form *dinarei* while the *seifa* uses *dinarin*, and it is this grammatical distinction that carries the interpretive weight. *Dinarei* signals gold; *dinarin* signals silver.

The distinction is admittedly somewhat artificial. *Dinarei* is the *smichut* form — the construct state linking two nouns, as in *dinarei zahav*. By itself, the word *dinarei* is not inherently a designation for gold; it is simply the grammatical form that *dinar* takes when bound to a following noun. The *Gemara* therefore asks: *u-mana teimra de-shani bein dinarei le-dinarin* — where is the evidence that this grammatical distinction tracks a difference in metal? And it answers by citing a *Beraita* from an entirely different domain.

*Detanya*: *ha-isha she-hayu aleha safek chamesh leidot safek chamesh zivot* — a woman who experienced five episodes of bleeding, uncertain in each case whether the bleeding constituted a *leida* (a legally significant birth or miscarriage, contingent on whether the fetus had been developing for forty days) or a *zivah* (three consecutive days of bleeding outside her regular menstrual cycle, creating an independent obligation for a *korban*). The practical import is that her liability for *korbanos* is anywhere between zero and five, depending on the nature of each episode.

The relevant background: a woman who gives birth or miscarries after forty days of fetal development is *mechuseires kapara* — she cannot eat *korbanot* until she brings two birds as a *korban*, one as an *olah* and one as a *chatas*, which completes her purification. A *chatas* may ordinarily not be brought unless there is certainty of obligation. But in the category of *mechuserei kapara* — where the *korban* is not an atonement for sin but a ritual completion of the *tahara* process — the tradition allows bringing the *chatas ha-of* even under uncertainty, since otherwise the woman could remain in a state of impurity indefinitely. Even so, the rabbis seek to minimize the use of this allowance.

Accordingly, the *Beraita* rules that such a woman brings one *korban* and thereby becomes permitted to eat *zevachim*, while the remainder is not obligatory upon her. Then the *Beraita* turns to the case of *hayu aleha chamesh leidot vadaot chamesh zivot vadaot* — five definite births and five definite instances of *zivah* — and rules again: *mavia korban echad ve-ochelet bi-zvachim*, one *korban* suffices. The principle seems to be that just as a woman who became *tamei* five separate times through contact with a *neveilah* requires only one immersion in the *mikvah* to become *tahor*, so too the *korban* here functions as a ritual completion of *tahara* rather than a personal discharge of individual obligations — one act of completion suffices regardless of how many times the state was incurred. The remainder, however, does remain as a genuine *chova*.

Now the connection to *dinarei* and *dinarin* emerges. The *Beraita* continues: *ma'aseh amdu kinim bi-Yerushalayim be-dinarei zahav* — there was an event in which the price of a *kan* of birds (the pair brought as *olah* and *chatas*) rose to *dinarei zahav*. Here the *Beraita* uses the form *dinarei zahav* for gold.

## Rabban Shimon's Market

### Price Crisis

*Amar Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*: *ha-maon ha-zeh, im alin ha-laila* — invoking an oath in the name of the *Beis Hamikdash*, he declared he would not sleep that night *ad she-yihu be-dinarim* — until the birds came down to *dinarim*. Here the *Beraita* uses *dinarim* for silver. This is the textual basis for Rav Ashi's distinction: the *Beraita* itself employs *dinarei* in connection with gold and *dinarim* in connection with silver, and Rav Ashi leverages this usage to differentiate the two forms in the *shtar* context.

### One Korban Rule

What did Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel actually do to bring down prices? *Nichnas le-veit ha-din ve-limed* — he entered the *beis din* and publicly taught the halacha: *ha-isha she-hayu aleha chamesh leidot vadai chamesh zivot vadai, mavia korban echad ve-ochelet bi-zvachim, ve-ein ha-she'ar aleha chova* — a woman who definitely had five births and five instances of *zivah* brings one *korban*, becomes permitted to eat *zevachim*, and has no remaining obligation for the rest.

### Price Collapse

The practical effect was immediate. Women who had accumulated multiple obligations over years — who had perhaps endured many births and miscarriages — and who had come to *Yerushalayim* expecting to purchase ten *korbanos* now needed only one. With demand collapsing, *ve-amdu kinim bo ba-yom be-riva'tayim* — the price of a *kan* fell that very day to a *reva*, a quarter of a silver *dinar*. Rabban Shimon had aimed to bring prices from gold *dinarei* down to silver *dinarim*, and the result went even further — to one-quarter of a silver *dinar*, which was already a fraction of the original gold price.

### Psak vs. Policy

This raises the obvious question: was Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel deliberately *paskening* a *halacha* he did not actually hold in order to manipulate market prices? The *Gemara* categorically rejects this reading. This was genuine *psak halacha*. The more nuanced account is that he held this position all along but had not previously pressed it into public practice — perhaps because others followed a stricter ruling and he did not feel compelled to override that consensus. The economic crisis, however, provided the *sha'at ha-dachak* that motivated him to publicize and enforce the lenient view he in fact believed in. A parallel appears in *Pesachim*, where *Shmuel* publicly taught a ruling about vessels after *Pesach* in response to price gouging — again, genuine *psak* whose timing was shaped by social need. The *Gemara* never assumes a *posek* is teaching something he does not believe; rather, it credits him with bringing forward a position he had not previously championed.

This speaks to a broader phenomenon: the conscious integration of communal welfare into *psak halacha*. The fish-market interventions of various later authorities — where rabbis prohibited purchasing fish for *Shabbos* until prices returned to normal — represent a related but structurally different maneuver, closer to a *takkanah* requiring people to forgo a *kiyum*, whereas Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's act was a straightforward ruling about a woman's obligations.

### Tosafot Challenge

*Tosafot* raises a sharp objection. Across the broader corpus of *Gemaros*, there is no evidence anywhere for the position that a woman who definitely had five separate births is exempt from bringing five *korbanos*. If five *leidot* are *vadai*, surely five *korbanos* are obligatory. *Tosafot* therefore argues that the correct *girsa* of the *Beraita* in the case of definite births is not *ve-ein ha-she'ar aleha chova* but rather *ve-ha-she'ar aleha chova* — the remainder is indeed obligatory. What Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel corrected was not the total number of *korbanos* owed, but the urgency of bringing them all before she could eat *zevachim*. The prior accepted practice, apparently, was that a woman had to bring all outstanding *korbanos* before she fully achieved *tahara*. Rabban Shimon taught that one *korban* suffices to permit eating *zevachim*, and the rest she may bring at her leisure. By removing the urgency — by telling women they need only purchase one *kan* immediately and could worry about the rest later, when prices might be more favorable — he effectively collapsed demand and with it the price.

## Zahav bi-Dinarin (II)

### Tosafot Grammar

Returning now to the grammatical question underlying Rav Ashi's distinction: *Tosafot* acknowledges the difficulty directly. The form *dinarei zahav* is simply *smichut* — the construct state — and *dinarei* by itself carries no inherent gold connotation. Indeed, one could argue the opposite: if *dinarei* alone meant gold, the *Beraita* would not need to add *zahav* as a modifier; the very fact that it says *dinarei zahav* rather than *dinarei* alone suggests that *dinarei* is neutral and requires qualification.

*Tosafot* addresses this by proposing an emended *girsa*. In *Tosafot*'s reading, the *Beraita*'s report of Rabban Shimon's oath has modifiers on both sides: the original price is given as *dinarei zahav*, and the target price he swore to achieve is *dinarim shel kesef*. The telling detail is the grammatical asymmetry: gold is expressed through *smichut* (*dinarei zahav*) while silver is expressed through the *shel* construction (*dinarim shel kesef*), rather than *dinarei kesef*. This divergence in construction, *Tosafot* argues, establishes a stylistic convention: when referring to gold one uses *dinarei*, and when referring to silver one uses *dinarim* — and this convention then carries over into the interpretation of ambiguous *shtar* language.

Whether this argument fully persuades is another matter. The distinction rests on a fairly fine grammatical observation about which possessive construction was chosen when modifiers were present in both cases, and it is not immediately obvious why that choice should establish a default meaning for the unmodified forms. *Tosafot* leaves the matter here without further resolution.

## Document Contradictions

### Mishna Purpose

We now turn to *amud bet*. The *Mishnah* addresses what happens when the top and bottom sections of a *shtar* contradict each other — for instance, if the top states one hundred and the bottom states two hundred. The rule is that one follows the bottom. The natural question is then: if the bottom always governs, why bother writing the amount at the top at all?

The *Mishnah* answers that the top serves as a backup. In the ancient world without duplicate copies, writing a key figure twice within the same document provided insurance against erasure. If one letter of the bottom text was erased and became illegible, it could be reconstructed from the top. The *Mishnah* specifies: *kedai she-im nimchak os achas* — so that if one letter is erased, it may be filled in from the top.

### One-Letter Limit

*Tanu Rabanan*: one may fill in a single erased letter in the bottom from the top — *yilmi tachton min ha-elyon be-os achas* — but not two letters. The *Gemara* illustrates this with a personal name rather than an amount. If the bottom reads *Chanan* and the top reads *Chanani* with one letter unclear, the reconstruction is permitted. But if two letters are missing — raising the possibility that the name was *Chananel*, for instance — the reconstruction may not be done.

### Majority Name

Why not fill in two letters? *Dilma mitrami she-ein bahen arba osiyos ve-havei lei palga de-shma* — perhaps the name in question has only four letters, and filling in two of them means supplementing half the name. This is treated as too radical an intervention.

But this rationale immediately invites a parallel question: *ei hachi os achas nami* — if we are worried about supplementing half a name, the same concern should apply to filling in one letter. *Dilma mitrami she-ein bahen shtei osiyos ve-havei lei palga de-shma* — perhaps the name has only two letters, and filling in one letter would mean filling in half. To which one might respond: what two-letter name actually exists? Dov, perhaps — though whether *dalet-bet* appears without a *vav* in *Tanakh* is itself an interesting question.

The *Gemara* ultimately restates the concern: *dilma mishtamesh bein shalosh osiyot ve-havei lehu ruba de-shma* — we are worried the name might have three letters, and filling in two of them would constitute the majority of a name. The logic then resolves as follows: filling in one letter is always at most half of a name (since the smallest plausible name has two letters), while filling in two letters risks constituting a majority. Half may be supplemented; a majority may not.

### Rashbam's Approach

At this point one might reasonably ask: why does any of this matter? Even when the concern about *ruba de-shma* does not apply — say, the name has nine letters and two erased letters are far from a majority — the *Gemara* still prohibits filling in two letters. Is this a *gezeira*? Why is a straightforward reconstruction denied to the legitimate creditor?

*Tosafot* raises exactly this complaint and concedes he has no satisfying answer. Before addressing *Tosafot*'s broader position, it is worth considering the *Rashbam*'s approach.

The *Rashbam* argues that we are not primarily dealing with erasure but rather with a *sofer* who forgot to write certain letters. A *sofer* might plausibly forget one letter — it happens — but he would not forget two. Therefore, when the bottom reads *Chanan* and the top reads what appears to be *Chanan* with some letters missing, the *Rashbam* concludes: this *sofer* did not forget two letters; he really wrote *Chanan*, and there is some creditor named *Chanan* to whom the debt is owed.

This approach faces serious difficulties. First, if the name at the top ends in a simple *nun*, it should be a *nun sofit*, and the absence of this is hard to explain away. Second, the *Mishnah* frames its discussion explicitly around erasures — *nimchak* — not around the *sofer* having forgotten letters in the first place. Third, in cases where the partial letters at the top cannot form any real name — for instance, if the top reads *yud* followed by a gap — there is no one to whom the *shtar* could be delivered on the *Rashbam*'s logic.

### Tosafot Objection

*Tosafot* therefore takes a categorically different position: this is unambiguously a case of erasure, and the *Mishna*'s scenario is one where something was clearly written and then erased. The question is simply whether we reconstruct it from the top. And *Tosafot*'s ruling is stark: if two letters are missing from an essential element of the *shtar* — whether an amount or, in this case, the name of the creditor — the *shtar* is simply unusable. It cannot be presented for collection.

One might ask why this is so unfair. After all, *Chananel* clearly exists and the debt is clearly his. But *Tosafot* seems to be reasoning from the structure of *edut* itself: testimony in a *shtar* must speak for itself. You cannot fill in what the witnesses did not explicitly attest. In a loose but illuminating analogy: the *Gemara* elsewhere rules that if witnesses saw a man running after another with a weapon, heard him announce his intent to kill, and then entered the room moments later to find the victim stabbed and the assailant standing over him — they cannot testify to murder. They did not see the act. *Edut* does not fill gaps, even obvious ones.

Applying this framework loosely here: the witnesses attested to what was written; the erased portion represents what they did not make explicit. One missing letter might be treated as a trivial gap, close enough to explicit attestation that we may fill it in from the backup copy at the top. Two missing letters, however, represent a more significant lacuna, and *Tosafot* concludes that the *shtar* simply cannot be used in that condition.

This reasoning still does not fully account for the *Gemara*'s invocation of *ruba de-shma* as the operative concern, and it leaves open the question of whether long names with two erased letters should be treated differently. The *Gemara* here is genuinely difficult, and the most honest reading may be that two erased letters represent, as a categorical matter, a *significant* gap — one whose significance is illustrated by the three-letter name case — and the rule is applied uniformly regardless of the actual length of the name in a given *shtar*.

As for what happens to the *shtar* in practice: the *Rashbam* holds that one finds a person whose name matches what appears in the document and gives the *shtar* to him. *Tosafot* finds this untenable. The *Rashbam*'s logic might work where the bottom is not erased but contradicts the top — there, the bottom governs, and one delivers the *shtar* accordingly. But where the bottom is clearly erased and clearly used to contain a different name, one cannot simply find a convenient *Chanan* and hand him someone else's *shtar*. In *Tosafot*'s view, the document is void for this purpose.

## Erasure vs. Contradiction

### Safal/Kafal Case

*Amar Rav Papa*: *peshita li* — obvious to me: if the top of the *shtar* says *safal* and the bottom says *kafal*, the bottom governs. This follows the same rule applied to contradictory amounts: when the top says one thing and the bottom another, *ha-kol holech achar ha-tachton*. Whether *safal* and *kafal* represent nonsense syllables (as the *Rashbam* suggests), or whether — as *Tosafot* proposes — *safal* stands for *saf palgi* (sixty half-shekalim) and *kafal* for *kof palgi* (one hundred half-shekalim), or whether the *Rashbam* takes them to mean a cup and a doubled garment (*tallit*) respectively, the principle is the same: with a clear contradiction, the bottom prevails.

### Teiku Stalemate

*Ba'ei Rav Papa* — Rav Papa's question: what if the top says *kafal* and the bottom says *safal*? At first this seems like the same case in reverse — go by the bottom. But a troubling possibility arises: *mi-chaishinan le-zvuv o lo?* Perhaps the bottom originally said *kafal* as well, but as the ink dried a fly landed on the critical letters and smudged them, transforming *kafal* into *safal*. In that scenario, the bottom has not been contradicted but damaged, and the correct reading of the bottom would be *kafal* — identical to the top and therefore a case for reconstruction rather than contradiction.

The dilemma is that we cannot tell from inspection alone whether we are looking at a genuine contradiction (in which case the bottom governs and the obligation is the lesser *safal*) or a case of erasure-by-fly (in which case we reconstruct the bottom from the top and the obligation is the higher *kafal*). The *Gemara* concludes: *teiku* — the matter is unresolved.

## Mixed Denominations

### Three Readings

The *Gemara* now presents a real case. *Hahu de-havah ketiv bei shis mea ve-zuza* — there was a *shtar* that read "six hundred and a *zuz*." Six hundred what? *Rav Sharvaya* sent the question to *Abaye*.

Three readings of the document suggest themselves. First: *shis mea istari ve-zuza* — six hundred *istari* and a *zuz*. Now, the *istari* can mean different things depending on the monetary system in use (*mana tzori* versus *mana medina*, which differ by a factor of eight), but taking the *istari* as a higher denomination than the *zuz* — comparable perhaps to a dollar relative to a quarter — this reading would mean a substantial sum plus one *zuz*. Second: *shis mea priti ve-zuza* — six hundred *prutot* (very small coins, roughly one two-hundredth of a *zuz*) and one *zuz*. This would represent a tiny amount. Third: *shis mea zuza ve-chad zuza* — simply six hundred and one *zuz*, with no change of denomination.

### Eliminate Low Coinage

*Amar lei: dal priti* — eliminate the *prutot* reading. *De-lo ketivi be-shtara* — small coins are simply not written in *shtaros* in this manner. *De-achochei mechachakei lehu* — they are summed up into larger denominations. If the debt were really six hundred *prutot* and a *zuz*, one would not write it that way; at approximately one two-hundredth of a *zuz* each, six hundred *prutot* amount to roughly three *zuza*, giving a total of approximately four or five *zuza* — and that is how it would be written. Writing "six hundred [prutot] and a *zuz*" would be absurd record-keeping when the sum could be expressed cleanly in *zuza*. The low-denomination reading is therefore eliminated on practical grounds.

### Abaye's Lesser Amount

We are left with two possibilities: six hundred *istari* and a *zuz* (a large sum) or six hundred *zuza* and a *zuz* (six hundred and one *zuza*). One might reason that since the denomination mentioned explicitly is the *zuz*, and there is no other denominator specified, the six hundred should naturally be read as *zuza* as well — *shis mea zuza ve-chad zuza*. But *Amar Abaye: yad ba'al ha-shtar al ha-tachtona* — the holder of the *shtar* bears the burden of proof, and we rule in favor of the lesser interpretation. The *istari* reading is the larger amount; the *zuza* reading is the lesser. Between these two viable possibilities, the principle of *yad ba'al ha-shtar al ha-tachtona* selects the six hundred and one *zuza* reading, awarding the debtor the benefit of the ambiguity. And there the *daf* ends.
