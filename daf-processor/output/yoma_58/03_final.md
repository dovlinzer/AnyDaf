# Yoma 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mixing Bloods

### Topic Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵירָה דַּם הַפָּר לְתוֹךְ דַּם הַשָּׂעִיר. תְּנַן כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: מְעָרְבִין לִקְרָנוֹת. דְּאִיתְּמַר: רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה וְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, חַד אָמַר: מְעָרְבִין, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין מְעָרְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that after the High Priest sprinkled the blood toward the curtain **he poured the blood of the bull into the blood of the goat.** The Gemara comments: **We learned** this mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who said** that the High Priest **mixes** the two types of blood before placing them **on the corners** of the inner altar, and he does not present each one separately. **As it was stated:** The later *tanna'im*, **Rabbi Yoshiya and Rabbi Yonatan,** debated this issue. **One** of them **said:** The High Priest **mixes** the blood for the purpose of placing the blood on the altar's corners, **and one** of them **said: He does not mix** the blood.

Today's daf is 58, and we pick up at the very bottom of 57b. The passage begins with the phrase *im dama hapar letoch dam hase'ir* — "if the blood of the ox fell into the blood of the goat" — and our *mishnah* is read as going *keman de'amar me'arvin lekrannot*, like the opinion that holds the bloods are mixed even for the horns.

To appreciate the question at hand, it is worth reviewing the full sequence of the *Yom Kippur* blood service. The *kohen gadol* performs one-and-seven sprinklings inside the *kodesh kodashim* on the *kaporet*, then one-and-seven directed toward the *parochet* — not on the *parochet* itself, though there was a dissenting opinion, but toward it. He then proceeds to the *mizbeach hazahav*, the golden altar, where he applies blood on the four horns and sprinkles seven times on top.

By way of contrast: when the *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* — the ox brought on behalf of the entire community that sinned based on a ruling of the *beis din* — is brought, one does seven sprinklings on the *parochet*, as opposed to the one-and-seven of *Yom Kippur*. This distinction was noted by the *Tosafos Yeshanim*. On *Yom Kippur*, we learn the one-and-seven structure from *vechein ya'aseh le'ohel moed*. And while on the golden altar a standard inner offering would require only the four horns, on *Yom Kippur* one also adds seven sprinklings on top.

### Par He'elam Davar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה הוּא דְּאָמַר מְעָרְבִין, דְּאָמַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא כְּתִיב ״יַחְדָּיו״ — כְּמַאן דִּכְתִיב ״יַחְדָּיו״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Conclude** that **Rabbi Yoshiya was** the *tanna* **who said** that the High Priest **mixes** the blood. In general, he maintains that whenever a verse mentions two matters, **although** the term: **Together, is not written** explicitly, **it is considered as though** the term: **Together, is** indeed **written.** In other words, two items that appear in the same verse are treated as combined unless expressly stated otherwise. By contrast, Rabbi Yonatan maintains that even if two matters are mentioned together they are treated separately unless the verse uses the term: Together. In this case, the verse states: "And he shall take of the blood of the bull and of the blood of the goat" (Leviticus 16:18). Therefore, Rabbi Yoshiya rules that the High Priest must place the two sets of blood together.

The scriptural basis for the *Yom Kippur* procedure is the *pasuk* (Vayikra 16): *veshachat es hase'ir vehevi es damo mi'beis laparochet ve'asah es damo ka'asher asah ledam hapar* — "he shall slaughter the goat and bring its blood within the curtain and do with its blood as he did with the blood of the ox." This makes clear that the *par* and the *se'ir* are kept separate: the *se'ir* is even slaughtered after the *par*. The verse *vechein ya'aseh le'ohel moed* then extends the same structure to the *parochet* sprinklings — they too are performed separately, one after the other, just as they were separate inside the *kodesh kodashim*. Although the *gemara* dealt elsewhere with the *be'di'avad* case of their becoming mixed, the *lechatchila* procedure is distinct applications.

The question now turns to the *mizbeach hazahav*. The *pasuk* states: *veyatzah el hamizbeach asher lifnei Hashem vechiper alav velakach midam hapar umidam hase'ir venasan al karnot hamizbeach saviv* — "and he shall go out to the altar that is before God and atone for it, and he shall take from the blood of the ox and from the blood of the goat and place it on the horns of the altar all around." When the *pasuk* subsequently says *vehizah alav min hadam be'etzba'o sheva pe'amim* — "and he shall sprinkle upon it from the blood with his finger seven times" — the language *min hadam*, "from the blood" in the singular, indicates that at the point of the seven sprinklings there is no question: the bloods are combined. But what of the application on the horns? The *pasuk* there says *midam hapar umidam hase'ir* — is that a combined mixture or two separate applications?

### Rav Yoshiya Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן הוּא, שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דִּכְתִיב: ״אַחַת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this contention. **Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan,** it remains possible to explain its ruling, since **it is different here, as it is written "once"** (Exodus 30:10), which indicates that there must be one act of sprinkling and not two. Consequently, the High Priest must mix the blood to ensure that there is only one presentation.

This is the subject of the debate before us. Our *mishnah* clearly holds they are mixed, but the *gemara* introduces the underlying dispute. It was stated: *Rav Yoshiya veRav Yonasan* — one says *me'arvin*, you mix them, and one says *ein me'arvin*, you do not.

The *gemara* concludes that it is Rav Yoshiya who holds they are mixed, based on a general principle known elsewhere. In questions of scriptural interpretation, Rav Yoshiya maintains: *af al gav delo ksiv yachdav keman dechsiv yachdav dami* — even when the *Torah* does not explicitly write the word *yachdav*, "together," it is treated as if it had. That is, a *vav* conjoining two items establishes a default presumption of combination.

This principle plays out in the famous case of *umakeh aviv ve'imo mos yumos* — "one who strikes his father and his mother shall be put to death." Does this mean one must have struck both, or does striking either one suffice? The *vav* is ambiguous: it can mean "and" (together) or "or" (each separately). Rav Yoshiya holds that the default reading of a *vav* is combination, and when the *Torah* means something separate, it requires additional contextual clues or another *pasuk* to override that default. Thus, when the *pasuk* says *midam hapar umidam hase'ir*, Rav Yoshiya reads that as a combined mixture by default.

### Yoshiya vs. Yonasan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא דְּלָא כְּשִׁנּוּיַין: ״וְלָקַח מִדַּם הַפָּר וּמִדַּם הַשָּׂעִיר״, שֶׁיִּהְיוּ מְעוֹרָבִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It was taught** in a *baraita* **contrary to our response,** but in accordance with the initial assumption: "And he shall go out to the altar that is before the Lord, and make atonement for it; **and he shall take of the blood of the bull and of the blood of the goat,** and put it on the corners of the altar round about" (Leviticus 16:18). This verse teaches **that** the blood of the bull and the goat should **be mixed.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.**

A *baraita* makes the debate explicit. Rav Yoshiya: *v'lakach midam hapar umidam hase'ir — me'uravin*, "they should be mixed." This is simply his standard interpretive position applied here. Then, at the top of 58a, Rav Yonasan says: *lo — mizeh bifnei atzmo umizeh bifnei atzmo* — "no; from this one separately and from that one separately." Exactly as expected: where Rav Yoshiya reads a *vav* as implying combination, Rav Yonasan reads it as indicating two distinct, independent entities.

### Once Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִזֶּה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, וּמִזֶּה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״אַחַת״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yonatan says:** He presents **from this** one, the bull's blood, **by itself, and** he presents **from that** one, the goat's blood, **by itself** and does not mix them. **Rabbi Yoshiya said to him: But wasn't it already stated:** "And Aaron shall make atonement upon its corners **once** a year; with the blood of the sin-offering of atonement" (Exodus 30:10), which indicates that the High Priest does not present twice?

The *gemara* then raises the possibility that even Rav Yonasan might agree to mixing in this particular case: *shani hacha d'chsiv achas* — here it is different, because the word *achas*, "once," appears. Where? Rashi explains that this refers to Shemos 30 — the passage in *Tetzaveh* describing the construction of the *mizbeach haktoret*. There it says: *v'chiper aharon al karnosav achas bashana midam chatas hakippurim achas bashana yichaper alav l'doroseichem* — "Aaron shall atone for its horns once in the year, from the blood of the *Yom Kippur* sin-offering, once in the year shall he atone for it throughout your generations." The double use of *achas* — "once" — in proximity to the *mizbeach hazahav* indicates that the atonement is performed as one unified act, which implies that the bloods are combined. This is far more contextually relevant than a verse appearing twenty *pesukim* later in Vayikra, and it makes considerably better sense as the textual basis for the argument.

### Midam Separate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״מִדַּם הַפָּר וּמִדַּם הַשָּׂעִיר״! אִם כֵּן, לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״אַחַת״? לוֹמַר לָךְ: אַחַת וְלֹא שְׁתַּיִם מִדַּם הַפָּר, אַחַת וְלֹא שְׁתַּיִם מִדַּם הַשָּׂעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yonatan said to him: But wasn't it already stated: "And he shall take of the blood of the bull and of the blood of the goat"** (Leviticus 16:18), which teaches that each set of sprinklings must be performed by itself? **If so, why is "once" stated?** This verse comes **to tell you** that he must perform the rite **once and not** perform **two** sets of sprinklings **from the blood of the bull;** and likewise he must perform the rite **once and not** perform **two** sets of sprinklings **from the blood of the goat.** From the blood of each of the animals, the High Priest presents only one set of sprinklings. This shows that Rabbi Yoshiya and Rabbi Yonatan indeed disagree with regard to this issue.

Rav Yoshiya then presses the point in the *baraita*: *halo kvar ne'emar achas* — "has it not already been stated 'once'?" — meaning even according to you, Rav Yonasan, the word *achas* compels combination. Rav Yonasan responds: *halo kvar ne'emar midam hapar umidam hase'ir* — "has it not already been stated 'from the blood of the ox and from the blood of the goat'"? Had the *Torah* intended one mixture, it would have written *midam hapar v'hase'ir* — the *se'ir* appended to the ox. Since it repeats *midam* — "from the blood of" — separately for each, it signals distinct applications. As for *achas*, Rav Yonasan reads it to mean: do not perform each blood's application twice. *Achas v'lo shtayim midam hapar, achas v'lo shtayim midam hase'ir* — "once and not twice from the blood of the ox, once and not twice from the blood of the goat." The "once" limits repetition, not separation.

A second *baraita* then resolves: *v'lakach midam hapar umidam hase'ir — shem'uravin zeh b'zeh*, they are mixed together. Should one ask whether they might be separate, *talmud lomar achas* — the *Torah*'s use of *achas* settles it in favor of combination. The *gemara* notes this *baraita* follows Rav Yoshiya's position, and *Tosafos* observes that the need to derive mixing from *achas* does not necessarily mean the *baraita* rejects Rav Yoshiya's principle about the *vav* — it may simply be adding an additional proof. The upshot is that there are two independent grounds for the combined position: the default reading of a conjunctive *vav*, and the verse's use of *achas* to indicate a single unified act of atonement. Rav Yonasan's dissenting view — that the horns are done with each blood separately, and only the seven sprinklings on top require mixture — nonetheless stands as a legitimate position in the *baraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״וְלָקַח מִדַּם הַפָּר וּמִדַּם הַשָּׂעִיר״ — שֶׁיִּהְיוּ מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר שֶׁיִּהְיוּ מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מִזֶּה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ וּמִזֶּה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַחַת״. וּסְתָמָא כְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in **another** *baraita*: **"And he shall take of the blood of the bull and of the blood of the goat"** (Leviticus 16:18). This verse teaches **that** the blood of the bull and the goat **should be mixed together.** Do **you say that they should be mixed together, or perhaps that is not** the case; rather, he presents **from this** blood **by itself and from that by itself?** Therefore, **the verse states: "Once."** The Gemara comments: **And** this **unattributed** *baraita* **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoshiya,** who said the two sets of blood are mixed, as stated in the mishna.

## Mixture Procedure

### Pouring Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָתַן אֶת הַמָּלֵא בָּרֵיקָן וְכוּ׳. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: הִנִּיחַ מִזְרָק בְּתוֹךְ מִזְרָק, וְקִבֵּל בּוֹ אֶת הַדָּם, מַהוּ? מִין בְּמִינוֹ חוֹצֵץ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ?
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that after the High Priest poured the bull's blood into the goat's blood, **he placed** the blood **from the full** bowl **into the empty** bowl, to mix the blood well. **Rami bar Ḥama raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda: What is** the *halakha* if **he placed** one **bowl inside** a second **bowl and collected the blood in** the inner bowl? Does one **type** of object mingled **with** another of **its** own **type interpose,** which would mean that the priest has not collected the blood himself, as the outer bowl interposed between him and the vessel? **Or** perhaps an object of one type **does not interpose** for another object of the same type, and therefore the two bowls are considered one object.

Having established the debate over whether the bloods are mixed for the horns, the *mishnah* describes the practical procedure: *nassan es hamalei b'reikan* — "he poured the full one into the empty one." The sequence in the *mishnah* is: *eirev dam hapar l'soch dam hase'ir* — he poured the blood of the ox into the bowl holding the blood of the goat. One might expect the reverse — pouring the smaller quantity into the larger — but the last service performed had been the *se'ir* at the *parochet*, so that is the bowl the *kohen* is already holding. He then retrieves the *par* blood from wherever it was resting and pours it in. The reversal that follows — pouring the combined full bowl back into the now-empty bowl — is simply good mixing technique, like combining two glasses: you pour back and forth to achieve a thorough blend. The bowls must have been of similar capacity to accommodate both volumes simultaneously.

### Vessel in Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: נָתַן אֶת הַמָּלֵא בָּרֵיקָן. מַאי לָאו: הוֹשִׁיב מִזְרָק מָלֵא לְתוֹךְ מִזְרָק רֵיקָן?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: We** have already **learned** the answer to this question in the mishna: **He placed** the blood **from the full** bowl **into the empty** bowl. **What, is it not** correct to infer from this statement that **he inserted** the **full bowl into** the **empty bowl?**

Against this background, Rami bar Chama posed a question to Rav Chisda. In an ordinary case — not *Yom Kippur* — if one placed one *mizrak* (receiving bowl) inside another and performed *kabbalas hadam* (collection of the slaughtered blood) with the inner bowl while holding the outer one, what is the *halacha*? Is this a *chatzitzah* — an interposition between the *kohen*'s hand and the bowl he is meant to be holding — or not? Rami bar Chama initially thought the answer might be found in the *mishnah*'s phrase *nassan es hamalei b'reikan*, reading it to mean that one literally placed the full bowl inside the empty bowl and proceeded to carry both to the altar. Rav Chisda corrected him: the *mishnah* simply means that the contents of the full bowl were poured into the empty bowl, not that one bowl was physically nested inside another. Nevertheless, the question itself is worth pursuing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, עֵירָה מִזְרָק מָלֵא לְתוֹךְ מִזְרָק רֵיקָן. הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא: עֵירָה דַּם הַפָּר לְתוֹךְ דַּם הַשָּׂעִיר! כְּדֵי לְעָרְבָן יָפֶה יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama responded: **No,** it means that **he poured** the **full bowl into** an **empty bowl.** Rav Ḥisda said to him: But this statement is unnecessary, **as** the *tanna* of the mishna already **taught** the requirement to pour from one vessel into another in the **first** clause: **He poured the blood of the bull into the blood of the goat.** The subsequent phrase: He placed the full into the empty, must therefore refer to the placement of one vessel inside another. Rami bar Ḥama responded: No, it means that he pours the blood from the full bowl into the empty bowl a second time **in order to mix them very well.** To this end, he pours from one vessel into the other before again pouring the mixture back into the first vessel, thereby mixing them thoroughly.

### Min B'Mino

The precise issue is: *min b'mino chotzetz* — does an object of the same type as what it is obstructing constitute a *chatzitzah*? A bowl inside a bowl is the same category of object. Does that matter? One might argue that when the interposing material is of the same type as the primary object — *min b'mino* — it should not be considered a foreign interruption at all, and thus not a *chatzitzah*.

### Vessel Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָיָה עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבֵּי כְּלִי, אוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי רֶגֶל חֲבֵירוֹ — פָּסוּל! שָׁאנֵי רֶגֶל, דְּלָא מָצֵי מְבַטֵּיל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a solution to this question: If a priest performed the Temple service while he **was standing on top of a vessel, or on the foot of another** priest, his service **is disqualified,** as the priest must stand directly on the floor of the Temple. The fact that the foot of another person is considered an interposition proves that one type serves as an interposition with regard to another object of the same type. The Gemara rejects this contention: **A foot is different, as he cannot nullify it.** The foot of another person cannot be considered nullified with respect to the priest's foot, but in the case of a vessel it is possible to say that one vessel is nullified in favor of another vessel.

The *gemara* attempts a proof: *ta shma* — come and hear. If a *kohen* was standing on a vessel during the *avodah* rather than directly on the floor of the *azarah*, the *avodah* is invalid. The *kohen* must stand barefoot on the floor of the *Mikdash*, and similarly his body must be in direct physical contact with the *klei sharet* he is using. The same sanctifying logic that applies to the floor connects through the *kohen*'s body to the vessels. A *kohen* standing on a vessel, or on his companion's foot, has his *avodah* rendered invalid because he is not in direct contact with the floor.

This might seem to prove that *min b'mino chotzetz* — two feet, both body parts, still constitute a *chatzitzah* — but the *gemara* deflects.

### Foot Exception

The concept of *bitul* in the context of *chatzitzah* functions as follows: if one can treat the interposing object as effectively merged with, or part of, the primary object, then it loses its identity as a foreign body and ceases to constitute a *chatzitzah*. Two vessels of the *Mikdash* can in principle be treated as one unified entity — together they are all the same sacred service-ware, and one can conceptually subsume the inner within the outer. But one cannot do this with another person's foot. That foot belongs to someone else, will always be reclaimed, and therefore retains its permanent identity as a separate, foreign object. It can never be *mevatel* — subsumed — into the primary entity, and thus always remains a *chatzitzah* regardless of the *min b'mino* consideration.

### Chatzitzah Concept

This discussion prompts a broader examination of the concept of *chatzitzah*. The *halacha* holds that if one is not *makpid* — not particular — about something resting on one's body, it does not constitute a *chatzitzah*. The clearest application is by *tevilah* in a *mikveh*: a bandage that one wishes to have on one's body is not a *chatzitzah*, even though it physically prevents water from reaching the skin beneath it. Why should the absence of subjective objection remove the legal barrier?

Two conceptual frameworks address this. The first holds that *chatzitzah* requires the object to be identifiable as a foreign body — a thing actively out of place. An object that the person affirmatively wants there lacks that identity; it is not foreign matter, and therefore it is not legally defined as a *chatzitzah* at all, regardless of whether water physically reaches the skin beneath it. The second framework goes in the opposite direction: the object is acknowledged to prevent contact, but when the person does not mind its presence, it becomes *tafel* — secondary, effectively treated as an extension of the body itself. The water therefore reaches "the body," including what has been subsumed into it.

The gap between these two frameworks is considerable. The first approach requires only that the object not be actively defined as foreign; the second requires that it be positively defined as part of the body. Contemporary *halachic* debates about *chatzitzah* often hinge precisely on this divide — for instance, whether a protective ear-plug worn by a woman with an ear infection constitutes a *chatzitzah* during *tevilah*. Rav Ovadia Yosef's basic approach is that since the woman currently needs the plug for medical reasons, it is considered part of her body at this moment. Rav Moshe addresses the question differently, noting that if the plug is coated with a permeable material such that water could theoretically seep through over time, the object cannot be considered a complete barrier, and there are additional grounds for leniency. The underlying principle — that something theoretically permeable is not a *chatzitzah* — will re-emerge momentarily in the *gemara*'s discussion of the *siv*.

Returning to the question of *mevatel lei*: Rashi explains the phrase in the context of the foot as follows: *d'lo mivatlei chaveiro es raglo ad sheyigmor avodaso* — the companion will not surrender his foot for the duration of the service; he will want it back immediately. A foot therefore always retains its character as something separate, which prevents it from ever being subsumed.

### Derech Sheirud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, הָכִי בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ: דֶּרֶךְ שֵׁירוּת בְּכָךְ, אוֹ אֵין דֶּרֶךְ שֵׁירוּת בְּכָךְ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״אֶת כׇּל כְּלֵי הַשָּׁרֵת אֲשֶׁר יְשָׁרְתוּ בָם בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ״ — שְׁנֵי כֵלִים וְשֵׁירוּת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that Rami bar Ḥama did not refer to the issue of interposition at all. Rather, **this is** the **dilemma he raised before** Rav Ḥisda: **Is** placing one vessel inside another vessel a proper **manner of service; or is this not** a proper **manner of service,** and if one does so his service is disqualified? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** that **which the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** "And they shall take **all the service vessels, with which they serve in the Sanctuary"** (Numbers 4:12). This verse is referring to **two vessels and one service,** which indicates that this is a proper manner of service.

A second version of the question is then presented: some say the issue here is not *chatzitzah* at all — one can assume *min b'mino* is not a *chatzitzah* — but rather *derech sheirus bekach o ein derech sheirus bekach*: is it considered an appropriate manner of service to perform the *avodah* with one vessel nested inside another? This would be a distinct concern, unique to the sanctity of Temple service, separate from the physical question of contact and interposition.

### Two Vessels One Service

The *gemara* resolves both dimensions with a *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *es kol klei hashareis asher yesharsu bam bakodesh* — "all the holy vessels with which they shall serve in the sanctuary." The plural form *bam* — "with them" — is read as authorizing *shnei kelim v'sheirus echad*: two vessels serving as one unit of service. This teaches simultaneously that (a) a vessel within a vessel is not a *chatzitzah*, and (b) it constitutes proper *derech sheirus*. Both concerns are resolved in a single stroke by the scriptural phrase. The *gemara* thus concludes leniently on all fronts.

### Sieve Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: הִנִּיחַ סִיב בְּתוֹךְ הַמִּזְרָק, וְקִבֵּל בּוֹ אֶת הַדָּם, מַהוּ: מִין בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ חוֹצֵץ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ? כֵּיוָן דִּמְחַלְחֵל — לָא חָיֵיץ, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rami bar Ḥama raised** another **dilemma before Rav Ḥisda** along the same lines: **What is** the *halakha* if **he placed bast,** the material that grows around palm trees, **inside the bowl, and collected the blood in** the bowl through the bast, such that the blood seeps through into the bowl? Does one **type** of object mixed **with** another **not** of **its** own **type interpose; or** perhaps **it does not interpose?** Could one say that **since the blood seeps** through, it therefore **does not interpose? Or perhaps** this case is **no different,** and as there is a foreign object in the vessel it is considered an interposition even if the blood seeps through.

Rami bar Chama then poses a related question to Rav Chisda: *hiniach siv b'soch hamizrak v'kibeil bo es hadam mahu* — if one placed a palm-bast (a coarse porous plant fiber) inside a *mizrak* and collected the blood through it, what is the *halacha*? Unlike the previous case of *min b'mino*, here the *siv* is an entirely different substance — *min beshe'eino mino* — so the question of *chatzitzah* is more acute. On the other hand, the *siv* is porous: if left long enough, the blood will eventually seep through. Is that porousness sufficient to remove its status as a *chatzitzah*, given that right now the blood has not yet penetrated to the vessel itself?

### Porous Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: זוֹלֵף וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לַסְּפוֹג! שָׁאנֵי מַיָּא דִּקְלִישִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him** in response: **We** already **learned** this matter with regard to sanctifying the purification waters, i.e., the water mixed with ashes of the red heifer. If a sponge was placed inside the container of water, the water in the sponge is disqualified and the priest may not sprinkle with it. How should he proceed? **He sprinkles** from the water **and continues until he reaches the sponge.** This shows that the presence of the sponge in the water container is not considered an interposition for the water in the container, despite the fact that it itself is unfit for the service. Rami bar Ḥama replied: This is no proof. **Water is different, as it is thin** and therefore it certainly seeps through the sponge and reaches the bowl; whereas the thicker blood will perhaps not seep into the fiber.

Rav Chisda replied that the answer may be found in a *mishnah* concerning *parah adumah*: if a sponge was dropped into the purification water, one cannot use the water absorbed within the sponge, but one can use the water remaining in the vessel around it. Why can one use that surrounding water if some of it sits atop the sponge without touching the vessel floor? The *gemara* answers: *shani maya deklishi* — water is different, being extremely non-viscous; water seeps through porous materials with great ease. The principle of *machalchel* — porousness rendering something not a *chatzitzah* — holds in principle, but the question is one of degree. Blood, being more viscous, may require so long to seep through the *siv* that we would not say it has effectively passed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, הָכִי פְּשַׁט לֵיהּ: בְּדָם — כָּשֵׁר. בְּקוֹמֶץ — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **this is how** Rav Ḥisda **resolved** the problem of placing the bast in the bowl **for** Rami bar Ḥama: In the case **of the blood it is valid,** as blood passes through, and the bast does not obstruct it. However, in the case **of the handful** of a meal-offering, which must be sanctified in a vessel immediately after the handful is taken, if he put bast in a vessel and placed the handful on top of it, **it is disqualified.** The reason is that the handful of a meal-offering consists of dough, which does not pass through the bast, and it is therefore considered a proper interposition.

A second version of the tradition reports that Rav Chisda did in fact reach a conclusion: for liquids (*bedam kasher*) the *siv* is not a *chatzitzah*, since the liquid will eventually permeate. For solids (*bekometz pasul*) — if one lined the vessel with this fibrous material and placed a handful of a *minchah* upon it — the *siv* would be a valid *chatzitzah*, since a solid will not seep through at all. The ruling thus establishes an important general principle in the laws of *chatzitzah*: a substance that is permeable to liquids, even if the liquid has not yet passed through it at the moment of use, is not considered a *chatzitzah* for liquids. This parallels the reasoning invoked in contemporary discussions of ear plugs and similar cases.

### Tosafos Distinction

*Tosafos* raises a question from a *gemara* in Sukkah that addresses taking a *lulav* through a handkerchief used as a handle. The *gemara* there considers whether this constitutes *lekichah al yedei davar acher* — taking via an intermediary — and whether that might itself be valid as an act of *lekichah*, especially when the handle is fashioned from *lulav* material and thus *min b'mino*. If *lekichah* through an intermediary can be valid, why does the *gemara* here treat vessel-within-vessel as a *chatzitzah* question at all?

*Tosafos* answers by drawing a fundamental distinction. When one uses an outer vessel as a handle and holds the inner one through it, that might indeed be classified as *lekichah al yedei davar acher* — and if the outer vessel functions as a handle, it may be acceptable. But when one grips a vessel such that an intermediary comes between the hand and the intended vessel — not a handle, but an obstruction — the question is one of *chatzitzah*, not merely of defining the act of taking.

More fundamentally, *Tosafos* points to a conceptual divide between *lulav* and *klei sharet*. The obligation by *lulav* derives from *ulekachtem lachem* — "you shall take for yourselves" — which defines a required act. The question is whether taking through a handle still constitutes *lekichah* as an act. But the requirement by *klei sharet*, as the *Ritzba* explains: *gabei klei sharet bechol inyan min beshe'eino mino chotzetz d'nihi dechi avid k'ein beis yad mikrei lekichah* — even if in the *lulav* context taking via a handle might be valid, *gabei klei sharet deksiv v'lakach hakohen*, where the *Torah* requires the *kohen* to take directly, the demand is *be'atzmo shel kohen* — the *kohen*'s own body must be in contact. That is a requirement of direct physical connection, not merely of performing an act of acquisition. The *chatzitzah* concern is therefore far more acute in the realm of Temple service than in the realm of *lulav*, because there the demand is bodily contact itself, not simply the performative definition of taking.

## Outer Altar Horns

### Northeast Start

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״וְיָצָא אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ אֲשֶׁר לִפְנֵי ה׳״, זֶה מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב. הִתְחִיל מְחַטֵּא וְיוֹרֵד, מֵהֵיכָן הוּא מַתְחִיל? מִקֶּרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית, דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית. מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּא מַתְחִיל בַּחַטָּאת עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן — מִשָּׁם הָיָה גּוֹמֵר עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It is stated: **"And he shall go out to the altar that is before the Lord**, and make atonement for it; and he shall take of the blood of the bull, and of the blood of the goat, and place it upon the corners of the altar round about" (Leviticus 16:18). **This** altar **is the golden altar,** since the outer altar is not before the Lord in the Sanctuary. **He began to cleanse** the altar, sprinkling the blood **downward. From where does he begin?** He begins **from the northeast corner,** and proceeds to the **northwest** corner, and then to the **southwest** corner, and finally to the **southeast** corner. A way to remember this is: At the **place** where **he begins** sprinkling the blood **for a sin-offering** sacrificed **on the outer altar,** the southeast corner, **there he finishes** sprinkling the blood **on the inner altar.**

We turn now to the *mishnah*'s account of the application of blood on the golden altar and then the outer altar. The *pasuk* states: *veyatzah el hamizbeach asher lifnei Hashem vechiper alav velakach midam hapar umidam hase'ir venasan al karnot hamizbeach saviv* — "and he shall go out to the altar that is before God, and he shall atone for it, and he shall take from the blood of the ox and from the blood of the goat and place it on the horns of the altar all around." The *mizbeach asher lifnei Hashem* is the *mizbeach hazahav*, the golden altar, as it stands closest to the *kodesh kodashim*.

The *mishnah* describes the procedure: *hitchil mechatei v'yored* — he would begin applying the blood, and his finger would move in a downward motion. The corners are addressed in the sequence: northeast, northwest, southwest, southeast. The *mishnah* then notes: *makom shehu matchil b'chatas al mizbeach hachitzon* — the place where one begins a *chatas* on the outer altar — *misham haya gomer al mizbeach hapnimi* — is where one finishes on the inner altar. On the outer altar one ascends the ramp, turns right (counter-clockwise), and descends; this ends at the southeast corner. Correspondingly, on the inner altar one begins at the northeast and travels counter-clockwise, ending at the southeast. The southeast of the outer altar is thus both the starting point for that service and the finishing point of the inner service.

The reason one does not simply begin at the first corner encountered upon exiting is derived, as the *gemara* will explain, from the word *veyatzah* — one must be fully outside before commencing.

### Rabbi Eliezer Stationary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּמְקוֹמוֹ הָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמְחַטֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The priest would not circle the altar; rather, **he stood in** one **place and sprinkled** the blood from there. Since the altar was only one square cubit, he could sprinkle the blood on all four corners without moving.

Rabbi Eliezer holds: *bimkomo haya omed umachatei* — he would stand in place and apply the blood without walking around the altar at all. This is because the golden altar measured only *amah al amah* — one cubit by one cubit — no larger than a single horn of the outer altar. Given its small dimensions, Rabbi Eliezer holds that one can reach all four horns from a single position.

### Direction Variation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל כּוּלָּן הָיָה נוֹתֵן מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה, חוּץ מִזּוֹ שֶׁהָיְתָה לְפָנָיו, שֶׁהָיָה נוֹתֵן מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And on all the corners he would present** the blood **from below upward, except for that** corner **that was** directly **before him,** on **which he would present** the blood **from above downward.**

Rabbi Eliezer also teaches: *ve'al kulam haya nosein milmatah lemalah chutz mizo shehayta lefanav shehaya nosein mima'alah lematah* — on all the corners he would apply from bottom to top, except for the one directly in front of him, on which he would apply from top to bottom. This directional variation is consistent with the *tana kamma*'s position as well, since the first opinion implicitly requires different handling of the nearest corner.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi explains the practical reasoning. The golden altar is two *amot* high — approximately three feet — which places the horns above the natural height of one's arm. To reach them, the *kohen* must extend his arm upward at an angle. When reaching corners that are farther away, one can manage the bottom-to-top motion without difficulty. But for the corner directly in front of one's body, if one attempts the same upward motion, the blood will run back down along the finger and into the palm. Therefore, for the nearest corner the *kohen* must reverse direction — applying from the top downward — so that the blood does not run into his hand. Rashi phrases it: *she'im moshcha milmatah lemalah hadam zav letoch beis yado* — "if he drew it from bottom to top, the blood would flow into his palm." This is why the first opinion, which has the *kohen* walking around the altar and standing directly in front of each corner in succession, consistently applies *mechatei veyoreid* — always top-to-bottom — since every corner is the "nearest" one in turn.

### Seven Sprinklings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִזָּה עַל טׇהֳרוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ שֶׁבַע פְּעָמִים, וּשְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם הָיָה שׁוֹפֵךְ עַל יְסוֹד מַעֲרָבִי שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, וְשֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן הָיָה שׁוֹפֵךְ אֶל יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִי. אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ מִתְעָרְבִין בָּאַמָּה וְיוֹצְאִין לְנַחַל קִדְרוֹן, וְנִמְכָּרִין לַגַּנָּנִין לְזַבֵּל, וּמוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **He sprinkled** blood **on** the **pure** gold **of the altar seven times** after clearing away the ashes. **And he would pour the remainder of the blood on the western base of the outer altar.** On a related topic, the mishna teaches that **he would pour** the remaining blood of an offering, after it was sprinkled, **on the outer altar, on its southern base. These** remainders of blood from the outer altar **and those** remainders of blood from the inner altar are **mixed in the canal** beneath the altar **and flow out** with the water used to rinse the area **to the Kidron River.** This water was **sold to gardeners for** use as **fertilizer.** The gardeners paid for this water and thereby redeemed it from its sanctity. Failure to do so would render them guilty of **misuse** of consecrated property.

After completing the application on the four horns, the *kohen* sprinkles seven times on *taharo shel mizbeach* — the "purity" of the altar. This language is borrowed from the *pasuk* in Vayikra: *vetaharo vekidesho mitum'ot benei yisrael* — "and he shall purify it and sanctify it from the impurities of the Children of Israel." The precise location of these seven sprinklings will be discussed on subsequent dapim.

### Base Pouring

The remainder of the blood — *shirei hadam* — is poured at the base of the outer altar. Specifically, it is poured at *yesod hamaaravi shel mizbeach hachitzon* — the western base of the outer altar. This is because after completing the inner service, the *kohen* exits and arrives first at the western side, so the western base is the natural first point of contact. For the blood of the outer altar's own *chatas* service, the remainder is poured at *yesod hadromi* — the southern base — because the *kohen* descending the ramp of the outer altar arrives first at the southern base.

### Blood Disposal

*Elu va'elu mit'arvin ba'amah* — all the bloods, regardless of which base they were poured upon, would eventually merge and flow together through the trough and channel in the floor of the *azarah*, emerging into *nachal Kidron*. There, *venimkarim legananim* — they were sold to gardeners, as the blood was rich in nutrients and served as fertilizer. However, *umo'alin bahem* — until the gardeners actually paid for it, the blood retained its sanctified status and was subject to the prohibition of *me'ilah*. This will be discussed in a future *sugya*.

## Vayatzah Exegesis

### Moving Outside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְיָצָא אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? אָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ בְּפַר הַבָּא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת, שֶׁכֹּהֵן עוֹמֵד חוּץ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ וּמַזֶּה עַל הַפָּרוֹכֶת בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהוּא מַזֶּה, יָכוֹל אַף זֶה כֵּן,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught: "And he shall go out to the altar"** (Leviticus 16:18). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** this? Upon his exit, after sprinkling toward the curtain, the High Priest must necessarily reach the golden altar. **Rabbi Neḥemya said:** It is **because we find with regard to the bull brought for** a violation of **all the mitzvot,** i.e., the bull that must be brought in the event that the community errs with regard to any of the mitzvot, which is called the bull for an unwitting communal sin, **that when the priest sprinkles** toward the curtain **he stands past the altar and sprinkles** back **toward the curtain;** therefore, one **might** have thought **that here, too,** the rite should be performed in **the same** manner.

The *gemara* now examines the word *vayatzah* in the *pasuk* *veyatzah el hamizbeach asher lifnei Hashem*. A *baraita* is cited in the name of Rabbi Nechemiah: since we find by the *par ha'elam davar* — the communal ox brought for an inadvertent national sin — that *shehakohen omed chutz lamizbeach umazeh al haparochet besha'ah shehu mazeh* — "the *kohen* stands outside the altar and sprinkles toward the *parochet* while he is sprinkling" — one might think the same applies on *Yom Kippur*: that the *kohen gadol* must likewise stand outside the golden altar when he sprinkles toward the *parochet*. The *baraita* therefore teaches from *veyatzah el hamizbeach*: the *yetziah* — the going out — occurs only after the *parochet* sprinkling, not during it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְיָצָא אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״. הֵיכָן הָיָה — לִפְנִים מִן הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "And he shall go out to the altar." Where was he** before? He was **on the inner side of the altar,** i.e., the western side, close to the curtain, when he sprinkled, not on the outer side of the altar.

### Par Ha'Elam Davar

The *baraita* explains: until the moment of *veyatzah*, the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur* is *lifnim min hamizbeach* — on the inner side of the altar, standing between the altar and the *parochet*. From that position he performs the one-and-seven sprinklings directed toward the *parochet*. Only afterward does *veyatzah* apply — he exits to the outer zone to perform the service on the golden altar's horns. This stands in contrast to the *par he'elam davar*, where the *kohen* stands outside and sprinkles from there.

It is worth noting the spatial dimension here. The *heichal* is forty *amot* deep; the golden altar stands twenty *amot* in, and the *parochet* is at the far wall. Sprinklings directed toward the *parochet* across that distance — particularly according to Rabbi Eliezer's position that they must actually strike the *parochet* — present obvious practical challenges.

### Lifnei Hashem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״לִפְנֵי ה׳״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? אָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ בְּפַר וְשָׂעִיר שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים שֶׁכֹּהֵן עוֹמֵד לִפְנִים מִן הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וּמַזֶּה עַל הַפָּרוֹכֶת בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהוּא מַזֶּה, יָכוֹל אַף זֶה, כֵּן,
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in **another** *baraita*: With regard to the bull for an unwitting communal sin the verse states: "And he shall sprinkle seven times **before the Lord** in front of the curtain" (Leviticus 4:17). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states:** Before the Lord? **Rabbi Neḥemya said:** It is **because we find with regard to the bull and the goat of Yom Kippur that when he sprinkles** the High **Priest stands on the inner side of the altar,** close to the curtain, **and sprinkles toward the curtain;** therefore, one **might** have thought that **here, too,** in the case of the bull for an unwitting communal sin, it should be **the same** rite.

A second *baraita* addresses the phrase *asher lifnei Hashem* — "which is before God." Rabbi Nechemiah now takes this as a proof for the *par he'elam davar* context: since on *Yom Kippur* we know the *kohen* stands *lifnim min hamizbeach* — inside, between the altar and the *parochet* — and sprinkles while there, one might think the same applies by *par he'elam davar*. The second *baraita* derives from the phrase *mizbeach ketoret hasamim lifnei Hashem* — "the altar of spices that is before God" — that it is the *mizbeach* that is *lifnei Hashem*, not the *kohen*. The *kohen* by *par he'elam davar* therefore stands *chutz lamizbeach* — outside — and sprinkles from there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מִזְבַּח קְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֲשֶׁר בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ — מִזְבֵּחַ לִפְנֵי ה׳, וְאֵין כֹּהֵן לִפְנֵי ה׳. הָא כֵּיצַד? עוֹמֵד חוּץ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ וּמַזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And the priest shall put the blood upon the corners of the **altar of sweet incense before the Lord, which is in the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 4:7). This verse teaches that the **altar** is **before the Lord, but the priest is not before the Lord. How so?** The priest **stands on the outer side of the altar and sprinkles.**

### Yom Kippur Sequence

The distinction between the two services thus becomes fully articulated. On *Yom Kippur*, the *kohen gadol* performs the one-and-seven sprinklings toward the *parochet* while standing *inside* — between the altar and the *parochet* — and only after completing that stage does *veyatzah* occur, moving him to the outer position for the horn applications. By *par he'elam davar*, the *kohen* stands *outside* the altar for the seven sprinklings toward the *parochet*, reflecting the greater intimacy of the *Yom Kippur* service.

### Clarification

The *gemara* thus reads the sequence as follows: the *kohen gadol* performs the seven and one on the inner altar while standing *between* the altar and the *parochet*. Then, after those are complete, *veyatzah el hamizbeach* — he exits to the outer zone. This ordering is reinforced by the plain reading of the *pasuk*, which places *veyatzah* as a distinct subsequent act rather than a description of his position during the *parochet* service. The heightened intimacy of the *Yom Kippur* rite — the *kohen* positioned closer to the *kodesh kodashim* during the sprinkling — is a notable feature of the overall service.

## Starting Corner

### Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִתְחִיל מְחַטֵּא וְיוֹרֵד וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִתְחִיל מְחַטֵּא וְיוֹרֵד. מֵהֵיכָן הָיָה מַתְחִיל? מִקֶּרֶן מִזְרָחִית דְּרוֹמִית, דְּרוֹמִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מִזְרָחִית, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: מִקֶּרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית, דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **He began to cleanse** the altar, sprinkling the blood **downward. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **He began to cleanse downward. From where would he begin?** He would begin **from the southeast corner** of the altar, and from there he would turn to the **southwest** corner, onward to the **northwest** corner, and finally to the **northeast** corner. This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says:** He began **from the northeast corner,** from which he turned to the **northwest** corner, then to the **southwest** corner, and finished at the **southeast** corner.

The *gemara* now records a *baraita* debating which corner of the golden altar to begin with. Rabbi Akiva holds: *mikeren mizrachit dromit, dromit maaravit, maaravit tzonit, tzonit mizrachit* — southeast, southwest, northwest, northeast. This is a clockwise direction, beginning from the southeast.

### Rabbi Yose HaGalili

Rabbi Yose HaGalili holds: *mikeren mizrachit tzonit, tzonit maaravit, maaravit dromit, dromit mizrachit* — northeast, northwest, southwest, southeast. This is the counter-clockwise direction we encountered in the *mishnah*, beginning from the northeast.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי מַתְחִיל, שָׁם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא פּוֹסֵק. מָקוֹם שֶׁרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַתְחִיל, שָׁם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי פּוֹסֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: The place where the High Priest **begins** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** i.e., the northeast corner, **there** is where **Rabbi Akiva** says he **ends;** and the place where the High Priest **begins** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** the southeast corner, **there** is where **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** says he **ends.**

The *baraita* then draws an elegant symmetry: *makom she-Rabbi Yose HaGalili matchil, sham Rabbi Akiva posek* — where Rabbi Yose HaGalili begins, Rabbi Akiva ends; *makom she-Rabbi Akiva matchil, sham Rabbi Yose HaGalili posek* — where Rabbi Akiva begins, Rabbi Yose HaGalili ends.

### Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהָא, בְּהָהוּא קֶרֶן דְּפָגַע בְּרֵישָׁא — לָא עָבֵיד. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָצָא אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, עַד דְּנָפֵיק מִכּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Everyone** agrees, **in any case,** that **he does not perform** the service **at the corner that he encounters first.** Since the High Priest approaches the altar from the west side, the first corner he encounters is located on the west side. **What is the reason** for this? **Shmuel said:** The reason is **that the verse states: "And he shall go out to the altar,"** which indicates that he does not begin **until he goes out from** the sacred area beyond the **entire** area of the **altar.** At this stage, he is no longer on the western side of the altar but on its eastern side.

Before analyzing the disagreement, the *gemara* establishes a point of consensus: *dekulei alma miha be-hu kerren depaga beresha lo avid* — everyone agrees one does not begin with the first corner encountered upon exiting the altar. That first corner would be on the western side, since one exits from the inner zone toward the west. Both Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yose HaGalili begin on the eastern side.

### Exit Requirement

Why does no one begin with the first corner encountered? Shmuel explains: *veyatzah el hamizbeach* — *ad denafik mikulei mizbeach* — you must be completely outside the altar before commencing. The *vayatzah* is not merely directional guidance but a prerequisite: the *kohen* must fully exit the altar zone before he is permitted to begin the horn applications. The corners encountered on the western side, which one naturally passes in exiting, therefore cannot be the starting point.

### Counterclockwise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, נַקֵּיף דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין? לֵימָא בִּדְרָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל קָא מִיפַּלְגִי,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, let him go around via the right.** Since the High Priest is standing at the eastern side of the altar facing the west, the corner on his right is the northeast one. **Let us say** that **they disagree with regard to** the opinion **of Rami bar Yeḥezkel.**

The *gemara* then raises the question of why Rabbi Akiva goes clockwise at all. The general rule — derived by Rami bar Yechezkel from the *yam* (the great basin) constructed by Solomon — is that all turnings in the Temple are counter-clockwise. The *pasuk* in *Divrei Hayamim* (I Melachim) describes the twelve molten cattle supporting the *yam*: *shlosh panim tzonah, shlosh panim yamah, shlosh panim negbah, shlosh panim mizrachah* — three facing north, three west, three south, three east. Moving from north to west is counter-clockwise movement, establishing the principle: *shekol pinot sheata poneh lo yehu ela derech yamin lemizrach* — all turnings shall be only rightward, toward the east — that is, counter-clockwise as seen from the inside.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל: יָם שֶׁעָשָׂה שְׁלֹמֹה — ״עוֹמֵד עַל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר בָּקָר שְׁלֹשָׁה פוֹנִים צָפוֹנָה וּשְׁלֹשָׁה פוֹנִים יָמָּה וּשְׁלֹשָׁה פּוֹנִים נֶגְבָּה וּשְׁלֹשָׁה פּוֹנִים מִזְרָחָה וְהַיָּם עֲלֵיהֶם מִלְמָעְלָה וְכׇל אֲחוֹרֵיהֶם בָּיְתָה״. הָא לָמַדְתָּ, שֶׁכׇּל פִּינּוֹת שֶׁאַתָּה פּוֹנֶה לֹא יְהוּ אֶלָּא דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין, לַמִּזְרָח. מָר אִית לֵיהּ דְּרָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל, וּמָר לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rami bar Yeḥezkel said:** A verse describes **the sea,** the basin **that Solomon built,** in the following terms: **"It stood upon twelve oxen, three looking toward the north, and three looking toward the west, and three looking toward the south, and three looking toward the east; and the sea was set upon them above, and all their hinder parts were inward"** (II Chronicles 4:4). From the direction in which the text lists the groups of oxen under the basin, **you learn that all turns that you turn should be only to the right** and **to the east** side. Let us say that **this Sage,** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, **is of** the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the opinion **of Rami bar Yeḥezkel, and this Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **is of** the opinion that the ruling is **not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rami bar Yeḥezkel.**

The *gemara* considers whether Rabbi Akiva might simply reject this principle, but concludes otherwise: *dekulei alma it lehu de-Rami bar Yechezkel* — everyone accepts Rami bar Yechezkel's rule. The debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yose HaGalili is instead about whether the counter-clockwise rule derived from the *yam* — which stands outside in the *azarah* — applies equally *inside* the *heichal*. *Mar savar yalfinan panim mi-chutz* — one holds we apply the same rule to the inside; *umar savar lo yalfinan panim mi-chutz* — the other holds we do not learn the inner from the outer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל. וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: יָלְפִינַן פְּנִים מִחוּץ, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא יָלְפִינַן פְּנִים מִחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, everyone is of** the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the opinion of **Rami bar Yeḥezkel, but here they disagree with regard to this** matter: **One Sage,** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, **holds: We derive** the order of the rite **inside** the Sanctuary **from** the manner of the sprinkling on the **outside:** Just as the priest sprinkles on the corners of the outer altar in that order, he sprinkles similarly on the inner altar. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds: We do not derive** the order of the rite **inside** of the Sanctuary **from** the rite performed **outside.**

### Return Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא נְהִי דְּלָא יָלֵיף פְּנִים מִחוּץ, אִי בָּעֵי — הָכִי נַעֲבֵיד, אִי בָּעֵי — הָכִי נַעֲבֵיד? אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִדִּינָא בְּהָהוּא קֶרֶן דְּפָגַע בְּרֵישָׁא — בְּהָהוּא עָבֵיד בְּרֵישָׁא, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֵין מַעֲבִירִין עַל הַמִּצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, although he does not derive** the **inside from** the **outside,** nevertheless **if** the High Priest **wants, let him perform** the rite in **this** manner, and **if he wants, let him perform** the rite in **that** manner. Why must he begin specifically at the southeast corner? The Gemara answers: Indeed, **by right he should begin** sprinkling **by that corner** of the altar **that he reaches first, as Reish Lakish said: One does not pass on** an opportunity to perform **mitzvot.** If one has the chance to perform a mitzva, he should not put it off for later but should do it immediately.

This creates the obligation articulated by the *gemara*: *ve-keivan de-yahiv be-hahu keren, hadar asi le-hahu keren de-chayiv lemisav be-reisha* — once he has applied blood at whatever corner he begins with, he must then return to the corner he was forced to bypass first. Since that bypassed corner is the first one he actually encountered — and *ein ma'avirin al hamitzvot* obligates him to return to it — the corner he missed becomes the next obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי לָא עָבֵיד — מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיָצָא אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״, עַד דְּנָפֵיק מִכּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ. וְכֵיוָן דְּיָהֵיב בְּהָהוּא קֶרֶן — הָדַר אָתֵי לְהָהוּא קֶרֶן דְּאִיחַיַּיב לְמִיתַּב בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **And why does he not do** so; why does the High Priest not begin the sprinkling on one of the western corners? This is **due to** that **which is written: "He shall go out to the altar,"** meaning that he does not begin **until he goes out from** the area of the **entire altar. And since he presents** the blood **at that corner** on the outside of the altar, **he then comes to that corner** on the west side **where he should have presented** the blood **first.**

### Directional Logic

For Rabbi Yose HaGalili, who begins at the northeast, this is seamless: he was forced to bypass the western corners on his way out, begins at the northeast as the first available eastern corner, and can then proceed counter-clockwise (northwest, southwest, southeast) in the standard direction, naturally returning to what he missed.

For Rabbi Akiva, however, the forced bypass works differently. Rabbi Akiva begins at the southeast — and to return to the first corner missed after being forced fully outside requires moving in the clockwise direction. The counter-clockwise principle therefore does not override the obligation imposed by *ein ma'avirin al hamitzvot* once a bypass has already occurred. The apparent paradox of Rabbi Akiva's clockwise movement is thus resolved: it is not that he rejects the counter-clockwise rule, but rather that the obligation to return to the bypassed corner takes him in the opposite direction. What remains to be resolved — and will be addressed tomorrow — is the underlying dispute between the two opinions over why one begins in the north and the other in the south.
