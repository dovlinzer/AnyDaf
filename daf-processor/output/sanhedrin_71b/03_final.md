# Sanhedrin 71b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ben Sorer U-Moreh

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בָּרַח עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקִּיף זָקָן הַתַּחְתּוֹן – פָּטוּר. וְאִם מִשֶּׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ בָּרַח, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקִּיף זָקָן הַתַּחְתּוֹן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the rebellious son **ran away before he was sentenced, and afterward,** before he was caught, **his lower beard grew around,** he is **exempt** from the death penalty. Once his lower beard grows around his genitals, he can no longer be judged as a stubborn and rebellious son. **But if he ran away** only **after he was sentenced, and afterward,** by the time he was caught, **his lower beard had** already **grown around,** he is **liable** to receive the death penalty. Once he is sentenced to death his sentence remains in force.

The first *mishnah* on 71b reads: *barach ad she-lo nigmar dino ve-achar kach hikif zakan hatachton patur; ve-im mishe-nigmar dino barach ve-achar kach hikif zakan hatachton chayav*. That is, if the *ben sorer u-moreh* fled before his judgment was completed and subsequently aged past the eligible period, he is exempt; but if he fled only after the *gmar din* had already been rendered, he remains liable even after aging out of the relevant stage.

### Gmar Din Timing

The operative principle is straightforward: everything turns on when the *gmar din* occurred. The status of *ben sorer u-moreh* applies only during a narrow window of months. Once he ages out of that window, the entire *parshah* no longer applies to him. If, however, the court had already concluded its judgment — finding him guilty — while he was still within the eligible age, that determination follows him. Even if he escapes and is apprehended only years later, he is still executed, because the *gmar din* was rendered at the time he was *chayav*. But if no *gmar din* was reached during that period, and he subsequently aged out, there is nothing to execute him for — had he remained present, no sentence could have been passed.

## Ben Noach Conversion

### Initial Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָה: בֶּן נֹחַ שֶׁבֵּירַךְ אֶת הַשֵּׁם וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְגַּיֵּיר – פָּטוּר, הוֹאִיל וְנִשְׁתַּנָּה דִּינוֹ, נִשְׁתַּנֵּית מִיתָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** If **a descendant of Noah blessed,** i.e., cursed, **the name** of God, **and afterward he converted** to Judaism, he is **exempt** from punishment, **since his trial** procedure **changed, and the death** penalty to which **he** is subject also **changed.** A descendant of Noah is tried by a single judge, and he can be sentenced to death on the basis of the testimony of a single witness even without having been forewarned; whereas a Jew is tried by a court of twenty-three and can be executed only on the basis of the testimony of two witnesses, and this only after having been forewarned. A descendant of Noah is killed by the sword for this transgression, whereas a Jew is killed by stoning.

Against this backdrop, Rabbi Chanina introduces a parallel case involving a *ben Noach*. A non-Jew who cursed God — *beirach et Hashem*, always expressed as a euphemism — and subsequently converted to Judaism: what is his status? Rabbi Chanina rules that he is *patur*, exempt from punishment, for the following reason: *ho'il ve-nishtaneh dino ve-nishtaneh mitato* — since both the judicial process and the mode of execution have changed.

### Dinav & Mitato

Two distinct changes must be identified. The first is *dino* — the court process. For a non-Jew, capital adjudication requires only a single witness, a single judge, and no formal *hatra'ah* (warning). For a Jew, capital cases require a court of twenty-three, two witnesses, and proper warning. These are categorically different procedures. The second change is *mitato* — the manner of execution. The *gemara* later makes clear, per Rashi, that the standard execution for a *ben Noach* is *sayif* (beheading), while the punishment for a Jew who curses God is *sekilah* (stoning). So in the case of *birkat Hashem*, not only is the court process transformed upon conversion, but the very form of death changes as well.

### Rashi's Explanation

Rashi addresses both dimensions explicitly. On *nishtaneh dino*: *de-ilu me-ikara nidon be-echad ve-lo dayan echad ve-lo hatra'ah* — originally he was judged with a single witness, a single judge, and without warning. This is radically different from the Jewish capital process. On *nishtaneh mitato*: *de-kol mitat ben Noach sayif ve-atah ba ve-nidon be-sekilah* — every *ben Noach* punishment is by the sword, whereas now he would be judged by stoning. Since he was never condemned under that new framework, *ve-lo itchayav be-ha mita*, he was never rendered liable to *that* form of death, and therefore he is *patur*.

### Patur Reasoning

The logic of Rabbi Chanina's ruling, then, is not simply that something changed — it is that *both* the judicial framework *and* the execution method changed upon conversion. This dual transformation is what severs his prior liability. Had only one of these things changed, the case would look different. But where the entire structure of judgment and punishment is replaced, the act committed under the prior framework cannot straightforwardly generate liability under the new one.

## Proof Attempts

### First Part Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ: בָּרַח עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקִּיף זָקָן הַתַּחְתּוֹן – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן: הוֹאִיל וְאִישְׁתַּנִּי – אִישְׁתַּנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that the mishna **supports him:** If the rebellious son **ran away before he was sentenced, and afterward,** before he was caught, **his lower beard grew around,** he is **exempt** from the death penalty? **What is the reason** for this exemption? **Isn't it because we say: Since** his legal status **has changed,** his liability **has** also **changed?**

The *gemara* now attempts to derive support for Rabbi Chanina's ruling from the *mishnah* itself. The first part of the *mishnah* states that if the *ben sorer u-moreh* fled *before* the *gmar din* and then aged out, he is *patur*. Perhaps this supports Rabbi Chanina — in both cases, something changed before judgment was finalized, and in both cases, the person goes free.

### Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאִי עֲבַד הַשְׁתָּא – לָאו בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this claim: **No, it is different here,** with regard to a stubborn and rebellious son, **as were he now,** after he grew his lower beard, **to perform** the same actions that render one liable as a rebellious son, **he would not be fit for execution,** and therefore he is exempt. This is different from a gentile who cursed God's name and then converted, as even if he cursed God after having converted, he would still be liable to receive the death penalty.

The *gemara* rejects this parallel: *lo, shani hacha de-i avad hashta lav bar katala hu*. The cases are not comparable. The reason the *ben sorer u-moreh* is exempt when he ages out is categorically different: had he performed the same act *after* aging out, he would never have been liable in the first place. The offense is age-specific. By contrast, a *yehudi* who curses God is liable regardless of age, and similarly a *ben Noach* who curses God is liable as a non-Jew and would remain liable as a Jew. The *ben sorer u-moreh* case cannot be analogized to the *ben Noach* case precisely because in the former, the very act loses its criminal character once the age has passed, whereas in the latter, the criminal character persists across both identities — only the procedural and punitive framework differs.

### Second Part Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אִם מִשֶּׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ בָּרַח, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקִּיף זָקָן הַתַּחְתּוֹן – חַיָּיב. נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ קָאָמְרַתְּ? נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ גַּבְרָא קְטִילָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the latter half of the mishna: But **if he ran away** only **after he was sentenced, and afterward,** by the time he was caught, **his lower beard had** already **grown around,** he is **liable** to receive the death penalty. This indicates that even though his legal status has changed, he is nevertheless executed. The Gemara rejects this argument: There is no proof from here; **you speak** of a case where **he was** already **sentenced** to death. Once **he was** already **sentenced, he is** considered **a dead man** and his verdict can no longer be changed.

The *gemara* then attempts to derive support from the *second* part of the *mishnah*: *im mishe-nigmar dino barach ve-achar kach hikif zakan hatachton chayav*. If the *gmar din* occurred while the *ben sorer u-moreh* was still eligible, and he then fled and aged out, he is nonetheless *chayav*. This now seems to challenge Rabbi Chanina, because the implication is that a judgment rendered under the earlier circumstances follows a person even when those circumstances change. The *ben Noach*, by contrast, gets off even though he committed a clear transgression — suggesting an inconsistency.

### Challenge to Proof

The *gemara* responds: *gmar din kaamart? Hatam gavra katila hu* — you invoke *gmar din*? There, the man was already a condemned person. A *gmar din* had been rendered; the sentence was fixed and only awaited execution. In the *ben Noach* case, there was no *din* at all — he was never brought to court, never adjudicated. You cannot derive a conclusion about what happens when circumstances change *after judgment* and apply it to a case where there was no judgment at all. The two situations are structurally incomparable, and therefore neither the first nor the second part of the *mishnah* settles the question of Rabbi Chanina's ruling.

## Ben Noach & Adultery

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בֶּן נֹחַ שֶׁהִכָּה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, וּבָא עַל אֵשֶׁת חֲבֵירוֹ, וְנִתְגַּיֵּיר – פָּטוּר. עָשָׂה כֵּן בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וְנִתְגַּיֵּיר – חַיָּיב. וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִישְׁתַּנִּי – אִישְׁתַּנִּי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof from what was stated in a *baraita*: If **a descendant of Noah struck** and killed **another** gentile **or engaged in intercourse with the wife of another** gentile, **and he** then **converted** to Judaism, he is **exempt.** But if **he did this to an Israelite,** killing a Jew or engaging in intercourse with the wife of a Jew, **and** then **converted,** he is **liable. But why** should this be so? **Let us say** that **since** his legal status **changed,** his liability **changed** as well, and therefore he should no longer be subject to the death penalty. The *baraita* indicates that this rationale is not accepted.

The *gemara* turns to a new set of cases, now working within the *dinim* of *ben Noach* directly. *Ben Noach she-hika et chaveiro u-va al eshet chaveiro ve-nitgayer — patur*. A non-Jew who struck another person or committed adultery with another's wife and then converted is exempt. *Asa ken be-Yisrael ve-nitgayer — chayav*. But if he committed these acts against a Jew and then converted, he is liable. The question the *gemara* raises is why: if conversion changes his *din* and his *mitah*, should he not be *patur* in both scenarios?

### Why No Patur?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינוֹ וּמִיתָתוֹ בָּעֵינַן, וְהַאי – דִּינוֹ אִישְׁתַּנִּי, מִיתָתוֹ לָא אִישְׁתַּנַּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: There is no comparison between the cases, because in order to nullify one's death penalty **we require** a change both in **his trial** procedure **and** in the specific **death** penalty to which he is subject. **But this** descendant of Noah, though **his trial** procedure **changed,** the **death** penalty to which he is subject **did not change.**

The answer rests on the principle already established by Rabbi Chanina: *nishtaneh dino ve-nishtaneh mitato* — *both* the court process *and* the mode of execution must change. We can readily concede that the court process changes upon conversion — a Jew faces a fundamentally different judicial framework. The question is whether the *mitah* also changes. *Ve-ha dino ishtani, mitato lo ishtani* — the *din* changed, but the *mitah* did not. If the mode of execution remains the same whether he is judged as a non-Jew or as a Jew, then only one of the two required elements has changed, and that is insufficient to exempt him.

### Murder vs. Adultery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רוֹצֵחַ, מֵעִיקָּרָא סַיִיף וְהַשְׁתָּה סַיִיף. אֶלָּא אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, מֵעִיקָּרָא סַיִיף וְהַשְׁתָּה חֶנֶק?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Granted,** with regard to the case of **a murderer,** this answer is understandable, as **initially,** while he was still a gentile, he was subject to death by the **sword, and now** that he is a Jew he is still subject to death by the **sword.** Consequently, there is no change in the punishment to which he is subject. **But** with regard to the case of one who engaged in intercourse with **a married woman, initially** when he was still a gentile he was subject to death by the **sword, but now** that he is a Jew he is subject to death by **strangulation.** Accordingly, there is a change both in his trial procedure and in the type of capital punishment to which he is subject.

The *gemara* considers the two cases separately. *Bishlama rotzeach* — the murder case is relatively straightforward. As a *ben Noach*, murder is punished by *sayif*. As a Jew, murder is also punished by *sayif*. The *mitah* is unchanged. Therefore, since neither the court process change alone nor any change in punishment suffices — only both together — the converted murderer remains *chayav*.

Adultery with a married woman — *eshet ish* — presents a greater difficulty. As a *ben Noach*, the adultery would be punished by *sayif*. But as a Jew, adultery with an *eshet ish* is punished by *chenek* (strangulation). So here, unlike the murder case, the mode of execution *does* change. Given that both *dino* and *mitato* have changed, why is the convert still *chayav*?

### Eshet Ish Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה, דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The case of a married woman mentioned here involves a man who engaged in intercourse **with a betrothed young woman, so that** in both **this** case, where he engaged in intercourse with her when he was still a descendant of Noah, **and** in **that** case, where he did so when he was already a Jew, he is subject to death **by stoning.** Since there was no change in the type of capital punishment to which he is subject, he remains liable and is executed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״עָשָׂה כֵּן בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״, דּוּמְיָא דְּאֵשֶׁת חֲבֵירוֹ קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **But** the *baraita* **teaches** the case where **he did this to an Israelite,** i.e., engaged in intercourse with the wife of a Jew, **similar to** the case where he engaged in intercourse with **the wife of another** gentile. Both cases seem to be addressing identical circumstances, and the unique *halakha* governing a betrothed young woman exists only for Jews, not for descendants of Noah. Accordingly, the *baraita* must be discussing the case of a fully married woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, קַלָּה בַּחֲמוּרָה מֵישָׁךְ שָׁיְיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the lenient** punishment **is included in the severe** one. If initially, when he was a gentile, he was liable to receive a more severe punishment, and now that he is a Jew he is liable to receive a more lenient punishment, the lenient punishment is included in the severe one, and he remains liable to receive the lenient punishment. By contrast, if the punishment that he is now liable to receive is more severe, he is exempt from all punishment.

The resolution lies in a careful examination of *kal v'chomer* and the ordering of the *mitot*. The established hierarchy, according to the *Chachamim*, places *sekilah* as the most severe, followed by *sereifah*, then *hereg* (*sayif*), and then *chenek*. On this ordering, going from *sayif* to *chenek* is a *downgrade* — the punishment becomes *lighter*. The principle is *kala be-chamura mishtachacha*: a lighter punishment is subsumed within a heavier one. If one was already liable to the more severe *sayif*, one is certainly still liable when the punishment is the lesser *chenek*. Because the change in *mitah* is a downgrade rather than an upgrade, it does not constitute a sufficient *nishtaneh mitato* to exempt him. He is still liable.

The answer to the adultery case with a *na'arah me'orasah* (a betrothed young woman) is different. A *na'arah me'orasah* is subject to *sekilah* — both before conversion and after. Since both *dino* and *mitato* remain effectively the same (*sekilah* to *sekilah*), there is no transformation sufficient to exempt him. And crucially, non-Jews have no halachic category of *na'arah me'orasah* — betrothal has no analogous legal structure in Noachide law. Therefore, when the text speaks of a *ben Noach* committing adultery with another's wife, this cannot refer to a non-Jewish *na'arah me'orasah*. By elimination, it must refer to a fully married woman, an *eshet ish*, which brings us back to the *sayif*-to-*chenek* scenario — a downgrade that does not suffice for exemption.

## Kal V'Chomer

### Mitot Ordering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי: ״סַיִיף חָמוּר״, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר: ״חֶנֶק חָמוּר״, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that death by the **sword is** more **severe** than death by strangulation. Therefore, if a descendant of Noah engaged in intercourse with a married woman, so that he was subject to death by the sword, and then he converted to Judaism, so that his sentence was lightened to death by strangulation, he is still executed. **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says** that death by **strangulation is more severe** than death by the sword, **what can be said?** The convert should now be exempt from all punishment.

The question of whether a change in *mitah* is sufficient to exempt a converted offender depends entirely on the *direction* of that change. The *Chachamim* order the four capital punishments from most to least severe: *sekilah*, *sereifah*, *hereg* (*sayif*), *chenek*. A *ben Noach* is always subject to *sayif*, which sits above *chenek* on this scale. Moving from *sayif* to *chenek* upon conversion is therefore a downgrade — and a downgrade is considered subsumed within the original, heavier sentence. You cannot say *nishtaneh mitato* in a meaningful sense when the conversion results in a *lesser* penalty.

### Downgrade vs. Upgrade

The logic runs as follows: if one was already held liable to *sayif*, then being now liable to *chenek* — a lighter form of execution — does not constitute a change sufficient to sever that liability. Conversely, if the *mitah* were to *upgrade* — that is, if conversion would subject him to a *more* severe form of execution — then we would genuinely say *nishtaneh mitato*, and in conjunction with *nishtaneh dino*, he would be *patur*. The rule is therefore: a downgrade in *mitah* is not enough; an upgrade, where the new punishment is genuinely worse than the original, is what triggers the exemption.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר לַהּ כְּתַנָּא דְבֵי מְנַשֶּׁה, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל מִיתָה הָאֲמוּרָה לִבְנֵי נֹחַ אֵינָהּ אֶלָּא חֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the *tanna* of the school of Menashe, who says: Any death mentioned in connection with the descendants of Noah is nothing other than** death by **strangulation,** and not death by the sword. Accordingly, if a descendant of Noah engaged in intercourse with a married woman, and he then converted, his punishment did not become more severe with his conversion; rather, it stayed the same, death by strangulation, and he is still executed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, מֵעִיקָּרָא חֶנֶק וְהַשְׁתָּה חֶנֶק. אֶלָּא רוֹצֵחַ, מֵעִיקָּרָא חֶנֶק וְהַשְׁתָּה סַיִיף? קַלָּה בַּחֲמוּרָה מֵישָׁךְ שָׁיְיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Granted,** with regard to the case of **a married woman,** this answer is understandable. **Initially,** when he was still a descendant of Noah, he was subject to death by **strangulation, and now,** after his conversion, he is subject to death by **strangulation. But** as for **a murderer, initially,** when he was still a descendant of Noah, according to the *tanna* of the school of Menashe he was subject to death by **strangulation, but now,** after his conversion, he is subject to death by the **sword.** The Gemara explains: **The lenient** punishment **is included in the severe** one, and according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, death by strangulation is more severe than death by the sword. Consequently, he is executed.

This entire analysis, however, is complicated by Rabbi Shimon's position. Rabbi Shimon holds that the execution method for *bnei Noach* is always *chenek* — *kol mitah amura be-vnei Noach einah ela chenek*. This stems from a *baraita* from the school of Menashya. If Rabbi Shimon is correct, then the *ben Noach* who committed adultery starts at *chenek*, and as a Jew committing adultery with an *eshet ish* would also face *chenek* — same *mitah*, no change, and he would be *chayav*. That part works. But the murder case now becomes a problem. According to Rabbi Shimon, the *ben Noach* murderer starts at *chenek*; but a Jewish murderer is executed by *sayif*, which is *more* severe than *chenek*. That would be an upgrade — and an upgrade, as established, generates *nishtaneh mitato* and should exempt him.

### Murder Reconciliation

The resolution for Rabbi Shimon's view on the murder case returns to the *kal v'chomer* logic, but now running in the opposite direction. According to the *Chachamim*, *ben Noach* is *sayif* and Jewish murderer is *sayif* — no change, remains *chayav*. According to Rabbi Shimon, *ben Noach* is *chenek* and the Jewish murderer would be *sayif* — *chenek* to *sayif* is a downgrade (since *sayif* is more severe than *chenek* on Rabbi Shimon's ordering where *chenek* is actually the mildest). Wait — here the reasoning must be tracked carefully: Rabbi Shimon holds *chenek* is *chamur* — the *most severe* — which inverts the *Chachamim*'s scale. If that is his position, then moving from *chenek* (most severe, for the *ben Noach*) to *sayif* (less severe) for the Jewish murderer is a *downgrade* even on Rabbi Shimon's scale, and a downgrade does not constitute *nishtaneh mitato*. He therefore remains *chayav*. A *baraita* is cited as supporting the *Chachamim*'s position: *tanya kavatei de-rabanan de-amrinan kala be-chamura*.

The case of *eshet ish* under Rabbi Shimon similarly resolves: since he holds all *ben Noach* execution is *chenek*, and adultery with an *eshet ish* as a Jew is also *chenek*, the *mitah* does not change at all — and he remains *chayav*.

## Ben Azzai

### Preemptive Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה נִידּוֹן עַל שֵׁם סוֹפוֹ. יָמוּת זַכַּאי וְאַל יָמוּת חַיָּיב, שֶׁמִּיתָתָן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים – הֲנָאָה לָהֶן וַהֲנָאָה לָעוֹלָם. לַצַּדִּיקִים – רַע לָהֶן וְרַע לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A stubborn and rebellious son is sentenced** to death not because of the severity of the transgression that he already committed but **on account of his ultimate end,** because a boy of his nature will grow up to lead an immoral life, and it is better that **he should die** while he is still **innocent,** before causing excessive harm, **and not die** after he becomes **guilty.** This is **because the death of the wicked is beneficial to them,** because they can no longer sin, **and** it is also **beneficial to the world,** which is now rid of those who do it harm. Conversely, the death **of the righteous is detrimental to them,** as they can no longer engage in the performance of mitzvot, **and** it is also **detrimental to the world,** as the righteous are now absent from it.

Ben Azzai articulates the underlying rationale of the *ben sorer u-moreh* ruling: *yamut zakai ve-al yamut chayav* — let him die relatively innocent rather than die later when he is truly guilty. The punishment is not simply retrospective accountability for what he has already done; it is preemptive. The *Torah* foresees where this trajectory leads and authorizes his execution before he has descended further into criminality.

### Reshaim & Tzadikim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַיִן וְשֵׁינָה לָרְשָׁעִים – הֲנָאָה לָהֶן וַהֲנָאָה לָעוֹלָם, וְלַצַּדִּיקִים – רַע לָהֶן וְרַע לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** By way of association, the mishna continues: The **wine and sleep of the wicked are beneficial to them and beneficial to the world,** as when they are sleeping or under the influence of wine, they do not cause harm to others. **And,** conversely, the wine and sleep **of the righteous are detrimental to them and detrimental to the world,** as wine and sleep prevent them from engaging in their good deeds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּיזּוּר לָרְשָׁעִים – הֲנָאָה לָהֶן וַהֲנָאָה לָעוֹלָם. וְלַצַּדִּיקִים – רַע לָהֶן וְרַע לָעוֹלָם. כִּנּוּס לָרְשָׁעִים – רַע לָהֶן וְרַע לָעוֹלָם. וְלַצַּדִּיקִים – הֲנָאָה לָהֶן וַהֲנָאָה לָעוֹלָם. שֶׁקֶט לָרְשָׁעִים – רַע לָהֶן וְרַע לָעוֹלָם. לַצַּדִּיקִים – הֲנָאָה לָהֶן וַהֲנָאָה לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The **dispersal of the wicked,** so that they are not found in close proximity to each other, **is beneficial to them,** as they are less likely to provoke each other to sin, **and** it is **beneficial to the world.** The dispersal **of the righteous is detrimental to them and detrimental to the world.** The **assembly of the wicked** in one place **is detrimental to them and detrimental to the world,** while the assembly **of the righteous is beneficial to them and beneficial to the world.** The **tranquility of the wicked is detrimental to them and detrimental to the world,** while the tranquility **of the righteous is beneficial to them and beneficial to the world.**

The *mishnah* continues with a series of contrasts between *reshaim* and *tzaddikim*. The death of the wicked is beneficial — *mitatan shel reshaim hana'ah lahem ve-hana'ah la-olam* — both for themselves, because their sinning stops and they do not accumulate further guilt, and for the world, because others are spared the consequences of their continued wrongdoing. The converse holds for *tzaddikim*: their death is a loss for them and for the world. Wine and sleep are beneficial for *reshaim* — at least while drinking or sleeping they are not actively causing harm — and harmful for *tzaddikim*, who are prevented from performing *mitzvot*. Dispersion of *reshaim* is good, because it prevents them from organizing for evil; dispersion of *tzaddikim* is harmful, because it deprives them of communal support. Conversely, the gathering of *reshaim* is destructive, while the gathering of *tzaddikim* is beneficial.

### Rabbi Yose HaGlili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָכַל זֶה תַּרְטֵימָר בָּשָׂר וְשָׁתָה חֲצִי לוֹג יַיִן הָאִיטַלְקִי אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה יֵצֵא לְבֵית דִּין לִיסָּקֵל? אֶלָּא הִגִּיעָה תּוֹרָה לְסוֹף דַּעְתּוֹ שֶׁל בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, שֶׁסּוֹף מְגַמֵּר נִכְסֵי אָבִיו וּמְבַקֵּשׁ לִמּוּדוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מוֹצֵא, וְיוֹצֵא לְפָרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים וּמְלַסְטֵם אֶת הַבְּרִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that further elaborates upon the words of the mishna: **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says:** Is it simply **due to** the fact **that** the boy **ate a *tarteimar* of meat and drank a half-*log* of Italian wine** that **the Torah states** that **he shall be taken out to court to be stoned? Rather, the Torah penetrated the ultimate mind-set of the stubborn and rebellious son** and the inevitable results of his actions, and it is understood **that** he will continue on this path, and **in the end he will squander his father's property, and** then, **seeking** the pleasures **to which he had become accustomed but not finding** them, **he will go out to the crossroads and rob people.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: יָמוּת זַכַּאי וְאַל יָמוּת חַיָּיב, שֶׁמִּיתָתָן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים – הֲנָאָה לָהֶם וַהֲנָאָה לָעוֹלָם, וְלַצַּדִּיקִים – רַע לָהֶם וְרַע לָעוֹלָם. שֵׁינָה וְיַיִן לָרְשָׁעִים – הֲנָאָה לָהֶם וַהֲנָאָה לָעוֹלָם, לַצַּדִּיקִים – רַע לָהֶם וְרַע לָעוֹלָם. שֶׁקֶט לָרְשָׁעִים – רַע לָהֶם וְרַע לָעוֹלָם, וְלַצַּדִּיקִים – הֲנָאָה לָהֶם וַהֲנָאָה לָעוֹלָם. פִּיזּוּר לָרְשָׁעִים – הֲנָאָה לָהֶם וַהֲנָאָה לָעוֹלָם, וְלַצַּדִּיקִים – רַע לָהֶם וְרַע לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Torah said** that it is better that **he should die** now when he is still **innocent, and** he should **not die** later when he is **guilty.** This is **because the death of the wicked is beneficial to them and** also **beneficial to the world,** while the death **of the righteous is detrimental to them and detrimental to the world.** The **sleep and wine of the wicked is beneficial to them and beneficial to the world,** while that **of the righteous is detrimental to them and detrimental to the world.** The **tranquility of the wicked is detrimental to them and detrimental to the world,** while the tranquility **of the righteous is beneficial to them and beneficial to the world.** The **dispersal of the wicked is beneficial to them and beneficial to the world,** while the dispersal **of the righteous is detrimental to them and detrimental to the world.**

Rabbi Yose HaGlili expands on the preemptive logic. Is it really conceivable, he asks, that a boy should be put to death merely because he ate a *tartimar* of meat and drank half a *log* of Italian wine? *Ela higiah ha-Torah le-sof da'ato shel ben sorer u-moreh* — rather, the *Torah* traces the endpoint of his trajectory. He will eventually exhaust his father's estate; when his money runs out he will take to the crossroads to rob people, and if confronted he will kill. *Amrah Torah: yamut zakai ve-al yamut chayav* — let the *Torah* intervene now, while he is still relatively innocent, rather than wait for him to commit the crimes that will make his guilt complete.

## Machteret Case

### Nidon Al Shem Sofo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבָּא בַּמַּחְתֶּרֶת נִידּוֹן עַל שֵׁם סוֹפוֹ. הָיָה בָּא בַּמַּחְתֶּרֶת וְשָׁבַר אֶת הֶחָבִית, אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ דָּמִים – חַיָּיב, אִם אֵין לוֹ דָּמִים – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A burglar** who is found breaking into a house may be killed by the owner of the house with impunity (see Exodus 22:1). He too is **sentenced on account of his ultimate end,** as it is presumed that if the owner of the house would resist the burglar, the burglar would kill the owner of the house. If the burglar **was breaking into** a house, **and** in the course of doing so **he broke a barrel, if there is blood**-guiltiness **for** killing **him,** i.e., if the homeowner would be liable for killing him, the burglar is **liable** to pay for the value of the barrel. An example of this is if a father broke into his son's house, in which case it is presumed that even if the son resists his father, his father would never kill him, and therefore the son may not kill his father, and if he does so he is liable. **If there is no blood**-guiltiness **for** killing **him,** i.e., if the homeowner would be exempt from punishment for killing him, the burglar is **exempt** from paying for the barrel.

The *mishnah* introduces another instance of *nidon al shem sofo* — being judged on the basis of one's ultimate destination rather than one's immediate act: *haba ba-machteret nidon al shem sofo*. The thief who tunnels into a home is judged based on what he is presumed to be heading toward. The *Torah* states: *im ba-machteret yimatzei ha-ganav ve-hukah va-met ein lo damim* — if the thief is found in the act of tunneling and is struck and killed, the homeowner bears no blood-guilt.

### Permission to Kill

This dispensation is remarkable: the thief has not yet killed anyone, and yet the homeowner is permitted to kill him. The justification is *nidon al shem sofo* — he is judged by where his path leads. The assumption is that if confronted, the thief will kill to protect himself and complete his theft. Because of this presumed lethal intent, the homeowner is authorized to act preemptively.

The clause *im zarchah ha-shemesh alav — damim lo, shalem yeshalem* is cited in contrast: if the "sun shines on him" — which the *gemara* will explain not literally but as a conceptual modifier — then the homeowner would be liable for killing him, and the thief must make full restitution for what he has stolen or broken.

### Damages Liability

The *mishnah* raises a further question about civil liability: *haba ba-machteret she-shibbar et ha-chavit — yesh lo damim chayav, ein lo damim patur*. If the tunneling thief broke a barrel in the course of his entry, does he owe damages? The answer depends on his *damim* status — whether there would have been liability for killing him. This invokes the principle of *kam lei bi-derabba mineih*: a person subject to the greater punishment (death) is not also assessed the lesser punishment (financial liability) for acts committed in the same episode. If the thief was in a situation where the homeowner would have been permitted — indeed authorized — to kill him, then he is subject to the death penalty in principle, and therefore cannot simultaneously be required to pay for the barrel he broke. Conversely, if the homeowner would not have been permitted to kill him — if there was no *ein lo damim* — then no death penalty applies, and he is fully liable for the property damage.

### Raba's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַחְתֶּרֶת? חֲזָקָה אֵין אָדָם מַעֲמִיד עַצְמוֹ עַל מָמוֹנוֹ, וְהַאי מֵימָר אָמַר: אִי אָזֵילְנָא, קָאֵי לְאַפַּאי וְלָא שָׁבֵיק לִי, וְאִי קָאֵי לְאַפַּאי – קָטֵילְנָא לֵיהּ. וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: אִם בָּא לְהוֹרְגְּךָ – הַשְׁכֵּם לְהוֹרְגוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava says: What is the reason for** this *halakha* concerning **a burglar** who breaks into a house? He explains: There is **a presumption** that **a person does not restrain himself** when faced **with** losing **his money, and** therefore **this** burglar **must have said** to himself: **If I go** in and the owner sees me, he will **rise against me and not allow me** to steal from him, **and if he rises against me, I will kill him. And the Torah stated** a principle: **If** someone **comes to kill you, rise and kill him** first.

Raba explains the underlying reasoning of the entire *machteret* dispensation: *chazakah ein adam maamid atzmo al mamono* — it is a presumption that a person will not stand passively by while his property is stolen. The thief understands this. His internal reasoning is: *ei azilna kayei appai lo shavik li* — if I go in, the owner will confront me and not simply let me take his property. *Ve-chi kayei appai katilna lei* — and if he confronts me, I will kill him. It is this presumed murderous intent — embedded in the very act of tunneling — that transforms the thief into a *rodef*, a pursuer, in the eyes of the *Torah*. The principle *ba le-horgecha hashkem le-horgo* then applies: one who comes to kill you, you may preempt and kill first. The *Torah*'s dispensation of *ein lo damim* therefore rests on the presumption that the homeowner, by encountering the thief, has effectively entered a life-threatening confrontation.
