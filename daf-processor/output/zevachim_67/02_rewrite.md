# Zevachim 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Olah/Chatat Debate

### Mishnah Overview

The Mishnah that opens this section of the Gemara presents an extended debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua concerning the laws of *me'ilah* as they apply to bird sacrifices. The basic framework is as follows: when a bird *chatat* is performed correctly, the *kohanim* are permitted to eat it, and consequently it is not subject to *me'ilah* — the prohibition against deriving benefit from consecrated property. The *olah*, by contrast, is never eaten and therefore retains its *me'ilah* status throughout. If, however, either bird sacrifice is performed incorrectly such that even the *chatat* cannot be eaten, both birds remain under *me'ilah*, since no one is permitted to benefit from them.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Theory

Into this straightforward framework Rabbi Yehoshua introduces a striking and counterintuitive position. He maintains that if one performs a bird *olah* as though it were a *chatat* — bringing it below the altar rather than above, performing the *melikah* on only one *siman* rather than two, and directing the act toward the name of a *chatat* — this is sufficient to shift the bird out of the category of an *olah* and place it within the category of a *chatat*, such that *me'ilah* no longer applies to it.

The obvious objection is that the bird was sanctified as an *olah*. When a person consecrated it, days or weeks before presenting it at the Temple, they designated it as an *olah*. How can the *kohen*'s subsequent conduct at the altar redefine it as something else entirely? This is precisely Rabbi Eliezer's difficulty — and it is the tension that the Gemara now undertakes to resolve.

---

## Eliezer's Challenge

### Asham Counterexample

The Gemara introduces a *brayta* in which Rabbi Eliezer presses Rabbi Yehoshua with a series of counterexamples, each designed to show that performing an act differently does not suffice to recategorize a sacrifice. The first example: one slaughtered an *asham* in the north — the correct location for an *asham* — but with the intent of a *shelamim*. Rabbi Eliezer argues that this constitutes a *shinuy shem*, a change of name or designation, and yet *me'ilah* still applies. The implication is that if changing the designation alone does not strip a sacrifice of *me'ilah*, neither should it for the *olah* that Rabbi Yehoshua is discussing.

It is worth noting, as Rashi observes, that this is already an imperfect counterexample. By an *asham*, once the *zerikah* — the sprinkling of the blood — has been performed, *me'ilah* no longer applies to the meat in any case. So the presence of *me'ilah* in this scenario is only temporary, and the analogy to the *olah* is not precise. Nevertheless, the Gemara follows the argument through.

### Yehoshua's Response

Rabbi Yehoshua replies that the comparison is flawed. In Rabbi Eliezer's case of the *asham* slaughtered in the north for the sake of a *shelamim*, the location was not changed — it was still slaughtered in the north, which is the proper place for an *asham*, not a *shelamim*. By contrast, in Rabbi Yehoshua's own case, not only did he change the name, he changed the *makom*, performing the *olah* below in the place designated for a *chatat*. The question is therefore put back to Rabbi Eliezer: can you show me a case where both name and location were changed and there is still *me'ilah*?

### Second Counterexample

Rabbi Eliezer responds with a refined example: an *asham* slaughtered in the south — the place of a *shelamim* — with the intent of a *shelamim*. Now both the name and the location have been changed. Rabbi Eliezer argues that this constitutes a *shinuy shem* and a *shinuy makom* together, and yet *me'ilah* still applies. Therefore, even Rabbi Yehoshua's case of the *olah* done below in the manner of a *chatat* should retain its *me'ilah*.

### Ma'aseh Distinction

Rabbi Yehoshua responds once more, and here he introduces his sharpest distinction. In Rabbi Eliezer's new counterexample, he concedes, you have changed both name and location. But *v'lo shinah ma'asav* — you have not changed the actual *ma'aseh*, the act itself. The manner in which you slaughtered the *asham* is identical to the manner in which you would slaughter any large animal; the act of *shechitah* is the same regardless of intent or location. In Rabbi Yehoshua's case, however, the *ma'aseh* was also changed: the *melikah* was performed on only one *siman* rather than two, which is precisely the act of a *chatat*, not an *olah*. It is this complete recapitulation of the *chatat* act — name, location, and *ma'aseh* together — that accomplishes the recategorization.

---

## Rava's Analysis

### Me'ilah Distinction

Rava pauses to examine Rabbi Eliezer's counterexample more carefully and raises a conceptual difficulty. He observes that the comparison between an *asham* and a *shelamim* with respect to *me'ilah* is peculiar. Both an *asham* and a *shelamim* share a similar *me'ilah* structure: before *zerikah*, there is *me'ilah*, and after *zerikah*, there is not. So arguing that changing an *asham* into a *shelamim* fails to remove *me'ilah* is not a useful counterexample to Rabbi Yehoshua's position. Rabbi Yehoshua's position is specifically about removing *me'ilah* from an *olah* by redesignating it as a *chatat*; the *chatat* is a sacrifice from which *me'ilah* is absent entirely, which is what makes the redesignation meaningful.

### Zerikah Problem

The more fundamental problem with Rabbi Eliezer's counterexample, Rava notes, is that it asks about a stage before *zerikah* has occurred. Of course there is still *me'ilah* at that point — there is *me'ilah* at that point even in Rabbi Yehoshua's case, which concerns what happens after the *zerikah* or the equivalent completion of the *chatat* process. Rabbi Eliezer's example, in other words, is comparing apples to oranges. After *zerikah* of an *asham*, there is no *me'ilah* either — so the example proves nothing about whether redesignation can remove *me'ilah*.

One might then ask: why did the Gemara allow this exchange to proceed at all? The answer is that the conversation was serving a different purpose. What Rabbi Eliezer was groping toward was the broader question of whether any large-animal sacrifice could be fully redesignated through changed conduct — not merely whether the specific *me'ilah* comparison was apt.

### Concession Moment

Rava draws a striking conclusion from the fact that Rabbi Eliezer did not further press the counterexample by adding yet a third point of difference — for instance, specifying that the *asham* was done also with intention for a different owner. The Gemara notes: *mid'lo ka'amar leh hachi, shema mina nachit Rabbi Eliezer l'ta'ameh* — from the fact that he did not say so, we learn that Rabbi Eliezer came to understand Rabbi Yehoshua's reasoning. The light went on, as it were. Rabbi Eliezer realized that Rabbi Yehoshua's claim was not simply "if you do enough strange things to a sacrifice, *me'ilah* disappears." Rather, Rabbi Yehoshua's claim was that the *olah* had been genuinely recategorized — and Rabbi Eliezer understood that with large-animal sacrifices, where the act of *shechitah* is always formally identical regardless of intent or location, no genuine recategorization is possible. The act itself never changes. Therefore, Rabbi Eliezer had no valid counterexample — not because he conceded Rabbi Yehoshua's point, but because he recognized that the two cases are structurally incomparable.

## Critical Moment Theory

### The Gap Concept

The Gemara now introduces a sharper formulation of Rabbi Yehoshua's position, attributed to Rav Adda bar Ahava. Until now, Rabbi Yehoshua's view had been understood as: if you perform enough acts consistent with a *chatat*, the *olah* gets redesignated as a *chatat*. But Rav Adda bar Ahava sharpens the reasoning considerably. The key is not the number of *chatat*-like acts performed, but a specific moment in time.

The *olah* of a bird requires that *both* simanim be cut in the *melikah*. The *chatat* of a bird requires that *only one siman* be cut. When a bird *olah* is being performed below, for the sake of a *chatat*, and the *kohen* cuts one *siman*, a specific and decisive moment has arrived. From the *chatat* perspective, the *melikah* is complete — this is exactly what a *chatat melikah* looks like. From the *olah* perspective, the *melikah* is not yet complete — you have only cut one *siman* and have yet to cut the second.

### Siman Echad Logic

This distinction is what generates the "gap." At the moment when one *siman* has been cut, the *chatat melikah* has already occurred, while the *olah melikah* has not yet occurred. The bird's identity as an *olah* has not yet been fully established through the act of *melikah*, because the defining act of an *olah melikah* — the cutting of both *simanim* — is still incomplete. The *chatat* definition, however, can already be said to have taken hold, because its defining act is already done.

### Two Lenses Framework

It is useful to think in terms of two analytical lenses applied to the identical moment. Through the *olah* lens: here is a bird being treated incorrectly — in the wrong place, with the wrong intent — and the *melikah* of an *olah* has not yet been performed. Through the *chatat* lens: here is a bird being processed in the correct place for a *chatat*, with the correct intent for a *chatat*, and the *melikah* of a *chatat* has just been completed. Both descriptions are simultaneously available at the moment after one *siman* is cut. The question is which identity takes hold.

### Identity Redefinition

The answer is that the *chatat* identity wins, because it is the one that has a completed act to its credit. The *olah* identity cannot fully assert itself because the defining act of an *olah* — the cutting of both *simanim* — has not yet taken place. In that gap between the completion of the *chatat melikah* and the still-incomplete *olah melikah*, the *chatat* definition jumps in and claims the bird. It is not magic, and it is not simply a matter of doing enough *chatat*-like things. It is a structural consequence of the asymmetry between the *chatat* and the *olah* with respect to how many *simanim* must be cut.

The *chatat* process finishes first. And finishing first, in a context where all the surrounding acts — location, intent — are oriented toward the *chatat*, is what allows the redesignation to take hold before the *olah* has had the opportunity to assert its identity.

## Reverse Case

### Challenge Posed

Having understood the logic of the redesignation, the Gemara immediately poses a symmetrical challenge. If the reasoning is that the *chatat* definition jumps into the gap before the *olah melikah* is complete, then why should the same logic not apply in reverse? Take a bird *chatat* and perform it above the altar in the place of an *olah*, for the sake of an *olah*, cutting both *simanim*. Should it not, by the same logic, become a valid *olah*?

The Mishnah treats such a bird as a *pasul chatat* — an invalid *chatat* — and requires that it be disposed of accordingly. But if Rabbi Yehoshua's principle really means that the sacrifice becomes the other *korban*, why is the reverse not equally true?

The Gemara initially considers whether the statement attributed to Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Benayah — that the specific framing of the Mishnah represents the precise contours of the dispute — might resolve the issue by confining Rabbi Yehoshua's rule to the one direction. The Gemara, however, does not accept this as a sufficient answer, since that statement could equally be read to mean that Rabbi Yehoshua's principle underlies the entire Mishnah.

### Rav Ashi's Answer

Rav Ashi resolves the question by demonstrating that the logic is inherently directional and cannot be reversed.

### Directional Asymmetry

The argument works as follows. When a bird *olah* is performed below like a *chatat*, the defining act of the *chatat* — cutting one *siman* — is completed before the defining act of the *olah* — cutting both *simanim* — can be completed. At the moment when one *siman* is cut, you have finished the *chatat melikah* but have not yet finished the *olah melikah*. Moreover, there is no valid *olah* process that takes place below at all — *olas ha'of lemata leisa*, there is no such thing as a bird *olah* offered below. So the *olah* identity has no foothold at that location. The *chatat* identity, having its *melikah* completed first and in the right place, seizes the definition.

Now consider the reverse: a bird *chatat* performed above like an *olah*. Here, the *kohen* cuts one *siman*. At this moment, the *chatat melikah* has already been completed — because a *chatat* requires only one *siman*. The *olah melikah* has not yet been completed. So the first identity to have a completed act is once again the *chatat* identity. And critically, performing the *melikah* above does not by itself identify the bird as an *olah*, because technically a *chatat melikah* may also be performed above; it is only the subsequent sprinkling of blood that must occur below. So the location alone does not establish an *olah* identity.

The result: even when you are attempting to treat a bird *chatat* as an *olah*, the moment you cut one *siman*, you have already reaffirmed — in its invalid, *shelo lishmo* form — the *chatat* identity. The *chatat pesulah* definition has already taken hold before the *olah* definition could jump in. There is no gap for the *olah* to occupy, because the *chatat* definition activates first and closes the window. The asymmetry is structural: cutting one *siman* always completes the *chatat melikah* first, and the *olah* never gets the opportunity to assert its identity before the *chatat* has already done so — in whichever direction the transformation is attempted.

## Kinnim Context

### Kinnim Overview

Having articulated Rabbi Yehoshua's position with precision, the Gemara turns to the tractate *Kinnim* to test it. *Kinnim* deals with the mathematically complex permutations that arise from bird sacrifices — particularly when birds belonging to different women are mixed together and the *kohen* no longer knows which bird belongs to whom or which is a *chatat* and which is an *olah*. Importantly, the *stam* of *Kinnim* — the anonymous, authoritative layer of its Mishnayos — is attributed to Rabbi Yehoshua. This authorial identification will become significant for the analysis.

### Women's Sacrifices

The primary context of *Kinnim* is women who bring their bird sacrifices following childbirth. A woman who gives birth is obligated to bring one bird as a *chatat* and one bird as an *olah*. The tractate's cases typically involve two or more women bringing their respective birds simultaneously, with confusion arising about which bird belongs to which woman and which serves as which offering.

### Designation Rules

An important principle governing these cases is that designations need not be made fully in advance. A woman may purchase two birds and leave the designation — which is the *chatat* and which is the *olah* — to be determined by the *kohen*'s actions at the altar: the bird sacrificed below becomes the *chatat*, and the bird sacrificed above becomes the *olah*. Similarly, when two women bring their birds together, the full designation of whose is whose can in some configurations also be deferred, with the *kohen*'s conduct at the altar resolving the question. This flexibility of designation is integral to the complexity that follows.

Additionally, a critical rule bears noting at the outset: if a person takes a *neder* — a vow — to bring bird sacrifices, those birds can only be *olos*. A *chatat* cannot be brought as a *neder*. This distinction between vow-birds and obligation-birds becomes important in the later cases.

---

## First Kinnim Case

### Uncertain Designation

The Gemara introduces the first relevant Mishnah from *Kinnim*. In this case, one woman still owes a *chatat* and another still owes an *olah* — each having previously brought one of her two post-childbirth birds. They now come together with their remaining birds. The *kohen* receives the two birds but does not know — or cannot remember — which belongs to which woman.

### Both-Above/Both-Below

If the *kohen* performs both birds above the altar, the result is *mechtza kasher, mechtza pasul* — half valid and half invalid. Whichever of the two birds was the *olah* is valid; the bird that was actually a *chatat* was done in the wrong place and that woman must bring another bird. Similarly, if the *kohen* performs both below, the *chatat* is valid and the *olah* is invalid — again half valid and half invalid, the woman obligated in an *olah* must return.

### Split Placement

The most problematic scenario is when the *kohen* performs one above and one below — one in each place. The Mishnah rules that both are invalid. The reasoning: we must assume the possibility that he got them backwards — performing the *chatat* above and the *olah* below, thereby invalidating both. Since this possibility cannot be excluded, neither woman can be considered to have fulfilled her obligation.

---

## Gemara Challenge

### Paradox Stated

The Gemara immediately raises a sharp question against this ruling. According to Rabbi Yehoshua's principle, when an *olah* is performed below in the manner of a *chatat*, it becomes redesignated as a *chatat*. If so, in the case where one bird was done above and one below, and we are concerned that the *olah* was the one done below — why not simply say that the *olah* done below has now become a *chatat*, and that woman should receive credit for her *chatat*? The ruling of total invalidity appears to contradict Rabbi Yehoshua's own principle.

### Person-Specific Limitati…

The Gemara offers a potential limitation: perhaps Rabbi Yehoshua's redesignation principle only applies when the woman bringing the *olah* is herself also obligated in a *chatat*. In that case, her own *olah*, done below, could be redesignated to fulfill her own *chatat* obligation. In the current scenario, however, the woman whose *olah* was done below is not the one who owes a *chatat* — it is the *other* woman who owes the *chatat*. An *olah* redesignated as a *chatat* cannot satisfy a third party's obligation. This could explain why the redesignation provides no benefit here.

### Testing Limitation

The Gemara, however, is not satisfied with this limitation, because it has not yet been proven. The question is whether Rabbi Yehoshua's principle can satisfy a *chatat* obligation in any case, or only in specifically configured ones. To test this properly, the Gemara needs a case where the woman whose *olah* was done below is herself obligated in a *chatat* — and yet we would still say she does not get credit. The Gemara therefore moves to a more complex Mishnah.

---

## Complex Kinnim Case

### Scenario Setup

The Gemara introduces a more intricate Mishnah from *Kinnim*. The scenario involves two women — call them Rachel and Leah — each of whom has had two childbirths. After the first childbirth, each brought one of the two required birds: Rachel brought her *olah* and Leah brought her *chatat*. This means that after birth one, Rachel still owes a *chatat* and Leah still owes an *olah*. After birth two, each now owes a full *chatat* and *olah*.

Tallying the outstanding obligations: Rachel owes a *chatat* (from birth one) plus a *chatat* and an *olah* (from birth two) — that is, two *chataos* and one *olah*. Leah owes an *olah* (from birth one) plus a *chatat* and an *olah* (from birth two) — that is, two *olos* and one *chatat*. Between them, they owe a total of three *chataos* and three *olos*, constituting three complete pairs of birds.

### Three Sets Types

They purchase three pairs of birds. Each pair is designated differently:

The first set — referred to simply as *chatat va'olah* — is fully designated. They specify explicitly: this bird is Leah's *chatat*, this bird is Rachel's *olah*.

The second set is *stumah* — unspecified. They hand both birds to the *kohen* without any designation, leaving it entirely to the *kohen* to determine which is the *chatat* and which is the *olah*, and to allocate credit between the two women. Whatever the *kohen* does establishes the identity of those birds.

The third set is *mefureshet*. Here, they have designated each bird by type — one is a *chatat* and one is an *olah* — but they have not specified *whose* is whose. The *chatat* is identified as a *chatat* and the *olah* as an *olah*, but which one belongs to Rachel and which to Leah is left for the *kohen* to resolve by his actions.

### All-Above Case

If the *kohen* performs all six birds above the altar, the outcome is straightforward. All three *olos* in the group are valid. Every obligation for an *olah* among the two women is thereby satisfied. All the *chataos*, however, were done in the wrong place and remain unfulfilled. The women will need to return with their *chatat* birds.

### All-Below Case

Symmetrically, if the *kohen* performs all six birds below, all the *chataos* are valid and all the *olos* are not. Every *chatat* obligation is satisfied; the *olah* obligations are not, and those birds must be brought again above.

### Split Case

The most complex situation arises when the *kohen* splits each set — performing one bird from each pair above and one below. For the explicitly designated set (*chatat va'olah*), this is potentially disastrous: the *kohen* knows which bird is which, but if he somehow reversed them, both are invalid. In the *mefureshet* set, the *kohen* knew which bird was the *chatat* and which was the *olah* but not whose was whose; if he reversed the locations of *chatat* and *olah*, both are invalid and no credit accrues to either woman. For the *stumah* set, however, the situation is actually favorable: since neither the identity of the birds nor whose is whose was designated in advance, the *kohen*'s actions themselves constitute the designation. Whatever he did above becomes the *olah* and whatever he did below becomes the *chatat*, and these count for one of the two women respectively. The Mishnah therefore rules: *eina kesheira ela stuma* — in the split scenario, only the *stumah* pair yields valid results; the *mefureshet* pair does not.

---

## Gemara Challenge

### Paradox Stated

The Gemara returns to its central difficulty. When the *mefureshet* set is split — one bird above and one below — why does the *olah* that was done below not simply become a *chatat* according to Rabbi Yehoshua's principle? In this pair, the birds were designated as a *chatat* and an *olah*, but not whose is whose. If the *olah* is redesignated as a *chatat* by virtue of being done below, then at least some woman should get credit for a *chatat* from that pair. Why does the Mishnah say both are invalid?

### Person-Specific Limitati…

The Gemara revisits the limitation it had proposed: perhaps Rabbi Yehoshua's rule only applies when the redesignation benefits the very person whose bird it was. But in the *mefureshet* case, the *olah* was not yet assigned to anyone — whose *olah* it was had not been determined. So it cannot be that the redesignation fails for lack of a matching personal obligation.

### Testing Limitation

The Gemara therefore presses the question: does Rabbi Yehoshua's principle lead to the conclusion that the woman whose *olah* was done below should receive credit for a *chatat* even in the *mefureshet* case? And if not, what precisely limits the scope of his ruling? To resolve this, the Gemara demonstrates that *Kinnim* is according to Rabbi Yehoshua — and then draws the decisive distinction about what his ruling actually means.

---

## Resolution

### Rabbi Yehoshua Authorship

The Gemara establishes that *Kinnim* was authored by Rabbi Yehoshua. The proof comes from the final Mishnah in *Kinnim*, on daf 68a, where after a complex ruling, Rabbi Yehoshua speaks in a distinctive narrator's voice: *zehu she'amru* — "this is what they meant when they said..." He is not merely being quoted as one opinion among others; he is wrapping up and interpreting the Mishnah's ruling as its author. This phrasing, the Gemara argues, marks Rabbi Yehoshua as the *stam* editor of the tractate. Since *Kinnim* is according to Rabbi Yehoshua, the question returns with full force: why, in the *mefureshet* split case, does the *olah* done below not count as a *chatat*?

### Me'ilah vs. Obligation

The Gemara provides its answer in the form of a precise limitation on Rabbi Yehoshua's principle. When did Rabbi Yehoshua say that an *olah* becomes a *chatat*? *L'apokei mi'yadei me'ilah* — to exempt it from *me'ilah*. His ruling was specifically and solely about the laws of *me'ilah*: by virtue of the redesignation, the bird is no longer subject to *me'ilah* liability. But *l'misak lei l'chova mi amar* — he never said that this redesignation fulfills a person's *chatat* obligation. The redesignation is real enough to remove *me'ilah*, but it does not constitute a genuine *chatat* that counts toward a person's outstanding sacrifice debt.

### Final Conclusion

The Gemara's resolution is therefore this: Rabbi Yehoshua's principle produces a partial and limited redesignation. The *olah* done below is, in a meaningful sense, no longer an *olah* — it has shed the *me'ilah* status that attaches to an *olah* and has taken on enough of a *chatat* identity to be exempt from it. In all likelihood, this also means the *kohanim* may eat the meat. But at the end of the day, the bird was sanctified as an *olah*, and its redesignation stops short of making it a fully valid *chatat* that satisfies a woman's post-childbirth obligation. The gap that the *chatat* jumped into was sufficient to resolve the question of *me'ilah*, but insufficient to transform the bird's fundamental status for purposes of obligation fulfillment.

## Neder Case

### Neder Setup

The Gemara now presents the final Mishnah of *Kinnim* under discussion, which involves a woman who took upon herself a *neder*, a vow, to bring two birds if she were to give birth to a male child. She subsequently does give birth to a male.

### Neder Constraint

The complication arises from the rule that a *neder* for bird sacrifices can only produce *olos*; a *chatat* cannot be brought as a *neder*. The two birds of her vow are therefore both *olos*. At the same time, her status as a woman who has given birth creates an independent obligation — one *chatat* and one *olah*. She thus owes two *olos* from the *neder* and one *chatat* plus one *olah* from the obligatory post-childbirth sacrifice: a total of three *olos* and one *chatat*.

### Kohen's Assumption

She brings two pairs of birds to the *kohen* — one pair for her *neder* and one pair for her obligatory post-childbirth sacrifice — but she does not specify clearly which set is which, or perhaps the *kohen* forgets. Seeing two pairs of birds presented by a woman who has given birth, the *kohen* reasonably assumes he is dealing with two post-childbirth pairs, each consisting of one *chatat* and one *olah*.

### Error Described

Operating under that assumption, the *kohen* does one above and one below from each pair. The correct procedure should have been: three birds above (the three *olos*) and one below (the *chatat*). Instead, he does two above and two below, incorrectly bringing one bird from the *neder* pair below as though it were a *chatat*.

### Obligation Shortfall

The consequence: from the *neder* pair, the bird brought below was an *olah* treated as a *chatat*. Since *neder* birds are always *olos*, this bird — one of her two vow-birds — was brought in the wrong place. She has therefore not fully satisfied her *neder*, and she must bring one additional bird above to complete it.

### Species Confusion

A further complication arises if the birds were not all of the same species — if one pair consisted of turtledoves and the other of pigeons. The *halacha* requires that a pair of birds within the same set be of the same species; a turtledove cannot be paired with a pigeon. The woman now needs to bring the mate to the *olah* she failed to fulfill, but she does not remember which set contained the turtledoves and which the pigeons. She therefore cannot know whether the remaining unfulfilled *olah* belongs to the turtledove pair or the pigeon pair, and consequently cannot identify which species she must bring.

### Mixed Species Solution

The resolution is that she must bring one bird of each species — one turtledove and one pigeon — to ensure that she covers the correct pairing for whichever set contained the unfulfilled *olah*. By bringing one of each, she guarantees that the required species is included. This is where the Mishnah leaves off for the day, with the more complex continuation of this case to be taken up in the following daf.
