# Shabbat 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Women's Carrying Objects

### Intro & Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא תֵּצֵא אִשָּׁה בַּמַּחַט הַנְּקוּבָה, וְלֹא בְּטַבַּעַת שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלֶיהָ חוֹתָם, וְלֹא בְּכוֹלֵיאָר, וְלֹא בְּכוֹבֶלֶת, וְלֹא בִּצְלוֹחִית שֶׁל פִּלְיָיטוֹן. וְאִם יָצְתָה — חַיֶּיבֶת חַטָּאת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין בְּכוֹבֶלֶת וּבִצְלוֹחִית שֶׁל פִּלְיָיטוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A woman may neither go out** to the public domain **with a perforated needle,** i.e., a standard needle with an eye, **nor with a ring that has a seal on it, nor with a *kulyar*, nor with a *kovelet*,** the identity of which will be discussed in the Gemara, **nor with a flask of balsam oil. And if she did go out** into the public domain, **she is liable to bring a sin-offering;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir,** who holds that in doing so she violated the Torah prohibition of carrying a burden in the public domain on Shabbat. **And the Rabbis exempt** one who goes out on Shabbat **with a *kovelet* and with a flask of balsam oil.** In their opinion, these are ornaments, and therefore they do not fundamentally violate the Torah prohibition of carrying in the public domain on Shabbat.

The daf begins with a Mishnah that continues a theme established in prior pages: which objects carried on Shabbat constitute a biblical violation of *hotzaah* and which constitute only a rabbinic concern. The earlier Mishnayot addressed items that men and women may not wear on Shabbat, not because wearing them is itself a biblical transgression, but because of the rabbinic concern that one might remove them in public and come to carry them. The present Mishnah shifts to a different category: items that, even when worn on or about the body, are classified not as clothing or adornment but as carrying outright—and therefore implicate a biblical prohibition.

### Needle with Eye

*Lo teitzei ha'ishah bemachat hanekuvah*: a woman may not go out with a needle that has an eye in it. A needle with a hole is an implement of sewing; wearing it stuck through one's garment is not a form of adornment but simply a convenient method of transport. The needle is carried by being on the body, not worn as clothing.

### Signet Ring

*Velo betaba'at sheyeish aleha chotam*: nor with a ring bearing a seal, a signet ring. As established in earlier discussion, a signet ring was normatively a man's article—used for authenticating documents and transactions—and not the customary ornament of a woman. Accordingly, when a woman wears it, it is classified not as wearing but as carrying around the finger.

### Kulyar & Kovelet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּכוֹלֵיאָר וְלֹא בְּכוֹבֶלֶת. מַאי כּוֹלֵיאָר? אָמַר רַב: מַכְבַּנְתָּא. כּוֹבֶלֶת — אָמַר רַב: חוּמַרְתָּא דְפִילוֹן. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: חוּמַרְתָּא דְפִילוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **Nor with a *kulyar*, nor with a *kovelet*.** The Gemara asks: **What is** a ***kulyar*? Rav said: A brooch** with which a woman fastens the collar of her garment. ***Kovelet*: Rav said** that it is **a bundle of fragrant herbs [*pilon*]. And, similarly, Rav Asi said: A bundle of fragrant herbs.**

*Velo bekhulyar velo bekoveles*: nor with a *kulyar* nor with a *koveles*. The Gemara will explain that the *kulyar* is some kind of clasp—Rashi identifies it as a *machbenasa*, though precisely what this is and why it would not qualify as clothing is not fully clear, and the Rishonim do not offer extensive discussion on the point. The *koveles*, as the Gemara shortly clarifies, is a small bundle or vial containing aromatic herbs or spices, worn around the neck.

### Aromatic Bottles

*Velo bitzluchit shel pilayton*: nor with a small bottle of *pilayton*. Rashi identifies *pilayton* as *balma*—balsam oil, *mishchat ha'afarsimon*. To clarify: the balsam familiar from northern latitudes is not the balsam of the Talmudic world. The balsam of the Torah is a bush, indigenous to the region, from which an aromatic oil was derived; one of the components of the *ketoret* is commonly identified with it. The *tzluchit shel pilayton*, then, is a small glass flask containing this aromatic oil, worn around the neck so that one would smell pleasant at all times.

### Meir vs. Chachamim

*Ve'im yatze'ah chayevet chatat, divrei Rabbi Meir*: if a woman goes out wearing any of these items she is obligated a *chatat*, a sin-offering—they are classified as carrying despite being on the body. *Vachachamim potrim bekoveles uvitzluchit shel pilayton*: the Sages, however, exempt her with respect to the last two items, the aromatic bundle and the balsam flask. This mirrors the three-way dispute already seen regarding the *ir shel zahav*, the golden tiara, where Rabbi Meir was more inclined to treat items as biblically prohibited carrying, the Sages considered them jewelry subject only to a rabbinic restriction, and Rabbi Eliezer permitted them outright.

One might ask whether wearing aromatic substances raises the separate rabbinic concern of *shchikat samanim*, the prohibition against grinding medicinal spices on Shabbat. That concern, however, applies specifically to medicinal use—not to fragrance applied to the body—and in any case is a rabbinic matter, whereas the Gemara's primary focus here is the biblical question of whether these items constitute *hotzaah*.

## Ulla's Gender Reversal

### Ulla's Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא וְחִילּוּפֵיהֶן בְּאִישׁ. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר עוּלָּא: כָּל מִידֵּי דַּחֲזֵי לְאִישׁ לָא חֲזֵי לְאִשָּׁה, וּמִידִּי דַּחֲזֵי לְאִשָּׁה לָא חֲזֵי לְאִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to that which we learned in the mishna that a woman may not go out on Shabbat with a ring that has a seal, and by inference that she may go out with a ring without a seal, **Ulla said: And the reverse** of **these** *halakhot* is true **with regard to a man.** A man who wears a ring with a seal in the public domain is exempt. However, if he wears a ring without a seal, he is liable to bring a sin-offering as it is not considered an ornament for a man. Based on that statement, the Gemara concludes: **Apparently, Ulla holds** that **every object that is suitable for a man is not suitable for a woman, and an object that is suitable for a woman is not suitable for a man.**

*Amar Ulla: vechilufeihen be'ish*—Ulla declares that the reverse applies to a man. The Mishnah, notably, does not specify what the law is for a man wearing these same items. The implication might simply be that what is customary clothing for men poses no problem. But Ulla goes further: not only is a signet ring not an issue for a man (since it is normatively men's jewelry), but a non-signet ring—the ordinary ornamental ring normatively worn by women—is classified as carrying when worn by a man, and would constitute a biblical violation.

### Clothing Definition

The Gemara pauses to analyze the logical structure of Ulla's claim. *Alma kasavar Ulla*: it emerges that Ulla holds that certain types of clothing are gender-specific—what is clothing for one gender is not clothing for the other. Read carefully, *kol midi dechazi le'ish lo chazi le'isha, midi dechazi le'isha lo chazi le'ish*: there are articles that are genuinely men's clothing and not women's, and vice versa, such that when worn by the opposite gender they do not constitute wearing but carrying.

This is not self-evident. One could argue—and the Gemara raises this possibility—that if the Torah's prohibition of *lo yilbash gever simlat isha* uses the language *yilbash*, "he shall wear," it presupposes that a man putting on a woman's garment is wearing it in the full sense, not carrying it on his body. The very prohibition implies that it counts as *levisha*. Ulla disagrees: when an item associated with one gender is worn by the other, it is not recognized as wearing at all, and therefore functions as carrying for purposes of *hotzaah*.

### Shepherd Sackcloth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: הָרוֹעִים יוֹצְאִין בְּשַׂקִּים. וְלֹא הָרוֹעִים בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא כָּל אָדָם. אֶלָּא שֶׁדַּרְכָּן שֶׁל הָרוֹעִים לָצֵאת בְּשַׂקִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised an objection** from the *Tosefta*: **Shepherds may go out** on Shabbat **in** garments made of **sacks. And not** with regard to **the shepherds alone did** the Sages **say** that they are permitted to go out in sacks on Shabbat; **rather, any person** may do so. **However,** the Sages taught the *halakha* with regard to shepherds because **it is the** standard **practice of shepherds to go out in sacks.** Apparently, although a sack is not a typical garment for most people, it is permitted even for one who is not a shepherd and would not generally wear it. Based on the same principle, although men do not generally wear women's ornaments and women do not generally wear men's ornaments, since occasionally a man might wear an ornament belonging to a woman or vice versa, each should be permitted to go out into the public domain with the ornament of the other.

*Metiv Rav Yosef*: Rav Yosef raises a challenge. A *Tosefta* teaches that shepherds may go out on Shabbat wearing their distinctive sackcloth garments—rough, coarse coverings—and that this principle extends to anyone who wears such garments, since the *Tosefta* adds that the reason shepherds are mentioned is simply that this is their custom. The implicit question: here is a type of clothing worn only by a specific sub-group, yet it is still classified as wearing and not carrying. Why should the same not apply to a man wearing a woman's garment or vice versa?

### Gender Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: קָסָבַר עוּלָּא נָשִׁים עַם בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef said: Ulla holds** that **women are a people unto themselves.** The difference between the standard practice of men and women is greater than the difference between the standard practice of practitioners of different professions.

*Ela amar Rav Yosef kasavar Ulla nashim am bifnei atzman hen*: Rav Yosef resolves the contradiction by articulating Ulla's underlying principle—women constitute, as it were, their own distinct "nation." Understood in its narrowest, most local sense, this means that clothing is so profoundly gendered that cross-gender wearing is categorically different from one sub-group of men wearing the clothing of another sub-group. A shepherd wearing sackcloth and a nobleman wearing sackcloth are still operating within the same clothing universe; a man wearing a woman's garment, or a woman wearing a man's garment, crosses a categorical boundary. The garment belongs to an entirely foreign domain and is therefore not recognized as *levisha* at all—it is carrying.

This raises a natural question about whether the same logic would apply across national or cultural lines—would a man wearing distinctively foreign men's clothing be considered carrying? The answer is almost certainly no. The principle *am bifnei atzman* seems to be specifically about gender as a category, not about ethnic or cultural distinctions within the same gender. What makes men's and women's clothing categorically different is the gendered nature of clothing itself.

## Women & Tefillin

### Tefillin on Road

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַמּוֹצֵא תְּפִילִּין — מַכְנִיסָן זוּג זוּג, אֶחָד הָאִישׁ וְאֶחָד הָאִשָּׁה. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ נָשִׁים עַם בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן הֵן, וְהָא מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא הוּא, וְכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection** to Rav Yosef's statement from the *Tosefta*: **One who finds phylacteries** outside of the city on Shabbat **should** don them and **bring them into** the city **one pair at a time.** This applies to **both a man and a woman. And if you say** that **women are a people unto themselves, isn't** the mitzva to don phylacteries **a time-bound, positive mitzva,** as there are times when the mitzva to don phylacteries is not in effect? **And** the following is a halakhic principle: **Women are exempt from every time-bound, positive mitzva.** If, in fact, the clothing and ornaments of a man are not suitable for a woman under any circumstances, why is a woman permitted to don the phylacteries and bring them into the city on Shabbat? Shouldn't that be considered a prohibited act of carrying?

*Hamotzi tefillin*: if one finds *tefillin* abandoned in the road on Shabbat and fears they will be stolen or trampled, one may bring them inside by wearing them—donning the *shel yad* and *shel rosh*, carrying them in by wearing, then returning for the next pair. The ruling states *echad ish ve'echad ishah*—this applies equally to a man and to a woman. A woman may wear *tefillin* to bring them inside, and it is not considered carrying on Shabbat.

This is a striking ruling. *Tefillin* are normatively associated with men. If Ulla's principle holds—that wearing the clothing of the other gender does not constitute *levisha* but *hotzaah*—then a woman wearing *tefillin* should be classified as carrying them, not wearing them. Why then does the Gemara permit her to bring them inside by wearing?

### Meir: Not Time-Bound

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם קָסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: לַיְלָה זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא, וְשַׁבָּת זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא. הָוֵה לֵיהּ מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא, וְכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא — נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** with regard to phylacteries, **Rabbi Meir holds** that **night is an** appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries, and Shabbat** and Festivals are similarly **an** appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries.** Consequently, the mitzva of phylacteries is **a positive mitzva** that is **not time bound; and** in **every positive mitzva** that is **not time bound, women are obligated.** Therefore, women are permitted to don the phylacteries and bring them into the city.

*Hatam kasavar Rabbi Meir*: the Gemara resolves the difficulty by attributing that earlier ruling to Rabbi Meir's position. *Laila zman tefillin hu veShabbbat zman tefillin hu*: Rabbi Meir holds that *tefillin* may be worn at night and on Shabbat—that there is no time-bound exclusion limiting their wearing. Since *tefillin* according to Rabbi Meir is not a time-bound *mitzvah*, women are obligated in it equally with men. And since women are obligated, *tefillin* are women's clothing as much as men's. There is therefore no cross-gender wearing, and a woman putting on *tefillin* constitutes *levisha*, not *hotzaah*.

### Obligation vs. Reality

This raises a genuinely interesting question about the relationship between halakhic status and social reality. The Gemara has been operating throughout this sugya on the assumption that clothing categories are defined by societal norms—what people in fact wear. Yet here, the classification of *tefillin* as women's clothing is determined entirely by Rabbi Meir's halakhic position that women are obligated, not by the social reality that in Rabbi Meir's time women presumably were not, in general, wearing *tefillin*. Rabbi Meir appears to be saying: regardless of what people actually do, since in principle women are obligated, *tefillin* is women's clothing—and that halakhic reality controls, not the sociological one.

There is a substantial discussion of this question in the corresponding passage in *Eruvin* concerning the wearing of *tefillin* found on the road. The extent to which that Gemara concludes definitively that the ruling reflects Rabbi Meir's specific position regarding women's obligation in *tefillin* requires separate examination. But here in Shabbat the Gemara simply asserts it as the explanatory framework.

This sugya also has implications for broader debates about women wearing *tefillin* and *tallit*. For some, the very identification of these objects with male religious practice makes a woman's wearing of them feel analogous to cross-dressing at a visceral level—an association with Ulla's principle of *am bifnei atzman*. The Gemara itself is not here engaging with the *beged ish* prohibition; it is asking the narrower question of whether the wearing of a gender-associated object by the other gender constitutes *levisha* or *hotzaah* for purposes of Shabbat. But the sugya implicitly raises the connection, and it is worth noting.

## Finger Carrying & Rings

### Finger Carrying Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הוֹצָאָה כִּלְאַחַר יָד הִיא! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בָּאִשָּׁה גִּזְבָּרִית עָסְקִינַן. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תֵּרַצְתָּ אִשָּׁה, אִישׁ מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Rabbi Meir's ruling in the mishna that a woman is liable by Torah law if she goes out into the public domain wearing a ring with a seal, the Gemara asks: **Isn't it** considered **carrying out in a backhanded manner?** A ring with a seal is not an ornament for a woman; it is an object like any other. One typically carries objects holding them in his hand. Wearing an object around one's finger is atypical. There is no Torah prohibition violated when a labor is performed in an atypical manner. **Rabbi Yirmeya said** that **we are dealing** in the mishna **with a woman who is a treasurer** for charity. A woman with that occupation typically wears a ring with a seal on her finger in order to perform her job. She does not, though, wear it as an ornament. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: You answered** and explained why, in the case of a **woman,** she would be liable to bring a sin-offering. However, in the case of a **man** who wore a ring without a seal, **what is there to say?** Why should he be liable?

The Gemara now identifies a further difficulty. Even granting Ulla's position—that for a woman a signet ring is not *levisha* but *hotzaah*, since it is men's clothing—the biblical prohibition of *hotzaah* requires not merely moving an object but doing so in the normal manner of conveyance. The normal manner of carrying an object is in the palm of the hand; carrying it on one's finger is *k'le'achar yad*, an abnormal mode of carrying. If the signet ring is not clothing for a woman, it should only be carrying—but carrying in an unusual way—and an unusual manner of carrying is exempt from biblical liability. Why then is the woman *chayevet chatat*?

*Amar Rabbi Yirmiyah: be'ishah gizbarit askinan*—the Gemara explains that the Mishnah is speaking of a woman who functions as a treasurer or businesswoman. For such a woman, using a signet ring is a regular feature of her professional activity; it is normal for her to have it on her finger as part of her work. Accordingly, while the ring is still not *levisha* for her—because collectively and socially it is men's jewelry—carrying it on her finger is the normal mode of conveyance for a woman in her position. The ring is not her clothing; it is something she regularly carries on her finger for business purposes, and that is a recognized way of carrying it. Hence she is *chayevet chatat*.

The implication is precise: for any other woman who might happen to wear a signet ring, it would not be a normal mode of carrying, and she would not be biblically liable unless she held it in her palm. Only the woman whose regular practice makes finger-carrying the recognized mode of transport incurs biblical liability.

### Spousal Ring Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: פְּעָמִים שֶׁאָדָם נוֹתֵן לְאִשְׁתּוֹ טַבַּעַת שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלֶיהָ חוֹתָם לְהוֹלִיכָהּ לַקּוּפְסָא, וּמַנִּיחָתָהּ בְּיָדָהּ עַד שֶׁמַּגַּעַת לַקּוּפְסָא. וּפְעָמִים שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה נוֹתֶנֶת לְבַעֲלָהּ טַבַּעַת שֶׁאֵין עָלֶיהָ חוֹתָם לְהוֹלִיכָהּ אֵצֶל אוּמָּן לְתַקֵּן, וּמַנִּיחָהּ בְּיָדוֹ עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ אֵצֶל אוּמָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that there is a different reason: **Sometimes a person gives his wife a ring that has a seal on it to take it to** his house and place it in **a box** for safekeeping, **and,** in order to ensure that she does not lose the ring, the woman **places it in on her hand,** i.e., on her finger, **until she reaches the box. And,** likewise, **sometimes a woman gives her husband a ring that does not have a seal on it to take it to a craftsman to repair it, and** the husband **places it on his hand,** i.e., on his finger, **until he reaches the craftsman.** The purpose of wearing these rings is not for ornamentation. Nevertheless, it is considered a typical manner of carrying them.

A further question arises symmetrically: *Rav Yochanan* (cited by Rabbah bar bar Chana) acknowledges that the woman treasurer case explains female liability for wearing a signet ring, but asks—*ish mai ika le'meimar?*—how does the reverse work for a man? If a non-signet ring is women's clothing and therefore constitutes carrying when worn by a man, the same problem recurs: carrying on the finger is not the normal mode of conveyance for men with respect to rings that are not their own. Why would a man be biblically liable?

*Ela amar Rava*: Rava offers a scenario that explains both cases simultaneously. *Pe'amim she'adam noten le'ishto taba'at she'yesh aleha chotam*: sometimes a man removes his signet ring and hands it to his wife, asking her to bring it home and store it—*le'holichah le'kufsa*. The wife then carries it on her finger until she reaches the storage box. This is a recognized real-world practice: when someone hands you a ring to transport, the natural way to avoid losing it is to place it on your finger. Conversely, *pe'amim she'ha'ishah notenet le'ba'alah taba'at she'ein aleha chotam*: sometimes a woman removes her own ring and gives it to her husband to bring to a craftsman for repair—*le'holichah etzel uman le'taken*. The husband then wears his wife's ring on his finger until he reaches the jeweler. Again, the most natural way to transport a ring without losing it is to wear it on the finger.

Rava's solution is elegant: for both men and women, there exist recognized social scenarios in which one carries a ring belonging to the opposite gender on one's finger as a normal mode of conveyance. The ring is not clothing for the person carrying it—it belongs to the other gender's clothing domain—but carrying it on the finger in these contexts is not *k'le'achar yad*; it is the recognized, ordinary way one transports a ring. Therefore the person is liable at the biblical level.

## Rashi vs. Tam

### Rashi's View

Having established Ulla's position that gender-specific items constitute carrying when worn by the opposite gender at the biblical level, the Gemara leaves open a related question: what about the rabbinic dimension? For a woman, a non-signet ring is a rabbinic concern—not because it is carrying, but because there is fear she might remove it to show a friend, thereby coming to carry it four *amot* in *reshut ha'rabim*. Does an analogous rabbinic concern apply to a man wearing a signet ring—which, for a man, is not carrying at all—lest he come to remove it and show it?

Rashi holds that yes, the same type of rabbinic concern operates for men as for women. Items that are attractive or unusual—secondary objects rather than major garments—might prompt the wearer to remove them and display them, regardless of gender. The rabbinic restriction against going out with such items on Shabbat accordingly applies to men as well.

### Tam's View

Rabbeinu Tam disagrees, drawing on other *sugyot*. In his view, the rabbinic decrees about removing and showing garments were specifically formulated with women in mind. The concern that one might impulsively remove a piece of jewelry or an ornament in the public street to show a friend was identified as characteristic of women's behavior in this domain; it was not extended with the same force to men. Accordingly, Rabbeinu Tam argues that all the rabbinic restrictions we have been studying are gender-specific: they apply to women's articles and not to men's. This is not explicit in the Mishnah or the Gemara; it is a reading Rabbeinu Tam derives from other contexts. The question of whether a man wearing a signet ring on Shabbat faces any rabbinic prohibition is thus genuinely disputed between these two positions.

## Kovelet Perfume Debate

### Three-Way Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא תֵּצֵא בְּכוֹבֶלֶת, וְאִם יָצְתָה — חַיֶּיבֶת חַטָּאת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא תֵּצֵא, וְאִם יָצְתָה — פְּטוּרָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יוֹצְאָה אִשָּׁה בְּכוֹבֶלֶת לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A woman may not go out** on Shabbat **with a bundle of fragrant herbs, and if she did go out she is liable to bring a sin-offering,** as she violated a Torah prohibition; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: She may not go out** *ab initio*; **however, if she went out, she is exempt. Rabbi Eliezer says: A woman may go out with a bundle of fragrant herbs** even ***ab initio*.**

The Gemara now examines the *koveles* and the *tzluchit shel pilayton* more carefully, presenting a full three-way dispute—Rabbi Meir, the Sages, and Rabbi Eliezer—that exactly parallels the tripartite debate over the *ir shel zahav* encountered earlier: *chayav chatat* (biblical carrying), *assur me'derabanan* (rabbinic prohibition), and *mutar le'chatchilah* (entirely permitted).

### Kovelet Definition

*Mai koveles?* What is a *koveles*? *Amar Rav: chumsa d'pilon*—a bundle of aromatic herbs or spices, worn to smell pleasant, analogous to the *tzluchit shel pilayton*, the small flask of balsam oil. *Vechen amar Rav Assi: chumsa d'pilon*. The two items in the Mishnah, *koveles* and *tzluchit shel pilayton*, are thus very similar in function. It should be noted that the precise definition of *koveles* is not entirely clear, and the Rishonim do not resolve it with certainty.

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר מַשּׂאוֹי הוּא. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי תַּכְשִׁיט הוּא, וְדִילְמָא שָׁלְפָא וּמַחְוְיָא, וְאָתְיָא לְאֵיתוֹיֵיהּ. וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: מַאן דִּרְכַּהּ לְמִירְמֵיהּ — אִשָּׁה שֶׁרֵיחָהּ רַע, אִשָּׁה שֶׁרֵיחָהּ רָע לָא שָׁלְפָא וּמַחְוְיָא, וְלָא אָתְיָא לְאֵתוֹיֵיהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do they disagree? Rabbi Meir holds** that **it is a burden.** Therefore, one who takes it out into the public domain on Shabbat is liable to bring a sin-offering. **And the Rabbis hold** that **it is an ornament.** Nevertheless, they prohibited going out with it *ab initio* due to concern **lest she remove** the bundle of herbs from its place, **and show it** to others, **and come to carry it** in the public domain. **And Rabbi Eliezer holds: Whose practice is it to place** fragrant herbs on herself? **A woman whose odor is foul.** But **a woman whose odor is foul does not remove and show** the bundle to others because, by doing so, her odor will be detected, a situation that she would prefer to avoid. **And,** therefore she **will not come to carry it four cubits in the public domain.**

*Tanu rabbanan*: a *Baraita* teaches: *lo teitzei bekoveles, ve'im yatze'ah chayevet chatat, divrei Rabbi Meir*—a woman may not go out wearing a perfume bundle, and if she did, she is obligated a *chatat*; so says Rabbi Meir. *Vachachamim omrim: lo teitzei*—the Sages say she should not go out, but if she did, she is exempt from biblical liability, since they classify it as *tachshit*, a form of adornment. Why do they nonetheless prohibit it rabbinically? The standard concern: *dilma shalfa umachviya ve'ata le'atuye*—she might take it off, show it to a friend, and come to carry it four *amot*. Someone who encounters a fragrant perfume bottle will naturally want others to smell it: "Look how wonderful this smells!"—and in showing it she might inadvertently carry it.

*Rabbi Eliezer omer: yotze'ah ishah bekoveles le'chatchilah*—Rabbi Eliezer says she may go out wearing it entirely permissibly.

### Woman's Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* U'vmai kamifligi? Rabbi Meir considers it a *massa'ui*, a burden—it is not worn as a garment but carried on the body for a functional purpose. The Sages consider it *tachshit*, jewelry, making it rabbinic rather than biblical. Rabbi Eliezer holds that there is not even a rabbinic problem. His reasoning: *man darko le'miremei?*—who is it that typically wears such a perfume bundle? *Ishah she'reichah ra'*—a woman who has an unpleasant body odor. And such a woman, precisely because of her condition, *lo shalfa umachviya*, will not remove it and show it in public, and therefore will not come to carry it. Tosafot clarifies that this is not about creating different laws for different individuals; rather, it is about determining what type of object this is by looking at whose practice it reflects. Since the class of women who wear this sort of item would not remove it in public, the object itself does not implicate the concern that motivates the rabbinic prohibition.

### Eliezer Reconciled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹטֵר בְּכוֹבֶלֶת וּבִצְלוֹחִית שֶׁל פְּלַיְיטוֹן! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא כִּי קָאֵי — אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר. הָא כִּי קָאֵי — אַדְּרַבָּנַן. כִּי קָאֵי אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר חַיָּיב חַטָּאת — אָמַר אִיהוּ פָּטוּר. כִּי קָאֵי אַדְּרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר — אָמַר אִיהוּ מוּתָּר לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara then notes an apparent tension in Rabbi Eliezer's position. A *Baraita* elsewhere quotes Rabbi Eliezer as saying one is *poter*—exempt—regarding the *koveles* and the *tzluchit shel pilayton*, language that implies only exemption from biblical liability rather than outright permissibility *le'chatchilah*. The Gemara resolves this: *lo kashya*. When Rabbi Eliezer is in dialogue with Rabbi Meir, who says one is *chayav chatat*, Rabbi Eliezer responds *poter*—"you are not biblically liable." When Rabbi Eliezer is in dialogue with the Sages, who say *poter aval assur* (exempt but rabbinically forbidden), Rabbi Eliezer counters *mutar le'chatchilah*—"it is entirely permitted." His substantive position is consistent throughout; the language he uses shifts depending on which interlocutor he is addressing.

## Keys & Minimum Amounts

### Key Carrying Barita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי רַבִּי מֵאִיר? דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא תֵּצֵא אִשָּׁה בְּמַפְתֵּחַ שֶׁבְּיָדָהּ, וְאִם יָצָאת — חַיֶּיבֶת חַטָּאת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹטֵר בְּכוֹבֶלֶת וּבִצְלוֹחִית שֶׁל פִּלְיָיטוֹן. כּוֹבֶלֶת — מַאן דְּכַר שְׁמַהּ? חַסּוֹרֵי מִחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: וְכֵן בְּכוֹבֶלֶת וְכֵן בִּצְלוֹחִית שֶׁל פִּלְיָיטוֹן לֹא תֵּצֵא, וְאִם יָצְאָה — חַיֶּיבֶת חַטָּאת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹטֵר בְּכוֹבֶלֶת וּבִצְלוֹחִית שֶׁל פִּלְיָיטוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** to **what** statement of **Rabbi Meir** is the Gemara referring? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **A woman may not go out** on Shabbat **with a key that is in her hand, and if she went out she is liable to bring a sin-offering;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Eliezer exempts** a woman who goes out **with a bundle of fragrant herbs and with a flask of balsam oil [*palyaton*]. A bundle of fragrant herbs; who mentioned** anything about **that?** Rabbi Meir did not mention a bundle of herbs; why did Rabbi Eliezer mention it in his response? The Gemara answers that the *baraita* **is incomplete and it teaches the following: And likewise, with a bundle of fragrant herbs, and likewise with a flask of balsam oil she may not go out, and if she went out she is liable to bring a sin-offering;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Eliezer exempts in** the cases of **a bundle of fragrant herbs and a flask of balsam oil.**

Having established the three-way dispute about the *koveles*, the Gemara cites a *Baraita* that appears to juxtapose the *koveles* discussion with the question of carrying a key: *lo teitzei ishah bemafteach she'bevyadah*—a woman may not go out carrying her key in her hand. The concern about carrying one's key when there is no *eruv* is of obvious practical significance. If she goes out with it, she is *chayevet chatat*, says Rabbi Meir. Then the *Baraita* adds: *Rabbi Eliezer poter bekoveles uvitzluchit shel pilayton*—Rabbi Eliezer exempts in the case of a *koveles* and a *tzluchit shel pilayton*.

The juxtaposition is jarring: we were discussing keys and suddenly Rabbi Eliezer is talking about perfume bottles. The Gemara resolves this through *chasuri mechasra*—the *Baraita* is incomplete as stated and must be reconstructed. The full text reads: Rabbi Meir says a woman may not go out with a key and, similarly, with a *koveles* or a *tzluchit shel pilayton*—and if she does, she is *chayevet chatat*. Rabbi Eliezer does not dispute Rabbi Meir on the key; he disputes only regarding the perfume bottles, where he says she is exempt (and, as established above, actually holds it is permitted *le'chatchilah*).

### Oil Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — כְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶם בּוֹשֶׂם, אֲבָל אֵין בָּהֶם בּוֹשֶׂם — חַיֶּיבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement said?** In a case **where** the vessels **have perfume in them; however,** in a case where **they do not have perfume in them, she is liable** for carrying the flask out into the public domain on Shabbat.

*Bameh devarim amurim?*—When does even the more permissive view hold? *Keshe'yehei bah bosem*—only when there is actually aromatic oil inside the flask. Then the flask is serving its function and may be classified as an adornment the woman genuinely wears. *Aval ein bah bosem*—but if there is no oil in it, she is *chayevet chatat*—biblically liable.

This ruling prompts *Rav Adda bar Ahava* to draw a significant inference: *zot omeret: ha'motzi ochlin pachot mike'shiur bikli chayav*—this teaches that one who carries out food in a vessel, even if the food is less than the minimum amount that would independently require biblical liability, is nonetheless *chayav*. The reasoning is as follows: there is a minimum quantity of food below which carrying it out does not constitute a biblical violation. Yet if that sub-minimum food is contained in a vessel, one might have thought the vessel alone generates liability—the vessel is significant in its own right. The case of the empty perfume bottle, however, seems to suggest otherwise: the bottle without its oil is still *chayav*—and if this is because the residual fragrance counts as the "food" in the vessel, then even a sub-minimum quantity carried in a vessel renders one liable.

### Empty Vessel Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת הַמּוֹצִיא אוֹכָלִין פָּחוֹת מִכְּשִׁיעוּר בִּכְלִי — חַיָּיב. דְּהָא אֵין בָּהּ בּוֹשֶׂם כְּפָחוֹת מִכְּשִׁיעוּר בִּכְלִי דָּמֵי, וְקָתָנֵי חַיֶּיבֶת. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בְּעָלְמָא אֵימָא לָךְ פָּטוּר, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּלֵיתֵיהּ לְמַמָּשָׁא כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: That is to say** that **one who carries out** a measure of **food** that is **less than the measure** that determines liability on Shabbat, but he does so **in a vessel, he is liable.** Although he is not liable for carrying the food out into the public domain, he is liable for carrying out the vessel. In that case, the vessel is not subordinate to the food, and therefore it is significant. **Since** the case of the flask **in which there is no perfume is comparable to** the case in which there is **less than the** required **measurement** of food **in a vessel, and it was taught** in the case of the flask that **she is liable** even though the fragrance of the perfume remains in the vessel, it stands to reason that one who carries a vessel containing less than a measure of food is also liable. **Rav Ashi said:** That is no proof because, **in general, I would say to you** that **he is exempt** in a case where there is less than the measure that determines liability for food. **However, it is different here,** in the case of the empty flask of perfume **as,** in that case, **there is no substance** at all. Because the vessel is completely empty, he is liable for carrying the flask.

The Gemara makes the analogy explicit. *Ein bah bosem, pachot mike'shiur bikli dami*—when there is no oil, it is like carrying a sub-minimum amount of food in a vessel. *Detanya: chayevet*—and the *Baraita* rules her liable. The implication is that the half-grape in a bowl—the paradigmatic case of sub-minimum food in a vessel—does render one biblically liable, since the vessel is secondary to the food and the food, though sub-minimum on its own, is carried along with its vessel as a unit.

This has practical implications in the realm of other Shabbat questions—for instance, the question of pushing a child in a stroller when there is no *eruv*. One principle is *chai nosei et atzmo*—a living being carries itself, and therefore the child may not constitute *hotzaah*. But what of the stroller? The Gemara's logic here suggests that the stroller is entirely secondary to the child being transported and should not independently implicate *hotzaah*. (This is noted as a matter of Talmudic analysis rather than *pesak*.)

### Rav Ashi's Logic

*Rav Ashi* challenges the inference drawn by Rav Adda bar Ahava. He says: in the normal case of sub-minimum food in a vessel—the half-grape in a bowl—one is indeed exempt, because the vessel is secondary to the food. Since the food alone does not constitute a minimum amount, and the vessel exists only as a container for that food, the entire unit does not meet the threshold for biblical liability. *Veshani hacha*: the case of the empty perfume bottle is different, and here is why—*de'leit lei mamasha klal*, there is no physical substance whatsoever. Fragrance is not a tangible object. Unlike a half-grape, which is at least a physical thing (merely insufficient in quantity), the residual scent in an empty bottle has no physical reality at all. One therefore cannot say that the vessel is secondary to the fragrance, as one might say the bowl is secondary to the grape. When there is nothing physical inside, the woman is simply carrying the bottle—a vessel—and the vessel alone is what is being transported. A vessel of significance is a normal object of carrying, and therefore she is *chayevet*. But this does not prove anything about the general rule of sub-minimum food in a vessel; that case remains exempt.

## Agadic: Poverty

### Amos Prooftext

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְרֵאשִׁית שְׁמָנִים יִמְשָׁחוּ״, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: זֶה פִּלְיָיטוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Related to the mention of perfume, the Gemara cites several statements. It is stated: "That drink wine in *mizrekei*, **and anoint themselves with the chief ointments;** but they are not grieved for the hurt of Joseph" (Amos 6:6). **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: "**The chief ointments" **is balsam oil.**

Having been discussing aromatic oils and women's ornaments, the Gemara pivots to an extended *agadata* section anchored in the verse from Amos: *vere'shit shemanim yimshachu*—"they anoint themselves with the choicest of oils." The context in Amos is a prophetic critique of a people who indulge in physical pleasure while ignoring God's word and the prophets' warnings.

### Pilayton Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: אַף עַל פִּלְיָיטוֹן גְּזַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ, מִשּׁוּם תַּעֲנוּג, אַמַּאי לֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דִּכְתִיב: ״הַשֹּׁתִים בְּמִזְרְקֵי יַיִן״ — רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי, חַד אָמַר קְנִישְׁקְנִין, וְחַד אָמַר שֶׁמְּזָרְקִין כּוֹסוֹתֵיהֶן זֶה לָזֶה. הָכִי נָמֵי דַּאֲסִיר? וְהָא רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא וְאִישְׁתִּי בִּקְנִישְׁקְנִין, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised an objection** from the *Tosefta*: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava issued a decree on balsam oil as well,** prohibiting its use due to mourning over the destruction of the Temple, **and** the Sages **did not agree with him. And if you say** that balsam oil is the chief ointment cited in the verse, and the decree was issued **due to** the **pleasure** it provides, **why didn't** the Sages **agree with his** decree? Doesn't the verse criticize those who do not feel the pain of the people? **Abaye said to him: And according to your reasoning, that which is written** in the same verse: **"That drink wine in *mizrekei*"; Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi** disagree over the meaning of the term *mizrekei*. **One said:** They are **multi-spouted vessels [*kenishkanin*],** wine vessels with spouts from which several people can drink at one time, **and one said that they throw [*mezarkin*] their cups to one another** in joy and jest. **Is that also prohibited? Didn't Rabba bar Rav Huna visit the house of the Exilarch and** see the Exilarch **drink** from **a multi-spouted vessel, and** Rabba bar Rav Huna **did not say anything to him?**

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: zeh pilayton*—this balsam oil we have been discussing is identified as the choicest of all oils. *Metiv Rav Yosef*: Rav Yosef challenges this identification. A *Tosefta* in Sotah records that *Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava* wished to forbid *pilayton* as part of the post-*Churban* restrictions—the series of practices curtailed as a *zecher le'Churban*, a commemoration of the Temple's destruction—but the other Sages did not agree with him. If *pilayton* is the most indulgent and pleasurable of all oils, why did the majority of Sages decline to forbid it? Presumably because it was considered a basic need rather than a luxury. But if it is truly the height of indulgence, as the verse implies, the Sages should have had strong reason to restrict it.

### Joy vs. Indulgence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: כׇּל מִידֵּי דְּאִית בֵּיהּ תַּעֲנוּג וְאִית בֵּיהּ שִׂמְחָה — גְּזַרוּ רַבָּנַן, אֲבָל מִידֵּי דְּאִית בֵּיהּ תַּעֲנוּג וְלֵית בֵּיהּ שִׂמְחָה — לָא גְזַרוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the principle is as follows: With regard to **any matter in which there is** an element of **pleasure and in which there is** an element of **joy, the Sages issued a decree** prohibiting it due to mourning over the destruction of the Temple. **However,** with regard to **a matter in which there is** an element of **pleasure and in which there is no** element of **joy, the Sages did not issue a decree.** Since there is no element of joy in balsam oil, even though it is precious and pleasurable, they did not issue a decree prohibiting it.

*Amar leh Abaye: ule'ta'amech*—and according to your logic, Abaye responds, anything mentioned in that verse in Amos should be evidence of forbidden indulgence. But look at the continuation: *hashotim bemizrekei yayin*—"those who drink wine in a *mizrak*." What is a *mizrak*? Rav Ami and Rav Assi debate: one says it refers to a vessel with openings at both ends from which multiple people could drink together; the other says it refers to the practice of tossing cups of wine from one person to another. Either way, a mode of drinking wine that is extravagant and indulgent. Yet when Rabbah bar bar Chana visited the house of the *Reish Galuta* and saw him drinking from a *kniskanin* (the same vessel, according to one opinion), he said nothing—it was not something he felt obliged to protest. So not everything mentioned in the verse is forbidden.

The real criterion, the Gemara explains, is this: *kol midi de'it beih ta'anug ve'it beih simcha, gazru beih rabbanan*—anything that combines both physical indulgence (*ta'anug*) and joyousness (*simcha*) is what the Sages restricted after the *Churban*. *Aval midi de'it beih ta'anug ve'leit beih simcha, lo gazru rabbanan*—but something that involves physical pleasure alone, without the dimension of joy, is not subject to the restriction. It is specifically the combination of indulgence and festive joy that is too much to permit in the shadow of the *Churban*.

This distinction carries practical significance for questions that arise during *sefirat ha'omer* and other periods of mourning observance. There is a widespread practice not to attend certain entertainments during *sefirah*, though this specific restriction does not appear in *Shulchan Aruch*. The Gemara's principle here—that it is the combination of joy and indulgence that is restricted, not pleasure alone—is important for understanding the correct parameters. The issues around *Churban* and *avelut* concern *simcha*, joy, not merely *ta'anug*, physical pleasure.

The passage in Amos continues: *vehashochvim al mitot shen*—"those who lie on beds of ivory"—and *useruchain al arsotam*, "and they stretch out on their couches." *Saruach* is read by the Gemara as related to *sirchon*, stench. *Amar Rabbi Yosi berabbi Chanina*: this means they would urinate in front of their beds while naked—since they were already undressed for sleep, they would relieve themselves without moving, creating a persistent stench by their sleeping place.

*Megadef beh Rabbi Abbahu*: Rabbi Abbahu mocks this interpretation. If the sin being described is merely urinating beside one's bed, how does that connect to the dramatic conclusion of the passage—*lachen ata yiglu be'rosh golim*, "therefore you shall go into exile at the head of the exiles"? That is too weighty a consequence for such an act, and the connection to indulgence described in the verse is weak. *Ela amar Rabbi Abbahu*: rather, the verse describes people eating and drinking together, reclining side by side on their couches, *vedovkin mitoteihen zu bezu*—pushing their beds together—*umachlifin nishoseihen zeh im zeh*—and exchanging wives with one another. The "stench" on the beds, *umasrichin arsotam*, refers to *shichvat zera she'eino shelahen*—seed that is not their own. The stench is thus a euphemism for sexual sin flowing from physical indulgence, and that is what explains the punishment of exile.

### Three Things

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים מְבִיאִין אֶת הָאָדָם לִידֵי עֲנִיּוּת, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הַמַּשְׁתִּין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי מִטָּתוֹ עָרוֹם, וּמְזַלְזֵל בִּנְטִילַת יָדַיִם, וְשֶׁאִשְׁתּוֹ מְקַלַּלְתּוֹ בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** On a related note, **Rabbi Abbahu said, and some say it was taught in a *baraita*: Three matters bring a person to** a state of **poverty** as a divine punishment from Heaven: **One who urinates before his bed** while **naked, and one who demeans the ritual washing of the hands, and one whose wife curses him in his presence.**

*Ve'amri lah bemasnisa tana*—and it was also taught in a *Baraita*: *gimel devarim mevi'in adam lidei aniyut*—three things bring a person to poverty: urinating naked in front of one's bed (*hamashtin mayim bifnei mitato arom*), denigrating hand-washing (*umezalzel benetilas yadayim*), and having one's wife curse him in his presence (*she'ishto mekallelto befanav*).

### Urination Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּשְׁתִּין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי מִטָּתוֹ עָרוֹם, אָמַר רָבָא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּמַהְדַּר אַפֵּיהּ לְפוּרְיֵיהּ, אֲבָל לְבָרַאי — לֵית לַן בַּהּ. וּמַהְדַּר אַפֵּיהּ לְפוּרְיֵיהּ, נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא לְאַרְעָא, אֲבָל בְּמָנָא — לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: With regard to **one who urinates before his bed** while **naked, Rava said: We only said this** prohibition in a case **where he turns his face toward his bed** and urinates toward it; **however,** if he turns his face and urinates **toward the outer** portion of the room, **we have no** problem **with it. And** where **one turns his face toward his bed, too, we only said this** prohibition in a case **where he urinates on the ground; however,** if he urinates **into a vessel, we have no** problem **with it** since that is not considered disgusting.

*Amar Rava*: the prohibition regarding urinating by one's bed is qualified. *Lo amran ella de'hadar appei le'purya*—this only applies if one faces the bed while urinating, *ve'le'ar'a*—and urinates onto the ground, so that the area around the bed becomes saturated and smells. *Aval le'varai*—if one's back is to the bed, or *aval bemana*—if it is done into a vessel (a chamber pot) which is then removed, *leit lan bah*—there is no concern. The issue is specifically the creation of a lasting stench in the sleeping area.

### Hand-Washing Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְזַלְזֵל בִּנְטִילַת יָדַיִם, אָמַר רָבָא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא מְשָׁא יְדֵיהּ כְּלָל, אֲבָל מְשָׁא וְלָא מְשָׁא — לֵית לַן בַּהּ. וְלָאו מִלְּתָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֲנָא מְשַׁאי מְלֵא חָפְנַי מַיָּא וִיהַבוּ לִי מְלֵא חָפְנַי טֵיבוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who demeans the ritual washing of the hands, Rava said: We only said** this statement in a case **where he does not wash his hands at all; however,** if **he washes** his hands **and does not wash** them with a significant amount of water, **we have no** problem **with it.** The Gemara notes: **And that is not** so, **as Rav Ḥisda said: I wash** my hands **with handfuls of water and** they **gave me** in reward **handfuls of prosperity.** Apparently, in order to garner the benefits of ritual washing of his hands, one should use a significant amount of water.

*Amar Rava*: the concern about denigrating hand-washing applies specifically to one who does not wash at all—*de'lo mashi yadei klal*. *Aval mashi velo mashi*—if one washes incompletely, that is not the problem addressed here. The *Maharsha* notes that this is not necessarily a ruling about whether one has fulfilled the *halakha* of *netilas yadayim*; it is a statement about cleanliness. The concern is the total absence of any washing, not marginal insufficiency.

However, the Gemara qualifies even this: *ve'lav milta hi*, this qualification itself is questioned. *De'amar Rav Chisda: ana mashai melo chafnai maya*—Rav Chisda said he washes his hands with a full double handful of water, completely covering both hands. *Ve'yahavu li me'hashamayim melo chafnai tivuta*—and in return, Heaven gives him full handfuls of goodness. The implication is that washing fully and completely brings abundant blessing; partial washing is not a neutral act that "covers" one. One should wash properly and thoroughly.

### Wife's Jewelry Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁאִשְׁתּוֹ מְקַלַּלְתּוֹ בְּפָנָיו, אָמַר רָבָא: עַל עִסְקֵי תַּכְשִׁיטֶיהָ. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי הוּא דְּאִית לֵיהּ וְלָא עָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one whose wife curses him in his presence, Rava said:** This is referring to a case where she curses him **over matters** relating to **her ornaments,** i.e., she complains that he does not provide her with jewelry. The Gemara comments: **And that applies only when he has** the resources to buy her jewelry **but does not do** so; however, if he does not have sufficient resources he need not be concerned.

*Al iskei tachshiteha*: the third cause of poverty—a wife who curses her husband in his presence—is connected to the *tachshit* discussion that immediately preceded this *agadata* section. Rava explains that the wife's cursing is specifically over *tachshit*, jewelry: she wants her husband to purchase jewelry for her and he refuses. *Ve'hanei mili de'is lei velo avid*—and this only constitutes a problem if he actually has the resources to do it but withholds them. The *Maharsha* observes that since the *Baraita* is describing what leads *to* poverty, it implies the person is not yet poor—he has means but refuses to use them for his wife's honor. The *Maharsha* understands the condition to mean that it is a reasonable and not extravagant expenditure given his means, yet he refuses.

A broader reading of these three causes suggests a unifying theme: each one reflects a failure of respect and dignity. Urinating in a manner that creates a stench around one's sleeping place is a failure to respect one's own living environment. Denigrating hand-washing is a failure to respect the act of preparing for food. And withholding jewelry from one's wife—the Gemara in discussions of *kibbud av va'em* and similar contexts identifies providing clothing as a primary expression of *kavod*—is a failure to respect the other. There is also a dimension of miserliness or stinginess running through all three: a reluctance to expend the minimal effort or resources needed to maintain dignity, toward oneself and toward others.

## Daughters of Zion

### Haughty Walking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב עִילָּאֵי, מַאי דִכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ יַעַן כִּי גָבְהוּ בְּנוֹת צִיּוֹן״ — שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכוֹת בְּקוֹמָה זְקוּפָה. ״וַתֵּלַכְנָה נְטוּיוֹת גָּרוֹן״ — שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכוֹת עָקֵב בְּצַד גּוּדָל. ״וּמְשַׂקְּרוֹת עֵינַיִם״ — דַּהֲוָה מָלְיָאן כּוּחְלָא לְעֵינַיְיהוּ וּמְרַמְּזָן. ״הָלוֹךְ וְטָפוֹף״ — שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכוֹת אֲרוּכָּה בְּצַד קְצָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Since the Gemara spoke of sins in the First Temple era, it continues to explain other verses with similar content. **Rava, son of Rav Ilai, interpreted** the following verse **homiletically. What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "The Lord says because the daughters of Zion are haughty** and walk with outstretched necks and wanton eyes, walking and mincing as they go and making a tinkling with their feet" (Isaiah 3:16)? "Because the daughters of Zion are haughty," indicates **that they would walk with upright stature** and carry themselves in an immodest way. **"And walk with outstretched necks,"** indicates **that they would walk** in small steps, **heel to toe,** so onlookers would notice them. **"Wanton eyes,"** indicates **that they would fill their eyes with blue eye shadow and beckon.** **"Walking and mincing [*tafof*] as they go,"** indicates **that the** wealthy women **would walk** a **tall** woman **alongside** a **short** one so that the tall woman would stand out. This is derived from the interchangeability of the letters *tet* and *tzadi*; *tzafo*, meaning, in this case, to be seen.

*Darash Rava brei de'Rav Ilai*: the Gemara now expounds verses from *Yeshayahu* describing the sins and punishments of the *benot Tzion*, the daughters of Zion. The broader context involves the critique of women's indulgence and seductive behavior in Jerusalem—a natural extension of the aromatic oils and jewelry themes throughout this daf. *Ya'an ki gavhu benot Tzion*: the daughters of Zion became haughty. *Shehayu mehalchot bekomah zkufah*—they would walk with a rigidly upright bearing, a posture of arrogance. *Vatelachna netuyot garon*: they walked with an outstretched neck. *Shehayu mehalchot eikev betzad godel*—they would walk very daintily, placing heel directly beside toe, taking small, deliberate steps rather than natural strides. Rashi understands this as a seductive, attention-drawing manner of walking. Rashi notes that the Gemara's description is specifically about married women, making the behavior not merely immodest but adulterous in its intent.

### Eyeshadow & Winking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבְרַגְלֵיהֶן תְּעַכַּסְנָה״ — אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק דְּבֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמַּטִּילוֹת מוֹר וַאֲפַרְסְמוֹן בְּמִנְעֲלֵיהֶן וּמְהַלְּכוֹת בְּשׁוּקֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁמַּגִּיעוֹת אֵצֶל בַּחוּרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בּוֹעֲטוֹת בַּקַּרְקַע וּמַתִּיזוֹת עֲלֵיהֶם וּמַכְנִיסוֹת בָּהֶן יֵצֶר הָרָע כְּאֶרֶס בְּכָעוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **"Making a tinkling [*te'akasna*] with their feet," Rabbi Yitzḥak from the school of Rabbi Ami said: This teaches that they would place myrrh and balsam in their shoes and would walk in the marketplaces of Jerusalem. And once they approached** a place where the **young men of Israel** were congregated, **they would stamp** their feet **on the ground and splash the perfume toward them and instill the evil inclination into them like venom of a viper [*ke'eres bakhos*].**

*Umeshakarot einayim*: and they would glance with their eyes—*dehavi malyan kuchla le'eineihu*, they would fill their eyes with *kuchla*, eyeshadow or eyeliner, *umramzan*, and then wink at men. The combination of painted eyes and suggestive eye contact is understood as deliberate sexual enticement.

*Haloch vetafof*: walking in a floating or teetering manner. *Shehayu mehalchot arukot betzad ketzarot*: tall women would walk beside short women, creating the visual impression that the tall women were floating above the ground, elevated above the crowd.

*Uveragleihem te'achasna*: the verse's word *te'achasna* is read in connection with *eres*, the venom of an adder. *Amar Rav Yitzchak debei Rabbi Ami*: this means they would place *mor ve'afarsimon*—myrrh and balsam—*bimnaleihem*, in their shoes. Walking through the markets of Jerusalem, when they would approach the young men of Israel, *bo'atot bakarka umatizot aleihem*, they would kick or stamp against the ground and the fragrance would splatter upward onto the men. *Umachnisos bahen yetzer hara ke'eres be'akhus*: and they would thereby introduce the *yetzer hara* into those men like the venom of a snake. These are again married women, per Rashi's reading—their seductive behavior directed at other men carries the full weight of potential adultery.

### Punishment Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי פּוּרְעֲנוּתַיְהִי? — כִּדְדָרֵישׁ רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא: ״וְהָיָה תַחַת בֹּשֶׂם מַק יִהְיֶה״ — מָקוֹם שֶׁהָיוּ מִתְבַּשְּׂמוֹת בּוֹ נַעֲשָׂה נְמָקִים נְמָקִים. ״וְתַחַת חֲגוֹרָה נִקְפָּה״ — מָקוֹם שֶׁהָיוּ חֲגוּרוֹת בְּצִלְצוֹל נַעֲשָׂה נְקָפִים נְקָפִים. ״וְתַחַת מַעֲשֶׂה מִקְשֶׁה קׇרְחָה״ — מָקוֹם שֶׁהָיוּ מִתְקַשְּׁטוֹת בּוֹ נַעֲשָׂה קְרָחִים קְרָחִים. ״וְתַחַת פְּתִיגִיל מַחֲגֹרֶת שָׂק״ — פְּתָחִים הַמְּבִיאִים לִידֵי גִּילָה, יִהְיוּ לְמַחֲגֹרֶת שָׂק. ״כִּי תַחַת יֹפִי״ — אָמַר רָבָא, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: חַלּוֹפֵי שׁוּפְרָא — כֵּיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What was their punishment** for these sins? **As Rabba bar Ulla taught: "And it shall come to pass that instead of a sweet smell, there shall be a stench;** and instead of a belt, a rope; and instead of well-set hair, baldness; and instead of a fine dress, a girding of sackcloth; instead of beauty, a brand" (Isaiah 3:24). He explains: "And it shall come to pass that instead of a sweet smell there shall be a stench," indicates that **the place that they would perfume became decaying sores.** **"And instead of a sash, a rope [*nikpe*],"** indicates that **the place where they were girded with a sash became** covered with **many bruises** [***nekafim***]. **"And instead of well-set hair, baldness,"** indicates that **the place where they would style** their hair **became bald** spots. **"And instead of a fine dress [*petigil*], a girding of sackcloth,"** indicates that the **orifices [*petaḥim*] that lead to pleasure [*gil*] will become** a place **for a girding of sackcloth.** **"Instead of beauty, a brand [*ki*]," Rava said: That is** the popular expression **that people say: Instead of beauty, a sore [*kiva*].**

*Mai puraniyoteihem?*—What are their punishments? The Gemara expounds the punishments described in subsequent verses as direct inversions of each transgression. *Ve'haya tachat bosem mak*: in the place where they applied fragrance, the flesh will rot—*nemakim nemakim*, putrefying. *Ve'tachat chagurah nikpah*: instead of the ornate belt, *nikfim nikfim*, wounds—the genital area which was adorned will be afflicted. *Ve'tachat ma'aseh miksheh karchah*: instead of an elaborate hairstyle, *krachim krachim*, bald patches. *Ve'tachat ptigil machagoret sak*: the Gemara reads *ptigil* as *petachim*, openings—the places that led to joy and pleasure will instead be covered in sackcloth. *Ki tachat yofi*: in exchange for beauty—and Rava cites a folk saying, *chilufa shufra kifa*: in the place of beauty comes sickness and sores.

### Leprosy & Afflictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְסִפַּח ה׳ קׇדְקֹד בְּנוֹת צִיּוֹן״ — אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁפָּרְחָה בָּהֶן צָרַעַת. כְּתִיב הָכָא ״וְשִׂפַּח״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״לַשְׂאֵת וְלַסַּפַּחַת״. ״וַה׳ פׇּתְהֵן יְעָרֶה״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, חַד אָמַר: שֶׁנִּשְׁפְּכוּ כְּקִיתוֹן, וְחַד אָמַר: שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ פִּתְחֵיהֶן כְּיַעַר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a different verse: **"The Lord will smite with a scab the crown of the head of the daughters of Zion** and the Lord will lay bare their secret parts" (Isaiah 3:17). And the Lord will smite with a scab the crown of the heads of the daughters of Zion; **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: This teaches that there was an outbreak of leprosy among them. It is written here, scab [*sippaḥ*], and it is written there,** among the types of leprosy: **"For a sore, and for a scab [*sappaḥat*],** and for a bright spot" (Leviticus 14:56). With regard to the verse: **And the Lord will lay bare their secret parts [*pot'hen ye'areh*], Rav and Shmuel** disagree. **One says:** It means **that they,** i.e., their innards, **were poured out [*ye'areh*] like a jug. And one says: That their orifices [*pitḥeihen*] were** covered with hair as thick **as a forest [*ya'ar*].**

*Ve'sipach Hashem kodkod benot Tzion*: God will strike the heads of the daughters of Zion. *Amar Rabbi Yose berabbi Chanina: melamed sheparcha vahen tzara'at*—this means that *tzara'at* will spread over them. The connection is linguistic: *vesipach* is related to *sapachat*, one of the forms of leprous plague listed in the Torah's *tzara'at* passages.

*VaHashem pitchan ye'areh*: the verse speaks of God "baring" or "spilling out" their *pitchan*, which the Gemara reads as *petachan*, their openings. *Sura amar Rav Yehuda*: in this matter, one opinion holds that *shenishpechu ke'kiton*—they will bleed profusely, like water poured from a pitcher, an affliction of abnormal menstrual bleeding. The other opinion holds that *shenasa'u fetacheihen ke'ya'ar*—their openings will become like a forest, meaning excessive hair will grow there. The Gemara elsewhere assumes that it was customary for women to remove hair from those areas, so this reversal is particularly pointed.

## Jerusalem Men & Cinnamon

### Jerusalem Men

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אַנְשֵׁי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם אַנְשֵׁי שַׁחַץ הָיוּ. אָדָם אוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵרוֹ: בַּמֶּה סָעַדְתָּ הַיּוֹם? בְּפַת עֲמִילָה, אוֹ בְּפַת שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲמִילָה? בְּיַיִן גּוֹרְדָּלִי, אוֹ בְּיַיִן חַרְדָּלִי? בְּמֵסַב רָחָב, אוֹ בְּמֵסַב קָצָר? בְּחָבֵר טוֹב, אוֹ בְּחָבֵר רָע? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וְכוּלָּן לִזְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of the sins of Jerusalem and the abundance that existed before its destruction, **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The people of Jerusalem were people of arrogance.** They would couch their crude behavior in euphemisms. **A person would say to another: On what did you dine today? Well-kneaded bread or bread that is not well-kneaded? On white wine or on black wine?** Sitting **on a wide divan or on a narrow divan? With a good friend or a bad friend? And Rav Ḥisda said: And all these** allude **to promiscuity.** These are all euphemisms for different types of women. Well-kneaded bread refers to a woman who is not a virgin; white wine refers to a fair-complexioned woman; a wide divan refers to a fat woman; a good friend refers to a good-looking woman.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: anshei Yerushalayim anshei shachatz hayu*—the men of Jerusalem were arrogant and boastful. Having described the seductive behavior of the women at length, the Gemara notes that the men were no less problematic. *Shehaya adam omer le'chavero*: a man would say to his friend, *bameh sa'adta hayom*—"what did you eat today?" And the conversation would proceed in elaborate sexual innuendo: *be'pat amila o be'pat she'eina amila*—worked dough or unworked dough, alluding to whether the woman involved was a virgin. *Be'yayin gudrani o be'yayin chardali*—white wine or dark wine, referring to the women they had been with. *Be'mita rechavah o be'mita katzarah*—a wide bed or a narrow one. *Ve'chaver tov o ve'chaver ra*—a good companion or a poor one. *Amar Rav Chisda: ve'chulan le'znut*—Rav Chisda states that all of these are allusions to sexual transgression.

### Cinnamon Fragrance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַחֲבָה אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: עֲצֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם שֶׁל קִינָּמוֹן הָיוּ, וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁהָיוּ מַסִּיקִין מֵהֶן, רֵיחָן נוֹדֵף בְּכָל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of Jerusalem, **Raḥava said** that **Rabbi Yehuda said: The logs of Jerusalem** used for fuel **were from** the **cinnamon** tree, **and when they would ignite them, their fragrance would waft through all of Eretz Yisrael.**

The Gemara concludes on a note of poignant beauty, connecting back to the theme of fragrance that has pervaded the daf. *Amar Rav Chaba amar Rav Yehuda: atzei Yerushalayim shel kinamon hayu*—the trees of Jerusalem were of cinnamon. Cinnamon wood was so plentiful in Jerusalem that it was used as ordinary firewood. *Uv'sha'ah shehayu masikin mehen*: when it was burned, *reichan nodef bechol Eretz Yisrael*—its fragrance would spread throughout all of the Land of Israel, suffusing the entire country with sweetness.

### Post-Churban Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשֶּׁחָרְבָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם נִגְנְזוּ, וְלֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר אֶלָּא כִּשְׂעוֹרָה, וּמִשְׁתְּכַח בֵּי גַזָּא דְּצִימְצְמַאי מַלְכְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And since Jerusalem was destroyed, these** fragrant logs **were buried, and only a** sliver the size of a grain of **barley remains, and it is located in the treasury of [*gazzai*] Tzimtzemai the queen.**

*Umishecharva Yerushalayim, nignazu*—when Jerusalem was destroyed, it was hidden away. *Ve'lo nishtayer ela ke'se'orah*—only a tiny amount remains, no more than a barley seed. *Umishtachach be'gizei de'Tzintzmai malkata*—and that remnant can be found in the treasure houses of the queen known as Tzintzmai, whoever she may be.
