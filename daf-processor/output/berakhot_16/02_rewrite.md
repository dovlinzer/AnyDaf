# Berakhot 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kavana in Shema

### Formal Act of Shema

The present daf, 16, opens by shifting gears from the discussions of the previous perek, which focused on the formal precision of the act of reciting *shema* — whether one must audibly hear what one is saying, whether letters must be carefully enunciated, and so on. Before moving to the next *mishna*, it is worth noting a final observation about those earlier discussions: those demands reflect the character of *krias shema* as a formal act. *Lechatchila*, we do require that one hear one's own recitation and that the letters be carefully enunciated. But *b'diavad*, even if neither of those requirements was met, one has fulfilled the obligation — and this is because of the emphasis on *achar kavanat halev hadvarim*, the principle that what ultimately animates the act is the intention of the heart. At the end of the day, *krias shema* is an act of *kabbalas ol malchus shamayim*, and even if the recitation is not entirely perfect, one is *yotzei*.

It is worth noting that this entire discussion — about audibility, about precise enunciation — does not appear in connection with *davening*, with *Shmoneh Esrei*. There, too, one is supposed to recite softly, and there, too, one might have raised questions about *medakdek*, about careful pronunciation, yet none of this surfaces in the context of *tefila*. Part of the reason is that *krias shema* involves a formal act of reciting — a text that comes from the *Torah* itself — and that formal act naturally generates *lechatchila* criteria. But *b'diavad*, since the animating purpose is *kabbalas ol malchus shamayim* through speech and thought together, as the *braita* citing Rabbi Meir emphasizes from the words *al levavecha*, the *mitzvah* can be fulfilled even when the formal precision of the act falls short.

### Three Kavana Categories

A student raised the question of what precisely *kavana* means across various *sugyot*, since in some contexts it seems to denote intent to fulfill the *mitzvah*, while in others it seems to mean comprehension of the words. In the context of *tefila*, where the *Rambam* and other commentators lay out the issue most fully, there are in fact three distinct categories of *kavana*.

The first is intent to fulfill the *mitzvah* — the generic question of *mitzvos tzrichos kavanah* — and that is not what is under discussion here, since it is not specific to the nature of *shema* or *tefila*. The second is awareness of what the words mean — knowing the content of what one is reciting. By *Shmoneh Esrei*, the ruling is that *b'diavad*, if one had awareness during the first *bracha* even while not attending carefully to the remaining *brachos*, that is sufficient. The third category — and this is what is most central both to *shema* and *tefila* — is awareness of the import of the act itself. By *tefila*, this means, as the *Rambam* states, knowing that one is *omeid lifnei hamakom*, standing before God. The way Reb Chaim explains the *Rambam*, this awareness must be sustained throughout *davening* — which, as Reb Efraim notes with some understatement, would mean that most people are not *yotzei* — but at least this constitutive awareness is what makes *tefila* what it is. Knowing what the words mean is required only for the first *bracha*; the awareness of standing before God is what is fundamental.

The same logic applies to *shema* by analogy. When the *gemara* says that *kavana* is required up to a certain point, the most natural reading is not that one must know the precise meaning of every word, but rather that one must have *kavana* in the sense of awareness of the overall import of the act — that through these words one is performing *kabbalas ol malchus shamayim*, accepting God as king and acknowledging the obligation of *ahavas Hashem*.

### Kavana Differs by Mitzvah

This leads naturally to a broader question: Is this emphasis on *kavana* specific to *tefila* and *shema*, or does it apply across all *mitzvos*? By most *mitzvos*, whose essence is the act, the only question is whether one needs to be aware that one is fulfilling a *mitzvah* — and we might even rule that one does not, which is the classic debate of *mitzvos tzrichos kavanah*. What is unique about *tefila* and *shema* is that the act is not simply one of producing words. *Davening* is an act of being *omeid lifnei HaMakom*; *krias shema* is an act of *kabbalas ol malchus shamayim*. In both cases, *kavana* is not an additional criterion layered onto the formal act — it is constitutive of the act itself. Without it, the act has not been performed.

### Speech as Action

This connects to a deeper philosophical point: to what degree is speech simply expressed thought, such that the thought is necessary for the speech to count as genuine expression at all? By *shema*, there is at least a dual structure — the formal act of reciting on one side, and the *kavana* on the other — so both elements are present. By *Shmoneh Esrei*, the emphasis falls far more heavily on the *kavana*, and it is precisely this that the next *mishna* will illuminate.

## Workers at Height

### Mishna: Workers Read Alo…

The *mishna* states: *ha'umnin korin berosh ha'ilan uverosh hanidbach* — laborers who are at the top of a tree picking fruit, or at the top of a layer of scaffolding at a construction site, may recite *shema* from where they are and need not descend. *Mah she'ein ken la'asos ken bitefila* — this is not the case for *davening*, where the requirements of *kavana* are far more stringent and *Chazal* insist on a settled state of mind before one may pray.

### Ritual vs. Employer

This *mishna* touches on a tension that plays out constantly: the balance between ritual requirements — performing them perfectly — and one's responsibilities to other people. Here, a worker perched atop a tree might reason that he can only achieve proper *kavana* for *shema* by descending to the ground. But he is being paid by his employer, and it is not fair to take that time at the employer's expense. The *mishna* therefore rules: do not descend; *shema* can be said at the top of the tree, even without the fullest possible *kavana*, because a sufficient level of *kavana* can be achieved there. The stricter requirements — descending, composing oneself — apply to *tefila*. There is also a subtler question lurking: is the worker even permitted to pause from his work to say *shema* at all? That question will be addressed as the *gemara* develops.

## Groom Exemption (III)

### Groom Exempt Mishna

The *mishna* continues: *chasan patur mikrias Shema lailah harishon ve'ad motza'ei Shabbos im lo asah ma'aseh* — a groom is exempt from *krias shema* on the first night, and indeed until Saturday night, provided the couple has not yet had marital relations. The background is that according to the beginning of Kesuvos, a marriage takes place on Wednesday; there are thus four nights — Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, and Saturday night — during which the exemption applies. After that point, either the urgency has passed or the groom has grown accustomed to the situation, and he is no longer considered so preoccupied as to be exempt.

### Rabban Gamliel Reads

The *mishna* then records: *ma'aseh beRabban Gamliel shenasa ishah vekara lailah harishon* — Rabban Gamliel married and recited *shema* on the very first night. His students said to him: *limadtanu rabbeinu shechasan patur mikrias Shema* — have you not taught us yourself that a groom is exempt? Rabban Gamliel replied: *eini shome'a lachem levatel mimeni malchus shamayim afilu sha'ah achas* — I will not listen to you in order to set aside from myself the yoke of heaven even for one moment.

This response underscores the dual nature of the concept of *kabbalas ol malchus shamayim*: on one hand, the "yoke" implies the gravity of the demand, such that the exemption exists because the groom genuinely cannot fulfill it adequately; on the other hand, the yoke is so precious that Rabban Gamliel refuses to be separated from it even for a night. It is worth noting in passing that this is already the second story in Berachos featuring Rabban Gamliel. The *gemara* also notes the general principle — developed more fully in the context of *tefila* — that many of the exemptions *Chazal* articulate are based on the assumption that one lacks sufficient *kavana*. The *Rishonim* observe that since nowadays nobody has sufficient *kavana* to begin with, those exemptions cannot be invoked, because applying them would mean no one would ever be *yotzei* any *davening*. Accordingly, the exemption of the groom on his wedding night is likewise no longer applied in practice today.

## Workers at Height (II)

### Shema Aloft, Tefila Below

The *gemara* now cites a *braita*: *ha'umnin korin berosh ha'ilan uverosh hanidbach* — the laborers may read *shema* at the top of the tree and at the top of the scaffold. *Umispallelin berosh hazayis uverosh hate'ena* — they may daven at the top of an olive tree or a fig tree. The distinction is that in most trees there is a real concern of falling, which would prevent proper *kavana* for *tefila*. But olive trees and fig trees — with their broad, low bases — allow one to be situated securely enough to daven without fear. *Resh kol ha'ilanos yored lemata umispallel* — for all other trees, one must descend to daven.

The *gemara* raises a challenge: Rav Mari the son of the daughter of Shmuel — the *gemara* notes that he was identified through his mother rather than his father, as Rashi explains that his father was a non-Jew who had relations with Shmuel's daughter during captivity; this man eventually converted and is known elsewhere in the *gemara* as Issur Giyora — poses a contradiction to Rava. The *mishna* says workers may read *shema* aloft — implying *kavana* is of no concern for *shema* whatsoever. Yet we also learn: *hakorei es shema tzarich sheyachavein libo* — one who reads *shema* must direct one's heart, and this is derived from the verbal parallel between *shema Yisrael* and *hasket ushema Yisrael* — "give ear and listen, Israel" — which implies deliberate attention. Rava was silent and offered no response.

Rav Mari then asked: had he heard an answer? Rava replied that Rav Sheshet had addressed this: *v'hu shebeteilin mimlachtan vekorin* — the *mishna* is speaking only of workers who stop their work before reciting *shema*. The initial assumption was that they recite *shema* while continuing to work; Rav Sheshet clarifies that they do indeed pause from their labor, even if they do not descend. This is sufficient *kavana* for *shema*, which requires less than *tefila* but does require some minimal measure.

A further challenge is raised from a *braita* in which Beis Hillel rules, based on the verse *uvlechtecha vaderech*, that workers may recite *shema* while engaged in their labor. The *gemara* resolves this: *lo kashya, ha beperek rishon, ha beperek sheni* — the first *perek* requires pausing; the second *perek* does not. Tosafos adds that this formulation should not be taken too literally — according to Rava, only the very first *pasuk* requires *kavana*, and accordingly it is specifically for that opening *pasuk* that one must stop and focus. After that, one may continue working while reciting the remainder of *shema*.

### Worker Education

One point worth pausing over: the *gemara*'s entire discussion assumes that ordinary laborers knew *Shmoneh Esrei* by heart. In a world without *siddurim*, this was a significant educational baseline. It is true that many people in that era would fulfill their obligation through the *shliach tzibur*, but the *braita*'s scenario — workers in the field or up trees at some distance from a *shul* — presupposes that they could daven on their own.

This raises a related question: when these workers say *shema*, are they saying it with or without the surrounding *brachos*? If they will be able to return to their location and daven *Shmoneh Esrei* in *zmanah* later, the logical structure would be to say *shema* with its *brachos* leading into *Shmoneh Esrei* at the appropriate time. If not, then they would presumably say *shema* with its *brachos* now. But this creates a further question: what of *smichas geula l'tefila* — the requirement to join the *bracha* of *gaal Yisrael* directly to *Shmoneh Esrei* without interruption? If the worker says *shema* with its *brachos* but then eats before davening, as the *braita* below describes, the sequence itself would constitute a *hefsek*. One might prioritize *shema* with its *brachos* over *smichas geula l'tefila* — the former being the original *takana* for the full fulfillment of *krias shema*, while the latter appears to have been added subsequently — but this question deserves further analysis and is likely addressed in the *poskim*.

### Employer's Obligation

*Uva'al habayis bein kach uvein kach yored lemata umispallel* — the owner himself, however, must always descend to daven, regardless of what type of tree he is in. Rashi's explanation is that everyone has the same problem of insufficient *kavana* when aloft, but in the case of the workers, that deficit is balanced against the cost to the employer. The employer bears no such counterbalancing consideration — he loses nothing by descending except his own time and effort — and therefore, even where fear of falling is not an issue, we still require him to come down. He cannot achieve the settled state of mind needed for *tefila* while up in the tree, and since there is no competing obligation to someone else, he must make the sacrifice.

## Groom Exemption (III)

### Exemption Grounds

The *gemara* now turns to the theoretical basis for the groom's exemption. The *braita* derives from the verse *beshivtecha beveisecha* that one engaged in a *mitzvah* is exempt from *shema* — *perat le'osek bamitzvah* — and from *uvlechtecha vaderech* that a groom is exempt — *perat lechasan*. The *braita* then states: *hakoneh es habesulah patur ve'es ha'almanah chayav* — one marrying a virgin is exempt, but one marrying a widow is obligated. Presumably the difference lies in the greater anxiety surrounding the wedding night with a virgin, including the *halakhic* concerns about *betulim* detailed in Kesuvos.

The *gemara* asks: *mai mashma*, how do we derive this from the verse? Rav Pappa provides an answer — the same answer given word-for-word earlier in the first perek, as Rashi himself notes by simply cross-referencing: *kvar peirashtia beperek rishon*. The *gemara* derives from *uvlechtecha vaderech* — "in your going on the way" — that it is specifically *your* discretionary going, a *reshus*, that generates the obligation. Where one is engaged in a *mitzvah*, one is exempt. The *gemara* then challenges: if the exemption is based on *osek bamitzvah*, why limit it to the one marrying a virgin? Getting married is a *mitzvah* in all cases, including when one marries a widow. The *gemara* answers: *hacha tarid vehacha lo tarid* — here he is distracted and there he is not.

This generates the central debate: is the groom's exemption grounded in *osek bamitzvah*, or in simple distraction — *tirda* — that prevents proper *kavana*? The *gemara* presses: if it is about distraction, then someone whose boat sank and who is consumed with worry should also be exempt. The *gemara* responds by citing Rabbi Abba bar Zavda in the name of Rav: an *aveil*, a mourner, is obligated in all *mitzvos* except *tefillin* on the first day, since *tefillin* are described as *pe'er*, glory, which is inappropriate for a day of acute grief. But a mourner is certainly distracted by grief and nonetheless must say *shema*. This shows that ordinary distraction, even intense distraction, does not create an exemption.

The *gemara* responds: *hatam tirda d'reshus, hacha tirda d'mitzvah* — there it is a discretionary distraction; here the distraction is bound up with a *mitzvah* one is about to perform. One is mentally preoccupied with an act that is itself a *mitzvah*, which constitutes a form of mental *osek bamitzvah*, even if it is less than a full *osek bamitzvah* in the technical sense.

At the end of the day, it is difficult to fully resolve why the groom's case should be categorized as *osek bamitzvah* while the mourner's case is not, particularly since the mourner's grief is also, in a sense, connected to the *mitzvah* of *avelus*. Perhaps part of the answer is that the entire structure of *avelus* is designed to move a person *out* of complete absorption and back toward functioning life — and requiring the mourner to say *shema* reflects that intention. This may in fact be the difference between *aninus* and *avelus*: *aninus* is the state of total absorption immediately after death and before burial, during which we do not ask the person to exit their grief; *avelus* is the beginning of the process of emerging from that absorption, and requiring *shema* reflects the expectation that the mourner is beginning to re-engage. To validate a distraction-based exemption for an *aveil* would entrench the very absorption that *avelus* is meant to move beyond.

This also bears on the case of Rabban Gamliel. According to the *gemara*, if the groom's exemption is grounded in *osek bamitzvah* rather than *kavana*, then Rabban Gamliel could not simply claim "I am able to focus" and thereby opt back in — because *osek bamitzvah* operates as a categorical exemption from performing another *mitzvah*, not a waiver that one's personal capacity can override. If, on the other hand, the exemption is grounded in the inability to have *kavana*, then Rabban Gamliel's claim is coherent: he is asserting that he personally is capable of the required *kavana* on his wedding night. The *gemara* seems to work through this tension without fully resolving it.

## Worker Meal Rights

### Worker Obligations

The *gemara* cites a *braita*: *hapoalim shehayu osim melacha etzel baal habayis, korin krias shema umvarchin lefaneha uleachareha* — workers employed by a homeowner may recite *shema* and may make the *brachos* both before and after it. *V'ochlin pittan umvarchin lefaneha uleachareha* — they may eat their bread and make the *bracha* beforehand and bentch afterward. *Umispallelin tefilas shel shmoneh esrei* — and they may daven the full *Shmoneh Esrei*.

### Meal Blessings

The *hava amina* that the *gemara* is implicitly displacing here is striking: one might have thought that a worker's responsibility to his employer was so pressing that he could not take even the moment required to say *Hamotzi lechem min ha'aretz* before eating. The *braita* rules that these basic ritual requirements — the *brachos* of *shema*, *Hamotzi*, and benching — are all permitted, because *Chazal* treat them as implicit terms of any employment arrangement. Once the law declares these acts permissible, the implicit structure of the employment agreement incorporates them.

It is worth noting, though, that the *braita* does not mention washing before the meal. Whether that omission is significant is left to further analysis.

The ordering of activities in the *braita* — *shema* with its *brachos*, then eating, then *Shmoneh Esrei* — is curious. One might ask why the worker does not simply complete *shema* with its *brachos* and proceed immediately to *Shmoneh Esrei*, thereby preserving *smichas geula l'tefila*. The most likely answer is that the time for *Shmoneh Esrei* has not yet arrived when these workers, who begin before dawn, say *shema*. The sequence therefore reflects an actual temporal gap: they recite *shema* in *zmanah*, eat, and then daven when *zman tefila* arrives.

### Prayer Restrictions

The *braita* continues: *aval ein yordin lifnei hateivah* — but workers may not serve as *shliach tzibur* at a *shul*, nor may they perform *birkas kohanim*. The prohibition on going to *shul* to lead the service is fairly obvious, but the addition of *birkas kohanim* reflects an analogous logic: one might think that a *kohen* among the workers has a special obligation — a role that depends on him — that would justify leaving work. The *braita* rules that it does not.

### Shortened Prayer

The *gemara* then cites a *braita* permitting workers to say only *me'ein shmoneh esrei*, the abridged *Shmoneh Esrei* known as *Havineinu* — the first three *brachos*, a middle summary paragraph encapsulating all the middle petitions, and the final three *brachos*. The *gemara* says: *amar Rav Sheshet lo kashya, ha Rabban Gamliel veha Rabbi Yehoshua* — this is a dispute. Rabbi Yehoshua holds that the abridged form may be used by anyone, any day. Rabban Gamliel holds the full *Shmoneh Esrei* is required except under special circumstances. The *gemara* presses: if it is Rabbi Yehoshua's view, why does the *braita* specify workers? Even a private individual could say *Havineinu* on any given day. The *gemara* concludes: *ella hadei v'adei Rabban Gamliel* — both the permissive and the restrictive *baraisos* reflect Rabban Gamliel's position. Under Rabban Gamliel, the abridged form is permitted for workers in certain circumstances and not in others.

### Worker Protection

The distinction, says the *gemara*, is: *kan b'osim bis'charan, kan b'osim bisudatan* — one case involves workers paid a monetary wage; the other involves workers whose remuneration is their meal. Rashi's explanation — understood in context — is that *Chazal* are functioning here as social engineers, protecting people at the lowest rung of the economic scale. If all you receive for your labor is a meal, *Chazal* are going to grant you a larger allowance for your religious obligations. If you are receiving a monetary wage, you have to perform only an abridged *Shmoneh Esrei*, limiting the time taken from your employer. There may also be a secondary consideration: a well-paid worker who takes more time is, in some sense, taking more from his employer.

An alternative reading of the distinction is possible: perhaps *osim bisudatan* refers not to low-wage workers but to workers whose terms of employment include a meal break. In that case, the implicit employment agreement already includes built-in breaks, and *Chazal* read that as extending to a longer break for *davening* as well. This reading focuses on what the employment agreement implicitly provides, rather than on *Chazal*'s protective intervention for the disadvantaged. The first reading appears more aligned with Rashi's understanding.

### Employment Models

The *gemara* then cites a second *braita* that seems to contradict the first: *hapoalim shehayu osim melacha l'baal habayis, korein krias shema umispallelin v'ochlin pittan v'ein mevarchin lefaneha aval mevarchin leachareha* — in this version, workers who eat may not make the *bracha* before eating (*Hamotzi*), since that is only Rabbinic, though they must bentch afterward since that is Biblical. Furthermore, even the bentching is abridged: *bracha rishona k'tikunah* — the first *bracha* is said in full; the second opens with the theme of *birchas ha'aretz*, the blessing of the land; and the theme of *boneh Yerushalayim* is folded into that second *bracha*; the fourth *bracha* is omitted entirely, as it is Rabbinic. This *braita* thus allows only the bare Biblical minimum: *shema* alone without its surrounding *brachos*, and an abridged bentching without its Rabbinic components.

The *gemara* reconciles the two *baraisos*: *b'meh devarim amurim? B'osim bis'charan* — the stringent version applies to workers paid a monetary wage, who have the least latitude. *Aval osim bisudatan* — workers paid in meals receive the fuller allowance. An additional case is added: *o shehaya baal habayis meiseiv imahem* — or if the employer is dining together with them, that signals that a larger break is acceptable, and in that case all *brachos* are said in full.

The overall trajectory here is striking. The *mishna* seemed to tip strongly toward full fulfillment of ritual obligations — come down from the tree, do what you need to do, make sure your *kavana* is right. The *gemara* reveals a more nuanced and in places contrary picture: there are circumstances under which *Chazal* actively limit a worker's religious fulfillment, where the responsibility to the employer not only reduces how well one can perform the *mitzvah* but actually forecloses performing parts of it altogether, including Rabbinic obligations.

A question is also raised regarding *Birkas Hamazon*: does the omission of the second and fourth *brachos* in the abridged form imply that one is not *yotzei d'oraisa* by saying only the first *bracha*? The general assumption among the *poskim* is that the three Biblical themes — *hazan et hakol*, *al ha'aretz v'al hamazon*, and *boneh Yerushalayim* — are all Biblically required, and the fourth alone is Rabbinic. This question will be explored further in the seventh *perek*.

## Groom Exemption (III)

### Virgin vs. Widow

Returning now to the question of the groom's exemption, the *gemara* notes: *hakoneh es habesulah patur ve'es ha'almanah chayav* — the one who marries a virgin is exempt, but the one who marries a widow is obligated. The distinction presumably reflects greater anxiety in the case of marrying a virgin, compounded perhaps by the *halakhic* concerns about *betulim* discussed extensively in Kesuvos.

### Mental Engagement

The deeper question is what precisely underlies the exemption. The *gemara* explores whether it is the categorical rule of *osek bamitzvah* or the more functional question of distraction and *kavana*. As noted above, the *gemara* introduces the proof from the *aveil*, who is obligated in *shema* despite being distracted by grief, to show that ordinary distraction does not suffice. The *gemara* responds: the mourner's distraction is *tirda d'reshus* while the groom's is *tirda d'mitzvah* — the groom is mentally engaged with an act he is about to perform that is itself a *mitzvah*.

It is, however, genuinely difficult to accept that the groom's case constitutes more of an *osek bamitzvah* than the mourner's, particularly since the mourner is actively engaged in the *mitzvah* of *avelus*. The most satisfying resolution is perhaps the one suggested above: the mourner is not exempt because the very structure of *avelus* is designed to move one out of total absorption. The groom's mental preoccupation, by contrast, is directed entirely forward, toward an act he has not yet performed and is still planning — and *Chazal* regarded that form of anticipatory mental engagement with a *mitzvah* as sufficient to constitute a version of *osek bamitzvah*, even if it falls short of the full technical category.

The tension carries directly into the case of Rabban Gamliel. If the exemption is purely about *osek bamitzvah*, the categorical exemption would prohibit him from performing another *mitzvah* — he could not simply override it by claiming personal confidence in his *kavana*. If it is about *kavana* and distraction, then his claim — that he will not surrender even one moment of *kabbalas ol malchus shamayim* — is legally coherent: he is asserting that his personal capacity allows him to have the required *kavana* despite the circumstances.

## Rabban Gamliel Stories

### Bathing in Mourning

The *gemara* now turns to two further stories about Rabban Gamliel. The first: *rachatz layla harishon shemes ishto* — he bathed on the very first night after his wife died. His students said to him: *lamedtanu rabbeinu she'aveil asur lirchotz* — have you not taught us that a mourner is forbidden to bathe? He replied: *eini keshe'ar bnei adam, istnis ani* — I am not like other people; I am an *istnis*, a fastidious person who cannot go a day without bathing.

### Istenis Exception

Tosafos explains this in terms of the nature of the prohibition: the bathing forbidden during *avelus* is bathing of pleasure. For someone who is *fastidious* and suffers genuine discomfort from not bathing, the act is not pleasure-bathing but necessity-bathing, and therefore falls outside the prohibition entirely. This has obvious practical relevance, as the same *din* applies during the Nine Days when one may bathe to clean off sweat and grime. The *gemara*'s own language, however, suggests a different analysis — it sounds not like the act is categorically outside the prohibition, but rather that it is genuinely prohibited yet waived in his case due to his special circumstances. The *gemara* later explains this waiver through the rubric of *aninus* versus *aveilus*.

### Tabi the Servant

The second story: *ucheshe'meis Tabi avdo, kibel alav tanchumim* — when Tabi his servant died, Rabban Gamliel accepted people who came to offer him consolation. His students said: *lo limadetanu rabbeinu she'ein mekablin tanchumim al ha'avadim* — have you not taught us that one does not accept consolers for the death of slaves? He replied: *ein Tabi avdi k'she'ar kol ha'avadim, kasher hayah* — Tabi was not like other slaves; he was a genuinely good and *kosher* person.

The question the *gemara* implicitly raises — and which Rabban Gamliel's statement seems designed to address — is not that *Chazal* thought poorly of slaves as a class. Rather, the concern is one of boundaries. Slaves become like members of the household, and treating their death with the full apparatus of communal mourning risks blurring the lines between free people and slaves in ways that have consequences for *yuchesin* and other *halachic* areas. The boundary is maintained not because slaves are unworthy of recognition as persons but to preserve a social and *halachic* distinction. Rabban Gamliel was prepared to make an exception for Tabi because Tabi was exceptional.

### Personal vs. Legal

This brings up the question of how to read Rabban Gamliel's pattern of behavior throughout these stories. His students consistently remind him of his own rulings — on the wedding night, on bathing during mourning, on consolers for slaves — and each time he explains why his case is different. One could read these stories as Rabban Gamliel simply making his own rules for himself. But a more generous and arguably more accurate reading is that his students exemplify a certain kind of *rosh katan*, a narrow application of law, unable to see that within the *halakhic* system there is genuine latitude, and that cases must sometimes be evaluated individually rather than treated as uniform instantiations of a rule. Rabban Gamliel demonstrates that he is above the schematic application and can read the particular case. As one participant in the discussion framed it: he is setting up a model in which rules are contextualized.

### Son's Caveat

The *gemara* then adds: *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer: lo kol harotzeh litol es hashem yitol* — not everyone who wishes to take on God's name in this way may do so. Rabban Gamliel's son qualifies his father's legacy: my father was a special individual, and not every groom who believes he can achieve *kavana* on his wedding night should presume he is able to do what my father did. The debate about the legacy of Rabban Gamliel's actions — whether they represent latitude within the law that extends to all, or a personal standard available only to exceptional individuals — is thus preserved within the text itself.

### Aninut vs. Aveilus

The *gemara* explains the theoretical basis for Rabban Gamliel's bathing on the night after his wife's death: *kasvar aninus lailah d'rabanan* — he held that *aninus* (technically, the relevant status here is *aveilus*) of the nighttime following the day of death is only Rabbinic, derived from the verse *ve'acharisah k'yom mar* — "and its end is like a bitter day" — which implies that the Biblical status applies only to the day, not to the following night. And *uve-makom istanis lo gazru bah rabbanan* — in a case of someone who is genuinely *fastidious*, the Rabbis waived their enactment.

The *gemara* here is speaking about *aveilus* rather than *aninus* strictly speaking — the period after burial — and the *gemara*'s own analysis holds that Biblical *avelus* applies, at most, only to the first day, and possibly not even then, with everything afterward being Rabbinic. The *gemara* thus posits: Rabban Gamliel held that the nighttime obligation is Rabbinic, and the Rabbinic enactment was waived for him as an *istnis*. This contrasts with Tosafos's approach, which holds that the act itself falls outside the prohibition because it was not pleasure-bathing. The *gemara* presents a different logic: it is a genuinely prohibited act, but the prohibition is Rabbinic and yields to the specific condition of the *istnis*.

## Mourning Slaves

### Slave Mourning Rules

The *gemara* cites a *braita*: *avadim ushfachos ein omdin aleihem be-shura ve'ein omrim aleihem birchas avelim* — for slaves and maidservants, one does not form the *shura*, the double line of consolers through which mourners walk after burial, and one does not recite *birchas avelim*, the formal blessing given to mourners. Nor does one perform other practices of consolation for *avelim*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Story

The *gemara* then relates: *maaseh umeisah shifchaso shel Rabbi Eliezer* — the maidservant of Rabbi Eliezer died. His students came to console him. When he saw them approaching, he fled to the upper floor; they followed him there. He moved to an antechamber; they followed. He entered the main hall; they followed still.

### Slaves Not Mourned

Finally he said to them: *kimdumeh ani she'atem nichvim befoshrim* — I thought even lukewarm water would scald you, meaning I assumed you were sharp enough to pick up a subtle cue. *Achshav ei atem nichvim afilu bechamei chamim* — but apparently even boiling water does not register with you. How much more obvious does this need to be? *Lo kach shanisi lachem* — have I not taught you: *avadim ushfachos ein omdin aleihem beshura ve'ein omrim aleihem birchas avelim* — none of these mourning rituals apply to slaves?

### Property Loss Formula

*Ela mah omrim aleihem* — so what does one say when a slave has died and one wishes to acknowledge the loss? *Keshem she'omrim lo l'adam al shoro ve'al chamoro she-meisu, HaMakom yemalei lecha chesroncha* — just as one would say to someone whose ox or donkey died: "May God fill your loss." That is the formula — not *tanchumim*, not the language of mourning, but the language of property loss.

### Slave Personhood

This is a striking passage, because it treats the slave entirely as property — not merely as outside the circle of family mourning, but as equivalent to a draft animal. The tension in *halacha* between slaves as property and slaves as persons runs through numerous areas: *halachically*, non-Jewish slaves held a clear property status in many respects, yet they also possessed dimensions of personhood — they could perform *mitzvos*, they had certain protections, they were subject to their own *halachic* obligations upon immersion. The formula *HaMakom yemalei lecha chesroncha* is notably ambiguous: it is literally "May God fill your loss," which could be read as simply expressing sympathy for any significant loss. But in this context it clearly implies substitutability — one slave for another — and that is precisely what gives it its harsh edge. It is not *HaMakom yenachem etchem*, "May God comfort you," with its irreplaceable overtone. It is the language of a deficit that can in principle be made whole.

This also opens an interesting practical question: does the formula *HaMakom yemalei lecha chesroncha* establish a precedent for expressing sympathy over the loss of an animal, or more broadly, over the loss of something to which one has a deep emotional attachment — a pet, for example — even when full mourning rituals do not apply?

### Rabbi Yose Dissent

A second *braita* takes up the question of *hesped*, the eulogy: *avadim ushfachos ein maspidin otan* — one does not eulogize slaves. This goes further still: not only is there no formal community mourning, there is no public acknowledgment of who this person was, what they accomplished, what they stood for. Rabbi Yose dissents: *im eved kasher hu, omrim alav: hoy ish tov vene'eman venehene miyegio* — if the slave was a righteous and trustworthy person, one may say: "Woe, this was a good man, faithful, who benefited from the work of his hands." The students respond: *im ken mah hanachta likesherim* — if that is what you say for a slave, what will you leave to say for free people? Rabbi Yose's proposed formula is, in other words, so dignified as to blur precisely the distinction the law sought to maintain.

### Law vs. Compassion

The final observation is worth dwelling on. There is a fascinating contrast between the two stories: in the Rabban Gamliel story, his students consistently tried to hold him to the law and he pushed back, showing the latitude within the *halacha*. Here, the students of Rabbi Eliezer want to act beyond the law — responding to their teacher's obvious grief with an instinct of compassion — while Rabbi Eliezer rigorously held them to the letter of the law. The students in both cases are responding to what they perceive as the human situation in front of them, but Rabbi Eliezer insists: this is the *halacha*, and the *halacha* means what it says.
