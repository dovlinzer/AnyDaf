# Zevachim 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Melikah Treifah Bird

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Its status is like any other carcass of an unslaughtered kosher bird, and its meat **renders** one who swallows it **ritually impure.**

The daf opens in the midst of a three-way debate whose core question is whether *melikah* performed on a bird that is a *treifah* — specifically, a *korban* bird — causes that bird to become a *neveilah* with attendant *tumah*, or not. The background is as follows: under normal circumstances, when *melikah* is performed correctly on a *korban* bird, the bird does not acquire *neveilah* status. The *Torah* accepts *melikah* as the prescribed mode of slaughter for bird sacrifices, and so a properly executed *melikah* does not produce *tumah*. But what happens when the *melikah* is invalid — for instance, because the bird was a *treifah* and therefore unfit as a *korban*? Is it then comparable to other forms of invalid *melikah* that do produce *neveilah* status?

### Meir vs. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: קַל וָחוֹמֶר; אִם נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה, שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – שְׁחִיטָתָהּ מְטַהֶרֶת טְרֵיפָתָהּ מִטּוּמְאָתָהּ; נִבְלַת הָעוֹף, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתְּהֵא שְׁחִיטָתוֹ מְטַהֶרֶת טְרֵיפָתוֹ מִטּוּמְאָתוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir said:** My opinion can be inferred ***a fortiori*. If an animal carcass transmits impurity** to a person **through touching** it **and through carrying** it, and nevertheless the **slaughter of** an animal **purifies it,** even if it is **a *tereifa*, from its impurity,** i.e., its slaughter prevents it from assuming the impurity status of a carcass, then with regard to **a bird carcass, which** possesses a lesser degree of impurity, as it **does not transmit impurity** to a person **through touching** it **and through carrying** it, but only through swallowing it, **is it not logical that its slaughter should purify it,** even if it is **a *tereifa*, from its impurity?**

Rabbi Meir holds that the *melikah* of a *treifah* bird does not render it a *neveilah*, and therefore does not cause *tumah*. Rabbi Yehuda goes considerably further. He maintains that even the *shechitah* — the ordinary slaughter — of a bird that is a *treifah* renders it a *neveilah* with respect to *tumah*. This is a striking position, because by *behemot* — domesticated animals — the categories of *neveilah* and *treifah* are completely distinct. A *neveilah* is an animal that was not properly slaughtered, and it is *tamei*; a *treifah* received valid *shechitah* but was mortally wounded, and it is not *tamei*. One would naturally assume that the same distinction applies to birds: if you *shecht* a bird and it turns out to be a *treifah*, it should be classified as a *treifah* but not as a *neveilah*, and therefore should not cause *tumah*. This is indeed what everyone agrees is the rule for animals — there is no debate on that point. The debate concerns only birds, and Rabbi Yehuda's position is that birds are governed differently.

### Yehuda's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַאי טַעְמָא? קְרָא אַשְׁכַּח וְקָדָרֵשׁ: ״נְבֵלָה טְרֵפָה״. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: טְרֵפָה לָמָּה נֶאֶמְרָה? אִם טְרֵיפָה חַיָּה – הֲרֵי נְבֵילָה אֲמוּרָה! אִם טְרֵיפָה אֵינָהּ חַיָּה – הֲרֵי הִיא בִּכְלַל נְבֵילָה! אֶלָּא לְהָבִיא טְרֵיפָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ, שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And what is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that a bird that is a *tereifa* imparts impurity even when slaughtered? He too **found a verse and interpreted** it. The Torah states with regard to the ritual impurity of kosher bird carcasses: "And every soul that eats **a carcass,** or **a *tereifa***…he shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 17:15). **Rabbi Yehuda said: Why was** the case of **a *tereifa* stated? If** the verse is referring to **a live *tereifa*,** it should not be impure, as the term **"a carcass" is stated,** indicating that to impart impurity the bird must be dead. **If** it is referring to **a *tereifa*** that **is not alive,** but rather has died of its wounds, **it** falls **within the category of a carcass. Rather,** the word *tereifa* is written **to include a *tereifa* that one slaughtered** before it had the opportunity to die by itself, to teach **that it imparts ritual impurity** as would a carcass.

Where does Rabbi Yehuda derive this? From a *pasuk*. The verse states: *asher yochal neveilah u'treifah v'tamei ad ha'erev* — one who eats a *neveilah* or a *treifah* is *tamei* until evening. The Sages understand this verse as speaking about *tumah* that is contracted through eating, and they apply it specifically to birds, since the *neveilah* of a large animal causes *tumah* through contact, not through ingestion. Rabbi Yehuda reads the word *treifah* in this verse literally: the verse says that eating a *treifah* makes one *tamei*, and so it does. A bird is governed differently from other creatures, and this *pasuk* makes that explicit.

## Treifah Interpretation

### Literal vs. Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְחֵלֶב נְבֵלָה וְחֵלֶב טְרֵפָה״ –
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi said to** the Sage who suggested this source for Rabbi Yehuda's opinion: **If that is so,** one should interpret another verse likewise, **as it is written: "And the fat of a carcass, and the fat of a *tereifa*,** may be used for any other service" (Leviticus 7:24), meaning that although the meat of a carcass imparts ritual impurity, those fats that would be forbidden even if the animal had been slaughtered do not impart impurity.

The *Gemara* challenges Rabbi Yehuda's literal reading by pointing out that the word *treifah* does not always carry its plain meaning when it appears in the pairing *neveilah u'treifah*. Sometimes, the *Gemara* suggests, the word *treifah* in such a context is not referring literally to a *treifah* animal at all, but is serving an entirely different exegetical function. This is a striking move: the *Gemara* is, in effect, questioning whether one may simply read a verse literally.

### Cholev Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי נֵימָא: אִם טְרֵיפָה חַיָּה – הֲרֵי נְבֵילָה אֲמוּרָה, אִם טְרֵיפָה אֵינָהּ חַיָּה – הֲרֵי הִיא בִּכְלַל נְבֵילָה! אֶלָּא לְהָבִיא טְרֵיפָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ, שֶׁחֶלְבָּהּ טָהוֹר. מִכְּלָל דְּהִיא מְטַמְּאָה?
>
> **Translation:** **There too let us say,** interpreting the verse according to Rabbi Yehuda's logic: Why is the case of a *tereifa* stated? **If** it is referring to **a live *tereifa*,** the case is superfluous, as **"a carcass" is stated.** Since the forbidden fat of a carcass is pure, obviously that of a live animal is pure. **If** it is referring to **a *tereifa*** that **is not alive,** but has rather died of its wounds, **it is** included **within the category of "a carcass,"** and likewise it need not be mentioned. **Rather,** the word "*tereifa*" is written **to include a *tereifa* that one slaughtered,** to teach **that its** forbidden **fat** is **pure. By inference,** one should then conclude **that** its meat **does impart impurity.**

The proof that *treifah* can serve an alternative function comes from a different *pasuk*, in which the *Torah* states: *v'chelev neveilah v'chelev treifah ye'aseh l'chol melachah v'achol lo tocheilu* — the fat of a *neveilah* and the fat of a *treifah* may be used for any labor, but you may not eat it. The prohibition against eating the *chelev* of a *neveilah* and a *treifah* is of course true: one may not eat the *chelev* of either. And the phrase *ye'aseh l'chol melachah* — it may be used for any labor — is understood by the Sages to mean that such fat is *tahor*; it does not cause *tumah*, and may even be used for purposes related to *kodashim*. Now, the *Gemara* notes a problem with reading *treifah* literally here. If the verse means literally that the *chelev* of a *treifah* is *tahor*, the implication would seem to be that the body of the *treifah* — the meat — is *tamei*. After all, the verse is marking the fat as the *tahor* portion, which suggests the rest is not. But that implication is simply false: by a cow, a *treifah* is not *tamei* at all. The meat of a *treifah* animal is perfectly *tahor*. Therefore, the word *treifah* here cannot be read literally, because the implication of reading it literally is incorrect.

### Mi Sheyesh Bemino

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טְרֵיפָה מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ – לְמַעוֹטֵי טְמֵאָה; מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּמִינָהּ טְרֵיפָה, יָצְתָה זוֹ שֶׁאֵין בְּמִינָהּ טְרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the word ***tereifa*** in the verse concerning forbidden fat (Leviticus 7:24) **is necessary to exclude non-kosher** animals from the *halakha* in the verse and to teach that their forbidden fat is impure. The word indicates that only the forbidden fat of **those** carcasses **to whose species** the *halakha* of ***tereifa* applies,** i.e., those of kosher animals, impart impurity. Forbidden fat of a carcass of a non-kosher animal **is excluded, as** the *halakha* of ***tereifa* does not apply to its species.** The status of *tereifa* is immaterial for a non-kosher animal as its consumption is prohibited in any event.

If *treifah* in this *pasuk* is not literal, what is it doing? The *Gemara* resolves that the word *treifah* serves as an exclusion: it tells us that we are only discussing animals *mi sheyesh bemino treifah* — animals of a species for which the category of *treifah* is meaningful. This excludes non-kosher animals, for which the concept of *treifah* is irrelevant. The ruling that *chelev* is *tahor* therefore applies only to kosher animals; the fat of a non-kosher animal is *tamei*. This is why *treifah* appears in the verse — not to teach about *treifah* animals literally, but to delimit the class of animals under discussion to kosher ones.

## Cholev Tahor Issue

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי – לְמַעוֹטֵי עוֹף טָמֵא, שֶׁאֵין בְּמִינוֹ טְרֵיפָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Here too,** the word *tereifa* in the verse concerning the impurity of kosher bird carcasses (Leviticus 17:15) should be interpreted as **excluding** a carcass of **a non-kosher bird** from ritual impurity, **as** the *halakha* of ***tereifa* does not apply to its species.** Therefore, this verse cannot serve as a source for Rabbi Yehuda's opinion with regard to the impurity of a slaughtered bird that is a *tereifa*.

Abaye accepts that the word *treifah* in this verse is indeed literal, and he offers a fresh reading to resolve the difficulty. The concern raised — that reading *treifah* literally implies the meat is *tamei* — need not follow. The verse's purpose, Abaye says, is *treifah legufa itzreich* — *treifah* is there to teach something about *treifah* itself. Specifically: one might have thought that the *chelev* of a *treifah* is *tamei*, just like the *chelev* of a non-kosher animal. The reasoning would be that both a non-kosher (*temeiah*) animal and a *treifah* are forbidden while still alive — the *issur* against eating them applies even before they die. Since the *chelev* of a non-kosher animal is *tamei*, one might have analogized: just as a non-kosher animal begins its life already forbidden, and its *chelev* is *tamei*, so too a *treifah* begins in a state of prohibition, and perhaps its *chelev* should likewise be *tamei*. The verse therefore comes to tell us: do not make this comparison. A *treifah* is not like a non-kosher animal. The *chelev* of a *treifah* is *tahor*, and when the verse states this, it is not implying anything about the meat being *tamei*. The meat is also *tahor* — that is not in question. The verse's point is solely to forestall the erroneous equation of *treifah* with non-kosher animals.

### Treifah vs. Tameia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: טְרֵיפָה לְגוּפֵיהּ אִיצְטְרִיךְ; שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאמַר: הוֹאִיל וּטְמֵאָה אֲסוּרָה מֵחַיִּים, וּטְרֵיפָה אֲסוּרָה מֵחַיִּים – מָה טְמֵאָה חֶלְבָּהּ טָמֵא, אַף טְרֵיפָה חֶלְבָּהּ טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** The word **"*tereifa*"** in this verse **was necessary for its own** sake, to teach that the forbidden fat of a carcass of a *tereifa* of a kosher domesticated animal is pure. The inclusion of the word teaches **that you should not say** that **since a non-kosher** animal is **forbidden while** still **alive, and a *tereifa*** is **forbidden while** still **alive,** therefore **just as the** forbidden **fat of a non-kosher** animal is **impure, so too the** forbidden **fat of a *tereifa*** is **impure.** The word "*tereifa*" therefore teaches that it is pure.

The *Gemara* pushes back on Abaye's reading. If the concern is that one might compare a *treifah* to a non-kosher animal because both are forbidden while alive, then the very same reasoning could apply to the *pasuk* about birds — *asher yochal neveilah u'treifah v'tamei ad ha'erev* — which Rabbi Yehuda uses to establish that eating a *treifah* bird causes *tumah*. The *Gemara* suggests: perhaps the word *treifah* in the bird *pasuk* is there to prevent the opposite inference — namely, that just as a non-kosher bird does *not* cause *tumah* when swallowed (for the law that a bird causes *tumah* through swallowing applies only to kosher birds), one might have thought a *treifah* bird similarly does not cause *tumah*, since the *treifah* status, like non-kosher status, begins while the animal is alive. The word *treifah* in the bird verse would then be teaching that this inference is wrong — a *treifah* bird *does* cause *tumah* when swallowed, unlike a non-kosher bird. This, the *Gemara* observes, would bring us back precisely to Rabbi Yehuda's position, but through a different route. However, the *Gemara* then deflects this line of reasoning: *temeiah lo hayta lah sha'at hakosher* — a non-kosher animal never had a moment of being permitted. A *treifah*, by contrast, *hayta lah sha'at hakosher* — was once kosher. The analogy between a *treifah* and a non-kosher animal therefore does not hold, and there was never a strong basis for thinking a *treifah*'s *chelev* would be *tamei*. Consequently, Abaye's proposed purpose for the word *treifah* collapses: one does not need a verse to rebut a comparison that was never compelling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, מִי אִיכָּא לְמֵילַף טְרֵפָה מִטְּמֵאָה?! טְמֵאָה לֹא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, טְרֵיפָה הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר! וְכִי תֵּימָא: טְרֵיפָה מִבֶּטֶן מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? בְּמִינַהּ מִיהָא אִיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, is it** possible **to derive** the *halakha* concerning **a *tereifa* from** that concerning **a non-kosher** animal, as suggested? The two cases are incomparable, as **a non-kosher** animal **never had a kosher period** before being forbidden, whereas **a *tereifa* had a kosher period** before becoming a *tereifa*. **And if you would say: What can be said** with regard to an animal that is **a *tereifa* from the womb,** which never had a kosher period? **In any event, there are** kosher animals **among its species,** i.e., the *tereifa* is a member of a kosher species, which cannot be said of a non-kosher animal.

### Rava's Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: יָבֹא אִיסּוּר נְבֵילָה וְיָחוּל עַל אִיסּוּר חֵלֶב, יָבֹא אִיסּוּר טְרֵיפָה וְיָחוּל עַל אִיסּוּר חֵלֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The word *tereifa* in the verse concerning forbidden fat (Leviticus 7:24) teaches a different *halakha*. By stating: "But you shall in no way eat of it," referring to the forbidden fat of a carcass, **the Torah states: Let the prohibition of** eating **a carcass come and take effect** where **the prohibition of** eating forbidden **fat** already exists. One who eats the forbidden fat of a carcass is liable both for eating forbidden fat and for eating from a carcass. Likewise, the word "*tereifa*" in the verse teaches: **Let the prohibition of** eating **a *tereifa* come and take effect** where **the prohibition of** eating forbidden **fat** already exists, so that one who eats the forbidden fat of a *tereifa* is liable for transgressing two prohibitions.

With Abaye's reading set aside, Rava offers a different account of what the word *treifah* is doing in the *chelev* verse. Rava says: *haTorah amrah yavo issur neveilah v'yachul al issur chelev* — the *Torah* is telling us that the *issur* of *neveilah* takes effect and applies in addition to the pre-existing *issur chelev*, and similarly, *yavo issur treifah v'yachul al issur chelev* — the *issur* of *treifah* also takes effect and applies on top of the *issur chelev*. In other words, the verse is not making a claim about *tumah* at all. It is teaching that when an animal dies without proper *shechitah* and becomes a *neveilah*, or when an animal becomes a *treifah*, a new and additional *issur* takes hold even with respect to the *chelev*, which already carries its own prohibition. Both *issurim* apply simultaneously to that fat. The verse thus needs to mention *treifah* literally — it is teaching that the *issur treifah* is superimposed upon the *issur chelev*, just as the *issur neveilah* is.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן נְבֵילָה – מִשּׁוּם דִּמְטַמְּיָא, אֲבָל טְרֵיפָה אֵימָא לָא; וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן טְרֵיפָה – מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיסּוּרָהּ מֵחַיִּים, אֲבָל נְבֵילָה אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** both the word "carcass" and the word "*tereifa*" **are necessary,** even though they teach similar *halakhot*. **As, had** the verse **taught us** about additional liability only with regard to the forbidden fat of **a carcass,** one might have thought that it applies to only a carcass, **as it imparts ritual impurity, but** with regard to **a *tereifa*,** which does not, one might **say** that the additional liability does **not** apply. **And had** the verse **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **a *tereifa*,** one might have thought that it applies only to a *tereifa*, **as its prohibition** takes effect **while** it is still **alive, but** with regard to **a carcass,** which becomes forbidden only when it dies, one might **say** that it does **not** apply. Both words are therefore **necessary.**

The *Gemara* acknowledges that this is surprising, since the general principle is *ein issur chal al issur* — a new prohibition does not take effect where one already exists. The verse's explicit teaching that both apply is precisely what overrides this principle. And why must both be stated, rather than deriving one from the other? *V'tzricha* — both are necessary. Had the verse mentioned only *neveilah*, one might have said that *neveilah* is particularly weighty because it also brings *tumah*, and perhaps only *neveilah* — with its attendant *tumah* — is potent enough to be superimposed on an existing *issur*. But perhaps *treifah*, which does not bring *tumah*, would not be. Conversely, had the verse mentioned only *treifah*, one might have said that *treifah* is uniquely powerful because its *issur* begins while the animal is still alive — and perhaps only *treifah*, with this feature, can be superimposed. But *neveilah*, whose prohibition begins only at death, perhaps cannot. Therefore both must be stated explicitly. This is Rava's reading of why *treifah* appears literally in the *chelev* verse, without carrying any implication about the *tumah* status of the meat.

Having worked through the *chelev* discussion at length, the *Gemara* returns to the original debate. Rabbi Yehuda reads *treifah* literally in the bird verse and concludes that swallowing a *treifah* bird causes *tumah*. His interlocutors argue that *treifah* in such verses can serve non-literal functions — restricting the discussion to kosher animals, for instance — and therefore the literal reading is not certain. Now the *Gemara* turns to ask: according to Rabbi Meir, who holds that neither *shechitah* nor *melikah* of a *treifah* bird causes *tumah*, what does he do with the word *treifah* in the bird verse? *Rabbi Meir hai treifah mai avid lei?*

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, הַאי ״טְרֵפָה״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמַעוֹטֵי שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁהִיא לִפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara has established that according to Rabbi Yehuda, the word "*tereifa*" in the verse concerning the impurity of a kosher bird carcass (Leviticus 17:15) teaches that a slaughtered bird that is a *tereifa* imparts ritual impurity. The Gemara asks: **And what does Rabbi Meir,** who holds that a slaughtered bird that is a *tereifa* does not impart ritual impurity, **do with this** word **"*tereifa*"?** The Gemara answers: **It is necessary to exclude** the **slaughter** of non-sacred birds **that** occurs **inside** the Temple courtyard, teaching that it does not cause them to impart ritual impurity as would a carcass, even though they are forbidden for consumption.

Rabbi Meir uses the word *treifah* to teach that the *tumah* produced by swallowing a bird applies only when the act that kills the bird has the same status inside and outside the *Beis HaMikdash* — *mimiutei shechitah she'hi bifnim*. The rule is that a bird causes *tumah* when swallowed only when the relevant act — *melikah* — is equally applicable inside and outside. *Shechitah* of *chullin* performed inside the *Beis HaMikdash*, being an act that is not uniform inside and outside, does not cause *tumah*. This is what Rabbi Meir derives from the word *treifah* in the bird verse — not a literal *treifah*, but the principle that inside and outside must mirror each other for *tumah* to apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – ״טְרֵפָה״ אַחֲרִינָא כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **Another** instance of the word ***tereifa* is written** concerning the ritual impurity of birds: "A carcass, or a *tereifa*, he shall not eat to become impure with it" (Leviticus 22:8). Rabbi Yehuda derives the *halakha* from this verse.

Where, then, does Rabbi Yehuda learn this principle, if he reserves the word *treifah* for its literal meaning? From the fact that the word *treifah* appears twice in the relevant verses — once in *asher yochal neveilah u'treifah v'tamei ad ha'erev*, and once in *neveilah v'treifah lo yochal letam'ah vah*. One mention is literal — teaching that a *treifah* bird causes *tumah* — and the other teaches the inside-outside principle. Rabbi Meir, lacking the literal use, draws both non-literal teachings from the two appearances of *treifah*: one to exclude non-kosher birds (*mi sheyesh bemino treifah*), and one to establish the inside-outside equivalence. Rabbi Yehuda draws the exclusion of non-kosher birds from the word *neveilah* itself, since the concept of *neveilah* is only meaningful for kosher animals.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – חַד לְמַעוֹטֵי שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁהִיא לִפְנִים, וְחַד לְמַעוֹטֵי עוֹף טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how does **Rabbi Meir** interpret the appearance of the word *tereifa* in both verses? The Gemara answers: **One** is necessary **to exclude** the **slaughter that** occurs **inside** the Temple courtyard as mentioned above, **and one** is necessary **to exclude a non-kosher bird,** to teach that the carcass of a non-kosher bird does not impart ritual impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – מִ״נְּבֵלָה״ נָפְקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda** derive that the carcass of a non-kosher bird does not impart ritual impurity? The Gemara answers: He **derives it from** the word **"carcass"** in the verse: "A carcass, or a *tereifa*, he shall not eat to become impure with it" (Leviticus 22:8), which indicates that only carcasses of birds that are forbidden for consumption due to their status as a carcass are impure. Non-kosher birds are forbidden due to their non-kosher status, not due to their status as a carcass.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – הַאי ״נְבֵלָה״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵהּ? לְשִׁיעוּר אֲכִילָה בִּכְזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what does Rabbi Meir do with this** word **"carcass"?** The Gemara answers: Since the verse mentions eating, Rabbi Meir holds that the word "carcass" is written **to** teach that the minimum **measure of consumption** of the meat of a bird carcass that renders one impure is **an olive-bulk,** which is the standard legal measure of consumption for Torah laws in general.

What, then, does Rabbi Meir derive from the word *neveilah*? The *shiur achilah* — the minimum measure of eating that causes *tumah*, namely a *kezayis*. And why can this not be derived from the verse's use of the language of eating (*achilah*) alone? Because one verse tells us the minimum quantity (a *kezayis*, derived from *neveilah*), while the other teaches us that the eating must occur within the time it takes to eat half a loaf of bread (*kdei achilas pras*). One might have thought that since *tumah* through eating is itself a novel concept, the normal parameters of *halachic* eating — both the quantity and the timeframe — might not apply. The *Torah* must state both.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לִי מִקְּרָא קַמָּא, מִדְּאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא בִּלְשׁוֹן אֲכִילָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let him derive** this measure **from the first verse:** "And every soul that eats a carcass…shall be impure" (Leviticus 17:15), **from** the fact **that the Merciful One expresses** this *halakha* using **the language of consumption.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד לְשִׁיעוּר אֲכִילָה בִּכְזַיִת, וְחַד לְשִׁיעוּר אֲכִילָה בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Both verses are necessary, **one to** indicate that **the measure of consumption** that renders one impure is **an olive-bulk, and one to** indicate that **the** maximum **measure of** time for **consumption** of the olive-bulk is the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread. One who takes longer than this standard measure of time will not contract impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְחִידּוּשׁ הוּא – יוֹתֵר מִכְּדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס נָמֵי לִיטַמֵּא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** It is necessary for the Torah to indicate this latter *halakha* as well, as otherwise it might **enter your mind to say: Since** the impurity of carcasses of birds **is a novelty,** as one contracts it by eating rather than by touching or carrying, perhaps its *halakhot* are unusually stringent and even one who eats an olive-bulk in **more than** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread **should also contract impurity.** Therefore, the verse **teaches us** otherwise.

## Cholev Derashah

### Neveilah Shechitah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְחֵלֶב נְבֵלָה וְחֵלֶב טְרֵפָה״ – בְּחֵלֶב בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a *baraita* concerning the impurity of the forbidden fat of a carcass. **The Sages taught:** When the verse: **"And the fat of a carcass, and the fat of a *tereifa*,** may be used for any other service" (Leviticus 7:24) teaches that such fat is pure, **the verse speaks of the** forbidden **fat of a kosher animal.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a *braita* that takes up the *chelev* verse more formally and asks from the beginning: when the verse says that the *chelev* of a *neveilah* and a *treifah* is not *tamei* (i.e., is *tahor* in the sense that it can be used for any labor), is it speaking of the *chelev* of a kosher animal or of a non-kosher animal? *Becheilev beheimah tehorah hakatuv medaber* — the *braita* proposes that the verse speaks of a kosher animal. The reasoning draws on a parallel: we know that when *shechitah* renders an animal *tahor*, it does so only for kosher animals — *shechitah* of a non-kosher animal does not make it *tahor*. By analogy, when the verse tells us that *chelev* is *tahor*, we should similarly restrict this to kosher animals.

### Bird Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּחֵלֶב בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּחֵלֶב בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* challenges: **Do you say** that **the verse speaks of the** forbidden **fat of a kosher animal, or** perhaps does it **only** speak **of the** forbidden **fat of a non-kosher animal?**

But the *braita* then raises a challenge to this reasoning. One could equally argue the opposite. The verse also uses *neveilah* as its reference point, and we know that in the bird world, there exists a case where a non-kosher animal's *neveilah* is *tahor* — the *neveilah* of a non-kosher bird does not cause *tumah*, which is precisely the opposite of what one finds with non-kosher mammals. (This is the striking rule that *tumah* through swallowing applies only to kosher birds — a non-kosher bird, even dead, does not cause *tumah* through ingestion.) So if we look at the *neveilah* paradigm from the perspective of the bird world, non-kosher animals are *tahor*, while kosher animals are *tamei*. Perhaps, therefore, when the verse says *chelev* is *tahor*, it is speaking specifically of non-kosher animals — the category that, in the bird domain, corresponds to *tahara*. Both paths are logically available: the first leads to kosher animals, the second to non-kosher animals. On the basis of logic alone, there is no way to resolve the question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: טִיהֵר מִכְּלַל שְׁחוּטָה, וְטִיהֵר מִכְּלַל חֵלֶב; מָה כְּשֶׁטִּיהֵר מִכְּלַל שְׁחוּטָה – בִּטְהוֹרָה וְלֹא בִּטְמֵאָה, אַף כְּשֶׁטִּיהֵר מִכְּלַל חֵלֶב – בִּטְהוֹרָה וְלֹא בִּטְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **You** may **say** in response: The Torah **renders** a slaughtered animal **pure** of the impurity of a carcass **due to the fact** that it was ritually **slaughtered. And** likewise the Torah **renders** the forbidden fat of a carcass **pure, due to the fact** that it is forbidden **fat.** Therefore, the two cases are comparable: **Just as when** the Torah **renders** a slaughtered animal **pure due to the fact** that it was **slaughtered,** it is referring only **to a kosher** animal **and not to a non-kosher** animal, which is impure even when ritually slaughtered, **so too, when** the Torah **renders** forbidden fat **pure due to the fact** that it is forbidden **fat,** it is referring only **to a kosher** animal **and not to a non-kosher** animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: טִיהֵר מִכְּלַל נְבֵילָה, וְטִיהֵר מִכְּלַל חֵלֶב; מָה כְּשֶׁטִּיהֵר מִכְּלַל נְבֵילָה – בִּטְמֵאָה וְלֹא בִּטְהוֹרָה, אַף כְּשֶׁטִּיהֵר מִכְּלַל חֵלֶב – בִּטְמֵאָה וְלֹא בִּטְהוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* challenges: **Or** perhaps **go this way** and maintain that since the Torah **renders** the carcass of a non-kosher animal ritually **pure,** removing it **from the category of a carcass, and** likewise the Torah **renders** the forbidden fat of a carcass **pure, due to the fact** that it is forbidden **fat;** therefore, the two cases are comparable: **Just as when** the Torah **renders** the carcass of a non-kosher animal **pure,** removing it **from the category of a carcass,** it is referring only **to a non-kosher** animal **and not to a kosher** animal, the carcass of which imparts impurity, **so too, when** the Torah **renders** forbidden fat **pure due to the fact** that it is forbidden **fat,** it is referring only **to a non-kosher** animal **and not to a kosher** animal.

### Treifah Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרַתְּ: כְּשֶׁבָּא בְּדֶרֶךְ זוֹ – הִיא בִּטְהוֹרָה, וּכְשֶׁבָּא בְּדֶרֶךְ זוֹ – הִיא בִּטְמֵאָה; תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״טְרֵפָה״ – מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּמִינָהּ טְרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When one approaches** the matter **this** first **way,** the *halakha* that forbidden fat is pure **applies** specifically **to kosher** animals, but **when one approaches** the matter **that** second **way,** the *halakha* **applies** specifically **to non-kosher** animals. To decide the matter, **the verse states:** "The fat of **a *tereifa*** may be used for any other service" (Leviticus 7:24), indicating that only the forbidden fat of **those** animals **to whose species** the *halakha* of ***tereifa* applies,** i.e., kosher animals, is ritually pure. The status of *tereifa* is immaterial to a non-kosher animal as its consumption is prohibited in any event.

It is here that the word *treifah* in the verse performs its work. *Talmud lomar treifah* — the verse says *treifah*, which teaches: *mi sheyesh bemino treifah* — we are speaking only of animals for which the category of *treifah* is relevant. Non-kosher animals do not have a meaningful concept of *treifah*; the designation is inapplicable to them. Therefore the verse, by including *treifah*, directs us onto the kosher-animal path and excludes non-kosher animals from the ruling that *chelev* is *tahor*. This is, importantly, a use of *treifah* that is neither literal nor the simple exclusion of non-kosher birds from *tumah*-through-swallowing — it is a separate derashah establishing which animals' *chelev* is governed by the *tahor* ruling of the verse.

One might ask: does the word *treifah* then overreach and exclude even *chayot* — wild kosher animals like deer — since the notion of *treifah* is indeed relevant to them? The *braita* addresses this.

### Achol Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹצִיא אֶת הַטְּמֵאָה – שֶׁאֵין בְּמִינָהּ טְרֵיפָה, וְלֹא אוֹצִיא אֶת הַחַיָּה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּמִינָהּ טְרֵיפָה! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאָכֹל לֹא תֹאכְלוּהוּ״ – מִי שֶׁחֶלְבָּהּ אָסוּר וּבְשָׂרָהּ מוּתָּר, יָצָא חַיָּה שֶׁחֶלְבָּהּ וּבְשָׂרָהּ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: One might still assume that **I will exclude** from this *halakha* only the forbidden fat of a carcass of **a non-kosher** animal, **as** the *halakha* of ***tereifa* does not apply to its species, but I will not exclude** the fat of a carcass of **an undomesticated** kosher **animal, as** the *halakha* of ***tereifa* applies to its species.** Therefore, **the verse states** in conclusion: **"But you shall in no way eat of it,"** indicating that the reference is only to animals **whose fat** is **forbidden** for consumption if slaughtered **but whose meat** is **permitted,** i.e., kosher domesticated animals. **Excluded** are kosher **undomesticated animals, whose fat and meat** are both **permitted** for consumption.

The *braita* continues: the word *achol* — "eating" — in the verse's phrase *v'achol lo tocheilu* ("and eating, you shall not eat it") teaches an additional restriction. We are discussing only animals whose *chelev* is forbidden — *mi shechelbo assur besaro mutar* — an animal whose fat is prohibited even though its meat is permitted. This describes kosher domesticated animals (*behemot*), whose *chelev* carries a distinct prohibition. It excludes *chayot* — wild animals like deer — whose *chelev* and meat are both permitted. Since by a *chayah* the *chelev* is not conceptually distinct from the meat, the *chelev* is treated like meat: it is *tamei* when the animal is a *neveilah*. The verse therefore does not need to state that the *chelev* of a *neveilah chayah* is *tahor*, because it is not — it is *tamei*, just like the meat. The word *achol* thus excludes *chayot* from the class of animals whose *chelev* remains *tahor* in the event of *neveilah*.

## Non-Kosher Birds

### Rav Yaakov Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יַעֲקֹב בַּר אַבָּא לְרָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה – נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה הוּא דִּמְטַמְּאָה, נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה לָא מְטַמְּאָה?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כַּמָּה סָבֵי שַׁבֵּישְׁתּוּ בַּהּ! סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לְנִבְלַת עוֹף טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ya'akov bar Abba said to Rava: If that is so,** it appears from the *baraita* that **the carcass of a kosher animal imparts impurity,** but **the carcass of a non-kosher animal does not impart impurity.** Rava **said to him: How many elders have you caused to err in** the interpretation of this *baraita*? In **the latter clause we come to** discuss only **the carcass of a non-kosher bird,** which does not impart impurity. By contrast, carcasses of non-kosher animals are impure.

Having explained the *braita*, the *Gemara* turns to a puzzling element within it. At a certain point in the *braita*'s reasoning, there is an implicit reference to a case where the *neveilah* of a non-kosher animal is *tahor*. Rav Yaakov bar Abba challenged Rava directly: how can the *neveilah* of a non-kosher animal possibly be *tahor*? The *neveilah* of a pig is *tahor*? This seems absurd on its face.

### Rava Clarification

Rava's response is characteristically terse: *kama savei shavishtu* — how many elders have been confused by this passage? *Seifa atan l'nevilat of tamei* — at the end, when the *braita* refers to a non-kosher animal whose *neveilah* is *tahor*, it has shifted to the world of birds. In the world of birds, ironically, it is the non-kosher bird whose *neveilah* is *tahor*. The *tumah* caused by swallowing a bird applies only to kosher birds. A non-kosher bird, even dead, does not cause *tumah* through ingestion. The *braita*'s otherwise bizarre claim is simply a statement about this well-established, if counterintuitive, principle.

## Rabbi Meir Limits

### Temimim Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא טִיהֵר רַבִּי מֵאִיר אֶלָּא בִּתְמִימִין, אֲבָל בְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין לָא. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין. אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְטַהֵר הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּאֲוָוזִין וְתַרְנְגוֹלִין.
>
> **Translation:** § In the mishna, Rabbi Meir states that a bird offering whose nape was pinched and was found to be a *tereifa* does not impart impurity as would a carcass. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Meir deemed pure only unblemished** birds whose napes were pinched, as they are fit to be sacrificed, **but** he did **not** deem pure **blemished** birds, which are not fit to be sacrificed. **And Rabbi Elazar says:** Rabbi Meir deemed **even blemished** birds pure and did not distinguish between the two. **It was also stated** that **Rav Beivai says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: Rabbi Meir would deem pure blemished** birds, **and** this was his opinion **even** concerning **geese and chickens,** species that may not be brought as offerings and as such are never supposed to be pinched.

Returning now to Rabbi Meir's principle that *melikah* on a *treifah* bird does not render it a *neveilah*, the *Gemara* asks: how far does this principle extend? Rabbi Yochanan says: *lo shanu Rabbi Meir ela bi-tmimim* — Rabbi Meir's position applies only to *temimim*, birds without a physical blemish. Even if such a bird turns out to be a *treifah* and therefore unfit as a *korban*, it at least presented externally as a valid offering. For such a bird, Rabbi Meir holds that *melikah* does not make it a *neveilah*. But *ba'alei mumin* — blemished birds, such as those missing a limb — are excluded from Rabbi Meir's leniency. A blemished bird is in a different category: it was never in the class of valid *korban* objects, and therefore its *melikah* does produce *neveilah* status.

### Even Ba'alei Mumin

Rabbi Elazar disagrees: *afilu be-ba'alei mumin*. Once Rabbi Meir has established that even a *treifah* is not rendered a *neveilah* by *melikah*, the same logic extends to *ba'alei mumin*. If the *treifah* status — which disqualifies the bird as a *korban* — does not strip *melikah* of its capacity to avoid *neveilah* status, then a physical blemish should not do so either. The two positions are logically parallel, and once you accept the *treifah* case, accepting the *blemish* case follows naturally.

### Chickens Ducks

Rav Bibi, citing Rabbi Elazar, takes this even further: Rabbi Meir would *metaher* — rule as *tahor* — even birds with blemishes, and indeed even ducks and chickens. This is remarkable. Ducks and chickens are not species that could ever be brought as *korbanot*, so their sanctification as *korbanot* produces, at most, a *kedushah* that might be transferred to the *bedek ha-bayit* fund. The *kedushah* they can bear is not a genuine *kedushat korban*. And yet, Rabbi Meir apparently holds that once such a bird has been sanctified — in whatever form that sanctification takes — the act of *melikah* performed on it is not a *neveilah*. The principle Rabbi Meir is applying seems to be: any bird that has been dedicated as a *korban*, regardless of how far it falls from the ideal, is sufficiently within the *korban* framework that the prescribed act of *melikah* does not produce *neveilah* status.

## Eglah Arufah

### Rav Yirmiah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: עָרַף עֵז, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** According to Rabbi Elazar's understanding of Rabbi Meir's opinion, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to one who **broke the neck of a goat?** Does the impurity of animal carcasses apply? The breaking of the neck is considered the proper procedure in certain cases, as the Torah commands that a heifer's neck be broken if a murdered body is found between two cities and the identity of the murderer is unknown (see Deuteronomy 21:4).

This expansive reading of Rabbi Meir's principle prompts Rav Yirmiah to raise a further question. *Ba'ei Rav Yirmiah: araf ez mahu?* — if someone performed the ritual of *eglah arufah* using a goat rather than a calf (*eglah*), what is the *halacha*? The question presupposes that the standard *eglah arufah*, performed correctly with a calf and the neck-breaking (*arifah*) ritual, does *not* produce *neveilah* status — just as *melikah* on a *korban* bird does not. The question is whether this extends to a case where the wrong animal species was used.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲוָוזִין וְתַרְנְגוֹלִין טַעְמָא מַאי – דְּמִינָא דְּעוֹפוֹת נִינְהוּ, אֲבָל עֵז לָאו מִינָא דְּעֶגְלָה נִינְהוּ; אוֹ דִילְמָא מִינָא דִּבְהֵמָה הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** One could claim: **What is the reason** that **geese and chickens** are pure if their napes are pinched? It is **because they are species of birds** and the napes of some birds are pinched; **but a goat is not** of the same **species** as **a heifer,** as goats are considered small livestock whereas cattle are large livestock. **Or perhaps** one could claim: Since a goat **is** still **a species of domesticated animal,** it is similar enough to a heifer that the breaking of its neck prevents it from imparting impurity as a carcass.

Note the significant point the question embeds: in the bird case, the argument for leniency is that the *melikah* was performed on an animal that, for all its disqualifications, at least belongs to the bird family. Chickens and ducks are birds — even if they are not *korban*-eligible species. But a goat is not a cow. *Lav minei d'eglah ninhu* — a goat is not of the same species as a calf. On the other hand, *minei deveheimah hu* — a goat is still a *behemah*, a domesticated animal, just like a calf. Is that degree of similarity sufficient?

### Eglah Arufah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵב רַב דִּימִי וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִכְּלָל דְּעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה טְהוֹרָה הִיא?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כַּפָּרָה כְּתִיב בָּהּ כְּקָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi was sitting and saying this *halakha*,** i.e., this dilemma. **Abaye said to him: By inference** from this dilemma, it seems that you assume that **a heifer whose neck is broken is** itself **pure** and does not impart impurity as would a carcass. Rav Dimi **said to him: Indeed,** as the Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai say:** Since the language of **atonement is written with regard to** the heifer whose neck is broken, in the verse: "Forgive, Lord, Your people Israel" (Deuteronomy 21:8), just **as** atonement is written with regard to **sacrificial** animals, the breaking of the heifer's neck is equivalent to the pinching of the nape of a bird offering, and breaking its neck prevents the heifer from imparting ritual impurity.

Before reaching the *goat* question, the *Gemara* pauses to surface the hidden assumption embedded in Rav Yirmiah's question — namely, that the *eglah arufah*, when performed correctly, is indeed *tahor* and not a *neveilah*. This is far from obvious. *Shechitah* makes an animal *tahor*, but the *eglah arufah* is not *shechted* — its neck is broken (*arufah*), a violent killing that in any other context would produce a *neveilah*. Why should the *eglah arufah* be different?

Abaye raises this question directly: *miklal d'eglah arufah tehorah hee?* — is the assumption really that an *eglah arufah* is *tahor*, not a *neveilah*? The response is: *in* — yes. The proof comes from the school of Rabbi Yannai: *kaparah ketiv bah kekodashim* — the verse about *eglah arufah* uses the language of *kaparah* (atonement), the same language used for *korbanot*. Because the *eglah arufah* functions as a form of *kaparah*, it is treated as a pseudo-*korban*. Just as *melikah* performed in the context of *kodashim* does not produce *neveilah* status, so too the neck-breaking of an *eglah arufah*, performed in its ritual context, does not produce *neveilah* status.

## Eglah Arufah Cholev

### Kol Chelev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב נָתָן אֲבוּהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא (בַּר נָתָן): ״וְאָכֹל לֹא תֹאכְלוּהוּ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא חֵלֶב שֶׁאָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה; חֵלֶב שֶׁל שׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל וְעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה – מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Natan, father of Rav Huna bar Natan, raises an objection** based on a *baraita* concerning the purity of the forbidden fat of a kosher animal, mentioned in the verse: "And the fat of a carcass, and the fat of a *tereifa*, may be used for any other service; **but you shall in no way eat of it"** (Leviticus 7:24). **I have** derived from the verse **only** that forbidden **fat that** is **forbidden for consumption but from which** deriving **benefit** is **permitted** is pure. **From where** is it derived that this *halakha* also applies to the forbidden **fat of an ox that is stoned** by the court **or** the forbidden fat of **a heifer whose neck is broken,** from both of which one is prohibited from deriving benefit?

A *braita* is now cited that provides a *chiddush* about *eglah arufah* and *chelev*. The *braita* states: the verse says *kol chelev* — *all* fat is governed by the same rule. This teaches that even the fat of a *shor haniskal* (an ox sentenced to stoning) and of an *eglah arufah* — which in the relevant scenario are *neveilot* — is *tahor* in terms of *tumah*. The reason this needs to be stated explicitly is that both the *shor haniskal* and the *eglah arufah* (in the scenario under discussion) are not merely *neveilot* — they are additionally forbidden in *hana'ah* (benefit). The verse *ye'aseh l'chol melachah*, which establishes that *chelev neveilah* is *tahor* and may be used for labor, does not apply to them, since one cannot derive benefit from them at all. Therefore, one might have thought their *chelev* is *tamei*. The word *kol* in *kol chelev* comes to include them and establish that their *chelev* is *tahor* nonetheless.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל חֵלֶב״.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states:** "You shall eat **no fat** nor blood" (Leviticus 3:17). The generalization "no fat" indicates that the same *halakhot* apply to the forbidden fats of all domesticated kosher animals, including those from which one is prohibited from deriving benefit.

### Implication

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה טְהוֹרָה הִיא – הִיא טְהוֹרָה וְחֶלְבָּהּ טָמֵא?!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Natan concludes his objection: **And if it enters your mind** that the meat of **a heifer whose neck is broken is pure,** why must the verse teach that its forbidden fat is pure? Could one entertain the possibility that its meat **is pure but its** forbidden **fat** is **impure?**

But this creates a new difficulty. The *braita* is teaching that the *chelev* of an *eglah arufah* is *tahor* — implying that this must be stated explicitly, and thus implying that the meat of that *eglah arufah* is *tamei*. But if *eglah arufah* is treated as a pseudo-*korban* and its neck-breaking renders it *tahor*, as we just established, why would the meat be *tamei*? There would be nothing to teach, since both the meat and the *chelev* would obviously be *tahor*.

### Merikah Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָא דַּעֲרַף מִיעְרָף – לָא אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ; כִּי אִיצְטְרִיכָא, הֵיכָא דְּשַׁחְטַהּ מִישְׁחָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The derivation in the *baraita* **was not necessary for** a case **where one broke the neck** of the heifer. **It was necessary** for the verse to teach that the forbidden fat is pure even in a case **where one slaughtered** a heifer whose neck was designated to be broken.

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing cases. *Heicha de'arif me'arif lo itztricha leih* — when the neck is properly broken in the prescribed manner, no *pasuk* is needed: the entire animal is *tahor*, meat and *chelev* alike. The *braita*'s teaching about *chelev* being *tahor* must therefore be addressing a different scenario — one in which the *eglah arufah* animal died before the neck-breaking could be performed. In that case, since the ritual was not completed, the animal is a *neveilah* and the meat is *tamei*. One might then have thought that since this *neveilah* is not the ordinary kind — since its *neveilah* status is intertwined with its having been designated for a ritual in which benefit is forbidden — even the *chelev* might be *tamei*. The verse therefore tells us: *kol chelev* — even this *chelev* is *tahor*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתֵיהָנִי לֵיהּ שְׁחִיטָה לְטַהֲרָהּ מִידֵּי נְבֵלָה! לָא צְרִיכָא, שֶׁמֵּתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** the **slaughter** itself **should be effective in purifying** the forbidden fat, as slaughter prevents an animal **from** assuming the impure status of **a carcass,** and the derivation is still superfluous. The Gemara responds: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach that the forbidden fat of a heifer **that died** before its neck could be broken is pure even though the heifer itself assumes that status of a carcass.

### Yeridato L'Nachal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּמֵחַיִּים אֲסוּרָה? אִין. אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: גְּבוּל שָׁמַעְתִּי וְשָׁכַחְתִּי, וְנָסְבִין חַבְרַיָּיא לְמֵימַר: יְרִידָתָהּ לְנַחַל אֵיתָן הִיא אוֹסַרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If the *baraita* is referring only to a heifer that died before its neck could be broken, and it describes benefitting from it as being prohibited, **by inference,** is it **prohibited** to derive benefit from the heifer **while** it is still **alive?** The Gemara responds: **Indeed,** as **Rabbi Yannai says: I heard** the **boundary,** i.e., stage, beyond which it is forbidden, **but I have forgotten** what it is, **and** yet the members of **the group** of scholars **were inclined to say** that **its descent to a hard valley** (see Deuteronomy 21:4), where its neck was broken, is the action that **renders it forbidden.**

This solution requires the *Gemara* to explain when the *eglah arufah* acquires its forbidden-in-benefit status. If it acquires this status only when the neck is broken, then an animal that dies before the neck-breaking was never truly an *eglah arufah*, and there is no *chiddush* in saying its *chelev* is *tahor*. The *Gemara* therefore establishes that there is a moment prior to the *arifah* at which the animal becomes a forbidden *eglah arufah*. Rav Yannai says: *gavu shamati v'shachachti* — I once heard what that moment is, but I have forgotten. The *chavrayah* — the scholars of the *beis medrash* — reached a consensus: *yeridato l'nachal eitan hi osarta* — it is the bringing of the animal down to the *nachal eitan*, the flowing wadi, that confers upon it the status of *eglah arufah* and renders it forbidden in benefit. Once the *beis din* has brought it down to the designated location and the full ritual apparatus is in place, the animal becomes a forbidden *eglah arufah* even before the actual neck-breaking. Therefore, if the animal then dies on its own before the *arifah* can be performed, it is a *neveilah* that also carries *eglah arufah* status — and it is about this scenario that the verse teaches that the *chelev* is *tahor*.

This also illuminates the question of a goat used for *eglah arufah*: since the ritual context established by bringing the animal down to the wadi is what confers the *eglah arufah* status, one can argue that even a goat, once brought down in this fashion by the *beis din*, has acquired that status. But the parallel to the chicken-and-duck case of Rabbi Meir's *melikah* ruling is imperfect, because in that case, the sanctification of the bird within a defined *kodashim* framework provides a clear basis for the leniency, while the *nachal eitan* is not a fixed or defined place in the way the *Beis HaMikdash* is.

### Goat Unresolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ חַטַּאת הָעוֹף
>
> **Translation:**

Rav Yirmiah's original question — whether breaking the neck of a goat used for *eglah arufah* would be *tahor* according to Rabbi Meir — remains unresolved. The *Gemara* closes the chapter without answering it. The question hinges on how we understand the basis for Rabbi Meir's leniency: is it sufficient that the animal belongs to the same broad category (*behemah*) as the prescribed *eglah*, or does it require species-level similarity as in the bird cases? That question, like so many of Rav Yirmiah's characteristic probes at the edges of *halacha*, is left open as we conclude the chapter of *Chatas Ha'Of*.
