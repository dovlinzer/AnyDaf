# Yevamot 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Saris Adam Status

### R. Akiva: Saris Adam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְרִיס אָדָם — חוֹלֵץ וְחוֹלְצִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר. סְרִיס חַמָּה — לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא חוֹלְצִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **A eunuch** caused **by man,** i.e., one who became emasculated after birth, **performs *ḥalitza*** with his *yevama* **and** his brothers **perform *ḥalitza* with his wife, because he had an hour of fitness,** a time when he was fertile. On the other hand, **a eunuch by natural causes,** i.e., who was entirely lacking in sexual capacity from birth, **does not perform *ḥalitza*** with his *yevama* **and** his brothers **do not perform *ḥalitza* with his wife, because he did not have an hour of fitness,** as he never had the potential to father children.

The daf opens by returning to Rabbi Akiva's ruling regarding the *saris adam* — a man who was born fertile and subsequently became infertile through some human act or accident. Rabbi Akiva holds that such a person remains within the halakhic category of *yibum* and *chalitzah*, whether as the deceased husband or as the surviving brother, because his having been born fertile places him fundamentally in that category even if he can no longer produce children.

### Issur Lav & Mamzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי שָׁמְעִינַן לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר חַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין כְּחַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת דָּמוּ, וְחַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת לָאו בְּנֵי חֲלִיצָה וְיִיבּוּם נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Now, we learned** that **Rabbi Akiva said: Those liable for** violating **a prohibition are like those liable to** receive ***karet*** with regard to the validity of their marriage and all its ramifications, **and those liable to** receive ***karet* are not eligible** to perform ***ḥalitza* or levirate marriage.** A eunuch caused by man has the status of a man with crushed testicles, and is therefore prohibited by a standard negative mitzva from marrying a Jewish woman. If he violated the prohibition and married her, his marriage is invalid according to Rabbi Akiva, just as if he had married a woman who is forbidden to him by a prohibition punishable by *karet*. Why, then, should his *ḥalitza* be valid?

The Gemara raises a difficulty with this position. A *saris adam* who is a *ptzua daka u'krot shofcha* is subject to an *issur lav*. Rabbi Akiva famously treats an *issur lav* similarly to an *issur kares* with regard to the offspring being a *mamzer* — which would seemingly exclude such a person from the *parsha* of *yibum* entirely.

### Rav Yosef: School Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַאי תַּנָּא — הָךְ תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מֵחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין דִּשְׁאֵר הָוֵי מַמְזֵר, מֵחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין גְּרִידִי לָא הָוֵי מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef said: This *tanna*** of our mishna **is the *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Akiva, who said** that only the child born of a union between those who **are liable for** violating **prohibitions involving incestuous relationships is a *mamzer*,** but the child born of a union between people who **are liable for** violating **ordinary prohibitions is not a *mamzer*.** The prohibition applying to a man with crushed testicles falls into the latter category, and therefore his marriage is valid and he may perform *ḥalitza*.

Several answers are offered, and the last of them is introduced at the bottom of 79b: *ela amar Rav Yosef — hai tana, tana de-bei Rabbi Akiva hi*. The *tanna* of our *mishnah*, who holds that a *ptzua daka* is nonetheless included in the category of *yibum* and *chalitzah* according to Rabbi Akiva, belongs to Rabbi Akiva's own school.

### Issur Kinship Types

The key to understanding this answer lies in a distinction within Rabbi Akiva's own position. When Rabbi Akiva says an *issur lav* produces a *mamzer*, he means specifically an *issur lav* that falls under the category of *she'er* — a kinship-based prohibition, one rooted in a familial or quasi-familial relationship. The school of Rabbi Akiva holds: *mi-chayvei lavin de-she'er havei mamzer, mi-chayvei lavin geridei lo havei mamzer* — a plain *issur lav* unrelated to kinship does not produce a *mamzer*. Rashi on an earlier daf cites two examples of *issur lav* prohibitions that would qualify as *she'er*: a woman with whom one's father had relations (*lo yigaleh knaf aviv*), and a woman with whom one performed *chalitzah* (*keivan she-lo banah shuv lo yivneh*). By contrast, a *kohen* with a *gerushah* or our case of a *ptzua daka* is a plain *issur lav* with no kinship dimension — and therefore would not produce a *mamzer* and would not exclude the person from the *parsha* of *yibum*. This is a clean and straightforward resolution.

## Rava's Objection

### No Children Possible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיקְּרִי כָּאן ״לְהָקִים לְאָחִיו שֵׁם״, וְהָא לָאו בַּר הָכִי הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** Why does the mitzva of levirate marriage apply at all to a eunuch? **Read here** the verse with regard to levirate marriage: **"To establish a name for his brother** in Israel" (Deuteronomy 25:7), **and this one is no** longer **capable of this,** even if previously he had an hour of fitness when he was fertile earlier in his life.

With the *saris adam* question settled, the Gemara turns to a broader and more fundamental challenge. Whatever the *saris adam*'s formal status, practically speaking he cannot have children. The verse speaks of *le-hakim l'achiv shem* — establishing a name for his brother — and this person is not capable of doing so. The Gemara thus asks: *ikri kan le-hakim l'achiv shem v'ha lav bar hachi hu* — how can there be a *mitzvah* of *yibum* or *chalitzah* when producing children is simply not possible?

### Death Moment Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם כֵּן, אֵין לְךָ אִשָּׁה שֶׁכְּשֵׁרָה לְיַבֵּם, שֶׁלֹּא נַעֲשָׂה בַּעְלָהּ סְרִיס חַמָּה שָׁעָה אַחַת קוֹדֶם לְמִיתָתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: If** it is **so** that anyone who cannot presently father children is exempt from levirate marriage even if he had previously been capable of doing so, **there is no instance of a woman who is fit for the *yavam*,** as even if **her husband** died of natural causes it is impossible that he **did not become** like **a eunuch by natural causes an hour before his death.** He had certainly lost his fertility before dying, and therefore he has the status of a eunuch, which means that the mitzva of levirate marriage should not apply in his case at all. Rather, it must be that a man who had previously been capable of fathering children is considered fit for the purposes of levirate marriage.

Rava responds with a striking argument: *im ken, ein lecha isha she-keshera le-yabem* — if practical fertility at the moment is the determining criterion, then there will never be a woman who is subject to *yibum*. Why? *She-lo na'aseh ba'alah saris chamah sha'ah achat kodem le-mitato* — because a man who dies naturally, whether from old age or illness, is in all likelihood not fertile in the final moments before his death. If we required actual capacity to produce children, *yibum* would never apply to any normal death. Rava's proof thus establishes that we do not measure eligibility by the last moment of incapacity; we look at the person's status more broadly. A man who was once fertile and capable of having children remains categorially within the *parsha*, even if that capacity has since ceased.

### R. Eliezer vs Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, פֵּירוּקָא דְרָבָא פִּירְכָא הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer** in the mishna that a eunuch caused by man does not perform *ḥalitza* and his marriage is invalid even though he had an hour of fitness when he was fertile, **Rava's answer,** his proof from the fact that all men on their deathbeds lack sexual capacity, **is** undoubtedly **a refutation.**

The Gemara notes that for Rabbi Eliezer, Rava's argument is actually a question rather than a proof: *peruka de-Rava pircha hi*. Rabbi Eliezer does not rely on the principle of *sha'at ha-kosher* — the fact that earlier in life the person was capable. Rabbi Eliezer grounds his position in the theoretical possibility of recovery. A *saris chama* may potentially heal; it is that reversibility, not prior fertility, that places him in the category.

### Temporary vs Permanent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כְּחִישׁוּתָא דְּאַתְחִילָה בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Eliezer maintains that a man who is about to die cannot be compared to a eunuch, as **there,** he can no longer father children due to **the** general **weakness that begins** to take over his body as death approaches, which prevents him from engaging in relations with a woman, but his basic ability to father children remains unimpaired.

Given Rabbi Eliezer's framework, one might ask: how does he respond to Rava's scenario of a man dying — since by Rabbi Eliezer's logic, a dying man is not in a theoretical state of recovery either? The Gemara answers: *hatam kichishuta de'atchala bei* — in a dying man, it is not that the reproductive capacity is truly broken; it is a weakening that has overtaken him. The equipment, as it were, is not itself defective, even if the person cannot in practice exercise it. The moment the physical apparatus actually breaks down, however — as with a *saris adam* — that is a different matter entirely. 

What emerges is a highly formalistic ruling: as long as people fall within the category of those who were at some stage capable of producing children — or, in Rabbi Eliezer's view, theoretically capable of recovery — the *mitzvah* of *yibum* applies, even if children are practically impossible at the time, even if the marriage itself produced no children, and even if the surviving brother is currently incapable. Conceptual membership in the category is what drives the obligation.

## Saris Chama Defined

### Never Fertile Once

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי סְרִיס חַמָּה? אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל שֶׁלֹּא רָאָה שָׁעָה אַחַת בְּכַשְׁרוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a practical question: **What are the circumstances of a eunuch by natural causes? Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Anyone** **who never saw a single hour** of life **in** a state of **fitness,** as he was born infertile.

Having established the theoretical framework, the Gemara turns to defining *saris chama* in practical terms. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Rav Yosef in the name of Rabbi Yochanan defines it precisely: *kol shelo ra'ah sha'ah achat be-chashruto* — someone who never had even one moment of fertility. This definition is the hinge of Rabbi Akiva's position: such a person was never in the category, and therefore Rabbi Akiva excludes him from *yibum* and *chalitzah* entirely.

### Urination Arc Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא יָדְעִינַן? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כׇּל הַמֵּטִיל מַיִם וְאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה כִּיפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How do we know** that one was born this way and was never capable of having children? **Abaye said: Anyone who passes water and does not form an arch** with his urine, but rather his urine dribbles out downward, never had sexual capacity.

The Gemara then asks: how does one identify someone who has never had that moment of fertility? Abaye offers a diagnostic: *kol ha-matil mayim ve-ein oseh kipa* — anyone whose urination does not form an arc. If this condition was observable even in early childhood, it indicates a permanent, congenital state rather than something acquired later in life.

### Birth Defect Causes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי הָוֵאי? דְּאָפְיָה אִימֵּיהּ בְּטִיהֲרָא וְשָׁתְיָא שִׁיכְרָא מַרְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** Incidentally, the Gemara inquires: **From what** does this defect arise? What is its cause? The Gemara answers: It results from **his mother baking** bread **at noon and drinking strong beer [*shikhra marka*]** while pregnant. The excessive heating of the mother's body causes her child to be born with defective reproductive organs.

The Gemara then asks what causes this condition to develop in utero. Two maternal factors during pregnancy are identified: *de-afya imei be-tihara* — the mother baked bread in the noonday sun, so that both the heat of the oven and the heat of the midday sun acted upon her while pregnant — and *ve-shashtya shikra* — she drank strong or heavily mixed intoxicating drink. These are the causes of this particular birth defect.

### Smitten from Womb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַיְינוּ דְּשָׁמַעְנָא לְאִמִּי דְּאָמַר: ״כֹּל שֶׁמִּמְּעֵי אִמּוֹ לָקוּי״, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי נִיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: This is** the meaning of that **which I heard** Rabbi **Ami say: Anyone who is impaired from his mother's womb, and** at the time **I did not know what** he was referring to. Now I understand that he was speaking about a man who was infertile from birth.

Rav Yosef adds: this is what I had heard Rav Ami teaching when he said *kol she-mi-me'ei imo laki* — whoever was smitten from his mother's womb — and I did not know what he was referring to. Now I understand: he was identifying the *saris chama*, someone whose condition traces back to the womb itself.

### No Recovery Presumed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא הִבְרִיא בֵּינָתַיִם! כֵּיוָן דִּתְחִלָּתוֹ וְסוֹפוֹ לָקוּי — לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let us be concerned that perhaps he was cured** for some period **in the meantime,** without our having known about it, in which case he would have had an hour of fitness at some point. The Gemara answers: **Since** both **his beginning and his end are impaired,** i.e., he was born with a defect and he presently suffers from the same condition, **we are not concerned** about such a possibility.

The Gemara raises a further concern: even if we observed this urination pattern when the child was six years old, perhaps at some point between that early observation and the present he recovered — even briefly — which would, according to Rabbi Akiva, place him in the category. The Gemara resolves this: *keivan de-tichilaso ve-sofo laki, lo chashinan* — since we see the condition at the beginning and at the end, we do not concern ourselves with the possibility of an intermediate recovery. A consistent beginning and end permit a reasonable presumption of continuity.

### Bechor Limb Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב מָרִי, רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים בְּתוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים יוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari raised an objection** from the following mishna (*Bekhorot* 38b): **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says: One examines** a firstborn animal that developed a blemish in its eye **three times within eighty days** to see whether the defect is permanent. This shows that no presumptions are made in such a case; rather, there is concern that the animal may have been cured in the meantime, even if it had the defect at the beginning and at the end of the period.

Rav Mari challenges this reasoning from a *braita* concerning a *bechor* animal with a certain eye condition. Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos rules there that one must examine the animal three times within eighty days — beginning, middle, and end — before concluding that the condition has persisted continuously. Why is a two-endpoint check sufficient here but not there?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְחַד אֵבֶר חָיְשִׁינַן, לְכוּלֵּיהּ גּוּפָא לָא חָיְשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With respect to** a blemish affecting **a single organ,** e.g., an eye, **we are concerned** that the blemish might have passed and then later redeveloped, but **with regard to** a defect affecting **the entire body, we are not concerned** about such a possibility. A eunuch is not impaired in a single organ; rather, he has a defect that affects his entire body. Consequently, there is no concern that, though he was born with the defect and presently suffers from it, he might have regained his potency for some time in the middle.

The Gemara answers: *le-chad evar chashinan, le-chula gufa lo chashinan* — for a condition affecting a single limb, we worry about possible intermediate recovery; for a systemic condition affecting the whole body, we do not. The *saris chama* condition, even though it manifests in a particular way, is understood as something systemic rather than a localized physical defect, and therefore the two-endpoint check suffices.

## Saris Adam Distinction

### Age 20 Indicator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה וְלֹא הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — יָבִיאוּ רְאָיָה שֶׁהוּא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים, וְהוּא הַסָּרִיס — לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא מְיַיבֵּם. בַּת עֶשְׂרִים וְלֹא הֵבִיאָה שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — יָבִיאוּ רְאָיָה שֶׁהִיא בַּת עֶשְׂרִים, וְהִיא הָאַיְלוֹנִית — לֹא חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת, דִּבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל. וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: זֶה וְזֶה בְּנֵי שְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הַזָּכָר כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל, וּנְקֵבָה כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה מְמַהֶרֶת לָבֹא לִפְנֵי הָאִישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** If **a twenty-year-old** man **has not grown two** pubic **hairs,** a sign of sexual maturity, the relatives of the widow who wish to exempt her from *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage **must bring proof that he is twenty years old, and he,** having been established as **a sexually underdeveloped man, does not perform *ḥalitza* or levirate marriage** with his *yevama*. If **a twenty-year-old** woman **has not grown two** pubic **hairs,** the relatives of her deceased husband's brother **must bring proof that she is twenty years old, and she,** having been established as **a sexually underdeveloped woman, does not perform *ḥalitza* or enter into levirate marriage** with her *yavam*. This is **the statement of Beit Hillel. And Beit Shammai say:** With regard to both **this and that,** males and females, the relevant age is **eighteen years old,** not twenty. The mishna continues: **Rabbi Eliezer says** that for a **male** the *halakha* is **in accordance with the statement of Beit Hillel, and** for **a female** the *halakha* is **in accordance with the statement of Beit Shammai, because a woman reaches** maturity **more quickly than** does **a man,** and therefore, if she fails to develop the signs of maturity by the age of eighteen it is assumed that she is a sexually underdeveloped woman. In any case, it is clear from this mishna that even Rabbi Eliezer agrees that one who lacks sexual capacity from birth may neither perform *ḥalitza* nor enter into levirate marriage.

The Gemara now introduces a *braita* that addresses how one identifies a *saris* and when that status becomes operative. The case: *ben esrim shana ve-lo heivi shtei se'aros* — a man who has reached the age of twenty without having grown two pubic hairs. Such a person must bring proof that he is indeed twenty years old, and he is then classified as a *saris*. The underlying reasoning is that a *saris chama* — someone with this systemic condition — never reaches sexual maturity, never develops secondary sex characteristics, and therefore will not grow pubic hair. Ordinarily, halakhic adulthood requires reaching thirteen years of age and having two pubic hairs. When someone reaches twenty without pubic hair, it is not a case of delayed puberty but of an absence of natural maturation altogether, and the person is classified as a *saris* — an adult by virtue of age alone.

### No Chalitzah/Yibum

The *braita* then states the *nafka mina*: *lo choletz ve-lo meyabem* — such a person does not perform *chalitzah* and does not perform *yibum*. The parallel ruling applies to a woman: *bas esrim ve-lo hevia shtei se'aros* — a woman of twenty without two pubic hairs is classified as an *ailonit*, the female counterpart, *lo choletzet ve-lo misyabemes* — she neither performs nor receives *chalitzah* or *yibum*. This is *divrei Beis Hillel*, who set the threshold at twenty. Beis Shammai says eighteen for both. Rabbi Eliezer says: follow Beis Hillel for males (twenty) and Beis Shammai for females (eighteen), since women mature earlier and their status can therefore be determined at a younger age.

It is worth noting what the *saris* category actually represents here. This is not merely a person who is infertile — an *akara* (a barren woman) would still be subject to *yibum* and *chalitzah*. The *saris chama* and *ailonit* are people whose entire physical development has not followed the normal trajectory; it is a systemic condition, not simply the absence of offspring.

### R. Eliezer Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה וְלֹא הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — יָבִיאוּ רְאָיָה שֶׁהוּא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים, וְהוּא הַסָּרִיס — לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא מְיַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the following mishna (*Nidda* 47b): If **a twenty-year-old** man **has not grown two** pubic **hairs,** a sign of sexual maturity, the relatives of the widow who wish to exempt her from *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage **must bring proof that he is twenty years old, and he,** having been established as **a sexually underdeveloped man, does not perform *ḥalitza* or levirate marriage** with his *yevama*.

This creates an apparent contradiction. Rabbi Eliezer, in participating in the *braita*'s debate about whether the threshold is eighteen or twenty, seems to accept its *nafka mina* — namely, that a *saris* does not perform *chalitzah* or *yibum*. Yet in our *mishnah*, Rabbi Eliezer explicitly says that a *saris chama* does perform *chalitzah*, because people with this condition can be healed in Alexandria in Egypt.

### Retraction Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר דִּיקּוּלֵי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָזַר בּוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מֵהֵי הֲדַר בֵּיהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: סְרִיס חַמָּה — חוֹלֵץ וְחוֹלְצִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, שֶׁכֵּן בְּמִינָן מִתְרַפְּאִין בַּאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Dikulei said** that **Shmuel said: Rabbi Eliezer retracted his** opinion. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Which** statement **did he retract?** Did he retract what he said here in the mishna, that a eunuch by natural causes performs *ḥalitza* with his *yevama* and his brothers perform *ḥalitza* with his wife? Alternatively, perhaps the mishna here reflects his final view, after he retracted what he said in the other mishna. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this question, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: A eunuch by natural causes performs *ḥalitza*** with his *yevama* **and** his brothers **perform *ḥalitza* with his wife, as such type** of men **are cured in Alexandria of Egypt.** This additional source and its reasoning suggest that Rabbi Eliezer did not retract what he said in the mishna here. Rather, he retracted his statement with regard to the dispute between Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai in the other mishna.

Rami bar Dikuli in the name of Shmuel says: *chazar bo Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Eliezer retracted one of these positions. The question then becomes: which one did he abandon? A further *braita* is cited in which Rabbi Eliezer explicitly states that a *saris chama* performs *chalitzah* and *chalitzah* is performed with his wife, because *she-ken be-minan misrapin be-Alexandria shel Mitzrayim* — those with this condition are healed in Alexandria in Egypt. This *braita* thus aligns with the *mishnah*. Rashi explains that the concreteness of Rabbi Eliezer's knowledge — that healing is actually available in Alexandria — indicates that this was his considered and final position, and that it was the statement in the *braita* excluding the *saris* from *chalitzah* that he abandoned.

### Not About Chalitzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם לָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ. וְכִי תְּנַן הָהִיא — לָעוֹנָשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Actually, he did not retract** anything at all. **And when we learned** Rabbi Eliezer's ruling in **that** mishna with regard to the age of a sexually underdeveloped individual, that ruling was stated **with regard to punishments,** i.e., the age at which such an individual is considered an adult so that he is liable to receive punishment, and not with regard to *ḥalitza* or levirate marriage.

Rabbi Elazar (not to be confused with Rabbi Eliezer) offers a different resolution: *le-olam lo hadar bei* — Rabbi Eliezer never retracted at all. *Ve-chi tnan ha-hi le-onshin itmar* — when Rabbi Eliezer participated in the discussion about eighteen versus twenty, he was not endorsing the *nafka mina* of exclusion from *chalitzah* and *yibum*. That *braita* is concerned with a different question entirely: the *onshin* — the question of when a *saris* or *ailonit* becomes halakhically adult for purposes of liability and punishment. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees with the other positions about the *nafka mina* of *chalitzah* and *yibum*, but agrees that there is a distinct question of when the person is considered a *gadol* for other legal purposes, and it is only on that question that he offers the eighteen-versus-twenty ruling. In Rabbi Elazar's reading, Rabbi Eliezer consistently holds throughout that a *saris chama* does perform *chalitzah* and *yibum*; both the *mishnah* and the *braita* about Alexandria reflect his unretracted view.

## Adult Status at 20

### Forbidden Fat Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: אָכַל חֵלֶב, מִבֶּן שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה [וְיוֹם אֶחָד] עַד בֶּן שְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה, וְנוֹלְדוּ בּוֹ סִימָנֵי סָרִיס, וּלְאַחַר מִכָּאן הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת. רַב אָמַר: נַעֲשֶׂה סָרִיס לְמַפְרֵעַ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: קָטָן הָיָה בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה.
>
> **Translation:** And **it was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed on this issue: With regard to one who **ate** forbidden **fats** or performed any other transgression for which one is liable to receive lashes or *karet*, when he was **between the age of twelve years and one day and the age of eighteen years, and he developed the signs of one who was a eunuch** by natural causes, as explained below, **and afterward he grew two** pubic **hairs, Rav said:** He is **retroactively considered a eunuch** by natural causes. In other words, these hairs are not viewed as a sign of maturity. Rather, he lacked sexual capacity from the outset, which means he became an adult at the standard age of thirteen and is held liable for his actions from that point in time. **And Shmuel said:** No, **he was a minor at the time** he committed his offense, as the two hairs are a sign of his maturity, albeit delayed.

Having established that Rabbi Eliezer did not retract, the Gemara now explores the practical implications of the age-twenty determination for *onshin*. The case: a person ate *chelev* (forbidden fat) from the age of twelve years and one day — the age of halakhic maturity for a girl, had she had pubic hair — through the age of eighteen, without ever developing pubic hair. At eighteen (or twenty, depending on the authority), the *simanei saris* or *ailonit* manifest, and the person is categorized accordingly. Then, afterward — *le-achar mikan* — pubic hair does appear. The question is when, exactly, this person became a halakhic adult, and whether the sins committed between twelve and eighteen carry liability.

### Rav: Retroactive

*Rav amar na'aseh saris le-mafreia* — Rav holds that once we identify a person as a *saris* at the age of eighteen or twenty, that status applies retroactively to age twelve or thirteen. The person was, in hindsight, a *saris* from the point of *bar* or *bas mitzvah* — they are therefore liable for the *aveiros* committed during that period, since retroactively they were an adult at the time.

### Shmuel: Forward

Shmuel disagrees: *katan haya be-osa sha'a* — at the time of those transgressions the person was still a minor. Adult status is conferred only from the moment the *saris* classification is established — at eighteen or twenty.

### Later Hair Appears

The *le-achar mikan* clause — the appearance of pubic hair after the *saris* classification — adds a further complication. One might think that subsequent pubic hair growth is evidence of delayed rather than absent puberty, suggesting that adult status should be assigned even later. Yet Rav maintains his retroactive position even in this case: once classified as a *saris* at eighteen or twenty, the person is retroactively an adult from twelve or thirteen, regardless of later pubic hair. The *nafka mina* here extends beyond questions of sin and punishment; it applies equally to financial transactions undertaken during that period, which would be binding retroactively according to Rav but not according to Shmuel.

### Ailonit Knas Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: לְרַב, אַיְלוֹנִית לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר יְהֵא לָהּ קְנָס!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef strongly objects to this:** If so, **according to Rav, a sexually underdeveloped woman according to Rabbi Meir should be entitled to the fine** paid by a rapist. Rabbi Meir maintains that a rapist is liable to be fined only if he raped a young woman between the ages of twelve and twelve and a half, but not if he raped a minor. And furthermore, a sexually underdeveloped woman is not entitled to the fine because she is considered a minor, as she never showed the signs of maturity. But according to Rav she should retroactively be viewed as an adult and would therefore be entitled to the fine.

Rav Yosef challenges Rav from the laws of *knas* in *Kesubos*. Rabbi Meir holds that *knas* — the fifty-shekel payment for rape or seduction — applies only to a *na'arah*, not to a *ketanah*. An *ailonit*, having no pubic hair, remains a *ketanah* and therefore has no *na'arus* period. According to Rav's retroactive logic, however, since an *ailonit* is retroactively an adult from age twelve, she should have a *na'arus* period from twelve to twelve-and-a-half — and should therefore be entitled to *knas* during that window.

### No Na'arus Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִקַּטְנוּתָהּ יָצְתָה לְבֶגֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** A sexually underdeveloped woman **passes** directly **from minority to** full **adulthood.** In other words, she is first considered a minor and then immediately an adult, without passing through the intermediate stage of young womanhood, and an adult woman is not entitled to the rapist's fine.

Abaye resolves the difficulty: *mi-katnusa nafka le-voger* — she goes directly from *ketanah* to *bogeres*. The period of *na'arus* — the transitional stage of physical maturation between twelve and twelve-and-a-half — presupposes a naturally maturing body. *Na'arus* is constituted by the process of puberty itself; it is the stage during which the body is actively developing the secondary sex characteristics. An *ailonit* undergoes no such process. Even according to Rav, who says that retroactively she became an adult at twelve, she became a *bogeres* directly — there is no *na'arus* interlude. This is equally true according to Shmuel: whether the transition to adulthood occurred at twelve or at eighteen, in neither case does the *ailonit* pass through a stage of *na'arus*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֹּל כִּי הָנֵי מִילֵּי מְעַלְּיָיתָא יִתְאַמְּרוּ מִשְּׁמַאי. דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין הַסָּרִיס נִידּוֹן כְּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה נִידּוֹן אֶלָּא בַּחֲתִימַת זָקָן הַתַּחְתּוֹן. וְאֵין אַיְלוֹנִית נִידּוֹנֶית כְּנַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה — שֶׁמִּקַּטְנוּתָהּ יָצְתָה לְבֶגֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Greatly impressed with this answer, Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: Would that **all such excellent matters be stated in my name. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A sexually underdeveloped man is not judged as a stubborn and rebellious son, as** a boy is **judged as a stubborn and rebellious son only when** he has **the mark of** his **lower beard,** i.e., when his pubic hair begins to grow in. At that point he has reached the age of maturity but is not yet a fully developed man, a stage that parallels young womanhood for women. A sexually underdeveloped man never passes through this intermediate stage between minority and full adulthood. **And** similarly, **a sexually underdeveloped woman** who was betrothed and raped **is not judged** in accordance with the laws governing **a betrothed young woman** (see Deuteronomy 22:23–27), **as she passes** directly **from minority to** full **adulthood** without the intermediate stage of young womanhood. Therefore, the *baraita* fully corroborates Abaye's view.

A supporting *braita* confirms: *ein saris nidon ke-ven sorer u-moreh* — a *saris* cannot be a *ben sorer u-moreh*, because a *ben sorer u-moreh* requires the presence of lower pubic hair, which a *saris* will never have. *Ve-ein ailonit nidonis ke-na'arah me'orasah* — an *ailonit* is not in the category of *na'arah me'orasah*, with its associated capital punishment for adultery, *she-mi-katnusah yatz'ah le-darga* — she leapfrogs directly from *ketanah* to *gedolah*, bypassing the *na'arah* status entirely.

## Physical Simanim

### Waiting Until 20

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: סִימָנֵי סָרִיס, וְאַיְלוֹנִית וּבֶן שְׁמֹנֶה אֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהֶן מַעֲשֶׂה — עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said:** If one has **the signs of a sexually underdeveloped man; or** the signs of **a sexually underdeveloped woman; or** the signs of a child **born during the eighth** month of pregnancy, whose survival is uncertain; **no action is taken in their regard,** i.e., the sexually underdeveloped male or female is not treated as an adult and the child born during the eighth month is not deemed viable, **until they are twenty** years old.

Rabbi Abbahu states: *simanei saris ve-ailonit u-ven shemonah ein osim bahem ma'aseh ad she-yihyu ben esrim* — for someone who displays the indicators of *saris* or *ailonit*, and for someone born in the eighth month of pregnancy, no halakhic determination is made until they reach the age of twenty. Even if at nineteen a person presents all the indicators of *saris* or *ailonit*, the formal classification cannot be finalized until twenty. The eighth-month baby (*ben shemonah*) is grouped here because the Gemara assumes such a child cannot survive, and therefore the child has no presumption of viability (*chazakas chayah*) until the twentieth year.

### Eighth Month Baby

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן שְׁמֹנֶה מִי קָחָיֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: בֶּן שְׁמֹנֶה — הֲרֵי הוּא כְּאֶבֶן, וְאָסוּר לְטַלְטְלוֹ, אֲבָל אִמּוֹ שׁוֹחָה עָלָיו וּמְנִיקָתוֹ מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Can** a child **born during the eighth** month of pregnancy **survive? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: A child **born during the eighth** month **is like a stone** with regard to the *halakhot* of set-aside [*muktze*] on Shabbat, and therefore **it is prohibited to move him** on Shabbat, as it may be presumed that he is not viable at all. **However, his mother may bend over him and nurse him,** **due to the danger,** both to the baby, who might in fact be viable, as well as to the mother, who might suffer fatal complications if she has to retain all her milk.

This prompts an immediate question: *u-ben shemonah mi chayei?* — does an eighth-month baby actually survive? A *baraita* is cited: *ben shemonah harei hu ke-even* — an eighth-month baby is like a stone; it is *muktzah* on *Shabbos* and one may not handle it. The implication is stark: such a child is presumed to be non-viable, essentially already dead. How, then, is it possible to speak of waiting until this child reaches twenty?

### Held-Over Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּשֶׁגָּמְרוּ סִימָנָיו. דְּתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ בֶּן שְׁמֹנֶה? כֹּל שֶׁלֹּא כָּלוּ לוֹ חֳדָשָׁיו. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: סִימָנִין מוֹכִיחִין עָלָיו, שְׂעָרוֹ וְצִפׇּרְנָיו שֶׁלֹּא גָּמְרוּ. טַעְמָא דְּלֹא גָּמְרוּ. הָא גָּמְרוּ — אָמְרִינַן הַאי בַּר שִׁבְעָה הוּא, וְאִישְׁתַּהוֹיֵי הוּא דְּאִישְׁתַּהִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where his signs** of viability **are** fully **developed,** and he has the appearance of a viable child. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Who is** a baby **born during the eighth** month? It is **anyone whose months** of gestation **have not been completed,** i.e., a baby that was born prematurely. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The **signs** that **prove** that the child falls into this category are that **his hair and nails are not** fully **developed.** Now, **the reason is that they are not** fully **developed; but** if his hair and nails **are** fully **developed, we say** that **this** fetus was fit to be **born after seven** months **but** for some reason **was delayed** in its mother's womb.

The Gemara resolves this: *kesha-gamru simanav* — the case is one where the physical characteristics at birth are those of a complete child. Hair, fingernails, and other markers are developed. In such a case, the Gemara concludes, *amina hai bar sheva hu ve-ishtahui de-ishtahi* — this is really a seven-month baby that was held over in the womb beyond its term. The standard view is that a seventh-month baby is viable while an eighth-month baby is not. A baby born in what appears to be the eighth month, but whose physical markers are those of a full-term child, is presumed to be a seven-month baby born late. The *chiddush* of Rabbi Abbahu is that even such a child — whose physical development looks normal — requires waiting until twenty before the full *chazakas chayah* is established. Conversely, even a person with all the *simanei saris* must wait until twenty before that status is formally confirmed.

### Twelve Month Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דַּעֲבַד רָבָא תּוֹסְפָאָה עוֹבָדָא בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם וְאִישְׁתַּהִי עַד תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא, וְאַכְשְׁרֵיהּ. כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי, דְּאָמַר מִשְׁתַּהֵא!
>
> **Translation:** **But** if so, with regard to **the action taken by Rava Tosfa'a concerning a woman whose husband went overseas and** her baby was **delayed** in her womb **for the twelve months** of **the year** following her husband's departure, **and** Rava Tosfa'a **rendered** the child **fit,** arguing that the husband is presumed to be the father and the child is not a *mamzer*, **according to whose** opinion did he issue this ruling? It must have been **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who says** that a baby **can be delayed** for an extended period of time in its mother's womb even after it is fully developed and ready to be born. But how could Rava Tosfa'a have ruled in accordance with the minority opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, against the majority opinion of his colleagues?

This idea of in-utero delay gives rise to a striking practical ruling: *ha de-avad Rava Tosfaah* — Rava Tosfaah issued a ruling in a case of a woman whose husband had traveled overseas and who gave birth twelve months after his departure. Rava Tosfaah declared the child *kasher* — not a *mamzer*. The reasoning: following Rabbi's view that babies can be held over in the womb, a nine-month fetus that was retained for three additional months would still be the husband's child.

### 30 Day Survival

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּאָמַר מִשְׁתַּהֵי, כְּרַבִּים עֲבַד. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁשָּׁהָה שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם בָּאָדָם — אֵינוֹ נֵפֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since there is** also **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who says** that a baby **can be delayed** in its mother's womb, Rava Tosfa'a in fact **acted in accordance with the majority,** as Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion is not that of a lone dissenting scholar. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Any human** child **who stays** alive **for thirty days is not a stillborn.** Even if the child was not carried for a full nine months, once he has survived for thirty days he is no longer treated like an infant whose viability is in doubt. The reason is that he is presumed to be a child that was fit to be born after seven months but for some reason was delayed in his mother's womb and not born immediately upon reaching full development.

The Gemara adds that Rava Tosfaah's ruling was not merely a lone opinion: *keivan de-ika Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel de-amar mishtahai* — since Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel also holds that babies can be held over in the womb, the ruling reflects a broader consensus. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's relevant teaching: *kol she-shaha sheloshim yom ba-adam eino nefel* — any infant who survives thirty days is no longer classified as a *nefel*. A child born in the eighth month who survives a full month is no longer treated as a stillborn; the thirty-day survival is itself evidence that the child was a viable holdover. This supports the broader framework: even an apparent eighth-month birth may represent a held-over seven-month fetus, and survival confirms that presumption.

## Saris Signs Listed

### No Beard/Hair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ סְרִיס חַמָּה? כׇּל שֶׁהוּא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים, וְלֹא הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת. וַאֲפִילּוּ הֵבִיא לְאַחַר מִכָּאן הֲרֵי הוּא כְּסָרִיס לְכׇל דְּבָרָיו. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן סִימָנָיו: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ זָקָן, וּשְׂעָרוֹ לָקוּי, וּבְשָׂרוֹ מַחְלִיק.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: Who is** considered **a eunuch by natural causes?** It is **anyone who is twenty years old and has not** yet **grown two** pubic **hairs. And even if he grows** pubic hairs **afterward, he is** still considered **a eunuch** by natural causes with regard to **all his matters. And his signs are as follows: Whoever does not have a beard, and his hair is defective,** unlike that of ordinary individuals, **and his skin is smooth,** i.e., hairless.

The Gemara now presents the full enumeration of the physical signs of *saris chama*. A *baraisa* teaches: *eizehu saris chama? Kol she-ben esrim ve-lo heivi shtei se'aros* — anyone who reaches twenty without two pubic hairs. *Ve-afilu heivi le-achar mikan, harei hu ke-saris le-chol devarav* — even if pubic hair appears afterward, this does not lead to a reassessment of status. Once classified as a *saris* at twenty, that classification is final.

Additional signs follow: *kol she-ein lo zakan* — anyone without a beard; *ve-sa'aro lakui* — whose hair is affected (seemingly sparse, soft, or insufficiently developed); *u-vesaro machlik* — and whose skin is smooth. These are characteristics that reflect the absence of normal male physical development and are understood as more feminine-type traits.

### Smooth Skin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן יָאִיר: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין מֵימָיו מַעֲלִין רְתִיחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Ya'ir:** It is **anyone whose urine does not raise foam.**

Several further traditions are cited. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Yair: *kol she-ein meimav ma'alin retichu* — anyone whose urine does not produce foam (or, according to another reading, steam) upon urination.

### Urination & Heat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַמֵּטִיל מַיִם וְאֵין עוֹשֶׂה כִּיפָּה. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁשִּׁכְבַת זַרְעוֹ דּוֹחָה. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין מֵימֵי רַגְלָיו מַחְמִיצִין. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁרוֹחֵץ בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים וְאֵין בְּשָׂרוֹ מַעֲלֶה הֶבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say:** It is **anyone who urinates without forming an arch. And some say:** It is **anyone whose semen dissipates** and fails to congeal in the proper manner. **And some say: Anyone whose urine does not ferment. Others say:** It is **anyone who bathes in the rainy season and his flesh does not give off steam.**

Another view: *kol ha-matil mayim ve-ein oseh kipah* — one whose urine does not form an arc (as noted earlier by Abaye). Another: *kol she-shichvat zaro dacha* — one whose semen is watery rather than viscous. Another: *kol she-mei raglav machmitzim* — one whose urine does not become malodorous. And yet another: *kol she-rocheitz bi-ymot ha-geshamim ve-ein besaro ma'aleh hevel* — one who bathes in winter and whose body produces no steam. All of these indicators point in the same direction: a systemic absence of the bodily heat and physical characteristics associated with normal male development.

### Voice Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁקּוֹלוֹ לָקוּי, וְאֵין נִיכָּר בֵּין אִישׁ לְאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** It is **anyone whose voice is defective, so that it is not evident** from it **whether** he is **a man or a woman.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar adds: *kol she-kolo lakui, ve-ein nikar bein ish le-isha* — anyone whose voice is affected such that one cannot distinguish whether it is a man's or a woman's voice. This is the male counterpart of the corresponding sign for an *ailonit*, where Rabban Shimon ben Elazar notes that a deep voice indistinguishable from a man's is a sign of that condition. The parallel list of *simanei ailonit* includes: absence of breasts, difficulty during intercourse, absence of the Mons pubis (*shipulei me'ayim*), and a low voice indistinguishable from a man's.

## All vs One Sign

### Rav Huna: All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: סִימָנֵי סָרִיס, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ כּוּלָּם. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed over the **signs of a eunuch. Rav Huna said** that one is not categorized as a eunuch **unless all** these signs **are present; Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He is categorized as such **even** if only **one of them** is present.

With the full list of *simanim* before us, the Gemara takes up the question of how many are required. *Simanei saris* — *Rav Huna amar: ad she-yihyu kulam* — Rav Huna holds that a person is classified as a *saris* only if all the *simanim* are present.

### R. Yochanan: One

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *afilu be-echad mehen* — even one of these signs suffices to establish *saris* status.

### Hair Present: All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָא דְּהֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת בַּזָּקָן — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּעַד שֶׁיְּהוּ כּוּלָּן. כִּי פְּלִיגִי — בְּשֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: In a case **when he has grown two hairs in** his **beard, everyone agrees that** he is not considered sexually impotent **unless all** the signs **are present. When they disagree,** it is with regard to a case **when he has not grown** two hairs.

The Gemara clarifies the scope of the dispute. *Heicha de-heivi shtei se'aros ba-zakan* — where a person has two hairs of the beard (or, per the *Gra*'s reading, two pubic hairs), everyone agrees that all the *simanim* together would be required to overcome the evidence of that hair growth. *Ki pligi be-shelo heivi* — the dispute between Rav Huna and Rabbi Yochanan applies specifically where the person has neither pubic hair nor facial hair (depending on the reading). In that case: does the presence of even one of the listed signs establish *saris* status (Rabbi Yochanan), or must all signs be present (Rav Huna)?

### Rav Nachman Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ לְרַבָּנַן: עַיִּינוּ בֵּיהּ בְּרַב נַחְמָן, אִי בְּשָׂרוֹ מַעֲלֶה הֶבֶל אִיתֵּיב לֵיהּ בְּרַת. כְּמַאן, כְּרַב הוּנָא! לָא, רַב נַחְמָן סִיכֵּי דִיקְנָא הַוְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, with regard to **that which Rabba bar Avuh said to the Sages: Examine Rav Naḥman** when he bathes and **if his flesh gives off steam I will give him** my **daughter** for a wife, **in accordance with whose** opinion did he issue these instructions? Is it not **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna,** who maintains that all the signs must be present, as presumably he could see that Rav Naḥman did not have a beard? The Gemara answers: **No, Rav Naḥman had wisps** of **a beard,** and therefore Rabba bar Avuh wanted to know whether he displayed the other signs of sexual incapacity.

The Gemara cites a remarkable incident. Rava bar Rav Huna instructed the rabbis to observe Rav Nachman: *inu bei Rav Nachman* — if when bathing his body produced steam, *i besaro ma'aleh havel*, then *asiv lei brat* — you could go ahead and give your daughter in marriage to him, knowing that he was not a *saris*. The implication is that Rava bar Rav Huna was looking to rule out *saris* status on the basis of a single *siman* — consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's position.

### Hair Likely Present

The Gemara, however, offers a different reading of this incident. *Rav Nachman sichei dikna havu leh* — Rav Nachman in fact had some stubble of beard, or some small amount of pubic hair. In that case — where some hair is present — everyone agrees that all the *simanim* are required for *saris* classification. Since Rav Nachman had that hair, checking for even one counter-indicator was sufficient to rule out *saris* status: if he had steam from his body, there was at least one *siman* working against *saris* classification, and in a case where all *simanim* are required, failing even one test means he is not a *saris*. The incident therefore does not compel the conclusion that Rava bar Rav Huna held like Rabbi Yochanan; it is compatible with Rav Huna's position as well.

## No Chalitzah/Yibum

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַסָּרִיס לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא מְיַיבֵּם, וְכֵן אַיְלוֹנִית וְכוּ׳. קָתָנֵי סָרִיס דּוּמְיָא דְּאַיְלוֹנִית: מָה אַיְלוֹנִית בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם, אַף סָרִיס בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם. וּסְתָמָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: בִּידֵי אָדָם — אִין, בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna that **a sexually underdeveloped man does not perform *ḥalitza* or enter into levirate marriage** with his *yevama*, **and similarly, a sexually underdeveloped woman** does not perform *ḥalitza* or enter into levirate marriage with her *yavam*. The Gemara comments that the *tanna* **teaches** the case of **a sexually underdeveloped man similarly to** that of **a sexually underdeveloped woman,** from which it can be inferred: **Just as** in the case of **a sexually underdeveloped woman,** her disability is **by the hand of Heaven, so too,** in the case of **a sexually underdeveloped man,** his disability must be **by the hand of Heaven. And** this **unattributed** view in the mishna is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who said:** With regard to one whose incapacity was brought about **by the hands of man, yes,** he is considered like any other man and performs *ḥalitza*, whereas one who suffers his condition **by the hand of Heaven** does **not** do so.

The Gemara returns to the concluding ruling of the *mishnah*: *ha-saris she-chalatz le-yivamto* — the *saris* who performs *chalitzah* and *yibum*, and similarly the *ailonit*.

The Gemara observes: *katani saris dumya de-ailonit* — since the *mishnah* groups *saris* and *ailonit* together, they must be parallel categories. An *ailonit* is unambiguously a natural condition — *bidei Shamayim*. Therefore the *saris* referred to in the *mishnah* is also *bidei Shamayim* — a *saris chama*, not a *saris adam* who acquired his condition through human action or injury.

*Ve-stama ke-Rabbi Akiva* — the *stam mishnah* ruling here reflects Rabbi Akiva's position. Rabbi Akiva holds: *bidei adam* — a *saris adam* — is included in the category of *chalitzah* and *yibum*; *bidei Shamayim* — a *saris chama* — is not.

### Yibum Invalidates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַסָּרִיס שֶׁחָלַץ לִיבִמְתּוֹ לֹא פְּסָלָהּ כּוּ׳. טַעְמָא דִּבְעָלָהּ הוּא, הָא אַחֵר — לָא,
>
> **Translation:** § It is further taught in the mishna that if **a eunuch performed *ḥalitza* with his *yevama*, he has not** thereby **disqualified her** from marrying into the priesthood, but if he had intercourse with her, he has disqualified her. The Gemara infers from this wording that **the reason** for her disqualification **is that he,** the *yavam*, **had intercourse with her,** as she had intercourse with her *yavam* outside the framework of permitted levirate marriage. **But** if **a different** individual had relations with her she would **not** be disqualified.

If the *saris* nonetheless performs *yibum* with his *yevamah*, he invalidates her — *pesulah le-kehunah*. The reason: he has engaged in a forbidden sexual union with his sister-in-law outside the context of *mitzvah*, an *issur arayot*, and this makes her prohibited for marriage to a *kohen*. If, on the other hand, he performed *chalitzah*, she is not invalidated, since a non-meaningful *chalitzah* carries no such consequence.

One might have thought that any man's sexual relations with the *yevamah* — not just the *saris* brother-in-law — would invalidate her, given that she is a *shomeret yavam* and her *zikah* bond constitutes a quasi-marital relationship. The Gemara indeed confirms this: *hu ha-din afilu le-acher nami* — even if another man had relations with her while she was a *shomeret yavam*, she would likewise be invalidated for the remaining brothers. The reason the *mishnah* frames the ruling in terms of the *saris* himself is literary: since the opening of the clause discusses what happens when *he* performs *chalitzah*, the *mishnah* naturally continues by discussing what happens when *he* performs *yibum*.

### Ailonit Rule

The same rules apply to an *ailonit*: if the brothers perform *chalitzah* with her, she is not invalidated; if they perform *yibum* — sexual relations with her outside of *mitzvah* — she is invalidated, because it constitutes relations with an *eishet ach* in the absence of the *mitzvah* of *yibum*.

### Ailonit Permitted

The Gemara notes the implicit assumption: *ta'ama de-ba'aluha* — it is specifically the sexual act that creates the invalidation. If no such act occurred, she would not be invalidated merely by virtue of being an *ailonit*. This ruling stands in contrast to Rabbi Yehuda's position, which holds that an *ailonit* is inherently a *zonah* — since she can never conceive, every sexual act she engages in is considered fornication outside the context of procreation. The *mishnah* here does not follow Rabbi Yehuda. According to the *mishnah*, an *ailonit* is not a *zonah* by nature, remains permitted to a *kohen*, and is only invalidated by the specific prohibited sexual act of *yibum* performed without the force of *mitzvah*.
