# Eruvin 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Akiva & R. Ishmael

### R. Akiva's Insistence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: לָא אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל דָּבָר זֶה, אֶלָּא אוֹתוֹ תַּלְמִיד אָמַר דָּבָר זֶה, וַהֲלָכָה כְּאוֹתוֹ תַּלְמִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva said: Rabbi Yishmael did not state this matter,** as it is unlikely that Rabbi Yishmael would err in this manner; **rather,** it was **that disciple who stated that matter** on his own, **and the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **that disciple.**

We begin on 13a with a review of the preceding passage. A student had reported in the name of Rabbi Ishmael that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not debate the case of less than four *amot*, and Rabbi Akiva flatly insists that Rabbi Ishmael never said such a thing. Yet Rabbi Akiva then declares that we rule like that student's position. The Gemara is puzzled: how could he rule like a student whose claim he just rejected? The answer is that Rabbi Akiva was not issuing a genuine ruling; he was provoking his students in order to sharpen them and see how they would respond.

### Relevance to Chatzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא אֲמָרָהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֶלָּא לְחַדֵּד בָּהּ הַתַּלְמִידִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Rabbi Akiva said** that the *halakha* is in accordance with that disciple **only to sharpen** the minds of **his students with** his statement.

This raises an obvious question: what practical difference does it make whether Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel debated the case of less than four *amot* by a *mavoi*? After all, we rule like Beit Hillel in any case, who permits a *lechi* or *korah* — so whether Beit Shammai agreed below four *amot* seems entirely moot. The Gemara addresses this by pointing out that such a ruling could carry implications beyond the *mavoi*. If we were to establish that less than four *amot* makes a meaningful halachic difference, that principle might extend to a *chatzer* as well. As discussed previously, a *chatzer* requires a *pas* on either side or a *pas* of four *tefachim*; it is possible that for a *chatzer* of less than four *amot*, a lesser requirement would apply. So there could be genuine halachic ramifications. Nevertheless, the Gemara concludes that Rabbi Akiva insists Rabbi Ishmael never said any such thing, and that his declaration that we rule like the student was made only to challenge and sharpen the other students.

## R. Yehoshua b. Levi

### The Anonymous Student

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא ״מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא״ — אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ אֶת רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְאֶת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: Anywhere that you find** a statement introduced with: **A certain disciple said before Rabbi Akiva in the name of Rabbi Yishmael, it is none other than Rabbi Meir,** who was the student **who served** both **Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva.**

The Gemara continues, approximately eight lines from the top of 13a, with the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *kol makom she-ata motzei mishum Rabbi Yishmael amar talmid echad lifnei Rabbi Akiva* — any place you find the formulation that a certain student said in the name of Rabbi Ishmael in the presence of Rabbi Akiva, *eino ela Rabbi Meir* — that student can only be Rabbi Meir. How often this precise formulation appears in the Talmud is uncertain, but Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's principle is clear: the student who transmitted Rabbi Ishmael's teachings to Rabbi Akiva was necessarily Rabbi Meir.

### R. Meir's Studies

The reason is that Rabbi Meir *shimesh*, studied and attended, both Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Akiva. He was apparently the singular prominent student who served as a crossover between the two great academies. The Gemara proceeds to a *braisa* that will tell us more about this remarkable biographical fact.

## R. Meir the Scribe

### Kankantom Additive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כְּשֶׁהָיִיתִי אֵצֶל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָיִיתִי מֵטִיל קַנְקַנְתּוֹם לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ, וְלֹא אָמַר לִי דָּבָר. כְּשֶׁבָּאתִי אֵצֶל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲסָרָהּ עָלַי.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir said: When I was** a student **with Rabbi Yishmael, I used to put iron sulfate [*kankantom*] into the ink** with which I wrote Torah scrolls, **and he did not say anything to me. When I came** to study **with Rabbi Akiva, he prohibited me** from doing **so.**

The *braisa* opens with Rabbi Meir's own testimony: *keshe-hayiti etzel Rabbi Yishmael* — when I was learning by Rabbi Ishmael, *hayiti matil kankantom letoch hadeyo* — I would add *kankantom* to my ink. According to Rashi, and as identified by the Steinsaltz commentary, *kankantom* is copper sulfate, and as we will see from the story, its effect is to make ink more permanent and far more resistant to scraping and erasure. Rabbi Meir was a *sofer* — a professional scribe — and he was adding this substance to his ink. Rabbi Ishmael saw him do so and said nothing. *Keshe-bati etzel Rabbi Akiva, asarah alai* — when he came to Rabbi Akiva, Rabbi Akiva forbade it to him.

The immediate relevance for the Gemara is simply to confirm that Rabbi Meir studied under both Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Akiva.

### R. Ishmael's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כְּשֶׁהָיִיתִי לוֹמֵד אֵצֶל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הָיִיתִי מֵטִיל קַנְקַנְתּוֹם לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ, וְלֹא אָמַר לִי דָּבָר. וּכְשֶׁבָּאתִי אֵצֶל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אָמַר לִי: בְּנִי, מָה מְלַאכְתֶּךָ? אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: לַבְלָר אֲנִי. אָמַר לִי: בְּנִי, הֱוֵי זָהִיר בִּמְלַאכְתֶּךָ, שֶׁמְּלַאכְתְּךָ מְלֶאכֶת שָׁמַיִם הִיא, שֶׁמָּא אַתָּה מְחַסֵּר אוֹת אַחַת אוֹ מְיַיתֵּר אוֹת אַחַת — נִמְצֵאתָ מַחֲרִיב אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this statement: **Is that so? Didn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Shmuel said in the name of Rabbi Meir: When I studied with Rabbi Akiva** as his disciple, **I used to put iron sulfate into the ink, and he did not say anything to me. But when I came** to study **with Rabbi Yishmael, he said to me: My son, what is your vocation? I replied: I am a scribe [*lavlar*]** who writes Torah scrolls. **He said to me: My son, be careful in your vocation, as your vocation is heavenly service,** and care must be taken **lest you omit a single letter or add a single letter** out of place, **and you will end up destroying the whole world in its entirety.** Addition or omission of a single letter can change the meaning from truth [*emet*] to death [*met*].

A second version of the story, transmitted by Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Shmuel in the name of Rabbi Meir himself, presents the account differently. There, Rabbi Meir begins by studying under Rabbi Akiva, who sees him add *kankantom* to the ink and says nothing. When Rabbi Meir then comes to Rabbi Ishmael, Rabbi Ishmael asks: *beni ma melachtecha* — my son, what is your profession? Rabbi Meir answers: *lavlari ani* — I am a scribe. Rabbi Ishmael responds with gravity: *beni hevei zahir bimlachtecha* — my son, be exceedingly careful in your work, *she-melachtecha melachet shamayim hi* — for your work is heavenly work, writing *Sifrei Torah*. The stakes could not be higher: *shema ata mechasser ot achat o meyater ot achat nimtset machriv et kol ha'olam kulo* — if you should lack even one letter or add even one letter, you stand to destroy the entire world.

Both Rashi and Tosafot address which specific letter could be so catastrophic. Rashi suggests that in the verse *ve-Hashem Elokim emes*, if one omitted the *alef* from *emes*, the word would become *meis* — dead — which would be devastating. Or, if *vayidaber Hashem* were written as *vayidabru* with an added *vav*, the result would be deeply heretical. Tosafot raises the example of *Bereishis bara Elokim* becoming *Bereishis bera'u*, potentially implying that God was created. The Maharsha, citing the *Ramban*, adds the kabbalistic dimension: the letters of the Torah collectively form an elaborated divine name, and each letter carries metaphysical weight in the structure of Torah's mystical reality, so that altering even a single letter transforms that whole reality.

It is worth noting that in the era of the *Chazal*, there was greater variance in Torah script than we have today — not in major matters, but in smaller questions such as whether a word was written *malei* or *chaser*. It was the work of the *masores*, the scholars who counted and categorized every single letter, that produced the high degree of standardization we have in our *Sifrei Torah* today. This context only sharpens the force of Rabbi Ishmael's admonition: at a time of less standardization, the temptation toward carelessness was correspondingly greater, and the call for extreme precision all the more urgent.

The Maharitz Chayes, a nineteenth-century scholar, adds a further insight. We know that Rabbi Meir was not only a scribe but was famous for his *drashos* on *psukim* through creative engagement with the letters — the well-known example being his reading of *kasnos or* (garments of leather) with an *alef* as *kasnos or* (garments of light), cited by Rashi. The Maharitz Chayes suggests that Rabbi Meir may have had a practice of noting in the margin of his *Sefer Torah* some symbol or mark corresponding to one of his *drashos* on that *pasuk*. This would explain why Rabbi Ishmael was particularly anxious. Knowing Rabbi Meir's habit of playing creatively with letters, Rabbi Ishmael feared that in a moment of inspiration — say, the idea of writing *kasnos or* with an *alef* — he might inadvertently carry it over into the actual script itself. Hence the urgent warning: be very careful, for your brilliant textual creativity must never bleed into what you actually write in the *Sefer Torah*.

### R. Meir's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: דָּבָר אֶחָד יֵשׁ לִי וְ׳קַנְקַנְתּוֹם׳ שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁאֲנִי מֵטִיל לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **I said to him: I have one substance called iron sulfate, which I place into the ink.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא בַּחֲסֵירוֹת וּבִיתֵירוֹת [דְּלָא טָעֵינָא] — דְּבָקִי אֲנָא, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ מֵיחַשׁ לִזְבוּב נָמֵי, דִּילְמָא אָתֵי וְיָתֵיב אַתָּגֵיהּ דְּדָלֶת וּמָחֵיק לֵיהּ וּמְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ רֵישׁ — דָּבָר אֶחָד יֵשׁ לִי וְקַנְקַנְתּוֹם שְׁמוֹ שֶׁאֲנִי מֵטִיל לְתוֹךְ הַדְּיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **this is** what Rabbi Meir **is saying to** Rabbi Yishmael: **There is no need** to mention **defective and plene** words, **as I am an expert; however, even with regard to the concern that a fly might come and land on the crown of** the letter ***dalet* and blot it out and render it a *reish*,** thereby changing the meaning of the word, I am not concerned, as **I have a substance called iron sulfate that I place into the ink** so that it will not be erased.

After Rabbi Ishmael's warning, Rabbi Meir responds: *davar echad yesh li ve-kankantom shemo* — I have this special additive called *kankantom* that I put into the ink. The Gemara then pauses to ask: *mai ka'amar lei u'mai ka'mehadar lei* — what was Rabbi Meir saying, and how was it responsive? Rabbi Ishmael had warned him not to add or drop letters, and Rabbi Meir answered by telling him about an ink additive. What is the connection?

The Gemara explains: *hachi ka'amar lei* — this is what Rabbi Meir was communicating. Not only, he was saying, do I not make mistakes of writing extra letters or fewer letters — *debaki ana*, I am a complete expert in the script and I do not err in that way. But I want you to know how scrupulous I truly am. I am not merely careful about deliberate errors; I am concerned even that *afilu michshla dezevuv nami delma ati veyasiv atage dedalet umachei lei umashvei lei reish* — a fly might land on the script while the ink is still drying, settle on the small protrusion of a *dalet* — the extension to the right of the bar beneath the letter — and obliterate it, transforming the *dalet* into a *reish*. To guard against even that kind of accidental erasure, I add this *kankantom* to my ink to make it impervious to such mishaps. So Rabbi Meir's response was entirely on point: his use of *kankantom* was a further expression of his extraordinary vigilance against any kind of letter alteration, deliberate or accidental.

### Contradictory Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא שִׁימּוּשׁ אַשִּׁימּוּשׁ, קַשְׁיָא אֲסָרָהּ אַאֲסָרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Nevertheless, **there is a difficulty between service and service,** as one source states that Rabbi Meir initially served Rabbi Akiva, whereas the other source states that he served Rabbi Yishmael first. **There is a difficulty between** the words **he prohibited it** in the *baraita*, which is referring to Rabbi Akiva, **and he prohibited it** in the statement of Rav Yehuda, which is referring to Rabbi Yishmael.

This brings us to a genuine textual problem. In the first version, Rabbi Meir says he was by Rabbi Ishmael who said nothing, and then went to Rabbi Akiva who forbade it. In the second version, he was by Rabbi Akiva who said nothing, and then went to Rabbi Ishmael who objected. The Gemara states: *kashya shimush a-shimush, kashya asiya a-asiya* — there is a contradiction regarding the order of study, and there is a contradiction regarding who it was that issued the prohibition.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא שִׁימּוּשׁ אַשִּׁימּוּשׁ לָא קַשְׁיָא: מֵעִיקָּרָא אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וּמִדְּלָא מָצֵי לְמֵיקַם אַלִּיבֵּיהּ — אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וּגְמַר גְּמָרָא, וַהֲדַר אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וּסְבַר סְבָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, there is no difficulty between** the accounts in the two sources with regard to **service and service,** as it can be suggested as follows: Rabbi Meir **initially came** to study **before Rabbi Akiva, and since he was unable to comprehend** the teachings **in accordance with his** opinion, **he came before Rabbi Yishmael and studied the tradition, and again came before Rabbi Akiva and studied logical** analysis.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֲסָרָהּ אַאֲסָרָהּ קַשְׁיָא! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Having reconciled the first difficulty, the Gemara continues: **However, the difficulty with regard to** whether Rabbi Akiva **prohibited** iron sulfate or Rabbi Yishmael **prohibited** it remains **difficult.** The Gemara notes: It indeed remains **difficult;** no answer was found.

*Bishlama shimush a-shimush lo kashya* — the contradiction about the order of study is resolvable. The Gemara proposes: *me'ikara ata l'kamei d'Rabbi Akiva* — initially Rabbi Meir came to Rabbi Akiva. *U-mid'lo matzi l'meikav al libei* — but he was not yet able to fully grasp Rabbi Akiva's teaching. This does not mean Rabbi Meir lacked the intellect; rather, he had not yet absorbed sufficient material. *Azal l'kamei d'Rabbi Yishmael, ve-gmar gemara* — so he went to Rabbi Ishmael and there he received the *gemara*, the entire body of transmitted tradition. *Gemara* here means not analytical method but the downloading, as it were, of the entire oral corpus — all the material, everything that needed to be received. *Ve-hadar ata l'kamei d'Rabbi Akiva vesavar svara* — and then he returned to Rabbi Akiva equipped with all the material, and now he was able to learn the analytical acuity, the *svara*, that Rabbi Akiva's teaching demanded.

This reflects an important principle. The Gemara elsewhere famously debates whether it is more valuable to be *Sinai* — one who knows all the material, as if one received the Torah from Mount Sinai — or *oker harim* — one who can uproot mountains, who possesses incisive analytical power. The point here is that the two are not in competition but in sequence: what good is *oker harim* if you do not yet have all the material? How can you analyze and bring all the relevant traditions to bear on a question if you have not yet received them? Rabbi Meir needed to be fully *Sinai* before he could be the preeminent *oker harim* in Rabbi Akiva's academy. Once he had received the entire tradition from Rabbi Ishmael, he could return to Rabbi Akiva and realize his full intellectual potential. So the order of study is reconcilable: Rabbi Akiva first, then Rabbi Ishmael for the *gemara*, then back to Rabbi Akiva for the *svara*.

The contradiction about who issued the prohibition, however, is not resolvable. *Ella asiya a-asiya kashya* — this remains a genuine difficulty. We have two irreconcilable versions, and the Gemara simply acknowledges: *kashya* — it is a problem. With that, the Gemara notes in passing that the entire purpose of quoting this story was to demonstrate that the student who reported Rabbi Ishmael's teaching to Rabbi Akiva must have been Rabbi Meir, since he is the one known student who moved between both academies.

The Gemara then continues with a series of teachings about *kankantom* and the *parshat sotah* that flow from this discussion. A *braisa* is cited in which Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Meir: *lechol matilin kankantom letoch hadeyo chutz miparshes sotah* — for all purposes one may add *kankantom* to the ink, except for the writing of the *parshat sotah*. For the *sotah*, the Torah requires *ve-chasav u-macha* — the priest writes and then erases — and therefore the writing must be of the kind that *can* be erased. *Kankantom* would make erasure impossible, rendering the scroll invalid for the *sotah* ritual.

This raises the question of what Rabbi Ishmael's original objection was. Was he objecting only to using *kankantom* for *parshat sotah*, or was his objection broader, applying to the entire *Sefer Torah*? Tosafot raises this explicitly: *amar Rabbi Yishmael she-haya oser bechol ha-Torah kulah* — Rabbi Ishmael forbade it across the entire Torah. Perhaps, Tosafot suggests, Rabbi Ishmael derived this from the verse *ve-chasav u-macha* through a *gezeira shava* applied universally, reasoning that all halachically valid writing must be of the erasable type — or alternatively, perhaps the prohibition was rabbinic in scope, extending beyond *sotah* as a precautionary measure. Rabbi Meir, by contrast, limited the problem strictly to *parshat sotah*.

Rabbi Yaakov then transmits a further refinement in Rabbi Meir's name: the prohibition applies only to *parshat sotah she-ba-mikdash* — to the actual scroll written for the *sotah* ceremony in the Temple. But if the *parshat sotah* appears within a full *Sefer Torah*, one may use *kankantom* even there. The practical question between the two versions of Rabbi Meir's position is whether one may erase the *parshat sotah* from a *Sefer Torah* to perform the *sotah* ritual. If the *Sefer Torah* contains *kankantom* throughout, it cannot be erased from it, and so you lose the option of using it for *sotah*. Whether that is a concern depends on whether you may even use a *Sefer Torah* for a *sotah* in the first place.

Rabbi Yirmiyah identifies this as the crux of the matter, and the Gemara then examines a parallel *Tannaic* debate: the *tana kama* holds that a scroll written validly for one *sotah* cannot be reused for another woman, while Rabbi Achai bar Yoshiya holds it can. The question is whether the same *lishmah* requirement that governs the first case governs the use of a *Sefer Torah*. Rav Papa argues that these are not necessarily parallel: the *tana kama* may restrict use for a different woman because once designated for Rachel, it can no longer be reassigned to Leah — but a *Sefer Torah* written generically, for no woman at all, might nonetheless be usable. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak argues the opposite: Rabbi Achai bar Yoshiya's permissive position may apply where the scroll was at least written for *some* woman as a *sotah*, whereas a *Sefer Torah* written with no intent for the *sotah* ritual at all might be unusable for that purpose.

The Gemara further asks whether Rabbi Achai bar Yoshiya's position is consistent with the *mishnah* concerning a *get* written for one woman that cannot be used for another (*shim'cha keshmi ve-shem ishtecha keshem ishti* — your name is like mine and your wife's name is like my wife's name). The Gemara distinguishes the cases: by a *get*, the Torah uses the language *ve-kasav lah* — he shall write *for her* — establishing a requirement of *ketiva lishmah*, writing specifically for this woman. By *sotah*, the Torah says *ve-asa lah* — the priest shall *do* for her — placing the *lishmah* requirement on the *doing*, which is the erasure into the water, not the writing itself. The writing is only a preparatory stage; the critical act is the scraping. Accordingly, there may be no requirement that the *ketiva* of the *sotah* scroll be for this particular woman, even if there is some minimal requirement that the writing be oriented toward the *sotah* ritual in general.

## R. Meir's Brilliance

### Meir's Unmatched Brilliance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לִפְנֵי מִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם שֶׁאֵין בְּדוֹרוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר כְּמוֹתוֹ, וּמִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא קָבְעוּ הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ? שֶׁלֹּא יָכְלוּ חֲבֵירָיו לַעֲמוֹד עַל סוֹף דַּעְתּוֹ. שֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר עַל טָמֵא טָהוֹר וּמַרְאֶה לוֹ פָּנִים, עַל טָהוֹר טָמֵא וּמַרְאֶה לוֹ פָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of Rabbi Meir and his Torah study, the Gemara cites an additional statement. **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina said: It is revealed and known before the One Who spoke and the world came into being that in the generation of Rabbi Meir there was no** one of the Sages who is **his equal. Why** then **didn't** the Sages **establish the *halakha* in accordance with his** opinion? It is **because his colleagues were unable to ascertain the profundity of his opinion.** He was so brilliant that he could present a cogent argument for any position, even if it was not consistent with the prevalent *halakha*. **As he** would **state with regard to** a ritually **impure** item that it is **pure, and display justification** for that ruling, and likewise he would state **with regard to** a ritually **pure** item that it is **impure, and display justification** for that ruling.

The Gemara now turns from the story of Rabbi Meir the scribe to a broader portrait of Rabbi Meir's extraordinary intellect. Having shown that Rabbi Meir needed to first study under Rabbi Ishmael before he could fully absorb Rabbi Akiva's teaching, the Gemara moves swiftly to put any resulting questions about Rabbi Meir's capacity thoroughly to rest.

Rabbi Akha bar Chanina states: *gali ve-yadu'a lifnei mi she-amar ve-haya ha'olam she-ein be-doro shel Rabbi Meir kamoso* — it is revealed and known before the One who spoke and the world came into being that there was no one in Rabbi Meir's generation at his intellectual level. This is a striking formulation: it is not a human assessment but a divine one.

### Why Not Rule Like Meir

If Rabbi Meir was unquestionably the greatest mind of his generation, why do we not rule like him as a general principle? It is not that we systematically rule *against* Rabbi Meir, but in standard disputes — Rabbi Meir against Rabbi Yehuda, for instance — we rule like Rabbi Yehuda. It should be noted that the Gemara does say *stam matnisin Rabbi Meir* — that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi used the teachings of Rabbi Meir as the foundation for the anonymous voice of the *Mishnayos*. Yet when Rabbi Meir appears in explicit debate, we often do not rule like him.

The reason the Gemara gives is: *shelo yachlu chaveirav la-amod al sof da'ato* — his colleagues could not reach the bottom of his thinking. He was so profound that nobody could fully comprehend his reasoning. More than that: *shehu omer al tamei tahor u-marei lo panim, al tahor tamei u-marei lo panim* — he could declare something that was *tamei* to be *tahor* and marshal evidence for it, and equally declare something *tahor* to be *tamei* and bring compelling proof. The implication cuts in two directions: not only could others not always follow his argument, they also could not always trust that his argument — however dazzling — was arriving at a sound conclusion. If a person is so brilliant that he can prove anything, then a correct-seeming proof may not be trustworthy, not because he intended to deceive, but because such extraordinary dialectical power can lead even the most sincere reasoner to conclusions that, in the end, most would not accept.

This raises a question about the *sanhedrin* and the requirement that its members be capable of making arguments for purity and impurity — a point the Gemara will develop further shortly.

The tension here is worth sitting with. The Gemara holds up Rabbi Meir as the supreme intellect of his generation while simultaneously explaining why his rulings often do not prevail. And it introduces the unsettling possibility that the same brilliance which makes someone the greatest *posek* in theory can make that person's rulings difficult to rely on in practice. The halachic process requires not only intellectual power but the capacity to carry one's colleagues — to reason in a way they can follow, evaluate, and ultimately endorse.

### The Name Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: לֹא רַבִּי מֵאִיר שְׁמוֹ אֶלָּא רַבִּי נְהוֹרַאי שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ רַבִּי מֵאִיר? שֶׁהוּא מֵאִיר עֵינֵי חֲכָמִים בַּהֲלָכָה. וְלֹא נְהוֹרַאי שְׁמוֹ אֶלָּא רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה שְׁמוֹ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲרָךְ שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ נְהוֹרַאי? שֶׁמַּנְהִיר עֵינֵי חֲכָמִים בַּהֲלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir was not his name; rather, Rabbi Nehorai was his name. And why was he called** by the **name Rabbi Meir?** It was **because he illuminates [*meir*] the eyes of the Sages in** matters of **the *halakha*. And Rabbi Nehorai was not the name** of the *tanna* known by that name; **rather, Rabbi Neḥemya was his name, and some say: Rabbi Elazar ben Arakh was his name. And why was he called** by the **name Rabbi Nehorai?** It is **because he enlightens [*manhir*] the eyes of the Sages in** matters of **the *halakha*.**

A *tana* teaches: *lo Rabbi Meir shemo, ella Rabbi Nehorai shemo* — his original name was not Rabbi Meir but Rabbi Nehorai. *Ve-lamah nikra shemo Rabbi Meir?* — and why was he called Rabbi Meir? *She-haya me'ir einei chachamim ba-halacha* — because he would illuminate the eyes of the sages in *halacha*.

This is itself a somewhat striking formulation given what was just said about him. If nobody could fully understand his reasoning, in what sense did he illuminate their eyes? The answer seems to be that the encounter with Rabbi Meir's intellect forced his colleagues to elevate their own. They could not keep up with him, but the very effort of trying to engage his arguments sharpened their minds and made them better at *halacha*. It is reminiscent of the *le-chadeid ba-hem et ha-talmidim* — teaching contrary positions in order to sharpen the students — mentioned earlier in this *amud*. The danger that such brilliance might prove false things true does not negate the fact that the encounter with it makes everyone around it more capable.

The Gemara continues: *ve-lo Nehorai shemo* — and the person known as Rav Nehorai was also known by a pseudonym. His actual name was Rabbi Nechemia, or according to another opinion, Rabbi Elazar ben Arach. He too was called Nehorai because *she-man-ir einei chachamim ba-halacha* — he illuminated the eyes of the sages in *halacha*.

There is an important tension the Gemara is navigating. On one hand, it presents Rabbi Meir's intellectual power as potentially dangerous — he could make the false appear true. On the other hand, it immediately affirms that this same power actually improved *halacha* by forcing everyone around him to rise to a higher standard. Intellect is not simply dangerous; it is dangerous *and* indispensable. The question is not whether to have it but how to channel it. This also raises the perennial question of the limits of *halachic* argumentation: if one knows enough sources and possesses enough analytical skill, one can construct a plausible argument for almost any position. What distinguishes a legitimate *halachic* argument from a merely clever one? The answer lies in integrity — not just in whether an argument *can* be made but whether one is genuinely being faithful to what the sources actually require.

### Seeing the Teacher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי: הַאי דִּמְחַדַּדְנָא מֵחַבְרַאי דַּחֲזִיתֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מֵאֲחוֹרֵיהּ, וְאִילּוּ חֲזִיתֵיהּ מִקַּמֵּיהּ הֲוָה מְחַדַּדְנָא טְפֵי — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיוּ עֵינֶיךָ רוֹאוֹת אֶת מוֹרֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said:** The fact **that I am** more **incisive than my colleagues is** due to the fact **that I saw Rabbi Meir from behind,** i.e., I sat behind him when I was his student. **Had I seen him from the front, I would be** even more **incisive, as it is written: "And your eyes shall see your teacher"** (Isaiah 30:20).

Rabbi continues this portrait of Rabbi Meir by saying: *hai de-mechadedna mechaberai, de-chazitei le-Rabbi Meir me-achorei* — the reason I am sharper than my contemporaries is that I at least saw Rabbi Meir from behind. *Ve-ilu chazitei mikamei, havei mechadedna tefei* — had I seen him from the front, I would have been even sharper still.

The Gemara cites the verse: *ve-hayu einecha ro'ot et morecha* — your eyes shall see your teacher. This is understood on one level as a practical claim about the completeness of communication: as much as ninety percent of communication is nonverbal, and seeing a teacher's facial expressions, gestures, and physical comportment conveys nuances that words alone cannot. Seeing only a teacher's back captures body movement and posture but misses the face; seeing from the front would yield still more. But there is another dimension here as well. Rabbi Meir was not merely a repository of information or a logical machine; he was a person whose passion and engagement had a galvanic effect on everyone around him. The personal encounter — the *gestures*, the *fire* — inspired students to push themselves further than they otherwise would have. The intellectual sharpening was inseparable from the relational and spiritual dimension of the teacher-student encounter.

One might also read this in light of the famous passage elsewhere about Rabbi Elazar ben Arach, who journeyed to a place of pleasant springs and beautiful surroundings — and forgot all his Torah. Intellect alone, divorced from the living engagement with teachers and colleagues, is ultimately insufficient. What sustains Torah is not brilliance in isolation but the dynamic of living transmission: the face of the teacher, the eyes of the student, the charge that passes between them.

The Gemara cites the verse to support Rabbi's statement — *ve-hayu einecha ro'ot et morecha* — and one cannot help noting the resonance with the famous divine declaration to Moshe in a different context: *panai lo yeira'u ve-ra'ita et achorai* — you cannot see My face, but you may see My back. Here, Rabbi saw the back of Rabbi Meir, and it was sufficient to make him the sharpest of his generation. Had he seen the face, one can only imagine.

### Sumchus & Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תַּלְמִיד הָיָה לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְסוֹמְכוֹס שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁהָיָה אוֹמֵר עַל כׇּל דָּבָר וְדָבָר שֶׁל טוּמְאָה אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנֶה טַעֲמֵי טוּמְאָה, וְעַל כׇּל דָּבָר וְדָבָר שֶׁל טׇהֳרָה אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנֶה טַעֲמֵי טׇהֳרָה.
>
> **Translation:** And the Gemara stated that **Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabbi Meir had a disciple, and his name was Sumakhus, who would state with regard to each and every matter of ritual impurity forty-eight reasons** in support of the ruling of **impurity, and with regard to each and every matter of ritual purity forty-eight reasons** in support of the ruling of **purity.**

The Gemara then introduces Rabbi Meir's student Sumchus: *she-haya omer kol davar ve-davar shel tumah arba'im u-shmonah ta'amei tumah, ve-al kol davar ve-davar shel taharah arba'im u-shmonah ta'amei tahara* — for every matter of *tumah* he could give forty-eight demonstrations of why it was *tamei*, and for every matter of *tahara* forty-eight demonstrations of why it was *tahor*. The Maharsha notes the significance of the number forty-eight: there are said to be fifty gates of wisdom, of which Moshe possessed forty-nine. Presumably no one below Moshe could exceed forty-eight — and Sumchus reached that peak. Crucially, however, Sumchus used his forty-eight proofs to demonstrate what was actually *tamei* and what was actually *tahor* — not to invert them. He absorbed the sharpness of Rabbi Meir but directed it toward truth rather than toward dazzling reversal.

### Anonymous Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: תַּלְמִיד וָתִיק הָיָה בְּיַבְנֶה שֶׁהָיָה מְטַהֵר אֶת הַשֶּׁרֶץ בְּמֵאָה וַחֲמִשִּׁים טְעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **There was a distinguished disciple at Yavne who could** with his incisive intellect **purify the creeping animal,** explicitly deemed ritually impure by the Torah, adducing **one hundred and fifty reasons** in support of his argument.

A *braisa* then records: *talmid vatik haya be-Yavne she-haya metaher et ha-sheretz be-me'ah va-chamishim ta'amim* — there was a distinguished student in Yavne who could give a hundred and fifty proofs that a *sheretz* — one of the eight ritually impure rodents enumerated in the Torah — was actually *tahor*. This is where the provocative capacity to prove the opposite, which was said of Rabbi Meir, now reasserts itself — but notice that it is attributed to an anonymous figure, not to Rabbi Meir himself, and certainly not to Sumchus. The Gemara seems deliberately to ascribe this inversion-of-truth capacity to an unnamed person rather than to any named sage whose rulings one would want to rely upon.

### Ravina's Failed Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, אֲנִי אָדוּן וַאֲטַהֲרֶנּוּ: וּמָה נָחָשׁ שֶׁמֵּמִית וּמַרְבֶּה טוּמְאָה — טָהוֹר, שֶׁרֶץ שֶׁאֵין מֵמִית וּמַרְבֶּה טוּמְאָה — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: I** too **will deliberate and purify it** employing the following reasoning: **And just as a snake that kills** people and animals **and** thereby **increases ritual impurity** in the world, as a corpse imparts impurity through contact, through being carried, and by means of a tent, **is ritually pure** and transmits no impurity, **a creeping animal that does not kill and** does not **increase impurity** in the world, **all the more so** should it be pure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא — מַעֲשֶׂה קוֹץ בְּעָלְמָא קָעָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **And it is not so;** that is not a valid *a fortiori* argument, as it can be refuted. A snake **is performing a mere act of a thorn.** A thorn causes injury and even death; nevertheless, it is not ritually impure. The same applies to a snake, and therefore this *a fortiori* argument is rejected.

Ravina then says: *ani adun ve-ataharenu* — let me try to prove that a *sheretz* should be *tahor*. His argument: *u-mah nachash she-meimit u-marbeh tumah tahor, sheretz she-eino meimit ve-eino marbeh tumah, lo kol sheken* — a snake, which can kill and thereby bring *tumah* into the world, is nonetheless *tahor* — it is not among the eight *shratzim* that the Torah declares *tamei*. If a snake, which causes death and spreads *tumah*, is *tahor*, certainly a *sheretz*, which neither kills nor spreads *tumah*, should be *tahor* — all the more so.

The Gemara dismisses this: *ve-lo hi, ma'aseh kotz be-alma hu* — it is not a valid proof. A snake is like a thorn: it injects venom, it stings. The *tumah* it generates is incidental, a byproduct of its action, not something intrinsic to the snake itself. This fails entirely as a *kal va-chomer*. The argument does not even count as one of a hundred and fifty. The Gemara's willingness to play this game for a moment, only to dismiss Ravina's attempt with a shrug, is itself telling. The entire arc of this section — from the praise of Rabbi Meir's brilliance, to Sumchus using that brilliance for truth, to an anonymous sage proving a *sheretz* is *tahor*, to Ravina's failed attempt — amounts to a sustained corrective: the capacity to prove the opposite is a real and formidable power, but the Gemara is not celebrating it. It holds it up precisely to frame its limits.

## Beit Shammai & Hillel

### The Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל, הַלָּלוּ אוֹמְרִים: הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתֵנוּ, וְהַלָּלוּ אוֹמְרִים: הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתֵנוּ. יָצְאָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ דִּבְרֵי אֱלֹהִים חַיִּים הֵן, וַהֲלָכָה כְּבֵית הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said** that **Shmuel said: For three years Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagreed. These said: The *halakha* is in accordance with our** opinion, **and these said: The *halakha* is in accordance with our** opinion. Ultimately, **a Divine Voice emerged and proclaimed:** Both **these and those are the words of the living God. However, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Hillel.**

Rav Abba said in the name of Shmuel: *shalosh shanim nechleku Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel* — for three years Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel debated. *Hallalu omrim halacha k'moseinu ve-hallalu omrim halacha k'moseinu* — each declared that the *halacha* should follow them. The *rishonim* explain that this was not simply each school claiming to be right on the merits; it was a procedural dispute. Beit Hillel was the majority in numbers, while Beit Shammai was the sharper and more brilliant school. The debate was therefore about the ground rules of *halachic* decision-making: does a larger number of voices outweigh greater intellectual acuity, or does brilliance override numbers?

*Yatza bat kol ve-amra* — a heavenly voice declared: *eilu ve-eilu divrei Elokim chayim* — these and these are both words of the living God — *ve-halacha ke-Veit Hillel* — and the *halacha* follows Beit Hillel.

What does *eilu ve-eilu divrei Elokim chayim* mean? The Ritva, drawing on the *Rambam*, offers what is arguably the most principled explanation. The *Rambam* held that the overwhelming majority of *Torah she-be-al peh* was never pre-given as a fixed set of rulings transmitted from Sinai. There is a small body of *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — a handful of direct transmissions — but the rest, the vast majority of the halachic corpus, is the product of human intellect and creativity applied to the interpretation of the written Torah, operating through defined hermeneutical rules such as the *yud-gimmel middos she-ha-Torah nidreshes bahen*. This stands in contrast to another view, based on a certain *aggadita* in the Gemara, that all rulings were given to Moshe at Sinai and disputes arise only from failures of transmission. The *Rambam* rejected this view on both logical and apologetic grounds: it does not accurately describe what the Gemara actually does, which is not recapture a forgotten tradition but reason from texts to new conclusions; and it hands critics — particularly the *Karaim* — the argument that if there are so many disputes, the transmission cannot be trusted.

Under the *Rambam*'s framework, *eilu ve-eilu divrei Elokim chayim* means that the Torah text is genuinely open to multiple legitimate interpretations, and both the position of Beit Shammai and the position of Beit Hillel represent valid readings of what the Torah means. There is no single original meaning that one school got right and the other got wrong. Nevertheless, we must *posken* — practical *halacha* requires a decision. The question then becomes: on what basis do we decide?

### Why Beit Hillel Won

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁאֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ דִּבְרֵי אֱלֹהִים חַיִּים, מִפְּנֵי מָה זָכוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל לִקְבּוֹעַ הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתָן? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנּוֹחִין וַעֲלוּבִין הָיוּ, וְשׁוֹנִין דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁמַּקְדִּימִין דִּבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְדִבְרֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Since both these and those are the words of the living God, why were Beit Hillel privileged to** have **the *halakha* established in accordance with their** opinion? The reason is **that they were agreeable and forbearing,** showing restraint when affronted, and when they taught the *halakha* they would **teach** both **their** own **statements and the statements of Beit Shammai. Moreover,** when they formulated their teachings and cited a dispute, **they prioritized the statements of Beit Shammai to their** own **statements,** in deference to Beit Shammai.

*U-mipnei mah zachu Beit Hillel likboa halacha k'mosan* — why did Beit Hillel merit that the *halacha* was established like them? The Gemara answers: *mipnei she-nochin ve-aluvin hayu* — because they were pleasant and unassuming, *ve-shonim divreihem ve-divrei Veit Shammai* — and they would teach not only their own positions but also the positions of Beit Shammai, *ve-lo od, ella she-makdimin divrei Veit Shammai le-divreihem* — and not only that, they would even mention Beit Shammai's position *before* their own.

The Gemara illustrates this with the *mishnah* in *Sukkah* about one whose head and most of his body were in the *sukkah* but whose table was inside the house. Beit Shammai rules this is invalid; Beit Hillel rules it valid. Beit Hillel invokes a precedent: the elders of both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel once visited Rabbi Yochanan ben HaChoranis and found him sitting in exactly this way — most of his body in the *sukkah*, his table indoors — and they said nothing. Beit Shammai responds that they did indeed say something: they told him, *im kach hayita noheg, lo kiyamta mitzvat sukkah miyamecha* — if you have been conducting yourself this way all along, you have never actually fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *sukkah*. The point for our purposes is that the *mishnah* mentions the elders of Beit Shammai before those of Beit Hillel, which is itself an expression of the principle. And some hold that the very structure of the *mishnah* — always presenting Beit Shammai's position before Beit Hillel's — reflects Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi following the precedent set by Beit Hillel itself, which always taught the other side's view first.

### Lessons on Humility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁכׇּל הַמַּשְׁפִּיל עַצְמוֹ — הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַגְבִּיהוֹ, וְכׇל הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ עַצְמוֹ — הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַשְׁפִּילוֹ. כׇּל הַמְחַזֵּר עַל הַגְּדוּלָּה — גְּדוּלָּה בּוֹרַחַת מִמֶּנּוּ, וְכׇּל הַבּוֹרֵחַ מִן הַגְּדוּלָּה — גְּדוּלָּה מְחַזֶּרֶת אַחֲרָיו, וְכׇל הַדּוֹחֵק אֶת הַשָּׁעָה — שָׁעָה דּוֹחַקְתּוֹ, וְכׇל הַנִּדְחֶה מִפְּנֵי שָׁעָה — שָׁעָה עוֹמֶדֶת לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **to teach you that anyone who humbles himself, the Holy One, Blessed be He, exalts him, and anyone who exalts himself, the Holy One, Blessed be He, humbles him. Anyone who seeks greatness, greatness flees from him, and,** conversely, **anyone who flees from greatness, greatness seeks him. And anyone who** attempts to **force the moment** and expends great effort to achieve an objective precisely when he desires to do so, **the moment forces him** too, and he is unsuccessful. **And** conversely, **anyone who** is patient and **yields to the moment, the moment stands** by **his** side, and he will ultimately be successful.

The Gemara draws a broader moral: *shekol ha-mashpil atzmo, HaKadosh Baruch Hu magbiho* — whoever humbles themselves, the Holy One lifts them up; *ve-chol ha-magbiya atzmo, HaKadosh Baruch Hu mashpilo* — whoever exalts themselves, the Holy One lowers them. *Kol ha-mechazer achar ha-gedolah, gedolah borachat mimenu* — whoever pursues greatness, greatness flees from him; *ve-chol ha-boreach min ha-gedolah, gedolah mechazeret acharav* — whoever flees from greatness, greatness pursues him. *Ve-chol ha-dochek et ha-sha'ah, sha'ah dochakato* — whoever presses the moment, forces things before their time, the moment presses him back; *ve-chol ha-nidcheh mipnei ha-sha'ah, sha'ah omedet lo* — and whoever yields to the moment and does not force things, the moment stands firm for him.

### Engagement vs. Brilliance

The deeper principle here is that Beit Hillel's humility was not merely a character virtue disconnected from their *halachic* standing. Because they did not treat themselves as the sole repository of truth, because they genuinely engaged with Beit Shammai's arguments and taught them — and taught them *first* — they were the school that truly absorbed all the voices. Even if Beit Shammai was sharper, Beit Hillel was the position that fully grappled with the *totality* of the debate. And *that* is what halachic authority requires: not just brilliance, but engagement.

This connects directly to the theme running through the entire *daf*. Rabbi Meir was the most brilliant of his generation, but his brilliance was so self-contained, so far above his colleagues, that it could not be fully relied upon — not because he was wrong, but because nobody could follow the reasoning well enough to endorse it. Beit Shammai was sharper than Beit Hillel, but Beit Hillel's engagement with all views, including Beit Shammai's, made theirs the position that genuinely represented the broader halachic conversation. There is a consistent message here: *halacha* is not a competition in individual brilliance but a collective enterprise, and it is ultimately those who engage all opinions, who are anchored in community and relationship, who earn the right to rule.

There is also a pointed contemporary implication. The tendency to seek out brilliant *rashei yeshiva* in isolation from community life, elevating their rulings simply on the basis of encyclopedic knowledge and analytic acuity, reflects exactly the model that this Gemara critiques. The great *poskim* of earlier generations were not only scholars; they were people embedded in the realities of their communities, who understood the full human texture of the questions they were deciding. Brilliance in a vacuum is exactly what the Gemara is warning against when it explains why we do not always rule like Rabbi Meir — and why we do rule like Beit Hillel.

One final point on the *bat kol*: it might seem paradoxical to resolve an impasse through a heavenly voice, given the general principle that we do not follow a *bat kol* in halachic disputes — as in the famous case of Rabbi Eliezer and the oven. The answer is that there the procedure was clear: *acharei rabbim lehatos* — we follow the majority. The *bat kol* was therefore out of place because there was an established method. Here, the impasse was precisely *about* the procedure itself — whether to go by majority or by superior intellect — and there was no pre-existing rule to resolve it. A procedural deadlock of that kind may be precisely the situation for which a *bat kol* is appropriate.

## Not to Be Created

### The Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים וּמֶחֱצָה נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל. הַלָּלוּ אוֹמְרִים: נוֹחַ לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא נִבְרָא יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁנִּבְרָא, וְהַלָּלוּ אוֹמְרִים: נוֹחַ לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁנִּבְרָא יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁלֹּא נִבְרָא. נִמְנוּ וְגָמְרוּ: נוֹחַ לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא נִבְרָא יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁנִּבְרָא, עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁנִּבְרָא — יְפַשְׁפֵּשׁ בְּמַעֲשָׂיו. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: יְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּמַעֲשָׂיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: **For two and a half years, Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagreed. These say: It would have been preferable had man not been created than to have been created. And those said: It is preferable for man to have been created than had he not been created.** Ultimately, **they were counted and concluded: It would have been preferable had man not been created than to have been created.** However, **now that he has been created, he should examine his actions** that he has performed and seek to correct them. **And some say: He should scrutinize his** planned **actions** and evaluate whether or not and in what manner those actions should be performed, so that he will not sin.

The Gemara closes with one final dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, this one of a philosophical rather than halachic character. *Shtei shanim va-mechetza nechleku Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel* — for two and a half years — notably shorter than the three years of the previous dispute — Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel debated.

### The Vote

*Hallalu omrim: noach lo le-adam shelo nivra yoser mi-she-nivra* — one school maintained that it would have been better for a person never to have been created than to have been created. *Ve-hallalu omrim: noach lo le-adam she-nivra yoter mi-shelo nivra* — the other held the opposite, that it is better to have been created. One naturally assumes that the more pessimistic position was Beit Shammai's and the more optimistic one Beit Hillel's, consistent with their general orientations. *Nimnu ve-gamru* — they took a vote and concluded: *noach lo le-adam shelo nivra yoter mi-she-nivra* — it would have been better for a human being not to have been created. This is the more pessimistic conclusion, and it is striking that the vote came out this way — particularly in light of the fact that the previous Gemara ruled like Beit Hillel.

### Examine Your Deeds

The Gemara continues: *achshav she-nivra, yefashpesh be-ma'asav* — now that one has been created, one should investigate one's deeds. *Ve-amri lei: yemashmesh be-ma'asav* — or, in another version, one should feel or examine one's deeds. Is there a difference between these two formulations? Rashi explains them in reverse of what one might expect: *yefashpesh be-ma'asav* refers to reflecting *after* the fact on what one has done, examining whether one needs to do *teshuvah* or could have acted better; *yemashmesh be-ma'asav* refers to deliberating *before* acting, considering carefully what the right course of action is. The *Mesillas Yesharim* understands the two as complementary: one addresses making sure you are doing the right thing rather than the wrong thing, and the other addresses ensuring that even when doing the right thing, you are doing it in the best possible way.

The conclusion — that it would have been better for a person not to have been created — might seem at odds with the opening of Bereishis, where *Vayar Elokim ki tov* — God saw that it was good — suggests that creation, including human creation, is unambiguously positive. The resolution lies in the phrase *noach lo*. The question is not whether creation is objectively good — it may well be — but whether it is *pleasant* for the person. *Noach* means ease or pleasantness, not absolute goodness. The demands of a life lived in moral seriousness, the weight of responsibility for one's actions, the ever-present risk of sin — these make human existence genuinely difficult. Beit Hillel's optimistic position may acknowledge the goodness of creation while still granting that the burden of living rightly is immense.

It is worth noting how unusual it is for a Talmudic vote to conclude with the position most naturally associated with Beit Shammai. The previous discussion went to considerable lengths to explain why we rule like Beit Hillel and not Beit Shammai. Yet here, the vote lands on the more Shammai-like conclusion. The Gemara does not comment on the irony. What it does insist upon — and this is the practical upshot of the entire exchange — is that now that we are created, the question of whether it was better not to have been born is beside the point. The task is to examine one's deeds, both before and after acting, and to make of this life, which is what we have, the most morally serious and considered one possible.
