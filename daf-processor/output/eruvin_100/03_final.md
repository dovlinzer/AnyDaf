# Eruvin 100 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Trees & Carrying

### Branches & Three Tefachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אִילָן שֶׁהָיָה מֵיסֵךְ עַל הָאָרֶץ, אִם אֵין גּוּפוֹ גָּבוֹהַּ מִן הָאָרֶץ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים — מְטַלְטְלִים תַּחְתָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a tree that was hanging over the ground,** i.e., its branches hung down on all sides like a tent so that it threw a shadow on the ground, **if** the tips of **its branches are no higher than three handbreadths from the ground, one** may **carry under it.** This applies even if the tree is planted in a public domain, as the branches form partitions which turn the enclosed area into a private domain.

The daf opens with the *mishnah* at the bottom of 99b. The *mishnah* states: *ilan*, a tree, *shemesach al ha'aretz* — that was overhanging the ground, its branches extending outward like a willow — if the foliage, the branches, are less than three *tefachim* removed from the ground, then by the principle of *lavud* they are considered as if connected to the ground and thereby create walls. Consequently, *metaltelin tachtav* — one may carry underneath such a tree on *Shabbos*, since the overhanging branches function as enclosing walls.

### Roots & Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׇׁרָשָׁיו גְּבוֹהִים מִן הָאָרֶץ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים — לֹא יֵשֵׁב עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** If **its roots** were **three handbreadths higher than the ground, one** may **not sit on them,** as it is prohibited to use a tree on Shabbat. Any part of a tree that is three handbreadths above the ground has the status of a tree with regard to this prohibition.

The *mishnah* then turns to a second *halacha* concerning trees, this one not about carrying but about use and contact. The question is: at what point is something considered part of a tree, and at what point is it simply part of the ground? If roots protrude from the ground but remain within three *tefachim* of the ground, they are treated as part of the ground itself — one may sit on them freely. If, however, roots extend higher than three *tefachim*, they are considered part of the tree. The same principle applies to a tree stump: if it remains within three *tefachim* of the ground, it is treated as ground. The distinction of tree versus ground is therefore defined by whether something rises more than three *tefachim* above the earth.

### Rav Huna's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ יָתֵר מִבֵּית סָאתַיִם, מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: One** may **not move** objects in the area **under** the tree if it is **more than two *beit se'a*. What is the reason** for this prohibition?

The *gemara* then records the ruling of Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: *ein metaltelin bo yoter mibeit sa'atayim* — even if overhanging branches create walls and a valid enclosed space, if that space exceeds *beit sa'atayim* (approximately one hundred by one hundred feet), one may not carry throughout the full area; one is limited to carrying within four *amot*. Rashi notes that this applies even if the tree was planted specifically for that purpose. Normally, a space larger than *beit sa'atayim* that was enclosed *l'dirah* — for the purpose of habitation — would permit carrying throughout. Why then does this case differ?

### Tashmishah L'Avir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי דִּירָה שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישָׁהּ לַאֲוִיר, וְכׇל דִּירָה שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישָׁהּ לַאֲוִיר — אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בָּהּ יָתֵר מִבֵּית סָאתַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** This was prohibited **because it is a dwelling that serves** only **the air,** i.e., it is used only by someone guarding the fields or the like. It is not used as permanent living quarters, despite its partitions. **And** the rule with respect to **any dwelling that serves** only **the air** is that **it is not permitted to carry in it** if its area is **more than two *beit se'a*.** As it is not a proper place of residence, the Sages treated it as an enclosure.

The *gemara* at the top of 100a explains: *mishum dehavi dirah she'tashmishah l'avir* — because this is a habitation whose function is oriented toward the air. Any such habitation, even if enclosed *l'dirah*, does not permit carrying beyond *beit sa'atayim*. The phrase *tashmishah l'avir* is deliberately ambiguous, and Rashi elaborates at the opening of 100a: *eino ladur bah tamid ela lehistofef bah shomrei hasadeh* — it is not a space for permanent dwelling, only for temporary shelter, such as when field-watchmen duck under the tree for shade. Rashi suggests two dimensions to this: first, it is not genuine habitation but merely temporary refuge; and second, those who use it are the *shomrei hasadeh*, whose attention is directed outward, toward the field they are guarding, rather than inward toward the space itself. Whether Rashi means to emphasize the temporariness of the shelter or the outward orientation of its occupants — or both — is not entirely resolved.

## Prohibit Climbing

### High Roots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׇׁרָשָׁיו גְּבוֹהִין מִן הָאָרֶץ וְכוּ׳. אִיתְּמַר: שׇׁרְשֵׁי אִילָן הַבָּאִים מִלְּמַעְלָה מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה לְתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה, רַבָּה אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: If **the roots** of the tree are three handbreadths **above the ground,** one may not sit on them on Shabbat. **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to **the roots of a tree that** rise up and then bend and **come** down **from above, from** a height of **three** handbreadths **to within three** handbreadths of the ground. **Rabba said: It is permitted to use them,** and **Rav Sheshet said: It is prohibited to use them.**

This raises a more intricate question. Suppose a root begins below three *tefachim* — within ground-level range — then rises above three *tefachim*, and then curves back down below three *tefachim* again. One might draw the situation as follows: the first portion of the root, running along the ground and below three *tefachim*, is unproblematic. The root then rises past the three-*tefachim* threshold, becoming classified as tree. But what of the portion that continues downward after having crossed that threshold? Is it still considered tree, because it emerged from a part already classified as tree? Or does the fact that it is now below three *tefachim* render it ground again?

The underlying concern with climbing a tree, as the *gemara* explains, is that one may come to break off a branch — a *melachah* on *Shabbos*. Yet once a *gezeirah* is enacted, the rabbis typically do not calibrate it case by case. Rather, a formal definition is established: anything above three *tefachim* is tree; anything below is ground. The practical question, then, is purely technical: what is the status of the descending portion of the root?

### Rava vs. Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, דְּכׇל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה דְּאַרְעָא — אַרְעָא הִיא. רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, דְּכֵיוָן דְּמִכֹּחַ אִיסּוּר קָאָתֵי — אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the rationale of each opinion. **Rabba said** that **it is permitted to use them,** as **anything less than three** handbreadths **from the ground is** considered as **the ground. Rav Sheshet said: It is prohibited to use them; since they come from a prohibited source, they are prohibited.** The section of the tree from which they grow is prohibited. Therefore, these roots should likewise be prohibited.

On this question, Rava and Rav Sheshet disagree. Rava rules *muttar lishhtamesh bahen* — one may use those portions of the root that dip back below three *tefachim*, because *kol pachos migimmel de'ara ara hi* — anything below three *tefachim* is treated as the ground itself, regardless of where it came from. Rav Sheshet rules *assur lishhtamesh bahen* — it is forbidden, because *keivan demikoach issur ka'ati* — since that descending portion emerges from what was already classified as tree, it carries the forbidden status with it. *Issur ka'ati assuri.*

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּדָמוּ כִּמְשׁוּנִּיתָא, דְּסָלְקִין לְעֵילָּא — אֲסוּרִין. דְּנָחֲתִין לְתַתַּאי — שְׁרוּ. לִצְדָדִין — פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבָּה וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to qualify the dispute: With regard to ascending and descending roots **that resemble** a rocky **crag, those that rise upward are** certainly **prohibited** according to all opinions; those that **fall downward are permitted** according to everyone. It is the roots that branch out **to the sides** that are the subject of the **dispute between Rabba and Rav Sheshet.** Rav Sheshet prohibits using them, while Rabba is lenient.

The *gemara* then introduces the case of *damu kemashunita* — something resembling a crag — which Rashi explains as a root that protrudes not only upward and downward but also laterally in multiple directions. The analysis proceeds: those portions that go up above three *tefachim* (*desalkin le'eila*) are forbidden; those that descend below three *tefachim* before ever having crossed the threshold (*denachtin letattai*) are permitted. Those that shoot off to the side (*litzdadin*) and then arc downward — this is the *pelugta de-Rava ve-Rav Sheshet*, the same dispute as before. The lateral offshoot emerges from a portion that was above three *tefachim* and thus classified as tree; according to Rav Sheshet, anything proceeding from that forbidden portion retains the forbidden status, even if it ultimately descends below three *tefachim*.

### Furrows & Corners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אַנִּיגְרָא, וְכֵן בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise,** Rabba and Rav Sheshet disagree about **a tree that grows in a ditch** that has elevated roots, some of which are concealed by the banks of the ditch. The *amora'im* dispute whether the roots concealed by the banks are considered part of the ground. **And likewise,** in the case of a tree that grows **in a corner** between two walls, they disagree as to whether the section between the walls is considered part of the ground.

The *gemara* then proceeds to two analogous cases, which it treats as subject to the same dispute. The first is *anigra* — a tree growing within a furrow. Here the ground level around the tree is at one height, but the tree's base sits in a depression below that level. A person standing on the ordinary ground level might be at a point on the tree that appears to be only one *tefach* above the floor of the furrow. Should one measure from the furrow itself, or from the level at which the person is standing? The *gemara* holds that this falls under the same dispute: the portion of the tree at which the person stands is technically less than three *tefachim* from the furrow floor, but it emerged from a segment of the tree that, from the perspective of the surrounding ground level, stands more than three *tefachim* high. Rav Sheshet would forbid it as *issur ka'ati*; Rava would permit it.

The second case is *b'keren zavit* — a corner. Here the tree grows in a corner formed by two walls. Someone standing atop the walls sees only one face of the tree, the rest being enclosed by the structure on two sides. From the perspective of the person on the wall, it is almost as if the tree begins afresh at that point. And yet the visible portion of the tree above the wall is continuous with what lies below, which from the perspective of the outside is already more than three *tefachim* high. This, too, is the same dispute. The *gemara* acknowledges the conceptual peculiarity that the same segment of tree could simultaneously appear permitted (from above the wall) and forbidden (from below it), depending on where one is standing.

### Abaye's Palm Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דִּיקְלָא דַּהֲוָה לְאַבָּיֵי, וַהֲוָה סָלֵיק בְּאִיפּוּמָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף וּשְׁרָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Abaye had a certain palm tree** that grew in his house and **that projected through an opening in the roof. He came before Rav Yosef** to ask him about it, **and he permitted him** to use the first three handbreadths of the palm tree above the roof, as the tree's lower part is treated as though it were in the ground.

From these cases the *gemara* moves to a story. There was a palm tree growing in Abaye's house — *dahu dikla dahavi lei b'Abaye* — that grew upward through his skylight. Abaye wished to know whether, standing on the roof on *Shabbos*, he could use the first three *tefachim* of the tree that protruded above the roof level. He came before Rav Yosef.

The situation is similar to the corner case: for someone standing on the roof, the tree seems to emerge fresh from the rooftop. And yet below, inside the house, it was already more than three *tefachim* of tree. Rav Yosef permitted it. Rav Acha bar Tachlifa observed that this ruling follows Rava, who holds that one begins counting afresh when the geometry changes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא: דִּשְׁרָא לָךְ, כְּרַבָּה שְׁרָא לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Taḥlifa said** to Abaye: **He who permitted** it **to you, permitted it to you** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabba,** who maintains that a section of a tree concealed from view in at least two directions is considered as though it were underground. Consequently, the first three handbreadths above that section may be used on Shabbat, as they have the status of the ground.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא אֲפִילּוּ לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת בֵּיתָא כְּמַאן דִּמְלֵי דָּמֵי, וְלִישְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לַגַּג, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: This is **obvious.** What novel element is Rav Aḥa bar Taḥlifa teaching us? The Gemara answers: It is necessary, **lest you say** that in this case it should be permitted **even according to Rav Sheshet,** as **the house is considered full,** i.e., it is as though it were filled with earth, **and** this would mean it is permitted **to use** the section **less than three** handbreadths **from the roof.** Rav Aḥa bar Taḥlifa therefore **teaches us** that Rav Sheshet is stringent even in this case.

The *gemara* objects: *peshita* — is this not obvious? The answer is that one might have thought that even Rav Sheshet would agree here, because of the principle that *beita k'man d'mali dami* — the interior of a house is considered as if completely filled. Since the tree passes through solid space inside the house, perhaps even Rav Sheshet would treat the portion above the roof as entirely new. The *gemara* therefore teaches that one may not use even the three *tefachim* immediately above the roof (*lishhtamesh b'pachot mi'gimmel samuch l'gagga*), and that Rav Sheshet's position applies here as well.

### Mishnah's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: שׇׁרָשָׁיו גְּבוֹהִים מִן הָאָרֶץ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים — לֹא יֵשֵׁב עֲלֵיהֶם. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלָא הָדְרִי כָּיְפִי — פְּשִׁיטָא. אֶלָּא לָאו, אַף עַל גַּב דְּהָדְרִי כָּיְפִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to adduce proof from the mishna, in which **we learned:** If **the roots** of the tree are **three handbreadths above the ground, one** may **not sit on them. What are the circumstances** of this case? **If** the situation is **that** the roots **do not bend over again,** this is **obvious,** as anything higher than three handbreadths is part of the tree. **Rather, doesn't** it mean that one may not sit on them **even though they bend back** downward to within three handbreadths of the ground? The mishna apparently indicates that if parts of the roots are more than three handbreadths above the ground, it is prohibited to use them along the rest of their entire length, as maintained by Rav Sheshet, contrary to Rabba.

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah* to clarify its precise scenario. The *mishnah* states that roots higher than three *tefachim* may not be sat upon. The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If the roots simply project upward without bending back down, that seems obvious — we know the prohibition against using a tree, and roots more than three *tefachim* off the ground are clearly part of the tree. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the *mishnah* must be speaking of a case where the roots do bend back down, and is coming to support Rav Sheshet's position: even though the roots return below three *tefachim*, they are forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּלָא הָדָרִי כָּיְפִי, וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן אַף עַל גַּב דְּצִידּוֹ אֶחָד שָׁוֶה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this contention: **No, actually** the mishna is referring to a case **where they do not bend back** downward, **and** the *tanna* comes to **teach us** the following: **Although** on **one side** of the tree the roots are **level with the ground,** nevertheless, it is prohibited to sit on them, as the roots on the other sides are more than three handbreadths above the ground.

The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps the *mishnah* always speaks of roots that do not bend back down, and its *chiddush* is different. It teaches *af al gav d'tzido echad shaveh la'aretz* — even if one side of the root is level with the (higher) ground. In other words, even if one is standing on high ground such that from one's vantage point the root appears to be less than three *tefachim* above the surface, and even three sides of the root are exposed and high, the prohibition still applies. Even Rava, who would normally permit use once the geometry suggests the root starts afresh, would agree here: only when a segment is enclosed on two, three, or all four sides does one begin counting anew. When only one side is at a higher ground level and three sides are fully exposed and more than three *tefachim* above the earth, one does not count afresh.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן שׇׁרְשֵׁי אִילָן שֶׁגְּבוֹהִים מִן הָאָרֶץ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים אוֹ שֶׁיֵּשׁ חָלָל תַּחְתֵּיהֶם שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁצִּידּוֹ אֶחָד שָׁוֶה לָאָרֶץ — הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יֵשֵׁב עֲלֵיהֶם, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עוֹלִין בְּאִילָן, וְאֵין נִתְלִין בְּאִילָן, וְאֵין נִשְׁעָנִין בְּאִילָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **roots of a tree that are three handbreadths above the ground, or if there is** a hollow **space beneath them** of **three handbreadths, although** on **one side** of the tree the roots **are level with the ground, one** may **not sit on them because** of the following rule: **One** may **not climb a tree, nor** may **one hang from a tree** by one's hands, **nor** may **one** even **lean against a tree** on Shabbat.

A *baraita* then elaborates: roots that are three *tefachim* above the ground (*shorashai ilan shegvoim min ha'aretz gimmel tefachim*), or under which there is a hollow of three *tefachim* (*shechalal tachteihem gimmel tefachim*) — even if one side is level with the ground — may not be sat upon. The first formulation may refer to a root that is itself physically thick enough to rise three *tefachim*, while the second refers to an arching root with a gap beneath it; in either case the three-*tefachim* threshold determines status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יַעֲלֶה בְּאִילָן מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם וְיֵשֵׁב שָׁם כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ. אֶחָד אִילָן וְאֶחָד כׇּל הַבְּהֵמָה, אֲבָל בּוֹר שִׁיחַ וּמְעָרָה וְגָדֵר — מְטַפֵּס וְעוֹלֶה מְטַפֵּס וְיוֹרֵד, וַאֲפִילּוּ הֵן מֵאָה אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, **one** may **not climb a tree** on Friday **while it is still day and sit there the entire day** of Shabbat. This constitutes the use of the tree itself, not merely climbing it, and it is therefore prohibited. This *halakha* applies **both** to **a tree and** to **all animals;** one may not climb upon them, hang from them, or lean against them. **However,** the prohibition is not due to the effort involved in climbing, as is evident from the case of **a cistern, ditch, cave, or a fence. One** may **climb up** and **climb down** them, **even if they are a hundred cubits** deep.

The *baraita* then restates the broader prohibition: one may not climb a tree (*ein olim be'ilan*), hang from a tree (*ein nitlin be'ilan*), lean on a tree (*ein nish'anin be'ilan*), nor ascend a tree on Friday afternoon and sit there when *Shabbos* begins — for sitting in the tree is itself a prohibited use. Significantly, the prohibition is not limited to the effort involved in climbing; it is about using the tree at all. This is why the *baraita* draws a parallel: just as one may not ride an animal on *Shabbos* (lest one break off a branch to guide it), the same structure applies to trees. But one may scale a wall or climb in and out of a pit of even a hundred *amot*, because there is no specific *gezeirah* about such structures.

### Two Contradicting Baraitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: אִם עָלָה — מוּתָּר לֵירֵד, וְתָנֵי חֲדָא — אָסוּר לֵירֵד. לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, כָּאן — מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It was taught** in **one** *baraita*: **If one climbed up** a tree, he is **permitted to climb down; and it was taught** in **one** other *baraita* that he is **prohibited to climb down.** The Gemara resolves this apparent contradiction: This is **not difficult. Here,** where it is permitted to descend, one climbed up on Friday, **while it was still day; there,** where it is prohibited to descend, one climbed up on Shabbat **after nightfall.**

The *gemara* then presents two *beraitot* that appear to contradict each other. One states that if one has climbed a tree before *Shabbos*, one may descend once *Shabbos* begins. The other states that one may not descend. The *gemara* resolves this in several ways: the first distinction is between having gone up before nightfall (*mib'od yom*), in which case the initial act was permitted and we show leniency by allowing descent, versus having gone up after nightfall (*misheshachashachah*), in which case the initial act was itself forbidden and we do not permit further activity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, הָא וְהָא מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בְּשׁוֹגֵג, כָּאן — בְּמֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **both** *baraitot* are referring to a case where one climbed up the tree **after nightfall. But** even so, it is **not difficult: Here,** it is permitted to descend, as one climbed up the tree **unwittingly; there,** it is prohibited to descend, as the *baraita* is dealing with one who climbed **intentionally.**

An alternative resolution: both cases involve going up after nightfall, but one was done *beshogeg* — unknowingly — and the other *bemeizid* — intentionally. When done unknowingly, we allow descent out of compassion; when intentional, we do not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, הָא וְהָא בְּשׁוֹגֵג, וְהָכָא בְּקָנְסוּ שׁוֹגֵג אַטּוּ מֵזִיד קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: קָנְסִינַן. וּמָר סָבַר: לָא קָנְסִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **both** *baraitot* are referring to a case where one climbed up **unwittingly, and they disagree about** whether or not the Sages **penalized** an **unwitting** offender **due to an intentional** offender. One **Sage,** who ruled that it is prohibited to descend, **maintains** that **they penalized** an unwitting sinner to prevent others from climbing up on purpose and descending. Therefore, one may not come down even if he ascended by mistake. **And** one **Sage,** who ruled that it is permitted, **maintains** that **they did not penalize** the unwitting sinner in this manner.

A third resolution: both cases are *beshogeg*, and the dispute is whether we penalize unintentional violators because of intentional ones — a familiar question of *kansinan shogeig atu meizid*.

## Marital Relations (III)

### The Comparison to Sacrificial Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כְּתַנָּאֵי. הַנִּיתָּנִין בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ בְּנִיתָּנִין מַתָּנָה אַחַת — יִנָּתְנוּ בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת, מַתַּן אַרְבַּע בְּמַתַּן אַרְבַּע — יִנָּתְנוּ בְּמַתַּן אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** This dispute between the two *baraitot* is **parallel to the** dispute of the ***tanna'im*,** who disagreed with regard to a different matter. The blood of certain sacrifices, e.g., the firstborn and tithe offerings, is sprinkled once on the altar, while the blood of other sacrifices, e.g., burnt-offerings, is sprinkled four times. They require two sprinklings that are four, i.e., two sprinklings on opposite corners, so that the blood falls on all four sides. If the blood of sacrifices **that require** only **one sprinkling becomes intermingled with** the blood of other sacrifices that **require** only **one sprinkling,** the mixture **will be sprinkled once.** Likewise, if the blood of sacrifices that require **four sprinklings** becomes intermingled **with** the blood of other sacrifices that require **four sprinklings,** the mixture **will be sprinkled four times.**

At this point Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua notes that this debate mirrors a dispute among *tanna'im* regarding a completely different subject — the mixing of sacrificial bloods — and it is to this comparison that the *gemara* now turns.

### Active vs. Passive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתַּן אַרְבַּע בְּמַתַּן אַחַת, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יִנָּתְנוּ בְּמַתַּן אַרְבַּע. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: יִנָּתְנוּ בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** If, however, the blood of a sacrifice that requires **four sprinklings** becomes intermingled **with** the blood of a sacrifice that requires only **one sprinkling,** the *tanna'im* disagree: **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The mixture of blood **is sprinkled four times. And Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It **is sprinkled once,** and this suffices for the atonement of the sacrifice.

The *gemara* invokes a well-known dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua concerning the case of sacrificial bloods requiring one application that became mixed with bloods requiring four applications (*matan arba b'matan achat*). If the blood of a *bechor*, which requires a single application, becomes mixed with the blood of an *olah*, which requires application at two opposite corners — effectively four sides — what should be done with the mixture?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: הֲרֵי הוּא עוֹבֵר עַל ״בַּל תִּגְרַע״! אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הֲרֵי הוּא עוֹבֵר בְּ״בַל תּוֹסִיף״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer said to** Rabbi Yehoshua: If one sprinkles the blood only once, **he transgresses** the prohibition **"you shall not diminish,"** which prohibits the omission of any elements of the performance of a mitzva, as he has not sprinkled the blood of the burnt-offering in the proper manner. **Rabbi Yehoshua said to** Rabbi Eliezer: According to your ruling, that one must sprinkle the blood four times, **he transgresses** the prohibition: **Do not add** (Deuteronomy 13:1), which prohibits the addition of elements to a mitzva, as he sprinkles the blood of the firstborn animal more times than necessary.

Rabbi Eliezer says *yinasnu b'matan arba* — apply four times, to ensure that the blood of the *olah* receives its full treatment. Rabbi Yehoshua says *yinasnu bematana achat* — apply only once, doing the minimum. Rabbi Eliezer charges that doing only one application violates *bal tigra* — the prohibition against diminishing a commandment. Rabbi Yehoshua retorts that doing four violates *bal tosif* — the prohibition against adding to a commandment. Each one then dismisses the other's objection: Rabbi Eliezer argues that *bal tosif* only applies when one is performing the extra application with the blood of the offering that requires only one; Rabbi Yehoshua responds symmetrically, that *bal tigra* only applies when one is diminishing the offering with its own blood, not when the situation is complicated by a mixture.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: לֹא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁהוּא בְּעַצְמוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לֹא נֶאֱמַר ״בַּל תִּגְרַע״ אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁהוּא בְּעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer said** to Rabbi Yehoshua: **They said** the prohibition against adding to the mitzvot **only where** the blood stands **by itself,** not when it is part of a mixture. **Rabbi Yehoshua said** to Rabbi Eliezer: Likewise, the prohibition: **Do not diminish, was stated only** in a case **where** the blood stands **by itself.**

It is worth pausing on the rarity of *bal tigra* as an operative prohibition. The verse states: *lo tosif alav v'lo sigra mimenu* — do not add and do not diminish. *Bal tosif* requires that one begin with the base of a *mitzvah* and augment it; one cannot violate *bal tosif* by simply performing an act outside the *mitzvah* context. Similarly, *bal tigra* is difficult to transgress in practice, because the moment one performs less than required, one simply has not fulfilled the *mitzvah* at all — one cannot be said to have diminished the *mitzvah* if one never completed it. This case is therefore unusual: because *bedi'eved* one is considered to have fulfilled the obligation with even a single application, there is a biblical substrate of the *mitzvah* against which a deliberate reduction can be measured. That is what makes *bal tigra* operative here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כְּשֶׁנָּתַתָּ — עָבַרְתָּ עַל ״בַּל תּוֹסִיף״ וְעָשִׂיתָ מַעֲשֶׂה בְּיָדֶךָ. כְּשֶׁלֹּא נָתַתָּ — עָבַרְתָּ עַל ״בַּל תִּגְרַע״ וְלֹא עָשִׂיתָ מַעֲשֶׂה בְּיָדֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua further said** in defense of his position: **When you sprinkle** four times, **you have transgressed** the prohibition: **Do not add,** with regard to one of the sacrifices, **and you** also **performed an action with your** own **hand,** i.e., you transgress the Torah's command by means of a positive act. By contrast, **when you do not sprinkle** four times, even if **you have transgressed** the prohibition: **Do not diminish, you did not perform** the **action with your** own **hand.** If one is forced to deviate from the commands of the Torah, it is better to do so in a passive manner.

Rabbi Yehoshua adds a decisive point: *keshenatata averta al Bal Tosif ve'asita ma'aseh beyadecha* — if you go ahead and apply four times, you have actively committed *bal tosif* with your own hands. Whereas if you apply only once, you may have passively transgressed *bal tigra* by not completing the *olah*'s requirement, but *velo asita ma'aseh beyadecha* — you have not done so through an active deed. Better to transgress passively than to transgress actively.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּאָמַר הָתָם ״קוּם עֲשֵׂה״ עָדִיף, הָכִי נָמֵי יֵרֵד. לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דְּאָמַר הָתָם ״שֵׁב וְאַל תַּעֲשֶׂה״ עָדִיף, הָכִי נָמֵי לֹא יֵרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, sought to argue the following: **According to Rabbi Eliezer, who said there,** with regard to sacrifices, that if both alternatives involve the violation of a prohibition **it is preferable to stand and take action,** i.e., perform a positive action, **here too,** one should **climb down** from the tree, as it is better to perform a single positive transgression by climbing down rather than commit a passive transgression throughout the entire Shabbat by remaining on the tree. By contrast, **according to Rabbi Yehoshua,** who **said** with regard to sacrifices that **it is preferable to sit and not take action, here too,** one should **not descend** from the tree.

This is precisely the principle the *gemara* invokes to illuminate the tree debate. Staying in a tree on *Shabbos* is a continuing passive transgression; climbing down stops the long-term violation but involves an active transgressive act in the process of descending. Rabbi Eliezer, who says in the blood case that one should actively apply four times — *kum aseh adif*, the active course is preferable — would correspondingly say here: *yered*, get down from the tree. Rabbi Yehoshua, who says in the blood case that passive omission is preferable to active transgression — *shev v'al ta'aseh adif* — would correspondingly say: *lo yered*, remain in the tree rather than actively violate in the process of descending.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא לָא הִיא, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם ״קוּם עֲשֵׂה״ עָדִיף — אֶלָּא דְּקָא עָבֵיד מִצְוָה. אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָא עָבֵיד מִצְוָה, הָכִי נָמֵי לֹא יֵרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this comparison: **Perhaps that is not** the case, as the two *halakhot* are not identical. **Rabbi Eliezer** might have **stated** his opinion that **it is preferable to stand and take action only** in the case dealt with **there, where one performs a mitzva** with respect to the additional sprinklings of the burnt-offering. **However, here,** where **one performs no mitzva** whatsoever by descending, **indeed, he** should **not descend.**

However, the *gemara* immediately qualifies this parallel. Perhaps the comparison does not hold in either direction. When Rabbi Eliezer said the active course is preferable, that was because the active element — applying four times — was itself a *mitzvah* act, fulfilling part of the blood service. Climbing down a tree, by contrast, is not a *mitzvah* act; it is only a means of preventing ongoing transgression. *Aval hacha delo avid mitzvah* — here the active part is not a *mitzvah*, so even Rabbi Eliezer might say *lo yered*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי נָמֵי, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הָתָם ״שֵׁב וְאַל תַּעֲשֶׂה״ עָדִיף — אֶלָּא דְּלָא קָא עָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּקָא עָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּיֵרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the comparison can be rejected in a different fashion: **Rabbi Yehoshua** may have **stated** his opinion that **it is preferable to sit and not take action only** in the case dealt with **there,** **where one does not commit a transgression** by refraining from action. **However, here, where one commits a transgression** every additional moment he remains in the tree, **indeed, he** should **descend** from it.

Similarly, when Rabbi Yehoshua said passive omission is preferable, that was because remaining passive meant not actively transgressing *bal tigra* — the passive option truly involved no active wrong. Sitting in a tree, however, is a form of continuous active use of the tree — one's sitting is itself the forbidden act. *Deka avid issura* — even the passive option here involves ongoing transgression. Consequently, perhaps even Rabbi Yehoshua would say *yered* — descend.

The *gemara* thus concludes that the parallel to the blood dispute illuminates the conceptual structure of the tree question without definitively resolving it. The positions on whether to descend correspond to intuitions about whether active or passive transgression is preferable, but the analogy has limits in both directions.

## Live & Dead Trees

### Moist vs. Dry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: אֶחָד אִילָן לַח וְאֶחָד אִילָן יָבֵשׁ, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּלַח, אֲבָל בְּיָבֵשׁ — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an apparent contradiction: **It was taught** in **one** *baraita* that **both a green tree and a dry tree** are included in the prohibition against climbing a tree, whereas **it was taught** in **another** *baraita*: **In what** case **are these matters,** that one may not climb a tree, **stated? With regard to a green** tree. **But in** the case of **a dry** one, **it is permitted** to climb it.

One *baraita* states that the prohibition against using a tree applies equally whether the tree is moist (*lach*) or dry (*yavesh*). A second *baraita* states that the prohibition applies only to a moist tree; a dry tree may be used. The tension between these two positions requires resolution.

### Regrowing Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בְּשֶׁגִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף, כָּאן — בְּשֶׁאֵין גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** It is **not difficult. Here,** the *baraita* that includes a dry tree in the prohibition is referring to a tree whose **stump sends out new** shoots when cut; whereas **there,** the *baraita* that excludes a dry tree from the prohibition is referring to one whose **stump does not send out new** shoots.

Rav Yehuda resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya* — the cases are not in conflict. *Kan shegezo machalif* — when the prohibition applies to a dry tree, it is because *gezo machalif*, the stump will regrow. A tree that will regenerate is not truly dead, even if it currently appears dried out, and therefore the prohibition remains in force. *Kan she'ein gezo machalif* — when a dry tree is permitted, it is because the tree is genuinely dead and will not regrow.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף יָבֵשׁ קָרֵית לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, כָּאן — בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at this answer: **You call** a tree whose **stump sends out new** shoots **dry?** This tree is not dry at all. **Rather,** it is **not difficult,** as both *baraitot* deal with a dry tree whose stump will not send out any new shoots. However, **here,** the *baraita* that permits climbing a dry tree, is referring **to the summer,** when it is evident that the tree is dead; whereas **there,** the *baraita* that prohibits climbing the tree is referring **to the rainy season,** when many trees shed their leaves and it is not obvious which remain alive and which are dead.

The *gemara* challenges this resolution: *gezo machalif yavesh karisu lei?* — if the tree will regrow, can you call it *yavesh*? *Yavesh* implies dead. A dormant but living tree would not normally be called dry.

The *gemara* therefore offers an alternative resolution: *lo kashya* — both *beraitot* deal with a genuinely dead tree. The difference is seasonal. *Kan biyemos hachamah kan biyemos hageshamim* — one *baraita* speaks of summer and the other of winter. A dead tree in summer is obviously dead — it is bare while everything around it blooms — and there is no concern that people will mistake it for living and permit themselves to use live trees. A dead tree in winter, however, looks no different from any dormant tree; people cannot tell whether it is dead or merely seasonally bare, and so there remains a concern that permitting its use will lead people to use live trees.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, הָא נָתְרִי פֵּירֵי! בִּדְלִיכָּא פֵּירֵי. וְהָא קָא נָתְרִי קִינְסֵי! בְּגִדּוּדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **In the summer, the fruit** of the previous year left on the dry tree **will fall off** when he climbs it, and climbing the tree should therefore be prohibited lest he come to pick the fruit. The Gemara answers: We are dealing here with a case **where there is no fruit** on the tree. The Gemara asks: **But small branches will fall off** when he climbs the tree, and once again this should be prohibited in case he comes to break them off. The Gemara answers: We are dealing here **with a tree that has** already **been stripped** of all its small branches.

The *gemara* then asks: even a dead tree in summer — if one climbs it, fruit will fall. Rashi explains that this does not mean one transgresses *kotzer* — since the tree is dead, nothing is being severed from a growing plant — but the concern is that allowing one to climb a dead tree bearing remnant fruit from the previous season may lead one to climb live fruit-bearing trees. The *gemara* responds: suppose there is no fruit. It then asks: even so, small chips and twigs (*kintzi*) will fall. The *gemara* ultimately limits the permitted case to *geduda* — a tree that has been stripped bare, with no remaining twigs or protrusions, so that nothing can fall or be dislodged.

### Rav's Fence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא רַב אִיקְּלַע לְאַפְסַטְיָא, וַאֲסַר בְּגִדּוּדָא! רַב בִּקְעָה מָצָא, וְגָדַר בָּהּ גָּדֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that** really **so? But Rav arrived at** a place called **Apsetaya and prohibited** its residents from climbing even **a tree that had** already **been stripped of** all **its branches.** The Gemara answers: In truth, no prohibition was involved, but **Rav found** an unguarded **field,** i.e., a place where transgression was widespread, **and fenced it in.** He added a stringency as a safeguard and prohibited an action that was fundamentally permitted.

Yet even this fully stripped dead tree was forbidden in practice by Rav in a specific context. The *gemara* reports: *rav ikla l'aspatya ve'asar begeduda* — Rav arrived in the town of Aspatya and forbade using even a stripped dead tree. The *gemara* explains this with the famous phrase: *rav bika matza vegadar bah gader* — Rav found an open valley and built a fence around it. Technically, using a stripped dead tree was permitted, but Rav observed that the population in that locale was apparently too casual about these distinctions, and he imposed a stricter standard than the letter of the law required, judging that these people needed a firm boundary rather than the nuanced case-by-case analysis that the *gemara* had just constructed.

## Grass on Shabbat

### Grass Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיְּהַלֵּךְ עַל גַּבֵּי עֲשָׂבִים בְּשַׁבָּת, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָץ בְּרַגְלַיִם חוֹטֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Abba said** that **Rav Asi said: It is prohibited for a person to walk on grass on Shabbat, due to** the fact **that it is stated: "And he who hastens with his feet sins"** (Proverbs 19:2). This verse teaches that mere walking occasionally involves a sin, e.g., on Shabbat, when one might uproot the grass on which he walks.

Rav Assi teaches, as quoted by Rabbi bar Abba: *assur l'adam sheyehaleich al gabi asavim b'Shabbos* — it is forbidden to walk on grass on *Shabbos*. The reason: one will inevitably uproot blades of grass, thereby violating *kotzer*. The source offered is the verse *v'atz beraglayim chotei* — one who hastens with the feet is a sinner; by the mere act of walking one can commit a transgression.

### Seasons & Grass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: מוּתָּר לֵילֵךְ עַל גַּבֵּי עֲשָׂבִים בְּשַׁבָּת, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אָסוּר. לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּלַחִים, הָא — בִּיבֵשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another apparent contradiction: **It was taught** in **one** *baraita* that **it is permitted to walk on grass on Shabbat, and it was taught** in **another** *baraita* that **it is prohibited** to do so. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** *baraita* is referring **to green** grass, which one might uproot, thereby transgressing the prohibition against reaping on Shabbat. **That** other *baraita* is referring **to dry** grass, which has already been cut off from its source of life, and therefore the prohibition of reaping is no longer in effect.

Two *beraitot* again appear to contradict each other: one permits walking on grass, the other forbids it. The first resolution: *ha belachim ha biyaveshim* — moist, growing grass is forbidden (since uprooting it constitutes *kotzer*); dry, dead grass is permitted (since there is nothing growing to uproot).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, הָא וְהָא בְּלַחִים, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, כָּאן — בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **both** *baraitot* are referring **to green** grass, **and** yet **there is no difficulty: Here,** the *baraita* that prohibits walking on grass is referring **to the summer,** when the grass includes seeds that might be dislodged by one's feet, whereas **there,** the *baraita* that permits doing so is referring **to the rainy season,** when this problem does not exist.

An alternative resolution: both *beraitot* refer to moist grass, and the distinction is again seasonal. *Kan biyemos hachamah kan biyemos hageshamim* — in summer versus winter. Rashi suggests that in summer there are seeds in the grass stalks that become scattered, creating an additional concern; in winter this does not apply, or alternatively the grass in winter is not actually pulled up by walking. Precisely why the season makes the operative difference is not entirely clear from Rashi's formulation.

### Shoes & Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, הָא וְהָא בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — דְּסָיֵים מְסָאנֵיהּ, הָא — דְּלָא סָיֵים מְסָאנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **both** *baraitot* are referring **to the summer, and** it is **not difficult: This** *baraita*, which permits walking on grass, is referring to a case **where one is wearing his shoes,** whereas **that** other *baraita*, which prohibits it, deals with a situation **where one is not wearing his shoes,** as the grass might get entangled between his toes and be uprooted.

The *gemara* offers further alternative resolutions, each more granular than the last. Perhaps both cases involve moist grass in summer, and the distinction is whether one wears shoes: *d'siyamei masanei* — shoes with flat soles do not pull up grass as easily as bare feet.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא דְּסָיֵים מְסָאנֵיהּ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — דְּאִית לֵיהּ עוּקְצֵי, הָא — דְּלֵית לֵיהּ עוּקְצֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **both** *baraitot* are referring to a case **where one is wearing his shoes, and** nevertheless this is **not difficult: This** *baraita* prohibits walking on grass, as it involves a case **where** one's shoe **has a spike** on which the grass might get caught and be uprooted, whereas **that** other *baraita* permits it, because it deals a case **where** one's shoe **does not have a spike.**

Or both cases involve shoes, and the difference is whether the shoes have cleats (*uktzei*): cleated shoes will catch and tear up grass, flat-soled shoes will not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, הָא וְהָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ עוּקְצֵי: הָא — דְּאִית לֵיהּ שְׁרָכָא, הָא — דְּלֵית לֵיהּ שְׁרָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **both** are referring to a case **where** the shoe **has a spike,** and it is not difficult: **This** *baraita*, which prohibits walking on grass, is referring to a case **where** the grass is **long and entangled,** and it can easily get caught on the shoe, whereas **that** other *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the grass **is not long and entangled.**

Or both cases involve cleated shoes, and the distinction is whether the grass is long enough to become entangled in the cleats (*sircha*) or short enough that it will not.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִידָּנָא דְּקַיְימָא לַן כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, כּוּלְּהוּ שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And now, when we maintain** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with the opinion** of **Rabbi Shimon,** who maintains that there is no liability for a prohibited act committed unwittingly during the performance of a permitted act, **all of these** scenarios **are permitted,** as here too, one's intention is merely to walk and not to uproot grass on Shabbat.

Having offered all these distinctions, the *gemara* concludes with a sweeping practical ruling: *ve'idna d'kaima lan k'Rabbi Shimon d'davar she'eino mitkavein mutar* — in contemporary practice we rule like Rabbi Shimon that an unintentional act (*davar she'eino mitkavein*) is permitted, provided it is not a *psik reisha* — an inevitable consequence. Walking on grass, even moist summer grass with cleated shoes, does not inevitably uproot every blade underfoot; it is not a *psik reisha*, and since one does not intend to uproot, it is therefore entirely permitted under Rabbi Shimon's ruling.

One might ask: if so, why is leaning on a tree still forbidden, given that one does not intend to break off a branch? The answer is that leaning on a tree is an *issur derabbanan* — a rabbinic prohibition enacted as a *gezeirah* against coming to use a tree deliberately. That prohibition does not turn on the *davar she'eino mitkavein* analysis; the rabbis forbade the act categorically lest one proceed to intentional violation, quite apart from what one's actual intention in the moment happens to be.

## Marital Relations (III)

### The Prohibition Against Forcing One's Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיָּכוֹף אִשְׁתּוֹ לִדְבַר מִצְוָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָץ בְּרַגְלַיִם חוֹטֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *halakha* derived from the verse mentioned in the previous discussion. **Rami bar Ḥama said** that **Rav Asi said: It is prohibited for a man to force his wife in the** conjugal **mitzva,** i.e., sexual relations, **as it is stated: "And he who hastens with his feet sins"** (Proverbs 19:2). The term his feet is understood here as a euphemism for intercourse.

The *gemara* now takes up the verse *v'atz beraglayim chotei* — one who hastens with the feet is a sinner — in a very different context. Rami bar Chama teaches in the name of Rav Assi: *assur l'adam sheyakof ishto l'dvar mitzvah* — a man may not compel his wife to have sexual relations. *Dvar mitzvah* here refers to the conjugal obligation. The source is the same verse: *v'atz beraglayim chotei*, where *raglayim* is understood as a reference to the sexual domain — hastening toward the wife before she is ready constitutes a sin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל הַכּוֹפֶה אִשְׁתּוֹ לִדְבַר מִצְוָה הָוְיָין לוֹ בָּנִים שֶׁאֵינָם מְהוּגָּנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: Anyone who forces his wife to** perform **the** conjugal **mitzva will have unworthy children** as a consequence.

It is worth pausing to note the historical context. Marital coercion was not legally recognized as a wrong in most Western legal systems until roughly the last fifty years. The *gemara*'s framing, while genuine in its moral concern, positions the prohibition primarily as a question of proper conduct rather than as a formal crime — the topic appears in Rambam under *Hilchot De'ot* rather than in the laws of prohibited acts. This framing is itself neither entirely progressive nor entirely traditional by contemporary standards; it reflects the complex position of the *gemara* on this question, which is frankly mixed. On one side, the *gemara* clearly says one may not force one's wife. On the other, a wife who refuses conjugal relations may lose her *ketubah*, and the asymmetry is never resolved. The dual nature of the situation should not be smoothed over.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds: *kol hakofer ishto l'dvar mitzvah havayan lo banim she'einam mehuganim* — whoever forces his wife to have sex will have children who are not fitting, not well-formed in character. This formulation situates the prohibition squarely within the *gemara*'s broader concern that the moral conditions surrounding conception influence the nature of the children conceived. The precise scope of the term *yakof* is not fixed by the *gemara*; whether it covers only physical compulsion or also subtler forms of psychological pressure is left open.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִיקָא בַּר חִינָּנָא, מַאי קְרָאָה: ״גַּם בְּלֹא דַעַת נֶפֶשׁ לֹא טוֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ika bar Ḥinnana said: What is the verse** that alludes to this? **"Also, that the soul without knowledge is not good"** (Proverbs 19:2). If intercourse takes place without the woman's knowledge, i.e., consent, the soul of the offspring will not be good.

Rav Ika bar Chanina locates the proof text in the rest of the same verse: *gam belo daat nefesh lo tov* — also without knowledge the soul is not good. When the act of relations occurs without the wife's *daat* — not mere awareness, but genuine assent and acquiescence — the souls that result will not be good.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״גַּם בְּלֹא דַעַת נֶפֶשׁ לֹא טוֹב״ — זֶה הַכּוֹפֶה אִשְׁתּוֹ לִדְבַר מִצְוָה. ״וְאָץ בְּרַגְלַיִם חוֹטֵא״ — זֶה הַבּוֹעֵל וְשׁוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: **"Also, without knowledge the soul is not good"; this is one who forces his wife to** perform **the** conjugal **mitzva. "And he who hastens with his feet sins"; this is one who has intercourse** with his wife **and repeats** the act in a manner that causes her pain or distress.

A *baraita* confirms this: *ze hakofeh et ishto ledvar mitzvah* — this refers to one who compels his wife. But the *baraita* continues: *va-atz beraglayim chotei — ze haboel veshoneh* — one who hastens is also the man who has relations and then, after completing the act, immediately has relations a second time. The assumption is that after the first act the wife is satisfied and no longer interested, and therefore pressing for a second act resembles the same form of imposition.

### Woman Demands Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: הָרוֹצֶה לַעֲשׂוֹת כׇּל בָּנָיו זְכָרִים יִבְעוֹל וְיִשְׁנֶה! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — לְדַעַת, כָּאן — שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara is surprised by this teaching: **Is that so? But didn't Rava say: One who wants all his children to be males** should **have intercourse** with his wife **and repeat** the act? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult: Here,** where Rava issued this advice, he was referring to a husband who acts **with** his wife's **consent. There,** the *baraita* that condemns this behavior is referring to one who proceeds **without** her **consent.**

The *gemara* challenges this from Rava's ruling: *harotzeh laasot kol banav zecharim yivol veyishneh* — one who wishes all his children to be male should have relations and then have relations a second time. How can the second act be simultaneously condemned and recommended?

The *gemara* resolves: *lo kashya — kan ledaat kan shelo ledaat* — the two rulings speak to different situations. The second act is condemned when the wife is no longer interested and the husband persists anyway; it is commended when the wife in fact desires to continue. Rashi illuminates the physiological reasoning embedded in the ruling: the first act of relations arouses the wife, who may not have been fully aroused at the outset, and it is at this point that she desires the second encounter. When a second act occurs with her full desire — *bishaat ta'avah* — she gives forth seed first (*hi mazra'at techilah*), which the *gemara* elsewhere associates with the conception of male children.

### Second Intercourse

The *gemara* offers a teaching on this matter. When a woman participates enthusiastically, she is the one who gives forth seed first, and this leads to male offspring. The assumption embedded in this teaching reflects ancient biological theory (which we would now understand differently), but what matters for our purposes is the practical implication: the husband's performance depends on the wife's participation and arousal. A husband who proceeds according to his own desires alone, without attending to his wife's experience, risks not achieving the very outcome the tradition prizes.

This leads to a broader observation. The rabbinic teaching that a male child results when the woman achieves arousal and seed before the man is a principle that, whether or not one accepts its medical premises, creates a practical imperative for the husband to attend to his wife's arousal. A man who proceeds entirely according to his own timeline risks not satisfying the conditions for the outcome the tradition prizes. Whether the *gemara* consciously intended this as an endorsement of the wife's pleasure or arrived at it incidentally through a different set of concerns, the practical effect is to orient marital intimacy toward the wife's experience in a way that is quite striking in its context.

Rashi's reading makes explicit what the *gemara* implies: after the first act, *shemachamat biah rishonah nitavah ha-ishah*, it is the first act that awakens the woman's desire, and it is at this point that the second act, if she wants it, fulfills the conditions for male offspring. This framing, Rivka notes, carries a sharp inverse risk: if a daughter is born, the logic of the teaching implies that the mother desired relations and the father held back, which can produce a deeply harmful stigma attached to the birth of a daughter. The *gemara*'s teaching does not resolve this tension.

## Eve's Curses (II)

### Seven Curses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: עֶשֶׂר קְלָלוֹת נִתְקַלְּלָה חַוָּה, דִּכְתִיב:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **Is that so?** Is it proper for a woman to demand her conjugal rights from her husband? **But didn't Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi say: Eve was cursed with ten curses,** due to the sin of the Tree of Knowledge, as **it is written:** "To the woman He said, I will greatly multiply your pain and your travail; in sorrow you shall bring forth children; and yet your desire shall be to your husband, and he shall rule over you" (Genesis 3:16)?

The teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — that a woman who initiates and demands sex produces exceptional children — seems to be in tension with what follows. The *gemara* introduces a counterpoint: Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi teaches that Chava received ten curses at the time of the sin in the garden, derived from the verse *el ha'ishah amar* — to the woman He said.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶל הָאִשָּׁה אָמַר הַרְבָּה אַרְבֶּה״, אֵלּוּ שְׁנֵי טִפֵּי דָמִים — אַחַת דַּם נִדָּה, וְאַחַת דַּם בְּתוּלִים. ״עִצְּבוֹנֵךְ״, זֶה צַעַר גִּידּוּל בָּנִים. ״וְהֵרוֹנֵךְ״, זֶה צַעַר הָעִיבּוּר. ״בְּעֶצֶב תֵּלְדִי בָּנִים״, כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi **proceeds to** explain this verse. **"To the woman He said: I will greatly multiply [*harba arbe*]"; these are** the **two drops of blood** unique to a woman, which cause her suffering, **one the blood of menstruation and** the other **one the blood of virginity. "Your pain"; this is the pain of raising children. "And your travail"; this is the pain of pregnancy. "In sorrow you shall bring forth children"; in accordance with its** plain **meaning,** i.e., the pain of childbirth.

The curses are enumerated as follows. *Harbeh arbeh* — I will greatly increase — is taken as a doubled expression, indicating two separate curses: *dam niddah*, the blood of menstruation, and *dam besulim*, the blood of the hymen. These are two forms of bleeding associated specifically with the female body. *Itzvonech* — your anguish — refers to *tza'ar gidul banim*, the anguish of raising children. *V'heronech* — your pregnancy — refers to *tza'ar ha'ibur*, the suffering of pregnancy itself. *Be'etzev teldi vanim* — in pain shall you give birth to children — is taken *k'mashma'o*, at face value, as the pain of childbirth. That accounts for five.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶל אִישֵׁךְ תְּשׁוּקָתֵךְ״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה מִשְׁתּוֹקֶקֶת עַל בַּעְלָהּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁיּוֹצֵא לַדֶּרֶךְ. ״וְהוּא יִמְשׇׁל בָּךְ״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה תּוֹבַעַת בַּלֵּב וְהָאִישׁ תּוֹבֵעַ בַּפֶּה. זוֹ הִיא מִדָּה טוֹבָה בַּנָּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **"And yet your desire shall be to your husband" teaches that the woman desires her husband,** e.g., **when he sets out on the road; "and he shall rule over you" teaches that the woman demands** her husband **in** her **heart** but is too shy to voice her desire, **but the man demands** his wife **verbally.** Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi adds: **This is a good trait in women,** that they refrain from formulating their desire verbally.

*V'el ishech teshukasech* — to your husband shall be your longing — teaches that a woman longs for her husband precisely *b'sha'ah shehu yotzei laderech*, when he goes away on a journey. The curse is not that she desires him in his presence, but that she feels his absence acutely when he is gone and she cannot fulfill that longing. *V'hu yimshol bach* — and he shall have dominion over you — teaches that *ha'ishah tova'as balev v'ha'ish tove'a bapeh*: when a woman wants to have relations, she keeps that desire within her heart and cannot bring herself to express it openly, while the man is able to voice his desire without inhibition. He controls the verbal expression of their sexual dynamic. These seven constitute the initial enumeration.

### Three More Curses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי שֶׁבַע הָוְויָן! כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: עֲטוּפָה כְּאָבֵל, וּמְנוּדָּה מִכׇּל אָדָם, וַחֲבוּשָׁה בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the above statement with regard to Eve's ten curses: Are they in fact ten? **They are** only **seven. When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that the other curses are: A woman is **wrapped like a mourner,** i.e., she must cover her head; and she is **ostracized from all people and incarcerated within a prison,** as she typically spends all her time in the house.

When Rav Dimi arrived from the Land of Israel, he added three further curses. *Atufah ke-avel* — she wraps herself like a mourner — refers to a woman covering her hair, as if in a state of *avel*, mourning. It is striking that the *gemara* here frames the requirement of hair-covering explicitly as a curse, not as an expression of modesty or sanctity. *Umenudah mikol adam* — she is set apart from all people — means that she is forbidden to two men simultaneously: she can be married to only one husband, while he may take multiple wives. This asymmetry in the exclusivity of marriage is identified as a curse. *Vachavushah be-veit ha-assurim* — she is imprisoned — refers to *kol kevudah bat melech pnimah*, the woman's place being inside the home, away from the public sphere.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מְנוּדָּה מִכׇּל אָדָם? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲסִיר לַהּ יִיחוּד, אִיהוּ נָמֵי אֲסִיר לֵיהּ יִיחוּד. אֶלָּא דַּאֲסִירָא לְבֵי תְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the meaning of ostracized from all people? If you say** this is **because it is forbidden for her to seclude herself** with a man, **it is also forbidden** for a man **to seclude himself** with women. **Rather,** it means **that it is forbidden** for her **to** marry **two** men, whereas a man can marry two women.

Notably, the language the *gemara* uses for these conditions is the language of curse, confinement, and mourning — not the language of virtue or ideal. With a single exception to be addressed shortly, the *gemara* does not present these conditions as praiseworthy; it presents them as aspects of an existence marked by loss and constraint.

### Woman's Modesty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: מְגַדֶּלֶת שֵׂעָר כְּלִילִית, וְיוֹשֶׁבֶת וּמַשְׁתֶּנֶת מַיִם כִּבְהֵמָה, וְנַעֲשֵׂית כַּר לְבַעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a *baraita*** that the three additional curses are: **She grows** her **hair** long **like Lilit,** a demon; **she sits and urinates, like an animal; and serves as a pillow for her husband** during relations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? הָנֵי שֶׁבַח הוּא לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** why doesn't **the other** Sage include these curses? The Gemara answers: He maintains that **these are praise for her,** not pain, either because they are modest practices, e.g., urinating in a seated position, or because they add to her comfort, e.g., her bottom position during relations.

A *baraita* teaches that the three curses added by Rav Dimi are not the right candidates for the additional three; other elements should replace them, and moreover those elements are in fact not curses at all but positive attributes. *Hani shvach haviyan* — those are actually praiseworthy things.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״מַלְּפֵנוּ מִבַּהֲמוֹת אָרֶץ וּמֵעוֹף הַשָּׁמַיִם יְחַכְּמֵנוּ״. ״מַלְּפֵנוּ מִבַּהֲמוֹת״ — זוֹ פְּרֵידָה, שֶׁכּוֹרַעַת וּמַשְׁתֶּנֶת מַיִם. ״וּמֵעוֹף הַשָּׁמַיִם יְחַכְּמֵנוּ״ — זֶה תַּרְנְגוֹל, שֶׁמְּפַיֵּיס וְאַחַר כָּךְ בּוֹעֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Ḥiyya said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "Who teaches us by the beasts of the earth, and makes us wiser by the birds of the sky"** (Job 35:11)? He explains: **"Who teaches us by the beasts of the earth"; this is the female mule, which crouches and urinates** and from which we learn modesty. **"And makes us wiser by the birds of the sky"; this is the rooster, which** first **cajoles** the hen **and then mates** with it.

Rabbi Chiyya teaches, drawing on the verse *malefenu mi-behemot aretz, ume'of hashamayim yachakmenu* — the earth's animals teach us, and the birds of the sky make us wise — that we learn proper conduct by observing animals. The things attributed in this alternative *baraita* to women — growing long hair, urinating in a seated position, and lying beneath her husband during relations — are each aligned with animal behavior and reinterpreted as dignified or modest. *Malefenu mi-behemot aretz — zu predah*, a donkey crouches when urinating. For a woman to urinate in this fashion is therefore more modest: the genitals are less exposed and the act is less visible. *Ume'of hashamayim yachakmenu — ze tarnigol*, this is the rooster, *she-mifayes achar boel*, who appeases his mate and then has relations. The rooster's courtship behavior, approached with tenderness and prior attention to the hen's disposition, is held up as a model.

### Rabbi Yochanan and the Rooster

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִילְמָלֵא לֹא נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה, הָיִינוּ לְמֵידִין צְנִיעוּת מֵחָתוּל, וְגָזֵל מִנְּמָלָה, וַעֲרָיוֹת מִיּוֹנָה. דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ מִתַּרְנְגוֹל — שֶׁמְּפַיֵּיס וְאַחַר כָּךְ בּוֹעֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even if the Torah had not been given, we would** nonetheless **have learned modesty from the cat,** which covers its excrement, **and** that **stealing** is objectionable **from the ant,** which does not take grain from another ant, **and forbidden relations from the dove,** which is faithful to its partner, **and proper relations from the rooster, which** first **appeases** the hen **and then mates** with it.

Rabbi Yochanan adds the famous teaching: *ilmalei lo nitnah Torah, hayinu lemadim tzniut me-chatul* — had the Torah not been given, we would have learned modesty from the cat who covers its excrement, *gezel mi-nemalah* — the prohibition of theft from the ant who toils in summer and stores for winter, *arayot mi-yonah* — the prohibition of forbidden sexual relations from the dove, who mates faithfully with only one partner, *ve-derech eretz mi-tarnigol* — and proper conduct in relations from the rooster, *she-mifayes ve'achar kach boel*, who first appeases and only then mates.

### The Rooster's Courtship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי מְפַיֵּיס לַהּ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, הָכִי קָאָמַר לַהּ: זָבֵינְנָא לִיךְ זִיגָא דְּמָטוּ לִיךְ עַד כַּרְעָיךְ, לְבָתַר הָכִי אָמַר לַהּ: לִישְׁמַטְתֵּיהּ לְכַרְבַּלְתֵּיהּ דְּהָהוּא תַּרְנְגוֹלָא אִי אִית לֵיהּ וְלָא זָבֵינְנָא לִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **What does** the rooster do to **appease** the hen? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** Prior to mating, it spreads its wings as if to **say this: I will buy you a coat that will reach** down to **your feet. After** mating, the rooster bends its head as if to **say this: May the crest of this rooster fall off if he has** the wherewithal **and does not buy you one.** I simply have no money to do so.

The *gemara* then gives a richly symbolic account of what exactly the rooster communicates through his courtship display. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: the rooster flaps his wings around the hen, and this is his way of saying, *zavinna lach ziga dematu lach ad karayich* — I will buy you a garment that will reach to your feet. After relations, he hangs his head as if ashamed, and this gesture means: *lishmatay seya lekarbala*, let my comb be removed — that is, if I have the money and did not buy you that garment, I deserve to be disgraced. And then he adds the poignant qualification: *i is li velo zavinna lach* — if I had it I would certainly have bought it for you; he acknowledges that he has made a promise he cannot quite keep, but at least he tried.

This aggadic passage closes the daf on a note that ties together several threads. The concern throughout — about forcing one's wife, about the second act of relations, about who initiates, about the curses and conditions of women's lives — finds a kind of culmination in the image of the rooster who appeases before acting. And crucially, the *gemara* throughout this section, particularly in the enumeration of Chava's curses, does something that is far from obvious: it surfaces these inequities, names them openly, and labels them as curses — as departures from an ideal, not as ideal conditions themselves. The one exception, that a woman's not verbally demanding sex is a *middah tovah*, is carefully carved out, and even that exception is immediately qualified. The overall posture of the *gemara* here, for all its complexity and internal tension, is one of acknowledging that the conditions of women's lives represent a diminishment — something to be recognized and, perhaps, worked against, not celebrated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמְרִינַן, דְּמַרְצְיָא אַרְצוֹיֵי קַמֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When we say** that a woman who demands her conjugal rights from her husband is praiseworthy, it does not mean she should voice her desires explicitly. Rather, it means **that she** should **make herself pleasing to him,** and he will understand what she wants on his own.

And we arrive back at the insight that resolves the tension between different positions in the *gemara*: when we say a woman should demand conjugal relations, we do not mean she should forcefully say "you will come to me" in explicit verbal form. Rather, we mean she should make her wishes known through gesture, approach, and non-verbal signaling — the equivalent of how the hen responds to the rooster's courtship, or how she reveals her desire through her comportment and presence. In this way, the *gemara* preserves both the principle that female participation and initiative matter — that children born from relations desired by both partners are superior — while also acknowledging the reality that a woman's public modesty and restraint in verbal expression are considered virtues within this culture.
