# Sukkah 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sukkah Tachat Sukkah

### Source & Derivation

The second part of the *mishna* on 9b addresses the case of a *sukkah tachat sukkah* — a sukkah beneath another sukkah. Even if both layers of *skhakh* are individually valid, a special invalidity applies to sitting in the lower one. The *gemara* derives this from the *tanna'im*: the verse states *bassukkot tishvu shivat yamim* — you shall dwell in *sukkot* for seven days. The *baraita* extends the prohibition: not in a *sukkah* beneath another *sukkah*, not beneath a tree, and not beneath a house. The latter two are intuitive, since an invalid roof stands above a valid *skhakh* and undermines its identity as a roof. But the case of *sukkah tachat sukkah* is puzzling — why not simply use the shade of the upper roof?

The *gemara* raises a counterargument: *bassukkot* is written in the plural, and *tartei mashma* — the plural form implies two, suggesting that dwelling beneath two *sukkot* might be specifically endorsed. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this by invoking the principle that *bassukkat ketiv* — the word is written without a *vav*, in the singular form. As the *gemara* earlier discussed in the context of walls, the written form (*ketiv*) can govern even when the vocalized reading (*krei*) differs. The textual basis may be somewhat strained, but it establishes the principle: *sukkah tachat sukkah* is invalid.

### Four Scenarios

Rabbi Yirmiya then presents a systematic analysis. He observes that the relationship between two stacked *skhakh* structures yields four distinct combinations: both may be valid (*kasher*), both may be invalid (*pasul*), the bottom may be valid and the top invalid, or the top may be valid and the bottom invalid. Each of these cases is not only theoretically possible but practically constructible.

### Both Valid

The first case — *pe'amim sheshteihem kesherot*, both are valid — is achieved as follows: the lower *skhakh* has more sunlight than shade (*chamata meruba mitzilata*), meaning it lacks sufficient coverage to constitute valid *skhakh* on its own. The upper *skhakh*, however, has more shade than sunlight (*tzilata meruba mechamata*), and the upper structure stands within twenty *amot* of its own floor. In this case, the lower structure does not truly function as a distinct *sukkah* — it lacks adequate *skhakh*. The actual roofing is provided solely by the upper layer, and since the upper *skhakh* is fully valid and properly sized, both are considered valid. The apparent doubling is not really a doubling at all; there is functionally only one *skhakh*.

### Both Invalid

The second case — *pe'amim sheshteihem pesulot*, both are invalid — is achieved when both layers have more shade than sunlight, but the upper structure stands higher than twenty *amot*. The upper *skhakh* is thus invalid due to excessive height, a disqualification established at the very opening of the *masechet*. The lower *skhakh*, however, is not rescued by the upper one's invalidity. Whether the *skhakh* above is *kasher* or *pasul*, the lower remains invalid by virtue of standing beneath a second layer of *skhakh* — the prohibition of *sukkah tachat sukkah* applies regardless.

### Bottom Valid

The third case — the bottom valid and the top invalid — is the one Rabbi Yirmiya identifies as the true *chiddush*. The bottom has more shade than sunlight and is within twenty *amot*, making it a fully valid *sukkah*. The top has more sunlight than shade, and therefore lacks sufficient *skhakh* to constitute a second roof. Since the upper layer does not qualify as *skhakh*, it cannot impose the invalidity of *sukkah tachat sukkah* on what lies beneath it.

This prompts a concern expressed in the *gemara*: *nigzor dilma mitztaref sechach pasul bahadi sechach kasher* — perhaps we should be worried that invalid *skhakh* might combine with valid *skhakh* below. Rashi explains the concern this way: while in the present case the upper layer is merely insufficient rather than inherently disqualified, if we permit such configurations broadly, we might come to permit a case where the upper *skhakh* is invalid because it exceeds twenty *amot*. *Skhakh* that is invalid due to excessive height carries its own disqualifying force — analogous to the overhang of a tree — and could then corrupt the valid *skhakh* directly beneath it. The *gemara* therefore teaches that this concern does not materialize: a sparse upper layer that lacks sufficient coverage is simply not *skhakh* at all, and creates no problem for what lies below.

An interesting question arises at the margins of this case: if neither the upper nor the lower layer individually has sufficient shade, could the two sparse layers combine to produce adequate coverage? The *gemara* does not address this directly here, but it is a genuine theoretical question.

### Top Valid

The fourth case — *pe'amim sheha'elyona keshera vetachtona pesula*, the top valid and the bottom invalid — is the paradigmatic *sukkah tachat sukkah*. Both layers have more shade than sunlight, and the upper one stands within twenty *amot*. The upper is fully valid; the lower is invalid precisely because it stands beneath a legitimate second *skhakh*. The *gemara* notes that this case is obvious (*peshita*) — it requires no derivation. The genuine novelty lies in the third case, where the bottom remains valid despite the presence of a structure above it, because the upper layer lacks the threshold of coverage required to qualify as *skhakh*.

## Gap Between Skhakhim

### Rav Huna (1 tefach)

Having established the principle of *sukkah tachat sukkah*, the *gemara* asks: *kama yehei bein sukkah lesukkah vetehei tachtona pesula* — what gap between the two layers of *skhakh* is required to render the lower one invalid? The intuitive answer might be ten *tefachim*, since that is the minimum height of a *sukkah*. The earlier opinions, however, do not go that far.

Rav Huna says: *tefach* — a single *tefach* gap suffices. The logic is not that the upper structure must qualify as a *sukkah*; it is enough that it constitute a distinct roof, a *skhakh* in its own right. Once a separate canopy is identifiable, it can impose the prohibition of *sukkah tachat sukkah* on what lies below, even if it would never qualify as an independent *sukkah*.

### Tumah Analogy

The support for the one-*tefach* standard comes from the laws of *ohel tumah*. A corpse imparts *tumah* to anything beneath the same roof, while the roof itself serves as a barrier preventing the *tumah* from spreading upward. But how high must a covering stand above the ground to function as a roof in this sense? The *mishna* in the relevant tractate states: *tefach al tefach be-rum tefach* — a surface of one *tefach* by one *tefach* in area, elevated one *tefach* above the ground, *mevi et ha-tumah* — causes *tumah* to spread beneath it — *ve-chotzetz bifnei ha-tumah* — and blocks the *tumah* from above. If the height is less than one *tefach*, it neither conveys nor blocks.

The operative principle is clear: a *tefach* of clearance is what defines something as a roof in the domain of *tumah* law. By analogy, Rav Huna argues, a *tefach* of clearance is what defines a second layer as a distinct *skhakh* in the domain of *sukkah* law.

### Rav Chisda (4 tefachim)

Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar Rav Huna disagree, requiring a gap of four *tefachim*. Their reasoning: *she-lo matzinu makom chashuv be-makom pachot me-arba'ah* — we do not find a legally significant space in an area smaller than four *tefachim*. The most familiar example is the *reshut ha-yachid* of *Shabbat*, where a minimum area of four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* is required for a space to constitute a private domain. Below that threshold, the space lacks legal significance.

*Tosafot* acknowledges the difficulty with this position: the four-*tefachim* standard normally governs horizontal area, not vertical height. The precedent for four *tefachim* as a significant height measure is rare, making this an unusual application of the principle. Nevertheless, Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar Rav Huna found one *tefach* insufficient while stopping short of the ten-*tefachim* threshold that the next opinion will require.

### Shmuel (10 tefachim)

Shmuel requires ten *tefachim*: *kehachshara kach pesula* — as with its validity, so with its invalidity. Just as a *sukkah* requires a minimum height of ten *tefachim* to be valid, the upper structure must stand ten *tefachim* above the lower *skhakh* to impose the prohibition of *sukkah tachat sukkah*. Shmuel's reasoning reframes the entire question: this is not merely about whether the upper layer constitutes a distinct *skhakh*, but about whether it constitutes a distinct *sukkah*. A *skhakh* under another *skhakh* is only prohibited when the upper one rises to the status of a separate *sukkah*. Anything less than ten *tefachim* remains, in Shmuel's view, categorically beneath that threshold.

## Habitability Requirement

### Rav Dimi Report

The *gemara* now engages the view of Rabbi Yehuda from the *mishna*, who states: *im ein diyur ba'elyona, ha-tachtona keshera* — if nobody dwells in the upper one, the lower one is valid. The *gemara* first considers whether this means actual occupancy — and dismisses the reading immediately: *atu diyur kagarmi* — does the physical fact of whether people happen to be using the space determine its halachic identity? A structure does not lose its status as a *sukkah* simply because it is unoccupied at the moment. The *gemara* therefore reinterprets Rabbi Yehuda: *kol she'aino reuia ledira* — the upper structure must be unfit for habitation. And the primary case of uninhabitability is that it stands less than ten *tefachim* high. This brings Rabbi Yehuda into alignment with Shmuel's position: the upper structure must have the dimensions of a real *sukkah* to impose invalidity on the one below.

When Rav Dimi returned from Eretz Yisrael, he reported a further clarification of Rabbi Yehuda's view: *im ein ha-tachtona yekholah lekabbel karim ukesatot shel elyona, ha-tachtona keshera* — if the lower structure cannot support the pillows and blankets of the upper one, the lower one is valid. This introduces an additional consideration beyond dimensional thresholds. Even if the upper structure technically has the correct parameters for a *sukkah*, if its floor cannot structurally bear weight — if anyone who tried to walk in would cause the floor to collapse — it lacks the practical habitability that makes it a real *sukkah*.

### Floor Collapse

The underlying image is vivid: a *sukkah* built upon the *skhakh* of another *sukkah* has no proper floor. The surface beneath your feet is simply *skhakh* material — branches and reeds that could give way under any significant weight. Rabbi Yehuda holds that such structural inadequacy means the upper structure never truly qualifies as a *sukkah*, and therefore cannot impose the prohibition of *sukkah tachat sukkah*. The *gemara* notes that the condition is framed in terms of pillows and blankets rather than a person — Rashi adds that the full phrase, drawn from his commentary, includes sleeping on those pillows and blankets, so the weight of a reclining person is implicitly part of the test. The formulation nonetheless sounds understated compared to the actual structural question of whether a person can stand in the space.

### Emergency Habitability

The *gemara* resolves the apparent dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda through a fine distinction: *ikha beinaihu deyekhola lekabbel al yedei ha-dechak* — the point of disagreement is a case where the floor can support weight, but only precariously, only under emergency conditions. According to Shmuel's reading of the *tanna kamma*, even precarious habitability suffices — if it can bear weight at a stretch, the upper structure qualifies as a *sukkah* and invalidates the lower one. Rabbi Yehuda, as interpreted by Rav Dimi, requires stable, genuine habitability. The disagreement is thus remarkably narrow: both opinions agree that ten *tefachim* of height is necessary, and both agree that the structure must be capable of bearing a person's weight. They differ only on whether barely adequate support — the structural equivalent of an emergency measure — is enough to confer full *sukkah* status on the upper level.

## Sheets & Decorations

### Mishna Cases

The *mishna* presents three cases of a sheet invalidating a *sukkah*: spreading a sheet over the *skhakh* because of the heat (*mipnei hachama*), spreading one beneath the *skhakh* to catch falling debris (*mipnei ha-nesher* — the pine needles or other small material falling from above), and spreading a sheet over the poles of a four-poster bed (*al gabei ha-kinof*) inside the *sukkah*. All three are declared *pesulah*.

The underlying logic unifies the cases. A *sukkah* under a tree is invalid because the tree branches serve as a distinct roof above the *skhakh*. A *sukkah* under a house is clearly invalid for the same reason. When a sheet is placed above the *skhakh*, the sheet becomes the functional roof, displacing the *skhakh*. When placed below, it introduces an interposing layer beneath the valid *skhakh*, so that the person sitting inside is effectively under the sheet rather than the *skhakh*. And when spread over the four-poster bed inside the *sukkah*, it creates a private canopy — the occupant is sheltered not by the *skhakh* above but by the fabric ceiling directly overhead.

### Rav Chisda

Rav Chisda introduces a critical limitation: the *mishna*'s ruling applies only when the sheet is spread to catch falling debris. A sheet spread for *lena'ota* — to beautify the *sukkah* — leaves the *sukkah* valid. This is a striking ruling. A sheet hung beneath the entire expanse of the *skhakh*, from wall to wall, would entirely block any direct visual or physical contact between the occupant and the *skhakh* above. And yet, because it is there to beautify and not to serve as a roof, it is valid.

### Function vs. Shade

The *gemara* responds to Rav Chisda's ruling with *peshita* — this should be obvious, since the *mishna* specifically cites catching debris as the problematic case, implying that beautification is not. The *gemara* then defends why the ruling is nonetheless necessary to state: one might have argued that the *mishna*'s choice of example was merely *orchah de-milta* — describing the typical scenario — without intending to exclude beautification. Rav Chisda therefore teaches that the distinction is genuine: purpose is halachically decisive.

This is conceptually profound and connects to a principle established at the very beginning of the *masechet*. The *skhakh* does not need to functionally provide shade to the occupant in practice — it must have the capacity to provide shade. The *gemara* earlier noted that a *sukkah* placed between two mountains that naturally blocks all sunlight is valid; the *skhakh* is never actually doing the shading, yet the *sukkah* is entirely *kasher*. Similarly, a *sukkah* whose *skhakh* is rendered practically inoperative by a decorative sheet beneath it remains valid, because the sheet is not competing with the *skhakh* — it is ancillary to it, *tafel* to the primary element. Once it is there purely to beautify, it is considered part of the *sukkah* rather than a distinct second roof.

One might ask: is the dividing line between valid and invalid sheet placement specifically about *noy sukkah*, or more broadly about any non-roofing purpose? The *mishna* specifies two problematic purposes — blocking sunlight and catching debris — both of which are paradigmatic roofing functions. What if a sheet were spread inside the *sukkah* for some other reason, neither beautification nor protection? The *gemara* does not resolve this explicitly here. A later passage will shed some light, but for the present, the operative principle is that when a sheet clearly serves to beautify the *sukkah*, it is considered subordinate to the *skhakh* and does not invalidate.

### Baraita

The *gemara* attempts to bring a proof for the permissibility of decorative sheets from a *tosefta*: *sikhakha kehilkhata* — if one placed valid *skhakh* on the *sukkah* and then adorned it, *veitrah*, crowned it with embroidered sheets, suspended nuts, pomegranates, apricots, clusters of grapes, wreaths of grain, and containers of wine, oil, and flour — *assur lehishtapek mehen* — it is forbidden to derive benefit from any of these items until after *Shemini Atzeret*. The reasoning is *huktza lemitzvato* — they have been designated for the mitzvah and cannot be casually removed and used. Even during *Shemini Atzeret* itself, once the threshold of *bein ha-shemashot* between the last day of *Sukkot* and *Shemini Atzeret* has passed, the prohibition persists throughout. If, however, one stipulated at the outset that the decorations remain subject to his ongoing use, the stipulation is effective — *hakol lefi tenao*.

The *gemara* notes, however, that this *tosefta* does not prove that sheets hung beneath the *skhakh* are permitted: *dilma min ha-tzad* — perhaps the embroidered sheets were hung on the sides as wall decorations rather than suspended beneath the *skhakh*. The proof therefore does not stand.

### No Effect on Size

A distinct ruling is then stated: *noy sukkah ein mema'atin ba-sukkah* — decorations do not reduce the minimum dimensions of a *sukkah*. This applies in two directions. If a sheet is hung beneath the *skhakh*, the measurement of the required ten *tefachim* of height is taken from the ground to the actual *skhakh*, not to the sheet. Similarly, if decorations line the interior walls, the question of whether the *sukkah* meets the minimum floor area of seven *tefachim* by seven *tefachim* is measured from wall to wall, not from decoration to decoration. The decorations are halachically transparent for the purposes of measurement.

This is noteworthy because structurally it makes sense — one evaluates the architectural dimensions of the *sukkah*, not its interior furnishings. But there is a functional puzzle: the earlier *gemara* discussed cases where *skhakh* material or strings hanging down into the required height of the *sukkah* create problems. Why should decorations be treated differently?

### Wall Decorations

Rav Ashi introduces a distinction: *umin ha-tzad mema'atin* — while decorations suspended from the *skhakh* do not reduce the *sukkah*'s dimensions, decorations hanging from the walls may reduce the minimum usable space. Rashi explains the reason: *d'ayno mahazeket rosho ve-rubbo ve-shulchano* — the halachic minimum size of a *sukkah* is defined by the ability to seat a person with his head, the majority of his body, and his table. If wall hangings physically intrude into that space and make it practically uninhabitable, the *sukkah* no longer meets its minimum dimensions in functional terms, even if the bare walls are far enough apart. This applies from the walls inward, however, and not from the *skhakh* downward — a distinction that remains somewhat puzzling but reflects the *gemara*'s understanding that the *skhakh* is the central element to which all else is subordinated.

A narrative illustrates the principle in practice. Manimei, the servant of Rav Ashi, had his cloak become soaked with water and spread it out on top of the *sukkah* to dry. Rav Ashi told him to take it down — *delo leimru kamesakhkheikh bedavar ha-mekabbel tumah* — lest people think he is using a garment, which is susceptible to *tumah*, as *skhakh*, which would be invalid. The *gemara* responds: but the cloak is wet — anyone would understand it is there to dry, not to serve as *skhakh*. Rav Ashi replies that his instruction is for when it dries. Once dry, a bystander would have no reason to know it was placed there to dry and might conclude it is being used as *skhakh*.

Implicit in this exchange is a question: when the cloak is wet and no one would mistake it for *skhakh*, is it permissible to leave it there? The *gemara* seems to assume so — which raises the question of whether a garment on top of the *sukkah* for a non-roofing purpose (here, drying) is analogous to a decorative sheet below the *skhakh*. The logic appears parallel: just as a sheet that is not serving a roofing function does not invalidate the *skhakh* beneath it, a wet garment placed to dry does not invalidate the *skhakh* below it. This extends the principle of *noy sukkah* somewhat: the exemption may not be limited strictly to beautification, but may cover any use that does not serve a roofing function.

## Separation Rule

### 4 Tefachim Rule

A further ruling is stated: *noy sukkah ha-muflag mimenah arba'ah* — if decorations (*noy sukkah*) hanging from the *skhakh* are separated from it by a gap of four *tefachim*, they may create an independent entity rather than remaining ancillary to the *skhakh*. This directly parallels the earlier discussion of how much space between two layers of *skhakh* is required to constitute a distinct second roof. Once a decoration hangs far enough below the *skhakh*, it can no longer be treated as subordinate to it — it begins to have its own halachic identity, and a person sitting directly beneath it may be positioned under a separate canopy rather than under the *skhakh* itself.

### Rav Nachman

Rav Nachman holds that even at four *tefachim* of separation, the decoration is still valid — it remains *noy sukkah* and does not invalidate the *sukkah*. Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar bar Chana disagree, ruling that once the decoration is four *tefachim* from the *skhakh*, it constitutes a distinct roof and the *sukkah* is *pesulah* for one sitting directly beneath it. This is a practically significant dispute, as it is common for *noy sukkah* — hanging fruits, garlands, decorative fabrics — to descend some distance below the *skhakh*, and a person seated beneath them may wonder whether their position is halachically problematic.

### Exilarch Story

Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar bar Chana, who ruled that four *tefachim* of separation renders the *sukkah* invalid, happened to visit the house of the *reish galuta* — the political head of the Jewish community in exile — during *Sukkot*. The in-house halachic authority at the *reish galuta*'s court was Rav Nachman, who held the position of *av beit din* there and who ruled that four *tefachim* of separation poses no problem. Rav Nachman made a point of lodging the two visiting rabbis in a *sukkah* whose decorations were suspended four *tefachim* from the *skhakh* — clearly intending to force the issue, since sleeping in the *sukkah* is an obligation that cannot be easily declined. *Ve-lo amru lei ve-lo midi* — they remained silent and said nothing, sleeping in the *sukkah* without protest.

Rav Nachman took their silence as concession: *hadaru rabbanan mi-shemataikhu* — it seems the rabbis have retracted their position? They responded with a deft counter: *anan shluchei mitzvah anan u-feturin min ha-sukkah* — we are performing a *mitzvah* by visiting the *reish galuta*, and those engaged in a *mitzvah* are exempt from *sukkah*. Rashi notes that greeting the *reish galuta* itself qualifies as a *mitzvah* obligation. Their sleeping outside the bounds of their own ruling was not a retraction — it was an exemption.

## Bed Canopy

### Mishna Setup

The *mishna* presents two types of bed structures and their halachic status inside a *sukkah*. The *kinof* is a four-poster bed: four poles at the corners of the bed, over which a sheet is spread, creating a flat canopy above the sleeper. This configuration creates a genuine horizontal roof over the bed, and the *mishna* rules that spreading a sheet over such a structure — *piras al gabei ha-kinof* — renders the *sukkah* invalid. The *naklitin*, by contrast, are two poles at either end of the bed with a single horizontal bar connecting them at the top. When a sheet is draped over this bar, it falls on both sides and forms a triangular cross-section — a teepee. The *mishna* permits this arrangement: *aval piras al gabei naklitei ha-mita* — but if spread over the single-bar structure, it is valid.

The distinction is structural. The four-poster bed creates a flat roof over the person lying beneath it — there is a genuine horizontal surface of fabric above the sleeper, creating a distinct interior space with its own ceiling. The teepee has no horizontal surface at its apex — it comes to a point or to a narrow bar, and the sloping sides, while providing total coverage in practice, are analogous to leaning walls rather than a roof. The *gemara* earlier discussed the case of a *sukkah* built between two mountains: even if the mountains' proximity means sunlight never reaches the *skhakh* from below, the *sukkah* is valid because the shade is provided by the leaning sides, not by a separate roof. Similarly, the teepee's coverage comes from its sloping fabric, not from a horizontal ceiling, and therefore does not constitute an independent canopy.

### Shmuel Position

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel then introduces a significant qualification. *Mutar lishon be-khilah ba-sukkah af al pi she-yesh lah gag, ve-hu she-einah gevohah asarah* — it is permitted to sleep under a bed canopy (*kilah*) inside a *sukkah*, even if the canopy has a genuine horizontal roof, provided that the canopy does not stand ten *tefachim* off the ground. The parallel to the earlier discussion of *sukkah tachat sukkah* is direct: just as the upper structure in that context must stand ten *tefachim* high to constitute a distinct *sukkah* and impose invalidity on the lower one, so too the canopy inside the *sukkah* must stand ten *tefachim* off the ground to be treated as an independent enclosed domain. Below ten *tefachim*, even a flat canopy is halachically equivalent to a blanket drawn over a person — it creates physical coverage but not a legally distinct *ohel*.

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* challenges this position from a *baraita*: *ha-yashen be-khilah ba-sukkah lo yatza yedei chovato* — one who sleeps under a canopy in a *sukkah* has not fulfilled his obligation. Rav Yehuda answers: *hacha be-mai askinan she-gevohah asarah* — that ruling refers specifically to a canopy standing ten *tefachim* or more above the ground. A second challenge follows from a *mishna*: *ha-yashen tachat ha-mita ba-sukkah lo yatza yedei chovato* — one who sleeps beneath a bed in a *sukkah* has not fulfilled his obligation. This appears fatal to Shmuel's position, because a standard bed stands well below ten *tefachim* from the ground, and yet the *mishna* declares the person beneath it to have been derelict. The *gemara* responds on Shmuel's behalf: *ha targemah Shmuel be-mita gevohah asarah* — Shmuel himself applied this ruling to a bed that was ten *tefachim* off the ground, the underside of which therefore constituted a ceiling standing at the ten-*tefachim* threshold. This is admittedly a somewhat forced reading, but it is required by Shmuel's position.

The pattern is striking: the *gemara* is progressively limiting what the *mishna* declared invalid. The *mishna* states that a sheet on the *skhakh* is invalid — the *gemara* limits this to a sheet placed for protection rather than beautification. The *mishna* states that sleeping under a bed canopy is invalid — the *gemara* limits this to canopies standing ten *tefachim* off the ground. In both cases the *gemara* is pulling back substantially from the plain sense of the *mishna*.

### Baraita Poles

A further *baraita* distinguishes the *naklitin* from the *kinofot* precisely: *naklitin shenayim* — the permissible structure involves two poles, while *kinofot arba'ah* — the problematic structure involves four. The two-pole arrangement with a single bar produces the teepee shape, which is permitted; the four-pole arrangement produces a flat canopy, which is prohibited. The *baraita* is explaining the distinction drawn by the *mishna* between the two structures.

### Height Invalidates

The *baraita* then states a condition that stands in apparent tension with Shmuel's position: *uvilvad she-lo yehu naklitin gevohim min ha-mita asarah* — the teepee is valid only on condition that the poles are not ten *tefachim* above the bed. This ruling implies that height alone is sufficient to invalidate even the teepee structure. Even though the teepee has no horizontal roof and would seem not to constitute a distinct *ohel*, once it stands ten *tefachim* high it becomes problematic.

This is precisely the reverse of Shmuel's formulation. According to the *baraita*, the two criteria — having a flat horizontal roof, and standing ten *tefachim* high — operate independently. Either criterion alone is sufficient to invalidate: a flat canopy at any height is problematic, and even a teepee becomes problematic at ten *tefachim*. According to Shmuel, both criteria must be present simultaneously: a flat canopy that is lower than ten *tefachim* is valid, and by implication a teepee at ten *tefachim* would also be valid. The *gemara* leaves this tension unresolved at this point, to be addressed in the continuation of the sugya.
