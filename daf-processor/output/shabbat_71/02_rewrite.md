# Shabbat 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Forgettings Case

### Case Review Setup

The daf opens in the middle of a case that requires some recapitulation. The scenario involves a person who forgot it was *Shabbat* and performed two *melachot* — cutting (*ktzira*) and grinding (*tchina*). While still in that state, he somehow remembered that it was *Shabbat*, but without having realized that he had just sinned, and then immediately entered a second state of forgetting — this time forgetting that these same two *melachot* are prohibited — and performed them again. Viewed in isolation, each of these two states of forgetting would independently generate two *korbanot*. The person then remembered his sins, but sequentially: first he became aware of the first sin (the forgetting of *Shabbat*), and then of the second sin (the forgetting of the *melachot*).

In the first forgetting, the obligation crystallizes upon remembering, generating a *korban* that covers both *ktzira* and *tchina*. When the person then remembers the second sin — which likewise comprises both *ktzira* and *tchina* — the question becomes whether a new and separate *korban* is required.

### Why Two Offerings

The *gemara* concludes that in this case no additional *korban* is necessary, because the *ktzira* of the second sin is already subsumed within the obligation generated by the *ktzira* of the first, and similarly the *tchina* of the second is already subsumed within the first. The second obligation is effectively dragged along by the first.

### One vs Two Forgettings

This raises an important structural question. Ordinarily, we hold that two separate acts of forgetting — with a moment of awareness in between — generate two separate *korbanot* for the same sin. Even if a person forgets, then remembers, and then forgets again and repeats the exact same transgression, the interrupting awareness divides the two episodes into distinct obligations. Why, then, does the case here produce fewer than two *korbanot*, given that there are effectively two different states of forgetfulness at play?

Rashi addresses this directly. He explains that the present case is genuinely ambiguous. On one hand, there was no intervening moment of awareness between the two states of forgetting — the person never realized he had sinned. On the other hand, it is not a single uniform state of forgetting: the person was forgetting different things at different times, first the nature of the day and then the prohibited character of the *melachot*. This ambiguity — neither the clean break of two forgettings divided by awareness, nor the seamless unity of a single forgetting — is precisely what allows for the discussion. The *gemara*'s resolution, at least at this stage, is that once the first obligation is crystallized, it subsumes and drags along the second.

## Reverse Order Case

### Reverse Order Setup

The more complicated scenario arises when the person remembers in reverse order. Rather than becoming aware of sins in the sequence in which they were incurred, he first becomes aware of one component — the *ktzira* — of the second forgetting, then of the entire first forgetting (which includes both *ktzira* and *tchina*), and finally of the *tchina* component of the second forgetting. The question is how many *korbanot* this sequence of awarenesses obligates him to bring.

### Rava's Dragging

Rava articulates a principle of *gerera* — dragging. When the person first becomes aware of the *ktzira* of the second forgetting, that awareness generates an obligation in one *korban* for *ktzira*. When he then becomes aware of the entirety of the first forgetting — comprising both *ktzira* and *tchina* — the *ktzira* component of that first forgetting is already represented in the *korban* he is obligated to bring. The overlap, even if partial, is sufficient: *ktzira goreret ktzirah* — the *ktzira* obligation drags along the other *ktzira* obligation, including together with it the *tchina* that accompanies it. In Rava's formulation: *ktzirah goreret ktzirah vetachina she'imah* — the *ktzira* drags the *ktzira* and the *tchina* that accompanies it. However, when the person finally becomes aware of the *tchina* of the second forgetting, that *tchina* stands on its own — *bimkomo omedet* — and requires a separate *korban*, since it was not represented in the first obligation.

The net result according to Rava is that the person brings one *korban* for the initial *ktzira* awareness (which subsumes the combined first forgetting), and one additional *korban* for the final *tchina*. At the end of the day, each category — *ktzira* and *tchina* — receives at least one *korban*.

### Abaye's Position

Abaye extends the principle of *gerera* further. He holds that the *tchina* of the first forgetting itself drags along the *tchina* of the second forgetting — *tachina nami goreret tachina*. Since it is all considered one category of *tchina*, the single *korban* for *ktzira* covers all the various sins committed, even though no *korban* is ever specifically brought for the act of *tchina*. As Abaye puts it: *shum tachina achat hi* — it is all one act of *tchina*. According to Abaye, therefore, only one *korban* need be brought, and it covers everything — a striking conclusion, since nothing in that *korban* specifically represents the *tchina* at all.

## Three Olives Proof

### Rava Consistency

Having presented Rava's position, the *gemara* immediately questions whether Rava truly holds the principle of *gerera* in the manner described. The challenge comes from another statement attributed to Rava — *umi it lei leRava gerera?* — does Rava really subscribe to this dragging principle? A different ruling of Rava appears to be in tension with it.

### Three Olives Setup

The *gemara* introduces a new case: a person ate two *zeisim* (olives' worth) of *chelev* (forbidden fat), which carries the penalty of *karet* and requires a *korban chatat* when performed unwittingly. Both olives were eaten in a single state of forgetfulness — *behelem echad* — meaning that even if consumed on different occasions during the week, as long as the person never awoke to the realization of his sin in between, a single *korban* suffices. The minimum measure (*kizayit*) is the threshold for the obligation.

Now, before becoming aware of both sins, the person became aware of only the first olive. He still had no recollection of having eaten the second. In that state — aware of the first, unaware of the second — he went ahead and ate a third olive of *chelev*, this time in the state of forgetfulness associated with his unawareness of the second.

The case therefore has three olives. Olives one and two were eaten in state of forgetting A. Between the first and third eating, there was an intervening awareness of the first olive only. The third olive was eaten in the state of forgetting B (which is the same unawareness that applied to olive two). Then the person eventually becomes aware of both olives two and three.

### Middle Olive Solution

Rava's ruling is both precise and practically ingenious. If one brings the *korban* designated for the first olive, that atones for sins one and two — since both were eaten in a common state of forgetfulness — but not for sin three, which was in a different state. If one brings the *korban* for the third olive, that atones for sins three and two — since both the third and the second share the same state of forgetfulness (B) — but not for sin one. However, if one is astute enough to designate the *korban* for the middle olive — sin two — then all three are atoned for: *niskapru kulam*. Why? Because olive two shares state of forgetfulness A with olive one, so the *korban* for two sweeps in one; and olive two shares state of forgetfulness B with olive three, so the *korban* for two sweeps in three as well. The middle olive sits at the intersection of both states of forgetting, and a *korban* brought for it therefore reaches in both directions.

It is notable that the designation of the *korban* is operative here. The person coming before the *beit din* can be instructed to designate his *korban* for the middle olive, and that single designation achieves complete atonement. There is something striking about this — the obligation is not objectively fixed by the acts themselves; the person retains the power to define which act his *korban* primarily addresses, and the others are subsumed accordingly.

### Abaye Alternative

Abaye, consistent with his broader position, holds that even greater latitude is available: *afilu hevi korban al echad mehen, niskapru kulam* — even if one brings the *korban* for any one of the three, they are all atoned for. The reason lies in the extended chain of *gerera*: if one brings for olive three, that includes olive two; but since olive two is included, a *korban* for olive two would have included olive one; therefore, bringing for olive three ultimately drags in olive one as well through the intermediary of olive two. Conversely, bringing for olive one includes two, and two includes three. The chain of dragging, in Abaye's view, means that any single *korban* reaches all the way through.

As the *gemara* observes, this case is actually a milder form of *gerera* than what appeared in the *Shabbat* scenario, since here all three sins are of the same type — all are *chelev* — whereas in the *Shabbat* case, Abaye allowed a *korban* for *ktzira* to discharge an obligation for *tchina*, which has no representation at all in the *korban* being brought.

### Challenge to Rava

The *gemara* now presses its original challenge to Rava directly. If Rava does not accept the extended form of *gerera* that Abaye employs in the *Shabbat* case — where a *tchina* that has no representation in the first *korban* gets dragged along — then how can Rava in the three-olives case allow a *korban* for olive one to discharge the obligation for olive two? After all, this too involves a partial overlap rather than complete identity: the *korban* for olive one is brought in state A, while olive two also has a connection to state B, which is not at all represented in the *korban* for olive one. From the *gemara*'s perspective, the two cases are roughly analogous — in both, there is some overlap between the *korban* being brought and the sin being discharged, but in both there is also a component of the discharged sin that is not represented in the *korban*. How, then, does Rava permit *gerera* in the three-olives case but not in the *Shabbat* case?

## Rava Persuaded

### Rava's Reversal

The *gemara* resolves the tension by reporting that Rava changed his position after debating Abaye on the three-olives case: *batar deshameih me'Abaye savrah* — after he heard Abaye's reasoning, he was persuaded. He therefore conceded that some degree of *gerera* operates in the three-olives scenario. Were he to revisit the *Shabbat* case in light of his revised view, he would presumably accept that the *korban* for the combined first forgetting can include the parallel obligation from the second forgetting.

### One Dragging Rule

The *gemara* then presses further: once Rava has conceded the principle of *gerera*, why does he not go all the way with Abaye and say that the *tchina* of the first forgetting drags along the *tchina* of the second, such that a single *korban* covers everything?

The *gemara*'s answer is a precise limiting rule: *gerera it lei, gerera degerera leit lei* — Rava accepts one dragging, not a dragging of a dragging. The first *korban* can drag in an obligation that has some overlap with it, but it cannot drag in an obligation that is only connected through an intermediate step. The *tchina* of the second forgetting has no representation whatsoever in the original *korban* brought for *ktzira*. It is connected only because the *gerera* of the combined first-forgetting *korban* sweeps in the second forgetting's *ktzira*, and it is only through that swept-in *ktzira* that the *tchina* could theoretically be reached. That is two degrees removed — a dragging of a dragging — and that is too far for Rava.

Rashi sharpens this point slightly. He notes that the reason the *tchina* of the second forgetting cannot be discharged is not merely that it is two steps removed, but more specifically that the combined *korban* only drags in the second obligation because of its *ktzira* component. The *tchina* of that second obligation is being dragged along by virtue of its *ktzira* — it is a passenger on the *ktzira*'s inclusion. That *tchina* does not itself have the power to then extend the drag further to the *tchina* of another separate obligation. The *tchina* of the second forgetting is, in Rashi's formulation, entirely foreign to the original first *korban*, and therefore Rava refuses to extend the *gerera* to cover it.

## Rabbi Zeira Question

### Half-Melacha Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a question posed by Rabbi Zeira — something that Abaye and Rava both took as self-evident, but which Rabbi Zeira himself was uncertain about. The new scenario involves a single *melacha* performed in two halves, each half under a different mode of forgetting.

To illustrate with a clearer example: a person forgot it was *Shabbat* and wrote one letter — half of a complete act of writing, since writing a complete word (*shem*, for instance) requires two letters. He then remembered it was *Shabbat* but somehow forgot that writing is prohibited on *Shabbat*, and proceeded to write the second letter, completing the word. Each half of the *melacha* was performed under a different type of forgetting — the first under forgetting of the day, the second under forgetting of the prohibition. At no point did the person realize he had sinned. There was no intervening awareness, merely two different modes of forgetting, each responsible for half of a single prohibited act.

### Combination Question

Rabbi Zeira's question, addressed to Rabbi Assi (or according to an alternative tradition, asked by Rabbi Yirmiyah of Rabbi Zeira), is: *mahu sheyitztarfu?* — do these two halves combine? The stakes are significant. If they combine, the person has completed a full *melacha* and is obligated in a *korban*. If they do not combine — because the two halves were performed in structurally distinct states of forgetting — then neither half independently constitutes a complete prohibited act, and the person may be exempt.

### Separation Answer

The answer given is unambiguous: *chalukin l'chataot v'lo mitztarfin* — since these two modes of forgetting, when a complete act is performed under each, generate separate *korbanot*, they are considered categorically separate states. They do not combine. The reasoning is that the very separateness of the two modes of forgetting — even in the absence of an intervening awareness — is sufficient to treat them as distinct legal states for all purposes. If doing a full *melacha* under each mode would result in two separate *korbanot*, it follows that those modes are considered genuinely separate, and therefore two halves under those separate modes also remain separate and do not merge into a single act.

This stands in direct tension with the assumptions underlying the earlier discussion of Abaye and Rava, which proceeded on the premise that some degree of combination across the two modes of forgetting was possible. Rabbi Zeira's question, and the answer he received, reveal that what Abaye and Rava treated as obvious was actually the subject of genuine halakhic uncertainty.

The *gemara* also takes a moment to acknowledge the distinctly abstract character of this entire sugyah. The cases discussed here are purely academic constructions — no one is bringing *korbanot* in the time of the *amoraim*, and the questions are entirely theoretical. This stands in stark contrast to the first six *perakim* of *Shabbat*, which were deeply embedded in the material world and practical life of the period. The present *sugyah* represents the *gemara* at its most formally speculative, playing with halakhic categories in the absence of any grounding in contemporary practice.

## Forbidden Fat Mishnah

### Same vs Different Sins

The *gemara* now examines a *mishnah* in *Kerisos* that bears directly on these questions. The *mishnah* states: if a person ate *chelev* and *chelev* — two separate acts of eating forbidden fat — in a single state of forgetfulness (*behelem echad*), he is liable for only one *korban*. Multiple acts of the same sin within one uninterrupted state of forgetting merge into a single obligation. By contrast, if a person ate *chelev*, blood (*dam*), *notar* (leftover sanctified food), and *pigul* (an offering consumed with improper intent) — all four categories carrying the penalty of *karet* — even in a single state of forgetfulness, he is liable separately for each one: *chayav al kol achas ve'achas*. Different categories of sin, even when committed in a single episode of forgetting, generate separate obligations.

The *mishnah* articulates this as a dual principle. Multiple categories bring stringency, because each carries its own *korban*. But a single category brings its own kind of stringency as well: if one ate half a *kizayit* of *chelev* and then another half a *kizayit* of *chelev* — within the time required to eat half a loaf (*kedei achilat pras*) — the two halves combine to constitute one complete act, and the person is liable. However, if one ate half a *kizayit* of *chelev* and half a *kizayit* of *notar*, those halves do not combine, and the person is exempt.

### Two Halves Debate

The *gemara* asks: when the *mishnah* says that two half-*kizayit* portions of *chelev* combine and the person is liable, is this not obvious? Of course two halves of the same sin combine into a whole act. What *chiddush* is being communicated?

Reish Lakish, in the name of Bar Tutini, answers that the *mishnah* is addressing a subtler case: *kegon she'achalo bishnei tamchuyin* — the two halves were eaten in two different dishes. One half of the *chelev* was in a soup; the other half was in a steak. Although the sin is identical — both are *chelev* — the experiential reality of the eating was different. One might have thought that, just as Rabbi Yehoshua holds that different dishes (*tamchuyin*) divide eating acts and can generate separate *korbanot* when full measures are involved, the same principle would apply in the lenient direction: different dishes would also prevent two halves from combining, exempting the person. The *mishnah* therefore teaches otherwise.

The *gemara* then refines the question of how to understand Rabbi Yehoshua's principle. One might have thought that Rabbi Yehoshua applied the rule that different dishes separate eating acts both *lekula* (leniently) and *lechumra* (stringently). To be stringent: if a full *kizayit* of *chelev* was in the soup and another full *kizayit* in the steak, they are considered separate acts and the person is obligated to bring two *korbanot* rather than one. To be lenient: if only a half *kizayit* was in the soup and a half *kizayit* in the steak, the different dishes would prevent combination and exempt the person. The *gemara* concludes that Rabbi Yehoshua applied his principle only stringently — to multiply *korbanot* — but not leniently. When it comes to two halves, the strict approach is to combine them and obligate the person, and that is what the *mishnah* teaches.

The *gemara* continues by reconsidering the framing. Initially the question was raised on the first clause of the *mishnah* — why must it tell us that two halves of *chelev* combine? But in fact the proper teaching of the Bar Tutini tradition belongs on the final clause — the case of exemption for two different categories. What might seem to be a case of half *chelev* and half blood is actually a case of the same *chelev* eaten in two different dishes. The reason they do not combine is not that they are different substances but that they were in different dishes — and it is that *chiddush* that belongs to Rabbi Yehoshua, who says different dishes separate acts both stringently and leniently. The *gemara* accordingly relocates its question: since the *sof* of the *mishnah* is addressing *chelev* in two different dishes, the *reisha* — the case where two halves do combine — must be referring to the same dish, in which case the combination is indeed not obvious?

### Rabban Gamliel Rule

Rav Huna resolves this. The case where the two halves combine is not simply a case of the same dish with no complicating features. It is a case where the person had a *yedi'ah* — an awareness — in between the two acts of eating. Normally, an intervening awareness divides sin-obligations and prevents combination. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah* is that even with an intervening awareness, the two halves still combine. How so? Because the awareness the person had was only of having eaten half a *kizayit*, not of having committed a complete sin. This is the ruling of Rabban Gamliel: *ein yedi'ah lachatzi shiur* — awareness of having performed only half the prohibited measure does not constitute the kind of legally operative awareness that divides obligations. A *yedi'ah* divides only when one becomes aware of a complete transgression. Awareness of a half-measure leaves the act legally undivided, and the two halves therefore combine notwithstanding the interrupting moment of partial awareness.

## Atonement Timing

### Half-Sin Awareness

The distinction just established — that *yedi'ah* of a complete sin divides, while *yedi'ah* of a half-measure does not — sets the stage for the next set of cases. The operative question going forward is not only whether there was an awareness in between, but what the *content* of that awareness was. Only awareness that one has committed a full, complete transgression carries the legal weight to sever one episode of sin from another and generate independent obligations.

---

## Two Olives Separate

### Scenario Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a case that at first appears to be a straightforward application of what has already been discussed, but which in fact opens a new debate. A person ate two full *zeisim* of *chelev* in a single state of forgetfulness (*behelem echad*). This alone would ordinarily generate a single *korban* — two complete acts of the same sin in one episode of forgetting. But the person then became aware of his first act and, separately, became aware of his second act. Rashi notes explicitly that the assumptions of this *sugyah* are in tension with those of the previous one — what was treated as given before is now openly contested.

### Rabbi Yochanan View

Rabbi Yochanan holds: *chayav shtayim* — the person is liable for two separate *korbanot*. Although the two acts of eating were performed in a single state of forgetting, the fact that awareness came in two separate stages is sufficient to divide them into independent obligations. The source Rabbi Yochanan invokes is the verse *al chatato v'hevi* — for his sin he shall bring — which he reads as requiring a separate *korban* for each discrete sin as it becomes known. Every separate act of remembering triggers a separate and independent obligation, regardless of the original unity of the forgetting.

### Reish Lakish View

Reish Lakish disagrees: *eino chayav ela achat* — he is liable for only one *korban*. Since both acts were committed in a single state of forgetfulness, the sins belong to a single undivided episode. When the person becomes aware of the first act and brings a *korban*, that *korban* is brought *me-chatato* — from his sin — which Reish Lakish reads as encompassing the whole category of sin committed in that episode. Even a *korban* brought for only part of a continuous sin-state atones for the whole. Separate awarenesses, in Reish Lakish's view, do not divide what was originally one.

The *gemara* then asks how each *amora* handles the verse that seems to favor his opponent. For Rabbi Yochanan, how does he account for the phrase *me-chatato* — "from his sin" — which suggests that a *korban* for part of a sin suffices for the whole? And for Reish Lakish, how does he account for *al chatato v'hevi*, which seems to require a separate *korban* per sin?

Reish Lakish's answer to the latter is that the verse applies *l'achar kapparah* — after actual atonement has been achieved. If the person became aware of act A and brought his *korban* for A — completing the act of atonement on the *mizbeach* — and only afterward became aware of act B, then B is an independent obligation and requires its own *korban*. But if awareness of B preceded the actual bringing of the *korban*, then B can still be subsumed within the single offering brought for A. What creates the division is not the awareness itself but the completed act of atonement in between.

---

## Atonement Timing (II)

### Complex Scenario

The *gemara* then constructs a deliberately complicated scenario in order to illustrate the point on which Rabbi Yochanan would agree that only one *korban* need be brought. The scenario involves a person who ate one and a half *zeisim* of *chelev*, had an awareness specifically of the one *kizayit* (not of the accompanying half), then ate another half a *kizayit* of *chelev* in a new state of forgetting. When awareness ultimately arrives, the person has an obligation for the one *kizayit*, is unaware of the first half (which was forgotten), and has a new half-*kizayit* from the second eating.

### Why Complicated

The reason the *gemara* requires such a tortured construction is to find a case where even Rabbi Yochanan — who generally holds that separate awarenesses divide obligations — would concede that a single *korban* covers multiple acts. In this scenario, when the person brings a *korban* for the one *kizayit* he is aware of, the accompanying first half is not remembered and is therefore effectively absorbed into that *korban*. The second half, eaten in a separate state of forgetting, might seem like it should combine with the forgotten first half — two halves of the same sin — but it does not, because the forgotten first half has already been subsumed into the *korban* brought for the whole *kizayit*. There is nothing left for the second half to combine with. The second half is therefore simply waived, and the person is exempt from bringing a *korban* for it. This is the scenario where even Rabbi Yochanan would say only one *korban* is brought for multiple sins — not because of dragging in the ordinary sense, but because one component disappears into the main *korban* and the other is left without a counterpart to combine with.

---

## Ravina Ashi Debate

### Three Possibilities

Ravina raises a meta-level question to Rav Ashi. In the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, what exactly is the scenario under discussion? Ravina poses three possibilities. First: perhaps they debate only in a case where awareness arrived before the person had designated (*hafrasha*) a *korban* at all — before any formal setting-aside of the animal. In that case, the debate is whether separate awarenesses alone (*yediot mechalkot*) are sufficient to divide obligations. If Ravina's first framing is correct, then once a *korban* has actually been designated, Reish Lakish would agree with Rabbi Yochanan that the obligations are divided and two *korbanot* are required. Second: perhaps they debate only in a case where the person had already designated the *korban* for the first act before becoming aware of the second. In that case, Rabbi Yochanan holds that the designation itself divides (*hafrashot mechalkot*), while Reish Lakish holds that even a designated *korban* can still incorporate the second sin, since only actual atonement (*kappara*) creates a true division. According to this second framing, if no designation had yet been made, even Rabbi Yochanan might agree that a single *korban* suffices. Third: they argue in both scenarios — both before designation and after.

### Designation Check

The *gemara* argues that it is most reasonable to assume they debate in both cases. The proof comes from the structure of the *gemara*'s earlier answers. When we asked how Reish Lakish accounts for the verse that seems to require a separate *korban* per sin, we answered that Reish Lakish reserves that verse for the case of *achar kapparah* — after actual atonement has been achieved. But if Reish Lakish would agree that two *korbanot* are required once a *korban* has been designated, then why did the *gemara* not frame his agreement in terms of designation rather than completed atonement? The fact that the *gemara* reached all the way to completed atonement as the dividing event — rather than the much earlier and less demanding step of designation — implies that the *gemara* assumed designation alone does not divide according to Reish Lakish. This in turn implies that the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is not limited to the post-designation case, but extends even to the pre-designation case.

Similarly, when we asked how Rabbi Yochanan accounts for the verse that seems to allow a single *korban* for multiple sins, we gave the elaborate answer involving one-and-a-half plus a half. If Rabbi Yochanan would agree that a single *korban* suffices in any case where no designation has yet been made, then there would have been a far simpler answer available: just say that Rabbi Yochanan agrees with Reish Lakish in the pre-designation case. The fact that we did not give that simpler answer implies that the *gemara* assumed Rabbi Yochanan holds two *korbanot* are required even before designation — again suggesting the debate covers both scenarios.

### Rashi Meta-Analysis

Rashi here makes a fascinating observation about the nature of the argument being made. The *gemara* is not citing Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish directly — it is reasoning from the way the *gemara* itself framed their debate to draw inferences about the scope of their positions. In other words, the *gemara* is treating its own earlier formulations as evidence for what was assumed by the Yeshiva about the parameters of the dispute.

Rashi notes explicitly: since the *gemara* had framed the cases in a specific way — using completed atonement as the dividing event for Reish Lakish, and the complex one-and-a-half scenario for Rabbi Yochanan — this framing reflects the assumptions of the *bnei yeshiva* who structured the *gemara*. *Depishita livnei yeshiva she-sidru ha-gemara de-le-achar hafrasha nami patar Reish Lakish* — it was self-evident to those who edited the *gemara* that Reish Lakish exempts even after designation. The editorial choices of the *gemara* itself become hermeneutic evidence about the scope of the underlying dispute.

This is a striking observation. The *gemara* is simultaneously the source of the argument and the object being examined. The *amoraim* Ravina and Rav Ashi are reading the assumptions embedded in the *gemara*'s own discourse back out from the text in order to clarify what was assumed about positions that were never explicitly defined with respect to the question at hand.

### Uncertain Scope

Ultimately, the *gemara* does not accept this line of reasoning as a definitive proof. The *gemara* concludes: *udi'lma sfukei misapka lei* — perhaps the reason those earlier answers were given is precisely because the scope of the debate was unknown, and the answers were formulated in a way that would hold regardless. The complicated case of one-and-a-half and a half, for instance, was given not because we assumed Rabbi Yochanan holds that separate awarenesses alone divide, but because we were uncertain, and that scenario serves as a valid case for Rabbi Yochanan's single-*korban* position under any reading of the debate. Similarly, the reference to completed atonement as the trigger for Reish Lakish's agreement may reflect uncertainty rather than assumption.

The *gemara* closes on this note of acknowledged inconclusiveness: the scope of the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — whether they argue before designation, after designation, or in both cases — cannot be established with certainty from the structure of the *gemara*'s own earlier answers, because those answers may themselves have been framed in conditions of uncertainty.
