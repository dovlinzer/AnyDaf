# Avodah Zarah 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Atlusa of Gaza

### Proximity Outside City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — חוּצָה לָהּ מוּתָּר, הָיָה חוּצָה לָהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — תּוֹכָהּ לָהּ מוּתָּר. מַהוּ לֵילֵךְ לְשָׁם? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַדֶּרֶךְ מְיוּחֶדֶת לְאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם — אָסוּר, וְאִם הָיָה יָכוֹל לְהַלֵּךְ בָּהּ לְמָקוֹם אַחֵר — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a city in which there is** active **idol worship,** it is **permitted** to engage in business transactions with gentiles who live **outside of** the city. If the **idol worship** is **outside** the city, it is **permitted** to engage in business **within** the city. **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **traveling there,** a place where a pagan festival is being celebrated? **When the road is designated** only **for that place,** it is **prohibited** to use the road, as onlookers will assume that the traveler intends to join the festival. **But if one were able to travel on it to** arrive at **another place,** it is **permitted** to use the road to reach the place that is observing the festival.

The *gemara* on 12a takes up immediately after the *mishnah* at the bottom of 11b. To reorient: the *mishnah* established that if a city is celebrating a *yom eid* — whether a festival of *avodah zarah* or a special market day devoted to that idol — then *chutz lah mutar*: outside the city, one is permitted to conduct business, unconstrained by the *yom eid* restrictions. Conversely, if the *yom eid* celebration is taking place outside the city, in one of the surrounding villages, then inside the city is permissible. The governing principle is that each jurisdiction follows its own rule; we do not say that one place is dragged after the next. The *mishnah* then raises the question of whether one may travel to the city while the celebration is underway — given that one's mere going might appear to be participation, or might even constitute a form of pilgrimage. The answer: if the road leads exclusively to that city and terminates there, it is forbidden. If, however, the road is a thoroughfare that continues beyond the city, then even if one's final destination is the city itself, onlookers will reasonably assume one is passing through, and the concern about appearances does not apply.

### Atlusa Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי דָמֵי חוּצָה לָהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כְּגוֹן עִטְלוֹזָהּ שֶׁל עַזָּה. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: עִטְלוֹזָהּ שֶׁל עַזָּה מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** that determine whether a place is sufficiently far from a city to be considered **outside of it? Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says in the name of Rabbi Ḥanina:** A place that is far enough away is, **for example, the bazaar [*atluza*] of Gaza,** which is located outside the city walls. **And some say** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish asked Rabbi Ḥanina** about this issue: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **the bazaar of Gaza?** May one conduct business there on the day of a festival celebrated in Gaza?

The *gemara* now asks: *heichi dami chutz lah* — what exactly constitutes "outside" of the city? Even a place that is immediately adjacent to the city walls — is that still considered a distinct jurisdiction governed by a different rule? The *gemara* answers by citing Reish Lakish in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *kegon atlusa shel Aza* — like the *atlusa* of Gaza. The *atlusa* is either the name of a town just outside the city of Gaza, or it refers to a marketplace or meat-selling district located immediately outside the city. The point is that even a place so close to Gaza as this *atlusa* is treated as an entirely distinct jurisdiction: if the *yom eid* is occurring in Gaza, it does not apply to the *atlusa*, and vice versa.

### Reish Lakish's Question

There is, however, an alternative version of this tradition. According to this reading, it was not an answer that Reish Lakish gave in the name of Rabbi Chanina, but rather a question that Reish Lakish posed *to* Rabbi Chanina: *atlusa shel Aza mahu* — what is the *halachic* status of the *atlusa* of Gaza? Does it follow Gaza, or is it governed independently?

### Shared Oven Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֹא הָלַכְתָּ לְצוֹר מִיָּמֶיךָ וְרָאִיתָ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי שֶׁשָּׁפְתוּ שְׁתֵּי קְדֵירוֹת עַל גַּבֵּי כִּירָה אַחַת? וְלֹא חַשּׁוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥanina **said to him: Did you never in your lifetime travel to Tyre and see a Jew and a gentile place two pots on one stove, and** yet **the Sages were not concerned** and did not issue a prohibition with regard to the meat that was in the pot belonging to the Jew, despite the fact that forbidden food was in close proximity to the permitted food?

Rabbi Chanina responded with a striking analogy. He said: have you never gone to the city of Tyre? And there, at the top of 12a, have you not seen a Jew and a non-Jew who were cooking two pots on one *kira* — a single shared oven? A *kira* was a hollowed-out rectangular structure with a fire inside and two openings on top into which the pots were lowered, so the two pots sat right next to each other on the same heat source. Rabbi Chanina said: it is common practice in Tyre for a Jew and a non-Jew to share such an oven, each cooking in his own pot, and *velo chashu lahem chachamim* — the *chachamim* were not concerned that what was happening in the non-Jew's pot would affect the Jew's pot. The analogy is clear: proximity alone does not collapse the distinction between two separate jurisdictions. Just as the two pots sit side by side on one oven yet remain entirely distinct, so too Gaza and its *atlusa*, however close, are governed independently.

## Abaye-Rava-Rabbah Bar Ul…

### Abaye: Neveilah Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לֹא חַשּׁוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים״? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִשּׁוּם בְּשַׂר נְבֵילָה לָא אָמְרִינַן, דִּלְמָא מַהְדַּר אַפֵּיהּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ וְשָׁדֵי גּוֹי נְבֵילָה בִּקְדֵירָה, דִּכְוָותַהּ הָכָא נָמֵי לֹא חַשּׁוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים מִשּׁוּם דְּמֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** did he mean in stating: **The Sages were not concerned,** with regard to the meat, and how does that case relate the issue here? **Abaye said:** The Sages were not concerned **with regard to** the possibility of eating the **meat** of an unslaughtered **animal carcass. We do not say:** Cooking in this manner is prohibited since **perhaps the Jew** will **turn his face and** at that moment **the gentile** will **throw** meat of **an animal carcass into** his **pot. Here too, in the corresponding** situation, although the permitted and prohibited places are in close proximity, **the Sages were not concerned** about engaging in business transactions in the bazaar of Gaza, even **due to** the possibility that **money** associated with **idol worship** would end up in the hands of the Jews.

The *gemara* does not simply accept the analogy and move on. It probes it: *mai lo chashu lahem chachamim* — what exactly were the *chachamim* not concerned about? What potential problem existed in the case of a Jew and a non-Jew each cooking a pot on the same oven, and what does that problem teach us about the *atlusa* and Gaza?

Three *amoraim* offer three distinct answers, and the answer matters enormously because it determines what the analogy is actually teaching.

Abaye says: *mishum basar neveilah* — the concern was *neveilah*, non-*kosher* meat. One might have feared that the Jew would look away for a moment and the non-Jew would toss a piece of *neveilah* into the Jew's pot. This would be a *d'oraita* concern of the most serious kind. Now, one might ask what motivation the non-Jew would have to do such a thing. The *gemara* considers a scenario where he might intend to help — for instance, stirring the Jew's pot with a spoon that had *treifa* residue on it — or, as Tosafot suggests, perhaps there was a financial incentive, such as the non-Jew swapping his lean piece of meat for the Jew's finer cut. Whatever the specific scenario, the point is that there was a conceivable basis for concern about a *d'oraita* prohibition. And the *chiddush* is that the *chachamim* were not concerned, in part because the Jew is present and could turn back at any moment, which itself serves as a deterrent.

### Money of Idols Analogy

Having established that Abaye reads the oven analogy as addressing a *d'oraita* concern about *neveilah*, the *gemara* now draws out the parallel to our case. *Hacha nami*, says Abaye — here too, by analogy, what the *chachamim* were not concerned about in the *atlusa* of Gaza is the possibility of receiving money that constitutes *d'mei avodas kochavim* — proceeds connected to the idol itself. This would be the *d'oraita* counterpart to *neveilah*: just as *neveilah* is forbidden *b'hana'ah* from the Torah, so too certain forms of money connected to *avodah zarah* may be forbidden *b'hana'ah d'oraita*. The *chiddush* is that even though one is in proximity to Gaza during a *yom eid*, one need not fear receiving such tainted money in the *atlusa*.

This raises a pointed question that Tosafot addresses at length. Is it genuinely true that money in a non-Jew's possession connected to an idol is forbidden at all? Throughout the *masechet* there is discussion of whether a non-Jew performs *bittul* on his idol when selling it, which would eliminate any prohibition. What exactly is the scenario in which money relating to a non-Jew's *avodah zarah* becomes forbidden *b'hana'ah d'oraita*? Two possibilities emerge: either it is proceeds from the outright sale of an idol — though this raises the *bittul* question — or it is money that was presented as an offering before the idol, though the precise parameters of what constitutes a forbidden monetary offering also require clarification.

A second difficulty is equally sharp. Tosafot observes that throughout the rest of the *masechet*, when the *gemara* discusses *yom eid*, there is never a concern that the non-Jew might simply be carrying money that happens to be proceeds from an idol sale. Why, then, would this be a concern specifically here, in the *atlusa*? Tosafot's answer is that we are not dealing with a generic *yom eid* but with a *specific* market day dedicated to that deity. On such a specially dedicated market day, occurring inside Gaza, there is a heightened likelihood that people are actively handling money that is directly connected to the idol. Thus, in Gaza itself during this market day, there is genuine cause for concern. The *chiddush* of the analogy to the oven is that even so — even though one is dealing with a potential *d'oraita* prohibition — in the *atlusa* right outside, we are not concerned. Just as the *chachamim* dismissed the *neveilah* concern for the adjacent pot, so they dismiss the *d'mei avodas kochavim* concern for the adjacent marketplace.

### Rava: Bishul Akum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מַאי ״לֹא חַשּׁוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים״? מִשּׁוּם בִּישּׁוּלֵי גוֹיִם. דִּכְוָותַהּ, הָכָא נָמֵי, לֹא חַשּׁוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים מִשּׁוּם יוֹם אֵידָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that there is a different explanation. The Sages were not lenient in the face of a potential violation of Torah law, but were lenient in a case where it was rabbinic law that might be violated. As for Rabbi Ḥanina's comparison to pots in Tyre, there is no concern that a gentile might throw his meat into the pot of a Jew, as he would derive no benefit from doing so and would be afraid that the Jew might see him. By contrast, here the gentile is engaged in business. Rather, **what** is it that **the Sages were not concerned about** in the case of the pots? Although the gentile is cooking food next to the Jew, there is no concern **with regard to** the possibility that the gentile might cook the Jew's food, causing the latter to violate the rabbinic prohibition against eating **food cooked by gentiles. Here too, in the corresponding** situation, it is referring to a case where the coins were the gentile's own money. **The Sages were not concerned** about engaging in business transactions in the bazaar of Gaza, even **due to** the possibility that the Jew might be engaging in business with residents of Gaza on **their festival day,** which would be a violation of rabbinic law.

Rava disagrees with Abaye's reading of the oven analogy. He says: *mai lo chashu lahem chachamim mishum bishulei ovdei kochavim* — the concern was not a *d'oraita* concern about *neveilah* at all. Rather, it was the *d'rabbanan* prohibition of *bishul akum*: perhaps the non-Jew lit the fire for the Jew, stirred the Jew's pot, or was otherwise sufficiently involved in the cooking that it would constitute *bishul akum* and render the food forbidden by rabbinic decree. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah* is that the *chachamim* were not concerned about this *issur d'rabbanan* when a Jew is present and largely in control of his own cooking.

And *v'hacha nami* — here too, Rava reads the analogy as addressing only a *d'rabbanan* concern: we are not concerned that the proximity of the *atlusa* to Gaza implicates one in the *yom eid* of the city. Importantly, this reading also clarifies that for Rava, we are dealing with the generic *yom eid* prohibition, not a special market day with heightened concerns about *d'mei avodas kochavim*. Rava does not need to invoke that scenario at all, because the analogy for him operates entirely on the *d'rabbanan* level. Rashi and Tosafot discuss what this implies about Rava's broader views — whether he regards *d'mei avodas kochavim* as ever a concern in the context of a standard *yom eid* — but that analysis need not detain us here.

### Rabbah Bar Ulla: Splatter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא אָמַר: לֹא חַשּׁוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים מִשּׁוּם צִינּוֹרָא. דִּכְוָותַהּ, הָכָא נָמֵי, לֹא חַשּׁוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים מִשּׁוּם לִפְנֵי אֵידֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ulla says:** Even if the concern in the case of the pots applied only to the gentile cooking the Jew's food, not the consumption of non-kosher meat, with regard to the bazaar the *halakha* would not be comparably lenient. The reason is the Jew need only stir the coals once to ensure that the food in his pot is not considered cooked by a gentile, an option that does not apply here. Rather, in the case mentioned by Rabbi Ḥanina, **the Sages were not concerned with regard to** the possibility that food might **splatter [*tzinnora*]** from the gentile's pot into the Jew's pot. This is an especially lenient case, both because this is an unlikely possibility and because that small amount of food would be nullified by a majority of the Jew's food. Rabba bar Ulla concludes: **Here too, in the corresponding** situation, **the Sages were not concerned** about engaging in business transactions in the bazaar of Gaza **with regard to** the days **before the festival of** Gaza.

Rabbah bar Ulla offers a third and more nuanced reading. He says: *lo chashu lahem chachamim mishum tzinora* — the *chachamim* were not concerned about a *tzinora*, a splatter. The concern is not that the non-Jew would actively introduce *treif* food into the Jew's pot, and not that *bishul akum* applies; rather, the concern is that a splash or splatter from the non-Jew's pot — which contains *neveilah* — might fly out and land in the Jew's pot. This is one degree further removed than anything Abaye or Rava was discussing: it is not the thing itself but something separated from it.

Tosafot notes that even if such a splatter were to occur, it would almost certainly be *batel* — nullified by the much greater volume of the Jew's food — so that the practical concern is minimal. And *v'hacha nami*, Rabbah bar Ulla draws the corresponding lesson for our case: the parallel concern in the *atlusa* of Gaza is *lifnei edeihen*, the days preceding the *yom eid*. The *atlusa* is like the splatter — adjacent to, and somewhat associated with, the main celebration in Gaza — but it is removed enough that we are not concerned even about the days leading up to the *yom eid*. The implication, interestingly, is that the *atlusa* might be treated more stringently on the *yom eid* itself, given its proximity to Gaza. But once one is dealing with a lesser concern — the *erev yom eid* period — the *atlusa* is distinct enough to be entirely permitted.

## Entering City on Yom Eid

### Cannot Enter City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ לֵילֵךְ לְשָׁם וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — אָסוּר לִיכָּנֵס לְתוֹכָהּ, וְלֹא מִתּוֹכָהּ לְעִיר אַחֶרֶת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהַדֶּרֶךְ מְיוּחֶדֶת לְאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם — אָסוּר, אֵין הַדֶּרֶךְ מְיוּחֶדֶת לְאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **traveling there,** a place that is celebrating a pagan festival? If the road leads only to that place, it is prohibited, but if the road leads to another place as well, it is permitted. In this connection, the Gemara cites a related *baraita*. **The Sages taught:** In the case of **a city in which there is** active **idol worship,** i.e., its residents are worshipping their idol on that day, it is **prohibited to enter** the city, **and** one may **not** leave **it for another city;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: As long as the road is designated** only **for that place,** it is **prohibited** to enter the city. But if **the road is not designated for** only **that place,** it is **permitted.**

The *gemara* now turns to the second question raised by the *mishnah*: what is the rule about traveling to such a city? A *baraita* is cited: *ir she'yesh bah avodat kochavim assur likaneis l'tochah* — if a city has *avodah zarah* being celebrated within it, it is forbidden to enter the city. The concern, as with the *mishnah*, is that one's entry either looks like participation in the celebrations or is itself a form of participation akin to pilgrimage.

### Rabbi Meir Position

*V'lo mitochah l'ir acheret*, the *baraita* continues — *divrei Rabbi Meir*: and one may not go from it to another city. This initially sounds strange — does Rabbi Meir mean one may not leave the city once inside? What seems intended is that one may not *enter* the city at all, even if one intends to pass through and proceed to another city beyond. For Rabbi Meir, the fact that the road continues beyond is irrelevant; the act of entering the city during its celebrations is itself forbidden, regardless of one's ultimate destination.

### Sages: Extended Road

The *chachamim* disagree, and their position is that of our *mishnah*. *Kol zman shederech miyuchedet l'oto makom assur* — if the road terminates at the city, it is forbidden, because anyone who sees you going will assume you are going to participate. But *einah derech miyuchedet l'oto makom mutar* — if the road continues beyond the city, it is permitted, and this is true even if one stops in the city, because the onlooker will reasonably assume you are on your way to a further destination.

The essential dispute is therefore this: Rabbi Meir holds that entering the city during its celebrations is intrinsically problematic, regardless of what the road looks like or what one's actual intentions are. The *chachamim* frame the issue entirely in terms of *maris ayin* — the concern is solely about how the going appears to others — and therefore context can fully resolve the concern. If the road continues beyond, the appearance is innocent.

## Marit Ayin Doctrine

### Thorn Before Idol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָשַׁב לוֹ קוֹץ בִּפְנֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — לֹא יִשְׁחֶה וְיִטְלֶנָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כְּמִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאִם אֵינוֹ נִרְאֶה — מוּתָּר. נִתְפַּזְּרוּ לוֹ מְעוֹתָיו בִּפְנֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — לֹא יִשְׁחֶה וְיִטְּלֵם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כְּמִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאִם אֵינוֹ נִרְאֶה — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If **a thorn became imbedded** in one's foot while he was standing **before** an object of **idol worship, he may not bend down and remove** the thorn, **because he appears to be bowing down to** the object of **idol worship; but if he is not seen,** it is **permitted.** If **one's coins were scattered** while he is **before** an object of **idol worship, he may not bend down and pick them up, because he appears to be bowing down to** the object of **idol worship; but if he is not seen,** it is **permitted.**

The *gemara* now transitions to a series of cases involving *maris ayin* directly before an idol, cases that go to the heart of the *marit ayin* doctrine in the context of *avodah zarah*. The first case: *yashav lo kotz bifnei avodah zarah* — if a thorn lodged in one's foot while standing in front of an idol, *lo yishcheh v'yitlenu* — one may not bend down to remove it, *mipnei shenireh k'mishtachaveh l'avodah zarah* — because it appears as though one is bowing down to the idol. But *v'im eino nireh mutar* — if it does not appear that way, it is permissible. The *gemara* will shortly clarify what this phrase means.

### Money Scatters

The second case: *nitpazru lo ma'otav bifnei avodah zarah* — if one's money purse broke and the coins scattered in front of an idol, *lo yishcheh v'yilketem* — one may not bend down to collect them, *mipnei shenireh k'mishtachaveh l'avodah zarah* — for the same reason. One may find other ways to retrieve the money — perhaps kicking the coins away from the area first — but one may not simply bend down in a manner that, viewed from a distance, would look like a bow before the idol. Again, *v'im eino nireh mutar*.

### Spring Water Idol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעְיָין הַמּוֹשֵׁךְ לִפְנֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — לֹא יִשְׁחֶה וְיִשְׁתֶּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כְּמִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאִם אֵינוֹ נִרְאֶה — מוּתָּר. פַּרְצוּפוֹת הַמְקַלְּחִין מַיִם לִכְרַכִּין — לֹא יַנִּיחַ פִּיו עַל פִּיהֶם וְיִשְׁתֶּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כִּמְנַשֵּׁק לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ — לֹא יַנִּיחַ פִּיו עַל סִילוֹן וְיִשְׁתֶּה, מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, if there is **a spring that runs before** an object of **idol worship,** one **may not bend down and drink** from it, **because he appears to be bowing down to** the object of **idol worship; but if he is not seen,** it is **permitted.** With regard to figures of human **faces [*partzufot*] that spray water in the cities,** i.e., fountains, **one may not place his mouth on the mouths of** the figures **and drink, because he appears to be kissing** the object of **idol worship. Similarly, one may not place his mouth on a pipe [*sillon*] and drink, **here **due to the danger** that this practice poses.

The third case: *ma'ayan hamoshech lifnei avodah zarah* — if there is a spring or pool whose water flows in front of an idol. Such arrangements were common in antiquity; a pool would be constructed with a statue above it. *Lo yishcheh v'yishteh* — one may not bend down to drink from the water, *mipnei shenireh k'mishtachaveh l'avodah zarah* — because it appears as if one is bowing toward the idol. As before, *v'im eino nireh mutar*.

### Idol's Mouth Water

The fourth case is somewhat different in character. *Partzufot hamakallchim mayim likrachim* — if there are faces, like a bust or statue of a deity, from whose open mouth water flows into the city — a feature visible in various ancient and medieval cities — *lo yaniche piv al pihem v'yishteh* — one may not press one's mouth against the mouth of this face and drink. The concern here is not bowing but *menashek* — it appears as though one is kissing the idol. While this is not *avodah* — formal service — hugging, kissing, or other gestures of honor toward an idol are independently forbidden as acts of reverence, and one may not perform an action that appears to be such a gesture. Even so, the same qualifier applies: *v'im eino nireh mutar*.

### Pipe Danger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּרְצוּפוֹת לְמָה לִי? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי: כַּיּוֹצֵא בוֹ לֹא יַנִּיחַ פִּיו עַל גַּבֵּי הַסִּילוֹן וְיִשְׁתֶּה, מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the *baraita* to teach that it is prohibited to drink from fountains formed in the figure of human **faces?** If the reason is to teach the *halakha* in a life-threatening situation, the *baraita* already addressed this issue in the case of the spring. The Gemara answers: It was included **because** the *baraita* **wanted to teach** the continuation of that *halakha*: **Similarly, one may not place his mouth on a pipe and drink, due to the danger** that this poses.

At the end of this passage, the *baraita* adds a related but distinct case: *lo yaniche piv al silon v'yishteh* — one may not place one's mouth on a pipe and drink directly — *mipnei hasakanah* — because of physical danger. This is not a *maris ayin* concern at all, and the *gemara* will explain momentarily what danger is meant. The reason this case is appended here, as the *gemara* will note, is to serve as a bridge to the subsequent discussion of water-drinking dangers.

## Eino Nireh Standard

### Marit Ayin Always Applies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֵינוֹ נִרְאֶה״? אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא מִתְחֲזֵי, וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָסְרוּ חֲכָמִים מִפְּנֵי מַרְאִית הָעַיִן, אֲפִילּוּ בְּחַדְרֵי חֲדָרִים אָסוּר! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אִם אֵינוֹ נִרְאֶה כְּמִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does the *baraita* mean when it states: If **he is not seen? If we say** it means **that he is not seen** by others, **doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says: Wherever the Sages prohibited** an action **due to the appearance** of prohibition, **it is prohibited even in the innermost chambers** where no one will see it, as the Sages did not distinguish between different circumstances in such cases. Accordingly, the fact that he is not seen by anyone should make no difference with regard to whether or not the action is prohibited. **Rather, say: If he is not seen as one who bows down to** an object of **idol worship,** i.e., he turns his side or back to the idol, then it is **permitted.**

What does *v'im eino nireh mutar* mean? The first interpretation one might suggest is that *eino nireh* means one is not visible — that no one is present to observe the action. If so, the *baraita* would be saying: if you are alone and no one sees you, it is permitted to bend down. The *gemara* immediately rejects this interpretation by citing the ruling of Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav: *kol makom she'asru chachamim mipnei maris ayin, afilu b'chadrei chadarim assur* — wherever the *chachamim* prohibited something on grounds of *maris ayin*, it is forbidden even in one's innermost chambers. Privacy is no exemption from *maris ayin*.

This principle deserves a moment's reflection, for it can be understood in two ways. One explanation is epistemic: one can never be certain of being unobserved, and the concern that someone might see you is always present. The other explanation is more fundamental: one of the harms of *maris ayin* is not solely reputational — the concern that others will suspect wrongdoing — but also intrinsic to the act itself. Performing an action that *looks* like a violation of a serious taboo, even in private, constitutes a breach in one's relationship with that taboo. On either account, the ruling is unambiguous: being alone does not dissolve the prohibition.

It is worth noting, parenthetically, that while the general assumption among *poskim* is that any action that *looks* like a violation is automatically forbidden by virtue of *maris ayin*, there is a minority position among *poskim* that the concern of *maris ayin* applies only to specific prohibitions that *chazal* explicitly singled out — that there is no blanket, free-standing *maris ayin* prohibition covering every situation that might be misinterpreted. This is not the mainstream view, but it is a recognized position.

### Reasonable Alternative

If *eino nireh* cannot mean "when no one can see you," what does it mean? *Ela eima*: *im eino nireh k'mishtachaveh l'avodas kochavim mutar* — if, given the context, it does not *appear* that one is bowing to the idol, it is permissible. The standard is not that everyone present will positively know what one is doing; it is that a reasonable person would understand that a permissible alternative explanation exists. The appearance of bowing must be implausible or readily dismissible given the circumstances.

This is precisely the logic of the *mishnah*: when the road continues beyond the city, people seeing you go there can reasonably assume you are heading farther. One need not announce one's intentions or make the innocent purpose absolutely transparent to every onlooker. It is sufficient that a reasonable interpretation of the action is something other than wrongdoing. A practical illustration: a person who enters an establishment that raises *maris ayin* concerns in order to use the facilities — does everyone present need to understand that immediately? No. It suffices that a reasonable observer would recognize that as a plausible purpose.

### Why All Four Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי תְּנָא קוֹץ, מִשּׁוּם דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְמֵיזַל קַמֵּיהּ וּמִשְׁקְלֵיהּ, אֲבָל מָעוֹת דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי תְּנָא מָעוֹת — דְּמָמוֹנָא, אֲבָל קוֹץ דְּצַעֲרָא — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי תְּנָא הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי — מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיכָּא סַכָּנָה, אֲבָל מַעְיָין דְּאִיכָּא סַכָּנָה, דְּאִי לָא שָׁתֵי מָיֵית — אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** for the *baraita* to list all of these cases, notwithstanding their similarity. **As, if** it had **taught** only the case of the **thorn,** one might have thought that bending down to remove a thorn is prohibited **because it is possible to walk past** the figure, **and** only then **take** out the thorn. **But** in the case of the **coins, where it is not possible** to collect them elsewhere, you might **say** that it is **not** prohibited to pick them up. **And** furthermore, **if** the *baraita* had **taught** only the case of the **coins,** one might have thought that the reason for the stringent ruling is **that** the loss is purely **financial. But** in the case of the **thorn, which** causes him **pain,** you might **say** that it is **not** prohibited to remove it. **And** finally, **if** the *baraita* had **taught** only **these two** cases, one might have thought that they are prohibited **because there is no danger** if the action is not performed on the spot. **But** in the case of the **spring, where there is** an element of **danger, that if he does not drink he** might **die,** one could **say** that it is **not** prohibited. Therefore, it is **necessary** to state each example.

Given this understanding of *maris ayin*, one might ask why the *baraita* needed to enumerate all four cases. Why not state one case — do not bend before an idol — and leave the general principle to be inferred? The *gemara* explains that each case is necessary because each one might have been treated as an exception on independent grounds.

Had the *baraita* stated only the case of the thorn, one might have thought the prohibition applies there because pulling out a thorn is not urgent — one can easily walk a few steps away from the idol and remove it without bending in front of it. Since there is an obvious alternative, *maris ayin* is a legitimate demand. But the case of scattered money, one might have argued, is different: one cannot relocate one's money, and requiring a person to abandon money to avoid *maris ayin* seems too demanding. The *baraita* therefore also states the money case to make clear that even financial loss does not override the *maris ayin* concern.

Conversely, had the *baraita* stated only the money case and not the thorn, one might have thought that accepting financial loss is required, but physical pain is different. A person suffering from a thorn in the foot is in genuine discomfort; perhaps under those circumstances *maris ayin* is waived. The *baraita* states the thorn case explicitly to make clear that even genuine *tza'ara* — physical pain — does not override the prohibition.

And had the *baraita* stated only those two cases, one might have drawn an even sharper distinction for the case of water. Water is a matter of life; *d'ilu lo shasi mayis* — if one cannot drink, one might die. Perhaps under conditions of actual danger to life, *maris ayin* of *avodah zarah* is set aside. The *baraita* must therefore also state the water case, to teach that even the possibility of danger to life does not, on the face of the *baraita*'s ruling, dissolve the *maris ayin* prohibition.

## Marit Ayin vs. Sakana

### Peshat: Yehareg ve'al Ya…

This brings us to a striking implication of the *gemara*. The *baraita* states that even when there is a danger (*sakana*) — specifically, the danger of dying of thirst — one may not bend down and drink from the water flowing before the idol. A straightforward reading of this *gemara* suggests something quite extreme: that *maris ayin* of *avodah zarah* carries the status of *yehareg v'al ya'avor* — one must be prepared to die rather than perform an action that merely *appears* to be *avodah zarah*, even when the underlying act is entirely permitted.

There is a certain *lamdus* logic that would support this reading. A fascinating question in *halacha* is whether derivative prohibitions take on the weight of the primary prohibition they are connected to. Does *lifnei iver* of *chilul Shabbos* carry more weight than *lifnei iver* of an *issur nazir*, because one is derived from an *issur kares* and the other from a simple *lav*? Does *chatzi shiur* of *chametz* on *Pesach* carry greater severity than *chatzi shiur* of *neveilah*? The same question applies here: if *avodah zarah* is itself *yehareg v'al ya'avor*, does *maris ayin* of *avodah zarah* inherit that status? The *peshat* of the *gemara*, taken on its face, seems to say yes — that performing an action which *looks* like *avodah zarah* is itself a form of *avodah zarah*, and that one must die rather than do it.

Tosafot, notably, engages with this passage and raises the difficulty of how to reconcile the *gemara*'s implication with the practical demands of real-life situations.

### Rema: Not Yehareg ve'al

Most *rishonim* and *poskim*, however, do not accept this as a *psak l'halacha*. They read the *gemara*'s invocation of mortal danger as hyperbolic — the *gemara* is emphasizing the seriousness of the water case to prevent one from thinking that thirst obviates *maris ayin*, not literally ruling that one must die. On this reading, *maris ayin* of *avodah zarah* does not rise to the level of *yehareg v'al ya'avor*.

The Rema addresses this question directly in *Yoreh De'ah* 150, *se'if* 3, in the context of the *Shulchan Aruch*'s ruling about the faces from whose mouths water flows. The *Shulchan Aruch* rules that one may not place one's mouth against the idol's mouth to drink, as it appears like kissing the idol. The Rema, citing the position of the *Ran* under the rubric of *yesh omrim*, states: any prohibition that exists only because of *maris ayin* — such as this case — if there is actual danger to one's life, such that one would die if one does not drink, then it is permitted to drink, and there is no *din* of *yehareg v'al ya'avor* in such a case. The Rema similarly applies this reasoning to *lifnei iver* of *avodah zarah*: causing another to stumble in *avodah zarah* does not carry the *yehareg v'al ya'avor* status of *avodah zarah* itself.

### Gra: Yehareg ve'al

The *Gra* takes strong exception to the Rema's position and argues that the *peshat* of the *gemara* should indeed be taken at face value: *maris ayin* of *avodah zarah* is *yehareg v'al ya'avor*. The *Gra* supports this with a reference to the classic *midrash* about Haman. Haman wore an idol around his neck, and when people bowed before him they were effectively bowing before the idol. Mordechai refused to bow down — and according to the *Gra*'s reading, this was not merely a case of actual *avodah zarah* but of the appearance of *avodah zarah*, which Mordechai treated as *yehareg v'al ya'avor*.

Interestingly, there is a compelling logic to the *Gra*'s position that does not depend simply on a mechanical inheritance of stringency from the primary prohibition. The *mitzvah* of *v'ahavta es Hashem Elokecha* — loving God with all one's heart, soul, and resources — is understood to encompass the obligation to sanctify God's name publicly and to refuse to deny one's faith even under mortal threat. If an act *appears* to people like a betrayal of one's faith, perhaps that appearance of betrayal is itself what *yehareg v'al ya'avor* is designed to prevent. Whether one actually commits *avodah zarah* or merely appears to do so may not be the relevant criterion if the public desecration is equally present in both cases. It is a genuinely powerful argument, and the *Gra* clearly gives it weight. Yet the Rema's position remains the dominant practical ruling.

### Rema's Practical Cases

The Rema goes further and addresses practical situations directly. He writes: *sarim o kohanim she'yesh lahem tzurot avodot kochavim b'vigdeihem* — lords or priests who bear images of *avodah zarah* on their garments, or who carry images of the sun or other religious symbols before them in a processional — *assur lehishtachavot lahem o lehasir kova lifneihem*: it is forbidden to bow before them or to remove one's hat as they pass. The problem is that doing so, in the context of a religious procession bearing sacred images, appears like *menashek* or *mishtachaveh* — kissing or bowing — to the idol.

But the Rema then offers practical solutions consistent with the principle of *eino nireh* established by the *gemara*. One may act in a way that does not *appear* to be directed at the idol: for instance, *sheyikalm lifneihem kodem bo'am* — one drops one's money before the procession arrives, so that bending down to retrieve it is plainly about the money and not about the passing procession. Similarly, *yasir hakova veyishtachaveh kodem bo'am* — one removes one's hat and bows before the procession reaches one, so that the gesture is not contextually connected to the images being carried. This is, incidentally, the reverse of the *gemara*'s original case: there, bending to pick up money looked like bowing to an idol; here, the very act of picking up scattered money becomes the mechanism by which one creates an innocent appearance for an action one is required to perform by social convention.

The Rema also notes a lenient position: since it is commonly understood — even by the non-Jews themselves — that when one bows before a lord or priest one is showing deference to the personage, not to the images on his garments or the symbols carried before him, the context itself may suffice to establish the permissible interpretation. Nevertheless, the Rema concludes that it is better to be stringent and follow the first approach, ensuring that the act is not contextually associated with the procession at all.

## Water Drinking Dangers

### Leech Danger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי סַכָּנָה? עֲלוּקָה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה אָדָם מַיִם לֹא מִן הַנְּהָרוֹת וְלֹא מִן הָאֲגַמִּים, לֹא בְּפִיו וְלֹא בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת, וְאִם שָׁתָה — דָּמוֹ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ, מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה. מַאי סַכָּנָה? סַכָּנַת עֲלוּקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What danger** is the *baraita* referring to here? It is referring to the danger of swallowing **a leech** in the water. As **the Sages taught: A person should not drink water from rivers or from ponds either** by drinking from the water directly **with his mouth, or** by collecting the water **with one hand** alone. **And if he drank** in this manner, **his blood is upon his** own **head, due to the danger.** The Gemara explains: **What** is this **danger?** It is **the danger of** swallowing **a leech.**

The earlier mention of drinking directly from a pipe *mipnei hasakana* — because of danger — prompts the *gemara* to ask: what exactly is this danger? The answer: *aluka* — a leech. Drinking directly from a river or pond without proper care risks swallowing a leech, which can then enter the digestive system and pose a serious mortal danger.

### No Direct Drinking

A person should not drink water directly from rivers or ponds in either of two ways: not *befiv* — placing one's mouth directly into the water — and not *beyado achat* — using only one hand to scoop water into one's mouth. The problem with both methods is that they do not allow the drinker to examine the water for creatures before drinking. When one holds water in two cupped hands, or draws it into a vessel, one can look at it; when drinking directly or with one hand, there is no opportunity for inspection. The *baraita* concludes: *v'im shata damo berosho* — if one drank in this manner, one's blood is upon one's own head. Having disregarded a known danger, any injury that results is one's own responsibility.

### Leech Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הַבּוֹלֵעַ נִימָא שֶׁל מַיִם — מוּתָּר לְהָחֵם לוֹ חַמִּין בְּשַׁבָּת. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁבָּלַע נִימָא שֶׁל מַיִם, וְהִתִּיר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה לְהָחֵם לוֹ חַמִּין בְּשַׁבָּת. אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לִיגַמַּע חַלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** This **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina, as Rabbi Ḥanina says:** In the case of **one who swallows a water leech [*nima*],** it is **permitted to** perform labor on Shabbat and **heat water for him** to drink **on Shabbat,** as his life is in danger. **And** in fact there was **an incident involving one who swallowed a water leech, and Rabbi Neḥemya permitted** them **to heat water for him on Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: **In the meantime,** until the water is ready, what should he do? **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: He should swallow vinegar.**

This *baraita* is cited in support of a teaching of Rabbi Chanina: *habole'a nima shel mayim mutar lehacham lo chamim b'Shabbat* — if one has swallowed a water leech, it is permitted to heat water for that person on *Shabbat*. The reasoning is twofold: it is an established *sakana*, and the remedy — drinking hot water — is understood to kill or neutralize the leech in the digestive system. The *baraita* about the danger of drinking without inspection supports the premise that swallowing a leech is indeed a life-threatening condition, which in turn validates the *heter* to perform the normally prohibited *melacha* of heating water on *Shabbat*.

The *gemara* recounts an incident: *uma'aseh be'echad shebala nima shel mayim* — there was a case of a person who swallowed a water leech. Rabbi Nechemya — or, in some versions, Rabbi Chanina, who would be the same authority whose ruling about heating water on *Shabbat* was just cited — came and heated water on *Shabbat* in accordance with that ruling.

While waiting for the water to heat — *ad dehacki vehacki* — Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua advised: *ligma chala* — swallow vinegar in the meantime. Vinegar may neutralize or weaken the leech sufficiently to buy time until the hot water, which will more thoroughly deal with the problem, is ready.

### Wasp Danger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: הַאי מַאן דִּבְלַע זִיבּוּרָא — מִחְיָיא לָא חָיֵי, מִיהוּ לַשְׁקְיֵיהּ רְבִיעֲתָא דְחַלָּא שַׁמְגַּז, אֶפְשָׁר דְּחָיֵי פּוּרְתָּא עַד דְּמַפְקֵיד אַבֵּיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Idi bar Avin said: One who swallowed a hornet will not live,** as the hornet will sting him to death. **Nevertheless, they should give him a quarter-***log* **of sharp [*shamgaz*] vinegar to drink.** In this manner **it is possible that he will live** for **a bit** longer **until he can instruct his household** with regard to his final wishes before dying.

The discussion of dangerous creatures ingested while drinking concludes with a grimmer case. Rav Idi bar Abin states: *hai man devala zibura michya lo chaye* — if a person swallows a live wasp, he will not survive. Unlike the leech, for which there are effective remedies, the wasp is fatally dangerous. *Mihu* — nonetheless, there is one palliative measure: *lishkiyei revi'isa de'chala* — one should give the person a *revi'isa* of sharp vinegar to drink, approximately three ounces. This will not save him, but *efshar de'chaye purta ad demifkad aveite* — it is possible that it will keep him alive a short while longer, enough time to set his affairs in order.

## Night Water & Demons

### Shavrizi Demon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה אָדָם מַיִם בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאִם שָׁתָה — דָּמוֹ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ, מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה. מַאי סַכָּנָה? סַכָּנַת שַׁבְרִירֵי. וְאִם צָחֵי, מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? אִי אִיכָּא אַחֲרִינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ — לִיתְרְיֵיהּ וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״צָחֵינָא מַיָּא״, וְאִי לָא — נְקַרְקֵשׁ בְּנִכְתְּמָא אַחַצְבָּא, וְנֵימָא אִיהוּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ: ״פְּלָנְיָא בַּר פְּלָנִיתָא, אָמְרָה לָךְ אִימָּךְ אִזְדְּהַר מִשַּׁבְרִירֵי בְּרִירֵי רִירֵי יְרֵי רֵי בְּכָסֵי חִיוָּרֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A person should not drink water at night. And if he drank, his blood is upon his** own **head, due to the danger.** The Gemara asks: **What** is this **danger?** The Gemara answers: **The danger of** the ***shavrirei*,** an evil spirit that rules over water. **And if he is thirsty, what is his remedy? If there is another** person **with him, he should wake him and say to him: I thirst for water,** and then he may drink. **And if** there is **no** other person with him, **he should knock with the lid on the jug and say to himself: So-and-so, son of so-and-so, your mother said to you** to **beware of** the ***shavrirei verirei rirei yirei rei*,** found **in white cups.** This is an incantation against the evil spirit.

A separate *baraita* addresses a different danger in drinking water, one with a different character entirely. *Lo yishte adam mayim ba'layla* — a person should not drink water at night. Here the concern is not leeches or wasps but *shedim* — demons. As with the earlier case, the *baraita* concludes: *v'im shata damo berosho mipnei hasakana* — if one did drink, one's blood is upon one's own head, for one has exposed oneself knowingly to danger. The *gemara* specifies the danger: *sakanas shavrizi* — the danger of the *shavrizi*, which Rashi explains as *shed ha'memuneh al makas sanverim* — the demon appointed over the affliction of blindness. *Shedim* are particularly active at night, and drinking water at night exposes one to this demon's influence, risking blindness.

If one is thirsty at night, what is one to do? *Ei ika achrina bahadei* — if there is another person with one in the room, *litariei* — one should wake that person up. *V'leima lei tzachina maya* — and one should say to him: I am thirsty for water. By involving another person in the situation, one averts the danger, since *shedim* do not accost people when they are not alone.

If, however, no other person is present, the *gemara* prescribes an alternative. *Nekarkesh be'kantamah de'chatzva* — one should bang on the lid of a jug, producing a sound. *V'neima le'nafshei* — and one should recite to oneself a specific formula: calling out one's own name — *ploni bar plonis*, so-and-so the son of so-and-so — one then invokes the warning, *amra lach imach* — your mother said to you — to be wary of the *shavrizi*. The incantation then proceeds to say the word *shavrizi* and progressively abbreviates it: *shavrizi, brizi, rizi, yizi, rizi*, removing a letter with each repetition until the word is consumed. This diminishing formula, a type of *lachash* or incantation, is understood to neutralize and drive away the *shed* known as *shavrizi* — whom Rashi identifies as dwelling in white cups — and thereby protect the solitary night-time drinker from harm.
