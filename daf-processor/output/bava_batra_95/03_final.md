# Bava Batra 95 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Din vs. Knas Framework

### 4% Threshold Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָאוֹנָאָה; פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁתוּת – נִקְנֶה מִקָּח, יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת – בָּטֵל מִקָּח. שְׁתוּת – קָנָה, וּמַחְזִיר אוֹנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a support for Rav Huna's ruling from a *baraita*: **The** *halakha* of price **exploitation** is that if the disparity is **less than one-sixth** of the value of the merchandise, the **merchandise is acquired** immediately and the sum of the exploitation need not be returned. If the disparity is **greater than one-sixth,** then **the transaction is nullified.** If the disparity is precisely **one-sixth,** the buyer has **acquired** the merchandise, **and** the one who benefited from the exploitation **returns** the entire sum of the **exploitation.**

The discussion on this *daf* continues from a preceding analysis of the tolerable amount of contaminant permitted within a quantity of grain. The threshold that had been established is approximately four percent: if the proportion of dross or foreign matter within a grain purchase exceeds that level, a question arises regarding the seller's liability. Specifically, is the seller obligated to reimburse the buyer for the entire amount of contamination, or only for the amount exceeding the tolerable threshold?

### Din vs. Knas Models

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? לַיהְדַּר עַד פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁתוּת! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, כׇּל הֵיכִי דְּבָעֵי לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי – כּוּלַּהּ מַהְדַּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof: **Why,** in the case where the disparity is precisely one-sixth, is the entire sum of the exploitation returned? Instead, **let him return** only a small amount of the exploitation **until** the difference is **less than one-sixth.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from** the fact that he must return the entire sum that **when one is required to return** part of a sale because of a discrepancy that is beyond the acceptable limit of deviation, **one is required to return** the **entire** discrepancy and not just the amount that is beyond the acceptable limit. This supports Rav Huna's ruling.

The *Gemara* frames this question as turning on one of two competing conceptual models. The first is *din* — that is, the buyer is fundamentally entitled to a full, clean quantity of grain. Within tolerable limits, however, he is understood to be *mochel*, willing to forgo his strict entitlement, since a small amount of contaminant is an ordinary feature of grain commerce. Once the contamination surpasses that limit, he is no longer *mochel* at all, and therefore the full quantity must be remedied.

The second model is *knas* — a penalty-based deterrent. Under this reading, it might actually be the case that grain inherently contains some measure of impurity, and the buyer is only entitled to a product within a certain range. Even when the tolerable threshold is exceeded, strict *din* might require only that the excess be corrected. Nevertheless, in order to prevent deliberate adulteration — since a seller who faced liability only for the overage would have no disincentive to mix in dross up to the very limit — the Rabbis imposed a *knas* requiring full restitution. It is not the underlying law that demands full correction; it is a rabbinic penalty designed to deter abuse.

### Im Ba Linafot Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, מֵעִיקָּרָא שָׁוֶה בְּשָׁוֶה אֲמַר לֵיהּ, מִיהוּ, פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁתוּת לָא יְדִיעָה בְּמָנֶה, וּמָחֵיל אִינִישׁ; שְׁתוּת – יְדִיעָה, וְלָא מָחֵיל אִינִישׁ, יָתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת – מִקָּח טָעוּת הוּא, וּבָטֵל מִקָּח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of exploitation, the seller **initially said to** the buyer that he would sell the merchandise for a sum **equal to its value.** Any price difference should be unacceptable. **But** a disparity of **less than one-sixth is not recognizable in** a sale worth **one hundred dinars, and a person** will **forgo** it. By contrast, a disparity of **one-sixth is considered** significant, **and a person will not forgo** it. Consequently, the entire sum of the exploitation must be returned. If the disparity is **greater than one-sixth, it is a mistaken transaction and the transaction is nullified.** By contrast, in the case of Rav Huna's ruling, the buyer is aware that it is normal to have a certain proportion of impurities mixed in and accepted that possibility from the outset. Accordingly, even if the proportion of impurities was greater than the acceptable limit, it might be sufficient if the seller takes back only the quantity of impurities above the acceptable limit.

One additional point deserves attention in framing this discussion. The *Rashbam* reads the *Gemara*'s ruling from the buyer's perspective: if the buyer wishes to sift out the contaminant, he must sift the entire quantity, and the seller must reimburse him for the full extent of the impurity. However, the *Gemara*'s actual language — *im ba linafot, menafe et kullah* — admits of a slightly different framing. Rather than focusing on the buyer's act of sifting and seeking reimbursement, one might understand this as placing the obligation on the seller: once a problem has been identified, the buyer can demand that the seller himself rectify it, and the question then becomes how thoroughly that rectification must be carried out.

### FDA Analogy

This alternative framing is illuminated by a practical analogy. Regulatory standards for food products — such as those of the FDA — acknowledge that certain contaminants cannot be eliminated entirely from the production process. Accordingly, a tolerable threshold is established. When a producer is found to have exceeded that threshold, the regulatory response is not to demand the impossible, namely complete elimination, but to require reduction to within the acceptable range. Implicit in this framework is the recognition that some level of contamination is an unavoidable reality, and that the tolerable threshold is set with that reality in mind. This is precisely the logic of *im ba linafot, menafe et kullah* when framed as the seller's obligation: you have exceeded the threshold; I demand that you reduce it, and once reduction is required, you must bring it fully into compliance.

## Grain vs. Ona'ah

### Ona'ah Price Bands

The *Gemara* now introduces a comparison between the grain-contaminant rule and the laws of *ona'ah* — overcharging in a commercial transaction. The rules of *ona'ah* operate across three price bands. If the seller charges less than one-sixth above the market price (*pachos mi-shtus*), the sale is fully binding and no reimbursement is required, since the deviation falls within a tolerable range. If the overcharge exceeds one-sixth (*yoter mi-shtus*), the sale is void entirely — *batel mekach* — and the buyer recovers the full purchase price. If the overcharge is exactly one-sixth (*shtus*), the sale stands, but the seller must return the precise amount of the overcharge, *machazir ona'ah*.

### Full Reimbursement Principle

The *Gemara* raises a pointed challenge. In the case of *ona'ah*, once the overcharge reaches exactly one-sixth and the seller is required to return money, why must he return the full amount of the overcharge? Why not return only the marginal amount that pushed the price from just below a sixth to exactly a sixth — in other words, just enough to bring it back below the tolerable threshold? The *Gemara* answers: *kol heicha deba'i lehadurei, kula mehadar* — wherever there is an obligation to return at all, one must return everything. Once the seller is no longer within the tolerable range, the governing principle reasserts itself: the transaction should have been at the proper market price. The full excess, not merely the marginal overage, must therefore be returned.

The *Gemara* now attempts to leverage this principle in favor of the grain case. Just as in *ona'ah*, once the tolerable limit is exceeded, one returns it all — so too in the case of grain, once the contamination exceeds the threshold, the seller should be obligated to remediate the entire contamination, not merely the excess above four percent. But the *Gemara* immediately rejects this comparison: *hachi hashta?* — what kind of comparison is that?

### Presupposition Difference

The *Gemara* explains the fundamental distinction. In the case of *ona'ah*, the underlying assumption of every sale is *meikara shaveh beshaveh* — from the outset, the seller represents that he is transacting at the fair market price. When I sell you an object, you understand you are paying the correct price. Deviations below one-sixth are tolerated not because they reflect an inherent feature of commerce, but because people make honest mistakes and the deviation is small enough that parties implicitly forgive it. But in principle, the price should have been correct all along. Therefore, once the deviation becomes intolerable — once the seller is required to make restitution — the starting principle is restored: the buyer should have paid the market price, nothing more. The full excess is therefore owed.

The grain case is structurally different. Grain is a product that inherently and unavoidably contains some measure of impurity. It is not practically possible, within the economics of ordinary grain commerce, to deliver a bag that is one hundred percent free of dross. The buyer knows this going in. What he purchases, from the very outset, is not pure grain but grain containing some amount of contaminant — between zero and four percent, as the tolerable range. His expectation is not of perfect grain, and his entitlement does not extend to perfect grain.

This is the point the *Gemara* makes, and it is the reason the grain case cannot be assimilated to *ona'ah*. In *ona'ah*, the baseline expectation is a transaction at the correct price, and any excess above that baseline is, in principle, an error. In the grain case, the baseline expectation already incorporates a certain level of contaminant. When contamination exceeds the tolerable threshold, the buyer has received somewhat more dross than he bargained for — but he did not bargain for zero dross. The principle to which one returns, once the limit is breached, is not purity but the tolerable range. This is why *im ba linafot, menafe et kullah* means reduction to the threshold, not elimination of all impurity.

## Planted Field Case

### 10% Tolerance Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵרוֹ לִיטַּע – הֲרֵי זֶה מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר בּוֹרִיּוֹת לְמֵאָה. יוֹתֵר מִכָּאן – מְגַלְגְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a support for Rav Huna's ruling from a *baraita*: When **one receives a field from another** under a contract **to plant** trees in it, then **this** field owner **accepts upon himself** that there may be **ten deficient trees per** every **hundred** trees planted, as he is aware that not every tree planted will necessarily flourish. If the number of deficient trees is **more than this,** the court **imposes upon him** the responsibility to replace **all** of those trees, and not only the number of trees above the acceptable limit. This supports Rav Huna's ruling.

The *Gemara* advances to the next *tashma* — a case involving the planting of a field. The scenario involves a contractor who is hired to plant a field with trees at the proper density. After the job is completed, it is discovered that ten out of every hundred trees are non-productive — they have failed to flower or bear fruit. The *Gemara* establishes that up to ten out of every hundred defective trees are within tolerable limits and require no correction. However, if there are eleven or more defective trees out of a hundred — *yoter mikan* — then *megavlin alav et hakol*: the contractor must replace not merely the excess above ten, but all eleven, or however many defective trees there are.

### Rashbam's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כׇּל יָתֵר מִכָּאן, כְּבָא לִיטַּע מִתְּחִלָּה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** There is no proof from this case, because **any** time there are **more than this** number of deficient trees, the overall area that contains the deficient trees is of a size equivalent to a whole field. Therefore, the contractor is **comparable to one who comes to plant** a whole field **from the outset,** who has not fulfilled his remit if he plants only a few trees; rather, he must plant the entire area. But in the case of Rav Huna's ruling, the impurities never constitute an independent unit; consequently, it might be sufficient if the seller takes back only the quantity of impurities that is above the acceptable limit.

The *Gemara* gives the following response, citing Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: *kol yeter mikan ke-ba lata'at betchila dami* — any case where there are more than ten defective trees is treated as though one is doing a new planting from scratch. The *Rashbam* explains this principle as follows. Imagine the field with trees planted at full appropriate density, and among them a cluster of eleven defective ones. Even if they are not contiguous, one may conceptually identify the section of the field occupied by those eleven trees. That section, for all practical purposes, is an unplanted part of the field. The contractor's original obligation was to plant the entire field. If he had simply left that section bare, the owner would immediately tell him he had not yet completed his work and demand that the remaining section be planted — at which point the contractor would be required to plant it with full density. The *Rashbam* argues that this is effectively the situation: once there are eleven defective trees, that portion of the field is deemed unplanted, and the contractor's obligation to plant it fully — including replacing all the defective trees — remains unfulfilled.

### Tosafot's Job Doctrine

*Tosafot* offer a substantially different explanation, which many find more compelling. Their approach rests on a distinction between two stages of the contractor relationship: the period during which the job is still in progress, and the period after the job has been completed and accepted.

During the planting process — which takes place over days and is therefore extended in time — if a tree is discovered to be withering or defective, the contractor cannot invoke the ten-percent tolerance rule and walk away. While the job is still ongoing, the contractor's fundamental obligation is to plant all healthy trees, and any defective one discovered mid-job must be uprooted and replaced.

The ten-percent tolerance rule applies only after the job is complete: the contractor has finished planting, been paid, and departed. Months later, if a few trees are found to be non-productive, that falls within the tolerable margin and the contractor cannot be recalled.

However, once there are eleven defective trees — exceeding the tolerable threshold — the contractor must return to address at least one of them. And this is the crux of *Tosafot*'s insight: the moment the contractor is obligated to return to correct even one tree, we must conclude that his job was, in fact, never truly completed. A job that still requires attention is an ongoing job. And as long as the job is ongoing, the principle that applies during the course of work reasserts itself — every defective tree must be replaced. The full set of eleven must therefore be corrected. This is precisely how contractor relationships work: while work remains in progress, any identified deficiency carries an obligation to remedy; it is only after true completion that limited tolerance applies.

### Disease Transmission

A further consideration was raised: the tolerable threshold may also have a basis in the practical concern for disease transmission. If defective trees are diseased, and if the density of diseased trees within an orchard remains below a certain threshold, the remainder of the orchard may be protected. But once that density threshold is crossed, the diseased trees pose a genuine threat to the health of the entire orchard. On this reading, the rule requiring full replacement once eleven trees are defective is not merely a question of what the contractor promised or whether his job is complete — it may also reflect the concern that beyond a certain density, the diseased trees actively endanger the rest of the orchard. This consideration remains suggestive rather than resolved, but it offers an additional dimension to the rationale underlying the threshold rule.

## Wine Cellar Case

### Wine 10% Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן וְכוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן״ סְתָם – קַשְׁיָא; אִי דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״מַרְתֵּף זֶה שֶׁל יַיִן״ – קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: When purchasing **a cellar** containing barrels **of wine,** one accepts upon himself that up to ten barrels of souring wine may be present in each hundred barrels purchased. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of the sale? **If** one **said to** the buyer: I am selling to you **a wine cellar, without specification** of which cellar he meant, it is **difficult,** as the Gemara will soon explain. And **if he said to him:** I am selling to you **this** particular **wine cellar,** it is **difficult.**

The *Gemara* turns to the wine cellar case presented in the *Mishna*. A seller who sells a wine cellar is permitted to have up to ten barrels out of every hundred that are of inferior quality. This appears generous as a tolerance — but the *Gemara* immediately asks: *heichi dami?* — what exactly is the scenario being described? The question arises because a *Braita* appears to present three distinct scenarios, and the *Mishna*'s ruling seems inconsistent with all three.

### Braita Three Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״מַרְתֵּף זֶה״ – קַשְׁיָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ יָפֶה. ״מַרְתֵּף זֶה שֶׁל יַיִן אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן הַנִּמְכָּר בַּחֲנוּת. ״מַרְתֵּף זֶה אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלּוֹ חוֹמֶץ, הִגִּיעוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **If he said to him:** I am selling to you **this** particular wine **cellar,** it is **difficult, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one said to a buyer: **I am selling to you a wine cellar,** then he is required to **give him wine that is all** of **good** quality, i.e., the buyer does not have to accept any quantity of souring wine. If he said: **I am selling to you this** particular **wine cellar,** then he may **give him** the **wine** that is in his possession, even if it is of the quality **that is sold in the shops,** i.e., it is beginning to sour. If he said: **I am selling to you this** particular **cellar,** without mentioning the word: Wine, then **even** if **everything** he gives him is wine that had turned into **vinegar, it has come to** the buyer and the sale is valid. The mishna's ruling that the buyer must accept that up to ten percent of the wine might be souring does not accord with any of the rulings of the *baraita*.

The *Braita* sets out three cases based on the language used in the transaction. First, *martef shel yayin stam* — if the seller says simply "a cellar of wine," without further qualification, then the wine must all be of good quality, *yayin she-kulo yafeh*. Second, *martef zeh shel yayin* — if the seller says "this cellar of wine," indicating the specific cellar as it stands, then what is owed is *yayin ha-nimkar be-chanut* — the quality of wine sold in the store, which is a lower standard, identified with *kosesot*, wine that is souring and on the verge of becoming vinegar. Third, if the seller says simply *martef zeh* — "this cellar" — without specifying wine at all, the buyer receives whatever is there, even if it has turned to vinegar.

The *Rashbam* notes, and it is implicit in the *Gemara*, that *kosesot* is equivalent to *yayin ha-nimkar be-chanut*: it refers to wine that is still technically wine and still drinkable, but is in the process of souring. This is the grade of wine one encounters in an ordinary shop, as distinct from what one would obtain from a dedicated wine merchant or premium cellar.

### Mishna-Braita Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ ״מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן״ – סְתָם, וְתָנֵי בְּרֵישָׁא דְבָרַיְיתָא: וּמְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר קוֹסְסוֹת לְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna concerns a case **when he said to** the buyer: I am selling to you **a wine cellar, without specification** of which cellar he was selling. **And** emend the *baraita* and **teach** the following qualification **in the first clause of the *baraita*: And** the buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **ten** barrels of **souring** wine may be present **in** each **hundred** barrels purchased.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between the *Mishna* and the *Braita* in the following way. The *Mishna*'s ruling — permitting ten percent of barrels to be of inferior quality — does not conflict with the *Braita*'s requirement that *martef shel yayin stam* produce all good wine. The *Braita* is addressing the question of quality level: what grade of wine did the buyer's language entitle him to? The *Mishna* is addressing a different question: given that the seller promised a certain quality, how much deviation from that standard is tolerable before liability attaches? Thus, *martef shel yayin stam* requires all wine to be of good quality in principle, but the *Mishna* permits a ten-percent tolerance for barrels that fall short. The two sources are not in conflict; they address different aspects of the transaction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִסְתָם מִי מְקַבֵּל?! וְהָא תָּאנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הַמּוֹכֵר חָבִית יַיִן לַחֲבֵרוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ יָפֶה! שָׁאנֵי חָבִית, דְּכוּלָּא חַד חַמְרָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this addition: **But** if he sold the buyer a wine cellar **without specification** of which one he was selling, **does** the buyer **accept** upon himself any souring wine at all? **Didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach: One who sells a barrel of wine to another** must **give him wine that is all** of **good** quality? The Gemara answers: **A barrel is different, because** the wine inside **is all one** body of **wine** of the same quality.

The *Gemara* then notes that Rabbi Chiya's *Braita* — which states that selling a single barrel of wine requires the wine to be entirely good — poses no contradiction, since a single barrel is a unified item: it is either all one *sug* (quality grade) or it is not. It is structurally incapable of being partially good and partially not, unlike a cellar of many separate barrels.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנֵי רַב זְבִיד דְּבֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ יָפֶה. ״מַרְתֵּף זֶה שֶׁל יַיִן אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ יָפֶה, וּמְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר קוֹסְסוֹת לְמֵאָה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara once again challenges the addition: **But didn't Rav Zevid teach** a *baraita* of the **school of Rabbi Oshaya:** If one says to a buyer: **I am selling to you a wine cellar,** then he is required to **give him wine that is all** of **good** quality? Similarly, if he said: **I am selling to you this** particular **wine cellar,** then he is required to **give him wine that is all** of **good** quality, but the buyer **accepts upon himself ten souring** barrels **per hundred** barrels.

The *Gemara* then introduces the *Braita* of Rav Zvid, from the school of Rabbi Oshaya. In Rav Zvid's version, *martef shel yayin* — without *zeh* — requires all good wine, consistent with the first *Braita*. But *martef zeh shel yayin* — with *zeh* — still requires all good wine, while nonetheless permitting ten percent of barrels to be *kosesot*. This conflicts with the first *Braita*, which understood *martef zeh shel yayin* to allow the entire cellar to be of the *yayin ha-nimkar be-chanut* standard. There is thus an apparent *machloket* between the two *braitot* as to what the inclusion of *zeh* implies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶהוּ אוֹצָר שֶׁשָּׁנוּ חֲכָמִים בְּמִשְׁנָתֵינוּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Zevid concludes: **And this is** the case of the **storeroom,** i.e., the cellar, **that the Sages taught in the mishna.** It is clear from this *baraita* both that it contradicts the suggested addition to the previous *baraita*, and that the mishna concerns a case where one specified which wine cellar he was selling.

The *Gemara* initially proposes that the *Mishna* follows the *Braita* of Rav Zvid — which itself, in its closing line, states explicitly that the ruling about *martef zeh shel yayin* with ten percent tolerance is precisely the case the Sages discussed in the *Mishna*. Rav Zvid's *Braita* thus self-identifies as the source for the *Mishna*'s position.

## Rav Zvid Modifier Framework

### Linguistic Modifiers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ ״זֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** as indicated by the *baraita*, **the mishna also** concerns a case where one **says to** the buyer: I am selling you **this** particular cellar, and because he specified a particular cellar the buyer accepts upon himself the possibility that up to ten barrels per hundred might be sour.

Accepting that the *Mishna* follows the *Braita* of Rav Zvid, the *Gemara* must now reconcile the two *braitot* rather than simply declaring a debate between them. The resolution turns on identifying linguistic modifiers embedded in the transaction — specifically the word *zeh* ("this," indicating an as-is purchase) and the word *lemiksa* (indicating the buyer's intention to use the wine for cooking, which requires the wine to last and therefore imposes a higher quality demand).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא ״זֶה״ אַ״זֶּה״!
>
> **Translation:** Having retracted the addition to the first *baraita*, the Gemara asks: If so, it is **difficult** to reconcile the first *baraita*, which rules that if the seller specified that he was selling **this** cellar, he must provide wine that is all of good quality, with the second *baraita*, that of Rav Zevid, which rules with regard to the same case, in which the seller specified he was selling **this** cellar, that the buyer accepts that up to ten barrels per hundred may contain souring wine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה, הָא דְּלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה. דְּרַב זְבִיד – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה, בָּרַיְיתָא – דְּלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** *baraita* is referring to a case where the buyer **said to him** that he needs the wine **for cooking,** and therefore he requires good-quality wine, whereas **that** *baraita* is referring to a case where the buyer did **not say to him** that he needs the wine **for cooking.** The Gemara elaborates: The second *baraita*, taught by **Rav Zevid,** is referring to a case where the buyer **said** that he needs the wine **for cooking,** whereas the first ***baraita*** is referring to a case where the buyer did **not say to him** that he requires it **for cooking.**

The *Gemara* proposes that the difference between the two *braitot* in the case of *martef zeh shel yayin* turns on whether the buyer specified *lemiksa*. If the buyer said he wanted the wine for cooking — *lemiksa* — he implicitly required the wine to remain usable over a prolonged period. *Kosesot* wine, on the verge of souring, would spoil before it could be consumed gradually over a cooking season. In such a case, even when the buyer said *zeh*, the wine must be of good quality, with only ten percent tolerance for *kosesot*. But if he did not say *lemiksa*, it is possible the buyer intended to use or consume the wine quickly, and thus it could all be of the lower *kosesot* standard.

The *Rashbam* notes that this distinction is not primarily about taste but about longevity: *kosesot* wine is problematic not merely because it is lower quality but because it is approaching the point of becoming vinegar, and a buyer who needs wine to last an extended period cannot be served by such wine.

### Rashbam vs. Gemara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ, ״מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן״ וְאָמַר לוֹ לְמִקְפָּה – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ יָפֶה. ״מַרְתֵּף זֶה שֶׁל יַיִן״ וְאָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ יָפֶה, וּמְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר קוֹסְסוֹת לְמֵאָה. ״מַרְתֵּף זֶה שֶׁל יַיִן״ וְלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן הַנִּמְכָּר בַּחֲנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Therefore,** the *halakha* is that if one said that he is selling: **A wine cellar, and** the buyer **said to him:** I need the wine **for cooking,** then the seller is obligated to **give him wine that is all** of **good** quality. If one said that he is selling: **This** particular **wine cellar, and** the buyer **said to him:** I need the wine **for cooking,** then the seller is obligated to **give him wine that is all** of **good** quality, but the buyer **accepts upon himself ten** barrels of **souring** wine **in** each **hundred** barrels purchased. If one said that he is selling: **This** particular **wine cellar,** and the buyer **did not say to him:** I need the wine **for cooking,** then the seller may **give to him** the **wine** that is in his possession, even if it is of a quality **that is sold in the shops,** i.e., it is beginning to sour.

At this point the *Rashbam* makes an important clarificatory remark about how to understand the *Gemara*'s language. When the *Gemara* says that one *amora* "infers" his position from the *Braita* of Rav Zvid and another "infers" his position from the earlier *Braita*, the natural reading suggests that the textual cue in each *Braita* is the basis for each *amora*'s conclusion. The *Rashbam* argues, however, that this is not the correct reading. Each *amora* arrived at his conclusion on the basis of principled reasoning about the underlying case. Having formed that view on principled grounds, each then selects the *Braita* that supports his position as the textual anchor. The *diyuk* from the *Braita* is not the source of the conclusion but its evidentiary support — a confirmation chosen after the fact, not a derivation.

### Lemikza Insertion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן״ וְלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה, מַאי? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא; חַד אָמַר: מְקַבֵּל, וְחַד אָמַר: לָא מְקַבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If one said that he is selling: **A wine cellar, and** the buyer **did not say to him:** I need the wine **for cooking, what** is the *halakha*? **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree about this. One says:** The buyer **must accept** ten barrels of souring wine per hundred, **and** the other **one says:** The buyer **need not accept** any barrels of souring wine.

After establishing the *lemiksa* framework, the *Gemara* considers what happens when a buyer says *martef shel yayin* — without *zeh* — and does not add *lemiksa*. Does the seller still owe entirely good wine, or must the buyer accept a ten-percent tolerance? This is the subject of a dispute between Rav Acha and Ravina: one holds that even without the *zeh*, absence of *lemiksa* means the buyer must accept ten percent off; the other holds that *martef shel yayin* without *zeh* always demands fully good wine, regardless of whether *lemiksa* was said.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר מְקַבֵּל – דָּיֵיק מִדְּרַב זְבִיד, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ יָפֶה, וְאוֹקִימְנָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה; טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה, הָא לָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה – מְקַבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their reasoning. **The one who says** that the buyer **must accept** ten barrels of souring wine per hundred **infers** this **through** a precise reading of the *baraita* **of Rav Zevid, as it teaches:** If one says: **I am selling to you a wine cellar,** then he is required to **give him wine that is all** of **good** quality. **And we interpreted** this *baraita* as referring to a case **where** the buyer **said to him:** I need the wine **for cooking.** The Gemara explains the inference: **The** only **reason** that he must provide him with wine that is all of good quality is **that** the buyer **said to him** that he needed it **for cooking.** By inference, where the buyer **does not say to him:** I need it **for cooking,** the buyer **must accept** ten barrels of souring wine per hundred.

Each position draws support from a different *braita*. Those who hold that the buyer must accept ten percent even without *lemiksa* point to the *Braita* of Rav Zvid: having introduced *lemiksa* as the operative distinction within the *martef zeh* case, it follows that *lemiksa* is what drove the "all good" ruling, and in its absence — even in a case without *zeh* — the buyer would have to accept some tolerance. Those who hold the opposite position point to the earlier *Braita*, where *martef shel yayin* without *zeh* clearly requires all good wine, without any reference to *lemiksa*. Absent *zeh*, the wine must all be good.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא מְקַבֵּל – דָּיֵיק מִבָּרַיְיתָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ יָפֶה, וְאוֹקֵימְנָא דְּלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the one who says** that the buyer **need not accept** any barrels of souring wine **infers** this **through** a precise reading **of** the first ***baraita*, as it teaches:** If one says: **I am selling to you a wine cellar,** then he is required to **give him wine that is all** of **good** quality. **And we interpreted** this *baraita* as referring to a case **where he did not say to him:** I need the wine **for cooking,** and even so, the buyer need not accept any barrels of souring wine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּדָיֵיק מִדְּרַב זְבִיד, קַשְׁיָא בָּרַיְיתָא! חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה; הָא לָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה – מְקַבֵּל. וּ״מַרְתֵּף זֶה שֶׁל יַיִן״ וְלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה – נוֹתֵן לוֹ יַיִן הַנִּמְכָּר בַּחֲנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** then, **according to the one who infers** his opinion **through** a precise reading of the *baraita* **of Rav Zevid, the** first ***baraita*** poses **a difficulty.** The Gemara answers that according to his opinion, the *baraita* **is incomplete and this** is what **it is teaching:** If one says: I am selling to you a cellar of wine, then he is required to give him wine that is all of good quality. **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** the buyer **said to him:** I need the wine **for cooking.** By inference, where the buyer **did not say to him:** I need the wine **for cooking,** the buyer **must accept** ten barrels of souring wine per hundred. **And** if one said that he is selling: **This** particular **wine cellar, and** the buyer **did not say to him:** I need the wine **for cooking,** then one may **give to him** the **wine** that is in his possession, even if it is of a quality **that is sold in the shops,** i.e., it is beginning to sour.

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty for the first position — those who infer from Rav Zvid's *Braita* — by applying the technique of *chasuri mechasra*: the original *Braita* must be read as though words are missing. Specifically, the word *lemikze* is inserted into the first *Braita*. The *Braita* is to be understood as follows: when it says that *martef shel yayin* requires all good wine, it is speaking of a case where the buyer specified *lemikze*. In the absence of *lemikze*, even the *martef shel yayin* case would permit some tolerance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּדָיֵיק מִבָּרַיְיתָא, קַשְׁיָא דְּרַב זְבִיד – דְּאוֹקֵימְנָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה, הָא לָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה – מְקַבֵּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who infers** his opinion **through** a precise reading of the first ***baraita*,** the *baraita* **of Rav Zevid** poses **a difficulty, as we interpreted it** as referring to a case **where** the buyer **said to him:** I need the wine **for cooking.** And this opinion inferred that if the buyer **did not say to him:** I need it **for cooking,** then the buyer **must accept** ten barrels of souring wine per hundred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא הַדִּין דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה – לָא מְקַבֵּל, וְהַאי דְּאוֹקֵימְנָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לְמִקְפָּה, מִשּׁוּם דְּקַשְׁיָא ״זֶה״ אַ״זֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The same is true** according to both *baraitot*, **that even where** the buyer **did not say to him:** I need the wine **for cooking,** the buyer **need not accept** ten barrels of souring wine. **And** there is no proof from the fact **that we interpreted** the *baraita* as referring to a case **where he said to him:** I need it **for cooking,** since we did so only **due to the difficulty** of the contradiction between the ruling of the first *baraita* in the case where the seller specified that he was selling **this** cellar and the ruling of the *baraita* of Rav Zevid in the same case where the seller specified he was selling **this** cellar. Due to that difficulty, the entire *baraita* was interpreted as referring to a case where the buyer said: I need the wine for cooking, despite the fact that in the case where he did not mention a specific cellar it makes no difference whether or not he said so.

### Marker Balancing Logic

The *Gemara*'s resolution thus produces the following balanced framework. Each transaction carries linguistic markers that can be classified as either "good" (tending toward higher quality and longevity requirements) or "bad" (tending toward as-is acceptance and lower expectations). The word *zeh* is a "bad" marker: it signals an as-is purchase and relaxes the quality requirement. The word *lemikze* is a "good" marker: it signals a requirement for longevity and imposes higher quality demands.

When both markers point toward quality — *martef shel yayin* without *zeh*, combined with *lemikze* — all the wine must be good, with no tolerance for inferior barrels. When both markers point away from quality — *martef zeh shel yayin* without *lemikze*, that is, with *zeh* but without *lemikze* — the entire cellar may be of the lower *yayin ha-nimkar be-chanut* standard. When one marker points toward quality and the other away — either *martef shel yayin* without *zeh* but also without *lemikze*, or *martef zeh shel yayin* with *zeh* but with *lemikze* — the result is a ten-percent tolerance for inferior wine. The two cases in the middle are equivalent: one good marker and one bad marker yields the same outcome regardless of which marker is which.

The *Gemara* then addresses the remaining question for the second position — those who infer from the original *Braita* and hold that *martef shel yayin* always requires all good wine regardless of *lemikze*. For them, the word *lemikze* introduced into Rav Zvid's *Braita* is not what drives the ruling in the *martef shel yayin* case; that case requires all good wine in any event. The *lemikze* word was inserted specifically to reconcile the contradiction within the *braitot* concerning the *martef zeh* case. The first case — *martef shel yayin* without *zeh* — requires all good wine whether *lemikze* is said or not; the *lemikze* distinction operates only in the *martef zeh* scenarios.

## Wine Souring Brachot

### Kossos = Borei Pri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: יַיִן הַנִּמְכָּר בַּחֲנוּת – מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו ״בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הַגֶּפֶן״. וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: גַּבֵּי חַמְרָא דְּאַקְרֵים לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos wine that is sold in the shops, the Gemara considers additional *halakhot* pertaining to such wine: **Rav Yehuda says: Over wine** of the same quality as that **which is sold in the shops, one recites the** standard **blessing** for wine: **Who creates fruit of the vine.** Despite the fact that such wine is not of the highest quality, it is still regarded as wine. **And Rav Ḥisda said: Over wine that has formed a film** as it begins to sour, **why do I** need to recite the blessing for wine? Since it has begun to sour, it is no longer regarded as wine. Instead, one should recite the generic blessing recited over foods of lower importance: By Whose word all things came to be.

The *Gemara* now shifts to a separate but related discussion concerning the appropriate *bracha* to recite over wine that is in the process of souring. Rav Yehuda holds that *yayin ha-nimkar be-chanut* — the store-quality souring wine that constitutes *kosesot* — still receives the *bracha* of *borei pri hagafen*. Since such wine still falls within the halachic category of *yayin*, the full *bracha* over wine is appropriate.

Rav Chisda disagrees and rules more stringently. He argues: *gabei chamra d'akrim, lama li?* — who needs wine that is souring? The implication is that wine so significantly degraded in quality is no longer what a person of discernment would recognize as proper wine. Such wine no longer deserves the *bracha* of *borei pri hagafen* and should instead receive the generic *bracha* of *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*.

### Severely Soured = Shehakol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: עַל הַפַּת שֶׁעִפְּשָׁה, וְעַל הַיַּיִן שֶׁהִקְרִים, וְעַל תַּבְשִׁיל שֶׁעִבְּרָה צוּרָתוֹ – אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיֶה בִּדְבָרוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Yehuda's opinion. It is taught in a *baraita*: **Over bread that has become moldy, and over wine that has formed a film, and over a cooked dish that has spoiled, one recites** the blessing: **By Whose word all things came to be.** Since these foods have partially spoiled, it is inappropriate to recite the specific blessings designated for such foods in their fresh state.

### Form Loss Downgrades

A *Braita* is then cited in support. The *Braita* states that over bread that has become moldy, over wine that is souring, and over a cooked dish (*tavshil*) — which the *Rashbam* identifies as a grain-based or rice-based dish — that has lost its proper form and has similarly deteriorated, one recites *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*. One does not recite *hamotzi*, *borei pri hagafen*, or *mezonot* respectively. The reason is that while such foods remain edible (otherwise no *bracha* at all would be recited), they no longer exist in their proper intended state. Having lost the form that defines them as the foods they are, they no longer merit the elevated and specific *bracha* associated with that form. This *Braita* appears to support Rav Chisda's position that wine which has soured sufficiently should receive only *shehakol*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: מוֹדֶה רַב יְהוּדָה בְּפוּרְצְמָא דְּמִיזְדַּבַּן אַקַּרְנָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said: Rav Yehuda concedes** that one does not recite the blessing of: Who creates fruit of the vine, **on wine made from grape seeds that is sold on the** street **corners.** It is to such significantly inferior wine that the *baraita* refers. Wine sold in the shops still has the taste and appearance of wine, so one should recite the standard blessing for wine.

Rav Zvid then notes that even Rav Yehuda concedes the point in a case he calls *furtzma* — understood by the *Rashbam* as wine that has deteriorated very severely, or, in the version of Rabbeinu Chananel, *partzufa*, meaning wine whose very appearance has changed, perhaps by discoloration or other visible transformation. Even if Rav Yehuda permits *borei pri hagafen* over standard *kosesot* — souring but still store-sold — he would agree that wine at this further stage of deterioration, so degraded that it is sold only from street-corner vendors rather than established shops, would receive only *shehakol*. The dispute between Rav Yehuda and Rav Chisda is therefore a question of degree: at what point along the souring spectrum does the wine lose its status as *yayin* sufficiently to forfeit the *bracha* of *borei pri hagafen*?

### Grape Juice Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: הָא רַב יְהוּדָה, הָא רַב חִסְדָּא; מָר כְּמַאן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר לֵיהּ: מַתְנִיתָא יָדַעְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: This** is the opinion of **Rav Yehuda,** and **this** is the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda. In accordance with whose** opinion **does the Master hold?** Rav Yosef **said to him: I know a *baraita*** from which it is possible to derive the *halakha*.

This discussion prompts an observation regarding grape juice, which bears on the nature of *yayin* more broadly. While the *Gemara* elsewhere indicates that even one-day-old wine — before fermentation has fully occurred — still receives *borei pri hagafen*, grape juice presents a more complex case. Commercial grape juice has been pasteurized, a process that destroys the yeasts responsible for fermentation. It is therefore not merely unfermented wine temporarily in an early stage, but a product that is constitutionally incapable of fermenting and becoming wine. Whether such a product constitutes *yayin* for purposes of *borei pri hagafen*, *kiddush*, and related obligations is a genuine halachic question.

The issue was addressed, in one form, by the *Tzitz Eliezer*, who was asked about reconstituted grape juice — produced by removing water, shipping the solids, and reconstituting with water. His response was pointed: before worrying about reconstituted grape juice, one should ask whether plain grape juice itself receives *borei pri hagafen* or *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*. The implication is that the *bracha* status of grape juice is not self-evident and should not be assumed.

## Vinegar Three-Day Rule

### Barrel Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַבּוֹדֵק אֶת הֶחָבִית לִהְיוֹת מַפְרִישׁ עָלֶיהָ תְּרוּמָה וְהוֹלֵךְ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצֵאת חוֹמֶץ; כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יוֹם – וַדַּאי, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 2:8): With regard to **one who inspects a barrel** to see if it still contains enough wine **to continually** mentally **separate *teruma* from it** to exempt other untithed wine he has, until all the wine in that barrel would be *teruma* and would be given to a priest, **and afterward** the contents of the barrel **were found** to have turned to **vinegar,** which cannot be set aside as *teruma* for untithed wine, then **all three days** after he had last inspected it, it is **definitely** viewed as having been wine, and any wine for which *teruma* was separated during those days is tithed. **From that** point **onward,** more than three days after the previous inspection, it is **uncertain** as to whether it had already turned to vinegar, and any wine for which *teruma* was separated during those days is not tithed.

The *Gemara* introduces a new *Braita* in response to a question posed to Rav Yosef about how he rules in the dispute between Rav Yehuda and Rav Chisda. Rav Yosef answers that he knows a *Braita* that resolves the matter. The *Braita* addresses the following scenario: a person identifies a specific barrel of wine and designates it as the source from which he will separate *terumah* and *ma'aser* for his other wine. Since *terumah* and *ma'aser* must be separated from wine — not from vinegar — the barrel must still constitute *yayin* at the time of each separation. The person continues making these designations on an ongoing basis, drawing on the barrel repeatedly over an extended period.

At some point it is discovered that the barrel has turned to vinegar — *chometz*. The question is critical: which of the prior *terumah* and *ma'aser* designations were valid, and which were not? The *Braita* states: *kol gimmel yamim vadai* — for the first three days, the status is certain. *Mikan va-ilech safek* — from that point onward, it is a matter of doubt.

### First 3 Days Certain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים – וַדַּאי יַיִן, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** the *baraita* **saying? Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **this** is what it **is saying:** For **all** of **the first three days** following the inspection, it is **definitely** viewed as having been **wine** that had not yet turned to vinegar. **From that** point **onward,** it is **uncertain** as to whether it had already turned to vinegar. Accordingly, any wine for which *teruma* was separated after those three days by means of designating the contents of that barrel as *teruma* has an uncertain status.

The ambiguity in the *Braita* lies in what exactly is certain for those first three days. Rabbi Yochanan explains: the first three days it is certainly wine — *vadai yayin*. From that point onward, there is doubt as to whether it is still wine or has already become vinegar.

### Spoilage from Top

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? חַמְרָא – מֵעִילַּאי עָקַר, וְהַאי טַעֲימֵיהּ וְלָא עֲקַר. אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר: בָּתַר דְּטַעֲימֵיהּ עֲקַר; הָוֵה רֵיחָא חַלָּא וְטַעְמֵיהּ חַמְרָא, וְכֹל רֵיחֵיהּ חַלָּא וְטַעְמֵיהּ חַמְרָא – חַמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason?** The process in which **wine turns sour** and becomes vinegar starts **with** the wine at the **top** of the barrel; **and** it is **that** wine which **he tasted** when he inspected it, **and** at that time **it had not** yet **soured.** And even **if you say** that immediately **after he tasted it** the wine began to **sour,** during the following three days **it** would **have the odor of vinegar and its taste** would be **of wine, and anything** that **has an odor of vinegar but its taste is of wine** is regarded as **wine.**

The reason is as follows: *chamra me-ilai akar* — wine spoils from the top. When one tastes the barrel to check its status, one tastes from the top. If the wine tasted good at the top, one can be confident that the wine had not yet begun to sour, since souring initiates at the surface. Having tasted it and found it good, one may be confident that for three days following that tasting, the wine remained wine. Beyond three days, one can no longer rely on that confidence, and the status becomes uncertain.

The *Gemara* will continue from this point to explore further dimensions of this rule — including questions about how the process of souring progresses, and what precisely the three-day window is anchored to — but that analysis will be taken up in the continuation of the *sugya*.
