# Eruvin 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Teaching & Repetition

### Aharon's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, כֵּיצַד סֵדֶר מִשְׁנָה: מֹשֶׁה לָמַד מִפִּי הַגְּבוּרָה. נִכְנַס אַהֲרֹן, וְשָׁנָה לוֹ מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקוֹ, נִסְתַּלֵּק אַהֲרֹן וְיָשַׁב לִשְׂמֹאל מֹשֶׁה. נִכְנְסוּ בָּנָיו, וְשָׁנָה לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקָן. נִסְתַּלְּקוּ בָּנָיו, אֶלְעָזָר יָשַׁב לִימִין מֹשֶׁה, וְאִיתָמָר לִשְׂמֹאל אַהֲרֹן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אַהֲרֹן לִימִין מֹשֶׁה חוֹזֵר. נִכְנְסוּ זְקֵנִים וְשָׁנָה לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקָן. נִסְתַּלְּקוּ זְקֵנִים, נִכְנְסוּ כׇּל הָעָם וְשָׁנָה לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקָן. נִמְצְאוּ בְּיַד אַהֲרֹן אַרְבָּעָה, בְּיַד בָּנָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה, וּבְיַד הַזְּקֵנִים שְׁנַיִם, וּבְיַד כׇּל הָעָם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: **What was the order of teaching** the Oral Law? How was the Oral Law first taught? **Moses learned** directly **from the mouth of the Almighty. Aaron entered** and sat before him, **and Moses taught him his lesson** as he had learned it from God. **Aaron moved** aside **and sat to the left of Moses.** Aaron's **sons entered, and Moses taught them their lesson** while Aaron listened. Aaron's **sons moved** aside; **Elazar sat to the right of Moses and Itamar** sat **to the left of Aaron. Rabbi Yehuda** disagreed with the first *tanna* with regard to the seating arrangements and **said: Actually, Aaron would return to** sit to **the right of Moses. The elders entered and Moses taught them their lesson. The elders moved** aside, and **the entire nation entered and Moses taught them their lesson. Therefore, Aaron had** heard the lesson **four times, his sons** heard it **three** times, **the elders** heard it **twice, and the entire nation** heard it **once.**

The daf opens by picking up a discussion from the bottom of 54b concerning the transmission of Torah from Moshe to Aharon and the broader question of how many times one must repeat a teaching. Having established the view of Rabbi Akiva — that the obligation is not limited to a fixed number but extends until the student actually knows the material — the *gemara* now asks: *keman azla ha detanya*, whose position does the following *baraita* accord with?

The *baraita* in question concerns the seating arrangement of Aharon relative to Moshe. Rabbi Yehuda had maintained that *le'olam Aharon limin Moshe chozer* — Aharon always returned to Moshe's right whenever another person was available to occupy the left. The *gemara* now asks whether a related teaching about walking arrangements reflects the view of Rabbi Yehuda exclusively, or whether the Sages agree as well. The *baraita* states: *sheloshah she'hayu mehalchim baderech* — three people walking together should arrange themselves so that the teacher (*rav*) is in the middle, the more senior student on his right, and the less senior on his left. The logic assumes that the right side connotes greater honor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ — הָרַב בָּאֶמְצַע, וְגָדוֹל בִּימִינוֹ, וְקָטָן בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **three** people **were walking along the way, the teacher** should walk **in the middle and the greater** of the two students should be **to his right and the lesser** one should be **to his left.**

The *gemara* asks: *leima Rabbi Yehuda velo Rabbanan* — is this the view of Rabbi Yehuda alone? After all, the Sages maintained that Aharon remained on Moshe's left even after another person was present. The *gemara* responds: *afilu teima Rabbanan* — even the Sages agree in principle that the more important person belongs on the right. Their leniency with Aharon was *mishum tircha de-Aharon* — so as not to inconvenience him by requiring him to get up and switch sides once he had already settled on the left. The normative rule, however, is that the right side is the place of honor. The basis for this arrangement is rooted in the verse *lev chacham liyemino velev kesil lismolo* — "the heart of the wise is to his right, and the heart of a fool to his left." These positional conventions function as social signifiers of respect — not unlike other *halakhic* expressions of *kavod* such as *lifnei seiva takum* — and while they may appear to be mere etiquette, once established, they carry genuine normative weight.

### Rav Preida's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי פְּרִידָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא תַּלְמִידָא דַּהֲוָה תָּנֵי לֵיהּ אַרְבַּע מְאָה זִימְנֵי וְגָמַר. יוֹמָא חַד בַּעְיוּהּ לְמִלְּתָא דְמִצְוָה. תְּנָא לֵיהּ וְלָא גְּמַר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָאִידָּנָא מַאי שְׁנָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדְּהָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְמָר אִיכָּא מִילְּתָא דְּמִצְוָה — אַסְּחַאי לְדַעְתַּאי, וְכׇל שַׁעְתָּא אָמֵינָא: הַשְׁתָּא קָאֵי מָר, הַשְׁתָּא קָאֵי מָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב דַּעְתָּיךְ וְאַתְנֵי לִיךְ. הֲדַר תְּנָא לֵיהּ אַרְבַּע מְאָה זִימְנֵי אַחֲרִינֵי. נְפַקָא בַּת קָלָא וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: נִיחָא לָיךְ דְּלִיסְפּוֹ לָךְ אַרְבַּע מְאָה שְׁנֵי, אוֹ דְּתִיזְכּוֹ אַתְּ וְדָרָךְ לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי? אָמַר: דְּנִיזְכּוֹ אֲנָא וְדָרַיי לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי. אָמַר לָהֶן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: תְּנוּ לוֹ זוֹ וָזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Perida had a certain student whom he would** have to **teach four hundred times, and** only then would he **learn** the material, as he was incapable of understanding it otherwise. **One day they requested** Rabbi Perida's presence **for a mitzva matter** after the lesson. Rabbi Perida **taught** his student four hundred times as usual, **but** this time the student **did not** successfully **learn** the material. Rabbi Perida **said to him: What is different now** that you are unable to grasp the lesson? **He said to him: From the time that they said to the Master** that **there is a mitzva matter** for which he is needed, **my mind was distracted** from the lesson **and every moment I said: Now the Master will get up, now the Master will get up** to go and perform the mitzva and he will not complete the lesson. Rabbi Perida **said to him: Pay attention** this time **and I will teach you,** and know that I will not leave until you have fully mastered the lesson. **He taught him again an additional four hundred times. Due to the merit of Rabbi Perida's great devotion to his students, a Divine Voice emerged and said to him:** Is it **preferable to you that four hundred years be added** to your life, **or that you and** the rest of **your generation** will **merit the World-to-Come? He said:** I prefer **that I and my generation merit the World-to-Come. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said** to the angels: **Give him both;** he shall live a very long life and he and the rest of his generation will merit the World-to-Come.

The *gemara* then relates the celebrated story of Rav Preida and his student. Rav Preida had a particular student whom he had to teach every lesson *arba mea zimnei* — four hundred times — before the material was retained. One day, a messenger arrived informing Rav Preida that he was needed for a *mitzvah* matter. He told the messenger to wait, and proceeded to teach the lesson the requisite four hundred times — yet this time the student still could not absorb it.

Rav Preida asked him: *mah idna maishana* — why is today different? The student replied that from the moment he heard Rav Preida had been called away for a *mitzvah*, he could not concentrate: *ascha lei da'ati* — his mind was distracted, and every moment he thought, *hashta kai mar* — "he is about to leave." Rav Preida reassured him — set aside that concern, I am here — and then taught him the lesson another four hundred times, *arba mea zimnei achrinei*, until the student finally mastered it.

Upon completing this second set of four hundred repetitions, *nafka bat kol* — a heavenly voice went forth — and offered Rav Preida a choice: either he would be granted four hundred additional years of life, *deleispu lach arba mea shani*, or he and his entire generation would merit *olam haba*. This is, in a sense, a question with only one correct answer. Rav Preida chose the latter: *de-nizku ana ve-darai l'alma de-atei* — "let me and my generation merit the World to Come." The *gemara* concludes that the Holy One, Blessed be He, then granted him both — *tenu lo zu ve-zu* — because he had chosen the more selfless option. This narrative powerfully illustrates the theme of the entire passage: the extraordinary self-sacrifice a teacher must be prepared to make to ensure his students truly acquire what they are taught.

## Signs & Memory

### Simanim Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֵין תּוֹרָה נִקְנֵית אֶלָּא בְּסִימָנִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׂימָהּ בְּפִיהֶם״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ׳שִׂימָהּ׳, אֶלָּא ׳סַיְּמֶנָּהּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: The Torah can be acquired only with** mnemonic **signs** that aid the memory, **as it is stated: "Put it in their mouths." Do not read** the phrase as: **Put it [*simah*], but** rather as: **Its sign [*simanah*],** thus indicating that mnemonic signs aid in memorizing the material.

Rav Chisda teaches: *ein Torah niknet ela be'simanim* — Torah is acquired only through *simanim*, signs or mnemonics. The support he cites is the verse *simah be'fihem* — "place it in their mouths" — read not as *simah* but as *simanah*, "its signs." The principle is that the retention of Torah depends on the active construction of memory aids and associative markers. This connects naturally to the foregoing discussion about the need to repeat a lesson many times: repetition and *simanim* are two sides of the same pedagogical coin.

### Rav Tachlifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁמְעַהּ רַב תַּחְלִיפָא מִמַּעְרְבָא, אֲזַל אַמְרַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אֲמַר: אַתּוּן מֵהָתָם מַתְנִיתוּ לַהּ, אֲנַן מֵהָכָא מַתְנִינַן לַהּ: ״הַצִּיבִי לָךְ צִיּוּנִים שִׂימִי לָךְ וְגוֹ׳״ — עֲשׂוּ צִיּוּנִים לַתּוֹרָה. וּמַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי צִיּוּן לִישָּׁנָא דְסִימָנָא הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרָאָה עֶצֶם אָדָם וּבָנָה אֶצְלוֹ צִיּוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Taḥalifa of the West,** i.e., from Eretz Yisrael, **heard this** statement and **went** and **said it before Rabbi Abbahu,** who **said: You learn this** idea **from there; we learn it from here,** as the verse states: **"Set up signposts [*tziyyunim*] for yourself; establish you** markers" (Jeremiah 31:20), which is understood to mean: **Establish** mnemonic **signs for the Torah. And from where** may it be inferred **that this** term ***tziyyun* denotes a sign? As it is written** in a different verse: "And when they that pass through shall pass through the land, **and any sees a human bone, he shall set up a sign** [***tziyyun***] **by it"** (Ezekiel 39:15), i.e., a sign that there is a source of ritual impurity at that spot.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״אֱמוֹר לַחׇכְמָה אֲחוֹתִי אָתְּ וּמוֹדָע לַבִּינָה תִקְרָא״ — עֲשֵׂה מוֹדָעִים לַתּוֹרָה. רָבָא אָמַר: עֲשֵׂה מוֹעֲדִים לַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer said** that we learn this same idea **from here: "Say to wisdom, you are my sister, and call understanding, your kinswoman [*moda*]"** (Proverbs 7:4), which means: **Establish signs [*moda'im*]** that convey knowledge of **the Torah. Rava said** with regard to this verse: **Set appointed times [*mo'adim*] for Torah** study.

Rav Tachlifa from the west — that is, from *Eretz Yisrael* — heard Rav Chisda's teaching and reported it to Rabbi Abbahu. Rabbi Abbahu replied: in Babylonia you derive this idea from that verse; we derive it from a different one: *hatzivi lach tziyunim* — "set up markers for yourself." The word *tziyun* is understood in the sense of a sign or signpost, as it appears in the verse *ra'ah etzem adam u-vanah etzlo tziyun* — "they saw a human bone and built a marker by it." Rabbi Elazar derives the same principle from yet another verse: *emor la-chochmah achoti at* — "say to wisdom: you are my sister" — reading *moda* as a *muda*, an informer or sign: *asu moda'im la-Torah*, make signs for the Torah.

Rava then adds a reading of his own: *asu mo'adim la-Torah* — make fixed times for Torah, reversing the letters of *muda* to yield *mo'ed*. Rashi explains this as an instruction to the teacher: *kiv'u itim l'talmideichem she-yihyu yode'im et lavo ve-lishnot* — establish fixed times so that students know when to come and review. The analogy is precisely that of *Daf Yomi*: a fixed, predictable schedule creates the focused mindset necessary for proper learning.

### Fixed Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר אַבְדִּימִי בַּר חָמָא בַּר דּוֹסָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא בַשָּׁמַיִם הִיא וְלֹא מֵעֵבֶר לַיָּם הִיא״. ״לָא בַּשָּׁמַיִם הִיא״, שֶׁאִם בַּשָּׁמַיִם הִיא — אַתָּה צָרִיךְ לַעֲלוֹת אַחֲרֶיהָ, וְאִם מֵעֵבֶר לַיָּם הִיא — אַתָּה צָרִיךְ לַעֲבוֹר אַחֲרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this** idea, that one must exert great effort to retain one's Torah knowledge, **is** in accordance with **what Avdimi bar Ḥama bar Dosa said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "It is not in heaven…nor is it beyond the sea"** (Deuteronomy 30:12–13)? **"It is not in heaven"** indicates **that if it were in heaven, you would have to ascend after it, and if it were beyond the sea, you would have to cross after it,** as one must expend whatever effort is necessary in order to study Torah.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: ״לֹא בַשָּׁמַיִם הִיא״ — לֹא תִּמָּצֵא בְּמִי שֶׁמַּגְבִּיהַּ דַּעְתּוֹ עָלֶיהָ כַּשָּׁמַיִם, וְלֹא תִּמָּצֵא בְּמִי שֶׁמַּרְחִיב דַּעְתּוֹ עָלֶיהָ כַּיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** Expounding the verse differently, **Rava said: "It is not in heaven"** means that Torah **is not to be found in someone who raises his mind over it, like the heavens,** i.e., he thinks his mind is above the Torah and he does not need a teacher; **nor is it to be found in someone who expands his mind over it, like the sea,** i.e., he thinks he knows everything there is to know about the topic he has learned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״לֹא בַשָּׁמַיִם הִיא״ — לֹא תִּמָּצֵא בְּגַסַּי רוּחַ, ״וְלֹא מֵעֵבֶר לַיָּם הִיא״ — לֹא תִּמָּצֵא לֹא בְּסַחְרָנִים וְלֹא בְּתַגָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: "It is not in heaven"** means that Torah **is not to be found in the haughty,** those who raise their self-image as though they were in heaven. **"Nor is it beyond the sea"** means that **it is not to be found among merchants or traders** who are constantly traveling and do not have the time to study Torah properly.

This theme of fixed times flows into a teaching of Rav Dimi bar Chama (found at the top of 55a), which expounds the verse *lo va-shamayim hi ve-lo me-ever la-yam hi* — "it is not in the heavens, nor is it across the sea." The literal meaning implies that Torah is accessible and need not be sought in remote places. But Rav Dimi reads the verse as teaching the *extent* of one's obligation: *she'im ba-shamayim hi, atah tzarich la-alot achareha* — were it in the heavens, you would be required to ascend after it; *ve-im me-ever la-yam hi, atah tzarich la-avor achareha* — were it across the ocean, you would be required to cross after it. The point is one of absolute commitment: even in the most extreme hypothetical, the pursuit of Torah would be obligatory. Rashi frames this as *l'harim be-tachbulot kedei she-yitkayem ha-Torah be-lomdeha* — to be crafty and resourceful in every device to ensure that Torah is retained by those who study it.

Rava then offers a homiletical reading of the same verse directed at a different failure mode: *lo timtza be-mi she-magbi'ah da'ato aleha ka-shamayim* — Torah will not be found among those who elevate themselves as high as the heavens in arrogance; *ve-lo timtza be-mi she-marchiv da'ato aleha ka-yam* — nor will it be found among those who spread themselves as broadly as the sea, rushing over more and more ground without proper review (*chazarah*). Rabbi Yochanan adds: *lo timtza begassei ruach* — it will not be found among the haughty; *lo timtza lo be-sachranin ve-lo be-tagrin* — nor among merchants, whether local traders or those who travel from city to city, whose professional demands leave them without the time and focus Torah requires.

What is distinctive about this entire passage — more so than other passages on the importance of *talmud Torah* — is its sustained practical focus: how do you remember what you learn? The answers given are repetition, oral recitation, *simanim*, fixed times, and the cultivation of humility. The moral dimension — that haughtiness and restlessness are incompatible with Torah retention — is woven into the same discussion as the practical pedagogical advice.

## City Boundaries

### Baraita Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד מְעַבְּרִין אֶת הֶעָרִים? אֲרוּכָּה — כְּמוֹת שֶׁהִיא. עֲגוּלָּה — עוֹשִׂין לָהּ זָוִיּוֹת. מְרוּבַּעַת — אֵין עוֹשִׂין לָהּ זָוִיּוֹת. הָיְתָה רְחָבָה מִצַּד אֶחָד וּקְצָרָה מִצַּד אַחֵר — רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא שָׁוָה. הָיָה בַּיִת אֶחָד יוֹצֵא כְּמִין פִּגּוּם, אוֹ שְׁנֵי בָתִּים יוֹצְאִין כְּמִין שְׁנֵי פִגּוּמִין — רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ חוּט מָתוּחַ עֲלֵיהֶן, וּמוֹדֵד מִמֶּנּוּ וּלְהַלָּן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה. הָיְתָה עֲשׂוּיָה כְּמִין קֶשֶׁת אוֹ כְּמִין גַּאם — רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא מְלֵאָה בָּתִּים וַחֲצֵירוֹת, וּמוֹדֵד מִמֶּנָּה וּלְהַלָּן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** After the lengthy aggadic digression, the Gemara returns to the topic of the mishna, extending the outskirts of a city. **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: **How does one extend** the boundaries of **cities?** If the city is **long,** in the shape of a rectangle, the Shabbat limit is measured from the boundary **as it is.** If the city is **round, one creates** simulated **corners for it,** rendering it square, and the Shabbat limit is measured from there. If it is **square, one does not create** additional **corners for it.** If the city **was wide on one side and narrow on the other side, one regards it as though** the two sides **were** of **equal** length, adding to the narrow side to form a square. If **one house** in a row of dwellings **was protruding like a turret, or** if **two houses** were **protruding like two turrets, one regards them as though a cord is stretched over their** outer edge along the length of the city, **and one measures two thousand cubits** beginning **from there.** If the city **was shaped like a bow or like** the Greek letter ***gamma*, one regards it as if** the interior space were **full of houses and courtyards, and one measures two thousand cubits** beginning **from there.**

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah* and the *halacha* of *me'avrin et ha'arim* — how one extends or expands the effective boundaries of a city for purposes of *eruvin* and *Shabbat tchumin*. The *gemara* introduces a *baraita* that systematically addresses various city configurations:

If the city is *arucha* — long — you leave it as it is. If it is *agula* — circular — *osim la zaviyot meruba*, you square it off and give it corners. If it is *meruba'at* — already square — you do not add corners. If it is wide on one side and narrow on the other (a trapezoid), *ro'in otah k'ilu hi shaveh* — you treat it as though it were uniform, converting it to a rectangle. If one house juts out (*pagum*), or two houses jut out, *ro'in otam k'ilu chut matuach aleihem* — you treat it as though a string were stretched from them — and measure the 2,000-*amah tchum* from that extended line. If the city is shaped like a bow (*keshet*) or like a *gam* (the Greek letter gamma), *ro'in otah k'ilu hi mele'ah batim u-chatzeirot* — you treat the open interior space as though it were filled with houses and courtyards — and then measure 2,000 *amah* outward.

### Long Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אֲרוּכָּה כְּמוֹת שֶׁהִיא. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲרִיכָא וְקַטִּינָא, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לִיתֵּן לַהּ פּוּתְיָא אַאוּרְכַּהּ. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to analyze the *Tosefta*. **The Master said:** If the city is **long,** the Shabbat limit is measured from the boundary **as it is.** The Gemara expresses surprise: That is **obvious.** The Gemara explains: **It was necessary** to teach this *halakha* **only** with regard to a case **where** the city is **long and narrow. Lest you say: Let us give its breadth** the dimension **of its length** and regard the city as if it were square, **it teaches us** that we do not do so.

The *gemara* notes that the ruling about a long city seems obvious — of course you leave it as it is. The *gemara* responds: *lo tzricha de'aricha u-ktina* — the point addresses a city that is not merely long but long and very narrow, like a thin rectangle. One might have thought that in such a case the city should be made square by giving it width equal to its length. The *baraita* therefore teaches that one does not go that far: you make a rectangle around the city, not a square.

### Square Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְרוּבַּעַת אֵין עוֹשִׂין לָהּ זָוִיּוֹת. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּמְרַבְּעָא, וְלָא מְרַבְּעָא בְּרִיבּוּעַ עוֹלָם. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לִירַבְּעָא בְּרִיבּוּעַ עוֹלָם. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Square** city ruling. The *Tosefta* stated: If the city is **square, one does not create** additional **corners for it.** Once again the Gemara asks: That is **obvious.** The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to teach this *halakha* **only** with regard to a case **where** the shape of the city **is square but** that **square is not** aligned with the **four** directions **of the world,** i.e., north, south, east, and west. **Lest you say: Let us** align the **square with the four** directions of the **world, it teaches us** that this is not done.

Similarly, the ruling that a city already square needs no further adjustment seems obvious. The *gemara* explains: *lo tzricha de-murbaat ve-lo merabaat be-ribua olam* — the case is one where the city is indeed rectangular but its sides do not align with the compass points. One might have thought that in such a case the city should be rotated or adjusted to align with *ribua olam* — the four cardinal directions — and in so doing gain additional corner area. The *baraita* therefore teaches that since the city is already the right shape, no such adjustment is made. The benefit of squaring off according to the compass points applies only when the city needs to be squared off in the first place; a city that is already rectangular does not get to re-square itself by the compass points.

The practical implication is significant. A city whose rectangle happens not to align with the cardinal directions would still have its *tchum* measured outward from its actual walls, without the benefit of the corner areas that would be gained by rotating the square to the compass. Conversely, a city that requires squaring off — say, a circular city — gets squared off according to the compass points and thereby gains those corners.

### Jutting Houses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה בַּיִת אֶחָד יוֹצֵא כְּמִין פִּגּוּם אוֹ שְׁנֵי בָתִּים יוֹצְאִין כְּמִין שְׁנֵי פִגּוּמִין. הַשְׁתָּא בַּיִת אֶחָד אָמְרַתְּ, שְׁנֵי בָתִּים מִיבַּעְיָא?! לָא צְרִיכָא, מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מֵרוּחַ אַחַת אָמְרִינַן, מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת לָא אָמְרִינַן. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The *Tosefta* also stated: If **one house** in a row of dwellings **was protruding like a turret, or** if **two houses** were **protruding like two turrets,** one regards them as though a cord is stretched over their outer edge along the length of the city, and one measures two thousand cubits beginning from there. The Gemara asks: **Now,** if with regard to **one house, you said** to extend the city's boundaries, with regard to **two houses, is it necessary** to say so? The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to teach this *halakha* **only** with regard to a case where the two houses were protruding **on two** different **sides** of the city. **Lest you say:** When a house protrudes **from one side, we say** that the city is extended even due to a single house, but if houses protrude **from two sides we do not say** so; therefore, **it teaches us** to regard the city as though it is extended on both sides.

The *baraita*'s mention of one and then two jutting houses is analyzed by the *gemara*. One jutting house makes sense as a case; why add two? The *gemara* explains: *lo tzricha mishtei ruchos* — the case of two houses addresses houses jutting in *opposite directions*. One might have thought that a single jutting house in one direction is sufficient to extend the *tchum* in that direction, but two small houses jutting in opposite directions — neither of which is the main city proper — would not both be honored as extensions. The *baraita* teaches that both extensions are recognized, even though each jutting house is only a single structure at the periphery.

### Bow-Shaped Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה עֲשׂוּיָה כְּמִין קֶשֶׁת אוֹ כְּמִין גַּאם, רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא מְלֵאָה בָתִּים וַחֲצֵירוֹת, וּמוֹדֵד מִמֶּנָּה וּלְהַלָּן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Bow-shaped city rule. If the city **was shaped like a bow or like** the Greek letter ***gamma*, one regards it as if** the interior space were **full of houses and courtyards, and one measures two thousand cubits** beginning **from there.**

The rule about *keshet*- and *gam*-shaped cities treats the entire open interior space as if it were filled with houses. This is essentially an extreme generalization of the jutting-house rule: in the bow-shaped city, there are so many effective "jutting outs" that the entire concave interior is treated as built-up. One draws the bounding rectangle from the outermost points and measures 2,000 *amah* from there in each direction. One might ask whether the interior space needs to be buildable — whether, for instance, a mountain or body of water in the center would affect the analysis. The *gemara* does not address buildability per se, focusing instead on the critical question of how large the interior space may be before the city loses its unified character.

## Unifying Cities

### Rav Huna's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: עִיר הָעֲשׂוּיָה כְּקֶשֶׁת, אִם יֵשׁ בֵּין שְׁנֵי רָאשֶׁיהָ פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים אַמָּה — מוֹדְדִין לָהּ מִן הַיֶּתֶר, וְאִם לָאו — מוֹדְדִין לָהּ מִן הַקֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said:** With regard to **a city that is shaped like a bow,** the following distinction applies: **If there are less than four thousand cubits between** the **two ends** of the bow, so that the Shabbat limits measured from the two ends of the city overlap, the interior space of the bow is regarded as if it were filled with houses, and **one measures** the Shabbat limit of the city **from the** imaginary **bowstring** stretched between the two ends of the bow. **But if** that is **not** the case, and the distance between the two ends of the bow is four thousand cubits or more, **one measures** the Shabbat limit **from the bow** itself.

Rav Huna now introduces a critical limiting principle for the *keshet* configuration. *Im yesh bein shnei rosheha pachas me-arba'as alafim amah* — if the distance between the two tips of the bow is less than 4,000 *amah*, then *modidin la min ha-yeter* — the *tchum* is measured from the chord (the *yeter*), treating the interior as filled. If the gap between the tips exceeds 4,000 *amah*, then the city cannot be treated as unified in this way, and each house must be measured individually.

The logic of 4,000 *amah* as the threshold is that for two points to be considered within a single unified city, it must be possible for a resident of one endpoint to reach the other within the standard *Shabbat tchum* of 2,000 *amah*. Each tip of the bow contributes its 2,000 *amah*, and if they overlap, the interior is treated as one. If the tips are more than 4,000 *amah* apart, no overlap is possible and the unified treatment fails.

Tosafot raises the question of what happens when the distance at the outermost tips exceeds 4,000 *amah* but at some point closer to the apex of the bow the distance between the two arms narrows to less than 4,000. Tosafot rules that one begins measuring from the point at which the gap first reaches 4,000 or less, and from that point downward the city is treated as unified. Others disagree.

### Broken Walled City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַב הוּנָא הָכִי, וְהָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חוֹמַת הָעִיר שֶׁנִּפְרְצָה — בְּמֵאָה וְאַרְבָּעִים וְאַחַת וּשְׁלִישׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Did Rav Huna** actually **say** that the distance between two sections of a single city that renders them separate entities is four thousand cubits? **Didn't Rav Huna say:** With regard to **the wall of a city that was breached,** even if there is a gap between two sections of the city, the city is still considered a single entity if the breach is no more than **141⅓** cubits? However, if the breach is wider, the two sections are considered separate entities. Apparently, a distance of 141⅓ cubits suffices to separate between two sections of a city and to render them separate entities.

A seemingly contradictory ruling of Rav Huna is then adduced. Rav Huna had also stated: *chomas ir she-nifretza be-me'ah arba'im ve-achas ve-shlish* — if the wall of a city broke and there is a gap in the middle of 141 and one-third *amah*, the city can still be considered unified. This figure of 141 and one-third is far smaller than 4,000, raising an apparent contradiction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בְּרוּחַ אַחַת, כָּאן — מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ulla said:** That is **not difficult. Here,** where Rav Huna speaks of four thousand cubits, he is referring to a case where the gap is **on** only **one side,** as the other side, the bow, is inhabited; but **there,** where he speaks of 141⅓ cubits, he is referring to a case where the breach is **from two sides,** which truly renders the city two separate entities.

The *gemara* resolves this through Rabbah bar Ulla: *kan be-ruach achas kan mishtei ruchos* — here the gap is on one side, there the separation is from two sides. In the case of the broken walled city, the city was originally unified and remains connected on three sides; the gap affects only one aspect of its continuity. In the *keshet* case, the two arms of the bow are entirely separate from each other — there is no built-up connection between them whatsoever. Because the *keshet* presents a far more radical form of disconnection, the standard for unification is set much higher, at 4,000 *amah*.

### Karpaf Buffer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? דְּנוֹתְנִין קַרְפֵּף לָזוֹ וְקַרְפֵּף לָזוֹ? הָא אַמְרַהּ רַב הוּנָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא, דִּתְנַן: נוֹתְנִין קַרְפֵּף לָעִיר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא אָמְרוּ קַרְפֵּף אֶלָּא בֵּין שְׁתֵּי עֲיָירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **what is** Rav Huna **teaching us** in the case of the breached city wall, that **one allocates a *karpef*,** an area measuring slightly more than seventy cubits, **to this** section of the city **and a *karpef* to that** section of the city? **Didn't Rav Huna** already **say** this **on one occasion? As we learned** in a mishna: **One allocates a *karpef* to** every **city,** i.e., an area of slightly more than seventy cubits is added to the boundary of a city and the Shabbat limit is measured from there; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Sages say: They spoke of** the measure of **a *karpef* only** with regard to the space **between two** adjacent **cities,** i.e., if adjacent cities are separated by a shorter distance than that, they are considered one city.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיתְּמַר, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: קַרְפֵּף לָזוֹ וְקַרְפֵּף לָזוֹ. וְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָמַר: אֵין נוֹתְנִין אֶלָּא קַרְפֵּף אֶחָד לִשְׁנֵיהֶם!
>
> **Translation:** **And it was stated** that the *amora'im* disputed this issue. **Rav Huna said: A *karpef*** is added **to this** city **and** another ***karpef*** is added **to that** city, so that as long as the cities are not separated by a distance of slightly more than 141 cubits, they are considered one entity. **And Ḥiyya bar Rav said: One allocates only one *karpef* to the two of them.** Accordingly, Rav Huna has already stated that the measure of a *karpef* is added to both cities in determining whether they are close enough to be considered a single entity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָכָא — מִשּׁוּם דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ צַד הֶיתֵּר מֵעִיקָּרָא. אֲבָל הָתָם, אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָתָם — מִשּׁוּם דִּדְחִיקָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתַּיְיהוּ, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָא דְּחִיקָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתַּיְיהוּ — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is necessary** for Rav Huna to state this *halakha* in both instances, **as, had he taught it to us** only **here,** in the case of the breached wall, one might have said that a *karpef* is allocated to each city only in that case **because it had an aspect of permissibility from the outset,** namely, the two sections originally formed one city. **But there,** with regard to the two cities, **say** that this is **not** the case and the two cities are only considered as one if they are separated by less than the measure of a single *karpef*. **And had he taught it** to **us** only **there,** with regard to the two cities, one might have said that only in that case is a *karpef* allocated to each city **because** one *karpef* would be too **cramped for the use** of both cities. **But here,** in the case of the breached wall, **where** one *karpef* **would not be** too **cramped for the use of** both sections, as the vacant space is inside the city, in an area that had not been used in this fashion before the wall was breached, **say** that this is **not** the case and a single *karpef* is sufficient. Therefore, **it was necessary** to state this *halakha* in both cases.

The *gemara* then clarifies what Rav Huna is actually teaching with the 141-and-one-third figure, which derives from the concept of *karpaf* — the buffer zone around a city. The standard distance for determining whether an outlying house is considered part of a city is 70 and two-thirds *amah*. This figure derives from the *beit se'atayim* measurement — the area required to plant two *se'ah* — which, when expressed as a square, has a side of approximately 70 and two-thirds *amah* (the square root of 5,000 square *amot*). A house within 70 and two-thirds of the city limit is considered part of the city; beyond that distance, it is not.

When two cities are to be unified, Rav Huna rules *karpaf la-zu ve-karpaf la-zu* — each city receives its buffer of 70 and two-thirds *amah*, and if these buffers overlap (meaning the gap between the cities is 141 and one-third *amah* or less), the cities are considered unified. Chiyya bar Rav disagrees, ruling *ein notnim ela karpaf echad li-shneihem* — only a single *karpaf* of 70 and two-thirds is shared between them; the cities must be within 70 and two-thirds of each other to be combined. The *gemara* explains why Rav Huna found it necessary to state this rule in both contexts — the broken walled city and the two separate cities — since each case might have been treated differently on its own terms. In the broken-walled city one might have been more lenient (because it once was unified); in the separate-cities case one might have been more strict (because each city needs breathing room). Both rulings are therefore necessary.

### Arc to Apex Distance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה הָוֵי בֵּין יֶתֶר לַקֶּשֶׁת? רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה, רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ יָתֵר מֵאַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much** distance may there be **between** the imaginary **bowstring and** the center of the **bow** in a city that is shaped like a bow? **Rabba bar Rav Huna said: Two thousand cubits. Rava, son of Rabba bar Rav Huna, said: Even more than two thousand cubits.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי בָּעֵי הָדַר אָתֵי דֶּרֶךְ בָּתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: It stands to reason in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava, son of Rabba bar Rav Huna, as if one wants, he can return and go** anywhere within the bow **by way of the houses.** Since one can always walk to the end of the city, and from there he is permitted to walk down the line of the imaginary bowstring, he should also be permitted to walk from the middle of the bow to the bowstring, even if the distance is more than two thousand cubits.

Beyond the question of the distance between the two tips of the bow, the *gemara* now asks: *ve-chama havi bein yeter la-keshet* — is there also a maximum permitted distance between the chord (the *yeter*, the straight line connecting the two tips) and the apex of the arc? Even if the tips are within 4,000 *amah* of each other, could the arc extend so far outward that the city effectively loses its unified character?

Rabbah bar Rav Huna rules: *alpayim amah* — the distance from the chord to the apex may not exceed 2,000 *amah*. His reasoning is that the houses at the tips of the bow each extend 2,000 *amah* into the interior, and if the apex is more than 2,000 *amah* from the chord, those houses cannot reach the apex, and the apex cannot be considered part of the same city as the tips.

His son Rava disagrees: *afilu yeter me-alpayim* — even if the distance exceeds 2,000 *amah*, there is no limit once the two tips are considered unified. Abaye concurs with Rava's position: *d'ee bei hadar asit derech patzin* — because one can always travel from the apex back through the built-up portions of the arc to reach any other part of the city. No point in the city is truly isolated; one can always traverse the city's actual built-up areas to get from one part to another. Once the two tips are connected at the chord level, the entire structure is unified, regardless of how far the arc extends outward.

## Habitation Structures

### Gedudiyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שָׁם גְּדוּדִיּוֹת גְּבוֹהוֹת עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים כּוּ׳. מַאי גְּדוּדִיּוֹת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שָׁלֹשׁ מְחִיצּוֹת שֶׁאֵין עֲלֵיהֶן תִּקְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: If **there were remnants** of walls **ten handbreadths high** on the outskirts of a city, they are considered part of the city, and the Shabbat limit is measured from them. The Gemara asks: **What are** these **remnants? Rav Yehuda said: Three partitions that do not have a roof over them,** which are considered part of the city despite the fact that they do not comprise a proper house.

The *gemara* introduces *gedudiyot* — ruined or partially destroyed structures — and asks whether these count as part of the city. The question is: *mai gedudiyot*? Rav Yehuda answers: *shalosh mechitzot she-ein aleihen tikrah* — three walls without a roof. Even a roofless three-walled ruin counts, because as Rashi explains, the criterion is not whether the structure is currently inhabited but whether it was built for habitation and retains the potential for habitation. The *gedudiyot* satisfy this standard.

This leads immediately to the question: what about *shtei mechitzot ve-yesh aleihem tikrah* — two walls *with* a roof? Does the presence of a roof compensate for the absence of a third wall?

### Inclusive List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שְׁתֵּי מְחִיצּוֹת וְיֵשׁ עֲלֵיהֶן תִּקְרָה, מַהוּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, אֵלּוּ שֶׁמִּתְעַבְּרִין עִמָּהּ: נֶפֶשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וְהַגֶּשֶׁר וְהַקֶּבֶר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן בֵּית דִּירָה, וּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ בֵּית דִּירָה לַחַזָּן, וּבֵית עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ בֵּית דִּירָה לַכּוֹמָרִים, וְהָאוּרְווֹת וְהָאוֹצָרוֹת שֶׁבַּשָּׂדוֹת וְיֵשׁ בָּהֶן בֵּית דִּירָה, וְהַבּוּרְגָּנִין שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ, וְהַבַּיִת שֶׁבַּיָּם — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מִתְעַבְּרִין עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The dilemma was raised before** the Sages: In the case of **two partitions** that **have a roof over them, what is** the *halakha*? Is this structure also treated like a house? **Come** and **hear** a proof from the *Tosefta*: **These** are the structures **that are included in** the city's **extension: A monument [*nefesh*]** over a grave **that is four cubits by four cubits; and a bridge or a grave in which there is a residence; and a synagogue in which there is a residence for the sexton** or synagogue attendant, and which is used not only for prayer services at specific times; **and an idolatrous temple in which there is a residence for the priests; and** similarly, horse **stables and storehouses in the fields in which there is a residence; and** small **watchtowers in** the fields; **and** similarly, **a house on** an island in **the sea** or lake, which is located within seventy cubits of the city; all of **these** structures **are included in the** city's boundaries.

To address this question, the *gemara* turns to a comprehensive *baraita* listing those structures that are *mitabrim imah* — included within the city's extended boundary — and those that are not.

The inclusive list begins with a *nefesh* — a funerary structure adjacent to a grave — provided it measures at least four *amot* by four *amot*, which is the minimum size for a habitable space (*beit dirah*). Also included: a bridge or a cemetery, provided *she-yesh bahem beit dirah* — they have a place for someone to live there; a *beit knesset* (synagogue), provided *she-yesh bah beit dirah le-chazzan* — it has a dwelling for the *chazzan* or sexton; and — strikingly — even *beit avodat kochavim*, a pagan temple, provided *she-yesh bah beit dirah le-komerim* — the priests who serve there have living quarters. The point is entirely functional: the religious character of the building is irrelevant; what matters is whether human beings live there.

The list continues: *urvot ve-otzarot she-ba-sadot* — stables and storehouses in the fields — with living quarters; *burganin she-betochah* — little huts out in the fields; and *ha-bayit she-ba-yam* — a house on the water, which Rashi understands as a house on a small island. All of these, if within 70 and two-thirds *amah* of the city, are considered part of it.

The common thread throughout is *beit dirah* — human habitation. Structures that serve functions other than dwelling — storing goods, marking graves, conducting worship — qualify only when they also contain a residential component. A cemetery is not part of the city by virtue of being a cemetery; it is part of the city by virtue of the caretaker who lives within it.

### Exclusive List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין מִתְעַבְּרִין עִמָּהּ: נֶפֶשׁ שֶׁנִּפְרְצָה מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹתֶיהָ אֵילָךְ וְאֵילָךְ, וְהַגֶּשֶׁר וְהַקֶּבֶר שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן בֵּית דִּירָה, וּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת שֶׁאֵין לָהּ בֵּית דִּירָה לַחַזָּן, וּבֵית עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ בֵּית דִּירָה לַכּוֹמָרִים, וְהָאוּרְווֹת וְהָאוֹצָרוֹת שֶׁבַּשָּׂדוֹת שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן בֵּית דִּירָה, וּבוֹר וְשִׁיחַ וּמְעָרָה וְגָדֵר וְשׁוֹבָךְ שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ, וְהַבַּיִת שֶׁבַּסְּפִינָה — אֵין אֵלּוּ מִתְעַבְּרִין עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And these** structures **are not included in** the boundaries of a city: **A tomb that was breached on both sides,** from **here** to **there,** i.e., from one side all the way to the other; **and** similarly, **a bridge and a grave that do not have a residence; and a synagogue that does not have a residence for the sexton; and an idolatrous temple that does not have a residence for the priests; and** similarly, **stables and storehouses in fields that do not have a residence,** and therefore are not used for human habitation; **and a cistern, and an** elongated water **ditch, and a cave,** i.e., a covered cistern, **and a wall, and a dovecote in** the field; **and** similarly, **a house on a boat** that is not permanently located within seventy cubits of the city; all of **these** structures **are not included in the** city's boundaries.

The exclusive list is, as noted, a near-perfect mirror image of the inclusive list. A *nefesh* whose walls were breached from two opposite sides does not count. A bridge or cemetery without living quarters does not count. A *beit knesset* without a *chazzan*'s residence does not count. A pagan temple without priests' quarters does not count. Stables and storehouses without living quarters do not count. A well, a ditch, a cave (*me'arah*), a wall (*geder*), a pigeon coop — all are excluded regardless of their size or structural significance. The principle is consistent: without *beit dirah*, a structure is merely a structure, not a habitation node capable of extending the city boundary.

One case that requires particular attention: *ha-bayit she-ba-sefinah* — a house on a boat. This is excluded not because no one lives in it — someone may well live aboard — but because it lacks a fixed location. The requirement is not merely habitation but habitation that is geographically anchored. A dwelling that moves cannot serve as a fixed extension of a city's boundary.

### Wall Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: נֶפֶשׁ שֶׁנִּפְרְצָה מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹתֶיהָ אֵילָךְ וְאֵילָךְ. מַאי לָאו דְּאִיכָּא תִּקְרָה! לָא — דְּלֵיכָּא תִּקְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **In any case, it was taught** that **a tomb that was breached on both sides,** from **here** to **there,** is not included in the city's boundaries. **What,** is this **not** referring to a case where **there is a roof** on the tomb, and the two remaining walls are not included in the city's boundaries even though they have a roof? The Gemara answers: **No,** the *Tosefta* is referring to a case **where there is no roof** on the tomb.

The *gemara* now returns to the question raised by *gedudiyot*: two walls with a roof versus three walls without. The *baraita*'s exclusive list mentions *nefesh she-nifretza mishtei ruchoteha* — a structure with two walls breached on opposite sides — as insufficient. The *gemara* asks: *mai lav de-ika tikra* — does this mean that even with a roof, two walls are not enough? The *gemara* deflects: *lo, de-leika tikra* — it refers to a structure that also lacks a roof. The *gemara* thus leaves open the possibility that two walls *with* a roof would qualify. The question is not fully resolved from this passage alone.

### Water & Caves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּיִת שֶׁבַּיָּם. לְמַאי חֲזֵי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בַּיִת שֶׁעָשׂוּי לְפַנּוֹת בּוֹ כֵּלִים שֶׁבַּסְּפִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **A house on** an island in **the sea, what is** it **suitable for** if it is not actually part of the inhabited area? **Rav Pappa said:** It is referring to **a house used to move a ship's utensils** into it for storage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְעָרָה אֵין מִתְעַבֶּרֶת עִמָּהּ, וְהָתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: מְעָרָה מִתְעַבֶּרֶת עִמָּהּ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ בִּנְיָן עַל פִּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another question with regard to the *Tosefta*: **And is a cave** on the outskirts of a city really **not included in its extension? Didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach** in a *baraita*: **A cave is included in its extension? Abaye said:** That statement applies **when there is a structure** built **at its entrance,** which is treated like a house on the outskirts of the city.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם בִּנְיָן גּוּפֵיהּ! לָא צְרִיכָא, לְהַשְׁלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If there is a structure at the entrance to the cave, why is the cave mentioned? **Let him derive** the *halakha* that it is treated like a house **because of the structure itself.** The Gemara answers: **No, it is necessary** only in a case where the cave serves **to complete** the structure, i.e., where the area of the structure and cave combined are only four by four cubits, which is the minimum size of a house.

Finally, the *gemara* confronts an apparent contradiction regarding caves. The *baraita*'s exclusive list includes *me'arah* — a cave is not included in the city. Yet Rabbi Chiyya taught in a *baraita*: *me'arah mitaberet imah* — a cave is included. Abaye resolves this: *ki she-yesh binyan al piha* — Rabbi Chiyya is speaking of a cave that has a structure built at its entrance. But if so, the structure itself would suffice — why mention the cave at all? *Lo, tzricha le-hashlem* — the cave is needed to supplement. Rashi understands this to mean that even if the built structure is less than the minimum four *amot* by four *amot*, the cave completes the measurement, and together they constitute the required *beit dirah*. Tosafot goes further and rules more significantly: once a built structure stands at the cave entrance, the entire cave — however deep — is considered part of the city boundary, since the presence of a *beit dirah* at the entrance transforms the whole space into a habitable unit.

This principle has striking practical implications. The same logic, as discussed, appears to apply to cemeteries and bridges: once there is a dwelling within that space, the entire space — the whole cemetery, the full length of the bridge — is drawn into the city boundary. Whether this would extend to, for example, a modern tunnel with a guardhouse at its entrance, or a bridge with toll booths that function as habitable structures, are questions that remain to be explored.
