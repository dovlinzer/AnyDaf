# Bekhorot 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tolash Lav Hainu Gozez

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם. בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב הוּנָא: כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב מַהוּ? טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר תּוֹלֵשׁ לָאו הַיְינוּ גּוֹזֵז, וּבְיוֹם טוֹב אָסוּר, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ עוֹקֵר דָּבָר מִגִּידּוּלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam.** The Sages **raised a dilemma before Rav Huna: What is** the *halakha* in **the parallel** case of one who wishes to slaughter a non-sacred animal **on a Festival?** The resolution depends on **the reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam:** Does he permit clearing hair with a cleaver **because he holds** that **plucking is not** the same as **shearing, and** as opposed to plucking the hair of a firstborn, **it is prohibited** to pluck hair **on the Festival, as this is** considered **uprooting an item from its** place of **growth?**

### Hair Removal for Bechor

The *mishna* addressed the permissibility of removing hair from a *bechor* prior to *shechita*, despite the explicit *pasuk*, *lo tagoz bechor shorecha* — one may not shear the firstborn of one's flock. The *gemara* proposes two possible explanations for why this removal is nonetheless permitted.

### Eino Mitkaven & Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא בְּעָלְמָא סָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם תּוֹלֵשׁ הַיְינוּ גּוֹזֵז, וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִּשְׁרֵי, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין, וּבְיוֹם טוֹב דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין מוּתָּר?
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps in general Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam holds** that **plucking is** considered a form of **shearing. And this is the reason that it is permitted** to clear space with a cleaver in the case of the firstborn, **as it is an unintentional act,** i.e., an action performed for a permitted purpose from which a prohibited labor may inadvertently ensue, since he does not intend to pluck out the hair; **and on a Festival an unintentional act is permitted.**

The first explanation invokes *eino mitkaven*. As the *gemara* will clarify later, this concept does not straightforwardly mean that the removal was accidental. It more closely resembles the *Shabbat* concept of *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa*: you are not removing the hair because you care about the hair itself, but only to clear the space for *shechita* or to inspect the wound. Strictly speaking, this is not *eino mitkaven* in the classic sense; it is closer to the *Shabbat* idea that one is not interested in the act for its primary purpose — a concept that does not ordinarily apply outside of *Shabbat* contexts.

### Melacha Irrelevant to Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְעוֹלָם סָבַר רַב: דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין אָסוּר, וְתוֹלֵשׁ לָאו הַיְינוּ גּוֹזֵז, וּבְיוֹם טוֹב הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִּשְׁרֵי — דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ עוֹקֵר דָּבָר מִגִּידּוּלוֹ כִּלְאַחַר יָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, actually Rav holds** that **an unintentional act is prohibited, and** he permits plucking the hair of a firstborn because he maintains that **plucking is not** considered a form of **shearing. And this** is the **reason** he **permits** clearing the hair with a cleaver **on a Festival, because it is** considered **uprooting an item from its** place of **growth in an unusual manner,** and performing a prohibited labor in an unusual manner is not prohibited by Torah law.

The second explanation takes a definitional approach: *gozez* — shearing — denotes a specific act. It refers to using clippers or shears, performed systematically over the animal's body. Removing individual hairs by hand in a localized spot does not meet that definition. Hence the *gemara*'s formulation: *tolash lav hainu gozez* — plucking is not the same act as shearing.

### Rav Ashi's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוֹלֵשׁ לָאו הַיְינוּ גּוֹזֵז? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַתּוֹלֵשׁ אֶת הַכָּנָף, וְהַקּוֹטְמוֹ, וְהַמּוֹרְטוֹ — חַיָּיב שָׁלֹשׁ חַטָּאוֹת. וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: תּוֹלֵשׁ — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם גּוֹזֵז, קוֹטֵם — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְחַתֵּךְ, מְמָרֵט — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְמַחֵק! שָׁאנֵי כָּנָף, דְּהַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **And** can it be claimed that **plucking is not** considered a form of **shearing? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who** unwittingly **plucks** a large feather from **the wing** of a bird on Shabbat, **and one who snips** the tip of the feather, **and one who pulls out** the thin threads that constitute the feather is **obligated** to bring **three sin offerings,** one for each transgression. **And Reish Lakish says,** in explanation: One who **plucks** the wing is **liable due to** the labor of **shearing.** One who **snips** the tip of the feather is **liable due to** the labor of **cutting.** And one who **pulls out** the threads is **liable due to** the labor of **smoothing.** The Gemara explains: Plucking **a wing is different** from plucking hair, **as that is the normal** method employed to remove feathers.

*Tosafot* attempts to explain the *eino mitkaven* approach as meaning that although one intends to move the hair, the *mishna*'s language — that the hair "was pulled out" — indicates it came free without deliberate intent to extract it. That is one reading of the *mishna*. The alternative remains that performing the act by hand rather than with an instrument is what categorically removes it from the definition of *gozez*.

### Tosafot: Nafka Mina

*Tosafot* raises the obvious *nafka mina*: does the explanation affect whether the act may be performed intentionally? The *gemara*, however, frames the practical difference around a different application — whether the same act is permitted on *Yom Tov*.

### Yom Tov Application

The question of *Yom Tov* is specifically relevant because on *Yom Tov* one is permitted to perform *melachot* necessary for food preparation, including *shechita*. Can one therefore remove wool from a *bechor* prior to *shechita* on *Yom Tov*? If the explanation for the *mishna* is *eino mitkaven*, that principle is also operative on *Yom Tov* and would permit it. But if the explanation is definitional — that plucking is not *gozez* — that reasoning does not carry over to *Yom Tov*, because on *Yom Tov* and *Shabbat* the categories of prohibited labor are not defined narrowly. Just as cooking encompasses frying and baking and is not limited to one precise method, *gozez* on *Shabbat* would include any manner of hair removal. A definitional distinction that works for the *bechor* prohibition does not automatically work for *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*.

### Rav's Stance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּרַב סָבַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב, וְסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין אָסוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And from** the fact **that Rav holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam** that plucking is not considered shearing, it can be inferred that **Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav** that an unintentional act is prohibited. The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam hold** that **an unintentional act is prohibited?**

Rav holds that *davar she'eino mitkaven assur* — an unintended act is forbidden. Therefore, the *eino mitkaven* approach cannot explain the *mishna* according to Rav, and certainly cannot justify the act on *Yom Tov*. The *gemara* states: *le'olam savar Rav de'ava she'eino mitkaven* — Rav genuinely holds that an unintended act is prohibited. So his explanation of the *mishna* must be the definitional one: *ve'tolash lav hainu gozez* — plucking by hand is simply not the act of shearing.

### Act Definition Differs

This resolves the *bechor* prohibition and explains the *mishna*, but it still does not explain why the act would be permitted on *Yom Tov*, since on *Yom Tov* the category of *gozez* is not defined so narrowly.

### K'lo'achar Yad on Yom Tov

The *gemara* therefore supplies a separate rationale for *Yom Tov*: *u'veyom Tov hainu ta'ama de'shari de'hava lei okeir davar migidulo k'lo'achar yad* — on *Yom Tov*, the reason it is permitted is that removing wool by hand constitutes *k'lo'achar yad*, an abnormal manner of performance. The two grounds are thus distinct. For the *bechor* prohibition, the reason is that plucking by hand is not definitionally *gozez*. For *Yom Tov*, the reason is that even if the broader category of hair removal is implicated, doing it in an unusual manner — by hand rather than with clippers — renders the act a *shinui*, an irregular mode of execution, which is why it is exempt. To use a parallel: if one fries instead of boils, the category of cooking is still violated. But if one performs that frying with one's toes, holding the pan in an abnormal grip, it is not merely that the method is different from the most common form — it is performed in so strange a fashion that it is *k'lo'achar yad*, and that exempts one.

### Tosafot: Chicken Feathers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְכַתְּחִלָּה אוֹ דִיעֲבַד? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: תָּא שְׁמַע, צֶמֶר הַמְסוּבָּךְ בָּאוֹזֶן — רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם אוֹמֵר: תּוֹלְשׁוֹ וּמַרְאֶה אֶת מוּמוֹ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is this permitted ***ab initio*, or** only **after the fact? Rabbi Yirmeya said: Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: If there is **wool that is entangled in** a firstborn's **ear,** and it is obscuring a blemish that must be examined, **Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam says: One plucks** the wool **and shows** the animal's **blemish** to a Sage in order to determine whether it is permitted to slaughter the animal outside the Temple. One can **conclude from** the *baraita* that it is permitted ***ab initio*.** The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is so.

*Tosafot* raises a practical question from this *gemara*. In the era before refrigeration, *shechita* of fresh chickens on *Yom Tov* was common. Can one pluck feathers from a chicken's neck before *shechita* on *Yom Tov*, by analogy to the case of removing wool from a sheep? The parallel seems close. *Tosafot* says: *ein lilmod mikan she'mutar litlosh notza mitzavar shel of kedei lishchot beyom Tov* — one cannot derive from here that plucking chicken feathers before *shechita* on *Yom Tov* is permitted.

### Feathers: Normal Method

The reason is straightforward. The entire justification for permitting the removal of wool from a sheep was *k'lo'achar yad* — using one's hand to pull wool is not the normal method of wool removal and therefore constitutes a *shinui*. But feathers from a bird are normally removed precisely by plucking with one's hand. There is no *shinui* here; *k'lo'achar yad* does not apply, and plucking feathers from a chicken is therefore not permitted on *Yom Tov*.

### Missing Melacha Concept

*Tosafot* then raises a broader question that also illuminates the underlying conceptual structure of the entire passage. He asks: even if plucking feathers is not *k'lo'achar yad*, why should it not still be permitted on the grounds of *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa*? You do not care about the feathers; you only want access to the neck for *shechita*. The product of the act — the feather — is entirely irrelevant to you. This is precisely the *Shabbat* concept of performing a *melacha* not for the purpose inherent in that *melacha*. Why does that not render it merely *derabbanan* and thereby permit it in the context of *Yom Tov* food preparation? The same question can be asked about the case of the sheep: the one removing wool from the sheep's neck also has no interest in the wool. Why is *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* not invoked throughout this *gemara*?

### Derabbanan Gradations

*Tosafot*'s answer is illuminating: *ein lemedin melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa mike'lo'achar yad* — one cannot compare these two *derabbanan* categories. This reflects a fundamental distinction between different levels of rabbinic prohibition. When one performs a *melacha k'lo'achar yad*, the departure from the prohibited act is visible and experiential — one is plainly not performing it in the ordinary way. This external *shinui* sends a signal, both to the actor and to observers, that something different is occurring, which keeps the act meaningfully distant from the *de'oraita*. By contrast, *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* looks and feels identical to the forbidden *melacha*; the only difference is the performer's inner intention. It is categorically harder to distinguish from the *de'oraita* — indeed, it is only one step removed. Since *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* sits so close to the *de'oraita*, the Rabbis did not permit it even in a case where *k'lo'achar yad* would be permitted. The question of why *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* goes entirely unaddressed in the body of the *gemara* — applicable as it would seem to be to both the *bechor* prohibition and the *Yom Tov* case — is one that *Tosafot* raises but the *gemara* itself leaves as the missing conversation of this passage.

## Goose Feather Quill

### Three Violations

The *gemara* now introduces a *braita*: one who on *Shabbat* takes a goose feather and fashions it into a quill — plucking it, trimming the tip, and smoothing the point — is liable for three *chataot*, having violated *Shabbat* three times.

### Reish Lakish's Breakdown

Reish Lakish identifies each of the three violations.

### Tolesh = Gozez (Birds)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: תּוֹלֵשׁ — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם גּוֹזֵז, קוֹטֵם — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְחַתֵּךְ, מְמָרֵט — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְמַחֵק!
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says,** in explanation: One who **plucks** the wing is **liable due to** the labor of **shearing.** One who **snips** the tip of the feather is **liable due to** the labor of **cutting.** And one who **pulls out** the threads is **liable due to** the labor of **smoothing.**

The first is *tolesh chayav mishum gozez* — plucking the feather constitutes *gozez*, shearing, because in the case of a bird, plucking is the normal and defining act of removing the animal's covering. Importantly, here one genuinely wants the product — the feather itself — so the act fits the full definition of *gozez*.

### Kotem: Cutting

The second is *kotem chayav mishum mechatech* — trimming the base of the feather is a form of cutting. Not every tearing act on *Shabbat* rises to the level of the prohibited *melacha* of cutting; for example, tearing open packaging is not necessarily cutting in the relevant sense. But a precise cut made to size something to a desired dimension, producing a usable article, certainly qualifies.

### Memarei'ach: Smoothing

The third is *memarei'ach*: whittling or smoothing the tip into a writing point. This derives from the *melacha* of smoothing leather — but the prohibition extends to any act of smoothing performed productively on an object. A digression arises naturally here: the prohibition of *memarei'ach* is sometimes invoked regarding brushing teeth on *Shabbat*, specifically regarding the spreading of toothpaste. This application has always seemed difficult. One's purpose in using toothpaste is not to create a smoothed surface; it is simply to distribute the material. One is not trying to produce a smooth product in the sense that underlies the prohibition. The precise scope of *memarei'ach* in that context will be addressed when studying *Masechet Shabbat*.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוֹלֵשׁ לָאו הַיְינוּ גּוֹזֵז? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַתּוֹלֵשׁ אֶת הַכָּנָף, וְהַקּוֹטְמוֹ, וְהַמּוֹרְטוֹ — חַיָּיב שָׁלֹשׁ חַטָּאוֹת. וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: תּוֹלֵשׁ — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם גּוֹזֵז, קוֹטֵם — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְחַתֵּךְ, מְמָרֵט — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְמַחֵק! שָׁאנֵי כָּנָף, דְּהַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **And** can it be claimed that **plucking is not** considered a form of **shearing? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who** unwittingly **plucks** a large feather from **the wing** of a bird on Shabbat, **and one who snips** the tip of the feather, **and one who pulls out** the thin threads that constitute the feather is **obligated** to bring **three sin offerings,** one for each transgression. **And Reish Lakish says,** in explanation: One who **plucks** the wing is **liable due to** the labor of **shearing.** One who **snips** the tip of the feather is **liable due to** the labor of **cutting.** And one who **pulls out** the threads is **liable due to** the labor of **smoothing.** The Gemara explains: Plucking **a wing is different** from plucking hair, **as that is the normal** method employed to remove feathers.

The *gemara* now raises a challenge to the *mishna*'s ruling. The *braita* just cited explicitly states that plucking a feather is *chayav mishum gozez*. How then can the *mishna* claim that *tolash lav hainu gozez* — that plucking is not the same as shearing?

### Shani Knaf: Species Difference

The *gemara* answers: *shani knaf dehaynu orchayei* — a feather is different, because plucking is the standard method of removal for birds. This answer runs deeper than merely saying it is not *k'lo'achar yad*. What counts as the act of shearing is species-specific. If one were asked how to shear a sheep, one would produce clippers. If one were asked how to "shear" a goose, one would pluck by hand. The very definition of what constitutes *gozez* differs by animal. Using hand-plucking on a sheep is not merely a *shinui* — an unusual method — it is not even the act of *gozez* at all. Conversely, using shears on a goose would be *k'lo'achar yad* for that species. The *gemara* thus distinguishes sharply: for the *bechor* prohibition, the question is whether the act constitutes *gozez* by definition, and for a sheep, plucking by hand does not. For *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, the relevant question is whether the method is normal or abnormal for that species — and for a bird, hand-plucking is entirely normal, hence no exemption applies.

## Rav's Understanding

### Rav Agrees; Disagrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — עִיקָּרָן מַאֲדִים, וְרֹאשָׁן מַשְׁחִיר, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם אוֹמֵר: גּוֹזֵז בְּמִסְפָּרַיִם, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a red heifer, which is rendered unfit if it possesses two black hairs, that had **two hairs whose roots were red but whose tops were black, Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam says: One shears** the tops **with scissors, and** he need **not** be **concerned** that he is violating the prohibition against shearing a consecrated animal. Apparently, the reason is that he does not intend to shear the red heifer but only to render it fit. This indicates that Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam holds an unintentional act is permitted.

The *gemara* now addresses how Rav relates to Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam. Rav rules in accordance with Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam's *mishna*, but he does so for different reasons than Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam himself.

### Rav's Core Principle

Rav holds *davar she'eino mitkaven assur* — an unintended act is forbidden. Therefore, if Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam's rationale in the *mishna* were *eino mitkaven*, Rav could not rule like him. Rav must be reading Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam as saying that hand-plucking is not defined as *gozez* — and on that reasoning Rav agrees, because that is his own position as well. So according to Rav, Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam shares his view that *davar she'eino mitkaven* is a problem; the *mishna* is permitted for the definitional reason, not the *eino mitkaven* reason.

### Challenge to R.Y.B.H.

But does Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam really hold that *davar she'eino mitkaven* is forbidden? This seems difficult to establish from a figure who appears rarely in the *sugyot*. The *gemara* now attempts to prove otherwise — that Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam in fact holds *eino mitkaven* is permitted.

### Para Aduma Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי פָּרָה, דְּלָאו בַּת גִּיזָּה הִיא. וְהָתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תַעֲבֹד בִּבְכֹר שׁוֹרֶךָ וְלֹא תָגֹז בְּכוֹר צֹאנֶךָ״, וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא שׁוֹר בַּעֲבוֹדָה וְצֹאן בְּגִיזָּה, מִנַּיִן לִיתֵּן אֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה וְאֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תַעֲבֹד... וְלֹא תָגֹז״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: A red **heifer is different, as it is not subject to** the prohibition against **shearing** a consecrated animal because it does not have wool, and that is why it is permitted to cut the tops of its hairs. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"You shall do no work with the firstborn of your ox, and you shall not shear the firstborn of your flock"** (Deuteronomy 15:19). **I have** derived **only** that a firstborn **ox** may not be used **for labor and** that a firstborn **sheep** may not be used **for shearing. From where** is it derived **to apply what is said about that** animal **to this** one, **and** what is said **about this** animal **to that** one? **The verse states: "You shall do no work…and you shall not shear."** The term "and" indicates the two parts of the verse apply to both animals. Evidently, the act of shearing does apply to an ox, including a red heifer.

The *gemara* cites a *Tosefta* in *Parah*. If a *para adumah* has two hairs that are red at one end and black at the other — not entirely red — one might think the animal cannot serve as a *para adumah*, since it lacks the required complete redness. Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam rules: *gozez b'misparayim v'eino choshesh* — cut them away with scissors and do not be concerned. What would one be concerned about? The prohibition of *gizah b'kodashim* — shearing a consecrated animal — which the Torah prohibits explicitly for *bechorim* and which is extended by derivation to all *kodashim*. Yet Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam says: do not worry about it.

### Eino Mitkaven Inference

The *gemara* infers that the reason he is not concerned is *eino mitkaven* — one does not care about the product. One is not cutting these hairs in order to benefit from the wool; one is cutting them away in order to restore the animal's eligibility as a *para adumah*. Since one is indifferent to the hair itself, this is *eino mitkaven*, and Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam permits it.

## Para Aduma Exception

### Para Not Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, שָׁאנֵי פָּרָה, דְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת הִיא. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת אֲסוּרִין בְּגִיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה! מִדְּרַבָּנַן. וְהָאִיכָּא אִיסּוּרָא דְּרַבָּנַן! אֶלָּא, שָׁאנֵי פָּרָה דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the red **heifer is different, as** it has the sanctity **of** items **consecrated for Temple maintenance,** and therefore the prohibitions that apply to offerings do not necessarily apply to it. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But doesn't Rabbi Elazar say** it is **prohibited to shear or** perform **labor** with items **consecrated for Temple maintenance?** The Gemara answers: That prohibition applies **by rabbinic law.** The Gemara retorts: **But** that does not resolve the difficulty, as **there is** nevertheless still **a prohibition by rabbinic law.** Why, then, did Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam deem it permitted for one to shear the tops of its hairs with scissors? The Gemara accepts this objection: **Rather,** the red **heifer is different, as it is uncommon,** and Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam therefore maintains that the prohibition by rabbinic law was not applied to this case.

The *gemara* responds: *shani parah delav bas giza hi* — the *para* is different, because a cow is not normally sheared. Just as the *mishna* could be explained by saying that plucking a sheep by hand is not *gozez*, so too shearing a cow is not ordinarily the act of *gozez*, since cows are not shearing animals. Therefore the prohibition simply does not apply.

### Kedushas Bedek HaBayit

The *gemara* challenges this immediately. A *braita* teaches: the verse prohibits working the *bechor* ox and shearing the *bechor* sheep — but from the conjunction (*vav*) the *gemara* derives that both prohibitions apply across categories. You may not work any *bechor*, and you may not shear any *bechor*, even a cow. So the prohibition of *gizah* does apply to a cow that is *bechor* or *kodashim*. Why then does Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam permit cutting the hairs of the *para adumah*?

The *gemara* answers: *ella shani parah dekodshei bedek habayis hi* — the *para adumah* is in a different category of sanctity. Its *kedusha* is classified not as *kedushat korban* but as *kedushat bedek habayit* — a sanctity relating to the Temple's domain and upkeep, not the sanctity of an offering brought on the *mizbe'ach*. The *para adumah* is slaughtered outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, and no genuine *korban* may be brought outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *gemara* therefore understands that the *para*'s sanctity, despite its many *korban*-like features, is legally analogous to *bedek habayit*: it is associated with the *Beit HaMikdash*, but it does not carry the sanctity of an actual offering.

### R. Elazar's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיחַלַּהּ וְלַפְּקַהּ לְחוּלִּין, וְלִיגֻזַּהּ וַהֲדַר לַיקְדְּשַׁהּ! דָּמֶיהָ יְקָרִין. וְלֶיעְבַּד לָהּ כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֶקְדֵּשׁ שָׁוֶה מָנֶה שֶׁחִילְּלוֹ עַל שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה מְחוּלָּל! אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל שֶׁחִילְּלוֹ, לְכִתְחִלָּה מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects to the claim that Akavya ben Mahalalel and the Rabbis agree that if an expert did not deem the firstborn permitted for slaughter before its wool was shed, use of the wool is prohibited. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But let** the owner **desacralize** the heifer **and render it non-sacred, and shear it, and then consecrate it** again. The Gemara explains it is unreasonable to demand he desacralize it, as **its price is expensive,** and it would take an exceptionally large sum of money to desacralize it. The Gemara challenges: **But let him act in** accordance with the statement **of Shmuel, as Shmuel says: A consecrated** animal **worth one hundred dinars that one desacralized upon the value of one *peruta* is desacralized.** The Gemara explains: You can **say that Shmuel said that he has** successfully **desacralized** the animal, but **did he say** an animal may be desacralized in this manner ***ab initio*?** The Gemara therefore concludes that Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam deems it permitted for one to cut the tops of a red heifer's hairs because it is an uncommon case.

The *gemara* objects: but Rabbi Elazar stated earlier in this *masechta* that even *kodshei bedek habayit* are prohibited from shearing and labor — *miderabbanan*. So there remains a rabbinic prohibition of *gizah* even here.

### Rare Case Exception

The *gemara* is not easily satisfied: so what, one cannot simply transgress a *derabbanan*? The answer given is *ella shani parah delo shechicha* — the *para adumah* is different in that this precise situation is extremely rare: a *para adumah* with exactly two hairs that are only partially disqualifying in color, such that clipping them could salvage the animal. A situation so unusual was never within the contemplation of the rabbinic enactment. The Rabbis' prohibition was not intended to extend to such an unlikely case, and particularly where there is a compelling reason to permit it — namely, preserving the *para adumah*'s fitness. So the answer is not that *eino mitkaven* overrides the *derabbanan*; rather, the prohibition was simply never meant to cover this scenario.

### Pidyon Rejected

The *gemara* suggests yet another possible solution: why not simply redeem the *para adumah*'s sanctity? Since it is not a *korban*, its *kedusha* can be transferred without the animal having a *mum*. One could be *mechalel* the *kedusha* — transferring it to money — leaving the animal as *chullin*, then shear it, and re-consecrate it.

### Demei Yakrin

The *gemara* rejects this: *demei yakrin* — the monetary value involved is very large, and it is not readily available to effect a proper *pidyon*.

### Shmuel's Transfer

One might suggest performing the *pidyon* with less than equal value, relying on Shmuel's rule: *hekdesh shaveh maneh shechilelo al shaveh perutah mechulal* — if one redeemed *hekdesh* worth a hundred *maneh* on a coin worth a *perutah*, the redemption nonetheless works. Why not use that principle here? The *gemara* answers: *eima de'amar Shmuel shechilelo* — Shmuel says this works *bedi'avad*, after the fact. *L'chatchilah mi amar* — he never said it is permitted to do so *l'chatchilah*. One is initially required to redeem at full value, and since full value is not easily assembled, the *pidyon* solution is unavailable.

### Rav's Final Position

The *gemara* concludes that Rav rules like Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam, but understands that Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam's reasoning in the *mishna* is the definitional one — plucking by hand is not *gozez* — not the *eino mitkaven* approach, since Rav holds *eino mitkaven* is forbidden.

## R.Y.B.H. True Position

### R.Y.B.H. Permits It

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַב סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם לָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that although **Rav holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam, Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam does not hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav.**

The *gemara* now reaches its conclusion regarding the true position of Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam. Rather than accepting Rav's reading, the *gemara* concludes that Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam himself genuinely holds that *eino mitkaven* is permitted. Rav rules like him on the act itself but for different reasons; Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam's own motivation is *eino mitkaven*.

### Eino Mitkaven Redefined

Crucially, however, the *gemara*'s use of *eino mitkaven* here does not mean the act was accidental. In the *Tosefta*'s case of the *para adumah*, one is deliberately and intentionally cutting the hairs with scissors. The act is entirely purposeful. What makes it *eino mitkaven* is that one has no interest in the product — in the hair itself. The scissors are wielded to restore the animal's eligibility, not to benefit from the wool. One's *kavana* is directed entirely elsewhere.

### Para Proof

The *para adumah* case therefore serves as the demonstration of what *eino mitkaven* means in Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam's system: you care about removing the obstruction, not about acquiring the hair. This is what permits the act despite it being a deliberate cutting.

### Rashi's Approach

This is precisely how Rashi explains *eino mitkaven* throughout this *sugya* — not as accidental removal, but as intentional removal in which the actor is indifferent to the product. Rashi understands this as essentially importing the *Shabbat* concept of *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* into other halachic domains: the classic definition of *gizah* presupposes that one wants the wool; if one is entirely uninterested in it, the act does not constitute *gizah* in the relevant sense. *Tosafot* attempts to read the *mishna* differently — understanding *eino mitkaven* in the more conventional sense of a classically unintended act — but the plain sense of both the *mishna* and the *para adumah* case supports Rashi: the act is intentional, but the interest in the product is absent, and that absence is what defines it as *eino mitkaven*.

## Hand vs. Vessel

### Vetolesh Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוֹלֵשׁ אֶת הַשֵּׂעָר, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְזִיזֶנּוּ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בַּיָּד, אֲבָל בִּכְלִי — אָסוּר. וְהָקָתָנֵי: עוֹשֶׂה מָקוֹם בְּקוֹפִיץ מִיכָּן וּמִיכָּן! תְּנִי: ״לְקוֹפִיץ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: One who is slaughtering a firstborn clears space with a cleaver from here and from there, on either side of the neck, although he thereby **plucks** out **the hair.** He may clear space in this manner **provided that he does not move** the hair **from its place. Rav Ashi says** that **Reish Lakish says: They taught** that this is permitted **only** if one plucks the hair **by hand, but** if he does it **with a tool** it is **prohibited,** as it appears as though he is shearing the firstborn. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** that one may **clear space with a cleaver from here and from there** on either side of the neck? The Gemara answers: **Teach** it as: One may clear space with his hands **for a cleaver,** but he may not clear space with a cleaver.

The *gemara* returns to the text of the *mishna*, which adds: one may pull out the hair, provided one does not remove it entirely from the rest of the wool — it should remain embedded in the fleece. This condition ensures the act does not have the appearance of shearing.

### Rav Ashi: Hand Only

Rav Ashi, citing Reish Lakish, clarifies: *lo shanu ella beyad* — all of the above applies only if one uses one's hand. *Aval bikhli assur* — if one uses any implement, it is forbidden. The reason connects back to the definitional approach: the act is not *gozez* because it is performed by hand in an irregular fashion. The moment one employs an instrument — even one that is not clippers — the act begins to resemble the defined act of *gizah*, and the leniency disappears.

### Cleaver Question

The *gemara* raises an objection from a *braita*: the *mishna* seems to permit *oseh makom bekupitz* — making space by using a cleaver. Does this not imply that using an instrument is permitted?

### Cleaver Space-Clearing

The *gemara* resolves: read *likupitz*, not *bekupitz* — one is not cutting hair with the cleaver, but rather clearing physical space to allow the cleaver itself to be used for *shechita*. The cleaver is the slaughtering implement, and the text means one makes room so that the *shechita* can proceed. The hair removal itself is still done by hand only.

### Hand-Removal Acceptable

The conclusion, therefore, is that hand-removal alone qualifies as *k'lo'achar yad* or as outside the definition of *gizah*, depending on which framework one applies to the *mishna*. Any implement, even an unconventional one, is insufficient to bring the act within the bounds of what is permitted.

## Checking for Blemishes

### Inspection Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן תּוֹלֵשׁ אֶת הַשֵּׂעָר לִרְאוֹת מוּם. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְכַתְּחִלָּה אוֹ דִיעֲבַד? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: תָּא שְׁמַע, צֶמֶר הַמְסוּבָּךְ בָּאוֹזֶן — רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן הַמְשׁוּלָּם אוֹמֵר: תּוֹלְשׁוֹ וּמַרְאֶה אֶת מוּמוֹ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches: **And likewise, one plucks the hair** to enable one of the Sages **to examine the place of a blemish. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is this permitted ***ab initio*, or** only **after the fact? Rabbi Yirmeya said: Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: If there is **wool that is entangled in** a firstborn's **ear,** and it is obscuring a blemish that must be examined, **Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam says: One plucks** the wool **and shows** the animal's **blemish** to a Sage in order to determine whether it is permitted to slaughter the animal outside the Temple. One can **conclude from** the *baraita* that it is permitted ***ab initio*.** The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is so.

The *mishna* adds a parallel case: *vekhen tolesh et ha-se'ar lir'ot mekom mum* — one may similarly pull out hair in order to inspect the site of a potential blemish. The question is what this teaches that is not already implicit in the preceding ruling.

### L'chatchila vs. Bi'di'avad

The *gemara* raises a *ibaya lehu*: should this second clause be read as permitting the removal *l'chatchilah* — actively and deliberately — or only as a *bedi'avad* description, meaning that if hair came loose while one was trying to inspect the wound, one is permitted after the fact to leave it embedded in the wool rather than having to entirely undo anything?

### Tosefta: Ear Wool

Rabbi Yirmiya brings a proof from a *Tosefta*. There is a case of wool that has become entangled inside the ear of the animal, concealing a potential blemish. One wishes to inspect the blemish.

### R.Y.B.H. Allows Plucking

Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam rules: *tolesho umareh et mumo* — pluck it out and show the blemish to whoever needs to inspect it. This is a clear, active instruction to remove the wool.

### L'chatchila Inference

From this ruling, *shema mina l'chatchilah* — one may conclude that removing wool for inspection purposes is permitted *l'chatchilah*, deliberately and actively, not merely *bedi'avad*.

### Rav Mari's Point

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, וְכֵן הַתּוֹלֵשׁ אֶת הַשֵּׂעָר לִרְאוֹת מְקוֹם מוּם. מַאי ״וְכֵן״? אִילֵּימָא שֶׁלֹּא יְזִיזֶנּוּ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ — הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה שׁוֹחֵט, דִּשְׁחִיטָתוֹ מוֹכַחַת עָלָיו, לֹא יְזִיזֶנּוּ — לִרְאוֹת מְקוֹם מוּם מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara corroborates its conclusion. **Rav Mari said: We learn** in the mishna **as well: And likewise, one plucks the hair to examine the place of a blemish. What** is the mishna referring to in its comparison: **And likewise? If we say** it is referring to the mishna's ruling **that** when one slaughters a firstborn he **may not move** the plucked hair **from its place,** then the second *halakha* is unnecessary. **Now** that it is taught that **if,** when **one** comes to **slaughter** the animal, **where its** imminent **slaughter renders it evident** that he does not intend to shear it, but nevertheless **he may not** remove the hair, **is** it **necessary** to teach that one may not remove the plucked hair in order **to examine the place of a blemish?**

Rav Mari argues that this proof is unnecessary because the *mishna* itself already implies *l'chatchilah* by its very structure. The *mishna* states: *vekhen hatolesh et ha-se'ar lir'ot mekom mum* — similarly, one who plucks hair to see the site of the blemish. If this were only *bedi'avad*, the clause would be trivial.

### Shechita Obviousness

Rav Mari's reasoning: in the first clause, when one is removing wool for *shechita*, the context makes obvious why the wool is being pulled — the animal is about to be slaughtered and everyone understands the preparation. Even in that case, where there is no ambiguity about intent, the *mishna* nonetheless requires that the wool not be removed from its place. If in that case, where the reason for the act is self-evident, one must still take care, then all the more so in the case of inspecting a blemish.

### Inspection Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו אַתּוֹלֵשׁ? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, isn't** the mishna's comparison referring **to** the fact that it is permitted to **pluck** the hair *ab initio*? Just as it is permitted to pluck the hair for the purpose of slaughtering the animal, so too, it is permitted to pluck the hair in order to examine a blemish. **Conclude from it** that it is permitted *ab initio*. The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is so.

In the inspection case, the context is less immediately obvious to observers. If the inspection reveals no blemish, the animal will not be slaughtered, and people who see wool being removed may misunderstand. Yet the *mishna* permits removal in that case — which must be *l'chatchilah*, because otherwise stating it would add nothing beyond what the first case already established. One would have said as much from the *shechita* case automatically. The fact that the *mishna* explicitly addresses the inspection case must indicate that it is permitting *l'chatchilah* removal for purposes of inspecting the wound.

The conclusion of this entire section is that both explanations of the *mishna* remain operative throughout the discussion. The definitional approach — that hand-plucking is not *gozez* — is the foundation for what is and is not permitted and how the act must be performed. The *eino mitkaven* approach, understood in Rashi's sense as indifference to the product rather than accidental removal, is what Rabbi Yosei ben HaMeshullam himself invokes as the underlying rationale.

## Bechor Ba'al Mum Hair

### Naturally Fallen Hair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׂעַר בְּכוֹר בַּעַל מוּם שֶׁנָּשַׁר, וְהִנִּיחוֹ בַּחַלּוֹן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ שְׁחָטוֹ — עֲקַבְיָא בֶּן מַהֲלַלְאֵל מַתִּיר,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the hair of a blemished firstborn** animal **that shed** from the animal, **and which one placed in a compartment** for safekeeping, **and thereafter he slaughtered** the animal; given that after the animal dies he is permitted to derive benefit from the hair the animal had on its body when it died, what is the halakhic status of hair that shed from the animal while it was alive? **Akavya ben Mahalalel deems** its use **permitted,**

The *mishna* now turns to a new case: *se'ar bechor ba'al mum shenashar* — the wool of a *bechor* with a *mum* that fell off naturally. Wool that detaches on its own has not been shorn, so in principle it should be entirely permissible. Nevertheless, because a *bechor* carries ongoing restrictions even when it has a *mum*, the practical question arises about when and how one may benefit from that wool.

### Setting Aside

One sets the naturally fallen wool aside — *vehinicho bechalon*, placing it in the cupboard. Before the animal is slaughtered, even that wool cannot be used without the appearance of having violated *gizat bechor*. Observers would not know it fell off naturally; they would assume it was shorn.

### Post-Shechita Usage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לֹא בָּזֶה הִתִּיר עֲקַבְיָא בֶּן מַהֲלַלְאֵל, אֶלָּא שְׂעַר בַּעַל מוּם שֶׁנָּשַׁר וְהִנִּיחוֹ בַּחַלּוֹן וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת — בָּזֶה עֲקַבְיָא בֶּן מַהֲלַלְאֵל מַתִּיר וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **and the Rabbis deem** its use **prohibited;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei said to him:** It was **not with regard to that** case **that Akavya ben Mahalalel deemed** use of the wool permitted. **Rather,** it was in the case of **the hair of a blemished** firstborn animal **that shed** from the animal **which one placed in a compartment and thereafter** the animal **died.** It was **in that** case that **Akavya ben Mahalalel deems** use of the wool **permitted, and the Rabbis deem** its use **prohibited** even after its death.

After the animal is subsequently slaughtered — *ve'achar kach shachato* — the question is whether one may now use the wool that was set aside. Once slaughter occurs, one is freely permitted to benefit from the animal's wool. So perhaps the earlier-fallen wool may also now be used without concern.

Akavya ben Mahalalel permits it. Since after *shechita* one may use the wool one shears from the carcass, there is no longer any confusion about the earlier wool — it is simply additional wool that one is permitted to use. The prohibition on *gizah* was never violated since the wool fell off naturally, and now that the animal is slaughtered, there is nothing misleading about benefiting from it.

The *chachamim* forbid it. Their concern, as Rashi explains, is that if one knows one can benefit from wool that falls off naturally, one will delay performing *shechita*, hoping more wool will fall off in the interim. This delay creates the risk of misuse — one might eventually come to shear the animal deliberately, or to use it for labor, violating the prohibitions that apply to *pesulei ha-mukdashin*.

Rashi makes the concern concrete: the longer the animal is kept around, the more wool falls off, and the greater the temptation to delay *shechita* further. The prohibition on benefiting from the naturally fallen wool before *shechita* is therefore a prophylactic measure.

Rabbi Yehuda frames the debate differently: he reports it as pertaining specifically to when the animal *died* rather than was slaughtered. In that case, the dynamics are more complex, and the *gemara* will clarify this below.

---

## Mixed Wool Case

### Embedded Hair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צֶמֶר הַמְדוּלְדָּל בַּבְּכוֹר, אֶת שֶׁנִּרְאֶה עִם הַגִּיזָּה — מוּתָּר, וְאֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִרְאֶה עִם הַגִּיזָּה — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **wool that is dangling from a firstborn** animal, i.e., which was not completely shed, **that which appears** to be part **of the fleece** is **permitted** when the animal is shorn after its death, **and that which does not appear** to be part **of the fleece** is **prohibited.**

The *mishna* continues with a further refinement: *tzemer ha-meduldal be-vchor* — wool that has come loose from the skin of the *bechor* but has not fully separated, remaining embedded within the rest of the animal's fleece.

### Indistinguishable Hair

If after *shechita* one shears the animal and that loosened wool blends in entirely with the rest — *et she-nireh im ha-gizah muttar* — it is permitted. When it appears indistinguishable from the freshly shorn wool, using it raises no concern.

### Clearly Separate Hair

But if the embedded wool is still visibly distinguishable after shearing — *et she-eino nireh im ha-gizah assur* — it is forbidden. It is apparent that this wool detached before *shechita*, and using it falls under the prohibition that the *chachamim* maintain against benefiting from wool that fell off the *bechor* while alive.

### Appearance Principle

The operative principle is appearance: wool that looks indistinguishable from legitimately shorn post-*shechita* wool is treated as such and is permitted. Wool that visibly stands apart, making clear to any observer that it detached earlier, remains under the *chachamim*'s restriction.

### Chachamim's Stance

The *chachamim*'s position is that no benefit should be derived under any circumstance from wool that fell off the *bechor* while alive, even after *shechita* — unless the appearance of the wool after shearing is such that it cannot be distinguished from legitimate post-*shechita* shearings.

## Death vs. Shechita

### Logical Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִכְּלָל דְּאָסַר, הַשְׁתָּא בְּמֵת — שָׁרֵי, שְׁחָטוֹ מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Rabbi Yosei's statement, that it was not with regard to the case where the animal was slaughtered that Akavya ben Mahalalel deemed use of the wool permitted, apparently indicates **by inference that** Akavya ben Mahalalel **deemed** use of the wool **prohibited** if the animal was slaughtered. This is difficult: **Now** that it has been taught that use of the detached wool of **a dead** firstborn **is permitted,** despite the fact that the attached hair must be buried, **is it necessary** to teach that detached wool is permitted in a case where **one slaughtered** the animal, where the use of the attached hair is permitted?

The *gemara* presses on Rabbi Yosei's statement that Akavya ben Mahalalel's ruling applied not to the *shechita* case but to the death case. The *gemara* asks: *michlal de'assur*? Does this imply that the *shechita* case would be forbidden? Surely the death case is more severe — when the animal dies unsanctioned, the wool attached to it must be buried together with the animal. If even there Akavya permits the use of wool that had previously fallen off, how much more so would he permit it in the *shechita* case.

### Death Case Severity

When a *bechor* with a *mum* dies without *shechita*, the carcass must be buried; no benefit may be derived from it. In that context, the separately-fallen wool has an even stronger claim to be treated independently — it never became part of the buried carcass, and one clearly cannot derive benefit from the animal itself.

### Shechita Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לֹא בָּזֶה הִתִּיר עֲקַבְיָא וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִין, שְׁחָטוֹ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל שְׁרֵי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּמֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Yosei means that it was **not in that** case that **Akavya deemed** use of the detached wool **permitted** while **the Rabbis deem** it **prohibited,** since if **he slaughtered** the animal, **everyone agrees** that use of the wool is **permitted. They disagree** in a case **where** the detached wool came from **a dead** animal that was not slaughtered.

When the animal was slaughtered properly, *divrei hakol shari* — everyone agrees that the wool that fell off earlier is permitted. Once *shechita* permits one to benefit fully from the animal, there is no remaining basis to restrict the earlier-fallen wool.

### Death Case Debate

*Ki pligi* — the debate concerns only the death case. This is the genuinely contested situation: the animal has died, the carcass must be buried, yet some wool fell off earlier. Akavya permits using that wool; the *chachamim* forbid it. Both positions are logical given the competing concerns.

---

## Expert Inspection Risk

### Expert Permits Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁהִתִּירוֹ מוּמְחֶה, דְּמָר סָבַר: גָּזְרִינַן דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְשַׁהוֹיֵיהּ, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא גָּזְרִינַן, אֲבָל לֹא הִתִּירוֹ מוּמְחֶה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi says** that **Reish Lakish says:** The **dispute** applies in a case **where an expert deemed** the animal **permitted** for slaughter due to a blemish before the wool was shed, **as** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **we decree** that use of the wool the animal sheds is prohibited, **lest one come to postpone** its slaughter in order to collect the wool that it sheds. The Rabbis wanted a blemished firstborn to be slaughtered as soon as possible, lest one shear it or use it for labor, both of which are prohibited by Torah law. **And** one **Sage,** Akavya ben Mahalalel, **holds** that **we do not** issue such **a decree. But** in a case where **an expert did not deem** the animal **permitted** for slaughter before its wool was shed, **all agree** that use of the wool is **prohibited,** even after the animal died or was slaughtered.

Rav Assi, citing Reish Lakish, provides a further refinement: *machlokes beshehitiru mumche* — the debate between Akavya ben Mahalalel and the *chachamim* applies specifically to a case where a *mumche*, a qualified expert, has already examined the animal and confirmed the *mum* as genuine, thereby certifying that the animal may be slaughtered.

### Delay Concern

One opinion holds: *gazrinan dilma ati lish-hu-ye* — even after the expert's certification, we are concerned that one will delay *shechita* in order to allow more wool to fall off naturally. This is the position of the *chachamim* who forbid benefiting from the fallen wool: the permission to benefit creates an incentive to postpone, which creates a risk of misuse.

### No Safeguard Needed

The other opinion holds: *lo gazrinan* — no such safeguard is necessary. Once the *mum* has been officially certified, the time horizon for *shechita* is clear and the concern about indefinite delay is unrealistic. How much wool can fall off, after all? The concern does not rise to the level that would justify forbidding the wool.

### Pre-Inspection Case

But what of the case where the expert has *not* yet certified the *mum* — *lo hitiru mumche*?

### All Forbid Pre-Inspection

Here *divrei hakol assur* — all opinions agree that one may not benefit from the fallen wool. This is not contested.

### Urgency Distinction

The reason for the distinction is the urgency calculus. Once a *mumche* has inspected and approved, one already knows the animal's status and presumably will act on it. Even the stricter opinion was only applying a rabbinic safeguard against delay. But when no expert has yet ruled, one has absolutely no incentive to hurry. The animal might turn out to have no *mum* at all, in which case it would eventually be brought as a *korban*. With no urgency — and indeed an interest in delay, since delaying accumulates fallen wool — the concern about misuse is acute. Additionally, as Rashi notes, wool that fell off before the *mum* was officially confirmed carries a more uncertain status: it fell off when the animal's classification was still *kodshim* of the ordinary kind, before any path toward permitted use had been established, and therefore its status at the moment of separation was more deeply problematic.

## Mixed Herds Question

### One Bad Animal Forbids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין אוֹסְרִין בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יְבַקֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Blemished** animals that become intermingled with unblemished animals **render** the entire group of animals **prohibited in any** ratio, even if there was only one blemished animal. Since blemished animals may not be sacrificed, and it is impossible to distinguish them from the unblemished animals, none may be sacrificed on the altar. **And Rabbi Yosei says: Let** the matter **be investigated.**

The *gemara* raises an objection from Rav Sheshes: *ba'alei mumim osrin bechol shehu* — if a *bechor* with a *mum* became mixed into a flock of other animals, even one *ba'al mum* in a thousand renders the entire flock forbidden, since any one of them might be the *bechor*.

### Rabbi Yosei's Solution

Rabbi Yosei says: *yivaker* — inspect, check it out.

### Yivaker Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: מַאי ״יְבַקֵּר״? אִלֵּימָא דְּבַעַל מוּם הוּא, וְלִישְׁקְלֵיהּ — מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא אָמַר לָאו?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet continues: **And we discussed it,** and inquired: **What** did Rabbi Yosei mean when he said: **Let** the matter **be investigated? Shall we say** he meant **that** one should search for **the blemished animal and remove it** from the group, so that the other animals may be slaughtered on the altar? This is difficult, as in that case, **by inference, the first *tanna* said** that in such a case the remaining unblemished animals are still **not** permitted. This cannot be so, as there is no reason to deem the remaining animals prohibited once the blemished animal has been removed.

The *gemara* asks: *mai yivaker* — what exactly does this mean?

At first glance, one might read it as: identify which animal has the *mum* and remove it from the flock, whereupon the rest are permitted. But this raises an obvious difficulty: would the first *tanna* really hold that you cannot simply identify and remove the blemished animal? Some element of the case must prevent straightforward identification, otherwise the *tanna kamma*'s position makes no sense.

### Wool Mix, Not Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: הָכָא בְּגִיזַּת בְּכוֹר בַּעַל מוּם עָסְקִינַן, שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ בְּגִיזֵּי חוּלִּין. וּמַאן תַּנָּא קַמָּא? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: נִשְׁחֲטוּ — אָסְרִי רַבָּנַן. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: נִשְׁחֲטוּ — שָׁרוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** And **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: Here we are dealing with** a case **of** the **shearings of a blemished firstborn, whose** detached wool **became intermingled with non-sacred wool. And who is the first *tanna*,** who holds that all the wool is prohibited? **It is Rabbi Yehuda, who says** in the mishna here that if the firstborn **were slaughtered, the Rabbis deem** use of their wool **prohibited. And Rabbi Yosei** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as he says:** If the firstborn **were slaughtered, the Rabbis deem** use of their wool **permitted.**

Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, clarifies: *hacha begizas bechor ba'al mum askinan* — the case is not about the animals themselves becoming mixed but about their shearings. The hair that fell off a *bechor ba'al mum* became mixed with the shearings of ordinary *chullin* animals.

Specifically, *shenisarvu begizei chullin* — the fallen hair commingled with regular wool. That forbidden hair, now mixed into a larger batch of permitted wool, renders all of it problematic.

---

## Two Positions Clarified

### R. Yehuda Position

The first *tanna* in the *baraita* about mixed animals is Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that even after *shechita* the Rabbis prohibited the fallen wool. And Rabbi Yosei follows his own reasoning — *v'Rabbi Yosei l'taamei*. Since Rabbi Yosei holds that after *shechita* the wool was always permitted — the only debate being the death case — that wool was never forbidden to begin with. Therefore when it fell off and mixed with other wool, it mixed as permitted material and there is no problem.

### R. Yosi's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי: ״יְבוּקַּר״, מַאי ״יְבוּקַּר״? לָאו אִי ״מוּם קָבוּעַ״ אִי ״מוּם עוֹבֵר״ הוּא, אַף עַל גַּב שֶׁלֹּא הִתִּירוֹ מוּמְחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet explains his objection to the interpretation of Reish Lakish: **And it is taught** that Rabbi Yosei says: **Let** the matter **be investigated. What** does he mean when he says: Let the matter **be investigated?** Does this **not** mean that an expert examines the animal to determine **whether** it is **a permanent blemish,** in which case the intermingled wool is permitted, or **whether it is a temporary blemish** and the use of the wool is prohibited? If so, Rabbi Yosei holds that wool shed from a firstborn is permitted **even though** it was shed before the **expert deemed** the blemish to be permanent and the animal **permitted** for slaughter. This contradicts the interpretation of Reish Lakish.

*D'tanya yivakar* — what then does *yivaker* mean in Rabbi Yosei's position? The *gemara* suggests it means checking whether the *mum* was permanent or transitory — *im mum kavua im mum over hu*. If the fallen hair came from an animal with a genuine, permanent *mum*, then once the animal is slaughtered the hair was always permitted and the mix is fine. But one must first confirm that the *mum* was indeed permanent; if it might have been transitory and healed, the animal's status was never definitively that of a *ba'al mum*, and the hair's permission cannot be assumed.

### Rava's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא; יְבוּקַּר, אִי הִתִּירוֹ מוּמְחֶה — אִין, וְאִי לָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in response: **No,** Rabbi Yosei meant that the matter **should be investigated** to determine **if an expert had deemed** the firstborn **permitted** for slaughter before its wool was shed. If he did, then **yes,** the wool is permitted; **and if not,** it is **not** permitted.

Rava disagrees with this interpretation. *Yivakar* does not mean checking whether the *mum* is permanent or transitory. Rather, it means: find out whether the animal has already been inspected and approved by a *mumche*. The question is not the nature of the *mum* but its certification status. If it has been approved by an expert, the wool is permitted; if not, it remains forbidden — consistent with the earlier ruling of Rav Assi.

## Babylonian Critiqued

### Jerusalem Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיק רָבִין, אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה. אֲמַר: בַּבְלָאֵי טַפְשָׁאֵי, מִשּׁוּם דְּיָתְבִי בְּאַרְעָא דַּחֲשׁוֹכָא אָמְרִי שְׁמַעְתָּא דִּמְחַשְּׁכָן! לָא שְׁמִיעַ לְהוּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁבָּדַק וְלֹא מָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When Rabin ascended** from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, **he stated this *halakha*,** including Rav Naḥman's interpretation of the *baraita*, **in the presence of Rabbi Yirmeya.** Rabbi Yirmeya **said: Foolish Babylonians! Because they dwell in a dark land, they state *halakhot* that are dim. Did they not hear that which Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** That mishna is dealing with the animals themselves, not their wool, and the **dispute** between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yosei is **with regard to** a case **where one examined** the flock **and did not find** the blemished animal?

When Rabin traveled from Babylon to *Eretz Yisrael* and related this entire discussion to Rabbi Yirmiya, Rabbi Yirmiya responded with the famous rebuke: *Bavla'ei tipsha'ei*, stupid Babylonians — *mishum d'yatvi b'ara d'chashocha*, because they dwell in a land of darkness, *amri shmata d'machshchan*, they produce discussions that are themselves dark. The framing of the debate in terms of expert inspection and *mumche* certification is, to Rabbi Yirmiya, a distortion of the actual case.

### Correct Framework

Rabbi Yirmiya invokes the teaching of Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *machlokes b'shebadak v'lo matza* — the dispute concerns a case where you have inspected the entire flock and found no animal with a *mum*. The mixed-up animal with a *mum* has been absorbed into the flock without identification, you have checked every single animal, and you found nothing.

### Found Nothing Case

This is the real *machloket*: you searched thoroughly and came up empty. One opinion says the entire flock remains forbidden — since the *mum* animal was in there, you cannot assume it simply vanished, and you have not definitively resolved the problem. The other opinion says a thorough inspection that finds nothing clears the doubt. This dispute, in Rabbi Yirmiya's view, is the principled disagreement underlying the case, not the Babylonian framing in terms of *mumche* certification.

## Finding the Source

### R. Meir's Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן תַּנָּא קַמָּא? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: נִשְׁחֲטוּ — אָסְרִי רַבָּנַן. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: נִשְׁחֲטוּ — שָׁרוּ רַבָּנַן. וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבָּנַן, דִּתְנַן, שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁהָיָה בְּחֶזְקַת טוּמְאָה — לְעוֹלָם הוּא בְּטוּמְאָתוֹ, עַד שֶׁתִּיוָּודַע הַטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** And Aḥai the Distinguished also inquired **with regard to** whether the pig-like animal is permitted for **consumption, and** this question applies **even according to** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: If you say** he **holds** that **an animal that has given birth** might **be merciful** and nurse another mother's offspring, perhaps that is so only with regard to an animal **that** is of **its** own **species,** but to an animal **that is not** of **its** own **species** it is **not** merciful, which means the pig-like animal is kosher. **Or perhaps** a mother might **also be merciful** toward an offspring **that is not** of **its** own **species,** in which case the pig-like animal might not be kosher and therefore may not be eaten. After explaining the dilemma of Aḥai the Distinguished, the Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* identifies the underlying *machloket* as that of Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim*, as found in a *mishna*. The case concerns a field in which a grave was known to be buried somewhere, but the location is unknown. Anyone entering the field is presumed *tamei*. You excavate the entire field and find nothing. Rabbi Meir holds: *kol davar shehaya b'chezkat tumah l'olam hi b'tumatah ad sheyitvada ha'tumah* — anything that was presumed *tamei* remains in its *tumah* until the source of *tumah* is positively identified. Failing to find the grave does not remove the presumption; the presumption persists until you actually locate what you are looking for.

### Chachamim Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חוֹפֵר עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לְסֶלַע אוֹ לִבְתוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** He continues **digging until he reaches bedrock or virgin** soil, under which there is certainly no ritual impurity. If he searched this extensively and failed to discover any impurity, it is evidently no longer there. Similarly, in the case of the *baraita*, the dispute is whether or not failure to locate the blemished animal constitutes sufficient reason to conclude that it is no longer intermingled with the rest of the flock. Accordingly, the first *tanna* holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir while Rabbi Yosei holds in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis.

The *chachamim* disagree: *chofer ad shemagia l'sela* — one digs until reaching bedrock or virgin soil. If after exhaustive excavation no grave is found, the field is presumed clean. A thorough inspection that produces no evidence can override a prior presumption of *tumah*.

Rabbi Meir's view is that the presumption is not lifted merely by the failure to find evidence — *ad sheyitvada ha'tumah*, until the source itself is identified. The *chachamim*'s view is that a sufficiently thorough inspection substitutes for positive identification.

The parallel to the blemish case is direct. If the *mum* animal was mixed into the flock and cannot be identified, does the flock remain forbidden (Rabbi Meir's position) or does a thorough inspection that finds nothing clear the problem (the *chachamim*)?

## Identifying the Blemish

### Found the Blemish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת כְּשֶׁבָּדַק וּמָצָא, וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another version of Rabbi Yoḥanan's interpretation of the *baraita*: **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says: The **dispute** is with regard to a case **where he examined** the flock **and found** the blemished animal, **and they disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute** between **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

Rav Assi, citing Rabbi Yochanan, frames the debate differently: *machlokes b'shebadak u'matza* — the dispute is in a case where you did inspect the flock and did find an animal with a *mum*. But there remains a question: is this the same *mum* animal that caused the problem, or a different one?

### Grave Identity

The *gemara* identifies the parallel *machloket* as that of Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, concerning a field in which a grave was known to be lost. Someone enters the field and contracts *tumah*. Then a grave is found in the field.

### Same Grave View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: שָׂדֶה שֶׁאָבַד בָּהּ קֶבֶר, נִכְנָס בְּתוֹכָהּ — טָמֵא. נִמְצָא בְּתוֹכָהּ קֶבֶר, נִכְנָס לְתוֹכָהּ — טָהוֹר, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: הוּא קֶבֶר שֶׁאָבַד הוּא קֶבֶר שֶׁנִּמְצָא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: תִּיבָּדֵק כׇּל הַשָּׂדֶה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a field in which a grave was lost, one who enters it** becomes **impure** due to the possibility that he passed over the grave and thereby contracted ritual impurity. **If a grave was** subsequently **found in it, one who enters** the field not in the place of the found grave remains **pure, as I say: The grave that was lost is the grave that was found.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Let the entire field be examined** before ruling that one who enters it remains pure. Similarly, in the *baraita* concerning the intermingled blemished and unblemished animals, the first *tanna* holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, while Rabbi Yosei holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

Rabbi holds: *shani omer hu kever she'avad hu kever shenimtza* — the grave that was lost is the grave that was found. Since the field was searched and only one grave emerged, one may assume it is the same one. Accordingly, the person who entered the field is now *tahor*: the lost grave has been located and delineated, and one who does not walk over it is not *tamei*.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: one cannot assume the found grave is the one that was lost. There may be another grave elsewhere in the field. One must excavate the entire field before the situation is resolved. Similarly, in the *baraita* concerning the intermingled blemished and unblemished animals, the first *tanna* holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, while Rabbi Yosei holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

### Why Rav Assi Didn't Adopt Chiyya's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אַסִּי, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא? אָמַר לָךְ: בִּשְׁלָמָא גַּבֵּי טוּמְאָה, אֵימַר בָּא עוֹרֵב וּנְטָלָהּ, בָּא עַכְבָּר וּנְטָלָהּ, אֶלָּא הָכָא — בַּעַל מוּם לְהֵיכָא אָזֵיל? וְאִידַּךְ? אֵימַר מוּם עוֹבֵר הֲוָה וְאִיתַּסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Asi, what is the reason he did not state** the explanation **of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba?** Rabbi Asi could have **said to you: Granted, with regard to impurity** one can say the impure object might no longer be there, as perhaps **a raven came and took it** or **a mouse came and took it. But here,** with regard to a blemished animal that became intermingled with unblemished animals, **where** could the **blemished** animal have **gone?** The Gemara asks: **And** how would **the other** *amora*, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, respond to this claim? He would **say** the blemish **was a temporary blemish and it was healed,** which is why there is no longer a blemished animal among the flock.

The *gemara* asks why Rav Assi did not adopt Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba's framing — the case where one inspected and found nothing — and instead favored the case where one inspected and found something.

Rav Assi explains: *bishlama gabei tumah*, regarding *tumah*, the case where one inspects and finds nothing is intelligible. There are natural explanations for why the source of *tumah* might disappear.

*Eimar ba oreiv unetalah, ba achbar unetalah* — perhaps a raven came and carried away the corpse. If the burial was without a casket and the body has been subject to decomposition, or has been carried off by scavengers, one can truly believe the field is now clean. The disappearance of the *tumah* source is physically explicable.

*Ela hacha* — but in the blemish case, this reasoning does not apply.

*Bemum leheichan azal* — where did the blemish go? A physical blemish on a living animal does not simply vanish the way a corpse does. You cannot point to a raven that carried off the *mum*. If you inspected all the animals and found no blemish, the situation is genuinely puzzling, and Rav Assi does not find it a plausible scenario for a *machloket*.

The *gemara* responds: *eimar mum over hayah* — perhaps it was a transitory *mum* that healed. Not every blemish is permanent; some conditions resolve themselves. If it was a *mum over*, its disappearance is entirely explicable, and the *machloket* about whether a clean inspection clears the doubt is well-founded.

### Why Chiyya Wouldn't Adopt Rav Assi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי אַסִּי? אָמַר לָךְ: בִּשְׁלָמָא שָׂדֶה, דִּרְכַּהּ לְמִיקְבַּר בַּהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּקְבַר בָּהּ הַאי — קְבַר אִינָשׁ אַחֲרִינָא. אֶלָּא קָדָשִׁים, כֵּיוָן דִּבְדִקִי לַן וְקָיְימִי, דִּרְכַּיְיהוּ לְמִיפַּל בְּהוּ מוּמָא? וְאִידַּךְ — אַגַּב דִּמְנַגְּחִי אַהֲדָדֵי, שְׁכִיחַ בְּהוּ מוּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, what is the reason he did not state** the explanation **of Rabbi Asi?** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba could have **said to you: Granted,** in the case of **a field it is normal** for people to **be buried in it,** and therefore Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel claims that **just as this** person **was buried in it,** so too, **another person** might have been **buried** there. Therefore, there is no reason to assume the grave that was found is the grave that was lost. **But** with regard to **sacrificial** animals, **once they were examined and they are** found to be whole, is it **normal for them to** suddenly **develop a blemish?** In such a case even the first *tanna* would not deem the animals prohibited, as it can be assumed they found the only blemished one. **And** what would **the other** *amora*, Rabbi Asi, say in response? **Since they gore each other,** the development of **a blemish is** in fact **common among them.**

Conversely, why did Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba not adopt Rav Assi's framing — the case where one found an animal with a *mum* and must determine if it is the same one?

*Bishlama sadeh* — regarding a field, the premise that finding one grave might not resolve the original problem is entirely reasonable. A field is a place where people are buried; *ki heichi dekavar behai kavar inish achrina* — just as one person was buried there, another might have been buried there as well. The assumption is that a field would have multiple graves is natural and justifies continued excavation.

*Ella kodashim* — but for consecrated animals, this reasoning does not work. These animals are pre-checked before sanctification. *Keivan debedkinhu vekaymi* — they were inspected and confirmed blemish-free before being brought into the *kodashim* category.

*Vedarchaihu lemipal behu muma* — is it the normal course of events for *kodashim* animals, once pre-inspected, to develop blemishes? No. It is unlikely. Therefore, if you check the flock and one animal has a *mum*, it is almost certainly the same animal that caused the original concern. The premise that there might be multiple *mum* animals, which drives Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's requirement to check the entire field, does not translate naturally to a pre-inspected flock of *kodashim*.

*Agav deminkachi ahadadi, shachich behu muma* — the *gemara* provides a counter: once the animals are together, they butt heads and rub against one another. Blemishes can arise. Even a pre-inspected flock of *kodashim* can develop new blemishes through the ordinary jostling of a herd. Therefore, finding one *mum* does not necessarily identify the original problem animal, and the debate about whether one may stop checking is meaningful and coherent.
