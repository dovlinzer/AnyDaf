# Shevuot 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shevuat Bituy Overview

### Opening & Parameters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶחָד דְּבָרִים שֶׁל עַצְמוֹ וְאֶחָד דְּבָרִים שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים, וְאֶחָד דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן מַמָּשׁ וְאֶחָד דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one unwittingly takes a false oath about the past or breaks an oath he made about the future, **both** if it is an oath that addresses **matters that concern oneself and** if it is an oath that addresses **matters that concern others,** he is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering for an oath on an utterance. **And** likewise, an oath on an utterance may address **both tangible matters and intangible matters.**

The daf opens with the *mishnah* on *shevuat bituy* — the oath addressed by the verse *lehara o leheitiv u'lechol asher yevatei ha'adam bishvuah*, the oath to do good, to do bad, or anything a person enunciates with an oath. The Torah specifies that when such an oath is violated *b'shogeg*, with the matter having become hidden from him (*vene'elam mimenu*), the person brings a *korban oleh v'yored*. The *daf* examines the basic parameters of this *shevuah* and the various debates that define its scope.

### Mishnah Four Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? אָמַר ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֶתֵּן לְאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶתֵּן״; ״שֶׁנָּתַתִּי״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא נָתַתִּי״;
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** Examples of oaths about future actions that concern others are if one **said:** On my **oath I will give so-and-so** a particular item, **or:** On my oath **I will not give** it to him. Examples of such oaths about the past are if one said: On my oath **I gave** another a particular item, **or:** On my oath **I did not give** it to him.

The *mishnah* opens by establishing two axes along which *shevuat bituy* operates. The first axis is *echad devarim shel atzmo v'echad devarim shel acherim* — the *shevuah* applies whether it concerns something you yourself will do or something that involves another person. This requires some nuance: an oath that Reuven will give Shimon a hundred dollars is not in your control, and such an oath would not be included. The cases involving others that *are* included are those still within the swearer's own power, as we shall see. The second axis is *v'echad devarim she'yesh bahen mamash v'echad devarim she'ein bahen mamash* — whether the oath pertains to something of concrete substance or something without substance, whether it relates to a physical object or not.

### Mishnah Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שֶׁאִישַׁן״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אִישַׁן״; ״שֶׁיָּשַׁנְתִּי״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא יָשַׁנְתִּי״; ״שֶׁאֶזְרוֹק צְרוֹר לַיָּם״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶזְרוֹק״; ״שֶׁזָּרַקְתִּי״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא זָרַקְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** Examples of oaths about the future that address intangible matters are where one said: On my oath **I will sleep, or:** On my oath **I will not sleep.** Examples of such oaths about the past are where one said: On my oath **I slept, or:** On my oath **I did not sleep.** Other examples of oaths about intangible matters are when one takes an oath, saying: **I will throw a stone into the sea, or: I will not throw** it, or: **I threw** it, **or: I did not throw** it.

The *mishnah* then illustrates these categories with concrete examples. The first example — *amar shevuah she'etein l'ploni v'shelo etein*, an oath to give or not give to someone, *she'natati v'shelo natati*, that I have given or have not given — illustrates a *shevuah* involving another person that remains within one's own control. The second example — *she'ishan v'shelo ishan*, that I will sleep or will not sleep, *she'yashanti v'shelo yashanti*, that I slept or did not sleep — illustrates a *davar she'ein bo mamash*, something without physical substance: sleep is an abstract condition, not a concrete object. The third example — *she'ezrok tzror l'yam v'shelo ezrok, she'zarakti v'shelo zarakti*, that I will throw or will not throw a rock into the sea, that I did throw or did not throw — is explained by Rashi as also capturing the notion of *ein bo mamash*, not in the sense that the rock is intangible, but that the act is entirely neutral, neither beneficial nor harmful. It is neither *lehara* nor *leheitiv* in any real sense, and so it represents a completely gratuitous act.

Each example pairs a positive and a negative formulation, and both a future and a past formulation, and this double pairing will become central to the debates that follow.

### Yishmael vs. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael says: One is liable only** for an oath on an utterance taken **about the future, as it is stated:** "Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips **to do evil, or to do good,** whatsoever it be that a man shall utter clearly with an oath" (Leviticus 5:4). The Torah refers explicitly only to oaths about what one will do in the future.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אִם כֵּן, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן הֲרָעָה וַהֲטָבָה; דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן הֲרָעָה וַהֲטָבָה מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: If so,** and one is liable only for oaths explicitly mentioned in the verse, then **I have** derived **only** that one is liable for an oath on an utterance with regard to **matters to which doing evil and doing good apply. From where** do I derive that one is liable for an oath on an utterance with regard to **matters to which doing evil and doing good do not apply?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מֵרִיבּוּי הַכָּתוּב. אָמַר לוֹ: אִם רִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב לְכָךְ, רִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב לְכָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him:** The *halakha* in these cases is derived **by amplification of** the meaning of **the verse.** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: If the verse is amplified for this,** i.e., to extend the *halakha* of an oath on an utterance to matters that do not involve doing evil or good, **the verse is amplified for that,** i.e., oaths about the past.

The *mishnah* records a significant *machloket* regarding the temporal scope of *shevuat bituy*. Rabbi Yishmael holds *eino chayav ela al ha'atid lavo* — liability attaches only to an oath about what one will or will not do in the future. His source is the verse itself: *lehara o leheitiv*, to do bad or to do good — both formulations are future-oriented.

Rabbi Akiva presses back: if you are going to read the verse so literally, then the *shevuah* should only apply to things that are genuinely harmful or beneficial — *devarim she'yesh bahen hara v'hatavah* — to the exclusion of a completely neutral act like throwing a rock into the sea. How do we know that neutral acts are included? Because the verse continues *l'chol asher yevatei ha'adam bishvuah* — for anything a person enunciates as an oath — which is broadly inclusive. Rabbi Yishmael grants this point. Rabbi Akiva then turns it back on him: once you have already expanded beyond the literal meaning of *lehara o leheitiv* through the inclusive phrase *l'chol asher yevatei*, why stop there? Once neutral acts are included, past acts should equally be included through the same expansion. The *gemara* will shortly examine how far this reasoning extends.

## Nedarim vs. Shevuot

### Nedarim Stringencies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חוֹמֶר בַּנְּדָרִים מִבַּשְּׁבוּעוֹת, חוֹמֶר בַּשְּׁבוּעוֹת מִבַּנְּדָרִים. חוֹמֶר בַּנְּדָרִים – שֶׁהַנְּדָרִים חָלִים עַל דְּבַר מִצְוָה כִּדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּשְּׁבוּעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to oaths about intangible matters, **the Sages taught:** There is **a stringency** that applies **to vows** and not **to oaths,** and there is **a stringency** that applies **to oaths** and not **to vows.** The **stringency** that applies **to vows** is **that vows take effect with regard to a matter** involving **a mitzva, like** they take effect with regard to **an optional matter, which is not the case with regard to oaths.** An oath not to perform a mitzva does not take effect at all.

Before drilling down into the specifics of the *mishnah*'s examples, the *gemara* presents a *baraita* that contrasts *nedarim* and *shevuot* along their respective axes of stringency. *Chomer bin'darim mibishvuot*: a *neder* has a broader scope than a *shevuah* in one critical respect. *Nedarim chalim al devar mitzvah k'dvar reshut* — a *neder* takes effect even on objects associated with a *mitzvah*, ultimately leading to the *mitzvah*'s non-performance. A *shevuah*, by contrast, cannot do this. If someone takes an oath that he will not sit in a *sukkah* or will not take a *lulav*, that *shevuah* does not take effect, because *mushba v'omed m'har sinai* — he is already bound from Sinai to perform the *mitzvah*, and a *shevuah* cannot obligate him to do what the Torah forbids or contradict an existing obligation. A *neder*, however, operates differently: it does not confront the obligation head-on but rather attaches forbidden status to the object. By declaring the *sukkah* or *lulav* prohibited to him, the person is not directly violating his obligation — but when the time comes, he is unable to fulfill it because the object itself has been rendered forbidden. That indirect mechanism is something a *shevuah* cannot replicate.

### Shevuot Stringencies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בַּשְּׁבוּעוֹת – שֶׁהַשְּׁבוּעוֹת חָלוֹת עַל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַמָּשׁ כְּדָבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מַמָּשׁ, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּנְּדָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The **stringency** that applies **to oaths** is **that oaths take effect with regard to an intangible matter like** they do with regard to **a tangible matter, which is not the case with regard to vows.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? אָמַר ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֶתֵּן לִפְלוֹנִי״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶתֵּן״. מַאי ״אֶתֵּן״? אִילֵּימָא צְדָקָה לְעָנִי, מוּשְׁבָּע וְעוֹמֵד מֵהַר סִינַי הוּא – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נָתוֹן תִּתֵּן לוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **How so?** Examples are if one **said:** On my **oath I will give so-and-so** some item, **or:** On my oath **I will not give** it to him. **What** is the case of an oath in which he says: **I will give? If we say** that he takes an oath that he will give **charity to a poor person,** that is not an oath that takes effect, since **he is under oath from Mount Sinai** to give charity, **as it is stated** with regard to a poor person: **"You shall give him"** (Deuteronomy 15:10).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַתָּנָה לְעָשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the case is where he said he would give **a gift to a rich person.** The oath takes effect, as there is no mitzva to do so.

The reverse stringency is *chomer bishvuot*: *hashvuah chalah al davar she'ein bo mamash kedavar sheyesh bo mamash* — a *shevuah* takes effect even on matters without substance, as when a person swears he will not sleep. *Ma she'ein kein binedarim* — a *neder* cannot operate this way, because a *neder* attaches to an object, and sleep is not an object. One might try to formulate it creatively — "my eyes are forbidden to me for the purpose of sleep" — but the sleep itself, as an abstract act, cannot be made *assur* through a *neder*. This contrast sets up the *mishnah*'s discussion nicely, since the examples it provides — giving, sleeping, throwing a rock — each illustrate dimensions that distinguish *shevuot* from *nedarim*.

## Gemara on Examples

### Giving Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שֶׁאִישַׁן״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אִישַׁן״ – אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָאוֹמֵר ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אִישַׁן שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים״ – מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ וְיָשֵׁן לְאַלְתַּר!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one takes an oath, saying: **I will sleep, or: I will not sleep,** the oath takes effect and he is liable to bring an offering if he fails to fulfill the terms of his oath. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that in the case of **one who says:** On my **oath I will not sleep for three days,** the court **flogs him immediately and he may sleep.** Since it is impossible for one not to sleep for three days, his oath is regarded as an oath taken in vain from the outset. How could someone who took an oath never to sleep be liable to bring an offering for an oath on an utterance if he slept unwittingly?

The *gemara* returns to the *mishnah*'s first example: *she'etein l'ploni v'shelo etein*, an oath to give to someone. The *gemara* asks: *mai etein*? If this refers to giving *tzedakah* to a poor person, then this *shevuah* cannot take effect — *mushba v'omed m'har sinai hu*, the person is already obligated, as the verse says *venason titein lo*, you must give. A *shevuah* cannot create a double obligation where you are already bound. The *mishnah* must therefore be speaking of *matanah l'ashir*, a gift to a wealthy person, where there is no pre-existing obligation and the *shevuah* therefore operates normally.

### Sleeping Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם דְּאָמַר ״שְׁלֹשָׁה״, הָכָא דְּלָא אָמַר ״שְׁלֹשָׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring to a case **where he said** explicitly that he will not sleep for **three** days. **Here,** the mishna is referring to a case **where he did not say three** days, and his oath extends only for the amount of time it is possible not to sleep.

The *gemara* then examines the *mishnah*'s second example — an oath about sleeping — and raises a question. Is it really true that an oath not to sleep takes effect as a *shevuat bituy*? But Rabbi Yochanan taught: if someone swears *shelo ishan gimel yamim*, that he will not sleep for three days, we administer *malkus* immediately, and he may then go and sleep. The reason: it is not possible for a person to go three days without sleeping, so this oath is patently impossible — it falls into the category of *shevuat shav*, an oath on something manifestly false, like swearing one can fly. A *shevuat shav* yields *malkus* immediately but does not create the *shevuat bituy* liability of the *korban oleh v'yored*. So how can the *mishnah*'s example about sleep be a *shevuat bituy* at all?

The *gemara* resolves this: *hasam d'amar gimel, hacha d'lo amar gimel* — the case of *shevuat shav* is where one specified three days, an explicitly impossible commitment. In our *mishnah*, no such time frame was specified. If one simply says "I won't sleep," context suggests it means tonight or some limited period, and we do not assume it means forever. Therefore it is not a *shevuat shav*, and the person's compliance or non-compliance with the oath is what determines liability.

### Rock Example & Rav/Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שֶׁאֶזְרוֹק צְרוֹר לַיָּם״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶזְרוֹק״. אִיתְּמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁזָּרַק פְּלוֹנִי צְרוֹר לַיָּם״ וְ״שֶׁלֹּא זָרַק״, רַב אָמַר חַיָּיב, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that an example of an oath on an utterance is where one took an oath, saying: **I will throw a stone into the sea, or: I will not throw** it. It **was stated:** With regard to one who says: On my **oath so-and-so threw a stone into the sea, or:** On my **oath he did not throw** it, **Rav says:** If it was later discovered that what he said was false, he is **liable** to bring an offering for his oath. **And Shmuel says:** He is **exempt.**

The third example of the *mishnah* — throwing a rock into the sea — leads the *gemara* to a new *sugyah*. The *mishnah* presents the case both in the future and past tense: *she'ezrok* and *she'zarakti*. Now a new case is introduced: *shvuah shezarak ploni tzror l'yam v'shelo zarak* — an oath that Ploni threw a rock into the sea or did not. An oath in the future that Reuven *will* throw the rock would not be valid as a *shevuat bituy*, since that is not within one's own control. But what about framing it in the past, according to Rabbi Akiva who holds that past oaths incur liability? Here one might have knowledge about what another person did, and that is not entirely outside one's purview. On this, *Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree. *Rav amar chayav*: one is liable. *Shmuel amar patur*: one is exempt.

## Rav vs. Shmuel Debate

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר חַיָּיב – אִיתֵיהּ בְּלָאו וְהֵן. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר פָּטוּר – לֵיתֵיהּ בִּלְהַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the opinions: **Rav says** that he is **liable,** as the oath **can be positive or negative.** The Sages derived from the verse: "Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips to do evil, or to do good" (Leviticus 5:4), that one is liable to bring an offering for an oath on an utterance only when the oath is such that it could be inverted from the positive to the negative and vice versa. Since he can take an oath either that he threw the stone or that he did not, he is liable. **And Shmuel says** he is **exempt,** because the oath **cannot be** stated **with regard to the future;** he cannot control what so-and-so will do in the future. Consequently, it is an oath taken in vain, rather than an oath on an utterance.

*Rav* holds that one is liable for such an oath — *ita belav hachi* — because the real test for *shevuat bituy* is whether the oath can be cast in both the positive and the negative, *lav v'hein*. Since one can say either "Ploni threw the rock" or "Ploni did not throw the rock," the oath satisfies the criterion of *lehara o leheitiv*. That is the only genuine criterion: can the oath be formulated in the positive, and can the same oath be formulated in the negative? If both are coherent, the oath is valid, and if it turns out to be false, one is liable.

### Shmuel's Position

*Shmuel* holds one is exempt — *leisa b'lehaba* — because there is an additional criterion beyond *lav v'hein*: even according to Rabbi Akiva who extends liability to past oaths, that extension is only to oaths that *could* be formulated as a future-oriented oath. The paradigm for *shevuat bituy* is *lehara o leheitiv*, which is inherently future-directed. A past oath is included only by virtue of the expansion *l'chol asher yevatei*, but that expansion brings in only past formulations that are modeled on a possible future formulation. Since saying "Reuven will throw a rock into the sea" would not be a valid *shevuat bituy* — it is not within one's control — there is no future version of this oath that qualifies, and therefore the past version equally fails to qualify.

### Within Akiva Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְהָרַע אוֹ לְהֵיטִיב״. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אִם כֵּן, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן הֲרָעָה וַהֲטָבָה, דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן הֲרָעָה וַהֲטָבָה מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that **they disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute** between **Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva? As we learned** in the mishna: **Rabbi Yishmael says: One is liable only** for an oath on an utterance taken **about the future, as it is stated:** "Or if anyone take an oath clearly with his lips **to do evil, or to do good,** whatsoever it be that a man shall utter clearly with an oath" (Leviticus 5:4). **Rabbi Akiva said to him: If so,** and one is liable only for oaths explicitly mentioned in the verse, then **I have** derived **only** that one is liable for an oath on an utterance with regard to **matters to which doing evil and doing good apply. From where** do I derive that one is liable for an oath on an utterance with regard to **matters to which doing evil and doing good do not apply?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מֵרִיבּוּי הַכָּתוּב. אָמַר לוֹ: אִם רִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב לְכָךְ, רִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב לְכָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him:** The *halakha* in these cases is derived **by amplification of the verse.** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: If the verse is amplified for this,** i.e., to extend the *halakha* of an oath on an utterance to matters that do not involve doing evil or good, **the verse is amplified for that,** i.e., oaths about the past.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי; הַשְׁתָּא מִילְּתָא דְּאִיתַהּ בִּלְהַבָּא לָא מְחַיַּיב עֲלַהּ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל לְשֶׁעָבַר, מִילְּתָא דְּלֵיתַהּ בִּלְהַבָּא מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Everyone agrees** that **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yishmael** one is exempt from liability for taking an oath that so-and-so threw an item. **Now,** with regard to **a statement,** i.e., an oath, **that could be** a valid oath referring **to the future,** e.g., I will throw an item, **Rabbi Yishmael does not deem** one **liable for it** when it refers **to the past. Is it necessary** to say that one is not liable for **a statement,** i.e., an oath, that so-and-so threw a particular item, **that cannot be** a valid oath when adjusted to be referring **to** someone else's actions in **the future,** since it is not under the oath taker's control?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי – אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַב – כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא; וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עַד כָּאן לָא מְחַיֵּיב רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הָתָם לְשֶׁעָבַר, אֶלָּא מִלְּתָא דְּאִיתַהּ בִּלְהַבָּא – מְחַיֵּיב רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְשֶׁעָבַר; אֲבָל מִידֵּי דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בִּלְהַבָּא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When they disagree,** it is with regard to how to understand the *halakha* **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva.** The opinion of **Rav** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** without any qualification of it. **And Shmuel says: Rabbi Akiva deems one liable only there,** in the case of an oath that refers **to the past,** when it is **a statement,** i.e., an oath, **that could be** a valid oath referring **to the future;** in such a case **Rabbi Akiva deems one liable** also if the oath was taken referring **to the past,** like an oath where one says: I threw a stone. **But** with regard to **statements that cannot** be valid oaths if adjusted **to the future,** like an oath about another's action, he does **not** deem one liable when they are made about the past.

One might initially suppose that *Rav* and *Shmuel* are simply replaying the dispute of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva — whether past oaths carry liability at all. The *gemara* rejects this. *Aliba d'Rabbi Yishmael kulei alma lo peligi*: there is nothing interesting to debate within Rabbi Yishmael's framework, because Rabbi Yishmael categorically excludes all past oaths. Even a past formulation that *could* be made into a future oath — such as "I ate" or "I did not eat," which maps onto "I will eat" or "I will not eat" — is excluded by Rabbi Yishmael, who insists the verse *lehara o leheitiv* refers only to the future. If so, something that cannot even be framed in the future would be excluded a fortiori, with no need for debate. *Ki peligi aliba d'Rabbi Akiva*: the debate is entirely within Rabbi Akiva's framework. *Rav* reads Rabbi Akiva simply as including past oaths, full stop. *Shmuel* reads Rabbi Akiva more narrowly: Rabbi Akiva extends liability to the past only where the oath is modeled on a possible future formulation. Something that cannot be cast in the future — because it concerns another person's actions outside one's control — Rabbi Akiva would also not include.

### Shmuel's Reasoning

The *gemara* now develops *Shmuel*'s reasoning in greater depth. The *gemara* asks whether the debate between *Rav* and *Shmuel* might itself be a reflection of the earlier dispute between Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and the Rabbanan. In the upcoming *mishnah*, there is a dispute about someone who takes a *shevuah lekayem et hamitzvah* — a sworn commitment to perform a *mitzvah* he is already obligated to perform — and then fails to do so. The *Tanna Kamma* says he is exempt, because such a *shevuah* is not valid as a *shevuat bituy*: since a *shevuah* to *negate* the *mitzvah* would not take effect (one cannot swear to violate an obligation), the positive and negative forms are not both valid, and the criterion of *lav v'hein* is not met. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira argues one *should* be liable — if the oath works in one direction and you violated it, you should be accountable. The Rabbanan counter: *sheken lo asah bo lav vahen* — a *shevuah* to perform a *mitzvah* fails the *lav v'hein* test, because the reverse oath — "I will not take the *lulav*" — is not a valid *shevuat bituy* but rather a nullified oath that contravenes an obligation.

The *gemara* tentatively suggests that *Rav* follows Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira (no *lav v'hein* requirement) while *Shmuel* follows the Rabbanan (*lav v'hein* is required). But the *gemara* rejects this, establishing a more refined hierarchy. *Aliba d'Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira kulei alma lo peligi*: if Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira does not even require *lav v'hein* — the criterion most explicitly rooted in the verse *lehara o leheitiv* — he certainly would not require *lehaba*, that the oath be formable in the future, which is a less explicit requirement. So all opinions would agree that within Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's framework, *Rav*'s position trivially follows, and no debate is needed. *Ki peligi aliba d'Rabbanan*: the live question is within the Rabbanan's framework, who do require *lav v'hein*. *Shmuel* holds that just as the Rabbanan derive from *lehara o leheitiv* that the oath must be formable in the positive and negative, they similarly require that a past oath be frameable in the future. *Rav* responds: the *lav v'hein* requirement is explicitly in the verse — *lehara o leheitiv*, positive and negative — and so it is a hard requirement. The *lehaba* requirement, by contrast, rests on the generic expansion *l'chol asher yevatei*, with no textual basis to import into it a future-formulation criterion. Accordingly, even the Rabbanan who require *lav v'hein* would not extend that logic to require *lehaba*, and therefore *Rav* and the Rabbanan alike would hold that a past oath about another person's actions is valid.

## Hamnuna Challenge

### Mishnah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא: ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי הַיּוֹם״ וְ״לֹא הִנַּחְתִּי תְּפִילִּין הַיּוֹם״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״ – חַיָּיב. בִּשְׁלָמָא ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ – אִיתֵיהּ בְּ״לֹא אוֹכַל״; אֶלָּא ״לֹא הִנַּחְתִּי״ – מִי אִיתֵיהּ בְּ״לֹא אַנִּיחַ״?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna raises an objection** to Shmuel's opinion from a mishna (29b): If one said: **I did not eat today, or: I did not don phylacteries today,** and another said to him: **I administer an oath to you** that your statement is true, **and** the original speaker **said: Amen,** he is **liable** for breaking an oath on an utterance if his statement was false, as responding amen to an oath administered to oneself is equivalent to stating the oath explicitly. **Granted,** he is liable in the case where he said: **I did not eat,** since it also **would have been possible** for him to take an oath saying: **I will not eat, but** in the case where he said: **I did not don** phylacteries, **would it have been possible** for him to take an oath saying: **I will not don** phylacteries, and in doing so abrogate a positive mitzva?

Rav Hamnuna raises a challenge to *Shmuel*'s position from a *mishnah* at the end of the chapter. The *mishnah* presents a case where someone says *lo achalti hayom* — I did not eat today — and *lo hinachti tefillin hayom* — I did not put on *tefillin* today — and another person then imposes a *shevuah* upon him (*mashbia ani aleicha*), and he responds *amen*, which is treated as equivalent to having uttered the *shevuah* himself. The *mishnah* rules him liable.

### Problem for Shmuel

Both examples are past-tense oaths, consistent with Rabbi Akiva. The first, *lo achalti* — I did not eat — poses no difficulty for *Shmuel*: one can easily formulate the future version, *lo ochel*, I will not eat. That future oath is a standard *shevuat bituy*, so the past formulation maps cleanly onto it and liability is established even by *Shmuel*'s criterion. But the second example, *lo hinachti tefillin*, is a powerful challenge. One cannot formulate the future version — "I will not put on *tefillin*" — as a valid *shevuat bituy*, because that is a *shevuah levatel et hamitzvah*, an oath to violate an obligation, which does not take effect. Here, then, is a past oath that has no valid future counterpart, yet the *mishnah* rules the person liable — precisely the outcome *Rav* predicts and *Shmuel* should deny.

### Shmuel's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מוֹתֵיב לַהּ וְהוּא מְפָרֵק לַהּ – לִצְדָדִין קָתָנֵי: ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ – לְקׇרְבָּן, ״לֹא הִנַּחְתִּי״ – לְמַלְקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **He,** Rav Hamnuna, **raises the objection and he resolves it:** The *tanna* **teaches** this mishna **disjunctively,** referring to two different cases. When he teaches that one is liable for taking the oath: **I did not eat,** it refers to one's liability **to** bring **an offering.** When he teaches liability for the oath: **I did not don** phylacteries, it refers to one's liability **to** receive **lashes** for taking a false oath. This liability does not require that it be possible to invert the oath to refer to the future.

Rav Hamnuna himself poses the challenge and himself supplies the answer. *Hu masiv lah v'hu mefarek lah*. The *mishnah*, he explains, speaks *l'stama* — it is not internally differentiating between the two cases in terms of the *type* of liability. *Lo achalti l'korban*: the first case, not eating, generates full *shevuat bituy* liability, including the *korban oleh v'yored* if violated *b'shogeg* — because it has a valid future version. *Lo hinachti tefillin l'malkus*: the second case, not having put on *tefillin*, generates only *malkus* liability in the event of willful violation — because the *malkus* prohibition of *lo tishvu vishmi lashaker* / *lo sisa shem* operates without the constraints imposed by the *psukim* governing the *korban oleh v'yored*. The requirements of *lehara o leheitiv*, whether something must be *lehaba*, and related criteria — all of these are features of the *sugyah* of *korban oleh v'yored*. For *malkus* alone, none of these limitations apply.

This is, admittedly, a strained reading of the *mishnah*, given that the entire chapter is focused on the *korban*. The simpler reading — and the reading that flows more naturally — is *Rav*'s: *lo hinachti tefillin* generates *korban* liability even though it lacks a future formulation. *Shmuel*'s reading requires understanding that the *mishnah* silently introduces a case of mere *malkus* amid a chapter devoted to the *korban*. Nevertheless, that is how *Shmuel* must answer.

## Shav vs. Bituy

### Shevuat Shav Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: אֵיזוֹ הִיא שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא? נִשְׁבַּע לְשַׁנּוֹת אֶת הַיָּדוּעַ לָאָדָם – וְאָמַר עַל עַמּוּד שֶׁל אֶבֶן שֶׁהוּא בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי, שֶׁהוּא שֶׁל זָהָב. וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: וְהוּא שֶׁנִּיכָּר לִשְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם. טַעְמָא דְּנִיכָּר; הָא לֹא נִיכָּר – עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי. וְאַמַּאי? הָא אֵינוֹ בִּ״יהֵא שֶׁל זָהָב״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** also **raises an objection** to Shmuel's opinion from a mishna (see 29a): **Which** oath **is an oath** taken **in vain?** It is when one **takes an oath to deny that which is known to people** to be true, **and,** for example, **says about a stone column that is in such and such a place that it is** made **of gold. And Ulla says** that the phrase: Known to people, in the mishna is referring to when **it is** a fact **that is known to three people.** The Gemara explains the objection to Shmuel's opinion: **The reason** he is liable for taking an oath in vain is due to the fact **that** his oath contradicted a fact **known** to three people. **But** if it is **not known, he violates** the prohibition **of** taking a false **oath on an utterance.** Given Shmuel's opinion, **why** should he be liable? **Isn't it impossible** to invert the oath to make it about the future, saying: That column **will be of gold?**

*Rava* raises a further inquiry concerning the boundary between *shevuat shav* and *shevuat bituy*. To sharpen the question, the *gemara* cites a *mishnah* defining *shevuat shav*: if one swears *levatel et hayadu'a la'adam* — to deny or affirm something that is patently known — that is a *shevuat shav*. The example given is swearing *al amud shel even shehu shel zahav* — that a stone pillar is gold. *Ulla* adds a qualification: *v'hu shenikar l'gimel bnei adam* — for it to be *shevuat shav*, the falseness of the claim must be apparent to at least three people. The reasoning is clear: when something is known to everyone to be obviously false, swearing that it is otherwise is an empty, silly oath — it fools no one and is therefore *shav*, vain. But if only one or two people happen to know the truth, and the broader public does not, then a false oath on the matter is a *shevuat sheker* — a genuine lie, a deception — not a *shevuat shav*.

### Stone Example Problem

The *gemara* deduces from this: *taima d'nikar* — the reason it is *shevuat shav* is because the stone-nature of the pillar is plainly evident to everyone. *Ha lo nikar* — but if it were not obvious, say because the stone had been painted to look like gold, then it would *not* be *shevuat shav*, and the implication seems to be that it would be *shevuat bituy*, seemingly making the person liable for a *korban* if he violated it. But this creates a problem, at least for *Shmuel*: such an oath is *leisa l'haba* — one cannot formulate a future version of it. You cannot swear "I will make this stone into gold," because that is not *b'yadecha* — not within human capability.

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מוֹתֵיב לַהּ וְהוּא מְפָרֵק לַהּ: נִיכָּר – עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא, לֹא נִיכָּר – עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **He raises the objection and he resolves it.** When the fact is **known** to three people, the one who takes an oath denying it **violates** the prohibition **of** taking **an oath in vain.** If it is **not known,** then **he violates** the prohibition **of** taking **a false oath,** for which he receives lashes if he did so intentionally. Nevertheless, he is not liable to bring an offering for falsely taking an oath on an utterance, since this oath cannot be inverted to be about the future.

*Hu masiv lah v'hu mefarek lah* — Rava himself poses and resolves the question. If the falseness *is* obvious — that is, three people can see it is stone — then swearing it is gold is *shevuat shav*, and one receives *malkus* for that. If the falseness is *not* obvious — the stone is painted and appears to be gold — then the false oath is *shevuat sheker*, not *shevuat bituy*. The implicit contrast in the *mishnah* between *nikar* and *lo nikar* is not drawing a distinction between *shevuat shav* and *shevuat bituy* at all; it is drawing a distinction between *shevuat shav* and *shevuat sheker*. The *mishnah* is not speaking about the *korban* context in that contrast — it is simply delineating when an oath is empty-and-vain versus when it is an outright lie. Whether the *sheker* case would generate *korban* liability is a separate question entirely not addressed by the *mishnah*, and no inference about the *lehaba* requirement can be drawn from it. This is, in any case, a notably weaker proof than the *tefillin* case, precisely because it can be dispatched so cleanly.

## Abaye on Lav v'Hein

### Testimony Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וּמוֹדֶה רַב בְּאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֲנִי יוֹדֵעַ לְךָ עֵדוּת״, וְאִשְׁתְּכַח דְּלָא יְדַע לֵיהּ – דְּפָטוּר, הוֹאִיל וְלֵיתֵיהּ בִּכְלַל ״שֶׁאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ לְךָ עֵדוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Abaye said: And Rav,** who holds one liable for an oath on an utterance even if it cannot be inverted to refer to the future, **concedes** in a case **where one says to another:** On my **oath I know testimony** that is relevant **to you, and it is found** afterward **that he did not know** testimony that was relevant **to him,** that in **that** case **he is exempt** from bringing an offering for an oath on an utterance. The reason is **that it is not in the category of** an oath that could be inverted to the negative oath of: **I do not know testimony** that is relevant **to you,** as that oath would be an oath of testimony, rather than an oath on an utterance. The Torah states: "And if anyone sin, in that he hears the voice of adjuration, he being a witness, whether he has seen or known, if he do not utter it, then he shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 5:1). One is liable to bring an offering for an oath of testimony if he takes an oath falsely denying that he has knowledge of testimony in a monetary matter.

The *gemara* now records a statement by Abaye: *u'modeh Rav b'omer l'chaveiro shvuah she'ani yode'a lecha edut v'ishtakhakh d'lo yada leih d'patur* — even *Rav* concedes that if someone swore "I know testimony for you" and it turned out he was mistaken, or did not in fact know, he is exempt. This is surprising at first glance. The oath "I know testimony for you" is past-tense, and it has a *lav v'hein* — one could say "I do not know testimony for you." *Rav* does not require *lehaba*, so there should be no obstacle. Why, then, does even *Rav* say the person is exempt?

### Abaye's Innovation

Abaye's innovation is the following: although the positive and negative formulations both exist — "I know" and "I do not know" — they do not occupy the *same* legal category. The positive, "I know testimony for you" (*she'ani yode'a lecha edut*), is a standard statement that would fall under *shevuat bituy* if anything. But the negative, "I do not know testimony for you," is not a *shevuat bituy* — it is a *shevuat ha'edut*, the subject of an entirely separate *parshah* in the Torah. Since the *lav* and the *hein* belong to different categories, they are not considered to constitute a proper *lav v'hein* for the purposes of *shevuat bituy*. For the *lav v'hein* criterion to be satisfied, the positive and negative formulations must both fall within the *same* category of oath. When the negative falls under *shevuat ha'edut* rather than *shevuat bituy*, the criterion is not met, and the positive oath — "I know testimony" — does not generate *korban* liability.

### Past Oaths Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יָדַעְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא יָדַעְתִּי״ – מַחְלוֹקֶת. ״הֵעַדְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא הֵעַדְתִּי״ – מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** But if he takes an oath saying: **I knew** testimony that is relevant to you, **or: I did not know** testimony that is relevant to you, the *halakha* is subject to the **dispute** between Rav and Shmuel. Similarly, if he took an oath saying: **I testified, or: I did not testify,** whether or not he is liable to bring an offering for an oath on an utterance depends on the **dispute** between Rav and Shmuel. In these cases, the oaths cannot be inverted from the past to the future and are also not oaths of testimony, since they do not involve the refusal to testify.

This analysis allows the *gemara* to clarify which past oath cases are subject to the *Rav-Shmuel* debate. *Yadati v'lo yadati* — "I knew testimony" or "I did not know testimony" — this is the *machloket* between *Rav* and *Shmuel*. Both formulations are in *lav v'hein* and both are past-tense, but neither can be made future-oriented (swearing "I will know testimony" or "I will not know testimony" relates to *mitzvah* obligations and does not operate as a standard *shevuat bituy*). Similarly, *he'adati v'lo he'adati* — "I testified" or "I did not testify" — is equally the *machloket*: both are past-tense and in *lav v'hein*, but a future oath about whether to testify would be either a *shevuah lekayem et hamitzvah* or *levatel et hamitzvah*, and therefore not applicable in the *korban oleh v'yored* framework. These cases, then, sit squarely in the dispute between *Rav* and *Shmuel*.

## Shevuat Ha'edut Category

### Shmuel's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר: מִילְּתָא דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בִּלְהַבָּא לָא מְחַיֵּיב עֲלֵיהּ לְשֶׁעָבַר; לְהָכִי אַפְּקַהּ רַחֲמָנָא לִשְׁבוּעַת עֵדוּת מִכְּלַל שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara says: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Shmuel, who says** that an oath about **a matter that cannot** be inverted to refer **to the future** is one **for which one is not liable** when it refers **to the past,** it is **for that** reason **the Merciful One removed an oath of testimony,** which cannot be inverted, **from the category of an oath on an utterance** and legislated it in a separate verse (see Leviticus 5:1).

The *gemara* now raises a foundational question about *shevuat ha'edut* as a separate Torah category. *Bishlama leShmuel*: the question works out elegantly for *Shmuel*. *Shmuel* holds that a past oath generates *korban* liability only if it can be formulated in the future. *Lo yada'ti* — "I do not know testimony for you" — cannot be made future-oriented: "I will not know testimony" is not a proper future *shevuat bituy* (it relates to a *mitzvah* obligation). Therefore, this oath would not be a *shevuat bituy* for *Shmuel*, and it would escape the *korban* framework entirely — unless the Torah created a separate category. *Lehachi afkei Rachmana lishvuat ha'edut michlal shvuat bituy*: that is precisely why the Torah extracted *shevuat ha'edut* from the general framework of *shevuat bituy* and established it as an independent category with its own *korban*. For *Shmuel*, the need for a separate category of *shevuat ha'edut* is entirely motivated: without it, the classic case of false denial of testimony would carry no *korban* liability at all.

### Rav's Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַב, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא אַפְּקַהּ רַחֲמָנָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rav, for what *halakha* did the Merciful One remove it** from the category of an oath on an utterance and relate to it as a distinct category? Every oath of testimony is a case of an oath on an utterance and there is no need to relate to it as a special case.

For *Rav*, however, a serious question arises. *Ela l'Rav lemai hilkheta afkei Rachmana* — why did the Torah bother creating a separate category of *shevuat ha'edut*? According to *Rav*, "I do not know testimony for you" is a past-tense oath that is in *lav v'hein* — one can say "I do" or "I do not" — and the *lehaba* criterion is not required. It should therefore be a perfectly ordinary *shevuat bituy* and generate the standard *korban*. What was accomplished by creating the separate category? If *shevuat ha'edut* is just a subset of *shevuat bituy* in disguise, the Torah's separate treatment of it needs explanation.

### Abaye's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּ לוֹ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: לְאִיחַיּוֹבֵי עֲלַיהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said before Abaye:** An oath of testimony is singled out, according to Rav, in order **to render** one who takes it falsely **liable** to bring **two** sliding-scale offerings **for it,** one for a false oath on an utterance and one for a false oath of testimony.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: תַּרְתֵּי לָא מָצִיתוּ אָמְרִיתוּ, דְּתַנְיָא: לְאַחַת מֵאֵלֶּה – לְאַחַת אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ שְׁתַּיִם!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to them: You cannot say** that one who takes a false oath of testimony is liable to bring **two** offerings, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the sliding-scale offering that is brought for both an oath of testimony and an oath on an utterance: The verse states: "And it shall be, when he shall be guilty **in one of these things"** (Leviticus 5:5). By inference, **for one** of the sins that render one liable to bring a sliding-scale offering **you may deem him liable, but you may not deem him liable** for **two.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְאַבָּיֵי, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא אַפְּקָיהּ רַחֲמָנָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if this is so, then **according to Abaye's** understanding of the opinion of Rav, **for what *halakha* did the Merciful One remove** an oath of testimony from the category of an oath on an utterance and relate to it as a distinct category?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכִדְתַנְיָא: בְּכוּלָּן נֶאֱמַר ״וְנֶעֱלַם״, וְכָאן לֹא נֶאֱמַר ״וְנֶעְלַם״; לְחַיֵּיב עַל הַמֵּזִיד כַּשּׁוֹגֵג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: An oath of testimony is singled out, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **For every** other one of the prohibitions for which one is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering, it **is stated** in the verse: **"And it is hidden** from him" (see Leviticus 5:2–4), **and here,** with regard to the oath of testimony, the phrase: **And it is hidden** from him, **is not stated.** This serves **to render** one **liable** to bring an offering **for** taking a false oath of testimony **intentionally like** one who is liable for doing so **unwittingly.**

The rabbinic students present before Abaye suggest: *lechiyuvei aleih tartei* — the reason the Torah created a separate category is so that one becomes liable for *two korbanot*, one as a *shevuat ha'edut* and one as a *shevuat bituy*. Abaye rejects this: *tarti lo matzis amras* — you cannot obligate someone to bring two *korbanot* for a single act. The Torah says *l'achat me'eileh* — "one of these" — and a *beraita* derives from this: *l'achat atah mechayevo v'ee atah mechayevo shtayim*, you can only hold him liable for one, not for two. Double jeopardy does not apply in this framework.

Abaye himself then provides the correct answer: *l'chiddetanya* — as we are taught in a *beraita*, by all the other cases of *korban oleh v'yored* — *tumah*, *shevuat bituy* — the Torah specifies *vene'elam mimenu*, that the matter was hidden from him, that the person sinned in a state of unawareness. But by *shevuat ha'edut*, the Torah omits this condition. The *halacha* derived from this omission is *lechayev al hameizid keshogeg* — to hold even a willful violator liable, treating *meizid* like *shogeg* for purposes of the *korban* obligation. This is admittedly a striking ruling — bringing a *korban* for a *meizid* act is highly unusual. But that is the *halacha* of *shevuat ha'edut*. The reason the Torah needed its own category, then, was to capture this unique ruling: when a person lies about testimony in precisely the *shevuat ha'edut* circumstances (a qualified witness, testimony of monetary consequence), he is liable for a *korban* even if he acted willfully.

### Ne'elam Criterion

The *gemara* then addresses the *ne'elam* criterion more specifically. By *shevuat bituy*, *shevuat tumah*, and similar cases, the Torah writes *vene'elam mimenu* — it was hidden from him — which is the basis for the requirement of *shogeg* to generate *korban* liability. By *shevuat ha'edut*, no such *ne'elam* condition is stated, and it is from this absence that the Rabbanan derived the rule that even *meizid* generates the *korban*. This structural difference — the presence or absence of the *ne'elam* language — is what formally separates *shevuat ha'edut* as its own category with its own internal logic, distinct from the standard requirements of *shevuat bituy*.

## Abaye Contradiction

### Earlier Abaye Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר בֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵהַהִיא. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: חֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ רַב פָּפָּא אַמְרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Abaye retracted that** opinion. **And if you wish, say** instead that **Rav Pappa said one of those** statements, not Abaye.

The *gemara* surfaces an apparent internal contradiction in Abaye's positions. Earlier, Abaye stated that even *Rav* concedes that swearing "I know testimony for you" (*she'ani yode'a lecha edut*) does not generate *korban* liability — because the corresponding negative form, "I do not know testimony for you," is not a *shevuat bituy* but a *shevuat ha'edut*, meaning the two forms belong to different categories and thus do not constitute proper *lav v'hein* for *shevuat bituy*. The implication of this statement is that *shevuat ha'edut* is its *own* separate category — entirely distinct from *shevuat bituy* — such that when the negative falls under *shevuat ha'edut*, it does not "count" as the negative version of a *shevuat bituy*.

### Later Implication

But later — in answering the question of why the Torah needed to create a separate category of *shevuat ha'edut* — Abaye's answer was that this category exists *lechayev al hameizid*, to obligate even the willful violator. Embedded in Abaye's dismissal of the students' suggestion (that *shevuat ha'edut* generates two *korbanot*) is the concession that *shevuat ha'edut* is *also* a *shevuat bituy* — just one that carries only a single *korban* because of *l'achat me'eileh*. But if *shevuat ha'edut* is also a *shevuat bituy*, then the negative form, "I do not know testimony," *is* within the *lav v'hein* of *shevuat bituy* — and Abaye's earlier statement that swearing "I do know testimony" is exempt would be incorrect. After all, the reverse oath, "I do not know," *does* exist as a *shevuat bituy* (even though it is also a *shevuat ha'edut*), and so the *lav v'hein* criterion for *shevuat bituy* should be satisfied.

### Contradiction Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר בֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵהַהִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Abaye retracted that** opinion. While it is not entirely clear which statement he retracted, the drift of the *gemara* is that he retracted the earlier ruling — that "I know testimony" is exempt even for *Rav*. His later position would be that *shevuat ha'edut* is simultaneously a *shevuat bituy*, and therefore the *lav v'hein* is met: "I do not know testimony" serves as the *lav* within the *shevuat bituy* framework (and it is also a *shevuat ha'edut*). Accordingly, swearing "I know testimony" would be a valid *shevuat bituy* with a proper negative counterpart, and a person who did so falsely *would* be liable — according to *Rav* — for a *korban*.

The *gemara* resolves this directly: *hadar beih Abaye mehahi* — Abaye retracted one of his positions. While it is not entirely clear which statement he retracted, the drift of the *gemara* is that he retracted the earlier ruling — that "I know testimony" is exempt even for *Rav*. His later position would be that *shevuat ha'edut* is simultaneously a *shevuat bituy*, and therefore the *lav v'hein* is met: "I do not know testimony" serves as the *lav* within the *shevuat bituy* framework (and it is also a *shevuat ha'edut*). Accordingly, swearing "I know testimony" would be a valid *shevuat bituy* with a proper negative counterpart, and a person who did so falsely *would* be liable — according to *Rav* — for a *korban*.

### Alternative Resolutions

The *gemara* offers an additional resolution at the opening of 26b: *chada mineihu Rav Pappa amrah* — one of these two statements was in fact made by Rav Pappa, not by Abaye, and it was attributed to Abaye. In that case, the contradiction dissolves entirely, because the two statements are from different authorities and need not be reconciled. The practical upshot in *halacha* appears to be: according to Abaye, *shevuat ha'edut* retains membership in the *shevuat bituy* category, but constitutes only one *korban* obligation (not two); whereas according to Rava's formulation, once the Torah extracted *shevuat ha'edut* as its own category — motivated by the need to create *chiyuv* for *meizid* — the act falls *only* under *shevuat ha'edut* and not under *shevuat bituy* at all, applying the principle of *davar shehayah bichlal veyatza lidon b'davar chadash*, that once something is given a new independent status it is treated exclusively by that new status.
