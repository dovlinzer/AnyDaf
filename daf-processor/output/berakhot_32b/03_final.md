# Berakhot 32b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prayer > Deeds

### Prayer > Good Deeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: גְּדוֹלָה תְּפִלָּה יוֹתֵר מִמַּעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים, שֶׁאֵין לְךָ גָּדוֹל בְּמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים יוֹתֵר מִמֹּשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, אַף עַל פִּי כֵן לֹא נַעֲנָה אֶלָּא בִּתְפִלָּה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַל תּוֹסֶף דַּבֵּר אֵלַי״ וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״עֲלֵה רֹאשׁ הַפִּסְגָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** This story proves that **prayer is greater than good deeds** without prayer (*Tosafot*), as **there was none greater in** the performance of **good deeds than Moses our teacher; nevertheless, his request was granted,** albeit in a limited manner, in his request to enter Eretz Yisrael, **only through prayer,** when God permitted him to climb the mountain and look out over the land. **As,** initially **it is stated: "Speak no more to Me," juxtaposed to which is: "Go up to the summit of the mountain."**

We pick up at the very top of 32b. The *gemara* opens with a statement of Rabbi Elazar: *gedolah tefillah yoter mi-ma'asim tovim* — greater is prayer than good deeds. The proof offered is from Moshe Rabbeinu himself. As the *gemara* puts it: *she-ein lekha gadol mi-ma'asim tovim yoter mi-Moshe Rabbeinu* — there is no one greater in their good deeds than Moshe Rabbeinu. Consider what he accomplished: he took *Bnei Yisrael* out of *Mitzrayim* and did everything for them. And yet, *af al pi khen lo ne'eneh ela bi-tefillah* — he was only answered through prayer.

The verse cited is from the opening of *Devarim*, at the end of the forty years in the wilderness: *al tosef daber elai* — "do not speak to me anymore" — and immediately adjacent to it, *aleh rosh ha-pisgah* — "go up to the head of the mountain and I will show you the land." This is an interesting choice of proof-text, because Moshe did not receive what he asked for — he wanted to enter the land of Israel and was denied. What makes this example compelling is precisely that dimension: Moshe was asking something *for himself*, something one might have thought he had earned outright through a lifetime of extraordinary deeds. Yet even this partial concession — being shown the land — was extended to him only in the context of prayer, not on the basis of merit alone.

### Tosafot: Both Required

*Tosafot* enters with an important qualification. On the phrase *gedolah tefillah yoter mi-ma'asim tovim*, *Tosafot* reads *be-lo tefillah* — the statement means that prayer *combined with* good deeds is greater than good deeds *without* prayer. As *Tosafot* explains: *she-harei Moshe Rabbeinu alav ha-shalom af al pi she-hayu biyado ma'asim tovim hutsrakh li-tefillah* — even though Moshe had good deeds in hand, he still required prayer. The point is not that one can lead a life devoid of *ma'asim tovim* and compensate through prayer alone. Such a reading would be a perversion of the values at stake here.

One might ask whether a student in the discussion raised a sharper challenge: could this be a polemic against certain ideas that ritual sincerity alone matters regardless of one's life? In context, that seems a stretch. Still, *Tosafot*'s concern is real: rhetorically, the *gemara* sometimes makes exaggerated statements in order to emphasize the power of something, and one must be careful not to be misled.

A more integrative reading of the relationship between *tefillah* and *ma'asim tovim* was offered in the course of discussion. *Ma'asim tovim* — or for that matter *tzedakah* — could in principle be performed in a purely secular spirit, as an expression of ordinary decency rather than of one's relationship with God. What *tefillah* adds, or what *ta'anit* adds (if understood as supplemental rather than substitutional), is the articulation of those deeds *within* a God-relationship. As one participant put it, an employee can behave in exemplary fashion toward an employer, but without verbalization that relationship remains somehow dormant, unactivated. *Tefillah* is what puts the relationship into words.

### Fasting > Charity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: גְּדוֹלָה תַּעֲנִית יוֹתֵר מִן הַצְּדָקָה. מַאי טַעְמָא — זֶה בְּגוּפוֹ, וְזֶה בְּמָמוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** After comparing and contrasting prayer and good deeds, the Gemara explores another comparison. **Rabbi Elazar said: A fast is greater than charity. What is the reason** that fasting is greater? Because a fast **is** a mitzva performed **with one's body** as he afflicts himself, **while** charity **is** performed only **with one's money.**

Rabbi Elazar makes a parallel statement: *gedolah ta'anit yoter min ha-tzedakah* — greater is fasting than the giving of charity. The rationale given is *zeh be-gufo ve-zeh be-mamono* — one involves your body, the other involves only your money.

It is worth noting that the comparison *zeh be-gufo ve-zeh be-mamono* appears elsewhere in the *gemara* in a different context — contrasting *tzedakah* with *gemilut chasadim*, where the latter involves giving of oneself and not merely of one's funds. But there the point is about helping another person; one who performs *gemilut chasadim* gives of their own efforts on behalf of someone else. Here, by contrast, the *ta'anit* is a ritual act of self-denial — one's blood and fat are, in a sense, diminished — while the *tzedakah* at least helps another person. This inversion is genuinely challenging when read against the background of the *nevi'im*, who famously said: why do I need your fasts? What I need is for you to clothe the naked and feed the hungry.

One possible way to read this more narrowly: in the context of a *ta'anit tzibbur* — a communal fast day called in response to drought or crisis — the point may be that if you want your *tefillah* to be answered, merely writing a check is not sufficient. Real extension of oneself, real willingness to suffer, is what is required. We recite *tefillah, teshuvah, tzedakah ma'avirin et ro'a ha-gezeirah*, and in that mode the *ta'anit* represents something qualitatively different from *tzedakah*. Read that narrowly, the statement is more defensible. Read broadly, it remains deeply challenging.

It is also worth flagging the *gemara* in 6b, cited by the *Maharsha*, which states *agra de-ta'anita tzidketa* — the reward of a fast day is the *tzedakah* one gives on the fast day. That statement appears to say the exact opposite, and it is striking that Rabbi Elazar's formulation stands in some tension with a number of other rabbinic statements on this point.

### Prayer > Sacrifices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: גְּדוֹלָה תְּפִלָּה יוֹתֵר מִן הַקָּרְבָּנוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לָמָּה לִּי רֹב זִבְחֵיכֶם״, וּכְתִיב: ״וּבְפָרִשְׂכֶם כַּפֵּיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** In another comparison, **Rabbi Elazar said: Prayer is greater than sacrifices, as it is stated: "To what purpose is the multitude of your sacrifices to Me,** says the Lord. I am full of the burnt-offerings of rams and the fat of fed beasts; I do not desire the blood of bulls and sheep and goats" (Isaiah 1:11). **And** several verses later **it is written: "And when you spread forth your hands** I will hide My eyes from you, and even if you increase your prayer, I will not hear; your hands are full of blood" (Isaiah 1:15). Not only Israel's sacrifices, but even their prayers, which are on a higher spiritual level, will not be accepted.

Rabbi Elazar continues: *gedolah tefillah yoter min ha-korbanot* — prayer is greater than sacrifices. This is directly relevant to the broader thematic arc the *gemara* has been building. The proof is from *Yeshayahu*: *lama li rov zivcheihem* — "why do I need all your sacrifices?" — after which it is written: *u-ve-farish'chem kapeichem* — "when you spread your hands in prayer, I do not answer." The *hava amina* embedded in the sequence is that even after God rejected the sacrifices, prayer might still have been accepted. The very next verse's denial of that possibility implies that prayer is on a higher plane than sacrifice — it carried the possibility of being answered even when sacrifice did not.

### Kohen/Murder/Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ — לֹא יִשָּׂא אֶת כַּפָּיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְדֵיכֶם דָּמִים מָלֵאוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Speaking of that verse in Isaiah, the Gemara cites that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Any priest who killed a person may not lift his hands** in the Priestly Blessing **as it is stated:** "And when you spread forth your hands I will hide My eyes from you…**your hands are full of blood."** Here we see that the Priestly Blessing, performed with hands spread forth, is not accepted when performed by priests whose "hands are full of blood."

Having quoted the verse about spreading one's hands, the *gemara* introduces a related ruling. Rabbi Yochanan states: *kol kohen she-harag et ha-nefesh lo yisa kapav* — any *kohen* who has committed murder may not perform *birkat kohanim*, the priestly blessing. The source is *yedeikhem damim male'u* — "your hands are full of blood," the continuation of the very verse just quoted. Hands that have taken a life cannot be the same hands used to bless the nation.

There are difficult questions that arise from tragic cases — a *kohen* whose wagon accidentally ran over someone, for instance — and whether *teshuvah* can restore the eligibility to perform *nesi'at kapayim*. These questions are derived from this very *pasuk*.

What is striking when stepping back is that this single thread of Rabbi Elazar's statements — *tefillah* greater than *ma'asim tovim*, greater than *ta'anit* which is greater than *tzedakah*, greater than *korbanot* — seems, if taken together without *Tosafot*'s qualification, to create a picture where the connection to God through prayer and *ta'anit* outweighs the doing of deeds in the world. Whether that was intended rhetorically or reflects a deeper theological position is a question worth sitting with.

## Post-Churban Prayer

### Locked Gates/Tears

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ נִנְעֲלוּ שַׁעֲרֵי תְּפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גַּם כִּי אֶזְעַק וַאֲשַׁוֵּעַ שָׂתַם תְּפִלָּתִי״. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשַּׁעֲרֵי תְפִילָּה נִנְעֲלוּ, שַׁעֲרֵי דִמְעָה לֹא נִנְעֲלוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁמְעָה תְפִלָּתִי ה׳ וְשַׁוְעָתִי הַאֲזִינָה אֶל דִּמְעָתִי אַל תֶּחֱרַשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** On the subject of prayer, **Rabbi Elazar also said: Since the day the Temple was destroyed the gates of prayer were locked** and prayer is not accepted as it once was, **as it is said** in lamentation of the Temple's destruction: **"Though I plead and call out, He shuts out my prayer"** (Lamentations 3:8). Yet, **despite** the fact **that the gates of prayer were locked** with the destruction of the Temple, **the gates of tears were not locked,** and one who cries before God may rest assured that his prayers will be answered, **as it is stated: "Hear my prayer, Lord, and give ear to my pleading, keep not silence at my tears"** (Psalms 39:13). Since this prayer is a request that God should pay heed to the tears of one who is praying, he is certain that at least the gates of tears are not locked.

The same Rabbi Elazar who just declared prayer greater than *korbanot* now makes a sobering statement about the current state of prayer: *mi-yom she-charav Beit Ha-Mikdash, nin'alu sha'arei tefillah* — from the day the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* was destroyed, the gates of prayer have been locked. The proof-text is from *Eichah*: *gam ki ez'ak va-ashave'a, satam tefillati* — "even when I cry out and call, my prayer has been stopped up." This is set against the backdrop of the *churban*, and it sets up a striking paradox: the very *tefillah* he praised over sacrifices has itself been compromised by the loss of the *Beit Ha-Mikdash*.

But the *gemara* adds an important qualification: *ve-af al pi she-sha'arei tefillah nin'alu, sha'arei dima lo nin'alu* — even though the gates of prayer are locked, the gates of tears have not been locked. The supporting verse — *shema tefillati Hashem, ve-shavati ha'azina, el dimati al techerash* — "hear my prayer, God, hearken to my calling out; to my tears, do not be silent." *Rashi* notes that the phrase "do not be silent" implies something stronger than merely "listen": to be told not to be silent implies that one has already, in some sense, heard. The tears penetrate further than the words.

Why would the *churban* affect prayer and not only sacrifice? As discussed here: the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* was not merely the site of *korbanot*. It was the locus of God's presence among the people, the embodiment of the intimacy of that relationship. Its loss changes the nature of the relationship itself, and that in turn affects the accessibility of *tefillah*, not only of sacrifice. This is one of the more powerful ways to conceptualize what *Tisha B'Av* asks us to mourn — not the loss of animal sacrifice per se, but the loss of a mode of closeness with God that reverberates through every subsequent act of prayer.

On a related note, *Rava* would not declare a fast on an overcast day, relying on the verse from *Eichah*: *sakota be-anan lach me-avor tefillah* — "You have covered yourself with a cloud so that prayer cannot pass through." When there are clouds, that symbolic barrier is already present.

### Iron Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ נִפְסְקָה חוֹמַת בַּרְזֶל בֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל לַאֲבִיהֶם שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה קַח לְךָ מַחֲבַת בַּרְזֶל וְנָתַתָּ אוֹתָהּ קִיר בַּרְזֶל בֵּינְךָ וּבֵין הָעִיר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: Since the day the Temple was destroyed an iron wall separates Israel from their Father in heaven, as it is stated** to the prophet Ezekiel, instructing him to symbolize that separation: **"And take for yourself an iron griddle, and set it as an iron wall between yourself and the city…**it will be a sign for the house of Israel" (Ezekiel 4:3).

Rabbi Elazar adds a second metaphor, even heavier than locked gates: *mi-yom she-charav Beit Ha-Mikdash, nifseka chomat barzel bein Yisrael le-avihem she-ba-shamayim* — from the day the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* was destroyed, a wall of iron has been interposed between Israel and their Father in Heaven. The proof is from *Yechezkel*: *ve-atah kach lecha machavat barzel, ve-natatah otah kir barzel beinecha u-vein ha-ir* — take an iron griddle and place it as an iron wall between you and the city.

A locked gate still implies a gate — something with the potential to be opened. A wall of iron implies a far harsher and more complete separation. These statements together create a post-*churban* theology of spiritual distance, one which stands in obvious tension with the affirmations of the power of prayer that immediately preceded them. The resolution, it seems, lies not in resolving the tension intellectually but in inhabiting it honestly — and, as was noted, in crying.

## Extended Prayer

### Long Prayer/Learn Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָנִין אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כׇּל הַמַּאֲרִיךְ בִּתְפִלָּתוֹ, אֵין תְּפִלָּתוֹ חוֹזֶרֶת רֵיקָם. מְנָא לַן — מִמֹּשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאֶתְפַּלֵּל אֶל ה׳״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״וַיִּשְׁמַע ה׳ אֵלַי גַּם בַּפַּעַם הַהִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites other statements in praise of prayer: **Rabbi Ḥanin said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said: Anyone who prolongs his prayer is** assured that **his prayer does not return unanswered;** it will surely be accepted. **From where do we** derive this? **From Moses our teacher, as it is stated** that Moses said: "So I fell down before the Lord the forty days and forty nights that I fell down; **and I prayed to the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 9:26–27), **and it is written thereafter: "And the Lord heard me that time as well,** the Lord would not destroy you" (Deuteronomy 10:10).

The *gemara* shifts to a series of practical observations about prayer. Rabbi Chanina states: *kol ha-ma'arich bi-tefillato, ein tefillato chozeret reikam* — anyone who extends their prayer will not have their prayer return empty. The proof is from Moshe Rabbeinu: *va-etpallel el Hashem* is followed by *va-yishma Hashem elai gam ba-pa'am ha-hi* — "and God also listened to me that time." The key is in what is left implicit: the full verse reads *va-etpallel el Hashem arba'im yom ve-arba'im lailah* — Moshe prayed for forty days and forty nights. A prayer of that scope and duration was answered.

### Heartache/Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כָּל הַמַּאֲרִיךְ בִּתְפִילָּתוֹ וּמְעַיֵּין בָּהּ — סוֹף בָּא לִידֵי כְּאֵב לֵב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תּוֹחֶלֶת מְמֻשָּׁכָה מַחֲלָה לֵב״. מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ — יַעֲסוֹק בַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעֵץ חַיִּים תַּאֲוָה בָאָה״, וְאֵין עֵץ חַיִּים אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֵץ חַיִּים הִיא לַמַּחֲזִיקִים בָּהּ״. לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּמַאֲרֵיךְ וּמְעַיֵּין בַּהּ. הָא דְּמַאֲרֵיךְ וְלָא מְעַיֵּין בַּהּ
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **Is that so? Didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Anyone who prolongs his prayer and expects it to be answered, will ultimately come to heartache,** as it will not be answered. **As it is stated: "Hope deferred makes the heart sick"** (Proverbs 13:12). **And what is the remedy** for one afflicted with that illness? He should **engage in Torah** study, **as it is stated: "But desire fulfilled is the tree of life"** (Proverbs 13:12), **and tree of life is nothing other than Torah, as it is stated: "It is a tree of life to those who hold fast to it,** and those who support it are joyous" (Proverbs 3:18). This is **not difficult. This,** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba's statement that one will suffer heartache refers to one **who prolongs** his prayer **and expects it to be answered; that,** Rabbi Ḥanin's statement that one who prolongs his prayer is praiseworthy refers to **one who prolongs his prayer and** does **not expect it to be answered.**

Yet Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan offers what sounds like a counterpoint: *kol ha-ma'arich bi-tefillato u-me'ayein bah, sof ba lidei ke'ev lev* — anyone who extends their prayer and then scrutinizes it — or rather, as *Rashi* explains, who waits for it to be answered, constantly looking to see when the response will come — will in the end come to heartache. The verse: *tochelet memushacha machalah lev* — prolonged anticipation makes the heart sick.

What then is the remedy? *Ya'asok ba-Torah* — engage in Torah study. The verse: *ve-etz chayim ta'avah ba'ah* — "the desire brings a tree of life" — and the tree of life is Torah, as it is written *etz chayim hi la-machazikim bah*.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent tension between these two statements: *lo kashya — ha de-ma'arich u-me'ayein bah, ha de-ma'arich ve-lo me'ayein bah* — one who prays at length and then watches anxiously for the response is in a different category from one who prays at length without that anxious scrutiny. The prayer itself is not the problem; the watching is.

Several readings of the *me'ayein bah* present themselves. One possibility is that the expectation itself undermines something — it is like the famous observation that one who runs from honor will find honor chasing him, but only if he is not looking over his shoulder as he runs. Another reading, perhaps more psychologically acute, is that the *gemara* is not saying the prayer won't be answered; it is saying that the expectation of *how* and *when* leads to heartache, because prayers are sometimes answered in ways not anticipated, or only recognized years later. The heartache comes from the gap between what one expected and what one perceives.

There is also a structural point worth noting. Rabbi Yochanan's prescription — learn Torah — may reflect a broader tension between *tefillah* and *talmud Torah* as competing claims on one's time. One who is *ma'arich bi-tefillah* is by definition not in the *beit midrash*. The alternative being offered may not be only about managing one's emotional state, but about a reorientation of priorities: fulfill your obligation in *davening*, bring your requests before God, and then go sit and learn — for that too is a form of connection, and perhaps a more sustainable one than prolonged prayer with its attendant anxious waiting.

### Persist/Retry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אִם רָאָה אָדָם שֶׁהִתְפַּלֵּל וְלֹא נַעֲנָה — יַחְזוֹר וְיִתְפַּלֵּל. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״קַוֵּה אֶל ה׳ חֲזַק וְיַאֲמֵץ לִבֶּךָ וְקַוֵּה אֶל ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: A person who prayed and saw that he was not answered** should **pray again, as it is stated: "Hope in the Lord, strengthen yourself, let your heart take courage, and hope in the Lord"** (Psalms 27:14). One should turn to God with hope, and if necessary turn to God again with hope.

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina adds: *im ra'ah adam she-hitpallel ve-lo na'anah, yachzor ve-yitpallel* — if a person prays and is not answered, they should return and pray again. The verse: *kaveh el Hashem, chazak ve-ya'ametz libecha, ve-kaveh el Hashem* — "hope to God; strengthen your heart; and hope to God again." The repetition in the verse is the proof: when the first round of hope does not produce visible results, strengthen yourself and hope again.

Chanah provides the paradigmatic illustration that had been discussed earlier. She had clearly not prayed for the first time when Eli encountered her at the *Mishkan*; she had waited long, never given up, and returned to pray at greater and greater depth. The reaffirmation of faith implicit in repeated prayer is itself something powerful and expressive — not merely a tactical strategy for finally wearing down divine resistance, but a statement about the relationship. One who prays once and expects immediate results treats prayer as a transaction. One who returns, persisting in faith, demonstrates something qualitatively different.

### Four Need Strength

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַרְבָּעָה צְרִיכִין חִזּוּק, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: תּוֹרָה, וּמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים, תְּפִלָּה, וְדֶרֶךְ אֶרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** Connected to the emphasis on the need to bolster one's effort in prayer, the Gemara notes that **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Four** things **require bolstering,** constant effort to improve, **and they are: Torah, good deeds, prayer, and occupation.**

A *baraita* now teaches: *arba'ah tzrichim chizuk* — four things require constant reinforcement. They are: *Torah*, *ma'asim tovim*, *tefillah*, and *derekh eretz* — here understood, as *Rashi* explains, as *parnasah*, one's livelihood and profession.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹרָה וּמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים מִנַּיִן? — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״רַק חֲזַק וֶאֱמַץ מְאֹד לִשְׁמֹר וְלַעֲשׂוֹת כְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה״, ״חֲזַק״ — בַּתּוֹרָה, ״וֶאֱמַץ״ — בְּמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** For each of these, a biblical proof is cited: **From where** is it derived that **Torah and good deeds** require bolstering? **As it is stated** in the instruction to Joshua: **"Only be strong and be extremely courageous, observe and do all of the Torah** that Moses My servant commanded you; do not deviate to the right or to the left, that you may succeed wherever you go" (Joshua 1:7). In this verse, observe refers to Torah study and do refers to good deeds (Maharsha); the apparently repetitive language is not extraneous. The Gemara derives: **Be strong in Torah and be courageous in good deeds.**

The sources: for *Torah* and *ma'asim tovim*, the verse *rak chazak ve-ematz me'od, lishmor ve-la'asot ke-khol ha-Torah* — "only be strong and very courageous, to observe and do all this Torah." *Chazak* corresponds to *Torah* study; *ematz* to *ma'asim tovim*. And the doubling of *lishmor ve-la'asot* — observing and doing — captures both the knowing and the acting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּפִלָּה מִנַּיִן? — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״קַוֵּה אֶל ה׳ חֲזַק וְיַאֲמֵץ לִבְּךָ וְקַוֵּה אֶל ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived that **prayer** requires bolstering? **As it is said: "Hope in the Lord, strengthen yourself, let your heart take courage, and hope in the Lord."**

For *tefillah*: *kaveh el Hashem, chazak ve-ya'ametz libecha, ve-kaveh el Hashem* — hope, then strengthen your heart, then hope again.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ מִנַּיִן? — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״חֲזַק וְנִתְחַזַּק בְּעַד עַמֵּנוּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived that **occupation** requires bolstering? **As it is stated: "Be strong and we will be strong for the sake of our nation** and for the cities of our God" (II Samuel 10:12). All of one's labor requires bolstering.

For *derekh eretz*: *chazak ve-nit-chazek be'ad ameinu* — "be strong and let us strengthen each other for the sake of our people." *Rashi* reads this expansively: *im uman hu, le-umanuso; im socher hu, le-socharato; im ish milchamah hu, le-milchamato* — whatever one's profession, success in it requires ongoing fortification. None of these things — learning, doing good, praying, making a living — come easily or sustain themselves without effort.

## Post-Churban Prayer (II)

### God Remembers Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֹּאמֶר צִיּוֹן עֲזָבַנִי ה׳ וַה׳ שְׁכֵחָנִי״. הַיְינוּ עֲזוּבָה, הַיְינוּ שְׁכוּחָה? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אָמְרָה כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אָדָם נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ רִאשׁוֹנָה, זוֹכֵר מַעֲשֵׂה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. אַתָּה עֲזַבְתַּנִי וּשְׁכַחְתַּנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify: **Forsaken is the same as forgotten.** They are synonymous; why repeat the same idea twice? **Reish Lakish said: The community of Israel said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe,** even when **a man marries** a second **wife after his first wife, he** certainly **recalls the deeds of his first** wife. Yet **You have** not only **forsaken me,** but You have **forgotten me** as well.

The *gemara* cites the verse from *Yeshayahu*: *va-tomer Tzion azavani Hashem, va-Hashem shichekhani* — "Zion said: God has abandoned me; God has forgotten me." Reish Lakish reads the doubled language as the voice of *knesset Yisrael*: a man who takes a second wife may still remember his first — he was at least there, he experienced those years together. But you, God — not only have you abandoned me, you have *forgotten* me. You don't even think about me anymore.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: בִּתִּי, שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מַזָּלוֹת בָּרָאתִי בָּרָקִיעַ, וְעַל כׇּל מַזָּל וּמַזָּל בָּרָאתִי לוֹ שְׁלֹשִׁים חַיִל, וְעַל כׇּל חַיִל וְחַיִל בָּרָאתִי לוֹ שְׁלֹשִׁים לִגְיוֹן, וְעַל כׇּל לִגְיוֹן וְלִגְיוֹן בָּרָאתִי לוֹ שְׁלֹשִׁים רַהֲטוֹן, וְעַל כׇּל רַהֲטוֹן וְרַהֲטוֹן בָּרָאתִי לוֹ שְׁלֹשִׁים קְרָטוֹן, וְעַל כׇּל קְרָטוֹן וּקְרָטוֹן בָּרָאתִי לוֹ שְׁלֹשִׁים גַּסְטְרָא, וְעַל כׇּל גַּסְטְרָא וְגַסְטְרָא תָּלִיתִי בּוֹ שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה אַלְפֵי רִבּוֹא כּוֹכָבִים כְּנֶגֶד יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה. וְכוּלָּן לֹא בָּרָאתִי אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִילֵךְ, וְאַתְּ אָמַרְתְּ ״עֲזַבְתַּנִי״ וּ״שְׁכַחְתַּנִי״?!
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to** Israel: **My daughter, I created twelve constellations in the firmament, and for each and every constellation I have created thirty armies, and for each and every army I have created thirty legions [*ligyon*], and for each and every legion I have created thirty infantry division leaders [*rahaton*], and for each and every infantry division leader I have created thirty military camp leaders [*karton*], and for each and every military camp leader I have created thirty leaders of forts [*gastera*], and on each and every leader of a fort I have hung three hundred and sixty-five thousand stars corresponding to the days of the solar year. And all of them I have created only for your sake; and you said** the Lord **has forsaken me and** the Lord **has forgotten me?**

God's response, as rendered by the *gemara*, begins with a staggering enumeration of the cosmos created on Israel's behalf. There are twelve constellations, and for each, thirty armies (*chayyil*); for each army, thirty legions (*ligyon*); for each legion, thirty officers of a certain rank (*rhaton*); for each such officer, thirty colonels (*karton*); for each colonel, thirty lieutenants (*gistra*); and hanging upon each of those, three hundred sixty-five thousand *ribo* — ten-thousands — of stars, corresponding to the days of the solar year. The arithmetic yields numbers approaching billions, and the *gemara* itself notes with some wonder that even this seems an understatement given what we know of the cosmos. And all of this, God says, was created *bishvilekh* — for you. *Ve-at amarat azavtani u-shechachtani* — "and you say I have abandoned and forgotten you?"

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲתִשְׁכַּח אִשָּׁה עוּלָהּ״, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: כְּלוּם אֶשְׁכַּח עוֹלוֹת אֵילִים וּפִטְרֵי רְחָמִים שֶׁהִקְרַבְתְּ לְפָנַי בַּמִּדְבָּר?! אָמְרָה לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, הוֹאִיל וְאֵין שִׁכְחָה לִפְנֵי כִּסֵּא כְבוֹדֶךָ, שֶׁמָּא לֹא תִּשְׁכַּח לִי מַעֲשֵׂה הָעֵגֶל? אָמַר לָהּ: ״גַּם אֵלֶּה תִשְׁכַּחְנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse goes on to say: **"Can a woman forget her suckling baby,** that she would not have compassion for the child of her womb? These may forget, but you I will not forget." The meaning of this verse is that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said** to the community of Israel: **Have I forgotten the ram offerings and firstborn animals that you offered before Me in the desert?** The community of Israel **replied to Him: Master of the Universe, since there is no forgetfulness before the Throne of Your Glory, perhaps you will not forget my sin of the Golden Calf?** God **responded to** Israel: **"These [*eleh*] too shall be forgotten." "**These" is a reference to the sin of the Golden Calf, regarding which Israel said: "These [*eleh*] are your gods."

The *gemara* then reads the verse: *ha-tishkach ishah ulah?* — God asks: can I forget? The word *ulah* is heard as *olah* — a burnt offering — do you think I will forget the rams you sacrificed before me? Or the *petrei rechem*, the firstborn animals, that you brought in the *midbar*?

*Knesset Yisrael* then responds with a remarkable double move. First: since there is no forgetting before Your throne — *ein shikchah lifnei kisei kvodekha*, a phrase echoed in the *Rosh Ha-Shanah Amidah* — perhaps You will not forget for me the sin of the *egel*, the golden calf, and I will be punished for it still? God responds: *gam eileh tishkachnah* — even these I will forget.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, הוֹאִיל וְיֵשׁ שִׁכְחָה לִפְנֵי כִּסֵּא כְבוֹדֶךָ, שֶׁמָּא תִּשְׁכַּח לִי מַעֲשֵׂה סִינַי? אָמַר לָהּ: ״וְאָנֹכִי לֹא אֶשְׁכָּחֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The community of Israel **said before Him: Master of the Universe, since there is forgetfulness before the Throne of Your Glory, perhaps You will** also **forget the events** revolving around the revelation at **Sinai?** God **said to** Israel: **I [*anokhi*] will not forget you** the revelation at Sinai, which began with: "I [*anokhi*] am the Lord your God."

Then the second move: since you are going to forget certain things — *hoil ve-yesh shikchah lifnei kisei kvodekha* — perhaps You will forget for me the good things, like *ma'aseh Sinai*, the covenant at *Har Sinai*? And God responds: *ve-anokhi lo eshkachekh* — I will not forget you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״גַּם אֵלֶּה תִשְׁכַּחְנָה״ — זֶה מַעֲשֵׂה הָעֵגֶל, ״וְאָנֹכִי לֹא אֶשְׁכָּחֵךְ״ — זֶה מַעֲשֵׂה סִינַי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **That** is what **Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rav Oshaya said: What is** the meaning of that which is **written: "These too will be forgotten"? That is the sin of the Golden Calf.** And what is the meaning of **I will not forget you? Those are the events** that transpired at **Sinai.**

*Rashi* observes that this is embedded in the very words of the verse: *gam eileh tishkachnah* refers to *eileh* — as in *eileh elokekha Yisrael*, the golden calf; while *ve-anokhi lo eshkachekh* refers to *anokhi* — as in *anokhi Hashem Elokekha*, the opening of the *Aseret Ha-Dibrot* at Sinai. And this is confirmed by Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: *gam eileh tishkachnah* — this is the sin of the *egel*; *ve-anokhi lo eshkachekh* — this is the doing of Sinai.

Placed immediately after the statements about the locked gates and the iron wall, this passage provides something like a theological counterweight. Yes, the *churban* introduced a barrier. Yes, the gates are locked. But God has not forgotten *Bnei Yisrael*. The entire universe was created for their sake. The sins of the *egel* will be forgiven; the covenant of Sinai will never be forgotten. The juxtaposition is not accidental: these are weeks of *shivat de-nechemta*, consolation, and the *gemara* allows these texts to speak to that moment.

One additional layer was noted in the course of discussion: it is a common psychological reality that when a relationship ends — or enters a painful period of distance — one tends to remember its positive dimensions, the ways in which it was nourishing. God's speech here does something similar: in the very moment that *knesset Yisrael* feels abandoned, God reaches back to the constructive, covenantal memories that remain undiminished.

## Prayer Integrity

### Hour Before/After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲסִידִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים הָיוּ שׁוֹהִין שָׁעָה אַחַת: מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״אַשְׁרֵי יוֹשְׁבֵי בֵיתֶךָ״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל צָרִיךְ לִשְׁהוֹת שָׁעָה אַחַת אַחַר תְּפִלָּתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַךְ צַדִּיקִים יוֹדוּ לִשְׁמֶךָ יֵשְׁבוּ יְשָׁרִים אֶת פָּנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **the early** generations of **pious** men **would wait one hour** in order to achieve the solemn frame of mind appropriate for prayer. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** This is alluded to when **the verse states: "Happy are those who dwell in Your House"** (Psalms 84:5), immediately after which it is said: "They will yet praise You, Selah." **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: One who prays must** also **wait one hour after his prayer, as it is stated: "Surely the righteous will give thanks unto Your name, the upright will sit before You"** (Psalms 140:14), meaning that after thanking God through prayer, one should stay and sit before Him.

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah*, which speaks about *kavvanah* and the practice of *chasidim ha-rishonim*. The *mishnah* states that these early pious ones would wait an hour before prayer. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi derives this from the verse *ashrei yoshvei veitekha* — "happy are those who dwell in Your house" — and only thereafter *od yehalelucha selah*, they will praise You. First one dwells; then one praises.

The same Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds that one must also wait an hour *after* prayer: *ha-mitpallel tzarikh li-shhot sha'ah achat achar tefilato* — *ach tzadikim yodu lishimekha, yeshvu yesharim et panekha* — "the righteous will give thanks to Your name; the upright will dwell before Your face." After the praise, they still sit. One does not rush out. Prayer is not only the verbalized request; it is also the experience of being in the presence of God, the sitting together in that space before and after the words.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶא שָׁעָה אַחַת קוֹדֶם תְּפִלָּתוֹ, וְשָׁעָה אַחַת אַחַר תְּפִלָּתוֹ. קוֹדֶם תְּפִלָּתוֹ, מִנַּיִן? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַשְׁרֵי יוֹשְׁבֵי בֵיתֶךָ״. לְאַחַר תְּפִלָּתוֹ מִנַּיִן? — דִּכְתִיב: ״אַךְ צַדִּיקִים יוֹדוּ לִשְׁמֶךָ יֵשְׁבוּ יְשָׁרִים אֶת פָּנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **That** opinion **was also taught** in a *baraita*: **One who prays must wait one hour before his prayer and one hour after his prayer. From where** is it derived that one must wait one hour **before his prayer? As it is stated: "Happy are those who dwell in Your House." And from where** is it derived that one must stay one hour **after his prayer? As it is written: "Surely the righteous will give thanks unto Your name, the upright will sit before You."**

A *baraita* states this formally: one must wait an hour before prayer and an hour after, with both practices derived from those same two verses. *Tosafot* notes a practical implication: this is why we recite *Ashrei yoshvei veitekha* — those opening verses — before *Tehillah le-David* at *Minchah*. The *gemara* in Berakhot teaches that one who recites *Tehillah le-David* three times daily is a *ben Olam Ha-Ba*, but the actual *perek* begins with *Tehillah le-David*. The prefatory verses of *Ashrei yoshvei veitekha* were added — particularly at *Minchah*, where there is otherwise only the *Shemoneh Esrei* — as a way of inhabiting this preparatory sitting before one enters *davening*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲסִידִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים הָיוּ שׁוֹהִין שָׁעָה אַחַת, וּמִתְפַּלְּלִין שָׁעָה אַחַת, וְחוֹזְרִין וְשׁוֹהִין שָׁעָה אַחַת. וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁשּׁוֹהִין תֵּשַׁע שָׁעוֹת בַּיּוֹם בִּתְפִלָּה, תּוֹרָתָן הֵיאַךְ מִשְׁתַּמֶּרֶת, וּמְלַאכְתָּם הֵיאַךְ נַעֲשֵׂית?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to waiting before and after prayer: **The early** generations of **pious** men **would wait one hour, pray one hour, then wait one hour again.** This raises the question: **Since** the early pious men **would spend nine hours per day** engaged either **in prayer** or the requisite waiting periods before and after prayer, three hours each for the morning, afternoon, and evening prayers, **how is their Torah preserved?** There was little time remaining to review their studies. **And how was their work accomplished?**

As for who the *chasidim ha-rishonim* were: there was certainly a group known as *chasidim* in the period of the Maccabees, pietistic Jews who famously refused to fight on *Shabbat* and who generally refrained from involvement in the broader society. They did not join the *Chashmonaim* because they reasoned that if God wants a *Beit Ha-Mikdash* restored, He will bring it down from heaven. Whether the *mishnah* refers to them as a defined sect or simply to certain pious individuals who prayed with great care and preparation is not entirely clear.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁחֲסִידִים הֵם — תּוֹרָתָם מִשְׁתַּמֶּרֶת, וּמְלַאכְתָּם מִתְבָּרֶכֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, because they were pious** they merited that **their Torah is preserved and their work is blessed.**

A second *baraita* confirms: *chasidim ha-rishonim hayu shohim sha'ah achat u-mitpallelim sha'ah achat ve-chozrim ve-shohim sha'ah achat* — they waited an hour, prayed an hour, and waited another hour. The *gemara* asks the natural question: if they spent nine hours a day in the framework of prayer, how was their *Torah* preserved and how did their work get done? The answer: *mitoch she-chasidim hem, toratam mishtameret u-melakhatam mitbarechet* — because they were so righteous, their *Torah* was preserved and their work was blessed, requiring far fewer hours to accomplish.

The *Netziv*, Rav Naftali Zvi Yehuda Berlin, notes a significant difference between this *baraita* and the parallel in the *Yerushalmi*, which reads not *toratam mishtameret* — their *Torah* was preserved — but *toratam mitbarechet* — their *Torah* was *blessed*. He argues these two formulations reflect different orientations toward *Torah she-be'al peh*. In *Bavel*, the emphasis falls on process — the *shakla ve-tarya*, the give-and-take, the creative dimensions of legal deliberation. From that perspective, if you do not invest the hours, even correct conclusions cannot be called *toratam mitbarechet*, because the *Torah* itself is the process, not only the outcome. The *Yerushalmi*, by contrast, oriented more toward reaching correct conclusions, can say that even compressed learning produces Torah that is genuinely blessed — just as work that yields results in few hours is work that has been blessed. It is a subtle but telling distinction in the respective characters of the two Talmuds.

### King/Greeting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ הַמֶּלֶךְ שׁוֹאֵל בִּשְׁלוֹמוֹ לֹא יְשִׁיבֶנּוּ. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְמַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל לְמַלְכֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם — פּוֹסֵק.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, we learned in the mishna: **Even** if **the king greets him** while he is praying, **he should not respond to him** as one may not interrupt his prayer. In limiting application of this principle, **Rav Yosef said: They only taught** this mishna **with regard to kings of Israel,** as a Jewish king would understand that the individual did not fail to respond to his greeting due to disrespect for the king. **However, with regard to kings of the nations of the world, he interrupts** his prayer and responds to their greeting due to the potential danger.

The *mishnah* rules: *afilu ha-melekh sho'el bi-shlomo lo yeshivenu* — even if a king greets someone during prayer, one does not respond. The reasoning is implicit in the context: when standing before God in prayer, one is in the presence of the King of Kings, and nothing should interrupt that.

Rav Yosef qualifies: *lo shanu ela le-malkhei Yisrael* — this was said only regarding a Jewish king, who will understand. But *le-malkhei akum* — regarding a non-Jewish king, *posek* — one interrupts, because one's life may be at genuine risk.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל וְרָאָה אַנָּס בָּא כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, רָאָה קָרוֹן בָּא כְּנֶגְדּוֹ — לֹא יְהֵא מַפְסִיק, אֶלָּא מְקַצֵּר וְעוֹלֶה! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְקַצֵּר (יְקַצֵּר. וְאִם לָאו — פּוֹסֵק).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raised an objection** to Rav Yosef's statement: **One who is praying and saw a violent person,** feared by all, **coming toward him,** or **a carriage coming toward him** and he is in the way, **he should not stop** his prayer **but rather abridge it and move** out of the way. The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult.** Rather, **this** that teaches to abridge one's prayer rather than stopping, refers to a case **where it is possible to abridge** his prayer and complete it in time, in which case he **should abridge** it. **And if** it is **not** a situation where he can abridge his prayer, **he interrupts** his prayer.

The *gemara* raises an objection from a *baraita*: if one is *davening* and sees a dangerous man (*anas*) approaching, or a wagon bearing down, *lo yehei mafsik ela me-katzer ve-oleh* — one should not stop, but rather finish up quickly. The *gemara* resolves: *lo kashya — ha de-efshar le-katzer* — if one can shorten and conclude, one does so; if one cannot, one stops. This is not a matter of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* — it is simply about completing one's *davening* where possible.

### Officer Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָסִיד אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מִתְפַּלֵּל בַּדֶּרֶךְ. בָּא הֶגְמוֹן אֶחָד וְנָתַן לוֹ שָׁלוֹם, וְלֹא הֶחְזִיר לוֹ שָׁלוֹם. הִמְתִּין לוֹ עַד שֶׁסִּייֵּם תְּפִלָּתוֹ. לְאַחַר שֶׁסִּייֵּם תְּפִלָּתוֹ, אָמַר לוֹ: רֵיקָא, וַהֲלֹא כָּתוּב בְּתוֹרַתְכֶם ״רַק הִשָּׁמֶר לְךָ וּשְׁמֹר נַפְשְׁךָ״, וּכְתִיב ״וְנִשְׁמַרְתֶּם מְאֹד לְנַפְשֹׁתֵיכֶם״. כְּשֶׁנָּתַתִּי לְךָ שָׁלוֹם לָמָּה לֹא הֶחְזַרְתָּ לִי שָׁלוֹם? אִם הָיִיתִי חוֹתֵךְ רֹאשְׁךָ בְּסַיִיף, מִי הָיָה תּוֹבֵעַ אֶת דָּמְךָ מִיָּדִי?!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There was **a** related **incident, involving a particular pious man who was praying** while traveling **along his path** when **an officer [*hegmon*] came and greeted him.** The pious man did not pause from his prayer **and did not respond with a greeting.** The officer **waited for him until he finished his prayer.** **After he finished his prayer,** the officer **said to him:** You **good for nothing.** You endangered yourself; I could have killed you. **Isn't it written in your Torah: "Take utmost care and guard yourself diligently"** (Deuteronomy 4:9)?  **And it is** also **written: "Take therefore good heed unto yourselves"** (Deuteronomy 4:15)? Why did you ignore the danger to your life?  **When I greeted you, why did you not respond with a greeting?**  **Were I to sever your head with a sword, who would hold me accountable for your** spilled **blood?**

A *baraita* tells of a *chasid* who was praying along the road. An officer (*sar*) approached and greeted him; the *chasid* did not respond. The officer waited until he finished. When prayer was complete, the officer confronted him: *reika* — worthless one — does your Torah not say *rak hishamer lekha u-shemor nafshekha* and *ve-nishmartem me'od le-nafshoteikhem* — to guard your life carefully? How could you risk your life by ignoring me? *Im hayasi choteikh et roshekha be-saif, mi hayah tove'a et damkha mi-yadi* — had I cut off your head with a sword, no one could have demanded your blood from me.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: הַמְתֵּן לִי עַד שֶׁאֲפַיֶּיסְךָ בִּדְבָרִים. אָמַר לוֹ: אִילּוּ הָיִיתָ עוֹמֵד לִפְנֵי מֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם, וּבָא חֲבֵרְךָ וְנָתַן לְךָ שָׁלוֹם — הָיִיתָ מַחֲזִיר לוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** The pious man **said to him: Wait for me until I will appease you with** my **words.**  **He said to him: Had you been standing before a flesh and blood king and your friend came and greeted you,** would **you** **return his** greeting?

The *chasid* asked: permit me to appease you with words. He said: if you had been standing before a human king — *melekh basar va-dam* — and a colleague had come and greeted you, would you have responded? The officer said: no. And if you had responded, what would they have done to you? They would have cut off my head with a sword, said the officer.

The *chasid* then drew the *kal va-homer*: if you, standing before a mortal king — *she-ha-yom kan u-machar be-kever*, here today and in the grave tomorrow — would not have interrupted your attendance upon him to respond to a colleague, then I, who was standing before the King of Kings, *she-hu chai ve-kayam la-adei ad u-le-olmei olamim*, who lives and endures forever — how much more so should I not have interrupted. *Al achat kamah ve-khamah*. The officer was immediately appeased, and the *chasid* went home in peace.

It is worth noting one slight puzzle in the argument: the *chasid* said "if a *friend* came and greeted you." Had one been the officer, one might have taken offense at the word *friend* — "I am not your friend." A more obvious analogy would have been "if an important dignitary came." Perhaps it is an intentional rhetorical choice, deliberately understating the comparison to make the conclusion more powerful. In any event, the point lands.

One also wonders about the *chasid* praying on the road in the first place — *mispalel ba-derekh* exposes one to exactly these kinds of interruptions. Presumably he had no choice in the matter; he was traveling.

### Danger/Interruption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ נָחָשׁ כָּרוּךְ עַל עֲקֵבוֹ, לֹא יַפְסִיק. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא נָחָשׁ. אֲבָל עַקְרָב — פּוֹסֵק.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **even if a snake is wrapped around his heel, he may not interrupt** his prayer. In limiting application of this principle, **Rav Sheshet said: They only taught** this mishna **with regard to a snake,** as if one does not attack the snake it will not bite him. **But** if **a scorpion** approaches an individual while he is praying, **he stops,** as the scorpion is liable to sting him even if he does not disturb it.

The *mishnah* had stated: *afilu nachash karukh al akeivo lo yafsik* — even if there is a snake coiled around one's ankle, one does not interrupt prayer.

Rav Sheshet qualifies: *lo shanu ela nachash* — this is only for a snake. *Aval akrav, posek* — a scorpion, however, requires that one stop. Perhaps not all snakes are poisonous; but the *gemara*'s further discussion suggests the distinction lies elsewhere. A *baraita* is cited as a challenge: if someone fell into a pit of lions, one cannot testify that he is dead — perhaps the lions were not hungry. But if he fell into a pit filled with snakes and scorpions, one *can* testify that he is dead. This implies that snakes are reliably lethal — so why treat them differently from scorpions?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: נָפַל לְגוֹב אֲרָיוֹת — אֵין מְעִידִין עָלָיו שֶׁמֵּת. נָפַל לַחֲפִירָה מְלֵאָה נְחָשִׁים וְעַקְרַבִּים — מְעִידִין עָלָיו שֶׁמֵּת! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאַגַּב אִיצְצָא מַזְּקִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on what was taught in a *Tosefta*: Those who saw one **fall into a lions' den** but did not see what happened to him thereafter, **do not testify that he died.** Their testimony is not accepted by the court as proof that he has died as it is possible that the lions did not eat him. However, those who saw one **fall into a pit of snakes and scorpions, testify that he died** as surely the snakes bit him. The Gemara responds: This is not difficult. **There,** in the case of one who falls into a pit of snakes, it **is different, as due to the pressure** of his falling on top of them, the snakes **will harm him,** but a snake who is not touched will not bite.

The *gemara* answers: *shani hatam de-agav itstsama* — it is different there, because the pressure of the fall agitated them and they would certainly attack. A snake that is merely coiled calmly is not necessarily about to bite.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: רָאָה שְׁוָורִים — פּוֹסֵק. דְּתָנֵי רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָה: מַרְחִיקִין מִשּׁוֹר תָּם חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה, וּמִשּׁוֹר מוּעָד — כִּמְלוֹא עֵינָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *halakha* stating that he must interrupt his prayer in a case of certain danger. **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: One who saw oxen** coming toward him, **he interrupts** his prayer, **as Rav Hoshaya taught: One distances himself fifty cubits from an innocuous ox [*shor tam*],** an ox with no history of causing damage with the intent to injure, **and from a forewarned ox [*shor muad*],** an ox whose owner was forewarned because his ox has gored three times already, one distances himself until it is beyond **eyeshot.**

Rav Yitzchak adds: even if one sees an ox (*reish tora*), one stops prayer and moves away. This seems counterintuitive — oxen are not typically thought of as dangerous — but a *baraita* taught by Rabbi Yoshiyah: *merachakin mi-shor tam chamishim amah* — keep fifty cubits (roughly seventy-five feet) away from even an ox that has never gored; and from an ox that has gored (*shor mu'ad*) — *ke-melo einav*, stay out of its entire field of vision. Rav Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Meir adds: even if the ox has its head in its feeding basket, distracted and apparently calm — *salik le-igra, u-shedi darga mitutakh* — go up to the roof and kick away the ladder. This is understood as hyperbole by *Rashi*, but the principle is clear: one does not take chances with oxen.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר: רֵישׁ תּוֹרָא בְּדִקּוּלָא — סְלֵיק לְאַגְרָא וּשְׁדִי דַּרְגָּא מִתּוּתָךְ. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּשׁוֹר שָׁחוֹר וּבְיוֹמֵי נִיסָן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַשָּׂטָן מְרַקֵּד לוֹ בֵּין קַרְנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** It was **taught in the name of Rabbi Meir:** While **the head of the ox is** still **in the basket** and he is busy eating, **go up on the roof and kick the ladder out from underneath you. Shmuel said: This applies only with regard to a black ox, and during the days of Nisan, because** that species of ox is particularly dangerous, and during that time of year **Satan dances between its horns.**

Shmuel adds a further specificity: *hani milei be-shor shachor u-ve-yomei Nisan* — this applies especially to a black ox in the spring, *mipnei she-ha-satan merakeid bein karnav* — because the Satan dances between its horns. After the confinement of winter, the animal has energy and is looking for trouble.

*Tosafot* notes that the *Yerushalmi* adds another qualification: if the snake was clearly preparing to strike — *im haya nachash martia lavo kenegdo* — then one stops, because it *is* intending to cause harm. This brings the whole discussion into clearer relief: the question is always whether there is genuine risk. The *mishnah*'s rule is not recklessness; it is a recognition that the default state of a coiled snake is not the same as an imminent attack.

What results from this sequence of qualifications is a significant moderation of the *mishnah*'s dramatic statement. Yes, one maintains one's standing before God and does not interrupt prayer lightly. But if there is genuine danger — a scorpion, an agitated ox, a snake positioned to strike — one takes care of oneself. *Ve-nishmartem me'od le-nafshoteikhem*.

### Chanina/Arod Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה עַרְוָד, וְהָיָה מַזִּיק אֶת הַבְּרִיּוֹת. בָּאוּ וְהוֹדִיעוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא. אָמַר לָהֶם: הַרְאוּ לִי אֶת חוֹרוֹ! הֶרְאוּהוּ אֶת חוֹרוֹ. נָתַן עֲקֵבוֹ עַל פִּי הַחוֹר, יָצָא וּנְשָׁכוֹ — וּמֵת אוֹתוֹ עַרְוָד. נְטָלוֹ עַל כְּתֵפוֹ וֶהֱבִיאוֹ לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. אָמַר לָהֶם: רְאוּ בָּנַי, אֵין עַרְוָד מֵמִית, אֶלָּא הַחֵטְא מֵמִית. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: אוֹי לוֹ לָאָדָם שֶׁפָּגַע בּוֹ עַרְוָד, וְאוֹי לוֹ לָעַרְוָד שֶׁפָּגַע בּוֹ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the praise for one who prays and need not fear even a snake, **the Sages taught:** There was **an incident in one place where an *arvad* was harming the people. They came and told Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa** and asked for his help. **He told them: Show me** the **hole of the *arvad*. They showed him its hole. He placed his heel over the mouth of the hole** and the ***arvad* came out and bit him, and died.** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa **placed** the *arvad* **over his shoulder and brought it to the study hall. He said to** those assembled there: **See, my sons, it is not** the ***arvad* that kills** a person, **rather transgression kills** a person. The *arvad* has no power over one who is free of transgression. **At that moment** the Sages **said: Woe unto the person who was attacked by an *arvad* and woe unto the *arvad* that was attacked by Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa.**

The *gemara* concludes this section with a famous story. In a certain place there was an *arod* — some creature understood to be a hybrid of snake and scorpion, in any case a poisonous reptile — that was injuring people. They came and told Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa. He said: show me its hole. They showed him, and he placed his heel over the opening of the hole. The *arod* came out, bit him — and the *arod* died.

Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa then picked it up, put it on his shoulder, brought it to the *beit midrash*, and said to his students: *re'u vanai, ein arod memit ela ha-chet memit* — see, my sons, it is not the *arod* that kills; it is the sin that kills. At that moment, it was said: *oy lo le-adam she-paga bo arod* — woe to the person that encounters an *arod*; *ve-oy lo la-arod she-paga bo Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa* — and woe to the *arod* that encounters Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa.

*Rashi* cites the *Ba'al Halachot Gedolot*, who in turn cites the *Talmud* of *Eretz Yisrael*: when an *arod* bites a person, whichever of them — man or *arod* — reaches water first causes the death of the other. *Rashi* suggests that a spring miraculously opened beneath Rabbi Chanina's heel. This is an attempt to naturalize the miracle somewhat; though the opening of a spring beneath his heel is itself miraculous, it gives the event a physical mechanism. The *gemara* of course does not frame it this way, and *Rashi*'s citation of the *Bahag* reads as a kind of commentary-level version of what one might call *nissim be-derekh ha-teva* — wonders that follow natural channels.

The placement of this story is worth noticing. It follows directly after the discussion about when one may or may not interrupt prayer in the face of danger. Having carefully qualified the *mishnah* — yes, stop for a scorpion, for an aggressive ox, for an imminent threat — the *gemara* ends with the story of one whose *davening* was so whole, whose piety was so complete, that the creature that bit him died. The juxtaposition carries a message: the *gemara*'s qualifications are correct and reasonable; but for one who has truly achieved *ein chet*, there is a different reality.

## Rain/Havdalah

### Rain/Resurrection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַזְכִּירִין גְּבוּרוֹת גְּשָׁמִים בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, וּשְׁאֵלָה בְּבִרְכַּת הַשָּׁנִים, וְהַבְדָּלָה בְּחוֹנֵן הַדָּעַת. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אוֹמְרָהּ בְּרָכָה רְבִיעִית בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בַּהוֹדָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna speaks of additions to the standard formula of the *Amida* prayer and the blessings in which they are incorporated. **One mentions the might of the rains** and recites: He makes the wind blow and the rain fall, **in** the second blessing of the *Amida* prayer, the blessing of **the revival of the dead. And the request** for rain: And grant dew and rain as a blessing, **in** the ninth blessing of the *Amida* prayer, **the blessing of the years. And the prayer of distinction [*havdala*],** between the holy and the profane recited in the evening prayer following Shabbat and festivals, **in** the fourth blessing of the *Amida* prayer: **Who graciously grants knowledge. Rabbi Akiva says:** *Havdala* **is recited** as **an independent fourth blessing. Rabbi Eliezer says** that it is recited **in** the seventeenth blessing of the *Amida* prayer, the blessing of **thanksgiving.**

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah* which discusses where rain and *havdalah* are mentioned in the *Amidah*. The *mishnah* states: *mazkirin gevurot geshamim bi-techiyyat ha-meitim* — the power of rain is mentioned in the *berakhah* of the resurrection of the dead, i.e., the second *berakhah* of the *Shemoneh Esrei*, which begins *mashiv ha-ruach u-morid ha-gashem*. The question the *gemara* asks is *mai ta'ama* — why specifically there?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַזְכִּירִין גְּבוּרוֹת גְּשָׁמִים: מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁשְּׁקוּלָה כִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים לְפִיכָךְ קְבָעוּהָ בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that **one mentions the might of the rains** in the second blessing of the *Amida* prayer, the blessing of the revival of the dead. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that the might of the rains is mentioned specifically in that blessing? **Rav Yosef said: Because** the might of the rains **is equivalent to the resurrection of the dead,** as rain revives new life in the plant world (Jerusalem Talmud), **therefore it was inserted in** the blessing of **the revival of the dead.**

Rav Yosef answers: *mitoch she-shkulah ki-techiyyat ha-meitim, lefichakh keva'uha bi-techiyyat ha-meitim* — because rain is literally a matter of life and death for agricultural societies. But beyond that, rain and *techiyyat ha-meitim* share a powerful metaphorical relationship. One plants a seed in the earth — it is buried, it decays, it seems to disappear entirely — and then rain comes, and new life emerges from what had been interred. The whole cycle of seed, burial, decay, and rebirth under rain is a living image of resurrection.

And the *berakhah* of *gevurot* — as this second *berakhah* is often called, reflecting God's *gevurah*, His power — is itself structured around many forms of divine life-giving. *Mechalkeil chayim be-chesed, mechayeh meitim be-rachamim rabbim, someich noflim ve-rofei cholim u-matir asurim u-mekayeim emunato li-sheinei afar* — God sustains the living, heals the sick, releases the bound, keeps faith with those who sleep in the dust. *Mashiv ha-ruach u-morid ha-gashem* fits naturally into this catalogue. The *berakhah* is not narrowly about *techiyyat ha-meitim* alone; it is about *gevurot*, the full range of God's power to give and sustain life. Rain belongs there.

The phrase *matzmiach yeshuah* — God who makes salvation sprout — also contributes: *tzemach*, growth from the ground, is yet another image interweaving rain and human origins. As was noted, in *Bereishit* 2, humans themselves were formed from the earth and brought to life. Rain does not merely water crops; it enacts the paradigm by which God brings forth life from what is buried.

### Request for Rain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁאֵלָה בְּבִרְכַּת הַשָּׁנִים: מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהִיא פַּרְנָסָה, לְפִיכָךְ קְבָעוּהָ בְּבִרְכַּת פַּרְנָסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** we also learned in the mishna that the **request** for rain is added to **the blessing of the years.** Here, too, the Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that the request for rain is recited specifically in that blessing? **Rav Yosef said: Because** rain is a component of **sustenance, therefore it was inserted in the blessing of sustenance** as part of our request for bountiful sustenance.

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-she'elah be-virkat ha-shanim* — one asks for rain in the *berakhah* of the years, i.e., the ninth *berakhah* of the *Shemoneh Esrei*, where one says *vten tal u-matar li-vrakhah*. The *gemara* asks *mai ta'ama* — why there?

Rav Yosef answers: *mitoch she-hi parnasah, keva'uha be-virkat parnasah* — because rain is *parnasah*, sustenance, and therefore it was placed in the *berakhah* that deals with *parnasah*. This distinction between where rain is *mentioned* (second *berakhah*, as part of divine power over life and death) and where it is *requested* (ninth *berakhah*, as a practical matter of livelihood) reflects a careful logic: the theological dimension of rain belongs in *gevurot*, while the pragmatic need for it belongs alongside one's request for a prosperous year.

### Havdalah/Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבְדָּלָה בְּ״חוֹנֵן הַדָּעַת״: מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהִיא חָכְמָה, קְבָעוּהָ בְּבִרְכַּת חָכְמָה. וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהִיא חוֹל, לְפִיכָךְ קְבָעוּהָ בְּבִרְכַּת חוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna that ***havdala*,** distinguishing between Shabbat and the weekdays, is added **in** the blessing of: **Who graciously grants knowledge.** Here too the Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that *havdala* is recited specifically in that blessing? **Rav Yosef said:** *Havdala* is recited in that blessing **because it** requires **wisdom** to distinguish between two entities, **they established it in the blessing of wisdom. The Rabbis say** a different reason: **Because** *havdala* **is** the distinction between the sacred and the **profane,** the Sages **established it in the blessing of weekdays.** The first three blessings of the *Amida* prayer are recited both on weekdays and on Shabbat and Festivals. The blessing: Who graciously grants knowledge, is the first of the blessings recited exclusively during the week.

The *mishnah* rules that *havdalah* — the declaration distinguishing *Shabbat* from the weekday — is recited within the *berakhah* of *chonein ha-da'at*, the fourth *berakhah* of the *Shemoneh Esrei*, which concerns God's gift of intellect and knowledge. Again, the *gemara* asks *mai ta'ama* — why?

Rav Yosef answers: *mitoch she-hi chokhmah, keva'uha be-virkat chokhmah* — because *havdalah* involves the act of distinguishing, and distinguishing is an act of intellect, and therefore it was placed in the *berakhah* of wisdom and knowledge.

The Rabbis add a complementary rationale: *mitoch she-hu chol, keva'uha be-virkat chol* — because *havdalah* concerns the beginning of the weekday, they placed it in the first *berakhah* unique to weekday *davening*. The first three *berakhot* of the *Shemoneh Esrei* are recited on *Shabbat* as well, but the fourth, *chonein ha-da'at*, is the first specifically weekday *berakhah*. It is the moment one enters the *chol*, and therefore it is where the declaration *mavdil bein kodesh le-chol* belongs.

The *Gra* points out that this dispute about rationale actually explains our current liturgical practice. We begin *havdalah* in *Shemoneh Esrei* with the phrase *atah chonantanu le-adam da'at u-malamed le-enosh binah* — "You have graced the human with knowledge and taught understanding" — which places *havdalah* in the framework of the *berakhah* about *da'at* and *chokhmah*, satisfying the first position. But then we insert *mavdil bein kodesh le-chol* before concluding the *berakhah*. Why specifically there and not at the very opening? Because by the end of the *berakhah*, the *Shemoneh Esrei* has already transitioned to personal requests — *choneinu mei-itkha da'ah binah ve-haskel* — and one does not make personal requests on *Shabbat*. One must therefore declare the separation of *Shabbat* from the weekday — *ha-mavdil bein kodesh le-chol* — before issuing the personal petition *choneinu mei-itkha*. But one does not begin with that declaration; instead one opens with *atah chonantanu*, keeping *havdalah* embedded within the *berakhah* of *da'at*. The *Gra*'s observation is that this ordering satisfies *both* positions simultaneously: the framing is about intellect and *chokhmah*, while the placement of *mavdil bein kodesh le-chol* before the personal petition reflects the view that this *berakhah* is the first *berakhah* of *chol*.
