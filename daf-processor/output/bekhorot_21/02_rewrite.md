# Bekhorot 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Minority

### Daf Overview

Daf 21 is a relatively short daf, and part of the reason for its brevity lies in the massive *tosafot* that appears on 21b, under the heading *chalav*. That *tosafot* is essentially devoted to working out the general rule of whether we are concerned for minority possibilities in halachic reasoning — and it made the preceding daf shorter as well by extending the analysis there. Today's daf is compact not because the *gemara* lacks conceptual depth, but because the actual details of the cases described are particularly challenging to reconstruct, resulting in unusually large *Rashi* and *Tosafot* commentaries.

### Minority Possibilities

The overarching concern driving that lengthy *Tosafot* is the question of minority possibilities and how *chazakah* interacts with them — specifically, whether we are concerned about a minority scenario when a *chazakah* supports one direction, and whether the answer changes depending on whether the question tends toward *l'chumra* or *l'kula*. *Tosafot* attempts to resolve how we generally rule across these variables, and it is that extended analysis that gives the daf its particular character.

---

## Multiple Births

### First Birth Recap

The broader topic we have been tracking concerns what happens when a sheep or goat gives birth and one does not know the animal's prior history — specifically, whether one must assume it is a *bechor*, and whether one must be concerned that it may have previously miscarried. The *gemara* yesterday presented a *baraita* with a scenario in which *ma'aser behemah* could be taken from the offspring of a single goat all within one year.

### Three Generations

*Ma'aser behemah* applies only to animals born between one *Rosh Chodesh Elul* and the next. The *baraita* described a case in which a goat gave birth to three kids, each of those kids gave birth to three more kids, and since the gestation period for goats is five months, this sequence can indeed unfold within a single year — yielding three kids and nine grandkids, thirteen animals in total, from which *ma'aser behemah* is taken. The question that arose from yesterday's discussion was why the *baraita* did not simply describe the mother giving birth a second time. Why go to three generations?

### Sequential Births

The *gemara* resolved this: it is possible for an animal that has miscarried to give birth again in the same year. But the case at hand concerns a mother who first had live children — three kids — and so to have her give birth a second time within the same year would require two separate live births in a single year, which may not be possible. Therefore, the *baraita* constructs the scenario through the children having children, not through the mother producing a second litter. With this background established, we turn to the daf before us.

## Rabbi Shimon Dispute

### Tanna Kamma vs Shimon

Four lines from the top of 21a, the *gemara* cites Rabbi Shimon as saying: "I saw a goat give birth such that *ma'aser behemah* could be taken all within the same year" — which sounds like little more than a paraphrase of the *tanna kamma*'s position. The *gemara* therefore asks: *mai ika bein tanna kamma l'Rabbi Shimon* — what exactly distinguishes them? Both describe a scenario in which *ma'aser behemah* is possible within a single year.

### Ze'iri Doctrine

The *gemara* answers: *ika beinaihu d'Ze'iri* — the difference between them turns on the doctrine of Ze'iri. Ze'iri had stated that after a miscarriage, an animal cannot become pregnant again for thirty days. The *tanna kamma* holds of Ze'iri's position; Rabbi Shimon does not. But this raises an immediate difficulty: Ze'iri's principle addresses the waiting period after a miscarriage, and the *baraita* has not been discussing miscarriages at all. What relevance does Ze'iri have here?

### Rashi's Scenario

This is precisely why there is such a substantial *Rashi* and *Tosafot* on this passage. *Rashi*'s explanation — though he himself has reservations about it for technical reasons — is that the scenario involves counting the mother herself as one of the animals born within the year, not just the kids and grandkids as assumed yesterday. That is, the mother was born at the start of the year, became pregnant, gave birth to three kids, and those kids themselves then went on to give birth — producing three generations all within the same year.

### Gestation Timeline

Here is how the timeline works according to *Rashi*. Assume the mother is born on *Rosh Chodesh Elul*, which is *Rosh Hashanah l'ma'aser behemah*. Assume further — for reasons we will return to — that she cannot become pregnant during her first month of life. So she becomes pregnant on *Rosh Chodesh Tishrei*. With a five-month gestation, she gives birth on *Rosh Chodesh Adar*: *Tishrei, Cheshvan, Kislev, Tevet, Shevat* — birth on *Rosh Chodesh Adar*. Those three kids, if they also cannot become pregnant in their first month, must wait until *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*. Their five-month gestation runs: *Nissan, Iyar, Sivan, Tammuz, Av* — bringing us to birth on *Rosh Chodesh Elul*, the very last day of the year, or even into the next year.

This is where Ze'iri enters. The one-month waiting period that Ze'iri mandates after a miscarriage is being applied here by analogy — or in one reading, literally — to the waiting period after birth. The question becomes: can the mother, having given birth on *Rosh Chodesh Adar*, become pregnant again and give birth a second time before the next *Rosh Chodesh Elul*? If she must wait a full month before conceiving again, she cannot do so. If she can conceive immediately, she might.

### Rashi's Concern

What troubles *Rashi* about this construction is that it seems to treat the mother differently from her offspring. The scenario requires the mother to wait a month before becoming pregnant, while the kids apparently do not. The asymmetry is difficult to justify. This is one of the reasons *Rashi* himself is uncomfortable with this reading, even as he presents it.

### Alternative Explanations

One resolution offered is that the requirement to wait a month is not absolute — it is the *tanna kamma*'s default assumption, but the kids could in principle have become pregnant within their first month. The scenario is constructed so that the mother did wait a month, producing the conditions for the dispute, while the kids became pregnant earlier than the one-month mark. This is technically permissible even if atypical, and this is what makes the scenario function.

The *gemara* goes on to offer multiple framings of what distinguishes the *tanna kamma* from Rabbi Shimon. The first is Ze'iri himself: the *tanna kamma* holds that an animal must wait a month after birth before conceiving again (*itai d'Ze'iri*); Rabbi Shimon does not (*leitai d'Ze'iri*). Given the timeline, if the one-month waiting period applies, the mother's second birth would fall on *Rosh Chodesh Elul* at the earliest — either still within the year or at the very boundary. If it does not apply, she could give birth earlier.

The second framing (*iba'it eima*): perhaps everyone agrees on Ze'iri's waiting period, but the dispute is whether an animal can give birth with less than five full months of gestation (*yoledet limkuta'in*). The *tanna kamma* holds that five full months are required, placing the second birth at *Rosh Chodesh Elul*; Rabbi Shimon holds that a birth can occur with less than five full months, allowing the second birth before *Rosh Chodesh Elul*.

The third framing (*ve'ee ba'isayma*): perhaps everyone agrees that five full months of gestation are required and everyone agrees on Ze'iri. The dispute is then about *miktzat hayom kekulo* — whether the final day of the fifth month counts as the complete last day of gestation. According to the *tanna kamma*, one does not say *miktzat hayom kekulo* in this context, meaning she cannot give birth until *Rosh Chodesh Elul* itself. According to Rabbi Shimon, one does say *miktzat hayom kekulo*, meaning she could give birth on the last day of *Av*, the 29th — while the year is still open. It is admittedly striking to apply a legal category like *miktzat hayom kekulo* to a biological reality, but that is how the *gemara* frames it.

The fourth framing (*ve'ee ba'isayma*): perhaps everyone agrees on all of the above, and the mother could indeed have given birth on the last day of *Av*. But then the *tanna kamma* says that second offspring cannot be counted for *ma'aser*, because the question arises — *mechusar zman nichnas l'dir l'hit'aser* — can one take *ma'aser behemah* on an animal that is not yet eight days old? That offspring, born on the last day of *Av*, would be only one day old on *Rosh Chodesh Elul*. Is it eligible for *ma'aser*? This fourth framing transitions us to an entirely new halachic discussion, which the *gemara* now takes up.

### Tosafot Calendar

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question about *Rashi*'s construction: the scenario depends on a precise five-month timeline that lines up exactly with the calendar. But what about *chaseirim* and *maleiim*, short and full months? And what about a *shanah me'uberet*, a leap year? A leap year adds an extra month, which would throw off the entire calculation. If the timeline *Rashi* constructs only works in an ordinary year, one might ask whether the scenario is actually achievable in practice.

### Leap Year Interpretations

*Tosafot* does not offer a fully direct resolution to this question. *Rashi* himself, in what appears to be an alternate version of his commentary, offers a different reading of the dispute that incorporates the *shanah me'uberet* concern. According to this reading, the *tanna kamma* merely requires that the relevant births occur before *Rosh Chodesh Elul* — which, in a leap year, is a wider window. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, says *betoch shenatah* — literally within twelve months. According to Rabbi Shimon, the scenario must be achievable within a strict twelve-month period. This is why Rabbi Shimon needs a more flexible understanding of gestation and waiting periods, whereas the *tanna kamma* can rely on the extended calendar of a leap year to make the scenario work. This reading ties the dispute back to whether *betoch shenatah* means a calendar year from *Elul* to *Elul*, which can expand with a leap year, or a literal twelve-month period.

*Rashi*'s various explanations of this *sugya* number at least three distinct versions, and the difficulties are significant enough that the scenario has resisted clean resolution across the *rishonim*. The large *Rashi* and *Tosafot* here are not the result of complex legal disputes but of a *gemara* whose underlying factual scenario is genuinely difficult to reconstruct. This happens occasionally when the *gemara* is very specific about a practical case and the details resist a clean reading.

## Mechusar Zman

### Young Animals

The final framing of the *tanna kamma*–Rabbi Shimon dispute introduced the question of whether an animal born just before *Rosh Chodesh Elul* — and therefore less than eight days old — can be sanctified as *ma'aser behemah* and counted toward the group from which *ma'aser* is taken. This question of *mechusar zman* — an animal that has not yet reached the minimum age for a *korban* — now becomes the focus.

### Baraita: <8 Days

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon ben Yehudah says in the name of Rabbi Shimon — and *stam* Rabbi Shimon in the *gemara* refers to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — that an animal which is *mechusar zman*, not yet eight days old, may nonetheless enter the pen for *ma'aser*: *mechusar zman nichnas l'dir l'hit'aser*.

### Bechor Comparison

Rabbi Shimon derives this position from an analogy to *bechor*: just as a *bechor* is sanctified with *kedushah* from the moment of its birth — even before it is eight days old and even though it cannot be brought as a *korban* until after the eighth day — so too *ma'aser* can be sanctified on an animal less than eight days old, and it is brought as a *korban* once it reaches the proper time. The formula is parallel: *kadosh lifnei zmano, v'karev l'achar zmano* — sanctified before its time, and offered after its time.

This is explicitly an exception to the general rule. Normally, the Torah states *miyom hashmini vahalah yeiratzeh l'hakriv korban laHashem* — only from the eighth day onward is an animal acceptable for offering — and *Chazal* understand this to mean that if one tries to sanctify an animal younger than eight days, the *kedushah* does not take effect at all. The exception is *bechor*, which is sanctified by its very identity as the firstborn the moment it is born. Rabbi Shimon argues that *ma'aser* shares this quality and therefore likewise applies before the eighth day.

### Korban Analogy

The *gemara* challenges: rather than deriving *ma'aser* from *bechor*, why not derive it from other *korbanot*, where sanctification of a *mechusar zman* animal is not effective? The *gemara* therefore moves to a classical comparison — to which category is *ma'aser* more properly analogous?

### Shared Properties

The *gemara* states that it makes more sense to compare *ma'aser* to *bechor* — *mistabra mibechor hava lei lemeilaf* — and lists four features shared by *bechor* and *ma'aser* that other *korbanot* do not share: *goel*, *mum*, *tmura*, and *achilah*.

### Goel & Mum

*Goel* means that neither a *bechor* nor *ma'aser* can be redeemed (*podeh*), even when they bear a blemish. A *bechor* with a *mum* is eaten by the *kohen*; *ma'aser* with a *mum* is eaten by the *Yisrael* — but neither can have its *kedushah* transferred through redemption. This feature is directly connected to the nature of their *kedushah*: it is tied to their identity as the first or as the tenth, and because it is not an abstracted, transferable sanctity, it cannot be removed.

*Mum* as a separate point highlights the flip side of the same principle. Since they cannot be redeemed, an animal with a blemish is simply eaten with that blemish — outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, without being brought as a *korban*. This is genuinely anomalous: a *korban* with *kedushat korban* and a blemish that ordinarily would have to be redeemed before it could be used — yet here it is eaten directly, without redemption. The rule in the *gemara* more broadly is that if an animal already had a *mum* before being sanctified, its *kedushah* is never *kedushat korban* but only *kedushat damim* — the value of its money, because it never had the capacity to be brought as a *korban*. The exception is *chutz min habechor min hama'aser*: for *bechor* and *ma'aser*, since the *kedushah* attaches by virtue of their identity as first or tenth, they receive *kedushat korban* even if the *mum* preceded the *kedushah*. This does not mean they are brought as *korbanot*; it means the character of their *kedushah* is full *kedushat korban*, and they are eaten outside the *Beit HaMikdash* even though they bear a blemish.

### Tmura

*Tmura* is the prohibition against substituting one animal for a sanctified animal. While *tmura* is forbidden across all categories of *korbanot*, by regular *korbanot* the substitution is effective — the *kedushah* expands and applies to the substitute animal as well. By *bechor* and *ma'aser*, *tmura* is not effective: the *kedushah* does not transfer. Again, this is a direct consequence of the identity-based nature of their *kedushah*. Because the sanctity of *bechor* is precisely its being the first, and the sanctity of *ma'aser* is precisely its being the tenth, the *kedushah* cannot extend to something that is not the first or the tenth. It is not an abstraction being instilled; it is an attribute inseparable from their particular identity.

### Achila

*Achila* is the fourth point. *Rashi* explains that this refers to the fact that *bechor* and *ma'aser*, when blemished, are eaten without first being brought as a *korban* — they are eaten outside the *Beit HaMikdash* entirely. This might seem to overlap with *mum*, but *Rashi* distinguishes them: *mum* addresses the rule established in the previous *perek* — that if an animal has a blemish before sanctification, the *kedushah* is ordinarily only of the monetary kind. *Achila* addresses the practical consequence: even with a *mum*, they are eaten rather than redeemed. All four properties point toward the same underlying principle — that *bechor* and *ma'aser* have identity-based *kedushah* rather than instilled *kedushah*, and that principle is what makes Rabbi Shimon's position coherent: if the *kedushah* can apply even when a *mum* precedes it, perhaps the *kedushah* can apply even when the animal has not yet reached the minimum age.

### Identity Kedushah

These four properties are deeply interconnected and all derive from the same source: the *kedushah* of *bechor* and *ma'aser* is not an abstract quality instilled by an act of the owner but is intrinsic to the animal's standing as first or tenth. It is worth noting that *ma'aser* occupies an interesting middle position in this regard — unlike *bechor*, it is the owner's act of counting that designates which animal is the tenth. The *kedushah* does not arise entirely without human action. And yet, once designated as the tenth, the *kedushah* is fully identity-based and cannot be transferred. It is a midpoint between naturally arising *kedushah* and deliberately instilled *kedushah*.

### Counter-Analogy

The *gemara* counters: *adaraba mikosham hava lei lemilaf* — perhaps one should rather derive *ma'aser* from regular *korbanot*. The four grounds offered are: *zachar* — *bechor* must be male, but *ma'aser* like other *korbanot* need not be; *kadosh* — *ma'aser* requires a human act of designation, as with regular *korbanot*, unlike *bechor* which is sanctified automatically; *b'matanos* — *bechor* is a *matnat kehunah* going to the *kohen*, whereas *ma'aser* like certain other *korbanot* is eaten by the owners; and the fourth, that *ma'aser* is not itself required to be a *bechor*, and indeed cannot be one. These are genuine similarities, though they feel less central than the four *bechor*-*ma'aser* parallels. Nevertheless, the *gemara* treats the comparison as four against four, leaving the analogy-based argument at a standoff.

### Gezeira Shava

Since the analogical argument cannot resolve the question definitively — either comparison could be argued — the *gemara* concludes that Rabbi Shimon actually learns the law through a *gezeira shava*. The word *ha-avarah* appears in the context of *ma'aser*: *yaavor tachat hashevet* — the animals pass under the staff. And the same root appears by *bechor*: *v'ha'avarta kol peter rechem* — you shall cause every opener of the womb to pass. This verbal linkage through *gezeira shava* ties *ma'aser* to *bechor* formally, even though the analogical comparison was inconclusive.

## Miscarriage Signs

### Miscarriage Definition

Returning to the central concern of the *masechta*, the *gemara* now addresses the question of how to identify whether what emerged from an animal constitutes a true miscarriage — with implications both for the mother's *tumah* status in the human case and for the *bechor* status of the next birth in the animal case. There are two distinct questions: first, was this in fact an aborted pregnancy rather than some other discharge? Second, even if it was an aborted pregnancy, was the fetus sufficiently developed that its miscarriage exempts the mother from *bechor* for the subsequent birth?

### Raba's Signs

*Heichi dami tinof* — how does one assess a miscarriage? *Amar Rava*: like the shepherds of Tzaltza say, *atzar chayuta*. *Rashi* explains this as meaning that the womb was somehow closed or constricted — *ne'etzar rachmah* — and the fetus dissolved within it, *venimok ha'ubar*. The term *chayuta* may mean the life of the fetus was somehow held back or squeezed out, and the result is what emerges from the animal. This describes the internal mechanism rather than the visual appearance of what one sees.

### Shmuel's Signs

Shmuel describes the appearance more directly: *bavu'ei dedama* — bubbles or clots of blood, a congealed and bloody discharge. This is a description of what one observes externally.

### Expert Consultation

The *gemara* then says: *vetzarich leharot lechacham* — it must be shown to a *chacham* for assessment. But the *gemara* immediately questions this: *chacham mena yada* — what does a Torah scholar in the *beit midrash* know about animal miscarriages? The answer is given by Rav Papa: *ro'eh chacham* — a shepherd who is an expert. When the *gemara* says to show it to a *chacham*, it means an expert in the relevant domain, not a *posek*. The conclusion is explicit: if you want to know whether an animal has miscarried, ask an expert shepherd, not a rabbi.

### Forty Days

Rav Chisda introduces the human parallel: *yetzirat havlad be'ishah arba'im yom* — fetal formation in a human woman occurs at forty days. The halachic significance of this threshold is twofold: if a woman miscarries before forty days, she does not become *teme'ah leidah*, as it is not considered a birth for these purposes; and in the context of *bechor*, if the miscarriage occurs before forty days it is not considered a prior birth and the next child remains a *bechor*, whereas if it occurs after forty days, the next child is exempt. The forty-day threshold is understood as the point at which the fetus has taken on enough form to be halachically recognized as a fetus. What exactly constitutes that formation is a matter of biological interest — the *gemara* seems to understand it in terms of the fetus taking on a recognizable shape, limbs acquiring some form — though the precise developmental biology involved is not fully resolved in the text.

### Animal Development

The question is then raised for animals: *ba'ei Rav Chisda — bevehemah bekammah* — what is the analogous threshold for animals? How much fetal development must there be for an animal's miscarriage to count as a prior birth and exempt the next offspring from *bechor*?

### Ze'iri 30 Days

Rav Papa responds to Abaye: *lav hainu deZe'iri* — is this not what Ze'iri meant when he said *ein nefalim pachot mishloshim yom*, there is no miscarriage with less than thirty days of development? That is, perhaps Ze'iri's statement means that a fetus of less than thirty days' development is not considered a *nefel* — and therefore does not exempt from *bechor*. This is a natural reading of Ze'iri's words, and it is worth noting that it is quite different from how Ze'iri has been understood throughout the preceding discussion, where his statement was read as meaning an animal cannot conceive again within thirty days after a miscarriage.

### Ze'iri Ambiguity

The *gemara* responds: *hahu lekabla itmar* — Ze'iri's statement was said regarding the waiting period after miscarriage, i.e., the animal does not conceive for thirty days afterward. That is the way we have been reading Ze'iri for the last two days. The reading Rav Papa now proposes — that thirty days refers to the minimum fetal development needed for the miscarriage to count — sounds more natural from the words themselves, but the *gemara* does not accept it as the correct interpretation of Ze'iri. And the *gemara* leaves unresolved the underlying question of how much fetal development is required in an animal for the miscarriage to create an exemption from *bechor*. This is a genuine *teku* — how much development is needed remains undetermined.

## Buyer Context

### Seller Type

Having discussed what assumptions can be made when an animal's prior birth history is unknown, the *gemara* now turns to a related question: to what degree can one rely on explicit or implicit statements made by the seller? Until now, the discussion assumed one bought from a non-Jew. The *gemara* now addresses the case of buying from a Jew.

### Rav: Silence=No Birth

*Amar Rav: bechor vadai.* Rav's position is that when one buys from a Jew and the seller says nothing, one can conclusively assume the animal has not yet had its firstborn, and the next birth is a *bechor* given to the *kohen*. The reasoning: *de'im itta debackera ishtabochei hava mishtabach lei* — if it had already given birth, the seller would have praised it as a selling point.

### Praise Inference

Why would a prior birth be a selling point? *Rashi*'s explanation is that the seller would have advertised: this animal has already given birth, so you do not need to worry about *bechor*. The animal is, in a manner of speaking, pre-*bechor*ed — the obligation has already been discharged. From the seller's silence we therefore infer that the animal has not yet given birth and the next birth is definitively a *bechor*.

### Health Benefit

*Tosafot* presents a different version of *Rashi*'s text and a different rationale for why a prior birth would be praiseworthy. According to *Tosafot*'s reading, the seller would advertise that the animal has already successfully survived pregnancy and childbirth — proving its capacity to bear young without risk to its life. An animal that has demonstrated this is thereby more valuable. From this angle, the seller's silence indicates he has no such track record to advertise, and the animal has not yet given birth.

### Shmuel: Safek

Shmuel disagrees: *bechor safek* — silence leaves the status uncertain. Nothing can be conclusively inferred from the seller's failure to praise the animal's prior birth history.

### Slaughter Assumption

Shmuel's reasoning: *lishchita ka ba'ei lei* — the seller may have assumed the buyer intended to slaughter the animal immediately, in which case its prior birth history is irrelevant and there would be no occasion to mention it. Indeed, once an animal is slaughtered it may not matter at all whether it had previously given birth, and if anything the prior birth might be a drawback — as one suggested, older animals that have already given birth can have tougher meat. Since silence can be explained by the seller's assumption that the buyer will slaughter, nothing can be inferred from that silence.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan takes the opposite extreme from Rav: *chullin vadai* — one can conclusively assume from the seller's silence that the animal has already given birth and is *chullin*. There is no *bechor* concern.

### Seller Warning

Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning: *im issa d'lo bichra, kivan d'ika issura* — if the animal had not yet given birth, and there is therefore a potential *issur* that the buyer might stumble into by not recognizing the next birth as a *bechor*, then *udoi havai modi lei* — the seller would surely have warned the buyer. A responsible person selling an animal to someone who might unknowingly misuse a *bechor* would feel obligated to flag that concern. From the seller's silence, we conclude he has nothing to warn about — the animal has already given birth.

### Responsibility

The *gemara* notes that a *baraita* appears to support Rabbi Yochanan: we know that on four occasions during the year when it is common to purchase animals for slaughter, a butcher who sold the mother and then the child on the same day, or the child and then the mother, must announce this to subsequent buyers — so that the next person who slaughters does not violate *oso v'et beno*. If the butcher said nothing, *holech v'shochet v'eino nimna* — the buyer may proceed to slaughter without concern. Silence implies there is no problem. The *gemara* therefore entertains that this *baraita* supports Rabbi Yochanan and refutes both Rav and Shmuel.

The *gemara* deflects: *hasam b'mocher talyo miltasa, hacha b'lokeach talyo miltasa* — there the obligation falls on the seller, here it falls on the buyer. What does this mean?

### Verse Mandate

*Rashi*'s explanation is that in the case of *oso v'et beno*, there is a specific Torah-derived mandate requiring the seller to announce. *Rashi* understands this from a *pasuk* — *yom echad*, *yom hamiyuchad* — indicating a formal requirement to declare on those particular days. Where such a mandate exists, silence carries evidentiary weight. But in the *bechor* case, no such mandate falls on the seller; the responsibility to inquire belongs to the buyer. Therefore the seller's silence cannot be used to draw any inference, and the cases are distinguishable.

### Knowledge Duty

*Tosafot* offers an explanation grounded in practical reality rather than a formal verse. In the *oso v'et beno* case, the seller knows exactly what has occurred — he sold the mother earlier that day, he knows the buyer intends to slaughter tonight, and he alone possesses the relevant information. Given that he knows what is about to happen, the responsibility falls squarely on him to prevent the buyer from sinning. It is not a verse-derived rule; it is simply that the seller is the only one positioned to prevent the sin and has full knowledge of the facts.

### Mother/Child Case

The seller in the *oso v'et beno* case possesses clear and complete knowledge: he sold the mother, he knows the buyer plans to slaughter, he knows what the consequence will be. There is nothing that should make the buyer suspect a problem — why would the buyer assume the seller had already sold the mother or the child? The buyer has no particular reason to ask. Therefore, the obligation falls entirely on the seller who does have full knowledge.

### Buyer Responsibility

By contrast, in the *bechor* case, the seller's knowledge may be uncertain, the buyer's exposure to risk is not immediate, and there are many scenarios in which the animal's history would not create any problem at all — perhaps the animal is female, perhaps the buyer will wait for a *mum*. There is nothing that specifically heightens the seller's obligation to speak. On the other hand, the buyer has every reason to suspect that an animal of unknown history might not yet have given birth, and it is a straightforward question to ask. The responsibility accordingly falls on the buyer, and the seller's silence carries no evidentiary inference.

According to *Tosafot*, this distinction is not arbitrary but arises from the relative positions of knowledge and moral exposure. How much is it my responsibility, and how much is it buyer beware? The answer depends on: does the seller have full knowledge? Does the seller know the buyer is about to sin? Is the seller the only one who can prevent it? In the *oso v'et beno* case, all three conditions are met and the obligation is fully the seller's. In the *bechor* case, none of them are, and the obligation is the buyer's. This is a more compelling analysis than *Rashi*'s, which makes the distinction somewhat mechanical through appeal to a verse.

### Non-Jew Seller

We now return to the case of buying from a non-Jew. The implicit question is what one can infer from a non-Jewish seller's statements — both explicit and implicit. The prior discussion assumed one simply could not rely on a non-Jew's statement. The *gemara* now makes this explicit.

### Tosafot: Non-Jew

*Tosafot* engages the question directly. *Rashi*'s text, as *Tosafot* reads it, is that even if a non-Jewish seller explicitly praised the animal — saying it had already given birth — one cannot trust him, because he may be saying it merely to increase the sale price: *d'lehashbiach mekach d'oveid kochavim omer kein*. Furthermore, *Tosafot* adds, even if the non-Jew was not speaking with any mercenary intent — even *masiach lefi tumo*, speaking in passing without any agenda of answering a halachic question — *eino ne'eman*, he is still not trusted, because even an unintentional statement may be motivated by the desire to make his merchandise appear more valuable.

### Testimony Principles

This raises a broader halachic question about the reliability of testimony and the factors that affect it. Two distinct concerns emerge in the *gemara*'s framework. The first is intentional misrepresentation for profit: if someone stands to benefit from a statement, we discount it. The second is more subtle — whether someone who is not part of the halachic system fully appreciates the weight of the question being decided. Someone who does not understand what is at stake halachically may give a technically accurate answer without grasping its significance, or may be cavalier about details that would matter greatly to a *posek*.

### System Understanding

These concerns are heightened specifically in the context of non-Jewish sellers because of the dynamic between the Jewish halachic system and those outside it. Within the Jewish community, the mechanism of *areivut* — mutual responsibility — and *lifnei iver* — the prohibition against causing another to sin — create strong incentives for honesty in halachic matters. A Jewish seller who knows a buyer is about to stumble into a *bechor* situation is personally at risk of violating *lifnei iver* if he says nothing. He is not merely being honest for the buyer's sake; he is protecting himself from his own transgression. A non-Jewish seller has no such system of incentives, and that absence is part of what the *gemara* identifies as a reason for reduced reliability in this specific context. The question of whether these observations are framed as general statements about non-Jewish trustworthiness or as statements about the specific dynamics of Jewish–non-Jewish commercial relations in the context of halachic compliance is a matter of ongoing discussion.

## Large Animal Miscarriage

### Mishna: Clots

The *gemara* now presents the *mishna* of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: if a large domesticated animal — *behemah gassah*, i.e., a cow — expelled clots or clumps of blood, *harei zo tikkaver* — it must be buried — *peturah min habechorah* — and the mother is exempt from *bechor* for the next birth.

### Bechor Exemption

The burial requirement is stated alongside the exemption from *bechor*, and the combination raises an immediate question. On one hand, the next birth is exempt — this implies that what emerged is considered to have halachic standing as a fetus, as a prior birth. On the other hand, the *mishna* mandates burial, which normally applies to *neveilah* — a carcass that conveys *tumah*. So is this a formed fetus? And if it is, why would it need to be specified that it must be buried?

### Rabbi Chiyya

*Tani Rabbi Hiyya*: it does not cause *tumah* — *einah metammah lo bemaga velo bemassa* — neither through contact nor through carrying. It is therefore not *neveilah* in the conventional sense.

### Burial Paradox

The *gemara* immediately asks: if it does not cause *tumah* — meaning the fetus was not formed — then why must it be buried, and why is the mother exempt from *bechor*? If there is no fetus, there is no prior birth, and burial seems unnecessary.

### Burial Purpose

The *gemara* answers the first question: *kedei lefarsem she'niptarah min habechorah* — the burial is not because the clots constitute *neveilah*, but because burying them announces publicly that the mother has been discharged from the obligation of *bechor*. The act of burial is a declaration: this was treated as a birth, and therefore the next offspring is not a *bechor*.

### Tumah Paradox

But this answer creates the second problem: if the burial functions as a declaration that what emerged is a fetus — enough to exempt the mother — then why doesn't it cause *tumah*? A formed fetus that constitutes a *nefel* ought to convey *tumah* through contact and carrying.

### Bittul B'Rov

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *mishum bittul be-rov* — it is nullified in the majority. There is indeed a fetus present within the discharge. But the fetus is mixed together with a large quantity of non-fetal material — blood, fluid, and other matter from the womb — and in that mixture, the fetal matter loses its legal identity through *bittul be-rov*, nullification in the majority. Since the fetus cannot be identified as a distinct, present entity, it does not convey *tumah*.

### Lost Identity

The result is an unusual combination: there is a fetus here, sufficient to cause the mother's exemption from *bechor*, but the fetus has lost its identity in the surrounding material, and therefore does not cause *tumah*. It exists halachically for one purpose — exempting the mother — but has lost its distinct identity for another — conveying impurity.

### Rabbi Shimon

The *gemara* notes that Rabbi Yochanan is going according to his own reasoning (*ve-azda Rabbi Yochanan le-taameih*), since he had stated that Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov and Rabbi Shimon said the same thing. The parallel from Rabbi Shimon is drawn from a *mishna* about the placenta: *ha-shilya ba-bayit, ha-bayit tamei* — if a placenta is found in a house, the house is *tamei*, not because the placenta itself is the fetus, but because there is no placenta without a fetus — *she-ein shilya belo valad*. Rabbi Shimon, however, says: *valad nimok ad she-lo yatza* — the fetus dissolved before it emerged, and therefore does not cause *tumah*. Rabbi Shimon's principle is that a fetus which has dissolved and mixed into the surrounding material does not retain its identity as an entity that conveys *tumah*.

### Mixed Fetus

This ties together the ruling in our *mishna* with Rabbi Shimon's broader principle. According to Rabbi Chiyya — and supported by Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning via Rabbi Shimon — a fetus that has dissolved into the fluid and other material of the womb loses its halachic identity as an entity. It retains enough existence to exempt the mother from *bechor*, since it did constitute a prior birth. But because it is dissolved and mixed with the majority of non-fetal material, it does not cause *tumah* through contact or carrying. The burial requirement is not a *neveilah* procedure but a declaration of the mother's status. The fetus exists, exempts, but no longer identifies.
