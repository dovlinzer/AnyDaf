# Bava Kamma 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ba'al HaShor Naki

### Overview & Applications

The discussion in today's daf opens at 42a, five lines from the top, at the *baraita* introduced by the formula *tanya idach*. We are in the middle of a series of teachings derived from the phrase *ba'al hashor naki* — the owner of the ox shall be clean — which appears in the context of the goring ox. When an ox gores a person to death for the first time, the ox is stoned, but the owner bears no personal liability. After three such incidents, the verse *gam ba'alav yumat* — also his owner shall die — is interpreted as *misah bidei shamayim*, divine death, and the owner atones through the payment of *kofer*. In the first three instances, however, *ba'al hashor naki* — the owner is clean — and *Chazal* derive additional teachings from this phrase beyond the simple peshat.

The first two applications had already been covered. The first is that one is forbidden to derive any benefit whatsoever from the gored ox — the animal is *assur be-hana'ah*, including its hide. The second is that Rabbi Eliezer derived from *ba'al hashor naki* that the owner of an innocuous ox (*shor tam*) is not liable to pay *chatzi kofer* — half the *kofer* — when his ox gores to death for the first time. Rabbi Akiva challenged this, arguing that the model of a *tam* is payment from the body of the animal (*migufo*), and since the animal is condemned to be stoned, it is worth nothing; the very concept of *chatzi kofer* is therefore incoherent in this context. The remainder of today's daf continues to explore what else is derived from *ba'al hashor naki*.

### Assur be-Hana'ah

As noted, the first teaching derived from *ba'al hashor naki* is that the condemned ox is *assur be-hana'ah* — one may not benefit even from its leather or hide. The phrase *ba'al hashor naki* signals the owner's complete disengagement from the animal and its value.

### No Chatzi Kofer

The second teaching is that there is no *chatzi kofer* for the owner of a *shor tam* when it gores a person to death. Since a *tam* pays *migufo* — from the body of the animal — and the body of the animal is condemned to stoning, there is nothing from which to collect. Rabbi Akiva's critique of Rabbi Eliezer was that no *pasuk* was needed to reach this conclusion, since it follows logically. This dispute, and the final *drasha* on exemption from *chatzi kofer*, set the stage for all that follows.

## Dmei Vlodot Exemption

### R. Yose HaGelili

Another *baraita* is introduced: *ba'al hashor naki* — Rabbi Yose HaGelili says — means the owner is exempt from *dmei vladot*, the payment for fetal loss. The case in view is one where an ox gores a pregnant woman and she miscarries. Separately, the Torah in *Mishpatim* establishes that when men fight and one strikes a pregnant woman causing a miscarriage, the assailant pays *dmei vladot* to the husband. One might therefore have thought that the same payment should apply when an ox causes a miscarriage. Rabbi Yose HaGelili teaches that *ba'al hashor naki* exempts the owner of the ox from this payment.

This *halakha* requires some unpacking, because it is genuinely puzzling. When an ox gores a woman, we do pay *nezek* — damages for injury to her body. The method of assessing that injury is to ask how much the woman's value on the hypothetical slave market has decreased as a result. One might expect that if a pregnant woman miscarries, her body is worth less — a pregnant slave fetches a higher price than a non-pregnant one, because the buyer also acquires the future value of the offspring. Yet the *halakha* treats the fetal loss as a separate category, *dmei vladot*, rather than as part of the *nezek* calculation for the woman's body. The very fact that a distinct *pasuk* is needed to impose liability for *dmei vladot* — and a separate *pasuk* to exempt the ox's owner — indicates that the straightforward *nezek* assessment of the woman's body does not automatically account for the lost fetus. This is admittedly strange, but it is the *halakha*.

### R. Akiva's Critique

Rabbi Akiva challenges Rabbi Yose HaGelili. The verse in *Mishpatim* introducing *dmei vladot* already says *ki yinatzu anashim* — when men fight. From this phrase we derive *anashim velo shevarim* — the law applies to people, not to oxen. If the Torah itself already excludes oxen from *dmei vladot*, why does Rabbi Yose HaGelili need to derive the exemption from *ba'al hashor naki*? The Gemara acknowledges that Rabbi Akiva's critique is well-taken: *shapir ka'amar Rabbi Akiva*. So what would Rabbi Yose HaGelili respond?

### Muad vs. Tam

Rav Ulla the son of Rav Idi answers that both *pesukim* are indeed necessary. Without *ba'al hashor naki*, one would reason as follows: *ki yinatzu anashim* — *anashim velo shevarim*, people and not oxen — but more precisely, people insofar as people are always treated as *muad*, fully liable. The derivation *anashim velo shevarim* would then exclude only a *shor muad*, which is comparable to a human being always liable. However, a *shor tam* — which is the ox we are discussing throughout the passage of *ba'al hashor naki* — would not be excluded by that reasoning, because a *tam* is not analogous to the fully liable human. One might therefore have concluded that the *tam* must pay *dmei vladot*. Hence *ba'al hashor naki* comes to exclude even the *tam*.

Rava, however, raises a sharp objection. If the reasoning is that *anashim velo shevarim* excludes the *muad* — since people are always *muad* — then I would have derived from *kal va'chomer* that the *tam* is likewise exempt: if the *muad*, which is more culpable, is exempt, how much more so the *tam*. I therefore already know both are exempt from *anashim velo shevarim* alone. So why does *ba'al hashor naki* need to address this at all? Rava answers by reversing the logic. Perhaps *anashim velo shevarim* — understood in the direction of comparability — would have led us to exempt the *muad*, and then to infer the *tam* as well. *Ba'al hashor naki*, then, does not come to teach exemption; rather, it comes to restrict the exemption to the *tam* alone and to affirm that the *muad is actually liable* for *dmei vladot*. This yields a striking conclusion: a *shor muad* that causes a miscarriage pays *dmei vladot*, while a *shor tam* does not.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges this approach. If we are prepared to use *ba'al hashor naki* to modify the implication of *anashim velo shevarim* in the case of *dmei vladot*, the same move could be made for *boshet* — the payment for indignity. The passage describing *boshet* also uses the phrase *ki yinatzu anashim*, and similarly one could apply *anashim velo shevarim* to derive that oxen are exempt. By the same reasoning Rava employed, one would then use *ba'al hashor naki* to teach that only the *tam* is exempt and the *muad* must pay *boshet*. Abaye's point is that Rava's method yields an absurd result if applied consistently: why does it produce an obligation to pay *dmei vladot* but not *boshet*? There is no principled reason to apply the method selectively.

The Gemara takes Abaye's challenge seriously. If we were genuinely prepared to derive an obligation for a *shor muad* to pay *boshet*, the *baraita* presenting Rabbi Yose HaGelili's teaching should have stated explicitly that the owner is exempt from both *dmei vladot* and *boshet*. The fact that only *dmei vladot* is mentioned signals that something is wrong with the general approach, and a different reading is needed.

### Anashim Resolution

Abaye and Rava together — *amarei tarvaihu* — offer a resolution that abandons the previous approach and reads *anashim velo shevarim* entirely differently. The *pasuk* in question reads: *ki yinatzu anashim venagefu ishah harah veyatzu yeladeiha velo yihyeh ason anosh ye'anesh* — when men fight and strike a pregnant woman and her children come out, and there is no tragedy, the striker shall be fined. *Ve'im ason yihyeh venasata nefesh tachat nafesh* — but if there is a tragedy, you shall give a life for a life.

The reading offered by Abaye and Rava is this: *anashim ein ason be'ishah ye'anshu* — by men, if there is no loss of life involving the woman, they pay all attendant damages including *dmei vladot*. *Yesh ason be'ishah lo ye'anshu* — but if the woman dies, they do not pay monetary damages for the lesser injuries. This is an expression of the principle *kim leh bederabbah mineih*: when a person incurs a capital or severe criminal liability, lesser monetary obligations are subsumed and forfeited. The death of the woman renders the attacker liable to the more severe sanction, and the financial payment for *dmei vladot* is absorbed.

*Velo shevarim* — and not oxen: by oxen, even if the ox kills, the owner does not say *kim leh*. The ox causes a death, the ox is stoned, but the owner's financial liabilities — for damages other than the loss of life itself — are not absorbed. It is important to note that the absence of payment for *loss of life* by an ox is not itself an expression of *kim leh*. As *Tosafot* stresses, there is no civil monetary compensation for loss of life in halacha; that is simply not a category of financial liability. *Kim leh* is a separate principle by which a person who incurs a capital obligation is released from lesser monetary obligations that would otherwise apply. By oxen, this principle does not operate.

The contribution of *ba'al hashor naki* is then to exclude *dmei vladot* specifically. Since *anashim velo shevarim* now teaches the inapplicability of *kim leh* by oxen, there is no longer a *pasuk* exempting oxen from *dmei vladot* — one might think an ox should pay *dmei vladot* since it does not say *kim leh*. It is precisely *ba'al hashor naki* that comes to say: nevertheless, an ox does not pay *dmei vladot*. The two *pesukim* thus work in tandem: one teaches there is no *kim leh* by oxen, the other teaches there is no *dmei vladot* by oxen.

## Kim Leh Principle

### R. Ada bar Ahava

Rav Ada bar Ahava challenges this framework. Is the exemption from payment by people really contingent on *ason* — on a loss of life triggering *kim leh*? He argues it is rather *bkavanah talya milsa* — it depends on intention. According to Rashi's reading of Rav Ada bar Ahava's position, he holds a remarkably narrow view of *kim leh*: it applies only when one performs a direct, intentional act of murder toward the very person who was killed. Accidental homicide, in his view, does not trigger *kim leh* at all. Furthermore, he holds — strikingly — that there is in fact a financial payment for loss of life, at least in certain circumstances.

### Intention Doctrine

On Rav Ada bar Ahava's reading, the *pasuk* is structured as follows. *Ki nishkavnu zeh la-zeh* — when people are fighting intending to harm each other — *af al gav she-asa'un be'ishah ye'anshu* — even if in the course of their fighting they cause injury or even death to a woman, they will still pay, because *kim leh* attaches only to the act directed at the intended target. If Reuven is trying to kill Shimon and accidentally strikes a woman who dies, *kim leh* operates only with respect to what was done to Shimon — not with respect to the collateral harm to the woman. Therefore, the attacker remains liable for whatever injury or loss of life befell the woman.

The one case where payment is not made is when the fighters *nishkavnu le'ishah atzmah* — when they actually intended to kill the woman herself. In that case, *kim leh* does attach to the woman, and neither payment for her life nor payment for the fetus is made. *Velo shevarim* — but not by oxen: even if the ox intentionally gored a woman — the paradigm case of *shor haniskal* — one does not say *kim leh* for the ox's act, and the owner remains liable. According to Rav Ada bar Ahava, who holds there is a financial payment for loss of life, the owner would even pay that financial penalty — not *kofer*, but some form of monetary liability — since *kim leh* is simply not invoked for oxen. *Ba'al hashor naki* then comes to exclude *dmei vladot* specifically.

The Gemara notes that a *baraita* is brought in support of Rav Ada bar Ahava's position, and similarly that Rav Chaggai, upon arriving from the south, brought a *baraita* consistent with Rav Ada bar Ahava's view.

### Reconciliation

To summarize the resolution achieved: from *anashim velo shevarim* we derive — according to the dominant view of Abaye and Rava — that *kim leh* does not apply to oxen. From *ba'al hashor naki* we derive that an ox is exempt from *dmei vladot*. According to Rav Ada bar Ahava, the reading differs in its mechanism but arrives at the same place: *anashim velo shevarim* teaches that there is no *kim leh* by oxen even when they intentionally kill, while *ba'al hashor naki* teaches exemption from *dmei vladot*.

The cumulative list of what is derived from *ba'al hashor naki* now stands at three: the prohibition of benefit (*assur be-hana'ah*) from the condemned ox; exemption from *chatzi kofer* for the *shor tam*; and exemption from *dmei vladot*. We turn next to a fourth derivation.

## Dmei Eved Exemption

### R. Akiva Slave Exempt

A further *baraita* introduces Rabbi Akiva's teaching: *ba'al hashor naki* — the owner is exempt from *dmei eved*, the thirty-*shekel* payment owed when an ox gores a slave to death. The relevant verse in the Torah reads: *im kofer yushat alav... im eved yigach hashor o ama kesef shloshim shekalim yitein la'adonav* — if the ox gores a male or female slave, thirty silver *shekalim* shall be given to the master. When an ox is a *muad* and kills a slave, the owner pays this fixed sum of thirty. The question is whether a *shor tam* would also be liable for this payment.

The cases discussed up to this point — *dmei vladot*, *assur be-hana'ah*, *chatzi kofer* — were all directly associated with the killing of a person, making them naturally proximate to the *shor haniskal* scenario. The slave case is similar: it involves a loss of life, even if not the life of a free person. The *hazmana* — the proximity — to the stoning context is maintained.

### Internal Contradiction

The Gemara immediately raises a pointed question: why does Rabbi Akiva need a *pasuk* to reach this conclusion at all? Rabbi Akiva himself previously challenged Rabbi Eliezer on the *chatzi kofer* question by saying: bring the ox to court and they will pay you — meaning, the *tam* pays *migufo*, from the body of the animal, and the condemned ox is worth nothing. That same argument should have applied here. If the ox is condemned, there is nothing to pay from; why does *ba'al hashor naki* need to exempt the owner from *dmei eved*?

### Pre-Court Slaughter

Shmuel bar Abba Yitzchak answers: the scenario contemplated is one where the owner slaughtered the ox before the *gmar din* — before the court issued its final judgment condemning the animal. Once the court's sentence is pronounced, the ox becomes a *shor haniskal* and is *assur be-hana'ah* even if subsequently slaughtered. But if the owner slaughtered it before *gmar din*, the animal was never formally condemned; it never assumed the status of *shor haniskal*, and its meat remains *mutar*. In that case, there would be genuine value in the animal's body from which payment could theoretically be made. One might then have said: let him pay *dmei eved* from the meat of the animal he slaughtered before *gmar din*. It is this scenario that *ba'al hashor naki* comes to exclude. Since the ox was fundamentally deserving of death — *bar katla* — even though the owner circumvented the process by slaughtering it early, we do not say he should pay from its value. He has beaten the system, but the Torah does not then impose *dmei eved* upon him.

### R. Eliezer's Answer

The Gemara asks a further question: if this pre-*gmar din* slaughter scenario is a valid answer for Rabbi Akiva, why could Rabbi Eliezer not have given the same answer when Rabbi Akiva challenged him over *chatzi kofer*? Rabbi Eliezer's scenario was different: he was discussing a case where the ox had intended to gore an animal but accidentally struck a person. In that case, the ox never becomes a *shor haniskal* at all, because stoning applies only to an ox that intentionally killed a human being. An ox that accidentally killed while targeting an animal is not condemned. Here, the ox has genuine legal standing, is not condemned, retains its value, and there is therefore a real *hava amina* that the owner would pay *chatzi kofer* from it. This is why a *pasuk* — *ba'al hashor naki* — was needed to exclude that payment.

By contrast, the pre-*gmar din* slaughter scenario is weaker, because the animal was fundamentally *bar katla* — it was deserving of death from the outset. The Torah would not naturally have been understood to impose payment in a case where the standard outcome would be stoning; the pre-*gmar din* slaughter is an aberration. Rabbi Eliezer's scenario, where the animal was never deserving of stoning at all, is a stronger *hava amina* and therefore requires a *pasuk* more obviously. The Gemara states explicitly: Rabbi Akiva himself would have agreed that Rabbi Eliezer's scenario is fundamentally stronger — *vadi hachi hava* — and he challenged Rabbi Eliezer precisely hoping Rabbi Eliezer would produce a better answer than the one Rabbi Akiva had in mind.

### Akiva's Doctrine

Rav Assi then introduces an entirely different explanation for why Rabbi Akiva needed a *pasuk* to exclude *dmei eved* from the *tam* — and it is a striking one. He says he heard this from a great man, namely Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Chanina. The insight turns on a position unique to Rabbi Akiva: Rabbi Akiva holds that when a *shor tam* injures or kills a *person* (as opposed to an animal), the payment is full damages, not half. This is in contrast to the standard rule that a *tam* pays half damages. Rabbi Akiva derives this from the verse *k'mishpat hazeh ye'aseh lo* — this law shall be done to him — and holds that in human cases, the *tam* pays full.

Now, Rabbi Akiva's *hava amina* comes into focus. If a *tam* that kills a person pays full — not half, not *migufo* from the condemned body — then perhaps the *tam* should pay the full thirty *shekel* of *dmei eved* as well, and it would not be paying *migufo* but rather from cash (*min ha'aliyah*). This is why Rabbi Akiva could not say to himself what he said to Rabbi Eliezer — "the animal isn't worth anything" — because in Rabbi Akiva's own system, the payment for killing a person is not necessarily tied to the value of the animal's body.

The Gemara, however, deflates this answer. Rav Zeira points out to Rav Ashi that Rabbi Akiva already blunted his own position elsewhere. A *baraita* records Rabbi Akiva himself saying: one might have thought a *tam* that injures a person pays from liquid assets (*min ha'aliyah*); the verse says *k'mishpat hazeh ye'aseh lo* — this law shall be done to it — teaching that even when the *tam* pays full, it pays only *migufo*, from the body, not from cash. So Rabbi Akiva's own ruling reintroduces the *migufo* limitation even in the context of injury to people, and the question of the condemned ox's worthlessness reasserts itself.

### Arbitrary Payment

Rava therefore provides the definitive explanation. The *hava amina* — the assumption that would have led us to impose *dmei eved* on the *tam* — is based on the apparently arbitrary character of the thirty-*shekel* payment itself. For a free person killed by a *muad*, the owner pays the actual assessed value: if the person was worth one *sela*, one pays one; if thirty, one pays thirty. *Ben chorin yafeh sela nosein sela, shloshim nosein shloshim*. But for a slave, the payment is always thirty *shekel*, regardless of the slave's actual worth: *eved yafeh sela nosein shloshim*. The Torah fixes a flat rate.

This flat, somewhat arbitrary payment gives rise to a *hava amina*: since the Torah is being arbitrary about the quantum — paying thirty regardless of the slave's value — perhaps it is also being arbitrary about the form of payment, dispensing with the *migufo* limitation and requiring payment even from cash, even when the ox is a *tam* and would normally pay only from its body. And since the payment is arbitrary in one dimension, perhaps it is also arbitrary in another: it does not matter whether the ox is a *tam* or a *muad*, it always pays thirty. This is a genuine *hava amina*, rooted in a reasonable reading of the *pasuk* that seems to treat the slave case as categorically distinct. Therefore, a *pasuk* — *ba'al hashor naki* — is needed to say: no, the owner of the *tam* is clean even from *dmei eved*.

### Final Resolution

The Gemara confirms: *tanya k'vosei d'Rava* — a *baraita* is brought consistent with Rava's explanation. The *baraita* records Rabbi Eliezer saying *naki mi-dmei eved* — the owner is clean from the payment of *dmei eved* — and then walks through precisely the *kal va'chomer* arguments in both directions. On one hand, since by a free person killed by a *muad* you pay the exact assessed value, and yet you exempt the *tam*, so too by a slave you should exempt the *tam*. On the other hand — and this is the counter-argument that necessitates the *pasuk* — one could argue the opposite: the Torah is stricter, or more precisely more arbitrary, by the slave, since the slave always costs thirty even if he is worth only one *sela*. That arbitrariness might suggest the thirty-*shekel* payment is regime unto itself, applicable regardless of *tam* or *muad*, and payable from cash rather than from the body. It is this counter-argument that *ba'al hashor naki* comes to rebut.

The final tally of teachings derived from *ba'al hashor naki*: prohibition of benefit from the condemned ox; exemption from *chatzi kofer*; exemption from *dmei vladot*; and exemption from *dmei eved*.

## Vehemit Ish

### New Derivation

With the *drasha* on *ba'al hashor naki* concluded, the Gemara moves to a new verse. The *baraita* opens: *vehemit ish o ishah* — and it kills a man or a woman. This is a distinct phrase in the passage of the goring ox, and the Gemara now mines it for its own legal teachings.

### Woman-Man Linkage

Rabbi Akiva asks: what does the Torah intend by specifying both a man and a woman? If the purpose is simply to establish that one is equally liable for the life of a woman as for the life of a man, that has already been stated earlier: *ki yigach shor et ish o et ishah vamet* — when an ox gores a man or a woman and they die. Rabbi Akiva therefore reads the second mention as establishing an analogy: *hikish ishah le'ish* — the Torah links the woman to the man. Just as a man, when killed, has his *kofer* paid to his heirs, so too a woman, when killed, has her *kofer* paid to her heirs.

This might seem obvious, but the subtle question is: who are her heirs? A woman's husband normally inherits her estate. Yet here Rabbi Akiva appears to direct the *kofer* to her children, not her husband. The Gemara asks: does Rabbi Akiva not hold that a husband inherits from his wife? Surely Rabbi Akiva himself derives from the verse *veyarash otah* — in the laws of inheritance — that a husband does inherit his wife's estate, and that this is a biblical rule, not merely rabbinic. So why does the *kofer* go to her children and not to him?

### Post-Mortem Obligation

Reish Lakish resolves this cleanly. The rule directing the *kofer* to the woman's children rather than her husband is limited to *kofer* specifically, because *kofer* is paid only *le'achar mitah* — after death. The *kofer* obligation crystallizes only once the person is dead. At the moment of death, the woman is no longer this man's wife in the ongoing sense. A husband inherits property that was in his wife's possession and control at the time of her death — property she *had*. But property that comes to her only after her death is a different matter.

### Inheritance Timing

The Gemara elaborates on the underlying principle. The obligation of *kofer* accrues from the moment of goring — *chovin mish'at negichah* — but is not paid until death — *lemitah*. It was therefore *ra'uy* to come to the woman — fit to come to her estate — but had not yet come into her actual possession. The rule is *ein haba'al notel b'ra'uy k'b'muchzak* — a husband does not inherit property that was merely fit to come to his wife in the same way he inherits property that was already in her control. He takes only what she actually held at the moment of death, not what was destined to arrive afterward.

The rationale is intuitive. The husband's right of inheritance is grounded in the marital bond. Once the wife dies, that bond dissolves. The children, by contrast, descend from their mother regardless of her death; their biological connection to her is permanent. Money that flows to her estate *after* her death therefore passes to her children — her blood heirs — not to the husband whose legal bond to her ended at the moment of her death. The *kofer* therefore belongs to the children, as Rabbi Akiva's derivation of *mah ish nizkak leyorshav af ishah nizkaket leyorsheiha* indicates.
