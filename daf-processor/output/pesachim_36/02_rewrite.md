# Pesachim 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review & Overview

### Prior Topics

The previous daf was devoted to the question of what is necessary to produce *matzah* and what causes *chametz*, focusing on the five grains — to the exclusion of rice and millet — which is the heart of the *kitniyos* issue. The shiur considered whether oats belong among those five grains, a question with particular relevance for those with celiac disease and gluten concerns. Central to that discussion was the precise meaning of *machmitz*: even rice ferments to some degree, and so the term must refer specifically to the release of gases and the puffing up of dough, a process that does not occur to any comparable degree in rice or similar grains.

The discussion also touched on *mei peiros* — fruit juices — and whether they cause *chametz*. Two opinions were considered: one holding that *mei peiros* produces no *chametz* at all, and another holding that it produces an intermediate category called *chametz nuksha*, which does not carry liability for *kares* but is nonetheless prohibited. The dominant position among the *Rishonim*, as codified in the *Shulchan Aruch*, is that *mei peiros* without the addition of water presents no problem, while the addition of water can render the mixture *chametz*. The *Rema*, however, takes the more stringent view that even pure fruit juices are problematic — except in cases of illness or for children, which is why egg matzah is certified *kosher l'Pesach* for such individuals. One important clarification: the *Rema*'s stringency applies only when the dough is left unattended. The classic rule is that eighteen minutes is the limit only for unattended dough; as long as one is actively working the dough, there is no fixed time constraint. Thus, if the dough is kneaded with fruit juices and placed in the oven immediately, the *Rema* has no additional stringency beyond what he holds with water.

### Mishnah Overview

The remainder of the previous daf addressed the *Mishnah*'s distinction between *matzah* that is halachically valid and *matzah* that, while edible in ordinary circumstances, carries some other legal problem. *Matzah* of *demai*, *terumah*, or *ma'aser* from which *terumah* has already been taken — these fall into a gray area. But *matzah* of genuine *tevel*, or *ma'aser* that has not yet been redeemed, cannot be eaten. The question the Gemara now turns to is a sharper one: granted that eating such *matzah* is forbidden — but why should that mean one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* as well? That is the inquiry that opens Daf 36.

## Tevel & Terumos

### Tevel Definition

The Gemara picks up at the bottom of 35b and cites a *Tanu Rabbanan*: one might think a person fulfills the obligation of *matzah* with *tevel* that has not been fixed — *tevel shelo nitkan*. The Gemara immediately notes that this phrasing is redundant: by definition, all *tevel* has not been fixed. The *Baraita* therefore must be speaking of *tevel shelo nitkan kol tzorcho* — grain that has not been fully rectified. Several graduated cases are enumerated. One took *terumah gedolah* from the grain but did not take *terumas ma'aser*. Or one took *maaser rishon*, which carries within it the *terumas ma'aser*, so that only *maaser sheni* remains outstanding. Or the year is the third or sixth year and the only thing not yet separated is *maaser ani*.

The gradation matters for the following reason. When *terumah* or *terumas ma'aser* has not been taken, the grain contains within it something that, if consumed by a non-*kohen*, carries liability for *misah bidei shamayim*. The severity of the *tevel* status is, one might suppose, a function of the severity of what has not yet been extracted. But when the only thing remaining is *maaser ani*, the prohibition is not that of a forbidden food at all — it is a form of *gezel*, theft from the poor. One might therefore reason that eating such grain is no worse than a monetary transgression, not a food prohibition, and that the *tevel* status has effectively lapsed. The *Baraita* rejects this: even if the only thing not separated is *maaser ani*, the *tevel* status remains and one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah*. Whether the severity is equivalent — whether eating this attenuated form of *tevel* still carries *misah bidei shamayim* — is a separate question left open.

### Tevel Source

The *Baraita* derives this from the verse: *lo sochel alav chametz* — do not eat *chametz* on it — followed by *shivas yamim tochal alav matzos*. The juxtaposition of *matzah* to the prohibition of *chametz* has been established previously: it tells us that *matzah* can only be made from things that are capable of becoming *chametz*, i.e., the five *minim*. But the verse is now extended further: one fulfills the *mitzvah* of *matzah* only with something whose sole potential prohibition is that of *chametz* — *mi she-issuro mishum bal tochal chametz*. The case of *tevel* is thereby excluded: *she-ein issuro mishum bal tochal chametz ella mishum bal tochal tevel* — its prohibition is not from eating *chametz* but from eating *tevel*. The underlying logic is that *matzah* of *tevel*, even if left to ferment, would never result in a prohibition of *chametz*, because the prevailing prohibition is that of *tevel*.

But this raises an obvious difficulty: why shouldn't the *chametz* prohibition also apply? Even if the grain is *tevel*, if it ferments it seems one should transgress *chametz* as well.

### Ain Issur Chal

The Gemara answers: *ha-mani Rabbi Shimon hi* — this entire teaching follows the position of Rabbi Shimon, who holds *ein issur chal al issur*: one prohibition cannot take effect upon an object that already carries another prohibition. Since the grain already carries the prohibition of *tevel*, the *chametz* prohibition cannot attach to it, even if it ferments. Thus, *matzah* of *tevel* can never become prohibited on account of *chametz*, and is therefore excluded from fulfilling the *mitzvah*.

The paradigmatic illustration of Rabbi Shimon's principle is the *Baraita* cited: if one eats a *neveilah* on *Yom HaKippurim*, Rabbi Shimon says one is *patur* for the *Yom HaKippurim* violation, because the animal was already forbidden as *neveilah* before *Yom HaKippurim* arrived. This is a striking example, not only because it means one could eat *treifas* on *Yom HaKippurim* without incurring the additional *Yom HaKippurim* liability, but also because the conceptual structure is somewhat unusual: the prohibition of *Yom HaKippurim* does not attach to the meat itself in the way that *tevel* attaches to grain. Rather, it is a personal obligation to fast. Nonetheless, Rabbi Shimon's position is consistent: once an object carries one prohibited status, a second prohibition does not take effect.

One might ask whether *chametz* should be considered an *issur mosif* — an additional prohibition that adds something the prior prohibition did not — since *chametz* carries *issur hana'ah* while *tevel* does not prohibit all forms of benefit equally. Whether *issur hana'ah* constitutes an *issur mosif* in this context is genuinely questionable, and the Gemara proceeds on the assumption that it does not.

The conclusion, then, is that the entire *Mishnah*'s ruling — that one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* with *matzah* that is forbidden for another reason — reflects the position of Rabbi Shimon alone. According to the *Chachamim*, who hold that one prohibition *can* take effect atop another, the *chametz* prohibition would indeed attach even to *tevel*, and one would presumably fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* even with such bread.

This is a remarkable conclusion: the *Mishnah* as a whole would not be the normative *halacha*. A number of *Rishonim*, while acknowledging that this appears to be the Gemara's conclusion, nevertheless rule otherwise — that even according to the *Chachamim* one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* with forbidden *matzah*. One compelling reason, raised by *Rashi* in multiple places but notably absent from the Gemara itself, is the principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* — a *mitzvah* fulfilled through the commission of a transgression. The *Rishonim* grapple with why this consideration is entirely absent from the Gemara's discussion, and several attempt to narrow the definition of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* to account for this omission. *Rashi* nonetheless insists that it is operative here as well, and several *Rishonim* conclude that the *Mishnah* holds even according to the *Chachamim*, with *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* as a possible underlying rationale.

One distinction worth noting: the classic case of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* — a stolen *esrog* — involves an object upon which a transgression was previously committed, which then becomes, as it were, stigmatized. The Gemara in *Sukkah* compares such an object to one with a *mum*, a blemish. In the case of *tevel matzah*, however, the transgression and the *mitzvah* occur simultaneously — at the moment of eating. The object has not had a transgression previously performed upon it. This temporal distinction is cited by several *Rishonim* as a reason to question whether *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* applies in precisely the same way here.

### Ravina's View

Ravina offers an alternative reading of the *drasha* that avoids limiting the *Mishnah* to Rabbi Shimon entirely. According to Ravina, the verse means: one fulfills the *mitzvah* of *matzah* only with something whose *sole* additional potential prohibition is *chametz* — *mi she-issuro mishum bal tochal chametz bilvad*. The case of *tevel* is excluded not because it lacks the *chametz* prohibition, but because it carries an *additional* prohibition of *tevel* alongside *chametz*. The *drasha* thus operates even according to the *Chachamim*: any grain that carries another prohibition beyond *chametz* cannot be used for *matzah*.

The Gemara, however, is dissatisfied: *mi-di bilvad ktiv* — does the verse actually say "only"? The verse speaks of things that can become prohibited as *chametz*; it does not say the *chametz* prohibition must be the *sole* prohibition. The Gemara therefore concludes: *ela mivarta k'Rav Sheshet* — the better reading is that of Rav Sheshet, and the *Mishnah* does indeed follow Rabbi Shimon.

That said, as noted, the *Rishonim* do not simply accept that the entire *Mishnah* is unrepresentative of normative *halacha*, and the principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* remains a serious and independently grounded reason why one cannot fulfill the *mitzvah* with *matzah* that was forbidden.

## Fruit Juices & Additives

### Lechem Oni Drasha

The Gemara introduces another *Tanu Rabbanan*: could one fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* with *maaser sheni* in *Yerushalayim*? One is permitted — indeed obligated — to eat *maaser sheni* in *Yerushalayim*; why not use it for *matzah*? The verse teaches: *lechem oni* — "bread of affliction." Reading with a play on the word *oni*, repointing it as *avon* (related to *aninut*), one learns that valid *matzah* must be the kind that can be eaten in a state of *aninut* — by one who has just lost a close relative and not yet buried them, the *onen*. The case of *maaser sheni* is then excluded: *shene'eno ne'echal b'aninut* — *maaser sheni* cannot be eaten by an *onen*, since the verse explicitly states *lo achalti b'oni mimenu*. The play on words *b'oni* / *b'avon* is deliberate. This is the position of Rabbi Yose HaGalili.

There is a deeper thematic dimension here: *matzah*, symbolizing affliction, cannot be composed of *maaser sheni*, which is associated entirely with *simcha* — the joy of eating before God in *Yerushalayim*. The contrast is pointed: the *matzah* of the *Seder* is meant to evoke profound humility and sorrow, while *maaser sheni* is eaten in a spirit of festival rejoicing.

### Akiva: Fruit OK

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He reads *matzot matzot* — the repetition of the word *matzot* in the Torah — as an inclusive *ribui* that specifically permits the use of *maaser sheni* in *Yerushalayim* for the *matzah mitzvah*. If so, what work does *lechem oni* do for Rabbi Akiva? In all likelihood, it excludes dough kneaded with wine, oil, and honey — enriched bread that is too flavored and sweet to qualify as "poor man's bread" for the purpose of the *mitzvah*.

The dispute then turns on how to read the word itself. Rabbi Akiva asks: *mi ksiv lechem oni*? The word is written as *ani* — without the *vav* — which means simply "poor person's bread." That reading excludes sweetened, enriched *matzah*. Rabbi Yose HaGalili, by contrast, reads it as *oni* with the *vav*, connecting it to *aninut*, and uses this to exclude *maaser sheni*. Rabbi Akiva counters that we do read it as *oni* — but, following Shmuel's teaching, *lechem she-onin alav devarim harbeh*: bread over which one recites many things, i.e., the *Haggadah* and the *Hallel*. The *matzah* of the *Seder* must be embedded within the narrative context of the *Haggadah*; that is what *oni* teaches. It does not exclude *maaser sheni* through a comparison to *aninut*.

It is worth noting *Rashi*'s comment on *she-onin alav devarim harbeh*: *Rashi* states *she-gomrin alav es ha-Hallel v'omrim alav Haggadah* — one completes the *Hallel* over it and recites the *Haggadah* over it. Notably, *Rashi* mentions *Hallel* first, which may seem surprising to us. Yet early records from the *Bayis Sheni* period — in Philo and Josephus and similar sources — emphasize above all that on *Pesach* night the people ate the *Korban Pesach* and sang songs of praise to God. The story-recounting aspect, while certainly present, was not always the primary emphasis. *Rashi*'s formulation echoes something of that earlier sensibility.

### Kosher vs. Seder

The Gemara resolves the apparent tension in Rabbi Akiva's position. He says one may knead dough with fruit juices — and indeed attests that he did so in the presence of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, who raised no objection. Yet he also seems to say that *matzah* kneaded with wine, oil, and honey does not fulfill the *mitzvah*. Is this a contradiction?

The Gemara answers: *lo kashya* — it is not difficult. These are two entirely separate questions. *Yom Tov rishon* — the Seder night — requires *lechem oni*, which cannot be enriched or sweetened. But for the remaining days of Pesach (*yom tov sheni* here meaning the other days of the festival, not specifically the second *Yom Tov* of the diaspora), such *matzah* is perfectly valid. Fruit-juice *matzah* is not *chametz* — it may be eaten throughout *Pesach* — but it does not fulfill the specific obligation of the first night's *mitzvah* of *matzah* because it is not *lechem oni*.

Thus both aspects of *ani* / *oni* apply specifically to the Seder night. The word *ani* without the *vav* teaches that the *Seder* night *matzah* must be plain and unadorned. The word *oni* with the *vav*, as read by Rabbi Akiva following Shmuel, teaches that the *matzah* must be accompanied by *devarim harbeh* — the *Haggadah* and *Hallel*. Both requirements pertain only to the first night. The other days of *Pesach* have no such restriction on what may constitute kosher, non-*chametz* bread.

### Milk Exception

The Gemara illustrates this distinction with a teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua to his son: on the first night of *Pesach*, do not knead the dough with milk; thereafter, you may knead it with milk. The implication is clear — *matzah* kneaded with milk is not *chametz* and is permissible on the other days of *Pesach*, but does not fulfill the *Seder* night *mitzvah* because it is not *lechem oni*.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: is it not taught in a *Baraita* that one may never knead dough with milk, and that if one did so, the entire bread is forbidden? This is because bread is eaten with everything, and *milchig* bread will inevitably lead one to eat it together with meat. This is the principle of *mipnei hergel aveira*. If *milchig matzah* is categorically forbidden, how could Rabbi Yehoshua permit it after the first night?

The Gemara offers two resolutions. First, perhaps he was speaking of honey rather than milk: do not knead with honey on the first night; thereafter, do so. Second, *ve-iba'is eima*, one may indeed maintain that he was speaking of milk, in accordance with Ravina's ruling: *milchig* bread is permitted when made *ke-ein tura*. *Rashi* understands this phrase to mean small, bite-sized loaves — each portion is consumed in a single sitting, eliminating any concern of mixing with meat afterward. Others read *ke-ein tura* as meaning that the loaves are shaped distinctively, like an ox, so that they are immediately recognizable as *milchig* — a visible *siman* that prevents confusion. The Gemara concludes that this applies here as well: Rabbi Yehoshua was permitting *milchig matzah* when produced in small individualized portions that would be entirely consumed without remainder. The broader principle is established: the first night of *Pesach* imposes heightened criteria for the *matzah* itself, above and beyond what is merely required to avoid *chametz*.

### Lukewarm Water

The *Mishnah* concludes with a point on which all opinions agree: one may not knead *matzah* dough with *poshrin* — lukewarm water — since warmth accelerates fermentation. This consensus is then compared to the laws of *minchos* in the *Beis Hamikdash*, since *minchos*, like *matzah*, are subject to the prohibition of *chametz* and must be made as *matzos*.

## Minchos & Sisah

### Kneading vs. Sisah

The Gemara asks: *mai sh'na mi-minchos* — how is the rule about lukewarm water different from the law of *minchos*? The *Mishnah* in *Menachos* explicitly teaches that *all minchos are kneaded with lukewarm water* — and one watches them so that they should not become *chametz*. The *Mishnah* in *Menachos* thus acknowledges the risk of lukewarm water causing *chametz*, yet permits its use provided proper vigilance is maintained. The standard method of vigilance is continuous kneading: as long as the dough is being actively worked, fermentation is prevented.

Why, then, is this not the rule for ordinary *matzah* baking at home? The Gemara answers: *im amru bi-zerizin yomru b'she-ein zerizin* — just because we are lenient for the meticulous, highly trained priests in the *Beis Hamikdash*, we cannot extend that leniency to every householder. Not everyone will maintain the necessary vigilance. The *Kohanim* in the *Beis Hamikdash* are entirely focused on this work; the general population baking at home cannot be assumed to exercise comparable care.

The Gemara then asks: if that is the reasoning, why do we not similarly permit *sisah* — the practice of soaking grain in water to soften the husk and facilitate the removal of bran, thereby producing fine white flour — in the *Beis Hamikdash* as well? This process was not done for *minchah* flour, since contact with water at that early stage risked *chametz*, but the argument of *zerizin* should apply.

The Gemara cites a teaching of Shmuel transmitted through Rav and Rabbah bar Yirmiyah: the wheat used for *minchos* may not undergo *sisah*. Why not? The distinction is precise: *lisha bi-zerizin isa* — the kneading of dough is done by *zerizin*, either by the *Kohanim* themselves or, at minimum, within the *Beis Hamikdash*. But *sisah*, the soaking to remove bran, is *lav bi-zerizin isa* — it is not done by those same meticulous individuals. A person could bring finished flour to the *Beis Hamikdash* and the *Kohanim* would take it from there. The preparation of fine flour — including *sisah* — was done at home, by laypeople, outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. We cannot assume that everyone processing grain at home will be sufficiently careful.

The Gemara questions whether kneading is truly performed only by *Kohanim*, noting that a verse in the Torah describes the *minchah* preparation — the pouring of oil and the mixing — as being done before the *kohen* is involved: *mi-kemitza va-eilach mitzvas kehunah* — only from the *kemitza* onward is priestly participation required. The pouring of oil, the *belila*, the kneading, the baking — all of these are valid when performed by a non-*kohen*.

The Gemara responds: *nehidu bi-zerizin leisa* — it is true that the kneading need not be done by *Kohanim* specifically. Nevertheless, *bemakom zerizin isa* — it is done in the *Beis Hamikdash* itself. Processing grain into fine flour happens at home; kneading the dough happens inside the *Beis Hamikdash*. And regarding the *belila*: while the *belila* may technically be valid when performed by a non-*kohen*, it is invalid if performed outside the walls of the Temple precinct — *chutz le-chomas ha-azarah pesulah*. *Rashi* and *Tosafos* both note, independently, that the kneading must in any case be done inside the *Beis Hamikdash* because the dough must immediately be placed in a *kli shares*, a sacred vessel, which is only found within the Temple. *Tosafos* further adds that the dough must go directly from kneading into the oven; it cannot be kneaded at home and transported, as it would become *chametz* in transit. For whatever precise reason, the Gemara's conclusion stands: kneading is done within the *Beis Hamikdash* and thus benefits from the atmosphere of *zerizus* that prevails there; *sisah* is done outside and therefore may not be performed even for *minchos*.

### Minchas Omer

The Gemara raises one further case. The *Minchas HaOmer* — the first barley offering, the *omer* — did in fact undergo *sisah*, the soaking to remove bran. How does this square with the general prohibition on *sisah* for *minchos*?

The Gemara answers: *tsibur shani* — a communal offering is different. The *Minchas HaOmer* is a *korban tsibur*, a communal sacrifice. Because it is centrally organized and supervised by those responsible for the entire community's offering, one may assume that the process will be carried out with complete vigilance, even at the earlier stages of grain preparation. There is no individual householder who might be careless. A single coordinated authority oversees the entire process from beginning to end, and the concern that motivates the general prohibition — that some individual will not be careful enough — simply does not apply.

The Gemara notes an elegant verbal echo: the word *tsover osah* — "you pile it up" — which describes a step in the *sisah* process, sounds similar to *tsibur*, the community. Some read *tsibur shani* as meaning the *pile* makes a difference, not the communal nature. *Rashi* firmly rejects that reading, insisting that *tsibur* refers unambiguously to the communal character of the sacrifice.

## Ma'aser & Bikkurim

### Yose: Ma'aser OK

The Gemara opens a new *Tanu Rabbanan*: one might think a person fulfills the obligation of *matzah* with *bikkurim* in *Yerushalayim*. The comparison to *maaser sheni* is natural — both are eaten in *Yerushalayim*, both have a festive character. Can one use *bikkurim* flour for the Seder night *matzah*?

The verse teaches: *be-chol moshvoseichem tochlu matzos* — "in all your dwelling places you shall eat *matzos*." This phrase establishes a criterion: valid *matzah* must be the kind that *can* be eaten in all dwelling places. The case of *bikkurim* is thereby excluded — *she-einan nekhalin be-chol moshvoseichem ela bi-Yerushalayim* — *bikkurim* can only be eaten within *Yerushalayim* and nowhere else. Even if one is presently in *Yerushalayim*, the *matzah* must in principle be capable of being eaten anywhere. This is the position of Rabbi Yose HaGalili.

There is something conceptually striking here. The paradigmatic setting for eating *matzah* in the Torah era was *Yerushalayim* — the entire nation would be in *Yerushalayim* for *Pesach*, eating the *Korban Pesach* together with *matzah*. Yet the *halacha* is that valid *matzah* must have the potential to be eaten outside of *Yerushalayim*. The universal accessibility of *matzah* is a halachic requirement even in a context where the actual eating was entirely localized.

### Akiva: Both?

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He derives from the juxtaposition of *matzah* and *maror* — *al matzos u-m'rorim* — that just as *maror* cannot be *bikkurim* (since the *shivas haminim* do not include the bitter herbs used for *maror*), so too *matzah* cannot be *bikkurim*. He agrees with the practical *halacha* — one cannot use *bikkurim* — but learns it from a different verse.

The Gemara challenges Rabbi Akiva's *drasha*. If the comparison to *maror* is taken strictly, it would imply not merely that *matzah* cannot happen to be *bikkurim*, but that *matzah* must be made from species that *cannot even in principle* become *bikkurim*. This would exclude wheat and barley from use for *matzah* entirely — since wheat and barley are among the *shivas haminim* and are precisely the species from which *bikkurim* are brought. One would be left using only the other three of the five *minim*. Rabbi Akiva invokes *matzos matzos riba* to prevent this overly restrictive reading. But the Gemara then turns the argument against him: if the repetition of *matzos* is inclusive, why does it not include *bikkurim* themselves?

### Redemption Logic

The Gemara concludes: *hadar beih Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva reversed himself, not in the practical ruling, but in the *drasha* from which it is derived. He ultimately accepted the *drasha* of Rabbi Yose HaGalili — *be-chol moshvoseichem* — as the source. A *Baraita* confirms this explicitly, giving the verse *be-chol moshvoseichem tochlu matzos* as the source for excluding *bikkurim*, in the name of a ruling that allows *maaser sheni* — which we know to be Rabbi Akiva's position.

The question then becomes: why does Rabbi Akiva include *maaser sheni* but exclude *bikkurim*, given that both can only be eaten in *Yerushalayim*? The answer is a crucial distinction: *maaser sheni* has a path to being eaten outside *Yerushalayim* — it can be redeemed. Under ordinary circumstances, once *maaser sheni* is brought into *Yerushalayim* it cannot be redeemed. But there is an exception: if *maaser sheni* becomes *tamei*, the verse says *ki lo tuchal se'eto* — "if you cannot carry it up" — and *Rashi* reads *se'et* through a play on words, connecting it to a verse about Yosef's gifts to his brothers where *mas'ot* refers to food portions. If you cannot eat it, you may redeem it. Therefore, even *maaser sheni* that has already entered *Yerushalayim* can theoretically be redeemed and carried out if it becomes *tamei*. This theoretical accessibility means *maaser sheni* qualifies under the criterion of *be-chol moshvoseichem*. *Bikkurim*, by contrast, have no such release mechanism; they can never be eaten outside *Yerushalayim* under any circumstances, and are therefore excluded.

### Yose Like Shimon

The Gemara then asks why Rabbi Yose HaGalili needed the *drasha* of *be-chol moshvoseichem* to exclude *bikkurim* at all. He already excludes *maaser sheni* on the grounds that it cannot be eaten *b'aninus* — invoking *lechem oni*. The same consideration — that *bikkurim* are also eaten *b'simcha* and not by an *onen* — should equally exclude *bikkurim*. Why does he need an additional verse?

The Gemara answers: *savar lah ke-Rabbi Shimon* — Rabbi Yose HaGalili holds in accordance with Rabbi Shimon's view on *bikkurim* and *onenin*.

### Onen & Bikkurim

A *Baraita* records the dispute directly: the majority view holds that *bikkurim* may not be eaten by an *onen*, linking *bikkurim* to *maaser sheni* through the verse *lo suchal le-echol bi-sharecha* — which enumerates things that must be eaten in *Yerushalayim*, mentioning *maaser degancha* (i.e., *maaser sheni*) and *trumas yadecha* (identified as *bikkurim*) together. Since *maaser sheni* cannot be eaten by an *onen*, neither can *bikkurim*, because the two are linked by the verse.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees. He reads the phrase *trumas yadecha* — the gift of your hands — as identifying *bikkurim* with the category of *terumah*. Just as *terumah*, though sacred, can be eaten by an *onen* (since *terumah* is not eaten in *Yerushalayim* but anywhere among *kohanim*, and the *onen*-restriction is associated with the Temple's sphere of sanctity rather than with holiness per se), so too *bikkurim* can be eaten by an *onen*.

Rabbi Shimon's position requires a response to the clear association of *bikkurim* with *simcha* — the verse says *ve-samachta be-chol ha-tov* — "and you shall rejoice in all the good." How can an *onen*, who is in acute mourning, eat something tied to *simcha*? The Gemara answers: *hahu l'zman simcha hu d'ata* — that verse is not establishing a legal condition for the eating of *bikkurim*; it is describing the time of year in which *bikkurim* are brought.

### Bikkurim Season

The *Mishnah* in *Bikkurim* specifies: from *Shavuos* until *Sukkos* — the harvest season — one brings *bikkurim* and recites the *mikra bikkurim* declaration (*ve-ata heiveiti et reishit pri ha-adama*). This is the true *zman simcha*, the time of joyous harvest. After *Sukkos* and until *Chanukah*, one may still bring *bikkurim* but no longer recites the declaration, because the season of harvest joy has passed and a different season has begun. The reference to *Chanukah* as the terminal date is a matter of the seasonal calendar, not a reference to the holiday itself; the question of whether this outer limit is rabbinic in character is noted.

The reason *bikkurim* begin only after *Shavuos* is that *Shavuos* marks the offering of the *shtei ha-lechem* — the two loaves — which is itself called *bikkurim*, serving as the first Temple-offering of the new produce. Only after this inaugural offering may the new produce be brought as individual *bikkurim*. The period thus runs from after *Shavuos* through *Sukkos*, when the final ingathering is complete, and *Sukkos* itself stands as the apex of that harvest joy — *zman simchateinu*.

Thus the verse *ve-samachta be-chol ha-tov* describes the setting in which *bikkurim* are brought — the season of rejoicing — but does not create a legal barrier preventing an *onen* from eating them. According to Rabbi Shimon, *bikkurim* are halachically identified as *terumah*, and the *onen* restriction does not apply. It follows that Rabbi Yose HaGalili, who aligns with Rabbi Shimon on this point, cannot use *lechem oni* to exclude *bikkurim* from the *matzah mitzvah* — since, in his view, *bikkurim* may be eaten by an *onen* and therefore do not conflict with the criterion of *matzah* eaten *b'aninus*. He must instead rely on the separate *drasha* of *be-chol moshvoseichem* to make the exclusion.

The larger thematic irony remains. *Matzah* on *Seder* night is *lechem oni* — the bread of affliction, poverty, and sorrow. *Bikkurim* are the paradigm of joyful harvest, *simcha*, and gratitude. That these two should be in such sharp halachic tension — that the most exuberant expression of agricultural thanksgiving cannot serve as the vehicle for the most solemn expression of historical memory — is itself a telling commentary on the layered meanings the Torah encodes in a single shared food.
