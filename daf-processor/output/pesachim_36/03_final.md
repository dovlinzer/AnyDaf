# Pesachim 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review & Overview

### Prior Topics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵי פֵירוֹת אֵין מַחְמִיצִין.
>
> **Translation:** **and fruit juice does not** cause dough to be **leavened.** Therefore, dough prepared with these liquids is not considered full-fledged leavened bread.

The previous daf was devoted to the question of what is necessary to produce *matzah* and what causes *chametz*, focusing on the five grains — to the exclusion of rice and millet — which is the heart of the *kitniyos* issue. The shiur considered whether oats belong among those five grains, a question with particular relevance for those with celiac disease and gluten concerns. Central to that discussion was the precise meaning of *machmitz*: even rice ferments to some degree, and so the term must refer specifically to the release of gases and the puffing up of dough, a process that does not occur to any comparable degree in rice or similar grains.

The discussion also touched on *mei peiros* — fruit juices — and whether they cause *chametz*. Two opinions were considered: one holding that *mei peiros* produces no *chametz* at all, and another holding that it produces an intermediate category called *chametz nuksha*, which does not carry liability for *kares* but is nonetheless prohibited. The dominant position among the *Rishonim*, as codified in the *Shulchan Aruch*, is that *mei peiros* without the addition of water presents no problem, while the addition of water can render the mixture *chametz*. The *Rema*, however, takes the more stringent view that even pure fruit juices are problematic — except in cases of illness or for children, which is why egg matzah is certified *kosher l'Pesach* for such individuals. One important clarification: the *Rema*'s stringency applies only when the dough is left unattended. The classic rule is that eighteen minutes is the limit only for unattended dough; as long as one is actively working the dough, there is no fixed time constraint. Thus, if the dough is kneaded with fruit juices and placed in the oven immediately, the *Rema* has no additional stringency beyond what he holds with water.

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹצֵא בִּדְמַאי וּבְמַעֲשֵׂר וְכוּ׳. דְּמַאי הָא לָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ! כֵּיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵי מַפְקַר לְנִכְסֵיהּ הָוֵי עָנִי וְאוֹכֵל דְּמַאי — הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי חֲזֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara turns to the cases of the mishna. The mishna taught that one can **fulfill** his obligation to eat *matza* **with doubtfully tithed produce and** with produce **of** the **first tithe,** from which *teruma* has been separated. The Gemara asks: Can one really fulfill his obligation with **doubtfully tithed produce?** It is **not suitable for him** to eat. The Sages prohibited the eating of doubtfully tithed produce that has not been tithed. The Gemara answers: **Since, if one** so **desires** he may **renounce** all **his property,** thereby becoming a poor person who is permitted to eat doubtfully tithed produce, **now too it is suitable for him,** even though he did not declare his property ownerless. Consequently, if he used doubtfully tithed produce as *matza*, he has fulfilled the mitzva after the fact.

The remainder of the previous daf addressed the *Mishnah*'s distinction between *matzah* that is halachically valid and *matzah* that, while edible in ordinary circumstances, carries some other legal problem. *Matzah* of *demai*, *terumah*, or *ma'aser* from which *terumah* has already been taken — these fall into a gray area. But *matzah* of genuine *tevel*, or *ma'aser* that has not yet been redeemed, cannot be eaten. The question the Gemara now turns to is a sharper one: granted that eating such *matzah* is forbidden — but why should that mean one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* as well? That is the inquiry that opens Daf 36.

## Tevel & Terumos

### Tevel Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָכוֹל יוֹצֵא אָדָם יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּטֶבֶל שֶׁלֹּא נִתְקַן. כׇּל טֶבֶל נָמֵי הָא לֹא נִתְקַן! אֶלָּא: בְּטֶבֶל שֶׁלֹּא נִתְקַן כׇּל צוֹרְכּוֹ. שֶׁנִּטְּלָה מִמֶּנּוּ תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה וְלֹא נִטְּלָה מִמֶּנּוּ תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר, [מַעֲשֵׂר] רִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, וַאֲפִילּוּ מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** I **might** have thought that **a person fulfills his obligation** to eat *matza* **with untithed produce that was not amended** with regard to tithes. The Gemara analyzes the apparently redundant phrase **untithed produce that was not amended.** But isn't **all untithed produce also not amended,** by definition? **Rather,** this *baraita* is referring to **untithed produce that was incompletely amended.** How so? For example, produce **from which *teruma gedola* was taken, but** the ***teruma* of the tithe was not taken from** it. Alternatively, the **first tithe** was separated from the produce, **but not** the **second tithe; or,** it is referring even to grain from which all the tithes were separated, apart from the **poor man's tithe.** Although no sanctity applies to the poor man's tithe, which is simply a monetary gift to the poor, until this tithe has been separated the grain remains untithed. **From where** is it derived that these types of grain cannot be used for *matza*?

The Gemara picks up at the bottom of 35b and cites a *Tanu Rabbanan*: one might think a person fulfills the obligation of *matzah* with *tevel* that has not been fixed — *tevel shelo nitkan*. The Gemara immediately notes that this phrasing is redundant: by definition, all *tevel* has not been fixed. The *Baraita* therefore must be speaking of *tevel shelo nitkan kol tzorcho* — grain that has not been fully rectified. Several graduated cases are enumerated. One took *terumah gedolah* from the grain but did not take *terumas ma'aser*. Or one took *maaser rishon*, which carries within it the *terumas ma'aser*, so that only *maaser sheni* remains outstanding. Or the year is the third or sixth year and the only thing not yet separated is *maaser ani*.

The gradation matters for the following reason. When *terumah* or *terumas ma'aser* has not been taken, the grain contains within it something that, if consumed by a non-*kohen*, carries liability for *misah bidei shamayim*. The severity of the *tevel* status is, one might suppose, a function of the severity of what has not yet been extracted. But when the only thing remaining is *maaser ani*, the prohibition is not that of a forbidden food at all — it is a form of *gezel*, theft from the poor. One might therefore reason that eating such grain is no worse than a monetary transgression, not a food prohibition, and that the *tevel* status has effectively lapsed. The *Baraita* rejects this: even if the only thing not separated is *maaser ani*, the *tevel* status remains and one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah*. Whether the severity is equivalent — whether eating this attenuated form of *tevel* still carries *misah bidei shamayim* — is a separate question left open.

### Tevel Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תֹאכַל עָלָיו חָמֵץ״ — מִי שֶׁאִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תֹּאכַל עָלָיו חָמֵץ״, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין אִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תֹּאכַל חָמֵץ״, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תֹּאכַל טֶבֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states: "You shall not eat leavened bread with it;** seven days you shall eat with it *matza*" (Deuteronomy 16:3). One fulfills his obligation to eat *matza* **with** food **whose prohibition** is solely **due to** the prohibition: **Do not eat leavened bread with it,** if it was not preserved in an unleavened state. This command **excludes this** grain, **which is not prohibited due to** the prohibition: **Do not eat leavened bread, but** rather **due to** the prohibition: **Do not eat untithed produce.**

The *Baraita* derives this from the verse: *lo sochel alav chametz* — do not eat *chametz* on it — followed by *shivas yamim tochal alav matzos*. The juxtaposition of *matzah* to the prohibition of *chametz* has been established previously: it tells us that *matzah* can only be made from things that are capable of becoming *chametz*, i.e., the five *minim*. But the verse is now extended further: one fulfills the *mitzvah* of *matzah* only with something whose sole potential prohibition is that of *chametz* — *mi she-issuro mishum bal tochal chametz*. The case of *tevel* is thereby excluded: *she-ein issuro mishum bal tochal chametz ella mishum bal tochal tevel* — its prohibition is not from eating *chametz* but from eating *tevel*. The underlying logic is that *matzah* of *tevel*, even if left to ferment, would never result in a prohibition of *chametz*, because the prevailing prohibition is that of *tevel*.

But this raises an obvious difficulty: why shouldn't the *chametz* prohibition also apply? Even if the grain is *tevel*, if it ferments it seems one should transgress *chametz* as well.

### Ain Issur Chal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיסּוּרָא דְחָמֵץ לְהֵיכָן אֲזַלָא? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָא מַנִּי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הָאוֹכֵל נְבֵלָה בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at this *baraita*. **And the prohibition of leavened bread, where did it go?** In other words, doesn't the prohibition: "You shall not eat leavened bread" apply to untithed produce as well? **Rav Sheshet said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said: One prohibition does not take effect where another prohibition** already exists. No additional prohibitions can apply to an object that is already prohibited, e.g., untithed grain. Consequently, the prohibition of leavened bread does not take effect on untithed produce. **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: One who** unwittingly **eats** an unslaughtered **animal** carcass **on Yom Kippur is exempt** from bringing a sin-offering to atone for this consumption. The prohibition against eating on Yom Kippur does not apply to the already prohibited meat of an animal carcass, which means only the prohibition against eating an unslaughtered animal carcass is violated by this act.

The Gemara answers: *ha-mani Rabbi Shimon hi* — this entire teaching follows the position of Rabbi Shimon, who holds *ein issur chal al issur*: one prohibition cannot take effect upon an object that already carries another prohibition. Since the grain already carries the prohibition of *tevel*, the *chametz* prohibition cannot attach to it, even if it ferments. Thus, *matzah* of *tevel* can never become prohibited on account of *chametz*, and is therefore excluded from fulfilling the *mitzvah*.

The paradigmatic illustration of Rabbi Shimon's principle is the *Baraita* cited: if one eats a *neveilah* on *Yom HaKippurim*, Rabbi Shimon says one is *patur* for the *Yom HaKippurim* violation, because the animal was already forbidden as *neveilah* before *Yom HaKippurim* arrived. This is a striking example, not only because it means one could eat *treifas* on *Yom HaKippurim* without incurring the additional *Yom HaKippurim* liability, but also because the conceptual structure is somewhat unusual: the prohibition of *Yom HaKippurim* does not attach to the meat itself in the way that *tevel* attaches to grain. Rather, it is a personal obligation to fast. Nonetheless, Rabbi Shimon's position is consistent: once an object carries one prohibited status, a second prohibition does not take effect.

One might ask whether *chametz* should be considered an *issur mosif* — an additional prohibition that adds something the prior prohibition did not — since *chametz* carries *issur hana'ah* while *tevel* does not prohibit all forms of benefit equally. Whether *issur hana'ah* constitutes an *issur mosif* in this context is genuinely questionable, and the Gemara proceeds on the assumption that it does not.

The conclusion, then, is that the entire *Mishnah*'s ruling — that one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* with *matzah* that is forbidden for another reason — reflects the position of Rabbi Shimon alone. According to the *Chachamim*, who hold that one prohibition *can* take effect atop another, the *chametz* prohibition would indeed attach even to *tevel*, and one would presumably fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* even with such bread.

This is a remarkable conclusion: the *Mishnah* as a whole would not be the normative *halacha*. A number of *Rishonim*, while acknowledging that this appears to be the Gemara's conclusion, nevertheless rule otherwise — that even according to the *Chachamim* one does not fulfill the *mitzvah* with forbidden *matzah*. One compelling reason, raised by *Rashi* in multiple places but notably absent from the Gemara itself, is the principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* — a *mitzvah* fulfilled through the commission of a transgression. The *Rishonim* grapple with why this consideration is entirely absent from the Gemara's discussion, and several attempt to narrow the definition of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* to account for this omission. *Rashi* nonetheless insists that it is operative here as well, and several *Rishonim* conclude that the *Mishnah* holds even according to the *Chachamim*, with *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* as a possible underlying rationale.

One distinction worth noting: the classic case of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* — a stolen *esrog* — involves an object upon which a transgression was previously committed, which then becomes, as it were, stigmatized. The Gemara in *Sukkah* compares such an object to one with a *mum*, a blemish. In the case of *tevel matzah*, however, the transgression and the *mitzvah* occur simultaneously — at the moment of eating. The object has not had a transgression previously performed upon it. This temporal distinction is cited by several *Rishonim* as a reason to question whether *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* applies in precisely the same way here.

### Ravina's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן: מִי שֶׁאִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תֹּאכַל חָמֵץ״ בִּלְבַד. יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין אִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תֹּאכַל חָמֵץ״ בִּלְבַד, אֶלָּא אַף מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תֹּאכַל טֶבֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Even** if **you say** that this *baraita* is in accordance with **the Rabbis,** who maintain that a prohibition can take effect where another prohibition already exists, it can be explained as follows: One fulfills his obligation to eat *matza* **with** food **whose prohibition** is **solely due to** the commandment: **Do not eat leavened bread,** which **excludes this** grain, **which is forbidden not only due to** the prohibition: **Do not eat leavened bread, but also due to** the prohibition: **Do not eat untithed produce.**

Ravina offers an alternative reading of the *drasha* that avoids limiting the *Mishnah* to Rabbi Shimon entirely. According to Ravina, the verse means: one fulfills the *mitzvah* of *matzah* only with something whose *sole* additional potential prohibition is *chametz* — *mi she-issuro mishum bal tochal chametz bilvad*. The case of *tevel* is excluded not because it lacks the *chametz* prohibition, but because it carries an *additional* prohibition of *tevel* alongside *chametz*. The *drasha* thus operates even according to the *Chachamim*: any grain that carries another prohibition beyond *chametz* cannot be used for *matzah*.

The Gemara, however, is dissatisfied:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי ״בִּלְבַד״ כְּתִיב? אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against this explanation: **Is** the word: **Only, written** in the *baraita*? This word, which is critical for Ravina's explanation, does not appear in the *baraita* at all. **Rather, it is clear** that this must be explained **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rav Sheshet,** who maintains that the *baraita* should be attributed to Rabbi Shimon.

The *baraita* speaks of things that can become prohibited as *chametz*; it does not say the *chametz* prohibition must be the *sole* prohibition. The Gemara therefore concludes: *ela mivarta k'Rav Sheshet* — the better reading is that of Rav Sheshet, and the *Mishnah* does indeed follow Rabbi Shimon.

That said, as noted, the *Rishonim* do not simply accept that the entire *Mishnah* is unrepresentative of normative *halacha*, and the principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* remains a serious and independently grounded reason why one cannot fulfill the *mitzvah* with *matzah* that was forbidden.

## Fruit Juices & Additives

### Lechem Oni Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָכוֹל יוֹצֵא אָדָם יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֶחֶם עוֹנִי״, מָה שֶׁנֶּאֱכָל בָּאֲנִינוּת. יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל בַּאֲנִינוּת, אֶלָּא בְּשִׂמְחָה — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** I **might** have thought that **a person can fulfill his obligation** to eat ***matza*** on Passover **with** *matza* of **second tithe in Jerusalem.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "You shall not eat leavened bread with it; seven days you shall eat with it *matza*, **the bread of affliction [*leḥem oni*]"** (Deuteronomy 16:3), *oni* with the letter *ayin*, i.e., poor man's bread. As this is similar to the phrase: Bread of acute mourning [*leḥem oni*], *oni* with an *alef*, it can be inferred that this mitzva must be fulfilled with *matza* **that** can **be eaten** during a period of **acute mourning,** on the day one's close relative has died. This **excludes this** second tithe, **which cannot be eaten during** a period of **acute mourning but only** in a state **of joy,** as the Torah states: "I have not eaten from it in my acute mourning" (Deuteronomy 26:14). This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.**

The Gemara introduces another *Tanu Rabbanan*: could one fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah* with *maaser sheni* in *Yerushalayim*? One is permitted — indeed obligated — to eat *maaser sheni* in *Yerushalayim*; why not use it for *matzah*? The verse teaches: *lechem oni* — "bread of affliction." Reading with a play on the word *oni*, repointing it as *avon* (related to *aninut*), one learns that valid *matzah* must be the kind that can be eaten in a state of *aninut* — by one who has just lost a close relative and not yet buried them, the *onen*. The case of *maaser sheni* is then excluded: *shene'eno ne'echal b'aninut* — *maaser sheni* cannot be eaten by an *onen*, since the verse explicitly states *lo achalti b'oni mimenu*. The play on words *b'oni* / *b'avon* is deliberate. This is the position of Rabbi Yose HaGalili.

There is a deeper thematic dimension here: *matzah*, symbolizing affliction, cannot be composed of *maaser sheni*, which is associated entirely with *simcha* — the joy of eating before God in *Yerushalayim*. The contrast is pointed: the *matzah* of the *Seder* is meant to evoke profound humility and sorrow, while *maaser sheni* is eaten in a spirit of festival rejoicing.

### Akiva: Fruit OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״מַצּוֹת״ ״מַצּוֹת״ רִיבָּה. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֶחֶם עוֹנִי״? פְּרָט לְעִיסָּה שֶׁנִּילּוֹשָׁה בְּיַיִן וְשֶׁמֶן וּדְבַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** The repetition of ***matzot matzot*** serves to **amplify,** and teaches that all types of *matza* may be eaten on Passover. The *baraita* asks: **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states *leḥem oni*,** poor man's bread? The *baraita* answers: This phrase **excludes dough that was kneaded with wine, oil, or honey,** which is not classified as poor man's bread and therefore cannot be used for this mitzva.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He reads *matzot matzot* — the repetition of the word *matzot* in the Torah — as an inclusive *ribui* that specifically permits the use of *maaser sheni* in *Yerushalayim* for the *matzah mitzvah*. If so, what work does *lechem oni* do for Rabbi Akiva? In all likelihood, it excludes dough kneaded with wine, oil, and honey — enriched bread that is too flavored and sweet to qualify as "poor man's bread" for the purpose of the *mitzvah*.

The dispute then turns on how to read the word itself. Rabbi Akiva asks:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִי כְּתִיב ״לֶחֶם עוֹנִי״? ״עָנִי״ כְּתִיב. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: מִי קָרֵינַן ״עָנִי״! ״עוֹנִי״ קָרֵינַן. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: הַאי דְּקָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״עוֹנִי״, כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״לֶחֶם עוֹנִי״ — לֶחֶם שֶׁעוֹנִין עָלָיו דְּבָרִים הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of Rabbi Akiva?** The Gemara explains: **Is it written** in the consonantal text ***leḥem oni*** with a *vav*? That would allude to the comparison of *matza* to food eaten by an *onen*, an acute mourner, as *onen* is also spelled with a *vav*. Actually, **it is written *leḥem oni*** without a *vav*, meaning poor man's bread. **And Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** could respond: **Do we vocalize** the word as ***ani*,** as would be appropriate for a phrase meaning a poor man's bread? In fact, **we vocalize** it ***oni*,** which means oppression, affliction, or mourning. **And Rabbi Akiva** could retort: The fact **that we vocalize** the word **as *oni*** is **in accordance with** a statement of **Shmuel. As Shmuel said:** The expression ***leḥem oni*** means **bread over which many matters are recited [*onin*],** an allusion to the Passover Seder, at which one recites the Haggadah and eats *matza*.

*mi ksiv lechem oni*? The word is written as *ani* — without the *vav* — which means simply "poor person's bread." That reading excludes sweetened, enriched *matzah*. Rabbi Yose HaGalili, by contrast, reads it as *oni* with the *vav*, connecting it to *aninut*, and uses this to exclude *maaser sheni*. Rabbi Akiva counters that we do read it as *oni* — but, following Shmuel's teaching, *lechem she-onin alav devarim harbeh*: bread over which one recites many things, i.e., the *Haggadah* and the *Hallel*. The *matzah* of the *Seder* must be embedded within the narrative context of the *Haggadah*; that is what *oni* teaches. It does not exclude *maaser sheni* through a comparison to *aninut*.

It is worth noting *Rashi*'s comment on *she-onin alav devarim harbeh*: *Rashi* states *she-gomrin alav es ha-Hallel v'omrim alav Haggadah* — one completes the *Hallel* over it and recites the *Haggadah* over it. Notably, *Rashi* mentions *Hallel* first, which may seem surprising to us. Yet early records from the *Bayis Sheni* period — in Philo and Josephus and similar sources — emphasize above all that on *Pesach* night the people ate the *Korban Pesach* and sang songs of praise to God. The story-recounting aspect, while certainly present, was not always the primary emphasis. *Rashi*'s formulation echoes something of that earlier sensibility.

### Kosher vs. Seder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא עִיסָּה שֶׁנִּילּוֹשָׁה בְּיַיִן וְשֶׁמֶן וּדְבַשׁ לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין לָשִׁין עִיסָּה בַּפֶּסַח בְּיַיִן וְשֶׁמֶן וּדְבַשׁ. וְאִם לָשׁ, רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: תִּשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יֵאָכֵל. וְאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: שַׁבַּתִּי הָיְתָה אֵצֶל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְלַשְׁתִּי לָהֶם עִיסָּה בְּיַיִן וְשֶׁמֶן וּדְבַשׁ, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לִי דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rabbi Akiva maintain** with regard to **dough that was kneaded with wine, oil, or honey,** that it may **not** be used for *matza*? **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One** may **not knead dough on Passover with wine, oil, or honey? And if one kneaded** dough in this manner, **Rabban Gamliel says:** The dough must **be burned immediately,** as it is leavened faster than other types of dough. **And the Rabbis say** that although it is leavened quickly, one can still prevent it from being leavened, and if he does so it may **be eaten. And Rabbi Akiva said: It was my Shabbat** to serve **before Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua** during Passover (Rav Yehuda ben Rav Binyamin HaRofeh), **and I kneaded for them dough with wine, oil, and honey, and they said nothing to me** by way of objection.

The Gemara resolves the apparent tension in Rabbi Akiva's position. He says one may knead dough with fruit juices — and indeed attests that he did so in the presence of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, who raised no objection. Yet he also seems to say that *matzah* kneaded with wine, oil, and honey does not fulfill the *mitzvah*. Is this a contradiction?

The Gemara answers:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּיוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן. הָא — בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **this** statement of Rabbi Akiva's, which maintains that one does not fulfill his obligation with *matza* prepared with wine, oil, and honey, is referring to **the first day of the Festival,** during which there is a special commandment to eat *matza*. However, **that** second *baraita*, in which Rabbi Akiva states that he prepared this type of dough himself, pertains to **the second day** of Passover, when no special requirement to eat *matza* is in effect. On the second day, there is no mitzva to eat *matza*. It is only forbidden to own or eat leavened bread; consequently, so-called rich, or enhanced *matza*, prepared from fruit juices, is permitted.

*lo kashya* — it is not difficult. These are two entirely separate questions. *Yom Tov rishon* — the Seder night — requires *lechem oni*, which cannot be enriched or sweetened. But for the remaining days of Pesach (*yom tov sheni* here meaning the other days of the festival, not specifically the second *Yom Tov* of the diaspora), such *matzah* is perfectly valid. Fruit-juice *matzah* is not *chametz* — it may be eaten throughout *Pesach* — but it does not fulfill the specific obligation of the first night's *mitzvah* of *matzah* because it is not *lechem oni*.

Thus both aspects of *ani* / *oni* apply specifically to the Seder night. The word *ani* without the *vav* teaches that the *Seder* night *matzah* must be plain and unadorned. The word *oni* with the *vav*, as read by Rabbi Akiva following Shmuel, teaches that the *matzah* must be accompanied by *devarim harbeh* — the *Haggadah* and *Hallel*. Both requirements pertain only to the first night. The other days of *Pesach* have no such restriction on what may constitute kosher, non-*chametz* bread.

### Milk Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדַאֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לִבְנֵיהּ: יוֹמָא קַמָּא לָא תְּלוּשׁוּ לִי בַּחֲלָבָא, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לוּשׁוּ לִי בַּחֲלָבָא. וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין לָשִׁין אֶת הָעִיסָּה בְּחָלָב, וְאִם לָשׁ — כׇּל הַפַּת אֲסוּרָה, מִפְּנֵי הֶרְגֵּל עֲבֵירָה. אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: יוֹמָא קַמָּא לָא תְּלוּשׁוּ לִי בְּדוּבְשָׁא, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — לוּשׁוּ לִי בְּדוּבְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that this is **as Rabbi Yehoshua said to his sons:** On **the first night** of Passover, **do not knead for me** dough **with milk,** but **from** the first night **onward, knead** my dough for **me with milk.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: Throughout the whole year **one** may **not knead dough with milk, and if he kneaded** dough with milk, **the entire bread is prohibited, due to** the fact that one will become **accustomed to sin,** by unwittingly eating it with meat? **Rather, this is what he is saying:** On **the first** night of Passover **do not knead me** dough **with honey;** however, **from then onward, knead me** dough **with honey.**

The Gemara illustrates this distinction with a teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua to his son: on the first night of *Pesach*, do not knead the dough with milk; thereafter, you may knead it with milk. The implication is clear — *matzah* kneaded with milk is not *chametz* and is permissible on the other days of *Pesach*, but does not fulfill the *Seder* night *mitzvah* because it is not *lechem oni*.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: is it not taught in a *Baraita* that one may never knead dough with milk, and that if one did so, the entire bread is forbidden? This is because bread is eaten with everything, and *milchig* bread will inevitably lead one to eat it together with meat. This is the principle of *mipnei hergel aveira*. If *milchig matzah* is categorically forbidden, how could Rabbi Yehoshua permit it after the first night?

The Gemara offers two resolutions. First, perhaps he was speaking of honey rather than milk: do not knead with honey on the first night; thereafter, do so. Second:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם בַּחֲלָבָא, כְּדַאֲמַר רָבִינָא: כְּעֵין תּוֹרָא — שְׁרֵי. הָכָא נָמֵי כְּעֵין תּוֹרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **actually** Rabbi Yehoshua said: **With milk, as Ravina said:** If this bread, kneaded with milk, is prepared in the shape **of an ox's eye, it is permitted.** In other words, if one forms this dough in a unique shape, e.g., the eye of an ox, one may eat it, as it is clearly distinguishable from ordinary bread. Therefore, there is no concern that it will be eaten with meat. **Here too,** the *baraita* is speaking about a case where he told them to prepare this *matza* in the shape **of an ox's eye.**

*ve-iba'is eima*, one may indeed maintain that he was speaking of milk, in accordance with Ravina's ruling: *milchig* bread is permitted when made *ke-ein tura*. *Rashi* understands this phrase to mean small, bite-sized loaves — each portion is consumed in a single sitting, eliminating any concern of mixing with meat afterward. Others read *ke-ein tura* as meaning that the loaves are shaped distinctively, like an ox, so that they are immediately recognizable as *milchig* — a visible *siman* that prevents confusion. The Gemara concludes that this applies here as well: Rabbi Yehoshua was permitting *milchig matzah* when produced in small individualized portions that would be entirely consumed without remainder. The broader principle is established: the first night of *Pesach* imposes heightened criteria for the *matzah* itself, above and beyond what is merely required to avoid *chametz*.

### Lukewarm Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁוִין שֶׁאֵין לָשִׁין אֶת הָעִיסָּה בְּפוֹשְׁרִין!
>
> **Translation:** **And everyone agrees that one** may **not knead the dough with warm** water, as this will cause it to be leavened quickly.

The *Mishnah* concludes with a point on which all opinions agree: one may not knead *matzah* dough with *poshrin* — lukewarm water — since warmth accelerates fermentation. This consensus is then compared to the laws of *minchos* in the *Beis Hamikdash*, since *minchos*, like *matzah*, are subject to the prohibition of *chametz* and must be made as *matzos*.

## Minchos & Sisah

### Kneading vs. Sisah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מִמְּנָחוֹת, דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת נִילּוֹשׁוֹת בְּפוֹשְׁרִין, (וּמִשְׁתַּמְּרִין) שֶׁלֹּא יֶחְמְצוּ. אִם אָמְרוּ בִּזְרִיזִין, יֹאמְרוּ בְּשֶׁאֵין זְרִיזִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is** this case **different from** that of **meal-offerings? As we learned** in a mishna: **All meal-offerings are kneaded with warm water and are watched so that they will not be leavened.** The Gemara explains that there is a distinction between these two cases: **If they say** it is permitted **for diligent** priests to bake with warm water, **shall they** also **say** the same with regard **to those who are not diligent?** The priests are diligent and expert in the Temple service and are able to take extreme care, but not everyone can be relied upon to guard the dough unfailingly.

The Gemara asks: *mai sh'na mi-minchos* — how is the rule about lukewarm water different from the law of *minchos*? The *Mishnah* in *Menachos* explicitly teaches that *all minchos are kneaded with lukewarm water* — and one watches them so that they should not become *chametz*. The *Mishnah* in *Menachos* thus acknowledges the risk of lukewarm water causing *chametz*, yet permits its use provided proper vigilance is maintained. The standard method of vigilance is continuous kneading: as long as the dough is being actively worked, fermentation is prevented.

Why, then, is this not the rule for ordinary *matzah* baking at home? The Gemara answers: *im amru bi-zerizin yomru b'she-ein zerizin* — just because we are lenient for the meticulous, highly trained priests in the *Beis Hamikdash*, we cannot extend that leniency to every householder. Not everyone will maintain the necessary vigilance. The *Kohanim* in the *Beis Hamikdash* are entirely focused on this work; the general population baking at home cannot be assumed to exercise comparable care.

The Gemara then asks: if that is the reasoning, why do we not similarly permit *sisah* — the practice of soaking grain in water to soften the husk and facilitate the removal of bran, thereby producing fine white flour — in the *Beis Hamikdash* as well? This process was not done for *minchah* flour, since contact with water at that early stage risked *chametz*, but the argument of *zerizin* should apply.

The Gemara cites a teaching of Shmuel transmitted through Rav and Rabbah bar Yirmiyah:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מִילְתָּת נָמֵי לָתֵית! אַלְּמָה אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר יִרְמְיָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חִיטִּין שֶׁל מְנָחוֹת אֵין לוֹתְתִין אוֹתָהּ! לִישָׁה בִּזְרִיזִין אִיתָא, לְתִיתָה לֵיתָא בִּזְרִיזִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** if we rely on the diligence of the priests, **let them also soak** the grains in water to help them remove the husks. **Why did Rabbi Zeira say** that **Rabba bar Yirmeya said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to the **wheat** used **in the meal-offerings, one** may **not soak it?** The Gemara answers: The **kneading** of the dough **is** performed **by diligent** priests; however, the act of **soaking is not** performed **by diligent** priests. Instead, this soaking is performed outside of the Temple confines by less reliable non-priests.

The wheat used for *minchos* may not undergo *sisah*. Why not? The distinction is precise: *lisha bi-zerizin isa* — the kneading of dough is done by *zerizin*, either by the *Kohanim* themselves or, at minimum, within the *Beis Hamikdash*. But *sisah*, the soaking to remove bran, is *lav bi-zerizin isa* — it is not done by those same meticulous individuals. A person could bring finished flour to the *Beis Hamikdash* and the *Kohanim* would take it from there. The preparation of fine flour — including *sisah* — was done at home, by laypeople, outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. We cannot assume that everyone processing grain at home will be sufficiently careful.

The Gemara questions whether kneading is truly performed only by *Kohanim*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִישָׁה מִי אִיתָא בִּזְרִיזִין? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְיָצַק עָלֶיהָ שֶׁמֶן וְגוֹ׳ וֶהֱבִיאָהּ אֶל הַכֹּהֵן״ — מִקְּמִיצָה וְאֵילָךְ מִצְוַת כְּהוּנָּה, לִימֵּד עַל יְצִיקָה וּבְלִילָה שֶׁכְּשֵׁירָה בְּכׇל אָדָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **kneading, is it** performed only **by diligent** priests? **But isn't it written:** "And when any one brings a meal-offering to the Lord, his offering shall be of fine flour; **and he shall pour oil upon it,** and place frankincense upon it. **And he shall bring it to** Aaron's sons **the priests;** and he shall take from it his handful of its fine flour, and of its oil, together with all its frankincense" (Leviticus 2:1–2). These verses indicate that **from the** scooping of a **handful and onward,** the **mitzva** must be performed **by** members of **the priesthood. This teaches about the pouring** of the oil **and the stirring** of the mixture **that** they **are valid** even if they are performed **by any person,** even a non-priest.

Noting that a verse in the Torah describes the *minchah* preparation — the pouring of oil and the mixing — as being done before the *kohen* is involved: *mi-kemitza va-eilach mitzvas kehunah* — only from the *kemitza* onward is priestly participation required. The pouring of oil, the *belila*, the kneading, the baking — all of these are valid when performed by a non-*kohen*.

The Gemara responds:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָׁה, נְהִי דְּבִזְרִיזִין לֵיתָא, בִּמְקוֹם זְרִיזִין אִיתָא. דְּאָמַר מָר: בְּלִילָה כְּשֵׁירָה בְּזָר, חוּץ לְחוֹמַת עֲזָרָה — פְּסוּלָה. לְאַפּוֹקֵי לְתִיתָה — דְּאֵינָהּ בִּזְרִיזִין, וְלָא בִּמְקוֹם זְרִיזִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: With regard to **kneading, although** it **is not** performed **by diligent** priests, **it is** nevertheless performed **in** the **place of the diligent.** Kneading may be carried out by a non-priest, but it must still be performed in the Temple courtyard. **As the Master said: Mixing is valid** if performed **by a non-priest;** however, if it is conducted **outside the walls of the courtyard, it,** the meal offering, **is disqualified.** This serves **to exclude soaking, which is neither** performed **by** the **diligent nor** performed **in** the **place of the diligent.**

*nehidu bi-zerizin leisa* — it is true that the kneading need not be done by *Kohanim* specifically. Nevertheless, *bemakom zerizin isa* — it is done in the *Beis Hamikdash* itself. Processing grain into fine flour happens at home; kneading the dough happens inside the *Beis Hamikdash*. And regarding the *belila*: while the *belila* may technically be valid when performed by a non-*kohen*, it is invalid if performed outside the walls of the Temple precinct — *chutz le-chomas ha-azarah pesulah*. *Rashi* and *Tosafos* both note, independently, that the kneading must in any case be done inside the *Beis Hamikdash* because the dough must immediately be placed in a *kli shares*, a sacred vessel, which is only found within the Temple. *Tosafos* further adds that the dough must go directly from kneading into the oven; it cannot be kneaded at home and transported, as it would become *chametz* in transit. For whatever precise reason, the Gemara's conclusion stands: kneading is done within the *Beis Hamikdash* and thus benefits from the atmosphere of *zerizus* that prevails there; *sisah* is done outside and therefore may not be performed even for *minchos*.

### Minchas Omer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִמִּנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר? דְּתַנְיָא: מִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר לוֹתְתִין אוֹתָהּ וְצוֹבְרִין אוֹתָהּ! צִיבּוּר שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in **what** way **is** this case **different from** that of **the *omer* meal-offering,** which must also be guarded from leavening? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the ***omer* offering, one soaks it and gathers it,** without concern that it will become leavened. The Gemara answers: An act performed by the **community is different,** as the Great Sanhedrin supervises these operations, and those executing them no doubt proceed with great caution.

The Gemara raises one further case. The *Minchas HaOmer* — the first barley offering, the *omer* — did in fact undergo *sisah*, the soaking to remove bran. How does this square with the general prohibition on *sisah* for *minchos*?

The Gemara answers: *tsibur shani* — a communal offering is different. The *Minchas HaOmer* is a *korban tsibur*, a communal sacrifice. Because it is centrally organized and supervised by those responsible for the entire community's offering, one may assume that the process will be carried out with complete vigilance, even at the earlier stages of grain preparation. There is no individual householder who might be careless. A single coordinated authority oversees the entire process from beginning to end, and the concern that motivates the general prohibition — that some individual will not be careful enough — simply does not apply.

The Gemara notes an elegant verbal echo: the word *tsover osah* — "you pile it up" — which describes a step in the *sisah* process, sounds similar to *tsibur*, the community. Some read *tsibur shani* as meaning the *pile* makes a difference, not the communal nature. *Rashi* firmly rejects that reading, insisting that *tsibur* refers unambiguously to the communal character of the sacrifice.

## Ma'aser & Bikkurim

### Yose: Ma'aser OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָכוֹל יוֹצֵא אָדָם יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּבִכּוּרִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּכֹל מוֹשְׁבוֹתֵיכֶם תֹּאכְלוּ מַצּוֹת״ — מַצָּה הַנֶּאֱכֶלֶת בְּכׇל מוֹשְׁבוֹתֵיכֶם, יָצְאוּ בִּכּוּרִים שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱכָלִין בְּכׇל מוֹשְׁבוֹתֵיכֶם אֶלָּא בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in another *baraita*: I **might** have thought that **one** can **fulfill his obligation by** eating *matza* prepared from the wheat of **first fruits;** therefore, **the verse states: "In all of your habitations you shall eat *matzot*"** (Exodus 12:20). This verse indicates that one fulfills his obligation only with ***matza* that** may **be eaten "in all of your habitations."** This expression **excludes first fruits, which** may **not be eaten in all of your habitations, but** only **in Jerusalem.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.**

The Gemara opens a new *Tanu Rabbanan*: one might think a person fulfills the obligation of *matzah* with *bikkurim* in *Yerushalayim*. The comparison to *maaser sheni* is natural — both are eaten in *Yerushalayim*, both have a festive character. Can one use *bikkurim* flour for the Seder night *matzah*?

The verse teaches: *be-chol moshvoseichem tochlu matzos* — "in all your dwelling places you shall eat *matzos*." This phrase establishes a criterion: valid *matzah* must be the kind that *can* be eaten in all dwelling places. The case of *bikkurim* is thereby excluded — *she-einan nekhalin be-chol moshvoseichem ela bi-Yerushalayim* — *bikkurim* can only be eaten within *Yerushalayim* and nowhere else. Even if one is presently in *Yerushalayim*, the *matzah* must in principle be capable of being eaten anywhere. This is the position of Rabbi Yose HaGalili.

There is something conceptually striking here. The paradigmatic setting for eating *matzah* in the Torah era was *Yerushalayim* — the entire nation would be in *Yerushalayim* for *Pesach*, eating the *Korban Pesach* together with *matzah*. Yet the *halacha* is that valid *matzah* must have the potential to be eaten outside of *Yerushalayim*. The universal accessibility of *matzah* is a halachic requirement even in a context where the actual eating was entirely localized.

### Akiva: Both?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מַצָּה וּמָרוֹר; מָה מָרוֹר שֶׁאֵינוֹ בִּכּוּרִים, אַף מַצָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּכּוּרִים. אִי: מָה מָרוֹר שֶׁאֵין בְּמִינוֹ בִּכּוּרִים, אַף מַצָּה שֶׁאֵין בְּמִינָהּ בִּכּוּרִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** That verse is not the source for this *halakha*; rather, the fact that one cannot fulfill his obligation with *matza* of first fruits is derived from the juxtaposition of ***matza* and bitter herbs: Just as bitter herbs are not first fruits,** as they are not included in the seven species to which the mitzva of first fruits applies, **so too *matza*** may **not be** from **first fruits. If** you will claim: **Just as bitter herbs** are **from a species** that are **not** brought as **first fruits, so too *matza*** must be prepared **from a species** that are **not** brought as **first fruits,** e.g., spelt,

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He derives from the juxtaposition of *matzah* and *maror* — *al matzos u-m'rorim* — that just as *maror* cannot be *bikkurim* (since the *shivas haminim* do not include the bitter herbs used for *maror*), so too *matzah* cannot be *bikkurim*. He agrees with the practical *halacha* — one cannot use *bikkurim* — but learns it from a different verse.

The Gemara challenges Rabbi Akiva's *drasha*.

### Redemption Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹצִיא חִיטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּמִינָן בִּיכּוּרִים. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מַצּוֹת״ ״מַצּוֹת״ רִיבָּה. אִי ״מַצּוֹת״ ״מַצּוֹת״ רִיבָּה, אֲפִילּוּ בִּיכּוּרִים נָמֵי! הֲדַר בֵּיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **I would** then think this comparison **excludes wheat and barley, which are from a species** that are brought as **first fruits** and should therefore not be used for the mitzva of *matza*. Therefore, **the verse states: "*Matzot*," "*matzot*"** (Deuteronomy 16:3, 8) to **amplify** and teach that any *matza* is acceptable for this mitzva. The Gemara asks: **If** the repetition of ***matzot matzot*** comes to **amplify** this *halakha* that any *matza* is fit for Passover use, it should **also** include *matza* prepared from **first-fruit** wheat. The Gemara answers: This is indeed true, and **Rabbi Akiva retracted** his statement. He too derives the *halakha* from the verse: "In all of your habitations."

If the comparison to *maror* is taken strictly, it would imply not merely that *matzah* cannot happen to be *bikkurim*, but that *matzah* must be made from species that *cannot even in principle* become *bikkurim*. This would exclude wheat and barley from use for *matzah* entirely — since wheat and barley are among the *shivas haminim* and are precisely the species from which *bikkurim* are brought. One would be left using only the other three of the five *minim*. Rabbi Akiva invokes *matzos matzos riba* to prevent this overly restrictive reading. But the Gemara then turns the argument against him: if the repetition of *matzos* is inclusive, why does it not include *bikkurim* themselves?

The Gemara concludes:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יֵצֵא אָדָם יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּבִיכּוּרִים, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּכֹל מוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם תֹּאכְלוּ מַצּוֹת״ — מַצָּה הַנֶּאֱכֶלֶת בְּכׇל מוֹשָׁבוֹת, יָצְאוּ בִּיכּוּרִים שֶׁאֵינָן נֶאֱכָלִין בְּכׇל מוֹשָׁבוֹת אֶלָּא בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מוֹצִיא אַף מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מַצּוֹת״ ״מַצּוֹת״ רִיבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: I **might** have thought that **a person** can **fulfill his obligation with** *matza* from **first fruit;** therefore **the verse states: "In all of your habitations you shall eat *matzot*"** (Exodus 12:20). The verse indicates that one can fulfill his obligation with ***matza* that** may **be eaten in all habitations.** It **excludes first fruits, which** may **not be eaten in all habitations, but only in Jerusalem.** I **might** have thought that **I** should **also exclude second-tithe** produce as fit for *matza*; therefore **the verse states: "*Matzot*," "*matzot*."** As stated above, this repetition serves to **amplify,** and it includes second tithe in the materials that may be used in the preparation of *matza*.

*hadar beih Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva reversed himself, not in the practical ruling, but in the *drasha* from which it is derived. He ultimately accepted the *drasha* of Rabbi Yose HaGalili — *be-chol moshvoseichem* — as the source. A *Baraita* confirms this explicitly, giving the verse *be-chol moshvoseichem tochlu matzos* as the source for excluding *bikkurim*, in the name of a ruling that allows *maaser sheni* — which we know to be Rabbi Akiva's position.

The question then becomes: why does Rabbi Akiva include *maaser sheni* but exclude *bikkurim*, given that both can only be eaten in *Yerushalayim*? The answer is a crucial distinction:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וּלְהוֹצִיא בִּיכּוּרִים? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ הֶיתֵּר בְּכׇל מוֹשָׁבוֹת, כִּדְרַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר), וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי בִּיכּוּרִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן הֶיתֵּר בְּכׇל מוֹשָׁבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did you see** that led you **to include second-tithe** produce due to the words *matzot matzot* **but to exclude first fruits?** The Gemara explains: **I include** the **second tithe, which has** a means by which it may be **permitted** to be eaten **in all habitations,** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** as will be explained. **And I exclude first fruits, which do not have,** under any circumstances, a manner by which it is **permitted** to eat them **in all habitations.**

*maaser sheni* has a path to being eaten outside *Yerushalayim* — it can be redeemed. Under ordinary circumstances, once *maaser sheni* is brought into *Yerushalayim* it cannot be redeemed. But there is an exception:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר): מִנְיָן לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁנִּטְמָא, שֶׁפּוֹדִין אוֹתוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי לֹא תוּכַל שְׂאֵתוֹ״. וְאֵין ״שְׂאֵת״ אֶלָּא אֲכִילָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשָּׂא מַשְׂאֹת מֵאֵת פָּנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **As Rabbi Eliezer said: From where** is it derived **with regard to second-tithe** produce **that became ritually impure, that one** may **redeem it even in Jerusalem?** Second-tithe produce that has been redeemed may be removed from the city. **The verse states:** "And if the way is too long for you, **so that you are not able to carry it [*se'eto*],** because the place is too far from you, which the Lord your God shall choose to set His name there, when the Lord your God shall bless you" (Deuteronomy 14:24). **And** it is derived by means of a verbal analogy that this **carrying [*se'et*] is nothing other** than **eating, as it is stated: "And portions [*masot*] were taken to them from before him"** (Genesis 43:34). In light of this parallel language, the Gemara understands the phrase "so that you are not able *se'eto*" to mean: So that you are not able to eat it. Like second-tithe produce that has not yet been taken into Jerusalem, second-tithe produce in the capital that cannot be eaten due to ritual impurity can be redeemed and taken out of the city. Consequently, second tithe can indeed be considered food that may be eaten "in all of your habitations."

If *maaser sheni* becomes *tamei*, the verse says *ki lo tuchal se'eto* — "if you cannot carry it up" — and *Rashi* reads *se'et* through a play on words, connecting it to a verse about Yosef's gifts to his brothers where *mas'ot* refers to food portions. If you cannot eat it, you may redeem it. Therefore, even *maaser sheni* that has already entered *Yerushalayim* can theoretically be redeemed and carried out if it becomes *tamei*. This theoretical accessibility means *maaser sheni* qualifies under the criterion of *be-chol moshvoseichem*. *Bikkurim*, by contrast, have no such release mechanism; they can never be eaten outside *Yerushalayim* under any circumstances, and are therefore excluded.

### Yose Like Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי נָפֵיק בֵּיהּ? רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְקָמְמַעֵיט לְהוּ לְבִיכּוּרִים ״מִבְּכֹל מוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הֲדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara applies this discussion to the issue at hand: **Who did you hear that said:** One can **fulfill his obligation** to eat *matza* **with second tithe?** It is the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, and** yet **he excludes first fruits** due **to** the expression: **"In all of your habitations." Learn from this that** Rabbi Akiva **retracted** his opinion and accepted Rabbi Yosei HaGelili's derivation.

The Gemara then asks why Rabbi Yose HaGalili needed the *drasha* of *be-chol moshvoseichem* to exclude *bikkurim* at all. He already excludes *maaser sheni* on the grounds that it cannot be eaten *b'aninus* — invoking *lechem oni*. The same consideration — that *bikkurim* are also eaten *b'simcha* and not by an *onen* — should equally exclude *bikkurim*. Why does he need an additional verse?

The Gemara answers:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לֵיהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא: בִּיכּוּרִים אֲסוּרִין לְאוֹנֵן, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **maintains in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who says that an acute mourner may eat first fruits. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **First fruits are prohibited to an acute mourner, and Rabbi Shimon permits** an acute mourner to eat first fruits.

*savar lah ke-Rabbi Shimon* — Rabbi Yose HaGalili holds in accordance with Rabbi Shimon's view on *bikkurim* and *onenin*.

### Onen & Bikkurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן — דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״, וְאָמַר מָר: ״תְּרוּמַת יָדֶךָ״ אֵלּוּ בִּיכּוּרִים, דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ בִּיכּוּרִים לְמַעֲשֵׂר: מָה מַעֲשֵׂר אָסוּר לְאוֹנֵן — אַף בִּיכּוּרִים אָסוּר לְאוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains this dispute: **What is the reason for the Rabbis'** opinion, which maintains that an acute mourner may not eat first fruits? **As it is written: "You may not eat within your gates** the tithe of your corn, or of your wine, or of your oil, or the firstlings of your herd or of your flock, nor any of your vows which you vow, nor your free-will offerings, nor the offering of your hand" (Deuteronomy 12:17). **And the Master said: "The offering of your hand," these are first fruits, as** this verse **compares first fruits to** the second **tithe: Just as** the second **tithe is prohibited to an acute mourner,** as stated explicitly in the Torah, **so too** are **first fruits prohibited to an acute mourner.**

A *Baraita* records the dispute directly: the majority view holds that *bikkurim* may not be eaten by an *onen*, linking *bikkurim* to *maaser sheni* through the verse *lo suchal le-echol bi-sharecha* — which enumerates things that must be eaten in *Yerushalayim*, mentioning *maaser degancha* (i.e., *maaser sheni*) and *trumas yadecha* (identified as *bikkurim*) together. Since *maaser sheni* cannot be eaten by an *onen*, neither can *bikkurim*, because the two are linked by the verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: ״תְּרוּמָה״ קְרִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא. כִּתְרוּמָה: מָה תְּרוּמָה מוּתֶּרֶת לְאוֹנֵן — אַף בִּיכּוּרִים מוּתָּר לְאוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon** says in response to this contention: First fruits **are called *teruma* by the Merciful One** and therefore their status is **like** that of ***teruma*: Just as *teruma* is permitted to an acute mourner, so too first fruits** are **permitted to an acute mourner.**

Rabbi Shimon disagrees. He reads the phrase *trumas yadecha* — the gift of your hands — as identifying *bikkurim* with the category of *terumah*. Just as *terumah*, though sacred, can be eaten by an *onen* (since *terumah* is not eaten in *Yerushalayim* but anywhere among *kohanim*, and the *onen*-restriction is associated with the Temple's sphere of sanctity rather than with holiness per se), so too *bikkurim* can be eaten by an *onen*.

Rabbi Shimon's position requires a response to the clear association of *bikkurim* with *simcha* — the verse says *ve-samachta be-chol ha-tov* — "and you shall rejoice in all the good." How can an *onen*, who is in acute mourning, eat something tied to *simcha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא לִזְמַן שִׂמְחָה הוּא דַּאֲתָא, דִּתְנַן: מֵעֲצֶרֶת וְעַד הַחַג — מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא, מֵהַחַג וְעַד חֲנוּכָּה — מֵבִיא וְאֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon does not learn from **that** verse that one must eat first fruits while he is personally happy; rather, **it comes** to teach us **that** first fruits should be eaten during the **period of rejoicing. As we learned** in a mishna: **From *Shavuot* until *Sukkot*, one brings** first fruits **and recites** the prayers of thanks to God that appear in the Torah. **From *Sukkot* until Hanukkah one** may **bring** first fruits, **but he does not recite** the portion from the Torah. This mishna states that the ideal time for bringing first fruits to the Temple is during the joyous harvest season, from which it may be inferred that this verse is not referring to personal joy of the one bringing the first fruits, but rather to the period of communal joy.

The Gemara answers: *hahu l'zman simcha hu d'ata* — that verse is not establishing a legal condition for the eating of *bikkurim*; it is describing the time of year in which *bikkurim* are brought.

### Bikkurim Season

The *Mishnah* in *Bikkurim* specifies: from *Shavuos* until *Sukkos* — the harvest season — one brings *bikkurim* and recites the *mikra bikkurim* declaration (*ve-ata heiveiti et reishit pri ha-adama*). This is the true *zman simcha*, the time of joyous harvest. After *Sukkos* and until *Chanukah*, one may still bring *bikkurim* but no longer recites the declaration, because the season of harvest joy has passed and a different season has begun. The reference to *Chanukah* as the terminal date is a matter of the seasonal calendar, not a reference to the holiday itself; the question of whether this outer limit is rabbinic in character is noted.

The reason *bikkurim* begin only after *Shavuos* is that *Shavuos* marks the offering of the *shtei ha-lechem* — the two loaves — which is itself called *bikkurim*, serving as the first Temple-offering of the new produce. Only after this inaugural offering may the new produce be brought as individual *bikkurim*. The period thus runs from after *Shavuos* through *Sukkos*, when the final ingathering is complete, and *Sukkos* itself stands as the apex of that harvest joy — *zman simchateinu*.

Thus the verse *ve-samachta be-chol ha-tov* describes the setting in which *bikkurim* are brought — the season of rejoicing — but does not create a legal barrier preventing an *onen* from eating them. According to Rabbi Shimon, *bikkurim* are halachically identified as *terumah*, and the *onen* restriction does not apply. It follows that Rabbi Yose HaGalili, who aligns with Rabbi Shimon on this point, cannot use *lechem oni* to exclude *bikkurim* from the *matzah mitzvah* — since, in his view, *bikkurim* may be eaten by an *onen* and therefore do not conflict with the criterion of *matzah* eaten *b'aninus*. He must instead rely on the separate *drasha* of *be-chol moshvoseichem* to make the exclusion.

The larger thematic irony remains. *Matzah* on *Seder* night is *lechem oni* — the bread of affliction, poverty, and sorrow. *Bikkurim* are the paradigm of joyful harvest, *simcha*, and gratitude. That these two should be in such sharp halachic tension — that the most exuberant expression of agricultural thanksgiving cannot serve as the vehicle for the most solemn expression of historical memory — is itself a telling commentary on the layered meanings the Torah encodes in a single shared food.
