# Kiddushin 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meir vs. Yehuda

### Intro: Two positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד – קִידֵּשׁ, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – קִידֵּשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if he betrothed a woman **with consecrated** property belonging to the Temple treasury, if he does so **intentionally he has betrothed** her, and if he does so **unwittingly he has not betrothed** her; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says** the opposite: If he did so **unwittingly he has betrothed** her, but if he does so **intentionally he has not betrothed** her.

The discussion in our *daf* actually begins toward the end of 53b, where the *Gemara* is working through a *Mishnah* that records two distinct disputes — one concerning *ma'aser sheni* and one concerning *hekdesh* — and the divergent positions of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda. By *ma'aser sheni*, Rabbi Meir holds that one can never use it to betroth a woman, because it is *mammon gavoh* — it belongs to God, not to the individual owner. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees in principle and allows it, but rules that the betrothal is invalid in a case of *shogeg*, when the parties acted unwittingly. Thus, according to Rabbi Yehuda, the unwitting case is worse. By *hekdesh*, the positions are reversed: Rabbi Meir holds that if one betroths a woman with *hekdesh* funds knowingly (*mezid*), it is effective, but unwittingly (*shogeg*) it is not. Rabbi Yehuda again takes the opposite view.

The challenge, then, is to explain what principle underlies each position. The *Gemara* frames the question in terms of who would not have wanted the transaction had they known the true nature of the funds. The key distinction is whether it was the woman alone who would have objected, or whether both parties would have objected — since the presence of a *ta'us*, an error that undermines the consent of both parties, voids the transaction entirely.

### Yirmiyah's answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: נִיחְזֵי אֲנַן: מַעֲשֵׂר – אִיהִי לָא נִיחָא לַהּ מִשּׁוּם טִרְחָא דְאוֹרְחָא, אִיהוּ נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּנִיקְנֵי אִיתְּתָא מִמֵּילָא. אֶלָּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ – תַּרְוַיְיהוּ לָא נִיחָא לְהוּ דְּנִתַּחִיל הֶקְדֵּשׁ עַל יְדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said: Let us see** which reason is applicable to which case. It must be that with regard to second **tithe,** which may be eaten only in Jerusalem, it is **not satisfactory for her** to be betrothed with it **due to the trouble of** transporting it all **the way** to Jerusalem to enjoy it, whereas it is **satisfactory for him** to betroth her with it, **since he acquires a woman by itself,** i.e., by the tithe alone, without having to spend additional money of his own. **But** in the case of **consecrated** items, it is **not satisfactory for the two of them that consecrated** property **be desacralized by their** using it for betrothal.

Rabbi Yirmiyah's initial resolution is that for *ma'aser sheni*, only the woman would have objected had she known, because accepting *ma'aser sheni* as her *kiddushin* money would obligate her to schlep the funds to Jerusalem before she could benefit from them. The man, however, would not have minded — if she was willing to accept *ma'aser sheni* and make the journey to Jerusalem, he would be perfectly content to have married her on those terms. Accordingly, the transaction is voided by *shogeg* because her consent was compromised.

By *hekdesh*, Rabbi Yirmiyah explains, neither party would have wanted the transaction had they known the funds were *hekdesh*. Both parties would have realized they were committing *me'ilah* — misappropriating consecrated property — and neither would have wished to be party to that transgression. The woman would not want to be implicated in the violation of *hekdesh*, and the man would not want to spend *hekdesh* funds in order to contract a betrothal. Since both parties are in *ta'us*, neither one's consent was genuine, and the transaction is entirely void.

Rashi notes a further implication: the relevant question of whose *ta'us* undermines the transaction depends on whom you would ask — meaning, in principle, one might think these presumptions about unwillingness are case-sensitive and could be rebutted by the actual parties. Nevertheless, the basic framework stands.

### Yaakov reverses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִי לָא אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: מַעֲשֵׂר, אִיהִי לָא נִיחָא לַהּ מִשּׁוּם טִירְחָא דְאוֹרְחָא, אִיהוּ לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם אוּנְסָא דְאוֹרְחָא?! אֶלָּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ, בִּשְׁלָמָא אִיהִי לָא נִיחָא לַהּ דְּנִתַּחִיל הֶקְדֵּשׁ עַל יְדַהּ, אֶלָּא אִיהוּ מִי לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּנִיקְנֵי אִיתְּתָא מִמֵּילָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ya'akov said the opposite is** more **reasonable. Couldn't it be said** that in the case of second **tithe** it is **not satisfactory for her** to be betrothed with it **due to the trouble of** transporting it all **the way,** and it is **not satisfactory for him** to betroth her with it **due to the** danger of an **accident along the way.** If the second tithe gets lost on the way to Jerusalem, she will not have derived any benefit from it. It will cause her distress to think she received nothing for her betrothal, and he does not want to cause her distress. **But** in the case of **consecrated** items, **granted** that with regard to **her,** it is **not satisfactory for her that consecrated** property **be desacralized through her, but why wouldn't** it be **satisfactory for him to acquire a woman by itself,** without the expenditure of redeeming a consecrated item? Therefore, the reasons for Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement cannot be determined by logic alone.

The *Gemara* now picks up approximately twelve to fifteen lines into 53b, with the position of Rabbi Yaakov, who says *ipcha mistabra* — the opposite makes more sense. Rabbi Yaakov argues that by *ma'aser sheni* as well, both parties would have objected had they known. The woman, as before, would not have wanted the schlep to Jerusalem. But Rabbi Yaakov adds: the man also would not have wanted to give her *ma'aser sheni* had he known, *mishum onsa de'orcha* — on account of the burden of the journey. Rashi's initial reading is that perhaps the woman would not be fully betrothed until the *ma'aser sheni* actually arrived in Jerusalem intact and could be consumed, leaving open the possibility that if the funds were lost along the way she would not be *mekudeshet*. Tosafot rejects this reading entirely and insists that the betrothal takes effect immediately regardless of what happens subsequently. Rashi himself offers the more persuasive explanation: even if the man bears no formal legal liability if something goes wrong along the way, he would not have wanted to give her something that she could only benefit from after a difficult journey to Jerusalem — because she would come back to him with legitimate grievances if anything went wrong. She had a *schlep*, the funds rotted or were lost, and now she is unhappy with him. The man, therefore, would also have preferred not to initiate such an arrangement.

So Rabbi Yaakov concludes that by *ma'aser sheni* both parties would have objected, and thus the *ta'us* is bilateral. This leaves him with a more difficult question about *hekdesh*: we understand why the woman would not have wanted to be party to a violation of *hekdesh* — she would not want the consecrated property to be profaned *al yadah*, through her. But why would the man have objected? Rabbi Yaakov raises the rather striking point that, from the man's perspective, if it turned out he accidentally used *hekdesh* funds and in the process managed to betroth a woman without spending his own money — well, perhaps he would not have minded all that much. He committed *me'ilah b'shogeg*, which is unfortunate, but he got a betrothal at no personal cost. The *Gemara* notes this candidly: *d'nafka itatta mimeila* — the woman came out of the *me'ilah*, as it were, at no expense to him.

The *Gemara* itself acknowledges that Rabbi Yirmiyah's original answer seems the more compelling one — that by *ma'aser sheni* the problem is fundamentally hers, and by *hekdesh* it is a problem for both — but Rabbi Yaakov's position is recorded as the reverse. In any case, the underlying logic of both answers is that the transaction fails in *shogeg* when we can say that the relevant parties, had they known, would not have wanted to go forward — because without that genuine *da'as*, no transaction takes effect.

## Ta'us & Kiddushin

### Rava's question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: אִשָּׁה אֵין מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת, מָעוֹת מַהוּ שֶׁיֵּצְאוּ לְחוּלִּין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִשָּׁה אֵין מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת – מָעוֹת הֵיאַךְ יֵצְאוּ לְחוּלִּין?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava inquired of Rav Ḥisda:** According to Rabbi Meir, **a woman cannot be betrothed** by receiving consecrated money, but **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the money he gives her for betrothal: Does the **money become desacralized** as a result of the act of betrothal, as is usually the case when one uses a consecrated item for his own benefit? Rav Ḥisda **said to him:** If the **woman is not betrothed, how can the money become desacralized?** Since the money has not actually been used, nothing has changed with regard to its status.

At this point the *Gemara* records a question posed by Rava to Rav Chisda. We have established, operating within Rabbi Yirmiyah's explanation, that the woman is not *mekudeshet* when *hekdesh* is used *b'shogeg* — because had the man known, he would not have wanted to spend *hekdesh* money, and thus the transaction was a *ta'us* from the start. Since the act was done in error, and had either of them known they would not have proceeded, the entire transaction is treated as if it never took place.

Rava's question is: if the woman is not betrothed because the transaction was void on account of *ta'us* — if no legal act of acquisition occurred — then how could the money possibly have become *chullin*? The man physically handed the woman some funds, but if we say the transaction never legally took effect, then there was no genuine transfer of possession. If there was no legal transfer, then there was no act of *me'ilah*. And if there was no *me'ilah*, the money did not exit the domain of *hekdesh* into *chullin*. This is Rava's sharply posed question: *ma'os heichi yeitz'u l'chullin* — how did the money ever become *chullin*?

### Void transaction

The answer given is direct: if the woman is not betrothed, then the transaction never took place. The money changed hands physically, but it was never legally taken into the woman's possession. Since no valid act of acquisition occurred, no act of *me'ilah* occurred either, and the money does not become *chullin*.

This response, however, immediately generates a further problem. If one never commits *me'ilah* when spending *hekdesh* funds *b'shogeg* — because in every such case we can say the person would not have wanted to spend the funds had they known, and therefore the transaction is always void — then it seems to follow that there can *never* be *me'ilah* involving the misuse of *hekdesh* funds through a spending transaction when the act was unintentional. That is precisely the opposite of the standard presumption about *me'ilah*, which assumes that the classic case of *me'ilah* is *b'shogeg*, the unwitting misuse of consecrated property. How can Rav Chisda's answer be correct?

Rav Chisda replies: indeed, the same logic applies to a sale. Rav Chiya bar Avin posed this directly: *b'mecher mai* — what if one used *hekdesh* funds to make a purchase without knowing they were *hekdesh*? Rav Chisda responds: *af b'mecher lo kanah* — even in a case of purchase, nothing is acquired. Because the buyer, had he known, would not have wanted to make the transaction, it is treated as void, there was no genuine acquisition, and therefore no *me'ilah* occurs.

This is a remarkable position. It implies that *me'ilah* through the spending or transferring of funds can never occur *b'shogeg*, since any such transaction will be retroactively void on the grounds that the party would not have agreed had they known the truth. One might then ask: is there ever *me'ilah b'shogeg* at all? The answer is yes — but only in cases where *me'ilah* does not depend on a transaction. If someone ate *hekdesh* food without knowing, or anointed themselves with *hekdesh* oil, or used *hekdesh* oil to light their lamp — these are cases of directly benefiting from *hekdesh* without any transactional element. There, *me'ilah* occurs *b'shogeg*, and the object may exit *hekdesh* into *chullin* to whatever extent it can. But where the *me'ilah* would consist of transferring ownership of funds through a transaction, *shogeg* renders it void and no *me'ilah* takes place.

### Storekeeper case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: חֶנְוָנִי כְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חֶנְוָנִי כְּשׁוּלְחָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin **raised an objection to him:** The *halakha* is that if a man deposited a bundle of money with a money changer, the latter may not use the money. If the money changer untied the bundle and used the money, and it turned out that it was consecrated, the money changer is liable to bring an offering for the misuse of consecrated property, since he acted without the permission of the depositor. If the money was not in a bundle, the money changer has the right to use it, since the depositor knows that a money changer frequently distributes money. Consequently, in this case, if the money was consecrated, it is the depositor who is liable to bring an offering for the misuse of consecrated property, since the money changer is considered to have acted with his awareness. If one deposited the money with a homeowner, i.e., not a money changer, the latter may not use it in either case, and he is liable to bring an offering for the misuse of consecrated property if he does. With regard to a storekeeper, the *tanna'im* disagree: The same *halakha* applies to **a storekeeper as** to **a homeowner;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** But **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The same *halakha* applies to **a storekeeper as** to **a money changer.**

The *Gemara* now raises a challenge from a *braita* that distinguishes between a *chanvani* (storekeeper) and a *shulchani* (money changer) — a *braita* presented explicitly as the position of Rabbi Meir. The case runs as follows: I hold *hekdesh* funds, having forgotten they are *hekdesh*, and I give them to someone to watch for safekeeping. If the recipient is an ordinary *baal habayit*, a homeowner, he has no implicit authorization to use the funds regardless of whether I hand them to him loosely or in a sealed bundle. If he uses them on his own initiative, the *me'ilah* is his. However, if I give the funds to a money changer, and I hand them to him loosely — loose coins given to a money changer signal, by common practice, that he may use them in his business and simply repay me equivalent funds — then I am considered to have implicitly authorized his use of those funds, and I am the *mo'el*.

The debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda in this *braita* concerns the intermediate case: a storekeeper. Storekeepers also typically have loose change available for making change with customers. If I give a storekeeper loose funds and he uses them, is he acting with my implicit authorization — making me the *mo'el* — or is he acting on his own, making himself the *mo'el*? Rabbi Meir holds the storekeeper is like the ordinary homeowner (*chanvani k'baal habayit*) and the storekeeper is liable. Rabbi Yehuda holds the storekeeper is like the money changer (*chanvani k'shulchani*) and I am liable.

This *braita* poses a direct challenge. The *braita* is framed entirely within a scenario of *shogeg* — neither the depositor nor the recipient knows the funds are *hekdesh* — and yet *me'ilah* is treated as occurring. Both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree that *me'ilah* takes place; they only debate *who* committed it. This seems to flatly contradict the position we have been developing in the name of Rabbi Meir, that there can be no *me'ilah b'shogeg* through a transaction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי אֶלָּא דְּמָר סָבַר: חֶנְוָנִי כְּשׁוּלְחָנִי, וּמָר סָבַר חֶנְוָנִי כְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת. אֲבָל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא: אִם הוֹצִיא – מָעַל! רַבִּי מֵאִיר לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קָאָמַר: לְדִידִי, אִם הוֹצִיא נָמֵי לֹא מָעַל, אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, אוֹדִי לִי מִיהָא דְּחֶנְוָנִי כְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת! וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, כְּשׁוּלְחָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree only with regard to** that issue: **As** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that the same *halakha* applies to **a storekeeper as** to **a money changer, and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that the same *halakha* applies to **a storekeeper as** to **a homeowner. But everyone** agrees that **if** the storekeeper did **spend** the money, **he has misused** consecrated property. This indicates that even according to the opinion of Rabbi Meir, whatever transaction he performed did take effect. The Gemara rejects this: **Rabbi Meir stated** his opinion **in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Yehuda,** as follows: **According to me, if he spent** the money **he has also not misused** consecrated property, **but according to you, at least concede to me that** the same *halakha* applies to **a storekeeper as to a homeowner. And** Rabbi Yehuda **said to him: No,** I do not concede to you even with regard to this point, as I hold that the same *halakha* applies to a storekeeper **as** to **a money changer.**

The *Gemara* offers a resolution: Rabbi Meir is not speaking in his own voice in this portion of the *braita*. He is responding *l'divrav d'Rabbi Yehuda* — within the framework of Rabbi Yehuda's position. As if to say: I, Rabbi Meir, fundamentally hold that no *me'ilah* occurs through a *shogeg* transaction because any such transaction is void. But *you*, Rabbi Yehuda, who believe that *me'ilah* does occur through spending *b'shogeg* — at least concede to me that in this case the storekeeper acted on his own authority, like a homeowner. To which Rabbi Yehuda replies: no, even on my view, the storekeeper is like the money changer, and the depositor is responsible. The entire debate about the storekeeper is therefore internal to Rabbi Yehuda's framework, with Rabbi Meir only arguing within that framework while maintaining his own fundamental position.

The *Gemara* acknowledges this is a strained reading, since the plain meaning of the *braita* strongly suggests both opinions accept that *me'ilah* occurs in such a case. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* will continue developing this position of Rabbi Meir.

### Rav rejects position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: חָזַרְנוּ עַל כׇּל צְדָדִים שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְלֹא מָצִינוּ הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּשׁוֹגֵג אֵין מִתְחַלֵּל, בְּמֵזִיד מִתְחַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: We reviewed all angles** of the opinion **of Rabbi Meir,** i.e., we have examined all of Rabbi Meir's statements with regard to consecrated property, **and we did not find** that he holds that **consecrated** property **is not desacralized** if it is misused **unwittingly** but it **is desacralized** if misused **intentionally.**

Rav himself pulls back from this framework. *Chazinu al kol tzedadim shel Rabbi Meir* — we have examined every position of Rabbi Meir from every angle. And we have never found Rabbi Meir articulating the extreme position that *hekdesh b'shogeg ein mitchalel, b'meizid mitchalel* — that *hekdesh* is only desanctified when the misuse is intentional and never when it is unintentional. This would be the exact inverse of what we understand about *me'ilah*. The classic, fundamental case of *me'ilah* is *b'shogeg*; when you bring the *korban me'ilah*, the entire institution is predicated on unwitting misuse. To say that unintentional misuse of *hekdesh* funds through a transaction never constitutes *me'ilah* is, to Rav, an astonishing and unattested position.

It is worth noting that Rabbi Meir does have a *chiddush* regarding *me'ilah b'meizid*. As Tosafot observes, Rabbi Meir derives from the *parasha* of *Sotah* — *ish ki tisteh ishto u-ma'alah vo ma'al* — that *me'ilah* can apply even to intentional violations. The standard ruling is that *b'meizid* the funds do not exit *hekdesh* into *chullin*; Rabbi Meir says they do. So Rabbi Meir's position, properly understood, is an expansion of *me'ilah* to include the *mezid* case — not an elimination of *me'ilah* in the *shogeg* case. The claim that Rabbi Meir abolishes *me'ilah b'shogeg* for transactional misuse is, according to Rav, nowhere to be found and would be an incoherent reading.

How, then, does Rav explain our *Mishnah*, in which Rabbi Meir rules that a woman is not betrothed when *hekdesh* is used *b'shogeg*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשְׁנָתֵינוּ בְּכׇתְנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה שֶׁלֹּא בָּלוּ, הוֹאִיל וְנִיתְּנוּ לֵיהָנוֹת בָּהֶן, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **our mishna,** which indicates that this is Rabbi Meir's opinion, it should not be understood as Rabbi Meir's opinion with regard to consecrated property in general, but **with regard to** a case where a priest betrothed a woman using **priestly tunics that have not worn out** and that can still be worn during the Temple service. If a priest betrothed a woman with such garments, Rabbi Meir holds that if he did so unwittingly she is not betrothed, since clothes of this kind are not desacralized, and do not become hers. This is **because they were** initially **given** on the condition that the priests may **benefit from them** even when not performing the Temple service, **since the Torah was not given to the ministering angels.** It is impossible for a priest to wear the garments only at the moment he is performing the service and at no other time. Therefore, these garments are desacralized only if he intended to desacralize them.

The *Gemara* proposes that the *Mishnah* is discussing a very specific case: *bigdei kehunah she-lo balu* — the garments of the priesthood that have not yet worn out. If someone attempted to betroth a woman with a priestly garment — perhaps he was a *kohen* who possessed such a cloak — and did so unwittingly, then Rabbi Meir rules there is no *me'ilah* and consequently no *kiddushin*. But this is due to a special exemption specific to *bigdei kehunah*, not a general principle about transactional *me'ilah b'shogeg*.

## Priestly garments

### Unworn garments

Why is there a special exemption for *bigdei kehunah* from *me'ilah b'shogeg*? The *Gemara* explains: *ho'il v'nitnu lihanis bahem* — since *kohanim* are given permission to derive benefit from these garments. The garments of the *kehunah* are *hekdesh*, but *kohanim* are authorized to wear them and benefit from them in the course of the *avodah*. Because there is a standing allowance for benefit, there is a corresponding latitude that extends to their incidental use.

### Heavenly angels

The *Gemara* then invokes one of the most celebrated principles in all of *halacha*: *she-lo nitna Torah l'malachei ha-shareit* — the Torah was not given to the ministering angels. A *kohen* cannot be held to the standard that the instant the *avodah* concludes, he must strip off his priestly garments immediately. If he lingered two minutes after the close of the day, still wearing his *bigdei kehunah* while chatting with a colleague, it would be absurd to say he committed *me'ilah* for those two minutes of inadvertent benefit. The Torah is given to human beings operating within human norms. Therefore, a general exemption from *me'ilah* applies to *bigdei kehunah* when the use is unintentional — the latitude given for their ordinary and necessary use extends to cover incidental overlap.

This principle — *she-lo nitna Torah l'malachei ha-shareit* — appears in a number of halachic contexts, including discussions of microscopic organisms that are halachically invisible. It is always an expression of the same idea: the Torah calibrates its demands to the realities of human life and human perception.

### Worn-out distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: כׇּתְנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה שֶׁבָּלוּ מוֹעֲלִים בָּהֶם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. מַאי לָאו אֲפִילּוּ לֹא בָּלוּ? לָא, בָּלוּ דַּוְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with Rav's statement. **Come** and **hear:** With regard to **priestly tunics that have worn out,** one **misuses** property consecrated to the Temple **by** using **them;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. What, is it not** so that this *halakha* would apply **even** if **they had not worn out,** and the *tanna* wanted to teach the additional *halakha* that it is still a transgression even after they are no longer fit for use? The Gemara rejects this: **No,** this *halakha* applies **specifically** to garments that **have worn out.** Since these cannot be used by priests, the permission to use them for non-sacred purposes lapses, and they are like other consecrated property that is subject to the *halakhot* of misuse.

The *Gemara* then investigates this principle by looking at a *braita* concerning worn-out priestly garments: *kotonos kehunah shebalu, mo'alin bahem, divrei Rabbi Meir* — if the priestly cloaks have worn out and are no longer in use, Rabbi Meir says *me'ilah* applies to them. The *Gemara* initially wonders whether to read this broadly — as if there would be *me'ilah* regardless of whether they were worn out or not. The answer is *lo, balu davka* — no, the ruling is specifically about worn-out garments.

The distinction is essential: the entire basis for the exemption from *me'ilah* was that these garments are objects in active use by *kohanim*, and the Torah grants a certain latitude in the context of that active use. Once the garments have worn out and are no longer being worn, they are simply property of *hekdesh*, removed from active priestly service. Perhaps the *Mikdash* will repurpose the fabric, sell the raw material, or use it for wicks. Whatever the case, the kohen's personal authorization to wear them has lapsed, and the special exemption no longer applies. They revert to the status of standard *hekdesh* property, and ordinary rules of *me'ilah* apply.

What, then, does Rabbi Yehuda say about *bigdei kehunah* that are still in use? Rashi explains that Rabbi Yehuda would hold that even though there is a standing allowance for *kohanim* to benefit from these garments, that allowance was specifically circumscribed: it covers the *kohen* wearing his garments too long after the *avodah*, but it does not extend to misusing the garment in some entirely different way — such as using it to betroth a woman. For Rabbi Yehuda, the limited allowance does not create a blanket immunity from *me'ilah* in every other conceivable context of misuse. This, then, is how Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir diverge on the specific case of *bigdei kehunah* within our *Mishnah*.

### New vs. old coins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מוֹעֲלִים בְּחַדְתִין וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִים בְּעַתִּיקִים. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מוֹעֲלִין אַף בְּעַתִּיקִים, שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מוֹעֲלִין בִּשְׁיָרֵי הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with the explanation of Rabbi Meir's statement. **Come** and **hear: One** is liable for **misuse** of property consecrated to the Temple **by** using **new** shekels, i.e., those given this year, which are used to purchase animals for this year's offerings, **but one** is **not** liable for **misuse** of property consecrated to the Temple **by** using **old** shekels, given by those who failed to give the previous year's half-shekel, since these old shekels are used not to purchase offerings but for Temple maintenance; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says: One** is liable for **misuse** of property consecrated to the Temple **even by** using **old** shekels, **as Rabbi Meir would say: One** is liable for **misuse** of property consecrated to the Temple not only **by** using money designated to purchase offerings, but even with **the remainder of the chamber,** i.e., the money that remained in the chamber after money was taken to purchase the animals used in communal offerings.

The *Gemara* now examines an analogous case involving Temple funds: *mo'alin b'chadatin v'ein mo'alin b'atikin* — one is liable for *me'ilah* with new *shekalim* but not with old *shekalim*. The background is as follows. Beginning in *Adar*, people would donate their annual half-*shekel* to the *Beit HaMikdash* for the purchase of communal sacrifices in the coming year. These new funds — the *chadatin* — would be drawn upon in three collections throughout the year to buy *korbanos*. If someone failed to donate in time and contributed a *shekel* from a prior year, those older funds — the *atikin* — were designated for a different purpose: municipal and Temple infrastructure needs, such as building and repairing the walls of Jerusalem and its towers, and other city needs. Rashi describes this as *kol tzorchei tikkun ha'ir* — all manner of maintenance of the city.

Now, funds used to build the walls of Jerusalem are something from which ordinary people will naturally benefit — shade, shelter, protection. This is analogous in some respect to *bigdei kehunah*, in that there is an allowance for benefit built into the purpose of these funds. The question the *Gemara* poses is: does that allowance create a blanket exemption from *me'ilah* in the same way we are arguing it does for *bigdei kehunah*? If I accidentally took those old *shekalim* and spent them on something entirely unrelated to the walls of Jerusalem, would there be *me'ilah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְנִיתְּנוּ לֵיהָנוֹת, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת, דְּהָא חוֹמַת הָעִיר וּמִגְדְּלוֹתֶיהָ מִשְּׁיָרֵי הַלִּשְׁכָּה אָתוּ, דִּתְנַן: חוֹמַת הָעִיר וּמִגְדְּלוֹתֶיהָ, וְכׇל צׇרְכֵי הָעִיר בָּאִין מִשְּׁיָרֵי הַלִּשְׁכָּה. לָא תֵּימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֶלָּא אֵימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the question: **But why** should one be liable for using the old shekels? **Let us say** the same logic: One is not liable, **because they were** initially **given** on the condition that people may **benefit** from them, **since the Torah was not given to** the **ministering angels** and people could not help but benefit from them. This is **as** the money to repair **the wall of the city and its towers comes from the remainder of the chamber, as we learned** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 4:2): **The wall of the city, its towers, and all** of the **requirements of the city** of Jerusalem **come from the remainder of the chamber.** It is not possible for passersby to avoid benefiting from the shade provided by the walls of the city. The Gemara answers: **Do not say** that the opinion that one is liable for misuse of property consecrated to the Temple by using money from the remainder of the chamber is that of **Rabbi Meir. Rather,** emend the mishna and **say** that it is the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

*Ta shema* — the *braita* states: *mo'alin b'chadatin* — the new *shekalim* are subject to *me'ilah*, *v'ein mo'alin b'atikin* — but not the old *shekalim*. Then Rabbi Meir says: *mo'alin af b'atikin* — even the old *shekalim* are subject to *me'ilah*. Rabbi Meir further invokes the rule of *shiyarei halishka*: leftover funds from the collection designated for purchasing *korbanos*, which were not drawn upon in that year, were allocated to these same communal infrastructure needs. Rabbi Meir holds that even those *shiyarei halishka* funds remain subject to *me'ilah*.

This poses a significant difficulty. If Rabbi Meir is the one insisting on *me'ilah* even when funds are earmarked for communal use from which the general public will benefit — which is an even more permissive context than *bigdei kehunah* — how can we maintain the reading that it is specifically Rabbi Meir in our *Mishnah* who exempts from *me'ilah* due to the allowance given to *bigdei kehunah*? The logic should run the other way: if even the old *shekalim* and *shiyarei halishka* are subject to *me'ilah* despite their broad allowances for public benefit, then certainly there should be *me'ilah b'shogeg* with *bigdei kehunah*.

### Fallen wall stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אַבְנֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם שֶׁנָּשְׁרוּ מוֹעֲלִים בָּהֶם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. לָא תֵּימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֶלָּא אֵימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another proof: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** With regard to **stones** of the walls and towers **of Jerusalem that fell, one** is liable for **misuse** of property consecrated to the Temple **by** using **them;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** This indicates that according to Rabbi Meir, the use of even such stones renders one liable, despite the fact that it is not possible for people to avoid benefiting from them. The Gemara again answers: **Do not say** that this *baraita* is recording the statement of **Rabbi Meir. Rather,** emend the *baraita* and **say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *Gemara* then brings a further *braita*: *avnei Yerushalayim shenashru, mo'alin bahen, divrei Rabbi Meir* — if stones fall from the wall of Jerusalem, Rabbi Meir says there is still *me'ilah*. This is precisely the question that arose regarding the old *shekalim*: once there is a broad allowance for people to benefit from the walls, can we say that when stones tumble off and someone uses them to build their own house — not realizing the stones belong to the *Beit HaMikdash* — there is *me'ilah*? Rabbi Meir says yes, there still is.

This again contradicts the attempt to explain our *Mishnah* as a case where Rabbi Meir grants blanket exemption from *me'ilah* because a limited allowance was given.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, יְרוּשָׁלַיִם מִי מִיקַּדְּשָׁא? וְהָתְנַן: כְּאִימְּרָא, כַּדִּירִים, כָּעֵצִים, כָּאִישִּׁים, כַּהֵיכָל, כַּמִּזְבֵּחַ, כִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר: ״יְרוּשָׁלַיִם״ לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, is Jerusalem consecrated** at all? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 10b): If one says that an item shall be considered **like the lamb** of the daily offering, **like the** animals designated as offerings and kept in special **enclosures, like the wood** of the altar, **like the fires** on the altar, **like the Sanctuary, like the altar,** or **like Jerusalem,** it is a vow and he is prohibited to derive benefit from the item, as he has compared it to something consecrated. The essence of a vow creating a prohibition is the statement that a certain item shall be like a consecrated item. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Anyone who says** that an item shall be considered **Jerusalem has not said anything,** indicating that Rabbi Yehuda holds that Jerusalem is not consecrated.

This answer is, as the *Gemara* itself implicitly acknowledges, a significant stretch. The approach of reading *bigdei kehunah* as a hyper-specific exception and then reversing all the attributed names to maintain internal consistency becomes increasingly strained — particularly because the general thrust of the other sources suggests that the more lenient position on the walls of Jerusalem and *shiyarei halishka* is precisely the one we should be attributing to Rabbi Yehuda, not Rabbi Meir.

Further complicating matters: the *Gemara* observes that Rabbi Yehuda's approach would require saying that the walls of Jerusalem retain *kedushah*, and that stones fallen from those walls remain *hekdesh* subject to *me'ilah*. But we know from a *Mishnah* in Nedarim that Rabbi Yehuda holds the opposite. If one says a loaf of bread shall be like a lamb, or like the wood of the altar, or *k'Yerushalayim* — like Jerusalem — Rabbi Yehuda rules it is not a valid *neder*, because Jerusalem does not have the type of personal-property *kedushah* that can be invoked in a *neder*. Even if one says the correct formula — *k'Yerushalayim*, with the comparative *kaf* — Rabbi Yehuda rules it is ineffective, because Jerusalem's *kedushah* is not of the operative kind. This strongly implies that Rabbi Yehuda does not treat the walls of Jerusalem as possessing the same *hekdesh kedushah* as Temple property, which would mean that assigning him the position that fallen wall-stones are subject to *me'ilah* is inconsistent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּלֹא אָמַר: ״כִּירוּשָׁלַיִם״, וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״כִּירוּשָׁלַיִם״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם, עַד שֶׁיִּדּוֹר בְּדָבָר הַקָּרֵב בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that Rabbi Yehuda's reason is not that he holds that Jerusalem is not consecrated, but **because** the one stating the vow **did not say: Like Jerusalem,** but stated only that the item shall be considered Jerusalem, which is not a clear expression of a vow, **but isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Nedarim* 1:6) that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Anyone who says** that an item shall be considered **like Jerusalem has not said anything, until he vows by** comparing the item to **an item that is sacrificed in Jerusalem.** This indicates that he holds that Jerusalem itself is not consecrated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי נִינְהוּ וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* and the mishna in tractate *Nedarim* **are two *tanna'im* and** they disagree **with regard to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda** concerning whether or not he holds that Jerusalem itself was sanctified.

The *Gemara* resolves this final tension with *trei tanna'im aliba d'Rabbi Yehuda* — there is a dispute among *tanna'im* about what Rabbi Yehuda's position actually entails. Some hold Rabbi Yehuda denies *kedushah* to the walls of Jerusalem entirely; others attribute to him a view that the walls do retain *kedushah* and *me'ilah* applies when their use is taken outside the permitted parameters. The *Gemara* is ultimately threading a very fine needle to maintain the interpretation that our *Mishnah* addresses only the narrow case of *bigdei kehunah*, rather than accepting the broader reading that Rabbi Meir fundamentally rejects transactional *me'ilah b'shogeg* altogether. And yet, as the *Gemara* will make clear in the next section, it is precisely that broader reading — however extreme — that is in fact Rabbi Meir's genuine position.

## Halachic ruling

### Follow Meir: Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמַעֲשֵׂר הוֹאִיל וּסְתַם לַן תְּנָא כְּוָתֵיהּ, וַהֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ הוֹאִיל וּסְתַם לַן תְּנָא כְּוָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir with regard to** second **tithe** in that it is considered consecrated, **as** the ***tanna* taught us an unattributed** mishna **in accordance with his** opinion. **And** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda with regard to consecrated** property, **as** the ***tanna* taught us an unattributed** mishna **in accordance with his** opinion.

With the analysis of the competing frameworks now complete, the *Gemara* turns to how we rule in practice. Rav Adda bar Ahava states: *halacha k'Rabbi Meir b'ma'aser* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Meir regarding *ma'aser sheni*. This means that *ma'aser sheni* is *mammon gavoh* — it belongs entirely to God — and one can never use it to betroth a woman, under any circumstances.

### Kerem revai proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, מַאי הִיא – דִּתְנַן: כֶּרֶם רְבָעִי, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין לוֹ חוֹמֶשׁ וְאֵין לוֹ בִּיעוּר. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this statement: **What is** the unattributed mishna that is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir with regard to** second **tithe?** It is **as we learned** (*Pe'a* 7:6): It is prohibited to eat or derive benefit from fruit during the first three years after the tree is planted. The fruit of the fourth year is to be taken to Jerusalem and eaten there. With regard to **a vineyard** in its **fourth year, Beit Shammai say: It does not have** the *halakha* of adding **one-fifth,** i.e., if the owner himself redeems the fruit to bring money to Jerusalem to spend on food there, as one may also do with second tithe, he does not add one-fifth to its value but redeems it for its true value. **And it** also **does not have** the *halakha* of **disposal,** i.e., there is no obligation to dispose of it on the eve of Passover of the fourth year of the Sabbatical cycle, when all tithes that have not yet been separated must be disposed of. **And Beit Hillel say: It does have** the *halakha* of adding one-fifth and the *halakha* of disposal.

The basis for ruling like Rabbi Meir on *ma'aser sheni* is that a *stam Mishnah* aligns with his position. The *Gemara* identifies this *stam* in the laws of *kerem revai* — the fruit of a vineyard in its fourth year.

### Shammai vs. Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ לוֹ פֶּרֶט וְיֵשׁ לוֹ עוֹלֵלוֹת. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כּוּלּוֹ לַגַּת.
>
> **Translation:** That mishna continues: Furthermore, **Beit Shammai say: It has** the mitzva requiring the owner of the vineyard to leave **individual fallen grapes** for the poor **[*peret*], and it has** the mitzva requiring the owner to leave **incompletely formed clusters** of grapes for the poor **[*olelot*].** Since the vineyard is considered the owner's property during this year, he must leave these gifts for the poor. **And Beit Hillel say: All of it** goes **to the winepress** to make wine. Since the grapes have sanctity, he is not obligated to leave these gifts for the poor.

The *Mishnah* records a debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel on the status of *kerem revai*. Beit Shammai holds that *kerem revai* does not require the addition of a *chomesh* — the twenty percent surcharge added when one redeems *ma'aser sheni* — and does not require *bi'ur*, the removal of the produce from one's home in the third and sixth years of the *shemita* cycle. Beit Hillel holds that it does require both *chomesh* and *bi'ur*, treating it consistently like *ma'aser sheni*. In a further point of dispute regarding the gifts to the poor: Beit Shammai holds that the normal gifts to the poor apply to *kerem revai* — specifically *peret* (individual grapes dropped during harvest) and *olelot* (unripe or inferior clusters) — while Beit Hillel says *kulo l'gat*, it goes entirely to the winepress, with no portion left for the poor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֵית הִלֵּל – גָּמְרִי ״קֹדֶשׁ״ ״קֹדֶשׁ״ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר, מָה מַעֲשֵׂר יֵשׁ לוֹ חוֹמֶשׁ וְיֵשׁ לוֹ בִּיעוּר – אַף כֶּרֶם רְבָעִי יֵשׁ לוֹ חוֹמֶשׁ וְיֵשׁ לוֹ בִּיעוּר. וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי לָא גָּמְרִי ״קֹדֶשׁ״ ״קֹדֶשׁ״ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Beit Hillel? They derive** a verbal analogy from the word **"holy"** stated with regard to a fourth-year vineyard, as the verse states: "Holy, for giving praise to the Lord" (Leviticus 19:24), **from "holy"** stated with regard to second **tithe,** as the verse states: "Holy to the Lord" (Leviticus 27:30). This verbal analogy teaches the following: **Just as** second **tithe has** the *halakha* of adding **one-fifth and it has** the *halakha* of **disposal, so too,** the **fourth-year vineyard has** the *halakha* of adding **one-fifth and it has** the *halakha* of **disposal. And Beit Shammai do not derive** the verbal analogy of **"holy"** with regard to a fourth-year vineyard and **"holy" from** second **tithe.**

The *Gemara* explains the reasoning. Beit Hillel derives the similarity to *ma'aser sheni* from a *gezeirah shavah*: *kodesh-kodesh*. The verse by *kerem revai* says *kodesh hilulim l'Hashem*, and by *ma'aser sheni* the word *kodesh l'Hashem* also appears. Beit Shammai does not accept this *gezeirah shavah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים כְּמַעֲשֵׂר, כְּמַאן סְבִירָא לְהוּ? אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אַמַּאי כּוּלּוֹ לַגַּת? הָאָמַר מַעֲשֵׂר מָמוֹן הֶדְיוֹט הוּא! אֶלָּא לָאו כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to clarify the opinions of Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai with regard to the obligation to leave *peret* and *olelot* from a fourth-year vineyard. **And** with regard to **Beit Hillel,** who **say** that the *halakha* of fourth-year produce is **like** the *halakha* of second **tithe, in accordance with whose** opinion **do they hold** with regard to second tithe? **If** they hold **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, why** does **all of it** go **to the winepress? Didn't** Rabbi Yehuda **say** that second **tithe is** itself **common,** i.e., non-sacred, **property,** and the owner must therefore leave *peret* and *olelot*? **Rather, is it not** the case that they hold **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who says that second tithe is consecrated property? The mishna aligning the opinion of Beit Hillel with that of Rabbi Meir is tantamount to there being an unattributed mishna in accordance with Rabbi Meir's opinion, as the *halakha* is generally in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel.

Beit Hillel's ruling that all the fruit goes to the winepress, leaving nothing for the poor, follows directly from their view that *kerem revai* is like *ma'aser sheni* — and since *ma'aser sheni* is *mammon gavoh*, it is not the owner's money in a meaningful sense, which means the owner does not bear the personal obligation to leave gifts to the poor from it. If it were his money — as Rabbi Yehuda maintains — then it would remain his property subject to those obligations, and he would need to leave the gifts to the poor. Beit Hillel's ruling that no gifts to the poor apply thus presupposes that *ma'aser sheni* is *mammon gavoh* — exactly the position of Rabbi Meir. Since Beit Hillel constitutes the *stam* ruling and the *halacha* follows them, this establishes that we rule like Rabbi Meir on *ma'aser sheni*.

### Follow Yehuda: Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ מַאי הִיא – דִּתְנַן: שִׁילַּח בְּיַד פִּיקֵּחַ וְנִזְכַּר עַד שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ אֵצֶל חֶנְווֹנִי – חֶנְווֹנִי מָעַל לִכְשֶׁיּוֹצִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues with the explanation of Rav Naḥman's statement: **What is** the unattributed mishna that is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda with regard to consecrated** property? **As we learned** (*Me'ila* 21a): In a case where one sent an agent to buy something on his behalf and unwittingly gave him consecrated money to use, if he **sent** it **in the possession of** a halakhically **competent** person **and remembered** that it was consecrated money **before** the agent **reached the storekeeper, the storekeeper** will have **misused consecrated** property **when he will** later **spend** the money. This indicates that one is liable for misuse of consecrated property even when one acts unwittingly, as the storekeeper did. The *Gemara* clarifies that the case is one in which both the sender and the *shaliach* recalled, so both are *b'meizid*, the money did not exit *hekdesh* through either of them, and when the unsuspecting storekeeper uses the funds, his *shogeg* act is what causes the money to become *chullin* and constitutes the *me'ilah*. This perfectly illustrates the principle of Rabbi Yehuda: *shogeg* causes the money to exit *hekdesh*, *meizid* does not. And since this *Mishnah* operates on that assumption without attribution, it functions as the *stam* that establishes the *halacha* like Rabbi Yehuda for *hekdesh*.

Conversely, Rav Adda bar Ahava states: *v'halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda b'hekdesh* — we follow Rabbi Yehuda regarding *hekdesh*. This is the classic, standard position on *me'ilah*: *b'shogeg* the money exits *hekdesh* and becomes *chullin*, and the woman is *mekudeshet*; *b'meizid* the money does not exit *hekdesh* and she is not *mekudeshet*. Here too the ruling is supported by a *stam Mishnah*.

## Contradiction: Rav Pappa's challenge

### Rav Pappa's question

With the ruling now established — we follow Rabbi Meir that *ma'aser sheni* is *mammon gavoh*, and we follow Rabbi Yehuda that *me'ilah* with *hekdesh* funds operates in the standard manner, *b'shogeg* desanctifying the money and *b'meizid* not — Rav Pappa addresses a difficulty to Abaye. We have ruled according to Rabbi Yehuda's framework on *hekdesh*: if one uses *hekdesh* money to betroth a woman *b'shogeg*, the *me'ilah* is effective, the money becomes *chullin*, and the *kiddushin* works. If done *b'meizid*, the money does not become *chullin* and the *kiddushin* fails.

But compare this to a *Mishnah* in Tractate *Me'ilah* involving a *balan*, a bathhouse keeper. If someone pays a bathhouse keeper for entry using *hekdesh* funds without realizing it (*b'shogeg*), the *me'ilah* takes effect, the donor is liable, and the money becomes *chullin*. If he did it *b'meizid* — knowingly — the money does not become *chullin*.

So far this is consistent with Rabbi Yehuda and our ruling. But Rav Pappa identifies a genuine tension: by *kiddushin*, we say that if the man intentionally (*b'meizid*) gives *hekdesh* money to betroth a woman, the money becomes *chullin* and the betrothal is effective — that is Rabbi Meir's position which governs the *kiddushin* case in the *Mishnah*. Yet by the bathhouse keeper, *b'meizid* it does not become *chullin*. Why should the intentional use of *hekdesh* funds produce different outcomes in these two cases — effective desanctification leading to valid *kiddushin* in one instance, and failure to desanctify in the other?

This is the question with which the *daf* concludes, and which will be taken up in the subsequent session.
