# Shevuot 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yediya Requirement

### Babylonian in Eretz Yisr…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בֶּן בָּבֶל שֶׁעָלָה לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְנֶעְלַם מִמֶּנּוּ מְקוֹם מִקְדָּשׁ – מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma** concerning the awareness of the Temple: If **a Babylonian** or a resident of another country **ascended to Eretz Yisrael, and the site of the Temple was hidden from him,** so that he unwittingly entered into the Temple in a state of ritual impurity, **what is** the *halakha*? Is he liable to bring a sliding-scale offering to atone for his offense, or not?

The *gemara* opens with a question posed by Rabbi Yirmiyah: *ben Bavel she-ala le-Eretz Yisrael ve-ne'elam mimeno mekom hamikdash* — what is the status of a Babylonian who traveled to Eretz Yisrael but never actually knew where the *mikdash* was located? The background to this question lies in the general framework of *yediya bas-tchilah*: in order to incur liability for entering the *mikdash* in a state of *tumah*, one must have had initial knowledge — both awareness of one's *tumah* and awareness of the *mikdash* — and then subsequently forgotten one of those components. The question here is whether this Babylonian ever possessed the requisite *yediya bas-tchilah* to begin with, given that he never knew the precise location of the *makom hamikdash*.

He may well have known that there is a concept of a *mikdash* — that is, he knew the *mikdash* existed in principle — but he never knew the *mekom hamikdash* with sufficient concreteness to know what to avoid. This scenario is, in a sense, applicable more broadly: one might know that *Har HaBayit* exists somewhere, yet not know exactly which ground one is standing on, especially when so much has been destroyed.

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְמַאן? אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּבָעֵי יְדִיעָה בַּתְּחִלָּה – הָא לָא מְחַיֵּיב עַל הֶעְלֵם מִקְדָּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **In accordance with whose** opinion was this dilemma raised? **If** it was raised **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva, who requires** that there be **awareness at the beginning** in order for one to become liable to bring an offering, that is difficult, as Rabbi Akiva **does not deem** one **liable** when he had **a lapse of awareness** that he was entering **the Temple.**

The *gemara* immediately considers whether the question is even meaningful according to each of the Tannaitic positions. *Ei aliba de-Rebbe Akiva de-ba'ei yediya bas-tchilah* — according to Rabbi Akiva, who requires *yediya bas-tchilah*, one is not liable for *he'elam mikdash* at all; liability attaches only to *he'elam tumah*. If the entire case is constructed around the question of whether there was adequate *yediya* at the outset regarding the *mikdash*, the question is moot under Rabbi Akiva's framework, since Rabbi Akiva does not hold one liable for forgetting the *mikdash* in the first place.

### Rabbi Yishmael's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל דִּמְחַיֵּיב עַל הֶעְלֵם מִקְדָּשׁ – הָא לָא בָּעֵי יְדִיעָה בַּתְּחִלָּה!
>
> **Translation:** And **if** the dilemma was raised **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yishmael, who deems** one **liable** when he had **a lapse of awareness** that he was entering the **Temple,** that is difficult, as Rabbi Yishmael **does not require** that there be **awareness at the beginning.**

Conversely, according to Rabbi Yishmael — *de-mechayev al he'elam mikdash* — the *he'elam mikdash* is indeed a basis for liability. The Babylonian clearly had *he'elam mikdash* when he walked into the *makom hamikdash* without knowing he was doing so. But Rabbi Yishmael does not require *yediya bas-tchilah* at all, so the absence of prior specific knowledge of the location poses no difficulty. Either way, the question seems to dissolve: according to Rabbi Akiva you are not liable regardless, and according to Rabbi Yishmael you are liable regardless.

### Rabbi's Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא; אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי – דְּבָעֵי יְדִיעָה בַּתְּחִלָּה, וּמְחַיֵּיב עַל הֶעְלֵם מִקְדָּשׁ, וְאָמַר יְדִיעַת בֵּית רַבּוֹ שְׁמָהּ יְדִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** to raise the dilemma **only in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who requires awareness at the beginning and** also **deems** one **liable** when he had **a lapse of awareness** that he was entering the **Temple, and he** also **says** that **awareness** that one gains in **the house of his teacher is called awareness.**

The *gemara* therefore concludes that the question must be asked *aliba de-Rebbe* — according to Rabbi, who holds both that *yediya bas-tchilah* is required and that one is liable for *he'elam mikdash*. Under this framework, the question becomes genuinely sharp: there was clearly *he'elam mikdash* when the Babylonian entered — he did not know he was in the *makom hamikdash* — but did he ever have the initial *yediya* that is required? If the very *he'elam* consists in never having known the location at all, what is the *hava amina* that there was ever a *yediya bas-tchilah*?

### Yediya Beis Rabo

The answer lies in another principle associated with Rabbi: *yediya bei rabbo shemah yediya* — knowledge acquired from one's teacher constitutes *yediya*, even when it is no longer consciously at the forefront of one's awareness. Rashi clarifies what this means in practice. It is not simply that one once learned something twenty years ago and has entirely forgotten it. Rather, Rashi describes a state in which the knowledge is still somewhere in one's awareness — one would acknowledge, if asked, that there is such a concept as *tumas sheretz* — yet when one actually touched the *sheretz*, one simply did not connect the dots. The knowledge existed but was not activated. That latent, background awareness is what Rabbi considers *yediya bas-tchilah*.

### Mikdash Knowledge Standa…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי? כֵּיוָן דְּיָדַע דְּאִיכָּא מִקְדָּשׁ בָּעוֹלָם – יְדִיעָה הִיא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דִּמְקוֹמוֹ לָא יְדַע לֵיהּ – הַעֲלָמָה הִיא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the *halakha* in such a case? The Gemara explains the two possibilities: Does one say that **since he knows that there is a Temple** someplace **in the world, it is awareness? Or perhaps** one says that **since he does not know the** precise **location of** the Temple, **it is** considered **hidden** from him. The Gemara comments: This dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Applying this principle to the case at hand: *keivan d'yada de-ika mikdash be-olam, yediya hi* — since the Babylonian knew that there exists such a thing as a *beis hamikdash*, perhaps that general awareness constitutes *yediya* in the same way that knowing there is such a thing as *sheratzim* does. One might never have translated that knowledge into a specific awareness of which mountain to avoid, yet the conceptual knowledge was there.

On the other hand, the *gemara* entertains the opposing view: *keivan d'bimkomo lo yada lei* — since he never knew the physical location, it was in principle impossible for him to connect the dots. In the *sheretz* case, the background awareness could in theory be activated — one knew what the eight *sheratzim* were and could, had one paused, have recognized the animal in front of them as one of them. Here, by contrast, even if one recalled everything one ever knew about the *mikdash*, one still could not have concluded "I should avoid this mountain," because one never knew which mountain it was. There is thus no way to bridge from general conceptual awareness to concrete application, and perhaps that means the person was always, in effect, in a state of *he'elam*.

### Teiku - Unresolved

The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved — *teiku*.

## Sanctified Spaces

### Three Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד הַנִּכְנָס לָעֲזָרָה וְכוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״כְּכׇל אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי מַרְאֶה אוֹתְךָ, אֵת תַּבְנִית הַמִּשְׁכָּן וְאֵת תַּבְנִית כׇּל כֵּלָיו וְכֵן תַּעֲשׂוּ״ – לְדוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **The same** *halakha* applies to **one who enters the** area that was part of the original Temple **courtyard** and to one who enters the later addition to the Temple courtyard, as additions can be made to the city of Jerusalem or to the Temple courtyards only by a special body comprising the king, a prophet, the *Urim VeTummim*, and the Sanhedrin of seventy-one judges, and with two thanks-offerings and with a special song. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya said: As the verse states: "According to all that I show you, the form of the Tabernacle, and the form of all its vessels, and so shall you do"** (Exodus 25:9). The final words: "And so shall you do," are superfluous and serve to teach: As was done in the Tabernacle, so shall you do **in** future **generations.**

The *gemara* now transitions to the next part of the *mishnah*, which turns from the parameters of *yediya* to the question of what space actually carries the *kedusha* of the *mikdash*. Liability for entering *tamei* attaches only to a *makom hamikdash* that was sanctified through the proper ritual. With this in mind, the *gemara* addresses how one expands the sanctified boundaries of *Yerushalayim* and the *azarah* — questions of considerable ongoing relevance, since *kedushat Yerushalayim* determines, among other things, where *kodshim kalim* may be eaten, and *kedushat azarah* determines where *kodshei kodashim* may be consumed and where one incurs liability for entering in a state of *tumah*.

To orient the discussion: the innermost space — the *heichal* and *kodesh hakodashim* — is the *azarat kohanim* together with the *ezrat Yisrael*. All of this constitutes the *azarah*, the area in which one who is *tamei* incurs *kares*. The *ezrat nashim*, though part of *Har HaBayit*, does not carry *kedushat azarah* in this sense; it has only *kedushat Har HaBayit*.

### Expansion Requirements

The *mishnah* states: *she'ein mosifin al ha-ir ve-al ha-azarot ela be-melech ve-navi be-urim ve-tumim ve-sanhedrin shivim ve-echad* — one may expand the sanctified boundaries of *Yerushalayim* or the *azarah* only through the participation of a king, a *navi*, the *Urim V'Tumim* via the *kohen gadol*, and a *sanhedrin* of seventy-one. The expansion also requires two *todah* offerings, *shirah*, and a procession in which the *beis din* and all of Israel follow behind the offerings as they walk around the space being sanctified.

### Three Camps

The framework of the three *machanot* — *machaneh shechinah*, *machaneh levayah*, and *machaneh Yisrael* — corresponds to the spatial arrangement of the *midbar* and maps onto the areas of the *mikdash*. The *azarah* proper corresponds to *machaneh shechinah*. *Har HaBayit*, including the *ezrat nashim*, corresponds to *machaneh levayah*. *Yerushalayim* as a whole constitutes *machaneh Yisrael*.

## Vechen Ta'asu

### Source from Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן תַּעֲשׂוּ״ – לְדוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **and so shall you do"** (Exodus 25:9). The final words: "And so shall you do," are superfluous and serve to teach: As was done in the Tabernacle, so shall you do **in** future **generations.**

The *gemara* asks: *mena hani mili* — where does the Torah source for this sanctification procedure come from? Rav Shimi bar Chiyya derives it from the verse at the end of the command to build the *mishkan*: *kechol asher ani mareh otcha et tavnit hamishkan ve-et tavnit kol kelav ve-chen ta'asu* — "as all that I show you, the form of the *mishkan* and the form of all its vessels, so shall you do." The phrase *ve-chen ta'asu* appears somewhat superfluous in context. The *gemara* therefore reads it as a directive to future generations: the ability to sanctify sacred space is not a one-time event but a recurring capacity — *l'dorot*, for future generations.

### Rashi on Future Sanctifi…

Rashi notes that *ve-chen ta'asu kra yateira l'midrash l'dorot* — it is a superfluous verse to be expounded for future generations. One might observe that the verse speaks nowhere about sanctifying a *space*; the surrounding context is entirely about the construction of the *mishkan* and its vessels. And yet this is the source we work with. Additionally, we learn the specific components of the ritual — the participation of the king, *navi*, *Urim V'Tumim*, and *sanhedrin* — partly from the parallel to the components present in Moshe's time: Moshe himself was king and *navi*, Aharon was *kohen gadol* with the *Urim V'Tumim*, and there were seventy elders.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: כׇּל הַכֵּלִים שֶׁעָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה – מְשִׁיחָתָן מְקַדַּשְׁתָּן, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – עֲבוֹדָתָן מְחַנַּכְתָּן. וְאַמַּאי? וְנֵימָא ״וְכֵן תַּעֲשׂוּ״ – לְדוֹרוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **all the** sacred **vessels that Moses fashioned, their anointment** with oil **consecrates them. From that** point **forward,** in the generations after Moses, new vessels do not require anointment to be consecrated; rather, **their** use in the Temple **service initiates them** and renders them fit for use. Rava clarifies the objection: **But why** is this so? **Let us say** here as well that the phrase **"and so shall you do"** teaches that the same must be done **in** future **generations.**

Rava raises a challenge. If *ve-chen ta'asu* means that future generations can replicate what was done in Moshe's time, the most natural application is to the sanctification of the *klei hamikdash* — the sacred vessels. Yet the established *halacha* is: *kol hakelim she-asah Moshe meshichatan mekadeshatan* — all the vessels Moshe made were sanctified through anointing with *shemen hamishchah*; *mikan ve-eilach avodatan mechanachtan* — from that point forward, vessels are initiated through use in service. If *ve-chen ta'asu* teaches that future generations should replicate the original practice, why do we not continue to use *shemen hamishchah* to sanctify new vessels?

### Otam Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיִּמְשָׁחֵם וַיְקַדֵּשׁ אֹתָם״ – אוֹתָם בִּמְשִׁיחָה, וְלֹא לְדוֹרוֹת בִּמְשִׁיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is different there,** as **the verse states** with regard to the Tabernacle and its vessels: **"And he anointed them and consecrated them"** (Numbers 7:1). This teaches that it is only **"them,"** the vessels fashioned by Moses, that are consecrated **through anointment, but** vessels fashioned **in** later **generations are not** consecrated **through anointment.**

The resolution lies in a careful reading of two verses. On the one hand, the verse states *asher yeshartu bam bakodesh* — "which they shall use in the sanctuary" — teaching that the vessels are sanctified through service. On the other hand, the verse states *vayimshachem vayikadesh otam* — "he anointed them and sanctified them" — the word *otam* acting as a limiting term. The *gemara* works through the logic: had the Torah written only *otam* without *asher yeshartu bam bakodesh*, one might have said the original vessels required anointing, and future vessels would require both anointing and use — since *ve-chen ta'asu* would extend the anointing requirement forward. Had the Torah written only *asher yeshartu* without *otam*, one would apply *ve-chen ta'asu* to require anointing in future generations as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא אוֹתָם – בִּמְשִׁיחָה, לְדוֹרוֹת – אוֹ בִּמְשִׁיחָה אוֹ בַּעֲבוֹדָה! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר יְשָׁרְתוּ בָם בַּקֹּדֶשׁ״, תְּלָאָן הַכָּתוּב בְּשֵׁירוּת. הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֲשֶׁר יְשָׁרְתוּ״, ״אוֹתָם״ לְמָה לִי? אִי לָא כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אוֹתָם״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי הוּא דְּבִמְשִׁיחָה, לְדוֹרוֹת – בִּמְשִׁיחָה וּבַעֲבוֹדָה, דְּהָא כְּתִב ״וְכֵן תַּעֲשׂוּ״; מִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא ״אוֹתָם״ – אוֹתָם בִּמְשִׁיחָה, וְלֹא לְדוֹרוֹת בִּמְשִׁיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why not **say: "Them,"** the vessels made by Moses, he consecrated specifically **through anointment,** but vessels made **in future generations** are consecrated **either through anointment or through** their initial **service? Rav Pappa said: The verse states:** "And they shall take all the service vessels, **with which they will serve in the Sanctuary"** (Numbers 4:12). The future tense "they will serve," indicates that the verse is referring to future vessels, and **the verse makes them dependent upon service,** teaching that in future generations vessels will be consecrated through their initial service.

> **The Gemara asks: Now that the Merciful One has written "with which they will serve," why do I** need the restrictive term **"them"?** The Gemara answers: **Had the Merciful One not written "them," I would say that they,** the vessels made by Moses, were consecrated **through anointment** alone, but vessels made **in** later **generations** must be consecrated both **through anointment and through service, as** the Torah **wrote: "And so shall you do,"** referring to future generations. Therefore, **the Merciful One excluded** future vessels from anointment by stating **"them,"** teaching that it is only **them** that were consecrated **through anointment, but in** later **generations** they are **not** consecrated **through anointment.**

Now that both verses exist: *asher yeshartu* establishes that there is an alternative mode of sanctification through use alone; *otam* limits the anointing requirement exclusively to the original vessels, teaching that *osam bemeshichah ve-lo ledoros bemeshichah* — those original vessels with anointing, but not future vessels with anointing. And *ve-chen ta'asu* is therefore redirected: it teaches not about replicating the method of sanctifying vessels, but about the capacity of future generations to carry out the broader sanctification of sacred *space*, replicating the components that were present in Moshe's time.

## Todah Procession

### Nehemiah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִשְׁתֵּי תּוֹדוֹת. תָּנָא: שְׁתֵּי תּוֹדוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ – בְּלַחְמָן וְלֹא בִּבְשָׂרָן. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וָאַעֲמִידָה שְׁתֵּי תוֹדֹת גְּדוֹלֹת וְתַהֲלֻכֹת לַיָּמִין מֵעַל לַחוֹמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And with two thanks-offerings.** A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: **And** with regard to the **two thanks-offerings that are mentioned** here, the reference is **to their loaves, but not to their flesh.** An animal thanks-offering is accompanied by forty loaves of bread that are brought as a meal-offering. Ten loaves are leavened, and the remainder is comprised of ten each of three types of unleavened bread. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Ḥisda said: As the verse states** with regard to the consecration of the wall of Jerusalem in the days of Ezra: **"And I placed two large thanks-offerings, and we went in procession to the right upon the wall"** (Nehemiah 12:31).

The *gemara* cites a *tanna*: *shtei todos she-amru belachman ve-lo bivsaran* — the two *todah* offerings mentioned in this context are used for their bread components, not for the flesh of the offering itself. The flesh of the *todah* is brought as well — indeed, the bread can only accompany a *todah* offering — but what is carried through the procession and marks the boundary of the sanctified space is the bread.

Where does the *gemara* derive the requirement for two *todot* and the procession at all? Rav Shizbi cites the verse from Nechemiah: *va-amida shtei todos gedolos ve-tahaluchot liyamin me-al hachomah* — "and I stationed two great thanksgiving-offerings and processions to the right, above the wall."

### Nehemiah Full Text

The full context of this passage from Nechemiah is worth examining, as it provides the narrative backdrop for the entire halachic discussion. The verse opens: *u-be-chanukat chomat Yerushalayim bikshu et ha-leviyim mikol mekomosam lehaviam la-asot chanukah be-simchah u-ve-todos u-ve-shir u-ve-mitzaltayim nevalim u-ve-chinnoros* — "at the dedication of the wall of Jerusalem they sought out the Levites from all their places to bring them to Jerusalem, to perform the dedication with joy, and with todot, and with song, and with cymbals, harps, and lyres." Here one appreciates something beyond the bare halachic framework: there was genuine joy in this moment, the sense that an era was opening, that the wall was being inaugurated as a prelude to the restoration of the *beis hamikdash*. The *todah* was not merely a ritual instrument for boundary-marking — it expressed genuine thanksgiving.

The passage continues describing how the officers of Judah went up onto the wall, and the two great thanksgiving-offerings and their processions went to the right; following them was Hoshaya with half the officers of Judah, along with Ezra, Meshullam, Yehudah, Binyamin, Shemaya, and Yirmiyahu. Later in the passage: *ve-ha-todah ha-shenis ha-holachet le-mul va-ani achareiha va-chetzi ha-am me-al ha-chomah* — the second thanksgiving-offering going to the opposite side, with half the people following above the wall. And *ve-chol Yisrael achareihem* — all of Israel after them.

This is, incidentally, why the *todah* is the appropriate offering for marking the boundaries of *Yerushalayim*: a *todah* is a *kodshim kalim* that may be eaten anywhere within *Yerushalayim*. By bringing the *todah* to the outermost boundary of the area being sanctified, one is effectively declaring — this is the furthest point where a *todah* may be eaten. The offering demarcates its own reach. It is analogous, in a sense, to the way one lights *Shabbos* candles just before *Shabbos* begins: the act at the boundary signals the transition.

### Two Todahs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״גְּדוֹלוֹת״? אִילֵּימָא מִמִּין גָּדוֹל מַמָּשׁ, נֵימָא ״פָּרִים״! אֶלָּא גְּדוֹלוֹת בְּמִינָן. מִי אִיכָּא חֲשִׁיבוּתָא קַמֵּי שְׁמַיָּא? וְהָתַנְיָא: נֶאֱמַר בְּעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה ״אִשֵּׁה רֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ״, בְּעוֹלַת הָעוֹף ״אִשֵּׁה רֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ״, בְּמִנְחָה ״אִשֵּׁה רֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ״; מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאֶחָד הַמַּרְבֶּה וְאֶחָד הַמַּמְעִיט, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּכַוֵּין אֶת לִבּוֹ לְאָבִיו שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם! אֶלָּא גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁבַּתּוֹדָה, וּמַאי נִיהוּ – חָמֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the meaning of this verse: **What** is meant by **"large"? If we say** that the animals of the thanks-offering were **literally from a large species,** i.e., oxen, rather than from a small species, i.e., sheep, **let** the verse **state** that they were **oxen. Rather,** let us say that he took animals from among **the largest** and the finest quality **of their species.** The Gemara challenges this: **Is** the size of the offering **of** any **importance before Heaven? But isn't it taught** in a mishna (*Menaḥot* 110a): **It is stated with regard to an animal burnt-offering: "A fire offering, a pleasing aroma"** (Leviticus 1:9), and it is also stated **with regard to a bird burnt-offering: "A fire offering, a pleasing aroma"** (Leviticus 1:17), and it is also stated **with regard to a meal-offering: "A fire offering, a pleasing aroma"** (Leviticus 2:2). The same term is used in all three cases even though the three offerings are of different value. This **teaches that one who brings a substantial** sacrifice **and one who brings a modest** sacrifice have equal merit, and both offerings are accepted as having a pleasing aroma, **provided that one directs his heart to his Father in Heaven.** **Rather,** let us say that "large thanks-offerings" means **the larger** element **in the thanks-offering** loaves. **And what is that?** The **leavened** loaves,

The *gemara* asks: *mai gedolos* — what does the verse mean in calling these *todos* "great"? Perhaps it means they were from a large species — *parim*, cattle rather than sheep or goats? But a *todah* cannot be brought from cattle, so that cannot be the meaning. Perhaps it simply means large animals of whatever species? The *gemara* rejects this as well, citing the *baraisa*: the Torah uses the same phrase *ishsheh reiach nichoach* — "a pleasing aroma" — for an *olas behemah*, an *olas ha-of*, and a *minchah* alike. *Melamed she-echad ha-marbe ve-echad ha-mam'it u-bilvad she-yechaven libo la-shamayim* — whether one brings much or little, what matters is that one's heart is directed toward Heaven. So God is not more impressed by a large animal than a small one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הַתּוֹדָה הָיְתָה בָּאָה מִן חָמֵשׁ סְאִין יְרוּשַׁלְמִיּוֹת, שֶׁהֵן שֵׁשׁ מִדְבָּרִיּוֹת, שֶׁהֵן שְׁתֵּי אֵיפוֹת; וְהָאֵיפָה שָׁלֹשׁ סְאִין, עֶשְׂרִים עִשָּׂרוֹן – עֲשָׂרָה לֶחָמֵץ וַעֲשָׂרָה לַמַּצָּה. וּבַמַּצָּה שְׁלֹשָׁה מִינִין: חַלּוֹת, רְקִיקִין וּרְבוּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **as we learned** in a mishna (*Menaḥot* 76b): The meal part of **the thanks-offering came from five Jerusalem *se'a*** of flour, **which are** equivalent to **six wilderness** *se'a*. The *se'a* referred to in the Bible when the Jewish people were in the wilderness is smaller than the *se'a* used later in Jerusalem. **This is** equivalent to **two ephahs,** each **ephah** being **three** wilderness ***se'a*.** These two ephahs are **twenty** measures of **a tenth** of an ephah. **Ten** of these tenths were used **to** make **leavened** loaves **and ten** of these tenths were used **to** make **unleavened** loaves. **And the unleavened** loaves were **of three types: Loaves** of matza, **wafers,** and **measures of flour mixed** with water and oil. Accordingly, the leavened loaves were three times the size of the unleavened ones, and it was the leavened loaves that were used to consecrate additions to the city.

*Ela gedola she-ba-todah* — the word "great" refers to something larger within the *todah* itself. Within the *todah's* bread components, what is larger? *Chametz*. The *todah* is accompanied by forty loaves: ten of *chametz* and thirty of *matzah*, all made from the same total quantity of grain. Since the grain is distributed equally between ten *chametz* loaves and thirty *matzah* loaves, each *chametz* loaf is three times the size of each *matzah* loaf. The verse thus indicates that it is specifically the *chametz* bread of the *todah* that is used in the procession — the larger component.

To state this precisely: the *todah* came from five Jerusalemite *se'im*, equivalent to six *midbar se'im*, which is two *eifot*. An *eifah* contains three *se'ah*, and there are ten *isaronot* — tenths — in an *eifah*, making twenty *isaronot* in total. Ten were devoted to *chametz* and ten to *matzah*. The *matzah* was divided into three varieties — *chalot*, *rekikin*, and *revuchah* — while the *chametz* was of only one type. Consequently, each *chametz* loaf was three times the size of any individual *matzah* loaf. That is the *gedolos* of the verse: the *chametz* loaves of the *todah*.

### Mincha Instead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: אֵין הָעֲזָרָה מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת, אֶלָּא בִּשְׁיָרֵי מִנְחָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? כִּירוּשָׁלַיִם; מָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם – דָּבָר הַנֶּאֱכָל בָּהּ מְקַדְּשָׁהּ, אַף עֲזָרָה – דָּבָר הַנֶּאֱכָל בָּהּ מְקַדְּשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama says:** One can infer from this *baraita* that **the** Temple **courtyard is consecrated only with the remainder of the meal-offering,** and not by the loaves of a thanks-offering. The remainder of the meal-offering is the part left over after a handful of it and its frankincense have been sacrificed on the altar; this remainder is eaten by a priest. **What is the reason** for this? The consecration of the Temple courtyard is **like** the consecration of **Jerusalem. Just as** with regard to **Jerusalem, an item that is eaten** specifically **in it,** i.e., the loaves of a thanks-offering, which are eaten anywhere in the city, **consecrates** the city, **so too,** with regard to the Temple **courtyard, an item that is eaten** specifically **in it,** i.e., the remainder of the meal-offering, **consecrates** the courtyard.

Rami bar Chama then introduces an important qualification: *ein zo mitkadeshet ela bi-shiyarei mincha* — the sanctification of the *azarah* is not accomplished with a *todah* but with the leftover portion of a *minchah*. The *mishnah* might give the impression that the two *todos* are used for both *Yerushalayim* and the *azarah*, but Rami bar Chama draws a distinction. For *Yerushalayim*, the *todah* is appropriate because *Yerushalayim* is the outer boundary of where a *todah* may be eaten; bringing it to the edge of the sanctified space marks that boundary. For the *azarah*, one would want something that corresponds to the *azarah's* own outer boundary of consumption — something that becomes *pasul* if it exits the *azarah*. The *shiyarei minchah* — the portion of the *minchah* consumed by the *kohanim* — fits this criterion: it must be eaten within the *azarah* and is invalidated upon leaving it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה, בַּעֲזָרָה מִי לָא מִתְאַכְלִי?! אֶלָּא כִּירוּשָׁלַיִם – מָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם דָּבָר הַנֶּאֱכָל בָּהּ וְיוֹצֵא מִמֶּנָּה נִפְסָל, אַף עֲזָרָה דָּבָר הַנֶּאֱכָל בָּהּ וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִמֶּנָּה נִפְסָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that the loaves of a thanks-offering are not eaten in the** Temple **courtyard? Rather,** the comparison should be drawn as follows: The consecration of the Temple courtyard is **like** the consecration of **Jerusalem. Just as Jerusalem** is consecrated with the loaves of a thanks-offering, which is **an item that is eaten inside** the city, **and** which, if **it emerges from** there, **is disqualified, so too,** the Temple **courtyard** is consecrated with **an item that is eaten inside** the courtyard, **and** which, if **it emerges from** there, **is disqualified.** This item is the remainder of a meal-offering, which can be eaten only by a priest and only in the Temple courtyard.

The *gemara* presses: could one not use *lachmei todah* for the *azarah* as well, since *lachmei todah* may also be eaten within the *azarah*? The *gemara* refines the principle: the point is not merely that the food *can* be eaten in that space, but that the space constitutes the *outer boundary* of where it may be eaten — *davar hane'echal bah ve-yotzei mimena nifsal*. The bread of a *todah* that exits *Yerushalayim* is *nifsal*; the *shiyarei minchah* that exits the *azarah* is *nifsal*. Each offering thus traces the outermost edge of its respective sanctified zone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה לְהַלָּן חָמֵץ, אַף כָּאן חָמֵץ?! וְתִסְבְּרַהּ? מִנְחַת חָמֵץ מִי אִיכָּא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the consecration of the Temple courtyard is derived from the consecration of Jerusalem, let us say as follows: **Just as there,** with regard to the consecration of an addition made to Jerusalem, the loaves used in the ceremony are **leavened, so too here,** with regard to the consecration of an addition made to the Temple courtyard, the remainder of the meal-offering should be **leavened.** The Gemara expresses surprise at this suggestion: **And** how can **you understand** this? **Is there a leavened meal-offering?** A meal-offering is always unleavened.

This prompts a further question: since we are maintaining the parallel between *Yerushalayim* and the *azarah*, and in *Yerushalayim* we used *chametz* bread specifically, should we not similarly seek *chametz* for the *azarah* procession? The *gemara* acknowledges the appeal of the parallel but points out that there is no such thing as a *minchat chametz* — all *minchot* are *matzah*. One might suggest leavening the *shiyarei minchah* after the fact, or adding water to an unbaked *minchah*, but Reish Lakish establishes that even the *kohanim's* portion of the *minchah* may not be eaten as *chametz*, based on the verse *lo te'afeh chametz chelkam* — their portion shall not be baked as *chametz*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּמַחְמֵיץ לְהוּ לְשִׁירַיִם וּמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּהוּ – וְהָכְתִיב ״לֹא תֵאָפֶה חָמֵץ חֶלְקָם״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֲפִילּוּ חֶלְקָם לֹא תֵאָפֶה חָמֵץ!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say that one leavens the remainder** of the meal-offering, which the priests partake of, **and he consecrates** the Temple courtyard **with it,** that too is difficult, as **isn't it written: "It shall not be baked leavened, for their portion** I have given it to them" (Leviticus 6:10)? **And Reish Lakish says** in explanation of this verse: **Even** the priests' **portion shall not be baked leavened,** as it is prohibited to bake even a portion of the meal-offering leavened.

One creative alternative is considered: the *shtei halechem* of *Shavuot* — the only communal bread-offering that is brought as *chametz*, whose bread is classified as *kodshei kodashim* and may be eaten only within the *azarah*. But the *gemara* dismantles each possible scenario for utilizing this. One cannot sanctify the *azarah* on *Shavuot* itself because the *shtei halechem* are not sanctified until the animal of the *todah* is slaughtered, and one must sanctify the space on the day the construction is completed — learned from the verse *be-yom hakim Moshe et hamishkan*, the day Moshe erected the *mishkan* he sanctified it. One cannot finish building on *Erev Yom Tov* and sanctify on *Yom Tov* because *ein binyan beis hamikdash docheh Yom Tov* — building the *mikdash* does not override *Yom Tov*. One cannot leave the bread overnight for use on the day after *Yom Tov* because the bread becomes *pasul* through *linah* — remaining overnight. One cannot finish building at *motzei Yom Tov* and use the bread in the remaining valid nighttime hours because *ein binyan beis hamikdash ba-laylah* — building the *mikdash* may only be done during the day, as derived from *be-yom hakim Moshe et hamishkan* — *be-yom*, by day, and not at night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְּמָה לָא? אֶפְשָׁר דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ בִּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם בָּעֲצֶרֶת! מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר. הֵיכִי נֶיעְבֵּיד? נִבְנְיֵיהּ מֵאֶתְמוֹל וְנִיקַדְּשֵׁיהּ מֵאֶתְמוֹל? שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם בִּשְׁחִיטַת כְּבָשִׁים הוּא דְּקָדְשִׁי. נִבְנְיֵיהּ מֵאֶתְמוֹל, וְנִיקַדְּשֵׁיהּ הָאִידָּנָא? בָּעֵינַן קִידּוּשׁ בִּשְׁעַת הַבִּנְיָן. נִבְנְיֵיהּ בְּיוֹם טוֹב וּנְקַדְּשֵׁיהּ בְּיוֹם טוֹב? אֵין בִּנְיַן מִקְדָּשׁ דּוֹחֶה יוֹם טוֹב. נִשְׁבְּקֵהּ לְבָתַר הָכִי, וְנִבְנְיֵיהּ וְנִיקַדְּשֵׁיהּ? אִיפְּסִילָא לֵיהּ בְּלִינָה. נִבְנְיֵיהּ מִמַּעֲלֵי יוּמָא וּנְשַׁיַּיר בֵּיהּ פּוּרְתָּא – דְּעַד דְּקָדֵישׁ יוּמָא לְאַלְתַּר נִגְמְרֵיהּ וְנִיקַדְּשֵׁיהּ? אֵין בִּנְיַן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ בַּלַּיְלָה; דְּאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין בִּנְיַן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ בַּלַּיְלָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְיוֹם הָקִים אֶת הַמִּשְׁכָּן״ – בְּיוֹם מְקִימוֹ, בַּלַּיְלָה אֵין מְקִימוֹ. הִלְכָּךְ לָא אֶפְשָׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Because** actually **it is not possible** to do so. The Gemara explains why not. **How could we do** it? If **we build** the addition to the Temple courtyard **on the eve** of *Shavuot* **and** also **consecrate it on the eve** of the Festival, there is a difficulty, as **the two loaves become consecrated** as a meal-offering **with the slaughter of the** two **lambs** that are sacrificed together with them as peace-offerings, and this occurs on the Festival itself, not on the eve of the Festival. If **we build** the addition to the Temple courtyard **on the eve** of the *Shavuot* festival, **but consecrate it** only **now,** on the Festival, this too is difficult, as **we require consecration at the time of** the completion of **the building.** If **we build it on the Festival and consecrate it on the Festival,** this also cannot be, as **the building of the Temple does not override the Festival.** If **we leave** the two loaves **until after** the Festival **and build** the addition to the Temple courtyard on the day after the Festival, **and consecrate it** by eating the two loaves on that day, this is difficult as well, as the two loaves **were already disqualified by** virtue of being **left overnight** after the Festival. If **we build** the addition to the Temple courtyard **on the eve of the Festival and leave a little** unbuilt until after the Festival, and **as soon as the day is over** and the Festival is finished, **we immediately finish** building the addition **and consecrate it** with the two loaves before they become disqualified, this too is impossible, as **the building of the Temple cannot** take place **at night.** This is **as Abaye says: From where** is it derived **that the building of the Temple cannot** take place **at night? As it is stated: "And on the day that the Tabernacle was erected"** (Numbers 9:15), from which it can be derived: One **may erect it during the day,** but one **may not erect it at night. Therefore, it is impossible** to consecrate the Temple courtyard with the two loaves; it must be done with the remainder of an unleavened meal-offering.

The conclusion is thus that it is practically impossible to use *chametz* bread for the *azarah* sanctification procession. One works with *shiyarei minchah* — which is *matzah* — and that is the best available parallel to the *chametz* of the *todah* used for *Yerushalayim*.

## Music & Psalms

### Shirah of Todah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשִׁיר. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁיר שֶׁל תּוֹדָה – בְּכִנּוֹרוֹת וּבִנְבָלִים וּבְצֶלְצֶלִים עַל כׇּל פִּינָּה וּפִינָּה וְעַל כׇּל אֶבֶן גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְאוֹמֵר ״אֲרוֹמִמְךָ ה׳ כִּי דִלִּיתָנִי וְגוֹ׳״, וְשִׁיר שֶׁל פְּגָעִים, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִין שִׁיר שֶׁל נְגָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches concerning the consecration of an addition to the city of Jerusalem or the Temple courtyard: **And with a song. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: They sang **the song of thanksgiving,** i.e., Psalms, chapter 100, which begins: "A psalm of thanksgiving," accompanied **by harps, lyres, and cymbals, at every corner and upon every large stone in Jerusalem. And** they also **recited** Psalms, chapter 30, which begins: **"I will extol You, O Lord, for You have lifted me up," and the song of evil spirits,** i.e., Psalms, chapter 91, which begins: "He that dwells in the secret place of the Most High." **And some say** that this psalm is called **the song of plagues.**

The *gemara* cites a *baraisa* on the *shirah* — the singing — that accompanies the procession: *shir shel todah be-chinnoros u-ve-nevalim u-ve-tziltzalim al kol pina u-fina* — with harps, lyres, and cymbals at every corner, *ve-al kol even gedola she-bi-Yerushalayim* — and at every great stone in Jerusalem. Whether this means musical instrumentalists were positioned at each corner and large stone or that song was performed each time the procession passed such a point, the text leaves somewhat open. In either reading, the musical accompaniment is extensive and tied to the physical features of the space being sanctified.

### Three Psalms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר דִּנְגָעִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנֶגַע לֹא יִקְרַב בְּאׇהֳלֶךָ״, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר פְּגָעִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״יִפֹּל מִצִּדְּךָ אֶלֶף״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״יֹשֵׁב בְּסֵתֶר עֶלְיוֹן בְּצֵל שַׁדַּי יִתְלוֹנָן״ עַד ״כִּי אַתָּה ה׳ מַחְסִי עֶלְיוֹן שַׂמְתָּ מְעוֹנֶךָ״; וְחוֹזֵר וְאוֹמֵר: ״מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד בְּבׇרְחוֹ מִפְּנֵי אַבְשָׁלוֹם בְּנוֹ, ה׳ מָה רַבּוּ צָרָי״, עַד ״לַה׳ הַיְשׁוּעָה עַל עַמְּךָ בִרְכָתֶךָ סֶּלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The reason of **the one who says** that it is called the song **of plagues** is **that it is written: "Nor shall any plague come near your dwelling"** (Psalms 91:10). **And** the reason of **the one who says** that it is called the song of **evil spirits** is **that it is written: "A thousand shall fall at your side** and ten thousand at your right hand; but it shall not come near you" (Psalms 91:7). **And they recited** the psalm from the verse: **"He that dwells in the secret place of the Most High shall abide in the shadow of the Almighty"** (Psalms 91:1), **until** they completed the verse: **"Because You, O Lord, are my refuge; You have made the most High Your habitation"** (Psalms 91:9). **And they would then recite** Psalms, chapter 3, which begins: **"A psalm of David, when he fled from Absalom his son. Lord, how many are my enemies become," until** they reached the verse: **"Salvation belongs to the Lord; Your blessing be upon Your people. Sela"** (Psalms 3:9), which is the end of that psalm.

At each such station, *omer: aromimcha Hashem ki dilisani* — one recites the opening of Psalm 30, *mizmor shir chanukas habayit*. A striking coincidence — or providence — that this *daf* is studied on the first day of Chanukah, the very day one might expect to encounter *mizmor shir chanukas habayit*.

The *baraisa* then introduces *shir shel pegaim* — there is a view that *yoshev be-seter elyon*, Psalm 91, was composed by Moshe on the occasion of the erection of the *mishkan*, since Moshe blessed Israel with *yehi ratzon she-tishre shechinah be-ma'asei yedichem*, and Psalm 91 concludes *vi-yehi noam Hashem Elokeinu aleinu u-ma'asei yadeinu konena aleinu* — may God favor the work of our hands. The connection between the dedication of a sanctified space and the prayer that God's presence rest upon it is exact.

A second view holds that the relevant psalm is *shir shel negaim*, understood to refer to Psalm 91's verse *ve-nega lo yikrav be-oholecha* — no affliction shall approach your tent. So three compositions emerge: *mizmor shir chanukas habayit*, dedicating the house; *yehi noam*, invoking God's desire for the work of our hands; and a psalm of protection, that no enemy or affliction should come near. *Mizmor l'David be-vorcho mi-penei Avshalom beno, Hashem ma rabu tzarai* is also recited — a *mizmor* born of persecution and trust — until *la-Hashem ha-yeshuah al amcha birchatecha selah*.

### Evening Recitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר לְהוּ לְהָנֵי קְרָאֵי, וְגָאנֵי. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אָסוּר לְהִתְרַפְּאוֹת בְּדִבְרֵי תּוֹרָה! לְהָגֵן שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi would recite these verses** to protect him from evil spirits during the night **and fall asleep** while saying them. The Gemara asks: **How could he do that? But doesn't Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** himself **say: One is prohibited from healing himself with words of Torah?** The Gemara answers: **To protect** oneself **is different,** as he recited these verses only to protect himself from evil spirits, and not to heal himself.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi *amar lehu l'hani karai ve-gani* — he would recite these verses and then go to sleep, using them for nighttime protection. The *gemara* asks: *heichi avid hachi* — how could he do this? For Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi himself said *assur le-hitrape'os be-divrei Torah* — it is forbidden to use words of Torah as a healing incantation. The *gemara* answers: *le-hagen shani* — protection is different from healing. Saying these *pesukim* before sleep is not a magical use of Torah text; it is a *tefillah*, a prayer asking God to guard one through the night. The parallel to *Krias Shema al ha-mitah* is exact: the *yoshev be-seter elyon* recited as part of the bedtime *Shema* and *Hashem ma rabu tzarai* that follows it are precisely these texts used in this protective function.

### Healing Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא כִּי אָמַר אָסוּר – דְּאִיכָּא מַכָּה? אִי דְּאִיכָּא מַכָּה אָסוּר, וְתוּ לָא?! וְהָתְנַן: הַלּוֹחֵשׁ עַל הַמַּכָּה, אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא! הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּרוֹקֵק שָׁנוּ; לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מַזְכִּירִין שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם עַל הָרְקִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But rather, when** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **said** that this **is prohibited,** he was referring to a situation **where there is** already **a wound** and one recites these verses in order to heal himself. But **if there is** already **a wound** and he recites these verses over it, is only this **prohibited, and nothing more? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Sanhedrin* 90a), that **one who whispers** an incantation **over a wound has no share in the World-to-Come?** The Gemara answers: **Wasn't it stated with regard to that** mishna that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The mishna was **taught with regard to one who spits** into the wound and then whispers these verses. And the reason for the severity of this action is **that the name of Heaven must not be mentioned in connection with spitting,** as doing so is a show of contempt for God.

The distinction between permissible and impermissible use of *pesukim* is then sharpened. The *mishnah* states that *ha-lochesh al ha-makkah ein lo chelek le-olam ha-ba* — one who whispers over a wound has no portion in the world to come. This is far more severe than a mere prohibition. The *gemara* reconciles: Rabbi Yochanan clarifies that the extreme penalty of *ein lo chelek le-olam ha-ba* applies specifically to one who spits on the wound while reciting the verse — *be-rokek shanu* — since one thereby invokes God's name over the act of spitting, which is disrespectful of the divine name. There is also the dimension that spitting is part of a recognized magical-healing ritual, making the verse into a talisman.

What emerges is a spectrum. Reciting *pesukim* before sleep as a prayer for protection — entirely permissible, even commendable. Reciting *pesukim* over a wound without spitting — forbidden, though without the extreme consequence. Reciting *pesukim* over a wound with spitting — *ein lo chelek le-olam ha-ba*, because one is both using Torah as a magical incantation and associating God's name with an act of spitting. The gradations reflect how closely the act resembles a *tefillah* versus a magical procedure.

## Procession Order

### Order Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית דִּין מְהַלְּכִין וּשְׁתֵּי תּוֹדוֹת אַחֲרֵיהֶן וְכוּ׳. לְמֵימְרָא דְּבֵית דִּין קַמֵּי תּוֹדָה אָזְלִי?! וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּלֶךְ אַחֲרֵיהֶם הוֹשַׁעְיָה וַחֲצִי שָׂרֵי יְהוּדָה״! הָכִי קָאָמַר: בֵּית דִּין מְהַלְּכִין, וּשְׁתֵּי תּוֹדוֹת מְהַלְּכוֹת וּבֵית דִּין אַחֲרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that as part of the consecration ceremony, **the court** would **move** forward, **and two thanks-offerings** would be brought **after them,** and all of the Jewish people would follow behind them. The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that the** members of the **court walk in front of the thanks-offering? But isn't it written** in the verse from which this ceremony is derived: "And I placed two large thanks-offerings, and we went in procession to the right upon the wall, toward the dung gate; **and after them went Hoshaiah, and half of the princes of Judah"** (Nehemiah 12:31–32)? The Gemara answers: **This** is what the mishna **is saying: The court moves** forward. **And** how is this done? **The two thanks-offerings move** forward, **and the court** follows **after them.**

The *gemara* returns to the mechanics of the procession. The *mishnah* states: *beis din mehalechim u-shtei todos achareihem* — the *beis din* goes, and the two *todos* go after them. This implies the *beis din* leads. Yet the verse in Nechemiah states *va-yelech achareihem Hoshaya va-chetzi sarei Yehuda* — Hoshaya and the officers of Judah, who represent the *beis din*, went *after* — implying the *todos* led and the *beis din* followed. The *gemara* therefore re-reads the *mishnah*: its words should be understood not as "the *beis din* goes and the *todos* follow," but rather as presenting two alternatives — either the *beis din* goes with the *todos* following, or the *todos* go with the *beis din* following. The plain reading of the *mishnah* is thus substantially reinterpreted to align with Nechemiah, where the offerings led and the leadership followed.

### Two Arrangements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד מְהַלְּכוֹת? רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי; חַד אָמַר: זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ, וְחַד אָמַר: זוֹ אַחַר זוֹ. מַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ, ״הַפְּנִימִית״ – הָךְ דִּמְקָרְבָא לַחוֹמָה. מַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ אַחַר זוֹ, ״הַפְּנִימִית״ – הָךְ דִּמְקָרְבָא לְבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this point: **How** exactly did the two thanks-offerings **move** forward? **Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, disagree about this. **One** Sage **says: One** thanks-offering **alongside the other. And one** Sage **says: One behind the other.** According to **the one who says** that the two offerings moved **one alongside the other,** the thanks-offering that the mishna refers to as **the inner one** is **that which is closest to the wall.** According to **the one who says** that the two offerings moved **one behind the other,** the thanks-offering that is called **the inner one** is **that which is closest to** the members of **the court** advancing behind the thanks-offerings.

Having established that the *todos* go first and the *beis din* follows, the *gemara* asks: *keitzad mehalechot* — in what arrangement did the two *todos* proceed? Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi disagree. One holds *zo k'neged zo* — they walked two abreast, side by side. The other holds *zo achar zo* — they walked in single file. The question of inner and outer follows from this: if abreast, the inner *todah* is the one closest to the wall; if single file, the inner *todah* is the one closest to the *beis din* behind it.

### Inner vs. Outer

The *mishnah* states: *ha-pnimit ne'echelet ve-ha-chitzonit nisrefet* — the inner one is eaten and the outer one is burned. This *halacha* generates the question of which is which under each arrangement.

### Sanctification Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַפְּנִימִית נֶאֱכֶלֶת וְהַחִיצוֹנָה נִשְׂרֶפֶת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ אַחַר זוֹ, אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי פְּנִימִית נֶאֱכֶלֶת – מִשּׁוּם דְּאָתְיָא חִיצוֹנָה קַמַּהּ, וְקַדְּשָׁהּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes these two opinions: **We learned** in the mishna: When they would reach the end of the place that they desired to consecrate, **the inner** thanks-offering would **be eaten and the outer one** would **be burned. Granted, according to the one who says** that the two thanks-offerings moved forward **one behind the other,** and that the inner one is the one that was in the rear, it is **due to this** that **the inner one is eaten, because the outer one came before it and consecrated** the additional area, so that it is now a sanctified place fit for the eating of the thanks-offering loaves.

Under the view of *zo achar zo* — single file — an elegant theory emerges to explain the *halacha*. If the first *todah* enters the space that is not yet sanctified, it thereby sanctifies the space through its entry. The second *todah*, coming after, enters into already-sanctified space and therefore may be eaten. The outer *todah* — the one that entered first — entered non-sanctified space and is therefore burned. This is a satisfying explanation, as it gives logical meaning to the distinction between eaten and burned.

### Theory Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ, תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָא מְיקַדְּשִׁי! וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ אַחַר זוֹ, חֲדָא מִי מְיקַדְּשָׁא?! הָא ״כׇּל שֶׁלֹּא נַעֲשֵׂית בְּכׇל אֵלּוּ״ תְּנַן! וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״בְּאַחַת מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ״, הָנֵי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ חֲדָא מִצְוָה הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that the two thanks-offerings moved forward **one alongside the other, the two of them together consecrated** the additional area. Why, then, is the inner one eaten and the outer one burned? The Gemara asks: **And according to your reasoning, according to the one who says** that the two offerings moved forward **one behind the other, does one** offering by itself **consecrate** the area? **Didn't we learn** in the mishna that with regard to **any** addition **that was not made with all these** ceremonial procedures, the addition is not consecrated? **And even according to the one who says** (see 16a) that the correct reading of the mishna is: With regard to any addition that was not made **with** any **one of all these** ceremonial procedures, the addition is not consecrated, nevertheless, **these two** thanks-offerings **are one mitzva,** and one without the other would not consecrate the area.

However, the *gemara* dismantles this theory on both fronts. Under *zo k'neged zo* — if the two *todos* go abreast — *tarvayhu ba-hadi hadadi kamkadshi* — they both enter simultaneously and sanctify together, so there is no basis to distinguish between them. Yet the theory also fails under *zo achar zo*, because the *gemara* invokes the principle: *ha-kol she-lo na'aseit be-chuloh* — the sanctification requires the entire composite act. No single element of the procession — not one of the two *todos* alone — is sufficient to sanctify the space. *Ve-afilu le-man d'amar be-achat mi-kol elu* — even according to the view that perhaps only one component is required, *hani tarvayhu chad mitzvah hi* — these two *todos* together constitute a single component. They work as a unit. Therefore, even in single file, the first *todah* does not independently sanctify anything; the full sanctification only occurs once both have passed through. If they work together, both are entering unsanctified space, and the theory of "first sanctifies for second" collapses.

### Teiku Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַל פִּי נָבִיא נֶאֱכֶלֶת וְעַל פִּי נָבִיא נִשְׂרֶפֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** **Based on a ruling of a prophet** the loaves of one thanks-offering **were eaten, and based on a ruling of a prophet** the loaves of the other thanks-offering **were burned.** The prophets Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi, who lived at the time of Ezra and Nehemiah's reconsecration of Jerusalem, instructed the people concerning how the ceremony should be conducted, without providing reasons for the procedures.

The *gemara* therefore concludes with Rabbi Yochanan's statement: *al pi navi ne'echelet, al pi navi nisrefet* — one is eaten and one is burned by prophetic instruction, not by any internal logic. Rashi identifies the *nevi'im* in question as Chagai, Zecharyah, and Malachi, who were present and through whom the *halacha* was transmitted *be-ruach ha-kodesh* — *ve-ein ta'am ba-davar* — and there is no logical explanation for it. The theory was compelling, but *le-ma'aseh* it does not hold: whether the *todos* go abreast or single file, the *halacha* that the inner one is eaten and the outer one is burned is an arbitrary transmitted tradition, established by prophetic authority, not derivable from the mechanics of sanctification.
