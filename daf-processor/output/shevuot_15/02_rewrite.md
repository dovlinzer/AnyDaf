# Shevuot 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yediya Requirement

### Babylonian in Eretz Yisr…

The *gemara* opens with a question posed by Rabbi Yirmiyah: *ben Bavel she-ala le-Eretz Yisrael ve-ne'elam mimeno mekom hamikdash* — what is the status of a Babylonian who traveled to Eretz Yisrael but never actually knew where the *mikdash* was located? The background to this question lies in the general framework of *yediya bas-tchilah*: in order to incur liability for entering the *mikdash* in a state of *tumah*, one must have had initial knowledge — both awareness of one's *tumah* and awareness of the *mikdash* — and then subsequently forgotten one of those components. The question here is whether this Babylonian ever possessed the requisite *yediya bas-tchilah* to begin with, given that he never knew the precise location of the *makom hamikdash*.

He may well have known that there is a concept of a *mikdash* — that is, he knew the *mikdash* existed in principle — but he never knew the *mekom hamikdash* with sufficient concreteness to know what to avoid. This scenario is, in a sense, applicable more broadly: one might know that *Har HaBayit* exists somewhere, yet not know exactly which ground one is standing on, especially when so much has been destroyed.

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

The *gemara* immediately considers whether the question is even meaningful according to each of the Tannaitic positions. *Ei aliba de-Rebbe Akiva de-ba'ei yediya bas-tchilah* — according to Rabbi Akiva, who requires *yediya bas-tchilah*, one is not liable for *he'elam mikdash* at all; liability attaches only to *he'elam tumah*. If the entire case is constructed around the question of whether there was adequate *yediya* at the outset regarding the *mikdash*, the question is moot under Rabbi Akiva's framework, since Rabbi Akiva does not hold one liable for forgetting the *mikdash* in the first place.

### Rabbi Yishmael's Position

Conversely, according to Rabbi Yishmael — *de-mechayev al he'elam mikdash* — the *he'elam mikdash* is indeed a basis for liability. The Babylonian clearly had *he'elam mikdash* when he walked into the *makom hamikdash* without knowing he was doing so. But Rabbi Yishmael does not require *yediya bas-tchilah* at all, so the absence of prior specific knowledge of the location poses no difficulty. Either way, the question seems to dissolve: according to Rabbi Akiva you are not liable regardless, and according to Rabbi Yishmael you are liable regardless.

### Rabbi's Framework

The *gemara* therefore concludes that the question must be asked *aliba de-Rebbe* — according to Rabbi, who holds both that *yediya bas-tchilah* is required and that one is liable for *he'elam mikdash*. Under this framework, the question becomes genuinely sharp: there was clearly *he'elam mikdash* when the Babylonian entered — he did not know he was in the *makom hamikdash* — but did he ever have the initial *yediya* that is required? If the very *he'elam* consists in never having known the location at all, what is the *hava amina* that there was ever a *yediya bas-tchilah*?

### Yediya Beis Rabo

The answer lies in another principle associated with Rabbi: *yediya bei rabbo shemah yediya* — knowledge acquired from one's teacher constitutes *yediya*, even when it is no longer consciously at the forefront of one's awareness. Rashi clarifies what this means in practice. It is not simply that one once learned something twenty years ago and has entirely forgotten it. Rather, Rashi describes a state in which the knowledge is still somewhere in one's awareness — one would acknowledge, if asked, that there is such a concept as *tumas sheretz* — yet when one actually touched the *sheretz*, one simply did not connect the dots. The knowledge existed but was not activated. That latent, background awareness is what Rabbi considers *yediya bas-tchilah*.

### Mikdash Knowledge Standa…

Applying this principle to the case at hand: *keivan d'yada de-ika mikdash be-olam, yediya hi* — since the Babylonian knew that there exists such a thing as a *beis hamikdash*, perhaps that general awareness constitutes *yediya* in the same way that knowing there is such a thing as *sheratzim* does. One might never have translated that knowledge into a specific awareness of which mountain to avoid, yet the conceptual knowledge was there.

On the other hand, the *gemara* entertains the opposing view: *keivan d'bimkomo lo yada lei* — since he never knew the physical location, it was in principle impossible for him to connect the dots. In the *sheretz* case, the background awareness could in theory be activated — one knew what the eight *sheratzim* were and could, had one paused, have recognized the animal in front of them as one of them. Here, by contrast, even if one recalled everything one ever knew about the *mikdash*, one still could not have concluded "I should avoid this mountain," because one never knew which mountain it was. There is thus no way to bridge from general conceptual awareness to concrete application, and perhaps that means the person was always, in effect, in a state of *he'elam*.

### Teiku - Unresolved

The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved — *teiku*.

## Sanctified Spaces

### Three Levels

The *gemara* now transitions to the next part of the *mishnah*, which turns from the parameters of *yediya* to the question of what space actually carries the *kedusha* of the *mikdash*. Liability for entering *tamei* attaches only to a *makom hamikdash* that was sanctified through the proper ritual. With this in mind, the *gemara* addresses how one expands the sanctified boundaries of *Yerushalayim* and the *azarah* — questions of considerable ongoing relevance, since *kedushat Yerushalayim* determines, among other things, where *kodshim kalim* may be eaten, and *kedushat azarah* determines where *kodshei kodashim* may be consumed and where one incurs liability for entering in a state of *tumah*.

To orient the discussion: the innermost space — the *heichal* and *kodesh hakodashim* — is the *azarat kohanim* together with the *ezrat Yisrael*. All of this constitutes the *azarah*, the area in which one who is *tamei* incurs *kares*. The *ezrat nashim*, though part of *Har HaBayit*, does not carry *kedushat azarah* in this sense; it has only *kedushat Har HaBayit*.

### Expansion Requirements

The *mishnah* states: *she'ein mosifin al ha-ir ve-al ha-azarot ela be-melech ve-navi be-urim ve-tumim ve-sanhedrin shivim ve-echad* — one may expand the sanctified boundaries of *Yerushalayim* or the *azarah* only through the participation of a king, a *navi*, the *Urim V'Tumim* via the *kohen gadol*, and a *sanhedrin* of seventy-one. The expansion also requires two *todah* offerings, *shirah*, and a procession in which the *beis din* and all of Israel follow behind the offerings as they walk around the space being sanctified.

### Three Camps

The framework of the three *machanot* — *machaneh shechinah*, *machaneh levayah*, and *machaneh Yisrael* — corresponds to the spatial arrangement of the *midbar* and maps onto the areas of the *mikdash*. The *azarah* proper corresponds to *machaneh shechinah*. *Har HaBayit*, including the *ezrat nashim*, corresponds to *machaneh levayah*. *Yerushalayim* as a whole constitutes *machaneh Yisrael*.

## Vechen Ta'asu

### Source from Torah

The *gemara* asks: *mena hani mili* — where does the Torah source for this sanctification procedure come from? Rav Shimi bar Chiyya derives it from the verse at the end of the command to build the *mishkan*: *kechol asher ani mareh otcha et tavnit hamishkan ve-et tavnit kol kelav ve-chen ta'asu* — "as all that I show you, the form of the *mishkan* and the form of all its vessels, so shall you do." The phrase *ve-chen ta'asu* appears somewhat superfluous in context. The *gemara* therefore reads it as a directive to future generations: the ability to sanctify sacred space is not a one-time event but a recurring capacity — *l'dorot*, for future generations.

### Rashi on Future Sanctifi…

Rashi notes that *ve-chen ta'asu kra yateira l'midrash l'dorot* — it is a superfluous verse to be expounded for future generations. One might observe that the verse speaks nowhere about sanctifying a *space*; the surrounding context is entirely about the construction of the *mishkan* and its vessels. And yet this is the source we work with. Additionally, we learn the specific components of the ritual — the participation of the king, *navi*, *Urim V'Tumim*, and *sanhedrin* — partly from the parallel to the components present in Moshe's time: Moshe himself was king and *navi*, Aharon was *kohen gadol* with the *Urim V'Tumim*, and there were seventy elders.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava raises a challenge. If *ve-chen ta'asu* means that future generations can replicate what was done in Moshe's time, the most natural application is to the sanctification of the *klei hamikdash* — the sacred vessels. Yet the established *halacha* is: *kol hakelim she-asah Moshe meshichatan mekadeshatan* — all the vessels Moshe made were sanctified through anointing with *shemen hamishchah*; *mikan ve-eilach avodatan mechanachtan* — from that point forward, vessels are initiated through use in service. If *ve-chen ta'asu* teaches that future generations should replicate the original practice, why do we not continue to use *shemen hamishchah* to sanctify new vessels?

### Otam Limitation

The resolution lies in a careful reading of two verses. On the one hand, the verse states *asher yeshartu bam bakodesh* — "which they shall use in the sanctuary" — teaching that the vessels are sanctified through service. On the other hand, the verse states *vayimshachem vayikadesh otam* — "he anointed them and sanctified them" — the word *otam* acting as a limiting term. The *gemara* works through the logic: had the Torah written only *otam* without *asher yeshartu bam bakodesh*, one might have said the original vessels required anointing, and future vessels would require both anointing and use — since *ve-chen ta'asu* would extend the anointing requirement forward. Had the Torah written only *asher yeshartu* without *otam*, one would apply *ve-chen ta'asu* to require anointing in future generations as well.

Now that both verses exist: *asher yeshartu* establishes that there is an alternative mode of sanctification through use alone; *otam* limits the anointing requirement exclusively to the original vessels, teaching that *osam bemeshichah ve-lo ledoros bemeshichah* — those original vessels with anointing, but not future vessels with anointing. And *ve-chen ta'asu* is therefore redirected: it teaches not about replicating the method of sanctifying vessels, but about the capacity of future generations to carry out the broader sanctification of sacred *space*, replicating the components that were present in Moshe's time.

## Todah Procession

### Nehemiah Source

The *gemara* cites a *tanna*: *shtei todos she-amru belachman ve-lo bivsaran* — the two *todah* offerings mentioned in this context are used for their bread components, not for the flesh of the offering itself. The flesh of the *todah* is brought as well — indeed, the bread can only accompany a *todah* offering — but what is carried through the procession and marks the boundary of the sanctified space is the bread.

Where does the *gemara* derive the requirement for two *todot* and the procession at all? Rav Shizbi cites the verse from Nechemiah: *va-amida shtei todos gedolos ve-tahaluchot liyamin me-al hachomah* — "and I stationed two great thanksgiving-offerings and processions to the right, above the wall."

### Nehemiah Full Text

The full context of this passage from Nechemiah is worth examining, as it provides the narrative backdrop for the entire halachic discussion. The verse opens: *u-be-chanukat chomat Yerushalayim bikshu et ha-leviyim mikol mekomosam lehaviam la-asot chanukah be-simchah u-ve-todos u-ve-shir u-ve-mitzaltayim nevalim u-ve-chinnoros* — "at the dedication of the wall of Jerusalem they sought out the Levites from all their places to bring them to Jerusalem, to perform the dedication with joy, and with todot, and with song, and with cymbals, harps, and lyres." Here one appreciates something beyond the bare halachic framework: there was genuine joy in this moment, the sense that an era was opening, that the wall was being inaugurated as a prelude to the restoration of the *beis hamikdash*. The *todah* was not merely a ritual instrument for boundary-marking — it expressed genuine thanksgiving.

The passage continues describing how the officers of Judah went up onto the wall, and the two great thanksgiving-offerings and their processions went to the right; following them was Hoshaya with half the officers of Judah, along with Ezra, Meshullam, Yehudah, Binyamin, Shemaya, and Yirmiyahu. Later in the passage: *ve-ha-todah ha-shenis ha-holachet le-mul va-ani achareiha va-chetzi ha-am me-al ha-chomah* — the second thanksgiving-offering going to the opposite side, with half the people following above the wall. And *ve-chol Yisrael achareihem* — all of Israel after them.

This is, incidentally, why the *todah* is the appropriate offering for marking the boundaries of *Yerushalayim*: a *todah* is a *kodshim kalim* that may be eaten anywhere within *Yerushalayim*. By bringing the *todah* to the outermost boundary of the area being sanctified, one is effectively declaring — this is the furthest point where a *todah* may be eaten. The offering demarcates its own reach. It is analogous, in a sense, to the way one lights *Shabbos* candles just before *Shabbos* begins: the act at the boundary signals the transition.

### Two Todahs

The *gemara* asks: *mai gedolos* — what does the verse mean in calling these *todos* "great"? Perhaps it means they were from a large species — *parim*, cattle rather than sheep or goats? But a *todah* cannot be brought from cattle, so that cannot be the meaning. Perhaps it simply means large animals of whatever species? The *gemara* rejects this as well, citing the *baraisa*: the Torah uses the same phrase *ishsheh reiach nichoach* — "a pleasing aroma" — for an *olas behemah*, an *olas ha-of*, and a *minchah* alike. *Melamed she-echad ha-marbe ve-echad ha-mam'it u-vilvad she-yechaven libo la-shamayim* — whether one brings much or little, what matters is that one's heart is directed toward Heaven. So God is not more impressed by a large animal than a small one.

*Ela gedola she-ba-todah* — the word "great" refers to something larger within the *todah* itself. Within the *todah's* bread components, what is larger? *Chametz*. The *todah* is accompanied by forty loaves: ten of *chametz* and thirty of *matzah*, all made from the same total quantity of grain. Since the grain is distributed equally between ten *chametz* loaves and thirty *matzah* loaves, each *chametz* loaf is three times the size of each *matzah* loaf. The verse thus indicates that it is specifically the *chametz* bread of the *todah* that is used in the procession — the larger component.

To state this precisely: the *todah* came from five Jerusalemite *se'im*, equivalent to six *midbar se'im*, which is two *eifot*. An *eifah* contains three *se'ah*, and there are ten *isaronot* — tenths — in an *eifah*, making twenty *isaronot* in total. Ten were devoted to *chametz* and ten to *matzah*. The *matzah* was divided into three varieties — *chalot*, *rekikin*, and *revuchah* — while the *chametz* was of only one type. Consequently, each *chametz* loaf was three times the size of any individual *matzah* loaf. That is the *gedolos* of the verse: the *chametz* loaves of the *todah*.

### Mincha Instead

Rami bar Chama then introduces an important qualification: *ein zo mitkadeshet ela bi-shiyarei mincha* — the sanctification of the *azarah* is not accomplished with a *todah* but with the leftover portion of a *minchah*. The *mishnah* might give the impression that the two *todos* are used for both *Yerushalayim* and the *azarah*, but Rami bar Chama draws a distinction. For *Yerushalayim*, the *todah* is appropriate because *Yerushalayim* is the outer boundary of where a *todah* may be eaten; bringing it to the edge of the sanctified space marks that boundary. For the *azarah*, one would want something that corresponds to the *azarah's* own outer boundary of consumption — something that becomes *pasul* if it exits the *azarah*. The *shiyarei minchah* — the portion of the *minchah* consumed by the *kohanim* — fits this criterion: it must be eaten within the *azarah* and is invalidated upon leaving it.

The *gemara* presses: could one not use *lachmei todah* for the *azarah* as well, since *lachmei todah* may also be eaten within the *azarah*? The *gemara* refines the principle: the point is not merely that the food *can* be eaten in that space, but that the space constitutes the *outer boundary* of where it may be eaten — *davar hane'echal bah ve-yotzei mimena nifsal*. The bread of a *todah* that exits *Yerushalayim* is *nifsal*; the *shiyarei minchah* that exits the *azarah* is *nifsal*. Each offering thus traces the outermost edge of its respective sanctified zone.

This prompts a further question: since we are maintaining the parallel between *Yerushalayim* and the *azarah*, and in *Yerushalayim* we used *chametz* bread specifically, should we not similarly seek *chametz* for the *azarah* procession? The *gemara* acknowledges the appeal of the parallel but points out that there is no such thing as a *minchat chametz* — all *minchot* are *matzah*. One might suggest leavening the *shiyarei minchah* after the fact, or adding water to an unbaked *minchah*, but Reish Lakish establishes that even the *kohanim's* portion of the *minchah* may not be eaten as *chametz*, based on the verse *lo te'afeh chametz chelkam* — their portion shall not be baked as *chametz*.

One creative alternative is considered: the *shtei halechem* of *Shavuot* — the only communal bread-offering that is brought as *chametz*, whose bread is classified as *kodshei kodashim* and may be eaten only within the *azarah*. But the *gemara* dismantles each possible scenario for utilizing this. One cannot sanctify the *azarah* on *Shavuot* itself because the *shtei halechem* are not sanctified until the animal of the *todah* is slaughtered, and one must sanctify the space on the day the construction is completed — learned from the verse *be-yom hakim Moshe et hamishkan*, the day Moshe erected the *mishkan* he sanctified it. One cannot finish building on *Erev Yom Tov* and sanctify on *Yom Tov* because *ein binyan beis hamikdash docheh Yom Tov* — building the *mikdash* does not override *Yom Tov*. One cannot leave the bread overnight for use on the day after *Yom Tov* because the bread becomes *pasul* through *linah* — remaining overnight. One cannot finish building at *motzei Yom Tov* and use the bread in the remaining valid nighttime hours because *ein binyan beis hamikdash ba-laylah* — building the *mikdash* may only be done during the day, as derived from *be-yom hakim Moshe et hamishkan* — *be-yom*, by day, and not at night.

The conclusion is thus that it is practically impossible to use *chametz* bread for the *azarah* sanctification procession. One works with *shiyarei minchah* — which is *matzah* — and that is the best available parallel to the *chametz* of the *todah* used for *Yerushalayim*.

## Music & Psalms

### Shirah of Todah

The *gemara* cites a *baraisa* on the *shirah* — the singing — that accompanies the procession: *shir shel todah be-chinnoros u-ve-nevalim u-ve-tziltzalim al kol pina u-fina* — with harps, lyres, and cymbals at every corner, *ve-al kol even gedola she-bi-Yerushalayim* — and at every great stone in Jerusalem. Whether this means musical instrumentalists were positioned at each corner and large stone or that song was performed each time the procession passed such a point, the text leaves somewhat open. In either reading, the musical accompaniment is extensive and tied to the physical features of the space being sanctified.

### Three Psalms

At each such station, *omer: aromimcha Hashem ki dilisani* — one recites the opening of Psalm 30, *mizmor shir chanukas habayit*. A striking coincidence — or providence — that this *daf* is studied on the first day of Chanukah, the very day one might expect to encounter *mizmor shir chanukas habayit*.

The *baraisa* then introduces *shir shel pegaim* — there is a view that *yoshev be-seter elyon*, Psalm 91, was composed by Moshe on the occasion of the erection of the *mishkan*, since Moshe blessed Israel with *yehi ratzon she-tishre shechinah be-ma'asei yedichem*, and Psalm 91 concludes *vi-yehi noam Hashem Elokeinu aleinu u-ma'asei yadeinu konena aleinu* — may God favor the work of our hands. The connection between the dedication of a sanctified space and the prayer that God's presence rest upon it is exact.

A second view holds that the relevant psalm is *shir shel negaim*, understood to refer to Psalm 91's verse *ve-nega lo yikrav be-oholecha* — no affliction shall approach your tent. So three compositions emerge: *mizmor shir chanukas habayit*, dedicating the house; *yehi noam*, invoking God's desire for the work of our hands; and a psalm of protection, that no enemy or affliction should come near. *Mizmor l'David be-vorcho mi-penei Avshalom beno, Hashem ma rabu tzarai* is also recited — a *mizmor* born of persecution and trust — until *la-Hashem ha-yeshuah al amcha birchatecha selah*.

### Evening Recitation

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi *amar lehu l'hani karai ve-gani* — he would recite these verses and then go to sleep, using them for nighttime protection. The *gemara* asks: *heichi avid hachi* — how could he do this? For Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi himself said *assur le-hitrape'os be-divrei Torah* — it is forbidden to use words of Torah as a healing incantation. The *gemara* answers: *le-hagen shani* — protection is different from healing. Saying these *pesukim* before sleep is not a magical use of Torah text; it is a *tefillah*, a prayer asking God to guard one through the night. The parallel to *Krias Shema al ha-mitah* is exact: the *yoshev be-seter elyon* recited as part of the bedtime *Shema* and *Hashem ma rabu tzarai* that follows it are precisely these texts used in this protective function.

### Healing Dispute

The distinction between permissible and impermissible use of *pesukim* is then sharpened. The *mishnah* states that *ha-lochesh al ha-makkah ein lo chelek le-olam ha-ba* — one who whispers over a wound has no portion in the world to come. This is far more severe than a mere prohibition. The *gemara* reconciles: Rabbi Yochanan clarifies that the extreme penalty of *ein lo chelek le-olam ha-ba* applies specifically to one who spits on the wound while reciting the verse — *be-rokek shanu* — since one thereby invokes God's name over the act of spitting, which is disrespectful of the divine name. There is also the dimension that spitting is part of a recognized magical-healing ritual, making the verse into a talisman.

What emerges is a spectrum. Reciting *pesukim* before sleep as a prayer for protection — entirely permissible, even commendable. Reciting *pesukim* over a wound without spitting — forbidden, though without the extreme consequence. Reciting *pesukim* over a wound with spitting — *ein lo chelek le-olam ha-ba*, because one is both using Torah as a magical incantation and associating God's name with an act of spitting. The gradations reflect how closely the act resembles a *tefillah* versus a magical procedure.

## Procession Order

### Order Question

The *gemara* returns to the mechanics of the procession. The *mishnah* states: *beis din mehalechim u-shtei todos achareihem* — the *beis din* goes, and the two *todos* go after them. This implies the *beis din* leads. Yet the verse in Nechemiah states *va-yelech achareihem Hoshaya va-chetzi sarei Yehuda* — Hoshaya and the officers of Judah, who represent the *beis din*, went *after* — implying the *todos* led and the *beis din* followed. The *gemara* therefore re-reads the *mishnah*: its words should be understood not as "the *beis din* goes and the *todos* follow," but rather as presenting two alternatives — either the *beis din* goes with the *todos* following, or the *todos* go with the *beis din* following. The plain reading of the *mishnah* is thus substantially reinterpreted to align with Nechemiah, where the offerings led and the leadership followed.

### Two Arrangements

Having established that the *todos* go first and the *beis din* follows, the *gemara* asks: *keitzad mehalechot* — in what arrangement did the two *todos* proceed? Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi disagree. One holds *zo k'neged zo* — they walked two abreast, side by side. The other holds *zo achar zo* — they walked in single file. The question of inner and outer follows from this: if abreast, the inner *todah* is the one closest to the wall; if single file, the inner *todah* is the one closest to the *beis din* behind it.

### Inner vs. Outer

The *mishnah* states: *ha-pnimit ne'echelet ve-ha-chitzonit nisrefet* — the inner one is eaten and the outer one is burned. This *halacha* generates the question of which is which under each arrangement.

### Sanctification Theory

Under the view of *zo achar zo* — single file — an elegant theory emerges to explain the *halacha*. If the first *todah* enters the space that is not yet sanctified, it thereby sanctifies the space through its entry. The second *todah*, coming after, enters into already-sanctified space and therefore may be eaten. The outer *todah* — the one that entered first — entered non-sanctified space and is therefore burned. This is a satisfying explanation, as it gives logical meaning to the distinction between eaten and burned.

### Theory Problem

However, the *gemara* dismantles this theory on both fronts. Under *zo k'neged zo* — if the two *todos* go abreast — *tarvayhu ba-hadi hadadi kamkadshi* — they both enter simultaneously and sanctify together, so there is no basis to distinguish between them. Yet the theory also fails under *zo achar zo*, because the *gemara* invokes the principle: *ha-kol she-lo na'aseit be-chuloh* — the sanctification requires the entire composite act. No single element of the procession — not one of the two *todos* alone — is sufficient to sanctify the space. *Ve-afilu le-man de-amar be-achat mi-kol elu* — even according to the view that perhaps only one component is required, *hani tarvayhu chad mitzvah hi* — these two *todos* together constitute a single component. They work as a unit. Therefore, even in single file, the first *todah* does not independently sanctify anything; the full sanctification only occurs once both have passed through. If they work together, both are entering unsanctified space, and the theory of "first sanctifies for second" collapses.

### Teiku Resolution

The *gemara* therefore concludes with Rabbi Yochanan's statement: *al pi navi ne'echelet, al pi navi nisrefet* — one is eaten and one is burned by prophetic instruction, not by any internal logic. Rashi identifies the *nevi'im* in question as Chagai, Zecharyah, and Malachi, who were present and through whom the *halacha* was transmitted *be-ruach ha-kodesh* — *ve-ein ta'am ba-davar* — and there is no logical explanation for it. The theory was compelling, but *le-ma'aseh* it does not hold: whether the *todos* go abreast or single file, the *halacha* that the inner one is eaten and the outer one is burned is an arbitrary transmitted tradition, established by prophetic authority, not derivable from the mechanics of sanctification.
