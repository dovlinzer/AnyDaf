# Bava Batra 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Structure & Themes

### Seder Nezikin Overview

We begin *Bava Batra* today, the last of the three *masechtot* that make up *Seder Nezikin* — though what we now call three tractates was originally a single *masechet Nezikin*, thirty *perakim* long, broken into three sections of ten each. *Bava Batra* simply means "the last gate," and there are indeed tractates named *Bava Kamma*, *Bava Metzia*, and *Bava Batra* also within *Kelim* in *Taharos*, which likewise contains thirty *perakim*. The *gemara* debates whether to treat these as one *masechet* or three, but it seems clear that initially the material was conceptualized as a single very long tractate with three distinct parts.

### Three Masechtot Structure

In terms of identity, *Bava Kamma* and *Bava Batra* have a clearer thematic character than *Bava Metzia* — perhaps the inevitable lot of the middle. *Bava Kamma* was entirely about torts: animals that damage, property that causes damage, people who injure other people or destroy property, theft, and burglary. *Bava Batra*, by contrast, fits largely within what the Rambam categorizes as *kinyan* — matters of acquisition — though its opening *perakim* deal with *hilchos shecheinim*, the laws governing neighbors.

### Thematic Organization

The final chapter of *Bava Metzia* served as a lead-in to the themes we now take up: what I may do on my property, how carefully I must ensure that my activities do not impinge on yours, and the distances required to keep certain damaging activities away from a neighbor's domain. The opening *perek* of *Bava Batra*, *HaShutafin*, concerns what happens when parties attempt to divide shared property. The second *perek* continues the theme of neighbors. Then comes the third *perek*, *Chazkat HaBatim* — the one classically studied in *yeshivos* — dealing with the principle that three years of unprotested presence on someone else's property can serve as evidence of a prior purchase, even without documentation. As the Gemara explains, if no one protested for three years, one may plausibly claim that documentation need no longer be preserved. Notably, if one openly admits to having no purchase agreement, three years of presence alone does not confer ownership — a point that is frequently confused.

From there the tractate turns from neighbor-law to questions of ownership and transaction. The fourth, fifth, and sixth *perakim* address the laws of sales: *HaMocher et HaBayit* (what is implicitly included when one sells a house), *HaMocher et HaSefinah* (what is implicit in the sale of a boat), and *HaMocher Peirot* (selling fruit, honest weights and measures, and liability when goods spoil). The final three *perakim* — *Yesh Nochalim*, *Mi she'meit*, and *Get Pashut* — deal with death and the transfer of property: the order of inheritance, deathbed bequests, and wills. Thus the tractate flows from neighbor-law through the laws of buying and selling to the transfer of ownership through death, a coherent arc across all ten *perakim*.

Looking ahead, the next *masechet* is *Sanhedrin*, followed by *Makkot*. One can think of the thirty-*perek* *masechet* of *Nezikin* as laying out the civil law — the rules governing property and interaction between people — while *Sanhedrin* establishes the court system that adjudicates it.

### Pedagogical Challenges

One interesting feature of *Bava Batra* is that almost none of its content can be anchored directly to a *pasuk* in the *Torah*. When we reach *Yesh Nochalim*, there is indeed a scriptural basis for the order of inheritance — though that *perek* is immediately followed by the laws of deathbed bequests that in many ways subvert that order. There is also some connection to *Torah* law in the provisions about honest weights and measures as they arise in the context of *mechirah*. But the bulk of this *masechet* represents *Chazal* legislating the ordering of society entirely on their own authority — not as interpretation of a *mitzvah*, and not merely as a safeguard, but as substantive, original law. For some learners, this is precisely what makes it exciting: it demonstrates the creative legislative power of *Chazal* acting in their own right. For others, the absence of a running conversation with a *pasuk* or *mitzvah* of the *Torah* makes for a less engaging experience.

### Rashi Ends, Rashbam Begi…

The density of *Bava Batra* is also shaped by its unique commentary situation. At the beginning of the third *perek* — specifically at 29a — Rashi's commentary ends and the Rashbam takes over. Right in the middle of the Rashi column one finds the notation *ad kan peirash Rashi zatzal*, and then *mikan ve'eilach peirash Rabbeinu Shmuel bar Meir*, the Rashbam, who was Rashi's grandson. An earlier printed edition, it is noted in the margin, actually read *nidpas kan met Rashi* — "printed here: Rashi died" — though this is not to be taken literally as meaning he died mid-composition of this tractate. There is extensive scholarship on the multiple editions of Rashi's *gemara* commentary, which is why the Rashi found in the Rif does not always match the Rashi on the *gemara* — the former is likely an earlier edition. In other *masechtot*, what appears as Rashi is actually the Rivan or another rishon. In any case, something about the state of Rashi's commentary to *Bava Batra* remains incomplete or unrevised.

The Rashbam is a fascinating and somewhat paradoxical commentator. On the *Torah*, he is even terser than Rashi, deeply oriented toward *pshat*. In the context of the *gemara*, however, he was among the first generation of the *Baalei HaTosafot* — the older brother of Rabbeinu Tam, who became the central driving force of the *tosafot* enterprise. The *tosafot* method is not simply to explain what a line of *gemara* means, smoothing over occasional difficulties, but constantly to compare it with other *sugyot*, working through all the tensions that arise when one looks across the tractates. The Rashbam blends Rashi and *Tosafot* in a way that, while intellectually rich, creates a distinctive pedagogical challenge: one wants a clear *pshat* of the *gemara* before the problematizing begins, but here the *pshat* and the cross-tractate complications arrive simultaneously. The practical effect is also that every page in the latter part of *Bava Batra* carries two major commentaries — *Tosafot* and Rashbam — making the individual *dapim* shorter.

### Word & Daf Analysis

*Bava Batra* is the longest tractate in *dapim*, running to 175 *daf*, yet the number of words per page is relatively low — in part because of the dense, thick *sugyot* that generate large *Tosafot*, in part because of the dual presence of *Tosafot* and Rashbam. There is a well-known story that the Vilna Gaon was asked which tractate contains the most words: he reportedly answered that if one takes everything as printed, it is *Berachos*; if one factors in the variant readings he himself and the Bach would add, it is *Shabbos*; and if one considers what he would add and the Bach would remove, it is *Bava Batra* — encompassing all possibilities across the spectrum of textual variants. A computer search could easily resolve this today, but regardless, while *Bava Batra* is certainly the longest tractate in *dapim*, it is genuinely uncertain whether it is the longest in terms of raw word count. For *daf yomi* study, this means there are somewhat fewer words to cover per sitting — leaving more opportunity, perhaps, to explore depth rather than merely to traverse the surface.

## Mishnah: Wall Division

### Partners & Shared Yard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוּתָּפִין שֶׁרָצוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת מְחִיצָה בְּחָצֵר – בּוֹנִין אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל בְּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Partners who wished to make a partition [*meḥitza*] in** a jointly owned **courtyard build the wall** for the partition **in the middle** of the courtyard.

The *mishnah* opens: *hashutafim* — partners who jointly own a *chatzer*, a yard. The scenario is that two people each have homes opening onto a shared yard, and they now wish to divide it and, in the course of that division, erect a wall separating their respective portions. The Mishnah's opening word — *sheratzu la'asos mechitza be'chatzer*, "they agreed to make a *mechitza* in the yard" — already raises a question: does *mechitza* here mean a noun (a physical wall) or a concept (a division)? That ambiguity will occupy the *gemara* at length, but for now we continue.

### Wall in Middle

The *mishnah* continues: *bonim et hakotel be'emtza* — they build the wall in the middle. This does not mean they divide the yard geometrically down the center. What *be'emtza* means is that wherever the property line runs between the two parties, the wall's thickness straddles that line equally — half of the wall's footprint on one person's side, half on the other's. The underlying division of the yard may reflect different proportions of ownership, but the wall itself sits symmetrically across whatever that dividing line turns out to be.

### Keminhag Hamedina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִבְנוֹת גְּוִיל, גָּזִית, כְּפִיסִין, לְבֵינִין – בּוֹנִין; הַכֹּל כְּמִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** What is this wall fashioned from? In **a place where it is customary to build** such a wall with **non-chiseled stone [*gevil*],** or **chiseled stone [*gazit*],** or **small bricks [*kefisin*],** or **large bricks [*leveinim*], they must build** the wall with that material. **Everything is in accordance with the regional custom.**

The *mishnah* then states: *makom shenahagu livnot gevil gazis kfisim leveinim, bonim* — in a place where the community standard is to build with a particular type of material, one builds accordingly. This principle — *hakol keminhag hamedina*, everything follows the local custom — governs not only the choice of building materials but also the thickness of the wall, and consequently how much of each party's yard is consumed by the wall's footprint. If one agreed to build a wall and the local standard was a particular material, the implicit understanding of the agreement is shaped by that norm, unless explicitly stipulated otherwise.

### Building Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּוִיל – זֶה נוֹתֵן שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים, וְזֶה נוֹתֵן שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים. בַּגָּזִית – זֶה נוֹתֵן טִפְחַיִים וּמֶחֱצָה וְזֶה נוֹתֵן טִפְחַיִים וּמֶחֱצָה. בַּכְּפִיסִין – זֶה נוֹתֵן טִפְחַיִים וְזֶה נוֹתֵן טִפְחַיִים. בִּלְבֵינִין – זֶה נוֹתֵן טֶפַח וּמֶחֱצָה וְזֶה נוֹתֵן טֶפַח וּמֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** If they build the wall with **non-chiseled stone, this** partner **provides three handbreadths** of his portion of the courtyard **and that** partner **provides three handbreadths,** since the thickness of such a wall is six handbreadths. If they build the wall with **chiseled stone, this** partner **provides two and a half handbreadths and that** partner **provides two and a half handbreadths,** since such a wall is five handbreadths thick. If they build the wall **with small bricks, this** one **provides two handbreadths and that** one **provides two handbreadths,** since the thickness of such a wall is four handbreadths. If they build **with large bricks, this** one **provides one and a half handbreadths and that** one **provides one and a half handbreadths,** since the thickness of such a wall is three handbreadths.

The *mishnah* then specifies the materials and their corresponding thicknesses. *Gevil* — unsmoothed, uncarved stone — requires a wall six *tfachim* wide in total, each party contributing three *tfachim* of their yard. *Gazis* — hewn, smoothed stone — requires five *tfachim* total, each contributing two and a half. *Kfisim* — half-bricks, which are bound together with plaster in the middle and therefore wider than whole bricks — require four *tfachim* total, each contributing two. *Leveinim* — whole bricks — require three *tfachim* total, each contributing one and a half. The differing widths matter practically: a party might be willing to pay for materials but unwilling to surrender more of their yard than anticipated, so the standard determines both the type of material and the spatial footprint, unless otherwise agreed.

### Presumption of Equality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ, אִם נָפַל הַכּוֹתֶל – הַמָּקוֹם וְהָאֲבָנִים שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore, if the wall** later **falls,** the assumption is that **the space** where the wall stood **and the stones belong to both of them,** to be divided equally.

From this flows a legal presumption. If a wall has stood between two properties for generations, with no record of how it was originally constructed or who contributed what, the *mishnah* establishes that we assume the standard was followed: each party contributed equally to the materials and equally to the footprint. Therefore: *lefikach im nafal hakotel, hamakom veha'avanim shel sheneihem* — if the wall falls down, both the space beneath and the stones are presumed to belong equally to both parties, in the absence of any evidence to the contrary.

### Gardens vs. Fields

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בַּגִּינָּה – מְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִגְדּוֹר – מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ. אֲבָל בַּבִּקְעָה – מְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִגְדּוֹר – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly with regard to a garden,** in **a place where it is customary to build a partition** in the middle of a garden jointly owned by two people, and one of them wishes to build such a partition, the court **obligates** his neighbor to join in building the partition. **But with regard to** an expanse of **fields [*babbika*],** in **a place where it is customary not to build a partition** between two people's fields, and one person wishes to build a partition between his field and that of his neighbor, the court **does not obligate** his neighbor to build such a partition.

The *mishnah* then extends the discussion: *vechen begina* — similarly in a garden (*gina*). There is an implicit hierarchy of privacy here. A *chatzer* — a private yard adjacent to one's home — is the space where one performs semi-private activities: resting, spending leisure time. A *gina*, a vegetable garden, occupies an intermediate position. A *bikah* — a valley or wheat field — is an open agricultural space where no expectation of privacy obtains.

The *mishnah* states: *makom shenahagu ligdor, mechayivin oto* — in a place where the norm is to build a wall (as is standard between neighboring gardens), you may *compel* the other party to build it. But *aval bivikah, makom shenahagu shelo ligdor, ein mechayivin oto* — in a field, where walls are not the norm, you cannot compel the other party. There may be some demarcating line to mark the property boundary, but there is no wall and no obligation to build one.

### Solo Building & Chazit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִם רָצָה, כּוֹנֵס לְתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וּבוֹנֶה, וְעוֹשֶׂה חֲזִית מִבַּחוּץ. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם נָפַל הַכּוֹתֶל – הַמָּקוֹם וְהָאֲבָנִים שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** one person **wishes** to erect a partition, **he must withdraw into his own** field **and build** the partition there. **And he makes** a border **mark on the outer side** of the barrier facing his neighbor's property, indicating that he built the entire structure of his own materials and on his own land. **Therefore, if the wall** later **falls,** the assumption is that **the space** where the wall stood **and the stones** belong only **to him,** as is indicated by the mark on the wall.

However, the *mishnah* continues, if one party wishes to build even in a context where it cannot be compelled — *ela im rotzeh, kones l'toch shelo uvoneh* — one may go ahead, build entirely on one's own land, at one's own expense. This raises a practical problem: the general presumption is that any standing wall is equally owned by both neighbors. How does a party who built entirely alone, at their own cost, on their own land, preserve evidence of that fact decades or generations later? The *mishnah* answers: *ve'oseh chazit mibachutz* — one makes a *chazit*, a marker, on the *outside* of the wall. The *chazit* is placed on the neighbor's side — the side the neighbor faces — so that the building party's side of the wall is finished and polished, while the outer face shows the structural marks of sole construction. This is an interesting inversion of contemporary practice, where convention requires that the finished face of a fence face the neighbor. Here, the rougher or structurally distinctive outer face serves as evidence that the wall was built entirely by the person on whose land it stands. *Lefikach im nafal hakosel, hamakom veha'avanim shelo* — therefore, if the wall falls, both the space and the stones belong entirely to the one who built it.

### Both Agree, Double Chazit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם עָשׂוּ מִדַּעַת שְׁנֵיהֶם – בּוֹנִין אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל בָּאֶמְצַע, וְעוֹשִׂין חֲזִית מִכָּאן וּמִכָּאן. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם נָפַל הַכּוֹתֶל – הַמָּקוֹם וְהָאֲבָנִים שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Nevertheless, in a place where it is not customary to build a partition between two people's fields, **if they made** such a partition **with the agreement of the two of them, they build it in the middle,** i.e., on the property line, **and make** a border **mark on the one** side **and on the other** side. **Therefore, if the wall** later **falls,** the assumption is that **the space** where the wall stood **and the stones** belong **to both of them,** to be divided equally.

The final clause addresses a different case: *im asu mida'at sheneihem* — if both parties agreed to build a wall in a place where it would not normally be built, such as a *bikah*. In that case, *bonim et hakotel be'emtza ve'osim chazit mikan umikan* — the wall is built in the middle, and a *chazit* is placed on both sides. *Lefikach im nafal hakosel, hamakom veha'avanim shel sheneihem* — if it falls, both the stones and the space belong equally to both.

This raises a natural question: why place a *chazit* on both sides rather than on neither side? In the standard *chatzer* case, no *chazit* is needed because the community norm already establishes equal ownership. Here, in a *bikah* where there is no norm, one might simply say that the absence of a *chazit* on either side signals equality. The *mishnah* nonetheless requires the double *chazit*, a point we will have reason to return to.

## Tosafot: Minhag Limits

### Tosafot Question

Before turning to the *gemara*, there is an important *tosafot* that demands attention. The *mishnah* opened with the sweeping principle *hakol keminhag hamedina* — everything is governed by local custom — and then proceeded to enumerate specific building materials and their corresponding widths. *Tosafot* asks the obvious question: if the principle truly is that everything follows local custom, why enumerate the specifics at all? Whatever the norm happens to be, that is simply the norm — why list *gevil*, *gazis*, *kfisim*, and *leveinim* with their respective measurements? One might respond that the list concretizes the abstract principle, but that answer is incomplete, and *Tosafot* presses further.

### Rabbeinu Tam Balance

Rabbeinu Tam's answer, found at the end of the *tosafot* on *gevil*, is that *minhag hamedina* does not govern without limit. *Minhag* operates within a range bounded by objective minimums and maximums. If the local practice were to use *gazis* blocks of eight *tfachim* — far beyond the standard measurement — one could not demand that. The point of specifying the particular measurements is to establish that even where *minhag* rules, it does so only within parameters set by objective norms.

### Minhag Hedyot

Conversely, at the lower end, Rabbeinu Tam writes: *venira leRabbeinu Tam dedavka behutza vedafna* — the minimum legitimate division is something like *hutza vedafna*, thin planks or similar light material. *Aval pachos mikan, afilu nahagu, minhag hedyot hu* — anything thinner than that, even if it were the local practice, is a *minhag hedyot*, a practice beneath the threshold of legitimacy. *Minhag hedyot* is perhaps a more diplomatic formulation than *minhag shtus*, but the point is the same: not every widespread practice rises to the level of a norm that can legally govern an agreement. The conclusion follows: *umuchach mikan deyesh minhagim she'ein lismoch aleihem, afilu heicha ditnan hakol keminhag hamedina* — there exist practices on which one may not rely, even where the general rule is that everything follows local custom.

### Minhag & Standards

This observation opens onto a broader conceptual point. There is an important distinction between two different uses of the word *minhag*. On one hand, *hakol keminhag hamedina* refers to the norms and standards that implicitly govern commercial and social arrangements — the background conventions that shape what parties to an agreement intend unless they explicitly stipulate otherwise. This is not quite "custom" in the sense we usually mean; it is more like the standard practice of a trade or a community that becomes, in effect, an implicit term of every relevant agreement. On the other hand, when we ordinarily speak of *minhagim* — customs like *kapparos* on *Erev Yom Kippur* or eating *milchigs* on *Shavuos* — we mean practices that, once established, carry their own normative weight and create obligations for a community or its descendants. Rabbeinu Tam, famously skeptical of *minhag* as an independent source of obligation (he is said to have noted that the letters of *minhag* are the same as those of *gehinnom*), is making a point that applies across both registers: in monetary matters, *minhag* must be bounded by objective standards, and in the realm of religious practice, *minhagim* that have no rational foundation or that conflict with *halacha* cannot simply entrench themselves by force of repetition. There must be some framework within which *minhag* operates and beyond which it cannot legitimately reach.

## Gemara: Compulsion Questions

### Mechitza: Noun or Verb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ סַבְרוּהָ מַאי ״מְחִיצָה״ – גּוּדָּא; כִּדְתַנְיָא: מְחִיצַת הַכֶּרֶם שֶׁנִּפְרְצָה, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״גְּדוֹר״. חָזְרָה וְנִפְרְצָה – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״גְּדוֹר״. נִתְיָאֵשׁ הֵימֶנָּה וְלֹא גְּדָרָהּ – הֲרֵי זֶה קִידֵּשׁ, וְחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sages initially **assumed: What** is the meaning of the term ***meḥitza*** mentioned in the mishna? It means **a partition, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: Consider the case where **a partition of [*meḥitzat*] a vineyard** which separates the vineyard from a field of grain **was breached,** resulting, if the situation is not rectified, in the grain and grapes becoming items from which deriving benefit is prohibited due to the prohibition of diverse kinds planted in a vineyard. The owner of the field of grain may **say to** the owner of the vineyard: **Build a partition** between the vineyard and the field of grain. If the owner of the vineyard did so, and the partition **was breached again,** the owner of the field of grain may **say to him** again: **Build a partition.** If the owner of the vineyard **neglected** to make the necessary repairs **and did not** properly **build a partition** between the fields, the grain and grapes are **rendered forbidden** due to the prohibition of diverse kinds planted in a vineyard, **and he is liable for the** monetary **loss.**

The *gemara* opens by noting: *savruah mai mechitza guda* — the initial assumption was that when the *mishnah* said the partners agreed to make a *mechitza*, this referred to a physical wall (*guda*). On this reading, the *mishnah* is saying: they agreed to build a wall, and here is how the wall must be built. The implication would be that building a wall requires agreement — one cannot be compelled to build a wall unilaterally.

But the *gemara* first pauses to confirm that *mechitza* is in fact a noun referring to a physical structure. The word is not so commonly used in *tannaitic* literature as to be self-evident. The *gemara* therefore turns to a *braita* for confirmation.

The *braita* cited: *mechitzat hakerem shenifretza* — a wall (*mechitza*) between a vineyard and a wheat field that has been breached. The relevance of this case lies in the laws of *kilayim*: grapevines and wheat planted in close proximity create a prohibited mixture, but a wall between them allows them to stand immediately adjacent without violation. When the wall breaks open, *kilayim* concerns arise. The *braita* states that one says to the vineyard owner *omer lo gdor* — repair the wall — because by the logic of *kilayim*, it is the vineyard that is understood to encroach: the Torah requires four *amos* of clearance for working around vines, so the vines define the spatial need, making the vineyard owner the *mazik*. The *braita* continues: if the wall falls again, you tell him again to repair it; if he despairs and does not bother — *nityaesh mimenu velo gadar* — it immediately becomes *kilayim* and he is liable for damages to the wheat field.

The critical point for our purposes is simply that *mechitza* in this *braita* unambiguously refers to a physical wall. *Tosafot* raises a separate question about whether the resulting damage might be *hezek she'eino nikkar*, but that need not detain us here.

### Agreement Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּרָצוּ, הָא לֹא רָצוּ – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ; אַלְמָא הֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה לָאו שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** According to the understanding that the term *meḥitza* means a partition, one can infer: **The reason** that they build a wall **is that they** both **wished** to make a partition in their jointly owned courtyard. **But if they did not** both **wish** to do so, the court **does not obligate** the reluctant partner to build such a wall, although his neighbor objects to the fact that the partner can see what he is doing in his courtyard. **Apparently,** it may be concluded that **damage** caused by **sight,** that is, the discomfort suffered by someone because he is exposed to the gaze of others while he is in his own private domain, **is not called damage.**

Returning to the *mishnah*: on the straightforward reading, *ta'ama deratza* — the reason a wall is built is that the parties agreed to it. *Ha lo ratza, ein mechayvin oso* — had one party not agreed, no wall could be compelled. The consequence is stark: *alma, hezek re'iyah lav shemei hezek* — evidently, damage through sight (*hezek re'iyah*) is not legally recognized as a *hezek*.

### Hezek Re'iyah Defined

It is worth pausing to clarify what *hezek re'iyah* means and does not mean. It does not refer — at least not primarily — to the metaphysical concern of *ayin hara*, the evil eye; that is a separate idea that will arise later. *Hezek re'iyah* refers to what we would call the right to privacy: if I can observe you engaged in activities in your private yard that you would naturally wish to conduct unobserved, is that considered an injury to you?

Notice that the *halachic* framing is deliberate and consequential. The discourse is not cast in terms of "rights" — your right to privacy, my right to gaze — but in terms of injury: am I *damaging* you by observing you in your private space? This framing matters for several reasons. If we say I am causing you *hezek*, then as a potential *mazik* I bear the obligation to prevent that damage; I must contribute to the wall. If we say it is merely your right, one might argue that the obligation falls on you — if you feel exposed, build your own wall. The framework of *hezek* also raises the question of *ha-mazik le-harchik et atzmo* versus *ha-nizak le-harchik et atzmo* — the classic debate from the end of *Bava Metzia* about whether the one causing damage or the one damaged bears the primary responsibility to create distance. By framing *hezek re'iyah* in terms of injury, the *gemara* situates it within this existing conceptual structure.

So: if *hezek re'iyah* is not a recognized *hezek*, then I cannot demand you participate in building a wall, and the *mishnah*'s requirement of agreement makes sense. You looking into my yard is simply not a legal injury to me.

### Alt: Division Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא ״מְחִיצָה״ – פְּלוּגְתָּא, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וַתְּהִי מֶחֱצַת הָעֵדָה״; וְכֵיוָן דְּרָצוּ – בּוֹנִין אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ, אַלְמָא הֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects to this conclusion: **But say** that the term ***meḥitza*** used in the mishna means **a division, as it is written: "And the division of [*meḥetzat*] the congregation was"** (Numbers 31:43), referring to the half of the spoil that belonged to the entire congregation. According to this interpretation the mishna means: **Since they wished** to divide the jointly owned courtyard, **they build** a proper **wall** in the center even **against the will** of one of the partners. **Apparently,** it may be concluded that **damage** caused by **sight is called damage.**

The *gemara* then proposes an alternative reading: *ve'eima mechitza pelugta* — perhaps *mechitza* means not a wall but a division, as in *vat'hi mechtzat ha'edah* (the division of the community's spoils of war in the *pasuk*). On this reading, the *mishnah* would be saying: partners who agreed to *divide* their property — once division is agreed upon, you can compel the other party to build a wall. The wall-building would be a consequence of the division, not something requiring separate agreement. This would mean *hezek re'iyah shemei hezek* — once you divide, the visual intrusion becomes a recognized damage that can be legally addressed.

### Mechitza = Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַאי ״שֶׁרָצוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת מְחִיצָה״?! ״שֶׁרָצוּ לַחֲצוֹת״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא מַאי – גּוּדָּא? ״בּוֹנִין אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל״?! ״בּוֹנִין אוֹתוֹ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אִי תְּנָא ״אוֹתוֹ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא בִּמְסִיפָס בְּעָלְמָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כּוֹתֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this line of reasoning: **If** it is **so** that the term *meḥitza* means a division, the words: **Who wished to make a division,** are imprecise, as the *tanna* **should have said: Who wished to divide. Rather, what** is the meaning of the term *meḥitza*? **A partition.** The Gemara retorts: If so, the words: **They build the wall,** are imprecise, as the *tanna* **should have said: They build it,** since the wall and the partition are one and the same. The Gemara responds: **Had** the *tanna* **taught:** They build **it, I would say that a mere partition of pegs [*bimseifas*]** would suffice. He therefore **teaches us** that they build an actual **wall,** all in accordance with the regional custom.

The *gemara* rejects this alternative. The *mishnah* uses *mechitza* as the object of *ratzu la'asot* ("they wished to make"), treating it as a noun — a physical thing to be constructed. Moreover, the *mishnah* then says *bonim et hakotel* — "they build the *kotel*" — switching to the word *kotel*. If *mechitza* were the noun (the wall they agreed to build), the subsequent sentence should have said *bonim oto* ("they build it"), referring back to the *mechitza*. The shift to *kotel* suggests the *mechitza* is not the object being built but a conceptual division, and *kotel* is what is separately constructed. Or alternatively: had the *mishnah* used *bonin oto* throughout, we might have thought a mere *mispis* — small pegs marking a boundary line — would suffice. By switching to *kotel*, the *mishnah* emphasizes that the structure must be a proper wall. In either case, *mechitza* most naturally reads as a noun meaning wall (*guda*), and the *gemara* confirms this is the correct interpretation.

### Building Be'emtza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹנִין אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל בָּאֶמְצַע וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: Partners who wished to make a partition in a jointly owned courtyard **build the wall** for the partition **in the middle** of the courtyard. The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that if they agree to build a wall it should be built in the middle? Why should one of them contribute more than the other?

The *gemara* then asks: *pshita* — is it not obvious that if both parties agreed to build the wall, it goes in the middle? Why does the *mishnah* need to state this?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא – דִּקְדֵים חַד וְרַצְּיֵּיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ; מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי אִיתְרְצַאי לָךְ – בְּאַוֵּירָא, בְּתַשְׁמִישְׁתָּא – לָא אִיתְרְצַאי לָךְ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case **where one** of the partners **went ahead and convinced the other** that they should build a partition. **Lest you say** that the second **can** later **say to** the first when the latter comes to begin construction: **When I was persuaded by you** to build a partition, it was **with regard to the airspace.** I agreed to the erection of a minimal barrier that would result in a loss of open space in the courtyard. **But I was not persuaded by you with regard to the use** of the courtyard. I did not agree to forfeit any usable space on the ground in my share of the courtyard for the building of a wall. To counter this, the mishna **teaches us** that since they agreed to make a partition, they must each contribute a part of the courtyard for the building of the wall.

The *gemara* answers: *lo tzricha d'kadim chad ve'ratzei l'chavreih* — it is necessary for the case where one party initiated and eventually persuaded the other. In that scenario, the persuaded party might argue: *ki itratzai lach be'avira* — I agreed to the wall in terms of airspace or building costs, but *be'tashmishta lo itratzai lach* — I never agreed to give up part of my property for the wall's footprint; I assumed the whole structure would be built on your land. The *mishnah* therefore establishes *kamashma lan* that without explicit stipulation, once one agrees to build a wall, everything — materials, footprint, ownership — is assumed to go equally down the middle. The persuasion of one party by another does not alter the legal default.

The underlying conclusion remains: *hezek re'iyah lav shemei hezek* — since agreement is required to build a wall, visual intrusion is not a legally recognized injury.

## Chatzer vs. Ginah

### Ginah Compulsion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה לָאו שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק?! תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְכֵן בְּגִינָּה״!
>
> **Translation:** After having determined that the wording of the mishna is unproblematic only if the term *meḥitza* means a wall, it follows that damage caused by sight is not called damage. The Gemara asks: **And is damage** caused by **sight** in fact **not called damage?** The Gemara provides **a mnemonic** for the proofs, which follow, that challenge this assumption. **Come** and **hear** that which the mishna teaches: **And similarly with regard to a garden,** in a place where it is customary to build a partition in the middle of a garden jointly owned by two people, and one of them wishes to build such a partition, the court obligates his neighbor to join in building the partition. This indicates that invading one's privacy by looking at him while he is in his private domain is called damage.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this conclusion: *tashma, vechen begina* — take note of the *mishnah*'s statement that *similarly in a garden* (*gina*), where the norm is to build a wall, one can *compel* the other party. If *hezek re'iyah* is not a recognized *hezek*, on what basis can compulsion be exercised in a *gina*?

### Ayin Hara Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גִּינָּה שָׁאנֵי, כִּדְרַבִּי אַבָּא – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לָאָדָם לַעֲמוֹד בִּשְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהִיא עוֹמֶדֶת בְּקָמוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **A garden is different** with regard to the *halakha* governing invasion of privacy, **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Abba, as Rabbi Abba says** that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** It is **prohibited for a person to stand in another's field** and look at his crop **while** the grain **is standing,** because he casts an evil eye upon it and thereby causes him damage, and the same is true for a garden. Since the issue in this case is damage resulting from the evil eye, no proof can be brought with regard to the matter of damage caused by sight.

The *gemara* proposes: *gina shani* — a garden is different, and the difference lies in a ruling of Rabbi Abba in the name of Rav Huna in the name of Rav: *asur le'adam la'amod bisde chaveiro bisha'ah she'hi omedet bekamoteha* — it is forbidden to stand in another's field and gaze at it while the grain stands tall and full. The concern is *ayin hara*: jealous observation causes real damage to the produce. The harm is not merely psychological or social, it is understood as a tangible injury to the crops themselves. In the case of a *gina*, therefore, one party's ability to observe the other's vegetables constitutes a genuine *hezek*: the gaze can damage the produce through *ayin hara*. That provides a ground for compulsion in the garden context — it is not merely a privacy concern but an actual risk of agricultural harm.

The *gemara*'s reasoning can thus be parsed as follows: *hezek re'iyah* in the sense of someone merely seeing me relaxing in my yard is not a recognized injury. But *hezek re'iyah* in the sense of someone gazing enviously at my thriving vegetable garden — causing *ayin hara* — may be a genuine *hezek*, sufficient to support compulsion.

### Ginah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״וְכֵן״ קָתָנֵי! אַגְּוִיל וְגָזִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** the mishna **teaches: And similarly** with regard to a garden, which suggests that a garden and a courtyard are governed by the same rationale. The Gemara answers: The term: And similarly, is stated not with regard to the reason for the obligation to construct a wall, but **with regard to** the *halakha* concerning **non-chiseled and chiseled stones.** A partition in a garden is built with the same materials used for the building of a wall in a courtyard, in accordance with regional custom.

The *gemara* then attempts to confine *gina* as a special exception on account of *ayin hara*, preserving the general rule that *hezek re'iyah lav shemei hezek* in other contexts. If *gina* can be explained by the specific danger of *ayin hara*, then the apparently compulsory norm of the garden does not undermine the basic principle established by the *chatzer* case. The analogy offered by *Tosafot* is instructive: in a wheat field people do not congregate and observe regularly the way they do around a neighboring garden, so the *ayin hara* concern is not triggered in the same way, and there no compulsion is possible.

### Fallen Chatzer Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: כּוֹתֶל חָצֵר שֶׁנָּפַל – מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ לִבְנוֹת עַד אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת! נָפַל שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (5a): In the case of a dividing **wall** in a jointly owned **courtyard that fell,** if one of the owners wishes to rebuild the wall, the court **obligates** the other owner **to build** the wall with him again **up to** a height of **four cubits.** This indicates that damage caused by sight is called damage. The Gemara rejects this proof: The case of a wall that **fell is different;** since a wall had already stood there, the court compels the owners to rebuild it as it was.

The *gemara* then cites the next *mishnah*: *kosel chatzer shenafal, mechayivin oto livnot ad arba amos* — if the wall of a *chatzer* falls down, one may compel the other party to rebuild it, up to a height of four *amos*. This appears to be strong evidence that *hezek re'iyah shemei hezek* — one can compel wall-building even in a *chatzer*, not merely in a *gina*.

The *gemara* deflects: *nafal shani* — the case of a fallen wall is different. When a wall pre-existed and then collapsed, the analysis changes. The *mishnah*'s point was not to teach us that *hezek re'iyah* generally supports compulsion; its *chiddush* was the upper limit of four *amos* — one cannot be compelled to rebuild a wall taller than it was or taller than four *amos*, even if it previously stood at ten *amos*. The basic ability to compel rebuilding in the case of a pre-existing wall does not prove anything about compulsion in the absence of a prior wall.

### Habituation & Real Damage

The *gemara* continues its analysis, which we may examine here:

Why is the fallen wall different? One might have thought the answer lies in contract law — there had been an implicit prior agreement, a kind of ongoing arrangement between the neighbors, and rebuilding the wall is simply living up to that earlier understanding. Rashi does appear to lean in this direction. But *Tosafot* offers a more conceptually interesting answer: *she'hurgelu la'asos devar hatznei'ah bechatzer veyilmedu zeh mizeh* — because the parties had been living with that wall, they had grown accustomed to conducting private activities in their yard with an expectation of privacy. Once that habituation is established, the removal of the wall genuinely *does* cause *hezek re'iyah* in a meaningful sense — not because visual intrusion is inherently a *hezek*, but because a level of privacy has been established and the other party has a legitimate claim to maintain it. The damage is real because the expectation is real.

This is a striking formulation. Whether *hezek re'iyah* rises to a legally actionable *hezek* may depend not on any abstract principle but on the actual history of the parties and the degree to which a norm of privacy has been established between them. To be continued.
