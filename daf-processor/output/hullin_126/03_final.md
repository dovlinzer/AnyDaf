# Hullin 126 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Introduction & Setup

### Daf & Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: חֲבִילֵי מִטָּה וּסְרִיגֵי חַלּוֹנוֹת חוֹצְצִין בֵּין הַבַּיִת לַעֲלִיָּיה, שֶׁלֹּא לְהַכְנִיס טוּמְאָה לְצַד שֵׁנִי. פְּרָסָן עַל פְּנֵי הַמֵּת בַּאֲוִיר, הַנּוֹגֵעַ כְּנֶגֶד הַנֶּקֶב – טָמֵא, שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד הַנֶּקֶב – טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I say** my opinion? It is **as we learned** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Oholot* 9:4) that **Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to **bundles** that serve as **a bed and grilles** taken from **windows,** if one placed them between the ground floor and the upper floor of the house such that they serve as a ceiling, since they have become part of the building they are not susceptible to impurity. Therefore, even if there are holes in the bundles and the grilles, **they serve as a barrier between the house and the upper floor such that the impurity** of a corpse present in the area of the house **cannot enter the other side,** i.e., the upper floor. And if **one spread out** the bundles and grilles outside the house in an impermanent manner by hanging them **over a corpse in the air** like a net, **one who touches,** i.e., overlies the corpse, **opposite a hole** in the netting becomes **impure.** By contrast, one who overlies it **not opposite a hole** remains **pure.**

We begin today's shiur a few lines back on 125b, at the passage commencing with the words *amar Rava mina amina lah* — "Rava said: from where do I derive this?" The overarching discussion concerns a *mishnah* that speaks of touching a bone from a corpse as a source of *tumah*. The question animating the preceding sugya is why the *mishnah* uses the language of touching rather than *ohel* — roofing or tenting — given that marrow inside a bone would seem to generate *tumas ohel* for anyone positioned above it. Rabbi Yochanan resolved this by arguing that in certain circumstances, touching and roofing (*ma'ahil*) are treated as equivalent categories, or more precisely, that a form of roofing is described by the term touching.

### Three Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְמַטָּה מִטֶּפַח – אֹהֶל נְגִיעָה, לְמַעְלָה מִטֶּפַח – אֹהֶל גְּרֵידָא. רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַעְלָה מִטֶּפַח נָמֵי אֹהֶל נְגִיעָה הוּא, וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי אֹהֶל גְּרֵידָא? בְּהַמְשָׁכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** With regard to a tent that overlies impurity at a height of **below** one **handbreadth,** that **tent** is referred to as **touching.** But a tent that overlies impurity at a height of **above** one **handbreadth** is referred to as **merely a tent. Rava said:** According to Rabbi Yosei, **even a tent** that overlies impurity at a height **above** one **handbreadth is** referred to as **touching. And what are the circumstances of** a tent that is referred to as **merely a tent** and not as touching? The term tent is referring to the case of a structure that overlies both a person and a source of impurity, thereby **spreading** the impurity from its source to the person.

The *gemara* presents three distinct explanations for when roofing is considered equivalent to touching, each advanced by a different *amora*. Rabbi Zeira held that the equivalence obtains when the *tumah* is squeezed between two structures. Abaye held that it applies when one is within a *tefach* of the *tumah* — so close that the roofing is practically indistinguishable from contact. Rava, by contrast, held the broadest position: whenever one is directly above or below the *tumah*, that is equivalent to touching, and only when both parties are beneath the same shared roof — neither one above the other — does roofing remain a purely distinct category from touching. The *nafka mina* among these three positions concerns whether one can combine a *chatzi kezayit* of *tumah* that one roofs over with a *chatzi kezayit* that one touches, to produce a full *kezayit* and thereby incur *tumah*. We now turn to Rava's proof for his position.

---

## Rava's Proof

### Bed Ropes & Lattice

The *gemara* cites the following *mishnah* as the source for his ruling: Rabbi Yosei says that *chavilei mitah* — the crisscrossed ropes forming the base of a bed frame — and *srigai chalonot* — lattice work — *chotzin bein ha-bayit la-aliyah*, serve as a partition between a lower story and an upper story of a building. When such lattice work is permanently installed and no longer functions as a vessel, it operates as a structural separator between the two floors. If there is *tumah* on the lower floor, it will not pass to the upper floor, even though the lattice has holes running through it.

### Separation Principle

The critical feature of this lattice work is that its openings are less than a *tefach* in width. The general principle is that an opening of a *tefach* or more allows *tumah* to pass through freely, while an opening smaller than a *tefach* does not. Once the lattice work is treated as part of the roof structure, those small holes are legally treated as if they do not exist — the lattice functions as a solid barrier. Accordingly, *tumah* arising directly beneath one of the holes is still considered to be under a solid roof, and it does not ascend to the upper floor.

### Lattice Over Corpse

The *mishnah* then shifts to a different scenario. The lattice work is not attached to any building structure but is suspended out in open air over a corpse — imagine a pole has been erected and the lattice placed above a body lying in a field. In this case, the unattached lattice does not have the full legal status of a roof, and the result is more nuanced: *ha-nogea ke-neged ha-nekev tamei*, one who touches opposite one of the holes in the lattice becomes *tamei*, because the *tumah* passes through the opening; *shelo ke-neged ha-nekev tahor*, one who touches opposite the physical material of the lattice itself is *tahor*, since the lattice matter itself blocks the *tumah* even without being structurally attached.

### Touching vs. Hole

This is the crux of Rava's proof. The *mishnah* uses the language *ha-nogea* — "one who touches" — in a context that describes someone holding a hand above the lattice work, which itself is suspended above the corpse. Rava argues that this person is not touching anything in the conventional sense; he is touching air. The *mishnah* would more naturally have said *ha-ma'ahil ke-neged ha-nekev* — "one who roofs opposite the hole." Instead it uses the verb *touch*, even in a scenario where the person is presumably above a *tefach*. Rava reads this as proof that in such a configuration — directly above the source of *tumah* — roofing is simply called touching, confirming his broad position.

## Abaye's Objection

### Within Tefach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם לְמַטָּה מִטֶּפַח, וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ: מֵת בִּכְסוּתוֹ הוּא – מֵת בִּכְסוּתוֹ מְבַטֵּל לֵיהּ, הַאי לָא מְבַטֵּל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye** rejected Rava's proof and **said: Actually,** the case is one where one spread the netting out **below** the height of one **handbreadth** above the corpse. **And** with regard to **that which you say:** If so, one who overlies the corpse not opposite a hole in the netting should also become impure as it **is** tantamount to a case of **a corpse** dressed **in its clothing,** this case is not similar to the case of a corpse dressed in its clothing. In the case of **a corpse** dressed **in its clothing** the clothing does not act as a barrier to the impurity because the one who dressed the corpse **nullified** the clothing by rendering its status as though it is a part of the corpse. But in **this** case in the *baraita*, the one who spread out the netting over the corpse intended to remove the bundles and grilles afterward and **he did not nullify** them.

Abaye rejects Rava's inference. He maintains that the *mishnah* must be describing a scenario in which the lattice work is within a *tefach* of the corpse, not above a *tefach*. On this reading, the *mishnah* uses the term "touching" because the lattice is so close to the body that something adjacent to it is, in the language of roofing law, essentially touching. The *gemara* probes this claim. It entertains the objection that if the lattice is within a *tefach* of the corpse, it should be treated like clothing on the body — *met kikesuto* — and disqualified as a partition entirely. But the *gemara* answers that the ordinary clothing analogy applies to garments one *mevatel* — incorporates — into the corpse itself, whereas this lattice work retains its independent character and is not subsumed into the body. It therefore may still function as a separator even within a *tefach*, at least opposite its solid portions.

### Buried Tumah Burst

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתֶהֱוֵי כְּטוּמְאָה טְמוּנָה בּוֹקַעַת וְעוֹלָה? קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: טוּמְאָה טְמוּנָה אֵינָהּ בּוֹקַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects with regard to Abaye's explanation: **But** if the case is one where he spread out the netting less than one handbreadth above the corpse, even if it is not considered the clothing of the corpse, **let it be** considered **like** a source of **impurity hidden** underneath an overlying structure at a distance of less than one handbreadth. In such a case, the impurity **breaks through and ascends,** and therefore it should transmit impurity even to one who overlies it not opposite a hole in the netting. The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Yosei holds** that **hidden impurity does not break through** and ascend.

However, the *gemara* raises a second, more forceful objection against Abaye's reading. There is a well-established principle: *tumah temunah* — *tumah* that is buried or enclosed without a *tefach* of airspace above it — *boka'at ve'olah*, bursts upward and is not contained by the physical barrier around it. This is treated as close to an iron rule of *tumas meis*: unless there is a *tefach* of airspace between the corpse and the covering above it, the *tumah* simply breaks through. If Abaye is correct that the lattice is within a *tefach* of the corpse, then why would the lattice stop the *tumah* at all? The *tumah* should burst straight through the lattice, reaching whoever is above, whether they are opposite a hole or opposite the solid lattice material.

### Yosei Rejects Burst

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא? דִּתְנַן: תֵּיבַת הַמִּגְדָּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ פּוֹתֵחַ טֶפַח וְאֵין בִּיצִיאָתָהּ פּוֹתֵחַ טֶפַח, טוּמְאָה בְּתוֹכָהּ – הַבַּיִת טָמֵא; טוּמְאָה בַּבַּיִת – מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָהּ טָהוֹר. מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדֶּרֶךְ טוּמְאָה לָצֵאת, וְאֵין דֶּרֶךְ טוּמְאָה לִיכָּנֵס.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do you say** that this is the opinion of Rabbi Yosei? It is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Oholot* 4:2): In the case of **a compartment** in **a chest** that is inside a house, in which the **open space** of the compartment itself **has** at least one cubic **handbreadth** in volume, **but** the **opening** of the compartment to the house does **not have** the area of one square **handbreadth,** and the volume of the entire chest is forty *se'a*, in that case, if there is a source of **impurity inside** the compartment, the compartment does not act as a barrier and **the house is impure.** If the source of **impurity is inside the house** but outside the chest, **whatever is inside** the compartment remains **pure,** because the opening of the compartment is less than one handbreadth. This differentiation exists **because it is typical for** a source of **impurity to exit** from its location, and therefore impurity can be transmitted out of the compartment and into the house. **But it is not typical for** a source of **impurity to enter** a location, and therefore an impure item that lies in the house does not transmit impurity into the compartment.

To preserve Abaye's reading, the *gemara* entertains a striking inference: perhaps Rabbi Yosei himself rejects the principle of *tumah temunah boka'at*. Perhaps Rabbi Yosei holds that any physical barrier — even one within a *tefach* of the corpse — stops *tumah* from ascending. This would be a remarkable departure from standard *halacha*. The practical implications would be enormous: if any physical covering stops *tumah*, even when less than a *tefach* above the body, the rules governing *kohanim* near cemeteries would be transformed entirely. But this is raised as a live possibility, and the *gemara* now turns to examine whether there is independent evidence for such a position in Rabbi Yosei's rulings.

## Cabinet Mishnah

### Cabinet Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מְטַהֵר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל הוּא לְהוֹצִיאָהּ לַחֲצָאִין, אוֹ לְשׂוֹרְפָהּ בִּמְקוֹמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei deems** the house **pure** when the impure item is in the compartment. The item will not necessarily impart impurity outside of the compartment **because one can remove** the source of impurity from the compartment **in halves,** i.e., in pieces that each measure less than the requisite amount to impart impurity. **Or,** alternatively, one can **burn** the source of impurity while it still remains **in its place** inside the compartment.

The *gemara* cites a *mishnah* involving a *teivat migdal* — a large, immovable cabinet or dresser. An object so large and heavy that it is intended to remain in one place permanently does not attain the legal status of a vessel and cannot become *tamei*. The *mishnah* describes a scenario in which *tumah* — a small piece of a human corpse — is trapped beneath such a cabinet. The cabinet sits on the floor with a *tefach* of space between the floor and the bottom of the cabinet. This *tefach* causes the *tumah* to be treated as having a roof over it, so it spreads beneath the cabinet and out to the sides. However, the opening at the sides through which it could exit is less than a *tefach*. The *tumah* is, in principle, perfectly trapped: the *tefach* of space prevents it from bursting upward through the cabinet, while the narrow opening prevents it from spreading out into the room. The *mishnah* then states two rulings: if there is *tumah* beneath the cabinet, the house is *tamei*; if there is *tumah* in the house, whatever is enclosed beneath the cabinet is *tahor*.

### Sof Tumah Latzeit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: הֶעֱמִידָהּ בַּפֶּתַח, וּפִתְחָהּ לַחוּץ – טוּמְאָה בְּתוֹכָהּ, הַבַּיִת טָהוֹר; טוּמְאָה בַּבַּיִת, מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָהּ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And the latter clause** of that mishna **teaches:** If one **placed** the chest **in the entrance** to the house **and the opening of** the compartment is facing **out** of the house, if there is a source of **impurity inside** the chest, **the house** remains **pure** because the impure item will typically exit the house. If there is a source of **impurity in the house, whatever is inside** the chest remains **pure,** because the source of impurity will not enter the chest.

The second ruling — that whatever is under the cabinet is protected from outside *tumah* — follows straightforwardly: there is no opening large enough for *tumah* to enter from the house into the space beneath the cabinet. The first ruling — that a corpse trapped under the cabinet makes the house *tamei* — requires explanation, since we just said the *tumah* cannot escape. The *gemara* explains it through the principle of *sof tumah latzeit*: the way of *tumah* is ultimately to go out. The practical meaning is that since any corpse or piece of a corpse will eventually be removed for burial, we treat it as though the removal has already occurred. We project forward to the inevitable moment of removal and count the *tumah* as having already traveled through the opening — even one smaller than a *tefach* — and entered the house. This principle is understood by the *gemara* to be rabbinic in origin, though many of the *mishnayot* that employ it do not make that explicit.

### Yosei's Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מְטַהֵר. אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַסֵּיפָא – תַּנָּא קַמָּא נָמֵי טַהוֹרֵי קָא מְטַהַר! אֶלָּא, דְּקָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא: טוּמְאָה בְּתוֹכָהּ – הַבַּיִת טָמֵא, אִי מִשּׁוּם דְּדֶרֶךְ טוּמְאָה לָצֵאת, וְאִי מִשּׁוּם דְּטוּמְאָה טְמוּנָה בּוֹקַעַת. וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ דֶּרֶךְ טוּמְאָה לָצֵאת – יָכוֹל הוּא לְהוֹצִיאָהּ לַחֲצָאִין אוֹ לְשׂוֹרְפָהּ בִּמְקוֹמָהּ, וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ טוּמְאָה טְמוּנָה בּוֹקַעַת – טוּמְאָה טְמוּנָה אֵינָהּ בּוֹקַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught with regard to** that *halakha* in the mishna that **Rabbi Yosei deems** it **pure. To which** *halakha* in the mishna is this referring? **If we say** that it is referring **to the latter clause** of the mishna, which discusses a chest placed in the entrance of the house, **the first *tanna* also deems** it **pure** in that case. **Rather,** it is clear **that the first *tanna* says** that if a source of **impurity** is **inside** the compartment of a chest that is inside the house, **the house is impure, either because it is typical for** a source of **impurity to exit** its location, **or because** he holds that **a hidden** source of **impurity breaks through** and ascends. **And Rabbi Yosei** disagrees and **says to him:** With regard to **that which you say** that **it is typical for** a source of **impurity to exit** its location, it is not necessarily so. **One can remove** the impure item **in halves or burn it in its place** inside the compartment. **And** with regard to **that which you say** that **a hidden** source of **impurity breaks through** and ascends, **a hidden** source of **impurity does not break through.**

Rabbi Yosei disagrees and says the house is *tahor*. His reasoning: *she-yachol hi le-hotzi chatza'im o lisrof bi-mekomah* — one can remove the corpse in half-pieces of a *kezayit* at a time, or could in theory burn it in place. Since there exists a mode of removal that does not involve passing the *tumah* through the opening of the house in a *kezayit*-sized amount at one time, we cannot be certain that the *sof tumah latzeit* principle will operate in the house-contaminating way the *tanna kamma* assumes. Tosafot notes that this reasoning applies specifically to a small piece of flesh; if there were a whole corpse inside, Rabbi Yosei would agree that it would be removed in the normal manner, and *sof tumah latzeit* would apply.

### Cabinet in Doorframe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵי דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** §Based on the previous statement of Rabbi Yosei, the Gemara established that Rabbi Yosei holds that a hidden source of impurity does not break through and ascend. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** between that previous statement **of Rabbi Yosei and** another statement **of Rabbi Yosei.**

The *mishnah* continues with a second case: the cabinet is positioned in the doorframe itself, with its opening pointing outward — toward the outside of the house. Now, whatever mechanism of *sof tumah latzeit* one applies, the imagined removal of the *tumah* would direct it outward, away from the house interior, not inward. Even granting the principle of *sof tumah latzeit*, the *tumah* goes out, not in. The house remains *tahor*, and whatever is enclosed under the cabinet is also *tahor* because the house is unaffected. The *mishnah* then appends: *Rabbi Yosei says tahor*.

---

## Contradiction & Clarification

### Redundancy Problem

This final appended statement — *Rabbi Yosei says tahor* — immediately strikes the *gemara* as redundant. In the second scenario, where the cabinet is in the doorframe with its opening pointing outward, the *tanna kamma* already rules the house *tahor*. Why would Rabbi Yosei need to restate that it is *tahor*? If it applies to this second case, both opinions agree and there is no dispute to record. This forces the *gemara* to reconsider which case Rabbi Yosei's statement actually addresses.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הַכֶּלֶב שֶׁאָכַל בָּשָׂר מֵת, וּמֵת הַכֶּלֶב, וּמוּטָּל עַל הָאִסְקוּפָּה – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ בְּצַוָּארוֹ פּוֹתֵחַ טֶפַח – מֵבִיא אֶת הַטּוּמְאָה, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶת הַטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Oholot* 11:7): In the case of **a dog that ate the flesh of a corpse, and the dog** then **died and is lying on the threshold** in such a manner that its neck and mouth are facing toward the inside of the house, **Rabbi Meir says: If there is an opening** the size of one cubic **handbreadth inside the neck of** the dog, i.e., the neck itself constitutes this measure, the dog **imports impurity** into the house because the upper portion of the dog's body overlies the impure item inside the dog and the impurity is transmitted through the neck and mouth of the dog into the house. **But if** there is **not** such a large cavity in the neck of the dog, then there is no *halakha* of a tent, and the dog **does not import the impurity** into the house.

The *gemara* resolves: Rabbi Yosei's appended statement must be referring back to the first scenario — the cabinet in the interior of the house with *tumah* trapped beneath it — where the *tanna kamma* ruled the house *tamei*. In that case, the *tanna kamma* relied on *sof tumah latzeit* to make the house *tamei*. Rabbi Yosei says the house is *tahor* even in that case. But now the *gemara* presses further: even aside from *sof tumah latzeit*, why would the *tanna kamma* assume the house is *tamei* in that first case? Because it is implicit in the *tanna kamma*'s framing that if there were *no* *tefach* between the corpse and the underside of the cabinet, the *tumah* would burst upward through the cabinet and fill the house — that case would be so obvious as to require no mention. The interesting case is precisely where there *is* a *tefach*, and the question is whether *sof tumah latzeit* then carries the *tumah* into the house.

### Yosei's Objections

Rabbi Yosei issues two rejections. First, against *sof tumah latzeit*: perhaps the piece is removed in sections or burned in place, so we do not apply the principle. Second, and more fundamentally, Rabbi Yosei also rejects the implicit premise of the *tanna kamma* — namely that *tumah temunah boka'at*, that *tumah* without a *tefach* roof above it bursts through. Rabbi Yosei holds *tumah temunah einah boka'at*: any physical barrier, even one within a *tefach* of the corpse, stops *tumah* from ascending. This is the position that the *gemara* is working to establish, and it is read into Rabbi Yosei's otherwise apparently redundant ruling of *tahor* on the second case — a ruling that, on closer examination, is his response to the *tanna kamma*'s implied assumption in the first case.

## Dog Stomach Case

### Dog & Flesh Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין מִכְּנֶגֶד הַשָּׁקוֹף וְלִפְנִים – הַבַּיִת טָמֵא, מִכְּנֶגֶד הַשְּׁקוֹף וְלַחוּץ – הַבַּיִת טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: One looks** to determine exactly where on the threshold the dog is located. If the impure item inside the dog is located anywhere **from opposite,** i.e., under, **the lintel and toward the inside** of the house, **the house is impure.** If the dog is located anywhere **from opposite the lintel and toward the outside** of the house, **the house** remains **pure.**

The *gemara* now introduces a further *mishnah* to test whether Rabbi Yosei consistently holds *tumah temunah einah boka'at*. The scenario: a dog ate flesh from a human corpse. While the dog is alive, the flesh causes no *tumah*, because *tumah belua* — *tumah* that is swallowed or absorbed within a living being — does not radiate outward; it is as though it does not exist. Once the dog dies, however, the principle of *belua* no longer applies. The flesh is now simply enclosed within a dead dog's body, and since it is no longer inside a living creature, it resumes its character as a source of *tumas meis*. If there is a *kezayit* of such flesh inside the dog's carcass, it becomes capable of causing everything beneath the same roof to become *tamei*.

### Doorstep Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: פִּיו לִפְנִים הַבַּיִת – טָהוֹר, פִּיו לַחוּץ הַבַּיִת – טָמֵא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁטּוּמְאָה יוֹצְאָה דֶּרֶךְ שׁוּלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** One must determine the exact manner in which the dog is lying on the threshold. If **its mouth** is located **inside** the house, but its rear is located outside the house, **the house** remains **pure.** If **its mouth** is located **outside** the house but its rear is located inside the house, **the house is impure.** This is **because** the source of **impurity exits** the dog's body **through its edge,** i.e., its rear.

The *mishnah* then describes the dog lying on the threshold — the *iskoppa* — of a house, half inside and half outside the doorframe. The question is whether the corpse-flesh inside the dog's stomach renders the interior of the house *tamei*. One possibility is that the *tumah* travels through the dog's throat and esophagus, which functions as an *ohel*, and carries the *tumah* into the house. Another is that the *tumah* simply bursts through the walls of the dog's stomach wherever the stomach happens to be located, and one then examines where that location falls relative to the house. The *mishnah* records a four-way dispute.

### Rabbi Meir

Rabbi Meir rules: *im yesh be-tzavaro pote'ach tefach*, if the dog's throat is a full *tefach* in diameter, it brings the *tumah* into the house; if not, it does not. The esophagus functions as an *ohel* only when it has the minimum qualifying dimension of a *tefach*.

### Rabbi Yosei

Rabbi Yosei rules: *ro'in mi-keneged ha-mashkof u-lifnim ha-bayit tamei, mi-keneged ha-mashkof u-le-chutz ha-bayit tahor* — one inspects from opposite the *mashkof* inward and outward. The *mashkof* is the point in the doorframe where the door itself makes contact and stops — the interior striker-point, so to speak. Everything from that point inward is definitively under the roof of the house; everything from that point outward, though still within the outer dimensions of the doorframe, is not. Rabbi Yosei appears to reject the throat-as-*ohel* theory and instead looks directly at where the piece of flesh is located relative to this *mashkof* line, with *tumah* bursting through wherever the flesh sits.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer rules: *piv lifnim ha-bayit tahor* — if the dog's mouth faces inward, the house is *tahor*; *piv le-chutz ha-bayit tamei* — if the mouth faces outward, the house is *tamei*. Rabbi Eliezer apparently rejects the throat as a vehicle for *tumah* transmission, ruling the house *tahor* when the mouth faces inward, since the throat does not constitute a qualifying *ohel*. When the mouth faces outward, however, the rear of the dog is inside the house, and he applies *sof tumah latzeit* in an unusual form: the natural route of excretion for the *tumah* is through the rear, so we treat the *tumah* as though it has already passed out through the rear and thus entered the house. Tosafot notes that Rabbi Eliezer may not actually disagree with the possibility that the throat carries *tumah* — he may simply be addressing cases where the throat is not a *tefach* wide — but he adds the point about the rear and the direction of the dog.

### R. Yehuda ben Batera

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ, הַבַּיִת טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: In** both **this** case **and that** case, i.e., whether the dog is lying such that its mouth is inside the house or outside the house, **the house is impure.** The reason is that the impure item can exit through either the mouth or the rear.

Rabbi Yehuda ben Batera rules: *bein kach u-vein kach ha-bayit tamei* — whether the mouth faces inward or the rear faces inward, the house is always *tamei*. When the mouth faces inward, the throat carries the *tumah* into the house as an *ohel*; when the rear faces inward, *sof tumah latzeit* applies through the natural direction of excretion.

## Rava Reinterprets Yosei

### Initial Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַאי לָאו, אַאֵין בְּצַוָּארוֹ פּוֹתֵחַ טֶפַח קָאֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי? וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: טוּמְאָה טְמוּנָה בּוֹקַעַת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the statement of Rabbi Yosei that if the dog is located anywhere from under the lintel and toward the inside of the house, the house is impure. **What, is** the statement of **Rabbi Yosei not referring to** a case where **there is not an opening** the size of one cubic **handbreadth inside** the dog's **neck,** with regard to which Rabbi Meir says that the house remains pure? Accordingly, Rabbi Yosei is responding to Rabbi Meir and saying: Even if the neck of the dog is not the size of one cubic handbreadth, as long as the impure item inside the dog is located from under the lintel and toward the house, the house is impure because it overlies the impure item. **And** therefore **learn from it** that Rabbi Yosei holds that **a hidden** source of **impurity breaks through** and ascends, contrary to Rabbi Yosei's opinion as understood from his previous statement.

The *gemara* initially reads Rabbi Yosei's ruling — *ro'in*, "you inspect" — as an instruction to examine where inside the dog the piece of corpse-flesh is actually located, and to check whether that location falls inside or outside the *mashkof* line. On this reading, Rabbi Yosei believes *tumah* bursts straight through the walls of the dog's stomach and spreads in all directions from wherever the flesh is, without any barrier stopping it. This appears to support the conclusion that *Rabbi Yosei does hold tumah temunah boka'at* — contradicting the position the *gemara* had tentatively attributed to him in the cabinet discussion. The *gemara* draws out the inference: *ki Rabbi Yosei u-shema minan tumah temunah boka'at* — from Rabbi Yosei here we derive that *tumah* buried or enclosed without a *tefach* bursts through.

### Cavity Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: רוֹאִין אֶת חֲלַל הַטּוּמְאָה קָתָנֵי, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּתַרְתֵּי פְּלִיג, וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: דְּקָאָמְרַתְּ כִּי יֵשׁ בְּצַוָּארוֹ פּוֹתֵחַ טֶפַח מֵבִיא אֶת הַטּוּמְאָה – אֲנַן בָּתַר חֲלָלַהּ אָזְלִינַן. וּדְקָא אָמְרַתְּ הַבַּיִת כּוּלּוֹ טָמֵא – מִכְּנֶגֶד הַשְּׁקוֹף וְלִפְנִים הַבַּיִת טָמֵא, מִכְּנֶגֶד הַשְּׁקוֹף וְלַחוּץ הַבַּיִת טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Rabbi Yosei holds that a hidden source of impurity does not break through and ascend. As for that which Rabbi Yosei teaches: One looks whether the impure item inside the dog is located opposite the lintel and toward the inside of the house, Rabbi Yosei actually **teaches: One looks at the empty space** adjacent to **the** source of **impurity. And Rabbi Yosei disagrees** with Rabbi Meir **with regard to two** matters, **and says to Rabbi Meir:** As for **that which you say** that if **there is the width of** one cubic **handbreadth in the neck** of the dog, **it imports the impurity** into the house, that is not so. Rather, **we follow** the measure of **the empty space,** i.e., the neck of the dog imports impurity into the house only if it contains a cubic handbreadth of space in addition to the thickness of the flesh of the neck itself. **And** with regard to **that which you say** that even if the dog is located on the outer portion of the threshold of the house, **the entire house is impure,** that is not so. Rather, if the impurity inside the dog is located anywhere **from opposite the lintel and toward the inside** of the house, **the house is impure.** If the impurity inside the dog is located anywhere **from opposite the lintel and toward the outside** of the house, **the house** remains **pure.**

Rava intervenes with a reinterpretation. When Rabbi Yosei says *ro'in*, he does not mean: inspect where the flesh is and assume it radiates straight through. Rather, he means: inspect whether the actual cavity — *challal* — of the esophagus is itself a *tefach* in diameter. There is a textual tradition cited in the name of Rav Acha son of Rava, who explicitly had the reading *ro'in et challal ha-tzavar* — "you inspect the hollow of the throat" — in his text of the *mishnah*. On this reading, Rabbi Yosei does not disagree with Rabbi Meir on the question of whether a *tefach*-wide throat carries *tumah*. He agrees that it does. What he disputes is what counts as *tefach*-wide: Rabbi Meir counts the entire anatomical throat, while Rabbi Yosei insists on measuring only the actual hollow passage within the esophagus.

### Meir vs. Yosei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ בְּהֶדְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין אֶת חֲלַל הַטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, teaches** that Rava's explanation is **explicit** in the mishna itself, as follows: **Rabbi Yosei says: One looks at the empty space** adjacent to the source **of impurity.**

Tosafot explains Rabbi Meir's logic by reference to a broader principle found elsewhere: Rabbi Meir in other *sugyot* holds that one can mentally project an object as carved out, treating a space as hollow even before any cutting takes place. Applied here, Rabbi Meir measures the full throat on the theory that if the meat surrounding the esophagus were cleared away, one would have a hollow of that full diameter. Rabbi Yosei rejects this and requires the actual hollow of the esophagus to be a *tefach*. In practice, this would be an extraordinary anatomical condition — imaginable perhaps for large animals like lions, but barely realistic for dogs. The theoretical possibility, however, is what the *halakha* must account for.

### Two Objections

Rava thus restates Rabbi Yosei's position as a dual objection against Rabbi Meir. First: where Rabbi Meir says the full throat being a *tefach* wide suffices, Rabbi Yosei says *anan batar challal azlinan* — we follow only the actual hollow. Second: where the *mishnah* appears to speak of any position within the doorframe as sufficient, Rabbi Yosei subdivides the doorframe at the *mashkof* line. The mouth of the dog being anywhere in the outer doorframe does not count; only from the *mashkof* inward is the dog's mouth considered to be beneath the roof of the house. On Rava's reading, then, Rabbi Yosei is not saying that *tumah* bursts straight through the dog's body from wherever the flesh is sitting. He is saying that the throat must have an actual hollow *tefach*, and even then, the right portion of the doorframe must be involved. Rabbi Yosei's position is thereby rescued from the apparent contradiction: he still holds *tumah temunah einah boka'at*.

## Rabbi Shimon

### Three Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן תַּנָּא דִּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שָׁלֹשׁ טוּמְאוֹת פּוֹרְשׁוֹת מִן הַמֵּת, שְׁתַּיִם בְּכׇל אַחַת וּשְׁלִישִׁית אֵין בָּהֶן, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: מְלֹא תַרְוָוד רָקָב, וְעֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה, וְגוֹלֵל וְדוֹפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** §Previously the Gemara established that Rabbi Yosei holds that with regard to the *halakhot* of impurity, overlying is referred to as touching, as the transmission of impurity via contact and via overlying are considered one category. But the mishna (*Oholot* 3:1) cited earlier (125b) states that in the case of one who touches half an olive-bulk of flesh of a corpse, if a tent simultaneously overlies him and another half an olive-bulk of flesh he remains pure. Evidently, there is a *tanna* who disagrees with Rabbi Yosei and holds that these two methods of transmitting impurity are not the same category. The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna* who disagrees with** Rabbi Yosei? The Gemara answers: **It is Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says: [Three types of impurity-bearing matter] derive from a corpse, each one of which imparts impurity in two ways but does not impart impurity in a third way. And they are the following: A full ladle of dust from a corpse, and a bone the size of a barley grain, and a grave cover [*golel*] and a grave wall [*dofek*].**

Having presented Rabbi Shimon's teaching, the *gemara* draws the key implication for our earlier discussion. The *gemara* now turns to identify which *tanna* explicitly disputes the position we have been attributing to Rabbi Yosei — namely that some form of roofing is called touching. A *beraita* is cited: *Rabbi Shimon says: shalosh tumot porshot min ha-met* — three types of *tumah*-matter that derive from a corpse possess only two of the three standard modes of conveying *tumah*, lacking one mode each. Normally, a corpse or part thereof causes *tumah* in three ways: *maga* — touching it, *massa* — carrying it, and *ohel* — being beneath the same roof as it. Rabbi Shimon identifies three substances, each of which is deficient in one of these three modes.

### Decomposed Dust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְלֹא תַרְוָוד רָקָב – מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא וּבְאֹהֶל, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע, וְהֵיכָן מַגָּעוֹ? עִם אַחַת מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **A full ladle of dust imparts impurity via carrying and in a tent, but it does not impart impurity via contact,** as it is impossible for one to touch all of the particles of dust simultaneously. **And where** among these sources of impurity is the imparting of impurity via **contact** applicable? It is applicable **with one of those** other two sources of impurity, i.e., a bone the size of a barley grain and the cover or wall of a grave.

*Melo tarvad rakev* — a ladle-full of decomposed corpse-dust — causes *tumah* through carrying and through *ohel*, but not through touching. The reason is that there is no practical way to touch all of the decomposed dust simultaneously; the material on the interior of one's cupped hand would never actually be in contact with one's skin. Since touching the entire quantity at once is impossible, the mode of *tumah* by touching does not apply. The *gemara* then asks: where, then, does one find a case of *tumah* through touching for this substance? The answer: in combination with one of the other two types listed, each of which does carry *tumah* through touching.

### Small Bone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה – מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא וּבְמַגָּע, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל, וְהֵיכָן אׇהֳלוֹ? עִם אַחַת מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **A bone the size of a barley grain imparts impurity via carrying and contact, but it does not impart impurity in a tent.** This *halakha* was transmitted to Moses from Sinai. **And where** among these sources of impurity is impurity imparted in **a tent?** It is **with one of those** other two sources of impurity.

*Etzem ke-se'orah* — a bone the size of a barley grain, without sufficient marrow to constitute a *kezayit* — causes *tumah* through carrying and through touching, but not through *ohel*. This connects directly back to our *mishnah*: a bare bone without marrow or flesh does not generate *tumas ohel*. Where does one find a case of *ohel* for this substance? Again, in combination with one of the other two types.

### Coffin-Boards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגּוֹלֵל וְדוֹפֵק – מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְאֹהֶל, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא, וְהֵיכָן מַשָּׂאוֹ? עִם אֶחָד מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **A grave cover and a grave wall impart impurity via contact and in a tent, but they do not impart impurity via carrying.** This *halakha* too was transmitted to Moses from Sinai. **And where** among these sources of impurity is impurity transmitted via **carrying?** It is **with one of those** other two sources of impurity.

*Golel va-dofek* — understood by Rashi as the sideboard and headboard of a coffin, and by Tosafot as perhaps referring to the headstone of a grave — causes *tumah* through touching and through *ohel*, but not through carrying. Rashi attributes the *tumah* of *golel va-dofek* to *Halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, while Tosafot attempts to ground it in a *pasuk*. The absence of the carrying mode for the *golel* is what completes the symmetry of the three: one type lacks touching, one lacks *ohel*, one lacks carrying.

## Yosei vs. Shimon

### No Roofing-as-Touch

Having presented Rabbi Shimon's teaching, the *gemara* draws the key implication for our earlier discussion. Rabbi Shimon states explicitly that the *melo tarvad rakev* — the decomposed corpse-dust — does not cause *tumah* through touching. This is a definitive statement: for this substance, the mode of touching simply does not exist. Now, if Rabbi Shimon held that any form of *ohel* — any form of roofing — could be subsumed under the category of touching, he could not have made this ruling. Even if direct physical touching of all the material at once is impossible, there would remain a form of *ohel*-as-touching that would preserve some mode of *tumah* by touching for the decomposed dust. The fact that Rabbi Shimon categorically excludes touching as a mode for *melo tarvad rakev* proves that he does not recognize any equivalence between roofing and touching.

### Yosei's Proof

This stands in direct contrast to the *beraita* on 125b that served as the starting point for the whole discussion: Rabbi Yosei there says that one who roofs over the decomposed dust does become *tamei* through touching. The *gemara* had asked how one could possibly touch all of the decomposed material at once, and the answer was that Rabbi Yosei must be counting a form of *ohel* — a form of roofing or being directly above — as equivalent to touching. That is what enabled Rabbi Yosei to say that one becomes *tamei* through touching the *melo tarvad rakev*.

### Fundamental Dispute

The conclusion is that we have identified a genuine *machlokes* between two *tannaim*. Rabbi Yosei holds that roofing, under at least some conditions, is subsumed under or described as touching, so that one who roofs over the decomposed dust is *tamei* through touching. Rabbi Shimon categorically rejects any such equivalence: no form of *ohel* counts as touching, and therefore the decomposed dust lacks the mode of *tumah* by touching entirely. This was precisely the answer to the *gemara*'s question about which *tanna* disputes Rabbi Yosei's position — it is Rabbi Shimon.

## Bone Shomer

### Bone Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בְּנִבְלָתָהּ״ – וְלֹא בְּקוּלִית סְתוּמָה. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ נִיקְּבָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַנּוֹגֵעַ … יִטְמָא״, אֶת שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לִיגַּע – טָמֵא, וְאֶת שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לִיגַּע – טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** §The mishna teaches: With regard to **the thigh bone of an unslaughtered carcass and the thigh bone of a creeping animal,** one who touches them when they are sealed remains ritually pure, because the bone itself does not impart impurity. With regard to this topic, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "One who touches the carcass thereof shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 11:39). The word **"carcass"** indicates that one who touches the carcass is impure, **but** one who touches **a sealed thigh bone is not.** One **might** have thought that **even** one who touches **a perforated** thigh bone remains pure. Therefore, **the verse states: "One who touches** the carcass thereof [*benivlatah*] **shall be impure,"** indicating that one who touches a part of the animal through **which it is possible to touch** the flesh, which has the halakhic status of an unslaughtered carcass [*neveila*], is **impure, but** one who touches a part of the animal through **which it is impossible to touch** the flesh remains **pure.** Therefore, one who touches a protective layer such as the outside of a sealed thigh bone does not become impure, as it is impossible to touch the marrow.

With the *ohel*-and-touching digression now concluded, the *gemara* returns to focus on the *mishnah*'s actual ruling, which concerns the bone of a *neveilah* — a carcass. The *mishnah* holds that a bone of a carcass causes *tumah* if touched or carried, but only when there is access to the marrow — even through a tiny hole. If there is no way to access the marrow at all, the bone is *tahor*.

### Shomer Concept

The governing principle is that of *shomer*: the bone serves as a guardian of the marrow. Touching the bone counts as touching the marrow, and carrying the bone counts as carrying the marrow, because the bone is the shell that protects and contains it. However, this *shomer* relationship only holds when it is at least theoretically possible to touch the marrow itself. If the bone is completely sealed and there is no opening whatsoever, the bone cannot function as a *shomer* and touching or carrying it causes no *tumah*.

### Source Analysis

The *gemara* derives this from the verse's language: *ha-nogea yitma* — "one who touches becomes *tamei*." The *talmud lomar* teaches that this applies only *she-efshar liga* — when it is possible to touch the actual flesh or marrow. If touching the flesh itself is impossible, *she-i efshar liga tahor* — one is *tahor*. The practical application to the bone of a *neveilah*: if there is a pinhole opening in the bone, it is possible to insert a hair and touch the marrow, so the bone functions as a *shomer* and one becomes *tamei* by touching the bone's exterior. If there is no hole, no such access is possible, and the bone is *tahor*.

## Challenging Shomer

### Rav Zeira's Q.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְאַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, בְּהֵמָה בְּעוֹרָהּ לֹא תְּטַמֵּא? פּוֹק חֲזִי כַּמָּה נְקָבִים יֵשׁ בָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Abaye: If that is so,** that one who touches the protective layer of a carcass via which it is impossible to touch the flesh of the carcass itself does not become impure, **an animal** still **in its hide should not impart impurity,** as one can touch the hide, which constitutes a protective layer, but not the flesh. Abaye answered: **Go out** and **see how many orifices there are in** the body of an animal via which one can touch the flesh, e.g., the eyes, the nostrils, and the mouth.

Rav Zeira poses a challenge to Abaye: if the principle is that one is *tamei* only when it is possible to touch the underlying flesh, how does touching a whole animal carcass — a standard *neveilah* — ever produce *tumah*? The animal has just died. Its skin is intact. There is no exposed meat anywhere on the surface. Yet it is universally accepted that touching a *neveilah* makes one *tamei*. This seems to contradict the rule that touching is only effective when the flesh is accessible.

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye answers without hesitation: *puch chazi kama kavvei it bei* — go see how many openings there are in a cow. An animal's body has natural orifices: the mouth, the nostrils. One can reach into the mouth and make direct contact with the flesh inside without touching the skin at all. Since it is possible to access the flesh through these openings, the condition is satisfied, and touching any part of the exterior — the skin, the bone — causes *tumah* because the flesh is accessible in principle.

### Rav Pappa's Q.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, כּוּלְיָא בְּחֶלְבָּהּ לֹא תְּטַמֵּא? תָּא חֲזִי כַּמָּה חוּטִין נִמְשָׁכִין הֵימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Pappa said to Rava: If that is so,** that one who touches the protective layer of a carcass via which it is impossible to touch the flesh of the carcass itself does not become impure, **the kidney** of a carcass that is completely covered **in its fat should not impart impurity** via contact. Rava answered: **Come** and **see how many sinews emerge from** the kidney that one is able to touch.

Rav Pappa raises a more refined challenge to Rava: if kidneys are surrounded by heavy fat — *cheilev* — and one were to pick up the kidneys, one would be handling the fat rather than the meat. Fat is not *metamei* in the same way meat is; the underlying kidneys are not directly accessible without first removing or penetrating the fat layer. How then does touching or carrying the kidneys cause *tumah* if the flesh itself is not reachable?

### Rava's Answer

Rava responds: *ta chazi kama chutin nimshachim heimena* — look and you will see that there are many thin strands of meat that run through the fat surrounding the kidneys. These strands are present throughout the fat layer and reach the surface. Since the meat is in fact accessible — it is exposed through and within the fat — the condition of *she-efshar liga* is satisfied, and touching the exterior causes *tumah*.

### Rav Ushaya's Q.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אוּשַׁעְיָא: חִישֵּׁב עָלֶיהָ לְנוֹקְבָהּ וְלֹא נִיקְּבָה, מַהוּ? מְחוּסַּר נְקִיבָה כִּמְחוּסַּר מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא? הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: מְחוּסַּר נְקִיבָה לָאו כִּמְחוּסַּר מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** §The mishna taught that the *halakha* distinguishes between a sealed and a perforated thigh bone of a carcass or a creeping animal. With regard to this topic, **Rav Oshaya raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if **one intended to perforate** the thigh bone **but did not** yet **perforate it?** This is the dilemma: Is a thigh bone **lacking perforation considered to be lacking** the necessary **action** for it to impart impurity, and it therefore retains its status as a sealed thigh bone, **or not?** Rabbi Oshaya **then resolves** the dilemma: A thigh bone **lacking perforation is not considered to be lacking** the necessary **action** for it to impart impurity. If one intends to perforate it, it imparts impurity immediately.

Rav Ushaya raises a more abstract question: what if one intends to drill a hole in the bone but has not yet done so? At the present moment, the bone is sealed and the marrow is inaccessible. But one is planning to make the hole. Does the intention to create access count as a form of potential accessibility — such that even now, without the hole, the bone functions as a *shomer* and causes *tumah*?

### Resolution

Rav Ushaya himself resolves the question: *mechusar nekiva lav ke-mechusar ma'aseh dami* — lacking a hole is not equivalent to lacking an action. The absence of a physical hole is simply a physical state; it does not have the legal status of an incomplete process the way an unmade transaction might. One requires actual, present physical accessibility. Intention to create future access does not transform the current sealed state of the bone into one where the marrow is reachable. The bone remains *tahor* until the hole is actually made. Strikingly, the *gemara* notes that Rav Ushaya reached this resolution by pure reasoning, without citing a text — an unusual feature worth noting.

## Reptile Eggs

### Sealed Egg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּיצַת הַשֶּׁרֶץ הַמְרוּקֶּמֶת טְהוֹרָה, נִיקְּבָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – טָמֵא. עַכְבָּר שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ בָּשָׂר וְחֶצְיוֹ אֲדָמָה – הַנּוֹגֵעַ בַּבָּשָׂר טָמֵא, בָּאֲדָמָה טָהוֹר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַנּוֹגֵעַ בָּאֲדָמָה שֶׁכְּנֶגֶד הַבָּשָׂר טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The egg of a creeping animal in which tissue** of an embryo **developed** and one who comes into contact with the egg are **ritually pure,** as the impure creeping animal is hermetically sealed. But if **one perforated** the egg with a hole of **any size,** one who comes in contact with the egg is **ritually impure.** In the case of **a mouse that** grows from the ground and **is half-flesh half-earth, one who touches the** half that is **flesh is impure;** one who touches **the** half that is **earth is pure. Rabbi Yehuda says: Even one who touches the** half that is **earth where it is adjacent to the flesh is ritually impure.**

The *gemara* now applies the same inaccessibility principle to *beitzat ha-sheretz ha-merukemet* — an egg of one of the *sheratzim* species (here referring to the reptiles listed in the Torah, which lay eggs, in contrast to the mice and rats that are mammals) in which the embryo has taken form. Rashi specifies these as the *tzav*, *leta'ah*, and *chomet* — the reptilian members of the *sheratzim* group. If such an egg is intact and sealed, it is *tahor*. The developing fetus inside is not accessible; one cannot touch it from the outside. The same principle that governs the sealed bone of a *neveilah* applies: since touching the fetus itself is impossible, touching the shell does not constitute *tumah*-bearing contact.

### Hole in Egg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הַטְּמֵאִים״ – לְרַבּוֹת בֵּיצַת הַשֶּׁרֶץ וְקוּלִית הַשֶּׁרֶץ. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ לֹא רִיקְּמָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַשֶּׁרֶץ״ – מָה שֶׁרֶץ שֶׁרִקֵּם, אַף בֵּיצַת הַשֶּׁרֶץ שֶׁרִקְּמָה. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ לֹא נִיקְּבוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַנּוֹגֵעַ … יִטְמָא״, אֶת שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לִיגַּע – טָמֵא, וְאֶת שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לִיגַּע – טָהוֹר. וְכַמָּה נְקִיבָתָהּ? כְּחוּט הַשַּׂעֲרָה, שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לִיגַּע כְּחוּט הַשַּׂעֲרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that in the case of an egg of a creeping animal in which tissue of an embryo developed, if it is perforated then one who touches it is ritually impure. With regard to this matter **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "These are the impure ones to you among all that creep; whoever touches them when they are dead shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 11:31). The term **"the impure ones"** is interpreted as **including the egg of a creeping animal and the thigh bone of a creeping animal** in the category of sources of impurity. One **might** have thought that **even** an egg **in which tissue** of an embryo **has not developed** imparts impurity. Therefore, **the verse states: "That creep,"** indicating that **just as a creeping animal has developed tissue, so too** only **the egg of a creeping animal in which tissue** of an embryo **has developed** imparts impurity. One **might** have thought that in the case of an egg in which tissue of an embryo has developed, it imparts impurity **even** if it **was not perforated.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Whoever touches** them when they are dead **shall be impure,"** indicating that **when it is possible to touch** the flesh, contact renders one **impure; but when it is impossible to touch** the flesh, one remains **pure.** **And how** large must **its perforation** be to render one touching the egg or thigh bone impure? Its width must be the size of **a strand of hair, as it is possible** for one **to touch** the inside of an egg or thigh bone **with a strand of** his **hair.**

If the egg has even the smallest hole — *nikvah kol she hu* — it becomes *tamei*. How small a hole? *Ke-chut ha-sa'arah* — the size of a hair. The *gemara* explains why: because even a pinhole-sized opening means one could, in principle, insert the hair of one's head — while it remains attached to one's body — through the hole and make contact with the fetus inside. That theoretical capacity for contact is sufficient to satisfy the condition of *she-efshar liga*, and the egg therefore causes *tumah* if touched. The connection back to the bone of a *neveilah* is explicit: the same derivation from *ha-nogea yitma* — possible touching renders one *tamei*, impossible touching does not — applies to both.

## Half-Flesh Mouse

### Basic Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַכְבָּר שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ [וְכוּ׳]. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: וְהוּא שֶׁהִשְׁרִיץ עַל פְּנֵי כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** §The mishna teaches: In the case of **a mouse that** grows from the ground and **is half-**flesh half-earth, one who touches the half that is flesh is impure. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says** in this regard: One becomes impure in such a case **only** if the flesh of the mouse has **developed along the entire** length of the mouse, from head to foot.

The *mishnah* presents what the *gemara* treats as a genuine phenomenon: a mouse in mid-stage spontaneous generation, *achbar she-chetziyo basar ve-chetziyo adamah* — half flesh and half dirt. The presumption is that certain small rodents could be found in this intermediate state, partially emerged from the earth as living creatures. The governing rule of the *mishnah* is that if one touches the flesh half, one becomes *tamei*; if one touches only the dirt half, one is *tahor*.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַנּוֹגֵעַ בָּאֲדָמָה שֶׁכְּנֶגֶד הַבָּשָׂר טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even one who touches the** half that is **earth where it is adjacent to the flesh is ritually impure.**

Rabbi Yehuda extends the ruling: *af ha-nogea ba-adamah she-keneged ha-basar tamei* — even one who touches the dirt half, if that dirt portion is situated opposite or parallel to the flesh half, becomes *tamei*. Rabbi Yehuda treats the organism as a unified whole: once part of it is flesh and constitutes a *sheretz*, the entire form — including the still-earth portion directly adjacent to the flesh — is drawn into the *tumah* orbit of the whole creature.

## Reptile Eggs Derivation

### Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַנּוֹגֵעַ בָּאֲדָמָה שֶׁכְּנֶגֶד בָּשָׂר טָמֵא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: וְהוּא שֶׁהִשְׁרִיץ עַל פְּנֵי כּוּלּוֹ. מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַרֵישָׁא, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אַסֵּיפָא, וּמַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא – אֲבָל רֵישָׁא, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא הִשְׁרִיץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Some teach** the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna: **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even one who touches** the half that is **earth where it is adjacent to the flesh is ritually impure. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** One becomes impure in such a case **only** if the flesh of the mouse has **developed along the entire** length of the mouse. **The one who teaches** the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **with regard to the first clause** of the mishna, **all the more so** he teaches this same *halakha* **with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna. **But the one who teaches** the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna holds that it applies only to that clause, with regard to one who touches the half that is earth where it is adjacent to the flesh according to Rabbi Yehuda. **But** according to the opinion of the first *tanna*, stated in **the first clause** of the mishna, **even if** the flesh of the mouse **has not developed** along the entire length of the mouse, one who touches the half of the mouse that is flesh is impure.

The *beraita* derives the rules about reptile eggs and bones from the verse: *ha-teme'im* — "those that are impure to you" — is expanded to include the egg of a *sheretz* and the *kulei* (thigh bone) of a *sheretz*. From the comparison *mah sheretz she-rakam* — just as a *sheretz* must be fully formed to cause *tumah* — the *gemara* derives that an egg only causes *tumah* if the embryo within is formed, *she-rikmah*. The verse *ha-nogea yitma* then limits the *tumah* to cases where touching is possible: *she-efshar liga tamei ve-she-i efshar liga tahor* — if it can be touched, one is *tamei*; if it cannot, one is *tahor*. And the minimum size of a hole that makes the interior touchable is *ke-chut ha-sa'arah* — the width of a single hair — because that is the smallest conduit through which meaningful contact can theoretically be made.

## Half-Flesh Mouse Continued

### Movement Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״עַכְבָּר״, שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי אֲפִילּוּ עַכְבָּר שֶׁבַּיָּם, שֶׁשְּׁמוֹ עַכְבָּר. וְדִין הוּא: טִימֵּא בְּחוּלְדָּה וְטִימֵּא בְּעַכְבָּר, מָה חוּלְדָּה מִין הַגָּדֵל עַל הָאָרֶץ, אַף עַכְבָּר מִין הַגָּדֵל עַל הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** §**The Sages taught** in a *baraita* concerning the following verse: "And these are they which are impure to you among the creeping animals that creep upon the earth: The weasel, and the mouse, and the great lizard after its kinds" (Leviticus 11:29). **Since "mouse" is stated** among the creeping animals that impart impurity, **I** would **derive** that **even a sea mouse,** i.e., a sea creature that has an appearance similar to a mouse, imparts impurity **because its name is** also **mouse. But** ostensibly, the opposite conclusion could be derived through **logical inference:** The verse **deems a weasel impure and deems a mouse impure.** Therefore, **just as a weasel is a species that grows on land, so too** the **mouse** to which the verse is referring is **a species that grows on land;** a sea mouse does not impart impurity.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi qualifies Rabbi Yehuda's ruling: *ve-hu she-hishritz pnei kulo* — Rabbi Yehuda's rule that touching the dirt half causes *tumah* applies only if the entire face of the creature has already begun to move, that is, if the organism has shown signs of life and locomotion. Without that sign of life, even the flesh half would not yet be considered a fully actualized *sheretz* for *tumah* purposes.

### Logical Inference

The *gemara* notes that this statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is attributed by some to the first clause of the *mishnah* — that is, to the basic rule that touching the flesh half causes *tumah* — and by others to Rabbi Yehuda's extension. The difference has a logical implication. If Rabbi Yehoshua's condition — that the creature must have begun to move — applies to the first clause, then it applies *a fortiori* to Rabbi Yehuda: if even the flesh half requires movement before causing *tumah*, certainly the dirt half that Rabbi Yehuda includes requires that the flesh half have begun to move. But if Rabbi Yehoshua's condition attaches only to Rabbi Yehuda's extension, then the first clause — touching the flesh half causes *tumah* — may hold even without movement having occurred, and only Rabbi Yehuda's more expansive ruling about the dirt half is limited to cases where the flesh has already stirred.
