# Bekhorot 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Athens Riddles & Parables

### Athens Story Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הַהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאָזֵיל וּבָעֵי אִתְּתָא וְלָא יָהֲבוּ לֵיהּ, מַאי חֲזֵי לֵיהּ דְּאָזֵיל הֵיכָא דְּמִדְּלוּ מִינָּיהּ? שְׁקַל סִיכְּתָא, דָּצַהּ לְתַתַּאי — לָא עָאל, לְעִילַּאי — עָאל. אֲמַר: הַאי נָמֵי מִיתְרְמֵי בַּת מַזָּלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The sages of Athens **said to him:** In the case of **a certain man who goes and asks** to marry **a woman and** her family **does not give** her **to him, why would he see** fit **to go to** a family **that is greater than** the first? Rabbi Yehoshua **took a peg** and **stuck it** into the **lower** part of the wall, but it did **not go in.** He then stuck it **into the upper** portion of the wall where there was a hole, and it **went in. He said** to them: In **this** case **too,** where he goes to a more distinguished family than the first, perhaps he will **find the girl destined** for him.

We begin in the middle of 8b, in the midst of a fascinating story about the sages of Athens. The Gemara had already presented the first question, which concerned why one would give a man a woman above his station. Now we proceed to the next exchange.

### Lending Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבְרָא דְּאוֹזֵיף וְטָרֵיף, מַאי חֲזָא דַּהֲדַר אוֹזֵיף? אֲמַר לְהוּ: גַּבְרָא אֲזַל לְאַגְמָא, קְטַל קַמָּא טוּנָא וְלָא מָצֵי בֵּיהּ, קָטֵיל וּמַנַּח עִילָּוֵיהּ, עַד (דְּאִיתְרְמִי) [דְּמִיתְרְמֵי] אִינִישׁ מַדְלֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The sages of Athens asked him another question: In the case of **a man who lends** money to an individual and the borrower does not repay the loan, **and** the lender **repossesses** the borrower's property as payment instead, **why would** he **see** fit **to lend** to others **again?** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them:** This is what people do: If **a man goes to the pond, initially cuts a bundle** of reeds, **but** finds that he **cannot** lift **it,** he does not stop cutting wood. Instead, he **cuts** more wood **and places** it **upon** the first, **until a person happens** to pass by and helps him **raise it** upon his shoulders, so that he can carry it home. So too, a person continues to lend based on the assumption that he will eventually find an appropriate borrower.

The passage opens with the phrase *gavra de'ozif vetarif* — a man who lent money and then had to seize property in payment of his debt, meaning he did not recover his money easily. The question posed to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya is: *mai chazi dehadar ozif* — what would motivate him to lend again, having already risked his principal and endured such difficulty in collecting?

He responds with a parable. *Gavra azal le'agma* — imagine a man who went into the forest. *Kataf kama tuna* — he cut down a first load of wood, but it was too large for him to carry. Rather than abandon the wood, *kataf u'manach alaihu* — he kept chopping and adding to the pile. *Ad de'rami inish u'matli lei* — until someone else came along and was able to help him carry it.

### Parable Meaning

On the surface this seems to say: if at first you don't succeed, try again. You keep at it, and eventually perhaps the next person you lend to will be more trustworthy. But the meaning is somewhat elusive. The Maharal notes that the subsequent riddles will be outright nonsensical, whereas at least here both the question and the answer are reasonable, even if the deeper significance is not immediately apparent.

Both the Maharal and the Maharsha read this passage with a similar interpretive lens, understanding it as carrying a subtext about Greek versus Jewish wisdom, or more broadly about the worldly success of Rome versus the apparent lack of worldly success of the Jewish people. Understood this way, the question is: you Jews performed *mitzvot* and did not fulfill them properly — like a borrower who failed to repay. God had to collect His debt by force, through the destruction of the *beit hamikdash* and exile. What reason is there for God to continue that relationship?

The answer is: we keep chopping wood. We keep performing our *mitzvot*. Even if one generation did not succeed, we continue, and other people come along and help sustain the relationship. We believe that through continued observance across generations, the bond endures and redemption will come, even if it has not come yet.

### Literal vs. Metaphorical

This is an example where the literal reading simply cannot hold. Taken literally, one would have a case for the banking crisis — a reason never to throw good money after bad. The point, of course, is not blind repetition but persistence guided by wisdom, hoping the next venture proves more worthy. One cannot remain locked in a purely literal reading of such material.

A further note: the Rabbi Yehoshua mentioned in this passage is Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya, who is not to be confused with the Rabbi Yehoshua who was the teacher of Rabbi Akiva.

## Impossible Riddles

### Mule Riddle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לַן מִילֵּי דִּבְדִיאִי! אֲמַר לְהוּ: הֲוָה הַהִוא כּוּדַנְיְתָא דִּילִידָא, וַהֲוָה תְּלֵי לֵיהּ פִּיתְקָא, וּכְתַב בֵּיהּ דְּמַסֵּיק בְּבֵי אַבָּא מְאָה אַלְפָּא זוּזֵי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְכוּדַנְיְתָא מִי יָלְדָה? אֲמַר לְהוּ: (הֵי נִיהוּ) [הָנֵי נִינְהוּ] מִילֵּי דִבְדִיאִי.
>
> **Translation:** The sages of Athens **said to him: Say to us a matter of nonsense.** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them: There was a certain mule that gave birth, and a note was hanging on** the newborn mule's neck **and on it was written that it is owed 100,000 dinars by its father's household. They said to him: But can a mule give birth?** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them: This is** why it is **a matter of nonsense,** as it is impossible for a mule to give birth.

The riddles now shift to a fundamentally different register — pure, unresolvable puzzles. The sages of Athens say to him: *eima lan mili de'bedai* — tell us a riddle, something fictitious, a made-up scenario. He responds: *havei hahu kudnisa deyaldah* — there was a female mule that gave birth, and around it hung a sign on which was written that the mule's father's household owed a debt of a hundred thousand *zuz*. They object: *vekudnisa miyalda* — but do mules give birth? He replies: *haynu milei de'beduyei* — precisely, that was the riddle I told you.

### Salt Riddle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִילְחָא כִּי סָרְיָא, בְּמַאי מָלְחִי לַהּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: בְּסִילְתָּא דְכוּדַנְיְתָא. וּמִי אִיכָּא סִילְתָּא לְכוּדַנְתָּא? וּמִילְחָא מִי סְרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The sages of Athens then asked another question: **When salt is spoiling, with what does one salt it** to preserve it? Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them: With the placenta of a mule.** They said to him: **But is there a placenta of a mule?** Rabbi Yehoshua said to them: **And does salt spoil?**

They then ask: *milcha ki sarya bemai malchi lei* — if salt goes bad, what do you salt it with? He answers: with the afterbirth of a mule. They ask again: *umi ika silta lekudnisa* — does a mule have an afterbirth? And he replies: *umilcha mi sarya* — and does salt go bad? The structure is identical: an impossible premise answered with another impossible premise.

### House in Air

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנִי לַן בֵּיתָא בַּאֲוִירָא דְּעָלְמָא! אָמַר שֵׁם, תְּלָא בֵּין רְקִיעָא לְאַרְעָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַסִּיקוּ לִי לִיבְנֵי וְטִינָא. מְצִיעֲתֵיהּ דְּעָלְמָא הֵיכָא? זַקְפַהּ לְאֶצְבַּעְתֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָכָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וּמִי יֵימַר? אַיְיתוֹ אַשְׁלֵי וּמְשַׁחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** They said to him: **Build us a house in the air of the world.** Rabbi Yehoshua **uttered a Name** of God and **hovered between the sky and the earth. He said to them: Bring up to me bricks and mortar** and I will build you a house here. They asked him: **Where is the center of the world?** Rabbi Yehoshua **raised his finger** and **said to them: Here. They said to him: And who says** that you are correct? He said to them: **Bring ropes and measure.**

Next: *banei lan beita ba'avira de'alma* — build us a house in the air. He uttered a divine name and *tala bein raki'a le'ara* — suspended himself between heaven and earth. He then called out: *asiku li livnei vetinei* — hand me bricks and mortar and I will build it. Presumably they could not reach him, and that was the end of that exchange.

### World's Center

They then asked: *metiatza de'alma haycha* — where is the middle of the world? He stuck out his finger and said: here. They challenged him — *umi yemar* — how can you prove it? He replied: bring me measuring ropes long enough to measure the entire world, and I will prove it. There clearly seems to be a subtext at work, though the Gemara does not spell it out explicitly.

### Physical vs. Divine

The Maharsha attempts to read the ongoing sequence as a confrontation between a physical orientation toward the world and an orientation toward God. If one is oriented toward the divine, then since God's presence is everywhere, every point is equally a center — the world is infinite in the relevant sense, and everywhere can be its middle. If one holds an exclusively physical orientation, then to establish the middle you would literally need to measure. The building of the house in the air and the pointing to the middle of the world both carry something of this sense of connecting between the divine and the physical, though the Gemara itself provides no systematic explanation and only comments on the first two exchanges. For the remainder, as the Maharal acknowledges, there is no clearly satisfying subtext — it was, at least in part, simply a battle of wits.

## Practical Riddles

### Well to City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִית לַן בֵּירָא בְּדַבְרָא, עַיְילֵאּ לְמָתָא! אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַפְשִׁילוּ לִי חַבְלֵי מִפָּארֵי וַאֲעַיְּילֵיהּ. אִית לַן רִיחְיָא דִּתְבִירָא, חַיְּיטַיהּ. אֲמַר: כְּרוּכוּ לִי מִינַּיהּ גַּרְדֵי וְאִיחַיְּיטַיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** They said to him: **We have a pit in the field; bring it to the city.** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them: Braid ropes** made **of bran for me and I will bring it** to the city with them. They then said to him: **We have a mill that broke; sew it** back together. Rabbi Yehoshua **said** to them: **Pull out threads from** the mill **for me and I will sew them** together.

The Athenians say: *ith lan bira bedavra, aila lamata* — we have a well out in the field; bring it to the city. This has the flavor of a Chelm story. He replies: *achviti li chavlei mipari va'aila* — show me a rope made of bran and I will use it to draw the well in. Just as you cannot make such a rope, you cannot bring in a well.

### Millstone Repair

They then present a broken millstone and ask him to sew it up. He says: *kukhu li minei darda'ei* — make me threads from the millstone itself, and I will sew it. Just as a garment is sewn with threads drawn from its own material, if you want the millstone sewn, provide the threads from the stone — which of course cannot be done.

### Knife Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְשָׁרָא דְּסַכִּינֵי בְּמַאי קָטְלִי? בְּקַרְנָא דַּחֲמָרָא. וּמִי אִיכָּא קַרְנָא לַחֲמָרָא? וּמִי אִיכָּא מְשָׁרָא דְּסַכִּינֵי?
>
> **Translation:** They asked him another question: **With what does one harvest a field of knives?** Rabbi Yehoshua answered: **With the horn of a donkey.** They said to him: **But is there** such an item as **a horn of a donkey?** He said to them: **But is there** such a thing as **a field of knives?**

They ask: *mishrada de'sakhina bemai katli* — how would you cut down a bed of knives? He says: with the horn of a donkey. They ask: is there a horn of a donkey? He replies: and is there such a thing as a bed of knives?

## Eggs & Soul

### Egg Origins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיְיתוֹ לֵיהּ תְּרֵי בֵּיעֵי, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הֵי דְּזַגְתָּא אוּכַּמְתִּי וְהֵי דְּזַגְתָּא חִיוַּורְתִּי? אַיְיתִי לְהוּ אִיהוּ תְּרֵי גְּבִינֵי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הֵי דְּעִיזָּא אוּכַּמְתִּי וְהֵי דְּעִיזָּא חִיוַּורְתִּי?
>
> **Translation:** **They brought him two eggs** and **said to him: Which is** the egg **of a black hen and which is** the egg **of a white hen?** Rabbi Yehoshua **brought them two cheeses,** and **said to them: Which** is the cheese **from the black goat and which** is **from the white goat?**

They brought him two eggs and asked: *hai de'ziksa ukhmita ve'hai de'ziksa khivarta* — which one came from the black hen and which from the white?

### Cheese Origins

In reply, he brought them two rounds of cheese and asked: which came from the black goat and which from the white? He turned the unanswerable question back on them in identical form.

### Soul Escape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַרְצוּצָא דְּמִית, מֵהֵיכָא נָפֵיק רוּחֵיהּ? מֵהֵיכָא דְּעָל נְפַק. אַחְוִי לַן מָנָא דְּלָא שָׁוֵי חֲבִילֵיהּ! אַיְיתִי (כֹּל חַד וְחַד) בּוּדְיָא, פַּשְׁטֵיהּ. לָא הֲוָה עָיֵיל בְּתַרְעָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַיְיתוֹ מָרֵי סִיתְרוּהּ. הַיְינוּ מָנָא דְּלָא שָׁוֵי חֲבִילֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** They asked him: **And** in the case of **a chick that dies** inside the egg, **from where does its spirit exit?** Rabbi Yehoshua said to them: **From where it enters, it departs.** They said to him: **Show us a utensil that is not worth the damage** that it causes. Rabbi Yehoshua said to them: **Each one of you bring a mat** and **spread it out.** When they did so, each mat **did not enter the gate** due to its size. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them: Bring axes** and **break** the gate so that you can bring it inside. **This is a utensil that is not worth its damage.**

The final riddle in this series is the most theologically charged. *Ve'rtzutza de'mis me'heikha nafik* — when a chick dies inside the egg, where does the spirit escape? It is trapped; there is no opening. He answers: *me'heikha de'al nafik* — it exits the same way it entered. The implicit argument is that spirit is not physical. If one imagines *ruach* as something wind-like and material, the question is genuinely difficult — how does it get in or out of a sealed egg? His answer insists that spirit is wholly non-physical, and therefore the question of where it exits is no more puzzling than the question of how it entered. The physical orientation that would make the question pressing is itself the problem.

## Mat & Door

### Worthless Vessel

They ask him to show them a vessel that is not worth the damage involved in preserving it — a case where the cost of protecting the object exceeds its value.

### Mat Through Door

He brought a reed mat, *budya*, and opened it up. It was too wide to fit through the door. He then asked for a hatchet and chopped down the door in order to get the mat through. *Hainu mana de'lo shavei khavlei* — this is the vessel not worth its damage. As the Maharal notes, for most of this sequence he simply reads it as a battle of wits without a clear deeper subtext — and apparently a whole book has been written attempting to unpack the full meaning of this Gemara. For now, we accept it as a remarkable exchange whose full significance remains elusive.

## Boat & Bei Beli

### Victory & Boat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיְתִינְהוּ כֹּל חַד וְחַד, כִּי חָזֵי שִׁיתִּין בִּיסְתַּרְקֵי, אָמַר: כּוּלְּהוּ חַבְרַאי לְהָכָא אָתוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְסָפוֹנָא: שְׁרִי סְפִינְתָּךְ. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאָתוּ שְׁקַל עַפְרָא מֵעַפְרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** After Rabbi Yehoshua defeated the sages of Athens in debate, **he brought them** to his ship as they had agreed. He then brought each one to a different room. **When each one saw the sixty mattresses** in his room, **he said** to himself: **All of my colleagues are coming here,** and waited for them without attempting to leave. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to the sailor: Release your ship,** i.e., begin sailing. **While they were coming** to the emperor in Rome, **he took** out some **dirt** that he had taken **from the dirt of** Athens.

Rabbi Yehoshua won the disputation. Having bested them, *asinhu kol khad ve'khad* — he brought each of the Athenian sages onto his boat. This has the quality of a classic joke in which each side interprets the same exchange entirely differently, each believing himself the victor. And yet Rabbi Yehoshua had indeed won.

### Chambers Trap

Each sage entered a separate chamber. But each chamber contained sixty seats, so upon seeing the sixty seats every sage assumed that the others would be joining him — no one felt he was being trapped because each assumed they would all be together. That collective sense of safety prevented resistance. In the end, Rabbi Yehoshua placed each one in a separate chamber and presumably secured it. Before departing, he took some dirt from Athens — *shakal afra me'afra* — which will prove relevant at the end of the story.

### Bei Beli Waters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מָטֵי לְבֵי בְלִיעֵי, מְלָא כּוּזָא דְּמַיָּא מִבֵּי בְלִיעֵי. כַּד אֲתוֹ, אוֹקְמִינְהוּ קַמֵּי קֵיסָר. חֲזָנְהוּ דַּהֲווֹ מְעַנִּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי לָאו נִינְהוּ. שְׁקַל מֵעַפְרַיְיהוּ וּשְׁדָא עִילָּוַיְיהוּ, אַקְשׁוֹ לְאַפֵּי מַלְכָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֹּל דְּבָעֵית עֲבֵיד בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When they reached the House of the Swallowed,** a location in the sea where the water absorbs everything in its vicinity, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya **filled up a small jug of water from the House of the Swallowed. When they arrived, he presented** the sages of Athens **before the emperor.** The emperor **saw that** the sages of Athens **were suffering** due to their having been taken captive. He **said to** Rabbi Yehoshua: **These** individuals **cannot be** the sages of Athens, because those sages are strong and arrogant. Rabbi Yehoshua **took** some **of the dirt** that he had taken from Athens **and threw** it **upon them.** When they smelled the scent of their own soil they began to act like themselves again, and **they spoke with arrogance before the king.** Upon hearing them, the emperor **said to** Rabbi Yehoshua: You may **do with them whatever you wish.**

When they arrived at *Bei Beli*, which appears in the *midrash* on Kohelet in connection with the verse *kol haNechalim holchim el hayam veha'yam einenu malei* — all the rivers flow to the sea yet the sea is never full — this is a place of primeval swallowing waters, a kind of aquatic black hole into which all waters pour and from which nothing returns. He filled a vessel with water from this place: *mallei kuza de'maya mibei beli*.

### Athens Dirt Remedy

When they arrived and were brought before Caesar, *chazinhu dehavu ma'anu* — he saw that the Athenian sages looked afflicted and diminished, not at all in their usual prestigious form. Caesar remarked that these did not appear to be the same men. Rabbi Yehoshua took the dirt he had collected from Athens and threw it upon them, and they revived — the smell or substance of their native place resuscitated them. Upon recovering, they spoke harshly to the king for having set this trap. Caesar, for his part, told Rabbi Yehoshua: *kol deba'it avid behu* — do with them as you wish, they are yours.

### Infinite Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיְיתִינְהוּ מַיָּא דְּאַיְיתִי מִבֵּי בְלִיעֵי, שְׁדִינְהוּ בְּתִיגְדָּא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַלְיוּהּ לְהוּ וְאֵיזִילוּ לְכוּ. מְלוֹ וּשְׁדוֹ בֵּיהּ קַמָּאֵי קַמָּאֵי, וּבְלַע לְהוּ, מְלוֹ עַד דְּשָׁמֵיט כַּתְפַּיְיהוּ, וּבְלוֹ לְהוּ וַאֲזוּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **brought the water that he had brought from the House of the Swallowed** and **poured it into a utensil [*betigada*]. He said to** the sages: **Fill it up and go on your** way. **They filled and poured one by one,** i.e., they filled the utensil by pouring in water numerous times, **and** each time the utensil **swallowed** up the water. They continued to **fill** it **until their shoulders became disjointed.** In this manner, the sages of Athens **were defeated.**

Rabbi Yehoshua then took the water from *Bei Beli*, placed it in a vessel, and told them: fill this vessel and you may go. *Malo ve'shadu bah kammai kammai uvala lehu* — they poured in water and it was absorbed immediately, again and again, until *ad deshamat katfayhu* — their shoulders gave out from the effort and they themselves wore away and were consumed. That is the end of the story.

What exactly is being conveyed here is not fully transparent. The sense that suggests itself at the end is something about the futility of a purely this-worldly orientation. All of the worldly success you pursue ultimately disappears into an infinite vessel. To fill such a vessel you would need some connection to the infinite — and that, perhaps, is the suggestion: a non-religious or purely worldly approach ultimately exhausts itself without ever being satisfied.

## Theology & Science

### Greek vs. Torah

This entire story arose from a digression about snakes, which itself emerged from a discussion about the *Baraita* on different animals and the status of kosher fish found inside non-kosher fish. The larger subtext seems to involve either Greek wisdom versus Torah wisdom, or the contrast between the this-worldly success of Rome and the different kind of success represented by the Jewish people.

We are, as a civilization, heirs of the Greek empirical approach to knowledge — the tradition of rational, observational inquiry rather than derivation from authoritative texts — and that tradition accounts for much of human scientific progress. This raises genuine questions about the degree to which the Gemara's scientific knowledge should be treated as normative.

### Rambam Science

The Rambam stated long ago that one should not hold it against *Chazal* if their science was not our science. They were men of Torah and religion, not professional scientists, and the principle that scientific progress can and should be incorporated into religious life is one the Rambam articulated clearly. There are indeed *poskim* who are willing to reassess *halacha* when scientific knowledge points otherwise. Rav Moshe Feinstein, for instance, stated in principle — outside the domain of *treifot*, which has *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai* dimensions — that *halacha* can in principle be revised in light of new scientific knowledge, though in practice he invoked this primarily in cases of profound human need.

### Gestation & Water

The riddles in this passage are not merely asking questions about the soul of a chick — those might be read as religious questions. They also include questions of a straightforwardly empirical kind, about animal gestation periods and the physiological behavior of water in animals. With respect to those kinds of questions, we do tend to side with the Greek empirical approach. One might ask: why would *Chazal* have assumed a gestation period of three years for a particular animal if it is in fact twelve months? One could simply observe. The answer to that kind of question remains genuinely unclear. What is clear is that when *halacha* is affected by such scientific claims — as when the Gemara distinguishes between different animals' urine, or makes claims about whether an animal passes water unchanged — these are questions that ongoing *halachic* discourse continues to grapple with, and there are two recognized camps on the question of how changing scientific knowledge should bear on *halacha*.

## Mishnah Donkeys

### Two Males

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חֲמוֹרָה שֶׁלֹּא בִּיכְּרָה וְיָלְדָה שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — נוֹתֵן טָלֶה אֶחָד לַכֹּהֵן, זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה — מַפְרִישׁ טָלֶה אֶחָד לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a female donkey that had not** previously **given birth and** now **gave birth to two male** offspring, as there is no doubt that one of them is firstborn, its owner **gives one lamb to the priest** in redemption of that firstborn. If it gave birth to **a male and a female** and it is not known which was born first, **he designates one lamb** as firstborn in case the male was born first. Nevertheless, since it is merely a monetary debt to the priest, the burden of proof rests upon the claimant, in this case the priest. Due to that uncertainty, the priest can offer no proof and the owner keeps the lamb **for himself.**

The next *mishnah* takes up a new topic: *chamora shelo bikra veyalda sheni zacharim* — a donkey that has never previously given birth, whose first offspring is therefore a *bechor* if male, and which gave birth to two males. You simply do not know which emerged first. In this case, unlike with sheep and cattle, the question of which one is the *bechor* is not critical, because the *mitzvah* is simply to redeem it with a *seh*. There is no *korban* at stake. Therefore: *noten echad le'kohen* — you give one sheep to the *kohen*, and the obligation is discharged regardless of which of the two males was technically first.

### Male & Female

If the donkey gave birth to a male and a female and you did not witness the birth — say you found them in the morning and do not know which came first — then if the female emerged first, there is no *bechor*, and if the male emerged first, there is. What do you do? *Mafrish seh echad le'atzmo* — you separate one sheep to perform the act of redemption, but you keep it for yourself rather than giving it to the *kohen*. This is because, as we learned regarding *matnot kehunah*, the sheep used for redemption carries no *kedusha*; it becomes the *kohen*'s monetary property. Here, however, since the *kohen* cannot prove you were obligated, the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* — the burden of proof rests on the claimant — applies. You performed the act of redemption out of caution, but the *kohen* cannot establish entitlement, and so you retain the sheep.

### Two Donkeys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי חֲמוֹרָיו שֶׁלֹּא בִּיכְּרוּ וְיָלְדוּ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — נוֹתֵן שְׁנֵי טְלָאִים לַכֹּהֵן. זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה, אוֹ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבָה — נוֹתֵן טָלֶה אֶחָד לַכֹּהֵן. שְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת וְזָכָר, אוֹ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים וּשְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת — אֵין לַכֹּהֵן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** If an individual has two donkeys, and both of **his two donkeys had not** previously **given birth and they** now **gave birth to two males,** one each, the owner **gives two lambs to the priest.** If they together gave birth to **a male and a female or** to **two males and a female, he gives one lamb to the priest,** as one of the males is certainly a firstborn. If they together gave birth to **two females and a male or** to **two males and two females, the priest receives nothing,** as perhaps the two firstborn were females.

If you had two donkeys, neither of which had previously given birth, and in the morning you find two males: *noten shnei t'la'im le'kohen* — you give two sheep to the *kohen*, straightforward. If you find a male and a female — or two males and a female — or two males and two females — the question in each case is how many *bechorot* there certainly are and how many are in doubt. Where there is certainly one *bechor*, one sheep goes to the *kohen*. Where there is a possible second, you separate a sheep but keep it for yourself, as above. Where there might be zero *bechorot* — as in the case of two females and one male, where both females might have been born first — *ein la'kohen klum*, the *kohen* gets nothing, because you might owe nothing at all. Though the act of redemption may still be performed out of caution, the *kohen* has no claim.

### One Experienced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַת בִּיכְּרָה, וְאַחַת שֶׁלֹּא בִּיכְּרָה, וְיָלְדָה שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — נוֹתֵן טָלֶה אֶחָד לַכֹּהֵן; זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה — מַפְרִישׁ טָלֶה אֶחָד לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **one** of his donkeys **had** previously **given birth and one had not** previously **given birth and** they now together **gave birth to two males,** the owner **gives one lamb to the priest** as redemption for the firstborn male. If they together gave birth to **a male and a female he designates one lamb for himself,** as it is uncertain whether or not the male was a firstborn and the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.

If one donkey had previously given birth and the other had not, and you find two males: *noten taleh echad le'kohen* — one sheep to the *kohen*, since precisely one of the males is a *bechor*, belonging to the previously undelivered animal. If there is a male and a female and you do not know which came from which animal: *mafrish taleh echad le'atzmo* — you separate one sheep for yourself, since you might have zero obligation if the inexperienced donkey gave birth to the female.

### Redemption Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר תִּפְדֶּה בְשֶׂה״, מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים וּמִן הָעִזִּים, זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה, גָּדוֹל וְקָטָן, תָּמִים וּבַעַל מוּם, פּוֹדֶה בּוֹ פְּעָמִים הַרְבֵּה, נִכְנָס לְדִיר לְהִתְעַשֵּׂר, וְאִם מֵת — נֶהֱנִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that the firstborn of a donkey is redeemed with a lamb? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And you shall redeem the firstborn of a donkey with a lamb [*seh*]"** (Exodus 34:20). The owner may give a lamb either **from sheep or from goats;** from **males or females,** from **older or younger** animals, and from **unblemished or blemished** animals. If the priest returns the lamb to the owner, he **may redeem** firstborn donkeys **with it many times.** In a case where he designates a lamb due to uncertainty and keeps it for himself, it is his in every sense. Consequently, **it enters the pen in order to be tithed** with the other non-sacred animals (see Leviticus 27:32), **and if it dies, one may derive benefit from its** carcass.

The *mishnah* now anchors the basic obligation in a verse: *ufeter chamor tifdeh veseh* — the firstborn of a donkey you shall redeem with a sheep. The verse specifies *min hakvasim umin ha'izim* — whether from the goats or from the sheep, meaning either species qualifies. The *mishnah* then enumerates the broad parameters: *zachar unekeivah* — either male or female; *gadol vekatan* — even a small one, even if its value is less than the donkey; *tamim uba'al mum* — even a blemished one, since this is not a *korban*; and *upodeh bo pe'amim harbeh* — and one may redeem with it multiple times.

### Multiple Redemptions

What does "multiple times" mean? Rashi explains that one could redeem with a sheep, give it to the *kohen*, buy it back, and then redeem another donkey with it. Tosafot offers a reading that fits the *mishnah* more naturally. Tosafot notes that in the context of doubtful cases — where the *kohen* is not going to receive the sheep in any event — one is not cheating the *kohen* by using the same sheep for multiple redemptions. Since all the sheep being separated are being kept by the owner anyway, a single sheep can discharge multiple doubtful obligations. You need not separate one sheep per donkey when all of them are being retained by the owner. This reading explains the larger *chiddush* and fits better with what the *mishnah* is actually presenting, since the *mishnah* never describes a case where a person gives two sheep to a *kohen* and keeps two more for himself. Tosafot's reading is that the rule of redemption with one sheep applies to multiple doubtful animals precisely because the *kohen* loses nothing.

### After Death

The *mishnah* concludes: *venichnas ladir lehite'aser* — the sheep used for redemption may enter the pen to be tithed as *ma'aser behemah*; *ve'im met nehenim bo* — and if it dies, one may derive benefit from it. This final clause could refer to the sheep, which of course carries no sanctity and may be used freely after death. But it may equally refer to the donkey: if the donkey died without having been redeemed and without having its neck broken, may one derive benefit from it? That question will be addressed in the Gemara.

## Tanna Attribution

### Rabbi Yose View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, דְּאִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי — הָאָמַר אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the first clause of the mishna the Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that if a female donkey that had never given birth before gives birth to twin males, the owner gives only one lamb to the priest? **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. As if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, doesn't he say** in a later mishna (17a) with regard to a kosher animal that gave birth to two males and both their heads emerged as one, that **it is possible that** both births **coincided precisely,** and therefore both of the twins are given to the priest? According to his opinion, in the case here as well, two lambs should be given to the priest. The mishna clearly follows the opinion of the Rabbis that only one of the two is considered a firstborn.

The Gemara now asks: *man tanna* — whose position does our *mishnah* reflect? Rabbi Yirmeyah says: it is not the position of Rabbi Yose HaGlili. For if it were Rabbi Yose HaGlili, he holds *efshar le'tzamtzem* — that two animals can emerge at precisely the same moment, and that when that appears to be the case, it is halachically defined as such. The Rabbis disagree, holding that nothing ever occurs at a truly identical moment — there is always a microsecond between them, making one first. Rabbi Yose HaGlili's view is that if it looks simultaneous, it is defined as simultaneous. He further holds that two firstborns emerging from the same mother at the exact same time are *shneihem kedoshim bevechorah* — both are equally sanctified. Accordingly, if our *mishnah* were Rabbi Yose HaGlili's, it should have presented the case of two males emerging simultaneously and required two sheep for the *kohen*, since both might be sanctified.

### Abaye's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דִּכְתִיב ״הַזְּכָרִים לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: You** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** and it **is different there** with regard to a kosher animal, **as it is written:** "Every firstborn that you have of animals, **the males shall be the Lord's"** (Exodus 13:12), i.e., the word "males" is written in the plural. This indicates that if they emerged simultaneously they both have firstborn status. By contrast, with regard to redeeming a firstborn donkey, only one has firstborn status.

Abaye responds: one might still say the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yose HaGlili. The reason Rabbi Yose HaGlili says both are sanctified by sheep is specifically because of a verse — *hazcharim la'Hashem*, "the males to God," a plural formulation indicating that both are consecrated when they emerge simultaneously. No analogous verse applies to donkeys. So Rabbi Yose HaGlili would admit that by donkeys, even if they emerge simultaneously, only one is sanctified — the verse providing double sanctity is unique to the context of sheep.

### Braita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, תַּנְיָא: חֲמוֹרוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּיכְּרָה וְיָלְדָה שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים, וְיָצְאוּ שְׁנֵי רָאשֵׁיהֶן כְּאֶחָד, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵיהֶן לַכֹּהֵן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הַזְּכָרִים לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Furthermore, it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one's **donkey had not** previously **given birth and** now **gave birth to two males, and both of their heads emerged as one, Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: Both of them** are given **to the priest, as it is stated: "The males shall be the Lord's."**

The Gemara then presents an explicit *Braita* that resolves the question: *chamor she'bikra veyaldah shnei zacharim, yatzu shnei rasheihem ke'echad mamash* — a donkey that had never previously given birth, and it gave birth to two males whose heads emerged simultaneously in every sense. Rabbi Yose HaGlili says: *shneihem la'kohen* — the *kohen* receives both, *shene'emar hazcharim la'Hashem*, since the verse uses the plural form just as it does for sheep. This is a direct *tiyuvta* — full refutation — of Abaye's position. *Tiyuvta de'Abaye tiyuvta*. Rabbi Yose HaGlili applies his position equally to donkeys, and therefore our *mishnah* indeed does not follow Rabbi Yose HaGlili.

### Chatitzah Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, לֵימָא קָסָבְרִי רַבָּנַן מִקְצָת רֶחֶם מַקְדֵּישׁ? דְּאִי כּוּלֵּיהּ רֶחֶם מַקְדֵּשׁ — נְהִי דְּאִי אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם, חֲצִיצָה מִיהָא אִיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to the opinion of **the Rabbis, let us say the Rabbis hold** that even **a portion of the** mouth of the **womb sanctifies** a firstborn despite the offspring never having touched the entire area. **As, if** only **the entire** mouth of the **womb sanctifies** the firstborn, then in a case where the heads emerged as one, **granted** that **it is impossible** for both births **to coincide precisely** and one certainly emerged first, but **in any event there is an interposition,** i.e., the head of the other fetus, between each fetus and the mouth of the womb.

According to the Rabbis — who hold that the two animals cannot emerge at precisely the same moment — when the first animal is exiting and the second is just slightly behind, the body of the second animal stands between the first animal and part of the walls of the *rechem*. The *rechem* imparts sanctity to the firstborn through contact at the moment of emergence. This is a remarkable way of thinking about it. We might naturally focus on the act of coming out first — the firstborn is first, therefore sanctified. But the Gemara is object-oriented: it is the *rechem* itself, the womb, the contact with the walls of the womb at the moment of exit, that constitutes the mechanism of sanctification. Given that framing, there is a problem: the body of the second animal creates a *chatitza*, an obstruction, between the first animal and a portion of the womb walls. How then is the first animal fully sanctified?

### Womb Sanctification

The Gemara asks: perhaps the Rabbis hold that *miktzat rechem makdish* — only partial contact with the womb is necessary for sanctification. If that is true, there is no problem. But if *kulei rechem makdish* — the entire womb contact is required — then the presence of the second animal divides the contact and creates a genuine *chatitza*.

### Same Species Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: מִין בְּמִינוֹ אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav says:** No proof may be drawn from here that a portion of the womb sanctifies a firstborn, as **a substance** that is in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance does not interpose.** Therefore, each fetus is still considered to be touching the entire womb.

Rav answers: *min be'mino eino chotzetz* — the same species does not constitute an obstruction. Both animals are donkeys; the second is not something foreign or extraneous. It does not interrupt the halachic contact in the way that a *chatitza* would. Tosafot adds a nuance: this principle may not apply in all configurations. If the second animal is a female — which has no *kedushat bechor* at all — or if the second animal is entirely wrapped around the first so that none of the first animal contacts the womb directly, those scenarios may be governed by a different *Gemara*, and the sanctification might not obtain. The principle being established here is fundamental: sanctity is conferred through the womb contact at the moment of birth, and what constitutes a *chatitza* in that process is governed by the rule of *min be'mino eino chotzetz*.

## Why Separate Sheep

### Separation Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה — מַפְרִישׁ טָלֶה כּוּ׳. וְכֵיוָן דִּלְעַצְמוֹ הוּא, לְמָה לִי לְאַפְרוֹשֵׁי? לְאַפְקוֹעֵי לְאִיסּוּרֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a female donkey that had not yet given birth gives birth to **a male and a female,** and there is uncertainty with regard to which was born first, the owner **designates a lamb** in case the male was born first, which he keeps for himself. The Gemara asks: **But since** he keeps **it for himself, why do I** need him **to designate** it? The Gemara answers: It is necessary **in order to abrogate its forbidden status.**

The Gemara returns to the case of *zachar unekeivah* — where there is a doubtful male and the owner keeps the sheep — and asks: *keivan de'le'atzmo hu lama li le'afroshei* — since you get to keep the sheep yourself, why bother performing the separation at all?

### Remove Prohibition

The Gemara answers: *le'afokei me'issurei minei* — to remove the prohibited status from the donkey. There is an opinion that a *peter chamor* is forbidden in benefit until redeemed, and therefore the owner performs the redemption not for the *kohen*'s sake but to render the donkey permissible for his own use.

### Donkey Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מַפְקַע לְאִיסּוּרֵיהּ, אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה. מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Apparently, since** prior to the designation the sanctity **is not** yet **abrogated, it is prohibited to** derive **benefit** from the donkey at that stage. If so, **whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna? It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: It **is prohibited to** derive **benefit** from **a firstborn donkey,** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda; **and Rabbi Shimon deems** it **permitted.**

This leads to an important inference. Significantly, the Gemara does not say the reason to perform the separation is to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *tifdeh ve'seh*. One might have thought that the owner separates the sheep in order to discharge the obligation even under doubtful circumstances. The Gemara's silence on that rationale strongly suggests that the *mitzvah* of *tifdeh ve'seh* — of giving the sheep as a redemption — only constitutes a *kiyum mitzvah* when the *kohen* actually receives it. When the owner retains the sheep, there is no *mitzvah* being fulfilled. The sole purpose of the act, in this case, is the practical benefit of lifting the prohibited status from the donkey. This also reinforces Tosafot's earlier point: the laws of this redemption are heavily shaped by whether the *kohen* ultimately receives the sheep.

## Yehuda vs. Shimon

### Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר עוּלָּא: יֵשׁ לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁצָּרִיךְ פְּדִיָּיה וּמוּתָּר? וְלָא?! וַהֲרֵי בְּכוֹר אָדָם שֶׁצָּרִיךְ פְּדִיָּיה וּמוּתָּר! אֶלָּא, יֵשׁ לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁהִקְפִּידָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה בְּשֶׂה וּמוּתָּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda? Ulla says: Do you have an item that requires redemption and** it is nevertheless **permitted** to derive benefit from it? The Gemara challenges: **And** is there **no** such item? **But doesn't** a woman's **firstborn son require redemption** with five shekels, **and** it is nevertheless **permitted** to derive benefit from him? **Rather,** this is what Ulla means: **Do you have an item about which the Torah was particular** and requires that it be redeemed specifically **with a lamb, and** yet it is still **permitted** prior to redemption?

The Gemara identifies our *mishnah* as following Rabbi Yehuda, based on a *Braita*: *peter chamor assur be'hana'ah* — the firstborn donkey is forbidden in benefit until redeemed, *divrei Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon disagrees and permits it. There is a *mitzvah* to redeem, but the donkey itself carries no prohibited status in the interim and is entirely permissible.

### Ulla Argument

Ulla poses the challenge underlying Rabbi Yehuda's position: *yesh lecha davar she'tzarich pidyah u'mutar* — is there any precedent for something that requires redemption and is nevertheless permitted? Redemption, by its nature, implies that something is being released from a prior status.

### Human Firstborns

The Gemara replies: the firstborn human. A firstborn son requires *pidyon haben* and yet there is no prohibition on deriving benefit from him before redemption. So Ulla's general principle is not watertight.

### Sheep Specificity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי הִקְפִּידָה?! וְהָא רַב נְחֶמְיָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף פָּרֵיק לֵיהּ בְּשִׁילְקֵי בְּשׇׁוְיוֹ! בְּשׇׁוְיוֹ לָא קָאָמְרִינַן, כִּי קָאָמְרִינַן שֶׁלֹּא בְּשׇׁוְיוֹ, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: יֵשׁ לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁהִקְפִּידָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה לְאַפְקוֹעֵי לְאִיסּוּרֵיהּ בְּשֶׂה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And is** the Torah **particular** about redeeming a firstborn donkey specifically with a lamb? **But didn't Rav Neḥemya, son of Rav Yosef, redeem** a firstborn donkey **with boiled vegetables** that were worth **its** full **value?** The Gemara answers: Ulla **is not saying,** i.e., referring to, a case where the donkey is redeemed **at its** full **value.** Rather, **when we say** the Torah is particular that the donkey be redeemed with a lamb, the reference is to redeeming it **not at its** full **value, and this** is what Ulla **is saying: Do you have an item about which the Torah was particular** so as **to require abrogating its forbidden status** specifically **with a lamb,** if the lamb is worth less than the donkey? Therefore, Rabbi Yehuda holds that deriving benefit from a firstborn donkey is forbidden.

Ulla refines the argument: *yesh lecha davar she'hikpida alav Torah be'seh u'mutar* — is there anything for which the Torah mandated a specifically ritualized redemption with a sheep, and yet which carries no prohibited status? Rashi explains: by a human firstborn, the obligation is five *selaim* and may be discharged with any equivalent value. Here the Torah specified a *seh* — a sheep — as the vehicle of redemption. That degree of ritual specificity implies a status is being transferred or removed. The argument is that this particular and prescribed ritual points toward a real metaphysical status on the donkey.

## Ma'aser & Kiddushin

### Ma'aser Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי מַעֲשֵׂר, שֶׁהִקְפִּידָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה בְּכֶסֶף צוֹרִי, וּתְנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּמֵזִיד — קִידֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** this contradicts Rabbi Yehuda's ruling with regard to second-**tithe** produce, concerning **which the Torah was particular about its** being redeemed only **with a minted coin, and we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If one betrothed a woman with second-tithe produce, if he did so **intentionally, he has betrothed** her. Apparently, he maintains that deriving benefit from second-tithe produce is not prohibited prior to redemption.

The Gemara raises a challenge: how about *ma'aser sheni*, which also has a specifically required form of redemption — coins bearing an image, *kesef tzuri* — and yet is not forbidden in benefit? If ritual specificity alone proves forbidden status, *ma'aser sheni* should disprove the claim.

### Kiddushin Case

A further challenge is raised from a *mishnah*: Rabbi Yehuda says that if a man betrothed a woman *be'mezid* with *ma'aser sheni* food — deliberately using it as the *kesef kiddushin* — the *kiddushin* takes effect. This means the woman has derived benefit outside of Jerusalem, and yet *ma'aser sheni* is not thereby shown to be forbidden in benefit.

### Redemption Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר נָמֵי מִיקַּדְּשָׁא, כִּדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אִשָּׁה יוֹדַעַת שֶׁאֵין מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי מִתְחַלֵּל עַל יָדָהּ, וְעוֹלָה וְאוֹכַלְתּוֹ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: That is not proof, as according to Rabbi Yehuda, if a man betrothed a woman **with a firstborn donkey, she is also betrothed. This applies** even if deriving benefit from the animal is prohibited, **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar. As Rabbi Elazar says:** The reason **a woman** is betrothed with second-tithe produce is that she **knows that second-tithe** produce **is not desacralized by her** betrothal. Rather, it remains prohibited, **and she** will subsequently **ascend and consume it in Jerusalem,** where consumption of second-tithe produce is permitted. She is therefore betrothed with the benefit of that future consumption.

The Gemara notes that the same could be argued regarding the *peter chamor*: *peter chamor nami mikadsha* — one could also use a *peter chamor* to betroth a woman, even according to Rabbi Yehuda who holds it is forbidden in benefit. How? Following Rabbi Eliezer, who says that a woman accepting *ma'aser sheni* as *kesef kiddushin* understands that she is not thereby redeeming it — it retains its *ma'aser sheni* status, and *ve'ola ve'ochalto bi'Yerushalayim*, she will bring it to Jerusalem and eat it there. She is receiving something of value — the right to bring and consume it in Jerusalem — not the food itself as unrestricted property. Similarly, a woman accepting a *peter chamor* for *kiddushin* knows it is forbidden in benefit according to Rabbi Yehuda, but she understands she will redeem it with a sheep, and if the donkey is worth more than the sheep required for redemption, she gains the difference in value. She receives real monetary worth — the spread between the value of the donkey and the cost of the redemption sheep. The *kiddushin* therefore takes effect even though the object itself is currently forbidden in benefit. Thus the question of whether something can be used for *kiddushin* tells us nothing about whether it is currently forbidden in benefit.

### Ma'aser Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, אִשָּׁה יוֹדַעַת דְּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר אִיסּוּרָא אִית בֵּיהּ, וּפָרְקָא לֵיהּ בְּשֶׂה, וּמַקְדְּשָׁא בְּהָךְ דְּבֵינֵי וּבֵינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Here too,** where one betroths a woman with a firstborn donkey, the betrothal is effective despite the fact that it is prohibited to derive benefit from the animal. This is because **a woman knows that a firstborn donkey has a forbidden status and she** will therefore **redeem it with a lamb. And she is betrothed with that** amount, i.e., **of** the difference **between** the value of the donkey **and** the value of the lamb, as the value of the lamb is lower.

The Gemara further addresses the *ma'aser sheni* parallel by pointing out that *ma'aser sheni* is not truly being redeemed in the way the donkey is. When *ma'aser sheni* is exchanged for money, the *kedushah* of *ma'aser sheni* transfers to the money — *acharon acharon assur*, each successive object it is exchanged for acquires the *kedushat shevi'it*-style status. The original food remains forbidden, and the money acquires the transferred status. There is no true removal of a prohibited status; the status simply migrates. This is fundamentally different from the redemption of a *peter chamor*, where the sheep has no sanctity at all after the transaction and the donkey is rendered fully permissible. The contrasts between the two cases explain why *ma'aser sheni* cannot serve as a model to disprove Rabbi Yehuda's position.

## Verses Debate

### Work Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּהַאי קְרָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תַעֲבֹד בִּבְכוֹר שׁוֹרֶךָ״ — אֲבָל אַתָּה עוֹבֵד בְּשֶׁלְּךָ וּבְשֶׁל אֲחֵרִים, ״וְלֹא תָגֹז בְּכוֹר צֹאנֶךָ״ — אֲבָל אַתָּה גּוֹזֵז שֶׁלְּךָ וְשֶׁל אֲחֵרִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers an additional basis for the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon: **And if you wish, say** that **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon disagree with regard to** the following **verse, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the prohibition against shearing and working a firstborn kosher animal that the Torah states: **"You shall do no work with the firstborn of your ox"** (Deuteronomy 15:19), **but you may perform labor with** a firstborn animal that is both **your own and** also **that of others,** i.e., an animal owned in partnership with a gentile. Although firstborn status applies to the Jew's share in the animal and he must give the value of his share to a priest, nevertheless, it has no sanctity and deriving benefit from it is permitted. The verse continues: **"Nor shear the firstborn of your flock," but you may shear** a firstborn animal that is **yours and** that is also **that of others.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The Gemara now presents a second, verse-based framework for the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon. A *Braita* derives from the verse *lo ta'avod bi'vechor shorecha* — you shall not work the firstborn of your ox — that one may work it if it is jointly owned with a non-Jew. Similarly, *lo tagoz bi'vechor tzonecha* — you shall not shear the firstborn of your sheep — is the basis for further rulings.

### Joint Ownership

Rabbi Yehuda reads: *aval ata oved be'shelcha uve'shel acherim* — but you may work or shear if it is jointly owned between yourself and a non-Jew. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *shutafut akum chayevet bivechorah* — joint non-Jewish ownership does not exempt the animal from the category of *bechor*; your portion remains obligated. Nevertheless, the verse teaches that the prohibition of shearing and working does not apply, because the non-Jewish ownership prevents the *bechor* from having the full *kedusha* of a *korban*. There remains a *bechor* obligation, but without the associated prohibitions on use.

### Consensus

All authorities agree that human firstborns carry no work or benefit prohibition — the verse *shorecha*, "your ox," implicitly excludes humans. The debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon centers on what the verse regarding *tzonecha*, "your sheep," comes to teach.

### Species Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא תַעֲבֹד בִּבְכוֹר שׁוֹרֶךָ״ — אֲבָל אַתָּה עוֹבֵד בִּבְכוֹר אָדָם, ״לֹא תָגֹז בְּכוֹר צֹאנֶךָ״ — אֲבָל אַתָּה גּוֹזֵז בְּכוֹר חֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says** the verse should be interpreted as follows: **"You shall do no work with the firstborn of your ox," but you may do work with** a woman's **firstborn son,** as no prohibition exists with regard to deriving benefit from the labor of a firstborn boy even before he is redeemed. **"Nor shear the firstborn of your flock," but you may shear a firstborn donkey,** as deriving benefit from it is permitted. Rabbi Yehuda, who does not interpret the verse in this manner, holds that it is prohibited to shear and derive benefit from a firstborn donkey as well.

Rabbi Shimon reads the verses differently. He holds that *shutafut akum petura min habechorah* — even partial non-Jewish ownership entirely exempts the animal from *bechor* status, so no verse is needed to permit work or shearing in that case. For Rabbi Shimon, the verse *lo ta'avod bi'vechor shorecha* comes to exclude human firstborns from any work prohibition, and *lo tagoz bi'vechor tzonecha* comes to teach that *aval ata gozez bi'vechor chamor* — the firstborn of a donkey, by contrast, carries no such prohibition. Sheep and cattle have the prohibited status; humans and donkeys do not. This is Rabbi Shimon's basis for holding that the *peter chamor* is not forbidden in benefit.

### Possessive Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי לְמַעוֹטֵי שֶׁלְּךָ וְשֶׁל אֲחֵרִים לְמָה לִי? וְתוּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, בְּכוֹר אָדָם נָמֵי נֵימָא דַּאֲסִיר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon, that is** the reason **that two verses,** i.e., phrases, **are written,** one that excludes a woman's firstborn son from the prohibition against deriving benefit and another that excludes a firstborn donkey. **But according to Rabbi Yehuda, why do I** need **two verses to exclude** a firstborn animal that is **yours and** that is also **that of others,** i.e., owned jointly by a Jew and a gentile, from the prohibition against deriving benefit from the firstborn? **And furthermore, according to Rabbi Yehuda, shall we say** that deriving benefit from a woman's **firstborn son** should **also be forbidden,** since it was not excluded by any verse?

The Gemara raises a difficulty against Rabbi Shimon: *bishlama le'Rabbi Shimon hainu de'chtiv trei kra'ei* — for Rabbi Shimon, the two verses are needed to exclude both humans and donkeys, so he needs both. But why does each verse use the possessive "your" — *shorecha*, *tzonecha*? For Rabbi Yehuda, the possessive signals the law of joint non-Jewish ownership. For Rabbi Shimon, who reads the verses as limiting the scope to certain species rather than addressing ownership issues at all, the possessive serves no apparent function. *Kashya* — this difficulty remains unresolved.

## Arifah Agreement

### After Wringing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְאַחַר עֲרִיפָה, שֶׁהוּא אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba says: And Rabbi Shimon concedes that** even if it is permitted to derive benefit from a firstborn donkey that was not redeemed, **following** the **breaking** of its **neck** in fulfillment of the verse: "And you shall redeem every firstborn donkey with a lamb, and if you do not redeem it, then you shall break its neck" (Exodus 13:13), **it is forbidden** to derive benefit from its carcass.

Despite his general position that the *peter chamor* is permissible in benefit, Rabbi Shimon agrees in one case. Rava states: *umodeh Rabbi Shimon le'achar arifa she'hu assur* — Rabbi Shimon admits that once the donkey's neck has been broken (*arifa*), it is forbidden in benefit.

### Obligation Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? גָּמַר ״עֲרִיפָה״ ״עֲרִיפָה״ מֵעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this ruling? **He derives** this *halakha* by a verbal analogy **between** the expression: **"Breaking the neck,"** stated here, and the expression: **"Breaking the neck,"** stated in the case of **the heifer whose neck is broken** by the Elders of the city due to a unresolved murder (see Deuteronomy 21:1–9). Just as it is forbidden to derive benefit from the heifer after its neck is broken, so too is it prohibited to derive benefit from a firstborn donkey that was not redeemed following the breaking of its neck.

The logic is as follows. For Rabbi Shimon, the obligation to redeem is a *mitzvah* incumbent upon the owner — but it is not a status removal. The donkey is permissible throughout its life even without redemption. However, if one fails to fulfill the *mitzvah* of redemption, the Torah requires that the neck be broken: *ve'im lo tifdeh va'arafto*. Once the neck is broken, the consequence of having failed to redeem is the forfeiture of any benefit from the animal. It is not that the donkey was forbidden from the start; the prohibition is the penalty for non-fulfillment. The *arifa* is therefore not simply a declaration of a pre-existing status, but a punitive consequence that then renders the carcass forbidden.

### Braita Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לֵיהּ דְּתַנְיָא: הָעׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם, וְשׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, וְעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, וְצִיפּוֹרֵי מְצוֹרָע, וּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר, וּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב — כּוּלָּן מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I say** that this is the opinion of Rabbi Shimon? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Okatzin* 3:12): **The fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting [*orla*], and diverse kinds in a vineyard, and** the flesh of **an ox that** was to be **stoned** for killing or copulating with a person but was slaughtered instead, **and** that of **a heifer whose neck** was to be **broken** but was slaughtered instead, **and** of **the birds** offered by **a leper** (see Leviticus 14:4–7), **and** of **a firstborn donkey** whose neck was broken, **and meat** cooked together **with milk, are all susceptible to the ritual impurity of food,** despite the fact that their consumption is forbidden.

A *Braita* is cited in support: among the items listed as forbidden in benefit yet still considered food for purposes of *tum'at ochlin* is *peter chamor*. Recall that *tum'at ochlin* status depends on whether the item is defined as food, and food is defined as something that can physically be eaten even if one may not eat it. The *Braita* lists *peter chamor* among items that are forbidden in benefit but still constitute food for *tumah* purposes — and Rabbi Shimon himself is cited as the voice of this *Braita*, with his qualification to follow.

### Meat & Milk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּן אֵין מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין, וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין, הוֹאִיל וְהָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: All of them are not susceptible to the ritual impurity of food,** since they are all items from which deriving benefit is prohibited and are therefore not considered food. **And Rabbi Shimon concedes with regard to meat** cooked together **with milk that it is susceptible to the impurity of food, since** both the meat and the milk **had a period of susceptibility** to contracting impurity before they were cooked together.

Rabbi Shimon's caveat is instructive. He holds that all the items in the *Braita* — *orla*, *kilaei hakerem*, *shor haniskal*, *egla arufa*, *tzipori metzora*, and *peter chamor* — are *not* considered food for *tum'at ochlin* purposes, because food is only defined as such if it can be eaten or fed to others. Since you cannot feed any of these to a non-Jew, they lack the halachic status of food. However, *umodeh Rabbi Shimon be'vasar be'chalav* — Rabbi Shimon admits that *basar be'chalav* (meat cooked with milk) does carry *tum'at ochlin* status, *heil ve'hayta lo sha'at hakosher* — because it once had a moment of permissibility: before they were cooked together, each was individually permissible. That prior permissibility confers a retained food status even after the combination becomes forbidden.

### Food Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דִּכְתִיב: ״מִכׇּל הָאֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל״, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים — קָרוּי אוֹכֶל, שֶׁאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים — אֵינוֹ קָרוּי אוֹכֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon** that an item from which deriving benefit is prohibited is not susceptible to the impurity of food? It is **as it is written: "All food therein that may be eaten [*ha'okhel asher ye'akhel*],** that on which water comes, shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:34). The use of the Hebrew root *alef*, *khaf*, *lamed* twice in this phrase indicates that specifically **food that you are able to feed to others,** i.e., gentiles, **is called food** with regard to susceptibility to the impurity of food. But food **that you are not able to feed to others is not called food.** Therefore, an item from which deriving benefit is prohibited and which one is consequently forbidden to feed to gentiles is not considered food in this context.

Rabbi Yochanan explains Rabbi Shimon's underlying principle: *ochel she'ata yachol lehachilo le'acherim karui ochel* — something you can eat or feed to others is called food; *she'i ata yachol lehachilo le'acherim eino karui ochel* — something you cannot eat or feed to another is not called food. The *peter chamor* after *arifa* cannot be given to a non-Jew and cannot be used by the Jew; it is therefore not food in any halachic sense, and Rabbi Shimon himself would classify it as forbidden in benefit from that point forward. The exception for *basar be'chalav* is structural: it was once permitted and therefore retains food status. The *peter chamor*, by contrast, never had a moment of permissibility once the neck was broken, and Rabbi Shimon accordingly places it outside the category of *ochel* entirely.
