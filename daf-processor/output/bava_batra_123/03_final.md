# Bava Batra 123 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pi Shnayim Definition

### One vs. All Sons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לָתֶת לוֹ פִּי שְׁנַיִם״ – פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּכׇל הַנְּכָסִים?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: **"But he shall acknowledge the firstborn, the son of the hated, by giving him a double portion of all that he has"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), this means the firstborn receives **double** the property received **by** any other **one** inheritor. The *baraita* analyzes this statement: **Do you say** the firstborn receives **double** the property received **by** any **one** inheritor, **or rather, is it a double** portion **of all the property,** such that the firstborn receives two-thirds of the entire estate, which is twice the portion left for the other inheritors to divide between themselves?

The daf opens with a *baraisa* addressing a fundamental definitional question regarding the *bechor*'s double portion: what precisely does *pi shnayim* mean? Two interpretations are possible. The first holds that the *bechor* receives double the share of any individual brother — *pi shnayim ke'echad*. The second, far more radical interpretation would grant the *bechor* double whatever all of the other sons receive in aggregate — *pi shnayim bechol hanechasim*. The practical difference is enormous. If there are five other sons, the first interpretation produces six equal units, with the *bechor* receiving two; the second would produce fifteen units, with the *bechor* receiving ten and each remaining son receiving only one. The *baraisa* takes up both possibilities seriously.

### Logical Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶלְקוֹ עִם אֶחָד, וְחֶלְקוֹ עִם חֲמִשָּׁה; מָה חֶלְקוֹ עִם אֶחָד – פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד, אַף חֶלְקוֹ עִם חֲמִשָּׁה – פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד! אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ – חֶלְקוֹ עִם אֶחָד, וְחֶלְקוֹ עִם חֲמִשָּׁה; מָה חֶלְקוֹ עִם אֶחָד – פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּכׇל הַנְּכָסִים, אַף חֶלְקוֹ עִם חֲמִשָּׁה – פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּכׇל הַנְּכָסִים!
>
> **Translation:** There may be times when a firstborn takes **his portion** of the inheritance, dividing the estate **with one** other brother, **and** there may be times when a firstborn takes **his portion** of the inheritance, dividing the estate **with five** brothers, and there is no indication that the Torah differentiates between the manner in which the respective firstborns collect in each scenario. Therefore, **just as** when a firstborn takes **his portion,** dividing the estate **with one** other brother, he receives **double** the property received **by one** inheritor, **so too,** when a firstborn takes **his portion** of the inheritance, dividing the estate **with five** brothers, he receives **double** the property received **by one** inheritor. The *baraita* suggests the opposite logical derivation: **Or** perhaps **go this way:** There may be times when a firstborn takes **his portion** of the inheritance, dividing the estate **with one** other brother, **and** there may be times when a firstborn takes **his portion** of the inheritance, dividing the estate **with five** brothers. Therefore, **just as** when a firstborn takes **his portion,** dividing the estate **with one** other brother, he receives **a double** portion **of all the property, so too** when a firstborn takes **his portion** of the inheritance, dividing the estate **with five** brothers, he receives **a double** portion **of all the property.**

The *baraisa* attempts a logical resolution through analogy. Consider the simplest case: one *bechor* and one other son. The *bechor* receives double. Under either interpretation, that result is identical — he receives two parts and his brother one. One could therefore read *pi shnayim* in that scenario as "double what one individual receives," or equally as "double what all the others combined receive," since in that case the two formulations coincide. The *baraisa* tries to extend this to the case of five additional sons, but the argument is reversible in both directions, and neither inference is conclusive on its own. The *baraisa* therefore turns to a *pasuk* for resolution.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהָיָה בְּיוֹם הַנְחִילוֹ אֶת בָּנָיו״ – הַתּוֹרָה רִיבְּתָה נַחֲלָה אֵצֶל אַחִין; הָא אֵין עָלֶיךָ לָדוּן כַּלָּשׁוֹן הָאַחֲרוֹן, אֶלָּא כַּלָּשׁוֹן הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** As the logical inference can lead to either conclusion, the *halakha* is determined by a derivation from a verse. **The verse states: "Then it shall be, in the day that he causes his sons to inherit"** (Deuteronomy 21:16). As the next verse spells out: "But he shall acknowledge the firstborn, the son of the hated, by giving him a double portion of all that he has" (Deuteronomy 21:17), the former verse is ostensibly redundant. The additional verse is therefore understood as an inclusion: **The Torah increased inheritance with regard to** the **brothers,** indicating that the more brothers there are, the greater the portion they collect from the entire estate. **Therefore, you should not reason in accordance with the final formulation,** in which the *baraita* suggests that the firstborn inherits twice as much as all the other brothers combined, **but in accordance with the first formulation,** according to which the firstborn inherits twice as much as each other brother.

The verse *vehayah beyom hanchilo et banav* — "it shall be on the day he bequeaths to his sons" — is read as placing all of the sons front and center in the inheritance. The word *banav*, "his sons," signals that the other brothers have a substantial stake in the estate. The *gemara* therefore concludes that we cannot adopt the second interpretation, which would render the remaining brothers' collective share trivial compared to the *bechor*'s. The correct reading is *pi shnayim ke'echad*: double any individual brother's portion.

### Yosef Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״וּבְנֵי רְאוּבֵן בְּכוֹר יִשְׂרָאֵל, כִּי הוּא הַבְּכוֹר, וּבְחַלְּלוֹ יְצוּעֵי אָבִיו נִתְּנָה בְּכֹרָתוֹ לִבְנֵי יוֹסֵף בֶּן יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְלֹא לְהִתְיַחֵשׂ לַבְּכֹרָה״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״כִּי יְהוּדָה גָּבַר בְּאֶחָיו וּלְנָגִיד מִמֶּנּוּ, וְהַבְּכֹרָה לְיוֹסֵף״ –
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* reinforces this statement by citing additional verses: **And** the verse **states: "And the sons of Reuben the firstborn of Israel, for he was the firstborn; but, inasmuch as he defiled his father's couch, his birthright [*bekhorato*] was given to the sons of Joseph the son of Israel, yet not so that he was to be reckoned in the genealogy as firstborn"** (I Chronicles 5:1). **And** the following verse **states: "For Judah prevailed above his brothers, and the prince came of him; but the birthright [*vehabekhora*] was Joseph's"** (I Chronicles 5:2).

The *baraisa* then adduces further evidence by linking the word *bechorah* as it appears in the laws of inheritance to the *bechorah* as it applied historically to Yosef. Two verses from *Divrei HaYamim* are cited. The first: *uvnei Reuven bechor Yisrael ki hu habechor uvechalelo yetzuei aviv nitnah bechorato livnei Yosef ben Yisrael* — the sons of Reuven, the firstborn of Israel, for he was the firstborn, but because he desecrated his father's bed, his firstborn rights were given to the sons of Yosef son of Israel. The verse is careful to add *velo lehityaches labechorah* — the personal title of firstborn remains with Reuven; only the *privileges* were transferred to Yosef. The second verse: *vehabechorah l'Yosef* — the *bechorah* belongs to Yosef. The *gemara* notes that the word *bechorah* appears in both this context and in the Mosaic law governing inheritance, thereby establishing an analogy: just as Yosef's *bechorah* meant a double portion relative to any individual brother, so too does the legal *bechorah* mean double any individual brother.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֶמְרָה ״בְּכוֹרָה״ לְיוֹסֵף, וְנֶאֱמְרָה ״בְּכוֹרָה״ לְדוֹרוֹת; מָה בְּכוֹרָה הָאֲמוּרָה לְיוֹסֵף – פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד, אַף בְּכוֹרָה הָאֲמוּרָה לְדוֹרוֹת – פִּי שְׁנַיִם כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* derives: **Birthright,** i.e., firstborn status, **is stated** here **with regard to Joseph, and birthright is stated** in another verse concerning the double portion **with regard to** the later **generations:** "By giving him a double portion of all that he has, for he is the first-fruits of his strength, the birthright [*habekhora*] is his" (Deuteronomy 21:17). **Just as the birthright stated with regard to Joseph** is **double** the property received **by one** inheritor, as the Gemara will explain, **so too the birthright stated with regard to** the later **generations** is **double** the property received **by one** inheritor.

But this raises an immediate sub-question: where do we see that Yosef's double portion was specifically double that of one individual brother, as opposed to double all the brothers combined? The *gemara* cites the verse *va'ani natati lecha Shechem echad al achecha* — I have given you one *Shechem* above your brothers. Whether *Shechem* refers to the city or to an additional portion, the word *echad* — "one" — establishes that Yosef received one extra share over his brothers, not double their combined total.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״וַאֲנִי נָתַתִּי לְךָ שְׁכֶם אַחַד עַל אַחֶיךָ, אֲשֶׁר לָקַחְתִּי מִיַּד הָאֱמוֹרִי בְּחַרְבִּי וּבְקַשְׁתִּי״ – וְכִי בְּחַרְבּוֹ וּבְקַשְׁתּוֹ לָקַח?! וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא בְקַשְׁתִּי אֶבְטָח, וְחַרְבִּי לֹא תוֹשִׁיעֵנִי״! אֶלָּא ״חַרְבִּי״ – זוֹ תְּפִלָּה, ״קַשְׁתִּי״ – זוֹ בַּקָּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* cites additional related verses: **And** with regard to Jacob's bequest to Joseph, the verse **states: "Moreover I have given to you one portion above your brothers, which I took out of the hand of the Amorite with my sword and with my bow"** (Genesis 48:22). The *baraita* explains the verse: **But** is it **so that** Jacob **took** the portion **with his sword and with his bow? But isn't it already stated:** "Through You do we push down our adversaries; through Your name do we trample those that rise against us. **For I trust not in my bow, neither can my sword save me"** (Psalms 44:6–7)? **Rather,** what is the meaning of "with **my sword"? This** is referring to **prayer.** What is the meaning of "with **my bow"? This** is referring to **petition.** This concludes the *baraita*.

The verse continues: *asher lakachti miyad ha'Emori becharbi uvekashti* — which I took from the Emorite with my sword and my bow. This is textually puzzling: Yaakov was not a warrior. The *Rashbam* addresses this by noting that Yaakov's "sword" and "bow" are understood as *tefillah* — prayer. The term *charbi* alludes to prayer, and *kashti* is a play on *bakashah*, beseeching. The *Rashbam* reads this as a reference to Yaakov's prayers on behalf of his sons and his efforts to acquire Eisav's *bechorah* — and it is this, the fruit of his prayers and strivings, that he now bequeaths to Yosef.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אֵימָא דִּיקְלָא בְּעָלְמָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ, עָלֶיךָ אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֶפְרַיִם וּמְנַשֶּׁה – כִּרְאוּבֵן וְשִׁמְעוֹן יִהְיוּ לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye:** Why not **say** that the term "one portion" is referring to **a mere palm tree** or some other relatively small extra gift, instead of a full, equal share? Abaye **said to him: For you,** i.e., to answer your question, the **verse states: "Ephraim and Manasseh, even as Reuben and Simeon, shall be mine"** (Genesis 48:5). Evidently, Joseph's children together received portions equal to that of Reuben and Simeon together, i.e., two full portions.

Rav Pappa challenges the proof from *Shechem echad*. Even if Yaakov says he gave Yosef "one *Shechem*," perhaps *Shechem* denotes something minor — a palm tree, a small additional gift — rather than a full tribal portion equal in size to any other brother's territory. On that reading, the verse would not establish that Yosef received a full double portion commensurate with each individual brother.

### Ephraim Menashe

Abaye responds simply: look at the explicit verse, *Ephraim u'Menashe k'Reuven v'Shimon yihiyu li* — Ephraim and Menashe shall be to me like Reuven and Shimon. Yaakov granted Yosef's two sons the status of independent tribes equal to full brothers. That means Yosef received precisely two full tribal portions — exactly double the one portion of any individual brother. This is *pi shnayim ke'echad*, and this is the *halacha* of the *bechor*.

## Reuven to Yosef

### Initial Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ מֵרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: מָה רָאָה יַעֲקֹב שֶׁנָּטַל בְּכוֹרָה מֵרְאוּבֵן, וּנְתָנָהּ לְיוֹסֵף? מָה רָאָה?! ״וּבְחַלְּלוֹ יְצוּעֵי אָבִיו״ כְּתִיב! אֶלָּא מָה רָאָה שֶׁנְּתָנָהּ לְיוֹסֵף?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ḥelbo asked Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani: What did Jacob see that he took** the status as **firstborn from Reuben and gave it to Joseph?** The Gemara wonders: What does he mean, **what did** he **see?** The matter is **written** explicitly: **"But, inasmuch as he defiled his father's couch,** his birthright was given to the sons of Joseph" (I Chronicles 5:1). **Rather,** Rabbi Ḥelbo's question was: **What did he see that he gave it** specifically **to Joseph?**

Having established the halachic point using the narrative of Yaakov and Yosef, the *gemara* transitions into a sustained *aggadic* passage. Rav Chelbo poses a question to Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani: *ma ra'ah Yaakov she'natal bechorah mi'Reuven u'netanah l'Yosef* — what motivated Yaakov to take the *bechorah* from Reuven and give it to Yosef?

### Simple Answer

The *gemara* dismisses the first half of the question almost immediately. Why did Yaakov take it from Reuven? The verses in *Divrei HaYamim* say it plainly: *uvechalelo yetzuei aviv* — because Reuven desecrated his father's bed. That requires no further explanation.

### Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶמְשׁוֹל לְךָ מָשָׁל, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁגִּדֵּל יָתוֹם בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ; לְיָמִים הֶעֱשִׁיר אוֹתוֹ יָתוֹם, וְאָמַר: אֲהַנֵּיהוּ לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת מִנְּכָסַי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאִי לָאו דַּחֲטָא רְאוּבֵן, לָא מְהַנֵּי לֵיהּ לְיוֹסֵף וְלָא מִדָּעַם?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani answered Rabbi Ḥelbo: **I will tell you a parable. To what is this matter comparable? To a homeowner who raised an orphan in his home. Eventually, that orphan became wealthy and said: I will provide benefit to the homeowner from my property.** Similarly, since Joseph sustained Jacob in Egypt for a number of years, Jacob saw fit to repay the kindness. Rabbi Ḥelbo **said to him: And if Reuben did not sin,** would Jacob **not have provided any benefit to Joseph?** It cannot be that Jacob repaid Joseph only as a result of Reuben' sin.

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani offers a parable. A homeowner raised an orphan in his house. The orphan later became wealthy and said: let me now benefit this man from my wealth, in gratitude for having raised me. The *bechor* and the homeowner in the parable are Yosef and Yaakov respectively — Yaakov came down to Egypt dispossessed and without means, and Yosef sustained him. At the end of his life, Yaakov had something of value to give back: the *bechorah*. He gave it to Yosef as a return of the favor.

### Counter-Objection

An objection is raised: if Yaakov was obligated to return the favor regardless, then even had Reuven not sinned, Yaakov would still have been compelled to give something to Yosef. Why then did the *bechorah* specifically have to be taken from Reuven to accomplish this? Yaakov could have returned the favor with his own property. The *Rashbam* makes this point explicit: you do not return a favor at a third party's expense. There must be a deeper explanation for why the *bechorah* in particular went to Yosef.

### Intended Firstborn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן רַבָּךְ לֹא כָּךְ אָמַר – רְאוּיָה הָיְתָה בְּכוֹרָה לָצֵאת מֵרָחֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֵלֶּה תֹּלְדוֹת יַעֲקֹב, יוֹסֵף״, אֶלָּא שֶׁקְּדָמַתָּהּ לֵאָה בְּרַחֲמִים; וּמִתּוֹךְ צְנִיעוּת שֶׁהָיְתָה בָּהּ בְּרָחֵל, הֶחְזִירָהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, doesn't your teacher Rabbi Yonatan say like this: It was appropriate for** the child receiving the status of **firstborn to emerge from Rachel, as it is written: "These are the generations of Jacob, Joseph"** (Genesis 37:2), indicating that Joseph was Jacob's primary progeny. **But Leah advanced** ahead of Rachel **with** appeals for **mercy,** i.e., with prayer, and thereby earned the status as firstborn for her firstborn. **But because of** the **modesty that Rachel possessed, the Holy One, Blessed be He, returned** the status as firstborn **to her.** This is why Jacob gave the status as firstborn to Joseph.

Rabbi Yonatan's formulation, as transmitted by his student, provides that deeper explanation. *Re'uya hayta bechorah latzet mei'Rachel* — the *bechorah* was properly destined to emerge from Rachel all along. The verse *elleh toldot Yaakov Yosef* — "these are the generations of Yaakov: Yosef" — places Yosef in the position of Yaakov's primary son, as if he were the firstborn. Had Yaakov married Rachel first, as he intended, Yosef would have been the literal firstborn. It was Leah who, through her prayers, *kidmatah l'Leah be'rachamim* — preceded Rachel by means of supplication — and thereby became the first wife and bore the first son. The *bechorah* was therefore not arbitrarily reassigned; it was returned to where it had always naturally belonged.

### Primogeniture Narrative

This leads the *gemara* into a broader reflection on what might be called the narrative of primogeniture running through *Bereishit*. The inversion of the firstborn's rights is not an isolated anomaly — it is a recurring pattern. Kayin and Hevel, Avraham, Yitzchak, Yaakov, Yosef: in every generation the expected right of the firstborn is displaced in favor of the more worthy. The irony is pointed: in *Devarim*, the Torah explicitly prohibits a father from giving the firstborn's portion to the son of the beloved wife over the son of the less-favored wife — *lo yuchal levaker et ben ha'ahuva al penei ben haSenu'ah habechor* — and yet that is precisely what Yaakov did, giving the rights to Yosef, the firstborn of his beloved Rachel, over Reuven, the firstborn of the less-favored Leah.

The *gemara*'s move here is subtle. It does not frame the *bechorah*'s transfer as merely a reward for virtue or a personal favor repaid. It frames it as the restoration of a destiny that was always rightfully Yosef's — rooted in the fact that Rachel was the wife Yaakov had always intended to marry first. This also reflects the broader dual narrative of the Torah: the structural norms of society demand that the firstborn maintain his position, but the deeper logic of the Torah is that the *bechorah* follows the deserving, not the merely chronologically prior. As God says of Israel: *beni bechori Yisrael* — my son, my firstborn, Israel — not because Israel was the first nation, but because Israel is the worthy one, elevated to the status of *bechor*.

## Rachel & Leah Merit

### Leah's Eyes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״קְדָמַתָּהּ לֵאָה בְּרַחֲמִים״? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעֵינֵי לֵאָה רַכּוֹת״ – מַאי ״רַכּוֹת״? אִילֵּימָא רַכּוֹת מַמָּשׁ, אֶפְשָׁר בִּגְנוּת בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה לֹא דִּבֵּר הַכָּתוּב, דִּכְתִיב: ״מִן הַבְּהֵמָה הַטְּהוֹרָה וּמִן הַבְּהֵמָה אֲשֶׁר אֵינֶנָּה טְהֹרָה״; בִּגְנוּת צַדִּיקִים דִּבֵּר הַכָּתוּב?! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁמַּתְּנוֹתֶיהָ אֲרוּכּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains this answer: **What** does it mean that **Leah advanced** ahead of Rachel **with mercy? As it is written: "And Leah's eyes were weak [*rakkot*]"** (Genesis 29:17). **What** is the meaning of **"*rakkot*"? If we say** that her eyes were **literally weak,** is it **possible** that the verse would say that? **The verse** there **did not speak to the disparagement of** even **a non-kosher animal, as it is written: "From the pure animals and from the animals that are lacking purity"** (Genesis 7:8). The verse states: "That are lacking purity" rather than stating explicitly and disparagingly: That are impure. If that is so with regard to animals, **did the verse speak** here **to the disparagement of the righteous? Rather, Rabbi Elazar says:** The term alludes to the fact **that her gifts,** i.e., the gifts given to her descendants, e.g., the priesthood and the monarchy, **were long-lasting [*arukkot*],** as they were passed down from generation to generation.

The *gemara* now elaborates on precisely how Leah preceded Rachel through prayer, and what merited Rachel's son the restoration of the *bechorah*. The starting point is the verse *ve'einei Leah rakot* — Leah's eyes were *rakot*, soft or weak. The *gemara* asks: what does *rakot* mean here? The straightforward reading — that her eyes were literally soft or weak — seems problematic. The Torah goes so far as to avoid saying the word *teme'ah* (impure) about a non-kosher animal, preferring the circumlocution "the animal that is not *tehorah*." If the Torah exercises such care about its language even regarding animals, would it speak disparagingly about the righteous Leah?

Rabbi Elazar offers a reading that turns the apparent slight into a compliment: *matnoteha aruchos* — her gifts are long, meaning her descendants will receive great gifts. The tribe of Levi will receive the priestly gifts — *matnot kehunah* — and the tribe of Yehudah will receive the kingship. The term *einei* may suggest a wellspring of blessing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם רַכּוֹת מַמָּשׁ, וְלֹא גְּנַאי הוּא לָהּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁבַח הוּא לָהּ. שֶׁהָיְתָה שׁוֹמַעַת עַל פָּרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: שְׁנֵי בָנִים יֵשׁ לָהּ לְרִבְקָה, שְׁתֵּי בָנוֹת יֵשׁ לוֹ לְלָבָן, גְּדוֹלָה לַגָּדוֹל וּקְטַנָּה לַקָּטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says** that there is a different explanation of the verse: **Actually,** the verse means that her eyes **were literally weak, and** this is **not a denigration of her but a praise of her. As she would hear people at the crossroads,** coming from the land of Canaan, **who would say: Rebecca has two sons,** and her brother **Laban has two daughters;** the **older** daughter will be married **to** the **older** son, **and** the **younger** daughter will be married **to** the **younger** son.

Rava, however, maintains the plain meaning — *rakot mamash*, literally weak — but reframes it as praise rather than criticism. Leah used to sit at the crossroads and ask passersby about the two sons of Rivkah and the two daughters of Lavan. People assumed the elder daughter would marry the elder son and the younger the younger. Travelers would tell her: the older son, Eisav? *Ish ra hu, melastem briyot* — an evil man, a bandit who attacks people. What of the younger? *Ish tam yoshev ohalim* — a wholesome man who sits in tents. And Leah wept until her eyelashes fell out — *ad she'nashru riseha* — so pained was she at the prospect of being assigned to Eisav. Her eyes' appearance, then, was the physical trace of her deep and sustained prayer to be spared from marrying a wicked man and to be joined instead with a righteous one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָיְתָה יוֹשֶׁבֶת עַל פָּרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים וּמְשָׁאֶלֶת: גָּדוֹל מָה מַעֲשָׂיו? אִישׁ רַע הוּא מְלַסְטֵם בְּרִיּוֹת. קָטָן מָה מַעֲשָׂיו? ״אִישׁ תָּם יֹשֵׁב אֹהָלִים״. וְהָיְתָה בּוֹכָה, עַד שֶׁנָּשְׁרוּ רִיסֵי עֵינֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav continues: **And she would sit at the crossroads and ask: What** are **the deeds of** the **older** son? The passersby would answer: **He is an evil man,** and he **robs people.** She would ask: **What** are **the deeds of** the **younger** son? They would answer: He is **"a quiet man, dwelling in tents"** (Genesis 25:27). **And** because she was so distraught at the prospect of marrying the evil brother, **she would cry** and pray for mercy **until her eyelashes fell out.** Since the weakness of her eyes was due to this cause, characterizing her eyes as weak constitutes praise. This is Leah's prayer for mercy to which Rabbi Yonatan referred.

### Hated Eisav's Ways

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּרְא ה׳ כִּי שְׂנוּאָה לֵאָה״ – מַאי ״שְׂנוּאָה״? אִילֵּימָא שְׂנוּאָה מַמָּשׁ; אֶפְשָׁר בִּגְנוּת בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה לֹא דִּבֵּר הַכָּתוּב, בִּגְנוּת צַדִּיקִים דִּבֵּר הַכָּתוּב?! אֶלָּא רָאָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁשְּׂנוּאִים מַעֲשֵׂה עֵשָׂו בְּפָנֶיהָ, ״וַיִּפְתַּח אֶת רַחְמָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** her desire not to marry Esau is the basis of that **which is written: "And the Lord saw that Leah was hated,** and He opened her womb" (Genesis 29:31). **What** is the meaning of **"hated"? If we say** that she was **literally hated,** is it **possible? The verse** there **did not speak to the disparagement of** even **a non-kosher animal,** so **did the verse** here **speak to the disparagement of the righteous? Rather, the Holy One, Blessed be He, saw that the behavior of Esau was hated by her,** and therefore: **"And He opened her womb."**

It was precisely because of this — because she hated the ways of Eisav and prayed fervently to avoid them — that *vayiftach et rachmah*: God opened her womb. Leah merited to bear Yaakov's firstborn son not arbitrarily but because of this personal righteousness. Her prayers and her love of goodness preceded Rachel's; and so, her son Reuven was the actual firstborn.

### Rachel Warns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי צְנִיעוּת הָיְתָה בָּהּ בְּרָחֵל? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּגֵּד יַעֲקֹב לְרָחֵל כִּי אֲחִי אָבִיהָ הוּא, וְכִי בֶן רִבְקָה הוּא״ וַהֲלֹא בֶּן אֲחוֹת אָבִיהָ הוּא! אֶלָּא אֲמַר לַהּ: מִינַּסְבַת לִי? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אִין, מִיהוּ אַבָּא רַמָּאָה הוּא, וְלָא יָכְלַתְּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now explains the second part of Rabbi Yonatan's explanation: **And what** was a demonstration of the **modesty that Rachel possessed? As it is written: "And Jacob told Rachel that he was her father's brother, and that he was Rebecca's son"** (Genesis 29:12). The Gemara asks: **But isn't he the son of her father's sister?** Why did he say that he was her father's brother? **Rather,** Jacob and Rachel had the following exchange: Jacob **said to** Rachel: **Will you marry me?** Rachel **said to him: Yes, but** my **father is a deceitful person, and you cannot defeat him.**

The *gemara* now turns to Rachel's *tzni'ut* — the modesty that merited the return of the *bechorah* to her son. The verse reads: *vayaged Yaakov l'Rachel ki achi aviha hu* — Yaakov told Rachel that he was her father's brother. The *gemara* notes the inaccuracy: Yaakov was not Lavan's brother but his nephew, the son of Lavan's sister. The *gemara* explains that what this verse really records is the following exchange. Yaakov asked Rachel to marry him. She agreed, but immediately warned him: *abba rama'ah hu* — my father is a deceiver, and *lo yachlat leh* — you will not be able to handle him.

### Deception Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לַהּ: מַאי רַמָּאוּתֵיהּ? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אִית לִי אֲחָתָא דְּקַשִּׁישָׁא מִינַּאי, וְלָא מַנְסְבָא לִי מִקַּמַּהּ. אֲמַר לַהּ: אָחִיו אֲנִי בְּרַמָּאוּת. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: וּמִי שְׁרֵי לְהוּ לְצַדִּיקֵי לְסַגּוֹיֵי בְּרַמָּאוּתָא? אִין, ״עִם נָבָר תִּתָּבָר וְעִם עִקֵּשׁ תִּתַּפָּל״; מְסַר לַהּ סִימָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** Jacob **said to her: What is his** method of **deceit** of which I need be aware? Rachel **said to him: I have a sister who is older than me, and he will not marry me** off **before** he marries **her** off, even if he promises that he will do so. Jacob **said to her: I am his brother,** i.e., equal, **in deceit,** and he will not be able to deceive me. That is why Jacob said that he was "her father's brother." Rachel **said to him: But is it permitted for the righteous to act deceitfully?** Jacob answered her: **Yes,** in certain circumstances. As the verse states concerning God: **"With the pure You show Yourself pure; and with the crooked You show Yourself subtle"** (II Samuel 22:27). Therefore, to counter Laban's deceit, Jacob **gave** Rachel secret **signs** to prove to him that she was the one marrying him.

Yaakov asked her: in what way will he try to deceive me? She explained that Lavan would not allow her to marry before her older sister. This was not simply a refusal — it was a warning that Lavan would agree and then substitute Leah. Yaakov replied: *achiv ani b'rama'ut* — I am his equal in deception. Rachel then asked a pointed question: *u'mi sharei l'tzaddiki l'saguyei b'rama'uta* — is it permitted for righteous people to engage in deception? Yaakov answered with the verse *im navar titbar v'im ikesh titapal* — with the pure, act in a pure way; with the devious, act deceptively. If that is the adversary one faces, that is how one must respond.

This exchange carries weight well beyond the immediate narrative. The figure of Yaakov navigating Lavan's household became a template for the Jew in *galut* — the disempowered party who must exercise a different kind of resourcefulness when faced with an adversary who operates by different rules. The tension between integrity and strategic accommodation in conditions of powerlessness runs through many of the narratives of *Tanakh*.

Yaakov then gave Rachel secret signs — a kind of private code — so that if Lavan substituted Leah on the wedding night, Yaakov would know he was not with Rachel, because Leah would not know the signs.

### Secret Signs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָא מְעַיְּילִי לַהּ לְלֵאָה, סָבְרָה: הַשְׁתָּא מִיכַּסְפָא אֲחָתַאי. מְסַרְתִּינְהוּ נִיהֲלַהּ. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בַבֹּקֶר וְהִנֵּה הִיא לֵאָה״ – מִכְּלָל דְּעַד הַשְׁתָּא לָאו לֵאָה הִיא?! אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ סִימָנִים שֶׁמָּסַר לָהּ יַעֲקֹב לְרָחֵל, וּמְסָרָתַן לְלֵאָה – לָא הֲוָה יָדַע לַהּ עַד הַהִיא שַׁעְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Laban did in fact attempt to have Jacob marry Leah instead of marrying Rachel. **When** Laban's associates **were bringing Leah up** to the wedding canopy to marry Jacob, Rachel **thought: Now my sister will be humiliated** when Jacob discovers that she is the one marrying him. Therefore, Rachel **gave** the signs **to** Leah. **And this is as it is written: "And it came to pass in the morning that, behold, it was Leah"** (Genesis 29:25). This verse is difficult, as **by inference,** should one derive **that until now she was not Leah? Rather, through the signs that Jacob gave to Rachel and that she gave to Leah, he did not know** it was **she until that moment.** This is the modesty of Rachel to which Rabbi Yonatan was referring.

When the time came, Lavan brought Leah into the tent. Rachel, at that moment, considered: *hashta michsafah achtai* — now my sister will be humiliated, publicly rejected. And so Rachel gave the secret signs to Leah. This is why the verse says: *vayehi vaboker vehineh hi Leah* — it was in the morning and behold, it was Leah. The *gemara* presses: does this imply that until that moment Yaakov did not know it was Leah? The answer: yes — *mitoch hasimanim shemasar l'Yaakov l'Rachel u'masartan l'Leah*, because of the signs that Yaakov had entrusted to Rachel, and that Rachel transmitted to Leah, Yaakov could not tell the difference.

### Sister's Honor

It is this act — Rachel's willingness to sacrifice her own marriage and her own position as Yaakov's first wife in order to spare her sister from public humiliation — that constitutes her *tzni'ut*. The word *tzni'ut* here does not refer to modesty in the narrow sense of dress or demeanor, but to a profound self-effacement in the service of another's dignity. Because of this act, her son Yosef ultimately merited the restoration of the *bechorah* that was, as Rabbi Yonatan said, always properly destined to come from her.

## Yocheved Birth

### Counting Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ אַבָּא חֲלִיפָא קִרְוָיָא מֵרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: בִּכְלָלָן – אַתָּה מוֹצֵא שִׁבְעִים; בִּפְרָטָן – אַתָּה מוֹצֵא שִׁבְעִים חָסֵר אֶחָד! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּאוֹמָה הָיְתָה עִם דִּינָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֵת דִּינָה בִתּוֹ״. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, תְּאוֹמָה הָיְתָה עִם בִּנְיָמִן – דִּכְתִיב:
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of Jacob's family. **Abba Ḥalifa Karoya asked Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: In the total** tally of Jacob's family members who descended to Egypt, **you find seventy,** as stated in the verse: "All the souls of the house of Jacob, that came into Egypt, were seventy" (Genesis 46:27). By contrast, **in their individual** listing, when the family members of each of his sons are listed by name, **you find seventy-less-one.** How can this be resolved? Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him: A twin** sister **was** born **with Dinah, as it is written: "And [*ve'et*] his daughter Dinah"** (Genesis 46:15). The term *et* implies an unspecified additional person. Abba Ḥalifa Karoya replied: **If that is so,** one would have to say that **a twin** sister **was** born **with Benjamin, as it is written:**

The *gemara* now turns to a separate question that arises from the same family narrative. Aba Chalifa Karuya posed the question to Rabbi Chiya bar Abba: when you look at the total number given for those who descended to Egypt with Yaakov's household, the Torah states *shivim* — seventy souls. But if you actually enumerate the names listed, you arrive at only sixty-nine.

### Leah's Count

The problem is already visible within the count of Leah's children specifically. The Torah states that Leah's descendants numbered thirty-three, yet an actual tally of the names yields only thirty-two. One cannot dismiss this as rounding, because the Torah itself gives a precise internal count for Leah that is off by one. The missing person must therefore be found among Leah's descendants.

### Dina Twin

One proposed solution is that Dina had a twin sister. The verse *v'et Dinah bito* — "and Dinah his daughter" — contains the word *v'et*, which some read as implying a twin. The *gemara*, however, challenges this: if *v'et* signals a twin, then by the same reasoning Binyamin should also have had a twin, since the verse reads *v'et Binyamin achiv*. The proponent of the Dina-twin theory responds: *margalit tovah haytah b'yadi* — I had a precious pearl in my hand, and you are trying to make me lose it. In other words, now that you are forcing me to give my real answer by showing that the Dina-twin reading leads to unwanted consequences, I must disclose the true solution, which I had preferred to keep as my own. The *Rashbam* explains: he was not truly upset about sharing the answer; he was dramatizing how fine the explanation was. The remark raises, in passing, the interesting question of intellectual ownership of *chiddushim* — a question that resonates differently in an age when private scholarship easily becomes public property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶת בִּנְיָמִן אָחִיו בֶּן אִמּוֹ״?! אֲמַר: מַרְגָּלִית טוֹבָה הָיְתָה בְּיָדִי, וְאַתָּה מְבַקֵּשׁ לְאַבְּדָהּ מִמֶּנִּי; הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: זוֹ יוֹכֶבֶד – שֶׁהוֹרָתָהּ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וְלֵידָתָהּ בֵּין הַחוֹמוֹת; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר יָלְדָה אוֹתָהּ לְלֵוִי בְּמִצְרָיִם״ – לֵידָתָהּ בְּמִצְרַיִם, וְאֵין הוֹרָתָהּ בְּמִצְרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** "And he lifted up his eyes, and saw ***et* Benjamin his brother, his mother's son"** (Genesis 43:29), which would render the count of seventy incorrect. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said:** There **was a goodly pearl [*margalit*] in my hand, and you are trying to have me lose it.** He continued: **So said Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina: This** missing seventieth person is **Jochebed, whose conception was on the journey,** as the family of Jacob descended to Egypt, **and she was born within the walls,** i.e., in Egypt, **as it is stated:** "And the name of Amram's wife was Jochebed, the daughter of Levi, **who was born to Levi in Egypt"** (Numbers 26:59). **Her birth** was **in Egypt, but her conception** was **not in Egypt.** Accordingly, the family had seventy persons upon arrival, but she could not have been listed as descending to Egypt.

### Yocheved Solution

The resolution, as stated by Rabbi Chama bar Hanina, is that the missing person is Yocheved — the daughter of Levi and the future mother of Moshe. *She'haratah baderech v'ladata bein hachomot* — she was conceived during the journey and born once they passed through the walls of Egypt. The verse *asher yaldah otah l'Levi b'Mitzrayim* is read carefully: her birth was in Egypt, *ladata b'Mitzrayim*, but her conception was not, *v'ein haratah b'Mitzrayim*. When the family set out from Canaan, there were sixty-nine souls. By the time they arrived within the walls of Egypt, Yocheved had been born, bringing the number to seventy. The count of thirty-three for Leah's descendants thus includes Yocheved, born in transit, and is exactly correct.

## Yosef Born

### Timing Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ מֵרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי, כְּתִיב: ״וַיְהִי כַּאֲשֶׁר יָלְדָה רָחֵל אֶת יוֹסֵף וְגוֹ׳״ – מַאי שְׁנָא כִּי אִתְיְלִיד יוֹסֵף? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רָאָה יַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ שֶׁאֵין זַרְעוֹ שֶׁל עֵשָׂו נִמְסָר אֶלָּא בְּיַד זַרְעוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה בֵית יַעֲקֹב אֵשׁ, וּבֵית יוֹסֵף לֶהָבָה, וּבֵית עֵשָׂו לְקַשׁ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ḥelbo asked Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani: It is written: "And it came to pass, when Rachel gave birth to Joseph,** that Jacob said to Laban: Send me away, that I may go to my own place, and to my country" (Genesis 30:25). **What was different when Joseph was born,** that Jacob decided only then to return home? Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani **said to him: Jacob our patriarch saw** prophetically **that the descendants of Esau** will be **delivered only to the hand of the descendants of Joseph, as it is stated: "And the house of Jacob shall be a fire, and the house of Joseph a flame, and the house of Esau for straw,** and they shall kindle in them, and devour them" (Obadiah 1:18). Jacob had left Eretz Yisrael to escape Esau, but he now felt confident that he could return without endangering his family.

A new question is posed by Rabbi Chelbo to Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani: the verse states *vayehi ka'asher yaldah Rachel et Yosef* — it was when Rachel gave birth to Yosef that Yaakov turned to Lavan and demanded to be released to return to his father's house. Why specifically at that moment? What was it about Yosef's birth that made Yaakov ready to leave?

### Yosef Defeats Eisav

The *gemara* explains: *ra'ah Yaakov Avinu she'ein zaro shel Eisav nimsar ela b'yad zaro shel Yosef* — Yaakov perceived prophetically that the descendants of Eisav would only be delivered into the hands of the descendants of Yosef. Yaakov knew that when he turned back toward Canaan he would immediately face Eisav, who was coming with four hundred men. He could not be ready to face that encounter — and, more broadly, could not be prepared for the long historical confrontation with Eisav's descendants — until Yosef had been born.

### Fire & Flame

The proof text is the verse from Ovadiah: *vehaya veit Yaakov eish uveit Yosef lehava uveit Eisav l'kash* — the house of Yaakov shall be fire, the house of Yosef a flame, and the house of Eisav shall be stubble. Fire does not ignite stubble without a flame as intermediary. The house of Yaakov alone — the other tribes — cannot bring Eisav down. It is the house of Yosef, the flame, that is the essential catalyst. Yaakov therefore waited until that catalytic force was present before he could contemplate the return journey.

### David Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״וַיַּכֵּם דָּוִד מֵהַנֶּשֶׁף וְעַד הָעֶרֶב לְמׇחֳרָתָם״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּאַקְרְיָךְ נְבִיאֵי לָא אַקְרְיָךְ כְּתוּבֵי; דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּלֶכְתּוֹ אֶל צִקְלַג, נָפְלוּ עָלָיו מִמְּנַשֶּׁה עַדְנָה וְיוֹזָבָד וִידִיעֲאֵל וּמִיכָאֵל וְיוֹזָבָד וֶאֱלִיהוּא וְצִלְּתָי – רָאשֵׁי הָאֲלָפִים אֲשֶׁר לִמְנַשֶּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥelbo **raised an objection to** Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani: Concering a battle between the Jewish people and the nation of Amalek, who descend from Esau, the verse states: **"And David smote them from the twilight even to the evening of the next day"** (I Samuel 30:17). David was from the tribe of Judah, yet he was able to defeat the descendants of Esau. Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani **said to him: Whoever read** and taught **Prophets to you did not read** and teach **Writings to you, as it is written: "As he went to Ziklag, there fell to him of Manasseh, Adnah, and Jozabad, and Jediael, and Michael, and Jozabad, and Elihu, and Zillethai, captains of thousands that were of Manasseh,** and they helped David against the troop" (I Chronicles 12:21–2). The verse in Writings teaches that David's campaign against Esau was led by Joseph's descendants, from the family of Manasseh.

A challenge is raised: was there not a case where Eisav's descendants — specifically Amalek — were defeated without the involvement of Yosef's tribe? The verse in *Shmuel* records: *vayakem David mei'haneshef ve'ad ha'erev* — David smote them from dawn until the following day. David was from the tribe of Yehudah, not Yosef.

### Menashe Help

The response: *d'akrayach Nevi'ei lo akrayach Kesuvei* — whoever taught you *Nevi'im* did not teach you *Kesuvim*. In *Divrei HaYamim* it is written: *uvlecho Tziklag naflu alav mi'Menashe* — when David went to Tziklag, members of the tribe of Menashe joined him. The verse then lists them by name: Adnah, Yozavad, Yediael, Micha'el, Yozavad, Elihu, and Zilsai — *rashei ha'alafim asher l'Menashe*, captains of thousands from Menashe. David did not defeat Amalek alone; he had with him the tribe of Menashe, which is the tribe of Yosef. The principle stands.

### Shimon Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: ״וּמֵהֶם מִן בְּנֵי שִׁמְעוֹן הָלְכוּ לְהַר שֵׂעִיר אֲנָשִׁים חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת, וּפְלַטְיָה וּנְעַרְיָה וּרְפָיָה וְעֻזִּיאֵל בְּנֵי יִשְׁעִי בְּרֹאשָׁם, וַיַּכּוּ אֶת שְׁאֵרִית הַפְּלֵטָה לַעֲמָלֵק, וַיֵּשְׁבוּ שָׁם עַד הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה״! אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא: יִשְׁעִי מִבְּנֵי מְנַשֶּׁה אָתֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבְנֵי מְנַשֶּׁה חֵפֶר וְיִשְׁעִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raises an objection:** Another verse indicates that the descendants of Simeon also have the ability to defeat the descendants of Esau: **"And some of them, even of the sons of Simeon, five hundred men, went to Mount Seir, having for their captains Pelatiah, and Ne'ariah, and Rephaiah, and Uzziel, the sons of Ishi. And they struck the remnant of the Amalekites that escaped, and dwelled there until this day"** (I Chronicles 4:42–43). **Rabba bar Sheila said** in response: **Ishi came from** the **children of Manasseh, as it is written: And the sons of Manasseh: Hepher and Ishi.**

Rav Yosef raises a second challenge. The verse in *Divrei HaYamim* records: *umeihem b'nei Shimon halchu l'Har Se'ir* — from the sons of Shimon, five hundred men went to Har Se'ir, the territory of Eisav. They are listed as going under the leadership of *b'nei Yish'i*, and the verse concludes *vayaku et she'erit hapeleita l'Amalek* — they struck down the remnant of Amalek. This was entirely under the banner of Shimon, with no apparent involvement of Yosef's descendants.

### Yish'ei Resolution

Rabba bar Shila resolves this: *Yish'i mibnei Menashe asi* — Yish'i, despite appearing in the context of Shimon, was in fact a descendant of Menashe. The textual support is a verse which lists Eifer and Yish'i among the descendants of Menashe: *v'eileh rashei beit avosam v'Eifer v'Yish'i*. Though the name appears in the context of Shimon's campaign, the leader Yish'i was from Menashe — from Yosef's tribe. The principle is thereby preserved: in every case where Eisav's descendants fell, the descendants of Yosef were present.

## Double Portion Categories

### Five Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַבְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם בַּזְּרוֹעַ וּבַלְּחָיַיִם וּבַקֵּיבָה, וּבְמוּקְדָּשִׁין, וּבְשֶׁבַח שֶׁשָּׁבְחוּ נְכָסִים לְאַחַר מִיתַת אֲבִיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 7:4–5): **The firstborn** priest **takes a double** portion **of the foreleg, and of the jaw, and of the maw,** which are given to a priest from all slaughtered non-sacred cattle, sheep, and goats (see Deuteronomy 18:3). **And** a firstborn, whether he is a priest or non-priest, takes a double portion **of** his father's **sacrificial** animals **and of the enhancement of the property** that occurred **after the death of their father.**

The *gemara* returns to the halachic discussion with a new *baraisa*. The *bechor* takes a double portion in five categories: *zro'a, lechayim*, and *keivah* — the gifts given to a *kohen* from slaughtered animals; *mukdashin* — sanctified animals; and *shevach* — the appreciation in value of the estate after the father's death.

### Unguarded Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? הִנִּיחַ לָהֶן אֲבִיהֶן פָּרָה מוּחְכֶּרֶת וּמוּשְׂכֶּרֶת בְּיַד אֲחֵרִים, אוֹ שֶׁהָיְתָה רוֹעָה בָּאֲפָר; וְיָלְדָה – בְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם. אֲבָל בָּנוּ בָּתִּים וְנָטְעוּ כְּרָמִים – אֵין בְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **How so?** To what type of enhancement is this referring? If **their father left them a cow** that was **leased or rented in the possession of others, or if it was grazing in the meadow, and it gave birth,** then the **firstborn takes a double** portion of the rental income or of the calf. **But** if the inheritors **built homes or planted vineyards** after their father's death, thereby enhancing the property, the **firstborn does not take a double** portion. This is not considered enhancement of the father's property, but profit due to their actions.

The *baraisa* illustrates the rule of *shevach* with a case involving a cow. If the father left a cow that was leased out to a third party under a profit-sharing arrangement (*muchkeres*), or rented out outright (*muskeres*), or was simply grazing in the field — and it then gave birth after the father's death — the *bechor* takes a double portion of the newborn animal.

### Improvement Rule

By contrast, if the heirs actively built houses on the land or planted vineyards after the father's death, the *bechor* does not receive a double portion of the value of those improvements. The principle governing both rules will be analyzed further, but the distinction in outline is clear: passive appreciation that flows from assets already in the father's possession benefits the *bechor* doubly; active improvements generated by the heirs' own labor do not.

### Kohen Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי הַזְּרוֹעַ וְהַלְּחָיַיִם וְהַקֵּיבָה – הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאָתֵי לִידֵי אֲבוּהוֹן, פְּשִׁיטָא! וְאִי דְּלָא אָתֵי לִידֵי אֲבוּהוֹן, רָאוּי הוּא – וְאֵין הַבְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל בָּרָאוּי כִּבְמוּחְזָק!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: Concerning **this** right to collect a double portion of **the foreleg, and the jaw, and the maw, what are the circumstances? If** they **came into the possession of their father,** then it is **obvious** that the firstborn collects a double portion. **And if** they **did not come into the possession of their father,** then these items **are** merely property **due** to their father, **and the firstborn does not take** a double portion **of** property **due** to the deceased **as** he does the property his father **possessed.**

The *baraisa*'s inclusion of *zro'a, lechayim*, and *keivah* — the arm, cheeks, and maw given as priestly gifts when a non-sacred animal is slaughtered — is immediately puzzling. These gifts do not belong to the father; they must be given to a *kohen*. How can the *bechor* receive a double portion of something that was never the father's property?

## Mechari Kehunah

### Double in Ra'uy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַכִּירֵי כְהוּנָּה עָסְקִינַן, וּדְאִשְׁתְּחִיט בְּחַיֵּי דַּאֲבוּהוֹן; וְקָסָבַר: מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ, כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here, we are dealing with associates of the priesthood,** i.e., people who have an arrangement with a specific priest to give him their priestly gifts. **And** it was a case **where** the animal **was slaughtered while the father was alive, and** the *tanna* of this *baraita* **holds:** Priestly **gifts that were not** yet **separated are considered as though they** have already been **separated.** Therefore, even though the gifts were not yet given, they were considered in the possession of the priest before his death.

The *gemara* sharpens the difficulty. If these priestly gifts had already been given to the father-*kohen* during his lifetime, then they are simply part of the estate and the *bechor*'s double portion is obvious — it falls within the ordinary rules of inheritance. But if the father died before receiving them, then they are *ra'uy* — something that was fit to come to him but had not yet arrived. And the rule of *bechorah* is explicitly that *ein habechor notal b'ra'uy k'vmuchzak* — the *bechor* does not receive a double portion in *ra'uy* as he does in property the father already held. So how can the *bechor* receive double in these gifts?

### Mechari Definition

The *gemara* answers: *hacha b'makirei kehunah askinan* — the case involves *makirei kehunah*, an established practice whereby particular families of householders always gave their priestly gifts to a specific *kohen* or *kohen* family. Under this arrangement, the gifts were not simply unclaimed goods awaiting assignment to any available *kohen*; they were as good as designated for this particular *kohen*. The relationship was sufficiently entrenched — and in some cases institutionalized across families — that the specific *kohen* effectively had a presumptive claim to them before they were formally handed over.

### Already Separated

Beyond the question of *makirei kehunah*, there is a further condition: *d'ishtachit b'chayei d'avuhon* — the animal must have been slaughtered during the father's lifetime. Only once the animal is slaughtered does the obligation to give the priestly portions take effect.

The underlying principle is then stated: *d'kasavar matnot shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu damu* — the priestly gifts, even before they have been physically separated and handed over, are regarded as if they have already been separated. This is a significant legal presumption: although the gifts still reside physically within the carcass of the animal, the obligation to separate them is treated as having already been fulfilled in a juristic sense.

### Gifts Presumption

The double *chiddush* operates as follows. The animal has been slaughtered, the obligation to separate the priestly portions has arisen, and because of the practice of *makirei kehunah* this *kohen* was the designated recipient. The combination of *matnot shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu damu* — gifts treated as if already separated — and the established designation to this particular *kohen* means that, juristically, the father-*kohen* is treated as having already received these portions before his death. They are therefore *muchzak*, not merely *ra'uy*, and the *bechor* takes a double share.

*Tosafot* raises the question of whether this constitutes genuine ownership. Could the *kohen*, for instance, betroth a woman with these gifts before physically receiving them? The answer is probably not — the *kohen* is not yet technically the owner. But there is a middle category: not yet owned, yet already quasi-possessed by virtue of designation and legal presumption. It is this intermediate status that is sufficient to allow the *bechor* to take a double portion upon the father's death.

## Kodashim Kalim

### Kodashim Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּקְדָּשִׁין – לָאו דִּידֵיהּ נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to analyze the *baraita*: Why does the firstborn take a double portion of the father's **sacrificial** animals? Isn't it so that once the father consecrates them, **they** belong to Heaven and **are not his?**

The *baraisa* also lists *mukdashin* — sanctified animals — as a category in which the *bechor* takes double. This is at first glance even more difficult than the priestly gifts. Sanctified animals are not the father's property at all; they belong to the *Beit HaMikdash*. A *chatat*, for instance, is entirely *hekdesh*; the father has no proprietary interest in it whatsoever. How could the *bechor* receive double of something his father never owned?

### Shelamim Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – דְּאָמַר: מָמוֹן בְּעָלִים הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּמָעֲלָה מַעַל בַּה׳״ – לְרַבּוֹת קֳדָשִׁים קַלִּים, שֶׁהֵן מָמוֹן בְּעָלִים; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is stated **with regard to** a case of **offerings of lesser sanctity,** such as peace offerings, **and in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says:** These sacrificial animals **are** considered the **property of** their **owners,** as opposed to property of Heaven. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that concerning one who steals another's property and takes a false oath denying he has done so, incurring the obligation to bring a guilt-offering, the verse states: **"And commits a trespass against the Lord,** and deals falsely with his neighbor" (Leviticus 5:21). The verse serves **to include offerings of lesser sanctity, which are** the **property of** their **owners;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.** Consequently, the firstborn collects a double portion of these as well.

The *gemara* resolves this by specifying: the case involves *kodashim kalim* — lesser-sanctified offerings — and specifically *shelamim*, peace offerings. In a *shelamim*, the owner eats virtually all of the meat; only the entrails are placed on the altar and a small portion goes to the *kohen*. The bulk of the animal remains, in a meaningful sense, the owner's.

### Mammon Ba'alim

According to Rabbi Yosi HaGelili, *shelamim* are considered *mammon ba'alim* — the financial property of the owner — even after they have been sanctified. This position allows the owner to betroth a woman with a *shelamim* animal, since he is giving her something of real value that he owns. The *gemara* uses this principle to resolve the *baraisa*: if a *kohen* — or indeed any person — sanctified a *shelamim* and then died before the offering was brought, his children bring it to the Temple, and upon eating the *bechor*'s portion of the meat, the *bechor* is entitled to double — because under Rabbi Yosi HaGelili's principle, the meat was and remains the owner's property, and the *bechorah* applies to it.

### Ma'ala Ma'al

The *gemara* cites a *baraisa* in support of Rabbi Yosi HaGelili's position. The verse dealing with a bailment reads: *uma'ala ma'al b'Hashem* — and he trespasses against God — in a case where one denies holding another's deposit and swears falsely. The plain meaning is that the false oath constitutes a trespass against God. Rabbi Yosi HaGelili reads it differently: the object itself has a dimension of belonging to God. An ordinary object held in trust does not belong to God. What object could simultaneously be denied to the human owner and constitute a trespass against God? A *shelamim* animal. If someone entrusted me with his *shelamim* before it was offered, and I deny it, I have robbed both him of his property *and* denied something that is also consecrated to God. The verse thereby implies that *shelamim* retain the character of *mammon ba'alim* — the owner's property — even while sanctified.

### Non-Kohen Shelamim

The implication extends beyond the case of a *kohen*-father. If any person sanctified a *shelamim* and died, his heirs bring the offering and the *bechor* among those heirs takes double of the meat. This is so because, according to Rabbi Yosi HaGelili, the meat of the *shelamim* is the owner's property — *mammon ba'alim* — and is therefore subject to the laws of inheritance and the *bechor*'s double portion, in contrast to other categories of *korbanos* where the concept of *mishulchan gavoah kazchu* applies: those who eat them eat from God's table, and the meat is not regarded as the personal estate of the owner.
