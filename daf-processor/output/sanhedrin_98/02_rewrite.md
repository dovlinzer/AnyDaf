# Sanhedrin 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

### Intro: Teshuvah Debate

The opening of Daf 98 returns us to the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua regarding the conditions under which Mashiach will come. Rabbi Eliezer holds that Israel must perform *teshuvah*, while Rabbi Yehoshua maintains that God will compel it if necessary. One might ask whether both Tannaim ultimately agree that *teshuvah* is required in principle, differing only on the mechanism. The answer may well be yes — but the deeper disagreement concerns the nature of the *teshuvah* itself.

### Nature of Teshuvah

Is the *teshuvah* in question a sincere, self-motivated return to God, or a coerced repentance — a *teshuvah* performed, as it were, with a gun to one's head? This distinction operates not only at the individual level but at the level of civilization itself. If one follows Rabbi Eliezer's approach, then the requirement for *teshuvah* implies something far more demanding than personal moral improvement: it implies that we must transform the world into a more righteous place, that the Messianic era can only be ushered in through genuine collective moral achievement. Rabbi Yehoshua's position, by contrast, envisions a *teshuvah* imposed by God — and even if such compelled repentance technically occurs, it would not reflect an intrinsic transformation of the world's character.

This touches on the fundamental question of whether the Messianic era is something humanity brings about or something God imposes upon history. If one takes the Rabbi Eliezer approach seriously, then bringing about the Messianic period is very much under human control — it requires genuinely turning the world into a better place, not merely producing a generation that performs *teshuvah* under duress.

### Generational Worthiness

This connects to the concept of *dor shekulo zakai* — an entirely meritorious generation — versus *dor shekulo chayav* — an entirely sinful generation. Rabbi Yehoshua's position, that God will bring it regardless, is consistent with the notion that Israel need not be collectively righteous; the generation could be entirely sinful. But even if one holds that *teshuvah* is necessary, there is a significant difference between a generation that genuinely transforms itself and one that does so only under the pressure of calamity — just as one might argue that repentance performed on *Yom Kippur* out of habit does not necessarily reflect a changed world. One might note, with a degree of dark irony, that it is far more within our current power to produce a *dor shekulo chayav* than a *dor shekulo zakai*.

### Shmuel & Rav Alignment

How does this debate map onto the positions of Rav and Shmuel? Shmuel's position — *dai lo la'avel sheya'amod be'evlo*, it is enough for the mourner to stand in his mourning — implies that the suffering of exile is itself sufficient and that God will bring Mashiach in His own time without requiring *teshuvah* at all. This seems to diverge even from Rabbi Yehoshua's framing, since Rabbi Yehoshua at least gestures toward God compelling *teshuvah*, whereas Shmuel's focus is simply on the inexorable arrival of the appointed time. The point is that the various formulations — Rabbi Yehoshua's *teshuvah* forced by God, Shmuel's simple faith in the appointed time — are all variations on a single theme: it is not our moral achievement that brings Mashiach, but God's unilateral action. In none of these versions is human-initiated transformation of the world the operative factor.

## Textual Debate

### Eliezer's Verse

The Gemara records the actual textual exchange. Rabbi Eliezer cites a verse from Isaiah: *Im tashuvu Yisrael ne'um Hashem elai tashuvu* — "If you return to Me, Israel, says God, return to Me." This verse makes the coming of redemption conditional upon Israel's repentance — the classic prooftext for his position.

### Yehoshua's Verse

Rabbi Yehoshua responds by citing a passage from Daniel. He says: *V'shamati et ha'ish levush habadim asher mima'al l'meimei haye'or* — "I heard the man dressed in linen who was above the waters of the river," who lifted up his right and left hands to the heavens and swore by the One who lives forever: *Ki l'mo'ed mo'adim vachatzi* — "in a time, the time of the times and a half." And: *U'kechalot napetz yad am kodesh tichlena kol eleh* — "when the spreading forth of the hand of the holy nation comes to an end, then all of this will be completed." The import of this prophecy is clear: there is a fixed time, and at that time, when the people are at their lowest and most powerless state, the Messianic era will arrive. The people need not be performing *teshuvah*; it comes at an appointed moment regardless.

### Eliezer's Silence

The Gemara records: *V'shatak Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Eliezer fell silent. He had no response. This is striking. It seems that what silenced him was not just any counterargument but specifically the prophecies in Daniel, which are the most explicitly eschatological texts in all of Scripture — the most precise and detailed descriptions of the end of days, of the time of resurrection or of Mashiach's coming (even if the word *mashiach* does not appear there explicitly). The vividness and specificity of Daniel's prophetic vision apparently struck Rabbi Eliezer as unanswerable, and he conceded the point, at least on the textual level.

## Signs of Mashiach

### Prosperity Sign

The Gemara now shifts away from the debate about *teshuvah* and turns to the question of how one recognizes the Messianic period. Rabbi Abba declares: *Ein l'cha ketz meguleh mizeh* — there is no more revealed sign of the end than this verse: *Va'atem harei Yisrael anafchem titnu ufriychem tis'u le'ami Yisrael* — "And you, mountains of Israel, your boughs will give forth and your fruit you will bear for My people Israel." The sign of the Messianic era, Rabbi Abba says, is prosperity — the land flourishing and yielding its fruit abundantly. This is the clearest, most openly revealed *ketz* one could point to.

### Scarcity Sign

Rabbi Elazar — note, this is Rabbi Elazar, not Rabbi Eliezer — disagrees, or rather offers what he considers an even better sign. He cites the verse: *Ki lifnei hayamim hahem sechar adam lo nihya usechar behema einena* — "For before those days there was no hire for man and no hire for beast." The sign he points to is the opposite of prosperity: a time of profound scarcity and economic collapse, when one cannot hire out people or animals for wages. This is consistent with earlier Gemara discussions that portrayed the period immediately preceding Mashiach as one of tremendous hardship.

### Affliction Period

The verse continues: *Ulayotzei velavo ein shalom min hatzar* — "And for those who go out and come in there is no peace from the oppressor." The Gemara asks: what does this phrase mean? Rav Abba explains: *Af talmidei chachamim* — even Torah scholars, who are normally associated with the verse *Shalom rav le'ohavei Toratecha* — "great peace for those who love Your Torah" — even they will have no peace. They will be unable to sit and study, forced instead to constantly exert themselves to earn a living during this period of extreme want. Rashi understands the *tzar* referenced in the verse as referring either to the general *tzarot* — the sufferings of that period — or to the *yetzer hara*, which would bring us back to the theme of a sinful generation.

### Equal Prices

Shmuel adds another sign: *Ad sheyishvu kol hashearim kulam shekulim* — until all prices become equal. Rashi offers two readings of this: either everything becomes equally cheap — wine costs no more than flour — or everything becomes equally expensive, with flour priced as dearly as wine. Both readings describe a period of economic distortion, though the two readings point in opposite directions regarding whether the Messianic era is preceded by abundance or collapse.

## Conditions for Mashiach

### Fish Scarcity

The Gemara now presents a series of statements about the conditions under which Ben David will come. Rabbi Chanina states: *Ein Ben David ba ad sheyitvakesh dag lechola velo yimatze* — the Son of David will not come until it reaches a point where one cannot even obtain a fish for a sick person who needs it. The destitution will be so extreme that even the smallest comfort cannot be procured. He cites the verse from Ezekiel: *Az ashkiya meimeihem venaharotam kashemen olichem* — "Then I will sink their waters and make their rivers run like oil" — meaning the rivers will be so thick, so stilled, that one cannot pull a fish from them. The following verse then continues: *Bayom hahu atzmiach keren leveit Yisrael* — "On that day I will cause the pride of the house of Israel to flourish."

### Loss Sovereignty

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina teaches: *Ein Ben David ba ad shetichleh malchut hazala miYisrael* — the Son of David will not come until even the most lowly, the most negligible form of sovereignty is removed from Israel. Rashi explains: *Shelo tehe lahem shum shiltonut leYisrael afilu shiltonut kala vedala* — Israel will not possess even the slightest, most trivial form of authority; they will be utterly subjugated to the non-Jewish powers. This is a challenging statement in its contemporary implications. One might ask: is the restoration of Jewish sovereignty in the Land of Israel a sign that we are moving *closer* to the Messianic era, or does this teaching suggest the opposite — that as long as Israel retains some form of rule, Mashiach will not come, and that total subjugation is the necessary precondition? The verse cited is *Vecharat hazalzalim bemazmerot* — cutting off even the smallest branches — with *zalzalim* read as representing even the slightest remnant of sovereignty. The following verse: *Ba'et hahi yuval shai l'Hashem tzevaot* — "At that time a gift will be brought to God."

### Remove Haughty

Rabbi Zeira citing Rabbi Chanina: *Ein Ben David ba ad sheyichlu gasei haruach miYisrael* — the Son of David will not come until the haughty ones are eliminated from Israel. The verse: *Ki az asir mikirbecha alizei ga'avasecha* — "Then I will remove from your midst those who exult in pride." The following verse adds: *Vehisharti bekirbecha am ani vadal vechasu beshem Hashem* — "I will leave among you a humble and poor nation, and they shall take refuge in God's name." Here, interestingly, the poverty may not be financial destitution but rather a quality of spiritual humility — a nation poor in arrogance rather than in wealth. This potentially points toward a more righteous period rather than simply a time of affliction.

### End Judges

Rav Simlai, citing Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Shimon, teaches: *Ein Ben David ba ad sheyichlu kol shoftim veshotrim miYisrael* — the Son of David will not come until all judges and officials are eliminated from Israel. The verse: *Va'ashiva yadai alayich ve'etzrof kavar sigayich* — "I will turn my hand against you and smelt out your dross" — and then: *Va'ashiva shoftayich kevarishona* — "I will restore your judges as in former times." The implication is that God will first purge all of the current judges — who are themselves dross — before restoring true judges of the caliber of antiquity.

### Righteousness Redemption

Ulla teaches — some textual versions read *Ein Yisrael nifdis* — *Ein Yerushalayim nifdis ela bitzedaka* — Jerusalem will be redeemed only through righteousness. The verse: *Tzion bemishpat tipadeh veshaveha bitzedaka* — "Zion will be redeemed through justice, and those who return to her through righteousness." Not *berachamim*, not through divine mercy alone — it must be earned. This is the basis on which the Vilna Gaon, in *Birkat HaMazon*, omits the word *berachamav* from the blessing *Boneh Yerushalayim*, since Zion is redeemed *bitzedaka* and not merely through mercy.

### Haughty & Magi

Rav Papa adds a series of parallel statements. *Ee batlu yeheiri batlu amgushi* — if the haughty ones cease, the Magi will also cease. The *amgushi* appears to refer to a priestly class in ancient Persia — the Zoroastrian Magi — who are associated with the oppressive non-Jewish powers. *Ee batlu dayani batlu gezirpati* — if the [corrupt] judges cease, so too will the *gezirpati*, some form of Persian official or oppressor. The prooftexts: for the haughty and the Magi, the verse *Ve'etzrof kavar sigayich ve'asira kol bedilayich* — "I will smelt your dross and remove all your dross," with Rashi explaining *bedilayich* as those who separate Israel from God, i.e., the Magi. For the judges and oppressors, the verse *Hesir Hashem mishpatayich pina oyvayich* — "God has removed your judges, turned away your enemies." The thrust of all these teachings is the same: poor and arrogant leadership is what keeps Israel from God and is ultimately responsible for Israel's suffering at the hands of foreign powers.

## Hope in Affliction

### Shrinking Generation

Rabbi Yochanan offers a teaching of profound pastoral importance: *Im ra'ita dor shemisma'et veholech* — if you see a generation where things are going from bad to worse, declining and shrinking — whether in terms of moral quality or material circumstances — *chakei lo*, nevertheless wait for him, continue to long for Mashiach.

### Divine Response

The prooftext: *Ve'et am ani toshia* — "And a poor nation He will redeem." When things are at their worst, that is not necessarily a sign that the Messianic era is most distant; it may be precisely the opposite — that God's redemption is most imminent. A second teaching from Rabbi Yochanan reinforces this: *Im ra'ita dor shetzarot rabot ba'ot alav k'nahar* — if you see a generation upon which many afflictions are coming like a rushing river — *achakeh lo*, still wait for him. The verse: *Ki yavo k'nahar tzar ruach Hashem nosesah vo* — "When affliction comes like a river, the spirit of God surges within it" — *usamich lei uva l'Tziyon goel* — and immediately following: "And a redeemer will come to Zion."

### Hope in Exile

This teaching carries enormous significance for understanding how Jews historically endured persecution. The belief in Mashiach was not merely theological doctrine but a psychological lifeline. The principle of *af al pi sheyitmahmeha im kol ze achakeh lo* — even if he delays, I will wait for him — is not passive resignation but active hope. The possibility that precisely when things are at their worst, redemption may be nearest, gave people the capacity to endure what would otherwise be unbearable. This also maps onto the broader debate: Rabbi Yehoshua's position, that God will bring it regardless of our merit, is what sustains hope in dark times. If Mashiach could only come when we had transformed the world into a righteous place, then suffering would suggest distance from redemption. But if God brings it when He sees there is no other way, then suffering itself becomes a potential harbinger of redemption. Notably, the Gemara at this point makes no mention of *teshuvah* at all — consistent with Rabbi Yehoshua's framework, God's intervention operates independently of human moral achievement.

### Hasidic Story

This dynamic is captured in a famous Hasidic exchange. A rabbi delivers a *drasha* urging his congregation to be *metzapeh l'viat haMashiach* — to constantly long and wait for Mashiach, to know that at any moment he could come and take the Jewish people to *Eretz Yisrael*. A man goes home inspired and tells his wife. She is unmoved — life is good here, the community is comfortable, the business is running, she has no desire to pick up and move to Jerusalem. He returns to the rabbi and reports her response. The rabbi asks: but what about the *pogromtchiks* — the violent enemies who bring persecution? He goes back and tells his wife the rabbi's question. She replies: tell the rabbi that Mashiach should come and take the *pogromtchiks* to *Eretz Yisrael*. The story, with its wry irony, makes a serious point: genuine longing for Mashiach is most naturally felt by those who need to be saved from their current reality, who feel the weight of exile and suffering. The desire for redemption is inseparable from the experience of exile.

## Merit or Force

### Zakai or Chayav

Rabbi Yochanan sharpens the dichotomy he introduced earlier: *Ein ben David ba ela b'dor shekulo zakai o kulo chayav* — the Son of David will not come except in a generation that is entirely meritorious or entirely sinful. The prooftext for *dor shekulo zakai*: *Ve'amech kulam tzadikim l'olam yirshu aretz* — "Your people will all be righteous, they will inherit the land forever." The prooftext for *dor shekulo chayav*: *Vayar ki ein ish vayishtomem ki ein mafgia* — "He saw that there was no man and was astounded that there was no one to intercede" — and then: *L'ma'ani e'eseh* — "For My own sake I will act," says God.

### Merit vs. Intervention

This is a sharper and more theologically precise formulation than Rabbi Yehoshua's earlier statement. Rabbi Yehoshua spoke of God bringing some external pressure to compel *teshuvah*, meaning there is still *teshuvah* in the equation, even if forced. Rabbi Yochanan's formulation removes *teshuvah* from the picture entirely in the case of *dor shekulo chayav*. It is not that God makes them repent — it is that when all is lost, when not a single righteous man remains and there is no one even to intercede, God acts for His own sake. The logic is essentially eschatological emergency: if Mashiach does not come now, everything will be lost entirely. As one student observed, this parallels the Egypt narrative — the forty-ninth level of defilement, the point at which another moment of delay would mean total assimilation and loss. The Gemara does not explicitly draw this connection here, but the structure is identical. And importantly, the correspondence between *dor shekulo zakai* and prosperity on the one hand, and *dor shekulo chayav* and affliction on the other, is suggested but not made explicit by the Gemara.

## Be'itah Achishena

### Two Verses

Rabbi Alexandri presents a teaching in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *Rami* — he posed a contradiction between two verses. It is written *be'itah* — "in its time I will bring it" — and immediately adjacent, it is written *achishena* — "I will hasten it." If there is a fixed time, how can it be hastened? If it can be hastened, how is there a fixed time? The resolution: *Zachu, achishena* — if they merit, I will hasten it. *Lo zachu, be'itah* — if they do not merit, it will come in its fixed time.

### Reassurance vs. Empowerm…

This resolution introduces a theological framework meaningfully different from the *dor shekulo zakai o dor shekulo chayav* model. The *be'itah achishena* framework insists on a fixed time: there is an absolute outer limit beyond which God will bring redemption regardless. In this sense it is reassuring — even if we keep failing, there is a moment at which God has committed to act. One need not be driven to anxiety by the thought that human moral failure could indefinitely delay the end of history. But this reassurance comes at a cost. The vision is a fundamentally disempowering one. Human action can only hasten the timetable; it cannot be the fundamental cause of redemption. The deepest initiative lies with God, and we can at most adjust the schedule somewhat by our merit. This stands in real tension with a different, more demanding orientation — the view that it is our responsibility to bring the world to a Messianic state, that *tikkun olam* is not just a way to hasten God's plan but is itself the operative mechanism of redemption. The *be'itah achishena* model is not that vision; it is, rather, a reassurance that God has a plan and we can only modestly accelerate it. The *dor shekulo chayav* model is different still — there the issue is not a fixed timetable but a moral cliff edge: either all are righteous and Mashiach comes through merit, or all are lost and God intervenes. Neither model gives us unlimited agency.

## Glory or Humility

### Clouds vs. Donkey

Rabbi Alexandri presents a second contradiction, again in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *Rami* — there is a verse that says *Ve'aru im ananei shamaya kevar enosh ata* — "And behold, with the clouds of heaven, one like a man was coming" (Daniel), suggesting Mashiach arrives with extraordinary glory and majesty. Yet another verse says *Ani verochev al hachamor* — "Humble and riding on a donkey" (Zechariah). The resolution follows the same structure: *Zachu, im ananei shamaya* — if they merit, he comes with the clouds of heaven, with glory and grandeur. *Lo zachu, ani verochev al hachamor* — if they do not merit, he comes humble on a donkey. The manner of Mashiach's arrival thus reflects the moral state of the generation receiving him. A generation that has achieved spiritual greatness receives a glorious redemption; a generation that is spiritually diminished receives a redemption that matches its lowly state.

### Shapur's Offer

The Gemara records an exchange between the Persian king Shapur and Shmuel. Shapur said to Shmuel: *Amritu Mashiach al chamara ati* — I hear that you Jews say Mashiach comes on a donkey. *Ishdar lei susaya* — I'll send him a horse, one of my finest. Why does he need to ride a donkey? Shmuel replied: *Mi is lach bar chivor gavnei?* — Do you have a horse of a hundred different colors? Rashi's reading is that Shmuel was essentially deflecting with a nonsensical answer — Mashiach does not come on just any donkey, he comes on a supernal donkey, and Shapur's horse is irrelevant to the matter. The exchange is mildly absurdist but the underlying point is that the imagery of the donkey is not to be taken as a practical transportation problem awaiting a royal equestrian solution.

## Rabbi Yehoshua's Vision

### Elijah's Location

The Gemara now presents one of its most celebrated *agadot*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi once encountered Elijah, who was standing at the entrance to the cave of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai. The significance of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's cave in this context is not elaborated by Rashi, who treats it as a historical detail. One might note that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai famously fled to a cave to escape the Romans — which may carry thematic resonance with the Rome imagery that follows — though Rashi does not draw this connection explicitly.

### Ask Mashiach

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asked Elijah: *Atina l'alma de'asei?* — Will I enter the World to Come? Elijah replied: *Im yirtzeh adon zeh* — if God wills it. Rashi explains *adon zeh* as referring to God, not to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — the question of why Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai would determine anyone's entry into the World to Come would be difficult to answer. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds: *Shnayim ra'iti ve'kol gimel shamati* — I saw two there, meaning himself and Elijah, but I heard a third voice. Rashi identifies this as the *Shechinah*. Rabbi Yehoshua then asked Elijah: *Eimatai asi Mashiach?* — When will Mashiach come? Elijah replied: *Zil shaylei l'didei* — go ask him yourself.

### Gates of Rome

*Ve'heikha yasiv?* — And where is he sitting, that I might find him? Elijah replied: *A'pitcha de'karta* — at the entrance to the city. Our version reads "the city," but the actual *girsa* of the Gemara specifies *Roma* — the gates of Rome. He is sitting there among the poor who are ill, those whose bodies are afflicted with wounds and sores.

### Ready Any Moment

The Gemara elaborates on how Mashiach can be identified among the sick poor. All the others, when they dress their wounds, take off all their bandages at once, treat all their wounds together, and then rebandage everything at once. *Ihu shari chad ve'asir chad* — he alone takes off one bandage at a time, treats it, and rebandages it before moving to the next. Why? *Dilma mive'ina de'lo i'akev* — perhaps I will be needed, and I do not want to be delayed. At any moment the call could come, and he must be ready to respond immediately. He cannot afford to have all his dressings off at once.

### Mashiach Answers

Rabbi Yehoshua went to Rome, found Mashiach, and greeted him: *Shalom alecha rabbi u'mori* — peace to you, my teacher and master. Mashiach replied: *Shalom alecha bar livai* — peace to you, son of Levi. Rabbi Yehoshua asked: *L'eimatai asi mar?* — When will the master come? Mashiach replied: *Hayom* — today.

Rabbi Yehoshua returned to Elijah. Elijah asked what Mashiach had told him. Rabbi Yehoshua reported: he greeted me as *bar livai*, calling me by my father's name — which Elijah explains is itself the answer to Rabbi Yehoshua's earlier question, since addressing him thus and saying *shalom alecha* signifies that both Rabbi Yehoshua and his father will enter the World to Come. But then Rabbi Yehoshua objected: *Shakorei kashkar bi* — he lied to me! *De'amar li hayom atina ve'lo asa* — he said he was coming today and he did not come.

### If You Listen

Elijah replied: *Hachi amar lach* — this is what he was saying to you: *Hayom im bekolo tishmau* — today — if you listen to God's voice. This *midrash* is of extraordinary beauty. Mashiach is always coming today. Every day is *hayom* — today — if only the people would listen to God's voice. Redemption is not postponed to some distant future moment; it is perpetually available, perpetually conditional on the same thing: *im bekolo tishmau*.

### Mashiach Symbol

The imagery of Mashiach sitting at the gates of Rome among the sick poor carries deep symbolic weight. Rome represents the sovereignty that oppresses the Jewish people — whether the literal Roman Empire or, in later generations, Christendom, the Holy Roman Empire, and the forces that keep Israel in subjugation. Mashiach afflicted and bandaging his wounds at the gates of Rome represents the Jewish people themselves: present at the threshold of the oppressive power, not fully absorbed into it but under its dominion, suffering its effects. The teaching about dressing wounds one at a time — never fully exposed, always ready to leap up at a moment's notice — is a model for how to live in exile: attend to your wounds as you must, but always with eyes lifted toward the possibility of instantaneous redemption. Do not become so absorbed in your suffering that you lose the orientation of readiness. *Hayom im bekolo tishmau* — the redemption could come at any moment, not after some extended preparation but today, if the conditions of the heart are met.

The appearance of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's cave in this narrative may carry additional resonance: Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai fled into a cave specifically to escape Roman persecution and to be able to continue performing *mitzvot* in freedom. The interplay of Roman oppression, the cave, and the encounter with Elijah and Mashiach may all be part of a unified symbolic landscape, even if Rashi does not develop it explicitly.

The Ramban, in his *Sha'ar HaGemul* (collected in *Kitvei HaRamban*), assembles many of the *midrashim* from *Chelek* and elsewhere about *Olam HaBa*, *techiyat hameitim*, and *sechar va'onesh*. This very *agadata* featured prominently in the famous Barcelona disputation between Ramban and the apostate Frai Paul. The adversary argued: the Talmud itself says Mashiach was sitting at the gates of Rome — that is Jesus, since Rome had long since become Christian. Ramban responded on multiple levels. First, the text shows plainly that Mashiach had *not* yet come — the whole point of the narrative is that Rabbi Yehoshua was asking *when* he would come, and Mashiach himself said "today if you listen" — meaning he had not come yet. Second, Ramban distinguished three categories of Jewish literature: the Bible, which all Jews accept as fully binding; the Talmud, which is the authoritative commentary on the *mitzvot* of the Torah; and *Midrash*, which is homiletical literature — like sermons that someone wrote down — which one may choose to believe or not. He said: I do not believe in this *agadata* literally, so it is not a proof for your claims. But even if you do believe in it literally, it does not support the Christian claim, because Jesus was not born after the destruction of the Temple, and the chronology does not match. The disputation offers a fascinating window into how our Gemara was read, debated, and deployed in the medieval world.

## Rome's Gate

### When Will Come

The students of Rabbi Yosi ben Kisma asked him: *Eimatai ben David ba?* — When will the Son of David come? He replied: *Mityarei ani shema tevakeshu mimeni ot* — I am afraid that if I tell you, you will ask me for a sign to confirm it. They assured him they would not ask for a sign.

### Gate's Cycles

He told them: *Lechesyipol hasha'ar hazeh veyibaneh veyipol veyibaneh veyipol ve'ein maspikin livnoto ad sheben David ba* — when this gate — the gate of Rome — falls, is rebuilt, falls again and is rebuilt again, and falls a third time before they can complete rebuilding it, then Ben David will come. The imagery extends beyond a single physical gate: Rome will collapse, something will rise in its place and collapse, and when the third collapse occurs faster than it can be rebuilt, the Messianic era arrives.

### Water to Blood

The students then — despite their promise — pressed him for a sign. He responded: *Im kach yehafechu mei me'arat Pamyas ledam* — if so, let the waters of *Pamyas* — which feeds, it seems, into the Jordan River — turn to blood. The evocation of the Egyptian plagues is unmistakable. *Venehefechu ledam* — and they turned to blood.

### Burial Request

As he was dying, Rabbi Yosi ben Kisma made a request of those around him: *Hamiku li aroni* — bury my coffin deep in the earth. He explained why: every palm tree in *Bavel* will have a horse of the Persian armies tied to it, so vast will be the armies of *Gog uMagog* that march through. Such will be the scale of the military forces assembled for the final war before the Messianic era.

### Gog & Magog War

And when those armies advance into *Eretz Yisrael*: *Ve'ein kol aron ve'aron shebe'eretz Yisrael she'ein sus Madai ochel bo teven* — every single coffin in the Land of Israel will be dug up and used as a feeding trough for the Median horses. This is why Rabbi Yosi ben Kisma wanted to be buried deeply: so that even his body would not be exposed to the anguish of that terrible period preceding Mashiach. The narrative marks a transition — from the question of when Mashiach comes to the nature of the *chevlei Mashiach*, the birth pangs of Mashiach, and the war of *Gog uMagog* that will precede it.

## Nine Months

### Evil Rule Period

Rav teaches: *Ein ben David ba ad shetifshot hamalchut haresha'ah al Yisrael tishah chodashim* — the Son of David will not come until the evil kingdom has spread its dominion over Israel for nine months. Some textual versions read *al kol ha'olam kulo* — over the entire world — rather than specifically over Israel. Rashi reconciles the two readings: *hainu kol ha'olam sheYisrael mefuzarim bo* — it means the entire world in which Israel is dispersed. Whether one reads the verse as applying to Israel specifically or the entire world, the point is that there will be a period of comprehensive domination by a single foreign power before Ben David arrives.

### Birth Pangs

The nine-month period is, of course, evocative: it is the duration of a human pregnancy. The Messianic era is being *born*, and the birth is preceded by birth pangs. The prooftext from Micah: *Lachein yitnem ad eit yoledah yaladah* — "Therefore He will give them up until the time when she who travails has given birth" — *veyeter echav yeshuvun al benei Yisrael* — "and the remnant of his brothers shall return to the children of Israel." This verse, on Rashi's reading, is consistent with understanding *al Yisrael* as referring to scattered Israel throughout the world: only after these nine months of gestation and travail will the scattered remnant return.

## Chevlei Mashiach

### Ulla's Wish

Ulla declares: *Yeitei velo eichminei* — let Mashiach come, but let me not see him. That is, let it happen, but not in my days. Ulla does not want to live through the *chevlei Mashiach* — the terrible suffering that precedes and accompanies the Messianic arrival. This is a remarkable statement: one should certainly long for Mashiach, but the desire not to personally witness the birth pangs is understandable.

### Rava & Yosef

Rava says the same: *Yeisei velo echaminei* — let it come but let me not see it. Rabbi Yosef, however, takes the opposite position: *Yeisei ve'ezkeh* — let it come and may I merit — *da'eisiv betulla dechufisa dechamra* — to sit in the shade of the dung of Mashiach's donkey. He is willing to endure any suffering whatsoever, however degrading the circumstances, in order to be alive at the time of Mashiach's coming.

### Yissurin Parallel

Abaye asks Rava: *Mai ta'ama* — why do you not wish to live through the Messianic era? This discussion has a direct parallel in the beginning of *Berachot*, in the discussion of *yissurin shel ahavah* — sufferings of love. In that Gemara, certain rabbis were afflicted and asked whether they cherished their sufferings as a sign of divine love and a vehicle for additional reward. Many responded: *Lo hein velo secharan* — neither them nor their reward. The attitude of Ulla and Rava toward the *chevlei Mashiach* is structurally identical.

### Torah & Kindness

A *Baraita* is cited: the students of Rabbi Eliezer asked him — what can a person do to be protected from the *chevlei Mashiach*? Rabbi Eliezer replied: *Ya'asok baTorah uvigmilus chasadim* — one should engage in Torah study and acts of kindness. If one is immersed in Torah and *gemilus chasadim*, one may be shielded even if the birth pangs occur in one's own lifetime.

### Fear of Sin

Abaye points out to Rava: but you have both Torah and *gemilus chasadim* — so why are you afraid? Rava responds: *Shema yigrom hacheit* — perhaps sin will cause it. Despite one's Torah and acts of kindness, one cannot be certain that some sin will not expose one to the sufferings. One cannot presume that one's merits are intact.

### Jacob's Fear

This is the principle of Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi, who noted a contradiction in the narratives about Jacob. God explicitly promised Jacob: *Hinei anochi imcha ushmarticha bechol asher telech* — "I am with you and will protect you wherever you go." Yet when Jacob returned and confronted the possibility of facing Esau, the Torah says *Vayira Yaakov me'od vayetzer lo* — "And Jacob was very afraid and distressed." Why was Jacob afraid if he had God's explicit promise of protection? *Mistafei shema yigrom hacheit* — he feared that perhaps sin had caused him to forfeit the protection. God's promise of protection is conditional on continuing to merit it. Even a direct divine promise does not constitute an unconditional guarantee independent of ongoing human conduct.

### Lost Miracle

This principle is further demonstrated by a *Baraita* that notes: the verse *Ad ya'avor amcha Hashem* refers to the first entry into *Eretz Yisrael*, while *ad ya'avor am zu kanita* refers to the second. *Emu'ata*, from this point one may derive: *Re'uyim hayu Yisrael la'asot lahem neis biviah shniyah* — the Jewish people should have had miracles performed for them at the time of the second entry into the Land, in the era of the Second Temple, comparable to the miracles of the first entry under Joshua. It should have been a time of full sovereignty, miraculous war, and divine intervention — not a return accomplished under the permission of the Persian king. But *ella shegaram hacheit* — sin caused the miracle not to materialize. Even where miracles were implicitly promised, they were forfeited through sin. Therefore, even Rava, with his Torah and *gemilus chasadim*, cannot be certain that he has not sinned in some way that would expose him to the *chevlei Mashiach*.

## Universal Suffering

### Lion Bear Snake

Rabbi Yochanan says he would prefer not to see Mashiach come in his days. Reish Lakish responds to him: *Mai ta'ama* — why? Is it perhaps because of the verse from Amos: *Ka'asher yanus ish mipnei ha'ari ufegasho hadov* — "As when a man flees from a lion and a bear meets him" — *uva habayit vesamach yado al hakir unishacho nachash* — "and he enters the house, leans his hand on the wall, and a snake bites him." The Messianic period will be one of running from one disaster to the next, with no escape and no safe harbor.

### Modern Examples

Reish Lakish says: do not take this imagery too literally. *Bo ure'ei dugmaso ba'olam hazeh* — come and see its analogue in this very world, in the present era. *Bizman she'adam yotzei lasadeh ufaga bo santeir* — when a person goes out to his field and encounters a *santeir* — a government official who does property assessments and determines field boundaries, levying taxes or altering the lines of one's land — *domeh kemi shepega'o ari* — it is as if a lion has met him. *Nichnas la'ir ufaga bo gabbai* — he enters the city and the tax collector confronts him — *domeh kemi shepega'o dov* — it is as if a bear has met him. *Nichnas leveiso umatza banav uvnotav mutalim bera'av* — he returns home and finds his children and daughters lying hungry and starving — *domeh kemi shenishacho nachash* — it is as if a snake has bitten him. The verse need not be fulfilled through supernatural calamity; it is the ordinary grind of economic oppression, governmental harassment, and domestic destitution. One should not, therefore, be so terrified of the period of Mashiach as if it will bring unprecedented cosmic catastrophe; even now we see its fulfillment in everyday life.

### Birth Pangs Verse

The verse from Isaiah: *Sha'alu na ure'u im yalad zachar* — "Ask now and see: has a male ever given birth?" — *Madua raiti kol gever yadav al chalatzav k'yoleda* — "Why do I see every man with his hands on his loins like a woman in labor?" — *Venehefechu kol panim l'yerakon* — "and all faces have turned to pallor and jaundice?" This describes a time of profound collective affliction. The verse *kol gever* — every man — is read as a reference to the One for whom all power belongs, i.e., God, as explained by Rav Yitzchak citing Rav: even God is in the anguish of these birth pangs. The birth pangs of Mashiach are not only experienced by human beings.

### God's Suffering

The Gemara elaborates: *U'mai venehefechu kol panim l'yerakon?* — what does the turning of all faces to jaundice represent? Rabbi Yochanan explains: *Pamalia shel ma'ala u'pamalia shel mata* — both the heavenly court above and the earthly assembly below. At the time of *Gog uMagog*, everyone suffers — the heavenly hosts and human beings alike. The reason God suffers is given: *Besha'ah she'amar HaKadosh Baruch Hu, halalu ma'aseh yadai v'halalu ma'aseh yadai* — at the time when God says, "These are My handiwork and those are My handiwork." Even if Israel emerges victorious and the nations that oppressed her are defeated, those nations are also God's creatures. The destruction of any human beings — Jewish or non-Jewish — causes God suffering. The Messianic war, even when won, is a time of grief for the Creator.

### Ox & Horse

Rav Papa adds a folk saying to illustrate: *Ratzya v'nafal tora v'azlu v'shadyu l'susa b'urya* — your ox went and fell, and so they put a horse at the feeding trough in its place. Rashi explains the image: you had an ox, you lost it, a horse was installed in its place, and now you have grown attached to the horse. To then replace the horse back with the original ox disrupts a new equilibrium and causes its own pain. In the Divine economy: God had the Jewish people in a position of favor and blessing. The Jewish people sinned and were displaced; the nations of the world were placed in their position of relative divine favor, so to speak, filling the stall. Now, when the time comes to restore the Jewish people and displace the nations, even that reversal — however just — carries cost. God must, as it were, remove creatures who have occupied that role, and that is painful. The Messianic restoration is not a simple, painless rectification; it involves transitions that cause suffering at a cosmic level.

## Hillel's Position

### Mashiach Benefits

Rav Gidel citing Rav: *Atidin Yisrael d'achli shne Mashiach* — Israel will benefit from the Messianic era; they are the ones who will enjoy its prosperity. Rav Yosef responds: *Pshita* — this is obvious. Who else would benefit — *Chivi u'Vili*? Rashi explains that *Chivi u'Vili* are just placeholder names for nobody in particular. Of course it is Israel that will benefit from the Messianic era. Why would this need to be stated?

### Hillel Claim

The answer is: *L'apukei m'de'Rabbi Hillel* — to exclude the position of Rabbi Hillel. For Rabbi Hillel said: *Ein Mashiach l'Yisrael shekvar achluhu b'imei Chizkiyahu* — there is no Mashiach for Israel, because they already consumed him — already received the fulfillment of the Messianic promises — in the days of Hezekiah. This statement, buried deep within a daf saturated with Messianic speculation, is arresting. One Amora holds that all of the prophetic promises of Mashiach were already fulfilled by Chizkiyahu HaMelech, that there is no future Mashiach to come. Rashi confirms: *She'Chizkiya haya Mashiach* — Hezekiah was the Mashiach, and the consolations promised by the prophets were realized in his era. Rav Gidel's statement — that Israel will benefit in the Messianic times — is therefore specifically formulated to reject this position.

### Dogma Questions

This raises profound questions. If one holds that belief in Mashiach is a binding article of faith — that one who denies it is an *apikorus* — what is the status of Rabbi Hillel, who apparently denied it? One might suggest that in his time the question was still open, and it was only subsequently settled that there will indeed be a Mashiach. But this raises a further and deeper question: can one *pasken* — can one render a binding halachic-like ruling — on matters of *emunah*? Is the existence of Mashiach a dogmatic fact that simply is or is not, independent of rabbinic decision? Or can it be the subject of deliberation and determination in the way that ritual questions are? The fact that our Gemara presents it as a disputed matter — where one Amora openly denies a future Mashiach and the response is not a ruling against him but a counter-argument from another sage — is itself significant. As the Rambam formulates this among his thirteen principles, the question of whether issues of *emunah* can be *paskened* the way *halacha* is *paskened* is a genuinely fascinating one, and our daf provides an extraordinary case study for that inquiry.
