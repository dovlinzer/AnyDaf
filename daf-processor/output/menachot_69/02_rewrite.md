# Menachot 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Omer & Shtei HaLechem

### Intro: Roles

The *mishnah* on this *daf* addresses not only the *omer* but the *shtei halechem* and how the two offerings operate in tandem. The *mishnah* states: *ha'omer haya matir bamedina v'Shtei Halechem bamikdash* — the *omer* would render the new grain permissible outside, in the general country beyond the *Mikdash*, while the *shtei halechem* would permit the new grain to be brought within the *Beis Hamikdash*.

### Omer permits all grain

A common misconception is that because the *omer* comes from barley and the *shtei halechem* from wheat, the *omer* permitted the barley and the *shtei halechem* permitted the wheat. This is incorrect. The *omer* permits all grain, as the verse states: *v'lechem v'kali v'karmel lo sochlu ad etzem hayom haze ad havi'achem et korban elokeichem* — bread, parched grain, and fresh grain may not be eaten until that very day, until you bring the offering of your God.

### Shtei HaLechem & Bikkurim

What, then, does the *shtei halechem* accomplish? One might have supposed it serves merely as a *korban* of thanksgiving on *Shavuos*, much as *bikkurim* is a *korban* expressing gratitude to God. However, *Chazal* understand that because it is called *bikkurim*, it must function as the very first *korban* of the new grain brought in the *Mikdash*. Technically, of course, the *omer* preceded it as the first new-grain *korban*, but setting the *omer* aside, the *shtei halechem* serves as the inaugural new-grain offering, and no *korban* may be brought from the new grain in the *Beis Hamikdash* until the *shtei halechem* is offered.

### Mishnah: Kodem l'Omer

The *mishnah* articulates this: *ein mevi'in menachot u'bikkurim u'minchat behemah kodem la'omer* — one may not bring a standard *mincha*, or *bikkurim* themselves, or the *minchat nesachim* that accompanies an animal offering, before the *omer* is brought. *Im hevi, pasul* — if one did bring any of these, it is invalid. The mention of *bikkurim* here is admittedly a little puzzling, since the *omer* does not directly permit *bikkurim* — particularly *bikkurim* consisting of produce other than wheat. We will bracket that difficulty for now.

Regarding the period after the *omer* but before the *shtei halechem*: *kodem l'Shtei Halechem lo yavi* — one should not bring these offerings before the *shtei halechem* is brought, *im hevi, kasher* — but if one did bring them, they are valid.

### Simple explanation

The simplest way to understand the asymmetry between the two cases — and *Tosafos* notes this as well — is straightforward. Before the *omer* is brought, the new grain is *chadash* and therefore *treif*; it is entirely forbidden. There is a principle that *treif* food cannot be acceptable as a *korban*, and therefore any offering brought before the *omer* is invalid. After the *omer*, however, the grain is *kasher*, and the food has been rendered permissible. The sole remaining concern about bringing new grain before the *shtei halechem* is the *halachic* requirement that the *shtei halechem* maintain its status as *bikkurim*. If one went ahead and brought a *korban* from new grain before the *shtei halechem*, he has somewhat compromised that *bikkurim* status — but the *korban* itself remains *kasher*. Violating the *bikkurim* priority is not comparable in severity to offering *treif* food. That is the straightforward reading of the *mishnah*. The *Gemara*, however, proves to have a real difficulty with this understanding.

## Tarfon & Yehuda

### Tarfon's question

The *Gemara* relates: *yativ Rebbi Tarfon v'kakashei lei* — Rabbi Tarfon was sitting and he was troubled by the following difficulty: *ma bein kodem la'omer l'kodem Shtei Halechem?* — what is the difference between before the *omer* and before the *shtei halechem*? Why is it that *b'di'avad*, after the fact, an offering brought before the *omer* is invalid, while one brought after the *omer* but before the *shtei halechem* is valid? Rabbi Tarfon found this troubling and had no ready resolution.

### Yehuda's answer

*Amar lei lefanav Yehuda bar Nechemya* — Yehuda bar Nechemya spoke up before him with an answer. It is not difficult, he said. *Im amarta kodem la'omer sheken hutra m'khlal l'hedyot* — before the *omer*, one can understand why the offering is invalid, because even a lay person, outside the *Mikdash*, cannot eat the grain. It has not yet been rendered permissible even to the *hedyot*. But *tomar kodem l'Shtei Halechem* — would you apply the same reasoning to the period between the *omer* and the *shtei halechem*? There, *shehutra m'khlala l'hedyot* — the grain has already been made permissible to the lay person. And since it has already traversed that threshold, it is understandable that it should be permissible *b'di'avad* in the *Mikdash* as well.

*Shatak Rebbi Tarfon* — Rabbi Tarfon fell silent. He had no response; it seemed a compelling answer.

### Akiva's rebuke

*Tsahu panav shel Rebbi Yehuda ben Nechemya* — Yehuda ben Nechemya's face lit up; he was visibly proud that he had given an answer that silenced the elder Rabbi Tarfon. At that point *Rebbi Akiva* said to him: *Yehuda, tsahu panekha she-heishavta et ha-zaken?* — Yehuda, your face glows because you answered the elder? *Tamihani im ta'arich yamim* — I would be astounded if you live many years. The implication is that one who does not appreciate what it means to stand before an elder, and who feels pride at having silenced him, cannot expect to himself become such an elder.

### Yehuda's death

Rabbi Yehuda berabbi Ilai adds: that period was *prasa d'Pascha*, the two weeks before Pesach when people gather to study the relevant *halachos*, which is why they were engaged in this discussion. When he later went up to Israel at *Shavuos* time, roughly nine weeks afterward, and asked after Yehuda ben Nechemya — *niftar v'halach lo* — he was told that Yehuda had passed away. Rabbi Akiva's words had proved true.

## Deeper concept

### Tarfon's real issue

Setting aside the human dimension of this episode, the pressing *halachic* question is: what exactly was troubling Rabbi Tarfon? The *Tosafos* already raises this: the answer Yehuda ben Nechemya gave seems obvious. Everybody understands that before the *omer* the grain is *treif*, and after the *omer* it is permitted to the *hedyot*. Why was Rabbi Tarfon so perplexed that the answer could silence him? And what is characterized as such a significant moment of insight?

The answer lies in how Rabbi Tarfon understood the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* states that the *omer* is *matir* and the *shtei halechem* is *matir*. Rabbi Tarfon read this language very literally and understood that they are parallel. Just as the grain is forbidden to a normal person before the *omer*, so too is the grain forbidden to the *Beis Hamikdash* before the *shtei halechem* — each is a *matir*, and until its respective *matir* is brought, the item is completely *treif* for its respective domain. This is why the question *vekashei lei* was a genuine and serious difficulty: if the *shtei halechem* has not yet been brought, then the grain is still completely forbidden to the *Mikdash*, and why should it be *kasher b'di'avad*? He was reading the *mishnah* with full seriousness and internal consistency.

### Rashba's models

The best explanation of this *sugya* appears in the work known as *Chiddushei HaRashba* — though it is well established that it is not actually the *Rashba* himself; it may belong to the school of *Tosafos Rid* or someone of similar orientation. This work is singular among the *Rishonim* we possess on Menachot, and the explanation it offers goes to the very core of how the *omer* and *shtei halechem* operate.

### Model One: Parallel

The first model — which is Rabbi Tarfon's position — understands there to be two distinct, parallel prohibitions. One prohibition governs lay people, and a separate prohibition governs the *Mikdash*. The *omer* is *matir* for lay people; the *shtei halechem* is *matir* for the *Mikdash*. Each prohibition operates on its own independent track.

### Model Two: Single

The second model — offered by Yehuda ben Nechemya in response — accepts Rabbi Tarfon's premise that the *shtei halechem* is genuinely *matir* and that there is a real prohibition for the *Mikdash*. But Yehuda ben Nechemya argues that it is not a separate, independent prohibition. Rather, it is a single prohibition whose impact extends from the lay person to the *Mikdash* as one continuous issur. There are not two tracks; there is one prohibition, and it applies both to the *hedyot* and to the *Mikdash*.

The critical consequence of this model is that this single prohibition can be weakened in stages. Before the *omer*, the prohibition is at full strength — the grain is forbidden to the *hedyot* and forbidden to the *Mikdash*. After the *omer*, the prohibition is partially lifted: the *hedyot* may now eat. The prohibition to the *Mikdash* remains in effect pending the *shtei halechem*, but it is no longer at its full original weight — it has been weakened, because the underlying shared prohibition has been partially dissolved. This is what Yehuda ben Nechemya means by his phrase *hutar miklalo etzel hedyot*: the prohibition has gone through a stage of becoming lighter. Because it is no longer as severe, *b'di'avad* the offering is *kasher*.

This is the conceptual depth of the exchange. Yehuda ben Nechemya is not merely making the obvious observation that the grain is now permitted to lay people. He is making the more sophisticated argument that the prohibition to the *Mikdash* is an extension of the prohibition to the *hedyot*, not a parallel structure, and that the partial removal of the underlying prohibition necessarily weakens even the remaining dimension of it.

### Grapes test

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak sharpens the conceptual distinction with the following application. According to Yehuda ben Nechemya's model: *nisuchi bikkurim she-hikrivam kodem la-Omer ksherim* — libation wine brought as *bikkurim* from new grapes, offered even before the *omer*, is valid. The *Gemara* responds: *pshita* — this seems obvious. Wine made from new grapes is simply not grain; the *omer* never had anything to do with grapes, so how could it possibly be invalid?

The *Gemara* then clarifies that this is in fact not obvious at all, given Rabbi Tarfon's model. The *Gemara* poses the concern: perhaps one would have said that the *omer*'s *matir* is a necessary precondition for something to be permissible before the *shtei halechem* — specifically, only something which the *omer* rendered permissible (*hutar miklalo*) can then be brought *b'di'avad* before the *shtei halechem*. If so, grapes, on which the *omer* had no effect whatsoever, might remain forbidden *b'di'avad* even when brought as *bikkurim* wine before the *shtei halechem*. The *Gemara* therefore teaches: *kol she-ken* — all the more so, precisely because the grapes were never subject to any prohibition in this domain at all, they are certainly not a problem. The *omer*'s *matir* is not a prerequisite; it was simply that the grain was never forbidden in the first place.

This exchange perfectly captures the *Rashba*'s reading of the underlying debate. Under Rabbi Tarfon's model — parallel, independent prohibitions — the prohibition of the *Mikdash* before the *shtei halechem* is not tied to the prohibition on the *hedyot*. It applies to all new produce, including new grapes. The wine of new grapes could therefore be subject to a separate parallel prohibition in the *Mikdash* before the *shtei halechem*, even though grapes are not subject to *chadash*. Under Yehuda ben Nechemya's model — a single prohibition extending from the *hedyot* to the *Mikdash* — grapes were simply never included. The prohibition to the *Mikdash* at most mirrors what is forbidden to the lay person, and *chadash* never applied to grapes.

There are thus three distinct conceptual positions on the nature of the prohibition before the *shtei halechem*: (1) an independent parallel prohibition applying to all new produce in the *Mikdash*, requiring the *shtei halechem* as its own *matir* (Rabbi Tarfon); (2) a single prohibition extending from the *hedyot* to the *Mikdash*, but weakened after the *omer* is brought (Yehuda ben Nechemya); and (3) no prohibition to the *Mikdash* at all — only the *bikkurim* concern that nothing new be brought before the *shtei halechem*, which is merely a *lechatchila* requirement (*Tosafos*'s straightforward reading).

## Out of order?

### Rami's question

The *Gemara* now records a question posed by Rami bar Chama: *Shtei HaLechem mahu sheyatiru shelo kesidran?* — can the *shtei halechem* operate in reverse order, that is, with the *shtei halechem* preceding the *omer* rather than following it?

### The case

The case is constructed as follows: *kegon dezarnahu bein Omer leShtei Halechem* — grain was planted between the *omer* and the *shtei halechem*. Because it only took root after the *omer* was brought, the *omer* of that year did not render it permissible. It took root during the *sefirah* period, and when the *shtei halechem* was subsequently brought, this grain was already in the ground and had taken root. The *shtei halechem* thus passed over it while it existed. Then the following year's *omer* was brought.

The result: this grain first encountered the *shtei halechem*, and only then encountered the *omer* — the reverse of the normal sequence. The question is whether this grain may be brought to the *Mikdash*, given that both the *omer* and *shtei halechem* have now related to it, albeit out of order. *Kesidran sharyun shelo kesidran lo sharyun* — do they permit only when occurring in the proper order, or *shelo kesidran nami sharyun* — even when reversed?

This question is well-suited to Yehuda ben Nechemya's model, which conceptualizes the prohibition to the *Mikdash* as being removed in two stages — first partially through the *omer*, then fully through the *shtei halechem*. Viewing the prohibition this way naturally raises the question: must those two stages occur in the prescribed sequence to be effective?

### Rava's proof

Rava offers a resolution: *ta shema* — come and hear from the following *braita*. The *braita* begins from the verse *ve'im takriv minchat bikkurim*, which *Chazal* apply to the *minchat Omer*. The *braita* asks: from where does the *omer* come? *Min hase'orim* — from barley. Now, this is a striking point: the Torah never explicitly states that the *omer* comes from barley. The verse says only *vehavetem et Omer reishit ketzirchem el hakohen* — bring the first of your harvest to the priest — without specifying the grain type.

### Three sources

The *braita* provides three distinct sources for the requirement that the *omer* come from barley.

The first is from Rabbi Eliezer, based on a *gezeirah shavah*: *ne'emar aviv beMitzrayim vene'emar aviv ledorot* — the word *aviv* appears both in the context of Egypt and in the context of the *omer* for all generations. Regarding Egypt, the Torah states *ki hase'orah aviv* — the barley was ripe — which is why it was destroyed by the hail while the wheat survived. Just as *aviv* in Egypt refers to barley, so too does *aviv* as it pertains to the *omer* for all generations refer to barley.

The second source is from Rabbi Akiva: *matzinu yachid shemeivi chovato min hachittin vechovato min hase'orin* — we find that an individual brings his obligatory sacrifice both from wheat and from barley. The obligatory individual *korban* from wheat is the *minchat chotei* (the *chatat* offering of a poor person, brought as flour — which, though it lacks oil and frankincense, does consist of wheat); the obligatory individual *korban* from barley is the *minchat sotah*. Similarly, *ve'tzibur shemevi'im chovatam min hachittin* — the community brings its obligatory sacrifice from wheat in the form of the *shtei halechem*. By parallel reasoning, the community must also have an obligatory sacrifice from barley, and that is the *omer*. If you were to say the *omer* comes from wheat, *lo matzinu tzibur shemeivi chovato min hase'orin* — we would never have a barley sacrifice of the community at all, which would disrupt the symmetry.

The third argument — *davar acher* — is directly relevant to our question: *im atah omer Omer ba min hachittin* — if the *omer* came from wheat, *ein Shtei Halechem bikkurim* — the *shtei halechem* cannot be *bikkurim*, the first of the new grain in the *Beis Hamikdash*. If the *omer* were also from wheat, a wheat offering would already have been brought before the *shtei halechem*, and the *shtei halechem* could not claim the status of *bikkurim*. This is considerably stronger than the first problem the *omer* poses for *bikkurim* status, since at minimum one can argue that the *omer* (being barley) is in a different grain category; if both were wheat, no such bracketing is possible.

### Different crops

The *Gemara* now applies this *braita* to Rami bar Chama's question. The argument runs as follows: if the *shtei halechem* can operate *shelo kesidran* — permitting grain that encountered the *shtei halechem* before the *omer* — then one could in theory bring the *omer* from wheat without violating the *bikkurim* status of the *shtei halechem*. Here is how: one takes grain that took root between the *omer* and the *shtei halechem* of the previous year — grain that first encountered the *shtei halechem* and then the *omer*, in reverse order — and uses it for the *omer* as wheat. For the *shtei halechem* itself, one uses grain that grew in the normal sequence — taking root after the previous year's *shtei halechem* and before the current *omer*. The *shtei halechem* could then claim to be *bikkurim* of the new wheat in the *Mikdash*, since the wheat used for the *omer* would be defined as "old" grain (having already passed through both the *omer* and *shtei halechem*) while the wheat used for the *shtei halechem* would be "new." This argument would explain how a wheat *omer* need not compromise the *bikkurim* status of the *shtei halechem* — and thus the third proof that the *omer* must come from barley would be unnecessary. Since that proof is in fact cited, it must be that we cannot bring the *omer* from wheat through this device, which in turn implies that the *shelo kesidran* mechanism does not work.

### Refutation

The *Gemara* rejects this attempted proof: *mi savrat bikkurim le'pirka amrinan?* — do you think that when we say the *shtei halechem* must be *bikkurim*, we mean merely the first of the new fruit in terms of crop cycle? No, *le'mizbe'ach amrinan* — it means the first new grain to make its way upon the *mizbe'ach*. And on that criterion, *ve'ha achlei le'mizbe'ach mi'peira de'hai shata* — the *mizbe'ach* would still have consumed wheat from this year's crop, since whether or not we define the grain that grew in the middle period as "old," the *mizbe'ach* at that stage still received wheat. Rashi attempts to finesse this difficulty by arguing that the wheat in question should still be called new because of how it relates to last year's *shtei halechem*, but that is essentially the very issue the question was exploring. Accordingly, the *Gemara* concludes that this *braita* cannot serve as a proof one way or the other regarding whether the *omer* and *shtei halechem* work *shelo kesidran*. That question remains open. What the *braita* does yield, however, is an illuminating threefold exploration of why the *omer* must come from barley.

## Stage of growth

### Rami: growth stage

The *Gemara* presents a further question of Rami bar Chama (found on 68b). The question is: *shtei halechem hanatza sharya o chanata sharya?* — what stage must a fruit reach for the *shtei halechem* to be said to have "passed over" it, such that the new fruit cannot be brought to the *Mikdash* before that point? We know that for the *omer*, grain must have taken root (*hashrasha*) before the *omer* is brought in order for the *omer* to render it permissible. What is the analogous stage for the *shtei halechem* in relation to fruit? Is it the *hanatza* — the flowering — or the *chanata* — the budding of the fruit?

### Taking root vs leaves

The *Gemara* immediately clarifies what is meant: *ilima hanatza de'peira ve'chanata de'peira* — if the question concerns the flowering or budding of the actual fruit itself, the answer is straightforward: *hashta hashrasha sharya hanatza ve'chanata mibaya?* — if even taking root is sufficient for grain, surely the far more advanced stage of the fruit flowering or budding is sufficient as well. That cannot be the question.

Rather, says the *Gemara*: *ella hanatza de'ala ve'chanata de'ala* — the question concerns the flowering or budding of the leaves that surround the fruit on the tree. Just as *hashrasha* is the relevant threshold for grain — the point at which the seed has entered a new mode of existence and is considered to "exist" in relation to the *omer* — so too there must be some analogous threshold for tree fruit in relation to the *shtei halechem*. The blossom of the encasing leaves precedes the emergence of the fruit itself, much as cherry blossoms appear before the fruit forms. Does the *shtei halechem* apply when those leaves flower, or when they bud? *Mi havei ke'hashrasha o lo* — is either of these stages analogous to taking root for purposes of the *shtei halechem*?

### Teiku

*Teiku* — the question is left unresolved.

This *sugya* makes explicit what is only implicit in the *mishnah*: the prohibition of bringing new produce before the *shtei halechem* extends beyond wheat and grain. It applies to new fruit as well, because the *shtei halechem* must be *bikkurim*, and no new fruit of any kind may be brought before it. The *Gemara* earlier raised this point in connection with wine made from new grapes. It is worth noting, however, that the general prohibition of *chadash* never applied to fruit — only to grain. The prohibition on new fruit before the *shtei halechem* operates on a distinct basis entirely, which is why the *Gemara* earlier concluded that *b'di'avad* such fruit would be *kasher* — because it bears no relation to the prohibition of *chadash* in any way.

## Planted seeds status

### Rabbah: seeds in ground

The *Gemara* presents a question posed by Rabbah bar bar Channah: *chittin she'zaran bekarka* — if one planted wheat seeds in the ground, *Omer matiran o ein Omer matiran* — does the *omer* render them permissible or not? At first glance this seems a completely absurd question. The *Gemara* responds: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? *Ee ashrush, tanina* — if they have taken root, we already learned the answer in a *mishnah*. *Ee lo ashrush, tanina* — and if they have not taken root, we also learned the answer. The *mishnah* states explicitly: *im hishrishu kodem la'Omer Omer matiran ve'im lav asurin ad she'yavo Omer haba* — if seeds took root before the *omer*, the *omer* renders them permissible; if they took root only after the *omer* was brought, they remain forbidden until the following year's *omer*. Both cases appear to be covered.

The *Gemara* therefore reframes the question: *lo tzericha* — the case that requires resolution is as follows: *de'chatzdinhu ve'zarinhu kodem la'Omer* — you harvested seeds from the previous year's grain and planted them before the *omer*, *ve'ata Omer ve'chalaf laihu* — and then the *omer* came and passed. The seeds have not yet taken root. Can you extract them from the ground and eat them?

The scenario requires careful framing. These seeds came from grain that had not yet been permitted by an *omer*. Had the person left them in his pocket, the *omer* would have arrived, and they would have become permissible — he could have eaten them directly. Instead, he put them in the ground. Now the *omer* has come and gone. The question is how the law treats these planted, unrooted seeds.

### Vessel analogy

*Ke'man de'shadi bekada damya* — are they like seeds sitting in a vessel? If so, the *omer*'s arrival made them permissible just as it would have had they been in a jar. *O dilma, batlu legabei ara* — or have they been absorbed into the ground such that they are no longer treated as food waiting for the *omer*, but rather as planted material on a different legal track — one requiring actual *hashrasha* before the *omer* can relate to them at all?

The ground makes things worse, not better. A jar simply holds the seeds as food; the ground transforms their status. If they are now framed as planted seeds rather than as food, then the rule is that the *omer* only operates on grain that has taken root, and since these have not, they are not yet within the *omer*'s reach. The question is precisely how quickly that transformation occurs upon planting.

### Three questions

This uncertainty ramifies into three subsidiary questions about the legal status of planted but unrooted seeds, each of which maps onto the same underlying axis: are they still independent *metaltelin*, or have they already been subsumed into the *karka*?

### Ona'ah

The first subsidiary question: *yesh lahem onaah o ein lahem onaah?* — do the laws of *onaah* (overcharging) apply to planted seeds or not? The laws of *onaah* do not apply to *karka* — real estate. This is derived from a *klal prat ukhlal* and supported by the logic that real estate is inherently difficult to value with precision, and that any given parcel may be worth far more to a particular buyer than its nominal market rate. By contrast, *onaah* does apply to *metaltelin*.

A preliminary point must be established: we are not discussing a case where a precise quantity was misrepresented. Rava rules that anything sold by an exact measure, weight, or number — *kol davar shebemidah veshebemishkal veshebeminyan* — is subject to reversal even for a discrepancy smaller than the standard *onaah* threshold (normally one-sixth), because when an exact quantity is specified, any deviation from it is simply not what was agreed. This applies regardless of whether the item is *karka* or not: if I sell you a plot of land and tell you it is exactly one acre, and it proves to be less, the defense of "no *onaah* by *karka*" does not apply, because I simply gave you a different quantity than I said.

The relevant case is therefore where no exact number was stated: *ella d'amar leh shadi bat kora hi* — the seller said, "This land has been sown with the appropriate amount of seed," without specifying a number. Witnesses then testify that in fact it was insufficiently seeded. Here, because no precise quantity was named, the standard *onaah* framework applies — and the question becomes whether the seeds are chattel (subject to *onaah*) or already part of the *karka* (not subject to *onaah*). Did I cheat you on the seeds, or did I cheat you on the land? *Keman d'shadi bekadya dami, v'yesh lahen onaah* — if the seeds are like those in a vessel, they are *metaltelin* and subject to *onaah*; *od dilma battalu legabei ara* — or if they are already part of the land, it is the land that was misrepresented, and *onaah* does not apply.

### Shevu'ah

The second question: *nishba'in aleihen o ein nishba'in aleihen?* — can an oath (*shevuah*) be administered regarding planted seeds? Oaths in civil disputes are only applicable to claims involving *metaltelin*; they cannot be administered over disputes about *karka*. If, for example, there is a *modeh bemiktzat* situation — partial admission — regarding these seeds, is the resulting *shevuah* possible? *Keman d'shadi bekadya dami u'kemetalteli damu, v'nishba'in aleihen* — if they are like seeds in a vessel and are treated as chattel, an oath is possible; *od dilma battalu agav ara u'kekarka damu, v'ein nishba'in aleihen* — or if they have been subsumed into the land, no oath can be administered.

### All Teiku

On all three questions — whether the *omer* renders them permissible, whether *onaah* applies, and whether a *shevuah* can be administered — the *Gemara* answers *teiku*. The legal status of seeds planted in the ground before they have taken root remains unresolved across all three dimensions.

## Dung & kernels

### Rami: kernels status

Rami bar Chama next poses a question that, while no longer directly about *chadash* or *omer*, flows from the broader focus on wheat: *chitim shebegilalei bakar use'orin shebegilalei behemah mahu?* — what is the status of wheat kernels found in cattle dung, or barley kernels found in the dung of other animals? Rashi notes that the *Gemara* mentions wheat specifically for cattle, since cattle tend to eat wheat, while other animals eat barley. The question is: what is the *halachic* status of these intact, undigested kernels visible in the dung?

### Extracting matters

The *Gemara* cites a *braita* (apparently from the *Tosefta*): *chitim shebirei bakar use'orin shebegilalei behemah* — wheat kernels in cattle dung and barley kernels in other animals' dung — *chishev aleihen l'achilah, ein mitammei tum'at ochlin* — if one merely intends to eat them, without doing anything, that intention alone does not confer upon them the status of food, and they cannot become *tamei* with *tum'at ochlin*. However, *liktan l'achilah, mitammei tum'at ochlin* — if one actually extracts them from the dung with the intention of eating them, the act of extraction transforms them into food, and they can then become *tamei*.

### Dung-grown grain

The question then shifts from the kernels themselves to grain grown from them. If one extracts these kernels from the dung and plants them, and from them grows new wheat — may that new grain be used for a *mincha*? The *Gemara* immediately cites the verse *hakrivehu na l'pachatecha hayirtzecha o hayisa panecha* — bring it to your governor; would he be pleased? Would he show you favor? The point is obvious: something inherently disreputable is not suitable as a *korban*. One would not bring such a thing to an important person, and certainly not to the Almighty.

### Two theories

This leads to the follow-up question about grain grown from such planted kernels: *lo tzericha d'naktinhu ve'zarinhu* — you extracted these kernels and planted them, and grew new wheat from them. *Mahu l'asuyei Menachos minei* — can you use this new grain for a *mincha*? Two theories are presented. *Mishum d'meisusa hu, ve'cheivan d'zarinhu, azlah lah meisusayih* — perhaps the disqualification was only because of *me'isus*, disgustingness, and once planted and grown anew, the disgustingness is entirely gone. After all, one fertilizes grain with dung regardless; one cannot be overly fastidious about this. The new grain is a completely fresh product with no remaining connection to its origins. Alternatively, *mishum k'chishusa hu, v'hacha nami k'chushen* — perhaps the problem runs deeper: the kernels that passed through an animal, even undigested, may have lost some of their potency or vitality, and the grain grown from them may therefore be of a weakened, substandard quality — making it unsuitable for the *Mikdash* regardless of its appearance. *Teiku* — this too remains unresolved.

## Digestion & status

### Elephant & palm

Rami bar Chama then asks a question that continues the theme of things passing through animals: *pil she'bala kfifah mitzris v'hiki'ah derech beis ha're'i* — an elephant that swallowed an Egyptian basket woven from palm leaves, and it came out the other end. What is the status of this basket?

### Dung vs palm

The *Gemara* clarifies the question: *l'mai halacha?* For what legal purpose? If the question is *l'mivtal tumas'ah* — whether the basket loses its prior *tumah* by having passed through the elephant — the answer is already established. The *mishnah* teaches: *kol hakeilim yordin lidei tumasan b'machshava* — all vessels attain susceptibility to *tumah* through intention (when the craftsman designates the completed object as a vessel). *V'ein olin mi'tumasan ella b'shinui ma'aseh* — but they do not lose their *tumah* except through a physical act of change. Since passing through an elephant presumably does not physically alter the basket, it would remain *tamei*. That question is therefore no question.

The real question is a different one: the elephant swallowed not a finished basket but raw palm leaves (*utzam*) — strips of palm used for weaving — and then excreted them. Someone took these excreted palm leaves and fashioned them into an Egyptian basket. *Mi havei ikul* — are they considered digested, *havei lehu k'chlei glalim* — and must the resulting basket be classified as a vessel made from dung? If so, *ein mikablin tumah* — it cannot become *tamei*, since dung vessels, along with stone and unbaked earthen vessels, do not become *tamei*, neither biblically nor rabbinically (*lo midivrei Torah v'lo midivrei Sofrim*). Or *lo havi ikul* — are they not considered digested, and is the material therefore still legally palm, making the basket susceptible to *tumah* as a normal woven vessel?

### Wolves story

The *Gemara* suggests an answer from a ruling cited by Ulla in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: *ma'aseh u'bau ze'evim shenei tinokot ever ha'Yarden* — there was a tragic case in which wolves swallowed two infants across the Jordan river and subsequently excreted them. The question came before the sages: does the excreted material convey *tumas meis*? *V'tiharu es ha'basar* — the sages ruled that the flesh was *tahor*, no longer considered human flesh. It had changed status through digestion. *V'timu es ha'atzamos* — but the bones were ruled *tamei*, retaining their original status as human bones capable of conveying *tumas meis*.

### Intermediate status

Why can't we derive our answer from this *braita*? The *Gemara* draws the distinction: *shani basar d'rachikh* — flesh is different because it is soft; it is genuinely transformed by digestion, and the sages therefore ruled that it changed status. *Shani atzamos d'kashi tfei* — bones are at the other extreme; they are very hard, and passing through an animal does not alter them at all. Palm leaves fall somewhere in between. They are neither as soft as flesh nor as hard as bone. Perhaps their status changes upon digestion; perhaps it does not. *Teiku* — the question is left unresolved.

### Tosafot's principle

*Tosafos* engages this question extensively, bringing parallel *sugyos* that involve items found inside animals — for example, a kosher fish with non-kosher crustaceans in its stomach (which have not yet been digested, having only been swallowed), and the case of a chicken slaughtered on Pesach with undigested wheat kernels found in its stomach: is that *chametz*? *Tosafos* notes that there are apparent contradictions across different *gemarot* on these questions. *Tosafos* articulates what the *Gemara* leaves implicit in its soft/hard framework: if the form of the object has not changed, one should presume its status remains the same. As long as the item still looks like what it originally was, we should be strict and treat it as retaining its original *halachic* identity.

## Grain from clouds

### Zeira: rain question

The *Gemara* concludes with a question posed by Rabbi Zeira: *chittim she-yardu be-avim* — if wheat fell from the clouds, what is the law? Rashi understands the scenario as one in which a whirlwind or similar phenomenon carried wheat aloft and it then fell back down; *Tosafos* frames it as a straightforward miraculous occurrence, *derech nes*. The question is whether such wheat may be used for the *shtei halechem*.

### Two interpretations

The question turns on the interpretation of the phrase *mi-moshvoseichem* — "from your dwellings" — which conditions the *shtei halechem*'s wheat. One might read this phrase to mean anywhere that is not *chutz la'aretz*: the wheat need not come from outside the Land of Israel, but it does not strictly have to grow from Israeli soil. Under this reading, Israeli clouds would be sufficient. *Aval avim shapir dami* — the clouds are, after all, Israeli clouds; they are not *chutz la'aretz*. Alternatively, *mi-moshvoseichem davka* — perhaps the verse requires that it grow specifically from where you dwell, from the land itself, and *ve-afilu de-avim nami lo* — even clouds do not suffice; the wheat must actually grow from the ground of Israel.

### It can happen

Before leaving the question unresolved, the *Gemara* asks: *u-mi ika ki hai gavna?* — can such a thing actually occur? The answer is yes: *ki de-addi taya nechisa lei rum kizba chitti bi-tlasa parsei* — there was an Arab nomad for whom it rained wheat: it fell to the height of approximately one or two *tefachim* deep, spread across three *parsa'ot* of land. Because this event actually occurred, Rabbi Zeira's question is not merely theoretical — it is a genuine *halachic* inquiry about a known phenomenon.
