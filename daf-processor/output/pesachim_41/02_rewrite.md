# Pesachim 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Flour + Charoset/Mustard

### Mishna Overview

The daf opens at the bottom of 41a with a *mishna* that continues a running series of discussions about *chametz* concerns arising from the mixing of flour or grain with various liquids. The *mishna* states: *ein notnim kemach letoch charoset* — one may not place flour into *charoset*. This *charoset* need not refer specifically to the *Seder* night preparation; as Rashi explains, it refers to a vinegar-based dipping sauce used with meat, into which flour might be added as a thickening agent. The *mishna* extends the same prohibition to mustard — *o letoch chardal* — which in its ancient form was a preparation made from mustard seed, vinegar, and other ingredients, into which flour might similarly be incorporated.

Both cases raise the same underlying question: does the presence of vinegar prevent or accelerate the process of fermentation? The vinegar in question throughout the *Gemara* is wine vinegar — not a wheat product itself — but the question remains whether its acidic character stalls or promotes the leavening of any flour mixed into these preparations.

### Vinegar Role

The concern is not abstract. If vinegar neutralizes fermentation, then mixing flour into these preparations may be essentially harmless from a *chametz* standpoint. If, however, vinegar does not inhibit fermentation — or worse, actively promotes it — then the addition of flour creates an immediate *chametz* problem. The *mishna*'s formulation does not resolve this question directly; it simply prohibits the addition of flour to either preparation and then addresses what happens if one goes ahead and does so anyway.

### Eat Immediately

The *mishna* rules: *v'im natan yochal miyad* — if one did add flour, one should eat the mixture immediately. The assumption is that fermentation will not occur instantaneously, so immediate consumption avoids any *chametz* violation. However, *Rabbi Meir* dissents: *Rabbi Meir oser*. In his view, the mixture poses a *chametz* risk from the very moment the flour is added, and one cannot rely on eating it quickly. According to Rabbi Meir, the mixture must be destroyed immediately.

With that, the *mishna* sets aside this theme momentarily and — in an organizational move that will require comment — pivots to discuss the *korban Pesach* and then returns to a baker's working water. The structural peculiarity of these *mishnayot* is noted by Tosafot and will be addressed below.

---

## Korban Pesach Prep

### Roasting Only

The *mishna* states: *ein mevashelin et hapesach* — one may not cook the *korban Pesach*. The Torah's prohibition (Shemot 12:9) reads: *al tochlu mimenu na uvashel mevushal bamayim* — do not eat from it rare, or cooked in water. The *korban Pesach* must be *tzeli esh*, fire-roasted. The *mishna* extends this to all liquids: *lo bemashkin* — not in any liquids — and *velo bemei peirot* — not even in fruit juices. The prohibition of cooking encompasses every liquid, not only water.

### Basting vs. Cooking

The *mishna* qualifies this prohibition: *aval sachin umatbilin oto bahen* — however, one may baste the *korban Pesach* as it roasts over the fire, applying liquids to the surface for flavor, and one may also dip the meat in liquids when eating it. The prohibition is specifically against cooking it *in* liquid — one may not prepare a *Pesach* stew or boiled *Pesach*. The question of pan-roasting will be addressed when the *Gemara* explores the precise contours of the prohibition.

### Baker's Water

The *mishna* then transitions abruptly to yet another *chametz* topic: *mei tashmisho shel nachtom* — the working water of the professional baker. A baker kneading dough keeps a bucket of water nearby to rinse his hands between batches. That water accumulates residual dough and, as it sits, begins to ferment. The *mishna* rules that this water must be poured out — *yishfechu* — because *mipnei shehein machmitzin*, it causes *chametz*.

The organizational structure here is, as the lecturer observes, genuinely puzzling, and Tosafot explicitly raises the question. One moment the *mishna* deals with flour in *charoset* and mustard; then it pivots to the laws of the *korban Pesach*; then it returns to a *chametz* concern involving a baker's water. A partial answer may be that the unifying thread is the interaction of flour or grain with liquids — the *charoset* and mustard cases involve flour with liquid, the *korban Pesach* case involves the prohibition of cooking in liquid, and the baker's water involves dough residue in liquid — but even granting this, the insertion of the *korban Pesach* material into what is otherwise a sequence of *chametz* discussions remains structurally jarring.

## Flour + Charoset/Musta…

### Rav Kahana's View

The *Gemara* now returns to the *mishna*'s ruling about flour in *charoset* and mustard, beginning with a distinction drawn by Rav Kahana: *machlokes letoch hachardal* — the debate between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* about whether one can eat the mixture immediately or must destroy it applies specifically to the mustard case. *Aval letoch hacharoset, divrei hakol yisaref miyad* — but if flour was mixed into the vinegar-based *charoset* dip, everyone agrees it must be burned immediately. Rashi explains that mustard, being more pungent, may retard fermentation sufficiently to permit immediate consumption according to the *Chachamim*, whereas the *charoset* dip does not have this property, and all opinions agree it must be destroyed.

This position is corroborated by a *braita*: *ein notnin kemach letoch hacharoset, v'im natan yisaref miyad* — you may not add flour to *charoset*, and if you did, it must be burned immediately. By contrast, in the mustard case, Rabbi Meir rules it must be burned while the *Chachamim* permit immediate consumption.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehuda then transmits a ruling in the name of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *halacha k'chachamim* — the ruling follows the *Chachamim* that immediate consumption is permitted. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then challenges Rav Huna: when you say we rule like the *Chachamim*, does that apply even to the *charoset* case, or only to the mustard case as Rav Kahana maintained? Rav Huna responds: *lemay nafka mina* — in the sense of asking what practical difference the distinction would make, and implicitly expressing skepticism about Rav Kahana's differentiation. He concludes: *lo shamia li* — I have not heard that teaching.

As the *Gemara* notes in a pointed phrase, *lo shamia li* here means *lo sevira lei* — "I haven't heard it" is idiomatic for "I don't accept it." Rav Huna thus rules that even in the *charoset* case, the *Chachamim* would permit immediate consumption, and the *halacha* follows the *Chachamim*.

### Vinegar Neutral

Rav Ashi, however, sides with Rav Kahana's distinction: *kevastei de-Rav Kahana mistavra* — Rav Kahana's differentiation makes logical sense. His proof is from a ruling of Shmuel himself: *ein halacha ke-Rabbi Yosi* — we do not rule like Rabbi Yosi. Rabbi Yosi had argued on an earlier daf that if grain soaking in water begins to crack and ferment, one can immerse it in vinegar to halt the process, since vinegar prevents fermentation. Shmuel rejected this view. The question then is: what precisely does Shmuel's rejection mean? Does it mean (*mai lav*) that vinegar does not merely fail to prevent fermentation, but actually *accelerates* it — *hachmei machme* — because it functions as a liquid that promotes *chametz* formation? If so, the vinegar in the *charoset* would make it ferment faster than mustard, and Rav Kahana's distinction would be well-supported.

The *Gemara* rebuts this inference: *dilma lo matzmit tzamit velo hachmei machme* — perhaps vinegar neither retards fermentation nor hastens it. When Shmuel says we do not rule like Rabbi Yosi, he may simply mean that vinegar provides no protective benefit — it does not shrivel or arrest the fermentation process — but this does not imply that vinegar actively causes fermentation faster than other substances. If so, the presence of vinegar in the *charoset* does not make it more problematic than mustard, and Rav Kahana's distinction loses its logical grounding.

The *Gemara* thus leaves the resolution incomplete: the argument that *charoset* is categorically more problematic than mustard is not clearly established, because rejecting Rabbi Yosi's view does not necessarily entail that vinegar accelerates fermentation.

## Other Liquids

### Kal Vachomer

The *Gemara* now turns to the broader question of whether liquids other than water are included in the biblical prohibition against cooking the *korban Pesach*. The verse (Shemot 12:9) specifies *uvashel mevushal bamayim* — cooked in water. A *braita* asks: *ein li ela bamayim, she'ar mashkin minayin?* — I know only that water is prohibited; how do I derive the prohibition of other liquids?

The answer offered is a *kal vachomer*: water, which does not mask the taste of the meat — it does not introduce a competing flavor — is nevertheless prohibited. Other liquids, such as fruit juices or wine, which do mask and overlay the meat's taste, should certainly be prohibited. The *kal vachomer* thus draws the following inference: if even the more neutral substance is prohibited, the more transformative substances are certainly included.

This application of a *kal vachomer* raises a technical concern, since we generally hold *ein mazhirin min hadin* — one does not derive prohibitions from logical inference in a way that would generate lashes for the derived case. Additionally, making a *kal vachomer* argument presupposes that we understand the *reason* the Torah prohibited cooking in water — namely, that it affects the meat's taste and character. If there were some other, unarticulated rationale for the prohibition, the *kal vachomer* to other liquids would not hold. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* uses the *kal vachomer* as a way of understanding that the word *bamayim* in the *pasuk* is not meant precisely — it functions as a paradigmatic example, with the *kal vachomer* establishing that the prohibition encompasses any liquid.

### Rabbi's Drasha

Rabbi (Rebbi, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) derives the same extension through a different exegetical route. Rather than a *kal vachomer*, he reads the double language of the verse — *uvashel mevushal* — as doing independent exegetical work. The doubling of the root expands the prohibition beyond what a single usage would indicate, encompassing cooking in any substance, not only in water.

### Pan-Roasting

The *Gemara* identifies the *nafka mina* between the two approaches: *tzeli kedeirah* — pan-roasting. Pan-roasting involves placing the meat in a pan directly over fire without adding any external liquid; the meat's own juices accumulate in the pan as heat is applied, and the meat essentially cooks in those internal juices. There is no external liquid introduced, only the meat's own exudate.

According to the *kal vachomer*, pan-roasting would not be included in the prohibition, since the *kal vachomer* only addresses external liquids — one derives from water that other external substances are likewise prohibited. The absence of any external liquid means the *kal vachomer* generates no prohibition for pan-roasting. By contrast, Rabbi's reading of *uvashel mevushal* as an expansive double language would include pan-roasting as a form of cooking, even in one's own juices.

Tosafot is careful to note, however, that this does not mean pan-roasting would be *permitted* according to the view that uses the *kal vachomer*. Even without a negative prohibition derived from *uvashel mevushal*, the end of the verse — *ki im tzeli esh* — requires fire-roasting. Pan-roasting does not qualify as *tzeli esh*, and therefore it remains impermissible regardless of whether it falls within the *bishul* prohibition. The practical difference between the two approaches is therefore not about permissibility but about whether pan-roasting violates a *lav* specifically.

The Rambam, it is worth noting, rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's position, such that pan-roasting would constitute a violation of a negative commandment.

## Before/After Roasting

### Cook Then Roast

Having established that the double language of *uvashel mevushal* carries additional exegetical weight beyond extending the prohibition to other liquids, the *Gemara* now identifies what the *Rabbanan* — who derive other liquids from the *kal vachomer* rather than from the double language — do with that double language. The answer is found in a *braita*: *bishlo v'achar kach tzla'o* — if one first cooked the *korban Pesach* and then roasted it, one transgresses. Similarly, *o shetzla'o v'achar kach bishlo* — if one first roasted it and then cooked it, one also transgresses. The double language of *uvashel mevushal* conveys that even a partial or secondary act of *bishul* is sufficient to render the *korban* transgressed — it need not be exclusively cooked; even if it was also roasted at some point, the cooking violates the prohibition.

The case of cooking first and then roasting is straightforward: *deha bashlei bashlei* — the initial cooking defines the meat's identity, and the subsequent roasting does not undo that status. The identity as a cooked item was established at the outset.

### Roast Then Cook

The reverse case — roasting first and then cooking — is more analytically challenging. *Ha tzeli esh hu* — might one not say that since the meat was originally fire-roasted, that original act stamps its identity as *tzeli esh*, and the subsequent cooking is merely incidental? Perhaps it should retain the status of *tzeli* even after having been cooked?

### Rabbi Yosi Matzah

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking the position of Rabbi Yosi, via Rav Kahana: this case follows Rabbi Yosi's ruling in an analogous context involving *matzah*. The *braita* records a dispute about whether one can fulfill the obligation of *matzah* with a baked wafer that was subsequently cooked. Rabbi Meir holds that one may fulfill the obligation with such a *matzah* — *uvimvushal* — provided it has not dissolved, since it was originally baked. Rabbi Yosi disagrees: while he agrees that a baked wafer that has been soaked in water (*rakik hashurui*) remains valid — because merely soaking it does not constitute a second act of cooking — he maintains that a baked *matzah* that has been subsequently cooked does not fulfill the obligation, even if it has not dissolved: *aval lo b'mevushal, af al pi shelo nimoach*.

The *Gemara* understands Rabbi Yosi's reasoning not as resting on the requirement that *matzah* be *lechem oni* (poor person's bread), since that rationale would be specific to *matzah* and irrelevant to the *korban Pesach*. Rather, the *Gemara* reads Rabbi Yosi as articulating a formal principle: when a second process is applied to an object, that second process attaches a new label to it. The baked *matzah*, once cooked, is no longer simply "baked bread" — it has now also become "cooked," and that new designation strips it of its exclusive *lechem* identity. Analogously, the *korban Pesach* that was roasted and then cooked acquires the label of *mevushal* through the secondary act of cooking, even though it was originally *tzeli*.

This discussion has significant implications beyond *korban Pesach*. The *Yeraim* (Rabbi Eliezer of Metz, a student of Rabbeinu Tam) cites this *Gemara* as proof for a principle in *hilchot Shabbat*: while *ein bishul achar bishul* — one cannot "cook" something already cooked, so reheating cooked food on *Shabbat* raises no *bishul* concern — the same is not true for *bishul achar afiyah* (cooking after baking). Taking baked bread and placing it into boiling liquid on *Shabbat* is a change of process category, not a repeat of the same process, and the *Yeraim* argues this constitutes a genuine act of cooking. His proof is precisely from this *Gemara*, where Rabbi Yosi and the *korban Pesach* discussion demonstrate that a secondary process of a *different* type changes the item's halakhic status. The *Shulchan Aruch* is stringent in accordance with the *Yeraim*'s position.

### Apply to Pesach

Ulla adds a further dimension: *afilu teima Rabbi Meir* — even according to Rabbi Meir, who rules that cooking after baking does not disqualify *matzah*, one could maintain that cooking after roasting *does* disqualify the *korban Pesach*. Why? *Shani hatam, de'amar kra uvashel mevushal mikol makom* — the *korban Pesach* is different because the Torah's double language of *uvashel mevushal* explicitly teaches that even a secondary act of *bishul* is enough to create a violation, regardless of what other processes were applied. The double language thus functions as a specific textual override for the *korban Pesach* context, ensuring that even those who would not extend Rabbi Yosi's principle globally must accept it here.

## Extremes

### Charred

Having established the parameters of the prohibitions governing the *korban Pesach*, the *Gemara* explores two extreme cases that lie outside those categories. The first: *yachol tzala'o kol tzorko yehei chayav* — one might think that if the *korban* was roasted completely — meaning charred beyond recognition — one would still transgress. The *Gemara* derives from the verse (*al tochlu mimenu na uvashel mevushal bamayim*) that only *na* and *mevushal* are included in the prohibition, but not *tzala'o kol tzorko* — completely roasted through. Rav Ashi explains: *de-shavei charucha* — the case refers to one who turned the meat into something resembling a piece of charcoal. Such an extreme state of overcooking is not included in the prohibitions of *na* or *mevushal*. One should note that this does not mean charring the *korban Pesach* is otherwise acceptable — presumably one has not fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *korban Pesach* by eating charcoal, and there may be other concerns — but the specific transgression of *al tochlu mimenu na* does not apply to it.

### Completely Raw

The second extreme: *yachol achal kezayit chai yehei chayav* — perhaps eating from the *korban* when it is completely raw, without any application of heat whatsoever, would constitute a transgression. The *Gemara* again derives from *na uvashel mevushal* that *na* and *mevushal* are included, but *lo chai* — not completely raw. The prohibition of *na* refers to undercooked or rare meat, as the *Gemara* will shortly clarify, not to meat that has never touched heat at all.

The irony is notable: one who eats a completely raw *korban Pesach* or one that has been charred to ash has not fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *korban Pesach* — the requirement of *tzeli esh* has obviously not been met — yet these extreme cases fall outside the specific biblical prohibitions enumerated in the verse. They are governed, as the *Gemara* goes on to explain, by the second half of the *pasuk*: *ki im tzeli esh*.

## Na and Tzeli Esh

### Definition Na

The *Gemara* now addresses a terminological question: *heichi dami na?* — what exactly is *na* as used in the *pasuk*? Rav explains that it corresponds to the Persian word *avarnim*, which denotes meat that has been subjected to heat but remains substantially undercooked — what we might today describe as rare or very underdone. It is not completely raw, but it has not been adequately roasted either. This is the specific state prohibited by *al tochlu mimenu na*.

### Hot Springs

Rav Chisda raises a distinctive case: what if one cooked the *korban Pesach* in the hot springs of Tiberias (*chamei Tveryah*), which are naturally heated by underground geothermal forces rather than by fire? In the context of *Shabbat*, cooking in the hot springs of Tiberias is exempt from the *bishul* prohibition — *beShabbat patur* — because *toldot esh ba'inan velieka*, one needs fire or its derivative (*toldot esh*), and geothermally heated water does not qualify. The *Shabbat* prohibition of cooking requires that the heat source trace back to fire. The hot springs draw their heat from the earth, not from a fire that was kindled, and therefore they do not constitute *toldot esh*.

One might expect the same definition to apply to *korban Pesach*: since geothermal heating is not *bishul* by *Shabbat* standards, perhaps cooking the *korban* in *chamei Tveryah* would similarly not fall under *uvashel mevushal*. Yet Rav Chisda rules: *pesach shebishlo bechamei Tveryah, chayav* — if one cooked the *korban Pesach* in the hot springs, one has transgressed.

The *Gemara* could have resolved this by arguing that for *korban Pesach*, what matters is the result — whether the product is functionally "cooked" — rather than the precise character of the process, as in *Shabbat*. But the *Gemara* does not take this route. Instead, Rava explains: *mai chayav, d'ka avar mishum tzlui* — what does "transgressed" mean here? Not that one has violated *uvashel mevushal*, since *bishul* for *korban Pesach* purposes, like for *Shabbat*, requires fire-based heating. Rather, the transgression is that the meat is *not tzeli esh*: it was subjected to a liquid environment in the hot springs without fire, and therefore it fails to satisfy *ki im tzeli esh*. The violation is the failure of the positive requirement of fire-roasting, which the *Gemara* reads as also carrying a negative dimension.

Rav Chiya the son of Rav Nachman confirms that this is the explicit teaching: the transgression in the *chamei Tveryah* case is *mishum tzlui* — it pertains to the *ki im tzeli esh* half of the *pasuk*, not to *uvashel mevushal*.

### Two Halves

This leads to a conceptual clarification about the structure of the *pasuk*. The *pasuk* (Shemot 12:9) has two halves: the first, *al tochlu mimenu na uvashel mevushal bamayim*, prohibits eating it rare or cooked in water (extended to all liquids); the second, *ki im tzeli esh*, commands that it be fire-roasted. The *Gemara* has now demonstrated that several categories of preparation fall outside the first half: completely raw meat (*chai*), charred meat (*charucha*), and — according to Rava's reading — meat cooked in *chamei Tveryah*. Yet all of these still fail to satisfy *ki im tzeli esh*. The question, then, is whether *ki im tzeli esh* functions as a positive commandment (*aseh*) or whether it carries the force of a negative prohibition (*lav*) as well — a question to which the *Gemara* now turns.

## Lashes Debate

### Rava Position

Rava articulates the view that *ki im tzeli esh* generates actual negative prohibitions that carry lashes. If one ate from the *korban* rare (*na*): *lokeh shtayim* — two sets of lashes. One set is for violating *al tochlu mimenu na*, and the second is for violating the implicit prohibition of *ki im tzeli esh* — since it was not roasted, one has violated the negative dimension of that clause. If one ate from it cooked (*mevushal*): again *lokeh shtayim*, one for *uvashel mevushal* and one for *ki im tzeli esh*. If one ate it both rare and cooked: *lokeh shalosh*, three sets of lashes — one for *na*, one for *mevushal*, and one for *ki im tzeli esh*.

### Abaye Position

Abaye disagrees: *ein lokin al lav shebichlalus* — one does not receive lashes for a *lav shebichlalus*, an umbrella prohibition. The phrase *ki im tzeli esh* does not specify a particular act to avoid; it is a catch-all that encompasses anything other than roasting. Such a generalized prohibition that does not identify a specific, narrowly defined act does not generate lashes. The prohibition of muzzling an ox (*lo sachsom shor b'disho*) serves as the paradigm for the kind of specific, action-defined *lav* that generates lashes, and *ki im tzeli esh* does not share that character.

### Abaye Dispute

There are two versions of Abaye's position. According to the first, Abaye says that when the *lav shebichlalus* appears together with a specific prohibition — for instance, one ate rare *and* it was not roasted — *tarti hu d'lo laki*, one does not receive two sets of lashes because the generic prohibition is subsumed under the specific one. But *chada meiha laki*, one still receives one set for the specific violation. According to the second version, Abaye goes further: *chada nami lo laki* — even one set of lashes for the generic prohibition is unavailable, since the prohibition is not sufficiently defined, like *lav d'chasimah*.

### Rashi Solution

Rashi offers a careful reading to reconcile these two versions within Abaye's position. Abaye, Rashi explains, would of course agree that one receives lashes for eating *mevushal* — the prohibition of *uvashel mevushal bamayim* is a perfectly specific *lav*. The question is only about the umbrella of *ki im tzeli esh*. When a specific *lav* is already operative (eating cooked or rare), adding lashes for the generic *ki im tzeli esh* on top of it is redundant and improper — one does not punish for the title of a category when one is already punishing for the specific act within that category. But what about a case where no specific *lav* applies — for example, one ate the *korban* completely raw (*chai*), which falls outside *na* and *mevushal*? In that case, might the generic *ki im tzeli esh* operate on its own to generate lashes? The first version of Abaye says yes; the second version says no, since even when it stands alone, an umbrella prohibition lacking the specificity of the *chasimah* paradigm never generates lashes.

### Nazir Example

The same dispute is played out in a parallel context involving a *nazir*. Rava holds that if a *nazir* ate the pit of a grape (*zag*): *lokeh shtayim* — two sets of lashes, one for the specific prohibition of *mechartzanim v'ad zag* (from the skin to the pit), and one for the general prohibition of *mikol asher yei'aseh migefen hayayin* (anything made from the grapevine). Similarly, eating the skin (*chartzan*) yields two lashes. Eating both yields *lokeh shalosh*, three sets. Abaye, consistent with his position, rules that the generic umbrella of "anything from the vine" does not generate independent lashes alongside the specific prohibitions. Whether the umbrella prohibition can ever stand alone to generate a single set of lashes — when one consumes some other grape derivative that does not fall under a specific prohibition — is subject to the same two versions as in the *korban Pesach* discussion.

## Timing Issue

### When Cooked

The *Gemara* now addresses a question about the timing of the transgression: at what point does the prohibition of eating the *korban Pesach* in an improper manner attach? One dimension of this question concerns when the *korban* was *cooked* — must the improper preparation have occurred after nightfall, or does it suffice that the cooking occurred on *Erev Pesach*? A *braita* teaches: if one cooked the *korban Pesach* on *Erev Pesach* during the day and then ate it on the night of the 15th, does one transgress? One might think the prohibition only applies if the cooking itself occurred at night, when the *mitzvah* of the *korban Pesach* takes effect. Rabbi derives from the double language of *uvashel mevushal* — *bashel mevushal mikol makom* — that the prohibition applies regardless of when the cooking was done. Whether the *korban* was cooked earlier in the day or at night, if one eats it improperly prepared, one has transgressed.

The *Gemara* acknowledges an objection: we already explained on 41a that Rebbi uses the double language of *uvashel mevushal* to derive the prohibitions against other liquids and pan-roasting. How can he simultaneously use it to derive this timing ruling? The *Gemara* responds with the standard answer that the double language, being genuinely redundant from a grammatical standpoint (*im kein leima kra o bashel bashel o mevushal mevushal*), can support multiple *drashos* simultaneously.

### When Eaten

The more fundamental timing question concerns when the *eating* must occur for the prohibition to apply. A *braita* teaches: *achalo kezayit na be-od yom, patur* — if one ate a *kezayit* of rare *korban Pesach* during the day of the 14th, before nightfall, one is exempt. *Kezayit na mishechasheicha chayav* — but if one ate it rare after nightfall, one has transgressed. The prohibition of eating it improperly applies only from the time the *mitzvah* of the *korban Pesach* takes effect — at nightfall on the 15th.

A related ruling distinguishes between eating improperly prepared meat before nightfall and eating properly roasted meat before nightfall. The *braita* teaches: *achalo kezayit tzali be-od yom, lo pasal atzmo mibenei chavurah* — if one ate a *kezayit* of properly roasted *korban Pesach* during the day of the 14th, one does not disqualify oneself from membership in the *chavurah* (the group with whom one is obligated to eat the *korban* at night). Eating the *korban* before nightfall lacks the halakhic significance of eating it at the designated time; it does not count toward the *mitzvah* and does not define one's *chavurah* affiliation. By contrast, once nightfall arrives and one has consumed a *kezayit* of roasted *korban* — *kezayit tzali mishechasheicha, pasal atzmo mibenei chavurah* — one has fulfilled the designated eating and may not then transfer to another group.

### Pasuk Contrast

Having established that the prohibitions only apply at nightfall, the *Gemara* considers a strikingly counterintuitive argument that the *braita* raises and then refutes. The *braita* entertains the possibility that Erev Pesach — when one is not yet permitted to eat the *korban* roasted — might be the time of greater stringency: since even roasting is forbidden during the day, perhaps eating it rare is *also* forbidden during that period. The argument goes further: one might even claim that the prohibition of eating rare applies *only* on Erev Pesach, and paradoxically that on the night of the 15th, when one is commanded to eat it roasted, one would not transgress if one ate it in a prohibited manner. This is admittedly a startling conclusion, and the *Gemara* acknowledges it: *al titema* — do not be amazed, since one could argue that when the *mitzvah* of eating roasted is permitted and indeed commanded, perhaps those prohibitions become inoperative.

The *Gemara* resolves this by appealing to the structure of the *pasuk* itself: *al tochlu mimenu na uvashel mevushal bamayim ki im tzeli esh*. Why does the Torah conclude by contrasting these prohibitions with the command to eat it roasted? This juxtaposition teaches: *besha'ah sheyeshno bekum echol tzli yeshno beval tochal na* — at the time when one is obligated to eat it roasted, that is precisely when the prohibition of eating it rare applies. *Besha'ah she'eino bekum echol tzli, eino beval tochal na* — when one is not yet obligated to eat it roasted, the prohibition of eating it rare does not yet apply. The *pasuk*'s contrast between the prohibited modes and the commanded mode of roasting is not a contradiction but a synchronization: the prohibitions and the positive obligation are coextensive in time, all taking effect at nightfall on the 15th.

## Early Consumption

### Eat Day Before

Returning to the case of eating roasted *korban Pesach* on the day before the 15th, the *Gemara* confirms: *achalo tzli mib'od yom chayav* — one who eats the *korban* roasted during the day has transgressed something. The question is what. The *braita* further notes that *kezayit nami mishechasheicha chayav* — eating it rare at nightfall also constitutes a transgression. The *Gemara* observes that the two cases are presented in parallel: *katani tzli dumya de-na* — the case of eating roasted prematurely is formulated analogously to the case of eating rare. This suggests that just as eating rare is a *lav* — a negative prohibition — so too eating roasted prematurely should be a *lav*. *Ma na belav, af tzli belav* — just as rare carries a *lav*, so should premature eating of the roasted *korban*.

### Negative from Positive

But where is this *lav* derived? For the case of *na*, the verse explicitly states *al tochlu mimenu na* — a clear negative prohibition. For premature consumption of roasted *korban*, there is no explicit *lav* in the text. The verse that establishes the timing of the *mitzvah* is *v'achlu et habasar balaila hazeh* — they shall eat the meat on this night — which is a positive commandment. From the positive command to eat at night, one infers *balaila in beyom lo* — at night yes, during the day no. But this inference is derived from an *aseh*, a positive commandment.

### Lav M'Klal Aseh

This is the principle of *lav haba mikhlal aseh*: a negative prohibition inferred from the implication of a positive commandment. The *Gemara* raises the question: *v'chol lav haba mikhlal aseh, aseh* — any "do not" that is derived from "do," is treated halakhically as a positive commandment, not as a negative prohibition. The practical consequence is significant: a *lav* that is inferred from an *aseh* carries the force of an *aseh*, which does not generate lashes.

If so, eating roasted *korban Pesach* before nightfall would not generate lashes — one has violated the *aseh* of *v'achlu et habasar balaila hazeh* by consuming it prematurely, but not a *lav* in the full sense. Yet the *braita* presents the case as parallel to *na*, which does carry a *lav*. How then are they truly analogous?

Rav Chisda resolves this: *ha mani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this ruling follows Rabbi Yehuda, who maintains that a *lav haba mikhlal aseh* is treated as a genuine *lav*, not merely as an *aseh*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, when a negative is inferred from the implication of a positive command, it retains the full force of a negative prohibition. The details of Rabbi Yehuda's position and its textual grounding will be the subject of the next day's learning.
