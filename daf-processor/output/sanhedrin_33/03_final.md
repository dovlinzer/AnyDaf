# Sanhedrin 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dinei Nefashos vs Mamonos

### Opening & Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת פּוֹתְחִין כּוּ׳
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that in cases of **monetary law,** the court **opens** the deliberations either with a claim to exempt the accused, or with a claim to find him liable.

Sanhedrin 33 opens in the midst of the discussion distinguishing *dinei nefashos* from *dinei mamonos*. The previous daf had already introduced several such distinctions, and we now continue with two further differences: how a case is opened, and whether a ruling may be reversed.

### Opening Procedure

In *dinei mamonos*, a case may be opened *bein lizchus bein lichova* — whether to find the party exempt or obligated. In *dinei nefashos*, the case must be opened *lizchus* — first arguing in the defendant's favor, seeking reasons for acquittal, as was discussed on the preceding daf.

### Reversibility Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת מַחֲזִירִין כּוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהוּ: דָּן אֶת הַדִּין, זִיכָּה אֶת הַחַיָּיב, חִיֵּיב אֶת הַזַּכַּאי, טִימֵּא אֶת הַטָּהוֹר, טִיהֵר אֶת הַטָּמֵא – מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי, וִישַׁלֵּם מִבֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that in cases of **monetary law,** the court **brings** the accused **back** to be judged again if new evidence arises, whether with a claim to exempt him or with a claim to find him liable. In cases of capital law, the court brings the accused back to be judged again with a claim to acquit him, but does not bring him back to be judged with a claim to find him liable. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Bekhorot* 28b): If a judge **issued a judgment** and erred, so that he **exempts a liable** party or **finds an innocent** party **liable,** or if he **ruled** that **a pure** item is **impure** or **ruled** that **an impure** item is **pure,** and by doing so he caused a litigant a monetary loss; **what he did is done,** i.e., the judgment stands, **and** the judge **must pay** damages **from his home,** i.e., from his personal funds. This indicates that the court does not bring the accused back to be judged again if new evidence arises.

The *mishna* then addresses the question of reversibility. In *dinei mamonos*, the court may reverse its ruling in either direction — whether to find someone liable who had been exempted, or to find someone exempt who had been found liable. In capital cases, the asymmetry is striking: if a defendant was found innocent, the ruling cannot be reversed — there is no double jeopardy. However, if a defendant was found guilty, the ruling can be reversed in his favor, functioning as a kind of appeal for a guilty verdict.

The *gemara* notes an apparent contradiction. Our *mishna* states that civil rulings may be reversed, yet an earlier source — quoted already in the first *perek* — states: *dan es hadin, zika es hachayav, chiyav es hazakai, tima es hatahor, tahar es hatamei* — if a judge rendered a ruling in error, whether finding the liable party exempt or the exempt party liable, or declaring something pure that was impure or impure that was pure, then *ma she'asa asa* — what is done is done — and the judge must pay damages from his own resources.

Several important issues converge here. One is the question of tortious liability: is the judge's error a form of *mazik biyadayim*, or is it rather an indirect cause, a *grama*, perhaps falling under the category of *hezek de'garmi* as discussed in Bava Kamma? Another is the question of who determines that a mistake was made at all. But the issue most immediately relevant to our passage is the principle of *ma she'asa asa* — that the ruling is not reversible — which stands in direct tension with our *mishna*'s statement that civil rulings may indeed be reversed.

## Mumche Framework

### Mumche Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּמוּמְחֶה, כָּאן בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּמְחֶה. וּבְמוּמְחֶה מַחְזִירִין? וְהָקָתָנֵי: אִם הָיָה מוּמְחֶה לְבֵית דִּין – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says:** This is **not difficult.** The mishna **here,** which teaches that the court does bring the accused back to be judged again if new evidence arises, is stated **with regard to** a judge who was **an expert,** whereas the mishna **there** in tractate *Bekhorot*, which teaches that what he did is done, is stated **with regard to** a judge **who was not an expert.** The Gemara asks: **And** does **one bring** the accused **back in** the case of a judge who was **an expert? But** that mishna in tractate *Bekhorot* **teaches: If he was an expert** accepted **by** the **court** he is **exempt from** liability **to pay.** This indicates that the court does not bring the accused back to be judged again if new evidence arises, even if the judge is an expert; otherwise there would be no need to state that the judge is exempt from liability to pay.

Rav Yosef resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — kan bemumcheh, kan beshe'eino mumcheh*. The difference turns on whether the judge is an expert, a *mumche*, or not. We will follow Rashi's reading and interpretation of this sugya.

### Rashi's Logic

Rashi's explanation throughout this passage rests on a structural observation about the nature of judicial reversal in civil cases. Any reversal of a ruling necessarily helps one litigant and harms the other. If, for example, the court initially found Reuven liable to pay Shimon a hundred dinars, and now the judge wishes to reverse that ruling and exempt Reuven, Shimon has every reason to object. He can reasonably say: you were wrong once, why should I believe you are wrong now rather than then? You found in my favor originally — perhaps that was correct and the reversal is the mistake.

This is precisely why *ma she'asa asa* applies, and why a judge who now admits his error nonetheless bears financial liability to the party his incorrect first ruling harmed. As Rashi states (*bemumche*): *yesh bo koach lachzor velo matzi lemimar leh avdin ana ketaama kama didach avdina* — if he is an expert, the other litigant cannot simply say "I will act on your first ruling," because the expert's reconsidered opinion carries genuine authority. The second judgment, coming from an acknowledged expert, has weight sufficient to override the first.

But if the judge is not a *mumche*, the other litigant may say: *mi yeymar detaama batra didach ikkar — dilma kama ikkar uvehai hu detaita* — who says your later reasoning is the authoritative one? Perhaps the first was right and this is the new mistake. Since there is no expertise establishing that the second judgment should be credited over the first, the original ruling stands, and the only remedy is financial liability on the part of the judge.

### Liability & Mumche

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כָּאן שֶׁיֵּשׁ גָּדוֹל הֵימֶנּוּ בְּחָכְמָה וּבְמִנְיָן, כָּאן שֶׁאֵין גָּדוֹל הֵימֶנּוּ בְּחָכְמָה וּבְמִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** a different explanation: The mishna **here,** which teaches that the court does bring the accused back to be judged again if new evidence arises, is stated with regard to a case **where there is** a court **greater than** this one **in wisdom and number** of students that can judge the case again. The mishna **there,** which teaches that what he did is done, is stated with regard to a case **where there is no** court **greater than his in wisdom and number** of students that can judge the case again. If this judge erred, the error will not be corrected.

The *gemara* presses further. The very same *tosefta* that declared *ma she'asa asa* also ruled: *haya mumche leveit din — patur melashalem* — if the judge was a court-recognized expert, he is exempt from paying damages. This seems to say that even for a *mumche*, *ma she'asa asa* applies, and the only distinction between a *mumche* and a non-expert is whether there is liability. For an expert, erroneous rulings happen without negligence — it is an accident rather than a failure of due care — and therefore no payment is required. But in both cases, the ruling appears to remain binding.

### Higher Authority

Rav Nachman offers a different resolution: *kan sheyesh gadol heimenu bechochmah uvevinyan, kan she'ein gadol heimenu bechochmah uvevinyan* — the distinction is whether there exists someone of greater authority who can endorse the reversal. The phrase *gadol bechochmah uvevinyan* is itself somewhat puzzling, since all senior courts share the same fixed size of seventy-one; some interpret *minyan* as referring to the number of students, others to the judge's seniority in years.

In any case, Rav Nachman's position — understood through Rashi — is that even a *mumche* cannot unilaterally reverse his own ruling simply on the strength of his own reconsideration. The litigant who benefited from the first ruling can still say: perhaps the error is now, not then. However, if there is a figure of even greater authority who validates the reversal — confirming that the later decision is the correct one — then the second judgment overrides the first. The *mumche* himself performs the reversal, but it is the backing of superior authority that gives it force. Where there is no such higher authority, even the *mumche*'s reversal cannot stand. And in either case, a *mumche* whose error caused a loss bears no liability — that exemption holds regardless.

## Dvar Mishna vs Shikul Ha…

### Rav Sheshet's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: כָּאן שֶׁטָּעָה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה, כָּאן שֶׁטָּעָה בְּשִׁיקּוּל הַדַּעַת. דְּאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: טָעָה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה – חוֹזֵר, טָעָה בְּשִׁיקּוּל הַדַּעַת – אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says** a different explanation: The mishna **here,** which teaches that the court does bring the accused back to be judged again if new evidence arises, is stated with regard to a case **where** the judge **erred in a matter** that appears in the **Mishna,** i.e., he forgot an explicit mishnaic ruling, in which case the verdict is void. The mishna **there,** which teaches that what he did is done, is stated with regard to a case **where** the judge **erred in** his **deliberation. As Rav Sheshet says** that **Rav Asi says:** In the case of a judge who **erred in a matter** that appears in the **Mishna,** the decision is **revoked,** but in the case of a judge who **erred in** his **deliberation,** the decision is **not revoked.**

Rav Sheshet offers an entirely different resolution, one that cuts across the *mumche* distinction altogether: *kan shetaah bidvar mishnah, kan shetaah beshikul hadaat* — the critical variable is not the judge's level of expertise, but the type of error he made. Did he err in *dvar mishnah* — black-letter law — or in *shikul hadaat* — a judgment call?

As Rav Sheshet states, citing Rav Assi: *ta'ah bidvar mishnah — chozer; ta'ah beshikul hadaat — eino chozer*. If the error was in established black-letter law, the ruling is reversed. If the error was a matter of judgment, the ruling stands. The *mumche* distinction, on this view, is relevant only to the question of liability — whether the judge pays — not to reversibility.

The logic is compelling. A ruling that contradicts accepted, unambiguous law was never a legitimate ruling to begin with. It had no valid basis, and therefore it can be undone. A ruling that was a reasonable judgment call, even if the judge now wishes he had decided otherwise, was legitimate when rendered. The party who prevailed under that ruling has a valid claim to rely on it, and it cannot be retracted.

### What is Dvar Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אֲפִילּוּ טָעָה בְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲפִילּוּ בִּדְרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲפִילּוּ בְּדִידִי וְדִידָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ אֲנַן קָטְלֵי קְנֵי בְּאַגְמָא אֲנַן?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** Does the category of: Erred in a matter that appears in the Mishna, include **even** one who **erred concerning** a statement of **Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Oshaya,** who were among the last of the *tanna'im*? Rav Ashi **said to him: Yes.** Ravina asked him: Does it include **even** one who erred **concerning** a statement **of** the first-generation *amora'im* **Rav and Shmuel?** Rav Ashi **said to him: Yes.** Ravina asked him: Does it include **even** one who erred **concerning** statements **of mine and yours,** as we are of the final generation of *amora'im*? Rav Ashi **said to him: Is that to say** that **we are reed cutters in the pond?** That is, our statements are in this category as well.

The *gemara* immediately probes the scope of *dvar mishnah*. Does it refer literally only to something recorded in a *mishna*? What if the error concerns a ruling in the *Tosefta* — the work of Rav Chiya and Rav Oshaya? The answer is *in* — yes, a clear ruling in the *Tosefta* also qualifies as *dvar mishnah* for these purposes. What about a ruling of the *Amoraim*, after the *mishna* itself? *Afilu bidvar Rav u'Shmuel* — yes, even a ruling of Rav and Shmuel counts. The *gemara* then pushes further: *afilu bdidi u'vdidach* — what about our own rulings, asks Ravina (or Rav Ashi) — do rulings of the last generation of *Amoraim* also have *dvar mishnah* status? The response is characteristic: *atu anan k'kitzay d'kanay b'agma nan* — are we like reeds cut from a swamp? Our rulings equally carry that authoritative weight.

### What is Shikul Hadaat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי שִׁיקּוּל הַדַּעַת? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּגוֹן תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי אוֹ תְּרֵי אָמוֹרָאֵי דִּפְלִיגִי אַהֲדָדֵי, וְלָא אִיתְּמַר הִלְכְתָא לָא כְּמָר וְלָא כְּמָר, וְאִיקְּרִי וַעֲבַד כְּחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ, וְסוּגְיָא דִּשְׁמַעְתָּא אָזְלִי כְּאִידַּךְ – הַיְינוּ שִׁיקּוּל הַדַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is considered** an error in **a deliberation? Rav Pappa says: For example, two *tanna'im* or two *amora'im* who disagree with each other, and the *halakha* was not stated in accordance with** this **Sage or in accordance with** that **Sage, and it occurred** that the judge **acted in accordance with one of them, and the discussion of the *halakha* goes in accordance with the other** opinion, i.e., most judges rule in accordance with the opposing opinion; **this is** an error in **a deliberation.**

Rav Pappa defines *shikul hadaat*: *k'gon tray tannay o tray amoray d'pligi ahadadi* — cases where two *tannaim* or two *Amoraim* dispute the matter, *v'lo itmar hilchata lo k'mar v'lo k'mar* — and no authoritative ruling was ever rendered as to which position is the *halacha*. The judge then decided to side with one of the opinions, *v'ikri v'avid k'chad minayhu*, but the *sugya d'shma'ta* — the general drift of *halachic* practice — ran in the other direction. That is *shikul hadaat*: a mistake in judgment, even though the position adopted was one that a legitimate authority had maintained.

### Implication & Logic

This requires some reflection. Why is it called an error if the judge sided with a recognized opinion in a genuine dispute? The answer appears to be that as long as the judge stood by his decision it would have been entirely legitimate — no one could compel him to change or find him liable. The ruling is called a *shikul hadaat* mistake only in retrospect, when the judge himself concedes that he should have ruled with the majority weight of opinion. At that point, his original ruling had legitimacy and remains binding, but he acknowledges it as a mistake, which may create financial liability — from which he is exempt if he was a *mumche*. The contrast with *dvar mishnah* is sharp: there, the original ruling had no legitimacy whatsoever, it was simply wrong, and it is therefore reversed entirely.

### Canonization Issues

This framework raises a fascinating question that occupied the *Rishonim*: how far does the category of *dvar mishnah* extend beyond the *Amoraim*? If a judge ruled in a manner contradicted by a clear consensus of the *Geonim*, does that constitute *ta'ah bidvar mishnah*? There are different positions among the *Rishonim* on the authority of the *Geonim*. Some distinguish between a judge who was simply unaware of the *Geonic* ruling — in which case it might qualify as *ta'ah bidvar mishnah*, since his error stemmed from ignorance of available authoritative material — and a judge who knew the *Geonic* position and chose to rule otherwise, in which case it remains within his prerogative, just as one *amora* could legitimately rule against another *amora*. These are profound questions about what gets canonized, what constitutes authoritative *halachic* knowledge at any given era, and where the threshold of *dvar mishnah* stops.

## Parah Case Study

### Story Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב הַמְנוּנָא לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּפָרָה שֶׁל בֵּית מְנַחֵם שֶׁנִּיטְּלָה הָאֵם שֶׁלָּהּ, וְהֶאֱכִילָהּ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן לִכְלָבִים. וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים בְּיַבְנֶה, וְהִתִּירוּהָ. שֶׁאָמַר תּוֹדוֹס הָרוֹפֵא: אֵין פָּרָה וַחֲזִירָה יוֹצֵאת מֵאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן חוֹתְכִין הָאֵם שֶׁלָּהּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תֵּלֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna raised an objection to Rav Sheshet** from the continuation of the mishna cited earlier (*Bekhorot* 28b): There was **an incident involving a cow of the household of Menaḥem whose womb was removed,** and when Rabbi Tarfon was consulted he ruled that it is an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], which is forbidden for consumption. **And** based on the ruling of **Rabbi Tarfon,** the questioner **fed it to the dogs. And the incident came before the Sages** of the court **in Yavne, and they ruled** that such an animal is **permitted** and is not a *tereifa*. **As Theodosius [*Todos*] the doctor said: A cow or pig does not emerge from Alexandria of Egypt unless** the residents **sever its womb so that it will not give birth** in the future. The Egyptians wished to protect their livestock as a commercial asset and prevent others from breeding their animals, so they surgically removed the uteruses of animals exported from Alexandria — and those animals plainly survived. The *mishna* thus records that an animal without a uterus is not a *treifah*. Rabbi Tarfon had ruled against a clear *mishna*-level halachic position, making this a textbook case of *ta'ah bidvar Mishna*.

The *gemara* now records a challenge that Rav Hamnuna posed to Rav Sheshet's framework, based on a famous incident. *Maaseh b'parah she-nitlah em shelah* — there was a case involving a cow from which the uterus had been surgically removed. Rabbi Tarfon ruled the animal a *treifah* — reasoning that an animal missing such an organ could not survive — and accordingly it was slaughtered and *he'echilah l'klavim*, fed to the dogs. The matter was subsequently brought before the sages at Yavneh, *u-va maaseh lifnei Chachamim be-Yavneh*, and they permitted such animals, because Todos the physician testified: *ein parah ve-chazirah yotzet me-Alexandria shel Mitzrayim ela im ken chotchin ha-em shelah* — no cow or sow leaves Alexandria in Egypt unless its uterus has been removed. The Egyptians wished to protect their livestock as a commercial asset and prevent others from breeding their animals, so they surgically removed the uteruses of animals exported from Alexandria — and those animals plainly survived. The *mishna* thus records that an animal without a uterus is not a *treifah*. Rabbi Tarfon had ruled against a clear *mishna*-level halachic position, making this a textbook case of *ta'ah bidvar Mishna*.

### Akiva's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: הָלְכָה חֲמוֹרְךָ, טַרְפוֹן! אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: פָּטוּר אַתָּה, שֶׁכׇּל הַמּוּמְחֶה לָרַבִּים פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this, **Rabbi Tarfon said: Your donkey is gone, Tarfon,** as he believed he was required to compensate the owner for the cow that he ruled to be a *tereifa*. **Rabbi Akiva said to him: You are exempt, as any** judge accepted as **an expert for the public** is **exempt from** liability **to pay.**

Upon realizing his error, Rabbi Tarfon exclaimed: *hacha chamor Tarfon* — he feared that he had effectively lost the equivalent of a donkey's load in liability, having caused a loss to the animal's owner through his erroneous ruling. Rabbi Akiva responded: *patur atah — she-kol ha-mumcheh l'rabbim patur milishalem* — you are exempt, because any recognized communal expert is exempt from paying damages.

### Gemara's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִיתָא, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: טוֹעֶה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה אַתָּה, וְטוֹעֶה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Hamnuna states his objection to Rav Sheshet: **And if it is so** that there is a distinction between the two types of judicial errors, **let** Rabbi Akiva **say to** Rabbi Tarfon: **You err in a matter** that appears in the **Mishna,** as the ruling that an animal whose womb has been removed is permitted is recorded in a mishna (see *Ḥullin* 54a), **and** in the case of one who **errs in a matter** that appears in the **Mishna,** the decision is **revoked.**

Rav Hamnuna's challenge to Rav Sheshet now comes into focus. If *ta'ah bidvar mishnah chozer* — if an error in black-letter law automatically reverses the ruling — why did Rabbi Akiva respond only by invoking Rabbi Tarfon's status as a *mumche* to exempt him from liability? He should have said: your error was *ta'ah bidvar Mishna*, and therefore your ruling was never binding in the first place, which means the owner who fed the cow to the dogs did so on his own initiative, and you bear no responsibility for his consequent act.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא וְעוֹד קָאָמַר: חֲדָא, דְּטוֹעֶה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה אַתָּה, וְטוֹעֶה בִּדְבַר מִשְׁנָה חוֹזֵר. וְעוֹד: אִי נָמֵי בְּשִׁיקּוּל הַדַּעַת טָעִיתָ – מוּמְחֶה לָרַבִּים אַתָּה, וְכׇל הַמּוּמְחֶה לָרַבִּים פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Akiva **states one** reason **and** adds **another** reason. **One: As you err in a matter** that appears in the **Mishna, and** in the case of one who **errs in a matter** that appears in the **Mishna,** the decision is **revoked. Another: Even if you erred in a deliberation, you are** a judge accepted as **an expert for the public, and any** judge accepted as **an expert for the public** is **exempt from** liability **to pay.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak clarifies the challenge. He asks: *mai ka-moteiv Rav Hamnuna le-Rav Sheshet mi-parah* — what was the force of this challenge? After all, the cow had already been fed to the dogs; there is nothing to reverse. The answer is that the challenge was not about reversibility per se, but about liability. *Ei amart bishlama Toeh b'dvar Mishna eino chozer* — if we were to say that even a *ta'ah bidvar Mishna* cannot be reversed, then *kam dina* — the ruling was binding the moment it was issued, and Rabbi Tarfon had reason to fear liability, which is exactly why Rabbi Akiva's assurance that he is a *mumche* and therefore *patur* was the relevant and necessary response.

### Practical Limits

But if we rule — as we do — that *ta'ah bidvar Mishna chozer*, then Rabbi Akiva had an additional and perhaps stronger argument available to him: *keivan de'ilu havei para dina lav dina* — had the cow still been alive, the ruling would have had no binding force, it would have been reversed, and the cow would have been permitted. Since that would have been the result, Rabbi Tarfon's ruling itself produced no halachic consequence; it was the owner's subsequent decision to feed the cow to the dogs — not the ruling — that caused the loss. Rabbi Tarfon should therefore be exempt not merely because he is a *mumche*, but because his ruling itself did nothing actionable, and any subsequent loss traces to the owner's own act.

The *gemara* accepts this: *chada ve-od ka-amar* — Rabbi Akiva was making two cumulative arguments. First, this was a *ta'ah bidvar Mishna* and the ruling was therefore never binding. Second, even if we were to treat the error as a *shikul hadaat* mistake and the ruling were binding, Rabbi Tarfon is still a *mumche lerabbim* and exempt from paying. Both grounds converge to exempt him. The upshot of this passage is thus to confirm Rav Sheshet's framework while also demonstrating that the *mumche* exemption from liability remains relevant in its own domain.

## Nital Venatan Beyad

### Rav Chisda's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: כָּאן – שֶׁנָּטַל וְנָתַן בַּיָּד, כָּאן – שֶׁלֹּא נָטַל וְנָתַן בַּיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says** that the contradiction between the mishna here and the mishna in tractate *Bekhorot* as to whether or not the court brings the accused back to be judged again if new evidence arises can be answered as follows: The mishna **there** in tractate *Bekhorot* is stated with regard to a case **where** a judge **took** the item in question from one litigant **and gave** it to the other litigant **with** his **hand.** In this case, the judge is liable to pay. The mishna **here** is stated with regard to a case **where** a judge of the court **did not take** the item in question from one litigant **and give** it to the other litigant **with** his **hand.** The court merely issued a ruling, and the litigants executed the verdict on their own. Therefore, the court is not liable to pay, and the judges retry the case.

Rav Chisda proposes yet a further distinction to resolve the contradiction between the *mishna* and the earlier *tosefta*: *kan shenatal venatan beyad, kan shelo natal venatan beyad* — the difference is whether the judgment was physically executed, whether the money was literally taken from one party's hand and placed in the other's. When the judge physically transferred the funds — *natal venatan beyad* — the matter is final and irreversible, and the question becomes only whether the judge is liable for the damage he caused. When the judge merely issued his verbal ruling without physically effecting the transfer, the ruling may still be reversed.

This distinction concerns reversibility, not liability. The *gemara* does not fully articulate the conceptual basis for this distinction, though a consideration of *ziluta debei dina* — the dignity and finality of the court — may be at work. Once the judge has acted in a manner that concretizes and finalizes the ruling, it takes on a definitive character that verbal rulings alone do not possess.

### Tosefta Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא חִיֵּיב אֶת הַזַּכַּאי, כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּטַל וְנָתַן בַּיָּד. אֶלָּא זִיכָּה אֶת הַחַיָּיב, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״פָּטוּר אַתָּה״, וְהָא לֹא נָטַל וְנָתַן בַּיָּד! כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״פָּטוּר אַתָּה״, כְּמוֹ שֶׁנָּטַל וְנָתַן בַּיָּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** one can explain the clause from the mishna in tractate *Bekhorot*: He **finds an innocent** party **liable,** in a case **where** the judge **took** the item in question from the innocent party **and gave** it to the other litigant **with** his **hand. But how can you find** him giving the item from one to another with regard to the clause of: **He exempts a liable** party? **As,** the judge **said to** the litigant only: **You are exempt** from liability to pay, **but** the judge **did not take** the item in question from one litigant **and give** it to the other litigant **with** his **hand.** The Gemara explains: **Once he says: You are exempt** from liability to pay, it is **considered as if he took** the item in question from the innocent party **and gave** it to the other litigant **with** his **hand,** as there is no need for any further action.

The *tosefta* that said *ma she'asa asa* and imposed liability was accordingly describing a case of *natal venatan beyad*, where the ruling had been physically executed. That is the scenario where what is done cannot be undone, and where the question of liability becomes pressing.

Working through the individual cases: *chiyav es hazakkai* — where the judge found the innocent party liable and took money from him to transfer to the other — this is readily intelligible as *natal venatan beyad*. But *zikah es hachayav* — where the judge exempted the truly liable party — how is this *natal venatan beyad*? The judge merely said "you are exempt"; there was no money to transfer.

### Mishna Context

The *mishna* that says civil rulings may be reversed is describing the case of *lo natal venatan beyad*, where the money was not yet physically transferred. In that case, the ruling remains open to reconsideration: if the judge had said *chayav ata* but not yet taken the money, he may call the case back and reverse it.

### Patur Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת מַחְזִירִין בֵּין לִזְכוּת בֵּין לְחוֹבָה, בִּשְׁלָמָא לִזְכוּת מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא: ״חַיָּיב אַתָּה״, וְלֹא נָטַל וְלֹא נָתַן בַּיָּד. אֶלָּא לְחוֹבָה הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״פָּטוּר אַתָּה״? וְהָאָמְרַתְּ: כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ ״פָּטוּר אַתָּה״, כְּמוֹ שֶׁנָּטַל וְנָתַן בַּיָּד דָּמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** what of **the mishna, which teaches:** In cases of **monetary law,** the court **brings** the accused **back** to be judged again if new evidence arises, **either** with a claim **to exempt** the accused, **or** with a claim **to find** him **liable? Granted, you** can **find** the example of: **To exempt, where** the court **says at the outset** to one litigant: **You are liable** to pay, **and** a judge **did not take** the item in question from one litigant **and give** it to the other litigant **with** his **hand,** as Rav Ḥisda holds that the verdict is revoked and the court does not pay. **But how can you find** the example of judging the case again **to find** him **liable? As** once the court **says to** the litigant: **You are exempt,** the case cannot be judged again. **But you said** that **once** a judge **says: You are exempt** from liability to pay, it is **considered as if he took** the item in question from the innocent party **and gave** it to the other litigant **with** his **hand.** If so, the court must pay and the judgment will not be revoked.

The difficulty is with the case where the judge said *patur ata* — "you are exempt" — and nothing remained to be physically transferred. How does this fit either category?

The *gemara* states: *keivan d'amar patur ata k'man d'naso v'nasav biyod dami* — since once you declared someone exempt and the parties went home with nothing further to do, that is functionally equivalent to *natal venatan beyad*. The finality of *patur ata* is total: nothing remains to be done, the case is complete, and that completeness mirrors the physical finality of an actual transfer.

This creates a problem for the *mishna*, however. The *mishna* says that civil rulings may be reversed *bein lizchus bein lichova* — both to exempt and to obligate. But if *patur ata* is already equivalent to *natal venatan beyad*, how can the *mishna* say we may reverse a ruling that initially exempted someone and now find him liable?

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this by reading the *mishna* as presenting only one case: *dinei mamonos machzirin l'zchus shehi chova* — when it says "reverse to exempt," it means a single case in which the original ruling of *chayav ata* is reversed to *patur ata*, and by that single reversal, the party previously found liable is now freed, while the party previously found in favor is now disadvantaged. The *mishna*'s "both ways" reflects the double impact of one ruling on two parties, not two separate proceedings.

But this resolution fails under scrutiny, because the parallel ruling for *dinei nefashos* — *machzirin l'zchus v'ein machzirin l'chova*, that in capital cases we reverse only toward innocence and not toward guilt — cannot similarly be read as a single case with two sides. *Machzirin l'zchus zechus geridasa* — reversing to find someone innocent in a capital case is simply finding that one person innocent, not necessarily hurting anyone else. And *v'ein machzirin l'chova* means we will not reopen the case to find the defendant guilty even if there is now a basis to do so. One might theoretically suggest that finding a murderer innocent harms the *goel hadam* — the blood-redeemer — and for that reason should also be prohibited. The *gemara* summarily dismisses this: *mishum chovasi d'goel hadam katlinan lei lehai* — we would not execute an otherwise innocent person in order to spare the *goel hadam* from disappointment. And further, the language *bein bein* — "whether... whether" — clearly signals two separate cases, not one case with dual impact. So the *gemara* acknowledges the difficulty: *kashya*.

### Ravina's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ בְּיָדוֹ מַשְׁכּוֹן, וּנְטָלוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ. טִימֵּא אֶת הַטָּהוֹר, דְּאַגַּע בֵּיהּ שֶׁרֶץ. טִיהֵר אֶת הַטָּמֵא, שֶׁעֵירְבָן בֵּין פֵּירוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another explanation of how one can find a judge giving the item from one to another with regard to the clause of: He exempts a liable party. **Ravina said:** It is possible in a case **where** the one who lodged the claim **had in his possession** an item belonging to the other litigant that functioned as **collateral** for a debt, **and** when the judge issued a verdict in favor of the other he **took** the collateral **from him,** thereby physically transferring it to the wrong party. In the case from the mishna in tractate *Bekhorot*: He **ruled** that **a pure** item is **impure,** how could he cause a loss with his own hands? It is **where** he **had** the litigant's ritually pure item **touch a creeping animal** to emphasize that he believes it was already impure, and he thereby imparted impurity to it. In that mishna's case of: He **ruled** that **an impure** item is **pure,** how could he cause a loss with his own hands? It is **where** he **mixed** this impure produce of the litigant's **with** the litigant's ritually pure **produce,** and he thereby caused all of the produce to be considered impure.

Ravina offers a more precise and satisfying account. The *natal venatan beyad* in a case of *patur ata* need not be a legal fiction. It describes a specific physical act: *kegon dehava lei mashkon unetalo heimenu* — where Reuven held collateral belonging to Shimon as security against the claimed debt, the judge declared Shimon exempt, and then physically took the collateral from Reuven's hand and returned it to Shimon. That physical return of the collateral is the *natal venatan beyad* that renders the ruling final. Where no such physical act occurred — where the judge merely said "you are exempt" without taking and transferring anything — the ruling is *lo natal venatan beyad* and may still be reversed.

This resolves the problem cleanly. A ruling of *patur ata* is not automatically equivalent to *natal venatan beyad*. It becomes final only when accompanied by a physical act, specifically the return of collateral. Without that act, reversal remains possible.

### Other Examples

The *gemara* extends this analysis to purity rulings. *Timas es hatahor* — declaring something pure to be impure — when is that *natal venatan beyad* such that it cannot be reversed? *Daadi bei sheretz* — if the judge actually took an insect (*sheretz*) and threw it onto the *terumah* to demonstrate and physically instantiate his ruling of impurity, that is *natal venatan beyad*. The ruling is now irreversible.

Conversely, *tihartas es hatamei* — declaring something impure to be pure — how does one physically demonstrate such a ruling *biyadayim*? *Sheiravan befirotav* — the judge mixed the suspected impure item into the person's other produce, thereby physically expressing his confidence in its purity. That physical act of mixing constitutes *natal venatan beyad* and renders the ruling final.

In sum, three frameworks are presented for when *dinei mamonos* rulings may be reversed. First, the *mumche* who is backed by someone of even greater authority — his second ruling, thus supported, can override his first. Second, and most significant conceptually, the *dvar mishnah/shikul hadaat* distinction: a judgment-call error leaves the original ruling binding and raises only the question of financial liability, while an error against black-letter law means the original ruling was never valid, and it is wholly reversed. Third, even within the *shikul hadaat* category, if the judge physically executed his ruling — *natal venatan beyad* — that finality bars reversal, while a ruling not yet physically executed remains open to correction.

## Dinei Nefashos Rules

### Innocence Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִין לְיוֹצֵא מִבֵּית דִּין חַיָּיב, וְאָמַר אֶחָד ״יֵשׁ לִי לְלַמֵּד עָלָיו זְכוּת״, מִנַּיִין שֶׁמַּחְזִירִין אוֹתוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נָקִי אַל תַּהֲרֹג״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that in cases of **capital law,** the court brings the accused back to be judged again with a claim to acquit him, but does not bring him back to be judged with a claim to find him liable. To explain the terms "innocent" and "righteous" in the verse: "And the innocent and the righteous you shall not slay" (Exodus 23:7), **the Sages taught: From where** is it derived **that** with regard to one who is **leaving the court** having been found **liable, and someone said: I have** the ability **to teach** a reason to **acquit him, from where** is it derived **that** the court **brings** the accused **back** to be judged again? **The verse states: The innocent you shall not slay,** and the accused may in fact be innocent.

The *gemara* now turns to the asymmetric rules governing capital cases. The first question is the source for calling a case back after a guilty verdict has been rendered, if someone comes forward with a reason to find the defendant innocent. The verse states: *venaki vetzadik al taharog* — "do not kill the innocent and the righteous." *Naki* here refers to one who is genuinely innocent regardless of how the court ruled — a person who was convicted but may in fact have done nothing wrong. The imperative *naki al taharog* therefore requires us to reopen the case whenever there is a possibility that a condemned person is truly innocent.

### No Double Jeopardy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִין לְיוֹצֵא מִבֵּית דִּין זַכַּאי, וְאָמַר אֶחָד: ״יֵשׁ לִי לְלַמֵּד עָלָיו חוֹבָה״, מִנַּיִין שֶׁאֵין מַחְזִירִין אוֹתוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״צַדִּיק אַל תַּהֲרֹג״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived **that** with regard to one who is **leaving the court,** having been **acquitted, and someone says: I have** the ability **to teach** a reason to find **him liable, from where** is it derived **that** the court **does not bring** the accused **back** to be judged again? **The verse states: "The righteous you shall not slay,"** and the accused was found righteous in his trial.

*Tzadik*, by contrast, refers to *tzadik bedino* — someone whose case was decided in his favor, someone the court ruled to be righteous in his judgment. *Tzadik al taharog* thus prohibits reopening a case against someone who has been acquitted. No matter what new evidence or argument might emerge against him, the person who has been ruled innocent cannot be brought back before the court to face the possibility of a guilty verdict and execution. This is the principle of no double jeopardy in capital cases.

### Meisit Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: וְחִילּוּפָא לְמֵסִית, דִּכְתִיב ״לֹא תַחְמֹל וְלֹא תְכַסֶּה עָלָיו״. רַב כָּהֲנָא מַתְנֵי מִ״כִּי הָרֹג תַּהַרְגֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi says: And the opposite of this** is the *halakha* **with regard to one who entices** others to engage in idol worship, **as it is written** concerning him: **"Neither shall you spare, neither shall you conceal him"** (Deuteronomy 13:9). He is brought back to court to find him liable, but not to acquit him. **Rav Kahana teaches** this last *halakha* citing a different verse concerning the enticer: **"But you shall kill him [*harog tahargennu*]"** (Deuteronomy 13:10). The repetition of the verb indicates that he is killed even in circumstances where transgressors of other prohibitions would not be.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi notes a striking exception: *vechilufa lemeisit* — the rules are reversed for the *meisit*, one who seduces others to commit idolatry. For this category, *lo tachmol velo techaseh alav* — the Torah explicitly commands that no compassion be shown and nothing be concealed on his behalf. As discussed elsewhere in this tractate, the *meisit* is treated with unique stringency: the court actively looks for reasons to convict, and finds none to acquit. Rav Kahana cites the verse *ki harog tahargenu* — "you shall surely put him to death" — as the basis for this inversion.

### Extension to Galus & Mal…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִנֵּיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: חַיָּיבֵי גָּלִיּוֹת מִנַּיִין? אָתְיָא ״רֹצֵחַ״ ״רֹצֵחַ״. חַיָּיבֵי מַלְקִיּוֹת מִנַּיִין? אָתְיָא ״רָשָׁע״ ״רָשָׁע״. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: חַיָּיבֵי גָּלִיּוֹת מִנַּיִין? אַתְיָא ״רֹצֵחַ״ ״רֹצֵחַ״. חַיָּיבֵי מַלְקוֹת מִנַּיִין? אָתְיָא ״רָשָׁע״ ״רָשָׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira asked Rav Sheshet: From where** is it derived that the *halakha* concerning **those liable** to be **exiled** to a city of refuge for killing unintentionally is the same with regard to retrying a court case as the *halakha* concerning one who killed intentionally, who is found liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment? Rav Sheshet answered: It is **derived** from a verbal analogy employing the term **"murderer"** stated with regard to one who kills intentionally (see Numbers 35:16) and the term **"murderer"** stated with regard to one who kills unintentionally (see Numbers 35:19). Rabbi Zeira asked Rav Sheshet: **From where** is it derived that the *halakha* concerning **those liable** to receive forty **lashes** is the same with regard to retrying a court case as the *halakha* concerning one who killed intentionally, who is found liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment? Rav Sheshet answered: It is **derived** from a verbal analogy employing the term **"wicked"** stated with regard to one who kills intentionally (see Numbers 35:31) and the term **"wicked"** stated with regard to those liable to receive lashes (see Deuteronomy 25:2). The Gemara comments: **This is also taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 7:3): **From where** is it derived that the *halakha* concerning **those liable** to be **exiled** for killing unintentionally is the same with regard to retrying a court case as the *halakha* concerning one who killed intentionally, who is found liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment? It is **derived** from a verbal analogy employing the term **"murderer"** stated with regard to one who kills intentionally and the term **"murderer"** stated with regard to one who kills unintentionally. **From where** is it derived that the *halakha* concerning **those liable** to receive forty **lashes** is the same with regard to retrying a court case as the *halakha* concerning one who killed intentionally, who is found liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment? It is **derived** from a verbal analogy employing the term **"wicked"** stated with regard to one who kills intentionally and the term **"wicked"** stated with regard to those liable to receive lashes.

Rav Zeira asks Rav Sheshet whether the principle of no double jeopardy extends beyond capital cases to *chayei galuyos* — those subject to exile. The answer is yes: *atia rotzeach rotzeach* — a verbal analogy (*gezeirah shavah*) is drawn between the word *rotzeach* as it appears in the context of the *goel hadam* and *rotzeach* in the context of intentional killing, establishing that the same rule that applies to capital cases applies to cases of *galut*.

The same question is then asked regarding *chayei malkuyos* — those subject to flogging. Here the derivation is *atia rasha rasha* — the word *rasha* appears in the verse *asher hu rasha lamut* in one context and in the laws of *malkot* in another, forming a *gezeirah shavah* that imports the capital case rules into the flogging context as well. The *baraita* confirms both derivations.

One should note the interesting tension this creates with the *mishna*'s structural division between *dinei mamonos* and *dinei nefashos*. *Malkot* is adjudicated by a court of three, which the *mishna* assigns to the *dinei mamonos* category in other respects. The categories are not perfectly consistent across all the *halachot* of the chapter: for the specific rule of no double jeopardy, *malkot* and *galut* are treated as part of the *dinei nefashos* framework, even though in other dimensions — such as the size of the court required — they fall on the *dinei mamonos* side. The question of where *potiching l'zchus* and *potiching l'chova* apply to these intermediate categories is similarly left unresolved by the *mishna* and remains to be examined further.
