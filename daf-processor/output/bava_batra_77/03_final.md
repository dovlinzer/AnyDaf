# Bava Batra 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boat Acquisition

### Prior Discussion

Today's *daf* is Bava Batra 77, and we pick up in the middle of 76b, approximately a third from the bottom of the page, at the line beginning with the word *Rava*: *leima Abaye ve-Rava*. We are resuming a discussion that had been underway regarding a very important topic: how one transfers a *shtar chov*, a document of debt, in a case where the goal is not merely to hand over the physical paper but to transfer the underlying loan itself.

The scenario is a familiar one. Shimon owes Reuven a hundred dollars, and Reuven wishes to transfer that debt to Levi. Levi pays Reuven fifty dollars now, and from that point Shimon will owe the money to Levi. The question is how Reuven effects that transfer. Physically handing over the *shtar* is not obviously sufficient. A whole discussion had developed around the concept of *otiyot* — the abstract debt represented by the letters of the document — and whether a *mesira*, a handing over of the *shtar*, suffices to transfer it, or whether one also requires a separately written document, a *shtar* that makes the transfer explicit.

In the middle of that discussion, the *Gemara* had introduced the question of a *sefina*, a boat, and how it is acquired. In one source, Rabbi holds that a boat is acquired through *mesira* — handing over the rope or some attached part of the vessel. In another source, Rabbi appears to say that a boat is acquired through *meshikha*, drawing or pulling it. The *Gemara* reconciles these by distinguishing between domains: in a *reshut harabim*, a public domain, *mesira* works whereas *meshikha* does not; in a *simta*, an alley or side street, *meshikha* works. There is a dispute between the Rashbam and Tosafot as to whether *mesira* also works in a *simta*, but it is clear that in a *reshut harabim* only *mesira* is effective.

### Rabbi vs Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא – דְּאָמְרִי כְּרַבִּי?
>
> **Translation:** **Shall we say** that **Abaye and Rava state** their opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, not that of the Rabbis? The *baraita* indicates that only Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi maintains that one can acquire ownership by means of passing in the public domain.

With that background in place, the *Gemara* now wraps up the boat discussion. The question raised is whether Abaye and Rava, who rule that in a *reshut harabim* only *mesira* works and *meshikha* does not, are thereby aligning themselves with Rabbi — a *da'at yachid*, a minority opinion — against the *Chachamim*, who require *meshikha* into a *simta* and do not allow *mesira* in a *reshut harabim* to suffice.

### Domain-Based Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִי דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ ״לֵךְ חֲזֵק וּקְנִי״ – הָכִי נָמֵי; הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֵךְ מְשׁוֹךְ וּקְנֵי״;
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The Rabbis agree that it is possible to effect acquisition in the public domain through the act of passing. Therefore, **if** it is a case **where** the seller **says to him: Go take possession and** thereby **effect acquisition, so too** he can effect acquisition through the act of passing, and does not need to pull it. **Here** the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, as **we are dealing with** a case **where** the seller **says to him: Go pull and** thereby **effect acquisition** of it.

The *Gemara* asks: does this mean Abaye and Rava must follow only Rabbi? The answer is nuanced. Rav Ashi says: *i de-amar lei lekh chazeki u-knei* — if the seller explicitly said "go take possession of it," using the language of *mesira*, then everyone agrees, both Rabbi and the *Chachamim*, that *mesira* in a *reshut harabim* is effective. In such a case, Abaye and Rava's ruling that *mesira* works in a *reshut harabim* would be consistent with the view of all authorities.

### Abaye & Rava Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר: קְפִידָא, וּמָר סָבַר: מַרְאֶה מָקוֹם הוּא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi elaborates: One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that the seller is **particular** about the method by which the item is acquired, and therefore it can be acquired only through pulling. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that the seller is merely **indicating the manner to him,** i.e., he advises him to use this act of acquisition but he does not mind if the buyer prefers to perform a different act of acquisition.

The question then becomes: what is the specific case in which Rabbi and the *Chachamim* actually disagree? The *Gemara* answers: *de-amar lei lekh mshokh u-knei* — the seller used the language of *meshikha*. He said "go draw it." Now the question is how to interpret that instruction. The *Chachamim* hold *kafida*: the seller was being *makpid*, insisting that the acquisition be effected specifically through *meshikha*, and therefore *mesira* will not work and the buyer must drag the boat into a *simta*. Rabbi, on the other hand, holds *marei makom hu lo*: the seller was merely indicating where the boat is, giving the buyer the option and the freedom to take it home — he was not imposing a condition on the mode of acquisition. Therefore, *mesira* in the *reshut harabim* still works according to Rabbi.

### Intent & Language

This resolution does raise a significant difficulty. If the entire dispute between Rabbi and the *Chachamim* turns only on the narrow question of how to interpret the seller's phrasing — whether it constitutes a binding condition — it is striking that the *baraita* states the disagreement so baldly, without indicating that it hinges entirely on a question of intent. Rabbi says *mesira*, the *Chachamim* say *meshikha*, with no qualification. And yet what the *Gemara* is really saying is that they have no fundamental disagreement about how a *sefina* may be acquired; they agree it can be done by *mesira* in a *reshut harabim*, and they differ only over what the seller meant when he used a particular form of words.

### Seller's Intent

Nevertheless, this is the *Gemara*'s resolution in order to bring Abaye and Rava into agreement with both Rabbi and the *Chachamim* simultaneously. This also occasions a brief aside, noted in the *Rishonim*, about *mesira* itself: there is a dispute among the *Rishonim* whether *mesira* requires a physical handing over, or whether it is sufficient that one verbally conveys it — saying *lekh chazek u-knei*, "go take possession" — without any physical act of transfer.

## Debt Transfer

### Rav Pappa's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי מַאן דִּמְזַבֵּין לֵיהּ שְׁטָרָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ, צָרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב לֵיהּ: ״קְנִי הוּא – וְכׇל שִׁעְבּוּדָא דְּבֵיהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: One who sells** a promissory **note to another must write to him: Acquire it and all liens on property that are** contained **within it.**

With the boat discussion concluded, the *Gemara* returns to its major theme: the transfer of a *shtar chov* and the mechanism required to effectuate that transfer. Rav Pappa rules: *hai man de-mazbin lei shtara le-chavrei, tzarich lemichtav lei kni hu ve-chol shibuda de-bei* — one who sells a promissory note to another must write in a document: "acquire this and all the liens attached to it."

To make this concrete: if Shimon owes Reuven a hundred dollars and Reuven transfers the *shtar* to Levi, Levi will now have in his possession the original *shtar* stating that Shimon owes money to Reuven. But what does that alone accomplish for Levi? This is why *mesira* alone is insufficient according to the *Chachamim*: Reuven must write a second document to Levi stating that he, Reuven, is transferring the debt — the obligation Shimon owes him — along with all the liens he holds on Shimon's property, to Levi. Only then, once Levi holds both the original *shtar* and this transfer document, does he fully acquire the debt and the attendant liens.

Rav Pappa's ruling thus accomplishes two things, according to the Rashbam: it specifies what language is required in the accompanying document (*kni hu ve-chol shibuda de-bei*), and it implicitly rules like the *Chachamim* against Rabbi — handing over the original *shtar* alone is not enough.

### Rav Ashi's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, וַאֲמַרִית לֵיהּ: טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב לֵיהּ הָכִי, הָא לָא כְּתַב לֵיהּ הָכִי – לָא קָנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I stated** this ***halakha* before Rav Kahana, and I said to him** the following analysis: **The reason** the buyer acquires it is **that** the seller **wrote this for him.** This indicates that if **he did not write this for him,** the buyer **does not acquire** the monetary rights recorded in the promissory note.

Rav Ashi reports that he presented this analysis before Rav Kahana and raised the following challenge. He reasoned: *ta'ama de-chsav lei hachi, ha lo chsav lei hachi lo kani* — it seems that Rav Pappa is saying the reason Levi acquires the debt is specifically because this additional language was written; had it not been written, Levi would not acquire even though Reuven handed him the *shtar*. But if so, asks Rav Ashi, why? Isn't the very act of Reuven handing the *shtar* to Levi self-evidently an expression of his intent to transfer the debt? *Le-tzoro pi tzluchosav hu tzarich?* — Does Levi need this document to serve as a bottle stopper? What else could he possibly want it for?

### Rav Kahana Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי לָצוֹר עַל פִּי צְלוֹחִיתוֹ הוּא צָרִיךְ?! אָמַר לִי: אִין; לָצוֹר וְלָצוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi asks: Why, then, did he purchase the promissory note? **But does he require** it **to tie around the mouth of his flask** as a stopper? Clearly, he purchased the document for the purpose of collecting the debt recorded in it. Rav Pappa **said to me: Yes,** it is possible that he purchased the promissory note in order **to tie** it around his flask. Since the owner did not transfer ownership of the obligation recorded in the promissory note, the buyer acquires only the paper itself.

Rav Kahana's response was direct: *in, letzor u-letzor* — yes, without the additional *shtar*, I will say that all Levi received is a bottle stopper. Unless something makes the transfer of the debt itself — as distinct from the physical paper — explicit in writing, then despite whatever Levi may have intended or expected, the debt itself has not transferred. Levi holds a piece of paper; he does not hold the loan.

### Rashbam Explanation

The Rashbam addresses what might seem like an obvious objection: surely if Levi paid fifty dollars for this hundred-dollar debt, no one imagines he paid for a piece of paper. The Rashbam concedes this point in full — of course Levi's intent was to acquire the debt — but insists that intent is not the operative question. The question is whether a halakhically sufficient act of transfer took place. If it did not, the debt did not transfer, regardless of what either party expected. Were Reuven to change his mind and say he would not go through with the sale, he would be entitled to do so, and would simply return the fifty dollars. The physical piece of paper is not enough; one needs the additional *shtar* to make explicit and to concretize the reality that the debt — not just the paper — is passing from Reuven to Levi.

## Otiyot Transfer Rules

### Ameimar's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר, הִלְכְתָא: אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה, כְּרַבִּי. אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: גְּמָרָא, אוֹ סְבָרָא? אָמַר לֵיהּ: גְּמָרָא. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: סְבָרָא נָמֵי הוּא – דְּאוֹתִיּוֹת מִילֵּי נִינְהוּ, וּמִילֵּי בְּמִילֵּי לָא מִיקַּנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar says: The *halakha*** is that **letters are acquired by** merely **transferring** the document to the buyer, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rav Ashi said to Ameimar:** Is your ruling based on **a tradition or** on your own **logical reasoning?** Ameimar **said to him:** It is based on **a tradition. Rav Ashi said: It also** stands to **reason** that the contents of a promissory note are acquired through transferring, **as letters,** i.e., the contents of a promissory note, **are words,** i.e., the buyer is acquiring the right to a monetary obligation, not a physical item, **and words cannot be acquired through** other **words.**

We now arrive at the top of 77a. Ameimar rules: *hilchasa, otiyot niknas be-mesira ke-Rabbi* — the *halakha* is that *otiyot* are acquired through *mesira* alone, in accordance with Rabbi's view. This would mean, against what Rav Pappa appeared to be saying, that handing over the original *shtar* is sufficient to transfer a debt.

### Manuscript Variants

However, this ruling is far from settled, and there is a very large Tosafot here — as well as a substantial Tosafot on the preceding *daf* — that complicates matters considerably. The difficulty is a *girsa* question: what did Ameimar actually say? Some manuscripts read the ruling exactly as just stated, *hilchasa otiyot niknas be-mesira ke-Rabbi*. Other manuscripts — and Tosafot prefers this version — read the precise opposite: *hilchasa ein otiyot niknas be-mesira ke-rabbanan*, the *halakha* is that *otiyot* are not acquired through *mesira* alone, and we rule like the *Chachamim*, consistent with Rav Pappa's ruling. Under this *girsa*, one must both hand over the *shtar* and write a second document of transfer. Given that this *halakhic* ruling exists in two diametrically opposed textual versions, the ultimate conclusion about how to transfer a debt is genuinely uncertain from the *Gemara* alone.

### Rav Ashi Inquiry

Proceeding now as Tosafot reads the *Gemara* — i.e., taking the ruling to be that *mesira* alone is insufficient — Rav Ashi asks Ameimar: *gemara u-sevara*? Is the ruling that one needs both *mesira* and a written *shtar* something received as a tradition, or is it a logical conclusion, a *sevara*? Ameimar answers: *gemara* — it is a received tradition.

### Sevara on Abstraction

The *sevara* that Rav Ashi articulates is crucial: *de-otiyot mili ninhu* — *otiyot*, the abstract debt, are in the final analysis just words. *U-mili be-mili lo miknan* — and words are not transferred merely with more words.

### Sevara Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר רַב: שְׁנֵי שְׁטָרוֹת הֵם – ״זְכוּ בְּשָׂדֶה לִפְלוֹנִי וְכִתְבוּ לוֹ אֶת הַשְּׁטָר״ – חוֹזֵר בַּשְּׁטָר, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר בַּשָּׂדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it true that documents **cannot** be acquired through words? **But doesn't Rabba bar Yitzḥak say** that **Rav says: There are two** types of **documents.** The first type is where one says to others: **Take possession of this field for so-and-so and write the document for him** as proof of the sale of the field. In this case, **he may renege with regard to the document,** i.e., he may change his mind and tell them not to write it. **But he may not renege with regard to the field,** as the buyer has already acquired it.

This *sevara* deserves careful unpacking, and Tosafot explains it well. When Reuven hands the original *shtar* to Levi and says, "By my handing this over to you, you now own the debt Shimon owed me" — what exactly is being transferred? The physical piece of paper is a trivial object. What Reuven is really trying to transfer is the debt itself, which is an abstraction. All of the standard modes of *kinyan* — *meshikha*, *hagbahah*, *chazakah* — are grounded in physical acts that concretize the reality of a transfer of ownership. An abstract debt cannot be transferred merely through the words used to frame the handing-over of a document. That is *mili be-mili*, abstractions framed by words, and it is insufficient.

What is required instead is that the transfer itself be concretized. Reuven writes a new *shtar* stating: I, Reuven, am transferring to Levi the debt Shimon owes me, together with all the liens I hold on Shimon's property. Now the abstract debt has been given a concrete written form, and that document — which expresses the new legal reality — is handed over to Levi. Levi takes possession of a physical document that encodes his new status as creditor. The abstraction has been rendered concrete through *ketiva*, and the combination of *mesira* and *ketiva* together effects the transfer.

This reading, following Tosafot, is far more coherent than the alternative. Reading the *Gemara* the other way — that *mesira* alone should suffice because the debt is only words, and therefore a *shtar* cannot do anything further — is extremely difficult to sustain. The *Gemara* reads most naturally in the Tosafot direction: *mesira* alone is not enough precisely because the debt is abstract, and abstraction requires concretization through writing.

## Challenge via Agav

### Land & Document Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּכְתְּבוּ לוֹ אֶת הַשְּׁטָר״ – חוֹזֵר בֵּין בַּשְּׁטָר בֵּין בַּשָּׂדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The second type of document is where he said: Take possession of this field for so-and-so **on the condition that you write him a document.** If the document has not yet been delivered **he can retract** his instruction **both with regard to the document and with regard to the field,** as the transfer of the field is dependent on the writing of the document.

The *Gemara* now challenges this *sevara*. Is it truly the case that something abstract cannot be transferred without a written document to accompany it? The *Gemara* invokes a statement of Rabba bar Yitzchak in the name of Rav, which sets out several scenarios involving the gifting of land together with a written deed.

Rabba bar Yitzchak says: *shnei shtarot hen* — there are two distinct scenarios involving a *shtar* in the context of a land transaction.

### Transfer Then Document

The first scenario: one says *zachu basadeh leploni ve-kitvu lo et ha-shtar* — take possession of this field on behalf of so-and-so, and also write a deed for him. The principle of *zachin le-adam she-lo be-fanav* allows a third party to receive an acquisition on behalf of someone else even in that person's absence. So Reuven and Shimon are summoned, a *kinyan* is performed — perhaps *chazakah*, perhaps *kinyan sudar* — and Levi thereby acquires the field. But the giftor also instructs them to write up a deed memorializing the gift.

The ruling is *chozer ba-shtar ve-eino chozer ba-sadeh*: once the *kinyan* is done, Levi owns the field and that cannot be undone. But since the *shtar* was mentioned as a separate instruction subsequent to the *kinyan*, the giftor retains the right to back out of having the *shtar* written until it is actually delivered to Levi. The deed has been authorized by the giftor, and only he can instruct the scribe to write it, but he may change his mind about that until delivery. Levi owns the field; he may not yet own the proof.

### Conditional Transfer

The second scenario: *al menat she-tichtevu lo et ha-shtar* — "take possession of this field for Levi on the condition that you write him the *shtar*." Here the acquisition of the field is explicitly conditioned on the eventual delivery of the deed. In this case, *chozer bein ba-shtar bein ba-sadeh* — the giftor may reverse course on the *shtar* until it is delivered, and if he does, that reversal also unravels the acquisition of the field itself, since the field was transferred conditionally upon the *shtar* being delivered. The *chiddush* here is not enormous — it is self-evident that a conditional acquisition may be undone if the condition is not met — though the primary novelty is that the giftor has the ability to back out of having the *shtar* written at all.

### Pre-Written Document

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה שְׁטָרוֹת הֵן; תְּרֵי – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן, אִידַּךְ – אִם קָדַם מוֹכֵר וְכָתַב אֶת הַשְּׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin** says that **Rav Huna says: There are** actually **three** types of **documents. Two** are **those that we stated** above, and **the other** is **if** the **seller wrote the document in advance.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Avin then says in the name of Rav Huna: *sheloshah shtarot hen* — there is in fact a third scenario. *Im kadam mocher ve-kasav et ha-shtar* — if the seller or giftor went ahead and had the *shtar* written before the transfer took place. This is analogous to what we learned: *kosevin shtar la-mocher af al pi she-ein lokeiach imo* — a deed may be written for the seller even in the absence of the buyer. So the giftor anticipated the transaction, had a *sofer* draw up the deed in advance — essentially a pre-signed document recording that he has given the field to Levi — and carries it with him before the actual transfer is made.

### Agav Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּאוֹתָהּ שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: כּוֹתְבִין שְׁטָר לַמּוֹכֵר אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹקֵחַ עִמּוֹ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהֶחְזִיק זֶה בַּקַּרְקַע – נִקְנָה שְׁטָר בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּא; וְזוֹ הִיא שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: נְכָסִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן אַחְרָיוּת נִקְנִין עִם נְכָסִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן אַחְרָיוּת, בְּכֶסֶף וּשְׁטָר וַחֲזָקָה!
>
> **Translation:** This is **like that which we learned** in a mishna (167b): A scribe **may write a bill** of sale **for the seller even if the buyer is not with him** when the seller presents his request. In a case of this kind, **once this** buyer **takes possession of the land the deed is acquired wherever it is. And this is that which we learned** in another mishna (*Kiddushin* 26a): **Property that does not serve as a guarantee,** i.e., movable property, can be **acquired** together **with property that serves as a guarantee,** i.e., land, when the land is acquired **by** means of giving **money, or** by means of giving **a document, or** by means of taking **possession.** This shows that a bill of sale can be transferred without any act of acquisition performed for the document, and certainly through words, which presents a difficulty to Rav Ashi's opinion.

Now when the transfer takes place and Levi (or an agent on his behalf) takes physical possession of the field — through *chazakah* or whatever *kinyan* applies — the ruling is: *keivan she-hechzik zeh ba-karka, niknah shtar be-chol makom she-hu* — as soon as the act of *kinyan* on the land is complete, the pre-written deed automatically passes into Levi's ownership regardless of where it physically is. Even if it is still sitting in the giftor's back pocket, Levi owns it by operation of the land transfer.

This is the mechanism of *agav*: *nechasim she-ein lahem achrayut niknin im nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayut be-kesef, be-shtar, u-ve-chazakah*. Property without liens — *metaltelin*, movable chattels — can be acquired along with and by virtue of the acquisition of property that does carry liens, i.e., real estate. This is *kinyan agav*: when one takes possession of real estate, that act of *kinyan* can simultaneously confer ownership of associated chattels, provided the transferor intends them to go together. If I am selling you my plot of land and my dining room chairs, you could separately perform *hagbahah* on each chair and *chazakah* on the land — or you could simply do *chazakah* on the land with the explicit understanding that the chairs are being transferred *agav*, alongside it.

What makes the pre-written-deed case interesting is that in such a case, no explicit bundling statement is even required. The deed is considered to automatically accompany the land, such that the moment Levi acquires the land, the deed passes to him without any additional act.

## Agav vs Mesira

### Gemara's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַגַּב שָׁאנֵי; דְּהָא מַטְבֵּעַ – דְּלָא נִיקְנֵי בַּחֲלִיפִין, וְאַגַּב אַרְעָא נִיקְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Acquiring a bill of sale **by means of** acquisition of land **is different,** as it is similar to acquisition through an item, not by means of words. The reason is **that money, which cannot be acquired through** symbolic **exchange,** a pro forma act of acquisition effecting the transfer of ownership of an item, nevertheless **can be acquired by means of land.**

Now the *Gemara* makes its connection. The challenge was: if the *sevara* is that words cannot transfer words and abstraction requires concretization, how can Levi acquire the pre-written *shtar* through the land transfer alone, without any additional document accompanying it? The *shtar* is *mili* — an abstraction — and yet it was transferred without a *shtar* coming with it. Does this not undermine the principle that *mili be-mili lo miknan*?

The *Gemara* answers: *agav shani* — the case of *agav* is different. When the transfer occurs by means of *agav*, through the *kinyan* of land, that is a categorically more powerful mechanism than *mesira*. *Agav* can accomplish what ordinary *mesira* cannot. Therefore, even though *mesira* alone would be insufficient to transfer the abstract debt represented in a *shtar*, *agav* through land can accomplish this. The two cases are not analogous.

### Agav Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב פָּפָּא הֲווֹ לֵיהּ תְּרֵיסַר אַלְפֵי זוּזֵי בֵּי חוֹזָאֵי, אַקְנִינְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ לְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַחָא אַגַּב אַסִּיפָּא דְבֵיתֵיהּ. כִּי אֲתָא, נְפַק לְאַפֵּיהּ עַד תְּווֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like that** incident **where Rav Pappa had** deposited **twelve thousand dinars** with bailees **in Bei Ḥozai. He transferred** ownership of the money in Bei Ḥozai **to** his agent **Rav Shmuel bar Aḥa by means of the threshold of his house.** The Gemara adds: **When** Rav Shmuel bar Aḥa **came** from Bei Ḥozai with the money, Rav Pappa was so happy that he was bringing him his money that he **went out** all the way **until Tavakh to** greet **him.**

One might ask: how do we know that *agav* is indeed so powerful as to work where other *kinyanim* would not? The *Gemara* demonstrates this from the case of coins. Normally, *chalipin* — the exchange-based *kinyan* such as *kinyan sudar* — works on almost any object. But it does not work on coins, because coins occupy a unique category: they are the medium of exchange, not the things exchanged, and as a result most *kinyanim* cannot be used to acquire them. And yet *agav arba niknei* — coins can be acquired through *kinyan agav* along with land. This demonstrates that *agav* is a uniquely powerful *kinyan*, capable of effecting transfers that other modes of acquisition cannot.

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a story. Rav Pappa had twelve thousand *zuz* that were owed to him — or perhaps physically held for him — by the family of Chuzai. *Ve-akni'inhu nihaleh le-Rav Shmuel bar Acha agav asifa de-veitei* — he transferred ownership of this money to Rav Shmuel bar Acha by means of *agav*, using the porch of his house as the piece of land. Rav Shmuel bar Acha was traveling to the location of the Chuzai family and needed to be able to collect the money on Rav Pappa's behalf. But the family would not hand over the money to a mere agent; they needed to be confident that in giving the money to Shmuel bar Acha they were truly and finally paying off their obligation. The only way to achieve that was for Rav Pappa to actually transfer ownership of the money to Shmuel bar Acha, who could then go to the family and say, "Rav Pappa transferred this to me; you now owe it to me, not to him — pay me." And they paid him.

The transfer of the money was accomplished through *kinyan agav*: by Shmuel bar Acha taking possession of the porch, he simultaneously acquired the twelve thousand *zuz* in Chuzai. *Ki ata nafik le-apei ad Tavakh* — when Shmuel bar Acha returned with the money, Rav Pappa went out to greet him all the way to the town of Tavakh, so relieved and grateful was he to have finally recovered the funds.

This story demonstrates that *agav* can transfer even coins, which *chalipin* cannot — proof positive that *agav* is a *kinyan* of exceptional power.

### Final Conclusion

The final conclusion is therefore this: when one wishes to transfer a *shtar chov*, the debt is an abstraction — *otiyot mili ninhu, u-mili be-mili lo miknan* — and *mesira* alone, being itself only a verbal framing of the handing-over of a physical object, cannot transfer what is abstract. To transfer the debt, one needs both *mesira* and *ketiva*: the physical handing over of the document together with a written instrument that explicitly transfers the debt and its liens to the new creditor. *Agav* is powerful enough to overcome this limitation because it is a *kinyan* that operates on a different and higher level — as the case of coins demonstrates — but ordinary *mesira* cannot accomplish the concretization that a written transfer document can. This concludes the discussion of *otiyot niknot be-mesira*, even as the precise *halakhic* conclusion remains subject to the textual uncertainty in the *girsa* of Ameimar's ruling.

## Boat Sale Contents

### Mishna Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא מָכַר לֹא אֶת הָעֲבָדִים וְלֹא אֶת הַמַּרְצוּפִין וְלֹא אֶת הָאַנְתִיקֵי וְכוּ׳. מַאי ״אַנְתִיקֵי״? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: עִיסְקָא דִּבְגַוַּוהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that when one sells a ship he has sold various other items; **but he has not sold the slaves, nor the packing bags, nor the *antikei*.** But when one said to the buyer: You are purchasing the ship and all that it contains, all of these are sold as well. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***antikei*? Rav Pappa said:** It means **the merchandise that is on** the ship. This merchandise is not sold together with the ship.

The *Gemara* now moves on to the *Mishnah*'s ruling about what is and is not included in the sale of a boat. *Aval lo machar lo et ha-avadim ve-lo et ha-mirtzufin ve-lo et ha-antiki* — when one sells a boat, the sale does not include the slaves who work on the boat, nor the *mirtzufin*, the containers or pouches (in modern terms, one might think of the cargo containers), nor the *antiki*.

### Antiki Definition

The *Gemara* asks: *mai antiki*? What is *antiki*? Rav Pappa explains: *iska de-veigava* — it is the actual commodities, the wares being transported and stored within those containers. This is unsurprising: it is self-evident that when one sells a ship, one does not thereby sell the cargo stored aboard it, just as one does not sell the containers themselves. The *Mishnah* is simply being thorough in listing what falls outside the scope of the sale.
