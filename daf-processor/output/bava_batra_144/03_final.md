# Bava Batra 144 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Brothers' Estate Investment

### Minors & Estate Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים גְּדוֹלִים וּקְטַנִּים; הִשְׁבִּיחוּ גְּדוֹלִים אֶת הַנְּכָסִים – הִשְׁבִּיחוּ לָאֶמְצַע. אִם אָמְרוּ: ״רְאוּ מַה שֶּׁהִנִּיחַ אַבָּא, הֲרֵי אָנוּ עוֹשִׂין וְאוֹכְלִים״ – הִשְׁבִּיחוּ לְעַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who** died and **left** behind **adult and minor sons,** if **the adult** sons **enhanced the property, they enhanced** it so that the profit goes **to the middle,** i.e., it is distributed among all the heirs. **If** the adult sons **said** from the outset: **See that which** our **father left** behind; **we are** going to **engage** in business with our share of the property **and profit** from it, then **they enhanced** the property **for themselves.**

The daf opens by continuing the discussion begun at the bottom of 143b concerning an estate left to both older and younger children. Tosafot reads the scenario as one in which the younger children are *ketanim* — minors — and this distinction carries significant halachic weight throughout the sugya.

The basic rule established by the *mishnah* is that if the older brothers invest estate funds, plant fields, or otherwise work the property without making any explicit declaration, we assume they are acting on behalf of the entire estate — even though ordinarily, if one *shutaf* were to work the land of another, he would be entitled to compensation for his efforts. Here, because they are brothers, the default assumption is that everything goes *l'emtza*, to the common estate.

If, however, the older brothers make a declaration — stating, as the Rashbam frames it, that they are acting for their own benefit and are prepared to divide if the others are ready — then whatever appreciation they generate accrues to themselves alone. The question immediately arises: why should such a declaration entitle them to profit from the portion of the estate that belongs to their younger brothers? Tosafot answers that the premise is precisely that the others are *ketanim*, minors who are incapable of investing the money themselves. The older brothers can therefore say: we are not depriving you of anything — without our intervention, your share would simply lie idle. Since the minors have no independent capacity to generate any return, they are not genuinely harmed when the older brothers declare and retain the profit.

Tosafot further cites a *Tosefta* requiring that this declaration be made before a *beit din*. The reason, Tosafot suggests, is that even granting the above logic, there is still something not entirely equitable about the arrangement. The *beit din*, before approving the declaration, would consider whether it is appropriate to appoint an *apotropos* — a guardian — to represent the minors' interests in the investment. By mandating that the declaration pass through *beit din*, the process gains legitimacy: the *beit din* has assessed the situation and concluded that the minors cannot meaningfully participate, and so the older brothers may proceed. This procedure makes the outcome more palatable, even if it cannot be characterized as fully fair in every scenario.

### Rava's Shevach Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב חֲבִיבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁשָּׁבְחוּ נְכָסִים מֵחֲמַת נְכָסִים, אֲבָל שָׁבְחוּ נְכָסִים מֵחֲמַת עַצְמָן – הִשְׁבִּיחוּ לְעַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥaviva, son of Rav Yosef, son of Rava, says in the name of Rava: They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to **where the property was enhanced on account of the property,** i.e., the heirs invested money from the common inheritance in order to enhance the property, and their input was only in the management of its investment. **But** if the **property was enhanced on their account,** i.e., on account of the efforts or expenditure of the adult sons, the *halakha* is that **they enhanced** it **for themselves.**

The Gemara now introduces a qualification attributed to Rava, transmitted through Rabbi Chanina son of Rav Yosef son of Rava: *lo shanu ela shevach nechasim machmat nechasim* — the rule that without a declaration everything goes to the estate applies only when the appreciation of the property came from the property's own resources. That is, if the brothers used estate funds to hire workers, plant the fields, or invest in the market — even though they expended some minimal effort — this is considered *shevach nechasim machmat nechasim*, appreciation generated by the assets themselves, and the default is that it belongs to the entire estate.

But *aval shevach nechasim machmat atzman* — if the appreciation came from the brothers themselves, meaning they personally invested their own money or exerted significant personal effort, working the fields daily in the manner of Boaz supervising his workers, then even without any declaration, the assumption is that they are doing it for themselves. The logic is that a serious personal financial or physical investment constitutes an implicit declaration. In that case, *hishbichu le'atzmam* — they enhanced it for themselves — and of course they could choose to dedicate their efforts to the estate, but absent any such commitment, the assumption runs the other way.

### Udayni Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא הִנִּיחַ לָהֶם אֲבִיהֶם אֶלָּא אוּדְיָינֵי – הַשָּׂכָר לָאֶמְצַע. וְהָא אוּדְיָינֵי, דְּמֵחֲמַת עַצְמוֹ הוּא! שָׁאנֵי אוּדְיָינֵי – דְּלִנְטִירוּתָא הוּא דַּעֲבִידָא, וַאֲפִילּוּ קְטַנִּים נָמֵי מָצוּ מְנַטְּרִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Rabbi Ḥanina say: Even** if **their father left them nothing but** **a mortar [*udaini*],** which people pay to use, **the rent** goes **to the middle? But isn't** the profit accruing from **a mortar** considered profit accruing **on his,** i.e., the adult son's, **own account,** as the adult son must supervise its operation, and there is no outlay from the property? The Gemara dismisses this objection: **A mortar is different, as for** its **typical use** it can suffice **with supervision, and even minors are able to supervise its** use. Since the adults do not contribute anything more than the minors, they cannot demand a greater share of the profits.

The Gemara then challenges this with a statement of Rabbi Yochanan: even if the father left the brothers nothing but an *udayni* — a cistern — the income is *hashachar la'emtza*, shared by the entire estate. But an *udayni* requires someone to stand there, manage the transactions, protect the water — is that not *machmat atzman*, effort from the brothers themselves? Especially since, the Gemara notes, if the entire estate consists of nothing but this cistern, there are no estate funds to hire an operator. The only way the cistern generates income is through the personal effort of one of the brothers.

The Gemara resolves this by distinguishing the *udayni* as a special case: *shani udayni delentiruta hu de'avida* — it requires nothing more than watching, *de'afillu ketanim nami mantru lei* — and even the minors can do that. Here Tosafot's framework becomes decisive. The entire basis for allowing the older brothers to retain the profit — with a declaration or through implicit declaration by investing their own funds — is that the minors are not being deprived of anything, because they could not have done it themselves anyway. But when even children can operate the asset, that justification collapses. The minors can say: we are capable of doing this ourselves; you are actively displacing us from income we could have earned. In such a case the income must be shared, and it remains *la'emtza* regardless.

### Rav Safra Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ ״רְאוּ מַה שֶּׁהִנִּיחַ אַבָּא, הֲרֵי אָנוּ עוֹשִׂין וְאוֹכְלִין״ – הִשְׁבִּיחוּ לְעַצְמָן. רַב סָפְרָא שְׁבַק אֲבוּהּ זוּזֵי. שַׁקְלִינְהוּ, עֲבַד בְּהוּ עִיסְקָא. אֲתוֹ אַחֵי, תַּבְעוּהוּ בְּדִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא; אֲמַר לְהוּ: רַב סָפְרָא גַּבְרָא רַבָּה הוּא, לָא שָׁבֵיק גִּירְסֵיהּ וְטָרַח לְאַחֲרִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the adult sons **said** from the outset: **See that which** our **father left** behind; **we are** going to **engage** in business with our share of the property **and profit** from it, then **they enhanced** the property **for themselves.** The Gemara relates: **Rav Safra's father left** him **dinars.** Rav Safra **took them** and **entered** into **a business venture with them. His brothers came** and **charged him in** a court of **law before Rava,** claiming their share of the profits. Rava **said to them: Rav Safra is a great man; he does not abandon his studies and toil for others.** It is therefore clear that if he invested the money, it was for his own profit, even if he did not explicitly declare so at the outset.

The Gemara introduces the case of Rav Safra. His father died and left him an inheritance — estate money. Rav Safra took that money and did business with it, *avad behu iska*. On its face this is *shevach nechasim machmat nechasim*: he used the estate's own funds, not his personal money. He made a profit, and the question is whether his brothers are entitled to share in it. The brothers came before Rava and demanded their portion.

Rava ruled in Rav Safra's favor: *Rav Safra gavra raba hu* — Rav Safra is a great personage — *velo shavik girseih vetarach leachrinei* — he does not abandon his learning to exert himself on behalf of others. Every moment taken away from his Torah study is precious; he works only to support himself. The Rashbam explains that this is considered *shevach nechasim machmat nechasim*, because what Rav Safra did was essentially buy and sell — *liknot velimkor* — which does not require, as the Rashbam puts it, an enormous amount of *torech*, of effortful exertion. The case is analogous to making a stock investment: the intellectual discernment to identify a good opportunity is real, but the physical effort is minimal.

At the same time, Rava's reasoning introduces something conceptually distinct: because it is well known that Rav Safra would never divert time from his learning to do work for others, any effort he invests carries an implicit declaration that he is acting for himself. He does not need to say so explicitly; his entire character and conduct broadcast the message. The Rashbam generalizes from this, stating: *ushma'inan meihacha dechol heicha deman dehu kemo Rav Safra* — we learn from here that anyone who resembles Rav Safra in this respect, even without explicitly saying "I am doing this for myself," is treated as though he made that declaration, and the appreciation belongs to him.

This raises a pointed question: is this standard limited to *talmidei chachamim* who never take time from learning except for their own sustenance, or does it extend to any individual who demonstrably never acts for the benefit of his brothers? If a brother is known to be completely indifferent to his siblings' welfare, acting always for his own gain, should that track record constitute the same implicit declaration? The text does not resolve this. And one might also note the tension in the ruling: the older brothers are using estate funds — funds that belong in part to their siblings — to enrich themselves. It is not merely that they fail to give *tzedakah*; they are leveraging communal assets for personal profit.

### Rashbam on Rav Safra

The Rashbam's analysis establishes that what functions as an implicit declaration is not merely the investment of personal funds but any circumstance that makes it apparent the person could not plausibly be acting on behalf of others. For Rav Safra, it is the standing reality that a scholar of his stature would not sacrifice Torah study for anyone else's financial benefit. The Rashbam codifies this as a general principle: *af al gav delo amar le'atzmi ani osek keman de'amar lehu vehishbiach le'atzmo* — even without saying "I am doing this for myself," such a person is treated as though he said it, and the appreciation is his.

The sugya thus presents a continuum. At one end is the *udayni*, which requires so little effort that even minors can operate it, and the income must always be shared. Somewhere in the middle is the ordinary business investment using estate funds, where the default is sharing unless a declaration is made or personal funds are invested. At the other end is a figure like Rav Safra, whose personal profile itself constitutes the declaration.

### Woman Heir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחָה אֶת הַנְּכָסִים – הִשְׁבִּיחָה לָאֶמְצַע. אִם אָמְרָה: ״רְאוּ מַה שֶּׁהִנִּיחַ לִי בַּעְלִי, הֲרֵי אֲנִי עוֹשָׂה וְאוֹכֶלֶת״ – הִשְׁבִּיחָה לְעַצְמָהּ. גְּמָ׳ הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחָה אֶת הַנְּכָסִים – הִשְׁבִּיחָה לָאֶמְצַע. אִשָּׁה בְּנִכְסֵי יַתְמֵי מַאי עֲבִידְתַּהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בְּאִשָּׁה יוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And similarly,** with regard to **a wife who enhanced the property** of her deceased husband, **she enhanced** it so that the profit goes **to the middle,** i.e., it is divided between her and the heirs. **If she said: See that which my husband left me; I am** going to **engage** in business with my share **and profit** from it, then **she enhanced** the property **for herself.** GEMARA: § The mishna teaches: With regard to **a wife who enhanced the property** of her deceased husband, **she enhanced** it so that the profit goes **to the middle.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the deceased's **wife doing with** the **orphans' property?** What rights does she have to the property? **Rav Yirmeya said:** The mishna is referring **to a wife** who is **an heiress,** e.g., if she were the daughter of her husband's brother, and both brothers died without leaving sons, in which case she inherits in her father's stead.

The Gemara now extends these rules to the case of a woman among the heirs. The case reads: *hahi isha d'ashbacha nechasim ashbacha l'emtza* — a woman who enhanced the estate property, the enhancement goes to the middle, to the estate. If she declares that she is acting for herself, she keeps it. But the Gemara asks: *isha b'nichsei yatmi mah avidta* — what is a woman doing as an heir among orphans? Rabbi Yirmiya answers: *b'isha yoreshet* — she is a *yoreshet*, an heir.

The Rashbam identifies two possible scenarios. The simpler case is that the deceased wrote a will through the instrument of *matnat shechiv mera*, a deathbed gift, designating his wife as an equal co-inheritor together with his children. This was discussed in the preceding daf. She thus becomes a full participant in the estate alongside the sons, and all the same rules apply to her as apply to the older brothers vis-à-vis the younger ones.

The more complex scenario involves inheritance by operation of law. Consider: Reuven marries his niece — the daughter of his brother Shimon. Their father Yaakov dies, leaving his estate jointly to Reuven and Shimon. The estate is never divided. Then Shimon dies, and his daughter inherits his share. Then Reuven dies, leaving his sons. Now Reuven's sons and the woman — their mother, who inherited from her father Shimon — are co-heirs in the same estate of Yaakov. They find themselves as joint inheritors through entirely distinct lines of succession, yet participating together in what is structurally a single estate.

This scenario also provokes a broader question: the concept of *tfisat habayit*, the estate as a corporate entity, has been the animating framework throughout this section. Does that entity arise only from standard *yerusha*, from ordinary inheritance by legal heirs? Or can it also arise from *matnat shechiv mera*? If a man distributes his estate by deathbed gift to a group of people who are not natural heirs — even complete strangers — can they constitute a *tfisat habayit* in the same sense? The answer would seem to depend on how the gift is framed. If specific assets are assigned individually to specific people, there is no shared estate — each person simply owns a parcel. But if the dying man designates a group of people collectively as inheritors of the whole estate, then a corporate entity seems to arise, perhaps even among strangers. The Gemara's framing of the woman as a *yoreshet* alongside the children is consistent with this reading.

Tosafot offers a variant of the second scenario that the lecturer acknowledges is difficult to reconstruct fully, but the principle is the same: the woman is a *yoreshet* together with brothers or other male heirs, and the rules of the estate apply to her equally.

### Woman's Unusual Effort

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו דַּרְכַּהּ לְמִטְרַח, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא פָּרֵישׁ – כְּמוֹ דְּפָרֵישׁ דָּמֵי, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאִם אָמְרָה: ״רְאוּ מַה שֶּׁהִנִּיחַ לִי בַּעְלִי, הֲרֵינִי עוֹשָׂה וְאוֹכֶלֶת״ – הִשְׁבִּיחָה לְעַצְמָהּ. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁבִיחָא לַהּ מִילְּתָא, דְּאָמְרִי: קָא טָרְחָא קַמֵּי יַתְמֵי – אַחוֹלֵי אַחֲלָה; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that the *halakha* in this case is the same as with regard to any other heir? The Gemara replies: It is necessary to state this, **lest you say** that **since it is not** a woman's **way to toil** to enhance property, then **even though she did not expressly state** that she is toiling for herself, **it is considered as though** she **had expressly stated** this. The mishna **teaches us** that only if she expressly stated this do the profits accrue to her. § The mishna teaches: **And if she said: See that which my husband left me; I am** going to **engage** in business with my share **and profit** from it, then **she enhanced** the property **for herself.** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** The Gemara explains: It is necessary to state this, **lest you say** that **since** it **is praiseworthy for her** to enhance the property on behalf of the orphans, **as** people will **say:** See how **she toils for the orphans, she relinquishes** her right to the profits and they should divide them equally. The mishna **teaches us** that this is not the case.

The Gemara entertains a possible distinction between the woman and the male heirs. One might have thought — *mahu d'teima, keivan delav darchah lemitrach* — since it is not typical for a woman to go out and engage in business activity, if she does go to that unusual effort, we should treat it analogously to Rav Safra: just as Rav Safra's deviation from his norm of Torah study signals that he is acting for himself, a woman's deviation from the norm of not engaging in commerce should signal that she is acting for herself. On this theory, even without a declaration, her effort would generate profit for herself alone.

The Gemara rejects this: *kama mashma lan* — the law teaches us that without a declaration, her *shevach* still goes to the estate, just as with the brothers. Her unusual effort does not by itself constitute an implicit declaration.

The Gemara further considers the converse: if she does make an explicit declaration — *re'u mah she-hiniach li ba'ali ha-reini osah ve-ochelet* — does she nonetheless keep it? One might argue that since a woman who goes out and does business on behalf of orphans earns significant public admiration — people will say, look how this woman sacrifices herself for the heirs — she may actually want to be seen as doing it for them, and her earlier declaration is therefore overridden by her subsequent desire for that acclaim. The Gemara rejects this as well: *kama mashma lan* — an explicit declaration stands, and we do not presume that public praise retroactively dissolves it. She made her declaration; she keeps the profit.

## Father's House Gift

### House Gift Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הַמַּשִּׂיא אִשָּׁה לִבְנוֹ גָּדוֹל בַּבַּיִת – קְנָאוֹ. וְדַוְקָא גָּדוֹל, וְדַוְקָא בְּתוּלָה, וְדַוְקָא אִשְׁתּוֹ רִאשׁוֹנָה, וְדַוְקָא שֶׁהִשִּׂיאוֹ רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** In the case of **one who marries a woman to his eldest son** and arranges the wedding feast **in a house** that he designated for the purpose of the wedding canopy and the wedding feast, the son **acquires** the house as a gift. The Gemara notes: **And** this is the *halakha* **specifically** in the case of the **eldest** son, **and specifically** if he is marrying **a virgin, and specifically** when this woman is **his first wife, and specifically when** the father **married him off first** among his sons. When all these conditions exist, it is presumed that the father has a particular fondness toward the son, due to which he gave him the house.

Rabbi Chanina states: *hamasi isha livno hagadol bevayit kna'o* — one who marries off his oldest son and holds the wedding in a house has acquired it for the son, meaning we presume the father has gifted the house to the son. The Gemara adds a series of conditions: this presumption operates only for the oldest son (*davka gadol*), only when the bride is a virgin (*davka besulah*), only when it is the son's first marriage (*davka isha rishona*), and only when the father is marrying off this son first. The rationale is that the combination of all these firsts — oldest son, first wedding for both — makes this such a significant occasion that we interpret the father's gesture as a genuine gift. A further limitation not explicit in this passage is that the father must have another house in the same town, so that the gift does not leave him without a residence.

### Limitations & Rooms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, יִיחֵד לוֹ אָבִיו בַּיִת וַעֲלִיָּיה – בַּיִת קָנָה, עֲלִיָּיה לֹא קָנָה. בַּיִת וְאַכְסַדְרָה, מַהוּ? שְׁנֵי בָתִּים זֶה לִפְנִים מִזֶּה, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the details of this *halakha*: It is **obvious** that if **his father designated a house for** the son's nuptials **and** there is **an upper story** above the house, the son **acquired the house,** but **he did not acquire the upper story.** If he designated **a house** for the son, **and** there is **a portico [*ve'akhsadra*]** in front of the house, **what is** the *halakha*? If there were **two houses, one within the other,** and the inner one was designated for the son's nuptials, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara concludes: These questions **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara begins qualifying the scope of the gift. If the father designated a house with an upper story and held the wedding in the lower story, *bayit kanah aliyah lo kanah* — the son acquires the lower story but not the upper. The gift extends only to the space in which the wedding took place. The Gemara raises further cases: if there was a house and a portico (*achsandra*), and the wedding was held in the house proper, does the son also acquire the portico? What if there were two houses, one accessible only through the other — does the son acquire both? These cases are left as *teiku*, unresolved.

### Dishes vs House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: יִיחֵד לוֹ אָבִיו בַּיִת וּכְלֵי בַיִת – כְּלֵי בַיִת קָנָה, בַּיִת לֹא קָנָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **his father designated a house and furniture for his** son's nuptials, the son **acquired the furniture,** but **he did not acquire the house.**

A *beraita* is then cited that appears to contradict the basic presumption. The *beraita* says: if the father designated a house and household dishes for the wedding — *bayit u-chlei bayit* — the son acquires the dishes (*klei bayis kana*) but not the house (*bayis lo kana*). This is precisely the reverse of the earlier ruling, which assumed the house is gifted.

### Father's Storage Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיָה אוֹצָרוֹ שֶׁל אָבִיו מוּנָּח שָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya says:** This is referring to a case **where his father's storeroom was placed there.** It is clear that just as his father did not intend to give him the storeroom, neither did he intend to give him the house.

Rabbi Yirmiya resolves the contradiction: *kegon dehayu otzaros shel aviv munachim sham* — the *beraita* refers to a case where the father's storage remained in the house. The continued presence of the father's belongings in the house signals that he has retained possession; he has not relinquished it. By contrast, the dishes designated for the wedding belong to the son because there is no such countervailing signal regarding them.

### Small Item Negates Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹבָכָא דְיוֹנֵי. רַב יְהוּדָה וְרַב פַּפֵּי אָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ עֲצִיצָא דְהַרְסָנָא. מָר זוּטְרָא אַנְסְבֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ, וּתְלָא לֵיהּ סַנְדָּלָא. רַב אָשֵׁי אַנְסְבֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ, וּתְלָא לֵיהּ אֲשִׁישָׁא דְמִשְׁחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of Neharde'a say: Even** if there is only **a dovecote** in the house that belongs to the father, the son does not acquire the house. **Rav Yehuda and Rav Pappi say: Even** if there is only **a pot [*atzitza*] of** small **fried fish,** the son does not acquire the house. **Mar Zutra married off his son, and hung a sandal** in the house, to indicate that he did not intend to give the house as a gift. **Rav Ashi married off his son, and hung a jug [*ashisha*] of oil** in the house.

The Gemara then dramatically limits what constitutes "the father's storage." It need not be a warehouse of grain filling the entire house. The Nardeans taught: *afilu shuvcha deyonei* — even if the father left only a pigeon coop there, that suffices to negate the presumption of a gift. Rav Yehuda bar Pappa extended this further: *afilu atzitza d'harsana* — even a small bowl of fish hash left behind is enough. The Amoraim drew the practical conclusion immediately. Mar Zutra, when marrying off his son, simply left a sandal hanging on a peg in the house — *v'tala lei sandala*. Rav Ashi, when marrying off his son, left a small pot of oil — *v'tala lei ashisha d'mishcha*. Both thereby retained ownership of the house. What began as a sweeping presumption of generosity has been qualified almost to the point of nullification: the father need leave almost nothing in the house to demonstrate that the gift was never made.

### Arbitrary Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא, הָנֵי תְּלָת מִילֵּי שַׁוִּינְהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּהִלְכְתָא בְּלָא טַעְמָא:
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra says: These three matters were instituted by the Sages as a *halakha* without any explanation** of their process, i.e., they instituted these enactments despite the fact that the mechanism by which they function is unclear:

Mar Zutra comments: *hanei tlat mili shavinhu rabanan k'hilchasa b'lo tama* — the following three laws the Rabbis established as halacha that seem arbitrary, lacking obvious derivation. Tosafot qualifies this by noting that the laws are not entirely without reason — they only appear somewhat arbitrary. The first is the presumption we just discussed, that the father who makes a wedding for his oldest son in a house is presumed to have gifted it. The second is the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *hakosev kol nechasav l'ishto lo asaa ela apitropah* — one who writes over all his property to his wife has merely made her an executor of the estate, not an outright beneficiary, because we presume he would not entirely disinherit his children. The third is the rule of *ma'amad sheloshtan*: if Reuven says to Shimon, "That hundred zuz you owe me — give it to Levi," and all three are present, Levi acquires the debt. This is a mechanism for transferring an abstract monetary obligation through words alone — without a formal *kinyan* — by virtue of all three parties being present together.

## Estate Partnership Jobs

### Job Profit Sharing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַחִין הַשּׁוּתָּפִין שֶׁנָּפַל אֶחָד מֵהֶן לְאוּמָּנוּת – נָפַל לָאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **brothers who were** also **partners,** and it occurred **that one of them was summoned to** public **service,** which is assessed per family, **he was summoned from the middle,** i.e., the profits or expenses of his service are divided among them.

The *mishnah* now turns to brothers who are partners in an estate — *achim hashotfin* — the shared estate functioning as a kind of corporate entity, as close as halacha comes to the concept of a corporation, with all brothers as equal shareholders. The *mishnah* states: *shenafal echad mehem l'umanut* — if one of them is appointed to a position or gets a job, *nafal l'emtza* — the income goes to the estate. The intuition here seems counterintuitive: this brother is earning his own money; why should his income from outside employment be shared simply because the brothers have not yet divided the estate?

The Gemara will limit this: the rule applies only when he obtained the position because of the family connection. If he would not have been hired but for being a member of this particular family, the income belongs to the estate.

### King's Service Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: הַאי אוּמָּנוּת – לְאוּמָּנוּת הַמֶּלֶךְ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֶחָד מִן הָאַחִין שֶׁמִּינּוּהוּ גַּבַּאי אוֹ פּוּלְמוֹסְטוּס; אִם מֵחֲמַת הָאַחִין – לָאַחִין, אִם מֵחֲמַת עַצְמוֹ – לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sages **taught: This service** mentioned in the mishna is referring **to** forced **imperial service,** but if one of the brothers engaged in a trade of his own volition, the profits are his alone. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 10:5): In the case of **one of the brothers who was appointed** as **a tax collector or a military commander [*polmustos*],** a position with the potential for profit, **if** he was appointed **on account of** all **the brothers,** as the procedure was to impress a representative from each family for this purpose, any profit accrues **to** all **the brothers. If** he was appointed **on account of himself,** the profit accrues **to himself.**

A *baraita* teaches that the relevant case is *umanut hamelech* — service in the king's employ. The Rashbam explains that certain families held rotating positions of service to the king — appointed on a family rotation, one member serving in the family's turn. Because the appointment came to any member of the family by virtue of the family's standing, whoever happened to serve in that rotation was in effect working on the family's behalf. The result is shared income, even though only one brother physically performed the service. It is true that this brother could not lie on the beach during his term of service; one might expect some compensation for his lost time and opportunity. But the Gemara does not address this nuance, and the basic rule is that because the appointment derived from the family connection, the income belongs to the estate.

### Personal vs Family Job

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם מֵחֲמַת אַחִין לָאַחִין – פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּחָרִיף טְפֵי; מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: חוּרְפֵּיהּ גָּרֵים לֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that **if** he was appointed **on account of** all **the brothers,** then the profit accrues **to** all **the brothers?** The Gemara responds: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case **where** the brother appointed was **sharper,** i.e., more capable, than the other brothers. **Lest you say** that **his sharpness caused him** to be selected for the position, and he should receive all the profits, the *baraita* **teaches us** that his talent notwithstanding, since he was selected as a representative of the family, the profit accrues to all the brothers.

The *baraita* draws the governing distinction clearly: *im machmat ha'achim — la'achim* — if the appointment came because of the brothers' family standing, the income is shared. *Im machmat atzmo — le'atzmo* — if the appointment came because of the individual's own demonstrated qualities, the income is his alone. If the king's court appointed this brother because it recognized his personal brilliance, his character, or his independently established reputation — not merely because he is a member of family X — then the income belongs to him personally.

### Ambiguous Job Cases

The *baraita* anticipates an ambiguous case. Suppose the family would ordinarily have had a candidate appointed, but this particular brother is also *charif* — unusually sharp and capable. The Gemara asks: *mahu d'teima churfeh garam leh* — perhaps one should say it was his personal sharpness that caused the appointment? The Gemara answers that we do not say this: if the family connection was itself a sufficient basis for the appointment, we attribute the appointment to the family and the income goes to the estate. The Gemara's default is to assume that connections, rather than individual talent, drove the outcome — a presumption, the lecturer notes, that reflects how the world often works. Of course, when both factors are genuinely intertwined and neither would have sufficed alone, the categorization remains difficult, and the sugya does not offer a definitive resolution for that scenario.

## Brother Leaves Estate

### Support Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֶחָד מִן הָאַחִין שֶׁנָּטַל מָאתַיִם זוּז לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה אוֹ לִלְמוֹד אוּמָּנוּת – יְכוֹלִין הָאַחִין לוֹמַר לוֹ: אִם אַתָּה אֶצְלֵנוּ – יֵשׁ לְךָ מְזוֹנוֹת, אִם אֵין אַתָּה אֶצְלֵנוּ – אֵין לְךָ מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one of the brothers, who took two hundred dinars** from the common inheritance to support himself when he went to another place **to study Torah or to learn a trade, the brothers can say to him: If you are** here **with us, you are entitled to sustenance** along with us. **If you are not** here **with us, you are not entitled to sustenance.**

The *mishnah* and accompanying *baraita* address a case in which one brother leaves the shared estate to go learn a trade or study: *echad min ha'achim shenatal matayim zuz lamasher v'lilmod amnut* — one of the brothers took two hundred *zuz* to go engage in commerce or learn a craft. The brothers may say to him: *im attah etzlenu yesh lecha mezonot* — as long as you are living with us, you receive your food from the estate; *ve'im ein attah etzlenu ein lecha mezonot* — if you are not living here, you do not. This ruling reflects the presumption that the estate's obligation of *mezonot* is tied to communal residence on the estate property. In the pre-modern world presupposed by the Gemara, the brothers are all living together on the father's property after his death, sharing food, clothing, and expenses from the common estate. The one who leaves that framework exits the support structure.

Several questions surrounding this case remain unaddressed by the Gemara: Was it the departing brother's own initiative to leave, or did the other brothers send him? Is the two hundred *zuz* he took relevant to the support question — did receiving startup funds waive his right to ongoing *mezonot*? What if he took nothing? The sugya does not resolve these questions.

### Rav Huna Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְּבוּ לֵיהּ כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ! מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בִּרְכַּת הַבַּיִת בְּרוּבָּה. וְלִיתְּבוּ לֵיהּ לְפִי בִּרְכַּת הַבַּיִת! הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But shouldn't they give him** his sustenance **wherever he is?** The Gemara answers: This ruling **supports** the opinion **of Rav Huna, as Rav Huna says: The blessing of the house** is **in its abundance.** This means that the extent to which blessing permeates a home is proportionate to the number of people who live there, and when many people live together the expenses per capita decrease. The Gemara asks: **But shouldn't they give him** his sustenance **according to the blessing of the house,** i.e., the expenses he would incur even if he were in the house? The Gemara responds: **Indeed,** they are required to provide for those expenses that he would incur in any event.

The Gemara asks: since the estate is obligated to support this brother regardless, why not simply pay his food costs wherever he happens to be? The Gemara answers that this supports a principle of Rav Huna: *birchas habayis berovah* — the blessing of a house depends on its population. The more people living in a household, the greater the prosperity of that household. The brother's absence therefore genuinely costs the estate something; his presence would have increased the household's fortune. Since his departure causes a loss to the remaining brothers, they are not required to support him away from home.

The Gemara then asks: if that is so, why not simply pay him according to the proportional *birchas habayis* he would have contributed — deducting that amount from his support? The Gemara accepts this: *hachi nami* — indeed, that is how it is calculated.

### Tosafot Cost Correction

Tosafot objects to the entire framework of *birchas habayis* as a basis for calculating the support reduction. How does one quantify the blessing a single person adds to a household? If the household income happened to rise in his absence and fall in his presence, does that mean his presence was a curse? Tosafot therefore proposes a different and more straightforward economic explanation. Supporting a large family is cheaper per capita than supporting an individual separately. If you are already feeding nine people and you add a tenth, you simply add a piece of chicken to the pot — the marginal cost is low. But to support that person entirely independently, in a separate household, is far more expensive. The estate's obligation is therefore measured by what it actually costs to add this person to the existing household, not by what it costs him to live on his own. The Gemara's resolution — pay his proportional share — is then understood as: pay the additional cost his presence in the house would have incurred, which is lower than independent living costs. This is a more tractable and reasonable calculation.

## Medical Expenses

### Negligence vs Accident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חָלָה וְנִתְרַפֵּא – נִתְרַפֵּא מִשֶּׁל עַצְמוֹ. גְּמָ׳ חָלָה וְנִתְרַפֵּא – נִתְרַפֵּא מִשֶּׁל עַצְמוֹ. שְׁלַח רָבִין מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אִלְעָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁחָלָה בִּפְשִׁיעָה, אֲבָל בְּאוֹנֶס – נִתְרַפֵּא מִן הָאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one of the brothers **became sick and sought treatment, the** cost of the **treatment is** paid **from his own** resources. GEMARA: § The mishna teaches: If one of the brothers **became sick and sought treatment, the** cost of the **treatment is** paid **from his own** resources. **Ravin sent** a ruling **in the name of Rabbi Ela: They taught** this **only** in a case **where he became ill through negligence. But** if he became ill **through circumstances beyond his control,** the cost of **the treatment is** paid **from the middle,** i.e., from the common inheritance.

The *mishnah* states: *chala venitrapa nitrapa mishel atzmo* — if a brother falls ill and recovers, the medical expenses come from his own funds, not from the estate. This is surprising given that the estate covers food and clothing for all the brothers. If the brothers are all living off the estate as a corporate unit, why should illness be treated differently?

The Gemara cites Ravin in the name of Rabbi Ila'i from Eretz Yisrael, who qualifies the *mishnah*: *lo shanu ela she-chala bifshia* — the ruling that the brother pays his own medical expenses applies only when the illness was caused by his own *peshiah* — his own negligence. But *be-ones* — if the illness came upon him through no fault of his own, through circumstances entirely beyond his control — then *venitrapa min ha-emtza*, the medical expenses are paid by the estate.

### Heaven Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי בִּפְשִׁיעָה? כִּדְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הַכֹּל בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם, חוּץ מִצִּנִּים פַּחִים; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צִנִּים פַּחִים בְּדֶרֶךְ עִקֵּשׁ, שׁוֹמֵר נַפְשׁוֹ יִרְחַק מֵהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances in** which it is considered **negligence?** This is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Ḥanina, as Rabbi Ḥanina says: All** occurrences that befall man **are in the hand of Heaven except for colds** and **obstacles [*paḥim*],** from which one is able to protect himself, **as** it **is stated: "Colds and snares are on the path of the crooked; he who guards his soul shall keep far from them"** (Proverbs 22:5).

What constitutes *peshiah*? The Gemara defines it by invoking a famous teaching of Rabbi Chanina: *hakol biyedei shamayim chutz mitzinim u-pachim* — everything is in the hands of heaven except *tzinim u-pachim*. Everything that befalls a person is ultimately *bashert*, divinely ordained — except for cold and *pachim*. *Pachim* can mean pits: falling into a pit because one was not watching where one walked. It can also mean heat, as Tosafot suggests, linking *pachim* to *pecham*, coal or burning. Regardless, the pasuk states: *tzinim pachim be-derech ikesh shomer nafsho yirchak me-hem* — cold and *pachim* are in the path of the crooked; one who guards himself keeps away from them. These are the paradigm of self-inflicted harm: going out into the cold without a coat, walking without watching where one steps. These result from negligence, not from divine decree, and the person is therefore responsible for the resulting medical expenses.

A normal illness — an ordinary cold contracted under normal circumstances — would be an *ones*, and the estate would cover it. By contrast, if someone went out in a snowstorm without a coat and fell ill, that is *peshiah*. The line between *ones* and *peshiah* is not always sharp; one might ski carefully and still be struck by another skier, so that even a dangerous recreational choice is not automatically negligent in every moment of its execution.

### Chronic vs Acute

Tosafot limits the scope of this distinction significantly. The *Tosefta* teaches: *bameh devarim amurim bi-refuah she-yesh lah kitzvah* — when is it that we apply the *peshiah/ones* distinction? Only when the illness requires a finite course of treatment — a discrete medical expense with a beginning and an end. *Aval bi-refuah she-ein lah kitzvah* — but if the illness is ongoing, requiring indefinite treatment, *harei hi bikhlal mezonot viyisrefu min ha-emtza* — it is treated as part of the brother's ongoing sustenance and is paid from the estate, just as food is. Chronic illness, in this framework, is not categorically different from food: just as every person requires nourishment as an ongoing condition of life, a person with a chronic condition requires ongoing treatment. The estate covers both.

Tosafot then anticipates an objection from earlier in the *masechta*: we were told that brothers cannot claim higher food allocations on the basis of greater appetite or need — each receives an equal share. How then can one brother's chronic illness generate disproportionate expenses covered by the estate? Tosafot answers that the equality principle applies only to ordinary *mezonot*, food costs that are discretionary in some sense. Ongoing medical necessity is different in kind and is always covered.

This raises a further difficulty that the Gemara itself creates. The Gemara limits estate liability to cases of *ones*; *peshiah* is the individual's own responsibility. But Tosafot's rule adds: if the condition is chronic, it is covered regardless. Does that mean that if someone was negligent — *peshiah* — but the resulting condition became chronic, the estate must pay? That conclusion seems counterintuitive: if the person's own negligence is what generates the liability, why should chronicity change the analysis? Tosafot's position here is not entirely resolved, and the sugya leaves this tension without a full answer.

## Reciprocal Gifts

### Formal Wedding Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאַחִין שֶׁעָשׂוּ מִקְצָתָן שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב, חָזְרָה שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת – חָזְרָה לָאֶמְצַע; שֶׁהַשּׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת נִגְבֵּית בְּבֵית דִּין. אֲבָל הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ לַחֲבֵירוֹ כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן – אֵין נִגְבִּין בְּבֵית דִּין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It was common practice for friends of a groom to give him gifts in order to help cover the expenses of the wedding feast. These gifts are known as gifts of groomsmen, and would be reciprocated in turn. While the groom and the groomsman were at times the recipient and the giver of the gifts, respectively, the gifts were at times provided by the father of the groomsman and received by the father of the groom. In the case of **brothers, some of whom brought gifts of groomsmen in their father's lifetime,** which were provided by their father, when **the gifts of groomsmen are reciprocated** after the father's death, when one of the brothers gets married, **they are reciprocated to the middle,** i.e., the gift is divided among the brothers. This is **because gifts of groomsmen are** a legal debt owed to the father, **collectible in court. But** with regard to **one who sends his friend jugs of wine or jugs of oil,** a reciprocal gift **is not collectible in court, because they are** considered **acts of kindness.**

The final *mishnah* of the daf introduces the laws of *shoshvinut* — the formal system of reciprocal wedding gifts. When one family sends representatives to another family's wedding bearing gifts, there exists a recognized social and legal obligation to reciprocate in kind when the occasion arises. The *mishnah* poses the following case: some of the brothers, while the father was still alive, went and represented the family at a neighbor's or friend's wedding, bringing *shoshvinut* gifts. The father has since died. Now that same family is sending *shoshvinut* back to the estate, on the occasion of one of the brothers' weddings. Does this reciprocal gift belong to that particular son whose wedding it is, or does it belong to the estate?

The answer is that *chazar shoshbinut chazar la'emtza* — the returned *shoshvinut* goes to the estate. The reasoning is that *shoshbinut nigvis b'veit din* — *shoshvinut* is a formal legal obligation, collectible through *beit din*. The gift brought in the father's time was brought on behalf of the family as a whole; the obligation to reciprocate is therefore owed to the estate, and the estate is the proper recipient. The fact that the occasion for repayment happens to be this particular son's wedding does not transform the gift into his personal property. It is the discharge of a debt owed to the estate, and it flows back to the estate accordingly.

### Informal Gifts

The *mishnah* draws a sharp contrast with informal acts of generosity. *Aval hashaleach l'chavero kad yayin v'chad shemen ein nigvin b'veit din mipnei shehen gemilus chasadim* — but one who sent a neighbor a jug of wine and a jug of oil, which were not sent as formal *shoshvinut* at a *chuppah* but simply as a gift — such an act of *gemilus chasadim* is not collectible through *beit din*, and its reciprocation is treated as a personal gift to the recipient, not as a payment to the estate.

The distinction tracks the difference between legal obligation and social kindness. *Shoshvinut* at a *chuppah* is a formal, legally enforceable commitment; it creates a debt. When someone sends wine and oil as a *Chanukah* gift, there is no legal obligation to reciprocate, even if the social expectation is that one will. When the recipient of that earlier kindness later sends a reciprocal gift back — addressed to the specific person who had shown him that generosity — it is a personal gift to that individual, not to the estate. The estate has no claim on it, because no debt was created in the first instance.
