# Sanhedrin 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Overview

### Previous Topics

The daf under discussion is 102b, and with it we transition into a new section of *Perek Chelek*. By way of orientation, the opening portion of the perek addressed *olam ha-ba* and *techiyas ha-meisim* — the denial of resurrection as a disqualification from the World to Come — along with *yemos ha-mashiach* and ancillary topics such as one who pronounces the Divine Name in its letters, the category of *apikoros* (which, for the Gemara, refers not to a dogmatic heretic in the Rambam's sense but to one who disrespects rabbinic authority), and the prohibition against *sefarim chitzonim*, which led into an extended discussion touching on Ben Sira.

### Second Mishnah Intro

We now arrive at the second *mishnah* of *Perek Chelek* — printed in most editions as a distinct *mishnah* — which begins to identify specific individuals who have no portion in the World to Come. The *mishnah* states that three kings and four commoners (*hedyotos*) are excluded. The three kings are *Yerovam ben Nevat*, *Achav*, and *Menashe*.

### Three Kings Focus

A significant portion of the Gemara's analysis focuses on each of these three kings in turn, examining both the textual basis for their exclusion and the deeper moral and historical dimensions of their failures. Strikingly, the discussion is not a simple catalogue of wickedness; it also probes the greatness these figures once possessed, why they merited positions of leadership in the first place, and what ultimately led to their downfall.

## Yerovam Etymology

### Yerovam Name Play

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָרׇבְעָם – שֶׁרִיבֵּעַ עָם. דָּבָר אַחֵר: יָרׇבְעָם – שֶׁעָשָׂה מְרִיבָה בָּעָם. דָּבָר אַחֵר: יָרׇבְעָם – שֶׁעָשָׂה מְרִיבָה בֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל לַאֲבִיהֶם שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Jeroboam [*Yorovam*]** is an abbreviation for one **who debased** the Jewish **people [*riba am*]. Alternatively, *Yorovam*** is an abbreviation for one **who engendered strife among the people [*meriva ba'am*],** causing the schism between the kingdoms of Judea and Israel. **Alternatively, *Yorovam*** is an abbreviation for one **who engendered strife between the Jewish people [*meriva bein ha'am*] and their Heavenly Father,** as he instituted the worship of the golden calves (I Kings 12:28–31).

The Gemara opens its treatment of *Yerovam* with a series of etymological plays on his name. *Tanu Rabbanan*: *Yerovam* — *she-riva am*, he trampled the people. Rashi understands the root *rova* in this context as connoting trampling or, alternatively, as suggesting sexual domination, though the more natural reading here is simply that he abused and downtrodden the people. A second interpretation: *she-asah merivah ba-am* — he created strife and contention among the people, splitting the nation in two. A third: *she-asah merivah bein Yisrael le-avihem she-bashamayim* — he drove a wedge between the people of Israel and their Father in Heaven, leading the ten tribes into *avoda zara* through the golden calves. As the Gemara will elaborate, while the division of the kingdom itself was prophetically ordained and carried out through the word of a *navi*, it was the manner in which Yerovam led the people astray that constituted his singular transgression.

### Ben Nevat Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן נְבָט – בֵּן שֶׁנִּיבַּט וְלֹא רָאָה. תַּנָּא: הוּא נְבָט, הוּא מִיכָה, הוּא שֶׁבַע בֶּן בִּכְרִי. נְבָט – שֶׁנִּיבַּט וְלֹא רָאָה. מִיכָה – שֶׁנִּתְמַכְמֵךְ בְּבִנְיָן. וּמָה שְׁמוֹ? שֶׁבַע בֶּן בִּכְרִי שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** He is called **son of Nebat** because he is the **son who looked [*nibat*]** in an effort to assess the situation **but did not see** the situation accurately. It is **taught** in a *baraita*: Based on a homiletic interpretation of their names, these three biblical figures are deemed to be the same person. **He is** called **Nebat, he is** called **Micah,** and **he is** called **Sheba, son of Bichri. Nebat, who looked [*nibat*] but did not see,** believed that he was destined for greatness, and that was achieved only by his son. **Micah, who was crushed [*nitmakhmekh*] in the building** of the storage cities of Pithom and Raamses, was miraculously saved. **And what is his** actual **name? His name is Sheba, son of Bichri.**

The Gemara then turns to the patronymic: *ben Nevat* — *ben she-nibat ve-lo ra'ah*, a son of one who saw but did not truly see. Nevat perceived a vision whose import he failed to grasp. A *tana* teaches that Nevat, Micha, and Sheva ben Bichri are all the same person — that is, the father of Yerovam goes by multiple names. The name *Micha* derives from *she-nismachmach ba-binyan*: according to the *midrash* cited by Rashi, when Moshe observed Jewish infants being embedded in the walls of Egyptian construction projects and protested to God, God responded that these children would grow up to be *resha'im* in any case. One child was saved — Micha — who later became the source of idolatrous influence in Israel for generations. The name *Sheva ben Bichri* was simply his given name.

### Three Who Saw

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה נִיבְּטוּ וְלֹא רָאוּ, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן – נְבָט, וַאֲחִיתוֹפֶל, וְאִיצְטַגְנִינֵי פַּרְעֹה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Three looked but did not see** and as a result were mistaken in their prognostications. **And these are they: Nebat, and Ahithophel, and the astrologers of Pharaoh.**

*Tanu Rabbanan*: three people *nibtu ve-lo ra'u* — they saw but failed to perceive correctly. They are Nevat, Achitophel, and the astrologers of Pharaoh.

### Nevat Fire Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְבָט רָאָה אֵשׁ שֶׁיּוֹצֵאת מֵאַמָּתוֹ. הוּא סָבַר אִיהוּ מָלֵיךְ, וְלָא הִיא. יָרׇבְעָם הוּא דִּנְפַק מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Nebat saw fire that emerges from his penis. He thought** that it meant that **he** would **rule. But that is not** so. It meant that it **would be Jeroboam who** would **emerge from him** and rule.

What did Nevat see? *Ra'ah esh yotze me-amato* — he saw fire issuing from his body. He interpreted this as a sign of his own kingship. *Ve-lo hi* — he was mistaken. The fire referred to Yerovam, who would emerge from him and become king.

### Achitophel Leprosy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחִיתוֹפֶל רָאָה צָרַעַת שֶׁזָּרְחָה לוֹ עַל אַמָּתוֹ. הוּא סָבַר אִיהוּ מָלֵךְ, וְלָא הִיא. בַּת שֶׁבַע בִּתּוֹ הוּא דִּנְפַק מִינַּהּ שְׁלֹמֹה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ahithophel saw leprosy that glowed on his penis** and **he thought** that **he** would **rule. But it is not so.** Instead **from Bathsheba his daughter,** i.e., his son's daughter, **came Solomon** the king.

Achitophel saw leprosy blazing across his bed — *tzara'at she-zarcha lo al ha-mitah* — and concluded that this portended his own kingship. *Ve-lo hi*: it referred instead to Bat-Sheva his daughter, from whom Shlomo would descend.

### Astrologers Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטַגְנִינֵי פַּרְעֹה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״הֵמָּה מֵי מְרִיבָה״? הֵמָּה שֶׁרָאוּ אִיצְטַגְנִינֵי פַּרְעֹה וְטָעוּ. רָאוּ שֶׁמּוֹשִׁיעָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמַיִם הוּא לוֹקֶה, אָמַר: ״כׇּל הַבֵּן הַיִּלּוֹד הַיְאֹרָה תַּשְׁלִיכֻהוּ״. וְהֵן לֹא יָדְעוּ שֶׁעַל עִסְקֵי מֵי מְרִיבָה לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **The astrologers of Pharaoh** saw and were mistaken, **as Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "These are the waters of contention"** (Numbers 20:13)? **These are** the waters **that the astrologers of Pharaoh saw, and they erred** concerning them. As **they saw** in the stars **that the savior of Israel would be stricken by water,** Pharaoh **said: "Every son that is born you shall cast into the river"** (Exodus 1:22), assuming that any potential savior would be drowned. **And they did not know that** it was **over matters** involving **the waters of Meribah** that Moses would be **stricken** and would not be allowed to enter Eretz Yisrael.

The astrologers of Pharaoh saw a vision involving water — the very waters alluded to in the phrase *mei Merivah*, as Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina explains. They perceived that the savior of Israel would be struck through water. This led them to counsel Pharaoh to drown all newborn Jewish boys, reasoning that the one destined to redeem Israel would be caught in that decree. *Ve-hein lo yad'u she-al iskei mei Merivah lokeh* — what they failed to understand was that Moshe's undoing would come not through drowning as an infant but through the waters of Merivah, decades later.

## Yerovam Merit & Fall

### Biblical Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא לַן דְּלָא אָתֵי לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בַּדָּבָר הַזֶּה לְחַטַּאת בֵּית יָרׇבְעָם וּלְהַכְחִיד וּלְהַשְׁמִיד מֵעַל פְּנֵי הָאֲדָמָה״. ״לְהַכְחִיד״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וּלְהַשְׁמִיד״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its discussion of Jeroboam. **And from where do we** derive **that** Jeroboam **does not enter into the World-to-Come?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "And by this matter there was a sin to the house of Jeroboam, and to eradicate it and to destroy it from off the face of the earth"** (I Kings 13:34). It can be interpreted to mean: **To eradicate it in this world, and to destroy it for the World-to-Come.**

Returning to the main subject: how do we know that Yerovam has no portion in *olam ha-ba*? *De-ketiv*: *vayehi ba-davar hazeh le-chatat beit Yerovam u-lehachid u-lehishmid me-al pnei ha-adamah* — "And this matter became a sin for the house of Yerovam, to cut off and to destroy from the face of the earth." The Gemara reads the double language: *lehachid ba-olam hazeh*, to be cut off from this world, *ve-lehishmid le-olam ha-ba*, and to be destroyed from the World to Come.

### Yerovam Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵי מָה זָכָה יָרׇבְעָם לַמַּלְכוּת? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוֹכִיחַ אֶת שְׁלֹמֹה. וּמִפְּנֵי מָה נֶעֱנַשׁ? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוֹכִיחוֹ בָּרַבִּים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזֶה הַדָּבָר אֲשֶׁר הֵרִים יָד בַּמֶּלֶךְ שְׁלֹמֹה בָּנָה אֶת הַמִּלּוּא סָגַר אֶת פֶּרֶץ עִיר דָּוִד אָבִיו״. אָמַר לוֹ: דָּוִד אָבִיךָ פָּרַץ פְּרָצוֹת בַּחוֹמָה כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּעֲלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לָרֶגֶל, וְאַתָּה גָּדַרְתָּ אוֹתָם כְּדֵי לַעֲשׂוֹת אַנְגַּרְיָא לְבַת פַּרְעֹה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: For what** virtue **was Jeroboam privileged** to ascend **to monarchy?** It is **due to** the fact **that he rebuked Solomon** for his sins. **And for what** misdeed **was he punished** and lost everything? It is **due to** the fact **that he rebuked** Solomon and humiliated him **in public, as it is stated: "And this was the cause that he lifted his hand against the king: Solomon built the Millo, and repaired the breaches of the city of David his father"** (I Kings 11:27). Jeroboam **said to** Solomon: **David, your father, created breaches in the wall so that the Jewish people could ascend for the pilgrimage Festival, and you sealed them in order to marshal forced labor [*angarya*] for the daughter of Pharaoh,** your wife.

Rabbi Yochanan asks: *mipnei mah zachah Yerovam le-malchus?* Given all his evil, why did he merit becoming king in the first place? *Mipnei she-hochiah et Shlomo* — because he rebuked Shlomo. Reading the narrative in *Tanach*, it is evident that Shlomo had acted improperly, and Yerovam was the one who first gave him *musar*, which ultimately led to the prophetic designation of Yerovam as the ruler over ten tribes, serving simultaneously as a reward for Yerovam's integrity and a punishment for Shlomo's failings.

Then the Gemara asks: *u-mipnei mah ne'enash?* Where were the seeds of his downfall already visible in that original act? *Mipnei she-hochiacho be-rabbim* — because he publicly humiliated Shlomo. The willingness to give *musar* was admirable, but the manner — before others, without proper respect for the king — already revealed something troubling. The verse says: *ve-zeh ha-davar asher herim yad ba-melech Shlomo* — "and this is the thing that he lifted his hand against King Shlomo." The specific act, notes Rav Nachman, was *she-chalatz tfilav befanav* — that he removed his *tefilin* in Shlomo's presence, an act of studied disrespect and perhaps a kind of brazen undressing before the king.

The verse that prompted this confrontation relates that Shlomo built up the *Millo* and closed the breach in the city of David. Yerovam challenged him: your father David intentionally left openings in the walls of Jerusalem to facilitate the *oleh la-regel* — the pilgrimage — so that all could enter freely. You have sealed them, imposing *angarya* — a king's levy — for the benefit of Bat Pharaoh, your Egyptian queen, by collecting tolls at the gates.

### Seeds Downfall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: גַּסּוּת הָרוּחַ שֶׁהָיָה בּוֹ בְּיָרׇבְעָם טְרָדַתּוּ מִן הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר יָרׇבְעָם בְּלִבּוֹ עַתָּה תָּשׁוּב הַמַּמְלָכָה לְבֵית דָּוִד. אִם יַעֲלֶה הָעָם הַזֶּה לַעֲשׂוֹת זְבָחִים בְּבֵית ה׳ בִּירוּשָׁלִַים וְשָׁב לֵב הָעָם הַזֶּה אֶל אֲדֹנֵיהֶם אֶל רְחַבְעָם מֶלֶךְ יְהוּדָה וַהֲרָגֻנִי וְשָׁבוּ אֶל רְחַבְעָם מֶלֶךְ יְהוּדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: The arrogance that existed in Jeroboam drove him from the** World-to-Come, **as it is stated: "And Jeroboam said in his heart: Now shall the kingdom return to the house of David. If this people go up to offer sacrifices in the House of the Lord in Jerusalem, then shall the heart of this people turn again to their lord, even to Rehoboam, king of Judea, and they shall kill me and return to Rehoboam, king of Judea"** (I Kings 12:26–27).

Rav Nachman observes: *gasut ruach she-hayah bo be-Yerovam tardato min ha-olam* — the arrogance inherent in Yerovam destroyed him from the world, and *olam* here encompasses *olam ha-ba*. The public rebuke was an expression of that arrogance; the removal of *tefilin* before the king was another. These were not merely incidental lapses — they were symptoms of the quality that would ultimately express itself in the most consequential way.

### Jerusalem Fear

The Gemara now cites the verse: *vayomer Yerovam be-libo: atah tashuv ha-mamlachah le-veit David im ya'aleh ha-am ha-zeh la'asot zevachim be-veit Hashem bi-Yerushalayim* — Yerovam reasoned to himself that if the people continued to make pilgrimage to Jerusalem three times a year, they would inevitably return their loyalty to the house of David and to Rechavam. *Ve-shav lev ha-am ha-zeh el adoneihem el Rechavam melech Yehudah* — their hearts would follow the king whose religious center they attended. *Ve-haraguni ve-shavu el Rechavam melech Yehudah* — they would kill me and go back to Rechavam. It is worth noting the sharp irony: Yerovam's original rebuke to Shlomo was precisely that he had obstructed *aliyah la-regel*, and yet Yerovam's own political survival now depended on preventing that same pilgrimage.

### Azarah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר: גְּמִירִי דְּאֵין יְשִׁיבָה בַּעֲזָרָה אֶלָּא לְמַלְכֵי בֵּית יְהוּדָה בִּלְבָד. כֵּיוָן דְּחָזוּ לֵיהּ לִרְחַבְעָם דְּיָתֵיב וַאֲנָא קָאֵימְנָא, סָבְרִי: הָא מַלְכָּא וְהָא עַבְדָּא. וְאִי יָתֵיבְנָא, מוֹרֵד בַּמַּלְכוּת הוּא, וְקָטְלִין לִי וְאָזְלוּ בָּתְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Jeroboam calculated and **said: It is learned** as a tradition **that sitting in the Temple courtyard** is permitted **only for kings of the house of Judah alone. Once they see Rehoboam, who is sitting, and** they see **me standing, they will think: This,** Rehoboam, **is king, and that,** Jeroboam, **is the servant. And if I sit** there, **I will be** considered **a traitor against the throne, and they will kill me and follow him.**

The Gemara adds a further dimension to Yerovam's calculations. *Gemiri*: there is a received tradition that only the kings of the house of Yehudah were permitted to sit in the *Azarah* — the Temple courtyard — while all others, including kings of other tribes, were required to stand. *Keivan de-chazu leh le-Rechavam de-yativ ve-ana ka'imna* — when the people saw Rechavam seated and Yerovam standing, they would conclude that Rechavam was the king and Yerovam the servant. One might ask: what if Yerovam simply sat as well, claiming equal status? The Gemara answers that doing so on Rechavam's territory would constitute *mored be-malchus* — rebellion against the king — an act for which he could be put to death. The phrase *mored be-malchus* is admittedly a surprising one given that Yerovam had already established a separate kingdom; as the Gemara seems to intend it here, it means specifically transgressing the special prerogatives of the Davidic crown, or perhaps more precisely, a brazen challenge to God's *malchus* itself. Either way, the conclusion is the same: Yerovam could not accompany the pilgrims to Jerusalem without exposing either his subordinate status or his sedition.

### Golden Calves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד ״וַיִּוָּעַץ הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיַּעַשׂ שְׁנֵי עֶגְלֵי זָהָב וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם רַב לָכֶם מֵעֲלוֹת יְרוּשָׁלִַים הִנֵּה אֱלֹהֶיךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר הֶעֱלוּךָ מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם וַיָּשֶׂם אֶת הָאֶחָד בְּבֵית אֵל וְאֶת הָאֶחָד נָתַן בְּדָן״.
>
> **Translation:** Jeroboam calculated and immediately the following took place: **"And the king took counsel, and made two calves of gold, and said to them: It is too much for you to ascend to Jerusalem; behold your gods, Israel, who brought you up from the land of Egypt. And he placed the one in Bethel and the other he placed in Dan"** (I Kings 12:28–29).

*Miyad vayiva'atz ha-melech vaya'as shenei eglei zahav* — immediately Yerovam took counsel and fashioned two golden calves. *Vayomer aleihem: rav lachem me-alot Yerushalayim* — he told the people, it is too great a burden to go up to Jerusalem. *Hineh elohecha Yisrael asher he'elucha me-eretz Mitzrayim* — consciously echoing the language of the original *eigel ha-zahav* in the wilderness. He placed one in Beit El and one in Dan, establishing two convenient cult sites so that the people would have no need to make the arduous journey southward. The *gasut ha-ruach* that had been visible from the beginning expressed itself here fully: he would do anything, even transform Israel into an idolatrous nation, to preserve his power and status.

### Manipulation Deception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וַיִּוָּעַץ״? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: שֶׁהוֹשִׁיב רָשָׁע אֵצֶל צַדִּיק. אֲמַר לְהוּ: חָתְמִיתוּ עַל כֹּל דְּעָבֵידְנָא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הִין. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַלְכָּא בָּעֵינָא לְמִיהְוֵי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הִין. כֹּל דְּאָמֵינָא לְכוּ עָבֵידְתּוּ? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הִין. אֲפִילּוּ לְמִפְלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ צַדִּיק: חַס וְחָלִילָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָשָׁע לְצַדִּיק: סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ דְּגַבְרָא כְּיָרׇבְעָם פָּלַח לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה? אֶלָּא לְמֵינְסִינְהוּ הוּא דְּקָא בָּעֵי, אִי קַבְּלִיתוּ לְמֵימְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"and** the king **took counsel"? Rabbi Yehuda says:** It means **that** he assembled the elders of the kingdom of Israel and **seated a wicked** person **alongside a righteous** person and consulted them. **He said** to the elders: **Do you sign** a writ of authorization **for all that I am doing? They said to him: Yes. He said to them: I want to be the king. They said to him: Yes.** Jeroboam said to them: **Will you do everything I tell you to do? They said to him: Yes.** He said to them: **Even to worship idols? A righteous** man **said: Heaven forfend;** idol worship is unacceptable. The **wicked** man seated alongside him **said to the righteous** man: **Does it enter your mind that a man like Jeroboam** would **worship idols? Rather, he** merely **seeks to test** those sitting here, to determine **if they accept** and will obey **his pronouncements.**

The Gemara asks: *mai vayiva'atz* — what did it mean that he "took counsel"? Rabbi Yehuda explains: *she-hoshiv rasha etzel tzadik* — he seated a wicked person alongside a righteous person. He circulated a document requiring all citizens of the ten tribes to affirm their loyalty and obedience to him. The exchange would proceed as follows: Yerovam's agent would ask, "Will you sign to do whatever I command?" — *kol de-amina lachon aviditu*? The people would answer yes. Then: "Even to worship *avodat kochavim*?" At this point the *tzadik* would recoil — *chas ve-shalom!* — and the *rasha* seated beside him would say: "Do you think someone as righteous as Yerovam would actually intend such a thing? He is merely testing our commitment; of course he does not mean it literally." And so the *tzadik* was persuaded to sign.

### Achiya Signed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי טָעָה וְחָתַם, דְּהָא יֵהוּא צַדִּיקָא רַבָּה הֲוָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל יֵהוּא יַעַן אֲשֶׁר הֱטִיבֹתָ לַעֲשׂוֹת הַיָּשָׁר בְּעֵינַי כְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר בִּלְבָבִי עָשִׂיתָ לְבֵית אַחְאָב בְּנֵי רְבִעִים יֵשְׁבוּ לְךָ עַל כִּסֵּא יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְיֵהוּא לֹא שָׁמַר לָלֶכֶת בְּתוֹרַת ה׳ אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּכׇל לְבָבוֹ לֹא סָר מֵעַל חַטֹּאות יָרׇבְעָם אֲשֶׁר הֶחֱטִיא אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And even Ahijah the Shilonite erred** in this matter **and signed** that he would accept all of Jeroboam's pronouncements. And generations later this caused Jehu to sin. The Gemara explains: **As Jehu was an extremely righteous person. As it is stated: "And the Lord said to Jehu: Because you have done well, to perform what is right in My eyes, and have done to the house of Ahab according to all that was in My heart, your children of the fourth generation shall sit on the throne of Israel"** (II Kings 10:30). **And it is written: "But Jehu took no care to walk in the Torah of the Lord, God of Israel, with all his heart; he departed not from the sins of Jeroboam, who made Israel sin"** (II Kings 10:31).

By the end of this process, Yerovam had secured the assent of every stratum of the population — including, shockingly, *Achiya ha-Shiloni*, the very prophet who had initially designated Yerovam as king. *Ve-af Achiya ha-Shiloni ta'ah ve-chatam* — even Achiya ha-Shiloni was deceived and signed.

### Yehu Yerovam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי גְּרַמָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּרִית כְּרוּתָה לַשְּׂפָתַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״אַחְאָב עָבַד אֶת הַבַּעַל מְעָט יֵהוּא יַעַבְדֶנּוּ הַרְבֵּה״. רָבָא אָמַר: חוֹתָמוֹ שֶׁל אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי רָאָה, וְטָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What caused** Jehu to do so? **Abaye says: A covenant is made with the lips, as it is stated** that Jehu said cunningly in an attempt to entrap the priests of the Baal: **"Ahab worshipped Baal a little, but Jehu shall serve him much"** (II Kings 10:18). Although it was not his intention, once the statement escaped his lips he began following the path to its fulfillment. **Rava says: He saw the seal of Ahijah the Shilonite** on the coronation document of Jeroboam **and he erred** and believed that it was permitted to act as Jeroboam did.

The Gemara uses the story of Yehu to demonstrate both Achiya's greatness and the significance of his having signed. Yehu was a great *tzadik*, as the verse attests: *ya'an asher hetivota la'asot ha-yashar be-einai* — God praised him for having done all that was right, having wiped out the entire house of Achav as commanded. Yet the verse continues: *ve-Yehu lo shamar lalechet be-Torat Hashem Elohei Yisrael be-chol levavo lo sar me-chatat Yerovam* — Yehu himself did not depart from the sins of Yerovam. The Gemara asks: *mai garmeh* — what caused this righteous person to go astray?

Abaye answers: *berit kerutah la-sefatayim* — a covenant is formed by one's lips. When Yehu, in the course of his campaign against the prophets of the *Ba'al*, declared *Achav avad ha-Ba'al me'at, Yehu ya'avdenu harbe* — "Achav served the Ba'al a little, but Yehu will serve him greatly" — he did so as a ruse to lure them in. Nevertheless, those words having passed his lips, they held power over him and ultimately drew him toward the worship he had only feigned.

Rava disagrees: *chotamto shel Achiya ha-Shiloni ra'ah ve-ta'ah* — he saw the signature of Achiya ha-Shiloni on Yerovam's loyalty document and was thereby led astray. If a man of Achiya's stature had signed, Yehu reasoned, there must be legitimacy to it. This connects the account of Yehu back directly to the earlier discussion of how Yerovam's deception had implicated even the most righteous figures, whose endorsement then perpetuated his influence across generations.

The Gemara then cites the difficult verse from Hoshea: *ve-shachata shetim he'emiku, va-ani musar le-chulam* — "those that went astray went deep, and I am a reproof to all of them." Rabbi Yochanan reads this as God saying: they went deeper than I did. *Ani amarti: kol she-eino oleh la-regel over be-aseh* — I said only that one who fails to make the pilgrimage transgresses a positive commandment. *Ve-hen amru: kol ha-oleh la-regel yidaker ba-cherev* — but they decreed that anyone who goes up to the Temple will be killed by the sword. That was Yerovam's prohibition against pilgrimage: not mere disobedience but a policy of lethal enforcement.

## Cloth & Covenant

### Times Places Trouble

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי בָּעֵת הַהִיא וְיָרׇבְעָם יָצָא מִירוּשָׁלִָים וַיִּמְצָא אֹתוֹ אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי הַנָּבִיא בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְהוּא מִתְכַּסֶּה בְּשַׂלְמָה חֲדָשָׁה״. תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: עֵת הִיא מְזוּמֶּנֶת לְפוּרְעָנוּת. ״בְּעֵת פְּקֻדָּתָם יֹאבֵדוּ״, תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: עֵת מְזוּמֶּנֶת לְפוּרְעָנוּת. ״בְּעֵת רָצוֹן עֲנִיתִיךָ״, תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: עֵת מְזוּמֶּנֶת לְטוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And it came to pass at that time, and Jeroboam left Jerusalem, and the prophet Ahijah the Shilonite found him on the way, and he was clad in a new garment,** and the two were alone in the field" (I Kings 11:29). It was **taught in the name of Rabbi Yosei:** The phrase "at that time" indicates that **it is a time ordained for calamity.** The verse states: **"At the time of their punishment, they shall perish"** (Jeremiah 51:18). It was **taught in the name of Rabbi Yosei:** The phrase "at the time" indicates that it is **a time ordained for calamity.** The verse states: **"In an acceptable time have I answered you"** (Isaiah 49:8). It was **taught in the name of Rabbi Yosei:** The phrase "in an acceptable time" indicates that it is **a time ordained for good.**

The Gemara observes that the encounter between Yerovam and Achiya ha-Shiloni was not coincidental in its timing. *Tana mishmei de-Rabbi Yosi*: the verse says *vayehi ba-et ha-hi* — and it was at that time. *Et hi mezumenet le-puranut* — that time was designated for calamity. The Gemara expands this principle: just as there are times designated for punishment — *uve-et pekudatam yovedu* — there are also times designated for blessing — *uve-et ratzon aniticha, et mezumenet le-tovah*. This is not merely coincidence; certain periods are intrinsically charged with either benevolent or destructive potential. Rashi makes explicit that *vayom pokdi* refers to Tisha B'Av, a day designated for disaster from the time of the *meraglim*, through the destruction of the First and Second Temples alike — a recurring moment of concentrated catastrophe.

The same *tana*, Rabbi Yosi, makes a parallel observation about a different verse: *uve-yom pokdi u-fakadeti aleihem chatatam* — this too is a designated time, connected directly to the sin of the *eigel*, and Rashi again identifies it with Tisha B'Av. Times of disaster recur; and certain sins continue to accumulate their punishment across multiple such moments.

### Shechem Disasters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֵּלֶךְ רְחַבְעָם שְׁכֶם כִּי שְׁכֶם בָּא כׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהַמְלִיךְ אֹתוֹ״. תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מָקוֹם מְזוּמָּן לְפוּרְעָנוּת. בִּשְׁכֶם עִינּוּ אֶת דִּינָה, בִּשְׁכֶם מָכְרוּ אֶחָיו אֶת יוֹסֵף, בִּשְׁכֶם נֶחְלְקָה מַלְכוּת בֵּית דָּוִד.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And Rehoboam went to Shechem; for all Israel came to Shechem, to make him king"** (I Kings 12:1). It was **taught in the name of Rabbi Yosei:** Shechem is **a place ordained for calamity. In Shechem, they tormented** and raped **Dinah, in** the outskirts of **Shechem the brothers sold Joseph, in Shechem the kingdom of the house of David was divided.**

From designated times the Gemara turns to designated places: *vayeilech Rechavam Shchem ki Shechem ba kol Yisrael lehamlikh oto* — Rechavam went to Shechem because all Israel gathered there to crown him. *Tana mishmei de-Rabbi Yosi: makom mezuman le-puranut*. Shechem is a place historically associated with catastrophe. It was in Shechem that Dinah was violated; it was in Shechem — or its environs, since Rashi identifies Dotan as essentially a suburb of Shechem — that the brothers sold Yosef; and it was in Shechem that the kingdom of the house of David was rent in two.

### New Cloak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּמְצָא אֹתוֹ אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי הַנָּבִיא בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְהוּא מִתְכַּסֶּה בְּשַׂלְמָה חֲדָשָׁה וּשְׁנֵיהֶם לְבַדָּם בַּשָּׂדֶה״. מַאי ״בְּשַׂלְמָה חֲדָשָׁה״? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כְּשַׂלְמָה חֲדָשָׁה. מָה שַׂלְמָה חֲדָשָׁה אֵין בָּהּ שׁוּם דּוֹפִי – אַף תּוֹרָתוֹ שֶׁל יָרׇבְעָם לֹא הָיָה בָּהּ שׁוּם דּוֹפִי. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״שַׂלְמָה חֲדָשָׁה״ – שֶׁחִידְּשׁוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁלֹּא שָׁמְעָה אֹזֶן מֵעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And the prophet Ahijah the Shilonite found him on the way, and he was clad in a new [*ḥadasha*] garment, and the two were alone in the field." What** is taught by the phrase **"in a new garment"? Rav Naḥman says:** It was **like a new garment; just as a new garment has no imperfection, so too, the Torah** studies **of Jeroboam** at that time **had no imperfection. Alternatively, "a new garment"** teaches **that they introduced [*sheḥideshu*] matters that** no **ear had ever heard.**

The meeting between Achiya ha-Shiloni and Yerovam is described as taking place while Achiya wore a new cloak: *ve-hu mitkaseh be-salma chadashah*. *Mai salma chadashah?* Rav Nachman explains: *ke-salma chadashah — ein bah shum dofi* — just as a new garment has no blemish or stain, so too the Torah of Yerovam had no blemish at that time. Before his descent into sin, his Torah learning was flawless and unblemished. A second interpretation: *she-chidshu devarim she-lo shama ozen me-olam* — the garment was "new" because the Torah insights they discussed were so brilliant and unprecedented that no ear had ever heard anything like them. They did not immediately turn to political matters; first they learned Torah together, and the learning itself was the "new cloak."

### Field Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וּשְׁנֵיהֶם לְבַדָּם בַּשָּׂדֶה״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שֶׁכׇּל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים דּוֹמִין לִפְנֵיהֶם כְּעִשְׂבֵי הַשָּׂדֶה. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמַר: שֶׁכׇּל טַעֲמֵי תּוֹרָה מְגוּלִּין לָהֶם כַּשָּׂדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is taught by the phrase **"and the two were alone in the field"? Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** It teaches **that all** the **Torah scholars** of that generation **seemed,** relative **to them, like grass of the field,** as they were wiser than all of them. **And there are** those **who say:** It teaches **that all the rationales of the Torah were revealed to them like a field** that is exposed for all to see.

The verse continues: *u-shneihem levadim ba-sadeh* — and the two of them were alone in the field. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav explains: *she-kol talmidei chachamim domim lifneihem ke-isvei ha-sadeh* — that all other Torah scholars of the generation appeared before them like blades of grass in a field; their stature was so far above their contemporaries. An alternative reading: *she-kol ta'amei Torah megulim lahem ka-sadeh* — all the hidden reasons of Torah were open and revealed to them, as exposed to view as a field.

This leads to a passage from Micha: *lachen titni shilluchim al Moreshet Gat, batei Achziv le-achzav le-malchei Yisrael* — therefore send farewell messengers to Moreshet Gat; the houses of Achziv will prove a deception to the kings of Israel. Rabbi Chanina bar Papa reads this as a divine rebuke: the one who killed the Philistines — King David, who slew Golyat of Gat — gave you the inheritance of Gat. And now you are sending farewell notices to his children, stealing the kingdom away from the house of David? Rashi explains: *le-malchei beit David atem mechazvim, ve-halachtem acharei malchei Yisrael* — you are deceiving the kings of the house of David and going after the kings of Israel instead.

This brings the Gemara to a statement by Rabbi Chanina bar Papa: *kol ha-neheneh min ha-olam ha-zeh be-lo bracha ke-ilu gozel le-Hakadosh Baruch Hu u-Knesset Yisrael* — anyone who derives benefit from this world without making a *bracha* is as though he stole from God and from the Jewish people. The proof text: *gozel aviv ve-imo ve-amar ein pesha, chaver hu le-ish mashchit* — one who steals from his father and mother and says it is no sin is a companion to a destructive person. *Ve-ein aviv ela ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — "father" is God, as the verse says *halo hu avicha kanecha*; *ve-ein imo ela Knesset Yisrael* — "mother" is the Jewish people, as the verse says *shema beni musar avicha ve-al titosh Torat imecha*.

The relevance here lies in the final clause: *chaver hu le-ish mashchit — chaver hu le-Yerovam ben Nevat she-hishchit Yisrael le-avihem she-ba-shamayim* — he is a companion to Yerovam, who destroyed the people by separating them from their Father in Heaven. The parallel is pointed: *avoda zara* draws people's worship away from God; similarly, when a person encounters the world without a *bracha*, without recognizing the divine source of what he enjoys, he is effectively separating his experience of the world from God — much as Yerovam directed the people's worship toward the golden calves. The connection to *Knesset Yisrael* as the aggrieved "mother" is somewhat less explicit in the proof text, though one might suggest that just as Yerovam's sin corrupted the whole people, one who models indifference to *brachos* may lead others astray as well.

The section on Yerovam concludes: *vayidach Yerovam et Yisrael me-acharei Hashem ve-hecheti'am chata'ah gedolah* — he drew Israel away from God and caused them to sin greatly. Rabbi Chanin offers an image: *ki-shtei maklot ha-matizah zo et zo* — like two sticks, one of which is struck so hard by the other that it goes flying far away. The separation Yerovam effected was not a modest drift but a violent and radical rupture.

## Cheit HaEigel Legacy

### Gold Complaint

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְדִי זָהָב״. אָמְרוּ דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי, אָמַר מֹשֶׁה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, בִּשְׁבִיל כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב שֶׁהִשְׁפַּעְתָּ לָהֶן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל עַד שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ ׳דַּיי׳, גָּרַם לָהֶם לַעֲשׂוֹת לָהֶם אֱלֹהֵי זָהָב. מָשָׁל: אֵין אֲרִי דּוֹרֵס וְנוֹהֵם מִתּוֹךְ קוּפָּה שֶׁל תֶּבֶן, אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ קוּפָּה שֶׁל בָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the calves of Jeroboam, the Gemara discusses the Golden Calf discussed in the Torah. The verse states: **"And Di Zahav"** (Deuteronomy 1:1). **The school of Rabbi Yannai said: Moses said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, because of the gold and silver that You lavished upon the Jewish people** during the exodus from Egypt **until they said enough [*dai*],** this wealth **caused the Jewish people to fashion for themselves gods of gold.** The combination of wealth and leisure left them open to a transgression of that sort. There is **a parable** for this: **A lion does not maul and roar over a basket of straw** from which he derives no pleasure; **rather,** he mauls and roars **over a basket of meat,** as he roars only when satiated. In other words, the guilt is not exclusively theirs.

The mention of the golden calves prompts the Gemara to step back and address the sin of the *eigel ha-zahav* in the wilderness. *Amru de-vei Rabbi Yannai*: the verse in Devarim contains the place name *Ve-Di Zahav*. The *derasha* reads this as *dai zahav* — too much gold. Moshe said before God: *Ribono shel Olam, bishvil ha-kesef ve-ha-zahav she-hishpata lahem le-Yisrael, ad she-yomru dai, garam lahem la'asot lahem elohei zahav* — it was the superabundance of silver and gold that You poured upon Israel that caused them to make the golden calves. The *mashal*: a lion does not trample and roar when it is fed from a trough of grain; only when it devours flesh does it become fierce and boisterous. Excess prosperity breeds arrogance and ultimately leads to transgression.

### One Calf Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: עַד יָרׇבְעָם הָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל יוֹנְקִים מֵעֵגֶל אֶחָד, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ מִשְּׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה עֲגָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Oshaya says: Until Jeroboam, Israel was suckling,** i.e., suffering the consequences, **from** worshipping **one calf** that they fashioned in the wilderness. **From that** point **forward,** they were suckling **from two and three calves,** as Jeroboam added the calves in Bethel and Dan.

Rabbi Yoshiyah notes: *ad Yerovam hayu Yisrael yonkim me-eigel echad* — until Yerovam, Israel suffered the consequences of only the one golden calf made in the wilderness. *Mi-kan ve-eilach mishnayim u-shlosha agalim* — from that point forward, they were burdened by the sins of two more: the two calves Yerovam erected at Beit El and Dan, added to the original of Moshe's time, making three altogether.

### Twenty-Fourth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֵין לָךְ כׇּל פּוּרְעָנוּת וּפוּרְעָנוּת שֶׁבָּאָה לְעוֹלָם שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אֶחָד מֵעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה בְּהֶכְרֵעַ לִיטְרָא שֶׁל עֵגֶל הָרִאשׁוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְיוֹם פׇּקְדִי וּפָקַדְתִּי עֲלֵהֶם חַטֹּאתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: You have no punishment that comes to the world in which there is not one twenty-fourth of the surplus of a *litra* of the first calf.** When weighing a substance, there would often be a disparity of up to one twenty-fourth of its weight. The point is that a minuscule portion of the sin of the Golden Calf is added to every punishment imposed upon the Jewish people, **as it is stated: "On the day when I punish, I will punish their sin upon them"** (Exodus 32:34), indicating that atonement for that sin is incomplete, and the Jewish people will continue to suffer for it.

Rabbi Yitzchak teaches: there is no calamity that comes upon the world that does not contain *echad me-esrim ve-arba be-hachra litra shel eigel ha-rishon* — one twenty-fourth of a scale-tipping measure of the original sin of the golden calf. The language evokes the practice of adding a tiny counterweight to a scale to ensure accurate measure: every catastrophe carries within it a fraction of that primordial sin. One might further suggest that the image implies something about causation — that tiny residual weight is sometimes the very thing that tips the scales toward punishment, even if the sin itself has long seemed settled.

### 24 Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לְאַחַר עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה דּוֹרוֹת נִגְבָּה פָּסוּק זֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקְרָא בְאׇזְנַי קוֹל גָּדוֹל לֵאמֹר קָרְבוּ פְּקֻדּוֹת הָעִיר וְאִישׁ כְּלִי מַשְׁחֵתוֹ בְּיָדוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says: After twenty-four generations** elapsed from the sin of the Golden Calf, the debt referred to in **this verse was collected, as it is stated: "And he cried in my ears with a loud voice, saying: Those who have charge over the city draw near, every man with his deadly weapon in his hand"** (Ezekiel 9:1). This prophecy occurred twenty-four generations after the sin of the Golden Calf.

Rabbi Chanina adds the longitudinal dimension: *le-achar esrim ve-arba dorot nigbah pasuk zeh* — this verse, in which God declares He will visit the sin of the *eigel* upon Israel, was finally collected after twenty-four generations. The verse: *vayikra be-oznai kol gadol leimor: kirvu pekudot ha-ir ve-ish kli mashchito be-yado* — the cry of Yechezkel announcing the coming destruction of the city, each destroying angel armed with his weapon of ruin. Rashi demonstrates that this moment — the destruction of Jerusalem — fell precisely twenty-four generations after the *cheit ha-eigel*. The *pekudot* of Yechezkel thus fulfills the *u-fakadeti* of Shemot. One might also hear in this a note of closure: the debt was ultimately discharged completely, rather than stretching indefinitely.

## Teshuva Complexity

### Teshuva Gan Eden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַחַר הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה לֹא שָׁב יָרׇבְעָם מִדַּרְכּוֹ הָרָעָה״. מַאי ״אַחַר״? אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: אַחַר שֶׁתְּפָשׂוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְיָרׇבְעָם בְּבִגְדוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: חֲזוֹר בְּךָ, וַאֲנִי וְאַתָּה וּבֶן יִשַׁי נְטַיֵּיל בְּגַן עֵדֶן. אָמַר לוֹ: מִי בָּרֹאשׁ? בֶּן יִשַׁי בָּרֹאשׁ. אִי הָכִי, לָא בָּעֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And after this matter, Jeroboam returned not from his evil way"** (I Kings 13:33). To **what** event is the verse alluding, when it states: **"After** this matter"? **Rabbi Abba says:** It is **after the Holy One, Blessed be He, grabbed Jeroboam by his garment,** i.e., he sent prophets and implored him, **and said to him: Repent, and you and I and** the **son of Yishai will stroll** together **in the Garden of Eden.** Jeroboam **said to Him: Who** will walk **in the lead?** God **said to** Jeroboam: The **son of Yishai** will walk **in the lead.** Jeroboam said: **If so, I do not wish** to repent.

*Achar ha-davar ha-zeh lo shav Yerovam mi-darko ha-ra'ah* — after all this, Yerovam did not return from his evil path. The Gemara asks: *mai achar* — after what? What event should have prompted him to return? Rabbi Abba explains: *achar she-tefaso ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu le-Yerovam be-vigdo* — after God, as it were, took Yerovam by the collar and said to him: return. *Va-ani u-ben Yishai netayel be-Gan Eden* — I, you, and the son of Yishai — King David — will stroll together in Gan Eden. God offered Yerovam not merely forgiveness but the most exalted spiritual companionship imaginable.

### Pride Rejection

Yerovam's response: *mi be-rosh?* — who walks first? *Ben Yishai be-rosh* — David goes first. *Ei hachi lo be'ina* — then I don't want it. This exchange crystallizes everything the Gemara has been building toward. The man who started off giving *musar* to a king, the man whose downfall the Gemara traces entirely to *gasut ha-ruach*, refused divine pardon because it came with a subordinate position. He would rather forfeit *olam ha-ba* than walk behind David.

### Abbahu Sickness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ הֲוָה רְגִיל דַּהֲוָה קָא דָרֵישׁ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְלָכִים. חֲלַשׁ, קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ דְּלָא דָּרֵישׁ. כֵּיוָן דְּאִתְּפַח, הֲדַר קָא דָרֵישׁ. אָמְרִי: לָא קַבֵּילְתְּ עֲלָךְ דְּלָא דָּרְשַׁתְּ בְּהוּ? אָמַר: אִינְהוּ מִי הֲדַרוּ בְּהוּ, דַּאֲנָא אֶהְדַּר בִּי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Abbahu was accustomed to lecture** at great length **about the three kings** enumerated in the mishna, who have no share in the World-to-Come. **He fell ill, and he undertook that he would not lecture** about those kings. **Once he recovered, he then lectured** on that topic. His students **said to him: Did you not undertake that you will not lecture about them?** Rabbi Abbahu **said: Did they repent, that I will reconsider** and refrain from condemning them?

Rabbi Abbahu was accustomed to lecture extensively about the three evil kings of *Perek Chelek*, dwelling on their misdeeds and punishments. At a certain point he fell ill — *chalash* — and understanding the possible significance of this, he accepted upon himself to cease. *Ve-kidel aleih de-lo darash*. Once he recovered, he resumed his lectures. His students challenged him: did you not commit to stop? He replied: *inhu mi-hadru behu de-ana ahadar bi?* — did they stop sinning, that I should stop preaching? The exchange is left somewhat unresolved, but the story serves as a prelude to what follows, suggesting that excessive focus on these kings' wickedness is itself potentially problematic.

### Rav Ashi Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אוֹקֵי אַשְּׁלֹשָׁה מְלָכִים. אֲמַר: לִמְחַר נִפְתַּח בְּחַבְרִין. אֲתָא מְנַשֶּׁה, אִיתְחֲזִי לֵיהּ בְּחֶלְמֵיהּ. אָמַר: חֲבֵרָךְ וַחֲבֵירֵי דַּאֲבוּךְ קָרֵית לַן?
>
> **Translation:** One day **Rav Ashi ended** his lecture just before reaching the matter **of the three kings. He said** to his students: **Tomorrow we will begin** the lecture **with our colleagues** the three kings, who, although they were sinners, were Torah scholars like us. **Manasseh,** king of Judea, **came and appeared to him in his dream.** Manasseh **said** to him angrily: **You called us your colleague and the colleagues of your father?** How dare you characterize yourself as our equal?

Rav Ashi was engaged in a sequential study of *Perek Chelek*. At the point where he was about to reach the section on the three kings, he stopped for the day and announced: *le-machar niftach be-chaverin* — tomorrow we will begin with our colleagues. His decision to call the three evil kings "colleagues" was an expression of genuine recognition: even these figures, with all their profound wrongdoing, were men of vast Torah learning, and as such they merited a degree of collegial respect from those who studied Torah in later generations.

That night, Menashe appeared to him in a dream.

### Menashe Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהֵיכָא בָּעֵית לְמִישְׁרֵא הַמּוֹצִיא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָדַעְנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵהֵיכָא דְּבָעֵית לְמִישְׁרֵא הַמּוֹצִיא לָא גְּמִירַתְּ, וַחֲבֵרָךְ קָרֵית לַן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַגְמְרֵיהּ לִי, וְלִמְחַר דָּרֵישְׁנָא לֵיהּ מִשְּׁמָךְ בְּפִירְקָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵהֵיכָא דְּקָרֵים בִּישּׁוּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** Manasseh said to him: I will ask you, **from where are you required to begin** cutting a loaf of bread when reciting the blessing: **Who brings forth** bread from the earth? Rav Ashi **said to him: I do not know.** Manasseh **said to him:** Even this, **from where you are required to begin** cutting a loaf of bread when reciting the blessing: **Who brings forth** bread from the earth, **you did not learn, and** yet **you call us your colleague?** Rav Ashi **said to** Manasseh: **Teach me** this *halakha* **and tomorrow I will lecture** and cite **it in your name during** my public **lecture** delivered on the Festival. Manasseh **said to him:** One cuts the loaf **from where it crusts** as a result of **baking.**

*Amar: chavrecha ve-chavrei de-avuch karita lan?* — you called us your colleagues and your father's colleagues? The impertinence of that designation! Then Menashe posed a question: *me-heicha de-ba'it le-mishra ha-motzi?* — where does one break the bread when reciting *ha-motzi*? Rav Ashi admitted he did not know. Menashe was incredulous: you perform *ha-motzi* every single day and you do not know the proper place from which to break it — and you consider yourself our colleague? Rav Ashi, rather than defending himself, asked Menashe to teach him. *Agmar li, ul-machar darishna mi-shmach be-pirka* — teach me, and tomorrow I will transmit this teaching in your name in the public assembly. Menashe replied: *me-heicha de-karim bishula* — from where the baking forms a crust. Rashi suggests this means one takes from the outer, firmer crust rather than the soft interior, or from the part that is more thoroughly baked.

### Seduction Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵאַחַר דְּחַכִּימַתְּ כּוּלֵּי הַאי, מַאי טַעְמָא קָא פָּלְחִיתוּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הֲוֵת הָתָם, הֲוֵת נָקֵיטַתְּ בְּשִׁיפּוּלֵי גְּלִימָא וְרָהֲטַתְּ אַבַּתְרַאי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: Since you were so wise, what is the reason you engaged in idol worship?** Manasseh **said to him: Had you been there** at that time, **you would have taken** and lifted **the hem of your cloak and run after me** due to the fierce desire to engage in idol worship and due to the fact that it was a common faith.

Then Rav Ashi turned the conversation around: *me-achar de-chakimtu kulei hai, mai ta'ama ke-pachitu la-avodat kochavim?* — given your tremendous wisdom, why did you worship idols? Menashe's answer has become one of the most cited passages in this perek: *i havit hatam, havat nakitat be-shipulei glimach ve-rahitat basrai* — if you had been there, you would have picked up the skirts of your cloak and run after it. The seductive power of *avodat kochavim* in that era was so overwhelming that one cannot judge from a later vantage point. The moral and cultural force of that pull is simply not accessible to those who did not live through it.

### Colleagues Rabbis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִמְחַר אֲמַר לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן: נִפְתָּח בְּרַבְּווֹתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The next day** Rav Ashi **said to the Sages** as a prelude to his lecture: **We will begin with** the treatment of **our teachers,** those kings who were greater than us in Torah knowledge but whose sins caused them to lose their share in the World-to-Come.

The next morning, Rav Ashi addressed the assembled scholars differently than he had planned. *Le-machar amar lehu le-rabbanan: niftach be-ravvata* — let us begin with the great ones. Not "colleagues" — *chaverin* — but "our rabbis" — *rabbanan* or *ravvata*. They were *resha'im*, yes, but they were also great figures whose depth of Torah and whose complexity of character demand a certain reverence, or at the very least, humility. This is a striking recalibration. The lesson of Menashe's dream appearance is not merely a charming anecdote about where to break *challah*; it is a demand for intellectual and moral modesty. The ease with which later generations pronounce judgment on those who served *avoda zara* reflects a failure to appreciate the pressures and temptations of those times.

This principle suffuses the entire discussion of the three kings: the Gemara's portrait of Yerovam is not one-dimensional villainy but a complex interweaving of genuine Torah greatness, corrupted by arrogance and fear, ultimately expressing itself in catastrophic national sin. And Menashe's rebuke to Rav Ashi asks that we carry that complexity with us.

## Achav

### Achav Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחְאָב: אָח לַשָּׁמַיִם, אָב לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אָח לַשָּׁמַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״אָח לְצָרָה יִוָּלֵד״. אָב לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״כְּרַחֵם אָב עַל בָּנִים״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to discuss the second king enumerated in the mishna, Ahab. **Ahab [*Aḥav*],** although he was **a brother [*aḥ*] to Heaven,** he was **a father [*av*] for idol worship.** He was **a brother to Heaven, as it is written: "And a brother is born for adversity"** (Proverbs 17:17), and in desperate times, he turned to Heaven. He was **a father for idolatry.** This is the highest level of attachment, **as it is written: "As a father has compassion for his children"** (Psalms 103:13).

The Gemara turns to Achav. Again, a series of name-plays: *ach la-shamayim* — using *ach* not as "brother" but as an exclamation of woe, a cry of anguish: woe to heaven, i.e., his very existence is a source of grief to heaven. The proof text: *ach le-tzarah yulad* — a woe, one born for trouble. *Av la-avodat kochavim* — a father to *avoda zara*, meaning he cherished and cultivated idolatry. The proof text: *ke-rachem av al banim* — as a father has compassion on his children.

### Achav Worse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי הֲנָקֵל לֶכְתּוֹ בְּחַטֹּאות יָרׇבְעָם בֶּן נְבָט״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קַלּוֹת שֶׁעָשָׂה אַחְאָב – כַּחֲמוּרוֹת שֶׁעָשָׂה יָרׇבְעָם. וּמִפְּנֵי מָה תָּלָה הַכָּתוּב בְּיָרׇבְעָם? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא הָיָה תְּחִילָּה לַקַּלְקָלָה. ״גַּם מִזְבְּחוֹתָם כְּגַלִּים עַל תַּלְמֵי שָׂדָי״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין לָךְ כׇּל תֶּלֶם וָתֶלֶם בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא הֶעֱמִיד עָלָיו אַחְאָב עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Ahab, it is written: **"And it came to pass, the most minor of his transgressions was his walking in the sins of Jeroboam, son of Nebat"** (I Kings 16:31). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The minor** sins **that Ahab performed** were **on par with the major** sins **that Jeroboam performed,** as the transgressions committed by Ahab were much more serious than those committed by Jeroboam. **And for what** reason **did the verse attribute** the sins of all the kings of Israel **to Jeroboam,** even though the sins of Ahab were more substantial? It is **due to** the fact **that he was first to** engage in **iniquity.** With regard to the verse: **"Their altars shall also be like droppings in the furrows of the field"** (Hosea 12:12), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: You do not have even one furrow in Eretz Yisrael upon which Ahab did not place** an object of **idol worship and bow to it.**

The verse says: *vayehi ha-nekel le-lechto be-chatat Yerovam ben Nevat* — it was trivial for him to walk in the sins of Yerovam. Rabbi Yochanan explains: *kalot she-asa Achav ke-chamurot she-asa Yerovam* — the lightest of Achav's sins were as weighty as the gravest of Yerovam's. He did Yerovam's program, and then multiplied it tenfold. Yet the verse still attributes his evil with reference to Yerovam — *mipnei mah tala ha-katuv be-Yerovam?* — because Yerovam was the *techila le-kalkalah*, the originator of the corruption. All subsequent evil among the kings of Israel flowed from the precedent and culture that Yerovam had established.

Rabbi Yochanan adds: *ein lecha kol telem ve-telem be-Eretz Yisrael she-lo he'emid alav Achav avoda zara ve-hishtachavah lo* — there was not a single furrow in all of the land of Israel upon which Achav did not erect an idol and bow before it. The verse from Hoshea — *gam mizbechotam ke-galim al talmei sadai* — their altars like mounds along the furrows of the fields — is read with literal comprehensiveness: every field, every furrow, every inch of cultivated land in Eretz Yisrael was desecrated with idolatrous worship.

### Mashtin Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא לַן דְּלָא אָתֵי לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִכְרַתִּי לְאַחְאָב מַשְׁתִּין בְּקִיר וְעָצוּר וְעָזוּב בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״. ״עָצוּר״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וְעָזוּב״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that** Ahab **does not enter into the World-to-Come?** The Gemara answers: It is derived **as it is written: "And I will excise from Ahab every man, and him that is shut up and him that is abandoned in Israel"** (I Kings 21:21). **"Him that is shut up"** indicates he will be excised **in this world,** while **"and him that is abandoned"** indicates he will be excised **in the World-to-Come.**

How do we know Achav has no portion in *olam ha-ba*? *De-ketiv: ve-hichrati le-Achav mashtin be-kir* — I will cut off for Achav every *mashtin be-kir*, which the Gemara reads as a reference to *talmidei chachamim*, and *atzur va-azuv be-Yisrael* — whether constrained in this world or abandoned in the next. The double language again: *atzur ba-olam ha-zeh, ve-azuv le-olam ha-ba*. Both categories of persons associated with Achav's regime will be destroyed — and by extension, Achav himself is without portion.

### Omri City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵי מָה זָכָה עׇמְרִי לַמַּלְכוּת? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוֹסִיף כְּרַךְ אֶחָד בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקֶן אֶת הָהָר שֹׁמְרוֹן מֵאֶת שֶׁמֶר בְּכִכְּרַיִם כָּסֶף וַיִּבֶן אֶת הָהָר וַיִּקְרָא [אֶת] שֵׁם הָעִיר אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה עַל שֶׁם שֶׁמֶר אֲדֹנֵי הָהָר שֹׁמְרוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: For what** virtue **was Omri,** king of Israel, who was Ahab's father, **privileged** to ascend **to** the **monarchy?** It is **due to** the fact **that he added one city in Eretz Yisrael, as it is stated: "And he bought the hill of Samaria from Shemer for two talents of silver, and built on the hill, and called the name of the city that he built after Shemer, the owner of the hill, Samaria"** (I Kings 16:24).

Rabbi Yochanan asks: *mipnei mah zachah Omri le-malchus?* — why did Omri, Achav's father, merit kingship despite his own wickedness? *Mipnei she-hosif kerach echad be-Eretz Yisrael* — because he added one city to the land of Israel. The verse: *vayiken et ha-har Shomron me-et Shemer be-kicharayim kesef, vayiven et ha-har, vayikra et shem ha-ir asher banah shem Shemer: Shomron* — he purchased the hill of Samaria from Shemer for two talents of silver, built it up, and named it Shomron after its former owner. That single act of building and expanding Israel's territory secured Omri a dynasty.

### Achav Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵי מָה זָכָה אַחְאָב לַמַּלְכוּת עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם שָׁנָה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכִּיבֵּד אֶת הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם אוֹתִיּוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשְׁלַח מַלְאָכִים אֶל אַחְאָב מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל הָעִירָה. וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ כֹּה אָמַר בֶּן הֲדַד כַּסְפְּךָ וּזְהָבְךָ לִי הוּא וְנָשֶׁיךָ וּבָנֶיךָ הַטּוֹבִים לִי הֵם״. ״כִּי אִם כָּעֵת מָחָר אֶשְׁלַח אֶת עֲבָדַי אֵלֶיךָ וְחִפְּשׂוּ אֶת בֵּיתְךָ וְאֵת בָּתֵּי עֲבָדֶיךָ וְהָיָה כׇּל מַחְמַד עֵינֶיךָ יָשִׂימוּ בְיָדָם וְלָקָחוּ״. ״וַיֹּאמֶר לְמַלְאֲכֵי בֶן הֲדַד אִמְרוּ לַאדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ כֹּל אֲשֶׁר שָׁלַחְתָּ אֶל עַבְדְּךָ בָרִאשׁוֹנָה אֶעֱשֶׂה וְהַדָּבָר הַזֶּה לֹא אוּכַל לַעֲשׂוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** It is **due to** the fact **that he respected the Torah, which was given with twenty-two letters, as it is stated: "And he sent messengers to Ahab, king of Israel, into the city, and said to him: So says Ben-Hadad: Your silver and your gold are mine; your wives and also your good children, are mine.** And the king of Israel answered and said: It is according to your saying, my lord, O king: I am yours, and all that I have. And the messengers came again, and said: So speaks Ben-Hadad, saying: I sent to you, saying: You shall deliver me your silver, and your gold, and your wives, and your children. **Yet I will send my servants to you tomorrow about this time, and they shall search your house, and the houses of your servants, and it shall be, that they shall put in their hand all the delight of your eyes and take it…And he said to the messengers of Ben-Hadad, tell my lord the king: All that you did send for to your servant at the first I will do; but this thing I may not do"** (I Kings 20:2-6, 9).

Rabbi Yochanan then asks the same question about Achav: *mipnei mah zachah Achav le-malchus* — and for how long? *Kaf-chet shanim* — twenty-two years, a substantial reign. *Mipnei she-kibed et ha-Torah she-nitnah be-chaf-bet osiyot* — because he honored the Torah given in twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet.

How did Achav honor the Torah? The Gemara cites the episode in which Ben-Hadad, the hostile king, sent messengers to Achav demanding: your silver and gold are mine, your wives and children are mine; and further, my servants will come and search your house and take whatever is most desirable to you. Achav responded by agreeing to the first demands but refusing the last: *ve-hadavar hazeh lo uchal la'asot* — this thing I cannot do.

### Sefer Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״מַחְמַד עֵינֶיךָ״? לָאו סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה? דִּילְמָא עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה? לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמְרוּ אֵלָיו כׇּל הַזְּקֵנִים וְכׇל הָעָם לֹא תֹאבֶה וְלֹא תִשְׁמַע״. וְדִילְמָא סָבֵי דְּבַהֲתָא הֲווֹ? מִי לָא כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּישַׁר הַדָּבָר בְּעֵינֵי אַבְשָׁלֹם וּבְעֵינֵי כׇּל זִקְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״? וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: סָבֵי דְּבַהֲתָא. הָתָם לָא כְּתִיב ״וְכׇל הָעָם״, הָכָא כְּתִיב ״וְכׇל הָעָם״, דְּאִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא הֲווֹ בְּהוֹן צַדִּיקֵי. וּכְתִיב: ״וְהִשְׁאַרְתִּי בְיִשְׂרָאֵל שִׁבְעַת אֲלָפִים כׇּל הַבִּרְכַּיִם אֲשֶׁר לֹא כָרְעוּ לַבַּעַל וְכׇל הַפֶּה אֲשֶׁר לֹא נָשַׁק לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is **"the delight of your eyes"** that Ahab refused to give Ben-Hadad after agreeing to give him his wives and his gold? **Is it not a Torah scroll?** Ahab treated the Torah with deference. The Gemara suggests: **Perhaps** the delight of his eyes was an object of **idol worship.** The Gemara answers: That could **not enter your mind, as it is written** that Ahab consulted the Sages with regard to this matter: **"And all the elders and all the people said to him: Do not consent and heed not"** (I Kings 20:8). The elders in this verse are presumably the Sages. The Gemara continues: **But perhaps they were wicked elders,** who worshipped idols. **Isn't it written: "And the saying pleased Absalom well and all the elders of Israel"** (II Samuel 17:4), **and Rav Yosef says:** The elders of Israel mentioned in that verse were **wicked elders.** Perhaps the elders of Israel with whom Ahab consulted were also wicked. The Gemara rejects this: **There,** with regard to Absalom **it is not written: "And all the people,"** whereas **here,** with regard to Ahab, **it is written: "And all the people." As it is impossible that there were not among them righteous** people, if the righteous people agreed, apparently, "the delight of your eyes" was sacred. **And it is written: "And I will leave seven thousand in Israel; all the knees that have not bent to the Baal, and every mouth that has not kissed it"** (I Kings 19:18), indicating that there were righteous people among them.

*Mai machmad eynecha?* — what was the most desirable possession he refused to surrender? *Lav sefer Torah?* — must it not be the *Sefer Torah*? The Gemara considers an objection: perhaps it was his idols — given his devotion to *avoda zara*, perhaps that was what he refused to relinquish? *Lo salka da'atcha*: the text records that *vayomru elav kol ha-zkenim ve-chol ha-am* — all the elders and all the people told him not to comply. Unanimous popular support for the refusal is inconceivable if the issue were idols — surely somewhere among all the people there would have been someone unwilling to take a stand in defense of *avoda zara*. But the one thing that would command truly unanimous assent — from every stratum of the population, including the *zkenim* who were actual *talmidei chachamim* — was the defense of the *Sefer Torah*. And that is indeed the reading Rav Yosef offers: those *zkenim* were genuine sages, not, as he says of the elders who supported Avshalom's rebellion, *savei de-vahata* — foolish old men. The proof: by Avshalom the verse does not say *ve-chol ha-am*, whereas here it does. Universal popular assent seals the identification.

Because Achav protected the *Sefer Torah*, even from the overwhelming military threat of Ben-Hadad, he merited twenty-two years of kingship — one year for each letter of the *aleph-beit* in which the Torah was given.

## Achav Beinoni

### Beinoni Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אַחְאָב שָׁקוּל הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ מִי יְפַתֶּה אֶת אַחְאָב וְיַעַל וְיִפֹּל בְּרָמֹת גִּלְעָד וַיֹּאמֶר זֶה בְּכֹה וְזֶה אֹמֵר בְּכֹה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: Ahab was balanced** in terms of the mitzvot and transgressions that he performed, **as it is stated: "And the Lord said, who shall entice Ahab that he may ascend and fall at Ramoth Gilead? And this one said in this manner, and that one said in that manner"** (I Kings 22:20), indicating that it is unclear whether or not he was a full-fledged transgressor.

Rav Nachman makes a striking claim: *Achav shakul hayah* — Achav was precisely balanced, a *beinoni*. His merits and transgressions were exactly equal.

### Seduction Proof

The proof: God asked *mi yefateh et Achav* — who will seduce Achav? If Achav had been wholly wicked — a complete *rasha* — seduction would have been unnecessary. One seduces someone who is not already committed to the sin. The very need for divine orchestration of temptation demonstrates that Achav retained some measure of resistance, that the scales were not already decided.

### Sold Evil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵף: מַאן דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״רַק לֹא הָיָה כְאַחְאָב אֲשֶׁר הִתְמַכֵּר לַעֲשׂוֹת הָרַע בְּעֵינֵי ה׳ אֲשֶׁר הֵסַתָּה אֹתוֹ אִיזֶבֶל אִשְׁתּוֹ״, וּתְנֵינָא: בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיְתָה שׁוֹקֶלֶת שִׁקְלֵי זָהָב לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ שָׁקוּל הָיָה?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this** statement: This is the person **about whom** the prophet **wrote: "But there was none like Ahab who devoted himself to do evil in the eyes of the Lord, whom Jezebel his wife had incited"** (I Kings 21:25). **And we learn** in a *baraita*: **Every day she would weigh gold shekels** equal to Ahab's weight **for idol worship. And you say he was balanced? Rather, Ahab was generous with his money** and did not scrimp.

Rav Yosef objects: the verse says *asher hitmaker la'asot ha-ra be-einei Hashem* — he sold himself to do evil. A *beinoni* does not sell himself; only a fully committed *rasha* does.

Furthermore, *asher hesatah oto Izavel ishto* — Izavel seduced him. And the *baraita* records: *be-chol yom hayta shokelet shiklei zahav la-avodat kochavim* — every single day Izavel would weigh out gold shekels to finance *avodat kochavim*. Taken together — that he sold himself to evil and that Izavel actively drove the idolatrous enterprise — Rav Yosef concludes that Achav was a complete *rasha*, not a *beinoni*.

### Tzedakah Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אַחְאָב וַותְּרָן בְּמָמוֹנוֹ הָיָה, וּמִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהֶהֱנָה תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים מִנְּכָסָיו כִּיפְּרוּ לוֹ מֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **Ahab was generous with his money** and did not scrimp, **and since he** also **benefited Torah scholars with his property,** the heavenly court **expiated half** his sins **for him.** The result was a balance between mitzvot and transgressions.

The Gemara resolves this: *vatran be-mamoneh hayah* — Ahab was extremely generous with his money. *U-mitoch she-henah talmidei chachamim mi-nechasav, kiperu lo mechtzah* — and because Torah scholars benefited from his wealth, God waived half his sins. So while Rav Nachman was right that the scales appeared balanced — balanced enough that seduction was required to tip them — the reason for the balance was not that Achav was intrinsically a *beinoni* in his moral constitution. Rather, the extraordinary *tzedakah* of supporting Torah scholarship neutralized precisely half the weight of his transgressions. The *cheshbon* came out even, but only because of this one overriding merit. Once again the Gemara insists: one never knows the full weight of a single *mitzvah*, and the support of Torah study in particular carries consequences that may offset sins of the gravest order.
