# Horayot 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intent & Righteousness

### Praised King

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֲשֶׁר נָשִׂיא יֶחֱטָא״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: אַשְׁרֵיהַ הַדּוֹר שֶׁהַנָּשִׂיא שֶׁלּוֹ מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ. אִם נָשִׂיא שֶׁלּוֹ מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן, צָרִיךְ אַתָּה לוֹמַר מַהוּ הֶדְיוֹט! וְאִם עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן, צָרִיךְ אַתָּה לוֹמַר מַהוּ זְדוֹנוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse states concerning a king: **"When [*asher*] a king sins"** (Leviticus 4:22). **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said: Happy [*ashrei*] is the generation whose king** feels the need to **bring an offering for his unwitting** transgression. **If** the generation's **king brings an offering, you must say** all the more so **what a commoner** will do to atone for his sin, i.e., he will certainly bring an offering. **And if** the king **brings an offering for his unwitting** transgression, **you must say** all the more so **what** he will do to atone for **his intentional** transgression, i.e., he will certainly repent.

The daf picks up on 11b in the middle of an aggadic passage. The *gemara* had opened with a *drasha* on the verse *asher nasi yecheta* — praised is the king who is able to publicly acknowledge that he has sinned and brings a *korban*. That posture of public accountability is presented as a model for the generation. From there the *gemara* moved to the verse *yesh hevel* and reflected on the deeds of the *tzadikim* and *resha'im* — on the rewards and punishments they receive in this world — culminating in the principle *tzadikim yelchu bam u'poshim yichashlu bam*: the very same act can be a *mitzvah* for one person and an *aveirah* for another. That theme was then applied to Lot and his daughters: what for the daughters may have been a *mitzvah* could have been an *aveirah* for Lot. The role of intent, in other words, can completely transform the moral character of an act.

### Righteous & Wicked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: תָּמָר זִנְּתָה וְזִימְרִי זִינָּה. תָּמָר זִנְּתָה – יָצְאוּ מִמֶּנָּה מְלָכִים וּנְבִיאִים, זִימְרִי זִינָּה – נָפְלוּ כַּמָּה רְבָבוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: גְּדוֹלָה עֲבֵירָה לִשְׁמָהּ מִמִּצְוָה שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּבֹרַךְ מִנָּשִׁים יָעֵל אֵשֶׁת חֶבֶר הַקֵּינִי מִנָּשִׁים בָּאֹהֶל תְּבֹרָךְ״, מַאן נִינְהוּ ״נָשִׁים בָּאֹהֶל״? שָׂרָה, רִבְקָה, רָחֵל וְלֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Ulla says: Tamar engaged in licentiousness** with Judah (see Genesis, chapter 38), **and Zimri engaged in licentiousness** with Cozbi (see Numbers 25:6–9). **Tamar engaged in licentiousness,** and **kings and prophets emerged from her. Zimri engaged in licentiousness,** and **tens of thousands from the Jewish people fell. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: A transgression** performed **for the sake of** Heaven **is greater than a mitzva** performed **not for its own sake, as it is stated: "Blessed above women shall be Yael, the wife of Heber the Kenite, above women in the tent shall she be blessed"** (Judges 5:24). **Who are** these **"women in the tent"?** They are **Sarah, Rebecca, Rachel, and Leah,** and Yael is more blessed than they are. Apparently, a mitzva performed not for its own sake is a negative phenomenon.

With that framework in place, the *gemara* continues with a comparison that crystallizes the point. *Ve'amar Ulla*: Tamar fornicated, and Zimri fornicated. On the surface, the acts appear identical. Yet from Tamar's union with Yehuda descended the *malchus Beis David* — kings and prophets emerged from her — because her intent was *leshem shamayim*. Zimri, by contrast, acted either out of pure lust or, worse, as a form of protest against God, and the result was the death of many thousands of Israelites. The contrast in outcomes flows directly from the contrast in motivation. The same act, the *gemara* is insisting, carries an entirely different moral valence depending on the inner orientation of the one performing it.

### Tamar & Zimri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם יַעֲסוֹק אָדָם בְּתוֹרָה וּבְמִצְוֹת אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, שֶׁמִּתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ בָּא לִשְׁמָהּ. אֵימָא: כְּמִצְוָה שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says: A person should always engage in Torah** study **and** the performance of **mitzvot, even** if he does so **not for its** own **sake, as through** the performance of mitzvot **not for its** own **sake,** one gains understanding and **comes** to perform them **for its** own **sake.** Apparently, even when performed not for its own sake a mitzva is still a positive phenomenon. The Gemara emends the statement: **Say** that the status of a transgression performed for the sake of Heaven is **like** that of **a mitzva** performed **not for its** own **sake.**

The principle is then articulated more sharply. *Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak*: *gedola aveirah lishmah mimitzvah shelo lishmah* — great is a transgression done for the right intention, greater than a *mitzvah* done for the wrong intention. The proof is drawn from *Shiras Devorah*: *tevorach minashim Yael eshes Chever HaKeini, minashim baohel tevorach* — blessed above women shall Yael be, the wife of Chever the Kenite; above women in the tent shall she be blessed. The *gemara* identifies the women in the tent as Sarah, Rivka, Rachel, and Leah, on the basis of the verse *ayeh Sarah ishtecha? — hineh baohel*. The *Tosafot Rosh* explains why the Matriarchs are categorized as performing a *mitzvah shelo lishmah*: since the verse says nothing to the contrary, we assume that their marital intimacy involved, as is natural, an element of personal desire and satisfaction. That is not a condemnation — it is simply the ordinary human reality. Yael's act with Sisera, by contrast — which is the heavily implied subtext of the entire *Shiras Devorah* and which *Chazal* are not manufacturing but reading from the text — was performed by a married woman with another man, entirely without personal pleasure or self-interest. It was an act done purely *leshem shamayim*.

### Aveirah Lishmah

The statement that an *aveirah lishmah* is greater than a *mitzvah shelo lishmah* is, to use a scholarly term, an antinomian principle — one that runs against the entire legal framework. *Nomos* is law; antinomian means going against the system of law. The logic, taken to its extreme, suggests that as long as one's heart is in the right place, the nature of the act does not ultimately matter. One can easily see how this could justify terrible things. The Frankists — the spiritual descendants of Shabbetai Tzvi — operated very much within this kind of thinking, performing *aveiros* on the premise that bringing the *Mashiach* requires a generation that is entirely *chayav*, and that it is easier to achieve total *chayav* than total *zakai*.

### Yael Superior

And yet the *gemara* offers Yael as its proof text, and *Chazal* present her act with full seriousness. She is declared more blessed than the Matriarchs. The *gemara* then raises an objection from a principle attributed to Rav Yehuda citing Rav: *le'olam ya'asok adam baTorah u'vmitzos afilu shelo lishmah, shemitoch shelo lishmah ba lishmah* — a person should always engage in Torah and *mitzvos* even if not for the ideal motivation, because from *shelo lishmah* one ultimately comes to *lishmah*. The implication of this teaching is that *mitzvah shelo lishmah*, while not the ideal, is a positive thing — something to be pursued. The *gemara* therefore asks: if *shelo lishmah* is genuinely positive and praiseworthy, how can one say that an *aveirah lishmah* is greater? The objection only makes sense if *shelo lishmah* were clearly negative. But if it is affirmed as good — if one is positively encouraged to do *mitzvos* even without pure motivation — then how can a transgression, even one done with the purest intent, ever be declared superior?

### Mitzvah Value

The *gemara* responds by moderating the original formulation. *Eima ki mitzvah shelo lishmah* — read it not as "greater than," but as "equivalent to." An *aveirah lishmah* is not being declared superior to a *mitzvah shelo lishmah*; rather it is on the same level. Accordingly, the phrase *minashim baohel tevorach* is reread: Yael is not more blessed than the women of the tent, but blessed *as* those women — in the same category of blessing as Sarah, Rivka, Rachel, and Leah. This is a significantly less radical claim, and it resolves the tension with Rav Yehuda's principle.

### Reconciliation

The tension between the two teachings is thus formally resolved: *aveirah lishmah* is at most equivalent to *mitzvah shelo lishmah*, not superior to it. The *gemara* is careful not to entirely deprecate *shelo lishmah*, since the very point of Rav's principle is that it has genuine and significant value — and moreover, that it leads, over time, to genuine *lishmah*.

### Dangers & Exceptions

That said, this entire passage poses serious dangers. The principle that the ends justify the means is one that *halacha* as a system is broadly designed to resist. *Halacha* insists on the rightness of the particular act — not merely the righteousness of the larger goal. There are *halachic* mechanisms, like *pikuach nefesh* overriding *Shabbat*, that allow one consideration to override another, but those operate within the *halachic* framework itself. The act of saving a life is itself recognized as an act of *mitzvah* — it is not a case of doing something prohibited for the sake of a greater good outside the system.

There are, to be sure, rare and extreme cases — one thinks of figures like Yael herself, or situations where *klal Yisrael* faces existential threats — where the circumstances seem to demand acts that cannot be fully contained within any legal framework. A legal system cannot anticipate every reality. But even acknowledging this, one must insist that these are the rarest of exceptions, and that giving general license to *aveirah lishmah* has historically produced catastrophic results when misused. The Chassidic literature engages seriously with the power of intention and passion, and there is a Chassidic teaching that the problem with the apikorsim of a certain era was not their rejection of a false conception of God — that rejection may have reflected genuine spiritual striving — but their apathy and indifference, which is itself a profound spiritual failure. As Rav Kook framed it: passionate rejection is at least a form of engagement; apathy is not. In that spirit, one can say that what *Chazal* are really pointing at here is not an invitation to sin, but a warning against the spiritual deadness of rote observance without any inner life.

### Esther & Examples

A student raised the example of Esther and Achashverosh as a case where *halacha* attempts to contain what appears to be a similarly exceptional situation. The *gemara* does indeed work to bring Esther's case within its framework — *Esther karka olam*, she was passive — though the fit is admittedly strained. Similarly, one might think of modern cases involving intelligence operatives who must perform deeply compromising acts in the service of a larger protective mission. These cases are real, and they do not resolve cleanly into standard *halachic* categories.

### Passion vs Apathy

The point that emerges from the entire discussion — one worth holding onto — is that what the *gemara* is ultimately warning against is not the failure to be a perfect *tzadik*, but the failure to care at all. The comparison between *aveirah lishmah* and *mitzvah shelo lishmah* is really, at one level, a comparison between passionate wrongdoing and passionless correctness. And Rav Kook's insight is precisely that passion — even misdirected passion — contains within it something that apathy does not. That does not make *aveirah* permissible. But it reframes what is at stake: the *gemara* is not encouraging transgression; it is insisting on the irreplaceable importance of inner life.

## Sisera's Acts

### Seven Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁבַע בְּעִילוֹת בָּעַל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בֵּין רַגְלֶיהָ כָּרַע נָפַל שָׁכָב וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Yael, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: That wicked** man Sisera **performed seven** acts of **intercourse** with Yael **at that time, as it is stated: "Between her legs he crouched, he fell, he lay;** between her legs he crouched, he fell; where he crouched, there he fell dead"** (Judges 5:27). Each of the seven verbs is a euphemism for intercourse.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: Sisera performed seven acts of intercourse with Yael at that moment, as derived from the repeated language in the verse: *bein ragleha kara nafal, bein ragleha kara nafal, ba'asher kara sham nafal shadud*. Counting all the verbal repetitions of bowing and falling, one arrives at the number seven. This is not a reading *Chazal* are imposing on the text — Bible scholars note that the verse's imagery clearly implies sexual intercourse.

### Pleasure Question

This immediately raises an obvious difficulty. How can Yael's act be described as *lishmah*, done purely for the sake of Heaven, if she was clearly experiencing physical pleasure? She was not forced — she was seducing him. How then can the *gemara* describe her motivation as entirely without self-interest?

### Good of Wicked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָא מִיתְהַנְיָא מֵעֲבֵירָה? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: אֲפִילּוּ טוֹבָתָם שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים רָעָה הִיא אֵצֶל צַדִּיקִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't she experience pleasure from** the **transgression** of engaging in intercourse with Sisera? Why does the verse praise her? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: Even the good** provided **by the wicked is bad for the righteous,** so Yael did not experience any pleasure from her intercourse with Sisera.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*: *afilu tovatan shel resha'im ra'ah hi eitzel tzadikim* — even the good that the wicked provide is considered evil and repugnant when received by the righteous. That is: even something that might otherwise register as pleasurable, when it comes from someone as morally corrupt as Sisera, is experienced by a person of genuine righteousness as something disgusting rather than satisfying. In Yael's case, whatever physical sensation occurred carried no positive valence for her precisely because of the nature of the source.

### Shelo Lishmah Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם יַעֲסוֹק אָדָם בַּתּוֹרָה וּבַמִּצְווֹת אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, שֶׁמִּתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ בָּא לִשְׁמָהּ, שֶׁבִּשְׂכַר אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁנַיִם קׇרְבָּנוֹת שֶׁהִקְרִיב בָּלָק הָרָשָׁע, זָכָה וְיָצְתָה מִמֶּנּוּ רוּת, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: רוּת בַּת בְּנוֹ שֶׁל עֶגְלוֹן, בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל בָּלָק מֶלֶךְ מוֹאָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A person should always engage in Torah** study **and** the performance of **mitzvot, even** if he does so **not for its own sake, as through** the performance of mitzvot **not for its own sake,** one gains understanding and **comes** to perform them **for its own sake.** Proof for this can be adduced from the incident involving Balak, **as in reward for the forty-two offerings that Balak the wicked sacrificed** to God, despite the fact that he did this in order to curse the Jewish people (see Numbers, chapter 23), **he merited and Ruth emerged from him, as Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: Ruth was the daughter of the son of Eglon, the son of the son of Balak, king of Moab.**

The *gemara* then returns to Rav Yehuda's principle — *le'olam ya'asok adam baTorah u'vmitzos afilu shelo lishmah, shemitoch shelo lishmah ba lishmah* — reaffirming it here in the wake of all that has been said about the value of *lishmah*. The point is important: even after establishing how exceptional it is to act purely *lishmah*, and even after comparing *aveirah lishmah* favorably to *mitzvah shelo lishmah*, the *gemara* does not abandon the value of the act itself. Do the *mitzvah* even for less than pure reasons, because that practice will, one hopes, lead over time to doing it for the right reasons. It does not always work out that way — many people, as the *poskim* note, end up with what is called *mitzvos anashim melumada*, purely habituated practice that never generates genuine inner motivation. But the hope is there, and the value of the act remains.

### Balak's Merit

The *gemara* then offers a striking illustration of *shelo lishmah* in an even more extreme register. *She'bischar arbaim u'shtayim korbanot she'hikriv Balak harasha* — in the merit of the forty-two *korbanot* that Balak the wicked brought, *zachah ve'yatzah mimenu Ruth* — he merited that Ruth descended from him. As Rashi explains, from Ruth came David, and David praised and sang to God — an echo, in some sense, of those *korbanot*. The *gemara* notes the genealogical chain: *de'amar Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina* — Ruth was the daughter of the son of Eglon, the son of Balak, king of Moav. The *korbanot* were brought in a context intended to harm Israel — Bilaam recognized God and sought prophetic access through these offerings in order to curse the people. This is worse than ordinary *shelo lishmah*; it is *korbanot* brought for an evil purpose. And yet God did not ignore the act entirely.

### Reward for Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְקַפֵּחַ אֲפִילּוּ שְׂכַר שִׂיחָה נָאָה? מֵהָכָא, דְּאִילּוּ בְּכִירָה דִּקְרָיתֵיהּ מוֹאָב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לְמֹשֶׁה: ״אַל תָּצַר אֶת מוֹאָב וְאַל תִּתְגָּר בָּם מִלְחָמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos Lot and his daughters, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From where** is it derived **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, does not withhold even** the **reward for euphemistic speech?** It is derived **from here, as the elder** daughter **called her son Moab,** an allusion to the fact that the child is from her own father [*me'av*], and **the Merciful One said to Moses: "Be not at enmity with Moab, neither contend with them in battle"** (Deuteronomy 2:9).

*Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan*: where do we know that God does not withhold from a person even the reward for pleasant and gracious speech? From the following case.

### Lot's Daughters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּלָא, הָא צַעוֹרֵי צַעֲרִינְהוּ. וְאִילּוּ צְעִירָה דִּקְרָיתֵיהּ ״בֶּן עַמִּי״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אַל תְּצֻרֵם וְאַל תִּתְגָּר בָּם״, כְּלָל, אֲפִילּוּ צַעוֹרֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **that** one may **not** contend with them, **but** it is permitted **to harass them. While** concerning the offspring of **the younger daughter, who called** her son **ben Ami,** son of my people, avoiding any direct mention of the baby's father, God **said to** Moses: **"Neither harass them, nor contend with them"** (Deuteronomy 2:19), **at all. Even** to **harass** them is **not** permitted.

The older daughter named her son Moav — *me'av*, from the father — a name that is blunt and somewhat shameless in its declaration of the child's origins. The younger daughter, by contrast, named her son Ben-Ami — son of my nation — a name that is vague and modest. God's instruction to Moshe regarding Moav is *al tatzar et Moav ve'al tityar bam milchama* — do not distress them and do not provoke them to war. The *gemara* reads this carefully: *milchama hu delo* — war is prohibited, but *tziyurei tzareinhu* — causing them some measure of anguish or hardship is apparently permitted, the word *al tatzar* notwithstanding. For Ammon, whose ancestress was more modest in her naming, the formulation is more complete: *al tatzurem ve'al tityar bam*, with even minor anguish prohibited. The reward for the younger daughter's modest phrasing is the greater protection God extends to her descendants.

### Priority Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: לְעוֹלָם יַקְדִּים אָדָם לִדְבַר מִצְוָה, שֶׁבִּשְׂכַר לַיְלָה אַחַת שֶׁקָּדְמָה בְּכִירָה לַצְּעִירָה, זָכְתָה וּקְדָמַתָּה אַרְבַּע דּוֹרוֹת לַמַּלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: A person should always be first to** perform **a matter of a mitzva, as in reward for one night that** the **elder** daughter **preceded the younger** daughter, **she merited and preceded her to royalty** by **four generations.** Ruth the Moabite, ancestor of King David, descended from her son Moab, and she preceded Naamah the Ammonite, who was married to King Solomon, by four generations.

*Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Avin amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha*: *le'olam yakdim adam li'devar mitzvah* — one should always run to seize a *mitzvah* before it passes. *She'bischar layla achat she'kidmata bechira li'tze'irah* — in the merit of the one night by which the older daughter preceded the younger in initiating the act, she merited that her line entered the *malchut* of Israel four generations before the Ammonite line. The older daughter gave rise to Moav, from whom Ruth descended, from whom David descended. The Ammonite line entered Israelite kingship through Na'amah Ha-Ammonit, who was the mother of Rechavam ben Shlomo — four generations after David. Whatever one makes of the moral status of the daughters' initiative — and the *gemara* has already addressed this through the principle of *tzadikim yelchu bam* — the older daughter's urgency in pressing forward earned her a temporal priority in the eventual unfolding of providential history.

## Korban Classes

### Me'am Ha'aretz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ״ – פְּרָט לְמָשִׁיחַ. ״מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ״ – פְּרָט לְנָשִׂיא,
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And if one soul from among the common people sins unwittingly in performing one of the mitzvot of God that may not be done and he is guilty" (Leviticus 4:27). The phrase **"from among the common people"** serves **to exclude the anointed** priest; the phrase **"from among the common people"** also serves **to exclude the king.**

The *gemara* now returns from the *agadata* to *halacha*. A *baraisa* teaches: the verse specifying the standard *korban chatat* says *me'am ha'aretz* — from the people of the land. This phrase is expounded to exclude the *Kohen Gadol* (*mashiach*) and to exclude the *nasi* from the standard sin offering that an ordinary Israelite would bring.

### Kohen Gadol Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר יָצְאוּ מָשִׁיחַ לִידּוֹן בְּפַר, נָשִׂיא לִידּוֹן בְּשָׂעִיר! שֶׁיָּכוֹל: מָשִׁיחַ עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה מֵבִיא פַּר, עַל שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה לְחוֹדֵיהּ מֵבִיא כִּשְׂבָּה וּשְׂעִירָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ״ – פְּרָט לְמָשִׁיחַ. ״מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ״ – פְּרָט לְנָשִׂיא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **But weren't** these individuals already **excluded** from bringing a ewe or a female goat as a sin-offering, as **an anointed** priest is **subject to** atonement **with a bull,** and **a king** is **subject to** atonement **with a male goat?** Why then is an additional exclusionary derivation necessary? The *baraita* answers: The derivation is necessary, **as** one **might** have thought that **an anointed priest brings a bull for absence** of awareness **of the matter** together **with the unwitting** performance **of an action,** but that **he brings a ewe or a female goat,** as does a non-priest, **for the unwitting** performance **of an action alone.** Therefore, **the verse states: "From among the common people,"** which serves **to exclude the anointed** priest, who is completely exempt from bringing a sin-offering for an unwitting transgression unless it was committed on the basis of his own erroneous ruling. The *baraita* concludes: The phrase **"from among the common people"** serves **to exclude the king.**

One might ask: why do we need a verse to exclude them? We already know from elsewhere that the *Kohen Gadol*'s special *korban* — the *par* — applies only when there is a combination of *psak* and *ma'aseh*, a mistaken ruling that he himself acted upon. When he simply performs a sinful act in error without that combination of conditions, perhaps he should bring the standard *chatat* of an ordinary person — a she-goat or a she-lamb. The verse *me'am ha'aretz* therefore teaches that the *Kohen Gadol* is entirely excluded from the standard *chatat*. He brings nothing when he commits a normal error in action alone. The only time he brings a *korban* is when the full conditions for the *Kohen Gadol*'s *korban* are met — *psak* and *ma'aseh* together.

### Nasi Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח מָשִׁיחַ, אֶלָּא נָשִׂיא בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה הוּא דְּמַיְיתֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Granted,** in the case of **an anointed** priest it is clear why an additional exclusionary derivation is necessary. **But** in the case of **a king, it is for the unwitting** performance **of an action that he brings** a goat as his offering. Why then is the additional derivation necessary?

The exclusion of the *nasi* raises a different question. Unlike the *Kohen Gadol*, the *nasi*'s special *korban* — the *se'ir* — is triggered by *shegegat ma'aseh* alone, just like the standard *chatat* of an ordinary person. Since the Torah already assigns the *nasi* his own *korban* for the same circumstances as the ordinary *chatat*, he is implicitly excluded from the standard one. Why then does the verse need to exclude him explicitly? *Amar Rav Zevid mishmei de-Rava*: the case being addressed is one where a person ate a *kezayis* of *chelev* when he was a commoner, was then appointed king, and subsequently became aware of his sin. One might have thought that since the sin occurred when he was a commoner, he should bring the *chatat* of a commoner. The verse therefore teaches that even in this case — where the sin was committed as a commoner but awareness arrived after becoming *nasi* — he does not bring the ordinary *chatat*. According to Rabbi Shimon, who holds that the relevant status is determined not only by when one sinned but also by when one became aware, the *nasi* who sinned as a commoner and became aware as a king would not bring a commoner's *korban* precisely because his awareness occurred when he was already a *nasi*. This accounts for Rabbi Shimon's position. According to the *Rabbanan*, however, who follow only the time of the sin, such a person would actually bring the commoner's *chatat* — and so the verse cannot be addressing that case. The *gemara* therefore reformulates the scenario.

### Half & Half Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכַל כְּזַיִת חֵלֶב כְּשֶׁהוּא הֶדְיוֹט, וְנִתְמַנָּה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹדַע לוֹ. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא נַיְיתֵי כִּשְׂבָּה אוֹ שְׂעִירָה, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן. הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָזֵל בָּתַר יְדִיעָה, אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן (דְּאָזְלוּ) [דְּאָזְלִי] בָּתַר חֲטָאָה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said in the name of Rava: What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where one** unwittingly **ate an olive-bulk of** forbidden **fat when he** was **a commoner, and he was** then **appointed** king, **and thereafter** his transgression **became known to him.** It could **enter your mind to say: Let him bring a ewe or a female goat** because he performed the transgression before he became king. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that he brings a male goat as he is now a king. The Gemara challenges: **This works out well according to Rabbi Shimon, who follows** the time of **knowledge,** and holds that one brings the offering on the basis of his status at the time when his transgression became known to him. **But according to the Rabbis, who follow** the time of the performance of the **sin, what can be said?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכַל חֲצִי כְּזַיִת חֵלֶב כְּשֶׁהוּא הֶדְיוֹט, וְנִתְמַנָּה וְהִשְׁלִימוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹדַע לוֹ. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא נִצְטְרֵף וְנַיְיתֵי כִּשְׂבָּה אוֹ שְׂעִירָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Zevid said in the name of Rava: What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where one** unwittingly **ate half an olive-bulk of** forbidden **fat when he** was **a commoner, and he was** then **appointed** king, **and he** then **finished** eating the olive-bulk of forbidden fat, **and thereafter it became known to him** that he had eaten forbidden fat. It could **enter your mind to say: Let** the two halves **combine and let him bring a ewe or a female goat.** Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that the status of a king is not like that of a commoner. Since a king brings a special offering, the half olive-bulk that he ate before becoming king does not combine with the half olive-bulk that he ate as a king.

*Ela amar Rav Zevid mishmei de-Rava*: the actual case the verse is speaking to is as follows. A person ate half a *kezayis* of *chelev* as a commoner, was then appointed king, and — still within the time of *toch k'dei achilas pras*, the halachically defined window within which two acts of eating are considered a single continuous act — completed eating the remaining half of the *kezayis*, after which he became aware of what he had done. Here, half the minimum measure was consumed as a commoner and half as a king. One might have thought that since the act began as a commoner, the entire act should be attributed to his status as a commoner, the two halves combining to yield a single complete sin for which he would bring a commoner's *chatat*. The verse therefore teaches that even so, becoming king mid-act is sufficient to prevent the two halves from combining. The result is that he brings neither the *nasi*'s *korban* — because he did not complete the act as a *nasi* — nor the ordinary *chatat* — because he did not complete the act as a commoner. He brings nothing.

For the *Kohen Gadol*, the *gemara* notes, there are many such situations where he brings nothing at all — any time he commits an ordinary error in action without the full conditions of *psak* and *ma'aseh*, he brings no *korban*. But the *nasi*, under normal circumstances, always brings either the *se'ir* if he sinned as a king or the *se'irah* or *kisbah* if he sinned as a commoner. The only case where he brings nothing is precisely this hybrid scenario — half as a commoner, half as a king.

### Status Change

This raises a further question. The royal family presumably knows from birth who its members are. One does not become *nasi* as a total surprise. And yet the *gemara* operates with the assumption that a member of the Davidic family is fully a *hedyot* — a commoner — until the very moment of formal appointment. That moment of *nismaneh* transforms his status entirely, mid-act if necessary.

### Kingship Interrupt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: נְשִׂיאוּת מַהוּ שֶׁתַּפְסִיק, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכַל חֲצִי כְּזַיִת חֵלֶב כְּשֶׁהוּא הֶדְיוֹט, וְנִתְמַנָּה וְעָבַר, וְהִשְׁלִימוֹ – הָתָם הוּא דְּלָא מִצְטְרֵף, דְּאַכְלֵיהּ פַּלְגָא כְּשֶׁהוּא הֶדְיוֹט וּפַלְגָא כְּשֶׁהוּא נָשִׂיא, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי כְּשֶׁהוּא הֶדְיוֹט אַכְלֵיהּ מִצְטְרֵף, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman:** With regard to **kingship, what is** the *halakha*? Does it **interpose** between two parts of a transgression and prevent them from combining? **What are the circumstances?** It is a case **where one** unwittingly **ate half an olive-bulk of** forbidden **fat when he** was **a commoner, and he was** then **appointed** king, **and** then was **removed** from his position, **and** then **finished** eating the olive-bulk of forbidden fat. Perhaps **it is there,** in the previous case, **that** the two actions **do not combine, as he ate half when he** was **a commoner and half when he** was **king. But here, where he ate** both **this** half **and that** half **when he** was **a commoner,** they **combine. Or perhaps it is no different,** and once he was appointed king and his status changed, the two actions do not combine. **What** is the *halakha*?

*Ba'i minei Rava me-Rav Nachman*: does *nesi'us* — becoming king — interrupt a sin? The case Rava poses is one step more complex than the previous scenario. Suppose a person ate half a *kezayis* of *chelev* as a commoner. He was then appointed king. Then — somehow, through contested succession or a change of circumstances — the appointment was reversed, and he was no longer king. All of this occurred within *toch k'dei achilas pras*. He then completed eating the second half of the *kezayis*, again as a commoner. Now both halves of the act were performed as a commoner, with only a brief interlude of kingship in between. Do the two halves combine?

The intuitive difference from the previous case, Rava notes, is the following: *hasam hu d'lo mitztaref* — in that case, combination was prevented because one half was as a commoner and one half as a *nasi*, *palga d'hedyot palga d'Nasi*. Here, *d'hai v'hai k'shehu hedyot achal* — both halves were consumed as a commoner. Does the brief interlude of kingship, even though it concluded before the act was complete, constitute enough of an interruption to prevent combination? Or, since he was a commoner at both ends, do they combine as though the interruption had not occurred?

### Unresolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מֵהָא דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָכַל חֵלֶב וְהִפְרִישׁ קׇרְבָּן וְנִשְׁתַּמֵּד וְחָזַר בּוֹ, הוֹאִיל וְנִדְחָה – יִדָּחֶה. הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? מְשׁוּמָּד לָאו בַּר אֵתוֹיֵי קׇרְבָּן הוּא, הַאי בַּר אֵתוֹיֵי קׇרְבָּן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Resolve** the dilemma **from that which Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If **one ate** forbidden **fat and designated an offering** for his unwitting sin, **and he became an apostate, and** then **retracted** his apostasy, **since he was disqualified** from bringing the offering as an apostate **he shall** remain **disqualified** from bringing an offering for that sin. The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? An apostate is ineligible** as far as **bringing an offering** of any kind is concerned; **this** king **is eligible** with regard to **bringing an offering,** albeit of a different type.

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the principle of *dichuy* cited in the name of Ulla and Rabbi Yochanan: if someone ate *chelev*, separated a *korban*, and then became a *mumar* — such that the *korban* could not be brought — and subsequently repented, the *korban* remains rejected. *Ho'il venidcha yidacheh* — once it has been pushed away, it stays pushed away. Could the same apply here — once the act was interrupted by kingship, even if the kingship passed, does it remain interrupted?

The *gemara* rejects the comparison. *Mumar lav bar asuyei korban hu* — a *mumar* is entirely removed from the world of *korbanot*; his *korban* was pushed away by someone fundamentally excluded from that system. Here, even as a king, the person remained within the world of *korbanot* — it was simply a different *korban* that would have applied. Furthermore, in the *dichuy* case, a *korban* had already been designated and separated; it was that specific *korban* that became *dichuy*. Here, no *korban* was ever separated — there was only an act and an interruption. So the comparison does not hold. The question remains unresolved.

### Safek Chelev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אָכַל סָפֵק חֵלֶב כְּשֶׁהוּא הֶדְיוֹט, וְנִתְמַנָּה וְנוֹדַע לוֹ עַל סְפֵקוֹ, מַהוּ? אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן דְּאָזְלִי בָּתַר חֲטָאָה לָא תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּמַיְיתֵי אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, אֶלָּא כִּי תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ – אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet:** If **one ate** meat **when he was a commoner,** and there was **uncertainty** as to whether or not it was forbidden **fat, and** subsequently he **was appointed** king **and his uncertainty became known to him, what is** the *halakha*? **According to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who follow** the time of the actual **transgression, do not raise the dilemma, as** in their opinion, he is liable to **bring a provisional guilt-offering. Rather, when you raise the dilemma** it is **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who follows the time of awareness of the transgression.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּאִשְׁתַּנִּי לְוַדַּאי, אִשְׁתַּנִּי לְסָפֵק, אוֹ דִלְמָא כִּי אִשְׁתַּנִּי לְוַדַּאי, דְּאִשְׁתַּנִּי קׇרְבָּן דִּידֵיהּ – אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָא אִשְׁתַּנִּי קׇרְבָּן דִּידֵיהּ, אֵימָא לַיְיתֵי אָשָׁם תָּלוּי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Is the *halakha* that **from** the fact **that** his status **changed with regard to a definite** transgression, as, if he was liable to bring a sin-offering and his sin became known to him after his coronation, he is completely exempt, it may be derived that his status **changed with regard to an uncertain** transgression as well, and he is exempt from bringing a provisional guilt-offering? **Or perhaps when** his status **changed,** it was **with regard to a definite** transgression only, **as** in that case **his offering changed,** since a king brings a goat as a sin-offering instead of a ewe or a female goat. **But here,** in the case of an uncertain transgression, **as his offering does not change, say: Let him bring a provisional guilt-offering.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

*Ba'ei minei Rav Zeira me-Rav Sheshes*: a separate but related question. Suppose a person ate *safek chelev* — something that might have been forbidden fat — as a commoner. Normally, such a person would bring an *asham taluy*, the provisional guilt offering brought when one suspects one has committed a sin for which a *chatat* would otherwise be required. This person was then appointed *nasi* and subsequently became aware of his *safek chelev* in his new status. What does he bring?

The complexity here turns on a structural question about the *asham taluy*. The *asham taluy* is keyed to an underlying *chatat*: if one definitely sinned, one would bring a *chatat*; the *asham taluy* is brought when that underlying obligation is in doubt. Now, the *chatat* that would be brought by a commoner who definitely ate *chelev* is different in kind from the *chatat* that would be brought by a *nasi* who did so. When his status changes from commoner to *nasi*, does the underlying *chatat* change? And if so, does his relationship to the *asham taluy* change accordingly?

### Rabbanan View

According to the *Rabbanan*, who determine the relevant *korban* based on the status of the sinner at the time of the sin, there is no question. The sin occurred when he was a commoner; therefore the applicable *chatat* is that of a commoner; therefore the *asham taluy* — which reflects that underlying *chatat* — is also that of a commoner, and he brings it. Status at the time of awareness is irrelevant.

### Rabbi Shimon View

According to Rabbi Shimon, however, who requires that both the sin and the awareness of the sin occur under the same status in order to generate an obligation to bring a *korban*, the question is live. Since his status has changed, does this affect his obligation in the realm of the *asham taluy* as well? The *gemara* frames two possibilities. On one hand: *midi de'ishtani levadai ishtani lesafek* — since his status has changed with respect to what *chatat* he would bring if he definitely sinned, his status in the realm of the *asham taluy* — which presupposes that underlying *chatat* — has changed as well, and he therefore brings nothing. On the other hand: perhaps when it comes to the *asham taluy* itself, the *korban* is the same whether brought by a commoner or a *nasi*; the actual *asham taluy* offering does not differ between the two. If so, one might argue that his status with respect to the *asham taluy* has not changed in any meaningful way, and he should still bring it.

### Teiku Status

The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

## Mumar Definition

### Mumar Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ״ – פְּרָט לִמְשׁוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** The verse states: "And if one soul **from among the common people** sins unwittingly in performing one of the mitzvot of God that may not be done and he is guilty" (Leviticus 4:27). This serves **to exclude an apostate.** When an apostate sins unwittingly, he is exempt from liability to bring a sin-offering even if he repents for that sin, as even his unwitting action is considered intentional.

A *baraisa* derives from the verse *me'am ha'aretz* the exclusion not only of the *nasi* and *Kohen Gadol* but also of a *mumar* — an apostate — from bringing a *korban chatat*. Rabbi Shimon bar Yosi, citing Rabbi Shimon, anchors this exclusion in the verse *asher lo se'asenah* — "which should not be done." The verse describes the one who brings the *chatat* as someone for whom the sin was a *shegaga*, an unintentional act.

### Shav B'Yedi'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא תֵעָשֶׂינָה בִּשְׁגָגָה וְאָשֵׁם״, הַשָּׁב בִּידִיעָתוֹ – מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ, לֹא שָׁב בִּידִיעָתוֹ – אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Shimon bar Yosei says in the name of Rabbi Shimon:** It is unnecessary to derive this *halakha* from that phrase, as it says in the same verse: "If any one of the common people sins **unwittingly** in performing one of the mitzvot of God **that may not be done and he is guilty;** or if his sin, which he has sinned, be known to him" (Leviticus 4:27–28). From the words "be known to him" it is inferred that only **one who repents due to his awareness,** i.e., who would not have sinned had he known that the act was forbidden, **brings an offering for his unwitting** transgression and achieves atonement in this way. But one who does **not repent due to his awareness** that he sinned, e.g., an apostate, who would sin even after becoming aware that the act is forbidden, **does not bring an offering for his unwitting** action.

Rabbi Shimon's *drasha* reads the condition this way: the person eligible to bring a *korban* is one who, had he known better, would have refrained — *shav biyedi'ah*. The korban is for someone who, on reflection, recognizes the act as a sin and would not have committed it had he been aware of its nature.

### Lo Shav Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי הַמְנוּנָא: מְשׁוּמָּד לֶאֱכוֹל חֵלֶב וּמֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן עַל הַדָּם – אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. מָר סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דִּמְשׁוּמָּד לֶאֱכוֹל חֵלֶב – לְדָם נָמֵי מְשׁוּמָּד הָוֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: לְדָם מִיהָא שָׁב בִּידִיעָתוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the practical difference **between** the opinions of the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon concerning whether the *halakha* is derived from the earlier or later verse? **Rav Hamnuna said:** The difference **between them** is in the case of **an apostate with regard to eating** forbidden **fat;** they disagree as to whether or not **he brings an offering for** unwittingly consuming **blood.** One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds: Since he is an apostate with regard to eating** forbidden **fat, he is also** considered **an apostate with regard to** consuming **blood. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds: With regard to** consuming **blood, in any event, he is** one who **repents due to his awareness,** as he is not considered an apostate with regard to blood.

By contrast, *lo shav biyedi'ah* — one who would have acted the same way even had he known it was prohibited — does not bring a *korban*. Two distinct approaches emerge from the *baraisa* to explain this exclusion. The first focuses on the personal status of the individual: a *mumar* has, by virtue of his apostasy, exited the category of those who bring *korbanot*, regardless of the nature of the specific act. The second focuses on the character of the action itself: the verse limits the *chatat* to acts that the person themselves would have wanted to avoid had they known; since the *mumar* would have done it anyway, this particular act does not qualify as the kind of *shegaga* that generates a *korban* obligation.

### Mumar Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רָבָא אָמַר: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מְשׁוּמָּד לֶאֱכוֹל חֵלֶב לָא הָוֵי מְשׁוּמָּד לְדָם! אֶלָּא, הָכָא בְּאוֹכֵל נְבֵלָה לְתֵאָבוֹן וְנִתְחַלֵּף לוֹ בְּשׁוּמָּן וַאֲכָלוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דִּלְתֵאָבוֹן אָכֵיל בְּמֵזִיד – מְשׁוּמָּד הוּא, וּמָר סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דְּאִילּוּ אַשְׁכַּח דְּהֶיתֵּרָא לָא אֲכַל דְּאִיסּוּרָא – לָאו מְשׁוּמָּד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rava say that everyone agrees** that **an apostate with regard to eating** forbidden **fat is not** considered **an apostate with regard to** consuming **blood?** The Gemara answers: **Rather, here** it is **with regard to** a person who **eats** forbidden fat and **an animal carcass due to appetite,** e.g., only when he does not have access to kosher meat. **And** forbidden fat **became confused for** that person **with** permitted **fat and he ate** the forbidden fat. It is in that case that the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon **disagree.** One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds: Since he intentionally eats** forbidden fat **due to appetite, he is an apostate. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds: Since if he finds** food **that** is **permitted he does not eat** food **that** is **prohibited,** as he merely seeks to satiate his appetite, **he is not an apostate.**

*Amar Rav Hamnuna*: the practical difference between these two approaches emerges in the case of a *mumar le'chelev* — someone who has habitually and deliberately eaten forbidden fat — who then eats blood in error. If the relevant factor is personal status, one might argue that since this person is a *mumar* in one domain, his status as a *mumar* extends globally, and he therefore does not bring a *korban* even for a sin in a different domain. If the relevant factor is the character of the action, then since this person does observe the prohibition of blood and would have refrained had he known better, he does bring a *korban* for that particular act.

### Rava Position

Rava, however, disputes the premise of this distinction. *De'chulei alma mumar le'chelev lo havei mumar le'dam* — according to all opinions, being a *mumar* in one area does not generate a *mumar* status in a different, unrelated area. One might say that a person who is *mechallel Shabbat* or who commits *avodah zarah* has in some sense rejected the entire system, such that a global exclusion might be warranted. But a *mumar* who has rejected only a specific prohibition — *chelev*, for instance — retains normal status with respect to other prohibitions. In the world of *dam*, such a person is not a *mumar*, and would therefore bring a *korban* for an unintentional violation. This means the distinction between the two approaches cannot be illustrated by the *chelev*-*dam* case, since both approaches yield the same conclusion there. The *gemara* therefore reframes the debate.

### Mumar Teivon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָכַל חֵלֶב – זֶהוּ מְשׁוּמָּד, וְאֵיזֶהוּ מְשׁוּמָּד? אָכַל נְבֵילוֹת וּטְרֵיפוֹת, שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים, וְשָׁתָה יֵין נֶסֶךְ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַלּוֹבֵשׁ כִּלְאַיִם. אָמַר מָר: אָכַל חֵלֶב זֶהוּ מְשׁוּמָּד, וְאֵיזֶהוּ מְשׁוּמָּד? אוֹכֵל נְבֵילוֹת כּוּ׳. מַאי קָאָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** One who **ate** forbidden **fat is an apostate. And who is an apostate?** It is one who **ate animal carcasses or animals with wounds that will cause them to die within twelve months [*tereifot*], repugnant creatures or creeping animals, and** one who **drank wine** used for **a libation** in idol worship. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** This applies **even** to **one who wears** garments fashioned of **diverse kinds,** containing wool and linen. The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*. **The Master said:** One who **ate** forbidden **fat is an apostate. And who is an apostate?** It is one who **ate animal carcasses, etc.** The Gemara asks: **What is he saying?** Why, after answering the question, does the *tanna* ask who is an apostate and then provide a different answer?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אָכַל חֵלֶב לְתֵאָבוֹן – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּמָּד, לְהַכְעִיס – הֲרֵי זֶה מִין. וְאֵיזֶהוּ מְשׁוּמָּד דְּבִסְתָמוֹ מִין? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: אוֹכֵל נְבֵילָה וּטְרֵיפָה, שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים, וְשָׁתָה יֵין נֶסֶךְ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַלּוֹבֵשׁ כִּלְאַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: This** is what **he is saying:** If **one ate** forbidden **fat due to appetite, he is an apostate.** If he ate it **to express insolence, this** person is **a heretic. And which is** the **apostate who is a presumptive heretic** merely on the basis of his actions? **You must say** that it is one who **eats an animal carcass or a *tereifa*, repugnant creatures or creeping animals, and** one who **drank wine** used for **a libation** in idol worship. Based on the fact that he violates serious transgressions for which one has no appetite such as repugnant creatures or creeping animals, it is clear that he is a heretic who denies the Torah in its entirety. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** This applies **even** to **one who wears** garments fashioned of **diverse kinds,** containing wool and linen.

*Amar Rabbah bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the *baraita* is presenting two distinct categories. The first — *ochel chelev l'teiavon* — is someone who eats *chelev* out of appetite and desire; this is a *mumar* in the ordinary sense. The second — the one who eats *neveilos* and the rest — is someone about whom we presume a more fundamental rejection, and this person is a *min*.

### Kilayim Mumar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ כִּלְאַיִם דְּרַבָּנַן. מָר סָבַר: מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הָוֵי מְשׁוּמָּד, דְּרַבָּנַן לָא הָוֵי מְשׁוּמָּד. וּמָר סָבַר: כִּלְאַיִם כֵּיוָן דִּמְפַרְסַם אִסּוּרֵיהּ, אֲפִילּוּ בִּדְרַבָּנַן הָוֵי מְשׁוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the practical difference **between** the opinion of the Rabbis and that of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda? The Gemara answers: The difference between them is in the case of one who wears a garment of **diverse kinds,** containing wool and linen prohibited **by rabbinic law.** One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds:** One who violates a prohibition **by Torah law is an apostate;** one who violates a prohibition **by rabbinic law is not an apostate. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds:** With regard to **diverse kinds, since his** violation of the **prohibition is well known,** as people see that he is wearing that garment, **even** though he violates a prohibition **by rabbinic law, he is an apostate.**

Rabbi Yehuda adds: even one who wears *kilayim* — forbidden mixtures of wool and linen — is in the category of *mumar*. The *gemara* discusses whether this is elaborating on the *mumar l'teiavon* category or the *mumar lehachis* category, with Rashi reading it as extending the *mumar l'teiavon* case to include even relatively minor areas of law such as *kilayim*.

### Teivon vs Hachis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָמַר: לְתֵאָבוֹן – מְשׁוּמָּד, לְהַכְעִיס – מִין. וְחַד אָמַר: לְהַכְעִיס נָמֵי מְשׁוּמָּד, אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ מִין? כָּל הָעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. מֵיתִיבִי: אָכַל פַּרְעוֹשׁ אֶחָד אוֹ יַתּוּשׁ אֶחָד – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּמָּד. וְהָא הָכָא דִּלְהַכְעִיס הוּא, וְקָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ מְשׁוּמָּד! הָתָם דְּאָמַר: אֶטְעוֹם טַעַם דְּאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree with regard to** this matter. **One said:** If one violated a prohibition **due to appetite** or convenience he is **an apostate,** while one who eats **to express insolence** is **a heretic. And one said:** One who violates a prohibition **to express insolence** is **also an apostate. Rather, who is a heretic?** It is **anyone who engages in idol worship.** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the first opinion from a *baraita*: If a person **ate one flea or one mosquito, this** person is **an apostate. But here,** isn't it a case **where it is** a violation performed **to express insolence,** as one has no desire to eat these insects, **and** yet the *tanna* **calls him an apostate?** The Gemara answers: The reference **there,** in that *baraita*, is to the case of one who **eats** the flea due to appetite, **as he says: I shall taste the flavor of prohibition.** He seeks to eat a food that he has never eaten before.

The fundamental conceptual distinction is between *mumar l'teiavon* — someone who violates because their appetite or convenience overrides their commitment, but who has not in principle rejected the law — and *mumar lehachis* — someone who violates on principle, out of spite or ideological rejection, even when there would be no practical benefit to doing so. The *mumar l'teiavon* still buys into the system at some level; the *mumar lehachis* has rejected it as a matter of stance. The question of whether both types equally lose *korban* eligibility, or whether *lehachis* represents a fundamentally different and more severe exclusion, is precisely what is at stake in the next dispute.

One practical dimension of the *mumar lehachis* / *mumar l'teiavon* distinction: when someone eats something that carries no obvious *yetzer hara* — something that offers no clear pleasure or benefit — the presumption is that the act must have been motivated by ideological rejection rather than appetite. Rashi applies this logic to *neveilah* understood as an animal that died of illness, which would be repugnant to eat; *shekatzim u'remasim* — insects and rodents — fit this description clearly. The case of *yein nesech* is harder, since non-Jewish wine might simply be cheaper and more available; Rashi suggests that wine literally poured as a libation to *avodah zarah* would be so intrinsically repugnant to an observant Jew that eating it must reflect principled rejection. The Rosh finds the entire categorization difficult and questions whether some of these items truly have no *yetzer hara*.

### D'oraita vs Rabbanan

The case of *kilayim* introduces a further dimension: the dispute between whether *mumar* status attaches only to one who violates a *de'oraita* prohibition, or whether it can also apply to violation of certain prominent *de'rabbanan* prohibitions. If the prohibition violated is only rabbinic — for instance, the rabbinic form of *kilayim* involving fabrics pressed together rather than woven — one opinion holds that this does not rise to the level of *mumar* status. The opposing view maintains that since the prohibition of *shatnez* is universally known and widely recognized, even repeated violation of its *de'rabbanan* form is sufficient to constitute *mumar* status.

### Min Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָמַר: לְתֵאָבוֹן – מְשׁוּמָּד, לְהַכְעִיס – מִין. וְחַד אָמַר: לְהַכְעִיס נָמֵי מְשׁוּמָּד, אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ מִין? כָּל הָעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree with regard to** this matter. **One said:** If one violated a prohibition **due to appetite** or convenience he is **an apostate,** while one who eats **to express insolence** is **a heretic. And one said:** One who violates a prohibition **to express insolence** is **also an apostate. Rather, who is a heretic?** It is **anyone who engages in idol worship.**

*Pligi bah Rav Acha u-Ravina*. One holds: *l'teiavon mumar* — someone who violates for the sake of appetite is a *mumar*, while someone who violates *lehachis*, out of principled rejection, goes beyond *mumar* into the category of *min* — a more fundamental exclusion from *klal Yisrael*. The other holds: *lehachis nami mumar* — even principled rejection places one only in the category of *mumar*, not *min*. On this view, *min* is reserved for one who has rejected the entire system: *kol ha'oved avodat kochavim* — one who worships idols, who has abandoned the foundations of Jewish belief entirely. Rejecting one or even many specific prohibitions, even from principle and out of spite, does not put a person outside *klal Yisrael* in the fundamental way that *avodah zarah* does.

### Min Criteria

The position that only *avodah zarah* constitutes *min* reflects a significant boundary: it is not enough to be a *mumar lehachis* when it comes to *chametz be-pesach* or *basar be-chalav* or any number of other laws. People have their particular areas of rejection, their particular failures of commitment or principle. None of that, on this view, removes someone from the body of *klal Yisrael* at the level that *min* implies. What is required for that fundamental exclusion is a rejection of the entire framework — expressed paradigmatically through the worship of other powers.

### Flea Case

*Meitivei*: a challenge is raised to the view that *lehachis* constitutes *min* status. A *baraisa* states that if someone eats a single flea or a single gnat, *harei zeh mumar* — this person is a *mumar*. Now, eating a single flea carries no apparent appetite motivation; it is inherently repugnant. The only plausible reading of such an act is that it was done *lehachis*, out of spite or principled rejection. And yet the *baraisa* calls this person a *mumar*, not a *min*. This appears to support the view that even *lehachis* is merely *mumar*, not *min*.

### One-Time Act

The *gemara* responds: *hatam de'amar eiteim tama de'issura* — there, the person was simply trying to taste something forbidden, to have the experience of it, a one-time act of curiosity or adventure. A single act of eating something disgusting does not necessarily demonstrate principled ideological rejection. It could even be categorized as *l'teiavon* in a broad sense — the desire for the experience or sensation, however strange that appetite might be. Only habitual repetition of such acts, when no appetite explanation is available, would indicate *lehachis*. Since the *baraisa*'s case is a single act, calling it *mumar* rather than *min* is consistent with the possibility that even *lehachis* patterns are *mumar*, without the single-act case definitively proving anything.

## Nasi & Mashiach

### Nasi Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזֶהוּ נָשִׂיא? זֶה מֶלֶךְ כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״נָשִׂיא״ – יָכוֹל נְשִׂיא שֵׁבֶט כְּנַחְשׁוֹן בֶּן עַמִּינָדָב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מִכׇּל מִצְוֹת ה׳ אֱלֹהָיו״, וּלְהַלָּן: הוּא אוֹמֵר ״לְמַעַן יִלְמַד לְיִרְאָה אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Who is** the ***nasi*? This is a king,** as it is stated: "When a *nasi* sins, and performs any one of all the mitzvot of the Lord his God that shall not be performed, unwittingly, and he is guilty" (Leviticus 4:22), referring to one who has only the Lord his God over him and no other authority. **The Sages taught: The verse states: "*nasi*."** One **might** have thought that the reference is to **the prince of a tribe, like Naḥshon, son of Amminadab.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And performed **any** one **of all the mitzvot of the Lord his God." Later,** in the passage with regard to the king, the verse **states: "That he may learn to fear the Lord his God"** (Deuteronomy 17:19).

A *baraisa* clarifies who qualifies as the *nasi* for the purposes of the *se'ir* offering. It is not the head of a tribe — not, for example, a *nassi shevet* in the mold of Nachshon ben Amminadav. The verse says *mikkol mitzvot Hashem Elokav*, and a parallel verse regarding the king states *lema'an yilmad leyirah et Hashem Elokav*: just as there the phrase implies that the only authority above the king is God Himself — that no human power stands above him — so too the *nasi* who brings the *se'ir* must be someone over whom no human authority stands. The *nasi* in this legal context means a king.

### Rabbi's Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי מֵרַבִּי חִיָּיא: כְּגוֹן אֲנִי, מַהוּ בְּשָׂעִיר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי צָרָתְךָ בְּבָבֶל. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמַלְכֵי בֵּית דָּוִד, אֵלּוּ מְבִיאִים לְעַצְמָם, וְאֵלּוּ מְבִיאִים לְעַצְמָם! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם לָא כַּיְיפִי אַהֲדָדֵי, הָכָא אֲנַן כַּיְיפִינַן לְהוּ לְדִידְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **raised a dilemma before Rabbi Ḥiyya:** In a case **where I** perform an unwitting transgression, **what is** the *halakha*: Would I be liable to atone **with a goat** as a sin-offering because I am the *Nasi*, or is my atonement with a ewe or a female goat, like a commoner, because I am not the king? Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: Your rival,** the Exilarch **in Babylonia,** is as great as you; therefore, you are not akin to a king. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **raised an objection to** Rabbi Ḥiyya from a *baraita*: If **kings of** the kingdom of **Israel and kings of the house of David** perform an unwitting transgression, **these bring** a sin-offering **for themselves** as kings, **and those bring** a sin-offering **for themselves** as kings. This indicates that even if a king has a counterpart who is as powerful as he is, he brings a male goat as his sin-offering. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **There,** the kings **were not subject to each other's authority. Here,** in Eretz Yisrael, **we are subject to their authority,** as the authority of the Exilarch is greater than the authority of the *Nasi*.

*Ba'i minei Rebbi me-Rebbi Chiya*: Rabbi posed a question to Rabbi Chiya — if there were a *Beis HaMikdash*, would someone in my position bring a *se'ir*? The question is not merely academic. Rabbi understood himself to be a descendant of the Davidic line, and the *nasi* in his time was understood to combine political and Torah authority. The question being probed is how much formal political power one must hold, or whether the ultimate position of authority — even if not carrying the title of king — would suffice. It is also worth noting that the question arises under Roman rule, which means the entire question of what constitutes sovereign political authority is already complicated.

### Bavel Competition

*Amar leh*: you have a *tzarah* — a competitor — in Bavel. The *Reish Galuta* of Bavel is the recognized political head of the Jews in *galut*, of the diaspora community outside *Eretz Yisrael*. Since there is another authority over the Jewish people alongside Rabbi's own, Rabbi is not the sole authority, and therefore does not meet the standard of *she'ein al gabav ella Hashem Elokav* — the one over whom no human power stands.

### Kings Separate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמַלְכֵי בֵּית דָּוִד, אֵלּוּ מְבִיאִים לְעַצְמָם, וְאֵלּוּ מְבִיאִים לְעַצְמָם!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **raised an objection to** Rabbi Ḥiyya from a *baraita*: If **kings of** the kingdom of **Israel and kings of the house of David** perform an unwitting transgression, **these bring** a sin-offering **for themselves** as kings, **and those bring** a sin-offering **for themselves** as kings. This indicates that even if a king has a counterpart who is as powerful as he is, he brings a male goat as his sin-offering.

An objection is raised: *malchei Yisrael u'malchei Beis David, eleh mevivim le'atzman ve'eleh mevivim le'atzman* — the kings of Israel and the kings of the House of David each brought a *se'ir* for themselves separately. This demonstrates that one need not be king of all the Jewish people to bring the *se'ir*; even a king ruling over only ten tribes, or only two, qualified. If so, the existence of another Jewish political authority should not disqualify Rabbi.

### Subject to Bavel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָא כַּיְיפִי אַהֲדָדֵי, הָכָא אֲנַן כַּיְיפִינַן לְהוּ לְדִידְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There,** the kings **were not subject to each other's authority. Here,** in Eretz Yisrael, **we are subject to their authority,** as the authority of the Exilarch is greater than the authority of the *Nasi*.

The *gemara* responds: *hatam lo chayfya hadadei* — there, the two kingdoms were not subject to each other; each had independent *malchuta*. *Hacha anan chayfinan le'hu le'didehu* — here, we are in some sense subject to the political authority of Bavel. This answer is admittedly difficult to understand historically; *Eretz Yisrael* in Rabbi's time was subject to Rome, not to Bavel, and it is not clear in what sense the Palestinian *nasi* was subordinate to the *Reish Galuta*. One might suggest that this formulation reflects the perspective of the Babylonian *Talmud*, which tends to view the Babylonian center as primary.

### Staff vs Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב סָפְרָא מַתְנֵי הָכִי. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי מֵרַבִּי חִיָּיא: כְּגוֹן אֲנִי מַהוּ בְּשָׂעִיר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם ״שֵׁבֶט״, הָכָא ״מְחוֹקֵק״, וְתַנְיָא: ״לֹא יָסוּר שֵׁבֶט מִיהוּדָה״ – זֶה רֹאשׁ גּוֹלָה שֶׁבְּבָבֶל, שֶׁרוֹדֶה אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמַקֵּל. ״וּמְחוֹקֵק מִבֵּין רַגְלָיו״ – אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי בָנָיו שֶׁל הִלֵּל שֶׁמְלַמְּדִים תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בָּרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Safra taught** the exchange in **this** manner: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **raised a dilemma before Rabbi Ḥiyya:** In a case **where I** perform an unwitting transgression, **what is** the *halakha*: Would I be liable to atone **with a male goat** as a sin-offering because I am the *Nasi*, or is my atonement with a ewe or a female goat, like a commoner, because I am not the king? Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: There,** the Exilarch has authority that is represented by **a scepter; here,** in Eretz Yisrael, we have lesser authority, which is represented by **a staff. And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"The scepter shall not depart from Judah"** (Genesis 49:10); **this** is a reference to **the Exilarch in Babylonia, who reigns over the Jewish people with a rod,** as he is authorized by the gentile monarchy to impose his will. **"Nor the ruler's staff from between his feet"** (Genesis 49:10); **these are the descendants of Hillel, who** serve in the role of the *Nasi* and **teach Torah to the Jewish people in public,** but who are not authorized by the government to impose their will.

Rav Safra offers an alternative formulation of the same answer that clarifies the distinction more precisely. *Hatam shevet hacha mechokek* — there, in Bavel, the *Reish Galuta* holds the *shevet*, the staff, the instrument of actual enforcement and physical power; there are accounts of the *Reish Galuta* dispatching officers and functionaries to compel compliance. Rabbi's authority in *Eretz Yisrael*, by contrast, is that of a *mechokek* — a lawgiver, a Torah authority — without comparable coercive force behind it.

### Roles Separated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: ״לֹא יָסוּר שֵׁבֶט מִיהוּדָה״ – זֶה רֹאשׁ גּוֹלָה שֶׁבְּבָבֶל, שֶׁרוֹדֶה אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמַקֵּל. ״וּמְחוֹקֵק מִבֵּין רַגְלָיו״ – אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי בָנָיו שֶׁל הִלֵּל שֶׁמְלַמְּדִים תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בָּרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"The scepter shall not depart from Judah"** (Genesis 49:10); **this** is a reference to **the Exilarch in Babylonia, who reigns over the Jewish people with a rod,** as he is authorized by the gentile monarchy to impose his will. **"Nor the ruler's staff from between his feet"** (Genesis 49:10); **these are the descendants of Hillel, who** serve in the role of the *Nasi* and **teach Torah to the Jewish people in public,** but who are not authorized by the government to impose their will.

*De'tanya*: the verse *lo yasur shevet mi'Yehudah* — the staff shall not depart from Yehuda — refers to the *Reish Galuta she'be-Bavel*, the one who rules Israel through the rod. *U'mechokek mibbein raglav* — and the lawgiver from between his feet — refers to the descendants of Hillel, *she'melammedim Torah le'Yisrael be'rabbim*, who teach Torah publicly. The two roles are thus formally separated: physical authority resides in Bavel with the *Reish Galuta*, while Torah authority resides with Rabbi and his descendants. This answer, taken seriously, implies something quite stark: Rabbi should understand himself primarily as a Torah authority rather than as a political sovereign. The first answer left open the possibility that Rabbi was a legitimate political authority who happened to be subject to a competing one; this answer reframes the entire picture and says the political and Torah roles have been divided, with the political one residing in Bavel.

### Inheritance Question

The question of what role *yerushah* — formal hereditary succession — plays in all of these determinations is noted but left for further discussion.

## Nasi & Mashiach (II)

### Mashiach Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵיזֶהוּ הַמָּשִׁיחַ? הַמָּשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה, לֹא הַמְרוּבֶּה בִּבְגָדִים. אֵין בֵּין כֹּהֵן הַמָּשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה לִמְרוּבֵּה בְּגָדִים, אֶלָּא פַּר הַבָּא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And who is the anointed** priest? It is the High Priest **who is anointed with the anointing oil, not the** High Priest consecrated by donning **multiple garments,** i.e., one who served after the anointing oil had been sequestered, toward the end of the First Temple period. **The difference between** a High **Priest anointed with the anointing oil and** one consecrated by donning **multiple garments** unique to the High Priest **is only** that the latter does not bring the **bull that comes for** the transgression of **any of the mitzvot.**

Having established who qualifies as *nasi*, the *gemara* turns to the parallel question: *ve'eizehu haMashiach* — who is the *Kohen Gadol* referred to as *Mashiach* for the purposes of his special *korban*? The answer is: *hamashuach beshemen hamishcha* — the one actually anointed with the oil of anointing — not a *merubeh begadim*, a *Kohen Gadol* who attained that status solely through the investiture of the special vestments in a time when the *shemen hamishcha* was no longer available.

### Anointed Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין בֵּין כֹּהֵן הַמָּשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה לִמְרוּבֵּה בְּגָדִים, אֶלָּא פַּר הַבָּא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The difference between** a High **Priest anointed with the anointing oil and** one consecrated by donning **multiple garments** unique to the High Priest **is only** that the latter does not bring the **bull that comes for** the transgression of **any of the mitzvot.**

A *Mishnah* in *Megilla* teaches: *ein bein Kohen haMashiach beshemen hamishcha lemerubeh begadim* — there is no difference between the *Kohen Gadol* anointed with the *shemen hamishcha* and the *Kohen Gadol* invested through the special vestments, with one exception.

### Par Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין בֵּין כֹּהֵן הַמָּשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה לִמְרוּבֵּה בְּגָדִים, אֶלָּא פַּר הַבָּא עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The single exception:** **the special *par*, the ox brought when the *Kohen Gadol* issues a mistaken ruling and acts upon it, is brought only by the one anointed with the *shemen hamishcha*, not by the *merubeh begadim* version.**

The single exception: *ela par haba al kol hamitzvot* — the special *par*, the ox brought when the *Kohen Gadol* issues a mistaken ruling and acts upon it, is brought only by the one anointed with the *shemen hamishcha*, not by the *merubeh begadim* version. This is the precise point established in our *Mishnah*: the *mashiach* brings the *par*; the *Kohen Gadol* who holds that position only through the vestments does not.

### Kohen Gadol Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה וָזֶה שָׁוִים בַּעֲבוֹדַת יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, וּמְצֻוִּוים עַל הַבְּתוּלָה, וַאֲסוּרִים עַל הָאַלְמָנָה, וְאֵינָם מִטַּמְּאִים בִּקְרוֹבֵיהֶם, וְלֹא פּוֹרְעִים, וְלֹא פּוֹרְמִים, וּמַחְזִירִין הָרוֹצֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Both **this** High Priest currently serving **and that** former High Priest **are equal with regard to** performing the rest of **the Yom Kippur service, and they are** both **commanded with regard to** marrying **a virgin** (see Leviticus 21:13), **and** it is **prohibited** for both **to** marry **a widow** (see Leviticus 21:14), **and they may not render themselves impure** with impurity imparted by a corpse even **in** the event that one of **their relatives** dies (see Leviticus 21:11), **and they may not grow** their hair **long and they may not rend** their garments as expressions of mourning (see Leviticus 21:10), **and** when they die **they restore the** unwitting **murderer** to his home from the city of refuge (see Numbers 35:25).

All other *halachot* of the *Kohen Gadol* apply equally to both: the obligation to marry a virgin, the prohibition against becoming *tamei* even for close relatives who die, and the prohibition against the mourning practices of letting one's hair grow wild or rending one's garments.

### Murderer Release

Similarly, both the serving *Kohen Gadol* and the *Kohen she'avar* — the former *Kohen Gadol* who stepped aside, typically for *Yom Kippur*, and whose predecessor has since resumed service — share all other aspects of the *Kohen Gadol*'s status. This includes the provision that a murderer exiled to an *ir miklat*, a city of refuge, may return when the *Kohen Gadol* dies. Rashi explains that even if one *Kohen Gadol* dies while another remains alive, the death of someone carrying *Kohen Gadol* status is sufficient to permit the murderer's return. Furthermore, the *Kohen she'avar* is still capable of performing other parts of the *avodah* on *Yom Kippur*, meaning that theoretically two individuals holding *Kohen Gadol* status could both serve on *Yom Kippur* — though only the currently serving one brings the *par* and the daily *mincha* of the tenth of an *eifa*.

## Anointing Oil

### Oil Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה שֶׁעָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה בַּמִּדְבָּר הָיוּ שׁוֹלְקִים בּוֹ אֶת הָעִיקָּרִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: וַהֲלֹא לָסוּךְ אֶת הָעִקָּרִים אֵינוֹ סוֹפֵק?! אֶלָּא: שׁוֹרִין אֶת הָעִקָּרִים בְּמַיִם, וּמֵצִיף עָלָיו שֶׁמֶן, וְקוֹלֵט אֶת הָרֵיחַ וְקוֹפְחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** To blend **the anointing oil that Moses prepared in the wilderness, they would boil in** the oil **the roots** of the spices in the quantities enumerated in the verse; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says: But isn't** that amount of oil **insufficient** even **to smear on the roots** of those spices, as the oil would be absorbed into the roots? How then could the roots be boiled in the oil? **Rather, they soak the roots in water.** Once the roots are waterlogged, they do not absorb the oil. The fragrance of the spices gradually rises **and they float oil on** the water **and** the oil **absorbs the fragrance. And** at that point, **one removed** the oil **[*vekippeḥo*]** from the water, and that was the anointing oil.

A *baraisa* discusses the preparation of the *shemen hamishcha* that Moshe made in the desert. The basic process involved combining the twelve *log* of oil with the roots of the specified fragrant plants. The dispute is between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi as to the method. *Hayu sholkim bo et ha'ikarim* — they would boil the roots in the oil: this is the position of Rabbi Yehuda.

### Boil vs Soak

Rabbi Yosi objects: how could you boil the roots in the oil? The quantity of oil was so small — twelve *log* — and the volume of plant material so large, that the roots alone would absorb virtually all of it. There would be nothing left. Rabbi Yosi therefore proposes an alternative method: *shorin et ha'ikarim bamayim* — first submerge the roots in water, so that they become fully saturated with liquid and can no longer absorb the oil. Then *umetzif aleihem shemen* — spread the oil over the water-saturated roots, where it floats on the surface rather than being absorbed; *vekolet et hareiach* — the oil absorbs the fragrance of the roots without being drawn into them — *vekapcho* — and then you collect the oil immediately.

### Miracles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וְכִי נֵס אֶחָד נַעֲשָׂה בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה? וַהֲלֹא תְּחִלָּתוֹ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר לוּגִּין, וּמִמֶּנּוּ הָיָה נִמְשָׁח מִשְׁכָּן וְכֵלָיו אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו כׇּל שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הַמִּלּוּאִים, וְכוּלּוֹ קַיָּים לֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שֶׁמֶן מִשְׁחַת קֹדֶשׁ יִהְיֶה זֶה לִי לְדוֹרוֹתֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said to him: And was** it merely **one miracle** that was **performed with regard to the anointing oil? But wasn't it initially** only **twelve *log*, and from it the Tabernacle, and its vessels, Aaron, and his sons** were **anointed** for the **entire seven days of inauguration, and all of it** remains **in existence for the future, as it is stated: "This shall be a sacred anointing oil unto Me throughout your generations"** (Exodus 30:31)?

Rabbi Yehuda responds by reframing the question entirely: *vechi nes echad na'asah beshemen hamishchah?* — do you think only one miracle was performed with the anointing oil? His point is that the practical objection, however well founded, is irrelevant, because the entire history of the *shemen hamishcha* is one continuous chain of miracles. What absorbed into the boiling process, what the roots drew in, what the fire consumed — none of that exhausted the oil, because the oil was miraculously preserved throughout.

### Kohen Gadol Anointing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בֶּן כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל טָעוּן מְשִׁיחָה, וְאֵין מוֹשְׁחִים מֶלֶךְ בֶּן מֶלֶךְ. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: מִפְּנֵי מָה מָשְׁחוּ אֶת שְׁלֹמֹה? מִפְּנֵי מַחְלוּקְתּוֹ שֶׁל אֲדוֹנִיָּה, וְאֶת יוֹאָשׁ מִפְּנֵי עֲתַלְיָה, וְאֶת יְהוֹאָחָז מִפְּנֵי יְהוֹיָקִים, שֶׁהָיָה גָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the anointing oil, the *baraita* continues: **And even a High Priest, son of a High Priest, requires anointing, but one does not anoint a king, son of a king. And if you say: For what** reason **did they anoint King Solomon** (see I Kings, chapter 1), who was the son of a king? It was **due to the challenge of Adonijah,** who sought to succeed their father David as king. **And** they anointed **Joash due to Athaliah** (see II Kings, chapter 11). **And** they anointed **Jehoahaz due to Jehoiakim, who was two years older than he** was (see II Kings 23:30). In all these cases, it was necessary to underscore that these men were crowned king.

A further *baraisa*: *ve'afilu kohen gadol ben kohen gadol ta'un meshichah* — even a *Kohen Gadol* who is the son of a serving *Kohen Gadol* must receive the anointing in order to attain *Kohen Gadol* status. The source is the verse *vehakohen hamashiach tachtav mibanav* — "and the priest anointed in his place, from among his sons." One might have thought the verse would simply say "the priest who serves from among his sons." The word *hamashiach* — the anointed one — teaches that even though it is the son who succeeds, he must receive the *meshichah*. If he is anointed, he is *Kohen Gadol*; if he is not, he does not attain the full status associated with that position. The *merubeh begadim* option — investing the *Kohen Gadol* status through the special vestments — remains available when the *shemen hamishcha* is not, but it confers a lesser form of the title, one that does not carry the obligation of the *par*.

### King Anointing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מוֹשְׁחִים מֶלֶךְ בֶּן מֶלֶךְ. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: מִפְּנֵי מָה מָשְׁחוּ אֶת שְׁלֹמֹה? מִפְּנֵי מַחְלוּקְתּוֹ שֶׁל אֲדוֹנִיָּה, וְאֶת יוֹאָשׁ מִפְּנֵי עֲתַלְיָה, וְאֶת יְהוֹאָחָז מִפְּנֵי יְהוֹיָקִים, שֶׁהָיָה גָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **one does not anoint the son of a king. Where succession is clear and uncontested, the anointing ritual is not performed. Exceptions occurred when the succession was disputed: Shlomo was anointed because Adoniyahu contested the throne; Yehoash was anointed because of Atalyah; Yehoachaz was anointed because Yehoyakim, who was two years his elder, might have had a competing claim.**

By contrast, *ve'ein mosh'chin melech ben melech* — one does not generally anoint the son of a king. Where succession is clear and uncontested, the anointing ritual is not performed. Exceptions occurred when the succession was disputed: Shlomo was anointed because Adoniyahu contested the throne; Yehoash was anointed because of Atalyah; Yehoachaz was anointed because Yehoyakim, who was two years his elder, might have had a competing claim.

### Inheritance Distinction

The underlying structural difference is this: the *Kohen Gadol* position is not treated as an automatic *yerushah*, a hereditary inheritance that passes automatically from father to son. There is a presumption in favor of the son, but there is also an expectation that the appointee be worthy of the position; it is not simply assumed. The *meshichah* is therefore necessary both to designate the new *Kohen Gadol* — making clear who has been chosen — and as an act of sanctification, a *cheftza* dimension of consecrating the *Kohen Gadol* to the *avodah*, parallel to the way the vessels of the *Mishkan* were consecrated. The kingship, by contrast, is understood as a *yerushah* that passes presumptively from father to son. When succession is clear, no external ritual act is needed to establish it. This is precisely why the *shemen hamishcha* is regularly used for *Kohanim Gedolim* but only occasionally for kings — the designation function of the anointing is built into the structure of the *Kehunah Gedolah* in a way that it is not for the monarchy.
