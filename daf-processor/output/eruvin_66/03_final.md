# Eruvin 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inn Scenario & Renting

### Landlord & Rental

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מֵרַב: יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי בַּחִיצוֹנָה וְיִשְׂרָאֵל בַּפְּנִימִית מַהוּ? הָתָם טַעְמָא מִשּׁוּם דִּשְׁכִיחַ דְּדָיַיר, דְּמִירְתַת גּוֹי, וְסָבַר: הַשְׁתָּה אָתֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָמַר לִי: יִשְׂרָאֵל דַּהֲוָה גַּבָּךְ הֵיכָא? אֲבָל הָכָא, אָמֵינָא לֵיהּ: נְפַק אֲזַל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer raised a dilemma before Rav:** If **a Jew and a gentile** live together **in the outer** courtyard, **and a Jew** lives alone **in the inner one, what is** the *halakha*? **There,** in the case where the Jew and the gentile share the inner courtyard, **the reason** the Sages prohibited carrying is **because it is common** for a Jew and a gentile **to live** together in such a fashion. Ordinarily a single Jew would not live together in the same courtyard as a gentile, for fear that the gentile might kill him. However, here, the Jew living in the inner courtyard believes **that** the **gentile would be afraid** to kill him, **as** the gentile **thinks** to himself: **Now,** were I to kill my neighbor, the **Jew** living in the outer courtyard **might come and say to me:** The **Jew who used to** live **by you, where is** he? The gentile would not be able to offer as an excuse that the Jew left, for the other Jew from the outer courtyard would know whether or not he passed through his courtyard. Therefore, since that living arrangement is common, the decree applies, and the gentile's residence in the courtyard renders it prohibited to carry there. **However, here,** where the gentile lives in the outer courtyard, he is not afraid of killing his Jewish neighbor, as he says to himself: If the other Jew comes to question me, **I will say to him: He went out** and **went** on **his** way; I do not know where he went. In this case, the gentile would not be concerned that the Jew from the inner courtyard might question his story. Since it is uncommon for a Jew and a gentile to live together in such a fashion, the Sages did not issue a decree that the gentile's residence renders the courtyard prohibited for carrying.

The present daf picks up at the bottom of 65b with the case of Rabbi Chanina bar Yosef. The discussion from the previous daf concerned a scenario where the landlord was present and the renter was absent. The principle established was that as long as the landlord retains the right to relocate the renter from one room to another, he maintains sufficient presence and control over the space, allowing the other residents to perform *sechirus* — a rental arrangement — from him. The relevant authorities made their practical decision without having confirmed the *halacha* in advance, and only afterwards received confirmation that they had acted correctly — a situation somewhat reminiscent of the episode of *tekias shofar* at Yavneh, where the precedent was set before the legal question was formally resolved.

These principles carry significant practical implications for the laws of *eruv*. They bear directly on how one rents from a city authority rather than from individual homeowners, and on questions such as whether an *eruv* is needed in an apartment building — given that a landlord may be unable to physically displace a tenant yet retains ownership of major appliances and shared spaces. Such questions of presence and control are at the heart of these laws and have ongoing relevance to contemporary *halacha*.

### Non-Jew Arrives Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר יוֹסֵף וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא וְרַבִּי אַסִּי אִיקְּלַעוּ לְהָהוּא פּוּנְדָּק דַּאֲתָא גּוֹי מָרֵי דְפוּנְדָּק בְּשַׁבְּתָא. אֲמַרוּ: מַהוּ לְמֵיגַר מִינֵּיהּ? שׂוֹכֵר כִּמְעָרֵב דָּמֵי — מָה מְעָרֵב מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, אַף שׂוֹכֵר מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם. אוֹ דִילְמָא שׂוֹכֵר כִּמְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת דָּמֵי, מָה מְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת, אַף שׂוֹכֵר וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Yosef and Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba and Rabbi Asi happened** to come **to a certain inn, and the gentile innkeeper,** who was absent when Shabbat began, **came on Shabbat. They said: What is** the *halakha* with regard **to renting from him** now? The Gemara explains the two sides of the question: **Is renting** from a gentile **like making an *eiruv*?** If so, **just as one who establishes an *eiruv*** may do so only **while it is still day, so** too, **one who rents** a gentile's property must do so **while it is still day. Or perhaps one who rents** from a gentile **is like one who renounces** rights to his **domain; just as one who renounces** rights to his **domain** may do so **even on Shabbat** itself, **so** too, **one who rents** a gentile's property may do so **even on Shabbat.**

The *gemara* now turns to a new episode. Rabbi Chanina bar Yosef, Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, and Rabbi Assi found themselves at a certain inn — *ikle'u l'hahu pundak* — together with various rented rooms. On *shabbos* itself, the non-Jewish owner of the inn arrived. The problem this created requires some background.

### Eruv Voided

The *gemara* had previously established, through a dispute among *tanna'im*, that if a non-Jew is absent from a shared space before *shabbos* begins, he does not create a *halachic* problem for those who share that space. It is only when the non-Jew is actually present before *shabbos* that his presence voids the *eruv*. Accordingly, the assumption here is that the guests at the inn had made an *eruv* before *shabbos* on the basis that the non-Jewish owner was not present — a perfectly sound arrangement. But when the owner arrived on *shabbos*, his presence retroactively rendered the *eruv* invalid. The assessment is made as if he had been present from before *shabbos*, and *sechirus* — renting the non-Jew's domain — would therefore have been required.

There is a lengthy *Tosafos* that labors over the question of precisely when a situation that was valid before *shabbos* began remains valid and when it is reversed by a problem that arises afterward. The *gemara* here operates on the assumption that it is reversed.

### Rent on Shabbat?

This raises an immediate practical question: *amru mahu l'meigar minei* — can we rent from the non-Jew on *shabbos* itself and thereby remedy the problem? The *gemara* frames the question as a comparison between two legal mechanisms: *socher k'me'arev dami* — is renting analogous to making an *eruv*? If so, just as an *eruv* must be established before *shabbos* begins, so too *sechirus* must be effected before *shabbos*. Or, *o dilma socher k'mevatel reshut dami* — perhaps renting is more akin to *bitul reshut*, the annulment of domain rights, which may be performed on *shabbos* itself when a problem arises.

*Sechirus* from a non-Jew and *bitul reshut* from a Jew are the two remedial strategies available when a standard *eruv* cannot function as intended. The *eruv* must be in place before *shabbos*; *bitul reshut* classically addresses situations discovered on *shabbos* itself. The question is therefore which of these two categories *sechirus* belongs to.

One might ask whether performing a rental transaction on *shabbos* itself is not inherently problematic as a prohibited commercial act. The relevant *Tosafos* on 66a addresses this directly, stating: *lo dami l'mikach u'mimkar de'asur b'Shabbos de'lo havei ela k'matanah b'alma she'ein asin ela l'hatir tiltul* — it is not comparable to a commercial transaction forbidden on *shabbos*, since this arrangement functions merely as a symbolic gift, performed solely to permit carrying. There is no genuine commercial exchange involved; indeed, it was already established earlier in the *masechta* that the *sechirus* may be performed for less than a *shaveh perutah*. The question, then, is not whether the act is permissible on *shabbos*, but whether it will be *halachically* effective.

## Sechirus Comparison

### Timing Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר: נִשְׂכּוֹר, וְרַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: לֹא נִשְׂכּוֹר, אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: נִסְמוֹךְ עַל דִּבְרֵי זָקֵן וְנִשְׂכּוֹר. אֲתוֹ שַׁיַּילוּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אָמַר לָהֶן: יָפֶה עֲשִׂיתֶם שֶׁשְּׂכַרְתֶּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Yosef said: Let us rent,** while **Rabbi Asi said: Let us not rent. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said to them: Let us rely** now **on the words of** the **Elder,** Rabbi Ḥanina bar Yosef, **and rent.** Later **they came and asked Rabbi Yoḥanan** about the matter, and **he said to them: You acted well when you rented.**

The *gemara* formulates the central question precisely: *socher k'me'arev dami* — does *sechirus* follow the same timing requirement as *eruv*, demanding that it be accomplished before *shabbos*? Or does it follow the rules of *bitul reshut*, which may be performed even on *shabbos* itself?

### Two Models

The narrative proceeds: Rabbi Chanina bar Yosef said *niscor* — let us rent. Rav Yosef, citing Rav Assi, said *lo niscor* — let us not rent. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba then resolved the impasse: *nismoch al divrei zaken v'niscor* — let us rely on the elder, Rabbi Chanina, and proceed to rent. This echoes the logic of that famous *mishna* in Rosh Hashanah, where Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai proceeded to sound the *shofar* at Yavneh on *shabbos* and only afterward sought formal confirmation.

After *shabbos*, they brought the question to Rabbi Yochanan. He replied: *yafeh asitem she-sechartem* — you did well to rent. As in the case from the previous daf, they either were genuinely confident in their judgment, or, since the matter is *derabanan* and the rule is *halacha k'divrei ha'meikel b'eruv*, they felt sufficiently grounded to rely on the lenient position.

### Lenient Comparisons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּהוּ בָּהּ נְהַרְדָּעֵי: וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שׂוֹכֵר כִּמְעָרֵב דָּמֵי. מַאי לָאו: מָה מְעָרֵב מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, אַף שׂוֹכֵר מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם! לָא, מָה מְעָרֵב וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אַף שׂוֹכֵר בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. וּמָה מְעָרֵב אֲפִילּוּ שְׂכִירוֹ וּלְקִיטוֹ, אַף שׂוֹכֵר אֲפִילּוּ שְׂכִירוֹ וּלְקִיטוֹ. וּמָה מְעָרֵב חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁשְּׁרוּיִם בְּחָצֵר אַחַת — אֶחָד מְעָרֵב עַל יְדֵי כּוּלָּן, שׂוֹכֵר נָמֵי, חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁשְּׁרוּיִם בְּחָצֵר אַחַת — אֶחָד שׂוֹכֵר עַל יְדֵי כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of Neharde'a wondered** at this teaching: **Did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say this? Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say** just the opposite: **Renting** from a gentile **is like establishing an *eiruv*? What, is** he **not** to be understood as imposing a stringency: **Just as one** who **establishes an *eiruv*** may do so only **while it is still day, so** too, **one** who **rents** a gentile's property must do so **while it is still day?** The Gemara rejects this argument: **No,** his statement was intended as a leniency: **Just as one who establishes an *eiruv*** may do so **even with less than the value of a *peruta*, so** too, **one who rents** a gentile's property may rent it for **less than the value of a *peruta*. And just as** the **one who establishes an *eiruv*** need not be the owner himself, but **even his hired laborer or harvester** may do so, **so** too, **one who rents** a gentile's property need not rent from the landlord himself, **but** may rent **even** from **his hired laborer or harvester** who are acting on his behalf. **And** similarly, **just as** with regard to **one who establishes an *eiruv*,** the *halakha* is that if **five** people **live in the same courtyard, one** of them may **establish an *eiruv*** with the residents of a different courtyard **on behalf of them all, so** too, with regard to **one who rents** a gentile's property; if **five** people **live in the same courtyard** together with a gentile, **one** of them may **rent** the gentile's property **on behalf of them all.**

The *gemara* then records that the scholars of Nehardea were astonished: *ve-chi amar Rabbi Yochanan hachi* — did Rabbi Yochanan truly say this? For it was known that Rabbi Yochanan had himself declared *socher k'me'arev dami* — that renting is like making an *eruv*! Does this not imply that *sechirus*, like an *eruv*, must be performed before *shabbos*?

The *gemara* rejects that reading: *lav ma me'arev afilu b'pachos mi'shaveh perutah af socher afilu b'pachos mi'shaveh perutah*. When Rabbi Yochanan compared *sechirus* to *eruv*, he was making a lenient comparison, not a strict one. The parallel is being drawn for the purpose of leniency, not stringency, and *Rashi* emphasizes this point: *d'kulah akshinu m'Rabbi Yochanan v'lo l'chumra d'kol b'eruv l'hakel* — all the comparisons being drawn from Rabbi Yochanan are toward leniency; everything in *eruvin* we interpret favorably.

The *gemara* then marshals three such lenient parallels. First: just as an *eruv* is valid even when the bread contributed is worth less than a *perutah*, so too *sechirus* may be performed for less than a *perutah*. Second: *u'ma me'arev afilu sochir v'lakito* — just as an *eruv* may be effected even by the hired laborer or gleaner of a non-Jew (a Jew employed on the property who may act in lieu of the owner), so too *sechirus* may be effected even through the non-Jew's hired hand rather than only from the owner himself. This extends the ease with which the non-Jew's domain can be brought within the *eruv* arrangement. Third: just as when five people share a courtyard, one person may deposit the *eruv* on behalf of all of them, so too one person may perform the rental from the non-Jew on behalf of all residents of the courtyard, since the *sechirus* is not directed at any individual member but at the collective. All three comparisons point in the same direction — toward making the *eruv* arrangement easier to accomplish, not harder.

### Sechirus Works Shabbat

The conclusion is that *sechirus* operates according to the rules of *bitul reshut*, not *eruv*. It may be performed on *shabbos* itself. Rabbi Yochanan's statement that *socher k'me'arev dami* was never intended as a timing restriction; it was a statement about leniency, that wherever *eruv* law is applied, we apply it generously. With this, the practical question raised by the scenario at the inn is resolved: the *sechirus* performed on *shabbos* was entirely valid.

## Shmuel's Principle

### Osrin & Mearvin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּהֵי בַּהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: מַאי תִּהְיָיא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: גַּבְרָא רַבָּה כְּרַבִּי זֵירָא לָא יָדַע מַאי תִּהְיָיא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר? קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל רַבֵּיהּ. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאוֹסְרִין וּמְעָרְבִין — מְבַטְּלִין. מְעָרְבִין וְאֵין אוֹסְרִין, אוֹסְרִין וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין — אֵין מְבַטְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar wondered** at Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling that the Sages had acted well when they rented the gentile's property on Shabbat and then they renounced their rights to that one, so that at least it would be permitted to use the courtyard. **Rabbi Zeira said: What** was the reason for **Rabbi Elazar's wonder? Rav Sheshet said:** Can it be that **such a great person as Rabbi Zeira did not know what** was the source **of Rabbi Elazar's wonder? He had difficulty with** a statement **of his teacher, Shmuel. As Shmuel said:** With regard to **any place where** the residents **render it prohibited for** each other to carry **but** where **they** may **establish a** joint ***eiruv*** if they so desire, in order to permit carrying, each may **renounce** his property rights for the other if they failed to establish an *eiruv* before Shabbat. However, in a place **where** the residents may **establish an *eiruv*** together **but they do not render it prohibited** for each other for carrying, or where **they render it prohibited for** each other for carrying **but they** may **not establish an *eiruv*** together, in such situations **they** may **not renounce** their property rights for each other.

It would seem that once the *sechirus* was performed, the problem should be fully resolved. However, *Rashi* and *Tosafos* both point out that renting from the non-Jew does not, by itself, restore the *eruv* that was made before *shabbos* — that *eruv* was already nullified by the non-Jew's arrival and cannot be retroactively revived. What the *sechirus* accomplishes is removing the non-Jew from the equation. But the courtyard is now in the position of a group that has no valid *eruv* in place. The remedy for that situation — a problem arising on *shabbos* itself when an *eruv* was not made or was nullified — is *bitul reshut*.

This implicit understanding is what animates the following discussion. The *gemara* now identifies what was troubling a certain Rabbi Eliezer about this case. The term used is *tohi bah* — he was probing it, trying to smell out what was going on. Rav Sheshet responds with some emphasis: could a great scholar like Rabbi Zeira not understand what was bothering Rabbi Eliezer? He was troubled by the statement of Shmuel, his teacher. For Shmuel stated: *kol makom she'osrin u'me'arvin m'vatlin* — in any place where the residents create a forbidden situation for one another and they are able to make an *eruv* together, they may also perform *bitul reshut*.

The logic behind this principle is as follows. An individual's presence in a shared space creates a problem for every other resident — this is the concept of *osrin*, the capacity to forbid. If they choose to work together, they can all unify the space through an *eruv* — this is *me'arvin*, the capacity to join. Where both these conditions exist, the residents are in a fundamental sense already one unit. It is because they are recognized as one unit that *chazal* permitted the fiction of *bitul reshut* — the waiving of domain rights on *shabbos* — as a remedy when the proper *eruv* arrangement failed. The flip side is equally clear: *me'arvin v'ein osrin, ein m'vatlin* — if they could make an *eruv* but their individual presence does not create a problem for one another, *bitul reshut* does not apply. And *osrin v'ein me'arvin, ein m'vatlin* — if their presence creates a problem but an *eruv* is not possible, *bitul reshut* likewise does not apply.

Both conditions must be present simultaneously.

### Nested Courtyards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאוֹסְרִין וּמְעָרְבִין — מְבַטְּלִין, כְּגוֹן שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת זוֹ לִפְנִים מִזּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **any place where** the residents render it **prohibited** for each other to carry **but** where **they** may **establish an *eiruv*, they may renounce** their rights for each other, **such as** in the case of **two courtyards, one within the other.** The residents of the two courtyards render each other prohibited to carry between the courtyards, but they may establish a joint *eiruv* in order to permit carrying. In such a case, the residents may renounce their property rights for each other if they failed to establish an *eiruv* before Shabbat.

The *gemara* illustrates the case where both conditions are met with the example of *shtei chatzeiros zo lifnim mi'zo* — two courtyards, one nested inside the other. In this configuration, which figured prominently in the previous daf's discussion, the residents of the inner courtyard (A) must pass through the outer courtyard (B) to access the street. This means that A's presence is inherently a concern for B — A is *oser* on B. At the same time, they are capable of unifying their space through a joint *eruv*. Therefore, if one person forgot to participate in the *eruv*, they satisfy both conditions and *bitul reshut* is available as a remedy.

This ruling is a *chiddush*, because one might have said that *bitul reshut* is only available within a single courtyard and cannot operate between two separate courtyards. The alternative opinion, to be discussed shortly, will indeed take that more restrictive view. Shmuel's logic here is that since the two courtyards are fundamentally one unit — they create problems for each other and could unify under one *eruv* — the fiction of *bitul reshut* can function across them.

### Separate Access

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעָרְבִין וְאֵין אוֹסְרִין — אֵין מְבַטְּלִין, כְּגוֹן שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת וּפֶתַח אֶחָד בֵּינֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** In a place **where** the residents may **establish an *eiruv*** together **but they do not render** each other **prohibited** to carry, **they** may **not renounce** their property rights for each other, in a case **where two courtyards** both opening to an alleyway **that have a single opening between them.** Even though the two courtyards may establish a joint *eiruv* and be considered a single courtyard, they do not render it prohibited for each other to carry if they did not do so, because neither needs to make use of the other. Consequently, there is no option of renouncing rights in favor of the other courtyard.

The contrasting case is *shtei chatzeiros u'petach echad beineihem* — two courtyards each with its own independent access to the public domain. In this configuration, neither courtyard imposes itself upon the other. Each has its own separate space. While they could, if they chose, make a joint *eruv*, they are under no obligation to do so, and the presence of residents in one courtyard does not create a problem for residents in the other. Here *osrin* does not apply, even though *me'arvin* does. Since they are not inherently bound together, they are not truly one unit, and the fiction of *bitul reshut* — which only functions when there is an underlying unity — cannot bridge the gap between them. If one courtyard failed to make an *eruv*, *bitul reshut* toward the other courtyard is not available. They would require an actual *eruv* to permit carrying between them.

### Non-Jew Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹסְרִין וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין — אֵין מְבַטְּלִין. לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי, לָאו לְאֵתוֹיֵי גּוֹי? וְאִי דַּאֲתָא מֵאֶתְמוֹל — לוֹגַר מֵאֶתְמוֹל! אֶלָּא לָאו, דַּאֲתָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא, וְקָתָנֵי: אוֹסְרִין וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין, אֵין מְבַטְּלִין. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a place **where they render** each other **prohibited** from carrying **but they** may **not establish an *eiruv*** together, **what** does this come **to include?** In reference to which case did Shmuel make this statement? **Wasn't it** meant to **include a gentile** who shares a courtyard with two Jews? The Jewish residents of the courtyard render each other prohibited from carrying in such a case, but they may not establish an *eiruv* due to the presence of the gentile. **Now, if** it is referring to a situation **where** the gentile **arrived on the previous day,** i.e., before Shabbat, **let him rent** the property from the gentile **on the previous day.** Before Shabbat, both options were available: They could have either established an *eiruv* or one Jew could have renounced his rights in favor of the other. Therefore, it would not have been considered a situation in which they render each other prohibited to carry but cannot establish an *eiruv*. **Rather, is it not** referring to a case where the gentile **arrived on Shabbat, and** Shmuel **is teaching:** In a place **where they render** each other **prohibited** from carrying **but they** may **not establish an *eiruv* together,** in such a situation **they** may **not renounce** their rights for each other. Therefore, you can **learn from this** that if the gentile arrived on Shabbat, they cannot rent his property and then renounce their rights to one of them. This explains Rabbi Elazar's surprise at Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling, as it appears to contradict this teaching of Shmuel, his first teacher.

The case of *osrin v'ein me'arvin* — where the presence of a party creates a forbidden situation but an *eruv* together with them is not possible — is identified as the scenario of the non-Jew. In the normal case, a non-Jew's presence is a problem because he cannot be incorporated into an *eruv* directly; *sechirus* is required. Yet if he was present before *shabbos*, the potential for *sechirus* existed — that potential is considered the functional equivalent of *me'arvin* — and therefore *bitul reshut* would remain available for the Jews who share the courtyard, should one of them fail to make an *eruv*.

The *gemara* clarifies: *d'ata me'etmol lo garmi me'etmol* — if the non-Jew came yesterday, we could have rented from him yesterday. That possibility classifies him in the *me'arvin* category, and consequently *bitul reshut* among the Jewish residents remains effective in principle. It is important to note the asymmetry here: the non-Jew does not perform *bitul reshut*, nor is *bitul reshut* directed at him. His presence creates a problem for the Jews, and the question is whether the Jewish residents may use *bitul reshut* amongst themselves. But if the possibility of *sechirus* existed, the answer is yes.

The scenario that does *not* allow *bitul reshut* is precisely the case at hand — *d'ata b'shabsa* — the non-Jew arrived on *shabbos* itself. He is *oser*, yes — his arrival on *shabbos* created the problem. But he is *ein me'arvin* — since he was not present before *shabbos*, the option of renting from him to bring him into the *eruv* framework never existed. Therefore, by Shmuel's principle, the case falls into the category of *osrin v'ein me'arvin* and *bitul reshut* cannot be used. A *baraisa* is cited confirming exactly this: *osrin v'ein me'arvin v'ein m'vatlin*.

The implication is striking. Even after the *sechirus* was performed on *shabbos* — which the *gemara* has already validated — the *bitul reshut* that would be needed to reconstitute carrying rights in the courtyard cannot be performed. Shmuel's principle stands in apparent tension with Rabbi Yochanan's approval of how the scholars at the inn acted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי הָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַתְּ אֲמַרְתְּ נִיהֲלַן, וְאַהָא אֲמַרְתְּ נִיהֲלַן, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר. וְאֵין בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת בְּחוּרְבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: I have not heard this *halakha*** of Shmuel's with regard to two courtyards situated one within the other, that the residents of the inner courtyard may renounce their rights to the outer courtyard in favor of the residents of that courtyard. **Abaye said to him: You** yourself **told** it **to us.** Rav Yosef forgot his studies due to illness, so his student Abaye would remind him of his own teachings. Abaye continued: **And it was with regard to this** that **you told** it **to us. As Shmuel said: There is no renunciation of rights from one courtyard to another.** In other words, while one may renounce his rights to his own courtyard for the other residents of that courtyard, he may not renounce his rights to another courtyard for the residents of that courtyard. **Likewise, there is no renunciation of** property **rights in a ruin.** If a ruin was shared by two houses, neither can renounce its rights to the ruin in favor of the other. The Sages instituted renunciation of rights only with regard to a courtyard, as that is the typical case.

The *gemara* then records that Rav Yosef said: *lo shemi'a li ha shema'ta* — I never heard this teaching of Shmuel about the two criteria. Abaye immediately corrected him: *at amrat neilan* — you yourself taught us this position of Shmuel. Abaye reminds him that Rav Yosef had cited it in connection with a further statement of Shmuel: *ein bitul reshut mi'chatzer l'chatzer v'ein bitul reshut b'churba* — there is no *bitul reshut* from one courtyard to another, and there is no *bitul reshut* in a *churba*, a back alley.

The prohibition on *bitul reshut mi'chatzer l'chatzer* directly corresponds to the case of two separate courtyards discussed above. The prohibition on *bitul reshut b'churba* rests on a distinct consideration: *bitul reshut* is a fiction that *chazal* permitted only for a *chatzer*, where the need for carrying is greatest and most practically significant. They did not extend this fiction to a back alley, even though houses may open into it. These are distinct restrictions, though both reflect the same underlying principle that *bitul reshut* has defined limits and cannot be applied universally wherever it would be convenient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַרְתְּ לַן עֲלַהּ: כִּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל אֵין בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר, לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת וּפֶתַח אֶחָד בֵּינֵיהֶן. אֲבָל זוֹ לִפְנִים מִזּוֹ, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאוֹסְרִין זֶה עַל זֶה — מְבַטְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And you said to us with regard to** this matter: **When Shmuel said that there is no renouncing of rights from one courtyard to another, we said** this **only** with regard to a case of **two courtyards,** one alongside the other and each opening into an alleyway, **that have a single opening between them. However,** if the two courtyards were situated **one within the other, since** the residents of the courtyards **render each other prohibited** from carrying, **they may** also **renounce** their rights in favor of each other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא אָמֵינָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין לָנוּ בְּעֵירוּבִין אֶלָּא כִּלְשׁוֹן מִשְׁנָתֵנוּ — ״אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר״, וְלֹא אַנְשֵׁי חֲצֵירוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye in surprise: **I said that in the name of Shmuel? Didn't Shmuel say: We may** be lenient with regard to the laws of ***eiruvin* only in accordance with the wording of the mishna,** which states that the **residents of a courtyard,** in the singular, may renounce their rights, **but not the residents of courtyards** in the plural. Therefore, the option of renouncing rights does not apply to two courtyards.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי אֲמַרְתְּ לַן אֵין לָנוּ בְּעֵירוּבִין אֶלָּא כִּלְשׁוֹן מִשְׁנָתֵנוּ — אַהָא אֲמַרְתְּ לַן: שֶׁהַמָּבוֹי לַחֲצֵירוֹת כֶּחָצֵר לַבָּתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him: When you told us** this ruling of Shmuel's that **we may** be lenient with regard to the laws of ***eiruvin* only in accordance with** the wording of **the mishna, you said it to us with regard to the following** mishna, which states: **That an alleyway in relation to** its **courtyards is like a courtyard in relation to** its **houses.** Shmuel inferred from this that there must be at least two courtyards with two houses each that open into an alleyway in order to permit carrying there by means of a side post or a cross beam.

Abaye then corrects another of Rav Yosef's recollections. When Rav Yosef had cited Shmuel's statement that *ein lanu b'eruvin ela l'shon mishnatenu* — in matters of *eruvin* we follow the precise language of our *Mishna* — this was not a universal interpretive principle. Shmuel was applying it specifically to one *Mishna* that reads *ha'mavo'in l'chatzerot k'chatzer l'battim*, drawing an analogy between the relationship of houses to courtyards and courtyards to an alley (*mavoi*). From the plural *chatzerot*, Shmuel derived that a valid arrangement for *shituf mavo'os* requires at least two courtyards each containing at least two houses. That *diyuk* was local to that *Mishna*, not a general rule foreclosing *bitul reshut* across courtyards whenever the *Mishna* uses the singular *chatzer*. When it comes to *bitul reshut* in the case of nested courtyards where *osrin* applies, Shmuel himself would permit it under the right conditions.

With this, Shmuel's various statements are rendered consistent with one another, and Rav Yosef is set back on track.

## Shmuel vs. Yochanan

### Shmuel Restricts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר, וְאֵין בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת בְּחוּרְבָּה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: יֵשׁ בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר, וְיֵשׁ בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת בְּחוּרְבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines the ruling of Shmuel that was cited in the previous discussion. Returning to **the matter itself, Shmuel said: There is no renunciation of rights from one courtyard to another, and there is no renunciation of rights in a ruin. But Rabbi Yoḥanan** disagreed and **said: There is renunciation of rights from one courtyard to another, and there is renunciation of rights in a ruin.**

The *gemara* returns to examine Shmuel's double ruling directly: *amar Shmuel ein bitul reshut mi'chatzer l'chatzer v'ein bitul reshut b'churba* — Shmuel holds that *bitul reshut* does not function from one courtyard to another, and does not function in a back alley.

### Yochanan Permits

Rabbi Yochanan takes the opposite position on both counts: *yesh bitul reshut mi'chatzer l'chatzer v'yesh bitul reshut b'churba* — he permits *bitul reshut* both between courtyards and in a back alley. The notational marking on both of Rabbi Yochanan's rulings signals that this is the operative *halacha*, consistent with the principle of *halacha k'divrei ha'meikel b'eruv*.

### Usage vs. Space

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר, בְּהָא קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתָּא לְחוּד וְהָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתָּא לְחוּד. אֲבָל חוּרְבָּה, דְּתַשְׁמִישְׁתָּא חֲדָא לְתַרְוַויְיהוּ. אֵימָא: מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. וְכִי אִתְּמַר בְּהָא, בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲבָל בְּהָךְ מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It is **necessary** to explain that Shmuel and Rabbi Yoḥanan disagreed with regard to both cases, as neither case could have been learned from the other. **As, if it had taught** only that there is no renunciation of rights **from one courtyard to another,** one could have said that it is only **with regard to this** case that **Shmuel said** that there is no renunciation of rights, **because the use** of the one courtyard stands **alone and the use** of the other courtyard stands **alone.** Each courtyard is not used by the residents of the other courtyard, and therefore there is no renunciation of rights from one courtyard to the other. **However,** with regard to **a ruin, where there is one** common **use for both** neighbors, as the residents of both houses use it, **I** would **say** that he **concedes to Rabbi Yoḥanan.** **And** conversely, **if it was stated** only **with regard to** the case of a ruin, one could have said that it is only **with regard to this** case that **Rabbi Yoḥanan stated** his position, **but with regard to the other** case, renouncing rights from one courtyard to another, perhaps **he concedes to Shmuel.** Therefore, it is **necessary** to teach both cases.

The *gemara* explains why both cases must be stated independently rather than deriving one from the other. One might have thought that Shmuel's restrictiveness is particularly intuitive in the case of two courtyards with separate access to the street, where *vadah tashmishta l'chud v'ha tashmishta l'chud* — each one has its own distinct and separate space for use. Where each courtyard functions entirely independently, there is no compelling need for *bitul reshut* and no meaningful shared framework to which the fiction can attach. But perhaps in the *churba*, a back alley that both sets of residents use jointly — *tashmishta chada l'tarvaihu* — where it is more like a shared courtyard, Shmuel might have conceded to Rabbi Yochanan. Conversely, one might have said Rabbi Yochanan's leniency is most intuitive in the *churba*, where the shared usage makes it function practically like one space, but that he might have deferred to Shmuel regarding separate courtyards. Since neither direction of inference is certain, the *gemara* establishes that both authorities maintain their respective positions across both cases, making it necessary to state each ruling explicitly.

The underlying conceptual tension between the two positions reflects two different understandings of when *bitul reshut* can function as a meaningful legal fiction. For Shmuel, the critical element is genuine unity — *bitul reshut* works only where the parties are already one unit in the relevant sense, which requires both *osrin* and *me'arvin*. For Rabbi Yochanan, *bitul reshut* is a broader remedy and need not be so tightly constrained.

## Abaye & Rava

### Abaye's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָא דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל אֵין בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר, לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בִּשְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת וּפֶתַח אֶחָד בֵּינֵיהֶן, אֲבָל שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת זוֹ לִפְנִים מִזּוֹ, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאוֹסְרִין — מְבַטְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** With regard to **that which Shmuel said,** that **there is no renunciation of rights from one courtyard to another, we said** this **only** with regard **to two courtyards,** one alongside the other and each opening into an alleyway, **that have a single opening between them. However,** if there were **two courtyards, one within the other, since** the residents **render** each other **prohibited** to carry, **they may** also **renounce** their rights in favor of each other.

The *gemara* now brings Abaye's formulation of Shmuel's position: when Shmuel said *ein bitul reshut mi'chatzer l'chatzer*, this applies only to *shtei chatzeiros she'petach echad beineihen* — the case where the courtyards have separate access and are not in a relationship of mutual dependence. But *aval zo lifnim mi'zo* — where one courtyard is nested inside the other and A is *oser* on B, since in that configuration one person's presence creates problems for others and they can make a joint *eruv*, the conditions of *osrin u'me'arvin* are met and *bitul reshut* functions. Abaye is here restating what had been attributed to Shmuel earlier in the discussion as the basis for reconciling Shmuel's various rulings.

### Rava Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת זוֹ לִפְנִים מִזּוֹ, פְּעָמִים מְבַטְּלִין וּפְעָמִים אֵין מְבַטְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Even** in the case of **two courtyards, one within the other, sometimes** the residents may **renounce** their rights in favor of each other, **and sometimes they** may **not renounce** them.

Rava sharply qualifies this conclusion: *afilu shtei chatzeiros zo lifnim mi'zo, pa'amim mevatlin u'pa'amim ein mevatlin* — even in the case of nested courtyards, there are times when *bitul reshut* is effective and times when it is not. The mere fact that they are in a nested configuration and create problems for each other is not sufficient, in Rava's view, to make *bitul reshut* automatically available across the two courtyards. The details of how the *eruv* was arranged and which person failed to participate determine whether the remedy is available at all.

## Shared Eruv Scenarios

### Eruv in Outer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? נָתְנוּ עֵירוּבָן בַּחִיצוֹנָה וְשָׁכַח אֶחָד, בֵּין מִן הַפְּנִימִית וּבֵין מִן הַחִיצוֹנָה, וְלֹא עֵירַב — שְׁתֵּיהֶן אֲסוּרוֹת. הַאי בַּר פְּנִימִית, לְמַאן נִיבַטֵּיל? לִיבַטֵּיל לִבְנֵי פְנִימִית — לֵיתָא לְעֵרוּבַיְיהוּ גַּבַּיְיהוּ. לִיבַטֵּיל לִבְנֵי חִיצוֹנָה — אֵין בִּטּוּל רְשׁוּת מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר!
>
> **Translation:** **How** so? If the residents of the two courtyards **placed their *eiruv* in the outer** courtyard, **and one** person **forgot** to do so, **whether** he was a resident **of the inner** courtyard **or of the outer** courtyard, **and** he therefore **did not establish an *eiruv*** with the others, then it is **prohibited** to carry in **both** courtyards. The person who neglected to establish an *eiruv* renders it prohibited for the residents of both courtyards to carry, because the *eiruv* for both courtyards is located in the outer one, and it is prohibited to carry there without an *eiruv* due to the right of passage of the residents of the inner courtyard through the outer courtyard. Therefore, there is no effective *eiruv* at all, not even for the residents of the inner courtyard. The Gemara explains why the residents of these courtyards cannot avail themselves of the option of renunciation: **That resident of the inner** courtyard who forgot to place his *eiruv*, **in favor of whom can he renounce** his rights? **Let him renounce** them **in favor of the residents of the inner** courtyard, yet that is ineffective, as **their *eiruv* is not with them** but in the outer courtyard. Consequently, they would remain without an *eiruv*, which means they would render it prohibited to carry in the outer courtyard. **Let him renounce** them **in favor of the residents of the outer** courtyard, but that too is ineffective, as Shmuel ruled that **there is no renunciation of rights from one courtyard to another.**

Rava proceeds to spell out the scenarios in full. The case under discussion involves two courtyards — inner (A) and outer (B) — that have made a joint *eruv*, depositing it in one or the other courtyard. The key variables are: where the *eruv* was placed, and who forgot to participate.

When the joint *eruv* was placed in the outer courtyard (B), and any member of either courtyard — whether inner or outer — forgot to join the *eruv*: *shteihen asuros* — both courtyards are forbidden, with no available remedy.

The *gemara* works through why this must be so. If one of the inner residents (A) forgot, to whom can he perform *bitul reshut*? If he says *levatel livnei pnimis* — I will do *bitul* to my fellow inner residents — that does not help, because *leis lei eruva gabaihu* — the *eruv* is not located with them. The inner courtyard cannot be considered functionally independent from the outer one, since the outer courtyard holds the *eruv* and the inner residents must go through the outer to benefit from it. Any meaningful *bitul* would have to encompass the outer courtyard as well. But *ein bitul reshut mi'chatzer l'chatzer* — *bitul* from one courtyard to the other is precisely what Rava (following Shmuel's restrictive reading) disallows, even in nested courtyards.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי בַּר חִיצוֹנָה לְמַאן נְבַטֵּיל, לִיבַטֵּיל לִבְנֵי חִיצוֹנָה — אִיכָּא פְּנִימִית דְּאָסְרָה עֲלַיְיהוּ. לִיבַטֵּיל לִבְנֵי פְּנִימִית — אֵין בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **that resident of the outer** courtyard who forgot to place his *eiruv*, **in favor of whom can he renounce** his rights? **Let him renounce** them **in favor of the residents of the outer** courtyard, but **there is** still **the inner** courtyard **that renders them prohibited** from carrying. **Let him renounce** them **in favor of the residents of the inner** courtyard, but **there is no renunciation of rights from one courtyard to another.** Therefore, the mechanism of permitting carrying by means of renunciation cannot be applied in these cases.

If one of the outer residents (B) forgot, the analysis is symmetrical but no more hopeful. If he says *levatel livnei chitzona* — I will do *bitul* to my fellow outer residents — *ika pnimis d'asru aleih* — the inner courtyard is also creating a problem for the outer one. The inner residents go through the outer courtyard and are inherently part of its domain; one cannot simply ignore them. To make the *bitul* effective, the outer resident would need to also do *bitul* to the inner courtyard, which again runs into *ein bitul reshut mi'chatzer l'chatzer*. There is no path forward in either scenario, and both courtyards remain forbidden.

### Inner Eruv, Inner Forgot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתְנוּ עֵירוּבָן בַּפְּנִימִית, וְשָׁכַח אֶחָד מִן הַפְּנִימִית וְלֹא עֵירַב — שְׁתֵּיהֶן אֲסוּרוֹת. הַאי בַּר פְּנִימִית לְמַאן נְבַטֵּיל? לִיבַטֵּיל לִבְנֵי הַפְּנִימִית — אִיכָּא חִיצוֹנָה דְּאָסְרָה עֲלַיְיהוּ. לִיבַטֵּיל לִבְנֵי חִיצוֹנָה — אֵין בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת מֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, if the residents of the two courtyards **placed their *eiruv* in the inner** courtyard, the following distinction applies: **If** a resident **of the inner** courtyard **forgot** to do so **and did not establish an *eiruv*,** it is **prohibited** to carry in **both** courtyards. The reason is as follows: **That resident of the inner** courtyard who forgot to place his *eiruv*, **in favor of whom can he renounce** his rights? **Let him renounce** them **in favor of the residents of the inner** courtyard, yet **there is** still **the outer** courtyard **that renders them prohibited** from carrying, as the *eiruv* shared by the courtyards is in essence a valid *eiruv*, which gives the residents of the outer courtyard the right to enter the inner one. **Let him renounce** them **in favor of the residents of the outer** courtyard, but that is ineffective, as Shmuel maintains that **there is no renunciation of rights from one courtyard to another.** In that case, since the inner courtyard is prohibited, it renders it prohibited to carry in the outer one as well.

When the joint *eruv* was placed in the inner courtyard (A), and a member of the inner courtyard forgot: *shteihen asuros* — both are again forbidden.

The analysis follows the same logic. The forgetting inner resident might try *bitul* to his fellow inner residents. But *ika chitzona d'asra aleih* — the outer courtyard is part of this arrangement, since the inner courtyard's *eruv* was made jointly with them. Since B is incorporated into A's *eruv*, one cannot treat them as irrelevant. To make the *bitul* work, it would have to extend to B, which is *bitul mi'chatzer l'chatzer* — and that is unavailable.

### Inner Eruv, Outer Forgot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁכַח אֶחָד מִן הַחִיצוֹנָה וְלֹא עֵירַב — פְּנִימִית מוּתֶּרֶת וְחִיצוֹנָה אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** On the other hand, **if a** resident **of the outer** courtyard **forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*,** it is **permitted** to carry in **the inner** courtyard **and** it is **prohibited** to carry in **the outer** courtyard. The residents of the inner courtyard have an *eiruv*, as they established an *eiruv* together, and therefore they may carry in their courtyard. The residents of the outer courtyard do not render it prohibited for them to carry, as they do not have the right to pass through the inner courtyard, and the inhabitants of the latter could bar their entrance to the inner courtyard by locking their doors.

The one case where a remedy is available: the *eruv* was placed in the inner courtyard (A), and a member of the outer courtyard (B) forgot. In this case, *pnimis muteres v'chitzona asura* — the inner courtyard is permitted and the outer is forbidden.

The *gemara* states the reason succinctly: *de'achad dasha u'mishtamsha* — A closes its door and uses its own space. The inner courtyard, holding the *eruv*, can simply seal itself off from the outer courtyard and proceed as though it were independent.

### Why It Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתְנוּ עֵירוּבָן בַּפְּנִימִית, וְשָׁכַח אֶחָד מִן הַחִיצוֹנָה וְלֹא עֵירַב — וַדַּאי פְּנִימִית מוּתֶּרֶת, דְּאָחֲדָא דַּשָּׁא, וּמִשְׁתַּמְּשָׁא, וְחִיצוֹנָה אֲסוּרָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרָבָא: וְכִי שָׁכַח אֶחָד מִן הַפְּנִימִית וְלֹא עֵירַב אַמַּאי שְׁתֵּיהֶן אֲסוּרוֹת, לְבַטֵּיל בַּר פְּנִימִית לִבְנֵי פְּנִימִית, וְתֵיתֵי חִיצוֹנָה וְתִשְׁתְּרֵי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, if the residents of the two courtyards **placed their *eiruv* in the inner** courtyard, the following distinction applies: if a resident **of the outer** courtyard **forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*,** it is **certainly permitted** to carry in the **inner** courtyard, **as** its residents **can close the door** between the two courtyards, thereby preventing the residents of the outer courtyard from entering, **and** they can then **use** their courtyard on their own. **However,** it is still **prohibited** to carry in **the outer** courtyard. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rava: And if a** resident **of the inner** courtyard **forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*, why** is it **prohibited** to carry in **both** courtyards? **Let the resident of the inner** courtyard who forgot to establish an *eiruv* **renounce** his rights **in favor of the** other **residents of the inner courtyard, and** then **let** the residents of **the outer** courtyard, who had established an *eiruv* with the inner one, **come and be permitted** to carry together **with them.**

The question is pointed: in this scenario, it appears that the inner courtyard is simply closing its door and benefiting from its own *eruv* without anyone performing *bitul reshut* at all. Where is the *bitul* in this case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד? כִּי קָאָמֵינָא לְרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: צָרִיךְ לְבַטֵּל לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** Rava replied: **In accordance with whose** opinion do you make this suggestion? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who said: It is not necessary to renounce** one's **rights in favor of each and every** resident. Rather, it is enough for a person to renounce his rights in favor of a single person, as once he no longer has any rights in the courtyard, he can no longer render it prohibited to carry there. According to this approach, a resident of the inner courtyard may indeed renounce his rights in favor of the other residents of his courtyard. The outer courtyard would then be rendered permitted together with the inner courtyard. However, **when I spoke,** it was **in accordance with the** opinion of **the Rabbis, who say: It is necessary to renounce** one's rights **in favor of each and every** resident. Therefore, in order to render the outer courtyard permitted, it would be necessary for the person who forgot to establish the *eiruv* to renounce his rights in favor of the residents of the outer courtyard as well. However, he may not do so, as one may not renounce rights from one courtyard to another. Therefore, the outer courtyard may not be rendered permitted in this manner.

*Rashi* at the top of 67a addresses this precisely. He explains that what the outer residents (B) do is *bitul* of a very specific and limited kind: *de'ha modeh Shmuel de'mevatlin benei chitzona livnei pnimis osah reshut she'ba'ah lahen l'tochan al yedei hergel eruvan* — Shmuel concedes that the outer courtyard performs *bitul* to the inner courtyard of that particular *reshus* that had come to them through the circuit of the joint *eruv*. When B joined A's *eruv*, they acquired a stake in A's *eruv* arrangement. When B withdraws — is *mochel* on — that stake, they remove themselves from the *eruv* and allow A to use it for itself alone.

This is a narrow and specific form of *bitul*: not a transfer of domain rights from one *chatzer* to another, which would be *bitul mi'chatzer l'chatzer* and therefore prohibited even by Shmuel's restrictive view. Rather, it is the outer courtyard relinquishing the claim it had acquired to A's *eruv*. Since B can be sealed off, and since B is merely withdrawing a right it had previously taken on rather than conferring rights in its own domain upon A, the arrangement is acceptable.

In every other scenario — where either an inner resident forgot or where the *eruv* was placed in the outer courtyard — the *bitul* that would be required involves genuine cross-courtyard transfer of domain rights, and that Rava, following the restrictive understanding of Shmuel, will not permit. The architecture of the *eruv* and the identity of the party that failed to participate thus determine, with considerable precision, whether any remedy is available at all.
