# Pesachim 113 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## R. Yishmael's Commands

### Mum B'Atzmecha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים צִוָּה רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אֶת רַבִּי: אַל תַּעַשׂ מוּם בְּעַצְמְךָ. מַאי הִיא? לָא תֶּיהְוֵי לָךְ דִּינָא בַּהֲדֵי תְּלָתָא, דְּחַד הָוֵי בַּעַל דִּינְךָ וּתְרֵי סָהֲדֵי. וְאַל תַּעֲמוֹד עַל הַמִּקָּח בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, commanded Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi with regard to **three matters.** The first matter is: **Do not inflict a blemish upon yourself.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of that statement? The Gemara explains: **Do not have a court case against three** people, **as one will be your opponent and two** will act as **witnesses** and testify against you whatever they wish. **And do not stand over** and display interest in **a purchase when you do not have** enough **money** even for the price you are offering, as this constitutes fraud.

The larger thematic context of this daf flows from the Mishnah's ruling that even a pauper who subsists from the communal *tamchuy* must have *arba kosot* at the Seder. The Gemara traced this back to Rabbi Akiva's maxim that one should make one's *Shabbat* like a weekday rather than become dependent on others — a teaching he transmitted to his son as part of an ethical will. That, in turn, opened a sustained series of such charges: ethical instructions that sages transmitted to their students and children. One tradition records that Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, when confined together in prison, would discuss precisely these kinds of practical and ethical teachings rather than formal *halakha*.

Continuing in that vein, the Gemara cites three things Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yosi commanded Rabbi. The first is *al ta'as mum b'atzmecha* — do not make a wound in yourself. The Gemara immediately asks *mai hi*, what does this mean in practice? The answer: *lo tehavei lach dina bahadi tlata* — do not enter into litigation or business disputes with three people simultaneously. The concern is structural: one of the three becomes your opponent in court and the other two serve as his witnesses. You are three against one, and that is asking for trouble.

### Onaat Devarim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשְׁתְּךָ טָבְלָה — אַל תִּזָּקֵק לָהּ לַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. אָמַר רַב: וּבְנִדָּה דְאוֹרָיְיתָא, הוֹאִיל וְהוּחְזַק מַעְיָן פָּתוּחַ, דִּילְמָא מָשְׁכָה זִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the third instruction that **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, commanded Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. After **your wife has immersed, do not engage** in intimacy **with her on the first night.** Rather, wait an additional night. **Rav said: And** this is referring **to a menstruating woman** whose status applies **by Torah** law. According to Torah law, even if a woman experiences a continuous emission of blood for seven days, if the flow stops on the seventh day, she may immerse that night and engage in relations with her husband without delay. However, Rav maintains that one must act stringently in this case. His reasoning is: **Since** there is **a presumption** that **the flow** of blood **is open, perhaps** her ***zava*** blood **will continue** afterward, i.e., she will see more blood after engaging in intimacy.

The second command is *v'al ta'amod al hamekach b'sha'at she'ein lecha damim* — do not examine merchandise when you have no money to purchase it. This is more than a counsel of self-interest; it touches on the biblical prohibition of *onaat devarim*, causing anguish through words or actions. The Gemara in Bava Metzia treats this at length. Here, the concern is raising the seller's expectations that you are a genuine buyer when you have no intention or means of purchasing. The Rashbam explains it as *mafsido* — causing a loss to the seller — because once you are seen handling an item and then setting it down, another potential buyer assumes something is wrong with it and moves on. Or, the seller loses time and other customers while attending to you. Either way, your presence and conduct generate a real harm to the owner, even without any explicit deceptive statement.

The third command — that if one's wife has gone to the *mikvah*, one should not be with her on the very first night when the immersion followed the biblical *niddah* count — generated some surprise. After all, the general understanding, reflected in the *poskim*, is that the first night after *mikvah* is considered a *mitzvah*. Rav explains the concern: *u'veniddah d'Oraita* — this refers to biblical *niddah* law, where a woman immerses seven days after the onset of bleeding without additional clean days. Since she may have been bleeding through those seven days, *hoil v'huchzak ma'ayan patuach* — her flow has been established as open — and there is concern that *dilma mashcha ziva*, perhaps some flow continues even after immersion, making relations potentially prohibited. The advice is therefore to wait a short additional period and verify that things have fully concluded. As the Gemara makes clear, this does not imply that women necessarily bleed for the entire seven days — most stop before then — it is simply a prudential buffer.

### R. Yossi's Commands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים צִוָּה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֶת רַבִּי: אַל תֵּצֵא יְחִידִי בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאַל תַּעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי הַנֵּר עָרוֹם, וְאַל תִּכָּנֵס לְמֶרְחָץ חָדָשׁ, שֶׁמָּא תִּפָּחֵת. עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: עַד שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. וְאַל תַּעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי הַנֵּר עָרוֹם, דְּתַנְיָא: הָעוֹמֵד בִּפְנֵי הַנֵּר עָרוֹם — הָוֵי נִכְפֶּה. וְהַמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ לְאוֹר הַנֵּר — הָוַיִין לוֹ בָּנִים נִכְפִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, commanded Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi with regard to **three matters: Do not go out alone at night; do not stand naked before a candle; and do not enter a new bathhouse, lest it collapse** when they light the fire beneath it. The Gemara asks: **Until when** is a bathhouse considered new? **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: Until twelve months** have passed. With regard to the statement: **And do not stand naked before a candle,** the Gemara comments that this is **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **One who stands naked before a candle will become epileptic, and one who engages in intimacy by candlelight will have epileptic children.**

The Gemara then records three things that Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda commanded Rabbi. The first is *al tetzei yechidi balayla* — do not go out alone at night, a ruling we have already encountered in the Mishnah in connection with *shedim*. The second is *v'al ta'amod bifnei haner arom* — do not stand naked before a candle. The third is *v'al tikanes lemerchatz chadash* — do not enter a brand new bathhouse, on account of the fear that the floor might collapse. The practical concern is very concrete: in those bathhouses, bathers stood on a wooden floor directly above hot coals and steam. If the structure had not yet been tested, the floor might give way. How long must one wait before entering? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says twelve months.

### Candlelight & Epilepsy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ עַל מִטָּה שֶׁתִּינוֹק יָשֵׁן עָלֶיהָ — אוֹתוֹ תִּינוֹק נִכְפֶּה. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא הָוֵי בַּר שַׁתָּא, אֲבָל הָוֵי בַּר שַׁתָּא — לֵית לַן בַּהּ. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּגָנֵי לַהֲדֵי כַּרְעֵיהּ, אֲבָל גָּנֵי לַהֲדֵי רֵישֵׁיהּ — לֵית לַן בַּהּ. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא מַנַּח יְדֵיהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ, אֲבָל מַנַּח יְדֵיהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ — לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **the Sages taught: One who engages in intimacy in a bed upon which a baby is sleeping, that child becomes epileptic. And we said** that this will occur **only if** the child **is not** yet **one year old; however,** if he **is one year old we have no** problem **with it,** as he is old enough not to be affected. **And** furthermore, **we said this only** concerning a baby **that is sleeping near** the father's **feet; but** if the baby **is sleeping near his head** he is sufficiently far away so that **we have no** problem **with it. And we said this only if he does not place his hand on** the baby at the time, **but** if **he places his hands on** the baby to serve as a barrier between them, **we have no** problem **with it.**

The Gemara now circles back to explain the prohibition of standing naked before candlelight. A *braita* teaches: *ha'omed bifnei haner arom havei nichpeh* — one who stands naked before candlelight will develop epilepsy (*nichpeh*). Presumably, this is connected to some form of demonic influence, though the Gemara does not spell it out. The *braita* continues: *hamishamshe mitato le'or haner havayan lo banim nichpin* — one who has marital relations by candlelight will have children who are epileptic. The Gemara appears to understand this in terms of the mixing of categories: marital relations are presumed to belong to the dark, and epilepsy involves falling into unconsciousness when one should be awake — a confusion of the awake and asleep, the light and the dark. There is also the consistent Talmudic concern that the manner and circumstances of marital relations affect the character and wellbeing of the children conceived.

### Tinok Asleep

A further *braita* teaches: one who has marital relations on the same bed upon which an infant is sleeping — *oto tinok nichpeh* — that infant will become epileptic. Notably, the Gemara qualifies this in three ways, each narrowing the scope of the danger. First, *delo havei bar shata* — this applies only if the infant is less than a year old; once a year old, there is no concern. This seems counterintuitive from a moral standpoint — surely an older child's presence would be more problematic — but the Gemara's framework appears to be one of vulnerability: the younger the infant, the more susceptible to whatever spiritual forces are at play. Second, *deganei lahadei krayeh* — this applies only if the infant is sleeping at the foot of the bed; if the infant is sleeping at the head of the bed, there is no concern. Third, *dela manach yadeih alaveih* — this applies only when one does not place one's hand upon the child; if one places a hand on the child in a protective gesture while the child sleeps, there is no concern. The Gemara's logic seems to be that nakedness and the act of relations attract forces that are particularly dangerous to an infant's vulnerability, and that proximity, position, and physical protection all mediate that danger.

## Demons & Spirits

### Agrath Bath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵל תֵּצֵא יְחִידִי בַּלַּיְלָה, דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא יֵצֵא יְחִידִי בַּלַּיְלָה, לֹא בְּלֵילֵי רְבִיעִיּוֹת וְלֹא בְּלֵילֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָגְרַת בַּת מָחֲלַת, הִיא וּשְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה רִבּוֹא שֶׁל מַלְאֲכֵי חַבָּלָה יוֹצְאִין, וְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד יֵשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְחַבֵּל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the instruction: **Do not go out alone at night,** the Gemara states that this is **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: One should **not go out alone at night, neither on Tuesday nights nor on Shabbat nights,** i.e., Friday nights, **because** the demon **Agrat, daughter of Maḥalat, she and 180,000 angels of destruction go out** at these times. **And** as **each and every one** of them **has permission to destroy by itself,** they are all the more dangerous when they go forth together.

The Gemara returns to explain the prohibition of going out alone at night. A *braita* teaches: *lo yetzei yechidi balayla* — do not go out alone at night — specifically not on Tuesday nights or Friday nights. The reason: *she'Igeret bat Machlat* — because Agrath bath Machlath, who is the queen of the *shedim*, *hi ushmoneh esreh ribo shel malachei chabalah yotzim* — she and 180,000 angels of destruction go out on these nights. Moreover, *kol echad ve'echad yesh lo reshut lechabel bifnei atzmo* — each of those 180,000 has individual permission to wreak havoc on its own.

### Chanina Encounter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיקָּרָא הֲווֹ שְׁכִיחִי כּוּלֵּי יוֹמָא. זִמְנָא חֲדָא פְּגַעָה בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אִי לָאו דְּמַכְרְזִן עֲלָךְ בְּרָקִיעַ: ״הִזָּהֲרוּ בַּחֲנִינָא וּבְתוֹרָתוֹ״ — סַכַּנְתָּיךְ. אֲמַר לַהּ: אִי חֲשִׁיבְנָא בְּרָקִיעַ, גּוֹזֵר אֲנִי עָלַיִךְ שֶׁלֹּא תַּעֲבוֹרִי בַּיִּישּׁוּב לְעוֹלָם. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: בְּמָטוּתָא מִינָּךְ, שְׁבֹק לִי רַוְוחָא פּוּרְתָּא. שְׁבַק לַהּ לֵילֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת וְלֵילֵי רְבִיעִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **Initially,** these demons **were present every day. Once** Agrat, daughter of Maḥalat, **met Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa** and **said to him: Had they not announced about you in the Heavens: Be careful of Ḥanina and his Torah, I would have placed you in danger. He said to her: If I am** considered **important in Heaven, I decree upon you that you** should **never travel through inhabited places. She said to him: I beg you, leave me a little space. He left for her Shabbat nights and Tuesday nights.**

Yet this does not match our experience of Tuesday and Friday nights. The Gemara explains that *me'ikara havu shechichi kulei yoma* — originally, these demonic forces were present every single day of the week. What changed? *Zimna chada paga beRabbi Chanina ben Dosa* — on one occasion, Agrath bath Machlath encountered Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa. She told him that were it not for the heavenly proclamation protecting him — *hizaheru beChanina u've'torato*, be cautious around Chanina and his Torah — she would have harmed him. Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa responded: if I am so significant in heaven, then I issue a decree — *she-lo ta'avri be-yishuv le-olam* — that you shall never be permitted to pass through inhabited areas. She pleaded with him, *bi-metuta minach*, please, *shevok li ravcha purta*, give me a little room. Whether she needed this space in order to have an opportunity to harm people or simply as living space is not entirely clear from the Gemara. What is notable is the phrase from earlier — that each of the 180,000 has *reshut lechabel*, permission to destroy — which suggests they are driven toward harm. In any case, Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa relented and permitted her the Tuesday nights and Friday nights, withdrawing his decree for those two nights only.

### Abaye & Agrath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ: חֲדָא זִמְנָא פְּגַעָה בֵּיהּ בְּאַבָּיֵי, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אִי לָאו דְּמַכְרְזִי עֲלָךְ בְּרָקִיעַ: ״הִזָּהֲרוּ בְּנַחְמָנִי וּבְתוֹרָתוֹ״ — הֲוָה סַכַּנְתָּיךְ. אֲמַר לַהּ: אִי חֲשִׁיבְנָא בְּרָקִיעַ, גּוֹזְרַנִי עֲלַיְיכִי שֶׁלֹּא תַּעֲבוּרִי בַּיִּישּׁוּב לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, once** Agrat, daughter of Maḥalat **met Abaye** and **said to him: Had they not announced about you in the Heavens: Be careful of Naḥmani,** Abaye, **and his Torah, I would have placed you in danger. He said to her: If I am** considered **important in Heaven, I decree upon you that you** should **never pass through inhabited places.**

*Ve-tu* — and furthermore — one might ask why Friday nights and Tuesday nights specifically became the designated danger nights. One suggestion raised is that every other night is either before or after a market day, so people are moving through the streets; Friday night, however, is when people are home, and perhaps that created a natural opening. In any event, some time later — *zimna achrita paga beih be-Abaye* — Agrath encountered Abaye. She said to him the same thing she had once said to Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa: were it not for the heavenly proclamation protecting *Nachmani* — Abaye's other name, which he bore because he was raised in the household of Rabbah bar Nachmani — and his Torah, she would have harmed him. Abaye, in response, issued the same decree she had once obtained a concession from: that she should never pass through inhabited areas. This time, it appears, he offered her no concession for Tuesday or Friday nights. This narrows the scope still further, and explains why the danger is not as prevalent as one might have expected from the initial framing.

### Narrow Alleys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא חָזֵינַן דְּעָבְרָה?! אָמְרִי: הָנֵי גָּזְיָיתָא נִינְהוּ, דְּשָׁמְטִי סוּסַיָּא וְאָתוּ דָּבְרִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But we see that,** notwithstanding these anecdotes, demons do **pass through** inhabited areas. The Sages **say** in explanation: **These** demons **are** found **on the paths [*gazyata*]** near the city, **as horses** belonging to the demons **flee** along those paths, **and** the demons **come to lead them** away. Generally, however, demons do not enter inhabited places.

The Gemara presses the point: *hacha chazinan de-avri* — but we do observe that *shedim* still seem to pass through inhabited places. The response: *hani gazyata ninhu* — these are instances of the narrow alleyways. The *shedim* travel on horses through the side alleys, and when those horses encounter people or other horses, *de-shamtei susaihu* — the horses bolt in fright, dragging the demons along with them into the more populated areas. They are not willfully entering *yishuv*; they are pulled in by their startled animals, and then must make their way back out. So the decree holds, but these edge-cases account for the occasional encounters that still occur.

## Urban Precautions

### Horses & Dogs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ) רַב לְרַב אַסִּי: לָא תְּדוּר בְּמָתָא דְּלָא צָנֵיף בַּהּ סוּסְיָא וְלָא נָבַח בַּהּ כַּלְבָּא. וְאַל תָּדוּר בְּעִיר דְּרֵישׁ מָתָא אָסְיָא, וְלָא תִּנְסֵיב תַּרְתֵּי. אִי נָסְבַתְּ תַּרְתֵּי — נְסֵיב תְּלָת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rav **said to Rav Asi: Do not live in a city where horses do not neigh and where dogs do not bark,** as these animals provide security and protection. **And do not live in a city where the mayor is a doctor,** as he will be too busy working to govern properly. **And do not marry two** women, as they will likely join forces against you. And **if you** do **marry two, marry a third** as well. If two of your wives plot against you, the third will inform you of their plans.

Rav gave practical urban advice to Rav Assi: *lo tadur be-mata delo tzanifei susya* — do not live in a city where horses do not neigh. The Rashbam explains that without horses, a city is vulnerable to enemies; horses provide the transportation and military capacity essential for both pursuit and escape. Similarly, *velo navach bah kalba* — do not live in a city where dogs do not bark, since dogs serve as a warning system against approaching danger. And do not live in a city *de-reish mata asya* — where the head of the city is a doctor. The reason given is that a doctor who leads the city will be too consumed by his medical practice to govern effectively. One variant of the text reads *de-reish mata Assi* — a subtle wordplay on Rav Assi's own name — implying: and I would not want you, Rav Assi, to be in charge of the city when you should be spending your time on Torah rather than politics.

### Multiple Wives

The data here does confirm something that is remarked at the end of the passage: that with multiple wives, they will not form a united coalition against the husband. But then comes the surprising follow-up: *ve-i nasbat tarti nasiv telas* — if you have already taken two, take a third. The logic is that with three wives, any two who might conspire against the husband will have a third who can serve as an independent voice and protect him. One might wonder whether this simply escalates the problem indefinitely — four wives requiring five, and so on — but the apparent assumption is that it is more likely that coalitions fracture among three than they solidify, compared to the neat balance of two.

### Rav Kahana Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: הֲפוֹךְ בִּנְבֵילְתָּא וְלָא תֵּיפוֹךְ בְּמִילֵּי. פְּשׁוֹט נְבֵילְתָּא בְּשׁוּקָא וּשְׁקֵיל אַגְרָא, וְלָא תֵּימָא ״כָּהֲנָא אֲנָא, וְגַבְרָא רַבָּא אֲנָא, וְסַנְיָא בִּי מִלְּתָא״. סָלְקַתְּ לְאִיגָּרָא — שֵׁירוּתָךְ בַּהֲדָךְ. מְאָה קָרֵי בְּמָתָא בְּזוּזָא — תּוּתֵי כַּנְפָיךְ נִיהְווֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to Rav Kahana:** It is better for one **to turn over a carcass than to turn over his word,** i.e., to break his promise. Rav further said: **Skin a carcass in the market and take payment, but do not say: I am a priest, or: I am a great man, and this matter disgusts me.** It is preferable for one to work, even in menial labor, than to be dependent on others. Rav also advised Rav Kahana: If **you ascend to the roof,** carry **your food with you.** One should always carry his sustenance with him, even if he goes only on a short trip. If **one hundred pumpkins in the city** cost **a *zuz*, place** them carefully under **the corners** of your clothes. Treat food respectfully even if it is inexpensive.

Rav said to Rav Kahana: *hafoch bi-nevielta* — it is better to involve yourself in trading animal carcasses as a livelihood *velo teifoch be-mili* — than to occupy yourself with words and idle talk. The point is that honest labor, even of an undignified kind, is preferable to living off speech or spending one's time in gossip and chatter. The practical instruction follows: *pshot nevielta be-shuka ve-shakil agra* — strip the carcass in the marketplace and accept your payment for it. And do not say, *velo teima Kahana ana ve-gavra raba ana ve-sanya bi milta* — do not say, "I am Kahana, I am a great man, such work is beneath my dignity." There is a genuine question whether Rav Kahana was actually a *Kohen*, or whether his name merely signaled a lineage associated with *kehuna*. Either way, Rav's message is clear: dignity of labor is not diminished by the nature of the work. The concern is entirely about the attitude one brings to earning a living.

The practical advice continues: *salkas le-igra sherusach be-hadach* — if you are climbing to a roof, take your provisions with you. Do not go on even a short excursion without food. And even if *me'ah karei be-mata be-zuza* — a hundred gourds are available in the market for a single *zuz*, *tuti knafecha lehavu* — make sure you have some of your own under your cloak. Even when food appears abundant and cheap, the prudent person maintains personal provisions.

Rav said to Chiyya his son: *lo tishti sama* — do not drink potions or medicinal preparations. The Rashbam's comment on this is remarkable: he says *al tisht samim* — do not take drugs — *mipnei shenikva lahem veset* — because your body will develop a pattern and dependence upon them; *v'libcha shocha* — your heart will always be craving them; *v'tachle maot* — and ultimately they will consume all your money. He adds explicitly that even for medical purposes one should avoid them if there is another option, because people become hooked even when they begin taking such substances for legitimate pain relief. The Rashbam's understanding maps with striking precision onto what we today recognize as addiction to narcotic pain medication.

Rav further told his son: do not take wide steps or jump over canals of water — which some understand as a warning against *pesi'ah gasah* — and *velo ta'aker kakka* — do not pull out teeth except as a last resort. Then: *velo tiknei b'chivya* — do not provoke a snake, and *velo tiknei b'arma'ah* — do not provoke an Aramean. The Gemara elaborates with a *braita*: there are three you do not provoke — *nochri katan*, a young non-Jew; *nachash katan*, a small snake; and *talmid katan*, a young student. Why? *D'malchuteihu achorei udneihu yativ* — their kingdom sits behind their ears, meaning their power is latent but coming. Do not be deceived by their present smallness into thinking you can harm them without consequence; they will grow into a position to retaliate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְחִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ: לָא תִּשְׁתֵּי סַמָּא, וְלָא תְּשַׁוַּור נִיגְרָא, וְלָא תִּעְקַר כַּכָּא, וְלָא תְּקַנֵּא בְּחִיוְיָא, וְלָא תְּקַנֵּא בְּאַרְמָאָה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה אֵין מִתְקַנְּאִין בָּהֶן, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: גּוֹי קָטָן, וְנָחָשׁ קָטָן, וְתַלְמִיד קָטָן. מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּמַלְכוּתַיְיהוּ אֲחוֹרֵי אוּדְנַיְיהוּ קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to Ḥiyya, his son: Do not** get into the habit of **drinking medications,** lest you develop an addiction. **And do not leap over a ditch,** as you might hurt yourself in the process. **And do not pull out a tooth,** but try to heal it if possible. **And do not provoke a snake** in your house to try to kill it or chase it away. **And do not provoke a gentile,** as this too is dangerous. Similarly, **the Sages taught:** There are **three** beings **one** should **not provoke: A small gentile, and a small snake, and a small** Torah **scholar. What is the reason? Because their authority stands behind their ears.** They will eventually grow up, assume power, each in his own way, and avenge those who have harassed them.

## Business Advice

### Aivo Trading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְאַיְבוּ בְּרֵיהּ: טְרַחִי בָּךְ בִּשְׁמַעְתָּא וְלָא מִסְתַּיַּיע מִילְּתָא. תָּא אַגְמְרָךְ מִילֵּי דְעָלְמָא. אַדְּחָלָא אַכַּרְעָיךָ — זְבִינָךְ זַבֵּין. כֹּל מִילֵּי זַבֵּין וְתִחָרַט — בַּר מֵחַמְרָא, דְּזַבֵּין וְלָא תִּחָרַט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to Ayvu, his son: I struggled** to teach **you *halakha* but** my efforts **did not succeed,** as you did not become a great scholar. **Come** and **I will teach you** about **mundane matters: Sell your merchandise while the dust** from the road is still **on your feet.** As soon you return from your travels, sell your wares, lest the prices fall in the meantime. Furthermore, it is possible that **anything you sell** might later cause you to **regret** the sale, **except for wine, which you** can **sell without regret.** Since wine might go bad and be entirely lost, its sale is always advisable.

Rav said to Aivo his son: *tarchi bach b'shma'ata tuva v'lo mistayei* — I have invested great effort in your learning and it is not proving successful. Come, *ta agmarcha milei d'alma* — let me teach you the ways of the world, some practical business wisdom.

The first piece of advice: *ad chala achraeicha zvincha zavin* — while the sand is still on your legs, sell your wares. Meaning: as soon as you return from a trading journey, sell immediately. Do not wait for the absolute best price; move your merchandise and repeat the cycle. *Kol milei zavin v'techaret* — you can sell almost anything and justifiably regret having gotten a lesser price than you might have. But *bar mechmra d'zvin v'lo techaret* — except for wine: sell wine as soon as possible and never regret it, because by the time you find a better buyer the wine may well have turned to vinegar.

### Beer Business

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁרֵי כִּיסָיךְ, פְּתַח שַׂקָּיךָ. קַבָּא מֵאַרְעָא, וְלָא כּוֹרָא מֵאִיגָּרָא. תַּמְרֵי בַּחֲלוּזָךְ — לְבֵית סוּדָנָא רְהוֹט. וְעַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רָבָא: עַד תְּלָת סְאָה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי לָא דִּרְמַאי שִׁכְרָא לָא אִיעַתַּרִי. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אִי לָא דִּרְמַאי שִׁכְרָא לָא אִיעַתַּרִי. מַאי ״סוּדָנָא״? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: סוֹד נָאֶה, וּגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav further advised his son: **Open your purse** to accept payment, and only then **open your sack** to deliver the goods, to ensure you will receive payment for your merchandise. It is better to earn **a *kav* from the ground than a *kor* from the roof.** A *kor* is one hundred and eighty times larger than a *kav*. This proverb means that it is preferable to earn a small amount from a local, safe transaction than to attempt to earn more through a distant, risky venture. Rav continued: If there are **dates in your storeroom, run to the brewery** to sell them. If you wait, there is a good chance the dates will go bad. The Gemara asks: **And how many** dates should one keep for himself? **Rava said: Up to three *se'a*. Rav Pappa said: If I were not a beer manufacturer I would not have become wealthy. Some say** that it was **Rav Ḥisda who said: If I were not a beer manufacturer I would not have become wealthy.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the word ***sudana*,** the Aramaic term for a brewer? **Rav Ḥisda said: A pleasant secret [*sod na'e*] and acts of loving kindness,** as brewing is a good way to make money and also enables one to perform good deeds.

Next: *sharei kisecha ptach, ptach sakeikha* — first open your money pouch, then open your sack of goods. Collect payment before delivering the merchandise; always position yourself holding the money rather than the goods when there is any ambiguity in the terms of a transaction.

Then: *kava me'ara velo kura me'igra* — a small measure from the ground is better than a large measure from the roof. The Rashbam explains this as preferring nearby trade with a smaller margin over distant trade with a potentially larger margin. The effort, risk, and uncertainty of distant commerce often outweigh the apparent advantage of a higher price.

*Tamrei bechalozach levei sudna rahit* — if you have dates in your basket, run to the brewers. Do not let the dates sit to be eaten or to spoil; instead, convert them into beer immediately and make a substantial profit. The Gemara asks: *v'ad kama* — up to how large a quantity does this apply? Rava says: *ad tlata sa* — up to three *sa'im*. Beyond that quantity, presumably there is less danger of the dates being eaten up before you can brew with them, so the urgency is somewhat reduced.

The profitability of the beer business is attested directly: *amar Rav Papa, ei lav deramina shikra lo i'atarei* — Rav Papa said, had I not gone into the beer business I would not have become wealthy. A parallel tradition attributes the same statement to Rav Chisda.

The Gemara asks: *mai sudna?* What does this Aramaic word for the brewers actually mean? Rav Chisda explains it as *sod na'eh* — a good secret. Perhaps the brewing process itself was a trade secret; or perhaps the real secret is that people do not appreciate how profitable it is to convert dates into beer rather than simply eating them. There is a further reading: *sod na'eh u'gemilut chasadim* — a good secret and acts of kindness — which appears to be a play on Rav Chisda's own name, *Chisda* evoking *chasadim*, suggesting that the profits of the beer trade enable one to engage in philanthropy.

### Credit Dangers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כׇּל אַגַּב — גְּבִיָּא בָּעֵי. כֹּל אַשְׁרַאי — סָפֵק אָתֵי סָפֵק לָא אָתֵי. וּדְאָתֵי — מָעוֹת רָעוֹת נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to offer advice about mundane matters. **Rav Pappa said: Anything** you acquire with a document **by means** of which ownership is transferred, i.e., a bill of acquisition or obligation, **requires collection,** despite the fact that you are the legal owner. **Any sale on credit is uncertain whether or not** it **will come** to fruition. **And** even **if it** does **come** to fruition, **the money is bad.** These funds are difficult to collect, and they are generally not paid on time.

Rav Papa offered a pair of warnings about the dangers of credit and liens. *Kol agav gavi ba'ei* — anything involving a lien or a written obligation still has to be collected; do not treat it as money in hand. He may be referring specifically to the mechanism of *metaltalei agav mekarkei* — attaching a lien on movable goods through a lien on land — where the chattel is particularly difficult to trace and recover. More broadly: *kol ashrai safek ati safek lo ati* — anything extended on credit may or may not ever be paid; *ve-d'ati mus ravnei mineih* — and even if it comes, it may arrive in degraded form, paid out in small installments rather than all at once. The practical counsel is clear: avoid having extensive notes and outstanding loans; they are risky investments that rarely perform as expected.

## Jerusalem Wisdom

### War & Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם אַנְשֵׁי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם: כְּשֶׁאַתָּה יוֹצֵא לַמִּלְחָמָה — אַל תֵּצֵא בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, אֶלָּא תֵּצֵא בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה, כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּכָּנֵס בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה. וַעֲשֵׂה שַׁבַּתְּךָ חוֹל, וְאַל תִּצְטָרֵךְ לַבְּרִיּוֹת. וֶהֱוֵי מִשְׁתַּדֵּל עִם מִי שֶׁהַשָּׁעָה מְשַׂחֶקֶת לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said three matters, citing the people of Jerusalem: When you go to war do not go out first, but go out last.** The reason is **so that** if your side is defeated and you need to flee for your life, **you will enter** the refuge of the city **first. And** it is better to **make your Shabbat** like an ordinary **weekday and do not be beholden to** other **beings. And exert yourself** to join together **with one upon whom the hour smiles.**

Rabbi Yochanan transmitted three teachings in the name of the people of Jerusalem. First: *keshe'ata yotze lemilchama al tetze berishona ela tetze be'achrona* — when going out to war, do not go in the front lines but in the rear guard — *kedei shetikanes berishona* — so that you can be the first to return safely. The Maharsha notes that it might have been simpler to say that those in the front are killed first, but the Gemara instead phrases it positively.

Second — and strikingly, this appears here in the name of the people of Jerusalem rather than in the name of Rabbi Akiva alone — *va'aseh Shabat-cha Chol ve'al titztarech labriyot*: make your Shabbat like a weekday rather than becoming dependent on others. This may reflect that both Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yochanan drew on a common Jerusalem tradition, or that the maxim circulated among different transmitters independently.

Third: *vehevay mishtadel im mi shehasha'ah mesacheket lo* — seek out and associate with those who are experiencing good fortune. This also appeared earlier in the context of Rabbi Akiva's teachings, suggesting again a shared or overlapping tradition.

### Rooftop & Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי מִשּׁוּם אַנְשֵׁי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם: אַל תַּרְבֶּה בִּגְנוּת, מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה. בִּתְּךָ בָּגְרָה — שַׁחְרֵר עַבְדְּךָ וְתֵן לָהּ. וֶהֱוֵי זָהִיר בְּאִשְׁתְּךָ מֵחֲתָנָהּ הָרִאשׁוֹן. מַאי טַעְמָא? רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם עֶרְוָה, רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם מָמוֹן. הָא וְהָא אִיתַנְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said three matters, citing the people of Jerusalem: Do not indulge in a shameful act** in public, **because of the incident that occurred** involving David and Bathsheba (see II Samuel 11–12). If **your daughter has grown up,** it is better to **free your** Canaanite **slave and give** him **to her** than to leave her to find a husband on her own. **And be careful with your wife with regard to her first son-in-law,** as she is especially fond of him. **What is the reason** for this warning? **Rav Ḥisda said: Due to** the possibility of **licentiousness. Rav Kahana said: Due to** the fact that she might give him all your **money** and leave you impoverished. The Gemara comments: Since **either of these** could **happen,** it is best to be prudent.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi transmitted three things in the name of the people of Jerusalem. The first: *al tarbe begnut mishum ma'aseh shehayah* — do not engage in behavior that is *gnut*, shameful or exposed, in light of the famous event that occurred. The Gemara understands this as a reference to the episode of David and Batsheva. The Rashbam, however, suggests that the correct reading is *al tirbe begagot* — do not do things on rooftops — since that is precisely the setting of the episode, and one should not assume that being on a rooftop means being out of sight.

The second teaching: *bitcha bagra, shachrer avdecha veten lah* — when your daughter reaches maturity, free your slave and marry her to him. The urgency here is about sexual awareness: once a daughter is of marriageable age and men — including slaves in the household — are of interest to her and she to them, one must act decisively. The structure of the advice reflects the patriarchal setting, where the father controls decisions about marriage; the parallel concern for young men appears in the Gemara's general exhortations to men to marry young, driven by the same awareness of the *yetzer hara*.

The third: *vehevay zahir be'ishtcha mechatanah rishon* — be careful with your wife in relation to her first son-in-law. The Gemara asks why. Two explanations are offered: *mishum ervah* — there is a concern about an illicit liaison, consistent with the sexual theme running through all three teachings — and *mishum mamon* — or perhaps the concern is financial, that she will shower him with excessive gifts out of her joy at her daughter's marriage. The Gemara concludes: *veha veha itnehu* — both concerns are real and valid.

## Righteous & Wicked

### Olam Ha-Ba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלֹשָׁה מִנּוֹחֲלֵי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. אֵלּוּ הֵן: הַדָּר בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְהַמְגַדֵּל בָּנָיו לְתַלְמוּד תּוֹרָה, וְהַמַּבְדִּיל עַל הַיַּיִן בְּמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת. מַאי הִיא? דִּמְשַׁיַּיר מִקִּידּוּשָׁא לְאַבְדָּלְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Three** people are **among those who inherit the World-to-Come: One who lives in Eretz Yisrael; one who raises his sons to** engage **in Torah study; and one who recites *havdala* over wine at the conclusion of Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the special importance of **that** mitzva, to recite *havdala* over wine? The Gemara answers: This is referring to an individual with only a small amount of wine, **who** nevertheless **leaves some of** his kiddush wine **for *havdala*.**

Rabbi Yochanan said: three people inherit the *olam ha-ba*. They are: *hadar be'eretz Yisrael* — one who lives in the Land of Israel; *hamigadel banav letalmud Torah* — one who raises his children for Torah study; and *hamavdil al hayayin b'motza'ei shabbatot* — one who makes *havdalah* on wine on *motzaei Shabbat*.

A student observed that Rabbi Yochanan himself lost all ten of his children while they were still young, which is poignant given his inclusion of *megadel banav letalmud Torah*. The Maharsha notes that the merit attaches to the act of raising children toward Torah, not to their ultimate success — so the reward belongs to the father regardless of outcome.

The question arises: what is the *chiddush* of making *havdalah* on wine? The Gemara answers: *d'mishayer mikiddusha l'avdalta* — that you reserve wine from *Kiddush* for *havdalah*. As Tosafot explains, if one has a limited supply of wine, one does not drink it entirely at *Kiddush* but holds some in reserve so that *havdalah* can be recited *al hakos*. This connects to the broader debate over whether *Kiddush* on bread is a true equivalent or only a *bedi'avad* option. The Maharsha's view, derived from this Gemara, appears to be that when wine is available, even a small quantity should be used for *Kiddush* — and if some can be preserved for *havdalah*, so much the better — rather than defaulting to *Kiddush* on bread in order to save the wine for *havdalah*.

The three categories — living in *Eretz Yisrael* rather than *chutz la'aretz*, raising one's children for Torah, and separating between *kodesh* and *chol* through *havdalah* — share a thematic coherence around the act of sanctification and separation: between holy and mundane land, between Torah-life and ordinary life, and between Shabbat and the rest of the week.

### Daily Proclamation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, שְׁלֹשָׁה מַכְרִיז עֲלֵיהֶן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּכׇל יוֹם: עַל רַוּוֹק הַדָּר בִּכְרַךְ וְאֵינוֹ חוֹטֵא, וְעַל עָנִי הַמַּחֲזִיר אֲבֵידָה לִבְעָלֶיהָ, וְעַל עָשִׁיר הַמְעַשֵּׂר פֵּירוֹתָיו בְּצִינְעָה. רַב סָפְרָא רַוּוֹק הַדָּר בִּכְרַךְ הֲוָה, תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא וְרַב סָפְרָא, צָהֲבוּ פָּנָיו דְּרַב סָפְרָא. אָמַר לוֹ רָבָא: לָאו כְּגוֹן מָר, אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן רַב חֲנִינָא וְרַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא דַּהֲווֹ אוּשְׁכָּפֵי בְּאַרְעָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וַהֲווֹ יָתְבִי בְּשׁוּקָא דְזוֹנוֹת, וְעָבְדִי לְהוּ מְסָאנֵי לְזוֹנוֹת, וְעָיְילִי לְהוּ. אִינְהוּ מִסְתַּכְּלִי בְּהוּ, וְאִינְהוּ לָא מַדְלָן עֵינַיְיהוּ לְאִיסְתַּכּוֹלֵי בְּהוּ. וּמוֹמָתַיְיהוּ הָכִי: בְּחַיֵּיהֶן רַבָּנַן קַדִּישֵׁי דִּבְאַרְעָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan** further **said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, proclaims about** the goodness of **three** kinds **of people every day,** as exceptional and noteworthy individuals: **About a bachelor who lives in a city and does not sin** with women; **about a poor person who returns a lost object to its owners** despite his poverty; **and about a wealthy person who tithes his produce in private,** without publicizing his behavior. The Gemara reports: **Rav Safra was a bachelor living in a city.** When **the *tanna* taught** this *baraita* **before Rava and Rav Safra, Rav Safra's face lit up** with joy, as he was listed among those praised by God. **Rava said to him:** This does not refer to someone **like the Master. Rather,** the statement applies to people like **Rav Ḥanina and Rav Oshaya, who were cobblers in Eretz Yisrael, and they would sit in the marketplace of prostitutes and fashion shoes for prostitutes. And** the prostitutes **would enter** their shops and **look at them.** However, due to their piety, these Sages **did not raise their eyes to look at** the women. And those prostitutes were so impressed with this behavior that when they **swore,** they would say **as follows: By the lives of the holy Sages of Eretz Yisrael.** It is this type of bachelor who is praised by Heaven.

Rabbi Yochanan further said: three are proclaimed about each day by the Holy One Blessed be He. First: *al ravak hadar bikrach v'eino chotei* — a single man who lives in a large city, a *krach*, and does not sin. The *krach* is even larger than an ordinary *ir*, a metropolis with abundant opportunity for sin; remaining celibate in such an environment is an act of extraordinary self-mastery over the *yetzer hara*. Second: *v'al ani hamachzir aveida l'va'aleha* — a poor person who returns a lost object to its owner. Here too, the *yetzer hara* — the desire for money, sharpened by genuine need — is overcome. Third: *v'al ashir ham'aser peirotav b'tzina* — a wealthy person who tithes his produce in private. The wealthy man's *yetzer hara* is not toward keeping the *ma'aser* itself but toward the *ga'avah* of public display — announcing how much he is giving. Doing it quietly conquers that desire.

The Gemara provides a vivid illustration. When a *tanna* recited the teaching about the single man in a large city in the presence of Rava and Rav Safra — who was himself such a man, a bachelor living in a great *krach* — *tzahavu panav de-Rav Safra*: Rav Safra's face lit up. Rava immediately deflated him: *lav kigon mar* — we are not talking about you. Who do they proclaim about in heaven? *Kigon Rav Hanina ve-Rav Oshaya* — like Rav Hanina and Rav Oshaya. These two were shoemakers in the Land of Israel whose shop was located in the market adjacent to the brothel district. They made shoes for the prostitutes and delivered them directly into those premises. The women would look at them, but *lo madlu einaihu le-istakulei behu* — they kept their eyes averted and would not look back. The prostitutes were so struck by their conduct that when they took oaths, they swore *be-hayeihon de-rabbanan kaddishei de-be-ara de-Yisrael* — by the lives of the holy rabbis of the Land of Israel. That is the standard against which celestial proclamation is measured — not simply living a celibate life, but maintaining holiness under conditions of maximum exposure and temptation.

### God Loves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אוֹהֲבָן: מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ כּוֹעֵס, וּמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִשְׁתַּכֵּר, וּמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַעֲמִיד עַל מִדּוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a similar statement. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, loves three** people: **One who does not get angry; one who does not get drunk; and one who is forgiving.**

The Gemara continues with three whom God loves: *mi she-eino ko'es* — one who does not get angry; *u-mi she-eino mishtaker* — one who does not get drunk; and *ve-mi she-eino ma'amid al middosav* — one who does not stand on his dignity, who is willing to let go of grievances and not demand satisfaction when wronged. The Maharsha connects these three to the principle in Pirkei Avot that one who is beloved by people is beloved by Heaven. These three qualities define a person who interacts with others in a way that does not provoke, alienate, or burden those around him. They are, at their core, *bein adam la-chavero* virtues.

### God Hates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שׂוֹנְאָן: הַמְדַבֵּר אֶחָד בַּפֶּה וְאֶחָד בַּלֵּב, וְהַיּוֹדֵעַ עֵדוּת בַּחֲבֵירוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מֵעִיד לוֹ, וְהָרוֹאֶה דְּבַר עֶרְוָה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ וּמֵעִיד בּוֹ יְחִידִי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, hates three** people: **One who says one** statement **with his mouth and** means **another in his heart,** i.e., a hypocrite; **one who knows testimony about another** person **and does not testify on his behalf; and one who observes a licentious matter** performed **by another** person **and testifies against him alone.** His testimony is meaningless, as he is the only witness; consequently, he merely gives the individual a bad reputation.

And three whom God hates: *ha-medabber echad ba-peh ve-echad ba-lev* — one who says one thing with his mouth while thinking another in his heart, the duplicitous person. *Ve-ha-yodei'a eidut be-havero ve-eino me'ido lo* — one who possesses testimony that would help his fellow and withholds it. And *ve-ha-ro'eh davar erva be-havero u-me'id bo yehudi* — one who witnesses his fellow committing a sexual sin and goes to testify against him alone, as a single witness. The grammatical distinction matters here: *bo* rather than *lo* — he testifies *against* him, not *for* him. And because testimony in matters of personal status and sexual prohibition requires a minimum of two witnesses to carry any legal weight — unlike monetary cases where a single witness at least triggers an oath — the sole witness accomplishes nothing legally. His testimony will not be believed or acted upon in court, and the only practical effect is to generate *shem ra*, an evil reputation that damages the subject without any legitimate process.

## Testimony & Hatred

### Tuvya & Zigud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּטוֹבִיָּה חָטָא, וַאֲתָא זִיגּוּד לְחוּדֵיהּ וְאַסְהֵיד בֵּיהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא. נַגְּדֵיהּ לְזִיגּוּד. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טוֹבִיָּה חֲטָא וְזִיגּוּד מִינַּגַּד?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יָקוּם עֵד אֶחָד בְּאִישׁ״, וְאַתְּ לְחוֹדָךְ אַסְהֵדְתְּ בֵּיהּ, שֵׁם רַע בְּעָלְמָא קָא מַפְּקַתְּ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This is **like** that incident **where Tuveya sinned** with immorality, **and Zigud came alone to testify about him before Rav Pappa.** Rav Pappa instructed that **Zigud be lashed.** Zigud **said to him: Tuveya sinned and Zigud is lashed,** an objection that became a popular saying. **He said to him: Yes, as it is written: "One witness shall not rise up against a man"** (Deuteronomy 19:15), **and you testified against him alone. You have merely given him a bad reputation.**

The Gemara provides a concrete illustration: *ki ha deTuvya chata* — Tuvya sinned, presumably in some form of sexual transgression. *Va'ata Zigud lechudei* — Zigud came alone and gave testimony against Tuvya before Rav Pappa. Rav Pappa's response was to administer lashes — not to Tuvya, the one who had sinned, but to Zigud, the one who testified. *Tuvya chata veZigud minged?* Tuvya sins and Zigud gets lashes? The explanation: *de-chituv lo yakum ed echad be'ish* — one witness cannot rise against a person in these matters. *Ve'at lechudach asda'at bei* — you have testified by yourself. *Shem ra be'alma kamafket bei* — all you have accomplished is to spread an evil report about him. The Torah's demand for two witnesses in personal status matters is not merely procedural; it is protective. Without that threshold, the sole witness has not initiated a legal process but committed something that functions as *motzi shem ra* — libel.

This case raises questions that extend far beyond the Gemara's own context. The tension between protecting potential innocent victims of misconduct and protecting the accused from unverifiable accusations is real and urgent, particularly in the contemporary community. A lone witness reporting serious misconduct — sexual abuse, improper behavior — places the community in a difficult position. Dismissing the report entirely silences those who may be telling the truth; accepting it uncritically and acting punitively on that basis can destroy a person's life on the basis of an accusation that may be false. One important factor that emerges from the Gemara is whether there are other corroborating accounts — whether other people have reported similar patterns — as opposed to a single isolated claim with no supporting context.

### Permitted Hatred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק אָמַר רַב: מוּתָּר לִשְׂנֹאתוֹ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי תִרְאֶה חֲמוֹר שׂוֹנַאֲךָ רוֹבֵץ תַּחַת מַשָּׂאוֹ״. מַאי ״שׂוֹנֵא״? אִילֵּימָא שׂוֹנֵא גּוֹי, וְהָא תַּנְיָא: שׂוֹנֵא שֶׁאָמְרוּ — שׂוֹנֵא יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלֹא שׂוֹנֵא גּוֹי. אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא, שׂוֹנֵא יִשְׂרָאֵל. וּמִי שְׁרֵיא לְמִסְנְיֵהּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״לֹא תִשְׂנָא אֶת אָחִיךָ בִּלְבָבֶךָ״. אֶלָּא דְּאִיכָּא סָהֲדִי דְּעָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא נָמֵי מִיסְנֵי סָנֵי לֵיהּ, מַאי שְׁנָא הַאי? אֶלָּא לָאו כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא, דַּחֲזָיא בֵּיהּ אִיהוּ דְּבַר עֶרְוָה. רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: מִצְוָה לִשְׂנֹאתוֹ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִרְאַת ה׳ (שׂוֹנְאֵי) רָע״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak said** that **Rav said:** Although one who sees another committing a sin should not testify against him by himself, **he is** nonetheless **permitted to hate him, as it is stated: "If you see the donkey of he who hates you lying under its load"** (Exodus 23:5). The Gemara clarifies this verse: **What** is the meaning of he **who hates you** mentioned in the verse? **If you say** it is referring to **a gentile who hates** you, **but wasn't** it **taught** in a *baraita* that the phrase: **He who hates, of** which the Torah **spoke, is a Jew who hates** you, **not a gentile who hates** you? **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the verse is referring to **a Jew who hates** you. **But is one permitted to hate** a fellow Jew? **But isn't it written: "You shall not hate your brother in your heart"** (Leviticus 19:17), which clearly prohibits the hatred of another Jew? **Rather,** perhaps you will say that the verse is referring to a situation **where there are witnesses that he performed a sin.** However, in that case, **everyone** else should **also hate him. What is different** about this particular person who hates him? **Rather, is it not** referring **to a case like this, when he saw him** perform **a licentious matter?** He is therefore permitted to hate him for his evil behavior, whereas others who are unaware of his actions may not hate him. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** Not only is this permitted, it is even **a mitzva to hate him, as it is stated: "The fear of God is to hate evil"** (Proverbs 8:13).

Rav Acha brei de-Rava asked Rav Ashi: *mahu lemeimrei leh lerabbeih lemisnaye* — what is the ruling regarding telling the person's master or employer, so that the master should also hate him? This is not a question of formal court testimony; it is a question of whether the lone witness has an obligation or permission to inform someone in authority over the transgressor — a *rebbe*, an employer — about the behavior he has witnessed, even though it cannot be formally proven.

The Gemara resolves: the verse must be speaking of a case where there are witnesses — *de'ika sahadi de'avid issura* — and the person has been publicly established as a habitual transgressor. But if there are witnesses, then *kuly alma nami misne sanei leh* — everyone should hate him; why does the verse say *your* enemy, as though it is specific to you? Therefore, the case must be the one described earlier: *d'hazye bei ihu dvar ervah* — you yourself saw him commit a sexual sin. That is why it is specifically *your* hatred: you know; others do not; and you may not tell them. Sexual sins are committed in private, and the *yetzer hara* operates most powerfully there, which is precisely why you who witnessed it are uniquely positioned to hate him while remaining unable to share what you know.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak goes further: *mitzvah lesanoso* — it is not merely permitted but a *mitzvah* to hate him, as the verse states *yirat Hashem sonei ra* — the fear of God is the hatred of evil.

### Report to Master

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מַהוּ לְמֵימְרָא לֵיהּ לְרַבֵּיהּ לְמִשְׂנְיֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי יָדַע דִּמְהֵימַן לְרַבֵּיהּ כְּבֵי תְרֵי — לֵימָא לֵיהּ, וְאִי לָא — לָא לֵימָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: **What is** the *halakha* with regard **to** whether one who saw someone sin may **tell his teacher** so that **he** too **will hate him?** Rav Ashi **said to him: If** the student **knows** that he is **trusted by his teacher as two** witnesses, and therefore his statement will be accepted, **he** should **tell him, and if** he is **not** trusted by his teacher as two witnesses, he should **not tell him.**

The underlying principle is about calibrating the communication of information to the likely consequence. When telling someone will actually help — will reach a person in a position to act wisely and protectively — then the information should be shared. When telling someone will merely generate suspicion, damage the accused, and accomplish nothing constructive, it should not be shared. This is a framework, not a formula; it requires real judgment about the specific situation, the relationship between the parties, and the reliability of the chain of communication.

### Hatred Breeds

A question about the seeming contradiction emerges. We have established that it is a *mitzvah* to hate a person who has sinned — specifically when you have witnessed their transgression and can therefore no longer engage with them as though they are innocent. But earlier in this same passage, we learned about subduing one's *yetzer hara* through action, and it was taught that you should help your enemy's animal before you help a friend's animal, specifically in order to suppress the impulse of hatred. If it is a *mitzvah* to hate this person, what *yetzer hara* is there to subdue? Where is the improper desire that needs to be fought?

The answer lies in a psychologically profound observation: *Kevan shehu soneh o, gam chavero soneh oto* — because once you hate him, he will hate you back. The hatred has a mechanism of self-perpetuation. *Kamayim hapanim lapanim ken lev haadam laadam* — as water reflects a face back to a face, so does the human heart respond to another human heart. Once he detects or suspects your hatred, he will return it. And that secondary hatred — his hatred of you — has nothing to do with the original sin you witnessed. It is pure mutual enmity, self-perpetuating and unjustified. *Uvah mitoch kach lidei sinah gemurah veshayach kfiyat hayetzer* — and from all of this one arrives at total hatred, and the suppression of the *yetzer hara* becomes relevant precisely there.

You may be right about the first step; your initial hatred of his wrongdoing is justified, even mandated. But the chain it sets off is not within your control. The very fact of your hatred, once known or perceived, creates a cycle of counter-hatred that can consume you both in a mutual enmity that bears no relationship to the original wrong. The *mitzvah* to subdue the *yetzer hara* through helping his animal, through concrete acts of restraint and kindness, is therefore not a denial of the justified hatred — it is a necessary counterweight to prevent that justified hatred from metastasizing into something destructive.

*Sinah goreret sinah* — hatred begets hatred. The insight is sobering: even righteous hatred carries within it a mechanism of moral corrosion. You may be right in your initial judgment; but the chain it initiates is not within your control, and it can consume you in hatred that bears no relationship to the original wrong. The *mitzvah* to subdue the *yetzer hara* in one's behavior toward a person one justifiably hates is, in the end, self-protective — a way of not allowing what begins as a principled moral response to degenerate into something far more destructive.
