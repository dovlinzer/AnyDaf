# Ketubot 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Feeding Children

### Mishnah Setup

The daf begins in the middle of 49b, continuing the Gemara's treatment of the father's obligation to feed his children. The Mishnah had ruled that there is no enforceable obligation to feed daughters; the Gemara extended this to sons as well — which is somewhat counterintuitive, since one might have expected a stronger obligation toward daughters, who had fewer means of self-support in that society. The apparent *pshat* of the Mishnah follows Rabbi Yehuda's position, which seems to reflect precisely that concern for daughters' vulnerability, and yet the Mishnah concludes that neither sons nor daughters carry an enforceable claim. There is, to be sure, a *mitzvah* — a moral and religious imperative — to feed one's children, but nothing the court can actually enforce.

### Infants Exception

Before turning to how this played out in practice, it is worth noting a brief *Tosafot* at the top of 49b, *d'Shem ketanim*. Tosafot observes: *aval kitanei kitanim l'chulei alma chayav* — that with respect to very young infants, everyone agrees there is an obligation. As the Gemara will demonstrate later, when children are still in their infant stage the obligation exists; it is only once they are a few years old that the enforceability falls away.

### Takanat Usha

The Gemara then turns to the question of what happens when a father simply does not provide. Rabbi Ila says in the name of Reish Lakish in the name of Rabbi Yehuda bar Chanina: *be-Usha hitkinu she-adam zan et banav v'et benotav k'shehen ketanim* — in Usha they established that a person must feed his sons and daughters while they are still minors. Rashi explains that Usha was one of the stations to which the Sanhedrin relocated as conditions worsened in the period leading up to the *churban ha-bayit*, and in that period a series of enactments — *takanos* — were issued. Today's daf contains several of these *takanos*, which are largely unrelated to one another.

### Ruling Status

What is striking is that the Gemara immediately raises a question: *ibaya lehu — hilchata kevataih o ein hilchata kevataih?* — do we actually rule in accordance with this *takanat Usha* or not? This is remarkable because, in general, *takanos Usha* are assumed to carry binding force — this was the Sanhedrin, after all, legislating formally. Yet the Gemara seems to understand that this particular *takana* was not universally accepted or embraced.

### Public Shaming

The Gemara proceeds to illustrate how the Amoraim actually handled cases where children appeared before them claiming their father was not feeding them. When such cases came before Rabbi Yehuda, he would say: *yarod yalda v'avnei mata shadya?* — would a snake give birth and cast its responsibility onto the city? As Rashi explains, *ha-tanin holid toldotav v'hital al b'nei ha-ir* — only the cruelest of creatures produces offspring and then burdens others with the responsibility of caring for them. The implication is clear: the Gemara reads this as proof that Rabbi Yehuda did not rule in accordance with the *takana*. If he had, he would simply have declared the father legally obligated to pay; instead, his response was rhetorical — an attempt to shame rather than to compel.

### Marketplace Proclamation

When cases came before Rav Chisda, his method was different: *kofu lei asita b'tzibora* — he would have a mortar turned upside down in the marketplace, serving as a kind of soapbox, and someone would stand on it and proclaim: *orva bei b'nei* — even a raven cares for its children — *v'hahu gavra lo bei b'nei* — and yet this man does not care for his own children and allows them to starve while refusing to provide their food. Rashi notes some ambiguity as to whether it was the father himself who was forced to make this proclamation or a public crier who made it on his behalf, but either way the mechanism is the same: a public notice, in effect, naming a neglectful father — not a legal obligation. The social pressure is real, but the legal apparatus is not deployed.

One might ask why the courts did not simply adopt the *takanat Usha* and use its full enforcement power rather than resorting to public embarrassment. This mirrors what is sometimes done today in *agunah* cases — when legal recourse is unavailable, shame becomes the instrument of last resort. The Gemara, whatever its reasons, clearly understood that reality in these cases was that the courts were not empowered to do more, and this is what they were left with. There is, it must be said, an unmistakable undertone of frustration in these narratives.

### Raven Example

The choice of the raven as the example of a creature that cares for its young immediately invites a challenge from the Gemara itself: *v'orva bei b'nei?* — does a raven actually care for its children? A verse is cited indicating that God provides for the children of the raven that cry out — implying that the raven does *not* take care of its own young and that divine providence steps in instead. How then can one say that failing to feed one's children is worse than a raven's behavior?

The Gemara resolves this: *ha b'chiveri ha b'uchmi* — there are different types of ravens, some white and some black. Tosafot understands this as referring to different species, while Rashi explains it in terms of developmental stages: newborn ravens are white, and at that stage the parent ravens do not recognize them as their own and do not care for them. Once the young ravens begin to darken and look like ravens, the parents take over. So some ravens do in fact care for their young, and the proclamation in the marketplace remains apt.

### Tzedakah Mechanism

When cases came before Rava, his approach was more targeted but still not strictly legal enforcement: *nicha lach d'miznu bnach mi-tzedaka?* — are you comfortable with your children being supported by charity funds? He sought to stir shame, not issue a ruling.

The Gemara then introduces an important qualification: *v'lo amran ela d'lo amid* — all of this discussion about shaming but not compelling applies only where the father is not a man of means. *Aval amid* — but if he is wealthy — *kafinan lei al korchei* — then we do compel him. The compulsion, however, is not based on a legal obligation to feed children per se; rather, it is based on the obligation of *tzedaka*. A court can compel a person of means to contribute to *tzedaka*, and if we can compel that, we can direct that *tzedaka* toward his children — for *tzedaka* begins at home, and his children are a primary obligation. This raises, as an aside, the interesting question of *ma'aser kesafim*: if technically a father is not obligated to feed his children, then the money he spends on their food is, strictly speaking, *tzedaka* — and one might ask whether that expenditure could count toward one's *ma'aser* obligations.

As a proof that courts were indeed empowered to compel *tzedaka* contributions, the Gemara cites the case of Rava compelling Rav Natan bar Ami: *v'afik minei arba me'ah zuzei l'tzedakah* — he extracted four hundred *zuz* from him for *tzedaka*. This demonstrates the principle in action, though it is not specifically about feeding children — it is simply the precedent that coercive power over *tzedaka* exists.

It is worth noting that in terms of personal *tzedaka* obligations, there is no truly quantifiable biblical minimum that one is required to give. The city, however, would organize its own *tzedaka* collections — a *kupa*, a *tamchui*, and other mechanisms that functioned almost like a social services tax, assessing contributions based on wealth. It is that community-organized compulsion that empowers a court to force a man of means to provide.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot raises a sharp question on this entire mechanism: *v'im tomar heichi kafyei mishum tzedaka v'haktiv matan sechara b'tzida* — how can you compel someone to give *tzedaka* when the Torah itself indicates the reward for doing so, and the Gemara elsewhere in *Kol HaBasar* rules that any positive commandment whose reward is stated alongside it — *kol mitzvat asei she-matan secharan b'tzida* — is not one that *beit din* can compel? The example elsewhere is forcing someone to put on *tallit* and *tefillin* or sit in a *sukkah*. *Tzedaka* appears to fall into that same category, given the explicit blessing promised for it.

Tosafot offers two answers. First: *v'yesh lomar d'achfyei bi-dvarim* — perhaps the compulsion was only through words, not physical force, which then brings us back close to where we started, since verbal pressure is essentially what the shaming approach involved all along. Second, and more significant: *v'yesh omrim ee nami kitzvu beineihem bnei ha-ir latet kach v'kach l'chol chodesh* — the townspeople may have collectively stipulated a fixed monthly contribution for each resident, and as the Gemara in the first chapter of Bava Batra establishes, *resha'in bnei ha-ir l'hasia al kitzotan* — the members of a city are empowered to enforce their own communal ordinances. This is not about the biblical *tzedaka* obligation per se, with its uncertain minimum, but about a community's self-governing authority to set and enforce contribution quotas. Tosafot's analysis here has direct implications for the mechanisms of Jewish communal self-governance throughout the medieval period, when Jewish communities were working out precisely how to enforce internally generated rules.

The bottom line of this entire *takana* is that the obligation to feed one's children is either a *takana* that the Gemara seems not to have adopted in practice, or a general *mitzvah* within the framework of *tzedaka* — and the question of how to compel compliance when the father refuses involves a combination of social shame and, for the wealthy, the community's coercive power over *tzedaka* allocations.

## Property Transfer

### Written Property

The Gemara moves to a second *takanat Usha*. Rabbi Ila says in the name of Reish Lakish: *be-Usha hitkinu ha-kotev kol nechasav l'vanav* — in Usha they established that if a person writes over all of his property to his sons during his lifetime — perhaps as a tax arrangement, perhaps simply as a gift — *hu v'ishto nizonin mei-hem* — he and his wife may continue to be supported from that property. The wife's *mezonot* rights arise from her *ketubah* obligations; the husband, who has signed everything away, has no obvious legal claim of his own. Nevertheless, this *takana* establishes that both may continue to draw support from the estate.

### Widow Extension

Rabbi Zeira challenges this ruling — or, some say, it was Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani who challenges — by noting: *gedolim mizo amru* — they have said something even greater than this. What is that greater ruling? *Almanato nizonet mi-nechasav* — that even a man's widow is supported from his property after his death. If a widow's right survives, then surely the rights of a living husband and wife should follow *a fortiori*: *hu v'ishto mibaya* — that he and his wife can be supported goes without saying.

### Widow Lien

The basis for the widow's rights comes from a letter sent by Ravin from Eretz Yisrael. The case: *mi she-meis v'hini'ach almana u-vas* — a man died leaving a widow and a daughter. The widow is entitled to *mezonot* from the estate; the daughter is also supported by the estate until she marries. Now, the daughter subsequently marries and the property passes with her into the hands of her new husband, who exercises control over it. Nevertheless, *almanato nizonet mi-nechasav* — the widow continues to be supported from that property even now that it is in the son-in-law's hands. Further, if the daughter then dies and the husband inherits from her, the widow's claim persists still. This was the ruling stated by Rabbi Yehuda ben achoso shel Rabbi Yossi bar Chanina: *alai hayah ma'aseh* — this is a case that actually came before me, and the widow continued to be supported even after the property passed through multiple hands.

The underlying legal question involves the concept of *shibudim* — liens. Generally, the wife's *mezonot* lien does not follow property into the hands of a purchaser for value, because an open-ended, ongoing obligation of uncertain duration is considered unfair to impose on someone who paid money for the property in good faith. A daughter supported until she reaches *bogeret* has at least a defined endpoint; a widow could theoretically claim *mezonot* for decades. Normally such liens survive only against the estate itself — when the sons inherit, the lien is exacted against them. When it sells out of the estate, the lien ordinarily falls away. Yet Ravin's letter establishes that even when the property passes through the daughter to the son-in-law, and even when the daughter dies and the son-in-law inherits, the widow's claim persists.

Given that, says Rabbi Zeira's challenge, if the widow's lien survives transfer through the daughter and into the hands of the son-in-law — who is now a third party — surely in the case of the *takanat Usha* where the father merely transferred to his own sons and the property has not truly left the family's orbit, the husband and wife should plainly continue to be supported.

### Living vs. Dead

The Gemara, however, does not accept the *kal va-homer* as self-evident. It responds: *mahu d'teima, hasam hu d'leika detarach* — one might have thought those are actually different cases. There, the man is dead. The widow cannot reasonably be expected to go out and earn a living in that society, and so we extend the lien out of consideration for her situation. *Aval hacha nitrach l'didei u'l'dida* — but here the man is still alive. He chose to give everything away. One might have said: he has no money, let him go work and support himself and his wife. Therefore the *takana* establishes that even though he is still alive, having transferred all his property to his children, he and his wife retain the right to be supported from that estate — a ruling the Gemara likens, in its social dimension, to the tragedy of King Lear, who gave everything away and was then left without care.

### Grateful Man

The Gemara then asks again whether we actually rule in accordance with this *takanat Usha*: *ibayu lehu — hilchasa k'vataih d'Usha o lo?* The proof-text offered is the story of Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Chanina standing together when a man approached, bowed down, and kissed Rabbi Yochanan on his feet in gratitude. Rabbi Chanina asked what prompted such extraordinary thanks. Rabbi Yochanan explained: this was a man who had written over all his property to his sons, and *v'asitinu l'zanei* — I compelled the sons to support him. The Gemara reasons: if we say this is not the strict law — *lav dina* — then this explains why the man was so deeply grateful, because Rabbi Yochanan had done something beyond the letter of the law through extra-legal persuasion. But if we say it is the law, then either Rabbi Yochanan was merely doing what was required — hardly cause for such gratitude — or the sons required compulsion despite being legally obligated, which also raises questions. Either way, the gratitude reflects that legal obligation was not at play.

### Ruling Question

The conclusion, again striking, is that both *takanos Usha* — the one about feeding minor children and the one about the man who transfers his property — are handled through social pressure and persuasion rather than strict legal enforcement. The Gemara does not explain why the *beit din* would not simply adopt rulings made by the Sanhedrin, and this remains genuinely puzzling, since one would expect precisely such a formal institution to have its enactments respected. Nevertheless, the Gemara's understanding is clear: these *takanos* were not fully adopted as binding law.

## Charity Limit

### 20% Cap

The third *takanat Usha* is perhaps the most famous: *amar Rabbi Il'a — be-Usha hitkinu ha-mevazbe z al yevazbe z yoter mi-chomesh* — a person who wishes to distribute his wealth should not give away more than one-fifth to *tzedaka*. This enactment appears against a historical backdrop in which certain movements — and the Gemara is implicitly pushing back against ascetic tendencies contemporary to the Rabbinic period — encouraged giving away all of one's wealth as a form of religious devotion. The Rabbis firmly opposed this.

### Rationale

The Gemara confirms this with a *baraita*: *ha-mevazbez al yevazbe z yoter mi-chomesh shema yitzarech la-briyot* — one who scatters his wealth should not give away more than twenty percent, lest he himself become impoverished and dependent on others. Even the framing is instructive: it does not say give as much as you can up to twenty percent. It says *do not exceed* twenty percent. The concern is fundamentally social and economic: a productive, self-sufficient person who gives steadily is more valuable to the poor than one who impoverishes himself in a dramatic gesture and then becomes a burden.

### Excessive Giver

A story illustrates the principle: *u-ma'aseh b'echad she-bikeish levazbe z yoter mi-chomesh* — there was someone who wanted to give away more than twenty percent, *v'lo hini'ah lo chaveiro* — and his companion did not permit him. Who was this? The Gemara offers two versions. In one, the one who wanted to give was Rabbi Yeshevav and his companion who stopped him was Rabbi Akiva. In the alternate version, it is reversed.

### Verse Proof

The scriptural basis is derived from the verse *v'chol asher titten li aseir a'asrenu lach* — "everything You give me, I will give a tenth thereof." The doubling of the root — *aseir a'asrenu* — is read as two tithes: give a tenth, and then give a tenth again. That yields twenty percent as the maximum.

### Two Tithes

The Gemara immediately notes a mathematical objection: *ha lo dami isura basra l'isura kama* — the second tenth is not the same as the first. If one begins with one hundred and gives ten percent, ninety remains; ten percent of ninety is nine, yielding a total of nineteen, not twenty. Rav Ashi resolves this: *basra k'kama* — the verse *a'asrenu* means the second tithe is computed on the same original base as the first. Both tenths are calculated from the original amount, yielding twenty percent in total.

### Chain Weakness

Rav Shimi bar Ashi then observes: *b'shmu'os hallalu mismat'os v'holchos* — these traditions become progressively weaker in their chains of transmission. What he means is structural: looking at the three *takanos Usha* presented thus far, each successive *takana* has a shorter citation chain than the previous one. The first was transmitted as: *amar Rabbi Il'a, amar Reish Lakish, mishum Rabbi Yehuda bar Chanina* — three links. The second dropped one: *amar Reish Lakish, mishum Rabbi Yehuda bar Chanina*. The third is cited simply as *amar Rabbi Il'a* — only one link. The *siman* the Gemara provides to remember the order is: *ktanim kasvu bizbezu* — children, wrote, squandered — corresponding to the three *takanos*: feeding minor children, writing over property, and squandering money in *tzedaka*.

One might ask: does a shorter chain actually mean weaker transmission? If the tradition reached Rabbi Il'a through his teachers regardless, does it matter how many links are cited? The answer is that the citation chain reflects not merely who received the teaching but who first publicly stated or formally published it. *Amar Rabbi Il'a* means that until Rabbi Il'a's time, this teaching had not been formally circulated — even if it had been passed down privately through a chain of teachers. So a shorter chain means that the tradition surfaced at a later stage, and the irony is that while the Gemara does not *pasken* like the first two *takanos Usha*, this third one — the twenty percent limit on *tzedaka* — is in fact accepted as *halacha*.

### Tosafot Scope

Tosafot raises a critical question: twenty percent of *what*? The *Yerushalmi*, Tosafot notes, understands it as follows: *pa'am rishona yachol levazbe z chomesh chomesh u-mikan va-eilach b'chol shana v'shana yitein chomesh me-ha-revach* — the first time, one may give twenty percent of one's principal, one's accumulated capital. Thereafter, one gives twenty percent of one's annual income, not of total assets, since giving twenty percent of total assets each year would rapidly reduce a person to poverty. This distinction between principal and income is significant: ordinarily *ma'aser* is calculated on income, but here, since we are speaking of someone who wishes to donate major assets, the *Yerushalmi* addresses that case specifically.

The twenty percent figure also roughly parallels the total agricultural obligations of the earlier system: *teruma*, *ma'aser*, *ma'aser sheni*, along with *bikkurim* and other *matanos*, together approach approximately twenty percent of agricultural produce. In a monetary economy divorced from agricultural tithes, the *takana* essentially imports that same benchmark.

The question raised in passing — whether the twenty percent limit applies even to the extraordinarily wealthy, for whom the social harm of reducing assets might be negligible — remains genuinely open. One might argue that large fortunes productively invested in the economy are themselves a social good, and that even the mega-rich should refrain from dispersing their capital beyond this threshold. The *takana* as stated draws no distinction.

## Education

### Age 12

The Gemara now presents another *takanat Usha*, this time addressing not feeding children but educating them: *amar Rabbi Yitzchak — be-Usha hitkinu she-adam mitgalgel im beno ad sheim-esrei shana* — in Usha they established that a father must *mitgalgel* — roll along — with his son, meaning guide and nurture him in Torah study, until the age of twelve. The image is of a patient, supportive style of education.

### After 12

*Mikan va-eilach* — from that point, if the son is still not applying himself: *yored lo le-hayyav* — one descends into his very livelihood and life. Rashi explains this concretely: *be-dochka be-ratzu'a* — with physical force, striking him with a strap, *o be-hosekh lechem* — or by withholding food. This is a strikingly different educational philosophy from what modern readers expect, though it must be understood within the context of an era in which physical discipline was the norm, and the stakes of Torah education were considered existential.

### Age 6 School

The Gemara questions whether this picture is accurate, since Rav had told Rav Shmuel bar Shilat — a teacher of young children — *batzir mi-bar sheish lo takbil, bar sheish kabil* — do not accept a student under the age of six into school; from six, accept him. And then: *v'safi lei ke-tora* — stuff it into him like an ox. The image here is of force-feeding: just as oxen were force-fed by having food pressed directly into their throats, so too a six-year-old student should have knowledge driven into him with real intensity.

### Demanding vs. Force

The Gemara resolves the apparent tension: *in, v'safi lei ke-tora — mihu eino yored lo le-hayyav ad le-achar sheim-esrei shana* — yes, you stuff the material in with force and intensity from age six, but you do not descend into his personal life — that is, you do not beat him or withhold food — until after the age of twelve. The distinction is between a demanding curriculum and demanding school environment on one hand, and physically invasive forms of discipline on the other. The former begins at six; the latter is reserved until twelve.

### Age Timeline

A further tension remains with the *Mishnah* in Avot, which assigns Torah study to age five, not six. The Gemara offers one resolution: *ha le-mikra ha le-mishna* — the age-six threshold applies to *Mikra* (written Torah), while the more patient, nurturing style continues until twelve in the context of *Mishnah* study, after which more forceful methods may be applied. A *memra* of Abaye adds further granularity: his nursemaid — who served as his foster mother — told him: *bar sheish le-mikra, bar eser le-mishnah, bar telasar le-tani'ta me-eit le-eit* — at six, begin Torah; at ten, Mishnah; at thirteen, full-day fasting. (For girls, the threshold for full fasting arrives at twelve — one year before they reach *bat mitzvah*, according to most *Rishonim*, though Tosafot disagrees on this point.) In the period before the relevant birthday, partial fasts are appropriate; the full fast-day is reserved for after *bar* or *bat mitzvah*. The implication for the *takana* is that from ten to twelve a nurturing approach continues, and from twelve onward more forceful educational methods may be employed.

### Nursemaid

The Gemara, now having introduced Abaye's nursemaid as a source of wisdom, continues with additional teachings attributed to her. *Ha-bar sheish de-tarka lei akrava be-yoma de-mishlem shit — lo chayei* — if a scorpion stings a six-year-old on the very day he turns six, he will not live. The cure, as Rashi understands it (applicable once the sting has occurred), is *mrarta de-daya chivarta be-shichra* — the bile of a white vulture mixed with mead, which is then rubbed on the child and given to him to drink. Similarly, if a hornet stings a six-year-old on his birthday, *lo chayei* — he will not survive — unless one takes *atzpata de-dikla be-maya* — a vine from a palm tree, boiled in water, and rubs it on the child and has him drink it.

Rav Katina then adds: *kol ha-machnis et bno pachos mi-ben sheish — ratz acharav v'eino magio* — anyone who puts his son into school before the age of six will run after him and not reach him. Rashi understands this as a warning: the child will be too delicate and you will spend all your energy trying to maintain his health without success.

### Weakness Paradox

The Gemara, however, offers an alternative reading: *chaveirav ratzim acharav v'ein magiin oso* — his classmates will run after him and not be able to reach him intellectually. He will be so far ahead of his peers that no one can keep up. The Gemara then says: *u-trvaihu isnahu* — both are true simultaneously. *Chalish* — he will be physically weakened by starting too early — *v'gamir* — but intellectually exceptional. The two effects coexist. This is, the Gemara suggests, analogous to cases where children are pushed to peak achievement at very young ages at significant personal cost.

One final resolution is offered: *ha de-kachish ha de-bari'a* — if the child is constitutionally weak, do not start him before six; if he is strong and healthy, starting before six is permissible.

## Nichsei Melog

### Recovery Rights

The Gemara now turns to yet another *takanat Usha*: *amar Rav Yosef bar Chanina — be-Usha tiknu ha-isha she-machrah be-nichsei melog be-chayei ba'alah u-meisah — ha-ba'al motzi mi-yad ha-lekuchos* — in Usha they established that if a woman sold her *nichsei melog* during her husband's lifetime and then died, the husband may recover that property from the purchasers.

*Nichsei melog* is property a woman brings into the marriage over which she retains title but whose *peiros* — the usufruct, the ongoing benefits — belong to her husband. If she sells this property, the purchaser acquires her title but not the husband's rights to the *peiros*; he continues to enjoy the *peiros* from property now in the buyer's hands. Then, if she predeceases him, the husband inherits her property — but this particular property has already been sold. The *takanat Usha* establishes that despite the sale, the husband may recover the property. In effect, he is treated not merely as someone with rights to *peiros*, but as having enough ownership over the property that upon her death he can take it from whoever holds it.

This means, strikingly, that the purchaser receives almost nothing from the transaction: during her lifetime the husband takes the *peiros*; upon her death the husband takes the property itself. The only scenario in which the purchaser benefits is if the husband predeceases his wife, at which point the purchaser becomes full owner. The purchaser presumably retains a claim in *acharayut* against the woman who sold, but in terms of the property itself, the protection is minimal.

### Ownership Fiction

Tosafot notes that this *takana* intersects with the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish over whether *kinyan peiros ke-kinyan ha-guf dami* — whether ownership of the fruits of a property is equivalent to ownership of the body of the property itself. Depending on how one resolves that debate, the *takanat Usha* may or may not be necessary; the point in any case is that the husband who has rights to *peiros* is treated as sufficiently an owner that upon his wife's death he can reclaim the property from its purchasers.

## Nichsei Melog (II)

### Usha Leadership

The Gemara records an incidental exchange that sheds light on the transmission of all these *takanos Usha*. Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef and Rabbi Abbahu were present at a large public gathering in Usha. He asked who was the *marei d'shmata d'Usha* — the head of the teachings there, the *Rosh Yeshiva*. He was told: *Rabbi Yoshi bar Chanina*. And the Gemara records with some amusement that he had to review this forty times — *tana minei arbin zimnin* — before it stuck, *v'dami lei k'man d'manach b'chisteih* — and then it felt as settled as something in his back pocket. It is a slightly comic note: the kind of intensive review one normally associates with difficult *halachic* rulings is here deployed simply to remember a name. The practical point, however, is that all the prior *takanos Usha* transmitted in the name of *Rabbi Yehuda bar Chanina* may in fact trace back to *Rabbi Yoshi bar Chanina* — the two names being easily confused — and the Gemara's meticulous concern for the precise chain of attribution is characteristic of its broader insistence that *who* said *what* matters enormously.

## Psalm Derash

### Child Support Charity

The Gemara now offers a series of *derashot* on the verse *Ashrei shomrei mishpat oseh tzedakah b'chol eit* — "praised are those who keep justice, who do righteousness at all times" (Psalm 106:3). The verse is read through the lens of *tzedaka* in its rabbinic sense of charitable support. The question posed is: *v'chi efshar la'asos tzedakah b'chol eit?* — can a person really be giving *tzedaka* at every moment? The sages of Yavneh interpreted this, and Rabbi Eliezer explains: *zeh ha-zan banav u'vnotav k'she-hein ktanim* — this refers to one who feeds his sons and daughters while they are young. The circle closes back to the opening of today's daf: since feeding one's children carries no strict legal obligation, every meal one provides is a voluntary act of *tzedaka*, making a parent who feeds young children someone who performs *tzedakah b'chol eit* — righteousness at every moment. This also reinforces the mechanism by which a court can compel a man of means to feed his children: since feeding them is *tzedaka*, and *tzedaka* can be compelled, the compulsion is available.

### Orphan Care

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani offers a different interpretation: *zeh ha-migadel yasom vi'yesomah be-toch beito* — this refers to one who raises an orphan boy or girl within his own home. *U-masia'n* — and then arranges their marriages, ensuring they are provided for beyond the period they live in one's household. Perhaps, the Gemara implies, merely feeding one's own children — while technically *tzedaka* — is so socially universal as to be unremarkable. Taking in orphans and raising them as one's own, and then marrying them off to secure their future, represents something genuinely exceptional and continuous: truly *tzedakah b'chol eit*.

### Torah Wealth

The verse continues: *hon va-osher be-veito ve-tzidkato omedet la-ad* — "wealth and riches are in his house, and his righteousness endures forever." Rav Huna and Rav Chisda apply this to Torah: one of them says this refers to *ha-lomed Torah u-m'lamdah* — one who learns Torah and teaches it to others. Torah knowledge is the wealth that abides in one's house, and because one's students teach their students in turn, the *tzedaka* of transmitting Torah endures across generations indefinitely.

### Written Torah

The other opinion of Rav Huna and Rav Chisda interprets *tzidkato omedet la-ad* differently: *zeh ha-kosev Torah Nevi'im u-Chtuvim u-mash'ilan la-acheirim* — one who writes out *Tanakh* texts and lends them to others. Here the *tzedaka* is concrete and physical: a written scroll that continues to enable others to learn long after the act of writing is complete. Both interpretations — whether one understands the enduring righteousness as intellectual transmission or material provision of texts — speak to the same aspiration: an act whose effects propagate forward without limit.

### Future Generations

The Gemara cites the verse *u-re'eih vanim le-vanecha shalom al Yisrael* — "may you see children to your children; peace upon Israel." Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi explains: *keivan she-banim le-vanecha — shalom al Yisrael* — once your children have children, there will be peace in Israel. The reason: *d'lo asu l'yidei chalitza ve-yibum* — it will not lead to situations of *chalitza* and *yibum*. If a man dies and leaves children, his widow does not require *yibum* or *chalitza*; those obligations arise only in the absence of children. And the act of *chalitza*, which involves a degrading ceremony — a matter the Gemara notes has attracted considerable public controversy in Israel in recent times — is thereby avoided. Ensuring one's children have children of their own is thus a form of *tzedakah* that preserves peace within Israel.

### No Disputes

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani offers a variant on this reading: *keivan she-banim le-vanecha — shalom al dayanei Yisrael* — once there are grandchildren, there will be peace for the *judges* of Israel — meaning the courts will not be burdened with contentious disputes. Rashi explains that the major inheritance disputes arise when there are no clear direct descendants and competing claims emerge among distant relatives. When an estate passes straightforwardly to known heirs, litigation is minimized. The *dayyan* is spared, and peace — at least in the beit din — prevails.

## Daughters Estate

### Objection

The Gemara turns back to the Mishnah's rules about daughters' maintenance from the estate, specifically in the context of the *benin dichrin* provision and the daughters' parallel right to support. The Mishnah had established that just as sons inherit their mother's *ketubah* portion from the estate, daughters are supported by the estate after the father's death. The Gemara now sits at the top of 50b with Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah teaching before the sages.

Rav Yosef was sitting before Rav Hamnuna when Rav Hamnuna stated: *keshem she-ein ha-banim yorshim ela min ha-karka — kach ein ha-banot nizonot ela min ha-karka* — just as the sons inherit only from the land, so too daughters are supported only from the land. The reaction was immediate: *avshu lei kulei alma* — everyone erupted in protest. The objection was sharp: *de-shavik ara hu de-yarti lei benei? de-lo shavik ara lo yarti benei?* — does this mean that sons inherit from their father *only* if there is real estate in the estate? If someone dies leaving nothing but a large bank account, do his sons not inherit? That cannot be right.

### Sons Ketubah

Rav Yosef intervened and offered the correct reading of Rav Hamnuna's statement: *ve-dilma ketubat benin dichrin ka-amar mar* — perhaps what you meant by "the sons inherit only from land" is not that ordinary inheritance is limited to real estate, but rather that the *special* inheritance of *ketubat benin dichrin* — the sons' right to recover their mother's dowry from the estate before the estate is divided among all heirs — is limited to real estate. This is indeed a technical rule: that lien-based claim, being a form of a debt against the estate, only attaches to land. General inheritance, of course, applies to all property, movable or otherwise. And parallel to that limitation on sons, the daughters' *mezonot* claim — which is similarly structured as a lien against the estate — likewise attaches only to real estate.

### Real Estate Lien

Rav Hamnuna confirmed: *mar de-gavra rabba hu — yadaat mai ka-amina* — you are a great scholar and you understood exactly what I was saying. Both the sons' special *ketubah* claim and the daughters' *mezonot* claim are liens against the estate, and liens operate only on real property.

### Practical Reality

Rav Hiyya bar Yosef then adds a significant practical note: *Rav zan mi-chitei de-aliya* — when Rav was obligated to provide for daughters from an estate, he fed them even from the grain stored in the attic. The import is that even though the legal analysis limits the daughters' *mezonot* claim to real estate, Rav in practice provided for daughters from whatever was available in the estate, including stored grain that was movable property. This mirrors the tension seen throughout today's daf: the legal framework on one side, the real human needs on the other — and the practical response of a posek making sure that children are actually fed.

The Gemara then begins to ask: *ibaya lehu — parnasa haviya...* — is this particular provision a case of *parnasa*, maintenance, or something more? This question will be taken up in the continuation of the daf.
