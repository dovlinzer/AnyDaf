# Zevachim 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shelo Lishmah Overview

### Opening

Today's daf is daf 10. We pick up four lines from the bottom of 9b, in the midst of an extended exploration of what constitutes *shelo lishmah* and its various gradations.

### Previous Day Review

At the close of yesterday's discussion, the *Gemara* examined cases where the intent is directed toward the wrong person — specifically, where the *kohen* performs the service with a person in mind who is obligated not a *chatas* but an *olah*. In such a case, the intent is so irrelevant to the actual *korban* that it does not invalidate. The *Gemara* also considered intermediate cases — from one *chatas* to another *chatas*, from a *chatas* to an *olah* — probing the degrees at which *shelo lishmah* crosses from valid to invalid. With those degrees established, we now turn to a different dimension of the problem: not what kind of intent, but *when* the intent occurs relative to the *avodah* it concerns.

---

## Machshava Me'avodah L'av…

### Shechita for Zrika Shelo…

The *Gemara* introduces a new case: *shachata lishmo lizrok damo shelo lishmo* — one slaughtered a *chatas* with proper intent for the *shechita* itself, but with a plan that when the time comes to throw the blood, he will do so with the intent of an *olah*. The *shechita* is performed correctly in the present; the problematic intention is directed at a future *avodah*. The question is whether this forward-looking *machshavah*, held at the moment of *shechita*, invalidates the *korban*.

### R. Yochanan Position

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *psulah* — it is invalid. His principle is *machshavim me'avodah l'avodah*: a *machshavah* formed during one *avodah* can be operative with respect to a different *avodah*. Therefore, thinking during *shechita* that one will perform the *zrikas hadam shelo lishmah* is sufficient to invalidate the *korban* at that moment. Rabbi Yochanan derives this principle from the model of *piggul*: the verse *lo yei'chashev lo piggul hu* is read by *Chazal* as centering on thought — when performing an *avodah*, one's intention to perform the *achilah* component (whether the throwing of the blood, the burning of the *emurim*, or human consumption of the meat) at the wrong time invalidates. This is precisely a case of a *machshavah* about a future *avodah* operating to invalidate the present act.

### Piggul as Model

Rashi is careful to clarify that the *vav* introducing this point connects rather than contrasts: Rabbi Yochanan's position and the *piggul* model are one and the same argument. The structure of *piggul* is instructive. Classic *piggul* cannot arise from performing an act at the wrong time — if one waits until the next day to throw the blood, the *korban* is already *nosar*. Rather, *piggul* operates precisely when the *avodah* is performed at the correct time, but the *kohen*'s intention is to perform a subsequent *achilah* act — throwing the blood, burning the *emurim*, or eating the meat — at the wrong time. It is an intention about a *future* act that retroactively defines the present one.

However, Rashi further notes a tension within this model that will become central to Reish Lakish's critique. The *machshavah* that generates *piggul* concerns acts that are not necessarily the critical *avodos* that render the *korban kasher* — it can concern the human eating of the meat, which is not itself one of the four essential *avodos*. Moreover, if one were to slaughter with the intent that the *shechita itself* be *shelo bizmano* — e.g., explicitly declaring "I am slaughtering this as a nighttime *shechita*" — that thought does not generate *piggul* and does not invalidate. *Piggul* is not created by a mistaken intention about the act being performed; it is created only by an intention about a *future* act.

### Reish Lakish Position

Reish Lakish rules: *kesherah* — it is valid. His principle is the mirror image: *ein machshavim me'avodah l'avodah* — a *machshavah* about one *avodah* does not import itself into and invalidate a different *avodah*. Accordingly, he rejects the *piggul* model.

### Piggul vs Shelo Lishmah

The basis of Reish Lakish's rejection is precisely the asymmetry Rashi identified. *Piggul* is a category unto itself: the problematic thought is specifically about a *future* act, and that future act need not even be one of the critical *avodos* — it can be the eating of the meat. The structure of *piggul* is therefore not analogous to *shelo lishmah*. In *shelo lishmah*, the question is whether thinking during *shechita* about performing *zrika shelo lishmah* — where *zrika* is itself an act that *shelo lishmah* would invalidate — should carry over. But *piggul* is defined uniquely: it is only operative when one performs a critical act at the proper time with an intention about a future consumption-type act. The fact that having a *machshavah* of *shelo bizmano* on the *shechita itself* does not invalidate shows that *piggul* operates in a manner entirely its own, not as a general precedent for *machshavah me'avodah l'avodah*.

## Avodas Zarah Parallel

### Shechita for Avodas Zarah

The *Gemara* presents a parallel case: *hashochet et habeheimah al menat l'hazrik damah l'avodas kochavim ulehaktir chelba l'avodas kochavim* — one slaughters an animal not with the present intention of offering it to *avodas zarah*, but with the plan that when he gets to the blood-throwing or the burning of the *chelev*, he will direct those acts as an offering to *avodas zarah*. The question is whether this plan, held at the time of *shechita*, constitutes sufficient *tikrovet avodas zarah* to render the animal forbidden in benefit — even if he never actually follows through.

As Tosafot notes, one might think the act of *shechita* itself with this intent already constitutes a performative act of *avodas zarah* for which one would be liable. But setting that aside, the distinct question here is whether the plan for the future *avodah* defines the *animal's* status at the moment of *shechita*.

### R. Yochanan Avodas Zarah

Rabbi Yochanan rules the animal is *asur* — forbidden. His reasoning: *machshavim me'avodah l'avodah*, and one may apply the *piggul* model across contexts, learning from what occurs inside the *Beis Hamikdash* to define the status of an act performed outside it (*chutz mipnim*). The intention for a future act of *avodas zarah* is sufficient to define the *shechita* as an act of *tikrovet avodas zarah*.

### Reish Lakish Avodas Zarah

Reish Lakish rules the animal is *muteres* — permitted. *Ein machshavim me'avodah l'avodah*, and one does not import rules of *avodas zarah* from the *piggul* model of the *Beis Hamikdash*. The categories are sufficiently different that the precedent does not transfer.

### Why Both Cases Needed

The *Gemara* establishes that both formulations of the dispute are necessary. Had only the *avodas zarah* case been stated, one might have said that Reish Lakish's leniency stems specifically from the fact that *avodas zarah* is not a *Beis Hamikdash* act — one would not learn it from *piggul*, a *pnim* principle, for a *chutz* context. But perhaps in the purely *Mikdash* context of *shelo lishmah*, Reish Lakish would concede to Rabbi Yochanan. Conversely, had only the *shelo lishmah* case been stated, one might have said that Rabbi Yochanan's stringency is specific to two *Mikdash* principles being compared, but he might concede to Reish Lakish in the *avodas zarah* case. Both cases must therefore be stated explicitly to establish that the dispute runs through both.

## Rav Yirmiya's Piggul Pro…

### Rav Dimi Report

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported that the debate had continued there with advocates taking sides. Rav Yirmiya brought a proof to support Rabbi Yochanan, and Rav Ila brought a proof to support Reish Lakish.

### Rav Yirmiya Support

Rav Yirmiya's approach is to construct a *kal vachomer* from the *piggul* model rather than simply invoking it as a parallel. By examining the internal structure of *piggul*, he attempts to build an argument that is logically compelling independent of whether *piggul* is a perfect analogue.

### Piggul Kal V'Chomer

The *kal vachomer* runs as follows: in the case of *piggul*, if one slaughters and declares "I am slaughtering this as *shelo bizmano*" — treating the present *shechita* as though it were a night-time *shechita* — that declaration is meaningless and the *korban* remains valid. The thought about the present act in the wrong-time register does not invalidate. Yet if one slaughters *al menat lizrok damo chutz lizmano* — with the intent to throw the blood at the wrong time — that does invalidate, generating *piggul*. So: in the world of *piggul*, a *machshavah* about a future act is more powerful than a *machshavah* about the current act.

Now apply this to *shelo lishmah*: in the *shelo lishmah* world, if one slaughters and declares "I am slaughtering this as *shelo lishmah*," that *does* invalidate — the thought about the current act works. *A fortiori*, if a *machshavah* about a future *avodah* is in the *piggul* context more powerful than the thought about the current act, then in the *shelo lishmah* context — where the thought about the current act already invalidates — certainly a *machshavah* about the future *zrika shelo lishmah* should also invalidate.

---

## Piggul Weightiness Issue

### Kares Distinction

Rava bar Ahilai challenges this *kal vachomer*. The issue is not the uniqueness of *piggul*'s structure per se, but that *piggul* is a categorically weightier legal category. When one performs *shechita* with intent to do *zrika shelo bizmano*, he transgresses a *lav* by generating *piggul*. But more significantly, once the animal becomes *piggul*, one who eats its meat is *chayav kares*. By contrast, *shelo lishmah* invalidates the *korban* but eating it does not incur *kares*. Since the *piggul* world carries *kares* and is therefore weightier, its laws may be stricter — one cannot derive that what applies in the *piggul* context must equally apply in the comparatively lighter *shelo lishmah* context.

### Chutz Limkomo Alternative

The *Gemara* therefore proposes replacing *piggul* with *chutz limkomo* as the basis of the *kal vachomer*. *Chutz limkomo* follows precisely the same structural rules as *chutz lizmano* — a *machshavah* during an earlier *avodah* to perform a later *achilah* act in the wrong place renders the *korban pasul* — but without the *kares* consequence. If one performs *shechita* with the intent to sprinkle the blood outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, or to eat the meat outside its proper location, the *korban* is *pasul*, but eating it does not incur *kares*. The *kal vachomer* from *chutz limkomo* thus avoids the *kares* objection entirely.

---

## Rav Ashi's Chutz Limkomo…

### Chutz Limkomo Model

The refined *kal vachomer* proceeds: by *chutz limkomo*, if one performs *shechita* declaring "I am slaughtering this as *chutz limkomo*" — treating the present *shechita* as though it were being done outside its proper location — that is meaningless and the *korban* is valid. The *machshavah* about the current act in the wrong-place register does not invalidate. Yet if one slaughters with the intent to throw the blood in the wrong place, the *korban* is *pasul*. Again, a *machshavah* about a future act is more powerful than a *machshavah* about the current act.

### Chutz Limkomo to Shelo L…

Applied to *shelo lishmah*: since a *machshavah shelo lishmah* about the current act of *shechita does* invalidate, certainly a *machshavah* about performing the future *zrika shelo lishmah* should also invalidate — in parallel with what we observe by *chutz limkomo* where the future-directed thought is more powerful.

---

## Rav Pappa's Challenge

### All Korbanos Affected

Rav Pappa challenges this *kal vachomer* on grounds of comparative weight. *Chutz limkomo*, he argues, is a weightier category than *shelo lishmah* in a specific respect: *chutz limkomo* applies to and invalidates all sacrifices — *olah*, *shelamim*, across the full range of *korbanos*. The *parasha* of *piggul* is principally discussed in the context of *shelamim*, but the principle of wrong-place thought applies universally.

### Limited Application

*Shelo lishmah*, by contrast, only invalidates two specific *korbanos*: *pesach* and *chatas*. All other *korbanos* — *olah*, *shelamim*, *asham* — are valid whether performed *lishmah* or *shelo lishmah*. Because *shelo lishmah* is therefore a less pervasive and less weighty category, it does not necessarily follow that it must possess the same feature of *machshavah me'avodah l'avodah* that *chutz limkomo* possesses by virtue of its universal application.

---

## Shelo Lishem Baalim Model

### Baalim vs Lishmah

Rav Ashi reformulates the argument on entirely different grounds, drawing a model not from *piggul* or *chutz limkomo* but from within the *shelo lishmah* world itself — specifically from the category of *shelo lishmah baalim*. To understand this, one must recall the *drashos* earlier in the *masechta*: the *psukim* teaching *shelo lishem baalim* are built around the phrase *l'chaper alav* — to atone *for him*. The word *l'chaper* points to *zrikas hadam* as the central act of atonement, and *alav* points to the *baalim*, the owner. Since the verse anchors the *baalim* requirement in the *zrika*, what is invalidated by *shelo lishem baalim* at the earlier *avodos* is specifically a *shechita* — or *kabala* or *holacha* — performed with an *intention* directed at the *zrika*: I am doing this *shechita* now with the plan to do the *zrika l'shem Shimon* (when the *korban* belongs to Reuven). That, and only that, constitutes *shechita shelo lishem baalim*. A plain *shechita* for Shimon's name, without any intention about the *zrika*, does not invalidate.

### Baalim Machshava Pattern

This structural point is crucial: *shelo lishem baalim* at the earlier *avodos* is not simply a mislabeling of the current act. It requires a *machshavah* specifically about the *zrika* — I am doing this *shechita* now with the intention that the *zrika* will be for the wrong person. This is precisely the same formal structure as the question under debate regarding *shelo lishmah*: does a *machshavah* during *shechita* about performing the *zrika shelo lishmah* carry operative legal weight?

### Lishmah Kal V'Chomer

The *kal vachomer* is now formulated with precision. By *shelo lishem baalim*: if one declares at *shechita* "I am doing this *shechita lishem Shimon*" — wrong person, current act — the *korban* is valid. This mislabeling of the present act does not invalidate. Yet if one does the *shechita* with the intent to do the *zrika lishem ploni* — wrong person, future act — that does invalidate. So even in the *shelo lishmah* world itself, a *machshavah* about a future *avodah* carries more force than a *machshavah* about the current one.

Now: if a *machshavah shelo lishmah* on the *shechita* itself invalidates, and we know from *shelo lishem baalim* that a future-directed *machshavah* is even more powerful than a current-act *machshavah* — certainly a *machshavah* during *shechita* to perform the *zrika shelo lishmah* should invalidate. This *kal vachomer* operates entirely within the *shelo lishmah* framework, does not depend on *piggul* or *chutz limkomo*, and is not vulnerable to the objections of *kares* or universal applicability. It remains without a rebuttal.

## Rav Ila's Textual Defense

### Zrika Redundancy Question

Rav Ila mounts a textual defense of Reish Lakish. The *Gemara* notes that we have explicit *psukim* teaching *shelo lishmah* at *shechita*, at *kabala*, at *holacha*, and at *zrika*. The question arises: why did the Torah need to state *shelo lishmah* separately at *zrika*? Given that we already have *shelo lishmah* taught at *shechita* and *kabala*, one could have inferred *zrika* from a *kal vachomer*: if *shelo lishmah* invalidates at those earlier *avodos*, certainly it should invalidate at *zrika*, which is the more weighty act — it is the central act of *kapara* and carries the added *psul* of *shelo lishem baalim* and all that flows from it.

Note: why couldn't we have inferred *zrika* from *shechita* alone? Because *shechita shelo lishmah* has its own particular source in the *korban pesach* requirement (*le'ochlav*, etc.), which is unique to *shechita*. And why not from *kabala* alone? Because there would have been grounds to distinguish *kabala* in ways that would prevent the inference to *zrika*. But between *shechita* and *kabala* together, for a *shelamim* (which is the context of these *psukim*), one could certainly infer *zrika*. So why did the Torah state it explicitly?

### Limiting Interpretation

Rav Ila's answer: the Torah's explicit mention of *shelo lishmah* at *zrika* is not expansive but limiting. It comes to tell us that *zrika* has its own *shelo lishmah* requirement — meaning, the *machshavah* must occur *during* the *zrika* itself. The extra word by *zrika* teaches: *ein machshavim me'avodah l'avodah* — a *machshavah* about *zrika* held during an earlier *avodah* does not invalidate. Only a *machshavah* during the *zrika* itself counts.

### Expansion Challenge

The *Gemara* challenges this reading. Perhaps the Torah's explicit inclusion of *zrika* is not limiting but expanding. Perhaps it is teaching precisely the opposite: that a *machshavah* about *zrika* is operative even when held at an earlier stage, thereby *expanding* the scope of when *shelo lishmah* applies. On this reading, the extra word supports Rabbi Yochanan, not Reish Lakish.

### Limiting Conclusion

The *Gemara* responds: given the power of Rav Ashi's *kal vachomer* from *shelo lishem baalim*, it would have been natural — without any additional *pasuk* — to conclude that a *machshavah* during *shechita* to perform *zrika shelo lishmah* invalidates, in direct parallel with the established *halacha* of *shelo lishem baalim*. One would have reached Rabbi Yochanan's position as the natural inference. Since the Torah nevertheless went out of its way to state *shelo lishmah* by *zrika* explicitly, that cannot be mere confirmation of what was already inferrable. It must be a limiting statement: only the *machshavah* during the *zrika itself* invalidates; a *machshavah* about *zrika* held at an earlier *avodah* does not.

The *Gemara* then records the back-and-forth between the positions regarding whether the *psukim* of *shechita* and *kabala* were already sufficient to infer *zrika*. The counter-argument notes that *shechita* and *kabala* of a *chatas* are weightier in certain respects — they must be performed at the north of the *mizbeiach* (*te'un tzafon*), and these *avodos* apply even in the context of inner *chataos* brought in the inner sanctuary — whereas *zrika* on the outer *mizbeiach* has neither characteristic. One might therefore not have inferred *zrika* from *shechita* and *kabala* at all. The rejoinder: but we are discussing *shelamim*, not *chatas*, and in the world of *shelamim* there is no *te'un tzafon*. Therefore the Torah indeed could have remained silent about *zrika* and it would have been inferred. The Torah's explicit statement at *zrika* must therefore carry the limiting message Reish Lakish proposes: only a *machshavah* at the moment of *zrika* invalidates — not a *machshavah* about *zrika* imported from an earlier stage.

## Rav Nachman & Rava

### Rav Nachman Position

The practical *halacha* among the Babylonian *Amoraim* is then recorded. Rav Nachman rules like Rabbi Yochanan: *psula* — the *korban* is invalid.

### Rava Retraction

Rava initially rules like Reish Lakish — *kesherah* — but subsequently retracts and adopts Rav Nachman's position.

### Rava's Reason

The reason for Rava's retraction is the force of Rav Ashi's *kal vachomer* from *shelo lishem baalim*. The argument is straightforward and compelling: we see in the established *halacha* of *shelo lishem baalim* that a *machshavah* during *shechita* directed at the *zrika* invalidates, even though a *machshavah shelo lishem baalim* on the *shechita* itself does not. If this is true in the *baalim* dimension, it should be equally true in the *lishmah* dimension — certainly when a *machshavah shelo lishmah* on the *shechita* itself already invalidates, a *machshavah* about performing the *zrika shelo lishmah* at the time of *shechita* should invalidate as well. This *kal vachomer*, operating entirely within the existing *shelo lishmah* framework and requiring no external model, persuaded Rava to retract.

It bears noting: this principle applies specifically to *zrika*. The unique legal force of *zrika* — *lizrok damo l'chaper alav* — is what underlies *shelo lishem baalim*. It is not a general rule that any *machshavah* about any future *avodah* imports itself into the current one; it is specifically a *machshavah* about *zrika* that carries this power at the earlier stages.

## Rabbi Eliezer Asham Proof

### Asham-Chatas Link

The *Gemara* now turns to the position of Rabbi Eliezer in the *Mishna*, who states that an *asham* is analogous to a *chatas*. The *Gemara* cites a *braisa* that elaborates.

### Rabbi Eliezer Argument

Rabbi Eliezer argues: *chatas* comes for sin and *asham* comes for sin; just as a *chatas shelo lishmah* is *pasul*, so too an *asham shelo lishmah* is *pasul*. The logic is intuitive — a *chatas*'s entire identity is bound up with atonement for a specific person's specific sin; once it is performed with a different identity in mind, it is disconnected from its purpose. Rabbi Eliezer maintains that the same is true of an *asham*, and he has the verse *kachatas ka'asham* as explicit textual support linking the two.

---

## Blood Location Distincti…

### Above vs Below Line

Rabbi Yehoshua responds to Rabbi Eliezer's initial argument by pointing to a distinction between *chatas* and *asham* regarding the placement of blood on the *mizbeiach*. The *mizbeiach* has a midpoint marker called the *chut hasikra* — the red line. Blood of a *chatas* is placed at the four *karnos* — the corners of the *mizbeiach* — which are above this line. Blood of most other *korbanos*, including *asham*, goes below the line. *Olas ha-of* goes above. The *chatas* is thus categorically distinct in its blood service, and perhaps it is precisely this distinction — reflecting the unique weightiness of *chatas* — that underlies its *shelo lishmah* invalidation. An *asham*, whose blood goes below, cannot simply be equated with it.

### Pesach Counterexample

Rabbi Eliezer counters: *pesach yochiach* — the *korban pesach* proves otherwise. A *pesach shelo lishmah* is also *pasul*, yet the blood of *pesach* goes below the line. Therefore, being above or below the line is not what determines whether *shelo lishmah* invalidates. You cannot use the blood-placement distinction to differentiate *asham* from *chatas*.

### Fixed Time Argument

Rabbi Yehoshua responds: *pesach* has a fixed time — it can only be brought at the appointed moment — and perhaps that unique characteristic accounts for the stringency of *shelo lishmah* by *pesach*, not the above/below distinction. An *asham*, which has no fixed time, cannot be derived from either *chatas* or *pesach*.

### Circular Reasoning

Rabbi Eliezer counters by returning to *chatas*: a *chatas* has no fixed time, so one cannot blame the *pesach* stringency on its fixed time. This is the classic *chozer hadin* — he wants to eliminate each distinctive characteristic through the other example, leaving a common denominator. Don't say it's about being above the line — *pesach* proves otherwise. Don't say it's about a fixed time — *chatas* proves otherwise. What remains must be the relevant factor.

### Common Denominator Need

Rabbi Yehoshua's response — *chozarni chalila*, "I have gone in a circle" — sounds, on the surface, like an admission of defeat. But the *Gemara* will explain that it is in fact a calculated move: by noting that the *chozer hadin* leads in a circle, Rabbi Yehoshua is flagging that no valid *tzad hashaveh* can be constructed from this pair.

---

## Hirhurim/Hu Proof

### Hu Word Proof

Rabbi Eliezer pivots to a textual argument. The Torah uses the word *hi* (or *hu*) in proximity to each of these three *korbanos*: *chatas hi* by the *chatas*, *zevach pesach hu* by *pesach*, and *asham hu* / *asham asham l'Hashem* by *asham*. The word *hu* or *hi* signals that the *korban* must be what it is — it must be performed as that which it is designated. The *Gemara* earlier established that *hi lishma kesherah shelo lishma psula* — only when the *korban* is done *lishmah* is it valid. Since all three *korbanos* carry this formulation, Rabbi Eliezer argues that all three should be invalid *shelo lishmah*.

### Timing Distinction

Rabbi Yehoshua distinguishes. By *chatas*, the word *hi* appears at the moment of *shechita* — the verse *shachato osah l'chatas* and *chatas hi* — so the *hu/hi* word applies precisely at the *avodah* that can be rendered *shelo lishmah*. By *pesach* as well, *zevach pesach hu* appears in the context of the *shechita*. But by *asham*, Rabbi Yehoshua argues, the word *asham hu* appears only after the description of the burning of the *emurim*.

### Too Late to Invalidate

By the time the *emurim* are burned, one has already passed through the four critical *avodos*. At that stage, *shelo lishmah* can no longer invalidate — the *korban* is already complete with respect to the blood service. Moreover, even if the *emurim* themselves were never burned, the *korban* would still be valid. The *hu* by *asham* therefore cannot serve as a source for *shelo lishmah* invalidation, because it appears at a point in the verse where such invalidation is no longer relevant.

---

## Explicit Verse Link

### Ka-Chatas Ka-Asham

Rabbi Eliezer offers a third and final proof. The Torah contains an explicit verse that links *asham* directly to *chatas* using the words *kachatas ka'asham*: just as a *chatas*, so too an *asham*. From this explicit equation, Rabbi Eliezer derives: just as *chatas shelo lishmah* is *pasul*, so too *asham shelo lishmah* is *pasul*. This is not an inferential analogy requiring one to navigate distinguishing characteristics — it is a direct textual linkage.

### No Response

The *braisa* records no response from Rabbi Yehoshua to this third proof. The structure of the *braisa* thus presents Rabbi Eliezer's position as prevailing by the third argument, even if the first two were successfully parried.

---

## Tzad Hashaveh Analysis

### Common Denominator Setup

The *Gemara* now unpacks what was happening in the back-and-forth between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. When Rabbi Yehoshua said *chozarni chalila* — "we have gone in a circle" — he was not simply complaining about circularity. He was strategically identifying that the *chozer hadin* Rabbi Eliezer was attempting requires a valid *tzad hashaveh*, and the *tzad hashaveh* here is vulnerable.

### Tzad Hashaveh Method

The *tzad hashaveh* method works by taking two cases, eliminating each one's distinguishing characteristics through the other, and identifying what they share in common. If that common denominator is what underlies the *halacha* in both, one can apply it to a third case. Rabbi Eliezer was using *chatas* and *pesach* as his two cases: don't blame the *shelo lishmah* stringency on being above the line (*chatas*), because *pesach* proves otherwise; don't blame it on a fixed time (*pesach*), because *chatas* proves otherwise. What remains is: both are *korbanos* that come for sin, and both are *shelo lishmah pasul* — therefore *asham*, which also comes for sin, should be *shelo lishmah pasul*.

### Kares Concern

Rabbi Yehoshua's real counterpoint was that the common denominator between *chatas* and *pesach* is not merely "comes for sin" — it is that both are connected to *kares*. A *chatas* atones for a sin punishable by *kares* (at least in the paradigmatic case), and failure to bring a *korban pesach* incurs *kares*. Their shared connection to *kares* may be what lends them the stringency of *shelo lishmah*. An *asham* carries no such connection to *kares*, and therefore cannot be derived from this *tzad hashaveh*. This is what Rabbi Yehoshua meant: by pointing to *kares* as the true common denominator, he shows that the *tzad hashaveh* cannot extend to *asham*.

---

## Blood Location Justifica…

### Location vs Other Factors

The *Gemara* now examines why Rabbi Yehoshua specifically chose the above/below-line distinction as his initial pushback against Rabbi Eliezer, rather than any number of other ways in which a *chatas* is demonstrably weightier than an *asham*. One might have thought: why focus on above and below the line? Is that really the most significant distinguishing feature?

The *Gemara* considers several alternatives. Why not say that a *chatas* is weightier because in certain cases (*chataos penimiyos*) its blood is brought into the inner sanctuary — *lifnai v'lifnim*? The answer: we are speaking of the *chatas hachitzonah*, the *chatas* whose blood is applied to the outer *mizbeiach*, and those *chataos* do not have that characteristic. Why not point to the *halacha* that if the blood of a *chatas* is brought into the *Heichal*, the *korban* is invalidated — *kol chatas asher yuva midamah el ohel mo'ed lachaper bakodesh lo te'achel ba'esh tisaref* — a stringency that does not apply to the blood of an *olah* or other *korbanos*? Here, however, the *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Eliezer, who consistently links *asham* to *chatas*, would himself say the same *halacha* applies to *asham* — if *asham* blood were brought into the *Heichal*, it too would be invalidated. So Rabbi Yehoshua could not use that as an asymmetric distinguishing feature.

### Alternative Distinctions

The *Gemara* continues to consider other potential distinctions. Why not say *chatas* is weightier because it is associated with sins of *kerisos* — atonement for sins punishable by excision? Rabbi Eliezer counters: there are *chataos* that come for non-*kares* sins — such as the *chatas* of *shemiat hakol* (oath relating to testimony), which is not a *kares* sin. So one cannot say the *shelo lishmah* stringency of *chatas* is rooted in its connection to *kares* sins.

Why not say *chatas* is weightier because it requires *arba matanos* — four separate applications of blood, one at each corner? The *Gemara* asks why Rabbi Yehoshua did not use this feature. The answer: perhaps Rabbi Eliezer holds like Rabbi Yishmael, who maintains that all blood applications — not just *chatas* — require four separate acts of throwing. If one follows Rabbi Yishmael's view, both *chatas* and other *korbanos* each receive four separate blood applications, so this feature would not differentiate *chatas* as uniquely weighty.

### Multiple Options Availab…

The *Gemara* concludes: there are indeed many ways one could demonstrate that a *chatas* is weightier than an *asham* — among them the four *karnos* (as opposed to two-that-are-four), the application with a finger (*etzba*), the placement at the *keren* specifically, the precise edge (*huda*) of the *keren*. Rabbi Yehoshua could have chosen any of these. He chose the above/below-the-line distinction as one example from among several available arguments. *Chada mi'trei tlas ta'ami nakat* — he selected one from two or three possible reasons.

---

## Asham Below Line Logic

### Expected Objection

The *Gemara* now raises a question about Rabbi Eliezer's silence. Given that Rabbi Eliezer consistently wants to make an *asham* equivalent to a *chatas*, why did he not simply respond to Rabbi Yehoshua's blood-placement argument by saying: I maintain that the blood of an *asham* should also go above the line? If *asham* is like *chatas* in matters of *shelo lishmah*, why not also in blood placement?

### Olah Kal V'Chomer

Abbaye explains that Rabbi Eliezer could not make this move, because a *kal vachomer* from *olah* precludes it. An *olah* is entirely consumed — *kalil* — the entire animal is burned on the *mizbeiach*, making it the most weighty *korban* in terms of its complete dedication to Heaven. Yet even the *olah*'s blood goes below the line. An *asham*, which is not *kalil*, most certainly should be below the line as well. This *kal vachomer* prevents Rabbi Eliezer from claiming the *asham* blood goes above.

### Not About Atonement

The *Gemara* questions whether one can push back against this *kal vachomer*: perhaps the reason *chatas* blood goes above the line is specifically because *chatas* atones, and an *olah*, which does not atone for a specific sin in the same way, naturally goes below. If so, the *olah* is not a good comparison for *asham*, which does atone. The *Gemara* counters with the *chatat ha-of*.

### Bird Chatas Counterexamp…

A *chatat ha-of* — a bird *chatas* — does atone, yet its blood goes below the line. So atonement alone does not place blood above the line. Therefore the *olah kal vachomer* stands: one cannot argue that an *asham*, which atones, must go above the line simply because it atones.

### Common Denominator

The *Gemara* then anticipates the counter: a bird is categorically different — *einah min hazevach*, it is not among the slaughtered large animals. Perhaps large animals that atone should go above the line. The *Gemara* responds by constructing a *tzad hashaveh*: between *olah* (large animal, not *kalil* in every sense, does not atone) and *chatat ha-of* (atones, but not a large slaughtered animal), the common denominator is that both go below the line.

### Kodshei Kodashim

This leads to the principle: *kodshei kodashim* — things of the highest sanctity — have their blood below the line. Both *olah* and *chatat ha-of* are *kodshei kodashim*, yet both go below. An *asham* is also *kodshei kodashim*. Therefore *asham damo l'mata* — the blood of an *asham* goes below the line — and Rabbi Eliezer had no basis to argue otherwise.

---

## Word Osah Limitations

### Asham Weightier

Rava from Pizka raises a challenge to the above analysis. He argues that an *asham* is in fact weightier than the comparison cases used above. Specifically, an *asham* has a minimum monetary value (*kitzvah*) — the Torah states *asham b'kesef shekalim* — whereas both an *olah* and a *chatat ha-of* have no fixed minimum worth. Therefore, the *tzad hashaveh* between *olah* and *chatat ha-of* is undermined: their common denominator of going below the line may be linked to their lesser weight in terms of monetary obligation. An *asham*, being weightier by virtue of its required minimum value, might be expected to go above the line, like a *chatas*.

### Osah Limits Chatas

The *Gemara* responds with a textual argument. The verse says *ha-Kohen ha-mechateh osah* — the *kohen* who makes it a *chatas* — in the context of the blood placement above the line. The word *osah* — "it," referring specifically and exclusively to the *chatas* — teaches: *v'ein damo shel acher l'ma'ala* — no other blood goes above the line. This *osah* is a limiting term that restricts the blood-above-the-line requirement to *chatas* alone, regardless of any comparative weightiness an *asham* might possess.

### Osah for Lishmah

The *Gemara* raises a further question: if *osah* is limiting, doesn't *osah* in the *lishmah* context also limit — teaching that only a *chatas* must be done *lishmah*, and nothing else? If so, Rabbi Eliezer's entire project of extending *shelo lishmah* to *asham* would be undermined by the same word.

### Pesach Exception

The *Gemara* answers: *osah* in the *lishmah* context cannot be fully excluding, because *pesach* is also *shelo lishmah pasul*. Since the *osah* does not exclude *pesach* from the *lishmah* requirement, it cannot be functioning as a strict exclusionary term in that context. It does not mean "only *chatas* and nothing else."

### Slaughtered Animals Only

By contrast, when *osah* limits blood placement above the line, it does function as a genuine exclusion. Among all *shechted* animals — large animals slaughtered in the conventional manner — only the *chatas* has blood above the line. The *olas ha-of* goes above the line as well, but that is a bird, not a slaughtered animal in the ordinary sense. So among *zevachim* — slaughtered animal offerings — *osah* limits the above-the-line placement exclusively to *chatas*. This is a genuine and operative exclusion, unlike the *lishmah* context where *pesach* already prevents *osah* from functioning as absolute exclusion.

---

## Separate Placement Doctr…

### Source Attribution

The *Gemara* offers an alternative explanation for why Rabbi Eliezer concedes that *asham* blood goes below the line, based on the position of Rabbi Eliezer b'Rebbi Shimon.

### Separate Places

Rabbi Eliezer b'Rebbi Shimon maintains that each *korban* requires its own distinct place on the *mizbeiach*. The different types of blood service are not simply categorized as "above" or "below" a line; rather, each type of *korban* has a specific location reserved for it, and those locations cannot overlap.

### No Space for Asham

Given this principle: *olas ha-of* has its specific location above the line; *chatas behemah* has the four *karnos* as its specific location. These two together account for the available space above the line. There is simply no remaining place above the line for the blood of an *asham* to go, since every position above the line is already occupied by the specific requirements of *chatas* and *olas ha-of*.

### Placement Constraints

Therefore, the blood of an *asham* must go below the line — not because of any inherent inferiority of *asham*, but because the spatial organization of the *mizbeiach* as understood by Rabbi Eliezer b'Rebbi Shimon leaves no available position above the line for it. Rabbi Eliezer thus concedes this point on structural grounds rather than because *asham* is categorically less weighty than *chatas*.

---

## Unresolved Tension

### Concession Explanation

With the analysis now complete, the *Gemara* has established why Rabbi Eliezer concedes the point about blood placement — whether through the *osah* limitation or through Rabbi Eliezer b'Rebbi Shimon's placement doctrine, the blood of an *asham* must go below the line, and this is not something Rabbi Eliezer can argue against.

### Maintains Position

Nevertheless, Rabbi Eliezer's concession on blood placement does not move him from his primary position. Even granting that *asham* blood goes below the line — making *asham* structurally different from *chatas* in that respect — Rabbi Eliezer maintains that *asham shelo lishmah* is *pasul*, just like *chatas*. The blood placement distinction, in his view, does not undermine the *shelo lishmah* equivalence established by the verse *kachatas ka'asham*.
