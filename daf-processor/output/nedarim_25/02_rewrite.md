# Nedarim 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nidrei Zeruzin & Havai

### Ein Piv Velibo Shavin

We pick up at the bottom of 24b, where the *mishnah* has already introduced us to the four categories of *nedarim* that are not binding. The unifying principle underlying all four is *ein piv velibo shavin* — the words spoken did not reflect the speaker's genuine intent. Even when the correct formulation was used, the vow lacks the essential congruence between speech and will that is required for it to take effect.

### Nidrei Zeruzin

The first category is *nidrei zeruzin* — vows made for the purpose of incentivizing or encouraging someone else. Whether the other party understood the speaker was not serious (as in the classic case of haggling) or genuinely believed the vow to be sincere, the bottom line is that the speaker never truly intended it to be binding. The vow was deployed as a social or rhetorical tool, not as a genuine act of prohibition.

### Nidrei Havai

The second category is *nidrei havai*. Here too the speaker never actually wants the object to become forbidden. Instead, the *neder* is used to lend weight to a statement — typically a patently false one. The *neder* functions as a kind of rhetorical emphasis, not as a genuine act of prohibition.

### Fish Stories & Guzma

The *mishnah* presents two examples of *nidrei havai*. The first — swearing that one saw as many people as those who left Egypt — is treated by the *Ran* as a case of *guzma*, a hyperbolic exaggeration. On his reading, the statement may not even be technically false; it is simply not to be taken literally, and therefore it does not properly belong to the category of *nidrei havai* at all. Setting aside the *Ran*'s somewhat distracting analysis, the essential case is the second one: claiming one saw a snake as large as the beam of an olive press. This is a transparently false statement — a "fish story," as it were. Just as people telling tall tales tend to embellish and pile on, so here the speaker adds a *neder* to back up the claim and give it emphasis. Since everyone understands the statement is false and the speaker never genuinely wanted anything forbidden, the *neder* does not take effect. In *nidrei zeruzin*, nobody takes the vow seriously, or at least the speaker does not. In *nidrei havai*, everybody knows the underlying claim is false, and the *neder* is merely its rhetorical backing.

## Shvuot Havai Analysis

### Tana: Nidrei/Shvuot

The *Gemara* on 25b opens with a *tanna* teaching: *nidrei havai mutarim* — vows of exaggeration are not binding — while *shvuot havai assurim* — oaths of exaggeration are forbidden. A second *girsa* reads instead *keshem shenidrei havai mutarim kach shvuot havai mutarim* — just as such vows are not binding, so too such oaths are not binding. The distinction between these two versions matters considerably, because the question of whether something is *assur* or *mutar* depends entirely on what one is looking at: is the question whether taking the *shvua* was forbidden, or whether the object the *shvua* was meant to prohibit actually becomes forbidden?

### Abaye's Translation

The *Gemara* asks: what exactly is a *shvuat havai*? If one says "a *shvua* that I did not see as many Jews as the *yotzei mitzrayim*," the *Gemara* objects — *midi ka'amar*? That formulation is grammatically incoherent; an oath attached to an "if" clause that goes nowhere says nothing. Abaye therefore reformulates: the oath is stated positively — *shvua she'ra'isi* — I swear that I did see so many Jews. This is the direct translation of the *neder* form into the oath form. Where the *neder* says "this will be forbidden if I did not see," the *shvua* says "I swear that I did see." In such a case — swearing to back up a patently false statement — the *shvua* would constitute a *shvuat shav*, an oath taken in vain, since everyone understands the statement to be false.

### Rava's Objection

Rava objects to Abaye's framing. If the case is simply that someone swore to back up an obviously false statement, we already know that is a *shvuat shav* — what is the novelty? Moreover, the *mishnah* in *Shvuos* frames the case in a way that must be analogous to the *neder*: not just backing up a statement, but actually making an object forbidden.

### Shvuat Shav vs. Forbiddi…

Rava therefore presents the properly analogous case: *be'omer ye'asru peiros ha'olam alai bishvua im lo ra'isi baderech hazeh ke'olei mitzrayim* — one says that all fruit of the world shall be forbidden to him by *shvua* if he did not see as many people as the *olei mitzrayim* on this road. This mirrors the *neder* precisely: in the *neder* case, one uses *konam* to prohibit food as backing for a false claim; here, one uses a *shvua* to achieve the same effect. Now the analysis splits into two distinct questions. First, was it permitted to take such a *shvua*? The *Ran* says no — it constitutes a *shvuat shav*, and one would receive lashes for it, because one is invoking God's name to back up what is obviously a false statement with no genuine intent to prohibit anything. Second, does the food actually become forbidden? Here the answer is no — the food remains permitted, because just as in the *neder* case the speaker never genuinely wanted the object to be prohibited, so here too the *shvua* was never about making food forbidden but only about lending rhetorical weight to a false claim.

This explains the two *girsaot*: *shvuot havai assurim* refers to the act of taking such a *shvua*, which is forbidden as a *shvuat shav*. *Shvuot havai mutarim* refers to the food, which remains permitted. The parallel with cases like one who swears he will not sleep for three days or will not sit in the *sukkah* is instructive: those *shvuot* are not binding, which is precisely why they constitute *shvuat shav* — because they are purposeless, they were forbidden to take in the first place. So here too: because the *shvua* cannot genuinely prohibit the food, the act of swearing was itself a *shvuat shav*.

## Interpretation in Oaths

### Ravina: Anthill Case

Ravina poses a challenge to Rav Ashi. The *Gemara* had been discussing the case where one swears that one saw as many people as *olei mitzrayim* on a road. Ravina asks: perhaps this man had an anthill at home that he called "*olei mitzrayim*" — and perhaps he saw forty ants, which is exactly how many people he saw in the road? In that case, the *shvua* would actually be true, and the food might genuinely be forbidden. One might note that this is in a sense the inverse of *perushan le-hakel*: there, a lenient private interpretation is invoked to escape a *neder*; here, a stricter private interpretation is invoked to render the food forbidden — a kind of *perushan le-hachmir*. And there is a practical dimension as well: if the *Gemara* was about to impose *malkus* on someone for taking an oath in vain, that person might well claim he genuinely meant his anthill and therefore took no *shvuat shav* at all.

### Rav Ashi: Standard Meani…

Rav Ashi responds: *ki mishtava ada'ata didan mishtava* — when a person takes an oath, it is based on what we understand, not on whatever private meaning was going through his head. This is another expression of the principle of *devarim shebalev einam devarim*: one cannot retroactively assign a private meaning to words that everyone understands in a standard way.

This ruling appears to be in tension with the earlier discussion of *perushan le-hakel* by *nedarim*. The distinction is significant. A *neder* is fundamentally about establishing the status of an object — it operates between the person and the Almighty. A *shvua*, by contrast, is oriented toward other people: it is a way of backing up statements, making commitments, communicating promises. Because *shvua* is inherently communicative and interpersonal, it must go by what the words are understood to mean by others. You cannot unilaterally determine what your oath means when oaths exist precisely for the purpose of conveying meaning to others. The phrase *ada'ata didan* — based on what we think — captures this orientation exactly.

### Braita: Beit Din Oaths

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from a *braita*: when *beit din* administers an oath, they explicitly say to the oath-taker: *hevei yodea she-lo al tnai she-be-libcha anu mashbi'in otcha* — know that we are not administering this oath on the basis of whatever mental stipulation you have in your heart — *ela al dateinu ve-al da'at beit din*, but rather based on our understanding and on the understanding of *beit din*. If the general rule were already that oaths are interpreted according to communal standard meaning and never according to private intent, why would *beit din* need to state this explicitly? The need to declare it implies that in the ordinary case — outside *beit din* — one could in fact rely on one's own private interpretation.

### Chips of Wood Case

The *Gemara* asks: what case is *beit din*'s declaration coming to exclude? It is not coming to exclude the anthill interpretation — that would never be valid in any context, since calling one's anthill "*olei mitzrayim*" is a crazy interpretation that no one would accept. Rather, the declaration is coming to exclude a case like this: a borrower repaid his debt not in actual money but in chips of wood, to which he privately assigned the name "dollars." He then swears in *beit din* that he paid back the money. He is technically telling the truth — he gave "dollars" — but the word means something that no one else would recognize as payment. This type of technically-accurate-but-privately-defined usage is what *beit din* explicitly forecloses by declaring *al dateinu*. Outside *beit din*, one might have been entitled to rely on such a technically accurate — if misleading — private meaning.

### Rava's Staff Story

A famous story illustrates this principle. There was a man who owed money to a creditor. Rava instructed him to go and pay. The debtor claimed he had already paid. Rava said, then go swear to him. The debtor took his staff, had it hollowed out, placed the coins inside, and gave it to the creditor to hold while he picked up a *sefer Torah* to swear on. He then declared — according to the *Ran*'s *girsa* — *yahavti*, I gave you the money, which was technically accurate, since the coins were at that moment in the creditor's hands inside the staff. The creditor grew furious, smashed the staff, and the coins spilled out. The debtor declared: you see, I was telling the truth all along.

The *Ran* adjusts the *girsa* from *parei* (I repaid) to *yahavti* (I gave), because if he had said *parei*, even handing over money would not constitute repayment of a loan in the technical sense. But the broader point stands: the permissible range of interpretation extends to statements that are technically accurate, even if not what the other party would have assumed. What is never permissible is a genuinely crazy interpretation — calling chips of wood "dollars," or calling one's anthill "*olei mitzrayim*."

### Crazy vs. Technical

The *Gemara* thus draws a clear line. There are two distinct categories. The first is a genuinely absurd private meaning: I called my anthill "*olei mitzrayim*"; I call these chips of wood "dollars." Such interpretations are never valid, in *beit din* or outside it. The second category is a technically accurate meaning: the words, understood according to their normal semantic range, could validly describe what the speaker meant, even if that meaning was not what the listener assumed. Outside *beit din*, the speaker may rely on this technically accurate meaning. Inside *beit din* — or wherever a party explicitly declares *al dateinu* — even technical accuracy is insufficient; the words must carry their fully shared, standard meaning.

### Moshe & Arvot Moav

The *Gemara* brings a further proof from a *braita*: *ve-chein matzinu be-Moshe Rabbeinu* — we find the same principle by Moshe. When Moshe caused *Bnei Yisrael* to take an oath in *Arvot Moav*, he declared: *hevei yode'im she-lo al da'atchem ani mashbi'a eschem* — know that I am not administering this oath based on your understanding — *ella al da'ati ve-al da'as haMakom*. The verse supports this: *ve-lo itchem levadchem*, which the *Ran* interprets as "not what is within you alone" — not the private thoughts in your hearts. It was necessary for Moshe to make this explicit declaration in order to prevent *Bnei Yisrael* from later claiming that when they swore to worship God alone, they had privately meant their own gods by the word *Eloha*.

### God vs. Idols Name

The *Gemara* asks: does this prove that outside such an explicit declaration, one *can* rely on one's private interpretation? The *Gemara* answers no. The case of calling an idol "*Eloha*" is not a crazy interpretation at all — the Torah itself uses the word *Eloha* to refer to foreign gods, as in the verse *u-le-elohei Mitzrayim* (Shemos 12:12). So calling an idol "*Eloha*" is not a private eccentricity but a technically accurate usage. Moshe's declaration was therefore coming to exclude precisely the technically-accurate-but-non-standard case, not the crazy case. The crazy case — calling one's anthill "*olei mitzrayim*" — would never be valid regardless.

### Tech Accurate Rule

The *Gemara* thus arrives at its conclusion. The general rule, outside *beit din* or an explicit *al dateinu* declaration, is that one may rely on a technically accurate interpretation of one's words. What one may never do is give a genuinely absurd interpretation that no one would recognize as a valid meaning of the words. Inside *beit din*, even technical accuracy is foreclosed — the oath is administered on the basis of *da'at beit din*, the standard shared understanding. This is precisely why both Moshe and the institution of *beit din* needed to make their declarations explicit: they were extending beyond the ordinary rule to close off even the technically-accurate loophole.

## Unambiguous Language

### Specific Language Alt

The *Gemara* now entertains a digression. If the concern was that *Bnei Yisrael* might claim they understood "God" to mean their idols, why not simply formulate the oath in language that admits no other possible interpretation? The *Gemara* works through a series of alternatives. Why not have them swear to fulfill *mitzvos*? Because *mitzvos* can mean the commands of any king. Why not *kol mitzvos*? Because that might be interpreted as referring only to *mitzvas tzitzis*, which the *amora* says is equivalent to all the *mitzvos* together. Why not *Torah*? Because *Torah achas* might mean just one instance. Why not *Toros*? Because that would refer to *Toras haMincha*, *Toras haChatas*, *Toras haAsham* — the specific legal sections — but not to the whole. Why not *Toros u-mitzvos* together? Because they might be understood as two specific commandments rather than as a comprehensive phrase. Why not *Torah kula*? Because rejecting *avodah zarah* is itself equivalent to affirming the entire Torah, as the *braita* states: *chamura avodas kochavim she-kol ha-kofer bah ke-ilu modeh ba-Torah kula* — one who denies *avodah zarah* is as if he affirms the entire Torah. Even formulating it as *she-lo ya'avdu avodas kochavim ve-Torah kula* does not fully close the gap. Why not *taryag mitzvos*? Perhaps one might say, I call my dog *taryag mitzvos* — though the *Gemara* concedes that this might well have been unassailable.

### Moshe Practical

The *Gemara* concludes: *ela Moshe milta de-la tricha nakas* — Moshe simply did not want to go through the exercise of constructing a perfectly airtight formulation. He took the straightforward approach and declared: it means what I say it means, end of discussion. There was no point in going through the entire exercise when a simple *al da'ati* declaration accomplished the same thing.

## Snake Beam Example

### Snake Exists?

The *Gemara* now returns to the *nidrei havai* case involving the snake as large as a beam of an olive press. It raises the question: is such a statement truly impossible? Perhaps the claim is not as patently false as assumed.

### Shavor Malka Snake

The *Gemara* provides historical evidence: *de-hahu chivya de-hava be-shnei Shvor Malka* — there was a snake in the time of Shvor Malka to which they threw thirteen stableloads of grain. Snakes of extraordinary size, it seems, were not entirely unknown. So the statement may not be transparently false after all.

### Shmuel: Notched

Shmuel therefore clarifies: *be-taraf* — the case of *nidrei havai* concerns a snake that is *taruf*, notched or spotted along its back. *Kulo nachash mitraf tarfi* — all snakes have notching of some kind. No, the *Gemara* objects — *agavo taruf kamina* — the reference is to a snake notched specifically on its back, and snakes with that particular characteristic do not grow to the size of an olive-press beam. That is the kind of snake one would typically encounter, and swearing one saw such a snake that large is therefore a patently impossible claim.

### Beam Business Law

The *Gemara* then asks: why phrase it in terms of the snake being like a beam of an olive press, rather than simply saying the snake was *taruf*? The answer is: *milta agav urcha kama-shma lan* — the comparison incidentally teaches us a practical rule about the beam itself. *De-koras beit ha-bad gabo taruf* — a beam of an olive press has a notch on its back as part of its standard construction, used to attach the mechanism for pressing. *Le-mai nafka mina? Le-mekach u-mimkar* — this matters for business law. If one sells a beam designated for an olive press and the beam lacks the notch, the buyer may insist on a properly finished beam, since the term *korat beit ha-bad* by definition implies one equipped with that notch. The comparison between snake and beam thus simultaneously defines what kind of snake is meant and conveys a legal teaching about the commercial specifications of the beam.

## Nidrei Shegagot

### False Condition

We now arrive at the third category: *nidrei shegagot* — vows made in error. The first type involves a false factual premise. One says: this bread is forbidden to me if I had breakfast this morning — and at the moment of the *neder*, one genuinely believed one had not eaten. It subsequently emerges that one had in fact eaten. The *halacha* is that the *neder* is not binding. The speaker imposed a condition that he was certain would not be met; the condition was in fact met, but through no genuine intention on his part. Since when he took the *neder* he never wanted it to be binding, it does not take effect.

### Forgot Vow Later

The second type of *nidrei shegagot* involves a future-oriented condition. One says: *konam* this bread to me if I eat anything later today — genuinely intending to commit oneself to a diet. Then, later that day, one absent-mindedly reaches for a snack and eats it, having entirely forgotten the *neder*.

### Forget at Exec

This case is genuinely striking. Unlike the first type, when the *neder* was taken here the speaker was completely serious and fully intended it to be binding. Yet when the triggering act occurred — the snacking — the speaker had no memory of the *neder* at all. And the ruling is: the *neder* does not take effect.

### Piv/Libo at Trigger

The conceptual basis is an extension of *ein piv velibo shavin*. One might object: the act of snacking is not an act of taking a *neder* — it does not require the intention or presence of mind associated with establishing the *neder* in the first place. Why should the speaker's state of mind at the moment of triggering matter? And yet the *Gemara* treats it as decisive. The underlying reasoning appears to be that *nidrei shegagot* are invalidated not only at the moment of taking but also at the moment of triggering, provided that at that moment there was a genuine disconnect — in this case, complete forgetfulness — between what the *neder* required and what the speaker understood himself to be doing. The *neder*, as a creation of human language operating one step removed from direct divine prohibition, requires a minimal continuity of awareness. When that is wholly absent at the moment the condition is triggered, the *neder* fails to become *chal*. The contrast with ordinary violation is important: if one simply forgot the *neder* and ate the forbidden food, that is an *issur be-shogeg* — a violation committed inadvertently. But here, crucially, the *neder* is treated as never having taken effect at all.

### Mistaken Assumption

The second type of *nidrei shegagot* involves a mistake about the people involved. The *mishnah* gives the case of one who saw figures eating figs from his tree and declared: *harei aleichem korban* — let all these figs be *korban* to you. He was furious at what he took to be strangers helping themselves to his property. It then emerged that the group included his own father and brother. The *mishnah* adds another case: one who declared *konam ishti nehenet li* — my wife is forbidden to derive benefit from me — on the grounds that she had stolen his wallet or struck his child, and it later emerged that neither happened. In this case, the speaker did not use the word *im* — "if" — yet the *neder* was clearly premised on a factual assumption. Since the assumption was false, and the *neder* was transparently based on that assumption, it does not take effect. This is distinct from a *petach*: a *petach* is when one failed to consider some consequence of the *neder* that, had one thought about it, would have prevented one from taking it. Here, the *neder* was based on a mistaken belief about a core factual matter — who was present, or what had occurred — and that error goes to the very substance of the *neder* itself.

### Mixed Group Debate

What if the group included both the father and brother and other people as well? *Beit Shammai* say: the father and brother are permitted, but the others remain forbidden. *Beit Hillel* say: once any part of the *neder* is invalidated, the entire *neder* is invalidated — *neder she-hutar miktzato hutar kulo*. The case must be understood carefully: obviously, if one had said "Group A is forbidden and Group B is forbidden" and Group B was one's father and brother, Group A would plainly remain forbidden. The force of *beit Hillel*'s ruling is that when a single undivided statement is made — *harei aleichem* — and part of that statement was based on an error, the *neder* as a whole may fall away.

## Shvuot Shegagot

### Shvuot Rule

The *Gemara* teaches: *kesheim shenidrei shegagot mutarin kach shevuot shegagot mutarot* — just as *nidrei shegagot* are not binding, so too *shvuot shegagot* are not binding. The parallel cases from the *mishnah* would apply straightforwardly: one swore that a loaf would be forbidden if one had eaten breakfast, while mistakenly believing one had not; or one swore and then forgot the *shvua* at the moment of triggering. But the *Gemara* presents a far more interesting case.

### Rav Kahana Story

*Kegon Rav Kahana ve-Rav Assi* — it is like Rav Kahana and Rav Assi, who would sometimes dispute what their teacher Rav had actually said. One would declare: *shevuasa de-hachi amar Rav* — I swear that Rav said this! The other would respond: *shevuasa de-hachi amar Rav* — I swear he said no such thing; he said this other thing! *Ve-chol chad ve-chad ada'ata de-nafshei shapir kamishtava* — each one believed entirely that he was telling the truth.

### Sheker Beshogeig

This is the *chiddush*: if one of them was mistaken, that person took what amounts to a *shvuat sheker be-shogeig* — a false oath taken inadvertently. The *Gemara* teaches that such an oath is not binding and carries no culpability, because the person genuinely believed he was telling the truth. This is *shvuot shegagot*.

### Oaths Extension

This ruling is genuinely surprising. One might have thought: the entire purpose of a *shvua* is to back up one's words with the force of God's name. If those words turn out to be false, it might not matter that the speaker was in *shogeg* — the speaker was, after all, swearing to back up a claim, and that claim was false. The intuition would be that at least with regard to *shvuat sheker*, one ought to have been more careful before invoking God's name, and if one was wrong the *shvua* should still be problematic even in *shogeg*. The *chiddush* of this *Gemara* is that even here, where the *shvua* is functioning precisely as a vehicle for backing up a statement, if the speaker genuinely believed the statement to be true and was in error, the *shvua* is not binding.

The *Ran* focuses the analysis on whether fruit would become forbidden in such a case — i.e., if one said *shvua* that if Rav did not say X then fruit would be forbidden, and one was wrong about what Rav said. Even granting that this involves a mistake about the condition rather than a direct false backing of a statement, the *Ran* treats the question of the food's status as the analogue to the *neder* case. Whether the person committed any *issur* in taking the *shvua* the *Ran* leaves somewhat open. But the simpler reading of the *Gemara* — the case of Rav Kahana and Rav Assi swearing directly about what Rav said — suggests that even the direct function of a *shvua* as a verbal commitment to truth is subject to the *shegagot* principle.

## Petach & Void

### Consequence vs. Fact

A conceptual distinction must be drawn between two different ways in which part of a *neder* can be invalidated. In *nidrei shegagot*, the invalidation flows from a factual error — the speaker did not know that his father was among the group, or did not know that his wife had not actually struck the child. The error concerns what actually happened or who was actually present; it goes to the substance of the *neder* itself. A *petach*, by contrast, involves not a factual error but a failure of foresight: the speaker knew the relevant facts but did not think through the consequences of the *neder*. The classic *petach* is the *shabbat* and *yom tov* case: one who vowed never to drink wine clearly knows that *shabbat* exists and that a year includes *shabbat*; one simply did not stop to consider that the vow would apply there. That failure of attention requires a *petach* to undo; it does not automatically render the *neder* invalid.

### Days Permitted

The earlier teaching — prior to Rabbi Akiva's ruling — held: *otam hayamim mutarim ve-she'ar kol hayamim assurim* — those particular days (like *shabbat* and *yom tov*) are permitted, and all other days remain forbidden. The *petach* got the person out of the *neder* only with respect to the days he had not contemplated; the rest of the *neder* remained in force.

### Hutar Miktzato

Rabbi Akiva then taught: *neder she-hutar miktzato hutar kulo* — a *neder* that has been released in part has been released in its entirety. Once any portion of the *neder* is annulled, the whole *neder* falls away.

### Rava: Qualifier

Rava clarifies the parameters of the debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*. Everyone agrees, he says, in the following case: if the person would have said, had he known his father was there, *kulchem assurim chutz me'abba* — you are all forbidden except for my father — then all agree that only the father is excluded and the rest remain forbidden. Why? Because the speaker himself says he would have maintained the statement *kulchem assurim*; he would simply have added a qualifier. Since the core statement was not in error, and the whole group would still have been included with just that one exception noted, the rest of the *neder* stands. The *machloket* between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* arises only when the person says: had I known my father was there, I would have said *Ploni va-Ploni assurim ve-abba mutar* — I would have named the others and excluded my father — meaning he would have formulated an entirely different statement. In that case, according to *Beit Hillel*, since the speaker would never have uttered the words *kulchem assurim* at all, the entire *neder* is void. *Beit Shammai* apparently maintains that even here the rest of the *neder* stands.

### Different Language

On Rava's first framing, then: where the speaker would have added a qualifier to the same basic language, everyone agrees the rest holds. Where the speaker would have used completely different language — never starting with *kulchem assurim* at all — that is where *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* disagree, with *Beit Hillel* voiding the entire *neder* by *hutar miktzato hutar kulo*.

### Rava Second

Rava then presents an alternative framing of where the *machloket* lies. On this second approach, the case of completely different language — where the speaker says he would have used an entirely different formulation — is so strong that even *Beit Shammai* would agree the entire *neder* is void. The *machloket* is then relocated to the qualifier case: where the speaker says he would have maintained *kulchem assurim* but added *chutz me'abba*, *Beit Hillel* still voids the entire *neder* by *hutar miktzato hutar kulo*, but *Beit Shammai* holds that the rest remains binding.

### Beit Shammai

We close at the question of *Beit Shammai*'s position in the qualifier case. What precisely is *Beit Shammai*'s reasoning when the speaker says he would have kept the core language and merely added an exception? This analysis will be taken up in the next session.
