# Ketubot 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nursing Obligation

### Intro: Nursing & Vows

The daf begins approximately ten lines from the bottom of 59b, with the Mishnah's discussion of *umeneket beno* — a wife's obligation to nurse her husband's child. Among the various domestic tasks incumbent upon a wife, nursing is enumerated as one of her obligations to her husband. The Gemara immediately raises a challenge: is our Mishnah consistent with the position of Beit Shammai?

A Baraita is cited in which a woman takes a vow — *nadra shelo lehanik* — that prevents her from nursing the child. The Gemara clarifies what type of vow this could be. A vow functions by rendering something forbidden to a person. In this context, she might make the benefit of her nursing forbidden to her husband, who benefits indirectly because his child is being nursed. Alternatively, she might make the act itself forbidden to herself, given that the Gemara acknowledges that a woman derives a measure of pleasure from the act of nursing. Either way, the vow is structured so as to prohibit the nursing.

### Vow Mechanics

The question of what precisely is being forbidden by the vow — the benefit to the husband or the act itself — bears on the vow's validity and its interaction with her marital obligations. The Gemara's discussion implicitly recalls the earlier treatment of *maaseh yadayim*: just as a wife cannot render her labor forbidden to her husband because he holds a prior claim on it, so too there is a question of whether she can render her nursing forbidden when it is itself a marital obligation.

### Shammai vs Hillel

Beit Shammai rules: *shometet dad mi-pi* — she removes the breast from the child's mouth. Even if she is in the midst of nursing, she stops. The vow is binding and she has no legal compulsion to continue. Beit Hillel, by contrast, rules: *kofo umeneket oto* — her husband can compel her to nurse the child. The implication of Beit Hillel's position is that the vow was never binding in the first place, since she is not in a position to render forbidden a service that her husband already has a prior claim upon. Beit Shammai, it follows, holds that she bears no strict legal obligation to nurse her child, allowing the vow to take full effect.

### Divorce & Nursing

Once she is divorced, *einah kofah* — he can no longer compel her, since her obligation to him has ended. At that point, presumably, the vow takes effect. This touches on the earlier discussion about a vow taking effect at a postponed moment in time.

However, if the child already *recognized* the mother — and Rashi adds, though it is not explicit in the Gemara, that this means the child will not nurse from any other woman — then even in the post-divorce situation, the husband pays her a nursemaid's fee and can compel his ex-wife to nurse the child: *notin lah secharah ve-kofo umeneket oto*. The Gemara underscores the clinical importance of this: it is understood as a genuine *sakanah* — a danger to the child's life — if a child who will only nurse from his mother is denied her.

This raises a penetrating question: if the vow is binding, is the woman now violating it when compelled to nurse? One possibility is that this involves *pikuach nefesh*, overriding the vow entirely. Yet if so, one would expect the Gemara to discuss annulling the vow through a *heter neder* rather than simply compelling her. A more coherent reading may be that in any scenario where the husband retains the legal authority to compel nursing — even with payment — the vow never achieved binding status to begin with, since it could never take hold in a domain subject to that compulsion. The Gemara does not develop this explicitly, but it is the most internally consistent reading.

### Blame & Payment

The Gemara then entertains a reinterpretation of the entire Beit Shammai–Beit Hillel dispute. Perhaps neither side is debating whether the vow is binding or whether she can be compelled to nurse. Perhaps both agree the vow is binding, and the real dispute is about *who is to blame* — and therefore who bears the financial consequences. This reading is associated with Tosafot.

### Sakana Override

The Gemara notes explicitly: *mipnei hasakanah* — even where a vow might otherwise apply, the danger to the child represents an overriding concern. The recognition that a nursing mother's milk, and especially the act of nursing from one's own mother, constitutes a life-sustaining need for the infant is treated as a foundational *halakhic* principle.

### Affirmed Vow

The Gemara then frames a specific scenario: *shenadra hi vekiyam lah hu* — she made the vow and he not merely failed to annul it but actively *affirmed* it. Once a husband affirms the vow, he can no longer annul it even on the same day. Now the question becomes: who is culpable for the resulting situation?

Beit Shammai holds: *hu nosein etzba bein shineha* — he put his finger between her teeth. He is to blame; he is the one who affirmed the vow. Beit Hillel holds: *hi nosna etzba bein shineha* — she put her finger between her teeth. She made the vow; she bears responsibility.

On Tosafot's reading, what the two schools are debating is not whether she can be compelled to nurse but rather who must bear the financial cost of hiring a nursemaid. If he is to blame, she is exempt. If she is to blame, she must cover the expense. The Gemara, however, objects: if the debate were simply about blame and financial responsibility in cases where a wife's vow frustrates her *ketuva* obligations, the Tannaim could have framed the dispute in general terms about *ketuva* obligations — with the *nafka mina* being whether he must pay her *ketuva* upon divorce. Why single out the nursing case? Moreover, another Baraita states explicitly: *Beit Shammai omrim einah menikah* — Beit Shammai says she does not have to nurse. This makes clear that the dispute is indeed about the existence of the obligation itself. The Gemara concludes: *ela mehavarta kesimin d'lo ke-Beit Shammai* — the Mishnah simply does not follow Beit Shammai.

## Child Recognition

### Recognition Age

The Baraita we examined establishes that if the infant already *makkira* — recognizes — his mother, and according to Rashi will refuse to nurse from another woman, the ex-husband can compel even a divorced wife to nurse for pay. The Gemara now asks: from what age do we assume that a child will recognize the mother in this operative sense?

### Age Dispute

Three Amoraic opinions are given. Rava citing Rav Yirmiya bar Abba citing Rav: *shlosha chodashim* — three months. Shmuel: *shloshim yom* — thirty days. Rav Yitzchak citing Rabbi Yochanan: *chamishim yom* — fifty days. Rav Shimi bar Chiya then rules: *halacha* follows the last-cited position of fifty days, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan.

The Gemara asks: the debate between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan — three months versus fifty days — is understandable, as it may reflect that different children develop recognition at different rates, each authority ruling according to the earlier or later range of typical cases. But Shmuel says the child recognizes the mother at thirty days — is such a thing possible at all?

The Gemara leaves this without definitive resolution. The practical question of what precisely "recognition" means is relevant here. Does it mean merely some inchoate awareness of the mother's presence, or specifically that the child refuses to nurse from anyone else — as Rashi's addition suggests? If it is the latter, testing would be more straightforward: simply offer the child another nursemaid and observe whether he accepts her.

### Case-by-Case

When Rav Dimi bar Yechezkel returned from Eretz Yisrael, he corrected the transmission. He said: do not follow these fixed rules that Yehuda my brother reported in the name of Shmuel. What Shmuel actually said is *kol zman shemakkira* — the determination is made case by case. Rather than applying a bright-line rule of thirty, fifty, or ninety days, one must examine the specific child to determine whether he recognizes his mother or not.

### Lineup Test

The Gemara then brings a story. A woman came before Shmuel: she had been divorced, and her husband sought to compel her to continue nursing. Shmuel instructed Rav Dimi bar Yosef: go and investigate whether the child recognizes her. Rav Dimi bar Yosef conducted a lineup — *dara d'neshei* — a row of women, and had the infant carried past them. When the child passed before his mother, *havat kemashfi le'apa* — he lit up, recognizing her face. She, seeking to avoid the obligation, averted her eyes — *kavashtanhu le'einei mineih*. Rav Dimi bar Yosef said to her: *netof einach kum dari brach* — lift your eyes, go carry your son. You must nurse him; he clearly recognizes you.

This story is notable precisely because the test was recognition, not refusal to nurse from another woman. The far simpler test — offering the child to a nursemaid and observing whether he nurses — was not employed. This suggests that the Gemara's operative criterion is recognition itself, and Rashi's addition about refusing to nurse from others may be his explanatory gloss rather than the Gemara's own requirement.

### Blind Child

One might ask: how would a blind child — *suma* — recognize his mother, given that the test used by the Gemara was visual? Rav Ashi answers: *be-reicha uve-ta'ama* — through smell and taste. The other senses are sufficient to enable identification even where sight is unavailable.

## Nursing Duration

### 24 Month Limit

The Gemara now transitions from the question of a wife's nursing obligation to a broader treatment of nursing in general, citing a Baraita: *yonek tinok ve-holech ad esrim ve-arba'ah chodesh* — a child may continue nursing until twenty-four months. Beyond that point, says the first opinion — that of Rabbi Eliezer — nursing is *ke-yonek sheket*, like nursing from something abominable.

The precise character of the prohibition is worth examining. The phrase *yonek sheket* suggests that the objection is not to the substance of human milk but to the *act* of nursing beyond a certain age. A nursing child beyond twenty-four months is described as nursing from something disgusting — the focus is on the act rather than the product. The *nafka mina* is significant: if a mother expresses her milk into a container and gives it to a child, would Rabbi Eliezer's objection apply? If the concern is the act of nursing, expressed milk would be unaffected; if the concern is the substance at that developmental stage, it would be equally problematic regardless of form of delivery.

### 4-5 Years Option

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees sharply: *afilu arba va-chamisha shanim* — even four or five years old, nursing remains permissible. The Gemara considers whether Rabbi Yehoshua means literally up to five years as an upper bound, or whether he means there is no practical limit whatsoever. The Gemara's treatment suggests he does intend some upper boundary around five years.

Rabbi Yehoshua does, however, concede one point: *peiresh le-achar esrim ve-arba'ah chodesh ve-chazar* — if after the twenty-four-month mark the child ceased nursing for a period and then returned to it, then even Rabbi Yehoshua would agree this is *ke-yonek sheket*. Once the child has transitioned out of the nursing stage and entered the normal world of eating solid food, a return to nursing is considered inappropriate even according to the lenient view. How long a cessation constitutes *peiresh*? Rav Yehuda bar Chaviva cites Shmuel: three days of uninterrupted abstention from nursing suffices.

### Act vs Product

As noted, the central distinction being drawn throughout this passage is between the *act* of nursing and the *product* — expressed milk. The discussion of *yonek sheket* appears to target the act. This distinction will be reinforced momentarily by the analogy to blood.

### Kashrut Proof

The Gemara now marshals a proof from *kashrut* reasoning. A *kal va-chomer* might suggest that human milk should be non-kosher: if the milk of a non-kosher animal is forbidden even though that animal does not convey *tumah* through touch while alive, then surely human milk should be forbidden, since humans *do* convey *tumah* when alive. The Torah, however, teaches from the verse concerning the camel — *et ha-gamal* — that the milk of non-kosher animals is forbidden, but through the same derivation, the verse specifies only non-kosher animals: *hu tamei* — *it* is *tamei*, implying that human milk, by contrast, *ve-ein chalav meha-mehalech al shtayim tamei ela tahor* — is not non-kosher but is in fact permitted.

Similarly, one might think that human blood is forbidden: all animal blood is consistently forbidden, so human blood should be forbidden as well. The same verse teaches otherwise: the prohibition applies specifically to animal blood, not to human blood — *ve-ein dam meha-mehalech al shtayim tamei ela tahor*. Rashi and Tosafot work out the precise derivation from the verse, but the operative conclusion is clear: both blood and milk of human beings are not Biblically non-kosher. Rav Sheshet adds: *afilu mitzvat perishah ein bo* — there is not even a rabbinic prohibition against consuming human milk. It is entirely permitted.

### Blood Analogy

This raises a sharp question: if human milk is entirely permitted, how can the Gemara speak of *yonek sheket* — as though nursing beyond a certain age involves something disgusting? The Gemara answers: *lo kashya — ha de-parish, ha de-lo parish* — there is no difficulty. The cases are categorically different. When the milk has been separated from the mother — expressed into a cup — there is no halachic concern and no element of impropriety. When the child nurses directly from the mother, the *act* of nursing is what is considered inappropriate beyond a certain developmental stage.

Fascinatingly, the exact *converse* applies to blood. *Ve-chilufa be-dam* — the reverse principle governs blood. With blood, the concern runs in the opposite direction: if blood has been separated from the body and collected in a vessel, one may not drink it — not because human blood is itself non-kosher, but because an observer would assume the person is drinking animal blood and the appearance is impermissible. However, if a person's gums are bleeding and the blood remains *bein ha-shinayim* — between the teeth — or if one receives a cut and sucks the wound, *motzetzah ve-eino choshesh* — one may suck it out and need not be concerned. Here, an observer clearly understands that the person is ingesting blood from their own body, and there is no appearance of impropriety.

The contrast is structurally elegant: expressed human milk is unproblematic; direct nursing beyond a certain age is considered inappropriate. Expressed human blood presents an appearance concern; blood sucked directly from one's own wound is entirely permitted.

A practical question naturally arises in light of this analysis: human milk expressed and prepared as food — even as cheese — would be not only *kosher* but not even *milchig*. Tosafot also cites a *Torat Kohanim* to the effect that human flesh is likewise not Biblically forbidden, though there are significant discussions among *poskim* about whether it would be prohibited on other grounds, such as *bal tishaktzu*. The term *mehalechei shtayim* — two-legged beings — is used as a euphemism in these sources rather than specifying *adam* directly, possibly to avoid the discomfort of explicit discussion.

### Halakha Ruling

Rav Yosef rules: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yehoshua* — the law follows Rabbi Yehoshua, permitting nursing up to approximately four to five years. This is the operative *halakha*.

## Shabbat Suckling

### Sick Suckling

Rabbi Merinus taught: *gonei'ach yonek chalav be-Shabbos* — a person who is ill may directly suckle milk from an animal's udder on *Shabbat*.

### Shinui Leniency

The reason: *yonek mefarek ke-achar yad* — the act of suckling from the udder involves the forbidden labor of *mefarek*, a subdivision of *dash* — threshing — which means separating a substance from the medium in which it is bound. Normally one milks a cow with the hands; to suckle directly with the mouth is therefore a *shinui*, an unusual manner of performing the action, and constitutes *mefarek ke-lachar yad*. The Gemara adds: *u-vimkom tzara lo gazru rabbanan* — where there is genuine distress or pain, the rabbis did not extend their decree to prohibit even this modified form of the labor. The leniency is thus grounded in two factors: the *shinui* reduces the violation, and the *tzaar* of illness creates a threshold sufficient to permit a single Rabbinic-level prohibition.

It is worth noting that this Gemara does not treat direct suckling from an animal as *yonek sheket*. An ill adult suckling from a cow's udder is a *Shabbat* question, not one of propriety. This further supports the inference that the impropriety of *yonek sheket* in the nursing context is specifically tied to something about the mother-child relationship beyond a certain developmental stage — perhaps related to the intimate character of nursing from a human being rather than the act of suckling per se.

### Mother Expressing

This Gemara has critical implications for the question of a nursing mother expressing milk on *Shabbat*. Two preliminary issues arise. First, Tosafot notes that *dash* — from which the prohibition of milking derives — technically applies only to *gidulei karka*, things that grow from the ground. Human beings do not grow from the ground. Accordingly, Tosafot raises the possibility that expressing human milk may not be Biblically prohibited at all, but only Rabbinic — a position that, if accepted, significantly shifts the *Shabbat* analysis.

Second, even setting aside that question, one might ask whether expressing milk by hand constitutes a *shinui* for a human mother. By a cow, the *normal* method of extraction from the human perspective is hand-milking; suckling directly is the deviation. For a human mother, the natural mode of the milk's release is through the infant nursing; expressing by hand might itself be considered the deviation, and therefore a *shinui* already. Or one might argue that either method is equally natural for a human mother, making neither a *shinui*. This is not easily resolved.

Beyond the question of *shinui*, the *sakana* argument may apply: if failure to express milk threatens the mother's milk supply in a way that endangers the infant's nutrition, that *sakana* framework — which we have already seen the Gemara invoke prominently — would provide independent grounds for permitting expression. As a practical matter, an ideal solution is to attach an electric pump on a timer before *Shabbat* so that the device activates automatically, with the mother already connected; she then plays no active role in the expression itself.

### Drain Pipe Case

A parallel case is brought from Nachum Ish Galia: if a drain pipe on a roof has filled with debris and rainwater is backing up into the house, one may use one's foot to compress and push through the blockage on *Shabbat* — *memachan be-raglo be-tzina be-Shabbos*. The reason is the same structure: using one's foot rather than one's hand is a *shinui*, and *u-vimkom psida lo gazru rabbanan* — in a case of financial loss, the rabbis did not extend their decree. Rav Yosef rules: *halacha ke-Nachum Ish Galia zu* — we rule in accordance with Nachum Ish Galia in this matter.

Both of these rulings — the *gonei'ach* case and the drain pipe case — permit overriding a single Rabbinic prohibition on the basis of *tzaar* or *psida* combined with a *shinui*. This sits in some tension with other *sugyot* that appear to require a double Rabbinic qualifier before leniency applies, and the precise threshold of need that justifies which level of override remains a matter of ongoing discussion across the relevant *sugyot*.

## Nursing Post-Death

### Forbidden Betrothal

The Gemara presents a new Baraita: *meneket she-met ba'alah be-tokh esrim ve-arba'ah chodesh* — a nursing mother whose husband dies within twenty-four months. Since the obligation to nurse is framed as a marital duty to the husband, one might think that with his death her obligation dissolves. Why, then, can she not simply hand the child to the heirs, declare herself free of responsibility, and move on?

The Baraita's answer is that she may not: *harei zo lo t'ares ve-lo tinasei* — she may neither accept *kiddushin* nor enter *nissuin* for the duration of the twenty-four months. This reflects the concern that if she enters a new relationship, becomes pregnant, that pregnancy will compromise her milk supply and thereby harm the nursing child. Notably, the *Talmud* elsewhere acknowledges that active nursing itself typically functions as a natural contraceptive — it is generally understood that nursing suppresses fertility. Nevertheless, the *halakhic* framework addresses the reverse chain: not that nursing prevents pregnancy, but that a new pregnancy threatens nursing.

### Milk Supply Concern

The operative concern is the integrity of the milk supply. Even if actual conception is unlikely during active nursing, entering *kiddushin* sets in motion a path that could eventually lead to pregnancy. The concern is precautionary: once betrothal is permitted, *nissuin* follows, and with *nissuin* pregnancy becomes a realistic risk.

### Duration Dispute

Rabbi Meir holds that this restriction extends for the full twenty-four months. Rabbi Yehuda permits it after eighteen months, holding that the child's nursing needs diminish sufficiently by that point that the risk to the child no longer justifies continued restriction on the mother's freedom to remarry.

Rabbi Natan bar Yosef notes that this is effectively the same debate as that between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel: Beit Shammai holds twenty-four months and Beit Hillel holds eighteen months. This is somewhat surprising given that Beit Shammai earlier maintained that a mother has no enforceable obligation to her husband to nurse at all — yet here Beit Shammai holds to the longer restriction vis-à-vis the *child*. The reconciliation is that even Beit Shammai acknowledges an obligation where the infant recognizes the mother; in such circumstances, the twenty-four-month commitment to the child exists even without a living husband.

### Reconciliation

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel offers a clarification — not so much a compromise between the positions as a reframing of each one. According to the view of twenty-four months, *muteret le-innasei be-esrim ve-echad chodesh* — she may actually get married at twenty-one months, because the first three months of a new pregnancy do not yet compromise the milk supply. *Lefi she-ein ha-chalav ne'echar ella achar shlosha chadashim* — the milk is not affected until after three months of pregnancy. So "twenty-four months" effectively means the mother may not get married more than three months before the twenty-four-month mark. Similarly, according to the view of eighteen months, she may get married at fifteen months, because again the first trimester does not impact nursing. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is not adjudicating between the two positions but unpacking what each actually requires in practical terms.

### Amoraim Rulings

Ulla rules: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — the law follows Rabbi Yehuda's eighteen-month position, though it is not entirely clear whether this means eighteen months as stated or fifteen months as reframed by Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Mar Ukva reports that Rabbi Chanina permitted him personally to marry a woman whose husband had died, once fifteen months had elapsed since the birth of the nursing child — confirming that the operative ruling is fifteen months.

### Sharecropper Case

Abaye's sharecropper came before Abaye with a case: his prospective wife was a nursing mother whose husband had died fifteen months earlier — may he now proceed with *kiddushin*? Abaye responded with confidence: the matter is completely clear. We rule like Rabbi Yehuda — eighteen months meaning fifteen. Beit Hillel, with whom we rule, also holds eighteen months meaning fifteen. Ulla ruled accordingly. And Rabbi Chanina permitted Mar Ukva's own case at fifteen months. With four independent convergent indications, and since this is *kiddushin* and not yet *nissuin*, the answer is obviously yes.

When Abaye reported this exchange to Rav Yosef, however, Rav Yosef corrected him sharply. Rav and Shmuel had both ruled: *tzricha le-hamtin arba'ah ve-esrim chodesh* — she must wait the full twenty-four months. Furthermore, the count does not even begin on the day of the birth — the clock starts the following day — and the day of the *kiddushin* itself does not count as the last day of the period. This is the most stringent possible reading, and it represents the ruling of Rav and Shmuel.

Abaye immediately ran after the sharecropper to stop the wedding — *ratz basei tlasa parsei*, three *parsangs*, and some say one *parsang* through sand — but could not catch him in time.

### Rebbe Authority

Abaye's own conclusion from this episode is striking: *ha milsa de-amru rabbanan afilu ke-beitza be-kutcha* — even a question as obvious as whether one may eat an egg with a dairy dip — *lo lishri inish be-makom rabei* — one should not issue a ruling in the place of one's *rebbe*. The reason is not merely one of respect or deference, which would be waived if the *rebbe* grants permission. Rather, *mishum de-lo mistayeim milta* — such a ruling will not succeed, will not be confirmed by heavenly assistance. A student who presumes to rule in his *rebbe*'s domain, even on an ostensibly clear matter, loses the *siyatta di-shemaya* that would otherwise guide him to the correct conclusion. In Abaye's own case: *deha ana hava gmirna leih la-hade de-Rav u-Shmuel* — I actually knew the ruling of Rav and Shmuel requiring twenty-four months. Yet even so, it slipped my mind at the critical moment. The impetuousness of confident self-sufficiency, even when the *rebbe* is present and available, is precisely what the episode illustrates.

## 24-Month Exceptions

### Exceptions Listed

A Baraita states: *natna bnah le-menekes* — if the mother gave the child over to a nursemaid, *o gemalto* — or weaned him, *o met* — or the child died, *mutteret le-hinnase miyad* — she may remarry immediately, within the twenty-four months. These are the three exceptions that remove the basis for the restriction: in each case, the infant is either being nursed by another or no longer nursing at all.

### Attempted Ruling

Rav Pappa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua wished to rule in practice according to this Baraita. An elderly woman corrected them: this very case happened to me, and Rav Nachman forbade it even though I had satisfied one of these criteria. Rav Nachman did not permit me to remarry before the twenty-four months.

### Resh Galuta Exception

The Gemara asks: but did not Rav Nachman himself permit the women of the house of the *Resh Galuta* to remarry earlier, once they had weaned their children or made other arrangements? The Gemara answers: *shani bei Resh Galuta de-lo hadru behu* — the household of the *Resh Galuta* is different, because those women will not reverse themselves. Once a decision is made in the *Resh Galuta*'s household — whether to give the child to a nursemaid or to wean — the new husband and the authority of that household ensure the decision will not be revisited. The fear that motivates the general restriction is that a woman who has weaned her child or arranged a nursemaid may have a change of heart, resume nursing, and then, having conceived with a new husband, find that the pregnancy compromises her milk. In the *Resh Galuta*'s household, that reversal is simply not going to happen. Ordinary women are subject to the more stringent standard.

### Paternity Analogy

Rav Pappi raises a further challenge. Rather than relying on the elderly woman's testimony, the ruling against the Baraita's leniency should have been derivable from an independent source. This leads to a discussion of the three-month waiting period before remarriage — a completely separate institution designed to prevent uncertainty about the paternity of a future child.

### 3-Month Cases

A Baraita lists women who must wait three months before remarrying even when there is no realistic concern that they are pregnant: a woman who is *redufah le-lech le-beit aviha* — always running to her father's house and presumably not having relations with her husband; a woman with severe marital discord in the home; a woman whose husband was imprisoned; a woman whose husband went overseas; a woman whose husband was elderly or infirm; and women who are clearly incapable of conception — sterile, elderly, *aylonit*, minor, or a woman who miscarried after her husband's death. In all these cases Rabbi Meir rules they must nonetheless wait three months.

### 3-Month Halakha

Rabbi Yosi permits immediate remarriage when there is no realistic concern of pregnancy. The ruling: *halacha ke-Rabbi Meir bi-gzeirosuav* — we rule like Rabbi Meir in his categorical decree. We do not make case-by-case exceptions even when the underlying concern is clearly inapplicable.

### Consistent 24-Month

The Gemara draws a structural parallel: just as we apply Rabbi Meir's categorical approach to the three-month waiting period and refuse to grant exceptions even where we know there is no pregnancy, the same categorical logic should govern the twenty-four-month restriction. Even if the child has been weaned or given to a nursemaid, we refuse to grant an exception. The *halakha* that emerges: *ve-hilkheta ve-halakha hi met* — the one case where we do permit remarriage within twenty-four months is where the child has died. *Gemaltathu* — if she weaned him — *asur*, she may not yet remarry, because we fear she may change her mind and resume nursing. All other exceptions fail for the same reason. Only the death of the child removes the concern entirely.

### Infanticide Fear

Mar bar Rav Ashi goes further: *afilu met nami asur* — even if the child has died, she may not remarry within twenty-four months. The fear: *dilma katla lei* — we are afraid that a woman desperately eager to remarry might kill her infant child in order to clear the way. An actual case is cited in which a woman strangled her child — *hahi uvda ve-chanaktia* — apparently driven by the desire to be free to remarry.

### Rejection

The Gemara rejects this stringency: *ve-lo hi* — that is not the correct ruling. *Hahi shota havat* — that woman was mentally ill. *Ve-lo avdi inshi de-chanki be-naihu* — normal women do not kill their own children. The extreme case cannot generate a categorical rule. The operative *halakha* therefore stands: the one recognized exception to the twenty-four-month restriction is the death of the child. All other putative exceptions — nursemaid, weaning — do not permit early remarriage.

A related question concerns the three-month waiting period in the context of conversion: if a woman converts together with her husband, must they wait three months to continue living together, in order to clarify whether any child conceived around that time was conceived before or after her conversion? Some *poskim* have permitted pregnancy tests to resolve this question, arguing that modern diagnostic tools create a factual certainty that moves the case outside the framework of the Gemara's categorical decree — though others maintain that the *lo plug* logic of Rabbi Meir's position admits no exceptions regardless of what can be empirically verified today.
