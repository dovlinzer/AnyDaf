# Bava Kamma 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Currency Exchange

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹתֵן לוֹ מַטְבֵּעַ הַיּוֹצֵא בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ, לֵךְ הוֹצִיאוֹ בְּמֵישָׁן. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִילְּתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל – דְּאִית לֵיהּ אוֹרְחָא לְמֵיזַל לְמֵישָׁן, אֲבָל לֵית לֵיהּ אוֹרְחָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The debtor **gives him a coin that is in circulation at that time** with which he repays the loan. **And Shmuel says** that the debtor **can say to** the creditor: I am giving you a coin like the one you gave me, although you cannot utilize it here. **Go spend it in Meishan,** i.e., a distant place where this coin is still in circulation. **Rav Naḥman said: Shmuel's statement is reasonable when** the creditor **has a way to go to Meishan,** i.e., he intends to travel there, and therefore the debtor can tell him to spend the coin when he arrives at his destination. **But** if **he has no way** to go to Meishan, i.e., he does not intend to go there, the debtor may **not** give him these coins. Rather, he must give him coins that are in circulation in their current location.

We pick up at the top of 98b, in the midst of a *sugya* concerning a case where somebody borrowed money — or sold something, or entered into a business arrangement — at a time when a certain currency was in circulation, and when the time comes to repay, that currency is no longer accepted in this locale. Can the debtor still discharge his obligation by paying with the old currency?

*Rav* holds that he must pay back with the new currency. *Shmuel*, by contrast, maintains that payment with the old currency is still valid, since that currency remains in use elsewhere — go to *Meshan*, he says, and you can use it there. Since the currency has not been rendered entirely *nifsal malchus* — completely invalidated everywhere — it retains legitimacy, and the original terms of the transaction can be honored with it.

### Rav Nachman's Limit

*Rav Nachman*, however, found *Shmuel*'s position somewhat radical and limited it considerably. It is only valid, he said, if the person receiving payment is actually headed to the place where that currency is still in use. If he is traveling to *Meshan*, then paying him back in that currency is acceptable. Otherwise, it is not.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: אֵין מְחַלְּלִין עַל הַמָּעוֹת שֶׁאֵינָם יוֹצְאוֹת. כֵּיצַד? הָיוּ לוֹ מָעוֹת כּוֹזְבִיּוֹת יְרוּשַׁלְמִיּוֹת, אוֹ שֶׁל מְלָכִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים – אֵין מְחַלְּלִין. הָא שֶׁל אַחֲרוֹנִים דּוּמְיָא דְרִאשׁוֹנִים – מְחַלְּלִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to Rav Naḥman** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ma'aser Sheni* 1:6): **One cannot desacralize** second-tithe produce by transferring its sanctity **onto coins that are not in circulation. How so? If one had coins of bar Kokheva [*Kozeviyyot*],** coins **of Jerusalem, or** coins **of earlier kings,** all of which are no longer in use, one **cannot desacralize** second**-**tithe produce by transferring its sanctity onto them. Rava infers: **But** if one had coins **of later,** i.e., current, kings, that are **similar to** the coins of the **earlier** kings in that they have been invalidated in his location, since they are still valid elsewhere, one **can desacralize** the second-tithe produce by transferring its sanctity onto them. The *baraita* does not make a distinction as to whether the owner of the produce intends to travel to the location where those coins are in use or not.

*Rava* then challenged *Rav Nachman*'s formulation by citing a ruling concerning *maaser sheni*. The *Torah* permits one who is too far away to transfer the sanctity of *maaser sheni* fruit onto money and bring the money to *Yerushalayim* — but only onto money that is currently in circulation. As the text states: *ein mechalelin al ma'os she'einam yotzos* — you cannot transfer the sanctity of *maaser sheni* onto money that is not current.

### Bar Kochba Coins

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a *baraita*: *hayu lo ma'os Kuzbios Yerushalmios* — if one has coins from *Kuzbios*. *Rashi* explains that *Kuzbios* refers to *Ben Kuzbi*, that is, *Bar Kochva*. Indeed, *Bar Kochva* minted his own coins, examples of which can still be seen in museums in *Eretz Yisrael*. The *baraita* continues: *o shel melachim harishonim* — or coins of earlier kings. Such currency, no longer in circulation, cannot serve as the vehicle for *chilul maaser sheni*.

### Current vs. Past Kings

This seems straightforward enough, but the *Gemara* raises a subtle implication. The phrase *shel achronim dumya de-rishonim* — of later, i.e., currently ruling kings, but similar to the earlier ones — suggests that if the currency of a presently reigning government happens not to be in circulation in one's location, it would nevertheless be valid for *chilul maaser sheni*. The inference is that currency from an existing government, even if not locally current, retains legitimacy. This appears to support *Shmuel*'s view.

### Makpido Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּשֶׁאֵין מַלְכִיּוֹת מַקְפִּידוֹת זוֹ עַל זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: With what are we dealing here** in the *baraita*? We are dealing with a case **where** the **kingdoms are not particular with one another.** They allow a coin that has been invalidated in their province to be taken out of their borders and spent in a province where it is valid. Since these coins can be given to those planning to travel to the location where it is valid, it is considered to be a valid coin even in the location of the one desacralizing the second-tithe produce.

*Rav Nachman* responds: *hacha be-mai askinan kshe-ein malchuyos makpidos zo al zo* — the permissive case concerns kingdoms that are not *makpid* on one another, meaning that the currency of one circulates or is easily transferable in the other's territory. In such a case, the currency is considered usable and valid even where it is not the primary tender. The lecturer notes some uncertainty about the precise practical meaning of *eino makpidos*: does it mean the currency is actually accepted here, or merely that it is easy enough to exchange or transport across the border? Either way, that is the answer for why the currency would work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל – כְּשֶׁמַּלְכִיּוֹת מַקְפִּידוֹת זוֹ עַל זוֹ. הֵיכִי מָצֵי מַמְטֵי לְהוּ? דְּמַמְטֵי לְה[וּ] עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק; דְּלָא בָּחֲשִׁי, וְאִי מַשְׁכְּחִי קָפְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But,** according to this explanation, **when Shmuel said** that the debtor can repay his debt with invalidated coins only if the creditor intends to travel to a location where they are valid, he is speaking of a case **where** the **kingdoms are particular with each other.** If so, **how can he bring** these coins to Meishan without the authorities confiscating them? The Gemara responds: Shmuel is discussing a case **where** the creditor can **bring them with difficulty,** as in a case **where** the authorities **do not search, but if they find** any invalid coins **they are particular** to confiscate them. It is therefore possible to bring these coins into the other kingdom. Nevertheless, since the authorities are particular, one who does not plan to travel there will not be able to exchange these coins with anyone in his location.

By contrast, when *Shmuel* says one can repay with the old currency — under *Rav Nachman*'s limiting reading — this must refer to a case where the governments *are* mutually *makpid*. The *Gemara* then asks: if they are mutually *makpid*, how can one even bring the currency to the other locale, even if traveling there? The *Gemara* answers: *demati lehu al yedei dachak* — one can get it there with some difficulty. *Delo pacheshi* — they do not search your bags at the border. *Ve-im meshkachi kapdei* — but if they find it, they will confiscate it. So practically, if one acts discreetly, the currency can be transported. That is the scenario where *Shmuel* permits repayment when the creditor is heading to that locale.

## Currency Exchange…

### Maaser Sheni Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין מְחַלְּלִין עַל מָעוֹת שֶׁל כָּאן וְהֵן בְּבָבֶל, וְשֶׁל בָּבֶל וְהֵן כָּאן. שֶׁל בָּבֶל וְהֵן בְּבָבֶל – מְחַלְּלִין. קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: אֵין מְחַלְּלִין עַל מָעוֹת שֶׁל כָּאן וְהֵן בְּבָבֶל – אַף עַל גַּב דְּסוֹפוֹ לְמִיסַּק לְהָתָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Shmuel's opinion: **Come** and **hear** what was taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ma'aser Sheni* 1:6): **One cannot desacralize** second-tithe produce by transferring its sanctity **onto money of** Eretz Yisrael, i.e., **here,** when **they,** the coins and their owner, **are in Babylonia. And** one cannot do so onto money **of Babylonia when they,** the coins and their owner, **are here** in Eretz Yisrael. If one uses money **of Babylonia and they are in Babylonia,** one **can desacralize** the second-tithe produce. **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches:** One **cannot desacralize** second-tithe produce by transferring its sanctity **onto money of** Eretz Yisrael, **here,** when **they are in Babylonia.** And this is stated **even though they will ultimately ascend to there,** Eretz Yisrael, as the very purpose of transferring the sanctity to the coins is to spend them in Jerusalem.

The *Gemara* then introduces a further *baraita* in an attempt to resolve the matter. *Ein mechalelin al ma'os shekan vehen be'Bavel* — you cannot transfer *maaser sheni* to *Eretz Yisrael* money if you and that money are located in *Bavel*. This is already an interesting ruling, as we generally assume *maaser sheni* applies only within *Eretz Yisrael* and its immediate environs — yet certain areas including *Bavel* were subject to the *takkanah* of *terumot u'maasrot*.

So: you are in *Bavel*, the money in question is *Eretz Yisrael* currency, and you cannot perform *chilul maaser sheni* onto it. The *Gemara* points out: *afgav desofei lemisik lehatam* — even though you are heading to *Eretz Yisrael* and intend to bring this money there. This seems like a decisive refutation of *Shmuel*: even if one is en route to the very place where the currency is current, it is still not considered valid money in the here and now.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּשֶׁמַּלְכִיּוֹת מַקְפִּידוֹת זוֹ עַל זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers, again, that this *baraita* speaks of a case where the kingdoms are mutually *makpid* — **with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the **kingdoms are** especially **particular with one another,** and it is impossible to transport coins from one location to the other.

The *Gemara* responds, again, that this *baraita* speaks of a case where the kingdoms are mutually *makpid* — *kshe-malchuyot makpidot zo al zo* — and so the *Eretz Yisrael* currency cannot actually be brought there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, שֶׁל בָּבֶל וְהֵן בְּבָבֶל לְמַאי חֲזוּ? חֲזוּ דְּזָבֵין בְּהוּ בְּהֵמָה, וּמַסֵּיק לִירוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if the kingdoms are especially particular with one another, then with regard to the case in the *baraita*, in which sanctity is transferred onto money **of Babylonia and they are in Babylonia, for what are these** coins **fit?** In any case they cannot be brought to Jerusalem. The Gemara responds: **They are fit for purchasing an animal with them** in Babylonia **that will be brought up to Jerusalem.**

The *Gemara* then challenges from the other case in the *baraita*: *shebe'Bavel vehen be'Bavel mechalelin* — if you are in *Bavel* and the money is Babylonian currency, you may transfer *maaser sheni* onto it. But what use is that? You have to spend the redemption money in *Yerushalayim*, and if the kingdoms are mutually *makpid*, how will you bring Babylonian coins into *Eretz Yisrael*?

### Jerusalem Money Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ הַמָּעוֹת יוֹצְאוֹת בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם מִפְּנֵי כָּךְ! אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁיַּד יִשְׂרָאֵל תַּקִּיפָה עַל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁיַּד אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם תַּקִּיפָה עַל עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the statement of the *baraita*, that one cannot desacralize second-tithe produce by transferring its sanctity onto Babylonian money when in Eretz Yisrael, the Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The Sages **instituted that** all **money shall circulate in Jerusalem because of this reason,** i.e., so that Jews from all locations will be able to use their local currencies? **Rabbi Zeira said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** the *baraita* that states that all money is valid in Jerusalem is referring to **a time when the authority of the Jewish people is dominant over the nations of the world** and could enforce the rabbinic enactments. **There,** the *baraita* that states that sanctity may not be transferred onto Babylonian money when in Eretz Yisrael, is referring to **a time when the authority of the nations of the world is dominant over them,** i.e., the Jews, at which time foreign currency was not usable in Jerusalem.

The *Gemara* then asks: if the kingdoms are mutually *makpid*, how was any currency universally accepted in *Yerushalayim* at all? Is it not taught *hitkinu sheyihu ma'os yotzos b'Yerushalayim* — that they established that all currencies should be accepted in *Yerushalayim*? *Rabbi Zeira* resolves this: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. When the hand of Israel was strong over the nations, *Yerushalayim* could decree universal currency acceptance. When the nations had power over Israel, they could impose restrictions on which currencies circulated.

### Coin Imagery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ מַטְבֵּעַ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם? דָּוִד וּשְׁלֹמֹה מִצַּד אֶחָד, וִירוּשָׁלַיִם עִיר הַקּוֹדֶשׁ מִצַּד אַחֵר. וְאֵיזֶהוּ מַטְבֵּעַ שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ? זָקֵן וּזְקֵינָה מִצַּד אֶחָד, וּבָחוּר וּבְתוּלָה מִצַּד אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the coins of Jerusalem, the Gemara notes: **The Sages taught: What is** the **coin of** ancient **Jerusalem?** The names **David and Solomon** were inscribed **on one side, and Jerusalem the Holy City** was **on** the **other side. And what is** the **coin of Abraham our forefather? An old man and an old woman,** representing Abraham and Sarah, were inscribed **on one side, and a young man and a young woman,** representing Isaac and Rebecca, were **on** the **other side.**

The *Gemara* then asks: what does the coin of *Yerushalayim* look like? *David u'Shlomo m'tzad echad, v'Yerushalayim ir hakodesh m'tzad acher* — David and Solomon on one side, and *Yerushalayim* the holy city on the other. Much like modern coins that bear a leader's portrait on one side and a building or symbol on the reverse.

The *Gemara* also describes the coin associated with *Avraham Avinu*: *zaken u'zekeina m'tzad echad, bachur u'vetula m'tzad acher* — an old man and woman on one side (presumably *Avraham* and *Sarah*) and a young man and woman on the other (perhaps *Yitzchak* and *Rivkah*). This description is drawn from a *Bereishis Rabbah* that expounds the phrase *agadel shimcha* — I will make your name great — as referring to a *monitin* (a stamp or coin) that went forth into the world.

*Tosafot* raises the question of whether such coin imagery would violate the prohibition against creating human forms — *assur la'asos tzorat adam*. *Tosafot* therefore suggests, citing *Rashi*, that the coins did not actually bear images of these figures, but rather the words *zaken u'zekeina* were inscribed on one side. *Rashi* applies this same interpretation to the *David u'Shlomo* coin: the names were written, not their portraits. *Tosafot* notes that the harder case is that of *Avraham*, because the *Midrash* language of *monitin* implies an actual image rather than merely a written name, making the purely textual explanation seem less natural there.

## Maaser Accessibility

### Bavel-EY Exchange

The *Gemara* continues developing the *maaser sheni* currency discussion, and several of the rulings canvassed here — particularly around whether *Eretz Yisrael* money held in *Bavel* can serve as the vehicle for *chilul maaser sheni*, and vice versa — reflect a broader principle: the currency must be accessible and functional in the place where one seeks to use it, not merely existent somewhere in the world.

### Animal Transfer

As noted in the discussion of the *baraita*, when neither *Eretz Yisrael* currency nor Babylonian currency provides a clean path to *Yerushalayim*, the *Gemara* permits — under duress — the indirect route of purchasing animals with the redeemed Babylonian money and transporting them to *Yerushalayim*. *Tosafot* acknowledges this runs against the general rule that *maaser sheni* sanctity can only be transferred to money, but treats it as an emergency concession when no other avenue exists.

## Flattened Coins

### Coin Image Problem

In passing, the *Gemara*'s discussion of coin imagery raises the broader question — taken up by *Tosafot* — of whether images found on coins create problems under the laws of *avoda zara*. The *Gemara* mentions a certain individual praised as *chasid* or *kadosh* because he never looked at the images on coins. This points to an ongoing sensitivity surrounding numismatic imagery and the *issur* of making or benefiting from human forms. The precise scope of that prohibition — whether it applies to two-dimensional representations, to partial forms, or only to fully three-dimensional figures — is a subject of significant *halachic* discussion, though the *Gemara* does not resolve it here. *Tosafot*'s resolution — that the coins of *Avraham*, *David*, and *Solomon* bore only inscribed words and not actual portraits — is offered precisely to sidestep that problem.

## Coin Value

### Enlarged Coins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל הַמַּטְבֵּעַ, וְהוֹסִיפוּ עָלָיו, מַהוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: נוֹתֵן לוֹ מַטְבֵּעַ הַיּוֹצֵא בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וַאֲפִילּוּ כִּי נָפְיָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲפִילּוּ כִּי תְּרִטָיא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava inquired of Rav Ḥisda: What is** the *halakha* in the case of **one who lends** money to **another on** the condition that he repay the loan in **coin,** i.e., with the specific denomination of a currency, **and** the government **added to** the size of the coin, so that a coin of the same denomination now weighs more? Is the debtor obligated to return the newly adjusted coin, or can he repay the debt according to the prior weight of the coin that he borrowed? Rav Ḥisda **said to him: He gives him** the **coin that is in circulation at the time** of the payment. Rava **said to him: And** is this the *halakha* **even** if the new coin is as large **as a sieve?** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: Yes.** Rava **said to him:** And is this the *halakha* **even** if it is as large **as a quarter-*kav* [*tartiya*]?** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: Yes.**

The *Gemara* now poses a new question. *Rava* asked *Rav Chisda*: *ha-malveh es chaveiro al ha-matbe'a ve-hosifu alav mahu* — if someone lent money when a coin was a certain size, and by the time of repayment the coin has been enlarged — what is the law? Does the borrower repay with the same number of coins (now containing more precious metal) or must he account for the discrepancy?

*Rav Chisda* answers: *nosein lo matbe'a ha-yotzei be-osah sha'ah* — he pays back with the currency currently in circulation. Even if the coin is now as large as a sieve — even as large as some great block — the answer remains yes, you pay back in current coin. One simply repays with the same denomination as borrowed.

### Buying Power vs. Metal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָא זָיְילִין פֵּירֵי! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חָזֵינַן; אִי מֵחֲמַת טִיבְעָא זִיל – מְנַכֵּינַן לֵיהּ, וְאִי מֵחֲמַת תַּרְעָא זִיל – לָא מְנַכֵּינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava challenged: **But** by increasing the weight of this coin, the **produce has decreased in price,** and since more produce can be purchased with this coin, giving it to the creditor as repayment is a form of interest. **Rav Ashi said: We examine** the situation: **If** the produce **decreased in price due to** the change in the weight of **the coin,** the debt **is reduced for** the debtor and the creditor is repaid with coins that equal the prior value of the loan. **And if** the produce **decreased in price due to the market** value, the debt **is not reduced for him.**

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *ve-ha ka zeili peiri* — but produce has become cheaper! If the coin is now larger, one gets more for the same coin, meaning the creditor is receiving back something of greater value. This raises the fundamental question of whether we assess repayment based on the coin's purchasing power or its precious metal content.

### Precious Metal Test

*Rav Ashi* resolves this: *chazinan* — we must assess the situation. *Ei machmas tiv'a zil* — if the reason produce has become cheaper is that the coin now contains more precious metal, then we reduce the number of coins the borrower must repay, since each coin is now intrinsically worth more. *Ve-ei machmas tar'a zil* — but if produce has become cheaper simply because market prices dropped, and the coin's actual purchasing power is the same as before, then *lo mankin lei* — no reduction is made, and the borrower repays in the current enlarged coins.

### Historical Revaluation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָא שָׁבַח לְעִנְיַן נְסָכָא! אֶלָּא כִּי הָא דְּרַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עָבְדִי עוֹבָדָא בְּזוּזֵי דַּאֲגַרְדְּמִיס טַיָּיעָא, עַד עֲשָׂרָה בִּתְמָנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** the value of the coin **has** been **enhanced for the purpose of** melting it down into **a bar of metal.** Since this coin now contains a larger quantity of metal, if it were to be melted it would be worth more than an earlier coin of the same type, so it should be considered interest. **Rather,** in this case one must act **in accordance with how Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** acted when they **performed an action with the dinars of Agardamis the Arab merchant [*tayya'a*]:** They considered **up to ten** old coins to be the equivalent **of eight** new coins. Since ten old coins were equivalent in value to eight new coins, they paid eight new coins in exchange for ten old ones.

The *Gemara* then returns to the question of excess precious metal. Even if the buying power is the same, there is more silver in the coin — and silver has intrinsic value independent of its monetary function. The *Gemara* cites the case of *Rav Papa* and *Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua*, who dealt with a case involving currency lent out to a certain *agardamis* — apparently some sort of monetary official or overseer (Soncino renders it as a title rather than a personal name). The ruling was *ad yahav tamnya* — eight of the new coins were determined to be equivalent to ten of the old ones. This suggests that when coins actually contain more precious metal, one does not simply repay face value for face value; rather, one adjusts for the difference in metallic content. The *Gemara* thus appears to reverse itself: if the coins contain genuinely more precious metal, the precious metal weight — not the face value — controls the repayment calculation.

## Mazik: Sea Case

### Sea Coin Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: הַזּוֹרֵק מַטְבֵּעַ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ לַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר: הָא מַנַּח קַמָּךְ, אִי בָּעֵית שִׁקְלֵיהּ. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּצְלוּלִין – דְּקָא חָזֵי לֵיהּ, אֲבָל עֲכוּרִין – דְּלָא קָחָזֵי לֵיהּ, לָא. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּאַדְּיֵיהּ אַדּוֹיֵי, אֲבָל שַׁקְלֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ – מִיגְזָל גַּזְלֵיהּ; הֲשָׁבָה בָּעֵי מֶיעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba says: One who throws another's coin into the Great Sea is exempt** from liability. **What is the reason** for this? He can **say:** The coin **is resting before you; if you wish, take it.** The Gemara comments: **And this statement** applies only if the coin is **in translucent** water, **where** the owner of the coin **can see** the coin, **but** in **turbid** water, **where he cannot see** the coin, it is **not** so, and the one that threw it will be liable to compensate the owner. **And this statement** applies only **when he rolled** the coin into the sea without picking it up, **but** if he **took** the coin **with his hands** and threw it into the sea, **he has robbed** the owner of **it, and** the robber **is required to perform** the mitzva of **returning** the coin to its owner.

The *sugya* now turns to a series of striking rulings of *Rava* concerning the *mazik* category. *Rava* states: *hazoreik matbeia shel chaveiro l'yama hagadol* — if someone takes another person's coin and throws it into the Mediterranean Sea — *patur* — he is exempt from payment. The reason: *amar hamunach kamach* — the money is right there, it is accessible to you. Go and retrieve it.

### Visibility Condition

This exemption is qualified: *vehani mili b'tzululin d'kachazei lei* — this applies only if the water is clear and the coin is visible. *Aval achurin d'lo chazei lei, lo* — if the water is murky and the coin cannot be seen, he is not exempt.

### Gezeilah vs. Mazik

There is a further distinction between *gezeilah* and *mazik*. The above ruling — that throwing a coin into the sea is *patur* — applies only *d'adiyaduye* — where one knocked the coin out of the owner's hand so that it flew into the sea, in which case the question is whether a *mazik* act of destruction was committed. If the coin was never in the *mazik*'s possession, whether it is "destroyed" depends on whether it remains retrievable. But *aval shaklei biyadei* — if he physically grabbed the coin and then threw it into the sea, *migu degazlei* — since he already committed an act of *gezeilah* the moment he took it, that act of theft immediately obligated him to make restoration — *hashavah ba'i me'vad*. What happens to the coin after that is irrelevant to his liability.

### Tosafot Position

*Tosafot* challenges the limiting condition of visibility. In *Tosafot*'s view, retrievability — not visibility — is the operative criterion. If one could theoretically hire someone to dive down and recover the coin (even at a cost exceeding the coin's value), the coin is not halachically "destroyed," and therefore no *mazik* liability attaches. On *Tosafot*'s reading, the ruling would remain the same even in murky water, as long as retrieval is physically possible.

The lecturer finds this position genuinely difficult: intuitively, the combination of non-visibility and difficulty of retrieval would seem to constitute destruction. If the coin is invisible and difficult to recover, it seems hard to say it has not been "destroyed" merely because recovery is theoretically possible. Yet *Tosafot* insists that the sole question is in-principle retrievability. This is a striking position — and the underlying conceptual tension is that even if we concede the object is not technically destroyed, the *mazik* has imposed real costs (time, expense, effort) on the owner in order to recover what is rightfully his. According to the framework developing here, that is merely indirect causation of loss — *grama be-nezikin* — and does not rise to the level of chargeable *nezek*, so long as the object remains extant.

## Maaser Accessibility…

### Inaccessible Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: אֵין מְחַלְּלִין עַל מָעוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ. כֵּיצַד? הָיוּ לוֹ מָעוֹת בְּקַסְטְרָא אוֹ בְּהַר הַמֶּלֶךְ, אוֹ שֶׁנָּפַל כִּיסוֹ לַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל – אֵין מְחַלְּלִין! אָמַר רַבָּה: שָׁאנֵי לְעִנְיַן מַעֲשֵׂר, דְּבָעֵינַן מָצוּי בְּיָדְךָ; דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״וְצַרְתָּ הַכֶּסֶף בְּיָדְךָ״, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** to the statement of Rabba from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ma'aser Sheni* 1:6): One **cannot desacralize** second tithe by transferring its sanctity **onto money that is not in his possession. How so?** If **he had money in a fortress [*kastera*], or in the King's Mountain [*Har HaMelekh*], or if his purse fell into the Great Sea,** one **may not desacralize** second tithe by transferring its sanctity onto that money. This indicates that money that fell into the Great Sea is considered lost. **Rabba said:** This is not difficult; it **is different with regard to** second **tithe, since it is necessary** that the money be **found in the hand** of the one who wishes to desacralize the tithe, **as the Merciful One states** in the Torah: "Then you shall turn it into money, **and bind up the money in your hand,** and shall go to the place that the Lord your God shall choose" (Deuteronomy 14:25), **and** if it is in the Great Sea, **it is not** in his hand, but nevertheless it is not considered lost.

The *Gemara* now brings a challenge from a *baraita* regarding *maaser sheni*: *ein mechalelin al ma'os she'einam birshuso* — one cannot transfer *maaser sheni* sanctity onto money that is not under one's control. *Hayu lo ma'os be-Kastara* — if your money is in a distant place called *Kastara*, *o be-Har Hamelech* — or in some other remote location, *o she-nafal kiso l'yama hagadol* — or if your wallet has fallen to the bottom of the sea — *ein mechalelin*. You cannot perform *chilul maaser sheni* onto such money.

This seems to establish that money at the bottom of the sea is treated as non-existent. One might ask: does this mean that if someone caused your money to end up in *Kastara* — without technically stealing it — it would be as though they destroyed it? At minimum, the *Gemara* treats such money as if it does not exist for purposes of *maaser sheni*.

### Hand Requirement

*Rabbah* resists this inference. *Shani inyan maaser d'beinan matzui b'yadcha* — the laws of *maaser sheni* are different, because the *Torah* specifically requires that the money be accessible — practically in hand. The verse states *v'tzarta hakesef b'yadcha* — you shall bind the money in your hand — which demands a heightened standard of direct accessibility. This is a special requirement unique to *maaser sheni*, not a general principle of *nezek*. The money at the bottom of the sea is not destroyed — it still exists — but for *maaser sheni* purposes, "existing" is not enough. It must be accessible, and accessibility here is an intermediate category between having the money and having lost it entirely, one that is specifically mandated by the *Torah*'s language for *maaser sheni* but does not apply to the general law of *mazik*.

## Flattened Coins…

### Flattening Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה: הַשָּׁף מַטְבֵּעַ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָא לָא עֲבַד וְלָא מִידֵּי. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּמַחְיֵיהּ בְּקוּרְנָסָא וְטַרְשֵׁיהּ, אֲבָל שַׁיְיפֵהּ בְּשׁוֹפִינָא – חַסּוֹרֵי חַסְּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba** also **says: One who effaces** the image of **another's coin is exempt** from paying damages, even though he caused the coin to lose value. **What is the reason** for this? **As he did not do anything,** the coin remains the same size as before. The Gemara comments: **And this statement** applies only in a case where **he struck it with a hammer [*kurnesa*] and flattened it; but** if **he filed it with a file [*shofina*]** he must pay the amount of the reduction in value, since **he caused it to diminish** in size.

*Rabbah* continues his series of rulings. *Hashaf matbeia* — if someone takes another's coin and flattens it, smoothing away the minted image — *patur* — he is exempt. He has not removed any silver; the metal is all still there. The coin can no longer circulate as currency, but its precious metal content is intact.

### Material Removal Test

*Mai ta'ama? D'lo avad velo midi* — the reason is that nothing was done, nothing was removed. The parts were merely rearranged. The lecturer notes that this might have made particular sense in the context of coins, where the great bulk of value was the precious metal content; the minting was primarily a guarantee of weight and purity. But the *Gemara* does not limit this principle to coins.

The principle being articulated, it seems, is that an act of *mazik* requires the physical removal of some material from the whole. Merely rearranging an object — flattening it, hammering it — without taking any piece away does not constitute legally cognizable damage. This is, as the lecturer remarks, a genuinely astonishing position. The abstract diminution in value — the loss of the coin's status as currency — does not register as compensable *nezek* because no material substance was removed from the world.

### Filing vs. Hammering

This principle is immediately refined. *V'hani mili d'machyei bekurnasa detarshei* — this rule applies where one struck the coin with a hammer and thereby flattened it. *Aval shapei beshufinei* — but if one used something like a file, which would actually scrape away some of the metal — *chisorei chasrei* — then material was removed, and that constitutes an act of *mazik* for which one is liable. The defining test is whether any physical material was removed. Filing: liable. Hammering to flatten: exempt.

## Mazik: Animal Cases

### Slave Deafness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: הִכָּהוּ עַל עֵינוֹ וְסִמְּאָהּ, עַל אׇזְנוֹ (וְחֵרְשׁוֹ) [וְחֵרְשָׁהּ] – עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת. כְּנֶגֶד עֵינוֹ וְאֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה, כְּנֶגֶד אׇזְנוֹ וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵע – אֵין עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** to the statement of Rabba from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:26): If a master **struck** his slave **on his eye and blinded it,** or **on his ear and deafened it, the slave is emancipated by means of these** injuries. If he struck the slave **near his eye and** as a result **he does not see,** or **near his ear and he does not hear, the slave is not emancipated by means of these** injuries. The first clause indicates that one is liable for damage caused even if it is not visible damage.

*Rava* now challenges this framework by citing a ruling from a different domain. *Heika'u al eino vesima'ah* — if one strikes a slave on his eye and blinds him, *al ozno vechershah* — or on his ear and causes deafness, *eved yetzei lecheirus* — the slave goes free. But *keneged eino ve'eino ro'eh* — if the blow landed beside the eye and the shock somehow caused blindness, or *keneged ozno ve'eino shomei'a* — near the ear and caused deafness, *ein eved yetzei lecheirus* — the slave does not go free. The implication of the first case is that striking someone's ear — without visibly removing any tissue — is considered a cognizable act of damage. Why, if no material was removed?

### Interior Wound Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: חֵרְשׁוֹ לְאָבִיו – נֶהֱרָג; שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לַחֲרִישָׁה בְּלֹא חַבּוּרָה, דְּטִפְּתָא דִּדְמָא נְפַלַת לֵיהּ בְּאוּנֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rabba** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rabba says: One who deafens his father is put to death,** even though no bruise is visible, **because it is impossible for deafening** to occur **without a bruise,** as it is certain **that a drop of blood fell into his ear** from the blow, even if it is not visible from the outside. Striking someone in the ear in a manner that causes deafness results in a significant physical change, while striking a coin with a hammer does not detract from the actual size of the coin.

*Rava leta'amei* — *Rava* resolves this in accordance with his own position. *De'amar Rava chersho le'aviv neherag* — *Rava* had already ruled elsewhere that one who causes his father to become deaf by striking him in the ear is executed. Now, causing a wound that draws blood is a precondition for that liability. How could striking an ear draw blood? *Ee efshar lecharisha belo chavurah* — it is impossible to cause deafness without causing some interior wound. *Detipas dama naflah leih be'ozneih* — a drop of blood must have fallen inside the ear canal; there must have been an internal injury. Therefore, causing deafness by a blow to the ear is categorized as *chisorei chasrei* — something was removed — and that is why it constitutes a cognizable act of *mazik* (and, in the context of the slave, triggers emancipation).

This is a significant move: *Rava* does not abandon the "material removal" test; rather, he argues that the physiology of deafness necessarily entails an interior wound, bringing the case within the principle. The framework is maintained, but the analysis of what physically occurs is refined.

### Pierced Ear Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה: הַצּוֹרֵם אוֹזֶן פָּרָתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? פָּרָה כִּדְקָיְימָא קָיְימָא, דְּלָא עֲבַד וְלָא מִידֵּי; וְכוּלְּהוּ שְׁווֹרִים לָאו לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ קָיְימִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba** also **says: One who slits the ear of another's cow is exempt** from paying damages, even though this cow is no longer fit to be sacrificed as an offering due to the injury. **What is the reason** for this? **As** the **cow was available** for many uses until now, so **it is** still **available** for those uses now, **since** the one who slit its ear **did not do anything** to damage the cow in a substantial way. **And** concerning the fact that it is rendered disqualified from being sacrificed as an offering, **not all oxen stand near the altar,** i.e., most animals are not sacrificed as offerings anyway, so it is not considered a loss.

*Rava* then articulates a case that goes the other way. *Hatzorem ozen parato shel chaveiro* — if one pierces the ear of another's cow — *patur* — he is exempt. *Para kidekaima kaima* — the cow stands as it stood before; its market value is unchanged. *Velo avad velo midi* — nothing was done, nothing of substance was altered. The fact that the cow can no longer be brought as a *korban* is dismissed: *vekulei shovarim lav legabei mizbe'ach kaimi* — not all oxen are destined to be sacrifices. The loss of eligibility for the *mizbe'ach* does not register as a market-value diminution in the context of ordinary commerce.

The lecturer suggests that *Rava*'s real point here may be less about the "material removal" test than about the definition of damage itself. Even granting that something comes out when an ear is pierced (some blood, at minimum), the market value of the cow is unchanged. In an agricultural society, a cow with a hole in its ear is worth exactly the same as one without. The *chidush* may therefore be that when the objective market value does not change, we do not treat the act as *mazik*, even if there was technically some minor physical intrusion. (One might even argue: if there were a premium in the market for unblemished animals — for buyers seeking *korbanot* — some nominal loss of value would perhaps be cognizable. But *Rava* holds that this marginal premium, relevant only to a small subset of buyers, does not constitute a legally actionable change in the cow's value.)

### Red Heifer Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: הָעוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכָה בְּמֵי חַטָּאת וּבְפָרַת חַטָּאת – פָּטוּר מִדִּינֵי אָדָם, וְחַיָּיב בְּדִינֵי שָׁמַיִם. מְלָאכָה הוּא דְּלָא מִינְּכַר הֶיזֵּיקַהּ; אֲבָל צוֹרֵם – דְּמִינְּכַר הֶיזֵּיקַהּ, הָכִי נָמֵי דִּמְחַיֵּיב בְּדִינֵי אָדָם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **One who performs a task with** the **water of purification,** i.e., the water that is mixed with the ashes of the red heifer and sprinkled upon one who has become impure as part of the purification ritual, **or with** the red **heifer of purification,** whose ashes are mixed with springwater and sprinkled on those who have contracted impurity imparted by a corpse on the third and seventh days of their impurity, is **exempt according to human laws,** since he has not caused any discernible damage, **but** is **liable according to the laws of Heaven.** It may be inferred from here that one is exempt from liability according to human laws only if he performed **a task** with those items, **since the damage** that he caused **is not evident, but** in the case of one who **slits** the ear, **where the damage is evident,** he is **indeed liable according to human laws.**

The *Gemara* then presses the principle. *Maysiv Rava* — a *baraita* is cited: *ha'oseh melacha bemei chatat* — one who performs work using water designated for use in preparing *mei chatat* (water to which the ashes of the *parah adumah* are added) — or *uvifarat chatat* — using the *parah adumah* cow itself — *patur midinei adam vechayav bidinei shamayim*. Such a person is exempt in human courts but liable in the heavenly court.

*Tosafot* anticipates the obvious objection: the entire *parah adumah* context involves sanctified objects destined for sacred use, so of course it is different from ordinary commerce. *Tosafot* therefore focuses the discussion on the water, which at the stage under discussion has not yet acquired any formal sanctified status — the ashes have not yet been added. Even so, the disruption of its intended use generates at least *dinei shamayim* liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ צוֹרֵם פָּטוּר, וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דַּאֲפִילּוּ מְלָאכָה דְּלָא מִינְּכַר הֶיזֵּיקַהּ, חַיָּיב בְּדִינֵי שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **The same is true that even one who slits** the ear **is exempt** according to human laws, **and** the *baraita* that specified cases where the damage is not evident **teaches us this: That even** one who performs **a task** with the water of purification or the red heifer, **where the damage is not evident, is liable according to the laws of Heaven.**

The *Gemara* then infers: *melacha hu d'lo mincar hezeka* — it is only because performing *melacha* with the *parah adumah* water or cow produces an invisible *hezek* that the result is exemption from *dinei adam*. But *aval tzorem* — had one instead pierced the ear of the cow — *demincair hezeka* — since that *hezek* would be visible, one might be *chayav* in human courts as well. The *Gemara* pushes back: *hu hadin d'afilu tzorem patur* — in fact, even piercing the ear would be exempt. *Veha kamashma lan d'afilu melacha d'lo mincar hezeka chayav bidinei shamayim* — the *chidush* of the *baraita* is that even invisible *hezek*, like performing *melacha* with the water or cow, incurs *dinei shamayim* liability. This is a strange result: one might have expected that piercing the ear of a *parah adumah* — visibly disqualifying it — would clearly be *chayav* even in human courts. *Tosafot* grapples with the difficulty, noting that the whole discussion sits awkwardly, and emphasizes that the focus really should be on the water, where the case for exemption is stronger.

## Burned Documents

### Document Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה: הַשּׂוֹרֵף שְׁטָרוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּטוּר. דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: נְיָירָא קְלַאי מִינָּךְ. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הֵיכִי דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba** also **says: One who burns another's** promissory **note is exempt, as** the one who burned it can **say to him: I have burned** only **your paper,** and he is not held liable for the fact that the creditor will no longer be able to prove that he had provided the loan. **Rami bar Ḥama objects to this: What are the circumstances?**

*Rava* extends his series of rulings: *hashoref shetarotav shel chaveiro* — if one burns another's loan documents — *patur* — he is exempt. To the creditor's protest that he has lost the ability to collect his loan, *Rava* replies: *nayara kaliti minach* — I burned paper from you. Pay you for paper I will. The loan itself was not destroyed; the debtor still owes the money. The difficulty in collecting it going forward is an indirect consequence — *grama* — not an act of direct *mazik*.

### Witness Rewrite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּאִיכָּא סָהֲדִי דְּיָדְעִי מַאי הֲוָה בִּשְׁטָרָא, לִיכְתְּבוּ לֵיהּ שְׁטָרָא מְעַלְּיָא! וְאִי דְּלֵיכָּא סָהֲדִי, אֲנַן מְנָא יָדְעִינַן? אָמַר רָבָא: תְּהֵא בְּמַאֲמִינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **If there are witnesses who know what was** written **in the** promissory **note, they should write a** new, **proper,** promissory **note for him,** and there will not be any loss. **And if there are no witnesses, how do we know** what was written in the promissory note in order to assess liability? **Rava says: Let it** refer to a case **where** the one who burned the promissory note **trusts** the creditor with regard to the details of the promissory note. Despite the concession of the one who burned the promissory note with regard to the amount of the debt, Rabba holds that he is exempt, since the value of the debt is not inherent in the actual paper.

*Rami bar Chama* challenges this: what exactly is the case? If witnesses are present who know what the document said — that David owed Michael a thousand dollars — *lichtevu lei shitra ma'alya* — let them write a replacement document. In that case, the creditor has suffered no real loss; he can simply reproduce the document. So why would *Rava* even need to declare him exempt? *Ei d'leika sahedi* — and if there are no witnesses who know the contents, then even if the tortfeasor were liable, how would we know for how much? The question of quantum would seem to collapse the claim entirely.

### No Intrinsic Value

*Rava* responds: *tehei bema'amino* — it can be a case where the court accepts the creditor's word as to what the document contained, and the tortfeasor is prepared to pay accordingly — yet *Rava* still holds he is *patur*. The reason is that the document has no intrinsic value. The tortfeasor burned paper; the creditor's loss is entirely abstract and indirect. *Rava* does not even add *chayav bidinei shamayim* here — the loss is seen as so thoroughly indirect as to generate no liability even in the moral realm.

The lecturer finds this genuinely puzzling. One might have thought a loan document functions as an instrument — a *kli*, in some sense — that enables the collection of a debt. Why does it not have intrinsic value? Is it because its value is purely conventional, rooted in social and legal norms rather than any physical functionality? And yet precious metals, whose "intrinsic" value is also largely a social construct, are treated as having such value. The lecturer does not resolve this, but suggests that perhaps the key distinction is marketability: a loan document that is non-transferable, valuable only to its specific holder, may lack the kind of objective market value that defines compensable *nezek*.

## Grama Category

### Machloket Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב דִּימִי בַּר חֲנִינָא: הָא דְּרַבָּה – מַחֲלוֹקֶת רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּנַן הִיא. לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר הַגּוֹרֵם לְמָמוֹן כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי – מְחַיֵּיב; לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי דָּבָר הַגּוֹרֵם לְמָמוֹן לָאו כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי – לָא מְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi bar Ḥanina said: This** statement **of Rabba is** the subject of **a dispute** between **Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis. According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says** that **an item that causes financial** loss **is considered** to have **monetary** value, the one who burned the promissory note **is liable. According to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that **an item that causes financial** loss **is not considered** to have **monetary** value, he **is not liable.** Rabba holds in accordance with the Rabbis, and therefore rules that one who burns a promissory note is exempt from liability.

*Rav Dimi bar Chanina* now offers a unifying observation: *hadar beh Rava* — *Rava* retracted from his various exempt rulings. The positions articulated in *Rava*'s series of cases are not his own final view but rather reflect the debate between *Rabbi Shimon* and the *Rabbanan*.

### Rabbi Shimon

*Rabbi Shimon d'amar davar ha-gorem le-mamon ke-mamon dami* — Rabbi Shimon holds that something whose loss causes a monetary obligation is treated as having monetary value. The classic case: one who steals a *korban* belonging to another person — since the owner now has an obligation to replace it, the loss of the stolen *korban* caused the owner a monetary loss. *Rabbi Shimon* therefore treats the *korban* as if it were the owner's monetary property, and the thief must pay *kefel* accordingly. Under *Rabbi Shimon*'s principle, all of *Rava*'s cases would generate liability, because in each case the tortfeasor's act caused the victim an indirect monetary loss.

### Rabbis Position

The *Rabbanan*, by contrast, hold *davar ha-gorem le-mamon lav ke-mamon dami* — the fact that something's loss causes a monetary obligation does not confer monetary status on the thing itself. Under the *Rabbanan*, all of *Rava*'s cases result in exemption.

### Original Value Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דָּבָר הַגּוֹרֵם לְמָמוֹן כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי – בְּדָבָר שֶׁעִיקָּרוֹ מָמוֹן, כִּדְרַבָּה; דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: גָּזַל חָמֵץ לִפְנֵי הַפֶּסַח, וּבָא אַחֵר וּשְׂרָפוֹ בַּמּוֹעֵד – פָּטוּר, שֶׁהַכֹּל מְצֻוִּוים עָלָיו לְבַעֲרוֹ. לְאַחַר הַפֶּסַח – מַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּנַן;
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, objects to this: Say that you heard** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon,** that **an item that causes financial** loss **is considered** to have **monetary** value, **with regard to** a case where he damaged **an item that** has **intrinsic monetary** value, **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabba. As Rabba says:** If one **robbed** another of **leavened bread before Passover, and another came and burned it during the festival** of Passover, when the leavened bread had already become forbidden, the one who burned it is **exempt** from paying the robber, **as all are commanded to destroy** the leavened bread, and he therefore performed a mitzva. If he burned it **after Passover,** that is the matter of **dispute** between **Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis.**

*Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua* challenges the equation. When did *Rabbi Shimon* say *davar ha-gorem le-mamon ke-mamon dami*? Only *b'davar she'ikaro mamon* — regarding something that originally had genuine monetary value. In *Rabbi Shimon*'s paradigm case, the stolen object was a cow or similar animal with real market value, which was subsequently sanctified. The debate between *Rabbi Shimon* and the *Rabbanan* concerns whether that original monetary value, transformed by sanctification, retains legal significance. That is entirely different from the cases in *Rava*'s series, where either the object has no intrinsic value to begin with (the loan document), or the harm is purely indirect (the coin at the bottom of the sea). Those cases are simply *grama be-nezikin* — indirect causation — and the *machloket* of *Rabbi Shimon* and the *Rabbanan* does not speak to them.

## Burned Documents…

### Indirect Loss Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר הַגּוֹרֵם לְמָמוֹן כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי – חַיָּיב, לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי דָּבָר הַגּוֹרֵם לְמָמוֹן לָאו כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי – פָּטוּר. בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין עִיקָּרוֹ מָמוֹן מִי אָמְרִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, explains: **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says** that **an item that causes financial** loss **is considered** to have **monetary** value, the one who burned it is **liable,** and he must pay the robber. Although it is prohibited to derive benefit from leavened bread, the robber could have returned it to the victim and been exempt from liability. Now that it has been burned, the robber will have to pay the monetary value of the leavened bread at the time of the theft. **According to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that **an item that causes financial** loss **is not considered** to have **monetary** value, he is **exempt.** But **with regard to an item** such as a promissory note, **which** has **no intrinsic monetary** value, **do we say** that in Rabbi Shimon's opinion it too is considered to have monetary value?

The *Gemara* now works through the *chametz* case that most cleanly illustrates the *machloket*. One stole *chametz* before *Pesach*. *Jenna* came along and burned the *chametz* — and notably, everyone is obligated to burn their *chametz*, so she did nothing wrong. Nevertheless, the thief could have returned the *chametz* and discharged his *gezeilah* obligation — *harei shelcha lefanecha* — since no physical change had occurred to the *chametz*. By burning it, *Jenna* deprived the thief of this avenue of discharge. If the thief now holds the *chametz* until after *Pesach* — when it becomes *chametz she-avar alav ha-pesach*, worthless to the world at large — it is still "worth" something to the thief, because he can return it to the owner and say *harei shelcha lefanecha*, thereby discharging his *gezeilah* obligation without paying its original value. *Jenna*'s destruction of the *chametz* thus caused the thief a real monetary loss.

In *this* case — *machlokes Rabbi Shimon ve-Rabbanan*. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, since the *chametz*'s loss will cause the thief a monetary loss, the *chametz* is treated as having monetary value to him, he is considered its owner for these purposes, and *Jenna* must pay him for the *chametz*'s original value. According to the *Rabbanan*, since objectively the *chametz* is worthless, *Jenna* is *patur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: מַאן דְּדָאֵין דִּינָא דְּגַרְמֵי – מַגְבֵּי בֵּיהּ דְּמֵי שְׁטָרָא מְעַלְּיָא, וּמַאן דְּלָא דָּאֵין דִּינָא דְּגַרְמֵי – מַגְבֵּי בֵּיהּ דְּמֵי נְיָירָא בְּעָלְמָא. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וְכַפְיֵיהּ רַפְרָם לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וְאַגְבִּי בֵּיהּ כִּי כְּשׁוּרָא לְצַלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a ruling for the case where one burns the promissory note of another. **Ameimar said: The one who rules** that there is **liability** for damage caused by **indirect** action **collects, in this** case, the **value of a proper** promissory **note,** i.e., the amount of the debt, from the one who burned the promissory note. **The one who rules** that there is **no liability** for damage caused by **indirect** action **collects, in this** case, **merely** the **value of the paper.** The Gemara relates that **there was an incident** like this one, **and Rafram forced Rav Ashi,** who had burned a document in his youth, to pay damages, **and he collected** payment, **in this** case, **as** if he had damaged **a beam** used **for** crafting **a sculpture,** i.e., he paid him the value of the debt listed in the promissory note.

This case, *Rav Huna* emphasizes, is the genuine terrain of the *machloket*: an object with original intrinsic value that was destroyed by a third party, where the question is whether the subjective, "obligational" value to the thief counts. That is distinct from the cases of *Rava* — loan documents and coins at the bottom of the sea — where the issue is purely indirect causation with no original object of intrinsic value being destroyed.

## Grama Category…

### Garmi Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: מַאן דְּדָאֵין דִּינָא דְּגַרְמֵי – מַגְבֵּי בֵּיהּ דְּמֵי שְׁטָרָא מְעַלְּיָא, וּמַאן דְּלָא דָּאֵין דִּינָא דְּגַרְמֵי – מַגְבֵּי בֵּיהּ דְּמֵי נְיָירָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a ruling for the case where one burns the promissory note of another. **Ameimar said: The one who rules** that there is **liability** for damage caused by **indirect** action **collects, in this** case, the **value of a proper** promissory **note,** i.e., the amount of the debt, from the one who burned the promissory note. **The one who rules** that there is **no liability** for damage caused by **indirect** action **collects, in this** case, **merely** the **value of the paper.**

*Meimar* now introduces the category that will fill the gap. *Man de-dan dinei de-garmi* — the one, meaning *Rabbi Meir*, who adjudicates the laws of *garmi*. There is a major *machloket Rishonim* about precisely what *garmi* is — whether it is a *knas*, a *takkanah*, or something else — but its functional role is clear: it bridges the gap between the cases one is technically exempt from under *grama be-nezikin* and the cases one ought practically to be liable for. The *Ramban* wrote an entire treatise — *kuntres de-garmi* — attempting to define its parameters, and even there the lines may not be crisply drawn. But its purpose is to say: all these cases of *grama* from which you are technically *patur*, the ones from which you should not escape liability we will call *garmi*, and you will pay.

### Rav Ashi Case

*Man de-dan dinei de-garmi magbi be-damei shtar ma'alya* — one who adjudicates *garmi* cases collects the full value of the document. Exactly what "full value" means is somewhat unclear: does it mean the face value of the loan (the thousand dollars), or the document's market value (what one could sell it for, discounted for the risk of non-collection)? *U-man de-lo dan dinei de-garmi magbi be-damei niyara be-alma* — one who does not apply *garmi* awards only the value of the paper.

The *Gemara* then relates: *hava uvda de-kafye Rafram le-Rav Ashi* — there was an actual case in which *Rafram* compelled *Rav Ashi* to pay, and *ve-agbi beh ke-khashura le-tsalma* — he assessed against him the full value of the document, like the value of timber used in construction. Here we see *garmi* applied in practice to close the gap.

### Rashi's Question

*Rashi* is troubled by the obvious question: what was *Rav Ashi* doing burning another person's *shtaros*? *Rashi* proposes that *Rav Ashi* did this as a child. This gives rise to an important discussion among the *Rishonim*: if a minor causes damage, does he become obligated to pay upon reaching adulthood? *Rafram*'s compelling *Rav Ashi* to pay — on *Rashi*'s reading of the episode — raises the question of whether there is an obligation, perhaps *bidinei shamayim* even if not fully *bidinei adam*, to make restitution for damage caused in one's youth. The *Gemaras'* larger point, however, is that *garmi* emerges as a vital category precisely because the strict logic of *grama be-nezikin* exemptions, followed to their conclusion, would produce outcomes that cannot stand in any functional legal order — and *garmi* is the mechanism by which the *halacha* draws the line.
