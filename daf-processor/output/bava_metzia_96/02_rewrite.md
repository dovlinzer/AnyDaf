# Bava Metzia 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ba'alav Imo Timing

### Two Moments Debate

The daf opens approximately ten lines from the top, at the word *aipoch*, within the line beginning *bish'at she'elah*. The Gemara has been analyzing the Torah's principle of *ba'alav imo lo yeshalem* — that if the owner was working for the borrower at the time the object was lent, the borrower is exempt from paying even for an accident. The previous daf established that the *pesukim* indicate one of two possible moments as the relevant point of time: either the moment the object was borrowed, or the moment the accident occurred. The Gemara had tentatively concluded that the relevant moment is the time of borrowing, and now it challenges that assumption.

*Aipoch ana?* — Why not reverse it? Perhaps the moment of the accident is the determinative time. The Gemara first defends the moment of borrowing: *mistavra she'elah adifa mishum dekamaytei lei lirshutei* — it makes more sense that the moment of borrowing is primary, because that is when the object enters the domain of the borrower. The parameters of liability are defined at the moment they are undertaken, and there is a sense — perhaps echoing the language of *imo* — that if the owner is present in some capacity at that defining moment, the object never fully leaves the owner's sphere of responsibility and control.

### Borrowing Precedence

The Gemara pushes back: *aderaba, shvura umesa adifa sheken chayav be'onseha* — to the contrary, the moment of breaking or death should be the more significant moment, because it is then that the actual debt comes into being. The obligation to pay arises when the accident occurs, so should not that moment define whether the borrower is obligated at all?

The Gemara responds that the accident alone does not generate the debt — the underlying liability was assumed at the time of borrowing, and the accident merely triggers it. The critical moment is the one that creates the obligation, not the one that activates it. But the Gemara is unpersuaded: *ve'ilu lav shvura umesa she'elah mai avid?* — without the accident, the underlying liability would produce nothing. The debt ultimately crystallizes at the moment of the accident, so why should the time of borrowing be privileged?

The Gemara's final answer shifts the ground entirely: *afilu hachi she'elah adifa sheken chayav bimzonoteha* — nevertheless, the time of borrowing is more significant, because it is precisely at that moment that the borrower assumes the obligation to feed the animal. This is somewhat surprising — if we are not dealing with an animal at all, there would be no such obligation — but the Gemara appears to be returning to the principle of *reshut*. The obligation to feed the animal signals that at the moment of borrowing, the borrower has taken on full possession and full responsibility over the object. It is that moment of possession that can be curtailed when the owner is simultaneously working for the borrower. The Gemara is satisfied with this answer.

What makes this discussion striking is that there is ultimately no direct textual indication in the *pesukim* as to which moment matters. The Gemara resolves the question through a logical assessment of where the decisive act of undertaking responsibility occurs, rather than from a close reading of the Torah's language.

### Rav Ashi's Proof

Rav Ashi offers an independent demonstration from the *pasuk* itself. The verse states *ve-chi yishal ish me-im re-ehu* — when a man borrows from his fellow. Rav Ashi reads this as implying *v'lo re-ehu imo* — excluding the case where the friend is already working together with the borrower. The very language of borrowing from one's fellow presupposes that the fellow is a separate party at the moment of borrowing, not someone already in the borrower's employ. The key word is *ve-chi yishal* — at the moment of borrowing. If at that moment the owner is already employed by the borrower, the exemption applies.

The Gemara raises an objection: if this exemption is already embedded in the words *ve-chi yishal ish me-im re-ehu*, why did the Torah need to state explicitly the principle of *ba'alav imo lo yeshalem*? Rav Ashi answers: without that explicit statement, one would have dismissed the reading of *ve-chi yishal* as merely the Torah's ordinary manner of expression, conveying nothing beyond the simple act of borrowing. It is only because the Torah separately and explicitly states that the owner's presence with the borrower affects liability that we return to *ve-chi yishal* and recognize what it was communicating all along — that the moment of borrowing, and whether the owner was then already in the borrower's employment, is the legally operative point.

## Shoel Parameters

### Forbidden Purpose

Having settled the timing question, the Gemara turns to a series of questions posed by Rami bar Chama concerning the parameters under which a *shoel* incurs liability. The first: *she'ela l'riva mahu* — what is the law if someone borrowed an object for a forbidden purpose? The example given is borrowing an animal for a sexual act with it. Does the law recognize liability arising from such a transaction at all?

Notably, the Gemara does not frame this primarily as a question about whether liability can arise from an *aveira*. Instead, it poses the question in terms of the normal mode of borrowing: *k'dishali inshi ba'inan* — do we require that the borrowing be of the kind that people ordinarily engage in, which this is not? Or, *o dilma ta'ama mai mishum hana'ah* — perhaps the governing principle is simply that the borrower derives benefit without payment, and since there is benefit here, even of this perverse kind, full liability follows? The question remains unresolved.

### Appearance Benefit

The second question: *shala l'raos bah mahu* — what if someone borrowed an object not to use it but merely for appearance's sake? The illustration is borrowing a friend's valuable car and parking it in one's driveway so that passersby will think one owns it, obtaining social benefit — perhaps a promotion, perhaps greater creditworthiness — without ever actually operating the vehicle. Is that the kind of benefit that generates *shoel* liability?

*Mamona be'ina v'ika* — perhaps one only needs to have borrowed an object of value and have done so; or, *o dilma mamona d'it lei hana'ah minei be'ina v'leika* — perhaps one needs specifically to have derived benefit from the use of the object itself. Rashi understands the latter as referring to direct benefit from the object's use. The question probes how narrowly the Torah defines the parameters of *shoel* liability: is it only when one borrows something for normal productive use that the full liability applies?

### Sub-Pruta Benefit

Third: *shala la'asot bah pachot mipruta mahu* — what if the borrower intended to use the object for a purpose worth less than a *pruta*? For example, borrowing a screwdriver momentarily to turn a single screw, where the rental value of that brief use is less than a *pruta*, and the screwdriver then breaks. *Mamona be'ina v'ika* — perhaps all that is required is that one borrowed an object of monetary value; or, *o dilma kol pachot mipruta lav klum hu* — perhaps any benefit amounting to less than a *pruta* is legally insignificant, meaning that when the entire plan was to derive less than a *pruta* of benefit, one was never truly functioning as a *shoel* at all, and full *shoel* liability never attached.

### Multiple Items Benefit

Fourth, building on the previous question: *sha'al shtei paros la'asot bahen pruta mahu* — what if someone borrowed two cows and planned to use them together to perform exactly one *pruta*'s worth of labor? The combined use amounts to a *pruta*, but each cow individually contributes less than a *pruta*.

*Mi amrinan zil batar shoel umash'il v'ika* — do we focus on the transaction between borrower and lender as a whole, in which case the borrowing was indeed for a *pruta*'s worth? Or, *o dilma zil batar paros* — do we look at each cow as a separate entity, *v'chol chada v'chada leika mamona*, in which case neither cow individually was borrowed for a *pruta*'s worth of benefit?

### Multiple Lenders

Fifth: *sha'al mishutfin v'nish'alu echad mehem mahu* — what if someone borrowed jointly-owned property from two partners, Reuven and Shimon, and at the time of borrowing, Reuven was working for the borrower? Is that sufficient for *ba'alav imo*?

The Gemara considers two possibilities: *kula be'alav bein'an ve-ha leika* — perhaps *ba'alav imo* requires that all owners be present in this relationship, and since Shimon was not, the exemption does not apply; or, *o dilma me-hai palga didei mi-pater* — perhaps Reuven's status as a part-owner who was working for the borrower at least exempts the borrower from liability to Reuven specifically. The Gemara does not entertain a third possibility — that one owner's presence might exempt the borrower entirely — and instead frames it as either an all-or-nothing rule or a partial exemption corresponding only to Reuven's share.

This connects to the underlying logic of *ba'alav imo*. If the rationale is that the owner's continued presence means the object remains, at some conceptual level, within the owner's *reshut*, then as long as both owners are not in that relationship with the borrower, the object has at least partially entered the borrower's exclusive control. If, on the other hand, the principle operates on the level of the personal dynamic between owner and borrower, then being employed by only one of two joint owners may not alter that dynamic sufficiently.

### Multiple Borrowers

The sixth question reverses the previous scenario: *shutfin she-sha'alu ve-nish'al le-echad mehem mahu* — Reuven and Shimon jointly borrowed a cow, and the owner was working for Reuven at the time. *Kula sho'el bein'an ve-leika* — does *ba'alav imo* require that the owner be in the employment of the entire borrowing entity, meaning both Reuven and Shimon together, which is not the case here? Or, *o dilma be-hai palga de-sha'il mi-pater* — does the owner's employment by Reuven at minimum exempt Reuven from his share of liability, even while Shimon remains liable?

Again, the question of which logic underlies *ba'alav imo* is directly relevant. If the cow is considered to remain under the owner's aegis by virtue of his working for the borrower, then being employed by one of two joint borrowers may leave the cow at least partially still within the owner's sphere. But if the operative factor is the owner-borrower relationship as a whole, then the absence of that relationship with one of the joint borrowers means the full dynamic of *ba'alav imo* has not been established.

## Wife's Property Cases

### Wife as Lender

The Gemara now turns to questions involving marital property. *She'eila min ha-isha ve-nish'al le-va'alah* — what if someone borrowed property belonging to a wife, specifically her *nichsei milog*, the property she brought into the marriage over which she retains title but her husband holds the right of use? Tosafot notes that this cannot mean she lent it unilaterally, since the husband has usage rights; rather, the question concerns a case where both parties agreed to the lending. If the husband was working for the borrower at the time of the loan, is that *ba'alav imo*? The husband does not hold title, but he is the one currently entitled to use the property. Does that make him the relevant "owner" for purposes of *ba'alav imo*?

### Wife as Borrower

The reverse case follows immediately: *isha she-sha'ala ve-nish'al le-va'alah mahu* — what if the wife borrowed a cow from a third party, and at the time, the owner of the cow was working for the husband? Since the husband has *kinyan peiros* over his wife's borrowed property — he is entitled to benefit from whatever she borrows in the same way he benefits from what she owns — is the owner considered to be working for the one who has the rights to the property, thereby triggering *ba'alav imo*?

### Kinyan Peirot Debate

Both of these questions reduce to a single underlying issue: *kinyan peiros ke-kinyan ha-guf dami o lo* — is the ownership right to the produce and benefit of a thing considered equivalent to full ownership of the thing itself?

To illustrate the general principle: one cannot purchase an apple harvest in advance because that would be acquiring something not yet in existence (*davar shelo ba la'olam*). However, one can purchase the tree itself for the sake of its apples — *kinyan guf le-peiros* — acquiring a property right in the tree that entitles one to its produce, analogous to mineral rights. This does not permit one to chop the tree down; it is not *kinyan ha-guf* in the absolute sense. But it is a genuine property right.

In the case of *nichsei milog*, the husband holds exactly this kind of right: *kinyan ha-guf le-peiros*, ownership for the sake of its benefit, for the duration of the marriage, with the possibility that he may eventually inherit it outright. The question is whether, for purposes of *halachot* in which the status of owner is operative, the husband is considered the owner by virtue of this right.

Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish dispute this in the context of *bikurim*. If one sells a field to another for all of its produce — giving the buyer *kinyan peiros* — and the field produces first fruits, may the buyer bring *bikurim* and recite the declaration of *et ha-adamah asher natata li*, "the land which You gave me"? Rabbi Yochanan says yes — *kinyan peiros ke-kinyan ha-guf dami*, the ownership of the produce is tantamount to ownership of the body of the land — and the buyer may both bring and recite. Reish Lakish says *kinyan peiros lav ke-kinyan ha-guf dami* — the buyer must bring the *bikurim* since the fruit is indeed his, but he may not recite the declaration, since the land itself is not his.

This debate is referenced here only briefly; it is developed at much greater length elsewhere in the Gemara. Its resolution governs both of the wife's-property questions above.

## Agency Questions

### Messenger Representation

Ravina poses a further question to Rav Ashi: *ha-omer lishlucho tzei ve-hishael li im parasi mahu* — what if the owner, rather than going himself to work for the borrower, sends his agent or messenger to accompany the borrowed cow and assist the borrower? Is that sufficient for *ba'alav imo*?

This question is genuinely puzzling. A *shliach* normally represents his principal in a legal act — purchasing, selling, delivering a *get*, accepting *kiddushin*. The question here is whether the concept of *shlichut* can extend to a non-legal act, namely, physically working for or assisting someone. The owner mowing the borrower's lawn is not itself a legal transaction; it is the fulfillment of an agreement or an act of goodwill. If someone does it in the owner's place, they are not the owner. *Ba'al mamash ba'ina ve-leika* — perhaps we literally require the owner himself, and a representative does not suffice; or, *o dilma shelucho shel adam kemoto* — perhaps the general principle that an agent is like the principal himself applies here as well.

### Related Debates

Rav Acha son of Rav Avya answers Rav Ashi by noting that these two questions — the husband's *kinyan peiros* and the effectiveness of a *shliach* — each corresponds to an established debate among the *Amoraim* and *Tannaim* respectively. The question of the husband's rights as a form of ownership is the debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish regarding *kinyan peiros ke-kinyan ha-guf*. The question of whether a *shliach* can serve as the owner's representative for *ba'alav imo* corresponds to the debate of Rabbi Yonatan and Rabbi Yoshiya.

### Rabbi Yonatan vs. Yoshiya

A *Baraita* is cited to illuminate the debate between Rabbi Yonatan and Rabbi Yoshiya. The case concerns an *apitropus* — a trustee or guardian given full power of attorney over one's affairs — to whom the principal says: annul any vow my wife makes while I am away. The *halacha* is that a husband may annul his wife's vow on the day he hears it. The question is whether this power of annulment can be delegated to an agent, such that the agent's hearing is treated as the husband's hearing and the agent's annulment as the husband's annulment.

Rabbi Yoshiya holds that it cannot: the verse states *ishah yekimenu ve-ishah yeferenu* — the husband establishes it and the husband annuls it — with the word "husband" appearing twice in close juxtaposition, indicating that the actual husband is required, not his representative.

Rabbi Yonatan disagrees: *matzinu be-chol makom she-shelucho shel adam kemoto* — the general principle of *shlichut* applies throughout *halacha*, and an agent is equivalent to the principal.

Tosafot notes that everyone accepts the general principle of *shlichut*; Rabbi Yoshiya's restrictive position must therefore be based on the specific language of the *pasuk*. By analogy, in the case of *ba'alav imo*, Rabbi Yoshiya's position would likely hold that the *pasuk*'s language of *ba'alav* — the owner — means the owner literally, not his representative, just as *ishah* means the husband himself.

One might add a more structural observation: *shlichut* typically operates in the domain of legal acts — buying, selling, giving a *get*, accepting *kiddushin*. These are transactions that can be performed by a representative because their essence is legal. By contrast, the owner physically working for the borrower is not itself a legal act; it is conduct that satisfies an agreement. The act of hearing a wife's vow similarly is not a legal act — the annulment is the legal act, but the hearing is not. If the non-legal act of working or of hearing cannot be delegated through *shlichut* in the same way a legal act can, that is a principled reason for Rabbi Yoshiya's limitation that goes beyond the *pasuk* alone. In any event, the Gemara connects these two debates.

### Slave Extension

A related question is then posed by Rav Ilish to Rava: *ha'omer l'avdo tzei ve-hishayel im parati imahu* — what if the owner sends not a free agent but his non-Jewish slave to accompany the borrowed object and assist the borrower? Does the slave's working for the borrower constitute *ba'alav imo*?

The question, says the Gemara, cuts both ways regardless of how one resolves the debate about a *shliach*. According to the view that a *shliach* does constitute *ba'alav imo* — *shelucho shel adam kemoto* — one might still exclude a slave: *hani mili shliach d'var mitzvah hu* — that principle applies to an agent who shares the principal's obligations, i.e., a Jew equally bound by the *mitzvot*. A non-Jewish slave, *d'lav bar mitzvah hu*, cannot represent the owner in this domain. Rashi elaborates: since a slave has no independent property rights and cannot engage in the legal and social framework of lending, borrowing, and voluntary employment, he is categorically excluded from serving as a representative in these matters.

Conversely, even according to the view that a *shliach* does not constitute *ba'alav imo* — *ein shelucho shel adam kemoto* — there is a distinct and arguably stronger argument for the slave: *aval eved yad eved ke-yad rabbo damya* — a slave's hand is like his master's hand. The slave is not a representative at all; he is an extension of the master's own person and property. Whatever the slave does is an expression of the master himself.

The Gemara concludes: *mistavra yad eved ke-yad rabbo damya* — it stands to reason that the slave is an extension of the master. Whatever one holds about the *shliach* question, the *eved* functions as a physical extension of the master and satisfies *ba'alav imo*.

## Husband Liability Type

### Liability Category

The final line of 96a introduces another question of Rami bar Chama: *ba'al b'nichsei ishto, shoel havei o socher havei* — with respect to the wife's *nichsei milog* that the husband uses during the marriage, is the husband's status analogous to that of a *shoel* or that of a *socher*?

This matters for liability. A *shoel* bears full liability even for accidents — *ones*. A *socher* is exempt from *ones*. The husband receives the benefit of his wife's property without paying her for it directly, which suggests a parallel to a *shoel*, who receives benefit without payment. On the other hand, *Chazal* established that in exchange for the husband's rights to the wife's property, he assumes obligations toward her — food, shelter, support. In that framework, the relationship begins to resemble a *socher*, who pays for his use, here in the currency of marital obligations rather than money.

It is worth noting in this context that the Rambam conceptualizes the *shoel*'s relationship to borrowed property as a kind of *kinyan ha-guf le-peiros le-zman* — a property right over the object for the purpose of its benefit, for a limited period. The borrower does not hold title, but has a genuine property interest for the duration of the loan. In that sense, the husband's *kinyan ha-guf le-peiros* for the length of the marriage fits the same conceptual mold — the same structure, simply over a longer and differently-defined period, with the additional possibility that he might eventually inherit the property outright.

### Rental to Marriage

Rava initially points out that Rami bar Chama's question is moot as ordinarily framed: *ממה נפשך*, either way, the husband is *ba'alav imo* with respect to his wife. If he is a *shoel*, it is a *she'ela b'ba'alim* — Rashi explains that the wife is always in the husband's employ in the sense that her labor belongs to him, *bimlachto ve-hi nish'eles lo techila misheniseis*. From the moment of marriage, the wife's labor is effectively borrowed to her husband, making any use of her property an instance of *ba'alav imo*. And if he is a *socher*, the same logic applies — it is *schirus b'ba'alim*. The question therefore dissolves before it can do any work.

The Gemara labors to find a scenario in which the *shoel-socher* distinction genuinely matters. The first attempt: *agrah mina para ve-hadar nasbah* — he first rented the cow from her before she became his wife, and then married her. At the time of the original rental, she was not working for him, so the relationship was not *ba'alav imo*. Upon marriage, his rights to the cow expanded — he now has *kinyan ha-guf le-peiros* for the duration of the marriage, beyond the original thirty-day rental term, and without continued payment. If we consider this to be a new *shoel* relationship, it is *ba'alav imo* from the moment of marriage. If it is continuous with the prior *socher* relationship, it remains not *ba'alav imo* from its inception.

But the Gemara objects: even if he is a *socher* after marriage, the terms of the *schirus* have changed so completely — different duration, different consideration — that it constitutes a new rental agreement in any event. Whether *shoel* or *socher*, the relationship to the cow is redefined upon marriage and assessed afresh. Either way, it is now *ba'alav imo* and the question remains moot.

The Gemara then refines the scenario further: *ela ki kamibaya lei le-Rami bar Chama ki-gon d'agra ihi para me-alma ve-hadar nasba* — what if the wife was the one who first rented the cow from a third party, and then got married? The husband now has rights to the cow she rented, in the same way he has rights to what she owns. The question is whether his relationship to this cow makes him a *shoel* or a *socher* — and critically, this matters *aliba de-Rabbi Yosi*.

The Rabbis hold that if a *socher* lends an object to a *shoel* and an *ones* occurs, the *shoel* pays the *socher*, and the *socher* goes free vis-à-vis the original owner because it was an *ones* for a *socher*. The *shoel*'s obligation runs to the *socher*, not to the original owner. Under that framework, the husband's obligation in this case runs to his wife, and it is *ba'alav imo* in any event — she is in his employ.

But Rabbi Yosi rules *tachzor parah la-ba'alim rishonim* — payment returns to the original owner. If a *shoel* borrows from a *socher*, and the *shoel* is liable for *ones*, his liability runs directly to the original owner, not to the *socher* intermediary. Applied here: the wife rented the cow from Reuven and the husband has the rights of use. The husband is the functional *shoel* of Reuven's cow. According to Rabbi Yosi, if the husband is a *shoel*, he owes Reuven directly, and the question of *ba'alav imo* — was Reuven working for the husband? — is not satisfied. If, however, the husband is only a *socher*, his liability to Reuven is at the level of *socher*, which does not extend to *ones*. That is the genuine *nafka mina* of the question.

### Husband as Owner

Rava cuts through the entire discussion: *ba'al lo shoel havei ve-lo socher havei, ela lokei'ach havei* — the husband is neither a *shoel* nor a *socher*; he is considered a full purchaser, a *lokei'ach*. In the case of the husband and wife, *Chazal* granted the husband rights that go beyond even the general category of *kinyan peiros*, making him effectively the first purchaser of the property.

### Usha Enactment

The source for this ruling is the statement of Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina: *be-Usha hitkinu* — at Usha they enacted the following. If a wife sells her title to her *nichsei milog* property during the lifetime of her husband and then she dies, the husband may repossess the property from the buyers. Why? Because the husband's *kinyan peiros*, his right to the benefit of the property, predates the buyer's acquisition of the title. When the wife dies and the husband inherits, he is considered a prior *lokei'ach* — a prior purchaser — whose claim precedes that of the later buyer.

This status as *lokei'ach rishon* does not grant the husband unlimited rights; he cannot sell the property out from under the wife, nor make structural changes. But it means that in certain contexts — specifically when competing against a later purchaser of the wife's title — the husband's *kinyan peiros* is treated as tantamount to prior ownership of the property itself. By the same logic, in the context of *basar* liability, the husband is not a *shoel* or *socher* who owes a duty of care to the wife; he is considered the owner, and an owner owes no liability to anyone.

Tosafot, however, notes: *umihu nireh le-Ri d'shomer chinam havei ve-chayav bifshiah* — even given this *takkanah* that treats the husband as effectively the full owner, it seems that he would at minimum be a *shomer chinam*, a gratuitous guardian, and liable for negligence. Making him a *lokei'ach* does not fully relieve him of even basic responsibility. This connects to a broader question treated earlier in the Gemara: whether *pshiah b'va'alim* — negligence in a case of *ba'alav imo* — also earns an exemption, which depends on whether *pshiah* is understood more as a category of damage than as a form of guardianship failure.

## Hekdesh Me'ilah

### Me'ilah Issue

Rami bar Chama poses yet another question: *ba'al b'nichsei ishto mi ma'al* — with respect to his wife's property, if among her *nichsei milog* there is money or an animal that was consecrated to the *Beis HaMikdash* — *hekdesh* — and neither she nor the husband is aware of it, who commits *me'ilah*? The husband, upon marriage, acquires *kinyan peiros* over the wife's property and, according to Rava's ruling above, is in some sense treated as a *lokei'ach*. Does the act of marriage — through which the husband's property rights automatically attach — constitute an act of taking possession of *hekdesh*, thereby triggering *me'ilah*?

### Me'ilah Culpability

Rava responds by working through all possible candidates for *me'ilah* and eliminating each. Should the husband be the one who commits *me'ilah*? *D'tafei nicha lei d'likni ve-issura lo nicha lei d'likni* — he would certainly have wanted to acquire her property, but he would never have wanted to acquire something that was forbidden to him. He had no intention of taking possession of *hekdesh*, and the mere fact that his rights automatically attached to all her property upon marriage cannot be said to be a volitional act of taking *hekdesh* into his control.

Should the wife commit *me'ilah* by virtue of transferring the property rights to her husband through marriage? *D'tafei nami lo nicha lah d'likni* — at some level she does not fully welcome his rights over her property even in general; certainly she would not want to be seen as transferring rights over *hekdesh* to him. And in any case, no physical act was performed with the *hekdesh* object.

Should *Beis Din* commit *me'ilah*, since it was their enactment — the *takkanat ha-rabbanan* granting the husband *kinyan peiros* and indeed *lokei'ach* status — that caused *hekdesh* property to flow into the husband's domain? *Ki avud Rabbanan takanta ve-amru ba'al lokei'ach shavei le-heteira le-issura lo avud Rabbanan takanta* — when the Rabbis enacted that the husband is treated as a purchaser, they did so only *le-heteira*, for permissible property; they never intended their enactment to apply to *issur*, to consecrated property that cannot be owned by private individuals. Their enactment simply does not extend to *hekdesh*, and therefore it cannot be said that *Beis Din*'s enactment effectuated any transfer of *hekdesh* into the husband's possession.

### Me'ilah at Spending

Having excluded all three candidates from committing *me'ilah* at the moment of marriage, Rava resolves: *ba'al mo'el lichsheyotzi, midi d'hava amotzi ma'os hekdesh le-chulin* — the husband commits *me'ilah* when he spends the money, by analogy to the general case of one who spends *hekdesh* funds.

The reasoning is as follows. If one has *hekdesh* money in one's home and is unaware of it, simply moving it or placing it in one's pocket does not constitute *me'ilah*, because that act of physical possession is not an act of taking something away from *hekdesh* when one imagines oneself already to be the owner. If one knowingly takes *hekdesh* money and places it in one's pocket to acquire it, that would be *me'ilah* — it is an act of appropriating something from the *Beis HaMikdash*. But in a case where one is unaware of the *hekdesh* status and considers oneself the owner, the act of moving it is not *me'ilah*. The act of *me'ilah* occurs only when one spends the money — at the moment of actual misappropriation of *hekdesh* funds for personal use.

Here too: the husband considers himself the *lokei'ach* of this property. His carrying the bag of coins from one room to another or placing it in his pocket is not an act of taking possession of *hekdesh* as such; he already regards himself as the owner. The *me'ilah* occurs when he actually spends the money or uses the animal, because that is the moment of genuine misappropriation.

## Meta Machmat Melacha

### Use Deterioration

The Gemara raises a new question: *kichash basar machmat melacha ma* — if through normal use the animal became thinner, is the *shoel* liable for this diminishment in value? As a *shoel*, one has full liability. Does that extend to ordinary deterioration resulting from the use one borrowed the animal for?

Rav Pappa and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua respond with a sharper challenge: the question about the animal getting thinner implies that you believe the *shoel* would be liable if it died from exertion during normal use — *meta machmat melacha*. But why should that be so?

### Implicit Agreement

*Lav le'okmeh be-chilta she'altiah* — the owner did not lend it to be kept locked in a shed. The very act of lending it was an acknowledgment that the borrower had the right to use it. And that right to use it carries with it an implicit acceptance that normal use entails normal wear. If the borrower has the right to use the object in the agreed manner, the owner has implicitly agreed to the consequences of that use. A cow that has a heart attack due to exertion during plowing — where the exertion was perfectly ordinary and within the scope of the loan — is in a sense a case of the owner having lent something that was going to wear down through its intended use. That is not the borrower's responsibility.

This creates a remarkable asymmetry: when a total *ones* occurs — the cow is struck by lightning — the *shoel* is liable, because that has nothing to do with the agreement between them. But when the cow dies precisely from the use the owner agreed to, the borrower is exempt, because the owner implicitly assumed that risk by making the loan. Rava states the rule explicitly: *lo mibaya kichash basar machmat melacha depatur* — it goes without saying that getting thinner does not trigger liability; *ela afilu meta machmat melacha nami patur* — even dying from normal use leaves the borrower exempt. *De'amra lei lav le'okmeh be-chilta she'altiah* — the owner agreed to the use, and therefore agreed to its consequences.

### Hammer Case

The practical application follows: a man borrowed a hammer from his friend, and it broke. He came before Rava. Rava said: *zil ayiti sahadi de-la shanita bah ve'iftar* — go bring witnesses that you used it in an entirely ordinary way, and you will be exempt, because if the hammer broke under normal use, that is *meta machmat melacha* and outside the borrower's liability.

### Proof Burden

The Gemara then asks: what if there are no witnesses? A second incident is cited — the same scenario of a borrowed hammer that broke — and Rava ruled: *zil shalim lei narga ma'alya* — go pay him a full hammer. Rashi explains that in this case the borrower was negligent; this is not the scenario of pure normal use but of *pshiah*. Rav Kahana and Rav Assi challenged Rava: is that really the law? The borrower should not have to pay the full value of a new hammer; he should return the broken pieces and pay only the difference between their value and the value of a whole hammer.

Rava was silent — they were correct — and the *halacha* is established accordingly: *de-mahardar lei tavrei u-mamlei lei dmei mana* — he returns the broken pieces and pays the difference in value between the broken pieces and a whole hammer, as was also established in Bava Kamma.

The broader implication is that while the category of *meta machmat melacha* carves out a significant exemption for the *shoel*, the burden of proof rests on the borrower to demonstrate that the object was used in a normal and unremarkable way. If no witnesses are available to confirm this, the borrower bears liability — and the measure of that liability, even in a case of negligence, is not the replacement cost of the object but the diminishment in value, with the broken item returned to its owner.
