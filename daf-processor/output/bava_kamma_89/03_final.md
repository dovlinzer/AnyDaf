# Bava Kamma 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mother's Gift & Kinyan

### Nichsei Miluog Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַבָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא, אוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּנִכְסֵי. אֲזַל רַבִּי אַבָּא אַמְרַהּ לְמִילְּתָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא, אֲזַל רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא אַמְרַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁמָּכְרָה בְּנִכְסֵי מְלוֹג בְּחַיֵּי בַּעְלָהּ, וָמֵתָה – הַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Abba went before Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba** to claim his mother's property. Rabbi Yirmeya **established him** as the owner **of the property,** in accordance with what was written in the document. **Rabbi Abba,** the mother's husband, **went** and **said** a report of **the matter before Rav Hoshaya. Rav Hoshaya went** and **said** a report of the matter **before Rav Yehuda.** Rav Yehuda **said to** Rav Hoshaya: **This** is what **Shmuel says:** In a case of **a woman who sold her usufruct property,** which belongs to her but whose profits her husband garners, **in her husband's lifetime, and** then she **died, the husband repossesses** the property **from the purchasers.** The granting of her property by means of the document is analogous to a sale, and Rabbi Abba can repossess the property from Rav Shmuel bar Abba.

The daf opens in the middle of a sustained discussion rooted in our *mishna*'s teaching that a married woman occupies a legally precarious position: if someone injures her, he must pay, but if she injures someone else, she has no accessible funds with which to compensate. We will return to that *mishna* in due course. The immediate subject, however, is a related problem that arose from the case of a mother who, before her death, gifted her property to her son in order to prevent her husband from inheriting it — and thereby prevent the property from passing to sons who were not hers. The question turns on a fundamental distinction in property law.

The wife in question owned *nichsei miluog* — property over which she retained title, but over which her husband held the right to benefit from the *peirot*, the fruits or usufruct. The husband's interest is technically called *kinyan peirot*, a right to the produce of the asset, as opposed to *kinyan haguf*, ownership of the asset itself. The central question is: does *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan haguf dami* — does the husband's right to the fruits rise to the level of ownership, such that he can block his wife's transfer of title? Or does he hold only a usufruct interest, leaving her free to gift or sell the property outright?

### Father-Son Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא מַתְנִיתָא יָדַעְנָא, דִּתְנַן: הַכּוֹתֵב נְכָסָיו לִבְנוֹ לְאַחַר מוֹתוֹ – הַבֵּן אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִמְכּוֹר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן בִּרְשׁוּת הָאָב; וְהָאָב אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִמְכּוֹר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן כְּתוּבִין לַבֵּן. מָכַר הָאָב – מְכוּרִים עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת. מָכַר הַבֵּן – אֵין לוֹ לַלּוֹקֵחַ עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said that** *halakha* **before Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba,** who had ruled that the property belongs to Rav Shmuel bar Abba. **He said to them: I know the mishna** that supports my opinion, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 136a): In the case of **one who writes** a document transferring ownership of **his property to his son,** stating that the transfer should take effect immediately but the son will have use of it only **after** the father's **death, the son cannot sell** the property **due to** the fact **that it is** still **in the father's possession. And the father,** even though he retained the right to use the property, **cannot sell** the property **due to** the fact **that it is written** as belonging **to the son.** If **the father sold** the property, then **it is sold** to the extent that the purchaser may use it **until** the father **dies.** If **the son sold** the property during his father's lifetime, **the purchaser does not have** the right to use the property **until the father dies.**

An attempt was made to resolve this from an analogous case involving a father and son. The father transferred title to property to his son while retaining the right to the *peirot* for himself. The question asked there was whether the son could in turn transfer that title to a third party while the father's usufruct right was still operative. The *mishna* in *Bava Batra* was invoked as evidence that the son can indeed do so — that is, the father's *kinyan peirot* does not rise to the level of *kinyan haguf* and cannot block the son's transfer of title.

### R. Yirmiyah's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מָיֵית אָב מִיהָא אִית לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּמֵת הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי אָב – דְּלָא אֲתוֹ לִידֵי הַבֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba infers: **In any event,** if the son sold the property during his father's lifetime, **when the father dies the purchaser has** rights to the property. **And** this would be so **even though** it is a case **where the son died in the father's lifetime, where** the property **never came into the son's possession.**

Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba ruled on the basis of this *mishna* that the same logic applies to the husband and wife: the husband's *kinyan peirot* is *lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*, and therefore the wife's gift of her *nichsei miluog* to her son should be valid. The transfer of title stands; the husband cannot block it.

## Rav Yehuda & Abaye

### Shmuel's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קַסָבַר: קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת לָאו כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, וְכִי (קָא) זַבֵּין – דִּידֵיהּ (קָא) זַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Apparently,** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **holds** that **ownership of** the rights to use an item and to **its produce is not considered to be like ownership of the item itself. And** therefore **when** the son **sold** the property, **he sold** property **that belonged to him.**

When this ruling was reported to Rav Yehuda, he responded with a tradition in the name of Shmuel: *zo einah doma le-mishnateinu* — this case of the wife's gift is not analogous to the *mishna* about the father and son. The two situations are not parallel, and the proof fails.

### Rav Yosef's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנַן הַשְׁתָּא, בֵּין רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא וּבֵין רַב יְהוּדָה – כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ סְבִירָא לְהוּ. וְקָאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, כִּי מָיֵית אָב וּמָיֵית הַבֵּן בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב, אַמַּאי אִית לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ? כִּי (קָא) זַבֵּין הַאי – לָאו דִּידֵיהּ (קָא) זַבֵּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the discussion of the opinion of Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba. **And now** for **us, whether** if one discusses the opinion of **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba and whether** one discusses the opinion of **Rav Yehuda, they** both **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** that ownership of the rights to use an item and to its produce is not considered to be like ownership of the item itself. **And Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says: If** it **enters your mind** that **ownership of** the rights to use an item and to **its produce is considered to be like ownership of the item itself,** then **when the father dies and the son** had **died in the father's lifetime, why does the purchaser have** rights to the property? **When** the son **sold** it, **didn't he sell** property **that did not belong to him?**

Rav Yosef articulates why the analogy breaks down. Had the *mishna* been formulated the reverse way — with the son writing title to his *father* while retaining the *peirot* for himself, and the father then attempting to transfer that title — the proof would have been clean. That would demonstrate directly that the party holding *kinyan peirot* cannot block the party with *kinyan haguf* from transferring title. That would map neatly onto our case of the husband and wife.

But as the *mishna* is actually formulated, it is the father who transfers title to the son. And here, Rav Yosef suggests, the reason the father cannot block the son's subsequent transfer may not be a general principle about *kinyan peirot* at all. It may simply reflect the fact that the son will eventually inherit the father anyway — so that the son's ultimate acquisition of the *peirot* is already latent in his standing as heir. This gives the son a more empowered ownership position than an ordinary title-holder would have. That enhanced position is not analogous to the wife's situation, where there is no certainty she will ever recover the *peirot* — it depends entirely on who dies first.

### Abaye's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַשְׁתָּא דְּקָתָנֵי: "הַכּוֹתֵב נְכָסָיו לִבְנוֹ", מִשּׁוּם דְּרָאוּי לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef continues: **But now that** the mishna **teaches** specifically: **One who writes** a document transferring ownership of **his property to his son,** the ability of the son to sell the property is **due to** the fact **that** the son **is fit to inherit** from **him** and already has a right to the property, and not just due to the existence of the document. In the case of Rav Shmuel bar Abba as well, the woman's property is inherited by her husband, not her son, so she cannot transfer it to Rav Shmuel bar Abba.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַטּוּ בְּרָא יָרֵית אַבָּא, אַבָּא לָא יָרֵית בְּרָא?! אֶלָּא לְאַבְרוֹחִינְהוּ לְנִכְסֵי מִבְּרֵיהּ קָא אָתֵי; הָכָא נָמֵי, לְאַבְרוֹחִינְהוּ לְנִכְסֵי מֵאֲחוּהּ אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **Is this to say that a son inherits** from **a father,** but **a father does not inherit** from **a son?** There is no difference between their rights to inherit from one another. **Rather,** even if the mishna taught a *halakha* concerning a son transferring his property to his father, the son's reason for doing so could have been that **he comes to hide his property from his** own **son** and wants his father to inherit from him instead. **Here too,** when a father transfers his property to his son, **he comes to hide his property from** the son's **brothers,** as the father has other sons but does not want them to inherit from him. Therefore, the mishna's choice of case cannot serve as a basis for inference.

Abaye enters with a counter-argument directed at the very premise of Rav Yosef's reasoning. He points out that the scenario in which a father transfers title to a son, while retaining the *peirot*, would only make practical sense if there are multiple sons. What benefit does the son gain from having title now, if the *peirot* accrue to the father and the son would inherit anyway? The only rationale for such an arrangement is that the father wishes to ensure this particular son receives the property — to the exclusion of his brothers who would otherwise share in the estate. In that case, the son is not simply a future heir to the whole; he is one of several potential heirs, and without this arrangement, he would only have inherited a proportionate share of the property.

This means the son cannot be said to be in a specially empowered position on account of his prospective inheritance. He would not have naturally received all the *peirot* of this property. Yet he is still able to transfer full title. This restores the parallel to the wife's case: just as the son can transfer title even though he would not naturally have gained all the *peirot*, so too should the wife be able to transfer title even though her husband holds the *kinyan peirot*. Abaye concludes that in principle, the analogy does hold.

## Takanas Usha

### Principle vs. Takana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי "אֵינָהּ דּוֹמָה לְמִשְׁנָתֵנוּ"? מִשּׁוּם תַּקָּנַת אוּשָׁא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: בְּאוּשָׁא הִתְקִינוּ, הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁמָּכְרָה בְּנִכְסֵי מְלוֹג בְּחַיֵּי בַּעְלָהּ, וּמֵתָה – הַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **Rather, what** was Shmuel's reason for saying that the right of a husband to the property that his wife sold before she died **is not similar to our mishna** that states that a father does not have rights to property sold by his son? The difference is **due to** the rabbinic **ordinance of Usha,** an ordinance instituted with regard to a husband's rights to his wife's property. **As Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says: In** the town of **Usha** the Sages **instituted** that in the case of **a woman who sold her usufruct property in her husband's lifetime and** then **died, the husband repossesses** it **from the purchasers.** This ordinance was instituted only for the benefit of a husband, but not for the benefit of a father or son.

At this point, the *gemara* concedes that in principle, Abaye is correct. The argument favors the wife's right to transfer title. Following the ruling of Reish Lakish that *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan haguf dami*, the logic should allow her to do so. Yet the practical *halacha* does not follow this principle in the case of the husband and wife. The reason is not logical but legislative: *mishum takanas Usha* — on account of a special *takana* instituted at Usha.

### R. Yosi's Statement

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina states explicitly: *be-Usha hitkinu* — in Usha, they enacted the following rule. A woman who sells the title to her *nichsei miluog* during her husband's lifetime — and then dies — does not successfully deprive her husband of that property. *Ha-ba'al motzi mi-yad ha-lekochos*: the husband reclaims the property from the purchasers. Whatever she sold or gifted, he can recover. Applied to our case, the mother's gift of her *nichsei miluog* to her son does not stand. When she dies, her husband inherits her, and the gift fails.

### Takana Timeline

A natural question arises: what is the chronological relationship between this *takana* and the dispute recorded earlier between Shmuel and Rabbi Yochanan — or indeed between Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba's ruling and Shmuel's response? Usha is a Mishnaic-era location, one of the places to which the *Sanhedrin* relocated after the period of *Kerem be-Yavneh*. One would expect the *takana* to have been well established by the time of the Amoraim. Yet Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba apparently did not know of it, and the *gemara* itself must work to establish the *takana*'s existence.

The resolution appears to be that not everyone had a tradition of this particular enactment. Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina, identified as a second-generation Amora who was a student of Rabbi Yochanan, is the one who transmits the tradition of *takanas Usha* in this form. Shmuel, who lived roughly a generation earlier, knew that the wife's case differed from the father-son case — but his tradition articulated the difference differently, through the logical distinctions explored above. Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba, who ruled in favor of the wife's transfer, evidently did not possess the tradition of *takanas Usha* at all. The *takana* was known to some but not all, a pattern not uncommon in the transmission of rabbinic legislation.

As for the rationale underlying the *takana*: the general architecture of *kesuvah* law reflects a carefully balanced set of financial obligations and rights between husband and wife. *Chazal* constructed a system in which the husband assumes certain obligations — including ultimate liability for the *nichsei miluog* — in exchange for certain rights, including inheritance. Permitting the wife to unilaterally transfer title to her *nichsei miluog* would allow her to effectively circumvent the husband's inheritance rights entirely, dismantling that balance. The *takana* closes that avenue.

## Braisa: False Witnesses

### False Witnesses Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי הִיא תַּחְתָּיו וּמְשַׁמַּשְׁתּוֹ; וְנִמְצְאוּ זוֹמְמִין; אֵין אוֹמְרִים יְשַׁלְּמוּ כׇּל כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, אֶלָּא טוֹבַת הֲנָאַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ. אֵיזֶהוּ טוֹבַת הֲנָאַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ? אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיתֵּן בִּכְתוּבָּה שֶׁל זוֹ – שֶׁאִם נִתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשָׁה, וְאִם מֵתָה – יִירָשֶׁנָּה בַּעֲלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **and she is under him and serves him,** i.e., she was in fact still married to him, **and** then these witnesses **were found to be conspiring** witnesses who were attempting to deny this woman the ability to ever collect payment of her marriage contract, then the court **does not say** that the witnesses **shall pay the entire** value of **her marriage contract. Rather,** the court says that they shall pay the **financial advantage of her marriage contract.** The Gemara explains: **What is the financial advantage of her marriage contract?** The court **evaluates how much a person** would **want to give** to purchase the rights **of this** woman's **marriage contract,** taking into account the *halakha* **that if she becomes widowed or divorced** the purchaser will receive its full value, **but if she dies** during her husband's lifetime, **her husband will inherit her** property and the purchaser will receive nothing.

The *gemara* now attempts to bring an independent proof for *takanas Usha* from a *braisa*. The case involves *edim zomemin* — false witnesses — who testified that a certain man had divorced his wife and paid her *kesuvah*. The woman in question is in fact still living with her husband, and the witnesses are lying. When they are exposed, the law of *edim zomemin* requires that we do to them what they attempted to do to their victim. The victim here is the wife: the false testimony was designed to establish that she had already received her *kesuvah*, thereby permanently foreclosing her ability to collect it in the future.

### Kesuvah Value

The *braisa* states that we do not require the false witnesses to pay the full face value of the *kesuvah*. The reason is straightforward: the wife does not hold the *kesuvah* as an immediately payable sum. It is a contingent obligation, payable only upon divorce or the husband's death — and there is a real risk she may predecease her husband and never collect anything. Therefore, the liability of the false witnesses is assessed not at face value but at *tovas hana'ah* — the discounted market value of a contingent future right. The question is: how much would a rational buyer pay in the present to acquire the right to collect this *kesuvah* under the circumstances where it would become payable?

The *braisa* spells out the calculation: we assess *kama adam rotzeh liten bi-kesuvasah shel zo* — how much a person would pay to purchase this *kesuvah*, gambling on the possibility that the wife survives to collect it through divorce or widowhood, against the risk that she predeceases her husband and the husband inherits. That assessed amount, not the face value, is what the false witnesses owe.

Now, the *kesuvah* has several components: the base obligation of 200 *zuz* (or 100 for a previously married woman) that the husband owes her; the *nichsei tzon barzel*, the dowry she brought in whose value the husband guaranteed in the *kesuvah* document; any *tosefet* the husband added; and the *nichsei miluog*, her titled property over which the husband holds *peirot* rights. All of these components together constitute what the false witnesses were trying to rob her of, and all of them are contingent in various ways.

### Abaye's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לֵיתַהּ לְתַקָּנַת אוּשָׁא, אַמַּאי יִירָשֶׁנָּה בַּעֲלָהּ? תְּזַבֵּין כְּתוּבְּתַהּ לִגְמָרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Idi bar Avin continues: **And if** it **enters your mind** that **there is no ordinance of Usha, why would her husband inherit** from **her?** Let the woman **sell her marriage contract entirely.**

The *gemara* now uses this *braisa* to attempt a proof of *takanas Usha*. The argument runs as follows: the *braisa* treats the entire *kesuvah* — including the *nichsei miluog* component — as something whose value is uncertain and contingent, presenting the whole as a risk to be assessed. But this should not be so, at least with respect to the *nichsei miluog*. The wife holds title to that property right now. Even if she cannot sell it outright — because after all, whoever buys it is only acquiring the title with the husband's *peirot* rights attached — she could still transfer that title for its current market value. And crucially, the title will eventually yield full value regardless of who dies first, since whoever holds the title will ultimately regain the *peirot* once the husband's interest terminates. So why does the *braisa* make the *nichsei miluog* component sound as though it is entirely at risk, rather than separating it out as a component that could be monetized right now?

The answer would be: because of *takanas Usha*. Since *takanas Usha* prevents the wife from transferring title to her *nichsei miluog* at all, there is no current monetizable value in that title. Everything in her *kesuvah* is equally contingent and at risk. This would constitute a proof that *takanas Usha* exists.

### Gemara's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָמְרוּ בְּנִכְסֵי מְלוֹג, יֹאמְרוּ בְּנִכְסֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is not a proof for the existence of the ordinance of Usha, because even **if** there is no ordinance, and the Sages **said** that the woman retains the right to sell **with regard to** the **usufruct property,** would they also **say** that the woman retains the right to sell **with regard to guaranteed property,** which is listed among the possessions she brought into the marriage but is entirely under her husband's authority? In any event, there is property that she does not have the right to sell and that the husband would inherit if she dies, regardless of whether or not there is an ordinance of Usha, so the marriage contract could not be sold for its full value.

Abaye, however, deflects this proof. He points out that the *braisa*'s language need not be referring to the *nichsei miluog* at all. The assessment of what the false witnesses owe can be understood as referring entirely to the other components of the *kesuvah* — the base 200, the *nichsei tzon barzel* dowry, and the *tosefet* — all of which are fully in the husband's title and therefore genuinely contingent. Those amounts the wife will either collect in full upon divorce or widowhood, or never collect at all. One cannot prove from this *braisa* whether *takanas Usha* exists or not, because the uncertain valuation is fully explained by these other *kesuvah* components, without any need to reference the *nichsei miluog*.

## Tovas Hana'ah

### Tovas Hana'ah Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה, הוֹאִיל וַאֲתָא לְיָדַן, נֵימָא בַּהּ מִילְּתָא: טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה – לְאִשָּׁה הָוְיָא. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לְבַעַל הָוְיָא, לֵימְרוּ לַהּ עֵדִים: מַאי אַפְסֵדְינִּךְ? אִי הֲוָה מְזַבְּנַתְּ לַהּ לְטוֹבַת הֲנָאָה, בַּעַל הֲוָה שָׁקֵיל מִינִּךְ! אָמַר רַב שַׁלְמַן: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא רְוַוח בֵּיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** With regard to **financial advantage, since** discussion of this matter **came to us, let us say something about it.** The money received for selling the **financial advantage is** given only **to the wife. For if** it **enters your mind** to say that it **is** given **to the husband,** then **let** the conspiring **witnesses,** who have been rendered liable to pay a penalty to the wife, **say to her: What loss have we caused you** by testifying that you had received payment of your marriage contract? Even **if you were to sell it for** the **financial advantage,** your **husband would have taken** it **from you. Rav Shalman said** in response: She still would incur a loss, **because there is a gain** to **the household.** Even if the husband were to be the one to receive the payment from the sale, the financial situation of the household would be improved, leading to a concrete benefit for the wife as well. This offsets the wife's potential loss.

Since *tovas hana'ah* has come into prominence in the discussion, the *gemara* takes the opportunity to clarify some of its legal contours. *Tovas hana'ah* denotes the present market value of a future, contingent right — in this context, the price at which a wife could currently sell her *kesuvah* to a third party. It is not the face value of what she might collect; it is the discounted price that a rational buyer would pay, accounting for the probability of various outcomes.

A fundamental question is: to whom does this *tovas hana'ah* belong — to the wife or to the husband? The point might seem obvious, but the *gemara* presses it seriously. If the wife sells her *kesuvah* to a third party, she receives the purchase price. Whose money is that? One might argue that since the general rule is *ma she-kaneta isha kanah ba'alah* — whatever a wife acquires, her husband acquires — the money she receives for selling her *kesuvah* should pass to her husband. If that were so, the false witnesses could protest: what did we actually rob her of? Had she sold the *kesuvah*, that money would have gone to her husband anyway.

### Rav Shaman's Objection

Rav Shaman partially resists the straightforward answer. Even if the *tovas hana'ah* money would technically transfer to the husband, the wife still benefits indirectly — the funds enter the household and improve the couple's financial situation. There is thus at least an indirect benefit to her, even if she does not personally pocket the proceeds. This complicates the simple claim that *tovas hana'ah* belongs entirely to the wife.

### Wife Owns Tovas

Nevertheless, the weight of the analysis favors the wife. The *kesuvah* is fundamentally her entitlement, and *tovas hana'ah* is the monetary expression of her rights under it. The indirect benefit argument is insufficient to transfer the ownership of those proceeds to the husband.

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הִלְכְתָא: טוֹבַת הֲנָאָה לָאִשָּׁה, וְאֵין הַבַּעַל אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? פֵּירָא תַּקִּינוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן, פֵּירָא דְפֵירָא לָא תַּקִּינוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The ***halakha*** is that the money received for selling the **financial advantage** of the marriage contract is given only **to the wife, and the husband does not** even **garner the profits** from that money or from property the wife would purchase with that money. **What is the reason? The Sages instituted** that **the profits** of her property are **for** the husband, but **the Sages did not institute** that **the profits of the profits** are **for him.**

Rava rules explicitly: *hilchasa tovat hana'ah l'isha* — the law is that *tovas hana'ah* of the *kesuvah* belongs to the wife. She pockets the proceeds of such a sale; they do not pass to her husband. Moreover — and this is the second part of Rava's ruling — *v'ein ha-ba'al ochel peirot*: the husband does not even collect *peirot* from that money. This requires explanation.

### Peirot d'Peirot

One might think: fine, the wife pockets the three thousand dollars she received for selling her *kesuvah*. But that money is now property she owns while married. Under the rules of *nichsei miluog*, she retains title but the husband gets the *peirot*. So shouldn't the husband receive the interest or yield from that three thousand dollars?

Rava says no: *peiros tiknu leih rabanan, peiri d'peiri lo tiknu leih rabanan*. The Sages established the husband's right to *peirot* of his wife's property. They did not establish a right to *peirot* of *peirot* — that is, to derivative or second-order fruit. The *tovas hana'ah* money is itself a kind of derivative: it is not the natural yield of the *nichsei miluog* but rather the monetized value of a contingent right, a once-removed benefit. When a woman brings sheep into a marriage and the sheep produce offspring, and those offspring produce further offspring, the husband does receive those subsequent yields — that is a natural and ongoing *peirot* process. But the *tovas hana'ah* from a *kesuvah* sale is not that kind of natural produce. It is a singular, contingent realization of value that *chazal* did not include in the husband's usufruct rights. Therefore both the principal and any yield from it belong entirely to the wife.

## Back to Mishna

### Mishna Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מִבֵּי רַב, אָמְרִי, תְּנֵינָא לְתַקָּנַת אוּשָׁא: הָעֶבֶד וְהָאִשָּׁה פְּגִיעָתָן רָעָה; הַחוֹבֵל בָּהֶן חַיָּיב, וְהֵם שֶׁחָבְלוּ בַּאֲחֵרִים – פְּטוּרִין. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לֵיתַהּ לְתַקָּנַת אוּשָׁא, תְּזַבֵּין נִכְסֵי מְלוֹג וְתִתֵּן לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, came from the study hall of Rav they said: We learned** a source **for** the **ordinance of Usha** in the mishna (87a): With regard to **a slave or** a married **woman, an encounter with them is disadvantageous,** since **one who injures them** is **liable. But** if **they** were the ones who **injured others they** are **exempt,** because they do not have money with which to pay. **And if** it **enters your mind** to say that **there is no ordinance of Usha,** there would be a method by which a married woman can obtain money with which to pay, as the court can have **her sell** her **usufruct property and give** the money received **to** the one she injured. It can therefore be inferred from the mishna that a woman cannot sell her usufruct property, in accordance with the ordinance of Usha.

Having established the rules of *tovas hana'ah* and its ownership, the *gemara* returns to the *mishna* that motivated this entire discussion. The *mishna* states: *ha-eved ve-ha-isha pegi'atan ra'ah* — encountering a slave and a married woman in the context of injury is bad business. If you injure them, you must pay. But if they injure you — *pturim* — they are exempt, or more precisely, payment is deferred: they will pay when the slave is freed or the woman is divorced or widowed and has funds of her own.

The point of the *mishna* is that a married woman has no accessible cash with which to pay damages. She owes the money — it is not forgiven — but she cannot pay now.

### Why Can't She Pay?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, נְהִי נָמֵי דְּאִיתַיהּ לְתַקָּנַת אוּשָׁא – וְלָא מָצֵי מְזַבְּנָה לִגְמָרֵי; תְּזַבֵּין לְנִכְסֵי מְלוֹג בְּטוֹבַת הֲנָאָה, וְתִתֵּן לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא דְּלֵית לַהּ; הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלֵית לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And according to your reasoning, though indeed there is an ordinance of Usha, and she cannot fully sell** her usufruct property, **she** should nevertheless be able to **sell** her **usufruct property for** the **financial advantage,** i.e., the purchaser will pay her for the future rights to the property in the event that she gets divorced or is widowed, **and she will give** that money **to** the one she injured. **Rather,** it must be that the mishna is discussing a case **where she does not have** usufruct property of which she can sell the future rights. **So too,** there is no source from the mishna for the ordinance of Usha, as the mishna is discussing a case **where she does not have** usufruct property to sell.

Now the *gemara* probes this claim. The *mishna* implies that a married woman has absolutely no means of raising cash in the present. But she does own something: her *nichsei miluog*. Even if she cannot transfer title outright — because of *takanas Usha* — she could sell the *tovas hana'ah*, the future rights in her *kesuvah*, to a third party and use those funds to pay the person she injured. Why does the *mishna* treat her as utterly without resources?

The *gemara* responds: *ai salka da'atach leis la takanas Usha* — if you do not accept *takanas Usha*, then certainly she could sell the title to her *nichsei miluog* outright, not merely the *tovas hana'ah*, and pay the injured party. The *mishna* presupposes that no such sale is possible — which is a proof that *takanas Usha* exists and prevents even the title transfer. But the *gemara* presses further: even granting *takanas Usha*, she still cannot transfer full title — but she could still sell the *tovas hana'ah*, the contingent future value, for ready cash. Why is even that not available to her?

### Shmuel's Debt Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְזַבֵּין כְּתוּבְּתַהּ בְּטוֹבַת הֲנָאָה, וְתִתֵּן לֵיהּ! הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אָסוּר לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁיְּשַׁהֶא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲפִילּוּ שָׁעָה אַחַת בְּלֹא כְּתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the ruling of the mishna: Why does the mishna assert that a married woman will not have money with which to pay, **but she should sell** the **financial advantage** of **her marriage contract and give** the money received **to** the one she injured? The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** ruling of the mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** that it is **prohibited for a man to remain** living together **with his wife** for **even one hour without** her having **a marriage contract.** Therefore, the woman cannot sell the financial advantage of her marriage contract.

The answer lies in Shmuel's ruling about the sale of debts. Shmuel states: *ha-mocher shtar chov le-chavero ve-chazar u-machlalo machul* — if a person sells a promissory note to another, and then the original creditor forgives the underlying debt, the forgiveness is effective. The buyer of the note loses his investment. This is so even if the original creditor subsequently dies and his heir is the one who forgives — *afilu yoresh mochel* — even the heir can forgive and thereby vitiate the transaction.

The logic underlying this ruling is that a debt is inherently personal. When a creditor sells a note, he transfers the right to collect, but the underlying obligation remains personal to the original parties. Because of this, the original creditor can still forgive the debt even after selling the note — the personal bond between debtor and original creditor is never entirely severed by the sale.

Applied to the wife's situation: if she sells the *tovas hana'ah* of her *kesuvah* to a third party, she retains the ability to forgive the underlying obligation — to be *mochelet* to her husband and thereby render the buyer's investment worthless. Therefore, forcing her to sell the *tovas hana'ah* to pay the person she injured would be requiring a sale that would inevitably harm the buyer, since she will certainly forgive the debt to her husband once she has raised the cash. A *beit din* will not compel a transaction that it knows in advance will cause loss to an innocent party. Consequently, neither the title sale (barred by *takanas Usha*) nor the *tovas hana'ah* sale (undermined by Shmuel's rule) is available to her, and the *mishna*'s characterization stands: she simply has no means of paying now.

## Woman Injured Husband

### Braisa Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: וְכֵן הִיא שֶׁחָבְלָה בְּבַעְלָהּ – לֹא הִפְסִידָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, אַמַּאי? תְּזַבְּנִינַּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ לִכְתוּבְּתַהּ לְבַעְלַהּ בְּטוֹבַת הֲנָאָה – בְּהָא חֲבָלָה, דְּאִי מָחֲלָה לְגַבֵּי בַעַל, לֵיכָּא פְּסֵידָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the reason she does not sell the financial advantage of her marriage contract to obtain money with which to pay the injured party is that she would then forgive the debt of the marriage contract, then with regard to **that which is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:22): **And similarly,** in a case where **she** was the one **who injured her husband she has not lost her marriage contract** as a result, as he does not collect compensation from the properties she brought into the marriage that are enumerated in the marriage contract, **why** does she not lose it? **Let her sell the financial advantage of her marriage contract to her husband** in order to obtain the money to compensate him **for that injury** that she caused him, **as, if she will forgive** the debt of her marriage contract **with regard to her husband there will be no loss** for the purchaser, i.e., her husband.

A *braisa* introduces a closely related case: *ve-chen hi she-chavlah be-ba'alah* — a woman who injured her husband. In that case too, *lo hifsidah kesuvasah* — she does not forfeit her *kesuvah*. She owes her husband the damage payment, but she retains her full *kesuvah* rights.

### Logical Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא וַדַּאי רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיְּשַׁהֶא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲפִילּוּ שָׁעָה אַחַת בְּלֹא כְּתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** *baraita* **is certainly** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** that it is **prohibited for a man to remain** living together **with his wife** for **even one hour without** her having **a marriage contract.** Therefore, the woman cannot sell the financial advantage of her marriage contract to her husband.

This ruling requires examination. Here the situation is different from the general case, because the person she injured is her own husband. One might think there is a clean solution: she can sell her *kesuvah* rights back to her husband at *tovas hana'ah* — the discounted current value — and thereby pay off the injury debt. After all, if she forgives the *kesuvah* obligation to her husband, that is precisely what she is doing: freeing him from a future payout, which has a real present value. Let that value offset what she owes him for the injury.

### Rabbi Meir's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא מַאי – כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא קַלָּה בְּעֵינָיו לְהוֹצִיאָהּ; הָכָא מְגָרֵשׁ לַהּ, וְגָבֵי לֵיהּ בַּחֲבָלֵיהּ מִינַּהּ. אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּה נָמֵי – מְגָרֵשׁ לַהּ, וְגָבֵי לֵיהּ בַּחֲבָלֵיהּ מִינַּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** for the ruling of Rabbi Meir? It is **so that she will not be demeaned in his eyes such that** he will easily **divorce her,** and **here,** if she were to sell the marriage contract to him, **he would** then **divorce her and collect** the properties listed in the marriage contract as payment **for his injuries from her. If that is so, even now,** where she does not sell her marriage contract to him, **he will divorce her, and** once she is divorced, he will **collect** the properties listed in the marriage contract as payment **for his injuries from her.** Preventing her from selling the marriage contract to him will not serve as an impediment to his divorcing her.

The *gemara* responds with Rabbi Meir's principle: *asur lo le-adam she-yisheh et ishto afilu sha'ah achat belo kesuvah* — it is forbidden for a man to remain married to his wife even for a moment without a *kesuvah*. The rationale is protective: the *kesuvah* creates a financial deterrent against easy divorce. If a husband can divorce his wife at will without financial consequence, he may do so impulsively. The *kesuvah* obligation slows him down. If the wife sells her *kesuvah* rights back to her husband, he is now free of that obligation, and nothing restrains him from divorcing her immediately. Rabbi Meir therefore prohibits any arrangement that leaves a marriage without a *kesuvah* in force.

One might object: even if she sold her *kesuvah* to a third party rather than back to her husband, the husband would still face a financial obligation upon divorce — he would owe the *kesuvah* to whoever purchased it. So the deterrent remains. Rabbi Meir's concern, one might think, is only that *some* financial obligation remains, regardless of to whom. Why should selling back to the husband be worse than selling to a third party?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן דִּנְפִישָׁ[א] כְּתוּבְּתַהּ, דְּמִשּׁוּם הָהוּא פּוּרְתָּא לָא מַפְסֵיד טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is discussing a case **where there** is **a large** sum recorded in **her marriage contract,** so **that because** of **that slight** sum due him as compensation for his injuries, **he would not** be willing to **lose** the **large** sum that he would have to pay were he to divorce her. Therefore, the marriage contract does serve as an impediment to his divorcing her.

The *gemara* responds: when the *kesuvah* is sold to a third party, the husband does face an obligation — if he divorces her, the buyers come to collect from him. The deterrent is intact. But if the wife sells the *kesuvah* back to the husband himself, the husband simply acquires his own obligation. He pays the *tovas hana'ah* purchase price now and is thereby freed from all future *kesuvah* liability. He can now divorce her without financial consequence. That is precisely what Rabbi Meir sought to prevent.

### Resolution: Kesuvah Base

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי דִּנְפִישָׁא כְּתוּבְּתַהּ מִכְּתוּבָּה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, נוֹקְמַהּ אַכְּתוּבָּה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – וְאִידַּךְ תְּזַבְּנַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲבָלֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But if** in fact the *baraita* is discussing a case **where** the sum recorded in **her marriage contract** is **larger than** the sum recorded in **a marriage contract** required **by Torah law, let them establish** her marriage contract **at** the sum of **a marriage contract** required **by Torah law, and** with regard to **the other,** additional, sum, **let her sell it to** her husband as payment **for his injuries.** Since she will be left with a marriage contract of standard value, they would be allowed to remain living together.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן דְּלָא נְפִישָׁא כְּתוּבְּתַהּ מִכְּתוּבָּה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא; דְּהָוֵי חֲבָלֵיהּ אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי, דְּמִשּׁוּם אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי לָא מַפְסֵיד עֶשְׂרִים וְחַמְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is discussing a case **where** the sum recorded in **her marriage contract** is **not larger than** the sum recorded in **a marriage contract** required **by Torah** law, **that** payment for **his injury was** assessed to be **four dinars, and because** of a gain of **four dinars,** the husband would **not** be willing to **lose twenty-five** dinars, which is the value of a marriage contract required by Torah law.

The *gemara* then raises a further question: even granting Rabbi Meir's concern, why not allow the wife to sell back only the *tosefet* — the amounts above the biblically-mandated *kesuvah* minimum — to offset the injury debt, while retaining the base *kesuvah de-oraisa* amount of 200 *zuz* (or 100 for a *almana*)? That would preserve the core deterrent while still giving the husband some monetary satisfaction.

The answer is that the scenario we are discussing involves a case where the *kesuvah* does not exceed the *kesuvah de-oraita* at all — only the base biblical amount exists, and the injury was a minor amount (the *gemara* specifies a case of four *zuzim*). Since the wife cannot be required to sell even part of her base *kesuvah* back to her husband — because doing so would eliminate the protective deterrent of the *kesuvah* entirely — and since the injury debt is comparatively small, the debt simply remains outstanding. The wife owes the money, but she is not forced to pay it now at the cost of her *kesuvah*.

## Braisa: Sale & Benin

### Third Party Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁלֹּא תִּמְכּוֹר וְהִיא תַּחְתָּיו, כָּךְ לֹא תַּפְסִיד וְהִיא תַּחְתָּיו; וְהָא זִימְנִין מַשְׁכַּח לַהּ דְּמַפְסְדָא, וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי – כְּגוֹן דִּנְפִישָׁא כְּתוּבְּתַהּ מִכְּתוּבָּה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **that which is taught** in the latter case of the *baraita*: **Just as she does not sell** her marriage contract to obtain money to pay damages **while she is under him,** i.e., married to her husband, **so too, she does not lose** the value of her marriage contract to pay damages **while she is under him. But** there are **times** when **she will be found to have lost** the value of her marriage contract, **and what are the circumstances?** It would be a case **where** the sum recorded in **her marriage contract** is **larger than** the sum recorded in **a marriage contract** required **by Torah law.** In that case she would be obligated to sell the portion of the marriage contract representing the additional amount in order to obtain money with which to compensate the one she injured.

The *gemara* closes with a *braisa* that addresses the parallel question of *ketubas benin dichrin* — the provision in *kesuvah* law by which male children born of the wife inherit her *kesuvah* principal from her husband's estate, thereby preserving her family's wealth for her own sons rather than allowing it to be distributed among all the husband's sons from other wives. The question is whether a wife's sale of her *kesuvah* to a third party forfeits this provision.

The *braisa* rules: *keshem she-lo timkor hi le-acheirim lo hifsidah ketubas benin dichrin* — just as when she sells her *kesuvah* to third parties she does not forfeit *ketubas benin dichrin*. This requires explanation.

### Economic Duress Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַמּוֹכֶרֶת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ לַאֲחֵרִים – לֹא הִפְסִידָה כְּתוּבַּת בְּנִין דִּכְרִין, מַאי טַעְמָא – זוּזֵי הוּא דְּאַנְסוּהָ; כָּךְ מוֹכֶרֶת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ לְבַעְלָהּ – לֹא הִפְסִידָה כְּתוּבַּת בְּנִין דִּכְרִין, מַאי טַעְמָא? זוּזֵי הוּא דְּאַנְסוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this** is what the *baraita* **is teaching: Just as** the *halakha* stipulates **that** a woman **who sells her marriage contract to others does not lose** the future implementation of the stipulation in the **marriage contract** that the **male offspring** inherit payment of their mother's marriage contract, and Rava clarifies: **What is the reason** for this? The need for **money is what** caused **her** to sell it under **duress,** and she did not intend to abrogate her sons' rights to inherit the property in the future. Rava continues his explanation of the *baraita*: **So too,** a woman who **sells her marriage contract to her husband does not lose** the future implementation of the stipulation in the **marriage contract** that the **male offspring** inherit payment of their mother's marriage contract, and Rava clarifies: **What is the reason** for this? The need for **money is what** caused **her** to sell it under **duress.**

When the wife sells her *kesuvah* to a third party, she sells the right to collect in the scenarios where collection occurs during her lifetime — that is, upon divorce or upon her husband's predeceasing her. The buyer acquires only those scenarios. *Ketubas benin dichrin* operates in a completely different scenario: it applies when the wife predeceases her husband and her sons inherit from his estate after his death. Since that scenario was not part of what she sold — the buyer acquires nothing in that case — *ketubas benin dichrin* is entirely unaffected by the sale.

Moreover, the *braisa* adds the rationale: *zuze hu de-antsuha* — she was simply compelled by economic need to sell the *kesuvah*. The sale represents financial duress, not a voluntary relinquishment of all *kesuvah*-related rights. Since she was selling only what she had to in order to raise cash, there is no basis to read into the transaction a surrender of rights unrelated to the sale itself.

### Husband Sale Parallel

The *braisa* then extends the same principle: *kach mokheret kesuvasah le-ba'alah lo hifsidah ketubas benin dichrin* — similarly, if she sells her *kesuvah* back to her husband, she also does not forfeit *ketubas benin dichrin*.

This raises a genuine question. When selling to a third party, the scenario-based logic is clear: the buyer's interest is in cases where the wife survives to collect, whereas *ketubas benin dichrin* operates in the case of her death, a scenario the buyer is indifferent to. But when she sells back to her husband, one might think she is releasing him from all her *kesuvah* claims — including, potentially, the sons' inheritance rights. The *braisa* rules otherwise.

### Benin Dichrin Protected

The same reasoning applies: *zuze hu de-antsuha* — she sold because she was pressed for cash. The sale represents a surrender of what she would personally have collected, not of rights that would belong to her children. The husband, by purchasing, acquires release from the obligation he would have faced toward her personally. He does not acquire, and she does not sell, the independent right of *ketubas benin dichrin* — which is, in any case, not purely her right to begin with but a provision designed to protect her sons.

Rava clarifies that the one case where the wife *would* lose some *kesuvah* rights by selling back to her husband is when the *kesuvah* exceeds the *kesuvah de-oraita* minimum. In that case, the excess amount could be sold back to offset an injury debt owed to the husband, and that portion would be lost. But the base *kesuvah de-oraita* is always protected, as is *ketubas benin dichrin*, which remains intact regardless of any such sale.
