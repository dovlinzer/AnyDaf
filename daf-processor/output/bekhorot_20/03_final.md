# Bekhorot 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Baraita & Miscarriage

### Species & Age Thresholds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֵז בַּת שְׁנָתָהּ — וַדַּאי לַכֹּהֵן, מִיכָּן וּלְהַלָּן — סָפֵק. רָחֵל בַּת שְׁתַּיִם — וַדַּאי לַכֹּהֵן, מִיכָּן וּלְהַלָּן — סָפֵק. פָּרָה בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ — וַדַּאי לַכֹּהֵן, מִיכָּן וּלְהַלָּן — סָפֵק. חֲמוֹרָה — כְּפָרָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חֲמוֹרָה בַּת אַרְבַּע שָׁנִים. עַד כָּאן דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one purchased **a goat within its first year** from a gentile and does not know whether or not it had previously given birth, the subsequent male offspring **certainly** is given **to the priest; from that** point **forward** an offspring's status as a firstborn is **uncertain.** If it was **a ewe within its second year** the offspring **certainly** is given **to the priest; from that** point **forward** an offspring's status as a firstborn is **uncertain.** If it was **a cow within its third year** the offspring **certainly** is given **to the priest; from that** point **forward** an offspring's status as a firstborn is **uncertain.** If it was **a donkey** it is subject to the same *halakha* **as a cow. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** The offspring of **a donkey within its fourth year** also has the status of a firstborn. **Until here is the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

The daf opens five lines from the top of 20a, picking up a *baraita* that revisits and elaborates upon the themes of the *mishnah* concerning when a purchased animal — whose history is unknown — is presumed to be a certain *bechor*, a doubtful *bechor*, or certainly not a *bechor*.

The *baraita* begins: a goat (*ez*) that gives birth within its first year is definitively a *bechor* — *vadai lakohein* — and goes to the *kohen* to be brought as a *korban*. From that point onward, if it gives birth, the status is *safek*, doubtful. The *gemara* had addressed yesterday why we cannot simply assume that a birth after the first year is certainly not a firstborn — there is sufficient basis for doubt, so the *Yisrael* retains the animal but waits for it to develop a *mum* and redeems it at that point. Similarly, a sheep (*rachel*) that gives birth within two years is definitively a *bechor* and goes to the *kohen*; thereafter, it is *safek*. A cow (*parah*) that gives birth within three years is definitively a *bechor*; thereafter, *safek*. And a donkey (*chamora*) follows the same rule as a cow. The visual symmetry of the *baraita* is striking: the words *bat*, *vadai*, and *lakohein* line up in each clause with notable regularity.

### Donkey Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חֲמוֹרָה בַּת אַרְבַּע שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** The offspring of **a donkey within its fourth year** also has the status of a firstborn.

Rabbi Yosi be-Rabbi Yehuda dissents regarding the donkey, holding that the threshold is four years rather than three: *chamora bat arba shanim*. In his view, donkeys do not typically give birth before the beginning of their fourth year, and so a birth anywhere up to that point would still be the first. This in itself is a remarkable position, since one might have thought this was simply a matter of observable animal biology. The *baraita* then notes: *ad kan divrei Rabbi Yishmael* — up to this point is the position of Rabbi Yishmael, who holds that one determines *bechor* status based on the earliest age at which the animal could have a live birth.

### Tinuf Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁנֶּאְמְרוּ דְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אָמַר לָהֶן: צְאוּ אִמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֲאֵל: טָעִיתָ! אִילּוּ בְּוָולָד בִּלְבַד הַבְּהֵמָה נִפְטֶרֶת — הָיָה כִּדְבָרֶיךָ, אֶלָּא סִימַן הַוָּולָד בִּבְהֵמָה דַּקָּה — טִינּוּף, וּבַגַּסָּה — שִׁלְיָא, וּבָאִשָּׁה — שָׁפִיר וְשִׁלְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **When these matters were stated** by the students **before Rabbi Yehoshua, he said to them: Go out** and **say to Rabbi Yishmael: You erred. Were an animal exempted only by** giving birth to **an offspring** and in no other manner the *halakha* would be **in accordance with your statement. But** the Rabbis said: **An indication of the offspring in a small animal** is **a murky discharge** from the womb, which exempts subsequent births from the mitzva of the firstborn. **And** the indication **in a large** animal is the emergence of **an afterbirth, and** the indication **in a woman** is **a fetal sac or an afterbirth.**

When this was reported to Rabbi Yehoshua — notably, the *baraita* does not say simply that he responded, but that when it was reported *in his presence* he sent back a reply — he told those who reported it: go back and tell Rabbi Yishmael, *ta'ita*, you have erred. Rabbi Yehoshua reasons as follows: if an animal were exempt from *bechorah* only through a prior live birth, then you would be correct — a goat born in its first year certainly had no prior live birth, a sheep born in its second year similarly had none, and so on. But the accepted tradition is otherwise. *Siman havelad bibeheima daka tinuf* — for small grazing animals such as goats and sheep, the sign of a prior "birth" sufficient to exempt from *bechorah* is a *tinuf*, a pathological discharge indicating a miscarriage, not a live birth. For large animals such as cows, it is the *shilya* — a placenta — and for a woman, either a *shapir* (fetal sac) or placenta. The point is that an animal need not have had a prior live birth to be exempt; a prior miscarriage suffices. And if that is the case, then even a goat that gives birth in its first year — which is concededly its first live birth — may nonetheless have miscarried earlier, and that prior miscarriage would have already exempted it from *bechorah*. Rabbi Yishmael's confident *vadai lakohein* is therefore premature.

### YH's Reformulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנִי אֵין אֲנִי אוֹמֵר כֵּן, אֶלָּא: עֵז שֶׁטִּינְּפָה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה — יוֹלֶדֶת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנָתָהּ, רָחֵל שֶׁטִּינְּפָה בַּת שְׁנָתָהּ — יוֹלֶדֶת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua added: That is the opinion of the Rabbis, **but I myself do not say so. Rather,** I hold that **a goat that expelled a murky discharge** from the womb **at** the age of **six** months can **give birth within its first year,** and **a ewe that expelled a murky discharge in its first year** can **give birth within its second year.**

Rabbi Yehoshua then adds: *ve-ani ein ani omer kein* — and I personally do not say it exactly as I just reported. The tradition I received holds generally that a prior miscarriage raises doubt about firstborn status. My own *sevara*, however, is more precise about the timing. *Az she-tinfah be-shisha yoledet betoch shnatah* — a goat that miscarried within the first six months of its life could still give birth within the first year. *Rachel she-tinfah bi-shnatah yoledet betoch shtayim* — a sheep that miscarried in its first year could still give birth within the second year. This framing sounds nearly identical to the tradition he just reported, and the *gemara* will analyze carefully what the difference actually is.

### RA's Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אֲנִי לֹא בָּאתִי לִידֵי מִדָּה זוֹ, אֶלָּא: כֹּל שֶׁיָּדוּעַ שֶׁבִּיכְּרָה — אֵין כָּאן לַכֹּהֵן כְּלוּם, וְכֹל שֶׁלֹּא בִּיכְּרָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ לַכֹּהֵן, סָפֵק — יֵאָכֵל בְּמוּמוֹ לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: I have not arrived at this method** of determining firstborn status. **Rather,** in **any** case **where it is known that** the animal **had** previously **given birth, the priest has nothing here. And** in **any** case **where it is known that** the animal **had not** previously **given birth, that** is given **to the priest. And if it is uncertain, it may be eaten in its blemished** state **by the owner.**

Rabbi Akiva then states: *ani lo bati lidei middah zo* — I have not arrived at this level of analysis. He is not engaging in the theoretical discussion about when miscarriages are possible and how they affect presumptions. His position is categorical: if you know for certain that the animal gave birth before — *kol she-yadu'a she-bikra* — then the *kohen* has no claim and you do not even need to wait for a *mum*. If it is certain that it has never given birth, it goes to the *kohen*. And if there is any doubt, *yochal be-mumo la-ba'alim* — the owner waits for a *mum* and eats it then. As we will see, Rabbi Akiva appears to be saying that in the absence of direct, certain observation, the case falls into the doubt category — he refuses to accept the theoretical demographic reasoning of Rabbi Yishmael about what is "certainly" a first birth.

## YI vs YH Debate

### Tinuf Exempts?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ? לֵימָא בְּטִינּוּף פּוֹטֵר קָמִיפַּלְגִי, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר טִינּוּף אֵינוֹ פּוֹטֵר, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר טִינּוּף פּוֹטֵר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **With regard to what** matter **do Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Yehoshua disagree? Shall we say they disagree with regard to** whether or not **a murky discharge** from the womb **exempts** an animal from the mitzva of the firstborn; as **Rabbi Yishmael holds** that **a murky discharge does not exempt** an animal because it is not a sign of a birth, **and Rabbi Yehoshua holds** that **a murky discharge exempts** an animal?

The *gemara* now analyzes the dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Yehoshua. The most obvious explanation is that they disagree about whether *tinuf* — a prior miscarriage — exempts from *bechorah* at all. *Leima be-tinuf poter kamifligi*: perhaps Rabbi Yishmael holds that miscarriage does not exempt, and only a live birth breaks the *bechorah* obligation, while Rabbi Yehoshua holds that miscarriage does exempt. This would make Rabbi Yehoshua's objection straightforward.

### Peter Rechem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דַּחֲזֵינָא דְּטַינִּוף — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּטִינּוּף פּוֹטֵר, וְהָכָא בְּחוֹשְׁשִׁין לְטִינּוּף קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְטִינּוּף, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְטִינּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **If we** actually **see a murky discharge, everyone agrees that** the **murky discharge exempts** an animal. **And here, it is** with regard to whether **one is concerned** about the possibility that an animal might have expelled **a murky discharge that they disagree. Rabbi Yishmael holds** that **we are not concerned about a murky discharge,** and it can therefore be assumed that the first offspring born after its purchase from the gentile is firstborn; **and Rabbi Yehoshua holds** that **one is concerned about a murky discharge,** and due to the uncertainty the next offspring remains with its owner.

The *gemara* immediately rejects this framing. If we had actually witnessed a miscarriage — *de-chazinan de-tanif* — everyone agrees that *tinuf poter*, that miscarriage exempts. The concept of *peter rechem*, the opening of the womb, cannot be limited only to live birth; any prior opening of the womb — including miscarriage — must disqualify the subsequent birth from *bechorah* status. The *gemara* takes this as axiomatic, and so it forces the debate into a more nuanced register. The question between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Yehoshua is not whether miscarriage would exempt in principle, but rather: *ve-hacha be-chosheshin le-tinuf kamifligi* — to what extent must we be concerned that a prior miscarriage *may* have occurred? How much does that theoretical possibility create a *safek*? Everyone agrees that if a goat gave birth in its first year, it is certainly the first live birth. The question is whether the mere possibility of a prior miscarriage is substantial enough to remove that case from the category of *vadai bechor*.

### Consistency Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא חָיֵישׁ? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: מְחַוַּורְתָּא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּחָיֵשׁ לְמִיעוּט!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And** is it correct that Rabbi Yishmael is **not concerned** about the possibility that the mother might have expelled a murky discharge before giving birth to a live offspring? **But doesn't Rava say: It is clear** that **Rabbi Yishmael holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who** says **one must be concerned for the minority?** If so, Rabbi Yishmael should be concerned for a murky discharge as well.

This reframing, however, generates a serious difficulty. Rabbi Yishmael seems to hold that such a possibility is simply not a significant concern — *ein chosheshin le-tinuf*. But this clashes with what was established at the end of yesterday's daf. Rava there concluded — *machvarta Rabbi Yishmael ke-Rabbi Meir svira lei* — that the best explanation of Rabbi Yishmael's position is that he holds like Rabbi Meir, *de-chaish le-miuta*, that he is indeed concerned for minority scenarios. That concern was what explained why, when a goat gives birth in its second year, Rabbi Yishmael treats it as *safek* rather than *vadai* not a *bechor*: since there is a small possibility that it is still the first birth, he does not write it off as certainly not a *bechor*. So Rabbi Yishmael pulls cases from the "certainly not" category into the middle *safek* category because of concern for the minority. By the same logic, he should pull cases from the "certainly is a *bechor*" category into the *safek* category as well, since there is a minority possibility of prior miscarriage. The asymmetry seems incoherent.

### Stringency Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי חָיֵישׁ — לְחוּמְרָא, לְקוּלָּא — לָא חָיֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Where** Rabbi Yishmael **is concerned** is when the concern leads **to a stringency.** But if the concern would lead **to a leniency,** as in this case, where it would mean that the animal born after a year is only an uncertain firstborn, he is **not concerned.**

The *gemara* resolves this with a principle: *ki chaish le-chumra, le-kula lo chaish* — Rabbi Yishmael is concerned for the minority when it leads to *chumra*, to stringency, but not when it leads to *kula*, to leniency. This resolution invites reflection. When a goat gives birth in its second year, the natural conclusion would be that the majority have already given birth before, so this is *vadai* not a *bechor* — and the owner could simply eat it once it has a *mum*. Playing it safe and treating it as *safek* — keeping the animal, not giving it to the *kohen*, waiting for a *mum* — is the *machmir* approach, and that is where Rabbi Yishmael applies his concern for the minority. In the converse case, where the goat gave birth in its first year, the conclusion is that it is *vadai* a *bechor* — give it to the *kohen* and bring it as a *korban*. To say, "but perhaps there was a miscarriage, so it might not be a *bechor*," would be a *kula*, removing it from the category of definite *bechorah* and keeping it from the *kohen* and the altar. That leniency, Rabbi Yishmael does not apply.

It is worth flagging that the *gemara*'s categorization here is somewhat counterintuitive. One could argue that treating the animal as a *safek* rather than a *vadai bechor* is itself a form of *chumra* — after all, if one brings an animal to the altar that turns out not to be *kadosh*, that is *chullin ba'azarah*, a serious transgression. Treating it as *safek* would prevent that possibility. So one could reason that holding it back as a *safek* is actually the safer, more *machmir* path.

The answer lies in understanding the nature of the *chashah le-miuta* principle. According to the *gemara*'s framing — and this connects to a broader discussion in *Tosafot* — concern for the minority is a rabbinic overlay, not a biblical requirement. Biblically, one may and should follow the majority. Rabbinically, we sometimes play it safe. But the motivation for that rabbinic care is always protective *chumra* — caution against transgression. When the majority determination leads to the conclusion that this is a *bechor* and should go to the *kohen*, that is the correct and proper disposition of the animal. Introducing doubt to prevent that outcome is not playing it safe; it is depriving the *kohen* of his due and depriving the altar of the *korban*. The "play it safe" instinct therefore does not operate here, and Rabbi Yishmael gives the animal to the *kohen* without concern for the rare possibility of a prior miscarriage. That is the first resolution.

The *gemara* then offers a second answer: *ve-iba'it eima* — one could alternatively say that Rabbi Yishmael applies concern for the minority symmetrically, in both the *chumra* and *kula* directions. If so, why isn't he concerned about the possibility of miscarriage in the first-year case? This leads to the second reframing of the dispute.

### Rebirth Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא בִּמְטַנֶּפֶת וְחוֹזֶרֶת וְיוֹלֶדֶת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנָתָהּ קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: מְטַנֶּפֶת אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת וְיוֹלֶדֶת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנָתָהּ, וְהָא מִדְּאוֹלִיד — וַדַּאי לָא טַנִּיף. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: מְטַנֶּפֶת חוֹזֶרֶת וְיוֹלֶדֶת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And here they disagree with regard to** whether or not an animal can **expel a murky discharge and** then **return** to its fertile state **and give birth within its first year. As Rabbi Yishmael holds:** An animal that **expels a murky discharge does not return** to its fertile state **and give birth within its first year, and** accordingly, **from** the fact **that this** animal **gave birth** it can be concluded that it **certainly did not expel a murky discharge** beforehand. **And Rabbi Yehoshua holds:** An animal that **expels a murky discharge returns** to its fertile state **and** can **give birth within its first year.**

*Ve-hacha be-mitanefet ve-chozeret ve-yoledet betoch shnatah kamifligi* — the debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Yehoshua may actually concern a different question entirely: is it biologically possible for an animal to miscarry *and* give birth within the same year? Rabbi Yishmael holds: *mitanefet eina chozeret ve-yoledet betoch shnatah* — if an animal has miscarried, it will not go on to give birth within that same year. And therefore, *mi-de-olid vadai lo tanif* — from the very fact that it gave birth, we know with certainty that it did not miscarry this year. The possibility of a prior miscarriage, while real in principle, simply cannot apply in the very year of the observed birth. Rabbi Yehoshua, by contrast, holds *mitanefet chozeret ve-yoledet betoch shnatah* — that an animal can miscarry and then go on to give birth within the same year, and therefore the concern for a prior miscarriage remains real even in the case of the first-year goat birth.

This, then, is the *gemara*'s preferred reframing of the dispute: not a debate over whether miscarriage exempts, not a debate over concern for minority possibilities, but a biological-empirical disagreement about whether miscarriage and live birth can occur within the same gestational year.

## YH's Timing Rules

### Time Windows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין גְּמָרֵיהּ לִסְבָרֵיהּ? שֶׁטִּינְּפָה בְּסוֹף שִׁשָּׁה, וְאִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דִּזְעֵירִי, דְּאָמַר זְעֵירִי: אֵין טִינּוּף פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Since according to both opinions an animal that expelled a murky discharge can still give birth within a year, **what** difference **is there between** Rabbi Yehoshua's **tradition** of the opinion of the Rabbis **and his** own **reasoning?** The Gemara answers: The difference between their opinions is in a case **where** a goat **expelled a murky discharge at the end of** its first **six** months of life, when the seventh month began, and then gave birth before the end of its first year. **And there is** a difference **between** them with regard to the statement **of Ze'eiri, as Ze'eiri says:** The expulsion of **a murky discharge,** which is sufficient to exempt an animal from having its future offspring counted a firstborn, prevents it from being impregnated for **no less than thirty days.**

Having established the broad contours of the dispute, the *gemara* now turns to Rabbi Yehoshua's own personal position — his *sevara* — which is distinct from the tradition he reported. His formulation: *az she-tinfah ba-shisha yoledet betoch shnatah* — a goat that miscarried within the first six months of its life can still give birth within the first year; *rachel she-tinfah bi-shnatah yoledet betoch shtayim* — a sheep that miscarried within its first year can give birth within the second year. This sounds very similar to the general tradition, except that Rabbi Yehoshua's own formulation adds a precise timing constraint — the miscarriage must have occurred within the first six months (for goats) or the first full year (for sheep) for the subsequent birth to fall within the relevant threshold period.

### Month Seven Case

The *nafka mina* between Rabbi Yehoshua's received tradition and his own *sevara* emerges in the following case: an animal that miscarried at the end of the sixth month — *she-tinfah be-sof shisha* — and then gave birth within the first year. The tradition simply says: be concerned about prior miscarriage. But Rabbi Yehoshua's own *sevara* specifies that the miscarriage must have occurred *within* the first six months for the scenario to work. If it miscarried at the end of the sixth month, was a subsequent birth in the first year actually possible?

### Ze'iri Principle

This is where Ze'iri's principle enters. Ze'iri rules: *ein tinuf pachot mi-shloshim yom* — after a miscarriage, an animal does not conceive (and in some formulations, does not copulate) for thirty days. Consider the implications for our case. If the goat miscarried at the beginning of the seventh month, it cannot conceive until the beginning of the eighth month. Goats require five months of gestation. So the earliest it could give birth would be the beginning of the thirteenth month — the second year. Therefore, if we hold like Ze'iri, a birth within the first year is impossible given a seventh-month miscarriage. The *nafka mina* is for a case where we have in fact been observing the animal: we see it miscarry late in the sixth month or early in the seventh, and then we see it give birth within the first year. How do we interpret what we saw?

### Gamara Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִגְמָרֵיהּ — אִית לֵיהּ דִּזְעֵירִי, לִסְבָרֵיהּ — לֵית לֵיהּ דִּזְעֵירִי.
>
> **Translation:** In this case, as the pregnancy of a goat lasts five months and a murky discharge was expelled at the end of the sixth month, a goat that gave birth by the end of the year, i.e., twelve months, must have become pregnant within a month of the discharge. The Rabbis, whose opinion Rabbi Yehoshua cited by **tradition, accept** the statement **of Ze'eiri,** and therefore they rule that this goat, which became pregnant within a month of the discharge, is not exempt from the mitzva of the firstborn. **According to** Rabbi Yehoshua's own **reasoning, he does not accept** the statement **of Ze'eiri,** which means that although this animal became pregnant within a month of the discharge, it was nevertheless exempt from the mitzva of the firstborn.

The *gemara* draws a distinction between Rabbi Yehoshua's received tradition and his own *sevara* with respect to Ze'iri's principle. *Le-Gemara it lei de-Ze'iri* — according to his received tradition, they hold of Ze'iri's thirty-day rule. *Le-svara leit lei de-Ze'iri* — according to his own *sevara*, he does not hold of Ze'iri's rule.

The practical consequence: if we hold like Ze'iri, and we observed this goat miscarry late in the seventh month and then give birth within the first year, the mathematics make that biologically impossible if the miscarriage was genuine. Therefore, we re-read the observed facts: what we thought was a miscarriage was not a real miscarriage, and the birth within the first year is the genuine first birth — *chayav be-bechor*. That is the application of Ze'iri under the received tradition. If, however, we do not hold like Ze'iri — Rabbi Yehoshua's own *sevara* — then we accept the miscarriage as genuine and treat this birth as not a *bechor*.

The *gemara* then raises a second formulation of the same *nafka mina*: *ve-iba'it eima de-kulei alma it lehu de-Ze'iri* — one could say that everyone holds of Ze'iri's principle, and the dispute between the received tradition and the *sevara* is instead about *yoledes le-mikutain* — whether an animal can give birth within a partial month, i.e., whether the five-month gestation period must be five complete months. According to the received tradition, partial months do not count and the animal cannot give birth within the fifth month. According to his own *sevara*, it can. This too alters the arithmetic of the seventh-month miscarriage case. If we require five full months and Ze'iri requires a thirty-day wait, then a seventh-month miscarriage cannot produce a first-year birth; if partial months count, perhaps it can.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לָא אָמְרִינַן יוֹלֶדֶת, וְהָכָא בְּמִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי. לִסְבָרֵיהּ — אָמְרִינַן מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ, לִגְמָרֵיהּ — לָא אָמְרִינַן מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** that everyone accepts Ze'eiri's statement and **we do not say** that an animal **gives birth** prematurely, **and here, they disagree** with regard to whether or not the halakhic status of **part of the day is like an entire** day: According **to** Rabbi Yehoshua's **reasoning we say part of the day is like an entire** day, and therefore it is possible for the goat to have become pregnant on the thirtieth day after experiencing the discharge and to give birth precisely five months later, just before the year ends. According **to** his **tradition** of the opinion of the Rabbis, **we do not say part of the day is like an entire** day, which means that the earliest possible birth is on the first day of the second year.

The *gemara* adds yet a third variation: *iba'it eima* — perhaps the dispute concerns the principle of *miktzat ha-yom ke-kulo*, the halakhic rule that a partial day counts as a full day. If, for instance, the animal miscarried one day into the seventh month, waited the Ze'iri-mandated thirty days, then began a five-month gestation, it could theoretically give birth on the last day of the first year. Does that last day count as a full fifth month of gestation? According to the received tradition, *lo amrinan miktzat ha-yom ke-kulo* for this purpose, so the scenario is impossible and the observed "miscarriage" must have been spurious. According to the *sevara*, *amrinan miktzat ha-yom ke-kulo*, the last day counts, so the miscarriage was genuine and the animal is not a *bechor*.

The underlying logic threading through all these formulations is the same: we are watching an animal whose behavior we have observed, and we are using our biological-halakhic principles to re-read the observed facts. If the scenario we witnessed is physically impossible given our principles, we reinterpret the evidence accordingly. The basic *pshat* of the received tradition and the personal *sevara* converge on the same essential concern — a prior miscarriage creates doubt about *bechorah* status — but diverge in the precise biological constraints that govern whether such a miscarriage-followed-by-birth scenario is even possible in the relevant time window.

## RA & Lactation

### Chalav Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִסּוּרָא: חָלָב פּוֹטֵר אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: חָלָב פּוֹטֵר, הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב בְּהֵמוֹת, וְרוֹב בְּהֵמוֹת אֵין חוֹלְבוֹת אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יוֹלְדוֹת, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: הָא אִיכָּא מִיעוּטָא דְּחוֹלְבוֹת אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין יוֹלְדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina of Sura says:** There is a difference **between them** in a case where the animal came into the Jew's possession after it had already started to produce milk. They differ as to whether or not the production of **milk is** sufficient to **exempt** an animal from having its next offspring counted a firstborn. **Rabbi Akiva holds: Milk exempts** it, as we **follow the majority of animals, and the majority of animals do not produce milk unless they** have **given birth. And Rabbi Yehoshua holds:** Since **there is a minority** of animals **that** do **produce milk even though they** have **not given birth,** the animal that is born later is an uncertain firstborn.

With the question of Rabbi Yehoshua's precise position clarified, the *gemara* turns to the dispute between Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Akiva, and the question of what would constitute a *vadai* not-*bechor* at the other end of the spectrum. Rabbi Chanina mi-Sura identifies the fulcrum: *chalav poter ika beinaihu* — the question that separates them is whether lactation exempts an animal from *bechorah* status. The scenario is as follows: the animal has not been observed to have given birth before, but it is visibly lactating when it delivers this offspring. Does lactation constitute sufficient evidence of a prior birth to classify this newborn as definitively not a *bechor*, not requiring even waiting for a *mum*?

Intuitively, one would expect lactation to be powerful evidence of prior birth. The question is whether the rare possibility of hormonal lactation without prior birth is substantial enough to generate a *safek*.

### Initial Proposal

The *gemara*'s initial attempt to frame the debate assigns the positions as one might expect: Rabbi Akiva holds *chalav poter* — lactation exempts, we go by the majority of animals that do not lactate unless they have given birth, *rov behemos ein cholvo ela im kein yaldu* — and therefore the lactating animal is presumed to have given birth before, and this newborn is *vadai* not a *bechor*. Rabbi Yehoshua, in this initial framing, holds that there is a *miut* — a minority of animals that lactate without prior birth — and therefore the case is *safek*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי חָיֵישׁ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְמִיעוּט? וְהָתְנַן: הָיְתָה לָהּ חֲמוֹת — אֵינָהּ חוֹשֶׁשֶׁת, יָצְאָה מְלֵיאָה — חוֹשֶׁשֶׁת. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ חוֹשֶׁשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is Rabbi Yehoshua concerned for a minority? Didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Yevamot* 119a): If a woman's husband passed away before she had given birth to any children and the husband had no known brothers who could perform levirate marriage, even though **she has a mother-in-law** who traveled overseas and may have conceivably given birth to a male who could later perform levirate marriage, **she does not** need **to be concerned** for that possibility and may marry another man without finding out if a male child had been born. But if the mother-in-law **left** while she was **full,** i.e., pregnant, the daughter-in-law must **be concerned** that a male might have been born. **Rabbi Yehoshua says: She does not** need **to be concerned.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this assignment. *U-mi chaish Rabbi Yehoshua le-miuta?* Is Rabbi Yehoshua really the one who is concerned for a minority? The *gemara* cites an explicit *mishnah*: a widow whose mother-in-law was pregnant when she last saw her does not need to be concerned that she has a brother-in-law she is unaware of before remarrying — and Rabbi Yehoshua explicitly rules *einah chosheshes*, she need not be concerned. The arithmetic there is striking: roughly half of pregnancies end in miscarriage, and of live births roughly half are male. The probability of a living brother-in-law is perhaps 45% — and yet Rabbi Yehoshua dismisses even this as a *miut* beneath concern. If he ignores a 45% possibility there, he can hardly be the one insisting on concern for the rare lactation-without-birth case here.

### Position Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵיפוֹךְ, וְהָתַנְיָא: חָלָב פּוֹטֵר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: חָלָב אֵינוֹ פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, reverse** the two opinions in the mishna. **And** in fact **it is taught** in that manner in a *baraita*: The production of **milk exempts** an animal from having its offspring counted a firstborn; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Akiva says:** The production of **milk does not exempt** an animal from having its offspring counted a firstborn.

The *gemara* therefore reverses the attribution: *ela ippoich* — flip the positions. Rabbi Yehoshua holds *chalav poter* — lactation exempts; the animal is *vadai* not a *bechor*, and Rabbi Yehoshua is not concerned with the minority possibility of lactation without prior birth. Rabbi Akiva holds that lactation does not suffice to definitively exempt — even this produces only a *safek*, and one must wait for a *mum*. This, notably, fits Rabbi Akiva's general stance perfectly. His categorical position from the *baraita* was that anything short of absolute certain knowledge falls into the *safek* category. Here too, even the presence of lactation does not constitute the kind of absolute certainty Rabbi Akiva requires, so he treats it as *safek*. And the reversal is confirmed by an explicit *baraita*: *chalav poter divrei Rabbi Yehoshua, Rabbi Akiva omer chalav eino poter* — it is stated plainly in the *baraita*.

### Tosafot Resolution

This reversal, however, creates a new difficulty. If Rabbi Yehoshua is not concerned for the minority — not even a 45% possibility — why was he concerned earlier about the minority possibility that an animal giving birth in its first year had previously miscarried? There, his objection to Rabbi Yishmael was precisely that miscarriage might have occurred. That minority concern led him to treat first-year births as *safek*. But here he dismisses a majority-range concern with respect to lactation. The inconsistency is pointed.

*Tosafot* addresses this directly, and the resolution is conceptually significant. Rabbi Yehoshua, *Tosafot* explains, is genuinely not concerned for the minority when it comes to *issur ve-heter* — the domain of religious prohibition and permission. He would let a woman remarry, ignoring a 45% risk. In principle, he would also be willing to treat a first-year birth as a *vadai bechor* brought to the altar, ignoring the minority chance of prior miscarriage. That, from the *issur* perspective, is entirely coherent with his position on lactation.

Why then does Rabbi Yehoshua object to Rabbi Yishmael? Not because he is *chaish le-miuta* in the *issur* domain, but because of a different consideration entirely: *dinei mammonot*. When the *kohen* comes to claim the animal, the governing principle is *ha-motzi me-chaveiro alav ha-re'ayah* — the burden of proof falls on the one seeking to extract an object from another's possession. In *dinei mammonot*, the standard of proof required to transfer property is higher than the standard for *issur* determinations. Even a minority possibility that there was a prior miscarriage means the *kohen* cannot definitively prove his claim, and the *Yisrael* is entitled to retain the animal.

This bifurcation generates a tension of its own, which *Tosafot* himself flags: *u-mihu hi ktzat teima* — this is somewhat difficult. Can the same animal simultaneously have two different legal statuses? From the *issur* perspective — questions of whether one may shear it, work it, or bring it to the altar — one ought to treat it as a *vadai bechor*, and misusing it would theoretically incur lashes. From the *dinei mammonot* perspective, the *kohen* cannot extract it. *Tosafot* raises the possibility, without fully resolving it, that the *dinei mammonot* determination might actually govern the *issur* question as well — since the *kohen* cannot prove his claim, one holds on to the animal and treats it as *safek bechor*, waiting for a *mum*, and even the *issur* dimension is then shaped by that outcome. *Tosafot* on 68b formulates the tension explicitly: *le-inyan malkot be-gizah va-avodah u-le-inyan lehakrivo al ha-mizbe'ach chashiv vadai bechor* — for purposes of lashes if one shears or works it, and for purposes of bringing it to the altar, it has the status of *vadai bechor* — *de-azlinan basar ruba ve-chazakah*; but *le-inyan litno la-kohen yechasheiv safek*, for the purpose of the *kohen* extracting it, it is treated as doubtful. Whether these two determinations can coexist without one contaminating the other is precisely the *teima* that *Tosafot* acknowledges without definitively answering.

This is not merely a technical question in laws of *bechorah*. It reflects a broader and genuinely important conceptual issue: when the same fact-pattern is governed by two different halakhic frameworks with different evidentiary standards, can they yield different results, and if so, does one framework's conclusion constrain the other? The practical analogy is a debt dispute where *dinei mammonot* says one need not pay — yet one wonders whether one incurs *safek gezel* by withholding. There, we say the civil framework governs the *issur* of ownership; but in *bechorah*, the *issur* and the monetary claim are truly distinct categories, and it is less obvious that the civil outcome should determine the *issur* status.

## Multi-Gen Baraita

### Scenario Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גְּדִיָּיה שֶׁיָּלְדָה שָׁלֹשׁ בָּנוֹת, וְכׇל בְּנוֹתֶיהָ יָלְדוּ שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁלֹשׁ — כּוּלָּן נִכְנָסוֹת לַדִּיר לְהִתְעַשֵּׂר.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a kid that gave birth to female triplets and all her offspring each gave birth to female triplets, all of them,** i.e., the offspring and their offspring, **enter the pen to be tithed.** The animal tithe applies only if one owns at least ten or more animals born in the same year that are not male firstborns.

The *gemara* introduces a new *baraita*: *tanu rabbanan: gedya she-yalda shalosh banos, ve-chol bnoseiha holidu — bnoseiha yaldu shalosh shalosh* — a doe-goat that gave birth to three daughters, and all of its daughters gave birth — each daughter gave birth to three more — all within one year. Since goats have a roughly five-month gestation cycle, it is biologically possible for a goat to become a grandmother within the first year. The conclusion: *kulan nichnasos la-dir le-hit'aser* — all of them enter the fold to be tithed under *ma'aser behemah*, the animal tithe applied to all animals born within a single year. The arithmetic yields twelve animals: three from the first generation of daughters, and nine from the second (three times three).

### Why Three-Three?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֲנִי רָאִיתִי שֶׁעִישְּׂרָה בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנָתָהּ. לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵי שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁלֹשׁ? תּוֹלֵיד חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ תְּלָת, וְאִינָךְ תַּרְתֵּי תַּרְתֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: I saw** a single kid **that** yielded enough offspring to be subject to the **tithe within its first year** of life. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the *baraita* **to teach** that **each** offspring gave birth to **three** other offspring? Let **one of them give birth to three, and** let **the other** two give birth to **two each,** so that there are a total of ten goats born within a single year, which are therefore subject to the tithe.

The *gemara* questions the choice of scenario. Why specify that each daughter had three offspring? *Tolid chada mineihu tlas ve-inach tarti tarti* — you could achieve ten animals (the minimum needed for *ma'aser*) with one daughter having three and the other two each having two: three plus five plus seven gives ten total, including the original mother — though one counts the mother? Actually: three daughters, then one has three and two have two each = three plus three plus two plus two = ten. The *gemara* answers: *aidi de-ika chada de-lo sagya bi-lo shalosh* — since at least one of them would need to have three, the *tanna* stated that all of them had three, for the sake of uniformity in presentation.

The *gemara* presses further: why do we need any of the daughters having three? *Lei-havu kulhu tarti* — if all three daughters each had only two, that gives three plus six = nine, and then *ve-teihadar ee ve-tolid bahadeihu* — the mother herself gives birth a second time, producing a tenth animal along with the grandchildren. That would require only two births per daughter and one additional birth by the original mother. Why didn't the *baraita* employ that simpler scenario?

### No Double Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא קָסָבַר: מְטַנֶּפֶת אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת וְיוֹלֶדֶת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנָתָהּ. אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר: מְטַנֶּפֶת חוֹזֶרֶת וְיוֹלֶדֶת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנָתָהּ — יוֹלֶדֶת וַדַּאי אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת וְיוֹלֶדֶת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shall we say** the *tanna* of this *baraita* **holds** that an animal that **expels a murky discharge does not return** to its fertile state **and give birth within its first year?** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: It is possible the *tanna* does not hold that opinion. Even **if you say** an animal that **expels a murky discharge returns** to its fertile state **and gives birth within its first year,** an animal **that** actually **gave birth** to a live offspring **certainly does not return** to its fertile state **and give birth within its first year.**

The *gemara* draws an implicit inference: *leima kasavar* — perhaps the *baraita* implicitly holds that *yoledes vadai eina chozeret ve-yoledet betoch shnatah* — an animal that has had a live birth does not give birth again within the same year. The *baraita* did not employ the scenario of the mother birthing twice because the *tanna* assumed that a second live birth within the same year simply does not occur.

The *gemara* is careful not to overstate: *im timtza lomar metanefet chozeret ve-yoledet betoch shnatah* — one might still argue that after a *miscarriage* an animal can go on to give birth within the same year; that remains a live debate. But *aval yoledes vadai eina chozeret ve-yoledet betoch shnatah* — after a full, successful live birth, a second live birth within the same year does not occur. This is not the explicit subject of the *baraita*, but it is the implicit biological assumption that explains why the *tanna* chose the three-by-three scenario over the simpler double-birth-by-the-mother scenario. The *baraita* will be examined further in the next *daf*.
