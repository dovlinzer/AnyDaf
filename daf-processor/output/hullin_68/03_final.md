# Hullin 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ben Pakua & Shechita

### Opening Remarks

We begin the fourth *perek* of Masechet Hullin, Daf 68. The previous *perek*, *eilu treifos*, was a major accomplishment — spanning some thirty *daf* and covering critical topics in *treifos*, including highly challenging material involving biology and anatomy, as well as questions of which species are *kosher*. All of that was directly and practically relevant to everyday *kashrus*. This fourth *perek*, by contrast, is occupied with more theoretical discussions and interesting conceptual questions before the tractate returns to more practical concerns.

### Ben Pakua Concept

The *perek* opens with a scenario involving *shechita* in the field: one slaughters an animal and wishes to eat its meat, but must consider the status of a potential *ubbar* — a fetus — found in the mother's womb at the time of *shechita*. This fetus is called the *ben pakua*, and the governing principle is that the *shechita* of the mother works for the *ubbar*, because until the fetus is born it is considered part of the mother. However, as the opening *mishna* will make clear, this is not entirely equivalent to any other limb of the mother. The *perek* therefore addresses a range of questions: at what stage is the fetus considered to have been born, such that the mother's *shechita* no longer covers it?

### Mishna: Limb Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּהֵמָה הַמְקַשָּׁה לֵילֵד, וְהוֹצִיא הָעוּבָּר אֶת יָדוֹ וְהֶחְזִירוֹ – מוּתָּר בַּאֲכִילָה. הוֹצִיא אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה כְּיָלוּד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: When a pregnant kosher animal is slaughtered, the slaughter also renders the consumption of its fetus permitted. Even if **an animal was encountering difficulty giving birth and** meanwhile **the fetus extended its foreleg** outside the mother animal's womb **and** then **brought it back** inside, and then the mother animal was slaughtered, the **consumption** of the fetus is **permitted** by virtue of the slaughter of the mother animal. But if the fetus **extended its head** outside the womb, **even if it** then **brought it back** inside, the halakhic status of **that** fetus is **like** that of **a newborn,** and the slaughter of the mother animal does not permit the consumption of the fetus. Rather, it requires its own slaughter.

The *mishna* here leaves open what precisely is permissible. The obvious implication is that the fetus itself may be eaten — but does the word *mutar* extend also to the limb that briefly protruded? This question will occupy the *gemara* at length.

### Mishna: Head Born

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹתֵךְ מֵעוּבָּר שֶׁבְּמֵעֶיהָ – מוּתָּר בַּאֲכִילָה, מִן הַטְּחוֹל וּמִן הַכְּלָיוֹת – אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה. זֶה הַכְּלָל: דָּבָר שֶׁגּוּפָהּ – אָסוּר, וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ גּוּפָהּ – מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** If, prior to slaughtering an animal, one **severs** pieces **from a fetus that is in** the **womb** and leaves those pieces in the womb, their **consumption** is **permitted** by virtue of the slaughter of the mother animal. By contrast, if one severs pieces **of the spleen or of the kidneys** of an animal and then slaughters it, then even if those pieces are left inside the animal their **consumption** is **prohibited,** because an organ severed from a living being is not permitted by the subsequent slaughter of the animal. **This is the principle: An item that is** part of an animal's **body** that was severed prior to its slaughter is **prohibited** even after slaughter, **and** an item **that is not** part of **its body,** i.e., its fetus, is **permitted** by virtue of its slaughter.

The *mishna* here leaves open what precisely is permissible. The obvious implication is that the fetus itself may be eaten — but does the word *mutar* extend also to the limb that briefly protruded? This question will occupy the *gemara* at length.

The *mishna* opens: *behema hamaksha leiled* — an animal experiencing difficult labor, meaning it is actively in the process of giving birth. *hotzi ha-ubbar yado vehechzira* — the fetus extended a limb, literally its arm, and then pulled it back in. After this, the mother is slaughtered. *mutar baachila* — the result is permissible to eat.

The *mishna* continues: *hotzi et rosho* — if the fetus extended its head out of the womb, *af al pi shehecheziro* — even if it was then pulled back in, *harei zeh k'yalud* — it is considered as though it has already been born. In that case, the mother's *shechita* does not cover the fetus.

The working assumption is that this applies when the majority of the head (*rubbo*) has emerged. The *mishna*'s primary focus here is establishing the criterion for birth: not the extension of a limb, but the emergence of the head — and, critically, once the head has emerged, even if it is pulled back, that status of birth is irrevocable.

### Fetus vs. Spleen

The *mishna* then introduces an illuminating contrast. *chotech me-ubbar shebim'eha* — if someone dismembered the fetus while it was still inside the mother's womb and then slaughtered the mother, the *shechita* of the mother still works and the pieces of the fetus are permitted. The fetus's dismembered parts remain within the animal, and the *shechita* covers them.

However, *min hat'chol umin haklayos* — if a person reached into a living animal and detached the spleen or the kidneys — and this case uses specifically the spleen and kidneys because, as established in the previous *perek*, detaching them does not render the animal a *treifa* — those organs, now disconnected and sitting in the body cavity, become *asur baachila*. Slaughtering the animal afterward does not render the spleen and kidneys permissible.

### Fetus Not True Limb

We thus have a striking contrast: a dismembered fetus inside the mother is covered by the mother's *shechita*, while a detached organ of the mother itself is not. The reason lies in the nature of the prohibition of *ever min hachai* — a limb severed from a living animal. When an intrinsic organ such as a spleen or kidney is cut from a living animal, that organ immediately acquires the status of *ever min hachai*, a detached limb from a living creature. Subsequent *shechita* of the mother cannot retroactively undo that status.

The fetus, though we speak of it as *ubbar yerech imo* — as though it were a limb of the mother — is not truly and intrinsically a limb of the mother. It has its own independent vitality, its own developing existence, merely sustained within the mother. Therefore, dismembering the fetus within the womb does not constitute severing a limb from the mother in the halachic sense, does not create *ever min hachai*, and the *shechita* of the mother continues to cover what remains within her.

The *mishna* closes with a *klal*: *davar shegufah asur* — something that is genuinely part of the animal's own body, when severed while the animal is alive, is forbidden as *ever min hachai*. *veshe'eino gufah mutar* — something that is not literally a part of the mother, such as the fetus, is permissible, because the mother's *shechita* still works for it. This closing principle elegantly encapsulates the entire *mishna*: the *shechita* of the mother operates on behalf of the fetus, but we are not asserting that the fetus is in every respect identical to a limb of the mother.

## Rav Yehuda's Ruling

### Limb Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: וְאֵבֶר עַצְמוֹ אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara qualifies the first ruling of the mishna: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: But** as for **the limb itself,** i.e., the foreleg, its consumption is **prohibited,** even though the fetus brought it back inside prior to the slaughter.

This is an entirely new idea, not found in the *mishna* at all. Rav asserts that when the fetus extends a limb out of the womb, the act of that limb's protrusion renders it forbidden — and it remains forbidden even after it is pulled back in.

This is striking. One might naturally have assumed that while the limb is protruding, the mother's *shechita* simply cannot reach it — it is outside the womb and therefore outside the domain of the *shechita*. But one would also have assumed that if the limb is pulled back in before *shechita*, everything returns to normal: the fetus and all its limbs are now inside the mother, and the *shechita* covers them. Rav rejects this. He holds that the limb, once it has gone out, remains forbidden — not only will the mother's *shechita* fail to cover it, but even if the fetus were subsequently born alive and then slaughtered in its own right, this particular limb would remain forbidden. It has acquired a permanent prohibited status from the moment of its protrusion.

### Basar Basadeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבָשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּצָא בָּשָׂר חוּץ לִמְחִיצָתוֹ – נֶאֱסָר.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this? It is **as the verse states: "And flesh, in the field, a *tereifa*, you shall not eat"** (Exodus 22:30). A *tereifa* is an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months; its consumption is prohibited even if it is ritually slaughtered. The Gemara interprets the verse as teaching a principle: **Once flesh** whose permitted status is dependent on being within a certain area, e.g., sacrificial meat within the Temple courtyard, **has gone outside of its boundary,** i.e., the area in which it is permitted, which the verse describes as being "in the field," **it becomes** permanently **prohibited,** like a *tereifa*. Likewise, the permitted status of a fetus is dependent on its being within the womb when the mother animal is slaughtered. Accordingly, if any part of the fetus leaves the womb before the slaughter, it is rendered permanently prohibited.

Where does this principle come from? The *gemara* cites a *pasuk*: *u'vasar basadeh tereifah lo tocheilu* — "and meat of a field that is torn, you shall not eat." Ordinarily, halachic attention falls on the word *tereifah* and the word *basadeh* is passed over. But here the *gemara* draws attention to *basadeh*: *keivan sheyatza basar chutz limechitzato* — once meat goes out beyond its rightful boundaries, like meat exposed out in the field, *ne'esar* — it becomes forbidden. The word *basadeh*, "in the field," is understood as representing the idea of being outside one's zone, beyond one's proper domain.

### Sacrificial Meat

This principle finds its most natural application in the laws of *korbanot*. *kodshei kodashim* — sanctified offerings of the highest degree, such as a *chatat* — may be eaten only within the Temple precincts proper, not even on the *har haBayit* but within the *beis haMikdash* itself. *kodashim kalim* — lesser sanctified offerings, such as *shelamim* — may be eaten throughout Jerusalem. If the meat of a *shelamim* is taken outside Jerusalem, or the meat of a *chatat* is taken outside the *beis haMikdash*, one might wonder: can it simply be brought back and eaten? The answer derived from this *pasuk* is no. Once the meat has left its permitted zone, it becomes forbidden — the sanctity has been, in some sense, violated by its displacement.

### Field Metaphor

This logic is now applied by Rav to the fetus. The proper zone for the fetus — the zone which allows the mother's *shechita* to function on its behalf — is the mother's womb. When a limb protrudes, it is, from the perspective of the *shechita*'s ultimate effect, out of bounds. If the animal is eventually slaughtered and the fetus is to be permitted via the mother's *shechita*, then the relevant domain is the inside of the womb. A limb that has gone outside that domain has, in the framework of *basar basadeh tereifah*, stepped out of bounds. And as Rav frames it: once out of bounds, one cannot return to the play. Even if the limb is pulled back in, it remains forever stamped with the status it acquired when it was outside.

This is a genuine *chiddush*, and admittedly a conceptually unusual one. The idea that a piece of the fetus, which was not yet *ever min hachai* and which is now physically back inside the mother, nonetheless carries a permanent prohibition — this requires the full force of the *basar basadeh* derivation to sustain it.

## Gemara Challenges

### What Is Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: בְּהֵמָה הַמְקַשָּׁה לֵילֵד, וְהוֹצִיא הָעוּבָּר אֶת יָדוֹ וְהֶחְזִירוֹ – מוּתָּר בַּאֲכִילָה. מַאי לָאו אַאֵבֶר? לָא, אַעוּבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **We learned** in the mishna: If **an animal was encountering difficulty giving birth and** as a result **the fetus extended its foreleg** outside the mother animal's body **but** then **brought it back,** and then the animal was slaughtered, the **consumption** of the fetus is **permitted** by virtue of the slaughter of the mother animal. The Gemara assumes: **What,** is the mishna **not referring to** the entire fetus, including **the limb,** i.e., the foreleg, when it states that its consumption is permitted by the slaughter? This would contradict Rav's ruling. The Gemara answers: **No,** the mishna is **referring to** the rest of the **fetus,** apart from the foreleg.

The *gemara* opens with a statement attributed to Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *v'ever atzmo asur* — the limb itself is forbidden. The *gemara* now turns to examine the *mishna* itself as a potential challenge to Rav's position. The *mishna* says: the fetus extends its limb and pulls it back, the mother is slaughtered — *mutar baachila*. The *gemara* asks: *mai lav eivar* — does this not mean the limb itself becomes permissible? If so, that directly contradicts Rav, who holds the limb remains forbidden. The *gemara* responds: *lo ha-ubbar* — no, the *mutar* refers to the fetus, not the limb. The *mishna* is teaching that the extension of a limb does not constitute a birth event, so the fetus remains covered by the mother's *shechita*.

### Mishna's Point

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אַעוּבָּר, מַאי אִירְיָא הֶחְזִירוֹ? אֲפִילּוּ לֹא הֶחְזִירוֹ נָמֵי! הוּא הַדִּין אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא הֶחְזִירוֹ. וְאַיְּידֵי דְּקָא בָּעֵי מִיתְנֵא סֵיפָא: הוֹצִיא אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה כְּיָלוּד, תְּנָא נָמֵי רֵישָׁא הֶחְזִירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the mishna is **referring to** the rest of the **fetus, why** state **specifically** that the fetus **brought back** its foreleg? **Even** if it did **not bring it back** the rest of the fetus would still be permitted. The Gemara answers: **The same is true,** that the rest of the fetus is permitted, **even if** it **did not bring back** its foreleg. **But since** the *tanna* of the mishna **wants to teach** in **the latter clause:** If the fetus **extended its head, even though it brought it back,** the halakhic status of **that** fetus is **like** that of **a newborn** and is permitted only through its own slaughter, therefore **he also taught** in **the first clause** that the fetus **brought back** its foreleg, for stylistic reasons, despite the fact that the ruling is not limited to that case.

The *gemara* presses further. If the only point is that the fetus is permissible, *mai irya dehachziro* — why does the *mishna* specify that it pulled the limb back? The conclusion that the fetus is not yet born would hold even while the limb is still extended. The mention of the retraction seems purposeful.

The *gemara* grants the point: *hu hadin af al gav delo hechzir* — yes, even without pulling the limb back, the fetus would not be considered born. The reason the *mishna* mentions the retraction is structural: since the *mishna* wants to conclude with the case of the head being extended and pulled back — and there, the pulling back is the pivot of the law, because once the head emerges the fetus is considered born even if pulled back — the *mishna* mirrors the language in its opening clause as well, mentioning the retraction of the limb to parallel the retraction of the head. The *mishna*'s real *chiddush* lies in the closing case, not in the opening one. The *gemara* therefore maintains that the *mishna* teaches nothing about whether the limb is permitted or forbidden; it is exclusively focused on the threshold of birth.

### Birth Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קָמַשְׁמַע לַן סֵיפָא: הוֹצִיא אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה כְּיָלוּד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: *And what is the mishna actually teaching in its closing section, that when the head emerges the fetus is considered born?

The *gemara* continues: *umai kamashma lan seifa* — so what is the *mishna* actually teaching in its closing section, that when the head emerges the fetus is considered born? The *gemara* responds that this already appears to be known from elsewhere, and it should not be necessary to teach it again. The source cited is from *masechet bechoros*: *eizehu bechor lenachalah ve'eino bechor lekohanim* — what constitutes a firstborn for the purposes of inheritance but not for the purposes of *pidyon haBen*?

To understand this, one must distinguish between the two aspects of *bechora*. The double portion in inheritance goes to the father's firstborn viable son — the first *ben kayama*, the first child who actually survived and was considered viable. The *pidyon haBen* obligation, by contrast, is triggered by *peter rechem*, the first breaching of the womb, regardless of viability. A child born after miscarriages or stillbirths is thus a firstborn for inheritance — he is the first viable child — but not a firstborn for *pidyon haBen*, because the womb was already breached.

The *gemara* adds the case of an eighth-month child — a child born after only eight months of gestation. In the *gemara*'s framework, such a child was considered non-viable regardless of whether it appeared to be living at birth; only a nine-month child was presumed viable. Such a child would count for *pidyon haBen* purposes as having breached the womb, but would not count as the viable firstborn for inheritance. The *mishna* in *bechoros* therefore states: *af al pi sheyatza rosho chai* — even if this non-viable child came out alive, or *ben tesha sheyatza rosho met* — a fully gestated child that was stillborn — in either case, the subsequent child is the firstborn for inheritance but not for *pidyon haBen*.

From this *mishna* the *gemara* infers: *ta'ama derosho met* — the reason the stillborn child did not count for inheritance is because when its head came out it was already dead. The implication is that *ha rosho chai* — had its head emerged alive, it would have been considered born and viable for the inheritance calculation, and the following child would not have received the double portion. This inference establishes that birth is reckoned from the emergence of the head.

### Human vs. Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָדָם מִבְּהֵמָה לָא יָלֵיף, דְּאֵין פְּרוֹזְדוֹר לִבְהֵמָה, וּבְהֵמָה מֵאָדָם לָא יָלְפָא, דַּחֲשִׁיב פַּרְצוּף פָּנִים דִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Before explaining the problem with this suggestion, the Gemara explains why it is necessary to teach the definition with regard to both people and animals: **Because** the definition of birth with regard to **a person cannot be derived from** that **of an animal, as an animal does not have a concealed opening [*prozdor*]** to the womb, unlike women, whose thighs conceal the opening to their womb. Consequently, even if the definition were stated with regard to an animal, one might limit it to animals, as that stage is immediately visible; whereas in women it is not. **And** conversely, the definition of birth with regard to **an animal cannot be derived from** that of **a person, as the form of** a person's **face is significant** because people are created in the image of God, and their faces bear the mark of their intelligence, which is not true of animals. Consequently, perhaps the emergence of the head alone is considered a birth only with regard to a person.

One might object that this evidence comes from the laws of human birth, not animal birth. The *gemara* acknowledges the distinction: *de'adam mibeheima lo yalif* — one cannot draw a direct inference from animals to humans, because *de'ein prozdor libeheima* — animals do not have a *prozdor*. The word *prozdor* ordinarily means an antechamber or vestibule. Rashi and Tosafot acknowledge that this is its standard meaning but find it difficult to understand why the presence or absence of such a canal should alter the threshold of birth.

Their explanation points in a different direction. For a human female, the anatomy is such that the birth canal is not directly exposed; as the fetus emerges, it must be drawn from between the mother's legs, and to the observer the emergence is less immediately visible. One might therefore have thought that more of the fetus must appear before it is considered born. For a female animal, by contrast, the relevant opening is directly exposed, and the emergence of the head is immediately perceptible. Therefore one might have applied the rule of the head more readily to animals than to humans, and it would have been necessary to state both cases.

Conversely, *de'chashiv partzuf panim didei* — for humans, the face carries special significance; the human face represents the entire person in a way that has no animal parallel. One might therefore have thought that specifically for humans, the emergence of the face constitutes birth, while for animals a different standard — perhaps the majority of the animal's body — applies.

Since neither inference would travel safely in either direction, it was necessary to state the rule in each domain independently. Hence the *mishna* in *bechoros* cannot serve as a prior source for the animal case in our *mishna*, and the *gemara* must look further.

## Placenta Evidence

### Placenta Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: שִׁלְיָא שֶׁיָּצְתָה מִקְצָתָהּ – אֲסוּרָה בַּאֲכִילָה, כְּסִימָן וָלָד בָּאִשָּׁה כָּךְ סִימָן וָלָד בַּבְּהֵמָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to explain why the above suggestion is not a solution: With regard to **this** *halakha* **also,** that the emergence of the head of an animal is considered a birth, **we** have already **learned** it in a mishna (77a): If **part of a placenta emerged** from the womb of an animal before it was slaughtered, its **consumption** is **prohibited** even after the mother animal is slaughtered. The reason for this is just **as** the placenta is **an indication of** the presence of **a fetus in a woman, so too,** it is **an indication of** the presence of **a fetus in an animal.** Consequently, it is possible that the part of the placenta that emerged contained the head of the fetus, and accordingly it would be considered to have been born. The slaughter of the mother animal would therefore not permit it for consumption. Evidently, it is unnecessary for the mishna here to teach the definition of a birth even with regard to an animal.

The *gemara* finds an additional source in a *mishna* dealing with placentas. The case is one in which a placenta emerges from an animal without any visible fetus accompanying it. Since a placenta necessarily implies a fetus, the *halacha* concludes that a fetus must have been present but dissolved or miscarried. The question is whether the placenta itself may be eaten after the mother is slaughtered, and when.

The *mishna* states: *shilya she-yatzta miktzasa* — if even part of a placenta emerged, *asura baachila* — none of the placenta may be eaten. The reasoning is: *kesiman vlad be-isha kach siman vlad bevehema* — what serves as a sign of birth for a human woman serves equally as a sign of birth for an animal. Since the placenta implies the presence of a fetus, and part of the placenta has emerged, we must assume that the fetus has been born — meaning that the head of the fetus, along with the placenta, has already come out. The *shechita* of the mother therefore no longer covers this placenta-fetus unit, and it is forbidden.

The *gemara* uses this *mishna* to demonstrate that even within the context of animal law, the emergence of the head constitutes birth. The reasoning, admittedly, involves some inference: from the partial emergence of a placenta we assume the head of the fetus came out, and from that we conclude that birth has occurred. The jump from placenta to head is acknowledged to require careful unpacking — Tosafot notes the difficulty — but the *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that partial placenta emergence can only signal birth if we connect it to the standard criterion of the head. Furthermore, none of these cases about the placenta address the scenario of pulling the head back, so the primary *chiddush* of our *mishna* — that once the head has emerged, pulling it back does not undo the birth — may still be a genuine novelty not derivable from these prior sources.

## Makom Chataach

### Limb Pulls Back

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא הֶחְזִירוֹ דְּרֵישָׁא דַּוְקָא, תְּנָא סֵיפָא אַטּוּ רֵישָׁא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: לָא דְּרֵישָׁא דַּוְקָא, וְלָא דְּסֵיפָא דַּוְקָא, לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִתְנְיַיהּ כְּלָל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara has established that the latter clause does not teach any novelty. Accordingly, it returns to challenging Rav's ruling: **Granted,** the mishna is understood **if you say** that the statement that the fetus **brought** its foreleg **back** inside the mother animal, which is mentioned **in the first clause** of the mishna, is written **specifically** in order to teach the novelty that the foreleg is permitted if it was brought back before the slaughter, in contrast to Rav's ruling. If so, then one can claim the mishna **taught** it in **the latter clause due to the first clause,** so that they would be parallel stylistically, and no novelty is necessary in the latter clause. **But if you say the first clause** is **not** written **specifically** and does not teach a novelty, but rather the mishna is referring to the permitted status of the rest of the fetus, which is permitted even if it does not bring back its foreleg, **and** you also say **the latter clause is not** written **specifically** to teach a novelty, since the definition of birth is already taught in a mishna elsewhere, then **why does it** need **to teach** the *halakha* that the foreleg or head were brought back **at all?** Perforce, the first clause must be referring to the permitted status of the foreleg and limiting it specifically to the case where it was brought back inside. This contradicts Rav's ruling.

The *gemara*, having pressed these arguments, introduces the resolution of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak. His principle is: *lo nitzrecha ela lemakom chataach* — the *mishna* is needed only to teach about the *makom chataach*, the place of cutting — the boundary line itself. This concept will be elaborated more fully later, but the kernel of the idea is as follows. When a limb extends out of the mother's womb, there is a portion of that limb that is unambiguously outside, a portion that is unambiguously inside, and a portion right at the line itself — neither in nor out. According to Rav, the part that was outside remains forever forbidden. But the part that was precisely at the line, being neither inside nor outside, does not fall under the *basar basadeh* prohibition. If the limb is pulled back in, that boundary portion — the *makom chataach* — is now fully inside the mother, and the mother's *shechita* covers it. The *mishna* teaches that this boundary portion becomes permissible when the limb is pulled back. This is a narrow reading, but it allows Rav's position to stand: the part that was outside remains forbidden, while the part on the line becomes permissible upon retraction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אַעוּבָּר, וְכִדְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לִמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ. הָכָא נָמֵי, לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לִמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No; actually,** the mishna is **referring to** the permitted status of the rest of the **fetus, but** nevertheless, the statement that the fetus brought back its foreleg does teach a novelty. This is **similar to that which Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said,** in explanation of a ruling in a *baraita* cited below, that it is **necessary only with regard to the location of a cut** limb on the fetus's body. If the foreleg were to be severed at precisely the point that lay on the boundary between the inside and the outside of the womb, then the location of the cut on the fetus's body would also be prohibited. **Here too,** one can explain that the ruling is **necessary only with regard to the location of the cut,** and it teaches that if the fetus did not bring back its foreleg, then not only is the foreleg prohibited but the location of the cut is also prohibited.

## Placenta Evidence…

### Braita Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּהֵמָה הַמְקַשָּׁה לֵילֵד, הוֹצִיא עוּבָּר אֶת יָדוֹ וְהֶחְזִירָהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁחַט אֶת אִמּוֹ – מוּתָּר בַּאֲכִילָה. שָׁחַט אֶת אִמּוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ הֶחְזִירָהּ – אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a challenge to Rav's ruling from a *baraita*: If **an animal was encountering difficulty giving birth,** and as a result **the fetus extended its foreleg** outside the mother animal's body **and then brought it back** inside **and** then **afterward one slaughtered its mother,** its **consumption** is **permitted** by virtue of the slaughter of the mother animal. But if **one** first **slaughtered its mother, and** only **afterward** did the fetus **return its** foreleg back inside, its **consumption** is **prohibited.**

The *gemara* now examines a *braita* that parallels the *mishna*. The *braita* describes an animal in difficult labor where the fetus extends a limb and pulls it back — and the mother is then slaughtered: *mutar baachila*, permissible. Once again the question of what is permissible arises.

### Limb Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹצִיא אֶת יָדוֹ וַחֲתָכוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁחַט אֶת אִמּוֹ – שֶׁבַּחוּץ טָמֵא וְאָסוּר, וְשֶׁבִּפְנִים טָהוֹר וּמוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** If the fetus **extended its foreleg** outside **and** one **cut it** off, **and** then **afterward one slaughtered its mother,** then the part of the foreleg **that was outside** and was cut off is **ritually impure and** its consumption is **prohibited,** as it has the status of a limb cut from a living animal, which is forbidden. **And** the rest of the fetus **that was inside** is **ritually pure and** its consumption is **permitted** by virtue the slaughter of the mother animal.

The *braita* continues: if the mother was slaughtered while the limb was extended and only then was the limb pulled back, *asur baachila* — it is forbidden. Here the contrast between the two cases — limb pulled back before *shechita* versus pulled back after — suggests that whether the limb is in or out at the moment of *shechita* determines its status.

### Cutting Limb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט אֶת אִמּוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ חֲתָכוֹ – הַבָּשָׂר מַגַּע נְבֵלָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מַגַּע טְרֵפָה שְׁחוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **one slaughtered its mother and** only **afterward cut off** the foreleg that had been extended outside, then **the flesh** of both the mother animal and the fetus, excluding the foreleg, is impure due to **contact with a carcass.** Since the foreleg of the now dead fetus was not permitted through an act of slaughtering, it is regarded as a carcass. It therefore imparts impurity to the rest of the flesh, which was in contact with it. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** Although the slaughter of the mother animal does not permit the consumption of the foreleg, it does serve to prevent the foreleg from imparting the ritual impurity of a carcass. Accordingly, the flesh has the status of having been in **contact with a slaughtered *tereifa*.** By Torah law, consumption of the animal is prohibited but it does not impart ritual impurity. The Sages decreed it to be impure in that it disqualifies sacrificial flesh with which it comes in contact.

The *braita* then introduces two further cases. If the limb was extended and cut off while in that state, *shebechutz tamei* — what was cut off outside is considered *tamei*, and indeed constitutes *ever min hachai* — a limb severed from a living creature — which carries *tumah* and is forbidden. The fetus remaining inside, *tahor umutar*, is *tahor* and permissible. If, on the other hand, the mother was slaughtered while the limb was extended and only afterward was the limb cut, the question becomes the status of that extended limb: Rabbi Meir holds it is like *neveilah* — meat that never had the benefit of *shechita* at all — while the Sages hold it is like *maga tereifah shechuta*, in contact with a *tereifah* that was slaughtered, since there was a *shechita* but it simply did not apply to this limb. This is an interesting dispute: does one say there was no *shechita* whatsoever on the limb, making it *neveilah*, or does one say the *shechita* occurred but failed to cover the limb, making it more analogous to a *tereifah*?

## Makom Chataach…

### Boundary Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא רֵישָׁא: הוֹצִיא עוּבָּר אֶת יָדוֹ וְהֶחְזִירָהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁחַט אֶת אִמּוֹ – מוּתָּר בַּאֲכִילָה; מַאי לָאו אַאֵבֶר? לָא, אַעוּבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the challenge to Rav's ruling: **In any event, the first clause** of the *baraita* **teaches:** If **the fetus extended its foreleg** outside the mother animal's body **and then brought it back** inside **and** then **afterward one slaughtered its mother,** its **consumption** is **permitted. What, is it not** referring **to the limb,** i.e., the foreleg, and the *baraita* rules that it is permitted, in contradiction to Rav's ruling? The Gemara responds: **No,** it **is referring to** the rest of **the fetus.**

Returning to the core question, the *gemara* presses the *braita*: the opening clause says that if the fetus extended its limb and pulled it back and then the mother was slaughtered, *mutar baachila*. *mai lav haeiver* — does this not mean the limb itself is permissible? The *gemara* tries to say *lo ha-ubbar* — no, it means the fetus. But this is immediately challenged: *ei ha-ubbar amai asur* — if we are talking about the fetus, why does the closing clause say that when the mother was slaughtered with the limb extended and then the limb was pulled back, it is *asur*? The fetus never becomes forbidden merely because of the limb's extension. Clearly, the subject of both clauses is the limb, not the fetus. And if so, the *braita* explicitly says the limb becomes permissible when pulled back — a direct contradiction to Rav.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אַעוּבָּר, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: שָׁחַט אֶת אִמּוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ הֶחְזִירוֹ – אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה; וְאִי עוּבָּר, אַמַּאי אָסוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this: **If** the *baraita* **is referring to** the **fetus, say the latter clause:** If **one** first **slaughtered its mother, and** only **afterward** the fetus **returned its** foreleg inside, its **consumption** is **prohibited.** The Gemara explains the question: **And if** it is referring to the **fetus, why** should it be **prohibited** in this case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לִמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ, הָכָא נָמֵי לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לִמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is referring to the fetus, and should be explained in a manner **similar to that which Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said,** in explanation of a ruling in a *baraita* cited below, that it is **necessary only with regard to the location of the cut** limb on the fetus's body. If the foreleg was severed at precisely the point that lay on the boundary between the inside and outside when it was extended outside the womb, the location of the cut on the fetus's body is also prohibited. **Here too,** one can explain that this ruling is **necessary only with regard to the location of the cut,** and it teaches that if the fetus did not return its foreleg, then not only is the foreleg prohibited but the location of the cut is prohibited as well. The foreleg itself, though, is prohibited even if it was brought back inside before the slaughter.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's answer is again: *lo nitzrecha ela lemakom chataach* — the *braita*'s permissibility and prohibition attach not to the limb proper, but to the *makom chataach*, the boundary portion. When the limb is pulled back, that boundary portion moves inside the mother and is covered by the *shechita* — hence *mutar*. When the limb is not pulled back, that boundary portion remains outside or on the line and the *shechita* does not cover it — hence *asur*. The limb itself, on this reading, remains perpetually forbidden as Rav maintains. This is admittedly a significant inference to draw from what appears to be a straightforward statement about whether the limb is in or out, but it is the resolution the *gemara* offers.

### Hoof Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כִּי אֲתָא אֲבִימִי מִבֵּי חוֹזָאֵי, אֲתָא וְאַיְיתִי מַתְנִיתָא בִּידֵיהּ: פַּרְסָה הֶחְזִיר – אֱכוֹל, פְּרָסוֹת הֶחְזִיר – אֱכוֹל. מַאי לָאו הֶחְזִיר פַּרְסָה – אֲכוּל פַּרְסָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But when Avimi came from Bei Ḥozai he came and brought a *baraita* with him:** One of the sources (see 69a) that the slaughter of a pregnant animal also serves to permit the consumption of the fetus is the verse: "And every animal that has a split hoof and is cloven into two hooves, chews the cud, of the animals, it you may eat" (Deuteronomy 14:6). The verse mentions both "hoof" in the singular and "hooves" in the plural to teach that sometimes one may eat two hooves and sometimes only one, as follows: If the fetus extended two of its hooves outside the womb, if it **returned** one **hoof one may eat,** if it brought back both **hooves one may eat. What, is it not** that the *baraita* means that if it **brought back** one **hoof one may eat** that **hoof,** and likewise if it brought back both **hooves one may eat** both of them, in contradiction to Rav's ruling?

A further *braita* is introduced, brought by Avimi who had studied in the *beis medrash* of Bei Chuzaei. This *braita* offers a *drasha* on the *pasuk* that establishes the permissibility of the *ben pakua*: *kol behemah mafreset parsah veshashat shesa shetei parasot maalat gerah babehemah otah tocheilu* — an animal within an animal may be eaten. The *pasuk* uses both the singular *parsah* (hoof) and the plural *parsot* (hooves), and the *braita* derives from this: *parsah hechezir echol* — if it pulls back its hoof, you may eat; *parasot hechezir echol* — if it pulls back its hooves, you may eat. The apparent implication is that pulling the extended limb back renders it permissible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, הֶחֱזִיר פַּרְסָה – אֱכוֹל עוּבָּר. אִי עוּבָּר, מַאי אִירְיָא הֶחְזִיר? אֲפִילּוּ לֹא הֶחֱזִיר נָמֵי! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לִמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** it means that if it **brought back** one **hoof** or even both, **one may eat** the rest of the **fetus** but not the hooves. The Gemara objects: **If** it is referring to the permitted status of the **fetus, why does it specifically** state that it **brought back** the hoof? **Even** if it did **not bring** it **back** the fetus would be permitted. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** the ruling of the *baraita* is **necessary only with regard to the location of the cut** limb on the body of the fetus. If the limb were to be severed at precisely the point that lay on the boundary between the inside and the outside when it was extended outside the womb, then the location of the cut on the fetus's body would also be prohibited, but only if the fetus had not brought back that limb before the mother animal was slaughtered.

The *gemara* resists this reading: *lo ha-ubbar* — you can eat the fetus, not the hoof. But then pulling back the hoof would be irrelevant to eating the fetus, and the *gemara* demands: *mai irya hechezir, afilu lo hechezir nami* — if it is about the fetus, why does pulling back matter? Here again Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak supplies the answer: *lo nitzrecha ela lemakom chataach* — one reference teaches about the *makom chataach*, the boundary line. The *gemara* then asks why we need two formulations, singular *parsah* and plural *parasot*. Could one not teach both lessons from a single reference? The *gemara* answers: *chad lemakom chataach* — one instance teaches about the boundary line. *vechad lekolat bidmei parah* — the other teaches about a calf born from a cow whose hooves are not cloven, what the *gemara* calls *kalut ben parah*. Rabbi Shimon holds that such an animal, once born into the world, is forbidden — it lacks the characteristic of split hooves that defines a *kosher* species. However, while it remains within the mother's womb, it is still covered by the mother's status. The word *parsah* in the singular — a hoof rather than split hooves — teaches that even a fetus without cloven hooves is permissible while in the mother, in accordance with Rabbi Shimon's view. Thus both the singular and plural forms carry independent teachings, and neither supports the reading that the limb itself becomes permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי קָא נָסֵיב לַהּ! מַאי לָאו חַד לְאֵבֶר, וְחַד לִמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ? לָא, חַד לִמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ, וְחַד לְקָלוּט בִּמְעֵי פָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara persists: **But** even given Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak's claim the *baraita* **adduces two verses,** i.e., the mention of hoof and hooves. **What, is it not** that **one** serves **to** permit the **limb,** in contradiction to Rav's ruling, **and** the other **one** serves **to** permit the **location of the cut,** in accordance with Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak's ruling? The Gemara responds: **No; one** mention does serve **to** permit the **location of the cut, but** the other **one** serves **to** permit a fetus with **non-cloven hooves [*kalut*]** found **inside the womb of a cow** that was slaughtered. Although the fetus does not bear the distinguishing characteristics of a kosher species, it is nevertheless permitted by virtue of the slaughter of its mother.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: קָלוּט בֶּן פָּרָה אָסוּר. הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּיָצָא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם, אֲבָל בִּמְעֵי אִמּוֹ – שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it is necessary to have a specific source to permit this **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon, as Rabbi Shimon says:** A calf with **non-cloven hooves born from a** kosher **cow** is **forbidden,** as the calf does not bear the distinguishing characteristics of a kosher species. The verse teaches that **this matter** applies only **where** the fetus **emerged into the airspace of the world,** i.e., it was born before the mother animal was slaughtered. **But** if it was still **inside its mother's womb** when the mother was slaughtered, it is permitted.

## Ulla Disagrees

### Limb Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְאֵבֶר עַצְמוֹ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara attempted to challenge Rav's ruling that once a limb of a fetus is extended outside the mother animal's body it becomes prohibited even if it was then brought back, but did not find a conclusive refutation. It now presents a dissenting opinion: **Ulla** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And** even the **limb itself is permitted** by virtue of the slaughter of the mother animal.

Having worked through all the *braitatot* under the framework of Rav's position and Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's resolution, the *gemara* now explicitly surfaces the opposing view. *ulla amar rebbi yochanan* — Ulla, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, states: *v'eiver atzmo mutar* — the limb itself becomes permissible when pulled back. This is the position that has been lurking behind all of the *gemara*'s challenges: that there is simply no concept of permanent prohibition attaching to a limb that protrudes and retracts. The only issue while the limb is out is that the *shechita* cannot cover it in that state; once it is pulled back, the *shechita* covers it fully.

### Rav Yehuda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְעוּלָּא: וְהָא רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ אֵבֶר עַצְמוֹ אָסוּר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן יָהֵיב לַן מֵעַפְרָא דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל וּמָלֵינַן עַיְינִין, אֶלָּא הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said to Ulla: But Rav and Shmuel both say** the **limb itself is prohibited;** how can you say otherwise? Ulla **said to him: Who will give us** some **of the dust of** the graves of **Rav and Shmuel and we will fill our eyes** with it; such is the greatness of those Sages. **But** I follow the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, and **this** is what **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:**

Rav Yehuda confronts Ulla directly: *veha Rav uShmuel de-amri taryehu, eiver atzmo assur* — but both Rav and Shmuel say the limb remains forbidden. This is significant — we had previously heard the ruling in Rav's name alone, and now we learn that Shmuel concurred.

Ulla's response is celebrated. *man yahiv lan mei'afar deRav uShmuel umalina einan* — who could give us from the dust of where Rav and Shmuel walk, and I would place that dust in my eyes. It is a statement of profound veneration: Ulla holds Rav and Shmuel in the highest esteem. Yet despite this reverence, he maintains: *ela hacha amar Rebbi Yochanan* — nonetheless, here is what Rabbi Yochanan says, and I stand by it.

## Rebbi Yochanan

### Chatat Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל הָיוּ בִּכְלַל ״בָּשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״, כְּשֶׁפָּרַט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב גַּבֵּי חַטָּאת שֶׁיָּצְתָה חוּץ לִמְחִיצָתָהּ וְחָזְרָה – אָסוּר, חַטָּאת הוּא דִּפְרַט רַחֲמָנָא בָּהּ, אֲבָל כֹּל מִילֵּי – כֵּיוָן דַּהֲדוּר – שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Initially one would assume **all** flesh that is permitted due to being within a certain boundary, e.g., a fetus inside its mother's womb, the flesh of offerings of the most sacred order within the Temple courtyard, and the flesh of offerings of lesser sanctity within Jerusalem, **was included** in the verse: **"And flesh, in the field, a *tereifa*, you shall not eat"** (Exodus 22:30). The verse is interpreted to teach that if such flesh leaves its boundaries it is rendered forbidden, even should it subsequently return. **Once** another **verse applies** the concept **for you specifically with regard to a sin offering,** teaching **that** if it **went outside of its boundary and returned** it is **prohibited** (see Leviticus 10:18), then it is apparent **that the Merciful One specifically applied** the concept **to a sin offering. But** with regard to **all** other **items** that left their boundary, **once** they are **brought back** they are **permitted,** including the limb of a fetus that was extended outside the womb and then was brought back.

Ulla then transmits the full reasoning of Rabbi Yochanan. *hakol hayu bichlal basar basadeh tereifah* — in principle, the verse *u'vasar basadeh tereifah lo tocheilu* communicates a broad idea: meat that exits its proper zone becomes forbidden. This would theoretically encompass *kodshei kodashim* taken outside the *beis haMikdash*, *kodashim kalim* taken outside Jerusalem, and — as Rav extrapolated — the limb of a fetus protruding from the mother's womb. However, *keshepierat lecha hakatuv gabei chatat* — when the Torah explicitly specified this rule in the context of the *chatat*, stating that if it goes outside its boundaries and returns it is nevertheless forbidden — *chatat hu depirat Rachmana bah* — it is specifically by the *chatat* that the Torah made this detail explicit. Rabbi Yochanan concludes that this principle, once particularized to the *chatat*, applies only there. *aval kol mili, keivan dehader, shari* — for everything else, once the meat or limb returns to its proper domain, it becomes permissible.

### Fetus Not Sacrifice

The implication is far-reaching. Even *kodashim kalim* — lesser sacred offerings taken outside Jerusalem — would, according to Rabbi Yochanan, become permissible if brought back. Certainly a fetus's limb, which is not a *korban* at all, does not fall under this stringency. The extension of the *basar basadeh* principle beyond the domain of the *chatat* is, in Rabbi Yochanan's view, an unwarranted extrapolation.

### Moshe's Inquiry


Where does Rabbi Yochanan find that the *chatat* specifically is governed by this rule? It is derived from Moshe's inquiry on the eighth day of the *milu'im*. The goat sin-offering of that day was burned, and Moshe asked why. *chazal* read the *pasuk* — *hen lo huva et dama el hakodesh pnimah* — as conveying two propositions: the blood was not brought into the inner sanctuary, and the meat remained *pnimah*, inside. Moshe was demonstrating that there was no basis to burn it, since neither condition that would have warranted burning applied. The implication drawn is that had the meat been taken outside the *mishkan*, it would have been burned — i.e., it would have been rendered permanently forbidden. This establishes specifically for the *chatat* that departure from its permitted space creates an irremediable prohibition. Rabbi Yochanan limits the rule to this case.

## Final Braita

### Braita Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״בָּשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וּבִכּוּרִים, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיָּצְאוּ חוּץ לִמְחִיצָתָן וְחָזְרוּ – מוּתָּרִין, יָכוֹל אַף זֶה כֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״טְרֵפָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Ulla and Rabbi Yoḥanan from a *baraita*: It is derived from the verse: **"And flesh, in the field, a *tereifa*, you shall not eat,"** that flesh that leaves the boundary in which it is permitted is thereby rendered forbidden like a *tereifa*. **Why** must **the verse state** that it is like a *tereifa*? The *baraita* explains: **Since we find with regard to second tithe and first fruits that even though they went outside of their boundary,** which is the city of Jerusalem and the only place where it is permitted to eat them, nevertheless if they **are brought back** to there **they are** once again **permitted,** one **might** have thought **that also** with regard to **this** prohibition, that of flesh that leaves its boundary, **it is so,** i.e., if it is brought back it once again becomes permitted for consumption. Therefore, **the verse states: "A *tereifa*."**

A *braita* is now adduced as a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan's position. The *braita* expounds on the *pasuk* *u'vasar basadeh tereifah lo tocheilu*: *mah talmud lomar* — what does the verse come to teach? *lefi shematzinu bemaaser sheni uvikurim* — because we have found in the context of non-sacrificial sanctified items such as *maaser sheni* and *bikurim*, which must be consumed in Jerusalem, that if taken outside Jerusalem and brought back they remain permissible — *sheaf al pi sheyatziu chutz lemchitzatan vechazru mutarin* — perhaps even meat that exits its zone and returns would also be permissible. Therefore the verse says *tereifah*: *mah tereifah keivan shenitrefa shuv ein lah heter* — just as a *tereifah* once rendered *treif* has no path back to permissibility, so too meat that exits its zone, *keivan sheyatza chutz lemchitzato shuv ein lo heter* — once it has gone beyond its boundaries, it does not regain its permitted status.

### Clarification of the *Baraita*

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: כִּטְרֵפָה, מָה טְרֵפָה – כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה שׁוּב אֵין לָהּ הֶיתֵּר, אַף בָּשָׂר – כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּצָא חוּץ לִמְחִיצָתוֹ שׁוּב אֵין לוֹ הֶיתֵּר! תְּיוּבְתָּא דְעוּלָּא, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is the** biblical **derivation** from the term "a *tereifa*"? **Rabba said:** The verse indicates that the prohibition of flesh that left its boundary is **like** the prohibition of **a *tereifa*. Just as** with regard to **a *tereifa*, once** an animal **is mortally wounded,** rendering it a *tereifa*, **it can no longer regain a permitted status, so too** with regard to **flesh** referred to in the verse: **Once it has gone outside of its boundary it can no longer regain permitted** status. Accordingly, if the limb of a fetus was extended outside the womb it would thereby become permanently prohibited, contradicting the opinion of Ulla and Rabbi Yoḥanan. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the statement of **Ulla** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

This *braita* states the principle broadly, applying it to meat beyond the specific context of the *chatat*, and thus appears to contradict Rabbi Yochanan's narrower reading.

## Rebbi Yochanan…

### Maaser Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וּבִכּוּרִים. הֵיכָן מָצִינוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*, which states: **The Master said: Since we find with regard to second tithe and first fruits** that although they left their boundary, if they are brought back there they are again permitted. The Gemara asks: **Where did we find** this, i.e., what is the source for this *halakha*?

Rabbi Yochanan's position finds support in the very contrast the *braita* draws. The *braita* itself acknowledges that *maaser sheni* and *bikurim*, which are sanctified but not sacrificial, do become permissible when taken out and brought back. This is precisely Rabbi Yochanan's point: the stringency of permanent prohibition upon departure from the permitted zone applies only to the *chatat* and similarly restricted *kodshei kodashim*, not to lesser sanctified items and certainly not to a fetus's limb.

---

## Final Braita…

### Favors Rav


Nonetheless, the *gemara* concludes with *tiyuvta de-Ulla tiyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation of Ulla. The final posture of the *gemara* is to weigh in favor of Rav's position: once the limb has protruded from the womb, it remains forbidden even upon retraction. All of the evidence from the *mishna* and various *braitatot* that seemed to indicate that the limb becomes permissible when pulled back is resolved through Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's principle of *makom chataach* — each time, the leniency applies only to that precise boundary portion that was neither inside nor outside, not to the limb proper.

This is a striking outcome. The weight of straightforward readings throughout the *sugia* consistently pointed toward the permissibility of the limb upon retraction, and the argument from *basar basadeh tereifah* had to work hard against that current. Yet the *gemara* ultimately endorses Rav — and with him Shmuel — that the limb, having once stepped outside the domain which the mother's *shechita* defines, acquires a permanent prohibition that no subsequent retraction can undo.
