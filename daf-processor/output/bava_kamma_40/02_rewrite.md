# Bava Kamma 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Apotropus & Reshus

### Guardian Setup

The daf under discussion is 40a, though the sugya picks up from the middle of 39b. The *mishnah* there addressed the case of a goring ox owned by a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan* — individuals who lack the legal competency to be held responsible for their animal. Because they cannot be held liable, the community is nonetheless left unprotected, and so *beis din* appoints an *apotropus*, a guardian, to oversee the animal.

### Two Types Guards

This *apotropus* is importantly distinct from the ordinary *apotropus* appointed to serve the interests of the incompetent person or the minor — to manage their affairs and protect their wellbeing. The *apotropus* appointed here is installed for the sake of the community. His role is not to serve the *cheresh shoteh v'katan* but to protect those who might be injured by the animal.

### Warning & Muad

By appointing such a guardian, the *mishnah* establishes that this *apotropus* can receive formal warnings on the owner's behalf. If the ox gores three times and the *apotropus* is warned on each occasion, the ox acquires the status of a *muad* — a confirmed habitual gorer.

### Tam Payment Query

The *gemara* raises the question of whether payment would be imposed for the first three gorings. The general principle is that the *torah* treats the first three incidents with leniency, making the *tam* payment — half damages — more akin to a *knas*, a penalty imposed only under specific conditions. The question is whether, given the unusual nature of this situation, anyone would pay even the *tam* half for those initial incidents. The specific formulation is *ma'amidin apotropus l'tam ligvos migufo* — whether a *beis din*-appointed guardian enables collection of *tam* payment from the body of the ox.

### Owner Liability

A further question concerns who bears liability once the ox becomes a *muad*. The *cheresh shoteh v'katan* are never liable even for damages they cause directly, which would suggest they should bear no liability here either. On the other hand, the *apotropus* has taken on responsibility for the animal and functions in some respects like a *shomer* — a guardian who has assumed custodial duties. Yet the *gemara* never suggests transforming the position into a paid arrangement. The conclusion reached is that the *cheresh shoteh v'katan* must pay — either directly from their own assets or by reimbursing the *apotropus*.

### Reshus Meshaneh

The final topic introduced from the prior session is the principle of *reshus meshaneh* — the puzzling idea that when the ox changes possession, its *muad* status may revert to *tam*. This applies even in a case where the *apotropus* had the ox warned and declared a *muad*, and then the *katan* grew up and the ox was returned to him. Even more strikingly, the *gemara* will shortly explore whether this reversion applies even when the ox is merely returned from a *shomer* to its original owner — a case in which there is no change of ownership at all, since it was always the owner's animal being watched on his behalf.

The conceptual underpinning of this principle, though the *gemara* never makes it explicit, seems to be that *muad* status is not primarily a statement about the animal's habitual temperament, but about the *owner's* habitual negligence. It is the owner who has repeatedly ignored warnings. If the person receiving the warnings is not the current owner, the negligence cannot be attributed to the new possessor — even if that new possessor is the original owner receiving his ox back from a *shomer*.

## Yaakov Half Payment

### Half Payment Intro

The *gemara* now takes up the statement of *Rabbi Yaakov*, who rules that for the ox of a *cheresh shoteh v'katan*, one pays *chatzi nezek* — half damages.

### Muad Context

The *beraita* supplies the context: the case involves a *shor tam* that became a *muad* while under the oversight of the *apotropus*.

### Half-Tam Status

This is where the unusual position of *Rabbi Yehuda* becomes relevant. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds *tzad tamuso bimkomo omedes* — the *tam* dimension of the ox remains in place even after it becomes a *muad*. In other words, the ox is viewed as half-*muad* and half-*tam*: it pays the additional half as a *muad*, but the original half it was always paying is treated as if it remains a *tam*. *Rabbi Yaakov*'s ruling of half damages is therefore understood as a *pesak* consistent with *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position.

### Shmira Paradox

This gives rise to a genuinely counterintuitive result. The levels of *shmirah* — watchfulness — produce a paradox. A *shmirah me'ula*, a superior level of guarding, exempts entirely. A *shmirah p'chusa*, a minimal level of guarding, paradoxically exempts from liability for the *muad* half of the animal, yet does not exempt from the *tam* half. The result is that a *muad* ox with inadequate but minimal *shmirah* still incurs half-payment — because minimal *shmirah* is insufficient for the *tam* dimension, even though it suffices for the *muad* dimension. This is the strange consequence of *Rabbi Yehuda*'s framework, and it is part of what *Rabbi Yaakov*'s statement encapsulates.

### Abaye's Challenge

*Abaye* challenges *Rava*: do *Rabbi Yaakov* and *Rabbi Yehuda* in fact disagree? The *beraita* records them debating — *Rabbi Yehuda* says *chayav*, fully liable, and *Rabbi Yaakov* says *chatzi nezek*, half — which implies a real dispute. If *Rabbi Yaakov* were merely clarifying *Rabbi Yehuda*, there would be no dispute at all.

### Rava's Read

*Rava bar Ulla* resolves this by saying that *Rabbi Yaakov* is indeed only explaining *Rabbi Yehuda*. When *Rabbi Yehuda* says *chayav*, he means liable for half — specifically, the half that results from the interplay of *tam* and *muad* status under the conditions of *shmirah p'chusa*. *Rabbi Yaakov* is spelling out what that liability amounts to.

### Abaye Disagrees

*Abaye* rejects this harmonizing reading and insists they do genuinely disagree. The actual dispute, on *Abaye*'s reading, concerns whether an *apotropus* for a *cheresh shoteh v'katan* enables collection of *tam* damages at all — the principle of *ma'amidin apotropus l'tam ligvos migufo*.

## Debate Clarified

### Apotropus Authority

On *Abaye*'s reading, the *beraita* presents a case where the ox received no *shmirah* whatsoever — *b'muad v'lo natru klal*. In that scenario, the owner ought in principle to pay fully, both for the *muad* half and the *tam* half.

### Tam Collection

*Rabbi Yehuda* says full payment is owed. This is because he holds *ma'amidin apotropus l'tam ligvos migufo* — the *apotropus* arrangement enables collection even for the *tam* portion of the ox belonging to a *cheresh shoteh v'katan*. Since there was no *shmirah* at all, one pays the full *muad* half and also the *tam* half, which under *Rabbi Yehuda* is collectible via the *apotropus*.

*Rabbi Yaakov*, by contrast, agrees with *Rabbi Yehuda* on one point — that the ox retains its *tam* dimension (*tzad tamuso bimkomo omedes*) — but disagrees on the other: he holds *ein ma'amidin*, that no *apotropus* arrangement enables collection of *tam* damages from an ox of a *cheresh shoteh v'katan*. Therefore, the only payment owed is the *muad* half — which is the *chatzi nezek* he rules. Notably, in this reading, when *Rabbi Yaakov* says half, he means the *muad* half, not the *tam* half — a more coherent result for property belonging to those who are not legally competent.

### Rav Acha Q

*Rav Acha bar Abaye* then challenges *Rava*'s reading. If *Rava* is correct that they do not disagree, *Rava* has constructed an unnecessarily elaborate scenario — a *muad* ox whose owner performed a partial level of *shmirah*, such that he is exempt from the *muad* half but still liable for the *tam* half. This is a complicated and multi-layered case.

### Tam Simplification

Why not simply present the straightforward case? Make it a *tam*, and say the reason for half-payment is *ma'amidin apotropus l'tam ligvos migufo* — we appoint a guardian who can collect from the body of the *tam* ox, and that is the *chidush*. For *Rabbi Yehuda*, the scenario would be that the owner performed a *shmirah p'chusa*, which is insufficient for a *tam* and therefore he is liable. For *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov*, who rules that minimal *shmirah* suffices even for a *tam*, the case would be no *shmirah* at all. In either scenario, there is no need to introduce the complicated *muad* framework.

The *gemara* responds on *Rava*'s behalf: the reason the *beraita* constructs the more elaborate case is that it teaches us multiple things simultaneously — that *ma'amidin apotropus l'tam ligvos migufo*, that *tzad tamuso bimkomo omedes*, and that *shmirah p'chusa* exempts from the *muad* portion but not the *tam* portion. The *beraita* prefers to communicate a greater *chidush* even at the cost of complexity.

### Ravina Reshus

*Ravina* then offers an entirely different framework for understanding the dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Yaakov*. On his reading, their debate is not about *ma'amidin apotropus l'tam ligvos migufo* at all, but about *reshus meshaneh*. The case is where the ox was declared a *muad* under the *apotropus*, and then the situation of the *cheresh shoteh v'katan* changed — the deaf-mute was cured, the mentally ill person recovered, the minor came of age — and the animal was returned to its now-competent owner. Does the ox retain its *muad* status or revert to *tam*?

*Rabbi Yehuda* holds *harei hu b'chezkas muad* — it remains presumed a *muad*. *Rabbi Yaakov* holds *reshus meshaneh* — the change of possession reverts it to *tam*, and consequently only half is paid.

## Apotropus Payment

### Payment Source

Having mapped the contours of the dispute, the *gemara* proceeds to a *beraita*: an *apotropus* pays *min ha'aliyah* — from quality property — but does not pay *kofer*.

The *min ha'aliyah* ruling raises the underlying question of who exactly is paying. The conclusion from the prior session was that the *cheresh shoteh v'katan* must bear the financial burden — either directly from their assets or by reimbursing the *apotropus*. When the ox is a *muad* and damages must be paid, payment comes from the highest-quality assets available, not merely from the body of the ox.

### Kofer Exempt

The second ruling — that the *apotropus* does not pay *kofer* — requires explanation. If the *apotropus* system enables collecting even full damages when the ox kills property or injures someone, one might expect that when the animal kills a person and the more severe obligation of *kofer* would apply, certainly some payment should be imposed. Yet the *beraita* rules that no *kofer* is paid.

### Kofer Concept

The *gemara* explains this by identifying the nature of *kofer*. *Kofer* is not simply a form of compensation assigned to represent the value of a human life. Rather, it functions as *kapparah* — atonement. The *pasuk* is explicit: the owner of the ox is described as one who deserves to die, since his animal killed a person through his negligence, and the *kofer* payment is the mechanism by which he redeems his soul. It is, in effect, a monetary substitute for a *korban*. The owner pays the family of the deceased not as compensation for their loss, but as the vehicle through which he achieves *kapparah* for his own culpability.

If that is the case, the *gemara* reasons, then *yesomim lav b'nei kapparah ninhu* — orphans and incompetent individuals are not in the category of those who require or are capable of achieving *kapparah*. They are not *bnei mitzvos*, and the entire framework of atonement and accountability that *kofer* addresses does not apply to them.

One might ask: why not impose *kofer* on the *apotropus* himself, as the one exercising custodial responsibility? The *gemara* does not press this point, apparently treating the *apotropus* as a representative of the *cheresh shoteh v'katan* rather than as independently liable in his own right. Since it is the orphans' *kapparah* at stake, and they are not subject to such obligations, the system cannot impose *kofer* at all.

### Kofer Self-Value

*Rav Hisda* identifies the ruling that *kofer* is *kapparah* as the position of *Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka*. A *beraita* presents a dispute on the quantum of *kofer* payment: the *pasuk* states *v'natan pidyon nafsho k'chol asher yushat alav* — he shall give the redemption of his soul according to what is assessed upon him. The *tanna kamma* rules that payment is assessed based on the value of the *nizak*, the person killed — calculated in market terms as one would assess a slave. *Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* rules that payment is assessed based on the value of the *mazik*, the owner of the ox — his own life's worth in the same terms.

## Kofer Debate

### Kofer Source

The *gemara* initially assumes this is a deep conceptual dispute: the *rabbanan* hold *kofra mamona* — *kofer* is fundamentally compensation — and therefore they assess it by the life of the one who was killed, the *nizak*. *Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* holds *kofra kapparah* — *kofer* is atonement — and therefore it is assessed by the life of the one who must atone, the *mazik*. If the payment is about expiation, it must correspond to the value of the one expiating, not the one lost.

The logic of the *kapparah* reading emerges directly from the *psukim*. The verse immediately preceding the *kofer* provision states explicitly that the negligent owner of a *muad* ox is deserving of death: *v'gam b'alav yumas*. The *kofer* is then introduced as an alternative — the owner redeems his soul rather than forfeit his life. *Pidyon nafsho*, the redemption of *his* soul, is the phrase the *torah* uses, which is why *Rabbi Yishmael* concludes that the assessment must be based on the owner's own worth.

This reading, the *gemara* notes, also has bearing on the general principle of *ayin tachas ayin*. Several *rishonim* understand that *ayin tachas ayin* means monetary payment, but the framing of "an eye for an eye" implies that what is owed is not the value of the victim's eye, but the value of the perpetrator's eye — because the underlying logic is that the perpetrator deserved the same injury but is instead paying money as a substitute.

### Kapparah Consensus

*Rav Pappa* intervenes to clarify that the dispute between the *tanna kamma* and *Rabbi Yishmael* need not be understood as a disagreement about whether *kofer* is *kapparah* or *mamona*. In fact, says *Rav Pappa*, *kulei alma kofra kapparah* — everyone agrees that *kofer* is *kapparah*.

### Nizak vs Mazik

The actual dispute, on *Rav Pappa*'s reading, is only about how *kapparah* is achieved. *Rabbanan* hold *b'de'nizak shayminan* — atonement is attained by paying for the life of the one killed. *Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* holds *b'de'mazik shayminan* — atonement is attained by paying the value of one's own life.

### Pidyon Value

The *rabbanan*'s textual basis is a *gezeirah shavah*: the same word *yushat*/*yushas* appears both in the *kofer* verse and in a verse concerning the miscarriage case, where the payment is assessed based on the value of what was lost — the life of the fetus. Therefore, the parallel usage indicates that *kofer* too is assessed by the value of what was lost, i.e., the *nizak*.

*Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* counters from the plain reading of *v'natan pidyon nafsho* — he shall give the redemption of *his* soul, meaning the *mazik*'s soul — which indicates that the payment is calibrated to the *mazik*'s own worth.

The *rabbanan* reply that *pidyon nafsho* does indeed mean he redeems his soul, but the manner in which he does so is by paying for the life of the other — *mihu ki shayminan b'nizak shayminan*. The vehicle of atonement is the valuation of the victim.

With this clarified, the *gemara* returns to reinforce the exemption from *kofer* for the orphans. If everyone agrees that *kofer* is *kapparah*, and if *kapparah* is inherently inapplicable to those not subject to *mitzvos* and personal accountability, then *kofer* cannot be imposed on them under any reading of the dispute, whether the quantum is assessed by the *nizak* or the *mazik*.

## Partners & Kofer

### Scholar Meeting

The *gemara* now narrates that *Rava* spoke highly of *Rav Acha bar Yaakov* to *Rav Nachman*, praising him as a great *talmid chacham*. *Rav Nachman* instructed *Rava* to bring him over, and when *Rav Acha bar Yaakov* arrived, *Rav Nachman* challenged him: ask me something — effectively inviting a scholarly examination.

### Partner Division

*Rav Nachman* then posed his question. If an ox is owned by two partners — *shor shel shnei shutfin* — and it gores and kills a person, how is the *kofer* paid?

### Payment Problem

The problem is as follows. If each partner pays a full *kofer*, one might object: *kofer echad amar Rachmana v'lo shnei kofrin* — the *torah* prescribed one *kofer*, not two. If each partner pays half a *kofer*, one might object: *kofer shalem amar Rachmana v'lo chatzi* — the *torah* prescribed a complete *kofer*, not a partial one.

The tension here runs deep. In ordinary damage law, when an ox injures someone's property, the concern is compensation — and two half-payments that together make the victim whole are entirely satisfactory. Here, however, *kofer* is *kapparah*, and atonement presents a different logic. By analogy, when two people jointly perform a *chilul Shabbat*, the obligation is not discharged by each bringing half a *korban*; each has a full sin requiring full atonement. That would argue for each partner paying a full *kofer*. Yet the *kofer* is paid not to the *beis hamikdash* but to the family of the deceased, and there is no evident reason the family should receive double payment simply because the ox has two owners.

As *Tosafos* notes: were *kofer* directed to the *beis hamikdash*, one could argue that both partners bring a full payment, as each has a full obligation of *kapparah*. But since the *kofer* goes to the *nizak*'s family, doubling the payment seems incoherent. Conversely, halving the payment seems to undercut the essential nature of the obligation. The question thus remains genuinely unresolved.

### Seizing Property

While *Rav Acha bar Yaakov* was still turning over the first question, *Rav Nachman* posed a second. *Chayvei erchin memashkin osan* — if someone has pledged a donation to the *beis hamikdash*, such as an *erech*, and has not paid, the *beis hamikdash* can seize his property as collateral. *Chayvei chataot va'ashamot ein memashkin osan* — but if someone owes a *korban chatas* or *asham*, the *beis hamikdash* does not seize his property, relying instead on the debtor's own sense of urgency about achieving *kapparah*.

The conceptual basis for this distinction may go beyond mere motivational psychology. One line of reasoning is that the obligation to bring a *korban* is a matter between the individual and God — it is not a debt to the *beis hamikdash* as an institutional creditor, and therefore it may not be appropriate for the *beis hamikdash* to exercise the coercive powers of a creditor. This parallels the broader question of *kofin oso ad she'yomar rotzeh ani* — whether someone can be compelled to bring a *korban* at all.

### Korban vs Debt

*Rav Nachman*'s question is: what is the rule regarding one who owes *kofer*? Two possibilities present themselves.

On one hand, *kofer* is *kapparah*, structurally similar to a *chatas* or *asham*, and the debtor therefore feels the weight of his obligation pressing upon him. He will pay without compulsion, and no seizure of property is necessary — *belo ba'i mashkunei*.

On the other hand, unlike a *chatas* or *asham*, the *kofer* is paid to a human being — the family of the deceased. Since the payment goes to another person rather than being brought to the *mizbeach*, the debtor may treat it psychologically as an ordinary monetary debt, without the same urgency. Moreover, since the owner himself did not directly kill anyone — his ox did — he may feel less personal guilt and less internal compulsion to pay. In that case, the *beis din* would need to seize his property to ensure payment.

### Unresolved Q

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* was unable to answer either question. He was, as the *gemara* says, still sealed within the first one when the second was presented. *Rav Nachman* had effectively demonstrated that even a celebrated *talmid chacham* could be presented with genuinely unresolved questions.

*Tosafos* notes a further nuance regarding the *mashkon* question: since the *kofer* is paid to the *yorshei ha'nizak* — the heirs of the victim — rather than to the *beis hamikdash*, the question of seizure involves not the *beis hamikdash* acting as institutional creditor, but whether the *yorshei ha'nizak* themselves can directly seize property. Some *meforshim* understand *Tosafos* as suggesting that once a judgment has been issued by *beis din*, the question of whether the debtor feels internally motivated becomes irrelevant — *beis din* enforces the judgment regardless of the nature of the underlying obligation. The question of *mashkon* without a prior court judgment is a separate issue, and it is specifically whether the *yorshei ha'nizak* can act on their own initiative, without waiting for the court's enforcement apparatus, that remains open.

## Borrowed Ox

### Borrow Setup

The *gemara* returns to the case of the *shomer* and *reshus meshaneh*, now with competent owners and a genuine borrowing arrangement. A *beraita* presents the following: one borrowed an ox presumed to be a *tam* — *sha'alah b'chezkat tam* — and it turned out to be a *muad*.

### Half Payment Case

The *beraita* rules: *ba'alim meshalem chatzi nezek v'shoel meshalem chatzi nezek* — the owner pays half damages and the borrower pays half damages. The logic is that even had the ox been a *tam*, the borrower, as a *shoel*, would have been liable for half the damages if the animal gored. Since the animal is a *muad* and the total damage payment is full, the borrower pays the half he would have owed in any case, and the owner — who knew it was a *muad* — pays the remaining half.

### Borrower Claim

The *gemara* challenges this: why should the *shoel* pay at all? He should be entitled to say to the owner: *tora sha'ali, arya lo sha'ali* — I thought I was borrowing an ox, not a lion. I borrowed what I understood to be a docile animal unlikely to gore; if it was a *tam*, it would not have gored. The fact that it turned out to be a *muad* and caused damage should be the owner's problem entirely.

### Owner Counter

The *gemara* answers: the scenario is one in which the borrower could see from the animal's temperament that it was aggressive. He recognized it was the kind of animal that would gore. Given that he accepted it with this knowledge, his claim that he was merely borrowing an ox and not a lion rings hollow. He knowingly accepted a potentially dangerous animal, and with it the risk of paying the half he would have owed had it been a *tam*.

### Borrower Escalates

The borrower might still argue: fine, I knew it was aggressive, but I thought it was technically a *tam* — it had not yet been warned three times or formally declared a *muad*. I expected to pay at most half, and I expected that payment to be limited to the body of the animal itself — *migufo*. Now that it turns out to be a *muad*, the liability structure has changed entirely and I should not be held to it.

### Tam Payment Rule

The *gemara* resolves this directly: *sof sof*, in the end, had it been a *tam* you would have paid half. The fact that it is a *muad* does not reduce your obligation below what you would have owed in the best-case scenario for you. You pay what you would have paid as a *tam* — half, up to the value of the animal — and the owner pays the balance.

The *gemara* then works through a series of further claims the *shoel* might raise. He might say: as a *tam*, I could have been *modeh* and thereby avoided the *knas* payment — *modeh b'knas patur*. Or he might say: I could have brought the animal to a swamp — *mavrikna lei l'agma* — and denied the victim access to it, using it as leverage to negotiate a lower settlement.

On the *modeh* claim, the *gemara* answers that the scenario assumes *beis din* seized the animal immediately, so the option of being *modeh* before the court would not have been available in any case.

On the swamp claim, *Tosafos* raises an objection: what kind of argument is this? Hiding the animal from the *nizak* would be *gezeila* — the *nizak* has a legal right to it. *Tosafos* explains that what the borrower means is not that hiding the animal would have been legally permissible, but rather that the threat of making recovery difficult would have given him leverage to negotiate a lower settlement from the *nizak*. The victim, anticipating difficulty in enforcement, might have accepted less. Since the ox is now a *muad* and any property can be seized directly, that leverage is lost. The *gemara*'s final resolution is that *beis din* seized the animal immediately, so this leverage would not have existed in any event — had it been a *tam*, the same immediate seizure would have occurred.

The *gemara* then turns to the owner's perspective. Why should the owner pay only half? Could he not demand full compensation from the *shoel*, arguing that the *shoel* caused the loss of his ox by letting *beis din* seize it? The *shoel* responds that even if he had returned the ox, *beis din* would have seized it from the owner as well. The owner counters: I would have hidden it. The *shoel* counters: you still would have had to pay the full damage from your own funds — *sof sof me'alya havei mishtalem* — so the seizure of the ox caused you no additional net loss. The *gemara* notes that this logic holds only if the owner has other assets; if the ox was his only property, then the seizure did cost him leverage he could have used in negotiating. In that case, the *shoel* invokes the principle of *Rabbi Nosson* — *d'tanya Rabbi Nosson omer: minayin l'nosheh b'chaveiro maneh v'chaveiro b'chaveiro* — whereby one can pay a creditor's creditor directly, and the chain of obligations collapses: the *shoel* owed the ox to the owner, but the owner owed the ox to the *nizak*, and therefore paying *beis din* on behalf of the *nizak* was a legitimate discharge of the *shoel*'s obligation.

## Return & Reversion

### Return Case

The *beraita* then presents the second case: *hu'ad b'veit shoel* — the ox became a *muad* while in the borrower's possession, and *v'hechziro l'ba'alim* — it was returned to the owners. The ruling is: *ba'alim meshalem chatzi nezek v'shoel patur miklum* — the owner pays half and the *shoel* is completely exempt.

### Inconsistency Q

The *gemara* identifies a striking inconsistency. In the first case, when the owner gave the ox to the *shoel* and it was already a *muad*, it retained its *muad* status — possession change to the *shoel* did not revert it. In the second case, when the *shoel* returns it to the owner, it does revert — becoming a *tam* again, with the owner paying only half. Why would giving it to the *shoel* not trigger *reshus meshaneh*, while returning it to the owner does?

### Tavra Position

*Rabbi Yochanan* responds with *tavra* — these two rulings cannot both come from the same authority. The first part of the *beraita* assumes that *reshus meshaneh* does not apply — changing possession does not change status. The second part assumes the opposite. They are irreconcilable.

### Rabba Consistent

*Rabba* offers a different response. He reads the *beraita* as consistent throughout: *reshus meshaneh*, the change of possession, does always revert the status. Therefore, consistent with the second part of the *beraita*, the *seifa* is correct that returning it to the owner reverts it to *tam*.

But then why, in the *reisha*, does giving it to the *shoel* not revert it back to *tam*?

### Fairness Argument

*Rabba* explains: *lav kol kaminach d'me'adas lei l'torai* — the owner can say to the *shoel*, you have no power to impose this change on my ox. The *shoel* took the animal with knowledge of its status — he willingly accepted it. But when the *shoel* returns it, the owner has no choice but to accept it back. The ox was always his; it must come back to him. If the *shoel* has, in the meantime, allowed it to become a *muad* — by receiving the warnings and failing to act upon them — and then simply hands the animal back, the owner finds himself stuck with a *muad* ox through no act of his own. He never chose to accept a *muad* in place of the *tam* he parted with.

This principle runs deeper than a simple fairness argument. Since the *shoel* knew the animal would necessarily return to the owner, the status of *muad* that was established under the *shoel*'s watch cannot, in fairness, be imposed on a party who had no say in that process. The owner had no ability to oversee the warnings, respond to them, or contest them — the *muad* status was created by someone who was temporarily custodian of property that was always going to revert. Therefore, upon return, the status reverts as well.

The converse applies to the *reisha*: when the owner initially gives the ox to the *shoel*, the *shoel* is a willing recipient. He accepts it knowingly, with full information. That voluntary acceptance is why the *muad* status is retained in his hands — he made a choice to take on responsibility for this animal as it stood.

This framework may also explain the broader application of *reshus meshaneh* discussed earlier. When an *apotropus* turns an ox into a *muad* and then returns it to the now-competent owner, the same logic applies. The reversion occurs not because of some inherent characteristic of the ox changing, but because of the unfairness of imposing a legal status — established by a custodian whose tenure was by definition temporary — onto a party who had no agency in the matter.

By contrast, *reshus meshaneh* does not apply when an ox changes hands through a voluntary sale. A purchaser who knowingly buys a *muad* assumes that status. He has made a choice to acquire this animal as it is.

### Rav Pappa Q

*Rav Pappa* raises a further question: if the *seifa* demonstrates that *reshus meshaneh* applies — that returning the ox to the owner reverts it — can we use the *seifa* to shed light on the *reisha*? The *gemara* explores whether the cases are truly parallel or whether a principled distinction between them is available.

### Shomer vs Ownership

The *gemara* ultimately identifies a clean distinction among three scenarios. The first is a full transfer of ownership, as when Reuven buys the ox from Shimon. Here, the ox genuinely changes its legal identity and there is a genuine question about whether it should revert — the new owner willingly accepted it, which suggests it should retain its status, but the change of ownership is real. The second scenario is that of a *shomer* who takes the ox to watch: here the ox formally remains the owner's property the entire time — *kol makom sheholech shem be'alav alav* — wherever it goes, the owner's name goes with it — so there is the least basis for reversion, and the *shoel*'s willing acceptance reinforces this. The third scenario is when the *shoel* returns it to the owner against whom the *muad* status was created by someone else's custodial relationship: here the case for reversion is strongest, because the owner is involuntarily receiving back property whose status was altered without his participation.

## Stadium Ox

### Stadium Status

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah* concerning a *shor itstadyon* — a stadium ox, an animal forced into combat in an arena. Such an ox, the *mishnah* rules, is not subject to the death penalty even if it kills a person.

### Sacrifice Q

The *gemara* raises the question: *mahu legabei mizbeach* — may such an ox be used as a sacrifice? Even where there are not two witnesses sufficient to impose the death penalty — for example, where the killing was established through the owner's own admission or a single witness — the ox might nonetheless be halachically disqualified for the *mizbeach*, on the grounds that it is an animal through which an *aveirah* was committed. The question is whether an ox that was forced to gore — provoked and not acting from its own inclination — carries the same stigma.

### Rava Shmuel

*Rava* says *kasher* — the ox is valid for the *mizbeach*. *Ones hu* — the ox was compelled; it did not act willingly. Since it was essentially used as a tool by another agent, the act does not stigmatize the animal. *Shmuel* says *pasul* — invalid. *Harei ne'evdah bo aveira* — an *aveirah* was performed through this animal, and that fact alone disqualifies it, regardless of whether the animal was willing or coerced.

### Korban Exclusions

The *gemara* challenges *Rava* from a *beraita*. The *torah*'s phrase *min habeheima* — from the animal — is interpreted as an exclusion: not all animals may be brought. Specifically, *lehotzi et harov'ea v'et hanirva* — to exclude an animal that engaged in an act of bestiality, whether penetrating or penetrated. *Min habakar* — from the cattle — *lehotzi et hane'evad*, to exclude an animal that was worshipped as an idol. *Min hatzon* — from the flock — *lehotzi et hamuktzeh*, to exclude an animal that was designated for idolatrous worship even without actually being worshipped. *U'min hatzon* — the additional *vav* — *lehotzi et hanogeach*, to exclude a goring ox. All of these categories are disqualified from use as a *korban*.

### Rabbi Shimon Q

*Rabbi Shimon* asks: if we already know that a *rov'ea* is excluded, why must the *torah* also state that a *nogeach* is excluded? And conversely, if we know the *nogeach* is excluded, why state the *rov'ea*? Both are cases where the animal committed an *aveirah* for which the ox would be stoned — seemingly the same category.

### Different Factors

*Rabbi Shimon* answers his own question: *yesh barov'ea she'ein banogeach v'yesh banogeach she'ein barov'ea* — each has a feature the other lacks. By *rov'ea* — an act of bestiality — *asa bo ones keratzon* — the *torah* treats a coerced act the same as a willing act. Whether the animal was the active or passive party, it is disqualified. By *nogeach* — goring — *lo asa bo ones keratzon* — the *torah* distinguishes: only voluntary goring disqualifies; a provoked ox is not treated the same way. On the other hand, a *nogeach* triggers the obligation of *kofer*, while a *rov'ea* does not.

### Korban Unresolved

The *gemara* considers whether this *beraita* supports *Rava*'s position. The *beraita* says that by *nogeach*, a coerced act is not treated the same as a willing act — which seems to suggest that a provoked ox is *kasher* for the *mizbeach*. However, the *gemara* pushes back: this distinction about *ones* vs. *ratzon* in the *beraita* refers to the question of whether the ox is stoned, not to whether it is disqualified for a *korban*. In the context of stoning, it is well established that a provoked ox is not stoned. The *beraita*'s statement that *nogeach lo asa bo ones keratzon* describes this rule about the death penalty — it cannot be taken as establishing the *korban* rule, since that is precisely the open question. To use the *beraita* to resolve the *korban* question would be circular.

The result is that neither side has been proven. The disanalogy between *rov'ea* — where coercion and volition are treated identically — and *nogeach* — where they diverge for purposes of stoning — cannot be cleanly extended to the *korban* question. Whether a provoked *nogeach* is valid for the *mizbeach* thus remains *teiku* — unresolved, to be continued.
