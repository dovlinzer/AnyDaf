# Shabbat 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mekomah Patur

### Ledge Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת וְנוֹתֵן לְעָנִי, מֵעָנִי וְנוֹתֵן לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת. וְאִם נָטַל וְנָתַן — שְׁלָשְׁתָּן פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master** also **said** in the *Tosefta:* A person standing on the threshold may take an object from the homeowner and give an object to him, and he may take an object from the poor person or give an object to him, **as long as he does not take** the object **from the homeowner and give** it **to a poor person** or **from a poor person and give** it **to the homeowner. And,** however, **if he took** an object from one **and gave** it to the other, certainly no labor prohibited by Torah law was performed in that case, and all **three of them are exempt.**

The daf opens at the very bottom of 8b, where the Gemara returns to a point raised in the Tosefta concerning the *makom patur* — an exempt space. The specific case involves a ledge or threshold that is less than four by four *tefachim* in area, a dimension too small to qualify as a significant domain. Because it is less than four by four, this space is a *makom patur*, and it therefore lacks the halachic standing of either a *reshut ha-yachid* or a *reshut ha-rabim*.

### Three Party Exemption

The Tosefta states: *u-vilvad she-lo yitol mi-ba'al ha-bayit ve-noten le-ani, mi-ani ve-noten le-ba'al ha-bayit* — one must not take from the homeowner and give to the poor man, or from the poor man and give to the homeowner, using this ledge as an intermediate stop. That would be problematic because even though each individual step is technically permitted — transferring from a *reshut ha-rabim* to a *makom patur* and from a *makom patur* to a *reshut ha-yachid* — combining them in sequence would easily lead to transferring directly from one domain to the other.

Nevertheless, the Tosefta adds: *ve-im natal ve-natan, shloshtan pturim* — if all three parties did participate in such a transfer, all three are exempt. The Gemara will soon probe this ruling. One might explain the exemption of all three by saying each party performed only half of the forbidden act, as in the famous case in the Mishnah where the homeowner and the poor man each participate equally and are both exempt. But the Tosefta implies something broader than that: even if the person standing on the *makom patur* picked the object up with one hand and placed it in the other — so that the object fully traversed the space — all parties remain exempt because it passed through a *makom patur*. The Gemara seizes on this.

### Rava's Kashya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרָבָא. דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הַמַּעֲבִיר חֵפֶץ מִתְּחִילַּת אַרְבַּע לְסוֹף אַרְבַּע בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהֶעֱבִירוֹ דֶּרֶךְ עָלָיו, חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **Say that this will be a conclusive refutation of Rava's** opinion, **as Rava said: One who transfers an object from the beginning of four** cubits **to the end of four** cubits **in the public domain, even though he transferred it** via the airspace **above it,** i.e., he raised the object more than ten handbreadths above the ground of the public domain, which is an exempt domain, still **he is liable** for carrying in the public domain.

The Gemara raises the question: *leima tehavi tiyuvta de-Rava* — is this not a refutation of Rava? For Rava stated: *ha-ma'avir chefetz mi-tchilas arba le-sof arba* — one who transfers an object from the beginning of four *amot* to the end of four *amot* in the *reshut ha-rabim*, *af al pi she-he'eviro derech alav chayav* — even though he did so *derech alav*, through or over himself, he is liable. The question is: if passing through the airspace of a *makom patur* in this way still renders one liable, how is that consistent with the Tosefta's ruling that passing through an actual *makom patur* exempts all three parties?

### Rashi on Derech

The term *derech alav* is the crux of the difficulty, and Rashi and Tosafot understand it differently. Rashi reads *alav* literally — "on top of him" — meaning the person lifted the object ten *tefachim* off the ground, above the *reshut ha-rabim*, so that in the middle of its journey it passed through airspace that qualifies as a *makom patur*. Nevertheless, Rava rules, the person is liable. This is structurally similar to the Gemara's earlier discussion of passing an object through a *karmelit* — there, too, the question was whether transiting through an intermediate domain breaks the continuity of the forbidden act — except that here the intermediate zone is a *makom patur*, which might seem an even stronger basis for exemption.

### Tosafot Interpretation

Tosafot, by contrast, reads *derech alav* not as "on top of him" but as "in front of him" — *lefanav*. On this reading, the person did not lift the object ten *tefachim* off the ground at all; rather, the point is that the object passed directly in front of the person while in motion. Tosafot's concern is to resolve the phrase with another Gemara that requires accommodation, and it is that textual pressure which drives this alternative reading. Tosafot himself notes that the term *al gavei* would more naturally mean "in front of," while Rashi's reading of "on top of" would ordinarily require a different phrase. One can evaluate which reading strains the language less, but what is pushing Tosafot is his need to harmonize a different Gemara. In any case, whichever reading one adopts, the Gemara's structural question remains: why is Rava's case any different from the case of the three parties on the *makom patur*?

### Nach vs. Lo Nach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָא נָח, הָכָא נָח.
>
> **Translation:** **There,** in the *halakha* stated by Rava, the object **did not come to rest** in an exempt domain; it merely passed through its airspace. However, **here,** when transferred via the threshold, the object **came to rest** in an exempt domain, and as a result, the act of carrying out was divided into two separate actions, neither of which involves a Torah prohibition.

The Gemara's answer is simple and decisive: *hatam lo nach, hacha nach* — there, the object did not come to rest; here, it did. In Rava's case, whether the object passed through the airspace above ten *tefachim* or passed in front of the person, it never stopped. It moved continuously from its starting point to its ending point, and therefore the person is liable despite the intermediate passage through a zone that might otherwise exempt. In the case of the three parties, however, the person standing on the *makom patur* actually set the object down — it came to rest on the ledge — and then picked it up again and transferred it to the next party. That constitutes two distinct acts, each of which, taken alone, is not only exempt but actually permitted: transferring from a *reshut ha-rabim* to a *makom patur*, and from a *makom patur* to a *reshut ha-yachid*. The combined result is rabbinically problematic — because it too easily approximates the full biblical prohibition of transferring from one major domain to another — but both parties engaged in each individual act remain exempt.

Rava's *chiddush*, then, is that when there is no *nach* — no coming to rest — you remain liable even when the object transits a *makom patur*. We previously had to agonize over the analogous question involving a *karmelit*; the case of transit through a *makom patur* is in some ways an even stronger candidate for leniency, and yet Rava rules *chayav*. That is a genuine *chiddush*. The *chiddush* of the Tosefta, by contrast, is not simply that one is *patur* — it is that the combined act, though producing that *patur* verdict, is nonetheless *asur d'rabbanan*. Both points stand.

## Iskofah Two Domains

### Iskofah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: אִסְקוּפָּה מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת שְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַפֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ — כְּלִפְנִים. פֶּתַח נָעוּל — כְּלַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** Later in the *Tosefta*, ***Aḥerim* say:** Depending on the circumstances, **a threshold serves two domains: When the entrance is open,** the threshold is subsumed within the house and **it is** considered to be a private domain **like the inside** of the house. And when **the entrance is locked,** the threshold is not subsumed within the house, and **it is** considered to be a public domain **like the outside.**

The Gemara moves to the final clause of the Mishnah, which discusses a ledge — an *iskofah* — that serves two domains simultaneously. The case is not a *makom patur* at all, but a ledge that can be classified as either *reshut ha-yachid* or *reshut ha-rabim* depending on circumstances. The specific scenario involves a *chatzer* — a courtyard surrounded by houses — with a ledge positioned at its entrance, opening onto the *reshut ha-rabim*.

### Door Open Status

The Baraita cited by the Gemara states: *iskofah meshameshet shtei reshuyot* — this threshold serves two domains. *Bizman shehapetach patuach, kilfanim* — when the door is open, the threshold is treated as part of the interior, that is, part of the *reshut ha-yachid* of the courtyard or house. Rashi understands the interior to refer to a courtyard; Tosafot extends this to include either a courtyard or a house, though that distinction is not the focus of the analysis here.

### Door Closed Status

*Petach na'ul, kilchutz* — when the door is closed, the threshold is treated as being on the outside, like the *reshut ha-rabim*. Both Rashi and Tosafot, however, are careful to point out that this does not mean the *iskofah* literally becomes a *reshut ha-rabim* in a biblical sense. Biblically, it retains the status of *reshut ha-yachid* — it is, after all, surrounded by three walls. What the Baraita means is only that one is not permitted to carry in that space. Tosafot explicitly states this: when the door is closed, *mid'oraisa* it remains *reshut ha-yachid*, but the rabbinic rule does not permit carrying there.

### Physical Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ לֶחִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: תּוֹךְ הַפֶּתַח צָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: When the entrance is open the threshold is considered to be like a private domain, **and** is this so **even though it does not have a post** on its side? **Didn't Rav Ḥama bar Gurya say** that **Rav said: The opening** in the wall, i.e., the doorway, **requires another post** in order **to permit** carrying there?

The Gemara's deeper question is: why does the state of the door make any difference at all? To understand this, one must picture the physical setup carefully.

Imagine a courtyard surrounded on three sides by the walls of houses. The fourth side is open to the *reshut ha-rabim*, except that the walls extend slightly inward — or there is a post, a *lechi*, positioned at the entrance — which allows carrying within the courtyard under the rules governing a *mavoi* or *chatzer*. The *iskofah* is the threshold space in the width of the doorway itself, between the interior courtyard and the *reshut ha-rabim*.

The difficulty is this: even when the door is open, the threshold space — the width of the doorway — has only three walls. The two sides of the doorway and the wall behind it give three walls, but there is no beginning of a fourth wall — no *lechi* and no *korah* (beam) — within that doorway space itself. A *lechi* positioned at the outer edge of the courtyard entrance serves the courtyard interior, but what helps the space within the doorway width?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵימָא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה — וְהָאָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: תּוֹךְ הַפֶּתַח, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה, צָרִיךְ לֶחִי אַחֵר לְהַתִּירוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that the *Tosefta* is referring to a threshold **that does not have** an area of **four by four** handbreadths, which is not considered an independent area and therefore does not require a post, **didn't Rav Ḥama bar Gurya say** that **Rav said** explicitly: **The opening, even though it does not have** an area of **four by four** handbreadths, **requires another post** in order **to permit** carrying there?

Rav Chama bar Gurya said in the name of Rav: *tocha pesach tzarich lechi acher le-hatiro* — the inside of a doorway requires an additional *lechi* to permit carrying in it. So even if the door is open, the space of the threshold itself lacks the fourth-wall element needed to allow carrying. One might propose that since the doorway width is less than four by four, it has no independent significance — it is *batel* to the *reshut ha-yachid* behind it and piggybacks on that domain's valid *lechi*. But Rav Chama bar Gurya explicitly rejects this: even if the doorway space is less than four by four — even if in isolation it would be a *makom patur* — one still needs an additional *lechi* outside the doorway to permit carrying there.

This then is the standing question: granted that when the door is closed, the space is problematic — only three walls with no fourth-wall element. But even when the door is open, it has the same three walls and no fourth-wall element. What does the open door change?

## Korah Beam Analysis

### Rav Yehuda Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הָכָא בְּאִיסְקוּפַּת מָבוֹי עָסְקִינַן, חֶצְיוֹ מְקוֹרֶה וְחֶצְיוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקוֹרֶה, וְקֵירוּיוֹ כְּלַפֵּי פְנִים. פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ, כְּלִפְנִים. פֶּתַח נָעוּל, כְּלַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Here we are dealing with the threshold of an alleyway** open to the public domain on only one side. Although, by Torah law, it is considered a private domain, the Sages required him to establish a fourth symbolic partition on the side open to the public domain. This alleyway was covered, and this covering extended to part of the threshold in a manner that **half of it is covered and half of it is not covered, and the covering is** over the part of the threshold **toward the inside.** In that case, if **the entrance is open,** its legal status is **like** that of **the inside,** as it is considered as if there were a partition extending from the edge of the roofing above to below, based on the halakhic principle: Lower the partition. The opening of the alleyway is thereby sealed, rendering it a private domain. However, when **the entrance is locked,** it is no longer possible to consider the covering as a partition, and therefore the part of the threshold that is beyond the locked door of the alleyway **is** considered **like the outside,** i.e., like a public domain.

The Gemara presents two answers to this question. The first is given by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *hacha be-iskofas mavoi askinan* — we are dealing with the threshold of a *mavoi*. Tosafot notes that this implies the earlier discussion was about a house or *chatzer*, but for the purposes of the core halachic question that distinction is not material. The critical detail Rav Yehuda introduces is this: *chetzyo mekureh ve-chetzyo she-eino mekureh, ve-kiroyav kelapei pnim* — half of the threshold space is covered by a partial roof — specifically, a beam — and the covered portion is directed toward the inside.

The physical picture is as follows: the threshold opening is spanned by a beam (*korah*) positioned toward the interior side of the doorway. When the door is open, the space under consideration from the beam outward encompasses the full interior of the courtyard, which is larger than four by four. In that case, the *korah* functions as a valid halachic marker for a fourth wall, and one may carry up to its outer edge. When the door is closed, however, the space directly under the beam — the space between the beam and the closed door — is less than four by four.

### Beam Efficacy Condition

This distinction is what drives the ruling. A *korah* only functions as a halachic device when it relates to a space of at least four by four. If the space it covers or relates to is less than four by four, it is too insignificant for the *korah* to do halachic work. So when the door is open, the *korah* relates to the full interior courtyard — well over four by four — and functions perfectly, extending the *reshut ha-yachid* to its outer edge. When the door is closed, the *korah* is left relating only to the tiny space between itself and the closed door — less than four by four — and therefore cannot function.

### Four-by-Four Rule

It is important to clarify what "four by four" means in this context. The requirement is not that the *korah* itself be four *tefachim* wide — a thin beam can serve perfectly well as a *korah* in a normal courtyard configuration. Rather, the requirement is that the *korah* must relate to a space of at least four by four beneath or beyond it. A *korah* covering or fronting a space less than four by four is halachically inoperative. This is the key to understanding why the open door and the closed door produce such different results: the door changes not the beam itself but the space to which the beam relates.

### Rav Ashi Lavud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם בְּאִיסְקוּפַּת בַּיִת עָסְקִינַן, וּכְגוֹן שֶׁקֵּירָהּ בִּשְׁתֵּי קוֹרוֹת שֶׁאֵין בָּזוֹ אַרְבָּעָה וְאֵין בָּזוֹ אַרְבָּעָה, וְאֵין בֵּין זוֹ לָזוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְדֶלֶת בָּאֶמְצַע. פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ, כְּלִפְנִים. פֶּתַח נָעוּל, כְּלַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Actually,** we can say that **we are dealing with the threshold of a house, and** in a special circumstance, **a case where he covered** the threshold **with two beams.** Furthermore, **neither this** beam **is four** handbreadths wide, **nor is that** beam **four** handbreadths wide, **and there is not** a gap of **three** handbreadths **between this** one **and that** one, and there is a **door between** the two beams. In this case, when **the entrance is open,** since there is a space of less than three handbreadths between the beams and, based on the principle of *lavud*, any space less than three handbreadths is considered non-existent, the two beams are considered to be one wide beam. It is considered as if there were a partition extending from the edge of the roofing above to below, based on the halakhic principle: Lower the partition. The threshold is thereby sealed and considered a full-fledged private domain **like the inside.** However, when **the entrance is locked,** the two beams do not join together to become one anymore. Since the door creates a separation between them and the outer beam is less than four handbreadths wide, it is not considered a roof from which a partition extends to the ground, and the area under this beam **is** considered to be a public domain **like the outside.**

Rav Ashi offers a second answer, which is a variation on the same principle but with an added sophistication. *Le-olam be-iskufas bayis askinan* — we may be dealing with the threshold of a house. The case he constructs is as follows: *u-kegon she-kira bi-shtei korot she-ein be-zu arba ve-ein be-zu arba, ve-ein bein zu le-zu sheloshah, ve-ha-delet be-emtza* — the threshold is covered not by a single beam but by two beams, neither of which is four *tefachim* wide, and the gap between them is less than three *tefachim*, with the door positioned in between the two beams.

The principle of *lavud* — the halachic rule that a gap of less than three *tefachim* is treated as if it were closed — allows two adjacent beams separated by less than three *tefachim* to combine and be treated as a single continuous beam. If the two beams together span enough to constitute four *tefachim*, they function together as a valid *korah*, enabling carrying in the space beneath them.

### Door Separates Lavud

But the door disrupts this mechanism. When the door is closed, it physically intervenes between the two beams, severing their ability to combine through *lavud*. Each beam is then evaluated independently, and since neither alone is four *tefachim* wide, neither functions as a valid *korah*. The space in front of the door is left with only three walls and no operative fourth-wall element, and carrying is not permitted.

When the door is open, the two beams are once again able to operate together. Through *lavud* they combine to form a halachically complete *korah*, the space they front is large enough — being the full interior plus the threshold — and carrying is permitted up to the outer edge.

To sharpen the contrast further: had the two beams been a single, solid *korah* genuinely four *tefachim* wide, the door's position between the sub-sections would be irrelevant — it would remain one complete *korah*, and carrying would be permitted even with the door closed. The reason Rav Ashi uses the more complex case of two beams combined by *lavud* is precisely to make the ruling interesting: it is only *because* their unity depends on *lavud* that the door can interrupt their function. The door is not interrupting a solid beam — it is interrupting the halachic fiction by which two separate, insufficient beams are treated as one. Sever that fiction, and both beams lose their efficacy simultaneously. The case of the solid four-*tefachim korah* would have been different, and that contrast illuminates the limits of *lavud* as a halachic tool.

A practical analogy worth noting: if a roof extends over the outer edge of a doorway — even if the space beneath that roof is less than four by four — the roof itself being genuinely four *tefachim* wide would be enough to make it function. The threshold case is intrinsically parallel to that scenario and helps make the abstract rule more concrete.

## Distinct Reshut

### Four-by-Ten Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיְתָה אִיסְקוּפָּה גְּבוֹהָה עֲשָׂרָה וּרְחָבָה אַרְבָּעָה, הֲרֵי זוֹ רְשׁוּת לְעַצְמָהּ. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי. דְּאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא שְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת וְהֵן רְשׁוּת אַחַת, כְּגוֹן עַמּוּד בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה וְרָחָב אַרְבָּעָה — אָסוּר לְכַתֵּף עָלָיו. גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם תֵּל בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Sage also said in the *Tosefta* that **if the threshold was ten** handbreadths **high and four** by four handbreadths **wide, it is an independent domain,** even if it was inside a private domain. The Gemara comments: **This supports** the opinion of **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi, as Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi said that Rabbi Meir used to say: Any place that you find two domains,** i.e., two places, each of which is sufficiently distinct to be an independent domain, **and** even though **they are** halakhically **one domain,** i.e., **in a case** where **a pillar** that is **ten** handbreadths **high and four** by four **wide** is standing **in the private domain,** even though the pillar is a private domain based on its measurements, **it is prohibited** by rabbinic law **to** adjust a burden on one's **shoulders upon it** and to lift an object from the ground of the private domain and place it atop the pillar, as the pillar is deemed by its measurements to be an independent domain. It is prohibited by **a decree** issued by the Sages **due to a** similar situation, the case of a **mound** of that size **in the public domain.** In the public domain, lifting an object from the ground and placing it on the mound constitutes a violation of the Torah prohibition of carrying out from the public domain to the private domain. Therefore, the Sages prohibited placing an object on a pillar even in the private domain.

The final point of the Mishnah introduces a different case: *iskofah gevoha asara ve-rechava arba'a, harei zu reshuta le-atzma* — if the threshold is ten *tefachim* high and four wide, it constitutes its own domain. This is not merely saying it is a *reshut ha-yachid*; it seems to be saying something more specific — it is a *reshut ha-yachid* that is structurally distinct from the courtyard behind it. The Gemara asks what the force of this ruling is.

### Rabbi Meir Two Domains

The Gemara says this lends support to Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi, who reported a ruling of Rabbi Meir: *kol makom she-ata motzei shenei reshuyot ve-hein reshuta achas* — wherever you find two structurally distinct spaces, each with the halachic status of *reshut ha-yachid*, the question arises whether you may transfer between them freely. The example Rabbi Meir uses is *amud bi-reshut ha-yachid gevoha asara ve-rachav arba'a* — a column or elevated structure standing in the middle of a *reshut ha-yachid*, ten *tefachim* high and four by four in area. Intuitively one might assume that since both the floor and the top of the column are *reshut ha-yachid*, there is no prohibition in placing objects on top of it.

### Filing Cabinet Gezerah

Rabbi Meir rules otherwise: *assur le-katef alav* — it is forbidden to load things onto it. Why? *Gezeria mishum ketef reshut ha-rabim* — a rabbinic decree lest one do the same with a similar elevated structure in the *reshut ha-rabim*. A column ten *tefachim* high and four by four standing in the *reshut ha-rabim* is itself a *reshut ha-yachid* — and transferring from the *reshut ha-rabim* up onto such a column would be a biblical violation. Since placing objects on top of elevated structures does not feel like carrying between domains — it simply feels like placing something on top of a piece of furniture — there is a risk one would do the same thing outdoors without recognizing the prohibition. Therefore, Rabbi Meir prohibits doing it even indoors as a protective decree.

The concern has an intuitive logic: the act of placing something atop a filing cabinet or a high shelf feels categorically different from the act of carrying an object from one room to another. One does not experience it as a domain transfer. That subjective gap between the act and its halachic characterization is precisely the kind of cognitive slip that rabbinic decrees are designed to close.

### Tosafot Limitation

Tosafot raises an obvious limiting question: if this *gezeira* applies to elevated structures inside a *reshut ha-yachid*, does it also apply to carrying from the ground floor of a house to the upper floor? After all, each floor is technically a distinct *reshut ha-yachid*.

Tosafot resolves this by distinguishing between a structure whose status as a separate *reshut ha-yachid* is not visually obvious — like a column or a tall cabinet standing in the middle of an open space — and a structure whose walls are clearly apparent. An upper story, surrounded by visible walls that make its *reshut ha-yachid* status unmistakable, does not trigger the same concern. The worry is specifically for elevated objects whose independent domain status is not intuitively apparent, so that carrying to or from them feels like simply placing an object rather than transferring between domains. By contrast, if one looks through a window between two clearly-walled adjacent houses and passes an object through, there is no concern — the domain boundaries are visually clear. It should be noted that we do not rule like Rabbi Meir in practice, and this position is therefore of theoretical rather than practical import.

## Mincha Time Restrictions

### Prohibited Activities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יֵשֵׁב אָדָם לִפְנֵי הַסַּפָּר סָמוּךְ לַמִּנְחָה עַד שֶׁיִּתְפַּלֵּל. לֹא יִכָּנֵס אָדָם לַמֶּרְחָץ, וְלֹא לַבּוּרְסְקִי, וְלֹא לֶאֱכוֹל, וְלֹא לָדִין, וְאִם הִתְחִילוּ — אֵין מַפְסִיקִין. מַפְסִיקִין לִקְרוֹת קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאֵין מַפְסִיקִין לִתְפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: After having dealt with the limited and defined topic of the *halakhot* of carrying out on Shabbat, the mishna begins to deal with the *halakhot* of Shabbat chronologically, beginning with activities that one may not perform prior to the onset of Shabbat. With regard to one's daily conduct, the mishna says: **A person may not sit before the barber adjacent to** the time of ***minḥa* until he recites** the afternoon **prayer. And a person may not enter the bathhouse and may not** enter **to** work in **a tannery** [***burseki***]. **And** he may **neither** begin **to eat** a meal **nor to** sit in **judgment** until he prays. **And** however, **if they** already **began** engaging in those activities, **they need not stop** and recite the *Amida* prayer. The *tanna* articulated a principle: **One stops** engaging in all of these activities **to recite *Shema* and one does not stop to** recite the *Amida* **prayer.**

The Gemara now turns to a completely new topic — the Mishnah on 9b — which is the second Mishnah of this chapter. To understand the larger context: this Mishnah is the middle of a three-Mishnah sequence. The first Mishnah addresses the laws of carrying on Shabbat; the third Mishnah will address going out on *Erev Shabbat* with objects in one's pocket, lest one forget them and carry on Shabbat. Bridging these two is the present Mishnah, which establishes a general concern about beginning activities that one might lose track of — potentially forgetting to perform a time-sensitive obligation.

The Mishnah states: *lo yeishev adam lifnei ha-sapar samuch la-mincha* — a person may not sit down before a barber close to *mincha* time *ad she-yispalel* — until he has davened. Similarly: *ve-lo yikanes adam le-merchatz* — one may not enter a bathhouse; *ve-lo le-burski* — nor begin work at a tannery; *ve-lo le-echol* — nor sit down to eat; *ve-lo la-din* — nor convene for a court case. All of these activities risk becoming so absorbing that one will forget to daven.

### No Interruption Rule

The Mishnah then states: *ve-im hitchil, ein mafsikin* — if one has already begun, we do not require him to interrupt. This rule applies because if there will be adequate time after completing the activity, we trust the person to daven afterward. A qualification not stated in this Mishnah but established elsewhere is that if completing the activity will cause one to miss the *tefilla* entirely, one would be required to stop and daven. But assuming time remains, the Mishnah adopts a lenient position: once one has begun, we do not impose the burden of interruption.

The Mishnah adds one exception: *mafsikin li-kri'at shema, ve-ein mafsikin li-tefilla* — we do interrupt for *kri'at shema* but not for *tefilla* (i.e., the *Amida*). This is because *kri'at shema* is a biblical obligation, and the heightened urgency of a biblical commandment overrides the allowance to continue a permitted activity. *Tefilla*, by contrast, is rabbinic in origin, and therefore once one has already begun a permissible activity, we do not impose the interruption.

### Mincha Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵי ״סָמוּךְ לַמִּנְחָה״? אִילֵּימָא לְמִנְחָה גְּדוֹלָה — אַמַּאי לָא? הָאִיכָּא שְׁהוּת בַּיּוֹם טוּבָא! אֶלָּא סָמוּךְ לְמִנְחָה קְטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: First, the Gemara seeks to clarify: **Which "adjacent to *minḥa*,"** in other words, adjacent to which *minḥa* is the mishna referring? There is a difference between the time of greater *minḥa* [*minḥa gedola*], which begins approximately a half hour after noon, and the time of lesser *minḥa* [*minḥa ketana*], which begins approximately two and a half hours before sunset. The Gemara elaborates: **If you say** that it is prohibited to perform all of these activities adjacent **to *minḥa gedola*, why not? Isn't there** still **much time** remaining **in the day? Rather,** the mishna means **adjacent to *minḥa ketana*.**

The Gemara asks: *hi samuch le-mincha* — which *mincha* does the Mishnah refer to? The day is divided into two relevant periods. *Mincha gedola* — the "large *mincha*" — begins at the sixth-and-a-half seasonal hour, which on a standard day corresponds to approximately 12:30 PM. This is the earliest time one may daven *mincha*. The rationale for the half-hour offset from noon involves a dispute among Rishonim: Rashi in one place suggests it is simply a practical allowance because people without clocks cannot detect the precise moment of noon; in another place Rashi explains that experientially, the sun does not begin to feel as though it is setting until after the window of 11:30 to 12:30, during which the sun appears directly overhead regardless of where the mathematical midpoint falls.

*Mincha ketana* — the "small *mincha*" — begins at the ninth-and-a-half seasonal hour, approximately 3:30 PM, and this is considered the ideal time for *mincha*. From 3:30 onward there are only two and a half hours of daylight remaining.

The question is which period triggers the Mishnah's restrictions. Logically, one would assume *mincha ketana*: at *mincha gedola*, one still has five and a half hours remaining in the day. How can anyone be concerned about forgetting to daven when there is that much time? The Gemara confirms this initial logic: *eileima le-Mincha Gedola, amai lo? Ha-ika she'os ha-yom tuba* — if it means before *mincha gedola*, why not? You have plenty of time. *Ela samuch le-mincha ketana* — so the Mishnah must mean right before *mincha ketana*, around 3:00 PM.

### Rav Yehoshua Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הִתְחִילוּ אֵין מַפְסִיקִין. נֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַן תְּפִלַּת הַמִּנְחָה, אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיִּטְעוֹם כְּלוּם קוֹדֶם שֶׁיִּתְפַּלֵּל תְּפִלַּת הַמִּנְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In that case, **if they started, they need not stop. Let us say that this will be a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: Once the time of the afternoon prayer has arrived, it is prohibited for a person to taste anything before he recites the afternoon prayer.** The implication is that even if one began to eat he must stop.

Having established this, the Gemara raises a contradiction with the position of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who said: *keivan she-higi'a zman tefilat ha-mincha, assur la-adam she-yitam klum kodem she-yispalel* — once the time for *mincha* prayer has arrived, it is forbidden for a person to taste anything before davening. The phrasing *assur litom klum* — forbidden to taste anything at all — sounds absolute, suggesting that even if one has already begun eating, one must stop. This stands in tension with the Mishnah's ruling that once one has begun, one does not interrupt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא: לְעוֹלָם סָמוּךְ לְמִנְחָה גְּדוֹלָה — וּבְתִסְפּוֹרֶת בֶּן אֶלְעָשָׂה. וְלֹא לַמֶּרְחָץ — לְכוּלַּהּ מִילְּתָא דְּמֶרְחָץ. וְלֹא לְבוּרְסְקִי — לְבוּרְסְקִי גְּדוֹלָה. וְלֹא לֶאֱכוֹל — בִּסְעוּדָה גְּדוֹלָה. וְלֹא לָדִין — בִּתְחִלַּת דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, that explanation is rejected and the Gemara says: **Actually** the mishna is referring to **adjacent to *minḥa gedola*,** and the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is dealing with adjacent to *minḥa ketana*. In response to the question: If the mishna means adjacent to *minḥa gedola* isn't there significant time remaining in the day? The Gemara explains that each of the activities enumerated in the mishna is performed in an especially time-consuming manner. When the mishna said: A person may not sit before the barber, it was referring **to a haircut of ben Elasa,** whose haircut was very complicated and required several hours to complete. When the mishna said: A person may **not** go **into the bathhouse** adjacent to *minḥa*, it was referring **to all matters** involved in a visit to **the bathhouse;** not only washing, but also washing one's hair, rinsing, and sweating. **And** he may **not** enter **the tannery** adjacent to *minḥa*, the reference is to **a large tannery** where there are many hides that require tanning and he must initiate the tanning process from the beginning. **And** he may **not** enter **to eat,** the reference is **to a big meal,** which lasts a long time. **And** he may **not** enter **to** sit in **judgment,** refers to a judge who enters at **the beginning of** the **trial,** and, generally, it will take a long time until a verdict is reached.

The Gemara reconciles this by distributing the two positions across the two time periods: the Mishnah's lenient "don't interrupt once you've started" rule applies to *mincha gedola*, while Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's absolute prohibition applies to *mincha ketana*. This reconciliation, however, pushes the Mishnah's "don't start" rule all the way back to *mincha gedola*. And if one cannot begin at *mincha gedola*, the question returns: why not? There are five and a half hours left.

The Gemara's first answer is that we are not talking about ordinary activities but about specifically extended ones: *u-vetisporet ben Elasha* — the haircut in question is the famously elaborate hairdo perfected by Ben Elasha, where each strand of hair is arranged so that the tip of one hair touches the root of the next — the kind of styling that can consume an entire afternoon. Similarly, *le-kula mirsa de-merchatz* — not merely a bath but the full spa regimen. *Le-burski gedolah* — the full tanning process from beginning to end. *Be-seuda gedolah* — a large meal on the scale of a wedding feast. *Be-techilat din* — a court case from its opening, which may run for days. Only these outsized activities are prohibited beginning at *mincha gedola*. The result is a relatively lenient ruling: ordinary versions of these activities may be begun even at *mincha gedola*, while only especially extended versions trigger the restriction.

## Rav Achai Stringent

### Haircut Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם בְּתִסְפּוֹרֶת דִּידַן, לְכַתְּחִילָּה אַמַּאי לֹא יֵשֵׁב — גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִשָּׁבֵר הַזּוּג. וְלֹא לַמֶּרְחָץ — לְהַזִּיעַ בְּעָלְמָא. לְכַתְּחִלָּה אַמַּאי לָא — גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִתְעַלְּפֶה. וְלֹא לַבּוּרְסְקִי — לְעַיּוֹנֵי בְּעָלְמָא. לְכַתְּחִלָּה אַמַּאי לָא — דִּילְמָא חָזֵי פְּסֵידָא בִּזְבִינֵיהּ וּמִטְּרִיד. וְלֹא לֶאֱכוֹל — בִּסְעוּדָה קְטַנָּה. לְכַתְּחִלָּה אַמַּאי לָא — דִילְמָא אָתֵי לְאִמְּשׁוֹכֵי. וְלֹא לָדִין — בִּגְמַר הַדִּין. לְכַתְּחִלָּה אַמַּאי לָא — דִילְמָא חָזֵי טַעְמָא וְסָתַר דִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** Indeed the mishna can be explained as referring to *minḥa gedola* and **actually,** even **our** ordinary **haircut** is prohibited. ***Ab initio*, why may he not sit** before the barber adjacent to the time of *minḥa*? Due to **a decree lest the scissors break,** and considerable time pass until they repair the scissors or obtain others. When the mishna said: A person may **not** enter **the bathhouse** adjacent to *minḥa*, it is prohibited even if he is entering **just to sweat. *Ab initio*, why** may he **not** enter? Due to **a decree** issued by the Sages **lest he faint** in the bathhouse and considerable time elapse until he recovers. **And** he may **not** enter **the tannery** adjacent to *minḥa*, even if he intends **just to examine** the skins. ***Ab initio*, why** may he **not** enter? Due to the concern that **perhaps he will notice damage to his merchandise and become anxious** and come to restore what was ruined. **And** he may not enter **to eat a meal** adjacent to the time of *minḥa* is referring even **to a small meal. *Ab initio*, why** may he **not** enter? There is concern that **perhaps he will come to extend** his meal for a long time. **And** he may **not** enter **to** sit in **judgment** adjacent to the time of *minḥa*, the mishna is referring even **at the conclusion of** the **trial. *Ab initio*, why** may he **not** enter? Due to concern that **perhaps he will find a reason,** contrary to what he originally thought, **and will overturn the verdict** completely, necessitating the restart of the trial from the beginning.

Rav Achai bar Yaakov offers a far more stringent position. He contends that even ordinary versions of these activities cannot be begun at *mincha gedola*, and he provides a reason for each. Regarding the haircut: *gezeira shema yishaver ha-zog* — we are concerned that the scissors might break mid-haircut. Obtaining replacement scissors or having them sharpened was not trivial — it could require locating a metalworker and waiting an extended time — so what was expected to be a brief visit could turn into hours.

### Bathhouse Risk

Regarding the bathhouse, even an ordinary *shvitz*: *gezeira shema yitalfe* — we are concerned the person might faint in the steam room. What begins as a short visit could turn into a medical emergency requiring hours of attention. The ordinary case is therefore also prohibited.

### Tanning Risk

Regarding the tannery, even a casual inspection visit — merely checking in on the progress of one's hides: *dilma chazi pseida bi-zvini u-mitrid* — perhaps the person will discover a problem or defect in the hides and become completely preoccupied with addressing it. A quick stop becomes an extended stay.

### Eating Risk

Regarding eating, even a small meal: *dilma ati le-amshuchei* — small meals have a tendency to become large meals. One sits down for something modest and finds oneself still at the table hours later.

### Court Case Risk

Regarding a court case, even when the litigants or judges come merely to wrap up — *be-gmar din*, the conclusion of a case already heard: *dilma chazi ta'ama li-stor ha-din* — perhaps someone will raise a new argument that overturns what seemed a settled matter, and the entire case must be relitigated from the beginning.

The implications of this position are quite sweeping. If one cannot begin even small activities starting at *mincha gedola* — 12:30 PM — because of the unpredictable ways in which they might extend, the restriction effectively covers the entire afternoon. One might even observe that such a rule would make it difficult to daven *mincha* at *mincha gedola* itself, given that one could not sit down for a meal beforehand.

The Gemara's final *halacha* on this question follows the first, more lenient position, because the first position was stated in the anonymous voice of the Gemara — the *stam*. Tosafot explicitly notes: *ve-hilcheta ke-lishna kama, de-satam lan ha-Shas* — we rule like the first approach because the Gemara anonymously adopted it; and the *stam* of the Gemara is attributable to Rav Ashi, who is *basra'i*, and we rule like the later authority. The *halacha* thus prohibits beginning only large-scale activities at *mincha gedola*.

The *Shulchan Aruch*, interestingly, rules most stringently — like the second position — holding that even small activities should not begin at 12:30 before davening. The *Rema*, by contrast, follows Tosafot's lenient ruling but adds his own nuance: he presents a spectrum of positions and concludes that we take both available leniencies. A large meal is prohibited only at *mincha ketana*; small activities are always permitted. The practical upshot is that the only genuinely prohibited case is sitting down to a wedding-style meal after 3:30 PM without having davened *mincha* first. Wedding guests routinely accommodate this by davening *mincha* at the *chassan's tish*. The *Rema* further notes that we have additional grounds for leniency in communities where people regularly announce the time of *mincha* services — the availability of reminders mitigates the concern that one will forget altogether.

## When Activity Begins

### Haircut Begin Point

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאֵימָתַי הַתְחָלַת תִּסְפּוֹרֶת? אָמַר רַב אָבִין: מִשֶּׁיַּנִּיחַ מַעְפּוֹרֶת שֶׁל סַפָּרִים עַל בִּרְכָּיו. וּמֵאֵימָתַי הַתְחָלַת מֶרְחָץ? אָמַר רַב אָבִין: מִשֶּׁיַּעֲרֶה מַעְפׇּרְתּוֹ הֵימֶנּוּ. וּמֵאֵימָתַי הַתְחָלַת בּוּרְסְקִי? — מִשֶּׁיִּקְשׁוֹר בֵּין כְּתֵיפָיו. וּמֵאֵימָתַי הַתְחָלַת אֲכִילָה? — רַב אָמַר: מִשֶּׁיִּטּוֹל יָדָיו. וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: מִשֶּׁיַּתִּיר חֲגוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that if he began one of the aforementioned activities, haircut, bath, tannery, meal, and judgment, he is not required to stop. The Gemara asked: **From when is** it considered **the beginning of the haircut? Rav Avin said: From when he places the barber's wrap over his knees. And from when** is it considered **the beginning of the bath? Rav Avin said: From when** the one entering the bathhouse to bathe **removes his** outer **wrap,** his cloak. **And from when** is it considered **the beginning of** his visit to **the tannery? From when he ties** the leather apron **between his shoulders** (*Me'iri*). **And from when** is it considered **the beginning of eating? Rav said: From when he** ritually **washes his hands** for the meal. **And Rabbi Ḥanina said: From when he loosens his belt.**

The Gemara now addresses a related question: at what point is an activity considered to have "begun," such that the principle of *ein mafsikin* — not interrupting — takes effect? Rav Avin rules: the haircut is considered begun *mi-she-yaniach mafores shel saparim al birkav* — from the moment the barber places his apron on the customer's lap. Even if not a single snip has yet been made, once the barber's cloth is draped over the customer, the process has formally commenced and one need not rise to daven.

### Bathhouse Begin Point

Rav Avin further rules: the bathhouse is considered begun *mi-she-ya'arim maphorto heimenu* — from when the person removes his headcovering, that is, the first article of clothing. Full disrobing is not required; the removal of the first garment already constitutes "having begun."

### Tanning Begin Point

The tanning process is considered begun *mi-she-yikeshor bein ketafav* — when the worker ties the leather apron around his shoulders, readying himself for the work. The donning of the work apron marks entry into the process.

### Eating Begin Point

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא לַן, וְהָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And they do not disagree.** Rather **this,** the statement of Rabbi Ḥanina, who said that the beginning of the meal is considered from when he loosens his belt, **is for us,** for the people of Babylonia, who are accustomed to close their belts tightly, and therefore the beginning of the meal is when one loosens his belt. **And that,** the statement of Rav, who said that the beginning of the meal is considered from when he ritually washes his hands, **is for them,** the people of Eretz Yisrael who did not close their belts tightly, and therefore only when one washes his hands does the meal begin.

The question of when eating begins generates a brief dispute. Rav says: *mi-she-yitol yadav* — from when one has washed one's hands in preparation for the meal. Rabbi Chanina says: *mi-she-yatir chagura* — from when one loosens one's belt. In preparation for a substantial meal, a person would untighten his belt to make room; that preparatory act, even before washing hands and certainly before eating, constitutes the beginning of the meal.

### Geographic Difference

The Gemara resolves this as follows: *lo peligi, ha lan ve-ha le-hu* — they are not actually arguing; each describes the custom of a different region. In *Eretz Yisrael*, Rashi explains, people did not wear their belts particularly tight, so the act of loosening a belt would not mark a meaningful threshold. For them, the beginning is washing hands. In Bavel, where tight belts were customary, loosening the belt was an earlier and more significant act of preparation, occurring even before handwashing — and that is the marker. The dispute, then, reflects a geographic difference in dress customs rather than a substantive halachic disagreement.

## Maariv Reshut Status

### Maariv Reshut Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָנֵי חַבְרִין בַּבְלָאֵי, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תְּפִלַּת עַרְבִית רְשׁוּת, כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁרָא לֵיהּ הֶמְיָינֵיהּ לָא מַטְרְחִינַן לֵיהּ, וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר חוֹבָה מַטְרְחִינַן לֵיהּ?! וְהָא תְּפִלַּת מִנְחָה דִּלְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא חוֹבָה הִיא, וּתְנַן אִם הִתְחִילוּ — אֵין מַפְסִיקִין. וְאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִשֶּׁיַּתִּיר חֲגוֹרוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Abaye said: Those Babylonian Torah scholars, according to the opinion of the one who said: The evening prayer is voluntary, once one** of them **loosens his belt, we do not impose upon him** to stop his meal and pray. And the Gemara wonders: **And according to the opinion of the one who said that the evening prayer is obligatory, we do impose upon him? Doesn't everyone agree that the afternoon prayer is obligatory? And we learned in our mishna that if they started eating, they need not stop. And** with regard to that *halakha*, **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** The beginning of the meal is **from when he loosens his belt.**

Abaye's comment leads the Gemara into a topic that might initially seem like a digression but is in fact one of the primary loci for a major halachic question discussed at length in Tractate Berachot: the status of *tefillat arvis* as either *reshut* (optional) or *chova* (obligatory). The full implications of that debate were not explored in Berachot, and the key discussion, as the lecturer observes, winds up here in Shabbat. Abaye says: *hani chavran Bavla'ei, le-man de-amar tefillat arvis reshut* — these Babylonian colleagues of ours, according to the view that *maariv* is a *reshut*, *keivan de-sharrei lahenne* — once they have loosened their belts and begun the meal, *u-matrichinnan lei* — we do not burden them by requiring them to interrupt and go daven *maariv* first.

### Mincha vs. Maariv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָא שְׁכִיחָא שִׁכְרוּת, הָכָא שְׁכִיחָא שִׁכְרוּת. אִי נָמֵי, בְּמִנְחָה — כֵּיוָן דִּקְבִיעָא לַהּ זִימְנָא מִירְתַת וְלָא אָתֵי לְמִפְשַׁע. עַרְבִית — כֵּיוָן דְּכוּלֵּהּ לֵילְיָא זְמַן עַרְבִית, לָא מִירְתַת וְאָתֵי לְמִפְשַׁע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds that there is a difference between the cases. **There,** at the time of the afternoon prayer, **drunkenness is uncommon,** as it is unusual to drink excessively during the day. However, **here,** in the case of the evening prayer, **drunkenness is common,** and therefore there was room to issue a decree requiring one to interrupt his meal to recite the evening prayer. **Alternatively,** it is possible to explain that **with regard to the afternoon prayer, since its time is fixed, he is anxious, and he won't come to be negligent** and forget to pray. However, with regard to the **evening prayer, since all night is the time for the evening prayer, he is not anxious, and he will come to be negligent.**

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *u-le-man de-amar chova, matrichinnan lei?* — if *maariv* is a *chova*, would we require interruption? But our own Mishnah regarding *mincha* — which everyone agrees is a *chova* — says *ein mafsikin*! If we don't interrupt for the obligatory *mincha* once one has loosened one's belt, why would we interrupt for *maariv*? The Gemara must explain what makes *maariv* more of a concern than *mincha*.

### Drunkenness Factor

The Gemara's answer: *hatam lo shchicha shichrut, hacha shchicha shichrut* — with *mincha*, drunkenness is not a realistic concern; people do not typically drink during the middle of the day when work remains. With *maariv*, however, drunkenness is a genuine concern: the evening meal is when wine is consumed, and by the time one finishes a substantial dinner, one may not be in a state to pray. Therefore, if *maariv* were a *chova*, we would require interruption — not simply because one is eating, but because we fear the meal will end with one too impaired to fulfill the obligation. But if *maariv* is only a *reshut*, this risk does not require the imposition of interruption.

This is a striking Gemara. The implication seems to be that when *maariv* is a *reshut*, we are prepared to accept the possibility that one might simply fail to daven it — through intoxication, forgetfulness, or the natural extension of the evening meal. That is a strong reading of what *reshut* means in practice.

### Time Window Difference

The Gemara adds a further distinction: *b'mincha, keivan de-kavi'a lei zimna, mirsas ve-lo ati le-mifsha* — with *mincha*, since there is a fixed and limited time window, a person is acutely aware of the ticking clock and will not be negligent. *Arvis, keivan de-kulei leilya zman arvis, lo mirsas, ve-ati le-mifsha* — with *maariv*, the entire night is valid time for the prayer, so a person does not feel the same urgency. The diffuse time window breeds complacency, and complacency breeds negligence. Combined with the risk of drunkenness, this makes *maariv* a genuinely more precarious obligation — and the question of interruption must be calibrated accordingly.

### Tosafot Reshut Clarification

Tosafot addresses head-on what *reshut* actually means in this context, and his answer is careful. He writes: *ve-nireh le-ri, afilu le-man de-amar tefillat arvis reshut, ein le-vatlah be-chinam* — even according to the view that *maariv* is a *reshut*, one does not simply blow it off. *Ella bishvil shum tzorech* — one may forgo or delay it only for a genuine reason, such as being in the middle of a meal (*tircha*), or being engaged in another *mitzvah* that cannot easily be interrupted.

The practical import of *reshut* status is therefore not license to skip *maariv* entirely, but rather that one need not treat it with the same level of anxious urgency as a *chova*. If one is occupied with a meal, the *tircha* of interruption is sufficient reason not to require it. If one is engaged in another *mitzvah* — even one that could theoretically be performed at a different time — the *reshut* status of *maariv* means one need not worry that continuing the other *mitzvah* will cause one to miss it.

Tosafot is thus reading the Gemara *ke-fshuto* while simultaneously limiting its scope: yes, the Gemara's logic holds, and *reshut* is a meaningfully different category that affects the practical calculus of interruption and priority — but it does not mean, as the Rambam and later authorities conclude, that one can simply choose not to daven *maariv*. The community has long since accepted *maariv* as obligatory, and any remaining discussion of *reshut* is largely theoretical. What is significant is that this theoretical discussion — which bears directly on the day-to-day practice of interrupting meals for prayer — finds its primary expression not in Tractate Berachot but here, in a passage of Tractate Shabbat seemingly concerned with the timing of haircuts and bathhouses.
