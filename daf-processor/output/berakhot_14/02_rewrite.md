# Berakhot 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shema Interruptions

### Rabbi Meir's Logic

The sugya opens at the bottom of 13b, with the phrase *uvaprakim*, concerning the rules governing interruption during *kriat shema*. The *mishnah* had recorded Rabbi Meir's position that between the sections (*bein haprakim*) one may ask after another's well-being out of honor (*mipnei hakavod*), and one may also respond. The *gemara* immediately presses: when Rabbi Meir says one may respond, under what conditions? If he means one may respond *mipnei hakavod* — out of honor — the statement is redundant. After all, if one may *initiate* a greeting out of honor, it goes without saying (*ein tzarich lomar*) that one may respond, since responding carries a lower social burden than initiating. Why bother stating it?

The *gemara* therefore suggests: perhaps when Rabbi Meir says one may respond, he means one may respond even at a lower threshold — even to an ordinary person, not only in a situation demanding formal deference. This reading is then tested against the *seifa* of the same teaching. In the middle of a section (*be'emtza haperek*), one may ask only out of awe (*mipnei hayirah*), and one may respond. Again the question: respond under what conditions? If the threshold for responding is the same as for asking — *mipnei hayirah* — then again the statement is redundant. It must therefore be that Rabbi Meir means one may respond even *mipnei hakavod*, a lower threshold than *yirah*. But that is precisely the position of Rabbi Yehuda.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

The *gemara* then notes: *hainu deRabbi Yehuda* — this winds up being identical to Rabbi Yehuda's position. As the *mishnah* records, Rabbi Yehuda says: in the middle of a section, one may ask *mipnei hayirah* and respond *mipnei hakavod*; between sections, one may ask *mipnei hakavod* and respond to everyone (*shalom lechol adam*). The positions are arranged in an ABBA structure, but the substance is the same: Rabbi Yehuda explicitly distinguishes between the threshold required to initiate and the lower threshold required to respond. If Rabbi Meir's mention of responding implies a similar lower threshold, his position collapses into Rabbi Yehuda's.

The *gemara* therefore concludes that Rabbi Meir did not intend a different threshold for responding. He mentioned it not to introduce a new rule but as a rhetorical setup — stating the obvious so that Rabbi Yehuda could then contrast it with his own position, which *does* distinguish between initiating and responding.

### Braita Framework

The *gemara* then introduces a *braita* to make this structure explicit: *chasuri michasra vehachi katani* — the *mishnah* is elliptical, and this is what it should be taken to mean. The *braita* formulates it clearly: between sections, one may ask *mipnei hakavod*, and there is no need to state (*ein tzarich lomar*) that one may respond — it is self-evident. In the middle of a section, one may ask *mipnei hayirah*, and again there is no need to state that one may respond under the same threshold. This is the view of Rabbi Meir: the threshold for responding is identical to the threshold for asking, and stating otherwise would be redundant. Rabbi Yehuda then takes the floor and distinguishes: in the middle of a section, one may ask *mipnei hayirah* but may respond *mipnei hakavod*; between sections, one may ask *mipnei hakavod* and respond to all. The *braita* thus confirms that Rabbi Meir's mention of responding was meant only to set the stage for Rabbi Yehuda's distinction, not to introduce one of his own.

### Tosafot on Kaddish

Before the *gemara* moves on to extend these laws to other contexts, it is worth pausing to examine a crucial application made by *Tosafot* on 13b. *Tosafot* notes that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda — as the *gemara* will state explicitly — meaning that even in the middle of a section, one may respond *mipnei hakavod*. *Tosafot* draws a direct analogy: if one is in the middle of reciting *shema* and the congregation reaches *kaddish* or *kedusha*, certainly responding to honor God's name is equivalent to responding out of honor to a person. There is no greater expression of *mipnei hakavod* than that. Therefore, one interrupts *shema* to respond to *kaddish* and *kedusha*. This rule applies, however, only within *shema*; interrupting *shemoneh esreh* is governed by an entirely different and more stringent standard.

The practical import of this *Tosafot* is considerable. The question of whether to respond to *kaddish* and *kedusha* while one is davening at a different point than the congregation is far more common in practice than the question of greeting a person mid-*shema*. *Tosafot* resolves it by identifying the honor accorded to God's name as the functional equivalent of *mipnei hakavod* toward a person of distinction.

## Hallel & Megilla

### Hallel Status

The *gemara* now extends the inquiry beyond *shema*. A *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Chiyya posed a question directly to Rabbi Chiyya: what is the rule for interrupting *Hallel* and *Megilla*? Does the same framework apply as with *shema*? The question formulates a *kal vachomer*: if *kriat shema*, which is biblical (*de'oraita*), may nonetheless be interrupted under the appropriate conditions, then *Hallel* and *Megilla*, which are only rabbinic (*derabbanan*) and therefore of lesser weight, should certainly permit interruption on similar or more lenient grounds. But a counterargument presents itself: *Hallel* and *Megilla* carry the quality of *pirsumei nisa* — publicizing the miracle — which may invest them with a particular gravity that argues against interruption, especially in a public setting.

### Rava's Distinction

Rabbi Chiyya's response is terse: *posek ve'ein bekach klum* — you may interrupt; it is not a concern at all. The phrase *ein bekach klum* suggests not merely that it is permitted but that it carries no particular weight as an issue. It is certainly no more stringent than *shema*, and may even be more lenient.

Rava then provides the precise rule. On days when an individual (*yachid*) completes the full *Hallel* — that is, days on which the complete *Hallel* is recited — the laws mirror those of *shema*: between one chapter and the next (*bein perek leperek*) one may interrupt; in the middle of a chapter (*be'emtza haperek*) one may not. On days when the individual does not complete the full *Hallel* — that is, days on which only *chatzi Hallel* (half *Hallel*) is recited, such as *Rosh Chodesh* — even interrupting in the middle of a chapter is permitted, since *chatzi Hallel* has the status of a *minhag* and carries lesser weight. The assumption throughout is that the interruptions in question are *mipnei hakavod*; it is under that threshold that Rava assigns these different standards.

### Rav Bar Shaba Case

The *gemara* then raises an apparent difficulty. Rav Bar Shaba once came to Ravina on a day when only *chatzi Hallel* was being recited, and Ravina did not interrupt — even in the middle of a chapter — to acknowledge him. But we just established that *chatzi Hallel* permits even mid-chapter interruption, presumably *mipnei hakavod*. Why did Ravina not interrupt?

The *gemara* resolves this with a pointed observation: *shani Rav Bar Shaba delo chashiv alei deRavina* — Rav Bar Shaba was simply not accorded sufficient importance by Ravina. Ravina did not regard him as someone whose presence created an obligation of *mipnei hakavod*; he treated him as an ordinary person (*kol adam*). The case therefore offers an interesting window into how these hierarchies of honor were assessed in practice — but it does not undermine the rule. The upshot stands: full *Hallel* carries the same rules as *shema*, and *chatzi Hallel* is more lenient than either.

### Yachid Definition

The phrase *yamim sheyachid gomer bahen et haHallel* — the days on which an individual completes the *Hallel* — prompts a lengthy discussion in *Tosafot*. The first issue is whether a *bracha* is recited over *chatzi Hallel*, given that it is only a *minhag*. *Tosafot* generally holds that it is. One of the arguments *Tosafot* adduces is that the very fact the *gemara* discusses the rules of interrupting *chatzi Hallel* implies that it is framed by a *bracha* — for in *Tosafot*'s understanding, the laws of interruption are tied to the notion of interrupting an act framed by a *bracha*. Without a *bracha*, there would be nothing to interrupt in a halachically meaningful sense. One might question this assumption — for instance, if a person were uncertain whether he had fulfilled his obligation to read the *Megilla* and was reading it again without a *bracha*, would the laws of interruption not apply? The assumption that interruption rules require a framing *bracha* is not self-evident. But that is *Tosafot*'s position, and it leads him to observe that the *Hallel* of the *seder* night, which is split by the *seudah*, cannot have a *bracha* spanning both halves — an argument against certain opinions that held it did.

Regarding the word *yachid* itself, *Tosafot* notes that strictly speaking it carries no independent significance here — the rules of interruption do not differ between an individual and a *tzibur*. However, others read the phrase as genuinely distinguishing between them. On the days of *chatzi Hallel*, perhaps the *tzibur* has a fuller level of obligation than the *yachid*. There are indeed authorities who hold that the *minhag* of *chatzi Hallel* applies only at the communal level; when an individual recites it in private (*beyachidus*), no *bracha* is recited at all, since the *minhag* was established at the level of the *tzibur* and not at the level of the *yachid*. This reading would give the phrase its full force: it is specifically the individual who on those days does not complete the *Hallel*, whereas the congregation may have a different standing.

In any case, the *gemara* concludes that these obligations — even though *derabbanan* — carry essentially the same status as *shema* for purposes of the interruption rules: no more stringent and no less.

## Tasting & Fasting

### Taste Definition

The *gemara* introduces a digression prompted by the phrase *ein bekach klum* used earlier. A *tanna* from the school of Rav Ami posed a question to Rav Ami: if a person is observing a private fast (*taanis*), may he taste food? *Tosafot* understands the discussion to be limited to a private fast; a communal fast may carry different standards. The question turns on how to understand the nature of a *taanis*. When a person accepts a fast upon himself, what precisely has he accepted? One possibility: he has accepted upon himself not to eat or drink (*achilah ushtiyah kabbilah alei*) — and tasting, which does not constitute eating, would be permitted. Another possibility: he has accepted upon himself to derive no pleasure (*hana'ah*) from food — and since tasting does produce pleasure and involves some ingestion, it would be forbidden.

### Revi'it Measure

Rav Ami's answer is direct: *to'em ve'ein bekach klum* — one may taste and it is not a concern at all. A *braita* confirms this: someone who merely tastes does not require a *bracha*, and someone on a fast who tastes does not violate the fast. Tasting is not eating. The *gemara* then asks: up to how much may one taste? Rav Ami and Rav Assi both state: one may taste up to the measure of a *revi'it* — approximately three and a half ounces — and it remains within the category of tasting rather than eating. This is a striking ruling. A *revi'it* is a substantial quantity, and to say that this amount carries neither *bracha* obligation nor a *taanis* violation is a considerable *chiddush*.

### Tosafot Spitting

*Tosafot* immediately qualifies the ruling: *peirush rak shechozer upolet* — the entire permission is contingent upon spitting the food out. Without that, one might ask what distinguishes tasting from eating. After all, food is entering one's mouth, pleasure is being derived, and some degree of ingestion occurs regardless. *Tosafot* answers: what makes *te'imah* categorically different from *achilah* is the intentional act of spitting out, which signals that there was never any intent to ingest. Something does enter the body — sensation requires contact — but the act is fundamentally not one of swallowing or ingesting. It is a sensory assessment, not consumption.

There is a related debate among the *poskim* regarding tasting at the very tip of the tongue — whether that constitutes a distinct category of act from eating, lighter even than the *te'imah* described here. That question may indeed underlie the *gemara*'s discussion of what makes tasting not eating. *Tosafot*'s answer focuses on the spitting out as the decisive criterion.

## Greeting Before Prayer

### Altar Metaphor

The *gemara* returns, albeit through another digression, toward the theme of *tefillah*. Rav states: whoever greets another person (*nosein shalom lechavero*) before davening is as though he has made that person into an altar (*bamah*). The statement is grounded in a *pasuk*: *chadlu lachem min ha'adam asher neshamah be'apo ki bameh nechshav hu* — cease from people, for of what value is he? The word *bameh*, meaning "of what value," is read as *bamah*, an altar. By initiating greetings before directing one's wishes and attention to God, a person has effectively offered up his morning efforts to another human being rather than to the Creator. The imagery carries an almost idolatrous subtext: one has erected an altar to a person in the space that belonged to God.

This statement connects naturally to earlier discussions in the *gemara* about eating before davening and about casting God behind one's back (*hashlakas Elohim acharei gavam*). There the concern was doing other things — attending to personal interests — before acknowledging God. Here the concern is more pointed: it is the explicit honor directed toward another person, which carries the specific quality of misplaced reverence.

### Shmuel's Point

Shmuel sharpens the point: *bameh chishavta l'zeh velo l'Eloha* — what are you doing giving significance to this person rather than to God? Shmuel removes any ambiguity. The problem is not incidental — it is the deliberate act of according weight and honor to a human being in a space that ought to be directed toward the Divine.

### Rav Sheshet Q

This raises an obvious difficulty. Rav Sheshet objects: but our *mishnah* explicitly permits asking after someone's well-being (*sho'el beshalom*) between the sections of *shema* — and at that point one has not yet davened! How can the *mishnah* permit this if Rav has just declared it forbidden?

### Incidental vs. Deliberate

Rav Abba resolves the contradiction by drawing a critical distinction: the problem Rav describes is when a person rises early in the morning and goes deliberately to knock on another's door (*mashkim lefischo*) — making a purposeful effort to honor that person before having davened. That is the case of making the person into a *bamah*: a calculated act of deference to a human being that precedes any acknowledgment of God. By contrast, if one simply encounters someone and responds to their presence, it would be insulting not to acknowledge them, and that incidental greeting does not carry the same weight.

It is worth noting that in Roman culture there was an established practice of patronage in which clients would rise early and attend upon their patrons, forming a kind of retinue to show deference. The *gemara*'s critique carries additional punch in that social context: do not make this person your morning priority, do not perform all of that elaborate human homage, before you have at minimum acknowledged God and davened.

## Own Interests First

### Personal Matters Ban

Rav Idi bar Avin said in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Ashyan: it is forbidden for a person to attend to his personal affairs (*chafatzav*) before davening. This generalizes the principle beyond the specific case of greeting another person. The entire orientation of the morning — attending to one's own interests, conducting one's business, arranging one's affairs — cannot precede the act of prayer.

### Tzedek First

The source is a *pasuk*: *tzedek lefanav yehalech veyasem laderech pe'amav* — righteousness goes before him and places his feet on the path. Rashi explains *tzedek* here as *tefillah* — the prayer which justifies a person before his Creator, which acknowledges God as the source of one's existence and one's day. That acknowledgment must come first. Only after *tzedek lefanav yehalech* — after righteousness has preceded — may one *yesem pe'amav*, set one's feet on the path of the day's affairs. The structure of the day must begin with this acknowledgment, and everything else follows.

This principle applies specifically to the first davening of the day. For *minchah* and *maariv* the concern is of a different character — whether one will forget when the time arrives — not this foundational issue of the proper ordering of one's morning. The morning *tefillah* is uniquely freighted with the demand that a person begin his day by recognizing God before turning to himself.

### God Provides

The same tradition — Rav Idi bar Avin in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Ashyan — adds a positive counterpart: whoever davens first and then goes out on his way, *Ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu oseh lo chafatzav* — the Holy One, blessed be He, takes care of his needs. This is the precise inversion of the prohibition: the *mishnah* forbade a person from attending to his *chafatzav* before davening; the *gemara* now promises that if one davens first, God will attend to those *chafatzav* in one's stead. The same *pasuk* is invoked: *tzedek lefanav yehalech veyasem laderech pe'amav* — put righteousness first, and God will set your feet on your path. Here the *gemara* applies this specifically to the context of departing on a journey: if circumstances permit, daven before you leave, and God will ensure things work out.

## Aggadic Teachings

### Dreams & Evil

The *gemara* closes this strand with several *aggadic* statements. Rav Yona said in the name of Rav Zeira: whoever sleeps for seven days without a dream is called evil (*nikra ra*). The *pasuk* is: *vesava yalin bal yipaked ra* — one who sleeps sated will not be visited by evil. By reading *sava* not as "sated" but as *sheva*, seven — if you sleep (*yalin*) for seven days (*sheva*) and are not visited (*bal yipaked*) by a dream, then *ra* — you are evil. The underlying belief appears to be that dreams carry some form of spiritual message or communication; if a person goes an entire week without receiving any such message, that absence is itself a negative sign. The statement has been interpreted to mean that if a week passes without the person achieving even a moment of perceiving the temporariness of this world — as on *Shabbat*, which falls within any seven-day period — then the absence of that awareness marks him as *ra*.

### Torah Sleep

Rav Acha the son of Rav Chiyya bar Abba then offers a closer reading of the same *pasuk* from Rabbi Yochanan. The *pasuk* in its plain meaning supports the following teaching: whoever satiates himself (*masbia atzmo*) with words of Torah before going to sleep will not be visited by evil reports (*ein mevasrim oto besoros ra'os*). *Vesava yalin* — if you go to sleep truly sated with Torah, *bal yipaked ra* — evil will not visit you. The connection of these *aggadic* statements to the surrounding *halacha* is not entirely clear from the structure of the passage; they appear to be associated with the same chain of tradition rather than arising organically from the legal discussion.

## Shema-Bracha Link

### No Interruption

Returning to the central halachic thread, Rav Avahu said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda. This means the *halacha* is that one may not interrupt between *Eloheichem* — the end of the third paragraph of *shema* — and *emet veyatziv*, the opening of the *bracha* that follows. The intent is to create a seamless statement: *Hashem Eloheichem emet* — the Lord your God is true. These are technically different units of the liturgy, but by not interrupting between them, they are joined into a single declaration.

### Emet Repetition

Rav Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: what is Rabbi Yehuda's reason? Because the *pasuk* reads *Hashem Eloheichem emet* — and we want to embody that *pasuk* in our recitation. This then generates a practical question: when the *chazan* finishes *shema* with *Ani Hashem Eloheichem* and must continue into *emet veyatziv*, does he repeat the word *emet* — so that it closes *shema* and also opens the *bracha* — or does he proceed directly with *veyatziv venachon vekayam*? Rabbi Yochanan's first position, as reported by Rav Abba, is that he does repeat *emet*: the word performs double duty, closing one unit and opening the next, so that *Ani Hashem Eloheichem emet* flows naturally into *emet veyatziv*.

### Emet Emet Problem

Rava disagrees: he should not repeat *emet*. The purpose of the connection is to allow the two units to flow seamlessly into each other; repeating *emet* interrupts that flow by attaching the word more explicitly to what follows rather than allowing it to bridge the two sections. A certain prayer leader who led services before Rava was heard to say *emet emet* twice — once closing *shema* and once opening *emet veyatziv*. Rava's response was sharp: *kol emet emet chasei l'hai* — this person has the *emet emet* sickness. The doubling was treated as an aberration. One wonders whether this sensitivity also reflects the broader concern, present elsewhere in the *gemara*, about doubling words in liturgy — similar to the prohibitions against saying *shema shema* or *modim modim* — concerns that may have been sharpened by the Zoroastrian and dualistic cultural context in which the Babylonian *amoraim* operated, where any apparent doubling could suggest a dual divine authority.

## Eretz Yisrael Custom

### Western Skip

Rav Yosef introduces the next topic with characteristic enthusiasm: *kama ma'alya hai shmaita* — how excellent is the following teaching. Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda, upon returning from *Eretz Yisrael*, reported the practice of *ma'arava* — the west — for the evening *shema* (*arvit*). In *Eretz Yisrael*, the full *parashat tzitzit* was not recited at night. Instead, they would say the opening of the first *pasuk* of the *parasha* — *daber bnei Yisrael ve'amarta aleihem* — and then jump to the end of the final *pasuk*: *Ani Hashem Eloheichem emet*. This created a kind of hybrid *pasuk* combining the beginning of the first verse with the end of the last. The rationale, as Rashi explains, is that the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* does not apply at night; therefore, reading about *tzitzit* in the evening *shema* was felt to be unnecessary, and the passage was abbreviated.

One might also suggest — and this possibility is raised in the discussion — that the practice reflects a different position on whether *zechiras yetziat Mitzrayim* is obligatory at night. If the *mitzvah* of mentioning the Exodus does not apply at night, there is less reason to recite the *parasha* whose primary function in the evening *shema* is to fulfill that *mitzvah*. The hybrid formulation preserved the structural integrity of the liturgy while bypassing the *tzitzit* section.

### Complete or Skip

Abaye responds to Rav Yosef's enthusiasm with a pointed question: *mai ma'alyusa* — what exactly is so wonderful about that? He invokes a ruling of Rav Kahana in the name of Rav: *lo yaschil*, one should not begin the third *parasha* at night; but *ve'im hitchil yigmor*, if one begins it, one must finish it. The objection is clear: the practice of *Eretz Yisrael*, which starts the *parasha* and then cuts it off to jump to the end, violates this principle. Either don't start it at all, or complete it. Rearranging and abbreviating *psukim* is itself problematic — as the principle *kol pasuk de'lo paskei Moshe anan lo paskeinan* establishes: any verse that Moshe did not divide, we do not divide.

### Starting Point

The *gemara* then considers whether *ve'amarta aleihem* constitutes a sufficient beginning to trigger the obligation to finish. Perhaps the opening phrase *daber bnei Yisrael ve'amarta aleihem* does not count as truly starting the *parasha*, and the practitioners in *Eretz Yisrael* therefore felt no obligation to complete it. Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak said in the name of Rav: *daber el bnei Yisrael* alone is not considered starting; but *ve'amarta aleihem* — once one has reached that phrase — is considered starting. Rav Papa then explains the *Eretz Yisrael* position: they held that even *ve'amarta aleihem* is not considered starting until one actually mentions *tzitzit* itself — *ve'asu lahem tzitzit*. Once *tzitzit* has been named, the *parasha* has been entered and must be completed. But before that point, even having said *ve'amarta aleihem*, one may still jump to the end.

### Babylonian Practice

Abaye draws the practical conclusion for Babylonia. Since the practice of *Eretz Yisrael* requires at minimum beginning the *parasha* — they do not allow it to be entirely omitted — we in Bavel will follow them in beginning it, saying *daber bnei Yisrael ve'amarta aleihem*. But since we also hold that once one begins (*ve'im hitchil yigmor*) one must complete the entire *parasha*, we are obligated to say the whole thing. The result is that in Bavel the full third *parasha* of *shema* was recited at night, while in *Eretz Yisrael* an abbreviated version was used. The Babylonian position implicitly endorses *zechiras yetziat Mitzrayim baleilos* — the obligation to mention the Exodus at night — since completing the full *parasha* is predicated on that obligation.

## Ani Hashem Connection

### Conditional Emet

Chiyya bar Rav states: if one has said *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* — the closing phrase of *parashat tzitzit* — then *emet* is recited as the transition into *emet veyatziv*. But if one has not said *Ani Hashem Elokeichem*, then *emet* is not said. This ruling reflects the principle established earlier: the word *emet* at the junction of *shema* and the *bracha* that follows is specifically meaningful as an affirmation of *Hashem Eloheichem emet*. If the phrase *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* was not recited — whether because the *parasha* of *tzitzit* was omitted or abbreviated — then there is nothing for *emet* to affirm, and the word loses its function at that junction.

### Yetziat Mitzrayim

The *gemara* immediately raises the obvious difficulty: if one has not recited *parashat tzitzit* and also does not say *emet veyatziv*, how has one fulfilled the obligation of *zechiras yetziat Mitzrayim*? The *bracha* of *emet veyatziv* is the vehicle through which the Exodus is commemorated in the morning liturgy; its evening counterpart, *emet ve'emuna*, serves the same function at night. If neither the *parasha* nor the *bracha* is recited, *yetziat Mitzrayim* has not been mentioned. This is a genuine problem, and it is here that the approach of Rashi becomes clear: Rashi understands the *gemara* throughout to be operating on the assumption that *zechiras yetziat Mitzrayim* is obligatory at night. The abbreviation of *parashat tzitzit* in *Eretz Yisrael* was driven not by rejecting that obligation but by the inapplicability of the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* at night. Mentioning the Exodus remained necessary; the question was how to accomplish it.

### Ga'al Yisrael

The *gemara*'s answer is that even in the case where *emet veyatziv* is not recited in its full form, a *bracha* must still be said — *shema* requires a concluding *bracha*. That *bracha* takes a different formulation: *modim anachnu lefanecha Hashem Elokeinu shehotzeitanu me'eretz Mitzrayim ufaditanu mibeit avadim ve'asita lanu nisim ugevuros al hayam vesharanu lach* — we give thanks before You, Lord our God, for taking us out of Egypt and redeeming us from the house of bondage and performing miracles and mighty acts for us at the sea, and we sang to You. This alternative *bracha* accomplishes *zechiras yetziat Mitzrayim* through its content, and it concludes — as the questioner in the *gemara* correctly anticipates — with *ga'al Yisrael*, the Redeemer of Israel. The *chatimah* of the *bracha* remains unchanged; what changes is the opening portion: instead of *emet veyatziv* or *emet ve'emuna* — both of which are the affirmations connected to *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* — a different *nusach* is used, one whose content centers directly on the Exodus narrative. The *bracha* of *ga'al Yisrael* is the constant; the text leading into it is what varies depending on whether *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* was recited.

### Custom Differences

The discussion as a whole illuminates a period of genuine liturgical flux. The differences between *Eretz Yisrael* and Bavel in the recitation of the third *parasha* of *shema* at night were not merely matters of local custom but reflected substantive halachic disagreements: about the applicability of *tzitzit* at night, about the obligation of *zechiras yetziat Mitzrayim baleilos*, and about the threshold at which beginning a *parasha* obligates one to complete it. In *Eretz Yisrael*, an abbreviated version of *parashat tzitzit* was used, connected directly to *Ani Hashem Elokeichem emet*; some there may also have held that *zechiras yetziat Mitzrayim* at night was less fully obligatory. In Bavel, the *gemara* firmly presupposes the obligation of *yetziat Mitzrayim baleilos* and requires the full *parasha* to be recited. The word *emet* at the junction, the presence or absence of *Ani Hashem Elokeichem*, and the *nusach* of the concluding *bracha* all follow from these underlying positions. The *Bavli* here does not definitively resolve every strand of the *Eretz Yisrael* practice — it leaves open, for instance, the precise extent to which the western communities accepted the nighttime obligation of *zechiras yetziat Mitzrayim* — but the Babylonian position is clear: the third *parasha* is recited in full, *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* is said, *emet* connects it to the *bracha*, and *yetziat Mitzrayim* is commemorated every night.
