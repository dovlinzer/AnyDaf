# Sanhedrin 113 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hekdesh in Ir Nidachas

### Hekdesh Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְרוּמוֹת – יֵרָקְבוּ, וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְכִתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ – יִגָּנְזוּ.
>
> **Translation:** *terumot* **must** be left to **decay, and second tithe and sacred scrolls must be interred.**

We conclude *masechet Sanhedrin* on daf 113, picking up from the bottom of 112b, where the Gemara has been discussing the range of items possessing some form of *kedushah* that are found within an *ir nidachas*. The governing *halacha* is that one does not actively destroy such items, yet neither does one simply remove them from the city; rather, they are buried, left to rot, or allowed to die. The question is what precisely characterizes this intermediate status.

Two fundamental approaches present themselves. The first holds that such items are in principle obligated to be destroyed as part of the *ir nidachas*, but because they are *hekdesh* one may not carry out that active destruction; hence one buries them. The second holds that such items are fundamentally *shel Shamayim* — belonging to Heaven — and therefore not included in the category of *shelalah* (spoil) at all, yet some other consideration prevents one from simply walking them out of the city. The Gemara appears to incline toward the first approach.

### Terumah Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא תְּרוּמָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל תְּרוּמָה בְּיַד כֹּהֵן, כֵּיוָן דְּמָמוֹנֵיהּ הוּא, תִּשָּׂרֵף. מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְכִתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ יִגָּנְזוּ. וְהָא מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל כִּתְרוּמָה בְּיַד כֹּהֵן דָּמֵי, וְקָתָנֵי יִגָּנְזוּ! אֶלָּא, אִי אִתְּמַר הָכִי אִתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא תְּרוּמָה בְּיַד כֹּהֵן, אֲבָל תְּרוּמָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל תִּנָּתֵן לְכֹהֵן שֶׁבְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says:** The Sages **taught** this **only** with regard to ***teruma*** that is still **in the possession of an Israelite,** who has not yet given it to a priest. **But** concerning ***teruma*** that is already **in the possession of a priest** who lives in the idolatrous city, **since it is his property, it shall be burned.** **Rav Yosef raises an objection** from the mishna: **Second tithe and sacred scrolls must be interred. But isn't** the status of **second tithe in the possession of an Israelite like** that of ***teruma* in the possession of a priest,** as he may partake of it and benefit from it in Jerusalem; **and** nevertheless, **it is taught:** They **must be interred? Rather, if** the statement of Rav Ḥisda **was stated, this is what was stated: Rav Ḥisda says:** The Sages **taught** this **only** with regard to ***teruma*** that is already **in the possession of a priest** who lives in the idolatrous city, as even though it is his property, it maintains the sanctity of *teruma*. **But *teruma*** that is still **in the possession of an Israelite,** which is not his property at all, **shall be given to a priest who is in another city,** and should not be left to decay.

Within this framework the Gemara had considered the case of *terumah*, which the Mishnah requires to be left to rot. The discussion turned on whether this refers to *terumah* in the hand of a *kohen* or *terumah* in the hand of a *Yisrael*. The conclusion reached was that the Mishnah speaks of *terumah* in the hand of a *kohen*. *Terumah* in the hand of a *Yisrael* is altogether saved from the laws of *ir nidachas* and may be given to a *kohen* elsewhere, since it does not truly belong to that *Yisrael* at all. *Terumah* in the hand of a *kohen*, by contrast, is treated as his own property; it fundamentally deserves to be destroyed, and therefore one does not actively burn it but leaves it to rot.

The Gemara now states this ruling explicitly: *amar Rav Chisda lo shanu ela terumah b'yad kohen* — Rav Chisda teaches that this law applies only to *terumah* in the hand of a *kohen*. But *terumah* in the hand of a *Yisrael* — *yinatnen l'kohen she'be'ir acheres* — one gives it to a *kohen* in another city, for it never truly belonged to the *Yisrael* who separated it, and it bears no connection to the *ir nidachas*.

One of the reasons the Gemara introduces this analysis is the parallel case of *maaser sheni*, which the Mishnah likewise requires to be buried. The Gemara assumes *maaser sheni* is analogous to *terumah* in the hand of a *kohen*: it is something that can actually be eaten, it is in the domain of the individual, and therefore it fundamentally belongs to its owner — and though one does not actively burn it, one buries it.

### Maaser Sheni Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי מָמוֹן גָּבוֹהַּ הוּא, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי מָמוֹן הֶדְיוֹט הוּא. אֲבָל בִּגְבוּלִין, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says:** This **dispute** is **with regard to second tithe in Jerusalem; as Rabbi Meir holds: Second-tithe** produce **is property** belonging **to the Most High,** but the Torah permitted its owners to partake of it in Jerusalem. Since it is the property of Heaven, one is exempt from separating priestly gifts. **And the Rabbis hold: It is non-sacred property** from which one is liable to separate *ḥalla*. **But** with regard to second-tithe dough **in the outlying areas, everyone agrees that one is exempt** from separating *ḥalla*, as outside of Jerusalem it is prohibited to partake of it.

At this point Rav Chisda offers a broader teaching about *maaser sheni*: *machlokes b'maaser sheni b'Yerushalayim* — the Tannaitic dispute concerning *maaser sheni* applies when the *maaser sheni* is in *Yerushalayim* and may actually be eaten there. Even so, Rabbi Meir maintains *maaser sheni mamon gavoah hu* — it fundamentally belongs to God, one is merely eating God's food — and therefore the dough of *maaser sheni* is exempt from *challah*, since it is not *arisoseichem*, your dough. The Sages, *verabanan savri mamon hedyot hu*, hold that it is the owner's property with an obligation to eat it in *Yerushalayim*, and therefore it is subject to *challah*.

Outside *Yerushalayim*, however, where *maaser sheni* may not be eaten — *avel bigvulin divrei hakol patur* — all agree it is exempt from *challah*, because at that stage, when it is off-limits, everyone agrees it has the status of *mamon gavoah*.

## Maaser Sheni Jerusalem

### Rav Yosef Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְכִתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ יִגָּנְזוּ. בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, מִי הָוְיָא עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת? וְהָתַנְיָא: עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְזוֹ אַחַת מֵהֶן – אֵינָהּ נַעֲשֵׂית עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת! וְאֶלָּא בְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת, וְאַסְּקוּהּ לְגַוַּהּ? הָא קַלְטוּהּ מְחִיצוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raises an objection** from the mishna: **Second tithe and sacred scrolls must be interred. What are we dealing with? If we say** that the reference is to second tithe **in Jerusalem, can** Jerusalem **be an idolatrous city? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Ten matters were stated with regard to Jerusalem, and this is one of them: It does not become an idolatrous city. Rather,** apparently the reference is to second tithe **in a different city** that was rendered an idolatrous city, **and** before it was so rendered **one took** the second-tithe produce **up into** Jerusalem. The Gemara asks: In that case, **wasn't** the produce **admitted** by **the walls** of Jerusalem, making it no longer associated with the idolatrous city, and it should therefore be permitted to partake of it?

The Gemara now subjects this analysis to a challenge from our Mishnah itself. Rav Yosef raises the following difficulty: the Mishnah states *maaser sheni vechisvei hekdesh yignazu* — *maaser sheni* and sacred texts must be buried. But in what case does this apply?

If we say the *maaser sheni* is located in *Yerushalayim*, where it may be eaten, then it resembles the *terumah* case and one could understand why it is treated as the owner's property. But *mi havyi Yerushalayim ir hanidachas* — how could *Yerushalayim* itself ever become an *ir nidachas*? A *braisa* teaches explicitly: *asara devarim ne'emru biYerushalayim*, ten special rules apply to *Yerushalayim*, and among them is *einah na'aseis ir hanidachas* — it cannot become an *ir nidachas*. So *Yerushalayim* itself cannot be the location.

### Klatu Mechitzot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו בִּגְבוּלִין, וְקָתָנֵי: יִגָּנְזוּ? לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת, וְאַסְּקוּהּ לְגַוַּהּ. וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? שֶׁנִּטְמָא. וְלִפְרְקֵיהּ? דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִנַּיִין לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁנִּטְמָא שֶׁפּוֹדִין אוֹתוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תוּכַל שְׂאֵתוֹ״, וְאֵין שְׂאֵת אֶלָּא אֲכִילָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשָּׂא מַשְׂאֹת מֵאֵת פָּנָיו״. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּלָקוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** is the reference in the mishna **not** to second-tithe produce **in an outlying area? And it is taught:** They **must be interred.** Apparently, second-tithe produce is not the property of Heaven; it belongs to an inhabitant of the idolatrous city. The Gemara rejects this: **No, actually,** the reference in the mishna is to second-tithe produce **in a different city, and** before it was rendered an idolatrous city **one took** the second-tithe produce **up into** Jerusalem. **And what are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where** that second-tithe produce **became ritually impure.** Since it is prohibited to partake of it, there is no alternative to interment. The Gemara challenges: **And let him redeem it** in Jerusalem, **as Rabbi Elazar says: From where** is it derived **with regard to second-tithe** produce **that became impure, that one may redeem it even in Jerusalem?** As **the verse states:** "And if the way is too long for you **so that you are unable to carry it [*se'eto*]…**and you shall turn it into money" (Deuteronomy 14:24–25). **And *se'et*** means **nothing other than eating, as it is stated: "And he took portions [*masot*] from before him"** (Genesis 43:34), indicating that second-tithe produce that cannot be eaten, whether due to the distance from Jerusalem or due to its impurity, may be redeemed. The Gemara answers: **What are we dealing with here?** It is not a case where the second-tithe produce became impure; rather, it is a case **where** an item **purchased** with second-tithe money became impure.

One might then suggest that the *maaser sheni* is in another city, and that it was previously brought into *Yerushalayim* and subsequently brought back out. This matters because of the principle of *klatu mechitzot*: once *maaser sheni* has entered within the walls of *Yerushalayim*, those walls are understood to have "absorbed" it, claiming a kind of jurisdiction over it. Even after the *maaser sheni* is physically removed from *Yerushalayim*, it cannot be redeemed — just as it could not be redeemed while it was within the city. If *Yerushalayim*'s walls still hold it in that sense, then the item is as if it remains in *Yerushalayim*; it is therefore not *shelalah* of the *ir nidachas* and is entirely exempt from its laws.

If that is so, however, why must it be buried? The Gemara attempts the answer that perhaps the reason it must be buried is simply that it became *tamei*, and a *tamei* *maaser sheni* that cannot be eaten must be dealt with accordingly — this having nothing to do with *ir nidachas* at all. But that raises a further difficulty: if it became *tamei*, one should be able to redeem it, since Rabbi Eliezer teaches — *minayin l'maaser sheni shentima shepodin oso afilu biYerushalayim* — that if *maaser sheni* became *tamei* it may be redeemed even within *Yerushalayim* itself. The derivation comes from the phrase *lo suchal se'eis*: the word *se'eis* can mean not only "to carry" but also "to eat," as the verse *vayisa mas'eis me'eis panav* demonstrates, so the verse means "you cannot eat it" — and once it cannot be eaten because it is *tamei*, even in *Yerushalayim* one may redeem it, and the same would apply to *maaser sheni* that was brought into *Yerushalayim* and then taken out.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיפְרְקֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: הַלָּקוּחַ בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁנִּטְמָא – יִפָּדֶה. כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: יִקָּבֵר. אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת? אֲפִילּוּ דְּעָלְמָא נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let him redeem** that which was purchased with second-tithe money and became ritually impure, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 3:10): An item **that is purchased with** second-**tithe money** and **that became ritually impure shall be redeemed.** The Gemara answers: The mishna is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says:** An item that is purchased with second-tithe money and that became ritually impure **shall be buried,** and it may no longer be redeemed. The Gemara asks: **If so,** and the mishna is referring to an item purchased with second-tithe money that became ritually impure, **why** did the mishna cite this *halakha* **specifically** in the case of **an idolatrous city?** The same would hold true **even** in cities **in general as well,** as in Rabbi Yehuda's opinion, the *halakha* there too is that the item is buried.

The Gemara then suggests that the case involves not the original *maaser sheni* produce, but *lakuach* — produce purchased with *maaser sheni* money. The original produce was redeemed onto money; that money was brought to *Yerushalayim* and used to purchase food; that purchased food then carries the *kedushah* of *maaser sheni*; and it is this purchased food that was brought out of *Yerushalayim*. Perhaps one cannot redeem it, since only the original *maaser sheni* can be redeemed, not what was purchased with its money. But this too is blocked by a Mishnah: *halokei'ach bekesef maaser shenitma yifdeh* — what was purchased with *maaser sheni* money and became *tamei* may indeed be redeemed. This is the ruling against which Rabbi Yehuda dissents, saying *yikaver* — it must be buried — and it is Rabbi Yehuda's position that the Mishnah here follows.

But then, as the Gemara itself asks pointedly: *amar Rav Yosef*, if the whole issue is just about a *Yehuda* versus the Sages dispute regarding *tamei* purchased *maaser sheni*, what does any of this have to do with *ir nidachas*? Why is it being taught in that context?

### Walls Fallen Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם בְּטָהוֹר, וּכְגוֹן דִּנְפוּל מְחִיצוֹת, וְכִדְרָבָא דְּאָמַר רָבָא: מְחִיצָה לֶאֱכוֹל – דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, לִקְלוֹט – דְּרַבָּנַן. וְכִי גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן כִּי אִתַנְהוּ לִמְחִיצוֹת, כִּי לֵיתַנְהוּ לִמְחִיצוֹת – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, actually,** the case in the mishna is **with regard to pure** second-tithe produce of an idolatrous city that was taken into Jerusalem, **and** it is a case **where the walls** of Jerusalem then **fell. And** this *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rava, **as Rava says:** The capacity of the **wall** of Jerusalem to enable one **to partake** of second-tithe produce is **by Torah law.** By contrast, the capacity of the **wall** of Jerusalem **to admit** second-tithe produce, in the sense that once it enters Jerusalem the produce assumes the status of the property of the Most High and may no longer be redeemed, is **by rabbinic law. And** the case **where the Sages issue the decree** that entry into Jerusalem admits the produce is **where the wall is** intact; however, in a case **where the wall is not** intact, **no,** the Sages did not issue a decree, and the second tithe remains the spoils of the idolatrous city.

The Gemara ultimately arrives at what it takes to be the correct case: *le'olam betahor* — the *maaser sheni* is *tahor*, it was brought into *Yerushalayim* and then brought back out, and the reason the principle of *klatu mechitzot* no longer shields it from the laws of *ir nidachas* is *kegon denaflu mechitzot* — the walls of *Yerushalayim* fell. This is the narrow, highly specific scenario under discussion.

The mechanism is explained by a teaching of Rava: *mechitzos le'echol de'oraisa* — the obligation to eat *maaser sheni* within the walls is biblical. But *liklot derabanan* — the rule that once *maaser sheni* has entered the walls it is perpetually "held" by them and cannot be redeemed even after removal is only rabbinic. And as the Gemara establishes: *ki gazru rabanan ki isnehu lemechitza ki leisnehu lemechitza lo* — the Sages enacted this rule only when the walls of *Yerushalayim* are still standing. Once the walls have fallen, the rabbinic conception of the *maaser sheni* remaining "within the walls" no longer applies. At that point the *maaser sheni*, having actually been brought into *Yerushalayim* and eaten there (so that it is *mamon be'alim* and not *mamon gavoah*), is now located outside *Yerushalayim* in a city that has become an *ir nidachas*, and the walls can no longer protect it. That very special configuration is what the Mishnah addresses.

The *upshot* of the entire extended discussion is that the Gemara, in all these cases, is confirming the first approach: wherever the Mishnah requires burying or rotting, it is because the item does in principle carry the *halacha* of *ir nidachas* — it is the owner's property and therefore fundamentally subject to destruction. If it were truly *mamon gavoah* and entirely *shel Shamayim*, it would be removed from the city freely, just as *terumah* in the hand of a *Yisrael* is given to a *kohen* in another city.

## Kitvei Kodesh

### Texts Buried

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּתְבֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ יִגָּנְזוּ. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, כׇּל עִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ אֲפִילּוּ מְזוּזָה אַחַת אֵינָהּ נַעֲשֵׂית עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׂרַפְתָּ בָאֵשׁ אֶת הָעִיר וְאֶת כׇּל שְׁלָלָהּ כָּלִיל״. וְהֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מְזוּזָה לָא אֶפְשָׁר, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן כֵּן לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Sacred scrolls must be interred.** The Gemara comments: The *halakha* cited in **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: Any city in which there is even one *mezuza* is not rendered an idolatrous city, as it is stated: "And you shall burn it with fire, both the city and all its spoils, entirely** for the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 13:17). **And** in a city **where there is a *mezuza* it is not possible** to burn all its spoils, **as it is written: "You shall not do so to the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 12:4), from which it is derived that it is prohibited to destroy any item upon which the name of God appears.

The Mishnah states *kisvei hakodesh yignazu* — sacred texts found in the *ir nidachas* must be buried. The Gemara notes: *masnisin delo k'Rabbi Eliezer* — our Mishnah does not follow the position of Rabbi Eliezer.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

The *braisa* teaches: *Rabbi Eliezer omer, kol ir sheyesh bah afilu mezuza achas einah na'aseis ir hanidachas* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that any city containing even one *mezuzah* cannot become an *ir nidachas*. His reasoning is derived from the verse *ve'sarafta va'esh es ha'ir ve'es kol shelalah kalil* — the entire city and all its spoil must be burned. But one is prohibited from burning *kitvei kodesh*, as the verse states *lo sa'asun kein l'Hashem Elokeichem* — one may not destroy sanctified divine writing. Therefore, *heicha de'ika mezuza lo efshar* — wherever there is a *mezuzah*, it is impossible to fulfill the requirement of burning everything. Rather than adopting the Mishnah's approach — that sacred texts are exempted from the burning and must instead be buried — Rabbi Eliezer concludes that the entire city is exempt from becoming an *ir nidachas* in the first place.

This is a position structurally reminiscent of Rabbi Eliezer's view elsewhere that certain Torah laws are constructed in such a way that their fulfillment is effectively impossible, and therefore the legal status in question simply cannot arise. The Mishnah, however, does not accept this reasoning; it permits the city to become an *ir nidachas* and addresses the *kitvei kodesh* separately by requiring their burial.

The Gemara proceeds to note that Rabbi Shimon offers a further teaching — connected to the Mishnah's statement *im atem osim din b'ir hanidachas ma'ala ani aleichem k'ilu atem ma'alim ola kalil lefanai*, that if Israel carries out this severe judgment, God accounts it as if they had brought a wholly-consumed burnt offering — but the Gemara pivots from Rabbi Shimon's statement directly to the next clause of the Mishnah regarding the *tel olam*.

## Eternal Mound Debate

### Barren vs City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בִּדְרַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי? דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא כְּלָל בַּעֲשֵׂה וּפְרָט בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, אֵין דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara posits: **Let us say** that it is **with regard to** the statement **that Rabbi Avin says** that **Rabbi Ile'a** says that these *tanna'im* **disagree, as Rabbi Avin says** that **Rabbi Ile'a says: Anywhere that you find a generalization** formulated **as a positive mitzva** followed by **a detail** formulated **as a prohibition, one does not deduce** from it that the generalization includes only the detail **based on** the hermeneutical principle of **a generalization and a detail;** rather, one interprets them as two independent *halakhot*.

The Mishnah states *ve'hayesa tel olam lo te'aseh ganot ufardesim divrei Rabbi Yose* — the condemned city is to remain an eternal mound, and Rabbi Yose holds one may not even make it into gardens or orchards. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *lo sibaneh od kemos shehayesa* — the prohibition is only against rebuilding it as it was, i.e., as a city, but one is permitted to make it into a garden.

### Klal Prat Rule

The Gemara asks whether they dispute the principle stated by Rav Avin in the name of Rabbi Ila'a: *kol makom she'atah motzei klal ba'aseh uferat b'lo sa'aseh, ein danin oso b'klal uferat* — wherever you find a general statement (*klal*) in a positive commandment (*aseh*) and a specific statement (*perat*) in a prohibition (*lo sa'aseh*), you do not apply the standard *klal u'ferat* hermeneutic that reads the general through the narrow lens of the specific. Rather, you treat them as separate statements.

The verse here reads *ve'hayesa tel olam* — a general *aseh*: leave it as an eternal mound entirely — followed by *lo sibaneh od*, a *lo sa'aseh*: do not build it. Under normal *klal u'ferat* logic, one would read the broad statement only through the prism of the narrow one: both would merely prohibit rebuilding it as a city. But Rav Avin's rule separates them: the *aseh* remains absolute (leave it as a mound, do nothing at all), while the *lo sa'aseh* is the more specific prohibition (do not rebuild it as a city).

Under this framework, the consequence would be that even if the *lo sa'aseh* is limited to not rebuilding a city, the *aseh* still forbids doing anything with the site whatsoever. Perhaps, then, Rabbi Yose follows Rav Avin and Rabbi Akiva does not?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי אָבִין, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר ״עוֹד״ לִגְמָרֵי מַשְׁמַע, וּמָר סָבַר ״עוֹד״ – לִכְמָה שֶׁהָיְתָה אֵינָהּ נִבְנֵית, אֲבָל נַעֲשֵׂית הִיא גַּנּוֹת וּפַרְדֵּסִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No, everyone is of** the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Avin, and here, they disagree about this:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, **holds** that the term **"again" indicates** that it is **entirely** prohibited to rebuild it at all. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that the term **"again"** indicates that **it is not built to** be **as it was, but it** may be **converted into gardens and orchards.**

The Gemara rejects this: *de'chulei alma is lehu d'Rav Avin* — in fact, both Tannaim can be understood as holding Rav Avin's principle.

### Od Meaning

The debate between them, the Gemara concludes, concerns the meaning of the word *od* in *lo sibaneh od*. One view — *od legamrei mashma* — holds that *od* means "at all, ever again": do not build anything here whatsoever. This would make the *lo sa'aseh* as absolute as the *aseh*, and both together forbid any construction or use of the land, including gardens. The other view — *od kemos shehayesa* — holds that *od* means "as it was before": do not restore it to what it was, i.e., do not rebuild it as a city; but other uses remain permitted.

Rashi grapples with the difficulty that if both Tannaim accept Rav Avin's principle — which separates the *aseh* from the *lo sa'aseh* and leaves the *aseh* in its full breadth — then Rabbi Akiva should still be constrained by the *aseh* of *ve'hayesa tel olam* even if he reads the *lo sa'aseh* narrowly. Rashi suggests that in this case we focus on the *lo sibaneh* clause as defining the meaning of *ve'hayesa tel olam*, and the debate is precisely over the parameters of *lo sibaneh* and the word *od*. Rashi also notes a second possible reading of Rav Avin's principle: rather than separating the *aseh* from the *lo sa'aseh*, one might instead read both through the lens of the *aseh*, making the *lo sa'aseh* as broadly construed as the *aseh*. These remain the two possibilities within Rav Avin's framework, and the Gemara here anchors the Tannaitic dispute in the narrower question of what *od* demands.

## Eternal Mound Debate (II)

### Trees Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיוּ בָּהּ אִילָנוֹת תְּלוּשִׁין – אֲסוּרִין, מְחוּבָּרִין – מוּתָּרִין. שֶׁל עִיר אַחֶרֶת, בֵּין תְּלוּשִׁין בֵּין מְחוּבָּרִין – אֲסוּרִין. מַאי ״עִיר אַחֶרֶת״? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: יְרִיחוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיְתָה הָעִיר חֵרֶם לַה׳... וַיַּשְׁבַּע יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בָּעֵת הַהִיא לֵאמֹר אָרוּר הָאִישׁ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֲשֶׁר יָקוּם וּבָנָה אֶת הָעִיר הַזֹּאת אֶת יְרִיחוֹ בִּבְכֹרוֹ יְיַסְּדֶנָּה וּבִצְעִירוֹ יַצִּיב דְּלָתֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where **there were trees in** the city, if they are **detached** from the ground, they are **forbidden** and must be burned as the spoils of an idolatrous city; if they are **attached** to the ground they are **permitted,** i.e., they are not destroyed. By contrast, trees **of another city, whether detached or attached,** are **forbidden.** The Gemara asks: To **what** is the *baraita* referring with the phrase: **Another city? Rav Ḥisda says:** The reference is to **Jericho, as it is written: "And the city shall be devoted,** it and all that is in it, **to the Lord…And Joshua charged them at that time by oath, saying: Cursed be the man before the Lord, that rises up to build this city Jericho; he shall lay its foundation with his firstborn, and with his youngest son shall he set up the gates of it"** (Joshua 6:17, 26).

The Gemara turns to a *braisa* that elaborates on the question of what may or may not be done with the site: *hayu bo ilanot tlushin asurin* — if there were chopped-down trees in the *ir nidachas*, they are forbidden. Such trees are property that has already been gathered, and as property of the condemned city they fall under the prohibition. *Mechubarim mutarin* — but trees that are still growing are permitted, either because uprooting and burning them would require an additional act of severance (*mechusar tlishah* or *mechusar ktsitsah*) not contemplated by the straightforward obligation of burning the *ir nidachas*, or because living, rooted trees are more akin to landscape than property. *Ve'shel ir acheret bein tlushin bein mechubarim asur* — but trees belonging to another city, whether chopped down or still growing, are forbidden.

This prompts the question: what is this "other city" whose trees are forbidden even while growing? *Amar Rav Chisda Yericho* — Rav Chisda answers: the reference is to *Yericho*. The case is not *ir nidachas* at all but the *cherem* that Yehoshua declared over *Yericho*, which was of an altogether different and more comprehensive character.

## Yericho Story

### Yericho Cherem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: לֹא יְרִיחוֹ עַל שֵׁם עִיר אַחֶרֶת, וְלֹא עִיר אַחֶרֶת עַל שֵׁם יְרִיחוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״בָּנָה חִיאֵל בֵּית הָאֱלִי אֶת יְרִיחֹה בַּאֲבִירָם בְּכֹרוֹ יִסְּדָהּ וּבִשְׂגוּב צְעִירוֹ הִצִּיב דְּלָתֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that this includes a prohibition **not** to build **Jericho** even after changing its name **to the name of another city, and not** to build **another city** after giving it the **name** of **Jericho, as it is written: "Hiel the Bethelite built Jericho; with Abiram, his firstborn, he laid its foundation, and with his young son Segub set up its gates"** (I Kings 16:34).

*Vehayta ha'ir cherem l'Hashem* — the city of *Yericho* was placed under a total *cherem* to God. Unlike the more specific legal categories of *ir nidachas* — where items requiring an additional act of preparation to be usable are exempt from the prohibition — the *cherem* of *Yericho* was absolute: everything was forbidden, even the growing trees. This explains why the *braisa* needed to specify a separate category for *Yericho*'s trees.

### Joshua Curse

The verse records Yehoshua's oath: *arur ha'ish lifnei Hashem asher yakum u'vanah et ha'ir hazot et Yericho* — cursed is the man before God who will rise up and build this city, *Yericho*. *Bivchoro yisdenah* — in his firstborn he will lay its foundation, *uvits'iro yatsiv daltayah* — and in his youngest he will set up its doors; meaning his eldest child will die when he begins construction and his youngest will die when he completes it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: בַּאֲבִירָם בְּכוֹרוֹ, רָשָׁע, לֹא הָיָה לוֹ לִלְמוֹד. בִּשְׂגוּב צְעִירוֹ, הָיָה לוֹ לִלְמוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: From the death **of Abiram, his firstborn, the wicked, it was not** incumbent **upon him to learn** not to build Jericho, as Abiram's death could be attributed to chance. But **with** the death of **Segub his young son, it was** incumbent **upon him to learn** that it was due to Joshua's curse that they died.

A *braisa* reflects on this: *Ba'Aviram bechoro rasha lo haya lo lilmod* — when Aviram, his firstborn, died, perhaps Chiel could have dismissed it as due to Aviram's own wickedness and not recognized the fulfillment of Yehoshua's curse. *Bishguv tze'iro haya lo lilmod* — but when his youngest son Seguv died, he should certainly have understood what was happening. *Hachi ka'amar* — the *braisa* clarifies: it is Chiel himself who is called the *rasha*, not Aviram; the statement means that from the death of his firstborn Aviram, that *rasha* Chiel should already have recognized the curse of Yehoshua, and certainly by the death of Seguv his youngest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבִירָם וּשְׂגוּב מַאי עֲבוּד? מַאי קָאָמַר? הָכִי קָאָמַר: בַּאֲבִירָם בְּכוֹרוֹ הָיָה לוֹ לִלְמוֹד לְאוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע, בִּשְׂגוּב צְעִירוֹ. מִמַּשְׁמָע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בַּאֲבִירָם בְּכוֹרוֹ״ אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁשְּׂגוּב צְעִירוֹ? מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״שְׂגוּב צְעִירוֹ״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיָה מְקַבֵּר וְהוֹלֵךְ מֵאֲבִירָם עַד שְׂגוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What did Abiram and Segub do** that they are characterized as wicked, and **what is** the *baraita* **saying?** The Gemara answers that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: From** the death of **Abiram, his firstborn, that wicked man** Hiel **should have learned about** the cause of the death of **Segub his young** son. **By inference from that** which **is stated: "With Abiram, his firstborn," do I not know that Segub** was **his young** son? Rather, **what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "His young son Segub"?** It **teaches that he gradually buried** all his sons **from Abiram through Segub,** and he should have suspected that Joshua's curse caused the deaths.

The verse also teaches: *mima shene'emar ba'Aviram bechoro eini yode'a sheSeguv tze'iro* — if the verse already told us about Aviram the firstborn, why did it also have to mention Seguv the youngest? *Melamed shehaya mekaver vehoelch me'Aviram ad Seguv* — this teaches that Chiel was burying child after child throughout the entire construction: beginning with his firstborn when he broke ground and ending with his youngest when he completed the city, having lost every child in between. This is likely the simple meaning of Yehoshua's curse and of the verse's fulfillment.

### Chiel Violation

*Dechativ bana Chiel beit ha'Eli et Yericho* — the verse records that Chiel of *Beit-El* built *Yericho*. Rashi notes that the word *alah* (*Eli* inverted) means "curse," flagging who this person was. According to the Gemara's reading — though the plain sense of the verse is that he rebuilt *Yericho* itself — Chiel built a new city and named it *Yericho*, thereby triggering the second clause of Yehoshua's curse. *Ba'Aviram bechoro yisda uvishguv tze'iro hetziv delateha* — with his firstborn Aviram he laid the foundation, and with his youngest son Seguv he set up the doors, precisely as Yehoshua had sworn.

## Elijah Keys

### Moses vs Joshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחְאָב שׁוֹשְׁבִינֵיהּ הֲוָה. אֲתָא אִיהוּ וְאֵלִיָּהוּ לְמִשְׁאַל בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּי טַמְיָא. יָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: דִּילְמָא כִּי לָט יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הָכִי לָט – לֹא יְרִיחוֹ עַל שֵׁם עִיר אַחֶרֶת, וְלֹא עִיר אַחֶרֶת עַל שֵׁם יְרִיחוֹ? אָמַר לֵיהּ אֵלִיָּהוּ: אִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא לְוָוטְתָא דְּמֹשֶׁה לָא קָא מִקַּיְימָא, דִּכְתִיב ״וְסַרְתֶּם וַעֲבַדְתֶּם וְגוֹ׳״ וּכְתִיב ״וְחָרָה אַף ה׳ בָּכֶם וְעָצַר אֶת הַשָּׁמַיִם וְגוֹ׳״, וְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא אוֹקֵים לֵיהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה עַל כׇּל תֶּלֶם וְתֶלֶם וְלָא שָׁבֵיק לֵיהּ מִיטְרָא לְמֵיזַל מִיסְגָּד לֵיהּ. לְוָוטְתָא דִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ תַּלְמִידֵיהּ מִקַּיְימָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Ahab was** Hiel's close friend and **groomsman. He and Elijah came to inquire about** Hiel's **welfare** in **the house of mourning [*bei tamya*].** Hiel **sat and said: Perhaps when Joshua cursed, this** is what **he cursed: Not** to build **Jericho** even after changing its name **to the name of another city, and not** to build **another city** after giving it the **name** of **Jericho. Elijah said to him: Yes,** that is the curse. Ahab **said to** Elijah: **Now the curse of Moses is not fulfilled, as it is written: "And you go astray and worship** other gods," **and it is written: "Then the Lord's anger will flare against you, and He will close the heavens,** and there will be no rain" (Deuteronomy 11:16–17). **And that man,** referring to himself, **established** an object of **idol worship on each and every furrow** in the kingdom of Israel, **and the rain** is so plentiful that **it does not allow him to go and worship it;** will **the curse of his student, Joshua, be fulfilled?**

The Gemara now unfolds a narrative that knits these events together. *Achav shoshvinei hava* — Ahab was the close companion (the *shoshvin*, the groomsman figure) of this Chiel, and Ahab and Eliyahu both went to pay a condolence call at the house of mourning — *lemishal bishlomei beveit tamya*, a term for the house of an *avel* (Rashi notes an alternate reading *beit ta'ama*, "the house of discourse," referring to the practice of articulating publicly why everyone was gathered, offering words of consolation).

There, Chiel was heard wondering why his children had died, and the Gemara's narrative presents him reasoning through the curses: perhaps Yehoshua's oath applied not only to rebuilding *Yericho* but also to building another city and naming it *Yericho*. Eliyahu confirmed: yes, that is precisely the scope of Yehoshua's curse.

At this point Ahab speaks up with what he presents as a logical objection. *Hashta luta deMoshe lo kamikayeim* — now, Moshe's curse was not fulfilled. *Dechativ vesartem va'avadetem* — the Torah warned that if Israel would stray and worship other gods, *vecharah af Hashem bachem ve-atzar es hashamayim*, God would restrain the heavens and there would be no rain. Yet Ahab himself, *okim lei avodas kochavim al kol telem va-telem*, had erected an idol on every single furrow throughout the land — and far from there being a drought, there had been so much rain that he could not even travel to prostrate himself before his idols. If Moshe's curse went unfulfilled, says Ahab, how can Yehoshua's curse — the curse of a student — have been fulfilled? *Levutata de'Yehoshua talmidei mekayema* — that the oath of Yehoshua, his disciple, should be realized?

### No Rain Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלִיָּהוּ הַתִּשְׁבִּי מִתֹּשָׁבֵי גִלְעָד חַי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אִם יִהְיֶה טַל וּמָטָר וְגוֹ׳״. בָּעֵי רַחֲמֵי, וִיהַבוּ לֵיהּ אַקְלִידָא דְּמִטְרָא, וְקָם וַאֲזַל.
>
> **Translation:** The verse relates Elijah's reaction: **Immediately: "And Elijah the Tishbite, who was of the inhabitants of Gilead, said** to Ahab: **As the Lord God of Israel lives,** before whom I stand, **there shall not be dew or rain** these years, but according to my word" (I Kings 17:1). Elijah **prayed for mercy and they gave him the key to rainfall** enabling him to dictate when it would rain, **and he arose and went.**

Eliyahu's response was immediate. *Miyad vayomer Eliyahu ha-Tishbi mi-toshevei Gilad chai Hashem Elokei Yisrael im yihye tal u-matar* — immediately he declared in God's name: there shall be neither dew nor rain. The juxtaposition of this declaration with the story of Chiel building *Yericho* is embedded in the text of *Melachim Alef*, chapters 16–17, and the Gemara reads them as causally linked: Ahab's provocative argument impelled Eliyahu to take this sweeping oath.

*Ba'ei rachamei ve-yahavu lei aklida de-mitra* — Eliyahu prayed and was granted the key to the rain, meaning control over whether rain would fall. He then departed, and God instructed him to hide in the *nachal Keris*, where the ravens would bring him bread and meat in the morning and evening. The Gemara notes: *mi-bei tavchei de-Achav* — the meat the ravens brought came from the slaughterhouses of Ahab himself, tying all the players together. After some time the wadi dried up for lack of rain. Once God perceived the tremendous suffering in the world — *keivan de-chaza de-ika tsaara be-alma* — He wished the rain to return, but He had handed the key over to Eliyahu.

### Keys Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי אַחַר הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה חָלָה בֶּן הָאִשָּׁה בַּעֲלַת הַבָּיִת״. בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי לְמִיתַּן לֵיהּ אַקְלִידָא דִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: שָׁלֹשׁ מַפְתְּחוֹת לֹא נִמְסְרוּ לְשָׁלִיחַ: שֶׁל חַיָּה, וְשֶׁל גְּשָׁמִים, וְשֶׁל תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים. יֹאמְרוּ: שְׁתַּיִם בְּיַד תַּלְמִיד וְאַחַת בְּיַד הָרַב! אַיְיתִי הָא וּשְׁקֵיל הַאי. דִּכְתִיב: ״לֵךְ הֵרָאֵה אֶל אַחְאָב וְאֶתְּנָה מָטָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written: "And it came to pass after these matters, that the son of the woman, the mistress of the house, became sick"** (I Kings 17:17). Elijah **prayed for mercy,** for God **to give him the key to the resurrection of the dead. They said to him** from Heaven: **Three keys were not** typically **passed to an agent:** The key **to a woman in childbirth,** the key **to rainfall, and** the key **to the resurrection of the dead.** You already have the key to rainfall; do you also request the key to the resurrection of the dead? People **will say: Two** keys **are in the possession of the student and one** key **is in the possession of the Master. Bring** Me **this** key to rainfall, **and take this** key to the resurrection of the dead. Due to Elijah's request, he was forced to revoke his oath, **as it is written: "Go, appear before Ahab; and I will give rain"** (I Kings 18:1).

God therefore directed Eliyahu to go to *Tzarfas*. There, a woman's son became gravely ill and died, and *ba'ei rachamei le-mitan lei aklida de-techiyas ha-meisim* — Eliyahu prayed to be given the key of *techiyas ha-meisim*, resurrection of the dead. Heaven responded: *shalosh mafteichos lo nimsru le-shaliach* — three keys are never entrusted to an agent and are held directly by God: the key of *chayyah* (childbirth), the key of *geshamim* (rain), and the key of *techiyas ha-meisim* (resurrection). These three are united by their life-giving character.

But Eliyahu already held the key of rain. Heaven therefore said to him: *yomru shtayim be-yad talmid ve-achas be-yad ha-rav* — shall people say that the student (i.e., Eliyahu) holds two of the keys while the Master (God) holds only one? If you want the key of *techiyas ha-meisim*, you must return the key of rain: *ayisi ha u-shakil hai* — bring this one and take that one. This is borne out by the very next verse: *lech hera'eh el Achav ve-etnah matar* — go and appear before Ahab and I will give rain — for now God held the key to rain and could return it.

## Elijah Keys (II)

### Door Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַשׁ הַהוּא גָּלִילָאָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: מָשָׁל דְּאֵלִיָּהוּ, לָמָּה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְגַבְרָא דְּטַרְקֵיהּ לְגַלֵּיהּ וְאַבְּדֵיהּ לְמַפְתְּחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain Galilean taught before Rav Ḥisda:** There is **a parable** for the actions **of Elijah; to what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a man who slammed his door and lost his key.** Elijah first prevented the rain from falling, and then no longer had possession of the key to enable it to fall again.

*Darsh hahu gelila kamei d'Rav Chisda* — a certain Galilean expounded before Rav Chisda a parable for Eliyahu's situation: *legavra d'tarkei legalei v'avdei l'miftacha* — he is like a man who slammed his door and thereby lost his key to get back inside. Eliyahu shut off the rain with his oath, but in doing so forfeited the very power to restore it; once he surrendered the key back to Heaven, the ability to bring rain returned to God alone.

### Kaptan Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּצִיפּוֹרִי: אַבָּא אֵלִיָּהוּ קַפְּדָן הֲוָה. רְגִיל לְמֵיתֵי גַּבֵּיהּ. אִיכַּסִּי מִינֵּיהּ תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי, וְלָא אֲתָא. כִּי אֲתָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי לָא אֲתָא מָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קַפְּדָן קָרֵית לִי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא דְּקַמַּן, דְּקָא קָפֵיד מָר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei of Tzippori taught: Father Elijah,** a deferential and affectionate characterization for Elijah the prophet, **was difficult.** Elijah **was accustomed to coming** and revealing himself **before** Rabbi Yosei each day. **He was obscured from him for three days and did not come. When he came** again, Rabbi Yosei **said to him: Why did the Master not come?** Elijah **said to him:** You denigrated me when **you called me difficult.** Rabbi Yosei **said to** Elijah: **This** example **that is before us** illustrates the point, **as my Master was** being **difficult** by not coming during those days.

*Darsh Rabbi Yosi b'Tzipori* — Rabbi Yosi expounded in *Tzipori*: *Abba Eliyahu kaptan haya* — the great Eliyahu (*Abba Eliyahu* meaning the master or, as Rashi renders it, *chavivi v'gidoli*, my beloved and great one) was a *kaptan*, someone easily provoked and quick to react with great force. The evidence is precisely the episode just described: he takes an immediate and sweeping oath, insists on it steadfastly, and must be maneuvered by Heaven before he relents. *Havi ragil l'meisei gabei* — Eliyahu would regularly visit Rabbi Yosi. Once he heard himself called a *kaptan*, he did not appear for three days. *Amar lei amai lo asa mar* — when he finally returned, Rabbi Yosi asked why he had not come. *Kaptan kerisa li* — you called me a *kaptan*. Rabbi Yosi's reply was pointed: *hada ka'amran d'ka kapid mar* — that is precisely what I said, and look — you reacted and got upset. The evidence for the characterization was the reaction itself.

## Wicked Righteous

### Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלֹא יִדְבַּק בְּיָדְךָ מְאוּמָה מִן הַחֵרֶם״ – כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁרְשָׁעִים בָּעוֹלָם, חֲרוֹן אַף בָּעוֹלָם וְכוּ׳. מַאן רְשָׁעִים? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: גַּנָּבֵי. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רָשָׁע בָּא לָעוֹלָם – חָרוֹן בָּא לְעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּבוֹא רָשָׁע בָּא גַם בּוּז וְעִם קָלוֹן חֶרְפָּה״. רָשָׁע אָבֵד מִן הָעוֹלָם – טוֹבָה בָּאָה לְעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבַאֲבֹד רְשָׁעִים רִנָּה״. צַדִּיק נִפְטָר מִן הָעוֹלָם – רָעָה בָּאָה לְעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַצַּדִּיק אָבָד וְאֵין אִישׁ שָׂם עַל לֵב וְאַנְשֵׁי חֶסֶד נֶאֱסָפִים בְּאֵין מֵבִין כִּי מִפְּנֵי הָרָעָה נֶאֱסַף הַצַּדִּיק״. צַדִּיק בָּא לָעוֹלָם – טוֹבָה בָּאָה לְעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֶה יְנַחֲמֵנוּ מִמַּעֲשֵׂנוּ וּמֵעִצְּבוֹן יָדֵינוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **"And there shall cleave nothing of that which was devoted to your hand"** (Deuteronomy 13:18), **as long as the wicked** exist **in the world,** there is **wrath in the world.** The Gemara asks: **Who are** these **wicked** people mentioned in the mishna? **Rav Yosef said:** They are **thieves.** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When **a wicked** person **comes into the world, wrath comes into the world, as it is stated: "When the wicked comes into the world, contempt also comes, and with ignominy, reproach"** (Proverbs 18:3). When **a wicked** person **is eliminated from the world, good comes into the world, as it is stated: "And when the wicked perish there is jubilation"** (Proverbs 11:10). When **a righteous** person **passes from the world, evil comes into the world, as it is stated: "The righteous perishes and no man lays it to heart; and merciful men are taken, none understand that due to the evil the righteous is taken"** (Isaiah 57:1) When **a righteous** person **comes into the world, good comes into the world** with him, **as it is stated** with regard to Noah: **"This is one who shall comfort us for our work and the toil of our hands"** (Genesis 5:29).

Returning to the end of the Mishnah's verse — *v'lo yidbak b'yadcha m'uma min hacharem* — the Gemara notes that the verse concludes *l'maan yashuv Hashem mecharon apo*, "so that God shall return from His fiery wrath." The Gemara reads this in conjunction with the principle: *kol zman she'resha'im ba'olam charon af ba'olam* — as long as there are wicked people in the world, divine wrath is in the world.

Remarkably, Rav Yosef identifies the *resha'im* in this context as *ganavim*, thieves — not, as one might expect given the entire context of *ir nidachas* and idolatry, as practitioners of *avodah zarah*. The Gemara continues with a *braisa*: *rasha ba la'olam charon ba la'olam* — when a wicked person enters the world, divine anger enters with it, *shene'emar b'vo rasha ba gam buz v'im kalon cherpah* — as the verse says, when the wicked one comes, there comes also contempt, and with disgrace comes reproach. The connection to *charon af* is understood through *cherpah*, shame and reproach being read as cognate with the heat of divine wrath.

*Rasha avad min ha'olam tova ba la'olam* — when a wicked person is lost from the world, good comes, *shene'emar u'v'ivud resha'im rina* — as the verse says, with the destruction of the wicked there is rejoicing.

*Tzadik niftar min ha'olam ra'ah ba la'olam* — but when a righteous person passes from the world, evil comes, *shene'emar hatzadik avad v'ein ish sam al lev v'anshei chesed ne'esafim ba'im m'vein ki m'pnei hara'ah ne'esaf hatzadik* — the righteous person is lost and no one takes it to heart; men of *chesed* are gathered in without anyone understanding that it is because of the evil to come that the *tzadik* was gathered in. The Gemara reads this as meaning that once the *tzadik* is gone, evil follows.

*Tzadik ba la'olam tova ba la'olam* — and when a righteous person comes into the world, good comes with him, *shene'emar ze yenachamenu mima'aseinu umitzvon yadeinu* — as Lemech said of Noach: this one will comfort us from our toil and the pain of our hands.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל יֵשׁ לָהֶם חֵלֶק וּסְלִיקָא לַהּ מַסֶּכֶת סַנְהֶדְרִין
>
> **Translation:** **We have completed *masechet Sanhedrin*.** [The *hadran* is recited upon completing a tractate of the Talmud.]

With this, the Gemara concludes:

*Hadran alach kol Yisrael yesh lahem chelek l'olam haba, u'selikah lach masechet Sanhedrin* — we have completed *masechet Sanhedrin*.
