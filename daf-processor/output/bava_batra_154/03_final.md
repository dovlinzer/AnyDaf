# Bava Batra 154 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shtar Matana Debate

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְאָיָה – בְּמַאי? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: רְאָיָה בְּעֵדִים. רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמְרִי: רְאָיָה בְּקִיּוּם הַשְּׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the **proof** that the recipients must bring, **in what** manner is it brought? **Rav Huna says:** The **proof** is presented **by** bringing **witnesses** who testify that the giver was healthy. **Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna say:** The **proof** is presented **by** the **ratification of the deed,** i.e., the recipients are required only to ascertain that the signatures of the witnesses on the deed are authentic in order to prove that it is not forged.

The *gemara* on 154a opens approximately ten lines down with the word *lekaymo*, and the context requires some orienting. The *mishnah* had presented a case in which a person wrote a *shtar matana* — a gift of all his property — without any explicit designation of his status at the time. Normally, a *shtar matana* would contain a formulaic phrase signaling whether it was issued by a *sh'chiv meira* or a *baria*. When the donor was ill and bedridden, the document would read *ketiv ve-rami be-arsei* — that he was lying in his bed. When the donor was healthy, it would read *ke-d-havi mehalech al raglohi* — as he was walking on his feet. The *shtar* in the *mishnah* contained neither phrase. The donor, now walking around in apparent good health, claims that he gave the gift as a *sh'chiv meira* and has since recovered, and therefore the gift is void and the property should be returned to him. The recipients counter that he gave it as a *baria*, and the gift is therefore irrevocable.

### Default Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רְאָיָה בְּעֵדִים – קָא מִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב וְרַבִּי נָתָן; (סִימָן: מַנִּיחַ.) רַבִּי מֵאִיר – כְּרַבִּי נָתָן, וְרַבָּנַן – כְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav Huna says** that the **proof** is presented **by** bringing **witnesses.** He maintains that Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute of Rabbi Ya'akov and Rabbi Natan** in the *baraita* (153b). The Gemara notes **a mnemonic** device that indicates which tannaitic opinions are correlated: ***Manniaḥ*,** which represents the letters *mem*, *nun*, *yod*, *ḥet*, stands for Meir, Natan, Ya'akov, and the Rabbis [*ḥakhamim*]. This indicates that **Rabbi Meir,** who says that the giver must bring proof that he was on his deathbed, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan,** who maintains that one presumes that the current situation reflects the situation at the time the gift was bestowed. **And the Rabbis,** who say that the recipients must bring proof that the giver was healthy, hold **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov.**

The *mishnah* records a dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* on the question of who bears the burden of proof. Rabbi Meir holds *alav le-havi ra'ayah she-sh'chiv meira haya* — the burden falls on the donor to prove he was a *sh'chiv meira* — which effectively means we presume the *shtar* was that of a *baria*. The *chachamim*, by contrast, invoke *hamotzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah*, placing the burden on whichever party seeks to extract the property from its current holder. This operates on the premise that the status of the *shtar* is in doubt, and when there is *safek*, the one who seeks to take from another bears the burden.

One might initially explain this dispute on simple circumstantial grounds: since the *shtar* carries no explicit designation, perhaps a default applies — either that an undesignated document is presumed to reflect a healthy gift, or that a person who gives away all his property is more likely a *sh'chiv meira*, creating at minimum a *safek*. However, the *gemara* rejects this as the operative framing and instead presents two distinct amoraic interpretations of the *mishnah*.

### Rav Huna's Retrojection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא; דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: רְאָיָה בְּעֵדִים, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: רְאָיָה בְּקִיּוּם הַשְּׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this dispute with regard to the statement of the Rabbis is also the subject **of a dispute** between other *amora'im*, **as Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The **proof** is presented **by** bringing **witnesses, and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** The **proof** is presented **by** the **ratification of the deed.**

The first interpretation belongs to Rav Huna, who explains the dispute as *ra'ayah be-edim* — the question of proof revolves around witness testimony about the donor's status. According to Rav Huna, Rabbi Meir's position is grounded in retrojection from present to past: since the donor is currently healthy, we presume he was healthy when he wrote the *shtar*. This makes the document a *shtar baria* by default, and if the donor wishes to establish that he was in fact a *sh'chiv meira*, he must bring witnesses to that effect. The *chachamim*, on this reading, decline to retroject. They treat the matter as a genuine *safek* — both possibilities remain open — and therefore invoke *hamotzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah*: whichever side seeks to extract from the other bears the evidentiary burden. Under this framework, the recipients, if they wish to prevail, must bring witnesses establishing that the *shtar* was that of a *baria*.

### Kiyum HaShtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמְרִי רְאָיָה בְּקִיּוּם הַשְּׁטָר – קָא מִיפַּלְגִי בְּמוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ; דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ – צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna say** that the **proof** is presented **by** the **ratification of the deed.** The Gemara explains: Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna maintain that Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to** whether when there is a debtor **who admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **must ratify it** in court in order to collect payment. The same ruling would apply to a case where the person on his deathbed admits that he wrote the deed granting the gift. They explain **that Rabbi Meir holds** that when there is a debtor **who admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **need not ratify it** in court in order to collect payment, and in this case the giver cannot invalidate the deed by claiming that he was on his deathbed. **But the Rabbis hold** that even when there is a debtor **who admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **must ratify it** in court in order to collect payment.

The alternative explanation belongs to Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar Rav Huna, who argue the dispute turns not on witness testimony about the donor's health but on *ra'ayah be-okimta de-shtra* — proof through document validation. Their position requires careful unpacking, because it rests on a principle that is initially counterintuitive.

According to Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar Rav Huna, the debate is not really about which state of health to assume. Rather, their reading holds that the recipients do not need to produce witnesses testifying to the donor's health. All they need to do is validate the signatures on the *shtar* — perform *kiyum ha-shtar* — and that suffices to defeat the donor's claim.

### Migo & Credibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיפְּלִיגוּ בַהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא! דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין נֶאֱמָנִין לְפוֹסְלוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמָנִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't they** already **disagree with regard to this** matter **once? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to witnesses who ratified their signatures but claimed that at the time they signed the document they were not fit to bear witness, their testimony is **not deemed credible to invalidate** the document; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** Their testimony is **deemed credible.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אִיתְּמַר הָהִיא – בְּהַהִיא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן, מִשּׁוּם דְּאַלִּימֵי עֵדִים וּמַרְעִי שְׁטָרָא; אֲבָל הָכָא – הוּא דְּלָאו כָּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ, אֵימָא לָא; וְאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּהָא, בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר; אֲבָל בְּהָךְ, אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **is necessary** to state both cases, **because if** only **that** case with regard to witnesses who disqualified their testimony **was stated,** one might think that **the Sages say** that their testimony is accepted only **in that** case, **due to** the fact **that** the testimony of **witnesses is powerful and they can impair** the validity of the **document, but here,** with regard to **him,** the giver, **who** admitted that he wrote the deed but it **is not in his** power to impair the validity of the deed, **I would say** that his claim is **not** accepted. **And if** only **this** case, with regard to a debtor who admits that he wrote a promissory note, **was stated,** one might think that **Rabbi Meir says** that the giver cannot invalidate the deed only **with regard to this** case, **but with regard to that** case, where the witnesses ratified their signatures, **I would say** that Rabbi Meir **concedes to the Rabbis** that witnesses can invalidate the deed. Therefore, it **is necessary** to state the dispute in both cases.

The deeper principle underlying this approach is that the *sh'chiv meira* claim has no inherent credibility. It does not create a genuine *safek*. The claim is believed only because of a *migo*: since the *shtar* has not been validated, the claimant could alternatively have said the document is *mezuyaf* — a forgery — and that alternate claim would be accepted. Because he could say *mezuyaf*, he is believed, via *migo*, when he says instead that he was a *sh'chiv meira*.

But this means the believability of the *sh'chiv meira* claim is entirely parasitic on the absence of *kiyum*. A valid, uncontested *shtar* — one whose signatures have not been challenged — is, according to this position, a hundred percent ironclad from the moment it exists. The rabbis require signature validation only when the *shtar* is challenged; absent any such challenge, the *shtar* operates at full legal force. In this framework, *modeh be-shtar she-ketavo ein tzarich le-kaymo* — if the opposing party admits the *shtar* was written, there is no need to validate it, and the *migo* of *mezuyaf* evaporates, because the *shtar* is already fully operative without validation. Just as a person is not believed to say *parati* — I paid the debt — against a fully operative *shtar hov*, the donor is not believed to say *sh'chiv meira* once the *shtar* is treated as unimpeachable. If the recipients wish to block the *sh'chiv meira* claim entirely, they need only perform *kiyum ha-shtar*, thereby removing any theoretical basis for a *migo de-mezuyaf*, and the claim collapses.

The *gemara* at this point establishes that the debate of Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* on the question of *modeh be-shtar she-ketavo* was not limited to the *sh'chiv meira* case but was also argued explicitly in a *beraita* involving the case of *parati*. In that case, Reuven holds a *shtar* against Shimon and Shimon admits the *shtar* was written but claims he already paid. The witnesses to the *shtar* come forward and say yes, those are our signatures — but *ketanim hayinu, pesulei edut hayinu*: we were minors and ineligible to testify. This is a case of *peh she-asar peh she-hitir*: the very testimony that validates the signatures simultaneously invalidates them, effectively neutralizing the *kiyum*. Is Shimon then believed to say *parati* against this weakened, de facto unvalidated *shtar*?

Rabbi Meir says the witnesses' statement does not invalidate the *shtar*: until an actual claim of *ziyuf* is raised, the *shtar* retains full legal force, and *parati* is not believed. The *chachamim* say that since the *shtar* is effectively *lo mekuyam*, the counter-claim is believed.

The *gemara* then explains why both cases were necessary — *tzricha*. Had the debate been stated only in the case of *sh'chiv meira*, one might have thought the *chachamim* were lenient there specifically because the *shtar* had never been challenged and the donor's counter-claim carried no special weight to weaken the *shtar*. Had the debate been stated only in the *parati* case, one might have thought Rabbi Meir was strict only there because the witnesses themselves had actively wounded the *shtar* by declaring their own ineligibility — whereas in the *sh'chiv meira* case, perhaps Rabbi Meir would concede that the counter-claim works. Both cases are therefore needed to demonstrate that both opinions apply across both scenarios.

## Amoraic Debate

### Rabbah's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה: רְאָיָה בְּעֵדִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מַאי טַעְמָא? אִי נֵימָא מִדְּכוּלְּהוּ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ: ״כַּד הֲוָה מְהַלֵּךְ עַל רַגְלוֹהִי בְּשׁוּקָא״, וּבְהָא לָא כְּתִיב בָּהּ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שְׁכִיב מְרַע הָוֵי; אַדְּרַבָּה! מִדְּכוּלְּהוּ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ: ״כַּד קְצִיר וּרְמֵי בְּעַרְסֵיהּ״, וְהָא לָא כְּתִיב בָּהּ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בָּרִיא הָוֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba also says:** With regard to the **proof** that the recipients must present, it is presented **by** bringing **witnesses** who testify that the giver was healthy. **Abaye said to him: What is the reason** for this? **If we say** that **due to** the fact **that in all** deeds of gift the following formulation **is written: When he was walking on his feet in the marketplace,** which indicates that the gift was the gift of a healthy person, **and in this** deed this **was not written,** therefore one may **conclude from** the deed that the giver **was on his deathbed,** that is not correct. **On the contrary,** one could say that **due to** the fact **that in all** deeds concerning the gifts of a person on his deathbed the following **is written: When he was sick and lying in his bed, and in this** deed this **was not written,** therefore one may **conclude from** the deed that the giver **was healthy.**

The *gemara* reports that Rabbah aligned himself with the *ra'ayah be-edim* approach, corresponding to the position of Rav Huna: the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* turns on the question of what we presume, and once we fix the presumption, proof to the contrary requires witness testimony.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges this position. He asks: if you are going to say it might be a *sh'chiv meira*, you would need a reason to entertain that possibility. The standard formula in other *shtaros* of healthy donors reads *kad hava mehalech al raglohi be-shuka* — as he was walking in the marketplace. That phrase is not written here. So, Abaye presses, why should we raise the possibility of *sh'chiv meira*? On the contrary — since *shtarei sh'chiv meira* consistently contain the phrase *rami be-arsei* and this *shtar* does not, we should conclude *shema mina bari hava* — he was healthy. Why does the *chachamim*'s position treat the matter as an open *safek* rather than defaulting to *baria*?

### Safek Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר הָכִי וְאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר הָכִי, אוֹקִי מָמוֹנָא בְּחֶזְקַת מָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba replied: Since one **can say this and** one **can say that,** nothing can be concluded from the formulation of the deed. Therefore, due to the uncertainty, **establish the property in the possession of its** last known **owner.**

The *gemara* answers: *ika le-meimar hachi ve-ika le-meimar hachi* — both possibilities genuinely exist. The *shtar* contains neither the language of illness nor the language of health. That creates a true *safek*, and the *chachamim* therefore apply *hamotzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah*. Rabbi Meir, by contrast, resolves the *safek* by retrojecting from present health to past health and treats the document as a *shtar baria* by default.

### R. Yochanan vs. R.L.

The *gemara* notes that the same dispute between the two interpretive approaches — *ra'ayah be-edim* versus *ra'ayah be-okimta de-shtra* — was also contested among the *amoraim* of Eretz Yisrael. Rabbi Yochanan maintained *ra'ayah be-edim*: the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* is about which status to presume and what witness proof is required to displace it. Resh Lakish maintained *ra'ayah be-okimta de-shtra*: the dispute is about whether *kiyum ha-shtar* suffices to defeat the counter-claim, and once the *shtar* is validated the *sh'chiv meira* claim is entirely defeated.

## Bnei Brak Story

### Story Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: מַעֲשֶׂה בִּבְנֵי בְרַק בְּאֶחָד שֶׁמָּכַר בְּנִכְסֵי אָבִיו, וּמֵת, וּבָאוּ בְּנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה וְעִרְעֲרוּ לוֹמַר: קָטָן הָיָה בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה, וּבָאוּ וְשָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מַהוּ לְבוֹדְקוֹ? אָמַר לָהֶם: אִי אַתֶּם רַשָּׁאִים לְנַוְּולוֹ. וְעוֹד, סִימָנִין עֲשׂוּיִים לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** from a *baraita*: There was **an incident in Bnei Brak involving one who sold** some **of his father's property** that he had inherited, **and he died, and** the **members of** his **family came and contested** the sale, **saying: He was a minor at the time of** his **death,** and therefore the sale was not valid. **And they came and asked Rabbi Akiva: What is** the *halakha*? Is it permitted to exhume the corpse in order **to examine it** and ascertain whether or not the heir was a minor at the time of his death? Rabbi Akiva **said to them:** It is **not permitted** for **you to disgrace him** for the sake of a monetary claim. **And furthermore, signs** indicating puberty **are likely to change after death,** and therefore nothing can be proved by exhuming the body.

The *gemara* presents a narrative from Bnei Brak — the *Rashbam* notes parenthetically that *Bnei Brak* is indeed a place, *makom be-Eretz Yisrael* — involving a person who sold property from his father's estate and subsequently died. The family members came and raised the claim that he had been a *katan* at the time of the sale and therefore the transaction was void. It should be noted that the relevant threshold was not necessarily age thirteen; certain *amoraim* held that in matters of selling ancestral land, a person might need to be twenty years of age to effect a valid sale.

### Nivul HaMet & Autopsy

The family brought their question before Rabbi Akiva: *mahu le-vodko* — may we disinter the body to examine the physical signs of maturity and thereby determine whether the seller had been a *katan* at the time of his death? Rabbi Akiva responded on two grounds. First, *ee atem resha'im le-navlo* — you are not permitted to desecrate the body. Second, and as a practical matter, *simanim asuyim le-hishtanot achar mitah* — the physical signs are liable to change after death and the examination would therefore prove nothing in any event.

This *gemara* is a major locus for later *halachic* discussion on autopsies and *nivul ha-met*. The *Noda Biyehuda* cites it prominently in his famous responsum opposing autopsies. What is striking here is that the threshold for allowing *nivul ha-met* appears to be quite low — it might be permitted for the sake of resolving a monetary dispute. The *gemara* does not prohibit the disinterment on the grounds that money is insufficient to override the prohibition; it prohibits it because the examination would be futile. This implies that had the examination been probative, the monetary interest might well have sufficed to permit it — a significant datum for the broader *halachic* analysis.

The *gemara* also raises a subsidiary question: who wanted to disinter the body — the family or the purchasers (*lekuchot*)? The *Rashbam* understands that it was the *lekuchot* who sought to prove that the seller had been an adult at the time of the sale. *Tosafot* notes two reasons why the *lekuchot* might have stronger standing to press for examination than the family members would: first, the *lekuchot* had paid money and bore a genuine financial loss, while the heirs had merely inherited and would recover the purchase price; second, the *lekuchot* were not the deceased's brothers, and the moral force of Rabbi Akiva's rebuke — how can you think to desecrate the body? — carries greater weight against family members than against strangers. A third consideration is whether the *halachic* obligation of *kevod ha-met* itself applies differently to family members than to others, though that question remains open.

### Property Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי – דְּאָמֵינָא רְאָיָה בְּעֵדִים; כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר לְלָקוֹחוֹת: ״אַיְיתוֹ עֵדִים״, וְלָא אַשְׁכַּחוּ, הַיְינוּ דְּקָא אֲתוֹ וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַהוּ לְבוֹדְקוֹ. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ: רְאָיָה בְּקִיּוּם הַשְּׁטָר, לְמָה לְהוּ לְבוֹדְקוֹ? לְקַיְּימוּ שְׁטָרַיְיהוּ, וְלוֹקְמוּ בְּנִכְסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan explains: **Granted, according to my** explanation of the mishna, **that I say** that the **proof** must be presented **by** bringing **witnesses,** I can explain the *baraita*. **Since** Rabbi Akiva **said to the buyers: Bring witnesses, and they did not find** witnesses, **this is** the reason **that they came and said to him: What is** the *halakha*? Is it permitted **to examine him? But according to you, that you say** that the **proof** is presented **by ratification of the deed, why do they** need **to examine him? Let them ratify their deed and they shall be established** as owners **of the property.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ נִכְסֵי בְּחֶזְקַת בְּנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה קָיְימִי – וְקָא אָתוּ לָקוֹחוֹת וּמְעַרְעֲרִי? נִכְסֵי בְּחֶזְקַת לָקוֹחוֹת קָיְימִי – וְקָא אָתוּ בְּנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה וְקָא מְעַרְעֲרִי!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish replies: **Do you maintain** that **the property stood in the possession of** the **members of** his **family and the buyers came and contested** their possession of the property? Rather, **the property stood in the possession of** the **buyers, and** the **members of** his **family came and contested** the sale. Since they claimed that the deed was invalid, they could not prove their claim by ratifying the deed, but only by bringing witnesses or examining the body.

Resh Lakish responds to Rabbi Yochanan's citation of this story: *mi savrat nichsei be-chezkat bnei mishpacha kaymi?* — you misread the case. Rabbi Yochanan had assumed that the *lekuchot* were the party pressing to prove their case against the *yorshim* who held the property. Resh Lakish corrects this: the *lekuchot* held the property, having prevailed on the basis of their *shtar mekuyam*, and it was the *yorshim* who came to challenge. The *yorshim*'s claim that the seller was a *katan* is analogous to a *sh'chiv meira* claim: it accepts the *shtar* but asserts a context that voids it. According to Resh Lakish, once there is a *shtar mekuyam*, such claims are not believed, and the burden falls entirely on the *yorshim* to prove minority. This reading is perfectly consistent with the *ra'ayah be-okimta de-shtra* approach.

### Alternate Reading

Rabbi Yochanan had read the case the opposite way: even with a *shtar mekuyam*, a claim of *katan* creates a legitimate challenge, and the *lekuchot*, who held a *shtar*, nonetheless still needed to prove through witnesses that the seller was an adult. The *yorshim*' claim of minority, like a *sh'chiv meira* claim, is the type of counter-claim that survives even a validated *shtar* on Rabbi Yochanan's view. The *lekuchot*, unable to locate witnesses, were therefore the ones trying to exhume the body.

### Gemara Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ: אִי אַתֶּם רַשָּׁאִים לְנַוְּולוֹ, וְאִישְׁתִּיקוּ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה קָא מְעַרְעֲרִי, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אִישְׁתִּיקוּ. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לָקוֹחוֹת קָא מְעַרְעֲרִי, אַמַּאי שָׁתְקִי? לֵימְרוּ לֵיהּ: אֲנַן זוּזֵי יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ, לִינַּוַול וְלִינַּוַּול!
>
> **Translation:** **This, too, stands to reason, as** Rabbi Akiva **said to** the claimants: **You are not permitted to disgrace him, and they were silent. Granted, if you say** that the **members of** his **family were contesting** the sale, **due to that** reason **they were silent,** as they accepted that they should not disgrace their relative. **But if you say** that the **buyers were contesting** the claim of the relatives, **why were they silent? They should have said to** Rabbi Akiva: **We gave him money,** and if our right to the property cannot be proven without disgracing him, **let him be disgraced.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא – לָא אִירְיָא; הָכִי קָאָמַר לְהוּ: חֲדָא, דְּאִי אַתֶּם רַשָּׁאִים לְנַוְּולוֹ. וְעוֹד, וְכִי תֵּימְרוּ זוּזֵי שְׁקַל – לִינַּוַול וְלִינַּוַּול; סִימָנִים עֲשׂוּיִין לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **If it is due to that** reason, i.e., this claim they could have said, **there is no** conclusive **argument. This is what** Rabbi Akiva **said to them: One** reason to prohibit exhuming the body is **that you are not permitted to disgrace him. And furthermore, if you should say: He took** the **money; let him be disgraced,** in any event nothing can be proved by exhuming the body, as **signs** indicating puberty **are likely to change after death.**

The *gemara* then notes that Resh Lakish's reading is the more natural one. Rabbi Akiva's words — *ee atem resha'im le-navlo* — silenced the petitioners. If it were the *lekuchot* who were pressing to disinter, his rebuke would have had no force: they would simply have responded that they paid good money and the financial stake justifies the examination, *linvul ve-linvul*. The fact that they fell silent — *ve-ishtaku* — indicates it was the *yorshim*, the family, who wanted to do this to their own brother, and Rabbi Akiva was able to shame them into dropping the request.

The *gemara* does entertain an alternative explanation of the silence: perhaps the *lekuchot* were present and fell silent before Rabbi Akiva's authority, or perhaps the practical futility argument — *simanim asuyim le-hishtanot achar mitah* — was sufficient to deter them regardless. But the *gemara*'s preferred conclusion is that the narrative structure supports Resh Lakish's reading: the *lekuchot* had already won on the strength of their validated *shtar*, and it was the *yorshim* who were trying and failing to sustain their counter-claim.

This confirms Resh Lakish's consistent position: *kiyum ha-shtar* suffices to defeat claims of the *sh'chiv meira* or *katan* type, and Rabbi Yochanan's position that witness proof remains necessary even after *kiyum* is the opposing view that must account for why such claims survive a validated document at all.

## Bar Kappara Mishna

### Field Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שָׁאַל רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אֶת רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, זוֹ שֶׁשְּׁנוּיָה בְּמִשְׁנַת בַּר קַפָּרָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה אוֹכֵל שָׂדֶה וּבָא – בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁהִיא שֶׁלּוֹ, וְקָרָא עָלָיו אֶחָד עַרְעָר לוֹמַר: ״שֶׁלִּי הִיא״, וְהוֹצִיא זֶה אֶת אוֹנוֹ לוֹמַר: ״שֶׁמְּכַרְתָּהּ לִי״ אוֹ ״שֶׁנְּתַתָּהּ לִי בְּמַתָּנָה״, אִם אָמַר: ״אֵינִי מַכִּיר בִּשְׁטָר זֶה מֵעוֹלָם״ – יִתְקַיֵּים הַשְּׁטָר בְּחוֹתְמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof: **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish asked Rabbi Yoḥanan** about **that which is taught in the Mishna of bar Kappara:** There was one **who was continually enjoying** the profits from **a field,** and it was the **presumption that it was his, and someone contested his** claim, **saying: It is mine. And that** person, who was profiting from the field, **produced a deed,** in order **to say:** It is mine, **as you sold** this field **to me, or:** It is mine, **as you gave me** this field **as a gift. If** the one who protested his claim **said: I do not recognize that deed** as one that I have **ever** written, **the deed** must **be ratified through its signatures.**

Resh Lakish now sends Rabbi Yochanan a challenge from the *mishnat Bar Kappara* — a teaching of the *tanna* Bar Kappara. The case: a person is found harvesting a field, operating under the presumption (*chazaka*) that it is his, when a second party appears and says the field is his, producing a *shtar* — either a deed of sale or a deed of gift. The person in possession responds by claiming the *shtar* is a forgery — *mezuyaf*. In that case, *Bar Kappara* rules *yitkayem ha-shtar be-chotmav*: the *shtar*-holder must validate the signatures, and if successfully validated, he wins. This is straightforward: a claim of forgery triggers the requirement of *kiyum ha-shtar*, and once validated, the forgery claim is defeated.

### Shtar Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָמַר: ״שְׁטַר פַּסִּים הוּא זֶה״ אוֹ ״שְׁטַר אֲמָנָה״; ״שֶׁמָּכַרְתִּי לָךְ, וְלֹא נָתַתָּ לִי דָּמִים״ – אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים, הַלֵּךְ אַחַר עֵדִים; וְאִם לָאו – הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַשְּׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** **If** the one who protested his claim **said: This is a document of appeasement [*shtar passim*],** a document written only so that the holder should appear wealthy, **or a document of trust,** which means **that I sold** the field **to you** and provided you with the deed, trusting you to provide payment, **and** since **you did not give me** the **money** the sale is void, then **if there are witnesses, follow** the testimony of the **witnesses, and if not, follow the deed.**

The more pointed cases follow. If the person in possession says: this is a *shtar pasim* — a document of appeasement, written merely for show, perhaps to make the holder appear creditworthy to his creditors, with no genuine transfer ever having occurred — or if he says: this is a *shtar amana* — a trust document, meaning I gave you the deed even though you had not yet paid me for it — in both cases he is admitting the *shtar* is genuine while asserting a context that would void it: *she-macharta li ve-lo natata li damim* — you sold it to me but I never actually paid you. What then? *Im yesh edim, halach achar edim; ve-im lav, halach achar shtar*: if there are witnesses supporting his claim, follow the witnesses; if not, follow the *shtar*.

### Counter-Claim Rules

The operative principle is thus: a counter-claim — one that admits the *shtar*'s authenticity while arguing that the underlying transaction was never consummated — is not believed in the absence of witnesses, and the *shtar* prevails on its own. Critically, this applies even without any formal *kiyum*. The claimant is not believed by virtue of a *migo de-mezuyaf*, because the principle of *modeh be-shtar she-ketavo ein tzarich le-kaymo* means the *shtar* stands without needing to be validated once its authenticity is admitted. Any counter-claim other than outright *ziyuf* therefore fails, and the *shtar* remains operative.

## Debate Resolution

### Challenge to R.Y.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ; וְלָא רַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish concludes: According to your explanation, the Rabbis maintain that even if the deed is ratified the claimant cannot take possession of the property without bringing witnesses. If so, **shall we say** that **this** *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** that when there is a debtor **who admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **is not required to ratify it** in court in order to collect payment, **and** it is **not** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis?**

Resh Lakish presses Rabbi Yochanan: this ruling by Bar Kappara demonstrates that counter-claims of the *shtar pasim* or *shtar amana* type — which are structurally analogous to a *sh'chiv meira* claim, in that all three admit the *shtar* while asserting vitiating circumstances — are defeated by the *shtar* even without *kiyum*. The person in possession need not validate the *shtar*; once the other party admits it was written, the document stands. If so, why does Rabbi Yochanan hold that in the *sh'chiv meira* case the *sh'chiv meira* claim is believed and a witness-based proof is needed? The Bar Kappara *mishna* seems to place us squarely in the column of Rabbi Meir — that until an actual *ziyuf* claim is raised, the *shtar* is unimpeachable and no counter-claim of any kind is entertained, even against an unvalidated *shtar*. Why does Rabbi Yochanan not concede the same?

### R.Y. Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא; שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל – מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ, אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ. וְהָא מִיפְלָג פְּלִיגִי! דִּתְנַן: אֵין נֶאֱמָנִין לְפוֹסְלוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמָנִין!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: **No,** it is not so. **As I say** that **everyone agrees** that in the case of a debtor **who admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **is not required to ratify it** in court in order to collect payment. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish asked: **But don't they disagree, as we learned** in a *baraita*: With regard to witnesses who ratified their signatures but claimed that they were not fit to bear witness when they signed the deed, their testimony is **not deemed credible to invalidate** the document; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** Their testimony is **deemed credible.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי עֵדִים אַלִּימֵי וּמַרְעִי שְׁטָרָא, אִיהוּ כָּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ, וַהֲלֹא מִשִּׁמְךָ אָמְרוּ: יָפֶה עִרְעֲרוּ בְּנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זוֹ – אֶלְעָזָר אֲמָרָהּ; אֲנִי לֹא אָמַרְתִּי דָּבָר זֶה מֵעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: Even **if** the testimony of **witnesses is powerful and they impair** the validity of the **deed,** which they admit to have written, with regard to **him,** the giver, **is it in his** power to impair the validity of a deed that he admits to have written? Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: **But wasn't it stated in your name** with regard to the aforementioned incident in Bnei Brak: The **members of** his **family contested** the claim **correctly,** even though they admitted that the deed was authentic? This means that the claimant is required to ratify the deed. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: **That** statement **was stated** in my name by Rabbi **Elazar,** my disciple, but **I never said that statement.**

Rabbi Yochanan's response is that he maintains *divrei ha-kol modeh be-shtar she-ketavo ein tzarich le-kaymo* — that everyone, including the *chachamim*, agrees that a *shtar* whose authenticity is admitted does not require *kiyum*. The debate earlier between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* regarding *parati* and the witnesses who declared themselves *ketanim* is a case distinguished by its own exceptional character: the witnesses themselves actively undermined the *shtar* by declaring their ineligibility, which weakens it in a way that an ordinary unvalidated *shtar* is not weakened. In the ordinary case — the field, the *shtar pasim*, the *shtar amana* — everybody agrees that *modeh be-shtar she-ketavo ein tzarich le-kaymo*, and counter-claims fail without witnesses.

But the *sh'chiv meira* claim, Rabbi Yochanan maintains, is different. It creates a genuine *safek* that other counter-claims do not create. This is what allows the *chachamim* to say that even against a *shtar* whose authenticity is not disputed, the *sh'chiv meira* claim raises a real question. It is that specific characteristic of the *sh'chiv meira* claim — its capacity to generate authentic doubt — that locates the debate within Rabbi Yochanan's column of *ra'ayah be-edim* rather than reducing it to a general question of *kiyum*.

### Closing Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קָטָן, מֵאֵימָתַי מוֹכֵר בְּנִכְסֵי אָבִיו? רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בֶּן שְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה, וְרַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that there was a dispute with regard to the following matter: **From when,** i.e., from what age, **can a minor sell his** deceased **father's property? Rava** says that **Rav Naḥman says:** From the time he is **eighteen years old, and Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana** says that **Rav Naḥman says: From** the time he is **twenty years old.**

The *gemara*'s closing analysis makes this precise. The debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* may not be about *kiyum ha-shtar* at all. Rather, Rabbi Yochanan's position requires saying that the *sh'chiv meira* claim is uniquely capable of creating a credible *safek* against the *shtar* — unlike *shtar pasim*, unlike *shtar amana*, and unlike *parati* in an ordinary case. The *chachamim* hold that this particular claim, by its nature, generates sufficient doubt to trigger *hamotzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah*. Rabbi Meir holds it does not. The debate is thus, on Rabbi Yochanan's account, not about procedure — when *kiyum* is required and what it accomplishes — but about the substantive credibility of the *sh'chiv meira* claim as a category of counter-argument. Resh Lakish, consistently, says the debate is procedural: it is always about whether *kiyum ha-shtar* was performed, and once performed, no counter-claim of this type survives.
