# Yoma 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Identical Goats

### Daf Overview

We begin today's daf at the very bottom of 62b, picking up from the *tanu rabbanan* approximately five lines down. The preceding discussion has concerned the two goats of Yom Kippur and the drawing of the lots — specifically, the requirement that the two goats appear identical. We concluded with the point that this requirement of looking the same applies not only here but also by the two lambs of Shabbat and in several contexts involving the *metzora*. Nevertheless, the requirement is particularly underscored by the Yom Kippur goats, and in a heightened sense: it is not merely a general resemblance that is demanded, but identical height, identical cost, and purchase at the same time. Even where we find parallels elsewhere, the concern here is of an altogether more rigorous character.

### Same Height, Cost, Time

From this point, the *gemara* transitions to a different topic — one to which the case of the goats serves as an illuminating application — namely, the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*: the *issur* of slaughtering a *korban* outside the *beis hamikdash*.

---

## Shechutei Chutz

### Before/After Lottery

The *tanu rabbanan* addresses the following case: the two *se'irim* of Yom Kippur that were slaughtered outside the *beis hamikdash* — what is the *halacha*? The *baraisa* distinguishes between two stages. Before the *hagralah*, before the lots have been drawn, either goat could theoretically be designated for some purpose. Therefore, *ad shelo higril aleihem chayav al sheneihem* — if one slaughters either goat outside before the drawing of the lots, one is *chayav*. After the lots have been drawn, *chayav al shel Hashem u'patur al shel Azazel* — one is liable only for the goat designated for God but exempt for the one designated *l'azazel*, because the latter is no longer *ra'ui l'pesach ohel mo'ed*: it will not be brought as a *korban* inside the *beis hamikdash*, and therefore slaughtering it outside carries no liability.

To be precise about the scenario: we are not speaking of a *shechita* performed before the *hagralah* as part of the Yom Kippur service, but rather a case where someone seized one of the designated goats, took it outside the *beis hamikdash*, and slaughtered it there. The question is whether that act incurs *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz*.

### Mechusar Zman Principle

The *gemara* now asks: before the lots are drawn, for what purpose is each goat considered fit, such that slaughtering it outside generates *chiyuv*? *Le'mai chazi?* Rav Chisda answers: *ho'il v'ra'ui l'se'ir hana'aseh bachutz* — since the goat is fit to be used for the Yom Kippur *musaf* offering, which is brought in the *azarah* (outside the *heichal* but within the *beis hamikdash* complex). True, each goat is not yet fit for the interior *avoda* of the *kodesh kodashim* — the blood-sprinkling service — because the *hagralah* has not yet taken place. But the *musaf* is a separate *korban* that could employ this animal later in the day.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: just as we say the goat is not yet fit for the *avoda* of the *kodesh kodashim* because it is *mechusar hagralah* — lacking the drawing of lots — so too one should say it is not yet fit for the *musaf*, since it is *mechusar avodot hayom*, lacking all the prior *avodot* of the day that must precede the *musaf*.

### Action vs. Waiting

Rav Chisda's response, as the *gemara* develops it, establishes a fundamental distinction. The reason the goats are not yet fit for the blood-sprinkling inside the *kodesh kodashim* is not merely that time has not yet elapsed — it is because something must be *done to the animal itself*: the lots must be drawn. This is *mechusar ma'aseh*, lacking an action that must be performed directly upon the thing in question. An animal in this condition is considered genuinely unfit.

The *musaf*, by contrast, requires only waiting for external events to transpire. The *ketoret*, the ox-blood service, the goat-blood service — these are not things done to the *musaf* animal itself. They are separate acts that occur elsewhere. Once those external events have taken place, this animal will be fit. The principle Rav Chisda applies is *ein mechusar zman l'vo bayom*: something that will be fit later today is considered fit now, provided that the only obstacle is the passage of time and the completion of external events, rather than an action that must be performed on the animal itself.

*Ravina* draws out the implication of Rav Chisda's position: *hasha d'amar Rav Chisda mechusar hagralah k'mechusar ma'aseh dami* — the lack of the *hagralah* has the same legal character as *mechusar ma'aseh*. The lots must be drawn on the animal itself; without that act, the animal's specific identity for the interior service remains undefined. Its generic *kedusha* exists, but its role as the *se'ir* whose blood enters the *kodesh kodashim* has not yet been established.

### Shelamim/Heichal Doors

Ravina then applies this principle to a ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *shelamim sheshachtan kodem sheniftachu daltot haheichal psulin* — a *shelamim* slaughtered before the doors of the *heichal* have been opened in the morning is invalid, as the *pasuk* states *ushchato pesach ohel mo'ed*, indicating that the doors of the *ohel mo'ed* must be open at the time of *shechita*. If Rav Chisda's framework holds — that lacking an action performed on the animal constitutes genuine unfitness — then by parallel reasoning, if one slaughtered a *shelamim* outside the *beis hamikdash* before the doors of the *heichal* were opened, one would be *patur*, since the animal was not yet fit: *mechusar pticha k'mechusar ma'aseh dami*.

This analogy, however, is somewhat strained. The drawing of lots actively defines the nature and status of the Yom Kippur goat — it determines which animal becomes the *se'ir Lashem* and which becomes the *se'ir l'azazel*. The opening of the doors, by contrast, is an external circumstance that does not redefine the *shelamim* itself; it is more analogous to the *musaf* waiting for prior *avodot* to conclude. Rashi addresses this by pointing to the *pasuk's* specific language — *ushchato pesach ohel mo'ed* — which makes the reality of *pesach ohel mo'ed* definitional to the *shelamim* itself, distinguishing it from a purely external event. The argument is acknowledgeably a stretch, and intuitively one might have classified the doors' opening as the *musaf*-type of waiting rather than the *hagralah*-type of direct action on the animal.

## Pesach Shelo Lishmo

### Pesach Liability

The *gemara* now challenges whether Rav Chisda is truly committed to the principle of *ho'il* — the concept that theoretical fitness for an alternative purpose generates *chiyuv*. It does so by citing another ruling in Rav Chisda's own name. A *korban pesach* that one slaughtered outside the *beis hamikdash* during the rest of the year: if one did so *lishmo*, intending it as a *pesach*, one is *patur*, because a *pesach* brought outside of its proper time — not on the fourteenth of Nisan — is invalid inside the *beis hamikdash*, and therefore slaughtering it outside incurs no liability. But if one slaughtered it *shelo lishmo*, intending it as a *shelamim*, one is *chayav* — because with that identity, the animal could have been validly brought inside the *beis hamikdash*.

### Akira Outside

The *gemara* presses: if one is *patur* when slaughtering the *pesach lishmo* outside, why not apply *ho'il* and say one should be *chayav* even *lishmo*, since in theory one could have slaughtered it *shelo lishmo* and thereby rendered it a valid *shelamim*? After all, this is precisely the logic applied to the Yom Kippur goats: even though they are designated for the interior service or for *azazel*, their potential use for the *musaf* creates *chiyuv*.

The *gemara* answers: *hachi hashta? Hasam ba'ei akira, hacha lo ba'ei akira.* The cases are not comparable. By the Yom Kippur goats, before the lots are drawn, the animals have no specifically defined *kedusha* for the interior service — their *kedusha* remains generic. To say they could be used for the *musaf* does not require any change to their basic identity; it simply acknowledges what they already are. By the *korban pesach*, in contrast, its identity is fixed and specific. To make it a *shelamim* requires *akira* — an active change of designation. The *gemara's* position is that we do not hold someone *chayav* for *shechutei chutz* on the basis of a theoretical fitness that would require first changing the animal's established identity.

### Natural Status

This distinction carries a subtle but important point about the nature of *kedusha*. The Yom Kippur goats, prior to the *hagralah*, possess a *kedusha* that has not yet been differentiated into a specific role. There is no identity that needs to be uprooted in order to use them for the *musaf*. The *pesach*, by contrast, carries a defined identity that does need to be uprooted — and that *akira* must take place through a valid *korban* service, not through an act performed outside the *beis hamikdash*.

## Pesach Shelo Lishmo…

### Rav Dimi/Yochanan

The *gemara* now records a tradition brought by Rav Dimi from *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *pesach sheshachto bachutz bish'ar yemot hashanah bein lishmo bein shelo lishmo patur* — a *pesach* slaughtered outside the *beis hamikdash* during the rest of the year is *patur* whether one did so *lishmo* or *shelo lishmo*.

### Challenge to Patur

Rav Dimi recounts that he reported this ruling before Rabbi Yirmiyah himself, who then raised the obvious challenge. The *patur* for *lishmo* is clear: a *pesach* brought as a *pesach* outside its proper time is invalid and therefore carries no *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz*. But *shelo lishmo* — why should one be *patur* when slaughtering it as a *shelamim*? In that form, the animal could validly have been brought inside the *beis hamikdash*. The animal is fit *shelo lishmo bifnim*, and the act of slaughtering it outside with that intent should create *chiyuv*.

### Akira Rejected

Rabbi Yirmiyah's answer: *akiras chutz lav shemah akira* — an act of identity-change performed outside the *beis hamikdash* does not constitute a valid *akira*. The mechanism by which a *pesach* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* becomes a *shelamim* operates only when the *shechita* is performed as part of a legitimate *korban* service inside the *beis hamikdash*. An act of re-designation performed outside — even if the person declares *shelo lishmo* — leaves the animal's fundamental identity unchanged. It remains a *pesach*. And since it is a *pesach* being slaughtered outside the *beis hamikdash* during the rest of the year, the person is *patur*.

## Pesach Chayav

### Both Ways Chayav

The *gemara* then presents the contrasting report of Ravin, who also transmitted a teaching of Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — yet with a diametrically opposite ruling. Ravin reports: *pesach sheshachto bachutz bish'ar yemot hashanah bein lishmo bein shelo lishmo chayav* — one is *chayav* in both cases. The liability for *shelo lishmo* is straightforward: one has designated the animal as a *shelamim*, and with that identity it could have been validly brought inside. But Ravin's version has Rabbi Yochanan ruling *chayav* even *lishmo* — even when one slaughtered the animal explicitly as a *pesach*, despite the fact that in its current state it cannot be brought as such inside the *beis hamikdash*.

### Asham Analogy

The *gemara* supports this position by citing a *baraisa* and a ruling of Rabbi Chilkiya bar Tuvia. The *baraisa* addresses a *zav*, *zava*, *yoledes*, or *metzora* who brought their *chatat* or *asham* outside the *beis hamikdash* before their requisite period had elapsed. Since the *korban* is *mechusar zman* — not yet its proper time — and therefore cannot be validly brought inside, they are *patur*. However, their *olah* and *shelamim*, being generic *korbanot* not tied to a specific remedial purpose, could be brought at any time, and therefore slaughtering those outside incurs *chiyuv*.

Rabbi Chilkiya bar Tuvia then refines the ruling regarding the *asham*: *lo shanu ela lishmo aval shelo lishmo chayav.* It is only when one slaughters the *asham lishmo* — as an *asham*, which is not yet fit because the owner's period has not concluded — that one is *patur*. If one slaughtered it *shelo lishmo*, changing its designation to a different type of *korban* that could validly be brought, one is *chayav*. This maps precisely onto the *pesach* case: *lishmo* the *korban* is unfit; *shelo lishmo* it becomes fit — and therefore *chayav*. This would seem to confirm the position of Rav Chisda and argue against the ruling that one is *chayav* even *lishmo* by the *pesach*.

### Why Lishmo Chayav?

The pressing question then is: why, according to Ravin's version of Rabbi Yochanan's ruling, is one *chayav* even when slaughtering the *pesach lishmo*? The *asham* before its time is *patur lishmo* — why should the *pesach* be different?

One might suggest that even by the *asham lishmo*, one should be *chayav*, since in theory one could have changed its designation to *shelo lishmo* and thereby rendered it fit. But the *gemara* rejects this: *hasam ba'ei akira* — by the *asham*, to make it fit requires actively changing its identity. We do not hold someone *chayav* for *shechutei chutz* on the basis of what they could have done had they changed the animal's identity. The current identity is unfit; one is therefore *patur*. This is the identical logic applied to the *pesach* by Rav Dimi's version: *akiras chutz lav shemah akira*.

### Natural Shelamim

The resolution offered by Ravin's version is a fundamentally different conceptual claim: *hacha pesach bish'ar yemot hashanah shelamim ninhu* — a *pesach* during the rest of the year is, by its very natural status, a *shelamim*. No *akira* is required. Since it cannot be brought as a *pesach* outside of its proper time, it naturally defaults to the category of *shelamim*. Therefore, even when one slaughters it *lishmo* — insisting on treating it as a *pesach* — one is *chayav*, because the animal's actual standing, independent of one's intent, is already that of a *shelamim* fit for the *beis hamikdash*. One cannot escape *chiyuv* by declaring *lishmo* when the animal is naturally a *shelamim* in its present state.

This distinguishes the *pesach* from the *asham*. The *asham* does not naturally become a different type of *korban* when it is not yet the right time; its identity remains that of an *asham*, and one would have to actively change it. The *pesach*, by contrast, naturally inhabits the identity of a *shelamim* during the rest of the year, requiring no *akira* at all. According to this view, the act of declaring *lishmo* is simply an attempt to override a status that already exists — and since the animal is already a *shelamim* in reality, one is *chayav* regardless.

---

## Ashi vs. Yirmiyah

### Ashi Position

The *gemara* records that Rav Ashi teaches: *chayav* — *chayav* in both cases, *lishmo* and *shelo lishmo*. His reasoning follows the line of Ravin's transmission: the *pesach* during the rest of the year is naturally a *shelamim*, and thus always carries *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz* regardless of the declared intent.

### Yirmiyah Position

Rav Yirmiyah of Difti, by contrast, teaches: *patur* — *patur* in both cases. *Kasavar pesach bish'ar yemot hashanah ba'ei akira*: in his view, the *pesach* does not naturally become a *shelamim*; its identity remains that of a *pesach*, and an active change of designation is required to make it into a *shelamim*. Therefore, when one slaughters it *lishmo*, it is an unfit *pesach* and one is *patur*. When one slaughters it *shelo lishmo*, one has attempted to change its identity, but *akiras chutz lav shemah akira* — identity-change outside the *beis hamikdash* is legally ineffective — and so the animal remains a *pesach*, and again one is *patur*.

### Identity Dispute

The dispute between Rav Ashi and Rav Yirmiyah of Difti therefore turns on a single foundational question: what is the default identity of a *pesach* during the rest of the year? Rav Ashi holds that it naturally is a *shelamim* — no *akira* is required, and *chiyuv* follows in both cases. Rav Yirmiyah of Difti holds that it retains its identity as a *pesach*, that *akira* is necessary to make it a *shelamim*, and that *akira* performed outside the *beis hamikdash* is no *akira* at all — yielding *patur* in both cases. The position that distinguishes *lishmo* from *shelo lishmo*, producing *chiyuv* only in the latter, represents the intermediate view in which the *pesach* does require *akira* but that *akira* is valid even outside — a position rejected by Rav Yirmiyah of Difti via *akiras chutz lav shemah akira*.

## Korban/Pesach Definition

### Non-Korban Exclusion

The *gemara* now turns to the scriptural basis of *shechutei chutz*. The *tanu rabbanan* cites the *pasuk* from *ish ish mi-beis Yisrael asher yishchat shor o kesev o ez ba-machane o asher yishchat mi-chutz la-machane v'el pesach ohel mo'ed lo hevi'o l'hakriv korban l'Hashem* — one who slaughters an animal without bringing it to the entrance of the *ohel mo'ed* as a *korban l'Hashem* is *chayav kareis*.

A preliminary question arises: does *korban* in this *pasuk* include *kodshei bedek habayis* — animals consecrated for their monetary value, to fund the upkeep of the *beis hamikdash*? Such animals are called *korban* in another context, as when donations were brought and described with that term. One might therefore think that slaughtering a consecrated animal, even one designated for the *bedek habayis* fund, outside the *beis hamikdash* creates *chiyuv*.

### Pesach Ohel Mo'ed Test

The *gemara* rejects this inclusion on the basis of the continuation of the *pasuk*: *v'el pesach ohel mo'ed lo hevi'o* — the animal must be one that is fit to be brought to the entrance of the *ohel mo'ed* as a *korban*. The interpretive rule established is: *kol hara'uy l'pesach ohel mo'ed chayav alav ba-chutz, kol she'eino ra'uy l'pesach ohel mo'ed ein chayavin alav ba-chutz*. Only an animal that is fit to be brought as a *korban* to the *ohel mo'ed* — that has significance as part of the sacrificial service there — generates *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz*. Animals consecrated merely for their monetary value have no role as *korbanot* within the *ohel mo'ed* and therefore fall outside the prohibition.

### Sa'ir Excluded?

Having established this standard of *ra'ui l'pesach ohel mo'ed*, the *gemara* raises the question of the *sa'ir hamishtalei'ach*. This goat is brought to the *ohel mo'ed* — the drawing of lots takes place there — and it is an integral part of the Yom Kippur service. One might therefore argue that slaughtering it outside the *beis hamikdash* should incur *chiyuv*, since it is indeed *ra'ui lavo pesach ohel mo'ed*.

### L'Hashem Exclusion

The *gemara* addresses this through the phrase *l'hakriv korban l'Hashem* — to bring a *korban* to God. Only a *korban* that is entirely for God — *mi shemeyuchadim l'Hashem* — creates *chiyuv* when slaughtered outside. The *sa'ir hamishtalei'ach*, being designated *l'azazel* rather than *l'Hashem*, falls outside this category. Once the lot has designated it as *l'azazel*, it is no longer going to be brought as a *korban* inside the *beis hamikdash*, and therefore slaughtering it outside incurs no *chiyuv*. The *se'ir lachatas*, too, is addressed here: its ritual takes place outside the *beis hamikdash*, and it is therefore not a *korban* that generates *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz* in the conventional sense.

---

## L'Hashem Scope

### Ramba Context

A striking tension now emerges. In the passage just discussed, *l'Hashem* served to *exclude* the *sa'ir l'azazel* from the *chiyuv* of *shechutei chutz*. But elsewhere the same term functions to *include* the *sa'ir l'azazel*. Rava resolves this by stating *hasam me'inyana dikra v'hacha me'inyana dikra*: in each context, the word *l'Hashem* must be read in light of what the surrounding *pasuk* is already speaking about, and its function — inclusive or exclusive — is determined by the default reading in that context.

### Ohel Mo'ed Exclusion

In the context of *shechutei chutz* and the *ohel mo'ed* passage, the *pasuk*'s language already naturally extends to include the *sa'ir* — it is brought to the *ohel mo'ed* and plays a central role in the Yom Kippur service. Since inclusion is the default, *l'Hashem* comes to redirect and *exclude*: it tells us that only what is entirely for God creates *chiyuv*, thereby removing the *sa'ir l'azazel* from the scope of the prohibition.

### Eight-Day Inclusion

In the context of the eight-day requirement — *yeratzeh l'korban isheh l'Hashem miyom hashmini vahal'ah* — the *pasuk* speaks of animals burnt on the altar, which would naturally *exclude* the *sa'ir hamishtalei'ach*, since it is not brought as an *isheh* offering on the altar. Since exclusion is the default there, *l'Hashem* comes to *include*: even the *sa'ir l'azazel* cannot be sanctified or used if it is less than eight days old.

---

## Mechusar Zman

### Lottery Principle

The *gemara* now explores why a separate scriptural derivation is needed to establish that the *sa'ir l'azazel* must be at least eight days old. The principle *ein hagoral kovei'a ela b'ra'uy l'shem* — the lot has legal force only when each goat is theoretically eligible to be designated for God — would seem to demand this automatically. For the lottery to be valid, each goat must be fit to serve as the *se'ir Lashem*; since a goat less than eight days old cannot serve *l'Hashem*, neither could it be eligible for the lot, and therefore it could not be used as a substitute *l'azazel* either.

### Substitute Goat

Rav Yosef answers that the derivation is needed for the position of Chanana ha-Mitzri, who teaches: *afilu dam bekos meivi chaveiro u'mezaveig lo* — even if the blood of the *se'ir Lashem* is already in the cup, ready for the service, and the *se'ir l'azazel* dies, one may bring a replacement goat and pair it with the blood of the first, without performing a new lottery. According to this view, in the scenario of a replacement goat, no lottery takes place — the animal is simply designated directly as the *se'ir l'azazel*. In such a case, there is no requirement that it be theoretically eligible to be the *se'ir Lashem*, and one might have thought an animal less than eight days old could serve. The *pasuk* is therefore necessary to tell us otherwise.

The *gemara* initially questions whether Chanana ha-Mitzri truly dispenses with the lottery, noting that one could read his position as requiring a new lottery with two goats. But Rav Yosef then identifies the ruling more precisely with the position of Rabbi Shimon, who states explicitly: *met echad meihem meivi chaveiro shelo b'hagralah divrei Rabbi Shimon* — if one of the two goats dies, one brings a replacement without a new lottery. Under Rabbi Shimon's view, then, the *sa'ir l'azazel* can be designated independently of the lottery mechanism, and the scriptural derivation for the eight-day requirement is necessary to preclude using an animal less than eight days old in such a case.

### Kedusha Transfer

Ravina offers a further scenario where the *sa'ir l'azazel* can be identified without a new lottery: *k'gon shehumam v'hifrish acher* — if the designated *sa'ir l'azazel* acquired a blemish, rendering it unfit, one brings a replacement and transfers the *kedusha* from the blemished animal to the new one. This is essentially a substitution through *kedusha*-transfer rather than through lottery, and the replacement animal's identity as *l'azazel* is established directly. In this scenario too, one might have thought to use an animal less than eight days old, and the scriptural exclusion is needed.

### Both Needed

The *gemara* then explains why both prohibitions — the eight-day minimum and the exclusion of a *ba'al mum* — require independent scriptural derivation and cannot be inferred from one another. Had the Torah written only the prohibition of *mechusar zman*, one might have thought that a *ba'al mum* poses no problem, since the animal is of proper age and its physical defect alone might not have been considered disqualifying for something designated *l'azazel* rather than *l'Hashem*. Had the Torah written only the exclusion of a *ba'al mum*, one might have thought that *mechusar zman* poses no problem for the *sa'ir l'azazel*, since the animal's immaturity is not a physical deformity and might not have been disqualifying when the destination is not the altar. *Tzricha* — both derivations are independently necessary.

### Sa'ir Status

This concludes with a conceptually rich observation. The *sa'ir l'azazel* is designated not *l'Hashem* but *l'azazel*. One might have thought its ritual function so different from that of a standard *korban* that requirements governing *korbanot* — no blemish, minimum age — simply would not apply. Yet the *gemara* demonstrates from two independent *psukim* that both requirements hold. The *sa'ir l'azazel*, despite its destination, retains a genuine status as a *korban*, and the demands placed upon it by these *psukim* underscore the degree to which it continues to inhabit the conceptual world of *korbanos*. The question of precisely how far that status extends — what it means for the *sa'ir l'azazel* to be, in some sense, *l'Hashem* even as it goes *l'azazel* — opens onto deeper questions about the nature of this remarkable *korban* that the *gemara* here begins, but does not conclude, to explore.
