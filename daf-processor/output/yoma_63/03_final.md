# Yoma 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Identical Goats

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁנֵי שְׂעִירֵי יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן בַּחוּץ, עַד שֶׁלֹּא הִגְרִיל עֲלֵיהֶן — חַיָּיב עַל שְׁנֵיהֶם. מִשֶּׁהִגְרִיל עֲלֵיהֶן — חַיָּיב עַל שֶׁל שֵׁם, וּפָטוּר עַל שֶׁל עֲזָאזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: If the High Priest **slaughtered the two Yom Kippur goats outside** the Temple courtyard **when he had not yet drawn lots** to determine which of **them** is to be sacrificed to God and which is to be sent to Azazel, **he is liable** to receive the punishment of *karet* **for both of them,** as they are both fit for use as an offering. If he slaughtered them **after he drew lots** for **them, he is liable** for slaughtering the goat designated **for God,** which is a sacrificial offering, **and he is exempt for** slaughtering the goat designated **for Azazel,** as it is not a sacrificial offering.

We begin today's daf at the very bottom of 62b, picking up from the *tanu rabbanan* approximately five lines down. The preceding discussion has concerned the two goats of Yom Kippur and the drawing of the lots — specifically, the requirement that the two goats appear identical. We concluded with the point that this requirement of looking the same applies not only here but also by the two lambs of Shabbat and in several contexts involving the *metzora*. Nevertheless, the requirement is particularly underscored by the Yom Kippur goats, and in a heightened sense: it is not merely a general resemblance that is demanded, but identical height, identical cost, and purchase at the same time. Even where we find parallels elsewhere, the concern here is of an altogether more rigorous character.

### Same Height, Cost, Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁלֹּא הִגְרִיל עֲלֵיהֶם חַיָּיב עַל שְׁנֵיהֶם. לְמַאי חֲזוּ? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְשָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the meaning of this *baraita*. With regard to the statement that if he slaughtered the goats **when he had not yet drawn lots, he is liable for both of them,** the Gemara asks: **For what are they fit?** Before the lots are drawn, neither goat is fit for use as an offering. The Gemara answers that **Rav Ḥisda said:** He is liable **since they are fit** to be used **as the goat** whose sacrificial rites are **performed outside.** These two goats are fit for use as the additional offerings of Yom Kippur, whose sacrificial rites are performed outside of the Sanctuary, in the Temple courtyard. Therefore, the goats have the status of sacrificial offerings.

From this point, the *gemara* transitions to a different topic — one to which the case of the goats serves as an illuminating application — namely, the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*: the *issur* of slaughtering a *korban* outside the *beis hamikdash*.

## Shechutei Chutz

### Before/After Lottery

The *tanu rabbanan* addresses the following case: the two *se'irim* of Yom Kippur that were slaughtered outside the *beis hamikdash* — what is the *halacha*? The *baraisa* distinguishes between two stages. Before the *hagralah*, before the lots have been drawn, either goat could theoretically be designated for some purpose. Therefore, *ad shelo higril aleihem chayav al sheneihem* — if one slaughters either goat outside before the drawing of the lots, one is *chayav*. After the lots have been drawn, *chayav al shel Hashem u'patur al shel Azazel* — one is liable only for the goat designated for God but exempt for the one designated *l'azazel*, because the latter is no longer *ra'ui l'pesach ohel mo'ed*: it will not be brought as a *korban* inside the *beis hamikdash*, and therefore slaughtering it outside carries no liability.

To be precise about the scenario: we are not speaking of a *shechita* performed before the *hagralah* as part of the Yom Kippur service, but rather a case where someone seized one of the designated goats, took it outside the *beis hamikdash*, and slaughtered it there. The question is whether that act incurs *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz*.

### Mechusar Zman Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא לְשָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים דְּלָא, דִּמְחוּסָּר הַגְרָלָה? לְשָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּחוּץ נָמוּ לָא חֲזֵי, דִּמְחַסְּרִי עֲבוֹדַת הַיּוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **the goat** whose sacrificial rites are **performed inside** the Sanctuary, i.e., the goat selected to be sacrificed as a sin-offering, for which the goats are **not** valid because **they lack the lottery? They are also unfit** to be used **as the goat** whose rites are **performed outside** the Sanctuary, **as they lack the service of the day.** The entire Yom Kippur service must be performed in the proper sequence, and several steps have to be executed before the additional offerings are sacrificed, including the sprinkling of the bull's and goat's blood, and the burning of the handful of incense. Since the additional offerings cannot yet be sacrificed, the goats cannot be considered fit for these offerings, and one should not be liable to receive *karet* for slaughtering the goats outside the Temple courtyard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֵין מְחוּסַּר זְמַן לְבוֹ בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rav Ḥisda holds** that an offering is **not** considered to be **lacking time** if it is to be brought on **that day.** An offering is considered lacking time, and therefore unfit, if it is supposed to be brought on a different day. However, if it is the proper day but the right moment has not yet arrived, the offering is considered valid. On the other hand, before the High Priest draws lots to determine which of the goats will be sacrificed to God and which sent to Azazel, even though it is the proper day, the goats are not yet considered valid offerings because there is a necessary action that the High Priest must take with regard to the goat itself before it can be brought as an offering.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: just as we say the goat is not yet fit for the *avoda* of the *kodesh kodashim* because it is *mechusar hagralah* — lacking the drawing of lots — so too one should say it is not yet fit for the *musaf*, since it is *mechusar avodot hayom*, lacking all the prior *avodot* of the day that must precede the *musaf*.

### Action vs. Waiting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא מְחוּסָּר הַגְרָלָה כִּמְחוּסָּר מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁלָמִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּפְתְּחוּ דַּלְתוֹת הַהֵיכָל פְּסוּלִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וּשְׁחָטוֹ פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד", בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא פָּתוּחַ, וְלֹא בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא נָעוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Now that Rav Ḥisda said** that although the drawing of lots is external to the animals themselves, an animal **lacking the lottery is comparable to** an animal **lacking an action** and is not yet considered a valid offering, a statement can be made with regard to **that which Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Peace-offerings that one slaughtered** in the Temple **before the doors of the Sanctuary were opened are disqualified,** as **it is stated** with regard to peace-offerings: **"And he shall slaughter it at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:2), which teaches that it must be slaughtered **when** the entrance **is open** and serves as an actual entrance, **but not when it is locked.** As long as the entrance to the Tent of Meeting, or in the Temple the doors to the Sanctuary, remain closed, one may not sacrifice the peace-offerings, and if they are sacrificed, they are disqualified.

Rav Chisda's response, as the *gemara* develops it, establishes a fundamental distinction. The reason the goats are not yet fit for the blood-sprinkling inside the *kodesh kodashim* is not merely that time has not yet elapsed — it is because something must be *done to the animal itself*: the lots must be drawn. This is *mechusar ma'aseh*, lacking an action that must be performed directly upon the thing in question. An animal in this condition is considered genuinely unfit.

The *musaf*, by contrast, requires only waiting for external events to transpire. The *ketoret*, the ox-blood service, the goat-blood service — these are not things done to the *musaf* animal itself. They are separate acts that occur elsewhere. Once those external events have taken place, this animal will be fit. The principle Rav Chisda applies is *ein mechusar zman l'vo bayom*: something that will be fit later today is considered fit now, provided that the only obstacle is the passage of time and the completion of external events, rather than an action that must be performed on the animal itself.

*Ravina* draws out the implication of Rav Chisda's position: *hasha d'amar Rav Chisda mechusar hagralah k'mechusar ma'aseh dami* — the lack of the *hagralah* has the same legal character as *mechusar ma'aseh*. The lots must be drawn on the animal itself; without that act, the animal's specific identity for the interior service remains undefined. Its generic *kedusha* exists, but its role as the *se'ir* whose blood enters the *kodesh kodashim* has not yet been established.

### Shelamim/Heichal Doors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחָטָן בַּחוּץ קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּפְתְּחוּ דַּלְתוֹת הַהֵיכָל — פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא: מְחוּסָּר פְּתִיחָה כִּמְחוּסָּר מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Based on Rav Ḥisda's statement, if **he slaughtered them outside** the Temple **before the doors of the Sanctuary were opened he is exempt. What is the reason? Lacking** the **opening** of the doors **is comparable to lacking an action.** Therefore, the offering was not yet fit to be sacrificed in the Temple, and when he slaughtered it outside, despite doing so with the intention that it is a sacrificial offering, he is not considered to have violated the prohibition against sacrificing an offering outside the Temple.

Ravina then applies this principle to a ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *shelamim sheshachtan kodem sheniftachu daltot haheichal psulin* — a *shelamim* slaughtered before the doors of the *heichal* have been opened in the morning is invalid, as the *pasuk* states *ushchato pesach ohel mo'ed*, indicating that the doors of the *ohel mo'ed* must be open at the time of *shechita*. If Rav Chisda's framework holds — that lacking an action performed on the animal constitutes genuine unfitness — then by parallel reasoning, if one slaughtered a *shelamim* outside the *beis hamikdash* before the doors of the *heichal* were opened, one would be *patur*, since the animal was not yet fit: *mechusar pticha k'mechusar ma'aseh dami*.

This analogy, however, is somewhat strained. The drawing of lots actively defines the nature and status of the Yom Kippur goat — it determines which animal becomes the *se'ir Lashem* and which becomes the *se'ir l'azazel*. The opening of the doors, by contrast, is an external circumstance that does not redefine the *shelamim* itself; it is more analogous to the *musaf* waiting for prior *avodot* to conclude. Rashi addresses this by pointing to the *pasuk's* specific language — *ushchato pesach ohel mo'ed* — which makes the reality of *pesach ohel mo'ed* definitional to the *shelamim* itself, distinguishing it from a purely external event. The argument is acknowledgeably a stretch, and intuitively one might have classified the doors' opening as the *musaf*-type of waiting rather than the *hagralah*-type of direct action on the animal.

## Pesach Shelo Lishmo

### Pesach Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַב חִסְדָּא "הוֹאִיל"?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda stated above that since the Yom Kippur goats could be used for the additional offerings, one is liable for slaughtering them outside the Temple. The Gemara asks: **Does Rav Ḥisda accept** the principle: **Since** a particular situation could come to be in the future, it is viewed as though it existed already in the present?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: פֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ בַּחוּץ בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, לִשְׁמוֹ — פָּטוּר, שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And didn't Rav Ḥisda say:** If one **slaughtered a Paschal lamb outside** the Temple **during the rest of the days of the year** other than Passover eve, if he slaughtered it **for its own sake,** i.e., as a Paschal lamb, **he is exempt,** since at that time it is not fit to be sacrificed as a Paschal lamb. If he sacrificed it **not for its own sake** but rather as a peace-offering, **he is liable.** A Paschal lamb that is slaughtered as a peace-offering on any day of the year other than Passover eve is considered a valid peace-offering. Therefore, it has the status of an offering that is fit to be sacrificed in the Temple, and he is liable for slaughtering it outside the Temple.

The *gemara* now challenges whether Rav Chisda is truly committed to the principle of *ho'il* — the concept that theoretical fitness for an alternative purpose generates *chiyuv*. It does so by citing another ruling in Rav Chisda's own name. A *korban pesach* that one slaughtered outside the *beis hamikdash* during the rest of the year: if one did so *lishmo*, intending it as a *pesach*, one is *patur*, because a *pesach* brought outside of its proper time — not on the fourteenth of Nisan — is invalid inside the *beis hamikdash*, and therefore slaughtering it outside incurs no liability. But if one slaughtered it *shelo lishmo*, intending it as a *shelamim*, one is *chayav* — because with that identity, the animal could have been validly brought inside the *beis hamikdash*.

### Akira Outside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, הָא סְתָמָא, לִשְׁמוֹ הוּא וּפָטוּר. וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ בִּפְנִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The reason** he is liable is because he specifically slaughtered it **not for its own sake. However,** if he slaughtered it **without specifying** what offering he had in mind, **it is** considered to have been slaughtered **for its own sake and he is exempt. But why** is he exempt? **Let us say: Since it is fit** to be sacrificed **within** the Temple **not for its own sake,** i.e., as a peace-offering, it should be considered as though he slaughtered it as a peace-offering outside the Temple, and he should be liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה, הַאי לָא בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** with regard to the Paschal lamb, it **requires** explicit **uprooting** from the status of a Paschal lamb in order to be considered a peace-offering. Therefore, if he did not specify his intent, it is still considered a Paschal lamb, which is not fit to be sacrificed. **These** Yom Kippur goats **do not require uprooting** from their previous status. The Yom Kippur goats have the status of a sin-offering and the goats sacrificed as part of the additional offerings of the day are also considered sin-offerings. Therefore, one who slaughters them outside the Temple is liable, as they are fit to be sacrificed at that time as sin-offerings, even without the principle: Since, etc.

The *gemara* presses: if one is *patur* when slaughtering the *pesach lishmo* outside, why not apply *ho'il* and say one should be *chayav* even *lishmo*, since in theory one could have slaughtered it *shelo lishmo* and thereby rendered it a valid *shelamim*? After all, this is precisely the logic applied to the Yom Kippur goats: even though they are designated for the interior service or for *azazel*, their potential use for the *musaf* creates *chiyuv*.

The *gemara* answers: *hachi hashta? Hasam ba'ei akira, hacha lo ba'ei akira.* The cases are not comparable. By the Yom Kippur goats, before the lots are drawn, the animals have no specifically defined *kedusha* for the interior service — their *kedusha* remains generic. To say they could be used for the *musaf* does not require any change to their basic identity; it simply acknowledges what they already are. By the *korban pesach*, in contrast, its identity is fixed and specific. To make it a *shelamim* requires *akira* — an active change of designation. The *gemara's* position is that we do not hold someone *chayav* for *shechutei chutz* on the basis of a theoretical fitness that would require first changing the animal's established identity.

### Natural Status

This distinction carries a subtle but important point about the nature of *kedusha*. The Yom Kippur goats, prior to the *hagralah*, possess a *kedusha* that has not yet been differentiated into a specific role. There is no identity that needs to be uprooted in order to use them for the *musaf*. The *pesach*, by contrast, carries a defined identity that does need to be uprooted — and that *akira* must take place through a valid *korban* service, not through an act performed outside the *beis hamikdash*.

## Pesach Shelo Lishmo (II)

### Rav Dimi/Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ בַּחוּץ בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, בֵּין לִשְׁמוֹ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that **Rabbi Yirmeya said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** said: If one **slaughtered a Paschal lamb outside** the Temple **during the rest of the days of the year, whether** he slaughtered it **for its own sake,** as a Paschal lamb, **or not for its own sake,** as a peace-offering, **he is exempt.**

The *gemara* now records a tradition brought by Rav Dimi from *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *pesach sheshachto bachutz bish'ar yemot hashanah bein lishmo bein shelo lishmo patur* — a *pesach* slaughtered outside the *beis hamikdash* during the rest of the year is *patur* whether one did so *lishmo* or *shelo lishmo*.

### Challenge to Patur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב דִּימִי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בִּשְׁלָמָא לִשְׁמוֹ, דְּהָא לָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, אַמַּאי? הָא חֲזֵי שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ בִּפְנִים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi said: I said this *halakha* before Rabbi Yirmeya** and asked him: **Granted,** when he slaughters it outside the Temple **for its own sake** he is exempt, **because it is not fit** to be brought as a Paschal lamb at that time, and one is liable for slaughtering an offering outside the Temple only if it was fit to be sacrificed in the Temple. **However,** if he slaughters it **not for its own sake, why** is he exempt? **Isn't it fit** to be sacrificed **not for its own sake** as a peace-offering **inside** the Temple?

Rav Dimi recounts that he reported this ruling before Rabbi Yirmiyah himself, who then raised the obvious challenge. The *patur* for *lishmo* is clear: a *pesach* brought as a *pesach* outside its proper time is invalid and therefore carries no *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz*. But *shelo lishmo* — why should one be *patur* when slaughtering it as a *shelamim*? In that form, the animal could validly have been brought inside the *beis hamikdash*. The animal is fit *shelo lishmo bifnim*, and the act of slaughtering it outside with that intent should create *chiyuv*.

### Akira Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לִי: עֲקִירַת חוּץ לָאו שְׁמַהּ עֲקִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Yirmeya **said to me** in response: **Uprooting** an offering's designation **outside** the Temple **is not called uprooting.** Since he slaughtered it outside, the status of the offering does not change from that of a Paschal lamb to that of a peace-offering, despite his intention to this effect.

Rabbi Yirmiyah's answer: *akiras chutz lav shemah akira* — an act of identity-change performed outside the *beis hamikdash* does not constitute a valid *akira*. The mechanism by which a *pesach* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* becomes a *shelamim* operates only when the *shechita* is performed as part of a legitimate *korban* service inside the *beis hamikdash*. An act of re-designation performed outside — even if the person declares *shelo lishmo* — leaves the animal's fundamental identity unchanged. It remains a *pesach*. And since it is a *pesach* being slaughtered outside the *beis hamikdash* during the rest of the year, the person is *patur*.

## Pesach Chayav

### Both Ways Chayav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ בַּחוּץ בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, בֵּין לִשְׁמוֹ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** a different version of the statement that **Rabbi Yirmeya said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** said: If one **slaughtered a Paschal lamb outside** the Temple **during the rest of the days of the year, whether** he slaughtered it **for its own sake or not for its own sake, he is liable.**

The *gemara* then presents the contrasting report of Ravin, who also transmitted a teaching of Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — yet with a diametrically opposite ruling. Ravin reports: *pesach sheshachto bachutz bish'ar yemot hashanah bein lishmo bein shelo lishmo chayav* — one is *chayav* in both cases. The liability for *shelo lishmo* is straightforward: one has designated the animal as a *shelamim*, and with that identity it could have been validly brought inside. But Ravin's version has Rabbi Yochanan ruling *chayav* even *lishmo* — even when one slaughtered the animal explicitly as a *pesach*, despite the fact that in its current state it cannot be brought as such inside the *beis hamikdash*.

### Asham Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ לִשְׁמוֹ? וְהָתְנַן: מְחוּסַּר זְמַן — בֵּין בְּגוּפוֹ, בֵּין בִּבְעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: Is he liable **even** if he slaughtered it **for its own sake? Didn't we learn** in a mishna in tractate *Zevaḥim* that one is exempt for sacrificing an offering that was not yet fit for sacrifice because it was **lacking time?** This is so **whether** it **itself** was lacking time, such as if it was less than eight days old, **or** it was lacking time **due to** its **owners.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהִקְרִיבוּ חַטָּאתָם וַאֲשָׁמָם בַּחוּץ — פְּטוּרִין. עוֹלוֹתֵיהֶן וְשַׁלְמֵיהֶן בַּחוּץ — חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** Any of these people **who sacrificed their sin-offering and,** in the case of lepers, **their guilt-offering outside** the Temple **are exempt.** Since they would not fulfill their obligations to bring these offerings if they would sacrifice them in the Temple, and the offerings are unfit to be brought as voluntary offerings, because sin-offerings and guilt-offerings are not brought as voluntary offerings, one who slaughters them outside the Temple is not liable. However, if they sacrifice **their burnt-offerings and peace-offerings outside** the Temple, **they are liable.** This is because these offerings could be valid as voluntary offerings if they were sacrificed in the Temple.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר רַב חִלְקִיָּה בַּר טוֹבִי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוֹ, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥilkiya bar Tovi said: They only taught** that one is exempt for the guilt-offering when it is slaughtered **for its own sake, but** if it is slaughtered **not for its own sake, he is liable,** because a guilt-offering slaughtered in the Temple not for its own sake is valid.

The *gemara* supports this position by citing a *baraisa* and a ruling of Rabbi Chilkiya bar Tuvia. The *baraisa* addresses a *zav*, *zava*, *yoledes*, or *metzora* who brought their *chatat* or *asham* outside the *beis hamikdash* before their requisite period had elapsed. Since the *korban* is *mechusar zman* — not yet its proper time — and therefore cannot be validly brought inside, they are *patur*. However, their *olah* and *shelamim*, being generic *korbanot* not tied to a specific remedial purpose, could be brought at any time, and therefore slaughtering those outside incurs *chiyuv*.

Rabbi Chilkiya bar Tuvia then refines the ruling regarding the *asham*: *lo shanu ela lishmo aval shelo lishmo chayav.* It is only when one slaughters the *asham lishmo* — as an *asham*, which is not yet fit because the owner's period has not concluded — that one is *patur*. If one slaughtered it *shelo lishmo*, changing its designation to a different type of *korban* that could validly be brought, one is *chayav*. This maps precisely onto the *pesach* case: *lishmo* the *korban* is unfit; *shelo lishmo* it becomes fit — and therefore *chayav*. This would seem to confirm the position of Rav Chisda and argue against the ruling that one is *chayav* even *lishmo* by the *pesach*.

### Why Lishmo Chayav?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִשְׁמוֹ מִיהָא פָּטוּר, אַמַּאי? נֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וּרְאוּיִן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ בִּפְנִים! הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה, הָכָא פֶּסַח בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה — שְׁלָמִים נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers this: If he slaughtered it **for its own sake, in any event he is exempt. Why? Let us say: Since they are fit** to be sacrificed **inside** the Temple **not for their own sake,** he should be liable for sacrificing them outside. The Gemara responds: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** the guilt-offering **requires** explicit **uprooting** of its designation in order to be valid. **Here, a Paschal lamb during the rest of the days of the year is** considered **a peace-offering,** even if its status is not explicitly uprooted.

The pressing question then is: why, according to Ravin's version of Rabbi Yochanan's ruling, is one *chayav* even when slaughtering the *pesach lishmo*? The *asham* before its time is *patur lishmo* — why should the *pesach* be different?

One might suggest that even by the *asham lishmo*, one should be *chayav*, since in theory one could have changed its designation to *shelo lishmo* and thereby rendered it fit. But the *gemara* rejects this: *hasam ba'ei akira* — by the *asham*, to make it fit requires actively changing its identity. We do not hold someone *chayav* for *shechutei chutz* on the basis of what they could have done had they changed the animal's identity. The current identity is unfit; one is therefore *patur*. This is the identical logic applied to the *pesach* by Rav Dimi's version: *akiras chutz lav shemah akira*.

### Natural Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי מַתְנֵי: חַיָּיב, כִּדְאָמְרִינַן. רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּפְתִּי מַתְנֵי: פָּטוּר. קָסָבַר: פֶּסַח בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה בָּעֵי עֲקִירָה, וַעֲקִירַת חוּץ לָאו שְׁמָהּ עֲקִירָה. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב חִלְקִיָּה בַּר טוֹבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the case of a Paschal lamb that was slaughtered outside the Temple during the rest of the year, and not for its own sake. **Rav Ashi taught: He is liable, as we stated. Rav Yirmeya of Difti taught: He is exempt.** Rav Yirmeya **held** that **a Paschal lamb during the rest of the days of the year requires uprooting** of its previous designation, **and uprooting** an offering's designation **outside** the Temple **is not called uprooting. And this** *halakha* **disagrees with** the statement **of Rav Ḥilkiya bar Tovi,** who held that uprooting an offering's designation outside the Temple is considered an effective uprooting of that designation.

The resolution offered by Ravin's version is a fundamentally different conceptual claim: *hacha pesach bish'ar yemot hashanah shelamim ninhu* — a *pesach* during the rest of the year is, by its very natural status, a *shelamim*. No *akira* is required. Since it cannot be brought as a *pesach* outside of its proper time, it naturally defaults to the category of *shelamim*. Therefore, even when one slaughters it *lishmo* — insisting on treating it as a *pesach* — one is *chayav*, because the animal's actual standing, independent of one's intent, is already that of a *shelamim* fit for the *beis hamikdash*. One cannot escape *chiyuv* by declaring *lishmo* when the animal is naturally a *shelamim* in its present state.

This distinguishes the *pesach* from the *asham*. The *asham* does not naturally become a different type of *korban* when it is not yet the right time; its identity remains that of an *asham*, and one would have to actively change it. The *pesach*, by contrast, naturally inhabits the identity of a *shelamim* during the rest of the year, requiring no *akira* at all. According to this view, the act of declaring *lishmo* is simply an attempt to override a status that already exists — and since the animal is already a *shelamim* in reality, one is *chayav* regardless.

## Ashi vs. Yirmiyah

### Ashi Position

The *gemara* records that Rav Ashi teaches: *chayav* — *chayav* in both cases, *lishmo* and *shelo lishmo*. His reasoning follows the line of Ravin's transmission: the *pesach* during the rest of the year is naturally a *shelamim*, and thus always carries *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz* regardless of the declared intent.

### Yirmiyah Position

Rav Yirmiyah of Difti, by contrast, teaches: *patur* — *patur* in both cases. *Kasavar pesach bish'ar yemot hashanah ba'ei akira*: in his view, the *pesach* does not naturally become a *shelamim*; its identity remains that of a *pesach*, and an active change of designation is required to make it into a *shelamim*. Therefore, when one slaughters it *lishmo*, it is an unfit *pesach* and one is *patur*. When one slaughters it *shelo lishmo*, one has attempted to change its identity, but *akiras chutz lav shemah akira* — identity-change outside the *beis hamikdash* is legally ineffective — and so the animal remains a *pesach*, and again one is *patur*.

### Identity Dispute

The dispute between Rav Ashi and Rav Yirmiyah of Difti therefore turns on a single foundational question: what is the default identity of a *pesach* during the rest of the year? Rav Ashi holds that it naturally is a *shelamim* — no *akira* is required, and *chiyuv* follows in both cases. Rav Yirmiyah of Difti holds that it retains its identity as a *pesach*, that *akira* is necessary to make it a *shelamim*, and that *akira* performed outside the *beis hamikdash* is no *akira* at all — yielding *patur* in both cases. The position that distinguishes *lishmo* from *shelo lishmo*, producing *chiyuv* only in the latter, represents the intermediate view in which the *pesach* does require *akira* but that *akira* is valid even outside — a position rejected by Rav Yirmiyah of Difti via *akiras chutz lav shemah akira*.

## Korban/Pesach Definition

### Non-Korban Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "אִישׁ אִישׁ מִבֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁחַט שׁוֹר אוֹ כֶשֶׂב אוֹ עֵז בַּמַּחֲנֶה אוֹ אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁחַט מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה. וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֹא הֱבִיאוֹ לְהַקְרִיב קׇרְבָּן לַה׳".
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* based upon the verses: **"Any man of the house of Israel who shall slaughter a bull, or a lamb, or a goat in the camp, or who slaughters it outside the camp, and has not brought it to the entrance to the Tent of Meeting to present it as an offering to the Lord** before the tabernacle of the Lord, blood shall be imputed to that man; he has shed blood; and that man shall be cut off from among his people" (Leviticus 17:3–4).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי "קׇרְבָּן", שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי אֲפִילּוּ קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ קׇרְבָּן, כָּעִנְיָן שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַנַּקְרֵב אֶת קׇרְבַּן ה׳", תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֹא הֱבִיאוֹ". כׇּל הָרָאוּי לְפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד — חַיָּיב עָלָיו בַּחוּץ, כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד — אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **If** it would have mentioned just the word **offering, I** would **derive** that one is liable **even** for slaughtering animals **consecrated for Temple maintenance** outside the Temple, **which are** also **called offering, as it is stated: "And we have brought the Lord's offering,** what every man has found: Articles of gold, armlets, and bracelets, rings, earrings, and pendants" (Numbers 31:50). Therefore, **the verse states: "And has not brought it to the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 17:4), to teach that with regard to **any** animal **fit** to be sacrificed within **the entrance to the Tent of Meeting** or the Temple, **one is liable for** slaughtering **it outside** the Temple. Conversely, with regard to **any** animal **that is not fit** to be sacrificed within **the entrance to the Tent of Meeting, one is not liable for** slaughtering **it outside** the Temple.

The *gemara* now turns to the scriptural basis of *shechutei chutz*. The *tanu rabbanan* cites the *pasuk* from *ish ish mi-beis Yisrael asher yishchat shor o kesev o ez ba-machane o asher yishchat mi-chutz la-machane v'el pesach ohel mo'ed lo hevi'o l'hakriv korban l'Hashem* — one who slaughters an animal without bringing it to the entrance of the *ohel mo'ed* as a *korban l'Hashem* is *chayav kareis*.

A preliminary question arises: does *korban* in this *pasuk* include *kodshei bedek habayis* — animals consecrated for their monetary value, to fund the upkeep of the *beis hamikdash*? Such animals are called *korban* in another context, as when donations were brought and described with that term. One might therefore think that slaughtering a consecrated animal, even one designated for the *bedek habayis* fund, outside the *beis hamikdash* creates *chiyuv*.

### Pesach Ohel Mo'ed Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹצִיא אֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין רְאוּיִן לְפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, וְלֹא אוֹצִיא פָּרַת חַטָּאת וְשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ שֶׁהוּא רָאוּי לָבוֹא אֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "לַה׳", מִי שֶׁמְּיוּחָדִין לַה׳, יָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין מְיוּחָדִין לַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore: **I** might **exclude these** animals consecrated for Temple maintenance, **which are not fit** to be sacrificed within **the entrance to the Tent of Meeting** because they are blemished, **and I will not exclude the heifer of a purification offering and** the **scapegoat, which are fit to come to the entrance to the Tent of Meeting.** Therefore, **the verse states "to the Lord"** to indicate that one is liable only for **those** animals **that are designated exclusively for God** as offerings. **These,** the heifer of a purification offering and the scapegoat, **are excluded, as they are not designated exclusively for God** as sacrificial offerings but are used for some other purpose.

The *gemara* rejects this inclusion on the basis of the continuation of the *pasuk*: *v'el pesach ohel mo'ed lo hevi'o* — the animal must be one that is fit to be brought to the entrance of the *ohel mo'ed* as a *korban*. The interpretive rule established is: *kol hara'uy l'pesach ohel mo'ed chayav alav ba-chutz, kol she'eino ra'uy l'pesach ohel mo'ed ein chayavin alav ba-chutz*. Only an animal that is fit to be brought as a *korban* to the *ohel mo'ed* — that has significance as part of the sacrificial service there — generates *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz*. Animals consecrated merely for their monetary value have no role as *korbanot* within the *ohel mo'ed* and therefore fall outside the prohibition.

### Sa'ir Excluded?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְ"לַה׳" לְהוֹצִיא הוּא? וּרְמִינְהוּ: "יֵרָצֶה לְקׇרְבַּן אִשֶּׁה לַה׳", אֵלּוּ אִישִּׁים,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about this halakhic midrash: **Does** the expression **"to the Lord"** come **to exclude?** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** based upon the verse: "When a bull, or a lamb, or a goat, is born it shall be seven days under its mother; but from the eighth day on **it may be accepted as an offering by fire to the Lord"** (Leviticus 22:27). An offering by fire to the Lord: **These are** offerings **by fire,** which are sacrificed on the altar and which may not be sacrificed before their proper time.

Having established this standard of *ra'ui l'pesach ohel mo'ed*, the *gemara* raises the question of the *sa'ir hamishtalei'ach*. This goat is brought to the *ohel mo'ed* — the drawing of lots takes place there — and it is an integral part of the Yom Kippur service. One might therefore argue that slaughtering it outside the *beis hamikdash* should incur *chiyuv*, since it is indeed *ra'ui lavo pesach ohel mo'ed*.

### L'Hashem Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא יַקְדִּישֶׁנּוּ מְחוּסָּר זְמַן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "קׇרְבָּן", "לַה׳" — לְרַבּוֹת שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ!
>
> **Translation:** **From where** do we derive **that he may not consecrate** an animal when it is **lacking time,** i.e., before the eighth day? **The verse states: An offering,** which indicates that it should not be designated as an offering before the eighth day. The expression: **To the Lord** comes **to include the scapegoat,** which is also brought for the sake of God. In other words, the expression: To the Lord, not only does not exclude the scapegoat from the category of offerings, but specifically includes it in this category.

The *gemara* addresses this through the phrase *l'hakriv korban l'Hashem* — to bring a *korban* to God. Only a *korban* that is entirely for God — *mi shemeyuchadim l'Hashem* — creates *chiyuv* when slaughtered outside. The *sa'ir hamishtalei'ach*, being designated *l'azazel* rather than *l'Hashem*, falls outside this category. Once the lot has designated it as *l'azazel*, it is no longer going to be brought as a *korban* inside the *beis hamikdash*, and therefore slaughtering it outside incurs no *chiyuv*. The *se'ir lachatas*, too, is addressed here: its ritual takes place outside the *beis hamikdash*, and it is therefore not a *korban* that generates *chiyuv* for *shechutei chutz* in the conventional sense.

## L'Hashem Scope

### Ramba Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָתָם מֵעִנְיָנָא דִקְרָא וְהָכָא מֵעִנְיָנָא דִקְרָא. הָתָם דְּ"אֶל פֶּתַח" לְרַבּוֹת — "לַה׳" לְהוֹצִיא. הָכָא דְּ"אִשֶּׁה" לְהוֹצִיא — "לַה׳" לְרַבּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that this can be resolved as follows: **There** the expression is understood **in the context of the verse and here** it is understood **in the context of the verse. There,** with regard to consecrated animals slaughtered outside the Temple, **where** the phrase: **To the entrance,** in that same verse comes **to include** other offerings, the phrase: **To the Lord** necessarily comes **to exclude.** Conversely, **here,** with regard to consecrating animals before the proper time, **where** the phrase: Offering **by fire** comes **to exclude,** the phrase: **To the Lord** comes **to include.**

A striking tension now emerges. In the passage just discussed, *l'Hashem* served to *exclude* the *sa'ir l'azazel* from the *chiyuv* of *shechutei chutz*. But elsewhere the same term functions to *include* the *sa'ir l'azazel*. Rava resolves this by stating *hasam me'inyana dikra v'hacha me'inyana dikra*: in each context, the word *l'Hashem* must be read in light of what the surrounding *pasuk* is already speaking about, and its function — inclusive or exclusive — is determined by the default reading in that context.

### Ohel Mo'ed Exclusion

In the context of *shechutei chutz* and the *ohel mo'ed* passage, the *pasuk*'s language already naturally extends to include the *sa'ir* — it is brought to the *ohel mo'ed* and plays a central role in the Yom Kippur service. Since inclusion is the default, *l'Hashem* comes to redirect and *exclude*: it tells us that only what is entirely for God creates *chiyuv*, thereby removing the *sa'ir l'azazel* from the scope of the prohibition.

### Eight-Day Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי רַחֲמָנָא, הָא לָא רַבִּי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ קָדוֹשׁ בִּמְחוּסַּר זְמַן. וְהָא אֵין הַגּוֹרָל קוֹבֵעַ אֶלָּא בְּרָאוּי לַשֵּׁם!
>
> **Translation:** Once the contradiction has been reconciled, the Gemara challenges the reasoning of the argument. **The reason** the scapegoat may not be consecrated before it is eight days old **is because the Merciful One** specifically **included** it among the other offerings. **But had it not included** it **I would have said: The scapegoat** may be **consecrated** even **when** it is **lacking time.** However, this is difficult: **Isn't** it true that **the lottery establishes** as the goat that is sacrificed to God, **only** one that is **fit** to be sacrificed **to God?** Since it is not known in advance which goat will be designated for this purpose, both goats must be eight days old and thereby fit to be sacrificed to God.

In the context of the eight-day requirement — *yeratzeh l'korban isheh l'Hashem miyom hashmini vahal'ah* — the *pasuk* speaks of animals burnt on the altar, which would naturally *exclude* the *sa'ir hamishtalei'ach*, since it is not brought as an *isheh* offering on the altar. Since exclusion is the default there, *l'Hashem* comes to *include*: even the *sa'ir l'azazel* cannot be sanctified or used if it is less than eight days old.

## Mechusar Zman

### Lottery Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָא מַנִּי, חָנָן הַמִּצְרִי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: חָנָן הַמִּצְרִי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ דָּם בַּכּוֹס — מֵבִיא חֲבֵירוֹ וּמְזַוֵּוג לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** the opinion of **Ḥanan the Egyptian, as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Ḥanan the Egyptian says: Even** if the goat sacrificed to God has already been slaughtered and its **blood** has been collected **in the cup,** if the scapegoat dies, **he brings** another goat as **a counterpart and pairs it with** the goat that has already been slaughtered. Since in this case the priest need not draw new lots and the second goat is immediately designated to be sent to Azazel, it was necessary to teach that this goat must not be lacking time.

The *gemara* now explores why a separate scriptural derivation is needed to establish that the *sa'ir l'azazel* must be at least eight days old. The principle *ein hagoral kovei'a ela b'ra'uy l'shem* — the lot has legal force only when each goat is theoretically eligible to be designated for God — would seem to demand this automatically. For the lottery to be valid, each goat must be fit to serve as the *se'ir Lashem*; since a goat less than eight days old cannot serve *l'Hashem*, neither could it be eligible for the lot, and therefore it could not be used as a substitute *l'azazel* either.

### Substitute Goat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְחָנָן הַמִּצְרִי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ דְּחוּיִין, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ הַגְרָלָה מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? דִּילְמָא מַיְיתֵי וּמַגְרִיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a challenge with regard to this answer. **Say that you heard Ḥanan the Egyptian** say **that he does not** hold that if the scapegoat dies, the blood of the other goat is **rejected. Did you** also **hear him** say **that he does not** require a new **lottery** for the second goat? **Perhaps** he meant that one **brings** two new goats **and draws lots,** and whichever is designated to be sent to Azazel is the counterpart to the goat that was already slaughtered.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָא מַנִּי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן — מֵבִיא חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַגְרָלָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another explanation of why it was necessary for the Torah to indicate that the scapegoat must not be lacking time. **Rather, Rav Yosef said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **If one of** the goats **dies, he brings another** goat in its place **without a lottery;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon.** Therefore, if the scapegoat would die, another goat would be designated as the scapegoat without a lottery, and it was necessary for the Torah to indicate that this goat had to be eight days old.

Rav Yosef answers that the derivation is needed for the position of Chanana ha-Mitzri, who teaches: *afilu dam bekos meivi chaveiro u'mezaveig lo* — even if the blood of the *se'ir Lashem* is already in the cup, ready for the service, and the *se'ir l'azazel* dies, one may bring a replacement goat and pair it with the blood of the first, without performing a new lottery. According to this view, in the scenario of a replacement goat, no lottery takes place — the animal is simply designated directly as the *se'ir l'azazel*. In such a case, there is no requirement that it be theoretically eligible to be the *se'ir Lashem*, and one might have thought an animal less than eight days old could serve. The *pasuk* is therefore necessary to tell us otherwise.

The *gemara* initially questions whether Chanana ha-Mitzri truly dispenses with the lottery, noting that one could read his position as requiring a new lottery with two goats. But Rav Yosef then identifies the ruling more precisely with the position of Rabbi Shimon, who states explicitly: *met echad meihem meivi chaveiro shelo b'hagralah divrei Rabbi Shimon* — if one of the two goats dies, one brings a replacement without a new lottery. Under Rabbi Shimon's view, then, the *sa'ir l'azazel* can be designated independently of the lottery mechanism, and the scriptural derivation for the eight-day requirement is necessary to preclude using an animal less than eight days old in such a case.

### Kedusha Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוּמַם וְחִילְּלוֹ עַל אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** that it is possible to answer according to all opinions. The derivation was necessary in a case **where** the scapegoat **became blemished** after the lottery **and they redeemed it with another.** In that situation, all agree that the new scapegoat does not need to be designated through a lottery, because the status of the original scapegoat was transferred to its replacement.

Ravina offers a further scenario where the *sa'ir l'azazel* can be identified without a new lottery: *k'gon shehumam v'hifrish acher* — if the designated *sa'ir l'azazel* acquired a blemish, rendering it unfit, one brings a replacement and transfers the *kedusha* from the blemished animal to the new one. This is essentially a substitution through *kedusha*-transfer rather than through lottery, and the replacement animal's identity as *l'azazel* is established directly. In this scenario too, one might have thought to use an animal less than eight days old, and the scriptural exclusion is needed.

### Both Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּפָסֵיל בֵּיהּ מוּמָא! דְּתַנְיָא: "וְאִשֶּׁה לֹא תִתְּנוּ מֵהֶם", אֵלּוּ הַחֲלָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do you say that** the scapegoat **is disqualified** by a **blemish?** A blemish disqualifies only a sacrificial offering, and the scapegoat is not a sacrificial offering. The Gemara answers: **As it was taught** in a *baraita* based upon the verse: "Blind, or broken, or maimed, or having a growth, or scurvy, or scabbed, you shall not offer these to the Lord, **nor make** an offering **by fire of them** upon the altar to the Lord" (Leviticus 22:22). The phrase: Nor make an offering by fire of them; **these are the fats** of blemished animals, which may not be sacrificed on the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא כּוּלָּן, מִקְצָתָן מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "מֵהֶם". "מִזְבֵּחַ", זוֹ זְרִיקַת דָּמִים. "לַה׳", לְרַבּוֹת שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **I have only** derived that it is prohibited to sacrifice **all** the fats of a blemished animal; **from where** do I derive that it is prohibited to sacrifice **some of them? The verse states "of them,"** which indicates that the prohibition applies even to some of them. **"The altar"; this is** referring to the prohibition against **sprinkling the blood** of blemished animals. The phrase **"to the Lord"** comes **to include the scapegoat,** which is also disqualified by a blemish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב בַּעַל מוּם, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב מְחוּסַּר זְמַן. דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא מְחוּסַּר זְמַן — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מָטֵי זִמְנֵיהּ, אֲבָל בַּעַל מוּם דְּמָטֵי זִמְנֵיהּ — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בַּעַל מוּם — מִשּׁוּם דִּמְאִיס, אֲבָל מְחוּסַּר זְמַן דְּלָא מְאִיס — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary to write** that **a blemished** scapegoat is disqualified **and** it is **necessary to write** that a scapegoat may not be **lacking time. Since if the Merciful One had written** only that a scapegoat is disqualified if it is **lacking time,** one might have said that this is **because its time has not** yet **come** and therefore it is unable to become consecrated. **However,** with regard to **a blemished** animal **whose time has** already **arrived, say** that it is **not** disqualified. **And** conversely, **if the Merciful One had written** only **that a blemished** scapegoat is disqualified, one might have said that this is **because** it is considered **abhorrent** to sacrifice a blemished animal as an offering. **However,** with regard to a goat that is **lacking time, which is not abhorrent, say** that it is **not** disqualified. It is therefore **necessary** for the Torah to include the scapegoat in both disqualifications.

The *gemara* then explains why both prohibitions — the eight-day minimum and the exclusion of a *ba'al mum* — require independent scriptural derivation and cannot be inferred from one another. Had the Torah written only the prohibition of *mechusar zman*, one might have thought that a *ba'al mum* poses no problem, since the animal is of proper age and its physical defect alone might not have been considered disqualifying for something designated *l'azazel* rather than *l'Hashem*. Had the Torah written only the exclusion of a *ba'al mum*, one might have thought that *mechusar zman* poses no problem for the *sa'ir l'azazel*, since the animal's immaturity is not a physical deformity and might not have been disqualifying when the destination is not the altar. *Tzricha* — both derivations are independently necessary.

### Sa'ir Status

This concludes with a conceptually rich observation. The *sa'ir l'azazel* is designated not *l'Hashem* but *l'azazel*. One might have thought its ritual function so different from that of a standard *korban* that requirements governing *korbanot* — no blemish, minimum age — simply would not apply. Yet the *gemara* demonstrates from two independent *psukim* that both requirements hold. The *sa'ir l'azazel*, despite its destination, retains a genuine status as a *korban*, and the demands placed upon it by these *psukim* underscore the degree to which it continues to inhabit the conceptual world of *korbanos*. The question of precisely how far that status extends — what it means for the *sa'ir l'azazel* to be, in some sense, *l'Hashem* even as it goes *l'azazel* — opens onto deeper questions about the nature of this remarkable *korban* that the *gemara* here begins, but does not conclude, to explore.
