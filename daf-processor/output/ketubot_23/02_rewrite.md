# Ketubot 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Death vs. Divorce

### Rav Yochanan

The daf opens by completing a discussion carried over from the bottom of 22b. Rav Yochanan had distinguished between contradictory witnesses in cases of divorce and cases of death: when witnesses contradict one another regarding whether a woman's husband died, she may remain in a second marriage contracted in reliance on the original testimony, whereas if the contradictory testimony concerned whether she received a *get*, she would be required to leave. The *gemara* had been working through several explanations for this asymmetry.

### Rav Asi

One of those explanations involved the notion that in divorce cases, the woman would not have been sufficiently careful before remarrying, because she could always counter her husband's appearance with a direct claim of having been divorced. Now the *gemara* delivers its final answer to close the *sugya*. Rav Asi says: *kegon de'amri edim achshav met, achshav gershah* — the case is where the witnesses specified that the event happened just now, yesterday, this very day. One set says the husband died just now; another set says he was seen alive just now. Or: one set says she was just divorced; another says she was not.

In the death case, *mita lo salka l'verurah* — if the witnesses are wrong about the death, there is no further means of verification that specifically undermines their testimony. There is nothing structurally suspicious about the contradiction, and therefore *bedi'eved* — after the fact, if she has already remarried — she need not leave. But in the divorce case, *gerushin ika l'verurah*: if she claims she was just divorced yesterday, the obvious question arises — where is the *get*? You haven't had the *get* long enough to lose it. If you claim you lost it since yesterday, that alone raises serious doubts.

### Missing Get

The *gemara* makes this explicit: *de'amrinan lah, i ita dehachi hava, achvi lan gitach* — we say to her, if this really happened, show us your *get*. The inability to produce a very recent *get* creates an additional layer of suspicion beyond the bare contradiction, and it is this additional factor — not present in the death case — that justifies the stricter ruling *bedi'eved* in divorce cases.

## Marriage vs. Divorce

### Mishnah Cases

A new *braita* is introduced. *Shenayim omrim nitkadesha u-shenayim omrim lo nitkadesha* — two witnesses say she was betrothed, two witnesses say she was not. *Harei zo lo tinasei* — she may not marry another man *lechatchila*; *ve'im nisset lo tetzei* — but if she did remarry, she need not leave. Contradictory testimony creates a *chumra lechatchila*, but does not undo the second marriage *bedi'eved*.

Then the second case: *shenayim omrim nitgarshah u-shenayim omrim lo nitgarshah* — two say she was divorced, two say she was not, in a case where her prior married status is already established. Again, *lo tinasei* — she may not remarry. But here, *ve'im nisset tetzei* — if she did remarry, she must leave. The *gemara* at the top of 23a asks: *mai shna reisha u-mai shna seifa?* What is the difference between the first and second case?

Tosafot immediately notes that the answer seems obvious: in the first case, she had a *chazakah* of being a *penuyah* — an unmarried woman — so contradictory testimony about whether she became *mekudeshet* leaves that prior status intact, and *bedi'eved* we revert to it. In the second case, her prior *chazakah* is that of a married woman — *eishet ish* — and so *bedi'eved* we revert to that status and require a divorce. The *gemara*, however, does not rest content with this answer and instead seeks a deeper analysis.

### Abaye

Abaye says: *targuma b'eid echad* — understand the case not as two witnesses against two witnesses, but as a single witness against a single witness. This reframing is clarifying. In the first case: *eid echad omer nitkadsha, v'eid echad omer lo nitkadsha* — one witness says she was betrothed, one says she was not. *Travaihu b'fnuyah kamas'hadi* — both witnesses actually share common ground: they agree she started out as a *penuyah*. The witness who says she was *nitkadesha* is therefore a single witness attempting to alter her established prior status. *V'ein devarav shel echad bimkom shnayim* — one witness does not prevail against the two-witness standard needed to override a presumed status. Her *chazakah* as an unmarried woman therefore holds.

The second case mirrors this precisely: *eid echad omer nitgarsha, v'eid echad omer lo nitgarsha* — both witnesses agree she began as *eishet ish*. The one who claims she was divorced is a single voice trying to change the *chazakah* of a married woman, and again, a single witness cannot accomplish that. So the *chazakah* of being married prevails, and even *bedi'eved* she must leave the second marriage.

The key conceptual move here is that the *chazakah* does not simply cancel out contradictory pairs of witnesses — it defeats the single witness who stands against it. When testimony is presented as two against two, the *chazakah* does not independently resolve the contradiction, and the result would be *teiku*. But when we reframe it as one against one, the prior status functions as the second voice, and thus the one witness opposing the *chazakah* is effectively outnumbered.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi offers a dramatically different reading — not only reversing which case permits and which forbids, but reconceiving the very structure of the testimony. *L'olam trei u-trei* — keep it as two witnesses against two, but *v'ipcha* — reverse the ruling. In the case involving a *penuyah* who may have become betrothed, Rav Ashi holds she must leave the second marriage; in the case involving a married woman who may have been divorced, she need not leave.

His key move is to recharacterize what the contradicting witnesses are actually saying. *Shnayim omrim reinuha she-nitkadsha u-shnayim omrim lo reinuha she-nitkadsha* — two say "we saw her get betrothed," and two say "we did not see her get betrothed." These are not symmetrical claims. The second pair are not asserting that no betrothal occurred; they are merely reporting their own absence of observation. *Lo raiti einah re'ayah* — not seeing is not proof. The absence of evidence is not the evidence of absence. The second testimony is therefore effectively null. We are left with only the witnesses who say she was betrothed, and so she must leave the second marriage.

### Chazakah Factor

The *gemara* asks: if the second witnesses' testimony is meaningless, what is the *chiddush* of their testimony in this case? The answer is the specific circumstance: *tzricha d'dayri b'chatzer achat* — all parties lived in the same courtyard. In that situation, one might argue *kala it lei lemilta* — such a matter has a rumor, a communal resonance; people in the same courtyard would surely have heard about a wedding. The second witnesses are therefore not just saying "we didn't see it" but implying "we were around and heard nothing." I might have thought that this argument should carry weight. *Ka mashma lan* — the *chiddush* is that it does not, because *d'avdi inshi d'mikadshi betzina* — sometimes people get married quietly and privately, without communal notice.

The same logic applies to the corresponding *seifa*. The two witnesses say "we did not see her get divorced," and again this is not an assertion that no divorce occurred. Even *bedi'eved*, we do not force her to leave on the basis of their silence. The possible *chiddush* here is that one might reason differently about divorce than about *kiddushin*: perhaps *mi ika d'migarsha kala it lei lemilta* — a divorce is the sort of thing that always gets talked about, so silence should be more probative. *Ka mashma lan d'avdi inshi d'mikadshi umigarshi betzina* — people conduct both marriages and divorces in private, and silence on the matter settles nothing, not even *bedi'eved*.

## Peh She'Asar

### Woman's Claim

Having worked through those cases, the *gemara* returns to the *mishnah* itself, which rests on the principle of *peh she'asar hu ha-peh she-hitir* — the same mouth that bound is the mouth that frees. The *mishnah* presented two such cases. In the first, a woman says *eshet ish hayiti vegrusha ani* — I was a married woman and I am now divorced. In the second, *shvuya hayiti ve-tehorah ani* — I was taken captive but I am pure, I was not violated. In both cases, since she herself created the initial problem through her own admission, and since she supplied the resolution in the same breath, she is believed. 

But the *mishnah* then records: if witnesses come and establish the first part of her statement — corroborating that she was indeed married or indeed taken captive — without corroborating the second part, then the *peh she'asar* principle no longer applies and she is not believed. However, if she already married before those witnesses arrived, *lo tetzei* — she does not have to leave.

The *gemara* now draws out the image of these stages in sequence. She makes her claim; she is *muteret*; she remarries on that basis; the witnesses then arrive. Because she acted on a valid *heter*, even the arrival of evidence undermining the *peh she'asar* does not undo the marriage *bedi'eved*.

### Oshaya vs. Avin

The *gemara* records a dispute: *Rabbi Oshaya matni la reisha* — Rabbi Oshaya reads the ruling about witnesses arriving post-marriage as applying to the first case, where a woman claims to have been divorced; *Rabba bar Avin matni la seifa* — Rabba bar Avin reads it as applying to the second case, where a woman claims not to have been violated while in captivity.

This distinction has substantial consequences. If we say the ruling of *lo tetzei* applies even in the first case — where witnesses establish she had been a married woman and there is now no testimony that she was properly divorced — we are tolerating the possibility of an ongoing *eishet ish* relationship, a biblical prohibition. If we apply it only to the second case, where the concern is *shvuya* — a rabbinic presumption of violation that could bar her from a *kohen* — the leniency is far more palatable, since the original rabbinic concern does not override the fact that she married on a valid *heter*.

The reasoning is direct: *d'shvuya hi kilu* — the prohibition of the *shvuya* is rabbinic in origin, resting only on the presumption that we cannot know she was not violated. If we permit her to remain married even in the first case, where biblical adultery is conceivably at stake, then *kol sheken seifa* — certainly we permit it in the lesser case. Conversely, if we hold that the ruling applies only in the *shvuya* case, then in the first case — where she may be a *married woman* whose divorce is unestablished — we would require her to leave even *bedi'eved*.

### Rav Hamnuna

The *gemara* asks whether this dispute tracks the position of Rav Hamnuna. Rav Hamnuna, cited the previous day, held that a woman who claims to her husband's face that she was divorced is believed — *chazakah ein isha me'iza paneha bifnei ba'ala* — on the presumption that a woman would not be so brazen as to make such a claim in her husband's presence. The *gemara* suggests that Rabbi Oshaya, who permits her to stay married even in the first case despite the arriving witnesses, must be relying on Rav Hamnuna's principle to supply the credibility her *peh she'asar* would otherwise have lost. And Rabba bar Avin, who does not extend the leniency to that case, would not accept Rav Hamnuna.

### Husband Present

The *gemara* declines this framing, however: *d'chulei alma it lehu d'Rav Hamnuna* — everyone may actually agree with Rav Hamnuna. The dispute is instead about the scope of his principle. One view holds that Rav Hamnuna's reasoning — that a woman would not fabricate in her husband's presence — applies *only* when she makes her claim directly before her husband: *befanav*. Without his physical presence, *me'iza* — she is willing to be brazen and lie, and therefore the *peh she'asar* does not carry sufficient weight once witnesses undermine the first half. The other view holds: *shelo befanav nami eina me'iza* — even when the husband is not physically present, she would not lie so brazenly, because he could always appear and contradict her. The prospect of being caught is itself a sufficient deterrent, and so her claim retains credibility even in his absence.

This means that in a case where a woman remarried, witnesses then established that she had been married to another man, and no *get* has been established — she may still remain in the second marriage, sustained by the weight of her own claim that she was divorced, even though her husband is not standing there to be confronted. The reasoning invokes also a related principle: *ein adam me'iz panav bifnei ba'al chovo* — one does not brazenly lie to the person to whom one owes a relational debt. The analogy to the creditor is apt: it is not merely that one avoids lying to those who know the truth, but that one's sense of obligation and relational accountability provides a natural brake on fabrication.

### Avuha d'Shmuel

The *gemara* then extends the ruling still further. *Amar Avuha d'Shmuel: lo nisset nisset mamash* — the father of Shmuel says that "she remarried" need not mean she literally remarried. *Ela keivan she-hitiruha l'inases* — once *beit din* has issued a ruling permitting her to remarry, even if she has not yet done so, she retains the benefit of that *psak*. The *heter* is locked in from the moment of the ruling, not from the moment of the marriage.

This is a striking and far-reaching *chiddush*. Even in a case where witnesses later arrive to establish that the woman had been a married woman with no corroborated *get*, the *psak* of *beit din* issued in reliance on her *peh she'asar* cannot be retracted. The practical implication is pointed: a woman who presents herself to a new *beit din*, declares *eshet ish hayiti vegrusha ani*, and receives a *heter* before any complicating evidence surfaces, is thereafter protected. The *psak* cannot be undone even by subsequently arriving witnesses — provided those witnesses stop short of actually testifying to the disqualifying event itself.

## Shvuyah

### Captive Claims

A *braita* is cited: a woman says *nishbeiti u-tehorah ani* — I was taken captive but I was not raped — *ve-yesh li eidim she-tehorah ani*, and there are witnesses who can confirm my purity. One might have thought the appropriate response is to wait for those witnesses to arrive before issuing a ruling. The *braita* says otherwise: *matirin otah miyad* — we permit her immediately. The *peh she'asar* applies now; the witnesses need not be awaited.

The case then develops: the *beit din* issues its ruling permitting her to marry; *ve-achar kach ba'u eidim* — and then witnesses arrive. They confirm she was indeed taken captive, thus undermining the *peh she'asar*. But they add: *lo yadanu* — we cannot testify as to what actually happened during the captivity. We were not always present; we cannot say she was not raped, but neither can we say she was.

*Lo yadanu harei zo lo tetzei* — since these witnesses cannot testify to the disqualifying event, and she had already been granted her *heter*, she does not leave the marriage. According to Avuha d'Shmuel, she would not even need to have acted on the *psak* — the ruling itself locks in the *heter*. But *ve-im ba'u edei tumah* — if witnesses arrive who can positively testify that she was violated — *afilu yesh lah kama banim*, even if she has had many children by the *kohen*, she must leave. Where the children are born after the disqualifying fact is established, they would be *chalalim*. Hard testimony of the event overrides all prior *heteirim*.

### Shmuel's Daughters

The *gemara* then presents a famous episode. *Hani shevuyata d'atyan l'Nehardea* — certain captive women were brought to Nehardea in the process of being ransomed. Avuha d'Shmuel appointed guards to watch over them during this period, presumably to prevent any further harm while the transaction was being concluded. Shmuel said to him: *ve-ad idna man natrinhu* — who was watching them until now? They were in the hands of their captors. Tosafot understands Shmuel to have meant this as a *chumra*: whatever protection you are now arranging, we must already presume that events had occurred during their captivity, rendering any current precautions futile for purposes of halachic status.

His father replied: *ilu bnatayikh havyon, mi havat mezalzel bekulhai hai* — if these were your own daughters, you would not be so dismissive of their welfare. The focus shifts from theoretical halachic analysis to the immediate human responsibility of protecting vulnerable women now present in the community. The *gemara* notes that this exchange functioned as an inadvertent *peh she'asar* of a different kind — *hava keshigaga sheyotzei milifnei ha-shalit* — it was like an error that issues from the mouth of a ruler, and *va-ishtabiyan bnatei demar Shmuel* — Shmuel's own daughters were subsequently taken captive.

His daughters were transported to *Eretz Yisrael* by their captors. When they were brought to a Jewish town where a ransom might be arranged, they instructed their captors to wait outside. *Va-ayilu levei midrasha de-Rabbi Chanina* — they entered the *beit midrash* of Rabbi Chanina. There, each one in turn declared: *nishbeiti u-tehorah ani* — I was taken captive but I am pure. Rabbi Chanina issued the ruling immediately, granting them their *heter*. *Ve-sof ve-atu shvuyinhu* — only then did the captors walk in.

Rabbi Chanina recognized the legal elegance of what had occurred: *bnan de-moran inun* — these are the daughters of a great sage. They had understood their grandfather's principle — that once a *psak* has been issued on the basis of *peh she'asar*, the *heter* is not retracted even when the disqualifying facts become publicly apparent — and they had acted on it with precision. By making their declaration and receiving a ruling before the captors entered the room, the visual presence of those captors, which might otherwise function as evidence equivalent to testimony that they had been captured, could not undo a *psak* already granted.

Rabbi Chanina then said to Rabbi Shemen bar Abba: *puk itapel bikrovotaich* — go take care of your relatives, indicating that Rabbi Shemen bar Abba was somehow related to them and implicitly suggesting a marital arrangement. Rabbi Shemen bar Abba hesitated, noting: *ve-ika edei bimdinat ha-yam* — are there not witnesses overseas? The concern was that witnesses to the actual violation might exist elsewhere. The *gemara* responds: *hashta mihat leitnehu kaman* — right now they are not before us. *Edei betzad astan ve-te'aser?* Witnesses on the other side of the world render her forbidden?

### Captors Appear

The *gemara* presses: *ta'ama de-la atu edei* — the reason things are permitted is only because the witnesses have not yet arrived. *Ha atu edei, mitsara* — if witnesses did come, they would become forbidden. But this creates tension with Avuha d'Shmuel's ruling: *keivan she-hitiruha shuv lo te'aser* — once the *heter* was issued it cannot be revoked. The *gemara* resolves the tension by noting that the *heter* is indeed not revocable — and the story of Shmuel's daughters itself bears this out, since the captors' very presence in the room after the fact did not undo the ruling.

### Rav Ashi Concern

Rav Ashi clarifies the one exception: *edei tumah itmar* — the concern about witnesses refers to witnesses who can testify to the rape itself, not merely to the captivity. It is only such testimony — direct evidence of the disqualifying act — that could override the *heter*. Witnesses to captivity alone cannot, since the *heter* was built on the *peh she'asar* and the *shvuya* concern is rabbinic. The *heter* was properly issued; it holds. Only witnesses to actual violation are in a different category, and that concern had been established from the *braita* earlier: *ve-im ba'u edei tumah, afilu yesh lah kama banim, tetzei*.

The broader principle that emerges from this entire discussion is substantial. Even in a case involving a *shvuya* — and, according to some readings, even in the more serious case of *eishet ish* — once a *psak* has been issued on the basis of a valid *peh she'asar*, the *heter* is locked in and survives the subsequent arrival of witnesses to the captivity. Only witnesses who testify to the disqualifying act itself — actual violation or actual continued marriage — can undo what has been established.

## Two Women Captive

### Self vs. Other

Turning to 23b, the *gemara* introduces a *braita* about two women who were both taken captive. *Zo omeret nishbeti u-tehorah ani* — this one says I was captive and I am pure; *ve-zo omeret nishbeti u-tehorah ani* — and so does the other. The presumption here is that there are witnesses establishing that they were taken captive — *shenishbu* — so their own testimony about themselves is discounted: *einan ne'emanot*.

However, *bizman she-hen me'idot zo et zo — harei elu ne'emanot* — when each woman testifies about the other's purity, they are believed. This relies on the principle, already established, that *edut ishah* — even a woman's testimony — is credited in cases of *shvuya* when she testifies about someone other than herself. The *gemara* acknowledges the concern of *gomlin* — reciprocity — that one might suspect the two women struck an arrangement to testify for each other. Nevertheless, their cross-testimony is accepted.

### Parsing Braita

A subsequent *braita* elaborates four cases involving compound statements. First: *ani tme'ah ve-chaverti tehorah* — I am impure and my friend is pure. *Ne'emenet* — she is believed; there is nothing problematic about testifying unfavorably about oneself and favorably about another. Second: *ani tehorah ve-chaverti tme'ah* — I am pure and my friend is impure. *Einah ne'emenet* — she is not believed, neither about herself (witnesses establish she was captured) nor about making her friend's situation worse. Third: *ani ve-chaverti tme'ah* — both of us are impure. *Ne'emenet al atzmah, ve-einah ne'emenet al chavertah* — she is believed about herself if she wishes to declare herself forbidden; she is not believed to make her friend forbidden. Fourth: *ani ve-chaverti tehorah* — both of us are pure. *Ne'emenet al chavertah ve-einah ne'emenet al atzmah* — she is believed about her friend but not about herself, since witnesses have established her captivity.

The *gemara* immediately notes a structural inconsistency. If the presence of witnesses explains why she is not believed about herself in the first case and the last, then in the middle case — where she says her friend is impure and the ruling is that her friend is nevertheless presumed pure — witnesses about the friend would already render the friend presumptively impure. Why would her testimony about the friend be dismissed as if the friend's status is unaffected? This can only make sense if, in the middle case, there are *no* witnesses. But then how can the first and last cases, which follow directly in the same *braita*, assume the presence of witnesses?

Abaye accepts the inconsistency and says plainly: *reisha ve-seifa d'ika edim, metzi'asa d'leika edim* — the first and last cases involve witnesses, the middle case does not. The *braita* simply shifts between scenarios without marking the shift explicitly.

### Rav Pappa

Rav Pappa offers a more unified reading. *Kula d'ika edim* — the entire *braita* concerns a case where there are witnesses. But he introduces an additional element: *ve-ika ed echad d'apich* — there is also a single witness whose testimony runs contrary to what the woman is saying about either herself or her friend. This new factor resolves the internal tension without requiring the middle case to lack witnesses.

### Purity Wins

The analysis of Rav Pappa's cases generates a major *chiddush*. Take the first scenario under his reading: the woman says *ani tme'ah ve-chaverti tehorah* — I am impure, my friend is pure — and a single witness says the opposite: *at tehorah ve-chavertach tme'ah* — you are pure and your friend is impure. For her own status: *ihi shavi l'nafshah chaticha d'issura* — she can render herself forbidden by her own word, even against a witness's testimony to her purity. For her friend: the woman says the friend is *tehorah* and the witness says *tme'ah* — and the ruling is that the friend is permitted, *ve-chavertah mishtraya apumah d'didah*, by the woman's testimony. 

This is striking. Even though a specific *eid echad* has testified that the friend was violated — not merely that we are uncertain about what happened during captivity, but that this witness actively says the friend was raped — we nevertheless credit the single voice claiming *tehorah* over the single voice claiming *tme'ah*. The principle that emerges is that in a case of one witness against one witness regarding the purity of a captive woman, we always follow the voice saying *tehorah*. This parallels the principle established just days earlier regarding contradictory testimony about a husband's death: when one witness says he died and one says he did not, we follow the testimony that permits the woman to remarry. Here, in the captive-woman context, when one witness says she was violated and one says she was not, we follow the testimony of purity.

### Reciprocal Cases

Rav Pappa then works through the reverse and combined scenarios, and the *gemara* challenges each additional case as redundant. The case of *ani tehorah ve-chaverti tme'ah* — I am pure, my friend is impure — with a witness asserting the opposite is challenged as already established by the previous case. The *chiddush* of this case, however, is the following: one might have thought that when the woman says her friend is *tme'ah*, and a witness says the friend is *tehorah*, perhaps the witness should be disbelieved because the woman's self-damaging claim about herself — simultaneously asserting her own impurity — lends special credibility to her testimony overall. The *gemara* says: even so, we follow the witness who says *tehorah* for the friend.

The final case introduced is *ani ve-chaverti tme'ah* — both of us are impure — with a witness saying *at ve-chavertach tehorot* — both of you are pure. One might have thought that since the witness exculpates both of them, and the woman may simply be seeking to bring her friend down with her — invoking the *tamus nafshi im plishtim* principle, being willing to harm oneself in order to harm another — we should not apply *shavya l'nafshah* and should not make the woman herself forbidden on her own word. *Ka mashma lan* — the ruling teaches that a person who declares herself forbidden is bound by it, even when a plausible hostile motive could explain the statement.

The cumulative *chiddush* that has emerged by the end of this discussion is the following: the *mishnah* had already established that a single witness — even a woman — is credited when testifying about *another* captive woman's purity. What Rav Pappa's elaboration now teaches is that this credibility holds even against a directly contradicting *eid echad* who testifies to the violation. As long as it is one voice against one voice, we invariably credit the testimony of purity when it concerns someone other than the testifying party herself.
