# Niddah 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Box & Rodent Case

### Box Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוּפָּה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמְּשׁוּ בָּהּ טְהָרוֹת בְּזָוִית זוֹ, וְנִמְצָא שֶׁרֶץ בְּזָוִית אַחֶרֶת
>
> **Translation:** A basket that was used as a container for ritually pure items, and those items were placed in one corner, and subsequently the carcass of a creeping animal was found in a different corner

The Gemara on the bottom of 3b presents an Amoraic dispute that echoes a case already introduced in the discussion of Hillel and Shammai. The case involves a *kuppa* — a box or basket — that was used to store *tahoros*, ritually pure food, in one section or corner. Subsequently, after the contents were removed or the box emptied, a dead *sheretz* (rodent) was discovered in a different corner. The central question is whether we must be concerned that this *sheretz* was already present when the *tahoros* were being handled, retroactively rendering them *tamei*, or whether we may assume the *sheretz* arrived only afterward.

### Hillel & Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וְהִלֵּל מוֹדוּ בְּקוּפָּה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ שׁוּלַיִם
>
> **Translation:** Shammai and Hillel agree with regard to a basket that has a base

The Gemara first establishes a point of agreement: both Shammai and Hillel concur that when the box has a floor (*shulayim*), we must be concerned that the *sheretz* was there earlier and that the prior *tahoros* are retroactively *tamei*. This was, in fact, the very case Hillel cited when he pressed Shammai during their famous exchange. Shammai had accepted the argument in principle — a *sheretz* found in a floored box may well have been resting there unnoticed throughout — but distinguished it from the case of the woman by noting that a woman has no such "floor" to retain the blood. So Shammai and Hillel agreed on the box with a floor; what remains is to understand where Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan enter the picture.

### Chizkiyah vs. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי חִזְקִיָּה וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּקוּפָּה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ שׁוּלַיִם. אֵין לָהּ שׁוּלַיִם, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן? אֵין לָהּ שׁוּלַיִם, וְיֵשׁ לָהּ אֳוגָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** When Ḥizkiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan disagree, it is with regard to a basket that has no base, i.e., both ends were open and the basket was used while it was lying on its side. If the basket has no base, what is the reasoning of Rabbi Yoḥanan for ruling that the produce is ritually impure? The basket has no base but it does have an inward-curved rim. Therefore, it is possible that the carcass of a small creeping animal might have been held back by the rim and remained inside the basket even after it was turned over.

Their dispute concerns a box without a floor. The practical implication of such a configuration is that in order to retrieve the contents, one would have to empty or pour everything out completely. In such a case, the logic runs: had the *sheretz* been present all along, it would have come out when the box was fully emptied. Since it was not seen then, one could reasonably conclude it only arrived later. Chizkiyah therefore rules that the prior *tahoros* are *tahor* — we need not be concerned retroactively.

The Gemara immediately asks why Rabbi Yochanan would still be concerned that the *sheretz* was there earlier. The answer: the box lacks a floor but possesses a lip (*oznayim*), a rim curled inward. It is therefore possible that when the contents were poured out, the *sheretz* became lodged in that lip, unnoticed, and later fell back to the bottom of the box when it was righted. Rabbi Yochanan holds that this possibility is sufficient to generate retroactive concern; Chizkiyah disagrees.

### Barrel & Buckets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּדְלֶה עֲשָׂרָה דְּלָיִים מַיִם בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, וְנִמְצָא שֶׁרֶץ בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן — הוּא טָמֵא, וְכוּלָּן טְהוֹרִין. וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין לָהּ אֳוגָנִים, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לָהּ אֳוגָנִים — כּוּלָּן טְמֵאִין.
>
> **Translation:** One who draws ten pails of water one after another, i.e., he draws out water in one pail and from it he fills another ten pails with water, and the carcass of a creeping animal is found in one of them, that pail of water is ritually impure but all the rest of them are pure. And Reish Lakish says in the name of Rabbi Yannai: This *halakha* was taught only in a case where the pail has no inward-curved rim. Consequently, its contents are entirely emptied each time it is overturned, which means that the creeping animal must have entered only this pail. But if the pail has a rim, all of the water that had been drawn is impure, as the creeping animal might have entered the pail earlier and the rim held it back each time the pail was overturned.

The Gemara now cites a *baraita* that makes this scenario considerably clearer. One who draws water from a well using successive buckets, each poured into a large barrel, ten times in sequence. When filling bucket number ten, a dead *sheretz* is discovered inside that bucket. The bucket itself is obviously *tamei*. But what of the water already collected in the barrel? All of it is *tahor*, because the logic is straightforward: had the *sheretz* been present in an earlier bucket, it would have been poured into the barrel along with the water. The fact that it is found still sitting in the bucket demonstrates it could not have been there during earlier pours.

Reish Lakish, citing Rabbi Yannai, introduces a qualification: the ruling that everything is *tahor* applies only if the bucket lacks a lip. But if the bucket has an inward-curling lip, then one must be concerned that all the water drawn from previous buckets is *tamei*, since the *sheretz* may have been caught in the lip all along and not poured into the barrel each time.

This naturally raises a tension: Chizkiyah, in the case of the box with a lip, ruled *tahor*, whereas Rabbi Yannai (as cited by Reish Lakish) in the case of a bucket with a lip rules *tamei*. Perhaps, the Gemara suggests, they simply disagree.

### Water vs. Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַיָּא שָׁרְקִי, פֵּירֵי לָא שָׁרְקִי.
>
> **Translation:** Water is slippery, whereas produce is not slippery.

The Gemara attempts to distinguish the two cases rather than leave them in simple disagreement. Water slides; solid contents do not. Water flows out with minimal tilting; a quick pour is sufficient to empty a bucket of water and a *sheretz* lodged in the lip might plausibly remain. By contrast, emptying a box of grain, flour, or fruit requires vigorous shaking and a far more thorough inversion, making it far less likely that something caught in a lip would remain behind. Thus Chizkiyah, dealing with solid contents, is not concerned that the *sheretz* was caught in the lip, whereas Rabbi Yannai's ruling about water is entirely consistent with his approach.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי: מַיָּא לָא קָפֵיד עֲלַיְיהוּ, פֵּירֵי קָפֵיד עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Alternatively, there is a different reason why one does not overturn a pail of water entirely: In the case of water, one is not particular about it, i.e., he does not mind if some is left over. Therefore he does not overturn the pail completely. By contrast, in the case of produce one is particular about it, so he raises one end of the basket entirely to ensure that every last piece of produce is emptied.

An alternative explanation is offered as well: with water, one is not meticulous about getting out every last drop, so one might well leave a small amount behind, and with it a *sheretz* that happened to be there. With solid food, however, one is careful to extract everything and shakes the container accordingly, making it highly unlikely anything was left behind. The two explanations complement one another and successfully reconcile Chizkiyah's ruling with Rabbi Yannai's.

## Box Applied to Woman

### Unchecked Box

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁכִיחִי בָּהּ דָּמִים — כְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ בְּדוּקָה דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Since blood is commonly found flowing from her, as women regularly experience menstrual flows, it is considered as though she were not examined.

Having analyzed the box-and-rodent case in its various dimensions, the Gemara turns to another distinction that connects directly to the case of the woman. Because menstruation is prevalent in women, her status is comparable to that of a box that has not been checked before use. This comparison is made in the context of establishing when Hillel and Shammai agree versus when Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan debate. According to one answer offered by the Gemara, Hillel and Shammai agree that the prior *tahoros* are *tamei* specifically when the box was not inspected before use — for in that case there was never a confirmed *status quo* of being free from *sheretz*. Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan debate the case when the box *was* checked (*bekuppah bedukah*) prior to use.

### Chazakah Liable Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דּוּמְיָא דְאִשָּׁה קָתָנֵי, וְאִשָּׁה בְּדוּקָה הִיא! כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁכִיחִי בָּהּ דָּמִים — כְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ בְּדוּקָה דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But isn't the case of a basket taught as being similar to the case of a menstruating woman? Hillel had cited the case of the basket as a difficulty with regard to Shammai's opinion in the case of a menstruating woman. And since a woman is considered fully examined, since she examines herself with examination cloths twice a day, the other case must also be referring to a basket that had been examined. The Gemara answers: Since blood is commonly found flowing from her, as women regularly experience menstrual flows, it is considered as though she were not examined.

This brings us back to the woman, and the Gemara's formulation is striking. When Hillel and Shammai compared their box case to the case of a woman who discovered blood, the Gemara asks: is a woman not like the checked box? She was known to be *tehorah* at some prior point; she has an established *status quo*. So why should she be treated like an unchecked box?

The answer: since blood is prevalent in a woman, since menstruation is a regular, recurring reality, the prior *status quo* of *taharah* is rendered conceptually unstable. In more formal terms, hers is a *chazakah ha'asuyah l'hishtanos* — a *chazakah*, a status quo, that is by its very nature in the process of changing, likely to shift at any time. A *chazakah* only reliably carries forward into the future and backwards to explain the past when the status it reflects is a stable one — when, in other words, there is no particular reason to expect it to change. The woman's status does not enjoy that stability, and therefore even though she is in a sense "checked," her case is more akin to the unchecked box for purposes of this analysis.

It is worth pausing here to note that this analysis operates not on the basis of raw probability — *rov* is not mentioned — but on the basis of formal rules about the reliability of a *chazakah*. A standard *chazakah* allows one to say: "What is true today was likely true yesterday, and the day before." A *chazakah ha'asuyah l'hishtanos* cannot sustain that inference with the same force. And this remains a purely formal principle, analogous in a certain sense to what philosophers call the problem of induction — the inference that the future will resemble the past has no strictly logical foundation, yet it is the basis on which we conduct our lives, and the *chazakah* functions in precisely that manner as a halachic instrument.

### Covered Box

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כִּי מוֹדוּ שַׁמַּאי וְהִלֵּל בְּקוּפָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְכוּסָּה, כִּי פְּלִיגִי חִזְקִיָּה וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּקוּפָּה מְכוּסָּה. מְכוּסָּה — הֵיכִי נְפַל? כְּגוֹן שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישָׁהּ עַל יְדֵי כִּסּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another resolution of the apparent contradiction between the ruling of Ḥizkiyya and the opinions of Hillel and Shammai. And if you wish, say: When Shammai and Hillel agree, it is with regard to a basket that is not covered. When do Ḥizkiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan disagree? With regard to a basket that is covered. If the basket is covered, how did the creeping animal fall inside? For example, if the basket is used by removing its lid.

The Gemara offers yet another way of distinguishing the case where Hillel and Shammai agree from the case where Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan debate. Hillel and Shammai agree that the prior *tahoros* are *tamei* when the box was not covered, meaning there was no active protection against a *sheretz* entering. Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan debate the case of a box that was used with a cover; it is not sealed absolutely at all times, but when not actively in use, it is kept closed.

This covered box represents the stronger end of the *chazakah* spectrum. Rather than a mere passive *status quo* — "as far as we know, no *sheretz* has entered" — the covered box has an active protection operating to preserve its *tahor* status. There is something positively working to maintain the state of affairs, not merely an absence of known disruption. And therefore in that case, at minimum, one might say the prior *tahoros* are *tahor*, or at least that there is room to debate. In the uncovered box, there is no such protection, and both Hillel and Shammai concede the concern is well-founded.

### Woman Covered Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דּוּמְיָא דְאִשָּׁה קָתָנֵי, וְאִשָּׁה מְכוּסָּה הִיא, כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁכִיחִי בַּהּ דָּמִים — כְּשֶׁאֵין מְכוּסָּה דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: But isn't the case of a basket taught as being similar to the case of a menstruating woman? And just as a woman is considered covered, since no outside blood can enter her, so too in the case of a basket, it must be one where it is constantly covered. The Gemara explains: Since blood is commonly found flowing from her, as women regularly experience menstrual flows, it is considered as though she is not always covered.

The Gemara now returns to the comparison: but Hillel and Shammai explicitly compared their case to the woman, implying the woman corresponds to the uncovered box. Yet the Gemara observes: a woman is "covered."

Rashi interprets this as a physical observation: just as a *sheretz* cannot enter a box from outside if the box is covered, nothing foreign enters the woman's body. But Tosafot finds this reading untenable, since a woman's *tumah* arises from her own blood, not from any external contamination. Tosafot therefore explains "covered" in terms of the position of Shammai himself: the blood becomes *tamei* only once it crosses the cervix into the vaginal canal, and the cervix is in a sense "closed," functioning like a cover. More broadly, this maps onto Shammai's earlier argument that a woman senses when she is beginning to menstruate, and that sensory awareness serves as a kind of protection of her prior status — analogous to the cover that prevents a *sheretz* from entering undetected.

If so, Hillel, who does not accept Shammai's experiential argument, would still agree that the box case is *tamei*, and that the same applies to the woman — and that the "covered" quality only appears to protect her status without actually doing so. Why? Because menstruation is so common, the theoretical "coverage" is counterbalanced and overridden by the statistical reality of frequent bleeding. The idea that she will sense the onset provides some analogy to a covered box, but the prevalence of menstruation neutralizes that advantage, rendering her status effectively equivalent to an uncovered box — and in an uncovered box, both Hillel and Shammai agree that the prior *tahoros* are *tamei*.

### Woman Objectified

Finally, the Gemara consolidates the comparison by suggesting that a woman is like a box with a dead *sheretz* inside of it. This is a point worth naming directly. The entire sustained discussion of this daf has involved objectifying the case of the woman — treating her as though she were a *mikvah* with a leak, a barrel of wine turning to vinegar, or a box containing a *sheretz*. These comparisons are not made with any pejorative intent as a matter of Talmudic method; they arise naturally from the process of abstraction, of identifying the formal halachic structure underlying a case and then comparing it to other cases with the same structure. Nevertheless, once those comparisons are made, the associations exist and carry their own weight, and that remains a genuine challenge that should be acknowledged.

## Place-to-Place Transfer

### Box Moved Rooms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוּפָּה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּמְּשׁוּ בָּהּ טְהָרוֹת בְּזָוִית בַּיִת זוֹ, וְטִלְטְלוּהָ בְּזָוִית אַחֶרֶת, וְנִמְצָא שֶׁרֶץ בְּזָוִית אַחֶרֶת. חִזְקִיָּה סָבַר: לָא מַחְזְקִינַן טוּמְאָה מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: מַחְזְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** If one has a basket that was used as a container for ritually pure produce in this corner of the house, and after the produce was removed it was subsequently carried to another corner, and the carcass of a creeping animal was found in the basket while it was in that other corner, Ḥizkiyya holds: The produce remains ritually pure, as we do not presume that ritual impurity moved from place to place. In other words, the impure creeping animal is not assumed to have moved from the first corner where the produce was kept. Instead, it fell inside while the basket was in the second corner, and therefore the produce that it previously contained remains pure. And Rabbi Yoḥanan holds: The produce is retroactively considered impure, as we do presume that ritual impurity, i.e., the carcass of the creeping animal in the basket, moved from place to place.

The Gemara now pivots to a different variant of the box-and-*sheretz* case. A box was being used to handle *tahoros* in one corner of a house — say, the living room. It was then moved to a different corner, or to another room entirely, the bedroom. And now, with the box sitting in the bedroom, a dead *sheretz* is discovered inside it. The question is whether the fact that we find the *sheretz* in the box now, in the bedroom, gives rise to retroactive concern about what happened earlier when the box was in the living room with the *tahoros*.

### Chizkiyah No Concern

Chizkiyah rules: we do not establish, or apply, the status of *tumah* from one location to another. Since the *sheretz* is found in the box now in the bedroom, we cannot project that status backward to when the box was in the living room. The prior use of the *tahoros* remains *tahor*.

### Yochanan Applies

Rabbi Yochanan rules: we do apply the status from one place to another. The fact that we find the *sheretz* in the box now is sufficient cause to be concerned that it was present earlier, regardless of which room the box happened to be in at the time. The physical relocation of the box does not break the inferential link between the current status and the prior one.

It is worth emphasizing the formal character of this dispute. It is not purely a matter of probability — one might construct scenarios in which rodents are more prevalent in the bedroom than the living room, or vice versa, and that would affect the likelihood of the *sheretz* having been present in either location. But the dispute of Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan is not framed in those terms. It is a formal question: can the current status — *sheretz* in the box, in the bedroom — be applied retroactively to characterize what occurred in a different place? That is a rule of status, not a statistical inference.

### Dead Body Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מַחְזְקִינַן? וְהָתְנַן: נָגַע בְּאֶחָד בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אִם חַי אִם מֵת, וּלְמָחָר הִשְׁכִּים וּמְצָאוֹ מֵת — רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְטַהֵר. וַחֲכָמִים מְטַמְּאִין, שֶׁכׇּל הַטֻּמְאוֹת כִּשְׁעַת מְצִיאָתָן. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: כִּשְׁעַת מְצִיאָתָן, וּבִמְקוֹם מְצִיאָתָן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: And do we presume that ritual impurity moved from place to place? But didn't we learn in a mishna: If someone touched one other person at night, and he does not know whether the person he touched was alive or dead, and on the following day he arose and found him dead, and he is uncertain whether or not he contracted ritual impurity from contact with a corpse, Rabbi Meir deems him ritually pure. And the Rabbis deem him ritually impure, as it is presumed that all ritually impure items had already been in the same state as they were at the time they were discovered. And it is taught with regard to this mishna: It is presumed that ritually impure items had been in the same state as they were at the time they were discovered, but only in the place in which they were discovered.

The Gemara challenges Rabbi Yochanan's position from a *Mishnah*. You touched a person at night, not knowing whether the person was alive or dead. The next morning you wake up and find the person is a corpse. Were you *tamei* last night when you touched him? Rebbi Meir rules *tahor*; the *Chachamim* rule *tamei*.

The *Chachamim*'s reasoning: all matters of *tumah* are determined by the status at the time of discovery. The person is found dead now; therefore we assume he was dead last night when you touched him. And attached to this ruling is an important qualification from a *baraita*: not only by the condition at which they are found, but by the *place* at which they are found. If the corpse was in a different location from where the nighttime contact occurred, we would not project the *tumah* backward to the place of contact.

This *baraita* appears to directly support Chizkiyah's position — we do not apply *tumah* from one location to another — and to challenge Rabbi Yochanan. The principle *ein machzikin tumah mimakom l'makom* seems well established.

### Doubtful Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי לִשְׂרוֹף, אֲבָל לִתְלוֹת — תָּלֵינַן? וּמִי תָּלֵינַן?!
>
> **Translation:** And if you would say in response: This statement, that impurity is presumed only in the place in which it was discovered, applies specifically with regard to definite impure status, i.e., to burn *teruma*, but with regard to uncertain impurity, i.e., to suspend the status of *teruma*, we do in fact suspend its status and it may be neither burned nor eaten; is this distinction correct? Do we in fact suspend the status of ritually pure items in such a case, due to the concern that the dead man whom this individual touched might have already been dead in the first location?

The Gemara attempts a reconciliation: perhaps the *baraita* means only that we would not declare the *tahoros* definitively *tamei* in a way that requires them to be burned (*l'isrof*), but we would still suspend them in doubt (*litlot*). The distinction in halachic terminology between *l'isrof* and *litlot* is crucial: *tahoros* that are definitely *tamei* are burned; *tahoros* in doubtful status cannot be eaten (for they might be *tamei*) but cannot be burned either (for they might be *tahor*), and must be set aside until they decompose naturally. Perhaps Rabbi Yochanan only meant that cross-place concern raises a *doubt* — placing the *tahoros* in suspension — while the *baraita* means only that we would not declare them definitively *tamei* in a manner requiring burning.

## Needle Principle

### Moldy Needle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: מַחַט שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת מְלֵאָה חֲלוּדָּה אוֹ שְׁבוּרָה — טְהוֹרָה, שֶׁכׇּל הַטֻּמְאוֹת כִּשְׁעַת מְצִיאָתָן. וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא: הַאי מֵעִיקָּרָא מַחַט מְעַלַּיְיתָא הִיא, וְהַשְׁתָּא הוּא דְּהֶעֱלָה חֲלוּדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** But didn't we learn in a mishna: With regard to a previously impure needle that is found on top of *teruma* and it is full of rust or broken, and therefore no longer contracts or transmit ritual impurity, the *teruma* remains pure, as it is presumed that in all cases of impurity, the items in question had already been in the same state as they were at the time they were discovered? But why should that be the case? Let us say that initially, when it had fallen onto the *teruma*, this needle was a proper, non-rusty and unbroken, needle, capable of contracting and transmitting ritual impurity, and it is only now that rust had formed on it. The status of the *teruma* should at least be held in suspension.

To explore the principle of *shekol hatumaot k'sha'at metzi'atan* further, the Gemara cites a *baraita* involving a needle. A needle found completely covered in rust. A *kli* (vessel or implement) that is broken or rendered entirely unusable can no longer contract *tumah*, and if it was already in that state, it could not transmit *tumah* either. This needle, fully rusted through, is functionally destroyed. It is found resting on top of a pile of *terumah*. The question: must we be concerned that the needle was once intact, contracted *tumah* earlier, fell onto this grain, and only later became completely rusted? The grain is *tahor*.

The Gemara asks the obvious: why not say the needle was originally whole and functional and only now has become fully rusted? The answer lies in the principle: we go by the status at the time of discovery. The needle is found broken; therefore we assume it was in that state when it came into contact with the grain.

### Shekol HaTumaot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תְּנַן: מָצָא שֶׁרֶץ שָׂרוּף עַל גַּבֵּי הַזֵּיתִים, וְכֵן מַטְלִית הַמְהוּמְהָם — טָהוֹר, שֶׁכׇּל הַטֻּמְאוֹת כִּשְׁעַת מְצִיאָתָן!
>
> **Translation:** And furthermore, we learned in a mishna: If one found the carcass of a burned creeping animal on top of a pile of olives, and that animal no longer transmits impurity as it is burned, and similarly if one finds a tattered rag of a *zav*, which likewise no longer transmits impurity, on top of a pile of olives, the olives are pure. The reason is that it is presumed in all cases of impurity, the items in question had already been in the same state as they were at the time they were discovered.

The explicit principle stated here is all matters of *tumah* are determined in accordance with the condition in which they are found at the time of discovery. This applies in both directions: whether strict or lenient. If you find the person dead, you assume he was dead before and you are *tamei*. If you find the needle broken, you assume it was broken before and the *tahoros* are *tahor*. The current status is taken as definitive and applied backward without qualification — not as a *safek*, not as a suspended doubt, but as a settled halachic conclusion.

### Place of Discovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, כִּשְׁעַת מְצִיאָתָן בֵּין לְקוּלָּא בֵּין לְחוּמְרָא, וּבִמְקוֹם מְצִיאָתָן, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם מְצִיאָתָן — מִשְׂרָף לָא שָׂרְפִינַן, מִתְלֵא תָּלֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** And if you would say that there is a difference between these last cases, where the items did not move, and the case of the basket that moved, this distinction is not correct. The suggestion is that in the last cases the items are treated entirely as if they had always been as they were at the time they were discovered, whether this leads to a leniency, as in the cases of the needle and the rag, and whether it leads to a stringency, in the case of one who touched someone at night, when he is considered to be definitely impure, but this is the *halakha* only with regard to the place where they were discovered, i.e., if they did not move. But with regard to a location that is not the place where they were discovered, we do suspect that ritual impurity moved from place to place, and therefore although we do not burn the *teruma* in question, we hold it in suspension.

The Gemara extends this principle: it applies specifically at the *place* of discovery. The Gemara draws the analogy: just as we take the current condition and apply it backward definitively — not merely doubtfully — the same should apply to the place. If the needle is found in one location with *terumah* underneath it, we apply the principle that the needle was broken at that location when it contacted that *terumah*, and the *terumah* is definitively *tahor*. The argument then runs: by analogy, in the case of the box moved from room to room, we should also say definitively *tahor*, not merely *safek*. This would undermine even the compromise position raised at the end of the previous section — that Rabbi Yochanan's view might amount only to placing the *tahoros* in *tiltul* (suspension) rather than declaring them definitely *tamei*.

### Loaf & Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: כִּכָּר עַל גַּבֵּי הַדַּף, וּמַדָּף טָמֵא מוּנָּח תַּחְתָּיו, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאִם נָפְלָה אִי אֶפְשָׁר אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נָגְעָה — טְהוֹרָה, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אָדָם טָהוֹר נִכְנַס לְשָׁם וּנְטָלָהּ, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר: ״בָּרִי לִי שֶׁלֹּא נִכְנַס אָדָם שָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** But didn't we learn in a *baraita*: There was a loaf resting on top of a shelf, and there was an item of light impurity, e.g., a garment of a *zav*, which transmits impurity to food but not to people or vessels, lying underneath it, and the loaf was later found on the ground. Even though the situation was such that if the loaf fell to the ground it would be impossible for it to have done anything other than touch the impure garment on its way down, nevertheless the loaf is pure. The reason is that I say that a ritually pure man entered there and took it off the shelf and placed it onto the ground without it touching the impure garment. This ruling applies unless someone says: It is clear to me that no person entered there.

To probe further, the Gemara introduces a *baraita*: a loaf of bread (*terumah*) placed on top of a shelf. Beneath the shelf lies a *tamei* garment — specifically, one referred to as *madaf*, the detailed nature of which we will return to shortly. Even though if the loaf fell off the shelf by itself, it would almost certainly have contacted the *tamei* garment on the way down. Nevertheless: the loaf is *tahor*. The loaf is found not on the garment but elsewhere; it did not come into contact with the *tamei* garment as far as we can establish.

This is a striking ruling. The most physically reasonable assumption — that the loaf fell, struck the garment, and rolled away — is set aside in favor of a *tahor* ruling. Why?

### Person Moved It

A *tahor* person entered the area and moved the loaf before it could fall. This is the operating assumption that preserves the loaf's *tahor* status, and it drives the principle that we make whatever reasonable assumption we can to uphold the current status of the object as found.

The Gemara qualifies: this only works unless the owner declares with certainty that no person entered the area. In that case, the loaf would be *tamei*, because the only remaining explanation for its displacement is that it fell on its own onto the *tamei* garment.

Tosafot notes that there may be a particularly strong reason to invoke the "a person moved it" assumption here: a *tahor* person seeing a loaf of *terumah* near a *tamei* garment would have a specific motivation to move it and prevent it from becoming *tamei*. This targeted rationale, Tosafot suggests, strengthens the inference beyond a generic "someone might have moved it."

Rabbi Elazar adds: this ruling is needed specifically for the case where the shelf is on an incline, where the loaf would very likely roll off on its own. Even there, we still assume a person moved it, as long as we cannot rule that out with certainty. The Gemara affirms: we go by the way things are found. The loaf is found not on the garment; we assume it was *tahor* at the time of discovery, and we use whatever assumptions are available to support that.

The Gemara then explicitly connects this to the *mimakom le'makom* principle. The garment is *tamei* "here," beneath the shelf. The loaf is found "there," on the floor away from the garment. We do not carry the *tumah* from the garment's location and apply it to the loaf's location. *Lo machzikin tumah mimakom le'makom* is thus operative here — though the Gemara notes that this case has an additional feature: the possible intentional intervention of a person. A person, who acts with intention and purpose, can be invoked as an explanatory factor. A raven, which acts randomly and without intention, cannot similarly be invoked to explain how a *sheretz* might have landed in a barrel from the outside. That distinction limits how far the "someone moved it" logic can be extended.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אָדָם טָהוֹר נִכְנַס לְשָׁם וּנְטָלָהּ. הָכָא נָמֵי נֵימָא: עוֹרֵב אֲתָא וּשְׁדָא! אָדָם דִּבְכַוָּנָה — אָמְרִינַן, עוֹרֵב דְּשֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּנָה — לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that no proof can be cited from there, as that *baraita* explicitly teaches the reason for its ruling: The reason is that I say that a ritually pure man entered there and took it off the shelf and placed it onto the ground, avoiding the impure garment it would have hit had it fallen. Here, too, let us say that a raven came and threw the creeping animal into the basket when it was in the second corner, after the produce had been emptied from it. In the case of a person, who acts with intent, we can say that perhaps a person moved the loaf from the shelf onto the ground. By contrast, with regard to a raven, which does not act with intent, we do not say that perhaps it committed such a purposeful act.

Returning to the main thread: the debate between Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan over place-to-place application of *tumah* thus remains open. The various *baraitot* and *mishnayot* cited suggest that as a general rule we do not apply *tumah* definitively from one place to another, but whether we would place it in suspension (*litlot*) or declare it definitively *tahor* is the contested point, and the special circumstance of a possible human actor provides one way to explain why certain cases come out as definitively *tahor* without settling the broader question.

## Private Domain Doubt

### Private Domain Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי הַאי כִּכָּר סְפֵק טוּמְאָה בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד הוּא, וְכֹל סְפֵק טוּמְאָה בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — סְפֵקָהּ טָמֵא, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל, בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — סְפֵקוֹ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: Now the status of this loaf found on the ground is one of uncertain impurity found in a private domain, and the guiding principle in any case of uncertainty involving impurity in a private domain is that the item with uncertain status is deemed impure. If so, shouldn't the loaf be deemed impure? The Gemara answers: No, as this is an entity that lacks consciousness in order for it to be asked, and the guiding principle is that with regard to any entity that lacks consciousness in order for it to be asked, whether it is found in a public domain or in a private domain, the item with uncertain status is deemed pure.

The Gemara now raises a further complication regarding the loaf of bread on the shelf. This loaf of bread, if it fell within one's private house, presents a case of doubtful *tumah* in a *reshut hayachid* (private domain). The established rule is: any doubtful *tumah* in a private domain is treated as definitely *tamei*. Why, then, is the loaf ruled *tahor*?

## Three Positions

### Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה וְלֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה, לֹא כְּדִבְרֵי שַׁמַּאי — שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה סְיָיג לִדְבָרָיו, וְלֹא כְּדִבְרֵי הִלֵּל — שֶׁהִפְרִיז עַל מִדּוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis say: The *halakha* is neither in accordance with the statement of this *tanna* nor in accordance with the statement of that *tanna*. It is not in accordance with the statement of Shammai, as he did not enact any safeguard for his statement. And it is not in accordance with the statement of Hillel, as he went beyond his bounds with his safeguard.

Before completing the analysis of the private domain exception, it is worth noting that all of this extensive discussion of boxes, needles, garments, and places has been running parallel to the central question of the tractate: the retroactive *tumah* status of the *niddah*. The three positions — those of Shammai, Hillel, and the *Chachamim* — now receive explicit statement. Shammai holds that a woman is *temeiah* only from the moment she discovers the blood. She cannot be assumed to have been *temeiah* beforehand; the discovery itself marks the onset of her *tumah*.

## Private Domain Doubt…

### Sotah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָכָא בְּטוּמְאָה דְרַבָּנַן. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי ״מַדָּף״, כְּדִכְתִיב ״עָלֶה נִדָּף״.
>
> **Translation:** And if you wish, say instead: That principle, that in any case of uncertainty involving impurity in a private domain the item with uncertain status is deemed impure, applies to a case of impurity by Torah law, whereas here we are dealing with ritual impurity by rabbinic law. The Gemara adds: The language of the *Tosefta* is also precise, as it teaches: There was a loaf resting on top of a shelf, and there was an item of light impurity [*madaf*] lying underneath it. The term *madaf* is similar to that which is written: "A driven leaf [*niddaf*]" (Leviticus 26:36), i.e., a light item. Likewise, the *Tosefta* is referring to a light, or rabbinic, impurity.

The Gemara answers that the principle of doubtful *tumah* in a private domain being *tamei* is derived from the *sotah* case — the suspected adulteress who enters seclusion with a man after being warned. That seclusion is analogized to a *reshut hayachid*, and the *halacha* that she is considered forbidden to her husband in a definitive rather than merely doubtful sense is the model for the broader rule. But the *sotah* case has a critical feature: she is a person capable of being asked. She knows what happened and can be questioned.

The logic underlying the rule that doubtful *tumah* in a private domain is *tamei* thus carries with it an implicit condition: it applies only where there are people involved who are capable of being questioned. The ruling of definite *tumah* functions, in a sense, as a halachic instrument to press for disclosure and resolution.

## Three Positions…

### Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מֵעֵת לְעֵת מְמַעֶטֶת עַל יָד מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה, וּמִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה מְמַעֶטֶת עַל יָד מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, a twenty-four-hour period reduces the time from examination to examination, i.e., if her most recent self-examination took place more than twenty-four hours earlier, she need concern herself with ritual impurity only for the twenty-four-hour period prior to discerning the blood. And from examination to examination reduces the time from a twenty-four-hour period, i.e., if she examined herself in the course of the previous day and discovered no blood, she was definitely pure prior to the examination.

Hillel's position stands at the opposite extreme from Shammai. A woman is retroactively *temeiah* all the way back to the last time she performed a bodily examination (*bedikah*). However long ago that was — even days — all *tahoros* she handled in the intervening period are *tamei*. The Gemara characterizes Hillel as having gone to excess, since this could push the retroactive *tumah* back a week or more.

## Private Domain Doubt……

### No Da'at

The loaf of bread and the worn garment beneath it are objects. They cannot be interrogated. There is no person involved whose testimony or self-knowledge might resolve the doubt. Because this is something that cannot be asked, the rule of doubtful *tumah* in a private domain simply does not apply. Anything incapable of being asked, whether in a public domain or a private one, its doubtful status is *tahor*.

## Three Positions……

### Sages Middle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעֵת לְעֵת מְמַעֶטֶת עַל יָד מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה, כֵּיצַד? בָּדְקָה עַצְמָהּ בְּאֶחָד בַּשַּׁבָּת וּמָצָאת טְהוֹרָה, וְיָשְׁבָה שֵׁנִי וּשְׁלִישִׁי וְלֹא בָּדְקָה, וְלִרְבִיעִי בָּדְקָה וּמָצְאָה טְמֵאָה — אֵין אוֹמְרִים תְּטַמֵּא מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה, אֶלָּא מֵעֵת לְעֵת. וּמִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה מְמַעֶטֶת עַל יָד מֵעֵת לְעֵת, כֵּיצַד? בָּדְקָה עַצְמָהּ בְּשָׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וּמָצָאת טְהוֹרָה, וְיָשְׁבָה לָהּ שְׁנִיָּה וּשְׁלִישִׁית וְלֹא בָּדְקָה, וְלִרְבִיעִית בָּדְקָה וּמָצְאָה טְמֵאָה — אֵין אוֹמְרִים תְּטַמֵּא מֵעֵת לְעֵת, אֶלָּא מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* elaborates: A twenty-four-hour period reduces the time from examination to examination, how so? A woman examined herself on Sunday and found that she was ritually pure, and then she sat through Monday and Tuesday and did not examine herself. And then on Wednesday she examined herself and found that she was impure. In such a case we do not say that she should be impure retroactively from the time of this examination extending back until the time of her most recent examination. Rather, she is impure retroactively for a twenty-four-hour period. And from examination to examination reduces the time from a twenty-four-hour period, how so? A woman examined herself in the first hour of a day and found that she was ritually pure, and then she sat through the second and third hours of the day and did not examine herself. And then at the fourth hour she examined herself and found that she was impure. In such a case we do not say that she should be impure retroactively for a twenty-four-hour period. Rather, she is impure retroactively from the time of this examination extending back until the time of her most recent examination, three hours earlier.

The *Chachamim* stake out the middle position: the retroactive *tumah* extends back the lesser of two limits: 24 hours or the last time the woman performed a *bedikah* and found herself *tehorah*. Each limit constrains the other.

## Private Domain Doubt………

### Rabbinic Tumaot

An alternative explanation is offered for why the loaf of bread in the private domain is *tahor* despite the general rule. The entire case may involve only rabbinic *tumah*, not biblical. The garment referred to in the *baraita* as *madaf* — the Gemara derives this from the verse *aleh nidaf*, a fluttering or flimsy leaf — refers to a garment that is treated as *tamei* only rabbinically. One example: garments of *amei ha'aretz*, those not careful about *taharah* even for *chullin*, are treated as *tamei* for those who eat their *chullin b'taharah*, and those who eat *chullin b'taharah* have their garments treated as *tamei* for *kohanim* eating *terumah*. These are entirely rabbinic hierarchies of *tumah*. Since the underlying *tumah* in this case is only rabbinic, the ruling is correspondingly lenient.

## Three Positions………

### 24 Hour Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּדְקָה עַצְמָהּ בְּאֶחָד בַּשַּׁבָּת וּמָצָאת טְהוֹרָה, וְיָשְׁבָה שֵׁנִי וּשְׁלִישִׁי וְלֹא בָּדְקָה, וּבִרְבִיעִי בָּדְקָה וּמָצְאָה טְמֵאָה — אֵין אוֹמְרִים תְּטַמֵּא מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה, אֶלָּא מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** A woman examined herself on Sunday and found that she was ritually pure, and then she sat through Monday and Tuesday and did not examine herself. And then on Wednesday she examined herself and found that she was impure. In such a case we do not say that she should be impure retroactively from the time of this examination extending back until the time of her most recent examination. Rather, she is impure retroactively for a twenty-four-hour period.

She examined herself on Sunday evening and found herself *tehorah*. Monday and Tuesday she did not examine herself. On Wednesday she examined herself and found that she was a *niddah*. In this case, we do not say she is *temeiah* all the way back to the last time she checked (Sunday). Only 24 hours back from the discovery on Wednesday.

### Last Check Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּדְקָה עַצְמָהּ בְּשָׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וּמָצָאת טְהוֹרָה, וְיָשְׁבָה לָהּ שְׁנִיָּה וּשְׁלִישִׁית וְלֹא בָּדְקָה, וְלִרְבִיעִית בָּדְקָה וּמָצְאָה טְמֵאָה — אֵין אוֹמְרִים תְּטַמֵּא מֵעֵת לְעֵת, אֶלָּא מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה.
>
> **Translation:** A woman examined herself in the first hour of a day and found that she was ritually pure, and then she sat through the second and third hours of the day and did not examine herself. And then at the fourth hour she examined herself and found that she was impure. In such a case we do not say that she should be impure retroactively for a twenty-four-hour period. Rather, she is impure retroactively from the time of this examination extending back until the time of her most recent examination, three hours earlier.

She examined herself at the first hour (approximately 6:00 AM) and found herself *tehorah*. She did not examine herself at the second or third hour. At the fourth hour (9:00 AM) she examined herself and found she was *temeiah*. In this case, we do not go back 24 hours, because the last time she checked, only three hours earlier, sets the outer limit of retroactive *tumah*.

### Both Limits Need

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! כֵּיוָן דְּבָדְקָה עַצְמָהּ בְּשָׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וּמָצָאת טְהוֹרָה, לָא מְטַמֵּינַן לַהּ מֵעֵת לְעֵת. אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא מֵעֵת לְעֵת מְמַעֶטֶת עַל יָד מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה, תְּנָא נָמֵי מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה מְמַעֶטֶת עַל יָד מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: This *halakha* is obvious. Since she examined herself at the first hour and found that she was pure, there is no reason to render her impure retroactively for a twenty-four-hour period. Why does the *baraita* state such an obvious *halakha*? The Gemara answers: Since the *baraita* taught that according to the Rabbis a twenty-four-hour period reduces the time from examination to examination, it also taught the parallel case, that from examination to examination reduces the time from a twenty-four-hour period, despite the fact that this *halakha* is obvious.

The Gemara notes that one of these two examples appears self-evident. In the case where she checked at 6:00 AM and found *tumah* at 9:00 AM, obviously we do not go back 24 hours; there is a confirmed *bedikah* showing she was *tehorah* just three hours earlier. Is it necessary to state this?

The Gemara answers: because it was necessary to state the first rule (that 24 hours limits the *mipkidah l'fkidah* in the case where she went days without checking), the *tanna* also stated the complementary rule, that when she checked recently, the *bedikah* limits the 24-hour window, even though that second case might have been derivable from the first. The two limits are stated in tandem because each is needed to define the other: 24 hours limits how far back a *mipkidah l'fkidah* can reach, and a *mipkidah l'fkidah* limits how far back the 24-hour window extends. Together they define the *Chachamim*'s middle position.
