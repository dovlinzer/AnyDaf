# Niddah 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Box & Rodent Case

### Box Setup

The Gemara on the bottom of 3b presents an Amoraic dispute that echoes a case already introduced in the discussion of Hillel and Shammai. The case involves a *kuppa* — a box or basket — that was used to store *tahoros*, ritually pure food, in one section or corner. Subsequently, after the contents were removed or the box emptied, a dead *sheretz* (rodent) was discovered in a different corner. The central question is whether we must be concerned that this *sheretz* was already present when the *tahoros* were being handled, retroactively rendering them *tamei*, or whether we may assume the *sheretz* arrived only afterward.

### Hillel & Shammai

The Gemara first establishes a point of agreement: *beshammai veHillel modu bekuppa sheyesh lah shulayim* — both Shammai and Hillel concur that when the box has a floor (*shulayim*), we must be concerned that the *sheretz* was there earlier and that the prior *tahoros* are retroactively *tamei*. This was, in fact, the very case Hillel cited when he pressed Shammai during their famous exchange. Shammai had accepted the argument in principle — a *sheretz* found in a floored box may well have been resting there unnoticed throughout — but distinguished it from the case of the woman by noting that a woman has no such "floor" to retain the blood. So Shammai and Hillel agreed on the box with a floor; what remains is to understand where Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan enter the picture.

### Chizkiyah vs. Yochanan

*Ki pligi Chizkiya veRebbe Yochanan bekuppa she'ein lah shulayim* — their dispute concerns a box without a floor. The practical implication of such a configuration is that in order to retrieve the contents, one would have to empty or pour everything out completely. In such a case, the logic runs: had the *sheretz* been present all along, it would have come out when the box was fully emptied. Since it was not seen then, one could reasonably conclude it only arrived later. Chizkiyah therefore rules that the prior *tahoros* are *tahor* — we need not be concerned retroactively.

The Gemara immediately asks: *ein lah shulayim mai ta'amei deRebbe Yochanan?* If there is no floor and everything necessarily empties out, why would Rabbi Yochanan still be concerned that the *sheretz* was there earlier? The answer: *ein lah shulayim veyesh lah oznayim* — the box lacks a floor but possesses a lip (*oznayim*), a rim curled inward. It is therefore possible that when the contents were poured out, the *sheretz* became lodged in that lip, unnoticed, and later fell back to the bottom of the box when it was righted. Rabbi Yochanan holds that this possibility is sufficient to generate retroactive concern; Chizkiyah disagrees.

### Barrel & Buckets

The Gemara now cites a *baraita* that makes this scenario considerably clearer. *Ha-medaleh asar dli-im mayim be-zeh achar zeh* — one who draws water from a well using successive buckets, each poured into a large barrel, ten times in sequence. When filling bucket number ten, a dead *sheretz* is discovered inside that bucket. The bucket itself is obviously *tamei*. But what of the water already collected in the barrel? *Ve-kulan tehorin* — all of it is *tahor*, because the logic is straightforward: had the *sheretz* been present in an earlier bucket, it would have been poured into the barrel along with the water. The fact that it is found still sitting in the bucket demonstrates it could not have been there during earlier pours.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish, citing Rabbi Yannai, introduces a qualification: *lo shanu ela she-ein lo oznayim* — the ruling that everything is *tahor* applies only if the bucket lacks a lip. *Aval yesh lo oznayim kulan tme'in* — but if the bucket has an inward-curling lip, then one must be concerned that all the water drawn from previous buckets is *tamei*, since the *sheretz* may have been caught in the lip all along and not poured into the barrel each time.

This naturally raises a tension: Chizkiyah, in the case of the box with a lip, ruled *tahor*, whereas Rabbi Yannai (as cited by Reish Lakish) in the case of a bucket with a lip rules *tamei*. Perhaps, the Gemara suggests, they simply disagree.

### Water vs. Fruit

The Gemara attempts to distinguish the two cases rather than leave them in simple disagreement. *Maya sharki peiri lo sharki* — water slides; solid contents do not. Water flows out with minimal tilting; a quick pour is sufficient to empty a bucket of water and a *sheretz* lodged in the lip might plausibly remain. By contrast, emptying a box of grain, flour, or fruit requires vigorous shaking and a far more thorough inversion, making it far less likely that something caught in a lip would remain behind. Thus Chizkiyah, dealing with solid contents, is not concerned that the *sheretz* was caught in the lip, whereas Rabbi Yannai's ruling about water is entirely consistent.

An alternative explanation is offered as well: *maya lo kapdi aleihu peiri kapdi aleihu* — with water, one is not meticulous about getting out every last drop, so one might well leave a small amount behind, and with it a *sheretz* that happened to be there. With solid food, however, one is careful to extract everything and shakes the container accordingly, making it highly unlikely anything was left behind. The two explanations complement one another and successfully reconcile Chizkiyah's ruling with Rabbi Yannai's.

## Box Applied to Woman

### Unchecked Box

Having analyzed the box-and-rodent case in its various dimensions, the Gemara turns to another distinction that connects directly to the case of the woman. *Keivan d'shchichi bah damim k'she'einah bedukah damya* — because menstruation is prevalent in women, her status is comparable to that of a box that has not been checked before use. This comparison is made in the context of establishing when Hillel and Shammai agree versus when Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan debate. According to one answer offered by the Gemara, Hillel and Shammai agree that the prior *tahoros* are *tamei* specifically when the box was not inspected before use — for in that case there was never a confirmed *status quo* of being free from *sheretz*. Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan debate the case when the box *was* checked (*bekuppah bedukah*) prior to use.

### Chazakah Liable Change

This brings us back to the woman, and the Gemara's formulation is striking. When Hillel and Shammai compared their box case to the case of a woman who discovered blood, the Gemara asks: *v'ha'ishah bedukah hi* — is a woman not like the checked box? She was known to be *tehorah* at some prior point; she has an established *status quo*. So why should she be treated like an unchecked box?

The answer: *keivan d'shchichi bah damim k'she'einah bedukah damya* — since blood is prevalent in a woman, since menstruation is a regular, recurring reality, the prior *status quo* of *taharah* is rendered conceptually unstable. In more formal terms, hers is a *chazakah ha'asuyah l'hishtanos* — a *chazakah*, a status quo, that is by its very nature in the process of changing, likely to shift at any time. A *chazakah* only reliably carries forward into the future and backwards to explain the past when the status it reflects is a stable one — when, in other words, there is no particular reason to expect it to change. The woman's status does not enjoy that stability, and therefore even though she is in a sense "checked," her case is more akin to the unchecked box for purposes of this analysis.

It is worth pausing here to note that this analysis operates not on the basis of raw probability — *rov* is not mentioned — but on the basis of formal rules about the reliability of a *chazakah*. A standard *chazakah* allows one to say: "What is true today was likely true yesterday, and the day before." A *chazakah ha'asuyah l'hishtanos* cannot sustain that inference with the same force. And this remains a purely formal principle, analogous in a certain sense to what philosophers call the problem of induction — the inference that the future will resemble the past has no strictly logical foundation, yet it is the basis on which we conduct our lives, and the *chazakah* functions in precisely that manner as a halachic instrument.

### Covered Box

The Gemara offers yet another way of distinguishing the case where Hillel and Shammai agree from the case where Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan debate. *Ki modu Shammai veHillel bekuppa she'einah mechusah* — Hillel and Shammai agree that the prior *tahoros* are *tamei* when the box was not covered, meaning there was no active protection against a *sheretz* entering. *Pligi Chizkiya veRebbe Yochanan bekuppa mechusah* — Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan debate the case of a box that was used with a cover; it is not sealed absolutely at all times, but when not actively in use, it is kept closed.

This covered box represents the stronger end of the *chazakah* spectrum. Rather than a mere passive *status quo* — "as far as we know, no *sheretz* has entered" — the covered box has an active protection operating to preserve its *tahor* status. There is something positively working to maintain the state of affairs, not merely an absence of known disruption. And therefore in that case, at minimum, one might say the prior *tahoros* are *tahor*, or at least that there is room to debate. In the uncovered box, there is no such protection, and both Hillel and Shammai concede the concern is well-founded.

### Woman Covered Concept

The Gemara now returns to the comparison: *v'hadumya d'isha katani* — but Hillel and Shammai explicitly compared their case to the woman, implying the woman corresponds to the uncovered box. Yet the Gemara observes: *d'isha mechusah hi* — a woman is "covered."

Rashi interprets this as a physical observation: just as a *sheretz* cannot enter a box from outside if the box is covered, nothing foreign enters the woman's body. But Tosafot finds this reading untenable, since a woman's *tumah* arises from her own blood, not from any external contamination. Tosafot therefore explains "covered" in terms of the position of Shammai himself: the blood becomes *tamei* only once it crosses the cervix into the vaginal canal, and the cervix is in a sense "closed," functioning like a cover. More broadly, this maps onto Shammai's earlier argument that a woman senses when she is beginning to menstruate, and that sensory awareness serves as a kind of protection of her prior status — analogous to the cover that prevents a *sheretz* from entering undetected.

If so, Hillel, who does not accept Shammai's experiential argument, would still agree that the box case is *tamei*, and that the same applies to the woman — and that the "covered" quality only appears to protect her status without actually doing so. Why? *Keivan d'shchichi bah damim ke'eino mechuso damya* — because menstruation is so common, the theoretical "coverage" is counterbalanced and overridden by the statistical reality of frequent bleeding. The idea that she will sense the onset provides some analogy to a covered box, but the prevalence of menstruation neutralizes that advantage, rendering her status effectively equivalent to an uncovered box — and in an uncovered box, both Hillel and Shammai agree that the prior *tahoros* are *tamei*.

### Woman Objectified

Finally, the Gemara consolidates the comparison by suggesting that a woman is *k'kuppa she'yesh lah sheretz bifnim* — like a box with a dead *sheretz* inside of it. This is a point worth naming directly. The entire sustained discussion of this daf has involved objectifying the case of the woman — treating her as though she were a *mikvah* with a leak, a barrel of wine turning to vinegar, or a box containing a *sheretz*. These comparisons are not made with any pejorative intent as a matter of Talmudic method; they arise naturally from the process of abstraction, of identifying the formal halachic structure underlying a case and then comparing it to other cases with the same structure. Nevertheless, once those comparisons are made, the associations exist and carry their own weight, and that remains a genuine challenge that should be acknowledged.

## Place-to-Place Transfer

### Box Moved Rooms

The Gemara now pivots to a different variant of the box-and-*sheretz* case. *Kuppa she'nishtamshu bah taharos be'zavit bayit zu* — a box was being used to handle *tahoros* in one corner of a house — say, the living room. *Ve'tilteluha be'zavit acheres* — it was then moved to a different corner, or to another room entirely, the bedroom. *Ve'nimtza sheretz be'zavit acheres* — and now, with the box sitting in the bedroom, a dead *sheretz* is discovered inside it. The question is whether the fact that we find the *sheretz* in the box now, in the bedroom, gives rise to retroactive concern about what happened earlier when the box was in the living room with the *tahoros*.

### Chizkiyah No Concern

Chizkiyah rules: *lo machzikinan tuma mi'makom le'makom* — we do not establish, or apply, the status of *tumah* from one location to another. Since the *sheretz* is found in the box now in the bedroom, we cannot project that status backward to when the box was in the living room. The prior use of the *tahoros* remains *tahor*.

### Yochanan Applies

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *machzikinan* — we do apply the status from one place to another. The fact that we find the *sheretz* in the box now is sufficient cause to be concerned that it was present earlier, regardless of which room the box happened to be in at the time. The physical relocation of the box does not break the inferential link between the current status and the prior one.

It is worth emphasizing the formal character of this dispute. It is not purely a matter of probability — one might construct scenarios in which rodents are more prevalent in the bedroom than the living room, or vice versa, and that would affect the likelihood of the *sheretz* having been present in either location. But the dispute of Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan is not framed in those terms. It is a formal question: can the current status — *sheretz* in the box, in the bedroom — be applied retroactively to characterize what occurred in a different place? That is a rule of status, not a statistical inference.

### Dead Body Baraita

The Gemara challenges Rabbi Yochanan's position from a *Mishnah*. *Taharos nig'a be'echad ba'layla* — you touched a person at night, not knowing whether the person was alive or dead. *Ve'la'machar hishkim u'metza'o mes* — the next morning you wake up and find the person is a corpse. Were you *tamei* last night when you touched him? Rebbi Meir rules *tahor*; the *Chachamim* rule *tamei*.

The *Chachamim*'s reasoning: *shekol hatumaot k'sha'at metzi'atan* — all matters of *tumah* are determined by the status at the time of discovery. The person is found dead now; therefore we assume he was dead last night when you touched him. And attached to this ruling is an important qualification from a *baraita*: *k'sha'at metzi'atan u'vimkom metzi'atan* — not only by the condition at which they are found, but by the *place* at which they are found. If the corpse was in a different location from where the nighttime contact occurred, we would not project the *tumah* backward to the place of contact.

This *baraita* appears to directly support Chizkiyah's position — we do not apply *tumah* from one location to another — and to challenge Rabbi Yochanan. The principle *ein machzikin tumah mimakom l'makom* seems well established.

### Doubtful Status

The Gemara attempts a reconciliation: *v'chi teima hanei milei l'isrof* — perhaps the *baraita* means only that we would not declare the *tahoros* definitively *tamei* in a way that requires them to be burned (*l'isrof*), but *aval litlot talinan* — we would still suspend them in doubt (*litlot*). The distinction in halachic terminology between *l'isrof* and *litlot* is crucial: *tahoros* that are definitely *tamei* are burned; *tahoros* in doubtful status cannot be eaten (for they might be *tamei*) but cannot be burned either (for they might be *tahor*), and must be set aside until they decompose naturally. Perhaps Rabbi Yochanan only meant that cross-place concern raises a *doubt* — placing the *tahoros* in suspension — while the *baraita* means only that we would not declare them definitively *tamei* in a manner requiring burning.

## Needle Principle

### Moldy Needle

To explore the principle of *shekol hatumaot k'sha'at metzi'atan* further, the Gemara cites a *baraita* involving a needle. *Machat shenimtzeit meleiah chaludah* — a needle found completely covered in rust. A *kli* (vessel or implement) that is broken or rendered entirely unusable can no longer contract *tumah*, and if it was already in that state, it could not transmit *tumah* either. This needle, fully rusted through, is functionally destroyed. It is found resting on top of a pile of *terumah*. The question: must we be concerned that the needle was once intact, contracted *tumah* earlier, fell onto this grain, and only later became completely rusted? *Tehorah* — the grain is *tahor*.

The Gemara asks the obvious: *v'amai leima hai me'ikara machat ma'alyata hi v'hashta hu deha'alah chaludah* — why not say the needle was originally whole and functional and only now has become fully rusted? The answer lies in the principle: we go by the status at the time of discovery. The needle is found broken; therefore we assume it was in that state when it came into contact with the grain.

### Shekol HaTumaot

The explicit principle stated here is *shekol hatme'os k'sha'as m'tzi'asan* — all matters of *tumah* are determined in accordance with the condition in which they are found at the time of discovery. This applies in both directions: whether strict or lenient. If you find the person dead, you assume he was dead before and you are *tamei*. If you find the needle broken, you assume it was broken before and the *tahoros* are *tahor*. The current status is taken as definitive and applied backward without qualification — not as a *safek*, not as a suspended doubt, but as a settled halachic conclusion.

### Place of Discovery

The Gemara extends this principle: *u'vimkom metzi'atan* — and it applies specifically at the *place* of discovery. The Gemara draws the analogy: just as we take the current condition and apply it backward definitively — not merely doubtfully — the same should apply to the place. If the needle is found in one location with *terumah* underneath it, we apply the principle that the needle was broken at that location when it contacted that *terumah*, and the *terumah* is definitively *tahor*. The argument then runs: by analogy, in the case of the box moved from room to room, we should also say definitively *tahor*, not merely *safek*. This would undermine even the compromise position raised at the end of the previous section — that Rabbi Yochanan's view might amount only to placing the *tahoros* in *tiltul* (suspension) rather than declaring them definitely *tamei*.

The Gemara cites another case from the *Mishnah*: *matza sheretz saruf al gabei hazeisim* — a burnt *sheretz* found on top of a pile of olives. A burnt *sheretz* no longer causes *tumah*. *Tehorah* — the olives are *tahor*, by the same principle: found burnt, assumed to have been burnt when it contacted the olives. Similarly, a thoroughly worn and unusable garment — *matlit hamehumemet* — found on top of *tahoros* does not render them *tamei*: *shekol hatme'os k'sha'as m'tzi'asan*.

The Gemara then makes the concession: *k'sha'as m'tzi'asan bein l'kula bein l'chumra* — yes, by the *condition* at which something is found, we rule consistently, whether strictly or leniently, definitively in either direction. *Aval shelo bimkom metzi'atan* — but when the question involves a *different location* from where the item was found, it remains possible to say *misraf lo sarfinan, matla talinan* — we would not burn the *terumah* definitively, but we might still place it in suspension as a doubtful case. The principle of *shekol hatumaot k'sha'at metzi'atan* applies definitively to condition; the question of place-to-place may still admit of a middle position between definitive *tahor* and definitive *tamei*.

### Loaf & Garment

To probe further, the Gemara introduces a *baraita*: *kikar al gabei ha-daf* — a loaf of bread (*terumah*) placed on top of a shelf. Beneath the shelf lies a *tamei* garment — specifically, one referred to as *madaf*, the detailed nature of which we will return to shortly. *Af al pi she-im naflah i efshar ela im kein nag'ah* — even though if the loaf fell off the shelf by itself, it would almost certainly have contacted the *tamei* garment on the way down. Nevertheless: *tehorah* — the loaf is *tahor*. The loaf is found not on the garment but elsewhere; it did not come into contact with the *tamei* garment as far as we can establish.

This is a striking ruling. The most physically reasonable assumption — that the loaf fell, struck the garment, and rolled away — is set aside in favor of a *tahor* ruling. Why?

### Person Moved It

*She-ani omer adam tahor nichnas le-sham u-netalah* — I will say that a *tahor* person entered the area and moved the loaf before it could fall. This is the operating assumption that preserves the loaf's *tahor* status, and it drives the principle that we make whatever reasonable assumption we can to uphold the current status of the object as found.

The Gemara qualifies: *ad she-yomar bari li she-lo nichnas adam le-sham* — this only works unless the owner declares with certainty that no person entered the area. In that case, the loaf would be *tamei*, because the only remaining explanation for its displacement is that it fell on its own onto the *tamei* garment.

Tosafot notes that there may be a particularly strong reason to invoke the "a person moved it" assumption here: a *tahor* person seeing a loaf of *terumah* near a *tamei* garment would have a specific motivation to move it and prevent it from becoming *tamei*. This targeted rationale, Tosafot suggests, strengthens the inference beyond a generic "someone might have moved it."

Rabbi Elazar adds: *lo nitzrecha ela le-makom madru'ah* — this ruling is needed specifically for the case where the shelf is on an incline, where the loaf would very likely roll off on its own. Even there, we still assume a person moved it, as long as we cannot rule that out with certainty. The Gemara affirms: *ki kol ha-tamei'ot ke-sha'at metzi'atan* — we go by the way things are found. The loaf is found not on the garment; we assume it was *tahor* at the time of discovery, and we use whatever assumptions are available to support that.

The Gemara then explicitly connects this to the *mimakom le'makom* principle. The garment is *tamei* "here," beneath the shelf. The loaf is found "there," on the floor away from the garment. We do not carry the *tumah* from the garment's location and apply it to the loaf's location. *Lo machzikin tumah mimakom le'makom* is thus operative here — though the Gemara notes that this case has an additional feature: the possible intentional intervention of a person. *Adam d'bikvanta amrinan* — a person, who acts with intention and purpose, can be invoked as an explanatory factor. *Orev d'shelo bikvanta lo amrinan* — a raven, which acts randomly and without intention, cannot similarly be invoked to explain how a *sheretz* might have landed in a barrel from the outside. That distinction limits how far the "someone moved it" logic can be extended.

Returning to the main thread: the debate between Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan over place-to-place application of *tumah* thus remains open. The various *baraitot* and *mishnayot* cited suggest that as a general rule we do not apply *tumah* definitively from one place to another, but whether we would place it in suspension (*litlot*) or declare it definitively *tahor* is the contested point, and the special circumstance of a possible human actor provides one way to explain why certain cases come out as definitively *tahor* without settling the broader question.

## Private Domain Doub (III)

### Private Domain Rule

The Gemara now raises a further complication regarding the loaf of bread on the shelf. *Lama michti hai kikar safek tumah b'reshut hayachid hi* — this loaf of bread, if it fell within one's private house, presents a case of doubtful *tumah* in a *reshut hayachid* (private domain). The established rule is: *v'chol safek tumah b'reshut hayachid safko tamei* — any doubtful *tumah* in a private domain is treated as definitely *tamei*. Why, then, is the loaf ruled *tahor*?

---

## Three Positions (IV)

### Shammai

Before completing the analysis of the private domain exception, it is worth noting that all of this extensive discussion of boxes, needles, garments, and places has been running parallel to the central question of the tractate: the retroactive *tumah* status of the *niddah*. The three positions — those of Shammai, Hillel, and the *Chachamim* — now receive explicit statement. Shammai holds that a woman is *temeiah* only from the moment she discovers the blood. She cannot be assumed to have been *temeiah* beforehand; the discovery itself marks the onset of her *tumah*.

---

## Private Domain Doub (III)

### Sotah Exception

The Gemara answers that the principle of doubtful *tumah* in a private domain being *tamei* is derived from the *sotah* case — the suspected adulteress who enters seclusion with a man after being warned. That seclusion is analogized to a *reshut hayachid*, and the *halacha* that she is considered forbidden to her husband in a definitive rather than merely doubtful sense is the model for the broader rule. But the *sotah* case has a critical feature: *yesh lah da'at lishol* — she is a person capable of being asked. She knows what happened and can be questioned.

The logic underlying the rule that doubtful *tumah* in a private domain is *tamei* thus carries with it an implicit condition: it applies only where there are people involved who are capable of being questioned — *yesh bo da'at lishol*. The ruling of definite *tumah* functions, in a sense, as a halachic instrument to press for disclosure and resolution.

---

## Three Positions (IV) (II)

### Hillel

Hillel's position stands at the opposite extreme from Shammai. A woman is retroactively *temeiah* all the way back to the last time she performed a bodily examination (*bedikah*). However long ago that was — even days — all *tahoros* she handled in the intervening period are *tamei*. *Velo k'divrei Hillel shehifriz al midotav* — the Gemara characterizes Hillel as having gone to excess, since this could push the retroactive *tumah* back a week or more.

---

## Private Domain Doub (III)t…

### No Da'at

The loaf of bread and the worn garment beneath it are objects. They cannot be interrogated. There is no person involved whose testimony or self-knowledge might resolve the doubt. *Mishum dehavi davar she'ein bo da'at lishol* — because this is something that cannot be asked, the rule of *safek tumah b'reshut hayachid* simply does not apply. *Vechol davar she'ein bo da'at lishol bein bireshut harabbim bein bireshut hayachid safko tahor* — anything incapable of being asked, whether in a public domain or a private one, its doubtful status is *tahor*.

---

## Three Positions (IV) (III)…

### Sages Middle

The *Chachamim* stake out the middle position: *ela me'et l'et mematet al yad mipkidah l'fkidah, u'mipkidah l'fkidah mematet al yad me'et l'et* — the retroactive *tumah* extends back the lesser of two limits: 24 hours (*me'et l'et*) or the last time the woman performed a *bedikah* and found herself *tehorah* (*mipkidah l'fkidah*). Each limit constrains the other.

---

## Private Domain Doub (III)t……

### Rabbinic Tumaot

An alternative explanation is offered for why the loaf of bread in the private domain is *tahor* despite the general rule. *Hacha bitumah derabbanan* — the entire case may involve only rabbinic *tumah*, not biblical. The garment referred to in the *baraita* as *madaf* — the Gemara derives this from the verse *aleh nidaf*, a fluttering or flimsy leaf — refers to a garment that is treated as *tamei* only rabbinically. One example: garments of *amei ha'aretz*, those not careful about *taharah* even for *chullin*, are treated as *tamei* for those who eat their *chullin b'taharah*, and those who eat *chullin b'taharah* have their garments treated as *tamei* for *kohanim* eating *terumah*. These are entirely rabbinic hierarchies of *tumah*. Since the underlying *tumah* in this case is only rabbinic, the ruling is correspondingly lenient.

---

## Three Positions (IV) (III)……

### 24 Hour Limit

*Badka atzma b'erev Shabbos u'matzah tehorah* — she examined herself on Sunday evening and found herself *tehorah*. *Yashva sheini v'shlishi v'lo badka* — Monday and Tuesday she did not examine herself. *U'birvii badka u'matzah tmeiah* — on Wednesday she examined herself and found that she was a *niddah*. In this case, *ein omrim titamei mipkidah l'fkidah* — we do not say she is *temeiah* all the way back to the last time she checked (Sunday). *Ela me'et l'et* — only 24 hours back from the discovery on Wednesday.

### Last Check Limit

*Badka atzma b'shaah rishonah u'matzah tehorah* — she examined herself at the first hour (approximately 6:00 AM) and found herself *tehorah*. She did not examine herself at the second or third hour. *L'revi'it badka u'matzah tmeiah* — at the fourth hour (9:00 AM) she examined herself and found she was *temeiah*. In this case, *ein omrim titamei me'et l'et* — we do not go back 24 hours, because *ela mipkidah l'fkidah* — the last time she checked, only three hours earlier, sets the outer limit of retroactive *tumah*.

### Both Limits Need

The Gemara notes that one of these two examples appears self-evident. *Pshita* — in the case where she checked at 6:00 AM and found *tumah* at 9:00 AM, obviously we do not go back 24 hours; there is a confirmed *bedikah* showing she was *tehorah* just three hours earlier. Is it necessary to state this?

The Gemara answers: *ayadi d'tana me'et l'et mematet al yad mipkidah l'fkidah* — because it was necessary to state the first rule (that 24 hours limits the *mipkidah l'fkidah* in the case where she went days without checking), the *tanna* also stated the complementary rule, that when she checked recently, the *bedikah* limits the 24-hour window, even though that second case might have been derivable from the first. The two limits are stated in tandem because each is needed to define the other: 24 hours limits how far back a *mipkidah l'fkidah* can reach, and a *mipkidah l'fkidah* limits how far back the 24-hour window extends. Together they define the *Chachamim*'s middle position.
