# Bava Batra 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dovecote & First Brood

### Mishna Overview

The *mishna* at the top of 80a opens a new subject: *halokeach peiros shovach mechaveiro* — one who purchases the fruit, meaning the offspring, of a dovecote from his fellow. The buyer acquires all the young doves born from the dovecote. This marks a shift from the earlier *mishnayot*, which asked whether the purchase of one item includes associated items — does buying a house include the well, does buying a ship include the cargo containers, and so on. Here the question is not what is included in the sale but rather what must nonetheless be left behind despite being technically included.

### First Brood Rule

The *mishna* rules *mafriach berecha rishona* — the buyer must cause to fly away, that is, must leave with the seller, the first brood. According to the *Rashbam*, doves give birth once a month for most of the year, producing each time a male and a female. The first pair of offspring must be left behind with the mother. The reason is straightforward: if all the young are removed, the mother will have no reason to remain in the dovecote and will fly away. Since the seller intends to maintain his dovecote for the future — whether to sell next year's offspring or keep them for himself — he needs the mother to stay. Leaving the first brood behind serves as the anchor that keeps her there.

### Beehive Swarms

The *mishna* turns next to *peiros kaveret* — the offspring of a beehive. If one purchases the bees born from a hive, the ruling is *notel gimel nechilin umesares*: the buyer takes the first three *nechilin*, three swarms, and then the owner is *mesares*. Bees reproduce in groups, and each such cluster of newborns constitutes a swarm that can be removed to start a new hive. The buyer receives the first three. As for *mesares*, the *gemara* will debate its precise meaning, but reading it provisionally as sterilization: once the buyer has taken his three swarms, the seller retains the right to sterilize the remaining bees so that they return to producing honey. The *Rashbam* explains that as long as bees are reproducing, they are not producing honey; sterilizing them redirects their activity toward honey production, which is what the seller wants to preserve.

### Honey Loaves

A related case: if one purchases the honey from a beehive rather than the bees themselves, the *mishna* rules *maniach shtei chalot* — one must leave behind two *chalot*, two loaves of honey still embedded in the wax. Honey is the food of the bees, and the seller, while willing to sell the honey, is not trying to destroy his beehive. Leaving two loaves sustains the remaining bees. Those two loaves are therefore not included in the honey the buyer purchased.

### Olive Branches

Similarly, *zeisim lilkot meniach shtei gurofiyot* — if one purchases olive trees in order to cut down their branches, he must leave behind two *gurofiyot*, two branches, so the tree can grow back. The word *likkot* here denotes cutting the branches entirely, not merely picking individual olives. Even so, the seller is not understood to have forfeited his tree's future productivity, and the two remaining branches ensure the tree's regeneration.

## Generations Theory

### Brood Discrepancy

The *gemara* raises an apparent contradiction. The *mishna* states that the buyer must leave behind the first brood — *berecha rishona* — while a *baraita* says the first and second brood — *berecha rishona ushniya*. How are these reconciled?

### Rav Kahana Resolution

Rav Kahana answers: *lo kashya — ha lah ha le'imah* — this is no difficulty; one teaching speaks about her, and one about the mother. The *Rashbam* acknowledges that there are many unsatisfactory explanations of this passage in the literature, and he offers one that more or less works. What Rav Kahana means is the following: yes, the first brood is left behind to keep the mother anchored in the dovecote. But that first brood will itself grow up, and when it does, what prevents it from flying away? If it leaves, the mother loses her offspring and will also fly away. So the first brood must itself be given a reason to stay.

### Multi-Gen Anchoring

Using generational labels for clarity: call the mother Generation A. A gives birth, and the buyer takes all the offspring — except the first brood, which is Generation B, left behind for A. Now B grows up. B begins giving birth, and the buyer is entitled to those offspring as well — except for B's first brood, which is Generation C, left behind for B. So when the *baraita* says one leaves behind *rishona ushniya*, the first and the second, it does not mean the first two consecutive broods born from A. It means the first and second *generations*: B, the first generation left for A, and C, the second generation left for B.

### Why Offspring?

The *gemara* challenges this. When we are dealing with the mother, A, she stays because *demitztatfa abarta ve'azuga* — she has an attachment, a bond of connection, to her daughter B and to her mate, who are both left behind for her. Very well. But then why does B need C in order to stay? B also has its mother, A, and its own mate still present. *Ee nami titztatef ima ve'azuga deshavkinan lah* — let B find its anchoring from its mother and its mate, just as A found anchoring from its daughter and mate. Why must we leave C behind for B?

### Mother vs. Daughter

The *gemara* answers: *ima abarta mitztatfa; abarta ima lo mitztatfa* — a mother is anchored by her daughter; a daughter is not anchored by her mother. A parent stays because her child is there. But a child does not stay simply because her parent is there — that is not how offspring behave. They grow up, and they leave. So if you want B to remain in the dovecote, you cannot rely on the fact that A is present. You must give B her own child, C, who will keep B there. And if B is there because of C, then A will stay because of B.

## Mesares Debate

### Mustard Method

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: when one purchases the offspring of a hive, *notel gimel nechilin umesares* — one takes three swarms and then *mesares*. The *gemara* asks: how exactly does one perform this *mesares*, this sterilization? Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *bechardal* — through mustard. The bees consume mustard and as a result are sterilized, redirecting their activity to honey production.

### Honey Consumption

The *amri bamaarava* — the sages of *Eretz Yisroel* — said in the name of Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina: it is not precisely that the mustard sterilizes the bees. Rather, *mitokh shepihen chad* — because the mustard leaves their mouths with a sharp, pungent taste, they then seek something sweet to neutralize it, *ve'okhlo et dovshan* — and they go and eat their honey. Once they are consuming honey and spending their time on that activity, they are not reproducing. According to the *Rashbam*, this is the mechanism: occupying the bees with eating honey rather than reproducing achieves the intended effect.

### Alternation Theory

Rabbi Yochanan offers a fundamentally different reading of the word. He says *mesares* does not derive from *sirus*, sterilization, but from *serugin*, alternation — the same root as *kippot serugin*, knitted caps, implying an interwoven pattern. Under Rabbi Yochanan's reading, *notel shlosha nechilin besirus* means the buyer takes three swarms *in alternation* — that is, swarm one, swarm three, and swarm five, while the seller retains swarms two, four, and six. The earlier swarms are presumably stronger and more valuable, so rather than the buyer taking all three of the first and best swarms, the arrangement alternates so that the benefit is shared.

### Three Then Alternate

A *baraita* then clarifies and provides the most satisfying reading of the *mishna*: *notel gimel nechilin zeh achar zeh* — the buyer takes the first three consecutive swarms, one after the other. *Mikan ve'eilach, notel achas umaniach achas* — from that point on, alternation begins: the buyer takes one, the seller keeps one, the buyer takes one, and so forth. So the word *mesares* in the *mishna* does mean alternation in the sense of Rabbi Yochanan, but it does not mean three swarms taken alternately from the start. The buyer first receives three straight, and the alternating pattern governs only subsequent swarms. This is, as noted, a considerably better reading of the *mishna*.

## Honey Status

### Rav Kahana Position

The *mishna*'s mention of the two honey loaves left for the bees prompts a broader *halakhic* discussion about the status of honey in the hive. Rav Kahana states: *devash bekiveret eino yotzei midei maakhal le'olam* — honey that is still in the beehive, still embedded in the wax, never loses its status as food. This carries two implications. First, the honey automatically has the status of *ochel*, food, even before it has been removed from the hive and extracted from the wax — which is somewhat surprising, since it is not yet in the form people would ordinarily eat. Second, it is not a liquid but a solid, and this distinction matters for *tumah* purposes, since the *halakhic* rules for food and liquid differ. The *gemara* adds that this means Rav Kahana holds *lo ba'ei machshava* — no conscious intent is required to confer food status. Just as one need not think "I intend to eat this apple" in order for an apple to carry the *halakhic* status of food, so too honey in the hive is automatically food.

### In Wax vs. Extracted

*Tosafos* notes that *halakhically*, honey is normally classified among the seven liquids. However, *Tosafos* draws a careful distinction: that classification applies to honey once it has been extracted from the wax. When honey is still inside the beehive in its wax state, it begins its existence as food, and while in that state it cannot be considered a liquid.

### Neither Status

The *gemara* challenges Rav Kahana's position. A *baraita* teaches: *devash bekiveret eino lo ochel ve-lo mashkeh* — honey while still in the beehive has neither the status of food nor the status of liquid. This stands in apparent contradiction to Rav Kahana's ruling that honey in the hive is automatically and irrevocably food. The question is how to reconcile these sources.

### Two Loaves Exception

Abaye resolves the tension: *lo tzricha ella le'otan shtei chalot* — that *baraita* is speaking specifically about the two loaves left behind for the bees. This is entirely reasonable. The totality of honey in a beehive exists for the purpose of human consumption — that is the nature of the enterprise — and therefore it automatically carries food status without any special intent. But the two loaves set aside expressly for the bees to eat are a different category: their default purpose is bee sustenance, not human consumption. Those two loaves, accordingly, do not automatically have food status; only if one specifically intends to remove them for human use will they acquire the *halakhic* status of food or liquid depending on how one intends to treat them.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

Rava offers a different resolution. He says: *Rabbi Eliezer hi* — that *baraita* which says honey in the hive is neither food nor liquid reflects the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer. The reference is to a *mishna* from 83b that was treated earlier in the tractate.

## Rabbi Eliezer Honey

### Ground Attachment

The *mishna* in question states that Rabbi Eliezer holds: *kiveret devorim — harei hi ke-karka* — a beehive of bees is treated as land. *Tosafos* clarifies that we are speaking here about the honey within the hive. Rabbi Eliezer's position is that honey within a hive is treated *halakhically* as though it were fruit still attached to a tree — it is considered part of the land, still connected to the ground. This is Rabbi Eliezer's *chiddush*: one might argue that honey does not grow in the hive the way an apple grows from a tree; rather, the bees manufacture it there. You could compare it to placing a basket of apples in a tree — that would not be *karka*. But Rabbi Eliezer rules that nonetheless, since the honey is a product of the hive and remains within it, it is treated as though still attached to the ground.

### Pruzbul & Tamei

Several *halachic* consequences flow from this position. *Ve-kotvin aleha pruzbul* — one may write a *pruzbul* using the honey in the hive as the debtor's landed property, since a *pruzbul* can only be written when the debtor possesses land, and Rabbi Eliezer treats this honey as land. Furthermore, *ve'eina mekabeles tumah bimkomah* — just as an apple attached to a tree cannot become *tamei* until it is detached, the honey in the hive, while still there and still considered attached to the ground, cannot become *tamei*.

### Shabbat Violation

Perhaps the most striking ruling is: *ve-ha-rodeh mimena be-shabbat chayav chatat* — one who removes honey from the hive on *Shabbat* is liable for a *chatat* offering, having violated *Shabbat*. One is liable on *Shabbat* for *kotzer*, harvesting — plucking something from where it grows. If one had a basket of apples hanging from a tree, taking apples from it would not constitute *kotzer*. But Rabbi Eliezer holds that since the honey is *halakhically* treated as growing from the ground, removing it is equivalent to picking an apple from a tree, and thus one has performed a prohibited *melacha* on *Shabbat*.

### Verse Proof

Rabbi Elazar the *Amora* asks: *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Eliezer* — what is the scriptural basis for Rabbi Eliezer's position? *Dichtiv vayitbol otah be-yaarot devash* — the verse describes someone dipping into *yaarot devash*, literally "forests of honey." *Ve-chi ma inyan yaar etzel devash* — what does a forest have to do with honey? The juxtaposition teaches: *mah yaar ha-toleish mimenu be-shabbat chayav chatat* — just as with a forest, plucking from a tree constitutes a *Shabbat* violation, *af devash ha-rodeh mimenu be-shabbat chayav chatat* — so too with honey: removing it from the hive on *Shabbat* constitutes a violation. The *halakha* treats the honey as though it had grown in and from the hive, as attached to the ground, and therefore all of these *halachot* apply.

This is precisely relevant to Rava's explanation of the *baraita*. If honey in the hive is treated as *karka* by Rabbi Eliezer — as attached to the ground like fruit on a tree — then it cannot become *tamei* while in that state, and therefore it has neither food nor liquid status for *tumah* purposes. That is what the *baraita* reflects.

## Separation Intent

### Separated Status

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* that poses a fresh challenge: *devash she-zav mi-kavarto eino lo ochel ve-lo mashkeh* — honey that has flowed out from the hive has neither food nor liquid status. This is striking because at this point the honey has already separated from the hive — it is no longer embedded in the wax and no longer inside the hive itself — and yet the *baraita* says it is still neither food nor liquid.

### Intent Determines

The *baraita* continues: if one had intent *le'achila*, to eat the honey, it becomes *metamei tumat ochlim* — it acquires the *tumah* status of food and is subject to the laws governing solid foodstuffs, with the relevant *shiur* of *ke-beitza*. If one had intent to treat it as a liquid — perhaps to use it as a dripping substance, or to drink it, or to produce mead — it becomes *metamei tumat mashkin*, a liquid for *tumah* purposes, with the different *shiur* of a *revi'is*. So the *baraita* posits that even after the honey has separated from the hive and the wax, it has no *halakhic* status until one consciously decides how to use it.

### Abaye Consistency

For Abaye this poses no difficulty. Under his reading, Rav Kahana's position is that normal honey in the hive is automatically food. The earlier *baraita* that said honey in the hive is neither food nor liquid was referring to the two loaves set aside for the bees. Now this *baraita* about honey that has flowed out is also consistent: the honey that flows out is still the honey of those two loaves — it began its existence set aside for bee consumption, not human consumption. Because it started without food status, it retains that statusless quality even after separation from the wax and hive. Only specific *machshava*, conscious intent, transforms it into food or liquid for *tumah* purposes. The entire picture — Rav Kahana's automatic-food ruling for normal honey, and the two *baraitas* — coheres into a clean package under Abaye's reading.

### Rava Reading

For Rava, however, there is a difficulty. Under Rava's reading, the *baraita* that describes honey in the hive as neither food nor liquid is following Rabbi Eliezer, who treats the honey as attached to the ground. The problem is the end of this same *baraita*, which says that once the honey has flowed out, *machshava* — intent — can confer food or liquid status. But under Rabbi Eliezer's framework, the operative factor is ground-attachment. Once the honey has separated from the hive and is no longer attached to the ground, it should simply revert automatically to its natural status as food. *Machshava* should not be required. So how does Rava account for this *baraita*?

Rava would respond: *taretz hachi* — read the end of the *baraita* with the word *eino* inserted. *Chishav alav le'achila eino metameh tumat ochlim; le-mashkeh eino metameh tumat mashkeh* — if one thought to eat it, it does **not** thereby become *tamei* as food; if one thought to drink it, it does **not** thereby become *tamei* as liquid. The *baraita* is not saying intent makes it food; it is saying intent does not suffice to make it *tamei* — because while it remains in its ground-attached state, nothing can make it *tamei*.

### Disgusting Vessel

The *gemara* then challenges Rava's position from another *baraita*: *devash she-zav mi-kavarto eino lo ochel ve-lo mashkeh* — honey that flowed from the hive is neither food nor liquid — followed again by the clause that intent to eat makes it food and intent to use as liquid makes it liquid. Rava faces the same problem: once it has flowed out, it should automatically be food; why does intent matter?

Rav Zevid offers a solution: the *baraita* is speaking *ke-gon she-zav agav keli ma'us* — of a case where the honey flowed into or over a disgusting vessel. Rav Acha bar Yaakov says: *ke-gon she-zav agav kaskasin* — it flowed over wood chips and was absorbed into them, making it unretrievable in any normal sense. The significance of these scenarios is as follows. Under Abaye's reading of Rav Kahana, once something acquires food status, it retains that status even if it falls into a disgusting vessel — as long as it is still edible by a dog, it remains *halakhically* food for *tumah* purposes. But Rava's reading follows Rabbi Eliezer, according to whom the honey started its existence as ground-attached and thus not yet food. When it then flows out into a disgusting vessel or onto wood chips, it is not edible by normal humans. Since its pre-existing status was "not food," and its current condition is "not normally edible by humans," it still does not automatically acquire food status. Only explicit *machshava* — a conscious decision to eat it or treat it as a liquid — will confer *halakhic* status. That is how Rava accounts for the *baraita*.

## Consensus & Baraita

### Rav Kahana Consensus

The *gemara* concludes: *tanya kevata de-Rav Kahana* — there is a *baraita* that has been taught in accordance with Rav Kahana's position. *Devash bekiveret metameh tumat ochlim she-lo be-machshava* — honey in the hive conveys the *tumah* of food without requiring any *machshava* at all. This establishes that the *halakha* follows Rav Kahana: honey in the hive is automatically food.

### Full Reconciliation

The full picture is now clear. Rav Kahana's ruling — honey in the hive is automatically food, requiring no intent — is the accepted position. Abaye explains the apparent contradictory *baraita* as referring exclusively to the two loaves left for the bees: since those are set aside for bee consumption by default, they do not automatically bear food status, and intent is required to reassign them for human use. Rava's explanation — that the *baraita* reflects Rabbi Eliezer's view of honey as ground-attached, such that no *machshava* can make it *tamei* while in that state — stands as a minority reading tied to Rabbi Eliezer's specific position.

## Trees Cutting

### Standard Cutting

The *mishna*'s ruling about olive branches is fleshed out by a *baraita*: *halokeach ilan mechaveiro lilkot magbi'ah min hakarka tefach vekotzetz* — one who buys a tree from his fellow to cut it down must cut from a height of one *tefach* above the ground, approximately three to four inches, in order to leave enough of the trunk that the tree can grow back. The buyer purchased the wood, not the right to uproot the tree entirely.

### Sycamore Heights

For a *bisculas hashikmah* — a virgin sycamore, one that has never previously been cut — one must leave *gimel tefachim*, three *tefachim*, near the ground. For a *sdan hashikmah* — a sycamore that has been cut before and has already grown back — *shtei tefachim* suffice, since a once-cut sycamore is sturdier and will regenerate with less trunk remaining.

### Other Trees

For reeds and grapevines, the buyer must cut *min hapkak ulema'alah* — above the knot in the reed or vine. For date palms and cedar trees, however, *chofer umesharesh* — one may dig down and uproot them entirely — because *she'ein gizmo machalif*, their trunk does not regenerate once cut. Since no regrowth is possible, there is nothing to preserve for the seller, and the buyer may remove them entirely.

## Sycamore Shevi'is

### Shevi'is Ban

The *gemara* challenges the requirement of three *tefachim* for a virgin sycamore from a *baraita* concerning *shevi'is*: *ein kotzetzin be-visculas hashikmah beshevi'is mipnei shehi avodah* — one may not cut a virgin sycamore on *shevi'is* because doing so constitutes *avodah*, a prohibited act that promotes growth. *Shevi'is* prohibits not only planting and sowing but any agricultural labor that encourages crops or trees to grow: *sadcha lo tizra, ve-charmecha lo tizmor*. Cutting a virgin sycamore causes it to regenerate, which the Rabbis understood as a prohibited form of agricultural enhancement.

This seems consistent with what we have been learning — cutting the sycamore encourages new growth. But the question from the *baraita* about *shevi'is* is: if cutting it at all causes growth, the cut should be forbidden. And indeed it seems that even cutting at one *tefach* would stimulate regrowth. So why does the buyer's case require three *tefachim* — what difference does the height make?

### Yehuda Exception

Rabbi Yehuda says: *kidarko asur* — cutting in the normal manner that allows regrowth is forbidden on *shevi'is*. However, two alternatives are permitted if one simply wants the wood without encouraging growth. First, *magbi'ah yud tefachim vekotzetz* — one may cut starting from ten *tefachim* above the ground; at that height, the cut will not stimulate regrowth. Second, *ugomem me'im ha'aretz* — one may cut the tree flush against the ground, leveling it completely; that too will not promote regrowth.

### Range Theory

This raises the question: the *baraita* about the sale requires three *tefachim*; Rabbi Yehuda says for *shevi'is* you must either go very high or cut completely flush. The implication seems to be that only cutting flush against the ground prevents regrowth — so why does the buyer in a normal sale need to leave three *tefachim* for the tree to grow back? Would leaving even one *tefach* not be sufficient?

Abaye explains that there is a range. *Gimel tefachim ma'ali lei* — cutting at three *tefachim* is optimal; the stump left behind is robust and the tree will regenerate vigorously. *Me'im ha'aretz vadai kashi lei* — cutting completely flush with the ground is certainly harmful to the tree and it will definitely not grow back. *Mikan ve'eilach lo makshe kashi lei, ve-lo ma'ale ma'ali lei* — anywhere between zero and three *tefachim*, the cut is neither clearly harmful to the tree nor truly beneficial; the tree will grow back, but not as well as it should.

### Shevi'is vs Sale

These two distinct legal contexts therefore require different standards. *Gabei shevi'is avdinan midi d'vadai kashi lei* — on *shevi'is*, since the goal is to permit cutting for the wood without in any way encouraging growth, one must ensure the cutting definitively impedes regrowth. Any height above ground, even one *tefach*, provides some encouragement to regrowth, so the only permissible option is cutting completely flush. *Gabei mekach umimkar avdinan midi d'vadai ma'ali lei* — in the context of a commercial sale, the seller must be left with something genuinely adequate for the tree to regenerate properly, so the buyer must cut at three *tefachim* to ensure robust regrowth. The different requirements are thus not contradictory but reflect the different purposes each rule serves.

## Cedar Regrowth

### Contradiction

The *baraita* stated that for date palms and cedar trees, the buyer may uproot them entirely *she'ein gizmo machalif* — because their trunk does not grow back. The *gemara* challenges this with respect to cedar: *ve'erez ein gizmo machalif* — is it really true that a cedar tree does not grow back once cut?

### Righteous Drash

Rabbi Chiya bar Lulina's *drash* demonstrates precisely the opposite. He asks: *mai dichtiv tzadik katamar yifrach ke'erez balvanon yisgeh* — what is meant by the verse that a righteous person will blossom like a palm tree and increase like a cedar of Lebanon? If both images are being used to describe the *tzadik*, what does each one contribute that the other does not?

*Im ne'emar erez velo ne'emar tamar* — had the verse mentioned only the cedar, I would have thought: just as a cedar does not bear fruit, so too a *tzadik* produces no spiritual fruit, no reward, no consequence from his good deeds. That would be a deeply discouraging message. *Lekach ne'emar tamar* — therefore the verse also compares the righteous person to a palm tree, which does bear fruit, to teach that the *tzadik*'s deeds do bear fruit and yield reward.

Conversely, *im ne'emar tamar velo ne'emar erez, hayisi omer mah tamar ein gizmo machalif af tzadik ein gizmo machalif* — had the verse mentioned only the palm tree, I would have thought: just as a palm tree does not regenerate when cut, so too if a *tzadik* stumbles and falls he cannot rise again. *Lekach ne'emar erez* — therefore the verse also compares him to a cedar, teaching that even if a *tzadik* stumbles, he will ultimately rise again. The *gemara* observes that this is notably being demonstrated from a textual source rather than from direct observation — which is, of course, the natural mode of reasoning in the *beis medrash*. The implication is unmistakable: cedar trees do in fact regenerate when cut.

### Arz Variety

How then does one reconcile this with the *baraita* that says cedar trees do not grow back? The *gemara* answers: *ela hacha bemai askinan — bishe'ar minei arazim* — when the *baraita* says that *arzim* do not regrow, it is referring to other species that are called *erez* but are not in fact the cedar tree. Those non-cedar varieties of *erez* do not regenerate; the true cedar does.

### Ten Types

This resolution draws on a teaching of Rabba bar Rav Huna: *asarah minei arazim hem* — there are ten species called *erez*. This is derived from a verse: *eten bamidbar erez* — I will place in the wilderness an *erez* — followed by a list of different tree species. The *gemara* identifies each in turn. *Erez* itself is *arza*, the cedar. *Shita* is *turnisa*, the acacia. *Hadas* is *asa*, the myrtle. *Etz shemen* is *afarsema*, which is the balsam tree. *Berosh* is *barti*, the juniper. *Tidhar* is *shaga*, identified by the Koren as teak. *Te'ashur* is *shurbina*, the cypress. That accounts for seven species from the verse.

### Additional Types

Since ten species were declared but only seven appear in that verse, the *gemara* notes that when Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisroel*, he transmitted that three additional species are also called *erez*. According to the first version, these are *alonim*, *almonim*, and *almugim*. *Alonim* is *butni*, the pistachio. *Almonim* is *baluti*, the oak. *Almugim* is *kasita*, the coral tree. A second version — *ika d'amri* — substitutes *aronim*, *armonim*, and *almugim*: *aronim* is *arei*, the laurel; *armonim* is *dulvi*, the plane tree; and *almugim* is again *kasita*, the coral tree.

The precise botanical identification of many of these species remains uncertain, and whether most of them truly resemble cedar in any meaningful way is an open question. The *halakhic* point, however, is clear: of all these species called *erez*, only the true cedar regrows when cut; the others do not. One *baraita* addresses the true cedar, which does regrow and which may therefore not be uprooted by a buyer; the other *baraita* addresses the remaining varieties called *erez*, which do not regrow and may be uprooted entirely.
