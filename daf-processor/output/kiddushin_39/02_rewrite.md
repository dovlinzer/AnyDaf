# Kiddushin 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Orlah vs. Kilayim

### Intro: Orlah/Kilayim

We begin at the bottom of 38b, picking up the thread of which prohibitions apply in *eretz yisrael* and which apply in *chutz la'aretz*. The *mishna* in *Orlah* teaches that *chadash* is *assur min hatorah* everywhere — that is the position of Rabbi Eliezer, and we have already discussed how we *pasken* that question for *chadash* in *chutz la'aretz*. The *mishna* then states that *orlah* is *halacha* and *kilayim* is *divrei soferim* — that is, *d'rabbanan*.

The question is: what does *halacha* mean in this context? One opinion, that of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, understands it as *hilchos hamedina* — adopted local practice, or at most *d'rabbanan*. The competing position understands *halacha* to mean *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, which would give *orlah* considerably more weight than a mere *d'rabbanan*. The *sugya* now proceeds to demonstrate that *orlah* is indeed weightier than *kilayim*, by examining how each handles the question of *safek* — doubtful cases — in *chutz la'aretz*.

### Ulla's Challenge

Ulla raises the question directly to Rav Yehuda: *bishlama ledidi* — according to my position, that *orlah* is *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, I can explain why the *mishna* distinguishes between a doubtful case of *orlah* and a doubtful case of *kilayim*, treating the latter more leniently than the former. *Orlah* is *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* while *kilayim* is *d'rabbanan*, and thus their respective treatments of *safek* differ accordingly.

But according to your view, Rav Yehuda, that both are *d'rabbanan*, why should there be any difference between them in cases of *safek*? The *mishna* should either make them equally strict or equally lenient — but not treat them differently.

### Leniencies Compared

To appreciate the force of Ulla's challenge, we must look carefully at what the *mishna* actually teaches. In a case of *safek orlah* in *eretz yisrael*, it is *assur* — the land itself is *d'oraita*. In *Surya* — the lands adjacent to Israel conquered by David — it is *mutar*, because the prohibition there is only *d'rabbanan*. In *chutz la'aretz* proper, it is even more lenient: *yored velokei'ach* — one may go down into the orchard and purchase from the seller there, provided only that one does not actually witness the seller picking directly from the *orlah* tree itself. One does not even need to ask. The level of *safek* required is remarkably minimal — as long as it is not blatantly apparent that the fruit came from that tree.

When we turn to *kilayim*, the *mishna* presents a parallel structure but with a notably greater leniency in *chutz la'aretz*. The case discussed is a vineyard in which vegetables have been planted — rendering those vegetables *kilayim* — with a small produce stand right outside selling the very same vegetables. In *eretz yisrael* this is *assur*; in *Surya* it is *mutar*. But in *chutz la'aretz*, the leniency goes even further: *yored veloket* — one may actually observe the seller picking the vegetables directly from the vineyard and still purchase and eat them. As Rashi explains at the top of 39a: *delo assru be-chutz la'aretz ela es hazoreya o es haloket beyadayim* — the only prohibition in *chutz la'aretz* falls on the one who planted the *kilayim* or the one who personally picks it. One who merely purchases — even having watched the picking occur — is permitted.

So we have two extreme *kulos*, but *kilayim* is more lenient still: by *orlah* one cannot witness the picking; by *kilayim* one can. Ulla's argument is that if both are *d'rabbanan*, they should be treated identically. Why is one permitted to witness the picking for *kilayim* but not for *orlah*?

### Shmuel Amends Mishna

Shmuel, who is precisely the one who taught that *orlah* in *chutz la'aretz* is *hilchos hamedina* — effectively *d'rabbanan* — concedes Ulla's point entirely. He instructed the *rabanana* to amend the *girsa* of the *mishna* so that both *orlah* and *kilayim* carry the same ruling. Either both prohibit witnessing the picking — i.e., the stricter version — or both permit witnessing the picking as long as one does not personally do the picking. Since Shmuel holds them to be at the same level, the *mishna* must reflect uniform treatment, and he accordingly emends the text to achieve that consistency.

### Mar Brei D'Ravina

Mar brei d'Ravina takes the more lenient of the two positions. He teaches: *zeh vezeh yored velokei'ach* — in both cases one may even watch the picking occur directly, *ubilvad shelo yilkat beyado*, as long as one does not pick it oneself. This presumably reflects his agreement with Shmuel's view that both *orlah* and *kilayim* stand at the same level, and if they are both *d'rabbanan*, one might as well adopt the more lenient ruling for both.

### Shivi's Proposal

Shivi said to Shmuel — calling him *aryoch*, apparently a term meaning something like "the great one" or "the lion," which may derive from the same root as *aryoch melech Elasar* — *sfeik li v'ana eichol*: make it a *safek* for me and I will eat it. Rashi explains: *sfeik li likot shelo bifnai sheyisafek etzli v'ana eichol uv'Orlah ka'i* — go ahead and pick when I am not present, so that there will be a genuine *safek* as far as I am concerned, and I will eat it. This is actually the stricter of the two positions: it does not permit witnessing the picking for *orlah*, but it does show a practical workaround for a Jew who has, say, an apple orchard in its third year. The seller picks when the buyer is not present, thereby maintaining the technical requirement that one not witness the picking directly.

### Exchange of Picking

Rav Avya and Rabba bar Bar Chana went one step further and would exchange the picking of doubtful produce between one another — each one picking for the other, so that neither was in the presence of his own orchard's picking. This is a practical mutual arrangement that achieves the same result as Shivi's proposal but between two parties acting in concert.

### Pumbedita's Leniency

The *charifei d'Pumbedita* — the sharp ones of Pumbedita, whom Rashi identifies as Ha'eifa u'Ramibi, the sons of Rechava — took a far more sweeping position: *ein orlah b'chutz la'aretz*, there is simply no *orlah* in *chutz la'aretz* whatsoever. This was the predictable endpoint of the slippery slope: once the leniencies are extended far enough, someone will ultimately make the blanket declaration that the prohibition does not apply at all.

## Yochanan's Response

### RavYehuda Reports

Rav Yehuda sent word to Rabbi Yochanan, who was in *eretz yisrael*, reporting that the scholars of Pumbedita were declaring *ein orlah b'chutz la'aretz* — that *orlah* does not apply at all outside the Land. Rabbi Yochanan's response was forceful and multidimensional. First, *setom sefeika*: silence the *safek*. Now that people are sliding into the position that *orlah* does not apply at all, one must stop publicizing the various leniencies in cases of *safek*, lest the teaching of those leniencies serve as the gateway to the blanket denial of the prohibition. Second, *v'abeid vadah*: actively destroy any produce that is a *vaday* case of *orlah*. Third, *v'hachreiz al peiroteihen she-te'unin genizah*: announce publicly that the fruit of those who engaged in these arrangements — the exchange of picking and similar tactics — requires *genizah*. And finally, regarding anyone who says *ein orlah b'chutz la'aretz* outright: *lo yiheyeh lo nin v'neched* — he should have no descendant. This is strikingly severe rhetoric, and reflects Rabbi Yochanan's alarm at how far the leniency had traveled.

The *gemara* then asks: upon which authority were the scholars of Pumbedita relying? If they were making the blanket statement that *orlah* does not apply at all in *chutz la'aretz*, they presumably had a tannaitic source for that position.

### Rabbi Eliezer Tradition

Indeed, there is such a tradition: *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yossi omer mishum Rabbi Yossi ben Durmaska she-amar mishum Rabbi Yossi ha-Gelili she-amar mishum Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri she-amar mishum Rabbi Eliezer ha-Gadol* — an unbroken chain of authorities going back to Rabbi Eliezer the Great: *ein orlah b'chutz la'aretz*. It is worth pausing to note that this itself is a remarkable feature of the *sugya*: if one holds that anything which is a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* is never subject to debate — as the Rambam maintains — then the very fact that Rabbi Eliezer is denying that *orlah b'chutz la'aretz* exists at all presents a difficulty, whether one frames it as a debate about whether it is a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* or as a flat rejection of that category's application here.

### Contradiction Resolved

The *gemara* immediately notes an apparent contradiction: we have a *mishna* in which Rabbi Eliezer says *af he-chadash* — even *chadash* applies outside the Land, in the context of a list that includes *orlah* and *kilayim*. If Rabbi Eliezer holds *ein orlah b'chutz la'aretz*, why does he seem to be adding *chadash* to a list that includes *orlah*? The resolution offered is to emend the *mishna*: remove the word *af* and read it as saying that Rabbi Eliezer holds that only *chadash* applies in *chutz la'aretz* — *orlah* and *kilayim* do not apply — whereas the *stam* view of the *mishna* holds that all three apply. This way, the two attributed statements of Rabbi Eliezer are brought into alignment.

### Halacha L'Moshe Claim

Rav Assi teaches in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *orlah b'chutz la'aretz halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*. This is a reiteration of Rabbi Yochanan's position — a strong stance that treats the application of *orlah* outside the Land as a law transmitted directly from Sinai, not merely a rabbinic practice or local custom.

### Zeira's Objection

Rav Zeira raises an objection to Rav Assi: how can you claim this is *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*? But we have the *mishna* that teaches *safek orlah ba'aretz assur, be-Surya mutar, u-vechutz la'aretz yored velokei'ach* — all these extensive leniencies in cases of *safek*! If *orlah b'chutz la'aretz* is a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, how can there be such extraordinary leniency even in what amounts to a near-*vaday* case? Rav Assi was momentarily speechless — *ishtomem kesha'a chada* — he was dumbfounded.

### Safek Mutar Solution

Then Rav Assi recovered: *eima kach ne'emar* — perhaps this is precisely how the *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* was formulated: *sveika mutar, vadai assur* — in a case of *safek* it is permitted, in a case of *vaday* it is forbidden. The *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* itself incorporated this leniency for *safek* as an intrinsic part of its content.

There are two significant observations to make here. First, note that Rav Assi does not say he has a clear transmitted tradition that the *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* included this *safek* provision; rather, he says *eima kach ne'emar* — "let us say it was stated this way." He is reconstructing the content of the *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* from what the *mishna* teaches, rather than recalling an unbroken explicit tradition about it. This itself raises questions about what it means to invoke *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — whether it always reflects a clearly remembered transmission or whether it can be a category that is inferred and in some measure reconstructed.

Second, Rav Zeira's question implicitly assumes that *safek d'oraisa l'chumra* should apply to a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — that if this has Sinaitic authority, one should be strict in cases of doubt. And indeed, the Rambam holds that the principle of *safek d'oraisa l'chumra* is itself only *d'rabbanan* — that as far as the *torah* is concerned, *safek d'oraisa l'kula*. Yet this *gemara* seems to proceed on the assumption that Sinaitic authority calls for stringency in *safek*, requiring a special built-in provision to permit leniency. One possible way of resolving this tension is to note that what may have been troubling the *gemara* is not the abstract principle of *safek l'kula* but rather the extraordinary practical extremity of the leniency — a situation so attenuated that it barely qualifies as a *safek* in any meaningful sense, such as buying from a seller when one knows the orchard contains *orlah* trees but simply did not witness the picking. That kind of manipulated or fictional *safek* may be what prompted the objection, quite apart from any general principle about *safek d'oraisa*.

This question — whether *safek* in a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* is *l'chumra* or *l'kula* — is discussed among the *rishonim*; if memory serves, the Ran or Rabbeinu Yona addresses it somewhere. It connects to the broader question of the weight of *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* relative to *d'oraisa* prohibitions in general.

A further note is worth adding about the category of *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* itself. The Rambam held that whatever is truly *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* was never debated — and he enumerated approximately thirty such items in the *gemara*. He used this framework in part as a response to the Karaite challenge: how can you claim an unbroken oral tradition when everything is debated? The Rambam's answer was that virtually all of *torah she-ba'al peh* was never directly transmitted as bottom-line *halachos* but rather as hermeneutic principles and an empowerment to interpret; that interpretation, applied by different minds in different circumstances, naturally generates debate. The one domain in which actual content was transmitted intact is *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, where there should be no *machlokes*. The problem, as this *sugya* illustrates, is that there plainly *is* debate even over some of those traditions. In addition, there are places in the *gemara* where the phrase *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* appears to be used rhetorically — the *Mishnah Yadayim* is one example — for matters that cannot literally be Sinaitic laws. The Rosh makes this observation explicitly. The Netziv, in his introduction to the *She'iltos*, discusses how certain *rishonim* understand the term *hilchasa* when it appears after a *gemara* dispute: Rashi reads it as *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, while others understand it as an ancient *d'rabbanan* whose reason was forgotten, without any claim that it goes back to Moshe. These questions of when the phrase is literal and when it is rhetorical are treated in modern scholarship as well.

## Kilayim Distinctions

### Lashes for Kilayim

We now turn to *kilayim* specifically. Abba teaches in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lokin al kilayim de-var Torah* — one receives lashes for *kilayim* as a biblical prohibition. The immediate response from Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yossi is a challenge: but we have an explicit *mishna* — *ha-kilayim mi-divrei sofrim*! *Kilayim* is rabbinic! The entire thrust of our *sugya* up to now has been that *kilayim* is *d'rabbanan*. How can Rabbi Yochanan say one gets lashes, which is a punishment reserved for biblical violations?

### Harkavah vs. Seeds

The resolution: *lo kashya, kan b'kilai kerem kan b'harkava* — there is no contradiction because we are dealing with two entirely different categories. The *mishna*'s statement that *kilayim* is *d'rabbanan* refers to *kilayim* of seeds — mixing different seeds in a field. But there is another form of *kilayim*: *harkavat ilan* — grafting one tree onto another. This form is more analogous to the crossbreeding of animals (*harva'at behema*), since it involves direct physical manipulation of living organisms rather than simply planting seeds in proximity. It is this category — *harkavat ilan* — for which one receives lashes even in *chutz la'aretz*, based on a teaching of Shmuel.

Shmuel derives this from the verse *et chukotai tishmoru* — you shall observe My statutes. The unusual word order (*et chukotai*, placing the object first) implies that these *chukim* are ancient, pre-existing the moment of their formal biblical statement. The next verse reads: *behemtecha lo tarbia kilayim, sadecha lo tizra kilayim* — you shall not crossbreed your animals with *kilayim*, and you shall not sow your field with *kilayim*. The derivation proceeds as follows: just as *behemtecha* refers to crossbreeding animals, so too *sadecha* here is read not at the level of *peshat* (planting seeds) but through *derash*, as referring to something analogous — grafting trees. And just as crossbreeding animals applies equally in *eretz yisrael* and in *chutz la'aretz*, so too grafting trees (*harkavat ilan*) applies *bein ba'aretz bein be-chutz la'aretz* — wherever one is.

There is a further dimension to this derivation: the antiquity implied by *et chukotai* suggests that these prohibitions predate the giving of the *Torah* at Sinai — they go back to the *benei Noach*. According to this position, even non-Jews are prohibited in crossbreeding animals and grafting trees, as these are counted among the *sheva mitzvos benei Noach*. The Rambam rules accordingly: *hamarkiv ilan be'ilan... harei zeh lokeh min haTorah bechol makom bein ba'aretz bein bechutz la'aretz* — one who grafts tree onto tree receives lashes from the *Torah* in every location, whether in *eretz yisrael* or in *chutz la'aretz*.

As for the word *sadecha* — "your field," which sounds possessive and might suggest the prohibition is limited to one's own land in *eretz yisrael* — the *gemara* resolves this by saying *hahu le-ma'uti zera'im she-ba-chutz*: *sadecha* is used specifically to exclude *kilayim* of seeds in *chutz la'aretz*, which is indeed not prohibited there at the biblical level. So the verse operates on two levels simultaneously: the *peshat* level (*lo tizra*, seeds, *sadecha*, limited to *eretz yisrael*) and the *derash* level (grafting, analogous to crossbreeding, which applies universally). Rashi's comment here is striking: *chutza la'aretz lo chasmu lachem min hashomayim* — outside the Land, heaven has not sealed off for you [the fruit of grafted trees]. The fruit itself remains permitted even where the act is forbidden.

### Biblical Sources

The two verses that govern these prohibitions are *behemtecha lo tarbia kilayim* — your animals you shall not crossbreed — and *sadecha lo tizra kilayim* — your field you shall not sow with mixed species. The hermeneutic reading of *sadecha* as parallel to *behemtecha* allows the prohibition to travel from the planting of seeds (its *peshat* meaning) to the grafting of trees (its *derash* meaning), and simultaneously the comparison to animal crossbreeding establishes that the prohibition is geographically unlimited. The *peshat* reading of seeds, by contrast, is confined by *sadecha* — "your field," meaning *eretz yisrael* — to the Land.

### Rav Chanan/Anan

Rav Chanan and Rav Anan were walking along a road when they observed a man planting seeds together. Rav Anan suggested: *neisei mar nishmasei* — let us go and put him in *cherem*, for he is transgressing the prohibition of *kilayim* in *chutz la'aretz*. His companion replied: *lo chavir su* — it is not clear to you what the *halacha* is. The exchange was not finished, however. They then saw another man planting wheat and barley inside a vineyard. Again Rav Anan suggested putting him in *cherem*. Again: *lo tzahiri tu* — it is not clear to you. The *gemara* explains that we do not rule like Rabbi Yoshiya, whose stringent definition of the act of *kilayim* would apply here.

### Rabbi Yoshiya's Rule

Rabbi Yoshiya holds: *ad she'yizra chittah u'se'orah ve'chartzan be'mapoles yad* — a person does not transgress the prohibition of planting *kilayim* unless he plants wheat, barley, and a grape seed all simultaneously, in a single action. Planting wheat and barley alone does not constitute the prohibition of *kilayim* of the vineyard according to his view, because a vineyard requires the grape to be present as part of the act. Given this ruling — especially in the lenient context of *chutz la'aretz* — there is no basis for putting the man in *cherem*.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot works through the relationship between the two types of *kilayim* with precision. First: Rabbi Yoshiya's requirement of all three seeds simultaneously — wheat, barley, and a grape seed — applies specifically to *kilayim hakerem*, *kilayim* of the vineyard. By contrast, *kilayim zeraim* — the prohibition of mixed seeds in a field, without a grapevine — requires only two different species and is violated by planting any two species together.

This raises an obvious question: even if the man planting wheat and barley in the vineyard has not violated *kilayim hakerem* (since he did not include a grape seed simultaneously), why is he not liable for *kilayim zeraim*? After all, he has planted two different seeds together. Tosafot addresses this with a clever reading of the verse. *Karmecha lo tizra kilayim* — your vineyard you shall not sow with *kilayim* — implies that in order to constitute *kilayim hakerem*, the *kilayim* must already exist before it comes into the vineyard; that is, one must bring the two grains together with the grapevine simultaneously, as a combined act. By contrast, *sadecha lo tizra kilayim* — your field you shall not sow with *kilayim* — does not carry the same implication, because a *sadeh* is simply a field, requiring no additional element to define it. Planting two seeds in a field is sufficient to create *kilayim zeraim*. But something that is already a vineyard is only a *kerem* by virtue of the grapes, not a plain *sadeh*. Therefore, when wheat and barley are planted inside an existing vineyard, it may not qualify as *kilayim zeraim* since the location is a *kerem*, not a plain *sadeh*.

The *halacha* follows Rabbi Oshaya, as Tosafot concludes: one may plant under a vine as long as all three seeds were not planted simultaneously, and *kilayim zeraim* does not apply in *chutz la'aretz* at all.

### Rav Yosef Practice

Rav Yosef would mix seeds together and plant them. Rashi adds: *v'lo b'kerem* — not in a vineyard. This reflects his position that *kilayim zeraim* does not apply in *chutz la'aretz* and therefore planting two different seeds together in a field outside *eretz yisrael* is not prohibited.

### Abye Distinguishes

Abaye challenges: but the *mishna* says *ha-kilayim mi-divrei sofrim* — *kilayim* is rabbinic! Even if we allow *kilayim zeraim* in *chutz la'aretz*, should it not at least be prohibited rabbinically? The response: *lo kashya, kan b'kilai kerem, kan b'kilai zrayim* — there is no difficulty, because we must distinguish sharply between the two. *Kilayim hakerem* — the vineyard mixture — is of such severity that even in *eretz yisrael* the produce is *assur behana'ah*, and therefore the rabbis extended its prohibition to *chutz la'aretz* as well. *Kilayim zeraim* — the mixing of seeds — is never *assur behana'ah* even in *eretz yisrael*; accordingly, the rabbis made no such extension, and it is not prohibited in *chutz la'aretz* at all.

### Summary of Rules

Let us now draw together the full picture that emerges from this *sugya*. In *eretz yisrael*: *kilayim hakerem* is biblically prohibited, both the act and the produce, which is *assur behana'ah*. *Kilayim zeraim* is prohibited as an act but the produce is not *assur behana'ah* — one may eat the fruit or vegetables that grew from mixed seeds. Outside *eretz yisrael*: crossbreeding animals is biblically prohibited — a *d'oraita* prohibition with no consequence for the produce (one may use a mule). Grafting trees (*harkavat ilan*) is similarly a *d'oraita* prohibition — but again, with no consequence for the produce: the fruit of a grafted tree is entirely permitted. *Kilayim hakerem* in *chutz la'aretz* is rabbinically prohibited, but only in the narrow definition of Rabbi Yoshiya — planting all three seeds (wheat, barley, and grape) simultaneously. *Kilayim zeraim* in *chutz la'aretz* is not prohibited at all, neither biblically nor rabbinically.

The only prohibition that renders produce *assur behana'ah*, whether in *eretz yisrael* or *chutz la'aretz*, is *kilayim hakerem* — and this is why the *mishna* discussing doubtful *kilayim* specifically chose the case of vegetables growing near a vineyard. The Rambam in *Ma'achalos Assuros*, chapter 13, reflects this: among the prohibitions that are of the *Torah* with respect to the produce of the earth, he lists *chadash*, *kilayim hakerem*, *tevel*, and *orlah* — not *kilayim zeraim*.

### Hybrid Fruits OK

This last point has important practical implications. Hybrid fruits — nectarines, tangelos, and similar products of botanical grafting or selective cultivation — are not *assur behana'ah* and are entirely permitted to eat. The reason is that *kilayim zeraim* never renders produce forbidden even where the act is prohibited, and *harkavat ilan*, while prohibited as an act even in *chutz la'aretz*, similarly carries no consequence for the fruit. One may make a *bracha* and eat the fruit of a grafted tree without any *halachic* concern. The only category of hybrid that would raise a concern regarding the produce is *kilayim hakerem*, which requires the specific combination of grain and grapevine — a scenario that does not describe the commercially available hybrid fruits of our time.

One detail does remain somewhat puzzling: that one should have a *d'oraita issur* such as *harkavat ilan* with no *gezeirah* extending the prohibition to the produce. Compare, for example, *basar b'chalav*, where some *rishonim* explain that the *Torah* prohibited eating the mixture precisely because there had to be some tangible consequence to the act of cooking them together. Here, by contrast, the act is gravely prohibited and yet the fruit emerges entirely permitted. Perhaps the explanation is that the concern of *kilayim* is the act of disrupting the divine order of creation through unnatural combination, rather than the product of that combination — and therefore the prohibition attaches to the act, not to its fruit.

## Reward/Punishment

### Mishna: One Mitzvah

We now turn to what is a welcome *aggadic* interlude — though it opens into some of the deepest theology in the tractate. The *mishna* states: *kol ha'oseh mitzvah achas metivin lo u'ma'arichin lo yamav v'nochel et ha'aretz* — whoever performs even one *mitzvah*, good is extended to him, his days are lengthened, and he inherits the land. *Nochel et ha'aretz* is linguistically picking up the theme of *eretz yisrael* that has dominated the preceding discussion, whether it is meant literally, metaphorically, or simply as a literary echo. And the passage concludes with the negative: whoever does not perform even one *mitzvah* receives none of these benefits. The literary structure also continues the *kol* formulation we have seen throughout — *kol mitzvos ha'av al ha'ben*, *kol mitzvos aseh she'hazman gerama*, and now *kol ha'oseh mitzvah* — now turning from the categorization of *mitzvos* to the power of performing a single one.

### Mishna Peah

This *mishna* appears to be in tension with the first *mishna* of *Peah*, which specifies a particular list of *mitzvos* for which one eats the fruit of the reward in this world: *kibbud av v'em, gemilus chasadim, hachnasat orchim, havaa't shalom bein adam l'chavero*, and *talmud Torah keneged kulam*. (The inclusion of *hachnasat orchim* in some versions is itself subject to textual question.) If any single *mitzvah* yields reward in this world, as our *mishna* in Kiddushin implies, what is distinctive about the list in *Peah*? Conversely, if the *Peah* list is exhaustive, then our *mishna* seems to overclaim.

It is worth pausing to note that the *mishna* in *Peah* may not be making a purely theological claim about divine reward. It is entirely possible to read *ochel peiroteihem ba'olam hazeh* as describing the natural and pragmatic fruits of those specific *mitzvos* — the person who honors parents, does acts of kindness, brings peace between people, and engages in *Torah* study will experience the concrete benefits of those behaviors in this world, quite apart from any supernatural reward. Research on human happiness consistently finds that acts of generosity toward others yield greater personal satisfaction than self-directed spending. The intrinsic payoff of these particular *mitzvos* may be precisely what the *mishna* is pointing to. The *gemara*, however, reads the conflict textually — the language of reward and its this-worldly versus other-worldly character — and proceeds to resolve it on those terms.

### Rav Yehuda Balance

Rav Yehuda resolves the tension by reframing the *mishna* entirely: *kol oseh mitzvah achas yeseira al zechuyosav meitivin lo* — the *mishna* is not saying that the reward for performing a *mitzvah* is delivered in this world. Rather, it is describing what happens when a person is at exact equilibrium — fifty percent merits, fifty percent demerits — and this one additional *mitzvah* tips the scale in his favor. In that case, *meitivin lo*, and it is as though he fulfilled the entire *Torah*. And correspondingly, the person who fails to perform one *mitzvah* when he is at fifty-fifty tips his scale in the negative direction. This reading confines the *mishna*'s applicability to the specific and relatively rare scenario of a precisely balanced accounting — which is a much narrower and less dramatically satisfying claim than a straightforward reading would suggest.

The question then becomes: what distinguishes the *mitzvos* listed in *Peah*, where this-worldly reward is promised even without the fifty-fifty precondition? The *gemara* answers in the name of Rav Shmaya: if one of those *mitzvos* from *Peah* is already among your fifty percent of merits, then it functions as a slight additional weight — you do not need a separate extra *mitzvah* to tip the scale, because that particular *mitzvah* itself carries additional weight. Our *mishna* then describes the case where none of the *Peah* mitzvos are part of your fifty percent, and you need an additional act to tip the balance.

### Baraita Opposites

The *gemara* then raises a further challenge from a *baraita* that teaches the precise opposite of what our *mishna* predicts. The *baraita* states: *kol she-zechuyosav merubin me-avonosav me-arin lo* — whoever has more merits than sins, it is done badly for him, *ve-domeh ke-mi she-saraf kol ha-Torah kullah* — and he will appear as though he burned the entire *Torah*; his suffering will be so severe that it will look as though he committed the greatest sins imaginable. Conversely, *kol she-avonosav merubin mi-zechuyosav meitivin lo* — one whose sins outweigh his merits will have it well in this world — *ve-domeh ke-mi she-kiyyem kol ha-Torah kullah* — and it will look as though he fulfilled the entire *Torah*.

This, of course, is the classic theodicy invoked to explain *tzaddik ve-ra lo*: God cleans the slate of the righteous through suffering in this world, and rewards the wicked in this world so as to deny them any credit in the world to come. But it directly contradicts our *mishna*, which promises that doing a *mitzvah* leads to good outcomes.

### Abye Days Theory

Abaye's resolution — and Tosafot's reading of Abaye is cleaner than Rashi's here — is that these opposing outcomes operate on the level of individual days rather than one's entire life. The *mishna* in Kiddushin describes the overall trajectory of one's life: a *tzaddik* will ultimately have it good and a *rasha* will ultimately have it bad. But the *baraita* describes what happens on any given day: there will be specific days on which the *tzaddik* suffers greatly — as though he had done the worst things imaginable — and specific days on which the *rasha* prospers remarkably. The two sources are thus not in conflict; they describe different timeframes.

### Rabbi Yaakov

Rava offers a different resolution entirely: the *baraita* that teaches these counterintuitive outcomes reflects the view of Rabbi Yaakov, because *de-ammar sechar mitzvah be-hai alma lekka* — Rabbi Yaakov maintains that there is no reward for *mitzvos* in this world whatsoever. All reward is reserved exclusively for the world to come. According to Rabbi Yaakov, the *tzaddik* suffering in this world is not a theodicy requiring explanation — it is simply the expected state of affairs. This world is not where the divine accounting is settled. Our *mishna* in Kiddushin, which speaks of this-worldly reward, reflects the majority view; the *baraita* reflects the position of Rabbi Yaakov.

### Problem: Tzaddik Vra Lo

Rabbi Yaakov's position is articulated in a *baraita* that explores it through concrete cases. Rabbi Yaakov says: there is not a single *mitzvah* written in the *Torah* alongside its stated reward where the real fulfillment of that reward does not depend on *techiyas hamessim* — the resurrection of the dead — rather than this-worldly realization. Consider *kibbud av v'em*, which promises *lema'an ya'arichun yamecha u-le'ma'an yitav lach*. Consider *shilluach haken*, which likewise promises *lema'an yitav lach ve-ha'arachta yamim*. Now consider the following case: a father tells his son to go up to a tower and bring down some chicks. The son goes up, performs *shilluach haken* by sending away the mother bird, takes the chicks, and on the way down falls and dies. *Heichen tovas yamav shel zeh? Ve-heichen arichas yamav shel zeh?* Where is the good that was promised? Where is the length of days? This is the problem of *tzaddik ve-ra lo* in its starkest and most personal form.

### Solution: Olam Haba

Rabbi Yaakov's answer is sweeping and unambiguous: *lema'an yitav lach* — to the world that is entirely good; *lema'an ya'arichun yamecha* — to the world that is entirely long. Every promise of reward in the *Torah* refers to *olam haba*, a world that is *kullo tov* and *kullo aroch*. There is no commitment in the *Torah* to reward in this world. This resolution eliminates the problem entirely by relocating its terms: the promises are real, but they belong to a different register of existence altogether.

### Hypothetical Challenge

The *gemara* immediately probes this answer. Perhaps, it suggests, the case Rabbi Yaakov describes — where someone performs two *mitzvos* and immediately suffers a fatal accident — is purely hypothetical, and in reality it never happens that way. Perhaps everyone who has ever honored their parents and performed *shilluach haken* has in fact lived long. If so, Rabbi Yaakov has built his theology on an imagined scenario that has no real-world instantiation.

The response is that *Rabbi Yaakov maiseh chaza* — Rabbi Yaakov actually witnessed such an event, and therefore he knows it is not hypothetical. The *gemara* continues probing: perhaps the young man died because he was harboring sinful thoughts at the time, and it was those thoughts, not the failure of the promise, that caused his death. To this the *gemara* responds: *machashavah ra'ah ein ha-kadosh baruch hu metzarfah le-ma'aseh* — God does not combine a sinful thought with an act and punish a person for it. Mere thought does not constitute transgression.

Perhaps, then, he was harboring thoughts of *avodah zarah* — for which there is a biblical source (*lema'an tpos es beis yisrael be-libbam*) suggesting that even the contemplation of idolatry can be held against a person? To this the *gemara* effectively says: if anything, that actually reinforces Rabbi Yaakov's point. *I-sal-ka da'atach sechar mitzvah be-hai alma* — if you truly believed that *mitzvos* are rewarded in this world — *ammai lo aggen mitzvah aleh ke-heichi de-lo leyessi lidei hirhur* — then the *mitzvah* he was in the midst of performing should have protected him from having that idolatrous thought in the first place. The very fact that the *mitzvah* failed to protect him is itself evidence that *mitzvos* carry no this-worldly protective guarantee. The real world does not work the way a naive reading of the *Torah*'s promises would suggest, and therefore those promises must be referring to something beyond this world entirely.
