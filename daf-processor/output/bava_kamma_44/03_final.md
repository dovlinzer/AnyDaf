# Bava Kamma 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kofer & Skilah Link

### Prior Discussion

We pick up at the bottom third of 43b, in the middle of a broad discussion about *kofer*. The previous sugya established a rich set of debates about whether *kofer* — the ransom payment owed when a habitual ox kills a person — and the *shloshim shel eved* — the thirty-shekel payment when an ox kills a slave — are linked to *skilah*, the stoning of the ox. A somewhat ironic pattern emerged: there is actually stronger textual evidence linking the *shloshim shel eved* to *skilah* than there is linking *kofer* to *skilah*, even though intuitively one might have expected the opposite. The *gemara* also explored whether, in cases where neither *kofer* nor the thirty-shekel payment applies, there might be a fallback payment of ordinary *tashlumin* for the value of a human life — a category that does not exist when one person kills another, but which might apply when an ox kills a person, or even when a fire kills a person.

### Rav Dimi: Kofer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״כֹּפֶר״; מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִם כֹּפֶר״? לְרַבּוֹת כּוֹפֶר שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – כְּכוֹפֶר בְּכַוָּונָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, he reported that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It would have been sufficient for the verse to state: **"A ransom** is laid on him." **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "If a ransom** is laid on him" (Exodus 21:30)? It is **to include** liability to pay **ransom** in a case where the ox killed **unintentionally,** just **as** one is liable to pay **ransom** when it killed **intentionally,** in contrast to Rabba's opinion.

The new passage begins with the formula *ki ata Rav Dimi amar Rabbi Yochanan*. Rabbi Yochanan's teaching focuses on a textual anomaly in the relevant verse: the Torah could have simply written *kofer*, but instead it writes *im kofer yushat alav* — "if a *kofer* is placed upon him." The word *im*, meaning "if," seems superfluous. Is there actually any scenario in which *kofer* would not be paid? What alternative is there — executing the owner? The extra word *im* must therefore be doing halachic work. Rabbi Yochanan concludes that it comes *l'rabot kofer shelo b'kavana k'kofer b'kavana* — to teach that *kofer* is paid even when the ox gored unintentionally, as in the case where it tried to kill an animal and killed a human being. In such a case, the ox is not stoned, yet *kofer* is still owed. This decouples *kofer* from *skilah*.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, עֶבֶד נָמֵי – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִם עֶבֶד״? לְרַבּוֹת עֶבֶד שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – כְּעֶבֶד בְּכַוָּונָה! וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שׁוֹר שֶׁהֵמִית אֶת הָעֶבֶד שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – פָּטוּר מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים שְׁקָלִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Dimi: **If** Rabbi Yochanan's interpretation **is so,** it should be implemented with regard to the fine for a Canaanite **slave as well,** and it would have been sufficient for the verse to state: The ox gores a slave or a maidservant, he shall give to their master thirty shekels of silver. **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "If** the ox gores **a slave"** (Exodus 21:32)? It is **to include** liability in a case where the ox killed a Canaanite **slave unintentionally,** just **as** when it killed **a slave intentionally. And if you would say** that **indeed** this is the *halakha*, **but doesn't Reish Lakish say** that for **an ox that killed a slave unintentionally** its owner is **exempt from** paying the **thirty shekels?**

Abaye immediately presses a parallel question: if the extra word *im* by *kofer* teaches that *kofer* is paid independently of *skilah*, why not apply the same logic to the *shloshim shel eved*? The verse there also contains the word *im* — *im eved yigach hashor o amah kesef shloshim shekalim* — and so, by the same hermeneutic, one could argue that the thirty-shekel payment is also decoupled from the stoning of the ox.

### Reish Lakish Disagrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּבְרָא אַגַּבְרָא קָא רָמֵית?!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Dimi **said to** Abaye: **Are you setting** the statement of one **man against** the statement of another **man?** It could be that although Reish Lakish maintains that he is exempt, Rabbi Yochanan holds that he is liable.

The *gemara* entertains this possibility and then presents a counterpoint: Reish Lakish explicitly rules that the ox does not pay the thirty shekels if it is not stoned — *shor ein mitat eved shelo b'kavana patur mishloshim shekalim*. Abaye's interlocutor, realizing that this simply pits one *amora* against another, responds with *gavra agavra karamit* — you are merely opposing one authority against another. The implied position of Rabbi Yochanan, if pushed, would be that both *kofer* and the *shloshim shel eved* are decoupled from *skilah* and must be paid independently.

### Ravin's Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״עֶבֶד״; מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִם עֶבֶד״? לְרַבּוֹת עֶבֶד שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – כְּעֶבֶד בְּכַוָּונָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael, he confirmed that this is the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** as he **says** that it would have been sufficient for the verse to state: "The ox gores a **slave." What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "If** the ox gores **a slave"?** It is **to include** liability in a case where the ox killed **a slave unintentionally,** just **as** when it killed **a slave intentionally.**

This inference is confirmed when the *gemara* reports a second transmission. When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he too quoted Rabbi Yochanan — but this time the teaching was explicitly about the *shloshim shel eved* rather than about *kofer*. Rabbi Yochanan stated directly that the extra word *im* by *eved* teaches *l'rabot eved shelo b'kavana k'eved b'kavana* — that the thirty-shekel payment applies even when the ox gored the slave unintentionally. Rabbi Yochanan thus explicitly holds that both payments are decoupled from *skilah*.

### Hermeneutics Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ נָמֵי, נֵימָא: מִדְּ״עֶבֶד״ – ״אִם עֶבֶד״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ, ״כֹּפֶר״ – ״אִם כֹּפֶר״ נָמֵי לָא דָּרֵישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And** according **to Reish Lakish, let us say similarly** that **from** the fact **that he does not interpret** the difference between the terms **"a slave"** and **"if a slave"** to derive an additional *halakha*, it may be inferred that **he does not interpret** the difference between the terms **"a ransom"** and **"if a ransom" either,** and holds that ransom is not paid if the incident was unintentional.

This raises a sharp question about Reish Lakish's position. Since Reish Lakish is only cited as ruling that the *shloshim shel eved* is linked to *skilah*, one might wonder whether he would similarly link *kofer* to *skilah*. After all, if Reish Lakish does not interpret the extra *im* by *eved* to teach decoupling, perhaps he does not interpret the extra *im* by *kofer* either. The *gemara* raises this possibility: *mididei eved im eved lo darish kofer im kofer nami lo darish* — if he does not derive anything from the word *im* in the context of *eved*, perhaps he derives nothing from the word *im* in the context of *kofer* either, yielding a position in which both payments remain linked to *skilah*.

### Textual Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא; ״עֶבֶד״ – ״אִם עֶבֶד״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ, ״כֹּפֶר״ – ״אִם כֹּפֶר״ דָּרֵישׁ. וּמַאי שְׁנָא? ״עֶבֶד״ – ״אִם עֶבֶד״ לָא כְּתִיב בִּמְקוֹם תַּשְׁלוּמִין, ״כֹּפֶר״ – ״אִם כֹּפֶר״ כְּתִיב בִּמְקוֹם תַּשְׁלוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said: No,** that is not a valid comparison. Although Reish Lakish **does not interpret** the difference between the terms **"a slave"** and **"if a slave," he does interpret** the difference between the terms **"a ransom"** and **"if a ransom."** **And what is different** between the two verses? The terms **"a slave"** and **"if a slave" are not written where** the liability for **payment** is stated, but rather in the presentation of the case: "If the ox gores a slave." Therefore, the use of the word "if" is warranted. By contrast, the terms **"a ransom"** and **"if a ransom" are written where** the liability for **payment** is stated, where it would have been sufficient for the verse to state: "A ransom is laid on him." Therefore, the superfluous word "if" lends itself to interpretation to include liability to pay ransom even in a case where the killing was unintentional.

The *gemara* rejects this inference. The two cases are textually distinct. By the *eved* payment, the word *im* appears in the conditional clause that sets up the scenario — *im eved yigach hashor* — not in the verse's description of the payment itself. That *im* is simply framing the case, not teaching anything about when the payment applies. By *kofer*, however, the word *im* appears in the very statement about the payment: *im kofer yushat alav* — it is part of the payment clause itself. An extra word situated at the moment the Torah describes the payment can legitimately teach us something about the scope of that payment. Reish Lakish therefore reads the *im* by *kofer* as decoupling it from *skilah*, while declining to read the *im* by *eved* in the same way, since that word occupies a different textual position.

### Three Positions

The *gemara* has thus identified three distinct positions. The first, which was the working assumption of much of the previous discussion, is that both *kofer* and the *shloshim shel eved* are tightly coupled to *skilah* — you pay neither without the ox being stoned. The second position, that of Rabbi Yochanan, is that both payments are decoupled from *skilah* — you pay both even when the ox is not stoned. The third, which the *gemara* now attributes to Reish Lakish as a coherent middle position, is that *kofer* is decoupled from *skilah* — a widely attested view — while the *shloshim shel eved* remains linked to *skilah*, grounded in the tighter textual connection in that verse. This last position is, as noted, somewhat ironic: one would expect the *kofer*, which functions as *kaparah* and is more conceptually tied to the gravity of the killing, to be the payment linked to *skilah*, while the *shloshim shel eved*, which appears to be straightforward monetary compensation, might more naturally be paid regardless of whether the ox is stoned. Yet the textual evidence points in the opposite direction.

## Goring of Children

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּבֵן אוֹ בְּבַת. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אוֹ בֵן יִגָּח אוֹ בַת יִגָּח״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל הַקְּטַנִּים כַּגְּדוֹלִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And the same** *halakha* applies **in** a case where the ox killed **a boy or in** a case where it killed **a girl. The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"Whether it has gored a son or has gored a daughter"** (Exodus 21:31), **to deem** the owner **liable for** the death of **minors** just **as for adults.**

The *gemara* now turns to the next portion of the biblical passage: *o ven yigach o vat yigach kamishpat hazeh ye'aseh lo* — "whether it gores a son or a daughter, the same law applies." The question immediately presents itself: why should the Torah need to state this? We have already established that an ox which kills a person is stoned and, if it is a *muad*, *kofer* is paid. Does anyone imagine that the life of a child is treated differently? As noted, historians point to the legal context of the ancient Near East, where codes prescribed that if your ox killed the child of another person, your child would be killed in retaliation. The Torah is emphatically rejecting that framework: there is no collective punishment, no killing of the owner or his family. You stone the ox and pay *kofer* if it is habitual. But beyond that historical context, the *gemara* proceeds to give the verse rigorous halachic content.

### Tanu Rabbanan

A *baraita* opens: *o ven yigach o vat yigach* — the Torah teaches that one is *chayav* for the goring of minors just as for the goring of adults. This is the operative *halacha* the verse establishes.

### Din Hu Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא – הוֹאִיל וְחִיֵּיב אָדָם בְּאָדָם, וְחִיֵּיב שׁוֹר בְּאָדָם; מָה כְּשֶׁחִיֵּיב אָדָם בְּאָדָם – לָא שְׁנָא בֵּין קְטַנִּים לִגְדוֹלִים, אַף כְּשֶׁחִיֵּיב שׁוֹר בְּאָדָם – לֹא תַּחְלוֹק בּוֹ בֵּין קְטַנִּים לִגְדוֹלִים!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **And** could **this not** be derived through **logical inference? Since** the Torah **renders a person liable** to receive court-imposed capital punishment **for** killing another **person, and,** similarly, **renders an ox liable** to be killed **for** killing **a person,** it should be derived that **just as when** the Torah **renders a person liable for** killing **a person** the Torah makes **no distinction between** killing **minors and** killing **adults, so too, when it renders an ox liable** to be killed **for** killing **a person, do not differentiate with regard to it between minors and adults.**

The *baraita* then asks whether we actually needed the verse to teach this, or whether logic alone would have sufficed. The argument from logic proceeds as follows. By a person who kills a person, the Torah makes no distinction between killing an adult and killing a child — it is murder either way. Similarly, when an ox kills a person, we have the laws of *skilah* and *kofer*. By analogy: just as the law of murder applies equally to adult and minor victims, the laws governing an ox that kills should apply equally as well. Furthermore, the *baraita* adds a *kal vachomer*: in the domain of murder (*adam b'adam*), when the perpetrator is a minor, he is not prosecuted — yet even so, when the victim is a minor, the full law applies. By an ox, no distinction is made based on whether the ox itself is young or old. Therefore, certainly the law should apply equally whether the victim is an adult or a minor.

### Four Payments Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ אָדָם בְּאָדָם – שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים, תֹּאמַר בְּשׁוֹר – שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ בֵן יִגָּח אוֹ בַת יִגָּח״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל הַקְּטַנִּים כִּגְדוֹלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this claim: **No,** this cannot be derived by logic alone. **If you say** that **a person** who kills **a person** is liable even when the victim is a minor, this may be due to the extra severity in the case of a human assailant, **as he is liable to** pay **four types of indemnity** for causing injury; pain, humiliation, medical costs, and loss of livelihood, in addition to payment for the actual damage. **Shall you** also **say** that this is the *halakha* **with regard to an ox, whose** owner **is not liable to** pay these **four types of indemnity?** Clearly, this *halakha* cannot be derived merely through logical comparison between the two cases. Therefore, **the verse states: "Whether it has gored a son or has gored a daughter," to render** it **liable for minors** as well **as adults.**

The *baraita* rejects the logical argument as insufficient. Even if murder law treats adult and minor victims identically, that does not mean the goring laws must follow. Murder law is a more stringent and comprehensive category: when a human kills a human, there are four additional payments for bodily injury — *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripuy*, *shevet*, and *boshet*. An ox, by contrast, never incurs those additional payments. Since the domain of human-on-human harm is altogether more weighted, perhaps one cannot automatically transfer its rules to the domain of ox-on-human harm. It is possible that the Torah would treat the goring and killing of a child differently from the goring and killing of an adult, precisely because the category of ox-caused death is a less robust legal framework. Therefore, an explicit verse is necessary.

### Extra Pasuk

The verse *o ven yigach o vat yigach* is thus required: *l'chayev al haktanim k'gedolim* — to obligate in the same way for minor victims as for adult victims.

### Muad vs Tam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּמוּעָדִין, בְּתָם מִנַּיִן? דִּין הוּא – הוֹאִיל וְחִיֵּיב בְּאִישׁ וְאִשָּׁה, וְחִיֵּיב בְּבֵן וּבַת; מָה כְּשֶׁחִיֵּיב בְּאִישׁ וְאִשָּׁה – לֹא חִלַּקְתָּ בּוֹ בֵּין תָּם לְמוּעָד; אַף כְּשֶׁחִיֵּיב בְּבֵן וּבַת – לֹא תַּחְלוֹק בּוֹ בֵּין תָּם לְמוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And I have** derived this *halakha* **only with regard to forewarned** oxen; **from where** do I derive that **in** the case of **an innocuous** ox, it is killed if it kills a boy or a girl? **Logical inference:** **Just as** the Torah **renders** an ox **liable** to be killed **for** killing **a man or a woman, and** likewise **renders** it **liable** to be killed **for** killing **a boy or a girl;** then **just as when it renders** it **liable** to be killed **for** killing **a man or a woman you do not differentiate between an innocuous** ox **and a forewarned** ox, as both are stoned, **so too, when it renders** an ox **liable** to be killed **for** killing **a boy or a girl do not differentiate between an innocuous** ox **and a forewarned** ox.

The *gemara* now raises a further question. The verse about the goring of children appears in a context that has been discussing a *muad*. So one could argue that the verse establishes equal treatment of children and adults only in the case of a *muad* ox — the one that is subject to stoning and *kofer*. How do we know the same law applies for a *tam*, the ox in its first three incidents? The *gemara* answers by constructing a *din hu* from within the very text. The opening of the *parsha* states *ki yigach shor et ish o et ishah vamat* — distinguishing between a man and a woman as victims of the ox. The Torah goes out of its way to say: do not treat male and female victims differently. And that principle — no distinction between man and woman — appears earlier in the *parsha*, in a context that refers to the *tam*. The argument then proceeds: just as when the Torah establishes equal treatment of man and woman, that equality applies whether the ox is a *tam* or a *muad* — since the *ish/ishah* rule appears in the *tam* context — similarly, when the Torah establishes equal treatment of child and adult, that equality should also apply to both *tam* and *muad*. The repeated phrase *kamishpat hazeh ye'aseh lo* — "the same law shall be applied to him" — even explicitly signals that equality across categories is the Torah's intent.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא: וּמָה אָדָם בְּאָדָם – שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה בּוֹ קְטַנִּים כִּגְדוֹלִים, חִיֵּיב בּוֹ עַל הַקְּטַנִּים כִּגְדוֹלִים; שׁוֹר בְּאָדָם – שֶׁעָשָׂה בּוֹ קְטַנִּים כִּגְדוֹלִים, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁחַיָּיב עַל הַקְּטַנִּים כִּגְדוֹלִים?
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** it **could be inferred ***a fortiori*: If** in the case of **a person** killing **a person** the Torah **does not render minors** entirely **like adults with regard to this** act, inasmuch as minors are exempt from liability for killing, but nevertheless **it renders** a person **liable for** killing **minors** as well **as adults,** then in the case of **an ox** killing **a person, where** the Torah **renders small** oxen **like large** ones **with regard to this** act, as a young calf that kills a person is killed just as an adult ox that kills a person, **is it not logical that** the Torah **renders** it **liable for** killing **minors,** i.e., a boy or a girl, just **as** for killing **adults?** Why is it necessary for the verse to teach this *halakha*?

The *baraita* further proposes a *kal vachomer* from *ish/ishah* to *ben/bat*. The reasoning goes: by a man and a woman, they are fully liable in the domain of *nezikin* — they can be held responsible when they cause damage. Despite having this disadvantaged status as potential tortfeasors, the Torah nonetheless says do not distinguish between them as victims, whether the ox is a *tam* or a *muad*. Children, by contrast, have a privileged position in the domain of *nezikin* — they are never liable for damage they cause, since they are not yet obligated in *mitzvot* and the act of the minor does not give rise to parental liability. This is one of the striking *chiddushim* of Bava Kamma: if one's ox causes damage, the owner pays because it is *mamon ha-mazik*. But a minor is not anyone's property in the relevant legal sense, and so neither the child nor the parents are obligated. The parents' abstract negligence in failing to supervise is insufficient; the formal categories of *arba avot nezikin* require a more direct causal link. The *kal vachomer* then reasons: since men and women — who are disadvantaged in *nezikin* — are treated equally as victims in both *tam* and *muad* cases, children — who are privileged in *nezikin* — should certainly be treated equally as victims. If the heavier category is covered, the lighter should be as well.

### Gemara's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: וְכִי דָּנִין קַל מֵחָמוּר לְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו? אִם הֶחְמִיר בְּמוּעָד הֶחָמוּר, תַּחְמִיר בְּתָם הַקַּל? וְעוֹד: אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּאִישׁ וְאִשָּׁה – שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיבִין בְּמִצְוֹת, תֹּאמַר בְּבֵן וּבַת – שֶׁפְּטוּרִין מִן הַמִּצְוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers that **you could say** in response: **But does one derive** the *halakha* of **a lenient** matter **from a stringent** matter **in order to** be more **stringent with regard to it? If** the Torah **is stringent with regard to** the case of **a forewarned** ox, **which is a stringent** matter, rendering it liable to be killed for killing a minor, does that mean that **you should be stringent with regard to an innocuous** ox, **which is** a relatively **lenient** matter? **And furthermore,** there is another reason to reject the earlier opinion: **If you say** that an innocuous ox is liable to be killed **for** killing **a man or a woman, as they are obligated to** observe the **mitzvot,** which gives them importance, does that mean that **you should say** the same **with regard to a boy or a girl, who are exempt from the mitzvot?**

The *gemara* rejects this *kal vachomer* on two grounds. First, the *kal vachomer* is moving from a stringent case (*muad*, where full liability applies) to a more lenient case (*tam*, where liability is partial). One does not ordinarily make a *kal vachomer* from the more stringent case in order to generate a ruling for the more lenient one. Second, even the underlying comparison to *ish/ishah* is imperfect: adults — men and women — are obligated in *mitzvot* and generally treated as more weighty subjects under *halacha* than minors. One could argue that the Torah's equal treatment of adult victims reflects their general halachic weight, and one cannot automatically extend that to children.

### Repeated Word

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ בֵן יִגָּח אוֹ בַת יִגָּח״ – נְגִיחָה בְּתָם נְגִיחָה בְּמוּעָד, נְגִיחָה לְמִיתָה נְגִיחָה לִנְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** Since this *halakha* could not have been derived through logic alone, **the verse states: "Whether it has gored a son or has gored a daughter,"** stating the phrase "has gored" twice, to teach that it is referring both to the **goring of an innocuous** ox and to the **goring of a forewarned** ox, and both to **goring** that causes **death** and to **goring** that causes **injury.** In all these cases the owner of the ox is liable even if the ox gores a minor.

The *gemara* therefore offers an alternative derivation. The verse *o ven yigach o vat yigach* contains the word *yigach* — "it gores" — twice, once for *ben* and once for *bat*. The verse could have been written *o ven o vat yigach*, with a single *yigach* at the end. The repetition of *yigach* is deliberate: it teaches that one should not distinguish between different types of *gorings* — *negicha b'tam*, *negicha b'muad*, *negicha le-mita*, *negicha le-nezikin*. Whether the ox is a *tam* or a *muad*, whether the outcome is death or injury, the same law applies when the victim is a minor as when the victim is an adult. The repeated word closes all possible distinctions.

## Unintentional Goring

### Exemption Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שֶׁהָיָה מִתְחַכֵּךְ בַּכּוֹתֶל – וְנָפַל עַל הָאָדָם; נִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה – וְהָרַג אֶת הָאָדָם; לְגוֹי – וְהָרַג בֶּן יִשְׂרָאֵל; לִנְפָלִים – וְהָרַג בֶּן קַיָּימָא; פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **an ox was rubbing against a wall, and** as a result the wall **fell on a person** and killed him; or if the ox **intended to kill** another **animal but killed a person;** or if it intended **to** kill **a gentile but killed a Jew;** or intended **to** kill **a non-viable baby but killed a viable person;** in all these cases the ox is **exempt** from being killed.

The *gemara* returns from its extended *derasha* of these verses to applied *halacha*, now examining the *mishna*'s cases of exemption from *skilah*. Three scenarios are presented. In the first, an ox was rubbing against a wall to scratch itself, the wall collapsed, and a person was killed — entirely incidental to the ox's purpose. In the second, the ox intended to kill an animal but killed a person instead. In the third, it intended to kill a *Canaanite* — the text uses the term *Canaanite* as a substitution, though the original manuscript formulation referred to a *goi* — and killed a Jew, or it intended to kill a *nefalim*, a fetus or infant not yet established as viable within its first thirty days, and killed a *ben kayama*, a viable child. In all these cases, the *mishna* rules *patur* — exempt from *skilah*.

### Owner-Ox Parallel

The underlying principle governing all these exemptions is *mitat habaal kemitat hashor* — the death of the ox mirrors the circumstances in which the owner would be executed. The ox is stoned only when the act it committed would, if performed by a human, constitute capital murder. Purely incidental killing does not meet that standard; nor does intending to kill one category of victim but killing another. The case that is theologically and morally most challenging is the killing of a non-Jew. The *gemara* is candid that in capital law, killing a non-Jew does not trigger the same penalty as killing a Jew. The footnote in the printed text attempts to soften this by noting that non-Jewish law would itself have exempted a person who intended to kill one individual and killed another, and that the ox's exemption is being assessed by that standard. But this explanation is far from satisfying. There are Tannaitic sources and explicit rulings by the *Sefer HaChinuch* and others affirming that the act of killing a non-Jew is indeed a biblical transgression of *lo tirtzach* — the prohibition against murder — and that what differs is only the court-imposed capital punishment. More troubling is the *Rambam*, whose printed text reads *kol horeg nefesh b'Yisrael over b'lo ta'aseh*, limiting the violation of *lo tirtzach* to killing a Jew, in contrast to the manuscript reading of *kol horeg nefesh ben adam*, which is universal. The tension in these sources is real and should not be minimized.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: פָּטוּר מִמִּיתָה, וְחַיָּיב בְּכוֹפֶר. וְרַב אָמַר: פָּטוּר מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel says:** The ox is **exempt from** being put to **death,** since it did not intend to kill, **but** its owner is **liable to** pay **ransom. And Rav says:** They are **exempt from this** liability **and from that** liability.

Having established that these cases are exempt from *skilah*, the *gemara* now asks whether *kofer* is still owed. This returns us directly to the dispute we have been tracking. Shmuel rules: *patur miskila v'chayav b'kofer* — exempt from stoning, but still obligated in *kofer*. This aligns with the position of Rabbi Yochanan that *kofer* is decoupled from *skilah*. Rava rules: *patur mizeh umizeh* — exempt from both. Rava holds that *kofer* and *skilah* remain linked.

### Muad Investigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? הָא תָּם הוּא! כִּדְאָמַר רַב: בְּמוּעָד לִיפּוֹל עַל בְּנֵי אָדָם בְּבוֹרוֹת, הָכָא נָמֵי – בְּמוּעָד לְהִתְחַכֵּךְ עַל בְּנֵי אָדָם בִּכְתָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about Shmuel's opinion: **And why** is he liable to pay ransom? **Isn't** the ox **innocuous** with regard to this action? The Gemara answers: **As Rav says** in a different context, it is referring **to** an ox that was **forewarned with regard to falling on people in pits. Here too,** it is referring **to** an ox that was **forewarned with regard to rubbing against walls,** causing them to fall **on people.**

The *gemara* then presses Shmuel on the mechanics of his ruling. *Kofer* is only owed by a *muad*. In what type of case is the ox in the wall-rubbing scenario a *muad*? At first glance, an ox scratching its back against a wall seems like a straightforward act, not something that would have been repeated three times with warnings to the owner. The *gemara* answers by analogy to a ruling of Rav about an ox that habitually leaned over into wells — attempting to eat vegetation off the walls — and fell onto people below. Rav ruled that such an ox could become a *muad* for this particular behavior. Similarly here, an ox can become a *muad* for rubbing against walls in a way that kills people. But this answer immediately creates a difficulty. If the ox has been established as a *muad* for rubbing against walls and killing people in the process — meaning it has done this multiple times, knowing people were present — then does it not *know* what it is doing? And if it knows people are there and keeps doing it, should it not be treated as acting intentionally, making it subject to *skilah* after all? The *gemara* asks: *bar katla hu* — it should be stoned.

### Ox's Motive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא! בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם – דַּחֲזָא יְרוֹקָא וּנְפַל, אֶלָּא הָכָא – מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? הָכָא נָמֵי – בְּמִתְחַכֵּךְ בַּכּוֹתֶל לַהֲנָאָתוֹ. וּמְנָא יָדְעִינַן? דְּבָתַר דִּנְפַל קָא מִתְחַכַּךְ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if it was forewarned with regard to this behavior, it clearly intended to kill the person and **is** therefore **subject to** being put to **death,** contrary to the ruling in the mishna. The Gemara explains: **Granted there,** in the case where the ox was forewarned with regard to falling on people in pits, it could be **that it saw a vegetable** on the edge of the pit **and** subsequently **fell** in, without any intention to kill. **But here,** where it rubbed against a wall, causing it to fall on a person, and was forewarned with regard to this behavior, **what is there to say** in its defense? The Gemara answers: **Here also** the case is **where it rubbed against the wall for its pleasure** and not in order to kill. The Gemara asks: **And from where do we know** that it did not intend to kill? The Gemara answers: **Because** even **after** the wall **fell it was still rubbing against it,** which proves that this was its intention.

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between the ox's purpose and the incidental consequences of its behavior. In the case of Rav's ox falling into the pit, the animal was attracted to the vegetation on the pit's walls and fell on people while pursuing food — its *hana'ah*, its pleasure or benefit. Even if it knew people were below, it was not *trying* to kill them; it was trying to eat. Similarly, in the wall-rubbing case, the ox is scratching its back for its own comfort. The deaths that result are incidental to its self-interested behavior. This makes it a *muad* — it has demonstrated a habitual pattern of causing death through this behavior — but it does not make it a *kavanah* case, because the ox's intention is self-scratching, not killing.

The *gemara* asks: how do we know the ox's motive was scratching and not killing? The answer is elegant: *d'vatar d'nafal kamchakech bei* — because even after the wall collapsed onto the person, the ox kept on scratching. Its continuing to scratch after the damage was done proves that scratching was its goal all along. It was not trying to kill; it was trying to relieve an itch. The killing was purely incidental.

Tosafot offers a different reading of this entire passage. Tosafot argues that rubbing against a wall for the sake of one's own comfort is an act that falls under the category of *shein* — damage done in pursuit of the animal's own pleasure or benefit — and *shein* is automatically a *muad* from the outset, without requiring three repeated incidents and owner warnings. On this reading, the *gemara* does not require proof that the ox had a prior history of this specific behavior; the nature of the act itself classifies it as *shein muad*. The practical difference would turn on the law of *reshut harabim*: *shein* is exempt when the damage occurs in the public domain, while *keren* is not. If the entire case is analyzed as *shein*, the *kofer* obligation would not apply when the wall-rubbing occurs in a public space. The *gemara*'s plain reading, however, appears to require establishing habitual behavior specific to this type of incident, which does not fit as neatly with the *shein* framework.

### Tzerorot Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי צְרוֹרוֹת נִינְהוּ! אָמַר רַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא: דְּקָאָזֵיל מִינֵּיהּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still, is it** not a case of **pebbles?** Is this case not analogous to damage caused by pebbles inadvertently propelled from under the feet of an animal while it is walking, which is not considered damage caused directly by the ox, but rather, damage caused indirectly? Ransom is not imposed for such indirect killing. **Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana, said:** It is a case **where** the wall **gradually gave way** under the pressure applied by the ox, and so while the ox was still pushing the wall it collapsed and killed the person.

A further complication arises. When the ox rubs against the wall and the wall collapses on a person, the immediate cause of death is not the ox's body but the stones of the wall rolling down — *tzerorot*, the indirect projection of force. *Kofer* and *skilah* are traditionally associated with the ox's direct bodily contact — *gufo* — with the victim. When has *kofer* ever been paid for damage caused by *tzerorot*, by stones rolling as indirect force? This is a genuine difficulty, because the entire case has up to this point been analyzed under *keren* — a direct act of goring — and the substitution of rolling stones for direct body contact seems to undermine the *kofer* claim.

### Direct Contact

Rav Mari son of Rav Kahana resolves this: *dekaza minei minei* — because the ox continues rubbing against the stone even as it rolls down onto the person. The ox's body remains in direct contact with the stone as the stone presses down on the victim. This is not *koach* — indirect force launched and then acting on its own. Rather, it is as though the ox is holding a stick and actively pressing down on the person. The stone is the medium, but the ox's body is continuously driving the force. This is classified as *gufo* — the body of the ox — rather than *koach*. Therefore *kofer* applies.

## Four Combinations

### Baraita Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל – וּתְיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב: יֵשׁ חַיָּיב בְּמִיתָה וּבְכוֹפֶר, וְיֵשׁ חַיָּיב בְּכוֹפֶר וּפָטוּר מִמִּיתָה, וְיֵשׁ חַיָּיב בְּמִיתָה וּפָטוּר מִן הַכּוֹפֶר, וְיֵשׁ פָּטוּר מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel, and** this *baraita* is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav: There are** cases where the ox is **liable to** be put to **death and** the owner is liable **to** pay **ransom, and there are** cases where the owner is **liable to** pay **ransom but** the ox is **exempt from** being put to **death, and there are** cases where the ox is **liable to** be put to **death but** the owner is **exempt from** paying **ransom, and there are** cases where they are **exempt from this** punishment **and from that** one.

The *gemara* now cites a *baraita* that provides a clean, comprehensive framework for all the cases we have been discussing. It states that there are four possible combinations of *mita* — the stoning of the ox — and *kofer*, and that the two obligations are governed by entirely independent variables.

### Case 1: Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? מוּעָד בְּכַוָּונָה – חַיָּיב בְּמִיתָה וּבְכוֹפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** In a case where **a forewarned** ox kills a person **intentionally,** the ox is **liable to** be put to **death and** the owner is liable **to** pay **ransom;**

A *muad* ox that gored with *kavanah* — intentionally — is subject to both *skilah* and *kofer*. The ox is stoned because the act was deliberate and, if performed by a human, would constitute murder. *Kofer* is owed because the ox is a *muad*. Both conditions are satisfied.

### Case 2: Kofer Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּעָד שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – חַיָּיב בְּכוֹפֶר וּפָטוּר מִמִּיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** if **a forewarned** ox kills **unintentionally,** the owner is **liable to** pay **ransom but** the ox is **exempt from** being put to **death;**

A *muad* ox that caused death *shelo b'kavana* — unintentionally, as when it tried to kill an animal and killed a person — owes *kofer* but is not stoned. The absence of *kavanah* means the act does not rise to the level of a capital crime, so no *skilah*. But the *muad* status means *kofer* is still owed.

### Case 3: Skilah Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּם בְּכַוָּונָה – חַיָּיב בְּמִיתָה וּפָטוּר מִכּוֹפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** if **an innocuous** ox kills **intentionally,** the ox is **liable to** be put to **death but** the owner is **exempt from** paying **ransom;**

A *tam* ox that gored with *kavanah* — deliberately, on one of its first three incidents — is stoned but does not pay *kofer*. The deliberate act triggers *skilah*, but the ox is a *tam* and *kofer* is never paid by a *tam*.

### Case 4: Both Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּם שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – פָּטוּר מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** and if **an innocuous** ox kills **unintentionally,** they are **exempt from this** punishment **and from that** one. The *baraita* states explicitly that although a forewarned ox that kills a person unintentionally is exempt from being put to death, its owner is liable to pay ransom, in accordance with Shmuel's opinion, and in contrast with Rav's opinion.

A *tam* ox that caused death *shelo b'kavana* — unintentionally and on a first offense — is exempt from both *skilah* and *kofer*. No *kavanah* means no *skilah*; *tam* status means no *kofer*.

### Independent Factors

The *baraita* thus establishes cleanly that *skilah* depends on *kavanah* and *skilah* alone, while *kofer* depends on whether the ox is a *muad* and nothing else. The two obligations are fully independent of each other, and the four-way combination covers all possibilities. This *baraita* constitutes at minimum a strong position in the longstanding debate: it is an explicit ruling that *kofer* is paid even when *skilah* does not apply, directly challenging those who would link the two. Shmuel is vindicated; Rava's view that both are always coupled is contradicted.

## Unintentional Damage

### Wrong Party Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַנְּזָקִין שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* adds: **And** for **injuries** caused by an ox **unintentionally,** from which the victim is not killed, **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the owner **liable** to pay for the injury **and Rabbi Shimon exempts** him.

The *baraita* continues with a related but distinct question: what is the law when an ox intended to gore one party but injured a different party? This is the *nezek* analog of the *mita* cases we have been discussing. If the ox tried to injure one animal and injured a different person, is the owner obligated to pay damages?

The intuitive answer is yes: why should it matter that the ox missed its intended target? But the question is not trivial. We are dealing with *keren* — the category of goring with intent to harm. *Keren* in its pure form involves the ox intentionally injuring the specific party it gores. Here, the ox had *kavanah l'hazik*, intention to cause harm, but its actual target was different from the party it harmed. Does the mismatch between intended and actual victim affect the liability?

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? יָלֵיף מִכּוֹפְרוֹ; מָה כּוֹפְרוֹ – שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה חַיָּיב, אַף הַנְּזָקִין נָמֵי – שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda?** The Gemara answers: **He derives** the *halakha* with regard to injury caused by the ox **from** the *halakha* with regard to **its** owner's **ransom** payment. **Just as** with regard to **its** owner's **ransom** payment he is **liable** even if the ox gores **unintentionally, so too,** with regard to **injuries** he is **also liable** even if it gores **unintentionally.**

Rabbi Yehuda rules that damages are owed even when the ox harmed an unintended party. He derives this by analogy to *kofer*: just as *kofer* is owed even when the ox killed an unintended victim — as established by the *baraita*'s case of *muad shelo b'kavana* — so too for *nezikin*, damages are owed even when the injured party was not the intended target. The logic is that payment obligations are assessed by what actually occurred, not by whether the outcome matched the ox's intention.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן יָלֵיף מִקְּטָלֵיהּ דְּשׁוֹר, מָה קְטָלֵיהּ – שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה פָּטוּר, אַף נְזָקִין – שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוָּונָה פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon derives** his opinion **from** the *halakha* of the putting to **death of an ox** by the court: **Just as** with regard to **its** being put to **death,** if it kills a person **unintentionally** it is **exempt, so too,** if it causes **injuries unintentionally** its owner is **exempt** from payment.

Rabbi Shimon exempts from damages when the ox harmed an unintended party. He derives this from the law of *skilah*: just as the ox is not stoned when it killed an unintended victim — as in the *mishna*'s cases of *shelo b'kavana* — so too no damages are owed when the ox injured an unintended victim. The standard for both death and damages is whether the ox acted against the party who was actually harmed.

### Payment vs. Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נָמֵי, נֵילַף מִקְּטָלֵיהּ! דָּנִין תַּשְׁלוּמִין מִתַּשְׁלוּמִין, וְאֵין דָּנִין תַּשְׁלוּמִין מִמִּיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the above explanation: **And let Rabbi Yehuda also derive** the *halakha* concerning an ox unintentionally causing injury **from** the *halakha* of **its** being put to **death.** The Gemara answers: In his opinion, **we can derive** a *halakha* with regard to **payment** for injury **from** the *halakha* of ransom, which is another *halakha* with regard to **payment. But we cannot derive** a *halakha* with regard to **payment from** a *halakha* concerning **death.** > > And conversely, the Gemara asks: **And let Rabbi Shimon also derive** the *halakha* here **from** the *halakha* concerning **its** owner's **ransom** payment. The Gemara answers: **We can derive** a *halakha* with regard to the **liability of an ox from** a *halakha* with regard to the **liability of an ox, to the exclusion of** the payment of **ransom, which is the liability of the owner.** Compensation for injury is considered the ox's liability, as it is the ox that caused the injury, whereas the ransom paid is for the owner's atonement. Therefore, the *halakha* concerning injury cannot be derived from the *halakha* of ransom, as they are dissimilar.

The *gemara* explains why each *tanna* adopts their particular analogy. Rabbi Yehuda's position is that the relevant model for *nezikin* is *tashlumin* — payment obligations — and therefore one looks to *kofer*, which is also a payment, as the better analogy. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, focuses on *chiyuvei d'shor* — the obligations that flow directly from what the ox did to the actual victim — and therefore looks to *skilah*, which tracks the ox's act against the actual victim, as the governing model. The *gemara* also offers a second dimension of Rabbi Shimon's reasoning: *kofer* is characterized as *kaparas nefesh* — an atonement for the owner's soul. Because it is *kaphara* and not purely a compensatory payment, it may apply in a broader range of situations. One should therefore not learn the law of ordinary damage payments from *kofer*, whose scope is expanded by its atoning function.

### Mishna's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וְהָרַג אֶת הָאָדָם [וְכוּ׳] – פָּטוּר. הָא נִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת זֶה, וְהָרַג אֶת זֶה – חַיָּיב, מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if an ox **intended to kill** another **animal but killed a person,** or if it intended to kill a person for whom it would not be liable to be put to death but killed a person for whom it would be liable, it is **exempt.** The Gemara infers: If the ox **intended to kill this** person, for whom it would be liable, **but killed that** person instead, it is still **liable.** Accordingly, **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: Even** if the ox **intended to kill this** person **but killed that** person, it is **exempt.**

The *mishna*'s language implies that when an ox tried to gore one person and gored another, the ox is stoned — which assumes liability even when the intended and actual victims do not match. This represents the position of Rabbi Yehuda. As the *gemara* notes, if the ox acted this way and it is treated as a capital offense, then a human who intended to kill one person and killed another would, under the same logic, be treated as a murderer. That is indeed the position of the *rabbanan* against Rabbi Shimon — which sets the stage for the next discussion.

## Wrong Victim Murder

### Mishna's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״הַשּׁוֹר יִסָּקֵל וְגַם בְּעָלָיו יוּמָת״ – כְּמִיתַת בְּעָלִים, כָּךְ מִיתַת הַשּׁוֹר; מָה בְּעָלִים – עַד דְּמִיכַּוֵּין לֵיהּ, אַף שׁוֹר נָמֵי – עַד דְּמִיכַּוֵּין לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon?** The Gemara answers that it is **because the verse states: "The ox shall be stoned, and its owner also shall be put to death"** (Exodus 21:29); the juxtaposition of the ox and its owner indicates that **as the death of the owner,** i.e., a person, for killing another person, **so** is **the death of the ox** for killing a person. In other words, the two *halakhot* are applied in the same circumstances. Specifically, **just as the owner,** i.e., a person, is not liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment **unless he intends** to kill the person whom he ultimately kills, **so too, an ox** is not put to death **either, unless it intends** to kill the one whom it ultimately kills.

The *mishna* implies that when an ox tried to gore one specific person and instead gored a different person, it is still subject to *skilah*. By the principle of *mitat habaal k'mitat hashor*, this means that a human who tried to kill ראובן and killed שמעון would be executed for murder.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְעָלִים גּוּפַיְיהוּ מְנָלַן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאָרַב לוֹ וְקָם עָלָיו״ – עַד שֶׁיִּתְכַּוֵּין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the owner himself, from where do we** derive that he is not liable unless he killed the one whom he intended to kill? It is **as the verse states: "And he lay in wait for him, and rose against him,** and struck him mortally and he died" (Deuteronomy 19:11). From the term "for him," Rabbi Shimon derives that the killer is not liable **unless he intends** to kill **him,** i.e., the one whom he ultimately killed.

Rabbi Shimon disputes this. He rules that even if the ox tried to kill one specific person and killed another, if the originally intended victim was someone for whom the killing would not have triggered a death penalty — an animal, a *nefal*, or a non-Jew — then the ox is exempt. More broadly, Rabbi Shimon holds that even in a case where both the intended and actual victims are Jews, if the killer intended to kill ראובן and killed שמעון, he is exempt from capital punishment.

### Specific Intent

Rabbi Shimon derives his requirement of specific intent from the verse: *ve'arav lo vekam alav* — "he lay in wait for him and rose up against him." The word *lo* — "for him" — implies specificity: the murderer intended that particular victim. Rabbi Shimon reads this as a definitional requirement: capital liability for murder attaches only when the killer specifically plotted against the person who was actually killed.

### Rabbanan's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״וְאָרַב לוֹ״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: פְּרָט לַזּוֹרֵק אֶבֶן לְגוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what do the Rabbis,** who deem the killer liable in that case and who therefore disagree with Rabbi Shimon's opinion, **do with this** phrase: **"And he lay in wait for him"?** How do they interpret it? The Gemara answers that the Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai say** that this phrase **excludes** from liability **one who throws a stone into** an area where there are several people, some of whom are people for whom he would not be liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment, e.g., gentiles, and a stone killed a person for whom he would receive court-imposed capital punishment.

The *rabbanan* agree that the verse *ve'arav lo vekam alav* teaches something about required intent, but they read it differently. The verse's requirement of "him" — a specific person — serves only to exclude a case where no particular victim was intended at all, as when someone throws a stone into a crowd without caring who it hits. In that case, the act cannot be characterized as targeted murder, and the perpetrator is exempt. But if the killer intended to kill ראובן and killed שמעון instead, the *rabbanan* hold that he is *chayav* — he committed a capital act of murder, and the mismatch of identity between intended and actual victim does not create an exemption.

### Kavua Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִיכָּא תִּשְׁעָה גּוֹיִם וְאֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל בֵּינֵיהֶם – תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּרוּבָּא גּוֹיִם נִינְהוּ; אִי נָמֵי פַּלְגָא וּפַלְגָא – סְפֵק נְפָשׁוֹת לְהָקֵל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say that there are nine gentiles** in the crowd **and one Jew among them,** even without the verse **derive** the exemption from the fact **that a majority of them are gentiles. Alternatively,** even if **half** the people are gentiles **and half** are Jews, derive the exemption from the principle that when there is **uncertainty** concerning **capital law,** the *halakha* is **to** be **lenient.**

The *gemara* now asks: what is the specific case in which the *rabbanan* use the verse to exempt someone who threw a stone into a crowd? If the majority of the crowd were non-Jews, we would not need a verse — by simple majority (*rov*), we would already characterize the act as one targeting non-Jews and exempt the perpetrator from capital liability. Similarly, if the crowd were half Jewish and half non-Jewish, the principle of *safeik nefashot l'hakel* — in cases of doubt regarding capital punishment we rule leniently — would already yield an exemption without any special verse. The verse must therefore be addressing a more difficult case.

### Safeik Nefashot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִיכָּא תִּשְׁעָה יִשְׂרְאֵלִים וְאֶחָד גּוֹי. דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּרוּבָּא יִשְׂרְאֵלִים נִינְהוּ; כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא חֲדא גּוֹי בֵּינַיְיהוּ – הָוֵי לֵיהּ קָבוּעַ, וְכׇל קָבוּעַ כְּמֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה דָּמֵי, וְסָפֵק נְפָשׁוֹת לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** the verse **is necessary** in a case **where there are nine Jews and one gentile. Although a majority of them are Jews,** the thrower is exempt from liability **because there is one gentile among them** who **is considered fixed** in his place, **and** the legal status of **any** item **fixed** in its place **is like** that of an uncertainty that is **equally balanced; and** when there is **uncertainty** concerning **capital law** the *halakha* is **to** be **lenient.** This is what the Rabbis derive from the phrase: "And he lay in wait for him."

The case the verse comes to address is one where the majority of the crowd is Jewish but one non-Jew is present among them. Without the verse, one might reason as follows: the majority of the crowd is Jewish, so by *rov* we characterize the act as one targeting Jews, and if the person killed turned out to be Jewish, the killer is executed. The verse comes to teach the principle of *kavua* — when a member of a distinct group is standing in a fixed position within a larger group, we do not apply the ordinary majority rule to dissolve his distinct identity. The non-Jew is *kavua* — fixed in place and identifiable as distinct. Therefore, *kol kavua kemechetza al mechetza dami* — any case of *kavua* is treated as a fifty-fifty doubt. Since there is doubt as to whether the thrown stone was targeting a Jew or a non-Jew, and since we rule leniently in capital cases (*safeik nefashot l'hakel*), the perpetrator is not executed. This is the source in the Talmud for the principle of *kavua*, which has wide-ranging application in other halachic domains.

## Ownership Requirement

### Mishna Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר הָאִשָּׁה, וְשׁוֹר הַיְּתוֹמִים, שׁוֹר הָאַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס, שׁוֹר הַמִּדְבָּר, שׁוֹר הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, שׁוֹר הַגֵּר שֶׁמֵּת וְאֵין לוֹ יוֹרְשִׁין – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין מִיתָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹר הַמִּדְבָּר, שׁוֹר הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, שׁוֹר הַגֵּר שֶׁמֵּת – פְּטוּרִין מִן הַמִּיתָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם בְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an ox belonging to a woman, and** similarly **an ox belonging to** orphans, and **an ox** belonging to orphans that is in the custody **of** their **steward,** and **a desert ox,** which is ownerless, and **an ox** that was **consecrated** to the Temple treasury, and **an ox belonging to a convert who died and has no heirs,** rendering the ox ownerless; all of **these** oxen **are liable** to be put to **death** for killing a person. **Rabbi Yehuda says: A desert ox, a consecrated ox,** and **an ox belonging to a convert who died are exempt from** being put to **death, since they have no owners.**

The *mishna* now addresses whether the ownership status of the ox at the time of the goring — or at the time of judgment — affects the law of *skilah*. It lists several cases: an ox owned by a woman, by orphans, or by an *apotropos* overseeing a minor's estate; a wild ox (*shor hamidbar*); an ox consecrated to the *Beis Hamikdash* (*shor hekdesh*); and an ox that was owned by a convert who died without heirs, rendering the ox ownerless. The *mishna* rules that in all these cases, *chayavim mita* — the ox is still subject to *skilah*. The identity or status of the owner, or the absence of a conventional lay owner, does not affect the ox's liability.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda disputes this for three of the cases: the wild ox (*shor hamidbar*), the consecrated ox (*shor hekdesh*), and the ox of a convert who died without heirs (*shor ger shemeit v'ein lo yorshim*). In all three, Rabbi Yehuda holds that the ox is *patur min hamita* — exempt from stoning — because it lacks an owner. The *parsha* is framed in terms of an owner's ox: *ki yigach shor ish*, *vehu'ad b'va'alav*. For Rabbi Yehuda, the framework of *skilah* presupposes the existence of a conventional lay owner, and where no such owner exists, the stoning does not apply.

### Rav Huna's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: פּוֹטֵר הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֲפִילּוּ נָגַח וּלְבַסּוֹף הִקְדִּישׁוֹ בְּעָלָיו – אֲפִילּוּ נָגַח וּלְבַסּוֹף הִפְקִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says: Rabbi Yehuda would deem** the ox **exempt even** if it **gored** and killed **and** its owner **ultimately consecrated** it, or if **it gored and he ultimately renounced** his **ownership** over it, since at the time of the trial in court the ox does not have an owner.

Rav Huna extends Rabbi Yehuda's position significantly. Rabbi Yehuda would exempt from *skilah* even an ox that gored while it had an owner, if the owner subsequently dedicated the ox to the *Beis Hamikdash* (*hikdish*) or declared it ownerless (*hifkir*) before the judicial process concluded. The moment the ox loses its owner, even post-goring, Rabbi Yehuda would hold it exempt. This has a somewhat obvious practical implication — an owner whose ox has killed someone might be tempted to donate the ox to the *Beis Hamikdash* in order to escape both the loss of the ox (since it is condemned anyway) and the *kofer* payment. The *gemara* does not shy away from this irony.

### Process vs. Event

The *mishna*'s reason for listing both the wild ox and the convert's ox separately now becomes clear. At first glance, a convert who died without heirs leaves the ox ownerless — which seems identical to a wild ox. But there is a critical difference. The wild ox was never owned, while the convert's ox was owned at the time of the goring but became ownerless afterward when the convert died. The *mishna* includes both cases to establish that for the *rabbanan*, ownership at the time of the goring is irrelevant to *skilah* — what matters is the act. But for Rabbi Yehuda, the convert's case teaches the additional ruling that even if the ox was owned at the moment of the goring, if ownership is lost before or during the judgment process, *skilah* does not apply.

### Textual Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? מִדְּקָתָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי – שׁוֹר הַמִּדְבָּר, וְשׁוֹר הַגֵּר שֶׁמֵּת וְאֵין לוֹ יוֹרְשִׁין. שׁוֹר הַגֵּר שֶׁמֵּת מַאי נִיהוּ? דְּכֵיוָן דְּאֵין לוֹ יוֹרְשִׁין – הֲוָה לֵיהּ שׁוֹר הֶפְקֵר; הַיְינוּ שׁוֹר הַמִּדְבָּר, הַיְינוּ שׁוֹר הַגֵּר שֶׁמֵּת וְאֵין לוֹ יוֹרְשִׁין! אֶלָּא לָאו הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן – דַּאֲפִילּוּ נָגַח וּלְבַסּוֹף הִקְדִּישׁ, נָגַח וּלְבַסּוֹף הִפְקִיר? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** did Rav Huna derive this assertion? **From** the fact **that** Rabbi Yehuda **teaches two** cases, **a desert ox and an ox belonging to a convert who died and has no heirs. What is** the legal status of **an ox belonging to a convert who died? Since he has no heirs it is considered to be an ownerless ox.** Accordingly, the case of **a desert ox is** the same as the case of **an ox belonging to a convert who died and has no heirs,** and it does not seem necessary for the *baraita* to state both cases. **Rather, does it not teach us this: That even if** the ox **gored and he ultimately consecrated** it, or, if **it gored and he ultimately renounced ownership** over it, it is exempt, just as in a case where a convert's ox gores and subsequently the owner dies? The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from** the *baraita* that this is the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

The textual basis for requiring ownership throughout the entire process is derived from reading the relevant verses as a unified sequence: *vehu'ad b'va'alav* — "it was warned to its owners" — followed by *vehashor yisakel* — "the ox shall be stoned." Rabbi Yehuda reads these elements as inseparable: the ownership (*ba'alav*), the warning, and the *skilah* must all occur within the same continuous legal reality. The ox can be stoned only if it has an owner through the entire process.

### Gemar Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, יָתֵר עַל כֵּן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אֲפִילּוּ נָגַח וּלְבַסּוֹף הִקְדִּישׁ, נָגַח וּלְבַסּוֹף הִפְקִיר – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוּעַד בִּבְעָלָיו״ – ״וְהֵמִית וְגוֹ׳״, עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא מִיתָה וְהַעֲמָדָה בַּדִּין שָׁוִין כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **This** assertion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Moreover, Rabbi Yehuda said** that **even if it gored and he ultimately consecrated** it, or if **it gored and he ultimately renounced ownership** over it, the ox is **exempt, as it is stated: "And warning has been given [*vehuad*] to its owner**…**and it killed**…the ox shall be stoned" (Exodus 21:29). It is derived from here that the owner of the ox is exempt **unless** the ox's status as the owner's property at the time of the **death** of the victim **and** at the time of the owner's **standing trial is the same,** i.e., that the ox has an owner.

A *baraita* confirms Rav Huna's position: *yaser al ken amar Rabbi Yehuda afilu nagach ulevasof hikdish nagach ulevasof hifkir patur* — even more so, Rabbi Yehuda rules that if the ox gored and the owner subsequently dedicated it to the *Beis Hamikdash* or declared it ownerless, it is exempt. The source is the verse *vehu'ad b'va'alav velo yishmerenu veheimit ish o ishah* — the warning, the failure to guard, and the killing must all be read as one integrated sequence. The *gemara* derives from this that the *mita* and the *amida badin* — the killing and the standing in judgment — must occur within the same framework of the ox having an owner: *ad shetehei misa ve'amida badin vegamar din shavin ke'echad* — the killing, the judicial proceeding, and the completion of judgment must all coincide with the ox having owners. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, reason that the *parsha*'s framing in terms of an owner's ox is descriptive of the typical case, not definitional. Logically, what matters is the act of killing, and the ox is stoned for what it did regardless of who owns it — or whether anyone does — at the time of judgment.
