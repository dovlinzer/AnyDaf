# Megillah 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Psichas & Midrashic Mode

### Psicha Genre

The *daf* opens in the middle of a series of *psichas* — the homiletic openings that introduce midrashic exposition of *Megillat Esther*. Anyone who has learned *Midrash Rabbah* will recognize the genre immediately: when the *Midrash Rabbah* opens a *parasha*, it typically begins not with the *pasuk* at hand but with a seemingly unrelated verse from elsewhere in *Tanakh* — often an obscure verse from *Nach* — and then finds a way to weave it into the story being expounded. That is precisely the mode at work here. The *Gemara* has been proceeding essentially as a *Midrash Rabbah* on *Megillat Esther*, moving through the *pesukim* of the *Megillah* one by one, and interspersing *Amoraic midrashim* throughout. The *psichas* belong to this same tradition: before the actual expository treatment of the opening verse begins, various Sages open from a different *pasuk* and find a way to tie it into the *Megillah*.

### Tanakh Unity

One of the primary effects achieved by this mode is a sense of the unity and interconnectedness of all of *Tanakh*. When a later verse in *Nevi'im* is shown to relate back to a story in the *Torah*, one can say that it is not surprising — the later text came after and could allude back. But what gets communicated in either direction is that it is all one great interconnected text.

### Esther & Earlier Texts

There is, however, a particular power in starting from *pesukim* in *Esther*, which stands at the very end of *Tanakh*, and relating them back to earlier *pesukim* — especially since so many of the *psichas* draw on verses from the *Torah* itself. The effect is to demonstrate that even this very late book was already foreshadowed, its ideas already present in earlier portions of scripture. The *Megillah* is thus anchored within the larger religious-historical arc of *Tanakh* as an integral part of that whole.

## Rava's Psicha

### Birbot Tzadikim

*Rava pasach lapitcha l'ha parshata* — Rava opened the beginning of the *parasha* of the *Megillah* from the following verse (*Mishlei* 29:2): *birbot tzadikim yismach ha'am, uvimshol rasha ye'aneich am* — "with the increase of the righteous the nation will rejoice; with the dominion of the wicked the people will moan."

### Mordechai vs Haman

The application is immediate. *Birbot tzadikim yismach ha'am* — this refers to Mordechai and Esther, as it is written: *v'ha'ir Shushan tzahala v'sameicha* — the city of Shushan was joyous and rejoicing. *Uvimshol rasha ye'aneich am* — this refers to Haman, as it is written: *v'ha'ir Shushan navocha* — the city of Shushan was bewildered and distraught. The contrast between the joy of the city under righteous leadership and its distress under wicked oppression maps precisely onto the trajectory of the Purim story.

---

## Rav Masna's Psicha

### God's Closeness

*Rav Masna amar meihacha* — Rav Masna would open from the verse in *Devarim* (4:7): *ki mi goy gadol asher lo elohim kerovim eilav k'Hashem elokeinu bechol koreinu eilav* — "for what great nation has a God as close to it as Hashem our God is whenever we call out to Him?" This is a striking choice: the verse expresses confidence in God's closeness, yet the story of Purim is defined by the sensation of God's absence — His name does not appear, and the people face imminent annihilation. Yet viewed in retrospect, even the story of Purim demonstrates that *bechol koreinu eilav* — at the time when God felt absent, when they called out, God was there for them.

## Rav Ashi's Psicha

### Nes Galui vs Hester

*Rav Ashi amar meihacha* — Rav Ashi opens from *Devarim* (4:34): *o hanissah elohim lavo lakachat lo goy mikerev goy bemasos uv'otot uv'moftim uv'milchama... kechol asher asah Hashem elokeichem lachem bemitzrayim l'einecha* — "Has God ever attempted to take for Himself a nation from the midst of another nation, with trials, signs, wonders, and war... like all that Hashem your God did for you in Egypt before your eyes?" This is quite fascinating: the verse is drawn from the context of *Yetziat Mitzrayim*, where the miracle was a *nes galui* — openly revealed, God's presence unmistakable to all. Yet Rav Ashi applies it to Purim, a period defined by *hester panim* — divine concealment. The point is that even in a time when it seemed as though it was not going to happen, it happened nonetheless: the same type of thing occurred.

### Jewish Distinctiveness

One also wonders whether there is a further hint embedded in the phrase *lavo lakachat lo goy mikerev goy* — taking a nation from the midst of another nation. The whole *Megillah* is deeply concerned with the degree of assimilation that had occurred; the people had become so thoroughly integrated into Persian society that they risked losing their distinctiveness entirely. It was only the pressure of Achashverosh's oppression — and ultimately the events of Purim — that caused them to crystallize their identity once again. There are *midrashim* that describe how thoroughly the people had been absorbed, and the Purim story thus becomes a moment of being separated out and restored to distinctiveness — *lavo lakachat lo goy mikerev goy*.

## Peshat of Vayehi

### Vayehi Woe

Having completed the *psichas*, the *Gemara* now turns to the *pasuk*-by-*pasuk* analysis of the *Megillah* itself. *Amar Rav: vayehi — vay vehi* — the word *vayehi* carries within it the cry of woe: *vay*. "In the days of Achashverosh" is an expression of mourning. And this, says Rav, is the fulfillment of the verse in *Devarim* (28:68): *v'hismakartem sham l'oyvecha la'avadim v'lishfachos v'ein koneh* — "you will be sold to your enemies as slaves and maidservants, and there will be no buyer."

### Rashi: No Buyers

The question is: where precisely does Rav see the fulfillment of that verse? Rashi explains: *ma shekasuv baTorah v'ein koneh gazar Haman shelo yihiyeh adam meshabed mehem l'eved* — Haman decreed that no one should enslave the Jews even as bondsmen. The tragedy was that the Jews had no means and needed to sell themselves into servitude, yet no one would purchase them.

### Haman's Money

An alternative reading is suggested by the verse in *Esther* itself, where Esther says: *v'ilu la'avadim v'lishfachos nimkarnu hecherashti* — had we merely been sold as slaves and maidservants, I would have held my peace. The verse *v'hismakartem... v'ein koneh* resonates with this, and may also allude to the episode where Haman offers to pay the king for the right to destroy the Jews and Achashverosh responds *hakesef nasun lach* — keep the money. There, the Jews are not being sold into servitude but into annihilation, and not even the money is accepted. Either way, the echoes of *v'hismakartem... v'ein koneh* reverberate throughout the *Megillah*.

## Shmuel's Lo Ma'astim

### Yevanim Period

*U'Shmuel amar: lo me'astim velo ge'altim lechalosam* — Shmuel opens with the verse in *Vayikra* (26:44). *Lo me'astim bimei Yevanim* — I did not despise them in the time of the Greeks, at the time of the Chanukah story. It appeared as though they were being abandoned, yet in the end there was victory.

### Nevuchadnezzar

*Velo ge'altim bimei Nevuchadnezzar* — in the time of Nevuchadnezzar, the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed and it seemed as though all was lost, yet they were not forgotten.

### Haman's Threat

*Lechalosam bimei Haman* — to destroy them utterly in the time of Haman. The threat of complete annihilation hung over the entire people, yet it did not come to pass.

### Persians & Messiah

*Lehafer brisi itam bimei Parsiyim* — to abrogate My covenant with them in the time of the Persians. The specific event referred to here is not entirely clear, and it is worth noting a textual observation raised in this connection: the *Avnei Nezer* argues that the original text of this passage referred not to Nevuchadnezzar but to *Aspasyanos* — that is, Vespasian — and that the reference was altered to avoid offending the Romans. This is significant because later in this same passage *bimei Parsiyim* appears to refer clearly to the Roman period as well, where the *Gemara* will identify God's representatives as the house of Rebbe and the sages of the generations — clearly not a Persian-era institution. The *girsa* is thus likely *Romiyim* in that instance.

*Ki Ani Hashem Elokeihem bimei Gog U'Magog* — the full expression of "I am Hashem their God" is reserved for the messianic era. Until then God has not abandoned them nor permitted their destruction, but the complete revelation of *ki Ani Hashem Elokeihem* will come only in the future, in the time of *Gog U'Magog*.

## Tannaitic Baraita

### Kasdim Leaders

*Tanu Rabbanan* — a *baraisa* now presents a parallel formulation. *Lo me'astim bimei Kasdim* — I did not despise them in the time of the Babylonians, *she'emadeti lahem Daniel Chananyah Mishael v'Azaryah* — for I raised up for them Daniel, Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah, who stood up for God and for the religion and did not flinch even before the furnace.

### Yevanim Leaders

*Velo ge'altim bimei Yevanim* — in the time of the Greeks, I raised up Shimon HaTzaddik, *v'Chashmonai u'vanav u'Mattisyahu Kohen Gadol* — the Chashmonayim and Mattisyahu the Kohen Gadol as God's representatives in that period.

### Haman Leaders

*Velo chalosam bimei Haman* — I did not let them be destroyed in the time of Haman, *she'he'emadti lahem Mordechai v'Esther* — for I raised up Mordechai and Esther.

### Parsiyim Sages

*Lehafer briti itam bimei Parsiyim* — in the time of the Persians, *she'he'emadti lahem shel Beis Rebbe v'chachmei dorot* — I raised up for them the house of Rebbe and the sages of the generations. As already noted, this clearly does not refer to the Persian period in the historical sense; the original *girsa* was almost certainly *Romiyim*, referring to the Roman period, and the text was emended to avoid censure.

### Messianic Future

*Ki Ani Hashem Elokeihem l'asid lavo* — in the future to come, *she'ein kol umah v'lashon yecholim lishlot bahem* — no nation or tongue will ever again have dominion over them. Not only will God not permit their destruction, but they will be fully recognized as God's people, no longer subjugated under foreign nations.

It is worth pausing to observe that one might look at this passage simply as another *psicha* — an introductory verse from *Vayikra* used to open the *Megillah*. But one participant in the discussion rightly noted that there is something more going on: the word *bimei* — "in the days of" — is itself being expounded. Shmuel is not merely offering an introductory verse; he is observing that history unfolds in *yamim*, in named epochs — the *yamei Yevanim*, the *yamei Parsiyim*, the *yamei Achashverosh* — and that God intervenes in each such epoch to save the people. This frames history as a sequence of distinct periods of divine engagement, which feels different from the model in which enemy nations are simply mobilized as instruments of God's plan.

## Rabbi Levi Psicha

### Amalek Connection

*Rabbi Levi amar meihacha* — Rabbi Levi would open with the verse in *Bamidbar*: *v'im lo torishu et yoshvei ha'aretz v'hayu litzninim b'tzideichem* — "if you do not drive out the inhabitants of the land, they will be thorns in your side." Rashi explains: *af elu ne'enshu al chamat Shaul al Amalek* — the people of that generation were punished on account of Saul's failure to destroy Amalek. The Purim story is thus the continuation and consequence of that earlier failure; Haman the Agagite descends from Amalek, and the catastrophe that nearly befell Israel traces back to the incomplete fulfillment of that command.

## Rhetorical Effect

### Oral Tradition

The mode of *psicha* was clearly an oral one. When a *darshan* opened with a puzzling or obscure verse — say, a verse from *Mishlei* nobody had heard of — the audience's interest was immediately engaged: how is he going to get from here to there? The suspense of watching the connection unfold is itself part of the pedagogical effect. This is the general mode of *Midrash Rabbah*: one opens a *parasha* in *Bereishis* or *Bamidbar*, and the *midrash* begins with some verse from *Tehillim* or *Mishlei* that seems entirely unrelated, and only gradually does the connection emerge.

### Structural Parallels

This same structure appears throughout classical midrashic literature. *Pesikta de-Rav Kahana*, for instance — a collection of homilies organized around the *Yamim Tovim* — employs *psichas* throughout: each homily opens with a verse from elsewhere and works its way toward the *pasuk* of the day. The *psichas* examined here in the *Gemara* belong to precisely this tradition.

## Divine Plan Debate

### Enemy Agency

A substantive question arises from this material: are the enemies of Israel presented as independent agents pursuing their own malevolent purposes, or as instruments consciously deployed within God's providential plan? The more the text emphasizes divine orchestration of events — as in *Shemot*, where God explicitly hardens Pharaoh's heart — the more it raises the difficult question of how the same agent can then be punished for doing what God caused him to do. The *rishonim* grapple with this by noting that even if the broad outlines of a divine plan are foreordained, no individual is compelled to volunteer for the role of oppressor. One's personal free choice remains real even within a framework of divine providence.

In the *Megillah*, the enemies are not described as acting under divine compulsion. The text prefers to present them as exercising their own malicious agency — and then records God's intervention to save the people. This is in fact the broader biblical pattern: in *Shoftim*, the Israelites sin, God sends a scourge in the form of another nation, and then sends a savior. We are not focused on the punishment of the other nation in those narratives. It is in *Yeshayah*, *Amos*, and *Hoshea* that the accounting of the nations for their crimes comes to the fore.

### Epochs & Bimei

As noted above, what Shmuel's formulation achieves — whether read as a *psicha* or as a *drashah* on the word *bimei* — is the framing of history in terms of distinct epochs. Rather than presenting individual enemies as agents or instruments, the *Gemara* speaks of the *yamei Yevanim*, the *yamei Haman*, the *yamei Parsiyim*: large periods of history during which forces of oppression dominate and God chooses a moment to intervene. This feels different, as one listener observed, from the model in which God actively mobilizes the nations. It feels like the flow of human history into which God steps at critical junctures.

## Achashverosh Character

### Achiv Shel Rav

*Amar Rav: achiv shel rosh uven gilo shel rosh.* Achashverosh is the brother of a leader, cut from the same cloth as a leader. Who is the *rosh*? *Achiv shel Nevuchadnezzar HaRasha shenikra rosh* — the brother of Nevuchadnezzar the Wicked, who is called "the head," as it says: *ant hu reisha di dehava* — "you are the golden head," in the famous dream of *Sefer Daniel* in which Nevuchadnezzar's statue has a head of gold.

### Parallel Actions

What does it mean to be cut from the same cloth? The parallel actions are spelled out: Nevuchadnezzar killed Jews; Achashverosh sought to kill Jews. Nevuchadnezzar destroyed the *Beit HaMikdash*; Achashverosh sought to destroy it — or at minimum, sought to prevent its rebuilding.

### Rashi Dating

Rashi explains *bikesh lachriv*: *yesod sheyasad Zerubavel b'Beit HaMikdash biyemei Koresh lifnei Achashverosh kemo sheamar b'Sefer Ezra* — the foundation that Zerubavel had already laid for the *Beit HaMikdash* during the days of Koresh, before Achashverosh. This places Achashverosh after the initial phase of rebuilding had already begun under Koresh, such that his edict — presumably encompassing the destruction of what had been built — amounted to a bid to destroy the foundation of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *Gemara* then cites the verse from *Ezra*: *uvelmalchut Achashverosh bitchilat malchuto katvu sitnah al yoshvei Yehudah v'Yerushalayim* — in the kingdom of Achashverosh, at the beginning of his reign, they wrote a hostile edict against those dwelling in Judah and Jerusalem. Rashi explains that *sitnah* here refers to the prohibition against continuing to build. So even setting aside the Purim story entirely, the verse in *Ezra* already locates Achashverosh as an enemy of the rebuilding of the *Beit HaMikdash*.

## Amoraic Opinions

### Rabbi Yochanan

*Rabbi Yochanan amar: kol shezichro amar ach l'rosho* — anyone who mentions Achashverosh says "woe to their head." They get a headache. He was, in Rabbi Yochanan's view, simply a bad or foolish king whose very name evokes suffering — and not only for the Jews.

### Rabbi Chanina

*Rabbi Chanina amar: shekol na'asu rashim b'yamav* — he made everyone poor. *Shene'emar: vayaseim hamelech Achashverosh mas* — as it says, the king Achashverosh imposed a tax. Raising taxes was political suicide even then, and Achashverosh is remembered for having impoverished not only the Jews but everyone.

### Hu Achashverosh

*Hu Achashverosh* — what does the word *hu* add? *Hu b'rishato mitechilato ve'ad sofo* — he was unchanging in his wickedness from beginning to end. This is the trigger for a broader *drashah* on the word *hu* wherever it appears in *Tanakh*.

## Hu Derasha

### Wicked Examples

*Hu Eisav avi Edom* — this Eisav, referenced in *Bereishit*, was *hu b'rishato mitechilato ve'ad sofo* — wicked from beginning to end. *Hu Datan va'Aviram b'nei Eliav* — in a verse in *Bamidbar*, *hein b'rishatan mitechilatam ve'ad sofam* — they were wicked from beginning to end.

### Righteous vs Wicked

*Hu hamelech Achaz* — King Achaz, *hu b'rishato mitechilato ve'ad sofo* — always a wicked king. On the other side: *Avraham hu Avraham*, a verse in *Divrei HaYamim* that most have not encountered — *hu b'tzidkato mitechilato ve'ad sofo* — he was always righteous.

### Aaron & Moses

*Hu Aharon u'Moshe* — in a verse in *Shemot*, *hein b'tzidkatam mitechilatam ve'ad sofam* — they were righteous from beginning to end.

### David's Humility

*Ve'Dovid hu hakatan* — he was the small one among the sons of Yishai. *Hu b'katnuso mitechilato ve'ad sofo.* But this requires explanation: he was not always small — he became a king. The *Gemara* explains: *kesheim sheb'katnuso hiktin atzmo l'mi shegadol mimenu b'Torah* — just as in his youth he knew how to be deferential and make himself small before one greater than him in *Torah*, *kach b'malchuto hiktin atzmo l'mi shegadol mimenu b'chochmah* — so too in his kingship he showed deference to those wiser than he. This is an even more impressive quality than deference in *Torah* matters: a king might reasonably defer to a greater *talmid chacham* in areas of *halacha*. But *chochmah* — judgment, wisdom, practical knowledge — is ostensibly the king's own domain. That even there David showed proper deference is the mark of genuine *katnuto*, genuine smallness.

## Self-Made King

### Self-Made Debate

*Amar Rav: shemalach mei'atzmo* — Achashverosh was not born to the throne but rose from among the commoners; he was a self-appointed king. *Amri lei l'shvach ve'amri lei l'gnai* — some say this is praiseworthy and some say it is blameworthy. The praiseworthy reading: *d'lo hava inish dechashiv l'malka k'vatei* — nobody else was as suited to be king; he was taken from the commoners because he was the most fitting. The blameworthy reading: *d'lo hava chazi l'malchut u'mamon yaseirah hu d'yahiv kamei* — he was not actually fit for kingship but gave extra money, meaning he bought his way in, just as — the *Gemara* evokes — the office of *Kohen Gadol* could be purchased in later periods.

## Hodu V'ad Kush

### Geographic Extent

*Hamoleich meiHodu ve'ad Kush* — he ruled from Hodu to Kush. *Rav u'Shmuel*: one says *Hodu besof olam veKush besof olam* — Hodu and Kush are at opposite ends of the inhabited world, and he ruled the entire span between them. The other says *Hodu veKush gabei hadadi havu kaimei* — Hodu and Kush were right next to one another. In that case, what is the point?

### Universal Rule

If they are adjacent, then the verse is saying: *kesheim shemalach al Hodu veKush kach malach misof ha'olam ve'ad sofo* — just as he had control over these two neighboring countries, so too he had control over the entire world. Either reading arrives at the same conclusion: Achashverosh ruled over the entire civilized world of his era.

---

## Shlomo Comparison

### Same Methodology

*Kayotzei badavar atah omer* — a parallel case applies the same debate to Shlomo's domain, as described in *Melachim* (1 Kings 5:4): *ki hu rodeh bechol ever hanahar mitafsach ve'ad Azah* — he had dominion over the entire Trans-Jordan from Tafsach to Gaza. Once again *Rav u'Shmuel* dispute: one says Tafsach is at one end of the world and Gaza at the other, so he ruled the entire inhabited world; the other says they are right next to each other, and by the same logic as above — *kesheim shemalach al Tafsach ve'al Azah kach malach al kol ha'olam kulo* — he ruled over the whole world. The identical methodology is applied in both cases.

## 127 Provinces

### Province Growth

*Sheva ve'esrim u'meah medinah* — 127 provinces. *Amar Rav Chisda: betchilah malach al sheva, ulevasof malach al esrim, ulevasof malach al meah* — he started with seven, then expanded to twenty, then to a hundred. This is a *drashah* derived from the way the verse counts: not simply "127" but "seven and twenty and a hundred" — as is the standard biblical enumeration of large numbers.

### Derasha Objection

The *Gemara* immediately objects: *ela me'ata — u'shenei chayei Amram sheva u'shloshim u'meah shanah* — if this is the methodology, what will you do with the verse that says the life of Amram was "seven and thirty and a hundred years"? Will you *darshan* that he experienced three separate stages of life corresponding to those numbers?

### Superfluous Text

The *Gemara* responds: *shani hacha dekra yeseirah hu* — here the *drashah* is justified because the verse is superfluous. *Michdi kesiv meiHodu ve'ad Kush* — the verse has already told us the domain ran from Hodu to Kush, which implies the entire inhabited world. *Vesheva ve'esrim u'meah medinah lamah li* — why then does it need to specify 127 provinces? Since the information is redundant, it must be there to teach something additional, namely the *drashah* of how the kingdom was built up in stages. By contrast, the years of Amram's life carry no such redundancy; *shema minah ledrashah* applies only where the enumeration is otherwise superfluous.

## Three Rulers

### The Three Kings

Expanding upon Achashverosh's universal dominion, the *Gemara* states: *shelosha malchu bikipah* — three ruled under the dome of the inhabited world. These are Achav, Achashverosh, and Nevuchadnezzar. Tosafot immediately raises the obvious question: what about Alexander the Great? The answer is that the list is limited to those whose dominion is recorded in *Tanakh*: *lo havu hashivi ella hanehu de-ketiv behu hedyot* — only those about whom Scripture records it. Alexander came after the biblical period and was therefore not included.

### Achav Example

The proof for Achav: when Eliyahu stopped the rain and Achav sought him, the text says — *chai Hashem elokecha im yesh goy u'mamlacha asher lo shalach adoni sham levakeshcha v'hishbiya et ha-goy ve'et ha-mamlacha* — my master sent to every nation and kingdom to seek you, and he had them take an oath. If Achav did not have power over these foreign nations, how could he compel them to take such an oath? This demonstrates that his authority extended over the entire civilized world.

### Nevuchadnezzar

The proof for Nevuchadnezzar comes from the verse in *Yirmeyahu*: *v'haya ha-goy veha-mamlacha asher lo yitnu et tzavaram o asher lo ya'avdu oto et Nevuchadnezzar melech Bavel* — every nation and kingdom that does not put its neck in the yoke of the king of Babylon... The universal submission demanded makes clear his total dominion.

### Achashverosh

For Achashverosh: *ha de-amran* — as we have already stated, *meiHodu ve'ad Kush, misof ha-olam ve'ad sof ha-olam* — from Hodu to Kush, spanning the entire inhabited world.

## Shlomo Excluded

### Shlomo's End

The *Gemara* asks: are there no others? And then runs through the *siman* *shin-samech-dalet-chaf*: Shlomo, Sancheriv, Daryavesh, Koresh. First: *veha ika Shlomo*? We just saw from the verse in *Melachim* that he ruled from Tafsach to Gaza, covering the whole world. The *Gemara* responds: *lo salik malchutei* — his kingship did not conclude properly. There is the famous tradition that Shlomo went through a period in which he lost his throne and was reduced to commoner status.

### Return to Kingship

The *Gemara* then notes: *hani'cha le'man de'amar melech ve'hedyot* — this works well according to the view that he was king, then a commoner, and that was the end. *Ela le'man de'amar melech ve'hedyot u'melech* — but according to the view that after his period as a commoner he once again became king, what can one say?

### Supernal Rule

The *Gemara* responds: *Shlomo milta achrita hava bei* — Shlomo was not excluded because his kingship was insufficient but because he was in an entirely different category. *De'malach a'elyonim ve'atachtonim* — he had dominion not only over the lower world but over the supernal realms as well, over the *shedim* and other metaphysical forces, *shene'emar va'yeshev Shlomo al kisse Hashem* — as it says, "Shlomo sat on the throne of God." He was therefore in a category apart, transcending ordinary earthly kingship altogether.

## Other Kings Objected

### Sancheriv Limited

*Veha ika Sancheriv*? The verse records him boasting — *mi bechol elohei ha'aratzot ha'eleh asher hitzilu et artzam miyadi* — who among all the gods of these lands has saved their land from me? Does that not demonstrate universal dominion? The *Gemara* responds: *ha'ika Yerushalayim de'la kavsha* — there remains Jerusalem, which he did not conquer. His dominion was incomplete.

### Daryavesh 120

*Veha ika Daryavesh*? If he wrote to all nations — *di dayarin bechol ar'a* — does that not imply universal dominion? The *Gemara* responds: *ha'ika sheva de'la malich aleihu* — he only ruled 120 provinces, not 127. *De'ketiv shepar kodam Daryavesh ve'hakim al malchuta le'achashdarpanaya me'ah ve'esrin* — as it is written, Daryavesh placed over his kingdom 120 satraps. He was therefore short of the full count.

### Koresh Unreliable

*Veha ika Koresh*? He himself proclaimed: *kol mamlechot ha'aretz natan li Hashem* — all the kingdoms of the earth God has given me. The *Gemara* dismisses this: he is merely praising himself. You cannot rely on a ruler's own rhetoric about his dominion. As anyone familiar with the royal stelae of the ancient world knows, kings routinely described themselves as rulers of the entire world; that self-description carries no evidentiary weight.

## Chronological Intro

### Timing Question

*Bayamim hahem keshevet hamelech Achashverosh* — in those days, when King Achashverosh was settled on his royal throne. But then the very next verse says *bishnat shalosh lemalcho* — in the third year of his reign, he made a feast. Was the feast at the beginning of his reign or only in his third year?

### Rava's Solution

*Amar Rava: mai keshevet* — what does *keshevet* mean? *Le'achar shenit'yashva da'ato* — after his mind became settled, after he felt confident. He wanted to make the feast from the start, but *keshevet* indicates that he had to wait three years until he felt settled and secure, and only then did he hold the celebration.

### 70-Year Connection

Why did he need three years to feel confident? This is connected to the calculations around the 70-year prophecy. As the *Gemara* now proceeds to explain, Achashverosh — like Belshatzar before him — was tracking the prophecy of redemption and waiting to confirm that it had not been fulfilled. It took him three years from the beginning of his reign to complete his reckoning and convince himself that the prophecy had failed.

## Belshatzar's Math

### Two Prophecies

*Belshatzar chashav veta'ah* — Belshatzar did the calculation and made a mistake. The background is that there are two prophecies in *Yirmeyahu* each speaking of 70 years: *ki lefi melot leVavel shiv'im shanah efkod etchem* — when it has been fulfilled for Bavel 70 years, I will remember you and bring you back; and *limlot lechorvot Yerushalayim shiv'im shanah* — when it is fulfilled for the desolation of Jerusalem 70 years. These two formulations — one measuring from the Babylonian dominion, one from the destruction of Jerusalem — are not necessarily identical in their starting point, and it is that ambiguity that these kings exploited.

### Belshatzar's Sum

Belshatzar's computation: *chashav arba'in vachamesh deNevuchadnezzar* — he counted 45 years of Nevuchadnezzar. He understood *leVavel* to mean from when Nevuchadnezzar became king of Babylon. *Ve'esrin u'tlat de'Evil Merodach* — 23 years of Evil Merodach the successor. That brings the total to 68. *Ve'tartei didei* — and two of his own years as king. *Hashiv'im* — that made 70.

### False Confidence

Belshatzar concluded: it has been 70 years and the people have not been redeemed; the prophecy has failed. *Amar hashta vadai tu lo mifarki* — now they are certainly not going to be redeemed. Acting on this false confidence, *afik manei devei mikdasha ve'ishtamesh behu* — he took out the vessels of the *Beit HaMikdash* and used them. That night, as Daniel reports to him: *T'amrei shmaya hitromamta v'le'manaya d'veitei hei'tayu kadamach* — you have elevated yourself against the King of Heaven and brought His vessels before you. *Beih b'leilya ktil Belshatzar malka Kasdaya* — that very night Belshatzar king of the Chaldeans was killed.

## Belshatzar Sources

### Year 1-2

How does the *Gemara* arrive at 45 years for Nevuchadnezzar? This requires reconciling several verses that speak of different years in connection with the various exiles. *Tana v'shana: kavash shanah zeh, shniyah kivash Yehoyakim* — in the first year of his dominion he conquered Nineveh; in the second year he conquered Yehoyakim. This establishes that the exile of *Galut Yehoyakim* began in Nevuchadnezzar's second year, which becomes the baseline for some of the subsequent counting.

### Dating Systems

*Galu b'sheva galu b'shmoneh galu b'shmoneh esreh galu b'tishah esreh* — the *Gemara* notes that various verses speak of exile occurring in the seventh year, the eighth year, the eighteenth year, and the nineteenth year of Nevuchadnezzar. The apparent contradictions are reconciled as follows: *galu b'sheva l'kivush Yehoyakim — galut Yehoyachin* — the exile in the seventh year refers to the exile of Yehoyachin, counting from the conquest of Yehoyakim, which began in Nevuchadnezzar's second year. Thus the seventh year from *Galut Yehoyakim* is the eighth year of Nevuchadnezzar's reign — hence some verses say seven and some say eight, depending on which starting point is used.

### Exiles Timeline

Similarly, *galut Tzidkiyahu* — the final exile — occurred in the nineteenth year of Nevuchadnezzar counting from his first year as king, but the eighteenth year counting from the beginning of the exile process. The two sets of numbers — 7 and 8, 18 and 19 — each reflect a different counting convention, and both are attested in *Tanakh*. Notably, the *Gemara* observes that this chronology is consistent with secular historical sources as well.

The calculation that follows: *Galut Yehoyachin* in year 8 of Nevuchadnezzar, plus 37 years until Evil Merodach released him — as it is written in *Melachim II* (25:27-29): *vayhi bishloshim v'sheva shanah l'galut Yehoyachin melech Yehudah... nasa Evil Merodach melech Bavel bishnat malchuto et rosh Yehoyachin* — in the 37th year of Yehoyachin's exile, Evil Merodach raised up Yehoyachin and released him from prison. 8 plus 37 equals 45: *harei arba'im v'chamish* — that is Nevuchadnezzar's 45 years. *Ve'esrim v'tlata de'Evil Merodach* — 23 years of Evil Merodach (a received tradition), bringing the total to 68. *Ve'tartei didei* — and two years of Belshatzar: *ha shiv'im* — that is the 70.

## Achashverosh's Sin

### Keilim Shonim

The *Gemara* now explains why the phrase in the *Megillah* — *v'cheilim mikeilim shonim* — carries a distinctive *trop* when chanted. The "vessels of different sorts" are the desecrated vessels of the *Beit HaMikdash*, which Achashverosh used at his feast. This is a direct repetition of Belshatzar's sin.

### Satan's Role

*Ba Satan v'rikeid beineihem* — Satan came and danced amongst the celebrants. The result of this intervention was the chaos that led to Vashti's execution — itself the catalyst for everything that followed in the Purim story.

### Different Fates

The outcomes, however, differed starkly. Belshatzar was killed that very night — *beih b'leilya ktil* — executed by God for his desecration. Achashverosh, by contrast, was not immediately punished in kind. His downfall came indirectly, through the reversal of Haman's decree and the death of Vashti, rather than through his own execution. The disproportion may be explained by the fact that Achashverosh was not the nation that had actually destroyed the *Beit HaMikdash* — that was Nevuchadnezzar's Babylon. The gravity of the sin is therefore on a different scale.

## Achashverosh's Error

### Different Method

Having shown Belshatzar's miscalculation, the *Gemara* now turns to Achashverosh's. Achashverosh claimed he would not repeat Belshatzar's error. *Mi ksiv l'malchut Bavel? L'Bavel ksiv* — the verse does not say 70 years from the beginning of the kingdom of Babylon; it says 70 years *l'Vavel*, which Achashverosh understood to mean from the exile of Babylon — specifically, from *Galut Yehoyachin*.

### Achashverosh Sum

Achashverosh reasoned: *kama b'tziran? Tmanya* — how many years were still lacking from Belshatzar's count? Eight more years were needed beyond Belshatzar's 70. *Chashiv ayu chilufayu*: one year of Belshatzar (since the event occurred in Belshatzar's third year, not his second, there was one additional year), five years for the combined reigns of Daryavesh and Koresh, and two years of his own reign. One plus five plus two equals eight — *hashiv'im* — seventy years from *Galut Yehoyachin*. The prophecy, by Achashverosh's reckoning, had just expired unfulfilled.

### Repeated Sin

*Kivan d'chazi d'malo shiv'in v'lo ifruk* — when he saw that 70 years had passed and the people had not been redeemed, *amar hashta vadai shuv lo mifarki* — now they certainly will not be redeemed. And like Belshatzar, *afik manei d'Beit HaMikdash ve'ishtamesh behu* — he took out the vessels of the *Beit HaMikdash* and used them, which is the sin of *keilim mikeilim shonim* in the *Megillah*.

## Evil Merodach 37 Years

### Yehoyachin Release

The proof text that anchors Nevuchadnezzar's 45-year count is the verse in *Melachim II* (25:27-29): *vayhi bishloshim v'sheva shanah l'galut Yehoyachin melech Yehudah b'shneim asar chodesh b'esrim v'chamishah lachodesh nasa Evil Merodach melech Bavel bishnat malchuto et rosh Yehoyachin melech Yehudah* — in the 37th year of Yehoyachin's exile, Evil Merodach king of Babylon, in his first regnal year, raised up Yehoyachin. *Vayotze oto mibeit hakela* — he took him out of the dungeon, after Yehoyachin had been imprisoned for 37 years.

### 45-Year Sum

*Galut Yehoyachin* occurred in the eighth year of Nevuchadnezzar. Adding 37 years brings the total to year 45 of Nevuchadnezzar — which is precisely when Evil Merodach became king. *Tanya: harei arba'im v'chamish* — the *baraisa* confirms: that is 45 years.

### Historical Alignment

The *Gemara* notes that this chronology is consistent with secular historical records regarding the reign of Evil Merodach (Amel-Marduk), lending additional confirmation to the calculation.
