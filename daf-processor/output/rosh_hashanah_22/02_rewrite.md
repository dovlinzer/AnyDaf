# Rosh Hashanah 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shabbat Violation

### Clear or Unclear

The daf begins by picking up from the *mishna* on 21b, which opens with the principle: *bein she-nira be'alil bein she-lo nira be'alil, mechallelin alav et ha-Shabbat* — whether the new moon was seen clearly or not clearly, witnesses may violate *Shabbat* to come and testify. One might have thought: if the moon appeared on a perfectly clear night and everyone could see it, surely there are witnesses who live closer to Jerusalem who did not have to violate *Shabbat* to get there — why should I, who live far away, be entitled to violate *Shabbat* to travel and testify when others will certainly arrive without any violation? The *mishna* rejects this reasoning: every witness who has seen the moon should come, because one never knows what will happen. Perhaps all the others will turn out to be invalid for some reason, and this witness's testimony will be the one on which *kiddush ha-chodesh* ultimately rests. The term *mechallelin* here implies not merely permission but obligation — if you have seen the moon, you must go.

### Rabbi Yosi Disagrees

*Rabbi Yosi omer*: if the moon was seen *be'alil* — clearly — then you do not violate *Shabbat*, because there will certainly be other witnesses available who did not need to travel far. The *mishna* then records an illustrative incident: more than forty pairs of witnesses were on their way to testify, and *Rabbi Akiva* detained them in Lod, reasoning that since so many people had seen the moon, there was no need for all of them to violate *Shabbat* — others closer to the destination would testify.

### Rabban Gamliel Policy

*Rabban Gamliel* sent word to *Rabbi Akiva*: *im me'akeiv attah et ha-rabbim, nimtza makhshilam le-atid lavo* — if you prevent the masses from coming now, you will cause them to stumble in the future. The concern is that next year, a witness will remember that the last time he made the long journey on *Shabbat*, he was turned away, and so he will not bother to go. This will undermine the entire institution of *kiddush ha-chodesh* going forward.

What is striking here is the nature of *Rabban Gamliel*'s argument. He is not claiming that this particular witness is necessary for the present *kiddush ha-chodesh* — it is entirely possible that forty other pairs will suffice without this one. Rather, the justification for *chilul Shabbat* is a macro-level policy consideration: in order to preserve the long-term functioning of the institution, it is necessary to maintain a norm of universal participation. This raises a conceptually interesting point: the *chilul Shabbat* is warranted not because of the micro-necessity of this individual witness, but because of the broader systemic requirement. This is somewhat analogous to the principle of *hativu sofam mishum techilatan* — permitting the return journey of those who traveled to save a life, even though the return itself involves no *pikuach nefesh*. The discussion there in *Beitzah* concerned rabbinic prohibitions, but the analogy is instructive: sometimes broader institutional considerations shape what is permitted.

## Be'alil Definition

### Psalms Source

The *gemara* asks: *mai mashma de-hai alio* — what is the linguistic basis for understanding *be'alil* as meaning "clearly"? *Rabbi Abba* cites the verse from Psalms 12:7: *imrot Hashem imrot tehorot, kesef tzaruf be'alil la-aretz, mezukak shivatayim* — the utterances of God are pure utterances, like silver refined *be'alil la-aretz*, purified sevenfold. It should be noted that standard English translations render *be'alil la-aretz* as referring to an earthenware crucible — the vessel in which silver is smelted. The *gemara*, however, reads the word *be'alil* as conveying purity and clarity, deriving from it the sense of something refined and clearly visible. Exactly what is done with *la-aretz* on that reading is left somewhat unclear. In any case, the root conveys refinement, purity, and clarity — and indeed, in modern Hebrew *nirah be'alil* means "clearly visible."

### Fifty Gates Debate

The verse's phrase *mezukak shivatayim* — refined sevenfold, meaning forty-nine — connects to a famous *midrash* that serves as the basis for a debate between Rav and Shmuel. One of them says: *nun sha'arei bina nivra'u ba-olam* — fifty gates of wisdom were created in the world, *ve-kulan nitnu le-Moshe chasar achat* — and all of them were given to Moshe except one, so Moshe possessed forty-nine. The source for this is the verse *va-techasrehu me'at me-Elokim* — you have diminished him only slightly from the divine. Forty-nine levels, then, represents the pinnacle of human wisdom, corresponding to the *shivatayim* — the seven sevens — of the verse.

### Kohelet's Ambition

Based on this, *bikesh Kohelet li-heyot ke-Moshe* — Kohelet aspired to attain the same level of wisdom as Moshe. A heavenly voice responded with the end of the verse: *ve-katuv yosher divrei emet* — and what is written straight in the words of truth? *Ve-lo kam navi od be-Yisrael ke-Moshe* — there arose no prophet again in Israel like Moshe. So Kohelet was told: you cannot be like Moshe.

The other opinion in the Rav-Shmuel debate responds: the prohibition *lo kam navi* refers specifically to a *navi* — a prophet. In the realm of kingship, Kohelet could indeed achieve that same level. So the aspiration itself was not the problem. The problem, according to this view, is the second clause of the verse: *bikesh Kohelet li-don dinim she-ba-lev, she-lo be-eidim ve-she-lo be-hatra'ah* — Kohelet sought to adjudicate cases based on his perception of people's innermost thoughts and motivations, without external witnesses and without formal warning. Because he was so wise, he believed he could see through to the truth without formal legal procedure. The heavenly voice responded with the words of truth inscribed in the Torah: *al pi shnayim eidim yakum davar* — on the testimony of two witnesses shall a matter be established.

The lesson is striking: however brilliant the judge, the legal system cannot be run on the basis of private insight. The world must be governed by objectively ascertainable facts, by formal procedure, not by the genius of any individual. This resonates with the famous observation about *Rabbi Meir* — that the *halacha* was not ruled like him because he was so penetrating that no one else could follow his reasoning. Raw intellectual brilliance, however extraordinary, does not override the institutional requirements of communal law.

## Shazpar Story

### Forty Pairs

The *mishna* records the incident of forty pairs of witnesses on their way to testify about the new moon, who were detained in Lod — apparently on their way to Yavneh, which was the seat of the *beit din* in the post-*Churban* period, and which is indeed closer to Lod than Jerusalem, making the journey more plausible in terms of covering the distance before the day's end.

### Shazpar's Role

A *braita* corrects the apparent attribution to *Rabbi Akiva*: *amar Rabbi Yehudah, chas ve-shalom she-Rabbi Akiva ikvan* — it never occurred to *Rabbi Akiva* to detain them, for he well understood the principle that witnesses must come even at the cost of *Shabbat*. Rather, it was *Shazpar rosh shel Gader* — a figure named Shazpar, the head of Gader (perhaps modern Gedera, near the coast, in the vicinity of Ashkelon) — who detained them. And *Rabban Gamliel* sent word and had him deposed from his position as a consequence of this action. This is quite a different story than one involving *Rabbi Akiva*.

### Day 30 Question

A related question arises, though it is not made fully explicit in the text: is the license to violate *Shabbat* specifically tied to cases where the *beit din* will be declaring the month on day 30, making it a twenty-nine-day month? If the moon was not seen on the night of day 30 and day 31 will automatically be *Rosh Chodesh* regardless, why would anyone violate *Shabbat* to come testify? There is still the question, raised earlier in the *gemara*, of whether there is a *mitzvah* to perform *kiddush ha-chodesh al pi ha-re'iyah* — based on actual testimony — even on day 31 when the calendar outcome is not in doubt. Whether that *mitzvah* alone would suffice to justify *chilul Shabbat*, and whether it would do so even if it were certain that many others had clearly seen the moon, remains not fully resolved.

## Valid Witnesses

### Father and Son

The next *mishna* addresses who may come to testify. *Av u-veno she-ra'u et ha-chodesh yelechu* — a father and son who have both seen the new moon should both go to testify. The *mishna* immediately clarifies: *lo she-mitztarfin zeh im zeh* — not that they combine together as a single valid pair of witnesses, since relatives cannot testify together. Rather, *she-im yippasel echad mehem, mitztaref ha-sheni im acher* — if one of them is disqualified for some reason, perhaps because he does not describe the moon's details correctly, the other remains available to pair with a different witness. Since we accept many more witnesses than the bare minimum of two, having a father and son travel together is no different from having ten unrelated people travel together — not all of them will be needed, but it is prudent to have backup.

### Rabbi Shimon Relatives

*Rabbi Shimon* goes further: *av u-veno ve-kol ha-kerovim kesherim le-edut ha-chodesh* — a father and son and all relatives are in fact *kasher* as witnesses for testimony about the new moon, even when combining together. This is a significant ruling that goes beyond the first clause of the *mishna*, and the *gemara* will search for its rationale.

### Tuvyah Story

*Rabbi Yosi* introduces a relevant incident: *ma'aseh be-Tuvyah ha-rofe* — the story of Tuvyah the doctor, who saw the new moon in Jerusalem together with his son and his freed slave. This was during the period of the *Beit ha-Mikdash*. The *kohanim* accepted Tuvyah and his son but invalidated the freed slave.

### Beit Din Reversal

When the group came before the *beit din* — the rabbinic court, which had ultimate authority — the court accepted Tuvyah and the freed slave and invalidated the son, on the grounds that Tuvyah and his son were relatives who could not testify together, whereas the status of the freed slave presented no such problem.

### Priestly vs. Rabbinic

The subtext of this episode is not subtle. The *kohanim* were exercised about the freed slave — a man of slave lineage, a former *Kena'ani* — even though he was now a full Jew. The *kohanim*, whose entire institutional identity rests on the principle of *ab u-veno*, of patrilineal descent and hierarchical lineage, were naturally sensitive to questions of status and lineage. They were unbothered by the father-and-son combination because that very structure is the foundation of *kehunah* itself. The rabbis, by contrast, were not impressed by lineage — for them, the freed slave's Jewish status was unimpeachable and irrelevant to his reliability as a witness, while the issue of relatives testifying together was the operative concern. This episode encapsulates in miniature the difference between the priestly and rabbinic approaches to communal hierarchy.

## Moshe Aharon Source

### Verse Interpretation

*Rav Levi* provides the textual basis for *Rabbi Shimon*'s ruling: *de-chtiv va-yomer Hashem el Moshe ve-el Aharon be-eretz Mitzrayim lemor, ha-chodesh ha-zeh lachem* — God commanded both Moshe and Aharon together regarding the sanctification of the new month. Since Moshe and Aharon were brothers, this implies that *edut zu tehi kesherah bachem* — this testimony is valid even when given by brothers or other relatives.

### Rabbanan Response

The *rabbanan* counter: the verse does not imply that brothers are qualified as joint witnesses. Rather, *edut zu tehi mesurah lachem* — this matter is entrusted to your leadership and responsibility. The verse addresses the leaders of the generation and indicates that *kiddush ha-chodesh* is their charge, not that it may be performed by relatives acting as co-witnesses.

### Type of Testimony

This debate opens a broader question: is testimony about the new moon a classic form of *edut* — formal legal testimony directed against a person — or is it something categorically different, more akin to reporting a state of affairs? In *halacha*, most formal testimony concerns events: a person did this act, committed this violation. Here, witnesses are simply stating a fact they observed — we saw the moon. The reason such factual statements usually still require two witnesses is likely not conceptual but practical: formal *halacha* generally demands direct evidence rather than circumstantial inference. But the *edut ha-chodesh* case raises the question of whether it truly belongs in the category of classic testimony at all.

### Lichtenstein View

A related framing was offered by Rav Aharon Lichtenstein: the operative verse governing classic testimony is *ki yakum ed hamas ba-ish* — when a witness rises up against a person. Formal *edut* is, at its core, testimony directed against someone. Testimony about the new moon is not directed against any person at all. It may therefore fall outside the classical category of testimony, which would explain why the normal rules of eligibility — including the disqualification of relatives — might not apply. This is related to the broader principle of *ed echad ne'eman be-issurin* — one witness is trusted regarding prohibitions — where again the testimony is not against a person but reports a state of affairs, and where accordingly the rules are significantly loosened.

## Rav Huna Challenge

### Conflicting Rulings

*Rav Chana bar Abba* ruled: *hilcheta ke-Rabbi Shimon* — the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Shimon* that relatives are *kasher* for *edut ha-chodesh*. *Rav Huna* challenged him: but *Rabbi Yosi* recorded the story of Tuvyah and his son being rejected by the *beit din* — and you are ruling like *Rabbi Shimon*?

### Rav Silence

*Rav Chana bar Abba* responded: I stated this ruling — *hilcheta ke-Rabbi Shimon* — in the presence of Rav many times, and Rav never objected. This silence, he argued, constituted implicit approval.

### Reversed Positions

*Rav Huna* then revealed the problem: how exactly did you present the positions to Rav? *Rav Chana* admitted that he had stated the positions in reverse — he attributed the ruling permitting relatives to *Rabbi Shimon* and the invalidating position to the other opinion. Since Rav heard the ruling as "*Rabbi Shimon* says relatives are invalid" and agreed, he was actually endorsing the stricter position. His silence was not an endorsement of the lenient ruling at all.

### Shmuel's Ruling

*Tavi brei de-Mari Tavi* reported in the name of *Mar Ukva* in the name of *Shmuel*: *hilcheta ke-Rabbi Shimon*. The question, of course, is which version of *Rabbi Shimon*'s position is being endorsed. Some manuscripts add the word *ein* — *ein hilcheta ke-Rabbi Shimon* — though this is clearly a later gloss, since the Aramaic formulation would be *leit* rather than *ein*. The general assumption, consistent with the plain reading of the *gemara* and with the story of Tuvyah's son being rejected, is that the *halacha* does not validate relatives for *edut ha-chodesh* — but the final conclusion remains genuinely murky.

## Invalid Witnesses

### Disqualified List

The *mishna* enumerates the categories of witnesses who are invalid: those who play dice (*ha-mesachek ba-kuvya*), those who lend money at interest (*malveh be-ribbit*), those who race pigeons (*mafrichei yonim*), those who engage in commerce with *shevi'it* produce (*socharei shevi'it*), and slaves (*avadim*). This is the well-known *mishna* from *Sanhedrin*.

### General Rule

The *mishna* then articulates the governing principle: *kol edut she-ein ha-ishah kesherah lah, af hen einan kesherim lah* — any testimony for which a woman is invalid, these categories of persons are likewise invalid. Tosafot notes an important distinction: this principle applies to those who are individually unfit. The earlier discussion about relatives was different — each relative is individually a *kasher* witness; the only question was whether they could combine. But the persons listed here are unfit as individuals, and everyone agrees they cannot testify for *kiddush ha-chodesh*.

### Woman's Testimony

The one domain in which a woman's testimony is accepted is to testify that another woman's husband has died, thereby permitting the widow to remarry. The rabbis granted very broad latitude for this category of testimony — *edut ishah* — including accepting hearsay (*ed mipi ed*). And by the rule of the *mishna*, these otherwise disqualified categories are likewise accepted for this testimony.

### Gazlan Categories

*Rav Ashi* draws out the implication: *zot omeret, gazlan de-divreihem, kesherim le-edut ishah* — this teaches that a rabbinic-level *gazlan* is valid for testimony about a woman's husband's death, but a biblical *gazlan* is not. The listed individuals are classified as *gazlan de-divreihem* — only rabbinically considered to be acting with the mentality of a thief — and it is only on that basis that they retain validity for *edut ishah*.

### Severity Question

This raises an obvious difficulty: several items on the list appear to involve biblical violations. Lending at interest and trading in *peirot shevi'it* are not obviously mere rabbinic offenses. Why are these individuals classified as only rabbinic *gazlanim*?

### Tosafot & Rashi

Tosafot offers two explanations. First, the *mishna* may be referring specifically to subcategories of these transgressions that are only rabbinic violations — there are indeed rabbinic forms of interest and rabbinic forms of prohibited *shevi'it* commerce. Second, and more conceptually interesting: a person who engages in these activities is typically able to rationalize his behavior and does not fully appreciate that he is committing a clear biblical violation. If a person genuinely does not perceive what he is doing as a serious transgression, then his willingness to engage in it does not demonstrate the same preparedness to put his testimony up for sale as would an out-and-out theft. It is this psychological dimension — the capacity for self-rationalization — that keeps him in the rabbinic rather than biblical *gazlan* category.

*Rashi* takes a somewhat different approach. Even if the violation is in fact biblical, these activities do not involve overtly taking something from another person — they are not straightforward theft. Because the nature of the interaction does not constitute the direct appropriation of someone else's property, it does not rise to the level that would make a person's testimony fundamentally untrustworthy. Only genuine, direct theft — the act of taking what manifestly belongs to someone else — raises the concern that the person's testimony can be bought. Absent that, even biblical violations of a financial character do not cross the threshold that invalidates one for *edut ishah*.

## Practical Provisions

### Transport Help

The *mishna* turns to the practical arrangements made for witnesses traveling to testify. If a witness saw the new moon but is unable to walk, he is transported on a donkey on *Shabbat* — and if necessary, even carried on his bed. The *Shabbat* violation is warranted by the same logic that permits the witness himself to travel.

### Defense & Supply

If people are lying in wait to waylay them along the route, the witnesses may carry weapons — sticks or arms — on *Shabbat*. If the journey is long, they may take provisions of food. All of this may be done despite it being *Shabbat*, because the testimony cannot be effective unless the witnesses actually arrive.

### Time Constraint

The limiting condition is that the journey must be completable within a night and a day — that is, the witness must be able to arrive before the following nightfall, since testimony delivered after the day of *Rosh Chodesh* has ended serves no purpose.

### Verse Justification

The *mishna* derives its authorization for *Shabbat* violation from the verse: *eleh mo'adei Hashem asher tikre'u otam be-mo'adam* — these are the appointed times of God that you shall proclaim in their proper time. The phrase *be-mo'adam* — in their time — implies that the proclamation must be made at the right moment even if that requires overriding *Shabbat*.

## Character Witnesses

### Vouching System

The *mishna* on 22b opens the new *perek* — though the discussion continues seamlessly from the previous chapter — with the following rule: *im einan makirin oto* — if the *beit din* does not recognize the witnesses who have come to testify, *meshalchin imo acher le-ha'ido* — they send another person along with the witness who can vouch for his trustworthiness and identity. This practice arose in response to the problem of sectarians attempting to manipulate the calendar by submitting false testimony. Originally, the *beit din* accepted testimony from any person who walked in. Once the *Baitusim* began corrupting the process, the rabbis instituted a requirement that only recognized witnesses, or those vouched for by recognized parties, would be accepted.

### One vs. Pair

The *gemara* immediately asks: *mai acher, chad* — does the *mishna* mean they send one additional person? The *gemara* questions whether one character witness would be sufficient, since the normal rules of *edut* require two witnesses.

### One Sufficient

At the level of the *mishna*'s plain meaning, one character witness does seem to be what is intended. The *mishna* uses singular language, and it would be reasonable to think that the *beit din*'s institutional discretion in setting up this character-witness system would allow them to accept a single vouching individual.

### Pappa's Solution

*Rav Pappa* resolves the apparent contradiction between the *mishna* and a *braita* (which implies two character witnesses are needed) by reinterpreting the *mishna*: *mai acher, zug acher* — when the *mishna* says "another," it means another *pair*, not a single individual. The basis for requiring a pair is the principle *mishpat kesiv bei* — the verse associates *mishpat*, the ordinary rules of a law court, with the process of *kiddush ha-chodesh*, which means the normal requirement of two witnesses applies to the character witnesses as well. As is acknowledged, this is a somewhat strained reading of the *mishna*, which clearly seems to speak of one person being sent. But the *gemara* commits to it in order to harmonize the two sources.

### Rabbi Nehorai

The *gemara* then cites a *braita*: *ma'aseh be-Rabbi Nehorai she-halach eitzel ha-eid le-ha'id alav be-Shabbat le-Usha* — Rabbi Nehorai traveled on *Shabbat* to Usha to serve as a character witness for another witness. This demonstrates not only that the witness himself may violate *Shabbat*, but that the character witness may do so as well — for without the character witness, the primary witness will not be accepted, making the entire exercise pointless. The logic is the same: whatever is necessary to make the testimony effective is permitted.

### Two Witnesses Needed

The *gemara* addresses the apparent implication that a single character witness suffices, since only *Rabbi Nehorai* is mentioned. Two explanations are offered. The first: there was in fact another character witness traveling with *Rabbi Nehorai*, but the *braita* only mentions *Rabbi Nehorai* because the *chiddush* — the novel point — is that even a figure of his stature would violate *Shabbat* merely to serve as a character witness. *Rav Ashi* offers a variant: the second character witness was already present in Usha; only *Rabbi Nehorai* needed to violate *Shabbat*, because the other witness was already there. In either case, there were indeed two character witnesses. The *chiddush* of the story, according to *Rav Ashi*, is that one should not hesitate to violate *Shabbat* on the assumption that the second witness might not be there — even in a case of uncertainty about whether one's own presence will be sufficient to complete the pair, one should go. The simple reading of both the *mishna* and the *braita* would allow a single character witness, but the *gemara* maintains that two are required.

## Reports vs. Witnesses

### Ulla's Report

When Ulla arrived from *Eretz Yisrael* and reported that the new month had been sanctified there, *Rav Kahana* noted that the question of credibility should not be limited to Ulla specifically.

### Revelation Principle

*Rav Kahana* articulates the principle: *lo mibaya Ulla, de-gavra raba hu* — it goes without saying that Ulla, a great scholar, can be trusted. *Ela afilu inish de-alma nami meheman* — even an ordinary person would be trusted to report what happened. Why? *Kol milta de-avida le-igluyi, lo meshakri bei inshei* — anything that is destined to become publicly known, people do not lie about. If the month was indeed sanctified, word will spread and the truth will emerge regardless. Anyone who falsely reported that *Rosh Chodesh* had been declared would quickly be exposed. The self-correcting nature of the information therefore removes the incentive to lie and makes any messenger credible.

### End-of-World Report

A *braita* confirms this: *ba echad be-sof ha-olam ve-amar kiddshu beit din et ha-chodesh, ne'eman* — someone who comes from the ends of the earth and reports that the *beit din* sanctified the month is believed.

### Identity vs. Revelation

This creates a striking asymmetry: witnesses who appear before the *beit din* to testify directly about the moon must be recognized or vouched for, while a messenger who simply reports the fact of *kiddush ha-chodesh* can be trusted even if completely unknown. The reason is structural. A witness who appears in person might be a sectarian impersonating a legitimate witness, or simply an unknown figure whose reliability cannot be assessed. But a reporter of a communal event is protected by the revelation principle — the facts will become known regardless, and a false report will be quickly exposed. The requirements therefore track the nature of the risk, not a uniform standard of trustworthiness.

## Baitusim Plot

### Original Openness

*Barishonah hayu mekabelin edut ha-chodesh mikol adam* — originally, the *beit din* accepted testimony about the new moon from any person without restriction. No prior recognition was required.

### False Testimony

*Mishe-kilkelu ha-Baitusim* — once the *Baitusim* corrupted the process. A *braita* elaborates: *pa'am achat bikshu Baitusim le-hat'ot et ha-chakhamim* — on one occasion the *Baitusim* sought to deceive the Sages about when *Rosh Chodesh* fell. They hired two people for four hundred *zuz* to give false testimony about when the new moon had appeared. Their motivation, as noted earlier, was to manipulate the calendar so that *Pesach* would fall on *Shabbat* — which would cause the day after *Pesach* to coincide with the day after *Shabbat*, thereby aligning the rabbinic calendar with the *Baitusim*' practice of counting fifty days from the Sunday after *Pesach* for *Shavuot*.

### Undercover Agent

*Echad mishelanu ve-echad mishelahem* — one of the two hired men was, as it turned out, sympathetic to the rabbinic cause. The *Baitusim* did not know this. He was not planted as a formal undercover agent; his sympathies simply lay with the rabbis, and when hired to deliver false testimony, he saw an opportunity to expose the scheme.

### Ridiculous Details

Their man testified first and departed. Then the second man — the one with rabbinic sympathies — was brought before the *beit din* and asked: tell us how you saw the moon. He proceeded to deliver the following account: *olei hayiti be-ma'aleh Adumim* — I was going up the ascent of Ma'aleh Adumim. I saw the moon crouching between two rocks. Its head resembled a calf, its ears resembled a goat, its horns resembled a deer, and its tail was between its thighs. I peered closely at it and fell backwards in astonishment. The absurdity of this description — the moon crouching like a beast with animal features — was clearly designed to expose the fraud. And to remove all doubt, he added: *harei masayim zuz tzerurim li be-sidini* — and here, tied in my garment, are the 200 *zuz* I was paid to say this.

### Confession

He then explained his motivation: *shamati she-Baitusim mevakshim le-hat'ot et ha-chakhamim* — I heard that the *Baitusim* were plotting to deceive you. I went along with it and took the money, he said, because I was afraid that someone less principled would come and actually succeed in misleading you. So he played along in order to expose the plot.

### Response & Reform

The *beit din*'s response: *masayim zuz netunim lecha be-matanah* — keep the 200 *zuz* as a gift. The person who hired him — the *Baitusim* agent — was brought to the post and flogged. And from that moment on, *hitkinu she-lo yehu mekabelin ela min ha-makirim* — they instituted the policy of accepting testimony only from recognized individuals. The entire recognition-and-vouching system of the *mishna* flows directly from this episode.

## Bonfire System

### Beacon Method

*Barishonah hayu masi'in masu'ot* — originally, the communication of *Rosh Chodesh* across *Eretz Yisrael* and into the *golah* was accomplished by a system of mountaintop bonfires. The principle is elegant: if people are stationed on successive mountain peaks and each lights a fire upon seeing the previous peak's fire, a message can travel hundreds of miles in minutes — a communication daisy chain of extraordinary efficiency.

### Sabotage

*Mishe-kilkelu ha-Kusim* — when the *Kusim* (with some manuscripts reading *Baitusim*) interfered by lighting false fires at unauthorized times. The difference between the sabotage of the testimony system and the sabotage of the bonfire system is worth noting. A person who delivers false testimony in person puts his reputation on the line; as the *gemara* noted earlier, people do not lie about things that will eventually be revealed. But lighting a bonfire on a mountaintop at night is entirely anonymous. Nobody knows who lit it, and the revelation principle does not apply. The communication channel was therefore vulnerable in a way that personal testimony is not — it could be hacked. The *Kusim* (or *Baitusim*) exploited precisely this vulnerability.

### Technical Details

The *mishna* describes the physical apparatus: *mevi'in kelonsa'ot shel erez aruchim* — they would take long cedar poles, together with reeds, olive wood, and flax fiber, wrapping the materials together with rope into a bundle at the top of the pole. The beacon-lighter would then go to the mountain peak, ignite the materials, and wave the fire *u-molik u-mevi u-ma'aleh u-morid* — forwards and back, upward and downward. Tosafot explains that the waving motion was necessary to ensure the signal could not be mistaken for a natural phenomenon such as a shooting star.

### Mountain Chain

The chain of beacons ran: from *Har ha-Mishchah* — the Mount of Olives — to Sartaba, from Sartaba to Grofina, from Grofina to Havran, from Havran to Beit Baltin.

### Full Coverage

*U-mi-Beit Baltin lo zazu misham* — from Beit Baltin, the final station in the chain, they did not move until *she-ro'eh kol ha-golah lefanav ke-madurat esh* — until the entire exile was illuminated before them like a great bonfire. The entire *golah*, covering hundreds of miles, received the signal effectively instantaneously.

### Messenger Switch

Once the *Kusim* began lighting false fires and the system was compromised, *hitkinu she-yehu sheluchim yotzim* — the rabbis switched to dispatching human messengers to communicate *Rosh Chodesh*. Messengers, unlike bonfires, are accountable individuals who put their reputations on the line. The communication system that was so elegant and instantaneous had to be abandoned for something far slower because the channel had been hacked — the price of sabotage was paid in time and efficiency for generations afterward.
