# Yevamot 31b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Safek Gierushin Review

### Opening

Today's daf is officially 32, though we continue picking up from the bottom of 31b. The *mishnah* under discussion deals with cases of *safek kiddushin* and *safek gierushin*, examining what happens when there is a question about whether an *erva* is in the picture and what must be done with her *tzarat erva*. The *mishnah*'s ruling is *chalitza*, not *yibum*.

### Safek Gierushin Summary

The *gemara* pointedly observes that the case of *safek gierushin* discussed in the *mishnah* is not really a classic *safek gierushin* at all. Rather, it involves a *gierushin* that is *pasul d'rabbanan*. This distinction is consequential: the *gemara* operates on the assumption that a genuine *safek gierushin* would not be legally meaningful, because the woman would retain her *chazaka*—her status quo presumption—as an *eshet ish*. Correspondingly, the co-wife, the *tzarat erva*, would retain her status as exempt from *yibum*.

### Cost of Chalitza

The *gemara* works through the implications of being *machmir* in such a case. In some situations, one can demand *chalitza* without incurring any cost. Here, however, if we were to be *machmir* and demand *chalitza*, it would come with a serious cost. Therefore, we leave the matter on its *d'oraita* footing: such a woman is considered still an *eshet ish*, her co-wife is a *tzarat erva*, and the co-wife walks away without *chalitza*.

---

## Gittin Safek Cases

### Gittin Mishna

The *gemara* now raises an apparent challenge. When it comes to *get*, does the *gemara* not have a real case of *safek gierushin* with the same consequence—that the co-wife requires *chalitza*? The challenge is introduced with the formula *v'ha tnan*: we taught in the *mishnah* in Gittin the following classic case of *safek*.

A woman was standing in the public domain—*hayso omedet birshus harabbim*—and her husband threw a *get* toward her. If the *get* landed closer to her, *megureshet*; if closer to him, *eina megureshet*. And if it landed exactly at the midpoint—*mechtza al mechtza*—then she is in a state of doubt: *megureshet v'eina megureshet*.

### Nafka Minos

What are the practical consequences of this doubtful divorce? The *gemara* in Gittin identifies two *nafka minos*. First, if her husband is a *kohen*, he cannot simply decide that perhaps the divorce was invalid and remain married to her—she might be divorced, and remarrying a *gerusha* would be forbidden to him. Second, and directly relevant to our discussion, if she is an *erva*, her co-wife is a questionable *tzarat erva*. The co-wife cannot simply walk away, because the woman might indeed be divorced and the co-wife therefore obligated in *yibum*; yet *yibum* cannot be performed because the woman might not be divorced, making the co-wife a genuine *tzarat erva*. The resolution: *ba'ee chalitza*, she requires *chalitza*.

The *gemara* thus observes that in another tractate we rule that a genuine *safek gierushin* calls for *chalitza*, not that she simply retains her *chazaka* and walks off. The concern about being *machmir* leading to *yibum* does not deter us there—we demand *chalitza*.

### Two Pairs Witnesses

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking a teaching of Rabbah and Rav Yosef, who both said: *hacha b'shtei kitei edim askinan*—the case in Gittin is specifically one involving two pairs of witnesses. *Achas omeret karov la v'achas omeret karov lo*: one group of witnesses says definitively that it landed closer to her, and the other group says definitively it landed closer to him.

The reason the *chazaka* does not simply hold in that case—the woman's *chazaka* as an *eshet ish* and the co-wife's *chazaka* as a *tzarat erva*—is that there is something directly opposing the *chazaka*. It is not merely an abstract doubt: we don't know where the *get* fell, so we default to the status quo. Here there is direct testimony against the *chazaka*. The only complication is that the testimony is contradicted. Nevertheless, because *dahavei leh sefeika d'oraisa*—this creates a biblical-level doubt—it is sufficient to displace the *chazaka* and place us genuinely in the realm of uncertainty.

By contrast, our *mishnah* in Yevamot—*u'masnisin d'haka be'chas akhas*—is dealing with a single pair of witnesses, split between themselves. There is no two-against-two confrontation with the *chazaka*. At most this would generate *sefeika d'rabbanan*, a rabbinic-level doubt. At the *d'oraita* level, one simply follows the status quo, and in such a case *chalitza* would not even be required.

## Chazaka Principles

### D'Oraisa Doubt

This distinction—between an abstract doubt and direct contradictory testimony—raises a deeper question about how *chazaka* functions. If you have a *chazaka* and a *safek*, you follow the *chazaka*. If there are witnesses testifying against the *chazaka*, you follow the witnesses. But what if there is a *chazaka*, and witnesses against it, and those witnesses are themselves contradicted by other witnesses? Do you say: once there is direct testimony, even if ultimately contradicted, that is sufficient to undermine the *chazaka* and leave you with a blank slate—a genuine *safek*? Or do you say: throw out both pairs of witnesses, since they contradict one another, and return to the *chazaka*?

The *gemara* initially stated that contradictory witnesses displace the *chazaka* and create a real *d'oraita* doubt. It now revisits that claim and says: *v'su*—moreover, that is not actually correct. When two pairs of witnesses contradict each other—*okei trei l'bahadi trei*—we set them against one another, cancel them out, and restore the woman to her *chazaka*. This is the true *halacha*: contradictory witnesses are rejected entirely, and one returns to the status quo.

### Bar Shatya Case

The *gemara* supports this from a concrete legal story: *meidiy de-hava a-nichsei d'bar shatya*—like the case of the property of Bar Shatya. Bar Shatya was a person who apparently experienced intermittent episodes of mental incapacity—a *shoteh*—and the legal question concerned business transactions he had conducted: were they carried out during a period of lucidity or not?

Bar Shatya sold some property. Two witnesses came and testified: *k'she-hu chalim zavin*—he was of sound mind when he sold it. Then another two witnesses came and testified: *k'she-hu shoteh zavin*—he was not of sound mind when he sold it.

### Rav Ashi's Rule

Rav Ashi ruled: *okei trei l'bahadi trei v'ara okmah b'chazakas Bar Shatya*—set the two pairs against each other, cancel them out, and have the property remain under Bar Shatya's presumptive ownership. This is the default position: when testimony is irreconcilably contradicted, we reject all of it and revert to the *chazaka*.

One could observe that there is something to discuss about the difference between a *chazaka* rooted in property ownership and one rooted in a person's legal status, but the operative principle here is clear. If the doubt were genuine, one might expect a different outcome—perhaps dividing the property, as in the famous opening *mishnah* of Bava Metzia. Instead, we reject the witnesses and default to the status quo.

This brings the *gemara* back to the original difficulty. If even contradictory witnesses against a *chazaka* are thrown out and the *chazaka* is restored, how can the *mishnah* in Gittin say that in a case of *safek get* one requires *chalitza*? Whether the doubt arises from contradictory witnesses or from an abstract uncertainty, the *d'oraita* result should always be to follow the status quo and not require *chalitza*. If *chalitza* is nonetheless required, it must be a *chumra* even in the face of a *chazaka*. And if that is so, why does our *mishnah* in Yevamot not present a classic case of *safek get*?

## Mishnah Structure

### Abaye's Logic

Abaye responds with the principle of *yagid alav rei'o*—quoting the verse in Iyov, let his companion reveal it—meaning: learn one case from its context and juxtaposition with the other. His answer is: *tana b'kiddushin v'hu ha-din l'gerushin*. The *mishnah* taught a classic *safek* scenario in the context of *kiddushin*—a case where one did not know whether the object of *kiddushin* landed closer to the woman or closer to him—and the same *halacha* of *chalitza* without *yibum* applies to *gierushin* as well. Conversely, the *mishnah* taught certain cases in the context of *gierushin*—specifically, a *get* that was *pasul d'rabbanan*—and the same would be true if one had *kiddushin* that were *pasul d'rabbanan*.

The point is: do not read the two sets of scenarios as mutually exclusive. The *mishnah* gave one cluster of examples under *kiddushin* and another cluster under *gierushin*, but both sets of *halachot* apply in both directions. The different examples were given to add scenarios, not to exclude the applicability of the other category.

### Rava Objects

Rava pushes back. If one can simply learn one case from the other in both directions, why does the *mishnah* use the language *zehu*—"this is the case"—for both *safek kiddushin* and *safek gierushin*? That formulation sounds precise and exclusionary. When the *mishnah* says *zehu*, it implies: this and no more, not a broader category.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava therefore offers his own resolution: *kol she-yesh b'kiddushin yesh b'gerushin*. Whatever applies to *kiddushin* also applies to *gierushin*. Any classic *safek* scenario—one did not know whether the item landed closer or farther—would generate the same *halacha* in both cases. In that sense, the cases of *kiddushin* are not limited to the specific scenarios mentioned; a classic *safek* by *gierushin* would equally call for *chalitza*.

However, *v'yesh b'gerushin ma she-ein b'kiddushin*: there are defects specific to a *get*—the absence of a *zman*, the absence of two *eidim*, and so on—that do not apply to a *shtar kiddushin*. When the *mishnah* says *zehu safek gerushin*, the word *zehu* is *lav davka*—it is not meant to limit the category. Even a classic *safek* would apply. But when the *mishnah* says *zehu* regarding *kiddushin*, that *zehu* is *davka*, because the defects of a *get* that render it *pasul* do not carry over to *shtar kiddushin*.

Specifically, *le-ma'utei zman de-leita b'kiddushin*: to exclude the case of the missing *zman*, which invalidates a *get* but does not invalidate a *shtar kiddushin*. This explains why the *mishnah* uses exclusive language regarding *kiddushin*: it is not limiting *kiddushin* to only those scenarios, but rather signaling that the problems of *gierushin*—particularly the *zman* requirement—do not apply to *kiddushin*. That prompts the next question: why?

## Zman (Date) Requirement

### Two Reasons

Why did the Rabbis not require a *zman* in a *shtar kiddushin*? To answer this, the *gemara* first identifies why a *zman* is required in a *get* at all. Two reasons are offered. *Hani'cha l'man d'amar mishum peiri*: according to the view that the purpose of the date is to protect the woman's right to recover *peiros*—the usufruct—from her husband. And the second reason, which the *gemara* calls *mishum bas achoso*: to prevent a husband from backdating a *get* in order to protect his wife—perhaps his niece—from a charge of adultery.

Rashi explains the *peiros* reason as follows: from the moment a woman is divorced, her husband loses his entitlement to consume the fruit from her *nichsei melog*—her personal property. She is entitled to recover from him the fruit he consumed after the date of divorce. Since the husband may be tempted not to date the *get*, or to date it as late as possible, the Rabbis required a date to protect her. The question of why the husband would not be motivated to include an early date—since that would reduce his liability—is resolved by the observation that *mi-sha'ah she-nasan einav bah l'garshah shuv ein l'ba'al peiros*: from the moment he began writing the *get* with the intention of divorcing her, he forfeits his right to the fruit. So it is actually against his interest to have any date at all.

Similarly, once the Rabbis required a date, the concern about predating is neutralized, because witnesses who sign the *get* will not sign a false date. The date is therefore an effective protection: it establishes the precise moment from which the woman can recover her fruit, and it prevents false claims about timing in cases touching on adultery.

### Peiros Inapplicable

Given these two reasons, the *gemara* now asks why the *zman* was not required for a *shtar kiddushin* as well. Regarding the *peiros* reason, the answer is straightforward: *arusa leit lah peiros*. A husband's right to eat the fruit of his wife's property begins only at *nissu'in*, not at *kiddushin*. No transfer of property rights occurs at the moment of *kiddushin*, and therefore a date serves no function with respect to *peiros*.

### Adultery Challenge

But what of the second reason—*mishum bas achoso*, the concern about covering up adultery? If we require a date in a *get* so that one can know precisely when the divorce took place and prevent someone from claiming that a woman was already divorced before an act of adultery, the same logic would seem to apply to *kiddushin*. Without a date on the *shtar kiddushin*, a woman could claim she was only married after a particular event, thereby retroactively altering the legal picture. Should we not therefore require a date in a *shtar kiddushin* for the same reason?

### Money vs. Document

The *gemara*'s answer: *mishum d'ika d'mikadesh b'chaspa v'ika d'mikadesh b'shtara lo tiknu rabanan zeman*. Since people perform *kiddushin* with money far more often than with a document—and when *kiddushin* is performed with money there is no dated document at all—we would not have precise knowledge of the date of *kiddushin* in most cases anyway. It would therefore be inconsistent and practically ineffective to suddenly require a date when the minority of people who perform *kiddushin* with a *shtar* do so. The absence of a date would be the normal state of affairs, and the Rabbis did not impose a *zman* requirement for this reason.

As a practical observation, it is worth noting that among all the evidence recovered from the Cairo Geniza—the vast corpus of marriage documents and *ketubot* analyzed by scholars such as Mordechai Friedman—there appears to be not a single documented instance of *kiddushei shtara* in practice. The *gemara* acknowledges that some do *kiddushin* with money and some with a document, but the historical record suggests overwhelmingly that *kiddushei kesef* was the universal practice.

### Slave Analogy

Rav Acha the son of Rav Yosef raised a challenge to Rav Ashi: *v'ha avda*—what about a slave? A slave can be acquired either with money or with a document, just as *kiddushin* can be performed either way. Yet the Rabbis did require a *zman* in the document of slave acquisition. Why, then, does the same logic not apply to require a *zman* in a *shtar kiddushin*?

### Majority Principle

The *gemara* answers: *hatam ruba b'shtara*—there, the majority of slave acquisitions are done with a document, since owners naturally want documented proof of ownership of their property. Therefore, the Rabbis require a *zman* in that document, and we are not concerned about the minority cases done with money. *Hacha ruba b'chesef*—here, the majority of *kiddushin* are performed with money. We do not restructure the entire institution for the rare case of *kiddushei shtara*.

### Witness Testimony

The *gemara* offers an alternative reason why a *zman* in a *shtar kiddushin* would be impractical: *ibba'it eima mishum d'lo efshar*—because it would simply not be effective. The question is: who would hold onto the *shtar kiddushin*?

If she holds onto it—*lenacheihu gabi didah*—she could erase the date and deny when the *kiddushin* took place: *machkah lei*. If he holds onto it—the same problem arises in reverse: *zimnin de-bat achoto hee, mechaplei lah*, perhaps she is his niece and he will want to protect her by making the document disappear. And if the witnesses hold onto it as a neutral third party—*lincha gabei edim*—that also will not help. If they still remember the event, *leisu lisan*, they can testify from their own memory without needing the document. But if they do not remember, they might look at the document and testify based on what they read, not based on genuine recollection. And the Torah says *mi-pihem velo miksavam*: testimony must come from their mouths, from what they actually remember, and not from what they read in a document.

### Memory vs. Writing

This principle—*mi-pihem velo miksavam*—opens a genuinely striking discussion. Tosafot raises an obvious objection: the entire institution of a signed *shtar* is premised on the document serving as evidence. Why would witnesses need to testify at all? They should simply produce the signed document and let it speak for itself. Tosafot concedes the point entirely: if the witnesses actually produce the document and present it to the court without claiming independent memory, that is perfectly valid—*shapir havu avdi*. The signed witnesses on a *shtar* are treated as if their testimony has already been examined by a *beit din*—*de-edim ha-chasumim a-shetar na-asu ke-mi she-nechkerah edutan be-Veit Din*. The concern is specifically that they might not produce the document but instead claim to testify from memory when in fact they are relying on what they read—*ela shema ya-idu al ha-ma'aseh atzmo ke-ilu hem zochrim ve-einam zochrim, ela al pi ha-ksav*. That is the invalid case.

There is a further refinement: if reading a document actually prompts genuine recollection—not simply reading and asserting the event happened, but actually remembering it—then testifying based on that recalled memory is valid. The problematic case is the one in the middle: the witnesses do not genuinely remember, they are not producing the document, but they are testifying as if they remember because they read it. What is permitted, then, is either presenting the signed document directly, or using a written note to jog one's memory so that one genuinely recalls the event and testifies from actual recollection.

Tosafot also notes the practice cited by Rabbeinu Tam of witnesses writing out their testimony and sending it in writing to the *beit din*. This is different from the problematic case: since the witnesses do genuinely remember, *d'lo karinan bei mipihem velo mipi kesavam*, the mode of communication through writing does not render the testimony invalid—*keivan d'heim zochrim edusam*. The problem arises only when the written record is substituting for, rather than expressing, actual memory.

## Three Brothers Case

### Case Setup

The *mishnah* now presents: *gimel achin nesuin gimel nochriyos*—three brothers married to three unrelated women. One of them dies—*v'meis echad meihem*—and the second one performs *ma'amar* with the available *yevamah*—*v'asah bah hasheini ma'amar*—and then the second one dies as well.

To set out the case concretely: Reuven is married to Rochel, Shimon is married to Devorah, and Levi is married to Chuldah. Reuven dies. Shimon does *ma'amar* with Rochel. Shimon then dies. Levi now has an obligation of *yibum* toward Devorah, coming from Shimon, and an obligation of *yibum* toward Rochel, coming from Reuven—and additionally a quasi-obligation toward Rochel from Shimon, since *ma'amar* establishes a semi-marital bond. Rochel thus comes to him through two separate brothers.

### Zika Problem

The *mishnah* rules: *harei elu choltzos v'lo misyabmos*—both women, Devorah and Rochel, receive *chalitza* and may not undergo *yibum*. Why is Rochel problematic is clear from the *drashah* on the verse: *mi she'aleha zikas yavam echad*, the *yibum* obligation applies only to a woman connected through one deceased brother, *v'lo she'aleha zikas shnei yevamin*—not to a woman connected through two brothers. Since Shimon's *ma'amar* created a quasi-bond with Rochel while Reuven's original marriage also binds her to Levi, she arrives at Levi through two separate brothers, and that dual *zika* is excluded. If *ma'amar* carries some *d'oraita* weight—as the school of Beit Shammai maintains—then Rochel could genuinely stand in a quasi-status that creates a real double *zika*; even according to those who hold *ma'amar* is purely *d'rabbanan*, the *drashah* would be applied at the *d'rabbanan* level.

### Yibum Question

What is less obvious is why Devorah cannot undergo *yibum*. Devorah is connected to Levi through Shimon, straightforwardly, without any complication. Why does the *mishnah* require that she also receive only *chalitza*?

### Gezeira Decree

The *gemara* addresses this. If the prohibition on Rochel were a genuine *d'oraita* problem—a real biblical disqualification—then why does she require *chalitza* at all? If the Torah excludes a woman in such a double-*zika* situation, she should simply walk away without any *chalitza*. The *gemara* answers: *ela miderabbanan*—this entire prohibition is only rabbinic in origin, most likely because *ma'amar* itself is a *d'rabbanan* institution. Since it is a *d'rabbanan* matter, Rochel still requires *chalitza*.

The reason Devorah also requires only *chalitza*—and cannot undergo *yibum*—is a *gezeira*: *shema yomru shtei yevamot habaot mibayit echad mityabmot*. People will think that since Rochel was quasi-married to Shimon through *ma'amar*, and Devorah was actually married to Shimon, both are Shimon's wives. If Levi does *yibum* with one and *chalitza* with the other, observers will conclude that when a brother dies leaving two wives, the surviving brother may do *yibum* with both. That conclusion is of course wrong: one does *yibum* with one wife and *chalitza* with the others.

### Mixed Forbidden

The *gemara* then considers: why not simply allow *yibum* with Devorah and *chalitza* with Rochel? The *gemara* answers with a further *gezeira*: *shema yomru bayit echad miktzato banuy umiktzato chalutz*. People will see both women in the picture, and they will conclude that when a brother dies with multiple wives, one can do *yibum* with one and *chalitza* with another interchangeably.

One might respond: so what if people think that? What is the actual harm? The *gemara* answers: *ee d'miabem v'hadar chalitz*—if the normal practice were *yibum* first and then *chalitza* for the remaining wives, the error would carry no intrinsic cost. Perhaps people would perform unnecessary *chalitza*, and the women would be disqualified from *kehuna*, but the *beit din* would know better, and the act of *chalitza* itself would not be inherently harmful. But here the concern is different: *dilma chalitz be'reisha v'hadar miyabem*—perhaps, based on the mistaken understanding, someone will first perform *chalitza* with one and then attempt *yibum* with the other. That reversal is catastrophic. Once he has performed *chalitza* he enters the category of one who has not built up his brother's house—*v'kam lei b'asher lo yivneh*—and the Torah says *shuv lo yivneh*: once he has failed to build, he can no longer do so. The *chalitza* would render the subsequent *yibum* a violation.

It is therefore to prevent this catastrophic wrong-order scenario that the *gemara* requires both women to receive *chalitza* and permits neither to undergo *yibum*.

### Rava's Get Case

Rava now introduces a new variant: *nasan get l'maamaro*. What if, after performing *ma'amar* with Rochel, Shimon changed his mind and gave her a *get* to undo the *ma'amar*, and then died? Is Rochel now out of the picture, freeing Levi to perform *yibum* with Devorah? And might Levi even be free to perform *yibum* with Rochel herself, since she reverts to being a simple *yevamah* awaiting *yibum* or *chalitza*?

Rava rules: *nasan get l'maamaro, hutra tzarasah*—if he gave a *get* to counteract his *ma'amar*, the co-wife Devorah is released and may undergo *yibum*. *Aval hi asura*—but Rochel herself remains forbidden to *yibum*, and Levi can perform only *chalitza* with her. Why? *D'michalfa b'baalas get*—because people will confuse her situation with that of a woman who received a *get* on the *zika* itself, not on the *ma'amar*.

To clarify the relevant structure of *halacha*: if a man performed *ma'amar* and wishes to separate, he requires both a *get* for the *ma'amar* and *chalitza* for the *zika*. If he did no *ma'amar* and wishes to release the *yevamah*, only *chalitza* is required. There is also the concept of a *get l'zika*—a *get* addressed not to a *ma'amar* but to the *zika* itself—which is a rabbinic act that prevents *yibum* going forward, while still leaving the woman requiring *chalitza*. The critical distinction in Rava's ruling is that the *get* in this case was given *l'maamaro*—specifically to counteract the *ma'amar*—and not to the *zika*.

### Get Scope

A second opinion is cited: *icha d'amri*, some say Rava's ruling is that *nasan get l'maamaro, hutra afilu hi*—even Rochel herself is permitted, and Levi may perform *yibum* even with her. The reasoning: *ma d'avad bah shaklei*—what he did to her, the *ma'amar*, he completely removed. The *get* neutralizes the *ma'amar* entirely, and she is treated as if no *ma'amar* was ever performed. We do not view her as a *baalas get* who received a *get* on the *zika*; we see her simply as a *yevamah* who no longer has any *ma'amar* in the picture.

Rashi clarifies the distinction between the two opinions: the *get* must explicitly specify that it is directed at the *ma'amar* and not at the *zika*—*she-peresh l'maamaro v'lo l'zikato*. A *stam get*, one without explicit specification, is presumed to be directed at the *zika* as well, thereby preventing all the brothers from performing *yibum*: *aval stama kaima v'lo yivne kulu achim*.

This ambiguity actually lends force to Rava's first position. Since it is quite unclear in practice whether a given *get* is directed at the *ma'amar* or at the *zika*, the concern about confusion—about people thinking this is a case of receiving a *get* on the *zika*—is not at all far-fetched. The *gezeira* is well-grounded precisely because the distinction is subtle and practically difficult to maintain.

## Two Sisters Case

### First Status

The *mishnah* presents: *shnei achim nesuim shtei achayot*—two brothers married to two sisters—and one of them dies. Reuven is married to Rochel and Shimon is married to Leah. Reuven dies. What is Rochel's status with respect to Shimon? She is *achot isha*—his wife's sister—an *erva*, and she is forbidden. She walks away without *yibum* or *chalitza*.

### Permanent Prohibition

The *mishnah* then adds: *v'achar kach meta ishto shel sheni*—and afterwards the wife of the second brother, Leah, dies. Could it now be argued that Rochel is no longer *achot isha*, since the sister is no longer alive, and Shimon might now be permitted to perform *yibum* with Rochel? The *mishnah* rules emphatically: *harei zu asura alav l'olam*—Rochel remains forbidden to Shimon forever. *Ho'il v'nasra alav sha'a achas*—since she was forbidden to him at that critical moment when the *yibum* obligation first arose, that prohibition is permanent.

### Third Brother Case

The *gemara* asks: *p'shita*—this is obvious! The *gemara* supports this with a comparison to a case discussed just prior, involving three brothers. Reuven, Shimon, and Levi. Reuven dies. Levi then marries Rochel. Levi dies. Shimon is now confronted with the question: may he perform *yibum* with Rochel? Even though by the time of Levi's death Rochel is no longer Shimon's wife's sister in any direct sense—Leah may be dead, or Rochel is no longer in that relationship—Shimon remains forbidden. The reasoning: at the initial critical moment when Reuven died, Rochel was *achot isha* to Shimon. That status was fixed at that moment.

### Obviousness

If that case—where Rochel was not completely excluded from the family because Levi had married her and she was still *mutar* to Levi—already established that the initial forbidden status is permanent, how much more so in the case of our *mishnah*, where Rochel was completely pushed away from the family the moment Reuven died. *Hacha d'ka midchaya mehai beita legamrei*—here she was entirely excluded from the entire fraternal household at that moment. If even a partial connection was insufficient to undo the forbidden status, surely a complete exclusion from the household makes the permanence of the prohibition all the more obvious. *Lo kol sheken*—this is an *a fortiori* case that requires no independent teaching.

### Order of Teaching

The *gemara* explains why this *mishnah* was taught nonetheless: *tana hai tana be'reisha*—this case was actually taught first, before the more complex three-brother case. At the time it was initially taught, the *tana* thought the three-brother case might be *mutar*, because Rochel was not completely out of the family circle—Levi had married her and she remained relevant to that household. The *tana* therefore initially contemplated that her status might change once Levi married her. Only afterward did the *tana* reconsider and conclude that even in that case she remains forbidden to Shimon: once she was *asura* to him at that initial moment, she remains *asura*.

Once the three-brother case was decided, the present *mishnah* became superfluous—it is now an *a fortiori* case that teaches nothing new. But since the *mishnah* had already been taught and fixed in the tradition as an important ruling in its own right, it was not removed: *u-mishna lo zaza mi-mekoma*. The *mishnah* remains in place, even though its independent necessity has been superseded by the more complex case that was ultimately resolved.
