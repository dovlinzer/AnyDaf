# Berakhot 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wine Exemptions

### Wine Quantity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: פַּת פּוֹטֶרֶת כׇּל מִינֵי מַאֲכָל, וְיַיִן פּוֹטֵר כׇּל מִינֵי מַשְׁקִים.
>
> **Translation:** and **disagree with** the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥiyya, as Rabbi Ḥiyya said: Bread exempts all the types of food** that one eats after it, **and wine exempts all types of drinks** that one drinks after it.

The daf begins with a review of several key *halachos* concerning *brachos*. The first concerns wine: a *bracha* on wine exempts *she'ar minei mashkim* — other liquids. This is an important *halacha* that many people are unaware of. The *Mishna Berurah* surveys a range of opinions on the matter, from those who require that one drink a very large quantity of wine — almost *koveiah seudah* on the wine — to those who hold that even the smallest amount suffices. His middle position is that one must drink at least a *mlo lugmav*, a majority of the *revi'is*, for *borei pri hagafen* to exempt other *mashkim*. He rejects the view that merely answering *amen* to a *kiddush* could exempt one, though such an opinion does exist.

## Machmat Haseudah

### Rashi vs Tosafot

The next major question is what is covered when one recites *hamotzi* at a meal. Rashi holds that the *bracha rishona* covers things that are fundamentally there to serve as an accompaniment to the bread — *lefapes es hapas*. According to Rashi, something that is a staple of the meal in its own right but not meant to accompany bread, such as oatmeal, would require a separate *bracha rishona*. Tosafot, and the normative ruling, hold instead that the framework is the meal itself: anything that is *machmat haseudah* is covered, while things that are not *machmat haseudah* require a separate *bracha rishona*.

### Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הִלְכְתָא דְּבָרִים הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת הַסְּעוּדָה בְּתוֹךְ הַסְּעוּדָה — אֵין טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לֹא לִפְנֵיהֶם וְלֹא לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם. וְשֶׁלֹּא מֵחֲמַת הַסְּעוּדָה בְּתוֹךְ הַסְּעוּדָה — טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לִפְנֵיהֶם וְאֵין טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם. לְאַחַר הַסְּעוּדָה — טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה בֵּין לִפְנֵיהֶם בֵּין לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** that **the *halakha*** is: Food **items that come due to the meal,** which are eaten together with the bread as part of the meal, **during the meal, neither require a blessing before** eating **them nor after** eating **them,** as they are considered secondary to the bread. **And** food items like fruit, **that do not come due to the meal,** as part of the meal, but may be brought **during the meal, require a blessing before** eating **them and do not require a blessing after** eating **them.** If they come **after the meal, they require a blessing both before** eating **them and after** eating **them.**

What does *machmat haseudah* mean? The general explanation is that it refers to foods that are present primarily for filling rather than purely for taste. Things present solely for taste — such as certain appetizers or desserts — are not *machmat haseudah* and raise questions about whether a separate *bracha* is needed. Although some argue that dessert today is so standard as to be considered *machmat haseudah*, the general consensus follows the more restricted view. A distinct third category is food consumed after the meal has ended: such food requires not only a *bracha rishona* but, according to the Gemara, even a *bracha achrona*, even if *birchas hamazon* has not yet been recited. Tosafot, however, holds that this post-meal phenomenon no longer applies in practice, since dessert today is always considered connected to the meal. One therefore will not encounter a case of eating something during a meal that requires its own *bracha achrona*, though a *bracha rishona* may still be required when the food is not sufficiently connected to the meal.

### Sfek Brachot

One might ask: given all these disputes, why not simply apply the principle of *safek brachos l'hakeil* and be done with it? Indeed, that is the general approach of the *poskim* — but there is a limit. One must first determine what qualifies as a genuine doubt before applying the principle. Moreover, there is a competing consideration running throughout Tosafot, drawn from the Maharim MiRothenburg: one can avoid the dilemma by eating the questionable food after *hamotzi*, thereby covering oneself. This strategy reflects the flip side of *safek brachos l'hakeil*, namely the principle that *assur l'hanos min ha'olam hazeh belo bracha* — it is forbidden to derive benefit from this world without a *bracha*. It is not merely a question of whether one has fulfilled a *mitzvah* of making a *bracha*, but whether one is entitled to derive *hanah* at all without having first recited one. That is why, although the general stance of the *poskim* is *safek brachos l'hakeil*, there is also this parallel strand encouraging one to find a way to cover oneself rather than risk deriving benefit improperly.

## Ben Zoma

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת בֶּן זוֹמָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ דְּבָרִים הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת הַסְּעוּדָה בְּתוֹךְ הַסְּעוּדָה אֵינָם טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לֹא לִפְנֵיהֶם וְלֹא לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם? אָמַר לָהֶם: הוֹאִיל וּפַת פּוֹטַרְתָּן. אִי הָכִי, יַיִן נָמֵי נִפְטְרֵיהּ פַּת! שָׁאנֵי יַיִן
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that the students **asked Ben Zoma: Why did** the Sages **say** that food **items that come due to the meal during the meal, neither require a blessing before** eating **them nor after** eating **them? He said to them: Because bread exempts them.** They asked: **If so, bread should also exempt wine.** Yet, one recites a blessing over wine during the meal. The Gemara responds: **Wine is different,**

The Gemara now picks up five lines from the bottom of 41b, with the passage *sha'alu es Ben Zoma*. The questioners ask Ben Zoma: *mipnei mah amru devarim habaim machmat haseudah betoch haseudah einam te'unim bracha lo lifnehem velo achareihem* — why did the Sages rule that foods that come as part of the meal require no *bracha* either before or after? He replied: *ho'il upas potartan* — because the bread exempts them. The Gemara then presses: if the bread exempts everything in the meal, then *yayin nami tipater bepas* — let the wine during the meal also be exempt through *hamotzi*. This takes for granted — as the next *Mishna* will make explicit — that one does indeed recite *borei pri hagafen* on wine consumed during a bread meal. This is another *halacha* that many are unaware of: even at a weekday meal with bread, if wine is brought to the table, one must recite *borei pri hagafen*. Wine is not covered by *hamotzi*.

## Wine Exemptions…

### Wine Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּגוֹרֵם בְּרָכָה לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **as it causes a blessing itself.** It is so significant, that one recites a blessing over it together with other blessings like *kiddush* and *havdala*, even though he does not particularly want to drink it. During a meal too, a blessing is recited over the wine and it is not exempted by the blessing over the bread.

The Gemara answers: *shani yayin d'goreim bracha l'atzmo* — wine is different, for it causes a *bracha* unto itself. This sounds somewhat circular, but the point is clarified by the *Rishonim*.

## Ben Zoma…

### Wine's Ritual Status

Rashi explains that *borei pri hagafen* is a *bracha* that carries a status beyond ordinary *birchas hanehenin*. We recite it at *kiddush*, at *havdalah* — it functions as a ritual *bracha* and not only as an expression of enjoyment. Therefore, even when wine appears in the middle of a meal, we maintain the elevated status of *borei pri hagafen* as a separate *bracha*.

Tosafot offers a related but somewhat different framing. Both bread and wine, says Tosafot, are singular in that they each have their own dedicated *bracha* not because they represent a class of foods, but because of the intrinsic importance of that particular food itself. Technically, *borei pri hagafen* should have been *borei pri ha'eitz*, and *hamotzi* should have been *borei minei mezonos* — but because of the special status of bread, it receives the elevated *bracha* of *hamotzi*, and because of the special status of wine, it receives the elevated *bracha* of *borei pri hagafen*. Wine and bread are the only two foods for which an entire, distinct *bracha* was instituted for that singular food type alone. Because wine carries that weight and significance, even when it appears in the middle of the meal, we do not allow it to be overshadowed. This holds even when one knew in advance that wine would be served — the planning does not subsume the *bracha*.

## Pas Ha-Ba'ah

### Rav Huna Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אֲכַל תְּלֵיסַר רִיפְתֵי בְּנֵי תְּלָתָא תְּלָתָא בְּקַבָּא וְלָא בָּרֵיךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: עָדֵי כַּפְנָא! אֶלָּא כֹּל שֶׁאֲחֵרִים קוֹבְעִים עָלָיו סְעוּדָה, צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** As the Gemara mentioned bread that comes as dessert, it now relates that **Rav Huna ate thirteen** substantially sized, sweetened **loaves, three** loaves **per *kav*** of flour, **and he did not recite** Grace after Meals because they were not genuine bread. **Rav Naḥman said to him: That is hunger.** One does not typically eat that much merely as dessert. **Rather, over anything which** is substantial enough to satiate and **others base a meal** upon it, **one must recite** Grace after Meals.

The Gemara now presents the following story. Rav Huna ate thirteen certain types of cakes or wafers — *pas haba b'kisnin* — of which every three cakes equaled a *kav*. These were substantial portions. *V'lo beirech* — and he did not recite *birchas hamazon* afterwards. Rav Nachman challenged him: *adai kafna* — these were eaten out of genuine hunger, or rhetorically: is this not food for hunger? You ate thirteen of these and filled yourself — how could you not bench? The Gemara then gives the *halacha*: *kol she'acherim kove'im alav seudah tzarich l'vareich* — if this is an amount upon which others would normally establish a meal, then even though it falls into the *mezonos* category of *pas haba b'kisnin*, if one is *koveia seudah* upon it, one must recite *birchas hamazon*. The *rav lo beirech* of the story thus refers to the omission of *birchas hamazon*, not to any *bracha* altogether.

### Keviat Seudah Rule

This leads to a broader discussion of *pas haba b'kisnin*: it is normally *borei minei mezonos* and *al hamichya*, but when the quantity consumed constitutes normal *kvias seudah*, or when the food itself is regularly used as a proper meal, it reverts to *hamotzi* and *birchas hamazon*.

### Three Categories

What exactly is *pas haba b'kisnin*? Among the *Rishonim* there are essentially three main positions, each corresponding to a type of food that has the technical ingredients of bread — flour, water, baked in an oven — yet does not function socially or culinarily as bread, and therefore receives *mezonos* status. It may, however, revert to *hamotzi* status under certain conditions.

The first approach, associated with Rashi and broadly the Rambam, is that the dough is sweetened with spices, eggs, or fruit juices, such that it tastes fundamentally different from bread dough. The paradigm case is cake; a croissant would be another example — highly enriched, very sweet, the additives alter the character of the dough itself.

The second approach derives the word *kisnin* from *kis*, a pocket, and understands *pas haba b'kisnin* as a filled dough — some type of pie, *borekas*, *knish*, or similar stuffed baked good. It is dough, but it does not function as bread.

The third approach, associated with Rabbeinu Chananel and others, identifies *pas haba b'kisnin* as a hard, thin, crunchy wafer — crackers, pretzels, breadsticks, and the like. Again, these have the ingredients of bread but do not function as bread in normal social consumption.

The normative practice tends to be lenient and applies *borei minei mezonos* and *al hamichya* to all three categories. However, the question remains: at what point does even a food that falls into one of these categories revert to *hamotzi* and *birchas hamazon*? The answer is: either when the quantity is such that normal people would establish a meal upon it — *kvias seudah* — or when the particular food is used in practice as a meal itself.

### Modern Examples

Several contemporary examples illuminate how these principles apply. The case of *mezonos* rolls is instructive. What makes a roll a so-called *mezonos* roll is the substitution of eggs or fruit juice for water in the dough. But if one has eaten those airline *mezonos* rolls, they taste entirely like a normal roll. For those authorities who hold that *pas haba b'kisnin* requires a genuinely different-tasting dough — not merely a technical substitution — such rolls are highly questionable. Furthermore, they are typically eaten in the same context and function as bread at a meal, which means that *lachen koveim alav seudah*, and the *bracha* would revert to *hamotzi* in any case. *Mezonos* rolls are therefore considered very problematic.

The case of *challah* with raisins is different: the dough itself is not affected by the raisins, which are merely an addition. There may be a question about where to draw the line — at what point does a dough taste sufficiently spiced or enriched to change its character — but the addition of raisins alone does not cross that threshold.

Pizza presents a more complex case. Some argue it is *pas haba b'kisnin* because the dough is covered with and accompanied by other primary ingredients, suggesting that the crust functions more as a base than as bread. But others argue that pizza is simply a meal — not a snack, not a treat — and that most people sit down to pizza as they would to any other meal. If that is the case, then either the social use of pizza takes it out of *pas haba b'kisnin* entirely, or at minimum the quantity is such that *koveim alav seudah* and *hamotzi* applies. Pizza as always requiring *hamotzi* is a defensible position.

Breadsticks raise a parallel question. If they function like pretzels — as a snack — they may well be *mezonos*. But if they are placed in a bread basket and function as bread at the table, a different analysis may apply.

Meat pies and *knishes* complicate things further. If one were to sit down to a meal of shepherd's pie as the central dish, the filling is the meal and the dough is secondary — which might suggest the dough itself is not serving as bread, but the food overall may be functioning as a meal in a way that affects the *bracha*. These lines are difficult to draw.

The general principle is this: *pas haba b'kisnin* foods technically contain the ingredients of bread, but because by convention they function in a non-bread-like way — as snacks, treats, or accompaniments — they are *mezonos* and *al hamichya*. When convention itself changes, or when the quantity reaches *kvias seudah*, the *bracha* reverts to *hamotzi* and *birchas hamazon*.

### Wedding Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה הֲוָה עָסֵיק לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ בֵּי רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר חֲבִיבָא. אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמַּיְיהוּ פַּת הַבָּאָה בְּכִסָנִין. כִּי אֲתָא, שַׁמְעִינְהוּ דְּקָא מְבָרְכִי ״הַמּוֹצִיא״. אָמַר לְהוּ: מַאי ״צִיצִי״ דְּקָא שָׁמַעְנָא? דִּילְמָא ״הַמּוֹצִיא לֶחֶם מִן הָאָרֶץ״ קָא מְבָרְכִיתוּ? אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אִין. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי מוּנָא אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: פַּת הַבָּאָה בְּכִסָנִין מְבָרְכִין עָלֶיהָ ״הַמּוֹצִיא״, וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara also relates: **Rav Yehuda was engaged** in preparations for **his son's** wedding **at the house of Rav Yehuda bar Ḥaviva when they brought bread that comes as dessert before them. When it arrived, he heard them reciting: Who brings forth** bread from the earth. **He said to them: What is this *tzitzi*** sound **that I hear? Perhaps you are reciting: Who brings forth bread from the earth? They said to him: Yes,** indeed, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Mona said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda: Over bread that comes as dessert, one recites: Who brings forth** bread from the earth. **And Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Mona.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהוּ: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מוּנָא אִתְּמַר. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: וְהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל לַחְמָנִיּוֹת מְעָרְבִין בָּהֶן, וּמְבָרְכִין עֲלֵיהֶן ״הַמּוֹצִיא״? שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּקָבַע סְעוֹדְתֵּיהּ עֲלַיְיהוּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא קָבַע סְעוֹדְתֵּיהּ עֲלַיְיהוּ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda **said to them:** You are mistaken. Actually, **it was stated** that Shmuel said: **The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Mona. They said to him: But aren't** you, **Master, the one who said in the name** of **Shmuel** with regard to **wafers: One may establish an *eiruv*,** the joining of courtyards, to permit carrying in a shared courtyard and the joining of cooked foods, to permit cooking on a festival for Shabbat, **with them and recite over them: Who brings forth** bread from the earth. Why is that the blessing over those wafers? They too are sweetened bread that comes as dessert. He answered them: **It is different there as he based his meal upon them, but here, where one did not base his meal upon them, no,** he does not recite: Who brings forth bread from the earth.

The Gemara now relates the story of Rav Yehuda. Rav Yehuda was arranging his son's marriage into the household of Rav Yehuda bar Chaviva — some type of engagement party or wedding celebration. *Ayitu lekamaeihu pas haba b'kisnin* — they brought before the guests a type of *pas haba b'kisnin*, whatever it was — some cake, wafer, or pie. When Rav Yehuda entered and heard that everyone was reciting *hamotzi*, he asked with some exasperation: *mai tzi tzi dekashmana* — what is all this *hamotzi hamotzi* I am hearing? *Dilma hamotzi lechem min ha'aretz kamvarchitu* — are you actually reciting *hamotzi lechem min ha'aretz*?

They replied: yes! For *detanya Rav Mona amar mishum Rav Yehuda pas haba b'kisnin mevorchin alav hamotzi* — Rav Mona taught in the name of Rav Yehuda that even on *pas haba b'kisnin* one recites *hamotzi*. And moreover, *veamar Shmuel halacha keRav Mona* — Shmuel ruled in accordance with Rav Mona.

Rav Yehuda responded: what Shmuel said was *ein halacha keRav Mona itmar* — that we do not rule like Rav Mona. They had heard his statement correctly but inverted its import — Shmuel said something about Rav Mona, but what he said was that the *halacha* does not follow him.

The guests then pressed Rav Yehuda: but you yourself have said in the name of Shmuel that *lachmaniyos* — some type of sweetened rolls or enriched baked item in the *pas haba b'kisnin* category — may be used for an *eruv* and over them one recites *hamotzi*. Surely you yourself hold they are bread?

Rav Yehuda replied: *shani hasam d'kava sudasa aleihu* — that case is different, because those are used for *kvias seudah*. The point is not merely that a food technically satisfies the definition of *pas haba b'kisnin* while simultaneously being used as a regular meal food. Once such a food is actually used as bread — once people are *koveia seudah* upon it — it is no longer functioning as *pas haba b'kisnin* and reverts to *hamotzi*. Here at the engagement party, by contrast, these were hors d'oeuvres, light fare — *lo kava sudasa aleihu* — nobody was establishing a meal upon them, and so they remain *mezonos*.

### Rav Pappa Visit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן. בָּתַר דְּגָמְרִי סְעוֹדְתַּיְיהוּ אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמַּיְיהוּ מִידֵּי לְמֵיכַל שָׁקֵל רַב פָּפָּא וְקָא אָכֵיל. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר גָּמַר אָסוּר מִלֶּאֱכוֹל? אָמַר לְהוּ: ״סִלֵּק״ אִתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappa happened** to come **to the house of Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan. After they finished their meal, they brought before them something to eat. Rav Pappa took** this food item **and ate** it without reciting a blessing. **They said to him:** Do you, **Master, not hold** that once **one finished** his meal **he is forbidden to eat** again without reciting a blessing? **He said to them** that in the correct version of that *halakha*, **it is stated: Removed.** One need recite a second blessing only when eating after the table was removed from before him.

The Gemara continues with a parallel story. Rav Pappa visited the house of Rav Huna the son of Rav Noson. *Basar d'gamru sudasaihu* — after they finished their meal, *aysu kameihu midi l'meichal* — they brought before them something to eat, a type of dessert. Rav Pappa took it and ate. The members of the household challenged him: does the master not hold that *gamar assur l'meichal* — once the meal is complete, one may not eat? The *Rishonim* clarify that this does not mean it is forbidden to eat entirely — one may eat, but only after reciting a *bracha*. Once the meal has been designated as complete, any subsequent food is no longer considered part of the meal and requires at least a *bracha rishona*, and possibly even a *bracha achrona* according to the Gemara. The challenge to Rav Pappa thus assumes he ate without reciting a *bracha*.

Rav Pappa replied: *lo salik itmar* — the *halacha* does not say when you finish eating, it says when you perform *salik* — when you have removed the table. In those times, each diner had a personal small table beside him. Even if one declared oneself full and unable to eat another bite, and even if food was subsequently brought out, the meal is not halachically concluded until the table has literally been removed. Until that moment, a new *bracha* is not required.

## End of Meal

### Table Removal

This raises the broader question of when the meal is considered to have ended in a way that requires a new *bracha* for anything subsequently consumed. The episode with Rav Pappa establishes one position: the threshold is *siluk hashulchan* — the physical removal of the personal table.

### Dependent on Host

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא וְרַבִּי זֵירָא אִיקְּלַעוּ לְבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא. לְבָתַר דְּסַלִּיקוּ תַּכָּא מִקַּמַּיְיהוּ שַׁדַּרוּ לְהוּ רִיסְתָּנָא מִבֵּי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא. רָבָא אָכֵיל וְרַבִּי זֵירָא לָא אָכֵיל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר סִלֵּק אָסוּר מִלֶּאֱכוֹל? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנַן אַתַּכָּא דְּרֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא סָמְכִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the Gemara relates: **Rava and Rabbi Zeira happened** to come **to the house of the Exilarch. After** the meal, when **they removed the table from before them, a portion [*ristena*]** of food **was sent to them from the house of the Exilarch. Rava ate** it **and Rabbi Zeira did not eat** it. Rabbi Zeira **said to** Rava: Do you, **Master, not hold** that once the table was **removed, he is forbidden to eat?** Rava **said to him: We are dependent upon the table of the Exilarch,** and so long as he has not completed his meal, his guests have not completed their meals either.

The Gemara then presents a further story. Rava and Rav Zeira were at the house of the *Reish Galuta*, the political head of Jewry in exile. *U-vatar de-saliku taka* — after their own dishes had been removed and they considered themselves finished, the *Reish Galuta* sent them a special portion from his table. Rava ate it; Rav Zeira did not. Rava asked Rav Zeira: do you not hold that once the table is removed one may not eat — implying Rav Zeira was being stringent? Rav Zeira responded: *anan a-takka de-Reish Galuta samchinan* — we are dependent on the table of the *Reish Galuta*. The meal is not over until he has finished.

### Da'at Ba'al Bayit

This is a significant *halacha* with implications well beyond the specific narrative. The *Rishonim* derive from here a general principle: a guest at someone's home makes a *bracha al da'at ba'al ha-bayit* — on the basis of the host's plans. Since the guest does not know what will be served and when, every *bracha* the guest recites is implicitly calibrated to whatever the host has planned to bring. Tosafot makes this explicit, ruling that when a host brings out fruits one after another, one need not recite a *bracha* on each successive item, because *somchim al shulchan ba'al ha-bayit* — one relies on the table of the host. This is the same principle operative in the *Reish Galuta* story.

The practical limit is that this applies when one is genuinely in the position of being fed by another. When one is one's own host — foraging in one's own kitchen, making individual decisions about what to eat — one cannot invoke *da'at ba'al ha-bayit*. In that case the *bracha* covers only what one had in mind at the time of recitation, not future unspecified decisions. One could in principle recite a *bracha* with an open-ended intent covering a range of foods — but whether a fully generic intent, covering anything one might subsequently decide to take from the kitchen, would suffice is a separate and more complex question.

### Three Immediates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּכׇל הָנֵי שְׁמַעְתָּתָא, אֶלָּא כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: שָׁלֹשׁ תְּכִיפוֹת הֵן: תֵּכֶף לִסְמִיכָה שְׁחִיטָה, תֵּכֶף לִגְאוּלָּה תְּפִלָּה, תֵּכֶף לִנְטִילַת יָדַיִם בְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** the Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is not in accordance with all of these statements** and the end of the meal is not determined by those factors. **Rather, it is** determined by **that which Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: There are three** pairs that **immediately** follow each other: **Immediately** following **placing hands** on the head of a sacrifice, is its **slaughter; immediately** following the blessing of **redemption** recited after *Shema*, is the *Amida* **prayer;** and **immediately** following the ritual **washing of the hands** after a meal, is **the blessing of** Grace after Meals.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אַף אָנוּ נֹאמַר: תֵּכֶף לְתַלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים בְּרָכָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְבָרְכֵנִי ה׳ בִּגְלָלֶךָ״. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְבָרֶךְ ה׳ אֶת בֵּית הַמִּצְרִי בִּגְלַל יוֹסֵף״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that on a similar note, **we too will say: Immediately** following the entrance of **Torah scholars** into a house, a **blessing** rests upon that house, **as it is stated** with regard to Laban and Jacob: **"The Lord has blessed me because of you"** (Genesis 30:27). **If you wish, say** instead, that the proof is **from here, as it is stated:** "And it was from when he placed him in charge of his house and over all that he owned, **the Lord blessed the house of the Egyptian on account of Joseph"** (Genesis 39:5).

The Gemara then moves to a different formulation of what defines the end of the meal. It cites Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi in the name of Rav: *shalosh techofot hen* — there are three actions that must follow immediately upon a prior action. *Tachef lismicha shechita* — immediately after *semicha* one slaughters the animal. *Tachef ligeula tefila* — immediately after the blessing of *Gaal Yisrael* one begins *tefila*. And *tachef lintilat yadayim bracha* — immediately after *netilat yadayim* one recites the *bracha*. These three are grouped together because the *Yerushalmi* derives all three from *pesukim*.

The *netilat yadayim* in question, in this context, is understood as referring to *mayim acharonim* at the end of the meal — the handwashing that signals the conclusion of the meal, after which *birchas hamazon* must follow immediately. This is the ruling the Gemara adopts as normative: *v'leit hilchata kechol hani shema'teta* — we do not rule like any of the other criteria that were proposed (finishing eating, removing the table, the oil). Instead, the definitive marker of the meal's end is *mayim acharonim*, and *birchas hamazon* must follow immediately upon it.

The Gemara adds that Abaye proposes a fourth *tachef*: *tachef letalmidei chachamim bracha* — a blessing follows immediately in the wake of *talmidei chachamim*, as attested by the verse *vayivarcheni Hashem biglalecha* — God blessed me on your account, what Laban said to Yaakov. Or alternatively from the verse *vayevarech Hashem et beit hamitzri biglal Yosef* — God blessed the Egyptian household on account of Yosef. The cases of Yaakov and Yosef are being invoked as illustrations of the *talmid chacham* whose proximity brings blessing, though it is noted that *tzaddikim* and *talmidei chachamim* are perhaps different categories.

### Mayim Acharonim and Birc…

Tosafot raises a difficulty with the picture the Gemara presents: we have a practice to leave bread on the table before *bensching*, which seems to conflict with an expectation that the table would be removed. Tosafot resolves this by noting that the practice of removing personal trays applied in Talmudic times, whereas today we remove plates but maintain the bread on the table. This distinction also bears on the question of how Tosafot conceptualizes the status of the *afikoman* at the *Seder*: since *halacha* demands that the *afikoman* be eaten, it is as if one is *someich al da'at hahalacha*, and the meal cannot be considered truly concluded until the *afikoman* has been consumed — even if one has otherwise performed *siluk hashulchan*. A halachically mandated eating, by this logic, extends the parameters of the meal.

Regarding *mayim acharonim* itself, Tosafot and the *Shulchan Aruch*, reinforced by the *Mishna Berurah*, indicate that once *mayim acharonim* has been performed, one should proceed directly to *birchas hamazon* — one should not speak and should not eat additional food even with a *bracha*. The *Aruch HaShulchan* dissents, arguing that if one wishes to speak, eat more, or recite another *bracha*, one may do so and then perform *mayim acharonim* again immediately before *bensching*. The *Mishna Berurah* acknowledges this position. In practice, many who do perform *mayim acharonim* follow the *Aruch HaShulchan*.

The further question arises for those who do not practice *mayim acharonim* at all, which is the case in many communities today: what then constitutes the end of the meal? This may revert closer to the earlier Talmudic criteria — clearing the table, or some analogous act — though no definitive answer is offered here. What is clear is that the functional concern remains: once one has signaled through words and action that the meal is over, any subsequent eating is not considered part of the meal and requires a new *bracha*.

## Mishna Coverage

### Before/After Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּרַךְ עַל הַיַּיִן שֶׁלִּפְנֵי הַמָּזוֹן — פָּטַר אֶת הַיַּיִן שֶׁלְּאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן. בֵּרַךְ עַל הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת שֶׁלִּפְנֵי הַמָּזוֹן — פָּטַר אֶת הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת שֶׁלְּאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן. בֵּרַךְ עַל הַפַּת — פָּטַר אֶת הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת. עַל הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת — לֹא פָּטַר אֶת הַפַּת. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אַף לֹא מַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna explains those cases and those circumstances in which blessings recited over particular foods exempt other foods at the meal from the requirement to recite a blessing over them. **One who recited a blessing over the wine that** one drank **before the meal,** with that blessing **he exempted the wine that** he drinks **after the meal.** Similarly, **one who recited a blessing over the appetizers that** one ate **before the meal,** with that blessing **he exempted the appetizers that** he eats **after the meal. One who recited a blessing over the bread exempted the appetizers,** as they are considered secondary to the bread. However, one who recited a blessing **over the appetizers did not exempt the bread. Beit Shammai say:** The blessing recited over the appetizers **did not exempt even a cooked dish** that he eats during the meal.

The *Mishna* now sets out a series of rules about which *bracha* covers which. *Beirach al hayayin shelifnei hamazon patar es hayayin she'achar hamazon* — if one recited a *bracha* on the wine served before the meal, that *bracha* exempts the wine served after the meal, even though the meal will have intervened and the post-meal wine constitutes a distinct drinking occasion. Similarly, *beirach al haparperes shelifnei hamazon patar es haparperes she'achar hamazon* — if one recited a *bracha* on the pre-meal snack (presumably a *mezonos* item), that covers the post-meal snack as well, even though wine and these snacks are not covered by *hamotzi* and even though the meal falls between them. The *bracha* before can extend to the corresponding item after, as the two remain loosely connected as part of the same occasion.

### Bread vs Snacks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּרַךְ עַל הַפַּת, פָּטַר אֶת הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת. עַל הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת, לֹא פָּטַר אֶת הַפַּת. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אַף לֹא מַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **One who recited a blessing over the bread exempted the appetizers,** as they are considered secondary to the bread. However, one who recited a blessing **over the appetizers did not exempt the bread. Beit Shammai say:** The blessing recited over the appetizers **did not exempt even a cooked dish** that he eats during the meal.

The *Mishna* then states: *beirach al hapas patar es haparperes* — reciting *hamotzi* on bread exempts the snacks, presumably even those consumed after the meal. This is interesting, since one might have thought that post-meal items are outside the scope of *hamotzi*. Tosafot addresses this by suggesting that the *parperes* referred to in this clause are not truly *mezonos* but rather something closer to bread chips — they are genuinely a form of bread, and therefore even when eaten after the meal they remain within the scope of *hamotzi*. However, *al haparperes lo patar es hapas* — reciting a *bracha* on the snack does not exempt the bread. Even if, as Tosafot holds, both items are technically in the *hamotzi* category, the snack is not of sufficient significance to exempt real bread.

### Beit Shammai

*Beis Shammai omrim af lo ma'aseh kedeirah* — Beit Shammai adds that even cooked foods, such as porridge or stew, are not covered. The Gemara will question precisely how to read this statement of Beit Shammai, as we will see shortly.

## Sitting Together

### Sitting Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין — כׇּל אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְעַצְמוֹ. הֵסַבּוּ — אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** An additional *halakha* is cited: If several people **were sitting** to eat not in the framework of a joint meal, **each recites a blessing for himself. If they were reclined** on divans to eat, which renders it a joint meal, **one recites a blessing on behalf of them all.**

The *Mishna* continues: *hayu yoshvin kol echad mevarech le'atzmo* — when they were sitting, each person recites their own *bracha*. *Heseivu echad mevarech lekulan* — but if they reclined together, establishing themselves as a *kvias seudah*, one person recites for everyone.

This raises a conceptual question. Normally, we do not recite *birchas hanehenin* on behalf of another, since *birchas hanehenin* is about one's personal, subjective experience of benefit — it must be the individual's own acknowledgment and thanks, not a piggybacking onto someone else's *bracha*. We permit one person to be *yotzei* through another's *bracha* only when they are defined as a group, so that the one reciting is expressing the *bracha* of the collective, not merely of themselves. This is the logic of *zimun* at the end of a meal — one person benches on behalf of a group because they constitute a unified entity. The same logic applies at the start: when all have reclined together and established themselves as a group engaged in a shared *kvias seudah*, they are a collective, and one person's *bracha* expresses the *bracha* of all.

What constitutes *heseivu* today, when *haseibah* is no longer our practice? One view is that without *haseibah* there can be no such grouping. Another view holds that *haseibah* is not the operative criterion for us; what matters is *kvias seudah* together. Tosafot, however, maintains that one cannot have *kvias seudah* without bread — that bread remains a necessary ingredient for the collective to function as a group in this sense.

### Wine Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן בְּתוֹךְ הַמָּזוֹן — כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְעַצְמוֹ. אַחַר הַמָּזוֹן — אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְכוּלָּם. וְהוּא אוֹמֵר עַל הַמּוּגְמָר, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מְבִיאִין אֶת הַמּוּגְמָר אֶלָּא לְאַחַר סְעוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally: If **wine came before them during the meal, each and every** diner **recites a blessing** over the wine **for himself.** If the wine came **after the meal, one recites a blessing on behalf of them all. And he,** who recited the blessing over the wine, also **says** the blessing **over the incense** [***mugmar***], **although they only bring the incense** to the diners **after the meal.**

The *Mishna* then distinguishes between wine drunk during the meal and wine drunk after. *Ba lahem yayin betoch hamazon kol echad ve'echad mevarech le'atzmo* — when wine comes during the meal, each person recites their own *bracha*, since drinking during the meal is understood as an individual act. Each person drinks because they are thirsty, or not — the drinking during a meal is personal and not a group activity. *Aval le'achar hamazon echad mevarech lekulan* — but after the meal, when everyone brings out wine to share together, the drinking is now a group occasion, and one person may recite on behalf of all. The person given the honor of reciting *borei pri hagafen* on the post-meal wine also recites the *bracha* on the *mugmar* — the incense burned after the meal for fragrance — even though that comes after everything, and possibly even after *birchas hamazon*.

## Wine Customs

### Shabbat/Yom Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים, הוֹאִיל וְאָדָם קוֹבֵעַ סְעוּדָּתוֹ עַל הַיַּיִן. אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, מְבָרֵךְ עַל כׇּל כּוֹס וָכוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's statement that wine that precedes a meal exempts wine that follows a meal, **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: This** *halakha* **was only taught with regard to Shabbat and Festivals, since,** because one can continue drinking at his leisure, **one bases his meal on the wine. However, during the rest of the days of the year,** one who drinks wine at a meal **recites a blessing over each and every cup,** as his original intention was not to drink a lot.

The Gemara now qualifies the *Mishna*'s rule that wine before exempts wine after. *Amar Rabbah bar bar Channah amar Rabbi Yochanan: lo shanu ela beshabbatot uveyamim tovim* — this applies only on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*. Why? *Ho'il ve'adam kove'a se'udato al hayayin* — because on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*, the custom is to sit and drink wine together after the meal. Knowing this in advance, the *bracha* on the pre-meal wine extends to cover the post-meal wine as well.

### Weekday Customs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר נָמֵי: אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר מָרִי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים, וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁאָדָם יוֹצֵא מִבֵּית הַמֶּרְחָץ, וּבִשְׁעַת הַקָּזַת דָּם, הוֹאִיל וְאָדָם קוֹבֵעַ סְעוּדָּתוֹ עַל הַיַּיִן. אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה — מְבָרֵךְ עַל כׇּל כּוֹס וָכוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated: Rabba bar Mari said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: This was only taught** in the mishna **with regard to Shabbat and Festivals, and when a person emerges** tired **from the bathhouse,** and wishes to eat and rest, **and during bloodletting,** after which one tends to drink a lot, **since** on these occasions **one** typically **bases his meal on wine. However, during the rest of the days of the year,** one who drinks wine at a meal **recites a blessing over each and every cup.**

*Aval bish'ar yemot hashanah mevarech al kol kos vekhos* — at other times of the year, however, one recites a *bracha* on each cup of wine separately. At a weekday meal, there is no established expectation of post-meal drinking, and so each decision to pour another cup represents a new drinking occasion requiring its own *bracha*. This ruling is attributed to Rabbi Yochanan and reflects conditions in *Eretz Yisrael*; it may also reflect a change from the time when the *Mishna* was composed, when the convention of post-meal drinking was more universal.

A parallel teaching is cited from Rabbah bar Mari in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who similarly limits the exempting power of the initial *bracha* to *Shabbos*, *Yom Tov*, and specific occasions of established drinking — such as after visiting a *shvitz* or after bloodletting — where the practice is to sit and drink a substantial quantity of wine. *Ho'il ve'adam kove'a se'udato al hayayin* — because in those contexts one is *koveia* upon wine. Outside those contexts, each cup requires its own *bracha*.

### Changing Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר מָרִי אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רָבָא בְּחוֹל. חַזְיֵיהּ דְּבָרֵיךְ לִפְנֵי הַמָּזוֹן, וַהֲדַר בָּרֵיךְ לְאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִישַׁר. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabba bar Mari happened** to come **to the house of Rava during the week. He saw him recite a blessing** over wine **before the meal, and again recite a blessing** on the wine **after the meal. He said to him: Well done. And so too, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said** that this was proper conduct.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי אַבָּיֵי בְּיוֹם טוֹב. חַזְיֵיהּ דְּבָרֵיךְ אַכֹּל כָּסָא וְכָסָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נִמְלָךְ אֲנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara also relates: **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Yosef happened** to come **to the house of Abaye on a Festival. He saw that he recited a blessing over each and every cup** of wine. Rabbi Yitzḥak **said to him:** Does the **Master not hold** in accordance with **that** *halakha* **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi,** who said that one blessing is sufficient? Abaye **said to him:** My original intention was not to base my meal upon wine and with each cup **I change my mind** and decide to drink it. Even Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi would agree that under those circumstances, one must recite a blessing over each and every cup.

The Gemara presents a case that tests the interaction between the default assumption and individual intent. Rabbah bar Mari visited Rava on a weekday. He recited a *bracha* on wine beforehand and then recited another *bracha* on wine after the meal. Rava said to him: *yeyasher* — well done. This confirms the ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that on a weekday the initial *bracha* does not cover the wine after.

Conversely, Rav Yitzchak visited Abaye on *Yom Tov* and recited a separate *bracha* on every cup. Abaye asked: does the master not hold like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, that on *Yom Tov* we are generally planning to drink and therefore one *borei pri hagafen* covers all? Rav Yitzchak replied: *nimlach ana* — I changed my mind. I initially planned to take it easy this *Yom Tov* and then decided to join in. Since my initial intent was not to drink extensively, the default assumption did not apply to me and each cup required its own *bracha*. The default assumptions about what is or is not planned on *Shabbos*, *Yom Tov*, or ordinary weekdays are precisely that — defaults. A specific context or specific intent can override them in either direction.

## During vs After

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן בְּתוֹךְ הַמָּזוֹן מַהוּ שֶׁיִּפְטוֹר אֶת הַיַּיִן שֶׁלְּאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן? אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר בֵּרַךְ עַל הַיַּיִן שֶׁלִּפְנֵי הַמָּזוֹן פּוֹטֵר אֶת הַיַּיִן שֶׁלְּאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן — מִשּׁוּם דְּזֶה לִשְׁתּוֹת וְזֶה לִשְׁתּוֹת. אֲבָל הָכָא — דְּזֶה לִשְׁתּוֹת, וְזֶה לִשְׁרוֹת — לָא. אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If **wine came out to them during the meal, what is** the *halakha* with regard to **exempting the wine after the meal** from a blessing? The dilemma is as follows: **If you say: One who recited a blessing over the wine that** one drank **before the meal,** with that blessing **he exempted the wine that** he drinks **after the meal,** perhaps that **is because** the purpose of drinking **this,** wine before the meal, **is to drink, and that,** wine after the meal, **is to drink** for its own sake. **However, here, where** the purpose of drinking **this,** the wine after the meal, **is to drink and that,** the wine during the meal, **is to moisten** the food and to facilitate its consumption, **no.** The blessing on one cannot exempt the other. **Or perhaps there is no difference,** and all drinking is considered the same.

The Gemara raises a further question. The *Mishna* established that wine during the meal and wine after the meal operate differently in terms of who recites the *bracha*. This raises the question: *ba'u lahem yayin betoch hamazon mah she-yiftar hayayin sheleachar hamazon* — can the *bracha* of *borei pri hagafen* recited on wine during the meal serve to exempt the wine drunk after the meal? The question is not simply about *da'as* — whether one had the later wine in mind — but may involve a categorical difference between the two types of drinking. Wine during the meal is consumed incidentally, to wash down food; wine after the meal is drunk for its own sake, as a drinking occasion. If these are qualitatively distinct categories, then no amount of advance intent may suffice to connect them.

### Amoraic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: פּוֹטֵר, וְרַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: אֵינוֹ פּוֹטֵר. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: פּוֹטֵר, וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵינוֹ פּוֹטֵר. רַב הוּנָא וְרַב יְהוּדָה וְכׇל תַּלְמִידֵי דְּרַב אָמְרִי: אֵינוֹ פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Opinions differed: **Rav said: It exempts, and Rav Kahana said: It does not exempt. Rav Naḥman said: It exempts, and Rav Sheshet said: It does not exempt. Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda and all the students of Rav said: It does not exempt.**

The *Amoraim* divide on the question. Rav holds that the wine during exempts the wine after. Rav Kahana holds it does not. Rav Nachman holds it does; Rav Sheshet holds it does not. And Rav Huna, Rav Yehuda, and all the disciples of Rav hold it does not. The students of Rav thus represent the dominant camp: wine during the meal and wine after the meal are fundamentally different types of drinking, and one *bracha* does not carry over to the other. This does seem to reflect a categorical distinction rather than a mere question of intent.

### Mishna Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן בְּתוֹךְ הַמָּזוֹן — כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְעַצְמוֹ. לְאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן — אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְכוּלָּם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם לֹא בָּא לָהֶם יַיִן בְּתוֹךְ הַמָּזוֹן אֶלָּא לְאַחַר הַמָּזוֹן, אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְכוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** Rava raised an objection to Rav Naḥman** from our mishna: If **wine came before them during the meal, each and every** diner **recites a blessing** over the wine **for himself.** If the wine came **after the meal, one recites a blessing on behalf of them all.** Apparently, even though they recited a blessing over wine during the course of the meal, they must recite a blessing over the wine after the meal as well. Rav Naḥman **said to him:** The mishna **says as follows:** There are two independent cases. The second case is: **If wine did not come before them during the meal, but only after the meal, one recites a blessing on behalf of them all.**

Rav challenges Rav Nachman from the *Mishna*: since the *Mishna* indicates that during the meal each person recites their own *bracha* on wine, while after the meal one person recites for all, it implies that a *bracha* was recited during and yet another is required after. This would prove that wine during does not exempt wine after. Rav Nachman responds that the *Mishna* should be read as presenting two separate scenarios: one in which wine is brought during the meal, and one in which wine is brought only after the meal. The clause stating *echad mevarech lekulan* applies to the second scenario — where no wine was drunk during the meal at all. If wine had been drunk during, the *bracha* on it would, according to Rav Nachman, cover the post-meal wine, and no further *bracha* would be needed.

### Separate Categories

The normative ruling follows those who hold that wine during and wine after are distinct categories. The *bracha* on wine consumed during the meal — which functions as a beverage accompanying food — does not carry over to wine consumed after the meal for its own sake. Partly this may be because bridging from during to after the meal requires that the two drinking occasions also be of the same type; where they are qualitatively different, the *bracha* cannot span them even if intent is present.

## Snack Coverage

### Asymmetry

The *Mishna*'s statement that *hamotzi* covers the *parperes* but not vice versa reflects a structural asymmetry. Bread is the foundation of the meal and is sufficiently significant to extend its *bracha* over lesser items. The *parperes*, even if it technically falls in the *hamotzi* category according to Tosafot, does not carry enough weight to exempt real bread. The two are qualitatively ordered.

### Beit Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּרַךְ עַל הַפַּת, פָּטַר אֶת הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת. עַל הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת, לֹא פָּטַר אֶת הַפַּת. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אַף לֹא מַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **One who recited a blessing over the bread exempted the appetizers,** as they are considered secondary to the bread. However, one who recited a blessing **over the appetizers did not exempt the bread. Beit Shammai say:** The blessing recited over the appetizers **did not exempt even a cooked dish** that he eats during the meal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אַרֵישָׁא פְּלִיגִי, אוֹ דִילְמָא אַסֵּיפָא פְּלִיגִי? דְּקָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא בֵּרַךְ עַל הַפַּת — פָּטַר אֶת הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן מַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה. וְאָתֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְמֵימַר לָא מִיבַּעְיָא פַּרְפֶּרֶת דְּלָא פָּטְרָה לְהוּ פַּת, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה נָמֵי לָא פָּטְרָה. אוֹ דִילְמָא אַסֵּיפָא פְּלִיגִי, דְּקָתָנֵי: בֵּרַךְ עַל הַפַּרְפֶּרֶת — לֹא פָּטַר אֶת הַפַּת. פַּת הוּא דְּלָא פָּטַר אֲבָל מַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה — פָּטַר. וְאָתוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְמֵימַר וַאֲפִילּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה נָמֵי לָא פָּטַר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this case, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Do **Beit Shammai disagree with the first clause** in the mishna **or with the latter clause,** as it may be explained in both ways? It can be understood that **the first *tanna* says: One who recited a blessing over the bread exempted the appetizers and all the more so** it exempted **a cooked dish. And Beit Shammai come to say: It goes without saying that** the blessing over **bread does not exempt appetizers; as** the blessing over bread **does not even exempt a cooked dish. Or perhaps they disagree with the latter clause, as it was taught: One who recited a blessing over the appetizers did not exempt the bread.** By inference, the blessing **did not exempt bread, but it did exempt a cooked dish. And Beit Shammai come to say** that the blessing over the appetizers **did not exempt even a cooked dish.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Let it stand,** as this dilemma remains unresolved.

The Gemara now turns to the disputed statement of Beit Shammai: *af lo ma'aseh kedeirah* — even cooked foods, such as porridge or stew. The question is whether Beit Shammai is disputing the first clause of the *Mishna* — that *hamotzi* covers the *parperes* — or the second clause — that *parperes* does not cover bread.

On the first reading, Beit Shammai holds that *hamotzi* does not cover the *parperes* and certainly does not cover *ma'aseh kedeirah* — porridge — which one might have thought would be covered as a meal staple. Beit Shammai would hold that *hamotzi* covers only foods that directly accompany the bread as *lefapes es hapas*, not anything simply eaten during the meal. On the second reading, Beit Shammai is arguing against the implied claim that *parperes* might at least cover *ma'aseh kedeirah* even if it cannot cover bread. Beit Shammai holds that *parperes* cannot even cover porridge — the two remain distinct even though both are presumably *mezonos*.

The Gemara concludes with *teiku* — the question is left unresolved. Practically, it does not matter, since we do not rule like Beit Shammai in any case.

## Keviat Seudah

### Sitting Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד כּוּ׳. הֵסַבּוּ — אִין, לֹא הֵסַבּוּ — לָא. וּרְמִינְהוּ: עֲשָׂרָה שֶׁהָיוּ הוֹלְכִים בַּדֶּרֶךְ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכּוּלָּם אוֹכְלִים מִכִּכָּר אֶחָד — כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְעַצְמוֹ. יָשְׁבוּ לֶאֱכוֹל, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד אוֹכֵל מִכִּכָּרוֹ — אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְכוּלָּם. קָתָנֵי ״יָשְׁבוּ״ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הֵסַבּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna distinguished between a case where several people **were sitting** to eat, which is not a joint meal, **and each and every** diner recites a blessing for himself; and a case where they were reclined on divans, which renders it a joint meal, and one recites a blessing on behalf of all of them. The Gemara infers: If **they reclined, yes,** it is considered a joint meal; if **they did not recline, no. And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction: Ten** people **who were walking on the road, even if they are all eating from one loaf, each and every one recites a blessing for himself.** If **they sat to eat, even if each and every one is eating from his** own **loaf, one recites a blessing on behalf of them all** as it is considered a joint meal. In any case, **it was taught:** If **they sat** to eat, **even though they did not recline.** Apparently, sitting together is enough to render it a joint meal and reclining is not required.

Returning to the *Mishna*'s rule of *hayu yoshvin kol echad mevarech le'atzmo / heseivu echad mevarech lekulan*, the Gemara formulates this as: *haseibu in lo haseibu lo* — it is specifically *haseibah* that creates the group; without it, even eating together does not suffice.

The Gemara then raises a difficulty from a *Tosefta*: ten people were walking along the road and all eating from the same loaf of bread; each must recite their own *bracha*. However, if they sat down to eat — even if each had their own separate bread — one person may recite for all. This seems to contradict the Gemara's ruling that *haseibah* specifically is required, since the *Tosefta* permits group recitation upon mere sitting, not formal reclining.

### Intent to Eat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: כְּגוֹן דְּאָמְרִי ״נֵיזִיל וְנֵיכוֹל לַחְמָא בְּדוּךְ פְּלָן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** With regard to those walking along the road, it was **in a case where they said: Let us go and eat in such-and-such a place.** Since they designated a specific location to eat together in advance, it is considered a joint meal.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves the difficulty: the *Tosefta*'s case of sitting together is one where they said *neizail v'neichol lachma biduch f'lan* — let us go and eat there. They planned to eat together. It is not that they merely happened to sit at the same table; they intentionally established a joint meal. This deliberate intent to eat as a group substitutes for *haseibah* and creates the *kvias seudah* necessary for one person to recite on behalf of all.

### Without Bread

Whether this grouping can occur without bread is debated. Tosafot holds that bread is a prerequisite — there is no concept of a *kevius* without *pas*. The *Rambam*, in his overview of *Hilchos Brachos*, uses language suggesting that *kvias seudah* might in principle apply to any food. The *Shulchan Aruch* rules, however, that in practice *zimun* and group recitation apply only with bread.

### Wine Zimun

The Gemara notes one additional case: if three people sit down and drink wine together — with deliberate intent to drink as a group — they may perform a *zimun* on the wine. However, this too is no longer practiced. In most communities, wine is not drunk in a manner of *kevius* — not as a regular, established group activity — and therefore the conditions for a *zimun* on wine are not met in ordinary circumstances.
