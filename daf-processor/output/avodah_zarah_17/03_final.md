# Avodah Zarah 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prohibited Buildings

### Three Basilica Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין בּוֹנִין כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שָׁלֹשׁ בָּסִילְקָאוֹת הֵן, שֶׁל מְלָכִים, וְשֶׁל מֶרְחֲצָאוֹת, וְשֶׁל אוֹצָרוֹת. אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁתַּיִם לְהֶיתֵּר וְאֶחָד לְאִיסּוּר, וְסִימָן: ״לֶאְסֹר מַלְכֵיהֶם בְּזִקִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **one may not build** a basilica in conjunction with gentiles. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: There are three** types of **basilicas:** Those **of kings, and** those **of bathhouses, and** those **of storehouses. Rava says: Two** of these types **are permitted,** as they are not used for inflicting the death penalty, **and one is prohibited [*le'isor*]. And a mnemonic** device for this ruling, that the basilica of kings is prohibited, is the verse: **"To bind [*le'esor*] their kings with chains"** (Psalms 149:8).

The *mishnah* that preceded our discussion addressed a range of prohibited transactions with the Roman government, including the sale of wild animals, which the *gemara* treated as analogous in spirit to the sale of weapons — not because non-Jews would consciously deploy the animals as instruments of violence, but because we are not permitted to keep dangerous wild animals ourselves and therefore should not be facilitating their ownership by others. The *mishnah* then turns to a related prohibition: one may not assist in building a *basilica*, understood as a platform or courthouse in which Roman judges sit; a *gardom*, a scaffold on which executions — including crucifixions of Jews — were carried out; or an *istadya*, a stadium for gladiatorial games. Helping construct these structures is seen as aiding and abetting the apparatus of Roman violence against the Jewish people.

On this point, Rabbah bar Bar Chana cites Rabbi Yochanan: there are three types of *basilica*. The first is a *basilica shel malchei ovdei kochavim* — a palace or castle of idolatrous kings. The second is one situated in the context of a *merchatza*, a bathhouse. The third is associated with a storage complex. Rava then rules: two of these are permitted to build and one is forbidden. The one that is forbidden is the *basilica* in which judgment takes place — the courthouse — which he connects to the phrase about binding kings in chains, using the double meaning of *leesore* (to bind / to forbid) as a *siman*. The other two involve no judicial function and are therefore permitted.

### Basilica Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: כּוּלָּם לְהֶיתֵּר. וְהָתְנַן: אֵין בּוֹנִין עִמָּהֶן בָּסִילְקֵי, גַּרְדּוֹם, אִיצְטַדְיָיא וּבִימָה! אֵימָא: שֶׁל גַּרְדּוֹם וְשֶׁל אִיצְטַדְיָיא וְשֶׁל בִּימָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that this is what **Rava says: All** these types of basilica are **permitted.** The Gemara asks: How can it be permitted to build any type of basilica; **but didn't we learn** in the mishna: **One may not build with them a basilica, a tribunal, a stadium, or a platform?** The Gemara answers: **Say** that the mishna means the following: One may not build in conjunction with gentiles a basilica **of a tribunal, or of a stadium, or of a platform.** But it is permitted to build a basilica that is not used for sentencing and inflicting the death penalty.

The *gemara* raises a challenge: the *mishnah* itself lists *basilki* alongside *gardom*, *istadya*, and *bima* as structures one may not help build. Is a *basilica* really a standalone prohibited category, or is it always understood as the *basilica* of a *gardom*, of an *istadya*, of a *bima*? The *gemara* resolves this by reading the *mishnah* as referring to a *basilica shel gardom* and a *basilica shel istadya* — a basilica of a scaffold, a basilica of a stadium — rather than treating *basilki* as an independent category prohibited in its own right. The prohibition attaches to the judicial or entertainment function embedded within the structure.

### Tosafot Altar Critique

Before turning to the narrative *aggadta* that follows, it is worth pausing on a *Tosafot* — the discussion of *bimosot* — that touches directly on the building prohibitions and reflects a striking exercise in religious common sense overriding technical classification.

*Rashi* explains *bimosot* as altars on which offerings were brought to *avodah zarah*, and holds that building such an altar is permissible because it is two degrees removed from idolatry itself: you do not place the idol on the altar, but the sacrifice, and the sacrifice in turn serves the idol. *Tosafot* is openly astounded by this. He notes first — at almost exactly the midpoint of *Tosafot bimosot* — the phrase *alma mamash tashmish avodat kochavim atzmo hu*: this is straightforwardly a direct accessory to *avodah zarah*. What greater *tashmish* of idolatrous worship could there be than an altar?

*Tosafot* then adds a second argument, one that appeals to basic religious sensibility rather than formal classification: *dinehi nami demishamesh demishamesh avodah zarah* — granted that technically, in some formalistic sense, one could call this two degrees removed (it serves the sacrifice, and the sacrifice serves the idol). But even conceding that technical point, *atu livnot imahem*? How could any *frum* Jew conceivably think it permissible to build an altar upon which incense would be offered to *avodah zarah*? Religious sensibility sometimes demands that we set aside a technical *halachic* argument. *Tosafot* therefore concludes that *Rashi*'s reading of the word should be corrected: the word is not *bimosot* (altars) but *dimosiyot* — a type of bathhouse. This aligns with the adjacent mention of *beit merchatza* (bathhouse), since the *mishnah* permits building a bathhouse even though bathhouses sometimes contained niches or apses housing idols. The operative point is that one avoids building the niche itself but may assist with the fundamentally utilitarian bathhouse structure. We are dealing with bathhouses, not with altars to *avodah zarah*.

## Rabbi Eliezer Trial

### Christian Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁנִּתְפַּס רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְמִינוּת, הֶעֱלֻהוּ לַגַּרְדּוֹם לִידּוֹן. אָמַר לוֹ אוֹתוֹ הֶגְמוֹן: זָקֵן שֶׁכְּמוֹתְךָ יַעֲסוֹק בִּדְבָרִים בְּטֵלִים הַלָּלוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the above discussion, the Gemara relates incidents involving Sages who were sentenced by the ruling authorities. **The Sages taught: When Rabbi Eliezer was arrested** and charged **with heresy** by the authorities, **they brought him up to a tribunal to be judged. A certain** judicial **officer [*hegemon*] said to him:** Why **should an elder like you engage in these frivolous matters** of heresy?

*Keshenitpas Rabbi Eliezer leminut* — when Rabbi Eliezer was seized by the Roman government on suspicion of heresy. This requires some historical context. *Minut*, as familiar from earlier discussions in this *masechet*, very often refers specifically to Jewish Christianity. The Romans persecuted not only Jews but early Christians as well, and with considerable ferocity. Christianity posed a particular political threat: it proclaimed a king, which was interpreted as a challenge to Roman sovereignty. The famous formulation "give unto Caesar what is Caesar's" arose precisely because Christians had to navigate whether rendering tribute to Caesar — who fashioned himself a god — constituted a form of *avodah zarah*. As a result, early Christians were frequently martyred by the Roman state, and identifying a fellow Christian in dangerous times required secret signals — the fish symbol, *ICTHYS*, being the known example.

The Roman government therefore suspected Rabbi Eliezer, who had previously been known as an observant Jew (a population with which Rome had its complicated but ultimately stable relationship), of having become a Jewish Christian — the dangerous heretical sect that was at that moment being actively suppressed. The reason this episode is introduced here is transparent: we have just been discussing why one may not help build *batei mishpat*, the courthouses of Rome, given how they serve as instruments by which Jews are falsely judged and executed on trumped-up charges. The story of Rabbi Eliezer's arrest exemplifies exactly that.

### Trial & Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: נֶאֱמָן עָלַי הַדַּיָּין. כְּסָבוּר אוֹתוֹ הֶגְמוֹן: עָלָיו הוּא אוֹמֵר, וְהוּא לֹא אָמַר אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד אָבִיו שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם. אָמַר לוֹ: הוֹאִיל וְהֶאֱמַנְתִּי עָלֶיךָ, דִּימוֹס — פָּטוּר אַתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: The Judge is trusted by me** to rule correctly. **That officer thought** that Rabbi Eliezer **was speaking about him; but** in fact **he said** this **only in reference to his Father in Heaven.** Rabbi Eliezer meant that he accepted God's judgment, i.e., if he was charged he must have sinned to God in some manner. The officer **said to him: Since you** put **your trust in me,** you are **acquitted [*dimos*]; you are exempt.**

*He'aluhu l'gardom lidon* — they brought him up to the *gardom* to be judged. And here we encounter that word again: *gardom* is the very platform mentioned in the *mishnah*, the place of judgment and execution. The presiding *hegmon* — the Roman officer and chief judge — said to him: *zaken shekmotecha ya'asok bidvarim betelim halalu*? You are a great sage, a respected elder — why have you involved yourself with this heretical sect?

Rabbi Eliezer replied: *ne'eman alai hadayan* — the judge is trusted by me. He meant, of course, that he trusted the Divine Judge to vindicate him. But the *hegmon* understood it as a compliment to himself: *kasavar oto hegmon alav hu omer* — the officer thought Rabbi Eliezer was saying "I trust you, this judge, to see my innocence." *V'hu lo amar ela keneged aviv shebashamayim* — but he was referring only to his Father in Heaven. The *hegmon*, satisfied that the accused was placing his trust in the Roman court's judgment and not attempting to mount a defense, declared: *ho'il v'hemanti alecha* — since you have trusted me — *dimus patur ata* — you are exempt, free.

### Dimus Meaning

What is the meaning of *dimus*? *Rashi* explains that *dimus* was the name of the officer's god, suggesting the dismissal was framed as a religious declaration. The Soncino commentary, and more accessibly Steinsaltz, suggests it simply means freedom or exemption. *Tosafot* provides the clearest explanation: *perash berachamei* — it is the word of mercy; *ki dimus lashon rachamim hi* — *dimus* is an expression of compassion or clemency. You are pardoned. Interestingly, *dimus* and *gardom* have both survived into modern Hebrew: *gardom* persists in the name of the Acre prison where the British hanged members of the Irgun, and *bedimus* describes a retired judge — a *shofet bedimus*, a judge emeritus, one who has been relieved of his post.

When Rabbi Eliezer returned home, his students gathered to comfort him — not that he had survived, for that was clearly cause for relief, but because the very fact that he had been seized under false suspicion deeply troubled him. *V'lo kibel alav tanchumim* — he refused to accept their consolation. Something he had done, he believed, had brought this upon him, whether as Divine punishment or as behavior that had given the Romans legitimate grounds for suspicion.

## Rabbi Akiva Teaching

### Permission Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: רַבִּי, תַּרְשֵׁינִי לוֹמַר דָּבָר אֶחָד מִמַּה שֶּׁלִּימַּדְתַּנִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: My teacher, allow me to say one matter from** all of **that which you taught me.**

Rabbi Akiva approached him and said: *rebbi, harshini lomar davar echad mimah shelimadtani* — Rebbe, permit me to say something from what you yourself taught me. This is a moment worth pausing on from a pastoral perspective. The act of asking permission before sharing difficult insight sets an entirely different tone than lecturing someone in distress. It signals sensitivity and signals that what follows is offered in service of the other person rather than imposed upon them. Rabbi Eliezer granted permission.

### Marketplace Encounter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, שֶׁמָּא מִינוּת בָּא לְיָדְךָ וְהִנְאַךָ, וְעָלָיו נִתְפַּסְתָּ? אָמַר לוֹ: עֲקִיבָא, הִזְכַּרְתַּנִי, פַּעַם אַחַת הָיִיתִי מְהַלֵּךְ בַּשּׁוּק הָעֶלְיוֹן שֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִי, וּמָצָאתִי אָדָם אֶחָד מִתַּלְמִידֵי יֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי, וְיַעֲקֹב אִישׁ כְּפַר סְכַנְיָא שְׁמוֹ. אָמַר לִי: כָּתוּב בְּתוֹרַתְכֶם: ״לֹא תָבִיא אֶתְנַן זוֹנָה וְגוֹ׳״, מַהוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת הֵימֶנּוּ בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל? וְלֹא אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **said to him: My teacher, perhaps** some statement of **heresy came before you** and you derived pleasure from it, and because of that you were held responsible by Heaven. Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: Akiva,** you are right, as **you have reminded me** that **once I was walking in the upper marketplace of Tzippori, and I found a man** who was one **of the students of Jesus the Nazarene, and his name was Ya'akov of Kefar Sekhanya. He said to me: It is written in your Torah: "You shall not bring the payment to a prostitute,** or the price of a dog, into the house of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 23:19). **What is** the *halakha*: Is it permitted **to make from** the payment to a prostitute for services rendered **a bathroom for a High Priest** in the Temple? **And I said nothing to him** in response.

Rabbi Akiva asked: perhaps some heretical teaching came into your hand and you derived pleasure from it — *shema minut ba leyadcha vehana'ah* — and on account of that you were seized? The phrase carries a double meaning: perhaps this intellectual engagement with a heretic both gave the Romans reason to suspect you of following the sect, and perhaps it is why God brought this upon you — since engaging such teachings, even with pleasure at their cleverness, is precisely what one is meant to avoid.

Rabbi Eliezer said: *Akiva hizkartani* — Akiva, you have reminded me. He recalled: once he was walking in the upper marketplace of *Tzippori* and he encountered a man — here the text as transmitted gives the sanitized form, naming only *Yaakov ish Kfar Sekanya*, but the original manuscripts and *rishonim* restore the full reading: *umatzati echad mitalmidav shel Yeshu HaNotzri* — he encountered one of the disciples of Jesus of Nazareth, and his name was *Yaakov ish Kfar Sekanya*. We are speaking, then, explicitly of early Jewish Christians.

### Non-Response Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לִי: כָּךְ לִימְּדַנִי יֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי, כִּי ״מֵאֶתְנַן זוֹנָה קֻבָּצָה וְעַד אֶתְנַן זוֹנָה יָשׁוּבוּ״, מִמְּקוֹם הַטִּנּוּפֶת בָּאוּ, לִמְקוֹם הַטִּנּוּפֶת יֵלֵכוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to me: Jesus the Nazarene taught me the following:** It is permitted, as derived from the verse: **"For of the payment to a prostitute she has gathered them, and to the payment to a prostitute they shall return"** (Micah 1:7). Since the coins **came from a place of filth, let them go to a place of filth** and be used to build a bathroom.

*Yaakov ish Kfar Sekanya* posed a *halachic* question to Rabbi Eliezer: it is written in your Torah, *lo tavi etnan zonah* — you may not bring the fee paid to a prostitute into the house of God. So what about using such money to build a *beit hakisse* — an outhouse — for the *kohen gadol*, who must reside on the Temple Mount for the seven days preceding *Yom Kippur*? Would that be a fitting use, converting money from a place of filth to a place of filth?

*Velo amarti lo klum* — Rabbi Eliezer said nothing in response. This was deliberate. The best approach to a missionary or a sectarian who seeks to draw you into debate is often silence — to engage is to invite further entanglement, which is precisely what leads a person deeper in. The man did not walk away, however, and continued: *kach limadani* — and here the original reads *kach limadani Yeshu HaNotzri*, this is what Jesus taught me — *ki meetnan zonah kibtza ve'ad etnan zonah yashuvu*: because it was gathered from the payment of a prostitute, so shall it return to the payment of a prostitute. *Mimmekom hatinofet ba'u lemakom hatinofet yelechu* — it came from a place of filth and to a place of filth it shall go. The argument is a kind of midrashic wordplay applied to a novel *halachic* conclusion.

### Prostitute Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִנְאַנִי הַדָּבָר, עַל יְדֵי זֶה נִתְפַּסְתִּי לְמִינוּת, וְעָבַרְתִּי עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה: ״הַרְחֵק מֵעָלֶיהָ דַּרְכֶּךָ״ — זוֹ מִינוּת, ״וְאַל תִּקְרַב אֶל פֶּתַח בֵּיתָהּ״ — זוֹ הָרָשׁוּת. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: ״הַרְחֵק מֵעָלֶיהָ דַּרְכֶּךָ״ — זוֹ מִינוּת וְהָרָשׁוּת, ״וְאַל תִּקְרַב אֶל פֶּתַח בֵּיתָהּ״ — זוֹ זוֹנָה. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And I derived pleasure from the statement,** and **due to this, I was arrested for heresy** by the authorities, because **I transgressed that which is written in the Torah:** "Remove your way far from her, and do not come near the entrance of her house" (Proverbs 5:8). **"Remove your way far from her," this** is a reference to **heresy; "and do not come near the entrance of her house," this** is a reference to **the ruling authority.** The Gemara notes: **And there are** those **who say** a different interpretation: **"Remove your way far from her," this** is a reference to **heresy and the ruling authority; "and do not come near the entrance of her house," this** is a reference to **a prostitute. And how much** distance must one maintain from a prostitute? **Rav Ḥisda said: Four cubits.**

*Vehenani hadavar* — and Rabbi Eliezer admitted: I was pleased by it. He found it a clever *chap* — a good reading. Now, as a pure matter of *halacha* the ruling itself is *pashut* and *muttar* in any case, since the prohibition of *etnan zonah* applies only to bringing the object directly into the *Beit HaMikdash* itself, not to these peripheral applications. But the pleasure he took in the cleverness of the argument, in the intellectual engagement — that, Rabbi Akiva was suggesting, was the problem. *Al zeh nitfasti* — it is for this that I was seized. *Ve'avarti al mah shekasuv batorah* — I transgressed what the Torah commanded: *harcheik me'aleha darkecha* — keep far from her, your path.

## Proverbs Interpretation

### Three Threats

Rabbi Eliezer identifies the verse from *Mishlei* — *harcheik me'aleha darkecha ve'al tikrav el pesach beisah* — as the operative *pasuk*. He reads it as referring to two distinct dangers: *harcheik me'aleha darkecha* — keep your path far from her — this is *minut*, heresy. *Ve'al tikrav el pesach beisah* — do not draw near to the opening of her house — this is *rashut*, the government. A second opinion (*ve'ika de'amrei*) holds that the first half of the verse refers to both *minut* and *rashut* together, while the second half — do not draw near to her house — refers to *zonah*, prostitution.

### Four Cubits Rule

How far must one keep one's distance from a house of heresy or prostitution? Rav Chisda rules: *arba amos* — four *amos*.

### Modern Threats

Here a striking observation demands attention. We are learning *Masechet Avodah Zarah* — a tractate devoted in its entirety to the prohibition of idolatry and maintaining distance from idolatrous practice. If someone were told, without context, that the *gemara* here cites *harcheik me'aleha darkecha* — keep far from her path — as protection against the dangers that confront Israel, what would one expect the *gemara* to identify as the primary threat? One would naturally say: a house of *avodah zarah*. Yet the *gemara* identifies three things to keep away from — heresy, government, and sexual immorality — and not one of them is *avodah zarah* in the classical sense of literal idol worship.

This speaks volumes about the world in which the *chachamim* lived. By the time of *Bayit Sheini* the *yetzer hara* for *avodah zarah* had already been extinguished — the *gemara* says this explicitly elsewhere. Even for many non-Jews in the Roman world, paganism had become largely *pro forma*, a matter of cultural convention rather than genuine religious devotion. And so the tractate that bears the name *Avodah Zarah* is really, in its lived texture, about something else entirely: about the seductions of intellectual heresy, which could draw a brilliant mind into foreign religious frameworks; about the allure of governmental power, which could compromise a person's integrity and independence; and about sexual freedom, which the larger Roman world offered in abundance.

These were the real threats. Not idols of stone and wood, but the pull of sectarian ideas, the glamour of Roman authority, and the freedom of a life unbound by the constraints of *taharah* and *kedushah*. In that sense the tractate's themes are perennial: the question of what constitutes *avodah zarah b'yameinu* — idolatry in our time — is never answered by pointing to pagan temples that no longer exist, but by identifying what actually competes with our loyalty to God and *Torah*.

On the related *halachic* matter: the *gemara* notes that the Rabbis' interpretation of *meetnan zonah* — the verse about the prostitute's fee — follows Rav Chisda's teaching that any prostitute who hires herself out for pay will eventually fall on hard times and lose her appeal, and will in the end find herself paying others for what she once sold: *uvisiteich esnan ve-esnan lo nisan lach va-tehi le-hefech* — in your giving of payment, and payment was not given to you, the dynamic has reversed.

## Physical Contact Rules

### Sexual vs. Neutral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי פְּדָת, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי פְּדָת: לֹא אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא קְרִיבָה שֶׁל גִּלּוּי עֲרָיוֹת בִּלְבַד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ אֶל כׇּל שְׁאֵר בְּשָׂרוֹ לֹא תִקְרְבוּ לְגַלּוֹת עֶרְוָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** Rav Ḥisda, who stated above that the Torah requires one to maintain a distance of four cubits from a prostitute, **disagrees with** the opinion **of Rabbi Pedat. As Rabbi Pedat says: The Torah prohibited only intimacy that involves engaging in prohibited sexual relations, as it is stated: "None of you shall approach to any that is near of kin to him, to uncover their nakedness"** (Leviticus 18:6). The prohibition against intimacy in the Torah applies exclusively to sexual intercourse, and all other kinds of intimacy that do not include actual intercourse are not included in the prohibition.

This ruling of Rav Chisda's regarding four *amos* disagrees with a position of Rav Padat. Rav Padat holds: *lo asrah Torah ela kereivah shel gilluy arayos bilvad* — the Torah prohibited only *kereivah* that constitutes an actual act of forbidden intercourse. When the Torah says *lo sikrevu legallos ervah* — do not draw near to uncover nakedness — it is not speaking about physical proximity to a building or a person; it is speaking about the act of intercourse itself. The proof-text is *ish ish el kol she'er besaro lo sikrevu legallos ervah* — a man shall not draw near to any close flesh to uncover nakedness.

*Tosafot* raises an apparent difficulty: even if the biblical prohibition is limited to actual intercourse, why could the Rabbis not decree a prohibition of physical proximity? *Tosafot* resolves this by explaining that Rav Padat's point is hermeneutical: since in the context of this verse *lo sikrevu* clearly means the act of intercourse itself rather than mere physical closeness, one cannot reinterpret the same phrase elsewhere in the verse to mean physical proximity. The *Ramban* offers an additional nuance, suggesting that the phrase *lo asrah Torah* in Rav Padat's formulation is best read as meaning the Rabbis did not forbid — since the Torah did not forbid it, neither did the Rabbis follow through with a rabbinic decree.

### Ulla's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא כִּי הֲוָה אָתֵי מִבֵּי רַב, הֲוָה מְנַשֵּׁק לְהוּ לַאֲחָתֵיהּ אַבֵּי יְדַיְיהוּ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: אַבֵּי חָדַיְיהוּ. וּפְלִיגָא דִּידֵיהּ אַדִּידֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: קְרִיבָה בְּעָלְמָא אָסוּר, מִשּׁוּם ״לָךְ לָךְ, אָמְרִין נְזִירָא; סְחוֹר סְחוֹר, לְכַרְמָא לָא תִּקְרַב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When Ulla would come from the study hall, he would kiss his sisters on their hands. And some say: On their chests. And** the Gemara points out that this action of **his disagrees with** another ruling that Ulla **himself** issued, **as Ulla says: Mere intimacy** with a woman with whom one is prohibited from engaging in sexual intercourse is **prohibited, due to** the maxim: **Go, go, we say to a nazirite, go around, go around** but **do not come near to the vineyard.** Just as a nazirite is warned not even to come into close proximity of a vineyard lest he consume a product of the vine, so too one is obligated to distance himself from anyone with whom intercourse is forbidden.

From this the *gemara* moves to a related episode. When *Ulla* would return from the *beit rav*, he would kiss his sister — *havi menashak lachasei* — on her hand. Some say on her chest (*abei chadaihu*). The *gemara* then notes: *u-pleigei didei adidei* — Ulla contradicts himself. For Ulla himself rules elsewhere that *kereivah be-alma asur* — even casual physical contact is forbidden — based on the principle of *lech lech amrinan lenezira sechor sechor lecharma lo sikrav*: we say to the *nazir*, go, go, stay away — do not even get close to the vineyard. The *nazir* is forbidden to drink wine, yet we tell him to keep distance from the vineyard itself as a safeguard. So too, the logic of safeguards would seem to prohibit even neutral physical contact.

*Tosafot* resolves the contradiction: Ulla was lenient with respect to his sister *mishum dedamyan alei ki kishura* — because in his own experience that contact carried no sexual charge whatsoever. There was no erotic overtone, no sexual thought, nothing of that nature.

### Lech Lech Safeguard

This resolution opens onto a significant point about the scope of the prohibition. The conventional framing heard in many communities is that any physical contact between a man and a woman who is *assurah* to him is rabbinically forbidden, and that one who shakes hands at work requires a *matir* such as *kevod habriyot* — human dignity — to permit it. But a careful reading of many *rishonim* reveals that this framing is not entirely accurate. The *rishonim* widely hold that there is, in fact, no formal rabbinic prohibition against neutral touch. The prohibition targets specifically *kereivah* that has sexual overtones or that is likely to lead to sexual thoughts or arousal. The *lech lech* principle — the safeguard logic — applies where there is concern that a given touch, though nominally neutral, carries or will carry some sexual charge. In that case, one should indeed keep distance. But where the touch is genuinely neutral, without any such undertone, there is no formal prohibition at all. This is a meaningfully different framework from invoking *kevod habriyot* as a *matir* for something otherwise forbidden.

It should also be noted that the *Ramban* emphasizes that much of this discussion — including Ulla's kissing of his sister — takes place specifically in the context of close relatives. The analysis may be more stringent regarding physical contact between a man and a woman who is married to another man, where there are additional parties whose dignity and marital sanctity are implicated, and where the social and emotional dynamics are quite different from a brother-sister relationship.

## Leech's Daughters

### Give Give Daughters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לַעֲלוּקָה שְׁתֵּי בָנוֹת הַב הַב״. מַאי ״הַב הַב״? אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא: [קוֹל] שְׁתֵּי בָּנוֹת שֶׁצּוֹעֲקוֹת מִגֵּיהִנָּם, וְאוֹמְרוֹת בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה ״הָבֵא הָבֵא״. וּמַאן נִינְהוּ? מִינוּת וְהָרָשׁוּת. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא, קוֹל גֵּיהִנָּם צוֹעֶקֶת וְאוֹמֶרֶת: הָבִיאוּ לִי שְׁתֵּי בָּנוֹת שֶׁצּוֹעֲקוֹת וְאוֹמְרוֹת בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה ״הָבֵא הָבֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** § In connection to the earlier mention of heresy and the ruling authorities, the Gemara cites a verse: **"The horseleech has two daughters: Give, give"** (Proverbs 30:15). **What** is meant by **"give, give"? Mar Ukva says:** This is the **voice** of **the two daughters who cry** out **from Gehenna** due to their suffering; **and** they are the ones who **say in this world: Give, give,** demanding dues and complete allegiance. **And who are they?** They are **heresy and the ruling authority. There are** those **who say** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Mar Ukva says: The voice of Gehenna cries** out **and says: Bring me two daughters who cry and say in this world: Give, give.**

The *gemara* now turns to a verse in *Mishlei* (30): *la'alukah shtei banot hav hav* — to the leech there are two daughters crying "give, give." Mar Ukva interprets: these are the two daughters that cry out from *gehinnom* and say in this world *hav hav* — give, give. They are the two great seductions of this world that drag a person down to *gehinnom*.

### Twin Temptations

And what are these two daughters? *Minut verashut* — heresy and the government. The two daughters are, in other words, the two forces that are most powerfully seductive in this world: heresy, which is intellectually alluring and captures brilliant minds, and the government, which is alluring on account of its power and the access it provides. An alternate version of the teaching (*ika de'amrei*) frames it as *gehinnom* itself crying out and demanding its two daughters who cry out in this world — but the identification of the daughters with *minut* and *rashut* remains consistent.

### Leaving Heresy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כׇּל בָּאֶיהָ לֹא יְשׁוּבוּן וְלֹא יַשִּׂיגוּ אׇרְחוֹת חַיִּים״, וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁלֹּא שָׁבוּ, הֵיכָן יַשִּׂיגוּ? הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְאִם יָשׁוּבוּ — לֹא יַשִּׂיגוּ אוֹרְחוֹת חַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The following verse in Proverbs makes reference to a foreign woman, which according to the Sages is a euphemism for heresy: **"None that go to her return, neither do they attain the paths of life"** (Proverbs 2:19). The Gemara asks: **Since** those that are drawn to heresy **do not return,** from **where would they attain** the path of life? Why is it necessary for the verse to add that they do not attain the paths of life? The Gemara explains that **this** is what the verse **is saying:** In general, those who go to her do not return, **and** even **if they return, they do not attain the paths of life,** i.e., the pain of their regret will shorten their lives.

The *gemara* then cites a verse: *kol ba'eha lo yeshuvun v'lo yasigu orchot chayim* — all who go into her will not return and will never find the path of life. This is read as a statement about those who follow after heresy. But a difficulty immediately arises: if they don't return, of course they won't find the path of life — that is circular. *Hachi ka'amar* — here is what it means: *v'ee shavu* — if they do try to return — *lo yasigu orchot chayim* — they will fail to achieve the path of life. The implication is stark: leaving heresy is so spiritually difficult, so wrenching to the soul that has become intertwined with it, that the attempt itself kills the penitent.

## Woman's Confession

### Rav Chisda Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא, דְּכׇל הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמִּינוּת מָיֵית? וְהָא הָהִיא דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, וְאָמְרָה לֵיהּ: קַלָּה שֶׁבַּקַּלּוֹת עָשְׂתָה בְּנָהּ הַקָּטָן מִבְּנָהּ הַגָּדוֹל, וַאֲמַר לַהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: טְרַחוּ לַהּ בִּזְוָודְתָּא, וְלָא מִתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that anyone who separates** himself **from heresy** and returns from his mistaken ways must **die? But** what about **that** woman **who came before Rav Ḥisda** to confess to him, **and she said to him: The lightest of the light,** i.e., the least of the sins that she committed, is that **she conceived her younger son from** engaging in intercourse with **her older son. And Rav Ḥisda said to her: Prepare funeral shrouds for her,** i.e., yourself, as you will certainly die soon, **but she did not die.**

The *gemara* raises an apparent challenge to this principle from a case involving Rav Chisda. A woman came before Rav Chisda and confessed: *kala she-bi-kalot asta b'na katan mi-b'na gadol* — the lightest of my sins is that my youngest son was fathered by my oldest son, meaning she had committed incest. This, she said, was among her lighter transgressions. Rav Chisda responded: *tarchu la b'zavdeta* — go prepare her burial shrouds. The *teshuva* she was attempting to do was of such magnitude, and the spiritual extraction involved so dangerous, that she would not survive the process.

### Incomplete Teshuva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָאָמְרָה קַלָּה שֶׁבַּקַּלּוֹת עָשְׂתָה, מִכְּלָל דְּמִינוּת [נָמֵי] הָוְיָא בַּהּ! הָהוּא דְּלָא הָדְרָא בַּהּ שַׁפִּיר, וּמִשּׁוּם הָכִי לֹא מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The above incident refutes the claim that anyone who repents for the sin of heresy must die, as **from** the fact **that she said** that **the lightest of the light** of her sins was that **she conceived** one son from engaging in intercourse with another son, **by inference** one can learn **that she was also involved in heresy,** and yet she did not die. The Gemara answers: **That** is a case **where** the woman **did not repent properly, and due to that** reason **she did not die.**

Yet the *gemara* reports: *v'lo meita* — she did not die. Why not? The answer: *d'lo hadra bah shapir* — she did not actually complete her *teshuva*. She did not fully extricate herself from her sins, and therefore the danger did not materialize. Only genuine, complete separation triggers the mortal risk.

### Heresy-Only Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מִמִּינוּת — אִין, מֵעֲבֵירָה — לָא? וְהָא הָהִיא דַּאֲתַאי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ [רַב חִסְדָּא: זַוִּידוּ לַהּ זְוַודְתָּא], וּמִתָה! מִדְּקָאָמְרָה ״קַלָּה שֶׁבַּקַּלּוֹת״, מִכְּלָל דְּמִינוּת נָמֵי הַוְיָא בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** there is a different version of the objection to the Gemara's statement that those who repent for the sin of heresy must die: Is that to say that if one repents **for** the sin of **heresy, yes,** the result is death, whereas if one repents **for** the **sin** of forbidden sexual intercourse he does **not** die? **But** what about **that** woman **who came before Rav Ḥisda** to confess to him **and Rav Ḥisda said to** those present: **Prepare funeral shrouds for her, and she died?** The Gemara answers: **From** the fact **that she said: The lightest of the light, by inference** one can learn **that she was also involved in heresy.**

A second version of the tradition (*ika de'amrei*) holds a sharper position: *mi-minus in me-aveira lo* — only *minut*, heresy, carries this danger of death upon separation; other sins, even severe ones, do not. Under this view the woman's case is explained differently: since she described those sins as among her *lighter* transgressions, *m'chlal d'minus nami hava bah* — it may be inferred that she had also been involved in heresy, which is why she died (in this version of the story she does die). Either way, it is specifically heresy — the complete enmeshment of the soul in a false theological framework — that creates this unique dynamic where *teshuva* itself becomes lethal.

## Elazar Durdaya

### Prostitute Hunter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן דּוּרְדְּיָא, שֶׁלֹּא הִנִּיחַ זוֹנָה אַחַת בָּעוֹלָם שֶׁלֹּא בָּא עָלֶיהָ. פַּעַם אַחַת שָׁמַע שֶׁיֵּשׁ זוֹנָה אַחַת בִּכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם, וְהָיְתָה נוֹטֶלֶת כִּיס דִּינָרִין בִּשְׂכָרָהּ. נָטַל כִּיס דִּינָרִין וְהָלַךְ וְעָבַר עָלֶיהָ שִׁבְעָה נְהָרוֹת. בִּשְׁעַת הֶרְגֵּל דָּבָר הֵפִיחָה, אָמְרָה: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהֲפָיַחַת זוֹ אֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת לִמְקוֹמָהּ, כָּךְ אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן דּוּרְדְּיָא אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ בִּתְשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that anyone who separates** himself **from heresy** and returns from his mistaken ways must **die? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **They said about Rabbi Elazar ben Durdayya that** he was so promiscuous that **he did not leave one prostitute in the world with whom he did not engage in sexual intercourse. Once, he heard that there was one prostitute in** one of the **cities overseas who would take a purse** full of **dinars as her payment. He took a purse** full of **dinars and went and crossed seven rivers** to reach **her. When** they were engaged in the **matters** to which they were **accustomed,** a euphemism for intercourse, **she passed wind** and **said: Just as this passed wind** will **not return to its place, so too Elazar ben Durdayya will not be accepted in repentance,** even if he were to try to repent.

*Vehatanya amru alav al Elazar ben Durdaya* — it was taught: they said of Elazar ben Durdaya that *she-lo hiniach zona achat ba-olam she-lo ba aleha* — he did not leave a single prostitute in the entire world with whom he had not had intercourse. This sets the stage: a life of comprehensive, relentless immersion in sexual immorality, the opposite of the scholar's life of *Torah* and discipline.

*Pa'am achat shama she-yesh zona achat b'charchei ha-yam* — once he heard that there was a particular prostitute living on an island overseas, and her fee was an entire purse of gold *dinarei*. He took the purse, crossed seven rivers, and reached her. In the midst of their encounter she released wind or breath and said: *k'shem she-heficha zu eina chozeret li-m'koma kach Elazar ben Durdaya ein mikablin oto bi-tshuva* — just as this breath I have released will not return to its source, so Elazar ben Durdaya will never be accepted back in *teshuva*.

One cannot help noticing the strange poignancy here: it is the prostitute herself who delivers the prophetic rebuke, and the nature of her utterance — an expulsion of breath, of wind, of something that departs and does not return — anticipates the departure of his own *neshama* at the story's end. What moved her to say this is never explained.

### Mountains Dialogue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָלַךְ וְיָשַׁב בֵּין שְׁנֵי הָרִים וּגְבָעוֹת, אָמַר: הָרִים וּגְבָעוֹת בַּקְּשׁוּ עָלַי רַחֲמִים! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: עַד שֶׁאָנוּ מְבַקְּשִׁים עָלֶיךָ נְבַקֵּשׁ עַל עַצְמֵנוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הֶהָרִים יָמוּשׁוּ וְהַגְּבָעוֹת תְּמוּטֶינָה״. אָמַר: שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ בַּקְּשׁוּ עָלַי רַחֲמִים! אָמְרוּ: עַד שֶׁאָנוּ מְבַקְּשִׁים עָלֶיךָ נְבַקֵּשׁ עַל עַצְמֵנוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי שָׁמַיִם כֶּעָשָׁן נִמְלָחוּ וְהָאָרֶץ כַּבֶּגֶד תִּבְלֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** This statement deeply shocked Elazar ben Durdayya, and **he went and sat between two mountains and hills** and **said: Mountains and hills, pray for mercy on my** behalf, so that my repentance will be accepted. **They said to him: Before we pray for mercy on your** behalf, **we must pray for mercy on our own** behalf, **as it is stated: "For the mountains may depart, and the hills be removed"** (Isaiah 54:10). **He said: Heaven and earth, pray for mercy on my** behalf. **They said** to him: **Before we pray for mercy on your** behalf, **we must pray for mercy on our own** behalf, **as it is stated: "For the heavens shall vanish away like smoke, and the earth shall wax old like a garment"** (Isaiah 51:6).

*Halach veyashav bein shenei harim ugvaot* — he went and sat between two mountains and hills. *Amar harim ugvaot bikshu alai rachamim* — mountains and hills, pray for mercy on my behalf. They replied: *ad she'anu mevakshim alecha nevakesh al atzmenu* — before we pray for you, we must pray for ourselves. *Shene'emar ki heharim yamushu vehagvaot temutena* — as it is written, the mountains shall be removed and the hills shall be shaken.

He turned to heaven and earth: pray for mercy on my behalf. The same answer: we must pray for ourselves. *Ki shamayim ke'ashan nimlachu veha'aretz kevaged tivale* — the heavens will dissipate like smoke and the earth will wear away like a garment.

He turned to the sun and moon: pray for mercy. Same answer: *vechafra halevana uvoshah hachama* — the moon will be embarrassed and the sun will be ashamed.

He turned to the stars and constellations: same answer, *venamaku kol tzeva hashamayim* — all the host of heaven will melt away.

The *Maharsha* reads these exchanges as the cosmos reassuring him: the entire world is transitory, impermanent. Do not cling to life in this world at the cost of your spiritual transformation. Everything passes. What matters is your *neshamah*.

There may also be an echo here of the *midrash* at the beginning of the *masechet*, on *daf 3*, where the heavens and earth were called as witnesses to the Jews' observance of *Torah* and were rejected as *nogeia badavar* — because the existence of the heavens and earth depends on Israel's keeping the *Torah*. Here, when Elazar ben Durdaya asks the heavens and mountains to pray for him, they respond: *your* sin has put *us* in danger. We must pray for ourselves. Human sin threatens the foundations of creation — which lends a deeper, cosmological gravity to his *teshuva*.

There is also a provocative dimension to the scene: Elazar ben Durdaya is beseeching entities other than God to intercede on his behalf. The same theological question that arises around *machnisei rachamim* and *malachei ha-shareit* — whether one may petition created beings to intercede with God — appears implicitly here, and the *gemara* does not flag it as problematic.

### Soul Departs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: אֵין הַדָּבָר תָּלוּי אֶלָּא בִּי. הִנִּיחַ רֹאשׁוֹ בֵּין בִּרְכָּיו וְגָעָה בִּבְכִיָּה עַד שֶׁיָּצְתָה נִשְׁמָתוֹ. יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן דּוּרְדְּיָא מְזֻומָּן לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא! [וְהָא הָכָא בַּעֲבֵירָה הֲוָה וּמִית], הָתָם נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דַּאֲבִיק בַּהּ טוּבָא, כְּמִינוּת דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Elazar ben Durdayya **said:** Clearly **the matter depends on nothing other than myself. He placed his head between his knees and cried loudly until his soul left** his body. **A Divine Voice emerged and said: Rabbi Elazar ben Durdayya is destined for life in the World-to-Come.** The Gemara explains the difficulty presented by this story: **And here** Elazar ben Durdayya **was** guilty of **the sin** of forbidden sexual intercourse, **and** yet **he died** once he repented. The Gemara answers: **There too, since he was attached so strongly** to the sin, to an extent that transcended the physical temptation he felt, **it is similar to heresy,** as it had become like a form of idol worship for him.

*Amar ain hadavar talui ela bi* — he recognized: this depends on no one but me. *Hiniach rosho bein birkav* — he placed his head between his knees, and *vagaa bivchiya* — he cried out in weeping, *ad sheyatzta nishmato* — until his soul departed. The image of the head between the knees evokes the position between the mountains, the man enclosed by forces greater than himself; and on another reading it evokes the position of the fetus, the head drawn into the body — as though this dying is also a birth.

*Yatzta bas kol* — a *bas kol* came forth and declared: *Rebbi Elazar ben Durdia mezuman lechayei ha'olam haba* — Rabbi Elazar ben Durdaya is destined for life in *Olam Haba*.

The word *mezuman* is worth pausing on. It does not say straightforwardly that he *has* *Olam Haba* or that he has entered it. *Mezuman* conveys readiness, preparation — he is prepared for it, he is on his way. Perhaps this leaves room for the possibility that he still underwent whatever process of purification is necessary before entering *Olam Haba* in its fullest sense.

### Rabbi's Reflection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּכָה רַבִּי וְאָמַר: יֵשׁ קוֹנֶה עוֹלָמוֹ בְּכַמָּה שָׁנִים, וְיֵשׁ קוֹנֶה עוֹלָמוֹ בְּשָׁעָה אַחַת. וְאָמַר רַבִּי: לֹא דַּיָּין לְבַעֲלֵי תְשׁוּבָה שֶׁמְּקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן, אֶלָּא שֶׁקּוֹרִין אוֹתָן ״רַבִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** When **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi heard this story of Elazar ben Durdayya, **he wept and said: There is** one who **acquires his** share in the World-to-Come only **after many years** of toil, **and there is** one who **acquires his** share in the World-to-Come **in one moment. And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi further **says: Not only are penitents accepted, but they are even called: Rabbi,** as the Divine Voice referred to Elazar ben Durdayya as Rabbi Elazar ben Durdayya.

*Bakha Rabbi v'amar* — Rabbi wept and said: *yesh koneh olomo b'khama shanim v'yesh koneh olomo b'sha'ah achat* — there are those who acquire their world across many years, and there are those who acquire their world in a single hour. And Rabbi added: *lo dayan l'ba'alei teshuva she'mekablin otan* — it is not enough that penitents are received; *ela she'korin otam Rabbi* — they are even called *Rabbi*, since the *bas kol* itself addressed him with that honorific. Who gave him *semikhah*? Rabbi asks implicitly, not without some mixture of wonder and poignancy. The answer, apparently, is *teshuva* itself.

The *gemara* connects the *bas kol*'s answer to the earlier question: what about Rav Padat's position and the woman who separated from a life of sin and did not die? The answer is: *keivan deadbak bah teshuva keminut damya* — when someone becomes so thoroughly immersed in a pattern of sin that it is intertwined with his very soul, that is treated like *minut* in terms of the danger of separation. It is not merely a series of external acts. The sin has become identity.

## Crossroads Story

### Fork Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא וְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן הֲווֹ קָאָזְלִי בְּאוֹרְחָא, מְטוֹ לְהָנְהוּ תְּרֵי שְׁבִילֵי, חַד פָּצֵי אַפִּיתְחָא דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְחַד פָּצֵי אַפִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי זוֹנוֹת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ חַד לְחַבְרֵיהּ: נֵיזִיל אַפִּיתְחָא דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה
>
> **Translation:** § In relation to the issue of distancing oneself from idol worship and prostitution, the Gemara relates: **Rabbi Ḥanina and Rabbi Yonatan were** once **walking along the road** when **they came to a certain two paths, one** of which **branched off toward the entrance of** a place of **idol worship, and** the other **one branched off toward the entrance of a brothel. One said to the other: Let us go by** the path that leads to **the entrance** of the place **of idol worship,**

*Rabbi Chanina v'Rabbi Yonatan havai ka'azli b'orkha* — Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Yonatan were traveling together. *Matu l'hanei trei shvilei* — they arrived at a fork in the road. One path passed by a house of *avodas kokhavim*, idol worship; the other passed by a house of prostitution.

### Idolatry Dead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּנְכִיס יִצְרֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אִידַּךְ: נֵיזִיל אַפִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי זוֹנוֹת וְנִכְפְּיֵיהּ לְיִצְרִין וּנְקַבֵּל אַגְרָא. כִּי מָטוּ הָתָם, חֲזוֹנְהוּ [לְזוֹנוֹת], אִיתְכְּנַעוּ מִקַּמַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **as the inclination** to engage in idol worship **has been slaughtered** and the temptation to sin in this manner no longer exists. **The other said to him: Let us go by** the path that leads to **the entrance of** the **brothel and overpower our inclination, and** thereby **receive a reward. When they arrived there, they saw** that **the prostitutes yielded before their presence,** i.e., they entered the building out of respect for the Sages.

One said to the other: let us go by the house of *avodas kokhavim* — *d'nakhis yitrei* — because the *yetzer hara* for idolatry has already been slaughtered. There is no seduction there. Walking past a house of *avodas kokhavim* poses no danger because it simply does not attract us. What the *gemara* here makes fully explicit — and what the earlier discussion of *Proverbs* had only implied — is that *avodah zarah* in its literal sense was no longer a live threat. This is stated plainly: the *yetzer hara* for idol worship is gone, so what is the danger of passing such a place?

### Torah Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״מְזִמָּה תִּשְׁמֹר עָלֶיךָ תְּבוּנָה תִנְצְרֶכָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** One **said to** the other: **From where did you know this,** that the prostitutes would retreat from us in embarrassment? **He said to him:** It is written: **"From lewdness [*mezimma*] it shall watch over you; discernment shall guard you"** (Proverbs 2:11), i.e., the Torah will serve as a safeguard against lewdness.

The other rabbi said: no, let us go by the house of prostitution. *V'nikhpyeih l'yitran* — let us subjugate our desire. We will feel the pull, we will confront the seduction, and we will stay on the straight and narrow path. *V'nekabel igra* — and we will receive reward for having overcome it.

*Ki matu hatam, chazinu l'zonot* — when they arrived, they saw the prostitutes. *Iskanu mikamaya* — and the prostitutes, rather than plying their trade, were embarrassed and withdrew into the house. The men were not themselves overcome by temptation; rather, the very presence of these Torah scholars caused the women to feel shame and retreat.

### Mezimah Tvunah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: מַאי ״מְזִמָּה״? אִילֵימָא תּוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב בַּהּ ״זִמָּה״ וּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן: עֵצַת חִטְאִין, וּכְתִיב: ״הִפְלִיא עֵצָה הִגְדִּיל תּוּשִׁיָּה״. אִי הָכִי, ״זִמָּה״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִדְּבַר זִימָּה תִּשְׁמוֹר עָלֶיךָ, תּוֹרָה תִנְצְרֶכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Rava: What** is the meaning of ***mezimma*? If we say** that it is referring to the **Torah** that will guard you, **as it is written in it: "*Zimma*"** (Leviticus 18:17), **and we translate** this term as: **The counsel of [*atzat*] the sinners,** demonstrating that *zimma* is referring to counsel or wisdom, **and** the term *etza* **is** also **written** with regard to the Torah: "This also comes forth from the Lord of hosts: **Wonderful is His counsel [*etza*], and great is His wisdom"** (Isaiah 28:29), this is difficult. The Gemara explains the difficulty: **If so,** the verse **should have** said: ***Zimma*,** and not *mezimma*. Rather, **this** is what the verse **is saying: From lewd matters [*midevar zimma*], it shall watch over you, the Torah shall guard you,** i.e., the term discernment is a reference to the Torah.

One asked the other: where does this confidence come from? The answer: *mizimah tishmor alecha, tvunah tintzrecha* — *mezimah* will protect you, *tvunah* will guard you. The rabbis asked Rava: what is *mezimah*? Perhaps it means *Torah*? The word *mezimah* can carry the meaning of counsel or wisdom — *u-matargaminan etza chochmata* — since it is sometimes rendered as *etza*, advice or counsel, and the verse says *hifli etza higdil tushia* — the counsel was wondrous, great was the salvation. So *mezimah* might mean the wisdom of *Torah*.

The *gemara* objects: *ei hachi zima mibaei lei* — if that were the intent, the verse should have used *zima* rather than *mezimah*. Therefore: *ela hachi ka'amar, midevar zima tishmor lach* — what it means is that *Torah* will protect you from *zimah*, from sexual immorality. *Torah tintzrecha* — the *Torah* guards you. And so what it is that keeps us safe from the seductions of the outside world — from the pull of foreign ideas, from the lure of power, from the freedom of immorality — is, in the end, *Torah* and the life it shapes.

## Torah & Kindness

### Arrest & Fates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁנִּתְפְּסוּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן, אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן: אַשְׁרֶיךָ שֶׁנִּתְפַּסְתָּ עַל דָּבָר אֶחָד, אוֹי לִי שֶׁנִּתְפַּסְתִּי עַל חֲמִישָּׁה דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to its discussion of the judgments of the Sages by the Roman rulers. **The Sages taught: When Rabbi Elazar ben Perata and Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon were arrested** by the Romans during the time of the religious persecution of the Jewish people, **Rabbi Elazar ben Perata said to Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon: Fortunate are you, as you were arrested on one charge** only, of teaching Torah publicly; **woe is me, as I have been arrested on five charges.**

*Keshenitpas Rabbi Elazar ben Prata veRabbi Chananya ben Teradyon* — when Rabbi Elazar ben Prata and Rabbi Chananya ben Teradyon were seized. This occurs in the context of the Hadrianic persecutions, when teaching *Torah* itself was a capital offense. Rabbi Elazar said to Rabbi Chananya: *ashreicha shenitpasta al davar echad* — praised are you that there is only one charge against you. *Oy li shenitpasti al chamisha devarim* — woe to me, for I face five charges. How will I escape all of them?

### Chananya Merits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אַשְׁרֶיךָ שֶׁנִּתְפַּסְתָּ עַל חֲמִישָּׁה דְּבָרִים וְאַתָּה נִיצּוֹל, אוֹי לִי שֶׁנִּתְפַּסְתִּי עַל דָּבָר אֶחָד וְאֵינִי נִיצּוֹל, שֶׁאַתָּה עָסַקְתָּ בַּתּוֹרָה וּבִגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים, וַאֲנִי לֹא עָסַקְתִּי אֶלָּא בַּתּוֹרָה [בִּלְבָד].
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina** ben Teradyon **said to him: Fortunate are you, as you were arrested on five charges but you will be saved; woe is me, as I have been arrested on one charge, but I will not be saved.** You will be saved **because you engaged in Torah** study **and in acts of charity, and I engaged in Torah** study **alone.**

Rabbi Chananya replied: on the contrary — *ashreicha shenitpasta al chamisha devarim ve-ata nitzol* — praised are you that you have five charges and you will be saved. *Kol hamosif goreia* — when a prosecution has to pile on five charges, it is a sign that none of them is solid. *Oy li shenitpasti al davar echad ve-eini nitzol* — and I, with only one charge, will not be saved. And then he explains why: *she-ata asakta baTorah uvigmilut chasadim* — because you devoted yourself to *Torah* and to acts of kindness. *Va-ani lo asakti ela baTorah bilvad* — and I devoted myself to *Torah* alone, and therefore I do not have your merit.

### Elazar Limitation

This is a powerful statement on the limits of *Torah* study in isolation. A person who remains exclusively in the *beit midrash* and never translates his learning into action — into helping people, into kindness, into engagement with the world — is, according to this passage, less meritorious than one who combines scholarship with deed.

### Rav Huna Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה בִּלְבַד דּוֹמֶה כְּמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אֱלוֹהַּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְיָמִים רַבִּים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל לְלֹא אֱלֹהֵי אֱמֶת וְגוֹ׳״. מַאי ״לְלֹא אֱלֹהֵי אֱמֶת״? שֶׁכׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה בִּלְבַד דּוֹמֶה כְּמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אֱלוֹהַּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** this is **in accordance with** a statement **of Rav Huna, as Rav Huna says: Anyone who occupies himself with Torah** study **alone is considered like one who does not have a God. As it is stated: "Now for long seasons Israel was without the true God,** and without a teaching priest, and without the Torah" (II Chronicles 15:3). **What** is meant by **"without the true God"?** This teaches **that anyone who engages in Torah** study **alone is considered like one who does not have** a true **God.**

This is supported by the teaching of Rav Huna: *kol ha-osek baTorah bilvad domeh kemi she-ein lo elo-ah* — anyone who occupies himself with *Torah* alone, to the exclusion of *gemilut chasadim*, is as though he has no God. The verse cited is *veyamim rabbim leYisrael lelo elohei emet* — many days in Israel without a true God. Normally one would read this as referring to idolatry, but Rav Huna applies it to *Torah* without action: if you have no God, if your relationship is not truly with the Divine but only with the text, your learning will not translate into living as God lives — as *gomel chasadim*, as *menachem avelim*, as one who does kindness and comforts mourners.

### Chisda Exemplar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים לֹא עָסַק? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִתֵּן אָדָם מְעוֹתָיו לְאַרְנָקִי שֶׁל צְדָקָה אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מְמוּנֶּה עָלָיו תַּלְמִיד חָכָם כְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן! (הֵימְנוּהּ) [הֵימוֹנֵי] הוּא דַּהֲוָה מְהֵימַן, מֶיעְבַּד לָא עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it true that Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **did not engage in acts of charity? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: A person should not donate his money to the charity purse [*le'arnakei*] unless a Torah scholar like Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon is appointed** as supervisor **over it?** The Gemara answers: **He was trusted** to distribute the charity with honesty and integrity, but he himself **did not perform** charitable acts.

The *gemara* then asks: but did Rabbi Chananya not engage in *gemilut chasadim*? The *baraita* of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov says: one should not entrust his charitable funds to a *tzedakah* collector unless that collector is a *talmid chacham* of the caliber of Rabbi Chananya ben Teradyon — the implication being that Rabbi Chananya was actively involved in collecting and distributing *tzedakah*. How then could Rabbi Chananya say he did not do *gemilut chasadim*?

The *gemara* answers: *himnuha hu dehavei meheiman, ma'avad lo avad* — he could be trusted, but perhaps he didn't actually give his own money; perhaps Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov was merely using him as an illustrative standard of trustworthiness, not claiming he personally ran a *tzedakah* operation.

But a *baraita* then recalls that Rabbi Chananya once said: *ma'os shel Purim nitchalfu li b'ma'os tzedakah v'chilaktim l'aniyim* — I accidentally confused money collected for *Purim* gifts with *tzedakah* funds and distributed it to the poor, and I made up the difference from my own pocket. So he clearly was engaged in *tzedakah* — both in distributing funds and in contributing personally. The *gemara* concludes: *ma'avad avad, k'debayei lei lo avad* — yes, he acted, but he did not act to the full extent of what he was capable. He fell short of his potential in *gemilut chasadim*, and it is for *that* shortfall that he is held accountable. This is a demanding standard: the question is not merely whether one did kindness, but whether one did all the kindness one could have done.

## Ben Prata Trial

### Sage vs. Thief

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְיוּהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא, אֲמַרוּ: מַאי טַעְמָא תָּנֵית, וּמַאי טַעְמָא גָּנְבַתְּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי סַיָּיפָא לָא סָפְרָא, וְאִי סָפְרָא לָא סַיָּיפָא, וּמִדְּהָא לֵיתַהּ הָא נָמֵי לֵיתַהּ. וּמַאי טַעְמָא קָרוּ לָךְ ״רַבִּי״? רַבָּן שֶׁל תַּרְסִיִּים אֲנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the description of the trial of the Sages. The Romans **brought Rabbi Elazar ben Perata** for his trial and **said: What is the reason** that **you taught** Torah, **and what is the reason** that **you stole,** as these were the crimes of which he was accused. Rabbi Elazar ben Perata **said to them: If one is an armed robber [*sayafa*], he is not a scholar [*safra*], and if one is a scholar he is not an armed robber,** i.e., I am accused of two mutually exclusive crimes; **and from** the fact **that this** characterization **is not** true, one may also conclude that **that** characterization **is also not** true. They asked him: **But** if you do not teach Torah, then **what is the reason** that **they call you rabbi?** He answered: **I am the master [*rabban*] of weavers [*tarsiyyim*].**

*Atyu lei l'Rabbi Eleazar ben Perata* — they brought Rabbi Elazar ben Perata before the Roman court. The *gemara* has already told us — from Rabbi Chananya's prophecy — that despite the five charges, Rabbi Elazar will be saved. The dramatic interest therefore lies not in suspense but in observing how each charge collapses in turn. They asked him: *mai ta'ama tanita?* — why have you been teaching the outlawed *mishnah*, rabbinic literature? And *mai ta'ama ganavta?* — why did you steal?

Rabbi Elazar's response to the dual charge is one of the most memorable lines in the *masechet*: *isayfa lo safra, v'isafra lo sayfa* — if the sword, then not the book; if the book, then not the sword. A man cannot be both a Torah scholar and a thief. These charges are mutually exclusive, and since at least one of them must be fabricated, both are suspect. *U'midahach la sa anami la* — since one is false, the other is false as well. When they pressed — *mai ta'ama karu lach Rabbi?* — why are you called *Rabbi*? — he replied: *Rabban shel tarsiyim* — I am a master of weavers, a craftsman, not a sage. Oh, they said — then identify these spindles.

### Bei Avidan Excuse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיְיתוֹ לֵיהּ תְּרֵי קִיבּוּרֵי, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הֵי דְּשִׁתְיָא וְהֵי דְּעִרְבָּא? אִיתְרְחִישׁ לֵיהּ נִיסָּא, (אָתְיָא) [אֲתַאי] זִיבּוּרִיתָא אִיתִּיב עַל דְּשִׁתְיָא, וַאֲתָאי זִיבּוּרָא וִיתֵיב עַל דְּעִרְבָּא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַאי דְּשִׁתְיָא וְהַאי דְּעִרְבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** In order to ascertain whether Rabbi Elazar ben Perata was in fact an expert weaver, **they brought him two coils** of wool and **said to him: Which is** the **warp, and which is** the **woof?** The threads used for each differ in their thickness and strength and would be immediately recognizable to an expert. **A miracle occurred,** as **a female hornet came** and **sat on** the coil **of warp, and a male hornet came and sat on** the coil **of woof.** Rabbi Elazar ben Perata **said to them: This** is a coil **of warp, and that** is a coil **of woof.** He realized that the male hornet was a sign that the coil was the woof, as the woof is threaded through the warp, while the warp, which is fixed in the loom and receives the woof, was the one on which the female hornet sat, as the female of a species receives the male.

Charge three: *mai ta'ama lo asis le-vei avidan?* — why did you not come to *Bei Avidan*, the Roman house of theological disputation? Rabbi Elazar answered: *zaken ani ve-hayiti mistafina she-ma tirmisuni be-raglechem* — I am old and frail and feared that in the crowded assembly I would be trampled underfoot. They objected: have you ever heard of an elder being trampled at *Bei Avidan*? At that very moment a miracle occurred: *israchish nisa ha-hu yoma irmis chad sava* — that day, one old man was in fact trampled at the assembly. The charge collapsed.

### Slave Freedom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וּמַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָתֵית לְבֵי אֲבִידָן? אֲמַר לְהוּ: זָקֵן הָיִיתִי וּמִתְיָירֵא אֲנִי שֶׁמָּא תִּרְמְסוּנִי בְּרַגְלֵיכֶם. [אֲמַרוּ]: וְעַד הָאִידָּנָא כַּמָּה סָבֵי אִיתְרְמוּס? אִתְרְחִישׁ נִיסָּא, הַהוּא יוֹמָא אִירְמַס חַד סָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Romans **said to him: And what is the reason** that **you did not come to the house of Abidan?** This was a gathering place where debates on wisdom and faith were conducted. Rabbi Elazar ben Perata **said to them: I was old and feared** that **perhaps I would be trampled under your feet,** due to the huge crowds. The Romans **said: And until now, how many elders have been trampled** there, that you would be worried about such a possibility? The Gemara comments: **A miracle occurred,** and on **that day, one old man was trampled.**

Charge four: *mai ta'ama kashvakt avdach le-cheirus?* — why did you free your slave? This may relate to the question of whether Jews were still observing the manumission requirements of *shemitah* or *yovel* in that period, practices which the Romans may have sought to suppress. Rabbi Elazar denied it: *lo hayu devarim me-olam* — it never happened. One of the Romans rose to testify against him. Elijah appeared, disguised as a senior Roman official, and warned the would-be witness: *mi-de-israchish lei nisa be-kuleihu* — given that miracles have occurred for him in everything so far — *u-ve-ha nami israchish lei nisa* — a miracle will occur here too, and *ve-duvra bishusa hu de-ka machzes* — you will find yourself shamed. The witness did not listen.

### Witness Sent Away

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וּמַאי טַעְמָא קָא שָׁבְקַתְּ עַבְדָּךְ לְחֵירוּת? אֲמַר לְהוּ: לֹא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם. קָם חַד [מִינַּיְיהוּ] לְאַסְהוֹדֵי בֵּיהּ, אֲתָא אֵלִיָּהוּ אִידְּמִי לֵיהּ כְּחַד מֵחַשִּׁובֵי דְּמַלְכוּתָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדְּאִתְרְחִישׁ לֵיהּ נִיסָּא בְּכוּלְּהוּ, בְּהָא נָמֵי אִתְרְחִישׁ לֵיהּ נִיסָּא, וְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא בִּישׁוּתֵיהּ הוּא דְּקָא (אַחְוִי) [מַחְוֵי].
>
> **Translation:** The Romans asked Rabbi Elazar ben Perata: **And what is the reason** that **you emancipated your slave?** Rabbi Elazar ben Perata **said to them: This matter never happened. One of them stood to testify against him,** and **Elijah came disguised as one of the of the Roman noblemen,** and he **said to** that individual: **From** the fact **that miracles occurred for** Rabbi Elazar ben Perata **in every** other case, **in this** instance **as well a miracle will occur for him, and that man,** i.e., you, **is** only **demonstrating his wickedness,** since you cannot succeed in your aim and are merely showing yourself to be desperate to cause harm.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אַשְׁגַּח בֵּיהּ, קָם לְמֵימַר לְהוּ. הֲוָה כְּתִיבָא אִיגַּרְתָּא, דַּהֲוָה כְּתִיב מֵחַשִּׁיבֵי דְּמַלְכוּת לְשַׁדּוֹרֵי לְבֵי קֵיסָר, וְשַׁדְּרוּהָא עַל יְדֵיהּ דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא. אֲתָא אֵלִיָּהוּ פַּתְקֵיהּ אַרְבַּע מְאָה פַּרְסֵי, אֲזַל וְלָא אֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But** the man **paid him no heed,** and he **stood to say** his testimony **to them.** At that time **there was a written letter that was composed by** some **of the** most **important people of** the Roman **Empire** in order **to send** it **to the Emperor's court, and they** had **sent it in the possession of that man,** i.e., the potential witness. **Elijah came** and **threw it** a distance of **four hundred parasangs.** The man **went and did not come** back, and therefore all the charges against Rabbi Elazar ben Perata were dropped.

As the witness rose to testify, an urgent letter needed to be delivered to Caesar in Rome. *Shadruha al yadeih de-hahu gavra* — they dispatched it by the hand of that very witness. *Asa eliyahu, idmi lei ke-chad mi-chashivei de-malchusa* — Elijah appeared to him on the journey, this time again as a Roman dignitary, and *patkei arba me'ah parsei* — threw him four hundred *parasangs* away. *Azal ve-lo asa* — he went, and he did not return — a phrase that faintly echoes *kol ba'eha lo yeshuvun*, all who go in do not return. Charge five is thus resolved through Elijah's intervention, and Rabbi Elazar ben Prata emerges from all five charges unscathed.
