# Nedarim 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## B'Ikaro vs. B'Hashta

### Shelamim After Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: "הֲרֵי עָלַי כִּבְשַׂר זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים לְאַחַר זְרִיקַת דָּמִים", מַהוּ? אִי דְּקָאָמַר בְּהָדֵין לִישָּׁנָא — בְּהֶיתֵּרָא קָא מַתְפֵּיס! אֶלָּא: כְּגוֹן דְּמַחֵית בְּשַׂר זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים, וּמַחֵית דְּהֶיתֵּרָא גַּבֵּיהּ, וְאָמַר: "זֶה כָּזֶה", מַאי: בְּעִיקָּרוֹ קָא מַתְפֵּיס, אוֹ בְּהֶיתֵּרָא קָא מַתְפֵּיס?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raises a dilemma:** If one said with regard to a particular item: **This** is prohibited **to me** like **the meat of peace-offerings after the sprinkling of** their **blood, what is** the *halakha*? **If he said it with this formulation, he is associating** the object of his vow **with a permitted** item, as the meat of peace-offerings is permitted to be eaten after the blood is sprinkled on the altar. Consequently, the declaration does not express a vow. **Rather,** it is a case **where he places the meat of peace-offerings** following the sprinkling of the blood in one place, **and he places** an item **that is permitted next to it. And he says: This is like that.** In this case, **what** is the *halakha*? **Is he associating** the object of his vow **with the original** forbidden status of the peace-offering before the blood is sprinkled, **or is he associating** the object of his vow **with the** current **permitted** status of the peace-offering?

The *daf* opens by picking up from the bottom of 11b, where Rami bar Chama poses a question about a case in which someone says: "This should be forbidden to me like the meat of a *shelamim* after *zrikat hadam*." The Gemara immediately objects: after *zrikat hadam*, a *shelamim* is permissible to be eaten — so the speaker has explicitly invoked something that is not in a forbidden state, making the question moot. The Gemara therefore reformulates the case. The man has before him a piece of *shelamim* meat after the blood has been sprinkled — he is in fact eating from it — and he gestures at a loaf of bread and says *zeh kazeh*, "this is like this." The question is: when he says "this," to what status of the *shelamim* is he referring?

Two possibilities present themselves. Either he refers to the object's current state (*b'hashta kamatpis*), in which case the *shelamim* meat is presently permitted and therefore not a *davar hanadur* in a forbidden sense, and the *neder* would have no effect. Or, he refers to the object's original state (*b'ikaro kamatpis*) — meaning the moment he originally sanctified the animal as a *shelamim*, when it was indeed a *davar hanadur* in a fully forbidden state — in which case the *neder* would take effect.

### Notar & Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: נוֹתָר וּפִיגּוּל. וְהָא נוֹתָר וּפִיגּוּל לְאַחַר זְרִיקַת דָּמִים הוּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן: בְּנוֹתָר שֶׁל עוֹלָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, לִיתְנֵי בִּבְשַׂר עוֹלָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: If he said the food shall be considered an offering that has become ***notar* or *piggul*,** i.e. an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to consume it after its appointed time, it is forbidden. **But *notar* and *piggul* are** conditions that apply **after the sprinkling of the blood,** when the prohibition against misusing consecrated property, which the individual is attempting to extend to a permitted item, no longer applies. Since the vow takes effect, this proves that the individual is associating the object of his vow with the original forbidden status of the offering. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said to him:** It is possible to say that it is speaking here about ***notar* of a burnt-offering.** Since a burnt-offering may not be eaten even after its blood is sprinkled, the original prohibition against misusing consecrated property continues to apply to the meat of this offering. Rava **said to** Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan: **If so, let it teach** explicitly that the individual referred **to the meat of a burnt-offering.**

Rava responds: come and hear. Our *Mishnah* lists *notar* and *pigul* as valid objects of *matpis b'davar hanadur*. The *Mishnah* states that one may declare "behold this is like *notar*" or "behold this is like *pigul*," and this constitutes an effective *neder*. Crucially, both *notar* and *pigul* arise only *after zrikat hadam*. *Notar* is meat from a *korban* that was permitted for eating during the designated time, but which the owner left past the deadline; it is now forbidden by the Torah as *notar*, a prohibition entirely distinct from the original *neder*. The *pigul* prohibition — arising from an improper thought (*machshava*) during the *shechita* or *zrika* — conceptually precedes the *zrika* even if it is identified *l'achar zrikat hadam*, since the disqualifying *machshava* prevents the *zrika* from ever rendering the meat permitted. The *rishonim* therefore either excise *pigul* from the proof or treat it as *lav davka*, taking the operative case to be *notar*.

The significance of *notar* is this: during the period when the meat was permitted, the *neder* was not operative upon it — it was *mutar*. The *issur notar* that now applies to it is not a product of the original *neder*; rather, it is a distinct Torah prohibition comparable to *basar b'chalav*. As such, in its current state, the *shelamim* meat that became *notar* is a *davar ha'assur*, not a *davar hanadur*. Yet the *Mishnah* treats "behold this is like *notar*" as an effective *neder*. This implies that when one says "*zeh kazeh*," he is understood as referring back to the object's original state as *shelamim* — *b'ikaro kamatpis* — when it was indeed a fully operative *davar hanadur*. The word "*notar*" identifies the object; what the speaker intends is the status it held before it became permitted.

One might ask: why does the nature of *pigul* matter here? *Pigul* is indeed a function of *machshava* — the wrong intention at the time of the sacrificial act distorts the status of the offering, rendering it perpetually forbidden. The *rishonim* note that since *pigul* never passed through a state of being *mutar*, it is in a different category from *notar*, and the *Mishnah*'s mention of it alongside *notar* is not essential to the question of *b'ikaro* versus *b'hashta*.

### Notar Shel Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא "בְּשַׂר עוֹלָה" דְּאָסוּר, דְּהָא בְּקׇרְבָּן קָא מַתְפֵּיס, נוֹתָר וּפִיגּוּל דְּעוֹלָה אִיצְטְרִיכָא, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא כְּאִיסּוּר נוֹתָר, כְּאִיסּוּר פִּיגּוּל, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּמַתְפֵּיס בְּדָבָר הָאָסוּר, וְלָא מִיתְּסַר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The *tanna* **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** to state that if one **associates** the object of his vow with **meat of a burnt-offering,** it is **forbidden, as he extends** the status of **an offering** to the other item. However, if one extends the status of ***notar* and *piggul* of a burnt-offering, it is necessary to say** that the other item is forbidden. It could **enter your mind to say** that the individual intended to declare the item forbidden **like the prohibition of *notar*** or **like the prohibition of *piggul*, and this would be like one who associates** the object of his vow with **an item that is forbidden** by the Torah rather than an item forbidden by means of a vow. Consequently, the item **is not forbidden,** as one can take a vow by associating the object of his vow with a forbidden item only if that item is itself prohibited due to a vow. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that his intention is to transfer the prohibition of the offering, and the vow takes effect.

Rav Huna bei d'Rav Nasan objects to the proof from *notar*: perhaps the *Mishnah* is specifically discussing *notar shel olah*. An *olah* is never eaten by anyone — even after *zrikat hadam* it remains entirely forbidden to lay persons, and that prohibition is a direct consequence of the *neder* by which it was sanctified. Thus *notar shel olah* never passes through a stage of being permitted; it remains a *davar hanadur* in its forbidden state at all times. If so, one could invoke *notar shel olah* in its current state and still be *matpis b'davar hanadur* without needing to go back to an earlier status at all. This would mean the *Mishnah* offers no proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis* — it might be entirely consistent with *b'hashta kamatpis*, so long as there exists some scenario in which the word "*notar*" describes an object currently forbidden as a *davar hanadur*.

Furthermore, the principle of *stam nedarim l'hachmir* — that an ambiguous *neder* is interpreted stringently — would lead us to read a bare utterance of "behold this is like *notar*" as referring to *notar shel olah*, the stricter category, rather than to *notar shel shelamim*, the lenient one. If, however, the speaker specified "behold this is like *notar shel shelamim*," we would interpret his words according to the current state of the object, which is no longer forbidden as a *davar hanadur*, and the *neder* would not take effect.

### Why Call It Notar?

The Gemara raises a pointed question against this reading: if the concern is the *olah* category, why say "*notar shel olah*" at all? Why not simply say "*basar olah*" — the meat of an *olah*? There is no question that "behold this is like an *olah*" constitutes *matpis b'davar hanadur*; the animal is sanctified through your speech act and is entirely forbidden. What is added by labeling it as *notar*?

### Resolution on Olah

The Gemara answers: *lo mibaya ka'amar* — it goes without saying that "behold this is like an *olah*" is forbidden, since you are explicitly being *matpis b'korban*, invoking a straightforwardly forbidden *davar hanadur*. The *chiddush* is precisely the case of "*notar* of an *olah*." One might have thought that by saying "*notar*," the speaker emphasizes the *issur notar* dimension of the object rather than its *korban* dimension — and the *issur notar* is not a *davar hanadur* but a *davar ha'assur* — and therefore the *neder* should not take effect. The Gemara teaches us that this is not so: since the object is still an *olah*, since it never passed through a stage of being permitted, the invocation of its *notar* status merely emphasizes a layer of its *issur*, but the speaker is understood as being *matpis* in its *davar hanadur* dimension.

The analogy is instructive. If one were to say "behold this is like *basar b'chalav* of an *olah*," one would clearly be invoking the *basar b'chalav* prohibition — a *davar ha'assur* — and the *neder* would not take effect even though the underlying object is an *olah*. By contrast, "*notar shel olah*" is so deeply intertwined with the *korban* identity of the *olah* that we read it as invoking the *davar hanadur* status. Whether this analysis applies only when the speaker explicitly says "*notar shel olah*," or whether a bare statement of "*notar*" triggers *stam nedarim l'hachmir* to read it as *notar shel olah*, remains a point worth considering, though the Gemara does not resolve this definitively.

The broader interpretive question underlying all of this concerns how we determine what a speaker meant by his words. The answer, as this tractate makes clear throughout, is that it does not primarily matter what the speaker privately intended — it matters what the words objectively mean. A *neder* requires not merely intent but the correct verbal formulation, and even the speaker himself is bound by the meaning of his words as assessed by *halacha*, not by whatever he may have privately meant. If a statement has multiple possible meanings, the task is to determine which *halachic* meaning the words carry — and *stam nedarim l'hachmir* is the operative principle for resolving ambiguity. This is why, in many of these cases, we do not simply ask the speaker; even if we did, the answer could not override the *halachic* reading of what was said.

## Taanit & Yahrtzeit

### Meat & Wine Neder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵיזֶהוּ אִיסָּר הָאָמוּר בְּתוֹרָה? אָמַר: "הֲרֵינִי שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל בָּשָׂר וְשֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה יַיִן כַּיּוֹם שֶׁמֵּת בּוֹ אָבִיו", "כַּיּוֹם שֶׁמֵּת בּוֹ רַבּוֹ", "כַּיּוֹם שֶׁנֶּהֱרַג בּוֹ גְּדַלְיָה בֶּן אֲחִיקָם", "כַּיּוֹם שֶׁרָאִיתִי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם בְּחוּרְבָּנָהּ", וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְהוּא שֶׁנָּדוּר בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based upon the following *baraita*: **Which is the** vow of **prohibition [*issar*] mentioned in the Torah?** It is a case where **one said: I hereby** declare **that I will not eat meat and I will not drink wine** today **like the day his father died,** referring to the father of the individual making the vow, as there is a custom to fast on the anniversary of a parent's death, or: **Like the day his teacher died,** as one mourns his primary teacher like a father, or: **Like the day Gedaliah, son of Ahikam, was killed** (see Jeremiah, chapter 41), i.e., the Fast of Gedaliah, or: **Like the day I saw Jerusalem in its** state of **destruction. And Shmuel said: And this is if he was obligated by** a previous **vow** to abstain from meat and wine **on that day** that he refers to in his declaration.

The Gemara now introduces a *Mishnah* that brings a different kind of case to bear on the question of *b'ikaro* versus *b'hashta*. The *Mishnah* describes a person who says: "Behold I am [forbidden from eating] meat and drinking wine like the day on which my father died" (*k'yom shemeis bo aviv*), or like the day his *rebbe* died, or like the day Gedaliah ben Achikam was killed, or like the day he visited Yerushalayim and saw it in its destruction. The practice of accepting a *ta'anis* or forms of self-denial on a *yahrtzeit* is thus attested as a phenomenon going back to the earliest layers of the tradition.

This case introduces a conceptual abstraction. Whereas in the *shelamim* case the *davar hanadur* was a piece of meat, here the object of the *neder* appears to be a *day*. The model seems to parallel the case of *tzedakah*: just as money set aside for *tzedakah* acquires the status of a *davar hanadur*, and just as bread can become a *davar hanadur* through a declaration of *konam*, so too a day can be given a status of *ta'anis* through the spoken acceptance. The day itself becomes, in a sense, the *davar hanadur*. Five years ago, the man declared that a particular day — his father's *yahrtzeit*, or *Tzom Gedaliah* — was *assur alav* for eating meat and wine. That day thereby became a *davar hanadur*. Now, five years later, he says: "This [day] is like that day." He is being *matpis* in the *davar hanadur* of the earlier day.

### Shmuel's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? לָאו כְּגוֹן דְּקָאֵי בְּחַד בְּשַׁבָּא דְּמִית בֵּיהּ אֲבוּהּ, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא טוּבָא חַד בְּשַׁבָּא דְּהֶיתֵּרָא, וְקָתָנֵי אָסוּר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בְּעִיקָּר הוּא מַתְפֵּיס. דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, הָכִי אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְהוּא שֶׁנָּדוּר וּבָא מֵאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? Is it not** a case where, **for example, it was Sunday,** the same day of the week **on which his father died? And although there were many permitted Sundays** in the interim, nevertheless, when he said he would not eat meat or drink wine like the day of the week on which his father died, his intention was to the particular Sunday when his father died, when he had vowed to abstain from meat and wine, **and** therefore **the *tanna* teaches** that it is **prohibited. Learn from this that he associates** the object of his vow with **the original** halakhic status of the Sunday his father died, and not the status of the intervening Sundays. Similarly, in the case of peace-offerings following the sprinkling of the blood, he is referring to the original status of the meat before the sprinkling of the blood. The Gemara responds that **this is how** the comment **of Shmuel was stated: Shmuel said: And this is if he was continuously obligated by a vow from that day forward** to abstain from meat and wine on the anniversary of his father's death. Consequently, when he associates another day with the day of his father's death, he expresses a vow based upon the current status of the day, and there is no proof with regard to the case of the meat of the peace-offering.

Shmuel clarifies the *Mishnah*: *v'hu shenadar u'va m'oso hayom v'eilach* — the case is specifically one where he has been making this *neder* continuously from that original day forward. It is not that there was a single *neder* five years ago with four intervening years of non-*neder* status; rather, every year since his father's death he has accepted the same *neder* upon himself. Thus when he says on the current *yahrtzeit* "this is like my father's *yahrtzeit*," every instantiation of that day — including the most recent ones — carries the status of *davar hanadur*, and there is no ambiguity about which day he is invoking.

### Proof from Years

One might attempt to read the *Mishnah* as a proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis*: even if there were four intervening years in which the day passed without a *neder*, the speaker is still understood as invoking the *neder* status of the original day five years ago, going back past four years of *mutar* instantiations. The Gemara rejects this reading in light of Shmuel's clarification. Since Shmuel requires that the *neder* be ongoing — *shenadar u'va m'oso hayom v'eilach* — every year has been a *davar hanadur*, and there is no scenario in the *Mishnah* where the speaker is reaching back past years of *heter*. The *Mishnah* therefore offers no proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis*; it is perfectly consistent with *b'hashta kamatpis*, since in its current and ongoing state the day is a *davar hanadur*.

The conceptual elegance here is striking. The day of a *yahrtzeit* or *Tzom Gedaliah* cycles back each year as the same day with a potentially new or renewed status. When one says "this day is like that day," the question is which instantiation of the day one is invoking — the original *neder*-bearing instance, an intermediate *mutar* instance, or the most recent one. Shmuel's ruling forecloses the ambiguity by requiring that all instantiations share the same *davar hanadur* status.

### Neder on Prohibited

The Ran discusses what the *chiddush* of the *Mishnah* actually is, if the *Mishnah* presents no proof for or against *b'ikaro kamatpis*. The Gemara in *Shevuos* addresses this same passage and identifies the *chiddush* as follows: one might have thought that since *Tzom Gedaliah* is already *assur m'drabanan*, a *neder* accepted upon that day would not take effect — *ki nadar nami lo chayil neder*. Perhaps a *neder* cannot take hold on something already forbidden. The *Mishnah* teaches us that this is not so: even when a day is already forbidden by rabbinic enactment, a *neder* accepted upon that day does take effect. The person then bears both the rabbinic prohibition and the *neder* prohibition.

### Kraft Cheese Example

The Ran illustrates this with a practical example. If someone says "*neder konam* all [non-Jewish cheese] upon me" — even though such cheese is already *assur m'drabanan* — the *neder* takes effect and adds an additional layer of prohibition. One who thereafter eats such cheese violates not only the rabbinic prohibition but also his own *neder*. Consequently, if this same person were then to take a piece of permitted cheese and say "*harei zo* like [that non-Jewish cheese]," he would be *matpis b'davar hanadur* — piggybacking on the *neder* he had placed upon the first cheese — and the permitted cheese would also become forbidden. The Ran states explicitly: *v'hitpiso bahen d'matpis b'neder havei v'assur* — the *matpis* act upon something already *assur d'rabbanan* is effective, and a subsequent *zeh kazeh* on the *neder*-bearing object also takes effect.

## Teruma & Todah

### Teruma Permissible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: "כְּחַלַּת אַהֲרֹן", "וְכִתְרוּמָתוֹ" — מוּתָּר. הָא "כִּתְרוּמַת לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה" — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another attempted proof. **Ravina said: Come** and **hear** that which was taught in the mishna (13b): If one declares an item to be **like the *ḥalla* of Aaron,** i.e., the portion of dough given to the priests, **or like his *teruma*,** the portion of agricultural produce given to priests, the item remains **permitted.** Although these items are prohibited to non-priests as soon as they are designated, they are considered forbidden by the Torah rather than forbidden by a vow. The Gemara infers: **But** if one declares an item to be **like the *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks-offering,** i.e., the four loaves of the thanks-offering that were eaten by the priests, the item is **forbidden.**

The Gemara cites a further case from the *Mishnah*: *k'chalas Aharon u'k'trumaso mutar* — if one says "behold this is forbidden to me like the *challah* given to the *kohanim*" or like *teruma* given to the *kohanim*, it is *mutar*. Even though *teruma* is a *davar hanadur* — it acquires its status through the verbal act of separation — it is not fully *assur* to all people; *kohanim* may eat it freely. Since *teruma* does not carry an absolute prohibition flowing from the *neder*, it cannot serve as an effective anchor for *matpis b'davar hanadur*. For *matpis b'davar hanadur* to work, the *davar hanadur* must also be something that is fully *assur*. The Ran notes the distinction: a *davar ha'assur* does not take effect as an anchor for *matpis* because its prohibition is not *neder*-derived; a *davar hanadur* that is only partially forbidden likewise fails to serve as an anchor. Both aspects — *nadur* and *assur* — must be fully present.

### Lachmei Todah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תְּרוּמַת לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה — לְאַחַר זְרִיקַת דָּמִים הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Having made this inference, Ravina comments: **But the *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks-offering is** designated only **after the sprinkling of the blood.** Consequently, the individual must have made his vow after the sprinkling of the blood, when these loaves are permitted to priests. If the vow nevertheless takes effect, it must be because the individual is referring to the original forbidden status of the loaves before the sprinkling of the blood. This indicates that one has in mind the original status of the item rather than its current status.

The *Mishnah* then continues: *ha k'trumas lachmei todah assur* — if, however, one says "behold this is like the *terumah* of *lachmei todah*," it is *assur*. The *terumas lachmei todah* is the loaf separated from the forty loaves brought as a *todah* offering. Someone who brings a *todah* brings forty loaves: thirty *matzah* of three different types, and ten *chametz*. From each group of ten, one loaf is given to the *kohen* — this separated portion is called *terumas lachmei todah* and carries a status analogous to *teruma*, restricted to *kohanim*.

The *todah* offering presents a case directly analogous to the *shelamim* after *zrikat hadam*: by the time the *terumas lachmei todah* is separated and given to the *kohen*, the blood has already been sprinkled and the bread is now permitted to *kohanim*. It is no longer in a forbidden state. Yet the *Mishnah* rules that invoking *terumas lachmei todah* renders the *neder* effective — apparently because we look back to its earlier status, before *zrikat hadam*, when it bore the *kedushah* of a *korban* and would have involved *me'ilah* to eat. This seems to be a proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis*: we attribute the object's earlier forbidden status to the invocation, even though its current status is permitted.

### Separation Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: "כִּתְרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה" — אָסוּר. אֲבָל תְּרוּמַת לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה מַאי — מוּתָּר? לִיתְנֵי לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן תְּרוּמָתוֹ! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן תְּרוּמַת לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה — תְּרוּמָתוֹ הִיא. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: תְּרוּמַת לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה נָמֵי קוֹדֶם זְרִיקַת דָּמִים הוּא. כְּגוֹן דְּאַפְרְשִׁינְהוּ בְּלֵישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this: **Say** that when the mishna specifies that if one said the food should be like the *teruma* of Aaron it remains permitted it thereby indicates that if he said it should be **like the collection of the** Temple treasury **chamber,** which is also called a *teruma* and is always forbidden, the food becomes **forbidden.** However, one cannot infer from the mishna that if one declares the food to be like the *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks-offering the food becomes forbidden. The Gemara asks: **But** according to this, if one said the food should be like **the *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks-offering, what,** does the food remain **permitted?** If so, **let it teach** in the mishna that if one said the food should be like the *teruma* of **the loaves of the thanks-offering** it remains permitted even though the thanks-offering is forbidden by means of a vow, **and** one would know on his own that if he says the food should be like the ***teruma* of** Aaron, **all the more so** the food remains permitted. The Gemara responds: It **teaches us this: *Teruma* of the loaves of the thanks-offering** is also referred to as **his *teruma*** and is therefore included in the mishna. The Gemara offers an alternative response to Ravina's attempted proof from the case of the *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks-offering, that one has in mind the original status of an item when one expresses a vow. **And if you wish, say** that the ***teruma* of the loaves of the thanks-offering** can **also** be designated **before the sprinkling of the blood, for example, when he separated** the *teruma* **during the kneading** of the dough. Consequently, the case may be where one makes the vow before the sprinkling of the blood, when the loaves are forbidden to all, and that is the reason the vow takes effect.

The Gemara raises a complication: is *terumas lachmei todah* truly always only found after *zricat hadam*? In fact, it is possible to separate the *terumah* from the loaves before the blood is sprinkled. Moreover, the Gemara introduces an even more exotic scenario through a teaching of Rabbi Tuvi bar Kisna in the name of Shmuel. If someone baked his forty loaves as four enormous loaves — one of each of the four types, each the size of ten — he has fulfilled his obligation (*yatza*), though this is only a *lechatchila* deficiency, since the Torah specifies forty. However, the practical problem now arises: how does one separate the *terumas lachmei todah*? One cannot take one of the four gigantic loaves as the *terumah* for all the others, since *one must take one from each type separately* and may not substitute one type for another. Nor can one break off a partial loaf (*prusa*), since the requirement is for a whole unit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הָא דְּאָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר קִיסְנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה שֶׁאֲפָאָן בְּאַרְבַּע חַלּוֹת — יָצָא. וְהָכְתִיב אַרְבָּעִים! לְמִצְוָה. וְהָא בָּעֵי לְמִשְׁקַל תְּרוּמָה! וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּשָׁקֵיל חֲדָא רִיפְתָּא עַל כּוּלַּהּ — וְהָתְנַן: אֶחָד מִכׇּל קׇרְבָּן. שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל מִקׇּרְבָּן עַל חֲבֵירוֹ! וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּשָׁקֵיל פְּרוּסָה מִכׇּל חַד וְחַד, וְהָתְנַן: "אֶחָד", שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל פְּרוּסָה! אֶלָּא דְּאַפְרְשִׁינְהוּ בְּלֵישָׁה. דְּשָׁקֵיל חֲדָא מֵחָמֵץ, וַחֲדָא מִן חַלּוֹת, וַחֲדָא מִן רְקִיקִים, וַחֲדָא מִן רְבוּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **in accordance with that which Rav Tovi bar Kisna said** that **Shmuel said:** If **one baked the loaves of the thanks-offering as four loaves** rather than the forty loaves that should ideally be baked, **he has fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara asks: **Isn't it written** that **forty** loaves must be brought with the thanks-offering, ten loaves of each of the four different types? The Gemara answers: One must bake forty loaves in order **to** fulfill the **mitzva** in the optimal fashion, but he has nevertheless fulfilled his obligation with four loaves, one of each type. The Gemara asks: **But he is required to take *teruma*,** i.e., designate one loaf of each type to be given to the priests. **And if you would say that he takes one** loaf of **bread** of the four as *teruma* **for all** the others, **but didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Menaḥot* 77b) with regard to the verse "And of it he shall present **one out of each offering** for a gift to the Lord; it shall be the priest's" (Leviticus 7:14), that it indicates **that he should not take from one offering,** i.e., one type of loaf, **for another? And if you would say that he takes a slice from each one** of the four loaves and gives them to the priest, **but didn't we learn** in that mishna that the word **one** in the verse indicates **that he may not take a slice** but rather a complete loaf? **Rather,** it must be **that he separated** the *teruma* **during** the time of **kneading. He took one** piece of dough **from the leavened bread, one from the loaves, one from the wafers, and one from the flour mixed with water and oil.** After separating one tenth of each type of dough for the priest, he then baked the remainder into four loaves. Since it is possible to separate the *teruma* at the time of the kneading, before the sprinkling of the blood of the offering, it is possible that the case is one where he expressed the vow at this time. Consequently, there is no proof that one has in mind the original status of an offering rather than its current status when one expresses a vow after the sprinkling of the blood.

The only solution is *d'aphrashinhu b'lisha* — one separates the dough designated as *terumah* while still kneading it. One takes a portion of the *chametz* dough and sets it aside, and similarly for each of the three *matzah* types — the *chalot*, the *rekikin*, and the *revucha*. The remaining dough of each type is then combined into a single large loaf. This demonstrates that it is possible — if unusual — for *terumas lachmei todah* to be separated before *zricat hadam*, even at the stage of raw dough.

### B'Hashta Interpretation

The Gemara therefore reinterprets the *Mishnah*'s ruling on *terumas lachmei todah*. Rather than a proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis*, the case can be explained as *b'hashta kamatpis* applied with maximal stringency. When one says "behold this is like *terumas lachmei todah*," we interpret the words as invoking *terumas lachmei todah* in its current status as a *davar hanadur* — meaning the unusual but extant scenario of *terumas lachmei todah lif'nei zricat hadam*, dough-stage *terumah* separated before the blood has been sprinkled. Just as the bare invocation of "*notar*" is read stringently as *notar shel olah* — because that is a scenario in which *notar* is currently a *davar hanadur* — so too *terumas lachmei todah* is read as the case in which the *terumah* is currently forbidden, namely the pre-*zricat hadam* scenario. We are prepared, under *stam nedarim l'hachmir*, to find a reading of the speaker's words that places the object in a *currently* forbidden state as a *davar hanadur*, even if that scenario is unusual. What we may not be prepared to do is attribute to the speaker a reference to an *earlier* status that no longer obtains at all. The question of whether *b'ikaro kamatpis* is ever operative thus remains open, and the Gemara has not yet provided a conclusive proof for it.

## Bechor Debate

### Bechor Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: "הֲרֵי עָלַי כִּבְכוֹר", רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹסֵר, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this question, whether one intends to extend the original or current status of an offering, is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** If one says: **This** meat is prohibited **to me** like the meat of **a firstborn, Rabbi Ya'akov renders** the meat **forbidden and Rabbi Yehuda renders** it **permitted.**

The Gemara now raises a *tannaitic* dispute that may directly bear on the question. Rabbi Yaakov holds that saying "behold this is forbidden to me like a *bechor*" is *assur*, while Rabbi Yehuda holds it is *mutar*. The central question is whether a *bechor* qualifies as a *davar hanadur*.

### Bechor After Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי נֵימָא לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, מַאי טַעְמָא דְמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי. וְאִי לְאַחַר זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָסַר? אֶלָּא לָאו, דְּמַחֵית בְּשַׂר בְּכוֹר וּמַחֵית בָּשָׂר דְּהַאיְךְ גַּבֵּיהּ, וְאָמַר "זֶה כָּזֶה". וְתַנָּאֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say** that he associates the object of his vow with the status of a firstborn animal **before the sprinkling of the blood,** when it is forbidden as a consecrated item, **what is the rationale of the one who renders** it **permitted? And if** he associates the object of his vow with the status of a firstborn animal **after the sprinkling of the blood,** when it belongs to the priest and is permitted to be eaten, **what is the rationale of the one who renders** it **forbidden? Rather, is it not that he places the meat of a firstborn** animal in one place **and he places another** piece of **meat next to it, and he said: This** second piece of meat is hereby **like that** meat of the firstborn animal, **and it is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** about whether he is referring to the original forbidden status of the firstborn animal or its current permitted status?

The Gemara asks: what exactly is the case? If the reference is to a *bechor* before *zricat hadam* — while the animal is still alive and walking — why would Rabbi Yehuda permit? At that stage the *bechor* is a *korban* before *zricat hadam* and seems fully forbidden. And if the reference is to after *zricat hadam*, when the *bechor* has been slaughtered and its blood sprinkled and its meat is now permitted — why would Rabbi Yaakov forbid? The Gemara concludes that the case must be like the *shelamim* case: the meat of a *bechor* after *zricat hadam* is lying in front of the person, along with some other permitted food, and he says "*zeh kazeh*" — this permitted food is like this *bechor* meat. The dispute thus maps precisely onto the question of *b'ikaro* versus *b'hashta*.

### Davar Ha'Assur View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, וּמַאי טַעְמָא דְמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי — אָמַר קְרָא: "כִּי יִדֹּר", עַד שֶׁיִּדּוֹר בְּדָבָר הַנָּדוּר, לְאַפּוֹקֵי בְּכוֹר, דְּדָבָר הָאָסוּר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No, everyone** agrees that he is referring to the status of the animal **before the sprinkling of the blood, and what is the reason of the one who renders** it **permitted? The verse states: "When** a man **takes a vow"** (Numbers 30:3), which indicates that he has not done so **until he takes a vow with an item forbidden by means of a vow,** i.e., by extending the status of an item that itself was forbidden by a means of a vow. This comes **to exclude a firstborn, which is an item that is forbidden** by the Torah rather than by means of a vow; a firstborn animal is sacred simply because it is born first.

Rabbi Yehuda's permitting opinion rests on a fundamental claim about the nature of *bechor*: a *bechor* is a *davar ha'assur*, not a *davar hanadur*. The Torah requires invoking a *davar hanadur* for *matpis* to be effective (*ki yiddor neder*, the principle being that one must be *matpis b'davar hanadur*), and a *bechor* does not qualify because it does not derive its *kedushah* from your verbal act. A *bechor* is *kadosh* from the moment of birth — *kadosh mei-imei imo* — without any declaration on your part. Its sanctity is inherent and automatic, not the product of a *neder*. Therefore invoking "behold this is like a *bechor*" is *matpis b'davar ha'assur*, which does not take effect.

### Sanctification View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָסַר, אָמַר קְרָא: "לַה׳", לְרַבּוֹת דָּבָר הָאָסוּר. וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי, "לַה׳" מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמַתְפִּיס בְּחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם. וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם וּלְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַבְּכוֹר? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם שֶׁהוּא מַתְפִּיס בְּנֶדֶר, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֶת הַבְּכוֹר שֶׁהוּא קָדוֹשׁ מִמְּעֵי אִמּוֹ. וּמַאן דְּאָסַר, בְּכוֹר נָמֵי מַתְפִּיסוֹ בְּנֶדֶר הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא, מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אָמְרוּ: מִנַּיִן לְנוֹלַד בְּכוֹר בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, שֶׁמִּצְוָה לְהַקְדִּישׁוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "הַזָּכָר תַּקְדִּישׁ". וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי, כִּי לָא מַקְדֵּישׁ לֵיהּ מִי לָא מִיקַּדֵּישׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **And the one who renders** it **forbidden** holds that it is because **the verse states: "To the Lord"** (Numbers 30:3), which comes **to include** one who takes a vow by associating an object with an **item that is forbidden** by the Torah. The Gemara asks: **And the one who renders** it **permitted** based on the principle that one cannot take a vow by associating the item with an item that is forbidden by the Torah, **what does he do with** the expression **"to the Lord"?** The Gemara answers: **He requires** this expression **to** include the case of **one who associates** the object of his vow with **a sin-offering and a guilt-offering.** Although these offerings are obligatory rather than voluntary, and in that regard they are dissimilar to items forbidden by means of a vow, one can render another item forbidden by associating it with these offerings. The Gemara asks: **And what did you see** that led you **to include a sin-offering and a guilt-offering and to exclude the firstborn?** The Gemara answers: **I include the sin-offering and guilt-offering** due to the fact **that he associates** the object of his vow with an animal forbidden **by means of a vow,** as it is the individual who designates a particular animal for one of these offerings. **And I remove the firstborn, because it is sacred from its mother's womb. And the one who renders** it **forbidden** is of the opinion that one who says that a piece of meat shall be considered for him like the meat of a **firstborn also associates** the object of his vow with an item forbidden **by a vow, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **They said in the name of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **From where** is it derived **that** if **one has a firstborn** animal **born in his home** it is still **a mitzva to consecrate it? As it is stated: "The males you shall consecrate"** (Deuteronomy 15:19). Although a firstborn animal is considered consecrated from birth, its owner is still commanded to declare it consecrated, and it is therefore considered an item forbidden by means of a vow. **And the one who renders** it **permitted** in this case argues that although the owner is required to declare it consecrated, **if he does not consecrate it, is it not consecrated?** Since it is consecrated in any event, it is considered to have been rendered forbidden by the Torah rather than through a vow.

Rabbi Yaakov's forbidding opinion does not deny that a *bechor* is *kadosh* from birth, but argues that a *bechor* is nonetheless a *davar hanadur* because there is a *mitzvah* to sanctify it. A *baraisa* teaches in the name of Rebbi: from where do we know that one born with a *bechor* in his house has a *mitzvah* to be *makdish* it? From the *pasuk* *hazachar takdish* — "you shall sanctify the male." Even though the animal is already *kadosh*, there is an obligation to add one's own verbal *hakdasha* to it. This means that a *bechor* also carries a *neder*-derived dimension of *kedushah*, and therefore it falls within the category of *davar hanadur*.

### Sanctity Discussion

Rabbi Yehuda's response to this argument is telling: *ki lo makdish leh mi lo mikdash* — even if one never sanctifies it, is it not already sanctified? The *mitzvah* of *takdish* is real, but its non-performance does not deprive the *bechor* of its *kedushah*. The fundamental identity of the *bechor* as a *korban* is not contingent on your words — it is inherent from birth. Whatever *kedushah* you add through your verbal sanctification is supplementary, not constitutive. The *bechor*'s basic identity is therefore that of a *davar ha'assur*, not a *davar hanadur*, and invoking it does not create an effective *neder*.

Rabbi Yaakov, for his part, could respond that even if the *bechor*'s inherent *kedushah* persists without your declaration, the fact that one's words *do* add a genuine dimension of *kedushah* is sufficient to classify it as a *davar hanadur* for purposes of *matpis*. The added human *kedushah* is not merely declaratory; it is real, and this reality places the *bechor* in the *neder* category. The rebuttal, however, is that this added dimension is not the central or essential identity of the *bechor* as a *korban* — and it is therefore insufficient to transform it from a fundamentally *ha'assur* object into a fundamentally *hanadur* one.

This distinction — between inherent sanctity and added sanctity, between *kedushah* that exists independently and *kedushah* that is constituted through human speech — carries profound implications well beyond *nedarim*. Rav Soloveitchik employed precisely this model in analyzing the *mitzvah* of *kiddush* on *Shabbos*. *Shabbos* possesses its own intrinsic *kedushah*, just as a *bechor* does. Yet there is a *mitzvah* of *kiddush* — to sanctify it with our words. The question is whether *kiddush* is merely a declaration acknowledging an existing reality, or whether it adds a genuinely human dimension of *kedushah* to what is already divinely given. Following the model of the *bechor*, one could argue that just as one has a *mitzvah lehakdisho* — to add one's own sanctification — even when the object already possesses independent *kedushah*, so too *kiddush* on *Shabbos* represents the human contribution of *kedushah* to the *kedushah* that *Shabbos* already bears in itself. This understanding of the *mitzvah* of *kiddush* — as the genuinely constitutive human act of sanctification rather than mere verbal acknowledgment — was, the lecturer notes, already anticipated by an earlier *rishon* who drew on this very comparison.
