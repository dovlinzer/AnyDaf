# Nedarim 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## B'Ikaro vs. B'Hashta

### Shelamim After Blood

The *daf* opens by picking up from the bottom of 11b, where Rami bar Chama poses a question about a case in which someone says: "This should be forbidden to me like the meat of a *shelamim* after *zrikat hadam*." The Gemara immediately objects: after *zrikat hadam*, a *shelamim* is permissible to be eaten — so the speaker has explicitly invoked something that is not in a forbidden state, making the question moot. The Gemara therefore reformulates the case. The man has before him a piece of *shelamim* meat after the blood has been sprinkled — he is in fact eating from it — and he gestures at a loaf of bread and says *zeh kazeh*, "this is like this." The question is: when he says "this," to what status of the *shelamim* is he referring?

Two possibilities present themselves. Either he refers to the object's current state (*b'hashta kamatpis*), in which case the *shelamim* meat is presently permitted and therefore not a *davar hanadur* in a forbidden sense, and the *neder* would have no effect. Or, he refers to the object's original state (*b'ikaro kamatpis*) — meaning the moment he originally sanctified the animal as a *shelamim*, when it was indeed a *davar hanadur* in a fully forbidden state — in which case the *neder* would take effect.

### Notar & Pigul

Rava responds: come and hear. Our *Mishnah* lists *notar* and *pigul* as valid objects of *matpis b'davar hanadur*. The *Mishnah* states that one may declare "behold this is like *notar*" or "behold this is like *pigul*," and this constitutes an effective *neder*. Crucially, both *notar* and *pigul* arise only *after zrikat hadam*. *Notar* is meat from a *korban* that was permitted for eating during the designated time, but which the owner left past the deadline; it is now forbidden by the Torah as *notar*, a prohibition entirely distinct from the original *neder*. The *pigul* prohibition — arising from an improper thought (*machshava*) during the *shechita* or *zrika* — conceptually precedes the *zrika* even if it is identified *l'achar zrikat hadam*, since the disqualifying *machshava* prevents the *zrika* from ever rendering the meat permitted. The *rishonim* therefore either excise *pigul* from the proof or treat it as *lav davka*, taking the operative case to be *notar*.

The significance of *notar* is this: during the period when the meat was permitted, the *neder* was not operative upon it — it was *mutar*. The *issur notar* that now applies to it is not a product of the original *neder*; rather, it is a distinct Torah prohibition comparable to *basar b'chalav*. As such, in its current state, the *shelamim* meat that became *notar* is a *davar ha'assur*, not a *davar hanadur*. Yet the *Mishnah* treats "behold this is like *notar*" as an effective *neder*. This implies that when one says "*zeh kazeh*," he is understood as referring back to the object's original state as *shelamim* — *b'ikaro kamatpis* — when it was indeed a fully operative *davar hanadur*. The word "*notar*" identifies the object; what the speaker intends is the status it held before it became permitted.

One might ask: why does the nature of *pigul* matter here? *Pigul* is indeed a function of *machshava* — the wrong intention at the time of the sacrificial act distorts the status of the offering, rendering it perpetually forbidden. The *rishonim* note that since *pigul* never passed through a state of being *mutar*, it is in a different category from *notar*, and the *Mishnah*'s mention of it alongside *notar* is not essential to the question of *b'ikaro* versus *b'hashta*.

### Notar Shel Olah

Rav Huna bei d'Rav Nasan objects to the proof from *notar*: perhaps the *Mishnah* is specifically discussing *notar shel olah*. An *olah* is never eaten by anyone — even after *zrikat hadam* it remains entirely forbidden to lay persons, and that prohibition is a direct consequence of the *neder* by which it was sanctified. Thus *notar shel olah* never passes through a stage of being permitted; it remains a *davar hanadur* in its forbidden state at all times. If so, one could invoke *notar shel olah* in its current state and still be *matpis b'davar hanadur* without needing to go back to an earlier status at all. This would mean the *Mishnah* offers no proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis* — it might be entirely consistent with *b'hashta kamatpis*, so long as there exists some scenario in which the word "*notar*" describes an object currently forbidden as a *davar hanadur*.

Furthermore, the principle of *stam nedarim l'hachmir* — that an ambiguous *neder* is interpreted stringently — would lead us to read a bare utterance of "behold this is like *notar*" as referring to *notar shel olah*, the stricter category, rather than to *notar shel shelamim*, the lenient one. If, however, the speaker specified "behold this is like *notar shel shelamim*," we would interpret his words according to the current state of the object, which is no longer forbidden as a *davar hanadur*, and the *neder* would not take effect.

### Why Call It Notar?

The Gemara raises a pointed question against this reading: if the concern is the *olah* category, why say "*notar shel olah*" at all? Why not simply say "*basar olah*" — the meat of an *olah*? There is no question that "behold this is like an *olah*" constitutes *matpis b'davar hanadur*; the animal is sanctified through your speech act and is entirely forbidden. What is added by labeling it as *notar*?

### Resolution on Olah

The Gemara answers: *lo mibaya ka'amar* — it goes without saying that "behold this is like an *olah*" is forbidden, since you are explicitly being *matpis b'korban*, invoking a straightforwardly forbidden *davar hanadur*. The *chiddush* is precisely the case of "*notar* of an *olah*." One might have thought that by saying "*notar*," the speaker emphasizes the *issur notar* dimension of the object rather than its *korban* dimension — and the *issur notar* is not a *davar hanadur* but a *davar ha'assur* — and therefore the *neder* should not take effect. The Gemara teaches us that this is not so: since the object is still an *olah*, since it never passed through a stage of being permitted, the invocation of its *notar* status merely emphasizes a layer of its *issur*, but the speaker is understood as being *matpis* in its *davar hanadur* dimension.

The analogy is instructive. If one were to say "behold this is like *basar b'chalav* of an *olah*," one would clearly be invoking the *basar b'chalav* prohibition — a *davar ha'assur* — and the *neder* would not take effect even though the underlying object is an *olah*. By contrast, "*notar shel olah*" is so deeply intertwined with the *korban* identity of the *olah* that we read it as invoking the *davar hanadur* status. Whether this analysis applies only when the speaker explicitly says "*notar shel olah*," or whether a bare statement of "*notar*" triggers *stam nedarim l'hachmir* to read it as *notar shel olah*, remains a point worth considering, though the Gemara does not resolve this definitively.

The broader interpretive question underlying all of this concerns how we determine what a speaker meant by his words. The answer, as this tractate makes clear throughout, is that it does not primarily matter what the speaker privately intended — it matters what the words objectively mean. A *neder* requires not merely intent but the correct verbal formulation, and even the speaker himself is bound by the meaning of his words as assessed by *halacha*, not by whatever he may have privately meant. If a statement has multiple possible meanings, the task is to determine which *halachic* meaning the words carry — and *stam nedarim l'hachmir* is the operative principle for resolving ambiguity. This is why, in many of these cases, we do not simply ask the speaker; even if we did, the answer could not override the *halachic* reading of what was said.

## Taanit & Yahrtzeit

### Meat & Wine Neder

The Gemara now introduces a *Mishnah* that brings a different kind of case to bear on the question of *b'ikaro* versus *b'hashta*. The *Mishnah* describes a person who says: "Behold I am [forbidden from eating] meat and drinking wine like the day on which my father died" (*k'yom shemeis bo aviv*), or like the day his *rebbe* died, or like the day Gedaliah ben Achikam was killed, or like the day he visited Yerushalayim and saw it in its destruction. The practice of accepting a *ta'anis* or forms of self-denial on a *yahrtzeit* is thus attested as a phenomenon going back to the earliest layers of the tradition.

This case introduces a conceptual abstraction. Whereas in the *shelamim* case the *davar hanadur* was a piece of meat, here the object of the *neder* appears to be a *day*. The model seems to parallel the case of *tzedakah*: just as money set aside for *tzedakah* acquires the status of a *davar hanadur*, and just as bread can become a *davar hanadur* through a declaration of *konam*, so too a day can be given a status of *ta'anis* through the spoken acceptance. The day itself becomes, in a sense, the *davar hanadur*. Five years ago, the man declared that a particular day — his father's *yahrtzeit*, or *Tzom Gedaliah* — was *assur alav* for eating meat and wine. That day thereby became a *davar hanadur*. Now, five years later, he says: "This [day] is like that day." He is being *matpis* in the *davar hanadur* of the earlier day.

### Shmuel's Explanation

Shmuel clarifies the *Mishnah*: *v'hu shenadar u'va m'oso hayom v'eilach* — the case is specifically one where he has been making this *neder* continuously from that original day forward. It is not that there was a single *neder* five years ago with four intervening years of non-*neder* status; rather, every year since his father's death he has accepted the same *neder* upon himself. Thus when he says on the current *yahrtzeit* "this is like my father's *yahrtzeit*," every instantiation of that day — including the most recent ones — carries the status of *davar hanadur*, and there is no ambiguity about which day he is invoking.

### Proof from Years

One might attempt to read the *Mishnah* as a proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis*: even if there were four intervening years in which the day passed without a *neder*, the speaker is still understood as invoking the *neder* status of the original day five years ago, going back past four years of *mutar* instantiations. The Gemara rejects this reading in light of Shmuel's clarification. Since Shmuel requires that the *neder* be ongoing — *shenadar u'va m'oso hayom v'eilach* — every year has been a *davar hanadur*, and there is no scenario in the *Mishnah* where the speaker is reaching back past years of *heter*. The *Mishnah* therefore offers no proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis*; it is perfectly consistent with *b'hashta kamatpis*, since in its current and ongoing state the day is a *davar hanadur*.

The conceptual elegance here is striking. The day of a *yahrtzeit* or *Tzom Gedaliah* cycles back each year as the same day with a potentially new or renewed status. When one says "this day is like that day," the question is which instantiation of the day one is invoking — the original *neder*-bearing instance, an intermediate *mutar* instance, or the most recent one. Shmuel's ruling forecloses the ambiguity by requiring that all instantiations share the same *davar hanadur* status.

### Neder on Prohibited

The Ran discusses what the *chiddush* of the *Mishnah* actually is, if the *Mishnah* presents no proof for or against *b'ikaro kamatpis*. The Gemara in *Shevuos* addresses this same passage and identifies the *chiddush* as follows: one might have thought that since *Tzom Gedaliah* is already *assur m'drabanan*, a *neder* accepted upon that day would not take effect — *ki nadar nami lo chayil neder*. Perhaps a *neder* cannot take hold on something already forbidden. The *Mishnah* teaches us that this is not so: even when a day is already forbidden by rabbinic enactment, a *neder* accepted upon that day does take effect. The person then bears both the rabbinic prohibition and the *neder* prohibition.

### Kraft Cheese Example

The Ran illustrates this with a practical example. If someone says "*neder konam* all [non-Jewish cheese] upon me" — even though such cheese is already *assur m'drabanan* — the *neder* takes effect and adds an additional layer of prohibition. One who thereafter eats such cheese violates not only the rabbinic prohibition but also his own *neder*. Consequently, if this same person were then to take a piece of permitted cheese and say "*harei zo* like [that non-Jewish cheese]," he would be *matpis b'davar hanadur* — piggybacking on the *neder* he had placed upon the first cheese — and the permitted cheese would also become forbidden. The Ran states explicitly: *v'hitpiso bahen d'matpis b'neder havei v'assur* — the *matpis* act upon something already *assur d'rabbanan* is effective, and a subsequent *zeh kazeh* on the *neder*-bearing object also takes effect.

## Teruma & Todah

### Teruma Permissible

The Gemara cites a further case from the *Mishnah*: *k'chalas Aharon u'k'trumaso mutar* — if one says "behold this is forbidden to me like the *challah* given to the *kohanim*" or like *teruma* given to the *kohanim*, it is *mutar*. Even though *teruma* is a *davar hanadur* — it acquires its status through the verbal act of separation — it is not fully *assur* to all people; *kohanim* may eat it freely. Since *teruma* does not carry an absolute prohibition flowing from the *neder*, it cannot serve as an effective anchor for *matpis b'davar hanadur*. For *matpis b'davar hanadur* to work, the *davar hanadur* must also be something that is fully *assur*. The Ran notes the distinction: a *davar ha'assur* does not take effect as an anchor for *matpis* because its prohibition is not *neder*-derived; a *davar hanadur* that is only partially forbidden likewise fails to serve as an anchor. Both aspects — *nadur* and *assur* — must be fully present.

### Lachmei Todah

The *Mishnah* then continues: *ha k'trumas lachmei todah assur* — if, however, one says "behold this is like the *terumah* of *lachmei todah*," it is *assur*. The *terumas lachmei todah* is the loaf separated from the forty loaves brought as a *todah* offering. Someone who brings a *todah* brings forty loaves: thirty *matzah* of three different types, and ten *chametz*. From each group of ten, one loaf is given to the *kohen* — this separated portion is called *terumas lachmei todah* and carries a status analogous to *teruma*, restricted to *kohanim*.

The *todah* offering presents a case directly analogous to the *shelamim* after *zrikat hadam*: by the time the *terumas lachmei todah* is separated and given to the *kohen*, the blood has already been sprinkled and the bread is now permitted to *kohanim*. It is no longer in a forbidden state. Yet the *Mishnah* rules that invoking *terumas lachmei todah* renders the *neder* effective — apparently because we look back to its earlier status, before *zrikat hadam*, when it bore the *kedushah* of a *korban* and would have involved *me'ilah* to eat. This seems to be a proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis*: we attribute the object's earlier forbidden status to the invocation, even though its current status is permitted.

### Separation Details

The Gemara raises a complication: is *terumas lachmei todah* truly always only found after *zrikat hadam*? In fact, it is possible to separate the *terumah* from the loaves before the blood is sprinkled. Moreover, the Gemara introduces an even more exotic scenario through a teaching of Rabbi Tuvi bar Kisna in the name of Shmuel. If someone baked his forty loaves as four enormous loaves — one of each of the four types, each the size of ten — he has fulfilled his obligation (*yatza*), though this is only a *lechatchila* deficiency, since the Torah specifies forty. However, the practical problem now arises: how does one separate the *terumas lachmei todah*? One cannot take one of the four gigantic loaves as the *terumah* for all the others, since *one must take one from each type separately* and may not substitute one type for another. Nor can one break off a partial loaf (*prusa*), since the requirement is for a whole unit.

The only solution is *d'aphrashinhu b'lisha* — one separates the dough designated as *terumah* while still kneading it. One takes a portion of the *chametz* dough and sets it aside, and similarly for each of the three *matzah* types — the *chalot*, the *rekikin*, and the *revucha*. The remaining dough of each type is then combined into a single large loaf. This demonstrates that it is possible — if unusual — for *terumas lachmei todah* to be separated before *zrikat hadam*, even at the stage of raw dough.

### B'Hashta Interpretation

The Gemara therefore reinterprets the *Mishnah*'s ruling on *terumas lachmei todah*. Rather than a proof for *b'ikaro kamatpis*, the case can be explained as *b'hashta kamatpis* applied with maximal stringency. When one says "behold this is like *terumas lachmei todah*," we interpret the words as invoking *terumas lachmei todah* in its current status as a *davar hanadur* — meaning the unusual but extant scenario of *terumas lachmei todah lif'nei zrikat hadam*, dough-stage *terumah* separated before the blood has been sprinkled. Just as the bare invocation of "*notar*" is read stringently as *notar shel olah* — because that is a scenario in which *notar* is currently a *davar hanadur* — so too *terumas lachmei todah* is read as the case in which the *terumah* is currently forbidden, namely the pre-*zrikat hadam* scenario. We are prepared, under *stam nedarim l'hachmir*, to find a reading of the speaker's words that places the object in a *currently* forbidden state as a *davar hanadur*, even if that scenario is unusual. What we may not be prepared to do is attribute to the speaker a reference to an *earlier* status that no longer obtains at all. The question of whether *b'ikaro kamatpis* is ever operative thus remains open, and the Gemara has not yet provided a conclusive proof for it.

## Bechor Debate

### Bechor Dispute

The Gemara now raises a *tannaitic* dispute that may directly bear on the question. Rabbi Yaakov holds that saying "behold this is forbidden to me like a *bechor*" is *assur*, while Rabbi Yehuda holds it is *mutar*. The central question is whether a *bechor* qualifies as a *davar hanadur*.

### Bechor After Blood

The Gemara asks: what exactly is the case? If the reference is to a *bechor* before *zrikat hadam* — while the animal is still alive and walking — why would Rabbi Yehuda permit? At that stage the *bechor* is a *korban* before *zrikat hadam* and seems fully forbidden. And if the reference is to after *zrikat hadam*, when the *bechor* has been slaughtered and its blood sprinkled and its meat is now permitted — why would Rabbi Yaakov forbid? The Gemara concludes that the case must be like the *shelamim* case: the meat of a *bechor* after *zrikat hadam* is lying in front of the person, along with some other permitted food, and he says "*zeh kazeh*" — this permitted food is like this *bechor* meat. The dispute thus maps precisely onto the question of *b'ikaro* versus *b'hashta*.

### Davar Ha'Assur View

Rabbi Yehuda's permitting opinion rests on a fundamental claim about the nature of *bechor*: a *bechor* is a *davar ha'assur*, not a *davar hanadur*. The Torah requires invoking a *davar hanadur* for *matpis* to be effective (*ki yiddor neder*, the principle being that one must be *matpis b'davar hanadur*), and a *bechor* does not qualify because it does not derive its *kedushah* from your verbal act. A *bechor* is *kadosh* from the moment of birth — *kadosh mei-imei imo* — without any declaration on your part. Its sanctity is inherent and automatic, not the product of a *neder*. Therefore invoking "behold this is like a *bechor*" is *matpis b'davar ha'assur*, which does not take effect.

### Sanctification View

Rabbi Yaakov's forbidding opinion does not deny that a *bechor* is *kadosh* from birth, but argues that a *bechor* is nonetheless a *davar hanadur* because there is a *mitzvah* to sanctify it. A *baraisa* teaches in the name of Rebbi: from where do we know that one born with a *bechor* in his house has a *mitzvah* to be *makdish* it? From the *pasuk* *hazachar takdish* — "you shall sanctify the male." Even though the animal is already *kadosh*, there is an obligation to add one's own verbal *hakdasha* to it. This means that a *bechor* also carries a *neder*-derived dimension of *kedushah*, and therefore it falls within the category of *davar hanadur*.

### Sanctity Discussion

Rabbi Yehuda's response to this argument is telling: *ki lo makdish leh mi lo mikdash* — even if one never sanctifies it, is it not already sanctified? The *mitzvah* of *takdish* is real, but its non-performance does not deprive the *bechor* of its *kedushah*. The fundamental identity of the *bechor* as a *korban* is not contingent on your words — it is inherent from birth. Whatever *kedushah* you add through your verbal sanctification is supplementary, not constitutive. The *bechor*'s basic identity is therefore that of a *davar ha'assur*, not a *davar hanadur*, and invoking it does not create an effective *neder*.

Rabbi Yaakov, for his part, could respond that even if the *bechor*'s inherent *kedushah* persists without your declaration, the fact that one's words *do* add a genuine dimension of *kedushah* is sufficient to classify it as a *davar hanadur* for purposes of *matpis*. The added human *kedushah* is not merely declaratory; it is real, and this reality places the *bechor* in the *neder* category. The rebuttal, however, is that this added dimension is not the central or essential identity of the *bechor* as a *korban* — and it is therefore insufficient to transform it from a fundamentally *ha'assur* object into a fundamentally *hanadur* one.

This distinction — between inherent sanctity and added sanctity, between *kedushah* that exists independently and *kedushah* that is constituted through human speech — carries profound implications well beyond *nedarim*. Rav Soloveitchik employed precisely this model in analyzing the *mitzvah* of *kiddush* on *Shabbos*. *Shabbos* possesses its own intrinsic *kedushah*, just as a *bechor* does. Yet there is a *mitzvah* of *kiddush* — to sanctify it with our words. The question is whether *kiddush* is merely a declaration acknowledging an existing reality, or whether it adds a genuinely human dimension of *kedushah* to what is already divinely given. Following the model of the *bechor*, one could argue that just as one has a *mitzvah lehakdisho* — to add one's own sanctification — even when the object already possesses independent *kedushah*, so too *kiddush* on *Shabbos* represents the human contribution of *kedushah* to the *kedushah* that *Shabbos* already bears in itself. This understanding of the *mitzvah* of *kiddush* — as the genuinely constitutive human act of sanctification rather than mere verbal acknowledgment — was, the lecturer notes, already anticipated by an earlier *rishon* who drew on this very comparison.
