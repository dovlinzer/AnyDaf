# Yoma 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Torah Reading Function

### Mishnah Overview

The Mishnah at the opening of this *perek* describes what the *Kohen Gadol* does once the central labors of Yom Kippur have been completed: the *ketoret* has been offered, the blood of the *par* and the *sa'ir* has been sprinkled, the *sa'ir la'azazel* has been sent, and the carcasses of the remaining animals have been dispatched to be burned outside the *Beit Hamikdash*. During the interval while those carcasses are being consumed, the *Kohen Gadol* performs a public reading of the *Torah* and then recites a series of *berakhot* — concerning the *avodah*, the atonement of sin, the people of Israel, and the *kohanim*. He does so either while still wearing his *bigdei lavan*, the linen garments of the *Kohen Gadol*, or in a personal white cloak that he may provide for himself.

### Rashi's Source

Before turning to the *Gemara* itself, it is worth addressing a question that the *Mishnah* naturally occasions: why is the *Kohen Gadol* performing this *Torah* reading? One might say simply that it is to connect the *avodah* being performed to its biblical source, demonstrating that what is being done flows from God's explicit command. But it is instructive to examine the two distinct sources offered by the *rishonim* for this practice.

Rashi, in his comment *ba likro*, directs us to the institution of the *miluim*, the seven-day consecration period that preceded the inauguration of the *Mishkan*. The *Gemara* back on Daf 5, in the first *perek*, derived from the verse *zeh hadavar asher tzivah Hashem* — "this is the word that God commanded" — that Moses was required to recite, or in some sense read aloud, what God had instructed him, and that this act of declaration was itself a necessary component of the *miluim* procedure. Rashi understands that the *Kohen Gadol*'s Torah reading on Yom Kippur is modeled on that paradigm. Just as the *miluim* involved not only the performance of a ritual but also a verbal articulation of the divine command — and indeed, just as the *miluim* were concerned not only with the preparation of the *kohanim* but with the sanctification and purification of the *Mikdash* itself — so too the blood service of Yom Kippur, which cleanses and sanctifies the *Mikdash*, is attended by a similar reading of the *parasha* to establish its divine origin.

### Tosafot Yeshanim View

*Tosfos Yeshanim* offers a strikingly different explanation. At the moment of this reading, the *Kohen Gadol* has reached a natural point of closure. The essential *avodot* of *kapara* have been completed; only peripheral tasks remain. *Tosfos Yeshanim* therefore argues that the reading is not primarily instituted for its own sake — that is, to connect the service to its scriptural foundation — but rather to create an occasion for the *berakhot* that follow. The *berakhot* on the *avodah*, on forgiveness, on the *Mikdash*, on Israel, on the *kohanim*: these are the heart of the matter. The *Torah* reading provides the framework and the formal occasion for those prayers.

This is a significant reframing. Rather than the *berakhot* being secondary to the *Torah* reading, the reading exists in order to give rise to the *berakhot*. What emerges is a remarkable convergence of the *Mikdash* experience with the *beit knesset* reality. The entire apparatus described — the *chazzan haknesset*, the *rosh haknesset*, the public assembly — represents the grafting of synagogue structure onto the *Mikdash* ritual. The closure of the great sacrificial service is framed and articulated through prayer and *Torah* reading, exactly as it would be in a *shul*. One can even say that the *miluim* model, which Rashi invokes, becomes in the hands of *Tosfos Yeshanim* a basis not merely for the reading but for the entire institution of *tefillah* as the interpretive complement to *avodah*.

### Yerushalmi Proof

*Tosfos Yeshanim* also quotes the *Yerushalmi*, which tracks more closely with Rashi's position. Rabbi Idi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan derives the obligation of the *Torah* reading from the word *vayaas* — "and he did" — in the *miluim* narrative, followed by *kaasher tzivah Hashem*, "as God commanded." From here, says the *Yerushalmi*, we learn *l'kriyat haparsha* — that a formal reading of the *parasha* is required. The derivation thus returns to the *miluim* model, as Rashi argued. Yet *Tosfos Yeshanim*'s underlying point remains striking: even granting that the reading is learned from the *miluim*, the primary function he sees in it is as a vehicle for the closing *berakhot* of the day.

## Reading as Avodah?

### Clothing Evidence

The *Gemara* opens its analysis with an inference from the *Mishnah*. Since the *Mishnah* records that the *Kohen Gadol* may wear his own personal white garment (*istali lavan*) rather than the prescribed *bigdei lavan*, this establishes by implication — *michlal d'kriya lav avodah hi* — that the *Torah* reading is not itself an *avodah* in the technical sense. That might have been provable from other considerations as well, but the *Mishnah* makes it explicit: no *bigdei kehuna* are required, therefore this is not formal *avodah*.

Yet the *Mishnah* also notes that if the *Kohen Gadol* wishes, he may perform the reading while still wearing the *bigdei butz*, the official linen priestly garments. This creates an interesting situation: an act that is *not* classified as *avodah* may nonetheless be performed while wearing *bigdei kehuna*. From this the *Gemara* attempts an inference — *shma mina bigdei kehuna nitnu leihanot bahem* — that one may derive benefit from *bigdei kehuna*, meaning that wearing them during an activity unconnected to the formal *avodat hamikdash* is not prohibited.

### Tzorech Avodah

The *Gemara* immediately deflects this inference. Perhaps *kriyat hatorah* is different because it is *tzorech avodah* — it is done for the sake of, and in connection with, the *avodah* of the day. According to Rashi, it is learned from the *psukim* as a necessary component of the day's service; according to *Tosfos Yeshanim*, even if it is not strictly derived from a *pasuk*, it functions as the framing and culmination of the *avodat hayom*. In either case, the reading is organically connected to the *avodah*, and therefore one cannot draw from it any general conclusion about wearing *bigdei kehuna* during activities that have no such connection. The question of *bigdei kehuna nitnu leihanot bahem* thus remains open.

## Benefit from Garments

### Fundamental Question

The *Gemara* now explicitly frames the standing question: *bigdei kehuna nitnu leihanot bahem o lo nitnu leihanot bahem* — may a *kohen* derive benefit from the *bigdei kehuna*, specifically by wearing them at times when he is not engaged in the *avodah*? To derive benefit from wearing clothing — warmth, covering, dignity — is a form of *hana'ah*. The question is whether the sanctified nature of the *bigdei kehuna* restricts that *hana'ah* to the performance of the *avodah* alone.

### Sleeping Prohibition

The *Gemara* brings a first proof: *lo hayu yeshenim b'bigdei kodesh* — the *kohanim* did not sleep in the priestly garments. This suggests that wearing the *bigdei kehuna* outside the immediate context of *avodah* is problematic — otherwise, why not sleep in them? The concern Rashi identifies is that during sleep one might pass gas, which would be disrespectful of the garments. This, incidentally, connects to the parallel concern about *tefillin* at night, where the same worry about *guf naki* motivates the prohibition of wearing them while asleep.

### Other Benefits

The *Gemara* objects that the prohibition against sleeping proves too little. Surely the *kohanim* ate while wearing the *bigdei kehuna* — does eating constitute impermissible *hana'ah*? The *Gemara* responds: no, because eating — specifically eating *korbanot* — is itself *tzorech avodah*. As the *baraita* derives from the verse *ve'achlu otam asher kupar bahem*, the eating of the *kohanim* is part of the *kappara* of the owners. The *kohanim* function as a kind of *klei hamikdash*; their consumption of *korban* meat, particularly *chatat*, is analogous to the consumption of the sacrifice on the *mizbe'ach*. Therefore, eating while wearing *bigdei kehuna* cannot serve as proof that benefit may be derived from them in a general, non-*avodah* context.

The *Gemara* then adds that walking around in the *bigdei kehuna* after the *avodah* is also not necessarily a permitted inference. Perhaps one is truly not allowed to do even that — perhaps one must remove them immediately upon completing the service. The *chiddush* of the teaching that they did not sleep in them is not to imply that all other activities are therefore permitted; rather, the novel teaching is what follows: that they would fold the garments and place them *tachas rasheihem* — under their heads.

## Pillow Debate

### Folding Garments

The *Gemara* now turns to the specific case of using the *bigdei kehuna* as a pillow. On first reading, this seems to constitute *hana'ah* — the garments are serving a non-*avodah* function. The *Gemara* therefore suggests, in the name of Rav Pappa, that the phrase *tachas rasheihem* should not be read literally as "under their heads" (i.e., as an actual pillow) but rather as *keneged rasheihem* — "near their heads," with no physical contact. Under this reading, the novel ruling is only that it is permissible to leave the folded garments close by, something one might have thought was itself problematic lest one roll onto them inadvertently or treat them without proper reverence. Rashi explains that the concern about leaving them near the head is precisely that one might forget the *issur hana'ah* and roll over onto them.

Rav Mesharshiya draws a comparison: from the ruling that one may place the *bigdei kehuna* near one's head, we infer that one may similarly wrap up one's *tefillin* and place them near one's head when going to sleep. The parallel is deliberate. Just as the concern with *bigdei kehuna* during sleep relates to the problem of *guf naki* — passing gas while unconscious — so too with *tefillin*, where the same *Chazal*ic concern motivates the prohibition against wearing them at night. This comparison is not incidental: there is a broader theme in *Chazal* that *tefillin* and *tzitzit* function for the layperson in the larger world as a kind of *bigdei kehuna* — garments that frame one's consciousness of God and one's activity as a form of *avodat Hashem*. The *Rosh*'s reason for *netilat yadayim* in the morning — modeled on the *kohanim*'s washing before their *avodah* in the *Mikdash* — reflects the same sensibility.

One might ask why *me'ila* is not a concern here, given that *bigdei kehuna* have been sanctified. Some *rishonim* invoke the principle of *lev beit din masneh aleihem* — the communal sanctity was stipulated with conditions, effectively turning the *kedusha* on and off at designated moments. The more substantive answer, found in *Tosfos Yeshanim*, is rooted in the principle *lo nitna Torah lemalachei hashareis*: if the *kohen* were forbidden to derive any benefit whatsoever from the garments outside the *avodah*, he would be required to strip them off the very instant the service ended — something humanly impossible. Since the *Torah* must grant some practical latitude, the garments cannot be fully within the domain of *kodshei Hashem*, and once they are not 100% exclusively dedicated, the category of *me'ila* does not apply. The residual question is how much latitude is granted: only while remaining in the *Mikdash* environs? Or more broadly? That question will surface shortly.

### Shatnez Issue

At this point the *Gemara* introduces a complication that reframes the entire pillow discussion. The *avnet* — the belt worn by an ordinary *kohen* — is made of *shatnez*, a mixture of wool and linen. Accordingly, even if one were to set aside all *kedusha* concerns, how could it be permissible to sleep on the *bigdei kehuna* as a pillow? Would that not constitute deriving benefit from *kilayim*?

The *Gemara* immediately qualifies: this difficulty only arises according to the opinion that the belt of the *Kohen Gadol* on Yom Kippur — which is entirely linen — is not the same as the belt of a *kohen hedyot*. According to that opinion, the ordinary *kohen*'s belt is indeed *shatnez*. But according to the opinion that the *Kohen Gadol*'s linen belt is the same type as the *kohen hedyot*'s belt, the ordinary priestly garments contain no *shatnez*, and the concern does not arise.

One might respond, however, that the prohibition of *kilayim* is specifically *bilvisha* — in wearing — and not in *hana'ah* generally. The verse says *lo ya'aleh alecha*, don't put it upon you; *lo tilbash*, don't wear it. Using *shatnez* as a pillow, which is neither wearing nor covering oneself with it, should be biblically permitted. The *Gemara* confirms this: the *baraita* explicitly states *aval ata mutar lehatzio tachtecha* — you may lay *shatnez* beneath you. This is not a biblical violation.

### Shatnez Baraita

Nevertheless, the *Gemara* cites a *baraita* establishing a rabbinic prohibition: *aval amru Chachamim assur la'asos kein* — the Sages forbade this, *shema ticharech nima achas al besaro* — lest a single loose strand from the *shatnez* garment come to rest upon one's skin, which would constitute *lo ya'aleh alecha*. Although an unintentional contact with a loose thread might ordinarily be dismissed as *eino miskaven*, *Chazal* were sufficiently concerned about this outcome that they prohibited the practice categorically, even when one has no intention of allowing the *shatnez* to come upon one's body.

### Ten Blankets Rule

Rav Shimon ben Pabi in the name of Rav Yehoshua ben Levi in the name of Rabbi, transmitted from the *kahala kadisha* of Jerusalem, extends this rabbinic prohibition absolutely: *afilu eser matzaot zo al gav zo* — even if ten blankets or mattresses are interposed between the person and the *shatnez* garment at the bottom, the rabbinic restriction still applies. Once the Sages prohibited lying upon *shatnez*, they did so categorically and comprehensively, regardless of whether physical contact with a loose strand is actually possible. The prohibition is not conditional on the realistic risk of contact; it is a blanket rabbinic ruling. By the same logic, this rabbinic restriction would apply to the *kohanim*, meaning that using the *bigdei kehuna* — which contain the *shatnez* belt — as an actual pillow is rabbinically forbidden. The *Gemara* therefore concludes *shema mina keneged rasheihem*: the text must mean near the head, not under it. This is taken as a good proof.

### Rav Ashi's View

The *Gemara*, however, does not rest there. Rav Ashi dissents and maintains *le'olam tachat rasheihem* — in truth it does mean literally under the head, and the passage does constitute a proof that *bigdei kehuna nitnu leihanot bahem*. How does he address the *shatnez* concern? Rav Ashi argues: *bigdei kehuna kashim hem* — the material of the priestly garments is stiff and firm. Because of this rigidity, there are no loose strands that might come to rest upon one's body. The rabbinic prohibition was enacted as a precautionary measure for soft garments from which loose threads can escape, not for hard, stiff materials from which this is impossible. He supports this with the teaching of Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua, that the hard felt made in the town of Narsh — a thick, compressed material — is similarly permitted, since one need not fear that a strand will come loose from felt.

Thus Rav Ashi's position is nuanced: the rabbinic prohibition on lying over *shatnez* is not categorical across all *shatnez* garments, but is calibrated to the actual risk of contact. Where the material is inherently stiff and no strand can come free, the concern that motivated the rabbinic enactment is absent, and the enactment does not apply. The *bigdei kehuna*, being of such stiff material, fall into this category. Therefore *tachat rasheihem* can be read literally, and the derivation of *nitnu leihanot bahem* stands.

It is worth noting, as *Tosfos Yeshanim* observes, that the *Gemara* raises the *shatnez* concern only in this pillow context, and not in the earlier discussions about wearing the *bigdei kehuna* while walking or during non-*avodah* activities. The implicit logic appears to be that as long as the *Torah* itself has authorized the garment as a worn item — similar to Rabbeinu Tam's ruling that a linen garment with a blue woolen string of *tzitzit* may continue to be worn even at night when the *mitzvah* is not being fulfilled — the *Torah*'s allowance of *shatnez* in that garment extends comprehensively to its use as a worn garment. The *shatnez* concern arises specifically when the garment is stripped off and repurposed as something other than a worn garment, as in the pillow case. This is a fascinating structural point about how the *Torah*'s permission for *shatnez* in *bigdei kehuna* interacts with rabbinic enactments around secondary uses.

## Explicit Baraita

### Baraita Rules

The *Gemara* at last adduces a clear proof-text. A *baraita* states explicitly: one who goes out wearing *bigdei kehuna* into the *medina* — anywhere outside the *Mikdash* — is prohibited. But inside the *Mikdash*, whether during the time of the *avodah* or not during the time of the *avodah*, it is permitted — *mipnei shebigdei kehuna nitnu leihanot bahem* — because *bigdei kehuna* were given to derive benefit from them. This is an explicit source establishing that wearing *bigdei kehuna* during non-*avodah* moments within the *Mikdash* is permitted, and the explicit reason given is *hana'ah*. *Shema mina* — this is the proof.

### Rashi's Explanation

This naturally raises the question: why then are they prohibited outside the *Mikdash* entirely? Rashi explains: *shenoheg bahem chol* — wearing the *bigdei kehuna* outside the *Mikdash* treats them in a mundane manner, which is not respectful of their sanctity. One might alternatively frame this in terms of the earlier discussion: the latitude granted by the principle *lo nitna Torah lemalachei hashareis* — the practical necessity of not stripping off immediately upon completing *avodah* — extends only within the *Mikdash* itself, where the garments belong. Once outside, that rationale no longer applies, and the full dignity of the sanctified garments demands that they not be worn in an ordinary context.

At this juncture the *Gemara* introduces an important narrative that bears on whether *bigdei kehuna* may ever be worn outside the *Mikdash*. A *baraita* records, in the mode of the *Megillat Ta'anit*, that the 25th of Tevet is *Yom Har Gerizim* — a day on which one may not eulogize or fast — because on that day the Samaritans (*Kutim*), whose sacred center was Har Gerizim, requested from Alexander of Macedonia permission to destroy the *Beit Hamikdash*, and were ultimately frustrated. The Samaritans had argued to Alexander that the Jews were in rebellion against him, and Alexander acceded, issuing a decree for the temple's destruction. When the news reached Shimon HaTzadik, he donned his *bigdei kehuna* and wrapped himself in them; together with the honored members of the community, carrying torches and walking in solemn procession through the night, they traveled to where Alexander's army was encamped. At dawn they encountered one another. When Alexander saw Shimon HaTzadik, he descended from his chariot and prostrated himself before him. His retinue asked in astonishment: how could a great king bow before a Jew? Alexander replied that the image of this man's face — *demut deyukno* — appeared before him in the heavens and led him to victory in all his wars. The entire civilized world he had conquered was in the merit of this figure. He then turned to the Samaritans and asked who they were. Shimon's companions pointed them out: these standing before you are the ones who sought to destroy the *Mikdash*, the house in which we pray for you and your kingdom. Alexander handed the Samaritans over to the Jews, who pierced their ankles and dragged them behind horses over thorns and thistles until they reached Har Gerizim, which they then plowed over and planted with leeks — precisely what the Samaritans had sought to do to the *Beit Hamikdash*. That day was established as a *Yom Tov*. The story unmistakably echoes the *Purim* narrative in its structure.

The obvious difficulty this story raises is that Shimon HaTzadik wore *bigdei kehuna* entirely outside the *Mikdash*. The *Gemara* offers two responses. First, perhaps what he wore were garments that were *re'uyim l'vigdei kehuna* — appropriate for *bigdei kehuna* in their appearance but not yet formally sanctified, meaning they were not technically *bigdei kehuna* in the halachic sense. Second, *eis la'asos la-Shem heferu Torasecha* — there are extraordinary moments when national exigency overrides ordinary practice.

## Passing Torah

### Chazan Role

The *Mishnah* describes the procedure by which the *Sefer Torah* reaches the *Kohen Gadol*: the *chazzan haknesset* takes the *Sefer Torah* and passes it to the head of the *knesset*, who passes it to the *segan*, and so on through a chain until it reaches the *Kohen Gadol*.

### Abaye's Explanation

This procedure raises an apparent difficulty. Does this not constitute giving honor to someone of lesser standing — a student, so to speak — in the presence of his *rav*, namely the *Kohen Gadol*? Abaye resolves this: *kulam mishum kevodo shel Kohen Gadol hen* — the entire procession is for the honor of the *Kohen Gadol* himself. Each person in the chain is not being honored for his own sake; rather, their involvement in the sequential passing of the *Sefer Torah* builds toward and heightens the honor of the *Kohen Gadol* who stands at the end of the chain to receive it.

### Azarah Restriction

The *Mishnah* also states that the *Kohen Gadol* receives the *Sefer Torah* standing and reads from it while standing. The emphasis on *omed* — standing — implies that he had been seated, which raises a difficulty. It is a well-established rule that sitting in the *Azarah* is prohibited for all except the kings of the House of David, as derived from the verse *vayavo hamelech David vayeshev lifnei Hashem* — a special allowance given to Davidic royalty alone. If the reading takes place in the *Azarah*, how can the *Kohen Gadol* have been seated there?

### Ezrat Nashim

Rav Chisda resolves this: *be-ezrat nashim* — the reading does not take place in the *Azarah* proper. After completing his *avodah*, the *Kohen Gadol* moves to the *Ezrat Nashim* to perform the reading. In addition to solving the sitting prohibition, this location also renders the reading significantly more public: the *Ezrat Nashim* was a large, accessible space, welcoming a mixed assembly. The *Torah* reading thus becomes not merely an intimate priestly ceremony but a public occasion for the entire community gathered for Yom Kippur. He had been sitting in the *Ezrat Nashim* while waiting for the reading to begin; upon receiving the *Sefer Torah*, he stood to accept it.

## Ezra's Reading

### Location Dispute

The *Gemara* situates Rav Chisda's ruling within a broader debate about where *Torah* readings in the *Mikdash* context took place. The *baraita* presents a dispute: one opinion holds the reading was done *ba-Azara*, in the Temple courtyard proper; Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov says it was on Har HaBayit. Rav Chisda's statement — *be-ezrat nashim* — appears to be clarifying one or both of these positions, specifying that what is meant is neither the inner *Azara* nor some unspecified location on Har HaBayit, but specifically the *Ezrat Nashim*. The three positions may therefore reduce to two real options.

### Rabbi Elazar View

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov derives his position from the episode in *Nechemia* where Ezra the Scribe conducted a great public reading of the *Torah*. The verse states: *vayikra bo lifnei ha-rechov asher lifnei sha'ar hamayim* — he read from it before the open square that is before the Water Gate. This location — before the Water Gate, on a major thoroughfare outside the *Mikdash* proper — suggests a reading that took place not in the inner courtyard but in a large public setting on Har HaBayit. The echo between that event, with its gathering of *anashim, nashim, v'hataph*, and the Yom Kippur reading is unmistakable, and the verse provides the precedent for a location that is accessible, public, and outside the inner *Azara*.

## Blessing After Reading

### Ineffable Name

The *Gemara* now turns to the verse in *Nechemia* that follows the *Torah* reading: *vayevarech Ezra et Hashem haElokim hagadol* — "Ezra blessed the Lord, the great God." What does it mean that he called God *gadol*, great? In what way did Ezra magnify or glorify God? Rav Yosef in the name of Rav answers: *shegidlo b'Shem HaMeforash* — Ezra glorified God by pronouncing the ineffable Name. This draws an implicit parallel to the *Kohen Gadol*'s own enunciation of the *Shem HaMeforash* during the Yom Kippur *avodah*.

### Baruch Hashem

Rav Giddel offers a different interpretation. Rather than the ineffable Name, Ezra glorified God through a formal blessing — *Baruch Hashem Elokei Yisrael min ha'olam ve'ad ha'olam*, the verse from *Divrei HaYamim*. This reading understands the "glorifying" as an act of formal *berakha*, not Name-utterance. Abaye noted the connection between this and the very purpose of the *Torah* reading as articulated by *Tosfos Yeshanim*: the whole design of the reading, on his account, was to allow for precisely these *berakhot*, and here we see *Ezra* himself closing the reading with just such a blessing.

### Name Outside Mikdash

Abaye questions Rav Giddel: why not say Ezra glorified God by saying the ineffable Name, as Rav Yosef suggests? Rav Giddel responds: *ein omrim Shem HaMeforash bigvulin* — one does not pronounce the *Shem HaMeforash* outside the *Mikdash*. If the reading — as established by Rav Chisda and Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov — takes place in the *Ezrat Nashim* or even on Har HaBayit rather than in the *Azara* proper, then the *Shem HaMeforash* would not have been pronounced there. Therefore Ezra's glorification must have been through *berakha*, not through the ineffable Name. The counter-proof — that Rav Giddel himself elsewhere states that Ezra glorified God with the *Shem HaMeforash* — is resolved by noting that the second event, a later chapter in *Nechemia* where the verse reads *vayitz'aku el Hashem Elokim b'kol gadol*, is a separate occasion entirely, and constituted a *hora'at sha'ah*, a special exception, not the standard practice during the *Torah* reading.

## Remove Idolatry Desire

### Avoda Zara Cause

The *Gemara* now turns to the famous *agadata* that the phrase *vayitz'aku el Hashem Elokim b'kol gadol* — "they cried out to God in a great voice" — introduces. What were they crying out about? Rav, and some attribute it to Rabbi Yochanan, explains: *baia baia* — a plea of desperate urgency. They were crying out against the *yetzer d'avoda zara*, the desire for idolatry, which they identified as the very force that had caused the destruction of the *Mikdash*, burned the *heichal*, caused the murder of the righteous, and driven Israel into exile from their land. This is striking, because if one reads the passage in *Nechemia* at face value, there is no explicit mention of *avoda zara* — the text speaks of sin and God's punishments in rather general moral terms, without singling out idolatry. Yet *Chazal* interpret the entire bemoaning of history as directed specifically at the *yetzer* of *avoda zara*, which they regarded as the root cause.

And yet, they said to God, the desire for idolatry was itself given by You only in order that we should receive reward for resisting it. But we do not want the desire, and we do not want the reward it would earn us — *lo ihi ba'inan v'lo agra*. It is too costly a bargain. This echoes the opening of *Berachot*, where one who faces *yisurin* may say *lo hen v'lo s'charan* — neither the suffering nor its reward. The prayer was granted.

### Prayer Granted

A confirmation descended from heaven in the form of a written note — a *pitka merakia* — bearing the single word *emet*, "truth." Rav Chanina derives from this the famous principle: *chotamo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu emet* — the seal of God is truth. God's affirmation of the people's claim, ratified with the word *emet*, reflects that truth is the fundamental attribute by which God engages with creation. This theme will resonate through the *agadata* that follows tomorrow.

After three days and three nights of fasting, the *yetzer* of *avoda zara* was delivered into their hands.

### Lion of Fire

When it emerged, it came out from the Holy of Holies in the form of a *gorya d'nura* — a lion of fire. A prophet, identified in context as Zechariah, told the people: what you are seeing depart from the Holy of Holies is the *yetzer* of *avoda zara*. The emergence from the Holy of Holies is significant and will be returned to, but for now the story continues with the struggle to contain the force. As the people seized it, a single hair fell from its beard, and its cry carried four hundred *parsangs*. This detail — rooted in the vision of Zechariah where the *yetzer* is depicted as a woman in a pot sealed with lead — also invokes Esau, who is described as hairy and who cried out with a great voice, a convergence of associations that the *Gemara* leaves suggestive.

### Sealing Yetzer Hara

What were the people to do with this force now that they had seized it? They feared that if they held it captive, heavenly mercy might be invoked on its behalf and it would be freed. The prophet directed them to follow the imagery of Zechariah's vision: place it in a lead pot and seal the opening with lead, *d'avra masha'iv kala* — because lead absorbs sound. The very medium that allows the captured *yetzer* to cry out to heaven is neutralized by the properties of lead. The *yetzer* of *avoda zara* was thus sealed and contained — not destroyed, but rendered incapable of acting or appealing to divine compassion.

## Sexual Desire Prayer

### World Fails

Emboldened by this success, the people reasoned: *ho'il v'eis ratzon hu* — since it is now a time of divine favor and our prayers are being answered, let us also pray to overcome the *yetzer* of sexual transgression. This prayer too was granted. But almost immediately, the consequences became apparent: the world began to fail. When they searched throughout all of Eretz Yisrael for a fresh egg, none could be found. Even the natural biological processes associated with reproduction — including unfertilized eggs — had ceased. It was not merely that human beings had lost the drive for sexual activity; the entire natural order of procreation was unraveling. The *yetzer* of sexual desire is so fundamental to the continuation of the world that its elimination would bring creation itself to a halt.

### Blinding Eyes

Unable to destroy the *yetzer* outright — *kalya alma*, the world would be destroyed — the people attempted a compromise: asking for the desire to be reduced by half. This too was denied: *palga b'reki'a lo yahavi*, heaven does not grant partial requests of this kind. The solution reached was to blind the *yetzer*'s eyes — *kachlinu l'einei* — placing some obscuring substance on them. This partial dimming achieved one specific benefit: *d'lo migrei beih b'inish b'krivosyei* — the desire no longer drives a person toward his close relatives, diminishing the pull toward incestuous relationships. The connection to vision is apt, echoing the verse in the *parasha* of *tzitzit*: *lo taturu acharei eineichem*, do not go astray after your eyes. The *yetzer* operates through sight, and it is precisely sight that is diminished.

### Holy Holies Question

The *agadata* concludes with a question that will be taken up further: why did the *yetzer* of *avoda zara* emerge specifically from the Holy of Holies? Is that where it resided? This question — about the relationship between the innermost sanctum of the *Mikdash* and the *yetzer hara* of idolatry — will connect as well to the broader theme already established that *God's seal is emet*, and to the *agadata* that follows. That material will be taken up in the next session.
