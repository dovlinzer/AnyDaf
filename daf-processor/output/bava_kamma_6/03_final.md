# Bava Kamma 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hatzad Hashaveh

### Intro: Principle & Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן. לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **The common denominator of** the components in all these categories is that it is their typical manner to cause damage. The Gemara asks: In order **to add what** *halakha* is this statement cited in the mishna?

The sixth daf opens with the *mishna*'s statement of the *hatzad hashaveh shebahen* — the common denominator shared by the four primary categories of damage. At this stage in the *mishna*, we have already established the four *avot nezikin*, abstracted and defined what is included in each, and examined specific cases such as *regel*'s liability for thrown stones. The *mishna* now articulates the overarching principle: because no single category could have been derived from any other, once we have all four together, there is a common denominator that captures a more general standard of liability.

As *Rava* demonstrated in yesterday's *gemara*, this general principle did not strictly require all four categories to be stated — *bor* plus any one other would have sufficed to derive it. The reason the *Torah* nonetheless defined four (or six, depending on how one categorizes them) distinct categories is, as the *gemara* answers emphatically, that each category carries its own *halachot*: *shen* and *regel* are exempt in *reshus harabim*; *keren* pays only half for the first three occurrences; *eish* is exempt for damage to buried objects; *bor* is exempt for the breaking of vessels; and a person who directly causes injury is liable for the four additional payments of *arba devarim*. It matters greatly, therefore, which category a given case falls into.

This generates an important question that will occupy the *gemara* from this point forward: if categorization is so consequential, what purpose does the general principle serve? One possibility is that certain cases genuinely do not fit neatly into any single category and must be placed in some overarching catch-all — but then which special *halachot* apply to them? Does the catch-all carry none of the restrictions of the individual categories, making it in some ways more stringent? Alternatively, perhaps everything must ultimately be assigned to one of the existing categories, and the *tzad hashaveh* merely provides flexibility in defining the boundaries of those categories. These are the questions the *gemara* now turns to address.

### Mammon & Shmiratan Alecha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאֵתוֹיֵי אַבְנוֹ סַכִּינוֹ וּמַשָּׂאוֹ שֶׁהִנִּיחָן בְּרֹאשׁ גַּגּוֹ, וְנָפְלוּ בְּרוּחַ מְצוּיָה וְהִזִּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The stating of the common denominator in the mishna serves **to add** that one is liable in a case of **his stone, his knife, or his load, where he placed them on top of his roof, and they fell** off the roof **by means of a common wind and caused damage.**

The *mishna* states the common denominator as follows: *shemamoncha* — it is your property; *v'darkan lehazik* — it has a propensity to cause damage; *ushmiratan alecha* — you bear the obligation to watch it; and when it damages, the damager is obligated to pay *tashlumei nezek* from the best of the land.

This raises two interconnected questions. First, the requirement of *mamoncha* — of ownership or some proprietary connection — is not straightforwardly satisfied by every one of the four categories. Is a *bor* dug in *reshus harabim* considered *mamoncha*? Is *eish* considered *mamoncha*? The *gemara* will grapple with precisely those questions. More broadly, what is the nature of the connection to an object that makes one the responsible party? If a random cow is wandering the street, there clearly needs to be some link to a particular person before that person can be held liable.

The second question concerns the role of *shmiratan alecha*. Is the obligation to watch the object a criterion that must be independently established before liability attaches — meaning one must demonstrate that the object is one's property, that it has a propensity to cause damage, *and* that one bears a duty of care — or is *shmiratan alecha* rather the *consequence* of establishing the first two elements? If ownership and propensity are shown, the duty to watch follows automatically as the legal consequence. The distinction matters because if *shmiratan alecha* is a separate criterion, one must ask how to determine whether it applies in a given case, which is itself not always obvious.

### Textual Variants

*Tosafot* raises the question of whether the word *mamoncha* even belongs in the *mishna* at all. If one holds that *adam* — personal liability — is included within the *mishna*'s framework, then *mamoncha* cannot be a universal criterion, since a person is not their own property. More broadly, since the printed text of the *gemara* often reflects the editorial interventions of *Rashi* and *Tosafot*, and since different manuscript traditions (*nuschaot*) vary on this point, the presence or absence of *mamoncha* in the *mishna* cannot be determined with certainty without consulting the parallel manuscripts — a task that was not undertaken here. What is clear is that *Tosafot* argued for its removal, and the standard printed edition appears to reflect that position.

What remains true regardless of the textual resolution is that something must constitute the relevant connection between the responsible party and the hazardous object. Whether that connection is ownership, creation, or ongoing control, some such link must exist to identify the party who bears responsibility.

### Abstract Principle

The abstract principle of the *hatzad hashaveh* thus functions as follows: we know there is a shared denominator across the categories, but the cases that require it are precisely those that do not fit neatly into any single box. The *gemara* will now ask, *le'atuyei mai* — what does this general principle actually include that could not have been derived from one of the individual categories alone?

There are two ways of understanding the answer. On one view, the *tzad hashaveh* creates a genuine residual category into which anomalous cases are placed, with the consequence that those cases carry neither the exemptions nor the restrictions of any one category. On another view, every case must ultimately be assigned primarily to one of the existing categories, and the *tzad hashaveh* merely permits us to avoid being overly rigid about which characteristics define that category's boundaries — one learns from the other categories that certain features are not disqualifying. As will become apparent, the latter approach seems closer to what the *gemara* is doing in practice, though the question deserves continued attention.

### During vs. After Fall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָא אָזְלִי קָא מַזְּקִי – הַיְינוּ אֵשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which one would be liable for the damage that was caused? **If** it is a case **where** those items **cause damage as they are moving** through the air propelled by the wind, **that is** a subcategory of **Fire.**

The *gemara* immediately asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the scenario? If the objects damaged someone in the process of falling — that is, *behadi deka azli kamazki*, as they were traveling through the air — then this *does* fall into an existing category.

### Eish: Koach Acher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא אֵשׁ – דְּכֹחַ אַחֵר מְעוֹרָב בּוֹ, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי, כֹּחַ אַחֵר מְעוֹרָב בָּהֶן, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ!
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, one could claim: If so, **what is different** about **Fire** that defines it as a unique category? It is **that another force is involved with it** in causing damage, as it is the wind that causes the fire to spread and cause damage; **and** the entity propelled by the wind is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.** In **these** cases, **too, another force is involved with them** in causing damage; **and** the objects are **your property, and** responsibility for **their safeguarding,** to prevent them from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.** Perforce, there is no need for the common denominator in the mishna to teach the case of an object that causes damage when propelled off a roof by the wind. That case is included in the primary category of Fire that is explicitly mentioned in the mishna.

Specifically, it falls into the category of *eish*, fire. The defining characteristic of *eish* is *koach acher meurav bo* — another force is mixed into the act of damage. In the case of fire, that other force is the wind spreading the flame. Here, the other force is similarly the wind blowing the object off the roof. The *gemara* notes that this case also satisfies *mamoncha ushmirasan alecha* — it is your property and you bear the obligation to watch it. So if the damage occurs during the fall, it is a variant of *eish* and requires no *hatzad hashaveh* to justify liability.

### Eish & Bor Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא בָּתַר דְּנָיְיחִי. אִי דְּאַפְקְרִינְהוּ – בֵּין לְרַב בֵּין לִשְׁמוּאֵל, הַיְינוּ בּוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps the common denominator serves to teach the *halakha* that one is liable when **after** those objects fall from the roof **they come to rest** on the ground, and they cause damage as an obstacle on which a person or an animal stumbles. The mishna teaches that the one who placed them on the roof is liable. The Gemara questions this explanation: What are the circumstances in which one is liable? **If** it is a case **where one declared them ownerless, both according to Rav and according to Shmuel, this is** a subcategory of **Pit.**

The *gemara* therefore redirects: *ve'ela batar denaychi* — rather, suppose the objects have already fallen and are now lying in the street, and someone subsequently trips on them. In that scenario, if the owner renounced ownership (*afkerinu*), then *bein l'Rav bein l'Shmuel* — whether one follows *Rav* or *Shmuel* — this constitutes a *bor*. It is a hazard in *reshus harabim* in the most basic sense. The question of why the *hatzad hashaveh* is needed at all for such a case will be addressed in the following section.

## Afkerinu & Bor

### Hefker in Public Domain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא בּוֹר – שֶׁכֵּן תְּחִילַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתוֹ לְנֵזֶק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי, תְּחִילַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתָן לְנֵזֶק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ!
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, one could claim: **What is different** about **Pit** that defines it as a unique category? It is **that its initial formation,** i.e., the digging of the pit, **is** done in a manner that can **result in damage, and** it is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage to others, is incumbent **upon you.** With regard to **these** items he had placed on the roof, **too, their initial formation,** i.e., when they are placed in the public domain, **is** done in a manner that can **result in damage, and** they are **your property, and** responsibility for **their safeguarding,** to prevent them from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.** Perforce, there is no need for the common denominator in the mishna to teach the case where the objects fell from the roof, came to rest in the public domain, and were declared ownerless. That case is included in the primary category of Pit that is explicitly mentioned in the mishna.

The *gemara* establishes that when objects are renounced and come to rest in *reshus harabim*, both *Rav* and *Shmuel* agree that the case constitutes a *bor*. The similarity to *bor* is clear: there is a stationary hazard in the public domain over which people may trip. Whether this is legally *mamoncha* when ownership has been disclaimed is a subtle point the *gemara* returns to — in fact, even in cases of *afkerinu*, the *gemara* continues to use the language *mamoncha ushmirasan alecha*, suggesting that the initial ownership connection may be sufficient to generate the ongoing duty, even after formal renunciation.

### Bor Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּלָא אַפְקְרִינְהוּ? לִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר: כּוּלָּם מִבּוֹרוֹ לָמַדְנוּ – הַיְינוּ בּוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps the common denominator serves to teach the *halakha* that one is liable even in a case **where one did not declare them ownerless.** The Gemara asks: **According to Shmuel, who says: We learned** that one is liable to pay for damage caused by **all** obstacles that one places in a public domain, whether or not he declares them ownerless, **from** the *halakhot* with regard to **one's pit; this is** a subcategory of **Pit.** The question remains: What *halakha* does the common denominator in the mishna teach?

The *gemara* then identifies what distinguishes a classic *bor*: *techilat asiyaso lenezek* — from its very inception, the *bor* constitutes a hazard. When one digs a pit in *reshus harabim*, the act of digging is simultaneously the act of creating the danger. The *gemara* suggests that the objects that fell from the roof similarly satisfy this criterion once they have landed: once the knapsack is lying in the street, it now has the identity of a hazard, even if the object itself was not inherently dangerous when manufactured.

However, the *gemara* goes on to identify why even the *hefker* case is not a simple *bor* and therefore requires the *hatzad hashaveh*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּאַפְקְרִינְהוּ; וְלָא דָּמוּ לְבוֹר, מָה לְבוֹר – שֶׁכֵּן אֵין כֹּחַ אַחֵר מְעוֹרָב בּוֹ; תֹּאמַר בְּהָנֵי, שֶׁכֹּחַ אַחֵר מְעוֹרָב בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna teaches one's liability in a case **where one declared** the objects **ownerless, and** even if he declared them ownerless, those objects **are not similar to** the primary category of **Pit. What** is notable **about** the primary category of **Pit?** It is notable **in that there is no other force involved with it** in causing the damage. **Will you say** the same **with regard to these** cases, **where** there is **another force involved with it** in causing the damage, i.e., the wind that propelled them from the roof to rest on the ground?

*mah lebor she'ein koach acher meurav bo* — a classic *bor* was created directly by the person who dug it. Here, the hazard in *reshus harabim* was created not through direct action but through the combination of the owner's negligence and the wind. The wind is a *koach acher* — another force — that participated in creating the danger. One might therefore argue that the person's act is too attenuated to generate full *bor* liability.

The *gemara* resolves this through the *tzad hashaveh*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵשׁ תּוֹכִיחַ! מָה לְאֵשׁ – שֶׁכֵּן דַּרְכּוֹ לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַזִּיק. בּוֹר תּוֹכִיחַ! וְחָזַר הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: If one attempts to challenge the derivation from Pit in this manner, it is possible to respond: The *halakha* of the primary category of **Fire will prove** that this factor, that there is another force involved with the object in causing the damage, is not a reason to exempt one from paying restitution for the damage, as although there is another force involved in the spreading of a fire, one is liable for the damage the fire causes, so the *halakha* with regard to these objects can be derived from that of Fire. The Gemara questions the derivation from Fire: **What** is notable **about Fire?** It is notable **in that its** typical **manner** is **to proceed** from one place to another **and** cause **damage.** Will you say the same with regard to these objects propelled by the wind into the public domain, where it is not their typical manner to proceed and cause damage? Accordingly, how can one learn the *halakha* in this case from that of Fire? The Gemara suggests: If one attempts to challenge the derivation from Fire in this manner, it is possible to respond: The *halakha* of the primary category of **Pit will prove** that the lack of this characteristic, of a typical manner being to proceed and cause damage, is not a reason to exempt the owner of the objects from paying restitution for the damage, as that is the nature of Pit, and one is liable for damage caused by his pit. The Gemara notes: **And** as Pit and Fire each prove that the defining characteristic of the other is not decisive in establishing liability or exemption, **the derivation has reverted** to its starting point, and liability in these cases is derived from the common denominator of Pit and Fire. This is the *halakha* added by the common denominator stated in the mishna.

*esh tochiach* — *eish* demonstrates that even when another force contributes to the damage, the person remains liable. But one cannot simply categorize this as *eish*, because *mah le'eish sheken darko leilech ulehazik* — *eish* characteristically spreads and moves, whereas this object sits stationary in *reshus harabim*. So *bor tochiach* — *bor* demonstrates that a stationary hazard generates liability even without active movement. Since neither category alone accounts for this case — *eish* captures the *koach acher* element, *bor* captures the stationary hazard element — the *gemara* applies *v'chazar hadin*, the back-and-forth argument: the common denominator of both categories establishes liability.

The practical takeaway seems to be that the case is fundamentally a type of *bor* — one learns from *eish* not to exclude it merely because a *koach acher* was involved in its creation. This illustrates the broader analytical point: the *tzad hashaveh* may not create a free-floating generic category so much as it prevents any single distinguishing feature of one category from serving as a ground of exclusion, by showing that another category shares liability despite that feature being absent.

### Tosafot: Rav Excluded

At this point, *Tosafot* intervenes in a significant textual matter. The *gemara* as standardly printed does not include a line stating that according to *Rav*, the case where the owner did *not* renounce ownership would constitute a *shor*. *Tosafot* explains that such a line presumably appeared in earlier manuscripts, but it was removed — because according to *Rav*, the case of a knapsack that one still owns, lying in *reshus harabim*, is precisely the type of case that *requires* the *hatzad hashaveh*. It cannot simply be called a *shor*, and therefore the *gemara* cannot use it as a case where the category is already determined.

Why not? Because *shen* and *regel* — the classic manifestations of *shor* that involve movement — are *patur* in *reshus harabim*. One cannot say this stationary knapsack is a *shen* or *regel* case. And if one says it is *keren*, the response is that *keren* involves *kavanato lehazik* — intentional or habitual goring — and an inanimate object cannot be characterized that way, nor does the distinction between *tam* and *mu'ad* apply to inanimate objects (*delo shayach lechalek ela bevaalei chayyim*). So according to *Rav*, this case genuinely does not fit neatly into *shor*, and the line stating that it is a *shor* was rightly removed.

### Tosafot: Shor-Bor Hybrid

*Tosafot* then offers its own positive characterization of the case, citing *Rashbam*: the knapsack lying in *reshus harabim* that one still owns is a hybrid — *mishor umibor*, from both *shor* and *bor*. It resembles *bor* because it is stationary and presents a hazard in *reshus harabim*. It resembles *keren* — as a variant of *shor* — because unlike *bor*, it is *mamoncha*, the owner's property. A classic *bor* is specifically not the owner's property in the same sense. The combination of these two elements — owned property (*shor* dimension) sitting stationary as a hazard in *reshus harabim* (*bor* dimension) — is precisely what requires a *tzad hashaveh* to justify liability.

This clarifies *Rav*'s position substantially. When *Rav* says *kulan mishor lamadnu* — all of these cases can be derived from *shor* — he does not mean a plain *shor*. He means something that partakes of *shor* (insofar as it is owned) but must be analyzed through the *hatzad hashaveh* incorporating *bor* as well.

The contrast with *Shmuel* is equally important. *Shmuel* holds that whether one owns the object or not is not dispositive — the case is fundamentally a *bor* in either event. *Shmuel* brackets the significance of ownership; for *Shmuel*, the hazard in *reshus harabim* is the defining feature. For *Rav*, ownership is central: it pulls the case toward *shor* and away from simple *bor*, which means that for *Rav*, the owned-but-stationary case genuinely requires a *tzad hashaveh* in a way it does not for *Shmuel*.

The *nafka mina* between these positions is concrete: if one's knapsack, still owned, is lying in *reshus harabim* and breaks someone's vessels, according to *Shmuel* the case is a *bor* and the owner is exempt (*bor* is exempt for damage to vessels). According to *Rav* the case is a *shor* — and therefore the owner may be liable even for vessels. The category to which one primarily assigns the case determines which special *halachot* apply, even when the *hatzad hashaveh* is invoked to establish basic liability.

### Hatzad Hashaveh Logic

The overall logical structure is now apparent. For cases where the objects were renounced (*afkerinu*), both *Rav* and *Shmuel* agree the case is a *bor*, but it still requires the *hatzad hashaveh* because the *bor* was not created through direct action but through negligence combined with a *koach acher* (the wind). The *tzad hashaveh* between *eish* and *bor* establishes that such indirect creation does not exempt the owner. For cases where ownership was retained, *Shmuel* still characterizes the case as *bor*, while *Rav* characterizes it as a *shor-bor* hybrid requiring the *tzad hashaveh* between those two categories.

In either configuration, it is not that the *gemara* is placing the case in some generic overarching category. Rather, it is assigning the case primarily to one existing category and using the *tzad hashaveh* to prevent a distinguishing feature of another category from serving as grounds for exclusion.

## Rava: Bor Hamisgalgel

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְאֵתוֹיֵי בּוֹר הַמִּתְגַּלְגֵּל בְּרַגְלֵי אָדָם וּבְרַגְלֵי בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The stating of the common denominator in the mishna serves **to add** the *halakha* that one is liable in a case of **Pit where** an object **is rolled by the feet of a person or the hooves of an animal.** This is a case where one placed an obstacle in a public domain and the obstacle caused no damage where it was placed, but subsequently was kicked by passersby to a different location where it caused damage.

*Rava* offers a second example of what the *tzad hashaveh* includes: *le'atuyei bor hamisgalgel beraglei adam u'vraglei beheima* — to include a *bor* (hazard) that is being kicked around by the feet of people and animals. The scenario is essentially the same knapsack left in *reshus harabim*, but now, rather than someone tripping on it where it initially landed, passersby have been kicking it around so that it comes to rest in a different location within *reshus harabim*, and ultimately someone trips and is injured there. The location of the hazard at the time of injury was not created directly by the owner, but by the accumulated movement of other people and animals.

### Renounced Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאַפְקְרִינְהוּ – בֵּין לְרַב בֵּין לִשְׁמוּאֵל, הַיְינוּ בּוֹר;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which one is liable for damage in that case? **If** it is a case **where one declared** the objects **ownerless, both according to Rav and according to Shmuel this is** a subcategory of **Pit.**

The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami*? If the owner renounced ownership (*afkerinu*), then according to both *Rav* and *Shmuel* it is already a *bor*. The criteria for *bor* are satisfied: *techilat asiyasan lenezek* — the object's placement in *reshus harabim* constituted an original hazard — and *ushmiraso alecha* — the obligation to protect against damage attaches. Here again, notably, the criterion *mamoncha* is absent from the *gemara*'s formulation, precisely because the owner renounced ownership. Yet the analysis proceeds as if the case is a *bor* nonetheless, suggesting that the prior ownership connection — the fact that this was originally *mamoncha* — suffices to generate the duty even after *hafkara*.

### Koach Acher Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּלָא אַפְקְרִינְהוּ? לִשְׁמוּאֵל – דְּאָמַר: כּוּלָּם מִבּוֹרוֹ לָמַדְנוּ, הַיְינוּ בּוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps the common denominator serves to teach the *halakha* that one is liable even in a case **where one did not declare** the objects **ownerless.** The Gemara asks: **According to Shmuel, who says: We learned** that one is liable for damage caused by **all** obstacles that one places in a public domain, whether or not he declares them ownerless, **from** the *halakhot* with regard to **one's pit, this is** a subcategory of **Pit.** What *halakha* does the common denominator in the mishna teach?

Even so, the case is not a simple *bor*, and the *tzad hashaveh* is needed. The reason: *mah lebor sheken ma'asav garmu lo* — by a classic *bor*, the person directly created the hazard, whether by digging the pit or by placing the knapsack. Here, the owner did not directly create the location of the hazard at the time of injury. The location was produced by others — the people and animals who kicked it. Just as the wind was a *koach acher* in *Abaye*'s case, here the feet of people and animals function as a *koach acher*: another force that moved the object to the place where it eventually caused harm.

### Shor & Bor Learn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּאַפְקְרִינְהוּ; וְלָא דָּמֵי לְבוֹר – מָה לְבוֹר, שֶׁכֵּן מַעֲשָׂיו גָּרְמוּ לוֹ; תֹּאמַר בְּהָנֵי, שֶׁאֵין מַעֲשָׂיו גָּרְמוּ לוֹ. שׁוֹר יוֹכִיחַ! מָה לְשׁוֹר, שֶׁכֵּן דַּרְכּוֹ לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַזִּיק. בּוֹר תּוֹכִיחַ! וְחָזַר הַדִּין, לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִרְאִי זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna teaches one's liability in a case **where one declared** the objects **ownerless, and** even if he declared them ownerless, it **is not similar to** the primary category of **Pit. What** is notable **about** the primary category of **Pit?** It is notable **in that one's actions,** the digging of the pit, **cause** the damage. **Will you say** the same **with regard to these** cases, **where one's actions do not cause** damage? Rather, it is the feet of the passersby that caused the damage, by rolling the object to a different location where it caused damage. The Gemara suggests: The primary category of **Ox will prove** that this fact, that one's actions do not cause the damage, is not a reason to exempt one from paying restitution for the damage, as although the owner's actions do not cause the ox to cause damage, one is liable for the damage caused by his ox. The Gemara questions the derivation from Ox: **What** is notable **about Ox?** It is notable **in that** it is the typical **manner** of the animals in these categories **to proceed** from one place to another **and** cause **damage.** By contrast, in the case of the obstacle kicked by passersby to a different location where it caused damage, it is not its typical manner to proceed and cause damage. Accordingly, how can one learn the *halakha* in this case from the primary category of Ox? The Gemara suggests: The primary category of **Pit will prove** that this fact, that the typical manner of a category's components is not to proceed and cause damage, is not a reason to exempt one from paying restitution for the damage, as that is the nature of Pit, and one is liable for damage caused by his pit. The Gemara notes: **And** as Pit and Ox each proves that the defining characteristic of the other is not decisive in establishing one's liability or exemption, **the derivation has reverted** to its starting point, which is that the defining **characteristic** of **this** primary category of Ox **is not similar to** the defining **characteristic** of **that** category of Pit, and liability in these cases is derived from the common denominator of Pit and Ox. This is the *halakha* added by the common denominator stated in the mishna.

The *gemara* applies the same *tzad hashaveh* structure: *shor yochiach* — *shor* demonstrates that liability can attach even when the owner did not perform the damaging act directly; the ox walked and gored on its own, and yet the owner is liable for his negligence. But *mah leshor sheken darko leilech ulehazik* — *shor* characteristically goes out and does active damage, whereas here someone trips on a stationary object. So *bor tochiach*, and *v'chazar hadin*: the back-and-forth between *shor* and *bor* establishes that even a hazard that was moved to its current location by others remains the owner's *bor*, and negligence in leaving the object in *reshus harabim* in the first place is sufficient. The *koach acher* of other people's feet does not sever the causal chain.

The pattern here is consistent with *Abaye*'s case. The case is fundamentally a *bor*; one learns from *shor* that even the indirect manner in which the hazard came to occupy its final position does not exempt the owner.

## Tosafot: Dual Liability

### Textual Variant

At this juncture, *Tosafot* addresses an important textual and conceptual question: what happens when two parties may both bear responsibility for a single act of damage? The specific scenario is: suppose someone kicks the knapsack lying in *reshus harabim* and it strikes a passerby. Is that kick the damager's *adam hamazik*, or is it the owner's *eish* — with the kicker serving as the *koach acher*, like the wind in *Abaye*'s formulation?

*Tosafot* notes that in all standard manuscripts, the *gemara* contains the line: if a person kicked the knapsack and it struck someone, *kocho hu* — it is that person's *koach*, and it constitutes an *adam hamazik*. *Rashi* apparently crossed out this line (*u'peirash b'kuntres d'lo garsinan lai*), reasoning from a later *gemara* (*b'hamaniach*) that *ein darchan shel b'nei adam l'hisbonen b'drachim* — people do not normally watch where they are going — and therefore the kicker cannot be held responsible. Under this reasoning, the kicker is analogous to the wind: a natural, expected force that the owner of the knapsack should have anticipated, making the case the owner's *eish*. *Tosafot* disagrees with *Rashi*'s deletion, arguing that *d'inyan k'dei shelo yeilchu kol kach b'chozek she'ya'iziku v'yaziku* — one should be careful enough not to kick something with such force that it injures another, and *darko l'hisbonen shapir* — a person can reasonably be expected to pay that degree of attention.

### Conscious Agent Principle

The deeper conceptual point *Tosafot* is making goes beyond the textual question. Even if both parties might theoretically bear some responsibility, *Tosafot* articulates a principle about what happens when a conscious agent — a *bar deia* — enters the picture. When a *bar deia* intervenes and through his action causes the damage, his agency eclipses the background causal role of the original owner. The act becomes entirely his.

### Dog vs. Human

*Tosafot* illustrates the contrast by reference to the famous case of a dog that takes a burning wafer (*charara*) and carries it to a haystack, igniting a fire. There, the *gemara* holds both parties liable: the owner of the fire for his *eish*, and the owner of the dog for the *shen v'regel* variant. But that is because *d'kelev lav bar deia hu* — a dog is not a *bar deia*, a conscious agent. The dog's action does not fully constitute an independent volitional act that can absorb and extinguish the other party's causal role. By contrast, *aval hacha hamikalkel o hamisgalgel bar deia hu* — here the person who kicked or moved the object is a *bar deia*, and that changes everything.

*Tosafot* makes the point vivid with an extreme illustration: if someone digs a pit and another person comes and deliberately pushes a third party's ox into it, one would not say the pit owner bears liability — the person who pushed the ox is entirely responsible. Similarly, if someone makes a fire and another person deliberately throws someone's garment into it, one cannot apportion the liability to the fire-maker. The conscious, volitional act of the second party entirely assumes the causal role.

### Adam Overrides Mamon

*Tosafot*'s conclusion follows from this: once a *bar deia* is involved, *adam hamazik* eclipses *mamon hamazik*. Even if the kicker was not acting intentionally — even if he was merely negligent — the fact that he is a conscious agent means that his action is the dominant causal event. The owner of the knapsack no longer remains a co-participant in the *nezek*; the entire act is attributed to the kicker. The *mamon hamazik* dimension of the owner's responsibility disappears in the face of the *adam hamazik* of the one who intervened.

## Rav Acha: Drainage Case

### Drainage Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר, לְאֵתוֹיֵי הָא דְּתַנְיָא: כׇּל אֵלּוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ פּוֹתְקִין בִּיבוֹתֵיהֶן וְגוֹרְפִין מְעָרוֹתֵיהֶן; בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה – אֵין לָהֶם רְשׁוּת, בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים – יֵשׁ לָהֶם רְשׁוּת. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁבִּרְשׁוּת, אִם הִזִּיקוּ – חַיָּיבִים לְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said:** The stating of the common denominator in the mishna serves **to add** the *halakha* that one is liable to pay damages in **this** case **that is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **all those** people **who** engage in activities that the Sages **stated** are permitted, i.e., those who **open their gutters** and drain the sewage from their houses into the public domain, **and** those who **flush out** the water from **their caves,** where foul-smelling water was stored, into the public domain, **during the summer they do not have permission** to do so, while **during the rainy season they have permission** to do so, since the street is rained upon in any event and thereby washed. And **although** all these people perform their actions **with permission, if they cause damage they are liable to pay** for it.

*Rav Acha bar Abba* offers a third example of what the *tzad hashaveh* includes, citing a *beraita*: *kol eilu she'amru potkin bivoseihen v'gorfin meiaroseihen biyemos hachama ein lahem reshus biyemos hageshamim yeish lahem reshus* — those who open their drainpipes and sweep out their refuse pits: in the summer months, when the streets are dry, they have no right to do so; in the rainy winter season, they do have permission. This special case — where someone causes damage through an action they were *permitted* to do — is what requires the *tzad hashaveh*.

### Active vs. Static Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאָזְלִי מַזְּקִי, כֹּחוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which one is liable for damage caused by the sewage? **If** the sewage **causes damage as it is moving,** after he poured it into the public domain, **it is** damage caused by **his** own direct **action,** and it is no different from any other damage caused by a person. According to Rav, it is under the rubric of *Maveh* in the mishna. According to Shmuel it appears in the next mishna, and it is not derived from the common denominator mentioned in the mishna.

Again the *gemara* asks: *heichi dami*? If the person is actively pouring and the water or refuse strikes someone in the process (*behadi deka azlei mazikei*), that is *kocho* — direct personal force — and constitutes *adam hamazik*. The *hatzad hashaveh* is not needed for that scenario. Rather, the case of concern is where the water landed in *reshus harabim*, settled, and then someone later slipped on it. If the owner renounced his interest in it, the case becomes a *bor*: *bein l'Rav bein l'Shmuel haynu bor*, because the water settled in the public domain and created a hazard. The *gemara* again invokes *mamoncha ushmirasan alecha* even for the *afkerinu* scenario, reinforcing the notion that the original proprietary connection generates the ongoing duty.

### Permission ≠ Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בָּתַר דְּנָיַיח. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאַפְקְרִינְהוּ – בֵּין לְרַב בֵּין לִשְׁמוּאֵל הַיְינוּ בּוֹר; מַאי שְׁנָא בּוֹר – דִּתְחִילַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתוֹ לְנֵזֶק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי – תְּחִילַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתָן לְנֵזֶק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is in a case where the damage was caused **after** the sewage **came to rest** on the floor, e.g., a passerby slipped on it. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** it is a case **where one declared** the sewage **ownerless, both according to Rav and according to Shmuel, this is** a subcategory of **Pit. What is different** about **Pit** that it is defined as a unique category? It is **that its initial formation,** i.e., the digging of the pit, **is** done in a manner that can **result in damage, and** it is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage to others, is incumbent **upon you.** With regard to **this** sewage, **too, its initial formation,** i.e., the placement of the sewage in the public domain, **is** done in a manner that can **result in damage, and** it is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.**

What genuinely requires the *tzad hashaveh* in this case is the element of *reshus* — permission. The drainpipe scenario is distinctive because the person acted with authorization. When one digs a *bor* in *reshus harabim* without permission, it is perhaps unsurprising that liability follows: not only did one create a hazard, one acted unlawfully. But this case involves someone who was explicitly permitted to pour refuse into the public domain in winter. Could that permission carry with it an implicit waiver of liability for resulting damage?

The *gemara* says: *shor yochiach* — *shor* demonstrates that liability exists even when no improper act was committed. The ox owner did nothing prohibited; his negligence in failing to watch his animal is what creates liability. But *mah leshor sheken darko leilech ulehazik* — *shor* does active damage, whereas this is a stationary hazard. *Bor tochiach*, and *v'chazar hadin*. The *hatzad hashaveh* establishes that permission to perform an act does not automatically generate exemption from damage caused through negligence as a result of that act. Even when one has the legal right to pour refuse into *reshus harabim*, one must ensure that no one is injured as a consequence. This is a striking ruling, and perhaps the most intuitively challenging of the cases discussed.

### Shor & Bor Learn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּלָא אַפְקְרִינְהוּ? לִשְׁמוּאֵל – דְּאָמַר: כּוּלָּם מִבּוֹרוֹ לָמַדְנוּ – הַיְינוּ בּוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps the common denominator serves to teach the *halakha* that one is liable even in a case **where he did not declare** the sewage **ownerless.** The Gemara asks: **According to Shmuel, who says: We learned** that one is liable for damage caused by **all** obstacles that one places in a public domain, whether or not he declares them ownerless, **from** the *halakhot* with regard to **one's pit, this is** a subcategory of **Pit.** The question remains: What *halakha* does the common denominator in the mishna teach?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּאַפְקְרִינְהוּ; וְלָא דָּמֵי לְבוֹר – מָה לְבוֹר שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, תֹּאמַר בְּהָנֵי דְּבִרְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna teaches one's liability in a case **where he declared** the sewage **ownerless, and** even if he declared it ownerless, it **is not similar to** the primary category of **Pit. What** is notable **about** the primary category of **Pit?** It is notable **in that** the pit was dug in the public domain **without permission. Will you say** the same **with regard to these** cases **where** the sewage was channeled into the public domain **with permission?**

The broader principle it establishes is genuinely important. *Reshus* — whether legal permission or communal authorization — does not translate into a waiver of liability for foreseeable harm. One may always have a *takana* or explicit communal override, but absent such a special provision, the fundamental principle is that creating a hazard, even with permission, requires one to prevent damage; and if through negligence one fails to do so, liability follows.

## Ravina: Wall & Tree

### Mishna Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר, לְאֵתוֹיֵי הָא דִּתְנַן: הַכּוֹתֶל וְהָאִילָן שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהִזִּיקוּ – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. נָתְנוּ לוֹ זְמַן לָקוֹץ אֶת הָאִילָן וְלִסְתּוֹר אֶת הַכּוֹתֶל, וְנָפְלוּ בְּתוֹךְ הַזְּמַן וְהִזִּיקוּ – פָּטוּר. לְאַחַר הַזְּמַן – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** The stating of the common denominator in the mishna serves **to add** the *halakha* that one is liable to pay damages in **this** case **that we learned** in a mishna (117b): In the case of **a wall or a tree that fell** on its own **into the public domain and caused damage,** the owner of the wall or tree is **exempt from paying** damages because he is a victim of circumstances beyond his control. But if the court had sensed the potential danger and had **allotted him** a certain amount of **time** during which he was obligated **to chop down the tree or to demolish the wall,** and he had not yet done so, **and** the wall or tree **fell** into the public domain **within the** allotted **time and caused damage,** he is **exempt** from paying damages. If he did not do so and it fell **after the** allotted **time** expired, he is **liable** to pay the damages.

*Ravina* offers the final example: *le'seifa detnan* — to include what is taught in the *mishna* itself: *hakotel veha'ilan shenafalu l'reshus harabim vehiziku patur milishalem* — if a wall or tree fell into *reshus harabim* and caused damage, the owner is exempt. But if authorities had warned the owner (*natnu lo zman*) — telling him he must cut down the tree or demolish the wall within a set period — and it fell within that allotted time, the owner is still exempt (since he had not yet been negligent in failing to act). If it fell *le'achar zman* — after the allotted time expired — he is liable.

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאַפְקְרֵהּ, בֵּין לְרַב בֵּין לִשְׁמוּאֵל – הַיְינוּ בּוֹר; מַאי שְׁנָא בּוֹר – דְּהֶזֵּיקוֹ מָצוּי, וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי – הֶזֵּיקָן מָצוּי, וּשְׁמִירָתָן עָלֶיךָ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** it is a case **where one declared** the fallen tree or wall **ownerless, both according to Rav and according to Shmuel, this is** a subcategory of **Pit. What is different** about the primary category of **Pit** that it is defined as a unique category? What is different is **that its damage is commonplace, and** it **is your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.** In **these** cases involving the tree and the wall, **too, their damage is commonplace, and** they are **your property, and** responsibility for **their safeguarding,** to prevent them from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.**

Once again the *gemara* asks: *heichi dami*? We again assume the scenario involves someone tripping on the fallen debris (not being struck during the fall, which will be addressed separately). If the owner renounced ownership (*afkerinu*), the case resembles a *bor*: *hanami hezeika matzui ushmiraso alecha* — here too damage is prevalent and the obligation to prevent it applies.

### Techilat Asiyaso Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּלָא אַפְקְרִינְהוּ, לִשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר: כּוּלָּם מִבּוֹרוֹ לָמַדְנוּ – הַיְינוּ בּוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **If** it is a case **where one did not declare them ownerless, according to Shmuel, who says: We learned** that one is liable for damage caused by **all** obstacles that he places in the public domain whether or not he declares them ownerless, **from** the *halakhot* with regard to **one's pit, this is** a subcategory of **Pit.**

Here, however, a crucial distinction emerges. In the earlier cases, the *gemara* could characterize the fallen objects as having *techilat asiyasan lenezek* once they were positioned in *reshus harabim* — their very placement there constituted their identity as a hazard. But a wall or a tree is different. When the owner originally built the wall or planted the tree, there was nothing hazardous about it. The object only became a hazard later, through deterioration or some other change in circumstances. Therefore, the *gemara* deliberately shifts its language away from *techilat asiyaso lenezek* and instead uses *hezeika matzui* — damage is prevalent — as the operative description.

When the *gemara* then tries to characterize this as a *bor*, the objection arises:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּאַפְקְרִינְהוּ; וְלָא דָּמֵי לְבוֹר, מָה לְבוֹר – שֶׁכֵּן תְּחִילַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתוֹ לְנֵזֶק; תֹּאמַר בְּהָנֵי – שֶׁאֵין תְּחִילַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתָן לְנֵזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna teaches one's liability in a case **where one declared** the tree or wall **ownerless, and** even if he declared them ownerless, they **are not similar to** the primary category of **Pit. What** is notable **about** the primary category of **Pit?** It is notable **in that its initial formation,** i.e., the digging of the pit, **is** done in a manner that can **result in damage. Will you say** the same **with regard to these** cases, **where their initial formation,** i.e., the planting of the tree and the building of the wall, **is not** done in a manner that can **result in damage?**

*mah lebor sheken techilat asiyaso lenezek* — a classic *bor* starts life as a hazard. *Tomar behani she'ein techilat asiyatan lenezek* — but here the object did not start life as a hazard.

### Shor Learns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹר יוֹכִיחַ! מָה לְשׁוֹר – שֶׁכֵּן דַּרְכּוֹ לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַזִּיק. בּוֹר תּוֹכִיחַ! וְחָזַר הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: The primary category of **Ox will prove** that this factor, of not being initially formed in a manner that can result in damage, is not a reason to exempt its owner from payment, as that is the nature of the primary category of Ox, i.e., an ox is not born in a manner that results in damage, and one is liable for damage caused by his ox. The Gemara questions the derivation from Ox: **What** is notable **about Ox?** It is notable **in that** it is the typical **manner** of the animals in these categories **to proceed** from one place to another **and** cause **damage.** By contrast, in the case of the tree and wall, it is not their typical manner to proceed and cause damage. Accordingly, how can one learn the *halakha* in these cases from the primary category of Ox? The Gemara suggests: If one attempts to challenge the derivation from Ox in this manner, it is possible to respond: The *halakha* of the primary category of **Pit will prove** that the lack of this characteristic, of the typical manner being to proceed and cause damage, is not a reason to exempt one from paying restitution for the damage, as that is the nature of Pit, and one is liable for damage caused by his pit. The Gemara notes: **And** as Pit and Ox each prove that the defining characteristic of the other is not decisive in establishing liability or exemption, **the derivation has reverted** to its starting point, and liability in the cases of the tree and the wall is derived from the common denominator of Pit and Ox. This is the *halakha* added by the common denominator stated in the mishna.

So: *shor yochiach* — *shor* demonstrates that an object need not start life as a hazard in order to generate liability. The ox was not born as a *keren*; it only became dangerous later. Yet the owner is liable for failing to prevent damage once the dangerous propensity manifested. *Mah leshor sheken darko leilech ulehazik*, and *bor yochiach*, and *v'chazar hadin*. The *hatzad hashaveh* establishes that even an object that did not originate as a hazard — a wall that stood peacefully for years before becoming dangerous — can generate *bor*-style liability once it presents a foreseeable hazard and the owner is negligent in failing to address it.

What is particularly striking about this series of cases is that all the examples generated by the *hatzad hashaveh* turn out to be variants of *bor*: a *bor* created through *koach acher*; a *bor* displaced by *koach acher*; a *bor* created with permission; and now a *bor* whose hazardous character emerged only after its initial creation. The exception, for *Rav*, is the owned knapsack in *reshus harabim*, which he characterizes as a *shor-bor* hybrid. But the dominant pattern is that the *tzad hashaveh* is being used to expand the operative scope of *bor* in cases where one of its defining characteristics — direct creation, stationary placement from the outset, absence of permission, or *techilat asiyaso lenezek* — is absent.

### Tosafot: Fall Damage

*Tosafot* raises one additional question at the bottom of *vav amud aleph*: what is the status of damage that occurs *during* the fall of the wall or tree, before it comes to rest? Should this not be characterized as *eish*, given the involvement of another force (gravity) in motion? Interestingly, *Tosafot* suggests that even damage during the fall could be characterized as *bor* rather than *eish*. The reasoning: *eish* characteristically spreads, moves through the medium of wind, and generates damage over a wide area. Gravity is not a *koach acher* of the same type as wind; it merely causes the object to fall within a limited vertical space, not to spread across *reshus harabim*. *Tosafot* therefore describes the falling tree as a moving *bor* — *af al gav d'bor ein holech umazzik v'elu holchim umazikin* — even though a classic *bor* does not move, one can learn from the stationary *bor* (which causes damage to those who come to it) that even a moving object that damages within its confined trajectory constitutes a *bor* variant rather than an *eish*. This is perhaps the most genuinely hybrid characterization of all the cases discussed.

*Tosafot* also raises the question of timing in the case of debris that has already fallen: must the owner be held immediately liable once it lands in *reshus harabim*, or is there a reasonable period during which he cannot yet be considered negligent in failing to clear it? *Tosafot* suggests that even after the wall falls, there may be some allowance of time for the owner to act before liability attaches — a point that connects directly to the broader question of how negligence is assessed circumstantially.

## Liability & Payment

### Damager's Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהִזִּיק חָב הַמַּזִּיק. ״חָב הַמַּזִּיק״?! ״חַיָּיב הַמַּזִּיק״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הַאי תַּנָּא יְרוּשַׁלְמִי הוּא, דְּתָנֵי לִישָּׁנָא קַלִּילָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: And **when** a component of any of these categories **causes damage, the** owner or generator of the component that **caused damage is obligated [*ḥav*]** to pay restitution with best-quality land. The Gemara asks: Why does the *tanna* of the mishna use the unusual expression: **The one liable for the damage is *ḥav*** to pay? He should have used the more standard expression for liability: **The one liable for the damage is *ḥayyav*** to pay. The Gemara explains: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: This *tanna* is a Jerusalemite, who teaches** using **a light** and concise **formulation,** as *ḥav* is more concise than *ḥayyav*.

The *mishna* concludes the section on the common denominator with the statement that when damage occurs, *chav hamazik leshalem tashlumei nezek* — the damager incurs a debt to pay the damages.

### Chav vs. Chayav

The *gemara* notes a linguistic peculiarity: the *mishna* uses the word *chav* rather than the more standard *chayav*. *Rav Yehuda* says in the name of *Rav*: *hai tanna Yerushalmi hu d'tani lishna kalila* — this *tanna* is from Jerusalem, and they speak a lighter, more refined dialect. *Tosafot* observes that *chav* is not unheard of elsewhere — it appears in contexts like *hamagbi'ah metziah l'chaveiro b'makom shechav l'acheirim* — but that *chav* specifically denotes incurring a debt, while *chayav* denotes being obligated. The *mishna*'s use of *chav* signals the creation of a monetary obligation.

### Meitav Sadeihu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי נֶזֶק. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם״ – מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ שֶׁל נִיזָּק, וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ שֶׁל נִיזָּק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: The owner or generator of the component that caused damage is obligated **to pay** restitution for damage with best-quality land. The one liable for the damage is indebted **to pay payments** of restitution **for damage** with his superior-quality land. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The Torah states with regard to paying damages: **"Of the best of his field, and of the best of his vineyard he shall pay"** (Exodus 22:4). What is the meaning of "his field" and "his vineyard"? It refers to that property of the injured party; the one liable for the damage pays from land of a quality that is commensurate to **the best**-quality **field of** the **injured** party **and** to **the best**-quality **vineyard of** the **injured** party, even if the property he damaged was of a lesser quality; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

The *mishna* specifies that payment is from *meitav ha'aretz* — the best of the land. The *gemara* will now investigate where this standard is derived from the verse and what it precisely means. The verse in question is *meitav sadehu umeitav karmo yeshalem* — he shall pay the best of his field and the best of his vineyard — and the dispute between *Rabbi Yishmael* and *Rabbi Akiva* turns on how to read that verse in relation to the injured party and the damager respectively.

## Yishmael vs. Akiva

### Yishmael: Nizak Standard

[Content established above]

### Akiva: Mazik Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לִגְבּוֹת לַנִּזָּקִין מִן הָעִידִּית. וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says** that **the verse comes only to** allow **injured** parties **to collect** compensation **from superior-quality** land belonging to the one liable for the damage, in the event that he has no money or movable property. **And** by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference one can derive that the Temple treasury of **consecrated** property collects from superior-quality land.

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees: *lo ba hakatuv ela ligbot lenizakin min ha'idit* — the verse comes only to teach that damages are collected from the highest quality land. For *Rabbi Akiva*, this refers to the highest quality of the *mazik*'s own land. The amount of the debt is fixed by the damage actually caused — one hundred dollars is one hundred dollars — but that sum is to be extracted from the best grade of *Reuven*'s (the *mazik*'s) property. *Rabbi Akiva* adds: *vechol shekein lehekdesh* — certainly when *hekdesh* (consecrated property) is the collector, it collects from the highest quality available.

### Initial Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אָכַל שְׁמֵינָה – מְשַׁלֵּם שְׁמֵינָה; אָכַל כְּחוּשָׁה – מְשַׁלֵּם שְׁמֵינָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** if one's animal **ate** from **a rich** garden bed, it is understandable that he **must pay** the injured party the value of **a rich** garden bed. But if it **ate** from **a poor** garden bed, is it reasonable that he **pays** the value of **a rich** garden bed? While this is the straightforward meaning of his statement, it is untenable, as he would be paying more than the value of the damage he caused.

The *gemara* initially reads *Rabbi Yishmael*'s position in a striking way. When he says one pays the best of the *nizak*'s land, the plain reading might suggest that whatever quality the animal actually ate is irrelevant — the *mazik* must always compensate as if the animal ate from the finest crop. If the animal ate from a poor quality field, one might still owe compensation at the rate of the best. This reading makes *Rabbi Yishmael*'s position seem quite severe.

### Rav Idi Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכַל עֲרוּגָה בֵּין הָעֲרוּגוֹת, וְלָא יָדְעִינַן אִי כְּחוּשָׁה אֲכַל אִי שְׁמֵינָה אֲכַל; דִּמְשַׁלֵּם שְׁמֵינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Idi bar Avin said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the animal that caused the damage **ate** from one **garden bed among** other **garden beds, and we do not know whether it ate** from **a poor** one **or it ate** from **a rich** one. In such a case, the *halakha* is **that** the animal's owner **pays** the injured party the value of **a rich** one.

*Rav Idi bar Avin* provides a clarification that resolves the apparent harshness: *achla aruga bein ha'arugot* — the animal ate one row amongst many rows, and *velo yadeinan i kechusha achla i shemeina achla* — we cannot determine whether it ate from a high-quality row or a low-quality one, because the evidence has been consumed. In that case of genuine uncertainty, the *Torah* instructs that one pays according to the highest quality of the range. The reasoning is intuitive: the *mazik* was negligent, the animal destroyed the evidence, and if any doubt remains, it resolves against the negligent party in favor of the *nizak*'s best.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמָה אִילּוּ יָדְעִינַן דִּכְחוּשָׁה אֲכַל – לָא מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא כְּחוּשָׁה; הַשְׁתָּא, דְּלָא יָדְעִינַן אִי כְּחוּשָׁה אֲכַל אִי שְׁמֵינָה אֲכַל, מְשַׁלֵּם שְׁמֵינָה?! הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** raised a question and **said: And if we would know that** the animal **ate** from **a poor** garden bed, its owner would have to **pay only** the value of **a poor** garden bed. **Now that we do not know whether** it **ate** from **a poor** garden bed or **if** it **ate** from **a rich** garden bed, is it reasonable that he should have to **pay** the value of **a rich** garden bed? There is a general principle governing monetary disputes that **the burden of proof falls on the claimant.** Therefore, so long as the injured party cannot prove that the animal ate from the rich garden bed, he should not be entitled to collect the value of such a garden bed.

*Rava* refuses to accept this answer, and his objection is striking. *Uma ilu yadeinan dekechusha achla, velo meshalem ela kechusha* — if we knew the animal ate from the worst quality, it would pay back only the worst. *Hashta delo yadeinan* — so now that we don't know, it pays back the best? *Hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the standard rule is that the one seeking to extract money from another bears the burden of proof. The *nizak* is trying to collect from the *mazik*; if there is doubt, the burden of proof lies with the *nizak*, and in the absence of proof, the *mazik* should not be compelled to pay at the highest rate.

This is a genuinely fascinating objection. One might have supposed that the *Torah*'s explicit verse — *meitav sadehu* — precisely overrides the normal burden-of-proof rule in cases of damage. But *Rava* resists any interpretation of a *pasuk* that works against the normal rules of the legal system without compelling necessity. The verse must be read in a way that is consistent with *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*, even if that requires a less straightforward reading. What is also noteworthy is that even after the *mazik*'s negligence has been established, *Rava* maintains that the *nizak* still bears the burden of proving the quantum of damages. Established negligence does not shift the burden with respect to the amount.

## Rav Acha Resolution

### Quality Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה עִידִּית דְּנִיזָּק – כְּזִיבּוּרִית דְּמַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where,** after appraising the damage, the court comes to collect from the one liable for the damage, but he does not have money available to pay for the damage. And the *tanna'im* disagree in a case where **the superior-quality** land **[*iddit*],** i.e., the best land, **of the injured** party **is equal** in quality **to the inferior-quality** land **of the one liable for the damage,** and the one liable for the damage also has land of superior quality.

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* offers the resolution that finally makes sense of the dispute. The disagreement between *Rabbi Yishmael* and *Rabbi Akiva* is not about a case of uncertainty, as *Rav Idi* suggested. Rather, it concerns a specific factual configuration: *kegon sheyesh idit denizak keziborit demazik* — suppose the best land of the *nizak* (*Shimon*) is equivalent in quality only to the worst land of the *mazik* (*Reuven*). That is, *Reuven* has grade A, B, and C fields; *Shimon*'s best fields are grade B. To use a simplified framework: *Reuven* possesses A and C, *Shimon*'s best is B. The question is: when *Shimon* comes to collect from *Reuven*, at what quality level does he collect?

### Yishmael: Gezeira Shava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּבְהָא פְּלִיגִי – רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: בִּדְנִיזָּק שָׁיְימִינַן, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: בִּדְמַזִּיק שָׁיְימִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And they disagree with regard to this: Rabbi Yishmael holds: We appraise** the value of the land **of the injured** party, and therefore the one liable for the damage can pay with his inferior-quality fields, which are equal in quality to the best fields owned by the injured party. **And Rabbi Akiva holds: We appraise** the value of the land **of the one liable for the damage** and have him pay with his fields of superior quality.

*Rabbi Yishmael* derives his position through a *gezeira shava* — an analogy based on shared terminology. The verse describes the damage: *bi'er besadeh acher* — the animal consumed in another's field. Then it says: pay back from the best field. Since the same word *sadeh* (field) appears in both clauses, *Rabbi Yishmael* applies a *gezeira shava*: just as the *sadeh* in the first clause refers to the *nizak*'s field (the field that was damaged), the *sadeh* in the payment clause refers to the *nizak*'s field as well. Therefore, the quality benchmark is set by the *nizak*'s best, not the *mazik*'s. *Mah sadeh ha'amur lemala denizak, af sadeh ha'amur lemata denizak.*

### Akiva: Plain Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? נֶאֱמַר ״שָׂדֶה״ לְמַטָּה, וְנֶאֱמַר ״שָׂדֶה״ לְמַעְלָה; מָה שָׂדֶה הָאָמוּר לְמַעְלָה – דְּנִיזָּק, אַף שָׂדֶה הָאָמוּר לְמַטָּה – דְּנִיזָּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason of Rabbi Yishmael?** The word **"field" is stated below,** toward the end of the verse: "Of the best of his field, and of the best of his vineyard he shall pay" (Exodus 22:4). **And** the word **"field" is** also **stated above,** at the beginning of that same verse: "If a man causes a field or vineyard to be eaten, and he sends forth his animal, and it consumed in the field of another." **Just as** the **field which is stated above** belongs to **the injured** party, **so too the field stated below** belongs to **the injured** party.

*Rabbi Akiva* reads the verse more straightforwardly: *meitav sadehu umeitav karmo yeshalem* — he shall pay his best field and his best vineyard. The *he* who pays is the *mazik*. Therefore the collection is from the *mazik*'s best. The *gezeira shava* that *Rabbi Yishmael* employs is not needed because the plain sense of the verse already establishes whose property is in question: it is the property of the one paying, i.e., the *mazik*.

### Synthesis: Equivalent Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם״ – דְּהַאיְךְ דְּקָא מְשַׁלֵּם. וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – אַהֲנִי גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה, וְאַהְנִי קְרָא; אַהֲנִי גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה – כִּדְקָאָמֵינָא. אַהֲנִי קְרָא – כְּגוֹן דְּאִית לֵיהּ לְמַזִּיק עִידִּית וְזִיבּוּרִית, וְעִידִּית לְנִיזָּק; וְזִיבּוּרִית דְּמַזִּיק לָא שַׁוְיָא כְּעִידִּית דְּנִיזָּק, דִּמְשַׁלֵּם לֵיהּ מִמֵּיטַב דִּידֵיהּ – דְּלָא מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: תָּא אַתְּ גְּבִי מִזִּיבּוּרִית, אֶלָּא גְּבִי מִמֵּיטָב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva** holds: When the verse says: **"Of the best of his field and of the best of his vineyard he shall pay,"** it means from the best **of** the one **who is paying,** i.e., from the best fields belonging to the one liable for the damage. **And** how would **Rabbi Yishmael** respond to this? The **verbal analogy** between the two instances of the word "field" **is effective, and** the straightforward meaning of **the verse** itself **is effective. The verbal analogy is effective, as I stated.** It serves to teach that when it says that he pays from the best of his field, it means from land equal in quality to the best field owned by the injured party. And the straightforward meaning of **the verse** itself **is effective,** as it serves to teach a different *halakha*, in a case **where the one liable for the damage has superior-quality** land **and inferior-quality** land**, and the injured** party **has superior-quality** land**, and the inferior-quality** land **of the one liable for the damage is not as good as the superior-quality** land **of the injured** party. In this case, the *halakha* is **that** the one liable for the damage **pays** the injured party **from his best** land, i.e., from the superior-quality land that he owns. This is **as** the one liable for the damage **is** legally **unable to say to him: You** should **come** and **collect from** my **inferior-quality** land. He cannot say this because the standard for classifying land as being of superior-quality is set according to the injured party's superior-quality land, and the inferior-quality land of the one liable for the damage is of a lower quality than that. **Rather,** the injured party **collects** his damages **from** the **best**-quality land of the one liable for the damage.

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov*'s synthesis reconciles the two positions into a coherent framework. Everyone agrees the amount of the debt is fixed by the actual damage. The dispute is only about the quality of land from which collection occurs. *Rabbi Yishmael* says: collect from whatever quality of *Reuven*'s land equals *Shimon*'s best. If *Reuven* has grade B land (equal to *Shimon*'s best), collect from that grade B. But *ahanei kra* — the verse is also operative: if *Reuven* does not have grade B (in the example, *Reuven* has only A and C, but not B), then *Shimon* does not have to accept grade C merely because that is what *Reuven* has. He is entitled to go up and collect from grade A — *Reuven*'s best. *Ve'lo matzi amar lei ta agbi mi'ziboris* — *Reuven* cannot tell *Shimon* to go collect from his worst quality land.

*Rabbi Akiva*, by contrast, says simply: always collect from *Reuven*'s best. The *nizak*'s land quality is irrelevant to the calculation of what the *mazik* must provide.

The practical synthesis under *Rabbi Yishmael* thus operates as follows: if the *mazik* has land of equivalent quality to the *nizak*'s best, collection occurs at that equivalent quality; if the *mazik* does not have such equivalent quality, collection occurs from the *mazik*'s best. The *nizak* never loses out merely because the *mazik*'s land does not happen to match the precise quality of the *nizak*'s finest fields.
