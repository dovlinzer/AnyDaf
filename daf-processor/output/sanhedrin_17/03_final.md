# Sanhedrin 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ir Nidachas

### 71 Judges Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְהוֹצֵאתָ אֶת הָאִישׁ הַהוּא אוֹ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה הָהִיא״. אִישׁ וְאִשָּׁה אַתָּה מוֹצִיא לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ, וְאִי אַתָּה מוֹצִיא כָּל הָעִיר כּוּלָּהּ לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **a city may be designated** as **an idolatrous city** only in accordance with the ruling of the Great Sanhedrin, consisting of seventy-one judges. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Oshaya says: As the verse states** with regard to one who engages in idol worship: **"And you shall take out that man or that woman** who did that evil thing to your gates" (Deuteronomy 17:5), and it is inferred: **You take out a man or a woman to your gates** for the lesser Sanhedrin to judge them, **but you do not take out the entire city to your gates;** rather, they are to be judged by the Great Sanhedrin.

The Mishnah rules that an *ir nidachas* — a condemned city — requires a *beis din* of 71 judges. The Gemara opens by asking *mena hani mili*, what is the scriptural source for this requirement? Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef citing Rabbi Oshaya derives it from the verse: *v'hotzeisa es ha-ish ha-hu o es ha-isha ha-hi asher asu es ha-davar ha-ra ha-zeh el sharecha* — "and you shall bring out that man or that woman who committed this evil thing to your gates." The verse indicates that individual defendants are judged at the gates by a local *sanhedrin*, but an entire city — a *rabbim* — cannot be judged by a local court. It must be brought before the *Sanhedrin Gedola*.

### Geographic Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין עוֹשִׂין עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת בַּסְּפָר. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״מִקִּרְבֶּךָ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא מִן הַסְּפָר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the court **may not designate** a city as **an idolatrous city** if it is **on the frontier.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara answers: The verse states: "Certain worthless people have gone out from your midst and have led astray the inhabitants of their city" (Deuteronomy 13:14). **The Merciful One states** that this *halakha* applies when they come **from your midst,** meaning from within your country, **but not from the frontier.**

The Gemara next addresses the geographic restrictions on an *ir nidachas*. The rule is that one does not declare a border city a condemned city. The reason: the Torah says *mikirbecha* — "from your midst" — implying the interior of the land, *v'lo min ha-sefar*, not from the border. The city must be located in the middle of the land.

### Number of Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא שָׁלֹשׁ עָרֵי הַנִּדַּחַת, דִּכְתִיב: ״אַחַת״, אֲבָל עוֹשִׂין אַחַת אוֹ שְׁתַּיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״עָרֶיךָ״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אַחַת״ – אַחַת וְלֹא שָׁלֹשׁ. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: אַחַת וְלֹא שָׁלֹשׁ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא אַחַת וְלֹא שְׁתַּיִם? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״עָרֶיךָ״, הֲרֵי שְׁתַּיִם אָמוּר. הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״אַחַת״? אַחַת וְלֹא שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And three** adjoining cities may **not** be designated as **idolatrous cities.** The source for this ruling is **as it is written:** "If you shall hear concerning **one** of your cities that the Lord your God has given you" (Deuteronomy 13:13), and not three cities. The mishna continues: **But** the court **may designate one** city, **or two** adjoining cities as idolatrous cities. The source for this is **as it is written: "Your cities,"** in the plural. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"One,"** from which it is inferred: **One, but not three. Do you say** that the meaning is **one, but not three, or rather,** is this **not** the meaning of the verse, that it is **one, but not two?** The *baraita* explains that this cannot be. **When** the verse **states: "Your cities," two are stated. How do I realize** the meaning of: **"One"? One, but not three.**

Nor may three cities be condemned together. The verse uses the word *achas* — "one city" — suggesting a limitation. But the Gemara raises the question: perhaps you cannot even condemn two simultaneously? After all, the verse says *achas*. The answer is that elsewhere the verse reads *arecha* — "your cities" — in the plural, which implies that two is permissible. The word *achas* then comes to exclude three, not two. Thus the rule is: one or two cities may be condemned simultaneously, but not three.

### Karcha Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִימְנִין אָמַר רַב: בְּבֵית דִּין אֶחָד הוּא דְּאֵין עוֹשִׂין, הָא בִּשְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה בָּתֵּי דִינִין – עוֹשִׂין. וְזִימְנִין אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה בָּתֵּי דִינִין לְעוֹלָם אֵין עוֹשִׂין. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? מִשּׁוּם קׇרְחָה. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד, אֲבָל בִּשְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת – עוֹשִׂין. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין עוֹשִׂין מִשּׁוּם קׇרְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **At times Rav said: It is in one court that they may not designate** more than two adjoining cities as idolatrous cities, **but in two or three courts they may designate** them. **And at times Rav said: Even in two or three courts they may never designate** them. **What is the reasoning of Rav?** It is **due to desolation,** to ensure there will not be large swaths of uninhabited land in Eretz Yisrael. **Reish Lakish says: They taught only** that the court may not designate three adjoining cities as idolatrous cities **in one region, but in two or three regions they may designate** them. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They may not designate** them, **due to desolation.**

The Gemara then records a further tradition. Rav states that one *beis din* may not rule on more than one *ir nidachas* at a time, though if a different *beis din* convenes — Rashi understands this to mean a later generation of judges after the previous ones had died — the process could apply anew. Yet in another formulation, Rav states that even across multiple courts it may never be done simultaneously.

What is Rav's reason? *Mishum karcha* — because it would leave a bald spot in the land of Israel. Reish Lakish qualifies: the *karcha* concern applies only when the cities are in the same geographic area. If they are in two or three separate regions, the objection falls away. Rabbi Yochanan, however, maintains that the *karcha* concern stands regardless, and a *baraisa* is cited in support of his position: one may not condemn three cities in *Eretz Yisrael* as condemned cities. However, two may be condemned if each is in a different region — for instance, one in Yehudah and one in the Galil. But two in Yehudah and two in the Galil — that cannot be done, precisely because of the *karcha*. It thus emerges that there are two distinct limitations operating here: the *achas v'lo shalosh* exclusion, which may operate even across scattered locations, and the *karcha* principle, which prohibits even two condemned cities within the same region.

### Border Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין עוֹשִׂין שָׁלֹשׁ עֲיָירוֹת מְנוּדָּחוֹת בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָם שְׁתַּיִם, כְּגוֹן אַחַת בִּיהוּדָה וְאַחַת בַּגָּלִיל. אֲבָל שְׁתַּיִם בִּיהוּדָה וּשְׁתַּיִם בַּגָּלִיל – אֵין עוֹשִׂין. וּסְמוּכָה לַסְּפָר – אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת אֵין עוֹשִׂין. מַאי טַעְמָא? שֶׁמָּא יִשְׁמְעוּ גּוֹיִם וְיַחְרִיבוּ אֶת אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 14:1) **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** The court **may not designate three** adjoining **cities** as **idolatrous** cities **in Eretz Yisrael, but they may designate two, such as one in Judea and one in the Galilee. But they may not designate two in Judea or two in the Galilee. And** if the city is **near the frontier, they may not designate even one. What is the reason** for this? **Perhaps the gentiles will hear** that there is a city on the border that is desolate, **and they will** seize the opportunity to invade and **destroy Eretz Yisrael.**

The Gemara then returns to the border restriction: even a single condemned city cannot be declared on the border of the land. Why? *Shema yishmu nochrim v'yacharivu es Eretz Yisrael* — lest the nations hear of the destruction and take the opportunity to attack. One might ask: if the verse already says *mikirbecha* — "from your midst" — excluding the border, why supply a rational explanation? The answer is that this is Rabbi Shimon, who characteristically explicates the reasons behind the *psukim*. Here, it would appear, he is not using the reason to expand or contract the halacha in a novel way, but rather his concern for the security of the land motivates his reading of the word *mikirbecha* in the first place.

## Great Sanhedrin

### Moshe's Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עִמָּךְ״, ״עִמָּךְ״ – וְאַתְּ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: ״עִמָּךְ״ מִשּׁוּם שְׁכִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **with you"** (Numbers 11:16), i.e., they will stand **"with you," and you** are to be counted **with them,** leading to a total number of seventy-one. **And Rabbi Yehuda** holds that the term **"with you"** is mentioned **due to the Divine Presence** that rested on Moses. According to Rabbi Yehuda, Moses was instructed to remain with the seventy Elders in order for the Divine Presence to rest upon them as well. He was not formally part of their court and therefore the number of Sages on the Great Sanhedrin is seventy.

The Gemara now turns to the composition of the *Sanhedrin Gedola*, which was 71. What is the Rabbanan's reason for saying that Moshe is counted as part of the 71 rather than standing apart from the 70 elders? The verse states *v'hisyatzvu sham imach* — they shall stand there with you — indicating that Moshe is grouped together with the 70 elders as part of a single body. Furthermore, the verse says *v'nasu itach b'masa ha'am* — "and they shall carry the burden of the people with you" — meaning all of them together, the 70 plus Moshe, form the collective that bears the burden, yielding a court of 71.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנָשְׂאוּ אִתְּךָ בְּמַשָּׂא הָעָם״, ״אִתְּךָ״ – וְאַתְּ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: ״אִתְּךָ״ – בְּדוֹמִין לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how would **the Rabbis** respond to this line of reasoning? The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "And they shall bear the burden of the people with you"** (Numbers 11:17), which indicates: **"With you," and you** are to be counted **with them. And** how would **Rabbi Yehuda** respond to that? He would explain that the term **"with you"** means **similar to you,** meaning, that the Elders appointed to the court had to be of fit lineage and free of blemish, like Moses.

Rabbi Yehudah disagrees. The word *imach* in the verse does not mean Moshe is counted as part of the same group; rather, *itach b'domeh lach*, it means that the elders must be as similar to Moshe as possible in terms of personal character and wisdom. We already have an instance of *itach* from the section concerning Yitro — referring to the smaller *sanhedrin* — which conveys the standard of resembling Moshe. The additional *itach* in the context of the 70 elders therefore teaches something new: that Moshe is included in the count, making the total 71.

### Lottery Selection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וַיִּשָּׁאֲרוּ שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים בַּמַּחֲנֶה״. יֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: בַּקַּלְפִּי נִשְׁתַּיְּירוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the appointment of the Elders by Moses, the Gemara discusses additional aspects of that event. There were seventy-two candidates for Elder but only seventy were needed. They were chosen by lots with their names put into a box. **The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"And there remained two men in the camp;** the name of one was Eldad and the name of the other Medad, and the spirit rested upon them, and they were among those who were written but who did not go out to the tent, and they prophesied in the camp" (Numbers 11:26). Where did they remain? **Some say** this means **they,** i.e., their names, **remained** excluded from those selected from the lots **in the box.**

The Gemara records a *baraisa* about how Moshe actually selected the 70 elders. When the Holy One told Moshe to gather 70 elders from among Israel, Moshe faced an arithmetical challenge: if he took six from each of the twelve tribes he would have 72 candidates — two too many. If he took five from each tribe he would have only 60 — ten short. And if he took six from some tribes and five from others he would create jealousy among the tribes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: ״אֶסְפָה לִּי שִׁבְעִים אִישׁ מִזִּקְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, אָמַר מֹשֶׁה: כֵּיצַד אֶעֱשֶׂה? אֶבְרוֹר שִׁשָּׁה מִכׇּל שֵׁבֶט וְשֵׁבֶט – נִמְצְאוּ שְׁנַיִם יְתֵירִים. אֶבְרוֹר חֲמִשָּׁה חֲמִשָּׁה מִכׇּל שֵׁבֶט וְשֵׁבֶט – נִמְצְאוּ עֲשָׂרָה חֲסֵרִים. אֶבְרוֹר שִׁשָּׁה מִשֵּׁבֶט זֶה וַחֲמִשָּׁה מִשֵּׁבֶט זֶה – הֲרֵינִי מֵטִיל קִנְאָה בֵּין הַשְּׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: **At the time that the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Moses: "Gather for Me seventy men of the Elders of Israel"** (Numbers 11:16), **Moses said: How shall I do** it? If **I select six from each and every tribe, there will be** a total of seventy-two, which will be **two extra.** But if **I select five from each and every tribe, there will be** a total of sixty, **lacking ten.** And if **I select six from this tribe and five from that tribe, I will bring about envy between the tribes,** as those with fewer representatives will resent the others.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה עָשָׂה? בֵּירַר שִׁשָּׁה שִׁשָּׁה, וְהֵבִיא שִׁבְעִים וּשְׁנַיִם פִּיתְקִין. עַל שִׁבְעִים כָּתַב ״זָקֵן״, וּשְׁנַיִם הִנִּיחַ חָלָק. בְּלָלָן וּנְתָנָן בְּקַלְפִּי. אָמַר לָהֶם: בּוֹאוּ וּטְלוּ פִּיתְקֵיכֶם! כׇּל מִי שֶׁעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ ״זָקֵן״, אָמַר: כְּבָר קִידֶּשְׁךָ שָׁמַיִם. מִי שֶׁעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ חָלָק, אָמַר: הַמָּקוֹם לֹא חָפֵץ בְּךָ, אֲנִי מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה לָךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **What did he do? He selected six** from every tribe **and he brought seventy-two slips [*pitakin*]. On seventy** of them **he wrote: Elder, and he left two** of them **blank. He mixed them and placed them in the box. He** then **said to** the seventy-two chosen candidates: **Come and draw your slips. Everyone whose hand drew up** a slip that said: **Elder, he said** to him: **Heaven has already sanctified you.** And **everyone whose hand drew up** a **blank** slip, **he said** to him: **The Omnipresent does not desire you; what can I do for you?**

What did Moshe do? He selected six men from each of the twelve tribes, yielding 72 candidates. He then prepared 72 lots: on 70 he wrote the word *Zaken* (elder), and two he left blank. He mixed them in a box (*kalfi*) and told each candidate to come and draw his lot. Whoever drew *Zaken* was told: heaven has already sanctified you. Whoever drew the blank was told: *Hamakom lo chafetz becha* — God does not desire you; I can do nothing for you. The Gemara draws a parallel to how Moshe resolved the redemption of the 273 extra *bechorim* over the number of Levites: he prepared 22,000 lots inscribed with *ben Levi* and 273 inscribed with *chamesh shekalim* — five shekel — mixed them, and had the *bechorim* draw, so that no individual could complain that Moshe had singled him out.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּיוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״וְלָקַחְתָּ חֲמֵשֶׁת חֲמֵשֶׁת שְׁקָלִים לַגֻּלְגֹּלֶת״. אָמַר מֹשֶׁה: כֵּיצַד אֶעֱשֶׂה לָהֶן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל? אִם אוֹמַר לוֹ: תֵּן לִי פִּדְיוֹנְךָ וָצֵא, יֹאמַר לִי: כְּבָר פְּדָאַנִי בֶּן לֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **You** can **say** something **similar to this** to explain the verse about the redemption of the firstborn by the Levites: "Take the Levites in place of all of the firstborn of the children of Israel…and as for the redemption of the 273 of the firstborn of the children of Israel who are in excess over the number of the Levites…**you shall take five shekels per head"** (Numbers 3:45–47). It can be explained that **Moses said: How shall I do** this **for the Jews? If I say to** one of the firstborns: **Give me** money for **your redemption and** you may **leave,** as you are among the 273 extra firstborns, **he will say to me: A Levite already redeemed me;** what is the reason you think that I am among those who were not redeemed?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה עָשָׂה? הֵבִיא עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁנַיִם אֲלָפִים פִּיתְקִין, וְכָתַב עֲלֵיהֶן ״בֶּן לֵוִי״, וְעַל שְׁלֹשָׁה וְשִׁבְעִים וּמָאתַיִם כָּתַב עֲלֵיהֶן ״חֲמִשָּׁה שְׁקָלִים״. בְּלָלָן וּנְתָנָן בְּקַלְפִּי. אָמַר לָהֶן: טְלוּ פִּיתְקֵיכֶם. מִי שֶׁעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ ״בֶּן לֵוִי״, אָמַר לוֹ: כְּבָר פְּדָאֲךָ בֶּן לֵוִי. מִי שֶׁעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ ״חֲמֵשֶׁת שְׁקָלִים״, אָמַר לוֹ: תֵּן פִּדְיוֹנְךָ וָצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **What did he do? He brought 22,000 slips** (see Numbers 3:39), **and he wrote on them: Levite, and on 273** additional ones **he wrote: Five shekels. He mixed them up and placed them in a box. He said to them: Draw your slips. Everyone whose hand drew up** a slip that said: **Levite, he said to him: A Levite already redeemed you. Everyone whose hand drew up** a slip that said: **Five shekels, he said to him: Pay your redemption** money and you may **leave.**

### Eldad & Medad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בַּמַּחֲנֶה נִשְׁתַּיְּירוּ. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה ״אֶסְפָה לִּי שִׁבְעִים אִישׁ״, אָמְרוּ אֶלְדָּד וּמֵידָד: אֵין אָנוּ רְאוּיִין לְאוֹתָהּ גְּדוּלָּה. אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: הוֹאִיל וּמִיעַטְתֶּם עַצְמְכֶם, הֲרֵינִי מוֹסִיף גְּדוּלָּה עַל גְּדוּלַּתְכֶם. וּמָה גְּדוּלָּה הוֹסִיף לָהֶם? שֶׁהַנְּבִיאִים כּוּלָּן נִתְנַבְּאוּ וּפָסְקוּ, וְהֵם נִתְנַבְּאוּ וְלֹא פָּסְקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** Eldad and Medad **remained in the camp,** as they did not want to come to the lottery for the Elders. **At the time that the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Moses: Gather for me seventy Elders, Eldad and Medad said: We are not fitting for that** level of **greatness;** we are not worthy of being appointed among the Elders. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Since you have made yourselves humble, I will add greatness to your greatness. And what is** the **greatness** that **he added to them?** It was **that all of the prophets,** meaning the other Elders, who were given prophecy, **prophesied** for a time **and** then **stopped** prophesying, **but they prophesied and did not stop.**

The Gemara now addresses the famous story of Eldad and Medad, who "remained in the camp." One opinion (*yesh omrim*) holds that they remained because of the lottery (*bakalfi nishta'yeru*): they drew the blank lots and were thereby excluded. This reading is consistent with the lottery narrative just described, and if so Eldad and Medad were among the original 72 candidates.

A close reading of Rashi here, however, reveals a nuanced interpretation distinct from the well-known account in Rashi's commentary on the Torah. Rashi on the Gemara explains that Eldad and Medad did not go to draw their lots because they feared that blank lots would come up in their hands. They preferred to disqualify themselves from the process rather than risk drawing the blank and having Moshe tell them, *Hamakom lo chafetz becha*. This portrayal is more ambiguous than the familiar Rashi on the Torah, where they are praised for their humility in voluntarily withdrawing out of deference. Here the motivation is closer to reluctance to face public rejection — which, as the Gemara will develop, leaves open the question of their virtue.

Rabbi Shimon offers a second opinion: Eldad and Medad chose on their own initiative to remain in the camp. When the Holy One told Moshe to bring 70 elders, Eldad and Medad said: *einenu re'uyim l'ota gedolah* — we are not worthy of this greatness. The Holy One responded: because you have made yourselves small, I will add greatness upon your greatness. And what was that greatness? All the other 70 elders prophesied and then stopped — *vayitnabu velo yasafu*; but Eldad and Medad began to prophesy and did not stop. They continued prophesying within the camp.

### Their Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה נְבוּאָה נִתְנַבְּאוּ? אָמְרוּ: מֹשֶׁה מֵת, יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מַכְנִיס אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לָאָרֶץ. אַבָּא חָנִין אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: עַל עִסְקֵי שְׂלָיו הֵן מִתְנַבְּאִים: עֲלִי שְׂלָיו, עֲלִי שְׂלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Eldad and Medad being prophets, the Gemara asks: **And what prophecy did they prophesy? They said: Moses will die,** and **Joshua will bring the Jewish people into Eretz** Yisrael. **Abba Ḥanin says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: They prophesied about the matter of the quail** that came afterward (Numbers 11:31–33), saying: **Arise quail, arise quail,** and then the quail came.

What did Eldad and Medad prophesy? The Gemara offers several opinions. One view holds that they prophesied: *Moshe mes v'Yehoshua machnis es Yisrael l'aretz* — Moshe will die and Yehoshua will bring Israel into the land. This is inferred from the context: Yehoshua became agitated at their prophesying, which makes most sense if their prophecy touched on a matter involving Moshe's honor — specifically, the announcement of his death and succession.

Abba Chanin in the name of Rabbi Eliezer offers a second view: they prophesied about the *slav* — the quail that was to come — saying *ali slav, ali slav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: עַל עִסְקֵי גּוֹג וּמָגוֹג הָיוּ מִתְנַבְּאִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ אֱלֹהִים הַאַתָּה הוּא אֲשֶׁר דִּבַּרְתִּי בְּיָמִים קַדְמוֹנִים בְּיַד עֲבָדַי נְבִיאֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל הַנִּבְּאִים בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם שָׁנִים לְהָבִיא אֹתְךָ עֲלֵיהֶם וְגוֹ׳״. אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״שָׁנִים״ אֶלָּא ״שְׁנַיִם״. אֵילּוּ הֵן שְׁנַיִם נְבִיאִים שֶׁנִּתְנַבְּאוּ בְּפֶרֶק אֶחָד נְבוּאָה אַחַת? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: אֶלְדָּד וּמֵידָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: They were prophesying about the matter of Gog and Magog, as it is stated** with regard to Gog and Magog: **"So says the Lord God: Are you the one of whom I spoke in ancient days, through my servants, the prophets of Israel, who prophesied in those days for many years [*shanim*] that I would bring you against them?"** (Ezekiel 38:17). **Do not read** it as: **"Years [*shanim*]"; rather,** read it as: **Two [*shenayim*]. And who are the two prophets who prophesied the same prophecy at the same time? You must say: Eldad and Medad.**

Rav Nachman proposes a third interpretation: they prophesied about Gog and Magog, the eschatological war at the end of days. The source is the verse in Yechezkel: *ha'atah hu asher dibarti b'yamim kadmonim beyad avdai nevi'ei Yisrael* — "Are you the one of whom I spoke in former days through my servants, the prophets of Israel?" The word *shanim* in the verse can be read not as "years" but as *shnayim* — two — referring to two prophets who prophesied the same prophecy at the same time in the same place. Who are those two prophets? Eldad and Medad.

### Content Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: כׇּל הַנְּבִיאִים כּוּלָּן נִתְנַבְּאוּ וּפָסְקוּ, וְהֵם נִתְנַבְּאוּ וְלֹא פָּסְקוּ. מְנָא לַן דְּפָסְקוּ? אִילֵּימָא מִדִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּתְנַבְּאוּ וְלֹא יָסָפוּ״? אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״קוֹל גָּדוֹל וְלֹא יָסָף״ – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא אוֹסֵיף הוּא? אֶלָּא דְּלָא פְּסַק הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master says:** The *baraita* said: **All of the prophets prophesied and** then **stopped, but** Eldad and Medad **prophesied and did not stop.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that** the other prophets **stopped** prophesying? **If we say** it is **from that which is written** about them: **"And they prophesied but they did so no more [*velo yasafu*]"** (Numbers 11:25), that is difficult: **But if that is so,** then concerning that which is stated in relation to the giving of the Torah: "These words the Lord spoke to all your assembly…with **a great voice, and it went on no more [*velo yasaf*]"** (Deuteronomy 5:19), **so too** shall it be understood **that** the great voice **did not continue? Rather,** the intention there is **that it did not stop,** interpreting the word *yasafu* as related to *sof*, meaning: End. Consequently, with regard to the seventy Elders as well, the word can be interpreted to mean that they did not stop prophesying.

The Gemara now examines the coherence of each of these three opinions against the textual clues. The opinion that they prophesied about Moshe's death fits naturally with the narrative: Yehoshua urges Moshe, *Adoni Moshe kela'em* — my master Moshe, restrain them — and the context clearly involves protecting Moshe's dignity. If they announced his death and succession by Yehoshua, Yehoshua's protective reaction is entirely understandable.

The two other opinions — the *slav* and Gog and Magog — raise a difficulty: what exactly was Yehoshua objecting to? Why would a prophecy about quail or an end-of-days war provoke him to try to silence them? The Gemara answers: *lav orach ar'a* — it is simply not proper conduct for students to prophesy in the presence of their master. Eldad and Medad were in some sense disciples in Moshe's orbit, and to deliver public prophecy while Moshe was present was like a student who rules on halacha in the presence of his teacher. That alone was reason enough for Yehoshua's distress.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הָכָא כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּתְנַבְּאוּ״, הָתָם כְּתִיב: ״מִתְנַבְּאִים״. עֲדַיִין מִתְנַבְּאִים וְהוֹלְכִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the proof is as follows: **It is written here** with regard to the seventy Elders: **"They prophesied"** (Numbers 11:25), and **it is written there:** "Eldad and Medad **are prophesying** in the camp" (Numbers 11:27), from which it can be derived that **they were continuously prophesying.**

### Silencing Them

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מֹשֶׁה מֵת, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״אֲדֹנִי מֹשֶׁה כְּלָאֵם״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי, מַאי ״אֲדֹנִי מֹשֶׁה כְּלָאֵם״? דְּלָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּתַלְמִיד הַמּוֹרֶה הֲלָכָה לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the content of Eldad and Medad's prophecy, the Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** their prophecy was that **Moses will die, this is** the reason for that **which is written** there: "And Joshua, son of Nun, the servant of Moses from his youth, answered and said: **My master Moses, imprison them"** (Numbers 11:28), as their prophecy appeared to be a rebellion against Moses. **But according to the one who says those** other **two** opinions with regard to the content of the prophecy, according to which their prophecy had no connection to Moses, **what** is the reason that Joshua said: **"My master Moses, imprison them"?** The Gemara answers: He said this **because** it is **not proper conduct** for them to prophesy publicly in close proximity to Moses, **as** by doing so **they are like a student who teaches a *halakha* in his teacher's presence,** which is inappropriate.

### Moshe's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״מִי יִתֵּן״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מֹשֶׁה מֵת, מֵינָח הֲוָה נִיחָא לֵיהּ? לָא סַיְּימוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says those** other **two** opinions, **this is** the reason for that **which is written:** "And Moses said to him: Are you jealous for my sake? **Would** that all of the Lord's people were prophets" (Numbers 11:29). **But according to the one who says** that Eldad and Medad prophesied that **Moses will die** and Joshua will bring Israel into the land, **would it have been satisfactory to Moses** that all of the people of God would utter similar prophecies? The Gemara answers: **They did not conclude it before him.** Moses was not aware of what they had said, but only that they were prophesying.

What then did Yehoshua mean by *kela'em* — restrain them? Various interpretations appear. One reading is that he meant: lock them up or punish them. But the Gemara offers a more pointed interpretation: *hatil aleihem tzorchei tzibur* — place communal responsibilities upon them, and *v'hein kalin mei'aleihen* — the demands of public leadership will destroy them on their own. Tosafos explains: the *shechinah* does not rest amid sadness, only amid joy. The pressures of communal administration bring anguish and preoccupation, and that alone would extinguish the *nevuah* without any active intervention.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״כְּלָאֵם״? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַטֵּל עֲלֵיהֶן צׇרְכֵי צִיבּוּר, וְהֵן כָּלִין מֵאֵילֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **"Imprison them [*kela'em*]"?** The Gemara answers: Joshua **said to him: Place** responsibility for the **needs of the public upon them,** so that they will be occupied like the other Elders of Israel **and they will cease [*kalin*]** prophesying, **on their own.** Due to the burden of public responsibility they would not be able to be prophets.

Moshe's response, *mi yiten ki yitein Hashem rucho aleihem*, is thus the expression of a man who genuinely desires that all of Israel be *nevi'im* — who shows no possessiveness about divine inspiration, even when told (as far as Yehoshua knew) that others were prophesying. This quality of *anivus* is itself what the Gemara highlights as the greatness of Moshe's character.

## Sanhedrin Makeup

### I Don't Know

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִין לְהָבִיא עוֹד שְׁלֹשָׁה?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna derives the *halakha* that there are twenty-three judges on a lesser Sanhedrin from the verses: "And the congregation shall judge," and: "And the congregation shall save" (Numbers 35:24–25). The mishna understands that the term "congregation" is referring to ten judges, so that the two congregations, one in each verse, total twenty judges. The mishna then asks: **From where** is it derived **to bring three more** judges to the court? The mishna answers: The implication of the verse: "You shall not follow a multitude to convict" (Exodus 23:2), is that your inclination after a majority to exonerate is not like your inclination after a majority to convict, and a conviction must be by a majority of two.

The Gemara now turns to the procedural and compositional requirements of the *Sanhedrin*. One issue concerns a judge who says *eini yodeia* — I do not know. Such a judge is effectively recused, treated *k'man d'leiseih damei* — as if he were not there. Importantly, even if that judge subsequently wants to offer a reason and participate in the second round of deliberations after additional judges have been added, we do not hear from him — *lo sham'inan lei*. This is a striking rule: not only can he not vote, but his voice is entirely removed from the proceeding.

### Odd Numbers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, לְרָעָה עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ. אִי אַחַד עָשָׂר מְזַכִּין וּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מְחַיְּיבִין – אַכַּתִּי חַד הוּא. אִי עֲשָׂרָה מְזַכִּין וּשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר מְחַיְּיבִין – תְּלָתָא הָווּ. אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אִי אַתָּה מוֹצֵא אֶלָּא בְּמוֹסִיפִין, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל, וּבְסַנְהֶדְרִי גְּדוֹלָה, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר שִׁבְעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Ultimately, you do not find** an occurrence of the inclination **for evil according to** a majority of **two** judges. **If eleven** judges vote to **acquit** the defendant **and twelve** vote to **convict, this is still** only a majority of **one,** and **if ten** vote to **acquit and thirteen** vote to **convict, they are** a majority of **three.** With a court of twenty-three judges, there is no possible way to convict with a majority of two. **Rabbi Abbahu says: You do not find** such a scenario **except** in a case **where they add** two additional judges because one of the judges abstained from the deliberation, the other judges are split in their decisions, and the two added judges both vote to convict. **And** this is a possibility **according to** all *tanna'im*, **and in** a case tried by **the Great Sanhedrin according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, who says** there are **seventy** judges on the Great Sanhedrin. With an even number, it is possible to have a majority of two.

This leads to the question of why the *Sanhedrin* must have an odd number of judges. The requirement is rooted in the rules of *dinei nefashos*: a conviction requires a majority of two. But as the Gemara demonstrates, with 23 judges you can never achieve a majority of two through the standard configuration if one judge says *eini yodeia*. If one judge recuses himself and we are now effectively at 22, and we add pairs to restore odd numbers — we go to 24. Now a vote of 13 to 10 yields a majority of three, but a vote of 12 to 10 yields a majority of two. That case — 12 against 10 — is precisely the case of *mosifin*, the case where judges were added, and according to all opinions this is achievable. Rabbi Abbahu notes that in the *Sanhedrin Gedola* according to Rabbi Yehudah — who holds the larger court has 70, not 71 — the same logic applies: you can achieve 36 against 34 through the same mechanism.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בְּמוֹסִיפִין עוֹשִׂין בֵּית דִּין שָׁקוּל לְכַתְּחִילָּה. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הַאי דְּקָאָמַר ״אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ״ כְּמַאן דְּאִיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי, וְאִי אָמַר מִילְּתָא – שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּהַאי דְּקָאָמַר ״אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ״ כְּמַאן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי, וְאִי אָמַר טַעְמָא – לָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Abbahu says: When they add** additional judges, they **create a court** consisting of **an even** number of judges ***ab initio*.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** What is the novelty in Rabbi Abbahu's statement? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say: This** judge **who says: I do not know, is** viewed **as one who is** still **there, and if he says something** afterward, **we listen to him** and include him in the count, so there are actually an odd number of judges on the court; therefore, Rabbi Abbahu **teaches us that this** judge **who says: I do not know, is** viewed **as one who is not** still **there, and if he says a reason** to rule in a certain manner afterward, **we do not listen to him.** Consequently, the court consists of an even number of judges.

Rabbi Abbahu further rules that when adding judges in the *mosifin* process, one may create a *beis din shakul* — an evenly divided court — as an acceptable *lechatchila* in that situation. One might have thought that the judge who said *eini yodeia* is still counted as present (*k'man d'iseih damei*), keeping the effective total at 23 and preserving odd numbers. The Gemara clarifies: no — once he says *eini yodeia* he is treated as entirely absent, which is precisely why we add judges and why an even division becomes permissible in that context.

### Unanimous Guilty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: סַנְהֶדְרִי שֶׁרָאוּ כּוּלָּן לְחוֹבָה – פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דִּגְמִירִי הֲלָנַת דִּין לְמֶעְבַּד לֵיהּ זְכוּתָא, וְהָנֵי תּוּ לָא חָזוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Kahana says:** In **a Sanhedrin where all** the judges **saw** fit **to convict** the defendant in a case of capital law, they **acquit him.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning** for this *halakha*? It is **since** it **is learned** as a tradition that **suspension of the trial** overnight is necessary in order **to create** a possibility of **acquittal.** The *halakha* is that they may not issue the guilty verdict on the same day the evidence was heard, as perhaps over the course of the night one of the judges will think of a reason to acquit the defendant. **And** as **those** judges all saw fit to convict him they **will not see** any **further** possibility to acquit **him,** because there will not be anyone arguing for such a verdict. Consequently, he cannot be convicted.

Rav Kahana states a rule familiar to many: if the entire *Sanhedrin* immediately voted that the defendant is guilty — *rau kullan le-chovah* — the defendant is acquitted (*potrin oso*). The rationale reflects the principle of *halanas din*, the requirement to deliberate overnight before imposing a capital verdict, precisely so that one or more judges can reconsider and perhaps find some argument in the defendant's favor. A unanimous instant guilty verdict is itself evidence that no one is genuinely arguing on his behalf. Since the Torah demands that someone advocate for the accused — as expressed in the formula *v'shafetu ha-eidah v'hitzilu ha-eidah* — a court in which every member immediately condemned the defendant has, by definition, failed to fulfill this function.

### Physical & Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין בְּסַנְהֶדְרִי אֶלָּא בַּעֲלֵי קוֹמָה, וּבַעֲלֵי חׇכְמָה, וּבַעֲלֵי מַרְאֶה, וּבַעֲלֵי זִקְנָה, וּבַעֲלֵי כְשָׁפִים, וְיוֹדְעִים בְּשִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן – שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא סַנְהֶדְרִי שׁוֹמַעַת מִפִּי הַמְתוּרְגְּמָן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** They **place on the** Great **Sanhedrin only** men **of** high **stature, and of wisdom, and of** pleasant **appearance, and of** suitable **age** so that they will be respected. **And** they must also be **masters of sorcery,** i.e., they know the nature of sorcery, so that they can judge sorcerers, **and** they must **know** all **seventy languages** in order **that the Sanhedrin will not** need to **hear** testimony **from the mouth of a translator** in a case where a witness speaks a different language.

Rabbi Yochanan enumerates the ideal qualifications for members of the *Sanhedrin*: they must be *ba'alei komah* — people of notable stature; *ba'alei chochmah* — learned and wise; *ba'alei mareh* — of good appearance; *ba'alei ziknah* — advanced in years and presumably in sagacity; and *ba'alei kishafim* — knowledgeable in the ways of witchcraft, so as to be able to counteract sorcery that defendants might employ, as Rashi explains. These qualifications include explicitly physical traits alongside intellectual ones — a recognition that human beings respond to appearance and bearing, just as the beauty of the *Beis ha-Mikdash* serves a purpose beyond the purely spiritual.

### 70 Languages

They must also be *yod'im b'shiv'im lashon* — knowledgeable in 70 languages. The Gemara clarifies that this requirement does not mean every member of the *Sanhedrin* must personally know all 70 languages; rather, enough members must know them so that the court does not have to rely exclusively on a translator. *Shelo tehei Sanhedrin shoma'as mipi hamisurgeman* — the *Sanhedrin* should not be entirely dependent on hearsay translation. At minimum, some members must hear testimony directly in the original language.

### Sheretz Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין אֶלָּא מִי שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לְטַהֵר אֶת הַשֶּׁרֶץ מִן הַתּוֹרָה. אָמַר רַב: אֲנִי אָדוּן וַאֲטַהֲרֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** They **place on the Sanhedrin only one who knows** how **to render** a carcass of **a creeping animal pure by Torah law.** The judges on the Sanhedrin must be so skilled at logical reasoning that they could even produce a convincing argument that creeping animals, which the Torah states explicitly are ritually impure, are actually pure. **Rav said: I will discuss** the *halakha* of the creeping animal **and render it pure,** i.e., I am able to demonstrate how it is possible to construct such a proof:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה נָחָשׁ שֶׁמֵּמִית וּמַרְבֶּה טוּמְאָה, טָהוֹר; שֶׁרֶץ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מֵמִית וּמַרְבֶּה טוּמְאָה, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא טָהוֹר? וְלָא הִיא, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקּוֹץ בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If a snake, which kills** other creatures whose carcasses are impure **and** thereby **increases impurity** in the world, is itself nevertheless **pure,** as it is not included in the list of impure creeping animals, then concerning **a creeping animal that does not kill and** does not **increase impurity, isn't it logical that it should be pure?** This argument is rejected: **But it is not so;** the logic of the *halakha* of a creeping animal is **just as it is** concerning the *halakha* **with regard to an ordinary thorn,** which can injure people or animals and can even kill and thereby increase impurity, but is nevertheless pure. It is therefore apparent that this consideration is not relevant to the *halakhot* of impurity.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav adds another memorable qualification: one may not seat on the *Sanhedrin* anyone who cannot argue that a *sheretz* — one of the eight categories of small creeping creatures that transmit *tumah* — is in fact *tahor* according to the Torah, at least as a counterfactual. Rav himself demonstrates: a snake kills people and animals, generating *tumah* through the resulting corpse, yet the snake itself is *tahor*. If so, a *sheretz* — which does not kill — should certainly be *tahor* by a *kal vachomer*. The Gemara immediately rejects this argument: *midi dehavei akoitz b'alma* — a thorn also kills and is *tahor*, so clearly lethality is irrelevant to *tumah* status. The *kal vachomer* therefore fails.

Yet the requirement itself remains. What is its point? Rabbeinu Tam in Tosafos asks why we would want judges who can construct invalid counterfactual arguments. The deeper lesson, as the Gemara implies, is that the very capacity to construct clever arguments from sources — even arguments that are ultimately wrong — could, in the wrong hands, pervert the law. Anyone can invoke the same texts and methods to reach a wrong conclusion. What distinguishes a proper *dayan* is not cleverness alone, but integrity, fidelity to the *Torah*, and an honest sense of what the law truly means. The ability to argue a *sheretz* is *tahor* tests whether someone understands the difference between argumentation and truth.

### City Language Needs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל עִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שְׁנַיִם לְדַבֵּר וְאֶחָד לִשְׁמוֹעַ, אֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין בָּהּ סַנְהֶדְרִי. וּבְבֵיתֵּר הֲווֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה, וּבְיַבְנֶה אַרְבָּעָה: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְשִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי דָּן לִפְנֵיהֶם בַּקַּרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **any city that does not have** among its residents **two** men who are able **to speak** all seventy languages **and one** additional man who is able **to listen** to and understand statements made in all the languages, even if he cannot speak all of them, **they do not place** a lesser **Sanhedrin** there. The members of the Sanhedrin do not all need to know all of the languages, but there must be at least this minimum number. **And in Beitar there were three** individuals who were able to speak all seventy languages, **and in Yavne** there were **four,** and they were: **Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Yehoshua, and Rabbi Akiva, and Shimon HaTimni,** who was not an ordained Sage, and he would therefore **deliberate before** the other judges while seated **on the ground,** not among the rows of Sages.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav further rules: a city that does not have two people who can speak a foreign language and one additional person who can understand it may not seat a *Sanhedrin*. This is a minimum linguistic infrastructure requirement. The reason is consistent with the broader concern: members of the court must be able to directly communicate with and understand parties who appear before them.

### Beitar & Yavneh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: שְׁלִישִׁית – חֲכָמָה, רְבִיעִית – אֵין לְמַעְלָה הֵימֶנָּה. הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁנִיָּה – חֲכָמָה, שְׁלִישִׁית – אֵין לְמַעְלָה הֵימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this from a *baraita*: **A third,** i.e., a Sanhedrin that has three individuals who can speak all seventy languages, is **a wise** Sanhedrin, and if it also has **a fourth** such person, **there is no** court **above it,** meaning that there is no need for additional language experts. Apparently the minimum requirement is three people who can speak the languages, not two. The Gemara answers: Rav **states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **the following *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: A Sanhedrin that has **a second** language expert **is wise;** and if it also has **a third, there is no** court **above it.**

The Gemara illustrates this with historical examples. In Beitar there were three individuals capable of meeting this standard; in Yavneh there were four: Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Yehoshua, Rabbi Akiva, and Shimon haTimni. Shimon haTimni is notable — he did not bear the title *Rabbi*, having not received *semikhah*, and he sat on the ground (*dan lifneihem b'karka*), participating in deliberations without being a fully ordained member of the court. The Gemara then records a tension: a *baraisa* suggests that three who know languages is praiseworthy, and four is unparalleled — yet we said that two who speak and one who understands is the minimum. The resolution is that this *tanna* follows the position that three is praiseworthy and two is already a mark of wisdom, consistent with the figures at Beitar and Yavneh.

## Rabbinic Titles

### Learning vs. Deliberating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵידִין לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים – לֵוִי מֵרַבִּי. דָּנִין לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים – שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן עַזַּאי, וְשִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן זוֹמָא, וְחָנָן הַמִּצְרִי, וַחֲנַנְיָא בֶּן חֲכִינַאי. רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק מַתְנֵי חֲמִשָּׁה: שִׁמְעוֹן, שִׁמְעוֹן, וְשִׁמְעוֹן, חָנָן, וַחֲנַנְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Since the *baraita* stated that Shimon HaTimni would deliberate before them on the ground, the Gemara now lists various standard formulations used to introduce the statements of various Sages throughout the generations. If a source says: **It was learned from the Sages,** the intention is that this was a statement made by the Sage **Levi** who sat before and learned **from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. If it says: They **deliberated before the Sages,** this is referring to **Shimon ben Azzai, and Shimon ben Zoma, and Ḥanan the Egyptian, and Ḥananya ben Ḥakhinai. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak** would **teach five** names for this list: **Shimon** ben Azzai, **Shimon** ben Zoma, **and Shimon** HaTimni, **Ḥanan** the Egyptian, **and Ḥananya** ben Ḥakhinai.

The mention of *dan lifneihem b'karka* — deliberating before the sages on the ground — prompts the Gemara to identify a series of technical phrases used throughout the Talmud, each of which designates a specific sage or group of sages. This is not a pure digression: the Gemara is operating in the context of *Sanhedrin* composition, and understanding the different statuses of sages — those who deliberated, those who merely learned, those with and without *semikhah* — is directly relevant.

When a Talmudic passage says *lemidim lifnei chachamim* — someone who was learning before the sages — this refers to Levi, who studied under Rebbi. When a passage says *danim lifnei chachamim* — deliberating before the sages — it refers to Shimon ben Azzai, Shimon ben Zoma, Chanan haMitzri, and Chananyah ben Chakinai. Significantly, none of these bear the title *Rabbi*: they were brilliant students who participated in the deliberations of the *Sanhedrin* without having received formal ordination. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds a fifth to this group: Shimon haTimni, already mentioned in connection with Yavneh, bringing the total to five.

### Regional Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבּוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁבְּבָבֶל – רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל. רַבּוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – רַבִּי אַבָּא. דַּיָּינֵי גוֹלָה – קַרְנָא. דַּיָּינֵי דְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי. דַּיָּינֵי דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא – רַב פָּפָּא בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל. דַּיָּינֵי דִּנְהַרְדְּעָא – רַב אַדָּא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי. סָבֵי דְּסוּרָא – רַב הוּנָא וְרַב חִסְדָּא. סָבֵי דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא – רַב יְהוּדָה וְרַב עֵינָא. חֲרִיפֵי דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא – עֵיפָה וַאֲבִימִי בְּנֵי רַחֲבָה. אָמוֹרָאֵי דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא – רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף. אָמוֹרָאֵי דִּנְהַרְדָּעֵי – רַב חָמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The expression: **Our Rabbis that are in Babylonia,** is referring to **Rav and Shmuel.** The expression: **Our Rabbis that are in Eretz Yisrael,** is referring to **Rabbi Abba.** The expression: **The judges of the Diaspora,** is a reference to the Sage **Karna.** The phrase: **The judges of Eretz Yisrael,** is a reference to **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi.** The phrase: **The judges of Pumbedita,** is referring to **Rav Pappa bar Shmuel,** who was the head of the court there, and: **The judges of Neharde'a,** is a reference to the court headed by **Rav Adda bar Minyumi.** The term: **The Elders of Sura,** is referring to **Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda,** and: **The Elders of Pumbedita,** is referring to **Rav Yehuda and Rav Eina. The sharp ones of Pumbedita** are **Eifa and Avimi, the sons of Raḥava.** The expression: **The *amora'im* of Pumbedita,** is referring to **Rabba and Rav Yosef,** and the phrase: **The *amora'im* of Neharde'a,** is referring to **Rav Ḥama.**

The Gemara continues with regional designations. *Rabboseinu she'be-Bavel* — our teachers in Babylonia — refers to Rav and Shmuel. *Rabboseinu she'be-Eretz Yisrael* — our teachers in the land of Israel — refers to Rabbi Abba and Rabbi Ami. *Dayyanei golah* — the judges of the exile — refers to Karna. *Dayyanei Eretz Yisrael* — the judges of the land of Israel — refers to Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi. *Dayyanei de'Pumbedisa* — the judges of Pumbedisa — refers to Rav Pappa bar Shmuel alone. *Dayyanei de'Neharde'a* — the judges of Nehardea — refers to Rav Adda bar Minyumi.

### Amoraim Roles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהַרְבְּלָאֵי מַתְנוּ – רָמֵי בַּר בְּרַבִּי. אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב – רַב הוּנָא. וְהָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: ״אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב״! אֶלָּא, רַב הַמְנוּנָא. אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא – רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה. שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא. מַחֲכוּ עֲלַהּ בְּמַעְרְבָא – רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** If it says: The Sages **of Neharbela taught,** this is referring to **Rami bar Berabi,** and the statement: **They say** in **the school of Rav,** is a reference to **Rav Huna.** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rav Huna** sometimes **say** with regard to a given *halakha*: **They say** in **the school of Rav?** From this, it is apparent that a statement introduced by that formula cannot be made by Rav Huna himself, as Rav Huna quotes someone else with that introduction. The Gemara responds: **Rather,** the expression: They say in the school of Rav, must be referring to **Rav Hamnuna.** The formula: **They say in the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, is referring to **Rabbi Yirmeya;** the expression: **They sent** a message **from there,** meaning from Eretz Yisrael, is referring to **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina;** and the statement: **They laughed at it in the West,** means that **Rabbi Elazar** did not accept a particular opinion.

The identifications continue with the Babylonian academies. *Sabei de'Sura* — the elders of Sura — refers to Rav Huna and Rav Chisda. *Sabei de'Pumbedisa* — the elders of Pumbedisa — refers to Rav Yehudah and Rav Eina. *Charifei de'Pumbedisa* — the sharp ones of Pumbedisa — refers to Eifa and Avimi the sons of Rechava. *Amora'ei de'Pumbedisa* — the Amoraim of Pumbedisa — refers to Rabba and Rav Yosef, the leading Amoraim of that academy. *Amora'ei de'Neharde'a* — the Amoraim of Nehardea — refers to Rav Chama of Neharbal.

The phrase *masni* — he taught — refers to Rami bar Berabi. *Amri bei Rav* — they say in the house of Rav — the Gemara first suggests this refers to Rav Huna, but immediately raises a challenge: we have a tradition that Rav Huna himself said *amri bei Rav*, citing the academy. Rav Huna therefore cannot himself be the referent of that phrase. The Gemara revises: *amri bei Rav* refers to Rav Hamnuna, though Rashi and Tosafot debate whether Rav Hamnuna is himself the referent or whether he is the one who transmitted sayings in the name of Rav Huna's academy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא! אֶלָּא אֵיפוֹךְ: שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם – רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר; מַחֲכוּ עֲלַהּ בְּמַעְרְבָא – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** in one instance it is reported that: **They sent** a message **from there** that began: **According to the statement of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina.** This indicates that the expression: They sent from there, is not itself a reference to a statement of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina. The Gemara answers: **Rather, reverse** the statements. The phrase: **They sent from there,** is a reference to **Rabbi Elazar,** and: **They laughed at it in the West,** means that **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina** did not accept a particular opinion.

*Amri be'ma'arava* — they say in the west, meaning in Eretz Yisrael — refers to Rabbi Yirmiya. *Shalchu misam* — they sent from there — initially attributed to Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina, and *machaku aleih be'ma'arava* — they mocked this in the west — attributed to Rabbi Elazar. But the Gemara challenges this assignment: we have a case where *shalchu misam* is followed by an opinion cited in the name of Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina explicitly, so clearly that phrase cannot refer to him. The Gemara resolves: reverse the assignment — *shalchu misam* is Rabbi Elazar, and *machaku aleih be'ma'arava* is Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina.

## City Population

### 120 People

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה יְהֵא בָּעִיר וִיהֵא רְאוּיָה לְסַנְהֶדְרִין? מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים וְכוּ׳. מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיְיהוּ? עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה כְּנֶגֶד סַנְהֶדְרִי קְטַנָּה, וְשָׁלֹשׁ שׁוּרוֹת שֶׁל עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה – הֲרֵי תִּשְׁעִים וְתַרְתֵּי, וַעֲשָׂרָה בַּטְלָנִין שֶׁל בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת – הֲרֵי מְאָה וּתְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And how many** men must **be in the city for** it **to be eligible for** a lesser **Sanhedrin?** The opinion of the first *tanna* is that there must be **120** men. The Gemara asks: **What is the relevance of** the number **120?** The Gemara explains that **23** are needed to **correspond to** the number of members of the **lesser Sanhedrin, and** it is necessary for there to be **three rows of 23** students who sit before the lesser Sanhedrin to learn and also to advise them; that **is** a total of **92** people. **And** since there also need to be **10 idlers of the synagogue,** people who are free from urgent work and are always sitting in the synagogue to take care of its repair and the other needs of the public, that **would be 102.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁנֵי סוֹפְרִים, וּשְׁנֵי חַזָּנִין, וּשְׁנֵי בַּעֲלֵי דִינִין, וּשְׁנֵי עֵדִים, וּשְׁנֵי זוֹמְמִין, וּשְׁנֵי זוֹמְמֵי זוֹמְמִין – הֲרֵי מְאָה וְאַרְבֵּיסַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in addition there are **two scribes** required for the Sanhedrin, **and two bailiffs, and two litigants** who will come to be judged. **And** there are **two witnesses** for one side, **and two** witnesses who could render those witnesses **conspiring** witnesses by testifying that they were elsewhere at the time of the alleged incident, **and two** additional witnesses who could testify against the witnesses who rendered the first witnesses **conspiring** witnesses, rendering the second pair **conspiring** witnesses. All of these are necessary in order for a trial to take place, as is described in Deuteronomy 19:15–21. Therefore, **there are** so far a total of **114** men who must be in the city.

The Gemara now addresses the minimum population required for a city to establish its own *Sanhedrin ketana* of 23. The *chachamim* require a city of 120 people. The Gemara asks *mai avidtaihu* — how is this figure derived? The calculation proceeds as follows: the court itself requires 23 judges. Beyond that, the *Sanhedrin* in session was accompanied by three rows of 23 scholars each — qualified replacements who could be elevated to the bench if a judge needed to be replaced or recuse himself. That gives 23 plus 69, totaling 92. Add to that 10 *batlanim* — communal functionaries permanently available to ensure a *minyan* in the synagogue — and the total reaches 102. The remaining positions include two scribes, two court attendants, two litigants, two witnesses, two witnesses who could render the first pair *zomemin*, and two who could render those *zomemin* — adding another 10, bringing the total to 112 and eventually 120 once the full tally is complete, though a separate *baraisa* accounting for various communal roles brings the count to 140, which Rashi reconciles by noting that one person can hold multiple roles.

### Ten Services

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: כׇּל עִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ אֵין תַּלְמִיד חָכָם רַשַּׁאי לָדוּר בְּתוֹכָהּ: בֵּית דִּין מַכִּין וְעוֹנְשִׁין, וְקוּפָּה שֶׁל צְדָקָה נִגְבֵּית בִּשְׁנַיִם וּמִתְחַלֶּקֶת בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, וּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, וּבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ, וּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, רוֹפֵא, וְאוּמָּן, וְלַבְלָר, וְטַבָּח, וּמְלַמֵּד תִּינוֹקוֹת. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אָמְרוּ: אַף מִינֵי פֵירָא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמִּינֵי פֵירָא מְאִירִין אֶת הָעֵינַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it **is taught** in a *baraita*: **A Torah scholar is not permitted to reside in any city that does not have these ten things: A court that** has the authority to **flog and punish** transgressors; **and** a charity **fund** for which monies **are collected by two** people **and distributed by three,** as required by *halakha*. This leads to a requirement for another three people in the city. **And a synagogue; and a bathhouse; and** a public **bathroom; a doctor; and a bloodletter; and a scribe** [***velavlar***] to write sacred scrolls and necessary documents; **and** a ritual **slaughterer; and a teacher of young children.** With these additional requirements there are a minimum of 120 men who must be residents of the city. **They said in the name of Rabbi Akiva:** The city must **also** have **varieties of fruit, because varieties of fruit illuminate the eyes.**

The *baraisa* teaches: any city that does not have these ten things, a Torah scholar is not permitted to reside within it. The ten are: a *beis din* empowered to flog and to punish; a *kuppah shel tzedakah* — a communal charity fund, collected by two people and distributed by three; a *beis knesses* — a synagogue; a *beis ha-merchatz* — a bathhouse; a *beis ha-kisei* — a privy; a physician; a *uman* — a bloodletter; a *lavlar* — a scribe; a *tabbach* — a butcher (according to the version that includes this); and a *melammed tinokos* — a teacher of young children. Rabbi Akiva adds: the city must also have various species of fruit, because the different varieties of fruit brighten the eyes and help maintain the clarity of mind needed for Torah study.

### Alternative Minimums

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: מָאתַיִם וְשִׁבְעִים וְשִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** There must be 230 men in the city in order for it to be eligible for a lesser Sanhedrin, corresponding to the ministers of tens appointed in the wilderness by Moses at the suggestion of his father-in-law, Yitro (see Exodus 18:21). Each member of the Sanhedrin can be viewed as a judge with responsibility for ten men. It **is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָאתַיִם שִׁבְעִים וּשְׁמֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The number of people needed in a city for it to be eligible for a lesser Sanhedrin is **278.** This number is based on the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya, who requires 230 men, but requires an additional 47 men in case it happens that the Sages of the court reach a situation where they are not able to arrive at a verdict, and pairs of judges need to be added, potentially increasing the number of judges on the court from 23 to 70, a maximum of 47 additional judges.

Rabbi Nechemiah sets the minimum population at 230, so that each person is fit to serve as a commander of ten — an allusion to the organizational structure Moshe established in the wilderness. Rebbi sets the figure at 277, and a *baraisa* cites Rebbi as requiring 278. The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction between 277 and 278 by reference to the disagreement between Rabbi Yehudah and the *chachamim* about whether the *Sanhedrin Gedola* consists of 70 or 71: the court of 23 may need to grow by adding judges until it reaches the size of the larger court, and the different totals reflect whether one counts 70 or 71 at the top.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: מָאתַיִם שִׁבְעִים וּשְׁמֹנָה! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא – רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The number of men needed in a city for it to be eligible for a lesser Sanhedrin is **278?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** *baraita* that said Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi requires 277 residents is stated in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that there are 70 judges on the Great Sanhedrin, and **this** *baraita* that quoted the number as 278 is stated in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that there are 71.

## Census Count

### Military Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשַׂמְתָּ עֲלֵיהֶם שָׂרֵי אֲלָפִים שָׂרֵי מֵאוֹת שָׂרֵי חֲמִשִּׁים וְשָׂרֵי עֲשָׂרֹת״. ״שָׂרֵי אֲלָפִים״ – שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת, ״שָׂרֵי מֵאוֹת״ – שֵׁשֶׁת אֲלָפִים, ״שָׂרֵי חֲמִשִּׁים״ – שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר אֶלֶף, ״שָׂרֵי עֲשָׂרוֹת״ – שֵׁשֶׁת רִיבּוֹא. נִמְצְאוּ דַּיָּינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שִׁבְעַת רִיבּוֹא וּשְׁמוֹנַת אֲלָפִים וְשֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya, which links the issue of the lesser Sanhedrin with the appointment of the ministers in the wilderness, the Gemara relates: **The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"And place over them ministers of thousands, ministers of hundreds, ministers of fifties, and ministers of tens"** (Exodus 18:21). The number of **ministers of thousands** was **600,** as there were 600,000 men in total; the number of **ministers of hundreds** was **6,000;** of **ministers of fifties, 12,000,** and of **ministers of tens, 60,000.** Therefore, **it is found** that the total number of **judges of the Jewish people** was **78,600,** and suitable people were found to fill all of these positions.

### Total 78,600

The Gemara concludes the chapter by drawing on the organizational hierarchy that Moshe established among the people. The *baraisa* cites the verse listing the military commanders: *sarei alafim* — commanders of thousands; *sarei meos* — commanders of hundreds; *sarei chamishim* — commanders of fifties; *sarei asaros* — commanders of tens.

The calculation unfolds as follows. *Sarei alafim* — 600,000 divided by 1,000 yields 600 commanders of thousands. *Sarei meos* — 600,000 divided by 100 yields 6,000 commanders of hundreds. *Sarei chamishim* — 600,000 divided by 50 yields 12,000 commanders of fifties. *Sarei asaros* — 600,000 divided by 10 yields 60,000 commanders of tens. Adding these together: 600 plus 6,000 plus 12,000 plus 60,000 equals 78,600. Thus the total number of judges of Israel appointed by Moshe was *sheva ribo u'shmonas alafim v'shesh meos* — 78,600. And with that calculation, the first chapter of *Sanhedrin* comes to a close.
