# Temurah 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chatat Meita Categories

### Five Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְלַד חַטָּאת, וּתְמוּרַת חַטָּאת, וְחַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ — יָמוּתוּ, וְשֶׁעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ, וְשֶׁאָבְדָה וְנִמְצֵאת בַּעֲלַת מוּם, אִם מִשֶּׁכִּפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים — תָּמוּת, וְאֵינָהּ עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The offspring of a sin offering and the substitute for a sin offering, and a sin offering whose owner has died shall** be sequestered and left to **die. And** with regard to a sin offering that is unfit for sacrifice because **its** first **year** from birth **has passed, and** a sin offering **that was lost and** when it was **found,** it was **blemished, if** it was **after the owner achieved atonement** through sacrifice of another animal as a sin offering, the blemished animal **shall die, and it does not render** a non-sacred animal exchanged for it **a substitute.**

The new perek begins at the very bottom of 21b and is devoted entirely to the laws of *chatat*, a subject that merits its own chapter precisely because of the unique category of *chatat meita* — the *chatat* that must be left to die. While we are familiar with the idea that there are five categories of *chatat meita*, a close reading of our *Mishna* reveals that the precise contours of those five are considerably less straightforward than one might assume.

The *Mishna* opens with three unambiguous cases: *vlad chatat* (the offspring of a *chatat*), *temurat chatat* (a substitution made for a *chatat*), and a *chatat* whose owners have died, rendering it permanently incapable of fulfilling its atonement purpose. In all three cases the ruling is simply *yamutu* — they are locked away and left to die. The animal cannot be actively killed; since it is *kodashim*, no form of *shechita* is permitted, nor may it be destroyed in any other manner. One simply withholds food and allows it to perish.

### Absolute vs Conditional

Beyond those three clear cases, the *Mishna* introduces two further categories that are conventionally listed among the five, but which are here presented with an important qualification. The *Mishna* speaks of an animal that has passed its first year (*avar shnata*) and of one that was lost and subsequently found to be *ba'alat mum* (blemished). What is striking is that neither of these two cases is stated as an absolute rule. Rather, the *Mishna* frames them as conditional: only under certain circumstances do these animals enter the category of *chatat meita*.

### Manuscript Reading Analysis

There is a significant textual question surrounding the exact *girsa* of the *Mishna*. The printed text reads *im mishe'kiperu ba'alim tamut* — if after the owners have atoned, it must die — which would limit the case to where the animal was found only after the owners had already achieved atonement through a replacement. Both Rashi and Tosafot, however, emend the *girsa* to *im kiperu ba'alim tamut*, meaning: even if the animal was found beforehand, once the owners subsequently went ahead and brought another *korban* for atonement, it must die.

This distinction is not merely textual — it maps onto a major *halachic* debate about the precise moment at which *nitkapru ba'alim b'acher* (atonement through another) creates the status of *chatat meita*. The more obvious case is where the animal was lost, was still missing when the replacement was brought, and was only found afterwards. In that scenario, when the lost animal was being dealt with it already bore the full status of *nitkapru ba'acher*, and everyone agrees it must die. The more complex and debated case is where the animal was found before the replacement was brought. Here there is a dispute — which will be elaborated later in the *Mishna* — between Rabbi (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) and the *Chachamim*. Rabbi holds that even being lost at the time the replacement was designated is sufficient to create *chatat meita* status; the *Chachamim* hold that only finding it after the owners have actually completed their atonement creates that status.

Rashi's approach is that the *Mishna* here is addressing a case of a "double problem" — the animal was found before the atonement was completed, yet it has an additional deficiency (either *ba'alat mum* or *avar shnata*) on top of having been lost. This double problem, in Rashi's view, is what leads to *tamut* status even according to Rabbi in cases where the animal was found prior to atonement. Tosafot reads the *gemara* differently and we will follow Rashi's approach throughout.

Practically, this means that what the *Mishna* presents is not two separate categories standing independently, but rather a combination: being lost, plus an additional problem preventing the animal from being brought. When both conditions are present and the owners have sought atonement through another animal, the first one must die. Without the combination, there is no *chatat meita*.

It is worth noting that even when an animal does not fall into the *chatat meita* category, the proceeds of a *chatat* that cannot be used (*motar chatat*) go toward an *olah*, as in the case of *nedavat tzibbur*.

### Chatat Meita Implications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין, וְאִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא כִּיפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים — תִּרְעֶה עַד שֶׁתִּסְתָּאֵב, וְתִמָּכֵר וְיָבִיא בְּדָמֶיהָ אַחֶרֶת, וְעוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה וּמוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, one **may not derive benefit** from any of these sin offerings *ab initio*, **but** if one derived benefit from them, after the fact, he is **not** liable to bring a sin offering for **misuse** of consecrated items. **And if** the lost animal was found and discovered to be unfit **before the owner achieved atonement** for his sin with a different animal, **it shall graze until it becomes blemished, and** then **it shall be sold. And he must bring another** sin offering **with the money** received from the sale. **And** this animal **renders** a non-sacred animal exchanged for it **a substitute, and** one who derives benefit from this animal is liable for **misusing it.**

Once an animal has acquired the status of *chatat meita*, several important *halachic* consequences follow. First, it cannot serve as a basis for *temura*. The ability to make *temura* is premised on the animal eventually making its way to the *mizbe'ach* — directly or through its proceeds. An animal designated to die will never reach the *mizbe'ach* and therefore cannot generate a valid *temura*. Second, *lo nehenin velo mo'alin* — not only is one prohibited from deriving benefit from it, one does not violate the biblical prohibition of *me'ila* if one does benefit from it. Since the animal's *kedusha* is never destined to reach the *Beit Hamikdash* in any form, it does not carry the full *kedusha* that would trigger *me'ila*.

By contrast, if the owners have not yet atoned through another means — *im ad shelo kiperu ha-ba'alim* — the animal retains its full sanctity. In that case, one waits for it to develop a *mum*, then redeems it and uses the proceeds to purchase a proper *chatat*. It retains the capacity to generate *temura* and is subject to *me'ila*. The status of *chatat meita* is therefore not simply a function of the animal's physical condition; it is a function of the entire complex of circumstances surrounding the owner's path to atonement.

One might note a practical difficulty: where the owner is under time pressure to achieve *kappara* — a *nazir* awaiting purification, a *yoledes* unable to enter the *Beit Hamikdash* — the requirement to wait for the animal to develop a *mum* before redeeming it could impose a significant delay. Whether one may proactively bring a replacement and thereby deliberately create a *chatat meita* status for the original is a subtle question that the *gemara* does not explicitly address, but the straightforward reading of the *halacha* does not appear to prohibit doing so.

## Reish Lakish Position

### Cemetery Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: חַטָּאת שֶׁעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ, רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ עוֹמֶדֶת בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת, וְרוֹעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Reish Lakish says:** With regard to **a sin offering whose** first **year** from birth **has passed, one considers it as though it is standing in a cemetery,** where a priest cannot enter in order to retrieve it and sacrifice it, **and** it is left to **graze** until it develops a blemish, after which it is sold and the money received in its sale is used to purchase another animal for a sin offering. Since Reish Lakish makes no distinction between an owner who already achieved atonement with another animal and one who did not, it seems that according to Reish Lakish, this is the *halakha* in all cases of a sin offering whose first year from birth has passed.

With the textual and conceptual framework of *chatat meita* in place, the *gemara* turns to a striking statement by Reish Lakish. He declares that a *chatat* that has passed its first year — *avar shnata* — is to be viewed as though it is standing in a cemetery, where the *kohen* cannot access it. One watches over it, lets it graze, and eventually waits for it to develop a *mum* and be redeemed. In Reish Lakish's formulation, *avar shnata* never actually reaches the status of *tamut*; the problem is external and circumstantial rather than a *halachic* transformation of the animal itself.

### Contradiction Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: שֶׁעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ, וְאָבְדָה וְנִמְצֵאת בַּעֲלַת מוּם, אִם אַחַר שֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ בְּעָלִים — מֵתָה, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to a sin offering **whose** first **year** from birth **has passed and** a sin offering that **was lost and** when it was **found** it was **blemished, if** it was **after the owner achieved atonement** through the sacrifice of another animal as a sin offering, the blemished animal is left to **die.** This is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Reish Lakish.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this position from our very *Mishna*, which teaches that *she'avra shnata v'avda v'nimtze'et ba'alat mum, im achar shekiperu ba'alim, meita* — if an animal passed its first year, was lost, and was found to be *ba'alat mum*, and the owners have already atoned through another means, it must die. This appears to contradict Reish Lakish's categorical assertion that *avar shnata* never leads to *meita* status, since the *Mishna* combines *avar shnata* with the scenario of *nitkapru ba'alim* and concludes that it dies. The *gemara* therefore initially declares this a *tiyuvta* — a refutation — of Reish Lakish's position.

### Reish Lakish Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כִּי קָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא ״מֵתָה״ — אַאָבְדָה וְנִמְצֵאת בַּעֲלַת מוּם, אִם אַחַר שֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ בְּעָלִים — תָּמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Reish Lakish** could **say to you: When** the mishna **teaches** in **the first clause** that the animal is sequestered and left to **die,** it is referring only **to** the case where it was **lost and** when it was **found** it was **blemished.** In that situation, **if** it was **after the owner achieved atonement** through the sacrifice of another animal as a sin offering, the blemished animal **shall** be left to **die.** By contrast, a sin offering whose first year from birth has passed is indeed left to graze until it develops a blemish.

Reish Lakish, however, deflects the challenge by reinterpreting the structure of the *Mishna*. He reads the phrase *avar shnata* as a standalone entry whose *halacha* is simply not stated at the end of the *Mishna*. The final ruling — that the animal dies if the owners were atoned after the fact, and grazes if they were not yet atoned — applies only to the last case in the *Mishna*: the animal that was lost and found to be *ba'alat mum*. The case of *avar shnata* appears parenthetically in the list but is not subject to the same conditional ruling at the end. According to Reish Lakish, *avar shnata* is entirely separate and is never in the category of *meita*.

### Rava's Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא כִּיפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים תִּרְעֶה עַד שֶׁתִּסְתָּאֵב״, וְאִי בַּעֲלַת מוּם — הָא מְסָאֲבָא וְקַיְימָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so, say the latter clause: If** the lost animal was found and discovered to be unfit **before the owner achieved atonement** for his sin with a different animal, **it shall graze until it becomes blemished.** Now, **if** the mishna is referring specifically to an animal that was lost and found when it was **blemished,** why is this statement necessary? **Doesn't** it already **stand blemished?**

The *gemara* presses further. If the final section of the *Mishna* — dealing with whether the owners were atoned before or after the animal was found — applies only to the case of *ba'alat mum*, then the instruction to "let it graze until it develops a *mum*" is internally incoherent: if the animal already had a *mum*, what does it mean to wait for one? Rava attempts to resolve this by suggesting that the *Mishna* was dealing with a transitory *mum*, not a permanent one. The animal had a temporary blemish when found; if it was found after the owners atoned, it must die, while if it was found before, you wait for the *mum* to become permanent (*mum kavua*) before redeeming it. The *gemara*, however, ultimately rejects this as well, on two grounds: first, the language of "let it graze until it becomes blemished" is inappropriate for an animal that already has some blemish; second, and more fundamentally — *she'ibur shnaso l'mai hilchasa katani*? — if *avar shnata* is excised entirely from the consequential part of the *Mishna*, what is it doing there at all?

### Avar Shnata Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָתָנֵי, עִבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ וְאָבְדָה, אוֹ אָבְדָה וְנִמְצֵאת בַּעֲלַת מוּם — אַחַר שֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים מֵתָה, קוֹדֶם שֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים תִּרְעֶה עַד שֶׁתִּסְתָּאֵב וְתִימָּכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** a different explanation of the mishna so that it does not contradict the statement of Reish Lakish: **This** is what the mishna **is teaching:** With regard to a sin offering **whose** first **year** from birth **has passed and was lost, or** one that **was lost and** when it was **found** it was **blemished,** which means that in each case, there are two disqualifying factors, the *halakha* is as follows: If the first animal is found **after the owner achieved atonement** with another sin offering, the animal shall be left to **die.** If it is found **before the owner achieved atonement** with another sin offering, **it shall graze until it becomes blemished and** then **it shall be sold.** Reish Lakish, by contrast, is referring to a situation where the sin offering was not lost. In such a case there is only one disqualifying factor, and therefore the animal is left to graze regardless of whether or not the owner achieved atonement with another animal.

Rava therefore proposes a complete reinterpretation that preserves Reish Lakish's position: read the *Mishna* as presenting two parallel cases under the conditional ruling. Either the animal passed its first year *and* was lost (*ibur shnaso v'avda*), or it was lost and found to be *ba'alat mum* (*avda v'nimtze'et ba'alat mum*). In both cases, the ruling is: if the owners were atoned afterwards, it dies; if beforehand, it grazes until redeemable.

What emerges from this reading is quite striking. We began by assuming that *avar shnata* is one of five independent categories of *chatat meita*. Our *Mishna* then showed that it requires combination with *nitkapru ba'alim b'acher*. Reish Lakish now shows that even *nitkapru ba'alim b'acher* by itself is insufficient; the animal must also have been *lost*. And not only must it have been lost — it must have had an additional problem beyond the loss alone, such as being *avar shnata* or *ba'alat mum*. *Avar shnata*, then, is not a category in its own right. It is a subset of *nitkapru ba'alim b'acher*, and even that status requires the double combination of being lost plus having an additional disqualifying characteristic.

## Double Problem Requirement

### Separate Listings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רָבָא הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: אֲבֵידָה דְּלַיְלָה לָא שְׁמַהּ אֲבֵידָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rava** actually **say this,** that an animal whose first year has passed and was then lost is left to die? **But didn't Rava say:** A sin offering **lost at night is not called lost?** In other words, if the offering is lost at a time that it is not fit for sacrifice, the animal is not considered lost. Consequently, provided that the owner finds the sin offering before morning, it is left to graze and not to die, even if the owner designated another animal in its stead. By the same logic, as a sin offering whose first year has passed is not fit for sacrifice as a sin offering, it should not be considered lost even if the owner cannot locate it. Therefore, it should not be left to die.

The *gemara* now asks: given that *avar shnata* and *ba'alat mum* are functionally parallel — both rendering the animal unfit for this particular *korban* and both requiring the additional element of having been lost in order to generate *chatat meita* status — why does the *Mishna* need to list both cases? Would not one suffice to teach the principle?

### Avar Shnata Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא דָּמֵי אֲבֵידָה דְּלֵילְיָא — לָא חַזְיָא לָא לְגוּפַהּ וְלָא לִדְמֵי, אֲבָל הָא — נְהִי דִּלְגוּפַהּ לָא חַזְיָא, לִדְמֵי חַזְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The case of a sin offering lost at night is **not comparable** to that of a sin offering lost after its first year has passed. When a sin offering is **lost at night, it is not fit** at all, **neither for itself,** i.e., to be sacrificed, **nor for** its **value,** to be sold and the proceeds used to purchase another offering. **But** with regard to **this** sin offering that was lost after its first year has passed, **granted that it is not fit for itself,** but **it is fit for** its **value.**

The answer is *tzricha* — both are necessary — but for opposite reasons. Each case contributes something the other cannot teach. If the *Mishna* had mentioned only *avar shnata*, we might have concluded: it is *specifically* the combination of loss with *avar shnata* that triggers *meita* status, because being over a year old renders the animal completely irrelevant — *lo chazya l'milsa* — to the category of *chatat* entirely. An animal more than a year old is not merely unusable for this particular *korban*; it has undergone a categorical change of identity (a *keves* becomes an *ayil*), and one might think that this degree of total irrelevance is what gives the loss its consequence.

### Ba'alat Mum Analysis

The following discussion of *ba'alat mum* and the principles governing *psul* emerges from the continuous *gemara* passage, though the essay's analysis flows into the subsequent sections. The principles being established are: a *ba'alat mum* is entirely unfit for *any* offering even though a blemish is an accidental characteristic; *avar shnata*, by contrast, represents an essential identity shift but remains fit for other offerings. These distinctions are woven throughout the Talmudic analysis.

Conversely, if the *Mishna* had mentioned only *ba'alat mum*, we might have concluded: it is specifically being *ba'alat mum* that, combined with loss, creates *meita* status, because a *ba'alat mum* is entirely unfit for *any* offering — *lo chazya l'hakravah* — even though conceptually a *mum* is an accidental characteristic of the animal rather than an essential one. One might then have thought that *avar shnata*, which is merely irrelevant to *this* offering but could in principle be brought for a *different* offering, does not rise to the level needed to combine with loss and create *meita* status.

### Essential vs Accidental

The distinction the *gemara* is drawing here engages a deep conceptual question about the nature of *psul*. A *ba'alat mum* has what one might call an accidental defect: it is still essentially the same animal, but something external has happened to it that prevents it from being offered. A *avar shnata*, by contrast, represents a categorical identity shift — the passage of time has made it a different type of animal. Yet paradoxically, this identity shift does not render it *completely* irrelevant, since it remains fit for other offerings. The *mum*, despite being accidental, does render it completely unfit for any offering at all.

The result is that each case is both stronger and weaker than the other in different dimensions. One cannot be inferred from the other, and therefore the *Mishna* must state both explicitly.

This analysis, it should be noted, follows Rashi's reading throughout. Tosafot reads the *gemara* in a fundamentally different direction — arguing that the additional problem is not what creates *meita* status but rather that the *chiddush* is that *even* with the additional disqualifying problem the animal is still subject to *tamut*. According to Tosafot the double problem actually argues *against* *meita* status in some sense, and the novelty is that the *halacha* still applies. This reading involves different *girsaot* as well, but the analysis here follows Rashi.

The upshot of all this, stated plainly, is as follows. The *halacha* of *nitkapru ba'alim b'acher* does not apply universally. At one extreme, if one sanctified two animals from the outset intending to use either, brings one, and the other is left over — everyone agrees that is not *chatat meita*; one simply waits for the second to develop a *mum* and uses the proceeds for an *olah*. At the other extreme, if the animal was completely lost, the replacement was brought and the owners fully atoned, and only then was the original found — everyone agrees it is *chatat meita*. The contested middle ground involves an animal that was lost, a replacement was designated (but not yet brought), and the original was then found. In that scenario, Rabbi says it is *meita*; the *Chachamim* say it is not. And even in that contested zone, our *Mishna* teaches that if the animal had a double problem — both being lost and having an additional disqualifying characteristic — even the *Chachamim* might agree it is *meita*. That remains the overall framework as we proceed.

A natural question arises regarding how these *halachot* are ruled upon in practice. The *Rambam* addresses these issues in *Hilchot Pesulei Hamukdashin*, but given the complexity of the *machloket* and the absence of practical *korbanot* today, a definitive review of the *Rambam*'s position is left for separate study.

## Lost at Night

### Night Loss Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: אָמַר רָבָא: אֲבוּדָה דְּלַיְלָה לָא שְׁמַהּ אֲבוּדָה. אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן — מַאי אִירְיָא אֲבוּדָה דְּלַיְלָה? אֲפִילּוּ אֲבוּדָה דְּיוֹם נָמֵי, דְּאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן: אֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה רוֹעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself,** which was cited above: **Rava said:** A sin offering **lost at night,** when one cannot sacrifice an offering, **is not called lost.** Provided that the offering is found before morning, then even if the owner designated another sin offering in its stead, the first sin offering is not left to die. The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this statement? **If we say** that it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who maintain that a sin offering is not left to die unless the owner achieved atonement with another offering, **why** does Rava **specifically** mention that it was **lost at night? Even if it was lost during the day** it is not left to die, **as the Rabbis say:** A sin offering that was **lost at the time** when another animal was **designated** in its stead, but was found before this second animal was sacrificed, is left to **graze** until it develops a blemish.

Having established the framework of the double-problem requirement, the *gemara* introduces a related principle in the name of Rava: *aveidah de'laila lav shemah aveidah* — loss that occurs at night does not constitute genuine loss for these purposes. The reasoning is straightforward: since *korbanot* cannot be brought at night, the entire framework of *zman hakrava* (the time relevant for offering) is inapplicable after dark. If an animal was lost during the night and found before daybreak, there was never a meaningful period during which its absence affected anything. It is therefore as though it was never truly lost.

### Rabbi vs Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלָּא אֲבֵידָה דְּיוֹם, אֲבָל אֲבֵידָה דְּלַיְלָה — אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי מוֹדֶה דְּלִרְעִיָּיה אָזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** you must say that it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who maintains that if a sin offering was lost at the time when another animal was designated in its stead, the first sin offering is left to die even if it was found before the second animal was sacrificed. Rava teaches that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **states** his opinion **only** with regard to an animal **lost during the day, but** if the animal was **lost** only **at night, even Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **concedes that it goes to graze** and is not left to die.

The *gemara* asks: according to whom is this principle relevant? If we are speaking according to the *Rabbanan*, the principle seems unnecessary — according to the *Rabbanan*, even a loss that occurred during the day is not a problem as long as the animal is found before the replacement is actually brought (*avuda bi-sha'at hafrasha ro'eh*). If the *Rabbanan* are lenient even for daytime loss, it goes without saying that nighttime loss presents no problem.

The principle is therefore relevant specifically according to Rabbi, who is otherwise strict and says that even loss at the time the replacement was designated (*sha'at hafrasha*) creates *meita* status. According to Rabbi, one might have thought that nighttime loss — which occurred at an irrelevant hour but nevertheless preceded the designation of a replacement — would also create *meita* status. Rava therefore teaches that even Rabbi agrees: if the loss occurred at night and the animal was found before daybreak, it is considered as though both animals were simultaneously before the owner and the designation of the replacement does not trigger *meita* status.

### Day vs Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן, וְהָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בְּאָבְדָה בִּשְׁעַת כַּפָּרָה. עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן אָבְדָה בִּשְׁעַת כַּפָּרָה מֵתָה, אֶלָּא הֵיכָא דְּעִיקַּר אֲבֵידָתָהּ בַּיּוֹם, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּעִיקַּר אֲבֵידָתָהּ בַּלַּיְלָה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say: Actually,** Rava's statement is **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis. And** as for the question of why Rava specifies that it is lost only at night when the same *halakha* should apply even if it was lost during the day, one may respond: **Here we are dealing with** an animal that is still **lost at the time** when the owner achieves **atonement** with another animal. Rava teaches that when **the Rabbis say** that a sin offering that remained **lost at the time** when the owner achieved **atonement** with another animal is left to **die,** this applies **only where the initial loss** of the animal was **during the day,** when the animal could have been sacrificed. **But** in a case **where the initial loss** of the animal was **at night,** it is **not** left to die. According to this interpretation, Rava is not merely saying that a sin offering is not classified as lost provided that it is found before morning. Rather, he maintains if it was initially lost at night it is never considered lost at all.

Furthermore, the *gemara* advances a second interpretation. Even if one says the principle of *aveidah de'laila* is relevant according to the *Rabbanan*, it is speaking of a case where the animal was lost at night but remained lost into the following day — and was still absent when the atonement was eventually brought. Here the question is whether the *status* of the loss is defined by when it first began or by its ongoing duration. According to this reading, since the initial loss occurred at a time when *zman hakrava* was inapplicable, the loss is never fully "counted" as a genuine *aveidah* — even when the animal remains absent during the day and the replacement is brought. The first occurrence of the loss establishes its character, and since that first occurrence was at night, the *halacha* of *chatat meita* does not apply even according to the *Rabbanan*.

### Irrelevance Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא דָּמֵי אֲבֵידָה דְּלֵילְיָא — לָא חַזְיָא לָא לְגוּפַהּ וְלָא לִדְמֵי, אֲבָל הָא — נְהִי דִּלְגוּפַהּ לָא חַזְיָא, לִדְמֵי חַזְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The case of a sin offering lost at night is **not comparable** to that of a sin offering lost after its first year has passed. When a sin offering is **lost at night, it is not fit** at all, **neither for itself,** i.e., to be sacrificed, **nor for** its **value,** to be sold and the proceeds used to purchase another offering. **But** with regard to **this** sin offering that was lost after its first year has passed, **granted that it is not fit for itself,** but **it is fit for** its **value.**

What ties together the principle of nighttime loss and the earlier discussion of the double problem is a single underlying idea: *lo chazya lo l'gufa v'lo l'damei* — at night, the animal is completely irrelevant, not only in its physical form but even in terms of its monetary value, since the entire institution of *korbanot* is suspended after dark. This is what distinguishes nighttime loss from the case of a *ba'alat mum* or *avar shnata*, where the animal remains relevant at least for its redemption value (*chazya l'damei*) even if it cannot be offered directly. The *gemara* concludes: complete irrelevance (nighttime) does not create *meita* status; partial irrelevance (a *mum* or advanced age) combined with loss does; and no irrelevance at all (a healthy, in-season animal that was simply lost during the day and found before atonement) creates a dispute between Rabbi and the *Chachamim*.

## Definition of Loss

### Lost Not Stolen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נָקְטִינַן, אֲבֵידָה וְלֹא גְּנוּבָה, אֲבֵידָה וְלֹא גְּזוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to this matter, **Abaye said: We have a tradition** that a sin offering that was **lost** is left to die, **but not** a sin offering that was **stolen;** a sin offering that was **lost** is left to die, **but not** a sin offering that was **robbed.** If a sin offering was stolen or robbed, and the animal was returned after the owner achieved atonement with another animal, the first sin offering is left to graze until it develops a blemish, after which it is sold, and the proceeds are used to purchase a gift offering.

Having clarified when loss is significant in terms of *zman hakrava*, the *gemara* turns to the question of what constitutes *aveidah* (loss) in the first place. Abaye states: *aveidah velo gnuva, aveidah velo gezulah* — only genuine loss qualifies, not theft and not robbery. If the animal was taken by a thief or robber, someone knows its whereabouts, and it therefore does not have the status of a truly lost animal.

### Yoshia's Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי אֲבֵידָה? אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת בְּעֶדְרוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ אַחַת בְּאַחַת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲחוֹרֵי הַדֶּלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which an animal is considered to be **lost? Rabbi Oshaya says: Even one** sin offering intermingled with other animals **in** the owner's **flock,** to the extent that he cannot discern which animal is the sin offering, is considered lost. **And even one** sin offering intermingled **with one** non-sacred animal, so that the owner does not know which is the sin offering, is considered lost. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** An animal found **behind the door** is considered to have been lost.

Rabbi Yoshia sets the standard broadly: *afilu achas be'edra, v'afilu achas b'achas* — even if the animal is mixed within the owner's own flock and he cannot identify which one it is, and even if it is mixed up one-to-one with another animal, the owner being unable to identify it constitutes *aveidah*. There is no principle of *bitul b'rov* here; even if the animal is visibly present but unidentifiable, that counts as lost.

### Behind Door Standard

Rabbi Yochanan states that an animal behind a door — *achorei ha-delet* — is considered *aveidah*.

### Visibility vs Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הֵיכִי קָאָמַר? אֲחוֹרֵי הַדֶּלֶת הוּא דְּלֵיכָּא דְּקָא חָזֵי לַהּ, אֲבָל אַבָּרַאי דְּאִיכָּא דַּחֲזֵי לַהּ — לָא הָוְיָא אֲבוּדָה, אוֹ דִילְמָא אֲחוֹרֵי הַדֶּלֶת דְּאִי מַהְדַּר אַפֵּיהּ חָזֵי לַהּ — הָוְיָא אֲבוּדָה, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן אַבָּרַאי דְּלָא קָחָזֵי לַהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before the** Sages with regard to the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan: With regard to **what** case **is he speaking?** Does Rabbi Yoḥanan mean that **it is** only when the animal is found **behind the door** that it is considered to have been lost, **as there is no one who sees it, but** if the animal was **outside** and was intermingled with other animals, **where there are** people **who see it, it is not** considered to be **lost? Or perhaps** Rabbi Yoḥanan means that even when the animal is found **behind the door, where if** he would **turn his face he** would **see it,** the animal **is** considered **lost, and all the more so** if the animal was **outside, where he does not see it,** it is considered lost. Since no resolution is offered, the Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* asks what exactly Rabbi Yochanan means. Is he saying that only when the animal is behind a door — where *no one* can see it — does it constitute *aveidah*, but if it is in the open and visible to passersby, even if the owner cannot identify it, it would not be *aveidah*? Or does he mean that even something behind a door, where one need only turn around to find it, qualifies as *aveidah* — because for practical purposes the owner does not know where it is? The question essentially asks whether *aveidah* is defined by physical invisibility or by the owner's epistemic state, i.e., his inability to locate it. The *gemara* concludes *teiku* — the question remains unresolved.

Rav Pappa adds a further principle: *aveidah mimenu velo mero'eh lo havya aveidah* — if the owner does not know where the animal is but the shepherd does, it is not considered lost. Equally, if the shepherd does not know but the owner does, it is certainly not lost. Only when neither the owner nor the shepherd knows the animal's location is it considered *aveidah*. Whether the presence of some unknown third party who happens to know its location removes the status of *aveidah* also remains *teiku*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: גָּמְרִינַן אֲבוּדָה מִמֶּנּוּ וְלֹא מֵרוֹעָהּ — לָא הָוְיָא אֲבוּדָה, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן אֲבוּדָה מֵרוֹעָהּ וְלֹא אֲבוּדָה מִמֶּנּוּ. אֲבוּדָה מִמֶּנּוּ וּמֵרוֹעָהּ, וְאֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם מַכִּיר בָּהּ — מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further discusses a sin offering that was lost. **Rav Pappa said: We learn** through tradition that if a sin offering is **lost from** the owner **but** is **not** lost **from the shepherd** of the flock, i.e., the shepherd knows which animal is the sin offering, **it is not** considered **lost. And all the more so,** if the animal is **lost from the shepherd but not lost from** the owner, it is not considered lost. The Gemara asks: If the sin offering was **lost from** the owner **and the shepherd, but** there is **one** individual located far away, even **at the end of the world,** who **recognizes** the animal, **what** is the *halakha*? Again the Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

## Blood in Vessels

### Post-Shechita Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: אֲבוּדָה בַּכּוֹס מַהוּ? אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי — הָאָמַר אֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה מֵתָה!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** In a case where a sin offering was **lost,** and the owner designated another sin offering in its stead, and the second sin offering was slaughtered, **what is** the *halakha* if the original sin offering was found after the blood of the second offering was collected **in a cup** and stands ready to be sprinkled upon the altar? The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion does Rav Pappa raise this dilemma? **If we say** that he raises it **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, this cannot be correct, as **didn't** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **say** that even a sin offering that was **lost at the time** when another animal was merely **designated** in its stead is left to **die?**

Rav Pappa then poses a more complex question: *aveidah b'kos mai hu* — what is the *halacha* when the "loss" occurs at the level of the blood in a vessel? The scenario here is not about the animal going missing before *shechita*, but about a situation where the replacement animal has already been *shechted* and its blood has been collected in a vessel — yet the original animal has now been found. The question is whether, at this stage, the blood in the vessel constitutes a "brought" replacement in the sense that triggers *chatat meita* for the original.

### Blood Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כִּי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן, מִי אָמְרִינַן: כִּי אָמְרִי רַבָּנַן בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה רוֹעָה, קוֹדֶם שֶׁיְּקַבֵּל דָּמָהּ בַּכּוֹס, אֲבָל הָכָא סְבִירָא לְהוּ: כָּל הָעוֹמֵד לִיזָּרֵק כְּזָרוּק דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, when** Rav Pappa **raises the dilemma,** he does so **according to** the opinion **of the Rabbis,** and the dilemma is as follows: **Do we say** that **when the Rabbis said:** A sin offering that was **lost at the time** when another animal was **designated** in its stead is left to **graze,** this applies only if the original animal was found **before one collects the blood** of the other sin offering **in a cup; but here,** where the other sin offering was already slaughtered and its blood was collected in a cup, **the** Rabbis **hold: Any** blood **that stands** ready **to be sprinkled** upon the altar **is considered like** it has already been **sprinkled,** and it is as though the owner has achieved atonement? If so, this sin offering that was found after the collection of the blood should be left to die.

The *gemara* frames this as a question that depends on whose opinion we are working with. According to Rabbi, who says *aveidah bi-sha'at hafrasha* is already sufficient to create *meita* status, the question of whether the blood was thrown or merely collected is irrelevant — the original animal's fate was already sealed when the replacement was designated. The question is therefore relevant only according to the *Rabbanan*, who require that the replacement actually be brought before the original is found in order for *meita* status to apply. For the *Rabbanan*, one must ask: does their requirement of "after the replacement was brought" mean after the blood was actually sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach*, or is it enough that the blood was caught in the vessel? The principle of *kol ha'omed lizrok ke-zaruak dami* — whatever is about to be sprinkled is considered as if already sprinkled — might indicate that collecting the blood in the vessel is equivalent to completing the offering. Or one might say: as long as the blood has not actually been thrown, the offering is not yet complete, and the original animal's status is still that of *aveidah bi-sha'at hafrasha* (loss at the time of designation), for which the *Rabbanan* hold *ro'eh* (it grazes). The *gemara* does not resolve this question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא, כְּמָה דְּלָא אִזְדְּרִיק דָּם — כִּי אָבְדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה דָּמְיָא, וְרָעֲיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** the Rabbis maintain that **as long as the blood has not** been **sprinkled** on the altar, **it is** still **considered like** a case where the original offering was **lost at the time** when another animal was **designated** in its stead but was found by the time the second offering was brought to the altar. If so, as the first sin offering was found before the sprinkling of the blood, it should be left to **graze** until it develops a blemish.

### Multiple Vessels

A second interpretation of the question reframes it entirely. Some say the question is not actually about two animals at all, but about a single animal whose blood was collected into two vessels, one of which was subsequently lost. When the remaining vessel is used for the *zerikat ha-dam* (sprinkling), what is the status of the missing vessel's blood if it is later found? This is a genuine sub-question of the *aveidah* framework: can part of an offering "go lost" in a way that parallels the loss of an entire animal?

### Vessel Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: לְעוֹלָם אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי, כִּי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ — כְּגוֹן שֶׁקִּיבֵּל דָּמָהּ בִּשְׁתֵּי כוֹסוֹת, וְאָבַד אֶחָד מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** an alternate explanation of the dilemma: **Actually,** Rav Pappa raises his dilemma **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And when** Rav Pappa **raises the dilemma,** it is not with regard to a case involving two sin offerings, as described above. Rather, it is with regard to a case **where** the original sin offering was slaughtered and the priest **collected its blood in two cups, and one of them was lost** at the time when the priest sprinkled the blood of the remaining cup.

The answer depends on a pre-existing dispute about the rule of *kos oseh chaveiro dichui* — whether using one vessel to perform the *avoda* causes the other vessel to become *nidcheh* (rejected). According to those who hold that it does — that using one cup to throw the blood "rejects" the others — there is no question: even if the missing blood were to reappear, it would be *nidcheh* regardless, since it would have been rejected the moment its companion vessel was used. The question therefore only arises according to those who say that using one cup makes the remaining blood in the other vessels *shirayim* (remnants to be poured at the base of the *mizbe'ach*), which presupposes that the blood remains valid as long as both cups are present. When one is missing, the *gemara* asks: do we still classify the absent blood as *shirayim*-equivalent (valid, merely temporarily absent), or does its absence at the moment of *zerikat ha-dam* render it rejected, analogous to an animal that was absent when the replacement was brought?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר כּוֹס עוֹשֶׂה חֲבֵירוֹ דָּחוּי — לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר כּוֹס עוֹשֶׂה חֲבֵירוֹ שִׁירַיִים. הָנֵי מִילֵּי — הֵיכָא דְּאִיתַנְהוּ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ, דְּכֹל הֵיכָא דְּבָעֵי זָרֵיק; אֲבָל הָכָא — הָא לֵיכָּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **And you should not raise the dilemma according to** the opinion **of the one who said** that when blood is collected in two cups, one **cup renders** the blood of **the other** cup **rejected,** as in such a case, the entire offering is disqualified. Rather, **when should you raise the dilemma?** Raise it **according to** the opinion **of the one who says** that one **cup renders the other** cup **a remainder,** i.e., the blood in the second cup is considered the remainder of the first cup and is poured out at the base of the altar. The dilemma is as follows: Do we say that **this statement,** that the second cup is considered the remainder of the first cup, applies only in a case **where both** cups **are present, since** with **whichever** cup the priest **wants, he may sprinkle; but here,** since one of the cups **is not** present, the missing cup is therefore considered rejected and the offering is disqualified? **Or perhaps** it makes **no difference** whether both cups are present or not. The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

## Rabbi vs Chachamim

### Unanimous Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַטָּאת, וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה — תָּמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who designates a sin offering, and** the animal **was lost, and he designated another in its stead** and sacrificed it, **and thereafter the first** animal **was found;** that is a sin offering whose owner achieved atonement with another animal, and **it shall** be left to **die.**

The *Mishna* now sets out the positions of Rabbi and the *Chachamim* in a series of carefully constructed cases. The baseline case on which everyone agrees: *ha-mafrish chatat v'avdah v'hikriv acheret tachteha v'achar kach nimtzet ha-rishonah, tamut* — one set aside a *chatat*, it was lost, a replacement was brought, and then the original was found. The original must die. This is the unambiguous case of *nitkapru ba'alim b'acher* with which the *gemara* began.

### Lost Money Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת לְחַטָּאתוֹ, וְאָבְדוּ, וְהִפְרִישׁ חַטָּאת תַּחְתֵּיהֶם, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצְאוּ הַמָּעוֹת — יוֹלִיכֵם לְיָם הַמֶּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who designates money for** purchase of **his sin offering, and** the money **was lost,and he designated** an animal as **a sin offering in its stead,** and he sacrificed it, **and thereafter, the money was found,** it is prohibited to derive benefit from the money, as the money attains the halakhic status of the sin offering that was to be purchased with it, and that sin offering would be left to die because the owner achieved atonement with another animal. Therefore, **he must take** the money and cast it **into the Dead Sea,** from where it cannot be recovered.

The *Mishna* then introduces the case of money. One set aside money for a *chatat*, the money was lost, a replacement *chatat* was brought, and then the money was found. Since money designated for a *chatat* has *kedushat chatat*, it is treated analogously to the animal: *yolichem le-yam ha-melach* — throw it into the Dead Sea. The money must be destroyed.

### Money Mixing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת לְחַטָּאת, וְאָבְדוּ, וְהִפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת תַּחְתֵּיהֶן, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לִיקַּח חַטָּאת מֵהֶן עַד שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת — יָבִיא מֵאֵלּוּ וּמֵאֵלּוּ חַטָּאת, וְהַשְּׁאָר יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who designates money for** purchase of his **sin offering, and** the money **was lost, and he designated other money in its stead, and he did not manage to purchase a sin offering with** that money **before the original** money **was found, he should bring a sin offering from** a combination of **this** original money **and that** money designated in its stead, **and the remainder shall be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

A more complex case: one set aside money for a *chatat*, lost it, then set aside a replacement *chatat* animal, and then found the original money before bringing the animal. Now one has two pools of money, each designated as a *chatat*. If one simply uses one pool and ignores the other, the unused pool would be subject to *nitkapru ba'alim b'acher* — according to Rabbi it would have to be destroyed. The solution the *Mishna* provides is *yavi me'elu u-me'elu le-chatato* — mix the two pools of money together and use the combined funds to purchase a single *chatat*. Whatever remains is leftover *chatat* money and is used for an *olah*. Since the money is fungible, combining the two pools eliminates the identity of either as a "rejected" pool; there is no single pile that was "chosen" over another.

### Animal Money Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת לְחַטָּאתוֹ וְאָבְדוּ, וְהִפְרִישׁ חַטָּאת תַּחְתֵּיהֶן, לֹא הִסְפִּיק לְהַקְרִיבָהּ עַד שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ הַמָּעוֹת, וַהֲרֵי חַטָּאת בַּעַל מוּם — תִּמָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא מֵאֵלּוּ וּמֵאֵלּוּ חַטָּאת, וְהַשְּׁאָר יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who designates money for** purchase of **his sin offering, and** the money **was lost, and he designated** an animal as **a sin offering in its stead,** and **he did not manage to sacrifice** the animal **before the money was found, and** the animal that he designated as **a sin offering is blemished,** the animal **shall be sold; and he brings a sin offering from** a combination of **this** original money **and that** money received for the sale of the blemished animal, **and the remainder shall be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

The *Mishna* continues with hybrid cases involving both animals and money. If one set aside money and lost it, then set aside a replacement *chatat* animal, and then found the original money before bringing the animal — the solution, if the *chatat* animal happens to have a *mum*, is to redeem the animal, producing money, and then mix *all* the money together and bring a *chatat* from the combined funds. The remainder goes for an *olah*. The mixing principle that works for money can be extended to an animal by first converting the animal into money through redemption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַטָּאת וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לִיקַּח מֵהֶן חַטָּאת עַד שֶׁנִּמְצָא חַטָּאתוֹ, וַהֲרֵי הִיא בַּעֲלַת מוּם — תִּימָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא מֵאֵלּוּ וּמֵאֵלּוּ חַטָּאת, וְהַשְּׁאָר יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who designates a sin offering and** the animal **was lost, and he designated money in its stead, and he did not manage to purchase a sin offering with** that money **before his sin offering was found, and** the animal **is blemished,** the animal **shall be sold; and he brings a sin offering from** a combination of **this** money that he designated **and that** money received for the sale of the blemished animal, **and the remainder shall be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

Similarly, if one set aside a *chatat* animal that was lost, then set aside money to replace it, and then found the original animal with a *mum* — redeem the animal, mix all the money together, buy a *chatat* from the combined funds, and the remainder goes for an *olah*.

### Two Animals Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, לֹא הִסְפִּיק לְהַקְרִיבָהּ עַד שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן בַּעֲלַת מוּם — יִמָּכְרוּ, וְיָבִיא מֵאֵלּוּ וּמֵאֵלּוּ חַטָּאת, וְהַשְּׁאָר יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who designates his sin offering and** the animal **was lost, and he designated another** animal **in its stead,** and **he did not manage to sacrifice** the sin offering **before the first** sin offering **was found, and both of** the animals **are blemished,** the animals **shall be sold; and he brings a sin offering from** a third animal that he buys with a combination of the money from the sale of **this** animal **and** from the sale of **that** animal, **and the remainder shall be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

When both the original and replacement are animals without a *mum*, the situation becomes more difficult.

### Finding Before/After — The Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַטָּאת, וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, לֹא הִסְפִּיק לְהַקְרִיבָהּ עַד שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, הָיוּ שְׁתֵּיהֶן תְּמִימוֹת — אַחַת מֵהֶן תִּיקְרַב חַטָּאת, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה תָּמוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין חַטָּאת מֵתָה אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת מֵאַחַר שֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים, וְאֵין הַמָּעוֹת הוֹלְכִים לְיָם הַמֶּלַח אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ מֵאַחַר שֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who designates** his **sin offering and** the animal **was lost, and he designated another** animal **in its stead,** and **he did not manage to sacrifice** the sin offering **before the first** sin offering **was found,** and **both of** the animals **are unblemished** and fit for sacrifice, **one of them shall be sacrificed as a sin offering and the other shall** be left to **die;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: A sin offering is not** left to **die unless it was found after** its **owner achieved atonement; and the money is not taken to the Dead Sea unless it was found after its owner achieved atonement.**

If both happen to be unblemished, then one brings one as a *chatat* and the other must die, for it has become a case of *nitkapru ba'alim b'acher* — *divrei Rebbi*.

This last ruling encapsulates the central dispute. According to Rabbi, the moment one animal was lost and another was designated in its place, the lost animal became subject to *meita* status — even if it is subsequently found before the replacement is actually brought. In the case of two unblemished animals, since one cannot be converted into money and combined with the other, one must bring one and the second must die, because it has the status of *chatat she-nitkapru ba'aleha b'acher*.

The *Chachamim* fundamentally disagree. Their position: *ein chatat meita ella she-nimtzet me-achar she-kiperu ha-ba'alim* — the status of *chatat meita* applies only when the original animal is found after the owners have completed their atonement through the replacement. If it is found beforehand — at any point before the replacement is actually brought — then no *meita* status applies, the owner may use whichever animal he wishes, and the remainder is treated as an *olah*. Correspondingly, the rule requiring money to be thrown into the *yam ha-melach* also applies only when the money is found after the owners have completed their atonement. All the elaborate mixing of money described above is, according to the *Chachamim*, unnecessary in any case where the original was found before atonement was completed — one simply treats the leftover as an *olah*.

### Sale Creates Meita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ, וַהֲרֵי הִיא בַּעֲלַת מוּם — מוֹכְרָהּ, וְיָבִיא בְּדָמֶיהָ אַחֶרֶת. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם קָרְבָה שְׁנִיָּה עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁחֲטָה — תָּמוּת, שֶׁכְּבָר כִּיפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who designates a sin offering and** the animal **is blemished, he sells** the animal **and** must **bring another** sin offering **with the money** received in **its** sale. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: If the second** animal **is sacrificed before** the first **is slaughtered** for non-sacred consumption, the first animal **shall** be left to **die.** Although it was sold and rendered non-sacred, its status is now that of a sin offering **whose owner already achieved atonement** with another animal.

The *Mishna* closes with a remarkable ruling by Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon. If one set aside a *chatat* that developed a *mum*, sold it, and used the proceeds to purchase and bring a new *chatat* — and if the second *chatat* was brought before the original purchaser had a chance to *shecht* the animal he bought — then *tamut shekvar kiperu ba'alim b'acher*: the animal that was sold must die. The reasoning is that from the moment the seller brought his replacement *chatat* on the *mizbe'ach*, the sold animal retroactively acquired the status of *chatat she-nitkapru ba'aleha b'acher*, because it had been consecrated as a *chatat* and the original owner has now achieved atonement through another means. The buyer's animal — which had been purchased as an ordinary *ba'al mum* being redeemed — is suddenly subject to *meita* status, apparently regardless of the buyer's expectations. Whether the buyer receives any recourse is not discussed.
