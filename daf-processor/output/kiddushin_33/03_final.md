# Kiddushin 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Mochel on Kevodo

### Rav Mochel - Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: הָרַב שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ – כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל, נָשִׂיא שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ – אֵין כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Even according to the one who says** that if **a rabbi forgoes** the **honor** due **him, his honor is forgone,** if **a *Nasi* forgoes** the **honor** due **him, his honor is not forgone.**

The *daf* opens on 33b, picking up where the previous session left off — namely, the question of whether a *rav* can be *mochel* on his *kavod*. The Gemara had already established that a father can waive honor owed to him, but the case of a *rav* is more complex. A *rebbe* does not merely represent himself; he embodies *kevod hatorah*. One might therefore argue that even if he personally does not object to being treated without ceremony, the *Torah* he represents demands that others show him respect regardless. Nevertheless, the Gemara concludes with a powerful statement about the nature of *Torah sheba'al peh* and the personal investment it entails. Because *Torah* becomes genuinely one's own through the process of learning and transmission — one has, in a real sense, an ownership stake in it — a *rav* can indeed be *mochel* on his *kavod*. The very intimacy of that ownership is what grants him the standing to waive the honor.

### Even After Mechila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא רָבָא מַשְׁקֵי בֵּי הִלּוּלָא דִּבְרֵיהּ וְדַל לֵיהּ כָּסָא לְרַב פָּפָּא וּלְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְקָמוּ מִקַּמֵּיהּ. לְרַב מָרִי וּלְרַב פִּנְחָס בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, וְלָא קָמוּ מִקַּמֵּיהּ. אִיקְּפַד וַאֲמַר: הָנוּ רַבָּנַן רַבָּנַן, וְהָנוּ רַבָּנַן לָאו רַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But Rava served drinks** to the guests **at his son's wedding celebration, and he poured a cup for Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, and they stood before him** when he approached them. When he poured a cup **for Rav Mari and** for **Rav Pineḥas, son of Rav Ḥisda, they did not stand before him.** Rava **became angry and said:** Are **these Sages,** i.e., Rav Mari and Rav Pineḥas, **Sages, and** are **those Sages,** who stood to honor me, **not Sages?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי צָדוֹק: עַד מָתַי אַתֶּם מַנִּיחִים כְּבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם וְאַתֶּם עוֹסְקִים בִּכְבוֹד הַבְּרִיּוֹת? הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מֵשִׁיב רוּחוֹת, וּמַעֲלֶה נְשִׂיאִים, וּמוֹרִיד מָטָר, וּמַצְמִיחַ אֲדָמָה, וְעוֹרֵךְ שׁוּלְחָן לִפְנֵי כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד, וְאָנוּ לֹא יְהֵא רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּרִיבִּי עוֹמֵד וּמַשְׁקֶה עָלֵינוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tzadok said to them: For how long will you ignore the honor** due **to the Omnipresent, and deal with the honor of people?** You could cite a proof from God Himself. After all, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, makes** the **winds blow, and raises** the **clouds, and brings** the **rain, and causes** the **earth to sprout, and sets a table before each and every** creature. **And should not the esteemed Rabban Gamliel stand over us and serve us drinks?**

Yet the Gemara's conclusion is not that the obligation disappears entirely. The line that opens this session reads: *afilu hachi hiddur me'avad leh ba'ei* — even so, some form of *hadarta* must still be shown. The *Rambam* leaves this deliberately open-ended: even if the *rav* has been *mochel*, his students remain in a curious state of being simultaneously exempt and obligated. They are exempt from the full requirement, yet still bound to demonstrate some gesture of respect. *Rashi* is characteristically precise: *linua me'at ke'ilu rotze la'amod mipanav* — one should shift slightly, as if intending to rise. Anyone familiar with *yeshiva* culture will recognize what is being described — the partial rising from one's seat, the so-called tush-move, which is not mere social awkwardness but an expression of this very *halakhic* balance. The *rebbe* has waived the full honor; his standing as a representative of *Torah* cannot, however, be entirely ignored. The partial gesture embodies that equilibrium precisely.

### Nasi Cannot Mechel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְרַבִּי צָדוֹק שֶׁהָיוּ מְסוּבִּין בְּבֵית הַמִּשְׁתֶּה בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְהָיָה רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל עוֹמֵד וּמַשְׁקֶה עֲלֵיהֶם. נָתַן הַכּוֹס לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְלֹא נְטָלוֹ, נְתָנוֹ לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְקִיבְּלוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מָה זֶה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ? אָנוּ יוֹשְׁבִין וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּרִיבִּי עוֹמֵד וּמַשְׁקֶה עָלֵינוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection:** There was **an incident involving Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Tzadok, who were reclining at the wedding of Rabban Gamliel's son. And Rabban Gamliel,** who was *Nasi* of the Sanhedrin at the time, **was standing over them and serving them drinks. He gave the cup to Rabbi Eliezer and he would not accept it; he gave it to Rabbi Yehoshua and he accepted it. Rabbi Eliezer said to him: What is this, Yehoshua? We sit and the esteemed Rabban Gamliel stands over us and serves us drinks?**

With that established, Rav Ashi introduces a further distinction. Even according to the opinion that a *rav* may be *mochel* on his *kavod*, a *nasi* cannot do so: *nasi shemachal al kevodo ein kevodo machul*. The *nasi* in this context refers to the role of the exilarch in *Eretz Yisrael* — a uniquely combined office of political and religious leadership, embodied most famously by *Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi* and his descendants. This convergence of authority is distinctive to *Eretz Yisrael*; in *Bavel* the political and rabbinic leaderships were separate institutions.

The Gemara's reasoning seems to be that certain institutions of authority are so fundamental to the structure of society that no individual can simply choose to dissolve them. The framework of social order — the subjection of persons to governance and law — is not a personal privilege that the leader may surrender at will. Just as the Gemara in *Pirkei Avot* teaches that one should pray for the welfare of the government because without it people would consume one another, the authority of the *nasi* represents something too structurally necessary to be quietly waived.

### Rabban Gamliel Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מָצִינוּ גָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ שֶׁשִּׁמֵּשׁ, אַבְרָהָם גָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ – וְשִׁמֵּשׁ. אַבְרָהָם גְּדוֹל הַדּוֹר הָיָה, וְכָתוּב בּוֹ: ״וְהוּא עָמַד עֲלֵיהֶם״. וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמְרוּ: כְּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת נִדְמוּ לוֹ – לֹא נִדְמוּ לוֹ אֶלָּא לְעַרְבִיִּים. וְאָנוּ, לֹא יְהֵא רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּרִיבִּי עוֹמֵד וּמַשְׁקֶה עָלֵינוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: We found** one **greater than him who served** his guests, as our forefather **Abraham** was **greater than him and he served** his guests. **Abraham was the greatest** man **of** his **generation and it is written about him: "And he stood over them** under the tree, and they ate" (Genesis 18:8). **And lest you say:** His guests **appeared to him as ministering angels,** and that is why he honored them, in fact **they appeared to him only as Arabs. And** if so, **should not the esteemed Rabban Gamliel stand over us and serve us drinks?**

### Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

*Rabban Gamliel* offered the cup to *Rabbi Eliezer*, who refused to accept it. He would not allow the *nasi* to occupy a role of service toward him, even at the *nasi's* own initiative. *Rabban Gamliel* then offered it to *Rabbi Yehoshua*, who accepted. *Rabbi Eliezer* challenged him: *mah zeh Yehoshua? Anu yoshvim v'Rabban Gamliel omeid umashkeh aleinu?* — How can we remain seated while the *nasi* stands and serves us?

*Rabbi Yehoshua* responded by invoking a greater precedent: *matzinu gadol mimenu sheshimash* — we have found someone even greater than *Rabban Gamliel* who served others. *Avraham* was the *gadol hador*, the greatest of his generation, and yet *v'hu omeid aleihem* — he stood over his guests and served them. Nor can one deflect this by saying that *Avraham* knew they were angels and therefore this was somehow exceptional — *k'arviyim nidmu lo*, they appeared to him as ordinary Arabs. Yet he served them. All the more so, *Rabbi Yehoshua* argued, we should be willing to allow *Rabban Gamliel* to attend to us.

One might note that a distinction exists: *Avraham* was a *gadol hador* but not technically a *nasi* in the institutional sense, and the question of whether *mechilas kavod* applies specifically to political authority is somewhat separate from the question of how great a person is. Moreover, while the guests *Avraham* served were not members of his own clan, the analogy is not exact. Still, it is the argument *Rabbi Yehoshua* deploys.

### Tzadok's Critique

*Rabbi Tzadok* then offered a striking counter-critique that reframes the entire conversation. *Ad masai atem manichim kevodo shel Makom v'atem oskim bichvod habriyos?* — For how long will you set aside the honor of God and occupy yourselves with the honor of human beings? If one wants to invoke a precedent for serving those beneath oneself, the proper model is not *Avraham* but *Hakadosh Baruch Hu* Himself. God causes the winds to blow, the clouds to rise, the rain to descend, the earth to bring forth produce — He effectively sets the table for every creature. If God, who is the ultimate authority, provides for every human being in this direct and personal fashion, then surely *Rabban Gamliel's* attending to his guests involves no compromise of his position.

This argument carries interesting implications. On one reading, *Rabbi Tzadok* is saying that *Rabban Gamliel* is not actually being *mochel al kevodo* at all — acting in a manner that provides for and serves others is not a diminishment of one's authority; it is a *Godly* act, an expression of *vehalchata bidrachav*. On another reading, even if such service could in theory be considered *mechila*, it is entirely legitimate because God Himself models it. Either way, the Gemara concludes from this episode that a *nasi* apparently can be *mochel* — and Rav Ashi's statement is therefore reformulated: even according to the opinion that a *nasi* who waives his honor has indeed waived it, a *melech* cannot do so.

### King Cannot Mechel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר הָכִי אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: נָשִׂיא שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ – כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל, מֶלֶךְ שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ – אֵין כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׂוֹם תָּשִׂים עָלֶיךָ מֶלֶךְ״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא אֵימָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if it was stated, it was stated as follows: Rav Ashi said: Even according to the one who says** that if **a *Nasi* forgoes** the **honor** due **him, his honor is forgone,** if **a king forgoes** the **honor** due **him, his honor is not forgone. As it is stated: "You shall set a king over you"** (Deuteronomy 17:15), which indicates **that his fear should be upon you.**

The verse invoked for the *melech* is *som tasim alecha melech* — you shall place upon yourself a king, with the word *alecha* ("upon you") signifying that the king's authority must always remain elevated over the people. His *aima*, his commanding presence, must be constant and may not be dissolved. The Gemara draws an analogy to practical governance: just as certain roles in civil society carry enhanced legal protections precisely because the stability they represent cannot be treated lightly, so too the authority of the *melech* cannot be compromised through personal *mechila*. This is ultimately about what is necessary for society to function — *u-mitpallel lishloma shel malkhut* — and no individual, not even the king himself, may unilaterally dissolve that.

## Scriptural Basis

### The Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מִפְּנֵי שֵׂיבָה תָּקוּם וְהָדַרְתָּ פְּנֵי זָקֵן וְיָרֵאתָ מֵאֱלֹהֶיךָ אֲנִי ה׳״
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: **"Before the hoary head you shall stand and you shall revere the face of an elder, and you shall fear your God, I am the Lord"** (Leviticus 19:32).

Having concluded the discussion of *mechilas kavod*, the Gemara now turns to the biblical foundation of the entire subject. *Tanu rabbanan* — the Sages taught in a *baraita*: *mipnei seiva takum ve-hadarta pnei zaken ve-yareta me-elokecha ani Hashem* — before the hoary head you shall rise, and you shall honor the elderly, and you shall fear your God, I am the Lord. At first glance, the verse sounds like a command to respect the elderly broadly. The *baraita* will now analyze precisely what it requires and from whom.

### Tanna Kamma View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ מִפְּנֵי זָקֵן אַשְׁמַאי? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זָקֵן״, וְאֵין זָקֵן אֶלָּא חָכָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶסְפָה לִּי שִׁבְעִים אִישׁ מִזִּקְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, אַשֶׁר יָדַעְתָּ כִּי הֵם זִקְנֵי הָעָם.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that it is obligatory to stand **before a simple [*ashmai*] elder.** Therefore, **the verse states: "elder," and an "elder"** means **nothing other** than **a wise man, as it is stated: "Gather unto Me seventy men of the Elders of Israel,** whom you know to be the Elders of the people"** (Numbers 11:16).

The *tanna kamma* begins with a potential misreading: *yakhol afilu mipnei zaken ashmai* — perhaps the verse obligates one to rise even before an elder who is wicked, or more neutrally in the view of *Tosafot*, one who is simply unlearned. The *talmud lomar* is the word *zaken* itself. *Seiva* emphasizes white hair and age, but *zaken* carries a different meaning: *ve-ein zaken ella chakham* — *zaken* denotes a sage. The prooftext is *asfa li shivim ish miziknei Yisrael* — gather for me seventy men from the elders of Israel — with the phrase *asher yadata ki hem ziknei ha'am* suggesting that what God *knows* about them, the quality that identifies them as *ziknei ha'am*, is not merely their age but their recognized wisdom and authority. Age alone is therefore insufficient; *zaken* in the *pasuk* means a Torah sage, and *seiva* is understood in light of that.

### R. Yosei HaGlili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: אֵין זָקֵן אֶלָּא מִי שֶׁקָּנָה חׇכְמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ קָנָנִי רֵאשִׁית דַּרְכּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: An "elder [*zaken*]"** means **nothing other** than **one who has acquired wisdom.** He interprets the word *zaken* as a contraction of the phrase *zeh kanna*, meaning: This one has acquired. Elsewhere the word *kanna* is used in reference to wisdom, **as it is stated** that wisdom says: **"The Lord acquired me [*kanani*] at the beginning of His way"** (Proverbs 8:22).

*Rabbi Yosei HaGlili* agrees that *zaken* means a sage, but arrives there differently: *ein zaken ella mi shekanah chochmah* — *zaken* refers to one who has *acquired* wisdom. *Rashi* notes the play on words: *ze kanah* — this one acquired. The prooftext is *Hashem kanani reishit darko*, the emphasis falling on the root *kinyan*, acquisition.

### Hiddur and Kiuma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְהַדְּרֶנּוּ בְּמָמוֹן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תָּקוּם וְהָדַרְתָּ״ – מָה קִימָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס, אַף הִידּוּר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **he should revere him through money,** i.e., that one is required to give an elder money in his honor. Therefore, **the verse states: "You shall stand and you shall revere." Just as standing includes no monetary loss, so too, reverence** is referring to an action **that includes no monetary loss.**

The *baraita* then asks: *yakhol ya'amod mi-panav mi-makom rachok* — perhaps one must rise even from a great distance? The *talmud lomar* is *takum ve-hadarta* read together — the rising and the honoring are linked. Several inferences flow from this pairing. First, *takum* is qualified by *ve-hadarta*: a rising that constitutes genuine honor (*hiddur*) is one performed in proximity, not from across the room. Second, the question arises whether *ve-hadarta* refers to something entirely independent of *takum* — perhaps lavishing the sage with gifts. The *talmud lomar* answers: *ma kima she-ein ba chisron kis af hiddur she-ein bo chisron kis* — just as rising costs nothing, so too the *hiddur* required involves no monetary expenditure. Honor is expressed through gesture and manner, not through financial outlay.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יַעֲמוֹד מִפָּנָיו מִבֵּית הַכִּסֵּא וּמִבֵּית הַמֶּרְחָץ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תָּקוּם וְהָדַרְתָּ״ – לֹא אָמַרְתִּי קִימָה אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ הִידּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **one should** also **stand before** an elder **in the lavatory or in the bathhouse.** Therefore, **the verse states: "You shall stand and you shall revere,"** which indicates: **I said** the mitzva of **standing only in a place where there is reverence.**

Third, might one be obligated to rise inside a bathhouse or while using a toilet? Again *takum ve-hadarta* — *lo amarti kima ela be-makom she-yesh hiddur*, rising is only required in a context where *hiddur* is socially operative. In a bathhouse, particularly the inner section where all are unclothed, the social distinctions that give *kavod* its meaning are suspended. Everyone is reduced to the same fundamental human condition; the context does not support the kind of status-based respect the verse demands. Indeed, the *halakha* holds that one does not even yield one's place in a bathhouse entrance to show deference — in that setting, social standing simply does not assert itself the same way.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יַעֲצִים עֵינָיו כְּמִי שֶׁלֹּא רָאָהוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תָּקוּם... וְיָרֵאתָ״ – דָּבָר הַמָּסוּר לַלֵּב נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ: ״וְיָרֵאתָ מֵאֱלֹהֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **one may close his eyes like one who does not see** the elder. Therefore, **the verse states:** "Before the hoary head **you shall stand** and you shall revere the face of an elder, **and you shall fear** your God" (Leviticus 19:32). **With regard to** any **matter given over to the heart, it is stated: "And you shall fear your God."**

Finally, *yakhol yaatzim einav mi-panav she-lo yirehu* — perhaps one may close one's eyes so as not to see the sage and thereby avoid the obligation. The *talmud lomar* invokes the conclusion of the *pasuk*: *ve-yareta me-elokecha* — you shall fear your God. This phrase always signals a *davar ha-masur la-lev*, something known only to the heart and hidden from others. Closing one's eyes to avoid seeing the sage is precisely such an act — it is visible to God alone, and therefore the obligation to fear God applies directly to it.

### Issi ben Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״מִפְּנֵי שֵׂיבָה תָּקוּם״ – אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל שֵׂיבָה בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** **Isi ben Yehuda says** that the verse: **"Before the hoary head you shall stand,"** indicates that **even any** person of **hoary head is included** in this mitzva, not only a Sage.

*Issi ben Yehuda* reads the verse differently: *mipnei seiva takum*, and *afilu kol seiva be-mashma* — the word *seiva* includes any person of white hair, any elder. The obligation to rise is not limited to Torah sages; it extends to any elderly person on the basis of age alone.

### One or Two Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ יַנִּיק וְחַכִּים. תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: יַנִּיק וְחַכִּים – לָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי סָבַר: אֲפִילּוּ יַנִּיק וְחַכִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*. Apparently the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili is** the same as that of **the first *tanna*,** as they both say that an elder is a Torah scholar. What does Rabbi Yosei HaGelili add? The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** with regard to one who is **young and wise. The first *tanna* maintains:** One who is **young and wise** is **not** considered an elder, as the mitzva applies only to one who is both elderly and wise. **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili maintains:** It is **even** a mitzva to honor one who is **young and wise.**

With three positions before it — the *tanna kamma*, *Rabbi Yosei HaGlili*, and *Issi ben Yehuda* — the Gemara asks whether the first two are in fact distinct. *Rabbi Yosei HaGlili hainu Tanna Kamma* — aren't they saying the same thing? Both hold that *zaken* means a sage. The Gemara answers: *ika beinaihu yanik ve-chakkim* — the difference is a young Torah sage. For the *tanna kamma*, both dimensions are necessary: *seiva* and *zaken* combine, meaning an older person who is also a sage. A young prodigy, however brilliant, does not qualify. For *Rabbi Yosei HaGlili*, wisdom acquired (*zeh kanah chochmah*) is sufficient on its own, independent of age.

### Tanna Kamma vs. R. Yosei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי? אָמַר לָךְ: אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כִּדְקָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא – אִם כֵּן נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״מִפְּנֵי שֵׂיבָה זָקֵן תָּקוּם וְהָדַרְתָּ״, מַאי שְׁנָא דְּפַלְגִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא – לְמֵימַר: דְּהַאי לָאו הַאי, וְהַאי לָאו הַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili? He** could have **said to you** that **if it enters your mind** to explain **as the first *tanna* says,** that for the obligation to honor another to be in effect that person must be both elderly and wise, **if so, let the Merciful One write: Before the hoary head of an elder you shall stand and you shall revere. What is the difference** between the two terms "hoary head" and "elder," **that the Merciful One separates them?** This serves **to say that this** term **is not** the same as **that** one, **and that** term **is not** the same as **this** one.

The Gemara probes further: what is *Rabbi Yosei HaGlili's* reason for separating *seiva* and *zaken* into distinct terms if in the end only *zaken* — the sage — matters? *Ei salka da'atakh k'de-amar tanna kamma* — if you thought like the *tanna kamma*, the Torah could have simply written *mipnei seiva zaken takum ve-hadarta*, combining them cleanly. *Mai shna d'palginhu rachmana* — why did the Torah separate them? *Le-meimra d'hai lav hai v'hai lav hai* — to indicate they are distinct categories. If so, one might reasonably conclude that each is independently sufficient: either a sage or an elder. But both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* resist this reading and explain that once *zaken* is established as independently sufficient, it effectively absorbs *seiva* — the result is a single category (the sage), and the mention of *seiva* serves another purpose.

*Tosafot* adds a nuanced intermediate reading of *Rabbi Yosei HaGlili's* position: *seiva* is not meaningless but operates as a supplementary factor. A modest Torah scholar who is also elderly falls into the *seiva* category; a young Torah giant falls into the *zaken* category. An average scholar who is young, or an ordinary elderly person with no Torah learning at all, would not qualify under either heading. The minimum threshold, in any case, is some degree of Torah knowledge — but age can compensate for a lower degree of scholarship, while exceptional scholarship can compensate for youth. This is how *Tosafot* explains why the verse mentions both terms even according to *Rabbi Yosei HaGlili*.

The *tanna kamma's* rationale for keeping the terms combined is then explained as well: the separation of *zaken* from *seiva* was necessary not to signal two independent categories, but to allow the word *zaken* to appear separately in the phrase *zaken ve-yareta* — the basis for the teaching that a *zaken* should not burden others by placing himself in situations that compel people to show him honor. Had the Torah written *seifa zaken* as a compound at the outset, that *derashah* would not have been available.

This point connects naturally to the broader question of what motivates the requirement in the first place. One might ask: is the respect demanded here purely about Torah knowledge quantitatively measured, or does it capture something about life experience and wisdom accumulated through years of living? The *tanna kamma's* insistence on combining age and scholarship suggests the latter — not raw learning alone, but the convergence of accumulated life experience with Torah authority. The question of whether life experience alone, without Torah, suffices is precisely what *Issi ben Yehuda* addresses, and what *Rabbi Yochanan's* ruling in his favor will eventually establish.

## Kiuma and Hiddur

### Does Hiddur Cost Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקִימָה לֵית בַּהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס? מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּקָא נָקֵיב מַרְגָּנִיתָא, אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי קָאֵים מִקַּמֵּיהּ וּבָטֵיל מִמְּלַאכְתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **standing include no monetary loss** at all? **Are we not dealing** with a case **where he was piercing pearls,** a highly remunerative task, **and in the meantime he must stand** for the elder **and** thereby **neglect his work,** which causes him a loss?

The Gemara returns to re-examine the *gezeira shava* between *takum* and *ve-hadarta*. The question was posed: *yakhol yehadreno be-mamon* — perhaps *hiddur* means to honor the sage with gifts and money? The *talmud lomar* was *takum ve-hadarta*: just as *kima* (rising) involves no expenditure, so too *hiddur* involves no expenditure.

But the Gemara challenges this premise: *ve-kima leit beih chisron kis?* Who says rising up costs nothing? Consider the case of a craftsman drilling holes in pearls — work that commands an exceptionally high hourly wage. If he must stop mid-task to rise before a sage, reorient himself, and then resume, he may lose twenty minutes of very expensive labor. Rising, in that case, is far from free.

### Gezeira Shava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: אַקֵּישׁ קִימָה לְהִידּוּר. מָה הִידּוּר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ בִּיטּוּל – אַף קִימָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ בִּיטּוּל. וְאַקֵּישׁ נָמֵי הִידּוּר לְקִימָה, מָה קִימָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס – אַף הִידּוּר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: אֵין בַּעֲלֵי אוּמָּנִיּוֹת רַשָּׁאִין לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בִּמְלַאכְתָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse **juxtaposes standing to reverence: Just as reverence does not include neglect** of work, **so too, standing does not include neglect** of work; therefore, one who is engaged in work is not obligated to stand before an elder. **And** the verse **also juxtaposes reverence to standing: Just as standing includes no monetary loss,** as standing applies only when it does not entail neglect of work, as explained previously, **so too, reverence** is referring to an action **that includes no monetary loss. From here** the Sages **stated: Craftsmen are not permitted to stand before Torah scholars when they are engaged in their work.**

The Gemara's resolution is to run the *hekesh* (analogy) in the opposite direction: *hekesh kima le-hiddur* — we compare *kima* to *hiddur* first. *Mah hiddur she-ein bo bitul af kima she-ein bah bitul* — just as *hiddur* does not require interrupting one's work, so too *kima* does not require interrupting one's work. The specific physical act of rising, from the perspective of *halakha*, is one that need not suspend ongoing labor.

Then the analogy reverses: *ve-akishana hiddur le-kima* — having established that *kima* involves no work-stoppage, and therefore no financial cost, we bring *hiddur* into alignment: *af hiddur she-ein bah chisron kis* — the *hiddur* required of a person is not something that costs money. It may take the form of verbal expression, attentive manner, or other non-monetary gestures; it does not extend to lavishing gifts.

### Craftsworkers Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָתְנַן: כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי אוּמָּנִיּוֹת עוֹמְדִים מִפְּנֵיהֶם, וְשׁוֹאֲלִים בִּשְׁלוֹמָם, וְאוֹמְרִים לָהֶם: אַחֵינוּ אַנְשֵׁי מָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי בּוֹאֲכֶם לְשָׁלוֹם! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵיהֶם עוֹמְדִים, מִפְּנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים אֵין עוֹמְדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** are craftsmen **not** required to stand before Torah scholars? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bikkurim* 3:3): When farmers bring their first fruits to Jerusalem, **all craftsmen stand before them, and greet them, and say to them: Our brothers from such and such a place, welcome!**

From this *gezeira shava* the Gemara draws a practical ruling: *ein ba'alei ummaniyot rasha'in la'amod mipnei talmidei chachamim be-sha'ah she-oskim be-melachtan* — craftsmen are not required (and *Tosafot* clarifies that *rasha'in* here means *required*, not literally *permitted*) to stand before Torah sages when they are engaged in their work.

*Tosafot* raises the obvious question: perhaps the exemption is specifically because they are working for an employer and rising would constitute stealing the employer's time — a concern that would be irrelevant if they were working independently, and in any case a different rationale than the one the Gemara was developing. *Tosafot* therefore offers an alternative: the better reading is simply that *rasha'in* means *obligated*, and the exemption applies broadly. The deeper logic, as emerges from the Gemara, seems to be less about financial cost and more about context: when a craftsman is visibly occupied with his work, failing to rise does not register as disrespect. Everyone understands why he remains seated — he is underneath the sink, he is mid-cut on the workbench. The social expectation of standing is simply not operative in that context.

### Bikkurim Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין: בּוֹא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה חֲבִיבָה מִצְוָה בִּשְׁעָתָהּ, שֶׁהֲרֵי מִפְּנֵיהֶם עוֹמְדִים, מִפְּנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים אֵין עוֹמְדִים. וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִם כֵּן, אַתָּה מַכְשִׁילָן לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** Based on this **Rabbi Yosei bar Avin says: Come and see how beloved is a mitzva** performed **in its** proper **time, as** the craftsmen **stood before those** who were fulfilling a mitzva, whereas **they did not stand before Torah scholars.** The Gemara responds: This does not prove that the same applies to all mitzvot performed in their proper times, as **perhaps it is different there,** with regard to the bringing of the first fruits; for **if so,** i.e., if one does not treat those who bring first fruits with such honor, they will not want to come at all, **and you will cause them to stumble** and sin **in the future.**

The Gemara presses back with a *mishna*: when those bringing *bikkurim* entered *Yerushalayim*, all the craftspeople in the city would rise for them and inquire after their welfare — *acheinu anshei makom ploni, boachem le-shalom* — welcoming them warmly. This seems to show that craftsmen do interrupt their work to show honor. Why should it be different for a Torah sage?

*Rabbi Yochanan* answers directly: *mipneihem omdim mipnei talmidei chachamim ein omdim* — for those bearing *bikkurim* they rise; for Torah sages they do not (when working). *Rabbi Yosei bar Avin* explains the distinction: *bo re'eh kamma chaviva mitzvah be-sha'atah* — the lesson is how precious a *mitzvah* performed at its proper moment is. The *bikkurim* bearers are performing a *mitzvah* in its irreplaceable window of opportunity, and that particular form of devotion commands a heightened degree of public respect — even more than the standing honor owed to a Torah sage. The verse and the institution of *bikkurim* together generate a special demand on all who witness it.

The Gemara counters: perhaps the case of *bikkurim* is distinctive for a more practical reason. *De-im ken ata machshilan le-atid lavo* — if no one greets the *bikkurim* bearers warmly, those who made the long journey from distant towns will feel unwelcomed and will be discouraged from making the trip in future years. The warmth shown them is as much about sustaining the institution of *bikkurim* for coming generations as it is about the honor of the present *mitzvah*. Either way, it is a special case, and the general rule remains: a craftsman engaged in his work need not rise for a Torah sage.

### Bathhouse Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: יָכוֹל יַעֲמוֹד מִפָּנָיו מִבֵּית הַכִּסֵּא וּמִבֵּית הַמֶּרְחָץ. וְלָא? וְהָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא הֲוָה יָתֵיב בֵּי מַסְחוּתָא וְחָלֵיף וְאָזֵיל רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי, וְלָא קָם מִקַּמֵּיהּ וְאִיקְּפַד, וַאֲתָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּהּ: שְׁנֵי חוּמָּשִׁים שָׁנִיתִי לוֹ בְּסֵפֶר תְּהִלִּים, וְלֹא עָמַד מִפָּנַי!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** previously: One **might** have thought that **one should** also **stand before** an Elder **in the lavatory or in the bathhouse;** therefore, the verse said: "You shall stand and you shall revere," which indicates that the mitzva of standing applies only in a place where there is reverence. The Gemara asks: **And** does one **not** show honor in a lavatory? **But Rabbi Ḥiyya was sitting** in **a bathhouse and Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **passed by, and he did not stand before him. And** Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **became angry and went and said to his father,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **I taught** Rabbi Ḥiyya **two** of the **five** parts **of the book of Psalms, and** yet **he did not stand before me.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ: בַּר קַפָּרָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הֲוָה יָתֵיב בֵּי מַסְחוּתָא. עָל וְאָזֵיל רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי וְלָא קָם מִקַּמֵּיהּ, וְאִיקְּפַד, וַאֲתָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּהּ: שְׁנֵי שְׁלִישֵׁי שְׁלִישׁ שָׁנִיתִי לוֹ בְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים – וְלֹא עָמַד מִפָּנַי! וְאָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמָּא בָּהֶן יוֹשֵׁב וּמְהַרְהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, bar Kappara, and some say** it was **Rabbi Shmuel bar Rabbi Yosei, was sitting in a bathhouse. Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **entered and passed by, and he did not stand before him.** Rabbi Shimon **became angry and went and said to his father: I taught him two** of the **nine** parts **of *Torat Kohanim*,** the halakhic midrash on Leviticus, **and** yet **he did not stand before me. And** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Rabbi Shimon: **Perhaps he was sitting and contemplating** what you taught him and did not see you come in.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּבָתֵּי גַּוָּאֵי, הָא בְּבָתֵּי בָּרָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this is **not difficult: This** *halakha*, that one is not required to stand in a bathhouse, applies **to the inner rooms,** where everyone is naked; standing in a place of this kind certainly does not bestow honor. **That** *halakha*, that one is obligated to stand in a bathhouse, applies **to the outer rooms,** where people are still dressed.

Turning back to an earlier point in the *baraita* — *yakhol ya'amod mi-panav mi-beit ha-kise u-mi-beit ha-merchat* — the Gemara addresses the bathhouse exception in more detail. *Rabbi Chiya* was sitting in a bathhouse when *Rabbi Shimon berabi* — the son of *Rebbe*, himself a figure of *nasi*-level standing — passed through, and *Rabbi Chiya* did not rise. *Rabbi Shimon* was offended and went to complain to his father *Rebbe*, saying: I taught him two-fifths of *Sefer Tehillim* and he did not rise before me.

This is a striking Gemara on its own. The complaint is not that *Rabbi Shimon* is objectively a Torah giant before whom all must stand. The claim is personal: *I taught him*, making *Rabbi Chiya* his *talmid* in a *rebbe-talmid* relationship. The principle in *Pirkei Avot* that one who teaches even a single matter to another is called that person's *rebbe* and receives the forms of respect associated with that relationship is clearly operative here. The detail that the teaching was two-fifths of *Tehillim* is notable — this may not have been instruction in the most advanced levels of scholarship. Yet the *rebbe-talmid* bond it created was considered sufficient to generate an obligation to stand.

*Tosafot* elaborates on the structure of *Sefer Tehillim* in this context. Just as the Torah has five *chumashim*, so too *Sefer Tehillim* is divided into five books. *Rashi* understands *shnei chumashim* as two-fifths of *Sefer Tehillim*, using *chumash* in the sense of one-fifth. *Tosafot* adds that the five books of *Tehillim* can be identified by where the word *Amen* appears as a conclusion — each of the first four sections ends with *Amen*, marking its close, while the fifth (ending with *Hallelujah*) has no closing *Amen* since it is the end of the entire book. The five books begin respectively at Psalm 1, Psalm 42, Psalm 73, Psalm 90, and Psalm 107.

A second incident is brought: *Bar Kappara* (and some say *Rabbi Shmuel bar Rabbi Yossi*) was sitting in a bathhouse when *Rabbi Shimon berabi* passed by, and again no one rose. *Rabbi Shimon* reported this to *Rebbe*, saying he had taught them two-thirds of a third — that is, two-ninths — of *Torat Kohanim*, the *Sifra*. *Rebbe* responded: *shema bahen yoshev u-meharher* — perhaps they were sitting and reviewing internally what you had taught them, so absorbed in your Torah that they did not notice your passage. The defense is offered gently, even affectionately — *because* of your Torah they were too distracted to stand for you. The Gemara notes that this defense implies they would have been wrong not to stand, had they simply failed to notice. And since they were reviewing Torah, the question arises whether they were in the inner room of the bathhouse — where Torah thought is generally inappropriate — or the outer room. The Gemara resolves: they must have been in the outer room, since *Rabbah bar bar Chana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* taught that one may engage in Torah thought in all places except a bathhouse and a privy. Alternatively, the Gemara suggests, perhaps they were in the inner room but the Torah was so embedded in their minds that they could not stop the thoughts even there — an involuntary *ones* of the intellect.

The bathhouse discussion thus bifurcates cleanly: the inner room, where everyone is unclothed, is a context in which social distinction is suspended and the obligation to rise does not apply. The outer room, where people remain partially clothed and social context is still operative, retains the full obligation. The exception for the inner room is not a license for disrespect but a recognition that *hiddur* is inherently contextual — it operates where the social conditions that give it meaning are present.

## Standing Requirements

### Four Amot Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אֵיזוֹהִי קִימָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ הִידּוּר – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּרַבּוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּבְהָק, אֲבָל בְּרַבּוֹ הַמּוּבְהָק – מְלֹא עֵינָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § A Sage **taught: What is** the type of **standing that indicates reverence? You must say** that this applies when it is clear that one is standing in the elder's honor, which is within **four cubits** of him. **Abaye said: We said** this *halakha*, that one must stand within four cubits of the elder, **only with regard to** one **who is not his primary teacher; but for his primary teacher** he must stand when he is **within his range of vision,** i.e., as soon as he sees him, even if he is more than four cubits away.

The *baraita* continues: *tanna eizehu kima she-yesh bah hiddur — havei omer ze arba amot* — what constitutes a rising that contains genuine *hiddur*? The answer is: within four *amot*. When the sage enters one's immediate zone of proximity, the rising is unambiguously directed toward him and personally felt by him. From across a room, standing is less clearly connected to the sage's presence and does not carry the same directness of honor.

### Abaye's Position

*Abaye* introduces a qualification: *lo amran ella be-rabbo she-eino muvhak* — the four-*amot* rule applies only to a teacher who is not one's primary *rebbe*. For *rabbo muvhak*, one's principal teacher, the standard is *melo einav* — as long as the teacher is within eyesight, one must rise.

This is genuinely striking. The Gemara had established that the *hiddur* of rising is most meaningful within four *amot* — in the sage's immediate space. Why, then, is the requirement *expanded* for one's primary teacher? The resolution lies in a different logic entirely. For one's *rabbo muvhak*, the act of rising is not primarily about demonstrating to others the respect due a great man. It is about the impossibility of remaining comfortably seated when the person from whom one has received the most Torah — who has shaped one's mind and soul — enters one's field of vision. The question is not how much the gesture communicates to onlookers; it is what remaining seated would mean for the *talmid* himself. One might also note, as raised in discussion, that the *rebbe* knows the *talmid* and understands why the student rises — the gesture is received personally in a way it cannot be when rising for an objective stranger across the room. There is also an element of cultivating the inner disposition of gratitude and reverence, not merely performing an outward act of honor.

### Donkey Ear Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי מִכִּי הֲוָה חָזֵי לֵיהּ לְאוּדְנֵיהּ דְּחַמְרָא דְּרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָתֵי, הֲוָה קָאֵים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara likewise reports that **Abaye would stand as soon as he saw the ear of Rav Yosef's donkey coming** toward him.

*Abaye's* practice exemplified this. *Abaye miki hava chazi lei le-udna de-chamra de-Rav Yosef de-ati* — when *Abaye* would catch sight of the ear of *Rav Yosef's* donkey as it came into view — he would already rise. So attuned was he to his teacher's presence that a glimpse of the donkey's ear crossing into his field of vision was sufficient.

### Attention Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי הֲוָה רְכִיב חֲמָרָא וְקָא מְסַגֵּי אַגּוּדָּא דִּנְהַר סַגְיָא, יָתֵיב רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא וְרַבָּנַן בְּאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא וְלָא קָמוּ מִקַּמֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: וְלָאו רַב מוּבְהָק אֲנָא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לָאו אַדַּעְתִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Abaye was riding a donkey along the bank of the Sagya River. Rav Mesharshiyya and** other **rabbis were sitting on the other bank** of the river, **and they did not stand before him.** Abaye **said to them: Am I not** your **primary teacher?** You are therefore required to stand before me, despite the fact that I am far away. **They said to him: That** did **not** enter **our minds,** i.e., we did not see you at all.

The Gemara then recounts an incident involving *Abaye* himself. He was riding his donkey along the riverbank when *Rav Mesharshiya* and other rabbis were sitting on the opposite bank. They were within his eyesight, and he within theirs, yet they did not rise. *Abaye* challenged them: *v'lav rabbo muvhak ana* — am I not your primary teacher? Why did you not stand? They replied: *lav ada'tin* — we were not paying attention.

Their answer illuminates a genuine difficulty with the *melo einav* standard. When a teacher is across a river rather than directly approaching, one's awareness of his presence may not reach the threshold that generates the felt obligation — not because the sight is physically absent but because it has not registered. The story implies that part of the *kavod* owed to a *rabbo muvhak* is precisely this attentiveness — remaining sufficiently present to one's surroundings that one notices the teacher even at a distance, and responds accordingly.

## Zaken's Obligation

### Don't Burden Others

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְזָקֵן שֶׁלֹּא יַטְרִיחַ – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זָקֵן וְיָרֵאתָ״.
>
> **Translation:** § It was further stated in the *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: From where** is it derived that **an elder should not trouble** others to honor him? **The verse states:** "And you shall revere the face of **an elder, and you shall fear** your God."

*Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* asks: *minayin le-zaken she-lo yatriach* — how do we know that a *zaken* has a responsibility not to burden others with the obligation to show him honor? The source is the phrase *zaken ve-yareta* — the *zaken* himself must fear God, must be conscious of the weight he places on those around him. The *zaken* is not merely the passive recipient of honor; he has his own responsibility to minimize situations that compel others to demonstrate respect they might find burdensome.

This is distinct from being *mochel al kevodo*. The *zaken* who avoids entering a crowded room does not thereby waive his *kavod* — he simply chooses not to invoke it unnecessarily.

### Abaye Takes Detours

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נָקְטִינַן דְּאִי מַקֵּיף – חָיֵי. אַבָּיֵי מַקֵּיף, רַבִּי זֵירָא מַקֵּיף.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We have a tradition that if** a Sage **circumnavigates** an area so that people will not have to stand before him, he **will live** a long life. The Gemara relates that **Abaye** would **circumnavigate** an area, and likewise **Rabbi Zeira** would **circumnavigate** an area.

*Abaye* is invoked again: *naktinan de-makif chaye* — we have a tradition that one who takes a detour to avoid situations requiring others to rise for him will live a long life. *Abaye* practiced this himself: he would go around places where he knew people would be compelled to stand for him.

This makes for an interesting contrast with the preceding story. *Abaye* was *makpid* when *Rav Mesharshiya's* students failed to rise across the river — he insisted on the *kavod* when the situation called for it. Yet he simultaneously went out of his way to avoid creating those situations in the first place. The two practices are not contradictory. Seeking honor is not the same as insisting on it when the circumstance already demands it. The *zaken* does not circulate hunting for occasions where people must stand for him; but when those occasions arise, the obligation is real and cannot simply be waved away.

### Mata Mechasya Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא הֲוָה יָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי, חֲלַף הָהוּא גַּבְרָא קַמֵּיהּ וְלָא מִיכַּסֵּי רֵישָׁא. אָמַר: כַּמָּה חֲצִיף הָא גַּבְרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּלְמָא מִמָּתָא מַחְסֵיָא נִיהוּ, דְּגִיסִי בַּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another incident involving honor one demonstrates for his teacher. Once, when **Ravina was sitting before Rabbi Yirmeya of Difti, a certain man passed before him and did not cover his head.** Ravina **said: How rude is this man,** who does not show respect by covering his head in honor of a rabbi. Rabbi Yirmeya of Difti **said to him: Perhaps he is from** the city of **Mata Meḥasya, where rabbis are common** and the people living there are consequently not as careful to display honor as those in other places.

The Gemara then narrates an incident in which *Ravina* was sitting before *Rav Yirmeya of Diphti* when a certain man passed before them with his head uncovered. Ravina remarked on the person's *chutzpah* — how brazen to walk before rabbis without a head covering. *Rav Yirmeya* responded: *dilma mi-Mata Mechasya nihu de-gissei be-rabbanan* — perhaps he is from Mata Mechasya, where people are on such familiar terms with their rabbis that they have ceased to cover their heads in their presence.

The relevance of this story is not immediately obvious, but it speaks directly to the tension at the heart of this entire section. *Hiddur* is not only rising; it encompasses other forms of social respect, including, apparently, covering one's head in the presence of a Torah scholar. The comment about Mata Mechasya carries what reads as a gentle but pointed critique: when rabbis make themselves so accessible, so thoroughly *mochel al kevodo*, that ordinary marks of deference fall away, those coming from that community may carry habits that are offensive elsewhere and that reflect an erosion of appropriate *kavod hatorah*. The distinction between *Abaye's* practice — avoiding situations that force others to show honor, while insisting on it when the situation arises — and the culture implied by the Mata Mechasya remark is meaningful. *Abaye* did not allow his *kavod* to be simply abandoned; he tried not to impose it unnecessarily. That is very different from a community in which the rabbis have become so casual that the whole framework of *kevod hatorah* has weakened.

## Respect for Elders

### Halakha ke-Issi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״מִפְּנֵי שֵׂיבָה תָּקוּם״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ כׇּל שֵׂיבָה בַּמַּשְׁמָע. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּאִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲוָה קָאֵי מִקַּמֵּי סָבֵי דְאַרְמָאֵי. אָמַר: כַּמָּה הַרְפַּתְקֵי עֲדוֹ עֲלַיְיהוּ דְּהָנֵי. רָבָא מֵיקָם לָא קָאֵי, הִידּוּר עָבֵד לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § It was stated previously that **Isi ben Yehuda says** that as the verse states: **"Before the hoary head you shall stand,"** it indicates that **even anyone of hoary head is included,** not only a Torah scholar. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Isi ben Yehuda.** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Yoḥanan** himself **would stand before Aramean,** i.e., gentile, **elders. He said: How many experiences [*harpatkei*] have occurred to these** individuals. It is appropriate to honor them, due to the wisdom they have garnered from their long lives. **Rava would not stand before them,** but **he displayed reverence to them.**

The Gemara now turns to *Issi ben Yehuda's* position that *mipnei seiva takum* includes any elderly person, and *Rabbi Yochanan* rules: *halakha ke-Issi ben Yehuda*. The obligation to rise extends beyond Torah sages to elderly people generally.

### R. Yochanan and Elders

*Rabbi Yochanan* himself would rise before elderly non-Jews (*sabei de-armai*). His reasoning: *kamma harpatkei adu alaiahu de-hanei* — how many life events, trials, and experiences have passed over this person. The sheer accumulation of what a long life entails — the navigating of loss, hardship, uncertainty, and the weight of years — commands a form of respect in its own right. This is a powerful statement about the intrinsic dignity of a life richly and fully lived, entirely independent of Torah learning.

### Rava's Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי יָהֵיב יְדָא לְסָבֵי. רָבָא מְשַׁדַּר שְׁלוּחֵיהּ. רַב נַחְמָן מְשַׁדַּר גּוֹזָאֵי. אָמַר: אִי לָאו תּוֹרָה כַּמָּה נַחְמָן בַּר אַבָּא אִיכָּא בְּשׁוּקָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye would extend a hand to elders** so that they could lean on him. **Rava would send his agent** to help them. **Rav Naḥman would send officers [*goza'ei*],** his servants, to assist elders. **He said: If not for the Torah, how many** people named **Naḥman bar Abba would there be in the marketplace?**

*Rava*, however, did not rise before elderly non-Jews, though he was not dismissive of the principle. *Mikam lo kai, chizur avid leh* — he would not fully rise, but he would perform a *chizur*, a slight lift from his chair. *Rava's* approach represents a calibrated balance: life experience commands genuine acknowledgment, but a Torah sage does not rise fully before someone whose distinction is not Torah. The honor of *Torah* cannot be entirely subordinated to the honor of mere age.

### Personal vs. Servant Help

*Abaye yahiv yada le-saba* — *Abaye* would offer his hand to elderly people, helping them walk and providing personal assistance. He gave of himself directly, this great Torah sage extending personal service. *Rava*, again consistent with his more tempered approach, *meshdar shluchei* — would send his attendants to assist, rather than doing so himself. *Rav Nachman* similarly sent attendants, *meshdar guzzei*.

### Rav Nachman's Logic

*Rav Nachman* articulated the logic behind his restraint explicitly: *amar ilav Torah, kamma Nachman bar Abba ika ba-shuka* — if not for Torah, how many *Nachmans bar Abba* are there in the market? What distinguishes me? Torah alone. To offer personal service to an elderly person would imply a kind of equivalence — that life experience and Torah stand on the same level — and that, *Rav Nachman* felt, compromises the unique and surpassing claim of *Torah*. It is not that age deserves no respect; it is that the representative of Torah cannot act in a way that implicitly diminishes what Torah represents. Hence the mediated response: assistance through an attendant acknowledges the elderly person's dignity without the Torah sage himself performing an act of personal service that would blur this distinction.

## Frequency Limit

### Twice Daily Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַיְיבוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אֵין תַּלְמִיד חָכָם רַשַּׁאי לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי רַבּוֹ אֶלָּא שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִהְיֶה כְּבוֹדוֹ מְרוּבֶּה מִכְּבוֹד שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aivu says** that **Rabbi Yannai says: A Torah scholar is permitted to stand before his teacher only** once **in the morning and** once in the **evening, so that** the teacher's **honor should not be greater than the honor of Heaven,** as one recites the *Shema*, which is tantamount to greeting God, once in the morning and once in the evening.

*Rav Yannai* teaches: *ein talmid chakham rasha'i la'amod mipnei rabbo ella shacharit ve-arvit kedei she-lo yehei kevodo merubbeh mi-kevod shamayim* — a Torah student is not permitted (or, as *Tosafot* notes, not *required*) to rise before his *rebbe* more than twice a day. The reason: one's honor of a *rebbe* should not exceed one's honor of Heaven. God receives acknowledgment through *Keriat Shema* in the morning and evening; the *rebbe* should not receive more formal rising than that.

*Rashi* formulates this in terms of *shepenei yotzro eino mekabbel ella shacharit ve-arvit* — the Creator Himself receives formal acknowledgment only morning and evening. Whether one accounts for *Mincha* by combining it with *Ma'ariv*, or whether the reference is specifically to *Keriat Shema* rather than *Tefilla*, the principle is clear: there is a ceiling on frequency.

The practical context matters. If a *rebbe* is present with his students throughout the day — in the same *yeshiva*, never leaving and returning — then just as one does not recite a new *berakha* on each lightning strike during a continuous storm, one does not rise each time the *rebbe* passes through. But if the *rebbe* departs and returns, each arrival may renew the obligation. The twice-daily limit likely speaks to the case of continuous presence, where morning and evening framings of the relationship suffice to express the ongoing honor, without requiring a fresh rising every time the teacher moves through the room.
