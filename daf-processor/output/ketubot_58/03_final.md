# Ketubot 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 12-Month Wait & Terumah

### Nisuin Delay Context

The daf opens by picking up from the final sugya of 57b, which introduced the twelve-month postponement available to either party when the time arrives to move from *kiddushin* to *nisuin*. As we learned, a *katana* — a minor girl — along with her father could defer the *nisuin* throughout the period of her minority; the actual cohabitation was not meant to begin until she was at least a *naara*, twelve and a half years old. By contrast, once a woman becomes a *bogeres*, the clock begins running automatically, without any formal demand. The debate about who caused the delay — whether it was the husband's fault, the wife's fault, or no one's — was addressed on the opening daf of the tractate and is not the concern here.

What the *gemara* now addresses is this: once the twelve-month period has arrived and the parties have not yet proceeded to *nisuin*, the husband must begin paying the wife's food bills and may provide her with *terumah* as part of that support — if the husband is a *kohen*. The immediate question is on what basis she is permitted to eat *terumah* at all, given that she is still only an *arusah* and not yet a *nesu'ah*.

### Ulla: Kinyan Kaspo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ זְמַן וְלֹא נִישְּׂאוּ. אָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, אֲרוּסָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכֹהֵן כִּי יִקְנֶה נֶפֶשׁ קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ״, וְהַאי נָמֵי קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ הוּא. מָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת — שֶׁמָּא יִמְזְגוּ לָהּ כּוֹס בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ, וְתַשְׁקֶה לְאָחִיהָ וְלַאֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **If the** appointed **time** for the wedding **arrived and they did not get married,** she may partake of *teruma*. **Ulla said: By Torah law, the daughter of a non-priest betrothed** to a priest **may partake of *teruma*** immediately, even before the wedding date arrives, **as it is stated: "If a priest buy any soul, the acquisition of his money,** he may eat of it" (Leviticus 22:11), **and this** woman **is also an acquisition of his money** through the betrothal. Therefore, she is entitled to partake of *teruma*. **What,** then, **is the reason** the Sages **said** that **she may not partake?** It is **lest someone pour her a cup** of *teruma* wine while she is **in her father's house.** Although she may drink it as the betrothed of a priest, since she is still living in her father's house there is a concern that **she will give her brother or sister to drink** from the wine, which is prohibited, as they are non-priests.

Ulla teaches: *devar Torah arusah bas Yisrael ocheles biterumah* — biblically, a betrothed daughter of a *Yisrael* household is permitted to eat *terumah* from the moment of *erusin*. The source is the verse, *v'kohen ki yikneh nefesh kinyan kaspo* — "and a *kohen* who acquires a soul, the acquisition of his money" — which in its simple reading addresses a slave purchased by a *kohen*, who thereby becomes entitled to eat *terumah*. The *gemara* extends this: *v'hai nami kinyan kaspo hu* — a wife is likewise the acquisition of his money, since *kiddushin* is effected through a monetary transfer. This is the foundational teaching of the first *mishnah* in *Kiddushin*: a woman is *nikneis* through *kesef*. Accordingly, from the moment of *kiddushin*, she falls within the category of *kinyan kaspo* and is in principle entitled to eat *terumah*.

### Yerushalmi Debate

The *Yerushalmi* takes a different approach. Rather than deriving the wife's right from *kinyan kaspo*, it derives it from *beiso* — "his household." The verse speaks of the household of the *kohen* eating *terumah*, and *beiso zo ishto*, "his household — that is his wife." Since *bayis* ordinarily implies the context of *nisuin*, the *Yerushalmi* appears to hold that biblically one must wait until *nisuin*, or at least until a stage sufficiently close to it. According to this view, the arrival of the twelve-month mark — at which point there is a binding obligation to proceed with *nisuin* and the husband is already paying the food bills — constitutes a state close enough to *nisuin* to qualify as *beiso*.

The *Bavli*, by contrast, holds that even from the moment of *erusin* the wife is in principle permitted to eat *terumah* by virtue of *kinyan kaspo*. This reflects a genuine dispute between the two Talmuds: the *Bavli* requires no proximity to *nisuin* for the biblical entitlement to attach; the *Yerushalmi* does.

### Concern: Sharing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הִגִּיעַ זְמַן וְלֹא נִישְּׂאוּ נָמֵי! הָתָם, דּוּכְתָּא מְיַיחֵד לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **if the time arrived and they did not get married,** there should **also** be concern that she might give it to members of her family, as she is still in her father's house. Why, then, does the mishna say that she is permitted to partake of *teruma* at that time? The Gemara answers: **There,** after the time for the wedding has arrived, **he designates** a specific **place for her.** Since her husband is obligated to provide for her sustenance, he will want to ensure that she receives her food in a particular place so that she not use it to feed her family. This mitigates the concern that she may inadvertently give it to her brother or sister to drink.

If the woman is biblically entitled to eat *terumah* from the time of *kiddushin*, why did the rabbis forbid it during that period? The *gemara* explains: *shema yimze'gu lah kos b'veit aviha v'tashkehu l'achia ul'achota* — lest they pour her a cup of *terumah* wine in her father's house and she go and give her brother and sister to drink. While she remains in her father's household, the practical danger of inadvertently sharing *terumah* with non-*kohanim* is too great, and the rabbis therefore prohibited it. Once the twelve months arrive, however, the prohibition is lifted.

The *gemara* immediately challenges: even when the twelve-month mark arrives and the wedding has been postponed, she is still not yet living with her husband — so why should we no longer be concerned that she will share with family members?

### Father's House Setup

The *gemara* answers: *hatam duchtam m'yached lah* — once the twelve-month deadline arrives, the husband will designate a separate place for her to live apart from her father's household. Even if the wedding itself has been delayed for some reason, he will set her up in a distinct residence. It is a striking assumption on the *gemara*'s part that this was so standard a social reality that it could be relied upon halachically — particularly when one considers that maintaining a third residence requires financial resources the groom may not always have.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לָקִיט כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל לָא לֵיכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה, דִּלְמָא אָתוּ לְמֵיכַל בַּהֲדֵיהּ! הַשְׁתָּא מִדִּידְהוּ סָפוּ לֵיהּ — מִדִּידֵיהּ אָכְלִי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, if that is so,** then the *halakha* should also be that **a gleaner** who is **a priest** and is employed **by an Israelite may not partake of *teruma*, lest** the other members of the household **come to eat with him** from the *teruma*. The Gemara rejects this: **Now,** even though the members of the Israelite household **feed** the priest **from their** food, as he is their employee, **would they eat from his** food? They would not. Therefore, there is no reason for concern and no reason to prohibit him from eating *teruma*. This is Ulla's opinion.

The *gemara* then raises an analogous challenge: if a *kohen* is a hired laborer working for a non-*kohen*, should he not be permitted to bring his *terumah* food to work, lest he share it with his employer? The *gemara* dismisses the comparison: *hashta mididei safu leh* — there, the employer is the one providing the worker's food anyway; there is no realistic concern that the employer will reach for the *kohen*'s sandwich. The domestic situation of a woman still in her father's household, surrounded by family, is categorically different.

## Simpon & Defect

### Defect Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם סִימְפּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** However, **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda said:** The reason for the rabbinic decree is **due to abrogation [*simfon*],** cancellation of the contract. It may become known after the betrothal that she has blemishes that can retroactively annul the betrothal, and it would then become apparent that she had partaken of *teruma* unlawfully.

Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda offers a second, independent reason why the rabbis forbade an *arusah* from eating *terumah* even though she is biblically entitled: *mishum simpon* — because of the concern over a defect. The term *simpon*, apparently a Greek loanword, describes a material flaw in an acquisition that would render the entire transaction a *mekach ta'ut* — a sale based on a mistake. Transposing this concept from commercial law to marriage: once the couple begins to live together and becomes physically intimate, the husband may discover something about his wife that he did not know before, something so fundamental that it constitutes a *mekach ta'ut* and retroactively nullifies the *kiddushin* altogether. Were she eating *terumah* in the interim — from the time of *kiddushin* — she would have been doing so without legal basis. A contemporary example that *poskim* grapple with: if it emerges that the husband was gay and had concealed this, the entire *kiddushin* may be void from the outset as *kiddushei ta'ut*, in which case no *get* would be required. These situations have arisen in practice.

### Response to Simpon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נִכְנְסָה לְחוּפָּה וְלֹא נִבְעֲלָה נָמֵי! הָתָם, מִיבְדָּק בָּדֵיק לַהּ וַהֲדַר מְעַיֵּיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if the rabbinic decree that prohibits a woman betrothed to a priest from partaking of *teruma* is due to concern for possible abrogation of the marriage, then a woman who **entered the wedding canopy but has not** yet **engaged in sexual intercourse** should **also** be prohibited from partaking of *teruma*, as the husband does not yet know whether she has blemishes. The Gemara answers: **There,** in that situation, **he investigates her** through the agency of his female relatives **and** only **then enters** the wedding canopy. Consequently, there is no longer any concern about abrogation.

The *gemara* immediately presses: if the concern is *simpon*, how does anything change after twelve months? And more pointedly, how can we permit *terumah* after *chupah* — the actual wedding — if the couple has not yet been intimate? Perhaps we should wait until after *biah* before permitting *terumah*.

The *gemara* responds: *hatam mivdak badik lah v'hadar minasav* — before proceeding to the wedding itself, with its attendant expense and social commitment, the parties will have spent time together getting to know one another. Physical intimacy may not yet have occurred, but by the time of the *chupah*, there has been sufficient personal acquaintance that we are no longer meaningfully concerned about *bitul kiddushin*. The *gemara* concedes that this does not eliminate all risk — theoretically, something could still come to light after *biah* — but the remaining concern is diminished enough that providing for the woman outweighs it. The earlier stage, when the parties had barely met, was a categorically different situation.

### Slave Purchase Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, עֶבֶד כֹּהֵן שֶׁלְּקָחוֹ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל לָא לֵיכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה, מִשּׁוּם סִימְפּוֹן! סִימְפּוֹן בַּעֲבָדִים לֵיכָּא, דְּאִי דְּאַבָּרַאי — הָא קָחָזֵי לֵיהּ. וְאִי דְּגַוַּאי — לִמְלָאכָה קָא בָּעֵי, וְשֶׁבַּסֵּתֶר לָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, if that is so,** then according to this rationale, **a priest's slave whom** the priest **purchased from an Israelite should not partake of *teruma*, due to** concern of **abrogation.** Perhaps the priest will discover a defect in the slave, resulting in the retroactive cancellation of the acquisition and causing the slave to return to his Israelite master after he had mistakenly eaten *teruma*. The Gemara answers: **There is no abrogation with regard to slaves,** since no type of defect could cause the cancellation of the transaction. The reason for this is **that if** the defect **is external, then he sees it** at the point of sale and accepts it. **And if** the defect **is internal,** since **he needs him for labor, concealed** defects **do not concern him.**

The *gemara* then poses a parallel from the world of acquisition: *eved kohen she'lakacho mi'Yisrael lo yochal b'trumah mishum simpon* — should not a slave purchased by a *kohen* from a non-*kohen* be barred from eating *terumah* on account of *simpon*, lest the purchase turn out to be a *mekach ta'ut* and the slave will have eaten *terumah* retroactively without authorization?

The *gemara* answers: *simpon b'avadim leika* — the concern of *simpon* does not apply to slaves. Why not? Two principles dispose of the cases: if the alleged defect is external and visible — such as a limp or a broken limb — then *de'ee d'eilai ha kachazi leh*, the buyer could see it, and if he did not look carefully, that is his own failing; he cannot claim *ta'ut*. And if the defect is internal or not readily apparent — things that would only be visible when the slave is undressed, for instance — *l'mlacha kaba'ei*, the slave is acquired for labor; what does the buyer care about private physical characteristics that are irrelevant to the work?

### Visible vs Hidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוּבְיוּסְטוּס — הִגִּיעוֹ. מַאי אִיכָּא — לִסְטִים מְזוּיָּין אוֹ מוּכְתָּב לַמַּלְכוּת, הָנְהוּ קָלָא אִית לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **a gambler [*kuvyustus*], it has come to him,** meaning that the seller has caught the buyer in a binding transaction, and he cannot annul the sale due to this kind of defect, as these characteristics are common in slaves. **What is** the *halakha* if it was discovered that the slave was **an armed bandit or** that **the king** had **signed** his death warrant, and there is a danger that the government will catch him and execute him? These are serious and uncommon defects that in principle could invalidate a sale. However, **these** defects **generate publicity.** In such unusual and severe circumstances, everyone is aware of them. Therefore, it is assumed that the buyer knew about them as well and nevertheless acquiesced to buy the slave. Consequently, there is no reason to revoke the sale of a slave.

The *gemara* then asks: does it truly not matter what is hidden? Some hidden facts are certainly significant. For instance, if the slave turns out to be a thief, or — and here the *rishonim* disagree on the translation — a *kuvyustus*: Rashi understands this as a kidnapper; Tosafot argue that it means a dice player, a gambler. ArtScroll follows Rashi's reading. Are these not hidden defects that one could not have known about?

The *gemara* replies: *hachi hu* — these are on you. In the social reality in which the *gemara* operates, such attributes were considered something a buyer of a slave ought to anticipate. More precisely, as Rashi frames it, the buyer should have expected this; and Tosafot frames it even more squarely as a matter of buyer responsibility: these were things of public knowledge, and entering into a purchase without investigating them is the buyer's own problem. He cannot claim *mekach ta'ut* for failing to do due diligence.

### Known Defects

What then would constitute a genuine hidden defect that could undo a slave purchase? The *gemara* offers two examples: *listim mezuyan* — an armed bandit, not merely a petty thief or gambler, but a violent criminal — and *muchtav lamalchut* — one who was already registered for execution or designated as a felon by the government. These, however, also do not support a *mekach ta'ut* claim: *hanehu kala it lehu* — these are matters of public record. If the slave was wanted by the government or known to be a dangerous criminal, this was knowable information. Rashi's position is that the buyer effectively knew and accepted; Tosafot's position is that even if he did not personally know, the information was publicly available and it was his responsibility to investigate. Either way, one cannot claim to have been duped when the defect was a matter of common knowledge. The only genuine basis for *mekach ta'ut* in a slave purchase would be something that was truly unknowable and not of public record at all.

### Practical Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי, בֵּין לְמָר וּבֵין לְמָר לָא אָכְלָה, מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ קִבֵּל, מָסַר, וְהָלַךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **After all, according to this Sage,** Ulla, **and according to that Sage,** Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda, **she may not partake** of *teruma*, so **what is** the difference **between them?** The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them is** in cases where **he accepted,** or **he transferred or went.** If the husband explicitly accepted her blemishes, there is no concern with regard to abrogation, but there is still a concern that she will give her family members *teruma* wine. Conversely, if the father transferred his daughter to the agents of the husband and entrusted her to their care, or if the father's own agents went with the girl and the agents of the husband, there is no longer concern that she might give her family members *teruma*, as she is not with them, but there is still a concern about abrogation.

The *gemara* then draws together the two concerns — sharing with family, and *simpon* — and asks *mai beinaihu*, what practical case would depend on which rationale is operative? One such case: *kabal u'masar v'halach* — if the husband said explicitly that he accepts whatever turns out to be true about his wife, whatever condition she is in, he waives any *mekach ta'ut* claim. According to the *simpon* concern, once he has accepted the risk, she could eat *terumah* immediately from *kiddushin*. According to the concern about sharing with family, it would not help at all — she is still living in her father's house and the risk of sharing remains.

A second case: *masar v'halach* — the situation discussed earlier where the father transfers the bride to the groom's emissaries and she is in transit between the two households, no longer in her father's home but not yet in the groom's. According to the sharing concern, she can now eat *terumah* — she is out of her father's house. According to the *simpon* concern, she still cannot — the intimacy that would reveal any defect has not yet occurred.

## Terumah Proportions

### R. Tarfon: All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: נוֹתְנִין לָהּ הַכֹּל תְּרוּמָה וְכוּ׳. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּבַת כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן, אֲבָל בְּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מֶחֱצָה חוּלִּין וּמֶחֱצָה תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that there is a dispute with regard to how a priest must provide for the sustenance of his betrothed once the appointed time for the marriage arrives. **Rabbi Tarfon says: He may give her everything** from ***teruma*.** Rabbi Akiva says: He must give her half non-sacred food and half may be *teruma*. **Abaye said:** This **dispute** is referring **to a daughter** of **a priest** betrothed **to a priest. But** with regard to **an Israelite woman** who was betrothed **to a priest, all agree** that he must give her **half non-sacred** food **and half** may be ***teruma*.** A priest's daughter is familiar with the *halakhot* of *teruma* and knows how to handle it when she is ritually impure, but when an Israelite woman is not yet familiar with these procedures, there is concern that she might defile the *teruma*. Consequently, she is given some non-sacred food to use as well.

The *gemara* now turns to a different dimension of the same question: not whether the wife may eat *terumah*, but how much of her food allocation the husband may provide in the form of *terumah*. *Terumah* requires more careful handling than *chulin* — most critically, a woman in a state of *niddah* may not eat it, and *terumah* typically fetches less on the market than ordinary food. Rabbi Tarfon holds that the husband may give her *kol terumah* — her entire food allocation in the form of *terumah*.

One should note that at the time of the *mishnah*, a woman was *niddah* for approximately one week out of the month — a strict seven-day period from the onset of bleeding. The later practice of counting seven clean days, which became universal and extended the period of *niddah* to roughly one third of the month, came about only in the *gemara*'s era. At the time of the *mishnah*, then, a woman was a *niddah* for only about a quarter of the month — which makes the option of providing all *terumah* somewhat less extreme than it would later appear, though still a meaningful challenge.

### Abaye's Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַחְלוֹקֶת בַּאֲרוּסָה, אֲבָל בִּנְשׂוּאָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מֶחֱצָה חוּלִּין וּמֶחֱצָה תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye** also **said** that this **dispute** is referring only to **a betrothed woman** whose wedding date has arrived, **but** with regard to **a married woman, all agree** that her husband must give her **half** of her needs from **non-sacred** food **and half** may be ***teruma*,** as it is not appropriate for his wife to have to go to the trouble of selling *teruma* in order to obtain non-sacred food.

Abaye clarifies the scope of the dispute: *machlokes be-vas kohen le-kohen* — the disagreement between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva applies only when the wife comes from a *kohen* household. If she herself is a *bas kohen*, she has grown up handling *terumah*, knows its laws, understands when she must refrain from eating it and how to arrange for its conversion to *chulin* when necessary. Only in that case does Rabbi Tarfon say the husband may give her all *terumah*.

*Aval be-vas Yisrael le-kohen* — if the woman is a daughter of a *Yisrael* household marrying a *kohen*, then *divrei hakol mechtza chulin mechtza terumah* — everyone agrees it must be half *chulin* and half *terumah*. A woman with no prior experience of *terumah* cannot reasonably be expected to manage the full complexity of an all-*terumah* food supply — knowing when to refrain, arranging for its sale when she is *niddah*, ensuring that *terumah* temeiah is not consumed. Providing her with only half *terumah* is both more manageable and removes the *michshol* of giving her *terumah* at times when she must be eating *chulin*.

Abaye adds a second dimension: *machlokes be-arusa* — this entire dispute applies only to an *arusah* whose twelve-month deadline has arrived and whose husband is paying her food bills before the *nisuin* itself. *Aval be-nesu'ah* — if the *nisuin* has already taken place and she is living as a married woman — then again *divrei hakol mechtza chulin mechtza terumah*, everyone agrees it must be half and half.

The reason for this distinction, as Rashi explains, is tied to logistics: while she is still an *arusah* living in her father's house, the father is available to help her arrange the conversion of *terumah* to *chulin* when necessary. A *bas kohen* in her father's house has that practical infrastructure. But once she is a *nesu'ah* and is living separately from her husband — for instance, if he has gone on a business trip and is sending her food — she no longer has that support structure, and therefore even a *bas kohen* receives only half and half.

A question arises: during the weeks when she is *niddah* and cannot eat *terumah*, why should the husband be permitted to provide *terumah* at all? He has an obligation to feed her; during that period, the *terumah* is inedible. The answer implicit in these opinions is that she converts the *terumah* she cannot eat into *chulin* — selling it and purchasing ordinary food. The question of whether she can efficiently do so, and at what cost to her, drives the later opinions in the *mishnah*.

### Baraita Echo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: נוֹתְנִין לָהּ הַכֹּל תְּרוּמָה. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מֶחֱצָה חוּלִּין וּמֶחֱצָה תְּרוּמָה. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּבַת כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן, אֲבָל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מֶחֱצָה חוּלִּין וּמֶחֱצָה תְּרוּמָה. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בַּאֲרוּסָה, אֲבָל בִּנְשׂוּאָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מֶחֱצָה חוּלִּין וּמֶחֱצָה תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Tarfon says: He may give her everything** from ***teruma*,** and **Rabbi Akiva says: Half** must be **non-sacred** food **and half** may be ***teruma*. In what** case **is this statement said?** With regard to **a daughter** of **a priest** betrothed **to a priest. But** with regard to **an Israelite woman** betrothed **to a priest, all agree** that she gets **half non-sacred** food **and half** may be ***teruma*. In what** case **is this statement said? With regard to a betrothed woman, but with regard to a married woman, all agree** that he must give **half non-sacred** food **and half** may be ***teruma*.**

A *baraita* is then cited — *tanya nami hachi* — and what follows is, word for word, what Abaye himself said. This raises a genuine methodological question: why was Abaye's statement needed? Why not simply quote the *baraita*?

This is part of a recognized phenomenon that scholars have noted: there exist *baraitot* in the *Bavli* that have no parallel in the *Yerushalmi* or anywhere else in *Shas*, and these *baraitot* are often word-for-word identical to the statements of an *amora*. The possibility worth considering is that either an *amora*'s statement was eventually preserved and transmitted as a *baraita*, or alternatively that the *amora* was quoting a *baraita* and the attribution shifted over time. Whatever the explanation, the phenomenon of an *amora*'s statement being immediately confirmed by a *baraita* in identical language — where that *baraita* has no independent attestation — is a recurring and noteworthy feature of the *Bavli*.

### R. Yehuda Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר: נוֹתְנִין לָהּ שְׁתֵּי יָדוֹת שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה, וְאַחַת שֶׁל חוּלִּין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֵן לָהּ הַכֹּל תְּרוּמָה, וְהִיא מוֹכֶרֶת וְלוֹקַחַת בַּדָּמִים חוּלִּין. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּזְכְּרָה תְּרוּמָה — נוֹתְנִין לָהּ כִּפְלַיִם בְּחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says:** He need not give half and half, but rather **he may give her two parts *teruma* and one** part **non-sacred** food. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He may give her all** of it **in *teruma*, but** as the value of *teruma* is lower than that of non-sacred food, the amount must suffice so that when **she sells** *teruma* during the days when she is ritually impure, **she can buy** a sufficient quantity of **non-sacred** food **with the money. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Wherever *teruma* is mentioned, he** must **give her double** the amount that she would receive **of non-sacred food,** so that she will not have difficulty locating buyers for her *teruma*. *Teruma* is less popular and its price is significantly lower, since its use is restricted. But if she receives a large amount of *teruma*, she will be able to sell it at an even lower price and locate a buyer more easily.

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira offers a different ratio: *shtei yadot shel terumah v'achat shel chulin* — two-thirds *terumah* and one-third *chulin*. This more closely tracks the actual calendar of her *niddah* status. At the time of the *mishnah*, a woman was *niddah* for one week out of the month — approximately one quarter. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira appears to reason that one third *chulin* provides an adequate buffer without giving her more *chulin* than strictly necessary.

Rabbi Yehuda takes yet another position: *notnin lah hakol terumah v'hi mocheret v'lokachat bi-dmei chulin* — one gives her all *terumah*, but she goes and sells it and uses the proceeds to purchase *chulin*. This sounds similar to Rabbi Tarfon's view, but Rashi identifies a crucial difference. *Terumah* does not sell at par; if *chulin* costs ten units and *terumah* sells for eight, then giving her the same nominal quantity of *terumah* as *chulin* leaves her unable to purchase the full *chulin* equivalent she needs. Rabbi Yehuda therefore holds that the husband must give her sufficient *terumah* — an augmented quantity — so that when she converts it to *chulin*, she receives the full equivalent of what she is entitled to eat. He must compensate her for the discount at which *terumah* trades.

### R.S. ben Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ טִירְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel? The Gemara answers: **The** practical difference **between them** is with regard to **effort.** According to Rabbi Yehuda, she must make an effort to find buyers who will provide her with enough non-sacred food for her needs, and consequently, according to him the husband must provide an amount of *teruma* that is equal in value to the amount of non-sacred food to which she is entitled. In contrast, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds additional *teruma* to her allotment beyond this amount, so that she will not be forced to go to as much trouble to sell it.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel extends this logic further: *kol makom she'huzkera trumah noten lah kiflayim bachulin* — wherever *terumah* is mentioned as the currency of her food support, he gives her double the quantity in *terumah* compared to what he would give in *chulin*. The difference between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the *gemara* explains, is *tircha* — the inconvenience and effort involved in converting *terumah* to *chulin*. Even if the monetary value could be recovered by patient dealing, the practical burden of arranging the sale, waiting for a buyer, and navigating the market must be compensated. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that the husband must give her enough of a surplus that she can convert the *terumah* quickly and without effort, rather than holding out for full market value.

## Yavam & Zika

### Yavam No Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיָּבָם אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל בִּתְרוּמָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהַאי קִנְיָן דְּאָחִיו הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **a *yavam* does not enable** the *yevama* **to partake of *teruma*.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? **The Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"The acquisition of his money"** (Leviticus 22:11) may partake of *teruma*, **but this** woman **is his brother's acquisition** and not his, since a *yavam* does not complete his marriage to the *yevama* until he consummates the marriage.

The *mishnah* states: *hayavam eino ma'achil b'trumah* — if the husband (a *kohen*) dies and the wife falls before the *yavam* for *yibum*, the *yavam* cannot feed her *terumah* during the period of *zika*. This applies whether the deceased husband was at the stage of *erusin* or *nisuin* — either way, the surviving brother has a *mitzvah* to perform *yibum*, and the *zika*, the legal bond that ties her to him, functions as a kind of quasi-*kiddushin*. One might have thought that since *zika* resembles *kiddushin* and since the *yavam* is in fact obligated to marry her, she should be permitted to eat *terumah* — perhaps even without waiting a further twelve months.

The *mishnah* rejects this. The *gemara* explains: *kinyan kaspo amar rachmana* — the Torah's basis for allowing a wife to eat *terumah* is *kinyan kaspo*, the acquisition through his money. The *kiddushin* of the original husband was an act of *kinyan kaspo*. The *zika* of the *yavam*, by contrast, is *kinyan d'achiv hu* — it is the *kinyan* of his brother, not his own. It does not carry the full legal force of his own *kiddushin* and therefore does not entitle her to eat *terumah* before the act of *yibum* itself.

### 6 Mo. Yavam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשְׂתָה שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים בִּפְנֵי הַבַּעַל. הַשְׁתָּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּעַל אָמְרַתְּ לָא, בִּפְנֵי הַיָּבָם מִיבַּעְיָא?! ״זוֹ וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר זוֹ״ קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna said that if **she completed six months** from the time of the request for marriage **under the aegis of the husband** and another six months under the aegis of the *yavam*, she may not partake of *teruma*. It then says that this is also true if she completed most of the year under the aegis of the husband, and then it says that this is also true if she completed most of the time under the aegis of the *yavam*. The Gemara asks: **Now** that **you say** that if she completed most of the time **under the aegis of the husband** this does **not** enable her to partake of *teruma*, **is** it **necessary** to say that the same is true if she spent most of the time **under the aegis of the *yavam*?** The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **teaches** the mishna employing the style: **This, and it is unnecessary to say that,** meaning that the cases are organized from the less obvious to the more obvious.

A further ruling: if the wife spent six months with the husband before his death and then six months with the *yavam* — or even eleven months and twenty-nine days with the husband and one additional day with the *yavam* — these periods do not combine to constitute the twelve-month threshold that would ordinarily permit *terumah* consumption.

The *gemara* notes a certain redundancy in the *mishnah*'s formulation. If eleven months and twenty-nine days with the husband himself does not suffice, it is obviously true that the same period partially spent with the *yavam* would not. The *gemara* answers: *zu v'zu v'ein tzarich lomar zu katani* — the *mishnah* employs the rhetorical device of stating both cases in ascending order, even when the second follows logically from the first.

There is a related question about the *kinyan kaspo* derivation. The verse reads *v'chol kohen ki yikneh nefesh kinyan kaspo* — and one might wonder why the *gemara* specifically emphasizes the language *kinyan kaspo* when *kiddushin* need not be performed through money; *kiddushei biah* or *kiddushei shtar* would seem equally applicable. Rashi (or Tosafot — the *shiur* notes some uncertainty in attribution) addresses this: the *gemara* highlights *kinyan kaspo* not to exclude other modes of *kiddushin*, but rather to avoid simply equating the wife with the slave under a single generic *kinyan* rule. The extra language of *kinyan kaspo* specifically addresses *kiddushin* as a distinct category of acquisition. Regardless of the mode of *kiddushin*, the underlying *halacha* would apply equally.

## Simpon & Defect (Structural Analysis)

### 12 Mo. vs Chupah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ מִשְׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר עוּלָּא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה: מִשּׁוּם סִימְפּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **this** ruling, that a woman betrothed to a priest may partake of *teruma* while she is still in her father's house if the time for her wedding has arrived, is according to the **initial** version of the **mishna.** But that according to the final version, she may not partake of *teruma* until she has actually entered the wedding canopy. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this later ruling? **Ulla said, and some say** that **Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda** said, that it is **due to** concern about **abrogation,** as the woman may have some blemish that will cause the annulment of the marriage, and it will then be clear that she consumed *teruma* unlawfully.

The *gemara* returns to the question of *simpon* in its broader structural context. The *mishnah* notes that there was an earlier ruling (*mishna rishona*) permitting the wife to eat *terumah* after twelve months, and a later teaching requiring her to wait until the actual *chupah*. The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what was the reason for the later, more stringent ruling?

Ulla, or according to another version Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda, says: *mishum simpon* — the same concern over a defect leading to *bitul kiddushin* that was introduced earlier in the *sugya*. The *gemara* then asks the obvious structural question: if the concern is *simpon*, why did the earlier ruling permit *terumah* after twelve months at all? And why does the later ruling then change course and require waiting until *chupah*? The *simpon* concern is equally present at both stages.

### Two Concerns Coherently Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְעוּלָּא, קַמַּיְיתָא שֶׁמָּא יִמְזְגוּ לָהּ כּוֹס בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ, וּבָתְרָיְיתָא מִשּׁוּם סִימְפּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Ulla, the first** ruling that the Sages taught in the initial version of the mishna, namely that she may not partake of *teruma* in her father's house immediately after betrothal, is because Ulla was concerned **lest** someone **pour her a cup** of *teruma* wine while she would be **in her father's house. And the latter** ruling, that she may not partake of *teruma* until she actually enters the wedding canopy, even after the time for marriage arrives, is **due to** concern about **abrogation.** Consequently, there are two different reasons for the two different rulings.

The *gemara* now draws out the important distinction: *bishlama le-Ulla* — according to Ulla, the progression is internally coherent. Initially the only concern was that she would share *terumah* with family members. Once the twelve months arrived and he designated a separate residence for her, that concern fell away, and she was permitted to eat *terumah*. Only later did an additional concern surface — *mishum simpon* — which led to the more stringent ruling requiring her to wait until *chupah*. Two separate concerns, discovered at two separate stages, explain why the law tightened over time.

But for Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda, who said from the very beginning that the reason for withholding *terumah* was *simpon*, the question is sharp: *kemeikara mishum simpon ul'vasof mishum simpon* — the concern at the outset was *simpon*, and the concern at the end was *simpon*. What changed? Why was twelve months once considered sufficient, and then later declared insufficient?

### Background Check vs. Cohabitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה, קַמַּיְיתָא מִשּׁוּם סִימְפּוֹן וּבָתְרָיְיתָא מִשּׁוּם סִימְפּוֹן, מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ בְּדִיקַת חוּץ. מָר סָבַר: בְּדִיקַת חוּץ — שְׁמָהּ בְּדִיקָה. וּמָר סָבַר: בְּדִיקַת חוּץ — לֹא שְׁמָהּ בְּדִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** according **to Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda, the first** ruling was **due to abrogation and the latter** ruling was also **due to abrogation.** If so, **what is** the difference **between** the reasoning of the initial version of the mishna and the decision of the court that convened after them? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them is** with regard to **superficial investigation,** i.e., the investigation that could have been conducted on his behalf by his female relatives, which could have been only a superficial investigation. One **Sage holds** that **superficial investigation is considered** a valid **investigation,** and therefore once he requested to marry her and the marriage date arrived, there is no concern of a later abrogation, **and** one **Sage holds** that **superficial investigation is not considered** a valid **investigation,** and consequently there is still concern that when he consummates the marriage he will find some blemish on her and abrogate the marriage.

The *gemara* answers: *ika beinaihu bdikat chutz* — the point of departure between the two rulings is the question of whether an external background check — investigating the other party through third parties and acquaintances — constitutes adequate due diligence. One sage held that *bdikat chutz shma bdika*: an external background check is a meaningful investigation, and after twelve months one could reasonably have conducted such an inquiry; we were therefore not concerned that *simpon* would follow. The other sage held that *bdikat chutz lo shma bdika*: external investigation, however thorough, cannot substitute for actually living with a person. True knowledge of a spouse only begins with cohabitation, and therefore twelve months of external investigation does not suffice to allay the *simpon* concern. Only once they have reached the stage of *chupah* — when they are on the verge of actual life together — can we say the background check has been meaningful enough.

This also explains, incidentally, why a post-discovery *simpon* claim is not defeated simply by the passage of time. The relevant question is not when the couple married but when the defect was discovered. If a husband concealed something and it came to light only years later, the *mekach ta'ut* analysis runs from the moment of discovery, not from the date of *kiddushin*. Where the husband himself was aware of the defect and concealed it, *savar v'kibel* — the principle that one who knew and said nothing implicitly accepted the condition — would not apply against the wife.

## Sanctify Labor

### Mishna Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּקְדִּישׁ מַעֲשֵׂה יְדֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ — הֲרֵי זוֹ עוֹשָׂה וְאוֹכֶלֶת. הַמּוֹתָר, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: הֶקְדֵּשׁ, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הַסַּנְדְּלָר אוֹמֵר: חוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one consecrates his wife's earnings,** meaning anything she produces, such as thread that she spins from wool, which, according to the Sages' ordinance, belongs to her husband, **she may work and sustain** herself from her earnings, as the consecration is ineffective. However, there is a dispute with regard to **the surplus,** meaning any earnings she produces in excess of the amount she is required to produce for her husband. **Rabbi Meir says:** The surplus is **consecrated** property, and **Rabbi Yoḥanan the Cobbler says:** The surplus is also **non-sacred.**

The *gemara* now transitions to a new *mishnah*: *hamakdish ma'aseh yedei ishto* — a man declares that his wife's labor is *hekdesh*, consecrated to *hakodesh*. The *mishnah* rules: *harei zo osah v'ochelet* — she goes ahead and does her work, and eats. The consecration is ineffective. The *gemara* will now explain why.

To understand what is at stake, one must appreciate the underlying structure of the *ketubah* arrangement. The husband is obligated to provide *mezonot* — to cover his wife's food expenses. In exchange, he is entitled to *maaseh yadeha* — the proceeds of her labor. This reciprocal arrangement, however, raises the question of who is the fundamental rights-holder: is the entitlement to *mezonot* the primary right that generates the wife's obligation to hand over her earnings, or is the husband's entitlement to her labor primary, with *mezonot* established as the corresponding counterbalance?

Additionally, one must note the *mishnah*'s later teaching about *motar* — the surplus that a wife produces beyond the minimum defined work expectation. The *mishnah* will state that Rabbi Meir holds that the husband can effectively consecrate the *motar*, while Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar holds that it remains *chulin*. It is striking that the extra amount — the *motar* — is more susceptible to sanctification in Rabbi Meir's view than the core amount. The *gemara* will work out why.

The minimum expectation of *maaseh yadeha*, it should be noted, does not require the wife to hold an outside job. The later *mishnah* will spell out that work performed within the home — including household tasks and child-rearing — satisfies the obligation. There is a defined minimum output, but it is not necessarily commercial in nature.

### Rav: Woman's Right

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: יְכוֹלָה אִשָּׁה לוֹמַר לְבַעְלָהּ ״אֵינִי נִיזּוֹנֶת וְאֵינִי עוֹשָׂה״. קָסָבַר: כִּי תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן מְזוֹנֵי — עִיקָּר, וּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם אֵיבָה. וְכִי אָמְרָה ״אֵינִי נִיזּוֹנֶת וְאֵינִי עוֹשָׂה״ — הָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: A woman may say to her husband: I will not be sustained** by you and, in turn, **I will not work,** i.e., you will not keep my earnings. **He holds** that **when the Sages instituted** the various obligations and rights of a husband and wife, the husband's obligation to provide for the wife's **sustenance** was **the primary** one, **and** they then decreed that **her earnings** belong to him in return, **due to** concern about **animosity.** If he would be obligated to provide for her sustenance but she would be allowed to work and keep her earnings, he would resent her. Since her right to sustenance is the primary one, **if she says: I will not be sustained** by you and, in turn, **I will not work,** i.e., you will not keep my earnings, **she has permission to** do so. As the arrangement was established for her benefit, she may cancel it if it is not suitable for her.

Rav Huna says in the name of Rav: *yechola isha lomar le'ba'ala, eini nizonet v'eini osa* — a woman may say to her husband, "You need not pay my food bill, and I will not hand over the proceeds of my labor." She may, in other words, opt out of the entire reciprocal arrangement, retain her own earnings, and forgo the *mezonot* support.

This is a statement of considerable significance for how we understand the *ketubah* framework. If the husband were the primary rights-holder — the one whose entitlement to *maaseh yadeha* was the bedrock — one would expect that it is he who determines the terms and she who must comply. But Rav frames it the opposite way: she has the power to reverse the arrangement unilaterally. This strongly suggests that the *ketubah* is fundamentally constructed for her benefit, and its terms are hers to waive.

This principle has had practical legal application. In Israel, before the *Knesset* legislated in this area, organizations concerned with women's rights in the *beit din* system would arrange for the *ketubah* to include a rider — effectively an exercise of the woman's *eini nizonet v'eini osa* right — so that both spouses could then enter into a separate arrangement of shared or independently held financial interests on terms of their own choosing.

An important implication follows: since she retains the right to opt out at any time, she can equally reverse course at any time. If she loses employment and wishes to return to the *ketubah* arrangement, she may do so — because the entire framework exists in her interest, and she may invoke or waive it as her circumstances change.

### Mezonot Primary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: תִּקְּנוּ מְזוֹנוֹת תַּחַת מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ! אֵימָא: תִּקְּנוּ מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ תַּחַת מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **They instituted** the husband's responsibility for her **sustenance in exchange for** his right to **her earnings.** This indicates that the primary enactment is the husband's right to his wife's earnings, and the ordinance that requires him to provide her with sustenance comes as a result. According to this, the wife would not be allowed to waive the arrangement, contrary to Rav Huna's statement. The Gemara responds: Emend the text of the *baraita* and **say** instead: **They instituted her earnings in exchange for** the husband's responsibility for her **sustenance.**

The *gemara* explains the theoretical basis for Rav's ruling: *ki takinu rabanan mezoni, mezoni ikar* — when the rabbis established the *ketubah* arrangement, *mezonot* — the obligation to provide for her sustenance — was the primary *takana*. *U'maaseh yadeha mishum eiva* — her corresponding obligation to hand over her earnings was added only secondarily, to prevent resentment (*eiva*) from arising if he were supporting her without receiving anything in return. Since *mezonot* is primary and its purpose is her protection, she is the one who holds the power: *v'chi amra eini nizonet v'eini osa, ha'reshut b'yadah* — if she says she neither wants the sustenance nor will hand over her labor, the right is hers.

### Woman Owns Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ מַעֲשֵׂה יְדֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ — הֲרֵי הִיא עוֹשָׂה וְאוֹכֶלֶת. מַאי לָאו, בְּנִיזּוֹנֶת? לָא, בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ נִיזּוֹנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **Let us say that** the mishna **supports** Rav Huna's opinion, as it is taught: **If one consecrates his wife's earnings, she may work and sustain** herself from her earnings. **What, is it not** discussing a woman **who is sustained** by her husband, meaning that he is willing to sustain her, although she relinquishes her right to sustenance in accordance with Rav Huna's principle? Therefore, her earnings do not belong to him to consecrate. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is discussing a woman **who is not sustained** by her husband, as he does not have sufficient funds to sustain her. Consequently, there is no proof with regard to Rav Huna's statement.

The *gemara* attempts to draw support from the *mishnah* itself: *mai lav bimzonot* — the case of *hamakdish maaseh yedei ishto* must be a case where he is in fact paying the food bills, and even so, the consecration fails. Why? Because even though he is currently providing *mezonot* and receiving her labor, she retains the power to say no at any moment. Since she can at any time withdraw from the arrangement, she remains the true owner of her labor — and one cannot sanctify what one does not own. Even before she has exercised her right to withhold, the ongoing possibility of her doing so means that the earnings are fundamentally hers, not his.

The *gemara* deflects this proof: *lav bimzonot* — no, the *mishnah* may be speaking of a case where he is *not* paying the food bills. Since he is not fulfilling his *mezonot* obligation, she is under no obligation to hand over her earnings, and therefore it is entirely unsurprising that he cannot consecrate them.

### Resh Lakish View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לָא תֵּימָא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר אָדָם מַקְדִּישׁ דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם. אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְכוּפָּהּ לְמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ — נַעֲשָׂה כְּאוֹמֵר לָהּ ״יִקְדְּשׁוּ יָדַיִךְ לְעוֹשֵׂיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This opinion of Rav Huna **disputes** that **of Reish Lakish, as Reish Lakish said: Do not say** that **the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** is that **since he maintains** that **a person may consecrate an object that has not** yet **come into the world,** the consecration can take effect even on her future earnings. **Rather,** say that **the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** is that **since he can compel her** to produce **her earnings** for him, it is as if he had a certain legal claim to the products of her hands, i.e., her earnings. Consequently, **he is considered as if he had said to her: Your hands are consecrated to the One Who made them,** and the consecration can therefore take effect on something that already exists. Since Reish Lakish said that he may compel her to produce earnings for him, the implication is that she may not say: I will not be sustained and I will not work.

Resh Lakish disputes the entire framework of Rav Huna citing Rav. Resh Lakish holds: *lo teima ta'amei de-Rabbi Meir mishum dekasavar adam makdish davar shelo ba l'olam* — do not say that Rabbi Meir permits sanctification of the *motar* because he holds, characteristically, that one may sanctify things that have not yet come into existence. Rather, says Resh Lakish, the reason is different and the underlying model is entirely different: *mi-tokh she-yakhol l'kofah l'maaseh yadeha* — since the husband can *compel* his wife to work, *na'aseh ke-omer lah yikdeshu yadekh l'oseihem* — it is as though he said, "Let your hands be consecrated to their Maker."

The implication is fundamental. In Resh Lakish's model, the husband is the *ba'al habayit* — he is in control. The wife *cannot* say no. Because he can coerce her labor, he has a present, real monetary ownership in her labor capacity — not merely in future earnings that do not yet exist, but in the very ownership interest in her hands, right now. When he declares *hekdesh*, he is not sanctifying something that does not exist (*davar shelo ba l'olam*); he is sanctifying a present ownership right that he already holds.

This stands in direct opposition to Rav's view. For Rav, she is the *ba'alat habayit*; for Resh Lakish, he is the *ba'al habayit*. For Rav, she can say no; for Resh Lakish, she cannot. And because Resh Lakish holds that the husband has a genuine present ownership in her labor, the act of *hekdesh* is legally coherent — it sanctifies something he truly owns right now.

### R. Meir: Motar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לָא אֲמַר לַהּ הָכִי! כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר: אֵין אָדָם מוֹצִיא דְּבָרָיו לְבַטָּלָה — נַעֲשֶׂה כְּאוֹמֵר לָהּ ״יִקְדְּשׁוּ יָדַיִךְ לְעוֹשֵׂיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Reish Lakish's statement, the Gemara asks: **But he did not say this to her;** rather, he said that he was consecrating her earnings. The Gemara answers: **Since we heard** that **Rabbi Meir said: A person does not say things for naught,** and according to this principle, when one says something that has no halakhic meaning, it is interpreted as if he had said something that does have halakhic relevance, **he is considered as if he had said to her: Your hands are consecrated to the One Who made them.**

This still leaves open the question raised by the *mishnah* itself: why can Rabbi Meir consecrate the *motar* — the surplus — but not the core *maaseh yadeha*? The *gemara* explains the structure this way: the core amount of *maaseh yadeha* is tied to the *mezonot* arrangement. He is obligated to feed her; her obligation to produce the minimum is the quid pro quo. His right to the core amount is entirely contingent on his fulfilling the *mezonot* obligation, and that contingency makes it insufficiently "his" to consecrate with full legal effect. But the *motar* — what she produces beyond that minimum — is tied not to *mezonot* but to the pin money (*ma'ah sela*) he gives her as discretionary spending. That amount is genuinely and independently his — it is not conditioned on the primary *mezonot* exchange in the same way — and therefore he can consecrate it.

### Future Things

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר אֵין אָדָם מַקְדִּישׁ דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם? וְהָתַנְיָא, הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי לְאַחַר שֶׁאֶתְגַּיֵּיר״, אוֹ: ״לְאַחַר שֶׁתִּתְגַּיְּירִי״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁאֶשְׁתַּחְרֵר״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּחְרְרִי״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁיָּמוּת בַּעְלִיךְ״, אוֹ ״שֶׁתָּמוּת אֲחוֹתִיךְ״, אוֹ: ״לְאַחַר שֶׁיַּחְלוֹץ לִיךְ יְבָמִיךְ״, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does Rabbi Meir hold** that **a person may not consecrate an object that has not** yet **come into the world? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if a gentile **says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me after I convert; or** if she was a gentile and he said to her: You are hereby betrothed to me **after you convert;** or if he was a slave and said to her: **After I am emancipated;** or if she was a maidservant and he said to her: **After you are emancipated;** or if she was married and he said to her: **After your husband dies; or** if he was married to her sister and he said: **After your sister dies,** as at that point the betrothal could take effect; **or** if she was a widow waiting for her *yavam* and he said to her: **After your *yavam* performs *ḥalitza* with you, Rabbi Meir says:** If any of these cases occurred, **she is betrothed.** Apparently Rabbi Meir maintains that betrothal can take effect even on something that has not yet come into being.

The *gemara* then notes that Resh Lakish's explanation — that Rabbi Meir sanctifies his present ownership in her hands rather than future earnings — carries an implicit assumption: that Rabbi Meir holds one cannot sanctify *davar shelo ba l'olam*. But this is surprising, because Rabbi Meir is elsewhere among the *tannaim* who are most permissive on this very issue.

The *gemara* proves the point from a *baraita*: *ha-omer le-ishah harei at mekudeshet li le-akhar she-etgayer* — if a man says to a woman, "You are betrothed to me after I convert," or "after you convert," or "after I am freed from slavery," or "after you are freed from slavery," or "after your husband dies," or "after your sister dies," or "after your *yavam* performs *chalitzah* for you" — *Rabbi Meir omer mekudeshet* — Rabbi Meir holds that such a conditional *kiddushin* is valid. This is structurally analogous to *davar shelo ba l'olam*, and Rabbi Meir accepts it.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהַהִיא — אִין, מֵהָא לֵיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Actually, **from that** *baraita*, **one can** learn that this is Rabbi Meir's opinion, but Reish Lakish merely wanted to say that **no** inference is **to be learned from this** mishna, as it is possible to explain Rabbi Meir's words in another way, based on the principle that a person does not say things for naught.

The *gemara* resolves: *me-hahu in* — yes, from that *baraita* one can prove that Rabbi Meir holds one may sanctify things that do not yet exist, and that could theoretically be the explanation for the *mishnah* as well. *Me-ha leika le-mishma minnah* — but from our *mishnah* alone, one cannot draw that conclusion. The *mishnah* is equally explicable on Resh Lakish's model — that Rabbi Meir is sanctifying a present ownership right in the wife's hands, rather than future produce. The *mishnah* therefore remains ambiguous between the two positions, and the deeper dispute — whether she is the *ba'alat habayit* who can say no, or he is the *ba'al habayit* who can compel — remains the animating question underlying the entire chapter.
