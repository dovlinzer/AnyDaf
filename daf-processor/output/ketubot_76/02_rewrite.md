# Ketubot 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kan Nimtzu Kan Hayu

### Mishnah Overview

The sugya opens with a mishnah that frames a fundamental question: a husband stipulated that his wife have no blemishes, and after marriage it is discovered that she does. The critical issue is whether those blemishes pre-dated the *kiddushin*—the giving of the ring—or emerged only afterward. The stakes are significant. If the blemishes pre-existed the *kiddushin*, the condition was never satisfied, the marriage never took effect, and there is no *ketubah*. If they appeared after the *kiddushin*, the marriage is valid, a *get* is required for dissolution, and she is entitled to her *ketubah*.

The mishnah resolves the question by reference to location of discovery. If the blemishes were found while she was still in her father's house—before *nissuin* and *chuppah*—the burden of proof falls on her (or her father) to demonstrate that the blemishes arose only after the *kiddushin*. If they were found only after she entered the husband's domain following *chuppah*, the burden of proof falls on him to demonstrate that the blemishes pre-dated the *kiddushin*. The first answer the Gemara offered to explain this distinction was that the mishnah is really two separate opinions stitched together—a rather unsatisfying resolution. The Gemara now provides a more substantive explanation.

### Rava's Answer

Rava proposes the principle of *kan nimtze'u kan hayu*—where they were found, there they occurred. The logic is as follows: if the blemishes were discovered while she was in her father's house, we presume they existed from before, as a longstanding feature of her body during her years growing up in that household. The burden of proof therefore falls on her to demonstrate otherwise. Conversely, if the blemishes were discovered only after she entered the husband's domain, we presume they arose there—that is, after the *kiddushin*. It then becomes the husband's burden to prove they pre-existed. The presumption tracks the location of discovery, and whichever stage she was in at the time of discovery determines where we assign the origin of the problem.

### Abaye's Question

Abaye raises a pointed challenge to Rava's principle. The mishnah states that when the husband bears the burden of proof, he must demonstrate that the blemishes existed from *before* the *kiddushin*—*ad she'lo titars*—and it is not sufficient to show merely that they existed at some point *after* the *kiddushin* but while she was still in the father's house. Abaye asks: why not? If Rava's principle of *kan nimtze'u kan hayu* operates symmetrically, then once the husband proves that the blemishes were observed while she was still in her father's house—even after the *kiddushin*—we should apply the same presumption and conclude that since they were found there, they were always there, pushing their origin back to before the *kiddushin*. Why does the husband need to prove directly that they pre-dated the *kiddushin*? Should it not suffice to demonstrate discovery in the father's house and let *kan nimtze'u kan hayu* do the rest?

To illustrate this with a timeline: there is the period in her father's house before *kiddushin*, then the *kiddushin* itself, then the continued period in her father's house post-*kiddushin*, and finally the *chuppah* and entry into the husband's domain. If the *mumim* were seen at any point in her father's house, even in the post-*kiddushin* stage, why can't the husband rely on *kan nimtze'u kan hayu* to extend that discovery back to a pre-*kiddushin* origin?

Rava responds: *mishum de'ika lemeimar chazakah ein adam shoteh be'kos ella im ken bodko*—because there is a countervailing presumption that a person does not drink from the cup without first inspecting it. A man does not proceed to *chuppah* without examining what he is getting into. If the husband can prove the blemishes were discoverable in the father's house during the post-*kiddushin* period, the logical response is: if it was discoverable, we presume he discovered it, and if he then proceeded to the *chuppah*, he must have been acceptable with it. The act of proceeding to *chuppah* thus neutralizes the force of *kan nimtze'u kan hayu* in that direction. It is no longer enough to show the blemish existed in that intermediate period; he would have known about it, and his decision to proceed to *chuppah* signals his acceptance.

### Presume Prior Knowledge

But then Abaye presses further: if going through with the *chuppah* shows he was accepting even of something discovered in the father's house post-*kiddushin*, why does it not equally signal acceptance of blemishes discovered before the *kiddushin*? After all, he went through with the *chuppah* in that case too, and if it was discoverable, we presume he saw it. Why does proof of a pre-*kiddushin* blemish enable him to void the marriage when proof of a post-*kiddushin* blemish discovered in the father's house does not?

The Gemara responds that the two cases are parallel: *ela mishenitarsah*—the same logic applies from the moment of betrothal. If the blemish was demonstrably pre-existing, the *chuppah* does not forfeit his claim, because the *ein adam shoteh be'kos* presumption operates equally in the later stage. The question then becomes: why does it specifically matter where the blemishes are found, and what happens to the competing presumptions once *chuppah* has taken place? The Gemara makes explicit that after *nissuin*, there are *two* considerations operating in her favor, and the resolution turns on how many presumptions each side can muster.

## Competing Presumptions

### Two Presumptions

When the blemishes are discovered after *chuppah*—in the husband's domain—and he can demonstrate they were visible in the father's house post-*kiddushin*, there are two things that favor the woman. The first is *chazakat ha'guf*—the presumption that her body was in its normal state, meaning she was not born with these blemishes, and therefore we presume they arose as late as possible. This presumption reinforces the claim that they emerged only after the *kiddushin*. The second is the *chuppah* itself: *ein adam shoteh be'kos ella im ken bodko*—the very fact that he went through with the *chuppah* suggests he examined her and found it acceptable. Together, these two arguments favor her position: the blemish probably came late, and even if it was visible, he was presumably accepting.

### Counterargument

Against these two arguments, the husband can deploy one counter-presumption: *chazakah ein adam mitpayes be'mumim*—a man does not simply make peace with blemishes, especially when he had explicitly stipulated against them. This counterargument directly neutralizes the *chuppah* presumption. He went through the *chuppah*, yes, but that does not necessarily mean he was aware of or accepting toward the blemish, since the presumption runs the other way: a man in his position would not be so cavalier about something he stipulated against.

### Neutralizing Arguments

The Gemara's resolution is elegant: the *chuppah* presumption and the *ein adam mitpayes be'mumim* counter-presumption cancel each other out—*chad be'makom tarti lo amrinan*, one argument does not prevail against two. When those two are neutralized, what remains is *chazakat ha'guf*, which operates uncontested in her favor, presuming the blemish arose as late as possible and thus after the *kiddushin*. That is why when the blemishes are found in the husband's domain, even if he proves they were visible in the father's house post-*kiddushin*, he still cannot win: her *chazakat ha'guf* stands undisturbed.

Conversely, if he proves the blemish pre-dated the *kiddushin*, the *chazakat ha'guf* can no longer help her—he has directly established that the blemish was there from before. At that point, the only argument in her favor would be the *chuppah* presumption, but that is neutralized by *ein adam mitpayes be'mumim*. And since money cannot be extracted without preponderant proof—*ve'oki mamona achezkatei*—he wins and the marriage is voided.

This, then, is the logic: when the blemishes are found post-*chuppah*, she wins unless he proves pre-*kiddushin* origin, because she always has *chazakat ha'guf* on her side unless it is directly dislodged by evidence. The *chuppah* dimension neither helps nor hurts in itself, since the competing *chazakot* neutralize each other.

### Tosafot's Framework

Tosafot raises a question: we have now essentially neutralized the *chuppah* presumption through the counter-argument. But the original question was: why doesn't *kan nimtze'u kan hayu* by itself determine the result, as it does when the blemish is found in the father's house before *chuppah*? There, even the *chazakat ha'guf* that would favor the woman is apparently overridden—we simply say, found in the father's house, always was there. Why does *chazakat ha'guf* prevail post-*chuppah* but not pre-*chuppah*?

Tosafot's answer introduces a powerful logical framework. In the case where *chuppah* has taken place, in order for the husband to win, two conditions must simultaneously be true: first, that going through the *chuppah* did not constitute acceptance—*ein adam mitpayes be'mumim*; and second, that the blemish in fact pre-dated the *kiddushin*—*kan nimtze'u kan hayu*. Call these condition A and condition B. Both A *and* B must hold for him to prevail.

For the woman to win, however, only one of the two needs to fail. Either: even if the blemish existed earlier, the *chuppah* shows he was accepting (not-A), or: the blemish did not pre-date the *kiddushin* (not-B). Since she needs only *one* of these to be true, while he needs *both*, the weight of the situation favors her. Tosafot states this explicitly: *afilu lo yehei ella echad maihem emes*—even if only one of these arguments is true, she wins and collects the *ketubah*. For him to escape liability, *both* must be true—and if even one is false, her claim prevails.

This explains the asymmetry. Before *chuppah*, there is no factor A in play at all. The only operative question is whether the blemish pre-dated the *kiddushin*, and *kan nimtze'u kan hayu* determines that presumptively in his favor. But once *chuppah* has occurred, a second dimension enters the picture, and the logical structure changes: he now carries the burden of satisfying two conditions simultaneously, while she wins if either condition is unsatisfied. The practical implication for burden of proof is clear: after *chuppah*, the entire weight of proof shifts to him, and proving only that the blemish existed post-*kiddushin* but pre-*chuppah* is insufficient—he must reach all the way back to pre-*kiddushin* to win.

One might ask: what does it mean in practice to prove that a man was or was not accepting of a blemish? The answer is that these are not individually provable facts so much as legal presumptions that shift the burden of proof. The force of *ein adam shoteh be'kos* is not that each case turns on a factual inquiry into the man's subjective state; rather, it means that having gone through with the *chuppah*, the legal burden on him is now heavier. He cannot simply point to a discoverable blemish and call the marriage void. He must establish in more absolute terms—pushing back to before the *kiddushin*—that the foundational condition was never satisfied. As soon as *chuppah* has occurred, that additional burden attaches, and without meeting it, the marriage stands and the *ketubah* is owed. Whether or not he had explicitly made a *tenai*, if he cannot satisfy both conditions, she is entitled to a *get* and her *ketubah*.

## Rav Ashi's Answer

### Father's Claim

Rav Ashi offers an entirely different explanation for the mishnah's distinction. When the blemishes are discovered while she is still in the father's house, this implies she is still in the legal status of a *ketanah* or *na'arah*—conceptually under her father's jurisdiction. In such a case, if the husband performed *kiddushin* and then *gerushin* without *nissuin*, who is entitled to the *ketubah*? The father. So the argument being made in court—that the blemish arose only after the *kiddushin*—is being made by the woman on her own firsthand knowledge of her body, but the beneficiary of the claim is the father, who lacks that firsthand knowledge. This weakens the claim. The woman knows when the blemish arose; the father does not. When the person with direct knowledge makes a claim on behalf of someone without that knowledge, it occupies a weaker legal footing. Therefore she bears the burden of proof: *mana l'abba biyadeich*—"you owe my father a hundred."

### Woman's Claim

After *nissuin*, the woman is her own legal person and asserts the claim entirely in her own right: *mana li biyadeich*—"you owe me a hundred." She is the one who experienced the blemish, she is the one with the information, and she is the one collecting. This places her in a stronger position, and accordingly the burden of proof shifts to the husband.

### Braita Challenge

Rav Acha the son of Rav Avya challenges Rav Ashi from a *baraita*: Rabbi Meir concedes that when the blemishes are of the type that could naturally have accompanied her from her father's house—*ha-ra'uyin lavo immah mibeit aviha*—even if they are found after she has entered the husband's domain, the burden of proof falls on the father. But according to Rav Ashi's explanation, once she is in the husband's house she is her own claimant and should be in the stronger position. Why would the father bear the burden even in that case?

### Congenital Blemishes

The Gemara resolves this by explaining that the *baraita* is referring to congenital blemishes—*yateret*, an extra finger or similar feature present from birth. If the blemish is congenital, the question of whose house it was "discovered in" is irrelevant: it obviously pre-dated the *kiddushin*. The only remaining question is whether the husband knew about it and was accepting. The "proof" the father or bride would bring in such a case is not to show when the blemish arose—that is not in dispute—but to demonstrate that the husband had been shown the blemish and consented, perhaps with witnesses present. This, then, is an entirely different category, and the *baraita* does not undermine Rav Ashi's position. The general framework of the mishnah applies to blemishes that are not congenital—those whose timing of appearance is genuinely uncertain—and the location of discovery determines where the burden of proof falls.

## Donkey & Cow Case

### Shmuel's Case

The Gemara now introduces an analogous case from the world of commerce to test and apply these principles. Rav Yehuda quotes Shmuel: if someone sells his cow in exchange for a donkey, and the buyer takes possession of the cow (*mashach*—which completes the transaction), and then the two go to collect the donkey only to find it dead—*al ba'al ha'chamor lehavi re'ayah she'hayah chamoro kayam bi'sha'at meshichat parah*—the owner of the donkey bears the burden of proof that his donkey was still alive at the moment the cow was taken. He must demonstrate that the transaction was valid and that the death occurred afterward.

What makes this striking is that at least three considerations would seem to favor the donkey-owner. First, there is a presumption that the transaction was a valid sale. Second, he has physical possession of the cow. Third, *chazakat ha'guf* would seem to apply: we should presume the donkey died as late as possible, meaning after the transaction was finalized. And yet, despite all this, the burden of proof rests on him.

### Comparison

The Gemara notes that this appears to align with the mishnah's case of the *kallah*, and attempts to identify which part of the mishnah provides the parallel. The case where the blemish is found in the father's house might seem to match—in both cases the claimant has the burden—but the comparison does not hold: in the mishnah, the party bearing the burden is the one trying to extract money, whereas in Shmuel's case, the donkey-owner bears the burden to *keep* what he already has. The Gemara then considers the second half of the mishnah, where it is the husband who bears the burden even though he holds the money. Rav Chanina objects that even this comparison is imperfect: there, the husband bears the burden because *chazakat ha'guf* operates in the woman's favor—a countervailing presumption offsets his possession. Here, the donkey-owner not only has the cow in hand but also has the *chazakah* that the donkey died late. He has both the possession and the favorable presumption, yet still bears the burden.

### Burden Analysis

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this by pointing to the first half of the mishnah after all—the *kallah* found with a blemish in the father's house. There too, she has *chazakat ha'guf* in her favor, and she still bears the burden of proof. Why? Because what she is really trying to protect is the *ring*—the object given over for *kiddushin*. The ring is in her possession, and she has a *chazakah* on her side, yet she must prove her case. The reason is that her possession of the ring is inherently contingent: the ring was given to her only on the assumption that the transaction—the *kiddushin*—was valid. Since the validity of that transaction is precisely what is in question, she cannot be considered to be in full undisturbed possession of the ring in the ordinary legal sense. Her claim to the ring is only as strong as her ability to establish that the *kiddushin* took hold.

The parallel to the donkey-and-cow case is therefore apt: the donkey-owner took the cow on the assumption that the exchange was valid. Since the validity of the exchange is the very thing in dispute—did the donkey die before or after the transaction was finalized?—he cannot stand on his possession of the cow as though it were unencumbered. His possession is conditional on the transaction having been good, and proving the transaction was good is his burden. This is, structurally, the same as the *kallah* keeping the ring: possession of something received conditionally does not relieve one of the burden of establishing the condition was met.

### Ring Conditional

The Gemara further explores whether the *kiddushin* ring must be returned if the transaction is voided. There is a broader dispute in the Gemara about whether *kiddushin* money is given *le'tivu'a*—"to be sunk," meaning the woman keeps it regardless of what follows—or not. One might think that even if the *kiddushin* is voided, if the money was given *le'tivu'a*, she keeps the ring. The Gemara responds: that principle applies only to valid *kiddushin* that subsequently falls apart—for instance, if the husband dies before *chuppah*. There the *kiddushin* was real, the ring was given for good, and even misfortune afterward does not undo her acquisition. But here the claim is *kiddushei ta'ut*—the *kiddushin* was never valid in the first place because the condition was not met. There was never a real *kiddushin* to which the *le'tivu'a* logic could apply. If she can prove the *kiddushin* was valid—that the blemish arose only afterward—she keeps the ring and the *ketubah*. If she cannot, there was no *kiddushin* and the ring reverts. This makes clear that the act of *chuppah* and *nissuin* are treated as legally distinct from *kiddushin*, a point the case underscores throughout.

## Shmuel's True Position

### Correction

Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta arrives with a correction: do not rely on the principles transmitted by Yehuda—his brother—in Shmuel's name. Those principles misrepresent Shmuel's actual position. *Lo tetzi'tu lehu lehani klalei dechayil Yehuda achi mishmeih di-Shmuel*—do not follow those general rules that Yehuda my brother formulated in Shmuel's name.

### True Principle

What Shmuel actually said is this: *kol she'nolad safek birshuto, alav ha'ra'ayah*—whoever's domain the doubt arose in, that party bears the burden of proof. The principle is not about who wants to extract money from whom; it is about where the uncertain event—the death of the donkey, the appearance of the blemish—is presumed to have occurred. And this maps directly onto the mishnah of the *kallah*: when the *mum* is found in the father's domain, the problem is presumed to have arisen there, and she bears the burden of proof. When it is found in the husband's domain, the problem is presumed to have arisen there, and he bears the burden. Shmuel's principle and the mishnah's structure are perfectly aligned.

### Needle Case

The Gemara brings the case of a needle found embedded in the thick folds of *beit ha'kosot*—one of the animal's stomachs. If the puncture exits both sides, the animal is a *treifah*; if only one side, it is *kasherah*. If there is a clot of dried blood at the wound—indicating it formed before *shechitah*—the animal is a *treifah*. If there is no such blood clot, the puncture is assumed to have occurred after *shechitah* and the animal is acceptable. A scab forming over the wound takes three days. So if the animal was sold to the butcher (*shocheit*) within the last two days, and after *shechitah* a scab is found, we know the injury pre-dated the sale—and the seller owes the butcher. But if there is no scab and the timing is ambiguous—it could have occurred before or after the sale—who bears the burden of proof?

The *baraita* states: *ha'motzi mei'chaveiro alav ha'ra'ayah*—whoever wishes to extract money from the other bears the burden of proof. This seems to say that if the butcher wants his money back, he must prove the animal was already injured at the time of sale. Yet Shmuel's principle says the burden follows where the doubt arose—and the doubt arose in the butcher's domain, since the animal was in his possession when slaughtered. These appear to contradict.

### Payment Presumption

The Gemara resolves this: *depara damei*—the *baraita* is speaking in the case where the butcher has already paid. In that situation, saying "whoever wants to extract the money bears the burden" simply means the butcher always bears the burden, since he is the one seeking a refund. This is consistent with Shmuel's principle: the problem arose in the butcher's domain, the butcher bears the burden. The formulation "whoever wants to extract money" is not an independent principle; it is a description of the butcher, premised on the assumption—*stama demilsa*—that ordinarily a seller does not hand over his cow without first receiving payment. The normal case is that the butcher has already paid, and so the *baraita*'s language, read in context, always describes the butcher as the one who would be extracting money if he seeks redress.

One might object: why not state the rule directly—"the butcher bears the burden"—instead of using the roundabout formulation about extracting money? The Gemara concedes the force of this question (*u'mai piska*—what makes you read it so categorically?) and answers with *stama demilsa*: it is the ordinary presumption of commercial transactions that payment precedes delivery, so the *baraita*'s language presupposes the butcher paid and simply follows from that assumption.

### Two Approaches

In the end, this sugya surfaces two distinct approaches to the allocation of the burden of proof, and the tension between them runs throughout. The first and more fundamental approach—which Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel identifies as Shmuel's true position—is that the burden follows the location of the doubt: wherever the problem is presumed to have arisen, that party bears the burden of proof. This is *safek birshuto*, and it maps cleanly onto the mishnah's structure for blemishes discovered in the father's house versus the husband's house.

The second approach, which surfaced from the *treifah* case and initially seemed to represent Shmuel's view, is the more familiar rule of *ha'motzi mei'chaveiro alav ha'ra'ayah*: whoever seeks to extract money from another bears the burden of proof. This principle is not wrong—it is a standard rule of monetary law—but it is not the operative logic in these cases. The Gemara works to show that the *baraita* about the needle, properly read, does not rest on this principle independently; it is merely describing a situation where the butcher, who already paid, happens to be the one seeking to extract. The underlying logic remains: the problem arose in his domain, and so the burden is his.

These two approaches—location of the problem versus direction of money extraction—stand as the two frameworks through which burden of proof is allocated when the timing of a disqualifying event is genuinely uncertain.
