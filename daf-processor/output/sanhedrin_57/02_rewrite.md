# Sanhedrin 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Classic Seven Mitzvot

### Overview & Context

Daf 57 continues the extended discussion of *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*. The sugya begins approximately nine lines from the bottom of 56b, at the words *hai Tana d'bei Menashe hu*. By way of orientation, the classic seven commandments are: the three cardinal prohibitions — *avodah zarah*, *giluy arayos*, and *shfichus damim* — together with *ever min hachai*, *birchat Hashem*, *gezel*, and *dinim*. All seven are initially derived from *pesukim* concerning Adam rather than being limited to the covenant with Noach.

The preceding discussion had already touched on several major themes. First, *dinim* was explored at some length. The Rambam understands *dinim* as requiring the establishment of a court system — courts in every city and region — while the Ramban understands it more broadly as substantive law, rooted in the *pasuk* concerning Avraham: *l'maan asher yetzaveh et banav v'et beito acharav v'shamru derech Hashem la'asot tzedakah u'mishpat*, to do righteousness and justice. The *gemara* had further explored whether *knassos* — fines — are included within *dinim*. According to Rashi, *knassos* go beyond mere compensation and the righting of wrongs; they are punitive rather than restorative, which would arguably place them within the broader *tzedakah u'mishpat* framework. The conclusion of that discussion is that *dinim* encompasses substantive civil law oriented toward creating a just society.

A second theme that was established and will recur throughout the daf is the procedural distinction between how a Jewish court adjudicates and how a non-Jewish court adjudicates *sheva mitzvos*. This can be distilled into three contrasts: *edah, eidim, v'hasra'ah* — the size of the court, the requirement for witnesses, and the requirement for warning. With respect to the court: for Jews, even capital cases require a *beit din* of 23; a non-Jewish court may consist of even a single judge. With respect to witnesses: Jews require two valid witnesses; non-Jews may rely on circumstantial evidence or hearsay. With respect to warning: Jews require *hasra'ah*, prior warning to the offender, while non-Jews may be adjudicated without any such warning.

### Tana D'Bei Menashe

The *gemara* now identifies the opinion that *dinim* was added to the Israelites only at Marah as belonging to *Tana d'bei Menashe* — the school of Menashe — precisely because that school substitutes *dinim* and *birchat Hashem* for two other commandments. The *gemara* states: *d'mappik dach v'ayil sach* — it removes *dalet-ches* (*dinim* and *birchat Hashem*) and inserts *samech-kaf* (*sirus* and *kilayim*).

The formulation of *Tana d'bei Menashe* is thus: *sheva mitzvos nitztavu Bnei Noach* — the seven commandments given to the descendants of Noach are *avodah zarah*, *giluy arayos*, *shfichus damim*, *gezel*, and *ever min hachai*, together with *sirus* (sterilization and castration) and *kilayim* (crossbreeding of animals and grafting of different species). Both of these substitutions are deeply rooted in *Bereishis*: *sirus* connects to *peru u'revu*, the imperative to procreate, and *kilayim* to *l'minehu*, the principle of species distinction established at creation.

### R. Yehuda View

Rabbi Yehuda offers a more minimalist position: *Adam HaRishon lo nitztaveh ela Avodah Zarah bilvad* — the first human being was commanded in nothing other than *avodah zarah* alone. Rashi suggests this need not mean that the remaining commandments were not eventually given; rather, the point is that they should not all be rooted in *Adam HaRishon*. The derivation turns on the verse *vayetzav Hashem Elokim al HaAdam*: the prohibition against eating from the *etz hadaat* does not apply to later generations, but what does carry forward is the fundamental idea embedded in the very fact of divine command — that there is a God who commands. *Vayetzav Hashem Elokim* conveys belief in and acceptance of God, and the rejection of *avodah zarah*, the substitution of any other divinity, flows from that foundational moment. Additional commandments, on this view, came later at the time of Noach.

### R. Yehuda ben Beteira

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira takes a similar approach but broadens it modestly. He adds *birchat Hashem* to the obligations incumbent upon *Adam HaRishon*: if the core of what was demanded at the beginning was the recognition and acceptance of God, then cursing God — attacking the very foundation of that theological relationship — is equally foundational. *Birchat Hashem* is not a separate category but flows directly from the same principle.

*V'yesh omrim af al hadinim* — some add *dinim* as well. The *gemara* then asks: *keman azla ha de'amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav* — like whom does this teaching go? The teaching expounds *vayetzav Hashem Elokim*: *Elohim ani* — know that I am your God; *lo tekaleluni* — do not curse Me; *lo temiruni* — do not replace Me with another deity. What does the third clause — *yehei morai aleichem*, let My fear be upon you — come to include? According to this, *dinim*.

The connection between fear of God and the obligation of *dinim* requires some explanation. Rashi's interpretation is that truly fearing God means judging justly, because the fear of human beings leads to the perversion of justice, while the fear of God ensures honest judgment — *lo tateh mishpat ki hamishpat l'Elohim hu*. However, that reading presupposes the obligation to judge and uses the divine fear merely as a motivator for doing so properly. A more fundamental reading would be: what kind of God are you accepting? The pagan gods are amoral — they represent no absolute moral code. The Jewish conception of God is inseparable from moral truth: *chotamo shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu emet*. If you genuinely accept a God of this character, and if the fear of that God rests upon you, then you understand that society must be structured around justice. The obligation of *dinim* flows from the theological content of the acceptance of God itself.

This connects powerfully to the *pasuk* about Avraham: *v'shamru derech Hashem la'asot tzedakah u'mishpat*. There is something profound in saying that the most basic foundation — prior to Noach, prior to *ever min hachai* or *giluy arayos* — is belief in God and the understanding that this belief entails responsibility for a just society. This resonates, incidentally, with Mendelssohn's idea of natural law.

What unites these positions — Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, and those who add *dinim* — is the recognition that *Adam HaRishon* received only the foundational universal principles: acceptance of God, not cursing God, and perhaps the framework of justice. The more specific commandments — explicit prohibitions of murder, of *ever min hachai*, and so forth — came later, at Noach.

## Sourcing from Noach

### Individual Sources

The *gemara* now turns to *Tana d'bei Menashe*'s approach: since this school substitutes *sirus* and *kilayim* for *birchat Hashem* and *dinim*, how does it derive its list from *pesukim*? The *gemara* poses the dilemma sharply: *ei darish vayetzav* — if *Tana d'bei Menashe* learns the commandments from the *pasuk* about *Adam HaRishon* (*vayetzav Hashem Elokim*), then it should include *birchat Hashem* and *dinim*, which are derived from that very *pasuk*. *Ei lo darish vayetzav* — and if it does not derive anything from that *pasuk*, then where does it derive any of the commandments at all?

The *gemara* answers: *le'olam lo darish vayetzav* — *Tana d'bei Menashe* does not base its system on the *pasuk* by *Adam HaRishon* at all. Instead, *kol chada v'chada b'apei nafsha ketiva* — each commandment is derived individually, and strikingly, all of them are derived from *Noach* rather than from *Adam*. This raises a significant conceptual question: were the *sheva mitzvos* given to *Adam HaRishon*, or to Noach, or in two stages? The difference is not merely historical. Noach came after ten generations of human failure, after the corruption that led to the destruction of the world. One way to understand the trajectory of *Sefer Bereishis* is that humans keep failing, requiring progressively more detailed guidance — first the basic idea of God alone, then the broader Noachide code, and ultimately the 613 commandments given to Israel. *Tana d'bei Menashe* links all seven to the Noachide period rather than to *Adam*.

### Avodah Zarah & Giluy

This section begins at the top of 57a. *Avodah zarah* and *giluy arayos* are derived together from the verse *vatishachet ha'aretz lifnei Ha'Elokim* — the earth was corrupted before God. The school of Rabbi Yishmael teaches: *kol makom shene'emar hashchasa eino ela dvar ervah va'avodas kochavim* — wherever the word *hashchasa* (corruption) appears, it refers to sexual immorality and idolatry. The proof for the association with sexual immorality is: *ki hishchis kol basar es darko al ha'aretz* — all flesh corrupted its way upon the earth. Rashi explains that the word *derech* evokes natural conduct, and *basar* carries a corporeal connotation, together pointing to the corruption of the natural pattern of sexuality — cross-species coupling and similar violations. The association of *hashchasa* with idolatry is proved from the verse *pen tashchisun va'asisem lachem pesel* — lest you act corruptly and fashion for yourselves an idol.

This does not constitute an explicit commandment in the ordinary sense; rather, if people are being punished for these acts, it may be inferred that they were commanded not to do them. The underlying principle is that every punishment presupposes a prior prohibition. This logic, of course, raises a question about Kayin: if the prohibition of murder was only commanded explicitly at the time of Noach — and this is indeed where the *gemara* will derive it — how could Kayin have been held responsible? The *gemara* does not explicitly address this. One resolution, drawing on a broader philosophical framework about the sin of the *etz hadaat*, is that the sin of the Garden of Eden consisted not merely in disobeying God but in arrogating to humanity the authority to determine what is good and evil — displacing divine moral judgment with human moral judgment. If that was the sin, then its aftermath is precisely that humans accepted responsibility for moral determination and are held accountable accordingly, regardless of explicit commandment. *Kayin* cannot claim he was never told murder was wrong, because the acceptance of autonomous moral judgment carries with it full moral accountability. This reading fits with the Rambam-Mendelssohn tension between commanded law and natural law: the sense of the *gemara* here is not that the *sheva mitzvos* are self-evident natural law that humans could have figured out independently, but rather that they are genuine divine commandments — for without commandment, the punishment would be unjust.

### Shfichus Damim

*Shfichus damim* is derived from the explicit divine command to Noach: *shofech dam ha'adam ba'adam damo yishafach* — one who sheds the blood of a person, by a person shall his blood be shed. This is among the few obligations stated expressly in the Noachide context, and it is noteworthy that the source is here rather than the Kayin narrative. The school that roots the prohibition in *Adam HaRishon* would read the Noachide verse differently: *katlei hu d'ka megalei* — it is not establishing the prohibition anew, but revealing the punishment. The prohibition was already known from the time of Adam; the verse by Noach tells us that the punishment is death.

### Gezel Source

*Gezel* is derived from the verse addressed to Noach: *ki yerek esev nasati lachem et hakol* — as the green vegetation I have given you everything. Rav Levi explains: *ki yerek esev v'lo k'yerek gina* — like grass that grows wild, not like the produce of a cultivated garden. Wild vegetation is ownerless; produce of a garden belongs to its owner. The implication is that while God permits the taking of what is ownerless, what belongs to another may not be taken — hence the prohibition of *gezel*.

The school that derives *gezel* from *Adam HaRishon* reads this verse differently: *hu l'mishrei basar hu d'asa* — the verse comes to permit Noach to eat meat. The understanding is that *Adam HaRishon* was permitted only vegetation; the flesh of animals was not given to him. Here, by Noach, meat is for the first time permitted, as God now includes the animals in the category of what may be consumed. On this reading the verse is not about theft at all.

### Ever Min Hachai

The prohibition of *ever min hachai* is derived from the verse: *ach basar b'nafsho damo lo socheilu* — but flesh with its life, its blood, you shall not eat. The rabbinic reading of this verse is: flesh of an animal while its blood and life force remain within it — that is, while the animal is still alive — may not be eaten. Do not eat the limb of a living animal. It is worth noting that the plain reading of the *pasuk* — and one that is entirely defensible as *peshat* — is a prohibition on eating blood: eat the flesh, but not while its blood is still within it, meaning drain the blood from the meat first. The rabbis, however, read it as a prohibition on *ever min hachai*.

The school that roots *ever min hachai* in *Adam HaRishon* reads this Noachide verse differently: *hahu l'mishrei shratzim hu d'asa* — it comes to clarify that the blood of rodents is not in the category of blood but is treated like their flesh, and therefore does not render them subject to the *ever min hachai* prohibition. The conceptual distinction underlying the prohibition of *ever min hachai* is the separateness of the life force from the body — a kind of body-soul dualism where the blood represents a life force distinct from the physical flesh. Rodents are not understood in these terms; their blood is considered like their flesh, and so the prohibition does not apply to them.

This analysis also resolves a prior difficulty: if *Adam HaRishon* was forbidden to eat meat entirely, why would a prohibition on *ever min hachai* be necessary? The answer is that *Adam HaRishon* was not required to be vegetarian in the sense of being forbidden from eating any animal flesh whatsoever. Rather, he was forbidden to kill animals for food. He was, however, permitted to eat from the flesh of animals that had died naturally. Consequently, a separate prohibition was needed to forbid tearing a limb from a living animal — even if one were not killing the animal, ripping a limb from it while alive was independently prohibited.

### Sirus & Kilayim

*Sirus* is derived from the command given to Noach: *shirtzу ba'aretz ur'vu vah* — swarm upon the earth and multiply in it — a form of *peru u'revu*. The opposing school reads this verse as a blessing rather than a command, and therefore draws no prohibition from it: *l'vircha d'alma* — it is a blessing for the world, not an imperative.

*Kilayim* is derived from the verse describing the animals brought into the ark: *meh'of l'minehu* — the birds according to their species. The requirement to maintain species distinction in the ark implies a prohibition on crossbreeding. The opposing school reads this verse as reflecting companionship — animals naturally prefer the company of their own kind — rather than as a prohibition: *l'tzavsa d'alma* — for the sake of fellowship, not as a legal boundary.

What is striking about *Tana d'bei Menashe*'s list is the exclusion of *dinim* — a prohibition whose absence seems conceptually difficult. If the seven commandments are meant to constitute the foundations of a just society, the omission of any obligation to establish and maintain a system of law is puzzling. *Sirus* and *kilayim*, while certainly rooted in the deepest structures of creation — the imperatives of procreation and species integrity — seem to belong to a different register than *dinim*. One might speculate that *dinim* is excluded on the grounds that it is sufficiently self-evident as natural law that it need not be explicitly commanded — a partial accommodation between the Mendelssohn natural-law view and the Rambam's commanded-law view. However, this does not sit entirely comfortably, since *shfichus damim* — which is no less intuitive — remains among the commanded prohibitions.

## Death Penalty Issues

### Three Mitzvot View

Having surveyed the scope and sources of the *sheva mitzvos*, the *gemara* turns to a distinct question: for how many of these commandments is a *ben Noach* actually executed? The widespread popular understanding is that the death penalty applies to all seven, and even for theft of less than a *prutah*. But the *gemara* reveals a spectrum of opinions.

*Amar Rav Yosef amar bei Rav al shalosh mitzvos ben Noach neherag* — Rav Yosef in the name of the school of Rav: a *ben Noach* is executed for only three. The mnemonic is *gimmel-shin-resh* (*gesher*): *giluy arayos*, *shfichus damim*, and *birchat Hashem*. This is a notably unusual grouping — it omits *avodah zarah*, which for Jews forms the first of the three cardinal sins, and substitutes *birchat Hashem* in its place.

### Four Mitzvot View

Rav Sheshes challenges this position. *Bishlama shfichus damim d'chtiv* — granted, murder is explicit: *shofech dam ha'adam ba'adam damo yishafach*. But for the other two — *giluy arayos* and *birchat Hashem* — where is the capital liability derived? If the answer is that we extend the death penalty from *shfichus damim* to the other *sheva mitzvos*, then *afilu kulhu nami* — extend it to all seven. If we are not doing that, we need a specific *pasuk* for each.

The *gemara* identifies the derivation: by *birchat Hashem*, the verse says *ish ish ki yekalel elohav v'nasa cheto* — followed ultimately by *ragom yirgmu*. The phrase *ish ish* is understood as including non-Jews, thereby establishing that the capital liability of the *birchat Hashem* prohibition extends to *bnei Noach*. Similarly, by *giluy arayos*, the verse reads *ish ish el kol she'er besaro lo tikrevu legalot ervah* — again, the double *ish ish* signals inclusion of non-Jews. For *avodah zarah*, there is also a verse using *ish ish*: *ish ish mi-bnei Yisrael u'min ha-ger ha-gar b'Yisrael asher yiten mi-zar'o la-Molech mos yumos* — making it four, not three.

The conclusion: *ella amar Rav Sheshes hani amri bei Rav al arba mitzvos ben Noach neherag* — the correct version of the teaching in the name of Rav's school is that a *ben Noach* is executed for four commandments: the three cardinal sins of *avodah zarah*, *giluy arayos*, and *shfichus damim*, together with *birchat Hashem*, which is in a sense an extension of *avodah zarah* — the rejection of God's exclusive sovereignty.

This is conceptually satisfying: these four are precisely the commandments for which Jews are also executed, at least in their most serious forms. The remaining three — *gezel*, *ever min hachai*, and *dinim* — while prohibited, do not carry the death penalty on this view.

### Azharah Shelo Mita

The *gemara* now tests this conclusion against a *braisa* that discusses the scope of the *avodah zarah* prohibition for *bnei Noach*. The *braisa* states: acts of *avodah zarah* for which a Jewish court would execute Jews — a *ben Noach* is *muzhar* (warned/prohibited) regarding those. Notably, the *braisa* does not say *neherag* (executed); it says *muzhar* (cautioned, prohibited). The inference is stark: *azharah in, mitah lo* — there is a prohibition, but not a death penalty. If so, the *braisa* itself seems to undercut the position that *bnei Noach* are executed for *avodah zarah*.

The answer offered is that of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *azharah shelahen zu hi mitatan* — their warning is their death penalty. On its most common philosophical interpretation, this principle means that any violation of a divine prohibition is in principle deserving of death, since God who prohibits is also the source of all life; any transgression of the divine will constitutes a forfeiture of the right to life in principle, and all lesser punishments represent divine leniency. This is a harsh and austere theological stance. Rav Soloveitchik noted, in a related context, that the final passage of the *Neilah* service includes the phrase *me-chamas yadeinu* — from the *chamas* of our hands — suggesting that any sin involves a kind of theft from God, since we were given our lives to serve the divine will, and any violation represents a misappropriation of that life.

However, Rashi indicates that this is not the intended meaning here. The *gemara* at this juncture is working with the position that only four of the seven commandments carry the death penalty, not all seven. Therefore the phrase *azharah shelahen zu hi mitatan* is not a philosophical statement about the nature of divine prohibition; it is a purely textual observation. The *braisa*, by using the word *muzhar* (warned), is not carefully distinguishing between warning and execution; it is simply not being specific. When it says they are warned against *avodah zarah*, it means they are executed for it — the word *azharah* is being used loosely to encompass the full consequence.

### Seven Mitzvot Death

The students of Rav — *Rav Huna, Rav Yehudah, v'kulehu talmidei de-Rav* — take the broadest position: *al sheva mitzvos Bnei Noach neherag* — a *ben Noach* is executed for all seven. The logic is: *gali Rachmana b'chada v'hu ha-din le-kulehu* — the Torah reveals the death penalty explicitly for one of the commandments (murder), and the same rule extends to all of them. This is the position that is most commonly cited and that underlies the widespread understanding of the *sheva mitzvos*: any violation, even theft of less than a *prutah*, is in principle punishable by death. It is here that the phrase *azharah shelahen zu hi mitatan* takes on its fuller philosophical resonance — any divine prohibition directed at *bnei Noach* carries the implicit consequence of death.

## Gezel Scope & Liability

### Gezel Braita

The *gemara* now questions whether the death penalty truly applies to theft: *v'al gezel ben Noach neherag?* And it cites a *braisa* that discusses the scope of the *gezel* prohibition. The *braisa* lists: *ganav* (burglary), *gezel* (robbery), *v'chen yefat toar* (taking a captive woman in war — which the *braisa* treats as a species of theft: the captor forcibly removes a woman from her family and husband, appropriating her as property), and *v'chen kayotze bahen* — similar cases.

The *braisa* then states: a *goy* who steals from a *goy*, or a *goy* who steals from a Jew — *assur*. A Jew who steals from a *goy* — *mutar*.

This is a deeply troubling passage, and it is important not to elide its difficulty. The text uses the word *Kusi* (Samaritan) throughout rather than *goy* — a form of self-censorship that appears even in editions that restore other censored passages. The ruling that a Jew may steal from a non-Jew is stated baldly. Rashi on this passage reads the *braisa* as holding that it is entirely *mutar*, with no rabbinic or Torah-level prohibition. The *gemara* in *Bava Kamma*, which discusses *gezel goy* more extensively, at minimum adds that even if it is technically permissible, it is prohibited where *chillul Hashem* would result. But bracketing that consideration, the *braisa* here seems to permit it outright. This is a *gemara* that demands acknowledgment of its difficulty rather than apologetics. The danger of teaching such material without problematizing it is that it can be internalized as a normative attitude — that stealing from or causing harm to a non-Jew is of no moral consequence. This has real-world implications, including in contemporary Israel where such *gemaras* have been cited in the context of practical political attitudes.

Returning to the technical question: the *braisa* says the non-Jewish thief is *assur*, not *chayav*. If all seven commandments carry the death penalty, why does the *braisa* use the weaker language of prohibition rather than liability? *V'ee ita nitni chayav* — it should have said *chayav*, deserving of punishment.

The *gemara*'s answer is that the word *assur* was chosen to create the proper contrast: since the *braisa* wanted to conclude with *mutar* (permissible) in the case of a Jew stealing from a *goy*, the natural contrast for the non-Jewish case is *assur* rather than *chayav*. The word *mutar* calls for its antonym *assur*, not for *chayav*. This is confirmed by the *braisa*'s treatment of murder, where no such contrast is needed. The *braisa* states: a *goy* who kills a *goy*, or a *goy* who kills a Jew — *chayav*. A Jew who kills a *goy* — *patur*. There, the *braisa* explicitly says *chayav*, because it cannot say *mutar* for the Jewish case (murder of a non-Jew, while apparently not punishable by a Jewish court, is not *mutar* — *lo maalin velo moridin*), and therefore the contrast is *chayav* versus *patur* rather than *assur* versus *mutar*.

The Rambam's text in *Hilchot Rotzeach u'Shmirat Nefesh* is instructive here. He opens by stating: *kol ha-horeg nefesh harei zeh over* — anyone who kills a person transgresses the prohibition of *lo tirtzach*. This formulation applies universally, without qualification by the identity of the victim. There is, however, a scholarly debate about whether this formulation is original or represents a later editorial modification; some hold that the original text read *kol ha-horeg nefesh mi'Yisrael*. The question of whether the prohibition of murder fully encompasses the killing of a non-Jew — and if so, what the legal consequences are — is thus an ongoing and unresolved tension within the *halachic* literature.

The broader point for the *sugya*: the use of *assur* in the theft *braisa* does not indicate an absence of the death penalty. It is simply a function of the rhetorical structure of the *braisa*, which required the word *mutar* at its conclusion and therefore used *assur* as its counterpart at the outset. The death penalty remains in force; the language is not being precise.

### Less Than Prutah

Rav Pappa explains what the phrase *v'chen kayotze bahen* — similar cases — teaches in the context of *gezel*: *lo nitzrecha ela l'pachos mishaveh prutah* — the additional clause is needed only to include cases of less than a *prutah*. That is, even theft of less than the minimum denomination of currency constitutes *gezel* for a *ben Noach* and carries the attendant liability. This is the *chiddush*: one might have thought that an amount too small to be treated as money at all could not constitute theft, but the *braisa* teaches otherwise.

### Jew vs. Non-Jew Threshold

This raises an immediate question: if a non-Jew who steals from a Jew is also *assur*, why would the theft of less than a *prutah* from a Jew not be included? The *gemara* answers: *ha bar mechila hu* — a Jew will be *mochel*, will forgive the debt. This is the *gemara*'s explanation for why less than a *prutah* does not trigger liability when stolen from a Jew: the Jewish victim will simply waive the amount. Non-Jews, by contrast, will not be *mochel* even a trivial sum.

This yields a sharp and somewhat ironic contrast: the stereotyped generosity of the Jew — quick to forgive a petty debt — is precisely what limits the legal liability of a non-Jew who steals from him, while the non-Jew's insistence on full recovery even of minimal amounts means that any taking, however small, constitutes a real wrong.

### Restitution vs. Punishme…

But this answer creates a new difficulty: if the reason less than a *prutah* from a Jew is not actionable is that the Jew will be *mochel*, why should the non-Jewish thief transgress at all? After the theft, the Jew says *machel* — forgiven — so no harm was ultimately done. The *gemara* responds: *tzara b'shaita miha ita* — at the moment of the taking, there was a genuine harm. The act of appropriating something from another, however small the amount and however willing the victim is to forgive it afterward, constitutes the violation. The subsequent *mechila* does not retroactively undo the act.

At this point, the *gemara* also addresses why the theft of less than a *prutah* from a non-Jew by another non-Jew does not need the *kayotze bahen* clause: *kevan d'lav bnei mechila ninhu gezel maalya hu* — since non-Jews are not of the type to waive small amounts, less than a *prutah* is simply ordinary theft, requiring no special derivation.

This distinction points to a deeper structural difference between how *gezel* functions for Jews and for non-Jews. By a Jew, the entire framework of theft law is oriented toward restitution: *v'heshiv et ha-gezeilah*, the verse emphasizes returning what was taken. Since the focus is on restitution, the threshold is significant — below a *prutah*, there is nothing meaningful to restore, and therefore no violation is established. A wound that causes financial injury of less than a *prutah* is handled differently: since wounding is independently culpable beyond the financial damage, lashes apply even where there is nothing to restore. But theft, which is defined entirely by the property interest, has no independent act requiring punishment when the value is below the threshold. By non-Jews, there is no restitutionary framework — the liability is purely punitive. Since the line between a *prutah* and less than a *prutah* has no operative significance in a punitive system, any taking is equally an act of *gezel* regardless of amount.

### Withholding Wages

The *gemara* offers an alternative interpretation of the *kayotze bahen* clause. Rav Acha bar Brei d'Rav Ika proposes: *lo nitzrecha ela l'koves sachar sachir* — the additional clause is needed to include withholding a worker's wages. This is a qualitatively different case: it is not an active taking but a passive failure to pay what is owed. One might have thought that *gezel* requires an affirmative act of misappropriation; withholding wages is an omission. The *braisa* teaches that it is nonetheless included. The *gemara* in *Bava Metzia* similarly holds that a Jew who withholds wages transgresses *lo tigzol*, though it is regarded as a *chiddush* there too.

## Keyotzei Bo Cases

### Yifat Toar Case

The *braisa* included *yifat toar* — taking a captive woman in war — among the cases similar to *gezel*. This is an interesting categorization: *yifat toar* can also be understood as an aspect of *giluy arayos*, particularly if she is a married woman and the captor has relations with her. The *braisa*, however, frames it as theft: the captor is forcibly removing a woman from her family, from her husband, from her community — appropriating her as property. The *gemara* extends the *kayotze bahen* of *yifat toar* as follows: *ben Noach she-yiched shifcha l'avdo uva aleha — neherag aleha*. If a *ben Noach* designated a female slave to his servant and then himself had relations with her, he is executed. This is not a case of *arayos* in the technical sense, since there is no marriage bond for slaves. But by taking a woman designated to another — appropriating her as his own — he has committed an act analogous to theft, a species of *kayotze bahen* within the *gezel* category.

### Murder Cases

*Keyotze bo d'shfichus damim lo tanya* — there are no *kayotze bahen* cases taught in connection with murder. Murder is categorical: it either is or is not the taking of human life. There are no peripheral or analogous cases that carry liability without being fully constitutive of the prohibition. The *gemara* notes, via *Abaye*, that if one were ever to find a *baraisa* that does list *kayotze bahen* cases for murder, it would be attributable to Rav Yonatan ben Shaul.

### Self-Defense Rodef

The relevant *baraisa* of Rav Yonatan ben Shaul teaches: *rodef achar chaveiro lehorgo yachol lehatzilo b'echad me'eivarav v'lo hitzilo* — if someone was pursuing another with lethal intent, and it was possible to stop the pursuer by wounding him in a limb, yet the one being pursued killed him outright, then the defender *neherag alav* — is executed. This is not a Jew versus non-Jew distinction; it applies equally to Jews and non-Jews. The case presupposes an objective assessment that the threat could have been neutralized with less than lethal force, and that the defender nonetheless chose to kill. Under those circumstances, the killing constitutes a form of unjustified homicide, even in a self-defense context.

This is a legally and practically difficult standard. The assessment of whether a reasonable alternative existed — whether one *yachol lehatzilo b'echad me'eivarav* — is inherently uncertain in the moment of threat. The IDF rules of engagement grapple with precisely this question: at what point is a threat sufficiently neutralized that continued lethal force becomes unjustified? And when, in retrospect, is it appropriate to conclude that a defender had a feasible non-lethal alternative? The *halacha* does not resolve these evidentiary difficulties, but it establishes the principle: if the objective assessment is that the threat could have been stopped without killing, then the killing is not justified, and this constitutes the *kayotze bahen* case of *shfichus damim*.

Not everyone agrees, of course. Alternative positions hold that even if one kills when a non-lethal alternative existed, it does not constitute murder in a culpable sense — it is still self-defense. But according to Rav Yonatan ben Shaul, it is precisely these borderline cases that constitute the *kayotze bahen* of *shfichus damim*.

### Non-Jew Permissions

A final and philosophically rich question emerges from the discussion of the *rodef* case: it is not self-evident that the permission to kill a *rodef* — which the *gemara* explicitly presupposes for a *ben Noach* as well — should apply to non-Jews at all. A *ben Noach* is prohibited in murder. How do we know that the exception of self-defense carries over to the non-Jewish framework?

The present *gemara* makes it clear that it does. But the question extends further: how do the various permissions and overrides that operate within Jewish law map onto *sheva mitzvos*? For example, a Jew is obligated to accept martyrdom rather than transgress *avodah zarah*, *giluy arayos*, or *shfichus damim* under duress. Does a *ben Noach* have any obligation of martyrdom? If someone holds a gun to a non-Jew's head and demands that he commit idolatry or be killed, must he refuse and die? It is possible that a *ben Noach* has no obligation of martyrdom whatsoever — that the *yehareg v'al ya'avor* framework is entirely internal to *halacha* as it applies to Jews, and that a *ben Noach* under duress simply has no such requirement. Similarly, how do concepts like *shogeg* (inadvertent violation) interact with the Noachide framework, given the harshness of *azharah shelahen zu hi mitatan*? These questions — how the internal parameters, limits, and overrides of the commandments operate within the *sheva mitzvos* context — constitute a rich and not fully mapped area of *halachic* inquiry.
