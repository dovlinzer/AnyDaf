# Sukkah 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mats & Tumah Intro

### Daf Overview

Daf 20 completes the perek, and the Mishnah that occupies us through the end of the chapter is devoted entirely to the laws of *tumah* and *taharah* as they bear on the materials used for *s'chach*. The transition into this territory is not incidental: the question of what may be used to roof a *sukkah* is inextricably bound up with the question of what kinds of objects are susceptible to *tumah*, and the Mishnah works through a series of cases to untangle the two.

### Mat Susceptibility

The basic issue concerns mats. The rule governing *s'chach* is well known: it must be made of something that is not susceptible to *tumah*. And mats present a genuine question, because a mat's susceptibility turns entirely on its designated purpose. If a mat is made for sitting — for reclining or lying upon — then it is subject to *tumat midras*. When a *zav* or *zavah* sits on an object that has been designated as a seat, that object becomes an *av ha-tumah*, a primary source of *tumah*. The mat in that case is functioning as a vessel — specifically, as a seat — and is therefore *mekabel tumah*.

### Purpose Definition

If, however, the mat is not made for sitting, the analysis changes. An object made of reeds, hemp, or wood that is flat and lacks a receptacle (*beit kibul*) would ordinarily be *tahor*, since *peshutei klei etz* — flat wooden or wood-like vessels without a receptacle — cannot receive *tumah*. And beyond that structural consideration, a mat made for roofing is treated as part of a structure rather than as a vessel, and objects that are part of a structure cannot become *tamei*. The practical upshot is that we must determine, when examining any given mat, what its basic purpose is — and how that purpose is established when it has not been explicitly stated.

This was a live question before dedicated *sukkah* mats with *hechsherim* became commercially available. If one went to a hardware store and purchased a thin bamboo mat, what was it for? Was it a wall hanging? Was it for roofing? Was it for sitting? What if the manufacturer simply produced it to meet demand, without specifying any use? Does one follow the majority of actual usage? Does the purchaser's intent matter? Does the season of purchase? These are the questions the Mishnah sets out to address.

## Mishnah: Express Cases

### Large Reed Mats

The Mishnah opens: *machatzelet kanim gedolah* — a large reed mat. The Mishnah states two express cases and then, through Rabbi Eliezer's addendum, hints at the problem of the unstated case.

### Reclining vs. Roofing

If one made the large mat *l'shchivah* — explicitly for the purpose of reclining, sitting, or lying on it — then it is *mekabelet tumah* and may not be used for *s'chach*. If one made it *l'sichuch* — for roofing purposes, not necessarily for a *sukkah* specifically, but for any roofing use — then one may use it for *s'chach* and it does not receive *tumah*. These two cases represent the obvious poles: express designation in one direction or the other is unambiguous. The crucial question — what happens when the mat was made without any express purpose — is precisely what the Gemara will interrogate.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer says: *achas ktanah v'achas gedolah* — whether small or large, if made for reclining it is *mekabelet tumah* and may not be used for *s'chach*; if made for *s'chach* it may be used and is not susceptible. The reason Rabbi Eliezer adds this is intelligible against the background assumption that the larger something is, the more naturally it is associated with construction and roofing purposes, while the smaller it is, the more naturally it is assumed to have been made for personal use and reclining. Rabbi Eliezer is therefore coming to include the small mat in the same framework: the express purpose, not the size, is what determines the status. But since both Rabbi Eliezer and the *tanna kamma* address only express cases, it remains entirely unclear what either holds about the *stama* — the case where no purpose was stated.

---

## Contradiction Issue

### Internal Contradiction

The Gemara observes: *hagufa kashya* — the Mishnah is internally contradictory. The opening line states that if made for reclining, the mat is *mekabelet tumah*. The implication is that the decisive factor is the express designation *l'shchivah*. If you made it for reclining — yes, it is a vessel. But that phrasing implies: if you did *not* make it explicitly for reclining, then by default it is considered made for *s'chach* and there is no problem.

### Stama Case Problem

Then the Mishnah states the second case: if made for *s'chach*, one may use it for *s'chach* and it is not *mekabelet tumah*. But this phrasing implies the opposite: only if you expressly made it for *s'chach* is it permitted; absent that explicit designation, one would assume it is made for reclining and it would be problematic. The two halves of the Mishnah thus pull in opposite directions on the *stama* case. The first half implies that *stama* is good; the second half implies that *stama* is not good. The same difficulty arises with even greater force in Rabbi Eliezer's formulation, since he explicitly states the rule applies whether small or large — yet his two statements are equally contradictory about what the *stama* case looks like.

### Lo Kashya Resolution

The Gemara's first answer is *lo kashya* — it is not difficult — *kan b'gedolah kan b'ktanah*: the two halves of the Mishnah are not talking about the same size mat. The opening speaks of a large mat (*gedolah*), whose *stama* is assumed to be made for roofing. For a large mat, then, explicit designation *l'shchivah* is the thing that creates a problem; absent that, one may use it for *s'chach*. The second half of the Mishnah shifts, without flagging the transition, to a small mat (*ktanah*), whose *stama* is assumed to be made for reclining. For a small mat, explicit designation *l'sichuch* is what permits it; absent that, it is assumed to be a seat and may not be used.

This resolution handles the *tanna kamma* adequately — the word *gedolah* in the opening clause signals which case is being discussed, and the small mat case is implicit in the second clause. But Rabbi Eliezer's formulation is harder to resolve this way, since he explicitly says *achas ktanah v'achas gedolah* — he covers both sizes — yet his two halves remain contradictory about the *stama*.

---

## Rava's Solution

### Rava Framework

The Gemara therefore introduces Rava's explanation. Rava proposes: *b'gedolah kulei alma lo pligi d'stama l'sichuch* — concerning a large mat, nobody disagrees that the *stama* is considered made for roofing. A large mat, by its nature, is not made for personal reclining and is universally assumed to be intended for construction or roofing purposes. The debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer, Rava says, concerns only the *ktanah*, the small mat.

### Tanna Kamma View

The *tanna kamma* holds *ktanah l'shchivah*: a small mat, when its purpose is unstated, is assumed to be made for reclining. Therefore, a small mat is *mekabelet tumah* in its default state and requires explicit designation *l'sichuch* to be usable for *s'chach*.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

Rabbi Eliezer holds *stam ktanah nami l'sichuch*: even a small mat, when its purpose is unstated, is assumed to be made for roofing. Rabbi Eliezer therefore comes to teach that whether small or large, the default is permissible; only explicit designation *l'shchivah* creates a problem.

### L'sichuch Reinterpretati…

This reading requires a subtle but important reinterpretation of the word *l'sichuch* as it appears in the Mishnah. On Rava's reading, when the Mishnah says "if made *l'sichuch*, one may use it for *s'chach*," the phrase *l'sichuch* does not mean literally that it must have been expressly designated for roofing. Rather, it means everything other than *l'shchivah* — any purpose other than explicit reclining counts. The *tanna kamma* is saying: for a *gedolah*, the only thing that creates a problem is explicit *l'shchivah*; everything else is treated as though it were made for roofing, and the mat is permitted. The *stama* of a *gedolah* is thus good, and the *stama* of a *ktanah* is implicitly not good under the *tanna kamma*'s position. Rabbi Eliezer then comes and says: whether *gedolah* or *ktanah*, the rule is the same — only explicit *l'shchivah* disqualifies.

Rava's reading is an elegant solution to the contradiction within Rabbi Eliezer's statement. The phrase *l'sichuch* effectively means "in any case other than *l'shchivah*," and the debate is simply whether this default-permissive rule applies only to large mats or to small mats as well.

The Gemara then raises a grammatical objection from Abaye: if Rabbi Eliezer's point is that even a *ktanah* is permissible, he should have said "whether large or even small" — placing the *gedolah* first as the obvious case and adding the *ktanah* as the novel point — rather than placing the *ktanah* first. There is in fact a textual variant debated between Rashi and Tosafot regarding the precise reading, and this grammatical issue turns on which version one accepts. Rather than parsing the full textual debate, the Gemara moves on to a more decisive challenge.

The Gemara cites a *baraita* that appears to contradict Rava's framing entirely. In the *baraita*, the *tanna kamma* says: a *gedolah* of reed mats, one may use for *s'chach* — that is, the *stama* of a *gedolah* is permitted. Rabbi Eliezer responds: only if it is not *mekabelet tumah* may one use it for *s'chach* — meaning Rabbi Eliezer is saying one may not assume it is not *mekabelet tumah* without explicit evidence. The *tanna kamma* in the *baraita* assumes the *stama* of a *gedolah* is fine; Rabbi Eliezer requires something more. This reverses the roles completely: Rabbi Eliezer here appears not as the lenient voice extending the *gedolah*'s default-permissive rule to the *ktanah*, but as the stringent voice who will not accept even the *gedolah*'s *stama* without explicit designation.

The Gemara therefore rejects Rava's solution and instead adopts Rav Pappa's formulation. Rav Pappa says: *b'ktanah kulei alma lo pligi d'stama l'shchivah* — concerning a small mat, nobody disagrees that the *stama* is for reclining. The debate is about the large mat. The *tanna kamma* holds that a *stama gedolah* is for *s'chach* — the default assumption for a large mat is that it was made for roofing and it is permissible. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even a *stama gedolah* is treated like it is for reclining; unless one expressly makes it for *s'chach*, one may not use it.

On this reading, what does Rabbi Eliezer mean when he says "whether small or large, if made for reclining it is *mekabelet tumah*"? The Gemara explains: the phrase "if made for reclining" in Rabbi Eliezer's statement is not referring to an explicit act of designation. Rather, since Rabbi Eliezer holds that even a *gedolah* is *stama* treated as though made for reclining, the phrase *asuyah l'shchivah* means: even the ordinary, unstated construction of a mat is treated as being for reclining — unless you go out of your way to make it *l'sichuch* explicitly. So when Rabbi Eliezer says "made for reclining," he means: every case that has not been explicitly designated for *s'chach* is treated as made for reclining. This is a very hard read of the Mishnah, but the Gemara commits to it in order to reconcile the Mishnah with the *baraita*. The phrase that now carries precise weight in Rabbi Eliezer's statement is the second phrase, *l'sichuch* — because only that explicit designation will save the mat from being treated as *l'shchivah*.

To summarize the positions as ultimately resolved: the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer agree that a *stama ktanah* is for reclining and is not usable for *s'chach*. They dispute the *stama gedolah*. The *tanna kamma* says a *stama gedolah* is permitted. Rabbi Eliezer says even a *stama gedolah* is treated as though made for reclining and may only be used if explicitly designated for *s'chach*. This is the mirror image of what Rava had proposed.

## Baraita: Mat Materials

### Soft Materials

The Gemara now cites a *baraita* that addresses the same underlying questions from a different angle, focusing on the material of the mat rather than its size alone. The *baraita* discusses mats of *shaifa* and *gemi* — papyrus and bullrush, soft water plants — which are softer materials and therefore more naturally associated with reclining and personal use.

### Soft Mats Size

For a large mat of these soft materials, one may use it for *s'chach*. This is consistent with what we have seen: even soft materials, when made large, carry the presumption of roofing use. A small mat of *shaifa* and *gemi*, however, may not be used for *s'chach* — the small size combined with the soft material makes it clearly a reclining mat.

### Hard Materials

The *baraita* then turns to mats of *kanim* and *chilat* — reeds and another harder material — which are less likely to be made for reclining and more likely to be made for construction purposes. For a large mat of these harder materials, one may obviously use it for *s'chach*.

### Hard Mats Weave

For a small mat of the harder material, the analysis becomes more nuanced and turns on how the mat is woven. If it is woven in a basket-weave style (*gedilim*) — a coarser weave using thicker strands — even a small mat may be used for *s'chach*, since such a weave is not associated with soft reclining surfaces. If, however, it is tightly woven (*arugah*) — using finer, thinner strands in a tight pattern — even a small hard-material mat may not be used for *s'chach*, since the tighter weave creates a softer surface more appropriate for sitting.

### Lenient Views

Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yosi says in the name of his father: in either case of the harder material — whether basket-woven or tightly woven — one may use it for *s'chach*. Rabbi Dosa holds similarly, that when it comes to the harder material, even the small mat is permissible for *s'chach* in all cases of weave. The Gemara does not attempt to map these positions onto the earlier dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *tanna kamma*.

## Eduyot Mishnah

### Chutzlot Tumah

The Gemara now introduces a Mishnah from tractate Eduyot. The Mishnah discusses *chutzlot* — a term that sounds similar to *machatzlot* (mats), though the precise identity of *chutzlot* will itself become a question. The Mishnah in Eduyot states: *kol ha-chutzlot metam'in tumei meit* — all *chutzlot* can receive the *tumah* of a dead body. This is the opinion of Rabbi Dosa, whom we just encountered in the *baraita* on hard-material mats.

### Rabbi Dosa Debate

The *chachamim* disagree and say the *chutzlot* can become *tamei midras* — the *tumah* of seats. So according to Rabbi Dosa, these objects can receive *tumat meit* but are not subject to *tumat midras*; according to the *chachamim*, they are susceptible to the more stringent *tumah* of *midras*.

### Midras-Mei Meis Link

The Gemara immediately questions the *chachamim*'s position. *Midras* — does that mean *midras* yes but *tumei meit* no? But we have a rule: *kol ha-metamei midras metamei tumei meit* — anything that can become *tamei midras* can also become *tamei meit*. Anything that functions as a seat and is therefore subject to the *tumah* of a *zav* or *zavah* sitting on it is *ipso facto* also susceptible to *tumat meit*. The categories are not parallel: anything that is *mekabel midras* is *mekabel tumei meit*, but not vice versa. A wooden spoon with a receptacle can become *tamei meit* without being subject to *midras*; but anything subject to *midras* is also susceptible to *tumat meit*. So when the *chachamim* say *midras*, they are implying *tumei meit* as well. The Gemara therefore amends its reading: the *chachamim* mean the *chutzlot* can receive *af midras* — even *midras*, and by extension *tumei meit* as well.

This also clarifies the structure of the dispute. Rabbi Dosa acknowledges that these objects are vessels of some kind — he says they can become *tamei meit* — but denies that they are designated as seats. The *chachamim* say they are made for sitting and are therefore subject to *tumat midras*. In the context of our earlier discussion, this is striking: until now we had assumed that if a mat is not designated for sitting, its only alternative is roofing, making it *tahor* altogether. Here Rabbi Dosa says something is not made for sitting and yet can still become *tamei*. This anomaly will require explanation.

## Reish Lakish Digression

### Chutzlot Identity

Before resolving the substance of the dispute, the Gemara pauses to ask: *mai chutzlot* — what are *chutzlot*? Rav Avdimi bar Hamduri says they are *marzuvli*. The Gemara asks what *marzuvli* means. Rava answers: *mazvli* — which Rashi explains refers to objects used for a pillow-like function, perhaps something like a pillowcase or a sack, an object that might be used to contain things. This interpretation suggests the *chutzlot* might not be mats in the ordinary sense at all but rather some type of enclosed or semi-enclosed textile object.

Reish Lakish, however, says: *machatzlot mamash* — they are simply mats, like the *machatzlot* we have been discussing throughout the chapter.

### Torah History Excursus

The Gemara then notes that Reish Lakish follows his own reasoning, for Reish Lakish made the following declaration: *hareini kapparat Rebbi Chiyya u-vanav* — I am the atonement for Rabbi Chiyya and his sons. This phrase, as Rashi explains, is the traditional expression one uses when mentioning a deceased *rav* or parent: one says *hareini kapparat mishkavo*, meaning that any suffering that comes upon me should serve as atonement for any shortcomings of the deceased. The Talmud in Kiddushin teaches that this is specifically appropriate during the first twelve months after death, when the *neshamah* is in a particular period of judgment; thereafter, one says *zichrono livracha l'chayei ha-olam ha-ba*.

Reish Lakish then proceeds with a remarkable account of Torah history. He says: *she-b'tchilla kshe-nishtachacha torah mi-Yisrael* — when Torah was forgotten from Israel, *ala Ezra mi-Bavel v'yisdah* — Ezra came up from Babylonia and laid its foundation anew. After the *churban ha-bayit*, when the *talmidei chachamim* were removed from Eretz Yisrael, Ezra had to come from Bavel to reinstitute Torah learning in the land. Then it went into decline again, and *ala Hillel HaBavli v'yisdah* — Hillel the Babylonian came and reinstituted it. The irony noted here is that Hillel, whom we think of as a foundational figure of rabbinic learning in Eretz Yisrael, was himself originally from Bavel — the famous story of the people of Eretz Yisrael who did not know whether a *korban pesach* could be brought on *erev shabbat* and had to be answered by "somebody from Bavel named Hillel." The further irony is that Hillel himself knew the answer from *Shemaya and Avtalyon*, who were themselves in Eretz Yisrael.

### Rabbi Chiyya Legacy

Then Torah declined again, and *v'alu Rebbi Chiyya u-vanav v'yisduha* — Rabbi Chiyya and his sons came and reinstituted it once more. This places Rabbi Chiyya and his sons in a line of succession with Ezra and Hillel as those who restored Torah to Eretz Yisrael from outside. This is why Reish Lakish prefaces his citation of Rabbi Chiyya's teaching with this declaration of atonement — he is about to quote a *halacha* in Rabbi Chiyya's name, which is precisely the context in which the *gemara* in Kiddushin situates the formula *hareini kapparat mishkavo*.

## Chutzlot Resolution

### Usha vs. Tiberias

Rabbi Chiyya and his sons say: *lo nechelku Rebbi Dosa v'chachamim ela al machatzlot shel Usha* — Rabbi Dosa and the *chachamim* were not debating the mats of Usha or the mats of Tiberias. The mats of Usha are so soft that they are always made for sitting and are always *tamei*. The mats of Tiberias are so hard that they are always made for roofing or other construction uses and are always *tahor*.

### Two Extremes

These two cities — Usha and Tiberias — also happen to be places where the *Sanhedrin* relocated after Yavneh, centers of Torah reconstitution in Eretz Yisrael, which lends an interesting resonance to their use here as the poles of the halachic spectrum.

### Other Places Debate

The actual dispute between Rabbi Dosa and the *chachamim*, Rabbi Chiyya explains, concerns the mats of other places — those that are intermediate in softness and hardness. One opinion holds: since people do not routinely sit on these mats — *keivan d'leika d'yatvi alaihu* — they are like the mats of Tiberias, and their purpose is roofing or other non-sitting use, and they are *tahor* from *midras*. The other opinion holds: since people do occasionally sit on them — *keivan d'mikrei v'yatvi alaihu* — they are like the mats of Usha, and that occasional use as a seat is enough to render them subject to *tumat midras*. This is the real substance of the debate: does occasional use as a seat suffice to create the status of a sitting vessel, even if the primary or typical use is otherwise?

## Chutzlot Analysis

### Contradiction Restated

The Gemara now returns to the substantive halachic question raised earlier: Rabbi Dosa says these objects can become *tamei meit* but are not subject to *midras*. But we have just explained that these objects — whether mats or pillowcase-like objects — are not designated for sitting. How then can they become *tamei meit* at all? Until now, we assumed that anything not made for sitting was functionally used for roofing and thus was *tahor* altogether. So what is the basis for *tumat meit* here?

### Rim Distinction

The Gemara answers: *lo kashya* — *ha d'it lei gedanpa, ha d'leit lei gedanpa* — the distinction is whether the object has a rim (*gedanpa*) around its edge or does not.

### No Rim = Tahor

If it does not have a rim, it is *peshutei klei etz*: treated like a flat wooden vessel without a receptacle. Such an object, when it is not designated as a seat, is *tahor* entirely — it cannot receive *tumah* of any kind.

### Rim = Tumah Possible

If, however, the object has a rim around it, it takes on the character of a vessel with a receptacle (*beit kibul*). Even if it is not a seat, and even if it would normally be used for roofing, the rim creates a vessel-like quality that enables it to receive *tumat meit*. This is what Rabbi Dosa means: the *chutzlot* he is discussing have a rim, making them susceptible to *tumat meit*, but they are not designated for sitting and therefore are not subject to *tumat midras*.

## Alternative Functions

### Soft Use Cases

The Gemara explores what functions these objects serve, aside from sitting or roofing, that would explain why they are considered vessels even when not designated for sitting. If the *chutzlot* are pillowcase-type objects — the *marzuvli* interpretation — then those made of softer materials like *sak* and *safira* (sackcloth and similar textiles) can be used as containers: for storing fruit, or for carrying beans and flour. This storage function gives them the character of a vessel and explains why they can become *tamei meit*.

### Hard Use Cases

If the *chutzlot* are mats — the *matzblei* or *machatzlot* interpretation — then those made of harder materials like *hutza* and *chalpa* can be used as covers for barrels, a function Rashi identifies as *neziata*. Tosafot, however, raises a difficulty: if the function is merely covering a barrel, the mat still lacks a genuine *beit kibul* and should be *tahor* as *peshutei klei etz*. Tosafot therefore explains that in this case the object must have a rim, and it is the rim that creates the receptacle character and enables *tumat meit*.

### Curtain Use

The softer-material mats, when understood as actual mats, can be used as curtains — hanging textile dividers that are not structurally part of a building. This is a halachically significant distinction: an object used for roofing is considered part of a structure and cannot be a vessel susceptible to *tumah*. But a curtain, even though it is hung from or within a structure, is not considered part of the structure itself — it remains a vessel. Indeed, a parallel principle operates in the laws of *Shabbat*, where hanging a curtain is not considered construction because it is not structurally integral to the building. Because a curtain retains its vessel identity rather than being absorbed into the structure, a mat used as a curtain can become *tamei* even though a mat used for roofing cannot.

## Budya Teaching

### Rabbi Chananya Account

A *tanna* teaches: Rabbi Chananya says that when he went out to the exile (*galah*), he found a certain elder who told him that one may use *budya* — a type of mat — for *s'chach*. The *baraita* leaves aside all the distinctions of size and material that have occupied us until now and focuses on a different criterion entirely.

### Confirmation & Condition

Rabbi Chananya then came to his uncle Rabbi Yehoshua — his father's brother — who confirmed this ruling: yes, mats (*budya*) may be used for *s'chach*. Rav Chisda adds the critical condition: *vehu d'leit lei gedanpa* — but only if the mat does not have a rim. If it has a rim, it has a *beit kibul*, is subject to *tumah*, and may therefore not be used for *s'chach*.

### Mechuza Mats

Ulla observes: these mats of the people of Mechuza — *ilmale kiyara shelahen* — were it not for their rim, one could have used them for *s'chach*. But because they are made with a rim around them, they cannot be used.

### Tannitic Parallel

A *baraita* then confirms the same principle: *mesachchin b'budya* — one may use *budya* for *s'chach*. *Ve-im yesh lahem kiyara* — but if it has a rim around it, *ein mesachchin bah* — one may not use it.

What is striking about this concluding passage is that it entirely sets aside the complex analysis of size (large versus small), material (soft versus hard), and weave (basket-weave versus tight-weave) that dominated the earlier discussion, and lands on a single, decisive criterion: the presence or absence of a *gedanpa*, a rim. With a rim, the mat has a *beit kibul* and is therefore susceptible to *tumah*, which disqualifies it from use as *s'chach*. Without a rim, it is *peshutei klei etz* — a flat wooden-category object without a receptacle — and when not designated as a seat, it is *tahor* and suitable for *s'chach*.
