# Niddah 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs. Levi

### Maayan Echad Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: רַב אָמַר: מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא — הַתּוֹרָה טִמְּאַתּוּ וְהַתּוֹרָה טִהֲרַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to blood emitted by a woman during her days of purity after childbirth, **it was stated** that there is a dispute between the Sages. **Rav says: It is** from **one source** in a woman's body that pure and impure blood are emitted, but **the Torah rendered impure** the blood emitted during her days of impurity **and the Torah rendered pure** the blood emitted during her days of purity.

The daf opens in the middle of 35b, approximately ten lines down from the top of the wide lines, with the word *meisivei*. The subject is a focused and clearly defined debate between Rav and Levi concerning the nature of the blood a woman produces during the *yemei tohar* — the 33 or 66 days of ritual purity following childbirth (33 days after a boy, 66 after a girl).

Rav's position is stated as *maayan echad hu hatorah timaso vehatorah tiharato*: it is a single source of blood. The blood during *yemei tohar* comes from the same biological origin as all uterine blood; it is the Torah itself that assigns it a *tahor* status during that period. The *tahor* designation is, on this view, a purely halachic reality — the Torah decrees it, and the decree is what determines its status.

### Shnei Maayanos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵוִי אָמַר: שְׁנֵי מַעְיָנוֹת הֵם — נִסְתָּם הַטָּמֵא נִפְתָּח הַטָּהוֹר, נִסְתָּם הַטָּהוֹר נִפְתָּח הַטָּמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Levi says: There are two sources** in a woman's body. Blood emitted during her days of impurity emerges from one source, while blood emitted during her days of purity emerges from the other, and these two sources are not active simultaneously. Rather, when **the** source of the **impure** blood **is closed,** i.e., following her days of impurity, **the** source of the **pure** blood **opens,** and when **the** source of the **pure** blood **is closed,** at the conclusion of her days of purity, either thirty-three days for a male child or sixty-six days for a female child, **the** source of the **impure** blood **is opened.**

Levi disagrees on what he presents as biological grounds. His formulation is *shnei maayanos heim nistam hatamei veniftach hatahor niftach hatahor venistam hatamei*: there are two distinct sources. When a woman transitions from the week or two weeks of *tumah* following childbirth into the *yemei tohar*, what actually occurs, according to Levi, is that the *tamei* source closes and a different, *tahor* source opens. The *tahor* character of the blood during *yemei tohar* is thus grounded in a genuine physiological shift, not merely in a halachic assignment. (The Gemara does not specify what this second source consists of biologically, and that question is left unresolved.)

### Dam Yoledes Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: דַּם הַיּוֹלֶדֶת שֶׁלֹּא טָבְלָה, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כְּרוּקָּהּ וּכְמֵימֵי רַגְלֶיהָ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מְטַמֵּא לַח וְיָבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the mishna: With regard to **the blood of a woman who gave birth** and reached the conclusion of her days of impurity but **did not** yet **immerse, Beit Shammai say:** The blood is **like her saliva and urine,** and it imparts impurity only while moist. **And Beit Hillel say:** Her blood is considered like that of a menstruating woman, and it **imparts impurity** whether **moist or dry.**

The Gemara now tests these two positions against a *mishna*. The *mishna* reads: *dam yoledes shelo tavla* — the blood of a woman who has given birth and has not yet immersed in the *mikvah*. What is the status of blood she produces during the *yemei tohar*? Beit Shammai says it has a middle-level *tumah*, comparable to her saliva or urine — the fluids of a *yoledes* — rather than full *dam tamei*. Beit Hillel, however, says it is full *dam tamei*, equivalent to *dam niddah*, because she has not yet gone to the *mikvah*.

The focus here is on Beit Hillel's position: even during the 33 or 66 days, the blood remains fully *tamei* if she has not immersed. This seems to indicate strongly that the *tahor* status of *dam yemei tohar* is a halachic reality contingent on immersion — not a biological fact. The Gemara notes: *bishlama leRav de'amar maayan echad hu*, according to Rav this is coherent; the Torah assigns a *tahor* status to this blood, and the Torah may condition that assignment on her having immersed from the preceding period of *tumah*. Accordingly, Beit Hillel's position that blood during *yemei tohar* is still fully *tamei* without *mikvah* immersion is entirely understandable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב, דְּאָמַר: מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מְטַמֵּא לַח וְיָבֵשׁ. אֶלָּא לְלֵוִי, דְּאָמַר: שְׁנֵי מַעְיָנוֹת הֵן, אַמַּאי מְטַמֵּא לַח וְיָבֵשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: If so, **granted,** the mishna is clear **according to Rav, who said** pure and impure blood emanate from **one source,** as it is **due to that** reason that Beit Hillel maintain that the blood **imparts impurity** whether it is **moist or dry,** since she did not yet immerse. **But according to Levi, who said there are two sources, why** do Beit Hillel maintain that the blood **imparts impurity** whether it is **moist or dry?** After all, blood emitted during her days of purity comes from the source of pure blood.

*Ela leLevi de'amar shnei maayanos heim* — but according to Levi, who says the blood is coming from an entirely different biological source, why should Beit Hillel say it is *tamei*? What difference does it make whether she went to the *mikvah* or not? If the source of the blood has physically changed, its origin renders it *tahor* regardless.

### Shofaat Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ לֵוִי: הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּשׁוֹפַעַת, אִי בְּשׁוֹפַעַת, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Levi** could **say to you: Here we are dealing with** a woman who **continuously discharges** menstrual blood from within her days of impurity until sometime during her days of purity. Since the continuous flow of blood indicates that the blood emanates from the source of the impure blood, Beit Hillel rule that it imparts impurity whether it is moist or dry. The Gemara asks: **If** the mishna is dealing **with** a woman who **continuously discharges** menstrual blood, **what is the reason that Beit Shammai** maintain this blood imparts impurity only while moist?

Levi's response is: *hacha bamai askinan beshofaat* — we are dealing with a case of *shofaat*, a continuous flow. If the bleeding carried over without interruption from day 7 into day 8 (or day 14 into day 15), then even according to Levi, we know the blood is still coming from the *tamei* source, because it is visibly flowing from the *tamei* period into the *tahor* period. In such a case, Beit Hillel's stringency is perfectly understandable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Beit Shammai hold** that **it is** from **one source** that pure and impure blood emanate, and the Torah deemed pure any blood emitted during her days of purity.

It should be noted, however, that this answer generates its own difficulty: if it is *shofaat* and thus biologically still from the *tamei* source, then it should be *tamei* regardless of whether she has or has not gone to the *mikvah*. The *mishna* speaks specifically about a woman who has *not* gone to the *mikvah*; but on Levi's reading, once it is established as a *shofaat* case, the *mikvah* status becomes irrelevant. This is noted as a very weak answer.

### Beit Shammai Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְלֵוִי, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיכָּא בֵּין בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל, אֶלָּא לְרַב מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the opinion of Rav: **Granted,** the mishna is clear **according to Levi,** who maintains there are two separate sources, as **that is** how there is a practical difference **between** the opinions of **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel.** Beit Hillel hold there are two sources, and the continuous flow of blood from her days of impurity into her days of purity indicates that the blood emanates from the source of the impure blood, whereas Beit Shammai contend that pure and impure blood emanate from one source, and the Torah deemed pure the blood of her days of purity. **But according to Rav, what is** the reason for the difference **between them?**

The Gemara presses Levi further: *ei beshofaat mai taama deBeit Shammai* — if it is a case of *shofaat*, what is Beit Shammai's reasoning? If the blood is flowing continuously from the *tamei* source, how can Beit Shammai say it becomes *tahor* the moment day 8 begins? Levi concedes: *ksavar Beit Shammai maayan echad hu* — Beit Shammai holds the position of one source, i.e., Rav's position. On that view, once the day changes, the Torah's assigned status changes and the blood is automatically *tahor* regardless of flow. Levi must concede that Beit Shammai is against him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ יוֹמֵי וּטְבִילָה, דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: בְּיוֹמֵי תְּלָה רַחֲמָנָא, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: בְּיוֹמֵי וּטְבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The difference **between them** is due to their opinions with regard to the significance of the **days and** the **immersion** at the conclusion of her days of impurity. **As, Beit Shammai hold** that **the Merciful One rendered** the purity of her blood **dependent on days,** which means that once she begins her days of purity her blood is pure regardless of whether or not she immersed. **And Beit Hillel hold** that it is dependent **on** both **days and immersion.** Accordingly, if she fails to immerse after her days of impurity, any blood she sees is impure.

The Gemara then turns this around and asks: *bishlama leLevi haini de'ika bein Beit Shammai leBeit Hillel* — fine, according to Levi's framing of the case as *shofaat*, we can explain the Beit Shammai/Beit Hillel debate as a dispute about whether the source is one or two. Beit Hillel says two sources, so a *shofaat* flow during day 8 is still *tamei*; Beit Shammai says one source, so once the day turns it is *tahor*.

But *ela leRav mai beinaihu* — according to Rav, where everyone agrees it is one source, what exactly is the debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel? The Gemara answers: *ika beinaihu yomei u'tvila* — they are debating a purely halachic question: what does the Torah require in order for the status to shift to *tahor*? Is it sufficient that she enters day 8 (*d'Beit Shammai savri b'yomei tala Rachmana*)? Or does the Torah additionally require immersion (*u'Beit Hillel savri lo b'yomei u'tvila*)? On Rav's reading, this is a clean and natural halachic dispute: does the status-change require only the passage of days, or does it also require *mikvah*?

## Yoledet BeZova

### Modim BeYoledet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, וּמוֹדִים בְּיוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב שֶׁהִיא מְטַמְּאָה לַח וְיָבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from the continuation of the mishna: **And** Beit Shammai **concede** to Beit Hillel **in** the case of **a woman who gives birth as a *zava*, that** any blood **she** sees **imparts impurity** whether it is **moist or dry.** It **enters your mind** to explain that **here too,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** a woman's discharge of menstrual blood **ceased** during her days of impurity, and she then experienced bleeding during her days of purity. Since she is still a *zava* at the beginning of her days of purity, the blood imparts impurity whether it is moist or dry.

The Gemara continues: *ta shma*, come and hear — *u'modim b'yoledes b'zova she'hi metama'ah lach v'yavesh*. Both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that when a woman gives birth while she is also a *zava* — that is, she was already in the process of counting *shiva nekiyim* when she gave birth — the blood she produces during *yemei tohar* is fully *tamei*, wet and dry.

The Gemara assumes this case involves a break in the bleeding (*salka datchach hacha nami d'paska*): she gave birth and is now in her *yemei tohar*, but she is simultaneously in a period of counting seven clean days as a *zava*. Blood she sees during this period is deemed *tamei* even though it falls within the 33 or 66 days.

### Zova Overlap Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב, דְּאָמַר: מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מְטַמֵּא לַח וְיָבֵשׁ. אֶלָּא לְלֵוִי, דְּאָמַר: שְׁנֵי מַעְיָנוֹת הֵן, אַמַּאי מְטַמֵּא לַח וְיָבֵשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: If so, **granted,** the mishna is clear **according to Rav, who said** pure and impure blood emanate from **one source,** as it is **due to that** reason that the blood **imparts impurity** whether it is **moist or dry,** since she is still a *zava* and the Torah has not yet deemed her pure. **But according to Levi, who said there are two sources, why** does the blood **impart impurity** whether it is **moist or dry?** Let it be considered like the saliva or urine of a *zava*, which imparts impurity only when moist.

The implications are immediate. *Bishlama leRav d'amar maayan echad hu* — according to Rav, who holds it is all one source and the *tahor* status is a Torah-assigned designation, it is perfectly coherent that the Torah would not apply its *tahor* designation when the woman simultaneously bears the status of a *zava* still counting *shiva nekiyim*. The Torah's decree of *tahor* blood was not issued for that situation.

### Levi's Zova Problem

But *ela leLevi d'amar shnei maayanos hen* — according to Levi, the blood during *yemei tohar* comes from a biologically different source. If so, why does it matter that she is also a *zava*? Blood from a different biological source ought to be *tahor* regardless of her *zava* status, just as blood from a cut finger would not affect her *zava* count. *Amai metamei lach v'yavesh?*

### Shofaat with Zova

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ: הָכָא נָמֵי — בְּשׁוֹפַעַת. אִי בְּשׁוֹפַעַת, לְמַאי אִיצְטְרִיךְ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Levi** could **say to you: Here too,** we are dealing **with** a woman who **continuously discharges** menstrual blood from within her days of impurity until her days of purity. The continuous flow of blood indicates that the blood emanates from the source of the impure blood. The Gemara asks: **If** the mishna is dealing **with** a woman who **continuously discharges** menstrual blood, **for what** purpose **was** it **necessary** for the mishna to teach this *halakha*? It is obvious that this blood is impure.

Levi again responds: *amar lach hacha nami b'shofaat* — again, it is a *shofaat* case. The blood was flowing continuously from before the transition into *yemei tohar*, demonstrating it is still coming from the *tamei* biological source.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אִיצְטְרִיךְ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָאָמְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא, וּבְיוֹמֵי תְּלָה רַחֲמָנָא — הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּיוֹלֶדֶת גְּרֵידְתָּא, דִּשְׁלִימוּ לְהוּ יוֹמֵי, אֲבָל יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב דְּבָעֵי סְפִירָה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that it **was necessary for** the opinion of **Beit Shammai.** The mishna is teaching that **even though Beit Shammai say** there **is one source, and the Merciful One rendered** the purity of her blood **dependent on days** alone, **that statement** applies only **in** the case of **a woman who only gave birth, as** her **days** of impurity **have been completed** at this point. **But** with regard to **a woman who gives birth as a *zava*, who requires** the **counting** of seven clean days from the end of her days of impurity, that statement does **not** apply, and blood emitted before she counted seven clean days imparts impurity whether moist or dry, like the blood of a menstruating woman.

The Gemara immediately asks: *ei b'shofaat l'mai itzterich* — if it is *shofaat*, why is the *zava* status even needed to make this *tamei*? It is *tamei* simply because it is flowing from the *tamei* source. The Gemara replies: *leBeit Shammai itzterich* — it is a *chiddush* specifically in relation to Beit Shammai. Since Levi had already conceded that Beit Shammai operates on the principle of one source (*maayan echad*), one might have thought that even according to Beit Shammai, the blood becomes automatically *tahor* upon entering day 8, as it is merely a Torah-assigned status that shifts with the day. The *chiddush* is that even Beit Shammai agrees that when a woman is also a *zava*, the *tahor* designation does not apply — even Beit Shammai holds that the Torah's *tahor* status for *yemei tohar* blood was not intended to override the *zava* counting period.

### Braita on Seven Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״דְּוֹתָהּ תִּטְמָא״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַיּוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב שֶׁצְּרִיכָה שֶׁתֵּשֵׁב שִׁבְעָה יָמִים נְקִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*, which addresses the verse: "If a woman be delivered, and bear a male, then she shall be impure seven days; as in the days of her menstrual sickness she shall be impure" (Leviticus 12:2). Furthermore, **"her** menstrual **sickness she shall be impure"** serves **to include a woman who gives birth as a *zava*,** teaching **that she must observe seven clean days.**

The Gemara introduces a *braita*: the verse *kimei niddas devosah tisma* — "as the days of her *niddah* flow, she shall be *tamei*" — is expounded in several ways, one of which is *l'rabos hayoledes b'zov*, to include the *yoledes b'zov*, a woman who gives birth while a *zava*, and to teach that *she'tzricha shiva neki'im* — she requires seven clean days.

### Status Shift Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב דְּאָמַר מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי בָּעֲיָא שִׁבְעָה יָמִים נְקִיִּים,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **Granted,** the *baraita* is clear **according to Rav, who said** there **is one source,** as it is **due to that** reason that a woman who gives birth as a *zava* **requires seven clean days.**

The Gemara presents the now-familiar challenge: *bishlama leRav d'amar maayan echad hu mishum hachi ba'ei shiva yamei neki'im* — according to Rav, we readily understand why she needs seven clean days even during *yemei tohar*, because the Torah's *tahor* designation does not apply when she is simultaneously a *zava* counting her days.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְלֵוִי דְּאָמַר שְׁנֵי מַעְיָנוֹת הֵן, לְמָה לִי שִׁבְעָה? בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ סַגִּיא!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Levi, who said there are two sources, why do I** need this woman to wait **seven** clean days? It should be **enough** for her to wait seven days after experiencing **any amount** of cleanliness from the conclusion of her days of impurity. After all, according to Levi, once the blood ceases to flow from her days of impurity, any blood emitted thereafter is from the source of pure blood. Consequently, even if she experiences a flow of blood during the seven clean days this should not negate her count.

But the question on Levi remains: if the blood is coming from a biologically different source, why does she need seven clean days at all? The Gemara responds on Levi's behalf: *hachi ka'amar* — the *braita* does not mean she needs seven literally blood-free days. Rather, *tzricha shetifsok mashu sheyalu la l'shiva neki'im* — she needs only that the continuous flow cease for a moment (*mashu*), after which the days can count toward *shiva neki'im* even if she is still bleeding, since once there has been a break, the subsequent bleeding is from the different, *tahor* source and does not disqualify the *nekiyim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: צְרִיכָה שֶׁתִּפְסוֹק מַשֶּׁהוּ, שֶׁיַּעֲלוּ לָהּ לְשִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: She requires that** the blood flow **should stop** for **any amount** of time after her days of impurity, so **that** she will have entered her days of purity, and consequently, the next seven days **will be considered for her as** the **seven clean** days required by a *zava*.

This is, as the Gemara acknowledges, a stretched reading of the *braita*. If Levi's position requires only a minimal break (*mashu*) to establish a new biological source, then the requirement articulated in the *braita* — *shiva neki'im* — collapses into something far less demanding than what the words suggest. The straightforward reading of *shiva neki'im* is seven days without blood; Levi is forced to reread it as seven days that can count even with blood, provided there was a brief interruption at some point. This is transparently a difficult position.

## Mesulekes Damim

### Months Combine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: יְמֵי עִיבּוּרָהּ עוֹלִים לָהּ לִימֵי מְנִיקוּתָהּ, וִימֵי מְנִיקוּתָהּ עוֹלִים לָהּ לִימֵי עִיבּוּרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: There are certain women with regard to whom the *halakha* is that if they notice an emission of menstrual blood they are deemed impure only from when they saw the blood, and one is not concerned they might have experienced the emission from an earlier time. Two such women are one who is pregnant and one who is nursing. This applies only to a pregnant woman who has been noticeably pregnant for three periods, each thirty days in length, and has not experienced bleeding during the three periods; and to a nursing woman who has been nursing for three periods, each also thirty days in length, and did not experience bleeding during that time. With regard to this the *baraita* teaches: **The days of her pregnancy** in which she saw no blood **count for her toward the days of her nursing** in which she saw no blood, to complete three periods, **and** likewise, **the days of her nursing count for her toward the days of her pregnancy.**

The Gemara brings another case: *yemei iburah olim la l'yemei minikuta* — the months of pregnancy count toward the months of nursing and vice versa for the purpose of establishing *mesulekes damim* status. This is the principle that a woman who has gone three *onot* without bleeding is considered *mesulekes damim*, meaning that the first time she subsequently bleeds, she need not be concerned retroactively for 24 hours. The Gemara specifies: if she did not bleed for two months during pregnancy and one month during nursing, or two during nursing and one during pregnancy, or one and a half from each — any combination reaching three *onot* establishes the *mesulekes damim* status.

### Rav's Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב דְּאָמַר: מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי בָּעֵי הֶפְסֵק שָׁלֹשׁ עוֹנוֹת,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **Granted,** the *baraita* is clear **according to Rav, who said there is one source,** as it is **due to that** reason that the woman **requires a cessation** of menstrual emissions for **three periods** in order that she may be considered a woman whose menstrual emissions have ceased.

The Gemara asks: *bishlama leRav d'amar maayan echad hu mishum hachi ba'ei hefsek shalosh onot* — according to Rav, it is clear why these months of non-bleeding are necessary. Even if she is in *yemei tohar* and any bleeding would be halachically *tahor*, the biological source is still active; she has not achieved true biological quiescence. *Mesulekes damim* is a biological concept: the source of blood has closed. If she is bleeding during *yemei tohar*, that bleeding is halachically *tahor* but biologically it still represents an active uterine source. Therefore, to establish *mesulekes damim* status, she genuinely needs to have stopped bleeding even during the *yemei tohar* period. The months that qualify for *mesulekes damim* must be months of actual non-bleeding, not merely months of *tahor* blood.

### Levi on Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְלֵוִי דְּאָמַר: שְׁנֵי מַעְיָנוֹת הֵן, לְמָה לִי הֶפְסֵק שָׁלֹשׁ עוֹנוֹת? בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ סַגִּי!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Levi, who said there are two sources, why do I** need **a cessation** of menstrual emissions for **three periods?** Provided the woman's menstrual emissions cease for **any amount** of time at the completion of her days of impurity, it should be **enough,** as any blood she may emit thereafter is pure and should not negate her count of three periods.

According to Levi, however, the blood during *yemei tohar* comes from a distinct biological source. If that is so, why does a woman need actual non-bleeding to establish *mesulekes damim*? Even if she bleeds during *yemei tohar*, it is coming from a different source — it is as though she never bled from the relevant source at all. She should be considered *mesulekes damim* regardless.

### Break = New Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: צְרִיכָה שֶׁתִּפְסוֹק מַשֶּׁהוּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּעֲלוּ לָהּ לְשָׁלֹשׁ עוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: She requires that** the blood flow **should stop** for **any amount** of time at the conclusion of her days of impurity, **in order that** her days of purity **will be counted for her as** part of the **three periods,** even if she experiences bleeding during her days of purity.

The Gemara resolves this in Levi's favor — but again with the same *mashu* formula: *hachi ka'amar*, the requirement is not strictly three months without any blood, but *tzericha shetifsok mashu kedei she-ya'alu lah shalosh onot* — she needs only a minimal break to establish that the subsequent bleeding is from the different *tahor* source. Once that is established, even bleeding during *yemei tohar* counts toward the three *onot* of *mesulekes damim*, because it is biologically from a source other than the one that ordinarily creates retroactive concern. As before, Levi's answer is technically coherent but requires a reading of the requirement that strips it of most of its intuitive force.

## Dayah Sha'atah

### Levi's Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וְשָׁוִין בְּרוֹאָה אַחַר דַּם טוֹהַר, שֶׁדַּיָּהּ שְׁעָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: **And** although Shammai and Hillel disagree with regard to all women who experience bleeding (see 2a), as Shammai maintains they are impure only from when they saw the blood while Hillel holds that they assume ritual impurity status retroactively from the last time they examined themselves and were found to be pure, **they agree with regard to** a woman who **sees** blood **after** seeing **blood** during her days **of purity, that it is sufficient for her** to be considered impure from the **hour** of **her** seeing the blood.

The Gemara now turns to what appears to be the one piece of evidence that genuinely supports Levi. *Ve-shavin be-ro'ah achar dam tohar she-dayah sha'atah* — everyone agrees that when a woman sees blood after the *yemei tohar* period (after day 40 for a boy, day 80 for a girl), *dayah sha'atah*: it is sufficient for her concern to begin from the moment of discovery; she need not be concerned retroactively for 24 hours. The case, as Rashi clarifies, is that she begins bleeding after day 40 or 80 — after the *yemei tohar* have concluded — and the question is whether she must be *choshesh* going backward 24 hours.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְלֵוִי דְּאָמַר: שְׁנֵי מַעְיָנוֹת הֵן, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי דַּיָּהּ שְׁעָתָהּ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **Granted,** the *baraita* is clear **according to Levi, who said there are two sources,** as it is **due to that** reason that **it is sufficient for her** to be considered impure from the **hour** of **her** seeing the blood.

*Bishlama deLevi* — according to Levi, this is entirely natural. During the 40 or 80 days, the normal uterine source (*maayan*) was closed; the *tahor* blood that flowed during *yemei tohar* came from elsewhere. Therefore, when she bleeds after day 40 or 80, it is as though she is beginning afresh from a source that had been dormant. She is essentially *mesulekes damim* in a biological sense, and consequently there is no basis to assume she was bleeding 24 hours earlier.

### Rav's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַב דְּאָמַר: מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא, אַמַּאי דַּיָּה שְׁעָתָהּ? תְּטַמֵּא מֵעֵת לְעֵת!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Rav, who said there is one source,** if this woman experienced bleeding during her days of purity, she cannot be considered a woman whose menstrual emissions have ceased. If so, **why is it sufficient for her** to be considered impure from the **hour** of **her** seeing the blood? **She should be** considered **impure** retroactively **for** a twenty-four-hour **period,** like all other women.

According to Rav, however, this is more difficult. Since Rav holds that it is one source throughout, and that the blood during *yemei tohar* was coming from the same uterine source — merely assigned a *tahor* status by the Torah — she was never biologically *mesulekes damim* during that period. If so, when she bleeds after day 40 or 80, she is simply a woman who has been bleeding all along from the same source. *Tipamei me'et l'et* — she should be required to be concerned 24 hours back, just like any woman who has never had a period of biological quiescence.

### Limited Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּלֵיכָּא שְׁהוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where there is no time** between the conclusion of her days of purity and the subsequent emission of blood. In such a situation, she cannot be considered impure retroactively for a twenty-four-hour period, as the previous twenty-four hours are part of her days of purity.

The Gemara answers on Rav's behalf: *deleika shahos* — there is no available span of time to retroactively implicate. The case is specifically that she began bleeding on day 41 or day 81. If she is told to be concerned 24 hours backward, that would carry her back into day 40 or day 80, which is still within *yemei tohar* — a period when even Rav agrees the blood was halachically *tahor*. Since the full 24-hour retroactive window cannot be applied (part of it falls within the *tohar* period), the Rabbinic requirement of retroactive concern simply does not engage at all. The rule is either 24 hours back or nothing; the Gemara does not allow for a partial retroactive concern of, say, 13 hours.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְטַמֵּא מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה! כֵּיוָן דִּמְעֵת לְעֵת לֵיכָּא, מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה נָמֵי לָא גְּזַרוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** still, why is she impure only from when she sees the blood? **She should be** considered **impure from examination to examination,** i.e., from the last time she examined herself and found that she was pure. The Gemara responds: **Since there is no** possibility of her being impure retroactively **for** a twenty-four-hour **period,** with regard to impurity **from examination to examination as well, the Sages did not decree** impurity **with regard to her.**

The Gemara then asks: but at minimum, should she not be *choshesh* back to the last time she checked (*mipikida lepikida*)? The Gemara answers: *keivan dem'et l'et leika mipikida lepikida lo* — since the primary concern of 24 hours cannot be applied, the secondary concern of *pekidah* likewise does not apply. They are treated as a single package.

### Rav's Answer

This resolution is, as noted, limited. The *dayah sha'atah* rule applies only because the scenario is specifically day 41 or day 81, not because of any underlying biological principle. The case that seemed to support Levi is thus explained by Rav through a procedural constraint. It is the one piece of evidence that *prima facie* appeared to favor Levi; once explained by Rav, it reduces to a technical limitation of retroactive *tumah* rather than to any statement about the biological reality of the sources.

## Pesak Le-Rav

### Yoledet BeZova

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב שֶׁסָּפְרָה וְלֹא טָבְלָה וְרָאֲתָה, הָלְכוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְשִׁיטָתָן, וּבֵית הִלֵּל לְשִׁיטָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: With regard to **a woman who gives birth as a *zava*, who counted** seven clean days after the conclusion of her days of impurity **but did not** yet **immerse, and** subsequently **saw** blood, **Beit Shammai follow their opinion** with regard to any woman who gave birth and concluded her days of impurity but did not yet immerse, **and Beit Hillel** likewise **follow their opinion.** In other words, according to Beit Shammai her blood imparts impurity only while moist, whereas according to Beit Hillel it imparts impurity whether it is moist or dry.

The Gemara presents one further case: *yoledes b'zov she'safra v'lo tavla v'ra'asa* — a woman who gave birth while a *zava*, who has counted her *shiva nekiyim* but has not yet immersed in the *mikvah*, and who now sees blood during *yemei tohar*. What is the status of this blood? The Gemara reports that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel each follow their respective positions: Beit Shammai says it is not full *dam tamei* as *niddah* or *yoledes* blood (though it may have a lesser degree of *tumah*), while Beit Hillel says it is fully *tamei*, since she has not immersed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב דְּאָמַר: מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מְטַמֵּא לַח וְיָבֵשׁ. אֶלָּא לְלֵוִי דְּאָמַר: שְׁנֵי מַעְיָנוֹת הֵן, אַמַּאי מְטַמֵּא לַח וְיָבֵשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **Granted,** the *baraita* is clear **according to Rav, who said there is one source,** as it is **due to that** reason Beit Hillel maintain that the blood **imparts impurity** whether it is **moist** or **dry.** In other words, as she has not yet immersed, the Torah does not deem pure her menstrual emissions. **But according to Levi, who said there are two sources,** any blood she may emit after counting seven clean days must have come from the source of pure blood. If so, **why does** it **impart impurity** whether it is **moist or dry?**

Again, Beit Hillel's insistence that the blood remains fully *tamei* absent *mikvah* immersion — even during *yemei tohar*, even after seven clean days — seems powerfully to indicate a status-based analysis, not a biological one.

### Status Not Biology

*Bishlama leRav d'amar maayan echad hu mishum hachi mitamei lach v'yavesh* — according to Rav, Beit Hillel's position is entirely coherent. It is one source, and the Torah-assigned *tahor* status has not yet taken effect because she has not gone to the *mikvah*. *Ela leLevi d'amar shenei maayanos hein, amai mitamei lach v'yavesh?* — but for Levi, if it is biologically a different source, why is it *tamei*? She has completed her seven clean days; she is not *shofaat*; the biological shift should have occurred. What grounds remain for treating it as *tamei*?

### Final Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ לֵוִי: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי — כְּתַנָּא דְּשָׁוִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Levi** could **say to you: I say** my opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **the *tanna*** of the *baraita* cited above, **that** Shammai and Hillel **agree** that with regard to a woman who experiences bleeding after experiencing bleeding during her days of purity, it is sufficient for her to be considered impure from the hour that she saw the blood. That *baraita* apparently indicates that there are in fact two sources.

Levi's first response is a frank concession: *amar lach Levi ana d'amri k'tanna d'shavyan* — I align myself with the *tanna* whose position supports mine, namely the *braita* about *dayah sha'atah* after day 40 or 80, which seemed to support a biological shift. You have your *braita* (the one from which Beit Hillel's ruling is derived); I have mine.

The Gemara then entertains a second response on Levi's behalf: *v'iba'it eima b'shofaat* — perhaps even here it is a case of *shofaat*. But the Gemara objects: *v'ha safra katani* — the case explicitly states she counted seven clean days; how can it be a continuous flow? The Gemara constructs an elaborate answer:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּיוֹלֶדֶת נְקֵבָה בְּזוֹב עָסְקִינַן, דְּשָׁבוּעַ קַמָּא פָּסְקָה, שָׁבוּעַ בָּתְרָא לָא פָּסְקָה, וְקָסָבַר: יְמֵי לֵידָתָהּ שֶׁאֵין רוֹאֶה בָּהֶן — עוֹלִין לָהּ לִסְפִירַת זִיבָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Here we are dealing with a woman who gave birth to a female as a *zava*,** and therefore her days of impurity are two weeks in length. And this is a case **where** in the **first week** her menstrual emissions **ceased,** and in the **latter week** her menstrual emissions **did not cease,** and they continued until her days of purity. **And** the *tanna* of this *baraita* **holds: The days of her birth,** i.e., the days of impurity, **in which she does not see** blood, **count for her toward the counting of her *ziva*.** Accordingly, although she is considered to have counted seven clean days, the blood emitted during her days of purity is from the source of impure blood.

*hacha b'yoledes nekeiva b'zov askinan* — this is the case of a woman who gave birth to a girl (14 days of *tumah*), who was a *zava*, and during the first of those two weeks there was no blood (which counted as her *shiva nekiyim* for the *zava* status — a position contingent on the view that *yemei leidah she'einah roah bahen olin lah lisfirat zivatah*, that days of the childbirth period without blood count toward the *zava* clean-day count). Then, during the second week, she began bleeding continuously into the *yemei tohar*, making her a *shofaat*. In this contrived scenario, she has seven clean days and yet is *shofaat*. This is Levi's last resort.

The discussion of *yemei leidah she'einah roah bahen* carries some contemporary relevance: since today all women follow the stringency of a *zava*, the question of whether *yemei leidah* without blood can count toward *shiva nekiyim* arises in practice particularly after a miscarriage, which halachically constitutes a *leidah*. There are halachic consequences for *harchakot* and other matters even when the couple cannot yet resume relations.

### Ravina and Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אָמַר לַן רַב שֶׁמֶן מִסִּכְרָא: אִקְּלַע מָר זוּטְרָא לְאַתְרִין, וּדְרַשׁ: הִילְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב לְחוּמְרָא, וְהִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּלֵוִי לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the dispute between Rav and Levi, **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Rav Shemen from Sikhra said to us: Mar Zutra happened to** come to **our locale, and he taught** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav as a stringency, and the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion **of Levi as a stringency.** In other words, if a woman continuously discharges menstrual blood from within her days of purity until sometime after the conclusion of her days of purity, the blood emitted after her days of purity is impure, in accordance with the opinion of Rav. By contrast, if she continuously discharges menstrual blood from within her days of impurity into her days of purity, the blood emitted during her days of purity is impure, in accordance with the opinion of Levi.

Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Rav Shaman from Sachra reported that Mar Zutra came to their place and ruled: *hilchata kevata deRav le-chumra ve-hilchata kevata deLevi le-chumra* — we adopt each position in whatever direction produces stringency, since we do not know how to definitively rule between them.

### Hilchata KeRav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב, בֵּין לְקוּלָּא בֵּין לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav, whether as a leniency or as a stringency.**

Rav Ashi responded: *hilchata kevata deRav bein le-kula bein le-chumra* — the halacha follows Rav consistently, whether in the lenient or the stringent direction. Mareimar likewise ruled that we follow Rav in both directions.

The final ruling is: *ve-hilchata kevata deRav bein le-kula bein le-chumra* — we rule like Rav consistently. This conclusion is well-supported by the Gemara's trajectory: virtually every case that the Gemara examined aligned naturally with Rav's position, while Levi was forced to limit his answers to *shofaat* scenarios that were not mentioned in any of the texts, and the very notion of a separate biological source is difficult to conceptualize. Classical halachic thinking understands these differences as halachic-status shifts decreed by the Torah, not as empirical biological claims.

### Tosafot on Chumrot

One final observation is warranted regarding the approach of Mar Zutra — ruling *le-chumra* in both directions simultaneously. Tosafot raises an objection: *ve-im tomar ha terei chumrei de-satri ahadadi ninhu* — is one not prohibited from adopting two self-contradictory stringencies? The two positions here — one source versus two sources — are mutually exclusive; one cannot simultaneously hold that the blood is halachically shifted in status *and* that it is biologically from a different source, at least not when deriving practical consequences in opposite directions. Tosafot answers: *yesh lomar de-hacha di-mesapka lan paska ke-mar le-hachmir u-ke-mar le-hachmir* — where the ruling in both directions is not a genuine *pesak* endorsing either position as true, but rather reflects uncertainty (*safek*), one may indeed adopt both stringencies even if they are logically contradictory. This resolution, Tosafot acknowledges, characterizes much of practical *pesak halachah* more broadly: when the posek cannot reach a conclusion, he plays it safe in all directions, even inconsistently. The further question of whether this represents genuine *safek* or an unwillingness to take halachic responsibility is left open.

## Kisha Pains Exception

### Niddah During Kisha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הַמְקַשָּׁה — נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a pregnant woman **who experiences** labor **pains,** and they are accompanied by an emission of blood**,** her status is that of **a menstruating woman.**

The *mishnah* now introduces the law of labor pains: *ha-maksheh niddah*. A woman who has labor pains (*kishui*) and bleeds during her *niddah* period is a *niddah*. This requires no elaboration: the general rule is that even *dam machmat ones* — bleeding caused by external forces — does not exempt a woman from *niddah* or *zava* status. Labor pains do not change this for the *niddah* period.

### Kisha Negates Zava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִשְּׁתָה שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים בְּתוֹךְ אַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם, וְשָׁפְתָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת, וְיָלְדָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** If **she experienced** these **pains** accompanied by emissions of blood for **three** consecutive **days within the eleven days** between periods of menstruation**,** during which time emissions of blood render a woman a *zava*, **and she rested** from labor **for** a twenty-four-hour **period, i.e.,** the pangs subsided**, and** she then **gave birth,** it indicates that the emissions were not due to her imminent labor**,** and **this** woman **is** considered **one who gives birth as a *zava*.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.**

There is, however, one exception to the rule that *ones* does not affect *tumah* status: a woman who bleeds during her *zava* period as a result of continuous labor pains leading up to childbirth is not thereby made a *zava*. The bleeding is attributed to the impending birth rather than to an independent uterine discharge.

### Three Days Rule

The *mishnah* implies (though does not state it in those terms) that if a woman bleeds for three days during her 11-day *zava* period (*betoch yud-alef yom*) while experiencing labor pains, this would ordinarily constitute *zivah*. But if those pains are continuous and connected to the birth, the bleeding is not *dam zivah*.

### Break Determines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לַיְלָה וָיוֹם כְּלִילֵי שַׁבָּת וְיוֹמוֹ, שֶׁשָּׁפְתָה מִן הַצַּעַר וְלֹא מִן הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** She is considered a *zava* only if the pangs subsided for a twenty-four-hour period of **a night and** the following **day, like Shabbat evening and its** accompanying **day.** Additionally, she is considered a *zava* in a case **where she rested from the pain** of labor **but not** necessarily **from the** flow of **blood.** In other words, the presence of labor pangs determines whether this blood is due to labor or the impure blood of *ziva*. Accordingly, if the pangs cease for twenty-four hours, she is considered a *zava* even if blood was discharging continuously from when she experienced her labor pains.

The critical variable is whether the labor pains were continuous all the way up to childbirth. *Ve-shafsah me'eis le'eis veyaldah harei zu yoledes bazov* — if there was a 24-hour break (*shafsa me'eis le'eis*) in the pains between the bleeding and the birth, she is a *yoledes bazov*. This is the ruling of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees: not 24 hours (*me'et le'et*), but a full halachic day — a night-and-day unit as measured like the night and day of Shabbat — is required to constitute an interruption. The difference is that 24 hours is a floating period, while a halachic day is anchored to nightfall; a full halachic day will almost always be longer than 24 hours.

The *mishnah* also specifies: *sheshafsah min hatza'ar velo min hadam* — what constitutes an interruption is a break in the *pains*, not merely a break in the bleeding. This is the operative criterion.

### Duration Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה הִיא קִישּׁוּיָהּ? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אַרְבָּעִים וַחֲמִשִּׁים יוֹם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: דַּיָּהּ חׇדְשָׁהּ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: אֵין קִישּׁוּי יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **How long** before birth **is** pain attributable to **her** labor **pains,** which means that the blood is not considered blood of *ziva*? **Rabbi Meir says: Even forty or fifty days** before the birth, any blood she sees during the eleven days of *ziva* is not considered blood of *ziva*. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is **sufficient** that this *halakha* applies only from within one **month** of **her** due date. **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say:** Labor **pains** do **not** occur **more than two weeks** before birth. Accordingly, if she experiences bleeding for three consecutive days during eleven days of *ziva* that occur before this time, she is a *zava*.

How far back from the birth can the *kishui* pains extend and still be attributed to the birth, thus exempting the bleeding from *zivah* status? Rabbi Meir says there is no limit — even 40 or 50 days of continuous labor pains prior to birth suffice. Rabbi Yehuda says only within the preceding month. Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon say no more than two weeks prior to birth.

The determination is made retroactively: once the child is born, one looks back to see whether the bleeding fell within the operative window and whether the pains were continuous throughout. A woman experiencing bleeding with labor pains during pregnancy would track each day to verify continuous pain, and only after birth can the full picture be assessed.

## Status During Labor

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ כׇּל הַמְקַשָּׁה נִדָּה הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that a pregnant woman who experiences an emission of menstrual blood due to labor pains is considered a menstruating woman. This statement apparently applies to all blood due to labor. The Gemara therefore asks: **Is this to say that any** woman **who experiences** an emission due to labor **pains is a menstruating woman?** But if a woman experiences an emission due to labor pains in the eleven days of *ziva*, she is not rendered a *zava*, nor can this render her a menstruating woman.

The Gemara asks: *atu kol hamakshah niddah hi* — is every woman who has labor pains automatically a *niddah*? Surely the *mishnah* cannot mean that labor pains alone, without specifying the period of the cycle, determine status. The question leads into a discussion of what a woman's status is in the interim — before it is known whether the pains will be continuous through the birth or whether there will be a break.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: נִדָּה לְיוֹמָא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא תִּשְׁפֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said:** It is correct that a woman who experiences an emission of blood due to labor during the eleven days of *ziva* is not rendered a *zava*, or even a lesser *zava*, who must observe one clean day for the day she experienced an emission. But she is **a menstruating woman for** one **day,** i.e., she is prohibited to her husband on the day of the emission, and in the evening she may immerse and become permitted to him. **And Shmuel said:** It is a rabbinic decree that she must observe a clean day, as **we are concerned lest she rest** from labor and thereby be rendered a lesser *zava*, as the cessation of pangs would indicate that her emission was not due to her imminent labor.

Rav answers: *niddah leyoma* — during the day she bleeds, treat her as a *niddah* (meaning, marital relations are prohibited on that day), but by nightfall relations may resume. On Rav's reading, the presumptive status is that the labor pains are efficacious — we assume continuity — and therefore, while it would be inappropriate to engage in relations on the day of bleeding, by nightfall we already assume the best.

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel disagrees: *chaishinan shema tishpeh* — we are concerned that there may be a break in the pains. *Tishpeh* means the pains will relax, producing a *shofi* that would interrupt the continuity. Since there is prospective concern that a break may yet occur and she will turn out to have been a *zava*, Shmuel holds we cannot yet permit relations. The debate between Rav and Shmuel is essentially: do we presume continuity and permit, or do we remain concerned about future interruption and prohibit? Rav adopts the presumption of continuity; Shmuel requires demonstrated continuity before permitting.

### Rabbi Yitzchak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר: הַמְקַשָּׁה אֵינָהּ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** A woman **who experiences** labor **pains** during the eleven days of *ziva* **is nothing,** i.e., she is completely pure and is permitted to her husband.

Rabbi Yitzchak goes further than Rav: *hamakshah einah klum* — labor pains during pregnancy amount to nothing halachically; even relations on that day itself are permitted. In principle, this is the same direction as Rav — assuming that the pain-induced bleeding is not problematic — but Rabbi Yitzchak does not even maintain the restriction for the day itself.

### Rava's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בִּימֵי נִדָּה — נִדָּה, בִּימֵי זִיבָה — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The mishna means that if she experienced an emission due to labor **during the days of menstruation,** she is considered **a menstruating woman;** if she experienced the emission **during the days of *ziva*,** she is **pure.**

Rava resolves the *mishnah*'s apparent ambiguity straightforwardly: *bimei niddah niddah bimei ziva tehora* — when the bleeding occurs during her *niddah* period, she is a *niddah* without qualification, and the *koshi* rule is irrelevant. When it occurs during her *ziva* period (the 11 days), she may be *tehora* if the pains are continuous. This clarifies that the *mishnah*'s statement that a woman with labor pains is a *niddah* refers specifically to the *niddah* period; it is not a universal statement about the effect of labor pains on all bleeding.

## Pain Patterns

### Ending with Pain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? קִשְּׁתָה יוֹם אֶחָד, וְשָׁפְתָה שְׁנַיִם, אוֹ שֶׁקִּשְּׁתָה שְׁנַיִם וְשָׁפְתָה יוֹם אֶחָד, אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁפְתָה וְקִשְּׁתָה וְחָזְרָה וְשָׁפְתָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **How** so, i.e., when does an emission during the eleven days of *ziva* render a woman one who gives birth as a *zava*? If **she experienced** labor **pains** for **one day and** then **rested** from labor for **two** days, and she experienced an emission on all three days; **or** if **she experienced** labor **pains** for **two** days **and rested** from labor for **one day,** and she experienced bleeding on all three days; **or** if **she rested** from labor for one day **and she** then **experienced** labor **pains** for one day, **and again rested** from labor for one day, and she experienced bleeding on all three days; in each of these cases **this** woman **is** considered **one who gives birth as a *zava*,** as the cessation of labor pains indicates that the emission of blood is not due to her imminent labor.

The Gemara introduces a *braita* that analyzes specific combinations of bleeding with and without labor pains. If a woman had one day of pain-accompanied bleeding and two days without pains, or two days with pain and one without, or alternating periods ending with a pain-free period (*shaftah ve-kashtah ve-chazarah ve-shaftah*), she is a *yoledes bazov* — because the final period, immediately preceding birth, was without labor pains.

### Ending with Break

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל שָׁפְתָה יוֹם אֶחָד וְקִשְּׁתָה שְׁנַיִם, אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁפְתָה שְׁנַיִם וְקִשְּׁתָה יוֹם אֶחָד, אוֹ שֶׁקִּשְּׁתָה וְשָׁפְתָה וְחָזְרָה וְקִשְּׁתָה — אֵין זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב. כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: קוֹשִׁי סָמוּךְ לַלֵּידָה — אֵין זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב, שׁוֹפִי סָמוּךְ לַלֵּידָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if she **rested** from labor for **one day and** then **experienced** labor **pains** for **two** days; **or** if **she rested** from labor for **two** days **and experienced** labor **pains** for **one day; or** if **she experienced** labor **pains** for one day **and** then **rested** from labor for one day, **and again experienced** labor **pains** for one day; **this** woman **is not** considered **one who gives birth as a *zava*,** since the labor pains indicate that the blood is due to her imminent labor. This is **the principle of the matter:** If she experienced labor **pains adjacent to** giving **birth, this** woman **is not** considered **one who gives birth as a *zava*;** if she was **resting** from labor **adjacent to** giving **birth, this** woman **is** considered **one who gives birth as a *zava*.**

Conversely: if she had one day without pain followed by two with pains, or two without and one with, or the reverse alternating pattern ending with pain (*kashtah ve-shaftah ve-chazarah ve-kashtah*), she is *ein zo yoledes bazov* — not a *yoledes bazov*.

The case of *kashtah ve-shaftah ve-chazarah ve-kashtah* is worth examining carefully. Read straightforwardly: one day with pain, one day without, one day with pain, then birth. In this pattern, the middle day (without pain) creates a break that severs the first pain-day from the sequence, making it potentially *dam zivah*. The second and third pain-days, however, remain connected to the birth. The net effect is that only one or two of the three days of bleeding can be attributed to *zivah*, not three — and without three, she is not a *zava* and therefore not a *yoledes bazov*.

### Koshi Negates

The general principle the *braita* articulates: *koshi samuch le-leidah ein zo yoledes bazov* — any period of labor pains leading immediately up to birth is not *yoledes bazov*.

### Shofi Before

*Shofi samuch le-leidah harei zo yoledes bazov* — conversely, a period without pains immediately preceding birth renders her *yoledes bazov*, because it severs all prior bleeding from attribution to the birth, leaving it potentially as *dam zivah*.

### Halachic Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן אֲחִי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁחָל קִישּׁוּיָהּ בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי שֶׁלָּהּ, אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ בְּשׁוֹפִי — אֵין זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥananya, son of Rabbi Yehoshua's brother, says:** In **any** situation **where** she experiences an emission due to **her** labor **pains** that **occur on her third day,** she is not a *zava*. **Even** if she was in a state **of resting** from labor for **the entire** third **day,** with the exception of one hour of labor pains, **this** woman **is not** considered **one who gives birth as a *zava*,** since a woman is considered to be resting from labor on the third day only if she was resting for the entire day.

Chananya ben achi Rabbi Yehoshua adds: as long as the pains occurred on the third day, even if the entire rest of that day was a *shofi* — a pain-free period — she is not *yoledes bazov*. This position resonates with the earlier view in the *mishnah* that a *halachic day* (night and day together) constitutes the meaningful unit; even a full day of *shofi* does not necessarily sever the connection if the third day itself included some *koshi*, and the birth follows the next day. The principle here is that the halachic day, not a floating 24-hour period, is the operative unit.

## Derasha on Damah

### Damah Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״דָּמָהּ״ — דָּמָהּ מֵחֲמַת עַצְמָהּ, וְלֹא מֵחֲמַת וָלָד.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a woman experiences bleeding due to labor pains during the eleven days of *ziva* she is not considered a *zava*. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a *zava*: "And if a woman has an issue of her blood many days" (Leviticus 15:25). The term **"her blood"** indicates that only **her blood** that comes **due to herself** is impure as blood of *ziva*, **but not** blood that comes **due to** her **child.**

The Gemara now asks *mana hani mili* — what is the scriptural source for these rules? The Gemara quotes a *braita* expounding the verse *zov damah* (Vayikra 15:19) — "the flow of her blood." The word *damah* — "her blood," or more precisely, "her flow" — is taken as indicative: it must be *her* discharge, belonging to her as the source. *Damah machmat atzmah v'lo machmat velad* — the *tumah*-producing flow must come from the woman herself, not be attributable to the fetus.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר מֵחֲמַת וָלָד, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מֵחֲמַת אוֹנֶס? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְאִשָּׁה כִּי יָזוּב זוֹב דָּמָהּ״, הֲרֵי אוֹנֶס אָמוּר. הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״דָּמָהּ״? דָּמָהּ מֵחֲמַת עַצְמָהּ, וְלֹא מֵחֲמַת וָלָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **Do you say** the verse excludes blood that comes **due to** her **child? Or** is it **only** excluding blood that comes **due to circumstances beyond** her **control? When** the verse **states: "And if a woman has an issue of her blood,"** the inclusion of *ziva* due to **circumstances beyond** her **control is** already **stated** in the inclusive phrase: Has an issue. If so, **how do I realize** the meaning of the term **"her blood"?** The verse is teaching that only **her blood** that comes **due to herself** is impure as blood of *ziva*, **but not** blood that comes **due to** her **child.**

One might have thought the exclusion was broader — perhaps any bleeding caused by external forces (*ones*) should be *tahor*. To exclude this reading, the Gemara notes: *keshehu omer isha ki yazuv zov* — the verse "when a woman flows a flow" is phrased inclusively, and the additional word *damah* following it teaches: *zuv zuv damah harei ones amur* — even an involuntary (*ones*) discharge is included. Thus *ones* in general does not exempt a woman from *niddah* or *zava* status.

What, then, does *damah* exclude? *Damah machmat atzmah v'lo machmat velad* — only bleeding whose cause is specifically the fetus is excluded. That narrower exclusion is what *damah* teaches.

### Fetus Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְטַהֵר אֶת הַוָּלָד וּלְטַמֵּא בְּאוֹנֶס? מְטַהֵר אֲנִי בַּוָּלָד — שֶׁיֵּשׁ טׇהֳרָה אַחֲרָיו, וּמְטַמֵּא אֲנִי בְּאוֹנֶס — שֶׁאֵין טׇהֳרָה אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** given that the verse contains both an inclusion and an exclusion, **what did you see to deem pure** the blood emitted due to **the child, and to deem impure** the blood she sees **due to circumstances beyond** her **control?** Perhaps the opposite is the case, that blood emitted due to circumstances beyond her control is pure, whereas blood caused by labor is impure. The Gemara answers: **I deem pure** the blood emitted **due to the child, as there is** a period of **purity after it,** i.e., her days of purity following her days of impurity; **and I deem impure** the blood she sees **due to circumstances beyond** her **control, as there is no** period of **purity after it.**

Why then is the fetus-caused bleeding excluded but not other forms of *ones*? This requires justification, since one has just established that *ones* in general is included.

### Ones Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מְטַהֵר אֲנִי בְּאוֹנֶס, שֶׁכֵּן אוֹנֶס בְּזָב טָהוֹר! הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא בְּאִשָּׁה קָיְימִינַן, וְאוֹנֶס בְּאִשָּׁה לָא אַשְׁכְּחַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **On the contrary, I** should **deem** the blood **pure in** a case where it was emitted due to **circumstances beyond** her **control, as** an emission of *ziva* due **to circumstances beyond** one's **control** in the case **of** a male ***zav* is pure.** The Gemara explains: **Now, in any event, we are dealing with** the *halakha* of **a woman, and in** the case of **a woman we do not find** that an emission caused by **circumstances beyond** her **control** is pure. It is therefore preferable to deem impure an emission caused by circumstances beyond her control, and to deem pure an emission due to childbirth.

The Gemara anticipates the question: perhaps we should reason by analogy from a *zav* (a man with abnormal discharge). For a *zav*, external causes — certain foods, physical exertion, and other factors — do exempt him from *zav* status; *ones* by a *zav* is *tahor*. If so, perhaps we should apply the same principle to a *zava* by analogy?

### Subset Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ לְטַהוֹרֵי בְּאוֹנֶס, וּלְטַמּוֹיֵי בְּוָלָד? אֵין לָךְ אוֹנֶס גָּדוֹל מִזֶּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And if you wish, say** instead: **What is your opinion, that** one should **deem** the blood **pure in** a case where it was emitted due to **circumstances beyond** her **control, and deem** the blood **impure** in a case where it was emitted **due to the child?** But **you have no greater** instance of **circumstances beyond** her **control than this,** the experience of labor pains.

The Gemara rejects the analogy from *zav* to *zava*: *hashta maya b'isha kayminan* — we are dealing with the laws of women, not men. *V'ones b'isha lo ashkhachun* — we have found no precedent for *ones* producing *taharah* by a woman, except in the specific context of childbirth. The Torah introduced *yemei tohar* — the 33 and 66 days of *tahor* blood — precisely in connection with childbirth. That Torah-originated concept of *taharah* associated with birth and the fetus is what grounds the exclusion.

Alternatively (*v'i ba'it eima*): even if one wanted to say that *ones* in general is *tahor* and childbirth-related bleeding is *tamei*, this would be logically untenable — *ein lach ones gadol mizeh*, for childbirth is itself a paradigmatic case of *ones*, a prime example of an involuntary external force. One cannot exclude the most obvious member of a category while claiming to include the category. What one *can* say is: childbirth is a specific *ones* that the Torah chose to treat as *tahor*, while all lesser forms of *ones* remain *tamei*. The direction of the exclusion must go from the more severe to the less severe, not the reverse.
