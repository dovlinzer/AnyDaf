# Bava Metzia 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dross & Lees

### Pure Oil Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה שֶׁמֶן מְזוּקָּק אֵינוֹ יוֹצִיא לוֹ שְׁמָרִים וְכוּ׳. וְהָא אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא בָּלַע! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בִּמְזוּפָּפִין שָׁנוּ. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְזוּפָּפִין, כֵּיוָן דִּטְעוּן – טְעוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **If it was refined oil, he does not deduct** any of the oil **for sediment.** If they were stored in old casks that are already saturated, he does not deduct any of the oil for absorption. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it impossible that** the cask **did not absorb** any oil at all, even if it was saturated? **Rav Naḥman says:** It is **with regard to** casks **coated with pitch** that the *tanna'im* **taught** the mishna, and if the cask is old and coated with pitch it does not absorb anything. **Abaye said: Even if you say that** the mishna is **not** referring to casks **coated with pitch, once they are saturated they are saturated,** and no more oil is absorbed.

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion about a *shomer* — one who watches another's property — and the question of what deductions he may legitimately make when returning the item. The Mishnah addresses, among other things, the case of oil: if the owner had entrusted *shemen mezukkak*, pure oil from which the lees had already been removed, the *shomer* may not deduct any quantity of *shmarim* when he returns it, since there were none to begin with.

The Gemara, however, presses a related case: what of the principle that a vessel always absorbs some of whatever is stored in it? The Mishnah states that if the storage vessels were already saturated — having previously absorbed their fill of wine or oil — the *shomer* may not deduct the amount that would normally be absorbed by the walls of the vessel, since the vessel, being already fully laden, cannot take in any more. The Gemara objects: *v'ha i efshar d'lo bala* — is it truly possible that a vessel absorbs nothing whatsoever? Rav Nachman resolves this: *b'mizufafim shanu* — the Mishnah is speaking of vessels that are both aged and plastered on the interior; the combination of those two conditions prevents any further absorption. Abaye offers an alternative: even without interior plastering, once a vessel is genuinely saturated — *keivan d't'on t'on* — it is laden and will not absorb more, and the Mishnah's ruling stands.

### R. Yehuda View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמּוֹכֵר שֶׁמֶן מְזוּקָּק לַחֲבֵירוֹ כׇּל יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, הֲרֵי זֶה מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו לוֹג וּמֶחֱצָה שְׁמָרִים לְמֵאָה. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּשֶׁתִּמְצָא לוֹמַר, לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוּתָּר לְעָרֵב שְׁמָרִים, לְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים אָסוּר לְעָרֵב שְׁמָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** in the case of **one who sells refined oil to another all the days of the year, this** buyer **accepts upon himself** that the seller will deduct **a *log* and a half of sediment for one hundred** *log*, as that is the standard measure of sediment. **Abaye said: When** you analyze the matter, **you will find** it necessary **to say** that **according to the statement of Rabbi Yehuda,** it is **permitted to mix sediment** that settled at the bottom of the barrel with the clear oil and sell the mixture. **And according to the statement of the Rabbis,** it is **prohibited to mix sediment** with the clear oil.

The Mishnah then cites Rabbi Yehuda: if a merchant agreed to sell *shemen mezukkak* to a customer over the course of the year — meaning the buyer would take the oil little by little, drawing against an agreed quantity — the seller is entitled to deduct one and a half *log* per one hundred *log*, representing the *shmarim* that would naturally have settled at the bottom. The reasoning is intuitive: had the buyer taken the entire quantity at once, he would have received the full barrel, lees and all. The pure oil he is receiving piecemeal is slightly less than the nominal agreed amount, but this reflects what the actual net content would have been.

Abaye draws out the underlying logic: *l'divrei Rabbi Yehuda muttar l'arvei shmarim* — according to Rabbi Yehuda, it is permissible for a seller to mix the lees back into the oil when selling in bulk. The seller can therefore say to the buyer: had I wanted, I could have sold you a full hundred units with the lees mixed in; instead I filtered it and gave you ninety-eight and a half units of pure oil with one and a half units of lees on the side. You receive your full entitlement — just in separated form.

### Waiver Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוּתָּר לְעָרֵב שְׁמָרִים, וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִּמְקַבֵּל, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי בְּעַי לְעָרוֹבֵי לָךְ – מִי לָא עָרֵבִי לָךְ? הַשְׁתָּא נָמוּ קַבֵּיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates. **According to the statement of Rabbi Yehuda,** it is **permitted to mix sediment, and that is the reason that** the buyer **accepts** upon himself that the seller will deduct a *log* and a half of sediment for one hundred *log*, **as** the seller **says to him: If I wished to mix** sediment and sell it **to you, couldn't I mix** it and sell it **to you? Now too, accept** upon yourself the deduction due to sediment.

A counterargument is raised: why can't the buyer say to the seller — *midelo arvat li acholei achalta li* — since you chose to give it to me pure, you have implicitly waived your right to include the lees? I should read your act of giving me clean oil as an implicit *mechila*, a relinquishing of your entitlement to sell it with the dross. If you had mixed it, I could have resold a full hundred units on the market. By separating it, you have in practice reduced my ability to resell — so your choice of filtering amounts to a concession that I receive the full quantity in pure form.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: אִי עָרֵבְתְּ לֵיהּ – הֲוָה מִזְדַּבַּן לִי, הַשְׁתָּא מַאי אֶעֱבֵיד לֵיהּ? לְחוֹדֵיהּ לָא מִזְדַּבַּן לִי! בְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת עָסְקִינַן – דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּצִילָא. וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: מִדְּלָא עָרֵבִית לִי – אַחוֹלֵי אַחֵלְתְּ לִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** the buyer **say to him: If you had mixed** sediment **into** the oil, **it** could **have been sold for me** to another. **Now what will I do with it?** The sediment **cannot be sold on its own,** and I will suffer a loss. The Gemara answers: **We are dealing with** a buyer who is **a homeowner,** not a merchant. He needs oil for his own use, **and filtered** oil **is preferable for him,** as his use of the oil is facilitated by removal of the sediment. The Gemara asks: **And let** the buyer **say to him: From** the fact **that you did not mix** the sediment with the oil **for me,** it is an indication that **you renounced** your rights to **it to me.**

This is, as the Gemara notes, the position that the Gemara itself briefly entertains. The buyer was a homeowner rather than a reseller, *bevaal habayit askinan*, and he actually preferred the oil clean. But the *mechila* argument remains pressing regardless: if you could have, but chose not to, why doesn't that act of goodwill bind you to deliver the full amount?

### Price Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ מְחִילָה. דִּתְנַן: מָכַר לוֹ אֶת הַצֶּמֶד – לֹא מָכַר לוֹ אֶת הַבָּקָר. מָכַר לוֹ אֶת הַבָּקָר – לֹא מָכַר לוֹ אֶת הַצֶּמֶד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yehuda** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he is not** of the opinion that one can presume **renunciation,** and therefore the buyer cannot presume that the seller renounced his right to receive the standard price, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 77b): If **one sold the yoke [*tzemed*] to** another, **he did not sell the cattle to him.** Literally, *tzemed* means the yoke that holds the animals together [*tzamud*] while plowing. It can be understood as referring to the two animals held together by the yoke. If **one sold the cattle to** another, **he did not sell the yoke to him.** The sale is limited to the literal meaning of what he said.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַדָּמִים מוֹדִיעִין. כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: מְכוֹר לִי צִמְדְּךָ בְּמָאתַיִם זוּז – הַדָּבָר יָדוּעַ שֶׁאֵין הַצֶּמֶד בְּמָאתַיִים זוּז. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הַדָּמִים רְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Yehuda says: The money informs** the scope of the sale. Based on the price, one can determine what is included in the sale. **How so?** If the buyer **said to** the seller: **Sell me your *tzemed* for two hundred dinars, the matter is well-known that a yoke** does **not** cost **two hundred dinars,** and he certainly meant the cattle. **And the Rabbis say: The money is not a proof,** as it is possible that one of the parties renounced part of the sale price.

The Gemara resolves this by appealing to Rabbi Yehuda's broader jurisprudential principle: *hadamim modiyim* — the price paid reveals the intent of the transaction. Rabbi Yehuda does not hold that we infer *mechila* from generous conduct; rather, we interpret every term of a transaction consistently with the price. This is derived from the case of the *tzemed*: if someone says "sell me your yoke for two hundred *zuz*," it is obvious he meant the two oxen, not the wooden beam, because no one pays two hundred *zuz* for a yoke. *Hadavar yadu'a she'ein hatzemed b'masayim zuz* — the price itself tells you what was transacted. The Rabbis (*chachamim*) disagree, holding that price is not determinative: *ein hadamim ra'ayah*. But Rabbi Yehuda insists that price and contractual terms must be read together, and therefore the transaction is never interpreted as a waiver of the seller's right to account for *shmarim*.

### R. Pappa Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא! לְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים מוּתָּר לְעָרֵב שְׁמָרִים, וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלָא מְקַבֵּל – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדְּלָא עָרֵבְתְּ לִי, אַחוֹלֵי אַחֵלְיתְּ לִי. לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אָסוּר לְעָרֵב שְׁמָרִים, וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִּמְקַבֵּל – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי בְּעַי לְעָרוֹבֵי – לָא שְׁרֵי לִי לְעָרוֹבֵי לָךְ, קַבּוֹלֵי לָא מְקַבְּלַתְּ. זְבוֹן וְזַבֵּין תַּגָּרָא אִיקְּרִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: On the contrary, the opposite is reasonable. According to the statement of the Rabbis,** it is **permitted to mix sediment. And this is the reason** that the buyer **does not accept** the deduction, **as** the buyer **said to** the seller: **From** the fact **that you did not mix** the sediment **for me,** apparently **you renounced** that sum **to me. According to the statement of Rabbi Yehuda,** it is **prohibited to mix sediment. And this is the reason** that the buyer **accepts** the deduction, **as** the seller **says to him: If I wished to mix** sediment, **it is prohibited for me to mix** it **for you,** and if you **do not accept** the deduction, I earn nothing from this sale. That is unacceptable according to the maxim: One who **buys and sells** at the same price, is **he called a merchant?**

Rav Pappa challenges Abaye's presentation with a striking reversal: *ipkha misstabra* — the opposite makes more sense. According to the *chachamim*, he argues, it is permissible to mix the lees into the oil when selling in bulk. And that is precisely why the buyer need not accept the one and a half units of dross: because the seller *could* have sold it to him mixed, but chose to give it pure — *acholei achalta li* — you waived your right. The buyer can legitimately read the seller's act of filtering as an implicit concession to deliver a full hundred units of pure oil.

Conversely, according to Rabbi Yehuda, mixing the lees into oil for sale is *forbidden*. If a seller is prohibited from selling mixed oil, then when the buyer says he wants a hundred units, both parties understand that this means a hundred units of pure oil, since there is no permissible alternative. And yet if the seller must deliver a full hundred pure units with no offset for the natural loss of *shmarim*, there is simply no profit margin: *zavin d'zavin tigra ikri* — a merchant who buys and sells at the same price is not really a merchant.

Tosafot addresses this difficulty. The implication seems to be that the merchant's profit is embedded in the very allowance to deliver ninety-eight and a half units rather than a full hundred — not through a higher sale price, but through the understood convention that a hundred units of oil, given the realities of the trade, means the net content that remains after the natural *shmarim* loss. While this is not fully self-explanatory, the essential point is that the hundred-unit agreement is implicitly premised on the standard of the trade, which already accounts for that residual loss.

### Ptitin Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא: אֶחָד הַלּוֹקֵחַ וְאֶחָד הַמַּפְקִיד לִפְקָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** It is **taught:** The legal status of **both one who buys and one who deposits** oil with regard **to residue [*piktim*],** e.g., olive pits floating on the oil, is the same.

The Mishnah teaches that the same rule — *echad haloke'ach v'echad hamafkid* — applies both to a purchaser and to one who deposits grain with a *shomer* when it comes to *ptitin*. Rashi understands *ptitin* as the cloudy residue or fine debris that settles out of oil — distinct from the *shmarim*, the lees of the actual seeds. The same deduction that applies in a sale applies when the *shomer* returns the deposit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אַלָּא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּמַפְקִיד מְקַבֵּל פְּקָטִים, לוֹקֵחַ נָמֵי מְקַבֵּל פְּקָטִים. וּמִי מְקַבֵּל לוֹקֵחַ פְּקָטִים? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא אָמְרוּ שֶׁמֶן עָכוּר אֶלָּא לַמּוֹכֵר בִּלְבָד, שֶׁהֲרֵי לוֹקֵחַ מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו לוֹג וּמֶחֱצָה שְׁמָרִים, בְּלֹא פְּקָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is teaching: **Just as the one who deposits** the oil **accepts the residue** when his oil is returned to him, so **too, the buyer accepts the residue** with the oil he purchases. The Gemara asks: **And does the buyer accept** upon himself a deduction for **residue? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The Sages **stated** that the loss for **murky oil** is **only for the seller, as the buyer accepts upon himself** a deduction for **a *log* and a half of sediment without residue?**

The Gemara works out the logic in both directions. On one reading, just as a buyer need not accept *ptitin* as part of his delivery, a depositor need not accept *ptitin* when the *shomer* returns his oil — in other words, the *shomer* must return it clean. On the other reading — which the Gemara appears to favor — just as a *mafkid* understands that the oil will be returned minus the *ptitin*, a buyer must accept that the quantity delivered will similarly reflect the loss of *ptitin*. The two cases track each other: whatever convention governs return of a deposit governs the terms of a sale as well, since in both cases the norm of the trade determines what the agreed-upon quantity actually entails.

### Cloudiness Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ זוּזֵי בְּתִשְׁרִי וְקָא שָׁקֵיל מִינֵּיהּ בְּנִיסָן, כִּי מִדָּה דְתִשְׁרִי. הָא דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ זוּזֵי בְּנִיסָן וְקָא שָׁקֵיל מִינֵּיהּ בְּנִיסָן, כִּי מִדָּה דְנִיסָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult, as this** *baraita*, in which it is taught that the buyer accepts residue, is referring to a case **where** the buyer **gave the seller money in Tishrei,** when olives are harvested, **and he takes** the oil **from him in Nisan according to** the **measure of Tishrei.** In Tishrei, due to the substantial supply, the price is lower, and immediately after the harvest the oil is murky. **That** *baraita*, in which it is taught that the loss for murky oil is only for the seller, is referring to a case **where** the buyer **gave the seller money in Nisan, and he takes** the oil **from him in Nisan according to** the **measure** of **Nisan,** as in Nisan both the buyer and the seller assume that the oil is refined.

The Gemara introduces a further distinction regarding *shemen achor*, cloudy oil. Rabbi Yehuda rules that the loss from cloudiness — the fact that freshly pressed oil is murky before the sediment settles — falls entirely on the seller: *lo amru shemen achor ela l'mocher bilvad*. The buyer's acceptance of one and a half *log* per hundred as the *shmarim* deduction already assumes that what he is receiving is clean, settled oil. The seller therefore absorbs the loss from cloudiness; he cannot charge that against the buyer in addition to the *shmarim* deduction. The buyer gets ninety-eight and a half units of pure, settled oil — and the turbidity of the oil at the time of pressing is the seller's problem, not something further deducted from the buyer's allotment.

### Seasonal Pricing

The Gemara resolves an apparent difficulty through the mechanism of seasonal pricing. The ruling on cloudiness is not absolute; it depends on when the price was fixed and when the oil was taken. *Ha d'yahiv lei zuzei b'Tishrei v'kashakil minei b'Nisan* — if the buyer paid *Tishrei* prices and took delivery in *Nisan*, then since *Tishrei* prices reflect the fresh, cloudy, unfiltered state of the oil at harvest time, the seller retains the right to account for that cloudiness. The price paid in *Tishrei* was a *Tishrei* price — one that anticipated receiving unfiltered oil, and from which the *shmarim* and cloudiness loss is legitimately deducted. But if the buyer paid *Nisan* prices and took delivery in *Nisan* — *ki middei d'Nisan* — then by *Nisan* the oil has naturally clarified, prices have adjusted accordingly, and the seller may not deduct further for cloudiness. The price paid in *Nisan* already reflects filtered, settled oil, and a full accounting of pure units is owed.

## Barrel Case

### No Designation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְקִיד חָבִית אֵצֶל חֲבֵירוֹ וְלֹא יִחֲדוּ לָהּ בְּעָלִים מָקוֹם, וְטִלְטְלָהּ וְנִשְׁתַּבְּרָה – אִם מִתּוֹךְ יָדוֹ נִשְׁבְּרָה, לְצוֹרְכּוֹ – חַיָּיב, לְצוֹרְכָּהּ – פָּטוּר. אִם מִשֶּׁהִנִּיחָהּ נִשְׁבְּרָה, בֵּין לְצוֹרְכּוֹ בֵּין לְצוֹרְכָּהּ – פָּטוּר. יִחֲדוּ לָהּ הַבְּעָלִים מָקוֹם, וְטִלְטְלָהּ וְנִשְׁבְּרָה – בֵּין מִתּוֹךְ יָדוֹ וּבֵין מִשֶּׁהִנִּיחָהּ, לְצוֹרְכּוֹ – חַיָּיב, לְצוֹרְכָּהּ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who deposits a barrel with another, and the owners did not designate** a specific **place for** the barrel to be stored in the bailee's house, and the bailee **moved it and it broke, if it broke while** still **in his hand,** there is a distinction: If he moved the barrel **for his purposes,** he is **liable** to pay for the damage. If he moved the barrel **for its** own **purposes,** to prevent it from being damaged, he is **exempt. If, after he replaced** the barrel **it broke, whether** he initially moved it **for his purposes** or **whether** he moved it **for its** own **purposes,** he is **exempt.** But if **the owners designated** a specific **place for** the barrel, **and** the bailee **moved it and it broke, whether** it broke **while** still **in his hand or whether** it broke **after he replaced** the barrel, if he moved it **for his purposes** he is **liable** to pay, and if he moved it **for its** own **purposes,** he is **exempt.**

The Mishnah turns to a new and more conceptually rich case: *ha-mafkid chavit etzel chaveiro v'lo yichadu lo ba'alim makom* — one who deposits a barrel with his fellow without designating a specific place for it. If the *shomer* subsequently moves the barrel and it breaks, his liability depends on the circumstances.

### Letzorkho Distinction

If the barrel broke while still in his hands — *mitoch yado* — the analysis turns on why he moved it. *Letzorkho chayav*: if he moved it for his own purposes, he is liable. *Letzorkhah patur*: if he moved it for the barrel's sake — to protect it, or simply as part of his duties as *shomer* — he is exempt. Moving it *letzorkhah* is simply fulfilling the role of a guardian; moving it *letzorkho*, for his own benefit, transforms his relationship to the object in a way that creates liability. The precise nature of that transformation — whether it makes him a *gazlan*, a *sholei'ach yad*, or a *sho'el she-lo bi-reshut* — is a question the Gemara will address at length.

### Return & Release

If the barrel broke after he had already set it back down — *mishe-hinichah nishberah* — he is exempt regardless of whether he had moved it *letzorkho* or *letzorkhah*. The act of returning the barrel ends whatever liability had attached to him. This is the rule when no specific place had been designated by the owners.

### Designated Place

When the owners had designated a specific place for the barrel, the analysis shifts. Here, if the *shomer* moved the barrel and it broke — whether while holding it or after setting it down — *letzorkho chayav, letzorkhah patur*. The rule about return no longer operates as a complete release in the way it did in the first case.

This initially appears puzzling. One would expect the designated-place case to be easier to resolve through return: there is a clear location to restore the barrel to, and returning it there should be at least as effective as returning it to an unspecified location in the first case. The Gemara will ultimately explain that in the *seifa*, the *shomer* did not in fact return the barrel to its designated place — and it is that failure of restoration, not the mere existence of a designation, that sustains the liability.

### R. Yochanan Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לָא בָּעִינַן דַּעַת בְּעָלִים. דְּתַנְיָא: הַגּוֹנֵב טָלֶה מִן הָעֵדֶר וְסֶלַע מִן הַכִּיס – לִמְקוֹם שֶׁגָּנַב יַחְזִיר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ דַּעַת בְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who says:** When a thief returns an item that he stole, **we do not require the knowledge of the owner** for the item to be considered returned, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In a case of **one who steals a lamb from the flock or a *sela* from the purse, he should return it to the place from which he stole** it, and it is unnecessary to inform the owner; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says: One requires the knowledge of the owner** for the item to be considered returned.

The Gemara raises a striking observation. *Man d'matargem li chavit aliba d'chad tana* — Rabbi Yochanan declared that whoever can explain this Mishnah of the barrel as the coherent opinion of a single *tanna*, he would personally carry that person's clothes to the bathhouse. The difficulty Rabbi Yochanan perceives is this: the *reisha* and the *seifa* appear to operate on contradictory logic regarding what constitutes a valid return. Without a designated place, it is admittedly harder to return the object, and yet the *reisha* treats it as sufficient for exemption. With a designated place, return is easier to execute, and yet the *seifa* treats it as insufficient. This inversion is what Rabbi Yochanan finds inexplicable as a unified ruling.

### R. Yishmael View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, מַאי אִירְיָא לֹא יִחֲדוּ? אֲפִילּוּ יִחֲדוּ נָמֵי! לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא יִחֲדוּ דִּמְקוֹמָהּ הוּא, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לֹא יִחֲדוּ דְּלָאו מְקוֹמָהּ הוּא – לָא בָּעֵינַן דַּעַת בְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, why** did the *tanna* in the first clause of the mishna establish the case **specifically** where the owner **did not designate** a specific place for the barrel to be stored in the bailee's house? **Even** in a case where the owner **designated** a place for the barrel, the bailee should be exempt because he replaced the barrel. The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **is speaking** utilizing the style: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** to state the *halakha* in a case where the owners **designated** a place for the barrel, as after the bailee replaced the barrel, **that is its place. But even** in a case where the owner **did not designate** a place for the barrel, and after the bailee replaces the barrel **that is not its place, we do not require the knowledge of the owners.** In both cases, once he replaces the barrel, he is exempt from payment.

The Gemara answers that the entire Mishnah — *v'khula Rabbi Yishmael hi* — follows Rabbi Yishmael's position: *lo ba'inan da'at ba'alim*, there is no requirement that the owners be informed of the return for the *shomer* to be released from liability. Rabbi Yishmael holds that if a thief returns a stolen object to the place from which he took it — *l'makom she-ganav yachazir* — he is exempt, even without the owner's knowledge. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *tzarich da'at ba'alim*, the owners must be aware.

With this framework, the Gemara reads the *reisha* as a *lo mibaya ka'amar* — a "goes without saying" structure. The *reisha* is not limiting itself to the case where no place was designated; it is saying: not only when a place was designated and you return it there — which would be the easier, more intuitive case for exemption — but even when no place was designated, and the notion of "return" is less clearly defined, once the *shomer* decides to resume his watching and ceases using it for himself, that constitutes return, and he is exempt. The *seifa*, in turn, says he is liable because he did not in fact return it to its designated spot — not because a designated place categorically prolongs liability, but because without restoring it to the right location, no return has occurred.

### Three Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר רַב וְלֵוִי. חַד אָמַר: שְׁלִיחוּת יָד צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן, וְחַד אָמַר: שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute between **Rav and Levi. One says: Misappropriation requires loss. And one says: Misappropriation does not require loss.**

Before fully resolving the Mishnah, the Gemara pauses to identify the precise basis for the *shomer*'s liability in each scenario — that is, what type of wrongful taking occurred that created the liability in the first place.

Three categories are laid out. *Gazel* is straightforward: the *shomer* took the object intending to keep it entirely for himself, which constitutes outright theft and requires a *ma'aseh kinyan* — an act of acquisition — such as lifting. *Shlichus yad* is a distinct category applicable specifically to *shomrim*: the *shomer* does not intend to keep the whole object, but does intend to use it in a manner that is *mechaser* it — diminishing it in some way, as when one picks up a barrel of wine with the intent to draw some off. This too requires a *ma'aseh kinyan*. The third category is *sho'el she-lo mida'as*: borrowing the object without the owner's permission, not necessarily with the intent to diminish it, but simply making use of it for one's own purposes — as when one moves a barrel to use as a stepladder. The Gemara's position is that *sho'el she-lo mida'as gazlan havei* — one who borrows without permission is classified as a type of *gazlan* and is liable accordingly.

## Shlichus Yad

### Still Holding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אַבָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: שֶׁנְּטָלָהּ עַל מְנָת לְגוֹזְלָהּ. תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַבִּי נָתָן בַּר אַבָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: שֶׁנְּטָלָהּ עַל מְנָת לִשְׁלוֹחַ בָּהּ יָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Abba interpreted** the mishna **before Rav:** The mishna is referring to a case **where** the bailee **took** the barrel **in order to rob** the owner of **it,** and that is the meaning of the term: For his purposes. Since he intended to rob the owner of the barrel, he must return it to its place. In the first clause of the mishna, where the owner of the barrel did not designate a place for it, anywhere that he places it constitutes a return to its place. In the latter clause of the mishna, where the owner designated a place for the barrel, since the bailee did not return the barrel to that place, it is not considered to have been returned. **Rabbi Natan bar Abba interpreted** the mishna **before Rav:** The mishna is referring to a case **where** the bailee **took** the barrel **in order to misappropriate it,** as one who misappropriates the property of another is responsible for any subsequent damage to it.

The Gemara now focuses on the case where the *shomer* is still holding the barrel — *mitoch yado* — and examines what exactly makes him liable. Two *Amoraim* offer competing interpretations of what the *shomer*'s act was. One says *she-netala al menat ligzola* — he picked it up with the intent to steal it outright. The other says *she-netala al menat lishloach bah yad* — he picked it up with the intent to commit *shlichus yad*, meaning to use it in a way that would diminish it, without necessarily intending to take the whole thing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? בִּשְׁלִיחוּת יָד צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן. מַאן דְּאָמַר לְגוֹזְלָהּ – קָסָבַר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר לִשְׁלוֹחַ בָּהּ יָד – קָסָבַר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do** Rabbi Ya'akov and Rabbi Natan **disagree?** The Gemara answers: It is **with regard to** whether **misappropriation requires loss:** Is one liable for misappropriation only if it results in depreciation of the deposit, or is one liable for misappropriation even if he only intended to damage the deposit but there was no depreciation? **The one who says** that the bailee took the barrel in order **to rob** the owner of **it holds** that **misappropriation requires loss. And the one who says** that the bailee **took** the barrel in order **to misappropriate it holds** that **misappropriation does not require loss.**

The underlying dispute between these two readings, the Gemara clarifies, is the question of *shlichus yad tzericha chisaron* — whether *shlichus yad* requires an actual diminishment of the object. According to the view that *shlichus yad* requires *chisaron*, the Mishnah must be describing a case where the *shomer* intended to steal the whole barrel outright, because mere intent to take some wine — without actually taking it — would not be sufficient to create liability. According to the view that *shlichus yad* does not require *chisaron*, the Mishnah can be describing a case where the *shomer* intended only to draw some wine from the barrel; his very taking of it with that intent is enough to make him liable for the whole.

Rav Nachman addresses the case where the *shomer* simply continues to hold the barrel without doing anything further. One might ask: what exactly did he do that makes him liable? The Gemara's discussion here leads into the detailed analysis of what kind of act generates *shlichus yad* liability in the first place.

### Hitting as Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מִידֵּי ״נְטָלָהּ״ קָתָנֵי? ״טִלְטְלָהּ״ קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁטִּלְטְלָהּ לְהָבִיא עָלֶיהָ גּוֹזָלוֹת, וְקָא סָבַר: שׁוֹאֵל שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת – גַּזְלָן הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet objects to** that explanation: **Does** the *tanna* **teach** that the bailee **took it? It is taught** in the mishna: The bailee **moved it,** indicating that he sought neither to misappropriate it nor to rob the owner of it. **Rather, Rav Sheshet said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the bailee **moved** the barrel **to** stand **upon it** and **bring fledglings** from a nest in a tree. The bailee did not attempt to use its contents. He merely climbed on the barrel. **And** the *tanna* of the mishna **holds:** The legal status of **one who borrows without the knowledge** of the owners **is** that of **a robber** in terms of responsibility.

Rav Sheshes raises a textual objection to the entire framework: *midei natla katani* — the Mishnah does not say the *shomer* "lifted" the barrel; it says he "moved" it — *tiltela*. Lifting is the classic *ma'aseh kinyan* that would make one a *gazlan* or a *sholei'ach yad*; mere movement (*tiltul*) is not. So what act of *kinyan* occurred?

Rav Sheshes therefore reframes the case: *she-tiltela lehavi aleha gozalot* — the *shomer* moved the barrel in order to stand on it and retrieve something from a high place. He was not committing *gazel* and not committing *shlichus yad* — he performed no act of *kinyan* with intent to take or diminish. Rather, he is a *sho'el she-lo mida'as*, a borrower without permission, and the Gemara establishes that *sho'el she-lo mida'as gazlan havei* — such a person bears the liability of a *gazlan*.

This reframing redirects the discussion. The question of *shlichus yad* requiring *chisaron* does not apply to a *sho'el she-lo mida'as* in the same way; the *sho'el she-lo mida'as* is already treated as having misappropriated the object by the unauthorized use itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״הִנִּיחָהּ״ בִּמְקוֹמָהּ מַשְׁמַע. וּמִדְּרַב סָבַר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן, לֵוִי סָבַר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן. וְאָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק אָמַר רַב: שֶׁהִכִּישָׁהּ בְּמַקֵּל וְרָצְתָה לְפָנָיו. וְהָא לָא חַסְּרַהּ! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קָסָבַר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why doesn't **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who claimed that it is not possible to establish both clauses of the mishna in accordance with the opinion of the same *tanna*, explain the mishna in that manner? He holds that the term: **He placed it, indicates** that he replaced it **in its** designated **place.** Therefore, the latter clause cannot be explained in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael, and the contradiction remains. The Gemara comments: **And from** the fact **that Rav holds** that **misappropriation requires loss,** it may be inferred that **Levi holds** that **misappropriation does not require loss.** **And Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak says** that **Rav says:** The *tanna* is referring to a case **where** the shepherd **struck** the animal **with a staff and it ran before him,** which is a form of pulling. The Gemara asks: **But** by causing the animal to run, **he did not cause a loss** to the animal. Why is he liable to pay? **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that Rav **holds: Misappropriation does not require loss?**

Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak then offers a different answer in the name of Rav to explain how *shlichus yad* can operate even without the *shomer* having performed a classical lifting *kinyan*: *she-hikisha b'makal v'ratza lefanav* — the shepherd struck the animal with his staff and made it run before him. This act of striking and directing the animal toward himself is treated as a *ma'aseh kinyan* through *meshicha*. The Gemara then asks: but was there any *chisaron*? The animal suffered no diminishment. And the answer, says the Gemara: *shma mina kasavar shlichus yad einah tzericha chisaron* — we may conclude from this that Rav holds *shlichus yad* does not require *chisaron*; the act of taking with the intent to use, even without actual diminishment, is sufficient.

### Rav/Levi Debate

The Gemara now articulates the debate directly. *Chad amar shlichut yad tzericha chisaron, ve-chad amar shlichut yad einah tzericha chisaron* — one holds that *shlichus yad* requires actual diminishment of the object, the other holds that intent and the act of taking alone are sufficient. From the analysis above, the Gemara concludes that Rav is the one who holds *shlichus yad einah tzericha chisaron*, since his resolution of the shepherd case involved a *meshicha* without any actual *chisaron* to the animal.

It is worth noting that *shlichus yad* as a legal category applies specifically in the context of *shomrim*. *Gazel*, *mazik*, and *sho'el she-lo mida'as* can arise with respect to any object, regardless of any prior custodial relationship. *Shlichus yad* is a specific violation of one's responsibilities as a *shomer*, transforming the guardian into something closer to a *gazlan* with respect to the object in his charge.

### No Chisaron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר רַב וְלֵוִי. חַד אָמַר: שְׁלִיחוּת יָד צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן, וְחַד אָמַר: שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן. תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּרַב הוּא דְּאָמַר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן, דְּתַנְיָא: רוֹעֶה שֶׁהָיָה רוֹעֶה עֶדְרוֹ, וְהִנִּיחַ עֶדְרוֹ וּבָא לָעִיר, וּבָא זְאֵב וְטָרַף וּבָא אֲרִי וְדָרַס – פָּטוּר. הִנִּיחַ מַקְלוֹ וְתַרְמִילוֹ עָלֶיהָ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute between **Rav and Levi. One says: Misappropriation requires loss. And one says: Misappropriation does not require loss.** The Gemara comments: **It may be concluded that** it **is Rav who says: Misappropriation does not require loss, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a shepherd who was herding his flock,** which included the animals of others, **and he abandoned his flock and went to the city, and a wolf came and devoured** an animal, **and a lion came and clawed** an animal, the shepherd is **exempt,** as in any case, the attacks occurred through circumstances beyond his control. If **he placed his staff and his satchel on** the animal that was later attacked, he is **liable** to pay for the animal. Since he utilized the animal, it is as if he misappropriated it, and therefore he is liable to pay even in a case involving circumstances beyond his control.

The Gemara turns to the textual basis for each position, examining why the Torah mentions *shlichus yad* in two places — once in the context of a *shomer chinam* and once in the context of a *shomer sachar* — and what the repetition is meant to teach.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בָּהּ: מִשּׁוּם דְּהִנִּיחַ מַקְלוֹ וְתַרְמִילוֹ עָלֶיהָ חַיָּיב? הָא שַׁקְלִינְהוּ! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַב: בְּעוֹדָן עָלֶיהָ. וְכִי עוֹדָן עָלֶיהָ מַאי הָוֵי? הָא לָא מַשְׁכַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed** this *baraita*: **Due to** the fact **that he placed his staff and his satchel** on the animal, is he **liable** to pay? **Didn't he** already **remove them?** Even if he improperly used the animal, he already removed his staff and satchel, and it is tantamount to returning it to the owners. And **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh said** that **Rav said:** The *tanna* is referring to a case where the wolf devoured the animal **when** the staff and satchel **were still on** the animal. Since the bailee is still using the animal, it is considered his in terms of liability to pay for the damage caused. The Gemara asks: **And if** the staff and satchel **are still on** the animal, **what of it? But he did not pull** the animal and therefore did not acquire it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר רַב וְלֵוִי. חַד אָמַר: שְׁלִיחוּת יָד צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן, וְחַד אָמַר: שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּלֵוִי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן נְהוֹרַאי: מְשׁוּנָּה שְׁלִיחוּת יָד הָאֲמוּרָה בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר מִשְּׁלִיחוּת יָד הָאֲמוּרָה בְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם. וּמַאי מְשׁוּנָּה? לֹא תֵּאָמֵר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, וְתֵיתֵי מִשּׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם: וּמָה שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם שֶׁפָּטוּר בִּגְנֵבָה וַאֲבֵדָה שָׁלַח בָּהּ יָד – חַיָּיב, שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁחַיָּיב בִּגְנֵבָה וַאֲבֵידָה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא כַּתְבִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא? לוֹמַר לָךְ: שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute between **Rav and Levi. One says: Misappropriation requires loss. And one says: Misappropriation does not require loss.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Levi? Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yosei ben Nehorai: Misappropriation that is stated with regard to a paid bailee is different from misappropriation that is stated with regard to an unpaid bailee.** There is no need for the Torah to state the *halakha* of misappropriation twice. If an unpaid bailee is liable to pay for misappropriation, all the more so is a paid bailee liable to pay. The reason that the Torah repeated this *halakha* is to teach that a paid bailee is liable to pay for misappropriation even if there is no loss. **And what** is meant by: Misappropriation that is stated with regard to a paid bailee is **different** from misappropriation that is stated with regard to an unpaid bailee? As one could claim: Let **misappropriation not be stated with regard to a paid bailee, and derive it from** misappropriation with regard to **an unpaid bailee** by means of an *a fortiori* inference: **And if an unpaid bailee, who is exempt in** cases where he claims **theft and loss, misappropriated** the deposit, he is **liable** to pay, then **a paid bailee, who is liable in** cases where he claims **theft and loss, all the more so** is it **not** clear that he is liable if he misappropriated the deposit? **With regard to what *halakha* did the Merciful One write** misappropriation in the case of a paid bailee? It is **to say to you: Misappropriation does not require loss;** intent to misappropriate is enough to render him liable to pay.

Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Yose ben Nehorai, argues that *shlichus yad* was not strictly necessary to mention by *shomer sachar*, since one might have derived it from *shomer chinam* by *kal v'chomer*: if a *shomer chinam*, who is exempt from liability for theft and loss, is nevertheless liable when he commits *shlichus yad*, then certainly a *shomer sachar*, whose baseline liability is higher, should be liable for *shlichus yad*. The repetition by *shomer sachar* must therefore be serving a different purpose — namely, to teach that *shlichus yad einah tzericha chisaron*, that intent and act alone are sufficient without actual diminishment.

The counter-view holds that the *kal v'chomer* fails and the repetition is necessary on its own terms, not to add the *ein tzericha chisaron* rule. The challenge to the *kal v'chomer* is: *mah l'shomer chinam she-kein m'shalem kefel b'to'en ta'anas ganav* — a *shomer chinam* has one form of heightened liability that a *shomer sachar* does not: if he falsely claims the object was stolen, swears to that effect, and is later found to have stolen it himself, he pays *kefel*, double. A *shomer sachar* will never be in that position, because a *shomer sachar* who claims theft owes the principal regardless and cannot take an exculpatory oath. Since *shomer chinam* can in some cases bear a more severe consequence than *shomer sachar*, the *kal v'chomer* does not cleanly hold, and the Torah needed to state *shlichus yad* explicitly for *shomer sachar*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר אֵינָהּ מְשׁוּנָּה. וּמַאי מְשׁוּנָּה? לֹא תֵּאָמֵר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, וְתֵיתֵי מִשּׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם: וּמָה שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם שֶׁפָּטוּר בִּגְנֵבָה וַאֲבֵדָה שָׁלַח בָּהּ יָד – חַיָּיב, שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁחַיָּיב בִּגְנֵבָה וַאֲבֵידָה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא כַּתְבִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא? לוֹמַר לָךְ: שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן. וּמַאן דְּלָא פָּרֵיךְ סָבַר: קַרְנָא בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה עֲדִיפָא מִכְּפֵילָא בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan stated: **And I say** that it is **not different, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who says: This** case **and that** case **are one.** The Gemara elaborates: **What** is the meaning of: **This and that** are **one?** It means that it was necessary to teach misappropriation in both cases **due to** the fact **that** it **can be refuted** by an *a fortiori* inference: **What** is notable **about an unpaid bailee?** He is notable **in that he pays** the **double payment when he** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit. A paid bailee reimburses the owner only for the cost of the deposit in that case. The legal status of the paid bailee is not consistently more stringent than that of an unpaid bailee, and therefore no *a fortiori* inference is possible. The Gemara comments: **And the one who does not refute** the *a fortiori* inference **holds:** The absolute requirement to pay **the principal** even **without** having taken a false **oath is more** stringent **than** the requirement to pay **the double payment** that is effected only **with** the bailee taking a false **oath.** In his opinion, the legal status of the paid bailee is consistently more stringent than that of an unpaid bailee, and therefore an *a fortiori* inference is possible.

The response to this challenge is also presented: *karna b'lo shvuah adifa mi'kfila b'shvuah* — being obligated to pay the principal without any possibility of oath is a weightier burden than the risk of paying *kefel* for a false oath. The liability of a *shomer sachar* who cannot swear his way out of paying the principal is categorically more severe than the contingent *kefel* exposure of a *shomer chinam*. On this view, the *kal v'chomer* does hold, and the repetition is indeed teaching the *ein tzericha chisaron* rule.

### Rava's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לֹא תֵּאָמֵר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד לֹא בְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְלֹא בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, וְתֵיתֵי מִשּׁוֹאֵל. וּמַה שׁוֹאֵל דִּלְדַעַת בְּעָלִים קָא עָבֵיד שָׁלַח בָּהּ יָד – חַיָּיב, שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר? חֲדָא לוֹמַר לָךְ: שְׁלִיחוּת יָד אֵין צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן. וְאִידָּךְ: שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאמַר, דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כְּנִידּוֹן. מָה שׁוֹאֵל בִּבְעָלִים פָּטוּר – אַף שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר בִּבְעָלִים פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The verse should **not state misappropriation, neither with regard to an unpaid bailee nor with regard to a paid bailee, and** one can **derive it** by means of an *a fortiori* inference **from** misappropriation with regard to **a borrower: And if a borrower, who** utilizes the deposit **with the knowledge of the owner, misappropriated** the deposit, he is **liable** to pay, then with regard to **an unpaid bailee and a paid bailee,** who may not utilize the deposit at all, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that they are liable to pay if they misappropriate the deposit? **Why,** then, **is** the *halakha* of misappropriation **stated** in the cases of the unpaid and paid bailees? **One** mention is **to say to you: Misappropriation does not require loss. And the other** mention is **so that you will not say:** With regard to this *a fortiori* inference, there is a principle: It is **sufficient for the conclusion inferred from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like the source** of the inference, and thereby conclude: **Just as a borrower** who is in partnership **with the owner** is **exempt, so too, an unpaid bailee and a paid bailee** who are in partnership **with the owner** are **exempt.** Consequently, it was necessary for the verse to mention the *halakha* of misappropriation with regard to both the paid and unpaid bailee.

Rava offers a different framework entirely. He argues that one need not even invoke *shomer chinam* as the source: *teisi misho'el* — derive *shlichus yad* liability by analogy from a *sho'el*. A *sho'el* who uses an object with the owner's full permission is *chayav b'ones* — liable for any accident that befalls the object. A *sholei'ach yad* who uses the object without permission — or beyond the scope of his custody — should certainly be no less liable. The *kal v'chomer* is compelling: if authorized use creates full liability, unauthorized misuse surely does as well.

Given that one could derive *shlichus yad* from *sho'el* by analogy, why did the Torah need to state it twice? Rava answers: *chad l'meimar lach shlichut yad ein tzericha chisaron* — the first statement teaches that *shlichus yad* does not require *chisaron*; intent and act suffice. And the second statement addresses a different concern: *she-lo tomar dai lo la-vin ha-din l'hiyot k'nidon* — do not say that since we are analogizing to a *sho'el*, we should limit *shlichus yad* to the full scope of *sho'el* liability, including its exemptions. Specifically, a *sho'el b'baalim* — one who borrows an object while its owner is in his employ — is exempt from liability. One might have thought that similarly, a *sholei'ach yad* whose owner is employed by the *shomer* at the time would be exempt. Therefore the Torah states *shlichus yad* explicitly for both *shomer chinam* and *shomer sachar*, to make clear that the *ba'alim* exemption does not apply to *shlichus yad*.

### Oath Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁלִיחוּת יָד צְרִיכָה חֶסְרוֹן, הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי שְׁלִיחוּת יָד לְמָה לִי? חֲדָא שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאמַר דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן. וְאִידָּךְ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וְנִקְרַב בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים״ לִשְׁבוּעָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לִשְׁבוּעָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְדִין? נֶאֶמְרָה שְׁלִיחוּת יָד לְמַטָּה, וְנֶאֶמְרָה שְׁלִיחוּת יָד לְמַעְלָה, מָה לְהַלָּן לִשְׁבוּעָה – אַף כָּאן לִשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says: Misappropriation requires a loss, why do I** need **these two** mentions of **misappropriation?** The Gemara explains: **One** mention is **so that you will not say** with regard to this *a fortiori* inference the principle: **It is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source. And the other** mention is **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*. It is written: **"And the master of the house shall approach the judges** to determine whether he misappropriated his neighbor's goods" (Exodus 22:7). This is stated with regard **to an oath.** Do **you say** that it is stated with regard **to an oath, or** is it stated **only** with regard **to judgment? Misappropriation is stated below,** in a later verse in the chapter: "Whether he misappropriated his neighbor's goods" (Exodus 22:10), **and misappropriation is stated above,** in an earlier verse in the chapter: "Whether he misappropriated his neighbor's goods" (Exodus 22:7). **Just as below** it is stated explicitly with regard **to an oath:** "The oath of the Lord shall be between them both to determine whether he misappropriated his neighbor's goods" (Exodus 22:10), **so too here,** it is stated with regard **to an oath** and not merely for judgment.

The view that holds *shlichus yad tzericha chisaron* — that actual diminishment is required — must then explain why the Torah states *shlichus yad* twice, since on its terms the extra statement cannot be teaching *ein tzericha chisaron*. The answer: one of the mentions of *shlichus yad* comes in the context of the verse *v'nikrav ba'al habayit el ha'elohim im lo shalach yado b'mlechet re'ehu* — the owner of the house shall come before the judges if he has not cast his hand upon the property of his fellow. This verse establishes not merely that *shlichus yad* carries liability, but that the *shomer* must address the claim through a *shvua*, a formal oath. One might have read the verse as requiring only adjudication — *l'din* — without an oath. The repetition, through a *gezeirah shavah* linking the two mentions of *shlichus yad* in the Torah, teaches that just as one mention clearly involves an oath, so too does the other: *af kan l'shvua*. The oath is not optional; it is an integral part of how *shlichus yad* liability is resolved before the court.

To summarize the essential framework: three categories govern misappropriation of a deposited object. *Gazel* — taking it outright with intent to keep it, requiring a *ma'aseh kinyan*. *Shlichus yad* — performing an act of *kinyan* with the intent to use the object in a way that diminishes it, with the debate turning on whether the diminishment must actually occur or whether intent and act alone suffice. And *sho'el she-lo mida'as* — making use of the object without permission, without necessarily intending to diminish it, treated by the Gemara as a form of *gazel* that generates full liability.

## Deposited Money

### Negligent Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְקִיד מָעוֹת אֵצֶל חֲבֵרוֹ, צְרָרָן וְהִפְשִׁילָן לַאֲחוֹרָיו, אוֹ שֶׁמְּסָרָם לִבְנוֹ וּלְבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנִּים, וְנָעַל בִּפְנֵיהֶם שֶׁלֹּא כָּרָאוּי – חַיָּיב, שֶׁלֹּא שִׁימֵּר כְּדֶרֶךְ הַשּׁוֹמְרִים. וְאִם שִׁימֵּר כְּדֶרֶךְ הַשּׁוֹמְרִים – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who deposited coins with another,** and that bailee **bound it** in a cloth **and slung it behind him,** or **conveyed them to his minor son or daughter** for safeguarding, **or locked** the door **before them in an inappropriate,** i.e., insufficient, manner to secure them, the bailee is **liable** to pay for the coins, **as he did not safeguard** the coins **in the manner** typical **of bailees. But if he safeguarded** the money **in the manner** that **bailees** safeguard items and it was nevertheless stolen, he is **exempt.**

The daf concludes with a new Mishnah: *ha-mafkid ma'ot etzel chaveiro* — one who deposits money with his fellow. The Mishnah enumerates several methods of guarding that are deemed negligent: the *shomer* bundled the money and slung it behind his back, or handed it to his young child, or locked the door in a manner that was insufficiently secure — and the money was subsequently stolen or lost. In each of these cases the *shomer* is liable, because none of these methods meets the standard expected of a proper guardian of money.

The Gemara's discussion of this Mishnah continues onto the following daf. It is striking, as one reflects on it, that the standard the Mishnah applies is contextual and practical — the benchmark of how money is properly secured. Slinging a bundle behind one's back might today seem oddly specific, but the underlying principle translates directly: a *shomer* is responsible not merely for good intentions but for guarding in a manner that actually provides security.
