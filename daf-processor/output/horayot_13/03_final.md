# Horayot 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Keriah

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל פּוֹרֵם מִלְּמַטָּה, וְהַהֶדְיוֹט מִלְמַעְלָה. כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מַקְרִיב אוֹנֵן וְלֹא אוֹכֵל, וְהַהֶדְיוֹט לֹא מַקְרִיב וְלֹא אוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A High Priest rends** his garments **from below** when he is in mourning, **and an ordinary** priest rends his garments **from above** like a non-priest. **A High Priest sacrifices** offerings as **an acute mourner,** i.e., on the day of the death of one of his close relatives, **but he may not eat** from those offerings. **And an ordinary** priest who is an acute mourner **neither sacrifices** offerings **nor eats** from those offerings.

The daf begins by picking up at the bottom of 12b, where the *Mishnah* had been treating differences between a *Kohen Gadol* anointed with oil and a *Kohen Gadol* invested through garments — and also differences involving the *Kohen Mashuach Milchama*. The next *Mishnah* shifts focus to distinctions between a *Kohen Gadol* and a *Kohen Hedyot*, a regular *Kohen*.

### Tearing & Onen

The *Mishnah* states: *Kohen Gadol porem milmatah vehedyot milmalah* — a *Kohen Gadol* tears his garment from the bottom, and a regular *Kohen* tears from the top. This ruling connects directly to the prohibition in the *parshah* of *Emor*: *ve'al rosho lo yifra uvgadav lo yifrom*, which is relevant to the episode in which Aharon and his sons suffer the loss of Nadav and Avihu.

The *Mishnah* adds: *Kohen Gadol makriv onen velo ochel onen* — a *Kohen Gadol* continues performing the *avodah* in the *Beit HaMikdash* even as an *onen* (one in immediate mourning before burial), in fulfillment of *umin haMikdash lo yeitzei*, but he does not eat the *korbanot*. This is derived from the episode in *Emor* where Aharon himself declares that eating the *chatat* on that day would not be proper in God's eyes. A regular *Kohen*, by contrast, neither performs the *avodah* nor eats when he is an *onen*. Rav clarifies that the state of *onen* applies essentially to the day of death itself, before burial.

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר [רַב]: לְמַטָּה – לְמַטָּה מַמָּשׁ, לְמַעְלָה – לְמַעְלָה מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav says: From below,** written with regard to the High Priest, means **actually from below,** from the bottom of the garment, and **from above** means **actually from above,** from the top of the garment.

Regarding the tearing of garments, Rav states: *milmatah milmatah mamash, milmalah milmalah mamash* — the *Mishnah* is to be understood literally. A *Kohen Gadol* tears at the actual hem of the garment, and a regular *Kohen* tears at the top, by the neck.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לְמַטָּה – לְמַטָּה מִקַּמֵּי שָׂפָה, לְמַעְלָה – לְמַעְלָה מִקַּמֵּי שָׂפָה. וְזֶה וָזֶה בַּצַּוָּאר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: From below** means **from below the neckline, and from above** means **from above the neckline,** i.e., from the neckline itself, **and** both **this** High Priest **and that** ordinary priest rend their garments **at the neck** of their garment.

Shmuel disagrees. He holds: *milmatah milmatah mikamei safah, milmalah milmalah mikamei safah* — both tear at the neck, but the *Kohen Gadol* tears slightly inset from the collar, while a regular *Kohen* tears precisely at the collar itself. In other words, *vezeh vezeh betzavar* — both tear at the neck, but the *Kohen Gadol* does not start at the collar's edge.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: עַל כׇּל הַמֵּתִים כּוּלָּן – רָצָה מַבְדִּיל קַמֵּי שָׂפָה שֶׁלּוֹ, רָצָה אֵינוֹ מַבְדִּיל קַמֵּי שָׂפָה שֶׁלּוֹ. עַל אָבִיו וְעַל אִמּוֹ – מַבְדִּיל, כֵּיוָן דִּבְעָלְמָא הָוֵי קֶרַע, קְרִי כָּאן ״בְּגָדָיו לֹא יִפְרֹם״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Shmuel from a *baraita*: **For all the dead** relatives for whom one rends his garments, if **he wishes he rends the neckline of his** garment **asunder;** if **he wishes he does not rend the neckline of his** garment **asunder.** If he is rending his garments **for his father or for his mother he rends** the neckline **asunder. Since in general, it is a tear** even without rending the neckline asunder, one can **read here** with regard to the High Priest: **"Nor rend his garments"** (Leviticus 21:10). This supports the opinion of Rav that the High Priest does not rend his garments from above like others do; rather, he rends his garments from below.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge against Shmuel's position. A *baraita* teaches that when mourning for relatives generally, a person may tear either at the collar or below it — both options are valid. Only for a father or mother is the tearing required specifically at the collar. The *Gemara* therefore asks: *keivan d'vemah havei keriah* — since tearing below the collar constitutes a valid *halakhic keriah* for other relatives, if the *Kohen Gadol* were to tear in that location, he should be in violation of the Torah's prohibition *bevagdav lo yifrom*. The rabbis are in fact trying to allow the *Kohen Gadol* some expression of grief — reading the verse not as an absolute ban on any tearing, but as a ban specifically on the *halakhically* valid form of tearing. A tear below the hem would be unproblematic for Rav, since it would never constitute proper *keriah*. But under Shmuel's reading, tearing just below the collar does constitute valid *keriah* for relatives other than father and mother — so how can this be permitted for a *Kohen Gadol*?

### Rabbi Yehuda Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל קֶרַע שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַבְדִּיל שָׂפָה שֶׁלּוֹ, אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא קֶרַע שֶׁל תִּפְלוּת. וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קְרִיעָה בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Shmuel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says: Any tear that does not rend his neckline asunder is only a gratuitous tear** that serves no purpose. Since according to Rabbi Yehuda rending of garments involves rending the neckline, the High Priest may rend his garment from above provided that he does not rend the neckline. The Gemara asks: **And is Rabbi Yehuda of** the opinion that there is **rending** of garments **for a High Priest?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנְיָא: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״רֹאשׁ לֹא יִפְרָע וּבֶגֶד לֹא יִפְרֹם״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: בְּרֹאשׁ וּבֶגֶד שֶׁל סוֹטָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ לֹא יִפְרָע וּבְגָדָיו לֹא יִפְרֹם״, שֶׁאֵינוֹ בִּפְרִיעָה וּפְרִימָה כׇּל עִיקָּר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ פּוֹרֵם כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁבְּנֵי אָדָם פּוֹרְמִין, אֶלָּא הוּא מִלְּמַטָּה וְהַהֶדְיוֹט מִלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Had it been stated: He shall neither let the hair of a head grow, nor rend garments, I would have said:** It is **with regard to the head and the garment of a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful [*sota*]** that **the verse is speaking,** and it means that the High Priest must not loosen her hair or rend her garments, in the manner that an ordinary priest does to the *sota*. Therefore, **the verse states: "He shall neither let the hair of his head grow, nor rend his garments"** (Leviticus 21:10), indicating **that he is not** included **in** the mitzva to **grow long** hair **and rend** garments **at all;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yishmael says: He does not rend** his garments **in the manner that people** typically **rend** their garments. **Rather, he** rends his garment **from below and an ordinary** priest rends his garments **from above.** Apparently, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, the High Priest does not rend his garments at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּחֲדָא, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Shmuel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda with regard to one** matter, i.e., the way in which garments are rent, **and disagrees with him with regard to one** matter, as Shmuel holds that the High Priest rends his garments.

The *Gemara* resolves this by explaining that *Shmuel kerabbi Yehuda savira lei* — Shmuel adopts the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *kol kera she'eino mavdil kamei safah shelo eino ela kera shel tiflut* — any tearing that does not begin precisely at the collar is a meaningless tear and does not constitute valid *halakhic keriah* at all. Accordingly, under Rabbi Yehuda's view, tearing anywhere other than at the collar never qualifies as proper mourning *keriah* for any relative. This raises a further question: does Rabbi Yehuda allow a *Kohen Gadol* to tear at all? A *baraita* records his view: *she'eino bepri'a uprima kol ikar divrei Rebbi Yehuda* — the *Kohen Gadol* is entirely excluded from both *peri'a* (disheveling hair) and *perima* (tearing), with no permissible form of either. Rabbi Yishmael, by contrast, holds: he may not tear in the manner of all people, but he tears from below while a regular *Kohen* tears from above.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes that Shmuel accepts the *Mishnah*'s ruling — against Rabbi Yehuda — that the *Kohen Gadol* is permitted some form of tearing, while simultaneously adopting Rabbi Yehuda's position that only collar-tearing constitutes valid *keriah* for regular mourning. Since sub-collar tearing is halakhically meaningless in ordinary *aveilus* under Rabbi Yehuda's view, Shmuel can permit a *Kohen Gadol* to tear anywhere other than directly at the collar: *Shmuel savar keRebbi Yehuda bechada ufalig leh bechada*.

## Tadir & Mikudash

### Tadir Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַתָּדִיר מֵחֲבֵירוֹ קוֹדֵם אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, וְכׇל הַמְקוּדָּשׁ מֵחֲבֵירוֹ קוֹדֵם אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ. פַּר הַמָּשִׁיחַ וּפַר הָעֵדָה עוֹמְדִים – פַּר הַמָּשִׁיחַ קוֹדֵם לְפַר הָעֵדָה בְּכׇל מַעֲשָׂיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any** mitzva **that is** more **frequent than another** mitzva **precedes** that **other** mitzva if the opportunity to fulfill one of them coincides with an opportunity to fulfill the other. **And anyone who is** more **sanctified than another precedes** that **other** person. If **the bull of the anointed** priest **and the bull of the congregation,** which are brought for absence of awareness of the matter, **are pending, the bull of the anointed priest precedes the bull of the congregation in all its actions,** i.e., its sacrificial rites.

The *Mishnah* introduces two organizing principles: *kol hatadir michavero kodem et chavero* — anything that is more frequent or regular takes precedence over something less frequent. This discussion arises naturally from the broader context of *Horayot*, which has been examining hierarchies among positions of authority — and now extends those hierarchies into the domain of *korbanot* and institutional priority.

### Mikudash Principle

The *Mishnah* pairs this with a second principle: *vechol hamikudash michavero kodem et chavero* — anything that is more sanctified takes precedence over something less sanctified. The immediate application in the *Mishnah* is the case where the ox of the *Kohen Gadol* (*par mashiach*) and the ox of the *Beis Din* (*par ha'eda*) are both present: *par mashiach kodem lepar ha'eda bechol ma'asav* — the *Kohen Gadol*'s ox comes first in all its rites.

It should be noted that neither principle maps neatly onto this case. The *par* of the *Kohen Gadol* is hardly more frequent than the *par* of the *Beis Din* — both arise only in rare circumstances. And the claim that the *Kohen Gadol*'s ox is more sanctified requires some elaboration. The *Gemara* does not attempt to derive this particular ruling from the *tadir* or *mikudash* principles directly; instead, it proceeds to establish each principle independently and then returns to the ordering of the sacrifices.

### Tamid Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״מִלְּבַד עוֹלַת הַבֹּקֶר אֲשֶׁר לְעוֹלַת הַתָּמִיד״, (לְמָה לִי) מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב ״עוֹלַת הַבֹּקֶר״ – ״עוֹלַת הַתָּמִיד״ לְמָה לִי? הָכִי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: כֹּל דִּתְדִירָה קָדְמָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions the statement in the mishna that the more frequent matter takes precedence: **From where are these matters** derived? **Abaye said:** It is **as the verse states** concerning the additional offerings brought on Festivals: **"Beside the burnt-offering of the morning, which is for a daily burnt-offering"** (Numbers 28:23). **Once it is written: "The burnt-offering of the morning," why do I** need: **"A daily burnt-offering"?** Clearly the reference is to the daily burnt-offering of the morning. **This** is what **the Merciful One is saying: Any** matter **that is** more **frequent takes precedence.** Since it is a daily offering, it is more frequent. Therefore, it precedes other offerings. This is understood as a general principle, not merely a rule for this specific verse.

### Sanctity Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הַמְקוּדָּשׁ מֵחֲבֵירוֹ הוּא קוֹדֵם אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ. מְנָלַן? דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְקִדַּשְׁתּוֹ״ – לְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁבִּקְדוּשָּׁה: לִפְתּוֹחַ רִאשׁוֹן, וּלְבָרֵךְ רִאשׁוֹן, וְלִיטּוֹל מָנָה יָפָה רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And anyone who is** more **sanctified than another precedes** that **other** person. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive these matters? It is **as the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught,** that from the verse written with regard to a priest: **"And you shall sanctify him,** as he sacrifices the bread of your God, he shall be holy unto you" (Leviticus 21:8), it is derived that a priest should be esteemed and granted precedence **with regard to any matter of sanctity.** He should be the one **to open first** in the reading of the Torah, **and to recite the blessing** of the *zimmun* **first, and to take a fine portion first.** The priest who is more sanctified takes precedence.

For the *mikudash* principle, the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives it from the verse *vekedashto* — "you shall sanctify him," referring to the *Kohen*. This is understood to mean that one accords him priority in all matters of sanctity: *liftoach rishon ulvarech rishon* — to read from the Torah first, to lead the *zimmun* first, *ulitol mana yafa berishon* — and to receive the choicest portion first. One might note that receiving the largest food portion does not obviously qualify as a *davar shebikdusha*, unless understood as referring to the portions of *korbanot* — though in its immediate context the verse discusses the *Kohen* relative to other *Yisraelim*, not *korbanot* per se. Still, if the point is that all forms of priority accorded to someone holier are expressions and demonstrations of that greater holiness, the principle is coherent even if the derivation is somewhat circular.

### Kohen Gadol Ox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּר כֹּהֵן מָשׁוּחַ וּפַר עֵדָה כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשָׂרַף אוֹתוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר שָׂרַף אֵת הַפָּר הָרִאשׁוֹן״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הָרִאשׁוֹן״? שֶׁיְּהֵא רִאשׁוֹן, קוֹדֵם לְפַר הָעֵדָה בְּכׇל מַעֲשָׂיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **the bull of the anointed priest and the bull of the congregation,** which are brought for absence of awareness of the matter, are pending, the bull of the anointed priest precedes the bull of the congregation in all its actions. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? It is **as the Sages taught: "And he shall burn it as he burned the first bull"** (Leviticus 4:21). **Why** must **the verse state "the first"?** The verse could simply state that he shall burn it as he burned the bull. It is in order to establish **that the first** offering **precedes the bull of the congregation in all its actions.**

The *Gemara* now provides an independent source for the rule that the *par* of the *Kohen Gadol* precedes the *par* of the *Beis Din*. The *tanna* cites the verse *vesaraf oso ka'asher saraf es hapar harishon* — "and he shall burn it as he burned the first ox." Since the Torah had already established that the burning procedure is the same, why must it add the word "the first"? The word *harishon* teaches that the *par* of the *Kohen Gadol* must be brought first: *lelamed sheyakdim par kohein mashiach lepar he'elam davar shel tzibur bechol ma'asav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פַּר כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ וּפַר הָעֵדָה עוֹמְדִים – פַּר כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ קוֹדֵם לְפַר הָעֵדָה בְּכׇל מַעֲשָׂיו. הוֹאִיל וּמָשִׁיחַ מְכַפֵּר, וְעֵדָה מִתְכַּפֶּרֶת – דִּין הוּא שֶׁיַּקְדִּים הַמְכַפֵּר לַמִּתְכַּפֵּר, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְכִפֶּר בַּעֲדוֹ וּבְעַד בֵּיתוֹ וּבְעַד כׇּל קְהַל יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **the bull of the anointed priest and the bull of the congregation are pending, the bull of the anointed priest precedes the bull of the congregation in all its actions. Since the anointed** priest **atones** for the entire Jewish people, **and the congregation gains atonement, it is logical that the one who atones will precede the one who gains atonement. And so** the verse **states: "And he shall atone for himself, and for his household, and for all the congregation of Israel"** (Leviticus 16:17).

A *baraita* then explains the reasoning: *ho'il umashiach mechaper vetzibur miskaper* — the *Kohen Gadol* is the one performing atonement, and the community is the one receiving atonement. If one must choose, it is logical to first cleanse the *mechaper* before he atones for others: *din hu sheyakdim hamechaper lamiskaper*. The *Gemara* cites the *Yom Kippur* service as confirmation: *vechiper ba'ado uva'ad beiso uva'ad kol kehal Yisrael* — Aharon first atones for himself and the priestly household, and only then for the entire congregation of Israel. This is thus not a general claim that the *Kohen Gadol* outranks the *Beis Din* as an institution, but a specific *halacha* of atonement: the atoner must be purified before he purifies others.

### Beit Din Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּר הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר שֶׁל צִבּוּר קוֹדֵם לְפַר שֶׁל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? הַאי חַטָּאת וְהַאי עוֹלָה, וְתַנְיָא: ״וְהִקְרִיב אֶת אֲשֶׁר לְחַטָּאת רִאשׁוֹנָה״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? אִם לְלַמֵּד שֶׁתְּהֵא חַטָּאת רִאשׁוֹנָה, הֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״וְאֶת הַשֵּׁנִי יַעֲשֶׂה עוֹלָה כַּמִּשְׁפָּט״! אֶלָּא זֶה בָּנָה אָב שֶׁיְּהוּ כׇּל חַטָּאוֹת קוֹדְמוֹת לְעוֹלוֹת הַבָּאִים עִמָּהֶם, וְקַיְימָא לַן דַּאֲפִילּוּ חַטַּאת הָעוֹף קוֹדֶמֶת לְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **A bull for an unwitting communal sin precedes a bull for idol worship. What is the reason** for this *halakha*? **This,** i.e., the bull for an unwitting communal sin, is **a sin-offering, and that,** i.e., the bull for idol worship, is **a burnt-offering, and it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"And he shall sacrifice that which is for the sin-offering first"** (Leviticus 5:8); **why** must **the verse state** this? **If** it is **to teach that the sin**-**offering will be first, it is already stated: "And the second he shall prepare as a burnt-offering according to the ordinance"** (Leviticus 5:10). **Rather, this established a paradigm** from which all similar cases may be derived, teaching **that all sin-offerings precede the burnt-offerings that accompany them, and we maintain that even bird sin-offerings precede animal burnt-offerings.**

Next: *par he'elam davar shel tzibur kodem lepar shel avoda zara* — the ox brought for the mistaken ruling of the *Beis Din* on a general matter (*par he'elam davar*) precedes the ox brought as the *olah* component of the *Beis Din*'s *korban* when their erroneous ruling concerned *avoda zara*. The reason is that one is a *chatas* and the other is an *olah*, and a *chatas* always precedes an *olah*. The *Gemara* derives this from a *binyan av*: the verse *vehibriv es asher lachatas rishonah* — "and he shall offer that which is for the *chatas* first" — establishes a general principle that in any pairing of *chatas* and *olah*, the *chatas* comes first. The principle holds even when the *chatas* is a bird and the *olah* is a large animal (*behemah*) — the *chatas* still precedes. The underlying logic is that one addresses the problem (sin) before proceeding to the elevation offering (*olah*), which is more of an aspirational gift. In this context, even though *avoda zara* is a weightier transgression, it is the *olah* that accompanies it which is being compared to the *chatas* of the regular ruling — and the *chatas* always goes first.

### Idolatry Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה קוֹדֵם לִשְׂעִיר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אַמַּאי? הַאי חַטָּאת, וְהַאי עוֹלָה! אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא בַּר מָרִי: חַטַּאת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה חַסִּירָא אָלֶף, ״לְחַטָּת״ כְּתִיב. רָבָא אָמַר: ״כַּמִּשְׁפָּט״ כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A bull for idol worship** brought by the entire congregation **precedes a goat for idol worship** brought by the entire congregation. The Gemara asks: **Why** is this so; **this,** i.e., the goat is **a sin-offering, and that,** i.e., the bull is **a burnt-offering? In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they say in the name of Rava bar Mari:** In the verse: "If it is performed unwittingly by the congregation, being hidden from their eyes, the entire congregation shall bring one young bull for a burnt-offering, for a pleasing aroma to the Lord, with its meal-offering, and its libation, according to the ordinance, and one goat as a sin-offering [*leḥattat*]" (Numbers 15:24), **the sin-offering for idol worship is lacking an *alef*,** i.e., **"*leḥattat*" is written** without an *alef*. This indicates that not all the *halakhot* of sin-offerings apply to it. **Rava said: "According to the ordinance" is written concerning it,** indicating that the service must be performed in accordance with the order stated in the verse, i.e., the bull is sacrificed before the goat.

The next ruling is striking: *par Avodah Zarah kodem l'sair Avodah Zarah* — the ox of the *avoda zara* communal *korban* comes before the goat, even though the ox is the *olah* and the goat is the *chatas*. This directly reverses the rule just stated. Two explanations are offered. The scholars of *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rabbah say: *chatas Avodah Zarah chasera aleph* — the word *l'chatas* in the *avoda zara* passage is written defectively, without the *aleph*, indicating that this *chatas* is somehow of a lesser status, and therefore the ox precedes it. Rava offers a different reading: *kamishpat ksiv bei* — the Torah uses the word *kamishpat* in describing the *korban* for *avoda zara*, and in that passage the Torah itself lists the ox first. Therefore, regardless of the general *chatas*-before-*olah* rule, the Torah's own sequencing in this specific *parsha* determines the order.

### Community vs. King

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂעִיר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה קוֹדֵם לִשְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא. מַאי טַעְמָא? הַאי צִבּוּר וְהַאי יָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **The goat for idol worship** of the congregation **precedes the goat of the king. What is the reason** for this? The reason is that **this** goat is brought by the general **public and that** goat is brought by **an individual.**

The ordering continues: *sair Avodah Zarah kodem l'sair nasi* — the communal goat for *avoda zara* precedes the king's goat for an ordinary sin. The reason: *hai tzibur v'hai yachid* — one represents the community and the other represents an individual. Even though the king might be conceived as the embodiment of the community, the *korban* for *avoda zara* brought by the *Beis Din* represents a communal sin and the institutional leadership acting in their communal capacity — whereas the king's *korban* is brought for his own private transgression. The community's sacrifice therefore takes priority over the king acting in his personal capacity.

### King Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא קוֹדֵם לִשְׂעִירַת יָחִיד. מַאי טַעְמָא? הַאי מֶלֶךְ וְהַאי הֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** **The male goat of the king precedes the female goat of the individual. What is the reason** for this? **This** male goat is brought by **a king, and that** female goat is brought by **a commoner.**

Next: *sair nasi kodem l'seiras yachid* — the king's goat precedes the goat of an ordinary individual. The reason: *hai melech v'hai hedyot* — one is a king and one is a commoner. Even when the king brings for a purely private sin and not in any public capacity, his status as king still gives him precedence over an ordinary person.

### Goat vs. Sheep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂעִירַת יָחִיד קוֹדֶמֶת לְכִבְשַׂת יָחִיד. וְהָא תַּנְיָא: כִּבְשַׂת יָחִיד קוֹדֶמֶת לִשְׂעִירַת יָחִיד! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, מָר סָבַר: שְׂעִירָה עֲדִיפָא, שֶׁכֵּן נִתְרַבְּתָה אֵצֶל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיָחִיד. וּמָר סָבַר: כִּבְשָׂה עֲדִיפָא, שֶׁכֵּן נִתְרַבְּתָה בְּאַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The female goat of an individual** brought as a standard sin-offering **precedes the ewe of an individual** brought as a standard sin-offering. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The ewe of an individual precedes the female goat of an individual? Abaye said: It is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** One **Sage holds** that **a female goat is preferable** and takes precedence, **as it has an increased** applicability in that it is brought **for idol worship by an individual,** in which case one must bring a female goat, not a female sheep. **And** one **Sage holds** that the **ewe is preferable** and takes precedence, **as it has more** sacrificial portions than a female goat, as its **tail** is also included, which indicates that it is a preferable offering.

Finally: *seiras yachid kodemes l'kivsas yachid* — the she-goat of an individual precedes the ewe of an individual. A *baraita*, however, teaches the opposite: *kivsas yachid kodemas le-se'iras yachid*. Abaye resolves the contradiction by noting that this is a dispute among *Tannaim*. One view holds that the goat is weightier — *sheken nisraba etzel avoda zara* — because for the weightier sin of *avoda zara* specifically a goat is brought, not a sheep, which indicates the goat's elevated standing. The other view holds that the sheep is weightier — *sheken nisraba be-alya* — because a sheep possesses the fat tail (*alya*), which the goat lacks, making the sheep a superior offering in terms of the quality of what is presented to God.

### Grain Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹמֶר קוֹדֵם לְכֶבֶשׂ הַבָּא עִמּוֹ, שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם קוֹדְמִים לִכְבָשִׂים הַבָּאִים עִמָּהֶם. זֶה הַכְּלָל: דָּבָר הַבָּא בְּגִין (לַ)יוֹם קוֹדֵם לְדָבָר הַבָּא בְּגִין לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The ***omer*** offering **precedes the lamb that accompanies it; the two loaves,** i.e., the public offering on *Shavuot* of two loaves of bread from the new wheat, **precede the sheep that accompany them. This is the principle: A matter that comes due to** a mitzva of **the day precedes a matter that comes due to** the **bread.** The *omer* and two loaves are meal-offerings brought due to the day. The accompanying sheep are brought due to the meal-offerings.

The *Mishnah* adds: *Omer kodem le-keves ha-ba immo* — the *Omer* offering precedes the lamb that accompanies it, and *she-lechem kodem li-kvasim ha-ba'im immo* — the *shetei ha-lechem* of *Shavuot* precede the animals that accompany them. The overarching principle is: *davar ha-ba bigin le-yom kodem le-davar ha-ba bigin le-lechem* — what makes the day significant is the grain offering. On the second day of *Pesach*, it is the first cutting of the *Omer* barley that defines the day; on *Shavuot*, it is the wheat harvest and the *shetei ha-lechem*. The accompanying animal sacrifices are understood as secondary, brought in honor of the grain — and therefore the grain offering, as the focal object of the day's sanctity, precedes the animals.

## Man vs. Woman Prior (III)ity

### Gender Priorities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִישׁ קוֹדֵם לָאִשָּׁה לְהַחֲיוֹת וּלְהָשֵׁב אֲבֵדָה. וְהָאִשָּׁה קוֹדֶמֶת לָאִישׁ לִכְסוּת וּלְהוֹצִיא מִבֵּית הַשְּׁבִי. בִּזְמַן שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם עוֹמְדִים בְּקַלְקָלָה – הָאִישׁ קוֹדֵם לָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The man precedes the woman** when there is uncertainty with regard to which of them **to rescue or to return a lost item** to first. **And the woman precedes the man** with regard to which of them **to** provide with **a garment** first, because her humiliation is great, **or to release from captivity** first, due to the concern that she will be raped. **When they are both subject to degradation,** i.e., there is also concern that the man will be raped in captivity, the release of **the man precedes** the release of **the woman.**

Having concluded the discussion of sacrificial hierarchies, the *Mishnah* turns to triage among people — a territory that challenges the modern egalitarian ethos directly. The *Mishnah* states: *ha-ish kodem le-isha le-hachayoso u-le-hashiv aveida* — a man takes precedence over a woman in saving life and in returning a lost object. Conversely, *veha-isha kodemes le-ish le-chasos* — a woman takes precedence over a man in the matter of clothing, because a woman's embarrassment at being improperly clothed is greater than a man's. And *u-le-hotzi mi-beis ha-shvi* — a woman is redeemed from captivity before a man, because she is far more vulnerable to assault and to grave harm to her dignity and status in that situation.

### Equal Danger Rule

The *Mishnah* qualifies the captivity rule: *bizman she-shneihem omdim be-kalkala* — if both the man and the woman are equally endangered in captivity — that is, both face the risk of sexual assault — *ha-ish kodem le-isha*, the man is redeemed first. Rashi clarifies that this is not a return to the general opening principle of the *Mishnah*; rather, it reflects the specific calculus of the captivity situation: when the woman's distinctive vulnerability no longer distinguishes the two cases, the default ordering applies.

### Self & Family

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיָה הוּא וְאָבִיו וְרַבּוֹ בַּשֶּׁבִי – הוּא קוֹדֵם לְרַבּוֹ, וְרַבּוֹ קוֹדֵם לְאָבִיו. אִמּוֹ קוֹדֶמֶת לְכוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Apropos precedence, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **one and his father and his teacher were in captivity, his** release **precedes his teacher's** because one's own life takes precedence, **and his teacher's** release **precedes his father's** release. **His mother's** release **precedes** the release of **all of them.**

A *baraita* further develops the hierarchy of obligations: if one finds oneself, one's father, and one's *rebbe* all taken captive with limited means of redemption, *hu kodem lerabbo* — one redeems oneself first. This reflects one of the foundational principles of Jewish ethics: *chayecha vechayei achicha chayecha kodmim* — your own life takes precedence over that of another. This does not mean that one spends all resources on personal comfort before attending to the poor, but in genuine life-threatening situations, self-preservation precedes obligation to others. Once one's own situation is secured, *rabbo kodem le'aviv* — one's *rebbe* comes before one's father, because the *rebbe* brought one into the life of the world to come, while a father brought one only into this world.

*Ve'immo kodemet lechullam* — the mother precedes others, as established in the *Mishnah*'s ruling that women are to be redeemed first from captivity. The *Gemara* does not spell out every permutation — for instance, the question of whether an unrelated woman would come before one's father when captivity is at stake is left unexplored.

### Torah over Kingship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָכָם קוֹדֵם לְמֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל: חָכָם שֶׁמֵּת – אֵין לָנוּ כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ. מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁמֵּת – כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל רְאוּיִם לַמַּלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **A Torah scholar precedes the king of Israel,** because in the case of **a Sage who dies, we have no one like him, but** in the case of **a king of Israel who dies, all of Israel are fit for royalty.**

*Chacham kodem lemelech Yisrael* — a *chacham* takes precedence over a king of Israel. This maps directly onto the broader questions running through *Horayot* about the hierarchy between Torah leadership and political authority: Torah leadership supersedes political power.

### Meritocracy Irony

The *Gemara* explains: *chacham shemet ein lanu kayotzei bo* — when a *chacham* dies, we have no one to replace him. *Melech Yisrael shemet kol Yisrael re'uyim lemalchut* — when a king of Israel dies, all of Israel is potentially eligible for kingship. The irreplaceability of the *chacham* thus grounds his priority. There is a deliberate irony here worth noting. The famous teaching that there are three crowns — *keter Torah*, *keter kehuna*, and *keter malchut* — points out that *keter Torah* is available to anyone who seeks it, while *keter malchut* belongs exclusively to the house of David. Yet it is precisely because *keter malchut* is hereditary rather than merit-based that the king is "replaceable" in the relevant sense: his position says nothing about his individual worth or attainment. The *chacham*, by contrast, occupies a meritocracy — his position is accessible to all but genuinely earned, and therefore he truly is irreplaceable in a way the king is not.

## Kohanim Hierarchy

### Kohen Gadol Grades

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה קוֹדֵם לִמְרוּבֵּה בְגָדִים. מְרוּבֵּה בְגָדִים קוֹדֵם לְמָשִׁיחַ שֶׁעָבַר מֵחֲמַת קִרְיוֹ. מָשִׁיחַ שֶׁעָבַר מֵחֲמַת קִרְיוֹ קוֹדֵם לְעָבַר מֵחֲמַת מוּמוֹ. עָבַר מֵחֲמַת מוּמוֹ קוֹדֵם לִמְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** A High Priest **anointed with anointing oil precedes** a priest consecrated by donning **multiple garments.** A High Priest consecrated by donning **multiple garments precedes an anointed** High Priest **who stepped down,** even if he did so **due to his seminal emission. An anointed** High Priest **who stepped down due to his seminal emission precedes** an anointed High Priest who **stepped down due to his blemish.** An anointed High Priest who **stepped down due to his blemish precedes** a priest **anointed for war.**

Within the category of *Kohanim*, the hierarchy descends as follows. A *Kohen Gadol* anointed with the sacred oil (*mashuach b'shemen hamishcha*) precedes one invested through garments (*merubeh begadim*). The one invested through garments precedes a past *Kohen Gadol* (*mashuach she'avar*). A past *Kohen Gadol* who was removed due to a seminal emission (*keri*) precedes one removed due to a bodily blemish (*mum*), because the disqualification caused by *keri* is temporary, while that caused by a *mum* is permanent — and a lesser or more recoverable disqualification retains greater priority over a more permanent one. A past *Kohen Gadol* disqualified by a blemish precedes the *Mashuach Milchama*, because having once held the status of *Kohen Gadol* — even if one can no longer serve — places one above a *Mashuach Milchama*, who holds an entirely different and lower office. The principle resembles the precedence an ex-president might be accorded over a sitting senator.

## King over Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֶלֶךְ קוֹדֵם לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ (אֲלֵיהֶם) [לָהֶם] קְחוּ עִמָּכֶם (אוֹ מֵעַבְדֵי) [אֶת עַבְדֵי] אֲדֹנֵיכֶם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **A king precedes a High Priest, as it is stated: "And the king said unto them: Take with you the servants of your lord"** (I Kings 1:33). King David was referring to himself as lord when speaking to Zadok the priest.

Returning to the cross-category hierarchy: *melech kodem l'kohen gadol* — the king precedes the *Kohen Gadol*. The source is the verse *vayomer hamelech lahem k'chu imachem avdei adoneichem* — "and the king said to them, take with you the servants of your master." This refers to David directing Tzadok and Nathan in the anointing of Shlomo, and demonstrates that the king gives orders to the *Kohen Gadol*. The organizational authority of the king over the *Kohen Gadol* thus determines the priority of the king in situations of triage or precedence.

### Kohen vs. Prophet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל קוֹדֵם לְנָבִיא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמָשַׁח אֹתוֹ שָׁם צָדוֹק הַכֹּהֵן וְנָתָן הַנָּבִיא״. הִקְדִּים צָדוֹק לְנָתָן. וְאוֹמֵר: ״שְׁמַע נָא יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל אַתָּה וְרֵעֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״. יָכוֹל הֶדְיוֹטוֹת הָיוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי אַנְשֵׁי מוֹפֵת הֵמָּה״, וְאֵין ״מוֹפֵת״ אֶלָּא נָבִיא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָתַן אֵלֶיךָ אוֹת אוֹ מוֹפֵת״.
>
> **Translation:** **A High Priest precedes a prophet, as it is stated: "And let Zadok the priest and Nathan the prophet anoint him there"** (I Kings 1:34); **Zadok** is written **before Natan. And** similarly, the prophet **says: "Hear now, Joshua the High Priest, you and your colleagues** who sit before you, for they are men that are a sign; for behold, I will bring forth My servant Zemah" (Zechariah 3:8). One **might** have thought that these colleagues **were laymen.** Therefore, **the verse states: "For they are men that are a sign," and "sign"** means **nothing other than a prophet, as it is stated: "And he gives you a sign or a wonder"** (Deuteronomy 13:2).

More striking is the next ruling: *kohen gadol kodem l'navi* — the *Kohen Gadol* precedes the *navi*. This is remarkable because the *navi* is God's direct oracle, while the *Kohen Gadol*'s role is the embodiment and performance of divine service — symbolic and institutional rather than prophetic. The source offered is *umshacho oso sham Tzadok hakohen v'Nasan hanavi* — in the verse describing the anointing of Shlomo, Tzadok the *Kohen* is listed before Nathan the *navi*: *hikdim Tzadok l'Nasan*. An additional source is cited: *shma na Yehoshua hakohen hagadol ata v'rei'echa* — "hear now, Joshua the High Priest, you and your companions." One might think *rei'echa* refers to ordinary people, but *talmud lomar ki anshei mofes hema* — "for they are men of wonder" — and *ein mofes ela navi*, wonder is a term that refers specifically to a *navi*, as in *v'nasan lecha os u'mofes*.

What accounts for the *Kohen Gadol*'s precedence over the *navi*? One reading is that the *navi*, however directly he channels the divine word, does not possess the power to alter the fundamental structure of *halacha* or the institutional fabric of society. His role is exhortation and transmission, not governance of the sacred service. The *Kohen Gadol*, by contrast, embodies and enables the people's ongoing access to God — he is the bridge through which *Klal Yisrael* approaches the divine on a regular basis, most dramatically on *Yom Kippur*. Hearing God's word through a *navi* is perhaps, counterintuitively, less central than being the instrument through which the people *reach* God. Whether a regular *Kohen* would similarly precede a *navi* is an interesting question the *Gemara* does not resolve here.

## Kohanim Hierarchy (II)

### Disqualification Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה קוֹדֵם לִסְגָן.
>
> **Translation:** A priest **anointed for war precedes a deputy** High Priest, who replaces the High Priest when he is unable to serve in the Temple.

As established above, a past *Kohen Gadol* who was removed due to *keri* precedes one removed due to a *mum* — because *keri* represents a temporary disqualification while a *mum* is permanent.

### War Priest Ranking

*Mashuach milchama kodem l'sgan* — the *Mashuach Milchama* precedes the *sgan* (*deputy Kohen Gadol*). The reason is that the *Mashuach Milchama* holds an independent role with full authority in his domain, whereas the *sgan* is merely the backup — his entire function is to stand in for the *Kohen Gadol* if needed.

### Administrative Ranks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְגָן קוֹדֵם לַאֲמַרְכָּל. מַאי אֲמַרְכָּל? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אָמַר כּוֹלָּא. אֲמַרְכָּל קוֹדֵם לְגִזְבָּר, גִּזְבָּר קוֹדֵם לְרֹאשׁ מִשְׁמָר, רֹאשׁ מִשְׁמָר קוֹדֵם לְרֹאשׁ בֵּית אָב, רֹאשׁ בֵּית אָב קוֹדֵם לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **A deputy** High Priest **precedes the overseer [*la'amarkal*],** one of the seven appointed officials in the Temple. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***amarkal*? Rav Ḥisda said:** *Amarkal* is a contraction for ***amar kulla*,** meaning: He says it all. The overseer of the Temple has the final word in matters concerning the administration of the Temple. The **overseer precedes** the Temple **treasurer.** The **treasurer precedes** the **head of** the priestly **watch** that would serve in the Temple for a period of one week at a time. The **head of** the priestly **watch precedes** the **head of** the **patrilineal family.** Each patrilineal family performed the Temple service for one day during the week of its priestly watch. The **head of** the **patrilineal family precedes an ordinary priest.**

Below the *Mashuach Milchama*, the hierarchy proceeds: *sgan kodem l'amarkal* — the *sgan* precedes the *amarkal*. What is an *amarkal*? Rav Chisda explains: *amar kula* — "he says everything," meaning all activities of the *Kohanim* go through his authority; he is the chief administrative officer of the *Kohanim*. The *amarkal* precedes the *gizbar* (treasurer); the *gizbar* precedes the *rosh mishmar* (head of the weekly division of *Kohanim*); the *rosh mishmar* precedes the *rosh beit av* (head of the daily group of *Kohanim*); and the *rosh beit av* precedes the ordinary *Kohen Hedyot*.

### Tumah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה, סְגָן וּמְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה אֵיזֶה מֵהֶם קוֹדֵם?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **With regard to the matter of ritual impurity,** when there is a corpse with no one to bury it [*met mitzva*], which even a priest and a nazirite are commanded to bury, and the **deputy** High Priest **and** the priest **anointed for war** are available to bury it, **which of them precedes** the other and becomes impure?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: סְגָן וּמְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִים בַּדֶּרֶךְ וּפָגַע בָּהֶם מֵת מִצְוָה, מוּטָב שֶׁיִּטַּמֵּא מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה וְאַל יִטַּמֵּא סְגָן, שֶׁאִם יֶאֱרַע בּוֹ פְּסוּל בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, נִכְנָס הַסְּגָן וּמְשַׁמֵּשׁ תַּחְתָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman, said: Come** and **hear** a resolution, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a deputy** High Priest **and** a priest **anointed for war who were walking along the path and they encountered a *met mitzva*** and one of them must bury him and become ritually impure, **it is preferable that** the priest **anointed for war will become ritually impure and the deputy** High Priest **will not become ritually impure.** The reason is **that if disqualification befalls the High Priest, the deputy enters and performs** the Temple **service in his stead.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה קוֹדֵם לַסְּגָן! אָמַר רָבִינָא, כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא לְהַחְיוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, one must ensure to every possible extent that the deputy High Priest remain ritually pure. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: A priest **anointed for war precedes a deputy** High Priest? **Ravina said: When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is not with regard to ritual impurity; rather, it is taught **with regard to rescuing him,** as the standing of the priest anointed for war is higher than that of the deputy High Priest.

A practical question is posed: *l'inyan tumah: sgan u'mashuach milchama eizeh mei-hem kodem* — regarding *tumah*, if both the *sgan* and the *Mashuach Milchama* are walking together and encounter a *met mitzvah* (an unattended corpse requiring burial), which one must become *tamei* to handle it, and which is more important to preserve in a state of *tahara*?

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Nachman cites a *baraita*: *mutav she-yitamei mashuach milchama v'al yitamei sgan* — it is preferable for the *Mashuach Milchama* to become *tamei* and not the *sgan*. The reasoning is that the *sgan*'s singular function is to be ready to step in should the *Kohen Gadol* become disqualified: *she-im yei'ara bo psul b'Kohen Gadol nichnas ha-sgan u'mishamesh tachtav*. If the *sgan* becomes *tamei*, he is entirely unable to fulfill this purpose. The *Mashuach Milchama*, by contrast, can still perform his role in the military context even while *tamei*.

One might object: but does the *Mishnah* not say that the *Mashuach Milchama* precedes the *sgan* in priority? Rav Ada resolves this: *ki tanya ha-hi l'hachayoto* — the ruling that the *Mashuach Milchama* takes priority refers to situations of life preservation and similar matters of general precedence, not to questions of preserving *tahara*. In terms of status, the *Mashuach Milchama* ranks above the *sgan*. But in terms of which one must guard his *tahara* for the sake of his job obligations, the *sgan*'s duty demands that he remain *tahor* above all others.

## Tribal Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּהֵן קוֹדֵם לְלֵוִי, לֵוִי לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַמְזֵר, וּמַמְזֵר לְנָתִין, וְנָתִין לְגֵר, וְגֵר לְעֶבֶד מְשׁוּחְרָר. אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁכּוּלָּם שָׁוִים. אֲבָל אִם הָיָה מַמְזֵר תַּלְמִיד חָכָם וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל עַם הָאָרֶץ – מַמְזֵר תַּלְמִיד חָכָם קוֹדֵם לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל עַם הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A priest precedes a Levite. A Levite** precedes **an Israelite. An Israelite** precedes **a son born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship [*mamzer*], and a *mamzer*** precedes **a Gibeonite, and a Gibeonite** precedes **a convert, and a convert** precedes **an emancipated slave. When** do these *halakhot* of precedence take effect? In circumstances **when they are all equal** in terms of wisdom. **But if there were a *mamzer*** who is **a Torah scholar and a High Priest** who is **an ignoramus, a *mamzer*** who is **a Torah scholar precedes a High Priest** who is **an ignoramus,** as Torah wisdom surpasses all else.

The *Mishnah* now lays out the full genealogical hierarchy. *Kohen* precedes *Levi*; *Levi* precedes *Yisrael*; *Yisrael* precedes *mamzer*; *mamzer* precedes *nasin*; *nasin* precedes *ger*; *ger* precedes *eved meshuchrar* (freed slave). The *Gemara* provides a scriptural basis for each step. The precedence of *Kohen* over *Levi* derives from: *vayivadel Aharon lehakdisho kodesh kodashim* — Aharon was separated out for a higher degree of sanctity than the rest of the tribe of Levi. The precedence of *Levi* over *Yisrael* derives from: *ba-eis hi hivdil Hashem es shevet ha-Levi mi-Yisrael* — God set the tribe of Levi apart from Israel. *Yisrael* precedes *mamzer* — *she-zeh meyuchas ve-zeh einu meyuchas* — one has unimpaired *yichus* and the other does not, even if the *mamzer*'s parents may themselves be of distinguished lineage; their act of sin taints the *yichus* of the offspring. *Mamzer* precedes *nasin* — *she-zeh ba mi-tipah kesheirah* — a *mamzer* is born of Jewish parents, even if through a forbidden union; a *nasin*, though converted generations ago, is regarded as not being of original Jewish stock. *Nasin* precedes *ger* — *zeh gadal imanu bi-kdushah ve-zeh lo gadal imanu bi-kdushah* — the *nasinim* have been part of the Jewish community in holiness for generations, whereas a new convert has only recently emerged from the non-Jewish world. *Ger* precedes *eved meshuchrar* — *zeh hayah bi-khlal arur ve-zeh lo hayah bi-khlal arur* — the freed slave was once in the category of *arur Kanaan*, the cursed status of the slave, whereas the convert was not.

### Genealogical Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כֹּהֵן קוֹדֵם לְלֵוִי – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״(וּבְנֵי) [בְּנֵי] עַמְרָם אַהֲרֹן וּמֹשֶׁה וַיִּבָּדֵל אַהֲרֹן (לְהַקְרִיב) [לְהַקְדִּישׁוֹ] קֹדֶשׁ (הַקֳּדָשִׁים) [קָדָשִׁים]״. לֵוִי קוֹדֵם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּעֵת הַהִיא הִבְדִּיל ה׳ אֶת שֵׁבֶט הַלֵּוִי (מִתּוֹךְ) וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A priest precedes a Levite, as it is stated: "The sons of Amram: Aaron and Moses, and Aaron was separated that he should be sanctified as the most sacred"** (I Chronicles 23:13). **A Levite precedes an Israelite, as it is stated: "At that time the Lord separated the tribe of Levi,** to bear the Ark of the Covenant of the Lord, to stand before the Lord to minister unto Him, and to bless in His name, unto this day" (Deuteronomy 10:8).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִשְׂרָאֵל קוֹדֵם לְמַמְזֵר – שֶׁזֶּה מְיוּחָס, וְזֶה אֵינוֹ מְיוּחָס. מַמְזֵר קוֹדֵם לְנָתִין – זֶה בָּא מִטִּפָּה כְּשֵׁרָה, וְזֶה בָּא מִטִּפָּה פְּסוּלָה. נָתִין קוֹדֵם לְגֵר – זֶה גָּדַל עִמָּנוּ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה, וְזֶה לֹא גָּדַל עִמָּנוּ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה. גֵּר קוֹדֵם לְעֶבֶד מְשׁוּחְרָר – זֶה הָיָה בִּכְלַל אָרוּר, וְזֶה לֹא הָיָה בִּכְלַל אָרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **An Israelite precedes a *mamzer* because this** Israelite is **of** legitimate **lineage and that** *mamzer* is **not of** legitimate **lineage** and is disqualified from entering into the congregation of Israel. **A *mamzer* precedes a Gibeonite because this** *mamzer* **comes from a fit drop** of semen, i.e., from Jewish parentage, **and that** Gibeonite **comes from an unfit drop** of semen, from gentile parentage. **A Gibeonite precedes a convert,** as **this** Gibeonite **grew among us in sanctity** and conducted his life as a Jew, **and that** convert **did not grow among us in sanctity. A convert precedes an emancipated** Canaanite **slave** as **this** emancipated Canaanite slave **was** included **in the category of** the **curse** while he was enslaved, **and that** convert **was not** included **in the category of** the **curse.**

### Equality Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁכּוּלָּם שָׁוִים כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **When** do these *halakhot* of precedence take effect? In circumstances **when they are all equal** in terms of wisdom.

The *Mishnah* then states: *eimasai? bizman shekulam shavim* — when does this entire hierarchy apply? Only when all parties are equal in all other respects. This qualification is significant, and it substantially undermines the practical application of the hierarchy.

### Torah Merit Overrides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אִם הָיָה מַמְזֵר תַּלְמִיד חָכָם וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל עַם הָאָרֶץ – מַמְזֵר תַּלְמִיד חָכָם קוֹדֵם לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל עַם הָאָרֶץ. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״יְקָרָה הִיא מִפְּנִינִים״, מִכֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁנִּכְנָס לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **But if there were a *mamzer*** who is **a Torah scholar and a High Priest** who is **an ignoramus, a *mamzer*** who is **a Torah scholar precedes a High Priest** who is **an ignoramus,** as Torah wisdom surpasses all else. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Aḥa, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said:** This is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "She is more precious than rubies [*mipeninim*]"** (Proverbs 3:15). The Torah is more precious **than** the **High Priest who enters the innermost sanctum [*lifnai velifnim*],** the Holy of Holies.

The *Mishnah* immediately introduces the case that overrides the genealogical hierarchy entirely: *aval im hayah mamzer talmid chacham v'Kohen Gadol am ha'aretz, mamzer talmid chacham kodem l'Kohen Gadol am ha'aretz* — if the *mamzer* is a *talmid chacham* and the *Kohen Gadol* is an *am ha'aretz*, the *mamzer talmid chacham* comes first. The ultimate hierarchy is not one of birth or institutional status but one of *Torah* — a meritocracy. The *Gemara* supplies the source: *yekarah hi mi-pninim* — the *Torah* is more precious than pearls (*pninim*), and by way of *drash*, more precious than the *Kohen Gadol* who enters *lifnai ve-lifnim*, the innermost chamber. The three crowns clarify the structure: *keter malchut* is restricted to the house of David, *keter kehuna* to the descendants of Aharon — but *keter Torah* is open to all. As the *Rambam* emphasizes, it is available to every man and woman, rich and poor alike.

### Rav Moshe Reading

This brings us to a practically momentous question raised in a responsum addressed to Rav Moshe Feinstein. A physician in a triage situation asked: does this *Mishnah* actually require saving the life of a man before a woman, or a *Kohen* before a *Yisrael*?

Rav Moshe focused his analysis on the words *bizman shekulam shavim* — "when all are equal." He argued that *kulam shavim* means equal in *all* respects — not merely in terms of *Torah* scholarship, but in every possible relevant factor: the urgency and severity of the medical condition, the order of arrival, any other rational, social, or logical criterion that could bear on the decision. If any such factor exists that would distinguish between the two cases — and some relevant factor almost always will exist — then the hierarchy of the *Mishnah* simply does not apply. Rav Moshe's reading effectively renders the *Mishnah*'s genealogical hierarchy applicable only in a situation of complete and absolute equivalence across every conceivable dimension — a situation that in practice will never occur. This is a ruling of enormous significance for the relationship between traditional hierarchy and egalitarian ethics in a modern medical or emergency context.

## Animal Behavior

### Dogs vs. Cats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק: מִפְּנֵי מָה הַכֹּל רָצִין לִישָּׂא גִּיּוֹרֶת, וְאֵין הַכֹּל רָצִין לִישָּׂא מְשׁוּחְרֶרֶת? אָמַר לָהֶם: זוֹ הָיְתָה בִּכְלַל אָרוּר, וְזוֹ לֹא הָיְתָה בִּכְלַל אָרוּר. דָּבָר אַחֵר: זוֹ הָיְתָה בְּחֶזְקַת שָׁמוּר, וְזוֹ לֹא הָיְתָה בְּחֶזְקַת שָׁמוּר. שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִפְּנֵי מָה הַכֶּלֶב מַכִּיר אֶת קוֹנוֹ, וְחָתוּל אֵינוֹ מַכִּיר אֶת קוֹנוֹ? אָמַר לָהֶם: וּמָה הָאוֹכֵל מִמַּה שֶּׁעַכְבָּר אוֹכֵל – מְשַׁכֵּחַ, הָאוֹכֵל עַכְבָּר עַצְמוֹ – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The students of Rabbi Elazar asked** him: **For what** reason **does a dog recognize its master, while a cat does not recognize its master?** Rabbi Elazar **said to them: If** it is established that with regard to **one who eats from that which a mouse eats,** eating that item **causes him to forget,** with regard to the cat, **who eats the mouse itself, all the more so** does eating it cause it to forget.

The *Gemara* presents a series of questions addressed to Rabbi Eliezer. Why does a dog recognize its owner while a cat does not? The *Gemara*'s answer connects to the immediately preceding discussion: *umah ha'ochel mima she'achbar ochel meshake'ach ha'ochel* — one who eats from what a rat has nibbled on is caused to forget; *ochel achbar atzmo al achat kama vechama* — if eating something nibbled by a rat causes forgetting, then eating rats themselves causes forgetting to a far greater degree. Cats, who constantly eat rats and mice, therefore do not remember who their owners are.

### Rat Dominance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִפְּנֵי מָה הַכֹּל מוֹשְׁלִים בָּעַכְבָּרִים? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסּוּרַן רַע. מַאי הִיא? רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ גְּלִימֵי גָּיְיצִי.
>
> **Translation:** **The students of Rabbi Eliezer asked** him: **For what** reason do **all** predators **dominate mice** and prey on them? He said to them: **Because** concerning mice, **their inclination [*shesuran*] is evil.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the indication of this? Rava said: **They gnaw even at cloaks,** despite the fact that cloaks do not provide nourishment for them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שׁוּפְתָּא [דְּ]מָרָא גָּיְיצִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: They gnaw even on the handle of a hoe.**

A second question: why do human beings have dominance over rats — why are rats universally chased and exterminated? *Mipnei shesoran ra* — because their path or inclination is bad. What does this mean in practice? Rava says: *afilu glimi gaytzi* — they even nibble on clothes, which are not food at all. Rav Papa adds: *afilu shufsha mara gaytzi* — they even gnaw on the handle of a shovel. Because rats constantly nibble on things that serve no nutritional purpose to them, they are regarded as inherently destructive, and therefore humanity's domination over them is warranted.

## Learning Obstacles

### Forgetting Causes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן. חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים מְשַׁכְּחִים אֶת הַתַּלְמוּד: הָאוֹכֵל מִמַּה שֶּׁאוֹכֵל עַכְבָּר וּמִמַּה שֶּׁאוֹכֵל חָתוּל, וְהָאוֹכֵל לֵב שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה, וְהָרָגִיל בְּזֵיתִים, וְהַשּׁוֹתֶה מַיִם שֶׁל שִׁיּוּרֵי רְחִיצָה, וְהָרוֹחֵץ רַגְלָיו זוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי זוֹ. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף הַמַּנִּיחַ כֵּלָיו תַּחַת מְרַאֲשׁוֹתָיו. חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים מְשִׁיבִים אֶת הַתַּלְמוּד: פַּת פֶּחָמִין וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן פֶּחָמִין עַצְמָן, וְהָאוֹכֵל בֵּיצָה מְגוּלְגֶּלֶת בְּלֹא מֶלַח, וְהָרָגִיל בְּשֶׁמֶן זַיִת, וְהָרָגִיל בְּיַיִן וּבְשָׂמִים, וְהַשּׁוֹתֶה מַיִם שֶׁל שִׁיּוּרֵי עִיסָּה. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף הַטּוֹבֵל אֶצְבָּעוֹ בְּמֶלַח וְאוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: There are **five factors** that **cause** one to **forget** his Torah **study: One who eats from that which a mouse eats and from that which a cat eats, and one who eats the heart of an animal, and one who is accustomed to** eating **olives, and one who drinks water that remains from washing, and one who washes his feet** with **this** foot **atop that** foot. **And some say: Also one who places his garments under his head.** Correspondingly, there are **five factors** that **restore** forgotten Torah **study:** Eating **bread baked on coals and all the more so** one who warms himself with the heat of the **coals themselves, and one who eats a hard-boiled egg [*beitza megulgelet*] without salt, and one who is accustomed to** eating **olive oil, and one who is accustomed to** drinking **wine and** smelling **spices, and one who drinks water that remains from** kneading **dough. And some say: Also one who dips his finger in salt and eats** it.

The *Gemara* presents a list of five things that cause one to forget Torah learning: eating from food that a rat nibbled on; eating from food that a cat ate from; eating the heart of an animal; eating too many olives; and drinking water left over from washing. Some add: keeping one's clothes under one's pillow. If counted carefully, the list yields six items, requiring some combination of entries to reach five.

### Restoring Learning

Five things restore Torah learning: bread baked on coals — and all the more so eating the coals themselves (these count as one); boiled eggs without salt; frequent use of olive oil; wine together with spices; water left over from kneaded dough; and some say, dipping one's finger in salt and eating it.

### Olive Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָרָגִיל בְּשֶׁמֶן זַיִת״. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַזַּיִת מְשַׁכֵּחַ תַּלְמוּד שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, כָּךְ שֶׁמֶן זַיִת מֵשִׁיב תַּלְמוּד שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on the *baraita*: **One who is accustomed to** eating **olive oil** restores forgotten Torah study. The Gemara notes: This **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Just as** eating **an olive causes** one **to forget seventy years' worth of** Torah **study, olive oil restores seventy years' worth of** Torah **study.**

The item of olive oil prompts a citation from Rabbi Yochanan: *keshem shehazayit meshake'ach limud shel shivim shana* — just as eating olives causes one to forget seventy years of learning, *kach shemen zayit meshiv limud shel shivim shana* — olive oil restores seventy years of learning. The olive and its oil thus stand in a precise paradoxical relationship: the fruit brings forgetting, the oil brings restoration.

### Wine & Salt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָרָגִיל בְּיַיִן וּבְשָׂמִים״. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: חַמְרָא וְרֵיחָנֵי פַּקַּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And one who is accustomed to** drinking **wine and** smelling **spices** restores forgotten Torah study. The Gemara notes: This **supports** the opinion of **Rava, as Rava said: Wine and spices rendered me wise.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַטּוֹבֵל אֶצְבָּעוֹ בְּמֶלַח״, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: וּבְאַחַת. כְּתַנָּאֵי, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַחַת וְלֹא שְׁתַּיִם, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שְׁתַּיִם וְלֹא שָׁלֹשׁ. וְסִימָנָיךְ: קְמִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **One who dips his finger in salt** and eats it restores forgotten Torah study. **Reish Lakish says: And** that is the case **with regard to one** finger. The Gemara notes: This is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. Rabbi Yehuda says: One** finger **but not two. Rabbi Yosei says: Two** fingers **but not three. And your mnemonic** for the fact that the dispute is between one and two fingers is ***kemitza*,** i.e., the ring finger. When one presses his ring finger to his palm, there remain two straight fingers on one side and one on the other.

Wine together with fragrances: *chamra vereichani pikchin* — wine and pleasant scents sharpen the intellect. Regarding dipping one's finger in salt: Resh Lakish specifies that this applies to *achat* — one finger only. This is presented as a dispute among *Tannaim*: Rabbi Yehuda says one finger and not two; Rabbi Yossi says two fingers and not three. The mnemonic is the word *kmitza* — the three-finger grip used in the *Kohen*'s taking of the handful of flour in the *mincha* offering — helping one recall the sequence: one, two, three.

### Learning Obstacles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים קָשִׁים לַתַּלְמוּד: הָעוֹבֵר תַּחַת הָאַפְסָר [הַגָּמָל] וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן תַּחַת גָּמָל [עַצְמוֹ], וְהָעוֹבֵר בֵּין שְׁנֵי גְּמַלִּים, וְהָעוֹבֵר בֵּין שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, וְהָאִשָּׁה הָעוֹבֶרֶת בֵּין שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים, וְהָעוֹבֵר מִתַּחַת רֵיחַ רַע שֶׁל נְבֵילָה, וְהָעוֹבֵר תַּחַת הַגֶּשֶׁר שֶׁלֹּא עָבְרוּ תַּחְתָּיו מַיִם אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם, וְהָאוֹכֵל פַּת שֶׁלֹּא בָּשַׁל כׇּל צָרְכּוֹ, וְהָאוֹכֵל בָּשָׂר מִזּוּהֲמָא לִיסְטְרוֹן, וְהַשּׁוֹתֶה מֵאַמַּת הַמַּיִם הָעוֹבֶרֶת בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת, וְהַמִּסְתַּכֵּל בִּפְנֵי הַמֵּת. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף הַקּוֹרֵא כְּתָב שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי הַקֶּבֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ten factors are detrimental for** Torah **study: One who passes beneath the bit of the camel, and all the more so** one who passes **beneath a camel itself; and one who passes between two camels; and one who passes between two women; and a woman who passes between two men; and one who passes beneath** a place where there is the **foul odor of an animal carcass; and one who passes under a bridge beneath which water has not passed** for **forty days; and one who eats bread that was not sufficiently baked; and one who eats meat from *zuhama listeron*,** a utensil consisting of a spoon and a fork, used to remove the film on the surface of soup; **and one who drinks from an aqueduct that passes through a cemetery; and one who gazes at the face of the dead. And some say: Also one who reads the writing that is on** the stone of **a grave.**

Ten things make Torah study difficult. Walking under a camel's saddle — and all the more so under the camel itself; walking between two camels; a man walking between two women or a woman walking between two men. These latter cases reflect a society in which gender separation was standard, and such crossings were considered disruptive. Walking under the odor of a carcass; Rashi explains this means the smell is wafting over one, which creates a prohibition against learning Torah in such conditions. Walking under a bridge that has not had water flowing beneath it for the last forty days. Eating bread that has not fully cooked.

### Death-Related

Additional obstacles: eating meat that is tainted or skimmed from the cooking ladle (*basar mizuhama*); drinking from a water channel that flows through a cemetery; gazing directly at the face of a dead body; and some add, reading the inscriptions written on gravestones. One might observe a certain irony in the last prohibition: if reading gravestone inscriptions impedes the capacity for Torah learning, one might ask why we take the trouble to write them. The *Gemara* does not address this.
