# Horayot 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Keriah

### Mishnah Setup

The daf begins by picking up at the bottom of 12b, where the *Mishnah* had been treating differences between a *Kohen Gadol* anointed with oil and a *Kohen Gadol* invested through garments — and also differences involving the *Kohen Mashuach Milchama*. The next *Mishnah* shifts focus to distinctions between a *Kohen Gadol* and a *Kohen Hedyot*, a regular *Kohen*.

### Tearing & Onen

The *Mishnah* states: *Kohen Gadol porem milmatah vehedyot milmalah* — a *Kohen Gadol* tears his garment from the bottom, and a regular *Kohen* tears from the top. This ruling connects directly to the prohibition in the *parshah* of *Emor*: *ve'al rosho lo yifra uvgadav lo yifrom*, which is relevant to the episode in which Aharon and his sons suffer the loss of Nadav and Avihu.

The *Mishnah* adds: *Kohen Gadol makriv onen velo ochel onen* — a *Kohen Gadol* continues performing the *avodah* in the *Beit HaMikdash* even as an *onen* (one in immediate mourning before burial), in fulfillment of *umin haMikdash lo yeitzei*, but he does not eat the *korbanot*. This is derived from the episode in *Emor* where Aharon himself declares that eating the *chatat* on that day would not be proper in God's eyes. A regular *Kohen*, by contrast, neither performs the *avodah* nor eats when he is an *onen*. Rav clarifies that the state of *onen* applies essentially to the day of death itself, before burial.

### Rav's Position

Regarding the tearing of garments, Rav states: *milmatah milmatah mamash, milmalah milmalah mamash* — the *Mishnah* is to be understood literally. A *Kohen Gadol* tears at the actual hem of the garment, and a regular *Kohen* tears at the top, by the neck.

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel disagrees. He holds: *milmatah milmatah mikamei safah, milmalah milmalah mikamei safah* — both tear at the neck, but the *Kohen Gadol* tears slightly inset from the collar, while a regular *Kohen* tears precisely at the collar itself. In other words, *vezeh vezeh betzavar* — both tear at the neck, but the *Kohen Gadol* does not start at the collar's edge.

### Gemara Challenge

The *Gemara* raises a challenge against Shmuel's position. A *baraita* teaches that when mourning for relatives generally, a person may tear either at the collar or below it — both options are valid. Only for a father or mother is the tearing required specifically at the collar. The *Gemara* therefore asks: *keivan d'vemah havei keriah* — since tearing below the collar constitutes a valid *halakhic keriah* for other relatives, if the *Kohen Gadol* were to tear in that location, he should be in violation of the Torah's prohibition *bevagdav lo yifrom*. The rabbis are in fact trying to allow the *Kohen Gadol* some expression of grief — reading the verse not as an absolute ban on any tearing, but as a ban specifically on the *halakhically* valid form of tearing. A tear below the hem would be unproblematic for Rav, since it would never constitute proper *keriah*. But under Shmuel's reading, tearing just below the collar does constitute valid *keriah* for relatives other than father and mother — so how can this be permitted for a *Kohen Gadol*?

### Rabbi Yehuda Synthesis

The *Gemara* resolves this by explaining that *Shmuel kerabbi Yehuda savira lei* — Shmuel adopts the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *kol kera she'eino mavdil kamei safah shelo eino ela kera shel tiflut* — any tearing that does not begin precisely at the collar is a meaningless tear and does not constitute valid *halakhic keriah* at all. Accordingly, under Rabbi Yehuda's view, tearing anywhere other than at the collar never qualifies as proper mourning *keriah* for any relative. This raises a further question: does Rabbi Yehuda allow a *Kohen Gadol* to tear at all? A *baraita* records his view: *she'eino bepri'a uprima kol ikar divrei Rebbi Yehuda* — the *Kohen Gadol* is entirely excluded from both *peri'a* (disheveling hair) and *perima* (tearing), with no permissible form of either. Rabbi Yishmael, by contrast, holds: he may not tear in the manner of all people, but he tears from below while a regular *Kohen* tears from above.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes that Shmuel accepts the *Mishnah*'s ruling — against Rabbi Yehuda — that the *Kohen Gadol* is permitted some form of tearing, while simultaneously adopting Rabbi Yehuda's position that only collar-tearing constitutes valid *keriah* for regular mourning. Since sub-collar tearing is halakhically meaningless in ordinary *aveilus* under Rabbi Yehuda's view, Shmuel can permit a *Kohen Gadol* to tear anywhere other than directly at the collar: *Shmuel savar keRebbi Yehuda bechada ufalig leh bechada*.

## Tadir & Mikudash

### Tadir Principle

The *Mishnah* introduces two organizing principles: *kol hatadir michavero kodem et chavero* — anything that is more frequent or regular takes precedence over something less frequent. This discussion arises naturally from the broader context of *Horayot*, which has been examining hierarchies among positions of authority — and now extends those hierarchies into the domain of *korbanot* and institutional priority.

### Mikudash Principle

The *Mishnah* pairs this with a second principle: *vechol hamikudash michavero kodem et chavero* — anything that is more sanctified takes precedence over something less sanctified. The immediate application in the *Mishnah* is the case where the ox of the *Kohen Gadol* (*par mashiach*) and the ox of the *Beis Din* (*par ha'eda*) are both present: *par mashiach kodem lepar ha'eda bechol ma'asav* — the *Kohen Gadol*'s ox comes first in all its rites.

It should be noted that neither principle maps neatly onto this case. The *par* of the *Kohen Gadol* is hardly more frequent than the *par* of the *Beis Din* — both arise only in rare circumstances. And the claim that the *Kohen Gadol*'s ox is more sanctified requires some elaboration. The *Gemara* does not attempt to derive this particular ruling from the *tadir* or *mikudash* principles directly; instead, it proceeds to establish each principle independently and then returns to the ordering of the sacrifices.

### Tamid Priority

As a source for the *tadir* principle, Abaye cites the verse *milvad olas haboker asher le'olas hatamid* — "besides the morning sacrifice which is the daily sacrifice." The verse first says "morning sacrifice" and then adds "daily sacrifice." The repetition teaches, says Abaye, that the *tamid* always comes first: *kol detadir machavero kodem es chavero* — because the daily offering is more regular, it precedes all others. This is understood as a general principle, not merely a rule for this specific verse.

### Sanctity Source

For the *mikudash* principle, the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives it from the verse *vekedashto* — "you shall sanctify him," referring to the *Kohen*. This is understood to mean that one accords him priority in all matters of sanctity: *liftoach rishon ulvarech rishon* — to read from the Torah first, to lead the *zimmun* first, *ulitol mana yafa berishon* — and to receive the choicest portion first. One might note that receiving the largest food portion does not obviously qualify as a *davar shebikdusha*, unless understood as referring to the portions of *korbanot* — though in its immediate context the verse discusses the *Kohen* relative to other *Yisraelim*, not *korbanot* per se. Still, if the point is that all forms of priority accorded to someone holier are expressions and demonstrations of that greater holiness, the principle is coherent even if the derivation is somewhat circular.

### Kohen Gadol Ox

The *Gemara* now provides an independent source for the rule that the *par* of the *Kohen Gadol* precedes the *par* of the *Beis Din*. The *tanna* cites the verse *vesaraf oso ka'asher saraf es hapar harishon* — "and he shall burn it as he burned the first ox." Since the Torah had already established that the burning procedure is the same, why must it add the word "the first"? The word *harishon* teaches that the *par* of the *Kohen Gadol* must be brought first: *lelamed sheyakdim par kohein mashiach lepar he'elam davar shel tzibur bechol ma'asav*.

A *baraita* then explains the reasoning: *ho'il umashiach mechaper vetzibur miskaper* — the *Kohen Gadol* is the one performing atonement, and the community is the one receiving atonement. If one must choose, it is logical to first cleanse the *mechaper* before he atones for others: *din hu sheyakdim hamechaper lamiskaper*. The *Gemara* cites the *Yom Kippur* service as confirmation: *vechiper ba'ado uva'ad beiso uva'ad kol kehal Yisrael* — Aharon first atones for himself and the priestly household, and only then for the entire congregation of Israel. This is thus not a general claim that the *Kohen Gadol* outranks the *Beis Din* as an institution, but a specific *halacha* of atonement: the atoner must be purified before he purifies others.

### Beit Din Priority

Next: *par he'elam davar shel tzibur kodem lepar shel avoda zara* — the ox brought for the mistaken ruling of the *Beis Din* on a general matter (*par he'elam davar*) precedes the ox brought as the *olah* component of the *Beis Din*'s *korban* when their erroneous ruling concerned *avoda zara*. The reason is that one is a *chatas* and the other is an *olah*, and a *chatas* always precedes an *olah*. The *Gemara* derives this from a *binyan av*: the verse *vehibriv es asher lachatas rishonah* — "and he shall offer that which is for the *chatas* first" — establishes a general principle that in any pairing of *chatas* and *olah*, the *chatas* comes first. The principle holds even when the *chatas* is a bird and the *olah* is a large animal (*behemah*) — the *chatas* still precedes. The underlying logic is that one addresses the problem (sin) before proceeding to the elevation offering (*olah*), which is more of an aspirational gift. In this context, even though *avoda zara* is a weightier transgression, it is the *olah* that accompanies it which is being compared to the *chatas* of the regular ruling — and the *chatas* always goes first.

### Idolatry Hierarchy

The next ruling is striking: *par Avodah Zarah kodem l'sair Avodah Zarah* — the ox of the *avoda zara* communal *korban* comes before the goat, even though the ox is the *olah* and the goat is the *chatas*. This directly reverses the rule just stated. Two explanations are offered. The scholars of *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rabbah say: *chatas Avodah Zarah chasera aleph* — the word *l'chatas* in the *avoda zara* passage is written defectively, without the *aleph*, indicating that this *chatas* is somehow of a lesser status, and therefore the ox precedes it. Rava offers a different reading: *kamishpat ksiv bei* — the Torah uses the word *kamishpat* in describing the *korban* for *avoda zara*, and in that passage the Torah itself lists the ox first. Therefore, regardless of the general *chatas*-before-*olah* rule, the Torah's own sequencing in this specific *parsha* determines the order.

### Community vs. King

The ordering continues: *sair Avodah Zarah kodem l'sair nasi* — the communal goat for *avoda zara* precedes the king's goat for an ordinary sin. The reason: *hai tzibur v'hai yachid* — one represents the community and the other represents an individual. Even though the king might be conceived as the embodiment of the community, the *korban* for *avoda zara* brought by the *Beis Din* represents a communal sin and the institutional leadership acting in their communal capacity — whereas the king's *korban* is brought for his own private transgression. The community's sacrifice therefore takes priority over the king acting in his personal capacity.

### King Status

Next: *sair nasi kodem l'seiras yachid* — the king's goat precedes the goat of an ordinary individual. The reason: *hai melech v'hai hedyot* — one is a king and one is a commoner. Even when the king brings for a purely private sin and not in any public capacity, his status as king still gives him precedence over an ordinary person.

### Goat vs. Sheep

Finally: *seiras yachid kodemes l'kivsas yachid* — the she-goat of an individual precedes the ewe of an individual. A *baraita*, however, teaches the opposite: *kivsas yachid kodemas le-se'iras yachid*. Abaye resolves the contradiction by noting that this is a dispute among *Tannaim*. One view holds that the goat is weightier — *sheken nisraba etzel avoda zara* — because for the weightier sin of *avoda zara* specifically a goat is brought, not a sheep, which indicates the goat's elevated standing. The other view holds that the sheep is weightier — *sheken nisraba be-alya* — because a sheep possesses the fat tail (*alya*), which the goat lacks, making the sheep a superior offering in terms of the quality of what is presented to God.

### Grain Priority

The *Mishnah* adds: *Omer kodem le-keves ha-ba immo* — the *Omer* offering precedes the lamb that accompanies it, and *she-lechem kodem li-kvasim ha-ba'im immo* — the *shetei ha-lechem* of *Shavuot* precede the animals that accompany them. The overarching principle is: *davar ha-ba bigin le-yom kodem le-davar ha-ba bigin le-lechem* — what makes the day significant is the grain offering. On the second day of *Pesach*, it is the first cutting of the *Omer* barley that defines the day; on *Shavuot*, it is the wheat harvest and the *shetei ha-lechem*. The accompanying animal sacrifices are understood as secondary, brought in honor of the grain — and therefore the grain offering, as the focal object of the day's sanctity, precedes the animals.

## Man vs. Woman Priority

### Gender Priorities

Having concluded the discussion of sacrificial hierarchies, the *Mishnah* turns to triage among people — a territory that challenges the modern egalitarian ethos directly. The *Mishnah* states: *ha-ish kodem le-isha le-hachayoso u-le-hashiv aveida* — a man takes precedence over a woman in saving life and in returning a lost object. Conversely, *veha-isha kodemes le-ish le-chasos* — a woman takes precedence over a man in the matter of clothing, because a woman's embarrassment at being improperly clothed is greater than a man's. And *u-le-hotzi mi-beis ha-shvi* — a woman is redeemed from captivity before a man, because she is far more vulnerable to assault and to grave harm to her dignity and status in that situation.

### Equal Danger Rule

The *Mishnah* qualifies the captivity rule: *bizman she-shneihem omdim be-kalkala* — if both the man and the woman are equally endangered in captivity — that is, both face the risk of sexual assault — *ha-ish kodem le-isha*, the man is redeemed first. Rashi clarifies that this is not a return to the general opening principle of the *Mishnah*; rather, it reflects the specific calculus of the captivity situation: when the woman's distinctive vulnerability no longer distinguishes the two cases, the default ordering applies.

### Self & Family

A *baraita* further develops the hierarchy of obligations: if one finds oneself, one's father, and one's *rebbe* all taken captive with limited means of redemption, *hu kodem lerabbo* — one redeems oneself first. This reflects one of the foundational principles of Jewish ethics: *chayecha vechayei achicha chayecha kodmim* — your own life takes precedence over that of another. This does not mean that one spends all resources on personal comfort before attending to the poor, but in genuine life-threatening situations, self-preservation precedes obligation to others. Once one's own situation is secured, *rabbo kodem le'aviv* — one's *rebbe* comes before one's father, because the *rebbe* brought one into the life of the world to come, while a father brought one only into this world.

*Ve'immo kodemet lechullam* — the mother precedes others, as established in the *Mishnah*'s ruling that women are to be redeemed first from captivity. The *Gemara* does not spell out every permutation — for instance, the question of whether an unrelated woman would come before one's father when captivity is at stake is left unexplored.

### Torah over Kingship

*Chacham kodem lemelech Yisrael* — a *chacham* takes precedence over a king of Israel. This maps directly onto the broader questions running through *Horayot* about the hierarchy between Torah leadership and political authority: Torah leadership supersedes political power.

### Meritocracy Irony

The *Gemara* explains: *chacham shemet ein lanu kayotzei bo* — when a *chacham* dies, we have no one to replace him. *Melech Yisrael shemet kol Yisrael re'uyim lemalchut* — when a king of Israel dies, all of Israel is potentially eligible for kingship. The irreplaceability of the *chacham* thus grounds his priority. There is a deliberate irony here worth noting. The famous teaching that there are three crowns — *keter Torah*, *keter kehuna*, and *keter malchut* — points out that *keter Torah* is available to anyone who seeks it, while *keter malchut* belongs exclusively to the house of David. Yet it is precisely because *keter malchut* is hereditary rather than merit-based that the king is "replaceable" in the relevant sense: his position says nothing about his individual worth or attainment. The *chacham*, by contrast, occupies a meritocracy — his position is accessible to all but genuinely earned, and therefore he truly is irreplaceable in a way the king is not.

## Kohanim Hierarchy

### Kohen Gadol Grades

Within the category of *Kohanim*, the hierarchy descends as follows. A *Kohen Gadol* anointed with the sacred oil (*mashuach b'shemen hamishcha*) precedes one invested through garments (*merubeh begadim*). The one invested through garments precedes a past *Kohen Gadol* (*mashuach she'avar*). A past *Kohen Gadol* who was removed due to a seminal emission (*keri*) precedes one removed due to a bodily blemish (*mum*), because the disqualification caused by *keri* is temporary, while that caused by a *mum* is permanent — and a lesser or more recoverable disqualification retains greater priority over a more permanent one. A past *Kohen Gadol* disqualified by a blemish precedes the *Mashuach Milchama*, because having once held the status of *Kohen Gadol* — even if one can no longer serve — places one above a *Mashuach Milchama*, who holds an entirely different and lower office. The principle resembles the precedence an ex-president might be accorded over a sitting senator.

## Man vs. Woman Priority…

### King over Kohen

Returning to the cross-category hierarchy: *melech kodem l'kohen gadol* — the king precedes the *Kohen Gadol*. The source is the verse *vayomer hamelech lahem k'chu imachem avdei adoneichem* — "and the king said to them, take with you the servants of your master." This refers to David directing Tzadok and Nathan in the anointing of Shlomo, and demonstrates that the king gives orders to the *Kohen Gadol*. The organizational authority of the king over the *Kohen Gadol* thus determines the priority of the king in situations of triage or precedence.

### Kohen vs. Prophet

More striking is the next ruling: *kohen gadol kodem l'navi* — the *Kohen Gadol* precedes the *navi*. This is remarkable because the *navi* is God's direct oracle, while the *Kohen Gadol*'s role is the embodiment and performance of divine service — symbolic and institutional rather than prophetic. The source offered is *umshacho oso sham Tzadok hakohen v'Nasan hanavi* — in the verse describing the anointing of Shlomo, Tzadok the *Kohen* is listed before Nathan the *navi*: *hikdim Tzadok l'Nasan*. An additional source is cited: *shma na Yehoshua hakohen hagadol ata v'rei'echa* — "hear now, Joshua the High Priest, you and your companions." One might think *rei'echa* refers to ordinary people, but *talmud lomar ki anshei mofes hema* — "for they are men of wonder" — and *ein mofes ela navi*, wonder is a term that refers specifically to a *navi*, as in *v'nasan lecha os u'mofes*.

What accounts for the *Kohen Gadol*'s precedence over the *navi*? One reading is that the *navi*, however directly he channels the divine word, does not possess the power to alter the fundamental structure of *halacha* or the institutional fabric of society. His role is exhortation and transmission, not governance of the sacred service. The *Kohen Gadol*, by contrast, embodies and enables the people's ongoing access to God — he is the bridge through which *Klal Yisrael* approaches the divine on a regular basis, most dramatically on *Yom Kippur*. Hearing God's word through a *navi* is perhaps, counterintuitively, less central than being the instrument through which the people *reach* God. Whether a regular *Kohen* would similarly precede a *navi* is an interesting question the *Gemara* does not resolve here.

## Kohanim Hierarchy…

### Disqualification Types

As established above, a past *Kohen Gadol* who was removed due to *keri* precedes one removed due to a *mum* — because *keri* represents a temporary disqualification while a *mum* is permanent.

### War Priest Ranking

*Mashuach milchama kodem l'sgan* — the *Mashuach Milchama* precedes the *sgan* (*deputy Kohen Gadol*). The reason is that the *Mashuach Milchama* holds an independent role with full authority in his domain, whereas the *sgan* is merely the backup — his entire function is to stand in for the *Kohen Gadol* if needed.

### Administrative Ranks

Below the *Mashuach Milchama*, the hierarchy proceeds: *sgan kodem l'amarkal* — the *sgan* precedes the *amarkal*. What is an *amarkal*? Rav Chisda explains: *amar kula* — "he says everything," meaning all activities of the *Kohanim* go through his authority; he is the chief administrative officer of the *Kohanim*. The *amarkal* precedes the *gizbar* (treasurer); the *gizbar* precedes the *rosh mishmar* (head of the weekly division of *Kohanim*); the *rosh mishmar* precedes the *rosh beit av* (head of the daily group of *Kohanim*); and the *rosh beit av* precedes the ordinary *Kohen Hedyot*.

### Tumah Exception

A practical question is posed: *l'inyan tumah: sgan u'mashuach milchama eizeh mei-hem kodem* — regarding *tumah*, if both the *sgan* and the *Mashuach Milchama* are walking together and encounter a *met mitzvah* (an unattended corpse requiring burial), which one must become *tamei* to handle it, and which is more important to preserve in a state of *tahara*?

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Nachman cites a *baraita*: *mutav she-yitamei mashuach milchama v'al yitamei sgan* — it is preferable for the *Mashuach Milchama* to become *tamei* and not the *sgan*. The reasoning is that the *sgan*'s singular function is to be ready to step in should the *Kohen Gadol* become disqualified: *she-im yei'ara bo psul b'Kohen Gadol nichnas ha-sgan u'mishamesh tachtav*. If the *sgan* becomes *tamei*, he is entirely unable to fulfill this purpose. The *Mashuach Milchama*, by contrast, can still perform his role in the military context even while *tamei*.

One might object: but does the *Mishnah* not say that the *Mashuach Milchama* precedes the *sgan* in priority? Rav Ada resolves this: *ki tanya ha-hi l'hachayoto* — the ruling that the *Mashuach Milchama* takes priority refers to situations of life preservation and similar matters of general precedence, not to questions of preserving *tahara*. In terms of status, the *Mashuach Milchama* ranks above the *sgan*. But in terms of which one must guard his *tahara* for the sake of his job obligations, the *sgan*'s duty demands that he remain *tahor* above all others.

## Man vs. Woman Priority…

### Tribal Hierarchy

The *Mishnah* now lays out the full genealogical hierarchy. *Kohen* precedes *Levi*; *Levi* precedes *Yisrael*; *Yisrael* precedes *mamzer*; *mamzer* precedes *nasin*; *nasin* precedes *ger*; *ger* precedes *eved meshuchrar* (freed slave). The *Gemara* provides a scriptural basis for each step. The precedence of *Kohen* over *Levi* derives from: *vayivadel Aharon lehakdisho kodesh kodashim* — Aharon was separated out for a higher degree of sanctity than the rest of the tribe of Levi. The precedence of *Levi* over *Yisrael* derives from: *ba-eis hi hivdil Hashem es shevet ha-Levi mi-Yisrael* — God set the tribe of Levi apart from Israel. *Yisrael* precedes *mamzer* — *she-zeh meyuchas ve-zeh einu meyuchas* — one has unimpaired *yichus* and the other does not, even if the *mamzer*'s parents may themselves be of distinguished lineage; their act of sin taints the *yichus* of the offspring. *Mamzer* precedes *nasin* — *she-zeh ba mi-tipah kesheirah* — a *mamzer* is born of Jewish parents, even if through a forbidden union; a *nasin*, though converted generations ago, is regarded as not being of original Jewish stock. *Nasin* precedes *ger* — *zeh gadal imanu bi-kdushah ve-zeh lo gadal imanu bi-kdushah* — the *nasinim* have been part of the Jewish community in holiness for generations, whereas a new convert has only recently emerged from the non-Jewish world. *Ger* precedes *eved meshuchrar* — *zeh hayah bi-khlal arur ve-zeh lo hayah bi-khlal arur* — the freed slave was once in the category of *arur Kanaan*, the cursed status of the slave, whereas the convert was not.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai notes that by the inner logic of the *nasin*-*ger* distinction, a freed slave — who was raised in a Jewish household — should precede a *ger* who was not. Yet the ruling is reversed, and the slave's former status of *arur* overrides the experiential advantage of having been raised in Jewish surroundings.

### Equality Condition

The *Mishnah* then states: *eimasai? bizman shekulam shavim* — when does this entire hierarchy apply? Only when all parties are equal in all other respects. This qualification is significant, and it substantially undermines the practical application of the hierarchy.

### Torah Merit Overrides

The *Mishnah* immediately introduces the case that overrides the genealogical hierarchy entirely: *aval im hayah mamzer talmid chacham v'Kohen Gadol am ha'aretz, mamzer talmid chacham kodem l'Kohen Gadol am ha'aretz* — if the *mamzer* is a *talmid chacham* and the *Kohen Gadol* is an *am ha'aretz*, the *mamzer talmid chacham* comes first. The ultimate hierarchy is not one of birth or institutional status but one of *Torah* — a meritocracy. The *Gemara* supplies the source: *yekarah hi mi-pninim* — the *Torah* is more precious than pearls (*pninim*), and by way of *drash*, more precious than the *Kohen Gadol* who enters *lifnai ve-lifnim*, the innermost chamber. The three crowns clarify the structure: *keter malchut* is restricted to the house of David, *keter kehuna* to the descendants of Aharon — but *keter Torah* is open to all. As the *Rambam* emphasizes, it is available to every man and woman, rich and poor alike.

### Rav Moshe Reading

This brings us to a practically momentous question raised in a responsum addressed to Rav Moshe Feinstein. A physician in a triage situation asked: does this *Mishnah* actually require saving the life of a man before a woman, or a *Kohen* before a *Yisrael*?

Rav Moshe focused his analysis on the words *bizman shekulam shavim* — "when all are equal." He argued that *kulam shavim* means equal in *all* respects — not merely in terms of *Torah* scholarship, but in every possible relevant factor: the urgency and severity of the medical condition, the order of arrival, any other rational, social, or logical criterion that could bear on the decision. If any such factor exists that would distinguish between the two cases — and some relevant factor almost always will exist — then the hierarchy of the *Mishnah* simply does not apply. Rav Moshe's reading effectively renders the *Mishnah*'s genealogical hierarchy applicable only in a situation of complete and absolute equivalence across every conceivable dimension — a situation that in practice will never occur. This is a ruling of enormous significance for the relationship between traditional hierarchy and egalitarian ethics in a modern medical or emergency context.

## Animal Behavior

### Dogs vs. Cats

The *Gemara* presents a series of questions addressed to Rabbi Eliezer. Why does a dog recognize its owner while a cat does not? The *Gemara*'s answer connects to the immediately preceding discussion: *umah ha'ochel mima she'achbar ochel meshake'ach ha'ochel* — one who eats from what a rat has nibbled on is caused to forget; *ochel achbar atzmo al achat kama vechama* — if eating something nibbled by a rat causes forgetting, then eating rats themselves causes forgetting to a far greater degree. Cats, who constantly eat rats and mice, therefore do not remember who their owners are.

### Rat Dominance

A second question: why do human beings have dominance over rats — why are rats universally chased and exterminated? *Mipnei shesoran ra* — because their path or inclination is bad. What does this mean in practice? Rava says: *afilu glimi gaytzi* — they even nibble on clothes, which are not food at all. Rav Papa adds: *afilu shufsha mara gaytzi* — they even gnaw on the handle of a shovel. Because rats constantly nibble on things that serve no nutritional purpose to them, they are regarded as inherently destructive, and therefore humanity's domination over them is warranted.

## Learning Obstacles

### Forgetting Causes

The *Gemara* presents a list of five things that cause one to forget Torah learning: eating from food that a rat nibbled on; eating from food that a cat ate from; eating the heart of an animal; eating too many olives; and drinking water left over from washing. Some add: keeping one's clothes under one's pillow. If counted carefully, the list yields six items, requiring some combination of entries to reach five.

### Restoring Learning

Five things restore Torah learning: bread baked on coals — and all the more so eating the coals themselves (these count as one); boiled eggs without salt; frequent use of olive oil; wine together with spices; water left over from kneaded dough; and some say, dipping one's finger in salt and eating it.

### Olive Paradox

The item of olive oil prompts a citation from Rabbi Yochanan: *keshem shehazayit meshake'ach limud shel shivim shana* — just as eating olives causes one to forget seventy years of learning, *kach shemen zayit meshiv limud shel shivim shana* — olive oil restores seventy years of learning. The olive and its oil thus stand in a precise paradoxical relationship: the fruit brings forgetting, the oil brings restoration.

### Wine & Salt

Wine together with fragrances: *chamra vereichani pikchin* — wine and pleasant scents sharpen the intellect. Regarding dipping one's finger in salt: Resh Lakish specifies that this applies to *achat* — one finger only. This is presented as a dispute among *Tannaim*: Rabbi Yehuda says one finger and not two; Rabbi Yossi says two fingers and not three. The mnemonic is the word *kmitza* — the three-finger grip used in the *Kohen*'s taking of the handful of flour in the *mincha* offering — helping one recall the sequence: one, two, three.

### Learning Obstacles

Ten things make Torah study difficult. Walking under a camel's saddle — and all the more so under the camel itself; walking between two camels; a man walking between two women or a woman walking between two men. These latter cases reflect a society in which gender separation was standard, and such crossings were considered disruptive. Walking under the odor of a carcass; Rashi explains this means the smell is wafting over one, which creates a prohibition against learning Torah in such conditions. Walking under a bridge that has not had water flowing beneath it for the last forty days. Eating bread that has not fully cooked.

### Death-Related

Additional obstacles: eating meat that is tainted or skimmed from the cooking ladle (*basar mizuhama*); drinking from a water channel that flows through a cemetery; gazing directly at the face of a dead body; and some add, reading the inscriptions written on gravestones. One might observe a certain irony in the last prohibition: if reading gravestone inscriptions impedes the capacity for Torah learning, one might ask why we take the trouble to write them. The *Gemara* does not address this.
