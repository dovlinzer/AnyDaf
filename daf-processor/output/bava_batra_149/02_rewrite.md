# Bava Batra 149 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Matnat Shchiv Mera

### Kol vs. Miktzat Nechasav

Today's daf is 149, and we continue our treatment of the different parameters of *matnat shchiv mera*. Yesterday's discussion centered on the fundamental distinction that determines whether a gift falls within the rabbinic category of *matnat shchiv mera*: namely, whether it constitutes *kol nechasav*, a gift of all one's assets, or *miktzat nechasav*, a gift of only some of one's assets. In the case of *kol nechasav*, the rabbinic institution of *matnat shchiv mera* applies, meaning that no formal *kinyan* is required and the operative principle of *amar chozer* — that if the *shchiv mera* recovers he may retract the gift — is in force.

### Amar Chozer Principle

The Gemara continued to probe the boundary between *kol nechasav* and *miktzat nechasav*, noting that even when a gift appears to involve all one's assets, there may be property whose existence is unknown, which would place many more situations within the category where the gift cannot be retracted upon recovery. The Gemara also addressed the more technical question of whether, when a *shchiv mera* gives away everything and then retracts, the retraction is full or partial — with all the attendant implications for which situations involve a straightforward *kinyan* and which involve the institution of *matnat shchiv mera*.

## Hedkesh, Hefker, Tzedakah

### Hedkesh Case

The Gemara now poses a series of questions beginning with the case of *hedkesh*: a person on his deathbed declares that all his property should be sanctified to the *Beis Hamikdash*. The question — *hikdish kol nechasav ve'amad mahu* — is whether, if he recovers, the principle of *amar chozer* applies and he may retract, or whether the dedication to *hedkesh* stands.

Two considerations pull in opposite directions. On one side: *kol lgabei hekdesh gamar u'makni* — perhaps, when it comes to the *Beis Hamikdash*, the person fully intends the dedication to take effect regardless of whether he survives. On the other side: *kol lgabei nafshei lo gamar u'makni* — at the end of the day, the person has his own financial needs, and we may assume he would not wish to be left penniless upon recovery. The Gemara leaves this unresolved.

### Hefker Case

The next case asks whether the same analysis applies when a *shchiv mera* declares *hefker* — that all his property should be ownerless, available to anyone who takes it. Here the argument for the dedication standing is framed somewhat differently: *keivan de'af le'aniym k'ashirim gamar u'makni* — since he is making his property available even to the poor, who are genuinely in need, perhaps his intent is wholehearted regardless of whether he survives. Against this: *kol lgabei nafshei lo gamar u'makni* — he too has his own needs, and if he recovers, we assume he did not intend for the renunciation to be irrevocable. Again, the Gemara leaves this unresolved.

### Tzedakah Case

The third case involves a *shchiv mera* who distributes all his property directly to the poor — *chilek kol nechasav le'aniym mahu*. The argument that this gift should stand unconditionally is strongest here: *tzedakah vadai migmar gamar u'makni* — when giving directly to the poor, who are genuinely destitute, surely his intention is complete and irrevocable. Against this, again: *kol lgabei nafshei lo gamar u'makni* — even charity does not override his own survival needs. The Gemara gives a *teiku*: the question is unresolved in all three cases.

### Psychology & Rashbam

How one reads the progression of these three cases is itself a matter of dispute. The natural reading of the Gemara suggests an escalating presumption in favor of the gift's irrevocability: even if we assume the *shchiv mera* would retract in the case of *hedkesh*, and even if we assume the same for *hefker*, perhaps by *tzedakah* — where the poor are directly and immediately helped — he genuinely intends the gift to stand. The Gemara's language for the *tzedakah* case, *tzedakah vadai migmar gamar u'makni*, sounds very much like the strongest argument in favor of irrevocability.

The Rashbam reads the progression in the opposite direction: even if we assume by *hekdesh* that the person wants the sanctification to stand, and even if we assume the same for *hefker*, perhaps by *tzedakah* he would ultimately want to retain his assets if he recovers. This reading does not sit easily with the Gemara's language. The underlying question — to which of these causes is a person psychologically most committed, to the point of being willing to part with his assets even upon recovery? — is genuinely interesting. One might observe that people often give more generously to prestigious institutions than to the poor, which would support reading the *tzedakah* case as the one where the person is least likely to have fully committed. But the Gemara's own language points the other way.

A student raised the question of whether *hedkesh* is a special case because, unlike a gift to the poor or a declaration of *hefker*, sanctification takes effect through speech alone, much as the *shchiv mera*'s words themselves effect a transfer. In response, it was noted that *hefker* similarly takes effect through speech, and that the entire force of *divrei shchiv mera* is precisely that his words function like an act of *kinyan* — so the distinction does not ultimately change the analysis in a clean way. A further version of these questions is cited more tersely — *hikdish kol nechasav mahu, hifkir kol nechasav mahu, chilek kol nechasav la'aniyim mahu, teiku* — and while it omits the arguments on each side, the fuller version better conveys the reasoning. What emerges from the discussion, in any case, is that *divrei shchiv mera* have legal force even in these unusual contexts — *hedkesh*, *hefker*, and gifts to the poor — sufficient to raise the question of whether the full transfer has been effected.

## Gift vs. Inheritance Lan…

### Valid Gift Terms

Rav Sheshet teaches that if a *shchiv mera* uses any of the following terms in his *tzava'ah* — *yitol*, *yizke*, *yachzik*, *yikne* — these are all valid formulations of a gift (*kulam lashon matanah hen*) and effect a proper *matnat shchiv mera*.

### Inheritance Language

A *baraita* adds that even the terms *yachsin* and *yeiraish* — both expressions drawn from the language of inheritance — are valid. This raises an important conceptual question: is *matnat shchiv mera* fundamentally a gift, or a pseudo-inheritance? The question has two dimensions — one concerns how the *shchiv mera* must frame his transfer, and the other concerns how the halakha treats its effects. We have already seen ways in which *matnat shchiv mera* operates like an inheritance, such as its capacity to transfer a loan; we will continue to see this. But does the person on his deathbed have to use the language of a *matanah*, even if the legal effect is structured like a *yerushah*?

### Rabbi Yochanan Exception

The Gemara qualifies the *baraita*'s ruling: *bera'uy leyorsho Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka hi*. The inheritance language is not a standard formulation for *matnat shchiv mera* in general; it is effective only within the framework of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, who holds that a *shchiv mera* may use inheritance language when directing his estate to someone who qualifies as an heir in some degree. Within that view, there are further questions about whether the person must be a first-order heir or whether a more distant heir may be elevated to primary status — but the Gemara's point here is that inheritance language as a vehicle for *matnat shchiv mera* belongs to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's position, not to the standard halakhic framework. If we do not rule in accordance with Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, the *shchiv mera* must use language of *matanah*, not *yerushah*.

This does not, however, close the question of whether we conceptualize the legal impact of *matnat shchiv mera* as ultimately resembling a *yerushah* or a *matanah*. The language required is that of *matanah*; but the structure of its effects, as we have already begun to see and will continue to see, has features that align with *yerushah*. It is a genuine hybrid. The distinction matters practically as well — a student noted that in many legal systems gifts and inheritances are taxed differently, and within halakha the rights of a widow and daughters to the estate differ depending on whether property was gifted, sold, or transmitted through inheritance.

## Ambiguous Benefit Langua…

### Yehana Bahen Question

The Gemara turns to a series of ambiguous formulations used by a *shchiv mera* and asks whether they constitute a full transfer. If the dying person says *yehana bahen* — let Ploni derive benefit from my property — what does this mean? Does it mean that Ploni should own the property outright, so that ownership itself is the source of his benefit? Or does it mean merely that Ploni should be allowed to derive some benefit — use the property periodically, for instance — without owning it?

### Other Ambiguous Terms

The same question applies to several related expressions. *Yeiraeh bahen* — let him be seen with the property — does this mean he should be recognized as the owner, or merely that he should have some occasion to appear with it? *Yaamod bahen* — let him stand upon it, in the sense of relying on it as a foundation — does this mean the property should be his, as one stands on and relies on one's own possessions, or does it merely mean some form of material support? *Yishaein bahen* — let him lean upon it — similarly: does this connote full ownership and support, or a more limited benefit?

### Teiku & Lo Kana

The Gemara leaves all of these formulations unresolved: *teiku*. The practical halakhic consequence, however, is clear. The Rambam rules *lo kana* — the recipient does not acquire — and the *Shulchan Aruch* in *Choshen Mishpat* 253:2 concurs, ruling straightforwardly *lo kana*. It is striking that a *teiku* resolves here in a definitive *lo kana* rather than a hedged position, though the logic is discernible: the *yorshim* are the *muchzak*, and the rabbinic institution of *matnat shchiv mera* should not be extended to ambiguous formulations without a clear basis. The resolution is stated without qualification, as a definitive ruling rather than merely a probabilistic outcome of doubt.

## Sale of All Assets

### Machar Kol Nechasav

The Gemara now asks a question one can readily imagine arising in practice: *machar kol nechasav mahu* — what is the rule if a *shchiv mera* sells all of his assets and then recovers? Does *amar chozer* apply, such that he can undo all the sales?

The grounds for *amar chozer* in standard *matnat shchiv mera* are rooted in *amdinan dateih* — we assess the person's intent and determine that he acted as he did only because he expected to die. If that assessment underlies the entire framework, then perhaps a *shchiv mera* who liquidates all his assets on the assumption of imminent death should similarly be able to reverse those transactions upon recovery.

### Devarim Shebalev Issue

Two powerful objections, however, cut against this. First, a sale is a two-sided transaction. The entire rationale for *amar chozer* — relying on the *shchiv mera*'s unexpressed intent — depends on the principle that in one-sided transactions, such as a *matanah* or a *get*, we may give legal weight to what was in the person's heart. But *devarim shebalev einam devarim* — unspoken conditions of the heart have no legal force — is precisely the rule that governs bilateral transactions, where a counterparty has entered the transaction in good faith without having stipulated to any condition of reversal. To undo a purchase because of the seller's undisclosed intent seems fundamentally unfair to the buyer.

Second, and relatedly, a person who has liquidated assets has not lost wealth — he simply holds it in a different form. He still has money with which to support himself and to leave to his heirs; it is simply now cash rather than property. There is no obvious harm to reverse.

Against these objections, one might argue that when everyone dealing with a *shchiv mera* knows his condition, the circumstances function not as *devarim shebalev* — purely private intentions — but as *devarim shebalev kol adam*, matters so universally understood that they operate more like explicit stipulations. A buyer purchasing assets from a dying person, in a face-to-face community context, arguably acquires with full awareness of the conditions. This would be a form of *anan sahadi*, where the communal knowledge is so evident that it stands in for an explicit declaration.

### Rav's Teaching

The Gemara records two versions of a ruling in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav — one holding *im amad eino chozer*, that recovery does not void the sale, and one holding *im amad chozer*, that it does. The Gemara reconciles these not as a dispute but as reflecting different circumstances: *ha de-is leh zuzei be-einaihu ve-ha de-parinehu be-chovo* — one ruling applies when the money from the sales remains on hand, and the other when the money was used to pay off debts.

If the proceeds were used to retire existing debts, then *amad eino chozer* — the transaction stands even if he recovers. The logic is that by applying the money toward a definite purpose — clearing liabilities — the *shchiv mera* demonstrated that the sale was genuinely intended to accomplish something real, not merely to transfer assets under the assumption of death. The funds are gone, the debts are settled; there is nothing to reverse and no basis to assume the sale was contingent. If, on the other hand, the money simply sits untouched, then *amad chozer* — he can reclaim it upon recovery — because the continued holding of the liquid proceeds suggests that his original intent was indeed predicated on dying, and upon recovery that predicate fails.

What remains striking, even with this reconciliation, is that the halakha entertains *amar chozer* even in the context of a bilateral commercial transaction. The typical force of *devarim shebalev einam devarim* would seem to preclude this. The resolution appears to rest on the quasi-public nature of a *shchiv mera*'s circumstances, in which the contingent character of his transactions may be sufficiently transparent to all parties to override the standard rule.

## Hoda'ah & Issur Giyora

### Shchiv Mera Hoda'ah

The Gemara now turns to a genuinely new question: *shchiv mera shehoda mahu* — what is the rule when a *shchiv mera* does not say "I want Ploni to have this field," but instead admits that the field has always belonged to Ploni? He claims, in effect, that what everyone assumed was his property is not his at all.

The concept at work here is *hoda'at baal din*, the admission of a litigant against himself. As a general principle, *hoda'at baal din kemeah edim dami* — a party's admission against his own interest is equivalent to a hundred witnesses. If a person stands before witnesses and, in a serious and deliberate context, declares that a specific piece of property belongs to someone else, that admission is binding regardless of whether it reflects objective reality. The admission, made under circumstances that exclude the possibility of idle joking, creates a legal reality that overrides even overwhelming contrary evidence. The person against whom the admission runs cannot subsequently retract by claiming it was false.

This mechanism is a powerful tool for transferring property without requiring a formal *kinyan*. By simply admitting that the property was always the other person's — even when this is counterfactual — one creates a binding legal entitlement in the other person. Why, then, is there even a question about whether this works for a *shchiv mera*?

The Rashbam explains that the question arises not because the *shchiv mera* lacks the power to make such an admission, but because there is a countervailing consideration specific to the deathbed context. The Gemara elsewhere records that if a *shchiv mera* says "I owe Reuven a million dollars," we do not necessarily credit that claim, because we suspect he is making it *shelo lehasbia et banav* — so that his children will not appear wealthy and thereby become targets for unscrupulous creditors or predators. The fear that strangers will try to take advantage of young heirs who appear to have inherited substantial wealth provides a motive for the dying person to make false debt claims. Given this, one might think that similar skepticism should extend to all admissions by a *shchiv mera*. The question is whether the skepticism is categorical or confined to claims of debt.

### Issur Giyora Story

The Gemara responds: *ta shema* — come and learn from the following episode. A convert named Issur — referred to as Issur Giyora — had in his early life taken captive a daughter of the sage Shmuel named Rachel, had relations with her, and she conceived. He subsequently converted while she was pregnant. The son born of this union was Rav Mari bar Rachel — known by his mother's name, since his father had been a non-Jew at the time of conception. Issur Giyora had twelve thousand *zuzei* deposited with Rava — apparently cash held in Rava's home for safekeeping.

### Rava's Dilemma

As Issur Giyora lay dying, Rava asked: *heichi kaninhu Rav Mari l'hanei zuzei* — how could Rav Mari acquire this money? Rava, who held the cash, was working through the available legal avenues. If Rav Mari should inherit it, the answer is that *lav bar yerusha hu* — he cannot inherit from his father. The Rashbam clarifies that the problem is patrilineal: since Issur Giyora was not Jewish at the time of conception, Rav Mari bears no halakhic relationship to him through patrilineal descent, which is the operative channel for inheritance. Although Rav Mari might in principle inherit through his mother, the relevant *yerushah* here is from father to son, and that relationship does not exist in halakha.

### Matnat Like Inheritance

This leads to the application of a principle we have been developing: *matnat shchiv mera k'yerushah shavyuha rabanan* — the Rabbis structured *matnat shchiv mera* to function like an inheritance. The consequence is: *kol heicha d'leisa b'yerusha leisa b'matana* — wherever inheritance is inapplicable, the rabbinic institution of *matnat shchiv mera* is equally inapplicable. If the mechanism depends on functioning like an inheritance, then a recipient who cannot stand in the position of an heir cannot receive through this vehicle.

### No Patrilineal Heir

The Rashbam reads the problem as applying to Rav Mari specifically: he is in a position of *leisa b'yerusha* through patrilineal descent, and therefore cannot receive a *matnat shchiv mera* from Issur Giyora. By extension, a *ger* who has no halakhic heirs, or any individual who bears no patrilineal relationship to the dying person, would be similarly excluded. This is a striking and somewhat counterintuitive limitation of *matnat shchiv mera*, underscoring how deeply the institution is structurally tied to the laws of *yerushah*, even though it requires the language of *matanah* to be effected.

### Failed Kinyan Methods

Rava proceeded to enumerate all possible methods by which Rav Mari might otherwise acquire the money:

Could Rav Mari simply come and take possession by *meshicha*? *Rachok migabei* — he is away at the yeshiva, and there is no time for him to come and physically take the money before his father dies.

Could the transfer be accomplished through *chalipin*, the symbolic exchange that effects a *kinyan*? *Ein matbe'a nikna bechalipin* — coins cannot be acquired through *chalipin*. That mode of acquisition works for land and movable goods but not for currency itself.

Could Issur Giyora transfer the money *agav karka* — by simultaneously conveying a piece of land, with the cash being transferred alongside? *Leit lei ara* — he has no land. Had he possessed even a tiny plot, he could have used it to effect the transfer of the cash.

Could a *ma'amad shelishtan* be arranged — the three-party standing meeting in which the creditor directs the depositor to transfer the funds to a third party, and the transfer is thereby effected? *Ishlach li lo azlina* — if Issur Giyora were to send for Rava to participate in such a proceeding, Rava would simply not go. This last point is deeply troubling. It makes plain that Rava was, at minimum as a matter of objective fact, obstructing the only remaining avenue by which the man whose money he held could direct it to his son. Whether Rava's motives were entirely proper is a question the Gemara invites us to confront.

### Udita Solution

At this point, Rav Ika bar Rav Ami objected and proposed a solution: *ve'odei Issur dehanei zuzei deRav Mari ninhu* — let Issur Giyora simply admit, before witnesses, that the money Rava is holding is not actually his. Let him declare that some time ago — a week, a year, whenever — he had already transferred the money to Rav Mari through a legitimate act of *kinyan*, perhaps a conveyance of land. This may be entirely counterfactual; Issur Giyora may never have done any such thing. But if he states it in front of witnesses, in a context that is clearly serious and deliberate, the admission is binding. *Hoda'at baal din kemeah edim dami* — his word against himself is worth a hundred witnesses, and what was Rava's claim to hold now becomes Rav Mari's property as a matter of law.

The mechanism is sometimes referred to as *kinyan udita* — acquisition through admission. It is not a standard mode of *kinyan* in the technical sense, but it functions in an analogous way: it creates a legal reality through a formal declaration, bypassing the need for actual physical transfer or any of the standard modes of acquisition. The fact that the Gemara frames it as *v'kaninu b'udita* — "let him acquire through the *udita*" — reflects the recognition that the admission serves as a practical substitute for a *kinyan*, even when everyone involved understands that the underlying factual claim is a legal fiction.

### Issur's Admission

Word of this solution reached Issur Giyora while the discussion was still ongoing, and he immediately made the admission before witnesses. The money was thereby transferred to Rav Mari bar Rachel. He had accomplished what he set out to do.

### Rava's Reaction

Rava was upset. His recorded reaction was: *ka magmrei ta'anta l'inshei umasdei li* — "you are teaching people claims and causing me a loss." The language is striking and, on its face, damning. The word *ta'anta* — claim, argument, legal stratagem — carries connotations of coaching a litigant in how to prevail in court, sometimes even through means that do not reflect the true underlying facts. And Rava explicitly identifies the result as a financial loss to himself. The plain reading of the episode is that Rava, holding money entrusted to him by a dying man who wished it to go to his son, was actively working to prevent that transfer and retain the money for himself through the operation of the rules governing a *ger shemet* — the property of a convert who dies without halakhic heirs, which becomes ownerless and is taken by whoever seizes it first.

### Rava Apologetics

The Soncino translation offers an extended apologetic note, arguing that Rava had no intention whatsoever of retaining the money, that the entire discussion of the available legal avenues was a pedagogical exercise for his students, that no one in his academy for a moment suspected the master of self-interest, and that his final exclamation of displeasure was nothing more than mild irony — the Soncino even arguing that Rava's public discussion of the matter, knowing that Rav Mari was himself a student and would hear of it, was actually evidence of his honor and good faith. The note concludes that Rava had intended to pass the money to Rav Mari as a generous personal gift rather than by legal compulsion, and was merely disappointed that the technical halakhic resolution preempted his own beneficent gesture.

This reading is, to put it plainly, extraordinarily strained. It is the kind of apologetics one associates with the ArtScroll tradition rather than with the more critical Soncino approach, a point that is all the more striking given the Soncino's usual tone. The apologetics were almost certainly shaped by the audience for whom the Soncino was written — a British public audience, including non-Jews, for whom the spectacle of a leading Talmudic sage apparently attempting to keep money entrusted to his care by a dying convert would have been acutely embarrassing.

One does note that a more charitable reading has some textual support: Rabbenu Yonatan of Lunel, an early Rishon, is quoted by Steinsaltz as holding that Rava did not intend to keep the money. And it is at least plausible that Rava's public airing of the issue, knowing that Rav Mari was present and would learn of it, reflects something other than pure self-interest. But the text remains what it is, and the discomfort is real.

### Debt vs. Property

What we do derive definitively from the story is that *hoda'ah* by a *shchiv mera* is effective. The Gemara uses this episode as the answer to its earlier question of *shchiv mera shehoda mahu*. And the Rashbam now addresses the apparent tension with the other *sugya* concerning *shelo lehasbia et banav*, where a *shchiv mera*'s claims of debt are regarded with suspicion.

The Rashbam distinguishes between two types of admission. A claim of debt — "I owe a million dollars to Reuven" — affects how the entire estate appears. A large apparent debt changes people's perception of the estate's worth, makes the heirs seem less wealthy, and thereby protects them from exploitation. The motivation of *shelo lehasbia et banav* is plausible and powerful in this context. Moreover, being known as heavily indebted carries social stigma, providing yet another incentive for a dying person to fabricate debt claims on behalf of his children's protection.

By contrast, an admission that a specific, identified piece of property belongs to a specific person is concrete and particularized. There is no general distortion of the estate's image, no social stigma, and the claim is much harder to attribute to an ulterior protective motive. When Issur Giyora admits that the twelve thousand *zuzei* held by Rava belong to Rav Mari, this is a targeted claim about a specific asset and a specific person, not a vague cloud cast over the entire estate. The Rashbam therefore holds that such targeted property admissions are binding even when made by a *shchiv mera*, while generalized debt claims remain subject to skepticism.

### Kinyan Udita

The upshot is the concept of *kinyan udita* — the recognition that formal admission before witnesses can function as an effective means of property transfer, even in the absence of any of the standard modes of *kinyan*. It almost operates as a species of *kinyan* in its own right, though technically it derives its force from the principle of *hoda'at baal din*. The practical significance is considerable: where all standard *kinyan* mechanisms fail — whether due to the absence of land, the impossibility of *meshicha*, the inapplicability of *chalipin* to coins, or the refusal of a necessary party to cooperate — a carefully constructed admission before witnesses can accomplish what formal *kinyan* cannot. The legal fiction is transparent to all parties, but its binding force is undiminished thereby.

## Shiyur: Leaving Over

### Shayar Karka Rule

We now turn to the question of *shiyur* — the requirement that in order for a *matnat shchiv mera* to be binding even upon recovery, the *shchiv mera* must have retained something. The basic rule: *shayar karka kol shehu matnaso kayemet* — if the *shchiv mera* left over even the smallest amount of land, his gift stands and cannot be retracted upon recovery. This is the rule of *miktzat nechasav* as opposed to *kol nechasav*.

### Kidei Parnasaso

The Gemara immediately asks: *vekama kol shehu* — how much is this "smallest amount"? The answer, given in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav, is: *karka kidei parnasaso* — land sufficient to support the person's livelihood. The *kol shehu* is not literally a token quantity. The reason a retained parcel of land renders the gift binding is that it demonstrates the *shchiv mera* understood that if he survived he would not be left destitute; he made provision for himself. Therefore the definition of a meaningful *shiyur* is not a nominal sliver but an amount sufficient to provide an ongoing livelihood. This could be a substantial figure — land that can produce enough to live on for potentially many years — and is calibrated to the person's individual circumstances and cost of living.

### Rabbi Zeira Reading

Rav Yirmiya bar Abba then extends the ruling: *metaltelin kidei parnasaso* — even if what remains is not land but movable property (*metaltelim*), so long as it is sufficient to support the person's livelihood, it qualifies as a meaningful *shiyur* and the gift is binding. The underlying rationale, as Rabbi Zeira articulates, is that the Mishnah's mention of *karka* reflects the classical role of land as a reliable source of ongoing sustenance — *de'i kai, samich alei* — something one can build upon and live off of indefinitely. But if the person retains sufficient *metaltelim* to provide the same ongoing support, the purpose is equally served. Rabbi Zeira praises this approach, noting how precisely attuned — *kama mechavnin shemaata desavei* — the teaching of these earlier sages is in reaching toward the deeper intention rather than being captive to the literal formulation.

### Rav Yosef Challenge

Rav Yosef objects sharply: *umai kavanaso* — what exactly is so precisely attuned about this? The extension to *metaltelim* involves simply disregarding what the Mishnah says. *Karka tenan* — the Mishnah says land. And in the same breath, redefining *kol shehu* to mean *kidei parnasaso* likewise ignores the plain meaning: *kol shehu tenan* — the Mishnah says any amount, however small. Two liberties have been taken simultaneously — replacing *karka* with *metaltelim* and replacing *kol shehu* with a substantial quantity — and Rav Yosef sees no warrant for either.

### Abaye's Precedent

Abaye responds by challenging the premise that *karka* in the Mishnah is always to be read with strict literalism: *vechol heicha detani karka karka davka* — is it true that every Mishnaic use of *karka* means only land and nothing else? He brings a proof from the Mishnah concerning a master who writes all his property to his slave. The slave goes free, because the slave is himself part of the property and thereby acquires himself. However, if the master says he is giving everything except for *karka kol shehu* — a small amount of land — the slave does not go free, because the *karka kol shehu* reserved can be understood to refer to the slave himself, who is treated as *karka* in halakha.

Rabbi Shimon, however, holds that if the master uses the specific word *karka*, he means land and does not mean the slave. The slave does not go free only if the master says "except for one ten-thousandth" without specification — which might refer to the slave — but "except for land" clearly refers to something other than the slave, and the slave is therefore freed.

Abaye then cites a further teaching of Rav Dimi bar Yosef in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *asu metaltelim shiyur etzel eved* — if the master says "except for a small amount of *metaltelim*," that too prevents the slave from going free, because a slave can be described as *metaltelim* as well, being mobile and not fixed like land. Thus the word *metaltelim* can, in context, refer to a slave. This demonstrates that the Mishnah's use of specific terminology about property is not always strictly limited to its literal meaning.

### Why Karka Mentioned

The Gemara, however, pushes back on the proof. The case of the slave-freeing Mishnah used *karka* in a context where the surrounding material had been discussing *karka* extensively. Specifically, the beginning of that Mishnah cited Rabbi Akiva's teaching that *karka kol shehu* — any smallest amount of land — entails obligations of *pe'ah*, *bikkurim*, the validity of a *prosbul*, and the capacity to transfer *metaltelim* alongside land. Since the entire preceding context was about *karka*, the subsequent use of *karka* in the exception clause naturally drew upon that context, even though the actual rule extends to *metaltelim* as well. The word *karka* was used, in other words, because it was contextually appropriate, not because it was strictly limited to land. *Mishum hachi tana karka* — because of the earlier context, the Mishnah said *karka*, though it was not literally exclusive.

The force of the pushback on Abaye's proof is this: the principle that a word can be used non-literally (*lav davka*) requires explanation — one must account for why the non-literal word was used. In the slave case, there is a clear contextual explanation: the Mishnah was already speaking of *karka*. But in the case of *shiyur* by the *shchiv mera*, what would explain the Mishnah's use of *karka* if it did not literally mean *karka*? Without such an explanation, the plain meaning stands.

### Kol Shehu Question

The Gemara concludes this amud with the question hanging: when the Mishnah says *shayar karka kol shehu*, does *kol shehu* always and invariably mean the smallest imaginable quantity, or might *kol shehu* in certain contexts refer to a specific type or category of something?
