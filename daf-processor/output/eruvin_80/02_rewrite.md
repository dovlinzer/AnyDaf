# Eruvin 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shituf Mevo'os Setup

### Shituf Intro

The *mishna* on the middle of 79b opens with the question *keitzad mishtatfin bamavoi* — how does one perform a *shituf* of the *mavoi*? The mechanics begin with *maniach es hachavis*, setting down a barrel of wine, or alternatively dates, figs, or any other food, because unlike *eruv chatzeiros*, a *shituf mevo'os* does not require bread. Once the food is designated, one declares: *harei zu lechol bnei hamavoi* — this is for all the residents of the *mavoi*.

The underlying mechanism involves a shortcut available to anyone willing to act: one takes one's own food, declares it to be the *shituf*, and in doing so transfers partial ownership to every resident of the *mavoi*. The result is that each resident now holds a share in the barrel. This same requirement — of actually transferring ownership to others — applies, as we will see, to the other forms of *eruvin* as well, but the *mishna* introduces it here in the context of *shituf*.

### Zikui Requirement

The *mishna* states *umizakeh lahen* — and one must transfer ownership to the others. The requirement of *zikui* (transfer of ownership) rests on a basic principle of *kinyan*: you cannot transfer an object from your own possession to another without a second party. Even though the rule of *zachin le'adam shelo befanav* — one may do something meritorious on behalf of another even in that person's absence — permits the transfer without the recipient's knowledge or consent, it does not permit a transfer without any second party at all. Removing something from one hand and placing it in the other is not an act of transfer. A third party is required to serve as the receiving agent.

The practical illustration is straightforward: if one wishes to give a gift to someone who is absent, one may appoint a second individual to take possession on behalf of the intended recipient. The recipient need not know, because the transfer is purely to her benefit. But without any second party, the act of *zikui* is incomplete.

### Valid Recipients

Who may serve as this second party? The *mishna* enumerates the options: *umizakeh lahen al yedei bno u'vito hagedolim* — through one's adult sons or daughters, who are the most conveniently available persons in a household. This is precisely why, in practice, the community *rav* who establishes an *eruv* for an entire city will hand the food to a designated individual and declare it possessed on behalf of all residents — the congregants themselves need never be individually solicited.

Also valid are Jewish male and female servants — *avdo ve'shifchaso ha'ivrim* — because their Jewish status makes them legally independent persons. One may have a lien on their labor, but their capacity for ownership is their own; they constitute a separate *yad*, a separate domain of ownership. Likewise, one's wife — *al yedei ishto* — may serve as the receiving party.

What is excluded? *Ein m'zakeh lo al yedei bno u'vito haktanim* — one's minor children may not serve, because they are treated as an extension of the parent's legal domain rather than as independent owners. Nor may one's non-Jewish slaves serve — *lo al yedei avdo ve'shifchaso hakena'anim* — because, as the *mishna* states, *yadan k'yado*, their hand is like his hand. Placing the object in their possession is legally equivalent to keeping it in one's own hand.

### Age vs. Dependency

A question arises — and *Tosafos* engages with it directly — about whether the relevant distinction in the *mishna* is truly one of literal age (adult versus minor) or rather one of practical and financial dependence. This question is rooted in discussions in other passages of *Gemara*. The *sugya* at hand takes the *mishna* at face value: it is a question of adulthood, even if the adult child still lives in the parental home.

Another *Gemara*, however, suggests that what is truly decisive is not age per se but practical financial dependence. Under that view, a minor who is financially independent might, in unusual circumstances, be treated as a separate domain. *Tosafos* raises a difficulty with this: since when can a minor legally take possession of an object? In response, *Tosafos* notes that *shituf* occupies a hybrid position — it is partly a genuine *kinyan* but also partly a quasi-ritual act. The standards for the *kinyan* dimension may therefore be lower than they would be in a purely commercial context. Moreover, this is a case of *da'as acher makneh oso* — the transfer is driven by the active will of the one transferring, not by the initiative of the recipient. Since the recipient may be more passive in such a scenario, perhaps a minor could suffice even where one normally could not.

The conclusion of this *Gemara*, however, is not to pursue that complexity. The *mishna* is read straightforwardly: adulthood versus minority is the operative criterion.

### Erev Shabbat Apps

A related application arises in practice on *Erev Shabbos*. There is a *halacha* prohibiting *kinyan* on *Shabbos* — one may not formally transfer ownership on *Shabbos* itself. This creates a challenge when one wishes to bring a gift to a host: if the item is *letzorech Shabbos*, needed for *Shabbos* use, the transfer is permitted. But if the item is not clearly for *Shabbos* use — a book one might not read until after *Shabbos*, or a bottle of wine when the host already has an open one — the issue becomes more complicated.

A practical solution is to call over one's spouse or a *bar* or *bat mitzvah* child before *Shabbos* and say: take possession of this on behalf of the people we are visiting. Once possession is transferred *before Shabbos*, the item already belongs to the recipients, and there is no issue with carrying it on *Shabbos*. Alternatively, one may bring the item and declare explicitly that ownership will not transfer until after *Shabbos* — or until the item is actually consumed, whichever comes first.

The question is also raised: can the wife or other valid recipients serve not merely as passive receiving agents but as the active party initiating the *eruv* — for example, by knocking on neighbors' doors and collecting food? The answer is yes, and the *Gemara* addresses the matter in greater detail. The more nuanced question, to which the *Gemara* will return, is whether a wife acting as the active party may contribute food that legally belongs to her husband, without his knowledge or consent — a question rooted in the standard property arrangements of the Talmudic period, where the husband owned the assets and the wife was supported from them.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Hagba'ah Tefach

*Amar Rav Yehuda*: when transferring ownership of the barrel used for *shitufei mevo'os*, the receiving party must lift it off the ground a minimum of one *tefach*. This is the *kinyan* of *hagba'ah* — the formal act of acquisition through lifting — and a *tefach* is both the minimum and, apparently, the sufficient amount.

One might infer from this ruling that *hagba'ah* in general requires only a *tefach*. *Tosafos* cautions against that inference. There is in fact contradictory evidence elsewhere in the *Gemara* suggesting that *hagba'ah* may require three *tefachim* in other contexts — a significant amount, given that three *tefachim* is also the threshold below which *lavud* applies. *Tosafos* explains that no general rule should be derived from this case, because the *kinyan* here is, once again, partly genuine acquisition and partly ritual. The lower standard of one *tefach* may apply specifically here without setting a broader precedent.

### Pumbedita Rulings

*Amar Rava*: the following two rulings were both transmitted by the elders of *Pumbedisa* — *Shmuel*'s *yeshiva* — and specifically attributed to *Rav Yehuda*, the principal disciple of *Shmuel*'s leading students. The first is the ruling just mentioned regarding *hagba'ah* of a *tefach*.

The second ruling: *ham'kadesh im ta'am melo lugmav yatza, ve'im lav lo yatza* — one who recites *Kiddush* must drink a mouthful that fills one cheek; if one takes only a negligible sip, one has not fulfilled the obligation. *Tosafos* notes a difficulty here: it is known that the *kos shel Kiddush* must hold a *revi'is*, and another *Gemara* seems to suggest that *melo lugmav* exceeds a *revi'is*, which would create a problem — how can one be required to drink more than the cup contains? *Tosafos* resolves that *melo lugmav* is in fact less than a *revi'is*. The fuller treatment of this question belongs to the discussion in *Pesachim*.

A further statement attributed to the elders of *Pumbedisa*: *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel* — *osim medura l'chaya b'Shabbos* — one may kindle a bonfire for a woman who has just given birth on *Shabbos*. She is considered a *chola* with a *sakana*, and the fire is deemed medically necessary. The *Gemara* initially infers from this that the permission applies only to a *chaya* (a woman post-partum) and not to a regular *chola*, even one who is *yesh bo sakana*, and only in winter rather than summer. However, *Amar Rav Chiya bar Avin amar Shmuel* introduces a qualification: if someone has undergone bloodletting and feels chilly, one may make a bonfire on *Shabbos* even during the summer month of *Tammuz*. The concern for a sick person who feels cold overrides all seasonal considerations, and one does not hesitate.

### Asherah Debate

*Amar Ameimar*: another ruling was also transmitted by the elders of *Pumbedisa*. The question: *eizu hi ashera stam* — what type of tree can be presumed to be an *ashera* (an idolatrously worshipped tree, mentioned repeatedly in the *Torah* in contexts such as *ashiroteihen tisrefun ba'esh*) without explicit confirmation that it was used for idol worship?

*Rav* says: any tree *kol she'mishamshei avoda zara shomrim osah ve'ein to'amin mipiroseha* — that is guarded by the attendants of idol worship and whose fruit they refuse to eat. The combination of protective custody and refusal to consume the produce is a strong indicator of cultic veneration.

*Shmuel* takes a different approach: a tree whose fruit the worshippers designate for a specific cultic purpose — for example, if they say, we are using the dates of this tree to brew beer *de'Bei Netzarphei* (apparently a reference to a Zoroastrian fire-temple, as *Steinsaltz* suggests, connecting *netzarphei* to the root meaning *fire*) — and the beer is to be drunk on a religious festival. Here the tree is not itself worshipped, but its produce is consecrated to serve a cultic function.

Some *Rishonim* note that according to *Shmuel*'s criterion, the tree might constitute only a rabbinic extension of *ashera* rather than a full Biblically prohibited *ashera*, since a true *ashera* by strict definition must be an object of worship, not merely a provider of materials for religious rituals. In any case, the elders of *Pumbedisa* — whose sympathy for *Shmuel*'s positions is acknowledged, given *Rav Yehuda*'s connection to that tradition — conclude: *hilcheta k'vatei d'Shmuel*, the *halacha* follows *Shmuel*.

### Baraita Shituf

Returning now to the main topic, the *Gemara* raises an objection from a *baraita*: *keitzad mishtatfin be'mavoi?* — How does one make a *shituf* for a *mavoi*? One brings a barrel of wine, oil, dates, figs, or any other produce, since *shituf* (unlike *eruv chatzeiros*) does not require bread.

The *baraita* then draws a critical distinction: *im mishelo, tzarich lezakot* — if one is using one's own food, one must perform an act of transfer of ownership. *Ve'im mishelahen, tzarich lehodiya* — if the food happens to belong to the other residents (for example, stored in one's possession), one must at minimum inform them and obtain their awareness and agreement before using their food for the *shituf*.

As for the mechanics of transfer: *u'magbihah min ha'karka mashehu* — one lifts it slightly from the ground. The *Gemara* explains that *mashehu* (a little bit) here means a *tefach*, consistent with what was said earlier. The *Gemara* also notes that the lifting seems to be performed even in the case where the food already belongs to the others, suggesting that the lifting is not purely about *kinyan* — it also carries a ritual dimension, a formal designation of the material as belonging to the *shituf*.

### Rav No Transfer

With that *baraita* on the table, the *Gemara* now presents the famous dispute: *itmar, shitufei mevo'os — Rav amar ein tzarich lezakot*. Contrary to what the *mishna* explicitly states, *Rav* holds that one need not perform an act of *zikui* — a formal transfer of ownership — when establishing a *shituf mevo'os*. One may simply take one's own wine, designate it for the *shituf*, and be done.

*Rashi* explains the logic: the person initiating the *shituf* has a compelling personal interest in making it work, because without the *shituf* no one will be able to carry in the *mavoi* or *chatzer*. This high degree of *gemirat da'at* — of genuine and total commitment — is powerful enough that his intention alone can effect the transfer without a second party. The blocking of that outcome by others who are unavailable is precisely what grants his unilateral will sufficient legal force. *Tosafos* suggests an alternative framing: one might argue that the entire mechanism of *shituf* has been so thoroughly ritualized that the formal *kinyan* requirements are simply inapplicable. *Rashi*, however, does not go that far; he grounds the leniency in the heightened *gemirat da'at* of the initiator.

This position is explicitly against the *mishna*, which states *tzarich lezakot* — transfer is required. The *Gemara* will need to address how *Rav* can stand against the *mishna*.

### Shmuel Position

*Shmuel* takes the position consistent with the *mishna*: by *shituf mevo'os*, one does require a formal act of *zikui*. However, the debate between *Rav* and *Shmuel* involves a striking inversion when it comes to *eruv techumin*.

For *eruv techumin* — where one deposits food beyond the city limits on behalf of another person, allowing that person to extend their *Shabbos* walking boundary two thousand *amos* in that direction — *Rav* holds that *tzarich lezakot*: one must formally transfer the food to that person. *Shmuel* holds the opposite: by *eruv techumin*, *ein tzarich lezakot*, no formal transfer is needed.

The inversion is striking. Why would *Rav* demand transfer for *eruv techumin* but not for *shituf mevo'os*, and *Shmuel* demand it for *shituf mevo'os* but not for *eruv techumin*?

*Rav*'s reasoning: when establishing a *shituf mevo'os*, the initiator has a powerful personal stake — without the *shituf* the entire street is unusable — so his *gemirat da'at* alone is sufficient. But when doing an *eruv techumin* for someone else, as a favor, there is no equivalent personal urgency, and so the food must actually belong to the beneficiary through a formal transfer.

*Shmuel*'s reasoning (as *Tosafos* explains): *eruv techumin* is typically done *l'dvar mitzvah* — for a religious purpose — and this heightened motivation either substitutes for or mitigates the need for a formal *maaseh kinyan*. Alternatively, *eruv techumin* is less about creating a shared ownership structure (as *eruv chatzeiros* is) and more about designating a location — so the transfer requirements may be inherently lower. *Shmuel* agrees with the *mishna* that by *shituf* (and by implication *eruv chatzeiros*) transfer is required, and by *eruv techumin* it is not.

The *Gemara* notes that *Shmuel*'s position is internally coherent and consistent with the sources: where the *mishna* mentions *zikui*, *Shmuel* requires it; where the *mishna* is silent (as with *eruv techumin*), he does not. *Rav*'s position, however, contradicts the *mishna* directly on *shituf mevo'os*. The *Gemara* responds: *tannai hi* — this is in fact a *tannaitic* dispute, and *Rav* has a *tanna* to rely upon.

*Tosafos* also observes, in the course of this discussion, that there is no general principle making *eruv techumin* consistently stricter or more lenient than *eruv chatzeiros*. By some measures *eruv techumin* is stricter — for example, by *eruv techumin* each participant must contribute two meals' worth, whereas by *eruv chatzeiros*, if there are many residents, two meals' worth for all of them combined suffices, and if there are few, the size of a pressed fig per person. By other measures *eruv techumin* is more lenient. The two categories simply have different parameters, with no overarching hierarchy.

### Daughter-in-Law

To demonstrate that *Rav*'s position has a *tannaitic* basis, the *Gemara* cites a story: *maaseh be'kallasa shel Rabbi Oshaya* — an incident involving the daughter-in-law of *Rabbi Oshaya*. She had gone to the bathhouse on *Erev Shabbos* and was detained there as *Shabbos* fell. In her absence, her mother-in-law took care of the *eruv* on her behalf. The case came before *Rabbi Chiya*, who ruled it invalid.

*Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yossi* challenged that ruling, invoking the name of his father *Rabbi Yossi*: *kol she'ata yachol lehakel b'eiruv hakel* — wherever possible to be lenient in matters of *eruvin*, one should be lenient. This is a principle we have encountered repeatedly in the *masechet*: *halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv*.

Two questions surround the story: What exactly was the problem? Was it that the mother-in-law used her own food but failed to perform an act of *zikui* on behalf of the daughter-in-law? Or was it that the mother-in-law used the daughter-in-law's own food but did so without the daughter-in-law's knowledge?

*Rashi* understands this as a case of *eruv techumin*: the daughter-in-law was stranded outside the city, and the mother-in-law established an *eruv techumin* for her. On that reading, the dispute concerns whether *zikui* is required for *eruv techumin*.

*Tosafos* disagrees. *Rav*'s position is that *zikui* is unnecessary for *shituf mevo'os* — but this is against the explicit *mishna*. To demonstrate that *Rav* has *tannaitic* support, the story must be one about *shituf mevo'os* or *eruv chatzeiros*. Therefore *Tosafos* understands the story as follows: the daughter-in-law was at home for *Shabbos* but simply absent at the moment the *shituf* was being established, and the mother-in-law stepped in on her behalf. The question is whether the mother-in-law's unilateral action — without performing a formal *kinyan* transferring the food to the daughter-in-law — was valid. *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yossi* says yes, reflecting the lenient position that *zikui* is not required.

The *Gemara* records a further chain of inquiry to resolve the ambiguity. A certain rabbinic student named *Rabbi Yaakov* reports that he heard from *Rabbi Yochanan* that the food was the mother-in-law's own food, and the issue was her failure to perform *zikui* on behalf of the daughter-in-law. *Rabbi Zeira* then sends a message to *Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi*, taking the roundabout coastal route through Tyre (as *Steinsaltz*'s maps illustrate), to determine which version of the story is historically accurate. *Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi* confirms: it was the mother-in-law's food, and the dispute concerned whether she had to transfer ownership — thereby establishing that *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yossi* held that *zikui* was not required, giving *Rav* his *tannaitic* precedent.

*Tosafos* adds a parenthetical note on the apparent inconsistency in strictness between *eruv techumin* and *eruv chatzeiros*: if *Rashi*'s reading is correct and this story concerns *eruv techumin*, then the lenient opinion (*Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yossi*) may reflect the view that *techumin* is *derabbanan*, while the stricter opinion (*Rabbi Chiya*) may reflect the view that it is *de'oraita*.

### RN Conclusion

Following this extended discussion, the *Gemara* arrives at a clear and authoritative conclusion. *Amar Rav Nachman*: *naktinan* — we take as our established position — *echad eiruvei techumin, ve'echad eiruvei chatzeiros, ve'echad shitufei mevo'os, tzarich lezakot*. Whether the issue is *eruv techumin*, *eruv chatzeiros* (now explicitly named for the first time in this discussion), or *shituf mevo'os* — in all three cases, a formal act of transfer of ownership is required.

This conclusion is more demanding than either *Rav* or *Shmuel* individually required. Each of them held that at least one category did not need *zikui*; *Rav Nachman* adopts the stringent position of each where applicable and requires *zikui* across the board. *Tosafos* confirms: *v'chein halacha* — this is the ruling we follow.

## Eruv Tavshilin

### Tavshilin Query

With the conclusion that *zikui* is required for all three types of *eruvin*, the *Gemara* raises one further question. *Ba'ei Rav Nachman*: what about *eiruvei tavshilin*? When a *rav* establishes an *eruv tavshilin* with the intention that it should serve as a *halachic* umbrella for all the residents of a city — formulating the intention *l'chol b'nei ha'ir*, as the familiar language of the *beracha* indeed states — must he perform a formal act of *zikui* to the residents, or is his declaration alone sufficient?

*Eruv tavshilin* differs fundamentally from the *eruvin* previously discussed — it involves permitting cooking on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbos* rather than establishing carrying domains — and its detailed analysis properly belongs to *Beitzah*, where it is treated at length. The *Gemara* here raises it only in connection with the *zikui* question.

### RY Challenge

*Amar Rav Yosef*: I don't understand the question. Has *Rav Nachman* not heard the teaching transmitted as *amar Rav Nachman bar Adda amar Shmuel* — that *eiruvei tavshilin* does require *zikui*? *Shmuel* already addressed this, and if so, what is the basis for *Rav Nachman*'s uncertainty?

*Amar lei Abaye*: obviously he hadn't heard it — *peshita d'lo shmei leh*. If he had heard it, he would not have asked. The question itself demonstrates ignorance of the prior ruling.

*Rav Yosef* responds: not necessarily. Perhaps *Rav Nachman* did hear it, and his question is whether to follow it. After all, *Shmuel* also said that *eruv techumin* does not require *zikui* — and *Rav Nachman* just ruled against that, requiring *zikui* for *eruv techumin* as well. Perhaps he is prepared similarly to deviate from *Shmuel* here.

The *Gemara* rejects this comparison. *Bishlama hatam* — there, by *eruv techumin* and *eruv chatzeiros*, there was a full debate: *Rav* and *Shmuel* both articulated explicit positions on both issues. *Rav Nachman*, in ruling that *zikui* is required across the board, did not reject either authority; he adopted the stringent ruling of each on its respective issue. *V'ka mashma lan k'chumrei d'mar u'k'chumrei d'mar* — he teaches us to apply the more stringent position of each *amora*. But regarding *eruv tavshilin*, *Shmuel* stated that *zikui* is required, and no one disputes it. Had *Rav Nachman* known of that uncontested ruling, he would simply have followed it. There is no competing opinion to navigate and no reason to deviate. The *Gemara* concludes, therefore, that *Shmuel*'s ruling stands: *eruv tavshilin* also requires *zikui*.

This will be revisited when we reach *Beitzah*. For now, the operative conclusion is: if one wishes the *eruv tavshilin* to function on behalf of the entire community, one must perform a formal act of transfer.

### Weapons Officer

The *Gemara* then introduces two parallel narratives. There was a certain *turzina* — a weapons officer, head of the municipal armory — living in the neighborhood of *Rabbi Zeira*. The residents asked him to rent his property to them, as required to permit carrying when a non-Jew's domain is part of the shared *reshus*. He refused. They came to *Rabbi Zeira* and asked: what about going to his wife? Can we rent from her?

A second, nearly identical story is told about a *turzina* in the neighborhood of *Rabbi Yehuda bar Oshaya*. Again the non-Jew refused to rent. The question again went to *Rabbi Yehuda bar Oshaya*, who did not know the answer. It was then brought to *Rav Masna*, who also did not know. Finally it reached *Rav Yehuda*, who answered in the name of *Shmuel*: *ishto shel adam me'arreves shelo mida'ato* — a man's wife may establish the *eruv* without his knowledge.

### Wife Authority

The ruling of *Reish Lakish*, cited in the first story, and the ruling of *Shmuel* cited by *Rav Yehuda* in the second, are consistent: the wife may act *shelo mida'ato* — without the husband's awareness. Notably, the language used is *me'arreves*, not *sokheres* — this applies not merely to the *sekhirus* of the non-Jew's property (which is itself a somewhat formal legal construct) but to contributing food directly to the *eruv* or *shituf*.

This creates an apparent tension with the *mishna*'s requirement of *zikui*. *Shmuel* himself insists on *zikui* — a formal transfer of ownership through a second party — when using one's own food to establish a *shituf*. Yet here, when going in the opposite direction — taking food from another person's household without their explicit consent — *Shmuel* seems to permit it. The point of reconciliation is that the wife in this context is not transferring ownership but rather contributing the household food, which is understood ultimately to be in the husband's interest as well. There is no genuine loss to him, and therefore his explicit consent is not required.

### Women Baraita

A *baraita* appears to contradict this ruling: *nashim she'eirvu venishtatfu shelo mida'at ba'aleihen — ein eiruvah eruv ve'ein shitufah shituf*. If women contributed to an *eruv* or *shituf* without their husbands' knowledge, the *eruv* is invalid and the *shituf* is invalid. This seems to flatly contradict the ruling that the wife may act without the husband's knowledge.

### Mandatory Status

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, ha de'assar, ha de'lo assar* — the cases are not the same. The key distinction is whether the household's non-participation would render the *eruv* forbidden for the other residents. There are configurations — the *Gemara* alluded to such cases earlier in the *masechet* — where a household opens onto two streets, and its participation or non-participation may be decisive for one street but not the other. For the street where their abstention creates no halachic problem for others, the residents cannot demand their participation, and the wife therefore cannot contribute without the husband's consent, since he has no stake in the matter that compels him. But where their non-participation would prevent other residents from using the *mavoi* or *chatzer*, then the household can be pressed to participate, and the wife's contribution without his explicit consent is valid — because at that point the husband is, in effect, blocking the community, and his abstention is not merely passive.

### Force Limits

This principle — that participation may be compelled when a household's abstention creates a communal halachic problem — leads the *Gemara* to a further question. *Leima msaye'a lei: kofin oto la'asot lechi ve'kora le'mavoi* — one may even compel a person to contribute to the construction of a *lechi* or *kora* for the *mavoi*. This resembles the principle in *Bava Batra* of compelling residents to contribute to communal needs. If we can force participation in the construction of the physical structures of the *mavoi*, perhaps the same principle applies to forcing participation in the *eruv* itself.

The *Gemara* rejects the parallel. *Shani hasam* — there it is different. *Rashi* explains: the absence of walls (*deleika mechitzos*) represents a more concrete, practical concern for the residents of the *mavoi*, not merely a halachic technicality. *Tosafos*, however, reports a different *girsa* from earlier manuscripts: *de'ika mechitzos* — because the walls are already there. Once physical walls exist, it does not matter whether anyone was forced to contribute to their construction — their halachic efficacy is determined by physical reality, not by the voluntariness of the participants. An *eruv*, by contrast, is conceptually about shared participation and partnership; it may therefore require more genuine consent than a physical structure does. On either reading, compelling contribution to the *eruv* may not follow straightforwardly from compelling contribution to the *lechi* or *kora*.

*Tosafos* adds that even the ruling of *bene mavoi nichnasim le'toch beito ve'notlin shitufan mimeno ba'al karcho* — that the residents may enter a person's home and take his contribution against his will if he normally participates but failed to do so — should be understood as meaning that they obtain it through his wife. The *Gemara* may sound more radical in its literal wording, but *Tosafos* tones it down to mean that the wife provides the food rather than that the residents break in and seize it.

### Asherah Materials

After these discussions, the *Gemara* records three words — *lashon acher mitzad sheni* — that the marginal commentators flag as entirely incomprehensible. A gloss printed in the margin notes: *shalosh teivos hallalu meshulalei havana lim'od* — these three words are utterly without meaning — and observes that in certain early printed editions of the *Gemara*, including the Venice printing and the Salonika printing, these words are entirely absent.

The *Gemara* then turns to a brief related ruling: *Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi* says one may fashion a *lechi* (an upright post) from an *ashera* — a tree that has been worshipped as an idol. Although an *ashera* is *assur b'hana'ah*, forbidden from benefit, obtaining the *halachic* ability to carry in a *mavoi* is not considered direct physical benefit, and the *lechi* therefore remains technically valid.

*Reish Lakish* goes further: one may even fashion a *kora* (the horizontal beam) from an *ashera*. If so, *man de'amar kora kol shekein lechi* — whoever permits the beam certainly permits the post. But *man de'amar lechi aval kora lo* — whoever permits only the post but not the beam — does so because of the principle *kisusei michsas shiureih*: since there is a *mitzvah* to destroy an *ashera*, it is treated as if already destroyed, and its *shiur* is considered burnt up and unavailable. A *kora* must be wide enough to support a brick (*aris leveinah*), and if its effective *shiur* is deemed annulled, it cannot meet that standard. A *lechi*, by contrast, has no minimum width requirement — though *Tosafos* notes it does have a minimum height — and so the problem of *kisusei michsas shiureih* may be less acute or at least differently applied. *Tosafos* concedes that the precise distinction is not fully clear.

## Quantities Rules

### Self Replenish

The *mishna* states: *nismait ha'ochel* — if the food of the *eruv* or *shituf* became depleted below the required minimum — *mosif u'mezakeh, ein tzarich lehodiya* — one may add to it and perform *zikui* without informing the other residents. The reasoning is straightforward: the person replenishing is using their own food and performing a *zikui* to the others. Since it is one's own food being given as a gift, there is no need to obtain the recipients' prior awareness; the transfer works unilaterally in their favor.

### New Residents

If new residents joined the *mavoi* or *chatzer* after the *eruv* was established — *nitosefu aleihen, mosif u'mezakeh* — one must add food to account for the new participants. *Rashi* explains that if the replenishment comes from the initiator's own food, it again requires no prior notification, since one is simply performing *zikui* of one's own assets. But if the new residents' own food is to be drawn upon, then *tzarich lehodiya* — one must inform them, because using their food without their awareness is not permitted when, as in this case, they are not yet part of the existing *eruv* structure.

### Min Quantities

How much food is needed? *Biz'man she'hen merubin, mazon shtei se'udos lekulam* — when the number of participants is large, two meals' worth of food for all of them combined is sufficient. This is the principle underlying the practice of many community *rabbis* who set aside a single box of *matzos* before *Pesach* as the *eruv* for an entire city of thousands — two meals' worth collectively is enough. *Biz'man she'hen mu'atin, kegrogeret lechol echad va'echad* — when the number of participants is small, each person must contribute the equivalent of a pressed fig (*grogeret*).

### RY Distinction

*Amar Rabbi Yosi*: these minimum quantities apply specifically to the initial establishment of the *eruv* — *bitechilat eruv*. But once an *eruv* has been properly established and some food remains — *bishiyurei eruv*, a leftover *eruv* — even the smallest amount, *kol shehu*, is sufficient to maintain its validity. One need not reconstitute the minimum amounts.

### Reason Waived

The rationale for this leniency: *lo amru le'arev bachatzerot ela kedei shelo tishkach et hatinokot* — the *eruv chatzeiros* requirement (even alongside a *shituf mevo'os*) exists primarily so that children do not forget the practice and the concept of *eruvin*. Since a *shituf mevo'os* using bread already covers the carrying requirements in most cases — rendering the separate *eruv chatzeiros* technically superfluous — the latter's minimum quantities need not be rigorously maintained as long as some food remains. The *eruv chatzeiros* in that context serves a pedagogical rather than strictly operative function.

This is also the basis for the ruling, followed in practice, that if one uses bread for the *shituf mevo'os*, there is no need to separately establish an *eruv chatzeiros* — the *rav* takes a single quantity of bread, two meals' worth for the entire city, and the matter is concluded.

### One vs. Two Types

The *Gemara* then examines what type of scenario the *mishna* is actually addressing when it says the food was "depleted" (*nismait*). If the *eruv* originally consisted of one type of food — say, wine — and one wishes to replenish it with the same type, the *Gemara* asks: why does the *mishna* mention depletion at all? Even if the wine were completely gone, one could presumably take more wine from the same contributors, since they already agreed to participate with wine. Conversely, if one wishes to replace the wine with oil — a different type — even a small depletion might be problematic, since the contributors consented to wine specifically, not oil.

### Depletion Baraita

A *baraita* clarifies: *kala ha'ochel mimin echad, ein tzarich lehodiya* — if the food was completely consumed and one wishes to replenish with the same type, no notification is required; the prior agreement covers the replenishment. *Mishnei minin* — if one wishes to replenish with a different type, *tzarich lehodiya* — notification is required. The prior consent covered the original food type, not a substitution.

This *baraita* makes the distinction between single-type and dual-type replenishment explicit for the case of complete depletion — a case that the *mishna* itself did not directly address, having spoken only of partial depletion.

### Two Interpret

The *Gemara* offers two ways of reading the *mishna* in light of the *baraita*. *Ibba'is eima min echad*: perhaps the *mishna* is discussing the case of a single food type, and when it says *nismait* (depleted), it actually means the food was completely used up — *mai nismat nismat* — rather than merely reduced. The *mishna* then teaches that even when fully depleted, if you replenish with the same type, no notification is needed.

*Ibba'is eima shnei minin*: alternatively, the *mishna* is discussing a case where one wishes to replenish with a different type, and *nismait* means genuinely diminished rather than entirely gone. Here the question is whether, when there is still some of the original food left, one may add a different type without notification. The answer, in that case, may be that partial remaining supply constitutes a kind of continuation of the original arrangement, so no new consent is needed. But *kalu shani* — once the original food is fully exhausted, the prior arrangement may be considered concluded, and a fresh contribution of a different type would require explicit agreement.

The practical upshot: once residents have agreed to participate in a *shituf* or *eruv*, replenishment is largely self-executing. As long as one is restoring the same arrangement — same food type, same parties — one need not reopen the matter. It is only when introducing something genuinely new — a new food type, or a completely fresh start after total depletion with a different substance — that renewed notification and consent are required.
