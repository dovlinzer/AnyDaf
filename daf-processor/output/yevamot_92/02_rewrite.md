# Yevamot 92 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Remarriage & Fault

### Culpability Question

The daf under discussion is 91b, and the broader sugya concerns the *kulan* in the *mishna* — the clause stating that if a woman remarries *shelo birshut*, without the authority of *beit din*, she may return to her first husband. The Gemara has been probing whether this leniency represents only the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, who holds generally that the child of such a union is not a *mamzer* and that she may not even need a *get* from the second man, or whether it is *divrei hakol* — a unanimous ruling. The practical stakes are high: Rav apparently ruled in practice according to this line, but if it reflects only Rabbi Shimon's view, serious questions arise about how we *pasken*.

The argument for *divrei hakol* proceeds as follows: the Gemara understands *shelo birshut* to mean that the woman remarried on the basis of two witnesses — stronger evidence than the single witness ordinarily required — and therefore she is an *anusah*, entirely blameless. She did everything correctly; there was nothing more she could have done. How, then, could all the severe negative consequences of the *mishna* be imposed on her? The argument runs that everyone should agree: where there is zero fault, there are no consequences.

### Ulla's Cases

The Gemara now challenges precisely this assumption. Ulla raises a pointed objection: do we really reason that consequences apply only when a woman was culpable? The Gemara invokes a series of *mishnayot* from tractate Gittin, each of which carries the same full consequence formula — *teitzei mizeh umizeh, ve'havalad mamzer mizeh umizeh*, she must leave both the first and second husband, the children from either are *mamzerim*, she loses her *kesubah* and *mezonos*, and all the other penalties enumerated in our *mishna*. The question is whether in any of those cases the woman was in fact at fault.

### Gittin Examples

The first case from Gittin: the *get* was dated using an unrecognized or non-standard kingdom — *shem malchus she'einah hogenes* — such as the kingdom of the Medes or the Greeks, or it was dated by the years of the construction or destruction of the *Beis Hamikdash*, rather than by the conventional regnal dating. Or alternatively, the location written on the *get* was incorrect — the transaction took place in the east but the document read "the west," or vice versa. Tosafos notes that the operative question is not precisely where the *sofer* sat but where the witnesses signed. In any event, none of these are obviously glaring defects, yet the *mishna* rules *teitzei mizeh umizeh* and all consequences follow if the woman relied on this *get* and remarried.

The question is immediate: what is this poor woman supposed to know about the technical details of *hilchos gittin*? Her husband handed her a *get* and she relied on it. The Gemara's answer is: *ibayei lah likro legittah* — she should have read the *get* and noticed the non-standard dating or the incorrect location. This is already a strained answer given that the vast majority of people were illiterate, and even a literate person would not necessarily identify a non-conventional date as technically disqualifying. But the Gemara nonetheless deploys this response.

### Yibum & Arayot

The second case, introduced by Rav Shimi bar Ashi: a man performed *yibum* with his sister-in-law, and thereupon the *tzarah*, the co-wife, considered herself free and went and remarried another man — which would seem entirely legitimate. Subsequently, however, it emerged that the woman with whom *yibum* had been performed was an *ailonit*, a woman incapable of childbearing, and therefore the act of *yibum* had no halachic validity. There is no *mitzvah* of *yibum* with an *ailonit*, and thus the *tzarah* had in fact still required *chalitza* or a valid *yibum* before she could remarry. Yet she had gone ahead. The ruling: *teitzei mizeh umizeh*, all consequences apply.

This is striking on two levels. First, as a matter of substantive *halacha*, we have spent the entirety of Masechet Yevamot learning that once one co-wife undergoes *yibum*, the others are free to remarry. Now we discover that if it retroactively emerges that the *yibum* was invalid — because the woman was an *ailonit* — the *tzarah* who had already remarried suffers all these dire consequences. Second, the Gemara's response to the question of fault is: *ibayei lah lamtonei* — she should have waited to see whether the co-wife would turn out to be an *ailonit*. How long should she have waited? Ten years? Twenty? The co-wife may already have been married for a decade. The answer seems almost absurd, yet the Gemara maintains it.

The third case, brought by Abaye: all the *arayos* — relatives who exempt their co-wives from *yibum* — if those co-wives go and remarry and it subsequently emerges that the woman who was the *ervah* was herself an *ailonit*, then she was never truly a co-wife in the marriage, and her status as *ervah* cannot exempt the others. The *tzarot* who had remarried now face the same consequences. Again the Gemara asks what they should have done, and again the answer is: they should have waited to determine whether the *ervah* was an *ailonit*. The halachic shockwave here is the same: everything we have studied about the *ervah* exempting co-wives from *yibum* is now qualified by this latent uncertainty.

### Scribe Mix-up

The next case, introduced by Rava: a *sofer* prepared two documents simultaneously — a *get* for the husband to give his wife, and a receipt (*shover*) for the wife to give the husband acknowledging payment of the *kesubah*. The *sofer* then mixed them up and handed each party the wrong document. The husband received what he believed was the receipt but was actually the *get*, and the wife received what she believed was the *get* but was actually the receipt. Each gave the other their respective document, so no valid *get* was ever transferred. Some time later, the husband produces the document he received and someone identifies it as a *get*; the wife produces hers and it turns out to be a receipt. The woman had in the meantime remarried, believing she possessed a valid *get*. The ruling: *teitzei mizeh umizeh* and all consequences.

The Gemara's answer: she should have read the *get* given to her and realized it was a receipt. The irony is manifest — the vast majority of people in that era were illiterate, and the demand that she read and identify the technical character of the document is in obvious tension with historical reality. Yet the Gemara asserts it was her responsibility.

### Bald Get

The final case in this series, introduced by Rav Ashi: a *get mekushar*, a folded-and-sewn *get* in which the witnesses sign on successive folds, had a *get kireiach* — a "bald *get*" — meaning one of the folds lacked a signature. The woman relied on this document, remarried, and then the defect was discovered. *Teitzei mizeh umizeh*, all consequences follow.

The *get mekushar* functions as follows: the *get* is written, then folded, and a witness signs on the fold; the document is folded again and another witness signs on the next fold, and so on, with more witnesses than the standard two. The purpose, among other reasons, was to slow down the process of divorce for *kohanim*, requiring a more elaborate procedure so they might reconsider. A *get kireiach* has a fold missing its signature — a bald spot. The Gemara asks what this woman should have done, and answers: she should have read the *get* and noticed the missing signature. Of course, she did read the *get* and found it in order — the problem is whether she was obligated to know the technical *halacha* that every fold requires a signature. That is a very demanding standard, and yet the Gemara insists this was her responsibility.

### Natural Consequences

At this point the Gemara's argument crystallizes. We have a substantial body of evidence — multiple *mishnayot* in Gittin — demonstrating that all these consequences apply even in cases where the woman bears no genuine fault. The Gemara nominally provides answers in each case by identifying some theoretical negligence, but as has been noted, these answers are *dachuk* — strained. The natural reading of all these cases is that fault is not the operative criterion at all.

This points toward a fundamentally different understanding of why these consequences exist — an approach associated with Shammai Friedman's reading of the *mishna*. The consequences are not a *knas*, a penalty designed to incentivize more careful investigation. They are not punitive. Rather, they are the natural result of a particular halachic reality: in certain circumstances, a woman appears halachically as though she is married to two men simultaneously. Whether because witnesses testified she could remarry (as in our *mishna*), or because a *get* appeared valid but contained a technical defect (as in the Gittin cases), the legal situation creates what amounts to a dual-marriage reality. The consequences flow not from punishment but from that reality itself.

If this is correct, then fault is simply irrelevant. The consequences arise whether or not the woman is blameworthy. And this has a direct implication for the present sugya: if fault is irrelevant, then the fact that a woman acted on two witnesses — and is therefore an *anusah* with no conceivable fault — does not change the analysis. She still faces all these consequences. The argument that the leniency of the *mishna* should be *divrei hakol* because she was blameless collapses.

## Two Witnesses Loophole

### Pappa's Distinction

Rav Pappa sought to issue a practical ruling based on the principle of *mai hava lah leme'avad* — what could she have done? Specifically, he wished to hold that when a woman remarries on the basis of two witnesses and the husband subsequently reappears alive, she is free of consequences because she was entirely blameless. On his reading, the *mishna*'s statement that she may return to her first husband when acting *shelo birshut* is *divrei hakol* precisely because with two witnesses there is absolutely no fault on her part, and therefore that specific leniency applies universally.

### Huna's Objection

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua challenged Rav Pappa directly: what about all those *mishnayot* in Gittin? They demonstrate plainly that these consequences apply even when the woman is not at fault. Rav Pappa replied that we did in fact answer each of those cases — we identified ways in which, theoretically, the woman bore some responsibility. But Rav Huna pressed further: *veshinuyei leiku velismoch*? Are we really going to rely on those answers? The *pshat* of all those *mishnayot* points overwhelmingly in one direction — consequences apply regardless of fault. To rest a practical ruling on weak, forced *terutzim* that strain against the plain meaning of multiple *mishnayot* is not a sound halachic foundation.

### Two Witnesses Rejected

The Gemara's conclusion is therefore that even when a woman remarries on the basis of two witnesses — even when she is completely blameless, even when there was no need to invoke the special authority of *beit din* to credit weak testimony — if the husband subsequently appears alive, all the negative consequences apply. The statement in the *mishna* that she may return to her first husband when acting *shelo birshut* reflects only the position of Rabbi Shimon, not *divrei hakol*.

This is an unexpected and striking conclusion, particularly given that the entire sugya was framed around the weakness of *eid echad* and the role of *beit din* in authorizing remarriage on thin evidence. By the end, we learn that even with two witnesses and even in cases from Gittin that have nothing to do with testimony about a deceased husband, the same consequences apply. The logical corollary — which the earlier framing of the sugya had implied but which is now effectively withdrawn — was that these consequences existed to bolster the reliability of *eid echad* and incentivize careful investigation. That rationale has been dismantled. As has been noted, it is somewhat surprising that the *Rishonim* and *Acharonim* did not more explicitly revisit the opening framing of the *masechet* in light of where the sugya actually lands.

### Rumors Excluded

Rav Ashi adds an important qualification: *ulekala lo chaishinan* — we are not concerned about mere rumors. The Gemara clarifies which rumor is at issue. If the rumor surfaces after the second marriage has already taken place, this is not a new principle — Rav Ashi himself already stated elsewhere that any *kala* that arises after *nisuin* is not acted upon. If you wish to disturb an existing marriage, you need concrete evidence; a rumor is insufficient.

One might have thought that this case is different. Since the woman had been married to the first husband — that was the established prior status — and *beit din* had to exercise authority to permit her to remarry, perhaps this existing foundation means that a subsequent rumor of the husband's continued survival should be treated as a pre-marital rumor and create a problem for the second marriage. The Gemara rejects this: *kala* alone does not disturb the second marriage. Only concrete evidence — the husband's appearance or witness testimony that he was alive — triggers the consequences. Rumors do not.

The practical halachic conclusion that emerges from the entire sugya, as codified by the *Rif* and as reported by the *Rosh* in the name of all the *Geonim*, is that even when a woman remarries on the basis of two witnesses and is completely without fault, if the husband turns out to be alive, all the negative consequences of the *mishna* apply. The one exception — the sole narrowing of the *mishna*'s severe reach — is that a bare rumor of the husband's survival does not trigger these consequences.

## Beit Din & Korban

### Korban Rule

The *mishna* continues with a further distinction: if a woman remarries *al pi beit din* — on the authority of *beit din* — and the husband subsequently proves to have been alive, she does not bring a *korban*. If she remarried *shelo al pi beit din* — without the authority of *beit din*, which in the Gemara's reading means on the basis of two witnesses rather than on *beit din*'s specific authorization — she does bring a *korban*. Here, paradoxically, the woman with better evidence (two witnesses) is subject to a stricter consequence in terms of the *korban*, because she was not acting under the special institutional authority of *beit din*.

### Zeiri's View

Zeiri, however, challenges the *mishna*'s entire premise. *Leisa lemasnisin* — we do not rule like our *mishna*. He invokes a teaching from the *beis medrash*: if *beit din* ruled that the sun had set — issuing their decision on a cloudy day, permitting people to perform *melacha* on what they believed to be *Motzaei Shabbos* — and it turned out the sun had not in fact set, that is not a *psak* in any meaningful sense. It is merely an error in fact. A *psak* is a mistaken legal judgment — an incorrect application of law to given facts. A mistake in fact, by contrast, is not a ruling at all; the facts simply were not what they appeared to be. The same applies here: *beit din*'s permission for the woman to remarry was premised on the belief that her husband was dead. If he was alive, there was no *psak* — there was only a factual error. Since it was never a genuine *psak* of *beit din*, the woman cannot claim exemption from a *korban* on the grounds that she was following their ruling.

### Rav Nachman

Rav Nachman disagrees: it is a *hora'ah*, a genuine ruling. His proof is elegant. In virtually every area of *halacha*, *eid echad* is not credited as testimony. Yet here, *beit din* believes the single witness who says the husband is dead and permits the woman to remarry. What is the source of this authority? It must be that *beit din* is exercising its institutional power — its *hora'ah* — to credit that testimony. Crediting *eid echad* is not simply a passive assessment of the facts; it is an active halachic act requiring rabbinic authority. Therefore, whenever *beit din* permits remarriage on the basis of *eid echad*, this constitutes a genuine *psak*, and the woman acting under it is exempt from a *korban* if the husband later appears.

Rashi understands this to mean that *beit din* is not merely assessing what happened — they are exercising judgment and authority in determining that *eid echad* is sufficient in this context. It is their authority, not just their assessment, that allows the woman to act.

### Rava's Fact Argument

Rava takes the opposing view: *teda detaus hi* — it is an error, not a *hora'ah*. His proof comes from the dynamics of sequential testimony. Suppose one witness says the husband died; *beit din* permits the woman to remarry. Then two witnesses say the husband was alive; *beit din* forbids her. Then another witness comes and says the husband subsequently died — and *beit din* permits her again. Why does this second single witness prevail over the earlier pair who testified he was alive? Because the second witness is not contradicting the first pair: he is saying that after they saw him alive, he then died. The most recent factual report governs.

But consider the implication. If this were a question of legal judgment rather than fact, we would not simply defer to the most recent argument. When *beit din* reasons through a question of law, first permitting something, then finding a stronger reason to forbid it, then finding a weaker reason to permit it again — the stronger argument for the prohibition prevails; you do not simply adopt the last position because it came later. The fact that we do follow the most recent testimony here demonstrates that this is not a matter of legal judgment at all. It is a question of what actually happened — a purely factual question. And therefore, when *beit din* was wrong about the husband being dead, it was a factual error, not a mistaken *psak*.

### R. Eliezer Strict

Rabbi Eliezer similarly holds that it is an error and not a ruling, and accordingly that the woman must bring a *korban*. This emerges from the famous dictum attributed to him: *yikov hadin et hahar* — let the law pierce through the mountain. In this context, the saying means that the facts must govern, regardless of how *beit din* characterized the situation: the husband was alive, the woman committed what amounts to a transgression, and she must bring a *chatat shmeinah* — a substantial sin-offering. The use of this phrase signals that Rabbi Eliezer sees the matter as a straightforward question of legal fact, not a case involving institutional deference to a ruling.

One might suggest that Rabbi Eliezer holds a general principle that even acting under a *beit din psak* does not exempt one from a *korban*. The Gemara rejects this: if that were his general position, why phrase it as *yikov hadin et hahar*, as though this case demands special emphasis? The phrasing implies that something unique to this case requires emphasis — namely, that despite the involvement of *beit din*, she still brings a *korban* because it was never truly a *psak* to begin with.

### Psak vs Fact

The Gemara draws out the logical structure: if it is an error (*taus*), then obviously she brings a *korban* — she was not operating under any genuine *psak*. But if it is a *hora'ah*, why does she bring a *korban*? Under normal principles, someone who acts in accordance with a *beit din* ruling is exempt. The Gemara entertains the possibility that Rabbi Eliezer simply holds that acting on *beit din*'s authority never exempts an individual from a *korban*, but dismisses this in light of the *yikov hadin* phrasing, which signals a specific ruling in this case, not a general principle.

There is a broader irony here. The entire early portion of the sugya emphasized the extraordinary authority of *beit din* — *yesh koach b'yad chachamim*, *mishum agunah akilu bah rabbanan*, the whole framework of *beit din* wielding special institutional power to rely on *eid echad* and permit remarriage. Now, at the sugya's conclusion, multiple opinions converge in minimizing that framing: the permission was not a genuine *psak*, it was a factual assessment, and factual assessments can be wrong without attracting the legal protection afforded to authoritative rulings. This dovetails with the earlier conclusion that even two witnesses trigger all the same consequences — both developments, taken together, pull back from the strong institutional-authority reading that opened the *masechet*.

The question of whether acting under two witnesses triggers a *korban* is somewhat implicit. Since the *mishna* already states *lo niset al pi beit din chayevet b'korban* — and Rashi glosses this as meaning she acted on the basis of witnesses — it is clear that acting on two witnesses does require a *korban*. This invites reflection: if she is entirely blameless — an *anusah* who relied on two witnesses and could not have done more — why does she bring a *korban* at all?

The answer illuminates the nature of the *korban* as an institution. A *korban* is not simply a punishment for culpable wrongdoing. Even a *tinok she'nishba* — a Jewish child raised entirely among non-Jews, with no knowledge of Torah, who desecrated *Shabbos* throughout his life without any fault whatsoever — must bring a *korban* for *chillul Shabbos* upon learning of his Jewish identity. The *korban* addresses a metaphysical rupture in the fabric of *halacha*, a transgression that occurred regardless of moral fault, and that rupture requires atonement. What exempts a person from a *korban* is not merely being blameless; it is when the act is legally not attributable to that person at all. When *beit din* issues an authoritative *psak* and a person acts on it, the act is in a meaningful sense *beit din*'s act, not hers — the responsibility is shifted institutionally. That is why *psak beit din* exempts from a *korban*. But if what occurred was only a factual error, no such institutional transfer of responsibility took place, and the *korban* remains her obligation.

## Violating Permission

### Incorrect Action

The *mishna* introduces yet another case: *hora'ah* — *beit din* gave her permission to remarry, but then she acted *shekilkela*, incorrectly. Even though she was operating under *beit din*'s authority, if she exceeded the scope of what she was permitted to do, she must bring a *korban*.

### R. Eliezer: Znut

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *kilkela* means she had sexual relations outside of marriage — *zenus*, relations with a man without *nisuin*, acting merely on the basis of *beit din*'s determination of her *pnuyah* status.

### R. Yochanan: Issur Lav

Rabbi Yochanan holds that *kilkela* refers to a specific *issur lav* — she entered a marriage that carries a negative prohibition, such as an *almanah* marrying a *kohen gadol*, or a *gerushah* or *chalutzah* marrying a regular *kohen*.

### Znut Analysis

The Gemara analyzes the dispute. According to Rabbi Yochanan, why does simple *zenus* not constitute acting outside *beit din*'s authority? Because she can argue: *atun hu deshaviten pnuyah* — you yourselves declared me a *pnuyah*, an unmarried woman. By ruling that her husband is dead and she may remarry, *beit din* effectively established her status as an unattached woman. If she is a *pnuyah*, then having sexual relations outside of formal marriage, while not ideal, is consistent with the status they conferred upon her. She is not operating outside the scope of their permission — she is simply acting as a *pnuyah* acts. By contrast, if she enters a marriage that involves an *issur lav* — such as marrying a *kohen* despite being a *gerushah* — that specific marriage arrangement was never within the scope of *beit din*'s permission. They permitted her to remarry in a halachically valid way; they did not authorize a prohibited union. There, she is acting outside their *psak* and must bring a *korban*.

### Multiple Korbanos

A further question: how many *korbanos* must she bring? Rabbi Eliezer holds *al kol biah u'viah* — a separate *korban* for each act of relations. The logic is that each act of sexual relations with the second man, while the first husband was alive, constitutes an independent act of adultery, each requiring its own atonement. Rabbi Yochanan holds that *chachamim* say *korban echad al kulam* — one *korban* covers all the acts, on the principle that multiple unknowing transgressions of the same kind constitute a single continuous *shogeg* requiring one *korban*.

The *chachamim* concede to Rabbi Eliezer, however, that if she married five different men sequentially and committed adultery with each, she brings a separate *korban* for each man. Relations with a different man constitutes a categorically different transgressive act, and each one requires its own *korban*.

## Death Sequences

### Husband Then Son

The *mishna* now turns to a series of cases involving reversed testimony about the sequence of deaths. A woman's husband and son both traveled to a distant country. Witnesses came and reported: your husband died, and then afterward your son died. Since the husband predeceased the son, the woman at the moment of the husband's death had a living son, and therefore fell into neither *yibum* nor *chalitza* — she was free to remarry without any levirate obligation, as the inheritance line was secured through her son. The son's subsequent death did not retroactively create a *yibum* obligation, since at the time she became a widow there was no occasion for *yibum*. She therefore remarried.

Afterward came testimony reversing the sequence: actually, the son died first and then the husband. This means that at the moment of the husband's death she had no living son, and the *yibum* obligation had attached. She had remarried another man while she was still bound by the levirate obligation.

### Mamzer Status

The ruling: *teitzei* — she must leave the second husband — and *havalad rishon ve'acharon mamzer*. The children, the first and the last, are *mamzerim*. The phrasing "first and last" requires explanation (addressed below), but the substance is that all her children from the second union carry *mamzer* status.

This prompts a question: she married the second man while she still required *chalitza* — that is an *issur lav*, not an *issur kares*. Why would the children be *mamzerim*? The classification of *mamzer* status for a violation of an *issur lav* is the position of Rabbi Akiva, as the Gemara will establish. The stricter *halacha* here reflects his view.

### Son Then Husband

The second case: witnesses report first that the son died, then that the husband died. The woman, obligated in *yibum*, performs the levirate marriage with the brother-in-law. Subsequently, testimony reverses the sequence: the husband actually died before the son, meaning the woman was never obligated in *yibum*. She has now engaged in relations with her brother-in-law outside of any valid *yibum* context — an *issur arayos* carrying the severity of *issur kares*. The ruling: *teitzei*, and *havalad rishon ve'acharon mamzer*, all children are *mamzerim*. The severity here is clear — the violation involves *kares*-level transgression.

### Later Death

The third case is structurally similar to the core case of our first *mishna*: witnesses say the husband died, the woman remarries, and then subsequent testimony establishes that actually the husband was alive at the time of the second marriage — he has only now died. This case introduces the critical distinction between the "first" and "last" children that motivated the *mishna*'s unusual phrasing. *Teitzei*, and: *havalad rishon mamzer ve'acharon eino mamzer* — the first children are *mamzerim*, the last are not. Children born while the first husband was still alive — children of the second union during that period — are *mamzerim*. But children born after the testimony that the first husband is now truly dead are not *mamzerim*, because at that point the second union is no longer adulterous — the first husband has actually died.

Tosafos notes that in the original *mishna*, where the Talmud states that children from both the *rishon* and *acharon* are *mamzerim*, this could mean — even according to Tosafos's reading — that even if the first husband gives a *get* and the woman remains with the second husband, we impose a Rabbinic *knas* declaring those children *mamzerim*. But in this third case, Tosafos implies no such Rabbinic *knas* applies, and the ruling is purely at the *de'oraisa* level: once the husband is actually dead, children of the second union are straightforwardly not *mamzerim*.

### Kiddushin Only

The fourth case narrows the scenario further: witnesses reported the husband's death, and the woman accepted *kiddushin* from a second man — but had not yet proceeded to *nisuin* and marital cohabitation. Then the first husband returned alive.

### No Get Needed

In this case, the analysis changes substantially. The woman may return to her first husband, and notably, even if the second man gave her a *get* as a precaution, that *get* does not affect her status — she is not rendered a *gerushah*, and were her first husband now to die, she could marry a *kohen*. The principle behind this emerges from the *drashah* of Rabbi Eliezer ben Masya: *ishah gerushah me'ishah* — a woman is a *gerushah* only from her husband, not from a man who is not her husband. The *get* of a man who was never validly her husband is halachically inoperative.

Why is this case so different? The key is that *nisuin* — actual marital cohabitation — had not occurred. All the severe consequences in the earlier cases derive from the reality that the woman appears as if she is actually living with two men. Where she accepted *kiddushin* only and never began living with the second man, that factual reality of apparent dual-marriage has not come into being. The transgression has not concretized in the same way, and therefore the natural consequences of being in that situation do not attach.

### First & Last Kids

The *mishna*'s use of the terms *rishon* and *acharon* — first and last — requires explanation. On its face, one might simply expect the *mishna* to say "the children are *mamzerim*" in each case. Why specify first and last? The answer is that the *mishna* adopts this formulation for the sake of the third case, where the distinction is halachically operative: children born before the second pair of witnesses testified that the first husband is now dead are *mamzerim*, while children born afterward are not. Because that distinction needs to be expressed, the *mishna* uses the "first and last" formulation throughout — even in the cases where all children have the same status — in order to frame the language uniformly across all cases and allow the third case to express itself naturally within the same grammatical structure.

## Mamzer by Lav

### R. Akiva Position

A *Baraita* clarifies: the ruling that children of a woman who remarried while still obligated in *yibum* — an *issur lav* — are *mamzerim* reflects the position of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva holds that *kiddushin* do not take effect in a relationship that carries an *issur lav*: *ein kiddushin tofsin be'chayavei lavin*. When *kiddushin* have no validity, the union has no marital standing, and children born from it are *mamzerim*.

### Chachamim Disagree

The *chachamim* disagree: *ein mamzer miyevamah* — there is no *mamzer* from a woman who required *yibum* or *chalitza* and married another man instead. For the *chachamim*, the threshold for *mamzer* status requires an *issur kares*, not merely an *issur lav*. A relationship that would have required no more than *chalitza* to resolve does not produce *mamzerim*.

### Relationship Lavin

The Gemara sharpens Rabbi Akiva's position further. One might ask: why does the *Baraita* specifically say there is no *mamzer miyevamah* rather than making the broader statement that there is no *mamzer* from *chayavei lavin* in general? This suggests that even within Rabbi Akiva's framework, there is a distinction. According to this *tanna*, Rabbi Akiva's rule that *mamzer* results from *issur lav* applies only to *lavin deshe'er* — prohibitions grounded in a family or quasi-family relationship (*she'er*). The examples Rashi adduces are: a woman with whom one's father had relations (but never married), whom some hold is forbidden as a *lav*; and a woman to whom one personally performed *chalitza*, who is thereafter forbidden to that man by a *lav* — since she was his brother's wife and thereby entered a relational orbit. These cases involve *she'er*, a dimension of kinship or quasi-kinship.

By contrast, a simple *lav* with no such relational dimension — such as a *kohen* marrying a *gerushah*, or the case of a woman requiring *chalitza* who instead marries a stranger — is *lav greida*, a bare prohibition unconnected to family relation. Rabbi Akiva, according to this *tanna*, would not produce *mamzer* status even from such a *lav*. The ruling in our *mishna* that children are *mamzerim* in the *yibum* case thus represents the most stringent application of Rabbi Akiva's view — the view that even a bare *issur lav* can generate *mamzer* status — which is only one position within the range of opinions attributed to Rabbi Akiva's school.
