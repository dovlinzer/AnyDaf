# Shabbat 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wax Candles & Wicks

### Wax as Wick

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל אֵלּוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בְּשַׁבָּת, אֲבָל עוֹשִׂין מֵהֶן מְדוּרָה — בֵּין לְהִתְחַמֵּם כְּנֶגְדָּהּ בֵּין לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ, בֵּין עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, בֵּין עַל גַּבֵּי כִּירָה. וְלֹא אָסְרוּ אֶלָּא לַעֲשׂוֹת מֵהֶן פְּתִילָה לַנֵּר בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: With regard to **all of those** materials about **which they said** that **one may not light** the lamp **with them on Shabbat; however, one may** use **them** *ab initio* **to make a bonfire.** One may do so **both to warm himself opposite it and to utilize its light,** and he may ignite it **both on the ground and on a stove. They prohibited** using **them only to make a wick for an oil lamp.**

We begin at the very top of 21a, with the *tanu rabbanan*. Before proceeding, it is worth resolving a question that arose the previous day regarding *sha'ava* — wax. The *Mishnah* listed wax among the substances one may not use for *Shabbos* candle lighting, and *Tosafot* indeed records an opinion that wax candles are prohibited. Ultimately, however, *Tosafot* permits wax candles, and this ruling is codified in the *Shulchan Aruch*.

The reasoning turns on a precise reading of the *Gemara*'s prohibition. The *Gemara* forbids using wax as a fuel — that is, as an oil — in a configuration where a thread wick is meant to draw up liquefied wax from below. In that scenario, wax does not draw up a wick effectively; it must first melt from the heat of the flame, and the mechanics differ fundamentally from oil. A standard wax candle, by contrast, works differently: the wick is surrounded and embedded in wax, such that the wax itself constitutes a very thick wick. *Halakhically*, the entire object is treated as one thick wick, not as a wick drawing fuel from a separated reservoir. It is on this basis that wax candles — and, by the same reasoning, paraffin candles — are permitted.

### Paraffin & Cotton

The same analysis applies to paraffin, which constitutes the majority of candles in common use. The prohibition the *Gemara* targets is the configuration in which wax or another substance sits separated from the wick and is expected to be drawn up into it. Where the material surrounds the wick, the concern does not apply.

## Flicker & Draw Issues

### Prohibited Uses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שֶׁמֶן קִיק? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁאֵילְתִּינְהוּ לְכׇל נָחוֹתֵי יַמָּא, וַאֲמַרוּ לִי: עוֹף אֶחָד יֵשׁ בִּכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם וְקִיק שְׁמוֹ. רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: מִשְׁחָא דְּקָאזָא. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: קִיקָיוֹן דְּיוֹנָה. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: לְדִידִי חֲזִי לִי קִיקָיוֹן דְּיוֹנָה, וְלִצְלוּלִיבָא דָּמֵי וּמִדֻּפְשְׁקֵי רָבֵי, וְעַל פּוּם חַנְוָתָא מַדְלָן יָתֵיהּ, וּמִפַּרְצִידוֹהִי עָבְדִי מִשְׁחָא, וּבְעַנְפוֹהִי נָיְיחָן כׇּל בְּרִיחֵי דְמַעְרְבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is *kik* oil? Shmuel said: I asked all the seafarers, and they said to me** that **there is a bird in the cities on the sea** coast, **and *kik* is its name.** *Kik* oil is produced from that bird. **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, said:** This is referring to **cotton oil. Reish Lakish said:** It is the oil made from the seed of a plant like the **castor plant [*kikayon*] of Jonah. Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: I have seen** the species of the **castor plant of Jonah, and it is similar to the ricinus tree and it grows in swamps, and they place it at the entrance of shops** for shade, **and they produce oil from its seeds, and all the sick people of the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **rest** beneath **its branches.**

The *tanu rabbanan* continues: *elu devarim she'ein madlikin bahen b'Shabbos* — those things which may not be used to light on *Shabbos* — *aval osin meihen medurah* — may nevertheless be used to make a bonfire. Whether one wants the heat or the light, whether the fire is on the ground, atop an oven, or on some kind of base, all of these configurations are entirely permissible. The restriction — *v'lo asru ela la'asot meihen petilah l'ner bilvad* — applies only to using these materials as wicks in a lamp. The specific concern is the mechanics of oil being drawn up through a wick. Anyone who has tried to use oil *menorot* knows how finicky the process can be, and it is precisely there that the quality of the oil and wick matters.

### Kik & Kikayon

The *Gemara* turns to the question of *shemen kik* — the oil of *kik* — which is among the prohibited oils. *Mai shemen kik?* What is *kik*? Shmuel reports that he asked all the seafarers and they told him: there is a certain bird found in the islands of the sea, called *kik*, and the oil is derived from it. Rav Yitzchak the son of Rav Yehuda offers a different identification: it is *mishcha d'kaza* — oil of *kaza*. Rashi explains that *kaza* refers to cotton, identifying it as oil pressed from the seeds of the cotton plant. The assumption throughout is that these oils do not burn evenly and cause the flame to flicker, which would lead one to adjust the candle on *Shabbos*.

Resh Lakish offers yet another identification: *kikayon d'Yona* — the *kikayon* of Yona. Rashi is careful to note, on the first narrow line of his commentary, that this is not the exact plant under which the prophet Yona sat, but rather a plant of that species. The Steinsaltz edition identifies this as the castor plant. Rabbah bar bar Chana reports having seen such a plant himself — again, Rashi clarifies, of the same species. He describes it: it grows from swamps, it is draped decoratively over the doorways of shops to attract customers, oil is made from its seeds, and the sick people of the west, of *Eretz Yisrael*, rest beneath its branches. The medicinal reference is consistent with what we know of castor oil, and it is notable that the *Gemara* here describes the oil as being made from the seeds without suggesting it is suitable for use as lamp oil, certainly not on *Shabbos*.

### Flicker Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: פְּתִילוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בְּשַׁבָּת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהָאוּר מְסַכְסֶכֶת בָּהֶן. שְׁמָנִים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין נִמְשָׁכִין אַחַר הַפְּתִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** Those **wicks** about **which the Sages said one may not light with them on Shabbat,** the reason is: **Because the fire flickers on them.** It sputters on the wick and does not burn well. Those **oils with which the Sages said** that **one may not light** on Shabbat, the reason is: **Because they are not drawn** effectively **by the wick.**

With the identifications complete, Rabbah now provides the underlying rationale for the two categories of prohibited materials. Regarding wicks: *mipnei sheha'or mesachsachet bahen* — the flame will flicker with those wicks. Regarding oils: *mipnei she'einan nimshachin achar hapetila* — they do not draw well after the wick. When oil does not draw up into the wick effectively, the lamp will burn poorly, and one is likely to come and adjust it.

## Mixing Oils Debate

### Abaye's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵרַבָּה: שְׁמָנִים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בְּשַׁבָּת, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּתֵּן לְתוֹכָן שֶׁמֶן כׇּל שֶׁהוּא וְיַדְלִיק? מִי גָּזְרִינַן דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַדְלוֹקֵי בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ, אוֹ לָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין מַדְלִיקִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מַדְלִיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised a dilemma before Rabba:** Those **oils with which the Sages said one may not light on Shabbat, what** is the ruling? May one, *ab initio*, **add to them any amount of oil** with which it is permissible to light **and light** with that mixture? The sides of the dilemma are: **Do we issue a decree lest one come to light** these oils **in their natural form,** without mixing them with permissible oils? **Or** no, that possibility is **not** a source of concern? Rabba **said to him: One may not light** that mixture. **What is the reason** for this? The reason is **because** the *halakha* is that **one may not light** (*Arukh*).

The *Gemara* records a question posed by Abaye to his teacher Rabbah. Given that certain oils are prohibited because they do not draw after the wick, what if one adds a small amount of kosher oil — say, olive oil — to the prohibited oil? The reasoning behind the question is that the addition of good oil might cause the mixture to draw after the wick effectively, rendering it acceptable. Do we issue a *gezeira* — a protective decree — forbidding even this mixture, *deilma ati leadlukei binhu*, lest it lead to using the problematic oils by themselves? Or not?

Rabbah responds: *ein madlikin* — you may not use the mixture. His reason: *lefi she'ein madlikin* — because you may not use them. This is, on its face, a tautology, and the *Gemara* itself acknowledges the humor. Rashi understands it as an expression of the principle of *lo plug* — no distinction is drawn. Since the prohibited oils may not be used by themselves, we do not allow the mixture either, lest one lead to the other. An alternative textual reading found in some manuscripts — *lefi she'einan nidlakin* — would instead be a factual claim: even the mixture does not burn as well as one might suppose. The *Gilyon HaShas* of Akiva Eiger notes parallels in the *Gemara* and even in the language of the Torah itself where a statement is essentially self-referential, citing the verse in *Vayikra* regarding blemished priests: *asher yihiyeh bo mum lo yikrav... ki kol ish asher bo mum lo yikrav* — he may not approach because he may not approach.

It is worth noting the broader context: this entire discussion is rabbinic in nature, and we have not yet been introduced in the *Gemara* to any *mitzvah* of lighting *Shabbos* candles per se. The concerns so far have been analogous to the earlier discussions about reading by candlelight and keeping food on the fire — all centering on the worry that one will come to fiddle with the flame.

### Wick Wrapping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב בְּרוֹנָא אָמַר רַב: חֵלֶב מְהוּתָּךְ וְקִרְבֵי דָגִים שֶׁנִּמּוֹחוּ, אָדָם נוֹתֵן לְתוֹכָן שֶׁמֶן כׇּל שֶׁהוּא וּמַדְלִיק? הָנֵי מִימַּשְׁכִי בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ, וְהָנֵי לָא מִימַּשְׁכִי בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ. וּגְזַרוּ רַבָּנַן עַל חֵלֶב מְהוּתָּךְ מִשּׁוּם חֵלֶב שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְהוּתָּךְ, וְעַל קִרְבֵי דָגִים שֶׁנִּמּוֹחוּ מִשּׁוּם קִרְבֵי דָגִים שֶׁלֹּא נִמּוֹחוּ. וְלִיגְזוֹר נָמֵי חֵלֶב מְהוּתָּךְ וְקִרְבֵי דָגִים שֶׁנִּמּוֹחוּ שֶׁנָּתַן לְתוֹכָן שֶׁמֶן מִשּׁוּם חֵלֶב מְהוּתָּךְ וְקִרְבֵי דָגִים שֶׁנִּמּוֹחוּ שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן לְתוֹכָן שֶׁמֶן! הִיא גּוּפָהּ, גְּזֵירָה, וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּם וְנִיגְזוֹר גְּזֵירָה לִגְזֵירָה?!
>
> **Translation:** In any case, to this point the conclusion is that one may not light with a mixture of permitted and prohibited oils. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? Didn't Rav Beruna say** that **Rav said:** With regard to **molten fat** or **fish innards that dissolved** and became like oil, **a person may place any amount of oil** fit for lighting **into it and light.** Apparently, one may light with a mixture of permitted and prohibited oils. Rabba answers: **These,** the fat and the fish innards, **are drawn by the wick** even **in their natural state, and those,** the prohibited oils, **are not drawn in their natural state.** Originally, **the Sages issued a decree** to prohibit **molten fat due to unmolten fat and** to prohibit **dissolved fish innards due to undissolved fish innards;** however, the Sages did not issue a decree in a case where one added to them any amount of oil suitable for lighting, and permitted lighting with it. The Gemara asks: **Let them also issue a decree** to prohibit **molten fat and** dissolved **fish innards to which he added oil due to molten fat and dissolved fish innards to which he did not add** permitted **oil.** The Gemara rejects this: **That** prohibition with regard to molten fat and dissolved fish innards **itself is** based on **a decree. And** will **we arise and issue** one **decree** to prevent violation **of** another **decree?** The Sages do not issue decrees under those circumstances. Therefore, there is no reason to prohibit their use.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge. A *braisa* teaches: if one wrapped a kosher wick around a non-kosher material, *ein madlikin bo* — one may not use it. Yet Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel states that in his father's house — the house of Rabban Gamliel, the *nasi* — they would wrap a wick around a nut and light it. The purpose of wrapping the wick around the nut is evidently to create a floating wick, of the kind familiar from *Chanukah* practice, where the nut serves as a flotation device keeping the wick suspended in oil.

### Ma'aseh Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבֵיהּ: כָּרַךְ דָּבָר שֶׁמַּדְלִיקִין בּוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מַדְלִיקִין בּוֹ — אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בּוֹ. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: שֶׁל בֵּית אַבָּא הָיוּ כּוֹרְכִין פְּתִילָה עַל גַּבֵּי אֱגוֹז וּמַדְלִיקִין. קָתָנֵי מִיהַת מַדְלִיקִין!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye raised an **objection** to Rabba's opinion from that which was taught in the *Tosefta*: **One who wrapped a material with which one may light around a material with which one may not light, may not light with** the bound wick. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In the** ancestral **house of** my **father, they would wrap a wick** with which one is permitted to light **around a nut, and** that was how they would **light. In any case, it is teaching** that, according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **one may light.** Apparently, one is permitted to light with a combination of permitted and prohibited wicks.

Rabbah responds that the *tanna kamma*'s ruling presents no difficulty — *maaseh rav*. When a practice is not merely a theoretical ruling but was actually enacted by a great rabbi, that carries greater *halachic* weight. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel testified that this is what they actually did in Rabban Gamliel's household, and that practical precedent overrides the theoretical position of the *tanna kamma*. Accordingly, one should be able to wrap a kosher wick around a non-kosher base, and similarly one ought to be able to mix kosher oil with prohibited oil and use it.

One might object that the *tanna kamma* represents a broader consensus and that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's practice is merely a *da'at yachid*. This is a genuine and more general question about how heavily *maaseh rav* in the *Mishnah* is to be weighed. The comparison to Rabban Gamliel's practice in the previous chapter — where he sent a white garment to a *kohen*, a ruling we do not follow, understood there as a personal *chumra* — is instructive: if the *maaseh rav* represents a *kula*, a leniency, it is arguably more indicative of the normative *halacha* than a personal stringency.

### Floating Wicks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּמוֹתְבַתְּ לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, סַיְּיעֵיהּ מִדְּתַנָּא קַמָּא! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: ״מַעֲשֶׂה רַב״. מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא: מַאי לָאו, לְהַדְלִיק? לָא, לְהַקְפּוֹת. אִי לְהַקְפּוֹת, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא? כּוּלָּהּ רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הִיא וְחַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: כָּרַךְ דָּבָר שֶׁמַּדְלִיקִין בּוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מַדְלִיקִין בּוֹ — אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בּוֹ. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — לְהַדְלִיק, אֲבָל לְהַקְפּוֹת — מוּתָּר, שֶׁרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: שֶׁל בֵּית אַבָּא הָיוּ כּוֹרְכִין פְּתִילָה עַל גַּבֵּי אֱגוֹז.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him: Before you raise an objection to my** opinion **from** the statement of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, support it from** the statement of the **first *tanna*,** who said that it is prohibited to light in that case. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as it is preferable to challenge from the statement of Rabban Gamliel with regard to the custom in his father's house. There is a principle that proof cited from **an action is great,** i.e., a practical precedent is more substantial than a theoretical *halakha*. **Nevertheless, the difficulty** from the statement of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel remains: **Is he not** speaking of a case where he combined the wick and the nut **to light** them together? If so, one is permitted to combine the prohibited and the permitted. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is speaking in a case where he combined them **to float** the wick on the oil with the help of the nut. The Gemara asks: **If** it is speaking only with regard to a case of **floating** the wick, **what is the reason** that the **first *tanna*** prohibits doing so? The Gemara answers: The **entire** *baraita* is the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and** it is **incomplete, and it teaches the following: One who wrapped a material with which one may light around a material with which one may not light, may not light with it. In what** case is **this statement said?** When he combines the materials **to light** them together. **However,** if he utilizes that with which one may not light merely in order **to float** the wick, **it is permitted,** as we learned **that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: In the** ancestral **house of** my **father, they would wrap a wick** with which one is permitted to light **around a nut.** That was how they would light.

The *Gemara* presses on a residual difficulty: how does one account for Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's practice if the *tanna kamma* prohibits wrapping a kosher wick around a non-kosher material? The resolution is that there is in fact no debate here at all. The *tanna kamma* speaks to the case where both materials are being used together as a wick — *l'hadlik*, for actual lighting. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel speaks to the case of *l'hakpos* — using the non-kosher material as a flotation device only, not as a wick. In that configuration, the nut is not functioning as a wick at all and the restriction does not apply. *Bamei devarim amurim* — the prohibition applies only when both are used for the purpose of burning; *aval l'hakpos*, when it is purely for flotation, it is *muttar*.

### Fat & Fish Issue

The *Gemara* introduces a further teaching. Rav Bruna said in the name of Rav: *chelev mehutach* — liquefied fat — and *kirvei dagim shenimochu* — fish innards that have been liquefied — if one adds a small amount of acceptable oil to them, one may use the mixture for lighting. This appears to contradict Rabbah's ruling that mixing kosher oil with prohibited oil is forbidden.

The *Gemara* resolves this by drawing a crucial distinction. Liquefied fat and liquefied fish innards are not inherently problematic — they do in fact draw after the wick. They were only prohibited by rabbinic decree: liquefied fat was forbidden *mishum chelev she'eino mehutach*, lest it be confused with non-liquefied fat, which does not draw properly; liquefied fish innards were similarly forbidden lest they be confused with non-liquefied fish innards. In both cases the prohibition is itself a *gezeira*, a protective fence around materials that are in principle acceptable. Where the underlying material is fundamentally fit and the only reason for prohibition is a *gezeira*, we permit the addition of kosher oil and allow the mixture. Where the oil is intrinsically poor — genuinely incapable of drawing after the wick — adding a small amount of kosher oil cannot remedy that, and the prohibition stands.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: why not extend the *gezeira* further and prohibit even the mixture of liquefied fat with kosher oil, lest it lead to using liquefied fat with no oil added, which itself was prohibited? The answer: *hai gufa gezira*, the liquefied fat prohibition is itself a *gezeira*. *V'anan neiku v'nigzor gezira l'gezira* — we do not pile a *gezeira* upon a *gezeira*. The *Gemara* is aware that this principle is not absolute and that sometimes the *Gemara* does build such layered decrees, but here it declines to do so.

A note in *Tosafot* raises a broader question: is not the entire framework of prohibited wicks and oils already a *gezeira l'gezira*? The underlying concern is that one might adjust the lamp on *Shabbos*, and the prohibition of these materials is a protective fence around that concern. So forbidding even the mixture of good oil with bad is already one level removed from the original concern. One of the *Baalei HaTosafot* — identified as the Rivan — offers a notably different framing: the reason to avoid oils that do not draw after the wick is not, as generally understood, the fear of adjusting the candle (*shema yateh*), but rather the concern that the lamp will go out entirely (*shema yichbeh*), leaving the household without light and thereby undermining *shalom bayis*. This is a significant reframing, because it implicitly treats the lighting of *Shabbos* candles as a genuine *mitzvah* in its own right — one aimed at ensuring light and domestic tranquility in the home — rather than merely a neutral activity hedged about with prohibitions against tampering. Whether this is the plain meaning of the *Gemara* is a separate question, since the concept of candle lighting as a *mitzvah* has not yet been formally introduced at this point in the discussion.

A further practical observation from *Tosafot* is worth noting. One of the *Baalei HaTosafot* records a tradition that if one has even a single kosher candle in the house, the remaining candles in other rooms need not meet the standards of acceptable wicks and oils — since the presence of one reliable light will prevent one from coming to adjust the others. This was apparently a solution to the real practical difficulty of not having sufficient kosher candles available. Whether this logic holds given the stated reason for the restriction is debated: if the concern is that one will fiddle with the problematic candle when reading or eating by it, the existence of a kosher candle in another room seems insufficient protection. Nonetheless, *Tosafot* records a second answer as well: if the concern is adjustment on *Shabbos*, one may use even non-kosher candles for activities that could theoretically be performed in darkness, since there is no inducement to adjust them.

## Temple Menorah

### Temple Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: פְּתִילוֹת וּשְׁמָנִים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בְּשַׁבָּת — אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְהַעֲלוֹת נֵר תָּמִיד״. הוּא תָּנֵי לַהּ, וְהוּא אָמַר לַהּ — כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא שַׁלְהֶבֶת עוֹלָה מֵאֵילֶיהָ, וְלֹא שֶׁתְּהֵא עוֹלָה עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama taught** a *baraita*: Those **wicks and oils, which the Sages said one may not light with them on Shabbat, one may not light** with them **in the Temple** either **because it is stated** with regard to the Temple candelabrum: "And you shall command the children of Israel, that they bring unto you pure olive oil beaten for the light, **to cause a lamp to burn continually"** (Exodus 27:20). Rami bar Ḥama **taught that** *baraita* **and he** also **said its** explanation: What is the proof from the verse? One may interpret the verse homiletically: The requirement is to light the candelabrum **so that the flame ascends of itself** when it is kindled, **and not that it ascends by means of something else,** i.e., adjusting the wick after it was lit.

Rami bar Chama teaches: the wicks and oils that the *Chachamim* said may not be used for *Shabbos* candle lighting are equally prohibited for use in the *Beis HaMikdash* — specifically for the *menorah*. The proof text is *leha'alos ner tamid*, which Rashi understands via the *hif'il* form of the verb: you cause the flame to ascend, but it must then be self-sustaining — *k'dei shetehei shalheves olah me'eleha*. The flame must rise on its own, *v'lo shetehei olah al yedei davar acher* — it must not require external assistance to keep burning. This requirement for self-sufficiency in the *mikdash* parallels the requirement for quality wicks and oils on *Shabbos*.

### Priestly Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מִבְּלָאֵי מִכְנְסֵי כֹּהֲנִים וּמֵהֶמְיָנֵיהֶם הָיוּ מַפְקִיעִין, וּמֵהֶן מַדְלִיקִין. שִׂמְחַת בֵּית הַשּׁוֹאֵבָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna: **They would unravel the** threads of the **tattered trousers of** the **priests and their belts** in order to make wicks from them, **and from** those same wicks they would **light** at the Celebration of Drawing Water. There was wool in the belts of the priests. It is said that their belts were made from, among other things, *tekhelet*, which in the Bible refers to dyed wool. Apparently, one may light with a mixture that includes a wick unsuitable for lighting. The Gemara answers: **The Celebration of Drawing Water is different,** as in that celebration, they did not light the Temple candelabrum. They lit special lanterns made specifically for that purpose and were not stringent with regard to the wicks placed in them.

A *Mishnah* is cited which states that worn-out undergarments and belts of the *kohanim* — *bigdei kehunah* — were torn into strips and used as torches for the *simchas beis hasho'evah*. The *Gemara* notes that since the belts of the *kohanim* contained a mixture of wool and linen (*kilayim*), one might ask whether using such material as wicks — even for the *mikdash* — is consistent with the standard of self-sufficiency established above. The *Gemara* answers: *lo de-butz* — the wicks used for the *menorah* itself were made from the linen garments, not from the belts. Linen is a kosher wick material, and those specific garments were made entirely of linen. The use of the priestly garments for the *simchas beis hasho'evah* torches is in any case in a separate category — *simchas beis hasho'evah shani* — distinct from the *mitzvah* of the *menorah* proper.

## Hanukkah Intro

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: פְּתִילוֹת וּשְׁמָנִים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בְּשַׁבָּת, אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בַּחֲנוּכָּה, בֵּין בְּשַׁבָּת בֵּין בַּחוֹל. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב הוּנָא? — קָסָבַר: כָּבְתָה זָקוּק לָהּ, וּמוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ. וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בַּחוֹל, אֲבָל לֹא בְּשַׁבָּת. קָסָבַר: כָּבְתָה
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said:** Those **wicks and oils with which the Sages said** that **one may not light** the lamp **on Shabbat, one may not light** the lamp **with them on Hanukkah** either; **both** when it falls **on Shabbat and** when it falls **during the week. Rava said: What is the reason** for **Rav Huna's** statement? **He holds** that if the Hanukkah light **becomes extinguished,** even though one lit it properly, **one is bound** to attend to it and relight it so that it will burn properly. Therefore, one must ensure that the wick burns properly from the outset. **And utilizing the light** of the Hanukkah lamp **is permitted** during the week. Consequently, in order to prevent him from inadvertently sinning on Shabbat, he must ensure from the outset that the wick burns well, lest he come to adjust the flame on Shabbat. Those wicks and oils do not burn well at all. **And Rav Ḥisda said:** Those same oils and wicks with which the Sages prohibited to light on Shabbat, **one may light with them** on Hanukkah **during the week, but not on Shabbat. He holds** that if the Hanukkah light is extinguished

The discussion now pivots to *Chanukah*, and it is here that the *Gemara* enters into what is essentially its only sustained treatment of the laws of *Chanukah*. The entry point is the question of whether the same restrictions governing wicks and oils for *Shabbos* apply equally to *Chanukah* candles.

Rav Huna rules: the wicks and oils that are prohibited for *Shabbos* are equally prohibited for *Chanukah* — *bein beshabbos bein bechol* — whether on *Shabbos* itself or on the weekday nights of *Chanukah*.

### Rava's Concern

Rava explains Rav Huna's reasoning. On *Shabbos*, the concern is familiar: using a problematic wick or oil might cause one to adjust the flame, leading to *chilul Shabbos*. But on a weekday of *Chanukah*, why should it matter if one fiddles with the flame? Rava explains: *kasavar kavsa zakuk la* — Rav Huna holds that if the *Chanukah* candle goes out, one is obligated to relight it. The *mitzvah* requires that the candle burn for the full prescribed duration. If one uses unreliable oils and wicks, they may sputter out and one will fail to fulfill the *mitzvah* without realizing it. Therefore, even on weekdays, only reliable materials may be used. On *Shabbos* there is the additional concern that since Rav Huna permits using the *Chanukah* light (*mutar lehishtamesh leorah*), one might come to adjust the flame while reading or eating by it.

### Rav Chisda

Rav Chisda disagrees: *madlikin bahen bechol aval lo beshabbos* — one may use the problematic wicks and oils on weekdays but not on *Shabbos*. His reasoning: *kasavar kavsa ein zakuk la* — if the candle goes out, one bears no further obligation. Accordingly, on weekdays the concern of the candle burning out is not pressing: one has discharged the *mitzvah* at the moment of lighting, provided the candle had a reasonable potential to last the required time. The question of whether a candle had the requisite potential to last is determined at the moment of lighting, not by whether it actually lasts. If one lights a candle with sufficient oil in a sheltered location, the *mitzvah* is fulfilled even if the candle subsequently goes out. On *Shabbos*, however, even Rav Chisda prohibits these materials — because since one is permitted to use the light (*mutar lehishtamesh leorah*), one will read or eat by it and will be tempted to adjust the flame, leading to a *Shabbos* violation.

### Rabbi Zeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: פְּתִילוֹת וּשְׁמָנִים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים אֵין מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בְּשַׁבָּת, מַדְלִיקִין בָּהֶן בַּחֲנוּכָּה, בֵּין בַּחוֹל בֵּין בְּשַׁבָּת. אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? — קָסָבַר: כָּבְתָה אֵין זָקוּק לָהּ, וְאָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Mattana said, and others say** that **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav said: The wicks and oils with which the Sages said one may not light on Shabbat, one may,** nevertheless, **light with them on Hanukkah, both during the week and on Shabbat. Rabbi Yirmeya said: What is Rav's reason? He holds** that if **it is extinguished, one is not bound** to attend **to it** and relight it, **and it is prohibited to use its light.** Therefore, even on Shabbat, there is no concern lest he come to adjust the wick, as it is prohibited to utilize its light.

Rabbi Zeira, citing Rav Matna (or, in another version, citing Rav directly), goes furthest: the wicks and oils prohibited for *Shabbos* may nonetheless be used for *Chanukah*, whether on weekdays or on *Shabbos* itself. Rabbi Yirmiyah explains this position: Rabbi Zeira's teacher holds both *kavsa ein zakuk la* — no obligation to relight — and *asur lehishtamesh leorah* — one may not use the *Chanukah* light for personal benefit. The prohibition on using the light removes the concern that one will fiddle with the flame on *Shabbos*, and the absence of any obligation to ensure continued burning removes the concern on weekdays.

The *Gemara* notes that Abaye initially heard this explanation in the name of Rabbi Yirmiyah and rejected it. When the same reasoning was later transmitted to him in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, he accepted it. Abaye then reflected: *ai zakai, gemarti shema'ata me'ikara* — had I merited, I would have learned it the first time. The *Gemara* asks: what practical difference does it make when one learns something? The answer: *nafka mina l'girsa de'yankusa* — material absorbed in youth adheres with far greater permanence. This is not mere sentiment; the *Gemara* treats early learning as qualitatively superior in terms of retention.

It is worth noting that we rule in accordance with this final opinion. On the question of *kavsa ein zakuk la* — if the candle goes out one need not relight it — this is the normative *halacha*. One must ensure at the time of lighting that the candle has the potential to burn for the required duration, but if it subsequently goes out one has discharged the *mitzvah*. Questions can arise in practice — for example, when lighting outdoors in the wind with the protective glass doors open: if at the very moment of lighting the candle had no reasonable prospect of lasting, some authorities question whether the *mitzvah* has been performed even if one immediately closes the doors. In most cases, however, providing sufficient oil and a sheltered location at the time of lighting is sufficient.

### Kavsah Ein Zakuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכָבְתָה אֵין זָקוּק לָהּ? וּרְמִינְהוּ: מִצְוָתָהּ מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁקַע הַחַמָּה עַד שֶׁתִּכְלֶה רֶגֶל מִן הַשּׁוּק. מַאי לָאו, דְּאִי כָּבְתָה הֲדַר מַדְלֵיק לָהּ! לָא, דְּאִי לָא אַדְלֵיק — מַדְלֵיק. וְאִי נָמֵי לְשִׁיעוּרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the opinion that one need not rekindle the Hanukkah light if it is extinguished, the Gemara asks: **And** is it true that if the Hanukkah light **is extinguished one is not bound** to attend **to it?** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from that which was taught in a *baraita*: **The mitzva** of kindling the Hanukkah lights **is from sunset until traffic in the marketplace ceases. Does that not** mean **that if** the light **is extinguished, he** must **rekindle it** so that it will remain lit for the duration of that period? The Gemara answers: **No,** the *baraita* can be understood otherwise: **That if one did not** yet **light** at sunset, **he** may still **light** the Hanukkah lights until traffic ceases. **Alternatively,** one could say that this is referring to the matter of **its measure.** One must prepare a wick and oil sufficient to burn for the period lasting from sunset until traffic ceases. If he did so, even if the light is extinguished beforehand, he need not relight it.

The *Gemara* now challenges the ruling of *kavsa ein zakuk la*. A *braisa* states: *mitzvasah mishetishka hachamah ad shetichleh regel min hashuk* — the *mitzvah* of *Chanukah* candles runs from sunset until the foot ceases from the marketplace, meaning until people have stopped milling about in the streets. One might read this as requiring the candle to burn throughout this entire period, implying an obligation to relight if it goes out.

The *Gemara* rejects this reading in two ways. First: the *braisa* is not prescribing the duration of burning, but the window of time within which the *mitzvah* may be performed. Anytime from sunset until the streets empty is a valid time to light. Second: *l'shiurah* — the *braisa* is specifying the required capacity of the oil at the time of lighting. The candle must contain enough oil to burn for that duration; it need not actually do so.

Rabbah bar bar Chana quotes Rabbi Yochanan's explanation of *ad shetichleh regel min hashuk*: specifically, until the *Tarmudayim* have left the marketplace. Rashi explains that these were wood vendors — non-Jews who remained open the latest because people would realize only after nightfall that they needed more firewood and would come to purchase it. The choice of non-Jews, Rashi notes, addresses the obvious question of when Jews would be available to see the *Chanukah* lights if they themselves were lighting at that hour.

The question of precisely when the *mitzvah* window opens — at sunset or at *tzeit hakochavim* — is debated among the *Rishonim*, with arguments on both sides. Similarly, the duration until the streets empty: the *Shulchan Aruch* sets this at approximately half an hour, a figure that reflected conditions in his time. There is a well-known story of a rabbi in *Yerushalayim* who maintained his *menorah* burning until midnight because he lived across from a cinema whose last showing ended at that hour. In the *Shulchan Aruch*'s framework, even indoors — following the transition from public to private *pirsumei nisa* due to conditions of *sakanah* — a half-hour duration is generally accepted as sufficient.

### Timing & Duration

The transition from outdoor to indoor lighting reflects a broader historical development. The original *mitzvah* was designed for *pirsumei nisa* aimed at passers-by — the candle placed in the doorframe facing the street. In times of danger, when public display was prohibited by hostile authorities (Rashi references a Zoroastrian prohibition against lighting fires outside designated sacred sites), the practice moved indoors and focused on *pirsumei nisa* within the household. This shift brought with it greater leniency regarding timing, since even late in the evening the family members at home can witness the candles. The contemporary situation, where religious freedom permits public display, has led many to return to placing the *chanukiah* in a window facing outward, which is to be encouraged.

## Hanukkah Levels

### One vs. Per Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִצְוַת חֲנוּכָּה, נֵר אִישׁ וּבֵיתוֹ. וְהַמְהַדְּרִין, נֵר לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. וְהַמְהַדְּרִין מִן הַמְהַדְּרִין, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יוֹם רִאשׁוֹן מַדְלִיק שְׁמֹנָה, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ פּוֹחֵת וְהוֹלֵךְ. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יוֹם רִאשׁוֹן מַדְלִיק אַחַת, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ מוֹסִיף וְהוֹלֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The** basic **mitzva of Hanukkah** is each day to have **a light** kindled by **a person,** the head of the household, for himself **and his household. And the *mehadrin*,** i.e., those who are meticulous in the performance of mitzvot, kindle **a light for each and every one** in the household. **And the *mehadrin min hamehadrin*,** who are even more meticulous, adjust the number of lights daily. Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree as to the nature of that adjustment. **Beit Shammai say:** On **the first day one kindles eight** lights and, from **there on, gradually decreases** the number of lights until, on the last day of Hanukkah, he kindles one light. **And Beit Hillel say:** On **the first day one kindles one** light, and from **there on, gradually increases** the number of lights until, on the last day, he kindles eight lights.

The *Gemara* now presents a *tanu rabbanan* delineating graduated levels of *Chanukah* observance. At the basic level: *mitzvat Chanukah ner ish u'beito* — one candle per household. A single person lights a single candle for the entire family; this is the minimum fulfillment. For *hammehadrin* — those who seek to beautify the *mitzvah* — the standard is *ner l'chol echad v'echad*, one candle per individual in the household. The root *hadar*, from which *mehadrin* derives, connotes the beautification of a *mitzvah*, not stringency in the sense of prohibition. It is worth noting the contrast to the contemporary use of *mehadrin* and *mehadrin min hamehadrin* as markers of strict kashrut supervision — the original sense here is entirely about the positive enhancement of *mitzvah* performance.

### Shammai vs. Hillel

For *hammehadrin min hamehadrin* — those who seek the most beautiful fulfillment — there is a dispute. Beis Shammai says: on the first day one lights eight candles, and one decreases each subsequent day: 8, 7, 6, 5, and so on. Beis Hillel says: on the first day one lights one, and one adds a candle each day: 1, 2, 3, up to eight.

There is an important dispute among the *Rishonim* regarding what exactly happens when one reaches the level of *mehadrin min hamehadrin*. *Tosafot* holds that at this level one reverts to a single *chanukiah* for the entire household, because when multiple individuals light different numbers of candles simultaneously, an observer cannot discern the number of nights from the display. Rambam, by contrast, maintains that each individual continues to light individually, with each person's candle count reflecting the number of the night. The practice among *Ashkenazim* follows the Rambam's framework despite the general pattern of ruling like the *Rishonim* of France and Germany — an interesting crossover.

### Dispute Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ תְּרֵי אָמוֹרָאֵי בְּמַעְרְבָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר זְבִידָא. חַד אָמַר טַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי כְּנֶגֶד יָמִים הַנִּכְנָסִין, וְטַעְמָא דְּבֵית הִלֵּל כְּנֶגֶד יָמִים הַיּוֹצְאִין. וְחַד אָמַר טַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי כְּנֶגֶד פָּרֵי הַחַג, וְטַעְמָא דְּבֵית הִלֵּל דְּמַעֲלִין בַּקֹּדֶשׁ וְאֵין מוֹרִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** There were **two *amora*'*im* in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, who **disagreed** with regard to **this** dispute, **Rabbi Yosei bar Avin and Rabbi Yosei bar Zevida. One said** that **the reason** for **Beit Shammai's** opinion is that the number of lights **corresponds to the incoming days,** i.e., the future. On the first day, eight days remain in Hanukkah, one kindles eight lights, and on the second day seven days remain, one kindles seven, etc. **The reason** for **Beit Hillel's** opinion is that the number of lights **corresponds to the outgoing days.** Each day, the number of lights corresponds to the number of the days of Hanukkah that were already observed. **And one said** that **the reason** for **Beit Shammai's** opinion is that the number of lights **corresponds to the bulls of the festival** of *Sukkot*: Thirteen were sacrificed on the first day and each succeeding day one fewer was sacrificed (Numbers 29:12–31). **The reason** for **Beit Hillel's** opinion is that the number of lights is based on the principle: **One elevates** to a higher level **in** matters of **sanctity and one does not downgrade.** Therefore, if the objective is to have the number of lights correspond to the number of days, there is no alternative to increasing their number with the passing of each day.

Ulla reports a dispute between two *Amoraim* of *Eretz Yisrael* — Rabbi Yose bar Abin and Rabbi Yose bar Zvida — regarding the rationale behind the Beis Shammai–Beis Hillel dispute. One opinion: Beis Shammai counts *yamim hanichnasin* — the days still to come — while Beis Hillel counts *yamim hayotzin* — the days that have passed. This is the most straightforward reading. The other opinion: Beis Shammai's descending count parallels the *parei hachag* — the bulls offered in the *musaf* of *Sukkos*, which descended from thirteen on the first day to seven on the seventh — while Beis Hillel relies on the principle of *ma'alin b'kodesh v'ein moridin*, that we ascend in holiness and do not descend.

Rabbah bar bar Chana reports that Rabbi Yochanan told him of two elders in Sidon who lived at a time when the practice had not yet fully consolidated around Beis Hillel: one followed Beis Shammai and one followed Beis Hillel, each offering his stated rationale — one citing the parallel to the *parei hachag*, the other citing *ma'alin b'kodesh v'ein moridin*.

Several explanations of this dispute deserve mention. The Maharal observes that starting with eight and descending focuses on *potential* — on the eight days yet to come — while starting with one and ascending focuses on *actuality* — on the night one is presently in. This distinction may reflect a broader pattern running through Beis Shammai–Beis Hillel disputes. On a more evocative level, there is an analogy to the optimist and the pessimist: the descending count emphasizes how many days remain — a countdown to the end — while the ascending count reflects an orientation of openness and increasing joy, indifferent to the endpoint. A striking explanation is offered by Rav Shlomo Zevin, who frames the dispute around whether the message of *Chanukah* is about eradicating evil or spreading good. Beis Shammai, whose jurisprudential tendency is often more focused on human failing and the need to restrain it, counts downward in a manner that parallels the diminishing *parei hachag*, which represent the nations of the world declining. Beis Hillel, whose orientation is toward the spreading of positive holiness, counts upward — *ma'alin b'kodesh*. Even the image of the flame, Rav Zevin suggests, is understood differently: fire as a force that destroys the *ra*, or light as a force that spreads the good.

## Placement & Shamash

### Where to Place

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נֵר חֲנוּכָּה מִצְוָה לְהַנִּיחָהּ עַל פֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ מִבַּחוּץ. אִם הָיָה דָּר בַּעֲלִיָּיה — מַנִּיחָהּ בַּחַלּוֹן הַסְּמוּכָה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. וּבִשְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה — מַנִּיחָהּ עַל שֻׁלְחָנוֹ וְדַיּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **It is a mitzva to place the Hanukkah lamp at the entrance to one's house on the outside,** so that all can see it. **If he lived upstairs, he places it at the window adjacent to the public domain. And in a time of danger,** when the gentiles issued decrees to prohibit kindling lights, **he places it on the table and** that is **sufficient** to fulfill **his** obligation.

The *tanu rabbanan* teaches: *ner Chanukah mitzvah lehannichah al petach beito mibachutz* — the *mitzvah* is to place the *Chanukah* candle at the doorway of one's home on the outside. Rashi explains this in terms of the courtyard: people entering and exiting the courtyard should see the candle in the doorframe. If one lives on an upper story (*aliyah*) where the doorway does not face the courtyard, one places it at the window nearest the public domain, so that passersby in the street can see it.

### Time of Danger

*Uvesha'at hasekanah* — in a time of danger — one simply places the candle on one's table indoors and that suffices. Rashi specifies the nature of the danger: a Zoroastrian prohibition requiring that fires be kindled only at their own sacred sites, not in private homes. The transition to indoor lighting — *al shulchano vedayo* — reflects the broader historical shift in how *pirsumei nisa* was understood, from an outward declaration to the public to an inward observance for the family. This is how the *Shulchan Aruch* rules. The question of how far to restore outdoor or visible lighting in contemporary conditions of religious freedom is one where the predominant practice is to place the *chanukiah* in a window.

### Shamash Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: צָרִיךְ נֵר אַחֶרֶת לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ. וְאִי אִיכָּא מְדוּרָה — לֹא צָרִיךְ. וְאִי אָדָם חָשׁוּב הוּא — אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא מְדוּרָה צָרִיךְ נֵר אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** One **must** kindle **another light** in addition to the Hanukkah lights **in order to use its light,** as it is prohibited to use the light of the Hanukkah lights. **And if there is a bonfire, he need not** light an additional light, as he can use the light of the bonfire. However, **if he is an important person,** who is unaccustomed to using the light of a bonfire, **even though there is a bonfire, he must** kindle **another light.**

Rava rules: *tzarich ner acher lehishtamesh leorah* — one requires an additional candle in order to use the light. This is the basis for the *shamash*. The purpose is twofold. First, since one may not use the *Chanukah* candles themselves for personal benefit (*ein lanu reshus lehishtamesh bahen*), one needs a separate candle whose light is permitted for use. Second — and this is Rashi's emphasis — the additional candle creates the *pirsumei nisa* by demonstrating that one does not need the *Chanukah* lights for illumination. An observer sees the *shamash* and understands that the *Chanukah* candles serve a different purpose entirely.

If there is already a *medura* — a larger fire or torch providing ample light — one does not need a *shamash*, since the abundant light already makes clear that the *Chanukah* candles are not for personal use. However, if one is a *chashuv* person — a prominent individual who would not normally avail himself of the coarser light of a torch and would instead use refined candlelight — then even in the presence of a *medura* one requires a *shamash*, so that observers will perceive the separate candlelight as sufficient for personal use and understand the *Chanukah* candles to be serving another purpose. In contemporary practice, with electric lighting universally available, the concern that prompts the requirement for a *shamash* is in effect answered by the electric lights themselves, which function as the equivalent of the *medura*.

## Hanukkah History

### Megillat Taanit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חֲנוּכָּה? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּכ״ה בְּכִסְלֵיו יוֹמֵי דַחֲנוּכָּה תְּמָנְיָא אִינּוּן דְּלָא לְמִסְפַּד בְּהוֹן וּדְלָא לְהִתְעַנּוֹת בְּהוֹן. שֶׁכְּשֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ יְוָוֽנִים לַהֵיכָל טִמְּאוּ כׇּל הַשְּׁמָנִים שֶׁבַּהֵיכָל. וּכְשֶׁגָּבְרָה מַלְכוּת בֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי וְנִצְּחוּם, בָּדְקוּ וְלֹא מָצְאוּ אֶלָּא פַּךְ אֶחָד שֶׁל שֶׁמֶן שֶׁהָיָה מוּנָּח בְּחוֹתָמוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְלֹא הָיָה בּוֹ אֶלָּא לְהַדְלִיק יוֹם אֶחָד. נַעֲשָׂה בּוֹ נֵס וְהִדְלִיקוּ מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁמוֹנָה יָמִים. לְשָׁנָה אַחֶרֶת קְבָעוּם וַעֲשָׂאוּם יָמִים טוֹבִים בְּהַלֵּל וְהוֹדָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is Hanukkah,** and why are lights kindled on Hanukkah? The Gemara answers: **The Sages taught** in *Megillat Ta*'*anit*: **On the twenty-fifth of Kislev, the days of Hanukkah are eight. One may not eulogize on them and one may not fast on them.** What is the reason? **When the Greeks entered the Sanctuary they defiled all the oils that were in the Sanctuary** by touching them. **And when the Hasmonean monarchy overcame** them **and emerged victorious over them, they searched and found only one cruse of oil that was placed with the seal of the High Priest,** undisturbed by the Greeks. **And there was** sufficient oil **there to light** the candelabrum for **only one day. A miracle occurred and they lit** the candelabrum **from it eight days. The next year** the Sages **instituted** those days **and made them holidays with** recitation of ***hallel* and** special **thanksgiving** in prayer and blessings.

The *Gemara* now asks: *mai Chanukah* — what is *Chanukah*? This launches the *Gemara*'s sole explicit historical account of the holiday. The *tanu rabbanan* begins by citing *Megillat Taanit*: *bekaf-hey beKislev, yomei deChanukah temanya inun, dela lemispad behon udela lehitanot behon* — from the 25th of Kislev are eight days of *Chanukah*, on which one may not eulogize or fast.

*Megillat Taanit* is an early work — one of the earliest pieces of rabbinic writing — that enumerated the days on which fasting and eulogy were prohibited due to historical events. Most of those events have been entirely forgotten; the 25th of Kislev appears on that list without any greater elaboration than the other entries. There is, however, a Babylonian gloss appended to *Megillat Taanit*, and the *Gemara* now incorporates that gloss, which expands upon the bare reference and provides the narrative behind *Chanukah*.

### Greek Defilement

*Keshenichnasu Yevanim leHeichal* — when the Greeks entered the *Heichal*, the inner sanctuary of the *Beis HaMikdash* — *timu kol hashemanim shebaHeichal* — they contaminated all of the oils stored there, the oils that would have been used for the *menorah*. *Ucheshegavrah malchut Beis Chashmonai venitzchum* — when the *Chashmonai* dynasty prevailed and defeated them, they searched and found only a single *pach* of oil.

### Oil Miracle

The cruse of oil bore the seal of the *Kohen Gadol* — *shehayah munach bechotamo shel Kohen Gadol* — indicating that it had not been opened or tampered with and therefore remained *tahor*. It contained sufficient oil to burn for only one day. *Naasah bo nes* — a miracle occurred — *vehidliku mimenu shemonah yamim* — and from it they kindled for eight days.

### Yom Tov Status

The following year, the rabbis established these eight days as *Yom Tov* — *beHallel vehodaah* — with *Hallel* and thanksgiving. Rashi is careful to clarify: this is not *Yom Tov* in the sense of a prohibition on *melachah*. The designation consists of the recitation of *Hallel* and the addition of *Al Hanissim* in prayer and *birkat hamazon*. Notably absent from the *Gemara*'s description of what was established is the lighting of candles itself — an observation that invites reflection.

### Oil Miracle Meaning

Several questions cluster around this account. The most commonly raised is the so-called "*Beis Yosef* question": if there was oil for one day and it lasted eight days, the miracle was only of seven additional days, not eight — and this is the question for which a wide range of answers has been offered. The most intuitive answer, which the Maharal articulates, is that each day only one-eighth of the available oil was consumed, so the miracle manifested on each of the eight days equally.

A different question — why the miracle of the oil was emphasized at all — leads to a historically significant observation. All of the sources contemporaneous with the events of *Chanukah*, and all of the sources originating in *Eretz Yisrael*, focus exclusively on the military victory and the rededication of the *Beis HaMikdash*. The book of Maccabees attributes the eight-day celebration to a parallel with *Sukkos* (whose celebration had been deferred during the fighting). *Al Hanissim* mentions the military victory without any reference to the oil miracle. Josephus, when explaining why Jews light candles on *Chanukah*, suggests only that light symbolizes redemption, with no mention of any miracle involving oil. The miracle of the oil appears solely in this Babylonian gloss on *Megillat Taanit*.

The Maharal addresses this directly. He argues that the miracle of the oil was designed to demonstrate the divine significance of the military victory. One might otherwise attribute the *Chashmonai* triumph to human valor alone, without recognizing God's role. The small miraculous sign — the single cruse burning for eight days — was intended to reveal the divine hand behind the larger events, connecting the military victory to the rededication of the *Beis HaMikdash* and infusing the entire episode with religious meaning. The oil miracle was, in the Maharal's reading, a vehicle for making legible what was truly at stake.

This has implications for how the concept of *mehadrin min hamehadrin* fits into the broader picture of *Chanukah*. One might suggest that the meticulous, layered care invested in *pirsumei nisa* — with its levels of basic, *mehadrin*, and *mehadrin min hamehadrin* — mirrors the extraordinary gesture God made in revealing the divine through this particular miracle. Just as that miracle went beyond what was necessary to make the point, so too the most beautiful fulfillment of *Chanukah* goes beyond the minimum. The fundamental educational question of *Chanukah* — whether the holiday is primarily about the oil miracle or about the historical struggle for religious independence — remains, and the *Gemara*'s account, read together with the Maharal's interpretation, suggests that the miracle of the oil and the victory of the *Chashmonaim* are not competing narratives but interlocking ones: the miraculous cruse gives the war its proper religious meaning, and the war gives the cruse its proper historical context.
