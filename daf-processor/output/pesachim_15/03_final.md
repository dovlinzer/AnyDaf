# Pesachim 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Witness Testimony & Chametz

### Rav Ashi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּמַאי חִדּוּשׁ קָא אַשְׁמַעַן — דִּלְמָא סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת תַּנָּאִים הִיא בִּמְכִילַת עֲדוּת לְחָמֵץ, קָא אַשְׁמַעַן דַּאֲפִילּוּ הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מַחְלוֹקֶת, כַּמַּחְלוֹקֶת בְּעֵדוּת כָּךְ הַמַּחְלוֹקֶת בַּחָמֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** **GEMARA:** **And what novelty is** the Gemara **informing us of?** The answer is: **Perhaps it enters your mind** that **there is a dispute among the** *tanna'im* **regarding the application of the witness testimony rules to the** *chametz* **context,** so **the Gemara informs us that even where there is a dispute,** the dispute in the witness testimony rules is **equivalent to the dispute regarding** *chametz*.

The daf opens near the bottom of 12b, where the Gemara presents the ruling of Rav Ashi. The broader sugya has been examining how much latitude witnesses are permitted in their determination of time — specifically, whether differing testimony by several hours, or testimony that spans the distinction between day and night, constitutes a contradiction that invalidates the witnesses altogether. The context motivating this entire discussion is, of course, the laws of *chametz*: when must one stop eating *chametz*, and does the imprecision of ordinary people's timekeeping bear on that determination?

Rav Ashi articulates a clean principle: *kamachloket b'edus kach machloket b'chametz* — whatever the rule is regarding the permissible discrepancy in *edus*, the same rule applies to *chametz*. If witnesses may be off by an hour, then one must stop eating *chametz* an hour before the prescribed time.

The Gemara immediately objects: *peshita* — this is obvious! That is precisely why the entire preceding discussion took place. The Gemara answers that the novelty lies in the following clarification: one might have supposed that there is a *machloket tannaim* as to whether the witness testimony rules can even be applied to the *chametz* context at all — perhaps the analogy is contested. Rav Ashi therefore comes to inform us that this is not a dispute about whether to apply the rule, but rather that the application is accepted by all. The *machloket* concerns only what the rule itself is — how far off may witnesses be before their testimony is discredited — and that same rule maps directly onto *chametz*.

### Witness Discrepancy Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אַשִׁי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁעוֹת. הַקֹּדֶם לַהֲנֵץ הַחַמָּה וְהַקֹּדֶם לְהַהֲנֵץ הַחַמָּה — בָּטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Shimi bar Ashi said: The leniency regarding discrepancy applies only when the disagreement is a matter of hours.** In a case where one witness says the event occurred **before sunrise and** another says it occurred **before sunrise,** their testimonies are not contradictory. However, if one witness says the event occurred **before sunrise and** another says it occurred **before sunrise,** in the sense of during the sunrise itself, their testimonies are contradictory and are **void.**

The discussion of how much divergence in witness testimony is tolerable now sharpens through the contribution of Rav Shimi bar Ashi. He rules: *lo shanu ela b'shaos* — the leniency regarding discrepancy applies only when the disagreement is a matter of hours within the same part of the day. However, if one witness says the event occurred before *hanetz hachama* — before sunrise — and another says it occurred after sunrise, their testimonies are mutually contradictory and are *bateilah*.

The Gemara interjects: *peshita* — surely this is self-evident? One witness says nighttime, the other says daytime. What novelty is there here?

### Applying to Chametz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי קָא אַשְׁמַעַן? הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַקֹּדֶם לַהֲנֵץ הַחַמָּה וּבְתוֹךְ הַהֲנֵץ הַחַמָּה — בָּטֵל. וּמַאי טַעְמָא? דִּלְמָא תִּקְנֵיהּ גִּלּוּיָא וּמִדַּעְתִּי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **If so, what novelty** is **the Gemara informing us of?** Rather, **the Gemara is saying: If one witness says the event occurred before sunrise and another says it occurred during the sunrise itself,** their testimonies are **void.** **And what is the reason?** The answer is: **Perhaps the witness** who said "during the sunrise" **was referring only to the glow on the horizon,** the *giluya*, **and his intention was** that the actual cresting of the sun had **not yet arrived.**

The Gemara therefore reformulates the case: what if one witness says the event occurred before sunrise and the other says it occurred *b'toch hanetz hachama* — during the sunrise itself? Here too, Rav Shimi bar Ashi rules that the testimonies are contradictory and invalid.

Again the Gemara objects that this seems obvious. The answer is that one might have thought the two witnesses are actually saying the same thing — that the one who says *b'toch hanetz hachama* means only that he saw the glow on the horizon, the *giluya*, the atmospheric light preceding the actual cresting of the sun, and not the sun itself. In that reading, both witnesses would be describing pre-dawn conditions and would be in agreement. Rav Shimi therefore comes to inform us that this is not so: the expression *b'toch hanetz hachama* is interpreted to mean that the sun had already begun to rise, and that creates a genuine contradiction with the testimony of the first witness who places the event before any sunrise at all.

### Day vs. Night

This ruling raises a natural methodological question. If the concern is ambiguity in how a witness phrases his description of the time, why not simply ask him to clarify? One might distinguish between a city dweller and a farmer or shepherd, who would be far more attuned by eye to the precise stages of dawn and would use this language with greater precision.

### Sunrise Terminology

A closer look reveals that the Gemara may be establishing principles for a structured context of inquiry — the formal *chakiros* and *derishos* applied to witness testimony — rather than purely abstract rules. At a certain point in the examination, once a witness has expressed himself using the phrase *b'toch hanetz hachama*, that formulation is treated as carrying a definite meaning, and one does not probe further lest one put words in his mouth and introduce uncertainty in the opposite direction. In other words, the rule is not about abstract testimony in a vacuum, but about the fixed legal weight assigned to particular formulations once they have been uttered in the context of formal examination. Two witnesses independently producing the same formulation lend one another precision. Whatever ambiguity might theoretically attach to the phrase is resolved by the legal convention that assigns it a single fixed meaning.

## Chametz Fifth Hour

### Rav Nachman's Psak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — שֶׁהַחָמִיר בַּרְבִיעִית, וּמִתְּחִלַּת חֲמִישִׁית תּוֹלִין, וְמִסּוֹף חֲמִישִׁית שׂוֹרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Nachman said** that **Rav said: The *halakha* follows Rabbi Yehuda.** According to Rabbi Yehuda, **one may eat *chametz* until** the beginning of **the fifth hour,** that the fifth hour is a suspended period of *toleh* — neither eating nor burning — **and one burns the** *chametz* **at the end of the fifth hour.**

The Gemara now records that Rav Nachman rules in the name of Rav: *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that one may eat *chametz* until the beginning of the fifth hour, that the fifth hour is a suspended period of *toleh* — neither eating nor burning — and that one burns the *chametz* at the end of the fifth hour.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: מִיבְּעֵי לָךְ לְמֵימַר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר — דִּסְתַם מִשְׁנָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר! דִּתְנַן: כׇּל שָׁעָה שֶׁמוּתַר לֶאֱכוֹל — מַאֲכִיל לַבְּהֵמָה וּמֵטִיל בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: You should have ruled that the *halakha* follows Rabbi Meir,** as **an unannotated mishna accords with Rabbi Meir.** **As we learned** in a mishna: **As long as one is permitted to eat** *chametz*, **one may feed it to one's animals and derive benefit from it,** implying that there is no intermediate suspended period at all, which is consistent with Rabbi Meir's position.

Rava challenges Rav Nachman: why rule like Rabbi Yehuda? One should instead rule like Rabbi Meir, since there is a *stam mishna* that accords with Rabbi Meir's view. The principle is well established that the *halacha* follows the *stam mishna*, and the Mishnah states *kol sha'ah she-mutar le-ekhol ma'akhil* — as long as one is permitted to eat *chametz*, one may feed it to one's animals and derive benefit from it — implying that there is no intermediate suspended period at all, which is consistent with Rabbi Meir's position.

### Stam Mishna Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: אִיכָּא מִדְּפִיסוֹל מִינַּהּ — דְּתָנְיָא בְּמַסֶּכֶת כׇּל שָׁעָה, דִּכְתִּיב בָּהּ ״מִשּׁוּם דְּקַשְׁיָא מוּתָּר״. דִּכְתִּיב ״כׇּל שָׁעָה שֶׁמוּתַר לֶאֱכוֹל״ וַדַּי סְתַם ״כׇּל שָׁעָה שֶׁאוֹכְלִין״!
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: There is** a **difficulty in** the Mishnah **itself,** preventing it from serving as an *unannotated mishna*. **As it is taught** in the chapter *Kol Sha'ah*, **the explanation** of why the Mishnah uses the specific language that it does: **"As long as it is permitted" is because** the Mishnah **is difficult.** The Mishnah says **"as long as it is permitted to eat"** rather than the simpler formulation **"as long as one eats,"** implying that it refers to someone for whom it remains permitted.

The Gemara rejects this argument. That Mishnah is not in fact a *stam mishna* in the relevant sense, because it contains a difficulty in its own language: *mishum de-kashi mutar*. The Mishnah says *kol sha'ah she-mutar le-ekhol* — as long as it is permitted to eat — rather than the simpler formulation *kol sha'ah she-okhel* — as long as one eats. The Gemara in the chapter *Kol Sha'ah* explicitly raises this linguistic difficulty: the word *mutar* implies "as long as some other person is permitted to eat it," meaning as long as there exists someone for whom it remains permitted. This would refer to a *kohen*, who is the third opinion in the Mishnah — one who can eat *chametz* longer than an ordinary Israelite. Since the language points in the direction of the third view rather than resolving the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda, the Mishnah cannot serve as a *stam* ruling in favor of either.

### Rabban Gamliel Not Machria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי לָא נִימָא כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל? דִּנַּיְחָא לְהוּ בִּנְקִיּוּתִים — אָמַר לֵיהּ: רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לָאו מַחְרִיא הוּא, אֶלָּא דִּבְרֵי עַצְמוֹ הִיא. וּמַאי חִדּוּשׁ קָא אַשְׁמַעַן — אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא מִדִּבְרֵי אַחֵר תַּנָּא קָאָמַר רַב, דְּמִן הַבָּרַיְיתָא תִּשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **And why not say that the *halakha* follows Rabban Gamliel,** who might serve as a *machria* — a tiebreaker? **He said to him:** The answer is that **Rabban Gamliel is not a *machria* at all; rather,** these are **his own independent views.** **And what novelty** is **the Gemara informing us of?** Rather, **if you wish, say that Rav is speaking in accordance with another *tanna'im* as his authority for determining the *halakha*,** and **from the *baraita* you will hear** the source.

One might then suggest: rule like Rabban Gamliel, whose view might serve as a *machria* — a tiebreaker tilting toward one of the other positions. The Gemara rejects this as well: Rabban Gamliel is not a *machria* at all. He does not tip the balance between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda; rather, he presents a third, independent opinion entirely his own — *ta'ama denafshei ka'amar*. Rabban Gamliel's position of *toleh chullin kol arba, terumah kol chamesh* operates according to its own logic and cannot be used to adjudicate between the other two views.

Alternatively, the Gemara suggests, *i ba'it eima*: Rav, in ruling like Rabbi Yehuda, was following a different *tanna* as his authority for determining the *halacha*, as will now be established from a *baraita*.

## Pre-Pesach Terumah Rules

### 13th Erev Shabbat Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כַּד אִית יוֹם אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר בְּשַׁבָּת — בִּשְׁלוֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שׁוֹרְפִין אֶת הַחָמֵץ וְשׂוֹרְפִים אֶת הַתְּרוּמוֹת הַטְּמֵאוֹת וְהַתּוֹלֻיוֹת וְהַטְּהוֹרוֹת. וּמִן הַטְּהוֹרוֹת מַנִּיחִין כְּדֵי שְׁתֵּי סְעוּדוֹת — בַּלַּיְלָה וַבַּבּוֹקֶר — עַד שֶׁיִּטְמֵא לוֹ בְּרְבִיעִית בַּיּוֹם שֶׁל עֲרוּבַת פְּסַח, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִישׁ בַּרְתּוֹתָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When the fourteenth of Nisan falls on Shabbat — so that one cannot perform *bi'ur chametz* on Shabbat itself — **on the thirteenth one destroys all *chametz* and burns *teruma* that is *tamei*, *teruma* that is *taluy* (whose status is doubtful), and *teruma* that is *tahor* (ritually pure). However, one leaves over from the *teruma tehorah* enough food for two meals — for Friday night and for Shabbat morning — sufficient to eat until the fourth hour on Shabbat *erev Pesach*. This is the opinion of Rabbi Elazar ben Yehuda of Bartota, stated in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua.**

The *baraita* presents the following case: when the fourteenth of Nisan falls on Shabbat — so that one cannot perform *bi'ur chametz* on Shabbat itself — one destroys all *chametz* on the thirteenth. The *baraita* adds that on this thirteenth, one also burns *terumah* that is *tamei*, *terumah* that is *taluy* (whose status is doubtful), and *terumah* that is *tahor* (ritually pure). However, one leaves over from the *terumah tehorah* enough food for two meals — for Friday night and for Shabbat morning — sufficient to eat until the fourth hour on Shabbat *erev Pesach*. This is the opinion of Rabbi Elazar ben Yehuda of Bartota, stated in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua.

It is worth noting that this *baraita* applies specifically to a *kohen* who possesses *terumah*. The underlying question — whether to leave over provisions for Shabbat or destroy everything early — is framed entirely around *terumah*, since for a non-*kohen* the question of leaving over *terumah* for a meal would not arise.

### Terumah Burning Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: טְהוֹרוֹת לֹא יִשָּׂרְפוּ, שׁוּמָּא יֵצְאוּ לְהֶן כֹּהֲנִים בַּבּוֹקֶר וְיֹאכְלוּ. אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּבָר בִּקַּשְׁנוּ וְלֹא מָצָאנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to him:** You **should not burn the *teruma tehorah* early, because perhaps *kohanim* will come in the morning who can eat it.** Rabbi Elazar **said to them:** The answer is: **We have already investigated and found** that **no *kohanim*** in the area **will come to eat it.**

The Sages object to Rabbi Elazar's ruling: *tehoros lo yisarefu shema yimtzu lahen ochlim* — you should not burn the *terumah tehorah* early, because perhaps *kohanim* will come in the morning who can eat it, and you will have destroyed it prematurely, before any prohibition required it. Rabbi Elazar responds: *kvar bikshu v'lo matzu* — we have already investigated and found that no *kohanim* in the area will come to eat it.

### Eliyahu Safek Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: שׁוּמָּא חוּץ לַחוּמָה לָנוּ. אָמַר לָהֶם: אִם כֵּן, גַּם תּוֹלוּיוֹת אַל נִשְׂרֹף, שׁוּמָּא יָבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ וִיטַהֲרֶנָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to him: Perhaps** there are *kohanim* **who have lodged outside the city walls and will come in the morning.** Rabbi Elazar **said to them:** If **one is going to reason from speculative possibilities, then even the *taluy* — the *teruma* of doubtful status — should not be burned, because perhaps **Eliyahu HaNavi will arrive on the morning of *erev Pesach* and declare all the doubtful cases to be *tahor*, at which point the *teruma* could have been eaten.**

The Sages push back further: *shema chutz la-choma lanu* — what of those who have lodged outside the city walls and will come in the morning? Rabbi Elazar's opponents argue that the possibility of such visitors should prevent premature burning.

Rabbi Elazar then turns the argument back on his interlocutors: if one is going to reason from speculative possibilities, then even the *taluy* — the *terumah* of doubtful status — should not be burned, because *shema yavo Eliyahu v'yitaharem* — perhaps Eliyahu HaNavi will arrive on the morning of *erev Pesach* and declare all the doubtful cases to be *tahor*, at which point the *terumah* could have been eaten. Since the opponents insist that no opportunity for consumption should be foreclosed, they cannot consistently permit burning the *taluy*.

### Eliyahu Won't Come Erev Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: כְּבָר מוּבְטַּח לוֹ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאֵין אֵלִיָּהוּ בָּא לֹא בַּעֲרוּבֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת וְלֹא בַּעֲרוּבֵי יְמֵי טוֹב מִפְּנֵי הַטּוֹרַח.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages responded:** The answer is that **it has already been assured to Israel that Eliyahu will not come on the eve of Shabbat or on the eve of Yom Tov, because of the *torach*, the burden of preparation** that the nation is engaged in.

The Sages respond with a striking ruling: *kvar muvtach lahen l'Yisrael she'ein Eliyahu ba lo b'arvei Shabbatot v'lo b'arvei yamim tovim mipnei ha-torach* — it has already been assured to Israel that Eliyahu will not come on the eve of Shabbat or on the eve of Yom Tov, because of the *torach*, the burden of preparation that the nation is engaged in. This is a remarkable statement. It reflects the principle that the legitimate demands of daily life and sacred preparation take precedence over the immediacy of redemption — the people are occupied building their lives as God has given them, and Eliyahu will not disrupt their Shabbat preparations. It bears noting that Eliyahu *could* come on *erev Pesach* itself; the restriction applies only to *erev Shabbat* and *erev Yom Tov*. The discussion here concerns the thirteenth of Nisan, which falls on a Friday in this scenario.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא זָזוּ מִשׁוּם עַד שֶׁקָּבְעוּ הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלְעָזָר בֶּן יְהוּדָה אִישׁ בַּרְתּוֹתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They did not leave that place until they established that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Elazar**, namely that **one should not burn the extra *teruma tehorah* prematurely, because someone might yet come to eat it.**

Following this exchange, the Gemara records: *lo zazu misham ad she-kav'u halacha k'Rabbi Elazar ben Yehuda ish Bartota* — they did not leave that place until they established that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Elazar, namely that one should not burn the extra *terumah tehorah* prematurely, because someone might yet come to eat it.

One might ask: does this ruling pertain to eating — that one may eat until the fourth hour — or only to the question of *bi'ur*, when one must destroy? Rashi clarifies: the ruling in favor of Rabbi Elazar concerns only the *bi'ur*, the timing of destruction on the thirteenth versus waiting until the fourteenth. Regarding the question of eating, *l'olam eima lach k'dei le'ekhol ad chamesh sha'os* — in truth one leaves over enough to eat until the fifth hour, and the *halacha* that we follow Rabbi Elazar does not imply that the eating deadline is the fourth hour.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ אָמַר לַיְיתִי, הִשְׁתַּמְּטוּתִי עַל הֵיכַל בִּישִׁי תִּי — שַׁפִּיר אִיתְנַהוּ. וְרַבִּי אוּמָא נָמֵי קָסַבַר הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **adds that *Rebbi* himself also holds with Rav Nachman's ruling** — that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda — as evidenced by the following *ma'aseh*.

## Lost Chametz Case

### Yochanan Chakuka Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד שֶׁהִפְקִיד שַׂק מָלֵא חָמֵץ אֵצֶל יוֹחָנָן חָקוּקַה, וַחֲזָרִים נִקְּבוּ בַּשַּׂק וְהַחָמֵץ בָּא לַחוּץ. וּבָא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **The case involves a certain individual who deposited a bag full of *chametz* with Yochanan Chakuka. Mice chewed through the bag and the *chametz* began leaking out.** It was *erev Pesach*, **and the man came before Rebbi** seeking guidance.

The case involves a certain individual who deposited a bag full of *chametz* with Yochanan Chakuka. Mice chewed through the bag and the *chametz* began leaking out. ### Fifth Hour Deadline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: בַּשָּׁעָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה — חַכֵּה, בַּשִּׁנִיָּה — חַכֵּה, בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית — חַכֵּה, בָּרְבִיעִית — חַכֵּה, בַּחֲמִישִׁית — צֵא וּמְכוֹר בַּשּׁוּק.

Rebbi's response was hour by hour: at the first hour he said, wait — the owner may yet come and retrieve it. At the second hour, the same. Third hour: wait. Fourth hour: wait. At the fifth hour, Rebbi said: *tze u-mchor ba-shuk* — go out and sell it in the marketplace. The moment the fifth hour arrived, Rebbi instructed that the *chametz* be sold, representing the last possible moment at which value could still be salvaged on the owner's behalf. This *ma'aseh* demonstrates that Rebbi held the fifth hour is indeed the final deadline — consistent with the ruling like Rabbi Yehuda.

### Rav Yosef Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי לָאו לְנוֹכְרִים? אִישׁ לְבֵירְיָא בִּיסְרָא וּבְהָא קָמַייְּתֵהּ וְאָכִיל? דִּלְמָא אֲפִילּוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי.

> **Translation:** **The Gemara asks:** Is **this sale presumably to non-Jews?** This would accord with Rabbi Yehuda's position that during the fifth hour one may no longer eat *chametz* but may still derive *hana'ah* from it. **Rav Yosef, however, suggests that perhaps** the instruction could be interpreted as **permitting sale even to a Jew** as well, in accordance with Rabbi Meir's view that *chametz* remains fully permitted until the end.

The Gemara asks: *mai lav l'nochrim* — presumably this sale is to non-Jews? This would accord with Rabbi Yehuda's position that during the fifth hour one may no longer eat *chametz* but may still derive *hana'ah* from it — which could take the form of selling it to a *nochri*. Rav Yosef, however, suggests that perhaps *lo l'Yisrael* — Rebbi's instruction could be interpreted as permitting sale to a Jew as well, in accordance with Rabbi Meir's view that *chametz* remains fully permitted until the end.

### Abaye's Question

### Chashda Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם חַשְׁדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Because of the concern for suspicion.**

The answer is: *mishum chashada* — because of the concern for suspicion. A guardian cannot transact with himself over property entrusted to him, as it creates the appearance of self-dealing. He might underpay, pocketing the difference, and no one would be the wiser.

### Charity Fund Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גַּבָּיֵי צְדָקָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם עֶסֶק בִּידֵיהֶם וְאֵין לָהֶם עַנִיִּים לְחַלֵּק לָהֶם, מַחְלִיפִין עִם בְּרִיתוּתָא.

### Soup Kitchen Rules

>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, the administrator of a *tamchui* — a communal soup kitchen — who has leftover food and no recipients to give it to, must sell the surplus** to others. **He may not purchase it for himself.**

Similarly, the administrator of a *tamchui* — a communal soup kitchen — who has leftover food and no recipients to give it to, must sell the surplus to others. He may not purchase it for himself, even at fair market value, and keep the proceeds for the institution.

### Nekyim Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִקְרִי אִיתְנָא, וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם אֶת הַמִּשְׁמֶרֶת, שׁוּ״ם הוּא ״וְהִיִּיתֶם נְקִיִּים מֵה׳ וּמִישְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **The source for both rulings is** related to **the verse *"you shall be clean before God and before Israel."*** **This is a striking demand: one must not only be honest before God, but must also appear honest before one's fellow human beings.**

The source for both rulings is the verse *v'heyitem nekiyim me'Hashem u-mi-Yisrael* — "you shall be clean before God and before Israel." This is a striking demand: one must not only be honest before God, but must also appear honest before one's fellow human beings. One's conduct must withstand scrutiny from both directions.

### Corruption Prevention

This principle is worth pausing over. It is not simply *marit ayin* — the rabbinic concern that onlookers might be led to erroneous conclusions about what is permitted. *Marit ayin* functions when observers might mistakenly infer that a forbidden act is allowed. Here the concern is different and in some ways more serious: the worry is not that people will imitate the act, but that they will suspect the actor of genuine wrongdoing. These are positions of institutional trust, and the temptation for self-dealing is inherent in the structure. The rabbis therefore impose procedural safeguards — and, as the *Rambam* records as *halacha* based on *Masechet Pe'ah*, communal charity administration must be carried out by at least two or three individuals together, never by one person alone.

### Spoilage Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּכְדֵי חֶסְרוֹנָהּ, אֲבָל יוֹתֵר מִכְּדֵי חֶסְרוֹנָהּ — מוֹכְרִים בְּבֵית דִּין. וְכׇל שְׁכֵן הָכָא דַאֲבִידָה לְגַמְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye adds a refinement from a ruling of Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan:** The **general rule that one must leave deposited produce alone applies only when the spoilage is proceeding at the normal expected rate.** However, **if the spoilage is advancing faster than would ordinarily be expected, then one sells through *beit din*.** **And certainly, in the case of *chametz*, where the loss is total and absolute,** the instruction to sell through *beit din* — or, in this practical case, in the marketplace — is entirely justified.

Abaye adds a refinement from a ruling of Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lo shanu ela bi-khdei chesronah* — the general rule that one must leave deposited produce alone applies only when the spoilage is proceeding at the normal expected rate. Every farmer knows that a certain percentage of produce will go bad; that is accepted and the guardian bears no responsibility to intervene. However, *aval yoter mi-khdei chesronah* — if the spoilage is advancing faster than would ordinarily be expected, then one sells through *beit din*, for at that point genuine loss is being inflicted.

And certainly, *v'chol sheken hacha de-aveida legamrei* — all the more so in the case of *chametz*, where the loss is total and absolute. Once the sixth hour arrives, the *chametz* has no value whatsoever and cannot be transferred or consumed by anyone. This is not ordinary spoilage proceeding at some rate; it is a cliff edge. The case of *chametz* is therefore a fortiori stronger than even the case of produce spoiling faster than expected, and the instruction to sell through *beit din* — or, in this practical case, in the marketplace — is entirely justified.

## Todah Signal System

### Two Loaf Signal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁתֵּי כִכּוֹת שֶׁל תּוֹדָה הָיוּ מֻנָּחוֹת עַל גַּג הָאִצְטְבָּה. כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמֻּנָּחוֹת — כׇּל הַעָם אוֹכְלִים. נִטְּלָה אַחַת — תּוֹלִים. נִטְּלוּ שְׁתֵּיהֶן — הִשְׁחִילוּ הַכֹּל וְשׂוֹרְפִים.
>
> **Translation:** **MISHNA:** **The Mishnah records that the public signal for when to stop eating *chametz* and when to burn it was based on two loaves of *todah* — thanksgiving offering bread — placed on** the platform **in the *Beit Hamikdash*. As long as both loaves remained in place, the public knew they could still eat. When one was removed, the signal for *toleh* had arrived: no more eating, but not yet burning. When both loaves were removed, everyone** began **burning their *chametz*.**

The Gemara now turns to the Mishnah that provides the subject matter for the remainder of the daf. The Mishnah records that the public signal for when to stop eating *chametz* and when to burn it was based on two loaves of *todah* — thanksgiving offering bread — placed *al gabei ha-itztaba*, on a platform or bench in the *Beit Hamikdash*. *Kol zman she-munachos kol ha-am ochlin* — as long as both loaves remained in place, the public knew they could still eat. *Nitlah achas* — when one was removed, the signal for *toleh* had arrived: no more eating, but not yet burning. *Nitlu shteihen hischilu kol ha-am sorfin* — when both loaves were removed, everyone began burning their *chametz*.

This is a striking system. The obvious question is how widely the signal could be perceived. The assumption appears to be that word travels quickly, particularly during the *regel* when Jerusalem was crowded with pilgrims and the *Beit Hamikdash* was at the center of communal life. The signal presumably reached those who were either in direct sight of the platform or close enough to hear from those who were. The Mishnah's system was evidently considered adequate for its purpose, even if it did not reach every individual simultaneously.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא קֻדְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: עַל גַּב הָאִצְטְבָּה. אָמַר לוֹ: דְּמַעְלְמִית הִנַּחְתְּ לָהּ? אֶלָּא עַל גַּג הָאִצְטְבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **GEMARA:** A *tanna* **who recited the Mishnah before Rabbi Yehuda had the text reading:** The loaves were placed **on** the **bench itself. Rabbi Yehuda objected: Do you put it there to keep it hidden?** Rather, the correct reading is **on the *roof* of the *itztaba*,** so that it could be seen from a distance.

A *tanna* who recited the Mishnah before Rabbi Yehuda had the text reading *al gav ha-itztaba* — on the bench itself. Rabbi Yehuda objected: do you put it there to keep it hidden? The whole point is that it serve as a visible signal. The correct reading is *al gag ha-itztaba* — on the *roof* of the *itztaba*, the elevated platform, so that it could be seen from a distance.

### Why Todah Pesulin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי — תּוֹדָה הִיא דַּאֲתַוְלוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהָיוּ מְרוּבִּים נִפְסְלוּ בְּלִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Mishnah specifies that the two *todah* loaves used as the signal were *pesulot* — disqualified. The Gemara investigates why.** **Rabbi Chanina explains:** The reason is that **because so many people brought *korban todah* at the time of the *regel*, there was far more bread than could be eaten within the allotted time period.** A *todah* must be consumed within a day and a night; bread left over beyond that time becomes *linah* — leftover — and is disqualified.

The Mishnah specifies that the two *todah* loaves used as the signal were *pesulot* — disqualified. The Gemara investigates why. Rabbi Chanina explains: *mitoch she-hayu merubos nifsalos be-linah* — because so many people brought *korban todah* at the time of the *regel*, there was far more bread than could be eaten within the allotted time period. A *todah* must be consumed within a day and a night; bread left over beyond that time becomes *linah* — leftover — and is disqualified. The congregations of pilgrims who brought their *todah* offerings typically did so on the thirteenth, and the sheer volume of forty-loaf sets accompanying each offering meant that inevitably some bread went uneaten and became *pesul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן: אֵין מְבִיאִים תּוֹדָה בַּחַג הַמַּצּוֹת מִפְּנֵי חָמֵץ שֶׁבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara cites a *baraita* supporting the timing: One does not bring a *korban todah* on Pesach itself because the offering involves leavened loaves.**

The Gemara cites a *baraita* supporting the timing: *ein mevi'im todah be-Chag HaMatzos mipnei chametz she-ba* — one does not bring a *korban todah* on Pesach itself because the offering involves leavened loaves. The Gemara notes this seems obvious, but Rav Adda bar Ahava clarifies: the *baraita* addresses the fourteenth of Nisan specifically, where one cannot bring a *todah* because even though the bread is still edible at the time of offering, it will become prohibited before the *korban* can be completed and consumed — a principle of *ein meviim kodshim le-beit ha-psul*, one does not bring sacred offerings into a situation where they will inevitably become disqualified. The *baraita* thus explains why everyone's *todah* obligations accumulated until the thirteenth, at which point the volume of bread overwhelmed the capacity for consumption, generating the *linah* that rendered the loaves *pesulot*.

### Rabbi Yannai Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כְּשֵׁרוֹת הָיוּ! וּמַאי קְרִיאִים להו״ פְּסוּלוֹת״? שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁחַט עֲלֵיהֶן הַזָּבַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai dissents and argues:** The **loaves used as the signal were in fact perfectly *kasher*.** **The reason they are called *pesulot* is** not **because they were inherently disqualified, but rather because the *todah* animal had not yet been slaughtered over them.** Until the *shechitah* is performed and the blood is sprinkled, the loaves, though baked and ready, have not yet been formally sanctified as part of the *todah* offering and therefore cannot be eaten.

Rabbi Yannai dissents and argues: *kesherot hayu* — the loaves used as the signal were in fact perfectly *kasher*. They should not be described as *pesulot* at all. The reason they are called *pesulot* is not because they were inherently disqualified, but rather *she-lo nishchat aleihen ha-zavach* — because the *todah* animal had not yet been slaughtered over them. Until the *shechitah* is performed and the blood is sprinkled, the loaves, though baked and ready, have not yet been formally sanctified as part of the *todah* offering and therefore cannot be eaten. They are called *pesulot* only loosely — they are not yet *kasher* for consumption, not because of any defect but because the process has not been completed.

### Pre-Shechitah Bread

This raises the question of why the loaves were placed on the platform in an incomplete state rather than being used after the offering was completed. The Gemara suggests they were placed there because they served as a public signal, not as part of ordinary consumption. The bread in this state — before *shechitah* — was understood by everyone to be inedible, and its presence and removal served the signaling function without creating any halachic confusion.

## Bread Sanctification

### Rava Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמִי לָא יָכִיל לְמֵימַר לִי נִידַּר חַד עִם חַד, נִלְנוּ — אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא יָכִיל, דְּכִדְאַת בָּר יוֹנָה: לֶחֶם גָּרוֹמָא תּוֹדָה. אַבֵּד הַלֶּחֶם — מְבִיא לֶחֶם אַחֵר. אַבְּדָה תּוֹדָה — אֵין מְבִיא תּוֹדָה אַחֵר, דְּלֶחֶם גָּרוּם תּוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a question** about the *kasher*-but-incomplete **loaves:** If the *todah* animal is subsequently lost and the *shechitah* can no longer be performed on it, can the bread be redeemed? **The Gemara works through the implications:** If one **explicitly designated** the loaves with the animal, **then** the loaves are **linked** to that specific animal. However, **Rava rules:** If the **bread is lost, one may bake new bread.** But **if the *todah* animal itself is lost, one may not bring a different *todah* animal** to complete the offering with the original loaves. The reasoning: **The bread exists on account of the *todah*,** the offering is primary and the bread is its accessory, not the reverse.

The Gemara then asks: why not simply redeem the bread and free it from its *kodesh* status, making it *chullin*? This would resolve the problem of bread that can neither be used nor consumed. The Gemara sets that possibility aside and redirects the discussion.

### Two Acts Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנִינַן: שְׁתֵּי הַלְּחֶם שׁוֹחֲטִין עֲלֵיהֶן שְׁנֵי כְּבָשִׁים, שְׁחַטוּם לִשְׁמָן וּזְרוֹק דָּמָן לִשְׁמָן — קִדַּשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחַטוּם שֶׁלּא לִשְׁמָן וּזְרוֹק דָּמָן שֶׁלּא לִשְׁמָן — לֹא קִדַּשׁ הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara establishes that there are two required acts:** *shechitah* and *zerika* — **slaughter and sprinkling of blood.** A **baraita addresses this** in the context of the **shtei ha-lechem** brought on Shavuot. The **baraita records:** If **both the *shechitah* and *zerika* were performed with proper intent for the *shtei ha-lechem*, the bread is sanctified. If both acts were performed for the wrong purpose, the bread acquires no sanctification at all.**

The Gemara then pivots to the underlying question of sanctification: what is the mechanism by which bread acquires its *kodesh* status in the context of a *todah* or *shtei ha-lechem*? The Gemara establishes that there are two required acts: *shechitah* and *zerika* — slaughter and sprinkling of blood. The question is whether performing only one of these two acts confers any intermediate status on the bread, or whether the bread is fully in or fully out depending on whether both acts have been completed.

### Shtei Halechem

A *baraita* addresses this in the context of the *shtei ha-lechem* brought on Shavuot. The *shtei ha-lechem* are accompanied by two lambs, and the *shechitah* of those lambs is what initiates the sanctification of the loaves. The *baraita* records: *shachtam lishman v'zarak daman lishman kidash ha-lechem* — if both the *shechitah* and *zerika* were performed with proper intent for the *shtei ha-lechem*, the bread is sanctified. *Shachtam shelo lishman v'zarak daman shelo lishman lo kidash ha-lechem* — if both acts were performed for the wrong purpose, the bread acquires no sanctification at all.

### Intermediate Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחַטוּם לִשְׁמָן וּזְרוֹק דָּמָן שֶׁלּא לִשְׁמָן — לֶחֶם קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כְּדַרְכוֹ דְּרַבִּי. וּרַבִּי אֱלְעָזָר בַּר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ הַלֶּחֶם עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁחַט לִשְׁמוֹ וְיִזְרֹק דָּמוֹ לִשְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The more complex case:** If the *shechitah* was done properly but the *zerika* was done for the wrong purpose, **the bread is in a suspended state:** It **has acquired some sanctity through the *shechitah*, yet it is not fully sanctified and cannot be eaten.** **This is the opinion of *Rebbi* — that the two acts together confer full sanctification, each act independently confers partial status.** **Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon disagrees:** The **bread is simply not sanctified unless both acts are performed correctly. There is no intermediate state;** the bread is either **fully sanctified or it has no special status at all.**

The more complex case: *shachtam lishman v'zarak daman shelo lishman* — the *shechitah* was done properly but the *zerika* was done for the wrong purpose. The ruling is *lechem kadosh v'eino kadosh* — the bread is in a suspended state: it has acquired some sanctity through the *shechitah*, yet it is not fully sanctified and cannot be eaten. This is the opinion of *Rebbi* — that the two acts together confer full sanctification, each act independently confers partial status, and the combination of a valid *shechitah* with an invalid *zerika* produces this intermediate condition.

Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *l'olam eino kadosh ha-lechem ad she-yishchat lishman v'yizrok daman lishman* — the bread is simply not sanctified unless both acts are performed correctly. There is no intermediate state; the bread is either fully sanctified or it has no special status at all. For Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon, the system is all-or-nothing.

### Spilled Blood Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי גַּדְּלִי הַדָּם לֶאֱמֹר דְּמִי — הָכָא קָחֵיתִי מִטִּיפוּלִין הַצּוֹמוּק — דְּלִי הַלֶּחֶם מִן הַקְּדוֹשִׁים? הָכִי תִּשְׁמַע — קׁאל הַקֹּדֶם שִׁלּוּחְמַת כִּבְרִיכָא לָהּ הַתְּרוּמָה. כְּעַד שׁוּ״מ הַקָּדוֹשׁים וּבִמְשַׁמַּן שָׁכוּן טוֹם וּמִמִּנַּיּוּם — פִּי הַתְּרוּמָה. וּמִן הַקֹּדֶם מִטְּמַן לִרְיֵי כִּנְסַת — טוֹם, אָבָל אַף דְּרַבִּי אֱלְעָזָר בַּר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַיְינוּ לֵא הַלֶּחֶם שִׁלְשׁוּם צִקּוֹן דַּם שׁוּם לַקִּדּוּשׁ בִּמְשַׁמַּן — וִיתָּה הַקִּדּוּשׁ אִיסּוּם דַּם שֶׁרַעִׁים לְהַקִּדּוּשׁ הַעַצְמִי לְבִנְיָן צִקּוֹן בַּקְּדוֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara resolves how to apply these positions** to the case at hand — *todah* bread placed on the platform where the *shechitah* has been done but the blood has spilled before *zerika*. **Even according to Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon, one might argue that the bread here does have a degree of sanctification, because whatever stands ready to be sprinkled is considered as if already sprinkled.** Once the blood was collected in the bowl and the *zerika* was imminent, that moment of readiness was legally equivalent to the *zerika* itself. The subsequent spilling is therefore a retroactive undoing of what had already been accomplished in legal terms. On this reading, even Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon would agree that such bread holds an intermediate status — at the moment it was placed on the platform, it was in a condition that was the equivalent of post-*zerika* — and thus the *baraita*'s description of the bread as occupying a distinct status is consistent across opinions.

## Signal Systems

### Loaf Signal Restatement

Returning to the Mishnah's signal system, the Gemara recapitulates: two *todah* loaves — whether *pesulot* through *linah* or in an intermediate state through incomplete *shechitah* and *zerika* — were placed on the elevated roof of the *itztaba* on the *Har Habayit*. Their placement, removal of one, and removal of both provided the three-stage public signal for *erev Pesach* chametz disposal.

### Cow Plowing Signal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: שְׁתֵּי פָּרוֹת הָיוּ חוֹרְשׁוֹת בְּהַר הַמִּשְׁחָה, וּהִיא הַר הַזֵּיתִים. כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁשְׁתֵּיהֶן חוֹרְשׁוֹת — כׇּל הָעָם אוֹכְלִים. נִשְׁמַרְתָּ אַחַת — תּוֹלִים. נִשְׁמַרְתָּא שְׁתֵּיהֶן — הִשְׁחִילוּ הַכֹּל וְשׂוֹרְפִים.
>
> **Translation:** A **baraita records that Abba Shaul describes an alternative signal system:** There **were two cows plowing on *Har ha-Mishcha*,** which is identified with *Har ha-Zeitim*, the Mount of Olives. **The same three stages applied:** As **long as both were plowing, eating was permitted.** When **one cow was removed, it signaled *tolein*.** When **both were removed, everyone began burning.**

A *baraita* records that Abba Shaul describes an alternative signal system: *shtei parot hayu chorshos be-Har ha-Mishcha* — there were two cows plowing on *Har ha-Mishcha*, which is identified with *Har ha-Zeitim*, the Mount of Olives. The same three stages applied: *kol zman she-shteihen chorshos kol ha-am ochlin* — as long as both were plowing, eating was permitted. When one cow was removed, it signaled *tolein*. When both were removed, everyone began burning.

The logic of visibility is appealing here: *Har ha-Zeitim* is visible from much of Jerusalem and from the *Beit Hamikdash*, and two large animals plowing in a field would be observable from a considerable distance — arguably more broadly than a loaf of bread on a platform.

### Cow Signal Problems

This signal, however, raises its own difficulties. Identifying from a distance which cows are *the* signal cows — as opposed to any number of other cows that might be plowing in fields around the city — presents a real problem of visibility and recognition. One would need to know exactly where on *Har ha-Mishcha* to look, and distinguish these particular cows from any others. Moreover — and this is a point worth noting on its own — cows do not plow; oxen do. The Mishnah's term *parot* (cows) is technically imprecise, and one might have expected the text to read *parim*. Whether this reflects a loose usage of terms or some other consideration, the Gemara does not address directly here.
