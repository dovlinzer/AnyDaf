# Gittin 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Co-Wife Status Dilemma

### Invalid Co-Wife Setup

The opening sugya of today's *daf* continues the *Mishnah*'s discussion of cases in which a woman suffers severe consequences — *teitzei mizeh umizeh*, *uvalad mamzer*, and all the attendant prohibitions — despite being entirely innocent. Having treated the case of an incorrectly written *get* and the case of mistaken names or locations, the *Mishnah* now addresses a situation arising from *yibbum* law. A woman was waiting for either *yibbum* or *chalitzah* from her brother-in-law, but the obligation was in fact void because her co-wife turned out to be an *erva* — a woman forbidden to the brother-in-law — meaning there was never a genuine bond of co-wife status to begin with. Under that misapprehension, however, the waiting woman went ahead and remarried. When the truth emerged, all of the severe consequences applied.

### Rav Hamnuna's Rule

The *Gemara* notes that the *Mishnah* specifies *nisu'in* — actual marriage — as the triggering act, but says nothing about a case where the woman merely had sexual relations with another man (*zinu*). This omission prompts the *Gemara* to ask whether this silence constitutes a challenge to Rav Hamnuna, who rules that a *shomeret yavam* — a woman awaiting *yibbum* — who commits a sexual act with another man is thereby forbidden to the *yavam*. If sexual relations with another man render her forbidden to the brother-in-law, then that act should carry the same severity as remarriage, and the same catalogue of negative consequences ought to follow.

### Consequence Discrepancy

The *Gemara* thus frames the discrepancy sharply: if sexual relations suffice to create the forbidden status described by Rav Hamnuna, why does the *Mishnah* restrict the consequences to the case of *nisu'in*? The implication seems to be that the *Mishnah* holds sexual relations short of marriage do not trigger these consequences — which would undercut Rav Hamnuna's position.

### Clean Language Explanati…

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking the principle of *lishna ma'alya* — clean or elevated language. The *Mishnah* chose to phrase the case in terms of *nisu'in* out of linguistic propriety. The actual law, however, is identical whether she married another man or merely had sexual relations with him; in either case, *vehu hadin lezinus*, the same negative consequences apply. The *Mishnah*'s use of the term "married" is not *davka* — it is simply the more refined way to express the matter.

### Alternate Version

An alternative version of this teaching frames the relationship between *nisu'in* and *zinus* in reverse: since the *Mishnah* says *nisu'in*, and *nisu'in* presumably also implies sexual relations, this version reads the *Mishnah* as supporting Rav Hamnuna — *leima mesayeya leih l'Rav Hamnuna* — since even sexual relations short of formal marriage would generate these consequences.

The *Gemara* then pushes back on this alternative reading and insists that the *Mishnah* should be read *davka* — only *nisu'in* triggers the consequences, not sexual relations alone. The reason is that the paradigm case underlying all these rulings is the scenario of a woman who heard her husband had died, remarried, and then the husband reappeared — *mishum demichalfa b'eishes ish shehalach ba'alah l'medinat hayam*. That model case involves remarriage, not mere sexual relations, and therefore the *Mishnah* deliberately frames matters in terms of the woman having taken a second husband.

## Invalid Yibum Case

### Yibum with Invalid

The *Mishnah* then presents a companion case: a man actually performed *yibbum* with one of his late brother's wives, which led the other wife to conclude she was free from any further obligation and entitled to remarry. It subsequently emerged that the wife with whom *yibbum* was performed was not in fact obligated — she was, for example, an *aylonit* — rendering the *yibbum* retroactively invalid. Nevertheless, the other wife who had remarried is subject to all the same severe consequences.

### Two Cases Necessity

The *Gemara* explains why both cases — the *erva* co-wife and the invalid *yibbum* — are necessary and cannot be collapsed into one ruling. With respect to the first case alone, one might have reasoned that because no act of *yibbum* or *chalitzah* was ever performed, the woman bore some residual responsibility to investigate before simply walking away from the situation. In the second case, by contrast, an act of *yibbum* was actually carried out — *dikayim mitzvas yibbum* — and one might have said that since everyone did everything correctly, the other wife should be entirely free of negative consequences. Conversely, had only the second case been stated, one might have concluded that because the situation did carry a genuine obligation of *yibbum* from the outset, people who participated in it are held to a higher standard even when they acted in good faith. Hence *tzricha* — both cases are required.

### Innocent Yet Punished

This leaves standing what the *Gemara* itself acknowledges is a deeply troubling reality: these women are entirely innocent, yet they bear the full weight of consequences as severe as any deliberate transgression. The *Gemara* offers no fully satisfying resolution to this basic moral difficulty, and it is important not to paper over that discomfort.

## Scribe's Document Mix-Up

### Mixed Documents Setup

The *Mishnah* turns to a scenario of scribal error: a scribe was asked to write a receipt (*shover*) for a man and a *get* for a woman, and he accidentally reversed the documents, giving the receipt to the woman and the *get* to the man. Since the woman received a receipt rather than a *get*, she is not divorced — *lo megureshet*. Rabbi Elazar, however, introduces a temporal qualification: the problem with the incorrect documents can be raised only *l'alter* — immediately — but not after a period of time has passed.

### Immediacy Requirement

Rabbi Elazar's ruling generates an obvious question: what constitutes *l'alter* and what constitutes *l'achar zman*?

### L'Alter Definition

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel defines *l'alter* as the period during which the parties are still sitting and engaged in the same matter — *kol zman sheyoshvin v'oskin b'oto inyan*. As long as the parties remain present and the conversation has not concluded, discovering the error falls within *l'alter* and the problem can be raised.

### After Meeting Breaks

Once the meeting breaks — *amdu* — any moment thereafter constitutes *l'achar zman*. From that point forward, we cannot know with certainty what transpired between then and the time the document is inspected, and the first husband therefore loses the ability to challenge the divorce. This definition effectively limits the window of challenge to the duration of the transaction itself, a remarkably narrow timeframe.

### Alternative: No Remarria…

Rav Adda bar Ahava offers a substantially different definition. *L'alter* extends for as long as the woman has not yet remarried — *lo niset zehu l'alter*. Under this reading, even ten years later, as long as she has not taken a second husband, the problem can still be raised. Once she marries a second man, *niset zehu l'achar zman*, that constitutes the temporal threshold beyond which the first husband can no longer upend her new marriage.

### Right to Remarry

The *Gemara* notes that the *Mishnah* specifically mentions a second husband — *lo kol heimenu min harishon l'aved zchuto shel sheni* — the first husband cannot act to destroy the rights of the second. This language fits naturally with Rav Adda bar Ahava's view, since it is the existence of the second marriage that blocks the challenge. The *Gemara* then asks how Shmuel's view accounts for the *Mishnah*'s mention of a second party. Shmuel answers: *zchut harauya l'sheni* — once she leaves the meeting, she has acquired the right to remarry. The *Mishnah*'s reference to the second husband is not about a second marriage already having occurred, but about the right that crystallizes the moment the meeting concludes. Once she walks out the door with the right to remarry, the first husband cannot retroactively vitiate that right on the basis of a document discrepancy.

## Get Written Then Retract…

### Beit Shammai Stringent

The *Mishnah* now presents the case of a man who instructed a scribe to write a *get* for his wife and then changed his mind — *v'nimlach* — and the divorce never took place. Beit Shammai rules: *pasla min hakehuna* — she is disqualified from marrying a *kohen* even though no *get* was ever given and the marriage remained intact.

### Rumor Problem

The reasoning is striking and worth examining carefully. The moment a man approaches a scribe and commissions a *get*, word begins to circulate — *yatza alav kol* — a rumor spreads that the husband is divorcing his wife. Even if the *get* is ultimately never delivered, even if the couple continues living together, the perception that a divorce occurred or was in process has been created. Beit Shammai holds that once this *rei'ach* — this whiff — of divorce has been generated, we must take it seriously: if the husband subsequently dies and the woman seeks to remarry a *kohen*, people who heard the rumor will assume she is a *gerusha* and conclude that the children of that marriage are *chalalim*. Because the perception cannot be reliably dispelled, Beit Shammai rules preemptively: do not allow her to marry a *kohen* in the first place.

The difficulty with this position is obvious, and the *Gemara* acknowledges it. The woman has done nothing wrong. Her husband wrote something and changed his mind. Yet Beit Shammai would impose a lifelong restriction on who she may marry based solely on a rumor generated by her husband's momentary intention. A student correctly notes that this concern would make much more sense if the *get* had been written on the husband's deathbed, where there would be little time for the couple's continued cohabitation to publicly dispel the rumor. If they lived together openly for years afterward, it is hard to understand how anyone would seriously believe she had been divorced.

### Beit Hillel Lenient

Beit Hillel takes the opposing position, framed as an even stronger case: *af al pi she-nasnu lah tnai v'lo na'asa hatnai, lo pasla min hakehuna* — even if he actually handed her a conditional *get* and the condition was not fulfilled, she is not disqualified from marrying a *kohen*. If the condition goes unsatisfied and she is therefore not actually divorced, there is no divorce and the law treats her accordingly. Beit Hillel refuses to impose legal consequences on the basis of a perceived status that does not correspond to reality. If she is not divorced, she is not divorced — and no rumor or perception changes that.

It is worth noting the concept of *rei'ach hagget* — the whiff of a *get* — which does exist in the *Gemara* in other contexts. There are cases where a *get pasul*, a *get* that was invalid but close enough to a valid *get*, does render a woman *pesulah min hakehunah*. This makes Beit Hillel's position all the more striking: Beit Hillel's principle is that if a *get* was not actually given and did not actually effectuate a divorce, no consequence follows.

## Dispelling Get Rumor

### Kohen Get Rumor Query

A question was sent from Rav Yosef son of Rav Menashya of Dovila to Shmuel: a rumor had spread in town that a certain *kohen* had written a *get* for his wife — *yatza alav kol ish ploni kohein kasav get l'ishto* — and yet the couple was still living together, *yosheves tachtav umeshamashto*. What is the law?

The case is more complex than it might appear. If the *kohen* did in fact give the *get*, he is now living with his divorced wife, which would constitute a serious prohibition for a *kohen* — *machzir grushato*. The question concerns how to respond to this rumor in the absence of established facts.

### Investigation Needed

Shmuel's response is: *teitzei v'hadavar tzarich bdika* — she should leave the marriage and the matter requires investigation. The *Gemara* notes that in other contexts, Shmuel used the phrase *hadavar tzarich talmud* — the matter requires further *halachic* analysis. Here, the matter requires *bdika* — factual investigation — since the question is not what the law is, but what actually occurred.

### Dispel Rumor Strategy

The *Gemara* then addresses what kind of investigation *bdika* refers to. Is the question whether, when a rumor exists, we attempt to *mevatel* the rumor — to publicly dispel it — or whether we accept the rumor and act accordingly?

There is a genuine policy question embedded here. If we know the rumor is false and we publicly declare so, we might successfully neutralize it. Alternatively, attempting to fight the rumor might backfire: people who believe the rumor strongly will suspect that the *beit din* has been co-opted — that the husband has the rabbis in his pocket — and the attempt to dispel the rumor will only entrench the perception further. The question is therefore not merely factual but strategic: is it ever effective to fight perception with official declarations?

### Naharda'a Doesn't Dispel

The *Gemara* notes that *Naharda'a* — Shmuel's own city, the locus of his *halachic* authority — does not attempt to be *mevatel* rumors of this kind. If even in Shmuel's home community the policy is not to dispel such rumors, what does Shmuel have to say to the questioner? The practical import is that if one does not dispel rumors, one must act on them — which in this case means imposing some consequence on the couple.

### Language Ambiguity

Before reaching that conclusion, the *Gemara* interrogates the language of the rumor itself. When people say a man "wrote a *get*" for his wife, does that necessarily mean he *gave* the *get*? It is entirely possible that *kasav* — "wrote" — means only that: he composed a document, not that he delivered it. If the rumor is only that he wrote a *get* and not that he gave one, there may be no rumor of actual divorce at all, and consequently no allegation that he is living with a *gerusha*.

### Double Uncertainty

Even granting that in common usage "wrote a *get*" might sometimes mean "gave a *get*" — *de-migalya milta de-karu li-nisuei ketiva* — this only means the rumor *might* refer to delivery. The result is a *safek* within a *safek*: there is a rumor, and the rumor might mean he gave the *get*, but it might not. This double uncertainty would seem to substantially weaken any grounds for imposing consequences.

### Rav Ashi Principle

At this point Rav Ashi's principle enters the discussion: *kol kala devatar nisuin lo chaishinan leih* — we do not give halachic weight to rumors that arise after a marriage has already taken place. This is a significant and welcome clarification. The concern about rumors — *kol* — is a *lechatchila* concern, a prospective one. We say: do not enter into a marriage if there is a credible rumor of prior divorce. But we do not use a rumor to undo a marriage that has already been contracted. The implications for Beit Shammai's ruling in the *Mishnah* are directly relevant: the perception created when a man commissions a *get* might lead us to restrict whom the woman may marry *going forward*, but it does not require dissolving any existing relationship.

### Second Marriage Bar

The *Gemara* thus clarifies what *teitzei* means in Shmuel's response: it does not mean she must be divorced from her current husband. Rather, *teitzei mi-sheni* — if the first husband were to die and she sought to remarry, she would not be permitted to marry a second husband, specifically a *kohen*. The consequence is prospective and limited, not retroactive and destructive.

## Perception Creates Reali…

### Rabbis Shape Perception

The *Gemara* then raises a striking counter-argument. If the consequence is only that she may not marry a second man — a *kohen* — while remaining with her first husband, there is a paradox: people will observe that the rabbis are permitting her to stay with her first husband while barring her from remarrying after his death. That combination of rulings will itself generate a perception. People will not say "the rumor was false." They will construct a narrative to explain the rabbis' behavior.

### Samuch L'Misa Logic

Specifically, the *Gemara* says people will say *samuch l'misa gershah* — he must have divorced her just before he died. People will reason: the rabbis would not bar her from remarrying unless there was something to the rumor; and since she lived with her husband until his death, the divorce must have occurred right at the end. The ruling itself thus generates a new and more damaging perception than the original rumor.

This is a genuinely unsettling passage. The *Gemara* is acknowledging that the rabbis' response to perception can itself shape perception — and yet rather than concluding that we should therefore impose no consequence at all (which would send the clearest message that the rumor was false), the *Gemara* proceeds to analyze which consequence is tolerable. The answer is: the limited consequence of barring remarriage to a *kohen* is tolerable because people who observe it will assume *samuch l'misa gershah* — and in that case they will understand that she was with her first husband until the end, which at least does not impugn the children of that marriage as *chalalim*.

This is, as acknowledged, a deeply difficult passage. The *Gemara* openly grapples with the fact that rabbinic rulings create as well as respond to social perception, and yet does not reach a fully satisfying resolution of how this interplay should be managed.

### Yeridat Hadorot

The *Gemara* introduces a teaching attributed to Rabbi Ami bar Chama citing Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Ilai: come and see that the later generations are not like the earlier generations — *bo r'eh shelo kedoros rishonim doros ha'achronim*. The contrast drawn is between Beit Shammai as representative of the *doros rishonim* and Rabbi Dosa as representative of the *doros achronim*.

This is a *yeridat hadorot* type of observation — a romanticization of the stringency and purity of earlier generations relative to later ones. But what is notable is that the particular example chosen complicates any simple evaluation of who was "better."

### Rabbi Dosa Example

The *baraita* records that a woman taken captive — *shvuya* — who was married to a *kohen* may, according to Rabbi Dosa, continue eating *terumah* upon her return. We do not presume she was raped. Rabbi Dosa's reasoning is direct and almost startling in its humanity: *v'chi mah asah lah aravi zeh? b'shvil shemeichah bein dadeiha — tifsel m'unakehunah?* What did this Arab do to her? Because he may have fondled her — should she be disqualified from *kehunah*? Rabbi Dosa refuses to presume rape even in conditions where it was entirely possible, in order to protect the woman's status and her marriage.

The contrast with Beit Shammai is therefore not straightforward. Beit Shammai, the *doros rishonim*, imposed restrictions on a completely innocent woman based on the faintest whiff of a rumor. Rabbi Dosa, the *doros achronim*, refused to strip a woman of her status based on a presumption of violation she may never have suffered. In this framing, the "later" generation actually shows greater sensitivity to human dignity and real consequences.

The *Gemara* cites a second contrast — that the *doros rishonim* would bring their produce through the main gate in order to be *chayiv* in *maaser*, while the *doros achronim* would bring it in through rooftops and courtyards to avoid the obligation. This is a less ambiguous case of decline. But the contrast with the *shvuya* case is worth sitting with: sometimes what looks like a relaxation of standards is actually a greater moral clarity. It is not that we think the *doros achronim* are doing wrong; it is, as framed here, more of a sense of loss — are we losing some dimension of *kedusha* and *tahara* even as we do the right thing by human beings? That tension is what the *Gemara* appears to be naming.

## Overnight Together Probl…

### Overnight Inn Scenario

The *Mishnah* now presents the case of a man who divorces his wife and then spends the night with her in an inn — *hamigaresh es ishto v'lan imah b'pundaki*.

### Beit Shammai No Second

Beit Shammai rules: she does not need a second *get* from him — *eina tzricha heimenu get sheni*. We do not presume from the fact that they spent the night together that they had sexual relations and thereby entered into a new marriage.

### Beit Hillel Requires Sec…

Beit Hillel rules more stringently here: she does require a second *get*, but only *bizman shenitgarsha min hanisu'in* — when the divorce follows a full marriage (*nisu'in*) in which the couple had actually lived together and been intimate.

### Eirusin Agreement

Both schools agree, however, that if the divorce followed only *eirusin* — betrothal, prior to cohabitation — no second *get* is required: *mipnei she'ein libo gas bah*. He is not sufficiently at ease with her — having never yet lived with her as a wife — to make it plausible that after granting her a *get* he would have sex with her the same night, let alone with the intent of remarriage.

This formulation makes clear that the *Mishnah*'s debate turns on whether we presume sexual relations occurred, since the presence of *nisu'in* versus *eirusin* is relevant only to the probability of sex, not to the legal significance of sex if we already know it happened.

It is worth noting the *Yerushalmi*'s insight cited here: this debate may be connected to the famous *Mishnah* at the end of *Gittin* (*daf* 90), where Beit Shammai rules that a man may not divorce his wife unless she has committed a sexual transgression — *ervas davar* — while Beit Hillel permits divorce even if she merely burnt his food. If Beit Shammai believes divorce only occurs because of a serious transgression, it follows naturally that Beit Shammai would not assume the divorced couple desired to reunite sexually. Beit Hillel, who permits divorce for minor reasons, must account for the possibility that the divorce was over something trivial and the couple, having spent a night together, may have reconciled.

## Edei Yichud vs. Edei Biah

### Sex Witnessed Version

Rabbah bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan offers a different version of the debate. He says the *machloket* between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel arises specifically in a case where witnesses actually observed the couple having sexual relations — *beshero'uha shenivala*.

### Beit Shammai Zenut

On this reading, Beit Shammai holds: *adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* — a person may engage in a sexual act that is not intended to constitute marriage. The sexual act itself does not imply matrimonial intent, and therefore we do not retroactively treat the couple as having remarried.

### Beit Hillel No Zenut

Beit Hillel holds: *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* — we presume that when a man has sexual relations, particularly with a woman who was formerly his wife, he intends it as an act of marriage, not mere fornication. The couple should therefore be treated as having remarried, and a second *get* is required.

### Rashi Clarification

Rashi clarifies an important limitation: this presumption — *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* — applies specifically to a woman who was previously his wife, *nashim shenitgarsha min hanisu'in*. In that case, the prior marital relationship provides the context in which we interpret the sexual act as matrimonial. The principle does not mean that any two people observed having sexual relations are thereby presumed married. Without the prior marital context, there is no basis for such a presumption.

### Seclusion Witnesses

The *Gemara* then recasts the *Mishnah* itself. If the debate is about a case where witnesses actually saw them have sexual relations, why does the *Mishnah* distinguish between *nisu'in* and *eirusin*? If we witnessed the act, the only question is its legal meaning — not whether it occurred. The *Mishnah*'s distinction based on whether they had a prior *nisu'in* relationship (*libo gas bah*) makes sense only if the question is whether sex occurred at all, not what the sex means. The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ella matnitin beshelo ro'uha shenivala* — the *Mishnah* must be dealing with a case where witnesses did not directly observe sexual relations. Rather, there were *edei yichud* — witnesses to seclusion — who saw the couple enter a private space together. The debate is whether seclusion witnesses are treated as tantamount to witnesses to sexual relations.

### Shammai Seclusion Only

Beit Shammai holds: *lo amrinan hein hein edei yichud hein hein edei biah* — we do not say that the witnesses to seclusion are the same as witnesses to sexual relations. Seclusion witnesses testify only to what they saw: the couple entered a private space. They cannot be treated as having implicitly testified to sex, and therefore no presumption of remarriage arises.

### Hillel Seclusion = Sex

Beit Hillel holds: *amrinan hein hein edei yichud hein hein edei biah* — witnesses to seclusion are implicitly witnesses to sex. Since we presume that a man does not intend a sexual act with his former wife as mere fornication, and since seclusion is treated as equivalent to the act itself, the couple is presumed to have remarried and a second *get* is required.

### Eirusin Exception

Even according to Beit Hillel, however, when the prior divorce followed only *eirusin* and not *nisu'in*, we do not make this presumption — *ein libo gas bah*, he is not sufficiently comfortable with her. Without the intimacy of prior cohabitation, even Beit Hillel does not extend the *edei yichud* presumption to cover the act of sexual relations.

The *Gemara* closes this section by noting a tension in Rabbi Yochanan's positions: elsewhere Rabbi Yochanan is reported to hold that the *halacha* follows a *stam Mishnah* — an unattributed *Mishnah*. But here Rabbi Yochanan's formulation aligns with the *baraita*'s version of the debate (witnesses saw sex), not the *Mishnah*'s version (witnesses saw only seclusion). The *Gemara* resolves this by saying these represent two *amoraim* debating what Rabbi Yochanan's view actually was: the *amora* who reports Rabbi Yochanan's opinion in line with the *baraita* does not accept the general principle that Rabbi Yochanan always follows the *stam Mishnah*.

## Get Kereiach Folded

### Accordion Structure

The final *Mishnah* of today's *daf* introduces the *get kereiach* — the "bald" or "folded" *get* developed specifically to slow down *kohanim* who might impulsively divorce their wives. Since a *kohen* may not remarry a divorced woman — even his own wife — the rabbis wanted to make the act of writing a *get* for a *kohen* significantly more laborious than for an ordinary Israelite.

The mechanism they devised was an accordion-style document. A sheet of parchment was written upon for a few lines, then folded over, and the fold was tied shut with a knot. A witness then signed on the back of that folded section. The process was repeated: more text was written, another fold was made, another knot was tied, another signature was affixed. The result was a document with multiple sealed sections, each bearing a witness signature on its exterior. The minimum number of such folds — and signatures — required by the rabbis for a *get kereiach* was three, as opposed to the two witnesses required for an ordinary *get*.

If a *kohen* chose to add more folds — say, ten — then ten sections needed to be signed. A *get kereiach* is defined as one in which the number of folds (*kesharav*) exceeds the number of signatures (*edav*) — *kol shekesharav merubim me'adav*. A fold left blank — unsigned — renders the *get* defective and treated as *pasul*, with all the attendant consequences.

### Ben Nanas Position

Ben Nanas rules that when there is a blank section — a fold without a signature — beyond the minimum three, *psulim* can fill in the blank: *hakol mashlimin alav*. Even witnesses who are disqualified for normal testimony may sign the surplus sections, and the *get* is nevertheless valid. The reasoning is that these extra signatures are not functioning as genuine *eidus* — testimony — but merely as a formal fulfillment of the requirement that no fold be left unsigned.

### Rabbi Akiva Restrictions

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He rules: *ein mashlimin alav ela kerovim hara'uyim leha'id bemakom acher* — only those who would be valid witnesses in other contexts may fill in the blank sections, specifically relatives (*kerovim*). Relatives are disqualified in cases involving their own family members, but in other contexts they are perfectly valid witnesses. Slaves (*avadim*) and thieves (*gazlanim*) may not sign, even for surplus sections.

### Kereiach Definition

The definition of *get kereiach* flows directly from this structure: it is any *get* in which the number of folds exceeds the number of signatures. The name *kereiach* — "bald" — evokes the image of a fold that has been left bare, without its requisite signature.

### Kulchem Fear

Why is a blank fold such a serious problem that it renders the entire *get* invalid and triggers all the negative consequences? The *Gemara* answers: *gezeira mishum kulchem* — a rabbinic decree arising from the concern that "everyone sign." The reasoning is as follows: if a man creates a *get* with ten folds, the creation of ten fold-sections implies that ten witnesses were originally designated to sign. If only three signatures appear and seven folds are blank, an observer might conclude that seven witnesses who were supposed to sign did not — and the *get* is therefore defective for having failed to gather all its designated witnesses. To eliminate this concern entirely, every fold must bear a signature. Leaving any fold blank invites the misperception that a witness who was supposed to sign failed to do so.

Interestingly, the fact that the *Gemara* permits *psulim* to sign the surplus folds demonstrates that the concern is not that the *get* will be seen as requiring more *kosher* witnesses. The worry is purely one of perception — that blank spaces will look like an incomplete process. Even an invalid signature fills the visual gap and eliminates the *gezeira*'s concern.

### Slave Freed Misconception

The *Gemara* then asks why Rabbi Akiva excludes a slave (*eved*) from signing even surplus sections. The initial suggestion is that people will say: "Look, a slave signed — slaves must be valid for *eidus*!" But this reasoning would equally apply to a relative signing, since people might say: "A relative signed — relatives must be valid!" So that cannot be the full explanation.

The *Gemara* therefore reformulates Rabbi Akiva's reasoning for excluding a slave: *dilma asu l'assukei l'yuchsin* — people will not say slaves are generally *kosher* for testimony, since everyone knows they are not. Instead, they will say: "How could a slave sign? He must have been freed!" If people believe the slave was freed, they will treat him as a free man for purposes of *yichus*, potentially allowing him to marry a free woman in contexts where that would be problematic.

### Thief Teshuvah Misconcep…

The analogous concern for a *gazlan* — a thief — is: *asu l'meimar teshuva avad* — people will assume he did *teshuva* and was thereby rehabilitated as a valid witness. This would cause people to trust him and have him sign other documents, leading to a reliance on invalid testimony in consequential halachic matters.

### Relatives Safe

For a *karov* — a relative — neither of these concerns applies. *Karov kulei alma yadei dekarov hu* — everyone knows he is a relative, and everyone knows that relatives are disqualified only in matters involving their own families. No one will draw any anomalous conclusions from seeing a relative's signature. It simply means this is a supplementary fill-in signature from a family member, and the situation is self-explanatory.

### Two Witnesses Dispute

The *Gemara* then cites a *baraita* that extends the analysis: seven folds with six signatures, six with five, five with four, four with three — in each case, the debate between Ben Nanas and Rabbi Akiva applies. As long as there are at least three *kosher* signatures, the question is merely what categories of people may fill in the surplus blanks. *Ad kan machlokes Ben Nanas v'Rabbi Akiva* — up to this point is the debate.

However, if there are three folds and only two witnesses — *shachrech sheloshah v'eideh shenayim* — *divrei hakol ein mashlimim alav ela karov* — everyone agrees that only a relative may sign the missing third position. The logic is that the minimum required for a valid *get kereiach* is three *kosher* signatures; without that minimum, the entire *get* is deficient in a more fundamental way, and the standards for filling the gap are consequently higher.

### Basic Three Challenge

This ruling, however, immediately raises a challenge. Rav Zeira poses it directly to Rabbah bar Shilas, from whom he had received this teaching: *michdi kol sheloshah bi-mekushar k'shenayim bi-pashut dami* — three witnesses on a folded *get* are the equivalent of two witnesses on a plain *get*, since the minimum threshold of valid witnesses is set at three for this document. *Mah hasam karov lo, af hacha nami karov lo* — just as on a plain *get* a relative may not serve as one of the two required witnesses, so too here, when you are down to the basic three required signatures, how can a relative serve as one of them?

Rabbah bar Shilas replied that he had asked the very same question of Rav Hamnuna, who had received the ruling from Rav Adda bar Ahava. Rav Adda's answer was: *hanicha sheloshah bi-mekushar delav de-oraisa* — indeed, the requirement of three signatures on a *get kereiach* is only of rabbinic origin; biblically, two witnesses suffice. But even granting that the three-signature requirement is rabbinic, the question remains: if the rabbis defined three as the minimum for this *get*, and one of those three is a relative, has the rabbinic minimum actually been met?

The *Gemara* then cites a confirming *baraita* and reads a specific case: if a *get kereiach* had a surplus blank filled in by a slave, Ben Nanas says it is *kasher* (at least *bediavad*), while Rabbi Akiva says *ha-vlad mamzer* — the child is a *mamzer* — the most stringent possible consequence. When the *Gemara* reaches the case of three folds with two witnesses, it reports that Rav Yosef reads the text differently: where the tradition says *karov*, Rav Yosef rules *kasher* — meaning that when you are down to the basic three, one cannot rely on a relative at all, and if the third signer was a relative, the *get* would only be treated as valid (*kasher*) if that position is filled by a proper *kosher* witness. Rav Pappa endorses this correction: *tani kasher* — the reading should be *kasher*, not *karov*.

The resolution emerging from this discussion is that for surplus blank sections — beyond the minimum three — the debate between Ben Nanas and Rabbi Akiva governs what categories of invalid signatories may fill in. For the basic three signatures themselves, however, the standards are categorically higher, and the question of whether a relative may serve as one of those three remains a serious difficulty that the *Gemara* is still pressing at the close of the *daf*.
