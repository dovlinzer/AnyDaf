# Gittin 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Co-Wife Status Dilemma

### Invalid Co-Wife Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל עֲרָיוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ כּוּ׳. נִישְּׂאוּ – אֵין, זִינּוּ – לָא;
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that in **all** of those cases in **which they said** that a man who died and left behind a widow who is to the *yavam* one of **those with whom relations are forbidden,** and the rival wives were thought to be permitted to remarry, if it later became clear that the forbidden relation was an *ailonit* and therefore they were in fact forbidden from remarrying, then they must leave the man whom they remarried, and they cannot enter into levirate marriage with the *yavam*, and many other penalties apply to them as well. The Gemara comments: It is possible to deduce from the language used by the mishna that only if **they married** other men, then **yes,** these *halakhot* apply to them. But if the rival wives **engaged in licentious sexual intercourse,** then **no,** these *halakhot* do not apply to them.

The opening sugya of today's *daf* continues the *Mishnah*'s discussion of cases in which a woman suffers severe consequences — *teitzei mizeh umizeh*, *uvalad mamzer*, and all the attendant prohibitions — despite being entirely innocent. Having treated the case of an incorrectly written *get* and the case of mistaken names or locations, the *Mishnah* now addresses a situation arising from *yibbum* law. A woman was waiting for either *yibbum* or *chalitzah* from her brother-in-law, but the obligation was in fact void because her co-wife turned out to be an *erva* — a woman forbidden to the brother-in-law — meaning there was never a genuine bond of co-wife status to begin with. Under that misapprehension, however, the waiting woman went ahead and remarried. When the truth emerged, all of the severe consequences applied.

### Rav Hamnuna's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב הַמְנוּנָא – דְּאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁזִּינְּתָה, אֲסוּרָה לִיבָמָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that this is a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Hamnuna, as Rav Hamnuna says: A widow awaiting her brother-in-law** to perform levirate marriage **who engaged in licentious sexual intercourse** is likened to a married woman who committed adultery, and **she is prohibited from** entering into levirate marriage with **her *yavam*.**

The *Gemara* notes that the *Mishnah* specifies *nisu'in* — actual marriage — as the triggering act, but says nothing about a case where the woman merely had sexual relations with another man (*zinu*). This omission prompts the *Gemara* to ask whether this silence constitutes a challenge to Rav Hamnuna, who rules that a *shomeret yavam* — a woman awaiting *yibbum* — who commits a sexual act with another man is thereby forbidden to the *yavam*. If sexual relations with another man render her forbidden to the brother-in-law, then that act should carry the same severity as remarriage, and the same catalogue of negative consequences ought to follow.

### Consequence Discrepancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; נִישְּׂאוּ – וְהוּא הַדִּין לְזִינּוּ. וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי נִישְּׂאוּ – לִישָּׁנָא מְעַלְּיָא נָקֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** this is not a refutation, since it is possible to explain that the mishna gave the example that **they married, and the same is true in** a case where **they engaged in licentious sexual intercourse. And this that** the mishna **teaches:** If **they married,** is because it **employed a euphemistic expression,** to refrain from discussing a case of licentiousness.

The *Gemara* thus frames the discrepancy sharply: if sexual relations suffice to create the forbidden status described by Rav Hamnuna, why does the *Mishnah* restrict the consequences to the case of *nisu'in*? The implication seems to be that the *Mishnah* holds sexual relations short of marriage do not trigger these consequences — which would undercut Rav Hamnuna's position.

### Clean Language Explanation

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking the principle of *lishna ma'alya* — clean or elevated language. The *Mishnah* chose to phrase the case in terms of *nisu'in* out of linguistic propriety. The actual law, however, is identical whether she married another man or merely had sexual relations with him; in either case, *vehu hadin lezinus*, the same negative consequences apply. The *Mishnah*'s use of the term "married" is not *davka* — it is simply the more refined way to express the matter.

### Alternate Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: נִישְּׂאוּ – וְהוּא הַדִּין לְזִינּוּ;
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that the exchange went as follows: From the mishna's statement about the rival wives that remarried, one can understand that the *halakha* is so if **they married, and the same is true in** a case where **they engaged in licentious sexual intercourse.**

An alternative version of this teaching frames the relationship between *nisu'in* and *zinus* in reverse: since the *Mishnah* says *nisu'in*, and *nisu'in* presumably also implies sexual relations, this version reads the *Mishnah* as supporting Rav Hamnuna — *leima mesayeya leih l'Rav Hamnuna* — since even sexual relations short of formal marriage would generate these consequences.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב הַמְנוּנָא – דְּאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁזִּינְּתָה – אֲסוּרָה לִיבָמָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that it **supports** the opinion of **Rav Hamnuna, as Rav Hamnuna says: A widow awaiting her brother-in-law** to perform levirate marriage **who engaged in licentious sexual intercourse is prohibited from** entering into levirate marriage with **her *yavam*.**

The *Gemara* then pushes back on this alternative reading and insists that the *Mishnah* should be read *davka* — only *nisu'in* triggers the consequences, not sexual relations alone. The reason is that the paradigm case underlying all these rulings is the scenario of a woman who heard her husband had died, remarried, and then the husband reappeared — *mishum demichalfa b'eishes ish shehalach ba'alah l'medinat hayam*. That model case involves remarriage, not mere sexual relations, and therefore the *Mishnah* deliberately frames matters in terms of the woman having taken a second husband.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; נִישְּׂאוּ דַּוְוקָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיחַלְּפָא בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it is **specifically** when **they married** that they are forbidden, **because** she **is confused with a woman whose husband traveled to a country overseas** and she went and remarried. In that case she is certainly prohibited from marrying both the first and the second husband. Similarly, they instituted the same decree for a *yevama* who married someone else. By contrast, in the case of a *yevama* who engaged in licentious sexual intercourse, which is completely different, they did not institute this decree.

## Invalid Yibum Case

### Yibum with Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכּוֹנֵס אֶת יְבִמְתּוֹ כּוּ׳. וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אִיקַּיַּים מִצְוַת יִבּוּם;
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that **one who marries his *yevama*,** and her rival wife went and married another man, and ultimately the *yevama* was found to be an *ailonit*, then the rival wife must leave her husband, and she cannot enter into levirate marriage with the *yavam*, and many other penalties apply to her as well. The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* as well, although it seemingly deals with the same issue as the previous *halakha*. **As, if** the mishna **had taught us** this *halakha* only **with regard to the first** case of a rival wife of a woman who is forbidden to the *yavam*, then one could say that the *halakha* is so, **because the mitzva of levirate marriage was not fulfilled** at all, since the rival wife married someone else, and the *yavam* did not perform levirate marriage.

The *Mishnah* then presents a companion case: a man actually performed *yibbum* with one of his late brother's wives, which led the other wife to conclude she was free from any further obligation and entitled to remarry. It subsequently emerged that the wife with whom *yibbum* was performed was not in fact obligated — she was, for example, an *aylonit* — rendering the *yibbum* retroactively invalid. Nevertheless, the other wife who had remarried is subject to all the same severe consequences.

### Two Cases Necessity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָכָא – דְּאִיקַּיַּים מִצְוַת יִבּוּם, אֵימָא לָא;
>
> **Translation:** **But here,** in this latter case, **where the mitzva of levirate marriage was fulfilled** in some way when he married the *yevama*, although ultimately it became clear that it was not a legitimate levirate marriage, **say** that the rival wives are **not** penalized, since she is not guilty by not having waited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָכָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא רַמְיָא קַמֵּיהּ; אֲבָל הָתָם – דְּלָא רַמְיָא קַמֵּיהּ, אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the mishna **had taught us** this *halakha* **here,** with regard to a *yavam* who married a *yevama* who was ultimately found to be an *ailonit*, then one could say that specifically here there is reason to penalize her, **because** this rival wife who remarried **was** also **placed before** the *yavam*, as he could have entered into levirate marriage with any of his brother's wives. Therefore, she could have waited to see if the levirate marriage was effective before remarrying. **But there,** in the first case of a *yevama* who is forbidden to the *yavam*, **that she is not placed before him,** as all of them are entirely exempt from levirate marriage, **say** that the rival wives are **not** penalized. Therefore, it is **necessary** to state both *halakhot*.

The *Gemara* explains why both cases — the *erva* co-wife and the invalid *yibbum* — are necessary and cannot be collapsed into one ruling. With respect to the first case alone, one might have reasoned that because no act of *yibbum* or *chalitzah* was ever performed, the woman bore some residual responsibility to investigate before simply walking away from the situation. In the second case, by contrast, an act of *yibbum* was actually carried out — *dikayim mitzvas yibbum* — and one might have said that since everyone did everything correctly, the other wife should be entirely free of negative consequences. Conversely, had only the second case been stated, one might have concluded that because the situation did carry a genuine obligation of *yibbum* from the outset, people who participated in it are held to a higher standard even when they acted in good faith. Hence *tzricha* — both cases are required.

### Innocent Yet Punished

This leaves standing what the *Gemara* itself acknowledges is a deeply troubling reality: these women are entirely innocent, yet they bear the full weight of consequences as severe as any deliberate transgression. The *Gemara* offers no fully satisfying resolution to this basic moral difficulty, and it is important not to paper over that discomfort.

## Scribe's Document Mix-Up

### Mixed Documents Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתַב הַסּוֹפֵר וְטָעָה, וְנָתַן גֵּט לָאִשָּׁה וְשׁוֹבָר וְכוּ׳; רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר אִם לְאַלְתַּר יָצָא וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that if **the scribe wrote** a bill of divorce, **and erred and gave** the **bill of divorce to the woman and the receipt** to the man, and consequently the husband gave his wife a receipt and she gave him a bill of divorce, **Rabbi Eliezer says: If** the bill of divorce **is immediately in** the husband's possession, it is not a valid bill of divorce. But if it is in his possession after some time, the assumption is that she was divorced in a correct manner and the bill of divorce was returned to him later.

The *Mishnah* turns to a scenario of scribal error: a scribe was asked to write a receipt (*shover*) for a man and a *get* for a woman, and he accidentally reversed the documents, giving the receipt to the woman and the *get* to the man. Since the woman received a receipt rather than a *get*, she is not divorced — *lo megureshet*. Rabbi Elazar, however, introduces a temporal qualification: the problem with the incorrect documents can be raised only *l'alter* — immediately — but not after a period of time has passed.

### Immediacy Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי לְאַלְתַּר, וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי לְאַחַר זְמַן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין וַעֲסוּקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן – זֶהוּ לְאַלְתַּר; עָמְדוּ – זֶהוּ לְאַחַר זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which the bill of divorce is **immediately** in the husband's hand **and what are the circumstances** in which it is in his possession **after** some **time? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: All** the **while that they are sitting and are engaged in the issue** of the divorce, **this is** considered **immediately.** If **they** already **arose** and concluded the proceedings, **this is** considered **after** some **time.**

Rabbi Elazar's ruling generates an obvious question: what constitutes *l'alter* and what constitutes *l'achar zman*?

### L'Alter Definition

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel defines *l'alter* as the period during which the parties are still sitting and engaged in the same matter — *kol zman sheyoshvin v'oskin b'oto inyan*. As long as the parties remain present and the conversation has not concluded, discovering the error falls within *l'alter* and the problem can be raised.

### After Meeting Breaks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר: לֹא נִישֵּׂאת – זֶהוּ לְאַלְתַּר; נִישֵּׂאת – זֶהוּ לְאַחַר זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** If **she was not married** to someone else, **this is** considered **immediately,** since they can rectify the situation by requiring him to give the bill of divorce properly. If **she was married, this is** considered **after** some **time.**

Once the meeting breaks — *amdu* — any moment thereafter constitutes *l'achar zman*. From that point forward, we cannot know with certainty what transpired between then and the time the document is inspected, and the first husband therefore loses the ability to challenge the divorce. This definition effectively limits the window of challenge to the duration of the transaction itself, a remarkably narrow timeframe.

### Alternative: No Remarriage Bar

Rav Adda bar Ahava offers a substantially different definition. *L'alter* extends for as long as the woman has not yet remarried — *lo niset zehu l'alter*. Under this reading, even ten years later, as long as she has not taken a second husband, the problem can still be raised. Once she marries a second man, *niset zehu l'achar zman*, that constitutes the temporal threshold beyond which the first husband can no longer upend her new marriage.

### Right to Remarry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: לֹא כׇּל הֵימֶנּוּ מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן לְאַבֵּד זְכוּתוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁנִי. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה – הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי ״שֵׁנִי״, אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל – מַאי ״שֵׁנִי״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We learned** in the mishna with regard to Rabbi Elazar's statement: **It is not in the power of the first** husband **to eliminate the right of the second** husband. **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rav Adda bar Ahava, this** explanation **is** consistent with **that which is taught:** The **second** husband, since the mishna is discussing a case in which she remarried and has a second husband. **But according to** the opinion of **Shmuel, what** is the reference to a **second** husband? Shmuel's opinion is that as soon as they arise and conclude the proceedings, it is considered to be after some time, and in this case there is no second husband. According to Shmuel's opinion, how does Rabbi Elazar's statement apply?

The *Gemara* notes that the *Mishnah* specifically mentions a second husband — *lo kol heimenu min harishon l'aved zchuto shel sheni* — the first husband cannot act to destroy the rights of the second. This language fits naturally with Rav Adda bar Ahava's view, since it is the existence of the second marriage that blocks the challenge. The *Gemara* then asks how Shmuel's view accounts for the *Mishnah*'s mention of a second party. Shmuel answers: *zchut harauya l'sheni* — once she leaves the meeting, she has acquired the right to remarry. The *Mishnah*'s reference to the second husband is not about a second marriage already having occurred, but about the right that crystallizes the moment the meeting concludes. Once she walks out the door with the right to remarry, the first husband cannot retroactively vitiate that right on the basis of a document discrepancy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְכוּת הָרְאוּיָה לַשֵּׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is not referring to the right of a second man that she already married, but rather to **the right that is due** to a suitor who wishes to become her **second** husband, meaning that the first husband cannot eliminate the right of a second man who wishes to marry her.

## Get Written Then Retracted

### Beit Shammai Stringent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כָּתַב לְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְנִמְלַךְ; בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנְּתָנוֹ לָהּ עַל תְּנַאי, וְלֹא נַעֲשָׂה הַתְּנַאי – לֹא פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one wrote** a bill of divorce **to divorce his wife, and reconsidered** and did not give it to her, **Beit Shammai say:** Although merely writing the bill of divorce does not dissolve the marriage, by doing so **he disqualified her from** marrying into **the priesthood. And Beit Hillel say: Even if he gave** the bill of divorce **to her conditionally and the condition was not fulfilled,** and therefore the bill of divorce did not take effect, **he did not disqualify her from** marrying into **the priesthood.** A woman is disqualified from marrying into the priesthood only if the divorce takes effect.

The *Mishnah* now presents the case of a man who instructed a scribe to write a *get* for his wife and then changed his mind — *v'nimlach* — and the divorce never took place. Beit Shammai rules: *pasla min hakehuna* — she is disqualified from marrying a *kohen* even though no *get* was ever given and the marriage remained intact.

### Rumor Problem

The reasoning is striking and worth examining carefully. The moment a man approaches a scribe and commissions a *get*, word begins to circulate — *yatza alav kol* — a rumor spreads that the husband is divorcing his wife. Even if the *get* is ultimately never delivered, even if the couple continues living together, the perception that a divorce occurred or was in process has been created. Beit Shammai holds that once this *rei'ach* — this whiff — of divorce has been generated, we must take it seriously: if the husband subsequently dies and the woman seeks to remarry a *kohen*, people who heard the rumor will assume she is a *gerusha* and conclude that the children of that marriage are *chalalim*. Because the perception cannot be reliably dispelled, Beit Shammai rules preemptively: do not allow her to marry a *kohen* in the first place.

The difficulty with this position is obvious, and the *Gemara* acknowledges it. The woman has done nothing wrong. Her husband wrote something and changed his mind. Yet Beit Shammai would impose a lifelong restriction on who she may marry based solely on a rumor generated by her husband's momentary intention. A student correctly notes that this concern would make much more sense if the *get* had been written on the husband's deathbed, where there would be little time for the couple's continued cohabitation to publicly dispel the rumor. If they lived together openly for years afterward, it is hard to understand how anyone would seriously believe she had been divorced.

### Beit Hillel Lenient

Beit Hillel takes the opposing position, framed as an even stronger case: *af al pi she-nasnu lah tnai v'lo na'asa hatnai, lo pasla min hakehuna* — even if he actually handed her a conditional *get* and the condition was not fulfilled, she is not disqualified from marrying a *kohen*. If the condition goes unsatisfied and she is therefore not actually divorced, there is no divorce and the law treats her accordingly. Beit Hillel refuses to impose legal consequences on the basis of a perceived status that does not correspond to reality. If she is not divorced, she is not divorced — and no rumor or perception changes that.

It is worth noting the concept of *rei'ach hagget* — the whiff of a *get* — which does exist in the *Gemara* in other contexts. There are cases where a *get pasul*, a *get* that was invalid but close enough to a valid *get*, does render a woman *pesulah min hakehunah*. This makes Beit Hillel's position all the more striking: Beit Hillel's principle is that if a *get* was not actually given and did not actually effectuate a divorce, no consequence follows.

## Dispelling Get Rumor

### Kohen Get Rumor Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מְנַשֶּׁה מִדְּוִיל, לִשְׁמוּאֵל: יְלַמְּדֵנוּ רַבֵּינוּ, יָצָא עָלָיו קוֹל – ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי כֹּהֵן כָּתַב גֵּט לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וְיוֹשֶׁבֶת תַּחְתָּיו וּמְשַׁמַּשְׁתּוֹ״, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yosef, son of Rav Menashe from Devil, sent** a query **to Shmuel: Our teacher, instruct us.** In the case of a priest **about whom** the following **rumor circulated: So-and-so** the **priest wrote a bill of divorce to his wife, but she is** still **residing under his** roof **and attending to him; what is** the *halakha*? Need one lend credence to this rumor?

A question was sent from Rav Yosef son of Rav Menashya of Dovila to Shmuel: a rumor had spread in town that a certain *kohen* had written a *get* for his wife — *yatza alav kol ish ploni kohein kasav get l'ishto* — and yet the couple was still living together, *yosheves tachtav umeshamashto*. What is the law?

The case is more complex than it might appear. If the *kohen* did in fact give the *get*, he is now living with his divorced wife, which would constitute a serious prohibition for a *kohen* — *machzir grushato*. The question concerns how to respond to this rumor in the absence of established facts.

### Investigation Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: תֵּצֵא, וְהַדָּבָר צָרִיךְ בְּדִיקָה. מַאי הִיא? אִילֵימָא דְּאִי מְבַטְּלִינַן קָלָא אוֹ לָא מְבַטְּלִינַן; וְהָא נְהַרְדְּעָא אַתְרֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל הִיא, וּבִנְהַרְדְּעָא לָא מְבַטְּלִי קָלָא! אֶלָּא דְּאִי קָרוּ לִנְתִינָה ״כְּתִיבָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Shmuel **sent** the following response **to him: She** must **leave** her husband; **but the matter requires investigation** before he is forced to divorce her. The Gemara asks: **What is** the investigation that is required? **If we say that** the investigation is **whether we** can **abolish the rumor** that circulated, **or we cannot abolish** it; that cannot be the question: **But isn't Neharde'a the place** where **Shmuel** is the halakhic authority and the rulings there are in accordance with his opinion, **and in Neharde'a they do not abolish a rumor? Rather,** the required investigation is to determine **whether** people in that place **refer to giving** a bill of divorce as **writing.** If that is the case, the rumor would be that he gave her a bill of divorce.

Shmuel's response is: *teitzei v'hadavar tzarich bdika* — she should leave the marriage and the matter requires investigation. The *Gemara* notes that in other contexts, Shmuel used the phrase *hadavar tzarich talmud* — the matter requires further *halachic* analysis. Here, the matter requires *bdika* — factual investigation — since the question is not what the law is, but what actually occurred.

### Dispel Rumor Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי קָרוּ לִנְתִינָה ״כְּתִיבָה״ – לִכְתִיבָה גּוּפַהּ מִי לָא קָרוּ לַהּ ״כְּתִיבָה״?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if they refer to giving** as **writing,** it proves nothing, as **don't they refer to writing itself** as **writing?** Therefore, the fact that they refer to giving as writing does not mean that he gave the woman the bill of divorce. The same term would be employed even if he only wrote the bill of divorce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין; דְּאִי מִיגַּלְּיָא מִילְּתָא דְּקָרוּ לִנְתִינָה כְּתִיבָה, דִּלְמָא ״נָתַן״ קָאָמְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes, as if it is discovered that they refer to giving** as **writing,** then there is reason to suspect that **perhaps they are saying** in the rumor **that he gave** her the bill of divorce, and there is concern that she is actually divorced.

The *Gemara* then addresses what kind of investigation *bdika* refers to. Is the question whether, when a rumor exists, we attempt to *mevatel* the rumor — to publicly dispel it — or whether we accept the rumor and act accordingly?

There is a genuine policy question embedded here. If we know the rumor is false and we publicly declare so, we might successfully neutralize it. Alternatively, attempting to fight the rumor might backfire: people who believe the rumor strongly will suspect that the *beit din* has been co-opted — that the husband has the rabbis in his pocket — and the attempt to dispel the rumor will only entrench the perception further. The question is therefore not merely factual but strategic: is it ever effective to fight perception with official declarations?

### Naharda'a Doesn't Dispel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּצֵא?! וְהָאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כֹּל קָלָא דְּבָתַר נִישּׂוּאִין – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Shmuel's statement that she must leave his home, the Gemara asks: **She** must **leave? But doesn't Rav Ashi say: We are not concerned for any rumor that** circulates **after marriage.** According to Rav Ashi, if a rumor circulates that a woman was betrothed to a man, there is concern about the rumor, and she is not allowed to marry someone else until she receives a bill of divorce from her rumored betrothed. But if such a rumor circulates after she has married someone else, there is no concern about such a rumor, and she is not obligated to leave her husband. Here too, in the case of this woman who is suspected of being divorced, she is currently living with her husband. Therefore, perhaps we should not be concerned about such a rumor.

The *Gemara* notes that *Naharda'a* — Shmuel's own city, the locus of his *halachic* authority — does not attempt to be *mevatel* rumors of this kind. If even in Shmuel's home community the policy is not to dispel such rumors, what does Shmuel have to say to the questioner? The practical import is that if one does not dispel rumors, one must act on them — which in this case means imposing some consequence on the couple.

### Language Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״תֵּצֵא״ נָמֵי – תֵּצֵא מִשֵּׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of Shmuel's directive: **She** must **leave?** This **also** means that **she** must **leave the second** husband. If her first husband dies, and she marries another priest, she must leave him due to the rumor that circulated that her first husband gave her a bill of divorce.

Before reaching that conclusion, the *Gemara* interrogates the language of the rumor itself. When people say a man "wrote a *get*" for his wife, does that necessarily mean he *gave* the *get*? It is entirely possible that *kasav* — "wrote" — means only that: he composed a document, not that he delivered it. If the rumor is only that he wrote a *get* and not that he gave one, there may be no rumor of actual divorce at all, and consequently no allegation that he is living with a *gerusha*.

### Double Uncertainty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִם כֵּן, אַתָּה מוֹצִיא לַעַז עַל בָּנָיו שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן! כֵּיוָן דְּמִשֵּׁנִי הוּא דְּמַפְּקִינַן לַהּ וּמֵרִאשׁוֹן לָא מַפְּקִינַן לַהּ, אָתֵי לְמֵימַר: סָמוּךְ לְמִיתָה גֵּרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so, you cast aspersions on the children of the first** husband. If the reason that she must leave the second husband is because she is assumed to be a divorcée, this means that she remained with her first husband while she was already divorced, and the children they had after the rumor circulated would be disqualified from the priesthood. The Gemara answers: **Since we remove her** only **from the second** husband, **and we do not remove her from the first** husband, **they will come to** explain the incident by **saying** as follows: The first husband **divorced her proximate to** his **death,** and the children from him are of unflawed lineage. Since he divorced her, she may not marry another priest. Because the assumption here is that the divorce took place proximate to the first husband's death, there is no concern of aspersions being cast on the children from the first husband.

Even granting that in common usage "wrote a *get*" might sometimes mean "gave a *get*" — *de-migalya milta de-karu li-nisuei ketiva* — this only means the rumor *might* refer to delivery. The result is a *safek* within a *safek*: there is a rumor, and the rumor might mean he gave the *get*, but it might not. This double uncertainty would seem to substantially weaken any grounds for imposing consequences.

### Rav Ashi Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אֵין הַטֶּבֶל מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, עַד שֶׁיִּרְאֶה פְּנֵי הַבַּיִת – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בִּעַרְתִּי הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִן הַבַּיִת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **As Rabbi Yannai says: Untithed produce does not become obligated in tithes until it sees the entrance of the house,** through which people enter and exit, **as it is stated** in the declaration of the tithes: **"I have put away the hallowed things out of my house"** (Deuteronomy 26:13). As long as untithed produce is not taken into the house through the primary entrance of the house, it is permitted to eat it casually without tithing it. Consequently, they would take the produce in through another entrance in order to exempt it from tithes.

At this point Rav Ashi's principle enters the discussion: *kol kala devatar nisuin lo chaishinan leih* — we do not give halachic weight to rumors that arise after a marriage has already taken place. This is a significant and welcome clarification. The concern about rumors — *kol* — is a *lechatchila* concern, a prospective one. We say: do not enter into a marriage if there is a credible rumor of prior divorce. But we do not use a rumor to undo a marriage that has already been contracted. The implications for Beit Shammai's ruling in the *Mishnah* are directly relevant: the perception created when a man commissions a *get* might lead us to restrict whom the woman may marry *going forward*, but it does not require dissolving any existing relationship.

### Second Marriage Bar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״תֵּצֵא״ נָמֵי – תֵּצֵא מִשֵּׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of Shmuel's directive: **She** must **leave?** This **also** means that **she** must **leave the second** husband. If her first husband dies, and she marries another priest, she must leave him due to the rumor that circulated that her first husband gave her a bill of divorce.

The *Gemara* thus clarifies what *teitzei* means in Shmuel's response: it does not mean she must be divorced from her current husband. Rather, *teitzei mi-sheni* — if the first husband were to die and she sought to remarry, she would not be permitted to marry a second husband, specifically a *kohen*. The consequence is prospective and limited, not retroactive and destructive.

## Perception Creates Reality

### Rabbis Shape Perception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, אַתָּה מוֹצִיא לַעַז עַל בָּנָיו שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן! כֵּיוָן דְּמִשֵּׁנִי הוּא דְּמַפְּקִינַן לַהּ וּמֵרִאשׁוֹן לָא מַפְּקִינַן לַהּ, אָתֵי לְמֵימַר: סָמוּךְ לְמִיתָה גֵּרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so, you cast aspersions on the children of the first** husband. If the reason that she must leave the second husband is because she is assumed to be a divorcée, this means that she remained with her first husband while she was already divorced, and the children they had after the rumor circulated would be disqualified from the priesthood. The Gemara answers: **Since we remove her** only **from the second** husband, **and we do not remove her from the first** husband, **they will come to** explain the incident by **saying** as follows: The first husband **divorced her proximate to** his **death,** and the children from him are of unflawed lineage. Since he divorced her, she may not marry another priest. Because the assumption here is that the divorce took place proximate to the first husband's death, there is no concern of aspersions being cast on the children from the first husband.

The *Gemara* then raises a striking counter-argument. If the consequence is only that she may not marry a second man — a *kohen* — while remaining with her first husband, there is a paradox: people will observe that the rabbis are permitting her to stay with her first husband while barring her from remarrying after his death. That combination of rulings will itself generate a perception. People will not say "the rumor was false." They will construct a narrative to explain the rabbis' behavior.

### Samuch L'Misa Logic

Specifically, the *Gemara* says people will say *samuch l'misa gershah* — he must have divorced her just before he died. People will reason: the rabbis would not bar her from remarrying unless there was something to the rumor; and since she lived with her husband until his death, the divorce must have occurred right at the end. The ruling itself thus generates a new and more damaging perception than the original rumor.

This is a genuinely unsettling passage. The *Gemara* is acknowledging that the rabbis' response to perception can itself shape perception — and yet rather than concluding that we should therefore impose no consequence at all (which would send the clearest message that the rumor was false), the *Gemara* proceeds to analyze which consequence is tolerable. The answer is: the limited consequence of barring remarriage to a *kohen* is tolerable because people who observe it will assume *samuch l'misa gershah* — and in that case they will understand that she was with her first husband until the end, which at least does not impugn the children of that marriage as *chalalim*.

This is, as acknowledged, a deeply difficult passage. The *Gemara* openly grapples with the fact that rabbinic rulings create as well as respond to social perception, and yet does not reach a fully satisfying resolution of how this interplay should be managed.

### Yeridat Hadorot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר אִילְעַי: בֹּא וּרְאֵה שֶׁלֹּא כְּדוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים דּוֹרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai: Come and see that the later generations are unlike the earlier generations,** as the earlier generations were more conscientious about maintaining an unflawed lineage.

The *Gemara* introduces a teaching attributed to Rabbi Ami bar Chama citing Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Ilai: come and see that the later generations are not like the earlier generations — *bo r'eh shelo kedoros rishonim doros ha'achronim*. The contrast drawn is between Beit Shammai as representative of the *doros rishonim* and Rabbi Dosa as representative of the *doros achronim*.

This is a *yeridat hadorot* type of observation — a romanticization of the stringency and purity of earlier generations relative to later ones. But what is notable is that the particular example chosen complicates any simple evaluation of who was "better."

### Rabbi Dosa Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דּוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים – בֵּית שַׁמַּאי. דּוֹרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנִים – רַבִּי דּוֹסָא. דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁבוּיָיה – אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי דּוֹסָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The earlier generations** is referring to **Beit Shammai.** Beit Shammai were so careful about the sanctity of the priesthood that they said, as stated in the mishna, that the writing of a bill of divorce alone renders a priest's wife forbidden to him. **The later generations** is referring to **Rabbi Dosa, as it is taught** in a mishna (*Eduyyot* 3:6): A priest's wife who was taken **captive** may **partake of *teruma*,** and there is no concern that perhaps she was raped while in captivity and thereby became forbidden to her husband and disqualified from partaking of *teruma*. This is **the statement of Rabbi Dosa.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי דּוֹסָא: וְכִי מָה עָשָׂה לָהּ עַרְבִי זֶה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמִּיעֵךְ לָהּ בֵּין דַּדֶּיהָ, פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה?!
>
> **Translation:** In explanation of this statement, **Rabbi Dosa says: And what did this Arab do to her** when he took her captive? **Because he fondled her breasts he disqualified her from the priesthood?** As long as it is not determined that her captors actually raped her, she is not prohibited from partaking of *teruma*. It is apparent from here that the later generations are more lenient than the early generations with regard to the sanctity of the priesthood.

The *baraita* records that a woman taken captive — *shvuya* — who was married to a *kohen* may, according to Rabbi Dosa, continue eating *terumah* upon her return. We do not presume she was raped. Rabbi Dosa's reasoning is direct and almost startling in its humanity: *v'chi mah asah lah aravi zeh? b'shvil shemeichah bein dadeiha — tifsel m'unakehunah?* What did this Arab do to her? Because he may have fondled her — should she be disqualified from *kehunah*? Rabbi Dosa refuses to presume rape even in conditions where it was entirely possible, in order to protect the woman's status and her marriage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, בָּתֵּי גוֹיִם מִי מִטַּמְּאִי?! וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתַתִּי נֶגַע צָרַעַת בְּבֵית אֶרֶץ אֲחוּזַּתְכֶם״ – אֶרֶץ אֲחוּזַּתְכֶם מִטַּמְּאָה בִּנְגָעִים, וְאֵין בָּתֵּי גוֹיִם מִטַּמְּאִין בִּנְגָעִים! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ ״חוּץ״ הוּא; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Moreover,** an objection against this interpretation may be raised as follows: **Do** the **houses of gentiles become ritually impure** at all? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that from the verse: **"And I put the plague of leprosy in a house of the land of your possession"** (Leviticus 14:34), it is derived that only **the land of your possession,** i.e., the houses of Jews, **becomes impure through leprous sores** of the house, **but** the **houses of gentiles do not become impure through leprosy? Rather, conclude from** the mishna that the meaning of *ella* **is except.** The Gemara concludes: **Conclude from it** that *ella* means except.

The contrast with Beit Shammai is therefore not straightforward. Beit Shammai, the *doros rishonim*, imposed restrictions on a completely innocent woman based on the faintest whiff of a rumor. Rabbi Dosa, the *doros achronim*, refused to strip a woman of her status based on a presumption of violation she may never have suffered. In this framing, the "later" generation actually shows greater sensitivity to human dignity and real consequences.

The *Gemara* cites a second contrast — that the *doros rishonim* would bring their produce through the main gate in order to be *chayiv* in *maaser*, while the *doros achronim* would bring it in through rooftops and courtyards to avoid the obligation. This is a less ambiguous case of decline. But the contrast with the *shvuya* case is worth sitting with: sometimes what looks like a relaxation of standards is actually a greater moral clarity. It is not that we think the *doros achronim* are doing wrong; it is, as framed here, more of a sense of loss — are we losing some dimension of *kedusha* and *tahara* even as we do the right thing by human beings? That tension is what the *Gemara* appears to be naming.

## Overnight Together Problem

### Overnight Inn Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְלָנָה עִמּוֹ בְּפוּנְדְּקִי, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי; וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who divorces his wife, and** afterward **she spent the night with him at an inn [*befundaki*], Beit Shammai say: She does not require a second bill of divorce from him, and Beit Hillel say: She requires a second bill of divorce from him,** since they may have engaged in sexual intercourse at the inn and thereby betrothed her once again.

The *Mishnah* now presents the case of a man who divorces his wife and then spends the night with her in an inn — *hamigaresh es ishto v'lan imah b'pundaki*.

### Beit Shammai No Second

Beit Shammai rules: she does not need a second *get* from him — *eina tzricha heimenu get sheni*. We do not presume from the fact that they spent the night together that they had sexual relations and thereby entered into a new marriage.

### Beit Hillel Requires Second

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁנִּתְגָּרְשָׁה מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין; וּמוֹדִים בְּנִתְגָּרְשָׁה מִן הָאֵירוּסִין – שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין לִבּוֹ גַּס בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When** did they say this *halakha*? **When she was divorced following** the state of **marriage.** Beit Hillel **concede that when she was divorced following** the state of **betrothal, she does not require a second bill of divorce from him, due to** the fact that **he is not accustomed to her.** Therefore, there is no concern that they engaged in sexual intercourse, even though they spent the night together at the inn.

Beit Hillel rules more stringently here: she does require a second *get*, but only *bizman shenitgarsha min hanisu'in* — when the divorce follows a full marriage (*nisu'in*) in which the couple had actually lived together and been intimate.

### Eirusin Agreement

Both schools agree, however, that if the divorce followed only *eirusin* — betrothal, prior to cohabitation — no second *get* is required: *mipnei she'ein libo gas bah*. He is not sufficiently at ease with her — having never yet lived with her as a wife — to make it plausible that after granting her a *get* he would have sex with her the same night, let alone with the intent of remarriage.

This formulation makes clear that the *Mishnah*'s debate turns on whether we presume sexual relations occurred, since the presence of *nisu'in* versus *eirusin* is relevant only to the probability of sex, not to the legal significance of sex if we already know it happened.

It is worth noting the *Yerushalmi*'s insight cited here: this debate may be connected to the famous *Mishnah* at the end of *Gittin* (*daf* 90), where Beit Shammai rules that a man may not divorce his wife unless she has committed a sexual transgression — *ervas davar* — while Beit Hillel permits divorce even if she merely burnt his food. If Beit Shammai believes divorce only occurs because of a serious transgression, it follows naturally that Beit Shammai would not assume the divorced couple desired to reunite sexually. Beit Hillel, who permits divorce for minor reasons, must account for the possibility that the divorce was over something trivial and the couple, having spent a night together, may have reconciled.

## Edei Yichud vs. Edei Biah

### Sex Witnessed Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁרָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The **dispute** between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel is specifically **in** a case **where they saw that she engaged in sexual intercourse,**

Rabbah bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan offers a different version of the debate. He says the *machloket* between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel arises specifically in a case where witnesses actually observed the couple having sexual relations — *beshero'uha shenivala*.

### Beit Shammai Zenut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה בְּעִילָתוֹ בְּעִילַת זְנוּת;
>
> **Translation:** **as Beit Shammai hold: A person does** engage in **licentious sexual intercourse.** Although they were seen engaging in sexual intercourse, one cannot assume that he intended to betroth her, since they were recently divorced. The assumption is that they were simply engaging in licentious sexual intercourse. Consequently, he is not required to give her a second bill of divorce.

On this reading, Beit Shammai holds: *adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* — a person may engage in a sexual act that is not intended to constitute marriage. The sexual act itself does not imply matrimonial intent, and therefore we do not retroactively treat the couple as having remarried.

### Beit Hillel No Zenut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: אֵין אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה בְּעִילָתוֹ בְּעִילַת זְנוּת. אֲבָל לֹא רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel hold: A person does not** engage in **licentious sexual intercourse.** Therefore, he had the intention to betroth her, and he must give her another bill of divorce. **But** if **they did not see that she engaged in sexual intercourse,** even though they spent the night together at an inn, **everyone agrees** that **she does not require a second bill of divorce from him,** as there is no concern that perhaps they engaged in sexual intercourse.

Beit Hillel holds: *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* — we presume that when a man has sexual relations, particularly with a woman who was formerly his wife, he intends it as an act of marriage, not mere fornication. The couple should therefore be treated as having remarried, and a second *get* is required.

### Rashi Clarification

Rashi clarifies an important limitation: this presumption — *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* — applies specifically to a woman who was previously his wife, *nashim shenitgarsha min hanisu'in*. In that case, the prior marital relationship provides the context in which we interpret the sexual act as matrimonial. The principle does not mean that any two people observed having sexual relations are thereby presumed married. Without the prior marital context, there is no basis for such a presumption.

### Seclusion Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: וּמוֹדִים בְּנִתְגָּרְשָׁה מִן הָאֵירוּסִין – שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי, שֶׁאֵין לִבּוֹ גַּס בָּהּ. וְאִי בְּשֶׁרָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה, מָה לִי מִן הָאֵירוּסִין וּמָה לִי מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this understanding of the mishna: **We learned** in the mishna that Beit Hillel **concede that when she was divorced following** the state of **betrothal, she does not require a second bill of divorce from him because he is not accustomed to her. And if** the mishna is referring to a case in **which they saw that she engaged in sexual intercourse, what** is the difference **to me** if it was **following** the state of **betrothal and what** is the difference **to me** if it was **following** the state of **marriage?** In either case, they saw that she engaged in sexual intercourse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין – בְּשֶׁלֹּא רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה; וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר – כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל שֶׁלֹּא רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי,
>
> **Translation:** **Rather** it can be explained that **the mishna** is actually speaking about a case in **which they did not see that she engaged in sexual intercourse. Rabbi Yoḥanan stated** his opinion **in accordance** with the statement of **that *tanna*. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said: Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai did not disagree about** a case **where they did not see that she engaged in sexual intercourse.** Everyone agrees **that** in such a scenario, **she does not require a second bill of divorce from him.**

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ella matnitin beshelo ro'uha shenivala* — the *Mishnah* must be dealing with a case where witnesses did not directly observe sexual relations. Rather, there were *edei yichud* — witnesses to seclusion — who saw the couple enter a private space together. The debate is whether seclusion witnesses are treated as tantamount to witnesses to sexual relations.

### Shammai Seclusion Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ – עַל שֶׁרָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה; שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה בְּעִילָתוֹ בְּעִילַת זְנוּת; וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵין אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה בְּעִילָתוֹ בְּעִילַת זְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what** case **did they disagree?** They disagreed **about** a case **where they saw that she engaged in sexual intercourse. As Beit Shammai say: A person does engage in licentious sexual intercourse. And Beit Hillel say: A person does not** engage in **licentious sexual intercourse.** Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar disagrees with the mishna.

Beit Shammai holds: *lo amrinan hein hein edei yichud hein hein edei biah* — we do not say that the witnesses to seclusion are the same as witnesses to sexual relations. Seclusion witnesses testify only to what they saw: the couple entered a private space. They cannot be treated as having implicitly testified to sex, and therefore no presumption of remarriage arises.

### Hillel Seclusion = Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַתְנִיתִין – דְּאוֹקֵימְנָא בְּלֹא רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה, בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? דְּאִיכָּא עֵדֵי יִחוּד, וְלֵיכָּא עֵדֵי בִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the mishna, which we established** as discussing a case in **which they did not see that she engaged in sexual intercourse, with regard to what do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: They disagree about a case in **which there are witnesses** to their **seclusion, but there are no witnesses** to an act of **sexual intercourse.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן הֵן הֵן עֵדֵי יִחוּד וְהֵן הֵן עֵדֵי בִיאָה. וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: אָמְרִינַן הֵן הֵן עֵדֵי יִחוּד וְהֵן הֵן עֵדֵי בִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to such a case, **Beit Shammai hold: We do not say** that **these are the witnesses of seclusion, these are the witnesses of sexual intercourse.** According to Beit Shammai, although there are witnesses that they were secluded, this is not considered to be tantamount to testimony that they engaged in sexual intercourse. **And Beit Hillel hold: We** do **say** that **these are the witnesses of seclusion, these are the witnesses of sexual intercourse.** Since it is assumed that they engaged in sexual intercourse, she is required to obtain a second bill of divorce from him.

Beit Hillel holds: *amrinan hein hein edei yichud hein hein edei biah* — witnesses to seclusion are implicitly witnesses to sex. Since we presume that a man does not intend a sexual act with his former wife as mere fornication, and since seclusion is treated as equivalent to the act itself, the couple is presumed to have remarried and a second *get* is required.

### Eirusin Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדִים בְּנִתְגָּרְשָׁה מִן הָאֵירוּסִין – שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי, דְּכֵיוָן דְּאֵין לִבּוֹ גַּס בָּהּ. לָא אָמְרִינַן הֵן הֵן עֵדֵי בִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Beit Hillel **concede that when she was divorced following** the state of **betrothal, she does not require a second bill of divorce from him,** even if they were alone together. **For since he is not accustomed to her, we do not say** that these are the witnesses of seclusion; **these are the witnesses of intercourse.**

Even according to Beit Hillel, however, when the prior divorce followed only *eirusin* and not *nisu'in*, we do not make this presumption — *ein libo gas bah*, he is not sufficiently comfortable with her. Without the intimacy of prior cohabitation, even Beit Hillel does not extend the *edei yichud* presumption to cover the act of sexual relations.

### Two Witnesses Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כִּסְתַם מִשְׁנָה; וְאוֹקִימְנָא לְמַתְנִיתִין בְּשֶׁלֹּא רָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה! אָמוֹרָאֵי נִינְהוּ וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say this,** that Beit Hillel require a second bill of divorce only when witnesses saw that they engaged in sexual intercourse? **But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** The ***halakha*** in all cases **is like an unattributed mishna. And we established the mishna** to be discussing a case in which **they did not see that she engaged in sexual intercourse.** How then does Rabbi Yoḥanan rule contrary to the mishna? The Gemara answers: **They are *amora'im*, and** they disagree **with regard to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Some of them hold that Rabbi Yoḥanan does not always rule in accordance with an unattributed mishna.

The *Gemara* closes this section by noting a tension in Rabbi Yochanan's positions: elsewhere Rabbi Yochanan is reported to hold that the *halacha* follows a *stam Mishnah* — an unattributed *Mishnah*. But here Rabbi Yochanan's formulation aligns with the *baraita*'s version of the debate (witnesses saw sex), not the *Mishnah*'s version (witnesses saw only seclusion). The *Gemara* resolves this by saying these represent two *amoraim* debating what Rabbi Yochanan's view actually was: the *amora* who reports Rabbi Yochanan's opinion in line with the *baraita* does not accept the general principle that Rabbi Yochanan always follows the *stam Mishnah*.

## Get Kereiach Folded

### Accordion Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כְּנָסָהּ בְּגֵט קֵרֵחַ – תֵּצֵא מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה, וְכׇל הַדְּרָכִים הָאֵלּוּ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a woman **was married** by her second husband **on the basis of** receiving **a bare bill of divorce,** i.e., a folded and tied bill of divorce that is missing signatures, **she** must **leave** both **this,** the first husband, **and that,** the second husband. **And all of those** previously mentioned **ways** of penalizing a woman who remarried based on the bills of divorce detailed in the earlier mishna (79b) apply **to her** in this case as well.

The final *Mishnah* of today's *daf* introduces the *get kereiach* — the "bald" or "folded" *get* developed specifically to slow down *kohanim* who might impulsively divorce their wives. Since a *kohen* may not remarry a divorced woman — even his own wife — the rabbis wanted to make the act of writing a *get* for a *kohen* significantly more laborious than for an ordinary Israelite.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּט קֵרֵחַ – הַכֹּל מַשְׁלִימִין עָלָיו, דִּבְרֵי בֶּן נַנָּס. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַשְׁלִימִין עָלָיו אֶלָּא קְרוֹבִים, הָרְאוּיִין לְהָעִיד בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a bare bill of divorce; anyone,** even those who are disqualified from bearing witness, **can complete it,** i.e., sign it in addition to the primary witnesses, so that it will not remain bare. This is **the statement of ben Nannas. Rabbi Akiva says: Not** all who are disqualified from bearing witness can **complete it. Rather, only relatives who are fit to testify in another case.** Rabbi Akiva permits only the inclusion of witnesses who would ordinarily be valid witnesses, but who are invalid here because they are relatives of either the husband and wife or the other witnesses.

The mechanism they devised was an accordion-style document. A sheet of parchment was written upon for a few lines, then folded over, and the fold was tied shut with a knot. A witness then signed on the back of that folded section. The process was repeated: more text was written, another fold was made, another knot was tied, another signature was affixed. The result was a document with multiple sealed sections, each bearing a witness signature on its exterior. The minimum number of such folds — and signatures — required by the rabbis for a *get kereiach* was three, as opposed to the two witnesses required for an ordinary *get*.

If a *kohen* chose to add more folds — say, ten — then ten sections needed to be signed. A *get kereiach* is defined as one in which the number of folds (*kesharav*) exceeds the number of signatures (*edav*) — *kol shekesharav merubim me'adav*. A fold left blank — unsigned — renders the *get* defective and treated as *pasul*, with all the attendant consequences.

### Ben Nanas Position

Ben Nanas rules that when there is a blank section — a fold without a signature — beyond the minimum three, *psulim* can fill in the blank: *hakol mashlimin alav*. Even witnesses who are disqualified for normal testimony may sign the surplus sections, and the *get* is nevertheless valid. The reasoning is that these extra signatures are not functioning as genuine *eidus* — testimony — but merely as a formal fulfillment of the requirement that no fold be left unsigned.

### Rabbi Akiva Restrictions

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He rules: *ein mashlimin alav ela kerovim hara'uyim leha'id bemakom acher* — only those who would be valid witnesses in other contexts may fill in the blank sections, specifically relatives (*kerovim*). Relatives are disqualified in cases involving their own family members, but in other contexts they are perfectly valid witnesses. Slaves (*avadim*) and thieves (*gazlanim*) may not sign, even for surplus sections.

### Kereiach Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזֶהוּ גֵּט קֵרֵחַ? כׇּל שֶׁקְּשָׁרָיו מְרוּבִּין מֵעֵדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is a bare bill of divorce?** It is **any** bill of divorce **where** the number of **its folds** is **more than** the number of **its witnesses.** In a folded and tied bill of divorce, the bill of divorce is folded and the folds are then tied. Instead of having two witnesses sign at the bottom of the document, witnesses would sign on each tied fold. A bare bill of divorce has more folds than signatures, i.e., some folds lack signatures.

The definition of *get kereiach* flows directly from this structure: it is any *get* in which the number of folds exceeds the number of signatures. The name *kereiach* — "bald" — evokes the image of a fold that has been left bare, without its requisite signature.

### Kulchem Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּגֵט קֵרֵחַ? גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם ״כּוּלְּכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason that a bare bill of divorce** is not valid, considering that there are more than two witnesses signed on it? The Gemara answers: It is a rabbinic **decree** that was issued **due to** one who says: **All of you** should sign. Since it can be assumed that one who writes a folded and tied bill of divorce wants to have each tied fold signed, there is concern that he may have instructed that all of them sign. If they do not sign, the condition is not fulfilled, and the bill of divorce is not valid.

Why is a blank fold such a serious problem that it renders the entire *get* invalid and triggers all the negative consequences? The *Gemara* answers: *gezeira mishum kulchem* — a rabbinic decree arising from the concern that "everyone sign." The reasoning is as follows: if a man creates a *get* with ten folds, the creation of ten fold-sections implies that ten witnesses were originally designated to sign. If only three signatures appear and seven folds are blank, an observer might conclude that seven witnesses who were supposed to sign did not — and the *get* is therefore defective for having failed to gather all its designated witnesses. To eliminate this concern entirely, every fold must bear a signature. Leaving any fold blank invites the misperception that a witness who was supposed to sign failed to do so.

Interestingly, the fact that the *Gemara* permits *psulim* to sign the surplus folds demonstrates that the concern is not that the *get* will be seen as requiring more *kosher* witnesses. The worry is purely one of perception — that blank spaces will look like an incomplete process. Even an invalid signature fills the visual gap and eliminates the *gezeira*'s concern.

### Slave Freed Misconception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, עֶבֶד מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? דְּאָתוּ לְמֵימַר כָּשֵׁר לְעֵדוּת. קָרוֹב נָמֵי, אָתוּ לְמֵימַר כָּשֵׁר לְעֵדוּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Akiva, what is the reason** that **a slave cannot** sign? The Gemara answers: The Sages were concerned **that** people **will** mistakenly **come to say** that since the slave served as a witness in this case, the slave is **fit to bear witness** in all cases. They will use a slave as a witness in situations that demand testimony accepted only from qualified witnesses. The Gemara challenges this: If so, with regard to **a relative, too, they will come to say** that a relative is **fit to bear witness** in all cases.

The *Gemara* then asks why Rabbi Akiva excludes a slave (*eved*) from signing even surplus sections. The initial suggestion is that people will say: "Look, a slave signed — slaves must be valid for *eidus*!" But this reasoning would equally apply to a relative signing, since people might say: "A relative signed — relatives must be valid!" So that cannot be the full explanation.

### Thief Teshuvah Misconception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא עֶבֶד הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא – דְּדִלְמָא אָתוּ לְאַסּוֹקֵיהּ לְיוּחֲסִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one could explain that with regard to **a slave, this is the reason** why he is disqualified: There is concern that **perhaps they will come to elevate** his status **with regard to lineage.** If a slave signs the bill of divorce, they might say that if he signed a bill of divorce, he must be an Israelite.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּזְלָן – דְּבַר יוּחֲסִין הוּא, לִיתַּכְשַׁר! אַלְּמָה תְּנַן, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַשְׁלִימִין עָלָיו אֶלָּא קְרוֹבִים, הָרְאוּיִין לְהָעִיד בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר; קָרוֹב – אִין, גַּזְלָן – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If this is the reason why a slave cannot sign, then **a robber, who is of** unflawed **lineage, should be fit** to complete the signatures of a folded and tied bill of divorce, according to Rabbi Akiva. **Why,** then, did **we learn** in the mishna that **Rabbi Akiva says: Not** all can **complete it. Rather, only relatives who are fit to testify in another case.** The Gemara deduces from this: **A relative, yes;** but **a robber, no.**

The *Gemara* therefore reformulates Rabbi Akiva's reasoning for excluding a slave: *dilma asu l'assukei l'yuchsin* — people will not say slaves are generally *kosher* for testimony, since everyone knows they are not. Instead, they will say: "How could a slave sign? He must have been freed!" If people believe the slave was freed, they will treat him as a free man for purposes of *yichus*, potentially allowing him to marry a free woman in contexts where that would be problematic.

The analogous concern for a *gazlan* — a thief — is: *asu l'meimar teshuva avad* — people will assume he did *teshuva* and was thereby rehabilitated as a valid witness. This would cause people to trust him and have him sign other documents, leading to a reliance on invalid testimony in consequential halachic matters.

### Relatives Safe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא עֶבֶד הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא – דְּאָתוּ לְמֵימַר: שַׁחְרוֹרֵי שַׁחְרְרֵיהּ; גַּזְלָן נָמֵי – אָתוּ לְמֵימַר: תְּשׁוּבָה עֲבַד; קָרוֹב – מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? קָרוֹב כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יָדְעִי דְּקָרוֹב הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, this is the reason** why **a slave** is disqualified: **They will come to say** that his master **emancipated him** and he signed because he is now like any other Israelite. Similarly, with regard to **a robber, they will also come to say** that **he repented** and will allow him to testify in other cases, as well. Therefore, Rabbi Akiva declared him to be unfit as well. With regard to **a relative, what can be said** to disallow him to sign this bill of divorce? With regard to **a relative, everyone knows that he is a relative,** and there is no concern that they will allow him to testify in other cases involving his relatives.

For a *karov* — a relative — neither of these concerns applies. *Karov kulei alma yadei dekarov hu* — everyone knows he is a relative, and everyone knows that relatives are disqualified only in matters involving their own families. No one will draw any anomalous conclusions from seeing a relative's signature. It simply means this is a supplementary fill-in signature from a family member, and the situation is self-explanatory.

### Two Witnesses Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שְׁאֵילְתָא אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא סָבָא אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: גֵּט קֵרֵחַ – קְשָׁרָיו שִׁבְעָה וְעֵדָיו שִׁשָּׁה; שִׁשָּׁה וְעֵדָיו חֲמִשָּׁה; חֲמִשָּׁה וְעֵדָיו אַרְבָּעָה; אַרְבָּעָה וְעֵדָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה – עַד כָּאן מַחְלוֹקֶת בֶּן נַנָּס וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אֲבָל קְשָׁרָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה וְעֵדָיו שְׁנַיִם – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין מַשְׁלִימִין עָלָיו אֶלָּא קָרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rabba bar She'eilta says** that **Rav Hamnuna the Elder says** that **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** The **dispute** between **ben Nannas and Rabbi Akiva** with regard to **a bare bill of divorce** that has more tied folds than witnesses is **only** in a case where **its ties** total **seven and its witnesses** total **six,** or its ties total **six and its witnesses** total **five,** or its ties total **five and its witnesses** total **four,** or its ties total **four and its witnesses** total **three.** But if **its ties** total **three and its witnesses** total **two, everyone,** even ben Nannas, **agrees** that **not** all disqualified witnesses can **complete it. Rather, only a relative** can complete it.

The *Gemara* then cites a *baraita* that extends the analysis: seven folds with six signatures, six with five, five with four, four with three — in each case, the debate between Ben Nanas and Rabbi Akiva applies. As long as there are at least three *kosher* signatures, the question is merely what categories of people may fill in the surplus blanks. *Ad kan machlokes Ben Nanas v'Rabbi Akiva* — up to this point is the debate.

However, if there are three folds and only two witnesses — *shachrech sheloshah v'eideh shenayim* — *divrei hakol ein mashlimim alav ela karov* — everyone agrees that only a relative may sign the missing third position. The logic is that the minimum required for a valid *get kereiach* is three *kosher* signatures; without that minimum, the entire *get* is deficient in a more fundamental way, and the standards for filling the gap are consequently higher.

### Basic Three Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבָּה בַּר שְׁאֵילְתָא: מִכְּדֵי כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה בִּמְקוּשָּׁר – כִּשְׁנַיִם בְּפָשׁוּט דָּמֵי; מָה הָתָם – קָרוֹב לָא, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי – קָרוֹב לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Rabba bar She'eilta: After all, all three** witnesses **in** a folded and **tied** bill of divorce are **like** the **two** witnesses **in an ordinary** bill of divorce, since the Sages said that a folded and tied bill of divorce requires at least three witnesses. Therefore, **just as there,** in the case of an ordinary bill of divorce, **a relative cannot** serve as one of the two witnesses, **so too here, a relative** should **not** serve as one of the primary witnesses.

This ruling, however, immediately raises a challenge. Rav Zeira poses it directly to Rabbah bar Shilas, from whom he had received this teaching: *michdi kol sheloshah bi-mekushar k'shenayim bi-pashut dami* — three witnesses on a folded *get* are the equivalent of two witnesses on a plain *get*, since the minimum threshold of valid witnesses is set at three for this document. *Mah hasam karov lo, af hacha nami karov lo* — just as on a plain *get* a relative may not serve as one of the two required witnesses, so too here, when you are down to the basic three required signatures, how can a relative serve as one of them?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַף לְדִידִי קַשְׁיָא לִי, וּשְׁאֵילְתֵּיהּ לְרַב הַמְנוּנָא, וְרַב הַמְנוּנָא לְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַנַּח לִשְׁלֹשָׁה בִּמְקוּשָּׁר, דְּלָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar She'eilta **said to him: To me as well** this was **difficult, and I asked that** question **to Rav Hamnuna, and Rav Hamnuna** asked it **to Rav Adda bar Ahava,** who had transmitted this statement, **and he said to him: Leave** the case of **three** witnesses **in a** folded and **tied** bill of divorce, **as** the requirement **is not by Torah law.** While the requirement to have a minimum of two witnesses in an ordinary bill of divorce is by Torah law, the requirement to have a minimum of three witnesses for a folded and tied bill of divorce is by rabbinic law. Consequently, it is of no concern if the third witness is not fit to bear witness.

Rabbah bar She'eilta replied that he had asked the very same question of Rav Hamnuna, who had received the ruling from Rav Adda bar Ahava. Rav Adda's answer was: *hanicha sheloshah bi-mekushar delav de-oraisa* — indeed, the requirement of three signatures on a *get kereiach* is only of rabbinic origin; biblically, two witnesses suffice. But even granting that the three-signature requirement is rabbinic, the question remains: if the rabbis defined three as the minimum for this *get*, and one of those three is a relative, has the rabbinic minimum actually been met?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: גֵּט קֵרֵחַ – קְשָׁרָיו שִׁבְעָה וְעֵדָיו שִׁשָּׁה; שִׁשָּׁה וְעֵדָיו חֲמִשָּׁה; חֲמִשָּׁה וְעֵדָיו אַרְבָּעָה; אַרְבָּעָה וְעֵדָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה – עַד כָּאן מַחְלוֹקֶת בֶּן נַנָּס וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **this is also taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 6:9): The **dispute** between **ben Nannas and Rabbi Akiva** with regard to **a bare bill of divorce** that has more tied folds than witnesses is **only** in a case where **its ties** total **seven and its witnesses** total **six,** or its ties total **six and its witnesses** total **five,** or its ties total **five and its witnesses** total **four,** or its ties total **four and its witnesses** total **three.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִשְׁלִים עָלָיו עֶבֶד, בֶּן נַנָּס אוֹמֵר: הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר; וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר. אֲבָל קְשָׁרָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה וְעֵדָיו שְׁנַיִם – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין מַשְׁלִימִין עָלָיו אֶלָּא קָרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If **a slave completed it** with his signature, and the woman remarried on the basis of this bill of divorce, **ben Nannas says: The offspring** of a subsequent marriage **is unflawed. And Rabbi Akiva says: The offspring is a *mamzer*. But** if **its ties** total **three, and its witnesses** total **two, everyone agrees** that **not** all disqualified witnesses can **complete it. Rather,** it can be completed **only** by **a relative** of either the husband and wife or the other witnesses.

The *Gemara* then cites a confirming *baraita* and reads a specific case: if a *get kereiach* had a surplus blank filled in by a slave, Ben Nanas says it is *kasher* (at least *bediavad*), while Rabbi Akiva says *ha-vlad mamzer* — the child is a *mamzer* — the most stringent possible consequence. When the *Gemara* reaches the case of three folds with two witnesses, it reports that Rav Yosef reads the text differently: where the tradition says *karov*, Rav Yosef rules *kasher* — meaning that when you are down to the basic three, one cannot rely on a relative at all, and if the third signer was a relative, the *get* would only be treated as valid (*kasher*) if that position is filled by a proper *kosher* witness. Rav Pappa endorses this correction: *tani kasher* — the reading should be *kasher*, not *karov*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף מַתְנִי: ״כָּשֵׁר״. וְהָתַנְיָא: קָרוֹב! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, תְּנִי: כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef taught:** Only **a valid** witness can complete it. The Gemara challenges: **But isn't it taught** explicitly in a *baraita*: **A relative? Rav Pappa said:** Correct the language of the *baraita*, and **teach: Valid.**

The resolution emerging from this discussion is that for surplus blank sections — beyond the minimum three — the debate between Ben Nanas and Rabbi Akiva governs what categories of invalid signatories may fill in. For the basic three signatures themselves, however, the standards are categorically higher, and the question of whether a relative may serve as one of those three remains a serious difficulty that the *Gemara* is still pressing at the close of the *daf*. This discussion will continue on the following *daf*.
