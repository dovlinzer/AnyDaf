# Bava Batra 140 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Estate Division Basics

### Perek Mi Shemeit Intro

Today's daf is 140, and we are in *Perek Mi Shemeit*, the penultimate chapter of the tractate. This *perek* deals with the legal framework governing inheritance when a man dies leaving behind both sons and daughters. The fundamental rule is that males inherit while females, if they are minors — that is, below the age of *bagrut*, maturity, which the *Gemara* sets at twelve and a half — are supported by the estate until they either marry or reach that age of maturity.

### Daughters as Creditors

The conceptual model underlying this framework is that the daughters are, in effect, creditors of the estate. There is an obligation that the estate bears toward them, analogous to a lien or a debt. This framing will prove crucial as we follow the *Gemara*'s analysis, because how one characterizes the daughters' claim — as a true creditor's debt, or as something more nuanced — determines the outcome across a range of practical scenarios.

### Merubim Definition

Against this backdrop, the *mishna* introduces the distinction between *nechasim merubim* and *nechasim muatim*, a large estate and a small estate. *Merubim* means the estate contains at least one additional unit of value beyond what is needed to support all the daughters through *bagrut*. To illustrate: if there are five daughters of varying ages, and the total remaining years until each reaches *bagrut* amounts to twenty girl-years, and it costs one thousand dollars per year to support a person, then the required support is twenty thousand dollars. Any estate worth more than twenty thousand dollars — even by a single *sela* — is classified as *nechasim merubim*, because something remains for the sons to inherit. If the estate is worth exactly twenty thousand or less, it is *nechasim muatim*, and the sons inherit nothing; the entire estate goes toward supporting the daughters.

## Merubim Shrinks

### Pshita: Heirs Protected

The *Gemara* opens by noting that one case is *pshita* — self-evident. *Merubin venismatu*: what happens when a large estate subsequently shrinks? Suppose at the moment of the father's death the estate was worth twenty-five thousand dollars — twenty thousand designated for the daughters' support, five thousand for the sons to inherit. Then the value declines. The *Gemara* rules: *kvar zachu bahen yorshim* — the sons have already acquired their portion. Their share is protected.

### Two Shrinkage Scenarios

This ruling, though stated as obvious by the *Gemara*, actually encompasses two quite different scenarios, each with its own internal logic. The first is a decline in the value of the assets themselves — say the estate was invested in property or stocks that lost value, so that what had been worth twenty-five thousand is now worth twelve thousand five hundred. In that case, everyone loses proportionally. The sons had an asset worth five thousand; if the entire estate has fallen by half, their portion is now worth two thousand five hundred, and the daughters' portion has similarly declined. One does not reallocate everything to the daughters on the grounds that they still need twenty thousand for support; rather, all parties absorb the loss proportionally.

The second scenario is different: the assets themselves have not lost value, but the cost of food has risen dramatically. What the court estimated would cost twenty thousand dollars to provide support until *bagrut* now will cost forty thousand. Here the sons clearly retain their five thousand, because the estate was definitively partitioned at the moment of death — twenty thousand for the daughters, five thousand for the sons — and subsequent inflation in food costs cannot retroactively strip the sons of what they had already been allocated.

### Creditor vs. Lien Model

This raises a sharper conceptual question. If the daughters are genuine creditors — if the estate owes them a debt — then in a case where the asset values have dropped and the twenty thousand dollars no longer suffices, why should the daughters not continue pressing their claim against the remaining estate until the full debt is paid? The answer could go either way depending on a further question: has any formal assignment of property actually taken place? If the sons had sat down, done the accounting, and transferred specific assets to the daughters — signed over stock certificates, conveyed title to real estate — then one can say the debt was paid. Whatever happens to those assets afterward is the daughters' problem, not the estate's. But what if the father died and nothing had yet been formally distributed? If two days later the stock collapses, why is the daughters' debt considered satisfied? They were never paid.

### Nachala DeOraisa

The answer that emerges, made explicit by *Tosafot* even if not spelled out verbatim in the *Gemara*'s text, is grounded in a different consideration: *nachala d'oraita*. There is a religious and legal value to the existence of a Torah-mandated inheritance. It is not ideal when an entire estate is consumed by the daughters' support obligation, even if that result is sometimes unavoidable when the estate is genuinely small. The institution of inheritance carries intrinsic weight. Therefore, even in a case where at the moment of death the estate would have produced a surplus for the sons and nothing has yet been formally divided, the *Gemara* protects that notional surplus. The sons retain their share as computed at the time of death, even if subsequent changes in value would, under a strict creditor model, have entitled the daughters to claim the entire estate. This is a prioritization of *nachala d'oraita* even at some cost to the daughters' support.

## Muatim Grows

### Muatim Growth Question

Having established that *merubin venismatu* is *pshita* — the sons retain their portion — the *Gemara* poses the harder question: *muatim venisrabu mai?* What if the estate was initially small — insufficient to fully support the daughters — and then grew in value? Say the estate was worth fifteen thousand dollars when the father died, in a situation where twenty thousand dollars was needed to support the daughters. The entire estate is effectively absorbed into the daughters' support obligation. Then, a day later, the assets surge in value to twenty-five thousand. Do the daughters keep the full twenty-five thousand? Or does the surplus of five thousand revert to the sons?

---

## Merubim Shrinks…

### Formal Assignment Impact

The *Gemara* does not distinguish between scenarios based on whether property was formally assigned before or after the change in value. Even if nothing was placed in a specific account for the daughters, even if the change occurred the moment after the father died, the ruling of *kvar zachu bahem yorshim* applies as of the moment of death. The *Gemara* does not require that an actual *kinyan* or formal transfer have occurred; the classification made at the moment of death is determinative. The question of formal assignment becomes significant in a different direction — in the debate between *Tosafot* and the *Rashbam* regarding *geviya*, which we will reach shortly.

---

## Muatim Grows…

### Two Property Models

The *Gemara* frames the *muatim venisrabu* question in terms of two competing models. On one view — *birshut yorshim kaime* — even in a case of *nechasim muatim*, the property remains fundamentally under the control and ownership of the sons. The daughters are creditors: the estate owes them a debt, and they are entitled to collect up to the amount of that debt, but no more. If the property increases in value, the benefit of that appreciation belongs to the sons, who own the underlying asset. The daughters collect twenty thousand dollars and nothing beyond that.

On the other view — *siluki mesalki yorshim mehacha* — in a case of *muatim*, we do not treat the sons as having inherited anything at all. The entire estate passes to the daughters outright. If the value subsequently rises, that increase accrues to them as the owners of property that has grown in their hands.

The *Gemara* brings a proof from a ruling of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *yetomim shekadmu umachru benechasim muatim, mah shemachru machru* — if the heirs went ahead and sold property in a case of *nechasim muatim*, the sale is valid. This is decisive evidence for the first model. A sale is valid only if the sellers have ownership. If the sons can sell the property even in a *muatim* situation, it means the property is fundamentally theirs; the daughters hold a lien against it, not title to it. Accordingly, if the value rises, the daughters have a twenty-thousand-dollar claim against an asset now worth twenty-five thousand — they collect their debt, and the five-thousand-dollar surplus remains with the sons.

### Support vs. Debt

*Tosafot* raises a sharper objection here, one that cuts to the heart of why the daughters' claim is not really like an ordinary creditor's debt. An ordinary creditor is owed a fixed sum: the estate owes him twenty thousand dollars, he collects twenty thousand dollars and walks away. The daughters' situation is fundamentally different. Their entitlement is not a fixed lump sum; it is an ongoing obligation of support — two dollars today, two dollars tomorrow, day after day, until they marry or reach *bagrut*. In that sense, even if property is formally set aside for them, it was never truly given to them as a lump sum payment. It was placed in reserve to ensure that ongoing support would be funded, but the daily support obligation continues to generate new entitlement in real time.

### Sons Can Sell

The validity of the sons' sale, as established by Rav Assi and Rabbi Yochanan, demonstrates that the daughters hold a lien rather than ownership. Crucially, that lien does not follow the property into the hands of a buyer. The daughters cannot collect their support from someone who purchased the property from the sons, because *mezon ha'isha vehabanot* — support of the wife and daughters — does not attach to buyers. The rationale is straightforward: the amount owed to the daughters is indeterminate, fluctuating with time and circumstance, and it would be grossly unfair to encumber a buyer with an open-ended and indefinite lien. The daughters may collect their support from the estate while it remains under the sons' control, but once sold, their claim against that property ends. Whatever proceeds the sons received from the sale, however, remain available to satisfy the daughters' support.

### Geviya & Ownership

There is an important dispute between the *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* regarding the moment at which the daughters' claim crystallizes into something more than a lien. The *Rashbam* holds that once *beit din* formally adjudicated the matter and collected (*gavya*) the property on the daughters' behalf — awarding specific assets to them through a court proceeding — the sons may no longer sell it. At that point the daughters have true ownership, and any subsequent changes in value, up or down, are theirs to bear.

*Tosafot*, however, disagrees. Even after *beit din* has formally allocated property to the daughters, the sons retain the ability to sell. The reason returns to the conceptual point above: what *beit din* did was not transfer ownership of a fixed debt that has now been discharged. Rather, it set aside property to fund an ongoing support obligation. Because the support is ongoing and never resolves into a single completed payment, the property is always, in a sense, still under the sons' control. The daughters have security and a guarantee of their support, but they do not hold title. According to *Tosafot*, therefore, there is no moment at which the daughters ever acquire full ownership of the designated amount; according to the *Rashbam*, formal court adjudication creates that moment.

## Widow & Creditors

### Widow's Impact

The *Gemara* now addresses a more complex configuration. Rabbi Yirmiya, seated before Rabbi Abbahu, asks: *almana mahu shetam'it benechasim?* — does a widow reduce the estate for purposes of the *merubim/muatim* classification? Suppose the deceased left an estate of twenty-five thousand dollars, and the daughters' support will cost twenty thousand dollars. At first glance this is *merubim* and the sons inherit five thousand. But the deceased also left a widow, whose ongoing support (*mezonot*) must be paid from the estate. If we estimate her support at ten thousand dollars, then taking her claim into account, there is nothing left for the sons at all. The question is: do we factor in the widow's entitlement at the outset, making this a *muatim* estate from the start? Or do we classify the estate without regard to the widow's claim, treat it as *merubim*, and then address her claim as a subsequent deduction — in which case the principle of *kvar zachu bahem yorshim* protects the sons' five thousand?

### Widow Uncertainty

The *Gemara* reasons as follows: the widow's support is inherently uncertain. She may remarry, at which point her entitlement to *mezonot* from this estate ceases entirely. Because her claim is contingent — *ilu minsava, leit lah*, were she to remarry she receives nothing — we do not factor it into the initial classification of the estate. The estate is assessed as *merubim* without deducting her potential entitlement. Her claim then arrives as a stage-two consideration, and the principle of *kvar zachu bahem yorshim* comes into play, protecting the sons' portion to some degree. *Tosafot* notes the difficulty: the daughters' entitlement is similarly uncertain in certain respects — they too may marry early, reducing the cost of their support — yet the daughters' claim is factored in from the outset. The *Gemara* does not fully resolve this tension; the distinction appears to rest on the widow's unique susceptibility to remarriage as a decisive event that terminates her claim entirely.

### Stepdaughter Obligation

The *Gemara* then asks the parallel question about a *bat ishto*, a stepdaughter from the wife's prior marriage. It was common practice for a man marrying a woman with children from a previous relationship to undertake in the *ketubah* a specific obligation to support her daughter for a fixed period — typically five years — after his death, regardless of whether the stepdaughter subsequently marries or reaches *bagrut*. This is a more definite obligation than either the widow's ongoing support or the daughters' ordinary entitlement, because the stepdaughter's support runs for the fixed contractual term without regard to marriage. The question is therefore: does her definite, marriage-proof entitlement reduce the estate from the outset?

The *Gemara* presents the argument in both directions. On one hand, *kivan d'chi minsava nami it lah* — even were she to marry she still receives her support — making this a virtually certain expenditure from the estate, which should reduce it from the outset. On the other hand, *kivan d'ei metah leit lah* — were she to die, she would not receive anything — so there remains some contingency, and perhaps we do not deduct her share initially either.

### External Creditor

The *Gemara* then asks the analogous question about a plain *ba'al chov*, an ordinary external creditor to whom the estate owes a fixed debt of, say, five thousand dollars. Does this debt reduce the estate for *merubim/muatim* purposes? The *Gemara* presents the argument for automatic deduction: *kivan d'chi mayet nami it leh* — even if the creditor himself dies, his heirs can collect the debt, making this an essentially certain expenditure. The counterargument: *kivan d'mechassar govayna* — since the debt has not yet been collected, it does not yet reduce the estate. The practical consequence of *not* reducing the estate initially is the same as in the previous cases: we classify the estate as *merubim*, the sons get their portion, and then the creditor's claim arrives as a secondary consideration governed by *kvar zachu bahem yorshim*.

### Reversal Framing

Strikingly, the *Gemara* presents the entire sequence of questions a second time in reverse order: starting from the *ba'al chov* and working up through the stepdaughter to the widow. This is not mere stylistic repetition. The direction of the questioning signals where the conclusion is being pushed. When the *Gemara* starts with the widow and escalates to the creditor, it seems to be reaching toward the conclusion that *none* of these reduce the estate — even the *ba'al chov* perhaps does not. When the *Gemara* starts with the creditor and escalates to the widow, it seems to be reaching toward the conclusion that *all* of these reduce the estate — even the widow perhaps does. The framing of the question reveals the direction of the intended *psak*, and the fact that both framings appear reflects the *Gemara*'s unresolved state on these questions.

Underlying all of this, as *Tosafot* emphasizes, is the principle of *nachala d'oraita*. The consistent thrust of refusing to deduct these various creditors from the initial estate assessment has the systematic effect of classifying more estates as *merubim* and triggering *kvar zachu bahem yorshim*, thereby ensuring that the sons retain some inheritance. This is not merely protecting the sons' financial interest; it is preserving the institution of Torah-mandated inheritance.

### Widow Priority

Rabbi Yirmiya had also posed a final question: as between the widow and the daughters in a *nechasim muatim* situation where the estate cannot fully support both, which claim takes precedence? Rabbi Abbahu said he had no immediate answer and asked Rabbi Yirmiya to return the following day. When Rabbi Yirmiya returned, Rabbi Abbahu reported that he had at least one clear ruling, citing Rabbi Abba citing Rabbi Assi: *asu almana etzel habat k'bat etzel ha'achin benechasim mu'atin* — they made the widow vis-à-vis the daughter as the daughter is vis-à-vis the sons in a small estate. Just as in a *muatim* estate the daughters are supported and the sons go begging, so too the widow is supported and the daughters go begging. The widow's claim takes priority over the daughters'.

The reasoning follows from the posture of the man at the time of marriage. When he undertakes obligations in the *ketubah*, he is committing to this woman standing before him. The daughters are as yet unborn; his present commitment is to his wife. It also reflects the practical reality that daughters are young and have a longer horizon for marriage and eventual self-sufficiency, while a widow — particularly an older one — may have fewer such prospects. Whatever the underlying rationale, the ruling is clear: the widow is fed first, and if nothing remains, the daughters must seek support elsewhere.

## Admon's Argument

### Admon's Torah Plea

The *mishna* records Admon's complaint: *bishvil she'ani zachar hifsadeti?* — just because I am male I lose out? In the *nechasim muatim* scenario, the daughters are supported and the sons receive nothing. Admon objects on the grounds that this result is unjust. The *Gemara* asks: what exactly is Admon's argument? Abaye initially explains it as follows: *bishvil she'ani zachar vera'ui ani la'asok baTorah hifsadeti?* — because I am male and suited for Torah study, I should lose out? The claim is that a male's spiritual superiority — his capacity for *talmud Torah* — entitles him to equal support even in a small estate.

### Rava Rejects Spirit

Rava immediately dismisses this reading. *Man de'asik baTorah hu deyaris, udela asik baTorah lo yaris?* — do you think inheritance rights depend on who is actually engaged in Torah learning? If so, one might argue that among three sons, only the one who studies in *yeshiva* should inherit while his brothers receive nothing. Spiritual attainment does not determine property rights. The *Gemara* emphatically rejects any conflation of religious virtue with legal entitlement in *yerusha*. Whatever Torah learning confers in terms of religious standing, it has no operative force in the law of inheritance.

### Privilege Argument

Rava then offers what he considers Admon's real argument: *bishvil she'ani zachar vera'ui ani lirash benechasim merubim, hifsadeti benechasim mu'atim?* — because I am male and therefore privileged to inherit the bulk of a large estate, should I lose out entirely in a small estate? This is a strictly legal argument, not a spiritual one. The system of inheritance explicitly favors males. In a large estate, the sons inherit the substantial surplus while daughters receive only their fixed support entitlement. The system is designed to advantage males. Given that the law privileges males in the large-estate scenario, Admon finds it incongruous that males receive no protection whatsoever in the small-estate scenario.

### Counter: Different Rights

The response to Admon is that having different rights in different contexts is not inherently contradictory. Males are privileged in large estates, but females have protected support in small estates. The asymmetry is explicable: males have greater capacity to earn a living, to go out into the marketplace, to knock on doors and find means of sustenance. Females in that society were not expected to do so and therefore required a protected legal entitlement to support. The two scenarios present different considerations that yield different results — not a logical inconsistency, but a coherent system in which distinct interests receive distinct protections depending on circumstances. That males sometimes win greatly and sometimes receive nothing does not make the system unfair on its own terms.

### Tosafot vs. Rashbam

There remains a dispute as to what Admon's precise standing is in *halacha*. The *Rashbam* rules that the *halacha* follows Admon, meaning that in a *muatim* scenario the sons do receive some support alongside the daughters. *Tosafot* disagrees, arguing that the *Gemara* consistently ignores Admon's position when analyzing the various *muatim* scenarios, and therefore we do not rule like him. In *Tosafot*'s reading, Admon is not making a binding *halachic* claim at all; he is simply drawing attention to the irony — pointing out that the privileged party in one context becomes the disadvantaged party in another — without asserting that the law should be otherwise.

## Tumtum & Gifts

### Tumtum Definition

The *mishna* now introduces cases involving a *tumtum* and an *androgynous*. These are distinct categories. An *androgynous* has both male and female genitalia and presents an irreducible legal ambiguity. A *tumtum* is different: the genitalia have either not descended or are obscured, so that sex is presently indeterminate, but the presumption is that only one set exists — we simply do not yet know which. Both create legal complexity in the context of inheritance.

### Tumtum Strategy

In a large estate — *nechasim merubim* — a *tumtum* finds himself caught between adversarial classifications. The sons want to classify him as female, in which case he gets only the daughters' limited support entitlement and does not inherit alongside them. The daughters want to classify him as male, in which case he is grouped with the sons and his claim comes from the sons' portion rather than from their support allotment. The *tumtum*'s maximum recovery in either case is bounded by *mima nafshach* reasoning: whatever he might be, his claim against the estate cannot exceed the lesser of what a daughter or a son would receive.

### Tumtum vs. Support

In a *nechasim muatim* situation, the *tumtum* fares worse. He cannot prove he is female, and the daughters reject his claim for support: *hanekeiva docha oto etzel zcharim* — the females push him toward the males. Since he cannot establish female status, he has no support entitlement and ends up in the unenviable position of the sons — receiving nothing and having to seek support elsewhere.

### Conditional Gifts

The *mishna* then takes up the case of conditional gifts to an unborn child. A father declares: if my wife gives birth to a boy, let him receive one hundred *maneh*; if a girl, two hundred *maneh*. The *mishna* establishes that if a boy is born, he receives one hundred, and if a girl, she receives two hundred. The substantive novelty here is that such gifts are effective at all — that a *kinyan* can be effected on behalf of an *ubar*, an unborn child. The asymmetry — more money designated for a daughter than a son — is also notable, appearing immediately after Admon's argument and implicitly reflecting the view that a daughter may need greater protection.

### Twins Disagreement

The *mishna* further provides: *im zachar maneh im nekeiva masayim, veyalda zachar unekeiva* — if the declaration covers both possibilities, and the wife delivers both a boy and a girl, then both conditions are met and each child collects the designated amount. The *Rashbam*, however, disagrees with this reading. In his view, if a man says "if a boy, a hundred; if a girl, two hundred," he is assuming a single child. The birth of twins was unforeseen, and since his conditional gift was predicated on a single birth, neither condition was met and neither child collects anything. The *Rashbam* reads the *vov* in the phrase *zachar unekeiva* as disjunctive — meaning "or" rather than "and" — rendering the *mishna* as simply saying that whichever sex the single child turns out to be, that child takes the designated amount.

### Tumtum Own Category

Finally, the *mishna* asks: what if a *tumtum* is born? One might reason that the child should at least receive the lesser of the two amounts — the hundred designated for a boy — on the principle of *mima nafshach*. The *mishna* rules otherwise: *eino notel* — the *tumtum* receives nothing. This ruling reflects the position that a *tumtum* is *beriya bifnei atzmo* — its own independent category of being, not a *safek* oscillating between male and female. If the *tumtum* were merely a factual uncertainty — we do not know whether this person is male or female — then logically the child should at minimum receive the lesser amount. But if the *tumtum* is treated as a distinct category altogether, then neither condition set by the father has been satisfied: the condition "if male" was not met, and the condition "if female" was not met. The child is something else entirely, and both gifts fail. This is a striking position — one that, as the *Gemara* will continue to explore, resonates in unexpected ways with contemporary thinking about biological sex as non-binary — and we will pick up with its further ramifications tomorrow.
