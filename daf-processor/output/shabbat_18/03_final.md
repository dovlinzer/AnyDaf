# Shabbat 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 18 Things Debates

### Context & Rambam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁוִין אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ, שֶׁטּוֹעֲנִין קוֹרַת בֵּית הַבַּד וְעִגּוּלֵי הַגַּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And,** however, **these,** Beit Shammai, **and those,** Beit Hillel, **agree that,** *ab initio*, **one may load the beam of the olive press** on the olives on Shabbat eve while it is still day, so that the oil will continue to be squeezed out of the olives on Shabbat. So too, one may load the **circular wine press** to accelerate the process of producing wine from the grapes.

We begin today on 17b, at the *mishnah* located approximately ten lines from the bottom of the page. At this point in the tractate, we have concluded several *dapim* dealing with the eighteen things debated by *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* — material that had almost nothing to do with *Shabbos* and was concerned primarily with *terumah* and *taharah*. We now return to *Shabbos* proper, and encounter a series of debates between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* that are specifically *Shabbos*-related.

It is worth noting at the outset that the *Rambam*, reading through both the *Yerushalmi* and the *Bavli*, understood that the original eighteen things were matters on which the schools debated and then reached agreement, all gathered into a single list. The *Yerushalmi*, however, speaks of three distinct lists. The *Rambam* follows this approach and identifies the debates we are about to study as a third list of eighteen things — matters that remained points of ongoing disagreement between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*.

## 18 Things Debates (II)

### Soaking & Dyeing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין שׁוֹרִין דְּיוֹ וְסַמָּנִים וְכַרְשִׁינִין אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּשּׁוֹרוּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין נוֹתְנִין אוּנִּין שֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן לְתוֹךְ הַתַּנּוּר אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּהְבִּילוּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, וְלֹא אֶת הַצֶּמֶר לַיּוֹרָה אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּקְלוֹט הָעַיִן, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In this mishna there is a fundamental dispute between Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai: Must one begin refraining from actions prohibited on Shabbat on Shabbat eve? Or, may one initiate an action prior to Shabbat, even if he knows that it will continue on its own on Shabbat itself? These are the details of that dispute: **Beit Shammai say: One may only soak** dry **ink** in water **and** dry plants, which produce **dyes,** in water **and vetch** for animal food to soften them in water on Shabbat eve, adjacent to Shabbat, **if** there is clearly **sufficient time for them to soak** for their designated purpose **while it is still day,** before Shabbat begins, and their continued soaking on Shabbat will have no effect. **And Beit Hillel permit** doing so. **Beit Shammai say: One may only place bundles of** combed **flax inside the oven** on Shabbat eve if there is sufficient time **so that they will be heated while it is still day. And** one may **only** place **wool into the** dyer's **kettle if** there is **sufficient time for** the wool **to absorb the dye** while it is still day. **And Beit Hillel permit** doing so.

The *mishnah* opens: *Beit Shammai* says one may not soak ink, dyes, or *karshinin* (vetch — a legume used as animal feed) unless they can complete their absorption before the day is out. *Beit Hillel* permits it.

What *Beit Shammai* is establishing, and what we will see repeated across a series of cases, is that one may not initiate a process before *Shabbos* that will continue on *Shabbos* itself — unless that process can be completed while it is still the day before. *Beit Hillel* disagrees and permits beginning such processes even if they continue into *Shabbos*.

One cannot take this position to its extreme, of course — for then one could never place food on a fire before *Shabbos* to cook on *Shabbos*, nor keep candles burning. The lines of distinction will need to be carefully drawn. Already at this stage, it is worth noting that there are two possible explanations for *Beit Shammai*'s position. The first is a standard rabbinic concern: if one begins something before *Shabbos* and it continues into *Shabbos*, this either leads to actually performing *melacha* on *Shabbos*, or at minimum creates an appearance problem — it looks as though one is engaged in *melacha* on *Shabbos*, since the activity is visibly happening for one's benefit throughout the day. The second possible explanation is something the *Gemara* calls *shevisas keilim* — the obligation to allow one's vessels to rest on *Shabbos*, analogous to the requirement that one's animals and servants rest. This could potentially be a biblical concern, not merely a rabbinic one. These two frameworks — rabbinic appearances-based concern versus biblical *shevisas keilim* — will drive the *Gemara*'s analysis throughout this *daf*.

## Beit Shammai Rules

### Ink & Dyes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין פּוֹרְסִין מְצוּדוֹת חַיָּה וְעוֹפוֹת וְדָגִים אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּצּוֹדוּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹכְרִין לְגוֹי וְאֵין טוֹעֲנִין עִמּוֹ וְאֵין מַגְבִּיהִין עָלָיו אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְמָקוֹם קָרוֹב, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין נוֹתְנִין עוֹרוֹת לְעַבְּדָן, וְלֹא כֵּלִים לְכוֹבֵס גּוֹי אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּעָשׂוּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם. וּבְכוּלָּן בֵּית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין עִם
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai say: One may spread traps** for an **animal and birds and fish only** if there is sufficient time remaining in the day **for them to be trapped** in them **while it is still day, and Beit Hillel permit** doing so even if there is not sufficient time remaining in the day. **Beit Shammai say: One may only sell** an item **to a gentile** on Shabbat eve, **and** one **may only load** a burden on his donkey **with him, and** one **may only lift** a burden **on him if** there remains **sufficient time for** the gentile **to arrive to a near place** prior to Shabbat, and the Jew will play no role in the performance of a prohibited labor by the gentile on Shabbat. **And Beit Hillel permit** doing so. **Beit Shammai say: One may not give skins to a** gentile **tanner, nor clothes to a gentile launderer, unless** there is **sufficient time for** work on **them to be completed while it is still day,** before Shabbat begins. **And in all of them Beit Hillel permit** doing so **with**

The *mishnah* continues with parallel cases. *Beit Shammai* says one may not place bundles of flax into an oven unless they have time to be heated through before *Shabbos*. The process involved is a whitening of the flax — part of the preparation of linen — achieved by exposing the bundles to sustained heat. Similarly, *Beit Shammai* says one may not place wool into a vat of dye unless it has time to absorb the dye before *Shabbos*. In each case, *Beit Hillel* permits it.

### Traps

*Beit Shammai* adds: one may not set traps for wild animals, birds, or fish before *Shabbos* unless the animals could have been caught from the day before. *Beit Hillel* permits it. This case is somewhat different from the others, since trapping is inherently uncertain — one never knows whether an animal will be caught. One reading of *Beit Shammai*'s requirement is that there must have been sufficient time, in principle, for the catch to occur before *Shabbos*. *Tosafos*, however, struggles with the plain sense of the text, suggesting perhaps that the scenario envisions a case where capture is virtually certain. This remains somewhat difficult on the straightforward reading of the *mishnah*.

### Non-Jew Sales

*Beit Shammai* states: one may not sell to a non-Jew, load merchandise onto his donkey together with him, or even hoist it onto his back, unless the non-Jew can reach a nearby destination while it is still the day before *Shabbos*. *Beit Hillel* permits it.

This case is qualitatively different from the previous ones. Here, once the sale is completed, the seller is entirely out of the picture — the goods belong to the non-Jew, carried on his own donkey, going to his own destination. Yet *Beit Shammai* requires that he reach his destination before *Shabbos*. The concern here is transparently one of appearances: it looks as though the Jew has dispatched the non-Jew on an errand on his behalf, even if that is not what has occurred. *Rashi* explains *kedei she-yagia l'makom karov* to mean that the destination must be close enough for the non-Jew to reach it while it is still day.

This case illustrates that *Beit Shammai*'s concern extends even to situations involving one's own *melacha* not at all — where the only issue is how things appear. *Beit Hillel*, by contrast, permits it entirely. In a case involving an actual Jew, one assumes the Jew will complete his activity before *Shabbos*; if not, the concern shifts to *lifnei iver*.

### Tanners & Cleaners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: נוֹהֲגִין הָיוּ בֵּית אַבָּא שֶׁהָיוּ נוֹתְנִין כְּלֵי לָבָן לְכוֹבֵס גּוֹי שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים קוֹדֶם לַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **the sun,** i.e., as long as the sun is shining on Friday. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: The** ancestral **house of** my **father,** the dynasty of *Nesi'im* from the house of Hillel, **was accustomed to give** its **white clothes to a gentile launderer** no fewer than **three days before Shabbat.**

*Beit Shammai* continues: one may not give leather to a tanner, nor clothing to a non-Jewish cleaner, unless the work can be completed before *Shabbos*. *Beit Hillel* permits it — and indeed, *Beit Hillel* permits it even if the item is handed over immediately before sunset.

This case is arguably more striking than the non-Jew sale case, because here the non-Jew genuinely will be performing labor on one's behalf on *Shabbos* — laundering one's clothes, or working on one's leather. And yet *Beit Hillel* permits it. The question of why *Beit Hillel* is so permissive here is a significant one with direct *halachic* relevance, and it will require further analysis. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* adds that in his father's household — the *nasi*'s household — they would give white garments to a non-Jewish cleaner three full days before *Shabbos*, so that there would be ample time for the work to be completed by *Shabbos*, in keeping with the stricter *Beit Shammai* practice.

## Agreed Cases

### Oil & Wine Vats

Notwithstanding all their disagreements, both schools agree that one may lower the beam of an oil press and the circular weights of a wine vat before *Shabbos*, even though the pressing will continue throughout *Shabbos*. The process is familiar from archaeological sites throughout Israel: enormous beams or heavy stones are lowered onto crushed olives or grapes, and their weight slowly presses out the oil or juice over many hours. Both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* permit initiating this process before *Shabbos*.

This agreement immediately raises the central questions that will occupy the *Gemara*: what is *Beit Shammai*'s reason for being strict about all the earlier cases, and why does he permit this one? And more broadly, how do we permit the obvious *Shabbos* staples — keeping lights burning and keeping food cooking — given *Beit Shammai*'s concerns?

## Melacha of Lisha

### Rav Yosef Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תְּנָא נְתִינַת מַיִם לִדְיוֹ זוֹ הִיא שְׁרִיָּיתָן? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַבִּי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד נוֹתֵן אֶת הַקֶּמַח וְאֶחָד נוֹתֵן אֶת הַמַּיִם — הָאַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיְּגַבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Before clarifying the matters themselves, the Gemara seeks to determine: **Who is the *tanna*** who holds that merely **adding water to ink** without any additional action **constitutes its soaking,** and one is liable for doing so on Shabbat, as he performed an act of kneading, one of the primary categories of labor? **Rav Yosef said: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where **one** person **adds the flour and** another **one adds the water** into one vessel, **the latter one is liable** for kneading the dough, which is a prohibited labor on Shabbat, even though he did not actually knead the dough; that is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Yosei says: He is not liable** for the prohibited labor of kneading **until he** actually **kneads** the dough. According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, merely soaking the dough in water is considered a prohibited labor.

Before engaging the broader questions, the *Gemara* first addresses the local issue of the ink. The *Gemara* asks: who is the *tanna* who holds that putting water into the ink mixture constitutes *sheriyasan* — that is, that the very act of adding the water is itself the prohibited action? The underlying assumption of the question is that the activities *Beit Shammai* forbids initiating before *Shabbos* must be ones that, if performed on *Shabbos* itself, would constitute *melacha*. So the *Gemara* is asking: what *melacha* is involved when one adds water to the raw materials for ink?

This is a surprising question, because one might have expected the answer to be *tzvia* — dyeing — since the ink, after all, is a coloring substance. But the *Gemara* understands that one is not really dyeing anything when one makes ink; one is coloring water, not a textile or other material. The *Gemara* instead identifies the operative *melacha* as *lisha* — kneading. The logic is that kneading involves combining small discrete particles with liquid to produce a homogeneous mass. Making ink involves exactly this: combining some material with water to form a unified substance. The parallel to making dough is clear in structure, even if the end product is far more liquid.

*Rav Yosef* answers that the *tanna* must be *Rebbi*, and cites a *baraisa* to make his case.

### Rebbi Dispute

The *baraisa* cited by *Rav Yosef* states: if one person adds flour and another adds water, the one who added the water is *chayav* — liable — for the *melacha* of *lisha*. This is the position of *Rebbi*. *Rebbi Yosei*, however, disagrees: one is not liable until one actually kneads. Merely adding water to flour is insufficient; the kneading itself is the constitutive act.

The *Gemara* understands this debate to be directly relevant to the ink case, since when making ink one adds water but does not knead. If *Rebbi Yosei* requires actual kneading, then no *melacha* occurs by adding water alone, and *Beit Shammai*'s prohibition would lack a *melacha* basis. Therefore, the *tanna* who grounds the prohibition in a *melacha* concern must be *Rebbi*.

### Abaiye Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְדִילְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אֶלָּא בְּקֶמַח, דְּבַר גִּיבּוּל הוּא. אֲבָל דְּיוֹ דְּלָאו בַּר גִּיבּוּל הוּא, אֵימָא לִיחַיַּיב! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד נוֹתֵן אֶת הָאֵפֶר וְאֶחָד נוֹתֵן אֶת הַמַּיִם — הָאַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיְּגַבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **And perhaps Rabbi Yosei only stated** that actual kneading is required to be liable for performing the prohibited labor of kneading **in** the case of **flour, which can be kneaded; however, ink, which cannot be kneaded, say** that its soaking is considered a full-fledged prohibited labor, and **he will** therefore **be liable,** even according to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei. The Gemara rejects this: **It should not enter your mind** to say so, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where **one places the ashes and one adds the water, the latter one is liable,** although he did not knead them. That is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** He is not liable **until he** actually **kneads** them. Apparently, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, he is only liable for committing the prohibited labor of kneading on Shabbat if he actually kneads the mixture, as he stated his *halakha* even with regard to ashes, which cannot be kneaded.

*Abaye* challenges this reasoning. Perhaps, he suggests, *Rebbi Yosei* only requires kneading in the case of flour, since flour is normally made into dough through kneading — kneading is part of its natural process. But ink, which is not normally kneaded, may be different: perhaps even *Rebbi Yosei* would agree that adding water suffices to constitute *lisha* for a substance whose preparation does not ordinarily involve kneading.

The *Gemara* rejects this suggestion by citing a *baraisa* that explicitly discusses ashes and water: one who adds water to ashes is liable according to *Rebbi*, while *Rebbi Yosei the son of Rebbi Yehuda* requires kneading even there. This *baraisa* appears to demonstrate that even with a non-flour substance — one not normally kneaded — *Rebbi Yosei* still demands actual kneading for liability. The *Gemara* then considers whether *efer* in this *baraisa* might mean dirt (*afar*) rather than ashes, since dirt is something that is normally worked with water into clay or plaster and thus would normally be kneaded, making *Rebbi Yosei*'s requirement of kneading more understandable. But the *Gemara* notes that there are two separate *baraisos* — one says *efer* and one says *afar* — and *Rashi* explains that they derive from different schools (*Rebbi Chiya* and *Rebbi Oshaya* respectively), and so the identical word need not bear identical meaning across independent sources. They may both refer to dirt, with one simply using a less precise term.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא מַאי ״אֵפֶר״ — עָפָר דְּבַר גִּיבּוּל הוּא?! וְהָתַנְיָא ״אֵפֶר״, וְהָתַנְיָא ״עָפָר״! מִידֵּי גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי תַּנְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And perhaps, what is** the meaning of **ashes [*efer*]** mentioned here? Perhaps it is **soil [*afar*], which can be kneaded.** In that case he is not liable until he actually kneads the mixture. However, with regard to ashes, which cannot be kneaded, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, also holds that even if he did not actually knead the mixture he is liable. The Gemara rejects this: **Wasn't** the dispute **taught** in one *baraita* with regard to **ashes, and wasn't it taught** in another *baraita* with regard to **soil?** In both cases, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, disagreed. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Were they taught next to each other?** Had both of these *baraitot* been taught together, it would have been truly possible to arrive at the conclusion that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, disagrees in both cases. However, since the *baraita* that speaks about ashes was taught elsewhere by a different *amora* who cited it in the name of Rabbi Yosei, the difference in language does not prove that Rabbi Yosei disagrees in both cases.

The practical dimensions of this debate are considerable. The parameters of the *melacha* of *lisha* remain actively relevant: Does adding water to oatmeal constitute *lisha*? What about substances that one normally stirs but does not truly knead? The concept of *bar gibul* — whether the substance is of the type that is normally kneaded — is central. There is a question among the *rishonim* whether for substances not normally kneaded, liability arises earlier (as *Rebbi* holds) or whether, to the contrary, it is harder to transgress since the act is less defined. *Tosafos* cites other *Gemaras* suggesting that if a substance is not normally kneaded, one does not transgress the *melacha* at all.

One notable contemporary application: *Rav Moshe* was asked whether adding mayonnaise to tuna fish constitutes *lisha*. The argument in favor of permitting it was that the mayonnaise functions as an adhesive rather than a transformative liquid — it causes pieces to stick together without chemically changing them into a unified mass. There is a compelling logic to this distinction. Nevertheless, *Rav Moshe*'s ruling was to be stringent. A reasonable practice is to spread the mayonnaise directly onto the bread rather than mixing it into the tuna, thereby being *machmir* in accordance with *Rav Moshe*'s position while acknowledging the underlying logic of leniency.

## Permitted Activities

### Water & Incense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פּוֹתְקִין מַיִם לַגִּינָּה עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה וּמִתְמַלֵּאת וְהוֹלֶכֶת כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, וּמַנִּיחִין מוּגְמָר תַּחַת הַכֵּלִים עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת וּמִתְגַּמְּרִין וְהוֹלְכִין כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, וּמַנִּיחִין גׇּפְרִית תַּחַת הַכֵּלִים עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה וּמִתְגַּפְּרִין וְהוֹלְכִין כׇּל הַשַּׁבָּת כּוּלָּהּ, וּמַנִּיחִין קִילוֹר עַל גַּבֵּי הָעַיִן וְאִיסְפְּלָנִית עַל גַּבֵּי מַכָּה עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה וּמִתְרַפֵּאת וְהוֹלֶכֶת כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ. אֲבָל אֵין נוֹתְנִין חִטִּין לְתוֹךְ הָרֵיחַיִם שֶׁל מַיִם אֶלָּא בִּכְדֵי שֶׁיִּטָּחֲנוּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: **One may open** a canal that passes adjacent to a garden **on Shabbat eve at nightfall,** so that **water** will flow **into a garden** and the garden **continuously fills** with water **all day long** on Shabbat. **Similarly, one may place incense,** perfumed herbs placed on coals to produce a fragrance, on coals **beneath the clothes on Shabbat eve and** the clothes **may be continuously perfumed all day long. And,** similarly, **one may place sulfur beneath the** silver **vessels** on **Shabbat eve at nightfall** for the purpose of coloring the vessels, **and they may be continuously** exposed to **sulfur all day long. And one may place an eye salve [*kilor*] on the eye and a bandage [*ispelanit*]** smeared with cream **on a wound** on **Shabbat eve at nightfall, and** the wound **may continuously heal all day long** on Shabbat. **However, one may not place wheat kernels into the water mill unless** he does so in a way **so that they will be ground while it is still day** on Friday and not on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* cites a *baraisa* listing activities that may be initiated before *Shabbos* and permitted to continue throughout *Shabbos*. One may open an irrigation channel into one's garden immediately before dark on *erev Shabbos*, and the water may continue flowing into the garden throughout the entire day. One may place burning incense beneath clothing before *Shabbos*, and the clothes may continue absorbing the aroma throughout the day — a common practice before the availability of deodorants, whereby garments were perfumed by exposing them to fragrant smoke. Similarly, one may burn sulfur beneath silver vessels so that the blackened designs become visible; one may apply a salve to the eye; and one may apply a medicated bandage to a wound — all before *Shabbos*, with the healing or treatment continuing throughout *Shabbos*.

### Watermill Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּשְׁמַעַת קוֹל. אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וְלֵימָא מָר מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִיתַת כֵּלִים! דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר אָמַרְתִּי אֲלֵיכֶם תִּשָּׁמֵרוּ״ — לְרַבּוֹת שְׁבִיתַת כֵּלִים. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִיתַת כֵּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that the *baraita* prohibited a mill and permitted other prohibited labor? **Rabba said: Because it makes noise** and the public will hear the mill grinding on Shabbat. Although no prohibited labor is being performed, doing so displays contempt for Shabbat. Therefore, the Sages prohibited it. **Rav Yosef said to** Rabba: **And let the Master say** a better reason, **due to the** obligation to ensure the **resting of utensils.** Even the utensils of a Jewish person may not be used for prohibited labor on Shabbat. **As it was taught** in halakhic midrash, the *Mekhilta*: That which is stated: **"And in all that I said to you, take heed"** (Exodus 23:13), is an allusion to matters mentioned in the Oral Torah. It comes **to include the resting of utensils** on Shabbat. **Rather, Rav Yosef said:** The reason for the prohibition of the mill on Shabbat is **due to the resting of utensils.**

The *baraisa* contains one notable exception: one may not place wheat into a water mill before *Shabbos* unless the grinding can be completed while it is still day. The obvious question is why this case differs from all the others in the *baraisa*, all of which are permitted.

*Rabbah* answers: *mipnei she-mashmi'a kol* — because the mill makes noise. *Rashi* explains that the noise publicizes the activity and constitutes a degradation of *Shabbos* — it is inappropriate for the day. This connects to a broader concept attested in the *Yerushalmi*, where quiet on *Shabbos* is itself presented as a significant value. The *Yerushalmi* even relates that one of the *tanna'im* heard knocking on a door on *Shabbos* and exclaimed that someone was violating *Shabbos* — so central was the association between *Shabbos* and silence. This approach does not appear to be the general orientation of the *Bavli*, however.

Even within the *Bavli*'s framework, there are two ways of understanding the *mashmi'a kol* concern. One reading is that the noise must be unusually loud — the grinding of a water mill impacts the entire surrounding area and creates a pervasive impression that commerce and labor are being conducted on *Shabbos*. A second, more specific reading is that the particular sound of a mill in operation signals business activity in a way that other ambient sounds do not.

The practical stakes of this question are real. *Tosafos* raises the question of whether we rule *l'halacha* in accordance with the concern of *mashmi'a kol*. If we do, then questions arise about the parameters. Setting a dishwasher on a timer to run on *Shabbos*, for instance — is this a violation of *mashmi'a kol*? *Rav Soloveitchik* apparently held it is not problematic. If the concern is strictly about volume, most dishwashers are not particularly loud. If the concern is about the experiential impression that *melacha* is being performed, that is a broader standard — though even then, the *Shabbos* clock that switches on lights has become normalized, and one does not experience that as the performance of *melacha*. The comparison between a household appliance and a water mill grinding through the night is not trivial; the mill was an industrial operation whose noise permeated an entire community in a way no modern domestic device approaches.

## Shevisas Keilim

### Rav Yosef Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ לְבֵית הִלֵּל אִית לְהוּ שְׁבִיתַת כֵּלִים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, גׇּפְרִית וּמוּגְמָר מַאי טַעְמָא שָׁרוּ? מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא קָעָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה. אוּנִּין שֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן מַאי טַעְמָא שָׁרוּ — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא עָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה, וּמֵינָח נַיְיחָא. מְצוּדַת חַיָּה וְעוֹף וְדָגִים דְּקָא עָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה, מַאי טַעְמָא שָׁרוּ? הָתָם נָמֵי בְּלֶחִי וְקוּקְרֵי דְּלָא קָעָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** Since the obligation of resting utensils on Shabbat was mentioned, the Gemara says: **Now** that **you said** that **Beit Hillel also hold** that **resting utensils** on Shabbat is required **by Torah law,** with regard to **sulfur and incense** on coals that are placed under silver vessels and clothes, respectively, **what is the reason that** the Sages **permitted** this on Shabbat? Isn't that performed on Shabbat in utensils? The Gemara answers: **Because** the utensil itself **does not perform an action** when the incense or sulfur is burning. With regard to the **bundles of flax, what is the reason that they permitted** placing them in the oven on Shabbat eve at nightfall to dry, even though the oven is performing a prohibited labor on Shabbat? **Because it does not perform an action;** rather, on the contrary, **it sits idle** in its place and the prohibited labor occurs on its own. However, with regard to **traps of an animal, and a bird, and a fish, which perform a** bona fide **action** of trapping, **what is the reason that they permitted** spreading them on Shabbat eve at nightfall? The Gemara explains: **There too,** it is referring **to a fish hook and nets [*kokrei*], which perform no action.** They stand in place, and the fish comes to them and is trapped. Indeed, a trap that performs an action is prohibited.

*Rav Yosef* challenges the *mashmi'a kol* explanation. Why invoke a noise-based rationale? Why not say instead that the water mill is forbidden because of *shevisas keilim*? The *Gemara* cites a *baraisa* in support: the verse *u-ve-chol asher amarti aleikhem tishameru* — "and everything I have told you, observe" — appears in a context where the *Torah* has commanded rest for one's animals. The word *u-ve-chol*, "and everything," is understood to broaden that obligation, including a requirement that one's vessels also rest on *Shabbos*.

If *shevisas keilim* is a valid biblical concern, then *Rav Yosef* proposes that this, rather than *mashmi'a kol*, is why the water mill is forbidden. The water mill is a vessel in motion performing labor on *Shabbos* — paradigmatically subject to *shevisas keilim*.

*Abaye* immediately pushes back: if *shevisas keilim* is invoked to explain the water mill prohibition, and if we are operating within the framework of *Beit Hillel* — as we presumably are, since we rule like *Beit Hillel* — then one must explain how *Beit Hillel* permits all the other cases in the *baraisa* and in the *mishnah*, all of which involve vessels of some kind. To say *Beit Hillel* holds of *shevisas keilim* creates an enormous explanatory burden.

*Rav Yosef* attempts to resolve this by defining *shevisas keilim* narrowly: the prohibition applies only when the vessel is physically in motion — when it has moving parts actively performing work. A bowl holding incense, or an oven holding flax, is passive; the vessel is stationary and is not itself performing the *melacha*. The water mill, by contrast, has a wheel in continuous rotation — it is actively working. Under this reading, *Beit Hillel* accepts *shevisas keilim* in its narrow form and that is why the mill is forbidden, while everything else is permitted because those vessels are inert.

The *Gemara* works through this framework case by case. The incense and the sulfur involve no moving vessel — just fire and the material itself. The flax in the oven: the oven is stationary, no vessel moves. The animal traps: *Rashi* explains that the reference is to stationary traps such as a hook (*lechi*) or a net — the fish bites the hook, the hook does not move; the net is spread but does not itself move to capture the fish. Under this very narrow reading, *Beit Hillel*'s *shevisas keilim* can be maintained while explaining virtually every permitted case. The one case that does not fit this framework — the moving wheel of the water mill — is exactly what the *shevisas keilim* prohibition targets.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: מַאן תְּנָא שְׁבִיתַת כֵּלִים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא וְלָא בֵּית הִלֵּל, לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי בֵּין קָעָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה בֵּין דְּלָא קָעָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה — אָסוּר. לְבֵית הִלֵּל אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָעָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה — שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And now that Rav Oshaya said** that **Rav Asi said: Who is the *tanna*** who states that the obligation of **resting utensils** on Shabbat **is by Torah law?** The *tanna* **is Beit Shammai and not Beit Hillel.** Consequently, according **to Beit Shammai, whether** the utensil **performs an action or whether it does not perform an action, it is prohibited. And** according **to Beit Hillel, even though it performs an action, it is** nevertheless **permitted.**

The *Gemara* then cites the view of *Rav Oshaya* in the name of *Rav Assi*: who is the *tanna* that holds *shevisas keilim* is *d'oraisa*? It is *Beit Shammai* — not *Beit Hillel*. This resolves the difficulty entirely. *Beit Shammai* holds *shevisas keilim* biblically, and prohibits all the cases in the *mishnah* on that basis — whether the vessel is moving or stationary, since even a passive vessel participating in a *melacha* is subject to the obligation. *Beit Hillel* does not hold of *shevisas keilim* at all. Under this reading, *Beit Hillel* permits all those cases freely, and the water mill is a separate matter entirely — forbidden not because of *shevisas keilim* but because of *mashmi'a kol*.

This is the clean resolution: *Beit Shammai* and *shevisas keilim*; *Beit Hillel* and *mashmi'a kol* as a distinct, limited rabbinic concern for the water mill specifically.

### Appearances View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּגְמָר וְגׇפְרִית מַאי טַעְמָא שָׁרוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי? הָתָם מַנַּח אַאַרְעָא. גִּיגִית וְנֵר וּקְדֵרָה וְשַׁפּוּד מַאי טַעְמָא שָׁרוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי? דְּמַפְקַר לְהוּ אַפְקוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** with regard to **placing incense and sulfur** beneath clothes and silver vessels, respectively, **what is the reason Beit Shammai permitted** this? The Gemara answers: The case under discussion was not one where the incense was placed in a vessel; rather, **there,** the incense **was placed on the ground,** and therefore there was no utensil that was obligated to rest. The Gemara asks further: **A tub** in which fruit or grains are placed to ferment into beer, and where they stay for an extended period; **and a** Shabbat **lamp; and a pot** in which food is being cooked, which they place on the fire while it is still day; **and a spit [*shapud*]** on which they placed food to roast while it is still day; **what is the reason Beit Shammai permitted** placing them on Shabbat eve while it is still day even though the prohibited labor continues over time, including on Shabbat? The Gemara answers: These are cases **where he declares** the utensils **ownerless.** According to Beit Shammai, the utensils must be declared ownerless while it is still day. Once the utensils are declared ownerless, they no longer belong to a Jew and, consequently, there is no obligation to let them rest.

*Tosafos* notes that before the *Gemara* introduced *shevisas keilim*, there was a different, simpler way of understanding *Beit Shammai*'s position — one that the *Gemara* was implicitly operating with initially. Under that reading, *Beit Shammai*'s concern is purely rabbinic: it looks as though one is performing *melacha* on *Shabbos*, or is involved in *melacha* being performed on *Shabbos*, whether through one's own vessel or through a non-Jew acting on one's behalf. This approach is more internally consistent with the non-Jew cases — the tanner and the cleaner — where there is no vessel of one's own involved at all, and the only issue is one of appearances. The *shevisas keilim* framework, by contrast, struggles to account for those cases without additional machinery.

So *Beit Shammai*'s position admits of two quite different characterizations: either a rabbinic, appearances-based concern of general application, or a biblical *shevisas keilim* obligation. These represent fundamentally different levels of stringency with very different legal implications.

## Cooking & Vessels

### Mafkir Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תְּנָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא תְּמַלֵּא אִשָּׁה קְדֵרָה עֲסָסִיּוֹת וְתוּרְמְסִין וְתַנִּיחַ לְתוֹךְ הַתַּנּוּר עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת עִם חֲשֵׁכָה, וְאִם נְתָנָן — לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת אֲסוּרִין בִּכְדֵי שֶׁיֵּעָשׂוּ. כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ: לֹא יְמַלֵּא נַחְתּוֹם חָבִית שֶׁל מַיִם וְיַנִּיחַ לְתוֹךְ הַתַּנּוּר עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת עִם חֲשֵׁכָה, וְאִם עָשָׂה כֵּן — לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת אֲסוּרִין בִּכְדֵי שֶׁיֵּעָשׂוּ. לֵימָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא וְלָא בֵּית הִלֵּל? אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בֵּית הִלֵּל, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יְחַתֶּה בַּגֶּחָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Based on these conclusions, **who is** the *tanna* **who taught this** *Tosefta* **that the Sages taught: A woman may not fill up a pot with pounded wheat and lupines,** a type of legume, **and place them in the oven** to cook on **Shabbat eve at nightfall. And if she placed them** in the oven, not only may they not be eaten on Shabbat itself, but even **at the conclusion of Shabbat they are forbidden for a** period of **time** that would be **sufficient for them to be prepared,** i.e., the time it takes to cook the dish from the beginning, so that he will derive no benefit from a prohibited labor performed on Shabbat. **Similarly,** the *Tosefta* said: **A baker may not fill a barrel of water and place it in the oven on Shabbat eve at nightfall** to boil the water that is in the barrel, **and if he did so,** even **at the conclusion of Shabbat it is forbidden for** the period of **time** that would be **sufficient for it to be prepared** from the beginning. **Let us say** that this *Tosefta* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai and not** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Hillel.** The Gemara answers: **Even if you say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Hillel,** in those cases the Sages issued **a decree** due to concern **lest** the one cooking **stoke** the **coals** on Shabbat in order to accelerate the cooking.

If *Beit Shammai* holds *shevisas keilim* as a biblical prohibition, a pressing question arises: how does *Beit Shammai* permit keeping oil lamps burning through *Shabbos*, or food cooking in a pot from *erev Shabbos*? All of these involve vessels performing work on *Shabbos*. The *Gemara* lists the cases: a barrel (*gigis*) in which beer ferments throughout *Shabbos*; an oil lamp (*ner*) burning throughout *Shabbos*; a pot (*kdeira*) of food cooking through *Shabbos*; a spit (*shaphud*) on which meat roasts. How can *Beit Shammai* permit any of these?

The *Gemara*'s answer is striking: *de-mafkir lehu* — one must declare these vessels *hefker* before *Shabbos*. By relinquishing ownership of one's oven, one's pots, and one's lamps before *Shabbos* begins, one is no longer obligated to ensure their rest — *shevisas keilim* applies only to vessels one owns. This is, the *Gemara* concedes, an extraordinary implication: every *erev Shabbos*, under *Beit Shammai*'s view, one would have to formally renounce ownership of one's cooking vessels and lamps. *Tosafos* observes that this creates a similarly interesting question about *shevisas behemto* — the biblical obligation for one's animals to rest — and whether a parallel *mafkir* move could be used to circumvent that obligation, though *Tosafos* rejects that application.

This is a position the *Gemara* has clearly painted itself into, and *Tosafos* points out that the alternative reading — that *Beit Shammai*'s concern is purely rabbinic — avoids all of this difficulty. Under the appearances-based rabbinic reading, one can draw reasonable distinctions between cases that look problematic and cases (like candles and cooking pots) that have been normalized as part of *Shabbos* life.

### Legumes Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי מוּגְמָר וְגׇפְרִית נָמֵי לִגְזוֹר! הָתָם לָא מְחַתֵּי לְהוּ, דְּאִי מְחַתֵּי סָלֵיק בְּהוּ קוּטְרָא, וְקָשֵׁי לְהוּ. אוּנִּין שֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן נָמֵי לִיגְזוֹר! הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּקָשֵׁי לְהוּ זִיקָא, לָא מְגַלּוּ לֵיהּ: צֶמֶר לַיּוֹרָה לִיגְזוֹר! אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּיוֹרָה עֲקוּרָה. וְנֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא מֵגִיס בָּהּ! בַּעֲקוּרָה וְטוּחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** with regard to **incense and sulfur,** the Sages **should also issue a decree** that prohibits placing them beneath clothes and silver vessels, respectively, on Shabbat eve at nightfall. The Gemara answers: **There,** in that case, **he will not stoke them, as if he stokes them smoke will rise into** the garments and the silver, **and that is damaging for them.** The smoke from the wood will ruin the fragrance and the coating of sulfur. The Gemara asks further: With regard to **bundles of flax,** the Sages **should also issue a decree.** The Gemara answers: **There, since wind is damaging for them, he does not expose them,** and he will not come to stoke the coals. The Gemara asks further: With regard to **wool** placed **in the** dyer's **kettle,** the Sages **should also issue a decree. Shmuel said:** The mishna is referring **to a pot that is removed** from the fire, where there is no concern lest he stoke the coals. The Gemara still asks: **Let us be concerned lest he stir** that same pot, thereby accelerating the cooking, which is prohibited by Torah law. Rather, the mishna is referring **to** a pot that is **removed** from the fire **and sealed with clay** spread around its cover to prevent it from opening.

The *Gemara* asks whose position is represented by the following *baraisa*: a woman may not fill a pot with legumes such as *asasiyos* and *turmasin* and place it in the oven on *erev Shabbos* at nightfall, because these will require the entire duration of *Shabbos* to cook. If she did so, the food is forbidden after *Shabbos* for the amount of time it would take to prepare — since it was the product of *melacha* performed on *Shabbos*, one must wait that duration before benefiting from it. Similarly, a baker may not fill a barrel with water on *erev Shabbos* at nightfall, if the water will be used in a process that continues into *Shabbos*. The question is whether this *baraisa* reflects *Beit Shammai* alone.

### Stirring Fear

The *Gemara* answers: *afilu teima Beit Hillel* — this can even represent *Beit Hillel*. *Beit Hillel*'s reason for forbidding these particular cases is a distinct concern: *gezeira shema yichateh ba-gechalim* — a rabbinic decree lest one come to stir the coals. This is the principle that will dominate the entire third *perek* of *Shabbos*, and we are here receiving an early preview.

The concern is this: if food is placed on a fire before *Shabbos* in a state that is not yet fully cooked, one may become anxious during *Shabbos* about whether it will be ready in time. That anxiety may cause one to forget that it is *Shabbos* and unconsciously stir or adjust the fire to accelerate cooking. This concern applies even according to *Beit Hillel* — it has nothing to do with *shevisas keilim* — and it is the reason the *baraisa* prohibits placing these particular items in the oven so close to *Shabbos*.

This is also why the *mishnah* in the next *perek* will specify conditions under which food may or may not be left on a flame before *Shabbos*, and why the requirement of a *blech* (a covering placed over the flame) exists in practice: the *blech* addresses this stirring concern by creating a physical and psychological reminder that one should not adjust the fire.

The parallel question is symmetrical: given that the stirring concern applies to *Beit Hillel*, how does *Beit Shammai* permit cooking from *erev Shabbos* at all — given that *Beit Shammai* additionally holds *shevisas keilim*? According to the *shevisas keilim* view, *Beit Shammai* must use the *mafkir* solution for the vessels; the stirring concern must then be addressed separately, presumably through whatever mechanism *Beit Hillel* also uses, such as a *blech*.

## Blech Exceptions

### Raw & Cooked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר מָר גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יְחַתֶּה בַּגֶּחָלִים, הַאי קְדֵרָה חַיְּיתָא שְׁרֵי לְאַנּוּחַהּ עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה בְּתַנּוּרָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? — כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חֲזֵי לְאוּרְתָּא, אַסּוֹחֵי מַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ מִינֵּיהּ וְלָא אָתֵי לְחַתּוֹיֵי גֶּחָלִים. וּבְשִׁיל — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. בְּשִׁיל וְלָא בְּשִׁיל — אֲסִיר. וְאִי שְׁדָא בֵּיהּ גַּרְמָא חַיָּיא — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And now** that **the Master said** that in these cases the prohibition of placing the pot on the fire is due to **a decree** issued by the Sages **lest he stoke the coals;** with regard to **this pot of raw meat, it is permitted to place it in an oven** on **Shabbat eve at nightfall. What is the reason** for this? **Since it is not fit for** consumption during **the night,** as it will not be cooked by then, **he diverts his thoughts from it and will not come to stoke the coals. And** the same is true of **cooked meat; it is permitted** to place it on the fire on Shabbat eve at nightfall. Since it is reasonably cooked, one will not come to stoke the coals to cook it more. Meat that is **cooked and not** sufficiently **cooked is prohibited,** as there is concern lest he come to stoke the coals. **And if he threw a raw bone into** this pot, he may **well** do so, as due to the bone he will not remove the meat to eat it in the evening.

Having established that the primary operative concern for cooking from *erev Shabbos* is the fear of stirring the coals, the *Gemara* now delineates when that concern applies and when it does not. The *Gemara* states: if one has a pot of raw meat — *kdeira chayasa* — one may place it in the oven on *erev Shabbos* even at nightfall without a *blech*. Why? Because raw meat will not be ready to eat that same evening; it will cook only by the following morning. Since one knows the food will not be ready for a very long time, one is not anxious about it, does not think about it, and will not come to stir the fire.

Conversely, if food is already fully cooked — *bashul shapir* — it also does not require a *blech*. There is no concern about stirring when the food is already done.

### Partially Cooked

The only situation that does require a *blech* is food that is partially cooked — *bashul ve-lo bashul*. Food in that intermediate state will finish cooking relatively soon, and one is likely to be anxious about whether it is ready. That anxiety is precisely what the rabbis feared would lead to forgetting it is *Shabbos* and stirring the coals. The *blech* requirement applies specifically, and only, to this intermediate category.

This preview of the third *perek* carries real *halachic* implications. A *cholent* placed on a fire totally raw before *Shabbos* genuinely does not raise the stirring concern — whatever one's practice regarding whether a crockpot requires a *blech* or what modifications a crockpot requires, the raw-food exception is substantively significant.

### Mixed Cooking Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר מָר כׇּל מִידֵּי דְּקָשֵׁי לֵיהּ זִיקָא לָא מְגַלּוּ לֵיהּ, הַאי בִּשְׂרָא דְּגַדְיָא וּשְׁרִיק — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. דְּבַרְחָא וְלָא שְׁרִיק — אֲסִיר. דְּגַדְיָא וְלָא שְׁרִיק, דְּבַרְחָא וּשְׁרִיק — רַב אָשֵׁי שָׁרֵי וְרַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי אָסַר. וּלְרַב אָשֵׁי דְּשָׁרֵי, וְהָתַנְיָא אֵין צוֹלִין בָּשָׂר בָּצָל וּבֵיצָה אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּצּוֹלוּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם! הָתָם דְּבַרְחָא וְלָא שְׁרִיק.
>
> **Translation:** **And now** that **the Master said** that **anything for which wind is damaging one does not expose,** one could say that with regard to **meat of a kid** and an oven whose opening **is sealed with clay,** he may **well** place it there on Shabbat eve at nightfall. Since the meat of the kid cooks quickly and the opening of the oven is sealed, there is no concern lest he come to stoke the coals. If it is the meat **of a ram** [***barḥa***] **and** the opening of the oven is **not sealed with clay, it is prohibited** to place it there on Shabbat eve at nightfall. The above are cases where the ruling is clear. However, with regard to the case of the meat **of a kid and** the opening of the oven is **not sealed with clay,** or the case **of a ram and** the opening of the oven is **sealed,** there is a dispute. **Rav Ashi permitted** placing it in the oven on Shabbat eve at nightfall, **and Rav Yirmeya from Difti prohibited** doing so. The Gemara asks: **And** according **to** the opinion of **Rav Ashi, who permitted** placing it there on Shabbat eve at nightfall, **wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **one may not roast meat, an onion, and an egg** on Shabbat eve **unless** there is **sufficient time for them to be roasted while it is still day?** Apparently, one may not place meat that is not sufficiently roasted in an oven on Shabbat eve. The Gemara answers: **There,** the *baraita* is referring to the meat **of a ram and** the opening of the oven is **not sealed with clay.** However, in other cases it is permitted.

The *Gemara* extends this analysis to a mixed case: if one has a pot in the intermediate partially-cooked state — which would ordinarily require a *blech* — but then throws in a single raw bone, one need not cover the flame. Since that one piece requires a long time to cook, one's focus shifts to that long-cooking item, and the anxiety about imminent readiness dissipates.

The *Rambam* goes further and holds that throwing in even a raw vegetable — something that would cook quickly — suffices as a *heker*, a distinguishing mark, to eliminate the stirring concern. We do not rule in accordance with the *Rambam* on this point. Nevertheless, the underlying principle is that wherever one element of the pot will require an extended cooking time, the concern of anxious stirring is attenuated even with respect to the other contents.

## Meat Types & Sealing

### Goat Sealed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: דְּגַדְיָא בֵּין שְׁרִיק בֵּין לָא שְׁרִיק — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. דְּבַרְחָא נָמֵי וּשְׁרִיק — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי דְּבַרְחָא וְלָא שְׁרִיק — דְּרַב אָשֵׁי שָׁרֵי וְרַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּפְתִּי אָסַר. וּלְרַב אָשֵׁי דְּשָׁרֵי, וְהָתַנְיָא אֵין צוֹלִין בָּשָׂר בָּצָל וּבֵיצָה אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּצּוֹלוּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם! הָתָם בְּבִשְׂרָא אַגּוּמְרֵי. אָמַר רָבִינָא: הַאי קָרָא חַיָּיא — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּקָשֵׁי לֵיהּ זִיקָא כְּבִשְׂרָא דְגַדְיָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Others say** that with regard to the meat **of a kid, whether** it is in an oven that is **sealed** or **whether** it is in one that is **not sealed,** everyone agrees that he may **well** do so. With regard to the meat **of a ram,** when the opening of the oven is **sealed,** one may **well** do so **too**. **Where they disagreed** was **in** the case of the meat of **a ram and** the opening of the oven was **not sealed. Rav Ashi permitted** placing it in the oven on Shabbat eve at nightfall, **and Rav Yirmeya from Difti prohibited** doing so. The Gemara asks: **And** according **to** the opinion of **Rav Ashi, who permitted** this, **wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **one may only roast meat, an onion, and an egg** on Shabbat eve **if** there is **sufficient time for them to be roasted while it is still day?** Apparently, one may not place meat that is not sufficiently roasted in an oven on Shabbat eve. The Gemara answers: **There,** the *baraita* is referring **to** the case of **meat** roasted directly **on the coals.** In that case, there is greater concern that he will come to stoke the coals. **Ravina said:** With regard to **that raw gourd,** one may **well** place it in a pot on the fire on Shabbat eve at nightfall. The reason for this is that **since the wind is damaging for it, it is considered like the meat of a kid.**

If one is cooking goat meat in a sealed oven — where the oven door or opening has been plastered shut — this is permitted. Two independent factors operate here to reduce the likelihood of opening the oven and stirring: first, the seal itself must be broken, which serves as a reminder; and second, goat meat is particularly susceptible to being harmed by exposure to air, so one would naturally be reluctant to open the oven door.

### Ram Unsealed

If, however, one is cooking ram meat in an unsealed oven, neither protective factor is present. Ram meat is not particularly sensitive to air, so there is no disinclination to open the door; and since the oven is unsealed, there is no physical barrier or reminder. This case is therefore forbidden.

### Mixed Cases

The intermediate cases — goat meat in an unsealed oven, or ram meat in a sealed oven — where exactly one of the two protective factors is present, are subject to a dispute between *Rav Ashi* and *Rav Yirmiyah mi-Difti*. *Rav Ashi* holds that as long as one factor is present, the concern is sufficiently addressed and the arrangement is permitted. *Rav Yirmiyah mi-Difti* holds it is forbidden.

A further difficulty is raised against *Rav Ashi*: if even a single protective factor suffices, how does one ever forbid roasting meat before *Shabbos*? The *mishnah* in the next *perek* will state that one may roast meat, onion, and egg before *Shabbos* only if they will be fully cooked while it is still day — implying a concern about stirring even for meat in an oven. According to *Rav Ashi*, one should always have at least some disinclination to open the oven door, so why is any roasting case ever problematic?

### Coals vs. Ovens

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing the type of cooking. The case of the *mishnah* refers to meat roasting slowly on coals on the ground — not inside a closed oven. When cooking on open coals at ground level, the entire situation is exposed: there is no door to deter opening, no seal to break, and the slow-burning coals naturally invite adjustment. This is precisely the kind of setting where the stirring concern is most acute and where even *Rav Ashi* would agree that the prohibition applies. Finally, *Ravina* notes that a raw gourd is analogous to goat meat, since gourd is particularly susceptible to harm from air exposure; one would therefore be highly unlikely to open the oven when a gourd is cooking, and the arrangement is accordingly permitted.

## Summary & Synthesis

### Three Explanations

To summarize the analysis of this *daf*: there are three distinct explanatory frameworks that have emerged for understanding the restrictions on initiating activities before *Shabbos* that continue into *Shabbos*.

The first and simplest is a rabbinic, appearances-based concern: activities that look like *melacha* being done on *Shabbos* — whether through one's own vessels or through a non-Jew — are prohibited by *Beit Shammai* on those grounds. This is a general rabbinic concern with no specific biblical hook, and it accounts naturally for the full range of cases in the *mishnah*, including the non-Jew cases where no vessel of the Jew's is involved at all.

The second is *shevisas keilim* as a biblical obligation. Under this reading, *Beit Shammai* holds that one's vessels must not perform labor on *Shabbos*, parallel to the obligation for one's animals and servants. This generates significant complications: one must declare one's cooking vessels and lamps *hefker* before *Shabbos* to permit their continued operation, a striking and demanding implication.

The third is *mashmi'a kol* — the concern about creating noise that gives the impression of labor being conducted on *Shabbos*. This concern, which *Beit Hillel* accepts, explains the single exception for the water mill in the *baraisa* permitting various preparatory activities.

### Stirring Anxiety

Underlying all of this, and entirely independent of the *Beit Shammai* versus *Beit Hillel* debate, is the concern about stirring the fire — *gezeira shema yichateh ba-gechalim* — which applies according to *Beit Hillel* as well, and which will be the dominant theme of the third *perek*. This concern is not triggered when food is completely raw (no anxiety, long cooking time ahead), nor when food is already fully cooked (no anxiety, nothing left to do). It applies specifically to partially cooked food, where one's anticipation of imminent completion creates the risk of impulsive intervention. Exceptions exist when the physical configuration of the cooking — a sealed oven, susceptible meat, slow coals — makes it unlikely that one would disturb the arrangement. These principles, previewed here on 18b, will receive full and detailed treatment as the tractate continues.
