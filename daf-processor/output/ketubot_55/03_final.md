## Ketubah Tosefet Basics

### Daf Overview

Today's official *daf* is 56, but we pick up on 55 with the *itmar* section. The *mishnah* at the opening of our *daf* has established the basic framework of the *ketubah*: the *ikar ketubah* of 200 for a *betulah* and 100 for a widow or divorcée, along with the *tosefet* — the additional sum a husband voluntarily adds to the base obligation.

### Erusin vs. Nisuin

One matter that is not made fully explicit in our *mishnah* is the question of what happens when a *ketubah* has not been written at all. The *mishnah* addresses a written *ketubah* with a *tosefet* and asks whether the *tosefet* takes effect from the time of *erusin* or only from the time of *nisuin*. The sense one gets from the surrounding *sugyot*, however, is that the base obligations of 200 and 100 do take effect even when no *ketubah* has been written, already from the time of *erusin*. Our *mishnah*'s particular focus is on the written *ketubah* that includes a *tosefet*, and the question of when that *tosefet* becomes operative.

### Tosefet Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַאי כְּתוּבָּה כִּכְתוּבָּה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The stipulation in the marriage contract** as well as additional amounts he chooses to add to the contract **are comparable to the marriage contract** itself.

The *gemara* then addresses a broader principle: when there is a *tosefet*, it acquires the same legal status as the *ketubah* itself. There is a substantial list of areas in *halachah* where it matters whether an obligation is merely an independent *shtar* obligation or specifically a *ketubah* obligation. The *gemara* proceeds from there into a terse series of debates between two great academies — Pumbedita and Mata Mechasya — across a range of cases, most of which bear directly on *ketubah* law.

## Itmar Debate

### Benin Dichrin Lien

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתוּבַּת בְּנִין דִּכְרִין, פּוּמְבְּדִיתָא אָמְרִי: לָא טָרְפָא מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי, ״יִרְתוּן״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that there was a debate among the Sages with regard to the stipulation in the **marriage contract** that the **male offspring** inherit the sum stipulated in their mother's marriage contract. The Sages of **Pumbedita say: It is not seized from liened** property that has been sold by the father. This is because **we learned** in a mishna (52b) that the text of the stipulation is: **They will inherit** the money set aside for their mother's marriage contract. From this phrase it is clear that the stipulation follows the *halakhot* of inheritance, and therefore their inheritance can be taken only from property in the father's possession at the time of his death, but not from property that he had sold.

The first of these debates concerns *ketuvat benin dichrin* — the provision by which the sons of a wife who predeceased their father inherit their mother's *ketubah* ahead of other competing heirs. The question is whether those sons' claim functions as a lien against the father's estate, such that they could collect from property the father sold during his lifetime, or whether it functions purely as an inheritance, entitling them only to whatever remains in the estate at the time of the father's death.

### Debt vs. Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי מָתָא מַחְסֵיָא אָמְרִי: טָרְפָא מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי, ״יִסְּבוּן״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **The residents of Mata Meḥasya say: It is seized from liened** property that has been sold. Their tradition is that **the mishna states** that the text of the stipulation is: **They will take** the money set aside for their mother's marriage contract. It is as if the husband transferred this property to the sons, and as their acquisition precedes those of the other buyers, they may seize the sold property from the buyers.

Pumbedita rules: *lo tarfi mimshubedi* — the sons cannot collect from encumbered property. The language of the *ketubah*, they argue, reads *yarsun* — "they will inherit" — and an inheritance is not a lien against the estate. Mata Mechasya rules: *tarfi mimshubedi* — they can collect from encumbered property. The language, they contend, reads *yisbun* — "they will take" — indicating a debt-like claim with lien power. The debate thus turns on the *girsah* of the *mishnah*, or alternatively on what the standard *nusach ketubah* was in different communities.

The underlying question is sharp: did the sons acquire a lien against their father's estate — as creditors would — such that if the father sold property during his lifetime, they can subsequently seize it from third-party buyers? Or is their right merely contingent on the father having property in the estate when he dies?

### Halacha Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: לָא טָרְפָא מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי, ״יִרְתוּן״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes that **the *halakha* is that it is not seized from liened** property that has been sold, as **the mishna states: They will inherit.**

*Vehilchata lo tarfi mimshubedi yarsun tenan* — the *halachah* follows Pumbedita. We treat *ketuvat benin dichrin* as an inheritance, not as a debt, and therefore the sons can collect only from property that remains in the estate at the time of the father's death. If the father sold or encumbered property during his lifetime, that property is beyond the sons' reach.

## Widow Shevuah

### Shevuah Purpose

The *gemara* now turns to the question of the widow's *shevuah* when she comes to collect her *ketubah*. Normally, a widow must take an oath before collecting her *ketubah* from the orphans. The reason for this *shevuah* is the concern that the husband, before he died, may have already designated certain *metaltelim* for her *ketubah* — for example, he may have pulled aside specific chattel and told her: after I die, collect your *ketubah* from this, so you will not have to deal with the heirs. If he did that, then her *ketubah* may have effectively already been provided for, and she would not be entitled to collect again from the estate. The *shevuah* she takes is that her *ketubah* has not been prepaid or pre-designated in this fashion.

### Metaltelim Designated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִטַּלְטְלִי וְאִיתַנְהוּ בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ — בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה,
>
> **Translation:** There is another dispute between the Sages of Pumbedita and the residents of Mata Meḥasya, with regard to one who set aside payment for his wife's marriage contract from **movable property, and** these objects **are in their pure,** unadulterated state at the time of the execution of the marriage contract after the husband's death. In this case, all agree that the widow may take them **without an oath** that confirms that her husband did not leave her any other money for the payment of her marriage contract, as it is clear that he set aside these objects for that purpose.

The *gemara* now considers a case where the husband explicitly designated *metaltelim* for the *ketubah* in the presence of witnesses — for example, he pointed to a specific set of china and declared it designated for her *ketubah* collection. In such a case, *belo shevuah* — she collects without a *shevuah*. The entire rationale for the *shevuah* is the concern that he may have designated other *metaltelim* for her *ketubah*, but here we know precisely what he designated. There is no residual concern that he had set aside something else, and therefore there is no need for the oath.

What if those designated *metaltelim* are no longer *be'einaihu* — they were broken or destroyed, and she now must collect from the *karka* of the estate? Here we have a hybrid situation: on one hand, she is now collecting from real property, which ordinarily triggers the *shevuah* requirement; on the other hand, we know the husband designated specific *metaltelim*, and there is no real concern that he had separately pre-designated other *metaltelim* or pre-paid the *ketubah* through some other means.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵיתַנְהוּ בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ — פּוּמְבְּדִיתָא אָמְרִי: בְּלֹא שְׁבוּעָה. בְּנֵי מָתָא מַחְסֵיָא אָמְרִי: בִּשְׁבוּעָה. וְהִלְכְתָא: בְּלֹא שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** However, if the movable objects **are not in their pure,** unadulterated state, e.g., they were lost, the Sages of **Pumbedita say** that she takes the payment for her marriage contract from other property, as all of the husband's property is liened to the marriage contract **without an oath. The residents of Mata Meḥasya say** she takes her payment only **with an oath,** because of a concern that she may have already received other property as payment for her marriage contract. **And the *halakha* is** that she may take it **without an oath,** in accordance with the opinion of the Sages of Pumbedita.

Pumbedita says: *belo shevuah* — she can still collect without a *shevuah*, because we can reasonably conclude there was no other *metaltelim* the husband would have pre-designated. Mata Mechasya says: *bishvuah* — since she is now coming to collect from *karka*, the general rule applies and she must take a *shevuah*. *Vehilchata belo shevuah* — the *halachah* follows Pumbedita.

### Land Designated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַיחֵד לַהּ אַרְעָא בְּאַרְבְּעָה מִצְרָנֶהָא — בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה. בְּחַד מִצְרָא — פּוּמְבְּדִיתָא אָמְרִי: בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה. בְּנֵי מָתָא מַחְסֵיָא אָמְרִי: בִּשְׁבוּעָה. וְהִלְכְתָא: בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **he set aside land for her,** which he demarcated **on** all **four of its borders,** she seizes the land **without an oath.** If he demarcated it **on** only **one border,** which is not as clear an indication, the Sages of **Pumbedita say** she takes it **without an oath,** and **the residents of Mata Meḥasya say** she takes it **with an oath. And the *halakha* is** that she takes it **without an oath.**

A parallel case: *yiched lah ar'ah b'arba mitzreha* — the husband explicitly designated a specific piece of land for collection of the *ketubah*, identifying it by all four borders. Here too, *belo shevuah* — there is no concern that he had prepaid the *ketubah* through other means, and she collects without an oath.

### Land Partial

If the husband designated the land with only *chad mitzra* — only one border rather than all four — the designation is incomplete. It is unclear how much land he is actually setting aside for her, and one might argue this is tantamount to no designation at all. Perhaps we should revert to the concern that he may have pre-designated other property, and she should be required to take a *shevuah*.

Pumbedita rules *belo shevuah* — even a partial designation is sufficient to eliminate the concern. Mata Mechasya rules *bishvuah* — an incomplete designation is insufficient. *Vehilchata belo shevuah* — again the *halachah* goes with Pumbedita, and we are lenient in her favor.

### Halacha Widow

To summarize the two rulings thus far: *ketuvat benin dichrin* is treated as an inheritance and not as a debt, meaning the sons cannot collect from encumbered property. And as for the widow's *shevuah* — if *metaltelim* were designated, even if those *metaltelim* are no longer *be'einaihu*, and if land was designated, even if it was designated on only one border — that is sufficient to dispense with the *shevuah*. The logic is that in each of these cases there is enough evidence that the husband did not separately pre-pay or pre-designate the *ketubah*, and the widow collects without an oath.

It is worth noting a subtle point that arose in this discussion. When the husband designates a specific piece of property for the *ketubah*, this is not intended to limit the wife's other liens or rights — it is to give her an enhanced or more direct right to collect from that specific property. The evidence for this reading is that when the designated *metaltelim* are no longer *be'einaihu*, she nonetheless collects from *karka* — her other rights are undiminished.

## Deathbed Gift Kinyan

### Gift Instructions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְעֵדִים ״כִּתְבוּ וְחִתְמוּ וְהַבוּ לֵיהּ״, קְנוֹ מִינֵּיהּ — לָא צְרִיךְ אִימְּלוֹכֵי בֵּיהּ. לָא קְנוֹ מִינֵּיהּ — פּוּמְבְּדִיתָא אָמְרִי: לָא צְרִיךְ אִימְּלוֹכֵי בֵּיהּ, בְּנֵי מָתָא מַחְסֵיָא אָמְרִי: צְרִיךְ אִימְּלוֹכֵי בֵּיהּ. וְהִלְכְתָא: צְרִיךְ אִימְּלוֹכֵי בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, they had a dispute in a case where someone **told witnesses: Write and sign** a deed of gift **and give it to** the intended recipient of the gift. In such a case, if the witnesses **acquired it from him** on behalf of the recipient by performing a formal act of acquisition, **they do not need to consult with him** again, as there can be no retraction after a formal acquisition. But if **they did not acquire it from him,** the Sages of **Pumbedita say they do not need to consult with him** again, and **the residents of Mata Meḥasya say they must consult with him. And the *halakha* is that they must consult with him** again.

The third debate between the two academies involves a case unrelated to *ketubah*. Someone — who may or may not have been on his deathbed — instructed witnesses: *kisvu ve-chasmu ve-havu lei* — write up a deed of gift, sign it, and give it to him. He wanted to transfer a piece of land to another person.

If the witnesses performed a *kinyan* — specifically a *kinyan sudar*, the handkerchief-based acquisition — before writing and delivering the deed, then *lo tzarich imluchei bei* — they do not need to check back with the giver before delivering the deed. The *kinyan* demonstrates full *gmiras da'as*, complete commitment to the transfer, and they may proceed. If, however, no *kinyan* was performed, and the instruction was purely verbal: Pumbedita says *lo tzarich imluchei bei* — verbal instruction is sufficient and no check-in is needed. Mata Mechasya says *tzarich imluchei bei* — without a *kinyan*, before finalizing the transfer, the witnesses must verify with the giver that he still intends it. *Vehilchata tzarich imluchei bei* — the *halachah* follows Mata Mechasya. Without a *kinyan*, a check-in is required before the deed is delivered.

## Umdana Method

### Umdana Reliance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה וְכוּ׳. אִיתְּמַר. רַב וְרַבִּי נָתָן, חַד אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה. תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּרַבִּי נָתָן הוּא דְּאָמַר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, דְּשָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי נָתָן דְּאָזֵיל בָּתַר אוּמְדָּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya** says that a woman who collects the payment for her marriage contract after marriage receives the main and additional sums, while one who collects it after betrothal receives only the main sum. **It was stated: Rav and Rabbi Natan** differed with regard to this issue. **One said** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. And one said** the ***halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.** **It may be concluded that Rabbi Natan is the one who said** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, as we have heard that Rabbi Natan follows** the principle of **assessing** intention. Even if one did not make an explicit statement, the court assesses what his intention must have been and decides the *halakha* based on that assessment. It is clear that he follows this principle.

The *gemara* now returns to Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah, whose position was already introduced: even though the *tosefet* was written into the *ketubah* document at the time of *erusin*, the obligation does not take effect until *nisuin*, because we assume the husband only intended the *tosefet* to become operative at the time of the actual marriage. This reasoning is based on *umdana* — an assessment of the person's intent that overrides or qualifies the literal text of the document.

The *gemara* introduces the question of which authority — Rav or Rabbi Natan — holds that the *halachah* follows Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah, and which holds that it does not. The *gemara* proposes to resolve this: *tistayem* — we can conclude — that it is Rabbi Natan who rules like Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah, because we know Rabbi Natan *azal batar umdana* — he follows the principle of *umdana*, assessing intent.

### Rabbi Natan Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי נָתָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי בִּמְסוּכָּן, וּבִתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁל דְּמַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **as Rabbi Natan said** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri in** the case of an ill person **in danger.** If this person says: Write a bill of divorce for my wife, that is understood as: Write it and give it to her, as his intention is to absolve her from the requirement of levirate marriage by means of the bill of divorce. Although he did not explicitly state this, Rabbi Natan holds that in such a situation the court assesses that this was the husband's intent and follows it. **And** the *halakha* is also in accordance with his opinion **with regard to the *teruma* of the tithe from doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*].** Produce purchased from a common, uneducated person [*am ha'aretz*] is considered *demai* and by rabbinic law it is regarded as uncertain whether the seller separated tithes and *teruma* of the tithe from it. Rabbi Natan rules in accordance with Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's opinion in a case where, after one bought *demai* and separated *teruma* of the tithe, this *teruma* becomes mingled with the produce from which it was separated. If all of the produce were treated as actual *teruma*, the only solution would be to sell all of the produce to a priest at low cost, as he is the only one who may use it. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri, however, rules that in the case of *demai*, the owner may ask the fruit seller if he properly separated the tithes. If the seller responds that he did, the owner may rely on that, despite the fact that the seller is an *am ha'aretz*, as the Sages do not apply their decree in a case where it would cause significant financial loss.

The evidence for Rabbi Natan's reliance on *umdana* comes from his ruling *ke-Rabbi Shimon ben Shezuri* in the case of a *mesukan* — a person in mortal danger. The *mishnah* in *Gittin* establishes that when a man says "write a *get* for my wife" while departing on a dangerous sea voyage or being led to execution, we assume he means write it *and give it* — because his intent clearly includes delivery, even though he only said "write." Rabbi Shimon ben Shezuri extends this principle even to a *mesukan*, someone lying on his deathbed, who says "write a *get* for my wife." Even in that case, Rabbi Shimon ben Shezuri holds, we assess his intent as including delivery, and the *get* is valid.

The *tanna kamma* already accepts *umdana* reasoning in the first two cases — the sea voyage and the execution. The debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon ben Shezuri is not about whether *umdana* applies at all, but about how much contextual pressure is needed before we invoke it. When a man is being rushed to a ship or dragged to the gallows, one can easily assume he did not complete his sentence; a man dying on his deathbed, by contrast, is not pressed for time in the same way, and perhaps we should not fill in words he did not say. Rabbi Shimon ben Shezuri is willing to extend *umdana* even to that more relaxed context, and Rabbi Natan rules accordingly.

Rabbi Natan also rules like Rabbi Shimon ben Shezuri in the case of *terumat ma'aser shel demai*: when a person bought grain from an *am ha'aretz*, separated *ma'aser*, took *terumat ma'aser* from it, and then the *terumat ma'aser* fell back into the main grain — a situation with no ordinary remedy — Rabbi Shimon ben Shezuri permits asking the *am ha'aretz* himself and relying on his answer, since otherwise the entire batch would have to be sold to a *kohen*. Whether the reasoning is that the *am ha'aretz* would be more reluctant to lie about *terumat ma'aser*, or, as Rashi explains, that otherwise there is simply no available remedy, Rabbi Natan adopts this lenient position. Both rulings demonstrate Rabbi Natan's willingness to go by *umdana* even when the context is not self-evident.

## Deathbed Gift Kinyan (II)

### Rav Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב לָא אָזֵיל בָּתַר אוּמְדָּנָא? וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: מַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁכָּתוּב בָּהּ קִנְיָן, בְּבֵי רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אָמְרִי: אַרְכְּבֵיהּ אַתְּרֵי רִכְשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** Having established that Rabbi Natan follows the principle of assessment, the Gemara asks: **And does Rav not follow** this principle of **assessing** intention? **But it was stated** that Rav and Shmuel disagreed about a specific case with regard to the **gift** of a person **on his deathbed, in which** it was also **written** that the gift was given with an act of **acquisition.** There is a rabbinic ordinance that one on his deathbed can effect the transfer of property without the ordinarily required act of acquisition, but in this case such an act was performed anyway. **In the school of Rav, they say in the name of Rav: He had him ride on two horses,** meaning that he gave him a gift with a document strengthened in two different ways.

The *gemara* challenges the inference that it is Rav, rather than Rabbi Natan, who does not rely on *umdana*. Surely Rav also applies *umdana* in other contexts — specifically, the case of *matnat shechiv mera she-katuv bah kinyan*: a deathbed bequest in which a *kinyan* was performed.

Ordinarily, a *matnat shechiv mera* requires neither writing nor *kinyan* — the Rabbis gave the words of a dying person the status of something already written and delivered. It is, however, conditioned on death: if the person recovers, the gift is reversed. In the case before us, the person was lying on his deathbed — the document was framed in the language of a deathbed bequest — but then he also performed a *kinyan sudar*, an act associated with the transfers of a *bari*, a healthy person. This created a hybrid document.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַתְּנַת בָּרִיא, וַהֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע. הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַתְּנַת בָּרִיא, דְּאִם עָמַד — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ. הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע, שֶׁאִם אָמַר ״הַלְוָאָתִי לִפְלוֹנִי״ — הַלְוָאָתוֹ לִפְלוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two opinions: **In the school of Rav, they say in the name of Rav: He had him ride on two horses,** meaning that he performed the transfer in a manner that took advantage of two separate *halakhot* to strengthen its validity. In one aspect, **it is similar to** the **gift of a healthy** person, **and** in a different way **it is similar to** the **gift** of a person **on his deathbed.** Both of these aspects act to strengthen the transfer. On the one hand, **it is similar to** the **gift of a healthy** person in **that if he arose** from the bed and recovered **he cannot retract it,** since he performed a proper act of acquisition. On the other hand, **it is like** the **gift** of a person **on his deathbed** in **that if he said: My loan,** i.e., money owed to me, is transferred **to so-and-so** as a gift, **his loan** is in fact transferred **to so-and-so.** Although ordinarily ownership of a debt cannot be transferred without a formal transference of the promissory note, the verbal statement is sufficient to effect the transfer since this is a gift of a person on his deathbed.

*D'vei Rav mishmeih d'Rav amri: archevyei atrei rechi* — in the house of Rav, they say in the name of Rav that this person has ridden on two chariots simultaneously. The gift has the power of both a *matnat bari* and a *matnat shechiv mera*. *Harei hi ke-matnat bari* — like a healthy person's gift — in that *im amad eino yachol lachazor bah*: if he recovers, he cannot retract it. And *harei hi ke-matnat shechiv mera* in that it can accomplish things a normal *kinyan* cannot — for instance, it can transfer a debt. Normally, transferring a debt from one creditor to another requires elaborate formalities; a *shechiv mera* can accomplish this directly. The result, according to Rav, is that the gift combines both sets of advantages: it is irrevocable even if the giver recovers, and it operates over the full range of things that a deathbed bequest can accomplish.

### Shmuel Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי אֵידוּן בַּהּ, שֶׁמָּא לֹא גָּמַר לְהַקְנוֹתוֹ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטָר, וְאֵין שְׁטָר לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: I do not know what to decide about it. Perhaps** his performance of an act of acquisition indicates that **he resolved to transfer it** to him **only with a bill** of sale. **And** since his intention is that the sale not take effect until he also gives a bill of sale, the transfer does not take effect, as a **bill** of sale is not effective **after the death** of the owner.

Shmuel says: *lo yadana mai adun bah* — I do not know what to make of it. His reasoning is that the hybrid nature of the document is self-undermining. The fact that the person is on his deathbed suggests he only wanted the gift to take effect upon his death — that is the essence of a *matnat shechiv mera*. But the fact that he performed a *kinyan* suggests he wanted it to function as a normal transfer by a healthy person. A normal transfer by a healthy person does not take effect after death — once a person dies, he can no longer transfer property; it passes to heirs. The two halves of the document thus contradict each other: one implies he wants it to work like a transfer at death, the other implies he wants it to work like a transfer during life — which is the one mode in which it *cannot* function as a *matnat shechiv mera*. Shmuel therefore leaves the matter unresolved; since he cannot determine what the person intended, no property is transferred.

In short: Rav is willing to assess intent and give the document maximal effect in both directions, while Shmuel is paralyzed by the ambiguity and allows nothing to transfer. This shows that Rav, no less than Rabbi Natan, is prepared to rely on *umdana*.

## Umdana Method (II)

### Transaction Types

This raises the obvious question: if both Rav and Rabbi Natan are willing to go by *umdana*, what exactly is their debate regarding Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's position on the *tosefet*?

Both the Rambam and Tosafot Rid make the same essential point, expressed in different formulations. The principle of *devarim she-be-lev einam devarim* — that unspoken intent does not override what was explicitly stated — applies to two-sided transactions. When there is a buyer and a seller, or two parties to a contract, one party cannot unilaterally reinterpret the transaction based on what he was privately thinking. The other party has no obligation to guess at unstated intentions, and the transaction is defined by what is explicit. There is an exception: if the unstated assumption was so universal that it would be known to all (*devarim she-be-lev kol adam*), then it can affect the transaction — but that is a narrow category.

### Umdana Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: תַּרְוַיְיהוּ אָזְלִי בָּתַר אוּמְדָּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** In any case, it has been established that Rav also follows the principle of assessing one's intention, which calls into question the conclusion that Rabbi Natan is the one who said that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. **Rather,** the Gemara concludes: **Both** Rav and Rabbi Natan **follow** the principle of **assessing** intention, and the debate can be explained in a different way.

*Azlinan batar umdana* — following *umdana* — applies to one-sided transactions. All three of the cases before us are one-sided. A *get* is entirely in the husband's domain; the wife has no say. A *matnat shechiv mera* is a unilateral gift. The *tosefet ketubah* is also one-sided — the husband is choosing what additional amount to give; even if there are reciprocal aspects to the *ketubah* overall (a larger dowry might motivate a larger *tosefet*), the *tosefet* itself is not explicitly framed as part of an exchange. In all of these cases, because only one party's intent is operative, we are permitted — indeed required — to assess that intent, and the concern of *devarim she-be-lev einam devarim* does not apply.

Even granting this shared commitment to *umdana*, however, the two authorities can still disagree about what the *umdana* actually yields in any given case. Both parties agree we must assess intent; they disagree about what that assessment reveals.

## Rabbi Elazar Halacha

### Rav Chanina Assert

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב חֲנִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי וְקָאָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanina,** who was known for teaching biblical verses, **sat before Rabbi Yannai and said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.**

The *gemara* now tracks the *halachic* debate over whether we rule like Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah. *Yasiv Rav Chanina kamei de-Rebbi Yannai v'ka'amar halacha ke-Rebbi Elazar ben Azarya* — Rav Chanina sat before Rabbi Yannai and declared that the *halachah* follows Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah.

### Rabbi Yannai Reject

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פּוֹק, קְרִי קְרָאָךְ לְבָרָא! אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yannai **said to him: Go out** and **read your verses outside.** Your area of expertise is biblical verses, not *halakha*. What you said is incorrect and should not be said in the study hall, as the ***halakha* is** actually **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.**

Rabbi Yannai dismissed him sharply: *puk kari kerach l'bara* — go recite your verse outside. The *halachah* does not follow Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah.

### Yitzchak Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי מִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi said in the name of our teacher,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.**

*Amar Rebbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi mishum Rabbeinu* — Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi said in the name of Rabbeinu, which refers to Rav: *halachah ke-Rebbi Elazar ben Azarya*. We rule like him.

### Nachman Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה. וְרַב נַחְמָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה. וּנְהַרְדָּעֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמְרִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.** **And Rav Naḥman** also **said his** own statement: The ***halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. And** the Sages **of Neharde'a say in the name of Rav Naḥman:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.**

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Shmuel halachah ke-Rebbi Elazar ben Azarya* — Shmuel rules like Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah. Yet *Rav Nachman didei amar ein halachah ke-Rebbi Elazar ben Azarya* — Rav Nachman himself personally rules against him. Indeed, Rav Nachman went further: *kol dayana de-dayen ke-Rebbi Elazar ben Azarya* — any judge who rules like Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah and does not award the *tosefet* from the time of *erusin* — *hachi ve-hachi tehavei* — such and such should befall him, the *gemara* declining to specify the precise content of the curse.

### Nahardai Position

*U'v'Nahardai mishmei de-Rav Nachman amri halachah ke-Rebbi Elazar ben Azarya* — but in Nehardea, in the name of Rav Nachman himself, they transmitted that the *halachah* does follow Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah.

### Final Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָט רַב נַחְמָן וְאָמַר: כֹּל דַּיָּינָא דְּדָאֵין כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה הָכִי וְהָכִי תֶּיהְוֵי, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה. וַהֲלָכָה לְמַעֲשֶׂה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And although Rav Naḥman cursed** them **and said: Any judge who rules in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, such and such** unspecified misfortune **will happen to him, even so** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.** Since the Gemara presented a number of different opinions, it concludes: **And the practical *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.**

*Afilu hachi halachah ke-Rebbi Elazar ben Azarya* — notwithstanding Rav Nachman's own personal ruling and his curse, the *halachah* follows Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah. We overrule Rav Nachman. And lest there be any remaining doubt, the *gemara* adds: *ve-halachah le-ma'aseh* — in practice, we rule like Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah. The *tosefet ketubah* does not take effect until *nisuin*. It is worth noting that *ve-halachah le-ma'aseh* is a Hebrew formulation rather than the Aramaic *hilchata*, an unusual choice that is worth observing even if its precise significance remains unclear.

## Nisuin Definition

### Chupa vs. Bia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבִין: נִכְנְסָה לְחוּפָּה וְלֹא נִבְעֲלָה, מַהוּ? חִיבַּת חוּפָּה קוֹנָה, אוֹ חִיבַּת בִּיאָה קוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Since the practical *halakha* is that a woman who was divorced or widowed after betrothal receives the main sum of her marriage contract but not the additional sum, **Ravin asks: What is** the *halakha* with regard to a woman who **entered the wedding canopy and** is then widowed or divorced **without having had sexual intercourse?** Does the **affection** manifest in the **wedding effect** the marriage, and therefore she receives the additional sum as a married woman? **Or,** is it the **affection** manifest in the **intercourse** that **effects** the marriage, and consequently this woman is no different than a betrothed woman for the purpose of this *halakha*?

Now that the *halachah* is settled that the *tosefet* takes effect at *nisuin*, the *gemara* raises Rava's question about what exactly constitutes that moment. This is not the ordinary question of when *nisuin* begins for standard *ketubah* obligations — that is well established. Here the question is specific to the *tosefet* under Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's rationale. Since Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's reasoning is *umdana* — the husband wrote the *tosefet* only in anticipation of the couple's actual life together — we must ask: what event in the husband's mind was he anticipating? *Nichnesa le-chupah ve-lo niv'alah* — if she entered the *chupah* but they did not yet have relations — *mahu*? Is the intimacy of the *chupah* (*chibat chupah*) sufficient to trigger the *tosefet*, or does it require the actual act of *bia*?

### First Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: שֶׁלֹּא כָּתַב לָהּ אֶלָּא עַל חִיבַּת לַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear that Rav Yosef taught** the following *baraita*: **He wrote** the additional sum in the marriage contract **for her only on account of the affection** characteristic **of the first night** of the marriage.

*Ta shema de-tani Rav Yosef* — come and hear what Rav Yosef taught as an explanation of Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's position: *shelo chasav lah ela al chibat laila ha-rishon* — he wrote the *tosefet* only because of the intimacy of the first night. This formulation, "the first night," is what the *gemara* now unpacks.

### Trigger Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא חִיבַּת חוּפָּה קוֹנָה — הַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר לַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ חִיבַּת בִּיאָה קוֹנָה, בִּיאָה — בְּלַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן אִיתַהּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לֵיתַהּ?! וְאֶלָּא מַאי, חוּפָּה? חוּפָּה בְּלֵילְיָא אִיתַהּ, בִּימָמָא לֵיתַהּ? וּלְטַעְמָיךְ — בִּיאָה בְּלֵילְיָא אִיתַהּ, בִּימָמָא לֵיתַהּ? הָא אָמַר רָבָא, אִם הָיָה בְּבַיִת אָפֵל מוּתָּר! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: אוֹרַח אַרְעָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּבִיאָה בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, if you say** that the **affection** manifest in the **wedding effects** the marriage, **this is** why **it says the** affection characteristic of **the first night,** as the wedding ceremony is performed on the first night only. **But if you say** that the **affection** manifest in the **intercourse effects** the marriage, **is there intercourse** only **on the first night** and then **from this** point **forward there is none?** Consequently, the *baraita* implies that the affection manifest in the wedding effects the marriage, and from that point on she is entitled to the additional sum of the marriage contract. The Gemara rejects this proof: **But rather, what** is the advantage of interpreting the expression: Affection characteristic of the first night, as a reference to the **wedding? Is there a wedding** only **at night** and **not during the day?** The Gemara responds: **And according to your reasoning, is there intercourse** only **at night** and **not during the day?** Didn't **Rava say** that although the Sages generally prohibited engaging in intercourse during the day, **if it was in a dark house** it is **permitted?** The Gemara rejects this question: **This** is **not difficult.** By employing this phrase, **it teaches us the ordinary mode of behavior,** i.e., **that intercourse** generally takes place **at night.**

If we say the triggering event is *chibat chupah* — the *chupah* without *bia* — then the phrase "first night" makes sense: *chupah* occurs only once, at the beginning of the marriage, and it is naturally associated with the first night. Why then does he say "first night" rather than simply "*chupah*"? Because *chupah* is a lead-in to *bia*, which is what takes place at night — the phrase is meant to evoke the anticipated *bia* without being explicit about it.

If, on the other hand, the triggering event is the *bia* itself, one might ask: is there *bia* only on the first night and not thereafter? The *gemara* answers: of course there is *bia* at other times as well, but the phrasing *laila ha-rishon* is simply the normal way (*orach ar'a*) of speaking about the beginning of conjugal life, since *bia* typically takes place at night. As Rava himself rules, *bia* in a dark house during the day is also permitted — so *bia* is not exclusively nocturnal — but the natural way to speak about *bia* is with reference to night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא חוּפָּה קַשְׁיָא? חוּפָּה נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא: כֵּיוָן דִּסְתַם חוּפָּה לְבִיאָה קָיְימָא, אוֹרַח אַרְעָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּבַלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the opinion that the expression is a reference to the **wedding is difficult,** as a wedding does not have to take place at night. The Gemara responds: **The wedding** reference is **also not difficult, since** a reference to **a wedding without specification** means a wedding that **takes place** in order to lead directly **to intercourse.** By using this phrase, **it** similarly **teaches us the ordinary mode of behavior,** i.e., that intercourse generally takes place **at night.** Consequently, this *baraita* cannot be used as a proof for either possibility.

By the same token, one might ask: if the triggering event is *chupah*, why say "first night"? Is *chupah* only at night? Here too the *gemara* responds: since *chupah* is normally a prelude to *bia*, which takes place at night, one would naturally speak of the *chupah* in terms of "the first night," either to indicate that it leads to *bia* at night, or simply because *chupah* itself customarily takes place at night. The phrase "first night" is thus equally consistent with either reading, and the question remains unresolved.

### Niddah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: נִכְנְסָה לְחוּפָּה וּפֵירְסָה נִידָּה, מַהוּ? אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר חִיבַּת חוּפָּה קוֹנָה, חוּפָּה דְּחַזְיָא לְבִיאָה, אֲבָל חוּפָּה דְּלָא חַזְיָא לְבִיאָה — לָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi asks** a question similar to Ravin's: If the bride **entered the wedding canopy and began menstruating,** and the husband then died without ever engaging in intercourse with his wife, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the additional sum of the marriage contract? **If you say** that the **affection** manifest in the **wedding effects** the marriage, does this refer specifically to **a wedding** in which the couple is **fit to** engage in **intercourse,** which involves greater affection, **and a wedding** in which the couple **is not fit to** engage in **intercourse** does **not** effect the marriage? **Or, perhaps it is not different.** The Sages could not answer this, so the question **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Ashi then adds a further question: *nichnesa le-chupah u-firsa niddah — mahu*? If she entered the *chupah* and became a *niddah* — what is the law? If we say *chibat chupah* is the triggering event, does that apply only to a *chupah* that was *chazya le-bia* — one that could lead to *bia* — but not to a *chupah de-lo chazya le-bia*, a *chupah* where *bia* was not possible? Or does it make no difference?

The question as phrased by the *gemara* — "she entered the *chupah* and became a *niddah*" — is deliberately ambiguous. If she became a *niddah* after the *chupah*, the question is trivial: the *chupah* was already complete and she was already entitled to the *tosefet*, just as she would be entitled to it if he dropped dead a moment after the *chupah*. The question must therefore refer to a case where she was already a *niddah* at the time of the *chupah* itself. This reading connects to a major dispute among the *rishonim* about whether *chuppat niddah* counts as *chupah* for any purpose — not just for the *tosefet*, but in general. The *gemara* here raises precisely that ambiguity.

### Teiqu Status

The *gemara* concludes: *teiku* — the question stands unresolved. The status of *chupah* when the woman is a *niddah* at the time of the *chupah* remains uncertain with respect to its ability to trigger the *tosefet* under Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's ruling.

## Ketubah Receipt

### Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: רָצָה, כּוֹתֵב לִבְתוּלָה שְׁטָר שֶׁל מָאתַיִם, וְהִיא כּוֹתֶבֶת ״הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי מִמְּךָ מָנֶה״, וּלְאַלְמָנָה מָנֶה וְהִיא כּוֹתֶבֶת ״הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי מִמְּךָ חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says** a related *halakha* with regard to the marriage contract: **If he wishes, he may write for a virgin a document for two hundred** dinars as is fitting for her, **and she may** then **write** a receipt stating: **I received one hundred dinars from you.** Even though she has not actually received the money, the receipt serves as a means for her to waive half of the amount due to her for her marriage contract. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the financial commitment in the marriage contract is a right due to the wife, which she may waive if she chooses to do so. **And** similarly, **for a widow** he may write **one hundred dinars** in the contract **and she may write** a receipt stating: **I received from you fifty dinars.**

The *mishnah* turns to the position of Rabbi Yehuda. His view is that the husband may write a *ketubah* for a *betulah* in the full amount of 200, or for a widow in the full amount of 100 — and then have the wife write a receipt (*shover*) acknowledging that a portion has already been paid, thereby effectively reducing the actual obligation below the nominal amount in the document. Rabbi Yehuda thus permits the base *ketubah* to be less than the required minimum, as long as the document itself records the proper minimum and the reduction is accomplished through a receipt.

### Yehuda vs. Yossi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּכוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר? וְהָתְנַן: מִי שֶׁפָּרַע מִקְצָת חוֹבוֹ — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יַחְלִיף. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יִכְתּוֹב לוֹ שׁוֹבָר!
>
> **Translation:** They ask: **And** did **Rabbi Yehuda hold** that one **writes a receipt** for partial payment of a debt? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 170b): In the case of **one who repaid part of his debt, Rabbi Yehuda says: He should exchange** the original promissory note for a new one that states the amount still owed, and **Rabbi Yosei says:** The lender **should write him a receipt** for the money he received? According to Rabbi Yehuda, a new note is preferable to a receipt because if the borrower loses the receipt, the lender is still in possession of a promissory note for the full amount and can collect a second time.

A question arises from a *mishnah* elsewhere. In the case of a borrower who repays part of his debt, Rabbi Yehuda says the solution is to tear up the old *shtar* and write a new one for the remaining amount (*yachlif*). Rabbi Yossi says the lender should write the borrower a *shover* — a receipt for the amount already paid — and when the lender subsequently produces the original *shtar*, the borrower produces his receipt. What is the difference? The receipt arrangement places the burden on the borrower to preserve the receipt; Rabbi Yehuda objects to this, since the borrower is the more vulnerable party, and writing a new *shtar* is more protective. Moreover, writing a new *shtar* costs the lender his lien priority from the original date — Rabbi Yossi nonetheless prefers the receipt arrangement precisely because it does not disturb the lender's prior lien.

But if Rabbi Yehuda is ordinarily opposed to receipts — because they unfairly burden the obligated party — how can he here require the wife to write a receipt to accomplish the reduction?

### Yirmiah Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: כְּשֶׁשּׁוֹבַרְתָּהּ מִתּוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** In the mishna, Rabbi Yehuda is referring to a case **where the receipt is** written **within** the marriage contract itself and not as a separate document. The husband is therefore not required to hold on to a receipt, and consequently Rabbi Yehuda's restriction against writing a receipt is not necessary.

*Amar Rabbi Yirmiya: ke-she-shovra tvu'a mitocha* — Rabbi Yirmiah answers that in this case there is no separate receipt handed to the husband. The receipt is embedded in the *ketubah* document itself: the *ketubah* records a debt of 200, of which 100 has already been received. The husband does not need to preserve a separate piece of paper; the reduction is already written into the document he signed.

### Abaye Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּשֶׁאֵין שׁוֹבַרְתָּהּ מִתּוֹכָהּ, בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם, וַדַּאי פַּרְעֵיהּ — דִּלְמָא מִירְכַס תָּבַרְתָּא, וּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ לִשְׁטָרָא, וַהֲדַר גָּבֵי זִימְנָא אַחֲרִינָא. הָכָא, וַדַּאי יְהַב לַהּ? מִילְּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הִיא דַּאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ, אִי נַטְרֵיהּ — נַטְרֵיהּ, אִי לָא נַטְרֵיהּ — אִיהוּ הוּא דְּאַפְסֵיד אַנַּפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is referring to a case **where the receipt is not** written **within it,** it is logical that Rabbi Yehuda would make an exception in this case. **Granted, there,** in an ordinary case of a receipt, **it is certain** that the borrower **repaid** part of the loan, and consequently there is concern that **perhaps** he **will lose the receipt** and the lender will **take out the** promissory **note and return and collect** the entire payment **again.** But **here,** in the mishna, did the husband **definitely give** the wife part of the payment for the marriage contract? The receipt **merely** amounts to **something she said to him** in order to waive part of the payment, although she did not actually receive it. **If he saved** the receipt, **he saved it; if he did not save it, it is he himself who will lose.** Therefore, in this case, Rabbi Yehuda agrees that one writes a receipt.

Abaye offers a broader answer: *afilu teima be-she'ein shovra tvu'a mitocha* — even if the receipt is not embedded in the *ketubah*, Rabbi Yehuda would still permit a separate receipt here. In the case of the borrower who repaid three thousand out of ten thousand, real money changed hands, and Rabbi Yehuda is concerned that the receipt might be lost (*mirchas tavrata*), allowing the lender to produce the original ten-thousand-dollar *shtar* and collect the already-repaid amount a second time. That concern justifies the protective measure of writing a new *shtar*. Here, however, the husband did not pay any real money. The receipt is a legal fiction — a way of reducing the wife's claim to less than the nominal amount written in the *ketubah*. Since he paid nothing, there is no danger he will be double-charged. *Ai natar natar, ai lo natar ihu de-afsid anafshei* — if he preserves the receipt, well and good; if he loses it, he brought it on himself. No special protection is warranted for a husband who received a reduction in his *ketubah* obligation without paying a single coin.

## Ketubah Receipt (II)

### Written Form

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא אַבָּיֵי לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, לָא קָתָנֵי שׁוֹבַרְתָּהּ מִתּוֹכָהּ. אֶלָּא רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּאַבַּיֵּי? גְּזֵירָה שׁוֹבָר דְּהָכָא אַטּוּ שׁוֹבָר דְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** They ask: **Granted,** it is understandable why **Abaye did not say** his explanation **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yirmeya,** as the mishna **does not teach** explicitly that the **receipt is** written **within** the marriage contract. **However, what is the reason** that **Rabbi Yirmeya did not say** an explanation **in accordance with** the opinion of **Abaye?** Why does Rabbi Yirmeya limit the mishna to a case where the receipt was written within the marriage contract? The Gemara responds: Although this is an unusual case, as there is no concern that the receipt may be lost, there is nevertheless a rabbinic **decree** with regard to **this receipt due to the typical** case of **receipts.** Therefore, Rabbi Yehuda would not allow a receipt unless it was written into the marriage contract itself.

The *gemara* now asks a deeper question. If Rabbi Yehuda holds that *davar she-be-mamon tnao kayam* — that one may stipulate against monetary obligations — then why must the husband go through the elaborate two-step of writing the full amount and then having a receipt written? Why not simply stipulate at the outset that the *ketubah* shall be for less than 200 or 100?

The *gemara*'s answer is that Rabbi Yirmiah operates with a concern about *gezeira*: since Rabbi Yehuda is in general opposed to the receipt mechanism, he would not allow it even in the case where no money was paid, lest people infer from this case that receipts are permissible generally and begin using them in cases where real money has changed hands, improperly burdening borrowers who really did pay. Only by embedding the "receipt" into the *ketubah* document itself — where it is not functioning as a standalone *shover* — does Rabbi Yehuda permit the reduction. *Taama de-kasav lei* — only because it is written — *aval al peh lo* — but verbally, or as a separate document in the ordinary sense of a receipt, it would not suffice.

## Monetary Stipulate

### Monetary Stipula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא — דְּכָתְבָה לֵיהּ, אֲבָל עַל פֶּה — לָא. אַמַּאי? דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמָמוֹן הוּא, וְשָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמָמוֹן — תְּנָאוֹ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the crux of the issue, the Gemara notes: **The reason** that Rabbi Yehuda holds that the wife can waive part of the main sum of her marriage contract **is** specifically because **she wrote him** a receipt. **However,** if she said it **verbally, no,** it is not effective, even according to Rabbi Yehuda. The Gemara asks: **Why** not? **This is a monetary matter, and we** have **heard that Rabbi Yehuda said:** With regard to **monetary matters** in which someone makes a verbal stipulation, **his stipulation stands.**

If Rabbi Yehuda holds that *davar she-be-mamon tnao kayam* — that one may stipulate against monetary obligations — then why must the husband go through the elaborate two-step of writing the full amount and then having a receipt written? Why not simply stipulate at the outset that the *ketubah* shall be for less than 200 or 100?

### Braisa Void

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לִיךְ עָלַי שְׁאֵר כְּסוּת וְעוֹנָה״ הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וּתְנָאוֹ בָּטֵל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּדָבָר שֶׁבְּמָמוֹן — תְּנָאוֹ קַיָּים!
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Kiddushin* 3:7): In the case of **one who says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me on the condition that you have no** ability to claim **from me food, clothing, or conjugal rights, she is betrothed and his stipulation is void;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to **monetary matters,** such as food and clothing, **his stipulation stands;** therefore, if she verbally waives part of the marriage contract, and thereby makes a stipulation about a monetary matter, it should be effective.

A *braisa* is cited: if a man says to a woman, *harei at mekudeshet li al menat she-ein li alayich she'eir kesut ve-ona* — you are betrothed to me on condition that I owe you no *she'eir*, no *kesut*, and no *ona* — the *kiddushin* takes effect but the condition is void, in the view of Rabbi Meir. The reason is *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* — he is stipulating against what the Torah has written — and such stipulations are null.

### Yehuda Distinct

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and holds that *be-davar she-be-mamon tnao kayam* — for monetary obligations, his stipulation stands. Rabbi Yehuda's understanding is that the Torah's establishment of *she'eir* and *kesut* as marital obligations represents a set of default assumptions about what the parties intend in a marriage — defaults that can be overridden by an explicit stipulation between the parties. *Ona*, however, is a personal obligation, not a monetary one, and cannot be stipulated away.

If Rabbi Yehuda is prepared to permit stipulations even against biblical monetary obligations, the question becomes more pointed: why cannot the husband simply stipulate that the *ketubah* will be for less than 200? The *ketubah* is rabbinic, not biblical — if he can override Torah-level monetary obligations by stipulation, surely he can override a rabbinic one.

### Ketubah Protect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כְּתוּבָּה דְּרַבָּנַן, וַחֲכָמִים עָשׂוּ חִיזּוּק לְדִבְרֵיהֶם יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁל תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yehuda holds:** The **marriage contract is a rabbinic** law, **and the Sages reinforced their pronouncements with greater** force **than Torah law.** Therefore, if the wife waives part of the main sum of the marriage contract, Rabbi Yehuda holds that her declaration has no force unless it is written down. However, a Torah obligation, such as food and clothing, does not require this reinforcement, and consequently the wife may waive it with a verbal stipulation.

The answer, paradoxically, is this: *kasavar Rebbe Yehuda ketuvah de-rabbanan* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *ketubah* is rabbinic. *Ve-chachamim asu chizku le-divreichem yoter mi-shel Torah* — and the Rabbis fortified their own enactments more rigorously than they would ordinarily apply. A direct stipulation to reduce the *ketubah* below the required minimum, they determined, would undermine the institution, and so they did not permit it.

### Common Protect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי פֵּירוֹת דְּרַבָּנַן, וְלָא עֲבַדוּ לְהוּ רַבָּנַן חִיזּוּק! דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם הוּא אוֹכֵל פֵּירֵי פֵירוֹת — עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב לָהּ: ״דִּין וּדְבָרִים אֵין לִי בִּנְכָסַיִךְ, וּבְפֵירוֹתֵיהֶן, וּבְפֵירוֹת פֵּירוֹתֵיהֶן עַד עוֹלָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this answer: The husband's entitlement to the **produce** of his wife's property **is a rabbinic** decree, **and** nevertheless **the Sages did not reinforce** his rights to **them, as we learned** in a mishna (83a): **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Even if the husband wrote that he waived his rights to the produce of his wife's property, **he may actually consume the produce of the produce** of her property, meaning that he could invest the produce in additional property, which would also belong to his wife, but he would consume its produce. This applies **unless he** explicitly **writes** to **her: I do not have any claim to your property, its produce, or the produce of its produce, forever.**

But this raises a further question. The husband's right to the *peirot* — the fruits of his wife's *nichsei milug*, the property she retains title to in marriage — is also rabbinic. Yet we find in another *mishnah* that Rabbi Yehuda allows the husband to waive his rights to the *peirot* verbally, and the *gemara* elsewhere establishes that *mai kosev* — what the *mishnah* means by "he writes" — is that verbal waiver suffices. Why is the waiver of *peirot* not similarly fortified by the Rabbis?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבַּיֵּי: לְחִיל אֵין כְּתוּבָּה, וְלוֹ כְּתוּבָּה, וּלְחִיל לֵית מִנִּיתִּא דּוּמְיָא, אֶלָּא אִיתַנְּהוּ לְחִיל זִימְנִין, וּלְלָא חִיל זִימְנִין.
>
> **Translation:** *Amar Abaye: le-chol yesh ketubah ve-lo le-chol yesh peirot* — Abaye answers: every woman has a *ketubah*, but not every woman has *peirot*. The *ketubah* is a universal entitlement; the *peirot* arrangement is contingent on the wife bringing in property over which the husband can exercise those rights. *Milta de-shchicha avdu Rabbanan chizuk* — something common, something that applies to every married woman, the Rabbis reinforced with special protections. *Milta de-lo shchicha lo avdu Rabbanan chizuk* — something that arises only in the more particular circumstance of a wife bringing in income-generating property, the Rabbis did not subject to the same degree of reinforcement.

*Amar Abaye: le-chol yesh ketubah ve-lo le-chol yesh peirot* — Abaye answers: every woman has a *ketubah*, but not every woman has *peirot*. The *ketubah* is a universal entitlement; the *peirot* arrangement is contingent on the wife bringing in property over which the husband can exercise those rights. *Milta de-shchicha avdu Rabbanan chizuk* — something common, something that applies to every married woman, the Rabbis reinforced with special protections. *Milta de-lo shchicha lo avdu Rabbanan chizuk* — something that arises only in the more particular circumstance of a wife bringing in income-generating property, the Rabbis did not subject to the same degree of reinforcement.
