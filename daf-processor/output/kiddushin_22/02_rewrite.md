# Kiddushin 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nirtza Overview

### Nirtza Freedom

The *mishnah* on Kiddushin 22 turns to the *nirtza* — the *eved ivri* whose ear is pierced and who thereby commits to extended service beyond the initial six-year term. The *mishnah* states: *ve'hanirtza nikneh birtziah* — the one who has his ear pierced is acquired through that act of piercing, and he then works until the *yovel*. The *gemara* cites the verse: *veratza adonav et ozno bamarzea* — his master shall pierce his ear with an awl.

The *mishnah* further teaches that the *nirtza* acquires his freedom in two ways: through the *yovel* and through the death of the master. The source for freedom at *yovel* is the explicit verse in *Behar*. The source for freedom upon the master's death is the phrase *va'avado* — serve *him* — followed by *velo es haben velo es habas*, meaning not the son and not the daughter. The Rambam notes that *le'olam* does not independently teach that service continues until the *yovel*; rather, it is forced into that reading by the explicit *pasuk* in *Behar*. The *nirtza* relationship, unlike the six-year term which carries the character of *schirus* (hired work), involves a more total form of ownership — and correspondingly, the death of the master brings it to an end in a way that differs from the ordinary six-year arrangement, where service can in certain circumstances continue for the master's son.

### Piercing Tools

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *ein li ela marzea* — I would only know that an awl is valid. The *tanna* then asks: how do we know to include *hasol* (which Rashi identifies as a piece of wood), *hasira* (a thorn), *hamachat* (a needle), *hamakdeach* (some other type of piercing implement), and *hamichtav* (a stylus)? The answer is *talmud lomar velakachta* — the word "and you shall take" comes to include anything that can be taken by hand. This is the position of Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda.

Rabbi, however, takes a different view: *mah marzea meyuchad shel matechet af kol shel matechet* — just as the awl is specifically of metal, so too anything valid for this purpose must be of metal, excluding the wooden implements mentioned above. The *baraita* adds a further point: *davar acher* — *hamartzea lehavi hamarzea hagadol* — the definite article *ha-* in *hamartzea* comes to include even a large awl, though the precise significance of this will emerge below.

### Piercing Location

Rabbi Elazar Yudan Beribbi — Rashi explains that *beribbi* denotes a man of prestige — taught that when the piercing is performed, it is done only in the *milsa*, the soft hanging lobe of the ear, not in the cartilage or main body of the ear itself.

The *chachamim* disagree and adduce a compelling proof: *ein eved ivri kohen nirtza mipnei shena'aseh ba'al mum* — a *kohen* who is an *eved ivri* does not undergo the *retziah* because it would render him a *ba'al mum*. Now, if the piercing were done in the lobe — as one would do for an earring — it would not disqualify a *kohen* from Temple service. The fact that the *halacha* excludes a *kohen* because of *ba'al mum* is therefore proof that the piercing is done in the ear itself, in the place where a permanent hole does indeed constitute a disqualifying blemish. The principle is stated directly: *ein retziah ela begova shel ozen* — the piercing is only in the height, that is, the upper body, of the ear.

The Ramban connects this discussion to the matter of the large *marzea*: once the possibility of using a large awl is introduced, the question of *ba'al mum* for a *kohen* becomes acute. Rabbi Elazar Yudan Beribbi's response was that the *retziah* must therefore be in the lobe. The *chachamim* counter that the *halacha* excluding a *kohen* from *retziah* entirely proves the location is precisely where a *ba'al mum* can result.

### Metal vs. Other

The *gemara* now addresses the underlying hermeneutic dispute between Rabbi and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda. What exactly divides them?

*Rebbi doresh klal uprat* — Rabbi employs the hermeneutic of *klal u'prat u'klal*, general-specific-general. In the verse, *velakachta* is a *klal* (general statement); *marzea* is the *prat* (specific); and *be'ozno uvadelet* — the ear and the door — constitutes a return to the general. The operative rule is: *klal u'prat u'klal iy ata dan ela ke'ein haprat* — one may include only what resembles the specific case. Since the *marzea* is of metal, anything of metal qualifies, and nothing else does.

The mechanics of this principle are worth pausing on. When a *klal* is followed by a *prat* alone — general then specific — the result is that we follow the specific. Similarly, a *prat* followed by a *klal* — specific then general — leaves us with the general. It is only when the pattern is general-specific-general that the principle of *ke'ein haprat* applies: one generalizes, but only to what is similar in kind to the specific item named. By identifying the *marzea* as the controlling *prat*, and metal as its defining characteristic, Rabbi concludes that any metal implement is valid.

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, *doresh ribui u'miut* — employs the hermeneutic of expansion and contraction. He reads the same words but frames them not as *klal* and *prat* but as *ribui* (expansive language) and *miut* (contracting language). *Velakachta* expands; *marzea* contracts; *be'ozno uvadelet* expands again. When the structure is *ribui-miut-ribui*, the result is *riba hakol* — nearly everything is included. What is excluded? *Miet sam* — something that achieves the piercing not through a pointed implement but through an entirely different mechanism, such as burning with acid. The contracting term does not define a category of similar items; it merely excludes what is most dissimilar. Because the middle term functions as a contraction rather than a specification, it does not require that other valid implements resemble the *marzea* in material. Wood is therefore not excluded under this reading.

The conceptual difference is subtle but real. Under *klal u'prat u'klal*, the specific term anchors the interpretation — everything valid must be *like* it. Under *ribui u'miut u'ribui*, the middle term only contracts the outer expansiveness; it does not define the genus. This places the *ribui u'miut* method one step further toward inclusion than *klal u'prat u'klal*.

### Big Martzea

The *gemara* inquires: *mai mashma* — how does one derive from the word *hamartzea* (with the definite article) that it includes a large awl? The answer is by analogy to Rava's principle regarding *gid hanasheh*: just as *asher kaf hayarech* — "the thigh" — singles out the prominent one, the right thigh, so too *hamartzea* singles out the prominent *marzea*, the significant or large one. The *gemara* then returns to the positions of Rabbi Elazar Yudan Beribbi and the *chachamim* as already analyzed above, affirming that the *halacha* excludes a *kohen* from *retziah* because of *ba'al mum*, which in turn proves the piercing is performed in the upper portion of the ear.

## Kohen Restrictions

### Kohen Nirtza

Having established that a *kohen eved ivri* cannot become *nirtza* because it would render him a *ba'al mum*, the *gemara* asks where the prohibition against making him a *ba'al mum* comes from in the first place. There is indeed a prohibition against creating a *mum b'kodashim* — a blemish in sacrificial animals — but what is the source for the prohibition against causing a *kohen* to become a *ba'al mum*, even though such a blemish would disqualify him from *ha'avoda*?

Rabba bar Rav Shila answers from the verse: *veshav el mishpachto* — he shall return to his family. This refers to the *eved ivri* going free at *yovel*, and the *gemara* understands it as meaning *lemuchzak sheb'mishpachto* — he must return to the status he held within his family before his servitude. Since a *kohen eved ivri* who becomes *nirtza* would be pierced in the upper ear and rendered a *ba'al mum*, foreclosing his return to priestly service, the verse's requirement that he be restored to his prior status prohibits this outcome.

### Kohen & Shifcha

The *gemara* now poses a fresh question: *eved ivri kohen mahu sheyimsor lo rabo shifcha kna'anit* — may a master assign a non-Jewish female slave to a *kohen* who is an *eved ivri* for the purpose of fathering children? The Torah permits a master to do this for an ordinary *eved ivri* (as stated: *im adonav yiten lo isha*), but the question is whether this permission extends to a *kohen*.

The case is a *chiddush* to begin with: ordinarily there is a prohibition against a man having sexual relations with a non-Jewish slave, rooted in the verse *lo yikadesh velo yikadesha*. The Torah's permit for the *eved ivri* overrides this *issur*. Does this override apply equally to a *kohen*, or do the *kohen*'s additional *mitzvos* — particularly the restrictions on the women he may marry or engage with — place him outside the scope of this permission? The Torah's focus in those laws is on marriage, and while this arrangement is not formal *kiddushin*, it approximates a quasi-marital relationship; *chazal* might well understand this woman to fall within the category of *zona* as they apply it to a *kohen*.

Rav says *mutar* — it is permitted. Shmuel says *assur* — it is forbidden.

### Challenge to Shmuel

Rav Nachman challenged the report of Shmuel's ruling: *ki havisos b'bei Mar Shmuel b'askamderi itatlisos* — when you were in Shmuel's house you must have been distracted (Rashi says *askamderi* refers to cards; the *Aruch* reads it as playing with puppies). You weren't paying attention, because had you been, you would have immediately challenged Shmuel. The challenge is straightforward: *chachamim omrim ein eved ivri kohen nirtza mipnei shena'aseh ba'al mum* — the *chachamim* teach that a *kohen eved ivri* cannot become *nirtza* because it would make him a *ba'al mum*. But why is that the *only* reason he cannot become *nirtza*? If Shmuel were right that a *kohen* cannot be given a *shifcha kna'anit*, then the *kohen eved ivri* could never satisfy the condition of *ahavti et adoni et ishti ve'et banai* — which, as we will see, requires that he actually have been given a slave wife and fathered children — and so the inability to become *nirtza* would have an entirely different and more basic cause. The fact that the *chachamim* locate the sole disqualification in the *ba'al mum* concern proves that the other preconditions *can* be met for a *kohen* — and that means the *shifcha kna'anit* is permitted to him. *Ve'su lo midi* — there is nothing more to say: Shmuel's position is untenable.

### Yefat To'ar

Still in the vein of a *kohen*'s sexual restrictions, the *gemara* asks: *kohen mah b'yefas to'ar* — may a *kohen* take a *yefat to'ar*, a captive woman from the battlefield whom he desires? The Torah describes this as a *chiddush* — a special permission extended to soldiers — and the same structural question applies: does this *chiddush* apply equally to a *kohen* and to a *yisrael*, or do the *kohen*'s additional *mitzvos* (particularly regarding forbidden women) exclude him?

Rav again says *mutar*; Shmuel again says *assur*.

The *gemara* then introduces a crucial distinction. As to *biah rishonah* — the first act of sexual relations — *de'chulei alma lo peligi deshari*: everyone agrees it is permitted. The reason: *lo dibrah Torah ela keneged yetzer hara* — the Torah speaks only in concession to the evil inclination. The lust of the moment, the heat of battle, the desire expressed in the verse *vachashakta bah* — all of this demands some concession, and the Torah provides it equally to *kohen* and *yisrael* alike.

The dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerns *biah shniyah* — the second act of sexual relations. Rav says *mutar*: once the Torah permitted this woman, the permission holds; *ishtarei ishtarei* — what was permitted was permitted. Shmuel says *assur*: by the time of the second act, the woman has gone through the process described in the *pesukim* — she cried for her parents, mourned her prior life, and converted — *de'hava lah giyores*, she has now become a *giyores*. And a *giyores* is precisely one of the women forbidden to a *kohen*, since *chazal* understood *zona* (one of the categories the *kohen* may not marry) to include a *giyores*. Whatever the Torah permitted was the *yefat to'ar* as a non-Jewish captive; it did not and could not permit the *kohen* to marry a *giyores*, which is a distinct and ongoing *issur*.

An alternative formulation of the dispute reverses the terms: according to *ika d'amri*, everyone agrees the *biah shniyah* is forbidden to a *kohen*, since by then she is a *giyores*; the dispute is whether the *biah rishonah* is permitted. Rav holds it is, since the Torah's concession to *yetzer hara* applies to *kohen* no less than to *yisrael*. Shmuel holds it is not: *kol heikha delo karinan beih ve'aveisa el toch beisecha lo karinan beih vera'isa bashivyah* — wherever the complete process (taking her home, completing the marriage) is not available to a man, the initial permission of *vera'ita bashivyah* never applied to him at all. For a *kohen*, since the *biah shniyah* and formal marriage are foreclosed, the Torah never extended the first concession either.

### When Sex Occurs

This discussion raises a deeper question about exactly when in the *pasuk*'s sequence the two acts of *biah* occur. The *pesukim* in *Devarim* read: *vera'ita bashivyah eshet yefat to'ar vachashakta bah velakachta lecha le'ishah veheveisa el toch beisecha* — and then only *ve'achar ken tavo eleha uvealtah* — and afterwards you may come to her and possess her. On a straightforward reading, all sexual relations occur after the entire process: bringing her home, the mourning period, and implicit conversion. The phrase *velakachta lecha le'ishah* means "take her to be your wife," initiating the process, not consummating a sexual act.

Yet *chazal*'s framework of *biah rishonah* before the conversion process requires locating a first act of sex somewhere prior to *veheveisa el toch beisecha*. The *gemara* understands this from *lo dibrah Torah ela keneged yetzer hara* combined with *vachashakta bah* — the lust of the moment demands an outlet that the Torah acknowledges. The exact verse from which *biah rishonah* is derived at the battlefield remains somewhat oblique, but the conceptual structure is that the Torah could not have withheld all concession from the man at the height of his desire.

The *baraita* addresses this through the phrase *besha'at shivyah* — at the moment of captivity — which anchors the first act to the battlefield. The word *melamed shelo yichtzenah bamilchamah* — he should not press/force her *bamilchamah* — is then difficult to interpret if one holds that the first act of sex does occur there. Three explanations emerge from the *rishonim*. The Rambam understands it to mean he should not do it there in the open battlefield but should take her somewhere more removed, though still before the formal process begins. A second approach attributed to the Re'em holds that *lo yichtzenah bamilchamah* means the Torah's permission for the first act of sex is conditional on her consent — she must agree at that moment, making it not an act of violent rape even if the context is inherently coercive. A third opinion understands it as limiting the first act to a single occurrence.

### Yetzer Hara Concession

Rashi's position, however, stands apart from all of these. Rashi reads *melamed shelo yichtzenah bamilchamah* straightforwardly: do not have sexual relations with her in battle at all. Wait until she has been brought home and completed the full process. On this reading there is no *biah rishonah* in the battlefield; all sexual relations occur after the *giyurus* is complete. The Torah's concession to *yetzer hara* lies not in permitting immediate sex on the battlefield but in establishing a formal process that channels the desire toward a legitimate outcome rather than leaving the woman as a sexual slave. The distinction between *biah rishonah* and *biah shniyah* for the *kohen* would then not be a chronological before-and-after conversion, but rather a matter of whether the Torah intended to permit even a single act (given the *kohen*'s prohibitions) or only ongoing marital relations — with the answer perhaps being that once is thinkable but the ongoing marriage is not.

The *Yerushalmi* actually records both of these as distinct opinions among the *tana'im*, which maps onto the debate between Rashi and the other *rishonim* in the *bavli*.

### Lesser of Two Evils

On 22a, the *gemara* articulates the underlying logic of the *yefat to'ar* concession through a vivid analogy: *mutav sheyochlu Yisrael basar temutos shechutos ve'al yochlu basar temutos neveilos* — better that Jews should eat meat from an animal that was going to die and was slaughtered while still alive, than that they should eat the meat of an animal that died without *shechita* and became a *neveilah*. The image is striking: a *treifa* on the verge of death, slaughtered at the last moment — the meat is technically *mutar*, but no one is pleased with the situation. The alternative, allowing it to die and become *neveilah*, is worse. So too here: the Torah fashions a barely-permissible path — forced *giyurus*, a quasi-consensual union — because the alternative, a man simply taking a non-Jewish woman as a permanent sexual slave or intermarrying with her outside of any conversion process, would be worse.

This line has been invoked by *poskim* in later generations when confronted with situations where the only available option is undesirable but the alternative is even more prohibited.

### Conversion Prevention

The Ramban develops a more structured account of what *issur* the Torah is protecting against. The verse says *vachashakta bah velakachta lecha le'ishah* — the man actually wants to *marry* this woman, not merely have a momentary encounter with her. The real danger is that without the Torah's intervention, he would keep her and marry her as a non-Jewish woman, violating *lo titchaten bam* — the prohibition of intermarriage. The Torah therefore channels the desire through a process of *giyurus*, even if effectively compelled, that places the union on a halakhically legitimate footing. The *biah rishonah*, too, is part of this framework: it is not purely an independent concession to *yetzer hara* but the beginning of a process that leads to a permissible marriage rather than an ongoing prohibited one. The Ramban further notes the element of *eishes ish*: the *baraita* states *va'afilu eishes ish* — even a woman married to a non-Jewish man is included in the Torah's permission. While the halakhic status of non-Jewish marriage vis-à-vis Jews is complex — the institution is recognized as part of *sheva mitzvos bnei Noach* but does not create a Jewish-law *issur* of *eishes ish* for a Jewish man — the Ramban explains that this is not a technical halakhic point. Rather, the Torah is emphasizing how far the concession extends: even in a case that is more *mechuar*, more morally degraded — tearing a woman from her husband — the Torah permits the process because the alternative (permanent forbidden cohabitation) is worse.

## Declaration & Conditions

### Timing of Declaration

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: the verse says *amar yomar ha'eved* — the slave shall say, shall say — teaching that he must make his declaration twice. Moreover, if he declared at the *beginning* of the six years and not at the end, *eino nirtza* — he does not undergo *retziah*. The source is *lo etzei chofshi* — he must say "I do not wish to go free" at the *moment of going free*, meaning at the conclusion of the six years.

Conversely, if he declared only at the end and not at the beginning, he also cannot become *nirtza*. The reason given is *shene'emar ha'eved* — it must be said *ke'shehu eved*, while he is still a slave. This initially seems strange, since at the end of six years he is still technically a slave. The *gemara* then poses the obvious objection: *atu sof shesh lav eved hu* — at the end of six years is he not a slave?

This leads directly to Rava's explanation.

### Last Penny Standard

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction: *bitchilas pruta achrona bsof pruta achrona* — the terms "beginning" and "end" refer not to the beginning and end of the six-year period, but to the beginning and end of the *last penny's worth* of service. When enough time remains that the master still has a monetary claim worth at least a *pruta* against the slave, the slave is fully an *eved* — but he is not yet at the *sha'at yetziah*, the moment of imminent departure. Once the slave has entered the last unit of time — the final hour or fraction of time worth less than a *pruta* — he is at the cusp of freedom and satisfies *lo etzei chofshi*, but he is no longer fully *ha'eved* in the complete proprietary sense.

To satisfy *both* requirements simultaneously, the declaration must be made at the precise juncture: before the last *pruta*'s worth expires (so that he is still fully an *eved*) and after all prior conditions — wife and children — are already in place. The precision required here is striking, and one senses that the halakha is not designed to make the path to *nirtza* an easy one.

### Wife & Children

A *baraita* sets out additional conditions. If the slave has a wife and children (the *shifcha kna'anit* and the children born from that union) but his master does not, *eino nirtza* — the process cannot proceed. The source is *ki ahevcha ve'et beisecha* — "because he loves you and your household" — implying the master must also have a household that parallels his own.

Conversely, if the master has a wife and children but the slave does not, *eino nirtza* — he cannot say *ahavti et adoni et ishti ve'et banai*. As noted above, this is precisely why the *gemara* proved that even a *kohen eved ivri* must be permitted a *shifcha kna'anit*: the *only* reason the *chachamim* gave for excluding a *kohen* from *nirtza* was *ba'al mum*, proving that the preliminary stage of wife and children is available to him.

### Love & Health

The conditions extend to the relational and physical. If the slave loves his master but his master does not love him, *eino nirtza* — the verse *ki ahevcha* ("because he loves you") requires the master's affection to be genuine. If the master loves the slave but the slave does not love his master, *eino nirtza* — the verse *ki tov lo imach* ("for it is good for him with you") must be true, and it cannot be if the slave is not genuinely content. The slave may have pragmatic reasons for wishing to remain — he loves his wife and children, he has no prospects outside — but if he cannot sincerely say he loves his master, the *retziah* is unavailable.

Similarly, if the slave is ill and his master is not, *eino nirtza* — *ki tov lo imach* cannot be said when he is suffering. If the master is ill and the slave is not, *eino nirtza* — *imach* requires good conditions on the master's side as well. The case of both being ill simultaneously is posed as a *ba'aya* by Rav Bibi bar Abaye: is *imach* a requirement of equality, such that if both are equally ill the condition is met, or does *ki tov lo* mean that the situation must be genuinely good — not merely ill together? The *gemara* leaves this as *teiku*.

### Equal Treatment

A *baraita* then unpacks the phrase *ki tov lo imach* in its material dimensions, and this passage is one of the most celebrated in this entire sugya. *Imach bema'achal* — with you in food; *imach bemishte* — with you in drink; *imach belo'in* — with you in lodging. The slave must receive exactly what the master provides for himself:

*Shelo tehei atah ochel pas nekiyah vehu ochel pas kivar* — you may not eat fine white bread while he eats coarse bran bread. *Atah shoseh yayin yashan vehu shoseh yayin chadash* — you may not drink aged wine while he drinks new wine. *Atah yashen al gabei mochin vehu yashen al gabei teven* — you may not sleep on a featherbed while he sleeps on straw.

From here the *gemara* derives the famous principle: *mikan amru kol hakoneh eved Ivri kekoneh adon le'atzmo* — whoever acquires an *eved Ivri* is as if he has acquired a master for himself. The Ramban sharpens this: if there is only one pillow in the house, giving it to oneself would create an inequality, violating *imach*; throwing it away so neither has it would be *middat Sedom*. The slave therefore takes precedence — he receives the single pillow. In that sense the *eved Ivri* is not merely the master's equal but, where resources are limited, his superior.

### Support Obligations

A further *baraita* addresses the master's obligations toward the slave's family. The verse *veyatza imach hu uvanav imo* — he shall leave you, he and his sons with him — prompts Rabbi Shimon to ask: if he was sold into slavery, who sold his sons? They were not sold. Yet the verse says they leave with him at *yovel*, implying they were present throughout the servitude. *Mikan sherabo chayav bemezonos banav* — from here we learn that the master was obligated to provide for the slave's children throughout the period of service.

An identical structure applies to the wife: *im ba'al ishah hu veyatza ishto imo* — if he was married, his wife leaves with him. Rabbi Shimon again asks: who sold his wife? The answer: *mikan sherabo chayav bemezonos ishto* — the master must support the wife as well.

The *gemara* explains why both rulings are necessary. Had the Torah stated only the obligation regarding children, one might have said the wife can find employment and feed herself. Had it stated only the wife, one might have said that since it is not customary for a woman *lehadori* — to go door to door begging — the master need not provide for children, who can beg. Each case is therefore independently required.

## Ear-Piercing Mechanics

### Through Ear & Door

A *baraita* analyzes the precise procedure of the *retziah*. Had the verse said only *be'ozno uvadelet* — in his ear and in the door — one might have thought: place his ear against the door and then pierce into the door only, the ear being merely positioned nearby. The word *be'ozno* forces us to require that the ear itself be pierced. But then, had the verse said only *be'ozno*, I might have thought: pierce the ear first, then separately make a hole in the door and align them. *Talmud lomar be'ozno uvadelet* — both together — teaches: *dokeir veholech ad shemmagi'a etzel dalet* — one pierces through the ear and continues until reaching the door. The ear must be pressed against the door, and a single act of piercing passes through both.

### Standing Door

One might have thought the door need not be standing and attached; perhaps even a detached door panel would suffice. *Talmud lomar mezuza* — the verse's juxtaposition with *mezuza* teaches: *mah mezuza me'umad af dalet nami me'umad* — just as a *mezuza* is only called by that name when the doorframe stands in its permanent upright position, so too the door for the *retziah* must be standing, properly installed.

### Ear at Sinai

Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai offered a famous *mashal* — Rashi understands *kemin chomer* as "like a fragrant mixture," meaning a beautifully composed explanation; Tosafot understand it as "like a story" — to illuminate why the ear specifically is the organ chosen for piercing.

*Mah nishtanna ozen mikkol ha'evarim shebaguf* — why was the ear singled out from all the organs of the body? The Holy One, blessed be He, declared: *ozen sheshame'ah koli al har Sinai besha'ah she'amarti ki li benei yisrael avadim velo avadim la'avadim* — the ear that heard My voice at Sinai when I said "the children of Israel are My servants and not servants to servants" — *vehalach zeh veqana adon le'atzmo* — and yet this person went and acquired a master for himself. That ear must be pierced.

The irony is layered: the very foundation of *mishpatim*, which begins with the laws of slavery, is prefaced by *anochi Hashem Elohecha asher hotzetecha me'eretz mitzrayim mibbeit avadim* — I freed you from a house of slaves. The first substantial legislation after *maamad har Sinai* concerns how one frees one's slave after six years. Yet this slave chooses to perpetuate his bondage — and his ear, which heard the proclamation of freedom, is marked accordingly.

### Door as Witness

Rabbi Shimon b'Rebbi offered a parallel *mashal* focusing on the door and doorpost rather than the ear. *Mah nishtanna dalet umezuza mikkol kelim shebabayit* — why were the door and doorpost chosen from all the vessels of the house? The Holy One, blessed be He, declared: *dalet umezuza shehayu edim bemitzrayim besha'ah shepasachti al hamashkof ve'al shetei hamezuzot* — the door and the doorpost were witnesses in Egypt when I passed over the lintel and the two doorposts. *Ve'amarti ki li benei Yisrael avadim velo avadim la'avadim vehotzetim me'avdut lecherut* — I declared they are My servants and freed them from slavery to freedom. *Vehalach zeh veqana adon le'atzmo* — and this one went and acquired a master for himself. *Yeratza bifneihem* — let him be pierced before them, before these witnesses to his people's redemption, witnesses to the fact that he should not be enslaving himself again.

The *mashal* of Rabbi Shimon prompts reflection on the distinction between the initial six-year period and the *retziah*. For the first six years, the slave was a *gannav* who could not pay his debt — he did not choose slavery freely, or he was driven to *moker atzmo* by economic desperation. One can understand, if not excuse, that first entry into servitude. But to then choose *ve'avado le'olam* — to serve forever — is something else entirely, something that demands a symbolic act before these ancient witnesses to say: this choice runs against everything your history and your God intended for you.
