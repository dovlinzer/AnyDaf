# Kiddushin 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nirtza Overview

### Nirtza Freedom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַנִּרְצָע נִקְנֶה בִּרְצִיעָה. דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרָצַע אֲדֹנָיו אֶת אׇזְנוֹ בַּמַּרְצֵעַ וְגוֹ׳״
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the mishna, which teaches: **And** a Hebrew slave **who is pierced** after serving six years **is acquired** as a slave for a longer period **through piercing his ear with an awl.** The Gemara explains that this is **as it is written: "And his master shall pierce his ear with an awl,** and he shall serve him forever" (Exodus 21:6).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹנֶה אֶת עַצְמוֹ בַּיּוֹבֵל וּבְמִיתַת הָאָדוֹן. דִּכְתִיב: ״וַעֲבָדוֹ״ – וְלֹא אֶת הַבֵּן וְלֹא אֶת הַבַּת. ״לְעֹלָם״ – לְעוֹלָמוֹ שֶׁל יוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that a pierced slave **acquires himself through** the advent of **the Jubilee** Year **and through the master's death.** The Gemara explains that this is **as it is written: "And he shall serve him** forever" (Exodus 21:6). This term indicates that he serves only the master, **but not the son and not the daughter.** The term **"forever"** is referring **to** the **forever** represented **by the Jubilee** Year. The word "forever" does not mean for eternity, but refers to the end of the cycle of the Jubilee Year.

The *mishnah* on Kiddushin 22 turns to the *nirtza* — the *eved ivri* whose ear is pierced and who thereby commits to extended service beyond the initial six-year term. The *mishnah* states: *ve'hanirtza nikneh birtziah* — the one who has his ear pierced is acquired through that act of piercing, and he then works until the *yovel*. The *gemara* cites the verse: *veratza adonav et ozno bamarzea* — his master shall pierce his ear with an awl.

The *mishnah* further teaches that the *nirtza* acquires his freedom in two ways: through the *yovel* and through the death of the master. The source for freedom at *yovel* is the explicit verse in *Behar*. The source for freedom upon the master's death is the phrase *va'avado* — serve *him* — followed by *velo es haben velo es habas*, meaning not the son and not the daughter. The Rambam notes that *le'olam* does not independently teach that service continues until the *yovel*; rather, it is forced into that reading by the explicit *pasuk* in *Behar*. The *nirtza* relationship, unlike the six-year term which carries the character of *schirus* (hired work), involves a more total form of ownership — and correspondingly, the death of the master brings it to an end in a way that differs from the ordinary six-year arrangement, where service can in certain circumstances continue for the master's son.

### Piercing Tools

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מַרְצֵעַ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא מַרְצֵעַ, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת הַסּוֹל וְהַסִּירָא וְהַמַּחַט וְהַמַּקְדֵּחַ וְהַמַּכְתֵּב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלָקַחְתָּ״ – לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁנִּקַּח בַּיָּד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** From the term **"an awl" I have** derived **only** that a master can pierce the ear of a Hebrew slave **with an awl. From where** do I derive that **a sharp thorn [*sol*], a thorn [*sira*], a needle, an auger, and a stylus** used to engrave in wax may be used? **The verse states: "And you shall take** the awl" (Deuteronomy 15:17). This term "and you shall take" serves **to include any implement that can be taken in the** master's **hand.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: מָה מַרְצֵעַ מְיוּחָד שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת – אַף כֹּל שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״הַמַּרְצֵעַ״ – לְהָבִיא הַמַּרְצֵעַ הַגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** Not all of these utensils may be used for piercing. **Just as an awl is unique** in that it is made **of metal, so too any** tool made **of metal** can be used; one may not pierce a slave's ear with non-metal implements. **Alternatively,** the verse states: "And you shall take **the awl," to include the large awl** for piercing.

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *ein li ela marzea* — I would only know that an awl is valid. The *tanna* then asks: how do we know to include *hasol* (which Rashi identifies as a piece of wood), *hasira* (a thorn), *hamachat* (a needle), *hamakdeach* (some other type of piercing implement), and *hamichtav* (a stylus)? The answer is *talmud lomar velakachta* — the word "and you shall take" comes to include anything that can be taken by hand. This is the position of Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda.

Rabbi, however, takes a different view: *mah marzea meyuchad shel matechet af kol shel matechet* — just as the awl is specifically of metal, so too anything valid for this purpose must be of metal, excluding the wooden implements mentioned above. The *baraita* adds a further point: *davar acher* — *hamartzea lehavi hamarzea hagadol* — the definite article *ha-* in *hamartzea* comes to include even a large awl, though the precise significance of this will emerge below.

### Piercing Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: יוּדָן בְּרִיבִּי הָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ: כְּשֶׁהֵן רוֹצְעִים אֵין רוֹצְעִים אֶלָּא בַּמִּילְתָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין עֶבֶד עִבְרִי כֹּהֵן נִרְצָע, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּעַל מוּם. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: בְּמִילְּתָא הֵם רוֹצְעִים – הֵיאַךְ עֶבֶד עִבְרִי כֹּהֵן יֵעָשֶׂה בַּעַל מוּם? הָא אֵין נִרְצָע אֶלָּא בְּגוֹבַהּ שֶׁל אֹזֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: Yudan the Distinguished would teach** as follows: **When they pierce, they pierce only the earlobe. And the Rabbis say:** The piercing is not performed on the earlobe, as the *halakha* is that **a Hebrew slave** who is **a priest is not pierced** at all **because** the piercing **renders him blemished** and unfit to serve in the Temple. **And if you say** that **they pierce** a slave's **earlobe, how does a Hebrew slave** who is **a priest become blemished** through piercing? A pierced earlobe is not considered a blemish. This indicates that **he is pierced only on the upper part of the ear** through the cartilage.

Rabbi Elazar Yudan Beribbi — Rashi explains that *beribbi* denotes a man of prestige — taught that when the piercing is performed, it is done only in the *milsa*, the soft hanging lobe of the ear, not in the cartilage or main body of the ear itself.

The *chachamim* disagree and adduce a compelling proof: *ein eved ivri kohen nirtza mipnei shena'aseh ba'al mum* — a *kohen* who is an *eved ivri* does not undergo the *retziah* because it would render him a *ba'al mum*. Now, if the piercing were done in the lobe — as one would do for an earring — it would not disqualify a *kohen* from Temple service. The fact that the *halacha* excludes a *kohen* because of *ba'al mum* is therefore proof that the piercing is done in the ear itself, in the place where a permanent hole does indeed constitute a disqualifying blemish. The principle is stated directly: *ein retziah ela begova shel ozen* — the piercing is only in the height, that is, the upper body, of the ear.

The Ramban connects this discussion to the matter of the large *marzea*: once the possibility of using a large awl is introduced, the question of *ba'al mum* for a *kohen* becomes acute. Rabbi Elazar Yudan Beribbi's response was that the *retziah* must therefore be in the lobe. The *chachamim* counter that the *halacha* excluding a *kohen* from *retziah* entirely proves the location is precisely where a *ba'al mum* can result.

### Metal vs. Other

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי דָּרֵישׁ כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי: ״וְלָקַחְתָּ״ – כָּלַל. ״מַרְצֵעַ״ – פָּרַט. ״בְּאׇזְנוֹ וּבַדֶּלֶת״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל. כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל, אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט. מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת, אַף כֹּל שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **expounds** the verses by means of the hermeneutical principle of reading the verse as consisting of **generalizations and details.** Accordingly, he maintains that the term **"and you shall take"** is **a generalization** that indicates piercing may be performed with any implement. **"An awl"** is **a detail,** and when the verse states: **"Through his ear and into the door"** (Deuteronomy 15:17), **it then generalized** again. When the verse writes **a generalization and a detail and a generalization, you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail: Just as the explicit detail** mentions an awl, which is made **of metal, so too, any** utensil used for piercing must be made **of metal.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי דָּרֵישׁ רִיבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי: ״וְלָקַחְתָּ״ – רִיבָּה. ״מַרְצֵעַ״ – מִיעֵט. ״בְּאׇזְנוֹ וּבַדֶּלֶת״ – חָזַר וְרִיבָּה. רִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט וְרִיבָּה, רִיבָּה הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **Rabbi Yosei expounds** the verses by means of the hermeneutical principle of reading the verse as consisting of **amplifications and restrictions.** Accordingly, he learns that the term **"and you shall take" amplifies,** i.e., it includes any item that can be taken in one's hand. The term **"an awl" restricts.** When the verse states **"through his ear and into the door," it then amplifies again.** When the verse **amplifies, restricts, and amplifies, it has amplified** the *halakha* to include **everything** except one item.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי – רַבִּי כֹּל מִילֵּי, מַאי מַיעֵט – מַיעֵט סַם.
>
> **Translation:** **What has it amplified,** i.e., included beyond an awl? **It has amplified** the category to include **everything** that can pierce an ear. **What has it restricted** from inclusion in the category? It excludes only the method that is most dissimilar to the use of an awl, which is **a corrosive substance.** Producing a hole in the slave's ear with a substance of this kind is not a valid form of piercing.

The *gemara* now addresses the underlying hermeneutic dispute between Rabbi and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda. What exactly divides them?

*Rebbi doresh klal uprat* — Rabbi employs the hermeneutic of *klal u'prat u'klal*, general-specific-general. In the verse, *velakachta* is a *klal* (general statement); *marzea* is the *prat* (specific); and *be'ozno uvadelet* — the ear and the door — constitutes a return to the general. The operative rule is: *klal u'prat u'klal iy ata dan ela ke'ein haprat* — one may include only what resembles the specific case. Since the *marzea* is of metal, anything of metal qualifies, and nothing else does.

The mechanics of this principle are worth pausing on. When a *klal* is followed by a *prat* alone — general then specific — the result is that we follow the specific. Similarly, a *prat* followed by a *klal* — specific then general — leaves us with the general. It is only when the pattern is general-specific-general that the principle of *ke'ein haprat* applies: one generalizes, but only to what is similar in kind to the specific item named. By identifying the *marzea* as the controlling *prat*, and metal as its defining characteristic, Rabbi concludes that any metal implement is valid.

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, *doresh ribui u'miut* — employs the hermeneutic of expansion and contraction. He reads the same words but frames them not as *klal* and *prat* but as *ribui* (expansive language) and *miut* (contracting language). *Velakachta* expands; *marzea* contracts; *be'ozno uvadelet* expands again. When the structure is *ribui-miut-ribui*, the result is *riba hakol* — nearly everything is included. What is excluded? *Miet sam* — something that achieves the piercing not through a pointed implement but through an entirely different mechanism, such as burning with acid. The contracting term does not define a category of similar items; it merely excludes what is most dissimilar. Because the middle term functions as a contraction rather than a specification, it does not require that other valid implements resemble the *marzea* in material. Wood is therefore not excluded under this reading.

The conceptual difference is subtle but real. Under *klal u'prat u'klal*, the specific term anchors the interpretation — everything valid must be *like* it. Under *ribui u'miut u'ribui*, the middle term only contracts the outer expansiveness; it does not define the genus. This places the *ribui u'miut* method one step further toward inclusion than *klal u'prat u'klal*.

### Big Martzea

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״הַמַּרְצֵעַ״ – לְהָבִיא מַרְצֵעַ הַגָּדוֹל. מַאי מַשְׁמַע? כִּדְאָמַר רָבָא: ״הַיָּרֵךְ״ – הַמְיוּמֶּנֶת שֶׁבַּיָּרֵךְ, הָכָא נָמֵי: ״הַמַּרְצֵעַ״ – מְיוּחָד שֶׁבַּמַּרְצְעִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above: **"The awl,"** this serves **to include the large awl** for piercing. The Gemara asks: **From where may** it **be inferred** that this expression is referring to a large awl? The Gemara answers that this is **as Rava said** with regard to the verse: "The sinew of the thigh vein which is upon the hollow of **the thigh"** (Genesis 32:33), that this is referring to **the most important [*meyummenet*]** part of **the thigh.** The definite article indicates something clear and obvious. **Here too,** the term **"the awl"** is referring to **a special awl.**

The *gemara* inquires: *mai mashma* — how does one derive from the word *hamartzea* (with the definite article) that it includes a large awl? The answer is by analogy to Rava's principle regarding *gid hanasheh*: just as *asher kaf hayarech* — "the thigh" — singles out the prominent one, the right thigh, so too *hamartzea* singles out the prominent *marzea*, the significant or large one. The *gemara* then returns to the positions of Rabbi Elazar Yudan Beribbi and the *chachamim* as already analyzed above, affirming that the *halacha* excludes a *kohen* from *retziah* because of *ba'al mum*, which in turn proves the piercing is performed in the upper portion of the ear.

## Kohen Restrictions

### Kohen Nirtza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵעָשֶׂה בַּעַל מוּם! אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְשָׁב אֶל מִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ״ – לְמוּחְזָק שֶׁבְּמִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** let him be pierced and **be rendered blemished** and disqualified for Temple service. Why is it prohibited to do this? **Rabba bar Rav Sheila says: The verse states** concerning a Hebrew slave at the end of his servitude: **"And he shall return to his own family"** (Leviticus 25:41), i.e., **to his status in his family.** He must be able to return to the position he had as a member of his family. If he was rendered a blemished priest while a slave, once he is emancipated he can no longer return to his status as a priest who can perform the Temple service.

Having established that a *kohen eved ivri* cannot become *nirtza* because it would render him a *ba'al mum*, the *gemara* asks where the prohibition against making him a *ba'al mum* comes from in the first place. There is indeed a prohibition against creating a *mum b'kodashim* — a blemish in sacrificial animals — but what is the source for the prohibition against causing a *kohen* to become a *ba'al mum*, even though such a blemish would disqualify him from *ha'avoda*?

Rabba bar Rav Shila answers from the verse: *veshav el mishpachto* — he shall return to his family. This refers to the *eved ivri* going free at *yovel*, and the *gemara* understands it as meaning *lemuchzak sheb'mishpachto* — he must return to the status he held within his family before his servitude. Since a *kohen eved ivri* who becomes *nirtza* would be pierced in the upper ear and rendered a *ba'al mum*, foreclosing his return to priestly service, the verse's requirement that he be restored to his prior status prohibits this outcome.

### Kohen & Shifcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: עֶבֶד עִבְרִי כֹּהֵן, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּמְסוֹר לוֹ רַבּוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית? חִידּוּשׁ הוּא, לָא שְׁנָא כֹּהֲנִים וְלָא שְׁנָא יִשְׂרָאֵל, אוֹ דִילְמָא: שָׁאנֵי כֹּהֲנִים הוֹאִיל וְרִיבָּה בָּהֶן הַכָּתוּב מִצְוֹת יְתֵירוֹת? רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **a Hebrew slave** who is **a priest, what is** the *halakha* concerning the permissibility **for his master to provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant** with whom to engage in sexual intercourse? The Gemara analyzes the two sides of the dilemma: Does one say that the *halakha* permitting a Hebrew slave to engage in intercourse with a Canaanite maidservant **is** a halakhic **novelty,** as a Jew is generally prohibited from engaging in intercourse with a gentile, and in light of this novelty, **it is no different** in the case of **priests and no different** in the case of **an Israelite? Or perhaps** the case of **priests is different** from Israelites, **since the Torah includes additional mitzvot for them,** which do not apply to all Jews. Therefore, it is prohibited for a priest to engage in sexual intercourse with a Canaanite maidservant, despite the fact she is permitted to a non-priest. The *amora'im* disagreed with regard to this issue. **Rav said: It is permitted** for the master to provide him with a Canaanite maidservant, **and Shmuel said it is prohibited.**

The *gemara* now poses a fresh question: *eved ivri kohen mahu sheyimsor lo rabo shifcha kna'anit* — may a master assign a non-Jewish female slave to a *kohen* who is an *eved ivri* for the purpose of fathering children? The Torah permits a master to do this for an ordinary *eved ivri* (as stated: *im adonav yiten lo isha*), but the question is whether this permission extends to a *kohen*.

The case is a *chiddush* to begin with: ordinarily there is a prohibition against a man having sexual relations with a non-Jewish slave, rooted in the verse *lo yikadesh velo yikadesha*. The Torah's permit for the *eved ivri* overrides this *issur*. Does this override apply equally to a *kohen*, or do the *kohen*'s additional *mitzvos* — particularly the restrictions on the women he may marry or engage with — place him outside the scope of this permission? The Torah's focus in those laws is on marriage, and while this arrangement is not formal *kiddushin*, it approximates a quasi-marital relationship; *chazal* might well understand this woman to fall within the category of *zona* as they apply it to a *kohen*.

Rav says *mutar* — it is permitted. Shmuel says *assur* — it is forbidden.

### Challenge to Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְרַב עָנָן: כִּי הֲוֵיתוּ בֵּי מָר שְׁמוּאֵל, בְּאִיסְקוּמְדְּרֵי אִיטַּלְּלִיתוּ? מַאי טַעְמָא לָא תֵּימְרוּ לֵיהּ מֵהָא: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין עֶבֶד עִבְרִי כֹּהֵן נִרְצָע מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּעַל מוּם. וְאִם תֹּאמַר אֵין רַבּוֹ מוֹסֵר לוֹ שִׁפְחָה כְּנַעֲנִית, תִּיפּוֹק לִי דְּבָעֵינָא: ״אָהַבְתִּי אֶת אֲדֹנִי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי וְאֶת בָּנָי״ וְלֵיכָּא, תּוּ לָא מִידִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to Rav Anan: When you were** studying **in the house of Mar Shmuel** did **you play with tokens [*iskumadri*]?** Didn't you take your studies seriously? **What is the reason** that **you do not say** a proof **to him from that** which we learned: **And the Rabbis say: A Hebrew slave** who is **a priest is not pierced** at all **because** piercing **renders him blemished.** Rav Naḥman explains the proof: **And if you say** that **if his master cannot provide him** with **a Canaanite maidservant,** it is not even relevant to suggest that a slave of priestly lineage could be pierced, as the fact that he cannot be pierced **could be derived** from another point, **as I require** the slave to say: **"I love my master, my wife, and my children"** (Exodus 21:5), **and** that declaration **cannot** be issued by a priest if his master may not provide him with a Canaanite maidservant. And **nothing more** can be answered to this proof. The reason given by the Rabbis proves that a Canaanite maidservant can be provided even to a priest.

Rav Nachman challenged the report of Shmuel's ruling: *ki havisos b'bei Mar Shmuel b'askamderi itatlisos* — when you were in Shmuel's house you must have been distracted (Rashi says *askamderi* refers to cards; the *Aruch* reads it as playing with puppies). You weren't paying attention, because had you been, you would have immediately challenged Shmuel. The challenge is straightforward: *chachamim omrim ein eved ivri kohen nirtza mipnei shena'aseh ba'al mum* — the *chachamim* teach that a *kohen eved ivri* cannot become *nirtza* because it would make him a *ba'al mum*. But why is that the *only* reason he cannot become *nirtza*? If Shmuel were right that a *kohen* cannot be given a *shifcha kna'anit*, then the *kohen eved ivri* could never satisfy the condition of *ahavti et adoni et ishti ve'et banai* — which, as we will see, requires that he actually have been given a slave wife and fathered children — and so the inability to become *nirtza* would have an entirely different and more basic cause. The fact that the *chachamim* locate the sole disqualification in the *ba'al mum* concern proves that the other preconditions *can* be met for a *kohen* — and that means the *shifcha kna'anit* is permitted to him. *Ve'su lo midi* — there is nothing more to say: Shmuel's position is untenable.

### Yefat To'ar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כֹּהֵן, מַהוּ בִּיפַת תּוֹאַר? חִידּוּשׁ הוּא, לָא שְׁנָא כֹּהֵן וְלָא שְׁנָא יִשְׂרָאֵל, אוֹ דִילְמָא שָׁאנֵי כֹּהֲנִים, הוֹאִיל וְרִיבָּה בָּהֶן מִצְוֹת יְתֵרוֹת? רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **a dilemma was raised before them: What is** the *halakha* with regard to the permissibility **for a priest** who goes to war to engage in intercourse **with a beautiful woman** captured in that war? Does one say that the case of a beautiful woman **is a novelty** in that the Torah permits a man to engage in intercourse with a gentile woman? Consequently, it **is no different** in the case of **a priest and no different** in the case of **an Israelite,** as both are permitted to engage in intercourse with this woman. **Or perhaps** the case of **priests is different, since** the Torah **includes additional mitzvot for them? Rav said:** It is **permitted, and Shmuel said:** It is **prohibited.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבִיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּשְׁרֵי, דְּלֹא דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד יֵצֶר הָרָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **With regard to the first act of sexual intercourse** between the soldier priest and the gentile woman, **everyone agrees that** it is permitted, **as the Torah spoke only in response to the evil inclination,** and the evil inclination of a priest is as strong as that of an Israelite. This passage serves to prevent intercourse performed in a prohibited manner, which is relevant to a priest as well.

Still in the vein of a *kohen*'s sexual restrictions, the *gemara* asks: *kohen mah b'yefas to'ar* — may a *kohen* take a *yefat to'ar*, a captive woman from the battlefield whom he desires? The Torah describes this as a *chiddush* — a special permission extended to soldiers — and the same structural question applies: does this *chiddush* apply equally to a *kohen* and to a *yisrael*, or do the *kohen*'s additional *mitzvos* (particularly regarding forbidden women) exclude him?

Rav again says *mutar*; Shmuel again says *assur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּבִיאָה שְׁנִיָּה, רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אָסוּר. רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר, הוֹאִיל וְאִישְׁתְּרַיא – אִישְׁתְּרַי. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אָסוּר, דְּהָא הָוְיָא לַהּ גִּיּוֹרֶת, וְגִיּוֹרֶת לְכֹהֵן לָא חַזְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When they disagree** it is **with regard to the second act of sexual intercourse.** Is a priest permitted to bring the captive into his house, convert her, and marry her? **Rav said** it is **permitted, and Shmuel said** it is **prohibited.** Their reasoning is as follows: **Rav said** it is **permitted: Since** she was **permitted** to him once, she remains **permitted** to him. **And Shmuel said** it is **prohibited, as** ultimately **she is a convert, and a convert is not fit to** marry **a priest.**

The *gemara* then introduces a crucial distinction. As to *biah rishonah* — the first act of sexual relations — *de'chulei alma lo peligi deshari*: everyone agrees it is permitted. The reason: *lo dibrah Torah ela keneged yetzer hara* — the Torah speaks only in concession to the evil inclination. The lust of the moment, the heat of battle, the desire expressed in the verse *vachashakta bah* — all of this demands some concession, and the Torah provides it equally to *kohen* and *yisrael* alike.

The dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerns *biah shniyah* — the second act of sexual relations. Rav says *mutar*: once the Torah permitted this woman, the permission holds; *ishtarei ishtarei* — what was permitted was permitted. Shmuel says *assur*: by the time of the second act, the woman has gone through the process described in the *pesukim* — she cried for her parents, mourned her prior life, and converted — *de'hava lah giyores*, she has now become a *giyores*. And a *giyores* is precisely one of the women forbidden to a *kohen*, since *chazal* understood *zona* (one of the categories the *kohen* may not marry) to include a *giyores*. Whatever the Torah permitted was the *yefat to'ar* as a non-Jewish captive; it did not and could not permit the *kohen* to marry a *giyores*, which is a distinct and ongoing *issur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּבִיאָה שְׁנִיָּה כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲסִירָא, דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ גִּיּוֹרֶת. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּבִיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר, דְּהָא לֹא דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד יֵצֶר הָרָע, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אָסוּר, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּקָרֵינָא בַּיהּ: ״וַהֲבֵאתָהּ אֶל תּוֹךְ בֵּיתֶךָ״ – קָרֵינָא בַּיהּ: ״וְרָאִיתָ בַּשִּׁבְיָה״. כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּלָא קָרֵינָא בַּיהּ: ״וַהֲבֵאתָהּ אֶל תּוֹךְ בֵּיתֶךָ״ – לָא קָרֵינָא בַּיהּ: ״וְרָאִיתָ בַּשִּׁבְיָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** a different version of this dispute. **With regard to the second act of intercourse everyone agrees that it is prohibited, as she is a convert,** and a priest may not marry a convert. **When they disagree** it is **with regard to the first act of intercourse. Rav said** it is **permitted, as the Torah spoke only in response to the evil inclination. And Shmuel said it is prohibited,** as **any** situation **that one can read with regard to it: "Then you shall bring her home to your house"** (Deuteronomy 21:12), **one** can also **read** and fulfill **with regard to** the earlier command of: **"And see among the captives** a beautiful woman, and you have a desire for her, and would take her to you as a wife" (Deuteronomy 21:11). Conversely, **any** situation **that one cannot read with regard to it: "Then you shall bring her home to your house,"** i.e., if the soldier may not marry her, **one does not read with regard to it: "And sees among the captives,"** and one may not engage in sexual intercourse with her.

An alternative formulation of the dispute reverses the terms: according to *ika d'amri*, everyone agrees the *biah shniyah* is forbidden to a *kohen*, since by then she is a *giyores*; the dispute is whether the *biah rishonah* is permitted. Rav holds it is, since the Torah's concession to *yetzer hara* applies to *kohen* no less than to *yisrael*. Shmuel holds it is not: *kol heikha delo karinan beih ve'aveisa el toch beisecha lo karinan beih vera'isa bashivyah* — wherever the complete process (taking her home, completing the marriage) is not available to a man, the initial permission of *vera'ita bashivyah* never applied to him at all. For a *kohen*, since the *biah shniyah* and formal marriage are foreclosed, the Torah never extended the first concession either.

### When Sex Occurs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְרָאִיתָ בַּשִּׁבְיָה״ – בִּשְׁעַת שִׁבְיָהּ. ״אֵשֶׁת״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ. ״יְפַת תּוֹאַר״ – לֹא דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד יֵצֶר הָרָע. מוּטָב שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּשַׂר תְּמוּתוֹת שְׁחוּטוֹת, וְאַל יֹאכְלוּ בְּשַׂר תְּמוּתוֹת נְבֵילוֹת. ״וְחָשַׁקְתָּ״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָהּ נָאָה. ״בָּהּ״ – וְלֹא בָּהּ וּבַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to a beautiful captive, the verse states: **"And sees among the captives,"** teaching that this *halakha* applies only if he notices her **when she is a captive.** The expression **"a woman"** teaches that she is permitted **even** if she is **a married woman.** The phrase **"a beautiful woman"** indicates that **the Torah** here **spoke only in response to the evil inclination,** as it is due to her beauty that he desired her. And why does the Torah permit this? **It is preferable for Jews to eat the meat of dying animals that were slaughtered, and let them not eat the meat of dying animals** that were not slaughtered but which will become **carcasses.** In other words, it is preferable for this act to be performed in a somewhat permitted way rather than in a manner that is entirely prohibited. The expression: **"And you have a desire** for her and would take her to you as a wife" (Deuteronomy 21:11), teaches that this *halakha* applies **even if she is not pretty,** as this is a subjective judgment dependent on one's desire. The term **"for her"** indicates that he may take her, **but not her and another** woman. A soldier is allowed to take only one captive in this manner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלָקַחְתָּ״ – לִיקּוּחִין יֵשׁ לְךָ בָּהּ. ״לְךָ לְאִשָּׁה״ – שֶׁלֹּא יִקַּח שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, אַחַת לוֹ וְאַחַת לְאָבִיו, אַחַת לוֹ וְאַחַת לִבְנוֹ. ״וַהֲבֵאתָהּ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלֹּא יִלְחָצֶנָּה בַּמִּלְחָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase **"and would take her"** teaches: **You have** the ability to **take her,** i.e., to marry her. **"To you as a wife"** teaches **that he may not take two women, one for him and one for his father,** or **one for him and one for his son.** The verse: **"Then you shall bring her** home into your house" (Deuteronomy 21:12), **teaches that he should not pressure her** to engage in sexual intercourse **during the war,** but he should first take her into his home.

This discussion raises a deeper question about exactly when in the *pasuk*'s sequence the two acts of *biah* occur. The *pesukim* in *Devarim* read: *vera'ita bashivyah eshet yefat to'ar vachashakta bah velakachta lecha le'ishah veheveisa el toch beisecha* — and then only *ve'achar ken tavo eleha uvealtah* — and afterwards you may come to her and possess her. On a straightforward reading, all sexual relations occur after the entire process: bringing her home, the mourning period, and implicit conversion. The phrase *velakachta lecha le'ishah* means "take her to be your wife," initiating the process, not consummating a sexual act.

Yet *chazal*'s framework of *biah rishonah* before the conversion process requires locating a first act of sex somewhere prior to *veheveisa el toch beisecha*. The *gemara* understands this from *lo dibrah Torah ela keneged yetzer hara* combined with *vachashakta bah* — the lust of the moment demands an outlet that the Torah acknowledges. The exact verse from which *biah rishonah* is derived at the battlefield remains somewhat oblique, but the conceptual structure is that the Torah could not have withheld all concession from the man at the height of his desire.

The *baraita* addresses this through the phrase *besha'at shivyah* — at the moment of captivity — which anchors the first act to the battlefield. The word *melamed shelo yichtzenah bamilchamah* — he should not press/force her *bamilchamah* — is then difficult to interpret if one holds that the first act of sex does occur there. Three explanations emerge from the *rishonim*. The Rambam understands it to mean he should not do it there in the open battlefield but should take her somewhere more removed, though still before the formal process begins. A second approach attributed to the Re'em holds that *lo yichtzenah bamilchamah* means the Torah's permission for the first act of sex is conditional on her consent — she must agree at that moment, making it not an act of violent rape even if the context is inherently coercive. A third opinion understands it as limiting the first act to a single occurrence.

### Yetzer Hara Concession

Rashi's position, however, stands apart from all of these. Rashi reads *melamed shelo yichtzenah bamilchamah* straightforwardly: do not have sexual relations with her in battle at all. Wait until she has been brought home and completed the full process. On this reading there is no *biah rishonah* in the battlefield; all sexual relations occur after the *giyurus* is complete. The Torah's concession to *yetzer hara* lies not in permitting immediate sex on the battlefield but in establishing a formal process that channels the desire toward a legitimate outcome rather than leaving the woman as a sexual slave. The distinction between *biah rishonah* and *biah shniyah* for the *kohen* would then not be a chronological before-and-after conversion, but rather a matter of whether the Torah intended to permit even a single act (given the *kohen*'s prohibitions) or only ongoing marital relations — with the answer perhaps being that once is thinkable but the ongoing marriage is not.

The *Yerushalmi* actually records both of these as distinct opinions among the *tana'im*, which maps onto the debate between Rashi and the other *rishonim* in the *bavli*.

### Lesser of Two Evils

On 22a, the *gemara* articulates the underlying logic of the *yefat to'ar* concession through a vivid analogy: *mutav sheyochlu Yisrael basar temutos shechutos ve'al yochlu basar temutos neveilos* — better that Jews should eat meat from an animal that was going to die and was slaughtered while still alive, than that they should eat the meat of an animal that died without *shechita* and became a *neveilah*. The image is striking: a *treifa* on the verge of death, slaughtered at the last moment — the meat is technically *mutar*, but no one is pleased with the situation. The alternative, allowing it to die and become *neveilah*, is worse. So too here: the Torah fashions a barely-permissible path — forced *giyurus*, a quasi-consensual union — because the alternative, a man simply taking a non-Jewish woman as a permanent sexual slave or intermarrying with her outside of any conversion process, would be worse.

This line has been invoked by *poskim* in later generations when confronted with situations where the only available option is undesirable but the alternative is even more prohibited.

### Conversion Prevention

The Ramban develops a more structured account of what *issur* the Torah is protecting against. The verse says *vachashakta bah velakachta lecha le'ishah* — the man actually wants to *marry* this woman, not merely have a momentary encounter with her. The real danger is that without the Torah's intervention, he would keep her and marry her as a non-Jewish woman, violating *lo titchaten bam* — the prohibition of intermarriage. The Torah therefore channels the desire through a process of *giyurus*, even if effectively compelled, that places the union on a halakhically legitimate footing. The *biah rishonah*, too, is part of this framework: it is not purely an independent concession to *yetzer hara* but the beginning of a process that leads to a permissible marriage rather than an ongoing prohibited one. The Ramban further notes the element of *eishes ish*: the *baraita* states *va'afilu eishes ish* — even a woman married to a non-Jewish man is included in the Torah's permission. While the halakhic status of non-Jewish marriage vis-à-vis Jews is complex — the institution is recognized as part of *sheva mitzvos bnei Noach* but does not create a Jewish-law *issur* of *eishes ish* for a Jewish man — the Ramban explains that this is not a technical halakhic point. Rather, the Torah is emphasizing how far the concession extends: even in a case that is more *mechuar*, more morally degraded — tearing a woman from her husband — the Torah permits the process because the alternative (permanent forbidden cohabitation) is worse.

## Declaration & Conditions

### Timing of Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִם אָמֹר יֹאמַר״ – עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר וְיִשְׁנֶה. אָמַר בִּתְחִילַּת שֵׁשׁ וְלֹא אָמַר בְּסוֹף שֵׁשׁ – אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא אֵצֵא חׇפְשִׁי״ – עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר בִּשְׁעַת יְצִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** It is stated with regard to a pierced slave: **"But if** the slave **shall say [*amor yomar*]:** I love my master, my wife, and my children, I will not go out free" (Exodus 21:5). The repeated verb teaches that he is not pierced **unless he says** this statement **and repeats** it. If **he said at the beginning** of his **six** years of service that he wants to be pierced, **but he did not say** it **at the end of six** years, **he is not pierced, as it is stated: "I will not go out free,"** i.e., he is not pierced **unless he says** it **when he leaves.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר בְּסוֹף שֵׁשׁ וְלֹא אָמַר בִּתְחִילַּת שֵׁשׁ – אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם אָמֹר יֹאמַר הָעֶבֶד״ – עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר כְּשֶׁהוּא עֶבֶד.
>
> **Translation:** If **he said** this statement **at the end of six** years **but did not say** it **at the beginning of** his **six** years, he is likewise **not pierced, as it is stated: "If the slave shall say [*amor yomar*],"** which indicates that he is not pierced **unless he states** it **while he is** still **a slave.** This concludes the *baraita*.

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: the verse says *amar yomar ha'eved* — the slave shall say, shall say — teaching that he must make his declaration twice. Moreover, if he declared at the *beginning* of the six years and not at the end, *eino nirtza* — he does not undergo *retziah*. The source is *lo etzei chofshi* — he must say "I do not wish to go free" at the *moment of going free*, meaning at the conclusion of the six years.

Conversely, if he declared only at the end and not at the beginning, he also cannot become *nirtza*. The reason given is *shene'emar ha'eved* — it must be said *ke'shehu eved*, while he is still a slave. This initially seems strange, since at the end of six years he is still technically a slave. The *gemara* then poses the obvious objection: *atu sof shesh lav eved hu* — at the end of six years is he not a slave?

This leads directly to Rava's explanation.

### Last Penny Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אָמַר בִּתְחִילַּת שֵׁשׁ וְלֹא אָמַר בְּסוֹף שֵׁשׁ – אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא אֵצֵא חׇפְשִׁי״. מַאי אִירְיָא, מִ״לֹּא אֵצֵא חׇפְשִׁי״? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּבָעֵינַן: ״אָהַבְתִּי אֶת אֲדֹנִי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי וְאֶת בָּנָי״ וְלֵיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*. **The Master said** above: If **he said** it **at the beginning of** his **six** years **and he did not say** it **at the end of six** years, **he is not pierced, as it is stated: "I will not go out free."** The Gemara asks: **Why** does the *tanna* of the *baraita* learn this *halakha* **specifically from** the phrase **"I will not go out free"? Let him derive** it from the fact **that we require** another condition. He has to be able to say: **"I love my master, my wife, and my children"** (Exodus 21:5) in order to become a pierced slave, **and** he **cannot** say this, as at the start of the six years he does not yet have children from the Canaanite maidservant his master provided for him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, אָמַר בְּסוֹף שֵׁשׁ וְלֹא אָמַר בִּתְחִילַּת שֵׁשׁ אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הָעֶבֶד״. אַטּוּ סוֹף שֵׁשׁ לָאו עֶבֶד הוּא? אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי בִּתְחִילַּת שֵׁשׁ – בִּתְחִילַּת פְּרוּטָה אַחֲרוֹנָה. וּמַאי בְּסוֹף שֵׁשׁ – בְּסוֹף פְּרוּטָה אַחֲרוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** the *baraita* states that if **he said** this statement **at the end of six** years **but did not say** it **at the beginning of** his **six** years, he is likewise **not pierced, as it is stated "the slave." Is that to say** that **he is not a slave at the end of six** years? **Rava said: What** is the meaning of: **At the beginning of six?** This is not referring to the actual beginning of his six years of service, but **to the beginning of** the **last *peruta*,** i.e., when he reaches the start of his final stage of work worth one *peruta*, when he is still a slave. **And what** is the meaning of the term: **At the end of six? At the end of** the **last *peruta*.**

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction: *bitchilas pruta achrona bsof pruta achrona* — the terms "beginning" and "end" refer not to the beginning and end of the six-year period, but to the beginning and end of the *last penny's worth* of service. When enough time remains that the master still has a monetary claim worth at least a *pruta* against the slave, the slave is fully an *eved* — but he is not yet at the *sha'at yetziah*, the moment of imminent departure. Once the slave has entered the last unit of time — the final hour or fraction of time worth less than a *pruta* — he is at the cusp of freedom and satisfies *lo etzei chofshi*, but he is no longer fully *ha'eved* in the complete proprietary sense.

To satisfy *both* requirements simultaneously, the declaration must be made at the precise juncture: before the last *pruta*'s worth expires (so that he is still fully an *eved*) and after all prior conditions — wife and children — are already in place. The precision required here is striking, and one senses that the halakha is not designed to make the path to *nirtza* an easy one.

### Wife & Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים, וּלְרַבּוֹ אֵין אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים – אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי אֲהֵבְךָ וְאֶת בֵּיתֶךָ״. לְרַבּוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים וְלוֹ אֵין אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים – אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָהַבְתִּי אֶת אֲדֹנִי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי וְאֶת בָּנָי״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If the slave **has a wife and children and his master does not have a wife and children, he is not pierced, as it is stated: "Because he loves you and your house"** (Deuteronomy 15:16). The word "house" is referring to a wife and children, and therefore if the master does not have a wife and children the verse cannot be fulfilled, and the slave is not pierced. Similarly, if **his master has a wife and children and he does not have a wife and children, he is not pierced, as it is stated: "I love my master, my wife, and my children"** (Exodus 21:5).

A *baraita* sets out additional conditions. If the slave has a wife and children (the *shifcha kna'anit* and the children born from that union) but his master does not, *eino nirtza* — the process cannot proceed. The source is *ki ahevcha ve'et beisecha* — "because he loves you and your household" — implying the master must also have a household that parallels his own.

Conversely, if the master has a wife and children but the slave does not, *eino nirtza* — he cannot say *ahavti et adoni et ishti ve'et banai*. As noted above, this is precisely why the *gemara* proved that even a *kohen eved ivri* must be permitted a *shifcha kna'anit*: the *only* reason the *chachamim* gave for excluding a *kohen* from *nirtza* was *ba'al mum*, proving that the preliminary stage of wife and children is available to him.

### Love & Health

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא אוֹהֵב אֶת רַבּוֹ וְרַבּוֹ אֵינוֹ אוֹהֲבוֹ – אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי טוֹב לוֹ עִמָּךְ״. רַבּוֹ אוֹהֲבוֹ וְהוּא אֵינוֹ אוֹהֵב אֶת רַבּוֹ – אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי אֲהֵבְךָ״. הוּא חוֹלֶה וְרַבּוֹ אֵינוֹ חוֹלֶה – אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי טוֹב לוֹ עִמָּךְ״. רַבּוֹ חוֹלֶה וְהוּא אֵינוֹ חוֹלֶה – אֵינוֹ נִרְצָע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עִמָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, if **he loves his master but his master does not love him, he is not pierced, as it is stated: "Because he fares well with you"** (Deuteronomy 15:16), which indicates that it is good for both of them to be with each other. If **his master loves him but he does not love his master, he is not pierced, as it is stated: "Because he loves you."** If **he is ill and his master is not ill, he is not pierced, as it is stated: "Because he fares well with you,"** which excludes a sick person. Similarly, if **his master is ill and he is not ill, he is not pierced, as it is stated "with you,"** which equates the well-being of the pair.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁנֵיהֶם חוֹלִין, מַאי? ״עִמָּךְ״ בָּעֵינַן – וְהָא אִיכָּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא ״כִּי טוֹב לוֹ עִמָּךְ״ בָּעֵינַן – וְהָא לֵיכָּא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye raised a dilemma:** If **both of them are ill, what** is the *halakha*? **Do we require** only that the slave be **"with you,"** i.e., in the same condition as the master, **and that is** the case **here,** as they are both ill, and the slave can be pierced? **Or perhaps we require "because he fares well with you,"** i.e., it must be good for both of them, **and that is not** the case **here,** as they are both ill. If so, he cannot be pierced. No answer was found, and therefore the Gemara says that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The conditions extend to the relational and physical. If the slave loves his master but his master does not love him, *eino nirtza* — the verse *ki ahevcha* ("because he loves you") requires the master's affection to be genuine. If the master loves the slave but the slave does not love his master, *eino nirtza* — the verse *ki tov lo imach* ("for it is good for him with you") must be true, and it cannot be if the slave is not genuinely content. The slave may have pragmatic reasons for wishing to remain — he loves his wife and children, he has no prospects outside — but if he cannot sincerely say he loves his master, the *retziah* is unavailable.

Similarly, if the slave is ill and his master is not, *eino nirtza* — *ki tov lo imach* cannot be said when he is suffering. If the master is ill and the slave is not, *eino nirtza* — *imach* requires good conditions on the master's side as well. The case of both being ill simultaneously is posed as a *ba'aya* by Rav Bibi bar Abaye: is *imach* a requirement of equality, such that if both are equally ill the condition is met, or does *ki tov lo* mean that the situation must be genuinely good — not merely ill together? The *gemara* leaves this as *teiku*.

### Equal Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי טוֹב לוֹ עִמָּךְ״ – עִמְּךָ בַּמַּאֲכָל, עִמְּךָ בַּמִּשְׁתֶּה. שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אַתָּה אוֹכֵל פַּת נְקִיָּה וְהוּא אוֹכֵל פַּת קִיבָּר, אַתָּה שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן יָשָׁן וְהוּא שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן חָדָשׁ, אַתָּה יָשֵׁן עַל גַּבֵּי מוֹכִין וְהוּא יָשֵׁן עַל גַּבֵּי תֶּבֶן. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: כׇּל הַקּוֹנֶה עֶבֶד עִבְרִי – כְּקוֹנֶה אָדוֹן לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse states concerning a Hebrew slave: **"Because he fares well with you,"** which teaches that the slave should be **with you,** i.e., treated as your equal, **in food,** meaning that his food must be of the same quality as yours, and **with you in drink.** This means **that there shall not** be a situation in **which you eat fine bread and he eats inferior bread,** bread from coarse flour mixed with bran, which is low quality. There shall not be a situation in which **you drink aged wine and he drinks** inferior **new wine.** There shall not be a situation in which **you sleep** comfortably **on** bedding made from **soft sheets and he sleeps on straw. From here** the Sages **stated: Anyone who acquires a Hebrew slave** is considered **like one who acquires a master for himself,** because he must be careful that the slave's living conditions are equal to his own.

A *baraita* then unpacks the phrase *ki tov lo imach* in its material dimensions, and this passage is one of the most celebrated in this entire sugya. *Imach bema'achal* — with you in food; *imach bemishte* — with you in drink; *imach belo'in* — with you in lodging. The slave must receive exactly what the master provides for himself:

*Shelo tehei atah ochel pas nekiyah vehu ochel pas kivar* — you may not eat fine white bread while he eats coarse bran bread. *Atah shoseh yayin yashan vehu shoseh yayin chadash* — you may not drink aged wine while he drinks new wine. *Atah yashen al gabei mochin vehu yashen al gabei teven* — you may not sleep on a featherbed while he sleeps on straw.

From here the *gemara* derives the famous principle: *mikan amru kol hakoneh eved Ivri kekoneh adon le'atzmo* — whoever acquires an *eved Ivri* is as if he has acquired a master for himself. The Ramban sharpens this: if there is only one pillow in the house, giving it to oneself would create an inequality, violating *imach*; throwing it away so neither has it would be *middat Sedom*. The slave therefore takes precedence — he receives the single pillow. In that sense the *eved Ivri* is not merely the master's equal but, where resources are limited, his superior.

### Support Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְיָצָא מֵעִמָּךְ הוּא וּבָנָיו עִמּוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אִם הוּא נִמְכָּר – בָּנָיו וּבְנוֹתָיו מִי נִמְכָּרִים? מִכָּאן שֶׁרַבּוֹ חַיָּיב בִּמְזוֹנוֹת בָּנָיו. כַּיּוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״אִם בַּעַל אִשָּׁה הוּא וְיָצְאָה אִשְׁתּוֹ עִמּוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אִם הוּא נִמְכָּר – אִשְׁתּוֹ מִי נִמְכְּרָה? מִכָּאן שֶׁרַבּוֹ חַיָּיב בִּמְזוֹנוֹת אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to a verse that deals with the emancipation of a slave: **"Then he shall go out from you, he and his children with him"** (Leviticus 25:41). **Rabbi Shimon said:** This verse is puzzling, as, **if he is sold, are his sons and daughters sold?** Rather, **from here** it is derived that **his master is obligated to** provide **sustenance for his children,** and when the slave is emancipated his sons are released as well. **You say something similar** with regard to the verse: **"If he is married then his wife shall go out with him"** (Exodus 21:3). **Rabbi Shimon said: If he is sold, is his wife sold?** Rather, **from here** it is derived **that his master is obligated to** provide **sustenance for his wife.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן בָּנָיו, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא בְּנֵי מֶיעְבַּד וּמֵיכַל נִינְהוּ. אֲבָל אִשְׁתּוֹ, דְּבַת מֵיכַל וּמֶיעְבַּד הִיא, אֵימָא תַּעֲבֵיד וְתֵיכוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And it is necessary** for the *baraita* to mention both cases, **as if it** had **taught us** only that the master is required to provide sustenance for the slave's **children,** one might say that this is **because they are not fit to work and eat.** Since they are unable to support themselves, the master is required to support them. **But** with regard to **his wife, who can eat and work,** one might **say** that **she should work and eat** in payment for her work, and the master is not required to support her for free.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן אִשְׁתּוֹ, דְּלָאו דִּירְכַּהּ לְהַדּוֹרֵי, אֲבָל בָּנָיו, דְּדִירְכַּיְיהוּ לְהַדּוֹרֵי, אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **if** the *baraita* had **taught us** only about **his wife,** one might say that the master is required to support her **since it is not her manner to circulate** and collect charity, as she is too embarrassed to do this. **But** with regard to **his children, since it is their manner to circulate** and beg, i.e., this is not beneath their dignity, one might **say no,** he is not required to support them. Therefore it is **necessary** to issue both rulings.

A further *baraita* addresses the master's obligations toward the slave's family. The verse *veyatza imach hu uvanav imo* — he shall leave you, he and his sons with him — prompts Rabbi Shimon to ask: if he was sold into slavery, who sold his sons? They were not sold. Yet the verse says they leave with him at *yovel*, implying they were present throughout the servitude. *Mikan sherabo chayav bemezonos banav* — from here we learn that the master was obligated to provide for the slave's children throughout the period of service.

An identical structure applies to the wife: *im ba'al ishah hu veyatza ishto imo* — if he was married, his wife leaves with him. Rabbi Shimon again asks: who sold his wife? The answer: *mikan sherabo chayav bemezonos ishto* — the master must support the wife as well.

The *gemara* explains why both rulings are necessary. Had the Torah stated only the obligation regarding children, one might have said the wife can find employment and feed herself. Had it stated only the wife, one might have said that since it is not customary for a woman *lehadori* — to go door to door begging — the master need not provide for children, who can beg. Each case is therefore independently required.

## Ear-Piercing Mechanics

### Through Ear & Door

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״אׇזְנוֹ בַּדֶּלֶת״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: יִדְקוֹר כְּנֶגֶד אׇזְנוֹ בַּדֶּלֶת. דֶּלֶת – אִין, אׇזְנוֹ – לָא. וְאֹזֶן לָא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְרָצַע אֲדֹנָיו אֶת אׇזְנוֹ בַּמַּרְצֵעַ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Had** the verse **stated: His ear to the door, I would say: He should pierce, opposite his ear, into the door** alone. In other words, with regard to **the door, yes,** it should be pierced, but **his ear** itself, **no,** it should not be pierced. The Gemara asks: **But** how could it even be suggested that **his ear** should **not** be pierced? **But isn't it written: "And his master shall pierce his ear with an awl"** (Exodus 21:6)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר יִרְצָעֶנָּה לָאֹזֶן מֵאַבָּרַאי וְיַנִּיחֶנָּה עַל הַדֶּלֶת, וְיִדְקוֹר כְּנֶגֶד אׇזְנוֹ בַּדֶּלֶת, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּאׇזְנוֹ וּבַדֶּלֶת״ – הָא כֵּיצַד? דּוֹקֵר וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ אֵצֶל דֶּלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, I would say** that the master **should pierce his ear outside,** i.e., not at the door, **and he should place it** afterward **on the door, and** then **he should pierce opposite his ear on the door.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And you shall take the awl and place it **through his ear and into the door"** (Deuteronomy 15:17). **How so? He bores through** his ear **until he reaches the door.**

A *baraita* analyzes the precise procedure of the *retziah*. Had the verse said only *be'ozno uvadelet* — in his ear and in the door — one might have thought: place his ear against the door and then pierce into the door only, the ear being merely positioned nearby. The word *be'ozno* forces us to require that the ear itself be pierced. But then, had the verse said only *be'ozno*, I might have thought: pierce the ear first, then separately make a hole in the door and align them. *Talmud lomar be'ozno uvadelet* — both together — teaches: *dokeir veholech ad shemmagi'a etzel dalet* — one pierces through the ear and continues until reaching the door. The ear must be pressed against the door, and a single act of piercing passes through both.

### Standing Door

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דֶּלֶת״ – שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי: בֵּין עֲקוּרָה בֵּין שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲקוּרָה, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מְזוּזָה״ – מָה מְזוּזָה מְעוּמָּד – אַף דֶּלֶת נָמֵי מְעוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* adds: Since the verse states **"door," I** would **derive** that this applies to any door, regardless of **whether** it is **detached** from its doorpost or **whether it is not detached.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "Then his master shall bring him to the court, and shall bring him to the door, or to the **doorpost"** (Exodus 21:6): **Just as a doorpost is upright** and attached, **so too, a door** must be **upright** and attached to the doorpost.

One might have thought the door need not be standing and attached; perhaps even a detached door panel would suffice. *Talmud lomar mezuza* — the verse's juxtaposition with *mezuza* teaches: *mah mezuza me'umad af dalet nami me'umad* — just as a *mezuza* is only called by that name when the doorframe stands in its permanent upright position, so too the door for the *retziah* must be standing, properly installed.

### Ear at Sinai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי הָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ אֶת הַמִּקְרָא הַזֶּה כְּמִין חוֹמֶר: מָה נִשְׁתַּנָּה אֹזֶן מִכׇּל אֵבָרִים שֶׁבַּגּוּף? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אֹזֶן שֶׁשָּׁמְעָה קוֹלִי עַל הַר סִינַי בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַרְתִּי: ״כִּי לִי בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל עֲבָדִים״ – וְלֹא עֲבָדִים לַעֲבָדִים, וְהָלַךְ זֶה וְקָנָה אָדוֹן לְעַצְמוֹ – יֵרָצַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai would expound this verse as a type of decorative wreath [*ḥomer*],** i.e., as an allegory: **Why is the ear different from all the other limbs in the body,** as the ear alone is pierced? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said:** This **ear heard My voice on Mount Sinai when I said: "For to Me the children of Israel are slaves"** (Leviticus 25:55), which indicates: **And** they should **not** be **slaves to slaves. And** yet **this** man **went and** willingly **acquired a master for himself.** Therefore, **let** this ear **be pierced.**

Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai offered a famous *mashal* — Rashi understands *kemin chomer* as "like a fragrant mixture," meaning a beautifully composed explanation; Tosafot understand it as "like a story" — to illuminate why the ear specifically is the organ chosen for piercing.

*Mah nishtanna ozen mikkol ha'evarim shebaguf* — why was the ear singled out from all the organs of the body? The Holy One, blessed be He, declared: *ozen sheshame'ah koli al har Sinai besha'ah she'amarti ki li benei yisrael avadim velo avadim la'avadim* — the ear that heard My voice at Sinai when I said "the children of Israel are My servants and not servants to servants" — *vehalach zeh veqana adon le'atzmo* — and yet this person went and acquired a master for himself. That ear must be pierced.

The irony is layered: the very foundation of *mishpatim*, which begins with the laws of slavery, is prefaced by *anochi Hashem Elohecha asher hotzetecha me'eretz mitzrayim mibbeit avadim* — I freed you from a house of slaves. The first substantial legislation after *maamad har Sinai* concerns how one frees one's slave after six years. Yet this slave chooses to perpetuate his bondage — and his ear, which heard the proclamation of freedom, is marked accordingly.

### Door as Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי הָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ אֶת הַמִּקְרָא הַזֶּה כְּמִין חוֹמֶר: מָה נִשְׁתַּנָּה דֶּלֶת וּמְזוּזָה מִכׇּל כֵּלִים שֶׁבַּבַּיִת? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: דֶּלֶת וּמְזוּזָה שֶׁהָיוּ עֵדִים בְּמִצְרַיִם בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁפָּסַחְתִּי עַל הַמַּשְׁקוֹף וְעַל שְׁתֵּי הַמְּזוּזוֹת, וְאָמַרְתִּי: ״כִּי לִי בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל עֲבָדִים״ – וְלֹא עֲבָדִים לַעֲבָדִים, וְהוֹצֵאתִים מֵעַבְדוּת לְחֵירוּת, וְהָלַךְ זֶה וְקָנָה אָדוֹן לְעַצְמוֹ – יֵרָצַע בִּפְנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **would** likewise **expound this verse as a type of decorative wreath: Why are the door and a doorpost different from all other objects in the house,** that the piercing is performed with them? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: The door and the doorpost were witnesses in Egypt when I passed over the lintel and** when I passed **over the two doorposts** of houses in which there were Jews (Exodus, chapter 12), **and I said: "For to Me the children of Israel are slaves," and** they should **not** be **slaves to slaves. And I delivered them** at that time **from slavery to freedom, and** yet **this** man **went and acquired a master for himself.** Therefore, **let him be pierced before them,** as they are witnesses that he violated God's will.

Rabbi Shimon b'Rebbi offered a parallel *mashal* focusing on the door and doorpost rather than the ear. *Mah nishtanna dalet umezuza mikkol kelim shebabayit* — why were the door and doorpost chosen from all the vessels of the house? The Holy One, blessed be He, declared: *dalet umezuza shehayu edim bemitzrayim besha'ah shepasachti al hamashkof ve'al shetei hamezuzot* — the door and the doorpost were witnesses in Egypt when I passed over the lintel and the two doorposts. *Ve'amarti ki li benei Yisrael avadim velo avadim la'avadim vehotzetim me'avdut lecherut* — I declared they are My servants and freed them from slavery to freedom. *Vehalach zeh veqana adon le'atzmo* — and this one went and acquired a master for himself. *Yeratza bifneihem* — let him be pierced before them, before these witnesses to his people's redemption, witnesses to the fact that he should not be enslaving himself again.

The *mashal* of Rabbi Shimon prompts reflection on the distinction between the initial six-year period and the *retziah*. For the first six years, the slave was a *gannav* who could not pay his debt — he did not choose slavery freely, or he was driven to *moker atzmo* by economic desperation. One can understand, if not excuse, that first entry into servitude. But to then choose *ve'avado le'olam* — to serve forever — is something else entirely, something that demands a symbolic act before these ancient witnesses to say: this choice runs against everything your history and your God intended for you.
