# Sanhedrin 7b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## True Justice & Shechina

### Emes L'Amito

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל דַּיָּין שֶׁדָּן דִּין אֱמֶת לַאֲמִיתּוֹ, מַשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהִים נִצָּב בַּעֲדַת אֵל בְּקֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים יִשְׁפֹּט״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: Any judge who judges a judgment** according to **absolute truth** [*emet*] **causes the Divine Presence to rest among Israel, as it is stated: "God stands in the congregation of God; in the midst of the judges He judges"** (Psalms 82:1), indicating that the Divine Presence is in the midst of the court.

The daf opens with a statement of Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *kol dayan shedan din emes l'amito, mashre Shechina beYisrael* — any judge who judges with *emes l'amito*, truth to its truth, causes the Divine Presence to dwell among Israel. The double formulation — truth to its truth — is striking, and recalls the *Yerushalmi*'s treatment of how part of the idea of truth concerns process and not merely result. The proof-text is *Elokim nitzav ba'adat El, bekerev Elokim yishpot*: God stands in the assembly of God, for in the midst of God do you judge. When justice is properly executed, God is able to dwell on earth.

### Perverting Justice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל דַּיָּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ דָּן דִּין אֱמֶת לַאֲמִיתּוֹ, גּוֹרֵם לִשְׁכִינָה שֶׁתִּסְתַּלֵּק מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִשֹּׁד עֲנִיִּים מֵאַנְקַת אֶבְיוֹנִים עַתָּה אָקוּם יֹאמַר ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And every judge who does not judge a judgment** according to **absolute truth causes the Divine Presence to withdraw from Israel, as it is stated: "For the oppression of the poor, for the sighing of the needy, now will I arise, says the Lord"** (Psalms 12:6). God will arise and leave the people as a result of oppression.

The converse follows immediately: *vekol dayan she'eino dan din emes l'amito, gorem laShechina shetistalek miYisrael* — the judge who fails to judge truth to its truth causes the Divine Presence to be withdrawn from Israel. The supporting verse is *mishod aniyim me'ankat evyonim, ata akum yomar Hashem* — from the despoiling of the poor and the outcry of the needy, now I will rise up, says God. The *gemara* reads "rise up" not as rising to take vengeance, but as rising to depart — to withdraw the Divine Presence from a people whose courts have abandoned justice. The verse evokes the judge who, rather than ruling correctly, recuses himself to avoid antagonizing powerful litigants, thereby ignoring the outcry of those who have been wronged.

### Theft in Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל דַּיָּין שֶׁנּוֹטֵל מִזֶּה וְנוֹתֵן לְזֶה שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא נוֹטֵל מִמֶּנּוּ נַפְשׁוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַל תִּגְזׇל דַּל כִּי דַּל הוּא וְאַל תְּדַכֵּא עָנִי בַּשָּׁעַר כִּי ה׳ יָרִיב רִיבָם וְקָבַע אֶת קֹבְעֵיהֶם נָפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says:** With regard to **any judge who takes** disputed property or money [*mamon*] **from this** litigant **and gives** it **to that** other litigant **unlawfully, the Holy One, Blessed be He, takes his soul from him** as punishment for his corruption, **as it is stated: "Rob not the weak, because he is weak, neither crush the poor in the gate; for the Lord will plead their cause and despoil of life those who despoil them"** (Proverbs 22:22–23).

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani continues in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *kol dayan shenotel mizeh venoten lizeh shelo kedin* — any judge who takes from one and gives to the other in a manner contrary to law — *Hakadosh Baruch Hu notel mimenu nafsho*, God will demand his soul. The proof-text is *al tigzol dal ki dal hu ve'al tedake ani basha'ar* — do not steal from the poor because he is poor, and do not crush the poor person in the gate. The word *sha'ar*, gate, evokes the place of justice — as in *Lot yoshev bisha'ar Sedom* — connecting the verse to the judicial context. *Ki Hashem yariv rivam, vekava es koveihem nafesh*: God will come to fight their fight and rob the soul of those who rob them. The weight of responsibility incumbent upon the judge could not be stated more sharply.

## Fear & Burden

### Sword & Gehinnom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: לְעוֹלָם יִרְאֶה דַּיָּין עַצְמוֹ כְּאִילּוּ חֶרֶב מוּנַּחַת לוֹ בֵּין יַרְכוֹתָיו, וְגֵיהִנָּם פְּתוּחָה לוֹ מִתַּחְתָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: A judge should always view** [*yireh*] **himself as if a sword is placed between his thighs,** so that if he leans to the right or to the left he will be injured, **and** as if **Gehenna is opened up beneath him.**

A third teaching from the same chain: *le'olam yireh dayan atzmo k'ilu cherev munachas lo bein yarchosav, veGehinnom pesucha lo mitachtav* — a judge should always see himself as though a sword is placed between his thighs and *Gehinnom* lies open beneath him. The consequences of judging incorrectly are that severe. This is, to put it plainly, not an encouraging portrait of what it means to hold judicial office — but the *gemara*'s point is precisely that one must enter that role with full awareness of what is at stake.

### Shlomo's Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִנֵּה מִטָּתוֹ שֶׁלִּשְׁלֹמֹה שִׁשִּׁים גִּבֹּרִים סָבִיב לָהּ מִגִּבֹּרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל כֻּלָּם אֲחֻזֵי חֶרֶב מְלֻמְּדֵי מִלְחָמָה אִישׁ חַרְבּוֹ עַל יְרֵכוֹ מִפַּחַד בַּלֵּילוֹ״. מִפַּחְדָּהּ שֶׁל גֵּיהִנָּם, שֶׁדּוֹמָה לְלַיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **as it is stated: "Behold, it is the bed of Solomon; sixty mighty men are around it, of the mighty men of Israel. They all handle the sword, and are expert in war; every man has his sword upon his thigh due to dread in the night"** (Song of Songs 3:7–8). The words "due to dread in the night" mean **due to the dread of Gehenna, which is similar to the night.**

The proof-text is the verse describing Shlomo's bed: *hinei mitaso shelishlomo shishim gibborim seviv la, migibborei Yisrael, kulam achuzei cherev, melumdei milchama, ish charbo al yerecho, mipachad baleilos* — behold the bed of Shlomo, sixty mighty warriors surrounding it, from the warriors of Israel, all gripping a sword, expert in battle, each man's sword upon his thigh, because of the fear of the night. The *gemara* reads *mipachad baleilos* as *mipachada shel Gehinnom shedomeh lalaylah* — from the fear of *Gehinnom*, which resembles the night. Rashi connects the *gibborim* to *talmidei chachamim*, and *melumdei milchama* to *milchamta shel Torah*, grounding the verse in the world of Torah scholarship — but the specific application here is to the consequence awaiting the judge who rules incorrectly.

## Morning Light Test

### Clarity Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״בֵּית דָּוִד כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ דִּינוּ לַבֹּקֶר מִשְׁפָּט וְהַצִּילוּ גָזוּל מִיַּד עוֹשֵׁק״ – וְכִי בַּבֹּקֶר דָּנִין וְכׇל הַיּוֹם אֵין דָּנִין? אֶלָּא אִם בָּרוּר לְךָ הַדָּבָר כַּבֹּקֶר – אׇמְרֵהוּ, וְאִם לָאו – אַל תֹּאמְרֵהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoshiya, and some say Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak, interpreted** a verse **homiletically. What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "House of David, so says the Lord: Execute justice in the morning, and deliver the spoiled out of the hand of the oppressor"** (Jeremiah 21:12)? **And** is it **so** that a court **may judge in the morning, and all** the rest of **the day** a court **may not judge?** Why does the verse specifically relate to judging in the morning? **Rather,** the meaning is: **If the matter is as clear to you as the morning, state** the verdict; **and if not, do not state it.**

The *gemara* cites a verse: judge justice in the morning and save the one being robbed from the hand of the oppressor. The obvious question is posed: *vachi baboker danni vechol yoma ein danni* — do you only judge in the morning? Don't you judge the entire day? The answer offered: *ella im barur lach davar kaboker amreihu* — if the matter is as clear to you as the morning, when the break of dawn makes everything vivid and the distinction between light and darkness unmistakable, then say it. *Ve'im lav al tomreihu* — and if not, do not say it.

### Tension: Certainty

This principle is immediately recognized as generating a real tension. If a judge is not one hundred percent certain which party is right, does that mean he must refrain from ruling? That cannot be the practical requirement, since *halacha* has mechanisms for handling doubt — *hamotzi mechaveiro alav harayah*, the burden of proof rests on the claimant — yet applying that rule may itself produce an unjust result if the judge suspects the claimant is actually correct but cannot be certain. One might also ask: what about the earlier principle that *ein lo ladayan ela mah she'einav ra'ot* — a judge has nothing but what his eyes see? That principle is understood to mean that a judge must rule based on his best assessment of the facts as he can evaluate them. These two principles — that one must be certain as the morning before ruling, and that a judge has only what his own eyes show him — live in genuine tension with one another, and both must be held together.

### Wisdom as Sister

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, מֵהָכָא: ״אֱמֹר לַחׇכְמָה אֲחֹתִי אָתְּ״ – אִם בָּרוּר לְךָ הַדָּבָר כַּאֲחוֹתְךָ שֶׁהִיא אֲסוּרָה לְךָ – אוֹמְרֵהוּ, וְאִם לָאו – אַל תֹּאמְרֵהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yonatan** says this principle may be derived **from here: "Say to wisdom: You are my sister"** (Proverbs 7:4). **If the matter is** as **clear to you as** the fact **that your sister is forbidden to you, state it, and if not, do not state it.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan derives the same principle from a different verse: *emor lachochmah achoti at* — say to wisdom, you are my sister. The *drasha*: if the ruling is as clear to you as the prohibition against relations with one's sister — which admits of no ambiguity whatsoever — *amreihu*, then deliver the judgment. *Ve'im lav al tomreihu* — and if not, withhold it.

## Shared Responsibility

### Ten Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: עֲשָׂרָה שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין בַּדִּין, קוֹלָר תָּלוּי בְּצַוַּאר כּוּלָּן. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא אֶלָּא לְתַלְמִיד הַיּוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** If **ten** judges **are sitting in judgment,** a prisoner's **collar [*kolar*],** referring to responsibility for the consequences of an incorrect verdict, **hangs around all of their necks.** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that all of the judges bear joint responsibility for the verdict? The Gemara answers: It **is necessary only** in order **to** include **a student who is sitting in front of his teacher** in the court, and notices that his teacher erred.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi states: *asarah sheyoshvim badin, kulo talui betzavar kulan* — when ten people sit in judgment, the yoke hangs on all of their necks. One might ask why the number ten, since ten is not a standard size for a *beit din*. The *gemara* anticipates the objection that of course all judges bear shared responsibility — *peshita*. The novel teaching is *lo tzericha ela letalmid hayosheiv lifnei rabbo*: even a student who is present before his teacher and could have spoken up but did not bears some portion of that responsibility. The practical implication is that being part of a large group does not displace one's individual responsibility for an incorrect ruling. If a group reaches a wrong decision, the presence of many participants is no excuse for any one of them.

### Splinter of Beam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא, כִּי הֲוָה אָתֵי דִּינָא לְקַמֵּיהּ, מְיכַנֵּיף וּמַיְיתִי עֲשָׂרָה רַבָּנַן מִבֵּי רַב. אָמַר: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִימְטְיַין שִׁיבָּא מִכְּשׁוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates concerning **Rav Huna** that **when** a case **would come before him** for **judgment,** he would **gather and bring ten rabbis from Rav's study hall.** He **would say:** I do this **so that** only a small part of the responsibility, comparable to **a splinter from a beam, will reach** each of **us.**

At the same time, the *gemara* presents the complementary principle through a practice of Rav Huna. When a case would come before him, *michneif umaitei asarah rabanan* — he would gather ten rabbis from the *yeshiva*, so that *ki heichi de'limtiyei shiva mikashura* — a splinter of the beam should go to each of them. The burden of responsibility should be shared across many.

This practice finds its parallel in the conduct of *poskim* who, when ruling on a difficult case, will decline to issue a ruling unilaterally and instead seek the signatures of multiple colleagues — not because they lack the authority, but in order that the weight of the decision be collectively borne. There is also something worth examining sociologically: it might be assumed that group decision-making produces more conservative outcomes, since competing views balance one another out. But the reality is more complicated — individuals can exert influence on group outcomes while simultaneously diffusing personal accountability for the result. The deeper point, which this practice embodies, is the principle not to be a *dayan yachidi*, not to think that all truth resides with oneself alone. If you are about to make an important decision, bring in other perspectives.

### Rav Ashi & Shochtim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי, כִּי הֲוָה אָתֵי טְרֵיפְתָּא לְקַמֵּיהּ, מְכַנֵּיף וּמַיְיתֵי לְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ טַבָּחֵי דְּמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא. אָמַר: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִימְטְיַין שִׁיבָּא מִכְּשׁוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with **Rav Ashi, when** a person **would come before him** with meat suspected to be from **an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifta*],** he **would gather and bring** together **all the butchers of Mata Meḥasya** and consult with them before ruling on the status of the meat. He **would say** to them: I do this **so that** only a small part of the responsibility, comparable to **a splinter from a beam, will reach** each of **us.**

Rav Ashi's parallel practice is more illuminating still. When a question of *tereifa* would come before him, *makhnif u-maiti lehu le-khulhu tavkhei de-Mata Mechasya* — he would gather all the *shochtim* of the city of Mata Mechasya, so that everyone should receive a *shiva mi-kishora*, a splinter of the beam. This case is instructive on multiple levels. First, the *shochtim* are precisely the people most affected by the ruling — bringing them in means hearing their concerns directly, and sometimes a *psak* must be understood in relation to how it impacts those subject to it. Second, they bring genuine expertise that the *posek* himself may not possess in the same form. Rav Ashi was certainly capable of ruling on his own, but he wanted to hear all perspectives and ensure that every relevant form of knowledge was brought to bear. This is not ignorance requiring consultation; it is wisdom choosing to consult.

## King vs. Kohen

### Self-Sufficiency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: דָּרַשׁ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר כֹּהֵן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״מֶלֶךְ בְּמִשְׁפָּט יַעֲמִיד אָרֶץ וְאִישׁ תְּרוּמוֹת יֶהֶרְסֶנָּה״? אִם דַּיָּין דּוֹמֶה לְמֶלֶךְ שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִכְלוּם – יַעֲמִיד אָרֶץ, וְאִם דּוֹמֶה לְכֹהֵן שֶׁמְּחַזֵּר בְּבֵית הַגְּרָנוֹת – יֶהֶרְסֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, he **said: Rav Naḥman bar Kohen interpreted** a verse **homiletically: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "The king by justice establishes the land; but he who exacts gifts [*terumot*] overthrows it"** (Proverbs 29:4)? This teaches that **if the judge** is **like a king** in **that he does not need anything** and is not dependent on anyone, he **establishes the land,** i.e., he can serve as a judge. **But if he is like a priest who seeks out** his *terumot* **from** various **granaries,** as he is dependent on others, he **overthrows** the land.

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported a *drasha* of Nachman bar Kohen — who was himself, notably, *bar Kohen*. The verse: *melech be-mishpat ya'amid aretz ve-ish terumot ye-hersena* — a king through justice establishes the land, but a man of gifts will destroy it. Reading *terumot* as an allusion to the *kohen* who goes from threshing floor to threshing floor collecting his portion, the *drasha* proceeds: *im dayan domeh le-melech she-eino tzarich le-khlum* — if the judge is like a king who needs nothing from anyone, *ya'amid aretz*, he will establish the land. *Ve-im domeh le-kohen she-mechazer be-veit ha-granot* — but if he is like a *kohen* who must make his rounds to collect, *ye-hersena*, he will destroy it.

### Knowledge vs. Questions

This is where we must consult Rashi to understand what exactly this analogy describes. *Tosafot* understands it in terms of material independence — a wealthy judge who requires no one's favor can rule without fear of compromising his judgment, while a judge who is financially dependent on the goodwill of others will inevitably find that independence compromised. But Rashi reads differently. The contrast is: *im domeh dayan le-melech she-baki be-tiv dinim* — if the judge is like a king in the sense of being thoroughly expert in the substance of the law — *male chochma*, full of wisdom — he will establish justice. But if he must constantly go around asking *shaylos*, like the *kohen* going from threshing floor to threshing floor seeking his portion, he will destroy it.

This Rashi must be held in tension with what was said earlier about gathering others to share the burden of judgment. The resolution lies in the difference between genuine expertise that chooses to consult, and ignorance that must always ask because it lacks the foundational knowledge to rule at all. A world-class surgeon who recommends a second opinion does so not because he lacks competence but because the case warrants it. One can be an expert who consults, or one can be someone who fundamentally does not know the material required to adjudicate properly.

### Unqualified Judge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי נְשִׂיאָה אוֹקְמוּ דַּיָּינָא דְּלָא הֲוָה גְּמִיר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לִיהוּדָה בַּר נַחְמָנִי, מְתוּרְגְּמָנֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״קוּם עֲלֵיהּ בְּאָמוֹרָא״. קָם, גְּחֵין עֲלֵיהּ, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **In the house of the *Nasi*, they appointed a judge who was not learned.** This judge **said to Yehuda bar Naḥmani,** who was the **interpreter of Reish Lakish** and whose role was to repeat and explain the Sage's lectures: **Stand over** me **as an interpreter,** and I will lecture. Yehuda bar Naḥmani **arose** and **bent over him** in the conventional manner, to hear the judge's words. **And,** being ignorant, the judge **did not say anything to him.**

The *gemara* illustrates the problem. *Debei nesi ukmei dayana delo hava gmir* — the house of the *nasi* appointed a judge who was not learned. This connects back to the concept of *yachid mumche*, which requires *gmir vesavir* — both thorough knowledge and analytical capability. Once a system of political appointment (*netilat reshut*) is in place, the *nasi* or *reish galusa* grants judicial authority — but the conditions are ripe for appointment without knowledge. They told Yehuda bar Nachmani, who served as the translator and announcer (*amora*) of Reish Lakish, to go serve as this judge's *amora*. He rose and leaned toward him — but the appointed judge had nothing to whisper, because he knew nothing. The *amora* received silence.

## Empty Vessel

### Innkeeper Judge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּתַח וְאָמַר: ״הוֹי אוֹמֵר לָעֵץ הָקִיצָה עוּרִי לְאֶבֶן דּוּמָם הוּא יוֹרֶה הִנֵּה הוּא תָּפוּשׂ זָהָב וָכֶסֶף וְכׇל רוּחַ אֵין בְּקִרְבּוֹ״. וְעָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לִיפָּרַע מִמַּעֲמִידָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַה׳ בְּהֵיכַל קׇדְשׁוֹ הַס מִפָּנָיו כׇּל הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** The interpreter **began and said:** The verse states: **"Woe to him who says to the wood: Awake, to the dumb stone: Arise. Can this teach? Behold, it is overlaid with gold and silver, and there is no breath at all in the midst of it"** (Habakkuk 2:19). So is this judge, appointed to teach the public for gold, i.e., for payment, but no more qualified than wood and stone. **And** in the **future, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will punish those who appoint** such judges, **as it is stated** in the next verse: **"But the Lord is in His holy Sanctuary; let all the earth be silent before Him"** (Habakkuk 2:20).

So Yehuda bar Nachmani opened and, improvising as though he were transmitting what had just been whispered, declared: *hoi omer la'etz hakitza, uri le'even domem* — woe to the one who says to the wood "wake up," stir yourself, O silent stone — *hu yoreh hinei hu tafus zahav vachesaf vechol ruach ein bekirbo* — should he be the one to judge? Behold, he holds gold and silver, and there is no spirit within him. Like the classic story of the rabbi and the wagon driver — the wagon driver memorizes the *drasha* and delivers it convincingly until someone asks a question — here the *amora* covered for the empty vessel, but delivered his critique through the covering itself.

The *gemara* concludes that God will ultimately exact payment not only from the unfit appointee but from those who do the appointing: *atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu l'hipara mimamidim* — from those who install such people. The verse: *v'Hashem b'heichal kodsho has mipanav kol ha'aretz* — God is in His holy sanctuary; silence before Him all the earth. When false speech and empty authority have filled the land, God will rise to silence it.

### Asherah Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הַמַּעֲמִיד דַּיָּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ הָגוּן, כְּאִילּוּ נוֹטֵעַ אֲשֵׁירָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שֹׁפְטִים וְשֹׁטְרִים תִּתֶּן לְךָ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״לֹא תִטַּע לְךָ אֲשֵׁרָה כָּל עֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** With regard to **anyone who appoints over the community a judge who is not fit,** it is **as though** he **plants a tree** used as part of idolatrous rites **[*ashera*] among the Jewish people, as it is stated: "You shall make judges and officers for yourself"** (Deuteronomy 16:18), **and juxtaposed to it,** it is written: **"You shall not plant yourself an *ashera* of any kind of tree"** (Deuteronomy 16:21).

Reish Lakish states: *kol hamaamid dayan she'eino hagun ke'ilu noteia asherah b'Yisrael* — anyone who appoints an unfit judge is as though he has planted an *asherah*, an idolatrous tree, in Israel. The connection is not arbitrary. We have seen that when justice is executed properly, it draws the *Shechina* into the world — the judge is, as it were, doing God's work and establishing the world according to the divine sense of right and wrong. The judge who knows nothing is like an idol: authority without substance, worship of something empty. And the one who installs such a person is actively driving out the Divine Presence and importing idolatry in its place. The proof-text proceeds by *semichut*: the Torah places the appointment of judges immediately adjacent to the prohibition of planting an *asherah* — *shoftim v'shotrim titein l'cha*, and directly following, *lo tita l'cha asherah kol eitz*.

### Beside Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: וּבִמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, כְּאִילּוּ נְטָעוֹ אֵצֶל מִזְבֵּחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֵצֶל מִזְבַּח ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: And in a place where there are Torah scholars,** it is **as though** he **planted** the tree **next to the altar, as it is stated:** "You shall not plant yourself an *ashera*…**beside the altar of the Lord your God."**

Rav Ashi extends the severity further: *uv'makom sheyesh talmidei chachamim* — where there were qualified scholars who could have served as judges and were bypassed, *ke'ilu netao eitzel mizbeach* — it is as though the *asherah* was planted right next to the altar. The verse: *eitzel mizbeach Hashem Elokecha* — do not plant this beside the altar of God. When the seat of justice sits alongside qualified *talmidei chachamim* who are ignored in favor of a political appointee, the affront to God's presence is direct and unmistakable.

### No Gods for Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן אִתִּי אֱלֹהֵי כֶסֶף וֵאלֹהֵי זָהָב״. אֱלֹהֵי כֶסֶף וֵאלֹהֵי זָהָב הוּא דְּלָא עָבְדִי, הָא דְּעֵץ שְׁרֵי? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֱלוֹהַּ הַבָּא בִּשְׁבִיל כֶּסֶף, וֶאֱלוֹהַּ הַבָּא בִּשְׁבִיל זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** It is **written: "You shall not make with Me gods of [*elohei*] silver and gods of gold"** (Exodus 20:20). The Gemara asks: It is **gods of silver and gods of gold that** you may **not make, but** are gods **of wood permitted?** Rather, **Rav Ashi says:** This verse discusses **a judge,** called *elohim*, **who comes,** i.e., is appointed, **due to** payment of **silver, and a judge who comes due to** payment of **gold.**

The *gemara* now turns to the verse *lo taasun iti elokei cheseph v'elokei zahav* — do not make gods of silver and gold. An obvious question: does this imply that gods of wood are permitted? Rav Ashi explains: *eloah haba bishvil cheseph* — do not appoint an authority over you who holds that position because of money paid. The judge is, in a sense, a *god* on earth — an authority representing the divine order of justice. That position must not be one that is bought. The *gemara* acknowledges the reality it was addressing.

## Humility & Honor

### Rav's Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב, כִּי הֲוָה אָתֵי לְבֵי דִּינָא, אָמַר הָכִי: בִּרְעוּת נַפְשֵׁיהּ לִקְטָלָא נָפֵק, וּצְבוּ בֵּיתֵיהּ לֵית הוּא עָבֵיד, וְרֵיקָן לְבֵיתֵיהּ עָיֵיל. וּלְוַאי שֶׁתְּהֵא בִּיאָה כִּיצִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav, when he would come to court** to judge a case, **would say this** about himself: **By his own will** he **goes out to** danger of **death,** as a judge who misjudges a case is liable to receive the punishment of death at the hand of Heaven; **and he does not do** what is necessary to provide for **the needs of his house, and he enters his home empty-handed,** because a judge does not receive a salary. He said: **If only** it should be so that his **entry** into his home **will be** the same **as** his **departure,** without sin or transgression.

Having addressed the grave weight and spiritual stakes of rendering judgment, and the particular corruption of political appointment, the *gemara* now turns to how a righteous judge deals with the honor that can accompany the role. Rav, when he would come to the *beit din* to serve as judge, would recite a declaration: *b're'ut nafshiah l'katla nafik* — through my own desire I am going out to death. If I judge incorrectly, I deserve to die; I am walking into that exposure voluntarily. *Utzvei beiteih leit hu avid* — and I am not attending to my family's needs. I am not working, I am not earning, I am not bringing home any material gain. *V'reikan l'beiteih ayeil* — I will return home empty-handed. *Velivai shetehei biah kiyitziah* — and I only hope that my going in is like my going out, meaning that I return without having made things worse.

This declaration, one imagines, was intended to pre-empt both the temptation of honor and others' perception that honor was sought. Yet it apparently did not prevent crowds from gathering to follow Rav when he would leave the court. When he would see the *ambua* — the crowd — gathering behind him, he would say to himself: *im yaaleh lashamayim seo, kegilalo lanetzach yoved* — if you rise to heaven, like your dung you will ultimately be destroyed. Whatever honor accrues in this world is passing and without ultimate significance; this was how he kept himself from being consumed by it.

### Rise to Heaven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָוֵי חָזֵי אַמְבּוּהָא דְסָפְרֵי אַבָּתְרֵיהּ, אָמַר: ״אִם יַעֲלֶה לַשָּׁמַיִם שִׂיאוֹ [וְגוֹ׳] כְּגֶלְלוֹ לָנֶצַח יֹאבֵד וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** In a similar demonstration of humility, **when** Rav **would see a convoy [*ambuha*] of scribes** following **after him** to honor him, he **would say: "Though his excellency mount up to the heavens** and his head reach the clouds, **yet he shall perish forever like his own dung;** they who have seen him shall say: Where is he?" (Job 20:6–7).

In a similar demonstration of humility, when he would see the *ambua* — the crowd — gathering behind him, he would say to himself the verse from Job beginning *im yaaleh lashamayim*.

### Mar Zutra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר זוּטְרָא חֲסִידָא, כִּי הֲווֹ מְכַתְּפִי לֵיהּ בְּשַׁבְּתָא דְרִיגְלָא, אָמַר הָכִי: ״כִּי לֹא לְעוֹלָם חֹסֶן וְאִם נֵזֶר לְדוֹר וָדוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** It is said of **Mar Zutra the Pious** that **when** the people **would carry him** to his lectures **on their shoulders during Shabbat of the Festival,** he would **say this** to avoid becoming arrogant: **"For power is not forever, and does the crown endure for all generations?"** (Proverbs 27:24).

Mar Zutra the *chasid* faced a similar challenge. On the *Shabbos* before the *regel*, when he would deliver public *drashos* to large crowds, people would carry him on their shoulders to bring him quickly through the assembled masses to the front. The physical experience of being borne aloft while everyone else sat below was its own form of *kavod*, however practically motivated. When they would do this, he would recite to himself: *ki lo leolam chosen veim nezer lador dor* — wealth is not forever, and glory is not for all generations. Honor, position, elevation — all of it passes.

## Steps in Judgment

### No Leaping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ בַּר קַפָּרָא: מְנָא הָא מִילְּתָא דַּאֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן, ״הֱווּ מְתוּנִין בַּדִּין״? דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲלֶה בְמַעֲלֹת״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ ״וְאֵלֶּה הַמִּשְׁפָּטִים״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִנַּיִין לְדַיָּין שֶׁלֹּא יְפַסֵּעַ עַל רָאשֵׁי עַם קוֹדֶשׁ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תַעֲלֶה בְמַעֲלֹת״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ ״וְאֵלֶּה הַמִּשְׁפָּטִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Bar Kappara taught,** based on a homiletical interpretation of a verse: **From where is this matter that the Sages stated** derived: **Be temperate in judgment** (*Avot* 1:1)? **As it is written: "Neither shall you go up by steps** onto My altar" (Exodus 20:23), i.e., do not ascend hurriedly, **and juxtaposed to it,** it is written: **"Now these are the ordinances** that you shall set before them" (Exodus 21:1). **Rabbi Eliezer says: From where** is it derived **that a judge may not step over the heads of the sacred nation,** walking among those assembled for the lecture, who would sit upon the floor, in such a manner that he has the appearance of stepping on them? It is derived from that **which is stated: "Neither shall you go up by steps," and juxtaposed to it** is an introduction to civil laws and courtroom regulations: **"Now these are the ordinances."**

The *gemara* now turns to the verse *lo saaleh bemaalos* — do not go up in steps — and reads it in proximity to *ve'eileh hamishpatim asher tasim lifneichem*. Bar Kappara asks: from where do we derive the rabbinic teaching of *hevei masun badin*, be deliberate in judgment? From this verse. Steps, as opposed to a ramp, involve leaping over intermediate points in a process. The judge must not skip stages; every step of the deliberative process must be covered. Rabbi Elazar adds a further derivation: from where do we know that a judge may not step over the heads of the assembled people — physically stride over those sitting — since this projects a sense of superiority, as though towering above them? From the same verse and its *semichut* to the laws of judgment.

### Instruments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר תָּשִׂים״? ״אֲשֶׁר תְּלַמְּדֵם״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: אֵלּוּ כְּלֵי הַדַּיָּינִין. רַב הוּנָא, כִּי הֲוָה נָפֵק לְדִינָא, אָמַר הָכִי: אַפִּיקוּ לִי מָאנֵי חֲנוּתַאי: מַקֵּל וּרְצוּעָה, וְשׁוֹפָרָא, וְסַנְדָּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets the second part of the verse cited above: "Now these are the ordinances **that you shall set** before them." The verse **should have** stated: **That you shall teach them.** What is indicated by the phrase: "Set before them"? **Rabbi Yirmeya, and some say Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, says: These are the judges' tools.** To illustrate this, the Gemara relates that **Rav Huna, when he would go out to a judgment, would say this: Take out for me tools from my shop: A rod and strap,** with which to flog transgressors; **and a shofar,** necessary in the event that someone must be excommunicated; **and a sandal,** necessary in the event of *ḥalitza*, the procedure by which a levirate marriage is rejected.

The *gemara* then asks why the verse says *asher tasim* — which you shall place — rather than *asher telamdem*, which you shall teach. Rabbi Yirmiyah, and some say Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, explains: *eileh klei hadayanim* — these are the instruments of the judges, placed before them to maintain order and project authority. When Rav Huna would go out to judge, he would say: *afiku li manei chanusai* — bring me the vessels of my store, my professional instruments. These were: a *makel* and *retzuah*, a staff and a leather strap for administering lashes; a *shofar* for imposing *cherem*; and a *sandal*, which Rashi explains as used for *chalitzah*. That last item seems somewhat out of place alongside the others — the staff, strap, and *shofar* all belong to the same category of judicial enforcement, while *chalitzah* is a distinct procedure — but it is apparently included among the instruments associated with the court's authority.

### Severity & Restraint

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וָאֲצַוֶּה אֶת שֹׁפְטֵיכֶם בָּעֵת הַהִיא״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּנֶגֶד מַקֵּל וּרְצוּעָה תְּהֵא זָרִיז.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets other verses related to the topic of adjudicating cases. **"And I charged your judges at that time,** saying: Hear the causes between your brethren, and judge righteously between a man and his brother, and the stranger who is with him" (Deuteronomy 1:16). Understanding that the word "charged" indicates alacrity, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Moses urged the judges: **With regard to the rod and the strap, be vigilant.**

Rabbi Yochanan derives from *va'atzaveh es shofteichem ba'es hahi* — and I commanded your judges at that time — an instruction regarding the staff and strap: *keneged makel u'retzuah tehei zariz*, regarding the staff and strap be zealous. But what exactly does *tehei zariz* demand? Rashi reads it as owning the responsibility to make people properly fearful and respectful of the court's authority — *sheyihyu metilin eimah al hatzibur leshem shamayim*. If you carry the instrument, people need to know you are serious about using it. *Tosafot*, however, reads it in nearly the opposite direction: *shelo lehatil eimah al hatzibur leradosam yoser midai* — be careful not to impose excessive fear on the public, not to go too far. The two readings, taken together, describe the balance a judge must strike: owning the authority fully while not abusing it.

## Both Parties Present

### Shamoa Bein

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שָׁמֹעַ בֵּין אֲחֵיכֶם וּשְׁפַטְתֶּם״ – אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אַזְהָרָה לַדַּיָּין שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵי בַּעַל דִּין קוֹדֶם שֶׁיָּבֹא בַּעַל דִּין חֲבֵירוֹ, וְאַזְהָרָה לְבַעַל דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יַטְעִים דְּבָרָיו לַדַּיָּין קוֹדֶם שֶׁיָּבֹא בַּעַל דִּין חֲבֵירוֹ. קְרִי בֵּיהּ נָמֵי: ״שַׁמַּע בֵּין אֲחֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the clause **"Hear the causes between your brethren, and judge," Rabbi Ḥanina says:** This is **a warning to a court that** it **may not hear the statement of** one **litigant before the other litigant comes, and** it is **a warning to a litigant that** he **may not explain his statement to the judge before the other litigant comes. Read into** the phrase in the verse: **"Hear the causes between your brethren,"** that it is **also concerning** the litigant.

Returning to the *pesukim* at the opening of *Devarim*, the *gemara* examines *shamoa bein achichem ushfatetem tzedek* — hear between your brethren and judge righteously. Rabbi Chanina derives two obligations from this phrase. First, *azharah l'beis din shelo yishma divrei baal din kodem sheyavo baal din chaveiro* — a prohibition on the court to hear one litigant's arguments before the other litigant has appeared. Second, *va'azharah l'vaal din shelo yat'im devarav l'dayan kodem sheyavo baal din chaveiro* — a parallel prohibition on the litigant himself from presenting his case to the judge before his counterpart arrives. The verse supports both: reading *shamoa* not only as "hear" but as "make heard" — *kari beih nami shama bein achichem* — when you make your case heard, both parties must be present.

### Anchoring Effect

Rashi's comment here is particularly incisive. When a litigant appears before the judge without his opponent present, *shema mesader divrei sheker kedivrei emes* — he will arrange false claims to sound like truth, because *ein machshisho* — no one is there to contradict him. And critically: *mikeivan shelev hadayan noteh lo lizkot, shuv ein bo mahapech bizchut hasheni kol kach* — once the judge's mind has tilted toward acquitting the first litigant, it is much harder to achieve a full reversal in favor of the second once the other side is heard. The first presentation anchors the judge's perception of the case; it becomes the default frame through which all subsequent information is processed. Even once both sides are present and both arguments heard, the asymmetry introduced by hearing one side first does not simply disappear. This is why the rule applies not only to the court but to the litigant himself: a litigant appearing before a judge when his opponent is absent will not spin out a complete fabrication as readily if the opponent were present, and in any case the judge's mind would already be framing the situation as one with two sides to hear. The rabbi or judge must always keep in mind, when hearing one party's account alone, that this is one version of a story and no more.

## False Reports

### False Reports

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: מֵהָכָא, מִ״לֹּא תִשָּׂא״ – לֹא תַשִּׂיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says** the litigant's responsibility may be derived **from here: From "you shall not bear [*tissa*]** a false report" (Exodus 23:1). Although conjugated in this manner the verb would seem to be addressed to the judges, commanding them not to lend credence to a false report, the term may also be read as: **You shall not deliver [*tassi*]** a false report, conjugated so that it addresses the litigants and the witnesses.

Rav Kahana derives from *lo tisa shema shav* — do not lift up a false report — a prohibition that extends beyond the context of oaths. Reading *lo tasi* (do not carry) alongside *lo tisha* (do not take up), the verse prohibits not only making a false claim but receiving and carrying one. *Al tashet yadcha im rasha lihyot ed chamas* — do not place your hand with the wicked to be a witness of violence — confirms that the prohibition encompasses the acceptance of false testimony, not only its production.

## Tzedek Din

### Know First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּשְׁפַטְתֶּם צֶדֶק״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: צַדֵּק אֶת הַדִּין, וְאַחַר כָּךְ חׇתְכֵהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the verse in Deuteronomy cited above: "And I charged your judges at that time, saying: Hear the causes between your brethren, **and judge righteously." Reish Lakish says: Verify the judgment** by meticulously examining the particulars of the case, **and** only **afterward, implement it.**

Back to *ushfatetem tzedek* — and you shall judge righteously. Reish Lakish states: *tzedek et hadin v'achar kach chatcheihu* — justify the judgment, and then cut it — meaning, deliver it. Know what the ruling is before you issue it; do not announce a *psak* before you have fully worked through your reasoning and reached a conclusion. Rashi does not fully explicate what *tzedek et hadin* demands beyond the general obligation of proper deliberation. One reading would have the judge articulate his reasoning before announcing the ruling, presenting the analysis and then the conclusion. But there is a countervailing consideration: a ruling prefaced by its entire reasoning has a different psychological weight than one stated directly, and one might argue the ruling should come first — *Reuven is right*, with reasoning available thereafter. In any event, the core principle of *tzedek et hadin v'achar kach chatcheihu* appears to require that the judge be fully certain of the *psak* before he states it publicly — know the result before you cut it loose.

## Among Relatives

### House & Chamber

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בֵּין אִישׁ וּבֵין אָחִיו וּבֵין גֵּרוֹ״. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֲפִילּוּ בֵּין בַּיִת לַעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The judge must distinguish **even between** the merits of **a house and the upper story** when dividing a two-floor property among inheriting brothers. Rav Yehuda says:** The judge must distinguish **even between** the merits of **an oven and a stove.**

*Bein ish u'vein achiv u'vein gero* — between one man and his brother and his sojourner. Rav Yehuda derives: *afilu bein bayit l'aliyah* — even between the house and the upper chamber. If two brothers inherit a property, do not summarily divide it by telling one to take the ground floor and the other the upper story. Those two portions may differ in value, and if so, real justice requires calculating the difference and having the one who receives the more valuable portion compensate accordingly. Similarly, if household items are divided, one item may be worth more than another, and splitting evenly by category rather than by value fails to deliver true justice. Even in cases between close relatives, even in cases that appear simple and easy to resolve, the obligation is to execute genuine justice, not an approximate or lazy approximation of it.

## Recognition & Bias

### No Favor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֹא תַכִּירוּ פָנִים בַּמִּשְׁפָּט״. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא תַּכִּירֵהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"You shall not respect [*takiru*] people in judgment;** you shall hear the small and the great alike; you shall not be afraid before any man, for the judgment is God's; and the cause that is too hard for you, you shall bring to me, and I will hear it" (Deuteronomy 1:17). **Rabbi Yehuda says: Do not recognize him [*takirehu*],** i.e., do not acknowledge the litigant as a friend in your role as a judge.

*Lo takiru fanim bamishpat* — do not recognize faces in judgment. Rabbi Yehuda reads this straightforwardly: *lo takiruhu* — do not recognize someone and on the basis of that recognition show them favor. This is the plain meaning.

### Balance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: לֹא תְּנַכְּרֵהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** Even if he is your opponent, **do not estrange him [*tenakerehu*]** in such a way as to prejudge him as liable, but treat him as though you do not know him at all.

Rabbi Elazar adds a refinement: *lo tenakruhu* — but do not make a stranger of him either. Rashi explains: if someone before you in judgment is a person you dislike, do not treat him as though he were a foreigner with whom you have no connection, as though you owed him nothing and could rule against him freely. The obligation is to maintain genuine objectivity — neither favoring the familiar nor penalizing the disliked.

### Innkeeper Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּשְׁפִּיזְכָנֵיהּ דְּרַב אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ לְדִינָא. אָמַר לוֹ: לָאו אוּשְׁפִּיזְכָנִי אַתְּ? אָמַר לוֹ: אִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינָא אִית לִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּסִילְנָא לָךְ לְדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav's host [*ushpizekhaneih*],** with whom he would stay occasionally, **came before him for a judgment.** Rav **said to** the host: **Are you not my host?** He **said to him: Yes,** I am. The host then **said to him: I have** a dispute with another that needs **a judgment.** Rav **said to him: I am disqualified to judge for you,** because I may be partial to you due to what you've done for me.

A story illustrates how even the act of acknowledging a connection can compromise judicial integrity, quite apart from any actual favoritism. The innkeeper where Rav was lodging came before Rav for a judgment. When the innkeeper saw Rav, he greeted him: *lav ushpixini at* — are you not my lodger? Rav confirmed that he was. The innkeeper then said he had a case to bring. Rav responded: *psilna lach l'dina* — you have disqualified me to be your judge. Rashi explains that it was the act of *mentioning* the connection — *shehizkartani tovascha* — that created the problem. Had the innkeeper not raised it, there might have been no disqualification. *Tosafot* notes that technically this does not rise to the level of *shochad* that invalidates a ruling, and Rav was distancing himself beyond the letter of the law; but once the connection is surfaced in the courtroom, the appearance and the reality of impartiality are both compromised.

Rav therefore said to Rav Kahana: *puk dayneih* — go and judge his case, since I cannot. Rav Kahana saw that the innkeeper was behaving with excessive familiarity, apparently assuming that his social connection to Rav's circle gave him an advantage. Rav Kahana warned him: *itsaisa itsaisa* — listen and show proper deference. *Ve'i lo, nafkina lach Rav me'onecha* — if you continue, I will pull Rav out of your ear, meaning, Rashi explains, I will make it clear that Rav cannot come to your aid by placing you in *cherem* — a finding of contempt of court.

### Deference

The case illustrates that the problem of *lo takiru fanim* is not limited to the judge's internal disposition. The litigant's conduct in the courtroom — his assumption of familiarity, his failure to show proper deference to the proceedings — is itself a corruption of the judicial process, and the court has both the authority and the obligation to address it directly.

## Small vs. Great

### Equal Attention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כַּקָּטֹן כַּגָּדֹל תִּשְׁמָעוּן״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שֶׁיְּהֵא חָבִיב עָלֶיךָ דִּין שֶׁל פְּרוּטָה כְּדִין שֶׁל מֵאָה מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to interpret clauses from the verse cited above. **"You shall hear the small and the great alike"** (Deuteronomy 1:17). **Reish Lakish says:** This teaches **that the judgment of one *peruta* should be as dear,** i.e., important, **to you as the judgment of one hundred *maneh*,** i.e., ten thousand dinars.

*Ke-katan ke-gadol tishme'un* — like the small and the great you shall hear. Reish Lakish interprets: *she-chaviv alecha din shel peruta ke-din shel me'ah maneh* — a case involving a *peruta* should be as important to you as a case involving a hundred *maneh*. The principle applies not only to the social status of the parties but to the magnitude of the case itself.

### First Come

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אִילֵּימָא לְעַיּוֹנֵי בֵּיהּ וּמִיפְסְקֵיהּ – פְּשִׁיטָא! אֶלָּא לְאַקְדּוֹמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this said? **If we say** it is with regard to the need **to study it** carefully **and** to **decide** the case justly, it is **obvious** that even cases relating to small sums must be judged thoroughly. **Rather,** Reish Lakish was speaking with regard **to giving it precedence:** The small claims case may not be deferred in favor of the larger claim merely because the disputed sum is smaller.

The *gemara* asks: *le-mai hilcheta*? If the instruction means to give equal deliberation and care to small and large cases, that is already obvious from the basic obligation of justice. The novel ruling, therefore, concerns priority: even when it comes to which case to hear first, do not set aside a small case in order to deal with the large one first. Whatever came before the court first is dealt with first — *le-akdomei*, to prioritize — regardless of the relative size of the claims at stake.

## Independence

### Fear None

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֹא תָגוּרוּ מִפְּנֵי אִישׁ״. אָמַר רַבִּי חָנָן: אַל תַּכְנִיס דְּבָרֶיךָ מִפְּנֵי אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the interpretation of the verse: **"You shall not be afraid before any man." Rabbi Ḥanan says: Do not suppress your statement** of opinion **due to** any **person.**

*Lo taguru mi-pnei ish* — do not fear any person. Rav Chanan reads this expansively: *al tachnis devarecha mi-pnei ish* — do not hold back your words because of anyone. This applies not only to the judge but to the court clerk or any officer of the court who might be in a position to suppress a message or a ruling out of deference to a powerful figure.

### God's Justice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי הַמִּשְׁפָּט לֵאלֹהִים הוּא״. אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, לֹא דַּיָּין לָרְשָׁעִים שֶׁנּוֹטְלִין מִזֶּה מָמוֹן וְנוֹתְנִים לָזֶה שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין, אֶלָּא שֶׁמַּטְרִיחִין אוֹתִי לְהַחֲזִיר מָמוֹן לִבְעָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"For the judgment is God's." Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, says: It is not enough for the wicked** judges, **that** they **take money from this** person **and** they **give** it **to that** person **unlawfully, but** they even **trouble Me to return the money to its** rightful **owners.**

*Ki ha-mishpat le-Elohim hu* — because justice belongs to God. Rabbi Chama son of Rabbi Chanina derives: *lo dayam la-resha'im she-notlin mi-zeh mammon ve-notnin le-zeh she-lo ke-din* — it is not enough that the wicked — and here the term encompasses the corrupt judges alongside ordinary wrongdoers — take money from one and give it to another against the demands of justice. Beyond the immediate injustice, *ela she-matrichin oti le-hachzir mammon le-ba'alav* — they force upon God the burden of restoring the money to its rightful owner. This returns to the earlier principle that unjust judgment is not only a wrong done to the parties; it is a wrong done against God, who is left to clean up the corruption of His world.

### Moshe's Overreach & Its Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַדָּבָר אֲשֶׁר יִקְשֶׁה מִכֶּם תַּקְרִיבוּן אֵלַי וּשְׁמַתִּיו״. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: עַל דָּבָר זֶה נֶעֱנַשׁ מֹשֶׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיַּקְרֵב מֹשֶׁה אֶת מִשְׁפָּטָן לִפְנֵי ה׳״. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מִי כְּתִיב ״וְאַשְׁמִיעֲכֶם״? ״וּשְׁמַעְתִּיו״ כְּתִיב! אִי גְּמִירְנָא – גְּמִירְנָא, וְאִי לָא – אָזֵילְנָא וּגְּמִירְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And the cause that is too hard for you,** you shall bring to me, and I will hear it." **Rabbi Ḥanina, and some say Rabbi Yoshiya, says: On** account of **this matter,** which displayed a degree of presumptuousness, **Moses was punished, as it is stated: "And Moses brought their cause before the Lord"** (Numbers 27:5). In this instance, when Zelophehad's daughters presented their case to Moses, he did not know the answer himself and was compelled to ask God. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak objects to this** critique of Moses: **Is it written: "And I will tell you,"** which would indicate that Moses assumed the authority for himself? No, **it is written: "And I will hear it,"** meaning: **If I** have **learned** the *halakha*, **I** have **learned** it. **And if not, I will go and learn** it.

*Ve-ha-davar asher yiksheh mi-chem takrivun elai u-shmati-v* — and the matter that is too difficult for you, bring it to me and I will hear it. Rabbi Chanina, and some say Rabbi Yoshiya, teaches that Moshe was punished for this declaration — for presenting himself as the ultimate arbiter who would always have the answer, rather than saying he would bring it to God. The case of *Benot Tzelofchad* demonstrates this: they came with a question, and Moshe had to bring it before God.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak challenges: the verse does not say *ashmi'achem*, I will tell you the ruling. It says *u-shmati-v*, I will hear it. Perhaps Moshe meant only that he would hear the question and then go to receive the ruling from God — not that he would always have the answer himself. *Ei gemirna gemirna* — if I know the ruling I will give it; *ve'i lo, azlina ve-gemirna* — if not, I will go and learn it from God. On this reading, Moshe said nothing wrong, and the fact that *Benot Tzelofchad* required him to ask God was not a form of punishment.

### The Deeper Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כִּדְתַנְיָא: רְאוּיָה פָּרָשַׁת נַחֲלוֹת שֶׁתִּיכָּתֵב עַל יְדֵי מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁזָּכוּ בְּנוֹת צְלָפְחָד וְנִכְתְּבָה עַל יָדָן. רְאוּיָה הָיְתָה פָּרָשַׁת מְקוֹשֵׁשׁ שֶׁתִּיכָּתֵב עַל יְדֵי מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב מְקוֹשֵׁשׁ וְנִכְתְּבָה עַל יָדוֹ. לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁמְגַלְגְּלִין חוֹבָה עַל יְדֵי חַיָּיב, וּזְכוּת עַל יְדֵי זַכַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the unusual manner in which the *halakha* of women's inheritance (see Numbers, chapter 27) was revealed may be understood **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: It would have been **fitting for the** Torah **portion about inheritances to have been written by** attributing it to **Moses, our teacher,** i.e., to introduce the *halakha* with the standard formulation: And the Lord spoke to Moses, saying. **But the daughters of Zelophehad achieved merit** as a result of their initiative in pursuing a portion in Eretz Yisrael, **and** therefore the *halakha* **was written by** attributing it to **them.** Similarly, it **would have been fitting for the** Torah **portion** concerning the punishment **of** the wood **gatherer** (see Numbers 15:32–36) **to have been written by** attributing it to **Moses, our teacher. But the** wood **gatherer was found guilty, and** the *halakha* **was written by** attributing it to **him.** This serves **to teach you that guilt is engendered by means of the guilty and merit by means of the innocent.**

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty differently. *Re'uya parashat nachalot she-tikatev al yedei Moshe Rabbeinu* — the section of inheritance was destined to be written through Moshe regardless of whether *Benot Tzelofchad* had ever come to him. Had they never raised the question, at some point within the forty years the ruling would have been issued anyway. *Ella shezachu Bnot Tzelafchad venichteva al yadan* — but they merited, and so it was written in conjunction with them, because they stood up for what was right and approached Moshe in the proper way. Similarly, *re'uya hi parshat ha-mekoshesh* — the portion concerning the one who gathered wood on *Shabbat* would have been written regardless, conveying the punishment for *chilul Shabbat*; but because that particular individual was guilty, *nitchayev mekoshesh venichteva al yado*, the section was attached to him. *Megalgelin chovah al yedei chayav vezachut al yedei zakai* — liability gravitates toward those who are guilty, and merit gravitates toward those who are righteous. The *mekoshesh* is forever associated with the *parasha* of *chilul Shabbat*'s punishment; *Benot Tzelofchad* are forever associated with the *parasha* of women's inheritance.

## Bear Complaints

### How Long?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״וָאֲצַוֶּה אֶת שֹׁפְטֵיכֶם בָּעֵת הַהִיא״, וּכְתִיב: ״וָאֲצַוֶּה אֶתְכֶם בָּעֵת הַהִיא״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי: אַזְהָרָה לַצִּיבּוּר שֶׁתְּהֵא אֵימַת דַּיָּין עֲלֵיהֶן, וְאַזְהָרָה לַדַּיָּין שֶׁיִּסְבּוֹל אֶת הַצִּיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to interpret verses pertaining to judges and judgment. **It is written: "And I charged your judges at that time"** (Deuteronomy 1:16), **and it is written** soon thereafter: **"And I commanded you at that time** all the things that you should do" (Deuteronomy 1:18). There is an apparent contradiction between these verses, as one indicates God commanded the judges and the other indicates He commanded the people. **Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Simlai says:** Moses issued **a warning to the community that the awe of the judge must be upon them, and** Moses issued **a warning to the judge that** he must **bear** the burden of **the community.**

*Va'atzaveh es shofteichem ba'es hahi* and *va'atzaveh etchem ba'es hahi* — I commanded your judges at that time, and I commanded you at that time. Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Simlai derives from the dual formulation two distinct obligations. *Azharah la-tzibur shetehei eimat dayan aleihem* — the public must maintain proper fear and deference toward the judges. *Va'azharah la-dayan sheyisbol et ha-tzibur* — and the judge must bear the burden of the public. Both sides have their obligations in the relationship between judge and community.

### Nursemaid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי חָנָן, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי שַׁבְּתַאי: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יִשָּׂא הָאֹמֵן אֶת הַיֹּנֵק״.
>
> **Translation:** Up to what** degree must the judge bear this burden? **Rabbi Ḥanan, and some say Rabbi Shabbtai, says:** It is as Moses said, that he carried Israel **"as a nursing father carries the sucking child"** (Numbers 11:12).

To what extent must a judge tolerate the friction and complaints that come from those he serves? Rabbi Yochanan, and some say Rav Shavtai, answers: *ka'asher yisa ha-omen et ha-yonek* — as a nursemaid bears a crying infant. The obligation of patience with the community's burden and complaint is without limit in this formulation, even as we know that Moshe himself protested the weight of the people's demands and was granted the *shivim zekeinim* in response.

## One Voice

### Go With Elders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב: ״כִּי אַתָּה תָּבוֹא״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי אַתָּה תָּבִיא״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָמַר לוֹ מֹשֶׁה לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ: אַתָּה וְהַזְּקֵנִים שֶׁבַּדּוֹר עִמָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, a distinction is drawn between two verses where Moses commanded Joshua concerning his impending leadership: **It is written: "For you shall go** with these people into the land" (Deuteronomy 31:7), **and it is written: "For you shall bring** the children of Israel into the land" (Deuteronomy 31:23). **Rabbi Yoḥanan** reconciled the difference between these two charges and **said: Moses said to Joshua,** in the first verse: **You and the elders of the generation** will enter into Eretz Yisrael together **with** the people, i.e., the elders will assist you in the leadership.

When Moshe spoke to Yehoshua before the people's entry into the land, the verse reads: *ki ata tavo et ha-am ha-zeh* — for you will go with this people. Rabbi Yochanan explains: Moshe was telling Yehoshua that he would lead together with the elders of the generation, not as sole authority. *Ata u-zekeinim shebador imahem* — you and the elders of the generation together with them.

### One Leader

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: טוֹל מַקֵּל וְהַךְ עַל קׇדְקֳדָם. דַּבָּר אֶחָד לַדּוֹר, וְאֵין שְׁנֵי דַּבָּרִין לַדּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** Subsequently, in the second verse, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to** Joshua: You yourself must bring the people: **Take a rod and strike** the people **upon their skulls.** There must be **one** clear and authoritative **leader for the generation, and** there may **not** be **two** or more **leaders for the generation.**

But God's instruction to Yehoshua uses a different formulation: *ki ata tavi* — for you will bring them. God told Yehoshua: *tol makeil vehach al kadkodam* — take the staff and strike them on the head. Do not be so retiring; you are the leader. *Dabar echad lador ve'ein shnei devarim lador* — one voice for a generation, not two. Consultation with the elders is appropriate; indeed, Rav Huna's practice of gathering colleagues precisely reflects the wisdom of hearing multiple perspectives. But leadership requires one person who owns the final authority. Even if Yehoshua consults with the *zekeinim*, he must lead, because a generation cannot have two overlapping authorities — they will climb on each other's heads. *Ata tavi* — you, singular, will bring them.

## Zimmun

### Full Court

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: זִימּוּן בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה. מַאי זִימּוּן? אִילֵּימָא בִּרְכַּת זִימּוּן, וְהָתַנְיָא: זִימּוּן וּבִרְכַּת זִימּוּן בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה! וְכִי תֵּימָא פָּרוֹשֵׁי קָמְפָרֵשׁ: מַאי זִימּוּן? בִּרְכַּת זִימּוּן. וְהָתַנְיָא: זִימּוּן בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, בִּרְכַּת זִימּוּן בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה! אֶלָּא מַאי זִימּוּן? אַזְמוֹנֵי לְדִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the discussion of halakhic matters related to the mishna. The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: **A *zimmun*** is to be issued **with three** people. The Gemara analyzes: **What** is meant by the term ***zimmun*** in the *baraita*? **If we say** it is referring to the invitation to recite Grace after Meals jointly, which is called **the *zimmun* blessing,** that is difficult. **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: ***Zimmun* and the *zimmun* blessing are** to be issued **with three** people? As the *baraita* lists these *halakhot* separately, it indicates that the term *zimmun* is referring to a different matter. **And if you would say** that the *baraita* **explains** itself, by clarifying: **What** is meant by the term ***zimmun*?** It is **the *zimmun* blessing,** another source indicates even more clearly that these terms are referring to two different procedures. **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: ***Zimmun*** is performed **with three** people, and **the *zimmun* blessing** is performed **with three** people? Evidently, therefore, *zimmun* is not referring to the *zimmun* blessing. **Rather, what** is ***zimmun*?** It is **a summons** to appear in court **for judgment.**

A *tanna* teaches: *zimmun bishlosha* — a *zimmun* requires three. The *gemara* asks what this *zimmun* means, since *birkat zimmun* — the invitation to *bentch* — is already known to require three, and this teaching seems to add nothing. The *gemara* therefore concludes that this is a different *zimmun* altogether: *azmuni l'din* — summoning someone to appear in court. That judicial summons must be issued through the *beit din*, three judges.

### Non-Court Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הָנֵי בֵּי תְלָתָא דַּיָּינֵי דְּיָתְבִי, וְאָזֵיל שְׁלִיחָא דְּבֵי דִּינָא וְאָמַר מִפּוּמָּא דְּחַד – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם, עַד דְּאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּכוּלְּהוּ. הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּלָא יוֹמָא דְּדִינָא, אֲבָל בְּיוֹמָא דְּדִינָא – לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this is **similar** to **that which Rava said:** With regard to **this court of three judges who are sitting** and waiting to adjudicate a case, **and the agent of the court went and said** to one of the litigants that he is summoned **in the name of** only **one** of the judges, the summons is insufficient. It is as if the agent **did not say anything, unless** he **said** it **in the name of all of them.** That is what is meant when it is taught that *zimmun* is issued by three. The Gemara clarifies: **That statement** applies only **when** it is **not the day** set for **judgment. But on the day** set for **judgment,** when it is understood that a summons in the name of only one judge actually indicates a summons from an assembled court, **we have no** problem **with it.**

Rava explains the practical rule: if the three judges of a *beit din* are sitting and a court messenger goes to summon someone, but issues the summons *mishmei d'chad* — in the name of only one of the judges — *lo amar klum*, he has said nothing effective and the recipient is not obligated to respond. The summons is only valid when issued *mishmei d'chulhu* — in the name of the entire court, all three judges. This applies on days when the court is not formally sitting — that is, not on Mondays and Thursdays, when the court convenes by established practice.

*Aval beyoma d'dina* — but on a day when the court is formally sitting, *mishmei d'chad* does suffice. When it is a day of judgment and the court is in session, a summons issued in the name of one judge is understood to carry the authority of the full sitting *beit din* — the single judge's name implies the entire court behind him. Outside of court days, that inference cannot be drawn, and the summons must therefore explicitly invoke all three.
