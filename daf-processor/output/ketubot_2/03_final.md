# Ketubot 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Ketubot

### Opening & Context

We begin a new *masechta*. There is a well-known saying in the *yeshiva* world that three *masechtot* will, as it were, wipe you out — and the acronym for them is *Ani*, meaning poverty: *Eruvin*, *Niddah*, and *Yevamos*. Having just completed *Yevamos* — which was particularly challenging at the outset, though the middle and end proved highly relevant and engaging — we turn now to *Ketubot*. *Niddah* remains for the very end.

*Ketubot* carries its own famous reputation in the *yeshiva* world: it is known as the *Shas Katan*, the mini-*Shas*, because so many diverse concepts from across *Shas* arise in the course of its discussions.

### Nisuin vs. Kiddushin

The primary topic of *Ketubot* is, one might say, the *ketubah* — but more precisely, it is the legal and practical state of *nisuin*, as distinct from *kiddushin*. *Kiddushin* is the formal legal act of marriage: the giving of the ring in contemporary practice, which renders the woman fully and legally married such that any subsequent infidelity would constitute adultery. Yet at this stage, the couple has not begun living together. That actualization — the setting up of a shared home and life — is *chuppah*, the moment of *nisuin*.

In ancient practice, *kiddushin* could take place when a woman was still a minor, arranged between the prospective husband and her father, and might precede *nisuin* by many years. *Chuppah* was thus the moment when the marriage became real, not merely legal. As a result, it is *chuppah* that is the great communal event — the *simcha* with the large *seudah*, the *sheva brachot* requiring a *minyan* and representing the blessing of the community upon the *chatan* and *kallah*. The *bracha* of *eirusin*, by contrast, does not require a *minyan* and is far more private in character. It is worth noting that while one certainly requires *edim* for *kiddushin*, it is not entirely clear that formal *edim* are required for *chuppah* itself, since *nisuin* may be understood simply as the actualization of the *kiddushin* that had already taken place — potentially years earlier.

### Three Parts Ketubah

Once *nisuin* begins, a range of reciprocal obligations take effect. The husband becomes obligated to provide food, lodging, and *mitzvat onah* — conjugal relations at prescribed intervals. The wife takes on obligations toward the husband as well, including the rabbinic institution that her earnings belong to him. These ongoing mutual obligations are referred to as *tnai ketubah*, the stipulations of the *ketubah* — not "conditions" in the technical sense, but rather the ongoing terms that govern the marriage.

The *ketubah* itself, then, has three major components. The first is this body of ongoing reciprocal obligations. The second is the lump-sum payment — 200 *zuz* for a virgin bride, 100 *zuz* for a widow or divorcee — which would be collected by the wife either upon her husband's death or upon divorce. Although the Torah does not provide for a wife to inherit her husband, the rabbis instituted that she may draw this sum from the estate. The third component is the documentation of whatever property, the *nedunyah*, the bride brings into the marriage as a dowry, which she is entitled to take back upon death or divorce.

These three elements together constitute the subject matter of *Masechet Ketubot*. Crucially, these obligations are legally binding whether or not any document was actually written. The reciprocal obligations exist; the lump-sum liability exists — the document records them, but they do not depend on it. In a deeper sense, *Ketubot* is a tractate about the legal texture of *nisuin* itself.

One might ask about the modern *ketubah*, in which the dowry property is not specifically enumerated. The phrase *bein b'chesef bein b'zahav bein b'tachshitin* functions as a pro forma statement encompassing all property brought into the marriage at a stated valuation, without itemizing it in detail.

It should also be noted that the ordering of the tractates may seem counterintuitive. *Kiddushin* — the legal act of betrothal — comes later in the order of *Seder Nashim*, and *Gittin* precedes it, simply because *masechtot* are ordered by length as measured in chapters. *Ketubot*, as the longest, comes first.

### Broader Themes

Beyond these central themes, *Ketubot* earns its reputation as *Shas Katan* through the many broader halakhic discussions it occasions. One major area is the law of *kam lei b'derabba minei*: since part of the *ketubah* payment derives historically from the fine imposed upon a man who rapes or seduces an unmarried woman — a fine calibrated to the *ketubah* sum she might otherwise have expected — the tractate engages extensively with the cases of rape and seduction from the Torah's relevant *parshiyot*. This in turn generates discussions of when a more severe transgression incurred through a given act displaces a financial obligation, which is the principle of *kam lei b'derabba minei*. Another broad *Shas* theme introduced by this tractate concerns *edim*: questions of when witnesses can retract testimony on a signed document, whether because they were compelled to sign, were minors at the time, or for other reasons. Since *Ketubot* deals centrally with a signed legal document, such questions arise naturally — and their implications extend far beyond the *ketubah* itself.

### Student Q: Dowry

As noted above, the listing of dowry property in the modern *ketubah* takes a pro forma form: the text encompasses all property brought by the bride without specific enumeration, ascribing it a stated value. The detailed itemization that characterized the classical *ketubah* is no longer the practice.

## Wedding Days

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּתוּלָה נִשֵּׂאת לַיּוֹם הָרְבִיעִי וְאַלְמָנָה לַיּוֹם הַחֲמִישִׁי. שֶׁפַּעֲמַיִם בְּשַׁבָּת בָּתֵּי דִינִין יוֹשְׁבִין בָּעֲיָירוֹת: בַּיּוֹם הַשֵּׁנִי וּבַיּוֹם הַחֲמִישִׁי, שֶׁאִם הָיָה לוֹ טַעֲנַת בְּתוּלִים, הָיָה מַשְׁכִּים לְבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A virgin is married on Wednesday and a widow on Thursday.** The reason for the former is **that twice a week courts convene in the towns, on Monday and Thursday,** so **that if** the husband **had a claim concerning** the bride's **virginity** when consummating the marriage on Wednesday night, **he would go early** the next day **to court** and make his claim.

The *masechta* opens with the *mishnah*: *betulah niset l'yom ha-revi'i* — a virgin would have her *nisuin* on Wednesday. *V'almanah l'yom hachamishi* — a widow or divorcee on Thursday. The *mishnah* then explains: *shepaamayim b'shabbat batei dinin yoshvin ba'ayarot* — because the courts would convene in the villages twice each week, on Monday and Thursday. Therefore, *she'im haya lo ta'anat betulim haya mashkim l'veit din* — if the groom had a *ta'anat betulim*, a claim regarding his bride's virginity, he could appear in court the very next morning.

It is worth being precise about what is meant here. The *nisuin* referred to is *chuppah*, not *kiddushin*. The *kiddushin* could have taken place at any time — even years earlier, even when the bride was a minor. The *chuppah*, the communal *simcha* with the *seudah*, the *sheva brachot* before a *minyan*: that is the event the *mishnah* is discussing.

### Ta'anat Betulim

*Ta'anat betulim* — the claim that one's bride was not a virgin — carries significant halakhic weight and will dominate the entire first *perek*. Two distinct consequences flow from it. The first is financial: the *ketubah* of a *betulah* is 200 *zuz*, while that of a non-virgin is 100 *zuz*, and the groom's claim that she was not a virgin therefore affects his payment obligations. The second and weightier consequence is the suspicion of adultery. Since *kiddushin* may have taken place years before *nisuin*, a bride who is found not to be a virgin on her wedding night raises the concern that she may have been with another man during the intervening period — that is, after the legal marriage of *kiddushin* — which would constitute adultery and render her forbidden to her husband.

### Wednesday Purpose

The logic of the *mishnah* is therefore clear. By scheduling the *nisuin* on Wednesday, the groom who discovers grounds for a *ta'anat betulim* on that night can appear before *beit din* the very next morning, Thursday, when the court is in session. The underlying concern is that if he waits, he may calm down, reconcile himself to the situation, and ultimately decide not to pursue the matter — while the halakhic problem, if she did in fact commit adultery, would remain unresolved.

### Why Not Sunday?

An obvious question presents itself immediately: why not have the *nisuin* on Sunday, allowing the groom to appear before *beit din* on Monday, when the court also convenes? This question is one the *gemara* will address, and it fundamentally changes the nature of the explanation offered by the *mishnah*.

The *mishnah* might be understood as describing a common social practice that developed organically — men found it convenient to marry on Wednesdays so that the courts would be available the next day. Alternatively, as the *gemara* will develop, it may represent a *takanat chachamim*, a formal rabbinic enactment requiring that *nisuin* take place on Wednesday. This distinction, seemingly technical, carries significant practical consequences, as will become clear.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

A major debate between Rashi and *Tosafot* concerns the mechanism by which *ta'anat betulim* operates. Rashi, in the very first line of his commentary, explains: *im hayu ta'anat betulim yashiv l'veit din b'od ka'aso alav* — while he is still angry; *shema yitpayes v'titkarer da'ato v'yikayemnah* — lest he cool down and they remain married; *v'shema hiznah tachtav v'ne'esra alav* — and perhaps she committed adultery and is forbidden to him; and therefore *mitoch she-yavo l'veit din yitbarer ha-davar kshe-yeitze ha-kol shemma yavo'u edim* — by bringing the claim before the court, word will spread and witnesses may come forward to clarify the matter. For Rashi, then, the groom's claim alone does not determine anything; it is the publicity generated by the court proceeding that may produce witnesses and objective clarification.

*Tosafot* disagrees. Based on the *gemara*, *Tosafot* understands that the groom's claim alone already carries consequences, through the principle of *shavyeh anafsheh chaticha d'issura*: by claiming that his wife was not a virgin — and thereby implying that she may have committed adultery — the groom renders her forbidden to himself by the force of his own declaration. He is bound by his own statement. This is a principle familiar from the laws of *kashrut*, here applied — as one might well note, rather strikingly — to a human being.

Yet according to *Tosafot*'s position, a difficulty arises. If the groom's declaration alone already creates the halakhic consequence, why is there a rabbinic interest in ensuring that he brings the matter to *beit din*? The objective clarification of witnesses, which was Rashi's answer, is not *Tosafot*'s mechanism — and *Tosafot* does not appear to directly address this difficulty.

There is also a separate question regarding why this situation does not generate a *sotah* proceeding, given the operative concern about adultery. The *sotah* process requires prior warning and testimony of seclusion. *Shavyeh anafsheh chaticha d'issura* is a different mechanism — it is the personal binding force of one's own claim, not an objective establishment of fact — and the two operate on distinct grounds.

Despite their disagreement on mechanism, both Rashi and *Tosafot* agree, based on the *gemara*, that the point is not merely to describe a communal practice but to ensure that the claim reaches the court rather than being abandoned.

## Mezonot Application

### Higia Zman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ בְּתוּלָה נִשֵּׂאת לַיּוֹם הָרְבִיעִי? לְפִי שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: הִגִּיעַ זְמַן וְלֹא נִישְּׂאוּ — אוֹכְלוֹת מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְאוֹכְלוֹת בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yosef said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Due to what** reason **did they say that a virgin is married on Wednesday?** It is **because we learned** in a mishna elsewhere (57a): **If the time** that the groom designated for the wedding **arrived,** and the wedding was postponed, **and they were not married,** the brides are entitled to **eat from his** food **and,** if he is a priest, **eat *teruma*.**

The *gemara* now introduces a chain of tradition: *amar Rav Yosef amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — Rav Yosef transmitting in the name of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel. The question posed is not why the *mishnah* ordained Wednesday — *mipnei ma amru*, as the phrase is used here, means something more like: what is the practical significance of this teaching? Why do we care? And the answer offered is: *lefi sheshaninu*, because of what we taught in a later *mishnah*: *higia zman v'lo nisu* — the designated wedding date arrived and they did not marry.

This later *mishnah* requires a word of explanation. The "year" referenced here is not a year from *kiddushin* — which might have taken place decades earlier. Rather, from the moment the groom declares his intention to proceed with *nisuin*, the bride is granted one year to prepare — to assemble her dowry, make her arrangements, and ready herself mentally, financially, and in all other respects. The wedding date is thus a year from that declaration of intent, not from the original *kiddushin*.

The later *mishnah* rules: *ocheles mishelo v'ocheles b'trumah* — once that date arrives, even if the *chuppah* has not yet taken place, the husband must begin providing food and support for his bride. His marital obligations commence on the appointed date. Furthermore, if he is a *kohen*, she may even eat his *terumah*, despite the fact that ordinarily eating *terumah* requires full *nisuin* rather than merely *kiddushin*. The rabbis suspended that requirement because by this date she ought already to have been *nesu'ah*. It is in any case practically convenient: if he is obligated to feed her, it is simpler if the food may consist of *terumah*.

### Sunday Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל הִגִּיעַ זְמַן בְּאֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת יְהֵא מַעֲלֶה לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת? לְכָךְ שָׁנִינוּ: בְּתוּלָה נִשֵּׂאת לַיּוֹם הָרְבִיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** **One might think** that if the designated **time arrived on Sunday,** and the wedding was postponed, **he would provide her sustenance** beginning on Sunday. **Therefore, we learned: A virgin is married on Wednesday.** Until Wednesday, the designated time is not considered to have arrived, even if the original date was earlier in the week.

The practical question that Rav Yosef is raising is this: suppose the year expires on a Sunday. Is the groom now obligated to pay *mezonot* from Sunday onward — even though Wednesday has not yet arrived? One might think so, since a year has passed.

### Wednesday Date

The answer is no. *L'kach shaninu besulah niset l'yom ha-revi'i* — that is precisely why the *mishnah* taught that virgins marry on Wednesday. The wedding date was never simply "one year from today." The wedding date is the first Wednesday that falls a year after the declaration of intent. If the year expires on a Sunday, the groom has no obligation to pay *mezonot* for those intervening days — Sunday through Tuesday — because the actual wedding date had always been Wednesday, not Sunday. The three days are not a delay; they were never part of the obligation.

## Mezonot Application (II)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מָרֵיהּ דְּאַבְרָהָם! תָּלֵי תַּנְיָא בִּדְלָא תַּנְיָא?! הֵי תַּנְיָא וְהֵי לָא תַּנְיָא? הָא תַּנְיָא וְהָא תַּנְיָא! אֶלָּא: תָּלֵי תַּנְיָא דִּמְפָרֵשׁ טַעְמָא בִּדְתַנְיָא דְּלָא מְפָרֵשׁ טַעְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** After citing the statements above, **Rav Yosef said** in astonishment: **Lord of Abraham,** Shmuel **makes** that which **is taught dependent on** that which **is not taught,** using the latter to explain the former. The Gemara asks: **Which is taught and which is not taught?** Both **this** mishna **is taught** and **that** mishna **is taught.** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** Shmuel **makes** the *halakha* that **is taught** in the mishna here, **whose reason is explicit, dependent on** a *halakha* that **is taught** in the later mishna, **whose reason is not explicit.** Citation of the later mishna contributed nothing to the understanding of the mishna here.

Rav Yosef, however, is troubled by the formulation he received from his teachers. *Mari d'Avraham* — God of Avraham! — he exclaims: *tali tanya bidla tanya?* You are making a *mishnah* that provides its own explicit reason dependent upon a different *mishnah* that gives no reason? The *gemara* challenges this immediately: *hai tanya v'hai tanya* — both are indeed *mishnayot*. To which Rav Yosef refines the objection: *ella tali tanya demefaresh ta'ama b'tanya d'lo mefaresh ta'ama* — you are making the *mishnah* whose reason is stated explicitly dependent, in terms of its practical import, upon a *mishnah* that states no reason. The explicit stated reason of the first *mishnah* is so that the groom can come to *beit din* the next morning. If you wish to derive an additional halakhic implication from it regarding *mezonot*, that is fine — but you cannot say that this implication is the point of teaching the *mishnah*.

## Why Wednesday

### Rav Yosef Objection

Rav Yosef's objection, then, is not merely formal but conceptual. The *mishnah* about *nisuin* on Wednesday states its own rationale: the groom should be able to appear in *beit din* on Thursday. To then say that the deeper reason for teaching this *mishnah* is to clarify the *mezonot* obligation — which depends on a second *mishnah* that offers no stated rationale — is to misread the teaching. Rav Yosef is not denying that both are *mishnayot*, but insisting that a *mishnah* with an explicit rationale should not be read as serving the purposes of a *mishnah* without one.

### Gemarah Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר הָכִי אִיתְּמַר. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ בְּתוּלָה נִשֵּׂאת לַיּוֹם הָרְבִיעִי? שֶׁאִם הָיָה לוֹ טַעֲנַת בְּתוּלִים — הָיָה מַשְׁכִּים לְבֵית דִּין. וְתִינָּשֵׂא בְּאֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת, שֶׁאִם הָיָה לוֹ טַעֲנַת בְּתוּלִים הָיָה מַשְׁכִּים לְבֵית דִּין? שָׁקְדוּ חֲכָמִים עַל תַּקָּנַת בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁיְּהֵא טוֹרֵחַ בִּסְעוּדָה שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים, אֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת וְשֵׁנִי בַּשַּׁבָּת וּשְׁלִישִׁי בַּשַּׁבָּת, וּבָרְבִיעִי כּוֹנְסָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if it was stated, this** is how **it was stated: Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Due to what** reason **did** the Sages in the mishna **say** that **a virgin is married on Wednesday?** It is so **that if** the husband **had a claim concerning** the bride's **virginity, he would go early** the next day **to court** and make his claim. The Gemara asks: **But** if that is the reason, **let her marry on Sunday, as** then too, **if** the husband **had a claim concerning** the bride's **virginity, he would go early** the next day **to court** and make his claim. The Gemara answers: The **Sages were assiduous in seeing to the well-being of Jewish women** and instituted that the wedding take place on Wednesday, **so that** the groom **would exert** himself **in** arranging **the** wedding **feast for three days: Sunday, Monday, and Tuesday, and on Wednesday, he marries her.**

The *gemara* responds: *ella ei itmar hachi itmar* — if the tradition was indeed transmitted, here is how it should have been formulated. The chain of transmission (*Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*) should have framed the question differently: *mipnei mah amru besulah niset l'yom ha-revi'i she'im haya lo ta'anat betulim haya mashkim l'veit din* — why did they ordain that a virgin marries on Wednesday? So that if the groom had a *ta'anat betulim* he could appear in *beit din* the next morning. This formulation cleanly raises the purpose question without subordinating it to the *mezonot* *mishnah*. And immediately the obvious challenge follows: *v'tinasai b'echad b'shabbat she'im haya lo ta'anat betulim haya mashkim l'veit din* — let her marry on Sunday, so he could appear in *beit din* on Monday?

### Takanat Reason

The answer the *gemara* provides is entirely different from what one might have expected: *shakdu chachamim al takanas bnot Yisrael sheyehe tore'ach b'seudah shlosha yamim* — the rabbis were solicitous of the wellbeing of Jewish women, and they instituted that the groom should exert himself in preparing the *seudah* for three days. The *takanat chachamim* was designed not primarily around the question of *ta'anat betulim* and court access, but around ensuring adequate preparation for the wedding feast.

### Three-Day Preparation

The specific logic is this: the bride had a full year to prepare, while men, proverbially, tend to address such things at the last moment. If the *nisuin* were scheduled for Sunday, the groom might begin his preparations only from *Motzaei Shabbat*. By requiring Wednesday, the rabbis ensured that the groom would spend Sunday, Monday, and Tuesday — three full days — in active preparation for the *seudah*, and only on Wednesday would the *chuppah* take place. The groom's family is, of course, responsible for the wedding celebration, and the rabbis wished to ensure a genuinely festive and properly prepared event.

### Rabbinic Demand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַכְשָׁו שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ ״שָׁקְדוּ״, אוֹתָהּ שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ ״הִגִּיעַ זְמַן וְלֹא נִישְּׂאוּ, אוֹכְלוֹת מִשֶּׁלּוֹ וְאוֹכְלוֹת בִּתְרוּמָה״, הִגִּיעַ זְמַן בְּאֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִכְנוֹס — אֵינוֹ מַעֲלֶה לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And now that we learned** that the reason for the ruling in the mishna that a woman is married on Wednesday is that the Sages **were assiduous** in this matter, then with regard to **that** *halakha*, **which we learned** in the later mishna: **If the time arrived and they were not married,** the brides are entitled to **eat from his** food **and eat *teruma*,** one may conclude: **If** the **time arrived on Sunday, since** he is **unable to marry** her because he is busy preparing the wedding feast, **he is not obligated to provide her sustenance** until Wednesday.

The implicit consequence of this reframing is significant. The *gemara* has now treated the *mishnah*'s ruling not as a description of communal practice but as a rabbinic enactment — a *takanah* — that the wedding must take place on Wednesday. If it is a mere practice, then one might ask: what halakhic force does it carry, and what practical difference does it make? But if it is a *takanat chachamim* that the wedding shall not be held before Wednesday, then from the outset the wedding date was always the first Wednesday following the year of preparation. Sunday was never the date. The date was never postponed; it was always Wednesday. This is what allows the *gemara* to rule, in the context of the *mezonot* obligation, that the groom does not owe payment for the days between Sunday and Wednesday: those days were not a delay at all.

## Ones Exception

### Blame vs. Date

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ: חָלָה הוּא אוֹ שֶׁחָלְתָה הִיא אוֹ שֶׁפֵּירְסָה נִדָּה — אֵינוֹ מַעֲלֶה לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** according to the same line of reasoning, if the husband **fell ill, or if** the bride **fell ill, or** in a case **where she began to menstruate [*peirsa nidda*]** and the wedding had to be postponed, he similarly need **not provide her sustenance** from the designated time, as he did not initiate the delay.

The *gemara* now draws a broader principle from this framework. Having established that when it is a *takanat chachamim* that prevents the wedding from occurring on an earlier day, the groom bears no *mezonot* obligation for those days — the *gemara* asks whether the same reasoning extends to other situations where the wedding is postponed through no one's fault. The underlying question is: does the obligation to pay *mezonot* commence automatically on the wedding date, regardless of why it was not observed, or does it only engage when there is someone to blame for the delay?

### Groom Illness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּבָעֵי לַהּ מִיבַּעְיָא: חָלָה הוּא, מַהוּ? הָתָם טַעְמָא מַאי — מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲנִיס, וְהָכָא נָמֵי הָא אֲנִיס. אוֹ דִּלְמָא: הָתָם אֲנִיס בְּתַקַּנְתָּא דְּתַקִּינוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן, הָכָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who raised it as a dilemma: If** the groom **fell ill, what is** the ruling with regard to his obligation to provide her sustenance? **There,** in the case where the designated time arrived on Sunday, **what is the reason** that he is exempt? It is **due to** the fact **that he was forced** to postpone the wedding by circumstances beyond his control. **And here too, isn't he forced** by circumstances beyond his control, and he should therefore be exempt? **Or, perhaps** there is a distinction between the cases. **There, he was forced by an ordinance that was instituted by the Sages** that a virgin marries on Wednesday, and they exempted him from providing for her until then. **However, here,** that is **not** the case. Therefore, despite the existence of circumstances beyond his control, he is not exempt from providing her sustenance due to his illness.

*Lefikach chala hu* — therefore, if the groom became ill and the wedding was postponed, does he pay *mezonot* from the appointed date or not? One might think so, since a year has passed.

One view within the *gemara* frames it as a question of *ones*: *hatam ta'ama mai mishum d'ones* — the reason he did not owe for the Sunday-to-Wednesday period was that it was beyond his control, a product of the rabbinic institution. *Hacha nami ones* — here too it is beyond his control; therefore he should not pay. Alternatively: *hatam ones b'takantah d'takinu leh rabanan* — there, the reason it was beyond his control was built into the rabbinic institution itself, such that the date was never Sunday to begin with. Here, by contrast, the date actually arrived — it was Wednesday, and the wedding did not happen. Perhaps once the actual date arrives, the obligation begins regardless of why it was not fulfilled.

The distinction is sharp. When the rabbis say Wednesday, the pre-Wednesday period was never the wedding date. But when illness prevents a Wednesday wedding, the Wednesday date did arrive and was missed. These are halakhically different situations, and the question of whether *ones* constitutes an exemption in the latter case remains open.

### Bride Illness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר חָלָה הוּא מַעֲלֶה לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת: חָלְתָה הִיא, מַהוּ? מָצֵי אָמַר לַהּ: אֲנָא הָא קָאֵימְנָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא מָצְיָא אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: ״נִסְתַּחֲפָה שָׂדֵהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say that if he fell ill, he provides her sustenance** at the time designated for the wedding, then **if she fell ill, what is** the ruling? **Can he say to her: I am standing here** prepared to wed you, and if you are not prepared, it is not my fault? **Or perhaps she can say to him that his field was inundated,** i.e., her illness is tantamount to a natural disaster that befell him, and he must nevertheless provide for her.

The uncertainty cascades further. *V'im timtzi lomar chala hu ma'aleh lah mezonot* — if you rule that when the groom falls ill he does pay *mezonot*, then *chaltsah hi mah* — what if she is the one who falls ill? Here the groom might argue: *ana kaina* — I am ready to marry; it is you who cannot proceed. Should he then be exempt from payment since the delay originates with her? Or does she respond: *nistachfah sadehu* — it is your field that flooded? The underlying logic of this latter phrase, as *Tosafot* on 2b explains, reflects a view that within the patriarchal framework of the marriage relationship, misfortune that befalls the marriage is seen as the husband's misfortune — he is the one bringing a wife into his domain, and if that is disrupted, the loss is categorized as his. Rashi on 2a suggests a parallel formulation: *mazlacha garam ki mehayom ani mutal alecha lazon* — your fortune has brought it about that from this day I am your responsibility to sustain. One might read this either as the wife's argument that luck cuts against him, or more simply as the position that once the date arrives, the obligation of support begins regardless of fault — as long as the postponement was not willful.

### Niddah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר אָמְרָה לֵיהּ ״נִסְתַּחֲפָה שָׂדֵהוּ״ — פֵּירְסָה נִדָּה, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say that** if she fell ill **she** can **say to him that his field was inundated,** if **she became a menstruating woman** and the wedding was postponed, **what is** the ruling? Is this too considered an unavoidable circumstance, like illness, and he must provide for her, or was this predictable?

A third scenario: *pirsah niddah* — what if the bride becomes a *niddah* on the wedding day? There are well-known differences between *Sephardim* and *Ashkenazim* as to whether a *chuppah* is legally valid when the bride is a *niddah*, since cohabitation would not be possible immediately. But regardless of the legal question, in practice no one wished to hold a *chuppah* when the couple could not begin their married life together. The question is therefore whether the *niddah* situation creates an exemption from *mezonot* for the groom.

### Rav Achai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּשֵׁיט רַב אַחַאי: הִגִּיעַ זְמַן וְלֹא נִישְּׂאוּ — אוֹכְלוֹת מִשֶּׁלּוֹ וְאוֹכְלוֹת בִּתְרוּמָה. ״לֹא נָשְׂאוּ״ לָא קָתָנֵי, אֶלָּא ״לֹא נִישְּׂאוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥai resolved** these dilemmas through a careful reading of the mishna: **If the time arrived and they were not married,** the brides are entitled to **eat from his** food **and eat *teruma*. It doesn't teach: And** the grooms **didn't marry,** in the active form. **Rather,** it teaches: **And** the brides **were not married,** in the passive form.

Rav Achai attempts to resolve these open questions from the language of the later *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* reads *higia zman v'lo nisu* — the time came and they were not married. Critically, the verb is *nistu*, passive: the women were not married by the men — not *nasu*, active, meaning the men did not marry. Rav Achai infers from this: *lo nasu lo katani, ella lo nistu* — it does not say the men did not marry; it says the women were not married. This implies that the women were somehow involved in the reason for the non-marriage — yet the *mishnah* still rules that he pays. Were it a case of the women willfully refusing — *d'kamakvan inu hi* — it would be obvious he should not pay. So it must be speaking of a case where the women were the involuntary cause of the delay — illness, or perhaps *niddah* — and yet the *mishnah* rules he pays. Rav Achai concludes that whenever the delay is involuntary on the woman's side, the man pays.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי: אִי דְּקָא מְעַכְּבָן אִינְהִי — אַמַּאי אוֹכְלוֹת מִשֶּׁלּוֹ וְאוֹכְלוֹת בִּתְרוּמָה? אֶלָּא לָאו, דְּאִיתְּנִיס כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא, וְקָתָנֵי: אוֹכְלוֹת מִשֶּׁלּוֹ וְאוֹכְלוֹת בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** described in the phrase: They were not married? **If the** brides **postpone** the marriage, **why do they eat from his** food **and eat *teruma*? Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where they were compelled** by circumstances beyond their control **in that manner,** e.g., the bride falling ill or beginning to menstruate, **and it teaches:** The brides **eat from his** food **and eat *teruma*,** and his bad fortune is responsible for his situation.

### Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ כׇּל אוּנְסָא, לָא אָכְלָה, וּדְקָא מְעַכְּבִי אִינְהוּ. וּבְדִין הוּא דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמִיתְנֵי ״לֹא נָשְׂאוּ״, וְאַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא בְּדִידְהִי, תְּנָא נָמֵי סֵיפָא בְּדִידְהִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Actually, I will say to you: In any** case **of unavoidable circumstances,** whether they affect him or they affect her, **she doesn't eat** from his food, **and** the mishna is referring to a case **where** grooms **postpone the wedding. And by right,** the *tanna* **should have taught: They didn't marry,** which would have established that grooms caused the postponement. **And since** the *tanna* taught **the first clause** of the mishna cited above **in** terms of a bride: The Sages give a virgin twelve months, **it taught the latter clause in** terms of a bride. Therefore, no inference may be drawn from the formulation of the latter clause. Only if the groom postpones the wedding is he obligated to provide sustenance for the women when the time designated for the wedding arrives.

Rav Ashi rejects this reading. *L'olam eima lach, kol ones lo achla* — any situation of *ones*, anything beyond anyone's control, exempts the man from payment. The man pays only when he willfully delays. *V'd'kamaachvi inhu* — the triggering case for payment is the man deliberately postponing the wedding. Grammatically, Rav Ashi notes, the *mishnah* should indeed have said *lo nasu* — the men did not marry; it uses the passive form only because the beginning of that *mishnah* speaks from the woman's perspective, and the end follows suit stylistically. It is not a substantive implication.

The *gemara* thus leaves us with two fundamental positions. One holds that the obligation begins automatically on the wedding date — as long as the woman is not herself willfully postponing, the man must pay from that date regardless of circumstances. The other holds that the man pays only when he is personally and willfully responsible for the delay; any genuine *ones*, on either side, exempts him from payment.

## Prenup Digression

### Historical Parallel

*Tosafot* raises a question that illuminates a dimension of this entire discussion: why did the rabbis stipulate that the *chuppah* must be held on Wednesday specifically during the day, rather than on Wednesday night — which is halakhically already Thursday? *Tosafot* answers that in Talmudic times there were frequently significant disputes surrounding the terms of the *ketubah*, and beginning the *chuppah* early on Wednesday allowed time for those disputes to be negotiated and resolved before the event proceeded. When the *ketubah* was a genuine financial document with real stakes — the specification of dowry property, the exact amount of the *ikar ketubah*, the various conditions — there was ample room for disagreement between the families, and the rabbis wished to ensure that such disputes did not derail the wedding at the last moment.

### Prenup Anecdote

This principle resonates powerfully in contemporary halakhic practice. A prenuptial agreement is today's functional analogue to those *ketubah* negotiations. The dangers of leaving such discussions to the last moment are very real. There are cases where a groom, coming from a family that had experienced a particularly acrimonious divorce, arrived at his own wedding refusing to sign the prenuptial agreement — raising objections that could and should have been addressed weeks earlier.

### Prenup Practice

The practical lesson is clear: a prenuptial agreement should be signed and notarized no later than two weeks before the wedding. The signing can certainly be acknowledged and celebrated at the *tish* if one wishes, but the document must not be left to the night of the wedding itself. Just as the Talmudic rabbis ensured that disputes over the *ketubah* would not disrupt the *chuppah* by building in lead time, so too contemporary practice should ensure that all such documents are fully in order well before the event.

## Ones in Gittin

### Ein Ones Gittin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּלְעִנְיַן גִּיטִּין אֵינוֹ כֵּן. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר רָבָא אֵין אוֹנֶס בְּגִיטִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And** although delays caused by circumstances beyond his control exempt the groom from providing support to his betrothed at the time originally designated for the wedding, with regard **to bills of divorce that is not so. Apparently, Rava maintains that unavoidable circumstances** have **no** legal standing with regard **to bills of divorce.** If one stipulated that the bill of divorce will take effect only with the fulfillment of a condition, even if that condition was fulfilled due to circumstances beyond his control, the bill of divorce takes effect.

Rava now introduces a striking contrast. *Ul'inyan gittin eino kein* — regarding *gittin*, this principle of *ones* does not apply in the same way. *Alma kasavar Rava ein ones b'gittin* — Rava holds that the fact that something occurred beyond one's control is not a relevant factor in the laws of *gittin*. Where we have just seen that *ones* can be a factor in the *mezonot* discussion — according to Rav Ashi's position, any *ones* exempts the man from payment — in the realm of *gittin*, *ones* carries no weight.

### Conditional Gittin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לֵיהּ לְרָבָא הָא? אִילֵּימָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטֵּיךְ אִם לֹא בָּאתִי מִכָּאן וְעַד שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ״, וּמֵת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ — אֵינוֹ גֵּט. מֵת הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ גֵּט, הָא חָלָה — הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where does Rava** learn **this** principle? **If we say it is from that which we learned** in a mishna (*Gittin* 76b) with regard to one who said to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce, if I do not return from now until** the conclusion of **twelve months, and he died within** those **twelve months,** the document **is not a bill of divorce.** Therefore, if she has no children from her deceased husband, the *halakhot* of levirate marriage would apply to her. The Gemara infers: If **he died,** that **is when it is not a bill of divorce,** since a divorce cannot take effect posthumously. By inference, in cases involving other circumstances beyond his control, e.g., if **he fell ill,** and therefore did not return, **it is a bill of divorce** and it takes effect. Apparently, if the reason for his failure to arrive is a circumstance beyond his control, the divorce takes effect.

The source for Rava's principle is sought in a *mishnah* in *Gittin*: a man says to his wife, *harei zeh gittech im lo basi mikan ad shneim asar chodesh* — here is your *get*; if I do not return from here within twelve months, the *get* takes effect. The scenario is familiar: the man is traveling overseas, perhaps to war or distant business, and fears he may not return. To protect his wife from becoming an *agunah* — permanently bound and unable to remarry — he gives a conditional *get*. *U'meis betoch shneim asar chodesh* — and he died within those twelve months.

At the end of twelve months, the condition appears to have been met: he did not return. Is this a valid *get*?

### Ein Get Achar Misah

The *mishnah* rules: *eino get* — it is not a valid *get*. The reason Rashi articulates is *ein hameisim megarshin* — the dead cannot divorce; a *get* cannot take effect *l'achar misah*, after death. The *kiddushin* has in any event been dissolved by death, and one might ask why Rashi finds it necessary to invoke this principle rather than simply noting that widowhood has already dissolved the marriage. The *yeshiva* world enjoys posing this question, though in practice the principle of *ein get l'achar misah* stands on its own terms. The condition was technically met — he did not return — but the *get* fails because one cannot divorce a wife posthumously.

### Ones Factor?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ חָלָה נָמֵי אֵינוֹ גֵּט, וְהִיא גּוּפָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּאֵין גֵּט לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes that proof. **And perhaps, actually I will say to you** that in the case where **he falls ill it is also not a bill of divorce,** and the mishna cited the case of death merely as an example of circumstances beyond his control. **And** the reason **that** example **itself** was chosen is **to teach us that there is no bill of divorce posthumously.** Even if the divorce is not conditional, and the husband simply states that it will take effect after he dies, it is not a valid bill of divorce.

The *gemara* now probes a deeper question. *Meiso de'eino get* — death prevents the *get*; that much is clear. But what if *chala* — what if he fell ill and was unable to return? In that case, the twelve months would also have passed without his return. Does the fact that his failure to return was due to *ones* — circumstances entirely beyond his control — mean that the condition is treated as if it were not met? That is: perhaps when he was prevented by force of circumstance, it is halakhically "as if he returned," and therefore the condition was not fulfilled and the *get* is not valid. Or is *ones* entirely irrelevant — the condition was not met regardless of why, and the *get* is therefore valid?

If *ones* is not a factor, then the *mishnah* teaches us only that death prevents the *get* via the principle of *ein get l'achar misah* — and nothing can be inferred about what would happen if he merely fell ill. The *get* in the illness case might well be valid. If *ones* is a factor, then even in the illness case the condition would be treated as unmet, and the *get* would be invalid — but then the *mishnah* about death is simply one application of the broader rule that *ones* negates condition-fulfillment.

### Social Policy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין גֵּט לְאַחַר מִיתָה — הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטֵּיךְ אִם מַתִּי״, ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטֵּיךְ מֵחוֹלִי זֶה״, ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטֵּיךְ לְאַחַר מִיתָה״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does it come to teach that **there is no bill of divorce posthumously? Wasn't it** already **taught in the first clause** of the mishna (*Gittin* 72a) that if one on his deathbed said to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce if I die,** or: **This is your bill of divorce** if I die **from this illness,** or: **This is your bill of divorce after I die, he said nothing.** The bill of divorce does not take effect after his death.

The *gemara* anticipates where this leads. If *ones* were a relevant factor in determining whether a condition was met, the consequences for practical halakha would be severe: we could never know with confidence whether conditions in *gittin* had been fulfilled or not, because we would always need to investigate whether any circumstance beyond the party's control had intervened. Such a regime would be unworkable. This social policy concern — that one cannot run a halakhic system if conditions can always be retroactively analyzed for possible *ones* — is precisely what Rava's principle is designed to prevent.

### Gemarah Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבּוֹתֵינוּ. דְּתַנְיָא: וְרַבּוֹתֵינוּ הִתִּירוּהָ לְהִנָּשֵׂא, וְאָמְרִינַן: מַאן רַבּוֹתֵינוּ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בֵּי דִינָא דִּשְׁרוֹ מִשְׁחָא. סָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: זְמַנּוֹ שֶׁל שְׁטָר מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Perhaps** it was necessary for the first clause to mention specifically the case of death, **to exclude that which our Rabbis** said and not to exclude the case of one who fell ill, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **And our Rabbis permitted her to remarry,** in a case where he died within the twelve months that he stipulated. **And we said: Who are our Rabbis** mentioned here? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** It is **the court that permitted** the **oil** of gentiles for consumption. In this regard, **they hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said: The time** written **in a document proves** when **it** takes effect. The fact that a certain date is written in the bill of divorce indicates that one's intention was that the divorce take effect from the day that it was written and delivered, not after his death. In any event, there is proof neither for nor against Rava's opinion from this *baraita*.

The *gemara*'s conclusion is that the *mishnah* teaches one thing and one thing only: a *get* does not take effect after the groom's death, by virtue of the principle of *ein get l'achar misah*. The *mishnah* is not making any statement about the illness case, and nothing can be inferred from it regarding whether *ones* would affect the determination of condition-fulfillment. That question — whether *ones* serves to treat a condition as unmet — remains a separate discussion, and the resolution of Rava's principle *ein ones b'gittin* must be derived from elsewhere.
