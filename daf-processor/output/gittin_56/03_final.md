# Gittin 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Edicts & Sicricon

### Sicricon Existence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא בַּהֲרוּגֵי הַמִּלְחָמָה לֹא הָיָה בָּהּ סִיקָרִיקוֹן, מֵהֲרוּגֵי מִלְחָמָה וְאֵילָךְ, יֵשׁ בָּהּ סִיקָרִיקוֹן?!
>
> **Translation:** Now if **in** the time that **people were being killed in the war, there were no Sicarii** stealing land, is it possible that **from** the time that **people were being killed in the war and onward there were Sicarii?**

The daf begins at the bottom of 55b, where the *Gemara* picks up a discussion emerging from the *mishnah*'s treatment of the *sicricon* — a type of assassin or violent extorter — and the question of when the legal framework governing property seized by such figures applies. The *Gemara* poses a pointed question: *hashta b'harugei milchama lo haya sicricon, me'harugei milchama v'ilech yesh sicricon?* If, during the very height of the killing during the destruction, the law of *sicricon* did not apply, how much more so should it not apply afterward? How is it possible that the phenomenon didn't generate legal consequences during the wars but did afterward?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״לֹא דָּנוּ בָּהּ דִּין סִיקָרִיקוֹן״ קָאָמַר. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: שָׁלֹשׁ גְּזֵירוֹת גָּזְרוּ. גְּזֵרְתָּא קַמַּיְיתָא: כֹּל דְּלָא קָטֵיל – לִיקְטְלוּהּ. מְצִיעֲתָא: כֹּל דְּקָטֵיל – לַיְיתֵי אַרְבַּע זוּזֵי. בָּתְרָיְיתָא: כֹּל דְּקָטֵיל – לִיקְטְלוּהּ. הִלְכָּךְ, קַמַּיְיתָא וּמְצִיעֲתָא, כֵּיוָן דְּקָטְלִי – אַגַּב אוּנְסֵיהּ גָּמַר וּמַקְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** The mishna **is saying** that in the time that people were being killed in the war **they did not apply the law of Sicarii,** but rather they would confirm the purchases of land made from the Sicarii. The reason for this is in accordance with what **Rabbi Asi said:** The gentile authorities **issued three decrees** during and in the aftermath of the war that ended in the destruction of the Temple. The **first decree** was that **anyone who does not kill** a Jew **should** himself **be killed.** The **second** decree was that **anyone who kills** a Jew **should pay four dinars** as a fine. The **last** decree was that **anyone who kills** a Jew **should** himself **be killed. Therefore,** during the time of the **first and second** decrees, the time when people were being killed in the war, **since** the gentile **would kill** Jews, then the owner of the field, **owing to the danger** posed to his life, **would fully transfer ownership** of his field to the Sicarius.

Rav Yehuda resolves this apparent difficulty: the *mishnah* does not mean to say the *sicricon* himself didn't exist during the wars. Of course violent extorters were operating then. Rather, it means that the specific *din* — the legal doctrine — governing transactions involving *sicricon*-seized land did not apply.

### Rav Assi's Edicts

The reason for this is explained by Rav Assi, who teaches that there were three consecutive edicts — *gimmel gezeiros* — during the period of the *Churban HaBayis*. The first edict, *gezeira kamaisa*, was *kol d'lo katil liktlu'u*: any soldier who refused to kill a Jew would himself be killed. The second edict, *metzi'asa*, was *kol d'katil leisi arba zuzei*: killing a Jew would earn a reward of four *zuz*, but there was no longer a death penalty for failing to do so. The third edict, *basraisa*, reversed course entirely: *kol d'katil liktlu'u*, killing a Jew was once again punishable by death, as the Roman authorities sought to restore order following the wars.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתְרָיְיתָא אָמְרִי: הָאִידָּנָא לִישְׁקוֹל, לִמְחַר תָּבַעְנָא לֵיהּ בְּדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Then, during the time of **the last** decree, after the time when people were being killed in the war, anybody whose field was stolen by a Sicarius would **say** to himself: **Now let him take** the field; **tomorrow I will claim it from him in court.** Although the gentile had the advantage and could force the owner to give him the field, the assumption is that the owner did not fully transfer possession of the field to him, as he thought that he would still be able to recover it in court.

### Sale Doctrine

The distinction between these three stages has direct *halachic* consequences. During the first and second edicts, when a *sicricon* threatened a Jew with death and demanded his land, the threat was entirely credible — the extorter either was obligated or at least rewarded for killing. Consequently, *keivan d'katlei, agav onseh gamur makni*: since the victim knew the threat was real, he genuinely and fully intended to transfer the land in exchange for his life. The sale was a complete *kinyan*. A third party who subsequently purchased that land from the *sicricon* acquired full ownership and need not return to the original owner to re-validate the transaction.

### Third Edict Rights

The third edict presents an entirely different legal picture. Under this edict, the *sicricon* was prohibited from killing the Jew. When the *sicricon* nonetheless demanded the land under threat, the owner reasoned: *idna lishkol, u'lemachar tavna lei b'dina* — let him take it today, and tomorrow I will pursue him in court. The owner never fully consented to the transfer; he merely yielded temporarily, expecting eventual legal redress. A third party who purchases such land from the *sicricon* therefore does not acquire good title. He must also go back and purchase it from the original owner in order for the transaction to be halachically sound.

### Narrative Setup

Notably, the *Gemara* does not linger on the further *halachic* details of the *mishnah* — the stages of *takkanah* governing whether a third party must pay the original owner a quarter of the land's value, and the proper order of purchase. Instead, it uses this brief legal excursus as a backdrop for the transition into the great *aggadata* of the *Churban HaBayis*, having established the violent historical context of soldiers killing Jews and the legal frameworks that emerged from it.

## Fear & Destruction

### Rabbi Yochanan's Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אַשְׁרֵי אָדָם מְפַחֵד תָּמִיד וּמַקְשֶׁה לִבּוֹ יִפּוֹל בְּרָעָה״? אַקַּמְצָא וּבַר קַמְצָא חֲרוּב יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, אַתַּרְנְגוֹלָא וְתַרְנְגוֹלְתָּא חֲרוּב טוּר מַלְכָּא, אַשָּׁקָא דְרִיסְפַּק חֲרוּב בֵּיתֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Happy is the man who fears always, but he who hardens his heart shall fall into mischief"** (Proverbs 28:14)? **Jerusalem was destroyed on account of Kamtza and bar Kamtza.** The place known as **the King's Mountain was destroyed on account of a rooster and a hen.** The city of **Beitar was destroyed on account of a shaft from a chariot [*rispak*].**

The *Gemara* opens its *aggadic* account of the destruction with a verse from Rabbi Yochanan: *ashrei adam m'fached tamid, u-maksheh libo yipol b'ra-ah* — praised is the person who is constantly in a state of fear, while the one who hardens his heart will fall into evil. This verse serves as the thematic key to everything that follows. The *m'fached tamid* is not mere anxiety; it is the posture of a person who is genuinely concerned about his actions, alive to the larger picture, aware of the possible ramifications of how he conducts himself. The *maksheh libo*, the one with the hardened heart, is the person moving through the world with blinders on, unable to see beyond the immediate moment.

### Causes of Destruction

Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* names three events that brought about three destructions: *a-Kamtza u-var Kamtza charuv Yerushalayim* — Jerusalem was destroyed on account of Kamtza and Bar Kamtza. *A-tarnagola v'tarnagolta charuv Tur Malka* — the city of Tur Malka was destroyed because of a hen and a rooster. *A-shaka d'rispak charuv Beitar* — Beitar was destroyed because of the axle of a carriage.

The stories to follow will be primarily concerned with Jerusalem, and the connection to the verse is already implicit. The destruction came not only through the hardness of heart of those who acted — the host who expelled his guest, the zealots who refused to make peace — but also through those who lacked the fear, the *yir'ah*, to stand up and intervene. Both forms of failure — active cruelty and passive acquiescence — find their indictment in this verse.

It is also worth noting the etymology of the name *Kamtza* itself. A *kometz* refers to a closed fist — the opposite of open-handed generosity. The very name telegraphs the theme of withholding, of miserly refusal to extend grace.

## Kamtza & Bar Kamtza

### Feast & Mistake

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַקַּמְצָא וּבַר קַמְצָא חֲרוּב יְרוּשָׁלַיִם – דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּרָחֲמֵיהּ קַמְצָא, וּבְעֵל דְּבָבֵיהּ בַּר קַמְצָא. עֲבַד סְעוֹדְתָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי לִי קַמְצָא. אֲזַל אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ בַּר קַמְצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Jerusalem was destroyed on account of Kamtza and bar Kamtza.** This is **as** there was **a certain man whose friend** was named **Kamtza and whose enemy** was named **bar Kamtza. He** once **made** a large **feast** and **said to his servant: Go bring me** my friend **Kamtza.** The servant **went** and mistakenly **brought him** his enemy **bar Kamtza.**

The story opens: *d'hahu gavra d'rachmei Kamtza u-va'al devaveih Bar Kamtza* — there was a man whose close friend was named Kamtza and whose enemy was named Bar Kamtza. He made a great feast and dispatched his attendant: *zil aysei li Kamtza* — go and invite Kamtza for me. The attendant went and by mistake invited Bar Kamtza. The host discovered his enemy sitting at his table.

### Bar Kamtza Pleas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה יָתֵיב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִכְּדֵי הָהוּא גַּבְרָא בְּעֵל דְּבָבֵאּ דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא הוּא, מַאי בָּעֵית הָכָא? קוּם פּוֹק! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוֹאִיל וַאֲתַאי, שִׁבְקַן וְיָהֵיבְנָא לָךְ דְּמֵי מָה דְּאָכֵילְנָא וְשָׁתֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The man who was hosting the feast **came and found** bar Kamtza **sitting** at the feast. The host **said to** bar Kamtza. **That man is the enemy [*ba'al devava*] of that man,** that is, you are my enemy. **What** then **do you want here? Arise** and **leave.** Bar Kamtza **said to him: Since I have** already **come, let me stay and I will give you money** for **whatever I eat and drink.** Just do not embarrass me by sending me out.

The host confronted him directly: *mai ba-yis hacha* — what are you doing here? Get out. Bar Kamtza, acutely aware of the public shame that expulsion would bring him, pleaded: *ho-il v'asai shavkan* — since I am already here, simply let me stay. I will pay for whatever I eat and drink. The host refused. Bar Kamtza escalated his offer: I will pay for half the feast. Refused again. He offered to pay for the entire feast. *Amar lei lo* — still no.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יָהֵיבְנָא לָךְ דְּמֵי פַּלְגָא דִּסְעוֹדְתָּיךְ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יָהֵיבְנָא לָךְ דְּמֵי כּוּלַּהּ סְעוֹדְתָּיךְ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא. נַקְטֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ וְאוֹקְמֵיהּ וְאַפְּקֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The host **said to him: No,** you must leave. Bar Kamtza **said to him: I will give you money for half of the feast;** just do not send me away. The host **said to him: No,** you must leave. Bar Kamtza then **said to him: I will give you money for the entire feast;** just let me stay. The host **said to him: No,** you must leave. Finally, the host **took** bar Kamtza **by his hand, stood him up, and took him out.**

*Naktye b'yadeih v'okmeih v'afkeih* — the host took him by the hand, stood him up, and drove him out. No offer of money, not even covering the full cost of the banquet, could overcome this man's commitment to his hatred. There is something almost parabolic here about the siege of Jerusalem itself — people so entrenched in their enmity that they cannot shift course even when an obvious path of accommodation presents itself.

### Public Expulsion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: הוֹאִיל וַהֲווֹ יָתְבִי רַבָּנַן וְלָא מַחוֹ בֵּיהּ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קָא נִיחָא לְהוּ, אֵיזִיל אֵיכוֹל בְּהוּ קוּרְצָא בֵּי מַלְכָּא. אֲזַל אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְקֵיסָר: מְרַדוּ בָּךְ יְהוּדָאֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי יֵימַר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁדַּר לְהוּ קוּרְבָּנָא, חָזֵית אִי מַקְרְבִין לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** After having been cast out from the feast, bar Kamtza **said** to himself: **Since the Sages were sitting** there **and did not protest** the actions of the host, although they saw how he humiliated me, **learn from it that they were content** with what he did. **I will** therefore **go and inform [*eikhul kurtza*] against them to the king. He went** and **said to the emperor: The Jews have rebelled against you.** The emperor **said to him: Who says** that this is the case? Bar Kamtza **said to him:** Go and test them; **send them an offering** to be brought in honor of the government, and **see whether they** will **sacrifice it.**

Bar Kamtza drew a conclusion that would have devastating consequences: *ho-il vehavu yatvi rabbanan velo machu bei, shema mina kanicha lehu* — since the rabbis were sitting there and did not protest his expulsion, evidently they found it acceptable. This raises one of the central themes of the entire passage. The rabbis bear responsibility not through what they did but through what they failed to do. The principle that evil flourishes when good people stand by in silence is directly implicated here. The rabbis' passivity is not a minor failing; the *Gemara* will return to it with force. The question is not only about the host who was cruel, or the zealots who forced the catastrophe — it is also about the leaders who could have intervened at multiple junctures and did not.

### Caesar & Sacrifice

Bar Kamtza decided to act: *izil eichol behu kurtza bei malka* — he went and spread a malicious report to Caesar, informing him that the Jews were in rebellion. Caesar was skeptical: *mi yeimar* — who says so? Bar Kamtza proposed a test: send them a sacrifice and see whether they offer it up.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל שַׁדַּר בִּידֵיהּ עִגְלָא תִּלְתָּא. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאָתֵי שְׁדָא בֵּיהּ מוּמָא בְּנִיב שְׂפָתַיִם, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּדוּקִּין שֶׁבָּעַיִן – דּוּכְתָּא דִּלְדִידַן הָוֵה מוּמָא, וּלְדִידְהוּ לָאו מוּמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The emperor **went and sent with him** a choice **three-year-old calf. While** bar Kamtza **was coming** with the calf to the Temple, **he made a blemish on** the calf's **upper lip. And some say** he made the blemish **on** its **eyelids, a place where according to us,** i.e., *halakha*, it **is a blemish, but according to them,** gentile rules for their offerings, it **is not a blemish.** Therefore, when bar Kamtza brought the animal to the Temple, the priests would not sacrifice it on the altar since it was blemished, but they also could not explain this satisfactorily to the gentile authorities, who did not consider it to be blemished.

Caesar sent a fine calf — *egla tilta*, a well-fattened animal. On the way to Jerusalem, Bar Kamtza introduced a blemish: *be-niv sefatayim* — in the upper lip, or according to another opinion, *be-dukin she-ba-ayin* — a tiny film over the eye. The halachic asymmetry was precise: *duchta de-ledidan havei muma, u-ledidehu lav muma hu* — by Jewish law this constituted a disqualifying blemish for a Temple offering, but by Roman standards it was not considered a blemish at all. The Romans would have no idea why the Jews refused to sacrifice it, and would take it as confirmation of rebellion.

The detail of the blemish on the lip — *niv sefatayim*, which also connotes speech — is not incidental. The power of speech, for both harm and good, runs as a thread through these stories.

## Sacrifice Debate

### Peace Consideration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבוּר רַבָּנַן לְקָרוֹבֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם שְׁלוֹם מַלְכוּת. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן אַבְקוּלָס: יֹאמְרוּ בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין קְרֵיבִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ! סְבוּר לְמִיקְטְלֵיהּ דְּלָא לֵיזִיל וְלֵימָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי זְכַרְיָה: יֹאמְרוּ מֵטִיל מוּם בַּקֳּדָשִׁים יֵהָרֵג!
>
> **Translation:** The blemish notwithstanding, **the Sages thought to sacrifice** the animal as an offering **due to** the imperative to maintain **peace** with the **government. Rabbi Zekharya ben Avkolas said to them:** If the priests do that, people **will say** that **blemished** animals **may be sacrificed** as offerings **on the altar.** The Sages said: If we do not sacrifice it, then we must prevent bar Kamtza from reporting this to the emperor. The Sages **thought to kill him so that he would not go and speak** against them. **Rabbi Zekharya said to them:** If you kill him, people **will say** that **one who makes a blemish on sacrificial** animals **is to be killed.** As a result, they did nothing, bar Kamtza's slander was accepted by the authorities, and consequently the war between the Jews and the Romans began.

When the blemished animal arrived, the rabbis found themselves confronted with a fateful choice. *Savar rabbanan le-kruvei mishum shelom malchut* — the rabbis considered offering the blemished animal for the sake of peace with the government. This reflects an important posture: the willingness to bend halachic norms for the sake of a larger national interest. The *Gemara* here implicitly endorses this flexibility — the wiser course, as will become clear, was to offer it.

### Zechariah's Refusal

Rabbi Zechariah ben Avkulos objected: *yomru ba'alei mumin kreivin legabi mizbeach* — people will say that it is permissible to bring a blemished animal to the altar. He could not look past the halachic precedent that might be set.

### Kill Bar Kamtza Plan

The rabbis then proposed an alternative: since they cannot offer the sacrifice and risk a diplomatic rupture, at least let them kill Bar Kamtza so he cannot carry back a negative report to Rome. Rabbi Zechariah objected to this as well: *yomru matil mum be-kodashim yehareg* — people will conclude that the punishment for blemishing a sacrifice is death.

### Yochanan Blames Zechariah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עִנְוְותָנוּתוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן אַבְקוּלָס, הֶחְרִיבָה אֶת בֵּיתֵנוּ, וְשָׂרְפָה אֶת הֵיכָלֵנוּ, וְהִגְלְתָנוּ מֵאַרְצֵנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The** excessive **humility of Rabbi Zekharya ben Avkolas destroyed our Temple, burned our Sanctuary, and exiled us from our land.**

Rabbi Yochanan's verdict is famous and severe: *anvetanuto shel Rabbi Zecharia ben Avkulos hechriva et beiteinu ve-sarfa et heichaleinu ve-higlitnu me-artzeinu* — the humility of Rabbi Zechariah ben Avkulos destroyed our Temple, burned our sanctuary, and exiled us from our land.

The choice of the word *anvetanuto* — humility — is striking and deliberate. This was not simple obstinacy or stubbornness. Rabbi Zechariah's position can be framed as a kind of piety: who are we to override the *halacha*? Who are we to take such drastic action? His posture was the humble one, the *frum* one, the one that deferred to established norms rather than arrogating to himself the authority to deviate from them. And yet this very humility — this refusal to exercise rabbinic initiative and flexibility — was catastrophic.

But the criticism cannot be aimed at Rabbi Zechariah alone. The other rabbis were present and did not stand up to him. Their own passivity, their unwillingness to confront his position and override it, is equally implicated. This mirrors the passivity at the feast: again, the rabbis stand by. If Rabbi Zechariah's *anavah* destroyed the Temple, then the rabbis' reluctance to challenge him was its own form of failure. This forms a pointed contrast with the material on the preceding pages of the *masechta*, where the Rabbis exercised sweeping authority — employing *hefker beis din hefker*, overriding what would normally be the rules governing *korbanos*, redefining personal status — all for the sake of *tikkun olam*. The question the *Gemara* seems to be raising is: where was that boldness when it was needed most?

## Nero's Omens

### Arrow Divination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁדַּר עִלָּוַיְיהוּ לְנֵירוֹן קֵיסָר. כִּי קָאָתֵי; שְׁדָא גִּירָא לְמִזְרָח – אֲתָא נְפַל בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. לְמַעֲרָב – אֲתָא נְפַל בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. לְאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹת הַשָּׁמַיִם – אֲתָא נְפַל בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Roman authorities then **sent Nero Caesar against** the Jews. **When he came** to Jerusalem, he wished to test his fate. **He shot an arrow to the east** and the arrow **came** and **fell in Jerusalem.** He then shot another arrow **to the west** and **it** also **fell in Jerusalem.** He shot an arrow **in** all **four directions of the heavens,** and each time the arrow **fell in Jerusalem.**

After the catastrophe was set in motion, *shadar alaihu le'Neron Keisar* — they dispatched Nero Caesar against Jerusalem. As he drew near, he engaged in a form of divination by shooting arrows. He shot to the east — *asa nefal bi'Yerushalayim*, the arrow fell toward Jerusalem. To the west — toward Jerusalem. *Le'arba ruchos ha'shamayim* — in all four directions — the arrow fell toward Jerusalem. The message was unambiguous: the divine will was pointing him to this city.

### Child's Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְיָנוֹקָא: פְּסוֹק לִי פְּסוּקָיךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וְנָתַתִּי אֶת נִקְמָתִי בֶּאֱדוֹם בְּיַד עַמִּי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר: קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא בָּעֵי לַחֲרוֹבֵי בֵּיתֵיהּ, וּבָעֵי לְכַפּוֹרֵי יְדֵיהּ בְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא. עֲרַק וַאֲזַל וְאִיגַּיּוֹרֵי, וּנְפַק מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** Nero then conducted another test: **He said to a child: Tell me a verse** that you learned today. **He said to him** as follows: **"And I will lay My vengeance upon Edom by the hand of My people Israel"** (Ezekiel 25:14). Nero **said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, wishes to destroy His Temple, and He wishes to wipe his hands with that man,** i.e., with me. The Romans are associated with Edom, the descendants of Esau. If I continue on this mission, I will eventually be punished for having served as God's agent to bring about the destruction. So **he fled** and **became a convert, and** ultimately **Rabbi Meir descended from him.**

Nero then asked a child to recite whatever *pasuk* he happened to be studying at that moment. The child recited: *ve'nasati es nikmasi be'Edom, be'yad ami Yisrael* — I will take my vengeance against Edom through my people Israel. For the rabbis, Edom was the code for Rome.

### Nero Converts

Nero drew his own conclusion: *kudsha Berich Hu ba'ei le'charuvi beiseih* — God wants His Temple destroyed, *u-ba'ei le-kichuyei yadeih be-hu gavra* — and He intends to wipe His hands on me. God will use me to do His dirty work, and then I will bear the blame. This was not something Nero was willing to accept. *Arak va-azil ve-igaier* — he fled and converted to Judaism.

### Rabbi Meir Lineage

The *Gemara* adds a remarkable coda: *ve-nafik mineih Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir descended from Nero. Whatever one makes of this tradition in the rabbinic imagination, it is a striking claim — that one of the great Tannaim of the generation after the destruction traced his lineage to the Roman emperor who had been sent to destroy Jerusalem and refused.

## Siege & Wealthy

### Vespasian & Siege

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁדְּרֵיהּ עִילָּוַיְיהוּ לְאַסְפַּסְיָינוּס קֵיסָר. אֲתָא, צָר עֲלַהּ תְּלָת שְׁנֵי. הֲווֹ בַּהּ הָנְהוּ תְּלָתָא עַתִּירֵי: נַקְדִּימוֹן בֶּן גּוּרְיוֹן, וּבֶן כַּלְבָּא שָׂבוּעַ, וּבֶן צִיצִית הַכֶּסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Roman authorities then **sent Vespasian Caesar against** the Jews. **He came** and **laid siege** to Jerusalem for **three years. There were** at that time **in** Jerusalem **these three wealthy people: Nakdimon ben Guryon, ben Kalba Savua, and ben Tzitzit HaKesat.**

With Nero gone, *shadreih ilaveih le-Aspasyanus keisar* — Vespasian was dispatched. He is called "Caesar" in the narrative, though at this point he was still a general. *Ata tzar alah telat shani* — he besieged Jerusalem for three years.

### Three Wealthy Men

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַקְדִּימוֹן בֶּן גּוּרְיוֹן – שֶׁנָּקְדָה לוֹ חַמָּה בַּעֲבוּרוֹ. בֶּן כַּלְבָּא שָׂבוּעַ, שֶׁכׇּל הַנִּכְנָס לְבֵיתוֹ כְּשֶׁהוּא רָעֵב כְּכֶלֶב, יוֹצֵא כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׂבֵעַ. בֶּן צִיצִית הַכֶּסֶת – שֶׁהָיְתָה צִיצָתוֹ נִגְרֶרֶת עַל גַּבֵּי כְּסָתוֹת. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: שֶׁהָיְתָה כִּסְתּוֹ מוּטֶּלֶת בֵּין גְּדוֹלֵי רוֹמִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their names: **Nakdimon ben Guryon** was called by that name **because the sun shined [*nakad*] on his behalf,** as it is related elsewhere (see *Ta'anit* 19b) that the sun once continued to shine in order to prevent him from suffering a substantial loss. **Ben Kalba Savua** was called this **because anyone who entered his house when he was hungry as a dog [*kelev*] would leave satiated [*save'a*]. Ben Tzitzit HaKesat** was referred to by that name because **his ritual fringes [*tzitzit*] dragged** along **on blankets [*keset*],** meaning that he would not walk in the street with his feet on the ground, but rather they would place blankets beneath him. **There are** those **who say that his seat [*kiseh*] was found among the nobles of Rome,** meaning that he would sit among them.

Within the besieged city, three extraordinarily wealthy men were positioned to sustain the population. *Nakdimon Ben Guryon* — a figure known elsewhere in the Talmud, whose name derives from *shenakdah lo chamah ba-avuro*, as if the sun itself broke through the heavens for his sake, a reference to the miracle associated with him. *Ben Kalba Savua* — *she-kol ha-nichnas le-beiso keshehu ra'ev kekelev yetzei keshehu savei'a* — anyone who entered his house as hungry as a dog would leave fully satisfied. And *Ben Tzitzit HaKeset* — *shehayesah tzitziyaso nigreret al gabei kesasos* — whose fringes would drag along cushions, because servants would walk beneath his garment holding pillows so the fringes would never touch the ground. Another tradition holds that his seat was literally placed among the great of Rome.

### Support Offerings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא זָיֵינָּא לְהוּ בְּחִיטֵּי וּשְׂעָרֵי, וְחַד אֲמַר לְהוּ: בִּדְחַמְרָא וּבִדְמִלְחָא וּמִשְׁחָא, וְחַד אֲמַר לְהוּ: בִּדְצִיבֵי. וְשַׁבַּחוּ רַבָּנַן לִדְצִיבֵי, דְּרַב חִסְדָּא כֹּל אַקְלִידֵי הֲוָה מָסַר לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ, בַּר מִדְּצִיבֵי. דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֲכַלְבָּא דְחִיטֵּי בָּעֵי שִׁיתִּין אֲכַלְבֵּי דְצִיבֵי. הֲוָה לְהוּ לְמֵיזַן עֶשְׂרִים וְחַד שַׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** These three wealthy people offered their assistance. **One** of them **said to** the leaders of the city: **I will feed** the residents **with wheat and barley. And one** of them **said to** leaders of the city: I will provide the residents **with wine, salt, and oil. And one** of them **said to** the leaders of the city: I will supply the residents **with wood.** The Gemara comments: **And the Sages gave** special **praise to he** who gave the **wood,** since this was an especially expensive gift. **As Rav Ḥisda would give all of the keys [*aklidei*] to his servant, except** for the key **to** his shed **for** storing **wood,** which he deemed the most important of them all. **As Rav Ḥisda said: One storehouse [*akhleva*] of wheat requires sixty storehouses of wood** for cooking and baking fuel. These three wealthy men **had** between them enough commodities **to sustain** the besieged **for twenty-one years.**

Each man stepped forward to guarantee provisions for the entire besieged city. One offered to supply all of Jerusalem's wheat and barley. A second offered all the wine, salt, and oil. The third offered to supply firewood. The *Gemara* notes that the rabbis gave special praise to the one who offered the firewood — citing a teaching of Rav Chisda: *akliba de-chitei ba'eia shisin aklibei de-detzivi* — one storage warehouse of wheat requires sixty warehouses of firewood to bake it. The sheer volume and value of what was being pledged in firewood was staggering.

### Zealots Burn Food

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲווֹ בְּהוּ הָנְהוּ בִּרְיוֹנֵי, אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ רַבָּנַן: נִיפּוֹק וְנַעֲבֵיד שְׁלָמָא בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ. לָא שַׁבְקִינְהוּ. אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: נִיפּוֹק וְנַעֲבֵיד קְרָבָא בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ רַבָּנַן: לָא מִסְתַּיְּיעָא מִילְּתָא. קָמוּ קְלֹנְהוּ לְהָנְהוּ אַמְבָּרֵי דְּחִיטֵּי וּשְׂעָרֵי, וַהֲוָה כַּפְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **There were certain zealots among** the people of Jerusalem. **The Sages said to them: Let us go out and make peace with** the Romans. But the zealots **did not allow them** to do this. The zealots **said to** the Sages: **Let us go out and engage in battle against** the Romans. But **the Sages said to them: You will not be successful.** It would be better for you to wait until the siege is broken. In order to force the residents of the city to engage in battle, the zealots **arose and burned** down **these storehouses [*ambarei*] of wheat and barley, and there was** a general **famine.**

Together, the three pledges were sufficient to sustain Jerusalem under siege for twenty-one years. But this immense resource was rendered worthless. *Havu behu hanahu biryoni* — there were zealots in the city. When the rabbis counseled making peace — *nafek vena'avid shelma bahadeihu* — the zealots blocked them. When the rabbis said that going to war would not succeed — *lo mistaye'a milta* — the zealots responded by going to war anyway. And to force the population's hand and eliminate any possibility of negotiated surrender, *kalin'hu lehannu ambarei dechitti usha'arei* — they burned down the warehouses of wheat and barley. The famine was now inevitable.

## Martha & Famine

### Martha's Flour Quest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָרְתָּא בַּת בַּיְיתּוֹס עַתִּירְתָּא דִּירוּשָׁלַיִם הַוְיָא. שַׁדַּרְתֵּהּ לִשְׁלוּחַה,ּ וַאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי לִי סְמִידָא. אַדַּאֲזַל אִיזְדַּבַּן. אֲתָא אֲמַר לַהּ: סְמִידָא לֵיכָּא, חִיוָּרְתָּא אִיכָּא. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי לִי. אַדַּאֲזַל אִיזְדַּבַּן. אֲתָא וַאֲמַר לַהּ: חִיוָּרְתָּא לֵיכָּא, גּוּשְׁקְרָא אִיכָּא. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי לִי. אַדַּאֲזַל אִזְדַּבַּן. אֲתָא וַאֲמַר לַהּ: גּוּשְׁקְרָא לֵיכָּא, קִימְחָא דִשְׂעָרֵי אִיכָּא. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי לִי. אַדַּאֲזַל אִיזְדַּבַּן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this famine it is related that **Marta bat Baitos was** one of the **wealthy women of Jerusalem. She sent** out **her agent and said to him: Go bring me fine flour [*semida*]. By the time he went,** the fine flour **was** already **sold. He came** and **said to her: There is no fine flour,** but **there is** ordinary **flour. She said to him: Go** then and **bring me** ordinary flour. **By the time he went,** the ordinary flour **was** also **sold. He came and said to her: There is no** ordinary **flour,** but **there is coarse flour [*gushkera*]. She said to him: Go** then and **bring me** coarse flour. **By the time he went,** the coarse flour **was** already **sold. He came and said to her: There is no coarse flour,** but **there is barley flour. She said to him: Go** then and **bring me** barley flour. But once again, **by the time he went,** the barley flour **was** also **sold.**

Martha the daughter of Baitos was among the wealthiest women in Jerusalem. With the famine setting in, she dispatched her attendant: *zil aeiti li smida* — go get me fine flour. By the time he arrived at the market, it had sold out. He returned and reported: no fine flour, but white flour is available. She sent him for the white flour. By the time he arrived, that too was gone. He returned: no white flour, but coarser flour is available. She sent him for that. Gone by the time he arrived. Barley flour remained. Gone as well.

### Martha's Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה שְׁלִיפָא מְסָאנָא, אֲמַרָה: אִיפּוֹק וְאֶחְזֵי אִי מַשְׁכַּחְנָא מִידֵּי לְמֵיכַל. אִיתִיב לַהּ פַּרְתָּא בְּכַרְעַאּ, וּמִתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **She had** just **removed her shoes,** but **she said: I will go out** myself **and see if I can find something to eat.** She stepped on some **dung,** which **stuck to her foot, and,** overcome by disgust, **she died.**

Finally, Martha decided to go out herself — barefoot, it would seem, since the text says *havah shalpa masana*, she had removed her shoe. She stepped into dung, and the shock and revulsion of it — she who had been so utterly refined — killed her.

### Martha's Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרֵי עֲלַהּ רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: ״הָרַכָּה בְךָ וְהָעֲנוּגָּה אֲשֶׁר לֹא נִסְּתָה כַף רַגְלָהּ״. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: גְּרוֹגֶרֶת דְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק אֲכַלָה, וְאִיתְּנִיסָא וּמִתָה. דְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק יְתֵיב אַרְבְּעִין שְׁנִין בְּתַעֲנִיתָא דְּלָא לֵיחָרֵב יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, כִּי הֲוָה אָכֵיל מִידֵּי הֲוָה מִיתְחֲזֵי מֵאַבָּרַאי. וְכִי הֲוָה בָּרֵיא, מַיְיתִי לֵיהּ גְּרוֹגְרוֹת, מָיֵיץ מַיַּיְהוּ וְשָׁדֵי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai read concerning her** a verse found in the section of the Torah listing the curses that will befall Israel: **"The tender and delicate woman among you who would not adventure to set the sole of her foot** upon the ground" (Deuteronomy 28:56). **There are** those **who say** that she did not step on dung, but rather **she ate a fig of Rabbi Tzadok, and became disgusted and died.** What are these figs? **Rabbi Tzadok observed fasts** for **forty years,** praying **that Jerusalem would not be destroyed.** He became so emaciated from fasting **that when he would eat something it was visible from the outside** of his body. **And when he would eat** after a fast **they would bring him figs** and **he would suck out their liquid and cast** the rest **away.** It was one such fig that Marta bat Baitos found and that caused her death.

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai applied to her the verse: *haraka becha vehanuga asher lo nista chaf ragla* — the tender and delicate woman who had never set the sole of her foot upon the ground. This was the very curse whose fulfillment she had now experienced.

An alternative tradition is offered: *ika de'amri grogerot deRabbi Tzadok achala* — some say she found and ate one of the dried figs that Rabbi Tzadok had discarded. Rabbi Tzadok, as will be described, was so emaciated from his decades of fasting that even illness clung to the scraps he rejected. She consumed one of these and died from the contagion.

### Rabbi Tzadok Fast

This brings the *Gemara* to Rabbi Tzadok himself. *Yativ arba'in shanin betanita* — he sat for forty years in a state of fasting, *dela licherav Yerushalayim* — so that Jerusalem might not be destroyed. When he ate at all, he was so thin that you could see the food going down from the outside of his body. When he was at his most recovered, they would bring him dried figs, *maitz meyayuhu* — he would suck out the juice, *v'shadileihu* — and throw them out. The contrast with Martha is pointed: Rabbi Tzadok is the *tzaddik* — in name and reality — who through private, invisible righteousness has been holding back the catastrophe. But his righteousness, as profound as it is, is not enough to reach others or redirect the course of events.

### Martha's Wealth Cast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲוָה קָא נִיחָא נַפְשַׁהּ, אַפִּיקְתֵּהּ לְכֹל דַּהֲבַהּ וְכַסְפַּהּ שְׁדֵיתֵיהּ בְּשׁוּקָא, אֲמַרָה: הַאי לְמַאי מִיבְּעֵי לִי! וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״כַּסְפָּם בְּחוּצוֹת יַשְׁלִיכוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** It is further related that **as she was dying, she took out all of** her **gold and silver** and **threw it in the marketplace. She said: Why do I need this? And this is as it is written: "They shall cast their silver in the streets** and their gold shall be as an impure thing; their silver and their gold shall not be able to deliver them in the day of the wrath of the Lord; they shall not satisfy their souls, neither fill their bowels" (Ezekiel 7:19).

As she lay dying, Martha did something extraordinary: *afikta lechol dahava vechaspa veshadisa beshuka* — she took out all her gold and silver and threw it into the marketplace. *Hai lemai mibaya li* — what use is any of this to me now? The *Gemara* connects this to the verse: *kaspam bachutzot yashlichu* — their silver they shall throw out into the streets.

## Yochanan's Escape

### Family Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא סִקְרָא – רֵישׁ בִּרְיוֹנֵי דִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ דְּרַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי הֲוָה. שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: תָּא בְּצִינְעָא לְגַבַּאי. אֲתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַד אֵימַת עָבְדִיתוּ הָכִי, וְקָטְלִיתוּ לֵיהּ לְעָלְמָא בְּכַפְנָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֶיעֱבֵיד, דְּאִי אָמֵינָא לְהוּ מִידֵּי קָטְלוּ לִי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזִי לִי תַּקַּנְתָּא לְדִידִי דְּאֶיפּוֹק, אֶפְשָׁר דְּהָוֵי הַצָּלָה פּוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Abba Sikkara was the leader of the zealots [*biryonei*] of Jerusalem** and **the son of the sister of Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai.** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **sent** a message **to him: Come to me in secret. He came,** and Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to him: Until when will you do this and kill everyone through starvation?** Abba Sikkara **said to him: What can I do, for if I say something to them they will kill me.** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to him: Show me a method** so **that I will** be able to **leave** the city, and it is **possible that** through this **there will be** some **small salvation.**

*Abba Sikara* — whose name connects back to the *sicricon* with which the *daf* opened, and who is identified with the Sicarii, the dagger-men — was *reish biryonei diyerushalayim*, the head of the Jerusalem zealots. He was also *bar achtei d'Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* — the nephew of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai.

### Confrontation

Rabban Yochanan sent for him privately: *ta betzina legabai* — come to me in secret. When Abba Sikara came, Rabban Yochanan confronted him directly: *ad matai avdisu hachi vekatlisu lehu l'alma dechafna* — how long will you continue like this, killing everyone through famine?

### Sikara's Dilemma

Abba Sikara's response was not defiance but despair: *mai avidei, de'amina lehu midi, katlu li* — what can I do? If I tell them to stand down, they will kill me. Even the head of the zealots was trapped by the logic of the movement he led.

### Fake Death Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נְקוֹט נַפְשָׁךְ בִּקְצִירֵי, וְלֵיתוֹ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא וְלִישַׁיְּילוּ בָּךְ, וְאַיְיתִי מִידֵּי סַרְיָא וְאַגְנִי גַּבָּךְ, וְלֵימְרוּ דְּנָח נַפְשָׁךְ. וְלִיעַיְּילוּ בָּךְ תַּלְמִידָךְ וְלָא לֵיעוּל בָּךְ אִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא, דְּלָא לַרְגְּשׁוּן בָּךְ דְּקַלִּיל אַתְּ, דְּאִינְהוּ יָדְעִי דְּחַיָּיא קַלִּיל מִמִּיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abba Sikkara **said to him:** This is what you should do: **Pretend to be sick, and have everyone come and ask** about your welfare, so that word will spread about your ailing condition. Afterward **bring something putrid and place it near you, so that** people **will say that you have died** and are decomposing. **And** then, **have your students enter** to bring you to burial, **and let no one else come in so that** the zealots **not notice that you are** still **light. As** the zealots **know that a living** person **is lighter than a dead** person.

Rabban Yochanan asked him to help engineer an escape: *chazi li takanta dididi, de'ipuk, efshar dehavi hatzalah purta* — find me some solution, that I might get out, perhaps there will be some small salvation. The phrase *hatzalah purta* — a little salvation — is significant and will recur.

Abba Sikara devised the plan. Rabban Yochanan was to take to bed feigning illness, let students and community come to pay their respects for many days, then arrange for something decomposing to be placed nearby so that the smell of death would be reported. *Denach nafshach* — it would be said that he had died. His students — who would be in on the scheme — would carry him out in a coffin. No one else could be admitted, because *dekalil at* — a live person is lighter than a dead one, and unfamiliar visitors would notice the difference in weight.

### Gate Escape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבֵיד הָכִי. נִכְנַס בּוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מִצַּד אֶחָד, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מִצַּד אַחֵר. כִּי מְטוֹ לְפִיתְחָא, בְּעוֹ לְמִדְקְרֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: יֹאמְרוּ: רַבָּן דָּקְרוּ! בְּעוֹ לְמִדְחֲפֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: יֹאמְרוּ: רַבָּן דָּחֲפוּ! פְּתַחוּ לֵיהּ בָּבָא, נְפַק.
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **did this. Rabbi Eliezer entered from one side and Rabbi Yehoshua from the other side** to take him out. **When they arrived at the entrance** of the city on the inside, the guards, who were of the faction of the zealots, **wanted to pierce him** with their swords in order to ascertain that he was actually dead, as was the common practice. Abba Sikkara **said to them:** The Romans **will say** that **they pierce** even **their teacher.** The guards then **wanted** at least **to push him** to see whether he was still alive, in which case he would cry out on account of the pushing. Abba Sikkara **said to them: They will say** that **they push** even **their teacher.** The guards then **opened the gate** and **he was taken out.**

Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua — the two great students who would themselves become teachers of Rabbi Akiva — carried the coffin out of the city. At the gate, the zealot guards wanted to pierce the body to verify death. Abba Sikara deflected them: *yomru rabanan dakru* — do you want it said that you stabbed your own Rabbi? They then wanted at least to push the coffin around. *Yomru rabanan dachfu* — do you want it said that you shoved your Rabbi around even in death? The gate was opened. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai emerged.

### Vespasian Meeting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מְטָא לְהָתָם, אֲמַר: שְׁלָמָא עֲלָךְ מַלְכָּא, שְׁלָמָא עֲלָךְ מַלְכָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִיחַיְּיבַתְּ תְּרֵי (קְטָלָא) [קָטְלִי], חֲדָא דְּלָאו מַלְכָּא אֲנָא וְקָא קָרֵית לִי מַלְכָּא, וְתוּ אִי מַלְכָּא אֲנָא עַד הָאִידָּנָא אַמַּאי לָא אָתֵית לְגַבַּאי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּקָאָמְרַתְּ לָאו מַלְכָּא אֲנָא,
>
> **Translation:** **When** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **reached there,** i.e., the Roman camp, **he said: Greetings to you, the king; greetings to you, the king.** Vespasian **said to him: You are liable for two death penalties, one** because **I am not a king and** yet **you call me king, and furthermore, if I am a king, why didn't you come to me until now?** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to him:** As for **what you said** about yourself: **I am not a king,**

When he reached Vespasian's camp, Rabban Yochanan shed his disguise and greeted the general: *shlama aleichah malka, shlama aleichah malka* — peace to you, O king, peace to you, O king.

## Honey & Serpent

### Identity Challenge

Vespasian responded sharply: *mechayeivat trei katla* — you deserve two deaths. First, *delav malka ana* — I am not king, and you are mocking me by calling me one. Second, *ei malka ana, ad idna amai lo aseit legabai* — if I were king, why did you wait until now to come and pay your respects?

### Scripture Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבְרָא מַלְכָּא אַתְּ; דְּאִי לָאו מַלְכָּא אַתְּ לָא מִימַּסְרָא יְרוּשָׁלַיִם בִּידָךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַלְּבָנוֹן בְּאַדִּיר יִפּוֹל״ – וְאֵין ״אַדִּיר״ אֶלָּא מֶלֶךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה אַדִּירוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ וְגוֹ׳״; וְאֵין ״לְבָנוֹן״ אֶלָּא בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָהָר הַטּוֹב הַזֶּה וְהַלְּבָנוֹן״. וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ: אִי מַלְכָּא אֲנָא, אַמַּאי לָא קָאָתֵית לְגַבַּאי עַד הָאִידָּנָא – בִּרְיוֹנֵי דְּאִית בַּן לָא שָׁבְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **in truth, you are a king,** if not now, then in the future. **As if you are not a king, Jerusalem will not be handed over into your hand, as it is written: "And the Lebanon shall fall by a mighty one"** (Isaiah 10:34). **And "mighty one"** means **only a king, as it is written: "And their mighty one shall be of themselves,** and their ruler shall proceed from the midst of them" (Jeremiah 30:21), indicating that "mighty one" parallels "ruler." **And "Lebanon"** means **only the Temple, as it is stated: "That good mountain and the Lebanon"** (Deuteronomy 3:25). **And** as for **what you said** with your second comment: **If I am a king why didn't you come to me until now, there are zealots among us** who **did not allow us** to do this.

Rabban Yochanan responded to both challenges. As for the first: you are indeed king, *de ee lav malka at, Yerushalayim lo mitnasyah be-yadach* — because only a king could receive Jerusalem into his hands. The proof is from scripture: *dechtiv vehalevanon beadier yipol* — the *Levanon* will fall to the *adier*, the mighty one. *Veein adier ela melech* — *adier* means king, as proven from the verse *vehaya adiero mimenu, umeshlo mikirbo yeitzei* — his ruler will come from within him. *Veein Levanon ela Beis HaMikdash* — and *Levanon* is the *Beis HaMikdash*, as proven from *hahar hatov hazeh vehalevanon* — the good mountain and the *Levanon*. Since Jerusalem and the *Beis HaMikdash* are being delivered into his hands, Vespasian must be king.

As for the second challenge: *biryonei deis behan lo shavkin* — the zealots would not permit me to come. They were the reason.

### Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ חָבִית שֶׁל דְּבַשׁ וּדְרָקוֹן כָּרוּךְ עָלֶיהָ, לֹא הָיוּ שׁוֹבְרִין אֶת הֶחָבִית בִּשְׁבִיל דְּרָקוֹן? אִישְׁתִּיק. קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״מֵשִׁיב חֲכָמִים אָחוֹר וְדַעְתָּם יְסַכֵּל״, אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר לֵיהּ: שָׁקְלִינַן צְבָתָא וְשָׁקְלִינַן לֵיהּ לִדְרָקוֹן וְקָטְלִינַן לֵיהּ, וְחָבִיתָא שָׁבְקִינַן לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Understanding that Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai was prepared to ask him not to destroy the Temple, Vespasian **said to him: If** there is **a barrel of honey and a snake [*derakon*] is wrapped around it, wouldn't they break the barrel in order to** kill **the snake?** In similar fashion, I am forced to destroy the city of Jerusalem in order to kill the zealots barricaded within it. Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **was silent** and did not answer. In light of this, **Rav Yosef** later **read** the following verse **about him, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Akiva** who applied the verse to Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai: "I am the Lord…**Who turns wise men backward and makes their knowledge foolish"** (Isaiah 44:25). As Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **should have said** the following **to** Vespasian in response: In such a case, **we take tongs, remove the snake, and kill it, and** in this way **we leave the barrel** intact. So too, you should kill the rebels and leave the city as it is.

Vespasian pressed back: *ilu chavis shel devash vedarkon karuch aleha, lo hayu shovrin es hachavis bishvil hadarkon* — if there is a barrel of honey and a serpent is coiled around it, would you not break the barrel open to get rid of the serpent? Here too: you should have broken the walls of Jerusalem, we would have come in, dealt with the zealots, and ended the whole matter far sooner.

### Response Failure

*Ishtik* — Rabban Yochanan was silent. He had no answer. Rav Yosef, and some say Rabbi Akiva, applied the verse *meishiv chachamim achor vedaatam yesakel* — God sometimes causes the wise to retreat and their wisdom to become folly. The proper response, they said, should have been: *shaklinan tzavta veshaklinan lei ledarkon vekatlinan lei vachavita shavkinan lah* — we would take tongs, remove the serpent, kill it, and preserve the barrel. Here too: the goal was to find a way to deal with the zealots without destroying Jerusalem.

## Caesar & Shoe

### Vespasian Crowned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּהָכִי, אֲתָא פְּרֵיסְתְּקָא עֲלֵיהּ מֵרוֹמִי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קוּם, דְּמִית לֵיהּ קֵיסָר, וְאָמְרִי הָנְהוּ חֲשִׁיבֵי דְּרוֹמִי לְאוֹתֹיבָךָ בְּרֵישָׁא. הֲוָה סָיֵים חַד (מסאני) [מְסָאנֵיהּ]. בְּעָא לְמִסְיְימֵהּ לְאַחֲרִינָא, לָא עָיֵל. בְּעָא לְמִישְׁלְפֵיהּ לְאִידַּךְ, לָא נְפַק. אֲמַר: מַאי הַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **In the meantime,** as they were talking, **a messenger [*feristaka*] arrived from Rome,** and **said to him: Rise, for the emperor has died, and the noblemen of Rome plan to appoint you as** their **leader** and make you the next emperor. At that time Vespasian **was wearing** only **one shoe,** and when **he tried to put on the other one, it would not go on** his foot. **He** then **tried to remove the other** shoe that he was already wearing, but **it would not come off. He said: What is this?**

While they were still in conversation, *asei pristaka miRomi* — a messenger arrived from Rome. *Kum, demeis lei keisar* — arise, the Caesar has died, *veamri hanu chashivei deRomi leoshivach bereisha* — and the senate of Rome has appointed you as the new Caesar.

### Swollen Feet

At that moment Vespasian was putting on his shoe. He went to put on the second shoe — it would not go on. He tried to remove the first — it would not come off. *Amar mai hai* — what is happening?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא תִּצְטַעַר, שְׁמוּעָה טוֹבָה אַתְיָא לָךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״שְׁמוּעָה טוֹבָה תְּדַשֶּׁן עָצֶם״. אֶלָּא מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? לֵיתֵי אִינִישׁ דְּלָא מְיַתְּבָא דַּעְתָּךְ מִינֵּיהּ, וְלַחֲלֹיף קַמָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרוּחַ נְכֵאָה תְּיַבֶּשׁ גָּרֶם״. עֲבַד הָכִי, עֲיַיל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to him: Be not distressed** or troubled, for **good tidings have reached you, as it is written: "Good tidings make the bone fat"** (Proverbs 15:30), and your feet have grown fatter out of joy and satisfaction. Vespasian said to him: **But what is the remedy?** What must I do in order to put on my shoe? Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to him: Have someone with whom you are displeased come and pass before you, as it is written: "A broken spirit dries the bones"** (Proverbs 17:22). **He did this, and** his shoe **went on** his foot.

Rabban Yochanan explained: *shemuah tovah asya lach, dechtiv shemuah tovah tedashen atzem* — good news has come to you, and as the verse says, good news fattens the bone. The sudden swelling of pride and joy has caused his feet to expand so that neither shoe fits.

### Pride Deflation

The solution: *leisi inish dela misava daatach mineih vechalif kamach* — bring someone whose presence is distressing to you, let him walk before you, *dechtiv veruach necheiah tyavesh garem* — and a downtrodden spirit dries up the bones. He did so, and his feet returned to normal.

## Request for Yavne

### Why Not Sooner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמֵאַחַר דְּחָכְמִיתוּ כּוּלֵּי הַאי, עַד הָאִידָּנָא אַמַּאי לָא אָתֵיתוּ לְגַבַּאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָא אֲמַרִי לָךְ?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא נָמֵי אֲמַרִי לָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** Vespasian then turned to Rabban Yoḥanan: **Since you are so wise, why didn't you come to me sooner?** Surely you could have found a way. [Rabban Yoḥanan repeated:] the zealots would not allow it. Vespasian's implied counter — that Rabban Yoḥanan should have found a means to neutralize them — reflects the same argument he had made with the serpent parable.

Vespasian then turned to Rabban Yochanan: *umiaechar dechachmitu kulei hai, ad idna amai lo aseitu legabai* — since you are so wise, why did you not come to me sooner? Surely you could have found a way.

### Zealot Barrier

Rabban Yochanan repeated the answer: the zealots would not allow it. Vespasian's implied counter — that Rabban Yochanan should have found a means to neutralize them — reflects the same argument he had made with the serpent parable.

### Three Grants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵיזָל אָזֵילְנָא, וְאִינָשׁ אַחֲרִינָא מְשַׁדַּרְנָא; אֶלָּא בָּעֵי מִינַּאי מִידֵּי דְּאֶתֵּן לָךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תֵּן לִי יַבְנֶה וַחֲכָמֶיהָ, וְשׁוּשִׁילְתָּא דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְאָסְווֹתָא דְּמַסַּיִין לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי צָדוֹק. קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״מֵשִׁיב חֲכָמִים אָחוֹר וְדַעְתָּם יְסַכֵּל״, אִיבְּעִי לְמֵימַר לֵיהּ: לִשְׁבְּקִינְהוּ הָדָא זִימְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** Vespasian then **said to** Rabban Yoḥanan: **I will be going** to Rome to accept my new position, **and I will send someone else** in my place to continue besieging the city and waging war against it. **But** before I leave, **ask something of me that I** can **give you.** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to him: Give me Yavne and its Sages** and do not destroy it, **and** spare **the dynasty of Rabban Gamliel** and do not kill them as if they were rebels, **and** lastly give me **doctors to heal Rabbi Tzadok. Rav Yosef read** the following verse **about him, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Akiva** who applied the verse to Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai: "I am the Lord…**Who turns wise men backward and makes their knowledge foolish"** (Isaiah 44:25), as **he should have said to him to leave** the Jews alone **this time.**

Vespasian then told him: *meizal azilna*, I must return to Rome, *ve-inash achrina meshadrana*, and I will send another commander to complete the campaign. *Ela bei minai midi de-aten lach* — but ask of me something and I will grant it.

Rabban Yochanan's request: *ten li Yavne ve-chachameiha* — give me Yavne and its sages. And *shishulta de-Rabbi Gamliel* — preserve the chain of Rabban Gamliel's family, meaning the *nasi* line. This is the great pivot of the passage and, in a sense, of Jewish history: the recognition that the *Beis HaMikdash* will fall, and that what must be preserved in its place is a Torah center and the continuity of Jewish communal leadership. The phrase *hatzalah purta* — the little salvation — echoes back. Rabban Yochanan will not save Jerusalem. But he will save something. The shift from a Temple-centered Jerusalem to a rabbinic Judaism centered on Torah study begins here, at Yavne.

### Doctors Request

The third request is of a different register entirely: *ve-asvata de-masyan lei le-Rabbi Tzadok* — provide doctors who will heal Rabbi Tzadok. After the grand national requests — a Torah center, the continuity of leadership — Rabban Yochanan asks for the care of a single righteous individual. Rabbi Tzadok, who had fasted for forty years to prevent the destruction, now needs to be healed so he can live into whatever comes next. There is something deeply moving in placing this personal request alongside the national ones — an insistence that even amid catastrophe, the individual *tzaddik* matters and must be carried into the future.

## Akiva's Critique

### Wisdom Diminished

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵאַחַר דְּחָכְמִיתוּ כּוּלֵּי הַאי, עַד הָאִידָּנָא אַמַּאי לָא אָתֵיתוּ לְגַבַּאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָא אֲמַרִי לָךְ?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא נָמֵי אֲמַרִי לָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** Vespasian then turned to Rabban Yoḥanan: **Since you are so wise, why didn't you come to me sooner?** Surely you could have found a way. [Rabban Yoḥanan repeated:] the zealots would not allow it. Vespasian's implied counter — that Rabban Yoḥanan should have found a means to neutralize them — reflects the same argument he had made with the serpent parable.

At this point the *Gemara* returns with a critique. Rav Yosef — and some say Rabbi Akiva — applied to Rabban Yochanan the verse *meishiv chachamim achor vedaatam yesakel*: God caused the wise to retreat, their wisdom diminished.

### Better Answer

What should Rabban Yochanan have asked for? *Ibei lei le-meimar lei shavkinehu hada zimna* — he should have asked Vespasian simply to leave Jerusalem alone entirely. If you will grant me something, leave. Do not destroy it at all.

### Yochanan's Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהוּא סָבַר: דִּלְמָא כּוּלֵּי הַאי לָא עָבֵיד, וְהַצָּלָה פּוּרְתָּא נָמֵי לָא הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** why didn't Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai make this request? **He maintained** that Vespasian **might not do that much** for him, **and there would not be even a small** amount of **salvation.** Therefore, he made only a modest request, in the hope that he would receive at least that much.

Why did he not ask this? *Dilma kulei hai lo avid, ve-hatzalah porta nami lo havei* — he was afraid that Vespasian would refuse even that, and then he would have gotten nothing at all. Not even Yavne. Not even the little salvation.

The voice of Rabbi Akiva here is characteristic. Rabbi Akiva, who would later support Bar Kochba and envision the full restoration of Jewish sovereignty, could not accept the compromise. He wanted to hold out for everything. Rabban Yochanan, with clearer eyes about what was politically achievable, chose to secure what he could. Neither position is without cost.

## Tzadok's Healing

### Gradual Nutrition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָסְווֹתָא דְּמַסַּיִין לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי צָדוֹק – מַאי הִיא? יוֹמָא קַמָּא אַשְׁקְיוּהּ מַיָּא דְפָארֵי, לִמְחַר מַיָּא דְסִיפּוּקָא, לִמְחַר מַיָּא דְקִימְחָא, עַד דִּרְוַוח מְיעֵיהּ פּוּרְתָּא פּוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** was he requesting when he asked for **doctors to heal Rabbi Tzadok?** How did they heal him? **The first day they gave him water to drink** that contained **bran [*parei*]. The next** day they gave him **water** containing **flour mixed with bran [*sipuka*]. The following day** they gave him **water** containing **flour.** In this way they slowly restored his ability to eat, allowing **his stomach to broaden little by little.**

The *Gemara* then describes in clinical detail how the doctors went about healing Rabbi Tzadok. *Yoma kama ashkyuha maya defari* — on the first day they gave him water in which bran had been soaked: mostly water, a trace of nourishment, nothing more than his devastated body could absorb. The next day: *maya desipuka* — water with bran and a bit of flour. The day after: *maya de-kimcha* — water with flour. Each day the diet was slightly more nutritious than the day before.

### Stomach Expansion

*Ad deravach me'eih purta purta* — until his stomach expanded little by little. The word *purta* — a little, a small amount — is the same word used earlier for *hatzalah purta*, the little salvation. The gradual physical recovery of Rabbi Tzadok mirrors in miniature the gradual spiritual and communal recovery that the founding of Yavne represents. Just as Rabban Yochanan chose to accept a partial salvation rather than lose everything, the doctors choose to introduce nourishment gradually rather than overwhelm the body. The *Churban* has happened; the rebuilding must be slow and careful, one small step at a time.

## Titus Defilement

### Holy of Holies Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל שַׁדַּרֵיהּ לְטִיטוּס. ״וְאָמַר אֵי אֱלֹהֵימוֹ צוּר חָסָיוּ בוֹ״ – זֶה טִיטוּס הָרָשָׁע שֶׁחֵירַף וְגִידֵּף כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Vespasian **went** back to Rome and **sent Titus** in his place. The Gemara cites a verse that was expounded as referring to Titus: **"And he shall say: Where is their God, their rock in whom they trusted?"** (Deuteronomy 32:37). **This is the wicked Titus, who insulted and blasphemed God on High.**

With the transition to what follows, the *Gemara* completely skips over the military details of the siege's end and the burning of Jerusalem, moving directly to the figure of Titus. The verse *ei eloheimo tzur chasayu vo* — where is their God, the rock in whom they take refuge — is identified as referring to *Titus HaRasha*, the wicked Titus, who blasphemed toward Heaven.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה עָשָׂה? תָּפַשׂ זוֹנָה בְּיָדוֹ וְנִכְנַס לְבֵית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים, וְהִצִּיעַ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וְעָבַר עָלֶיהָ עֲבֵירָה. וְנָטַל סַיִיף וְגִידֵּר אֶת הַפָּרוֹכֶת, וְנַעֲשָׂה נֵס וְהָיָה דָּם מְבַצְבֵּץ וְיוֹצֵא, וּכְסָבוּר הָרַג אֶת עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שָׁאֲגוּ צוֹרְרֶיךָ בְּקֶרֶב מוֹעֲדֶיךָ שָׂמוּ אוֹתוֹתָם אוֹתוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **What did** Titus **do** when he conquered the Temple? **He took a prostitute with his hand, and entered the Holy of Holies** with her. **He** then **spread out a Torah scroll** underneath him **and committed a sin,** i.e., engaged in sexual intercourse, **on it.** Afterward **he took a sword and cut into the curtain** separating between the Sanctuary and the Holy of Holies. **And a miracle was performed and blood spurted forth.** Seeing the blood, **he** mistakenly **thought** that **he had killed himself.** Here, the term himself is a euphemism for God. Titus saw blood issuing forth from the curtain in God's meeting place, the Temple, and he took it as a sign that he had succeeded in killing God Himself. **As it is stated: "Your enemies roar in the midst of Your meeting place; they have set up their own signs for signs"** (Psalms 74:4).

*Tafas zonah beyado ve'nichnas le-veit kodshei kodashim* — he took a prostitute in his hand and entered the Holy of Holies. He spread out a *sefer Torah* and committed his act of desecration upon it. The specific detail of the *sefer Torah* is significant: not content with the physical destruction of the Temple, Titus enacts a deliberate symbolic desecration of the Torah itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא חָנָן אוֹמֵר: ״מִי כָמוֹךָ חֲסִין יָהּ״ – מִי כָמוֹךָ חָסִין וְקָשֶׁה, שֶׁאַתָּה שׁוֹמֵעַ נִיאוּצוֹ וְגִידּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע, וְשׁוֹתֵק. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״מִי כָּמֹכָה בָּאֵלִים ה׳״ – מִי כָּמוֹכָה בָּאִלְּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abba Ḥanan says:** The verse states: **"Who is strong like You, O Lord?"** (Psalms 89:9). **Who is strong and indurate like You, as You hear the abuse and the blasphemy of that wicked man and remain silent.** Similarly, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that the verse: **"Who is like You, O Lord, among the gods [*elim*]"** (Exodus 15:11), should be read as: **Who is like You among the mute [*ilmim*],** for You conduct Yourself like a mute and remain silent in the face of Your blasphemers.

He then took a sword — *ve-gider et ha-parochet* — and pierced the curtain, the *parochet* separating the Holy of Holies from the *heichal*. A miracle occurred, though not a saving one: *ve-hayah dam mivatzbetz ve-yotzei* — blood spurted from the curtain. According to Tosafot, this was to demonstrate how great and severe the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash* was — or alternatively, it was intended to mislead Titus.

### God's Silence

*Kasavar harag et atzmo* — Titus thought, through the euphemism of *atzmo*, that he had killed God Himself. He saw this as confirmation, citing the verse *sha'agu tzorecha bekerev mo'adecha* — your enemies have cried out within your sanctuary. Abba Chanan responded: *mi kamocha chasin yah, mi kamocha chasin ve-kasheh* — who is like You in Your strength, who is like You in Your harshness and restraint, *she-attah shome'a ni'utzo ve-giduffo shel oto rasha ve-shotek* — that You can hear the blasphemies of that wicked man and be silent?

The Rabbis reframe God's silence as an expression of incomprehensible might — not weakness, but a restraint so great it staggers comprehension. The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: *mi kamocha ba'eilim Hashem* — who is like You among the mighty — *mi kamocha ba'ilmim* — who is like You among the silent ones?

## Titus Plunder

### Vessel Plunder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה עָשָׂה? נָטַל אֶת הַפָּרוֹכֶת וַעֲשָׂאוֹ כְּמִין גַּרְגּוּתְנִי, וְהֵבִיא כׇּל כֵּלִים שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ וְהִנִּיחָן בָּהֶן, וְהוֹשִׁיבָן בִּסְפִינָה לֵילֵךְ לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּחַ בְּעִירוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְכֵן רָאִיתִי רְשָׁעִים קְבוּרִים וָבָאוּ, וּמִמְּקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ יְהַלֵּכוּ, וְיִשְׁתַּכְּחוּ בָעִיר אֲשֶׁר כֵּן עָשׂוּ״ – אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״קְבוּרִים״ אֶלָּא ״קְבוּצִים״; אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״וְיִשְׁתַּכְּחוּ״ אֶלָּא ״וְיִשְׁתַּבְּחוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **What** else did Titus **do? He took the curtain and formed it like a large basket, and brought all of the** sacred **vessels of the Temple and placed them in it. And he put them on a ship to go and be praised in his city** that he had conquered Jerusalem, **as it is stated: "And so I saw the wicked buried, and come to their rest; but those that had done right were gone from the holy place, and were forgotten in the city;** this also is vanity**"** (Ecclesiastes 8:10). **Do not read** the word **as "buried [*kevurim*]." Rather,** read it as **collected [*kevutzim*].** And **do not read** the word **as "and were forgotten [*veyishtakeḥu*]." Rather,** read it as: **And they were praised [*veyishtabeḥu*].** According to this interpretation, the verse speaks of those who will gather and collect items "from the holy place," the Temple, and be praised in their city about what they had done.

*Ve-asao kemin gargutni* — Titus took the *parochet* and made it into a sack, then filled it with all the vessels of the *Beis HaMikdash* — *kol keilim shebamikdash* — and loaded them onto a ship, intending to return to Rome and display his triumph.

The *Gemara* applies a verse from Kohelet: *ubchen raiti reshaim kburim* — I have seen the wicked buried. The *Gemara* re-reads this: *al tikrei kburim, ela kbutzim* — do not read "buried" but "gathered," meaning the wicked gathered together. *Al tikrei veyishtakchu, ela veyishtabchu* — do not read "and they were forgotten" but "and they praised themselves" in their cities, meaning Titus parading the Temple vessels through Rome. An alternative reading accepts *kburim* as meaning "buried" literally: *de'afilu mili demitamran iglei lehu* — even the buried treasures were revealed to Titus.

### Wave Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמַד עָלָיו נַחְשׁוֹל שֶׁבַּיָּם לְטוֹבְעוֹ, אָמַר: כִּמְדוּמֶּה אֲנִי שֶׁאֱלֹהֵיהֶם שֶׁל אֵלּוּ, אֵין גְּבוּרָתוֹ אֶלָּא בַּמַּיִם; בָּא פַּרְעֹה – טְבָעוֹ בַּמַּיִם, בָּא סִיסְרָא – טְבָעוֹ בַּמַּיִם; אַף הוּא עוֹמֵד עָלַי לְטוֹבְעֵנִי בַּמַּיִם. אִם גִּבּוֹר הוּא, יַעֲלֶה לַיַּבָּשָׁה וְיַעֲשֶׂה עִמִּי מִלְחָמָה! יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: רָשָׁע בֶּן רָשָׁע בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל עֵשָׂו הָרָשָׁע, בְּרִיָּה קַלָּה יֵשׁ לִי בְּעוֹלָמִי, וְיַתּוּשׁ שְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is further related about Titus that he was once traveling **at sea** and **a wave rose up against him** and threatened **to drown him.** Titus **said: It seems to me that their God,** the God of Israel, **has power only in water. Pharaoh rose** against them and **He drowned him in water. Sisera rose** against them and **He drowned him in water.** Here **too, He has risen up against me to drown me in water. If He is** really **mighty, let Him go up on dry land and** there **wage war against me. A Divine Voice issued forth and said to him: Wicked one, son of a wicked one, grandson of Esau the wicked,** for you are among his descendants and act just like him, **I have a lowly creature in My world and it is called a gnat.**

Out at sea, a massive wave rose up to destroy him. Titus interpreted this theologically: *kmidumeh ani she'eloheihem shel elu ein gvurato ela bamayim* — it seems that the God of the Jews only exercises His power in water. He listed the precedents: Pharaoh was drowned, Sisera's forces were swept away by the waters of the Kishon — *nachal Kishon grefam* — so of course God tries to fight him in the sea.

### Gnat Plague

Titus then issued his challenge: *im gibor hu ya'ale layabasha veya'aseh imi milchama* — if God is truly mighty, let Him come up on dry land and fight me there. *Yatzta bat kol* — a heavenly voice issued forth: *lo larasha ben rasha ben bno shel Esav harasha* — wicked one, son of a wicked one, grandson of the wicked Esau — the connection to Edom and Rome is explicit. *Birya kala yesh li be'olami veyitush shma* — I have a tiny creature in My world called a gnat. *Ala layabasha veta'aseh ima milchama* — go up on land and fight it.

## Gnat Torment

### Gnat Entry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָלָה לַיַּבָּשָׁה, בָּא יַתּוּשׁ וְנִכְנַס בְּחוֹטְמוֹ, וְנִקֵּר בְּמוֹחוֹ שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים. יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה קָא חָלְפָא אַבָּבָא דְּבֵי נַפָּחָא, שְׁמַע קָל אַרְזַפְתָּא, אִישְׁתִּיק; אֲמַר: אִיכָּא תַּקַּנְתָּא. כֹּל יוֹמָא מַיְיתוּ נַפָּחָא וּמָחוּ קַמֵּיה. לְגוֹי – יָהֵיב לֵיהּ אַרְבַּע זוּזֵי, יִשְׂרָאֵל – אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִיסָּתְיָיךְ דְּקָא חָזֵית בְּסָנְאָךְ. עַד תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין עֲבַד הָכִי; מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, כֵּיוָן דְּדָשׁ, דָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its story about Titus. The Divine Voice continued: **Go up on dry land and make war with it. He went up on dry land,** and **a gnat came, entered his nostril, and picked at his brain for seven years.** Titus suffered greatly from this until **one day he passed by the gate of a blacksmith's shop.** The gnat **heard the sound of a hammer** and **was silent** and still. Titus **said:** I see that **there is a remedy** for my pain. **Every day they would bring a blacksmith who hammered before him. He would give four dinars** as payment **to a gentile** blacksmith, and **to a Jew he would** simply **say: It is enough for you that you see your enemy** in so much pain. **He did this for thirty days** and it was effective until then. **From that** point **forward, since** the gnat **became accustomed** to the hammering, **it became accustomed** to it, and once again it began to pick away at Titus's brain.

When Titus came ashore, the gnat entered his nose — *venichnas bechotmo* — and began boring into his brain — *venakar bemocho* — and this continued for seven years.

### Blacksmith Relief

One day Titus happened to pass the doorway of a blacksmith's forge. He heard the sound of hammering — *shama kol arzafta* — and the gnat, startled by the harsh clanging, stopped its drilling. *Amar ika takanta* — here, finally, was some relief.

### Daily Visits

Every day thereafter they would bring a blacksmith and have him bang before Titus. *Lenochri yahiv leih arba zuzei* — if the blacksmith was a non-Jew, Titus would pay him four *zuz*. *LeYisrael amar lei* — if the blacksmith was a Jew, Titus would say: *mistayach dekachazit besanach* — it is enough for you that you have seen your enemy in torment. No payment necessary.

### Immunity Develops

For thirty days this provided relief. Then, *keivan dedash dash* — once the gnat had grown accustomed to the sound, it developed an immunity, and the hammering no longer startled it. The relief ceased.

## Titus's Death

### Gnat Growth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי פִּנְחָס בֶּן עֲרוּבָא: אֲנִי הָיִיתִי בֵּין גְּדוֹלֵי רוֹמִי, וּכְשֶׁמֵּת פָּצְעוּ אֶת מוֹחוֹ, וּמָצְאוּ בּוֹ כְּצִפּוֹר דְּרוֹר מִשְׁקַל שְׁנֵי סְלָעִים. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: כְּגוֹזָל בֶּן שָׁנָה – מִשְׁקַל שְׁנֵי לִיטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Pineḥas ben Arova said: I was** at that time **among the noblemen of Rome, and when** Titus **died they split open his head and found** that the gnat had grown to **the size of a sparrow weighing two *sela*. It was taught in** another ***baraita*:** It was **like** a one-**year-old pigeon weighing two *litra*.**

Rabbi Pinchas ben Aruvah testifies: I was among the prominent people of Rome, and *ukshemet patzu et mocho umatzu bo* — when Titus died and they cracked open his skull, they found that the gnat had grown. According to one tradition it had reached the size of *ketzippor dror*, a free bird, *mishkalo shnei sela'im* — weighing two *sela'im*. A *Baraita* describes it as the size of a year-old pigeon, weighing two *litrin*. Abaye adds a received tradition: *piv shel nechoshet* — its mouth was of brass, *vetzipornav shel barzel* — and its claws of iron. The creature that had been growing inside Titus for seven years — fed, in a sense, by the same iron and brass of the blacksmith's forge that briefly gave him respite — had become a metallic instrument of divine retribution.

### Cremation Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נָקְטִינַן, פִּיו שֶׁל נְחוֹשֶׁת וְצִפּוֹרְנָיו שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל. כִּי הֲוָה קָא מָיֵית, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לִיקְלְיוּהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא וּבַדַּרוּ לְקִיטְמֵיהּ אַשַּׁב יַמִּי, דְּלָא לַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ אֱלָהָא דִיהוּדָאֵי וְלוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We have a tradition** that **its mouth** was made **of copper and its claws were** fashioned of **iron. When** Titus **was dying, he said to** his attendants: **Burn that man,** i.e., me, **and scatter his ashes across the seven seas, so that the God of the Jews should not find me and stand me for judgment.**

On his deathbed, Titus issued a final command: *liklyeh l'hahu gavra* — cremate me. *U'nivdrei l'kitmei ashivah yamei* — scatter my ashes across the seven seas.

### Afterlife Fear

The reasoning was explicit: *d'lo lishkachayeh eloha d'yehudai v'lyokmayeh b'dina* — so that the God of the Jews should not find me and bring me to judgment in the afterlife. Titus, who had blasphemed and desecrated with apparent impunity in this world, feared the accounting that awaited him in the next. His entire strategy of cremation and dispersal was an attempt to evade divine justice — a final act of defiance that itself testifies to his acknowledgment that such justice exists. The *Gemara* will continue this account on the following *daf*.
