# Yevamot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arayot & Tzarot Exempt

### Sister Prohibition

The Gemara on 3b opens with the fundamental question: *mana hani milei*? From where do we derive the entire framework established in the Mishnah — that there is no *mitzvah* of *yibum* when the *yevamah* is an *ervah* to the *yavam*, and that the co-wives of such *arayot* are likewise exempt? The Gemara answers: *detanu rabanan* — as our Rabbis taught in a *braita*.

The *braita* begins with the verse: *isha el achota lo tikach litzror legalot ervata aleha bechayeha* — do not take a woman together with her sister, to create friction between them, to uncover one's nakedness upon the other, during her lifetime. The prohibition of marrying a wife's sister, the *braita* notes, applies only while the first wife is still alive, as the verse specifies *bechayeha*.

### 'Aleha' Indicator

The *braita* then raises the following consideration: since the verse regarding *yibum* states *yevama yavo aleha* — the brother-in-law shall come upon her — one might have thought (*shomeia ani*) that *yibum* overrides even the prohibitions of *arayot* listed in the Mishnah. The word *aleha* in the context of *yibum* establishes that the act is being performed in the context of a *mitzvah*. By means of a *gezerah shavah* on the word *aleha*, the *braita* connects the two verses: just as *aleha* in the *yibum* verse denotes a case where there is a *mitzvah*, so too the *aleha* in the prohibition of a wife's sister — *legalot ervata aleha* — refers even to a case where there is a *mitzvah* of *yibum*. The Torah thus teaches that even when *yibum* would otherwise apply, the prohibition of marrying a wife's sister holds firm. The verse *lo tikach* — do not take — stands even in that context.

### Tzarata Exemption

Having established that the wife's sister herself is exempt from *yibum*, the *braita* turns to the *tzara* — the co-wife. *Ve'ein li ela hi* — I know only that the wife's sister herself is exempt. How do I know the co-wife (*tzaratah*) is also exempt? *Talmud lomar litzror* — the verse uses the word *litzror*, which is read as an allusion to *tzara* (co-wife), as in the verse regarding Chana and Penina, *ve'ki'asata tzaratah gam ka'as*. Thus the word *litzror* extends the exemption to the co-wife as well.

The *braita* then asks: *tzarat tzaratah minayin* — how do I know that if this co-wife subsequently marries another brother who then dies, her own co-wife is also exempt? *Talmud lomar litzror* — the doubled *resh* of *litzror*, as opposed to the single *resh* of *latsor*, indicates even the *tzara* of a *tzara*. This is the textual basis for the Mishnah's principle that exemption extends to *tzarat tzaratah* and beyond, *ad sof kol ha'olam*.

It is worth pausing to consider whether a separate *pasuk* is truly needed for the case of *tzarat tzaratah*. Consider the case concretely: Reuven is married to Rachel; Shimon is married to Leah (Rachel's sister) and also to Chana. Shimon dies. Chana is the *tzara* of Leah, who is an *ervah* to Reuven. There is no *mitzvah* of *yibum* for Chana. Now Chana marries Levi, who also has a wife Penina. Levi dies. The question is whether Penina is exempt as a *tzarat tzaratah*, or whether we can say that once Chana became a *tzara* without a *mitzvah* of *yibum*, she acquired the *halachic* status of *eshet ach* — and any subsequent co-wife of hers would be exempt not as a *tzarat tzaratah* but simply because she is the co-wife of someone who is herself an *ervah* of *eshet ach*. This is a genuine conceptual question: is *tzarat tzaratah* truly an independent category, or does the exemption always reduce to the generation of new cases of *eshet ach*? The *braita* treats it as requiring its own textual source, derived from the reading of *litzror* rather than *latsor*.

### Generalize Arayot

Having established the rule by the wife's sister, the *braita* extends it to all *arayot*: *she'ar arayot minayin*? The answer proceeds by *mah matzinu* — analogy from a paradigmatic case. The wife's sister is distinctive in that she is an *ervah* whose intentional violation incurs *kareit* and whose inadvertent violation requires a *chatat*, and she is forbidden to the *yavam*. From this case we generalize: any woman who is an *ervah* of similar weight — *kareit* for intentional violation, *chatat* for inadvertent — is similarly forbidden to the *yavam*. This produces the general principle: *ha'arayot pturot u'poterot tzoroseihen* — the *arayot* are exempt and exempt their co-wives.

The *braita* then similarly extends the *tzara* exemption to all *arayot* through the same type of *mah matzinu*: just as the wife's sister, who is an *ervah* with *kareit*, exempts not only herself but her co-wife, so too any *ervah* of comparable severity exempts her co-wife. This is the basis for the Mishnah's statement that fifteen women exempt their co-wives and the co-wives of their co-wives from both *chalitzah* and *yibum* forever.

### Six Serious Arayot

The *braita* then raises a further possibility: *yachol she'ani marbeh af sheish arayot ha'chamurot me'elu* — perhaps I should include even the six *arayot* that are more severe than those already discussed, such that their co-wives would also be forbidden. To understand what this means, one must look ahead to the Mishnah on 13a, which speaks of *sheish arayot chamurot me'elu* — six *arayot* that are more weighty *mipnei shenesu'ot l'acherim*, because these women are married to others — meaning they are forbidden relations whom a man's brother could never have married in the first place. A mother or a sister, for example, can never be a brother's wife; the scenario of *yibum* simply cannot arise. Since there is never a *yibum* context in such cases, co-wives of a stranger who married one's mother or sister are entirely permissible — the entire framework of *tzara* is predicated on *yibum*.

The *braita* responds: the paradigm case is specifically an *ervah* that *efshar l'hinasay l'ach* — that could have been married to the brother. In such a case, *asurah l'yavam v'tzaratah asurah* — she is forbidden to the *yavam* and her co-wife is forbidden. The six more severe *arayot* are excluded — *yatzu sheish arayot ha'chamurot* — because they cannot be married to a brother, and therefore *tzoroseihen mutarot*. The entire category of *tzara* is meaningful only within the context of *yibum*; without it, there is no *tzara* at all. As the *braita* concludes: *she'ein tzara ela mei'ach* — the concept of *tzara* exists only in relation to a brother's wife.

### Kareit Punishment

The *braita* then addresses a final question: *azharah shamanu*, we have heard the prohibition — but what of the punishment? If a man proceeded and performed *yibum* with an *ervah*, would the full weight of *kareit* still apply? One might have thought that since *yibum* is itself a *mitzvah*, the violation is somehow attenuated. The *braita* answers from the verse: *ki kol asher ya'aseh mikol hato'evot ha'eleh venichretu* — whoever performs any of these abominations, they shall be cut off. This verse of *kareit* appears at the close of the entire section of *arayot* in the Torah, and since the prohibition of a wife's sister — *isha el achota lo tikach litzror* — has been shown via the word *aleha* to include even a case of *yibum*, the *kareit* that follows that section applies equally in the *yibum* context. The *tzara* is also covered: the word *litzror* has already extended the prohibition to include the co-wife, and the subsequent *venichretu* encompasses her as well.

## Mitzvah Override Logic

### Hava Amina Premise

The Gemara steps back from the *braita* and asks: *ta'ama de-kata rachmana aleha* — it is only because of the word *aleha* that we know *yibum* does not override the prohibition of *arayot*. *Hava amina*, had the Torah not written that limiting word, I would have thought *achot isha meyabemet* — that a wife's sister could indeed be taken in *yibum*.

### Asei-Lo Ta'aseh Rule

But this *hava amina* is immediately puzzling. The prohibition of a wife's sister carries *kareit* — why would anyone have thought a positive *mitzvah* could override it? The Gemara answers: one might have said *amrinan ati asei ve-dachi lo ta'asei* — we apply the principle that a positive commandment comes and overrides a negative prohibition. Since *yibum* is a *mitzvat asei*, perhaps it overrides the *lo ta'aseh* of a wife's sister.

### Kareit Exception

The Gemara immediately qualifies this: the principle of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh* applies to a plain negative prohibition — *lo ta'aseh gerida* — not one that carries *kareit*. A *lo ta'aseh she-yesh bo kareit* is never overridden by a *mitzvat asei* through this principle alone. But, as the Gemara notes, *yibum* already overrides one *ervah* with *kareit*: the prohibition of *eshet ach*. So the *hava amina* is that since *yibum* overrides one *issur kareit*, perhaps it overrides all *arayot* as well. This is the most compelling version of the *hava amina* — and it is precisely to rule this out that the Torah's word *aleha* is needed.

The Gemara then shifts to a broader inquiry: *lo ta'aseh gerida minalan de-dachi* — where do we actually derive the principle that a positive commandment overrides a plain negative prohibition at all? This broader question launches an extended discussion of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh*, designed ultimately to help us understand the logical foundation of the *hava amina* that *yibum* might have been permitted even in a case of *arayot*.

## Asei Docheh Principle

### Shatnez-Tzitzit Case

The Gemara derives the principle of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh* from the juxtaposition of two laws in the Torah: the prohibition of wearing *shatnez* — *lo tilbash shatnez* — followed immediately by the commandment to make *gedilim*, fringes — the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit*. The juxtaposition teaches that the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* overrides the prohibition of *shatnez*: if one has a linen garment and the only *kasher* thread available for the blue (*techelet*) string is made of wool, the resulting *shatnez* in the *tzitzit* is permitted. The positive commandment of *tzitzit* overrides the negative prohibition of *shatnez*. This is the source for the general principle of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh*.

### Smuchin Validity

This derivation, however, rests on the hermeneutical method of *smuchin* — drawing legal inferences from the juxtaposition of adjacent passages in the Torah. Rabbi Elazar validates this method by citing a verse from Tehillim: *smuchim la'ad le'olam asuyim be'emes ve'yashar* — they are supported forever, made in truth and uprightness. From this the Gemara infers that deriving meaning from textual juxtapositions is a legitimate and enduring hermeneutical tool.

A digression appears here that is worth noting because of its thematic significance: Rav Yosef cites in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah the teaching that a *yevamah* who falls before a *yavam* who is repulsive to her — *mukeh shechin* — is not to be silenced. The source is the juxtaposition of *lo tachsom shor b'disho* — do not muzzle an ox while it threshes — with the passage of *ki yeshvu achim yachdav*, the commandment of *yibum*. This *drasha* via *smuchin* establishes that the woman's objection to *yibum* must be heeded and that *chalitzah*, not *yibum*, must be performed in such a case. This teaching stands in important counterpoint to the broader discussion, which focuses almost entirely on the man's *mitzvah*: right at the outset the Gemara introduces the idea that the woman is not voiceless, and her refusal is treated as a legitimate halachic consideration.

### Yehuda's Smuchin Rule

Rav Yosef then introduces a critical qualification: *afilu l'man d'lo darash smuchin b'alma, b'Mishneh Torah darash* — even those who do not generally apply the method of *smuchin* do apply it in *Mishneh Torah*, the book of Devarim, where laws are repeated in new contexts. This is crucial for the present argument, because the juxtaposition of *shatnez* and *tzitzit* appears in Devarim — meaning that even those who would normally reject *smuchin* as a valid tool accept this particular *drasha* and therefore accept the principle of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh*.

The Gemara then establishes the two sides of this distinction with proof. On the one hand, Rabbi Yehuda *b'alma lo darash* — in general does not draw inferences from *smuchin*. This is demonstrated from a *braita* in which Ben Azzai argues that a *mekhasheifa* (witch) is punishable by *skilah* (stoning) because the passage prohibiting witchcraft is juxtaposed with the passage requiring stoning for *shochev im behemah* (bestiality). Rabbi Yehuda rejects this outright: *v'chi mipnei she-samchu inyan lo notzi zeh l'skilah* — just because the two passages are adjacent, does that mean we should stone the person? For Rabbi Yehuda, *smuchin* alone does not constitute a reliable basis for deriving a *halachah*, particularly one as severe as a capital punishment. Rabbi Yehuda agrees with the conclusion — the *mekhasheifa* is stoned — but derives it differently: *ov v'yidoni b'chlal mekhashefim hayu*, the *ov* and *yidoni* (types of necromancy) were subsumed within the general category of witchcraft; they were singled out and the verse explicitly states *b'even yirgemu otam dmeihem bam* — they are stoned. Since they were singled out from the *klal* not to teach something about themselves alone but to illuminate the whole category, their *din* of *skilah* extends back to all *mekhasheifim*. This is one of the standard *midot* of biblical interpretation — *davar she-haya b'klal v'yatza min ha-klal lelamed* — not *smuchin*.

## Mishneh Torah License

### Aviv Case

The Gemara then turns to the positive proof: *u'b'Mishneh Torah minalan de-darash* — and how do we know Rabbi Yehuda is willing to apply *smuchin* in Devarim? From a *Mishnah*: *nosei adam anusat aviv u'mefutat aviv* — a man may marry a woman his father raped or seduced. The *Mishnah* records that there is no prohibition for a man to marry a woman who was not married to, but merely had sex with, his father (or his son). Rabbi Yehuda, however, disagrees and prohibits *anusat aviv u'mefutat aviv* — a woman the father had sex with outside of marriage.

The Gemara explains Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning. The verse in Devarim states: *lo yikach ish et eshet aviv velo yigaleh knaf aviv* — a man shall not marry his father's wife, and shall not uncover his father's hem. Rabbi Yehuda reads the phrase *velo yigaleh knaf aviv* broadly: *knaf she-ra'ah aviv* — the woman his father "saw" in a state of nakedness, i.e., any woman with whom the father had sexual relations, whether or not she was his wife. This reading is not in itself *smuchin* — it can be understood as the plain meaning of the phrase. But what establishes that the verse is speaking about a case of *anusah* — rape or seduction rather than marriage — is the context supplied by the passage immediately preceding, which discusses the case of a man who rapes a woman and pays her father fifty pieces of silver: *venatan ha-ish ha-shochev imah l'avi ha-na'arah chamishim kasef*. It is the juxtaposition of that passage with *lo yikach ish et eshet aviv* that leads Rabbi Yehuda to read *velo yigaleh knaf aviv* as referring to a non-marital sexual relationship. That is *smuchin* — and it is being applied in Devarim.

The *Rabbanan* push back: if the two passages were immediately adjacent — *im hava samich lei* — you would be right. But as it stands, the passage *lo yikach ish et eshet aviv* intervenes between the rape case and the phrase *velo yigaleh knaf aviv*, making the juxtaposition less direct. According to the Rabbanan, therefore, *velo yigaleh knaf aviv* does not refer to a woman the father had sex with outside of marriage, but rather to a *shomeret yavam* — a woman who stands in a quasi-marital relationship to the father by virtue of *zikah*, the levirate bond. That is, if the father's brother died and left a wife, the father now has a *mitzvah* of *yibum* and a relationship of *zikah* to that woman, who is in a sense quasi-married to him. The verse forbids the son from having relations with her. Notably, the Gemara points out that in this case the prohibition is redundant, because the woman in question is the man's aunt — *she-lavu aleih b'shnei lavin* — there are already two prohibitions covering the case. But regardless, the Rabbanan's interpretation does not rely on *smuchin*.

### Muchach or Mufneh

The Gemara articulates two possible rationales for why *smuchin* is permissible in *Mishneh Torah* while it is not generally used elsewhere. The first is *mishum de-muchach* — because it is evident from the context. If something appears in Devarim in a new context and was not mentioned in its original halachic setting in the earlier books, its presence in the new context must be communicating something that can only be learned from that new context. The fact that it is being placed here, rather than where it naturally belongs, is itself an act of deliberate instruction. The second rationale is *mishum de-mufneh* — because the text contains a superfluous element. If Devarim's formulation includes something beyond what was already stated earlier, that extra element is available to support a *drasha*. The extra word *aviv* in *velo yigaleh knaf aviv* — when the context already makes clear we are speaking of the father — is identified as potentially superfluous. However, as the Gemara notes, the *mufneh* rationale is less distinctively a feature of Devarim and more of a general hermeneutical principle applicable anywhere. The truly interesting claim about *Mishneh Torah* is the *muchach* rationale.

### Repetition Doctrine

The Gemara applies these two rationales equally to the case of *tzitzit* and *shatnez* in Devarim. The *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* had already appeared in Parashat Shelach in Bemidbar; why is it appearing again here in Devarim? If it adds nothing new, its presence in this new context, juxtaposed immediately with the prohibition of *shatnez*, must be teaching something that can be learned only from this juxtaposition — namely, that *tzitzit* overrides *shatnez*. That is the *muchach* argument.

This broader hermeneutical principle is worth emphasizing. The *Gemara* is articulating something significant about how to read Devarim: when *Moshe Rabbeinu* retells and recaps the earlier laws, he sometimes frames them differently, places them in new sequences, and sets them in different contexts. These differences are not incidental. When something that had its natural home in an earlier context reappears in Devarim — particularly in a new setting and alongside different material — the very fact of its relocation is halachically meaningful. It is not mere repetition but deliberate recontextualization, and what can be learned from the new context is as authoritative as anything learned elsewhere.

## Redundancy Resolution

### Two Verbs Needed

The Gemara now anticipates an objection: if the verse about *shatnez* in Devarim is merely superfluous, then it is available for the *drasha* that teaches *asei docheh lo ta'aseh*. But is it truly superfluous? The Gemara argues that in fact both verses about *shatnez* — the one in Vayikra and the one in Devarim — are necessary, because they use different verbs that teach different things.

The verse in Vayikra reads *u'veged kilayim shatnez lo ya'aleh alecha* — a garment of mixed fibers of *shatnez* shall not come up upon you. The verb is *ya'aleh* — putting something upon oneself. The verse in Devarim reads *lo tilbash shatnez* — do not *wear* *shatnez*. The verb is *tilbash* — wearing. Each verse is independently necessary. Had the Torah only said *lo ya'aleh alecha*, one might have thought that any manner of placing the garment on one's body is forbidden, even if it is not in the manner of wearing — for example, merchants who carry multiple garments stacked on their bodies for transport. The verse *lo tilbash* is therefore needed to teach that the prohibition applies *dumya d'levisha* — in a manner similar to wearing, where there is genuine benefit and bodily use. But had the Torah only said *lo tilbash*, one might have thought the prohibition is limited to classic wearing in which one derives direct warmth and comfort. The verse *lo ya'aleh alecha* is needed to extend the prohibition to cases like covering oneself with a blanket — a form of bodily covering that, while not precisely *levishab*, still constitutes a meaningful form of benefit. The two verbs together define the scope of the prohibition: it must resemble wearing in that there is genuine benefit, but it is not limited to the narrow act of wearing.

### Wool-Linen Repeat

The Gemara then argues that the phrase *tzemer u'fishtim yachdav* — wool and linen together — in the Devarim verse is also necessary and not superfluous. One might object: surely this phrase is the *least* superfluous element in the verse, since without it we would not know what *shatnez* is composed of. The Gemara responds that in fact this could have been derived independently. *Tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* teaches: wherever the Torah speaks of *begadim* generically, it means garments of wool or linen, because in one instance — the laws of *tzara'at* — the Torah explicitly specifies *beged tzemer o beged pishtim*, a garment of wool or a garment of linen. Since *beged* always denotes wool or linen, and *shatnez* involves mixing two fabrics, the only possible mixture is wool and linen. One would therefore know that *shatnez* is wool and linen from *Rabbi Yishmael*'s principle, without needing the Devarim verse to spell it out.

Nevertheless, the Gemara argues that *tzemer u'fishtim* in Devarim is genuinely necessary, and not merely for the reason just given. The two verses use different verbs: Vayikra's *lo ya'aleh* describes a lesser form of bodily contact with less benefit; Devarim's *lo tilbash* describes full wearing with substantial benefit. One might therefore have supposed that when the prohibition is of the more serious kind — full wearing, *de-nafish hana'ah*, where the pleasure is greater — perhaps the definition of *shatnez* is broader, encompassing any mixture of fabrics, not only wool and linen. After all, the greater the severity of the act, the more one might expect a broader prohibition. *Tzemer u'fishtim* in Devarim is therefore needed to reaffirm explicitly that even in the case of full wearing, the definition of *shatnez* remains identical — it is wool and linen, no more.

### Superfluous Drasha

The Gemara appears to have demonstrated that both verses are fully necessary, leaving nothing superfluous to support the *drasha* of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh*. But the Gemara resolves this with a final move: *im ken lishtok kera minei — shatnez shatnez*. Even if one concedes that *tzemer u'fishtim* is needed to define *shatnez* in the context of *levishab*, it was not necessary for Devarim to repeat the word *shatnez* itself. The definition of *shatnez* as wool and linen could have been derived by *gezeirah shavah* — verbal analogy — between the word *shatnez* appearing in Vayikra and the same word *shatnez* appearing in Devarim. Therefore, the actual statement *tzemer u'fishtim yachdav* is superfluous: it communicates nothing that could not have been derived from the *gezeirah shavah* of the shared term *shatnez*. It is this superfluous phrase that is available for the *drasha*, establishing via the juxtaposition with *tzitzit* that a *mitzvat asei* overrides a *lo ta'aseh*.

The full arc of the argument is now in view. The Gemara has sought to explain why a *pasuk* was needed to tell us that *yibum* does not override *arayot*. The answer is rooted in the principle of *asei docheh lo ta'aseh*, derived from the juxtaposition of *shatnez* and *tzitzit* in Devarim. The Gemara has established that this derivation via *smuchin* is accepted by all — even by those such as Rabbi Yehuda who do not generally apply *smuchin* — because the juxtaposition occurs in *Mishneh Torah*, where the tools of *muchach* (evident new context) and *mufneh* (superfluous element) license such inferences. And it has worked carefully to identify what is genuinely superfluous in the *shatnez* verses so that something remains available to anchor the *drasha*. With this foundation established, the Gemara will continue to build toward explaining precisely why the *mitzvah* of *yibum* was thought capable of overriding *arayot* — and why ultimately it does not.
