# Keritot 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Awareness & Chattat

### Torah's Language

The overarching concern of this new *perek* flows directly from the Torah's distinctive language when it introduces the obligation to bring a *chatat*: *o hoda alav chatato* — "or his sin becomes known to him." This phrasing is suggestive on two levels simultaneously. On one hand, it implies that someone else might inform a person of his sin; on the other, it carries the connotation that ultimately the person himself must acknowledge and own up to what he has done. The entire *perek* is therefore organized around a central tension: how much must the sinner himself be personally aware that he has transgressed, and to what degree can external testimony substitute for that personal awareness?

### One Witness Rule

The *mishna* on the previous *daf* addressed the case where one witness, or any testimony short of two witnesses, informs a person that he has sinned. The conclusion there is that the sinner is obligated to bring a *chatat* only if he responds with silence — which is interpreted as implicit acknowledgment. In other words, even in this scenario, he is not bringing the *chatat* on the basis of the other party's testimony as such, but rather on the strength of his own implicit admission. The testimony of a single witness serves only as the occasion that elicits his personal acknowledgment.

### Tosafot Standard

Tosafot pushes this standard even further. If a witness informs someone that he sinned and the person responds "I don't know" — meaning, perhaps you are right, I cannot personally confirm or deny it — Tosafot holds that this is insufficient. The obligation to bring a *chatat* requires an explicit or implicit positive acknowledgment that one has sinned. Silence functions as acknowledgment; "I don't know" does not.

## Two Witnesses Debate

### Kal Vachomer

The debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* centers on a considerably more fraught case: two witnesses testify that a person has sinned, yet he contradicts them and denies it. Two witnesses ordinarily constitute incontrovertible proof under Jewish law. The question is whether even that level of objective proof can compel someone to bring a *chatat* when he himself actively denies the transgression, or whether his own personal acceptance remains an indispensable element of the *korban* process.

Rabbi Meir advances a *kal vachomer*: if two witnesses can bring a person to the death penalty — surely the gravest consequence imaginable — then they can certainly bring him to the comparatively minor consequence of having to bring a *korban*. The objective fact of the transgression, established by two witnesses, is sufficient.

### Claiming Intent

The *chachamim* disagree, and the *mishna* states their reasoning as follows: *im ratza lomar meizid hayiti* — he could have said "I acted intentionally." Had he claimed intent, he would have been exempt from a *korban*, since a *chatat* is only brought for inadvertent sin. Since he had this avenue of escape available to him and chose not to use it, the *chachamim* hold that his denial still has operative force.

### Two Reasons Query

The *gemara* immediately poses a fundamental question: *mai ta'ama de-rabbanan* — what is the actual reason of the *chachamim* for exempting this person? Is it *mishum de-adam ne'eman al atzmo yoter mi-me'ah ish* — because a person is simply believed about himself more than a hundred witnesses — or is it *mishum de-amrinan migo* — because we apply the principle of *migo*, meaning that since he could have avoided obligation by claiming intent, we extend that latitude to cover his outright denial as well?

---

## Chachamim Rationale

### Self-Belief Approach

Both of these possibilities, the *gemara* acknowledges, are somewhat unusual. Normally when we say that a person is believed about himself — *hoda'at ba'al din ke-me'ah edim dami* — it is in the context of self-incrimination: I admit I owe you money, and that admission is treated like the testimony of a hundred witnesses because I am obligating myself. The credibility flows from the fact that people do not ordinarily obligate themselves falsely. Here, by contrast, the person is *exculpating* himself — saying "I didn't do it" — so why should the ordinary principle of self-belief apply?

### Atonement Qualifier

Rashi and Tosafot immediately qualify the principle. In this particular context, they explain, a person is believed about himself because we do not think he would lie when the issue at stake is his own atonement before God. A person would naturally want to be forgiven; there is therefore a presumption that when it comes to matters of *korban* and atonement, his self-report carries special weight — people do not lie about such things. That is why in this specific circumstance we believe him even against witnesses.

### You & God

There is, however, another and arguably more penetrating way to understand why the individual is believed in this context, and it does not rest solely on the assumption that people don't lie about atonement. Consider two independent reasons.

The first goes back to the *pasuk* itself — *o hoda alav* — and the requirement of *ve-hitvada*, acknowledgment and confession. Even if we believe the witnesses entirely, the very structure of bringing a *chatat* requires that the sinner himself own up to the transgression. *Hoda'at ha-chet*, personal admission of sin, is intrinsic to the *korban* process; it is not merely a factual finding to be established by evidence. If that is correct, then the question of "do we believe you or the witnesses" is somewhat beside the point — even granting everything the witnesses say, the *chatat* cannot be compelled without his own acceptance. The *gemara*'s two formulations do not include this as a separate option, though it remains a coherent reading of the *chachamim*'s position.

The second reason, which perhaps fits better with the *gemara*'s language of *ne'eman al atzmo*, draws on a principle established in *Erchin*: *chayavei chataot ein memashkenin osam* — we do not seize the property of those obligated in *chataot* to compel them to bring the offering. The reason is that this is not the business of the court. If you have sinned in a way that triggers civil or criminal liability, *beis din* gets involved. But the *chatat* is not a punishment — it is about *teshuvah*, about your relationship with God. That is fundamentally between you and God. If that is why we do not compel a person to bring a *chatat* even when we know he is obligated, it also explains why when we must decide whom to believe, the testimony of witnesses carries less weight. Witnesses are a mechanism of the court. If this matter is not fundamentally a court matter — if it is between you and God — then why should witnesses determine the outcome? The primary question is what you say. This reading makes a strong statement, but it may be the most coherent account of the first approach.

### Migo Reinterpretation

The second approach offered by the *gemara* is *migo*: since the person could have avoided the *korban* by claiming intentionality, we believe him even when he denies the act entirely. However, this formulation faces two serious objections. First, claiming intentionality — *meizid hayiti* — would make a person look terrible; it paints him as a willful sinner, something virtually no one would want to say about himself. Why should we treat this as a realistic alternative he could have invoked? Second, and more fundamentally, the general rule is *migo bemakom eidim lo amrinan* — we do not apply *migo* to override the testimony of witnesses. Two witnesses constitute the strongest form of proof available; the mere fact that a person could have made a different claim does not ordinarily suffice to overturn their testimony.

### Tosafot Refinement

Tosafot therefore clarifies that this is not *migo* in its classical sense. What the *gemara* really means, and what it will elaborate momentarily, is the concept of *mefarshinan diburei* — we reinterpret what the person said. When he says "I didn't do it," we do not take that as a categorical denial that we then pit against the witnesses. Rather, we reframe the statement: "I didn't do it" means "I didn't do it inadvertently" — I did it intentionally, and therefore I am not obligated in a *korban*. The witnesses' testimony that he ate forbidden fat, for instance, is not directly contradicted; we simply read his words as a claim about intent rather than about the act itself. This is *mefarshinan diburei* — reframing the statement — not *migo* in the formal sense.

The underlying question remains: why are we so invested in reinterpreting his words in his favor against the witnesses? The answer loops back to what was said before: bringing a *korban* is ultimately more an issue between the individual and God than a matter for the court and its evidentiary procedures. That motivates us to stretch as far as we reasonably can to accommodate what he said, short of directly ignoring the witnesses entirely.

## Tumah Test Cases

### Categorical Belief

The *gemara* now asks: *u-mai nafka mina* — what practical difference does it make which approach we adopt? The answer: it matters for cases of *tuma*, ritual impurity. If the *chachamim*'s reason is that a person is simply believed about himself more than a hundred witnesses — categorically, without qualification — then it would make no difference whether the case involves old or new *tuma*. In either scenario, we would simply believe him over the witnesses.

### Old Tumah Reframe

But if the reason is *migo* — meaning, really, that we believe him because we can reinterpret his words in a way that removes him from obligation — then the result depends on whether that reinterpretation is available. Consider: witnesses say to a person that he became *tamei* and subsequently ate a *korban* or entered the *Beis HaMikdash*. He says, "I did not become *tamei*." Now, unlike the case of eating forbidden fat — where "I didn't eat it" can be reinterpreted as "I didn't eat it *b'shogeg*, I did it *bemezid*" — saying "I didn't become *tamei*" cannot be reinterpreted along those lines. The issue is not the manner in which he became *tamei*; the issue is the transgression of entering the *Beis HaMikdash* or eating the *korban* while *tamei*.

However, there is one available reinterpretation: "I did not become *tamei*" can be read as "I did not *remain tamei*" — meaning, I went to the *mikvah*. If witnesses said he became *tamei* yesterday — *tuma yeshana*, old *tuma* — and he ate a *korban* today, then it is entirely plausible that in the intervening time he immersed in a *mikvah*. In that case, his statement can be reinterpreted meaningfully to get him out of the obligation.

### New Tumah Blocked

But if witnesses say he became *tamei* today and ate the *korban* today — *tuma chadasha*, new *tuma* — then there is no exit path. Even if he claims he went to the *mikvah*, since nightfall has not yet arrived, immersion alone does not restore full *tahara*. He is still *tamei* and would still have transgressed. There is no way to reinterpret the words "I did not become *tamei*" in a manner that gets him off the hook. Therefore, under the second approach — where we believe him only insofar as we can reinterpret his words — he would be obligated to bring a *korban* in the case of new *tuma*, even if he denies it.

## Braita & Rav Ami

### One Witness Braita

The *gemara* now introduces a *braita* in an attempt to resolve the question. The *braita* states: *amru lo ed echad nitmeta* — one witness said to him "you became *tamei*," *ve-hu omer lo nitmeiti* — and he says "I did not become *tamei*" — *patur*, he is exempt. The *braita* then raises the possibility: *yachol afilu shenayim* — perhaps even if two witnesses say so, he would still be exempt? Rabbi Meir responds with the *kal vachomer* familiar from our *mishna*: if two witnesses can bring a person to the death penalty, they can certainly obligate him in a *korban*. The *chachamim*, by contrast, state: *adam ne'eman al atzmo yoter mi-me'ah ish* — a person is believed about himself more than a hundred people.

This *braita* appears to be strong evidence for the first approach — the categorical one. The *chachamim* state their reason directly as "a person is believed more than a hundred people," without any mention of reinterpreting words or of the *migo* framework. Moreover, the case involves *tuma*, a scenario where — as we just analyzed — the reinterpretation path is not obviously available. The *braita* seems to be saying that even there, we simply believe him, categorically, because this is between him and God.

### Rav Ami Limits

Rav Ami, however, maintains that the second approach — reinterpretation — remains the operative one, and he limits the *braita* accordingly. He argues: *le-olam ta'ameihu de-rabbanan mishum de-amrinan migo* — the real reason of the *rabbanan* is always because of the *migo* framework. The *braita*, he explains, should be read as implicitly involving a case of *old tuma*, not new *tuma*. In that scenario, *mitoch she-im ratza lomar lo amadeti be-tumati patur* — since he could have said "I did not remain *tamei*, I went to the *mikvah*" and been exempt, we therefore accept that reading when he says "I did not become *tamei*." The *braita*'s statement that he is believed "more than a hundred people" does not mean categorical credibility; it means that in *this* case, where such reinterpretation is available, we side with him over the witnesses.

What this demonstrates, the *gemara* notes, is that the initial question the *gemara* posed was not merely theoretical but genuinely reflected different bodies of tannaitic material. The *mishna* frames the *chachamim*'s reasoning in terms of reinterpreting available statements — "he could have said *meizid*" — while the *braita* frames it as categorical belief. Rav Ami's resolution insists they are not contradictory; the *braita* is simply addressing a case where the reinterpretation happens to be available.

## Ravina's Technique

### Witness Emphasis

Ravina adds a further technical point that sharpens the limits of reinterpretation. He proposes a scenario where even *old tuma* does not provide the expected exit. The case: witnesses do not merely testify that a person became *tamei*. Rather, they emphasize the specific act of wrongdoing — *achalta kodashim be-tum'at ha-guf* — "you ate *kodashim* while in a state of bodily *tuma*." The focus of the testimony is on the eating, with the *tuma* appearing as the background condition. The person responds: *lo nitmeiti* — I was not *tamei*.

### Non-Responsive Issue

Now, normally we would reinterpret *lo nitmeiti* as *lo amadeti be-tumati* — I did not remain *tamei*, I went to the *mikvah*. But Ravina argues: *dehacha lo metartzinan diburei* — here we cannot reinterpret his words. The problem is one of responsiveness. When witnesses say "you ate while *tamei*," the statement that directly responds to their charge — the one that would actually exculpate him — would be something like "when I ate, I had already gone to the *mikvah*." That is the answer that addresses what they are saying. But his actual statement, "I was not *tamei*," does not directly respond to their claim about eating; it addresses only the background condition.

To construct the exculpatory statement from his words, we would have to infer: "I was not *tamei*" → "I did not remain *tamei*" → "I had already immersed" → "therefore when I ate, I was *tahor*." That chain of inference does not represent a reinterpretation of what he said; it requires constructing a new, responsive statement. And that new statement — "I went to the *mikvah*" — is itself in tension with his original claim of "I was not *tamei*," since saying "I went to the *mikvah*" implicitly acknowledges that he *had* become *tamei* in the first place. He would thus be contradicting his original statement. Since reinterpretation must work *with* what was originally said rather than against it, the *mefarshinan diburei* mechanism is unavailable here.

### Contradiction Limit

The upshot of Ravina's analysis is a principled limitation on the reinterpretation approach: we can reinterpret a person's statement for his benefit only when the reinterpreted meaning can be derived organically from the words he actually used and without requiring him to contradict himself. Where the only responsive reading would require constructing a new statement that implicitly undermines his original one, the reinterpretation mechanism fails. In such a case, the *chachamim*'s middle position cannot rescue him, and he would be obligated in a *korban*.

## Three Positions

### Rabbi Meir View

Drawing the full analysis together, three distinct positions emerge. Rabbi Meir holds that the objective testimony of witnesses is essentially determinative. One brings a *chatat* when witnesses establish the fact of the transgression, and the person's own denial is insufficient to override that. Rabbi Meir does, as we will see, recognize narrow exceptions, but his basic stance is that witnesses govern.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

Rabbi Yehuda takes the opposite extreme: *ne'eman adam al atzmo yoter me'ah ish* — a person is believed about himself more than a hundred people, categorically, when it comes to *korban* obligation. This is the position that emerges most forcefully from the *braita*, and it rests on the foundational principle that the *chatat* is between the individual and God, not a matter for the court and its evidentiary apparatus. Witnesses simply have no role to play in this domain.

### Middle Position

The middle position — reflecting the *chachamim* in the *braita*'s presentation of the debate — holds that neither extreme is correct. We are invested in believing the individual over the witnesses because the *chatat* is ultimately a matter between him and God. But we cannot completely ignore witness testimony. We will therefore try as hard as possible to reinterpret what the person said in order to make his words compatible with not being obligated in a *korban*. If such reinterpretation is available and coherent, we will go by what he said. Only when no such reinterpretation is available — when his words cannot be made to work for him no matter how we read them — will we fall back on the witnesses.

## Halacha & Privacy

### Rav Nachman Ruling

Rav Nachman rules: *halachah ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — we rule like Rabbi Yehuda. We follow the categorical position that a person is believed about himself more than a hundred witnesses when the question concerns his personal *korban* obligation. This is, as noted, the strongest of the three positions.

### Privacy Limit

Rav Yosef immediately qualifies this ruling. Rav Nachman's ruling applies only *beino l'vein atzmo u'l'atzmo* — only when the matter is truly between the person and himself, confined to his own private sphere. It does not extend to situations where his claim intersects with the interests of others or with public observance.

### Public Visibility Issue

Rashi explains the force of this qualification practically. Suppose a person denies having become *tamei*, and under Rav Nachman's ruling we believe him for the purposes of the *korban*. If he then proceeds to take out his *terumah* food and eat it publicly in front of those who heard the witnesses testify, he creates a serious problem. Everyone present believes him to be *tamei*, based on the witness testimony they heard. Watching him eat *terumah* — a food restricted to *kohanim* who are *tahor* — while in their perception he is *tamei*, undermines the seriousness and integrity of the *halacha* of *terumah be-tumah*. Even though he personally knows he is *tahor*, he cannot act publicly in a way that appears to brazenly violate the prohibition before those who believe the witnesses.

### Private vs. Public

This generates a genuinely striking principle. The ruling that a person is believed about himself over witnesses operates only in the private space between him and God. If he knows he is *tahor* and nobody is watching, he may eat his *terumah* in private, because that act remains purely between him and God. Similarly, the question of whether he brings a *chatat* remains between him and God. But this private credibility cannot be extended to actions that visibly affect others or carry public consequences.

There are two distinct applications of this limit. The first is the visibility issue: even if one is personally confident in one's *tahara*, one may not eat *terumah* in front of people who believe the witnesses, because it appears to undermine the *halacha* publicly. The second is more substantive: if the question concerns *other people's* property or interests — for instance, whether he can now touch *Michael's* *terumah* — he is *not* believed over the witnesses. Others must go by the objective testimony. He is believed for his own *korban* and for his own private consumption; he is not believed in a way that would impose consequences on others who have legitimately relied on the witness testimony. And the *gemara* confirms this: one may give *terumah* to a *kohen* who has declared himself *tahor* against witness testimony, but once he touches the *terumah*, others would have to treat it as potentially *tamei*.

## Rabbi Meir Exceptions

### Shifcha Charufa

Having established the general positions and the *halachic* ruling, the *gemara* turns to cases where even Rabbi Meir would concede that witnesses can be set aside. Reish Lakish opens: *modeh Rabbi Meir la-chachamim* — Rabbi Meir agrees with the *chachamim* — in the case of a *shifcha charufa*, a half-freed slave. If two witnesses say he had relations with a *shifcha charufa* and he denies it, he is believed. Why would Rabbi Meir concede here? Because his denial can be reinterpreted in a way that actually reflects well on him, not badly: *lo gamarti bi'ati* — I did not complete the act. The obligation in a *korban* for *shifcha charufa* applies only to full penetration, *gmar bi'ah*. His "I didn't do it" can be naturally and favorably read as "I did not complete the act" — a plausible, not far-fetched, reinterpretation that requires no self-incrimination whatsoever. Even Rabbi Meir, who generally insists on going by the witnesses, is prepared to allow the reinterpretation when it works this cleanly.

### Nazir Tamei

Rav Sheshet adds another case: *nazir tamei*. If two witnesses say that a *nazir* became *tamei* and he denies it, he is exempt. The reasoning is *d'i ba'ei amar nish'alti al nezirai* — he could have said that he had his *nezirus* annulled entirely by a *chacham*, in which case there would have been no ongoing *nazir* status to violate through *tuma*. This is admittedly somewhat unusual as a reinterpretation, since saying one's *nezirus* was annulled carries its own implications. But the operative principle is the same: where a credible alternative reading of his denial exists — one that removes the predicate of the entire obligation — Rabbi Meir would be prepared to work with it.

### Unknown Testimony

Abaye adds a third case: if two witnesses say to a person *yode'a ata be-eidus ploni* — you know testimony that could help so-and-so in court — and he swore that he did not know such testimony and they are now contradicting him, he would nonetheless be exempt from a *korban*. Why? *Lo niskavanti l'eidus* — he could have said he never intended to serve as a witness. The *gemara* in *Makkot* and elsewhere establishes that if a person saw something without intending to function as a witness, his testimony would not be accepted in court. So his claim "I don't know testimony" can be reinterpreted as "I don't know usable testimony — I never established in my own mind the intent to testify." Since that reinterpretation is available, Rabbi Meir agrees that witnesses do not prevail.

The broader point emerging from all three cases is that Rabbi Meir's position is not as absolute as it might initially appear. His insistence on going by the witnesses is not categorically inflexible. Rather, it is the default when no credible reinterpretation of the person's words is available. When a reasonable reinterpretation *does* exist — particularly one that reflects well on the person or removes the predicate of the obligation — even Rabbi Meir is prepared to leverage it. The difference between Rabbi Meir and the middle position of the *chachamim* is therefore more a matter of degree than of kind: Rabbi Meir requires a very clean, obvious reinterpretation before setting aside witnesses, while the *chachamim* are more expansive in finding ways to read the person's words in his favor.

## Multiple Sins Count

### One Sin Repeated

The *gemara* now transitions to the second major topic of the *perek*: how many *korbanos* must one bring when multiple violations occur? The *mishna* establishes that if one ate multiple portions of *cheilev* (forbidden fat) in a single state of forgetfulness — *achal cheilev v'cheilev b'he'elam achat* — one brings only a single *korban*.

### Zeira Question

Rav Zeira challenges this. If a person ate two *kezaysim* of *cheilev*, that means he committed what might be thought of as two acts of the same sin. Why, then, is he obligated in only one *korban*? Let each *kezayis* give rise to its own *chatat*.

### Abaye Answer

Abaye responds with the governing principle: *he'elamot mecholakot* — it is states of forgetfulness, not individual quantities consumed, that divide and multiply sin. A single unbroken state of forgetfulness constitutes one unified act, regardless of how much was eaten within it. The *kezayis* defines the *minimum* required for any obligation to arise; once the minimum is met, eating additional quantities within the same forgetfulness does not multiply the *chatat*. One act of forgetting equals one act of sinning equals one *korban*.

There is an illuminating parallel to the laws of lashes: if witnesses warn a person not to eat a particular item and he proceeds to eat a hundred *kezaysim* in one continuous act, he receives only one set of lashes, not a hundred. The only way to generate multiple sets of lashes is if a new warning is issued after each eating, which then redefines each subsequent act as its own transgression. Similarly here, the only mechanism for multiplying the *chatat* within the same type of sin is an intervening moment of awareness that breaks the state of forgetfulness — and then a return to forgetting. That pattern of forgetting, remembering, and forgetting again generates distinct acts, each requiring its own *korban*.

---

## Different Sins

### Multiple Sin Types

If, however, one commits multiple categorically different transgressions within a single state of forgetfulness — eating *cheilev*, drinking *dam*, eating *pigul*, eating *nosar* — one brings a separate *korban* for each distinct prohibition violated. The unity of the forgetfulness does not merge different *issurim* into a single *chatat*. Each sin carries its own independent obligation, and a single forgetfulness cannot collapse distinct categories of transgression into one.

## Half-Measures

### Same Dish Combine

The *mishna* presents a further scenario: eating two half-*kezayis* portions. If both halves were of *cheilev* from the same dish, they combine to form a full *kezayis* and one brings a *korban*. This is straightforward — same prohibition, same preparation, same dish; the two halves add up to a single obligating act.

### Different Dishes Query

Reish Lakish in the name of Bar Tutini raises the more subtle case: what if both halves were *cheilev*, same prohibition, but they came from two different dishes — one from a stew and one from a roast? The question is whether the fact that they were prepared differently, and thus represent distinct culinary preparations of the same forbidden substance, prevents their combination into one *kezayis* sufficient to obligate a *korban*.

### Yehoshua Separates

The question arises specifically against the backdrop of Rabbi Yehoshua's position, which holds that *tamchuyin mechulakin* — different dishes are legally separate. Rabbi Yehoshua's stated view is that if a person ate a full *kezayis* of *cheilev* from a stew and a full *kezayis* from a roast, he brings *two* separate *korbanos*, not one. The different preparation distinguishes them sufficiently to generate independent obligations.

### Yehoshua Scope

The question is whether Rabbi Yehoshua's principle operates symmetrically in both directions. When he says different dishes are separate, does he mean this uniformly — both to create additional obligations (*le-chumra*) when each dish contributes a full *kezayis*, and to eliminate any obligation (*le-kula*) when each dish contributes only a half-*kezayis* that otherwise would have combined? Or does the principle apply only stringently — to create multiple *korbanos* from multiple full *kezaysim* — but not leniently, so that two half-*kezaysim* from different dishes would still combine to create a single obligation?

### Mishna Interpretations

The *gemara* presents two opposing readings of the *mishna* on this point. The first reading focuses on the opening clause — *mimin echad chayav*, from the same type one is obligated — and reads the case as involving different dishes. According to this reading, the *mishna* is telling us that even Rabbi Yehoshua would agree that two half-*kezaysim* of the same *issur* combine, even from different dishes. That is, Rabbi Yehoshua applies his distinction only *le-chumra* and not *le-kula*. The second reading focuses on the concluding clause — *mi-shnei minim patur*, from two types one is exempt — and understands even that ruling as a case of *same type but different dishes*, following Rabbi Yehoshua's view. According to this reading, Rabbi Yehoshua's principle is fully symmetrical: different dishes separate, whether the result is to multiply *korbanos* or to prevent the combination of two halves.

### Awareness Mid-Act

Ravina then raises yet another variant: what if a person is in the middle of eating — having consumed a half-*kezayis* — and at that point becomes aware of the prohibition, then forgets again and proceeds to eat the second half-*kezayis*? Does that intervening moment of awareness prevent the two halves from combining into a single obligating act? The question parallels the one about different dishes: just as we asked whether different dishes divide what would otherwise be a single act, we now ask whether awareness-and-forgetting mid-eating can divide two halves.

### Gamliel vs. Chachamim

This question is addressed by the dispute between Rabban Gamliel and the *chachamim*. Rabban Gamliel holds *ein yediya le-chatzi shiur* — awareness of having done only a half-measure is not legally significant awareness. One is not yet aware of having *sinned*, since the minimum threshold for the prohibition has not yet been reached. Therefore, the intervening moment of awareness does not divide the act; the two halves combine, and he is obligated in a *korban*. The *chachamim*, by contrast, hold *yeish yediya le-chatzi shiur* — awareness at any point, even mid-act and before the full *shiur* is consumed, is meaningful and divides the act into two separate halves that no longer combine.

The parallel with the writing of two letters on *Shabbos* — one in the morning, one in the evening, with intervening awareness in between — is instructive. Rabban Gamliel obligates in that case as well, consistent with his view that awareness before completion of the full prohibited act does not sever the continuity of the transgression.

## Continuous Action

### Time Gap Question

The *mishna* now introduces the principle that governs all cases of eating that spans multiple moments: *kama yishei b'ochlin* — how much of a time gap between acts of eating still allows them to be counted as one continuous act? This principle has implications far beyond the laws of *korban*; it is the foundational framework for *Yom Kippur* fasting, eating *matzah* on *Pesach*, and numerous other *mitzvos* and prohibitions defined by a single act of eating.

## Continuous Action…

### Meir: Kalyos

Rabbi Meir describes the standard as eating in the manner of *kalyos* — consuming small amounts in steady, continuous succession, the way one might eat sunflower seeds, with no meaningful break between one piece and the next. This formulation will be unpacked by the *gemara* in terms of whether Rabbi Meir is being more lenient or more strict than the *chachamim*.

### Chachamim: Pras

The *chachamim* set the standard as: *mi-techila v'ad sof ke-dei achilas pras* — from the beginning to the end, the total time must not exceed the time it takes to eat a *pras*, half a loaf of bread. How large is a *pras*? Rashi and the Rambam dispute the number of *kezaysim* it represents: Rashi holds eight *kezaysim*, the Rambam holds nine. In practical terms, one is eating a single *kezayis* within the time it would normally take to consume eight or nine times that quantity — a substantial window, commonly estimated at approximately four minutes, though there is debate.

### Duration vs. Break

There is, however, a major dispute between the *rishonim* over what exactly is being measured. The straightforward reading of the phrase — "from the beginning to the end, the time of *achilas pras*" — is that we measure the total duration from when one starts eating to when one finishes. This is how Rabbeinu Gershom explicitly understands it. Tosafot, by contrast, reads *mi-techila v'ad sof ke-dei achilas pras* as measuring the permissible *break* between one act of eating and the next — not the total duration of eating from start to finish, but the maximum interruption allowed between two portions before they are considered separate acts. This is a significant interpretive dispute with far-reaching practical consequences: are we limiting the total time window of a single act of eating, or limiting the gap between discrete sub-acts within that eating?

## Yom Kippur & Matzah

### Food & Wine Acts

The *mishna* extends the principle to two additional cases. First: if a person ate *tamei* food, which renders the eater *tamei*, or drank ritually *tamei* liquids, the *halacha* is that eating *tamei* food causes the person himself to become *tamei*. Second: if a person drank a *revi'is* of wine — the quantity that defines drunkenness — and subsequently entered the *Beis HaMikdash*, he is liable. The *mishna* establishes that in both cases, the relevant consumption must occur within the span of *ke-dei achilas pras* for it to be counted as a single, consequential act of eating or drinking.

### Eliezer Exception

Rabbi Eliezer adds a qualification that applies specifically to the case of drunkenness and the *revi'is* of wine. Since the issue in that case is not the inherent *issur* of the food or drink but rather the *status* of drunkenness — which is what disqualifies one from entering the *Mikdash* — additional factors can interrupt or negate that status. If he took any break while drinking the wine, or if any water was mixed into the wine at any point, Rabbi Eliezer holds that he is *patur* — he is not considered drunk simply by virtue of having consumed a *revi'is*. The concern is whether he has reached the state of being *shikur*, drunk; a break or dilution prevents the accumulation from producing that state. This is a self-contained rule specific to the drunkenness case and does not affect the broader analysis of what defines a single act of eating in other contexts.
