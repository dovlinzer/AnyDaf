# Avodah Zarah 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Torah & Future Reward

### Intro & Themes

Daf 5b opens approximately ten lines down, at the *siman* beginning *yom me'itaref ba'agal*, which serves as a heading for the various discussions to follow, after which the *gemara* proceeds *gufa*. The bulk of what we encounter here is *aggadata* — and this *aggadata* is, in a meaningful sense, the theological and moral framing of *masechet Avodah Zarah* as a whole.

The *masechet* is ultimately about navigating a world that is largely non-Jewish, often pagan, and sometimes hostile. The *aggadata* addresses the logical and emotional challenges this raises: the nations seem to prosper; we seem to suffer; their accomplishments appear impressive. The opening sections of the *masechet* have already introduced several major themes in response. First, in the future time the nations will be punished, and even their great achievements — building roads, conquering kingdoms — will be granted no credit, not even their claim that such works were done to facilitate Torah study. Second, and relatedly, what truly matters is Torah: learning it, performing its *mitzvos*. Third, the punishment of the nations will ultimately be harsher than our suffering, and our present suffering is directed toward our ultimate reward. These themes continue now as the *gemara* resumes.

### Today vs. Tomorrow

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: what is meant by the *pasuk*, *asher anochi metzavcha hayom la'asotam* — that I command you today to do them? *Hayom la'asotam velo lemachar la'asotam*: today is the time to perform the *mitzvos*, not tomorrow. The *lemachar* here carries a double meaning — not only "don't delay" in the ordinary sense, but that the *atid lavo*, the *olam haba*, is not the time for doing *mitzvos*. And correspondingly: *hayom la'asotam velo hayom litol secharam* — in this world you perform the *mitzvos*, but you do not receive their reward here. The reward belongs to *olam haba*. This directly addresses the question of why those who devote themselves to Torah and *mitzvos* do not always appear as materially successful as the surrounding nations.

### Mitzvot as Witness

*Va'amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: all the *mitzvos* we perform in this world will come and testify to our righteousness in the world to come — *kol mitzvos she'osin ba'olam hazeh bo'os umididots osam la'olam haba*. Even if in this world we are mocked or degraded by surrounding nations, in the end the *mitzvos* will bear witness to our rightness. As the *pasuk* says: *yitnu adeihem veyitzdaku yishme'u veyomru emet* — they will give their testimony and be found righteous, they will hear and say it is true. *Eilu Yisrael* — these are Israel, who will demonstrate through a life of Torah and *mitzvos* that they are righteous. *Eilu ovdei kochavim* — it is the idolaters who will ultimately acknowledge this. The formulation *adeihem* also echoes back to the word *yom edam* cited at the very opening of the *masechet*, a deliberate textual resonance.

### Mitzvot Rebuke Nations

A third statement from *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: all the *mitzvos* we perform in this world will not only vindicate us but will come and *slap the nations in the face* — *bo'os vetorfas al pneichem*. As the *pasuk* says: *ushmartem va'asitem ki hi chochmatchem uvinatchem le'einei ha'amim* — you shall observe and perform them, for this is your wisdom and understanding in the eyes of the nations. Note that the *pasuk* says *le'einei ha'amim*, in their eyes, not *neged ha'amim*, opposite the nations. *Melamed she-ba'at v-tarpas bifneihem* — it is as if the *mitzvos* smack the nations right in their face, demonstrating that their own lives have been worthless and that we were the truly wise ones.

One might note that the plain sense of that *pasuk* — *ki chachmatchem uvinatchem le'einei ha'amim asher yishme'un es kol hachukim v'omru rak am chacham v'navon hagoy hagadol hazeh* — seems to describe the nations recognizing Jewish wisdom *in this world*. Yet the *gemara* consistently places all of this vindication *le'asid lavo*, in the future time.

## Cheit HaEgel

### Gate for Repentance

*Va'amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: the Israelites made the *eigel* — the Golden Calf — only in order to open a *pischon peh*, an opening, for *ba'alei teshuvah*. It is worth noting that *pischon peh* ordinarily carries a negative connotation — one does not want to give a *pischon peh* to the *resha'im* or to the *apikorsim*. Here, however, the *pischon peh* is precisely what is needed: an opening for those who seek to return.

The argument is rooted in the *pasuk*: *mi yitein vehayah levavam zeh lahem l'yira osi kol hayamim* — who would only give that their heart would be like this, to fear Me all the days. This was said immediately after *Matan Torah*, when they stood at an extraordinary level of *yirat Hashem*. How could it be that within forty days they descended to worshipping the *eigel*? The very incomprehensibility of the sin suggests that it was allowed to happen only to demonstrate that even from the greatest heights, even after the most catastrophic fall, the gates of *teshuvah* remain open. Whether *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* means this was literally the Israelites' motivation, or rather that this was the ultimate purpose served by the event being permitted, is itself a question — but the theological function is clear.

### David & Israel

This connects to what *Rabbi Yochanan* says in the name of *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*: *lo David ra'uy l'oso ma'aseh* — David was not at a level where he should have been capable of that act with *Bas Sheva*, and *lo Yisrael re'uyim l'oso ma'aseh* — the Israelites at *Har Sinai* were not at a level where they should have been capable of the sin of the *eigel*. How do we know this about David? *V'libi chalal b'kirbi* — my heart is as if emptied within me, meaning he was entirely free of lust and *yetzer hara*. How do we know this about Israel? From the very same *pasuk*: *mi yitein vehayah levavam zeh* — they were at such a level of *yirat Hashem* that it is simply incomprehensible that they could have sinned. The conclusion: both events happened as they did in order to establish a model of return.

### Individual & Community

Moving to the top of *amud alef*: *l'omar lach she-im chata yachid omerim lo k'la eitzel yachid* — if an individual sins, even grievously, one can point him to the individual who sinned, meaning David HaMelech, who sinned and did *teshuvah*, and thereby show that the path of *teshuvah* is open. *V'im chatu tzibur omerim lahem l'chu eitzel tzibur* — and if an entire community has sinned, one can point them to the community that sinned and returned, namely *Am Yisrael* at the time of the *eigel*, to show that the gates are open for them as well.

The *gemara* then notes *v'tzricha* — it was necessary for both examples to exist. Had we only had the case of the individual — David — one might have argued that *teshuvah* was available because an individual's sin is not widely publicized and does not constitute so large a *chilul Hashem*, and therefore God permits recovery. But for an entire community whose sin is *mefarsam*, broadcast openly, perhaps *teshuvah* would be unavailable. And conversely, had we only had the communal case, one might argue that a whole nation merits *teshuvah* because *nafishei rachamei* — God has great compassion on the many, or their collective merit is substantial. But for a single individual whose merit is comparatively limited, *aval yachid d'lo aleem zechutei*, perhaps the path would be closed. *Tzricha* — you need both.

It is worth pausing here to notice how this *aggadata* is in conversation with the deeper themes of *masechet Avodah Zarah*. The *masechet* grapples with the temptations of the surrounding pagan world. One might wonder: if all of *Am Yisrael* could descend to *avodah zarah* at *Har Sinai* itself, does that say something about the pull of idolatry? Does it lend some legitimacy to it? The *gemara*'s answer is precisely the opposite: this happened at such a miraculous, inexplicable level that the only meaning to be drawn from it is the openness of the path of *teshuvah* — not any validation of idolatrous impulses.

## Mitzvot & Sins

### Mitzvot Proclaim

*V'hainu d'Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani* — and this is akin to what *Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani* says, that *Rabbi Yonatan* expounded: the *pasuk ne'um David ben Yishai v'ne'um hagever hukan al* — David the son of Yishai, and the man *hukan al*, raised above — means that David *hekim olah shel teshuvah*, he lifted up the yoke of *teshuvah*, he established that *teshuvah* is possible.

Continuing in the name of *Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani* citing *Rabbi Yonatan*: *kol ha'oseh mitzvah achat ba'olam hazeh, makdimas v'holechet l'fanav l'olam haba* — whoever performs even a single *mitzvah* in this world, that *mitzvah* will go before him and herald him in the world to come, proclaiming his righteousness. It is a striking and beautiful image: your *mitzvos* will be announced before you like a herald before a king. The *pasuk* cited: *v'halach l'fanecha tzidkecha u'kvod Hashem ya'asfecha* — your righteousness will walk before you and the glory of God will gather you in.

### Sins Chase You

However, the flip side is equally stark. *V'kol ha'over aveirah achat, melafefto u'molichto l'yom hadin* — whoever transgresses even a single sin, it clings to him and drags him to the day of judgment. You cannot shake it; it wraps around you. *Rabbi Eliezer* sharpens the image: *keshurah bo k'chelev* — it is tied to him like a dog, chasing after him relentlessly. The *pasuk* brought is *v'lo shama eileiha lishkav etzlah lihyot imah* — referring to Yosef's refusal of Potiphar's wife — and the *gemara* reads it as *lishkav etzlah ba'olam hazeh lihyot imah l'olam haba*: refusing to lie with her in this world means not having her cling to him in the world to come. The image is powerful: the good deeds you think are unnoticed will herald you magnificently; the sins you hope to escape will pursue you like a dog — unless, of course, one does *teshuvah*, as the preceding discussion has established.

## Gratitude Theme

### Existence & Sin

*Amar Reish Lakish*: *bo u'nachzik tov l'avoteinu* — let us come and hold gratitude toward our forefathers — and here he means not *Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov* but the generation of *Am Yisrael* who stood at *Har Sinai*. *She'malei heim lo chatu anu lo banu l'olam* — for had they not sinned, we never would have been born.

The *pasuk*: *ani amarti elohim atem u'vnei elyon kulchem* — I said that you would be like gods, like the most high. The understanding is that being like angels means, among other things, not engaging in sexual reproduction — and therefore no future generations would have been born. *Chibbaltem ma'aseichem* — but you corrupted your deeds — and so *achen k'adam t'mutun* — you will die like human beings, with all that entails. *Reish Lakish*'s move here is striking: he takes the catastrophic sin of the *eigel* and finds in it a reason for gratitude — we exist because of it. There is an obvious tension in this approach, since one might misread it as suggesting that great sins can be endorsed because of their downstream benefits. The difficulty of what precise *educational* message this carries is real and worth sitting with.

### Challenge: Later Gen

The *gemara* now challenges the implication of *Reish Lakish*'s statement. *Lemeimar de'ilu chatu lo havei moldu* — are you saying that had they not sinned, they would not have sired children? But the Torah commands *atem peru u'revu* — be fruitful and multiply — and clearly people were having children before the *eigel*. The *gemara* initially answers: *ad Sinai* — yes, up until *Har Sinai* people obviously had children, but once they reached the extraordinary *madreigah* of standing at *Har Sinai* and the experience of *mi yitein vehayah levavam zeh*, at that point they would have been like angels and would no longer have reproduced.

The *gemara* then presses further: but doesn't the Torah say at *Sinai* itself, even before *cheit ha'eigel* — *lechu imru lahem shuvu lachem le'ohaleichem* — go back to your tents? This is understood by *Chazal* as a permission to return to their wives, to *lesimchat onah*, the joy of marital intimacy. The *gemara* responds that this does not imply procreation — they could have continued marital relations for the sake of the *mitzvah* of *onah* without bearing children. This is a conceptually interesting distinction: even granting a kind of angelic elevation, the *mitzvah* of husband and wife as a married couple retains its value independent of procreation.

But a further challenge comes from the *pasuk*: *lema'an yitav lahem u'livneihem achareihem* — in order that it be good for them and their children after them. This appears to be spoken in the context of *Ma'amad Har Sinai*, before *cheit ha'eigel*, and speaks of children born later. The *gemara* answers that *le'otan ha'omedim al Har Sinai* — it refers to the children already alive who stood at *Har Sinai*, not to children yet to be born.

### Never Born Reading

*Va'amar Reish Lakish*: what is meant by *zeh sefer toldot Adam*? Did *Adam HaRishon* actually have a book? Rather, *melamed she'her'ah lo ha'Kadosh Baruch Hu le'Adam HaRishon dor dor ve'dorshav dor dor ve'chakhamav dor dor u'farnassav* — God showed *Adam HaRishon* all the future generations, their leaders, their sages, their communal figures. When God reached the generation of *Rabbi Akiva*, *Adam HaRishon* rejoiced in his Torah but was anguished by his terrible martyrdom, saying *veli mah yakru rei'ekha El* — how heavy and incomprehensible are Your thoughts, O Lord.

*Va'amar Rabbi Yosei*: *ein Ben David ba ad sheyichlu kol haneshamot shebaguf* — *Mashiach* will not come until all the souls stored in the heavenly repository called *guf* have been distributed, with each new birth drawing from that storehouse. The *pasuk*: *ki lo l'olam ariv v'lo netzach ektzof ki ruach milfanai yaatof uneshamot ani asiti* — I will not contend forever, nor will I be angry forever; a spirit from before Me will be held back because of the souls that I have created. *Rashi* explains *yaatof* as indicating that the souls are holding things back — once they are all used up, the time of *Mashiach* will arrive.

This *aggadic* passage establishes that it was always part of the divine plan for there to be future generations — God showed *Adam HaRishon* all future generations, and *Mashiach*'s arrival is contingent on the completion of those generations. The *gemara* therefore reinterprets *Reish Lakish*: *lo teima anu lo banu l'olam* — do not read him as saying we never would have been born. *Ela eima k'mi shelo banu l'olam* — rather, what he means is that it would have been *as if* we had never been born. Had that generation not sinned, they would have lived forever, and our existence would have had no significance in relation to theirs.

### Death Decreed

The *gemara* now challenges this revised understanding from a *braisa*. Going back to *mi yitein vehayah levavam zeh* — even at that extraordinary level, it was not within the cards that they could have nullified the *malach hamaves*, the angel of death. Why? *Shekvar nigzira gzeira* — the decree of death was already decreed from the time of *Adam HaRishon*. *Halo kibelu Yisrael es haTorah ela kedei shelo tehei uma velashon sholetes bahen* — the level they reached through accepting the Torah would have achieved that no nation would ever subjugate them, as the *pasuk* promises *lema'an yitav lahem ulevineihem le'olam*. But conquering death itself was never possible. The *gemara* responds that *Reish Lakish*'s position accords with a different *tanna*.

## Angel of Death

### Like Gods

*Detanya Rabbi Yosei omer*: *lo kibelu Yisrael es haTorah ela kedei shelo yehei malach hamaves sholet bahen* — at the moment of accepting the Torah, the Israelites reached a level at which the angel of death had no dominion over them. The *pasuk*: *ani amarti elohim atem u'vnei elyon kulchem* — I said you would be like gods, like the most high, *i.e.*, like angels who do not die.

### Corrupted Deeds

*Chavaltem ma'aseichem* — you corrupted your deeds — *achen k'adam temutun* — and therefore you will die like a human being. This *chavaltem ma'aseichem* is *Reish Lakish*'s interpolation into the *pasuk*, marking the hinge between the exalted possibility and the fallen reality. It is worth noting that once the *gemara* moved away from the literal reading of *Reish Lakish* — that they literally would not have been born — toward the reading that they would have lived forever, this *pasuk* fits precisely: you would have been like the most high, beyond death, but now *achen k'adam temutun*.

### Death as Suffering

*V'tanna kamma ha kesiv achen k'adam temutun* — what does the *tanna kamma* do with this *pasuk*, which seems to say explicitly that had they not sinned they would not have died? The *tanna kamma* holds that death was always decreed from *Adam HaRishon* and that the level of *Matan Torah* could only have protected them from subjugation, not from death itself. So how does the *tanna kamma* read *achen k'adam temutun*? *Mai misa aniyus* — the *misa* here does not mean literal death; it means suffering that is *like* death. Had they not sinned, no nation would have subjugated them, and they would have lived well. Now they will be subject to experiences that feel like death — poverty, blindness, illness, childlessness.

The underlying disagreement between *Rabbi Yosei* and the *tanna kamma* is thus: *Rabbi Yosei* holds that at *Ma'amad Har Sinai* they literally conquered death; the *tanna kamma* holds that death was always decreed, and the only gain from accepting the Torah at that level was immunity from national subjugation.

### Four Like Dead

*De'amar mar arba'a chashuvim k'mes* — the master taught that four types are considered like dead. *Elu hen*: *ani*, a poor person; *suma*, a blind person; *metzora*, one afflicted with *tzara'as*; and *mi she'ein lo vanim*, one who has no children.

How do we know poverty is like death? *Ki mesu kol ha'anashim* — for all the men who sought your life have died, said to *Moshe*. Who were those men? *Datan va'Aviram*. But did they actually die? We know they were alive later when they rebelled against *Moshe*. Rather, *sheyardu minechaseihem* — they had become impoverished, and therefore it is as if they had died, stripped of influence and standing. How do we know blindness is like death? *Bemakhshakhim hoshivani kemetei olam* — You have placed me in darkness like the eternally dead. For *metzora*: *al na tehi kemet* — let our sister not be like dead, said of *Miriam* when she was afflicted with *tzara'as*, banished from the community. For one without children: *havah li banim ve'im ayin metah anokhi* — give me children, and if not I am dead.

These are striking statements that deserve careful reading. The *gemara* is likely pointing to societal realities — that those who lack wealth, sight, physical wholeness, or children are sometimes rendered invisible, devalued, as if they do not exist. This does not mean that such people *ought* to be treated that way, but it reflects a reality that we are obligated to work against. The four together, then, give content to the *tanna kamma*'s reading of *mai misa aniyus* — death here means the range of profound suffering and marginalization that human beings can experience.

## Moses' Rebuke

### God's Offer

*Tanu rabbanan*: *im be-hukkotai telechu* — the *ein im ela lashon takhnunim*, the word *im* here carries a tone of pleading, as if *Moshe* is beseeching Israel to keep the *mitzvos*. As the *pasuk* says: *lu ammi shome'a li* — oh, if only my people would listen to me — *kim'at oyeveihem akhni'a* — I would subdue their enemies swiftly. *V'lu hikshavta le-mitzvotai vayehi khannahar shelomekha* — had you listened to My commandments, your peace would flow like a river.

The discussion turns to *mi yitten vehayah levavam zeh lahem*. *Amar lahem Moshe le-Yisrael, kefuyyei tovah benei kefuyyei tovah* — *Moshe* said to Israel: ingrates, children of ingrates. When God said *mi yitten vehayah levavam zeh* — if only their hearts would be like this — *hayah lahem lomar ten attah* — they should have said: give it to us, God. God was opening a door: if only you could be at this level forever. The correct response was to seize that moment and say, yes, grant us this. Instead, they turned away from the goodness being offered before their eyes.

### Ungrateful

*Kefuyyei tovah dikhtiv* — and how do we know they are ingrates? Because *Moshe*, speaking forty years later in *Sefer Devarim*, frames it this way — retroactively demonstrating that ingratitude is a pattern. The *pasuk* cited: *venafshenu katzah balekhem hakkalokel* — our soul is disgusted by this insubstantial bread. They complained about the *manna*. The *manna*, as *Rashi* explains, left no waste product — an extraordinary blessing, in that there was nothing unclean or burdensome about it. Yet Israel framed it as something unnatural and punishing, failing to recognize the tremendous gift God had given them.

### Adam's Blame

*Benei kefuyyei tovah* — and they are also the children of ingrates — *dikhtiv ha'ishah asher natata imadi hi natna li min ha'etz v'ochel* — Adam said, the woman *You* gave me, she gave me from the tree, and I ate. Adam took the goodness God had given him — a companion — and turned her into the excuse for his transgression. The pattern is the same: receiving great goodness and converting it into a complaint or a deflection rather than gratitude.

### Forty Years Later

*Af Moshe Rabbeinu lo rimzam l'Yisrael ela l'achar arba'im shanah* — even *Moshe* himself did not hint this idea to Israel until forty years had passed. Only in *Sefer Devarim*, when retelling the events of *Ma'amad Har Sinai*, did *Moshe* frame it in this way — that they had missed an extraordinary opening that God had offered them. The *pasuk*: *va'olech es'chem bamidbar arba'im shanah* — and I led you in the wilderness forty years — *v'lo natan Hashem lachem lev lada'at v'einayim lirot v'oznayim lishmo'a ad hayom hazeh* — and God did not give you a heart to understand, eyes to see, and ears to hear until this day.

### Master's Teaching

*Amar Rabbah shma mina*: from this we may infer that *lo ka'i inish ada'teih d'rabbeih ad arba'im shanin* — a person does not truly understand his master until forty years have passed. The question is who the *inish* refers to. *Rashi* understands that even *Moshe* did not grasp what God had meant at that moment — that God was opening a door with the words *mi yitten* — until forty years later. Others understand it to mean that *Bnei Yisrael* could not have appreciated what that moment signified until forty years of growth had passed, and *Moshe* knew this and therefore waited.

## Torah Controls Yetzer

### Torah & Charity

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Banna'ah*: what is meant by the *pasuk ashreichem zorei al kol mayim m'shalchei regel hashor v'hachamor* — praised are you who sow upon all waters, who send forth the leg of the ox and the donkey? *Ashreihem Yisrael bizman she'oskim baTorah u'vigmilut chasadim* — praised is Israel when they engage in Torah and *gemilut chasadim*. *Yitzram masur beyadam* — their *yetzer hara* is delivered into their hands; they control it. *V'ein hem mesurim beyad yitzram* — the *yetzer* does not control them.

This connects directly to the larger themes of the *masechet*. The *masechet*'s halachic *takanot* — don't drink their wine, limit commercial interaction — are one layer of protection against the temptations of the surrounding world. But the deeper protection is Torah and *gemilut chasadim*. If you are truly engaged in these, your *yetzer hara* is *masur beyadecha*.

*V'ein zri'ah ela tzedakah* — "sowing" refers to *tzedakah*, which is *gemilut chasadim*, as the *pasuk* says: *zir'u lachem l'tzedakah v'kitzru l'fi chesed* — sow for yourselves for righteousness and harvest for *chesed*. *V'ein mayim ela Torah* — and "waters" refers to Torah, as the *pasuk* says: *hoy kol tzamei l'chu lamayim* — all who are thirsty, come to the waters.

### Ox & Donkey

What then is meant by *m'shalchei regel hashor v'hachamor*? *Tana d'vei Eliyahu*: a person should always make himself toward the words of Torah *k'shor l'ol* — like an ox to the yoke — *u'chachamor l'masoy* — and like a donkey to the burden. You must subjugate yourself to Torah with the acceptance of a working animal, willing to bear the full weight of the commitment. *Rashi* notes that this final teaching of *Tana d'vei Eliyahu* is somewhat distinct from the preceding point: the image of *meshalchin u-mashlichin raglei yetzer hara me'al adam me'alehem* — casting away the feet of the *yetzer hara* from upon a person — is the first interpretation, while the dedication of the ox and donkey is a parallel but independent point about the posture one must take toward Torah.

This concludes the extended opening *aggadata* of *masechet Avodah Zarah*.

## Three Days Rule

### Mishna Stated

We now return to the *mishna* and the halachic discussion: one may not conduct business with non-Jews for three days before their holidays — *gimel yamim*. The presumed concern is that they will rejoice at the good fortune of the transaction and bring that joy to their *avodah zarah* worship. However, the *gemara* here will focus more specifically on the concern that transactions may enable them to purchase items for their idolatrous rituals.

### Three Days Logic

The *gemara* asks: *u'mi be'inan kulei hai* — do they really need three full days to acquire what they need for their holiday? Is such advance preparation genuinely required? The *gemara* raises a *mishna* as a comparison: *be'arba'ah perakim ba-shanah ha-mocher behemah le-chavero tzarich le-hodi'o* — at four times in the year, one who sells an animal to another must inform him whether the animal's mother or offspring was sold that same day, because of the prohibition of *oto ve-et beno lo tishchatu beyom echad* — the mother and child may not be slaughtered on the same day. Note that the transgression belongs to whoever causes both to be slaughtered on the same day, so the seller must inform the buyer.

### Four Festivals

The four times in the year when people are assumed to slaughter immediately upon purchase — so that this notification is practically necessary — are: *erev yom tov ha-acharon shel chag*, the eve of the last day of *Sukkot*, meaning *Shemini Atzeret*. *Tosafot* notes that the first day of *Sukkot* is not listed because people are occupied with their *sukkah* and *lulav* and are not necessarily focused on preparing a major festive meal; furthermore, *Shemini Atzeret* has its own independent identity and deserves particular emphasis as a day of *simcha*. Then: *erev yom tov ha-rishon shel Pesach*, the eve of the first day of *Pesach*; *ve'erev Atzeret*, before *Shavuot*; and *ve'erev Rosh Hashanah*, before *Rosh Hashanah*. *U'ke-divrei Rabbi Yose ha-Gelili af erev Yom ha-Kippurim ba-Galil* — and according to *Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili*, also the eve of *Yom Kippur* in the *Galil*, because of the *mitzvah* to eat on *erev Yom Kippur*.

What the *gemara* draws from this *mishna* is that even for a major feast, people buy only one day in advance — not three. There is an inherent awkwardness in the *gemara*'s reasoning here: it is applying the patterns of Jewish festival practice to the question of what non-Jewish pagans do in preparation for their idolatrous holidays. The *gemara* is working within a framework of rabbinic discourse and treating this as a question that must be resolved through the available halachic models, even if those models reflect specifically Jewish realities. One possible way to understand this is that the *gemara* is seeking a standardized model — since pagan practices vary across Rome, Syria, North Africa, and elsewhere, some fixed benchmark is needed, and the Jewish model provides the available reference point.

### Sacrifice vs. Feast

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *hasam de'achilah, sagi be'chad yoma* — there, for a feast, one day's advance purchase is sufficient. *Hacha de'hakravah, binan tlata yomei* — here, the concern is not festive eating but sacrificial offering, and for that, three days are needed to verify the animal.

### Blemish Standards

But does inspection for sacrifice really require three days? *V'hatanya: sho'alin vedorshin behilchos hapesach kodem hapesach sheloshim yom* — the *braisa* teaches that one begins asking and studying the laws of *Pesach* thirty days before, which the *gemara* understands to include beginning the process of acquiring *korbanot*. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says two weeks. Why do Jews need thirty days or two weeks? *Anan de'shchichi mumei de'paslei afilu bidukim she'ba'ayin* — for us, even minute blemishes — such as a slight film over the eye — are sufficient to disqualify an animal, so *binan tlashin yomei*, we need thirty days of preparation and inspection.

### Blemish Standards

*Inhu de'mechusar eivar it lehu* — for them, the *gemara* holds, an animal is disqualified only if it is missing a limb entirely. This is a *halacha* pertaining to *Bnei Noach* who wish to bring a *korban* in the *Beis Hamikdash* — for them, a simple blemish is not disqualifying; only a missing limb renders it unfit. The *gemara* is again transparently applying the *halachos* of *Bnei Noach* and the *Beis Hamikdash* to the question of pagan sacrificial standards, which is a legal fiction of sorts: the reality of pagan sacrificial practices bears no direct relationship to these *halachos*. But the only lens the *gemara* has to work with is what it knows. Given this standard, *itlehu be'tlata yomei sagi* — three days is sufficient for them to determine whether the animal is missing a limb. Though it must be said: checking for a missing limb takes a matter of moments, not three days, and this gap in the reasoning is not fully resolved.

### Rabbi Elazar

*De'amar Rabbi Elazar minayin lemechusar eivar de'asur livnei Noach* — how do we derive that a *Bnei Noach* may not bring an animal missing a limb? *Dichsiv umikol hachai mikol basar shenayim mikol* — from the *pasuk* commanding *Noach* to bring two of every living creature into the ark, which implies a standard of completeness.

*Rashi* at this point attempts to bridge the gap between the *halachos* of *Bnei Noach* bringing *korbanot* to the *Beis Hamikdash* and the reality of pagan sacrificial practice. He writes: *lehakriv legavoah, vedumya de'korbanos shenahagu avoseihem leshamayim hem nohagim b'avodah zarah shelahem* — we assume that the same standards their ancestors maintained when bringing *korbanot* to God, they have carried over into their *avodah zarah* practice. It is an attempt to create a bridge, though the underlying strangeness remains — the *gemara* is consistently reading non-Jewish pagan practice through the prism of the only sacrificial law it knows: that of *Bnei Noach* and the *Beis Hamikdash*.
