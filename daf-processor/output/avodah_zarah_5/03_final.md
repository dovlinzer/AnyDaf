# Avodah Zarah 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Torah & Future Reward

### Intro & Themes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (יוֹם, מֵעִיד, טָרַף, בָּעֵגֶל — סִימָן.) גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אֲשֶׁר אָנֹכִי מְצַוְּךָ הַיּוֹם לַעֲשׂוֹתָם״?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara presents **a mnemonic** for the ensuing statements of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: **Today, bear witness, shake, the** golden **calf.** The Gemara returns to an earlier discussion (3a), first by citing **the** matter **itself. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Which I command you this day, to do them"** (Deuteronomy 7:11)?

Daf 5b opens approximately ten lines down, at the *siman* beginning *yom me'itaref ba'agal*, which serves as a heading for the various discussions to follow, after which the *gemara* proceeds *gufa*. The bulk of what we encounter here is *aggadata* — and this *aggadata* is, in a meaningful sense, the theological and moral framing of *masechet Avodah Zarah* as a whole.

The *masechet* is ultimately about navigating a world that is largely non-Jewish, often pagan, and sometimes hostile. The *aggadata* addresses the logical and emotional challenges this raises: the nations seem to prosper; we seem to suffer; their accomplishments appear impressive. The opening sections of the *masechet* have already introduced several major themes in response. First, in the future time the nations will be punished, and even their great achievements — building roads, conquering kingdoms — will be granted no credit, not even their claim that such works were done to facilitate Torah study. Second, and relatedly, what truly matters is Torah: learning it, performing its *mitzvos*. Third, the punishment of the nations will ultimately be harsher than our suffering, and our present suffering is directed toward our ultimate reward. These themes continue now as the *gemara* resumes.

### Today vs. Tomorrow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַיּוֹם לַעֲשׂוֹתָם״ — וְלֹא לְמָחָר לַעֲשׂוֹתָם, ״הַיּוֹם לַעֲשׂוֹתָם״ — וְלֹא הַיּוֹם לִיטּוֹל שְׂכָרָן.
>
> **Translation:** This verse teaches that **today** is the time **to do them,** i.e., to perform the mitzvot, in this world, **but tomorrow,** in the World-to-Come, **is not** the time **to do them.** Furthermore, **today** is the time **to do them, but today is not** the time **to receive** one's **reward,** which is given in the World-to-Come.

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: what is meant by the *pasuk*, *asher anochi metzavcha hayom la'asotam* — that I command you today to do them? *Hayom la'asotam velo lemachar la'asotam*: today is the time to perform the *mitzvos*, not tomorrow. The *lemachar* here carries a double meaning — not only "don't delay" in the ordinary sense, but that the *atid lavo*, the *olam haba*, is not the time for doing *mitzvos*. And correspondingly: *hayom la'asotam velo hayom litol secharam* — in this world you perform the *mitzvos*, but you do not receive their reward here. The reward belongs to *olam haba*. This directly addresses the question of why those who devote themselves to Torah and *mitzvos* do not always appear as materially successful as the surrounding nations.

### Mitzvot as Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל מִצְוֹת שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, בָּאוֹת וּמְעִידוֹת אוֹתָם לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִתְּנוּ עֵדֵיהֶם וְיִצְדָּקוּ יִשְׁמְעוּ וְיֹאמְרוּ אֱמֶת״. ״יִתְּנוּ עֵדֵיהֶם וְיִצְדָּקוּ״ — אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, ״יִשְׁמְעוּ וְיֹאמְרוּ אֱמֶת״ — אֵלּוּ אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: All of the mitzvot that the Jews perform in this word** will **come and bear witness for them in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "Let them bring their witnesses that they may be justified, and let them hear, and say: It is truth"** (Isaiah 43:9). He explains: **"Let them bring their witnesses that they may be justified"; these are** referring to **the Jews. "And let them hear, and say: It is truth"; these are** referring to **the nations of the world.**

*Va'amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: all the *mitzvos* we perform in this world will come and testify to our righteousness in the world to come — *kol mitzvos she'osin ba'olam hazeh bo'os umididots osam la'olam haba*. Even if in this world we are mocked or degraded by surrounding nations, in the end the *mitzvos* will bear witness to our rightness. As the *pasuk* says: *yitnu adeihem veyitzdaku yishme'u veyomru emet* — they will give their testimony and be found righteous, they will hear and say it is true. *Eilu Yisrael* — these are Israel, who will demonstrate through a life of Torah and *mitzvos* that they are righteous. *Eilu ovdei kochavim* — it is the idolaters who will ultimately acknowledge this. The formulation *adeihem* also echoes back to the word *yom edam* cited at the very opening of the *masechet*, a deliberate textual resonance.

### Mitzvot Rebuke Nations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל מִצְוֹת שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, בָּאוֹת וְטוֹרְפוֹת אוֹתָם לְאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא עַל פְּנֵיהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם וַעֲשִׂיתֶם כִּי הִוא חׇכְמַתְכֶם וּבִינַתְכֶם לְעֵינֵי הָעַמִּים״, ״נֶגֶד הָעַמִּים״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״לְעֵינֵי הָעַמִּים״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁבָּאוֹת וְטוֹרְפוֹת לְאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם עַל פְּנֵיהֶם לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: All of the mitzvot that the Jewish people perform in this world** will **come and strike the faces of the nations of the world in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "Observe therefore and do them; for this is your wisdom and your understanding in the eyes of the nations"** (Deuteronomy 4:6). It **is not stated: Before the nations; rather,** the verse states: **"In the eyes of the nations,"** which taken literally **teaches that they** will **come and strike the faces of the nations of the world in the World-to-Come.**

A third statement from *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: all the *mitzvos* we perform in this world will not only vindicate us but will come and *slap the nations in the face* — *bo'os vetorfas al pneichem*. As the *pasuk* says: *ushmartem va'asitem ki hi chochmatchem uvinatchem le'einei ha'amim* — you shall observe and perform them, for this is your wisdom and understanding in the eyes of the nations. Note that the *pasuk* says *le'einei ha'amim*, in their eyes, not *neged ha'amim*, opposite the nations. *Melamed she-ba'at v-tarpas bifneihem* — it is as if the *mitzvos* smack the nations right in their face, demonstrating that their own lives have been worthless and that we were the truly wise ones.

One might note that the plain sense of that *pasuk* — *ki chachmatchem uvinatchem le'einei ha'amim asher yishme'un es kol hachukim v'omru rak am chacham v'navon hagoy hagadol hazeh* — seems to describe the nations recognizing Jewish wisdom *in this world*. Yet the *gemara* consistently places all of this vindication *le'asid lavo*, in the future time.

## Cheit HaEgel

### Gate for Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לֹא עָשׂוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הָעֵגֶל אֶלָּא לִיתֵּן פִּתְחוֹן פֶּה לְבַעֲלֵי תְּשׁוּבָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִי יִתֵּן וְהָיָה לְבָבָם זֶה לָהֶם לְיִרְאָה אֹתִי כׇּל הַיָּמִים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: The Jewish people fashioned the** Golden **Calf** (see Exodus, chapter 32) **only to give a claim to penitents, as it is stated** after the revelation at Sinai: **"Who would give that they had such a heart as this always, to fear Me,** and keep all My commandments, that it might be good for them, and with their children **forever"** (Deuteronomy 5:26). If the nation was truly at such a lofty spiritual state, how could they worship the Golden Calf? Rather, their sin occurred so that it would be made clear that one can repent for any sin, as even a sin as severe as the Golden Calf was forgiven.

*Va'amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: the Israelites made the *eigel* — the Golden Calf — only in order to open a *pischon peh*, an opening, for *ba'alei teshuvah*. It is worth noting that *pischon peh* ordinarily carries a negative connotation — one does not want to give a *pischon peh* to the *resha'im* or to the *apikorsim*. Here, however, the *pischon peh* is precisely what is needed: an opening for those who seek to return.

The argument is rooted in the *pasuk*: *mi yitien vehayah levavam zeh lahem l'yira osi kol hayamim* — who would only give that their heart would be like this, to fear Me all the days. This was said immediately after *Matan Torah*, when they stood at an extraordinary level of *yirat Hashem*. How could it be that within forty days they descended to worshipping the *eigel*? The very incomprehensibility of the sin suggests that it was allowed to happen only to demonstrate that even from the greatest heights, even after the most catastrophic fall, the gates of *teshuvah* remain open. Whether *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* means this was literally the Israelites' motivation, or rather that this was the ultimate purpose served by the event being permitted, is itself a question — but the theological function is clear.

### David & Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: לֹא דָּוִד רָאוּי לְאוֹתוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, וְלֹא יִשְׂרָאֵל רְאוּיִן לְאוֹתוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, לֹא דָּוִד רָאוּי לְאוֹתוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלִבִּי חָלַל בְּקִרְבִּי״,
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** similar to that **which Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: David was not fit to** act as he did in **that incident** involving Bathsheba, **and the Jewish people were not fit to** act as they did in **that incident** of the Golden Calf. **David was not fit to** act as he did in **that incident** involving Bathsheba (see II Samuel, chapter 11), **as it is written: "And my heart is wounded within me"** (Psalms 109:22), i.e., he had vanquished his evil inclination, and therefore it should not have been able to rule over him to that extent.

This connects to what *Rabbi Yochanan* says in the name of *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*: *lo David ra'uy l'oso ma'aseh* — David was not at a level where he should have been capable of that act with *Bas Sheva*, and *lo Yisrael re'uyim l'oso ma'aseh* — the Israelites at *Har Sinai* were not at a level where they should have been capable of the sin of the *eigel*. How do we know this about David? *V'libi chalal b'kirbi* — my heart is as if emptied within me, meaning he was entirely free of lust and *yetzer hara*. How do we know this about Israel? From the very same *pasuk*: *mi yitien vehayah levavam zeh* — they were at such a level of *yirat Hashem* that it is simply incomprehensible that they could have sinned. The conclusion: both events happened as they did in order to establish a model of return.

### Individual & Community

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹמַר לָךְ, שֶׁאִם חָטָא יָחִיד — אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל יָחִיד, וְאִם חָטְאוּ צִבּוּר — אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** This serves **to say to you that if an individual** has **sinned, one says to him: Go to** that famous **individual** who sinned, King David, and learn from him that one can repent. **And if the community sinned, one says to** them: **Go to** the **community** that sinned, i.e., the Jewish people at the time of the Golden Calf.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן יָחִיד — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְפַרְסַם חִטְאֵיהּ, אֲבָל צִבּוּר דִּמְפַרְסַם חִטְאַיְהוּ — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן צִבּוּר — מִשּׁוּם דִּנְפִישִׁי רַחֲמַיְיהוּ, אֲבָל יָחִיד דְּלָא אַלִּימָא זְכוּתֵיהּ — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** it is **necessary** to learn about repentance both in the case of an individual and in the case of a community. The reason is **that if we had learned** this idea only with regard to **an individual,** one might have thought that he has the option to repent only **because his sin is not publicized. But** in the case of **a community, whose sin is publicized,** one might **say** that the community **cannot** repent. **And** likewise, **if we had learned** this idea only with regard to **a community,** one might have said that their repentance is accepted **because their prayers are more numerous** than those of an individual, and they are heard before God. **But** in the case of **an individual, whose merit is not** as **strong,** one might **say** that he is **not** able to repent. Therefore, it is **necessary** to teach both cases.

Moving to the top of *amud alef*: *l'omar lach she-im chata yachid omerim lo k'la eitzel yachid* — if an individual sins, even grievously, one can point him to the individual who sinned, meaning David HaMelech, who sinned and did *teshuvah*, and thereby show that the path of *teshuvah* is open. *V'im chatu tzibur omerim lahem l'chu eitzel tzibur* — and if an entire community has sinned, one can point them to the community that sinned and returned, namely *Am Yisrael* at the time of the *eigel*, to show that the gates are open for them as well.

The *gemara* then notes *v'tzricha* — it was necessary for both examples to exist. Had we only had the case of the individual — David — one might have argued that *teshuvah* was available because an individual's sin is not widely publicized and does not constitute so large a *chilul Hashem*, and therefore God permits recovery. But for an entire community whose sin is *mefarsam*, broadcast openly, perhaps *teshuvah* would be unavailable. And conversely, had we only had the communal case, one might argue that a whole nation merits *teshuvah* because *nafishei rachamei* — God has great compassion on the many, or their collective merit is substantial. But for a single individual whose merit is comparatively limited, *aval yachid d'lo aleem zechutei*, perhaps the path would be closed. *Tzricha* — you need both.

It is worth pausing here to notice how this *aggadata* is in conversation with the deeper themes of *masechet Avodah Zarah*. The *masechet* grapples with the temptations of the surrounding pagan world. One might wonder: if all of *Am Yisrael* could descend to *avodah zarah* at *Har Sinai* itself, does that say something about the pull of idolatry? Does it lend some legitimacy to it? The *gemara*'s answer is precisely the opposite: this happened at such a miraculous, inexplicable level that the only meaning to be drawn from it is the openness of the path of *teshuvah* — not any validation of idolatrous impulses.

## Mitzvot & Sins

### Mitzvot Proclaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״נְאֻם דָּוִד בֶּן יִשַׁי וּנְאֻם הַגֶּבֶר הֻקַם עָל״? נְאֻם דָּוִד בֶּן יִשַׁי שֶׁהֵקִים עוּלָּהּ שֶׁל תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** similar to that **which Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani** says that **Rabbi Yonatan says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "The saying of David, son of Yishai, and the saying of the man raised on high [*al*]"** (II Samuel 23:1)? This is the meaning of the verse: **The saying of David, son of Yishai, who raised** and lightened **the yoke [*ullah*] of repentance,** as he taught the power of repentance through his own example.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הָעוֹשֶׂה מִצְוָה אַחַת בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, מְקַדַּמְתּוֹ וְהוֹלֶכֶת לְפָנָיו לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָלַךְ לְפָנֶיךָ צִדְקֶךָ וּכְבוֹד ה׳ יַאַסְפֶךָ״, וְכׇל הָעוֹבֵר עֲבֵירָה אַחַת, מְלַפַּפְתּוֹ וּמוֹלִיכָתוֹ לְיוֹם הַדִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִלָּפְתוּ אׇרְחוֹת דַּרְכָּם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani** further **says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says:** With regard to **anyone who performs one mitzva in this world,** the mitzva will **precede him and walk before him in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And your righteousness shall go before you, the glory of the Lord shall be your reward"** (Isaiah 58:8). **And** with regard to **anyone who commits one transgression,** that transgression will **shroud him and lead him on the Day of Judgment, as it is stated: "The paths of their way do wind,** they go up into the waste, and are lost" (Job 6:18).

*V'hainu d'Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani* — and this is akin to what *Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani* says, that *Rabbi Yonatan* expounded: the *pasuk ne'um David ben Yishai v'ne'um hagever hukan al* — David the son of Yishai, and the man *hukan al*, raised above — means that David *hekim olah shel teshuvah*, he lifted up the yoke of *teshuvah*, he established that *teshuvah* is possible.

Continuing in the name of *Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani* citing *Rabbi Yonatan*: *kol ha'oseh mitzvah achat ba'olam hazeh, makdimas v'holechet l'fanav l'olam haba* — whoever performs even a single *mitzvah* in this world, that *mitzvah* will go before him and herald him in the world to come, proclaiming his righteousness. It is a striking and beautiful image: your *mitzvos* will be announced before you like a herald before a king. The *pasuk* cited: *v'halach l'fanecha tzidkecha u'kvod Hashem ya'asfecha* — your righteousness will walk before you and the glory of God will gather you in.

### Sins Chase You

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: קְשׁוּרָה בּוֹ כְּכֶלֶב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא שָׁמַע אֵלֶיהָ לִשְׁכַּב אֶצְלָהּ לִהְיוֹת עִמָּהּ״, ״לִשְׁכַּב אֶצְלָהּ״ — בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״לִהְיוֹת עִמָּהּ״ — בָּעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** The transgression **is tied to him like a dog** and does not leave him, **as it is stated** with regard to Joseph and Potiphar's wife: **"And he did not listen to her, to lie by her, or to be with her"** (Genesis 39:10). This teaches that Joseph refused **"to lie by her" in this world,** which would have meant that he would have had **"to be with her" in the World-to-Come.**

However, the flip side is equally stark. *V'kol ha'over aveirah achat, melafefto u'molichto l'yom hadin* — whoever transgresses even a single sin, it clings to him and drags him to the day of judgment. You cannot shake it; it wraps around you. *Rabbi Eliezer* sharpens the image: *keshurah bo k'chelev* — it is tied to him like a dog, chasing after him relentlessly. The *pasuk* brought is *v'lo shama eileiha lishkav etzlah lihyot imah* — referring to Yosef's refusal of Potiphar's wife — and the *gemara* reads it as *lishkav etzlah ba'olam hazeh lihyot imah l'olam haba*: refusing to lie with her in this world means not having her cling to him in the world to come. The image is powerful: the good deeds you think are unnoticed will herald you magnificently; the sins you hope to escape will pursue you like a dog — unless, of course, one does *teshuvah*, as the preceding discussion has established.

## Gratitude Theme

### Existence & Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בּוֹאוּ וְנַחֲזִיק טוֹבָה לַאֲבוֹתֵינוּ, שֶׁאִלְמָלֵא הֵן לֹא חָטְאוּ — אָנוּ לֹא בָּאנוּ לָעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲנִי אָמַרְתִּי אֱלֹהִים אַתֶּם וּבְנֵי עֶלְיוֹן כֻּלְּכֶם״, חִבַּלְתֶּם מַעֲשֵׂיכֶם — ״אָכֵן כְּאָדָם תְּמוּתוּן וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara further discusses the sin of the Golden Calf. **Reish Lakish says: Come and let us be grateful to our ancestors** who sinned with the Golden Calf, **as had they not sinned we** would **not have come into the world.** Reish Lakish explains: **As it is stated** about the Jewish people after the revelation at Sinai: **"I said: You are godlike beings, and all of you sons of the Most High"** (Psalms 82:6), which indicates that they had become like angels and would not have propagated offspring. Then, God states: After **you ruined your deeds: "Yet you shall die like a man,** and fall like one of the princes" (Psalms 82:7).

*Amar Reish Lakish*: *bo u'nachzik tov l'avoteinu* — let us come and hold gratitude toward our forefathers — and here he means not *Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov* but the generation of *Am Yisrael* who stood at *Har Sinai*. *She'malei heim lo chatu anu lo banu l'olam* — for had they not sinned, we never would have been born.

The *pasuk*: *ani amarti elohim atem u'vnei elyon kulchem* — I said that you would be like gods, like the most high. The understanding is that being like angels means, among other things, not engaging in sexual reproduction — and therefore no future generations would have been born. *Chibbaltem ma'aseichem* — but you corrupted your deeds — and so *achen k'adam t'mutun* — you will die like human beings, with all that entails. *Reish Lakish*'s move here is striking: he takes the catastrophic sin of the *eigel* and finds in it a reason for gratitude — we exist because of it. There is an obvious tension in this approach, since one might misread it as suggesting that great sins can be endorsed because of their downstream benefits. The difficulty of what precise *educational* message this carries is real and worth sitting with.

### Challenge: Later Gen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּאִי לָא חָטְאוּ לָא הֲווֹ מוֹלְדוּ? וְהָכְתִיב ״וְאַתֶּם פְּרוּ וּרְבוּ״! עַד סִינַי. בְּסִינַי נָמֵי כְּתִיב ״לֵךְ אֱמֹר לָהֶם שׁוּבוּ לָכֶם לְאׇהֳלֵיכֶם״! לְשִׂמְחַת עוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that if they had not sinned** with the Golden Calf **they would not have sired** children? **But isn't it written** that Noah and his children were instructed: **"And you, be fruitful, and multiply"** (Genesis 9:7)? The Gemara answers: This instruction was issued only **until** the revelation at **Sinai,** but the Jewish people would have become like angels there, had they not sinned. The Gemara asks: Isn't it **also written** about the Jewish people who were at the revelation **at Sinai: "Go say to them: Return to your tents"** (Deuteronomy 5:27), which means that they were instructed to resume marital relations? The Gemara answers: That verse is referring **to the enjoyment of conjugal rights,** not to procreation.

The *gemara* now challenges the implication of *Reish Lakish*'s statement. *Lemeimar de'ilu chatu lo havei moldu* — are you saying that had they not sinned, they would not have sired children? But the Torah commands *atem peru u'revu* — be fruitful and multiply — and clearly people were having children before the *eigel*. The *gemara* initially answers: *ad Sinai* — yes, up until *Har Sinai* people obviously had children, but once they reached the extraordinary *madreigah* of standing at *Har Sinai* and the experience of *mi yitien vehayah levavam zeh*, at that point they would have been like angels and would no longer have reproduced.

The *gemara* then presses further: but doesn't the Torah say at *Sinai* itself, even before *cheit ha'eigel* — *lechu imru lahem shuvu lachem le'ohaleichem* — go back to your tents? This is understood by *Chazal* as a permission to return to their wives, to *lesimchat onah*, the joy of marital intimacy. The *gemara* responds that this does not imply procreation — they could have continued marital relations for the sake of the *mitzvah* of *onah* without bearing children. This is a conceptually interesting distinction: even granting a kind of angelic elevation, the *mitzvah* of husband and wife as a married couple retains its value independent of procreation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״לְמַעַן יִיטַב לָהֶם וְלִבְנֵיהֶם וְגוֹ׳״! לְאוֹתָן הָעוֹמְדִים עַל הַר סִינַי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **But isn't it written: "That it might be good for them, and with their children** forever" (Deuteronomy 5:26), which indicates that they would continue to bear children? The Gemara answers: This verse is referring **to those** children **who stood** with them **at Mount Sinai,** not to future generations.

But a further challenge comes from the *pasuk*: *lema'an yitav lahem u'livneihem achareihem* — in order that it be good for them and their children after them. This appears to be spoken in the context of *Ma'amad Har Sinai*, before *cheit ha'eigel*, and speaks of children born later. The *gemara* answers that *le'otan ha'omedim al Har Sinai* — it refers to the children already alive who stood at *Har Sinai*, not to children yet to be born.

### Never Born Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״זֶה סֵפֶר תּוֹלְדֹת אָדָם וְגוֹ׳״? וְכִי סֵפֶר הָיָה לוֹ לְאָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֶרְאָה לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְאָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן דּוֹר דּוֹר וְדוֹרְשָׁיו, דּוֹר דּוֹר וַחֲכָמָיו, דּוֹר דּוֹר וּפַרְנָסָיו. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְדוֹרוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שָׂמַח בְּתוֹרָתוֹ וְנִתְעַצֵּב בְּמִיתָתוֹ, אָמַר: ״וְלִי מַה יָּקְרוּ רֵעֶיךָ אֵל [וְגוֹ׳]״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: **But doesn't Reish Lakish say: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "This is the book of the generations of Adam,** in the day that God created man" (Genesis 5:1)? **Did Adam the first** man **have a book?** Rather, the verse **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, showed Adam, the first** man, **every generation and its expositors, every generation and its Sages,** and **every generation and its leaders. When** Adam **arrived at the generation of Rabbi Akiva, he rejoiced in his Torah and was saddened by his death,** as Rabbi Akiva was tortured and murdered. Adam **said: "How weighty also are Your thoughts to me, O God!** How great is the sum of them" (Psalms 139:17). It is evident from here that the Jews were destined to bear future generations from the beginning of time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֵין בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא עַד שֶׁיִּכְלוּ נְשָׁמוֹת שֶׁבַּגּוּף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״[כִּי לֹא לְעוֹלָם אָרִיב וְלֹא לָנֶצַח אֶקְּצוֹף] כִּי רוּחַ מִלְּפָנַי יַעֲטוֹף וּנְשָׁמוֹת אֲנִי עָשִׂיתִי״!
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, **Rabbi Yosei says:** The Messiah, **son of David, will not come until all the souls of the body have been finished,** i.e., until all souls that are destined to inhabit physical bodies will do so. **As it is stated: "For I will not contend for ever, neither will I be always wroth; for the spirit that enwraps itself is from Me, and the souls that I have made"** (Isaiah 57:16). According to Rabbi Yosei, in order for the Messiah to come in the end of days, it is necessary for the future generations to be born.

*Va'amar Reish Lakish*: what is meant by *zeh sefer toldot Adam*? Did *Adam HaRishon* actually have a book? Rather, *melamed she'her'ah lo ha'Kadosh Baruch Hu le'Adam HaRishon dor dor ve'dorshav dor dor ve'chakhamav dor dor u'farnassav* — God showed *Adam HaRishon* all the future generations, their leaders, their sages, their communal figures. When God reached the generation of *Rabbi Akiva*, *Adam HaRishon* rejoiced in his Torah but was anguished by his terrible martyrdom, saying *veli mah yakru rei'ekha El* — how heavy and incomprehensible are Your thoughts, O Lord.

*Va'amar Rabbi Yosei*: *ein Ben David ba ad sheyichlu kol haneshamot shebaguf* — *Mashiach* will not come until all the souls stored in the heavenly repository called *guf* have been distributed, with each new birth drawing from that storehouse. The *pasuk*: *ki lo l'olam ariv v'lo netzach ektzof ki ruach milfanai yaatof uneshamot ani asiti* — I will not contend forever, nor will I be angry forever; a spirit from before Me will be held back because of the souls that I have created. *Rashi* explains *yaatof* as indicating that the souls are holding things back — once they are all used up, the time of *Mashiach* will arrive.

This *aggadic* passage establishes that it was always part of the divine plan for there to be future generations — God showed *Adam HaRishon* all future generations, and *Mashiach*'s arrival is contingent on the completion of those generations. The *gemara* therefore reinterprets *Reish Lakish*: *lo teima anu lo banu l'olam* — do not read him as saying we never would have been born. *Ela eima k'mi shelo banu l'olam* — rather, what he means is that it would have been *as if* we had never been born. Had that generation not sinned, they would have lived forever, and our existence would have had no significance in relation to theirs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא תֵּימָא אָנוּ לֹא בָּאנוּ לָעוֹלָם, אֶלָּא כְּמִי שֶׁלֹּא בָּאנוּ לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Do not say** that if our ancestors had not sinned **we would not have come into the world,** as we still would have been born; **rather,** it would have been **as though we had not come into the world.** We would have been of no importance, due to the previous generations that would have still been alive.

### Death Decreed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּאִי לָא חָטְאוּ לָא הֲווֹ מָיְיתִי? וְהָכְתִיב פָּרָשַׁת יְבָמוֹת וּפָרָשַׁת נַחֲלוֹת! עַל תְּנַאי. וּמִי כְּתִיבִי קְרָאֵי עַל תְּנַאי? אִין, דְּהָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹקֶע יוֹם הַשִּׁשִּׁי״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִתְנָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עִם מַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית, וְאָמַר: אִם מְקַבְּלִין יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַתּוֹרָה — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — אַחְזִיר אֶתְכֶם לְתוֹהוּ וָבוֹהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that if** the Jewish people **had not sinned** with the Golden Calf then **they would not have died? But isn't the chapter** that addresses **widows whose husbands die childless** (Deuteronomy 25:5–10) **written** in the Torah, **and the chapter** that addresses **the inheritance** a deceased father bequeaths to his sons (Numbers 27:8–11) is also written? The Gemara answers: These passages were written **conditionally,** i.e., if the Jewish people were to sin and not become like angels, those *halakhot* would take effect. The Gemara asks: **And are verses written conditionally** in this manner? The Gemara answers: **Yes, as this** is what **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And there was evening and there was morning, the sixth day"** (Genesis 1:31)? This **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, established a condition with the acts of Creation, and He said: If the Jewish people accept the Torah** at the revelation at Sinai, all is **well** and the world will continue to exist. **But if** they do **not** accept it, **I will return you to** the primordial state of **chaos and disorder.**

The *gemara* now challenges this revised understanding from a *braisa*. Going back to *mi yitien vehayah levavam zeh* — even at that extraordinary level, it was not within the cards that they could have nullified the *malach hamaves*, the angel of death. Why? *Shekvar nigzira gzeira* — the decree of death was already decreed from the time of *Adam HaRishon*. *Halo kibelu Yisrael es haTorah ela kedei shelo tehei uma velashon sholetes bahen* — the level they reached through accepting the Torah would have achieved that no nation would ever subjugate them, as the *pasuk* promises *lema'an yitav lahem ulevineihem le'olam*. But conquering death itself was never possible. The *gemara* responds that *Reish Lakish*'s position accords with a different *tanna*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״מִי יִתֵּן וְהָיָה לְבָבָם זֶה לָהֶם״, לְבַטֵּל מֵהֶם מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת אִי אֶפְשָׁר, שֶׁכְּבָר נִגְזְרָה גְּזֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* to the new formulation of Reish Lakish's statement, according to which the Jewish people would have become immortal had they not sinned with the Golden Calf. The verse states about the Jewish people after the revelation at Sinai: **"Who would give that they had such a heart as this always,** to fear Me, and keep all My commandments, that it might be good for them, and with their children forever" (Deuteronomy 5:26). The *baraita* states that although they had reached such an elevated state, it **was not possible to nullify** the power of **the Angel of Death over them, as the decree** of death **was already issued** from the time of creation.

## Angel of Death

### Like Gods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לֹא קִיבְּלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַתּוֹרָה אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת שׁוֹלֵט בָּהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲנִי אָמַרְתִּי אֱלֹהִים אַתֶּם וּבְנֵי עֶלְיוֹן כֻּלְּכֶם״, חִבַּלְתֶּם מַעֲשֵׂיכֶם, ״אָכֵן כְּאָדָם תְּמוּתוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Reish Lakish **said** his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says: The Jewish people accepted the Torah only in order that the Angel of Death would not rule over them, as it is stated: "I said: You are godlike beings, and all of you sons of the Most High"** (Psalms 82:6), i.e., they had become immortal like angels. Then, God states: After **you ruined your deeds, "yet you shall die like a man,** and fall like one of the princes" (Psalms 82:7).

*Detanya Rabbi Yosei omer*: *lo kibelu Yisrael es haTorah ela kedei shelo yehei malach hamaves sholet bahen* — at the moment of accepting the Torah, the Israelites reached a level at which the angel of death had no dominion over them. The *pasuk*: *ani amarti elohim atem u'vnei elyon kulchem* — I said you would be like gods, like the most high, *i.e.*, like angels who do not die.

### Corrupted Deeds

*Chavaltem ma'aseichem* — you corrupted your deeds — *achen k'adam temutun* — and therefore you will die like a human being. This *chavaltem ma'aseichem* is *Reish Lakish*'s interpolation into the *pasuk*, marking the hinge between the exalted possibility and the fallen reality. It is worth noting that once the *gemara* moved away from the literal reading of *Reish Lakish* — that they literally would not have been born — toward the reading that they would have lived forever, this *pasuk* fits precisely: you would have been like the most high, beyond death, but now *achen k'adam temutun*.

### Death as Suffering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לֹא קִיבְּלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַתּוֹרָה אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אוּמָּה וְלָשׁוֹן שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְמַעַן יִיטַב לָהֶם וְלִבְנֵיהֶם עַד עוֹלָם״.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, the *baraita* explains that **the Jewish people accepted the Torah only in order that no nation or tongue would rule over them, as it is stated** in the same verse: **"That it might be good for them, and with their children forever."** This indicates that had the Jewish people not sinned they would not have achieved immortality, which contradicts Reish Lakish's statement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״לְמַעַן יִיטַב לָהֶם וְלִבְנֵיהֶם עַד עוֹלָם״, טוֹבָה הוּא דְּהָוְיָא, הָא מִיתָה אִיכָּא! (רַבִּי יוֹסֵי) אָמַר לָךְ: כֵּיוָן דְּלֵיכָּא מִיתָה, אֵין לְךָ טוֹבָה גְּדוֹלָה מִזּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And also,** according to **Rabbi Yosei, isn't it written: "That it might be good for them, and with their children forever,"** from which it may be inferred that although **it** will be **good** for them if they remain in this elevated state, **there** will still be **death?** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yosei** could have **said to you: Since there is no death, there is no greater good than this,** i.e., the promise of the verse is immortality.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״אָכֵן כְּאָדָם תְּמוּתוּן״, מַאי מִיתָה? עֲנִיּוּת, דְּאָמַר מָר: אַרְבָּעָה חֲשׁוּבִים כְּמֵתִים, אֵלּוּ הֵן: עָנִי, סוּמָא, וּמְצוֹרָע, וּמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **And** according to **the first *tanna* as well, isn't it written: "Yet you shall die like a man,"** which indicates that their mortality was decreed only due to the sin of the Golden Calf? The Gemara answers: **What** is meant by **death?** It means **poverty. As the Master said: Four are considered as** though they were **dead: These are a pauper, a blind person, a leper, and one who has no children.**

*V'tanna kamma ha kesiv achen k'adam temutun* — what does the *tanna kamma* do with this *pasuk*, which seems to say explicitly that had they not sinned they would not have died? The *tanna kamma* holds that death was always decreed from *Adam HaRishon* and that the level of *Matan Torah* could only have protected them from subjugation, not from death itself. So how does the *tanna kamma* read *achen k'adam temutun*? *Mai misa aniyus* — the *misa* here does not mean literal death; it means suffering that is *like* death. Had they not sinned, no nation would have subjugated them, and they would have lived well. Now they will be subject to experiences that feel like death — poverty, blindness, illness, childlessness.

The underlying disagreement between *Rabbi Yosei* and the *tanna kamma* is thus: *Rabbi Yosei* holds that at *Ma'amad Har Sinai* they literally conquered death; the *tanna kamma* holds that death was always decreed, and the only gain from accepting the Torah at that level was immunity from national subjugation.

### Four Like Dead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָנִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי מֵתוּ כׇּל הָאֲנָשִׁים״, וּמַאן נִינְהוּ? דָּתָן וַאֲבִירָם. וּמִי מֵתוּ? מִיהְוֵי הֲווֹ! אֶלָּא שֶׁיָּרְדוּ מִנִּכְסֵיהֶם. סוּמָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּמַחֲשַׁכִּים הוֹשִׁיבַנִי כְּמֵתֵי עוֹלָם״. מְצוֹרָע, דִּכְתִיב: ״אַל נָא תְהִי כַּמֵּת״. וּמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּנִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״הָבָה לִּי בָנִים וְאִם אַיִן מֵתָה אָנֹכִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **A pauper** is considered as though dead, **as it is written** that God said to Moses: "Go, return to Egypt; **for all the men** that sought your life **are dead"** (Exodus 4:19). **And who were these** men? They were **Dathan and Abiram. But did they** really **die? They were** still alive, as they participated in the rebellion of Korah, which took place years later. **Rather,** the verse does not mean that they had died, but **that they had lost their property** and become impoverished. This demonstrates that a pauper is considered as though he were dead. **A blind person** is considered as though he were dead, **as it is written: "He has made me to dwell in dark places, as those that have been long dead"** (Lamentations 3:6). **A leper** is considered as though he were dead, **as it is written** that Aaron said to Moses when Miriam was struck with leprosy: **"Let her not, I pray, be as one dead"** (Numbers 12:12). **And one who has no children** is considered as though he were dead, **as it is written** that Rachel said to Jacob: **"Give me children, or else I am dead"** (Genesis 30:1).

*De'amar mar arba'a chashuvim k'mes* — the master taught that four types are considered like dead. *Elu hen*: *ani*, a poor person; *suma*, a blind person; *metzora*, one afflicted with *tzara'as*; and *mi she'ein lo vanim*, one who has no children.

How do we know poverty is like death? *Ki mesu kol ha'anashim* — for all the men who sought your life have died, said to *Moshe*. Who were those men? *Datan va'Aviram*. But did they actually die? We know they were alive later when they rebelled against *Moshe*. Rather, *sheyardu minechaseihem* — they had become impoverished, and therefore it is as if they had died, stripped of influence and standing. How do we know blindness is like death? *Bemakhshakhim hoshivani kemetei olam* — You have placed me in darkness like the eternally dead. For *metzora*: *al na tehi kemet* — let our sister not be like dead, said of *Miriam* when she was afflicted with *tzara'as*, banished from the community. For one without children: *havah li banim ve'im ayin metah anokhi* — give me children, and if not I am dead.

These are striking statements that deserve careful reading. The *gemara* is likely pointing to societal realities — that those who lack wealth, sight, physical wholeness, or children are sometimes rendered invisible, devalued, as if they do not exist. This does not mean that such people *ought* to be treated that way, but it reflects a reality that we are obligated to work against. The four together, then, give content to the *tanna kamma*'s reading of *mai misa aniyus* — death here means the range of profound suffering and marginalization that human beings can experience.

## Moses' Rebuke

### God's Offer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִם בְּחֻקֹּתַי תֵּלֵכוּ״, אֵין ״אִם״ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן תַּחֲנוּנִים, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לוּ עַמִּי שׁוֹמֵעַ לִי [וְגוֹ׳] כִּמְעַט אוֹיְבֵיהֶם אַכְנִיעַ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״לוּ הִקְשַׁבְתָּ לְמִצְוֹתָי וַיְהִי כַנָּהָר שְׁלוֹמֶךָ וְגוֹ׳ וַיְהִי כַחוֹל זַרְעֶךָ וְצֶאֱצָאֵי מֵעֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: **"If you walk in My statutes"** (Leviticus 26:3): In this context, **"if"** is **a term** that means **nothing other than supplication,** i.e., God is hoping that the Jewish people will observe the Torah. **And similarly, it is stated: "Oh that My people would hearken to Me,** that Israel would walk in My ways, **I would soon subdue their enemies"** (Psalms 81:14–15). **And it states: "Oh that you would hearken to My commandments! Then your peace would be as a river,** and your righteousness as the waves of the sea. **Your seed also would be as the sand, and the offspring of your body** like its grains" (Isaiah 48:18–19).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מִי יִתֵּן וְהָיָה לְבָבָם זֶה לָהֶם״, אָמַר לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: כְּפוּיֵי טוֹבָה בְּנֵי כְּפוּיֵי טוֹבָה, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: ״מִי יִתֵּן וְהָיָה לְבָבָם זֶה לָהֶם״, הָיָה לָהֶם לוֹמַר: תֵּן אַתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to a verse cited above. **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: **"Who would give that they had such a heart as this** always, to fear Me, and keep all My commandments, that it might be good for them, and with their children forever" (Deuteronomy 5:26). At a later stage, **Moses said to the Jewish people: Ingrates, children of ingrates! When the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to the Jewish people: "Who would give that they had such a heart as this always," they should have said: You should give** us a heart to fear You.

*Tanu rabbanan*: *im be-hukkotai telechu* — the *ein im ela lashon takhnunim*, the word *im* here carries a tone of pleading, as if *Moshe* is beseeching Israel to keep the *mitzvos*. As the *pasuk* says: *lu ammi shome'a li* — oh, if only my people would listen to me — *kim'at oyeveihem akhni'a* — I would subdue their enemies swiftly. *V'lu hikshavta le-mitzvotai vayehi khannahar shelomekha* — had you listened to My commandments, your peace would flow like a river.

The discussion turns to *mi yitten vehayah levavam zeh lahem*. *Amar lahem Moshe le-Yisrael, kefuyyei tovah benei kefuyyei tovah* — *Moshe* said to Israel: ingrates, children of ingrates. When God said *mi yitten vehayah levavam zeh* — if only their hearts would be like this — *hayah lahem lomar ten attah* — they should have said: give it to us, God. God was opening a door: if only you could be at this level forever. The correct response was to seize that moment and say, yes, grant us this. Instead, they turned away from the goodness being offered before their eyes.

### Ungrateful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּפוּיֵי טוֹבָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנַפְשֵׁנוּ קָצָה בַּלֶּחֶם הַקְּלֹקֵל״. בְּנֵי כְּפוּיֵי טוֹבָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״הָאִשָּׁה אֲשֶׁר נָתַתָּה עִמָּדִי הִיא נָתְנָה לִּי מִן הָעֵץ וָאֹכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that Moses calls the Jewish people **ingrates, as it is written** that the Jewish people spoke disparagingly of the manna: **"And our soul loathes this light bread"** (Numbers 21:5), despite the fact it was the highest-quality food. Moses further called them **children of ingrates, as it is written** that after sinning and eating from the tree of knowledge, Adam said: **"The woman whom You gave to be with me, she gave me of the tree, and I did eat"** (Genesis 3:12). Adam complained that the woman had been given to him in order to cause him to sin, whereas in fact she had been given to him to serve as a helpmate.

*Kefuyyei tovah dikhtiv* — and how do we know they are ingrates? Because *Moshe*, speaking forty years later in *Sefer Devarim*, frames it this way — retroactively demonstrating that ingratitude is a pattern. The *pasuk* cited: *venafshenu katzah balekhem hakkalokel* — our soul is disgusted by this insubstantial bread. They complained about the *manna*. The *manna*, as *Rashi* explains, left no waste product — an extraordinary blessing, in that there was nothing unclean or burdensome about it. Yet Israel framed it as something unnatural and punishing, failing to recognize the tremendous gift God had given them.

### Adam's Blame

*Benei kefuyyei tovah* — and they are also the children of ingrates — *dikhtiv ha'ishah asher natata imadi hi natna li min ha'etz v'ochel* — Adam said, the woman *You* gave me, she gave me from the tree, and I ate. Adam took the goodness God had given him — a companion — and turned her into the excuse for his transgression. The pattern is the same: receiving great goodness and converting it into a complaint or a deflection rather than gratitude.

### Forty Years Later

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לֹא רְמָזָהּ לָהֶן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶלָּא לְאַחַר אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאוֹלֵךְ אֶתְכֶם בַּמִּדְבָּר אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא נָתַן ה׳ לָכֶם לֵב וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבָּה: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָא קָאֵי אִינִישׁ אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּרַבֵּיהּ עַד אַרְבְּעִין שְׁנִין.
>
> **Translation:** Yet **even Moses our teacher,** who said this to the Jewish people, **did not allude to the Jewish people until after forty years** that they should have stated this request, **as it is stated: "And I have led you forty years in the wilderness"** (Deuteronomy 29:4), which shows that Moses was speaking forty years after the revelation at Sinai. **And** at that point **it is written: "But the Lord has not given you a heart** to know, and eyes to see, and ears to hear, until this day" (Deuteronomy 29:3). **Rabba said: Conclude from** here that **a person does not understand the opinion of his teacher until** after **forty years,** as Moses said this to the Jewish people only after forty years of learning Torah.

*Af Moshe Rabbeinu lo rimzam l'Yisrael ela l'achar arba'im shanah* — even *Moshe* himself did not hint this idea to Israel until forty years had passed. Only in *Sefer Devarim*, when retelling the events of *Ma'amad Har Sinai*, did *Moshe* frame it in this way — that they had missed an extraordinary opening that God had offered them. The *pasuk*: *va'olech es'chem bamidbar arba'im shanah* — and I led you in the wilderness forty years — *v'lo natan Hashem lachem lev lada'at v'einayim lirot v'oznayim lishmo'a ad hayom hazeh* — and God did not give you a heart to understand, eyes to see, and ears to hear until this day.

### Master's Teaching

*Amar Rabbah shma mina*: from this we may infer that *lo ka'i inish ada'teih d'rabbeih ad arba'im shanin* — a person does not truly understand his master until forty years have passed. The question is who the *inish* refers to. *Rashi* understands that even *Moshe* did not grasp what God had meant at that moment — that God was opening a door with the words *mi yitien* — until forty years later. Others understand it to mean that *Bnei Yisrael* could not have appreciated what that moment signified until forty years of growth had passed, and *Moshe* knew this and therefore waited.

## Torah Controls Yetzer

### Torah & Charity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי בְּנָאָה, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אַשְׁרֵיכֶם זֹרְעֵי עַל כׇּל מָיִם מְשַׁלְּחֵי רֶגֶל הַשּׁוֹר וְהַחֲמוֹר״? אַשְׁרֵיהֶם יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּזְמַן שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בְּתוֹרָה וּבִגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים, יִצְרָם מָסוּר בְּיָדָם וְאֵין הֵם מְסוּרִים בְּיַד יִצְרָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַשְׁרֵיכֶם זֹרְעֵי עַל כׇּל מָיִם״, וְאֵין זְרִיעָה אֶלָּא צְדָקָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זִרְעוּ לָכֶם לִצְדָקָה וְקִצְרוּ לְפִי חֶסֶד״, וְאֵין מַיִם אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹי כׇּל צָמֵא לְכוּ לַמַּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Bana'a: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Happy are you that sow beside all waters, that send forth freely the feet of the ox and the donkey"** (Isaiah 32:20)? **Happy are you Israel; when they,** i.e., the Jewish people, **engage in Torah** study **and in acts of kindness, their** evil **inclination is given over to them, and they are not given over to their** evil **inclination, as it is stated: "Happy are you that sow beside all waters." And** the term **sowing** is referring to **nothing other than** performing **charitable** deeds, **as it is stated: "Sow to yourselves according to charitableness, reap according to mercy"** (Hosea 10:12). **And** the term **waters** is referring to **nothing other than** the **Torah, as it is stated: "Ho, every one that thirsts, come for water"** (Isaiah 55:1).

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Banna'ah*: what is meant by the *pasuk ashreichem zorei al kol mayim m'shalchei regel hashor v'hachamor* — praised are you who sow upon all waters, who send forth the leg of the ox and the donkey? *Ashreihem Yisrael bizman she'oskim baTorah u'vigmilut chasadim* — praised is Israel when they engage in Torah and *gemilut chasadim*. *Yitzram masur beyadam* — their *yetzer hara* is delivered into their hands; they control it. *V'ein hem mesurim beyad yitzram* — the *yetzer* does not control them.

This connects directly to the larger themes of the *masechet*. The *masechet*'s halachic *takanot* — don't drink their wine, limit commercial interaction — are one layer of protection against the temptations of the surrounding world. But the deeper protection is Torah and *gemilut chasadim*. If you are truly engaged in these, your *yetzer hara* is *masur beyadecha*.

*V'ein zri'ah ela tzedakah* — "sowing" refers to *tzedakah*, which is *gemilut chasadim*, as the *pasuk* says: *zir'u lachem l'tzedakah v'kitzru l'fi chesed* — sow for yourselves for righteousness and harvest for *chesed*. *V'ein mayim ela Torah* — and "waters" refers to Torah, as the *pasuk* says: *hoy kol tzamei l'chu lamayim* — all who are thirsty, come to the waters.

### Ox & Donkey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מְשַׁלְּחֵי רֶגֶל הַשּׁוֹר וְהַחֲמוֹר״, תָּנָא דְּבֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ: לָעוֹלָם יָשִׂים אָדָם עַצְמוֹ עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה כְּשׁוֹר לְעוֹל וְכַחֲמוֹר לְמַשּׂאוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the continuation of the verse: **"That send forth freely the feet of the ox and the donkey,"** one of the Sages of **the school of Eliyahu taught: A person should always make himself** subjugated **to matters of Torah like an ox to a yoke and like a donkey to a burden.**

What then is meant by *m'shalchei regel hashor v'hachamor*? *Tana d'vei Eliyahu*: a person should always make himself toward the words of Torah *k'shor l'ol* — like an ox to the yoke — *u'chachamor l'masoy* — and like a donkey to the burden. You must subjugate yourself to Torah with the acceptance of a working animal, willing to bear the full weight of the commitment. *Rashi* notes that this final teaching of *Tana d'vei Eliyahu* is somewhat distinct from the preceding point: the image of *meshalchin u-mashlichin raglei yetzer hara me'al adam me'alehem* — casting away the feet of the *yetzer hara* from upon a person — is the first interpretation, while the dedication of the ox and donkey is a parallel but independent point about the posture one must take toward Torah.

This concludes the extended opening *aggadata* of *masechet Avodah Zarah*.

## Three Days Rule

### Mishna Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים אָסוּר לָשֵׂאת וְלָתֵת עִמָּהֶם וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § After concluding its long introduction, the Gemara analyzes the mishna. The mishna taught that on **the three days** before the festivals of gentiles **it is prohibited to engage with them in business** or to lend them items.

We now return to the *mishna* and the halachic discussion: one may not conduct business with non-Jews for three days before their holidays — *gimel yamim*. The presumed concern is that they will rejoice at the good fortune of the transaction and bring that joy to their *avodah zarah* worship. However, the *gemara* here will focus more specifically on the concern that transactions may enable them to purchase items for their idolatrous rituals.

### Three Days Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי בָּעִינַן כּוּלֵּי הַאי? וְהָתְנַן: בְּאַרְבָּעָה פְּרָקִים בַּשָּׁנָה, הַמּוֹכֵר בְּהֵמָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעוֹ: ״אִמָּהּ מָכַרְתִּי לִשְׁחוֹט״, ״בִּתָּהּ מָכַרְתִּי לִשְׁחוֹט״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it the practice of a gentile to buy an animal so far in advance of his festival that it is **required** of Jews to refrain from selling an animal to idol worshippers for **all of these** days before their festivals? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Ḥullin* 83a): **On four occasions during the year one who sells an animal to another is required to inform him: I sold the mother of** this animal today for the buyer **to slaughter** it, or: **I sold the daughter of** this animal today for the buyer **to slaughter** it. This notification is necessary so that this buyer will not slaughter the animal on the same day that its mother or its young is slaughtered, thereby violating the prohibition: "You shall not kill it and its young both in one day" (Leviticus 22:28).

The *gemara* asks: *u'mi be'inan kulei hai* — do they really need three full days to acquire what they need for their holiday? Is such advance preparation genuinely required? The *gemara* raises a *mishna* as a comparison: *be'arba'ah perakim ba-shanah ha-mocher behemah le-chavero tzarich le-hodi'o* — at four times in the year, one who sells an animal to another must inform him whether the animal's mother or offspring was sold that same day, because of the prohibition of *oto ve-et beno lo tishchatu beyom echad* — the mother and child may not be slaughtered on the same day. Note that the transgression belongs to whoever causes both to be slaughtered on the same day, so the seller must inform the buyer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: עֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁל חַג, עֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל פֶּסַח, וְעֶרֶב עֲצֶרֶת, וְעֶרֶב רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, וּכְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי — אַף עֶרֶב יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים בַּגָּלִיל!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And those** four occasions **are: The eve of the last day of the festival** of *Sukkot*, **the eve of the first day of the festival of Passover, and the eve of *Shavuot*, and the eve of Rosh HaShana. And according to the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, the eve of Yom Kippur in the Galilee** is included **as well.** This mishna indicates that one prepares for a festival for only one day prior to the event. Why does the mishna here prohibit sales three days prior to gentile festivals?

The four times in the year when people are assumed to slaughter immediately upon purchase — so that this notification is practically necessary — are: *erev yom tov ha-acharon shel chag*, the eve of the last day of *Sukkot*, meaning *Shemini Atzeret*. *Tosafot* notes that the first day of *Sukkot* is not listed because people are occupied with their *sukkah* and *lulav* and are not necessarily focused on preparing a major festive meal; furthermore, *Shemini Atzeret* has its own independent identity and deserves particular emphasis as a day of *simcha*. Then: *erev yom tov ha-rishon shel Pesach*, the eve of the first day of *Pesach*; *ve'erev Atzeret*, before *Shavuot*; and *ve'erev Rosh Hashanah*, before *Rosh Hashanah*. *U'ke-divrei Rabbi Yose ha-Gelili af erev Yom ha-Kippurim ba-Galil* — and according to *Rabbi Yosei ha-Gelili*, also the eve of *Yom Kippur* in the *Galil*, because of the *mitzvah* to eat on *erev Yom Kippur*.

What the *gemara* draws from this *mishna* is that even for a major feast, people buy only one day in advance — not three. There is an inherent awkwardness in the *gemara*'s reasoning here: it is applying the patterns of Jewish festival practice to the question of what non-Jewish pagans do in preparation for their idolatrous holidays. The *gemara* is working within a framework of rabbinic discourse and treating this as a question that must be resolved through the available halachic models, even if those models reflect specifically Jewish realities. One possible way to understand this is that the *gemara* is seeking a standardized model — since pagan practices vary across Rome, Syria, North Africa, and elsewhere, some fixed benchmark is needed, and the Jewish model provides the available reference point.

### Sacrifice vs. Feast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם דְּלַאֲכִילָה, סַגִּיא בְּחַד יוֹמָא. הָכָא דִּלְהַקְרָבָה, בָּעִינַן תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the mishna in *Ḥullin*, **where** the animal is purchased **for consumption, it is sufficient** to prepare **one day** before the festival. **Here,** in the case of this mishna, where the animal is purchased **for sacrifice,** the gentile **requires three days** to ensure that the animal has no blemish.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *hasam de'achilah, sagi be'chad yoma* — there, for a feast, one day's advance purchase is sufficient. *Hacha de'hakravah, binan tlata yomei* — here, the concern is not festive eating but sacrificial offering, and for that, three days are needed to verify the animal.

### Blemish Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַקְרָבָה סַגִּי בִּתְלָתָא יוֹמֵי? וְהָתְנַן: שׁוֹאֲלִין בְּהִלְכוֹת הַפֶּסַח קוֹדֶם הַפֶּסַח שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: שְׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת. אֲנַן דִּשְׁכִיחִי מוּמִין דְּפָסְלִי, אֲפִילּוּ בְּדוּקִּין שֶׁבָּעַיִן, בָּעִינַן תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין; אִינְהוּ, דִּמְחוּסַּר אֵבֶר אִית לְהוּ, בִּתְלָתָא יוֹמֵי סַגִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And for** the purpose of **sacrifice,** is it **sufficient** to prepare merely **three days** in advance? **But didn't we learn** in a *baraita*: **One** begins to **ask about the *halakhot* of Passover thirty days before Passover; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** One begins to inquire only **two weeks** before Passover. According to both opinions, far more than three days are needed in order to begin preparing for the sacrifice of an offering. One can assume that the same applies to gentiles. The Gemara answers: **We** Jews, **for whom blemishes that disqualify** the animal **are common,** as **even** a small blemish **on the cornea of the eye** disqualifies it, **we require thirty days** to prepare for the sacrificial offerings of the festivals. By contrast, with regard to **them,** gentiles, **they hold** that the only blemish that disqualifies the animal is if it is **lacking** an entire **limb;** therefore, **three days are sufficient** for them to prepare for the sacrifice.

But does inspection for sacrifice really require three days? *V'hatanya: sho'alin vedorshin behilchos hapesach kodem hapesach sheloshim yom* — the *braisa* teaches that one begins asking and studying the laws of *Pesach* thirty days before, which the *gemara* understands to include beginning the process of acquiring *korbanot*. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says two weeks. Why do Jews need thirty days or two weeks? *Anan de'shchichi mumei de'paslei afilu bidukim she'ba'ayin* — for us, even minute blemishes — such as a slight film over the eye — are sufficient to disqualify an animal, so *binan tlashin yomei*, we need thirty days of preparation and inspection.

*Inhu de'mechusar eivar it lehu* — for them, the *gemara* holds, an animal is disqualified only if it is missing a limb entirely. This is a *halacha* pertaining to *Bnei Noach* who wish to bring a *korban* in the *Beis Hamikdash* — for them, a simple blemish is not disqualifying; only a missing limb renders it unfit. The *gemara* is again transparently applying the *halachos* of *Bnei Noach* and the *Beis Hamikdash* to the question of pagan sacrificial standards, which is a legal fiction of sorts: the reality of pagan sacrificial practices bears no direct relationship to these *halachos*. But the only lens the *gemara* has to work with is what it knows. Given this standard, *itlehu be'tlata yomei sagi* — three days is sufficient for them to determine whether the animal is missing a limb. Though it must be said: checking for a missing limb takes a matter of moments, not three days, and this gap in the reasoning is not fully resolved.

### Rabbi Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִנַּיִן לִמְחוּסַּר אֵבֶר דְּאָסוּר לִבְנֵי נֹחַ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמִכׇּל הָחַי מִכׇּל בָּשָׂר שְׁנַיִם מִכֹּל וְגוֹ׳״, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: הָבֵא בְּהֵמָה שֶׁחַיִּין רָאשֵׁי אֵבָרִים שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Elazar says: From where** is it derived with regard to an animal **that is lacking a limb, that it is prohibited for descendants of Noah** to sacrifice it as an offering? **As it is written: "And of every living thing of all flesh, two of every** sort you shall bring into the ark, to keep them alive with you" (Genesis 6:19). In this verse **the Torah states: Bring an animal whose extremities are alive,** as some of those animals were to be used later as offerings.

*De'amar Rabbi Elazar minayin lemechusar eivar de'asur livnei Noach* — how do we derive that a *Bnei Noach* may not bring an animal missing a limb? *Dichsiv umikol hachai mikol basar shenayim mikol* — from the *pasuk* commanding *Noach* to bring two of every living creature into the ark, which implies a standard of completeness.

*Rashi* at this point attempts to bridge the gap between the *halachos* of *Bnei Noach* bringing *korbanot* to the *Beis Hamikdash* and the reality of pagan sacrificial practice. He writes: *lehakriv legavoah, vedumya de'korbanos shenahagu avoseihem leshamayim hem nohagim b'avodah zarah shelahem* — we assume that the same standards their ancestors maintained when bringing *korbanot* to God, they have carried over into their *avodah zarah* practice. It is an attempt to create a bridge, though the underlying strangeness remains — the *gemara* is consistently reading non-Jewish pagan practice through the prism of the only sacrificial law it knows: that of *Bnei Noach* and the *Beis Hamikdash*.
