# Nazir 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening Mishnah & Tumah

### Opening & Daf Overview

We open today with daf 47, amud alef, picking up with the *mishnah* at the very top of the page. We are concluding one *perek* and beginning the next.

### Beard Shaving Q&A

Before turning to the text, one clarification worth noting: a *nazir* is only prohibited from cutting the hair of the head. Shaving the beard presents no problem whatsoever, since the prohibition of *nezirut* concerns only the long hair of the head.

### Mishnah: Blood & Tumah

The *mishnah* reads: *mi shenizrak alav echad min hadamim venitma* — one upon whom one of the blood-sprinklings has been performed and then became *tamei*. To set the context: we have just concluded the extended discussion in which the *chachamim* hold that even a single *korban* completes one's *nezirut*. The *mishnah* now addresses the case where, after one *korban* has already been brought, the *nazir* becomes *tamei*. What is the consequence?

### Eliezer vs. Chachamim

*Rabbi Eliezer omer soter et hakol* — Rabbi Eliezer says it undoes everything. This is a natural extension of Rabbi Eliezer's position from earlier, that *achar ma'aseh kulam* — one requires all the *korbanot* to complete *nezirut*. Since the *nezirut* was not yet concluded, the subsequent *tumah* destroys whatever had been accomplished.

*Vachachamim omrim yavi shear korbanotav veyit'har* — the *chachamim* say that the *nezirut* is indeed finished. The *tumah* does not undermine anything; the *korban* already brought remains valid. All that remains is the obligation to bring the remaining *korbanot*, which she completes after becoming *tahor*.

## Tosafot: Tahara Before K…

### Tosafot: Veyit'har Order

The language of the *mishnah* — *yavi shear korbanotav veyit'har* — might seem to suggest that one brings the *korbanot* first and only afterwards becomes *tahor*. Tosafot addresses this directly, explaining that *veyit'har* in this context means: *lichsheyit'har* — only once one has become *tahor* may the remaining *korbanot* be brought. The order in the *mishnah* should not be read literally as "bring and then purify," but rather "bring [once purified]."

### Sources: Yerushalmi & Pe…

Tosafot cites the *Yerushalmi*, which reads the *mishnah* straightforwardly as requiring *taharah* first and only then bringing the *korbanot*: *kach mefaresh biyerushalmi stam matnita d'tahara v'achar kach yavi korbanotav*.

The basis for this reading lies in the *pesukim* themselves. The *Torah*, when addressing a *nazir* who became *tamei meit*, describes a process in which he first achieves full *taharah* and only on the eighth day brings his *korban*. This reflects an implicit *Torah* demand: a *nazir* may not have his *korbanot* of *nezirut* brought while in a state of *tumah*. There is a broader *halachic* discussion about *tamei meshalayach korbanotav* — whether a *tamei* person may have *korbanot* brought on his behalf — and the ruling is that only certain light levels of *tumah* permit this. One who is *tamei meit*, or one who is a *metzora* or *zav*, certainly cannot be *meshalayach korbanotav*. But Tosafot's point is more specific: even if one might debate the general rule, in the particular context of a *nazir* who is *tamei meit*, the *pesukim* make clear that the *Torah* insists on full *taharah* before any *korbanot* of *nezirut* are brought.

### Why Tumah Can't Destroy…

Once we understand this, Tosafot explains why the *tumah* cannot destroy what has already been accomplished. The *korban* that was brought while the *nazir* was still *tahor* was valid — *harei hu kodem shenitma ra'uy litiglachas*, before he became *tamei* he was fit for the *tiglachat*, and the *korban* was brought in a state of *hechsher*. There is therefore no basis to disqualify it retroactively. However, any *korban* brought after the *tumah* but before achieving *taharah* would indeed be invalid: *aval hazevachim shehikriv mishenitma yistor* — whatever he brought after becoming *tamei* and before becoming *tahor* does not count.

The principle Tosafot articulates is: *dekapid kra sheyavi korbanot neziroot betaharah* — the *Torah* insists that the *korbanot* of *nezirut* be brought in a state of *taharah*. After the *nazir* undergoes *taharah*, including the *hazayah* of days three and seven, he may then bring the remaining *korbanot*.

## Eliezer: Soter Korba (II)

### Tosafot Clarification

This leads naturally to a clarification of what Rabbi Eliezer actually means when he says *soter et hakol*. The phrase sounds sweeping — it destroys everything — but we already know from an earlier *mishnah* that Rabbi Eliezer ruled that *tumah* after the full thirty days of *nezirut* requires only seven additional days, not the full thirty. So *soteret hakol* cannot literally mean all the days.

Tosafot makes this explicit in his very first comment here: *v'nitma Rabbi Eliezer omer soter hakol v'lo kol hayamim k'mforesh ba-Gemara d'Rabbi Eliezer hu d'amar l'eil nitma l'achar milo soter zayin ela soter korbanot kama* — Rabbi Eliezer says it destroys everything, but not all the days, as the *Gemara* clarifies; Rabbi Eliezer is the same one who said earlier that becoming *tamei* after the full thirty days requires only seven additional days. What is destroyed is not the count of days but the *korbanot* — the prior *korbanot* are nullified, and any subsequent *korbanot* are also invalid until the *nazir* properly completes his *nezirut*.

The context of the *mishnayot* throughout this section has been precisely this: whether the *korbanot* already brought remain valid or are destroyed. This is the *machloket* between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim*, and the relationship to the number of days is secondary — a natural consequence of whether the *nezirut* itself was complete, but not the primary focus.

## Miriam from Tarmod Story

### Miriam's Daughter Dies

The *chachamim* bolster their position with a practical precedent: *ma'aseh b'Miriam haTarmodit* — there was a story involving Miriam from Tarmod. She had taken upon herself a *neder* of *nezirut* and one of the blood-sprinklings had already been performed on her behalf — *shenizrak aleha echad min hadamim*. At that moment, she was informed that her daughter was in mortal danger. She ran to her daughter and found that she had died — *vehalcha umatza shemeta*. She thus became *tamei meit* after one *korban* had already been brought.

### Chachamim's Ruling

The *chachamim* ruled: *tavi shear korbanoteha v'tit-har* — let her bring the remaining *korbanot* once she becomes *tahor*. Her *nezirut* was complete after that first *korban*; the subsequent *tumah* did nothing to undermine it. The *korban* that had been brought stood, and her obligation was only to complete the remaining *korbanot* after achieving *taharah*.

This ruling stands as a direct practical refutation of Rabbi Eliezer's position. Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *tumah* destroys the *korbanot* and effectively nullifies what was accomplished; the *chachamim* demonstrated through the case of Miriam that this is not so.

## Eliezer: Soter Korba (II)

### Gemara: Soter Definition

The *Gemara* now steps back and presses the question: the *mishnah* states that Rabbi Eliezer says *soter et hakol*, and elsewhere Rabbi Eliezer ruled that *tumah* after the full thirty days requires only seven additional days — so what does *soter* mean here? *Amar Rav mai soter d'ka'amar Rabbi Eliezer soter korbanotav* — Rav states: the *soter* Rabbi Eliezer refers to is that it destroys the *korbanot*. Any *korban* previously brought is nullified, and any *korban* brought in an improper state is likewise invalid. The *chachamim*, by contrast, hold that the first *korban* remains fully valid — *hacha nami savarta d'chachamim im tavi shear korbanoteha v'tit-har, shma mina* — as the case of Miriam from Tarmod confirms: let her bring the remaining *korbanot* once she becomes *tahor*, and this resolves the question.

The focus, then, in both the *mishnah* and the story of Miriam, is on the *korbanot* — not on the number of days. Days are implicated insofar as destroying *nezirut* means more days are required, but what is immediately at stake is whether the *korbanot* already brought retain their validity.

## Transition to Kohanic Tu…

### Perek Summary & Tumah Fo…

With the declaration of *hadran alach sheloshah minim*, we conclude the *perek* of *sheloshah minim*. That *perek* was structured around the three core prohibitions of a *nazir*: wine, shaving, and *tumah*. These three areas opened broader *halachic* discussions with general relevance. The wine prohibition led into the discussion of *ta'am ke'ikar* — the principle that taste functions like substance in matters of forbidden mixtures. The prohibition on shaving led to an examination of what constitutes shaving with respect to the beard and the *pe'ot*. And the prohibition on *tumah* opened the discussion of *tumat kohanim* — whether a *kohen* or *nazir* who is already *tamei meit* transgresses again by becoming *tamei* again, and the laws of *meit mitzvah*.

### Preview: Nazir-Kohen Pri…

Now, as we enter the new *perek*, we continue and deepen the discussion of *tumah*, because there is much more to learn from the relationship between *nazir* and *kohen* with respect to the restrictions on *tumah*. The central organizing principle of the coming *perek* is the rule known as *kol tumah she'ein haNazir megale'ach aleha ein Kohen muzhar aleha* — any *tumah* for which a *nazir* does not shave, a *kohen* is not prohibited against.

This principle might seem to refer only to *tumat sheretz*, *tumat neveilah*, *zivah*, *tzara'at*, and the like — types of *tumah* that are simply outside the scope of what *nezirut* addresses. But in fact it reaches further: even within the domain of *tumat meit* itself, there are categories that, by received tradition, do not trigger the obligation for a *nazir* to shave or to restart his *nezirut*, and therefore a *kohen* is likewise not prohibited against them. This creates an important area of inquiry — things that are technically *tumat meit* and yet fall outside the prohibitions both of *nezirut* and of the *kohen*'s restrictions against *tumah*. This has direct practical relevance for contemporary questions involving *kohanim*.

## Kohen Gadol, Nazir & Mei…

### Priority: Kohen Gadol vs…

The *mishnah* states: *Kohen Gadol veNazir ein mitamim likroveihem* — a *kohen gadol* and a *nazir* do not become *tamei* even to their immediate relatives. This is a striking parallel. A normal *kohen* may become *tamei* to his seven close relatives; a *kohen gadol* cannot. Conceptually, the *kohen gadol* is always in a state of readiness to serve — *min hamikdash lo yeitzei* — he is never permitted to remove himself from that role, even for the death of an immediate family member. The *nazir*, interestingly, shares this same restriction according to the *pesukim*: he too cannot become *tamei* even to an immediate relative.

Both, however, do become *tamei* for a *meit mitzvah*: *aval mitamim lemeit mitzvah*. This is not stated explicitly in the *Torah* but is understood by the *rabbis* from the *pesukim*, as the *Gemara* will explain. The *mishnah* then addresses the question of priority: *hayu mehalchim baderech u-matzu meit mitzvah* — if a *kohen gadol* and a *nazir* are traveling together and encounter a *meit mitzvah*, who should become *tamei* first?

The underlying issue here touches on the concept of *hutra* versus *dichuya* — whether in situations like *meit mitzvah* or *pikuach nefesh*, the prohibition is entirely suspended (*hutra*), or merely overridden by a competing value (*dichuya*). The *dichuya* perspective leads naturally to the effort to minimize the degree of override: if only one person needs to become *tamei* to fulfill the obligation, we try to determine which person's *tumah* is least consequential.

### Eliezer: Kohen Gadol Pri…

Rabbi Eliezer says: *yitma Kohen she'eino meivi korban al tumato v'al yitma Nazir shemeivi korban al tumato* — let the *kohen* become *tamei*, because a *kohen*'s *tumah* (under normal circumstances) does not require him to bring a *korban* as part of the consequences. The *nazir*, on the other hand, whose becoming *tamei* destroys his *nezirut* and requires him to bring a *korban*, suffers far more grievous consequences. Even though under normal circumstances both would be violating a *lav*, the practical fallout for the *nazir* is far weightier. Therefore, protect the *nazir* and let the *kohen gadol* become *tamei*.

One might ask about a *nazir Shimshon* — such a *nazir* does not bring a *korban* for *tumah* to begin with, since a *nazir Shimshon mitamei lemeitim* in any case. But that would be a moot point, as the *nazir Shimshon*'s status is sui generis.

### Chachamim: Nazir Priority

The *chachamim* respond with a fundamentally different framework: *yitma Nazir she'ein kedushato kedushat olam v'al yitma Kohen shekedushato kedushat olam* — let the *nazir* become *tamei*, because the *nazir*'s *kedushah* is only a *kedushah* of time, a temporary status, while the *kohen*'s *kedushah* is permanent.

The argument is not about the practical consequences of becoming *tamei*, but about the quality of the violation. When a *kohen*'s *kedushah* is breached, something essential to his very identity is transgressed. A *nazir*'s *kedushah*, while real and meaningful, is a status he accepted upon himself, not an intrinsic identity. It may even be a temporary status — though Tosafot notes that even a *nazir olam* does not change this calculus, because the fundamental character of *nezirut* as a *kedushah sha'ah* remains. The *chachamim* go further: they maintain this principle even as against a *kohen hedyot*, not just a *kohen gadol*. For Rabbi Eliezer, even a *kohen gadol* becomes *tamei* before a *nazir*; for the *chachamim*, even a *nazir* becomes *tamei* before a *kohen hedyot*. This explains why the *girsa* shifts in the text when recording the *chachamim*'s position — they ground the priority in *kedushat olam*, which belongs to any *kohen*, not only the *kohen gadol*.

The tension between the two positions is illuminating: Rabbi Eliezer measures by *halachic* consequence, the *chachamim* by the ontological weight of the violation.

## Two Kohen Gadolim Cases

### Anointed vs. Garmented

The *Gemara* now proceeds through a series of thought experiments involving cases where two figures of comparable but not identical status encounter a *meit mitzvah* together. The first case: *Mashuach beshemen hamishcha u'merubeh bivgadim* — one *kohen gadol* who was anointed with the anointing oil, and another who attained the office of *kohen gadol* by having worn the eight priestly garments (*merubeh begadim*). The anointing oil was secreted away in the time of King Yoshiyahu, so the question concerns what would have been the rule had such a scenario existed.

The *Gemara* rules: *mashuach beshemen hamishcha adif* — the one anointed with the anointing oil has the higher status and is therefore protected from *tumah*. The reason: *de'illu mashuach beshemen hamishcha meivi par al kol hamitzvot* — there is a special *halacha* in Masechet Horayot that if a *kohen gadol* who was *nimshach beshemen hamishcha* issued an incorrect ruling and then followed his own ruling, he brings a special *par* as an *atonement korban*. This privilege and the elevated status it reflects belongs only to the *mashuach beshemen hamishcha*, not to the *merubeh begadim*. Therefore, the anointed one has an objectively higher status and is accordingly protected.

The question of how such a scenario could realistically arise — with both simultaneously alive and both either equally active or equally retired — is something Tosafot addresses, though the precise reconstruction is complex.

### Off-Duty vs. Active

The second case: *mashuach she'avar u'merubeh begadim* — a *mashuach beshemen hamishcha* who is no longer in active service, alongside one who is *merubeh begadim* and currently serving. The ruling here reverses the first: *merubeh begadim adif*, *de'merubeh begadim avid avodah* — the one currently in active service takes priority and is protected. The reason is straightforward: what overrides the inherent prestige of having been anointed with the oil is the immediate practical reality that this person is actively performing *avodah*, and *tumah* would interfere with that. *Ve'ilu mashuach she'avar lav bar avodah hu* — the one who has been permanently removed from service cannot perform *avodah* regardless; there is no comparable disruption if he becomes *tamei*.

### One-Day vs. Permanent Is…

The third case: *avar machmas kiryo ve'avar machmas muma* — two *kohanim gedolim* who are both currently off duty. One was temporarily removed because he was a *ba'al keri* (a seminal emission), and the other because of a bodily blemish. The ruling: *avar machmas kiryo adif* — the one temporarily off duty due to *keri* is protected, *de'hu chazi le'avodah lemachar* — because he will be able to return to *avodah* the following day after immersing in the *mikveh*. The one removed due to a blemish, *ve'ilu avar machmas muma lo chazi le'avodah* — cannot currently serve, and Tosafot adds that even if the blemish is potentially temporary, one does not know when it will heal, whereas the *ba'al keri*'s disqualification is precisely one day. All three of these cases follow a clear logical structure: the one whose active relationship with the *avodah* is more immediate and more restorable is always the one whose *taharah* is more protected.

## Mashuach Milchamah vs. S…

### Question Posed

Now the *Gemara* raises a question that is less immediately obvious: *iba'ya lehu mashuach milchama ve'sgan hai minaihu adif* — which has higher priority, the *mashuach milchama* or the *sgan*?

The *mashuach milchama* is the *kohen* anointed to lead the people into battle — the one who addresses the people before war with the words *mi ha'ish hayarei ve'rach halevav yelech ve'yashuv leveiso*. He holds a specially anointed role of considerable stature. The *sgan* is the vice *kohen gadol*, whose role is to stand ready to substitute for the *kohen gadol* should he become *tamei*, particularly on *yom kippur* when a *kohen gadol* is absolutely indispensable.

Both are a rank below the *kohen gadol*, functioning as executive vice-presidents of sorts. The question: if they both encounter a *meit mitzvah*, which one is protected and which one must become *tamei*? The *mashuach milchama* has an active, weighty role — he is not merely waiting in the wings, he is on stage. The *sgan*, one might think, is essentially defined by his readiness to step in, which only rarely becomes relevant. So perhaps the *mashuach milchama*'s status warrants protection?

### Baraita Answer

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in response: *tashma detanya, ein bein mashuach milchama lesgan ela she'im hayu mehalchim baderech u'matzu meit mitzvah, yitamei mashuach milchama ve'al yitamei sgan* — the only difference between the *mashuach milchama* and the *sgan* is this: if they are going on the way and find a *meit mitzvah*, the *mashuach milchama* becomes *tamei* and not the *sgan*. So the *sgan* is the one who is protected.

### Rationale: Sgan's Role

The rationale is precisely what makes this a striking ruling. The *sgan*'s entire job description is to remain *tahor* and ready. *Rebbi Chanina ben Antigonus omer: lama tiknu sgan lekohen gadol? she'im ye'arei bo psul harei nichnas umeshamesh tachtav* — why did they institute a *sgan* for the *kohen gadol*? So that if the *kohen gadol* becomes disqualified, the *sgan* enters and serves in his place. His primary responsibility is not some active task he is currently performing — it is the maintenance of his *taharah* and readiness. Becoming *tamei* would be a fundamental violation of the *sgan*'s core function in a way that the *mashuach milchama*'s becoming *tamei* is not. The *mashuach milchama*'s role does not require him to preserve his *taharah* as its central purpose; the *sgan*'s role does, almost by definition.

## Two Different Priorities

### Contradiction Noted

But this ruling seems to contradict another *baraita*: *mashuach milchama kodem lesgan* — the *mashuach milchama* comes before the *sgan*, apparently suggesting that the *mashuach milchama* has higher priority. Does this not contradict the rule that the *sgan* is protected from *tumah*?

### Two Contexts, Two Rules

The *Gemara* resolves: *le'inyan hachayoso mashuach milchama adif* — when it comes to saving a life, the *mashuach milchama* takes priority. *Mai ta'ama? detali beih rabim* — because the welfare of many depends on him. If one must choose whose life to save, the *mashuach milchama*'s role in protecting the entire people gives him priority, much as one would prioritize saving the Secretary of Defense over a deputy official whose role is primarily one of readiness.

*Ule'inyan tumah sgan adif* — but when it comes to *tumah*, the *sgan* is given priority and is protected. *Detanya Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus omer: lama tiknu sgan lekohen gadol* — because the entire rationale for the *sgan*'s existence is to remain *tahor* and ready to serve if needed. These are two different questions yielding two different answers, and there is no contradiction.

### Tosafot: Hierarchy Clari…

Tosafot raises an important point here. The *baraita* states *ein bein mashuach milchama lesgan* — there is no difference between them except for this matter of *tumah*. But this seems plainly incorrect: there are in fact many significant differences between the two. The *mashuach milchama* wears the garments of the *kohen gadol*, while the *sgan* wears the garments of a *kohen hedyot* — even when performing his role, and even in the context of *yom kippur*, when the *kohen gadol* himself wears *bigdei lavan*. Moreover, the five restrictions enumerated in the *parasha* for the *kohen gadol* — not disheveling his hair, not rending his garments, not becoming *tamei* to the dead, not marrying a widow, and being required to marry only a *betulah* — all of these apply to the *mashuach milchama* but not to the *sgan*.

This means the *mashuach milchama* is effectively a quasi-*kohen gadol* in a different sphere: not in the *Beit HaMikdash* but in the domain of the battlefield and the camp. So what does the *baraita* mean by *ein bein*?

Tosafot explains: the *ein bein* means that there is no area in which we give greater weight to the *sgan* over the *mashuach milchama*, except for this one matter — that the *sgan* does not become *tamei* when they encounter a *meit mitzvah* together. In every other domain, the *mashuach milchama* has equal or greater status. But in this one instance, because the *sgan*'s entire identity is bound up with maintaining *taharah*, that is the single exception where the *sgan* is protected and the *mashuach milchama* is not.

## Individual Override for…

### Individual Permission

Having completed the analysis of who takes priority when a *kohen gadol* and *nazir* travel together, the *Gemara* steps back to address the foundational premise. *Ad kan lo peligi ela be-Kohen Gadol ve-Nazir ki ka azli bahadi hadadi* — all the debate has been about who takes priority when they are traveling together. *Aval chad chad le-chudei bartzit meiyei inun* — but if each of them is alone, it is taken for granted that each one individually is permitted to become *tamei* to a *meit mitzvah*.

### Source Question

This prompts a fundamental question: *minani milei* — where does this come from? The *Torah* never states explicitly that a *meit mitzvah* overrides the prohibitions on *tumah* for a *kohen gadol* or a *nazir*. What is the textual source?

## Pesukkim: Al Kol Nafshot…

### Pesuk: Le'aviv U'le'imo

The *Gemara* cites the relevant *pesukim*. Regarding the *kohen gadol* it is written: *al kol nafshot met lo yavo* — he shall not approach any dead person, and separately: *le'aviv u'le'imo lo yitama* — not even to his father and mother shall he become *tamei*.

### Kal Va'chomer Logic

The *Gemara* asks: who is the subject of *al kol nafshot met lo yavo*? If it is addressing non-relatives, this would be redundant — *kal va-chomer hu*. A normal *kohen* who may become *tamei* to his relatives may not become *tamei* to non-relatives; how much more so a *kohen gadol* who cannot even become *tamei* to relatives — surely he cannot become *tamei* to non-relatives. There would be no need for the *Torah* to state this explicitly. Tosafot frames the same point more directly, without the circularity of a formal *kal va-chomer*: a *kohen gadol* is already a *kohen*, and as such is already prohibited against *tumah* to non-relatives in his basic capacity as a *kohen*. Therefore *al kol nafshot met* cannot be teaching us about non-relatives.

### Both Phrases Extra?

It follows that *al kol nafshot met lo yavo* must be referring to relatives — the *Torah* is telling us that a *kohen gadol* may not become *tamei* even to his own family. If that is so, then the subsequent phrase *le'aviv u'le'imo lo yitama* — not to his father and mother — appears to be entirely redundant. We have already been told he cannot become *tamei* to any relative. Why repeat it for father and mother specifically?

### Extra Words Teach New Ru…

Because both phrases are extra, each can be expounded to teach something new. *Le'aviv* — "to his father" — is cited as an example: *ha mitamei hu le-met mitzvah* — but he does become *tamei* to a *meit mitzvah*. The specific mention of *le'aviv* signals that there is someone to whom he may become *tamei* — and this is the *meit mitzvah*. The logic follows the principle of *miyut achar miyut ela lerabbot*: two restrictions in sequence imply that within those restrictions there is a permitted category. The *Torah* has already said he cannot become *tamei* to relatives; adding "not to the father" implies — but there is one case where he can, namely a *meit mitzvah*.

## Gezeira Shava: Le'imo Co…

### Nazir's Le'imo Scope

The *Gemara* now turns to *le'imo* — "to his mother" — and brings a *braisa* in the name of *Rebbi* that opens with the *nazir*. A *nazir* is told *lo yitama lahem bemotam* — he shall not become *tamei* to them in their deaths. The word *bemotam* — "in their deaths" — is significant: it specifies *tumat meit* as the prohibited *tumah*, thereby implying that other types of *tumah* — such as *tzara'at* or *zivah* — do not fall under the *nazir*'s prohibition. *Gabi nazir bemotam eino mitamei aval mitamei hu lenigam ulezivatam* — for a *nazir*, the prohibition is specifically against becoming *tamei* through death-related *tumah*; contact with a *metzora* or a *zav* is not prohibited.

This might seem obvious, since the *nazir*'s prohibition is textually framed around corpse-*tumah*. But it sets up the crucial *gezeira shava*.

### Applied to Kohen Gadol

With respect to the *kohen gadol*, the question is different. The *pesukim* say *le'aviv u'le'imo lo yitama* — not to his father and mother shall he become *tamei*. These words do not specify *bemotam*; they simply say he shall not become *tamei*. One might therefore think the prohibition encompasses any *tumah* his father or mother might have — whether *tumat meit*, *tzara'at*, or *zivah*. *Bekohen gadol minalan* — how do we know that for a *kohen gadol* as well, the restriction is limited to *tumat meit*?

The answer is a *gezeira shava*: *ne'emar imo benazir vene'emar imo bekohen gadol* — the word *imo* ("his mother") appears both by the *nazir* and by the *kohen gadol*. This verbal parallel licenses a *halachic* connection: *mah imo ha'amur benazir bemotam eino mitamei aval mitamei hu lenigam ulezivatam* — just as by the *nazir*, the prohibition is limited to *tumat meit* (as established by *bemotam*), *af imo ha'amur bekohen gadol bemotam eino mitamei aval mitamei hu lenigam ulezivatam* — so too by the *kohen gadol*, the restriction is only against *tumat meit*, and contact with a *metzora* or *zav* is not prohibited.

### Redundancy Analysis

The overall structure of the exposition is now complete. Since *al kol nafshot met lo yavo* must refer to relatives (as argued above), both *le'aviv* and *le'imo* are redundant within the *pesuk*. Each extra phrase comes to teach a distinct *halacha*: *le'aviv* teaches that there is a case — *meit mitzvah* — where even a *kohen gadol* becomes *tamei*; and *le'imo*, through the *gezeira shava* with the *nazir*, teaches that the *kohen gadol*'s prohibition is restricted to *tumat meit* and does not extend to other forms of *tumah*. These are not references to the *kohen gadol*'s specific father or mother — once *al kol nafshot met* encompasses all relatives, *le'aviv* and *le'imo* become free to carry new independent *halachic* content, as extra words in the *Torah* are never without purpose.
