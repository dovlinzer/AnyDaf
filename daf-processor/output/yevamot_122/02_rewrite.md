# Yevamot 122 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jew Testimony

### Non-Jew Threat Case

The final daf of Masechet Yevamot picks up three lines from the bottom of 121b, in the midst of the broad question of who may be believed to testify that a man has died. The mishnah had already introduced the categories of women, children, and even incidental statements — *mesiach lefi tumo* — made in passing without deliberate intent to testify. The Gemara now turns to a specific case that tests the limits of accepting such testimony from a non-Jew.

A certain non-Jew told a Jew: cut down this fodder and throw it to my animals on Shabbat. And if you refuse, I will kill you just as I killed a certain other Jew — Reuven ben Shimon — who refused to cook for me on Shabbat. The non-Jew's threat was overheard by the wife of the man he claimed to have killed. She came before Abaye, reasoning that the statement sounded like *mesiach lefi tumo*: the non-Jew was not trying to testify but was simply invoking the killing as a point of intimidation, incidentally revealing what had happened. Could she assume her husband was dead and remarry?

Abaye kept her waiting through three *regalim*. Rashi offers two explanations for this delay. The first is that the *regalim* — the pilgrimage festivals — were occasions when Torah scholars gathered to hear discourses on the laws of each *chag*, and Abaye was using those assemblies to consult colleagues. The second reading comes from a responsum of the Geonim: that *regalim* as used by Amoraim does not refer to the pilgrimage festivals at all, but to the annual yahrzeit commemorations of great sages. According to this reading, Abaye waited three years. The Geonim describe the yahrzeit of a major *talmid chacham* as an occasion when scholars from the surrounding area would gather at his grave and hold a day of Torah study in his memory — essentially a yahrzeit lecture and commemorative gathering. This is fascinating not because the Gemara explicitly says any of this, but because the Geonim read the practice into it, revealing that such commemorations were already established by their time.

In any event, Abaye remained unresolved after all three consultations. Rav Adda bar Ahava told the woman she should go to Rav Yosef, Abaye's own teacher, who has a sharp knife — *deharif sakhina* — meaning he can cut through ambiguous questions decisively. She went, and Rav Yosef resolved the matter from a *braita* about a non-Jew selling fruit in the marketplace who said: these fruits are from a tree only in its second year, or from the place called Azaka, or from a fourth-year planting — *neta revai*. The Gemara rules that such a statement carries no weight — *lo amar klum* — because the seller was not speaking incidentally but with an ulterior motive: to make his wares sound more appealing. He was not *mesiach lefi tumo*; he had a reason to say what he said.

A brief but significant digression arises regarding the term *azaka*. Rashi prefers the reading that it refers to a place in Eretz Yisrael, raising questions of *tevel* when the fruit is sold in *chutz la'aretz*. He rejects the alternative reading — that the fruit came from a field that had been guarded during *shmita* — because he sees no reason why guarding a field during the sabbatical year would render its fruit forbidden. However, Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Tam, who brings the *Torat Kohanim* explicitly: "Do not harvest your grapes from the guarded" — *min hamshumar*; one may only harvest from produce that has been declared *hefker*. The act of guarding the field — exerting ownership over it — renders the fruit forbidden for harvest and likely forbidden altogether. It is striking that so central a question of *shmita* law finds its primary textual basis in the *Torat Kohanim* alone, with neither the mishnah nor the Yerushalmi addressing it directly.

Returning to the main thread: how is the fruit-seller case relevant to the woman whose husband may have been killed? Because in that case too, the non-Jew had a reason to say what he said — not to inform, but to threaten. He was coercing the Jew into violating Shabbat by claiming to have already killed someone who refused him. That is not *mesiach lefi tumo*. There is good reason to think he was lying, or at minimum not speaking as a disinterested party. The answer Rav Yosef gave was therefore that the woman could not remarry — not a useful result for her, though one might speculate that Abaye, who kept delaying, sensed this conclusion and was searching for a way to permit her rather than to forbid.

One might push back: the non-Jew was extremely specific — he named Reuven ben Shimon — and the man had not been seen for three years. That specificity might have been a reason to credit the testimony. But the Gemara is unpersuaded, because the motivation to threaten overrides the specificity. The principle that emerges is that *mesiach lefi tumo* is qualified: it does not operate when there is affirmative reason to think the speaker may be fabricating or exaggerating.

## Witnessing Standard (III)

### Geonic Responsum

As noted above, within the discussion of Abaye's extended deliberation, Rashi introduces a Geonic responsum identifying the *regalim* mentioned in the Gemara not as pilgrimage festivals but as the yearly yahrzeit gatherings held at the graves of major sages. The Geonim describe a practice in which, *midei shanah be-shanah*, scholars from the surrounding region would convene at the sage's burial site each year on the anniversary of his death, together with the broader community, to conduct a *yeshivah* — a day of Torah study — in his honor. This represents the earliest known reference to something resembling the yahrzeit lecture as an institution, though the Gemara itself says nothing of the kind. It is the Geonim who read this practice into the text, revealing both their interpretive creativity and the antiquity of the commemoration as a living custom by their era.

## Non-Jew Testimony (II)

### Fruit Seller Example

The analysis of the fruit-seller case establishes the controlling principle for when non-Jewish testimony is rejected: not because of a categorical rule against non-Jews, but because in that instance there was an identifiable motive for the speaker to say what he said that was independent of its truth. When the non-Jew in the market claimed his fruit came from a young tree or a protected field or from Eretz Yisrael, he was doing so to enhance the perceived value of his merchandise. His statement was therefore not a genuine *mesiach lefi tumo* — it was not an incidental, unmotivated disclosure, but a sales pitch. If we do not treat such a statement as reliable enough to be *machmir* — to create a *halakhic* concern about *orla* or *tevel* — then certainly we cannot treat it as reliable enough to be *meikel* — to permit a woman to remarry. The asymmetry is significant: even the lenient application of non-Jewish testimony requires the baseline of *mesiach lefi tumo*, which is absent when the speaker has an ulterior motive.

### Accepted Testimonies

In contrast, the Gemara proceeds to a series of cases in which non-Jewish testimony was fully accepted. Abba Yudan Ish Tzaidan reports: a Jew and a non-Jew were traveling together. The non-Jew returned alone and said, *chaval al Yehudi she-hayah imi baderech* — woe to the Jew who was traveling with me — he died on the way and I buried him. On that basis, his wife was permitted to remarry. This is a straightforward case of *mesiach lefi tumo*: the non-Jew was not being questioned, not trying to establish a legal fact, simply lamenting out loud.

A second story involves a group of people, described as a *kolar shel bnei adam*, who were traveling to Antioch. A non-Jew who had come from the same direction — not even a member of their party — arrived and said: woe to that group of people, they all died and I buried them. Their wives were married off on this basis. A third story: sixty people traveling toward the besieged city of *Karkom Beitar* — where *karkom* means besieged, not a place name — and a non-Jew who reported that they had all died and he had buried them. Their wives too were permitted to remarry.

It is worth pausing on the context of these final two cases. If *kolar* means that the first group were prisoners shackled with collars, then there was already an elevated likelihood that something terrible had happened to them. And people traveling toward a city under siege face obvious danger. It is not entirely clear how much the surrounding context of likely peril contributed to the credibility of the non-Jew's report, but the Gemara does not explicitly make that a condition. The principle appears to be that *mesiach lefi tumo* — the incidental statement without identifiable motive — is sufficient, and the Gemara is willing to be quite broad in applying it.

One might note that in none of these cases does the Gemara describe any attempt to verify the facts — to locate the grave, to find corroborating witnesses, to identify the body. The question the Gemara addresses is purely one of whose testimony is admitted, not of evidentiary sufficiency in the modern sense. These *halakhot*, it is worth noting, have found real application in tragic historical circumstances, including in *responsa* literature dealing with mass casualties where identifying individual deaths was impossible.

## Witnessing Standard (III)

### Light Requirements

The next mishnah shifts from the question of *who* may testify to the question of *how* clearly the death must have been witnessed. The mishnah rules: one may testify — and on that basis a woman may remarry — even if the witnessing occurred by candlelight or by moonlight. The requirement that the witness see the deceased's face does not mean he must have seen it under ideal conditions; limited illumination suffices.

### Bat Kol Testimony

The mishnah adds a remarkable ruling: *umasin al pi bat kol* — a woman may even be remarried based on a *bat kol*. This term, so common in the Bavli in the sense of a heavenly voice, carries a different and more primary meaning here. Rashi explains: *bat kol* means a disembodied voice — a voice whose source is not visible to the hearer. The witness heard someone announce that so-and-so had died, but did not see who was speaking. The *bat kol* is not a supernatural phenomenon; it is simply unattributed testimony heard without visual identification of the speaker. And since the Gemara has already extended acceptance to any category of testimony in this context, the identity of the speaker becomes irrelevant — which is precisely why even a *bat kol* is sufficient.

### Mountain Voice Case

The mishnah records: a person heard from the top of a mountain a voice declaring that so-and-so the son of so-and-so, from such-and-such a place, had died. They went to investigate who had spoken — and found no one. Even so, the wife was permitted to remarry. This case illustrates both the broad acceptance of unattributed testimony and also an important procedural instinct: when the source of testimony is unclear, one makes an effort to investigate further. Here the effort was made and came to nothing, yet that did not undermine the ruling. There was an attempt to verify; the attempt was inconclusive; the *bat kol* remained sufficient.

### Snake Bite Case

A parallel story from the town of Tsalmon: a man declared — *ani Ish Ploni ben Ish Ploni nashchani nachash* — I, so-and-so the son of so-and-so, have been bitten by a poisonous snake, and I am dying. By the time people arrived, they could not identify him — his features had apparently changed due to the venom. Nevertheless, his wife was permitted to remarry. The combination of self-identification and the plausible mechanism of death was deemed sufficient, even without a confirmable corpse. Once again, the effort to gather more information was made; once again it was frustrated; once again the testimony stood.

What emerges across these cases is a pattern: the Gemara expects an attempt to investigate further when the source of testimony is unknown or unclear, but it does not require that the attempt succeed. And the standard for what constitutes testimony acceptable enough to permit remarriage is, by this point, remarkably permissive.

## Beit Shammai/Hillel

### Bat Kol Dispute

Rabba bar Shmuel cites a *braita*: Beit Shammai says a woman may not be remarried on the basis of a *bat kol*; Beit Hillel says she may. The Gemara immediately asks: given that we have a *stam mishnah* — an anonymous mishnah — ruling in accordance with Beit Hillel, why does it matter that the *braita* records Beit Shammai's position? The Gemara answers: to tell us that if one finds another *stam* source appearing to say that a woman may not remarry on the basis of a *bat kol*, one should not conclude that the *halakha* is undecided. Rather, recognize it as the position of Beit Shammai and set it aside accordingly.

### Recording Minority Views

This answer surfaces a well-known question from Masechet Eduyot: why record a minority opinion if we do not rule in accordance with it? Two quite opposite answers are offered there. The first is inclusive: minority opinions are recorded so that they remain available for future use in cases of pressing need — *sha'at hadechak*. The second is exclusionary and is the one the Gemara invokes here: minority opinions are recorded precisely so that if anyone ever advocates for that position, it can be identified and dismissed. Recording Beit Shammai's view on *bat kol* is not an invitation to reconsider it; it is a taxonomic act, filing it away so that it can never be mistaken for mainstream *halakha*. These two rationales are not complementary; they represent genuinely opposite approaches to the status of rejected legal positions within the tradition.

There is an additional irony worth noting. The celebrated dispute between Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai over which school's rulings we follow was itself famously resolved by a *bat kol* that declared the *halakha* to follow Beit Hillel. Here it is Beit Shammai who rejects the evidentiary weight of a *bat kol*, and Beit Hillel who accepts it — which lends a certain poetic consistency to each school's position across these two quite different contexts.

## Witnessing Standard (III)

### Demon/Co-wife Question

The Gemara raises an obvious challenge to the mountain-voice case: perhaps the voice came not from a human being at all, but from a demon. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav responds that the figure appeared to have the *dmus adam* — the form of a human being. The Gemara presses: but demons can appear in human form. What if a shadow was seen? Demons too can cast shadows. What if a shadow of a shadow was observed? Rav Chanina cites Rabbi Yonasan, who reports having been told by a demon directly: demons have shadows, but they do not have shadows of shadows. Seeing a shadow of a shadow is thus the identifying mark of a genuine human presence.

A further concern is raised: perhaps the voice belonged to a *tzarah* — a co-wife — who has a clear motive to lie about her rival's husband's death and is explicitly excluded from this type of testimony. The Gemara invokes a parallel teaching from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: in a time of danger, one may write and deliver a *get* even without knowing the person, relying entirely on their self-identification, because the alternative is that the woman will be left without recourse. Similarly here — *kasha'at sakana damya* — if we cannot determine who spoke, and the only alternative is leaving the woman as an *agunah*, we proceed on the reasonable assumption that the speaker was not one of the five excluded categories.

It is admittedly difficult to know exactly where the threshold of *mesiach lefi tumo* lies — the Gemara elsewhere counted as *mesiach lefi tumo* a person who came specifically intending to inform the wife, without being questioned at all. Yet that same Gemara did not consider it *nitkavein l'haid* — deliberate testimony. The category seems to resist clean definition. What is clear is that the further from a formal testimonial context, the more comfortable the Gemara is in accepting what is said.

## Halacha Development

### Akiva in Nehardea

The Gemara now delivers one of its most historically remarkable passages — a window into how a sweeping *halakhic* innovation actually developed. Rabbi Akiva says: when I went down to Nehardea — the phrase *yaradti* reflecting the geographical and perhaps spiritual descent from Eretz Yisrael to the diaspora — I went to intercalate the year, which itself is striking, since the authority for *ibbur hashanah* ordinarily resided in Eretz Yisrael. There I found a certain Nechemya from Beit Deli, who told me: I have heard that in Eretz Yisrael a woman is not permitted to remarry on the basis of a single witness — *ed echad* — except according to Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava. Only he holds that position; no one else does.

This statement stops the reader cold. Everything the Gemara had seemed to take for granted — that *ed echad* is broadly accepted — turns out to have been the solitary position of one sage. The broad acceptance of testimony that has been the assumed framework throughout this *masechet* was not established practice at all; it was a contested minority view. Nechemya reported this to Rabbi Akiva and confirmed it was accurate. Nechemya then added: I cannot travel to testify myself because the country is *meshubbeshet begaysot* — riddled with military forces — but tell them in my name: *mekublani me-Rabban Gamliel Hazaken* — I have a tradition going back to Rabban Gamliel the Elder — that a woman may be remarried based on a single witness.

### Tel Arza Incident

Rabbi Akiva returned and reported this tradition to Rabban Gamliel the Younger — Rabban Gamliel of his own generation, not the Zaken. Rabban Gamliel rejoiced, saying: we have found a *chaver* — a supporting authority — for Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava. And in the midst of this conversation, Rabban Gamliel recalled that there had been a group of people killed at Tel Arza, and that their widows had not been permitted to remarry because the reports of their deaths rested only on *ed echad*, which had not been established practice. Armed now with the tradition from Rabban Gamliel Hazaken, Rabban Gamliel the Younger acted: he married off the wives of those killed at Tel Arza based on *ed echad*. The *halakha* moved from contested theory to executed practice.

And once it became established through practice — *ve-huchzeku* — the expansion did not stop there.

### Expansion of Testimony

The Gemara reports: once the practice was consolidated, they went further and permitted remarriage based on *ed mipi ed* — hearsay testimony, where the witness did not personally observe the death — and based on the testimony of a slave, a woman, and a female slave. Until this point, slaves had not been mentioned as a distinct category; the Gemara does not assume that because non-Jews are believed, slaves are believed automatically. And the category of hearsay testimony is itself a major *chidush* — one may remarry based on testimony from someone who did not witness the death firsthand but merely heard it reported.

### Tannaim Positions

The final line of the mishnah then reveals that this entire developmental history has a *tannaitic* antecedent. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua — who were Rabbi Akiva's own teachers — held that a woman may not be remarried based on *ed echad*; only two qualified witnesses or the wife's own testimony sufficed. Rabbi Akiva, in his earlier position before the Nehardea episode, agreed to accept *ed echad* but insisted it must be a qualified male witness — not a woman, not a slave, not a relative. The Gemara asks: but does Rabbi Akiva truly hold that a woman's testimony is insufficient? And it cites a *braita* in which Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar reports in Rabbi Akiva's name that a woman is believed to bring her own *get* based on a *kal vachomer*.

### Women's Credibility

The *kal vachomer* runs as follows: the women whom the Sages said are *not* believed to testify that a husband has died — the co-wife, the mother-in-law, and the others in that category — are nonetheless believed to bring a *get* for a woman and attest that it was properly executed, since the physical document supports their word. If even those disqualified women are trusted in that context, how much more so should a woman who *is* believed to testify about death be trusted to bring her own *get*? The implication is that the five excluded women are the exception, and ordinary women are assumed to be reliable. Rabbi Akiva clearly held this.

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Akiva's general principle of women's credibility and his specific ruling against accepting women's testimony in *eidut isha* cases as follows: *lo kashya* — the early position of Rabbi Akiva excluding women was *kodem she-hecheziku* — before the practice was consolidated by the Nehardea report and Rabban Gamliel's action. His later position accepting women was *me-acharei she-hecheziku* — after that consolidation.

The picture that emerges is breathtaking in its historical specificity. At the earliest stratum — the Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai debate — all that had been established was that a *wife* could testify about her own husband's death in a narrow set of circumstances. The acceptance of any other category of witness — *ed echad*, women, non-Jews, slaves, hearsay — was not a natural outgrowth of that ruling but a subsequent and contested development. The last line of the mishnah preserves the early, pre-consolidation positions as a kind of archaeological stratum within the text, a reminder of a moment when the *halakha* could have taken an entirely different path.

## Innkeeper Story

### Levites/Innkeeper

The next and final mishnah of the *masechet* presents a challenge to the pre-consolidation position of Rabbi Akiva. They said to him — to the early Rabbi Akiva who rejected women's testimony in *eidut isha* — there is a story about people called *Bnei Levi* who went to Tzo'a, the city of date palms. One of them fell ill, and they left him at an inn while they continued their journey. On their return, they asked the innkeeper: where is our companion? She said to them: he died and I buried him. On the basis of this testimony, his wife was permitted to remarry. If you can believe the word of this innkeeper, Rabbi Akiva, can you really maintain that women are not believed?

### Akiva's Challenge

Rabbi Akiva's response, in his early position: *le-she-tihiye ke-fundakit ne'emenet* — if women in general were to be like this innkeeper, they would indeed be believed. What distinguished this innkeeper?

### Physical Evidence

The innkeeper did not merely make a verbal report. She produced his effects: his staff, his traveling sack, and the *Sefer Torah* he had carried. *Mesiach lefi tumo* alone was not what made her credible in the early Rabbi Akiva's view — it was the physical evidence she brought forward that corroborated her statement. *Ze maklo ve-ze tarmilo ve-ze kever she-kevartiv bo* — this is his staff, this is his sack, and here is the grave where I buried him.

The Gemara then confirms, via Rav Kahana, that the innkeeper was in fact a non-Jewish woman — *pundakis ovedes kochavim hayasah* — which means the *kal vachomer* being pressed on Rabbi Akiva was even sharper than it had appeared. Not only was she a woman; she was a non-Jew. And she was believed — implicitly establishing that non-Jewish testimony was already a matter of *tannaitic* debate even at this early pre-consolidation stage, woven into the fabric of the dispute before Rabban Gamliel's decisive action.

The Gemara then raises a technical challenge: the innkeeper's testimony was not truly *mesiach lefi tumo*, because she was being directly asked — *aychan chaveirenu* — where is our friend? A direct question would seem to elicit deliberate testimony rather than an incidental remark. The Gemara resolves this: *keivan de-chazatinhu bachya* — because she saw them return and began to cry before they questioned her, the emotional response came first, naturally, and only then did the questioning follow. The crying established the spontaneous, unguarded quality of the disclosure — and then the verbal statement, though prompted, rode on that foundation of *mesiach lefi tumo*. The operative principle seems to be that testimony ceases to be *mesiach lefi tumo* when the speaker is being formally drilled for information in a context that resembles examination — but a natural emotional response, even if followed by direct questioning, preserves the character of an incidental statement.

## Cross-Exam Cases

### Spontaneous ID

The Gemara presents a case in which a man came to testify about a woman's status before Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Tarfon asked him: how do you know this testimony? The witness explained: I was traveling with this man when a military troop — *gayas* — pursued us. My companion grabbed the branch of an olive tree, tore it off, and used it to beat the pursuers back. Impressed, I called out: *Aryeh, yishar kochacha!* — Lion, may your strength be straight! He turned to me and said: how did you know my name was Aryeh? That is indeed what they call me in my city — Yochanan ben Yehonasan, Aryeh, from Kfar Shichayah. The name had come out spontaneously — it was a metaphor the witness offered, and it turned out to be the man's actual nickname. Some time later the man fell ill and died. Rabbi Tarfon accepted this testimony and permitted the wife to remarry.

This case presents a striking feature: the identifying information emerged through coincidence, through the witness's use of a metaphorical epithet that happened to match the subject's actual name. The detail is concrete, the self-identification was unrehearsed, and the testimony was sufficiently specific.

### Rigorous Questioning

A second *braita* tells what appears to be the same story with a crucial difference. In this version, after the witness delivered his account — this time involving a fig tree rather than an olive tree — Rabban Gamliel, not Rabbi Tarfon, put the witness through a rigorous consistency check. He said to him: wait — didn't you tell me his name was arranged differently? Was it not Yochanan ben Yehonasan from Kfar Shichayah, Aryeh — in a different order than you just gave? The witness pushed back: no, I said it exactly as I said it now. Rabban Gamliel pressed him two and three times — *dikdek oto b'shtayim u-beshalosh pe'amim* — trying to find any inconsistency, any shift in how the details were presented. The witness remained consistent throughout, and only then was the wife permitted to remarry.

This is a very different posture toward testimony. Rather than accepting what the witness said at face value, Rabban Gamliel was actively trying to induce an inconsistency — the kind of technique one might recognize from interrogations designed to detect unreliable accounts by repeatedly asking the subject to repeat their story and looking for variation. Had the witness altered even an apparently insignificant detail, one senses the testimony would have been rejected. The fact that he did not, and was then believed, shows that the rigor was real.

### Drisha/Chakirah

The two versions of the story — one before Rabbi Tarfon accepting the testimony without such review, the other before Rabban Gamliel with elaborate consistency-checking — prompt the Gemara to ask: did Rabbi Tarfon really not require *drisha vechakirah*? And a *braita* is cited: the debate is explicitly a dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Akiva says *ein bodkin edei nashim b'drisha vechakirah* — witnesses in *eidut isha* cases are not subjected to *drisha vechakirah*. Rabbi Tarfon says they are. Some versions of the text read the lenient position as anonymous and Rabbi Tarfon as the stricter one; the implication is similar.

### Monetary vs. Capital

The underlying dispute is traced to a principle stated by Rabbi Chanina: biblically, both monetary cases and capital cases require *drisha vechakirah* — the formal inquiry into time and place that is the classic tool of witness examination — as derived from the verse *mishpat echad yihye lachem*, one law for all proceedings. The Sages relaxed this requirement for monetary cases to avoid *neilas delet bifnei lovin* — locking the door before borrowers, meaning that if creditors had to subject their claims to full *drisha vechakirah* before recovering debts, lenders would simply stop lending.

The question then is how to categorize *eidut isha*. One view holds that since collecting the *ketubah* is involved, the case resembles a monetary case and *drisha vechakirah* is not required. The other view holds that since we are binding or releasing a woman from *isur eshet ish* — a prohibition of the severity of capital law — it resembles *dinei nefashot* and *drisha vechakirah* is required.

### Takkanat Agunah

The Gemara does not leave the tension unresolved. Tosafot, citing *Chad Mi-Kamai*, states explicitly: since we have already extended *eidut isha* to include testimony from men, women, slaves, non-Jews, children, and even *ed mipi ed*, and we have done all of this *mishum takanat agunah* — on account of the enactment to free chained wives — we have so fundamentally reshaped the evidentiary framework here that it cannot be made to conform to the rules of either *dinei mamonot* or *dinei nefashot*. The Rambam rules accordingly, as does Rabbeinu Tam in Tosafot Yeshanim: *drisha vechakirah* is not required. The framing of the case as *dinei mamonot* by virtue of the *ketubah* is thus best understood not as a genuine classification but as a legal hook — a way of reaching the conclusion the *halakha* demands once the *takkanat agunah* lens is applied. The entire evidentiary edifice for *eidut isha* is its own category, shaped not by the standard rules of testimony but by the overriding imperative of preventing women from being trapped as *agunot* for life.

## Conclusion/Aggada

### Scholars & Peace

The Gemara closes with the statement of Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *talmidei chachamim marbim shalom ba'olam* — Torah scholars increase peace in the world — as it is written, *ve-chol banayich limudei Hashem ve-rav shalom banayich* — and all your children will be students of God, and great will be the peace of your children. The choice to end Masechet Yevamot with this *aggadic* statement is not incidental. The final daf has shown, in remarkable historical detail, how the Sages expanded and reshaped the *halakha* of *eidut isha* — from narrow spousal testimony to the acceptance of any witness, in any form, under any ordinary circumstances — in order to free women from the trap of *iggun*. The peace the Sages increased was the peace of women whose lives were otherwise suspended indefinitely.

### Maharsha Exegesis

The Maharsha, commenting on this closing *aggadata*, addresses the obvious tension directly. One might object that the entire final section of Yevamot — indeed, the final several chapters — appears to be a systematic setting aside of Torah evidentiary requirements: the rules of *shnei eidim*, the rules of *eidut*, the prohibition of *eshet ish*. Is this not *akiras davar min ha-Torah* — a uprooting of biblical law?

The Maharsha answers: *ain zeh akirah ela mi-midat shalom* — this is not an uprooting but an expression of the quality of peace. The Torah itself declares *deracheha darchei no'am ve-chol nesivoteha shalom* — its ways are ways of pleasantness and all its paths are peace. A woman left permanently *me'ugenet* — chained — is incompatible with the Torah's own stated character. The Sages did not override the Torah; they read it through the lens the Torah itself provided. And the acceptance of *ed echad*, of women, of non-Jews, of *ed mipi ed*, of slaves — all of it flows from that same lens. *Mishum takanat agunah* is not a departure from Torah; it is Torah's way of peace made concrete in *halakha*.

The note that *banayich* may also be read as an acronym is left, appropriately, as the final word of the masechet's discourse — *hadran alach Ha'Ishah Basra ve-salikah lah Masechet Yevamot*.
