# Bekhorot 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & 3 Bechorot

### Kodashim Context

We begin today a new *masechta*: *Masechet Bekhorot*. With this tractate, we move into a portion of *Kodashim* that is almost never studied in *yeshivot* or anywhere else — a genuinely rare corner of the Talmud. The territory is largely uncharted in the traditional *beit midrash* curriculum, which itself accounts for much of its character.

### Rishonim Sources

Part of what makes *Bekhorot* unusual is the near-total absence of *Rishonim*. Beyond Rashi and Tosafot, there is almost no classical commentary to guide the learner. One might even say we should be grateful for Tosafot — without it, the *daf* would feel considerably more exposed. The exception to this general neglect has historically been the *yeshivot* of Brisk, which were drawn precisely to *masechtas* like this one: virgin territory, fewer settled conclusions, more room to develop original analysis.

### Masechet Size

Despite its rarity in study, *Bekhorot* is not a small *masechta*. It spans 60 *dappim* — a substantial body. By way of comparison, *Masechet Niddah*, which we will reach in roughly six months, is only 70 *dappim*. So *Bekhorot* is not marginal in size, even if it is marginal in practice.

### 3 Types Overview

The *masechta* covers three categories of *bechorot* as treated by the Torah. The first is the firstborn of a donkey — the *peter chamor* — which one redeems with a sheep. The Torah states *ve'im lo tifdeh va'arafto*: if you do not redeem it, you break its neck. Notably, the sheep given for the redemption has no *kedusha* of its own; it simply belongs to the *kohen*. The donkey itself, once its neck is broken, is *assur b'hana'ah* — forbidden in benefit. Before the neck-breaking, its status is itself subject to debate.

The second category is the firstborn of a domestic animal — *bechor behema* — which is given to a *kohen* as a *korban*. If it has a *mum*, a disqualifying blemish, it is still given to a *kohen* but does not attain the status of a *korban*.

The third is *bechor adam*, the firstborn human male, whose redemption — *pidyon bechor* — consists of five *sela'im* given to a *kohen*.

The *masechta* also touches on aspects of *bechor* beyond *kedusha* and *pidyon*, including the laws of *bechor le-nachala*, the firstborn's double inheritance portion. These are not merely theoretical matters. And *bechor behema*, while in principle relevant, will turn out to have practical escape routes — as we will see from the role of *matnot kehuna* — that tend to make it easier to avoid in practice.

### Peter Chamor Start

Strikingly, the *masechta* opens not with the most central of the three *bechorot* but with perhaps the least central: *peter chamor*, the firstborn donkey. This is not entirely unprecedented — *Masechet Yevamot*, for instance, opens not with the laws of *yibum* itself but with the scenario of co-wives. Still, the choice calls for explanation. The *Gemara* will provide one in approximately ten *dappim* from now: since *peter chamor* is a relatively contained topic, the *masechta* addresses it first in order to clear it away and then devote its full attention to the primary subject, *bechor behema*.

## Mishna: Ownership

### Buy Fetus Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַלּוֹקֵחַ עוּבַּר חֲמוֹרוֹ שֶׁל נָכְרִי, וְהַמּוֹכֵר לוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי, הַמִּשְׁתַּתֵּף לוֹ, וְהַמְקַבֵּל הֵימֶנּוּ, וְהַנּוֹתֵן לוֹ בְּקַבָּלָה — פָּטוּר מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״, אֲבָל לֹא בַּאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who purchases the fetus of a donkey that belongs to a gentile, and one who sells** the fetus of his donkey **to** a gentile **although he is not permitted** to sell a large animal to a gentile, and **one who enters into a partnership with** a gentile in ownership of a donkey or its fetus, **and one who receives** a donkey **from** a gentile in order to care for it in exchange for partnership in its offspring, **and one who gives** his donkey **to** a gentile **in receivership,** in all of these cases the donkeys are **exempt from the** obligations of **firstborn status,** i.e., they do not have firstborn status and are not redeemed, **as it is stated:** "I sanctified to Me all the firstborn **in Israel,** both man and animal" (Numbers 3:13), indicating that the mitzva is incumbent upon the Jewish people, **but not upon others.** If the firstborn belongs even partially to a gentile, it does not have firstborn status.

The *mishna* opens: *ha-loke'ach ubar chamoro shel oved kochavim* — if one purchases the fetus of a donkey belonging to a non-Jew. Before we even begin analyzing the case, it is worth noting the assumption embedded in this opening: we have already established, through the laws of *matnot kehuna*, that part-ownership by a non-Jew creates an exemption from certain obligations. That principle is operative here and throughout the *masechta*. Indeed, the practical mechanism by which people avoid the obligation of *bechor behema* in general is precisely this — selling a portion of the mother to a non-Jew, such that every subsequent offspring is partially owned by the non-Jew and therefore exempt.

In our opening case, the Jew purchases the fetus while the non-Jew retains ownership of the mother donkey.

### Exemption Principle

The governing principle of the *mishna* is straightforward: as long as the non-Jew owns any portion — whether of the mother or the fetus — that is sufficient to exempt the Jew from the obligation of *peter chamor*. This applies regardless of where in the ownership structure the non-Jewish stake exists.

### Sell Fetus Case

The *mishna* then considers the reverse: *ha-mocher lo* — the Jew sells the fetus to the non-Jew while retaining ownership of the mother. Even here the exemption applies. But the *mishna* immediately adds a qualification: *af al pi she-eino rashai* — even though one is not permitted to do this.

### Az Prohibition

The reason one is not permitted to sell the fetus raises a subject that will dominate the entire *daf*: the prohibition derived from *Masechet Avodah Zarah* against selling *behema gassa* — large working animals — to a non-Jew. One may not sell a cow, and arguably not a horse either, to a non-Jew. The concern, on its face, is that the animal will be worked on *Shabbat*. But this requires unpacking: why should the Jew care once the animal has been sold and belongs entirely to the non-Jew?

The concern, as understood by *Chazal*, is more nuanced. In one formulation, the worry is that there may be a transitional period — a trial or demonstration — during which the animal is not yet formally the non-Jew's property but is already in his possession, and during that period the Jew's animal may be used for *melacha* on *Shabbat*. There are in fact debates in *Masechet Avodah Zarah* about the true basis of this prohibition, and the *Gemara* there works through multiple possible explanations. The general accepted position, though, is that one does not sell *behema gassa* to a non-Jew, and this prohibition, the *mishna* notes, applies even to the fetus — though the *Gemara* will examine the fetus case carefully.

The critical point for our *mishna* is that despite the prohibition — despite the fact that one should not have sold the fetus — the legal consequence in terms of *bechor* law still follows: the Jew is now exempt from *peter chamor*, because the non-Jew owns the fetus.

### Partnership/Consignment

The *mishna* continues with additional cases. *Ha-mishtatef lo* — if one forms a partnership with the non-Jew from the outset, jointly acquiring a donkey, so that the non-Jew is a part-owner. The exemption applies.

*Ve-ha-mikabel mimenu ve-ha-noten lo be-kabala*: here the term *mikabel* carries a technical meaning distinct from simply "receiving." It derives from *kablan* — contractor — and denotes a consignment or contracting arrangement. In one version of the case, the non-Jew gives his donkey to the Jew to tend and raise; the Jew's fee is a share of the offspring. Since the donkey remains the non-Jew's, the Jew is obviously exempt. In the reverse case — *ha-noten lo be-kabala* — the Jew owns the donkey but gives it to the non-Jew to raise, with the non-Jew receiving half of the offspring as his fee. Since the non-Jew thereby holds a half-ownership interest in every fetus as it is born, the Jew is exempt even in this case, despite the fact that it is the Jew's own animal.

### Exemption Source

The scriptural basis for the entire principle is cited: *hikdashti li kol bechor be-Yisrael* — "I have sanctified to Me every firstborn among Israel" — which *Chazal* read as *aval lo ba-acherim*: not among others, not when a non-Jew holds any share of ownership in the animal.

## Multiple Cases Need

### Redundancy Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כׇּל הָנֵי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **all these** examples in the mishna to demonstrate the principle that a firstborn donkey must belong exclusively to a Jew for the obligations of firstborn status to apply?

The *Gemara* opens with a direct challenge to the *mishna*'s structure: *kol hanei lama li?* — why are all these cases necessary? If the principle is simply that non-Jewish part-ownership exempts, why enumerate buying, selling, partnership, and both forms of *kabala*? Surely one case would establish the rule.

### All Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי תְּנָא לוֹקֵחַ — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ לִקְדוּשָּׁה, אֲבָל מוֹכֵר דְּקָא מַפְקַע לַהּ מִקְּדוּשָּׁה — אֵימָא לִיקְנְסֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: All of these examples **are necessary. As, had** the *tanna* **taught** that a donkey is exempt from the obligations of firstborn status only in the case where a Jew **purchases** a fetus from a gentile, **I would say** that this is **because** the Jew **brings it to** a state of **sanctity** in that it will not be worked on Shabbat. **But** in the case where a Jew **sells** the fetus of his donkey to a gentile, **where he abrogates** its state of **sanctity,** I would **say** that the Sages **should penalize him [*likneseih*]** for his actions by rendering the fetus subject to the obligations of firstborn status. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that it is exempt from these obligations.

The *Gemara* answers: *tzrichi* — all the cases are necessary. Each one addresses a distinct concern that the others do not resolve.

### Kedusha Concepts

The first distinction drawn is between the two ownership cases in the *mishna*: buying the fetus and selling the fetus. Had the *mishna* taught only the case of buying the fetus — where the Jew acquires the fetus while the non-Jew retains the mother — one might have thought the exemption is granted because the Jew is, in a sense, *bringing the fetus into sanctity*: *mishum de-kamaytei lah li-kedusha*, since the Jew is acting in a way that moves the animal toward the realm of Jewish obligation and holiness. In such a case, one might reason, we can be lenient.

But in the reverse case — *aval mocher*, when the Jew sells the fetus — *de-kamapkei lah mi-kedusha*, the Jew is removing the fetus from the domain of sanctity. One might then have said *eima liknesei* — perhaps we should impose a rabbinic fine and deny the exemption as a penalty for this improper act. The *mishna* therefore teaches that even in the selling case, no fine is imposed and the exemption stands.

It is worth noting that the *mishna* itself already signals this concern through the phrase *af al pi she-eino rashai* — even though you were not permitted — addressing the sale to a non-Jew in violation of the prohibition against selling *behema gassa*. The point is that even wrongdoing does not cost the Jew the exemption.

### Rashi View

Rashi explains the language of "bringing into *kedusha*" and "removing from *kedusha*" with reference to *Shabbat*: an animal under Jewish ownership has the opportunity to rest on *Shabbat*, which is itself a dimension of *kedusha*. When one sells the fetus to a non-Jew, the animal loses that opportunity. That is what Rashi means by removing from *kedusha*.

### No Knas Conclusion

The lesson — *ka-mashma lan* — is that despite the impropriety of the sale, no rabbinic *knas* is imposed. The exemption from *peter chamor* stands.

### Tosafot View

Tosafot offers a more contextually natural reading: the *kedusha* in question is *kedushat bechor* itself — the sanctity inherent in firstborn status. Under this reading, buying the fetus brings it into the orbit of potential *kedusha*, while selling it removes it. Tosafot acknowledges the difficulty, however: if the fetus is exempt because the non-Jew owns the mother, in what sense does buying the fetus create *kedusha*? Tosafot resolves this by noting that if the fetus turns out to be female, its future offspring would be subject to *bechor* obligations — so there is some residual proximity to *kedushat bechor*. There is nonetheless a pointed question about whether the term *kedushat bechor* is even appropriate in the context of *peter chamor*, where the redeemed sheep has no *kedusha*, the *kohen* simply owns it, and the *chamor* itself has no sacrificial dimension. This may be precisely why Rashi avoided Tosafot's reading, even though on the surface it seems more contextually fitting.

## Rabbi Yehuda View

### Partnership Case Need

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמִּשְׁתַּתֵּף לוֹ, לְמָה לִי? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: שׁוּתָּפוּת גּוֹיִ חַיֶּיבֶת בִּבְכוֹרָה, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דִּפְטוּרָה מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And why do I** need the mishna to state the case of **one who enters into a partnership with** a gentile? The case is necessary **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, who says:** An animal owned in **partnership** with **a gentile is obligated,** i.e., subject to accounting its offspring **a firstborn.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us that** it **is exempt from** its offspring being counted **a firstborn.**

The *Gemara* continues: *ve-ha-mishtatef lo lama li?* — why was the partnership case necessary? If we've already established that partial ownership in either the mother or the fetus creates an exemption, why add the case of joint ownership?

### R.Y. Exclusion

The answer: *le-apukei mi-Rabbi Yehuda* — to exclude the position of Rabbi Yehuda.

### R.Y. Partnership

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *shutafus oved kochavim chayevet bi-vchora* — a partnership with a non-Jew is still subject to the *bechora* obligation. In other words, Rabbi Yehuda takes the position that even where the non-Jew is a part-owner, the Jewish co-owner remains obligated for his share.

### Matnot Contrast

This stands in striking contrast to the other extreme we encountered earlier in the context of *matnot kehuna*, where one opinion held that even Jewish partnership exempts — that even a Jewish co-owner need not give *matnot kehuna*. Here, Rabbi Yehuda holds the opposite: even non-Jewish co-ownership does not exempt. The *mishna* therefore needs to include the partnership case explicitly, to make clear that we do not rule like Rabbi Yehuda — that *shutafut* with a non-Jew does exempt.

The logic of the first two cases — where the non-Jew owns the entire fetus or the entire mother — would be acceptable even to Rabbi Yehuda, since the Jewish party holds nothing of the relevant ownership. But in the partnership case, where both parties are co-owners in both the mother and the offspring, Rabbi Yehuda would say the Jewish partner is obligated for his half. The *mishna* comes to teach that the *halacha* does not follow that position.

## Maris Ayin Issue

### Consignment Opacity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְקַבֵּל, לְמָה לִי? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי וְהַנּוֹתֵן לוֹ בְּקַבָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And why do I** need the mishna to state that the fetus does not have firstborn status in the case of **one who receives** a donkey from a gentile in exchange for partnership in its offspring? This *halakha* is already included in the previous one. The Gemara answers: It is **because** the *tanna* **wants to teach** in the parallel case of: **And one who gives** his donkey **to** a gentile **in receivership,** that its offspring does not have firstborn status.

The *Gemara* then asks: *ve-ha-mikabel lama li?* — why was it necessary to state the *kabala* case, where the non-Jew gives his donkey to the Jew to raise in exchange for a share of the offspring? Surely, if the non-Jew still owns the mother animal, the exemption is obvious.

The answer: the *mishna* needed this case not for its own sake but in order to introduce the far less obvious reverse case — *ve-ha-noten lo be-kabala* — where the Jew owns the mother but gives it to the non-Jew to raise, the non-Jew receiving half the offspring as his fee.

### Consequentialist View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַנּוֹתֵן לוֹ בְּקַבָּלָה, לְמָה לִי? אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְעִיקַּר בְּהֵמָה דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא, לִיקְנְסֵיהּ, דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בִּבְהֵמָה אַחֲרִיתִי, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And why do I** need the case of **one who gives** his donkey **to** a gentile **in receivership** to be stated? It **was necessary,** because it might **enter your mind to say** that **since the primary animal belongs to the Jew,** the Sages **should penalize him** by treating the offspring as a firstborn and requiring it to be redeemed, **lest** this case **be confused with another** case of an **animal** that the Jew puts in the care of a gentile where the gentile does not own rights to the offspring, and the offspring is counted a firstborn. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that he is not penalized.

*Salka daitach amina*: one might have thought to impose a rabbinic sanction in this case. Why? Because in the *noten* case, the Jew owns the mother, and the Jew is seen taking some of the offspring. The non-Jew also takes some, but *ho'il ve-ikar ha-behema de-Yisrael hi* — since the fundamental animal belongs to the Jew, a bystander observing the arrangement might reasonably conclude that the Jew is the sole owner of both the mother and the offspring. If the Jew is not giving *peter chamor*, this would appear to onlookers as a violation — as if a Jew who fully owns the animal is simply ignoring his obligation.

### Gezeira Rejected

One might therefore have thought that the Rabbis would impose a rabbinic *gezeira* — a decree compelling the Jew to treat the firstborn as if he were obligated, so that observers are not misled. The *mishna* teaches that no such *knas* is applied. The Jew is technically exempt and remains so.

### Confusion Risk

The precise nature of the concern is worth examining. The Jew owns the mother and receives half the kids. Observers see him taking offspring home and may not notice that the non-Jew also receives a share. Or they may notice that the non-Jew receives some offspring but assume he is merely a hired laborer being paid a daily wage, not an actual half-owner. In either case, the overall impression is that this Jew is the sole owner and is disregarding the *mitzvah* of *peter chamor*. One might further imagine the concern that observers could mistake the arrangement as one where the firstborn specifically goes to the Jew — precisely the case that would trigger the obligation — while the subsequent offspring go to the non-Jew as payment.

Despite all of this, *ka-mashma lan*: the Rabbis do not impose any rabbinic *gezeira* or *knas*. Since the Jew is technically exempt, he remains exempt.

## AZ Transition

### AZ Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר בַּשְּׁבוּרָה, בֶּן בְּתִירָא מַתִּיר בַּסּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a discussion where the mishna is used as a proof: **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Avoda Zara* 14b) with regard to the prohibition against selling large livestock to a gentile due to concern that it will be worked on Shabbat: **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the sale **of a damaged** animal **permitted** because it is incapable of performing labor, **and ben Beteira deems** the sale **of a horse** for riding **permitted,** because riding a horse on Shabbat is not prohibited by Torah law.

Having established the framework of the *mishna*, the *Gemara* now pivots — and this pivot occupies essentially the rest of the *daf* — to the side point embedded in the *mishna*'s phrase *af al pi she-eino rashai*: the prohibition against selling animals to a non-Jew. The *Gemara* invokes the relevant *mishna* in *Masechet Avodah Zarah*: *tnan hosam* — it was taught there.

### R.Y. Broken Leg

In that *mishna*, Rabbi Yehuda *matir bi-shvura* — Rabbi Yehuda permits selling a broken-legged animal to a non-Jew, because the animal cannot perform *melacha*. If the entire basis of the prohibition is concern that the animal might be used for labor on *Shabbat*, then an animal that is physically incapable of labor presents no such concern.

### Ben B. Horse

*Ben Beteira matir be-sus* — Ben Beteira permits selling a horse to a non-Jew outright, even without any physical defect. His reasoning rests on the principle of *chai noseh et atzmo* — a living being bears its own weight. The primary use of a horse is carrying a rider, and since the rider is a living being who partially supports his own weight, riding is not classified as *melacha* at the biblical level. Therefore, the concern that a horse will be used for *melacha* on *Shabbat* in a biblically significant sense does not apply, and Ben Beteira holds that the decree against selling animals to non-Jews does not extend to horses.

The broader question of whether rabbinic restrictions on *melacha* also apply to the prohibition against letting one's animals work, and whether one would then be creating a *gezeira* atop a *gezeira* atop a *gezeira*, is a question that touches on deep issues in *Masechet Avodah Zarah*. What matters for present purposes is that Ben Beteira is carving out a narrow exception based on the nature of the animal's use.

### Fetus Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: עוּבָּר מָה לִי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה? טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הָתָם דְּשָׁרֵי, מִשּׁוּם דִּשְׁבוּרָה, עוּבָּר נָמֵי שָׁבוּר הוּא. אוֹ דִלְמָא: שְׁבוּרָה לָאו הַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ, אֲבָל עוּבָּר, כֵּיוָן דְּהַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ, לָאו שָׁבוּר הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **a fetus, what** would **Rabbi Yehuda say to me** about selling it to a gentile? Is the **reason that Rabbi Yehuda deems** selling the animal **permitted there because** it is **damaged** and it cannot work, **and a fetus is also damaged** in the sense that it cannot work? **Or, perhaps** it is permitted to sell **a damaged** animal because **that is not its natural** state; it is defective and is therefore not included in the prohibition against selling large livestock. **But** with regard to **a fetus, since that is its natural** state, and it will become capable of working after growing up, perhaps it **is not** considered **damaged,** as it is not defective.

*Iba'aya lehu* — the *Gemara* raises a question: what would Rabbi Yehuda say about selling a fetus? An animal in the womb clearly cannot perform *melacha* at present. Does Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning from the broken-leg case apply here as well?

### Reason vs. Unusual

The question is framed in two directions. *Ta'ama de-Rabbi Yehuda hosam de-shari mishum de-shvura* — is the reason Rabbi Yehuda permits the broken-leg case simply because the animal cannot work? If so, *ubar nami shavur hu* — a fetus also cannot work, and Rabbi Yehuda should permit it equally.

Or perhaps: *shvura lav hainu orchai* — a broken leg is not a normal state of affairs for an animal. It is an unusual and visually unmistakable condition. *Aval ubar, keivan de-hainu orchai, lav shavur hu* — but a fetus, since being in the womb is the normal developmental state of a living creature, is not analogous to something broken. Because it is *orchai* — normal — it does not stand out the way a broken leg does.

This distinction carries halakhic weight in two ways. First, a fetus, unlike a broken-legged animal, will eventually be born, grow, and be capable of performing *melacha* on *Shabbat*. The broken-leg exception holds only because the animal is presumed incapable of healing; a fetus presents no such permanence. Second, there is the question of *lo plug* — the rabbinic principle of not making fine distinctions. Rabbi Yehuda is willing to create an exception for the broken-leg case precisely because it is so patently obvious that the animal cannot work. A fetus, being a normal condition, does not present that same visual clarity, and therefore even Rabbi Yehuda might agree that *lo plug* applies — that we do not carve out exceptions for normal developmental states where the distinction would be unclear to an observer.

## Mishna Evidence

### Proof Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וְהַמּוֹכֵר לוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי. וְלָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from the mishna: **And one who sells** the fetus of his donkey **to** a gentile, his donkey is exempt from the obligations of firstborn status, **although** the owner **is not permitted** to do so. **And Rabbi Yehuda does not disagree** and claim that he may sell it. Apparently, Rabbi Yehuda agrees that it is prohibited to sell the fetus of one's animal to a gentile.

*Ta shema* — the *Gemara* attempts to resolve the question about Rabbi Yehuda's position on the fetus by invoking our *mishna* itself as a source of evidence.

### Fetus Prohibition

The *mishna* states: *ha-mocher lo af al pi she-eino rashai* — if one sells the fetus to a non-Jew, even though one is not permitted to do so. The *mishna* thus establishes that selling the fetus is prohibited. The *Gemara* suggests that since Rabbi Yehuda does not object to this ruling in the *mishna*, he must implicitly agree that selling a fetus is forbidden.

### Silence Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, הַמִּשְׁתַּתֵּף לוֹ, וְהַמְקַבֵּל מִמֶּנּוּ, וְהַנּוֹתֵן לוֹ בְּקַבָּלָה, דְּלָא קָתָנֵי — הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא פְּלִיג?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the resolution: **And according to your reasoning,** concerning the other cases in the mishna, namely, **one who enters into a partnership with** a gentile, **and one who receives** a donkey **from** a gentile in exchange for partnership in its offspring, **and one who gives** his donkey **to** a gentile **in receivership,** with regard to **which** the mishna **does not teach** that Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, **so too** does this indicate **that he does not disagree** with the ruling in the mishna in these cases? That is impossible, as Rabbi Yehuda holds that an animal subject to a partnership between a Jew and gentile is subject to accounting its offspring a firstborn, as is evident from a *baraita* that will soon be cited.

*Ve-lo palig Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda is not recorded as dissenting from this clause of the *mishna*. Can we infer from his silence that he agrees?

### No Inference Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא — פְּלִיג, וְלָא קָתָנֵי. הָכָא נָמֵי — פְּלִיג, וְלָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** clearly Rabbi Yehuda **does disagree, but** the mishna **does not teach** his opinion. **Here too,** with regard to selling the fetus, **he disagrees, but** the mishna **does not teach** his opinion.

The *Gemara* rejects this inference decisively. *U-letamecha* — by your logic, consider: the *mishna* also does not record Rabbi Yehuda as objecting to the cases of *mishtatef* and *kabala* — partnership and consignment. Yet we know from elsewhere that Rabbi Yehuda holds *shutafus oved kochavim chayevet bi-vchora* — a position that directly contradicts the *mishna*'s ruling in the partnership case. Clearly, Rabbi Yehuda disputes part of this *mishna* without the *mishna* recording his objection there.

The lesson: *ela palig ve-lo katani* — he disagrees, but it is simply not stated in this *mishna*. A *stam mishna* introducing a topic does not necessarily catalogue every dissenting opinion. One cannot draw conclusions from silence. The same may be true of the fetus prohibition — Rabbi Yehuda may disagree but the *mishna* does not record it. The evidential attempt fails.

## R.Y. Obligations

### Receive Consignment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, הַמְקַבֵּל בְּהֵמָה מִן הַגּוֹי וְיָלְדָה — מַעֲלִין אוֹתוֹ בְּשׇׁוְויוֹ, וְנוֹתֵן חֲצִי דָּמָיו לַכֹּהֵן. וְהַנּוֹתֵן לוֹ בְּקַבָּלָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי — קוֹנְסִים אוֹתוֹ עַד עֲשָׂרָה בְּדָמָיו, וְנוֹתֵן כׇּל דָּמָיו לַכֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that in the case of **one who receives an animal from a gentile** in order to care for it and receives some of the offspring in exchange for his work, **and it gave birth** to a firstborn, **they assess its value, and** the Jew **gives half of its value to the priest** to redeem his portion, which is sanctified by the firstborn status. **And** in the case of **one who gives** the gentile an animal **in receivership even though he is not permitted** to do so, the Sages **penalize him** by requiring that he purchase the gentile's portion of the animal for **up to ten** times **its value, and he gives all of its value to the priest.**

The *Gemara* proceeds to cite a *braita* that lays out Rabbi Yehuda's affirmative positions on these cases. *Rabbi Yehuda omer: ha-mikabel behema mi-yad oved kochavim* — if one takes an animal from a non-Jew under a *kabala* arrangement, such that the Jew tends the animal and receives half the offspring, and the animal gives birth to a firstborn, *ma'alin oto be-shavin* — one assesses the animal's value, *ve-noten chatzi damav la-kohen* — and gives half of that value to the *kohen*.

### Half Value Obligation

This ruling is easier to understand in the context of a *chamor*: the obligation of *peter chamor* is discharged through a *se'eh* of redemption money — or a sheep — given to the *kohen*, without any *kedusha* attaching to the redemption. The Jew who is a half-owner in the offspring is obligated for his half, and the way he discharges that obligation is by giving the *kohen* half the assessed value of the animal. The case of *bechor behema* is more complex: a *bechor behema* is supposed to be brought as a *korban*, and it is difficult to conceptualize half an animal as a *korban* when the other half is owned by a non-Jew. Rashi understands we are dealing with small animals, and the solution is to give the *kohen* the monetary value of the half-share rather than the animal itself. Tosafot prefers to read the entire discussion as pertaining to the *chamor*.

It is worth pausing on a point raised in the course of the discussion: here, in the *kabala* case, the non-Jew owns the entire mother. The Jew owns only a half-share in the offspring — and specifically in the firstborn offspring. Yet Rabbi Yehuda holds that the Jew is still obligated. This is a striking implication of his position: ownership in the *ubar* alone — not in the mother — is sufficient to generate a *bechora* obligation.

### Owner Gives Case

The *braita* then presents the reverse: *ve-ha-noten lo be-kabala* — the Jew owns the mother and gives it to the non-Jew to raise, with the non-Jew receiving half the offspring as his fee. *Af al pi she-eino rashai* — even though one is not permitted to do this.

### Redemption Fine

*Konsin oto ad asara bi-damav* — the Rabbinical court imposes a fine: the Jew is compelled to buy back his share from the non-Jew, at up to ten times the value if necessary. The *Gemara* will later examine whether "ten times" is meant literally or hyperbolically. The severity of the fine reflects how strongly the Rabbis disapprove of this transaction.

### Full Value Penalty

Beyond buying back the share, the Jew is further required: *ve-noten kol damav la-kohen* — to give the full value of the firstborn to the *kohen*. Not half — the full value. This is a penalty imposed because the Jew acted improperly by creating this arrangement.

## Braita Clarification

### Violation Identification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו אַעוּבָּר? לָא, אַבְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** referring **to the fetus** when the *baraita* states that he is not permitted to sell it? The Gemara responds: **No,** it is referring **to the animal** itself that he may not sell to the gentile, and that is why he is penalized.

The *Gemara* now subjects this *braita* to careful analysis. *Mai lav* — is it not the case that when the *braita* says the Jew violated by doing something he was *not rashai* to do, the violation refers to selling or conveying a share in the *ubar* — the fetus — to the non-Jew? If so, this would be direct evidence that Rabbi Yehuda holds selling the fetus is a violation of the prohibition against selling animals to non-Jews.

### Mother Violation

The *Gemara* rejects this: *lo, a-behema* — no, the real violation the *braita* is referring to is selling a portion of the mother animal itself. In the *kabala* arrangement as constructed, the Jew may have done more than simply arrange for the non-Jew to receive offspring — he may have effectively conveyed a share in the mother.

### Grammar Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״דָּמָיו״ קָתָנֵי! תְּנִי ״דָּמֶיהָ״. וְהָא ״נוֹתֵן כׇּל דָּמָיו לַכֹּהֵן״ קָתָנֵי, וְאִי בְּהֵמָה — כֹּהֵן מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ בְּהֵמָה מְעַבַּרְתָּא לְפַטּוֹמַהּ, דְּמִיגּוֹ דְּקָנְסִינַן לֵיהּ אַבְּהֵמָה, קָנְסִינַן לֵיהּ אַעוּבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** the *baraita* **teach:** The Sages penalize him up to ten times **its value [*damav*],** in masculine form, indicating that it is referring to the fetus and not the mother? The Gemara responds: **Teach** the following wording in the *baraita*: The Sages penalize him up to ten times **its value [*dameha*],** in feminine form. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't it teach: He gives all of its value to the priest? And if** it is referring to **the animal** and not the firstborn, **what is the relevance of** the priest? The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the Jew **gave** the gentile **a pregnant animal to fatten it** in order to divide the profits as well as the offspring. **Since we penalize him for** selling **the animal** in a prohibited manner, **we penalize him with regard to the fetus** as well.

The *Gemara* challenges this reading on grammatical grounds: the *braita* uses the masculine form *damav* — "its value" — which would naturally refer to the *ubar* (fetus, a masculine noun), not to *behema* (animal, feminine). The *Gemara* responds: read *dameha* — with a feminine suffix — to refer to the *behema*. This is one of the moments in the *Gemara* where knowing grammar matters — and equally where the *Gemara* simply overrides the grammatical form by emending the *girsa*.

The *Gemara* then notes a further problem: the *braita* says *ve-noten kol damav la-kohen* — he gives all its value to the *kohen*. "All its value" in context plainly refers to the value of the *bechor*, not of the mother. What would a *kohen* have to do with the monetary value of the mother animal?

### Pregnant Animal

The *Gemara* therefore reframes the case: *kegon de-yahvei behema me'ubarta* — we are dealing with a case where the Jew gave the non-Jew a pregnant animal. The animal is already carrying a fetus, and the arrangement is not merely that the non-Jew will receive future offspring, but also that he receives a benefit-share in the animal itself.

### Fattening Rights

Specifically, the arrangement includes *l'ishtumei* — fattening rights. The non-Jew not only receives half of every offspring born but also benefits from the animal's increase in value through his tending, feeding, and fattening of it. This means he holds a partial economic stake in the mother animal — not just in the *velados*. When the arrangement includes benefit from the mother's increase in value, the Jew has effectively given the non-Jew a share in the mother itself, which is a clear violation of the prohibition against selling *behema gassa* to a non-Jew.

### Extension Logic

*De-migo de-kansinan leih a-behema* — since the court would impose a fine on the Jew for the violation regarding the mother, *kansinan leih a-ubar* — the court extends that fine to the fetus as well. The Jew is compelled to buy back both, and must give the full value to the *kohen*. The *braita*'s harsh consequences — buying back at up to ten times the value and giving the full amount to the *kohen* — flow from the violation concerning the mother. They then cascade onto the fetus as a further extension of the penalty.

The implication is significant: the *braita*, on this reading, does not establish that Rabbi Yehuda considers the fetus transaction itself to be a violation. The violation is the mother, and the fetus penalties are derivative. We therefore still lack a definitive answer from the *braita* alone about whether Rabbi Yehuda independently considers selling the fetus to be prohibited.

## Rav Ashi Logic

### Exception Rehash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר בַּשְּׁבוּרָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לְהִתְרַפְּאוֹת. הָא יְכוֹלָה לְהִתְרַפְּאוֹת — אָסַר, וְהָא עוּבָּר נָמֵי כְּיָכוֹל לְהִתְרַפְּאוֹת דָּמֵי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: Come** and **hear** a resolution from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the sale to a gentile of **a damaged** animal **permitted, because it is incapable of being cured.** This statement indicates that if it is **capable of being cured,** Rabbi Yehuda **deems** its sale **prohibited. And a fetus** is **also similar to** one who is **able to be cured,** as it ultimately will be capable of working. **Learn from it** that Rabbi Yehuda deems the sale of a fetus to a gentile prohibited.

At this point, Rav Ashi intervenes: *ta shema* — come and hear. He returns to the *mishna* in *Avodah Zarah* about Rabbi Yehuda's broken-leg exception.

### No Healing

Rav Ashi highlights a detail embedded in that ruling: Rabbi Yehuda permits selling a broken-legged animal *because it cannot heal* — *einah yecholah le-hitrapot*. The exception is conditioned on the irreversibility of the injury.

### Future Capacity

This formulation carries an implicit corollary: *ha-yecholah le-hitrapot — assur* — if the animal could heal, it would be forbidden. The exception holds only when there is no future possibility that the animal will recover and become capable of *melacha*.

### Fetus Analogy

*Ve-ha ubar nami, de-hayecholah le-hitrapot dami* — and the fetus is precisely analogous to something that can heal. A fetus will be born, will grow, and will eventually be capable of working on *Shabbat*. In that sense, it is in exactly the category that Rabbi Yehuda himself would classify as forbidden — the category of conditions that are not permanent and from which the animal will emerge capable of labor.

### Conclusion

*Shema minah* — the *Gemara* is satisfied with this reasoning. The conclusion follows: Rabbi Yehuda holds that selling a fetus to a non-Jew does violate the prohibition. Our *mishna*, which says *af al pi she-eino rashai* regarding the fetus sale, does indeed reflect Rabbi Yehuda's view, not just that of the Sages.

## Alt Teaching Order

### Version Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַמַּתְנִיתִין, וְהַמּוֹכֵר לוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי. לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר בַּשְּׁבוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And there are those who teach** this discussion as referring **to** the statement in **the mishna:** With regard to **one who sells** the fetus of his donkey **to** a gentile, **although he is not permitted** to sell a large animal to a gentile, it is exempt from the obligations of firstborn status. The discussion then proceeded as follows: **Let us say** that **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as we learned** in a mishna (*Avoda Zara* 14b): **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the sale **of a damaged** animal **permitted** because it is incapable of performing labor. The *halakha* is presumably the same with regard to a fetus, which is also not capable of performing labor.

The *Gemara* notes: *ve-ika de-matni lah a-matnitin* — there is an alternative version of the tradition in which this entire discussion is framed differently. While above the discussion arose in the context of learning *Masechet Avodah Zarah* — where someone cited our *mishna* in *Bekhorot* as relevant evidence — in this alternate version, the discussion begins from our *mishna* in *Bekhorot*, and someone cites the material from *Avodah Zarah* as relevant.

### Mishna Hypothesis

In this version, the starting point is the question: *leima matnitin dela ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — perhaps our *mishna* does not reflect Rabbi Yehuda's position at all? Since the *mishna* says you are not permitted to sell the fetus, and since Rabbi Yehuda allows the broken-leg case, perhaps Rabbi Yehuda would similarly permit the fetus — meaning our *mishna* does not accord with his view.

### Cross-Ref

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה — שְׁבוּרָה, לָאו הַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ; עוּבָּר, הַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **You** may **even say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** as it is permitted to sell **a damaged** animal because **that is not its natural** state; it is defective and is therefore not included in the prohibition against selling large livestock. But with regard to **a fetus, since it is** in **its natural** state, and will become capable of working after growing up, perhaps it is not considered damaged, as it is not defective.

The response is: *de-tnan, Avodah Zarah* — no, consider what is taught in *Masechet Avodah Zarah*: Rabbi Yehuda permits specifically the broken-leg case, and the reason, as will emerge, is precisely the *einah yecholah le-hitrapot* condition.

### Repeated Analysis

The *Gemara* then works through essentially the same analysis we already encountered: *shvura lav hainu orchai* — a broken leg is abnormal, while *ubar hainu orchai* — a fetus is a normal condition. Therefore, even Rabbi Yehuda would agree that the fetus falls under the general prohibition, not under his broken-leg exception. And the conclusion from Rav Ashi's point — *ha-yecholah le-hitrapot assur*, and the fetus is analogous — is reached again. The two versions of the tradition converge on the same conclusions through the same reasoning.

## Future Fetuses

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מָכַר בְּהֵמָה לְעוּבָּרֶיהָ, מַאי? תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages concerning the prohibition against selling large livestock to a gentile: In the case of a Jew who **sold an animal** to a gentile only with regard to rights **to its fetuses,** but retained ownership of the animal itself, **what** is the *halakha* concerning the permissibility of the sale? The Gemara clarifies: **Let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who permits the sale of a damaged animal, and **let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who dispute that ruling.

*Iba'aya lehu* — a new question is now raised: *mocher behema le-ubara mahu?* — what is the law if one sells the mother specifically in order to convey rights to its future offspring, to fetuses that do not yet exist and are not currently in the mother's womb? Does this constitute a violation of the prohibition against selling *behema gassa* to a non-Jew?

### Ownership Transfer

The conceptual mechanism being invoked here is the notion of *mocher behema li-veladoseha* — selling the animal for its offspring-rights. The principle at work is that while one cannot sell something that does not yet exist — *davar she-lo ba le-olam* — there is a recognized rabbinic concept by which one can transfer a defined dimension of ownership. The Rabbis have an analogous concept from the laws of trees: I can sell you a tree *li-feroteiha* — for its fruit. I am not selling you the fruit, which does not yet exist; rather, I am transferring to you that aspect of ownership in the tree which entitles the owner to receive its fruit. You retain the other aspects of ownership — the right to cut it down, to use the wood — while I now hold the fruit-rights.

## Future Fetuses…

### Non-Existent Rule

The doctrine of *davar she-lo ba le-olam* — that one cannot effect a legal transfer of something that does not yet exist — is a foundational principle of rabbinic property law. Ordinarily, a sale requires that the subject of the sale have present existence.

### Fruit Analogy

The tree-for-fruit model shows how this constraint can be worked around: ownership itself is not a monolithic concept. A single object can be owned in distinct, separable dimensions. By transferring one specific dimension of ownership — the entitlement to benefit from future fruit — while retaining all others, the parties accomplish a meaningful legal transfer without running afoul of *davar she-lo ba le-olam*. The buyer does not own nonexistent fruit; he owns a present, existing stake in the tree that will yield fruit.

### Rights Ownership

The same structure applies here: *livladoseha* — selling for offspring-rights. The seller retains the right to use the animal for work, to milk it, to shear it, to slaughter it. But the buyer acquires that dimension of the seller's ownership which entitles him to the offspring as they are born. This is a present transfer of a real ownership stake, not a sale of nonexistent animals.

## Whole Animal Debate

### Both Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who permits the sale of a damaged animal, and **let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who dispute that ruling.

*Tiba'ei le-Rabbi Yehuda, tiba'ei le-Rabbanan* — the question of selling for offspring-rights can be asked according to both Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis. Each has its own framework for determining what falls within the prohibition and what does not, and it is not obvious which direction the analysis points.

### Limited Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: עַד כָּאן לָא קָא שָׁרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּרָה, דְּלָא אָתְיָא לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי, אֲבָל שְׁלֵמָה דְּאָתְיָא לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי — אָסַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** as follows: Perhaps **Rabbi Yehuda permits only** the sale **of a damaged** animal, **as** people **will not come to confuse** this animal with a healthy animal, which is prohibited for sale to a gentile, as they are visibly different. **But** in the case of one who sells **an unblemished** animal with regard to the rights to its fetuses, **which** people may **come to confuse** with the case of selling the animal entirely and consider that sale permitted as well, Rabbi Yehuda **deems** the sale **prohibited.**

Considering Rabbi Yehuda first: *ad kan lo shari ela bi-shvura* — Rabbi Yehuda permits only the broken-leg case, and the reason, as established, is *de-lo ati le-ichlufei* — because a broken-legged animal will not be confused with a normal animal. It is visually distinct, obviously incapacitated, and therefore an exception can be made without creating confusing precedents. People can see that this is a special case and will not extrapolate to other situations.

### Visual Distinction

The logic of Rabbi Yehuda's exception rests on visual distinctiveness: the broken leg is observable and unmistakable. This is why *lo plug* does not apply — the distinction is so obvious that it does not invite confusion.

### Whole Confusable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא, וּמָה שְׁבוּרָה דִּפְסַקָה מִינֵּהּ, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שְׁלֵמָה דְּלָא פְּסַקָה מִינֵּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** Rabbi Yehuda would claim that in the case of **a damaged** animal, **which is separated from him** in its entirety in the sale, the sale is permitted, **and all the more so** with regard to the sale of **an unblemished** animal with regard to the rights to its fetuses, **where** the animal **is not** entirely **separated from him,** as he did not sell the actual animal, the sale is permitted.

*Aval shleima* — but a whole, healthy animal sold for its future offspring looks, externally, identical to a whole animal that has been fully transferred to the non-Jew. An observer sees the non-Jew with the animal in his possession or sees him receiving offspring and cannot tell from appearances whether the non-Jew owns everything or merely the offspring-rights. The visual distinction that justified Rabbi Yehuda's broken-leg exception is entirely absent here. Therefore, Rabbi Yehuda might well say that even this arrangement falls under the general prohibition — *lo plug* applies because there is no observable marker that would prevent confusion.

### Distinction Debate

On the other hand, the argument can be inverted. One could say: Rabbi Yehuda permits the broken-leg case even though the non-Jew takes the entire animal and severs all connection with the Jewish owner. The animal leaves the Jew's possession entirely. How much more so should Rabbi Yehuda permit the case where the animal remains fundamentally the Jew's property — where the Jew retains ownership, the animal stays in the Jew's domain, and only the offspring-entitlement has been transferred? The lesser degree of divestiture should more clearly fall outside the concern.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבָּנַן: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָסְרִי רַבָּנַן אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּרָה, דִּפְסַקָה מִינֵּהּ, אֲבָל שְׁלֵמָה דְּלָא פְּסַקָה מִינֵּהּ — שָׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **the dilemma can be raised according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis:** Perhaps **the Rabbis deemed** the sale **prohibited only in** a case of **a damaged** animal, **as** the animal **is separated from him. But** in the case of **an unblemished** animal that is sold only with regard to the fetuses, **where it is not separated from him,** and there is no concern that people will mistakenly sell the entire animal to a gentile, **they deem** the sale **permitted.**

The same bifurcated argument applies to the Rabbis. They might argue: we only prohibit the broken-leg case — where the Jew fully divests himself of the animal — because the complete transfer looks like a full sale. But where the animal remains the Jew's property and only offspring-rights are conveyed, that is sufficiently distinct that we need not prohibit it. Or conversely: if we prohibit even the broken-leg case — which is visually obviously different from a normal sale — how much more so must we prohibit the case of a whole, healthy animal where the visual similarity to a full prohibited sale is even greater?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא: וּמָה שְׁבוּרָה דְּלָא אָתְיָא לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי — אָסְרִי, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שְׁלֵמָה דְּאָתְיָא לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** the Rabbis would claim that in the case of **a damaged** animal, **which** people **will not come to confuse** with the case of an unblemished animal, the Rabbis **deem** the sale **prohibited, and all the more so** they would prohibit selling **an unblemished** animal with regard to the rights to its fetuses, **which** people may **come to confuse** with the case of selling an unblemished animal in its entirety.

The *Gemara* leaves this as an unresolved question — *teku*.

## Concern Basis

### Rabbis' Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא דְרַבָּנַן מִשּׁוּם הָכִי הוּא? וְהָתַנְיָא: אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא מַרְבִּיעִין עָלֶיהָ וְיוֹלֶדֶת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is the reason of** the opinion of **the Rabbis due to that** concern that people may confuse the sale of a damaged animal with the sale of an unblemished animal in its entirety? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that the Rabbis **said to Rabbi Yehuda: But** why do you hold that is it permitted to sell a damaged animal? **Don't they breed it with** another animal, **and it gives birth** to offspring, which will then be owned by a gentile? **Apparently,** the reason the Rabbis prohibit the sale **is due to** the animal's **fetuses.** If so, they should prohibit selling large livestock to a gentile with regard to the fetuses as well.

The *Gemara* now asks a foundational question that reframes the entire previous discussion: *ve-ta'amei de-Rabbanan mishum hachi hu?* — is it really true that the Rabbis' reason for disagreeing with Rabbi Yehuda is the *lo plug* concern — the worry about making confusing distinctions? Is that the actual basis of their position?

### Rabbis Respond

The *Gemara* invokes a *braita* in which the Rabbis respond to Rabbi Yehuda's broken-leg exception: *amru lo* — the Rabbis said to Rabbi Yehuda: even if this animal has a broken leg and cannot itself perform *melacha* on *Shabbat*, there is still a concern.

### Future Breeding

*Ve-halo marbi'in aleiha bnei veladot* — one may use this broken-legged animal for breeding. The non-Jew will have it mate with other animals, and the offspring will eventually be healthy animals that can and will be put to work on *Shabbat*. The concern is not limited to what this specific animal can do — it extends to what becomes possible through this animal's presence in the non-Jew's household.

### Initial Impression

The *Gemara* initially reads this as saying that the Rabbis have an independent concern: simply having young animals born into the non-Jew's possession is itself objectionable — *mishum ubra hu* — irrespective of any *Shabbat*-work calculation. If so, the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda are not even in the same conversation; the Rabbis' concern would be categorically different and could not be analyzed using Rabbi Yehuda's *lo plug* framework.

### Real Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: טַעְמָא דִידַן מִשּׁוּם דְּאָתְיָא לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בִּבְהֵמָה, אֶלָּא אַתְּ, מַאי טַעְמָא שָׁרֵית? מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין יְכוֹלָה לְהִתְרַפְּאוֹת, כְּמַאן דְּזַבְּנַהּ לִשְׁחִיטָה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is not truly the Rabbis' opinion. Rather, **this is what they were saying to** Rabbi Yehuda: **Our reason** for prohibiting the sale of a damaged animal is **because** people might **come to confuse** this case **with** the sale of an unblemished **animal. But you, what is the reason** that **you permit** selling a damaged animal? It is **because** the animal is **incapable of being cured,** and you therefore consider it **similar to one who sold** an animal explicitly **for slaughter,** which is permitted.

The *Gemara* corrects this impression: *hachi ka'amar leh* — this is what the Rabbis are really saying to Rabbi Yehuda. Our concern is the same as yours — *ta'ama didan mishum de-ati le-ichlufei bi-behema* — we are concerned about *lo plug*, about people confusing permissible and prohibited cases. You say the broken-leg case is an exception because it is visually obvious. We say: it is not so obvious, because even a broken-legged animal will be kept around for breeding rather than immediately slaughtered. The animal stays in the non-Jew's possession, it is used, and observers will see what appears to be a Jew's animal in non-Jewish hands — and will not understand why the case is different.

### Lo Plug Focus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא מַרְבִּיעִין עָלֶיהָ וְיוֹלֶדֶת, וְכֵיוָן דְּמַרְבִּיעִין עָלֶיהָ וְיוֹלֶדֶת — מְשַׁהֵא לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **But** actually it is not similar to that case, as **don't they breed** a damaged animal **with** another animal, **and it gives birth? And since they breed it and it gives birth,** the gentile will **delay** its slaughter. Others may then mistakenly believe that selling large livestock for purposes other than slaughter is permitted.

Rabbi Yehuda assumed that a broken-legged animal would promptly be slaughtered and would not remain in the non-Jew's home, making the exception visually clean. The Rabbis respond that this assumption is wrong — *marbi'in aleiha bnei veladot* — the animal may well be kept for breeding, and the visual clarity Rabbi Yehuda assumed will not materialize. Therefore, the Rabbis maintain *lo plug*: we do not permit even the visually differentiated cases, because the differentiation is not reliable.

Both positions, then, share the same underlying concern about appearances, *Shabbat*, and the risks of confusion. Their dispute is entirely about the reliability of the visual distinction in the broken-leg case. Rabbi Yehuda is convinced the distinction is patent enough to be an exception; the Rabbis are not. And since they share the same framework, the question about selling for future offspring-rights — whether there is a sufficiently reliable distinction — can legitimately be asked according to both positions.

## Oath Concern

### Partnership Banned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה שׁוּתָּפוּת עִם הַגּוֹי, שֶׁמָּא יִתְחַיֵּיב לוֹ שְׁבוּעָה וְנִשְׁבָּע לוֹ בְּשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁלּוֹ, וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״לֹא יִשָּׁמַע עַל פִּיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The teaching cited is from the father of Shmuel: **It is prohibited for a person to enter** into **a partnership with a gentile** because it might lead them to quarrel and his gentile partner **will be obligated to take an oath to him and he will take an oath in the name of his** object of **idol worship; and the Torah states:** "Neither let it be heard out of your mouth" (Exodus 23:13), which includes causing a gentile to take an oath in the name of an idol.

The *Gemara* now turns to the prohibition of *shutafut* — the concern about entering a business partnership with a non-Jew, as invoked above in explaining why the *mishna*'s *af al pi she-eino rashai* extends beyond the fetus-sale case. The teaching cited is from the father of Shmuel: *assur le-adam le-hishtatef im ha-oved kochavim* — it is forbidden for a person to enter into a partnership with a non-Jew.

### Oath Risk

The reason: *shema yitchayev lo shevuah* — perhaps in the course of the partnership, the Jewish partner will demand that the non-Jewish partner take an oath to verify his honesty in keeping the accounts or managing the business.

### Foreign God Oath

*Ve-nishba lo be-shem avodat kochavim shelo* — and the non-Jew will swear in the name of his deity. This is not a theoretical concern. In the ancient and medieval world, business partners who suspected misconduct routinely compelled oaths from one another, and those oaths were sworn by whatever god the swearer revered.

### Torah Prohibition

*Ve-ha-Torah amrah lo yishama al picha* — the Torah states that the name of other gods shall not be heard upon your lips. The formulation is notably passive or causative: not merely that one shall not personally invoke a foreign deity, but that one shall not be the cause of a foreign deity's name being uttered in one's presence or in one's business context.

### Causation Issue

The prohibition thus extends beyond active invocation: even causing a non-Jew to swear by his deity — by placing him in a situation where such an oath might be demanded — constitutes a violation of *lo yishama al picha*. The Jew who enters a partnership thereby creates the conditions under which such an oath might arise, and is therefore responsible for the resulting invocation.

## Partnership Omission

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, תְּנָא מְכִירָה, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְשׁוּתָּפוּת. הָכִי נָמֵי, תְּנָא מְכִירָה וְהוּא הַדִּין לְקַבְּלָנוּת. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מְכִירָה דְּנָקֵט? דְּעִיקָּר מְכִירָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *tanna* **taught** that **a sale** to a gentile is not permitted, **and the same is true for** the case of **a partnership. So too,** the *tanna* **taught** that **a sale** to a gentile is prohibited, **and the same is true for** a case of **receivership.** The Gemara asks: **And what is different** about the case of **selling that** the *tanna* **cited** the prohibition specifically in that case? The Gemara answers **that** the **primary** prohibition **is** that of **selling** the animal to a gentile.

*U-letamecha* — but if the concern about partnership is the oath prohibition, why does our *mishna* not explicitly say *af al pi she-eino rashai* — that one is not permitted — in the partnership case (*mishtatef*) as well? The *mishna* states the prohibition only in the context of selling the fetus.

### Mechira Teaching

The *Gemara* answers: *tana mechira avuha de-shutafut* — the *mishna* teaches the case of *mechira* — the sale — and that teaching implicitly encompasses *shutafut* and the other cases as well. Since *mechira* is mentioned and the *mishna* attached the *af al pi she-eino rashai* qualifier to it, the reader understands that other forms of prohibited dealings similarly carry the qualifier.

### Mechira Fundamental

*De-ikar mechira hi* — *mechira* is the foundational and prototypical case of these transactions. It is therefore the natural locus for the *mishna*'s explicit statement of prohibition.

### Tosafot View

Tosafot adds the simpler observation: *mechira* was mentioned first in the *mishna*, and the qualifier *af al pi she-eino rashai* was attached to that first case. It then carries forward implicitly to all subsequent cases. There was no need to repeat it. The *mishna* follows the standard practice of attaching a qualification to the first relevant instance, which then extends by implication.

## Critical Tosafot

### Actual Scope

This Tosafot on *shutafut* is notable not only for its immediate textual role but for what it signals about the practical scope of the partnership prohibition. The prohibition — based on the father of Shmuel's statement that one may not partner with a non-Jew lest one be brought to compel an oath — would, if applied broadly, impose severe economic constraints on Jews whose entire commercial lives were intertwined with non-Jewish neighbors and business partners.

### Oath Reality

Tosafot observes that across the Talmud there are numerous cases in which Jews and non-Jews do appear to enter partnerships and joint business arrangements, and not all *Tannaim* and *Amoraim* accept the father of Shmuel's prohibition in its full scope. The cases in *Gemara* that seem to presuppose joint arrangements — buying a field in partnership, splitting *orlah* produce, and others — constitute apparent counterexamples that Tosafot catalogues, noting *kol hanei uvdei pligi a-avuha de-Shmuel* — all of those cases imply disagreement with the father of Shmuel.

### Next Tosafot

The very next Tosafot — on the word *shema* — is one of the most halakhically significant *Tosafot* in the entire tractate. It engages directly with the question of whether, and in what circumstances, Jews and non-Jews could in fact have financial dealings with one another, given this oath concern.

### Christian Oaths

The question is whether the oath concern applies to Christians specifically. When a Christian takes an oath in business dealings, he does so in the name of Jesus or the saints. Does such an oath constitute swearing *be-shem avodat kochavim* — in the name of an idolatrous deity — such that a Jew who compels or enables such an oath has violated *lo yishama al picha*? Or is the status of Christian oath-taking different, given the theological character of Christianity's relationship to monotheism? This question has enormous practical implications for Jewish commercial life throughout the medieval period and beyond.

### Tomorrow Preview

This Tosafot is among the foundational texts for how the Rishonim and Acharonim think about the halakhic status of non-Jews in general, and of Christians in particular, in the context of commercial dealings, oaths, and *avodat kochavim*. We will begin with it first thing tomorrow.
