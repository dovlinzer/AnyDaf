# Moed Katan 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Righteous Death & Atonem…

### Women's Burial

The daf opens on the final line of 27b. The Mishnah taught that we do not allow a woman's coffin to lie in the street even on *Chol HaMoed*, out of concern for her dignity. The Sages of Nehardea qualify this: the ruling applies specifically to a woman who died in childbirth, whose body is in a state of physical deterioration, making disfigurement a pressing concern. For other women, *aval sh'ar nashim m'nichim* — the body may be left while people gather for eulogizing and wailing. Rabbi Eliezer, however, reads the Mishnah literally: even other women must be buried without delay. His proof is from the verse describing Miriam's death — *vatamas sham Miriam vatikaver sham* — "Miriam died there and was buried there," which implies that burial followed immediately upon death. The precise social and halakhic contours of *kavod* here are admittedly context-dependent and not entirely transparent to later readers, but the verse was clearly understood to mandate swift burial.

### Miriam Divine Kiss

Having invoked Miriam's death, the Gemara turns to a further teaching: *af Miriam b'nishika mesa* — Miriam too died by the divine kiss. This conclusion is derived through a *gezera shava* from the word *sham*: just as the Torah says *vatamas sham* by Miriam, and by Moshe it says *vayamas sham*, and Moshe's death is described explicitly as *al pi Hashem* — by the mouth of God — so too Miriam's death is understood to have been of that intimate, tranquil character. One might ask why the verse does not state this explicitly in Miriam's case. The Gemara answers: *mipnei shegnai hadavar l'omro* — it would have been unseemly to speak of God's kiss in relation to a woman, a concern that did not apply in the same way to Moshe.

### Tzadikkim Atonement

Rabbi Ami asks: why is the death of Miriam juxtaposed in the Torah with the passage of *para aduma*? The answer: *mah para aduma m'chaperes af misas tzadikkim m'chaperes* — just as the red heifer atones, so the death of the righteous atones. This is, on its face, a striking comparison. The *para aduma* would not ordinarily top one's list of atoning sacrifices — it is indeed called a *chatas*, but it functions in ways that set it apart from conventional atonement. What it shares with certain other rituals — the *egla arufa* and the *seir hamishtaleach* — is that it operates outside the *Mikdash* and involves the giving of a life. In that sense, the comparison to *misas tzadikkim* carries internal logic: here too is a life given over outside the sanctuary, and here too atonement is effected.

This idea — that the death of the righteous atones for the generation — is one that people sometimes reflexively associate with Christian theology. But as the Gemara makes plain, it belongs to the tradition itself. Whether or not it sits comfortably within all frameworks of Jewish thought, it is genuinely present here, and it reflects a broader theological move the *masechta* makes as it draws toward its close: shifting from the technical *halachot* of *avelut* toward the pastoral and theological dimensions of death and loss.

Rabbi Elazar adds a parallel teaching: why is the death of Aharon juxtaposed with the *bigdei kehuna*? The context is the scene in which Aharon is stripped of the priestly vestments and Elazar is clothed in them before Aharon's death. From this juxtaposition the Gemara derives: *mah bigdei kehuna m'chaprin af misas tzadikkim m'chaperes* — just as the priestly garments atone — and the Gemara has established elsewhere that Aharon "carried the iniquity of the holy things" through the wearing of certain *bigadim* — so too the death of the righteous atones. The analogy here is perhaps less jarring than the *para aduma* comparison, though the precise mechanism of comparison remains somewhat opaque. The common thread is the conclusion: *misas tzadikkim m'chaper*.

What is notable, standing at the end of *Moed Katan*, is this thematic turn. The *masechta* has spent much of its length in the domain of practical *hilchot avelut* — who may and may not cut hair, what may be done on *Chol HaMoed*, what constitutes excessive mourning. Now, in its final pages, it pivots toward something more pastoral: how does one think theologically about death? For the death of a *tzaddik*, one framework offered is that the life given over was itself an act of cosmic significance — atoning for the generation, much as the *para aduma* or the *bigdei kehuna* atoned for Israel.

## Death Categories

### Sudden Death Types

The Gemara proceeds to a *baraita* that categorizes death according to the manner in which it comes. *Met pitom* — dying suddenly, without any warning or opportunity for the person or family to prepare — is called *mita chatufa*, a snatched death. *Chala yom echad umet* — if one fell ill for a single day and then died — this is *mita dchufa*, a rushed or pressed death. Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel disagrees: a one-day death is in fact a *mita mageifa*, a plague death, based on the verse in which God tells the prophet that He will take "the delight of your eyes" — referring to his wife — *bemageifa*, in a plague, and the following verse reports that she died the same evening. Two days of illness followed by death is *mita dchuya*; three days is called *ge'ara*, a reprimand; four days is *nezifa*, some form of rebuke; and five days or more is *misas kol adam* — a normal human death.

*Tosafot* flags an honest difficulty here: *lo yadana mai shna* — it is not entirely clear what distinguishes each of these categories from the next, nor is the Gemara's source for the full progression obvious. Nevertheless, the underlying concern is evident: the tradition is attempting to give families a framework for understanding — and perhaps some degree of consolation — in the face of the particular circumstances of a death, even if individuals cannot influence these things. *Amar Rabbi Chanina*, what is the verse? *Hen karevu yamecha lamut* — behold your days have drawn close to die. *Hen* is one — because, as the Gemara notes, in Greek *hen* means one. *Karevu* is plural, hence two. *Yamecha* is also plural, hence two more. That yields five total — corresponding to the five-day threshold of *misas kol adam*.

### Ages of Death

The Gemara now turns from the speed of dying to the age at which one dies. Dying at fifty years is called *mitat kares* — a death of *kares* — reflecting the Torah's notion of being "cut off," understood here as a shortened life. Dying at fifty-two is called *misato shel Shmuel HaRamasi*, because that is understood to be the age at which the prophet Shmuel died. Dying at sixty is *mita bidei shamayim* — death at the hands of Heaven. The question arises immediately: is that a good or a bad thing? The term *mita bidei shamayim* is sometimes used in contexts of divine punishment. Mar Zutra resolves this: the verse says *tavo be'chelach alei kever* — you shall come into the grave at the proper time — and the gematria of *bakelach* equals sixty. So sixty is understood here as a natural and dignified death. Seventy is *seiva*, old age; eighty is *gevuros*, the strength of old age, corresponding to the verse in Psalms, *yemei shnosenu bahem shivim shana* — our years are seventy, *v'im bigvuros shmonim shana* — and if with strength, eighty years.

Rava clarifies the range for *kares*: from fifty to sixty years of age constitutes *misas kares*. That means Shmuel HaRamasi, who died at fifty-two, died within the *kares* range. The Gemara then asks: if so, why did the earlier formulation state only "fifty" without specifying the full range? The answer: *mishum kvodo shel Shmuel HaRamasi* — out of respect for Shmuel, so as not to spotlight his death as falling within this troubling bracket.

### Rav Yosef Age 60

When Rav Yosef reached sixty, he made a celebratory feast for the rabbis and declared: *nafki li mikares baruch Hashem* — I have passed out of the *kares* range. Abaye, one of his students, immediately offered a pointed qualification: it is true, he said, that the Master has exited the *kares* of years, *aval mikares deyomi mi nafik mar* — but how do you know you have exited the *kares* of days? The discussion earlier about a one-day or two-day death still looms as a possible indicator. Rav Yosef's response was characteristically balanced: *nekot lach mi'palga beyadach* — hold onto at least half a victory; half I've managed to clear, and we will deal with the rest when the time comes.

One naturally wonders how psychologically healthy this entire system of categories is — the anxiety it might generate in a dying person or their family as they track whether the death fits into a good or bad symbolic pattern. The Gemara seems aware of this tension, as we will see.

A brief narrative follows: Rav Huna died suddenly, and the rabbis were disturbed — what does this signify about so great a man? A pair of scholars from Hadaiv came and offered a resolution: the rule that sudden death is a poor sign applies only when the person has not yet reached *gevuros* — eighty years. But if a person has indeed reached eighty, then dying suddenly in one's sleep is *misas neshika*, death by divine kiss. The old age outweighs the suddenness.

## Mazal vs. Merit

### Rava Mazal

Against the entire framework of death-as-indicator, Rava offers a powerful counter-teaching: *chayei benei u'mezonei lo bizchusa talia milsa ela b'mazala talia milsa* — a person's length of life, their children, and their financial sustenance are not determined by their merit and righteousness, but by *mazal*, by fortune. People of equivalent piety may fare entirely differently in all of these dimensions, and we should not read those outcomes as signs or indicators of the person's standing before God.

Rava does not leave this as an abstract philosophical claim. He offers what one might call empirical evidence — and one has to admire the Gemara's willingness to test theological propositions against observable reality.

### Rava Chisda Compare

*Rava ve'Rav Chisda tarvaihu rabanan tzadiki havu* — both Rava and Rav Chisda were thoroughly righteous rabbis. The evidence for their equivalent righteousness: when either one would pray for rain during a drought, rain would come. This is the standard established in *Taanis* for divine connectedness of the highest order.

And yet: Rav Chisda lived to ninety-two; Rava lived to forty. In Rav Chisda's house there were sixty wedding celebrations; in Rava's house, sixty tragedies — or by some readings, sixty bereavements. In Rav Chisda's house, fine flour was so abundant it could be fed to the dogs without concern; in Rava's house, barley bread for human beings was barely to be found.

Here, then, is the *benei*, the *chayei*, and the *mezonei* — children and joy, length of life, and sustenance — all distributed with radical asymmetry between two men of identical righteousness. The conclusion Rava draws is pointed: do not go looking for *simanim* in any of this. When Rav Huna died suddenly, the anxiety the rabbis felt about what it signified about their master is understandable, but Rava's teaching here is a corrective: these things do not map onto merit.

The Gemara then adds Rava's own personal testimony: *hani talas mili ba'i kamei shamaya* — I asked three things of Heaven. The wisdom of Rav Huna and the wealth of Rav Chisda were both granted; the humility of Rabba bar Rav Huna was not. The implicit irony — a man who openly confesses that humility eluded him — is hard to miss. This teaching sits in interesting tension with the preceding one: Rava has just argued that *mazal* determines outcomes with no reference to merit or divine involvement, and yet here he describes asking God for personal qualities and receiving some of them. The tension is real, and the Gemara does not resolve it.

## Malach HaMavet Stories

### Rava Brother Deaths

Rav Se'orin was the brother of Rava. As Rava sat beside him and watched him begin to slip away, Rava asked his brother: *leimal lei mar d'lo litzaran* — tell the Angel of Death not to cause me anguish. Rav Se'orin replied: *mar lav shushpinei hu?* — but aren't you his colleague? You're so righteous, surely you have standing to make the request yourself. Rava answered: *keivan de'imsar mazla lo ashgach bi* — once my *mazal* has been handed over, once I am in the process of dying, he no longer pays attention to me.

Here again the motif of *mazal* recurs: even the righteous person, at the moment of death, loses whatever standing or influence they might have had. This is a deeply humbling idea — something akin to the king who is dead losing all of his royal significance, as *Koheles* suggests.

Rav Se'orin then made his request: appear to me after you die. Rava did appear to him, and when Rav Se'orin asked whether dying was painful, Rava replied: *kividit de'chusilta* — like the prick of a bloodletting horn. A minimal, momentary pain.

### Rav Nachman Appears

The Gemara now offers what appears to be an earlier, parallel scene. Rava was sitting before Rav Nachman as the latter was slipping away. Rava made the same request: tell him not to cause you pain. Rav Nachman replied: *man chashiv, man safin, man rakia?* — who has importance, who has weight, who has significance? The response is more poetic than Rava's own answer to his brother, but it carries the same essential meaning: at the moment of dying, no one has standing before the *Malach HaMavet*, regardless of their stature in life.

Rava asked Rav Nachman to appear to him after death. When Rav Nachman appeared, Rava asked whether the death had been painful. Rav Nachman replied: *kinishcha benisa meichalva* — like the drawing of a hair from milk. Smoother even than what Rava himself would later report. And yet, Rav Nachman added: even so, if God were to offer him the chance to return to the living world as he had been, *lo baina* — he would not want it. *Denafish bisusei* — the fear of dying a second time would be too great. This is a striking observation: death without pain is still death, and the prospect of facing it again is something he would refuse even at the cost of continued life.

### Rabbi Elazar Truma

The Gemara now turns to stories of negotiating with or deflecting the *Malach HaMavet*. Rabbi Elazar was in the middle of eating *truma* when the Angel of Death appeared to him. He said: I am eating *truma*, performing a *mitzvah* — this is something holy, you cannot interfere. The moment passed. The implication is significant: this was not merely a brief delay; the *sha'ah* — the moment — passed entirely, presumably meaning the death was postponed for a substantial period, not merely pushed off temporarily.

### Rav Sheshes Encounter

When the Angel of Death appeared to Rav Sheshes in the marketplace, Rav Sheshes objected: *beshuka keveheima?* — you're going to take me down in the marketplace like an animal? That is no place for a person of my standing to die. *Isa legabei beisa* — come to my house, and we will see to it there. And so, presumably, that is what occurred.

One student pointed out that Rav Sheshes was blind — a reasonable question about how he perceived the *Malach HaMavet* in the marketplace. The answer offered was that physical sight may not be what is involved in such a perception. Whatever the case, the story depicts Rav Sheshes as asserting his dignity even in that moment.

### Rav Ashi Review

Rav Ashi's encounter takes a different form. When the Angel of Death appeared to him in the marketplace, Rav Ashi said: *itrich li tlasin yomi ve-ahadri talmudai* — give me thirty days so I can review all my learning. *Ashrei mi sheba lekan vetalmudo beyado* — praised is the one who arrives in Heaven with his learning intact. The *Malach HaMavet* granted the thirty days. But on day thirty, he returned. Rav Ashi asked: what is the great hurry? The reply: *kedachaka raglei d'bar nassan* — you are pressing on the feet of Bar Nassan; he is ready to take your place at the head of the academy, and as long as you remain alive, he cannot rise to his rightful station. *D'ein malchus nogaas bachaverta afilu k'mlo nima* — one kingship cannot encroach upon another, even by a hairbreadth.

### Chisda Chiya Silent

The Gemara offers two final portraits. Rav Chisda, it is said, could not be seized by the *Malach HaMavet* at all: *d'lo hava shtik pumei m'girsa* — his mouth never stopped learning Torah, and the *Malach HaMavet* could find no opening. The Angel therefore ascended and sat in a cedar tree in the *yeshiva*. The cedar burst — presumably from the weight of the divine presence — and the momentary silence that followed gave the *Malach HaMavet* the opening he needed.

Rabbi Chiya similarly could not be approached. One day the *Malach HaMavet* disguised himself as a poor person and knocked at the door asking for bread. Rabbi Chiya brought it to him. The Angel then said: if the Master has compassion on this poor man, why can he not have compassion on me? I have a task to fulfill. He revealed himself, showing Rabbi Chiya a rod of flame. Rabbi Chiya, in compassion, yielded his soul willingly. The moral texture of this final story is particularly rich: Rabbi Chiya's great virtue — his compassion for the poor — is precisely the quality that ultimately leaves him unable to refuse.

## Mourning Restrictions

### Women Wailing Rules

The final Mishnah of the tractate opens on 27b. *Nashim b'moed meanos v'lo metapchos* — on *Chol HaMoed*, women may engage in *meanos* but may not perform *metapchos*. The terms will be defined shortly. Rabbi Yishmael says that those women positioned closest to the bier may perform *metapeach* — a limited exception that balances the honor due to the deceased against the restrictions of *Chol HaMoed*. On days of intermediate sanctity but lesser weight — *Rosh Chodesh*, *Chanuka*, and *Purim* — both *meanos* and *metapchos* are permitted. But in all cases — whether *Chol HaMoed* or these lesser holidays — *kina* is prohibited. Once the deceased has been buried, none of these practices are permitted at all: *nikbar hames lo meanos velo metapchos* — burial marks the end of all such communal mourning.

### Mourning Types

The Mishnah then defines the terms. *Inuy* — the *meanos* permitted even on *Chol HaMoed* — means *shekulan onot k'achas*, all the women cry out together simultaneously. *Metapchos* — the more emphatic practice that is restricted on *Chol HaMoed* but permitted on *Rosh Chodesh*, *Chanuka*, and *Purim* — involves the physical striking, the slapping of the hands against the thigh, which makes the mourning more emphatic and publicly demonstrative. *Kina* — the responsive dirge that is prohibited on all these occasions — is defined as *she'achas medaberes v'kulan onot achareha*: one woman leads and all the others respond, an antiphonal structure. The verse cited in support: *v'lamdena b'noseichem nehi v'isha re'usa kina* — you shall teach your daughters a lament, and a woman her friend a *kina* — which implies this call-and-response form.

The Mishnah closes with a forward-looking verse: *bila hamaves lanetzach umacha Hashem Elokim dima me'al kol panim* — death shall be swallowed up forever, and God will wipe every tear from every face. This is the verse sometimes recited when leaving a *shiva* house.

### Meanos Kina Words

*Tosafot* comments here that the tractate ends on a positive note, and adds a further observation: *mishum d'Mo'ed Katan hu sof Seder Mo'ed* — because *Moed Katan* is the concluding tractate of *Seder Mo'ed*. This is not entirely self-evident, since *Moed Katan* and *Chagiga* have the same number of chapters and their ordering is not fixed; *Tosafot*'s position is that *Moed Katan* comes last. The point about ending *seder* on a good note has a parallel in *Taanis*, which concludes with the joyful note about *Tu B'Av* and *Yom Kippur* as the greatest festivals of Israel.

The Gemara then asks: what, in fact, is said during *meanos* and *kina*? What are the actual words of these communal wailings? Rav says: *vaye l'azla vaye l'chavila* — woe to the one who is departing, woe to the wounding or, per the reading of the Soncino translation, woe to the security — meaning the life that was given to us as a kind of collateral we must eventually return to God.

### Shnitziv Wailing

Rava then quotes a series of phrases used by the women of *Shnitziv*, a community that was apparently renowned for its mourning poetry. The Gemara presents five examples, each introduced with the formula: *nashei d'Shnitziv amrin* — the women of *Shnitziv* would say.

The first phrase matches what Rav cited: *vaye l'azla vaye l'chavila* — woe to the one going, woe to the collateral, or the loss. The second: *gud garma mikacha v'namtei maya l'antichi* — remove the bone from the jaw, and bring the water to the vessel. Rabbeinu Gershom understands this metaphorically: the bone from the pot represents the soul departing the body, and the water to the vessel is the soul returning to its divine source. The Soncino offers a more prosaic reading: the bone is removed from the cooking pot because this person no longer needs to be fed, and the hot water in the kettle is for the *tahara* process. Rabbeinu Gershom's reading is the more elegant of the two. The third: *atifi kessut tura* — wrap yourselves, O mountains, cover yourselves in mourning, for *d'bar rami u'bar ravrevi hu* — this was a great person, a son of heights; one of your elevated ones has died. The fourth: *shayulei itztala d'milsa l'bar chorin d'shalimu zavdeh* — borrow a cloak of fine linen for a free man, because his portion is now complete; prepare for him his burial shrouds, for he is now freed from the needs of this physical world. The fifth: *rahit v'nafil a'mabra v'ziphusa yazif* — run and fall by the ferry, and borrow the money. This image evokes the idea of crossing over to the other world and requiring some payment at the threshold — an image that recalls, though is not identical to, the ancient Greek notion of payment for passage across the River Styx.

Rava continues with a sixth phrase from the women of *Shnitziv*: *achna tagrei azavzegi mivadku* — our brothers' merchants have been searched at the customs house, a metaphor for the accounting of deeds that takes place upon one's arrival in the world to come, an audit of one's spiritual inventory. And finally: *mota ki mota u'marein chavula* — death is like death, and the suffering is the collateral. Life is given to us on loan; suffering is the interest, and death is the return of the principal.

Returning to an earlier teaching: *tanya*, Rabbi Meir would cite the verse from *Koheles*, *tov lalechet el beit avel* — it is better to go to a house of mourning — and explicate it as follows: if you are present at a person's death or funeral, carry that experience back with you into your own life. *Deyasped yispedunei* — one who eulogizes others will be eulogized; *deyekaver yikverunei* — one who buries others will be buried; *deyit'an yit'anunei* — one who carries the burden of the funeral will be carried; *deyadal yedalunei* — one who raises his voice in mourning will be lifted. Others read the last point differently: *delo yadal yedalunei* — one who does not raise himself up in arrogance will be raised up in honor at his death, as the verse says, *ki tov amar lecha aleh heina* — it is better when others say to you, "Come up here."

## Consoling Mourners

### Yishmael Consoled

The Gemara now moves from the performative aspects of mourning — the wailing, the dirges — to the practice of *nichum aveilim*, consoling mourners. When the children of Rabbi Yishmael died — note again the plural, echoing earlier stories of multiple children dying together — four elders came to console him: Rabbi Tarfon, Rabbi Yose HaGelili, Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah, and Rabbi Akiva. Before they entered, Rabbi Tarfon said to the others: know that this man is a great scholar, deeply knowledgeable in *agadot*. Do not interrupt one another — let each one speak in turn. Rabbi Akiva added: I will go last.

When they entered, Rabbi Yishmael himself opened the conversation, as the mourner: *rabu avonotav tachfu aveilav* — great are my sins, for the mournings have piled upon me, one upon another. And now, he said, he had burdened his teachers by requiring them to make a second condolence visit — once for each child.

### Kal Vachomer Proofs

Rabbi Tarfon responded with a verse from the story of Nadav and Avihu: *ve'achichem kol beit Yisrael yivku et hasreifa* — all the house of Israel shall weep for the burning. A *kal vachomer* follows: Nadav and Avihu performed only one *mitzvah* that the Torah records, yet all of Israel mourned their deaths. *Kach banav shel Rabi Yishmael al achat kama vechama* — how much more so, for your children who performed so many *mitzvot*, all of us will mourn their passing.

Rabbi Yose HaGelili cited the verse about Aviyah son of Jeroboam: *vesafdu lo kol Yisrael vekaveru oto* — all of Israel eulogized and buried him. *Kal vachomer*: Aviyah did only one good thing in his entire life, and nevertheless all of Israel mourned him. What was that one good thing? A dispute: one opinion holds that he abandoned his guard duty and went up to Jerusalem for *Yom Tov*, refusing to enforce his father Jeroboam's prohibition on the pilgrimage. The other opinion says he went further — he dismantled the entire system of guards that Jeroboam had placed on the roads to prevent the *aliyah la'regel*. Either way, only one virtuous act — and still, *kol Yisrael* mourned. How much more so the sons of Rabbi Yishmael.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah cited the verse addressed to Tzidkiyahu: *beshalom tamut* — you shall die in peace — and *ubemisfrot avotecha* — and you will be burned with the pyres of the earlier kings. A *kal vachomer*: Tzidkiyahu performed only one *mitzvah* that the Torah records — he rescued Yirmeyahu from the mud — and yet he received this promise of honor in death and was mourned by all. How much more so the sons of Rabbi Yishmael.

Rabbi Akiva, who had asked to go last, cited the verse from Zechariah: *bayom hahu yigdal hamisped b'Yerushalayim kemisped Hadadrimon bevik'at Megido* — on that day the mourning in Jerusalem will be as great as the mourning of Hadadrimon in the valley of Megido. Rav Yosef interjects that without the Aramaic *Targum*, the reference would be incomprehensible: Hadadrimon had no connection to the valley of Megido. The *Targum* clarifies that the verse is compressing two separate events — the mourning over Achav son of Omri, who was killed by Hadadrimon (hence "the mourning of Hadadrimon," meaning the mourning occasioned by Hadadrimon's act), and separately, the mourning over Yoshiyahu son of Amon, who was killed by Pharaoh Neco in the valley of Megido. Two distinct laments, merged in the verse. Rabbi Akiva's *kal vachomer* then follows: Achav performed only one good deed that the Torah records — *ve'hamelech hayah ma'amad bamerkava nochach Aram* — the king stood in his chariot opposite Aram, apparently maintaining leadership and preventing some collapse at a crucial moment — and nonetheless the mourning for him was enormous. How much more so for the children of Rabbi Yishmael.

What is striking is that Rabbi Akiva's contribution appears structurally identical to those of his colleagues: a citation, a *kal vachomer*, the refrain about the sons of Rabbi Yishmael. One is left wondering why Rabbi Akiva insisted on going last, and what the story means to convey by placing him there without a visibly differentiated teaching.

### Death Verses Resolved

Rava raised a question to Rabba bar Mari: the verse promises Tzidkiyahu *beshalom tamut* — you will die in peace — and yet another verse reports that Tzidkiyahu's eyes were blinded by the king of Babylon. How are these consistent? The answer, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *shemet Nevuchadnetzar beyamav* — Nebuchadnezzar died in Tzidkiyahu's lifetime. That outliving of one's tormentor constitutes a kind of peace.

A parallel question about Yoshiyahu: the verse promises him *vene'asafta el kivrotecha beshalom* — you will be gathered to your grave in peace — and yet another verse reports that the archers shot through King Yoshiyahu, and Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav said that they made him like a sieve. Dying in a hail of arrows — is that dying in peace? The answer, again from Rabbi Yochanan: *shelo charav Beit HaMikdash beyamav* — the Temple was not destroyed in his days. That preservation constitutes his peace.

These resolutions do more than untangle problematic verses. They constitute a direct response to the discussion earlier on the page about reading *simanim* into the manner of a person's death. One might look at Tzidkiyahu's blinding or Yoshiyahu's death by arrows and read catastrophe. But Rabbi Yochanan insists that "dying in peace" can mean something that transcends the physical circumstances — it is a perspective, a larger frame. The rabbis who worried about what Rav Huna's sudden death signified should, perhaps, take note.

### Consoler Etiquette

The Gemara records an important practical ruling from Rabbi Yochanan: *ein menachamin mishum lomar davar ad sheyiphtach avel* — the consolers may not speak until the mourner opens. The reason is implicit but powerful: one does not know what the mourner is ready to hear, or where they need the conversation to begin. Imposing one's own opening risks missing the mourner entirely. The proof text is from *Iyov*: *acharei ken patach Iyov et pihu* — only after that did Iyov open his mouth — *vahadar vayaan Eliphaz HaTeimani* — and then Eliphaz responded. The consolers waited, as difficult as that must have been, until Iyov himself broke the silence.

### Mourner Head Seat

Rabbi Abbahu asks: how do we know that the mourner sits at the head of the table in the *shiva* house? The question is somewhat counterintuitive — one might have assumed that the mourner, in a diminished state, would not be given the seat of prominence. The verse cited: *evchar darkam ve'eshev rosh ve'eshkon kemelech begdud ka'asher avelim yenachem* — I would choose their path and sit at the head, dwelling like a king among troops, as one who consoles mourners. The word *yenachem* here is read as the mourner consoling others — sitting at the head and receiving the community, as it were. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak notes that the *ketiv* can also be read as *yenuchamu* — as one who is consoled — making the connection to the *avel* sitting at the head even more explicit. Mar Zutra derives the same point from a different verse: *vesar marze'ach seruchim*, read as the mourner becoming the prince among the assembled, the center around whom the household orients.

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina then asks the parallel question: how do we know that the *chatan* also sits at the head? The verse: *kechatan yichahen pe'er* — like a bridegroom he shall be adorned with glory. The word *yichahen* evokes the *kohein*. Just as the *kohein* is given primacy — he opens first, blesses first, receives the first and finest portion, as derived from the verse *vekidashto* — so too the *chatan* receives the place of honor.

The parallel between *avelut* and marriage runs deep in the tradition. Both last seven days in their primary phase; both involve ten people for the recitation of *brachot*; both ask whether the central figure — mourner or *chatan* — counts toward the required quorum of ten. And now, both the mourner and the bridegroom sit at the head. The *avel yoshev berosh* and the *chatan yoshev berosh* — the mourner at the head and the groom at the head — emerge from the same structural logic of honored centrality, even though the emotional register of the two occasions could not be more different.

## Soul Departure

### Soul Struggle

Rabbi Chanina teaches: *kasha yetziat neshama min haguf* — the departure of the soul from the body is profoundly difficult. He compares it to pulling a rope through a narrow passage — the friction, the resistance, the effort required. Rabbi Yochanan uses a similar image: like the sail rope through the mast fitting. The imagery of constriction and difficulty underscores that even a death described elsewhere as smooth or peaceful still involves a genuine struggle at the moment of separation.

### Parting Blessings

Rabbi Levi bar Chita teaches a distinction that is observed to this day: *haniftar min hamet* — one who takes leave of the dead — should not say *leich l'shalom*, go toward peace, but rather *leich b'shalom*, go in peace — because the person has already arrived at a state of *shalom*. Conversely, *haniftar min hachai* — one who takes leave of the living — should not say *leich b'shalom* but rather *leich l'shalom*, go toward peace — for the living are always in the state of striving, always moving toward something not yet reached.

The proof for the formula for the living: David said to Avshalom *leich b'shalom* — go in peace — and Avshalom went and was killed. Whereas Yitro said to Moshe *leich l'shalom* — go toward peace — and Moshe went and succeeded. For the dead, the proof is from Abraham: *v'ata tavo el avotecha b'shalom* — you will come to your fathers in peace — which speaks of the state already attained.

### Scholars Ascent

Rabbi Levi offers a final teaching: anyone who moves from the *beit knesset* to the *beit midrash*, and from the *beit midrash* to the *beit knesset*, will *zoche umekabel pnei shechina* — merit to receive the presence of the *Shechina*. There is a deliberate ambiguity here: does *zoche umekabel* refer to a reward received in death, or to a presence experienced now, within the walls of the *beit midrash* and *beit knesset* themselves? The verse cited — *yelchu mechayil el chayil yeira'eh el elohim beTzion* — suggests an ongoing process: going from strength to strength, always ascending.

This leads to the *masechta*'s final statement. Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi in the name of Rav: *talmidei chachamim ein lahem menucha* — the *talmidei chachamim* have no rest. Not in this world, and not even in the world to come. *Afilu olam haba* — even there — *yelchu mechayil el chayil yeira'eh el elohim beTzion*. The scholars continue to ascend, to grow, to strive. Even the vision of God in the future does not constitute a terminus but a milestone along a path that continues without end.

With this, the *masechta* closes: *hadran alach eilu megalchin uslika la masechet Moed Katan*.
