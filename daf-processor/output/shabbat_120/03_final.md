# Shabbat 120 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gerama & Fire

### Rabbi Yossi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן נַנָּס אוֹמֵר: פּוֹרְסִין עוֹר שֶׁל גְּדִי עַל גַּבֵּי שִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל שֶׁאָחַז בָּהֶן אֶת הָאוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מְחָרֵךְ. וְעוֹשִׂין מְחִיצָה בְּכׇל הַכֵּלִים, בֵּין מְלֵאִין בֵּין רֵיקָנִים, בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁלֹּא תַּעֲבוֹר הַדְּלֵיקָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹסֵר בִּכְלֵי חֶרֶס חֲדָשִׁים מְלֵאִים מַיִם, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין יְכוֹלִין לְקַבֵּל אֶת הָאוּר וְהֵן מִתְבַּקְּעִין וּמְכַבִּין אֶת הַדְּלֵיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Shimon ben Nannas says: One may spread out a** moist **goat's hide over a box, a chest, or a closet that caught fire, because** the fire **singes** and does not burn it. The fire does not burn the wet goat's hide but merely singes it, and by doing so the wooden vessels are preserved. **And one may establish a barrier** against the fire **with all vessels, both full and empty, so that the fire will not pass. Rabbi Yosei prohibits** using **new earthenware vessels full of water, because they cannot withstand** the heat of **the fire and they** will **burst and extinguish the fire,** and it is prohibited to cause the fire to be extinguished on Shabbat even indirectly.

The present daf picks up in the middle of 120b, ten lines before the lines widen, at the words *osin mechitza*. Having concluded the previous *mishnah*, the Gemara turns to another *mishnah* that addresses issues central to *hilkhot Shabbat* — in particular, the question of saving property from a fire and the concern that rescue efforts might lead to outright prohibited labor.

The *mishnah* records a dispute regarding placing pottery vessels filled with water around a fire. The fear is that the heat will cause the vessels to burst, releasing the water and thereby extinguishing the fire. Rabbi Yossi prohibits this; the *chakhamim* permit it. The Gemara seizes on this dispute as a window into the broader question of *gerama* — indirect causation — on Shabbat.

### Gerama Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי גְּרַם כִּבּוּי מוּתָּר, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר גְּרַם כִּבּוּי אָסוּר? וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Is that to say that the Rabbis hold** that **indirect extinguishing is permitted** on Shabbat, **and Rabbi Yosei holds** that **indirect extinguishing is prohibited?** **Didn't we hear them** state **the opposite?**

*Lemeima*, says the Gemara: *d'rabbanan savri geram kibuie mutar* — the *chakhamim* hold that causing a fire to extinguish itself indirectly is permitted. This formulation is striking, for it sounds like an outright, unconditional permission — some *rishonim* indeed hold that *gerama* is permitted even without any special justification, though at minimum the Gemara implies it is permitted in the context of a fire. *Ve'Rabbi Yossi savar geram kibuie assur* — Rabbi Yosei holds that indirect causation of extinguishment is forbidden.

### Baraita Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: עוֹשִׂין מְחִיצָה בְּכֵלִים רֵיקָנִין, וּבִמְלֵאִין שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּר. וְאֵלּוּ מְלֵאִין שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּר — כְּלֵי מַתָּכוֹת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף כְּלֵי כְּפַר שִׁיחִין וּכְלֵי כְּפַר חֲנַנְיָה אֵין דַּרְכָּן לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **One may establish a barrier with empty vessels, and with full ones that do not typically break. And these are full** vessels **that do not typically break; metal vessels. Rabbi Yosei says: Even** earthenware **vessels** from **the village of Shiḥin and vessels from the village of Ḥananya do not typically break.**

Yet *ve'ipcha shemanan lehu* — we have apparently heard that they hold the reverse positions. A *baraita* teaches: *osin mechitza b'kelim reikim u'mle'im she'ein darkan l'hishtaber* — one may make a wall of empty vessels or full ones that will not normally break. This implies that the *tanna* of this *baraita* agrees with Rabbi Yosei that full vessels which would break and extinguish the fire are forbidden, and Rabbi Yosei himself then adds that even the pottery of Kfar Shichin and Kfar Chananya does not normally break — a more lenient position than the *chakhamim*. The positions appear inverted relative to the *mishnah*.

One resolution proposed is to reverse the attributions in the *mishnah* — the *chakhamim* would then be the stringent party and Rabbi Yosei the lenient one, and his statement in the *baraita* about the hard pottery would be understood as arguing against the *chakhamim* on their own terms. The Gemara, however, rejects this: *Rabba bar Tachlifa* says in the name of Rav that the *tanna* who holds *geram kibuie assur* is Rabbi Yosei — this is an established tradition that fixes Rabbi Yosei as the stringent position and cannot be reversed. Instead, the Gemara resolves the *baraita* by reading it as entirely Rabbi Yosei's view — *v'chisorei mechasra v'hachi kattani* — the *baraita* is incomplete and must be read as follows: one may make a wall of empty or full vessels that do not normally break; and vessels from Kfar Shichin and Kfar Chananya likewise do not normally break — because Rabbi Yosei says those vessels do not normally break. The entire *baraita* is Rabbi Yosei's, and the apparent role reversal dissolves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: מַאן תָּנָא גְּרַם כִּבּוּי אָסוּר — רַבִּי יוֹסֵי! אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, וּבָרָיְיתָא כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא. וְחַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: עוֹשִׂין מְחִיצָה בְּכֵלִים רֵיקָנִין וּבִמְלֵאִים שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּר. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן כֵּלִים שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּר: כְּלֵי מַתָּכוֹת. וּכְלֵי כְּפַר שִׁיחִין וּכְלֵי כְּפַר חֲנַנְיָה נָמֵי אֵין דַּרְכָּן לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּר, שֶׁרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף כְּלִי כְּפַר שִׁיחִין וּכְלֵי כְּפַר חֲנַנְיָה אֵין דַּרְכָּן לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Didn't Rabba bar Taḥalifa say in the name of Rav: Who is** the *tanna* who **teaches** that **indirect extinguishing is prohibited?** It is **Rabbi Yosei. Rather, actually, do not reverse** the attribution of the opinions in the mishna, **and the entire *baraita* is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, and it is incomplete and is teaching the following: One may establish a barrier with empty vessels and full ones that do not typically break. And these are vessels that do not typically break: Metal vessels; and vessels** from **the village of Shiḥin and vessels** from **the village of Ḥananya also do not typically break, as Rabbi Yosei says: Even vessels** from **the village of Shiḥin and vessels** from **the village of Ḥananya do not typically break.**

### God's Name Gerama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה שֵׁם כָּתוּב לוֹ עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ — הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִרְחוֹץ וְלֹא יָסוּךְ וְלֹא יַעֲמוֹד בִּמְקוֹם הַטִּינּוֹפֶת. נִזְדַּמְּנָה לוֹ טְבִילָה שֶׁל מִצְוָה — כּוֹרֵךְ עָלֶיהָ גֶּמִי, וְיוֹרֵד וְטוֹבֵל. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם יוֹרֵד וְטוֹבֵל כְּדַרְכּוֹ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְשַׁפְשֵׁף!
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **If** one **had a** sacred **name** of God **written on his skin he may neither wash** it in water lest it be erased, **nor may he smear** it with oil, **nor may he stand in a place of filth** because it is disrespectful of God's name. **If an immersion of mitzva happened to** present itself, **he wraps a reed over** God's name **and** then **descends and immerses. Rabbi Yosei says: Actually, he descends and immerses in his** usual **manner,** even if it is not an immersion of mitzva, **provided that he does not rub** the spot and erase the name.

The Gemara now presents a genuine internal contradiction: *verami Rabbi Yossi ad Rabbi Yossi* — Rabbi Yosei contradicts himself. A *baraita* teaches that if one has written God's name in ink on one's arm, one may not bathe, anoint, or stand in a foul-smelling place, lest one erase it — for *mechikat Hashem*, erasing God's name, is a *de'oraita* prohibition. Yet if immersion in a *mikveh* for the sake of a *mitzvah* becomes necessary, the *chakhamim* say to wrap a band of reed around the arm and immerse; Rabbi Yosei says simply immerse in the normal manner, provided one does not scrub the arm. Immersing with God's name on one's arm will, naturally, cause the water to wash it away — an act of *gerama* in erasing God's name. Here Rabbi Yosei is not troubled by *gerama*, whereas in the fire case he is.

### Biblical vs Rabbanic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִבַּדְתֶּם אֶת שְׁמָם מִן הַמָּקוֹם הַהוּא לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן כֵּן לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״ — עֲשִׂיָּיה הוּא דְּאָסוּר, גְּרָמָא שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is different there** because **the verse says:** "And you shall break down their altars, and you shall smash their pillars, and their sacred trees, and burn with fire, and the graven images of their gods you shall hew down **and you shall destroy their name from that place. You shall not do so to the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 12:3–4). From the prohibition: "You shall not do so," Rabbi Yosei derives that actually **doing so is that which is prohibited; indirectly causing** that result **is permitted.**

The Gemara proposes that perhaps the prohibition of *mechikat Hashem* is derived from the verse *lo taasun ken l'Hashem Elokeichem* — "do not do so to Hashem your God" — which forbids only direct action: *assiya hu d'assur, grama shari*. The Gemara then asks: but the same logic applies to Shabbat, where the verse reads *lo ta'aseh kol melacha* — "do not do any labor" — equally excluding indirect causation. This framing makes clear that *gerama* was never a *de'oraita* concern on Shabbat; the entire question is at the *de'rabbanan* level.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הָכָא נָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה [כׇל] מְלָאכָה״ — עֲשִׂיָּיה הוּא דְּאָסוּר, גְּרָמָא שְׁרֵי! מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאָדָם בָּהוּל עַל מָמוֹנוֹ, אִי שָׁרֵית לֵיהּ — אָתֵי לְכַבּוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, here too,** with regard to Shabbat, **it is written:** "And the seventh day is Shabbat for the Lord your God, **you shall not perform any labor"** (Exodus 20:9). And here, too, one could derive: **Performance is that which is prohibited; indirectly causing** a prohibited action **is permitted.** The Gemara answers: Actually, Rabbi Yosei maintains there is no prohibition in indirectly causing a fire to be extinguished; however, **since a person is agitated about his property, if you permit him** to indirectly extinguish the fire, **he will come to extinguish** it directly.

### Anxiety & Fire

The Gemara's resolution now becomes a *de'rabbanan* distinction. By the prohibition of erasing God's name, *gerama* is simply permitted — the verse excludes it from the prohibition entirely. By Shabbat, there is likewise no biblical concern, but one might still have expected a *rabbinic* prohibition. The Gemara proceeds to explain why Rabbi Yosei differs on Shabbat specifically.

The Gemara's answer is: *mitoch she'adam bahul al mamono* — because a person is agitated over his property. Rabbi Yosei in principle has no conceptual objection to *gerama*; it is permitted even on Shabbat. But in the specific context of a fire, if one is allowed to engage in the elaborate arrangement of water-filled vessels, one's anxiety over the loss of possessions will lead one to go further and directly extinguish the fire. This is the same delicate balance that runs through the entire chapter: if too much is prohibited, people will simply violate Shabbat outright; if too much is permitted, anxious homeowners will overstep in their rescue efforts. *Grama kibuie*, therefore, is permitted in principle, but Rabbi Yosei forbids it by fire due to this special *de'rabbanan* concern — a concern that does not apply in the case of God's name on one's arm.

This prompts a further question: the *chakhamim* who argue on Rabbi Yosei permit the water-filled vessels by fire. But then why do they require covering God's name before *tevilah*? If a person is agitated enough during a fire that we fear *gerama* will lead to direct extinguishment, should we not *a fortiori* be lenient in the case of the *mikveh*, where no such anxiety is present? And if *gerama* is permissible, why does anyone require the covering at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא הַאי גֶּמִי הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּמִיהַדַּק — קָא הָוֵי חֲצִיצָה, אִי לָא מִיהַדַּק — עָיְילִי בֵּיהּ מַיָּא! חֲצִיצָה, תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּיוֹ! בְּלַחָה. דְּתַנְיָא: הַדָּם וְהַדְּיוֹ הַדְּבַשׁ וְהֶחָלָב, יְבֵשִׁין — חוֹצְצִין, לַחִים — אֵין חוֹצְצִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **And** how can **you understand** it that way? **What are the circumstances** of **this reed** with which God's name is covered while immersing according to the Rabbis? **If** it is **firmly attached, it is an interposition; if it is not firmly attached, the water enters** and erases the name. The Gemara questions this: If the concern is with regard to an **interposition, derive** that **it** is an interposition in any case **due to** the **ink** on his skin. The Gemara answers: This is referring to **moist** ink that does not interpose, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **blood, and ink,** and **honey, and milk** on a person's skin, when they are **dry,** they **interpose** during immersion; when they are **moist,** they **do not interpose.**

At this point the Gemara reconsiders the entire framework. *Ve'dilma*, it asks — does the reed band (*gemi*) even accomplish anything? If it is tight on the arm, it constitutes a *chatzitzah* and invalidates the *tevilah*; if it is loose, the water penetrates anyway and provides no protection for the ink. And one might further think the ink itself is a *chatzitzah* — but a *baraita* resolves this: blood, ink, honey, and milk are *chatzitzah* only when dry; when moist, they are water-permeable and are not a *chatzitzah*. The *gemi*, however, remains problematic.

The Gemara therefore abandons the *gerama* framework for this case entirely, and Rava bar Rav Shila provides the true explanation: *d'kasvari assur la'amod bifnei Hashem arom* — the *chakhamim* hold that it is forbidden to stand naked before God's name. The covering is not about preventing erasure of God's name; it is about not exposing God's name while unclothed. This explains why a loose cover suffices — it need not keep water out, only conceal the name.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא, הַיְינוּ טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן, דְּקָסָבְרִי: אָסוּר לַעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי הַשֵּׁם עָרוֹם. מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר מוּתָּר לַעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי הַשֵּׁם עָרוֹם? דְּמַנַּח יְדֵיהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava bar Rav Sheila said** that **this is the reasoning of the Rabbis: They hold** that **it is prohibited** for a person **to stand before the name** of God while **naked,** and therefore there must be a barrier between one's naked body and the name. The Gemara asks: Is that to say **by inference** that **Rabbi Yosei holds** that **it is permitted to stand before the name** while **naked?** The Gemara answers: No, he does not allow doing so; however, he does not require that the name be covered with a reed. It is sufficient for **one to place his hand over** the name.

### Tevilah Ba-Zmanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְבִילָה בִּזְמַנָּהּ לָאו מִצְוָה וּמַהְדְּרִינַן, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר טְבִילָה בִּזְמַנָּהּ מִצְוָה וְלָא מַהְדְּרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** that performing **immersion at its** designated **time is not a mitzva, and we seek** a reed to wrap around God's name even if it means postponing immersion to the next day, **and Rabbi Yosei holds** that **immersion at its** designated **time is a mitzva, and** therefore **we do not seek** a reed, since immersion cannot be postponed.

The *chakhamim* require one to seek out such a covering, even at some inconvenience, while Rabbi Yosei holds one may simply place one's hand over the arm. The Gemara asks: might one's hand slip? And the response is that the same concern applies symmetrically. The Gemara concludes that the ideal solution — when a loose cover is readily available — is accepted by all. The dispute reduces to whether one must delay the *tevilah* to go searching for a cover when none is at hand.

This brings us to the question of *tevila bizmana*: is there a *mitzvah* to perform one's immersion precisely at its appointed time, or may one delay? The *chakhamim* hold *tevila bizmana lav mitzvah* — there is no obligation to immerse at the exact time. Therefore, if one must search for something to cover God's name, the *tevilah* may simply wait. Rabbi Yosei holds *tevila bizmana mitzvah* — immersion at its designated time is a *mitzvah* — and therefore one should not be compelled to postpone it in order to search for a cover. Placing one's hand over the arm is sufficient.

A *baraita* is then cited concerning *Yom Kippur*, which categorizes various categories of those who require *tevilah* — *zav*, *zavah*, *metzora*, *bo'el niddah*, *tamei meit* — and specifies whether their immersion falls by day or by night. It concludes that a *ba'al keri* may immerse at any time during the day. Rabbi Yosei says that from *Mincha* onward he need not immerse. This might suggest Rabbi Yosei holds *tevila bizmana lav mitzvah* — why immerse on *Yom Kippur* if one can simply wait until nightfall? The Gemara, however, identifies this Rabbi Yosei as Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda, not the *stam* Rabbi Yosei of our *mishnah*. Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda explicitly holds *tevila bizmana lav mitzvah* — as evidenced by his ruling that a woman in complex cases of uncertain status need only immerse once at the end of the entire uncertain period rather than at every possible time. The *stam* Rabbi Yosei of our *mishnah*, by contrast, holds *tevila bizmana mitzvah*.

The upshot of the entire sugya is that *gerama* is permitted — certainly at the *de'oraita* level, and the language of the Gemara suggests it may be entirely permitted even *de'rabbanan*. The *chakhamim* permit it by fire outright; Rabbi Yosei agrees that *gerama* is in principle permitted but introduces a special *de'rabbanan* concern specific to fires, where agitation over property loss may lead to direct violation. By the *mikveh* scenario, the covering requirement has nothing to do with *gerama* at all, but rests on the separate concern of standing unclothed before God's name. The practical ruling is that *gerama* is at least *assur de'rabbanan* on Shabbat — and this principle underlies many of the leniencies developed by responsa authorities for indirect activation of electrical devices on Shabbat, much of which rests on the *gerama* foundation.

## Amirah L'Goy & Katan

### Non-Jew Fire Help

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ גּוֹי שֶׁבָּא לְכַבּוֹת — אֵין אוֹמְרִים לוֹ ״כַּבֵּה״ וְ״אַל תְּכַבֶּה״, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין שְׁבִיתָתוֹ עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a gentile comes to extinguish** a Jew's fire on Shabbat, **one may not say to him: Extinguish, and: Do not extinguish, because** responsibility for **his rest is not** incumbent **upon** the Jew.

The *mishnah* teaches: *nochri sheba lekhabot* — if a non-Jew comes of his own accord to extinguish a fire, one may not say to him "extinguish it," for that constitutes *amirah l'akum* — enlisting a non-Jew to perform *melacha* on one's behalf. Yet one need not say to him "do not extinguish it" either. *Mipnei she'ein shvitato aleihen* — because his resting is not incumbent upon you. You have no obligation to ensure that a non-Jew refrains from *melacha* on Shabbat. One may not actively engage him, but one need not stop him either.

One might ask: since extinguishing a fire without the purpose of producing charcoal is only a *de'rabbanan* prohibition — it is *melacha she'einah tzrikhah le-gufah* in the view of Rabbi Shimon — why is *amirah l'akum* prohibited even here? *Tosafot* addresses this directly: *ein omrim lo kabbe afilu bidvar de'rabbanan amirah l'nochri shevut* — one may not instruct a non-Jew even for a *de'rabbanan* prohibition. *Amirah l'akum* itself is a *de'rabbanan* prohibition, but it applies regardless of whether the underlying act is biblical or rabbinic.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: בִּדְלֵיקָה הִתִּירוּ לוֹמַר ״כׇּל הַמְכַבֶּה אֵינוֹ מַפְסִיד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said:** During **a fire, the** Sages **permitted to say** in the presence of gentiles: **Anyone who extinguishes** the fire **will not lose.**

The Gemara then cites Rabbi Ami: in the case of a fire, the rabbis permitted one to make a general public announcement — to no one in particular — that whoever chooses to come and extinguish the fire will not lose out, and after Shabbat one may pay them for their services. This is not a direct instruction to a specific non-Jew to extinguish the fire; it is a general declaration addressed to whoever happens to hear it, and the non-Jew acts entirely of his own volition. The question of whether one may go further — instructing a non-Jew to engage in *grama kibuie* — is foreclosed by *Tosafot*'s ruling that even *de'rabbanan* acts are off-limits for *amirah l'akum*.

The Gemara attempts to derive support for the permissibility of the general announcement from the *mishnah* itself: if the *mishnah* says "do not tell him to extinguish," this implies that telling him in the general, indirect fashion — "whoever extinguishes will not lose out" — would be permitted. But the Gemara rejects this inference, noting that the *mishnah*'s parallel clause — "do not tell him not to extinguish" — could equally be read to mean that any statement more directive than this silence is forbidden, including the general announcement. *Ella mihacha leka l'mishmana mineh* — one cannot prove either way from the *mishnah*. Nevertheless, *Tosafot* rules that the general announcement is permitted, and this is the practical ruling.

### Yosef ben Simai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה וְנָפְלָה דְּלֵיקָה בַּחֲצֵירוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף בֶּן סִימַאי בְּשִׁיחִין, וּבָאוּ אַנְשֵׁי גִּיסְטְרָא שֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִי לְכַבּוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאַפִּטְרוֹפּוֹס שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ הָיָה, וְלֹא הִנִּיחָן מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד הַשַּׁבָּת, וְנַעֲשָׂה לוֹ נֵס וְיָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים וְכִיבּוּ. לָעֶרֶב שִׁיגֵּר לְכׇל אֶחָד מֵהֶן שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִין, וְלָאִפַּרְכוֹס שֶׁבָּהֶן — חֲמִשִּׁים. וּכְשֶׁשָּׁמְעוּ חֲכָמִים בְּדָבָר, אָמְרוּ: לֹא הָיָה צָרִיךְ לְכָךְ, שֶׁהֲרֵי שָׁנִינוּ: גּוֹי שֶׁבָּא לְכַבּוֹת — אֵין אוֹמְרִים לוֹ ״כַּבֵּה״ וְ״אַל תְּכַבֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **There was an incident that a fire ignited** on Shabbat **in the courtyard of Yosef ben Simai in** a place called **Shiḥin. And men came from the fortress [*gistera*] of Tzippori to extinguish** the fire, **because he was a steward [*apotropos*] of the king** and they wanted to help him. **However,** Yosef ben Simai **would not allow them** to extinguish the fire **in deference to Shabbat; and a miracle transpired for him and rain fell and extinguished** the fire. **That evening** after Shabbat **he sent two *sela* to each one of** the soldiers who came to his aid, **and fifty to their commander [*iparkhos*]. And when the Sages heard about this, they said: He need not** have prevented them from extinguishing the fire, **as we learned** in the mishna: If **a gentile comes to extinguish** a Jew's fire on Shabbat, **one may not say to him: Extinguish, and: Do not extinguish, because** responsibility for **his rest is not** incumbent **upon** the Jew; rather, the gentile may do as he pleases.

A *baraita* recounts: a fire broke out in the courtyard of Yosef ben Simai in Shichin, and the soldiers of the garrison of Tzippori came to extinguish it — apparently from a neighboring village, drawn by the news, perhaps because Yosef ben Simai held a position of authority as an officer of the king. Yosef ben Simai refused to allow them to extinguish the fire, *mipnei kvod Shabbat* — out of concern for the honor of Shabbat. A miracle occurred: rain fell and extinguished the fire. That evening he sent each of the soldiers two *sela'im* and fifty *sela'im* to their commander, rewarding their goodwill even though he had turned them away.

When the rabbis heard of this, they said: he need not have done that. The *mishnah* already teaches that one need not stop a non-Jew from extinguishing a fire on his own initiative. The narrative carries a dual message: it celebrates the extraordinary piety of Yosef ben Simai, while making unmistakably clear that ordinary people should not attempt to replicate it. Miracles are not to be assumed, and the halakha provides an adequate path that does not require turning away help. The concern Yosef ben Simai expressed was perhaps not about a specific halakhic violation — he was not technically violating anything by allowing them to proceed — but about the experiential dimension of Shabbat: having an active firefighting effort underway undermines the spirit of the day, particularly for a public figure whose conduct represented *kevod Shabbat* before the surrounding population.

### Minor Agency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל קָטָן שֶׁבָּא לְכַבּוֹת אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשְּׁבִיתָתוֹ עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** if **a** Jewish **child comes to extinguish** a fire on Shabbat, **they do not listen to him** and allow him to extinguish it, even though he is not yet obligated in mitzva observance, **because** responsibility for **his rest is** incumbent **upon** the Jew.

The *mishnah* continues: *aval katan sheba lekhabot ein shomin lo* — but if a minor comes to extinguish the fire, one may not allow him to proceed, *mipnei sheshvitato aleihem* — because his resting is your obligation. Unlike the non-Jew, whose Shabbat rest is not the Jew's responsibility, the minor's Shabbat observance is. The Gemara then asks whether this *mishnah* implies a general principle — that one is obligated to prevent a minor from performing any biblical prohibition (*katan ochel nevelot beit din metzuvin alav lehafrisho*). Rabbi Yochanan answers: no. The *mishnah* is speaking specifically of a case where the minor acts *al daat aviv* — for his father's sake, to benefit his father.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּקָטָן הָעוֹשֶׂה לְדַעַת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This mishna is referring to **a child who is acting with** the intention of fulfilling **his father's will.**

### Acting for Parent

When a minor acts on his own — for example, eating *treifa* food for himself — it is not one's obligation to interfere, at least where the child is below the age of *chinuch* or is not one's own child. *Tosafot* notes that if the minor has reached the age of *chinuch* or is one's own child, the parent does bear an obligation to stop him. But the case of the *mishnah* is different: the minor is extinguishing the fire to benefit his father. In that case he is not acting for himself; he is an extension of the father. Allowing him to proceed is therefore analogous to *amirah l'goy* — it is as if the father had effectively enlisted him, even without explicit instruction.

### Non-Jew vs Minor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי גּוֹי, דְּקָא עָבֵיד לְדַעְתֵּיהּ דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל — מִי שְׁרֵי?! גּוֹי לְדַעְתֵּיהּ דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ עָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** If so, the case **with regard to a gentile** in the mishna must be interpreted in a **similar** manner as referring to a case where **he is acting with** the intention to fulfill the **will** of **a Jew. Is** that **permitted?** It is prohibited to derive benefit from an action performed by a gentile for a Jew on Shabbat. The Gemara responds: This is not the case; **the gentile** is **acting of his own volition.** Because he is paid for extinguishing the fire he is not doing so in order to help the Jew.

This analysis yields a striking halakhic observation. The *mishnah* appeared to ground the prohibition of allowing one's minor to extinguish the fire in the biblical verse — *atah u'vincha u'vitecha* — which explicitly includes one's children among those who must rest on Shabbat. But the Gemara reframes the entire discussion: the question becomes one of general obligations to prevent minors from performing *issurim*, compounded by the specific concern that when a minor acts for a parent he becomes an extension of that parent, akin to *amirah l'akum*. The affirmative biblical obligation to ensure that one's children rest — implied by the *mishnah*'s language — is effectively subsumed into these generic categories. Some *rishonim* and *poskim* do revisit this independent biblical obligation of *shevisat katanim*, but it is a striking feature of the Gemara's analysis that this dimension nearly disappears from view.

## Vessels & Protection

### Covering Candles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כּוֹפִין קְעָרָה עַל גַּבֵּי הַנֵּר בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁלֹּא תֶּאֱחוֹז בַּקּוֹרָה, וְעַל צוֹאָה שֶׁל קָטָן, וְעַל עַקְרָב שֶׁלֹּא תִּישָּׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may overturn a bowl on top of a lamp so that** fire **will not take hold in the** ceiling **beam** on Shabbat. **And** similarly, one may overturn a bowl **on top of a child's feces** inside the house so he will not touch it and dirty himself, **and on top of a scorpion so that it will not bite.**

The next *mishnah* states: *kofin k'ara al gabei ner beshvil shelo te'echoz bekora* — one may overturn a vessel over a burning candle so that the beam above does not catch fire. This is not an act of extinguishing; it is a protective measure. Similarly, one may cover the excrement of a minor and a scorpion so that one does not step on it and is not bitten.

### Minor Excrement

The *mishnah*'s mention of covering the excrement of a minor prompts discussion. The *mishnah*'s apparent *chiddush* is that one may move a vessel to cover something that might otherwise be *muktzah*. But the Gemara challenges the *chiddush*: *hi atzmah mukhenet le-klavim* — the excrement of a minor is not *muktzah* to begin with, since it has some use. Perhaps the *chiddush* is that it constitutes *nolad* — something new that came into being on Shabbat? A *baraita* teaches that flowing rivers and springs are not treated as *nolad* because their flow is continuous and expected. Similarly, the excrement of a minor is expected and therefore not *nolad*.

The broader context of this discussion arose during a visit of several rabbis — Rav Yehuda, Rav Yirmiya bar Abba, and Rav Chanan bar Rava — to the home of Avin of Neshikaya. The host provided benches for Rav Yehuda and Rav Yirmiya bar Abba but not for Rav Chanan bar Rava, who sat on the floor. Feeling slighted, Rav Chanan bar Rava, overhearing the host teaching his son the version of this teaching about covering the excrement of a minor, declared that the man was teaching his son nonsense — pointing out that there was no *chiddush* since the excrement was not *muktzah*. The host asked how he should teach the passage. The resolution: the teaching should read *al tzo'ah she-l tarnegolim mipnei katan* — covering the droppings of chickens so that the minor does not dirty himself. Bird droppings, unlike the excrement of a minor, are not *ra'uy le-klavim* and would be *muktzah*, and the *chiddush* is therefore that one may cover them to protect the child.

### Bird Droppings

Even so, the Gemara challenges: the bird droppings in the yard could simply be removed as *graf shel re'i* — the rule that permits removing repulsive waste from one's domain. The Gemara responds that *graf shel re'i* allows one to move the waste contained *within* a vessel, not to pick up the waste directly. But another source shows that even repulsive waste may be moved directly — the case of a rat found in Rav Ashi's perfumes, where Rav Ashi instructed that it be taken by its tail and removed. The Gemara concludes that the case of the *mishnah* concerns waste in the garbage area of one's yard — a location where the rules permitting removal of *graf shel re'i* do not apply — and therefore covering it is the only available option.

### Scorpion Trapping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לִפְנֵי רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי בַּעֲרָב, וְאָמַר: חוֹשְׁשַׁנִי לוֹ מֵחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: An incident came before Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai in** his village of **Arav,** where a person covered a scorpion on Shabbat, **and** Rabban Yoḥanan **said: I am concerned** that **he** is liable to bring **a sin-offering** because he might have violated a Torah prohibition.

The *mishnah*'s permission to cover a scorpion relates to the laws of trapping on Shabbat. Trapping the scorpion constitutes *melacha she'einah tzrikhah le-gufa* — labor performed not for the sake of the usual objective of that labor (one does not want the scorpion for any productive purpose) — and is therefore only a *de'rabbanan* prohibition in the view of Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Yehuda records that a case involving trapping a scorpion in the town of Arav came before Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, who expressed concern that the person might be liable to bring a *chatat* — indicating that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai may have held like Rabbi Yehuda that *melacha she'einah tzrikhah le-gufa* is a full biblical violation.

## Killing Dangerous Animals

### Dangerous Animals Kill

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches: *kol ha-mazikin neheragin be-Shabbat* — all dangerous animals that cause injury to people may be killed on Shabbat. *Rashi* specifies that this refers to those that are potentially fatal. Crucially, this permission extends even to proactive killing — if one encounters a poisonous snake or scorpion that is not actively attacking, one may still kill it, since this protects others from danger. This constitutes *melacha she'einah tzrikhah le-gufa*; one is not killing for the usual productive purpose of the labor but purely as a protective measure.

### Five Animals

Rav Yosef challenges this from a *baraita*: *chamisha neheragin be-Shabbat* — there are specifically five animals that may be killed on Shabbat: the fly of Egypt, the wasp of Ninveh, the scorpion of Chadyav, the snake of Eretz Yisrael, and a rabid dog anywhere. These five are either particularly lethal or particularly prone to attacking people. But the *baraita* limits the permission to these five — not all dangerous animals — which contradicts Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's general ruling.

The Gemara asks: whose position is the *baraita*? If it represents Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *melacha she'einah tzrikhah le-gufa chayav* — that such labor is a full biblical violation — then even these five could only be killed if they pose an immediate threat of *pikuach nefesh*. If it represents Rabbi Shimon, who permits *melacha she'einah tzrikhah le-gufa*, then the list of five represents an implicit limitation: only these, not all dangerous animals. Either way, it poses a difficulty for Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's sweeping ruling.

Rabbi Yirmiya dismisses the challenge: *dilma meshabeshta hi* — perhaps the *baraita* is a corrupt text. The Gemara does acknowledge that only *baraitot* edited by Rabbi Chiya and Rabbi Oshaya — essentially the *Tosefta* — carry full textual authority; other *baraitot* may contain errors.

### Piety vs Pragmatism

Rav Yosef, however, objects to this dismissal: *ana matniti lah* — that is my *baraita*, a tradition I myself preserve, and it should not be discarded simply because it is inconvenient. Yet he also resolves the difficulty himself: *be'rutzo batreihu de'kulei alma la peligi* — the *baraita* is discussing the case where these animals are actually pursuing and threatening a person. In that case, all authorities agree they may be killed, for it is *pikuach nefesh*. The *baraita* is therefore not about proactive killing at all; it is about reactive self-defense. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling, by contrast, permits proactive killing according to Rabbi Shimon for all genuinely dangerous animals, not only these five.

A *tanna* teaches before Rava bar Rav Huna: *ha'horeg nechashim ve'akravim be'Shabbat ein ruach chasidim nocha heimenu* — one who goes out of his way to kill snakes and scorpions on Shabbat, the spirit of the pious does not rest easily with him. Even if such killing is only a *de'rabbanan* prohibition under Rabbi Shimon, it is unseemly to go about actively hunting these animals. Rava bar Rav Huna responds sharply: *ve'otan chasidim ein ruach chachamim nocha mehem* — those so-called pious people do not find favor with the sages. The sages take a broader view of public welfare, and one should not be excessively stringent at the expense of communal safety.

### Divine Providence

A *baraita* offers a striking theological framing: *nizdamnu lo nechashim ve'akravim* — if snakes and scorpions happen to cross one's path on Shabbat and one kills them, *be'yaduah shenizdamnu lo lehorgan* — it is evident that heaven arranged for them to cross one's path so that one would have the merit of eliminating them. If one did not kill them, *be'yaduah shenizdamnu lehorgo* — they were sent to kill, and a miracle from heaven intervened to spare the person. This framing functions as a theological endorsement of proactive killing: encountering such creatures is itself providential, and acting to eliminate them is meritorious. Ulla (or in some versions, Rabbah bar bar Chana) in the name of Rabbi Yochanan qualifies this: the providential inference — that they were sent to kill — applies only when the animals were actually hissing aggressively at the person, not when they were simply encountered incidentally.

### Incidental vs Proactive

A final story is cited: Rabbah bar bar Chana reports that once a scorpion or snake appeared in the study hall and a certain person stood up and killed it. Rabbi said of him: *paga bo ke-yotzei bo* — "something just like it encountered him." The *baraita* leaves ambiguous whether this was a compliment — the creature was attacking, and he courageously dispatched it — or an insult — he himself behaved like a dangerous creature in going out of his way to kill it.

The Gemara concludes with a practical clarification from the incident involving Rabbi Abba son of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba and Rav Zevid, who were sitting on the porch of Rabbi Yanai when a snake appeared from between them. They asked Rabbi Yanai whether one may kill snakes and scorpions on Shabbat. He replied: I even kill wasps — snakes and scorpions, *a fortiori*. The Gemara qualifies this ruling: *dilma le-fi tumo* — perhaps this applies only incidentally. That is, the permission may extend to stepping on or passing over a creature in the course of one's normal movement, but not to going out of one's way specifically to chase it down and kill it. Within the framework of Rabbi Shimon — who treats proactive killing as only a *de'rabbanan* violation of *melacha she'einah tzrikhah le-gufa* — there remains a spectrum of permissibility: some hold it is entirely encouraged, others discourage it, and still others permit it only when done incidentally rather than through deliberate pursuit.
