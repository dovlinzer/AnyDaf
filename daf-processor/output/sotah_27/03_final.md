# Sotah 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zera & Bracketing

### Zera Theme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אִשָּׁה מְזוּנֶּנֶת כְּתוּבָתָהּ קַיֶּמֶת, בֵּין שֶׁהָיְתָה אַלְמָנָה בֵּין שֶׁהָיְתָה גְּרוּשָׁה, אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁהִשְׁמִרָה עַל עַצְמָהּ בִּנְשׁוּאִין, אִם אֵין עֵדִים, תִּשָּׁמַר עַל עַצְמָהּ בִּנְשׁוּאִין, וְתִתְבָּעֵל, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁכָּל נִשְׁמַרְנוּ עַל עַצְמָן בִּנְשׁוּאִין. מִכָּאן שָׁלוֹם לַשׁוֹמְרוֹת הִימוּד עַל בַּעְלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A married woman who is supported by her husband receives her marriage contract even if she is unfaithful. [However, if the husband dies, the following law applies:] both when she is a widow and when she is a divorcée, if there are witnesses that she guarded herself while married, she receives her marriage contract. If there are no witnesses, she must have guarded herself while married in order to receive her marriage contract, and she may marry again, as all women are presumed to have guarded themselves while married. From this the Sages derived: Peace to those women who guarded themselves for their husbands.

The central theme running through the opening discussion of Daf 27 is the role of *zera* — seed — in the *parsha* of *sotah*. The term appears repeatedly in the relevant *pesukim*: *v'nikesah v'nizre'ah zera*, "she will be cleansed and give forth seed," and *v'shachav ish osah shichvat zera*, "a man slept with her, a lying of seed." These formulations place *zera* at the heart of the *sotah* ritual both linguistically and experientially.

### Ramban's Zera

The Ramban observed that much of the jealousy underlying *sotah* is fueled by the husband's deep-seated anxiety — not merely that his wife has been with another man, but that she may have been impregnated by him. The fundamental concern is whether he will find himself raising another man's children. This is why the *pesukim* emphasize *zera* so insistently.

### Ritual Effects

This emphasis on *zera* is not incidental but structural. The very effects of the *sotah* water mirror this concern. If she is innocent, the outcome is *v'nikesah v'nizre'ah zera* — she conceives and gives birth. If she is guilty, the water enters her and causes her belly to swell, producing a kind of perverse pregnancy. The water thus functions as a liquid that travels inward, toward the same place *zera* would travel, but in the opposite mode: rather than generating life, it generates a grotesque distension. The concern about whose *zera* is within her is literalized in the ritual's physiological effects.

### Sterile Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בַר זְרִיעָה מִן הַדְּבָרִים שֶׁנִּשְׁמְרוּ עַל עַצְמָן בִּנְשׁוּאִין? אֱיָלוֹנִית. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַזְּקֵנָה וְהַעֲקָרָה בִּשְׁמַר עַצְמָן בִּנְשׁוּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** And [regarding] one who is unable to produce seed, [which applies to] matters where women guarded themselves while married: [It refers to] a *haylonit*, a girl who will never be able to conceive. **Rabbi Yosei says: Even an elderly woman and a barren woman [are in this category if they] guarded themselves while married.**

Yet precisely here the *Gemara* begins a systematic process of bracketing the halachic significance of *zera*. Even a woman who is *akarah* or *zekeinah* — sterile or post-menopausal — remains within the *parsha* of *sotah*, despite the fact that pregnancy would be impossible. The only figure whose inclusion was genuinely debated was the *aylonit*, a categorically infertile woman, and even that was contested. The *Gemara*'s willingness to include women for whom the *zera*-concern is biologically irrelevant already signals that the formal halachic category operates independently of the underlying anxiety.

### Impotent Husbands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בַר זְרִיעָה מִן הַמְקַנִּין? הַשָּׁחוּף. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף הָעִיוֵּר וְהַחִיגֵּר מִן הַמְקַנִּין.
>
> **Translation:** And [regarding] one who is unable to produce seed, [which applies to] those who issue a warning [to their wives]: [It refers to] a sick man [with an inability to complete intercourse]. **Rabbi Yosei says: Even a blind man and a lame man [can issue] a warning.**

The same logic applies on the husband's side. Even if the husband is unable to be *mazria* — unable to ejaculate — this does not remove his wife from the *parsha*. Whatever the concern about whose *zera* the child carries, the husband's own incapacity in that regard is halachically immaterial. Perhaps, one might say, it makes things worse — or perhaps, paradoxically, better, since he already knows any child cannot be his. Either way, the *Gemara* does not allow that calculus to enter the legal framework.

### Impotent Suspected Men

Continuing on the same trajectory, the *Gemara* establishes that even if the suspected adulterer — the *shachuf*, a man described as unable to be *mazria* — cannot ejaculate, the case still falls within the *parsha* of *sotah*. The *pesukim* themselves, read carefully, teach that this condition is irrelevant. In every direction — the woman, the husband, the suspected man — *halacha* brackets the significance of *zera* even as the *pesukim* make it central.

### Mi She'eino Ish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאֵלּוּ מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בַר זְרִיעָה, הַשָּׁחוּף קָא מַיְּתַבְיָא לַן, דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר שָׁחוּף מְקַנִּין עַל יָדוֹ. אֲמַר לְהוּ דְּרַב סָחוֹרָה: הֵיכָא דְּקָא שְׁמַע מַר מִשְׁמוּאֵל, דִּי הָכִי אֲמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בַר זְרִיעָה בִּמְדִינַת שְׁלוֹמוֹ, אֲבָל בִּמְדִינַת הַצָּעָר לֹא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks with regard to the statement that one who is unable to produce seed, a *shaḥuf*, cannot issue a warning: But didn't we learn from Shmuel that a *shaḥuf* can issue a warning to his wife?

The *Gemara* then turns to the phrase *mi she'eino ish* — "one who is not a man" — asking what category of person this expression is meant to exclude from the *sotah* framework. Several possibilities are considered. It cannot mean a man who cannot be *mazria*, since such a man is included. It cannot mean a non-Jew, since a woman suspected of relations with a non-Jew is also included in the *parsha*. The discussion thus moves toward more categorical exclusions.

### Sex Between Women

One possibility conspicuously absent from the list of *mi she'eino ish* is the case of another woman. The reason is structural rather than accidental: for halachic purposes, sexual contact between two women is not defined as an act of sex. In *halacha*, the act of sex is always defined in terms of penile penetration — vaginal or anal. This is why there can be a formal prohibition concerning sexual acts between two men, whose act is defined as sex, but no parallel prohibition structured as *bi'ah* between two women, whose contact does not meet that formal definition. The absence of this case from the discussion is not an oversight; it reflects a definitional boundary.

### Bestiality Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְמַעוֹטֵי בְּהֵמָה, דְּאֵין זְנוּת בִּבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, what does the term: And of one who is not a man, serve to exclude? Rav Pappa says: This serves to exclude an animal, as the concept of licentiousness does not apply with regard to an animal. Therefore, the *halakhot* of a *sota* do not apply in this case.

What *mi she'eino ish* ultimately excludes, according to the *Gemara*, is *behemah* — an animal. An act of bestiality is a gravely prohibited act, but it does not make a woman a *sotah* in the technical sense. The *Gemara* reasons that because this is *mi she'eino ish* — not a human male — the *sotah* framework does not apply.

### Shade Case

An interesting marginal case was raised in the name of an *acharon* — cited by Rabbi Shmuel Herzfeld — who suggested that *mi she'eino ish* might also encompass a shade or spirit. A historical case is invoked in which a woman whose husband had been away returned pregnant and claimed to have been impregnated by a shade. The argument that this constituted *mi she'eino ish* could have allowed her to continue living with her husband — but only in a social context in which such a claim was considered plausible. That such a line of reasoning could be entertained at all reflects the degree to which these categories were understood to depend on the nature of the entity involved, not merely on the act itself.

## Shichvat Zera

### Rav Papa Behemah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא מִפַּרְזִקְיָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מְנַָא הָא מִילְּתָא דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן אֵין זְנוּת בִּבְהֵמָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תָבִיא אֶתְנַן זוֹנָה וּמְחִיר כֶּלֶב וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava of Parzakya said to Rav Ashi: From where is this matter that the Sages stated derived, that licentiousness does not apply with regard to an animal? Rav Ashi replied that it is as it is written: "You shall not bring the hire of a harlot, or the price of a dog, into the house of the Lord your God for any vow; for both of them are an abomination to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 23:19).

Having established that *behemah* falls under *mi she'eino ish*, the *Gemara* returns to the precise mechanism of exclusion. The *Gemara* asks: what does the *idach* — the alternative category — come to exclude? Rav Papa answers: *le'a'tuyei beheimah* — to exclude the case of bestiality. The reason, he says, is *de'ein zenut be-beheimah* — bestiality is not considered an act of *zenut*, fornication. This does not mean bestiality is not a prohibition; it is *chayav mitah*. But it is not categorized within the ordinary framework of sexual sin, of a woman betraying her husband. It is a perverse act, but not *zenut* in the technical sense.

### Rava Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: אֶתְנַן כֶּלֶב וּמְחִיר זוֹנָה — מוּתָּרִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גַּם שְׁנֵיהֶם״, שְׁנַיִם וְלֹא אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** And it is taught in a mishna (*Temura* 30a): In the converse cases, the hire of a dog, i.e., a kosher animal that was given to the owner of a dog as payment for engaging in intercourse with it, and the price of a prostitute, i.e., a kosher animal which served as payment in the purchase of a maidservant acquired for prostitution, are permitted to be sacrificed, as it is stated in the aforementioned verse: For both of them. This term indicates that only those two animals may not be sacrificed, i.e., those which served as the hire of a harlot and as the price of a dog; and not four animals, as the reverse cases are excluded from this *halakha*. This indicates that the concept of licentiousness does not apply with regard to animals, as the payment for intercourse with a dog is not considered payment for prostitution.

Rava of Parzikaya then challenges: from where do we know that bestiality is not considered *zenut*? The principle that *ein zenut be-beheimah* needs a source, not merely an intuition.

### Etnan Zonah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: אֶתְנַן כֶּלֶב וּמְחִיר זוֹנָה — מוּתָּרִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גַּם שְׁנֵיהֶם״, שְׁנַיִם וְלֹא אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** And it is taught in a mishna (*Temura* 30a): In the converse cases, the hire of a dog, i.e., a kosher animal that was given to the owner of a dog as payment for engaging in intercourse with it, and the price of a prostitute, i.e., a kosher animal which served as payment in the purchase of a maidservant acquired for prostitution, are permitted to be sacrificed, as it is stated in the aforementioned verse: For both of them. This term indicates that only those two animals may not be sacrificed, i.e., those which served as the hire of a harlot and as the price of a dog; and not four animals, as the reverse cases are excluded from this *halakha*.

The source is derived from the verse *lo tavi etnan zonah u-mechir kelev beit Hashem Elokecha* — do not bring the payment of a prostitute into the Temple. The *etnan zonah* — the gift paid to a prostitute — cannot be used as a *korban*. This establishes the formal category of *zenut* as operative in the context of human prostitution.

### Etnan Kelev

*U-mechir kelev* — the exchange price of a dog — similarly cannot be brought as a *korban*. The *peshat* understanding is that a sheep exchanged for a dog is excluded, though scholars note the phrase may euphemistically refer to payment to a male prostitute.

### Baraita Exception

A *baraita* then teaches: *etnan kelev u-mechir zonah* — however, if the payment was for bestiality with a dog, or a woman was sold for *zenut*, these are *mutarin*, permitted in the *Beit Hamikdash*. The source is the phrase *gam sheneihem* — "both of them" — which the *Gemara* reads as *shenayim v'lo arba*, meaning only the two originally mentioned categories are prohibited, not four.

### Not Zenut

The implication is clear: since there is no concept of *etnan kelev* — no formal category of "payment for sex with an animal" — bestiality does not fall within the legal structure of sexual sin. It is an obvious prohibition, gravely serious, but not categorized as *zenut*.

### Zenut Meaning

This raises a genuinely difficult question: what, precisely, is the halachic content of the statement that bestiality is *ein zenut*? What specific *halacha* turns on whether something is classified as *zenut*? The *Gemara* does not fully spell this out. One dimension relevant here is whether the act constitutes a betrayal of the husband in the relational sense that would bring a woman within the *parsha* of *sotah*. What "not *zenut*" means in terms of other categories — whether it affects *gilui arayot*, *yehareg ve'al ya'avor*, and so forth — remains debated and not resolved in this context.

### Why Shichvat Zera?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא ״שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע״, לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע״ — פְּרָט לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But rather, why do I need the verse to state: "And a man lay with her carnally [*shikhvat zera*]"? The Gemara answers: It is necessary for that which is taught in a *baraita*: The term "*shikhvat zera*" excludes something else.

The *Gemara* now asks a fundamental question: *ve-ela shichvat zera lama li* — given all we have said, what function does the phrase *shichvat zera* actually serve? It does not exclude the impotent husband, the impotent suspected man, or the sterile woman — all of whom remain within the *parsha*. The *Gemara* has systematically bracketed every concrete implication of *shichvat zera*. So what, then, is it doing in the *pesukim*?

### Anal vs Vaginal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״דָּבָר אַחֵר״? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: פְּרָט לְשֶׁקִּינֵּא לָהּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** What is meant by the term: Something else? Rav Sheshet said: This excludes a case where the husband issued a warning to his wife not to engage in sexual intercourse in an atypical manner, i.e., anal intercourse, with another man, and teaches that this is not considered a valid warning.

The *Gemara* answers by citing a *baraita*: *shichvat zera, perat l'davar acher* — *shichvat zera* comes to exclude something else. What is this "something else"? Rav Sheshes explains: *perat she-kina lah she-lo ke-darkah* — it excludes the case where the husband's *kinui* was specifically directed at the suspicion of anal sex. In that case, even though the act is halachically defined as *bi'ah* in other contexts — it is an act of sex — it is excluded from *sotah* because it is not an act that could produce *zera* in any biological sense. *Shichvat zera* thus demands vaginal intercourse, a physical act that under other circumstances could lead to pregnancy, as opposed to anal intercourse, which could never do so.

This is a striking claim. The argument is that even though anal sex is defined as *bi'ah* throughout *halacha*, *shichvat zera* narrows the *sotah* case specifically to the vaginal act — the act that, categorically speaking, is associated with the possibility of pregnancy.

### Abaye Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ, ״מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה״ כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** Rava said to Rav Sheshet: Intercourse in an atypical manner is considered sexual intercourse, as it is written: "The cohabitations of a woman" (Leviticus 18:22), indicating that there are two forms of sexual intercourse with a woman, vaginal and anal, and there is no halakhic differentiation between them.

Abaye challenges Rav Sheshes immediately. He points out that *she-lo ke-darkah* — anal sex — is indeed defined as sex by *halacha*. One cannot so easily exclude it.

### Mishkevei Isha

Rava responds by invoking the phrase *mishkevei ishah*, "the lyings of a woman," which appears in a different context and teaches that there are *two* forms of *bi'ah* that are halachically recognized: vaginal and anal. Both are formally defined as acts of sex. This directly undermines Rav Sheshes' position.

### Anal Sex Defined

The *Gemara* reinforces the point: even *mishkav zachar*, the prohibition of sex between men, is framed in terms of *mishkevei ishah*, demonstrating that anal sex is indeed categorically defined as sex. There is no room within *halacha* to treat it as something less.

### Limbs Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: פְּרָט לְשֶׁקִּינֵּא לָהּ דֶּרֶךְ אֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rava said: It excludes a case where the husband issued a warning to his wife not to engage in intimate contact with another man by way of other limbs, as this is not considered sexual intercourse.

Rava therefore revises the answer: *perat she-kina lah derech evarim* — *shichvat zera* excludes the case where the *kinui* was directed at sexual activity performed through limbs — oral or manual stimulation, not penetrative sex of any kind. That would be the excluded category.

### Peritzuta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: פְּרִיצוּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הִיא, וּפְרִיצוּתָא מִי אָסַר רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said to Rava: That is merely licentious behavior, and does the Merciful One render a woman forbidden to her husband on account of merely licentious behavior, without sexual intercourse? Since this does not render her forbidden to her husband, it is obvious that if the husband issues a warning in this manner, violating the warning does not cause her to become a *sota*. The verse is therefore not required to exclude this case.

Abaye, however, challenges this as well. He argues: does one need a *pasuk* to exclude such cases? *Peritzuta be-alma hi* — that is simply licentiousness, not an act of adultery. And *peritzuta mi asar Rachmana* — the Torah does not define that as forbidden adultery. Abaye is insisting on a rigidly formal position: what falls outside the halachic definition of adultery requires no exclusion, because it was never included to begin with. The *Gemara* is being highly formalistic at this juncture — rather than allowing the husband's subjective jealousy to expand the scope of *sotah*, the *Gemara* insists on applying the same categories that operate throughout the rest of *halacha*.

### Shichvat Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פְּרָט לְשֶׁקִּינֵּא לָהּ בִּנְשִׁיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Abaye said: The verse excludes a case where the husband issued a warning to his wife with regard to engaging in genital contact without actual penetration.

And yet, Abaye himself acknowledges that *shichvat zera* still performs a necessary function — the *pasuk* is not superfluous.

### Neshika Dispute

The *Gemara* therefore offers yet another proposal. The case *shichvat zera* excludes is one where the *kinui* involved *neshika*. This term requires careful definition. *Neshika* here is not simply kissing; it is a technical euphemism for genital contact that stops short of full penetration.

### Ha'arah Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַעֲרָאָה זוֹ הַכְנָסַת עֲטָרָה, אֲבָל נְשִׁיקָה — וְלֹא כְּלוּם הִיא, הַיְינוּ דְּאָתֵי קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי נְשִׁיקָה. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַעֲרָאָה זוֹ נְשִׁיקָה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** This works out well according to the one who says that the initial stage of intercourse is the insertion of the corona but that genital contact is nothing; this is the reason that the verse came to exclude genital contact. However, according to the one who says that the initial stage of intercourse is genital contact, what is there to say? Why should this case be excluded from the *halakhot* of a *sota*?

The operative concept is *ha'arah*, derived from the phrase *mekorah he'erah*, "he uncovered her nakedness." Two definitions of *ha'arah* appear in the literature: one holds it means *hachnasat atarah*, the actual entry of the crown of the penis into the vagina — and this is the dominant understanding by which *ha'arah* constitutes the beginning of a complete sexual act. The other holds that *ha'arah* means *neshika*, the "kissing" of the genitals, meaning the placement of the penis against the vaginal opening without actual entry.

### Pasuk Paradox

The *Gemara* notes a paradox. If *ha'arah* means *hachnasat atarah* — actual entry of the crown — then it is clearly an act of sex, and excluding it via *shichvat zera* makes sense: we understand why the *pasuk* needed to specify. But if *ha'arah* means *neshika* — mere contact without entry — then it is already excluded from the definition of sex without any *pasuk* being required. Why would the *pasuk* need to exclude what was never included?

### Tosafot Reversal

Tosafot notes that the expectation seems reversed: logically, one would think the *pasuk*'s exclusion is necessary precisely when the thing excluded would otherwise have been included. If *neshika* is not sex, no exclusion is needed. If it *is* sex, the exclusion is meaningful. Yet the *Gemara* seems to be struggling in precisely the opposite direction.

### Jealousy vs Adultery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם — לְשֶׁקִּינֵּא לָהּ דֶּרֶךְ אֵבָרִים. וּמַהוּ דְּתֵימָא בִּקְפִידָא דְבַעַל תַּלְיָא רַחֲמָנָא, וּבַעַל הָא קָא קָפֵיד, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Actually, the verse serves to exclude a case where the husband issued a warning to his wife not to engage in intimate contact with another man by way of other limbs. And the verse explicitly excludes this case from the *halakhot* of a *sota*, lest you say that the woman is rendered a *sota* due to this warning, as the Merciful One made this *halakha* dependent on the husband's objection, and the husband objects to contact of this nature. The verse therefore teaches us that this is not considered a warning, as it does not involve sexual intercourse.

This paradox brings the discussion back to its deepest conceptual tension. The question underlying all of this is: is *sotah* fundamentally about the husband's jealousy, or is it about an objective halachic category of adultery? If *sotah* tracks the husband's subjective state — his anxiety, his sense of betrayal, his concern about *zera* — then arguably the *parsha* could expand or contract based on his particular *kinui*. He might be jealous of acts that are not technically adultery; he might not care about acts that technically are. From that perspective, the *parsha* of *sotah* would be shaped by the contours of his concern.

The *Gemara* throughout this entire discussion refuses that framework. It insists that *sotah* must be mapped precisely onto the objective halachic definition of adultery — no broader and no narrower — and that *shichvat zera* functions to enforce exactly that alignment.

### Resolution

The *Gemara*'s resolution reflects this principle directly. *L'olam v'shikina lah derech evarim* — we return to the case where the *kinui* was directed at sexual activity through limbs. And the reason *shichvat zera* is needed to exclude this: *u-mahu d'teima b'kpeida d'ba'al talya rachmana* — one might have thought that *sotah* is determined by the husband's anger and jealousy, and if he is angry about this form of sexual activity, the *Torah* would include it.

### Jealousy Limited

*Ka mashma lan* — *shichvat zera* teaches that even where the husband is genuinely jealous, even where his *kinui* was directed at precisely this activity, if it does not constitute halachic adultery, the *parsha* of *sotah* does not apply. His jealousy cannot broaden the category beyond what the *Torah* objectively defines.

### Objective Definition

The upshot is clear: *sotah* applies only to what falls within the narrow, formal definition of adultery throughout the rest of the *Torah*. Not broader because of his subjectivity; not narrower because of biological considerations like the incapacity of *zera*. The same categories of act that constitute adultery everywhere in *halacha* constitute the *sotah* case — and only those categories.

### Insemination Debate

This conclusion resonates directly with a major modern dispute. The debate between Rav Moshe Feinstein and the *Tzitz Eliezer* over the permissibility of artificial insemination using another man's semen turns precisely on the question of what role *zera* plays in the definition of adultery.

### Rav Moshe Position

Rav Moshe's position was that without an act of sex, there is no adultery and no *mamzerus*. The legal category requires the act, not merely the biological material.

### Tzitz Eliezer Position

The *Tzitz Eliezer* disagreed, arguing that the Ramban's analysis of *shichvat zera* shows that another man's *zera* is itself central to the concern of adultery. Even absent a physical act of sex, the introduction of another man's *zera* into the picture raises concerns analogous to adultery — and potentially touches on *mamzerus* as well.

### Rav Moshe Dismisses

Rav Moshe reportedly dismissed this argument sharply, characterizing the *Tzitz Eliezer*'s framework as foreign to Torah values — a *traif* non-Jewish idea smuggled into *halacha*. He insisted that *pesak halacha* must be grounded in the categories of the *Torah* itself, not in philosophical ideas imported from outside. His implicit reading of *shichvat zera* was entirely consistent with what the *Gemara* on this *daf* has demonstrated: *halacha* systematically empties *shichvat zera* of its biological content and treats it as a formal legal marker — not a substantive concern about *zera* per se.

### Halacha Brackets Zera

The *Gemara* on this *daf* has thus completely bracketed the meaning of *shichvat zera*. Despite its prominence in the *pesukim* and despite the Ramban's compelling reading of the ritual's underlying psychology, *halacha* refuses to assign *shichvat zera* any independent weight. What *shichvat zera* ultimately teaches is not that *zera* matters, but that *sotah* applies only where adultery would apply — and only where adultery would apply. Rav Moshe's position, far from being an innovation, reflects exactly the lesson this *daf* teaches.

## Shmuel Marriage

### Shmuel's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יִשָּׂא אָדָם דּוּמָה, וְאַל יִשָּׂא בַּת דּוּמָה. שֶׁזּוֹ בָּאָה מִטִּיפָּה כְּשֵׁרָה, וְזוֹ בָּאָה מִטִּיפָּה פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Shmuel says: It is better that a man marry a woman whose suspected promiscuity is publicly spoken of [*duma*], and not marry the daughter of a woman suspected of promiscuity, as this woman, who is herself suspected of promiscuity, comes from seed of unflawed lineage; but that woman, the daughter of a woman suspected of promiscuity, comes from seed of flawed lineage, as she might be a *mamzeret*.

The *Gemara* transitions now from the formal categories of *sotah* to a related but distinct concern — the practical questions that arise around women who are not technically *sotah* but about whom there are questions and suspicions. This is connected to earlier discussions about women who are *overet al das* and whether the husband may continue to live with such a woman, or is compelled to divorce her. The present discussion concerns women who are free — widowed or divorced — but whose reputation carries a cloud.

*Amar Shmuel: yisa adam dumah v'al yisa bat dumah* — a man should sooner marry a *dumah*, a woman about whom people are gossiping, than her daughter. The scenario is a married woman about whom there is communal gossip — *dumah* — suggesting acts of adultery. There is no concrete evidence, no formal prohibition, merely talk. Now she is free — divorced or widowed — and the question is: if a man must choose between marrying this woman and marrying her daughter, which is preferable?

### Dumah Explained

Rashi explains *dumah* as *nishanes v'nidaberes b'fi kol al niyufeiha* — she has become spoken of and gossiped about throughout the community regarding acts of adultery. He derives the term from *dumah ircha alayich savach*, a phrase in which the same root connotes communal talk or chatter. Related words can denote either praise or criticism, excessive talk in general; here the context is clearly derogatory. Notably, the woman is not *assur* to remarry on any formal halachic ground — the gossip alone does not constitute evidence. Even if she did commit adultery, she is only forbidden to her husband and to the specific man she committed adultery with; she is not forbidden to this third party who now wishes to marry her.

### Seed Quality

Shmuel's reasoning is as follows: *she-zu ba mi-tipa ksheira* — this woman, the *dumah* herself, came from valid seed; her own status is halachically unimpeachable. *V'zu ba mi-tipa psula* — her daughter, by contrast, may have come from invalid seed, having potentially been conceived through an adulterous union. Shmuel phrases the concern in terms of *tipa psula* rather than simply *safeik mamzeret*, which is interesting. The language of *tipa psula* suggests something deeper than a formal halachic classification — it evokes the idea that a soul formed through such a union is itself marked, not merely categorized differently in *sefer yuchasin*. This parallels older discussions about whether ancestry concerns are fundamentally about observed character, halachic status, or something more metaphysical about the nature of a person formed through a *bi'ah psula*.

Rashi's formulation adds a further dimension: *shema me-oved kochavim u-mamzer* — perhaps the man who impregnated her was a non-Jew or a *mamzer*, which would complicate the daughter's status further. The concern is not only that the daughter is a *safeik mamzeret* in the technical sense, but that her father is a figure of unknown and possibly problematic lineage entirely.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: יִשָּׂא אָדָם בַּת דּוּמָה, וְאַל יִשָּׂא דּוּמָה. שֶׁזּוֹ עוֹמֶדֶת בְּחֶזְקַת כַּשְׁרוּת, וְזוֹ אֵינָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת בְּחֶזְקַת כַּשְׁרוּת.
>
> **Translation:** And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: It is better that a man marry the daughter of a woman suspected of promiscuity, and not marry a woman suspected of promiscuity, as this daughter maintains the presumptive status of virtuousness, but that woman suspected of promiscuity does not maintain the presumptive status of virtuousness.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees and rules in the opposite direction: *yisa adam bat duma v'lo yisa duma* — better to marry the daughter. His reasoning: *she-zu omedet b'chezkat kashrut* — the daughter has a *chazakah* of *kashrut*, no suspicion has ever been raised about her personally. The mother, by contrast, *eina omedet b'chezkat kashrut* — she is under suspicion.

### Chazaka Logic

One might ask: didn't the mother also begin with a *chazakat kashrut*? She too was once a young woman about whom no suspicion existed. Why does Rabbi Yochanan treat the daughter as having a *chazakah* that the mother lacks?

### Chazaka Paradox

This apparent contradiction is noted by the *poskim*. The answer seems to lie in distinguishing between two different concerns. The *chazakah* of the daughter addresses the question of halachic *mamzerus*: she was born without any formal *pesul*, there is no reason to suppose she is a *mamzeret*, and one can apply the standard presumption of *kashrut*. The concern about the mother, by contrast, is not primarily halachic — she is not formally *assur* to remarry. The concern is one of character: communal observation has generated reasonable suspicion about her fidelity. These are different categories of concern.

### Character vs Halacha

Rabbi Yochanan's position prioritizes the halachic dimension — *mamzerus* — and finds the daughter superior on that ground. Shmuel prioritizes the character dimension and finds the daughter inferior: no one has raised questions about the daughter's character, but the mother's character is in question. Both are legitimate concerns; the debate is over which concern is weightier.

### Baraita Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: נוֹשֵׂא אָדָם דּוּמָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection to Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement from a *baraita*: A man may marry a woman suspected of promiscuity.

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *nosei adam duma* — a man may marry a *dumah*. This seems to support Shmuel's position, since Shmuel holds that the *dumah* herself is the preferable choice.

### Rava Caveat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְתִסְבְּרָא נוֹשֵׂא לְכַתְּחִלָּה?! אֶלָּא: אִם נָשָׂא, תְּנִי נָמֵי: ״בַּת דּוּמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said in response: And how can you understand the *baraita* at face value? The *baraita* states that a man may marry her ***ab initio,** yet clearly it is undesirable to do so. Rather, the wording of the *baraita* is imprecise, and one must render it: If one married a woman suspected of promiscuity, she is permitted to him. Since the *baraita* is imprecise, one should also correct it and teach: The daughter of a woman suspected of promiscuity.

Rava immediately qualifies: *v'tisbra? nosei l'chatchila?* — does that make sense? Is marrying a *dumah* really the recommended course of action, the top priority? Clearly not. What the *baraita* means is *im nasa* — if one has already married, it is acceptable, it is not grounds for annulment. The *baraita* is stating a *b'diavad* ruling, not a *l'chatchila* preference. And the same would apply to marrying the *bat duma* — *b'diavad*, it is acceptable as well. The *baraita* therefore does not resolve which is preferable.

### Final Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: יִשָּׂא אָדָם בַּת דּוּמָה, וְאַל יִשָּׂא דּוּמָה. דְּתָנֵי רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר מַעְרְבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אִשָּׁה מְזַנָּה — בָּנֶיהָ כְּשֵׁרִין, רוֹב בְּעִילוֹת אַחַר הַבַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: And the *halakha* is that it is better that a man marry the daughter of a woman suspected of promiscuity, and not marry a woman suspected of promiscuity. This is as Rav Taḥalifa, from the West, i.e., Eretz Yisrael, taught before Rabbi Abbahu: If a married woman engages in adulterous sexual intercourse, her children are considered of unflawed lineage, as most instances of sexual intercourse are attributed to the husband, and consequently it is presumed that the children were conceived by the husband and not by the paramour. Therefore, the daughter of a woman suspected of promiscuity is not suspected of being a *mamzeret*.

The *hilchata* — the final ruling — sides with Rabbi Yochanan: *yisa adam bat duma v'al yisa duma* — better to marry the daughter. The dominant concern in the final halachic determination is the character concern: the daughter has no mark against her character, while the mother does. This represents a prioritization of observed behavior over speculative halachic concern about lineage.

### Rov Beilos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר מַעְרְבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אִשָּׁה מְזַנָּה — בָּנֶיהָ כְּשֵׁרִין, רוֹב בְּעִילוֹת אַחַר הַבַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** This is as Rav Taḥalifa, from the West, i.e., Eretz Yisrael, taught before Rabbi Abbahu: If a married woman engages in adulterous sexual intercourse, her children are considered of unflawed lineage, as most instances of sexual intercourse are attributed to the husband, and consequently it is presumed that the children were conceived by the husband and not by the paramour.

An additional principle is invoked by Rav Tachlifa bar Maarava, taught before Rabbi Abbahu: *isha m'zanah baneiha k'sheirim, rov beilos achar ha-ba'al* — a woman who commits adultery, her children are nonetheless presumed *k'sheirim*, because the majority of her sexual relations are with her husband. Even in cases where adultery is confirmed, the statistical presumption is that any given child was conceived through legitimate relations. This principle of *rov beilos achar ha-ba'al* is a powerful tool: even when infidelity is known, it does not automatically render the children suspect.

### Halachic Presumption

This is a striking ruling. Even a confirmed adulteress — a woman about whom it is established, not merely rumored, that she has been with another man — her children carry the presumption of being her husband's children, because statistically, even a woman engaged in an affair continues to have relations with her husband more frequently than with a lover. The halachic status of the children is thus insulated from the confirmed fact of the mother's conduct.

## Prutza Beyoter

### Rami's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב עַמְרָם: הָיְתָה פְּרוּצָה בְּיוֹתֵר, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Amram raises a dilemma: What is the *halakha* if the woman was extremely promiscuous and one cannot reasonably attribute most instances of intercourse to the husband?

Rami bar Chama raises a sharper question: *haysa prutza b'yoter mahu* — what if the woman is extraordinarily licentious? Not merely a woman about whom there is gossip, nor even a confirmed occasional adulteress, but a woman of extreme and persistent licentiousness — someone seen constantly with strange men, leaving no doubt that she is engaged in habitual infidelity. Does the principle of *rov beilos achar ha-ba'al* still apply?

### Fertile Window Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין אִשָּׁה מִתְעַבֶּרֶת אֶלָּא סָמוּךְ לְוִוסְתָּהּ — לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּלָא יָדַע בַּהּ וְלָא מְנַטַּר לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** According to the opinion of the one who says that a woman becomes pregnant only close to the time of the onset of her menstruation, you do not need to raise this dilemma, as the husband does not know the time of this, i.e., the time of her menstruation, in advance, and he does not watch her actions in order to prevent her from conceiving from another man.

The *Gemara* first considers a view that a woman only becomes pregnant at a specific point near her expected period. Under this view — which is biologically incorrect but was the operative assumption in this context — pregnancy can only occur during a narrow window.

### Timing Uncertainty

If this view is correct, then even in the case of a very licentious woman, the question depends on whether the husband knows precisely when his wife's fertile window occurs. Women do not always have perfectly regular cycles, and a husband may not know the exact date of her expected period. If he does not know when she is fertile, he cannot specifically guard her during that time — he cannot watch her closely enough during the window that matters — and therefore even his awareness of her licentiousness does not enable him to ensure that any child is his.

### Mikveh Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין אִשָּׁה מִתְעַבֶּרֶת אֶלָּא סָמוּךְ לִטְבִילָתָהּ, מַאי? כֵּיוָן דְּיָדַע בָּהּ, נַטּוֹרֵי מְנַטַּר לַהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דִּפְרוּצָה בְּיוֹתֵר — לָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** When do you need to raise this dilemma? It is necessary according to the opinion of the one who says that a woman becomes pregnant only close to the time of her immersion. What is the *halakha*? Can it be assumed that since he knows the time of this, i.e., of her immersion, he watches her actions on that day in order to prevent her from conceiving from another man; or perhaps, since she is extremely promiscuous, her husband cannot adequately watch her and her children are considered of uncertain lineage? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma shall stand unresolved.

The second view holds that a woman becomes pregnant specifically near the time of her immersion in the *mikveh*. This is, in fact, biologically accurate: a woman immerses approximately twelve days after the onset of her period, which places immersion very close to ovulation under Rabbinic *halacha*, and somewhat less precisely so under purely *Torah*-level reckoning. According to this view, the husband knows exactly when his wife's fertile period is, because it is tied directly to the *mikveh* calendar, which is structured and predictable.

If the husband knows precisely when the fertile window is, he can in principle watch his wife closely during that time. But, the *Gemara* asks, what if she is so extraordinarily licentious that even his heightened vigilance during the critical period is futile? She simply walks out on him. He knows, he is watching, but he cannot control her. Does *rov beilos achar ha-ba'al* still operate under those conditions?

### Teiku

The *Gemara* does not resolve this question. *Teiku* — the question stands unanswered.

### Evidence Standard

This unresolved question has practical significance. For a woman to be forbidden to her husband — whether as a *sotah*, as an *overet al das*, or on grounds of confirmed adultery — some evidence is required. The *Gemara* earlier discussed *edei kiyur*, witnesses of unseemly behavior: witnesses who saw the couple enter a hotel room, who observed conduct that strongly implies an adulterous relationship, even without witnessing the act itself sufficient to impose capital punishment. At what point does the accumulation of such evidence reach a threshold that makes it no longer a matter of the husband's discretion whether to stay married?

### Modern Issues

This question has direct practical application in contemporary cases. A situation in which a husband suspects his wife of adultery, hires a private investigator, and obtains photographs and other evidence of the wife entering a hotel with another man presents precisely this problem. No witness observed the act directly in a manner sufficient for a formal *chiyuv misa*, yet the evidence of *kiyur* is overwhelming. If the couple wishes to reconcile, can they? And at what stage does *halacha* intervene to say they may not?

This connects as well to the principle discussed in *Nedarim* at the end of the tractate: a woman who claims to have committed adultery is not believed — which has the practical consequence of protecting reconciliation efforts when the wife's own statement is the only evidence. The asymmetry between the treatment of a woman's self-incriminating claim and the treatment of external *edei kiyur* is a tension that emerges naturally from these discussions.

## Conditions & Cases

### Sotah Eligibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִישׁ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֵשֶׁת חֵרֵשׁ, וְאֵשֶׁת שׁוֹטֶה, וְאֵשֶׁת שַׁעֲמוּם, וְשֶׁהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, וְשֶׁהָיָה חָבוּשׁ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין — שֶׁבֵּית דִּין מְקַנִּין לָהֶן לְפוֹסְלָן מִכְּתוּבָּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: The verse states: "If the wife of any man goes astray" (Numbers 5:12). As the verse could have said: The wife of a man, what is the meaning when the verse states: "The wife of any man"? It serves to include the wife of a deaf-mute, and the wife of an imbecile, and the wife of an insane person, and one whose husband went overseas, and one whose husband was incarcerated in prison; and it teaches that the court issues a warning to these women in order to disqualify them from receiving payment of their marriage contract.

The *Gemara* now transitions to the next *perek*, which deals with the conditions under which the *sotah* process may be carried out and who qualifies as subject to it.

### Physical Conditions

The *Gemara* addresses a series of physical conditions that exclude a woman from the *sotah* process: if she is blind, a *cheresh* (deaf-mute), lame, armless, or mute. Each exclusion is derived from specific *pesukim* in the *parsha*.

### Rav Sheshes Blind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאִם הוּא סוֹמֵא לֹא הָיָה מַשְׁקָהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנֶעְלַם מֵעֵינֵי אִישָׁהּ״, כָּךְ הִיא, אִם הָיְתָה סוֹמָא — לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet says: This teaches that just as if the husband was blind he would not have her drink, as it is written: "And it was hidden from the eyes of her husband" (Numbers 5:13), indicating that the *sota* ritual applies only if the husband was capable of seeing her infidelity but did not do so; so too, with regard to the woman, if she were blind, she would not drink.

Rav Sheshes derives the exclusion of a blind woman from the phrase *v'nistarah*, "it was hidden," and the related phrase *u-minalam me'einei ishah*, "hidden from the eyes of her husband." The verse implies that the husband can normally see — *v'nistarah* presupposes a seeing husband — and by analogy, a seeing woman is required. A blind woman is thus excluded because the verse presupposes normal vision on both sides of the relationship.

### Beisi Lame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁחִיגֶּרֶת וְגִידֶּמֶת לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהֶעֱמִיד הַכֹּהֵן אֶת הָאִשָּׁה לִפְנֵי ה׳ ... וְנָתַן עַל כַּפֶּיהָ״ — כָּךְ הוּא — אִם הָיָה חִיגֵּר אוֹ גִידֵּם, לֹא הָיָה מַשְׁקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi also says: Just as a lame woman and a woman without hands would not drink, as it is written: "And the priest shall stand the woman before the Lord…and place the meal-offering of memorial in her hands" (Numbers 5:18), indicating that if she is unable to stand up straight or if she does not have hands with which to accept the offering, then she does not drink; so too, if the husband were lame or without hands, he would not cause his wife to drink.

Beisi derives additional exclusions from the procedural requirements of the ritual itself. The verse *v'he'emid ha-kohein et ha-ishah lifnei Hashem* — "the *kohen* shall make her stand before God" — requires that she be able to stand, which excludes a woman who is lame. The verse *v'natan al kapeha* — "and he shall place upon her hands" the *minchah* — requires that she have hands, which excludes a woman who is armless. By the principle of *lehakish ish le-ishah*, these requirements apply symmetrically to the husband: a lame or armless husband similarly cannot initiate the *sotah* process.

### Mar bar Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאִילֶּמֶת לֹא הָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאָמְרָה הָאִשָּׁה אָמֵן אָמֵן״, כָּךְ הוּא — אִם הָיָה אִילֵּם, לֹא הָיָה מַשְׁקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Mar Bar Rav Ashi says: Just as a mute woman would not drink, as it is written: "And the woman shall say: Amen, Amen" (Numbers 5:22), indicating that she must be able to speak; so too, if the husband were mute, he would not cause his wife to drink.

Mar bar Rav Ashi adds the exclusion of a mute woman, derived from *v'amrah ha-ishah amein amein* — "and the woman shall say *amein amein*." The ritual requires her verbal response. A mute woman cannot give that response and is therefore excluded. By the same principle of symmetry, a mute husband cannot bring his wife through the *sotah* process.

### Husband Conditions

As the *Gemara* makes explicit through the verse *asher tisteh ishah tachat ishah* — *lehakish ish le-ishah v'ishah le-ish* — the man and the woman must be in equivalent categories. Every physical condition that disqualifies the wife from the *sotah* process disqualifies the husband from initiating it. The symmetry is structurally built into the *parsha*.

### Perek End

With this, the *Gemara* concludes the *perek* of *HaMekane* — a remarkable chapter that has exhaustively examined which types of men, which types of women, and which types of marriages fall within the *parsha* of *sotah*.

## Water Effects

### Mutual Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַמַּיִם בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתָהּ, כָּךְ הַמַּיִם בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבָאוּ״ ״וּבָאוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Just as the water evaluates her fidelity, so too, the water evaluates his, i.e., her alleged paramour's, involvement in the sin, as it is stated: "And the water that causes the curse shall enter into her" (Numbers 5:24), and it is stated again: "And the water that causes the curse shall enter into her and become bitter" (Numbers 5:27). It is derived from the double mention of the phrase "and…shall enter" that both the woman and her paramour are evaluated by the water.

The new *perek* opens with a striking *mishnah*: *k'sheim she-hamayim bodkin osah, kach hamayim bodkin oso* — the same way the water tests her, it tests him. The "him" here requires immediate clarification.

### Uvau Uvau

The *mishnah* derives this principle from the phrase *u-va'u u-va'u* — "the waters will come in and come in" — which appears twice in the *pesukim*, in different verses, indicating that the waters act upon both parties.

### Who Is Him?

One might have assumed that "him" refers to the husband — perhaps because he too must be *menukkeh me-avon*, free of sin, for the water to work, or perhaps because sleeping with a *sotah* after her status is established might incur its own punishment. But the *Gemara* clarifies: "him" is the *boel* — the suspected adulterer. When the waters act upon the woman and she suffers their consequences, the man who committed adultery with her, wherever he may be, suffers the same fate.

### Forbidden Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֲסוּרָה לַבַּעַל — כָּךְ אֲסוּרָה לַבּוֹעֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִטְמָאָה״ ״וְנִטְמָאָה״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, prior to her drinking the water, just as she is forbidden to her husband, so too is she forbidden to her paramour, because in contrast to the verse stating: "Is defiled [*nitma'a*]" (Numbers 5:14), a superfluous conjoining prefix *vav* is added to a later verse, rendering the phrase: "And is defiled [*venitma'a*]"** (Numbers 5:29). The addition indicates another prohibition, that of the woman to her paramour. This is the statement of Rabbi Akiva.

The *mishnah* continues: *k'sheim she-asurah la-ba'al kach asurah la-boel* — the same way a *sotah* is forbidden to her husband, she is forbidden to the *boel*, the man with whom she committed adultery.

### Practical Case

This has immediate practical implications. A woman who commits adultery and is subsequently divorced — or whose husband dies — cannot then marry the man she committed adultery with. Even if years have passed, even if the formal *sotah* process was never completed, the adultery itself forbids her to that specific man. The waters' failure to "test" her does not undo the underlying prohibition.

### Practical Effect

The case becomes even sharper in a contemporary context. A woman whose husband has not given her a *get* — perhaps they have a civil divorce but she remains halachically married — who then lives with another man is, during that entire period, committing adultery in halachic terms. When she finally receives the *get*, she is formally free from her husband — but she may not marry the man she has been living with. That man falls within the category of *boel*, and the rule of *asurah la-boel* applies.

### Venitma'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כָּךְ הָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים הָאֲמוּרִים בַּפָּרָשָׁה ״נִטְמָאָה״ ״וְנִטְמָאָה״ — אֶחָד לַבַּעַל וְאֶחָד לַבּוֹעֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua said: That was how Zekharya ben HaKatzav would interpret it, i.e., he also derived from the superfluous *vav* that the woman is forbidden to her paramour. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says an alternate source: The two times that the defilement of the wife is stated in the passage, namely: "And he warns his wife, and she is defiled" (Numbers 5:14), and the later verse: "When a wife, being under her husband, goes astray and is defiled" (Numbers 5:29), indicate that her defilement results in two prohibitions. One is that she is forbidden to her husband and one is that she is forbidden to her paramour.

The textual basis for this dual prohibition is the word *v'nitme'ah*, "and she is defiled," which appears twice in the *parsha*. Each instance of *nitme'ah* is read as teaching a separate forbiddance — one to the husband, one to the *boel*.

### Rabbi Akiva View

Rabbi Akiva derives both prohibitions from the extra *vav* in *v'nitme'ah* — the prefix *vav* adds an additional instance of defilement, extending the prohibition to the second man.

### Rabbi Yehoshua View

Rabbi Yehoshua, citing the teaching of Zecharya ben HaKatzav, holds that it is not the *vav* that is being *doreshed*, but rather the fact that the word *nitme'ah* appears twice in the *parsha* entirely. *Shnei pe'amim amur ba-parsha nitme'ah v'nitme'ah — echad l'ba'al v'echad l'boel*: one instance teaches defilement to the husband, and one teaches defilement to the *boel*. The two *amoraim* reach the same conclusion through different hermeneutical paths.

## Rabbi Akiva Drashos

### Beit Medrash Opens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״וּכְלִי חֶרֶשׂ אֲשֶׁר יִפֹּל מֵהֶם אֶל תּוֹכוֹ כֹּל אֲשֶׁר בְּתוֹכוֹ יִטְמָא״, אֵינוֹ אוֹמֵר ״טָמֵא״ אֶלָּא ״יִטְמָא״, לְטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים. לִמֵּד עַל כִּכָּר שֵׁנִי שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא אֶת הַשְּׁלִישִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** On that same day that Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya was appointed head of the Sanhedrin, Rabbi Akiva interpreted the verse: "And every earthen vessel into which any of them falls, whatever is in it shall be impure [*yitma*], and you shall break it" (Leviticus 11:33), as follows: The verse does not state: Is impure [*tamei*], but rather: "Shall be impure," in order to indicate that not only does the vessel itself become ritually impure, but it can now render other items ritually impure. This teaches with regard to a loaf that has second-degree ritual impurity status due to its being placed inside an earthenware vessel that had first-degree impurity, that it can render other food with which it comes into contact impure with third-degree impurity status.

The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Akiva's *drashot* that follow are introduced with the phrase *bo bayom* — on that day. Rashi explains, citing the *Gemara* in *Maseches Brachos*, that whenever the phrase *bo bayom* appears, it refers to the day that Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah was appointed *nasi* in place of Rabban Gamliel. On that day, the gates of the *Beit Medrash* were opened broadly.

### Bo Bayom Context

Rabban Gamliel had maintained a strict policy: *kol talmid chacham she-ein tocho k'baro al yikanes l'veit ha-midrash* — any Torah scholar whose inner character does not match his outer presentation may not enter the *Beit Medrash*. This was an elitist standard of admission. When Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah was elevated to the *nesiut* and relaxed this restriction, many additional students entered — *v'rabta Torah bo bayom*, and Torah was greatly expanded on that day, because the diversity of voices allowed many previously unresolved matters to be clarified.

Rashi further notes that the *drasha* immediately preceding — the teaching about *v'nitme'ah v'nitme'ah* — may itself have occurred on that same day, so all the teachings clustered under *bo bayom* belong together.

### Yitma Drasha

Rabbi Akiva's first *drasha* on that day addresses a verse in *Vayikra* 11 about the *kli cheres*, a pottery vessel, and the *sheretz*, a dead rodent. The verse states: *u-chli cheres asher yipol mehem el tocho, kol asher b'tocho yitma* — any pottery vessel into which a *sheretz* falls, everything inside it becomes *tamei*. Rabbi Akiva focuses on the word *yitma*: the verse does not say *tamei* — it will be *tamei* — but *yitma*, "shall become *tamei*," which he reads as causative — it shall *make others tamei*.

### Vessel Drasha

The structure of the *tumah* transmission is as follows. The *sheretz* is an *av ha-tumah*. The *kli cheres*, even without direct contact — merely by virtue of being an open vessel in whose airspace the *sheretz* exists — becomes a *rishon l'tumah*. Any food inside the vessel contracts *tumah* from the *kli*, making the food a *sheni*. Now Rabbi Akiva derives from *yitma* that this *sheni* food can then render other food a *shlishi* — a third-degree *tumah*. This is the teaching: *limed al kikar sheni she-metamei et ha-shlishi* — a *kikar* (loaf) that is *sheni* can make something else *shlishi*.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מִי יְגַלֶּה עָפָר מֵעֵינֶיךָ רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, שֶׁהָיִיתָ אוֹמֵר: עָתִיד דּוֹר אַחֵר לְטַהֵר כִּכָּר שְׁלִישִׁי, שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מִקְרָא מִן הַתּוֹרָה שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא, וַהֲלֹא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא תַּלְמִידְךָ מֵבִיא לוֹ מִקְרָא מִן הַתּוֹרָה שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל אֲשֶׁר בְּתוֹכוֹ יִטְמָא״.
>
> **Translation:** After hearing Rabbi Akiva's statement, Rabbi Yehoshua said: Who will remove the dirt from your eyes, Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai, so that you could live and see this? As you would say: In the future, another generation is destined to deem pure a loaf that contracted third-degree impurity, as there is no explicit verse from the Torah stating that it is impure. But now Rabbi Akiva, your disciple, brings a verse from the Torah indicating that it is impure, as it is stated: "Whatever is in it shall be impure."

Rabbi Yehoshua's reaction is famous and deeply moving. He invokes the name of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai: *mi gala afar me'einecha, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* — who will remove the dust from your eyes, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai! The idiom suggests: if only you were alive to witness this. Rashi understands this as a near-literal image: the dust of the grave, removed from the eyes of the deceased, so that he might see what is happening.

The context is this: Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai had taught that the *kashrut* of *kikar shlishi* — third-degree *tumah* in food — had no explicit *pasuk*, and he was troubled by this. He feared that a future generation might reject the ruling as lacking a *mikra min ha-Torah*. Rabbi Akiva's *drasha* now provides precisely that explicit verse. Rabbi Yehoshua, on behalf of his departed master, celebrates: Rabbi Akiva — your student — has found the *pasuk* you sought. *Matza lo mikra min ha-Torah she-hu tamei*, as it says *kol asher b'tocho yitma*.

### Levite Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״וּמַדֹּתֶם מִחוּץ לָעִיר אֶת פְּאַת קֵדְמָה אַלְפַּיִם בָּאַמָּה וְגוֹ׳״, וּמִקְרָא אַחֵר אֹמֵר: ״מִקִּיר הָעִיר וָחוּצָה אֶלֶף אַמָּה סָבִיב״.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, on that same day Rabbi Akiva interpreted the verses with regard to the Levite cities as follows: One verse states: "And you shall measure outside the city for the east side two thousand cubits…this shall be for them the open land outside the cities" (Numbers 35:5), and another verse states: "And the open land around the cities, which you shall give to the Levites, shall be from the wall of the city and outward one thousand cubits round about" (Numbers 35:4).

The second *drasha* of that day addresses a contradiction between two *pesukim* concerning the *arei Levi'im*, the cities of the *Levi'im*. One verse states *u-madotem mi-chutz la-ir pe'at kedma alpayim ba-amah* — you shall measure two thousand *amot* outside the city in each direction. Another verse states *mi-kir ha-ir v'chutzah elef amah saviv* — from the city wall and outward, one thousand *amot* around.

### Pasuk Conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר אֶלֶף אַמָּה, שֶׁכְּבָר נֶאֱמַר אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה. וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה, שֶׁכְּבָר נֶאֱמַר אֶלֶף אַמָּה. הָא כֵּיצַד? אֶלֶף אַמָּה מִגְרָשׁ, וְאַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה תְּחוּם הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** It is impossible to say that the area around the cities given to the Levites was only one thousand cubits, as it is already stated: "Two thousand cubits." And it is impossible to say that two thousand cubits were left for them, as it is already stated: "One thousand cubits." How can these texts be reconciled? One thousand cubits are to be set aside as a tract of open land surrounding the city, and the two thousand cubits are mentioned not in order to be given to the Levites, but to indicate the boundary of the Shabbat limit, beyond which it is forbidden to travel on Shabbat.

The two *pesukim* appear to contradict each other directly: one says two thousand *amot*, the other says one thousand. *Ee efshar lomar elef amah she-kvar ne'emar alpayim ba-amah, v'ee efshar lomar alpayim ba-amah she-kvar ne'emar elef amah* — it is impossible to say one thousand if it already says two thousand, and it is impossible to say two thousand if it already says one thousand.

### Akiva Resolution

Rabbi Akiva's resolution: *elef amah migrash* — the one thousand *amot* constitute the *migrash*, the open land, the equivalent of landscaped fields immediately surrounding the city. *V'alpayim ba-amah* — and the two thousand *amot* define the *techum Shabbat*, the outer boundary of the city for purposes of *Shabbat* travel. The two thousand *amot* include the one thousand of *migrash*; the *Levi'im* actually own real estate only out to one thousand *amot*, and the next thousand constitutes the formal city limit. This is the source for the *halacha* of *techum Shabbat* being two thousand *amot*.

### Alternative View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: אֶלֶף אַמָּה מִגְרָשׁ, וְאַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה שָׂדוֹת וּכְרָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, says otherwise: One thousand cubits were given to the Levites as an open tract of land, that could not be planted or built upon, and two thousand cubits of additional land were given to the Levites for planting fields and vineyards.

Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Yose HaGlili offers a different reading. He agrees that *elef amah migrash* — the first thousand is the open field surrounding the city — but holds that *alpayim ba-amah sadot u-chramim* — the second thousand is actual agricultural land, fields and vineyards owned by the *Levi'im*. According to this view, the *Levi'im* receive two thousand *amot* of real estate: one thousand of open *migrash* and one thousand of productive agricultural land. The two-thousand-*amah* figure is not a city limit for *Shabbat* but represents actual territorial holdings.

### Red Sea Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״אָז יָשִׁיר מֹשֶׁה וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַשִּׁירָה הַזֹּאת לַה׳ וַיֹּאמְרוּ לֵאמֹר״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֵאמֹר״, וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֵאמֹר״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹנִין שִׁירָה אַחֲרָיו שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה עַל כׇּל דָּבָר וְדָבָר כְּקוֹרְאִין אֶת [הַ]הַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, on that same day Rabbi Akiva interpreted the verse: "Then Moses and the children of Israel sang this song to the Lord, and said, saying" (Exodus 15:1), as follows: As there is no need for the verse to state the word "saying," because it states the word "said" immediately prior to it, why must the verse state the word "saying"? It teaches that the Jewish people would repeat in song after Moses every single statement he said, as is done when reciting *hallel*.

The third *drasha* of that day concerns the singing at the Red Sea. The verse states: *az yashir Moshe u-vnei Yisrael et ha-shirah ha-zot la-Hashem va-yomru leimor* — then Moshe and the children of Israel sang this song to God and they said, to say.

### Vayomru Leimor

The word *leimor* is seemingly redundant. The verse already says *va-yomru*, "they said"; why add *leimor*, "to say"?

### Hallel Reading

Rabbi Akiva derives from this redundancy that the singing was *responsive* in structure: Moshe would sing a phrase and Israel would respond. He compares it to the way *Hallel* is read: the *chazan* recites and the congregation responds after each verse. *U-l'chach ne'emar leimor* — that is why the word *leimor* was needed, to indicate that Israel was saying back what Moshe said.

### Nechemia View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כְּקוֹרִין אֶת שְׁמַע, וְלֹא כְּקוֹרִין אֶת [הַ]הַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Neḥemya says: The people sang the song together with Moses as is done when reciting *Shema*, which is recited in unison after the prayer leader begins, and not as is done when reciting *hallel*.

Rabbi Nechemia disagrees. He holds that the singing was structured not like *Hallel* but like the reading of *Shema*: the leader begins and everyone reads together in unison, rather than responsively. The model is communal recitation, not responsive antiphony.

### Job Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן הוּרְקָנוֹס: לֹא עָבַד אִיּוֹב אֶת הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶלָּא מֵאַהֲבָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֵן יִקְטְלֵנִי לוֹ אֲיַחֵל״. וַעֲדַיִין הַדָּבָר שָׁקוּל: לוֹ אֲנִי מְצַפֶּה, אוֹ אֵינִי מְצַפֶּה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַד אֶגְוָע לֹא אָסִיר תֻּמָּתִי מִמֶּנִּי״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמֵּאַהֲבָה עָשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** On that same day Rabbi Yehoshua ben Hyrcanus taught: Job served the Holy One, Blessed be He, only out of love, as it is stated: "Though He will slay me, still I will trust in Him" (Job 13:15). And still, the matter is even, i.e., the verse is ambiguous, as there are two possible interpretations of the verse. Was Job saying: I will await Him, expressing his yearning for God; or should the verse be interpreted as saying I will not await Him. As the word "lo" can mean either "to him" or "not," it is unclear which meaning is intended here. This dilemma is resolved elsewhere, where the verse states a clearer indication of Job's intent: "Till I die I will not put away my integrity from me" (Job 27:5). This teaches that he acted out of love.

Also on that day, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Horkenos taught: *lo avad Iyov et ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu ela me-ahavah* — Iyov served God not out of fear but out of love. This might seem counterintuitive: the text of *Sefer Iyov* explicitly states *haya yereh Elokim*, "he feared God." Rabbi Yehoshua ben Horkenos argues that despite the language of fear, the deeper reality of Iyov's service was love.

### Job Pasuk

The textual basis is subtle. The verse reads: *hen yikteleini lo ayachel* — "if He kills me, I will still hope." But there is a *keri-ktiv* discrepancy. The *ktiv* — the written text — is *lo* with an *alef*, meaning "not": "if He kills me, I will no longer hope." The *keri* — the oral reading — is *lo* with a *vav*, meaning "to Him": "if He kills me, I will hope toward Him." The *Gemara* notes the ambiguity: *v'adayin ha-davar shakul* — the matter remains balanced, indeterminate, between the two readings.

### Love Proof

The resolution comes from another verse: *ad egva lo asir tumati mimeni* — "until I die, I will not remove my righteousness from me." This verse, read alongside the ambiguous *lo/lo* verse, demonstrates that Iyov's commitment was absolute — even unto death, he would not swerve. This is the language not of mere fear, which might bend under sufficient pressure, but of love, which remains steadfast regardless of consequence. *Melamed she-me-ahavah asa* — this teaches that Iyov acted out of love.

### Student Vindication

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מִי יְגַלֶּה עָפָר מֵעֵינֶיךָ רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, שֶׁהָיִיתָ דּוֹרֵשׁ כׇּל יָמֶיךָ שֶׁלֹּא עָבַד אִיּוֹב אֶת הַמָּקוֹם אֶלָּא מִיִּרְאָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִישׁ תָּם וְיָשָׁר יְרֵא אֱלֹהִים וְסָר מֵרָע״, וַהֲלֹא יְהוֹשֻׁעַ תַּלְמִיד תַּלְמִידְךָ לִמֵּד שֶׁמֵּאַהֲבָה עָשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua said: Who will remove the dirt from your eyes, Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai, so that you could live and see this? As you taught all your life that Job worshipped the Omnipresent only out of fear, as it is stated: "And that man was wholehearted and upright, and God-fearing, and shunned evil" (Job 1:1); but now Yehoshua ben Hyrcanus, the disciple of your disciple, has taught that Job acted out of love.

Rabbi Yehoshua then mirrors the earlier reaction of his namesake to Rabbi Akiva's *drasha*, offering the same exclamation to Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai: *mi yigaleh afar me-einecha, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* — who will remove the dust from your eyes! For Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai had spent his lifetime teaching that Iyov served God only out of fear, based on the verse *ish tam v'yashar, yereh Elokim v'sar me-ra*. And now Rabbi Yehoshua ben Horkenos — a student of your student, your halachic grandchild — has demonstrated that it was love all along. *V'halo Yehoshua talmid talmidecha limed she-me-ahavah asa* — look at what your student's student has taught. Even in disagreement with the master, the student vindicates the deepest aspiration: that Torah be clarified, and that its most profound truths come to light.
