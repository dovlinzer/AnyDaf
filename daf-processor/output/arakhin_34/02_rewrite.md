# Arakhin 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Levite Redemption Rights

### Mishna Intro

Daf 34 opens at the bottom of 33b, with the final line of the *mishna*. The *mishna* has concluded its treatment of houses in unwalled cities and the structure of the cities of the *leviim* — including the conversion of parks to fields and the preservation of that urban arrangement. It now closes with a statement about the *leviim*'s rights of sale and redemption: the *leviim* may sell at all times, and — more significantly — they may redeem at all times. The perpetual right of redemption, *geulat olam*, means that whatever they sell, they may redeem from the very outset. Unlike a *sedeh achuza* of an ordinary Israelite, where the seller must wait until the third year before redeeming, and unlike a house in a walled city, where the right of redemption lapses after one year, a *levi* retains the right to redeem continuously, right up through the *yovel*.

### Geulat Olam Fields

A *baraita* is introduced: *Geulat olam tihyeh laleviim* — an eternal redemption shall belong to the *leviim*. The *Gemara* asks: what does this teach? It proceeds to identify three distinct *chiddushim* conveyed by this phrase, each addressing a different situation in which one might have thought the *levi*'s right to redeem would be curtailed.

The first *chiddush* concerns fields. The verse concerning a *sedeh achuza* of an ordinary Israelite states *mispar shenei tevuot yimkor lach* — the field is sold for a number of years' worth of produce, and *Chazal* derive from this that the seller must wait at least two years before redeeming. One might have thought that this restriction applies equally to a *levi*'s field. Therefore the *Torah* teaches *geulat olam tihyeh laleviim* — the *levi* may redeem even from the very beginning, without any waiting period.

The second *chiddush* addresses sanctification. By a standard *sedeh achuza*, the verse states *veyatza hasadeh b'yovel kodesh l'Hashem kesedeh ha-cherem* — if the Israelite sanctified the field and someone else redeemed it from *hekdesh*, then at *yovel* the field passes to the *kohanim*. One might have thought this rule applies equally when a *levi* sanctifies his field and an Israelite redeems it from *hekdesh* — that at *yovel* it would go to the *kohanim* and be lost to the *levi* entirely. Therefore the *Torah* teaches *geulat olam tihyeh laleviim*: the *levi* retains the perpetual right to redeem.

What precisely happens in this latter scenario is not spelled out explicitly in the *Gemara*. If the *Torah* is not directing the field to the *kohanim*, one must conclude that the field remains in the hands of the person who redeemed it from *hekdesh*, and the *levi* retains the right to redeem it from him. Alternatively, it is conceivable that at *yovel* the field passes to the *kohanim*, and the *levi* would then have the right to redeem it from them. This possibility is not foreclosed, though it would represent a considerable stretch of the principle, since the *geulat olam* is framed as an override of what the *Torah* would otherwise prescribe. The *Gemara* does not resolve these details — and indeed, much of this area of *halacha* was not practiced and has not received the detailed treatment one finds in the great *masechtot* of *kodashim*. Accordingly, many of the particulars remain unspecified.

One further question left open is whether this right of redemption is inheritable. The *Gemara* had discussed previously whether the rights are limited to the *arei ha-leviim* specifically, and whether they attach to the *levi* personally or to the city's status. One opinion required both — that the seller be a *levi* and the property be in an *ir leviim*; the other required only that the property be in an *ir leviim*. Whether a *levi*'s son could inherit his father's right to redeem — a question distinct from whether a completely unrelated *levi* could redeem another *levi*'s field — remains an interesting open question that the *Gemara* does not address.

### Geulat Olam Houses

The third *chiddush* concerns houses in walled cities. The verse states *ve-kam ha-bayit asher ba-ir asher lo chomah litzmitut* — if a house in a walled city is not redeemed within the year, it passes permanently to the buyer. One might have thought this rule applies to a *levi*'s house in a walled city as well, such that after the year the house is lost forever. Therefore the *Torah* teaches *geulat olam tihyeh laleviim*: even after the year has passed, the *levi* may redeem. And notably, unlike the case of a house in an unwalled city — where the *Gemara* elsewhere grappled with a gap period between the second year and certain transitional stages — here the *Gemara* understands the rule to be unqualified: across the board, regardless of what year it is, the *levi* may always redeem.

### Walled City Problem

Having stated this third *chiddush*, the *Gemara* pauses to ask a sharp question. The first two *chiddushim* — regarding fields and sanctification — are perfectly comprehensible: the scenarios are real and the *chiddushim* meaningful. But the third raises a difficulty. *Bishlama hani tarti lachai* — the first two are fine, but as for *battei arei chomah* for *leviim*, how would a *levi* ever come to have a house in a walled city in the first place? This problem requires resolution, and the *Gemara* immediately turns to it.

## Walled City Definition

### Cities of Refuge

To understand why a *levi*'s owning a house in a walled city is problematic, the *Gemara* cites a *baraita* from Maseches Makkos. The *baraita* states that the *arei miklat* — the cities of refuge — may not be made too small, for the accidental killer who is exiled there must have adequate provisions and must remain within its boundaries; a tiny village would be insufficient and would force him to venture out. Nor, however, may the *arei miklat* be major metropolises. Rashi explains that in a very large city — like a great urban center — it is impossible to track the movements of everyone within; the *goel ha-dam* could too easily slip in undetected and kill the inadvertent killer. Therefore the *arei miklat* must be *arim beinoniyot* — medium-sized towns, neither too small nor too large.

The implicit corollary, though not stated explicitly in the *baraita*, is that *arei miklat* must be unwalled: walls are the hallmark of major fortified cities. This means that if the *levi* cities served as *arei miklat*, as they did according to the account in Yehoshua, they would by definition be unwalled. And if they are unwalled, how could a *levi* ever have a house with the legal status of a *bayit b'ir chomah* — a house in a walled city?

### Wall Precedence

Rav Kahana says: *lo kashya* — this is not difficult. There are two distinct scenarios: one where the wall preceded the settlement (*hukaf u-levasof yashav*), and one where the settlement preceded the wall (*yashav u-levasof hukaf*). His suggestion is that a *levi* city could have first been designated as a city and then had a wall built around it afterward. But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this resolution.

### Non-Jewish Walls

The *Gemara* objects: does a wall built after settlement even count as a wall (*mi havei chomah*)? A *baraita* is cited: the verse *ve-ish ki yimkor beit moshav ir chomah* — when a man sells a house in a walled city — means specifically a city that was *hukaf u-levasof yashav*, walled first and then settled. A city settled first and then walled does not qualify. The *baraita* continues: one might think that even if Jews built the wall after the conquest of the land, that should suffice to confer the status of *ir chomah*. The *Gemara* responds with a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *chomah* is used here in the context of walled-city laws, and the same word *chomah* appears in the description of the sixty walled cities in *Ever HaYardem* that were conquered. Just as there the walls were built by non-Jews, so too here, the qualifying wall must be one built by non-Jews.

### Pre-Conquest Standard

A further refinement follows. One might propose that the non-Jewish wall need not predate the conquest — perhaps a non-Jewish wall built *after* the Israelites entered the land would also qualify. The *Gemara* again applies the same *gezeirah shavah*: just as by the cities in *Ever HaYarden* the wall predated the Israelite arrival, so too here, the wall must have preceded the conquest. The upshot is stark: the only cities that carry the *halacha* of *battei arei chomah* are those that, at the time of Yehoshua's conquest, already had walls built by non-Jews, and in which the walls had preceded the city's settlement — walls that stood for generations before the city grew up within them.

This tightens the problem considerably. The *arei miklat* were designated at the time of Yehoshua; the only *batei arei chomah* are cities walled in the time of Yehoshua. So how could there exist a city that was simultaneously designated as an *ir miklat* — which requires being unwalled — and also qualifies as an *ir chomah*? One might suggest *Yericho* as a candidate, since it did famously have a wall that then fell. But the *Gemara* does not pursue this line.

### Refuge Then Walled

Rav Yosef son of Rav Sala Chasida, speaking before Rav Pappa, offers a resolution: *kegon she-naflu lahem hen u-migresheihem* — it is a case where a city and its surrounding *migrash* fell to the *leviim* by lot. The word *naflu* here does not mean that the walls fell; it means that the city fell to them by lottery when the *arei miklat* were being assigned. In other words, in the random assignment of *arei miklat* among the *levi* cities, a city happened to be chosen that had a wall — one that had been walled by non-Jews before settlement, and thus qualified as an *ir chomah*. Since an *ir miklat* must not be a walled city, the plan would have been to knock down the wall. The *Gemara* notes that the *migrash* perimeter was maintained, but the wall surrounding it was slated for demolition.

### Wall Knockdown

Rav Ashi clarifies why the *pasuk*'s ruling is still necessary even within this scenario. One might have thought: until the wall is actually knocked down, if a *levi* sells his house during that interim period, the house would carry the full status of a house sold in a walled city and would be forfeited permanently after the year. Therefore the *Torah* teaches *geulat olam tihyeh laleviim* — even during that transitional window, before the wall has been demolished, the *levi* retains the right to redeem his house at any time.

It is worth noting that Tosafos raises a pointed question here: we have established that for the purposes of *battei arei chomah*, the status of a walled city is fixed from the time of Yehoshua and does not change even if the wall subsequently falls down. If so, it would seem that once the wall is knocked down, the house would *still* retain the *ir chomah* status — in which case the scenario of the interim period before demolition would have been a perfectly natural answer to the original question. For some reason, the *Gemara* does not pursue that argument. Tosafos flags the difficulty, but the *Gemara* chooses to present the ruling through Rav Ashi's narrower formulation.

## Sanctified Kohen Fields

### Non-Levitical Field

The *Gemara* now introduces a new *baraita*: *kisdeh ha-cherem lakohen tihyeh achuzato* — it shall be to him like a *cherem* field belonging to a *kohen*. This formulation echoes a *Gemara* encountered earlier in the tractate almost word for word, but it is being revisited here in the context of *kohanim* and *leviim*, since the preceding discussion concerned their special rights regarding fields and sanctification.

To situate the discussion properly, it is necessary to distinguish two categories of permanent *kohen* fields. The first is the *arei ha-leviim* — the cities of the *leviim*, with their surrounding fields, which are the subject of the *geulat olam* rule. That case is already resolved: if a *levi* or *kohen* sells or sanctifies property in the *arei ha-leviim*, the right to redeem is always retained and the property does not pass permanently to others.

The second category is a field that a *kohen* received permanently, but *not* by virtue of the *arei ha-leviim*. How does such a situation arise? An Israelite could declare a field *cherem*, whereupon it passes to the *kohanim*; or an Israelite could sanctify his *sedeh achuza*, another Israelite redeems it from *hekdesh*, and then at *yovel* it is distributed to the *kohanim*. In either case, the *kohen* now holds a field permanently — it will not revert to any prior owner — but it is not part of the *arei ha-leviim*. This is the *kohen*'s *sedeh charmo* (his field acquired through a *cherem*), and it constitutes its own legal category.

The *baraita* now asks: if a *kohen* sanctifies this *sedeh charmo*, what is the result? One might have thought the answer is obvious from analogy to the *arei ha-leviim* rule. But the *baraita* signals that a distinct treatment applies, and the question is precisely what law governs sanctification of a *kohen*'s permanent field that falls outside the *arei ha-leviim* framework.

### Kohen's Claim

At *yovel*, a *sedeh achuza* of an ordinary Israelite that was sanctified and not redeemed is distributed among the *kohanim*. When a *kohen* sanctifies his own *sedeh charmo*, the question arises whether the same fate befalls it. And here the *kohen* advances a claim: the verse states *vehaya hasadeh betzeito ba-yovel kodesh l'Hashem kesedeh ha-cherem* — at *yovel* the field becomes sanctified to God like the *cherem* field and goes to the *kohanim*. But the *kohen* argues: *v'harei hi tachat yadi* — it is already in my hands. It will go to *kohanim* at *yovel* regardless; I am a *kohen*; I should simply retain it.

He frames this as a logical argument: *beshel acherim ani zocheh, besheli lo kol sheken* — if I am entitled to receive a share in other people's fields when they are distributed at *yovel*, how much more so should I simply retain a field that was originally my own.

The *Torah* responds by teaching the parallel to the *sedeh achuza* of the Israelite: just as the Israelite's sanctified field, when redeemed by another and then reaching *yovel*, leaves his hands and is distributed equally among all the *kohanim*, so too the *kohen*'s *sedeh charmo* that he sanctified leaves his hands and is distributed equally among his fellow *kohanim* — *yotzei miyado u-mitchalek le-echav ha-kohanim*. He has no special status with respect to that field.

This parallel serves an important structural purpose. It places in sharp relief the fundamental distinction between the *kohen*'s *sedeh charmo* and the *arei leviim*. Property in the *arei leviim* — whether sold or sanctified — is subject to *geulat olam*: the *levi* or *kohen* can always redeem, and it does not transfer permanently. The *sedeh charmo* — though it is the *kohen*'s permanently, and though it did not originate in the *arei leviim* — is treated for purposes of sanctification exactly like the *sedeh achuza* of an ordinary Israelite.

### Cherem Comparison

The *baraita* asks: why does the verse invoke the imagery of a *sedeh ha-cherem* when it speaks of what happens to the Israelite's sanctified *sedeh achuza* at *yovel*? The connection seems to go in the direction of the *cherem* field teaching something to the *sedeh achuza*. But *harei zeh ba lilamed ve-nimtza lamed* — the *cherem* field appears to come to teach yet ends up being taught. The *hekesh* — the Scriptural analogy — runs in both directions, or rather, the verse connects the *kohen*'s *sedeh charmo* to the Israelite's *sedeh achuza*: *makish sdeh charmo l-sdeh achuza shel Yisrael*. The law governing each is identical. Just as the Israelite's *sedeh achuza* that was sanctified, redeemed, and then reached *yovel* leaves his control and is divided among the *kohanim*, so too the *kohen*'s *sedeh charmo*, if sanctified, leaves his control at *yovel* and is divided equally among all the *kohanim*. The *kohen* who held the field has no greater share than any of his colleagues.

### Group vs Individual

The *Gemara* then returns to scrutinize the *kohen*'s argument. His reasoning was: since I receive a share of other people's fields at *yovel*, I should certainly receive my own field in its entirety. The *Gemara* objects: *mi dami* — are these comparable? *Hatam zocheh be'alma* — there, you receive a portion as part of a group; you are one of many *kohanim* who together inherit the distributed field. *Hacha ka shakil lei* — here, you want to take the entire field for yourself alone. Being entitled as a member of a group of a thousand to a fractional share of another's field is no basis for claiming sole individual ownership of your own field. The argument fails on its own terms.

### Korbanot Analogy

Rami bar Chama notes that despite the refutation, the *baraita*'s ruling remains necessary, because without it a *kohen* could have constructed a different argument. The verse states *ve-ish et kodashav lo yihiyu* — a man's sanctified offerings belong to him. This means a *kohen* who brings a *korban* to the *Beit HaMikdash* may declare himself the officiant for that offering and thereby retain the priestly portions. One might argue by analogy: just as a *kohen* can sanctify his *korban* and still benefit from it by performing the *avodah*, so too a *kohen* who sanctifies his field should be entitled to stand at the receiving end when the field is distributed at *yovel*.

The *Gemara* rejects this analogy: *mi dami? Kodashav lav birshuto, hacha birshuto* — your sacrificial portions are not yours in the ordinary sense of ownership; here, the field is fully yours. At first glance this seems to argue *in favor* of the *kohen*'s claim — the field started as his, so he should have greater rights, not fewer. But the *Gemara*'s point is more precise: by *korbanot*, the entitlement to benefit from the offering is grounded in having performed the *avodah*. The *kohen* brings his animal, performs the service, and by virtue of that service receives the meat. There is a functional basis for his receipt. Here, by contrast, once the *kohen* has sanctified the field, there is nothing that entitles him to be the one who receives it at the other end. His prior ownership provides no operative claim once he has removed the field from his domain through *hekdesh*. The analogy to *korbanot* therefore cannot sustain his argument.

### Acharit Olam

Rav Nachman identifies a final, more fundamental argument that required the *Torah*'s explicit ruling to refute. The verse states *ka-achuzat olam hi lahem* — it is an eternal inheritance for them — referring to the *leviim* and *kohanim* with respect to their *arei leviim*. One might have reasoned as follows: since the *leviim* and *kohanim* have the principle of *geulat olam* operative for their *arei leviim*, perhaps that same principle should extend to everything they hold permanently — including the *sedeh charmo* and other fields they receive through *cherem* or through distributions at *yovel*. After all, these too are their permanent inheritance. If the *acharit olam* — the eternal inheritance — is the basis for *geulat olam* in *arei leviim*, should it not equally apply to all things they hold permanently?

Therefore the *Torah* teaches: *achuzato in, cherem lo* — the special rights of *geulat olam* apply to their genuine ancestral inheritance, the *arei leviim*, and not to property acquired through *cherem* or through the *yovel* distributions. Fields that *kohanim* receive permanently through *cherem* or similar mechanisms have the same legal status as a *sedeh achuza* of an ordinary Israelite. If sanctified, they leave the *kohen*'s control at *yovel* and are distributed equally among all the *kohanim*.

This final point brings the *perek* to a clean and satisfying conclusion. The organizing distinction throughout has been between the *arei leviim* — the true, Divinely allocated inheritance of the tribe — and everything else that *kohanim* and *leviim* may come to hold permanently. Only the former carries the privilege of *geulat olam*. All else follows the ordinary rules applicable to any Israelite's *sedeh achuza*.

*Hadran alakh ha-mocher sadehu, ve-slik lah Masekhet Arakhin.*
