# Avodah Zarah 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sun Standing Still

### Sefer HaYashar Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיִּדֹּם הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ וְיָרֵחַ עָמָד עַד יִקֹּם גּוֹי אֹיְבָיו הֲלֹא הִיא כְתוּבָה עַל סֵפֶר הַיָּשָׁר״, מַאי סֵפֶר הַיָּשָׁר?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a series of expositions with regard to what is called the book of Yashar. The verse states: **"And the sun stood still, and the moon stayed, until the nation had avenged themselves of their enemies. Is this not written in the book of Yashar"?** (Joshua 10:13). The Gemara asks: **What is the book of Yashar?**

The daf opens mid-discussion of miraculous episodes connected to earlier topics — specifically the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani — and continues with a series of *drashot* on scriptural passages. The first of these concerns the miracle of the sun standing still for Yehoshua during his war against the kings of Canaan. The relevant verse reads: *vayidom hashemesh vayarei'ach amad ad yikom goy oyvav* — the sun was still and the moon stood in place until the nation took vengeance from its enemies — and the text adds: *halo hi k'tuvah al Sefer HaYashar* — behold it is written in the Book of the Upright.

The Gemara immediately asks: *mai Sefer HaYashar* — what is this Book of the Upright?

### Sefer HaYashar Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זֶה סֵפֶר אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב, שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ יְשָׁרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "תָּמֹת נַפְשִׁי מוֹת יְשָׁרִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is Genesis, which is the book of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who were called righteous [*yesharim*], as it is stated: "Let me die the death of the righteous [*yesharim*]"** (Numbers 23:10).

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *zeh sefer Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov* — this is the book of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, meaning Sefer Bereishit, *shenikru yesharim* — for the Patriarchs were called *yesharim*, upright. The proof text is Bilam's declaration: *tamut nafshi mot yesharim* — let my soul die the death of the upright. Since that verse praises Bnei Yisrael and refers to individuals of distinguished righteousness, it presumably refers to the Patriarchs themselves.

### Ephraim Blessing Hint

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא רְמִיזָא? "וְזַרְעוֹ יִהְיֶה מְלֹא הַגּוֹיִם״, [אֵימָתַי יִהְיֶה מְלֹא הַגּוֹיִם?] בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָמְדָה לוֹ חַמָּה לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where is it alluded to in Genesis that the sun would stand still for Joshua? The verse states in reference to Ephraim, who was Joshua's ancestor: "And his seed shall become a multitude of nations"** (Genesis 48:19). The Gemara asks: **When will he become a multitude of nations? He became a multitude of nations at the time when the sun stood in place for Joshua,**

The Gemara then asks: *v'heicha r'miza* — if the miracle of the sun standing still for Yehoshua was indeed recorded in *Sefer HaYashar*, where exactly is it hinted in Bereishit? The answer is found in the blessing of Ephraim: *v'zaro yihye mlo hagoyim* — his descendants shall be the fullness of the nations. Yehoshua descended from Ephraim. *Eimatai yihye mlo hagoyim* — when would Ephraim's descendant fill the world? *B'sha'ah she'amdah lo chamma l'Yehoshua* — at the hour the sun stood still for Yehoshua, for then all the nations came to know of this miracle.

### Two Standing Moments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיַּעֲמֹד הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ בַּחֲצִי הַשָּׁמַיִם וְלֹא אָץ לָבוֹא כְּיוֹם תָּמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** as it is written: **"And the sun stayed in the midst of heaven, and hastened not to go down for an entire day"** (Joshua 10:13).

Returning to the miracle itself, the Gemara draws attention to two distinct moments described in the passage: *vaya'amod hashemesh b'chatzi hashamayim* — the sun stood at the center of the sky, meaning at midday — and *v'lo atz lavo k'yom tamim* — and it did not descend like a full day. The Gemara understands that the sun paused both at the height of noon and again at the point of setting, and asks: *v'kamma* — how long, in total, did it stand still?

### 24-Hour Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע [שָׁעֵי], אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם שֵׁית, אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם שֵׁית, כּוּלַּהּ מִלְּתָא כְּיוֹם תָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **And how much time elapsed before the sun finally set? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said that it took twenty-four hours: It traveled across the sky for six hours and stood still for six hours, and again traveled six hours and stood still for six hours, so that the entire matter lasted the duration of an entire day.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: twenty-four hours in total. The sun traveled six hours to reach midday, stood for six hours, traveled another six, and then stood again for six before setting. The entire passage of the sun across the sky — what would normally have been twelve hours — took twenty-four. This is the meaning of *k'yom tamim*: the whole event lasted a full day.

### 36-Hour Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשֵׁת, אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם תְּרֵיסַר, אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם תְּרֵיסַר, עֲמִידָתוֹ כְּיוֹם תָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said that it lasted thirty-six hours: The sun traveled for six hours and stood still for twelve hours, and again traveled six hours and stood for twelve hours, so that the accumulated time of its suspension was that of an entire day.**

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees and says the entire day was thirty-six hours. In his reading, the sun traveled six hours, stood for twelve, traveled another six, and then stood again for twelve at the point of setting. *Amidato k'yom tamim* — the standing itself was like a full day, meaning twenty-four hours of standing in total, on top of the normal twelve hours of travel, giving thirty-six.

### 48-Hour Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה, אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם תְּרֵיסַר, אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבְּעָה, [שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר]: "וְלֹא אָץ לָבוֹא כְּיוֹם תָּמִים״, מִכְּלָל דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָאו כְּיוֹם תָּמִים [הֲוָה].
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said: Forty-eight hours elapsed before it set. It traveled six hours and stood for twelve hours, and then traveled six hours and stood for twenty-four more hours. It is with regard to the second suspension that the verse states: "And hastened not to go down for an entire day." By inference, it can be understood that initially it was not suspended for an entire day.**

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani says the entire day was forty-eight hours. The sun traveled six, stood twelve, traveled six, and then stood twenty-four before setting — *v'lo atz lavo k'yom tamim* — it did not move to set for a full day, meaning twenty-four hours. Since the verse implies the standing before setting was the full *yom tamim* of twenty-four hours, and the earlier standing was a separate and distinct episode, we infer it was half that — twelve hours. The two *pesukim* are in parallel: *vaya'amod b'chatzi* and *v'lo atz lavo k'yom tamim* — and Rashi attempts to explain how the derivation of twelve hours for the midday standing follows from that parallel, though the inference is admittedly not entirely explicit.

### Tosefta Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, בְּתוֹסֶפְתָּא פְּלִיגִי: רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבְּעָה — אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם תְּרֵיסַר, אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם תְּרֵיסַר, עֲמִידָתוֹ כְּיוֹם תָּמִים. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: שְׁלֹשִׁים וָשֵׁשׁ — אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם תְּרֵיסַר, אֲזַל שֵׁית וְקָם עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבְּעָה, "וְלֹא אָץ לָבוֹא כְּיוֹם תָּמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **There are those who say that these Sages do not disagree over how much time had elapsed before the sun set. Rather, they disagree with regard to the additional time by which the day was extended. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: Twenty-four hours were added to that day, because the sun traveled six hours and stood twelve hours, and again traveled six hours and stood for another twelve hours, which meant that its standing time lasted for an entire day. Rabbi Elazar said that thirty-six hours were added: It traveled six hours and stood for twelve hours, and then traveled six hours and stood for twenty-four more hours. It is with regard to the second suspension that the verse states: "And hastened not to go down for an entire day."**

A second version of this debate is introduced: *ika d'amrei b'Tosefta* — some say, according to a *Tosefta*, that the figures of twenty-four, thirty-six, and forty-eight were not the total length of the day but the *additional* hours beyond the normal twelve. Under this reading, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's twenty-four means the sun stood an extra twelve at midday and an extra twelve before setting — *amidaso k'yom tamim*, the total standing amounted to a full day — giving thirty-six hours altogether for the day. Rabbi Eliezer's thirty-six means an additional twelve at midday and twenty-four before setting, for a total of forty-eight hours in the day. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani's forty-eight means an additional twenty-four at each point — *makish amida l'vi'ah*, the standing at midday is equated with the standing at setting, both being twenty-four hours — giving a total of sixty hours for the entire day. Every opinion accordingly yields a number twelve hours larger than its counterpart in the first version of the debate.

## Moshe & Yehoshua Miracles

### Three Recipients

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁעָמְדָה לוֹ חַמָּה לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ, כָּךְ עָמְדָה לוֹ חַמָּה לְמֹשֶׁה וּלְנַקְדִּימוֹן בֶּן גּוּרְיוֹן. יְהוֹשֻׁעַ — קְרָאֵי, נַקְדִּימוֹן בֶּן גּוּרְיוֹן — גְּמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught: Just as the sun stood still for Joshua, so too the sun stood still for Moses and for Nakdimon ben Guryon. It is known that it stood still for Joshua from a verse, and it is known that it stood still for Nakdimon ben Guryon by tradition.**

A *tanna* teaches: just as the sun stood still for Yehoshua, so too it stood still for Moshe and for Nakdimon ben Guryon. That the sun stood for Yehoshua is clear from the verses already cited. The miracle performed for Nakdimon ben Guryon is a tradition — a *gemara* — whose details are elaborated elsewhere, in Masechet Taanit. Nakdimon ben Guryon is, notably, a commoner in this context, not one of the great biblical figures, which makes his inclusion in this list striking. What requires derivation is the claim that a comparable miracle was performed for Moshe.

### Gezera Shava Links

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֹשֶׁה מְנָלַן? אָתְיָא "אָחֵל״ ״אָחֵל״: כְּתִיב הָכָא "אָחֵל תֵּת פַּחְדְּךָ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם בִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ "אָחֵל גַּדֶּלְךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** From where do we derive that it stood still for Moses as well? The Gemara answers: It is derived by means of a verbal analogy between the terms "I will begin" and "I will begin." The Gemara elaborates: It is written here, with regard to Moses: "I will begin to deliver the dread of you and the fear of you upon the peoples" (Deuteronomy 2:25), and it is written there, with regard to Joshua: "I will begin to magnify you"** (Joshua 3:7).

The Gemara asks: *l'Moshe minalan* — what is the source that the sun stood still for Moshe? The answer is a *gezera shava* linking Moshe to Yehoshua. The word *acheil* — I will begin — appears in the context of Moshe's war against Sichon king of Emori and Og king of Bashan: *acheil teis pachdicha* — I will begin to give your fear before the nations. The same word appears in the promise made to Yehoshua: *acheil gadlicha* — I will begin to make your name great. Since the same miraculous context applies to Yehoshua's *acheil*, the same applies to Moshe's.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר, אָתְיָא "תֵּת״ "תֵּת״: כְּתִיב הָכָא "אָחֵל תֵּת פַּחְדְּךָ״, וּכְתִיב בִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ "בְּיוֹם תֵּת ה׳ אֶת הָאֱמֹרִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: It is derived from a verbal analogy between the words "delivered" and "delivered." It is written here, with regard to Moses: "I will begin to deliver the dread of you," and it is written there, with regard to Joshua: "In the day when the Lord delivered up the Amorites before the children of Israel"** (Joshua 10:12).

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different *gezera shava*, based on the word *teis* — to give. By Moshe: *acheil teis pachdicha* — I will give your fear. By Yehoshua: *b'yom teis Hashem es haEmori* — on the day God gave the Emori before you. Since both verses use the same root in connection with miraculous divine intervention, the parallel miracle is derived from that shared language.

### Shmuel bar Nachmani

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר, מִגּוּפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ: "אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁמְעוּן שִׁמְעֲךָ וְרָגְזוּ וְחָלוּ מִפָּנֶיךָ״, אֵימָתַי "רָגְזוּ וְחָלוּ מִפָּנֶיךָ״? בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָמְדָה לוֹ חַמָּה לְמֹשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said that there is no need for a verbal analogy, as you can learn this idea from the verse itself:** "This day will I begin to deliver the dread of you and the fear of you upon the peoples that are under the whole heaven, who, when they hear the report of you, shall tremble and be in anguish because of you" (Deuteronomy 2:25). **When was the fulfillment of the prophecy that all the nations "shall tremble and be in anguish because of you"? It was at the time when the sun stood still for Moses.**

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani says there is no need for a *gezera shava* at all — the derivation can be made from the *pasuk* itself. The verse by Moshe concludes: *asher yishm'un shim'acha v'ragzu v'chalu mipanecha* — all who hear your report shall tremble and fear before you. The question is: *eimatai ragzu v'chalu mipanecha* — what event would cause that trembling and fear? It must be *b'sha'ah she'amdah lo chamma l'Moshe* — at the moment the sun stood still for Moshe. That fearsome celestial event is what made Moshe's reputation spread among the nations in the way the verse describes, paralleling precisely the miraculous effect the sun's standing had in the case of Yehoshua.

### Uniqueness Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: "וְלֹא הָיָה כַּיּוֹם הַהוּא לְפָנָיו וְאַחֲרָיו״! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: שָׁעוֹת הוּא דְּלָא הֲווֹ (נפיש) [נְפִישָׁן] כּוּלֵּי הַאי, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אַבְנֵי בָרָד לָא הֲווֹ, דִּכְתִיב: "וַיְהִי בְּנֻסָם מִפְּנֵי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל הֵם בְּמוֹרַד בֵּית חוֹרֹן וַה׳ הִשְׁלִיךְ עֲלֵיהֶם אֲבָנִים גְּדֹלוֹת מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם עַד עֲזֵקָה וַיָּמֻתוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: After the sun stood still, the verse states: "And there was no day like that before it or after it" (Joshua 10:14). If there was no day comparable to it, how can it be asserted that the sun stood still for Moses as well? The Gemara answers: If you wish, say that the number of hours the sun stood still for Moses were not as many in the case of Joshua. And if you wish, say instead: When the sun stood still for Moses there were no hailstones, whereas in the case of Joshua there were hailstones, as it is written: "And it came to pass, as they fled from before the children of Israel, while they were in the descent of Beth Horon, that the Lord cast down great stones from heaven upon them into Azeka and they died"** (Joshua 10:11).

The Gemara raises a challenge: *v'lo haya k'yom hahu lifanav v'acharav* — there was no day like that one before it or after it. The verse by Yehoshua explicitly states that this miracle was unprecedented. Yet here we are saying that Moshe preceded Yehoshua with a similar miracle, and Nakdimon ben Guryon followed him with another. How can it be called unprecedented?

Two answers are offered. First: *sha'os hu d'lo havo nefishei kulei hai* — the uniqueness was in the number of hours. Yes, the sun stood for others, but for no one as long as it stood for Yehoshua. Second, and perhaps more telling: *avnei barad lo havo* — the hailstones were not present for Moshe. By Yehoshua, the miracle was not merely the sun standing still; it was accompanied by a miraculous barrage of great stones from the heavens: *v'Hashem hishlich aleihem avanim gedolos min hashamayim* — God hurled great stones upon them at the descent of Beit Choron until Azeka, and they died. That combined miracle — sun and hailstones together — was never replicated, neither before nor after. The Gemara thus preserves the uniqueness of Yehoshua's miracle while allowing the tradition that the sun had also stood still, in some form, for others. One senses the Gemara is navigating a delicate point: it does not wish to entirely overshadow Moshe, yet it allows Yehoshua his moment of singular greatness, as the verses indeed present it.

## Yehudah Archery

### Archery Teaching Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב "וַיֹּאמֶר לְלַמֵּד בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה קָשֶׁת הִנֵּה כְתוּבָה עַל סֵפֶר הַיָּשָׁר״, מַאי "סֵפֶר הַיָּשָׁר״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another exposition with regard to the book of Yashar. **It is written: "And said: To teach the sons of Judah the bow. Behold, it is written in the book of Yashar"** (II Samuel 1:18). The Gemara again asks: **What is the book of Yashar?**

The Gemara now turns to another passage that mentions *Sefer HaYashar*, this one from Shmuel Bet: *vayomer l'lamed b'nei Yehudah kashet hinei k'tuvah al Sefer HaYashar* — and he said to teach the children of Yehudah archery; behold it is written in the Book of the Upright. Again the Gemara asks: *mai Sefer HaYashar* — what is this book? The same question that arose in the context of Yehoshua's miracle now recurs, and the three Amoraim offer three different answers.

### Yochanan: Bereishit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זֶה סֵפֶר אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב, שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ "יְשָׁרִים״, דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ "תָּמֹת נַפְשִׁי מוֹת יְשָׁרִים וּתְהִי אַחֲרִיתִי כָּמוֹהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is the book of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, who were called righteous [*yesharim*], as it is written with regard to them: "Let me die the death of the righteous [*yesharim*] and let my end be like his"** (Numbers 23:10).

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *zeh sefer Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov* — this is Sefer Bereishit, as before, since the Patriarchs were called *yesharim*. The supporting verse is the same: *tamut nafshi mot yesharim*. The question then follows: *v'heicha r'miza* — where is it hinted in Bereishit that the children of Yehudah would be archers?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא רְמִיזָא? "יְהוּדָה אַתָּה יוֹדוּךָ אַחֶיךָ יָדְךָ בְּעֹרֶף אֹיְבֶיךָ״, וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא מִלְחָמָה שֶׁצְּרִיכָה יָד כְּנֶגֶד עוֹרֶף? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זוֹ קֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further analyzes the verse in Samuel: **And where is it alluded to in Genesis that Judah must be taught to use the bow? The verse states: "Judah, your brothers shall praise you; your hand shall be on the nape of your enemies"** (Genesis 49:8). The Gemara explains: **What is the form of warfare that requires one's hand to be held opposite the nape? You must say that this is referring to the use of a bow.**

The answer is found in the blessing Yaakov gave Yehudah: *Yehudah atah yoducha achecha* — Yehudah, your brothers will praise you — and: *yadcha b'oref oyvecha* — your hand is against the neck of your enemy. The Gemara asks: *v'eizu hi milchamah she'tzrichah yad k'neged oref* — what type of warfare requires the hand to be against the back of the neck? The answer: archery. When drawing a bow, the hand that pulls the string back reaches behind, toward the nape of one's own neck. Thus *yadcha b'oref* alludes to the archer's draw, and this is the hint in Bereishit that archery is Yehudah's domain.

### Eliezer: Devarim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: זֶה סֵפֶר מִשְׁנֵה תּוֹרָה, וְאַמַּאי קָרוּ לֵיהּ "סֵפֶר הַיָּשָׁר״? דִּכְתִיב: "וְעָשִׂיתָ הַיָּשָׁר וְהַטּוֹב בְּעֵינֵי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a different interpretation: **Rabbi Elazar says that the book of Yashar is the book of Deuteronomy. And why did they call it the book of Yashar? As it is written: "And you shall do that which is right [*yashar*] and good in the sight of the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 6:18).

Rabbi Eliezer says: *zeh Sefer Mishneh Torah* — here, *Sefer HaYashar* refers to Devarim. Why is Devarim called the book of the *yashar*? Because Devarim opens the moral mandate with the words *v'asita hayashar v'hatov* — you shall do what is right and good in the eyes of God. The *yashar* is thus the defining theme of that book.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא רְמִיזָא? "יָדָיו רַב לוֹ״, וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא מִלְחָמָה שֶׁצְּרִיכָה שְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זוֹ קֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **And where is it alluded to in Deuteronomy that Judah must be taught to use the bow? The verse states: "And this for Judah, and he said: Hear, Lord, the voice of Judah, and bring him in unto his people; his hands shall contend for him"** (Deuteronomy 33:7). **What is the form of warfare that requires the use of two hands? You must say that this is referring to the use of a bow.**

The hint to archery in Devarim is found in the blessing of Yehudah there: *yadav rav lo v'ezer mitzarav tihyeh* — his hands shall be great for him and help shall come against his enemies. The word *yadav* is in the plural — his hands. The Gemara asks: *v'eizu hi milchamah she'tzrichah shtei yadayim* — what warfare requires both hands? *Hevei omer zeh kashet* — that is archery, since one hand holds the bow and the other draws the string.

### Shimon: Shoftim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: זֶה סֵפֶר שׁוֹפְטִים, וְאַמַּאי קָרוּ לֵיהּ "סֵפֶר הַיָּשָׁר״? דִּכְתִיב: "בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם אֵין מֶלֶךְ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל אִישׁ הַיָּשָׁר בְּעֵינָיו יַעֲשֶׂה״, וְהֵיכָא רְמִיזָא? "לְמַעַן דַּעַת דֹּרוֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לְלַמְּדָם מִלְחָמָה״, וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא מִלְחָמָה שֶׁצְּרִיכָה לִימּוּד? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זוֹ קֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides yet another explanation: **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says that the book of Yashar is the book of Judges. And why did they call it the book of Yashar? As it is written: "In those days there was no king in Israel; every man did that which was right [*yashar*] in his own eyes"** (Judges 17:6). **And where is it alluded to in Judges that Judah must be taught to use the bow? The verse states: "Only that the generations of the children of Israel might know, to teach them war"** (Judges 3:2). **What is the form of warfare that requires teaching? You must say that this is referring to the use of a bow.**

Rabbi Shimon bar Nachmani says: *zeh Sefer Shoftim* — *Sefer HaYashar* refers to the Book of Judges. The identification is, notably, ironic: Shoftim is called the book of the *yashar* because its recurring refrain is *ish hayashar b'einav ya'aseh* — every man would do what was right in his own eyes — precisely because there was no king and no central authority. The hint to archery in Shoftim comes from the verse: *l'maan da'at dorot b'nei Yisrael l'lamdam milchamah* — so that the generations of Yisrael would know, to teach them warfare. The Gemara asks: *v'eizu hi milchamah she'tzrichah limud* — what warfare requires actual training and instruction? *Hevei omer zeh kashet* — archery, since it demands a level of skill and practice that other forms of ancient combat apparently did not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּבִיהוּדָה כְּתִיב? דִּכְתִיב: "מִי יַעֲלֶה לָּנוּ בַתְּחִלָּה אֶל הַכְּנַעֲנִי לְהִלָּחֵם בּוֹ, וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ יְהוּדָה יַעֲלֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we derive that Judges 3:2 is written in reference to Judah? We derive it as it is written:** "The children of Israel asked the Lord, saying: **Who shall go up for us first against the Canaanites, to fight against them? And the Lord said: Judah shall go up"** (Judges 1:1–2).

One might then ask: the verse speaks of the children of Yisrael generally — how do we know it refers specifically to Yehudah? The answer is that Shoftim itself records the following: *mi ya'aleh lanu el haK'naani l'hilachem bo bat'chilah* — who shall go up first against the Canaanites to make war? *Vayomer Hashem Yehudah ya'aleh* — God said: Yehudah shall go first. Since Yehudah was designated as the vanguard of Israel's military campaigns, any general teaching about warfare in Shoftim is understood to refer primarily to Yehudah.

## Sacrificial Portions

### Portions Before Shaul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּרֶם הַטַּבָּח אֶת הַשּׁוֹק וְהֶעָלֶיהָ וַיָּשֶׂם לִפְנֵי שָׁאוּל״, מַאי ״וְהֶעָלֶיהָ״?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another dispute between the same *amora'im*. The verse states: **"And the cook took up the thigh, and that which was upon it [*vehe'aleha*], and set it before Saul"** (I Samuel 9:24). To **what does the term "and that which was upon it" refer?**

The Gemara now turns to a third dispute among the same Amoraim, this one concerning the interpretation of a verse in Shmuel Aleph describing the portions of meat set before Shaul: *vayarem hatabach et hashok v'ha'alyah vayasem lifnei Shaul* — the butcher raised the *shok* and the *alyah* and set them before Shaul. The question is: *mai v'ha'alyah* — what exactly is this *alyah*?

### Yochanan: Shok-Alyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹק וְאַלְיָה. מַאי ״וְהֶעָלֶיהָ״? דִּמְסַמְּכָא שׁוֹק לְאַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The verse refers to the thigh and the tail. The Gemara asks: What is the reason that the tail is referred to as: "And that which was upon it"? The reason is that the thigh supports the tail, as it rests upon it.**

Rabbi Yochanan says: *shok v'alyah* — it is literally the calf and the fat tail of the animal. The *alyah* in the verse is the *alyah*, the fatty appendage near the tail that is characteristic of certain breeds of sheep. Why does the verse use the form *ha'alyah* rather than simply listing both items? Because *aleha* — it sits upon the *shok*, adjacent to it. The reading thus yields: the *shok* along with the *alyah* that rests beside it.

### Eliezer: Shok-Chest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹק וְחָזֶה, מַאי ״וְהֶעָלֶיהָ״? דְּמַחֵית לֵהּ לְחָזֶה עִילָּוֵיהּ דְּשׁוֹק כִּי בָעֵי אֹנוֹפֵי, וּמֹנְפִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar says: The verse refers to the thigh and the breast. What is the reason that the breast is referred to as: "And that which was upon it"? The reason is that one places the breast upon the thigh when it requires sacrificial waving, and waves it.**

Rabbi Eliezer says: it is *shok v'chazeh* — the calf and the breast. What then is the *alyah* of the verse? It is the breast, *d'manach lechazeh iluyei d'shok* — because when performing *tenufah*, the waving of the *shelamim* offering, the breast was placed on top of the calf. *Alyah* thus means "what is elevated upon it" — the breast elevated atop the *shok* for the waving ceremony.

### Shimon: Shok-Shufi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: שׁוֹק וְשׁוֹפִי, מַאי ״וְהֶעָלֶיהָ״? שׁוֹפִי עִילָּוֵיהּ דְּשׁוֹק קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says: The verse refers to the thigh and the cap [*shofi*], i.e., the very top part of the leg. What is the reason that the cap is referred to as: "And that which was upon it"? It is because it is referring to the cap that stands above the thigh.**

Rav Shimon bar Nachmani says: *shok v'shufi* — the calf and the thigh, which Rashi identifies as a portion of the upper leg. Why *alyah*? Because *shufi iluyei d'shokai* — the thigh is anatomically above the calf. *Alyah*, on this reading, simply means what is higher up on the limb.

## Yichud Laws

### Woman Seclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא תִּתְיַיחֵד אִשָּׁה עִמָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **a woman may not seclude herself with** gentiles.

With the completion of these *drashot*, the Gemara returns to the *Mishna*. The *Mishna* rules: *lo tityached isha imahem* — a woman may not be secluded with non-Jews, out of concern for licentiousness — whether that means rape or seduction.

### Licentiousness vs Murder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּחַד, דִּכְוָותַהּ גַּבֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָתְנַן: ״לֹא יִתְיַיחֵד אִישׁ אֶחָד עִם שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are we dealing with? If we say that we are dealing with a woman who secludes herself with one gentile, is this permitted in the corresponding situation involving a Jew? But didn't we learn in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 80b): One man may not seclude himself even with two women?** It is certainly forbidden for one man to be secluded with one woman.

The Gemara asks: *bemai askinan* — what is the scenario? If we are speaking of a single non-Jewish man being alone with a Jewish woman, that would be prohibited even if the man were Jewish, since *yichud* with even a single Jewish man is forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בִּתְלָתָא. דִּכְוָותַהּ גַּבֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל, בִּפְרוּצִים מִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָתְנַן: ״אֲבָל אִשָּׁה אַחַת מִתְיַיחֶדֶת עִם שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים״, וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּכְשֵׁרִים, אֲבָל בִּפְרוּצִים אֲפִילּוּ עֲשָׂרָה נָמֵי לָא. מַעֲשֶׂה הָיָה וְהוֹצִיאוּהָ עֲשָׂרָה בְּמִטָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the mishna is referring to a case in which a woman secludes herself with three gentiles. The Gemara asks: Is this permitted in the corresponding situation involving Jews steeped in sexual immorality? But didn't we learn in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 80b): But one woman may be secluded with two men; and Rav Yehuda says that Shmuel says: They taught this *halakha* only with regard to men of fit morals; but with regard to those steeped in sexual immorality, she may not be secluded even with ten men. There was an incident where ten men carried a woman out of the city on a bier, as though she were dead, and they engaged in intercourse with her.**

And if we are speaking of multiple men, where the presence of multiple people might otherwise suffice to permit *yichud* in the case of Jewish men — that too is not straightforwardly resolved by their being Jewish, because even regarding Jews the law distinguishes between those who are *kesherim* and those who are *prutzim*, sexually loose. As the Gemara cites: *lo shanu ela bik'sherim* — this leniency applies only to upright men; *aval beprutzim afilu asura nami lo* — but with those who are dissolute, even ten men together with a woman would not suffice. And indeed, the Gemara records a troubling incident: *maaseh haya v'hotziu'ha asara bemitta* — it once happened that ten men smuggled a woman out in a manner suggesting the worst. The precise interpretation is debated — Rashi suggests the woman may have been a willing participant, though the image also evokes something far darker, and the incident itself reflects the broader question of whether the prohibition of *yichud* is primarily directed at seduction or at rape.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, צְרִיכָא בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ עִמּוֹ. גּוֹי אֵין אִשְׁתּוֹ מְשַׁמַּרְתּוֹ, אֲבָל יִשְׂרָאֵל אִשְׁתּוֹ מְשַׁמַּרְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No, the ruling of the mishna here is necessary in a case where the gentile's wife is with him. The difference between the two rulings is that in the case of a gentile, it is assumed that his wife does not guard him against sinning with another, but with regard to a Jew, his wife does guard him against sinning with another.**

In any case, the Gemara's challenge stands: if the concern in the *Mishna* is simply about being *prutzim*, that concern applies to Jewish men as well and is not distinctive to non-Jews. What distinguishes non-Jews?

The Gemara resolves this: *lo tzricha* — the ruling is needed for the case of *b'ishto imo* — where even a man who is *parutz* has his wife present with him. In that circumstance, we trust that the wife will serve as a check on her husband's behavior; the concern is mitigated. This would apply even to a *parutz* — if his wife is with him, another woman may be in *yichud* with them. But that assumption does not hold for a non-Jew: *oveid kochavim ein ishto m'shamarato* — we do not trust a non-Jewish man's wife to keep him in check. *Aval Yisrael ishto m'shamarato* — but for a Jewish man, his wife keeps him in line. It is therefore specifically in that borderline case — where the wife's presence would ordinarily resolve the concern — that the *Mishna* needed to specify that a non-Jewish man's wife does not provide the same protection.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בְּאִשָּׁה חֲשׁוּבָה עָסְקִינַן, דְּמִירַתְתִי מִינַּהּ. רַב אִידִי אָמַר: אִשָּׁה כְּלֵי זַיְינָהּ עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that a woman may not seclude herself with a gentile due to the concern that they might engage in illicit sexual relations. The Gemara challenges: **And let us derive that a woman may not seclude herself with a gentile due to** the concern about **bloodshed. The Gemara cites two responses. Rabbi Yirmeya says: We are dealing with a distinguished woman, whom gentiles will not kill, as they are fearful of her status. Nevertheless, there is a concern with regard to illicit sexual relations. Rav Idi bar Avin says: A woman carries her weapons upon her.** In other words, there is no need to be concerned that a woman might be killed by gentiles; rather, the very fact that she is a woman protects her, as they would most likely rape her and not kill her.

This resolves the licentiousness concern. But the Gemara then raises a further question: *v'tipok lei mishum shfichus damim* — why specify the concern about sexual misconduct at all? The next part of the *Mishna* will prohibit a man from being in *yichud* with a non-Jew out of concern for murder. Should that concern not extend equally to a woman? Why is the ruling about a woman framed around licentiousness rather than the fear of being killed?

### Important Woman Status

Rabbi Yirmiyah answers: *b'isha chashuva askinan* — we are speaking of a woman of status and importance. Such a woman would not face a realistic concern of being murdered, because the non-Jew would fear the repercussions of harming someone whose death would draw the attention of the authorities. Therefore, the murder concern that applies to an ordinary person simply does not apply here. Yet even an important woman still faces a concern of sexual misconduct — perhaps seduction rather than outright rape (which, as Rashi notes, might itself be deterred by fear of consequences), but seduction remains a real risk regardless of her social standing. Hence the *Mishna* frames the prohibition around that concern rather than murder.

### Rav Idi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ: אִשָּׁה חֲשׁוּבָה בֵּין אֲנָשִׁים, וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ חֲשׁוּבָה בֵּין הַנָּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the practical difference between these two answers? The Gemara answers: The difference between them is with regard to a woman who is distinguished among men due to her political position, but is not distinguished among the women, i.e., she is unattractive. According to Rabbi Yirmeya, there is no concern that she will be killed, as she is distinguished, nor is there a concern that they will engage in illicit sexual relations. Therefore, it would be permitted for her to seclude herself with them. By contrast, as Rav Idi bar Avin stated his explanation in general terms, he evidently holds that the concern of illicit sexual relations always applies, regardless of the woman's appearance.**

Rav Idi offers a different framing: *isha klei zayna aleha* — a woman's weapons are upon her. Rashi and Tosafot offer somewhat different readings of this phrase. Rashi understands it to mean that non-Jews will not be interested in killing a woman — they see her not as a threat to be eliminated but as a potential object of sexual desire. Tosafot cites a *Yerushalmi* with an additional explanation: a woman can claim to be a non-Jew herself, whereas a Jewish man's circumcision would expose him. Since non-Jews would have no reason to kill someone they take to be one of their own, murder is not a realistic concern for a woman in the way it is for a man. This is not a contextual ruling dependent on the woman's status — it is a categorical statement that murder is simply never the operative concern when a woman is in *yichud* with non-Jews. The only concern is always *arayot*.

### Status Implications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: הָאִשָּׁה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהַשָּׁלוֹם עִמָּהּ, לֹא תִּתְיַיחֵד עִמָּהֶן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֲשׁוּדִין עַל הָעֲרָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that it **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Idi bar Avin:** With regard to **a woman, even though her protection** accompanies **her,** i.e., she is not in danger of being killed, she **may not seclude herself with** gentiles **because they are suspected of engaging in forbidden sexual relations.**

This difference between Rabbi Yirmiyah and Rav Idi has practical implications, and the Gemara spells them out: *ika beinaihu isha chashuva bein hanashim v'she'eina chashuva bein hanashim*.

Following Tosafot's reading: the first case is an important woman who is among men. For Rabbi Yirmiyah, this was precisely the case of the *Mishna* — since she is important, there is no murder concern, but there is a sexual misconduct concern, hence *yichud* is forbidden. For Rav Idi, who says murder is categorically not a concern for any woman, the question would then turn on whether an important woman's status might also dispel the concern about sexual misconduct — since both rape and seduction may be deterred when the woman commands sufficient standing. If so, Rav Idi might permit *yichud* for an important woman with men, a leniency Rabbi Yirmiyah would not grant.

The second case is a woman of no particular status who would be in *yichud* among women. For Rabbi Yirmiyah, since she is not *chashuva*, the concern of murder is still present, and women may kill her as much as men might. He would therefore prohibit even *yichud* among women. For Rav Idi, murder is never a concern for a woman regardless of her status, and women among women present no concern of *arayot* either — so he would permit it.

The Gemara then cites a *baraita* that supports Rav Idi's position: *ha'isha af al pi she-shalom ima lo tityached imahen mipnei she-chashudin al ha-arayot* — a woman, even though there is peace with them, may not have *yichud* with non-Jews because they are suspect regarding *arayot*. The phrase *af al pi she-shalom ima* — even though there is peace with her — implies categorically that a woman is not in danger of being killed; and yet *yichud* remains forbidden because of *arayot*. This confirms Rav Idi's principle: for women, it is always and only the concern of sexual misconduct that drives the prohibition, never murder.

## Encounter Precautions

### Positioning Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יִתְיַיחֵד אָדָם עִמָּהֶן. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּזְדַּמֵּן לוֹ גּוֹי בַּדֶּרֶךְ — טוֹפְלוֹ לִימִינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches that **a person may not seclude himself with** gentiles because they are suspected of bloodshed. **The Sages taught: A Jew who encounters a gentile on the road and continues his travels with him should position the gentile to his right,** so that the Jew's dominant hand is closest to the gentile, which will enable him to defend himself against a potential attack.

The Gemara now moves to the next part of the *Mishna*, which deals with a man encountering a non-Jew while traveling. The concern here is *shfichus damim* — the possibility that the non-Jew will attempt to kill him. The *baraita* provides practical guidance: if a Jew encounters a non-Jew on the road, *toflo limino* — he should position him to his right.

### Weapon Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: בְּסַיִיף — טוֹפְלוֹ לִימִינוֹ, בְּמַקֵּל — טוֹפְלוֹ לִשְׂמֹאלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says:** If the gentile is armed **with a sword, the Jew positions him to his right,** because a blade is usually carried on one's left side. But if the gentile is armed **with a stick, the Jew should steer him to his left,** as a stick is usually carried in one's right hand. By walking on the same side as the gentile's weapon, the Jew can best prevent him from drawing it.

Rav Shimon, son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, elaborates on why and how. If the non-Jew is carrying a sword, *toflo limino* — keep him to your right. The reason: a right-handed person wears his sword on his left side and draws it with his right hand crossing to the left. If the non-Jew walks on your right, his sword-bearing left side is adjacent to you, and any motion of his right arm to draw will be visible and interceptable. But if he carries a stick — *b'makkel toflo lismolo* — keep him to your left. A stick is held in the dominant hand, the right. If he walks to your left, his stick arm is oriented toward you, and you can more readily block or deflect it. The logic in both cases is the same: position the non-Jew so that the weapon he is most likely to use is on the side where you can most easily monitor and counter it.

### Elevation Advantage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ עוֹלִין בְּמַעֲלֶה אוֹ יוֹרְדִין בִּירִידָה, לֹא יְהֵא יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַטָּה וְגוֹי לְמַעְלָה, אֶלָּא יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַעְלָה וְגוֹי לְמַטָּה, וְאַל יָשׁוּחַ לְפָנָיו, שֶׁמָּא יָרוֹץ אֶת גּוּלְגׇּלְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: If the Jew and gentile were ascending an incline or descending a decline, the Jew should not be positioned below while the gentile is above, so that the gentile will not have the advantage of height if he decides to attack. Rather, the Jew should be located above while the gentile is below. And the Jew should not bend down before him, lest the gentile break his skull.

When traversing an incline or a descent, *lo yehei Yisrael lemata v'oved kochavim lemala* — the Jew should not be in the lower position while the non-Jew is above, whether ascending or descending. Rather, the Jew should maintain the higher position. Rashi notes that one should still try to keep the non-Jew to the right or left as described, but that maintaining the higher ground is independently important — the elevated position is always the more defensible one.

### Avoidance Tactics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁאָלוֹ לְהֵיכָן הוֹלֵךְ — יַרְחִיב לוֹ אֶת הַדֶּרֶךְ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁעָשָׂה יַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ לְעֵשָׂו הָרָשָׁע, דִּכְתִיב: ״עַד אֲשֶׁר אָבֹא אֶל אֲדֹנִי שֵׂעִירָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְיַעֲקֹב נָסַע סֻכֹּתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, if the gentile asks him where he is going, the Jew should extend the journey when relating his destination to him, i.e., he should claim that he is traveling to a place beyond his actual destination, in the manner that Jacob our father did to the wicked Esau. As it is written that Jacob said to Esau: "Let my lord pass over before his servant and I will journey on gently, according to the pace of the cattle that are before me and according to the pace of the children, **until I come unto my lord unto Seir"** (Genesis 33:14). The Gemara continues: **And it is written subsequently: "And Jacob journeyed to Succoth, and built himself a house"** (Genesis 33:17). Rather than meeting Esau in Seir, Jacob traveled to the nearer location of Succoth. Telling a gentile that one is traveling to a farther destination might cause the gentile to defer his attack due to the assumption that he has a longer period of time to plan and execute it. As a result of the delay, the Jew has a better chance of reaching his true destination safely.

The *baraita* continues: *v'al yishach lefanav* — do not bow down in front of him. If one were to stoop — to pick up a coin, to tie a sandal — *shema yerotzetz gulgelto* — perhaps he will crush one's head. The vulnerability of bending over in the presence of a potentially hostile individual is obvious.

If the non-Jew asks where one is traveling, *yarchiv lo et haderech* — one should extend the destination. That is, one should name a location further away than one's actual destination. The non-Jew, believing he has a longer journey alongside his companion, will not feel urgency to act — and when the Jew departs at his actual stop, he will have avoided any window of opportunity the non-Jew might have sought.

### Yaakov Example

The Gemara grounds this tactic in the precedent of Yaakov Avinu: *kederech she'asah Yaakov Avinu l'Esav haRasha* — as Yaakov our Patriarch did before Esav the wicked. Yaakov told Esav: *ad asher avo el adoni Se'irah* — I will come to my lord in Seir. Yet the verse then records: *v'Yaakov nasa Succotah* — and Yaakov traveled to Succoth, not to Seir at all. By declaring a further destination, Yaakov created space between himself and Esav, allowing him to safely divert without confrontation.

## Evasion Narratives

### Akiva Students Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּתַלְמִידֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שֶׁהָיוּ הוֹלְכִים לִכְזִיב, פָּגְעוּ בָּהֶן לִיסְטִים, אָמְרוּ לָהֶן: לְאָן אַתֶּם הוֹלְכִים? אָמְרוּ לָהֶן: לְעַכּוֹ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעוּ לִכְזִיב, פֵּירְשׁוּ. אָמְרוּ לָהֶן: תַּלְמִידֵי מִי אַתֶּם? אָמְרוּ לָהֶן: תַּלְמִידֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמְרוּ לָהֶן: אַשְׁרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְתַלְמִידָיו שֶׁלֹּא פָּגַע בָּהֶן אָדָם רַע מֵעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara illustrates this idea through a relevant story. **An incident occurred involving several students of Rabbi Akiva who were traveling to Keziv. Along the way, bandits encountered them and said to them: Where are you going? Rabbi Akiva's students said to them: To Akko. Once they reached Keziv, Rabbi Akiva's students separated from the bandits. At this point the bandits said to them: Whose students are you? The students said to the bandits: We are students of Rabbi Akiva. The bandits said to them: Fortunate are Rabbi Akiva and his students, as they were never harmed by an evil person.** The bandits were impressed with the wisdom that the students demonstrated in evading harm.

The Gemara now offers two illustrative narratives. The first: *maaseh b'talmidei Rabbi Akiva shehayu holchim l'Chziv* — students of Rabbi Akiva were traveling to Chziv, a city north of Eretz Yisrael, somewhat short of Akko. Bandits approached them and, without immediately revealing their intentions, asked: *le'an atem holchim* — where are you going? The students replied: *le'Akko* — to Akko, a city even further than their actual destination. When they arrived at Chziv, they announced their departure: *parshu* — they took their leave. The bandits, realizing they had been misled, nonetheless expressed admiration: *ashrei Rabbi Akiva v'talmidav shelo paga bahen adam ra me'olam* — praised is Rabbi Akiva and his students, who have never been harmed by an evildoer. The technique had worked, the students were safe, and even those who sought to harm them acknowledged the wisdom of the teaching.

### Menasha & Cherem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מְנַשֶּׁה הֲוָה אָזֵל לְבֵי תוֹרְתָּא, פְּגַעוּ בֵּיהּ גַּנָּבֵי, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לְאָן קָאָזְלַתְּ? אָמַר לָהֶן: לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא. כִּי מְטָא לְבֵי תוֹרְתָּא, פָּרֵישׁ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: תַּלְמִידָא דִּיהוּדָה רַמָּאָה אַתְּ! אֲמַר לְהוּ: יָדְעִיתוּ לֵיהּ? יְהֵא רַעֲוָא דְּלִיהְווֹ הָנְהוּ אִינָשֵׁי בְּשַׁמְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: **Rav Menashe was traveling to Bei Torta. Along the way, thieves encountered him, and said to him: Where are you going? Rav Menashe said to them: To Pumbedita, which was farther away than Bei Torta. When he arrived at Bei Torta, he separated from the thieves, who had planned to steal from him. They said to him: You are a student of Yehuda the swindler! Rav Menashe said to them: Do you know him so well that you can accuse him of swindling others? May it be God's will that these people be subject to Rav Yehuda's ban of excommunication.**

The second story: Rabbi Menasha was traveling to Bei Torsa when he was similarly approached by robbers. Asked where he was going, he named Pumbedita — significantly further away. When they reached Bei Torsa and he departed, the robbers confronted him: *talmido d'Yehuda rama'a at* — you must be a student of Yehuda the deceiver. Notice the contrast with the previous story: in Eretz Yisrael, the robbers praised Rabbi Akiva; in Bavel, they insulted Rabbi Yehuda.

Rabbi Menasha was indignant. He turned to them and said: *yedaitu leih* — you know who he is? You dare call him a *rama'a*? He then declared: *yehei ra'ava d'leihavu hanu inshi b'shamta* — may it be that these people be under Rabbi Yehuda's *cherem*. The robbers went off and continued their criminal trade for twenty-two years — *v'lo atzlach* — with no success. The *shamta* had taken hold and affected everything they did.

### Weaver Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַלוּ עֲבַדוּ גְּנֵיבָתָא עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין שְׁנִין וְלָא אַצְלַחוּ, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ, אֲתוֹ כּוּלְּהוּ תְּבַעוּ שַׁמְתַּיְיהוּ, וַהֲוָה בְּהוּ חַד גִּירְדְּנָא דְּלָא אֲתָא לְשָׁרוֹיֵה שַׁמְּתֵיהּ, אַכְלֵיהּ אַרְיָא. הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: גִּירְדָּנָא דְּלָא טַיְיזָן, שַׁתָּא (בציר משני) [בְּצִירָא מִשְּׁנֵיהּ].
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: This curse was effective, as the thieves subsequently went and carried out thefts for twenty-two years, but they were unsuccessful. Once they saw that their efforts were for naught, they all went and claimed their curse, i.e., they asked forgiveness and requested that the curse be lifted. But there was one weaver among them who did not come to lift his curse, and a lion ate him. The Gemara notes that this explains the adage that people say: With regard to a weaver [*girdana*] who is not shy [*taizan*] but impudent, a year is deducted from his allotted years.

Eventually, when they realized that their persistent failure was connected to the *cherem* under which they had been placed, *atu kulhu tavu shamtayeihu* — they all came and sought to have their *cherem* removed, presumably asking forgiveness for the disrespect they had shown Rabbi Yehuda. The question of whether to remove a *cherem* from people who are seeking it primarily in order to resume their criminal activities is, of course, a pointed one, though the Gemara does not resolve it.

Among the robbers, however, there was one who stood apart: *chad giridna d'lo ata lishruyei shamtiah* — a weaver among them who did not come to have his *cherem* removed. *Achleih aryeh* — a lion ate him. The Gemara then invokes a folk saying: *giridna d'lo tivzan shata batzar mishnei* — a weaver who has no shame has a year deducted from his years. Rashi explains that weavers were associated with poverty and shamelessness, and the proverb reflects the idea that such a person — poor and without self-awareness — continues to suffer. The weaver's failure to seek *mechila*, his stubborn indifference, cost him his life.

### Bavel vs EY Respect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא חֲזִי, מָה בֵּין גַּנָּבֵי בָבֶל וְלִסְטִין דְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to these two anecdotes, the Gemara comments: **Come and see what the difference is between the thieves of Babylonia and the bandits of Eretz Yisrael.** Although thieves are typically less violent than bandits, the thieves in Babylonia reacted to Rav Menashe's action by denouncing Rav Yehuda, whereas the bandits in Eretz Yisrael praised the wisdom of Rabbi Akiva.

The Gemara draws a broader lesson from comparing the two stories: *ta chazi ma bein ganvei Bavel u'listim d'Eretz Yisrael* — come and see the difference between the thieves of Bavel and the bandits of Eretz Yisrael. In Bavel, the robbers mocked and insulted Rabbi Yehuda, calling him a *ramai*. In Eretz Yisrael, even the bandits recognized the greatness of Rabbi Akiva and blessed him and his students. The point is pointed and perhaps a little wry: even in the realm of those who live outside the law, the culture of Eretz Yisrael maintained a respect for Torah scholars that Bavel could not match.
