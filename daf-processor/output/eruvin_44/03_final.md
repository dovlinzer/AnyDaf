# Eruvin 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boat Landing & Techum

### Setup & Rabban Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּעַם אַחַת לֹא נִכְנְסוּ לַנָּמָל וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: On **one occasion, they did not enter the port** until after nightfall on Shabbat eve, and they asked Rabban Gamliel whether they were permitted to alight from the boat. He told them that they were permitted to alight, for he had been watching, and he knew that they had entered within the city's limit before nightfall, and therefore they may walk throughout the city.

The daf opens still in the context of the boat scenario introduced on 43b. The *mishna* considers a case in which a boat failed to enter port before *Shabbat* began — *paam achat lo nichnasu la-namal* — and the passengers found themselves more than two thousand *amot* from the shore when *Shabbat* commenced. Under ordinary circumstances, having traveled *chutz la-techum*, they would be restricted to four *amot* from wherever they stood. The question is whether they could disembark at all. Rabban Gamliel resolved the matter by reporting that he had been watching as they approached and confirmed that the vessel was already within two thousand *amot* of the port when *Shabbat* began. That being so, the passengers could disembark, even if walking home remained a separate question.

### Disembark Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: שְׁפוֹפֶרֶת הָיְתָה לוֹ לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל שֶׁהָיָה מַבִּיט וְצוֹפֶה בָּהּ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה בַּיַּבָּשָׁה, וּכְנֶגְדָּהּ אַלְפַּיִם בַּיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** In order to clarify this issue, the Gemara cites that which was **taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabban Gamliel had a** special **tube through which he would look and see** a distance of **two thousand cubits on land, and** also determine **a corresponding** distance of **two thousand cubits at sea.**

The *gemara* presses the point: can they in fact disembark? Given the uncertainty inherent in the exact moment *bein hashmashot* transitions into *Shabbat* — "like the blink of an eye," one moment outside and one moment inside, impossible to pinpoint precisely — any claim about the boat's precise position at that instant is inherently precarious. The assumption is that Rabban Gamliel's observation was made from the outset of *bein hashmashot*, and the *braita* now cited explains how he was confident in his measurement.

### Shfoferet Device

The *braita* records: *shfoferet hayta lo le-Rabban Gamliel* — Rabban Gamliel possessed a *shfoferet*, a tube, through which *she-haya mabit ve-tzofeh bah alpayim ama ba-yabashah* — he would look and ascertain two thousand *amot* on dry land, *u-k'negdo alpayim ba-yam* — and correspondingly two thousand *amot* on the sea. By means of this device, he could determine whether the boat was within the permitted boundary even while at sea.

### Rashi's Focus Theory

Rashi explains it differently. Rashi says it was not designed to see farther; in fact, it shortened the distance of vision. By narrowing the aperture at the far end of the tube — collapsing or extending it to open or close that opening — the device limited how far one could see. Once calibrated through actual measurement on flat ground to correspond to exactly two thousand *amot*, anything visible through the tube fell within that distance, while anything beyond it would not be visible. The device could then be applied at sea to make the same determination.

The difficulty with Rashi's explanation is apparent: a narrow aperture limits one's *field* of vision but does not restrict the *distance* one can see. It is hard to understand technically how this would work as described. One suggested reading of Rashi — proposed during the *shiur* — is that the tube was focused at a fixed focal length of two thousand *amot*, so that an object at exactly that distance would appear sharply in focus, while anything further would not. That may be a defensible way of reading Rashi.

### Surveying Methods

The *Steinsaltz* edition cites the *Geonim* and others who understand the *shfoferet* not as an optical device at all but as a surveying instrument of the kind used even today — something like a theodolite or a sextant — employing angular measurements and mathematics to estimate distances. Surveyors are familiar with such instruments, typically mounted on tripods, which use angular geometry to calculate distance without needing to traverse it physically. This would explain how Rabban Gamliel could determine distances across water using measurements previously taken on land.

### Valley & Tree Measuring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרוֹצֶה לֵידַע כַּמָּה עוֹמְקוֹ שֶׁל גֵּיא, מֵבִיא שְׁפוֹפֶרֶת וּמַבִּיט בָּהּ, וְיֵדַע כַּמָּה עוֹמְקוֹ שֶׁל גַּיְא. וְהָרוֹצֶה לֵידַע כַּמָּה גּוֹבְהוֹ שֶׁל דֶּקֶל — מוֹדֵד קוֹמָתוֹ וְצִלּוֹ, וְצֵל קוֹמָתוֹ וְיֵדַע כַּמָּה גּוֹבַהּ שֶׁל דֶּקֶל.
>
> **Translation:** In general, **one who wishes to know the depth of a valley can bring** such **a tube and look through it, and he will know the depth of the valley.** The Gemara cites another statement with regard to measurements: **One who wishes to know the height of a palm tree,** but does not want to actually climb the tree to measure it, **can measure his** own **height, and** the length of **his** own **shadow, and** the length of **the shadow of the height** of the palm tree, and calculate the proportions, **and he will know the height of the palm tree.**

The *gemara* proceeds to describe two further applications of the same device. *Ha-rotzeh leida kama omko shel gei* — if one wishes to know the depth of a valley — one brings the *shfoferet* and uses it there as well. Assuming the device has been calibrated by actual measurement on flat ground, one uses that same calibration to determine the distance at the bottom of the valley, or alternatively, one first sights down to the valley floor and then applies that same calibration along flat ground to derive the depth.

For measuring the height of a palm tree, the *gemara* offers an even more elegant method. *Moded komato ve-tzilo* — one measures one's own height and the length of one's shadow, then measures the length of the shadow cast by the palm tree on the same day. Since the shadows are proportional to the objects casting them, a simple ratio yields the palm tree's height. If one's shadow is twice one's height, then the palm tree's height is half the length of its shadow; if one's shadow is half one's height, one multiplies the shadow's length by two. The logic is straightforward and mathematically exact.

### Grave Shadow Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁרֶה חַיָּה רָעָה בְּצֵל קֶבֶר — נוֹעֵץ קָנֶה בְּאַרְבַּע שָׁעוֹת בַּיּוֹם, וְיִרְאֶה לְהֵיכָן צִלּוֹ נוֹטֶה, מְשַׁפֵּיעַ וְעוֹלֶה מְשַׁפֵּיעַ וְיוֹרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites related advice: If, out of honor for the dead, **one wishes that a wild beast should not rest in the shade of a grave, he should insert a reed** into the ground **at** the end of **the fourth hour of the day,** roughly ten o'clock in the morning, when it is hot in the sun and cooler in the shade, and beasts begin to seek shelter in the shade. **And** he should **observe in which direction the shadow** of the reed **inclines,** and then **slant** the gravestone **upwards and downwards** until he finds an angle at which it casts no shadow at that hour, and the beasts will not come to rest at the grave during the heat of the day.

The *gemara* then introduces a case that, while related to the theme of shadows and measurements, seems somewhat unexpected in this context. *Ha-rotzeh she-lo tishre chaya ra'ah be-tzel kever* — one who does not wish poisonous or biting animals, such as scorpions, to take shelter in the shadow of a tombstone. Such creatures seek shade when the day grows warm. The method is as follows: at four hours into the day — approximately 10:00 AM, when warmth begins in earnest — one inserts a stick into the ground near the grave and observes where its shadow falls. That shadow marks exactly where the dangerous creatures will congregate. One then builds a mound at that location so that no shaded area remains at that time of day. Rashi explains that since the animals do not find shade there at 10:00 AM, they will not bother coming to that spot at all during the rest of the day. One might wonder whether they would simply find another nearby spot — but that is the *gemara*'s practical solution.

## Nechemya & Wall

### Nechemya's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְחֶמְיָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חֲנִילַאי מְשַׁכְתֵּיהּ שְׁמַעְתָּא, וּנְפַק חוּץ לַתְּחוּם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב נַחְמָן: נְחֶמְיָה תַּלְמִידְךָ שָׁרוּי בְּצַעַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Neḥemya, son of Rav Ḥanilai, was** once so **engrossed in** his **learning** that he did not notice that **he was going out beyond** his Shabbat limit. **Rav Ḥisda said to Rav Naḥman: Your student Neḥemya is in distress,** as he is outside the Shabbat limit and cannot enter. What can we do for him?

The *gemara* now presents a narrative. *Nechemya brei de-Rav Chanilai* — Nechemya the son of Rav Chanilai — became so deeply absorbed in his learning, *mashchatya shmata*, literally, his *sugya* drew him away, that he walked without awareness of his surroundings and found himself *nafak chutz la-techum* — outside the boundary. He was now stranded, unable to return to the city.

### People Wall Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: עֲשֵׂה לוֹ מְחִיצָה שֶׁל בְּנֵי אָדָם, וְיִכָּנֵס.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Establish a human partition for him,** i.e., people who are permitted to go out there should line up and form human walls, through which he is permitted to walk and thereby **reenter** the Shabbat limit.

Rav Chisda reported the situation to Rav Nachman: Nechemya, your student, *shari be-tza'ar* — is sitting in distress out there. Rav Nachman replied: *asu lo mechitza shel bnei adam ve-yikanes* — make a wall of people and let him enter. The plan was to assemble a group of people, surround Nechemya with them in a circle, and thereby create an enclosed space. This is significant because the ruling follows the position of Rav, who rules like Rabban Gamliel in the *mishna*: even someone who has gone *chutz la-techum* and is limited to four *amot* may traverse an entire enclosed space if those four *amot* bring him within it. If the enclosure, moreover, brings him back within the original two thousand *amot*, he can return home.

The obvious question is how the people making the wall could themselves reach Nechemya without violating their own *techum*. Rashi suggests they had made some form of *eiruv*, or alternatively they had not gone so far — they were closer to the boundary to begin with, with the houses near the edge of the *techum*.

### Rabban Gamliel Rule

This entire solution depends on ruling like Rabban Gamliel: that an enclosed space, even one created on the spot, allows a person who was stranded *chutz la-techum* to move freely within it. Rava had already established — *halacha ke-Rabban Gamliel be-dir ve-sahar ve-safina* — that the law follows Rabban Gamliel with respect to a fold, a corral, and a boat. Though one might question whether this principle applies straightforwardly to a newly improvised enclosure rather than a pre-existing one, the *gemara* proceeds on that assumption.

### Question on Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אֲחוֹרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, וְיָתֵיב רָבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרָבָא: מַאי קָא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְרַב חִסְדָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak sat behind Rava, and Rava sat** in the first row **before Rav Naḥman. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to Rava: What** precisely **was Rav Ḥisda's dilemma** that he addressed to Rav Naḥman with regard to Neḥemya's distress?

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak was sitting behind Rava, who was sitting before Rav Nachman, when this exchange took place. After it concluded, Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak turned to Rava and asked: what exactly was Rav Chisda's question? What difficulty prompted him to seek Rav Nachman's guidance? Surely the answer seemed clear. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak formulates the possibilities. If the situation was *bi-dmalu gavrei* — that there were enough people to form an enclosure reaching back within the two thousand *amot* — then the only question would have been whether we rule like Rabban Gamliel, and that has already been settled by Rava's ruling. So evidently Rav Chisda's question must have been something more subtle.

### Two Cubit Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא בִּדְמָלוּ גַּבְרֵי עָסְקִינַן, וְקָא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: הִלְכְתָא כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. אוֹ דִילְמָא, בִּדְלָא מָלוּ גַּבְרֵי עָסְקִינַן, וְקָא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **If you say** that **we are dealing with** a case where the space between Neḥemya and the Shabbat limit could be **filled with people** who had established an *eiruv* and were permitted to go out beyond the Shabbat limit and establish a human partition for Neḥemya, **and** then it can be argued that **the dilemma** that **he raised** was: Is **the *halakha* in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel** that a person may walk throughout an enclosed area, although he had not established residence there before Shabbat while it was still day, and the same applies to a human partition of this kind; **or is** the ***halakha* not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel? Or perhaps we are dealing with** a case where the space between Neḥemya and the Shabbat limit could **not be filled with people** who had established an *eiruv* and were permitted to establish a human partition for Neḥemya. In that case, there were enough people to establish partitions from where Neḥemya was standing to within two cubits from the limit, **and** the dilemma that Rav Ḥisda **raised** was: **Is** the ***halakha* in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who says that someone who went two cubits outside of his Shabbat limit may reenter it, **or is** the ***halakha* not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer?**

The true problem, concludes the *gemara*, was *bi-de-lo milu gavrei* — there were not enough people to form a continuous enclosure from Nechemya's position back to within the original two thousand *amot*. They could only bring the enclosure to within two *amot* of the boundary. And that raises the question of whether we rule like Rabbi Eliezer.

Rabbi Eliezer holds two positions: first, a stringency — one's four *amot* are measured as two *amot* in each direction from one's position rather than four in any direction. But second, a leniency — if after stepping outside the boundary one's four *amot* overlap with the original two thousand *amot*, one is considered still within the *techum*, effectively receiving two thousand and two *amot*. The *Chachamim* disagree: once one has crossed the line, even if one's four *amot* would reach back across it, that provides no benefit. So the question was whether Nechemya could benefit from this leniency of Rabbi Eliezer to bridge the remaining two *amot*.

### Awareness Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא בִּדְלָא מָלוּ גַּבְרֵי עָסְקִינַן, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בִּדְמָלוּ גַּבְרֵי עָסְקִינַן, מַאי תִּיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ? הָאָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּדִיר וְסַהַר וּסְפִינָה! אֶלָּא וַדַּאי בִּדְלָא מָלוּ גַּבְרֵי עָסְקִינַן, וּדְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **obvious that we are dealing with** a case where the space between Neḥemya and the Shabbat limit could **not be filled with people, as if it should enter your mind that we are dealing with** a case where the space between Neḥemya and the Shabbat limit could be fully **filled with people, what is** Rav Ḥisda's **dilemma? Didn't Rav say:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel with regard to a pen, a stable, and a boat? Rather, we** must be **dealing with** a case where the space between Neḥemya and the Shabbat limit could **not be filled with people, and** the dilemma that **he raised was** about the ruling **of Rabbi Eliezer.**

The *gemara* then proceeds on that assumption.

## Wall Contradiction

### First Braita Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל דּוֹפְנָהּ — לֹא יַעֲמִיד בָּהּ אָדָם בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים. וְלֹא יִזְקוֹף אֶת הַמִּטָּה לִפְרוֹס עָלֶיהָ סָדִין, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין אֹהֶל עֲרַאי בַּתְּחִילָּה בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בַּשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **If the wall** of a *sukka* **fell** on a Festival or on Shabbat, thus rendering the *sukka* unfit for the mitzva, **one may not position people, animals or utensils there** in its place in order to form a wall, **nor may one turn a bed upright in order to spread a sheet over it,** which will thereby serve as a partition, **because one may not make a temporary tent for the first** time **on a Festival, and, needless to say,** this is prohibited **on Shabbat.** This indicates that a human partition may not be erected on Shabbat.

At this point Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak raises a more fundamental challenge to the entire solution of surrounding Nechemya with people: is it even permitted to create a wall out of people on *Shabbat*? A *braita* rules: *nafal dofenah* — if a wall of one's *sukkah* fell down — *lo ya'amid bah adam behema ve-kelim* — one may not use a person, an animal, or vessels to reconstitute that wall, even temporarily. And not only with new construction materials is this forbidden — even these improvised substitutes are prohibited. *Ve-lo yizkof et ha-mitah li-fros aleha sadin* — and one may not upturn a bed and spread a sheet over the frame to serve as a wall. *Le-fi she-ein osim ohel arai ba-rishona be-Yom Tov* — because one may not create a temporary *ohel* de novo on *Yom Tov*. *Ve-ein tzarich lomar be-Shabbat* — and certainly not on *Shabbat*. This *braita* would seem to forbid the solution proposed for Nechemya entirely.

### Second Braita Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹשֶׂה אָדָם אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ דּוֹפֶן כְּדֵי שֶׁיֹּאכַל וְיִשְׁתֶּה וְיִשַׁן. וְיִזְקוֹף אֶת הַמִּטָּה וְיִפְרוֹס עָלֶיהָ סָדִין כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּפּוֹל חַמָּה עַל הַמֵּת וְעַל הָאוֹכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: You state to me** that this is prohibited **from this** *baraita*, **but I** can **state to you** that it is permitted **from this** other *baraita*: **A person may position his fellow as a wall, so that he may eat, drink, and sleep** in a *sukka*, **and** he is likewise permitted to **turn a bed upright** in order to **spread a sheet over it, so that the sun should not beat down on a corpse, or on food.**

Against this, a second *braita* permits exactly what the first forbids. *Oseh adam et chavero dofen* — one may use one's friend as a wall for a *sukkah* — *kedei she-yochal ve-yishte ve-yishan* — so that one can eat, drink, and sleep in the *sukkah*. And further: *ve-yizkof et ha-mitah ve-yifros aleha sadin kedei she-lo tilke chama al ha-met* — one may upturn a bed and spread a sheet to shade a corpse from the sun; and similarly to protect food from spoiling. So a wall made from a person for the *sukkah* is permitted, and a temporary structure for shade is permitted.

### Reconciliation Attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הָא — רַבָּנַן, דִּתְנַן: פְּקַק הַחַלּוֹן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּזְמַן שֶׁקָּשׁוּר וְתָלוּי — פּוֹקְקִין בּוֹ, וְאִם לָאו — אֵין פּוֹקְקִין בּוֹ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ פּוֹקְקִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: If so, these two *baraitot* **contradict one another.** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult; this** *baraita* that teaches that it is prohibited reflects the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** whereas **this** other *baraita* that teaches that it is permitted reflects the opinion of **the Rabbis. As we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **a window shutter** that is not fixed to the wall with hinges, **Rabbi Eliezer says: If it is tied** to the wall **and hangs** from the window, **one may shut** the window **with it; but if not, one may not shut** the window **with it,** since one may not erect a tent, even a temporary one, on Shabbat. **But the Rabbis say: In either case, one may shut** the window **with it.** This indicates that the Rabbis permit constructing a temporary wall of this sort on Shabbat, and they also permit the construction of a temporary wall in the case of a *sukka*.

These two *beraitot* directly contradict one another. The *gemara* first attempts to resolve the contradiction by assigning one to Rabbi Eliezer and the other to the *Rabbanan*, based on a dispute recorded in a *mishna* concerning a window stopper (*pekak ha-chalon*). Rabbi Eliezer holds that a window stopper may be used only if it is tied and suspended — essentially already part of the wall's structure, like a shutter. If not, he forbids it, treating even a temporary addition as prohibited. The *Rabbanan* permit it in either case. On this reading, the debate about people and animals serving as walls for a *sukkah* would map onto this dispute.

However, the *gemara* — citing Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — rejects this framing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא: אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין אֹהֶל עֲרַאי בַּתְּחִילָּה בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּשַׁבָּת. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא לְהוֹסִיף, שֶׁרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹסִיפִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּשַׁבָּת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מוֹסִיפִין בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּיוֹם טוֹב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But wasn't it stated with regard to this** dispute: **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: All agree that one may not make a temporary tent for the first** time **on a Festival, and, needless to say,** this is prohibited **on Shabbat.** The Rabbis and Rabbi Eliezer **disagree only with regard to adding** a temporary tent to a permanent structure, as in the case of a window shutter. **As Rabbi Eliezer says: One may not add** a temporary tent to a permanent structure even **on a Festival; and, needless to say,** this is prohibited **on Shabbat. And the Rabbis say: One may add** a temporary tent to a permanent structure **on Shabbat, and needless to say,** this is permitted **on a Festival.** This indicates that there is no opinion that grants license to construct a temporary wall for the first time.

*Ha-kol modim she-ein osim ohel arai be-Yom Tov* — everyone agrees that one may not create a temporary structure de novo on *Yom Tov*, and certainly not on *Shabbat*. The dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Rabbanan* is only about *adding* (*mosif*) to an existing structure. Rabbi Eliezer forbids even adding on *Yom Tov*; the *Rabbanan* permit adding even on *Shabbat*. But creating something entirely new — building a first-time wall — is universally forbidden. Since the fallen wall of a *sukkah* represents the third wall, replacing it is not an addition but a creation, and that is forbidden according to everyone. The contradiction between the two *beraitot* therefore cannot be resolved by attributing them to Rabbi Eliezer and the *Rabbanan*.

### Animal Wall Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא וְהָא רַבָּנַן. וְכֵלִים אַכֵּלִים לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — בְּדוֹפֶן שְׁלִישִׁית, הָא — בְּדוֹפֶן רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say that both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* follow the opinion of **the Rabbis,** and this is the resolution of the various contradictions: With regard to the contradiction between the one ruling concerning **utensils** and the other ruling concerning **utensils,** this is **not difficult,** as **this** ruling that prohibits the construction of an additional wall refers **to** the **third wall** of a *sukka*, which renders it fit for the mitzva; whereas **this** other ruling that permits the construction of an additional wall refers **to** the **fourth wall** of a *sukka*, which is insignificant, as a *sukka* need not have four walls.

The *gemara* then proposes resolving the contradiction through the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda. A *braita* teaches: *asah le-vehema dofen le-sukkah* — if one used an animal as a wall for a *sukkah* before *Yom Tov* — Rabbi Meir invalidates it (*posel*) and Rabbi Yehuda validates it (*machshir*). Whether Rabbi Meir's invalidation stems from concern that the animal might wander off, or from a categorical position that a living creature cannot halakhically constitute a wall, the *gemara* proposes the following reading: the *braita* that permits using a person as the third wall goes according to Rabbi Meir, who holds that an animal — and by extension a person — does not constitute a halakhic wall. Therefore, the argument runs, using a person to stand as the third wall on *Shabbat* is not really doing anything halakhically significant, and hence not prohibited.

This *hava amina* is admittedly strained, as Rashi himself acknowledges. The *gemara* quickly dismantles it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: נָפַל דּוֹפְנָהּ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **This** interpretation **is also precise** in the wording of the *baraita*, **as** the *baraita* that prohibits the construction of an additional wall uses the following phrase: **If its wall fell.** This indicates a wall that is significant, i.e., a wall that renders it fit for use, rather than any wall, as stated in the *baraita* that permits it. The Gemara concludes: **Learn from this** that the correct resolution is to differentiate between the third and fourth wall of a *sukka*.

First: even granting Rabbi Meir's position about animals, one cannot extend it to people and certainly not to vessels — the prohibition cited in the first *braita* covered all three, and there is no source for saying Rabbi Meir would regard vessels as non-halakhic walls. Second, and more structurally: *Rabbi Meir aliba de-man* — under whose authority is Rabbi Meir operating? If under Rabbi Eliezer, even *adding* is forbidden on *Yom Tov*, which makes the question of whether it is halakhic irrelevant. If under the *Rabbanan*, then the *Rabbanan* only permit *adding* to an existing structure; creating the third wall from scratch — *oseh le-khatechila* — remains forbidden even to them, regardless of whether the new wall is halakhically significant.

The *gemara* arrives at the true resolution. *Ela ha va-ha Rabbanan* — both *beraitot* reflect the position of the *Rabbanan*. The contradiction is not a dispute at all; the two *beraitot* address different situations. The first *braita* — which prohibits everything — speaks of the *third* wall of the *sukkah* (*dofen shlishit*), the wall whose creation constitutes the de novo construction of a halakhically functional enclosure. Creating this is forbidden on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* because it is the equivalent of building an *ohel* afresh. The second *braita* — which permits everything — speaks of the *fourth* wall (*dofen revi'it*), or of a wall erected separately that serves no formal halakhic definition of an enclosure — such as providing shade for a corpse or protecting food. This distinction can be inferred from the language of the first *braita* itself, which says *nafal dofenah* — *its* wall fell — implying the wall that was halakhically constitutive. The second *braita* simply says *oseh dofen* — making *a* wall — without that definite possessive force.

## Halachic Distinction

### Halachic Wall Defined

The resolution of the *braita* contradiction turns on a fundamental distinction that the *gemara* here articulates and that has far-reaching practical implications. A halakhic wall (*mechitza*) is one that serves a formally recognized halakhic function: defining the boundaries of a *reshut*, completing the required walls of a *sukkah*, or establishing an enclosed space within which movement is permitted on *Shabbat*. Creating such a wall on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* is prohibited, even if it is entirely temporary, because it constitutes the act of building — *oseh ohel arai* — in a halakhically significant sense.

### Non-Halachic Allowed

A wall that serves only a practical or incidental function — providing shade, blocking wind for comfort, protecting a corpse or food — does not rise to the level of an *ohel arai* in the prohibited sense. Such a wall may be erected on *Shabbat* even if it is newly created. The distinction is not merely formal; it reflects a substantive halakhic category. Only when a wall is *doing something* in the halakhic sense — defining a *reshut*, completing a *sukkah* — is its creation treated as an act of building.

### Mechitza Example

This principle has practical application in the laws of the *mechitza* in a synagogue. The *Mordechai* records that when women came to hear the rabbi's *derasha*, a *mechitza* could be erected — at the time of the *Rishonim*, women typically prayed in a separate room or building, but the rabbi did not wish to deliver the *derasha* twice, so the women would gather in the main space and a partition would be put up between them and the men. This was permitted because the *mechitza* served a practical purpose of modesty and separation, not a formal halakhic function of dividing *reshuyot* or defining an enclosure. It was therefore not considered a halakhic wall.

This connects to a broader dispute among later authorities regarding the nature of the *mechitza* in a *shul*. Rav Henkin cites his grandfather, Rav Yosef Eliyahu Henkin, who argued that the fundamental role of the *mechitza* is to halakhically designate the men's and women's sections as separate domains — not primarily to obstruct physical interaction. Under that view, a *mechitza* of *asara tefachim* — approximately thirty inches — would suffice, since it accomplishes the formal halakhic function of separation. Indeed, some synagogues, even within the OU, historically maintained *mechitzot* of that height. Rav Moshe Feinstein strongly disagrees with this position.

Importantly, Rav Henkin's approach carries its own stringency: if the *mechitza* must serve as a formal halakhic wall, it must come within three *tefachim* of the ground (*lavud*). In one of his *teshuvot*, he critiques a particular kibbutz where the *mechitza* was a suspended fabric screen that began more than three *tefachim* off the floor — which would be halakhically invalid on that understanding. The practical consequence for our *sugya* is direct: if the *mechitza* serves a formal halakhic function, it cannot be erected on *Shabbat*, because creating a halakhic wall is prohibited. If it serves only a practical function of modesty or visual separation, it may be erected on *Shabbat*.

### Unaware People Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָדָם אַאָדָם נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — לְדַעַת, כָּאן — שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: With regard to the contradiction between the one ruling concerning **a person** and the other ruling concerning **a person,** it is **also not difficult. Here,** where it is prohibited, the *baraita* refers to a case where that person **knowingly** served as a partition; whereas **here,** where it is permitted, it refers to a case where that person **unknowingly** served as a partition, which is not the usual manner of building. This is not the case with regard to utilizing a utensil as a partition. Since the utensil lacks knowledge, it is considered a partition regardless of how it is placed, and it is prohibited in all cases.

With the distinction between halakhic and non-halakhic walls established, the *gemara* returns to the contradiction regarding people. The first *braita* forbids using a person as the third wall of a *sukkah*; the second permits it. The *gemara* resolves: *kan la-da'at kan she-lo mi-da'at* — here the person is aware he is serving as the wall; there, he is not. When the person standing as a wall knows he is functioning as a formal halakhic division, the wall has been consciously constructed and is treated as a prohibited act of building. When he is unaware — when he is simply standing there, and another person has arranged the scenario without informing him of his halakhic role — the wall is not considered to have been formally erected in the prohibited sense.

The underlying logic seems to be that a human being is not simply an object. The idea that a person can be designated as a halakhic wall has something inherently tenuous about it. If the person himself is unaware, the designation lacks the consciousness that would transform him into something formally defined. He is still just a person standing there. Yet paradoxically — and this is a striking feature of the halakha — he is simultaneously *sufficient* to function as a wall for the *sukkah*, permitting eating, drinking, and sleeping. The halakha thus plays both sides: he counts enough to function as a wall, but not enough for his placement to constitute a prohibited act of construction.

### Unaware Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ בְּנֵי גְנָנָא דְּאַעִילוּ מַיָּא בִּמְחִיצָה שֶׁל בְּנֵי אָדָם. נַגְּדִינְהוּ שְׁמוּאֵל. אֲמַר: אִם אָמְרוּ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת, יֹאמְרוּ לְדַעַת?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that there were **these members of a wedding** party who engaged the many people present **to bring water in** on Shabbat from a public domain to a private domain **through walls comprised of people** who knew that they were being used as partitions for that purpose. **Shmuel** instructed that **they** should **be flogged. He said** with regard to this matter: **If** the Sages **said** that a partition is effective when the people act **unknowingly,** does this mean that **they would** also **say** that this is permitted *ab initio* when they **knowingly** serve as a partition?

The *gemara* illustrates the principle with several cases. A group of gardeners used a circle of people as a *mechitza* to carry water — apparently from a well in the field into a domain where carrying was otherwise prohibited — and Shmuel had them flogged, because the people forming the wall were aware of their role. Subsequently, when Rava was leaving the *beit midrash* surrounded by his students — as was typical, the disciples walking around him — someone exploited the moment: while the entourage moved through the street of Mechuza, he retrieved some flasks that had been lying in the thoroughfare and carried them within the moving circle of students. The students were unaware they were functioning as a *mechitza*, and so this was permitted. The following *Shabbat*, when the same trick was attempted again, Rava forbade it — *de-hayu yadei* — because by then everyone knew what was being done, and awareness had been established.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ זִיקֵי דַּהֲוָה שַׁדְיָין בְּרִיסְתְּקָא דְמָחוֹזָא, בַּהֲדֵי דַּאֲתָא רָבָא מִפִּירְקֵיהּ, אַעְלִינְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ. לְשַׁבְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי בָּעֵי עַיְּילִינְהוּ, וַאֲסַר לְהוּ, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּלְדַעַת — וְאָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that there **were** once **these flasks lying in the market [*ristaka*] of Meḥoza** on Shabbat and could not be moved. **When Rava was coming from his discourse** accompanied by a throng of people, his attendants **brought** the flasks **into his** house, as the crowd of people created human partitions, upon which the attendants capitalized for this purpose. **On another Shabbat they wanted to bring them in** again, **but** Rava **prohibited them** from doing so, reasoning: **This is like** the case where the people **knowingly** served as partitions, for presumably the people now knew that they were being used for this purpose, **and it is** therefore **prohibited.**

Similarly, Levi's household used surrounding people to bring in *tivna* — straw; Zeiri's for *asfasta* — some variety of fodder; Rav Shimi bar Chiya's for water. In each case the people involved were presumably unaware of their halakhic role.

This leads to the broader conclusion: awareness of halakhic purpose converts the act of using people as a wall into a prohibited act of building on *Shabbat*. This is why *Tosafot*'s solution — moving the circle of people in successive steps to gradually bring Nechemya back — would not have worked. Each successive repositioning, done deliberately to extend a *mechitza*, would necessarily involve awareness, and the prohibition would apply.

## Techum Above Ground

### No Techum Aloft

The *gemara*'s treatment of the boat scenario rests on a crucial halakhic premise: *ein techum le-mala me-asura tefachim* — there is no *techum* obligation above ten *tefachim* off the ground. Since *techumim* are in any case rabbinic in nature — and on the sea or in the air even the opinion that a *techum* of twelve *mil* might be biblical does not apply, as all authorities agree that *techumin d'oraita* pertains only to travel on dry land — the prohibition of *techum* above ten *tefachim* is at most a rabbinic question, and given the dispute, the rule is resolved *le-kula*.

### Plane Landing

This principle applies equally to air travel. If a plane lands on *Shabbat* and throughout its approach and taxiing the aircraft remains above ten *tefachim* off the surface — which is typically the case during flight and much of the landing roll — the passenger's *techum* begins only from the point where the plane effectively comes to ground level contact with a four-by-four *tefach* base. Precisely when during taxiing this occurs — whether the aircraft's contact with the tarmac while still in motion constitutes grounding in the halakhic sense, given that the body of the plane is still elevated — is a question worth examining further. But the basic principle is that so long as the aircraft remains above ten *tefachim*, no *techum* has been established.

### Boat Passenger Ruling

This principle has practical application in a foundational *Shulchan Aruch* ruling on passengers on vessels. One who travels by boat on *Shabbat* and arrives at port: *im mi-she-nichnas ha-Shabbat ad she-higia la-namal le-olam hayata le-mala me-asura mi-karka ha-yam o ha-nahar* — if from the moment *Shabbat* began until arrival at port the vessel was always above ten *tefachim* from the floor of the sea or river — *yored ve-aino nimnah ve-yesh lo alpayim ama mi-makom she-paga bo le-mata me-asura* — the passenger disembarks and is not counted as having a prior *techum* location, and receives two thousand *amot* from the point where the vessel first came within ten *tefachim* of the ground.

### Cruise Ship Case

This ruling applies to cruise ships and any other vessel that travels in waters deep enough to remain above ten *tefachim* throughout the voyage. Wherever such a ship docks on *Shabbat*, the passengers receive two thousand *amot* from the landing point. They do not carry a *techum* established from some distant home port, having spent *Shabbat* above ten *tefachim* for the duration.

## Mitzvah Departure Case

### Mitzvah Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁיָּצָא בִּרְשׁוּת, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״כְּבָר נַעֲשָׂה מַעֲשֶׂה״ — יֵשׁ לוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who was permitted to leave** his Shabbat limit, i.e., he went out to testify that he had seen the new moon or for some life-saving purpose, **and they said to him** along the way: **The action has already been performed,** and there is no need for you to travel for that purpose, **he has two thousand cubits in each direction** from the location where he was standing when this was told to him.

The *gemara* now turns to a new *mishna*. *Mi she-yatza bi-reshut* — one who went out *chutz la-techum* with halakhic permission. The cases paradigmatically cited in *Masechet Rosh Hashanah* include one who went to give testimony regarding the new month, a midwife going to assist at a birth, or one going to rescue a person in mortal danger. When a person leaves the *techum* to perform a *mitzvah*, the rabbis granted a leniency: upon arrival at the destination, one receives two thousand *amot* in every direction from that new location, even without having made an *eiruv*.

### Services Unneeded

The *mishna* now addresses a complication: *ve-amru lo k'var na'asa ma'aseh* — and they told him, the situation has already been resolved. The midwife arrives and learns that another midwife delivered the baby. The witness arrives and learns that enough other witnesses have testified. At that point, the *mitzvah* purpose of the journey has evaporated. What is this person's status? *Yesh lo alpayim ama le-chol ruach* — he has two thousand *amot* in every direction from wherever he stands when he receives that information. He does not get to continue to the destination city and receive two thousand *amot* from there. He may be stuck in the middle of a road, two thousand *amot* from neither his home nor his destination — a potentially uncomfortable situation, and one that *Hatzolah* volunteers and emergency responders must navigate in practice.

### Original Techum Reversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם — כְּאִילּוּ לֹא יָצָא. כׇּל הַיּוֹצְאִים לְהַצִּיל — חוֹזְרִים לִמְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **If he was within** his original **limit,** it is considered **as if he had not left** his limit, and he may return to his original location. The Sages formulated a principle: **All who go out to** battle and **save** lives **may return to their** original **locations** on Shabbat.

The *mishna* then adds: *im haya be-soch ha-techum ke-ilu lo yatza* — if he was still within his original *techum* when he received this information, it is as if he never left. Rava clarifies what this means: *ke-ilu lo yatza mi-toch beito* — it is as if he never left his house. That is, he does not receive a new two thousand *amot* centered on his current position within the *techum*. He reverts entirely to his original *techum* from home. The *gemara* notes that one might have thought *ho'il ve-akar akar* — since he uprooted himself halakhically and was granted the traveler's two thousand *amot*, perhaps that grant persists even upon return. The *mishna* teaches otherwise.

### Overlapping Techumim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״אִם הָיָה בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם כְּאִילּוּ לֹא יָצָא״? אָמַר רַבָּה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם הָיָה בְּתוֹךְ תְּחוּם שֶׁלּוֹ — כְּאִילּוּ לֹא יָצָא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the statement: **If he was within** his original **limit, it is** considered **as if he had never left?** Given that he has not left his original boundary, it is clear that he remains within his original limit. **Rabba said:** The mishna **is saying as follows: If he was within his** original **limit, it is** considered **as if he had never left his house.** He is allowed to walk two thousand cubits in each direction from his house.

Rachumi bar Chiya offers a more expansive reading of the *mishna*'s ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא אָמַר, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם הָיוּ תְּחוּמִין שֶׁנָּתְנוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים מוּבְלָעִין בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם שֶׁלּוֹ, כְּאִילּוּ לֹא יָצָא מִתְּחוּמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya said** that the mishna is **saying as follows: If** he left his original Shabbat limit, but **the** new **limit** of two thousand cubits in each direction **that the Sages granted him is subsumed within his** original **limit,** so that if he walks those two thousand cubits, he can return to within his original limit, then **it is as if he had never left his** original **limit,** and he may return to his house.

He reads *im hayu techumim she-nasnu lo chachamim muvla'in be-soch techumo ke-ilu lo yatza mi-techumo* as follows: if the two thousand *amot* granted to him from his current position — wherever he received the news — *overlap* with his original *techum* from home, then he is treated as if he never left his original *techum*. The practical consequence is significant: since both *techumim* are operative and they overlap, he may travel from his home two thousand *amot* in one direction and from his current position two thousand *amot* in the other direction, with free movement throughout the overlapping zone. In one reading, Rashi indicates that the two *techumim* effectively merge into a single enlarged *techum* — allowing movement from the home's far boundary all the way to the position's far boundary in the opposite direction, potentially close to eight thousand *amot* in total.

### Overlap Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר הַבְלָעַת תְּחוּמִין מִילְּתָא הִיא, וּמָר סָבַר לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **With regard to what** principle **do** Rabba and Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya **disagree?** One **Sage,** Rav Shimi, **holds** that **the subsuming of** Shabbat **limits,** i.e., if one's original limit is subsumed within the new limit, one may pass from one to the other, **is something** significant and may be relied upon, whereas this **Sage,** Rabba, **holds** that **it is nothing** significant and cannot be relied upon.

The underlying dispute is whether *havla'at techumim* — the absorption of one *techum* within another, or their overlap — *milsa hi*, constitutes something significant, or whether it is halakhically inconsequential. According to the position that it is significant, the overlap creates a merged *techum* from which one may benefit in both directions. According to the opposing view, the two *techumim* remain independent, and overlap does not extend one's rights.

### Cave Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַבָּה: וְאַתְּ לָא תִּסְבְּרָא דְּהַבְלָעַת תְּחוּמִין מִילְּתָא הִיא? וּמָה אִילּוּ שָׁבַת בִּמְעָרָה, שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ אַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים וְעַל גַּגָּהּ פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּעַת אֲלָפִים אַמָּה — לֹא נִמְצָא מְהַלֵּךְ אֶת כּוּלָּהּ, וְחוּצָה לָהּ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rabba: Do you not hold** that **the subsuming of** Shabbat **limits is something** significant? **And what if he established residence in a cave** that has entrances at its two ends, which **on the inside** of the cave **is four thousand** cubits across, **but atop its roof** it **is less than four thousand cubits** across? Is it **not the case that he may walk the entire** length of the roof **and two thousand cubits outside it** in either direction? The entire interior of the cave is considered as if it were four cubits, and he is permitted to walk another two thousand cubits in each direction from each of its entrances. Consequently, he is permitted to walk along the roof, two thousand cubits from the eastern entrance in the direction of the western entrance and vice versa. However, since the distance across the roof is less than four thousand cubits, these two limits are subsumed within one another, and he is permitted to walk the entire length of the roof, given that when two limits are subsumed within one another, one may pass from one to the other.

Abaye challenges Rava: surely you must agree that *havla'at techumim milsa hi*. He proves it from the case of one who began *Shabbat* at the bottom of a cave (*shavat bi-me'ara*). If the interior of the cave at ground level is four thousand *amot* wide, and the cave narrows toward the top so that the opening at each end is less than four thousand *amot* across, the person at the bottom — surrounded by the cave's walls — has established his *techum* in an enclosed space. He exits either opening and receives two thousand *amot* in that direction. But since the cave bottom is four thousand *amot* wide, and both exits feed from the same enclosed space, he effectively has two thousand *amot* beyond each exit — even though the total distance from one outer boundary to the other exceeds four thousand *amot*. This appears to be a case where the two *techumim* overlap and merge into one enlarged domain.

Rava's response is that the cave case is categorically different: there, the person *began Shabbat* surrounded by *mechitzot* — the walls of the cave enclosed him on all sides from the start of *Shabbat*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָא שָׁנֵי לָךְ בֵּין הֵיכָא דְּשָׁבַת בָּאֲוִיר מְחִיצּוֹת מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, לְהֵיכָא דְּלֹא שָׁבַת בַּאֲוִיר מְחִיצּוֹת מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to** Abaye: **Do you not distinguish between** a case **where** the person **established residence within the airspace of partitions** before Shabbat **while it was still day,** as in the case of the cave, **and** a case **where he did not establish residence within the airspace of partitions** before Shabbat **while it was still day,** as in the case of the mishna? The principle governing the Shabbat limits being subsumed in one another only applies in the former case, where both of the Shabbat limits were established before Shabbat, but not in the latter case, where the two limits were established at different times, one before Shabbat and one on Shabbat.

The two exits are not two separate positions but two openings of a single enclosed space. It is the cave's enclosure, present from the outset, that generates the two directional *techumim*; they are not two independent *techumim* that happen to overlap. The case of the traveler whose *techum* from his current position overlaps his *techum* from home is structurally different — those are genuinely two distinct *techumim* — and their overlap does not necessarily produce the same merger.

## Coerced Departure

### Coercion Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהוֹתְצִיאוּהוּ נׇכְרִים אוֹ רוּחַ רָעָה — אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who was taken out of his** Shabbat **limit by gentiles or by a** spirit of madness or compulsion — **he has only four cubits** from wherever he was deposited.

The *gemara* moves to the final *mishna* of the *daf*. *Mi she-hotziu'uhu nochrim o ruach ra'ah* — one who was taken out of his *techum* by gentiles or by a *ruach ra'ah* — typically understood as a spirit of insanity or some compulsion that removed him involuntarily — *ein lo ela arba amot*. He has only four *amot* from wherever he was deposited. He had no agency in the departure; the exit was entirely coerced. The halakha therefore does not grant him any extended *techum* beyond the bare minimum.

### Return Clause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הֶחֱזִיר אוֹתוֹ לִמְקוֹמוֹ — כְּאִילּוּ לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If he was returned to his original place — it is as though he never left.**

If, however, the gentiles or the spirit returned him to his original place — *ke-ilu lo yatza* — it is as though he never left. His original *techum* is fully restored.

### Fold/Corral Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְתָנוּהוּ בְּדִיר אוֹ בְּסַהַר — רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמְרִים: מְהַלֵּךְ אֶת כּוּלָּהּ. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמְרִים: אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **If they placed him in a pen or a corral, Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah say: He may walk the entire** enclosure. **Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Akiva say: He has only four cubits.**

The *mishna* then presents a dispute regarding a specific subcase: *netanuhu be-dir o be-sahar* — if they placed him in a *dir* (a fold or pen for animals) or a *sahar* (a corral or enclosure). Within such an enclosed space, does he have the right to walk throughout the entire area, or is he still limited to four *amot*?

### Rabban Gamliel Position

Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah rule: *mehalech et kulah* — he may walk the entire enclosure. Even though he was brought *chutz la-techum* by coercion and receives only four *amot*, if those four *amot* bring him within a pre-existing enclosed space, he may traverse the entire space. This follows the logic of Rabban Gamliel's broader position on enclosed spaces and *techum*.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Position

Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Akiva disagree: *ein lo ela arba amot* — even within the fold or corral, he is limited to four *amot*. The coercive nature of his removal, in their view, restricts him to the absolute minimum regardless of the physical structure he finds himself in.

### Brindisi Ship Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבָּאוּ מִפְּרוּנְדִּיסִין וְהִפְלִיגָה סְפִינָתָן בַּיָּם — רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה הָלְכוּ אֶת כּוּלָּהּ, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לֹא זָזוּ מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, אֶלָּא שֶׁרָצוּ לְהַחְמִיר עַל עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **It once happened that travelers came from Brindisi, and their ship was sailing at sea, Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah walked through the entire ship, Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Akiva did not move beyond four cubits, but they chose to be stringent upon themselves.**

The *mishna* records a historical incident that maps directly onto this dispute. *Ma'aseh she-ba'u mi-Prundisin* — there was an episode involving travelers coming from Brindisi, the Italian port — *ve-hifligah sefinatam ba-yam* — and their ship was sailing at sea on *Shabbat*. Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah *halchu et kulah* — walked through the entire ship, treating the vessel as an enclosed space in which they were free to move. Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Akiva *lo zazu me-arba amot* — did not move beyond four *amot*, *ela she-ratzu le-hachmir al atzmam* — because they chose to be stringent upon themselves. The phrasing is notable: the *mishna* does not say they ruled the halakha required it, but rather that they voluntarily adopted the stringent position. Both pairs of *Tannaim* were on the same ship, and each acted in accordance with his own ruling.
