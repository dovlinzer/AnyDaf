# Bava Batra 173 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Identical Names Problem

### Two Dov Linzers

Daf 173 opens by continuing a discussion the Gemara had developed in the preceding pages concerning what might be called a "bare bond" — a document that says, in effect, "I, Dov Linzer, owe you a thousand dollars," where the identity of the creditor is determined by whoever happens to be holding the document. That discussion led the Gemara to confront the case of the *mishnah* that deals with two people who share an identical name. The *mishnah* implies that if I hold a document stating that Eli owes Dov Linzer a thousand dollars, and I am Dov Linzer, the document is valid and Eli cannot defeat my claim by pointing to the existence of another Dov Linzer in town. The mere fact that I am bearing the *shtar* creates a presumption that it was written in my name; we are not, as the Gemara put it, *chashinan lenifila dechad* — we do not entertain the concern that specifically that one other person with my name happened to drop it and I happened to find it.

### Otiyot Niknot Bimesira

At the top of 173a, however, the Gemara confronts a *braita* that complicates this picture considerably. The *braita* states: *keshaym she'ain motzi'in shtar chov zeh al zeh* — just as two people with identical names cannot use a debt document against one another, so too neither of them can use such a document against third parties. At first glance this seems puzzling. If I am Dov Linzer and I hold a *shtar* stating that Eli owes Dov Linzer a thousand dollars, why should the existence of another Dov Linzer prevent me from collecting?

The Gemara resolves this by explaining that the *mishnah* and *braita* disagree over the principle of *otiyot niknot bimesira* — whether the debt recorded in a document is transferred by the mere act of handing over the document. To appreciate how this principle bears on the two-Dov-Linzer problem, it helps to understand what *otiyot niknot bimesira* means in its clearest application. If I hold a *shtar* stating that Eli owes Dov Linzer a thousand dollars and I hand that *shtar* to Charlie, does Charlie now become the creditor, despite his name appearing nowhere in the document? According to the view that *otiyot niknot bimesira* — that a debt is transferred by delivery — the answer is yes. Whoever holds the *shtar* is presumed to be the party to whom the debt is owed, regardless of whose name is written in it.

This principle, once established, has an immediate consequence for the two-Dov-Linzer case. Even if we entertain the theoretical concern that the *shtar* may have originally been written for the other Dov Linzer, the very fact that I am holding it creates a legal presumption — either that it was written in my name, or that the other Dov Linzer transferred it to me by handing it over. If *otiyot niknot bimesira*, that theoretical concern is legally irrelevant. The *tana* of our *mishnah* therefore holds *otiyot niknot bimesira*, and the bearer of the *shtar* may collect. The *tana* of the *braita*, by contrast, holds *ein otiyot niknot bimesira* — a document cannot be transferred by mere delivery; a new *shtar* is required to effect any transfer. Without that mechanism, holding a *shtar* written in someone else's name proves nothing, and the concern that it originated with the other Dov Linzer therefore becomes legally operative.

### Proof of Transfer Debate

The Gemara then offers a second way to frame the same dispute. One might separate two distinct questions: (1) Does the act of handing over the *shtar* actually accomplish a transfer of the debt? (2) If it does, does holding the *shtar* constitute sufficient proof that such a transfer occurred? The Gemara introduces the formulation *ve-iba'is eima de-chulei alma otiyot niknot bimesira* — both *tanna'im* may agree that handing over a document does in fact transfer the debt. The dispute concerns only whether the holder of a *shtar* bearing someone else's name must *tzarich lehavi rayah* — bring additional proof that the transfer actually took place — or whether holding the document is itself a sufficient presumption.

The Gemara cites a direct dispute between Abaye and Rava on this question. Abaye holds *tzarich lehavi rayah*: even if *otiyot niknot bimesira*, a person appearing in court with a *shtar* written in the wrong name must bring corroborating proof — witnesses, an additional document, or other evidence — that the debt was actually transferred to him. Rava holds *eino tzarich lehavi rayah*: no additional proof is required; the fact of holding the *shtar* is itself the presumption.

Abaye derives his position from a *braita* dealing with one of several brothers who appears in court bearing a *shtar chov* written in his deceased father's name. The son claims the debt was assigned to him when the estate was divided; the *braita* rules that *alav lehavi rayah* — the burden of proof falls on him. Abaye reads this as evidence for a general rule that holding a *shtar* in the wrong name requires corroborating proof. Rava, however, rejects this inference: *shani achin de-shamtu mehadadi* — brothers are different, because they live together and have access to one another's documents, making the concern that one brother took the *shtar* illegitimately quite realistic. It is precisely in the brother case, where suspicion of improper access is elevated, that we require additional proof; with unrelated parties, who would have no such access to the other's *shtar*, no corroboration is needed.

The Gemara also records the *ikka d'amri* — the reverse version of the dispute — in which Rava himself cites the same *braita* about brothers as the source of his own position. In that version, Rava reads the *braita* as proof precisely because it speaks only about brothers: it is *achin hu de-shamtu mehadadi* — specifically brothers, where snatching is common, who must bring proof. Other people, by contrast, do not. Abaye would counter that even in the brother case one might have thought additional proof unnecessary — perhaps brothers are actually *more* careful with one another than with outsiders, so that a transfer among brothers would be presumed legitimate. The *braita* therefore needed to tell us that even among brothers, proof is required. But this is all within the framework of the general rule, which according to Abaye always requires additional proof.

Returning now to the case of two Dov Linzers: the *mishnah*'s position that I may collect using my *shtar* reflects either (a) *lo chashinan lenifila de-chad* — we simply do not worry about the remote possibility that the other person with my name dropped it, which is the most intuitive reading; or (b) even if we entertain that concern theoretically, once we accept *otiyot niknot bimesira* and *ein tzarich lehavi rayah*, any bearer of a *shtar* is the presumed creditor, regardless of whether the name matches. The *braita*'s stricter position — that neither Dov Linzer can collect — reflects both *chashinan lenifila de-chad* and *tzarich lehavi rayah*: we are concerned about the possibility of confusion, and holding the *shtar* alone does not establish entitlement.

## Same-Name Debts Allowed

### Braita Leniency

The Gemara then introduces a third text — still another *braita* — that is even more permissive than the *mishnah*. This *braita* states that *keshem shemotzi'in hen shtar chov aleihen kach motzi'in zeh al zeh* — just as the two people with identical names can use debt documents between themselves, so too can either of them use a document against the other. This is a striking position. If a *shtar* states that Dov Linzer owes Dov Linzer a thousand dollars, either party might claim to be the creditor and accuse the other of being the debtor. How could such a document ever be adjudicated?

The Gemara asks *be-mai kamiflegi* — what is the substantive legal question underlying this dispute?

### Writing Without Lender

The answer, the Gemara explains, is the principle of *kotvin shtar le-loveh af al pi she'ein malveh imo* — whether we permit a scribe and witnesses to write a promissory note for the borrower in advance, even without the lender present. The halachic background here is that the borrower, not the lender, is considered the party disadvantaged by the document, and it is therefore permissible, under certain views, to write the document at the borrower's request before the loan actually takes place. The borrower then holds the document until the lender hands over the money, at which point the borrower gives the *shtar* to the lender.

The *tana* of the *mishnah* holds that one *may* write the *shtar* for the borrower without the lender present. The problem this creates in an identical-name case is obvious: I go to the scribe and witnesses and say, "Write a *shtar* that Yosef ben Shimon borrowed from Yosef ben Shimon a thousand dollars — I am planning to borrow from my cousin who shares my name." I walk out with the document, and instead of giving it to my cousin when he lends me the money, I turn the tables and use it against him, claiming that he is the borrower and I am the creditor. Because the *mishnah* permits writing the *shtar* for the borrower in advance, this scheme is possible, and therefore neither Yosef ben Shimon can use the document against the other — each one can always claim the other is the debtor.

The *tana* of this permissive *braita*, by contrast, holds that one may *not* write the *shtar* for the borrower without the lender being present. If the lender must be there at the time of writing, both parties are present and the lender ensures that the *shtar* does not leave the room until the loan has been handed over. The risk of the scheme described above is therefore neutralized, and consequently the document can be used — even though both parties share an identical name.

### Receipt Ambiguity

The Gemara then cites a further clause that addresses what happens when a receipt (*shovar*) creates ambiguity. If a *malveh* finds among his own documents a receipt stating that Yosef ben Shimon's *shtar* has been paid up — *parua* — he cannot collect from either Yosef ben Shimon, since he cannot determine which one the receipt refers to. The Gemara asks an obvious question: why do we need the receipt to prevent collection? Even without it, the *mishnah* has already established that a bearer cannot enforce a *shtar* against either of two identically named parties, since each will claim the *shtar* refers to the other.

Rav Yirmiyah resolves this by explaining that the *shtaros* in question are *meshulashim* — written with three generations of names. One *shtar* says Reuven ben Shimon ben Yaakov owes a thousand dollars, and the other says Reuven ben Shimon ben Moshe owes five thousand dollars. With three-generation identification, both *shtaros* are individually valid and usable. The problem arises only because the *shovar* identifies the debtor using only two generations — "Reuven ben Shimon paid up his *shtar*" — and it is therefore ambiguous which of the two men it refers to. The receipt neutralizes both otherwise-valid documents.

The Gemara then asks: since the *shtaros* are *meshulashim* and thus distinguishable, why not simply look at which three-generation name appears in the receipt? Rav Oshaya answers: the *shtaros* went three generations, but the *shovar* was written with only two generations — *meshulashim be-shtar ve-ein meshulashim be-shovar*. That is the source of the ambiguity, and it is entirely plausible as a scribal oversight.

## Solutions to Ambiguity

### Three Generations

Abaye, however, offers a different reading of the *mishnah* altogether. He reframes the case: it is not the *malveh* who found the receipt — it is the *loveh* who produced it. The two Yosef ben Shimons are here the *creditors*, not the debtors; a single borrower took loans from each of them. Without any receipt, each Yosef ben Shimon could proceed independently: the one holding the *shtar* for a thousand dollars could collect his thousand, and the one holding the *shtar* for ten thousand could collect his ten thousand, since each *shtar* is presumed to belong to the person holding it. But the borrower produces a receipt — *shtar she-Yosef ben Shimon alai parua* — the *shtar* that Yosef ben Shimon holds against me is paid up. Since this receipt does not specify which Yosef ben Shimon's debt has been satisfied, neither can collect.

The Gemara asks: given this ambiguity, *keitzad ya'asu* — what should be done? *Yashalshu* — write three generations of names. If the *shtaros* themselves identify the creditors as Yosef ben Shimon ben Yaakov and Yosef ben Shimon ben Moshe, and the receipt says only "Yosef ben Shimon," we still have the problem of ambiguity in the receipt. Here the Gemara confirms the point: it is precisely when the *shtaros* are *meshulashim* but the *shovar* is not that the difficulty arises. The *mishnah* then adds that if both creditors happen to be *Kohanim* or both *Yisraelim* — such that the priestly designation cannot serve as a distinguishing marker and the family names happen to coincide through three generations — one must extend the identification to four or five generations until a distinction emerges. Notably, the Gemara nowhere considers using a mother's name or other identifiers as an alternative solution.

### Power of Attorney

The *Rashbam* adds a practical solution for the case of identical-name ambiguity with a shared receipt. If neither Yosef ben Shimon can individually collect his full amount — since the borrower will claim the receipt satisfies whichever *shtar* is being pressed — the two creditors can jointly execute a *shtar harsha'ah*, a power of attorney. One Yosef ben Shimon authorizes the other to act on his behalf, and that agent appears in court and argues: "You owe me at least a thousand dollars. Either you owe me a thousand dollars from my own claim, or you owe me even more because of the other Yosef ben Shimon's claim — and the receipt can have eliminated at most one of those two claims." By consolidating the two claims into a single court appearance rather than two separate proceedings, the agent can ensure that at minimum the lower amount is recovered. Individually, each creditor is defeated; together, they can collect the lesser sum.

## Deathbed Confession

### All Debts Nullified

A new case introduces the principle of an honest person's deathbed ambiguity. A man says to his son: *shtar bein shtarotai parua ve'eini yode'a eizehu* — one of the promissory notes among my documents has been paid up, but I cannot remember which one. The ruling is *shtarot kulam paruim* — all of the notes are treated as paid up, and the son cannot collect any of them.

One might ask what legal principle underlies this ruling. It appears to rest on *hoda'at ba'al din* — the admission of the litigant himself. The holder of the *shtaros*, while still alive, admitted that one of his claims had been satisfied. Even though his admission was in the form of a *safek* — an uncertainty — it is apparently sufficient to neutralize all of his *shtaros*. This is a somewhat surprising application of *hoda'at ba'al din*, since he did not admit to any specific debt being paid; nevertheless, once he acknowledged that one of the notes is void and cannot say which, the son is left unable to use any of them.

### Specific vs. General

Rava introduces a related but distinct case. If I say to Michael *shtar lecha beyadi parua* — "the *shtar* that I hold against you is paid up" — and it turns out I hold two *shtaros* against Michael, one for a large amount and one for a smaller amount, the ruling is *hagadol parua ve-hakatan eino parua*: I can still collect the smaller amount. The reasoning is that whichever *shtar* I meant to declare paid up, Michael certainly owes me at least the lesser sum. If the larger *shtar* is the one I admitted to be paid, he still owes me the smaller; if the smaller is the one I admitted to be paid, he certainly owes me the larger. Either way, the lesser amount is collectable.

However, if I say *hov shelecha beyadi parua* — "the *debt* you owe me is paid up" — using the word *chov* rather than *shtar*, the ruling changes: *shtarot kulan paruim*, all notes are voided. The word *chov* refers to the totality of the debt, not to a specific document, and therefore is read as an admission that the entire outstanding obligation has been satisfied.

### Burden of Proof

Ravina then challenges Rava: if we interpret indeterminate language so expansively in favor of the opposing party, should we not apply the same logic elsewhere? If a *shtar* states that Dov sold Michael "my field," and Dov owns multiple fields, should we assume Michael acquired the largest one — just as in the debt case we assumed the larger *shtar* was the one declared paid up? Or if the *shtar* says "the field I own," should we read it as encompassing all of Dov's fields?

Rava responds: *hatam yad ba'al ha-shtar al hatachtona* — in those cases, the burden of proof falls on the person wielding the *shtar*. The point is not that the ambiguous language has an inherent meaning; rather, every case of genuine ambiguity is resolved in favor of the party against whom the *shtar* is being asserted, following the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*. In the debt case, Michael is being asked to pay, so we resolve the ambiguity in his favor by assuming it is the *larger* debt that was declared paid — leaving only the smaller obligation enforceable. In the sale case, Michael is trying to take Dov's field, so the ambiguity is resolved against Michael, and we assume only the smaller field was sold unless Michael can prove otherwise. The word itself does not determine the outcome; the allocation of the burden of proof does.

## Guarantor Institution

### Not First Creditor

The *mishnah* now opens an entirely new topic: the institution of the *arev*, the guarantor or co-signer. The *mishnah* rules *lo yifra min ha-arev techila* — the lender may not collect from the guarantor first. If I lend money to Michael and David serves as guarantor, I must first pursue Michael. Only if Michael defaults may I then turn to David.

Before analyzing the mechanics, it is worth noting the broader resonance of the term *arev*. The phrase *kol Yisrael arevim ze lazeh* — all of Israel are guarantors for one another — uses the same root in a non-legal, moral sense: just as a legal guarantor becomes responsible for another's debt, each Jew bears a form of responsibility for the religious conduct of fellow Jews. Here, of course, we are concerned with the strictly legal institution.

### Direct Collection Rights

The *mishnah* provides one exception to the rule of *lo yifra min ha-arev techila*: if the lender stipulated at the time of the loan *al menat she-efra mimi she-ertzeh* — on condition that I may collect from whomever I choose — then *yifra min ha-arev*, the lender may go directly to the guarantor. This stipulation must, of course, have been made with David's knowledge and consent; it is not a unilateral reservation by the lender.

### Visible Assets Exception

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds a further qualification. Even if the lender made this stipulation, *im yesh nechasim la-loveh bein kach u-vein kach lo yifra min ha-arev* — if the borrower visibly has assets, the lender still may not bypass him and go directly to the guarantor. The underlying logic relates to the degree of the borrower's apparent solvency. There is a meaningful distinction between a borrower who clearly has accessible property — in which case even the stipulation does not permit going straight to the guarantor — and a borrower whose assets are not readily apparent, in which case the lender may need to choose between extended judicial proceedings and invoking the guarantor. The Gemara will elaborate on this distinction further.

### Ketubah Guarantor

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel also addresses a separate scenario involving an *arev* in the context of a *ketubah*. If a husband could not persuade his prospective wife to marry him without a guarantor for the *ketubah*, and David agreed to co-sign — meaning that if the husband divorces her and lacks the funds to pay the *ketubah* settlement, David will cover it — a problem arises when the husband actually proceeds toward divorce. The concern is *she-ma ya'asu knunya al nechasim shel zeh ve-yahazir ishto* — that the husband and wife may be colluding to extract money from the guarantor and then remarry. The solution is *yadirenah hana'ah*: when divorcing, the husband must declare that he renounces all benefit from his ex-wife, making remarriage impossible and thereby eliminating the incentive for collusion. The *Rashbam* notes that this must be done through a *neder al da'at rabbim* — a vow formulated in a manner that cannot be annulled — to ensure the protection is real.

## Guarantor Mechanism

### Bail Bondsman Theory

The Gemara now asks *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for the rule that I cannot collect from the guarantor first? Before addressing this, one might have asked an even more fundamental question: what is the basis for the institution of *areivut* at all? Why should David's verbal declaration that he will stand as guarantor create any legal obligation? That question will be addressed shortly; the Gemara begins with the narrower question of priority.

Rava and Rav Yosef together offer an explanation: *gavra ashlimit li gavra ashlimi lach* — you entrusted to me a person; I return to you that person. Under this theory, the guarantor's role is analogous to that of a bail bondsman: David's function is not to pay the debt but to ensure that Michael remains available and appears before the court. As long as Michael can be produced, David's obligation is discharged. Only if Michael has fled entirely and cannot be located does David become liable to pay.

Rav Nachman challenges this sharply: *hai dina de-Parsaye* — that is the law of the Persians, not ours. (The Gemara clarifies that the characterization is inverted: Persian law actually allowed the lender to go directly to the guarantor; it is the "bail bondsman" reading that is being called arbitrary and unfounded.) The objection is substantive: characterizing the guarantor's role as merely producing the debtor drastically and illogically narrows the institution. The whole point of a guarantor is that the lender can rely on *someone* for payment if the borrower defaults — not merely that someone will drag the borrower into court.

The Gemara therefore rejects the bail bondsman theory as the basis for the rule. Instead, the Gemara clarifies that *lo yifra min ha-arev techila* should not be read as "the lender can *never* collect from the guarantor" — which is what the words seem to say on a plain reading — but rather as *lo yitba areiv techila*: the lender may not *pursue* the guarantor first. The guarantor is fully liable; he is simply the second line of defense. A *braita* confirms this reading explicitly, using the language *lo yitba areiv techila* — the guarantor cannot be summoned first — while also preserving the exception when the lender stipulated discretionary collection rights at the outset.

### Biblical Source

The Gemara turns to the question of where the institution of *areivut* comes from in the first place. Rav Huna asks: *minayin le-areiv de-mishta'abed* — what is the source that a guarantor becomes legally obligated? The answer cited is a verse from Bereishit: *anochi e'erevenu mi-yadi tevakshenu* — "I will guarantee him; from my hand shall you seek him" (Bereishit 43:9). Yehudah's declaration to his father Yaakov is understood as establishing the paradigm of the *arev*: Yehudah accepts personal liability for Binyamin's return, such that if Binyamin is not returned, Yaakov may seek restitution from Yehudah himself.

### Kablanus vs. Areivus

Rav Chisda immediately challenges this proof: *ha kablanut hi* — what Yehudah did was not *areivut* but *kablanus*. The distinction is a significant one. *Areivut* is secondary liability: the guarantor stands in the background, and the lender's primary relationship is with the borrower. *Kablanus* is direct liability: the *kablan* inserts himself into the transaction as a primary party, effectively saying "lend the money to him and I will be the one responsible." The difference in practice is that a *kablan* — an *arev kablan* — can be pursued directly by the lender without first attempting to collect from the borrower, whereas an ordinary *arev* cannot. Rav Chisda points to the verse *tenahu al yadi va'ani ashivenu alecha* — "give him into my care and I will return him to you" — which uses language of direct personal responsibility, making it an instance of *kablanus* rather than background *areivut*, and therefore not a proof for the general institution.

Rav Yitzchak therefore offers a different biblical source, from *Mishlei*: *lakach bigdo ki areiv zar* — "take his garment, for he became guarantor for a stranger" (Mishlei 20:16). The verse reflects the practical reality that a guarantor's property can be seized when the debt is not paid — a clear indication that the guarantor bears genuine legal liability. Further verses from *Mishlei* (6:1–5) are cited, using the root *arev* in contexts that unmistakably connote binding financial obligation.

## How Liability Works

### Areivut vs. Asmachta

Having established the institution's biblical basis, the Gemara turns to the deeper question of *mechanism*. Even granting that the Torah endorses the idea of a guarantor, how does a purely verbal commitment — David saying "don't worry, I'll back the loan" — create a legal obligation? Normally, to obligate oneself to pay money to another person, one would need at minimum a *kinyan sudar* — a formal act of acquisition, taking hold of a garment or similar object — to effectuate the commitment. David performs no such act. He merely speaks.

Moreover, even if we could find a mechanism, there is the problem of *asmachta*. An *asmachta* is a conditional promise that the promisor does not genuinely expect to have to fulfill — an overcommitment made in the hope that the triggering condition never materializes. The classic ruling is *asmachta lo kanya* — an *asmachta* does not create a binding obligation, because there is no genuine intent (*gemirat da'at*) to pay. A guarantor is a paradigmatic case: David agrees to back the loan precisely because he expects Michael to repay it himself. He hopes, and perhaps genuinely believes, he will never have to pay. This looks like a textbook *asmachta*.

The Gemara frames this as a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose. Rabbi Yose holds *asmachta kanya* — an *asmachta* does create a binding obligation — and therefore an *arev mishta'abed*, the guarantor is legally obligated. Rabbi Yehuda holds *asmachta lo kanya*, and therefore *arev lo mishta'abed*, the guarantor does not become legally bound.

### Hana'ah of Reliance

This creates an immediate practical difficulty. Rav Ashi points it out to Ameimar: *hama'asim b'chol yom de-asmachta lo kanya* — the daily practice of the courts is to invalidate *asmachta* obligations. Yet *va'arev mishta'abed* — those same courts regularly enforce guarantor obligations. How can both be true simultaneously? If *areivut* is a form of *asmachta*, the courts should be throwing out guarantor claims just as they throw out other *asmachta* claims.

Rav Ashi himself resolves the paradox: *be-hai hana'ah de-kamheimin leh gemar u-mishta'abed nafsheh* — because of the benefit (*hana'ah*) that the lender is trusting him, the guarantor truly commits and becomes obligated. The word *hana'ah* here should not be read superficially as mere social prestige — "how nice that people trust me." The deeper point is this: the guarantor knows that the lender is extending the loan *solely because* of his commitment. He understands that his word is the operative cause of the transaction. A person of integrity who knows that someone else is relying on him — that the money changed hands only because of his promise — cannot truly say he did not mean it. He knows the lender is depending on the veracity of his commitment, and therefore he truly *does* commit, fully and without reservation. It is that genuine *gemirat da'at*, arising from the knowledge that the lender is relying on him, that distinguishes *areivut* from ordinary *asmachta* and renders the guarantor's obligation fully enforceable.
