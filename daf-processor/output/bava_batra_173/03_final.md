# Bava Batra 173 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Identical Names Problem

### Two Dov Linzers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהָיוּ בְּעִיר אַחַת, שֵׁם אֶחָד יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן וְשֵׁם אַחֵר יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן – אֵינָן יְכוֹלִין לְהוֹצִיא שְׁטַר חוֹב זֶה עַל זֶה, וְלֹא אַחֵר יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא עֲלֵיהֶן שְׁטַר חוֹב. הָא הֵם עַל אֲחֵרִים – יְכוֹלִין; וְאַמַּאי? לֵיחוּשׁ לִנְפִילָה! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לִנְפִילָה לָא חָיְישִׁינַן?
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in the mishna: If there are **two** people **who were** living **in one city, one named Yosef ben Shimon and the other** also **named Yosef ben Shimon, one cannot present a promissory note against the other,** as the purported debtor can claim: On the contrary, it is you who owed me money; you repaid me and I returned this note to you upon payment. **Nor can another,** third person, **present a promissory note against** either of **them. This** indicates that one of **them can** present a promissory note **against others. But why** can they do so? **Let** there be **a concern for** the possibility of the promissory note **falling** from one Yosef ben Shimon and being found by the other. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from this** mishna that **we are not concerned for** the possibility of the promissory note **falling** from one Yosef ben Shimon and being found by the other?

Daf 173 opens by continuing a discussion the Gemara had developed in the preceding pages concerning what might be called a "bare bond" — a document that says, in effect, "I, Dov Linzer, owe you a thousand dollars," where the identity of the creditor is determined by whoever happens to be holding the document. That discussion led the Gemara to confront the case of the *mishnah* that deals with two people who share an identical name. The *mishnah* implies that if I hold a document stating that Eli owes Dov Linzer a thousand dollars, and I am Dov Linzer, the document is valid and Eli cannot defeat my claim by pointing to the existence of another Dov Linzer in town. The mere fact that I am bearing the *shtar* creates a presumption that it was written in my name; we are not, as the Gemara put it, *chashinan lenifila dechad* — we do not entertain the concern that specifically that one other person with my name happened to drop it and I happened to find it.

### Otiyot Niknot Bimesira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין מוֹצִיאִין שְׁטַר חוֹב זֶה עַל זֶה – כָּךְ אֵין מוֹצִיאִין עַל אֲחֵרִים, בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what of **that which is taught** in another *baraita*: **Just as** the two men named Yosef ben Shimon **cannot produce a promissory note, this** one **against that** one, **so they cannot produce** promissory notes **against others,** as those others can claim: I did not borrow from you but from the other Yosef ben Shimon, and somehow the note he was holding is in your possession? This *baraita* contradicts the mishna, from which it was inferred that one of the two men named Yosef ben Shimon can produce promissory notes to collect the debt from others. **With regard to what** principle **do** the mishna and *baraita* **disagree?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי –
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree with regard to** whether **letters,** i.e., the content of a promissory note, **are acquired by** merely **transferring** the document to a new owner. In other words, they disagree about whether or not a creditor can transfer his right to collect a debt to another by merely transferring the document to him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא דִּידַן סָבַר: אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה; וְתַנָּא בָּרָא סָבַר: אֵין אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The *tanna* of our** mishna **maintains** that **letters are acquired by transferring** the document. There is no concern that the promissory note was inadvertently transferred from one Yosef ben Shimon to the other by being lost and then found, as explained earlier. Nevertheless, there is still the possibility that one Yosef ben Shimon deliberately transferred the promissory note to the other, transferring the right of collection to him. According to this *tanna*, the second Yosef ben Shimon is fully entitled to collect the debt by producing the promissory note. **And the *tanna* of the *baraita* holds** that **letters are not acquired by transferring** the document, so that even if one Yosef ben Shimon transferred his promissory note to the other, the new holder of the document is not entitled to collect the debt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה, וְהָכָא בְּצָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – תַּנָּא דִּידַן סָבַר: אֵין צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה, וְתַנָּא בָּרָא סָבַר: צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה –
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say that everyone,** i.e., both the *tanna* of the mishna and the *tanna* of the *baraita*, holds that **letters are acquired by transferring** the document, **and here they disagree with regard to** whether or not the new holder of the promissory note is **required to bring proof** that the original creditor transferred it to him for collection, as opposed to his having received it for safekeeping or having found it by chance. **The *tanna* of our** mishna **holds** that the present holder of the document is **not required to bring proof** that the debt was transferred to him and therefore he can collect the debt through the promissory note in his possession. **And the *tanna* of the *baraita* holds** that he is **required to bring** such **proof,** and in its absence he cannot collect the debt with the document he holds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְּמַר: אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה; אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה, וְרָבָא אָמַר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it was stated: Letters are acquired by transferring** the document. **Abaye says:** The new holder of the document is **required to bring proof** that he has in fact acquired the right to collect the debt. **And Rava says: He is not required to bring** such **proof;** it is assumed that if he is holding the document it was transferred to him knowingly by the original creditor.

### Proof of Transfer Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד מִן הָאַחִין שֶׁהַשְּׁטַר חוֹב יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ, עָלָיו לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה. מַאי, לָאו הוּא הַדִּין לְאַחְרִינֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: From where do I say** that he is required to bring proof? This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one of** several **brothers,** heirs of an estate, **who has a promissory note in his possession** listing a debt owed to the deceased father, and he seeks to collect the debt for himself, it is **upon him to bring proof** that the document was transferred to him legally by his father in his lifetime, or that it was bequeathed to him specifically and he did not appropriate it from the other brothers. Abaye concludes from this: **What, is the same not true for** all **others** in a similar situation? In other words, whenever someone holds a promissory note that did not initially belong to him he is required to prove that he acquired it legally.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: שָׁאנֵי אַחִין, דְּשָׁמְטוּ מֵהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said** in response: Proof is not generally required in such a case. But the case of **brothers** who are heirs **is different, as** it is common for brothers who are co-heirs to **seize** property belonging to the estate **from one another.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד מִן הָאַחִין שֶׁהַשְּׁטַר חוֹב יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ – עָלָיו לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה. אַחִין הוּא דְּשָׁמְטוּ מֵהֲדָדֵי, אֲבָל אַחְרִינֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** there is a different version of this exchange between Abaye and Rava. According to this version, **Rava said: From where do I say** that no proof is required? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one of** several **brothers,** heirs of an estate, **who has a promissory note in his possession** listing a debt owed to the deceased father, and he seeks to collect the debt for himself, it is **upon him to bring proof** that the document was transferred to him legally by his father in his lifetime, or that it was bequeathed to him specifically and that he did not appropriate it from the other brothers. The implication is that **it is** only **brothers** who must bring such proof, as it is common for brothers who are co-heirs to **seize** property belonging to the estate **from one another. But others** in a similar situation do **not** have to bring proof that they acquired the promissory note legally.

Returning now to the case of two Dov Linzers: the *mishnah*'s position that I may collect using my *shtar* reflects either (a) *lo chashinan lenifila de-chad* — we simply do not worry about the remote possibility that the other person with my name dropped it, which is the most intuitive reading; or (b) even if we entertain that concern theoretically, once we accept *otiyot niknot bimesira* and *ein tzarich lehavi rayah*, any bearer of a *shtar* is the presumed creditor, regardless of whether the name matches. The *braita*'s stricter position — that neither Dov Linzer can collect — reflects both *chashinan lenifila de-chad* and *tzarich lehavi rayah*: we are concerned about the possibility of confusion, and holding the *shtar* alone does not establish entitlement.

## Same-Name Debts Allowed

### Braita Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמּוֹצִיאִין הֵן שְׁטַר חוֹב עַל אֲחֵרִים – כָּךְ מוֹצִיאִין זֶה עַל זֶה; בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss the case of two men with the same name. **But** what about **that which is taught** in another *baraita*: **Just as** the two men named Yosef ben Shimon **can produce a promissory note against others, so can they produce** promissory notes **against one another.** This represents a third opinion, standing in opposition to both the mishna and the previous *baraita*. The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do** the mishna and first *baraita* on the one hand, and this *baraita* on the other, **disagree?**

The Gemara then introduces a third text — still another *braita* — that is even more permissive than the *mishnah*. This *braita* states that *keshem shemotzi'in hen shtar chov aleihen kach motzi'in zeh al zeh* — just as the two people with identical names can use debt documents between themselves, so too can either of them use a document against the other. This is a striking position. If a *shtar* states that Dov Linzer owes Dov Linzer a thousand dollars, either party might claim to be the creditor and accuse the other of being the debtor. How could such a document ever be adjudicated?

### Writing Without Lender

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכוֹתְבִין שְׁטָר לַלֹּוֶה וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מַלְוֶה עִמּוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **They disagree about** whether or not a scribe **may write** a promissory **note for a debtor even if** the **creditor is not with him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא דִּידַן סָבַר: כּוֹתְבִין שְׁטָר לַלֹּוֶה, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מַלְוֶה עִמּוֹ; זִימְנִין דְּאָזֵיל לְגַבֵּי סָפְרָא וְסָהֲדֵי, וְאָמַר לְהוּ: כְּתֻבוּ לִי שְׁטָרָא, דְּבָעֵינַן לְמֵיזַף מִיּוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן חַבְרַי; וּבָתַר דְּכָתְבִי וְחָתְמִי לֵיהּ, נָקֵיטא לֵיהּ וְאָמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי מְאָה דִּיזַפְתְּ מִינַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **The *tanna* of our** mishna **holds** that a scribe **may write** a promissory **note for a debtor even if the creditor is not with him.** The Gemara explains: At **times,** it could happen **that** one of the men named Yosef ben Shimon **goes to a scribe and** to **witnesses and says to them: Write** a promissory **note for me, as I wish to borrow** money **from another Yosef ben Shimon. And** then, **after they write** the document **and sign** it **for him,** he will **take it and say to** the other Yosef ben Shimon: **Give me the hundred** dinars **that you borrowed from me,** as attested to in this document. That is the reason why the mishna rules that two people of the same name cannot collect a debt through a promissory note from one another, thereby preventing this occurrence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא בָּרָא סָבַר: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין שְׁטָר לַלֹּוֶה, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא מַלְוֶה עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **the *tanna* of the** other ***baraita* holds** that a scribe **may not write** a promissory **note for a debtor unless** the **creditor is with him** and consents to the writing of the document. If one Yosef ben Shimon consents to the writing of the document before the other, they apparently trust one another, and there is no concern that the one holding the promissory note will defraud the other.

The answer, the Gemara explains, is the principle of *kotvin shtar le-loveh af al pi she'ein malveh imo* — whether we permit a scribe and witnesses to write a promissory note for the borrower in advance, even without the lender present. The halachic background here is that the borrower, not the lender, is considered the party disadvantaged by the document, and it is therefore permissible, under certain views, to write the document at the borrower's request before the loan actually takes place. The borrower then holds the document until the lender hands over the money, at which point the borrower gives the *shtar* to the lender.

The *tana* of the *mishnah* holds that one *may* write the *shtar* for the borrower without the lender present. The problem this creates in an identical-name case is obvious: I go to the scribe and witnesses and say, "Write a *shtar* that Yosef ben Shimon borrowed from Yosef ben Shimon a thousand dollars — I am planning to borrow from my cousin who shares my name." I walk out with the document, and instead of giving it to my cousin when he lends me the money, I turn the tables and use it against him, claiming that he is the borrower and I am the creditor. Because the *mishnah* permits writing the *shtar* for the borrower in advance, this scheme is possible, and therefore neither Yosef ben Shimon can use the document against the other — each one can always claim the other is the debtor.

The *tana* of this permissive *braita*, by contrast, holds that one may *not* write the *shtar* for the borrower without the lender being present. If the lender must be there at the time of writing, both parties are present and the lender ensures that the *shtar* does not leave the room until the loan has been handed over. The risk of the scheme described above is therefore neutralized, and consequently the document can be used — even though both parties share an identical name.

### Receipt Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא לְאֶחָד בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹתָיו ״שְׁטָרוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן פָּרוּעַ״ – שְׁטָרוֹת שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּרוּעִין וְכוּ׳. טַעְמָא דְּנִמְצָא, הָא לֹא נִמְצָא – מָצֵי מַפֵּיק; וְהָאֲנַן ״וְלֹא אַחֵר יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא עֲלֵיהֶן שְׁטַר חוֹב״ תְּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If a document is **found among one's documents** stating: The promissory **note against Yosef ben Shimon is repaid,** and both men named Yosef ben Shimon owed this man money, **the** promissory **notes of both of them are** considered **repaid,** as it cannot be determined which debt was repaid and which is outstanding. The Gemara comments: The implication of the mishna is that **the reason** this man cannot collect his debt is **that** this note **was found** among his papers; **but** if it was **not** suddenly found, rather he was always aware of its existence, **he could present** a promissory note against one of the men named Yosef ben Shimon and collect the debt from him. The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** earlier in the mishna: **Nor can another present a promissory note against** either of **them?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בִּמְשׁוּלָּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** This later line in the mishna is stated **with regard to** a promissory note in which the debtors Yosef ben Shimon are identified by having their names **tripled,** i.e., through mentioning the name of their respective grandfathers, as is explained at the end of the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֶחֱזֵי תְּבָרָא – בִּשְׁמָא דְּמַאן דִּכְתִיב! אָמַר רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: בִּמְשׁוּלָּשִׁין בַּשְּׁטָר, וְאֵין מְשׁוּלָּשִׁין בַּשּׁוֹבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But let us see in whose name** the **receipt,** i.e., the note stating that Yosef ben Shimon's debt has been repaid, **is written.** Why is there uncertainty as to which Yosef ben Shimon is referred to in the document? **Rav Hoshaya said:** The case is **where** the debtors are identified by having their names **tripled in the** promissory **note, but** the names are **not tripled in the receipt.**

The Gemara then cites a further clause that addresses what happens when a receipt (*shovar*) creates ambiguity. If a *malveh* finds among his own documents a receipt stating that Yosef ben Shimon's *shtar* has been paid up — *parua* — he cannot collect from either Yosef ben Shimon, since he cannot determine which one the receipt refers to. The Gemara asks an obvious question: why do we need the receipt to prevent collection? Even without it, the *mishnah* has already established that a bearer cannot enforce a *shtar* against either of two identically named parties, since each will claim the *shtar* refers to the other.

Rav Yirmiyah resolves this by explaining that the *shtaros* in question are *meshulashim* — written with three generations of names. One *shtar* says Reuven ben Shimon ben Yaakov owes a thousand dollars, and the other says Reuven ben Shimon ben Moshe owes five thousand dollars. With three-generation identification, both *shtaros* are individually valid and usable. The problem arises only because the *shovar* identifies the debtor using only two generations — "Reuven ben Shimon paid up his *shtar*" — and it is therefore ambiguous which of the two men it refers to. The receipt neutralizes both otherwise-valid documents.

The Gemara then asks: since the *shtaros* are *meshulashim* and thus distinguishable, why not simply look at which three-generation name appears in the receipt? Rav Oshaya answers: the *shtaros* went three generations, but the *shovar* was written with only two generations — *meshulashim be-shtar ve-ein meshulashim be-shovar*. That is the source of the ambiguity, and it is entirely plausible as a scribal oversight.

## Solutions to Ambiguity

### Three Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר, הָכִי קָאָמַר: נִמְצָא לַלֹּוֶה בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹתָיו ״שְׁטָרוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן עָלַי, פָּרוּעַ״ – שְׁטָרוֹת שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּרוּעִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye stated** a different answer to this question, that the mishna is speaking about the debtor, not the creditor, and **this** is what **it is saying:** If a receipt is **found among the debtor's documents** stating: **The** promissory **note of Yosef ben Shimon** against me has been **repaid,** and this debtor had borrowed money from both men named Yosef ben Shimon, **the** promissory **notes of both of them are** considered **repaid,** i.e., they are not valid for collection, as it cannot be determined which debt was paid and which one is outstanding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד יַעֲשׂוּ? יְשַׁלְּשׁוּ כּוּ׳. תָּנָא: אִם הָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם כֹּהֲנִים, יִכְתְּבוּ דּוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **What should** two people with the same name in a single city **do** in order to conduct their business? **They should triple** their names by writing three generations: Yosef ben Shimon ben so-and-so. If their grandfather's names are identical and they have no prominent physical difference to indicate who is the one listed in the document, they write that the one referred to in the document is a priest, if that is the case. The Sages **taught** the following continuation of the mishna in a *baraita*: And **if both of them were priests, they should write** several **generations** of ancestors, until a divergence in names is found that can serve as a means of identification.

Abaye, however, offers a different reading of the *mishnah* altogether. He reframes the case: it is not the *malveh* who found the receipt — it is the *loveh* who produced it. The two Yosef ben Shimons are here the *creditors*, not the debtors; a single borrower took loans from each of them. Without any receipt, each Yosef ben Shimon could proceed independently: the one holding the *shtar* for a thousand dollars could collect his thousand, and the one holding the *shtar* for ten thousand could collect his ten thousand, since each *shtar* is presumed to belong to the person holding it. But the borrower produces a receipt — *shtar she-Yosef ben Shimon alai parua* — the *shtar* that Yosef ben Shimon holds against me is paid up. Since this receipt does not specify which Yosef ben Shimon's debt has been satisfied, neither can collect.

The Gemara asks: given this ambiguity, *keitzad ya'asu* — what should be done? *Yashalshu* — write three generations of names. If the *shtaros* themselves identify the creditors as Yosef ben Shimon ben Yaakov and Yosef ben Shimon ben Moshe, and the receipt says only "Yosef ben Shimon," we still have the problem of ambiguity in the receipt. Here the Gemara confirms the point: it is precisely when the *shtaros* are *meshulashim* but the *shovar* is not that the difficulty arises. The *mishnah* then adds that if both creditors happen to be *Kohanim* or both *Yisraelim* — such that the priestly designation cannot serve as a distinguishing marker and the family names happen to coincide through three generations — one must extend the identification to four or five generations until a distinction emerges. Notably, the Gemara nowhere considers using a mother's name or other identifiers as an alternative solution.

### Power of Attorney

The *Rashbam* adds a practical solution for the case of identical-name ambiguity with a shared receipt. If neither Yosef ben Shimon can individually collect his full amount — since the borrower will claim the receipt satisfies whichever *shtar* is being pressed — the two creditors can jointly execute a *shtar harsha'ah*, a power of attorney. One Yosef ben Shimon authorizes the other to act on his behalf, and that agent appears in court and argues: "You owe me at least a thousand dollars. Either you owe me a thousand dollars from my own claim, or you owe me even more because of the other Yosef ben Shimon's claim — and the receipt can have eliminated at most one of those two claims." By consolidating the two claims into a single court appearance rather than two separate proceedings, the agent can ensure that at minimum the lower amount is recovered. Individually, each creditor is defeated; together, they can collect the lesser sum.

## Deathbed Confession

### All Debts Nullified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לִבְנוֹ: ״שְׁטָר בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹתַי פָּרוּעַ, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אֵי זֶהוּ״ – שְׁטָרוֹת כּוּלָּן פְּרוּעִין. נִמְצָא לְאֶחָד שָׁם שְׁנַיִם – הַגָּדוֹל פָּרוּעַ, וְהַקָּטָן אֵינוֹ פָּרוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who says to his son** before dying: One promissory **note among** the promissory **notes** in **my** possession **is repaid, but I do not know which one, the** promissory **notes of all of** those who owe him money **are** considered **repaid,** i.e., they are not valid for collection, as it cannot be determined which debt was repaid and which are outstanding. If there were **found** among his papers **two** promissory notes owed **by one** person, the one for **the greater** amount **is** considered **repaid, and** the one for **the smaller** amount **is not** considered **repaid** and can be collected; the debtor is favored in the case of an uncertainty.

A new case introduces the principle of an honest person's deathbed ambiguity. A man says to his son: *shtar bein shtarotai parua ve'eini yode'a eizehu* — one of the promissory notes among my documents has been paid up, but I cannot remember which one. The ruling is *shtarot kulam paruim* — all of the notes are treated as paid up, and the son cannot collect any of them.

One might ask what legal principle underlies this ruling. It appears to rest on *hoda'at ba'al din* — the admission of the litigant himself. The holder of the *shtaros*, while still alive, admitted that one of his claims had been satisfied. Even though his admission was in the form of a *safek* — an uncertainty — it is apparently sufficient to neutralize all of his *shtaros*. This is a somewhat surprising application of *hoda'at ba'al din*, since he did not admit to any specific debt being paid; nevertheless, once he acknowledged that one of the notes is void and cannot say which, the son is left unable to use any of them.

### Specific vs. General

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: ״שְׁטָר לְךָ בְּיָדִי, פָּרוּעַ״ – הַגָּדוֹל פָּרוּעַ וְהַקָּטָן אֵינוֹ פָּרוּעַ. ״חוֹב לְךָ בְּיָדִי, פָּרוּעַ״ – שְׁטָרוֹת כּוּלָּן פְּרוּעִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava says** that if a creditor says to a debtor who borrowed money twice from him: One promissory **note** of the two that I have **of yours in my possession is repaid,** without specifying which one, the one for **the greater** amount **is** considered **repaid, and** the one for **the smaller** amount **is not** considered **repaid,** and can be collected. If the creditor said instead: **The debt of yours** that I have **in my possession is repaid, all** the promissory **notes** this creditor has from this debtor **are** considered **repaid,** as the term debt covers all the loans at once.

Rava introduces a related but distinct case. If I say to Michael *shtar lecha beyadi parua* — "the *shtar* that I hold against you is paid up" — and it turns out I hold two *shtaros* against Michael, one for a large amount and one for a smaller amount, the ruling is *hagadol parua ve-hakatan eino parua*: I can still collect the smaller amount. The reasoning is that whichever *shtar* I meant to declare paid up, Michael certainly owes me at least the lesser sum. If the larger *shtar* is the one I admitted to be paid, he still owes me the smaller; if the smaller is the one I admitted to be paid, he certainly owes me the larger. Either way, the lesser amount is collectable.

However, if I say *hov shelecha beyadi parua* — "the *debt* you owe me is paid up" — using the word *chov* rather than *shtar*, the ruling changes: *shtarot kulan paruim*, all notes are voided. The word *chov* refers to the totality of the debt, not to a specific document, and therefore is read as an admission that the entire outstanding obligation has been satisfied.

### Burden of Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״שָׂדִי מְכוּרָה לָךְ״ – שָׂדֶה גְּדוֹלָה מְכוּרָה לוֹ; ״שָׂדֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי, מְכוּרָה לָךְ״ – כׇּל שְׂדוֹתָיו מְכוּרִין לוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rava: If that is so** that when one makes an ambiguous statement it is interpreted to his detriment, does the same *halakha* apply to sales? If a seller of land writes a document for a purchaser stating: **My field is** hereby **sold to you,** without specifying which of his two fields he is referring to, is it the **larger field** that is **sold to him?** And if he writes a document for the purchaser stating: The **field that I have is sold to you,** is it so that **all of his fields are sold to him,** as the Hebrew word for field can refer to several fields as well as a single field?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, יַד בַּעַל הַשְּׁטָר עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains: **There, the holder of the document** of sale **is at a disadvantage,** as it is he who seeks to take more property from the seller, and must accept the minimal meaning. Here too, in the case of the promissory notes, it is the holder of the document, the creditor, who seeks to collect more money from the debtor. Therefore, the creditor is at a disadvantage.

Ravina then challenges Rava: if we interpret indeterminate language so expansively in favor of the opposing party, should we not apply the same logic elsewhere? If a *shtar* states that Dov sold Michael "my field," and Dov owns multiple fields, should we assume Michael acquired the largest one — just as in the debt case we assumed the larger *shtar* was the one declared paid up? Or if the *shtar* says "the field I own," should we read it as encompassing all of Dov's fields?

Rava responds: *hatam yad ba'al ha-shtar al hatachtona* — in those cases, the burden of proof falls on the person wielding the *shtar*. The point is not that the ambiguous language has an inherent meaning; rather, every case of genuine ambiguity is resolved in favor of the party against whom the *shtar* is being asserted, following the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*. In the debt case, Michael is being asked to pay, so we resolve the ambiguity in his favor by assuming it is the *larger* debt that was declared paid — leaving only the smaller obligation enforceable. In the sale case, Michael is trying to take Dov's field, so the ambiguity is resolved against Michael, and we assume only the smaller field was sold unless Michael can prove otherwise. The word itself does not determine the outcome; the allocation of the burden of proof does.

## Guarantor Institution

### Not First Creditor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל יְדֵי עָרֵב, לֹא יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who lends** money **to another with** the assurance of **a guarantor cannot collect** the debt **from the guarantor.**

The *mishnah* now opens an entirely new topic: the institution of the *arev*, the guarantor or co-signer. The *mishnah* rules *lo yifra min ha-arev techila* — the lender may not collect from the guarantor first. If I lend money to Michael and David serves as guarantor, I must first pursue Michael. Only if Michael defaults may I then turn to David.

Before analyzing the mechanics, it is worth noting the broader resonance of the term *arev*. The phrase *kol Yisrael arevim ze lazeh* — all of Israel are guarantors for one another — uses the same root in a non-legal, moral sense: just as a legal guarantor becomes responsible for another's debt, each Jew bears a form of responsibility for the religious conduct of fellow Jews. Here, of course, we are concerned with the strictly legal institution.

### Direct Collection Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֶפָּרַע מִמִּי שֶׁאֶרְצֶה״ – יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ נְכָסִים לַלֹּוֶה – בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ לֹא יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **But if** the creditor **said to** the debtor: I am lending the money **on the condition that I will collect** the debt **from whomever I wish,** i.e., either the debtor or the guarantor, he **can collect** the debt **from the guarantor. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: If the debtor has property** of his own, then **whether** in **this** case, where the creditor stipulated this condition, **or that** case, where he did not, he **cannot collect** the debt **from the guarantor.**

The *mishnah* provides one exception to the rule of *lo yifra min ha-arev techila*: if the lender stipulated at the time of the loan *al menat she-efra mimi she-ertzeh* — on condition that I may collect from whomever I choose — then *yifra min ha-arev*, the lender may go directly to the guarantor. This stipulation must, of course, have been made with David's knowledge and consent; it is not a unilateral reservation by the lender.

### Visible Assets Exception

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds a further qualification. Even if the lender made this stipulation, *im yesh nechasim la-loveh bein kach u-vein kach lo yifra min ha-arev* — if the borrower visibly has assets, the lender still may not bypass him and go directly to the guarantor. The underlying logic relates to the degree of the borrower's apparent solvency. There is a meaningful distinction between a borrower who clearly has accessible property — in which case even the stipulation does not permit going straight to the guarantor — and a borrower whose assets are not readily apparent, in which case the lender may need to choose between extended judicial proceedings and invoking the guarantor. The Gemara will elaborate on this distinction further.

### Ketubah Guarantor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הָיָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הֶעָרֵב לְאִשָּׁה בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ, וְהָיָה בַּעְלָהּ מְגָרְשָׁהּ – יַדִּירֶנָּה הֲנָאָה; שֶׁמָּא יַעֲשׂוּ קְנוּנְיָא עַל נְכָסִים שֶׁל זֶה, וְיַחְזִיר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And so Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would say:** If there is **a guarantor for a woman for her marriage contract,** from whom the woman can collect payment of her marriage contract instead of collecting it from the husband, **and her husband was divorcing her,** the husband **must take a vow** prohibiting himself from deriving any **benefit from her,** so that he can never remarry her. This precaution is taken **lest** the couple **collude [*kenunya*]** to divorce in order to collect payment of the marriage contract **from this** guarantor's **property, and** then the husband **will remarry** his wife.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel also addresses a separate scenario involving an *arev* in the context of a *ketubah*. If a husband could not persuade his prospective wife to marry him without a guarantor for the *ketubah*, and David agreed to co-sign — meaning that if the husband divorces her and lacks the funds to pay the *ketubah* settlement, David will cover it — a problem arises when the husband actually proceeds toward divorce. The concern is *she-ma ya'asu knunya al nechasim shel zeh ve-yahazir ishto* — that the husband and wife may be colluding to extract money from the guarantor and then remarry. The solution is *yadirenah hana'ah*: when divorcing, the husband must declare that he renounces all benefit from his ex-wife, making remarriage impossible and thereby eliminating the incentive for collusion. The *Rashbam* notes that this must be done through a *neder al da'at rabbim* — a vow formulated in a manner that cannot be annulled — to ensure the protection is real.

## Guarantor Mechanism

### Bail Bondsman Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: גַּבְרָא אַשְׁלֵימְתְּ לִי, גַּבְרָא אַשְׁלֵימִי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: One who lends money to another with a guarantor cannot collect the debt from the guarantor. The Gemara at first understands that the mishna is ruling that a guarantor's commitment is limited to when the debtor dies or flees. **What is the reason** the guarantor's commitment is limited? **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say** that the guarantor can tell the creditor: **You gave a man over to me,** to take responsibility for him if he dies or flees; **I have given a man** back **to you.** The debtor is here before you; take your money from him, and if he has nothing, suffer the loss yourself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן: הַאי – דִּינָא דְפָרְסָאֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman objects to this: This is Persian law.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, בָּתַר עָרְבָא אָזְלִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interjects: **On the contrary,** the Persian courts **go after the guarantor** directly, without even attempting to collect the debt from the debtor himself. Why, then, did Rav Naḥman say that excusing the guarantor from payment is Persian law?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בֵּי דִינָא דְפָרְסָאֵי – דְּלָא יָהֲבִי טַעְמָא לְמִילְּתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies Rav Naḥman's intent: **Rather,** Rav Naḥman meant to say that this kind of ruling would be appropriate for the members of **a Persian court, who do not give a reason for their statements,** but issue rulings by whim. Rav Naḥman was saying that it is not fair or logical to excuse the guarantor and cause a loss to the creditor who was depending on him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַאי ״לֹא יִפָּרַע מִן הֶעָרֵב״ – לֹא יִתְבַּע עָרֵב תְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman said: What** does the mishna mean when it says that the creditor **cannot collect** the debt **from the guarantor?** It means that he **cannot claim** payment from the **guarantor at the outset,** until after it is established that the debtor has no means to repay the debt. After the borrower defaults, the creditor can collect the debt from the guarantor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל יְדֵי עָרֵב – לֹא יִתְבַּע עָרֵב תְּחִלָּה. וְאִם אָמַר: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֶפָּרַע מִמִּי שֶׁאֶרְצֶה״ – יִתְבַּע עָרֵב תְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **This** *halakha* **is also taught** in a *baraita*: One **who lends** money **to another with** the assurance of **a guarantor cannot claim** payment of the debt **from the guarantor at the outset,** rather, he must first attempt to collect the debt from the debtor. **But if** the creditor **said to** the debtor: I am lending the money **on the condition that I will collect** the debt **from whomever I wish,** he can **claim payment** of the debt **from the guarantor at the outset,** bypassing the debtor.

The Gemara now asks *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for the rule that I cannot collect from the guarantor first? Before addressing this, one might have asked an even more fundamental question: what is the basis for the institution of *areivut* at all? Why should David's verbal declaration that he will stand as guarantor create any legal obligation? That question will be addressed shortly; the Gemara begins with the narrower question of priority.

Rava and Rav Yosef together offer an explanation: *gavra ashlimit li gavra ashlimi lach* — you entrusted to me a person; I return to you that person. Under this theory, the guarantor's role is analogous to that of a bail bondsman: David's function is not to pay the debt but to ensure that Michael remains available and appears before the court. As long as Michael can be produced, David's obligation is discharged. Only if Michael has fled entirely and cannot be located does David become liable to pay.

Rav Nachman challenges this sharply: *hai dina de-Parsaye* — that is the law of the Persians, not ours. (The Gemara clarifies that the characterization is inverted: Persian law actually allowed the lender to go directly to the guarantor; it is the "bail bondsman" reading that is being called arbitrary and unfounded.) The objection is substantive: characterizing the guarantor's role as merely producing the debtor drastically and illogically narrows the institution. The whole point of a guarantor is that the lender can rely on *someone* for payment if the borrower defaults — not merely that someone will drag the borrower into court.

The Gemara therefore rejects the bail bondsman theory as the basis for the rule. Instead, the Gemara clarifies that *lo yifra min ha-arev techila* should not be read as "the lender can *never* collect from the guarantor" — which is what the words seem to say on a plain reading — but rather as *lo yitba areiv techila*: the lender may not *pursue* the guarantor first. The guarantor is fully liable; he is simply the second line of defense. A *braita* confirms this reading explicitly, using the language *lo yitba areiv techila* — the guarantor cannot be summoned first — while also preserving the exception when the lender stipulated discretionary collection rights at the outset.

### Biblical Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִנַּיִן לְעָרֵב דְּמִשְׁתַּעְבֵּד? דִּכְתִיב: ״אָנֹכִי אֶעֶרְבֶנּוּ, מִיָּדִי תְּבַקְשֶׁנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna said: From where** is it derived **that a guarantor becomes obligated** to repay a loan he has guaranteed? **As it is written** that Judah reassured his father concerning the young Benjamin: **"I will be his guarantor; of my hand shall you request him"** (Genesis 43:9). This teaches that it is possible for one to act as a guarantor that an item will be returned to the giver.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: הָא קַבְּלָנוּת הִיא – דִּכְתִיב: ״תְּנָה אֹתוֹ עַל יָדִי, וַאֲנִי אֲשִׁיבֶנּוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda objects to this: This** incident involving Benjamin is not a case of a standard guarantor, but a case of **an unconditional guarantee, as it is written,** also in the context of Benjamin, that Reuben said: **"Deliver him into my hand, and I will bring him back to you"** (Genesis 42:37). One who undertakes unconditional responsibility for a loan has a different status than a standard guarantor, as will soon be elaborated. Therefore, a biblical source has yet to be adduced to teach that one can become a standard guarantor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, מֵהָכָא: ״לְקַח בִּגְדוֹ כִּי עָרַב זָר, וּבְעַד נׇכְרִיָּה חַבְלֵהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that the source is **from here: "Take his garment that is surety for a stranger; and hold him in pledge that is surety for an alien woman"** (Proverbs 20:16). The verse advises a creditor to take the garment of the debtor's guarantor as payment for the loan.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״בְּנִי, אִם עָרַבְתָּ לְרֵעֶךָ תָּקַעְתָּ לַזָּר כַּפֶּיךָ, נוֹקַשְׁתָּ בְאִמְרֵי פִיךָ נִלְכַּדְתָּ בְּאִמְרֵי פִיךָ, עֲשֵׂה זֹאת אֵפוֹא בְּנִי וְהִנָּצֵל כִּי בָאתָ בְכַף רֵעֶךָ, לֵךְ הִתְרַפֵּס וּרְהַב רֵעֵיךָ״ – אִם מָמוֹן יֵשׁ לוֹ בְּיָדֶךָ, הַתֵּר לוֹ פִּיסַּת יָד; וְאִם לָאו, הַרְבֵּה עָלָיו רֵיעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is stated: "My son, if you have become surety for your neighbor, if you have shaken your hands for a stranger, you have become ensnared by the words of your mouth, you have been caught by the words of your mouth. Do this now, my son, and save yourself, seeing that you have come into the hand of your neighbor: Go, humble yourself [*hitrappes*], and assemble your neighbors"** (Proverbs 6:1–3). This last part of the passage means: **If** your neighbor's **money is in your possession,** as you owe it as a guarantor, **open up [*hatter*] the palm [*pissat*] of your hand** and repay him. **And if** it is **not** money that you owe him, but rather "you have become ensnared by the words of your mouth" and owe him an apology for a personal slight, **gather together many neighbors** through which to seek his forgiveness.

The Gemara turns to the question of where the institution of *areivut* comes from in the first place. Rav Huna asks: *minayin le-areiv de-mishta'abed* — what is the source that a guarantor becomes legally obligated? The answer cited is a verse from Bereishit: *anochi e'erevenu mi-yadi tevakshenu* — "I will guarantee him; from my hand shall you seek him" (Bereishit 43:9). Yehudah's declaration to his father Yaakov is understood as establishing the paradigm of the *arev*: Yehudah accepts personal liability for Binyamin's return, such that if Binyamin is not returned, Yaakov may seek restitution from Yehudah himself.

### Kablanus vs. Areivus

Rav Chisda immediately challenges this proof: *ha kablanut hi* — what Yehudah did was not *areivut* but *kablanus*. The distinction is a significant one. *Areivut* is secondary liability: the guarantor stands in the background, and the lender's primary relationship is with the borrower. *Kablanus* is direct liability: the *kablan* inserts himself into the transaction as a primary party, effectively saying "lend the money to him and I will be the one responsible." The difference in practice is that a *kablan* — an *arev kablan* — can be pursued directly by the lender without first attempting to collect from the borrower, whereas an ordinary *arev* cannot. Rav Chisda points to the verse *tenahu al yadi va'ani ashivenu alecha* — "give him into my care and I will return him to you" — which uses language of direct personal responsibility, making it an instance of *kablanus* rather than background *areivut*, and therefore not a proof for the general institution.

Rav Yitzchak therefore offers a different biblical source, from *Mishlei*: *lakach bigdo ki areiv zar* — "take his garment, for he became guarantor for a stranger" (Mishlei 20:16). The verse reflects the practical reality that a guarantor's property can be seized when the debt is not paid — a clear indication that the guarantor bears genuine legal liability. Further verses from *Mishlei* (6:1–5) are cited, using the root *arev* in contexts that unmistakably connote binding financial obligation.

## How Liability Works

### Areivut vs. Asmachta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: עָרֵב דְּמִשְׁתַּעְבַּד, מַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי; לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי דְּאָמַר: אַסְמַכְתָּא קָנְיָא – עָרֵב מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד. לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: אַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא – עָרֵב לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ameimar said:** The issue of **whether** or not **a guarantor** in fact **becomes obligated** to repay the loan he has guaranteed is **a dispute** between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei. According to Rabbi Yosei, who says** that **a transaction with inconclusive consent [*asmakhta*] effects acquisition, a guarantor becomes obligated** to repay the loan, whereas **according to Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition, a guarantor** does **not become obligated** to repay the loan. Any obligation one undertakes that is dependent on the fulfillment of certain conditions that he does not expect will be fulfilled, in this case the debtor's default on the loan, is considered an *asmakhta*.

Having established the institution's biblical basis, the Gemara turns to the deeper question of *mechanism*. Even granting that the Torah endorses the idea of a guarantor, how does a purely verbal commitment — David saying "don't worry, I'll back the loan" — create a legal obligation? Normally, to obligate oneself to pay money to another person, one would need at minimum a *kinyan sudar* — a formal act of acquisition, taking hold of a garment or similar object — to effectuate the commitment. David performs no such act. He merely speaks.

Moreover, even if we could find a mechanism, there is the problem of *asmachta*. An *asmachta* is a conditional promise that the promisor does not genuinely expect to have to fulfill — an overcommitment made in the hope that the triggering condition never materializes. The classic ruling is *asmachta lo kanya* — an *asmachta* does not create a binding obligation, because there is no genuine intent (*gemirat da'at*) to pay. A guarantor is a paradigmatic case: David agrees to back the loan precisely because he expects Michael to repay it himself. He hopes, and perhaps genuinely believes, he will never have to pay. This looks like a textbook *asmachta*.

The Gemara frames this as a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose. Rabbi Yose holds *asmachta kanya* — an *asmachta* does create a binding obligation — and therefore an *arev mishta'abed*, the guarantor is legally obligated. Rabbi Yehuda holds *asmachta lo kanya*, and therefore *arev lo mishta'abed*, the guarantor does not become legally bound.

### Hana'ah of Reliance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: הָא מַעֲשִׂים בְּכׇל יוֹם, דְּאַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא וְעָרֵב מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Ameimar** that he was conflating these two issues: **But** it is **a daily occurrence,** i.e., it is taken for granted, **that an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition, and** it is also taken for granted that **a guarantor becomes obligated** to repay the loan he has guaranteed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּהָהוּא הֲנָאָה דְּקָא מְהֵימַן לֵיהּ – גָּמַר וּמִשְׁתַּעְבַּד נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said: Through that satisfaction that** the guarantor feels when the creditor **trusts him** and loans the money based on his guarantee, the guarantor **resolves** to **obligate himself** to repay the loan. Guaranteeing a loan is unlike a usual case of an obligation undertaken that is dependent on the fulfillment of certain conditions that he does not expect will be fulfilled, in which the commitment is not considered a real one. Here, the one obligating himself experiences a sense of satisfaction when the money is loaned to the debtor, and due to that, fully commits to fulfill his obligation.

This creates an immediate practical difficulty. Rav Ashi points it out to Ameimar: *hama'asim b'chol yom de-asmachta lo kanya* — the daily practice of the courts is to invalidate *asmachta* obligations. Yet *va'arev mishta'abed* — those same courts regularly enforce guarantor obligations. How can both be true simultaneously? If *areivut* is a form of *asmachta*, the courts should be throwing out guarantor claims just as they throw out other *asmachta* claims.

Rav Ashi himself resolves the paradox: *be-hai hana'ah de-kamheimin leh gemar u-mishta'abed nafsheh* — because of the benefit (*hana'ah*) that the lender is trusting him, the guarantor truly commits and becomes obligated. The word *hana'ah* here should not be read superficially as mere social prestige — "how nice that people trust me." The deeper point is this: the guarantor knows that the lender is extending the loan *solely because* of his commitment. He understands that his word is the operative cause of the transaction. A person of integrity who knows that someone else is relying on him — that the money changed hands only because of his promise — cannot truly say he did not mean it. He knows the lender is depending on the veracity of his commitment, and therefore he truly *does* commit, fully and without reservation. It is that genuine *gemirat da'at*, arising from the knowledge that the lender is relying on him, that distinguishes *areivut* from ordinary *asmachta* and renders the guarantor's obligation fully enforceable.
