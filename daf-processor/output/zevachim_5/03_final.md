# Zevachim 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lishmah Requirement

### Shelamim Lishmah Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם דָּנִין, וְ״לַה׳״ חָזַר וְכָלַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says** there is a different answer: **Actually, one can derive** a *halakha* from verses with incomplete generalizations using the principle of a generalization and a detail. **And** the reason this *halakha* applies to other rites besides slaughter is that by adding the term **"unto the Lord,"** it **then makes a generalization.**

We pick up in the middle of the previous discussion, at the point where the *Tosafot* of *shkulin heim* appears on 4b, a few lines down from which we find the words *Ravina amar*. The discussion until now has established that one is required *lechatchila* to perform the *avodot* of a *korban* with *lishmah* — that is, for the proper sake of the offering — and that this requirement applies to all four primary *avodot*. The foundational case was *zevach shelamim*: the *zevichah* must be *l'sheim shelamim*, for the sake of it being a *shelamim*. We then sought to expand this to the other *avodot*, and further, to show that the *avodah* must be performed *l'sheim be'alim* — for the sake of the owners.

The primary *pasuk* establishing the requirement of *l'sheim be'alim* was *ve-nirtzah lo l'chaper alav*. The word *l'chaper* refers to the blood, and specifically to the *zerikat hadam*, while *alav* refers to the owner. It is important to keep in mind that the requirement of *l'sheim be'alim* is ultimately rooted in the *zerikah*. Even when we speak of the other *avodot* needing to be performed for the sake of the owner, what we mean is that at the time of *shechita*, one must have in mind on whose behalf the *zerikah* will ultimately be performed. If someone performs the *shechitah* without thinking at all about who the *zerikah* will be done for, that is not a problem. The *be'alim* requirement is intrinsically about the *zerikat hadam*; the other *avodot* can be invalidated only if one actively intends the *zerikah* for the wrong owner. *Sitmah* — the default, implicit state — is sufficient for *shechitah*, *kabbalah*, and *holacha* with respect to *be'alim*.

### Four Avodot Lishmah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתֵיהּ מֵאֵיל נָזִיר; דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת הָאַיִל יַעֲשֶׂה זֶבַח שְׁלָמִים״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא עֲשִׂיָּתוֹ לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים; וְאִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ – דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהָתָם, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְשִׁינּוּי בְעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi says** that the *halakha* that the collection of the blood must also be performed for the sake of the owner is derived **from** the case of **a nazirite's ram. As it is written: "And he shall offer the ram for a sacrifice [*zevaḥ*] of peace offerings** unto the Lord" (Numbers 6:17), indicating **that its offering should be** performed **for the sake of a peace offering. And if** the verse **is not** necessary for the **matter of deviation** from the type of **offering, as we derived that from there,** i.e., the verses cited earlier (4a), **apply it** to the **matter of deviation** with regard to the **owner.** It is thereby derived that its sacrificial rites must be performed for the sake of its owner.

The *Gemara* now turns to establishing that all four *avodot* must be performed *lishmah*. One *pasuk* available for this purpose is *es ha-ayil ya'aseh zevach shelamim l'Hashem*. The word *zevach* refers to *zevicha* — *shechitah* — and we would wish to expand the *lishmah* requirement from *shechitah* to the remaining *avodot*. However, reading *ya'aseh* as a *klal*, *zevach* as a *prat*, and *l'Hashem* as another *klal* raised the possibility of applying *klal u-prat u-klal*, which would allow expansion beyond the *prat* to other similar *avodot*.

### Zerika as Root

Before proceeding to Ravina's answer, a conceptual point warrants emphasis. The *lishmah* requirement as it relates to *be'alim* is specifically anchored in the *zerikah*. The *pasuk* of *ve-nirtzah lo l'chaper alav* — *l'chaper* meaning the blood, *alav* meaning the owner — fixes the *be'alim* consideration precisely at the moment of *zerikat hadam*. This is why, even as the *Gemara* extends the *lishmah* requirement to cover all four *avodot*, the conceptual center of gravity remains the *zerikah*.

### Ravina Klal Prat Klal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט; מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – עֲבוֹדָה וּבָעֵינַן לִשְׁמָן, אַף כׇּל עֲבוֹדָה וּבָעֵינַן לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** Since the term: "Unto the Lord," is another generalization, the verse is formulated as **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization,** and **you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail. Just as the** mentioned **detail,** slaughter, is **defined** as an essential **rite and we require** that it be performed **for the sake of** the owner, **so too, we require** that **every** essential **rite** be performed **for the sake of** the owner.

Ravina offers an answer to how the *lishmah* requirement is extended beyond *shechitah* alone. He reads *ya'aseh zevach shelamim l'Hashem* as a *klal u-prat u-klal*: *ya'aseh* is the first *klal* — any type of *avodah* — *zevach* is the *prat* — specifically *zevicha* — and *l'Hashem* returns as a second *klal*, again encompassing any *avodah* performed for Hashem. By the rules of *klal u-prat u-klal*, we expand beyond the *prat* to include other similar *avodot*, thereby requiring *lishmah* across all of them.

### Rav Achai Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר אַבָּא לְרָבָא, אֵימָא: ״יַעֲשֶׂה״ – כָּלַל, ״זֶבַח״ – פָּרַט; כְּלָל וּפְרָט – אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁבַּפְּרָט; זְבִיחָה אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Abba said to Rava,** in objection to this derivation: **Say** that the term: **"Shall offer," is a generalization** and: **"A sacrifice [*zevaḥ*]," is a detail.** According to the principles of biblical hermeneutics, where there is **a generalization and a detail, the generalization includes only that which is** specified **in the detail.** Accordingly, **slaughter [*zeviḥa*], yes,** must be performed for the sake of its owner; but **no other** rite must be performed for the sake of its owner in order for the offering to be fit.

Rav Achai mi-Difti raises an objection to Ravina's reading. He argues that the two *klalim* in this *pasuk* are not equivalent — *lo dami klala kama l'klala basra* — and that this asymmetry disqualifies a straightforward *klal u-prat u-klal* derivation. The first *klal*, *ya'aseh*, refers specifically to the *avodot* that constitute the act of bringing a *korban* — the four classic *avodot* of *shechitah*, *kabbalah*, *holacha*, and *zerikah*. This is exactly where we want to arrive. But the second *klal*, *l'Hashem*, is broader: it encompasses *kulo l'Hashem* — anything done in the service of the *korban* that is *l'Hashem* — which would include even *shefichat shirayim* (pouring the remainder of the blood on the base of the *mizbe'ach*) and *haktarat emurim* (burning the *emurim* on the *mizbe'ach*). These occur after the *korban* is already complete and valid. Because the two *klalim* are not coextensive — the first encompasses the four defining *avodot* while the second is far broader — this does not constitute a proper *klal u-prat u-klal*.

### Rabbi Yishmael Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בִּכְלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי דָּרֵישׁ כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina responded: **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught generalizations and details in cases like this.**

The *Gemara* responds that *hai tanna d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* — this *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael — applies *klal u-prat* in precisely this manner. Rabbi Yishmael's school is somewhat more flexible in its hermeneutical approach (*dibra Torah k'lashon bnei adam*), and it will accept a *klal u-prat u-klal* structure even where the second *klal* is broader than the first. The operative requirement remains that whatever is included must be similar to the *prat* in the middle: *mah haprat meforash avodah u-veinan lishmah* — the *prat* is *zevach*, which is *shechitah*, an *avodah* that must be done *lishmah* (here meaning *l'sheim be'alim*) — *af kol avodah beinan lishmah*, all *avodot* must similarly be *lishmah*.

### Expansion Principles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – עֲבוֹדָה וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ בַּחוּץ, אַף כׇּל עֲבוֹדָה וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ בַּחוּץ; שְׁחִיטָה וּזְרִיקָה – אִין, קַבָּלָה וְהוֹלָכָה – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the verse is referring only to actions similar to the detail, not all of the essential rites are necessarily included. Perhaps it should be derived that **just as the** mentioned **detail,** slaughter, is **defined** as an essential **rite, and one is liable** to be punished with death at the hand of Heaven **for** performing **it outside** the Temple, **so too, any** essential **rite** for which **one is liable** to be punished with death at the hand of Heaven **for** performing **it outside** the Temple is included in the verse. Accordingly, **slaughter and sprinkling** the blood **are** included, but **collection** of the blood **and conveying** it are **not.**

However, the *Gemara* presses further. Even granting *klal u-prat u-klal* via Rabbi Yishmael's school, the expansion from *shechitah* need not encompass all four *avodot*. The principle of *k'ein haprat* — similarity to the *prat* — could be applied in more than one direction, each pointing to a different subset of *avodot*.

On one reading, the defining characteristic of *shechitah* is that it is among the *avodot* for which one is liable if performed outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*. Expanding *lishmah* to all *avodot* similarly prohibited outside the *Beit HaMikdash* would include *shechitah* and *zerikah*, but not *kabbalah* or *holacha*.

On another reading, the defining characteristic of *shechitah* is that it sometimes must be performed at the *tzafun* (northern) side of the *mizbe'ach*, and it also applies in the case of the *chatat ha-penimiyot* — the inner *chatat* whose blood is applied with a finger to the inner altar. Expanding *lishmah* to all *avodot* sharing these characteristics would include *shechitah* and *kabbalah* (since the blood of animals slaughtered at the *tzafun* is also caught at the *tzafun*, and both apply to *chatat ha-penimiyot*), but would exclude *zerikah* — which is not confined to the *tzafun* and does not apply to *chatat ha-penimiyot*, whose blood is placed with a finger rather than sprinkled.

### Shkulim Hem Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר הָכִי וְאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר הָכִי, שְׁקוּלִין הֵן וְיָבֹאוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן. לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: וַחֲדָא חֲדָא תֵּיקוּ בְּמִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Both possibilities are equally valid; **this** derivation **can be said and that** derivation **can be said.** Either collection or sprinkling might be included in the *halakha*. Consequently, **they are equal, and** therefore **both are included.** Or according to **another version** of the Gemara's answer, they are equal, **and** therefore **each one,** i.e., both collection and sprinkling, **shall stand in** its **status.**

The *Gemara*'s resolution is elegant. Since both modes of expansion are equally valid — *ika lemaimar hachi ve'ika lemaimar hachi* — and there is no principled reason to prefer one over the other, the *Gemara* invokes the principle of *shkulim hem*: they are equivalent. When two directions of expansion are equally justified and neither can be ruled out, both are included. *Yavo'u shneihem* — both expansions apply. The first expansion covers *shechitah* and *zerikah*; the second covers *shechitah* and *kabbalah*. Together, they encompass *shechitah*, *kabbalah*, and *zerikah*. *Holacha* is subsumed under *kabbalah*, as it is consistently treated together with *kabbalah* in such derivations. Thus all four *avodot* are now covered.

An alternative formulation — *chada v'chada teki b'milsa*, each one becomes established in its own domain — conveys the same principle in different language: since you can go either way, each expansion is effectuated, and both are applied simultaneously.

There is an additional approach: *zrikah mid'Rav Ashi nafka* — we already know *zerikah* from the *pasuk* of *ve-nirtzah lo l'chaper alav*, which applies specifically to *zerikah l'sheim be'alim*. Since *zerikah* is already covered, the *klal u-prat u-klal* expansion is naturally directed toward *kabbalah*, and *holacha* follows along with *kabbalah*. The combination of these *pesukim* — *ve-nirtzah lo l'chaper alav* for *zerikah*, *ya'aseh zevach shelamim* for *shechitah*, and the *klal u-prat u-klal* for *kabbalah* and *holacha* — thus yields the requirement of *lishmah l'sheim be'alim* for all four *avodot*.

### Zot HaTorah Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שְׁלָמִים, שְׁאָר כׇּל קֳדָשִׁים מְנָלַן? וְכִי תֵּימָא נֵילַף מִשְּׁלָמִים, מָה לִשְׁלָמִים שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין סְמִיכָה וּנְסָכִים וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק! אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה לָעֹלָה וְלַמִּנְחָה וְלַחַטָּאת וְלָאָשָׁם וְלַמִּלּוּאִים וּלְזֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים״ – הַקִּישָׁן הַכָּתוּב לִשְׁלָמִים; מָה שְׁלָמִים – בֵּין שִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ בֵּין שִׁינּוּי בְעָלִים, בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ; אַף כׇּל בֵּין שִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ בֵּין שִׁינּוּי בְעָלִים, בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that **a peace offering** must be sacrificed for its own sake and for the sake of its owner. **From where do we** derive that this *halakha* applies with regard to **all other offerings?** **And if you would say: Let us derive from a peace offering** that all offerings must be sacrificed with these intentions, this derivation can be refuted: **What** is unique **about a peace offering?** It is unique **in that it requires placing hands** on the head of the offering, **and** it is accompanied by **libations, and** it requires the **waving of** the **breast and** the right **hind leg** by the priest and owner together. No other offering possesses all three of these requirements. **Rather, the verse states: "This is the law of the burnt offering, of the meal offering, and of the sin offering, and of the guilt offering, and of the consecration offering, and of the sacrifice of peace offerings"** (Leviticus 7:37). **The verse juxtaposes** all types of offerings **with peace offerings,** indicating that **just as we require** the sacrifice of **a peace offering** to be performed **for its sake, both** concerning **deviation** from the type of **offering and** concerning **deviation** with regard to the **owner, so too, we require** the sacrifice of **every** type of offering to be performed **for its sake, both** concerning **deviation** from the type of **offering and** concerning **deviation** with regard to the **owner.**

Having established the *lishmah* requirement for *shelamim*, the *Gemara* confronts the question of how to extend it to all other *korbanot*. One might suggest learning from *eil nazir*, but that is readily countered: *eil nazir* has special stringency because it is accompanied by other *korbanot* — an *olah* and a *chatat* — and so it cannot serve as a general source.

The *Gemara* then addresses why the word *shelamim* is written in the plural: *mai shelamim — lerabos kol hashelamim*, to include all *shelamim*. But we still need to extend the requirement to *korbanot* other than *shelamim* entirely. One might suggest learning from *shelamim* directly, but *shelamim* are in some respects the most demanding of *korbanot* — they require *semikhah*, *nesachim*, and *tenufah* of the *chazeh* and *shok* — and so their requirements cannot simply be extended outward. The fact that *shelamim* have these unique additional components means one might attribute the *lishmah* requirement specifically to those unique features.

The *Gemara* therefore turns to the *pasuk* of *zot hatorah la-olah v'la-minchah v'la-chatat v'la-asham v'la-miluim u-l'zevach ha-shelamim* — "This is the law of the *olah*, the *minchah*, the *chatat*, the *asham*, the *miluim*, and the *zevach shelamim*." This *pasuk* links all *korbanot* to *shelamim* via *hekesh*. Since *shelamim* appears last in the list, the *pasuk* is read as taking *shelamim* and expanding its requirements — including *bein shinuy kodesh bein shinuy be'alim* — to all the others: *af kol bein shinuy kodesh bein shinuy be'alim ba'inan lishmah*.

We now have three *pesukim* working together. The *shelamim* passages establish the *lishmah* and *l'sheim be'alim* requirements. *Zot hatorah* extends these requirements to all *korbanot*. And *motza sefatecha* — which will be developed shortly — establishes what happens when these requirements are not met.

## Shelo Lishmah Status

### Should It Be Invalid?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא, הֵיכָא דְּשָׁחַטוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ לִיפַּסְלוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presses: **Why should a korban performed shelo lishmah not simply be rendered invalid entirely?**

Having established that all *korbanot* require *lishmah*, the *Gemara* now asks: what is the status of a *korban* performed *shelo lishmah*? The *Mishnah* teaches that, with the exception of a *chatat* and a *pesach*, a *korban* slaughtered *shelo lishmah* is *kasher* — valid — but does not fulfill the obligation of the owners. The *Gemara* presses: *ema, heicha de-shachatei shelo lishmah lifselu* — why should a *korban* performed *shelo lishmah* not simply be rendered invalid entirely?

### Meakev Requirements

An important principle governs this question. By ordinary *mitzvot*, if the *Torah* establishes a requirement, its absence invalidates the act. A *lulav* that lacks *hadar* is *pasul*; an *esrog* that does not meet the criteria is *pasul*. One might assume the same applies throughout *halachah*. But in the domain of *kodshim*, a distinct rule applies: a requirement does not automatically render the *korban* invalid if unfulfilled unless the *Torah* repeats the requirement twice — *shanah alav ha-katuv le-akeiv*. Since there are so many requirements in the *avodah*, the default presumption is that not all of them are *me'akeiv*, and only explicit repetition signals that the absence of a given requirement invalidates the *korban*. *Tosafot* raises this question explicitly: given this principle, why does the *Gemara* assume that *shelo lishmah* should be *pasul* at all? The answer *Tosafot* offers is that by the case of *shinuy kodesh* — performing the *avodah* for a different type of *korban* — there were multiple *pesukim*, which is sufficient to raise the question of *pesul*.

### Motza Sefatecha Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ תִּשְׁמֹר וְעָשִׂיתָ כַּאֲשֶׁר נָדַרְתָּ וְגוֹ׳״ – הַאי נְדָבָה?! נֶדֶר הוּא! [אֶלָּא] אִם כְּמָה שֶׁנָּדַרְתָּ עָשִׂיתָ – יְהֵא נֶדֶר, וְאִם לָאו – יְהֵא נְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "That which has gone out of your lips you shall observe and do; according to what you have vowed** freely to the Lord your God, that which you have promised with your mouth" (Deuteronomy 23:24). The Gemara interprets the words *nadarta* and *nedava* exegetically: How can the offering mentioned in **this** verse be **a gift** offering **[nedava]? It is** already referred to as **a vow** offering **[neder]. Rather,** the verse indicates that **if you did what you had vowed** to do, i.e., sacrificed your vow offering properly, **it shall be** a satisfactory **vow** offering; **but if** you did **not** sacrifice it properly, **it shall be** rendered a voluntary **gift** offering. Although it does not satisfy the obligation of your vow, it remains a valid offering.

The *Gemara* cites the *pasuk* of *motza sefatecha tishmor ve-asita ka-asher nadarta l'Hashem Elokecha nedavah* — "What comes forth from your lips you shall observe and do, as you vowed to Hashem your God, a *nedavah*." The surface reading is puzzling: if one made a *neder*, why does the *pasuk* call it a *nedavah*?

The *Gemara* resolves this by reading the *pasuk* as follows: *im ka-asher nadarta asita* — if you did it exactly as you vowed, performing all the *avodot* for the sake of the designated *korban* and its owners — *yehei neder*, it counts as a *neder* and satisfies your personal obligation. But *v'im lav* — if something went wrong, if the *avodah* was not performed for the proper sake — *yehei nedavah*: it still stands as a valid *korban*, functioning like a *nedavah* — a freewill offering — but it does not count as a *neder*. That is, it does not fulfill the owner's personal obligation.

A *neder* is an acceptance of personal obligation; a *nedavah* is the sanctification of an animal without a binding personal obligation. The *pasuk* teaches that when the *avodah* is not performed correctly — when it is *shelo lishmah* in some respect — the *korban* exists in an intermediate state: it is *kasher* as a *korban*, but it does not discharge the owner's obligation. It functions *k'nedavah* rather than *k'neder*.

### Three Pesukim Combined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ״, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה״; דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מוֹצָא״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא [לא יָדַע בְּמַאי; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא] לִיפַּסְלוּ; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was necessary** for the Torah to include the verse: **"That which has gone out of your lips," and it was necessary** to include the verse: **"This is the law." As, had the Merciful One written** in the Torah only the verse: **"That which has gone out** of your lips," **I would say** that we **do not know what** requirement does not disqualify the offering if not fulfilled. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes** the verse: **This is the law,** juxtaposing all offerings with a peace offering, which must be performed for its own sake. **And if the Merciful One had written** only the verse: **This is the law, I would say** that offerings sacrificed not for their sake **should be disqualified.** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes** the verse: **That which is gone out of your lips,** teaching that they are accepted, though they do not satisfy their owners' obligations.

The *Gemara* makes explicit that all three *pesukim* are necessary and each contributes something distinct. *Ve-itzterich motza sefatecha* — you need the *pasuk* of *motza sefatecha* to tell you what happens when the *avodah* is not done correctly, namely that the *korban* enters this intermediate state. *Ve-itzterich zot hatorah* — you need *zot hatorah* to tell you which requirements, when violated, trigger this intermediate state: it was *zot hatorah* that taught that all the *lishmah* requirements applying to *shelamim* extend to all *korbanot*. Had we only the *motza sefatecha pasuk*, we would not know which specific deficiencies it addresses. And had we only *zot hatorah*, we would have concluded that any violation of *lishmah* renders the *korban* invalid entirely, without the *motza sefatecha* *pasuk* to inform us that it results instead in this middle status of *kasher* but not *olu l'be'alim*.

The *Gemara* then raises a question about the *chatat*: *zot hatorah* includes the *chatat* in its list, yet the *Mishnah* teaches that a *chatat* performed *shelo lishmah* is invalid altogether. The resolution is that the special status of the *chatat* — that *shelo lishmah* renders it fully *pasul* — is derived from a different *pasuk*, as the *Gemara* will develop. For purposes of the present derivation from *zot hatorah*, that *pasuk* establishes the *lishmah* requirement across all *korbanot lechatchila*; the *chatat*'s unique rule of *pesul bedi'avad* comes from elsewhere.

## Asham Ownership Problem

### Reish Lakish Throws Self…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְמֵי רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ עַל מְעוֹהִי בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא, וּמַקְשֵׁי: אִם כְּשֵׁרִים הֵם – יְרַצּוּ! וְאִם אֵין מְרַצִּין – לָמָּה בָּאִין?
>
> **Translation:** § **Reish Lakish raised a difficulty while lying on his stomach in the study hall: If** offerings that were sacrificed for the sake of the wrong type of offering or someone other than the owner **are fit, let them propitiate** God, i.e., let them satisfy the obligation of their owners; **and if they do not propitiate** God, **why are they brought** as offerings at all?

The *Gemara* now pivots from the *pesukim* to a fundamental conceptual question. The intermediate status — *kasher* but not working for the owners — is established textually, but can it be sustained conceptually? The *Gemara* introduces this question with a vivid image: *rami Reish Lakish al me'ohi bei midrasha u-makshi* — Reish Lakish threw himself on his stomach on the floor of the *beit midrash* and posed this question. The verb *rami* ordinarily introduces a contradiction between two Tannaitic sources; here it seems to capture instead a logical tension that Reish Lakish found so acute that it could not be contained in ordinary discourse.

His question: *im kesherim heim yirtzu* — if these *korbanot* are *kasher*, they should work for the owners. *Ve-im einam mirtzim lama ba'im* — and if they are not going to work for the owners, why should they be brought at all? The intermediate status seems logically unstable. It should be all or nothing: either a *korban* is fully valid and counts for the owner, or it is invalid.

### Olah After Death Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר) [אֶלְעָזָר]: מָצִינוּ בְּבָאִין לְאַחַר מִיתָה, שֶׁהֵן כְּשֵׁרִין וְאֵין מְרַצִּין. דִּתְנַן: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהֵבִיאָה חַטָּאתָהּ וּמֵתָה – יָבִיאוּ יוֹרְשִׁין עוֹלָתָהּ. עוֹלָתָהּ וּמֵתָה – לֹא יָבִיאוּ יוֹרְשִׁין חַטָּאתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said to him: We have found** a precedent for this **in** the case of offerings **brought after** the **death** of their owners, **as they are fit, but they do not propitiate** God, as they do not have owners requiring atonement. This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Kinnim* 2:5): With regard to **a woman** after childbirth **who brought her sin offering and** then **died,** the **heirs shall bring her burnt offering.** If she brought **her burnt offering and** then **died,** the **heirs shall not bring her sin offering.** Evidently, a burnt offering is sacrificed even if it does not satisfy its owner's obligation.

Rabbi Elazar responds to Reish Lakish: *matzinu be-va le-achar misa she-hein kesherim ve-ein mirtzim* — we already find cases where a *korban* is valid but does not work for its owners, namely a *korban* that is brought after the owner's death. The *Gemara* cites a *Mishnah*: *ha-isha she-hevia chatasa u-mesa* — a woman who brought her *chatat* (associated with the *korban* brought after childbirth, the subject of *parashat Tazria*) and then died. *Yaviu yorshim olasah* — her heirs should bring her *olah*, if she had already designated it. An *olah* can be brought after the owner's death; a *chatat* cannot. This is the principle of *chatat le-achar misa*: a *chatat* designated by a deceased person is left to die, while an *olah* or *shelamim* that was sanctified is brought even after the owner's death. In that case, the *korban* is *kasher* — its blood is sprinkled, its meat or *emurim* may be consumed or burned as appropriate — yet there is no living owner for it to work for.

### Asham Not After Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מוֹדֵינָא לָךְ בְּעוֹלָה, דְּאָתְיָא לְאַחַר מִיתָה; אָשָׁם, דְּלָא אָתֵי לְאַחַר מִיתָה – מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to him: I concede to you with regard to a burnt offering** that it is sacrificed even if it does not satisfy its owner's obligation, **since it is brought** even **after** its owner's **death** in the case of the woman who died after bringing her sin offering. But **from where do we** derive that **a guilt offering, which** is brought for atonement and therefore **not brought after** its owner's **death,** is brought even in a case where it was slaughtered not for its sake and therefore will not satisfy its owner's obligation?

Reish Lakish is not satisfied. *Modeh ani lach be-olah de-asya le-achar misa* — he concedes the point regarding an *olah* or *shelamim* that comes after the owner's death. These are *korbanot* that can arise from a *nedavah*, a freewill offering, and are by their nature less tightly bound to the personal standing of the owner. It is intuitive, if not simple, that such *korbanot* can function without a living owner. But the *asham* is categorically different. An *asham* does not come after death. If a person designated an *asham* and died, the *asham* is not brought. It is entirely defined by its connection to a living obligated owner — it is a *korban* of atonement, rooted in a specific *chet* and a specific person. The phenomenon of an *olah* operating without an owner cannot serve as a precedent for an *asham*, because an *asham* never exists without its owner in any recognized form. The conceptual problem — how can an *asham* be *kasher* and yet be severed from its owner — remains unresolved.

Rabbi Elazar points out that Reish Lakish's position is exactly that of Rabbi Eliezer in the *Mishnah*, who rules that an *asham* performed *shelo lishmah* is invalid. But Reish Lakish dismisses this: *ka-amina ana mishnah shelemah* — he is troubled by the *stam Mishnah*, the accepted *halachah* that rejects Rabbi Eliezer's view and holds that the *asham* is *kasher* but does not count for the owner. The fact that a dissenting view shares his intuition does not resolve his question about the accepted *halachah*.

### Reish Lakish's Own Solut…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אֶפְתַּח אֲנָא פִּתְחָא לְנַפְשַׁאי: ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״ – הַאי נְדָבָה?! נֶדֶר הוּא! כּוּ׳ כְּדִלְעֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Reish Lakish said: I will introduce a solution to my own** difficulty. He then recited the exegesis stated above: The verse states: **"That which has gone out of your lips** you shall observe and do, **etc."** How can **this** be referring to **a gift** offering? **It is** already referred to as **a vow** offering; **and so on, as** stated **above.** In other words, it is derived from a verse that although the offering does not satisfy the obligation of its owner, it is fit to be sacrificed as a gift offering.

Finding no satisfactory answer from others, Reish Lakish declares *eftecha ana pitcha lenafshai* — he will open the door himself. He derives the intermediate status from the *pasuk* of *motza sefatecha*: *im ka-asher nadarta l'Hashem Elokecha nedavah* — if it was done as you vowed, it is a *neder*; if not, it stands as a *nedavah*. This *pasuk* establishes that a *korban* which was initiated as a personal obligation but whose *avodah* was not performed correctly can exist in a middle state, functioning *k'nedavah* — as a freewill offering not tethered to the owner's obligation.

A notable point about the chronology of this *sugya*: Rashi observes that the attribution of this reading of *motza sefatecha* to Reish Lakish suggests that it was Reish Lakish himself who first worked out this derivation. The *Gemara* had already cited the *pasuk* twice earlier in the *sugya* in the voice of the *stam*, without attribution — likely because once Reish Lakish's derivation became known, the *Gemara* incorporated it into the earlier discussions. When we read the earlier citations and wonder why Reish Lakish did not simply recall them, the answer is that he was the one who first found the *pasuk*; the earlier appearances are the *Gemara*'s retroactive integration of his contribution.

## Chatat Shelo Lishmah

### Gemara Challenges Soluti…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַבִּי זֵירָא וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַבָּא, וְיָתֵיב אַבָּיֵי גַּבַּיְיהוּ, וְיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָשָׁם דְּלָא אָתֵי לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וְנָסֵיב לַהּ תַּלְמוּדָא ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ״; אֵימָא: הַבָּא בְּנֶדֶר וּבִנְדָבָה – לֵייתֵי וְלָא לִירַצֵּי, אָשָׁם – לָא לֵייתֵי כְּלָל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Abba were sitting, and Abaye was sitting with them. And they were sitting and saying:** The case of **a guilt offering was difficult for Reish Lakish, as** a guilt offering is **not brought after** the owner's **death, and he adduced the derivation** from the verse: **"That which has gone out of your lips,"** as a solution **to it.** This resolution is difficult: Why not **say** that only an offering **that is brought for a vow or a gift is brought** even in a case where it **does not propitiate,** since the verse mentions a vow and a gift; but **a guilt offering,** which is not brought voluntarily, **should not be brought at all** if it was slaughtered for the sake of the wrong type of offering. How does the verse resolve Reish Lakish's difficulty?

A group of Amoraim — Rav Zeira, Rav Yitzchak bar Abba, and Abaye — are sitting together and discussing Reish Lakish's dramatic question and the *motza sefatecha* solution he offered. They raise a challenge: *eima haba b'neder u-vindavah laysu ve-lo yirtzu, asham lo laysi klal* — the *pasuk* of *motza sefatecha* speaks the language of *neder* and *nedavah*, which is the conceptual world of *olah* and *shelamim*, *korbanot* that can originate from either a personal vow or a freewill designation. In that world, it is intelligible that something can shift from the status of a *neder* to that of a *nedavah* — it remains a *korban* but its tether to the owner's personal obligation is loosened. But an *asham* does not exist in that world. An *asham* arises only from a Torah-imposed obligation; it is never a *nedavah*. You cannot apply a *pasuk* about *neder* and *nedavah* to the *asham* and thereby establish a middle state for it. Reish Lakish's problem, they argue, remains unsolved.

### Otah Lechatas Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מֵהָכָא פְּתַח: ״וְשָׁחַט אוֹתָהּ לְחַטָּאת״; אוֹתָהּ – לִשְׁמָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה, שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ פְּסוּלָה; הָא שְׁאָר קֳדָשִׁים – שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן כְּשֵׁרִין. יָכוֹל יְרַצּוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to them:** Although **Reish Lakish** cited that verse, he actually **introduced** the solution to his difficulty **from here: "And slaughter it for a sin offering"** (Leviticus 4:33). It is derived from the word **"it"** in this verse that if a sin offering is slaughtered **for its sake,** it is **fit;** if it is slaughtered **not for its sake,** it is **unfit. Consequently, other offerings** slaughtered **not for their sake,** including a guilt offering, **are fit.** And since one **might** have thought that once they are fit, they also **propitiate** God, **the verse states: "That which has gone out of your lips,"** from which it is derived that such an offering does not satisfy its owner's obligation.

Abaye offers an answer. He says that Reish Lakish did not ultimately derive the *asham*'s middle status from *motza sefatecha* alone. Rather, his foundation was a different *pasuk*: *ve-shachat osah le-chatat* — "and he shall slaughter *it* as a *chatat*." The word *osah* — *it* — introduces a limiting principle. *Osah lishmah kesherah, shelo lishmah pesulah* — doing *it* for the sake of a *chatat* renders it *kasher*; not doing it for the sake of a *chatat* renders it *pasul*. The word *osah* signals that this harsher consequence — invalidity *bedi'avad* when performed *shelo lishmah* — is unique to the *chatat*.

### Chatat Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא ״אוֹתָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The Merciful One excluded** all other offerings from the *halakha* concerning a sin offering in this matter, as derived from the verse: "And slaughter **it** for a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:33).

The derivation proceeds: *osah* specifies that the *chatat*'s demand that *lishmah* be *me'akeiv bedi'avad* is unique to the *chatat* alone. *She'ar kodashim*, all other *kodashim* — including the *asham* — are therefore *kasher bedi'avad* even if performed *shelo lishmah*. We now know from *otah le-chatat* that the *asham* is *kasher* when done *shelo lishmah*. The remaining question is whether being *kasher* means it also counts for the owner. For this, *motza sefatecha* becomes relevant: the *pasuk* teaches that even when a *korban* is *kasher*, it does not fulfill the owner's obligation if the *avodah* was not performed correctly — *yehei nedavah* rather than *yehei neder*. Together, *otah le-chatat* tells us the *asham* is *kasher*, and *motza sefatecha* tells us it does not count for the owner. The two *pesukim* together establish the *asham*'s intermediate status.

## Asham Owner Counting

### Asham Meratze Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: הַבָּא בְּנֶדֶר וּנְדָבָה – לֵייתֵי וְלָא לִירַצֵּי, אָשָׁם (נָמֵי) – אַרְצוֹיֵי נָמֵי לִירַצֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Abba asked him: **But** since the phrase in the verse "that which has gone out of your lips" is referring to offerings **brought for a vow or a gift,** why not **say** that only these offerings **do not propitiate** God even though **one must bring** them if slaughtered for the sake of the wrong offering, but **a guilt offering** slaughtered for the sake of the wrong offering **propitiates** God **as well?**

The *Gemara* now poses a further challenge. Even accepting that an *asham* performed *shelo lishmah* is *kasher*, perhaps it should be fully *meratze* — perhaps it should count entirely for the owner. After all, the *asham* is so tightly bound to the person who brought it, so thoroughly defined by its connection to a specific owner and a specific *chet*, that if it is *kasher* at all, it ought to count for the owner completely. The logic that *motza sefatecha* places it in an intermediate state may work for *olah* and *shelamim*, where the connection to the owner is looser, but perhaps for an *asham*, if it is valid, it must be *meratze*.

### Kal VaChomer Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אָשָׁם דְּמִירַצֵּי לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵעוֹלָה; וּמָה עוֹלָה שֶׁאֵינָּהּ מְכַפֶּרֶת – אֵינָהּ מְרַצָּה, אָשָׁם שֶׁמְּכַפֵּר – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְרַצֶּה?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** in response: **You cannot say that a guilt offering** that was slaughtered for the sake of the wrong offering **propitiates** God, due to **an *a fortiori*** inference **from a burnt offering: Just as a burnt offering, which does not atone** for a sin as it is brought as a vow or gift offering, still **does not propitiate** God if it was slaughtered for the sake of the wrong offering, so too, with regard to **a guilt offering, which atones** for a sin and is therefore treated more stringently, **is it not logical that it does not propitiate** God?

Abaye offers a *kal va-chomer* to counter this. *U-mah olah she-einah mechaperet einah mirtzah* — an *olah* does not normally serve the function of *kaparah*, atonement, and yet when performed *shelo lishmah* it is not *meratze*, does not count for the owner. *Asham she-mechaper* — an *asham*, whose very function is *kaparah* — *eino din she-eino mirtzah* — is it not all the more so that it should not be *meratze* when performed *shelo lishmah*?

The logic here requires unpacking. The argument is not that the *asham* is weaker than the *olah* and therefore a *kal va-chomer* applies in the usual direction of strength to weakness. Rather, the point is precisely about the direct connection between purpose and person: an *olah* has no inherent *kaparah* function in the ordinary sense — it is a gift *korban*, an ascent offering, not primarily a means of atonement. And even so, when performed *shelo lishmah*, it does not count for the owner. An *asham*, by contrast, is entirely defined by its *kaparah* function — it is the most personally connected *korban* to the owner's obligation. If even the *olah*, with its looser connection to the owner, does not count when performed *shelo lishmah*, then the *asham*, so intimately bound to the owner's personal sin and atonement, certainly should not count when performed *shelo lishmah*. The tightness of the connection to the owner works against the *asham* being *meratze*, not in its favor.

### Comparing Korbanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְעוֹלָה, שֶׁכֵּן כָּלִיל! שְׁלָמִים יוֹכִיחוּ. מָה לִשְׁלָמִים, שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין נְסָכִין וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק! עוֹלָה תּוֹכִיחַ. וְחָזַר הַדִּין. לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה; הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁהֵן קֳדָשִׁים, וּשְׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן כְּשֵׁירִין וְאֵין מְרַצִּין; אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא אָשָׁם – שֶׁהוּא קוֹדֶשׁ, וּשְׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ כָּשֵׁר וְאֵינוֹ מְרַצֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: If so, **a burnt offering can prove** the point, since these stringencies do not apply to it. **And the inference has reverted** to its starting point. At this stage the *halakha* is derived from a combination of the two sources: **The aspect of this** case, a burnt offering, **is not like the aspect of that** case, the peace offering. **And the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case. **Their common element is that they are offerings and** if one **slaughtered them not for their sake, they are fit but they do not propitiate. So too, I shall include a guilt offering** in this *halakha*, **as it is an offering, and** therefore if one **slaughtered it not for its sake,** it is **fit but it does not propitiate.**

The *Gemara* challenges the *kal va-chomer* step by step. Perhaps an *olah* is distinctive because it is *kalil* — entirely burned on the *mizbe'ach* — and this unique quality, not the absence of *kaparah* function, is what explains why it is not *meratze shelo lishmah*. In that case, we cannot extend the rule to an *asham*, which is not *kalil*. The *Gemara* counters: *shelamim yochiachu* — a *shelamim* is also not *meratze* when performed *shelo lishmah*, and a *shelamim* is certainly not *kalil*. But then the *Gemara* objects to the *shelamim* side of the argument: *mah le-shelamim she-kein te'unim nesachim tenufah va-chazeh va-shok* — *shelamim* have greater weight due to their requirement of *nesachim*, *tenufah*, and the *chazeh va-shok*; perhaps these additional components explain why the rule applies to *shelamim* and does not extend to an *asham*.

The back-and-forth continues: *olah tochiah* — an *olah* also lacks *nesachim*, *tenufah*, and *chazeh va-shok*, and yet the rule applies to it. *Ve-chazar ha-din* — the argument goes in circles. Each *korban* has unique characteristics that could potentially explain the rule applying to it specifically, and none of those unique characteristics is shared by all the others.

### Common Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה – הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁהֵן קֳדָשִׁים וְשָׁחְטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן כְּשֵׁרִים וְאֵין מְרַצִּים; אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא אָשָׁם שֶׁהוּא קוֹדֶשׁ וְשָׁחְטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ כָּשֵׁר וְאֵינוֹ מִרְצֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** At this stage the *halakha* is derived from a combination of the two sources: **The aspect of this** case, **is not like the aspect of that** case, **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case. **Their common element is that they are offerings, and** if one **slaughtered them not for their sake, they are fit but they do not propitiate.** **So too, I shall include a guilt offering** in this *halakha*, **as it is an offering, and** therefore if one **slaughtered it not for its sake,** it is **fit but it does not propitiate.**

The *Gemara* resolves this through the principle of *tzad ha-shaveh*: *lo rei zeh ke-rei zeh ve-lo rei zeh ke-rei zeh — hatzad ha-shaveh sha-bahen she-hein kodshim ve-shachtan shelo lishmah kesherim ve-einam mirtzim*. The distinctive characteristics of each *korban* are not identical to those of the others. But the common denominator across all of them — *olah*, *shelamim*, and *todah* — is that they are all *kodshim*, and when slaughtered *shelo lishmah* they are *kasher* and yet not *meratze*. Through this *tzad ha-shaveh*, we include the *asham* as well: *af ani avi asham she-hu kodesh ve-shachto shelo lishmo kasher ve-eino mirtze*. The *asham* is *kodesh*, and when slaughtered *shelo lishmah* it is *kasher* and not *meratze*.

The *Gemara* raises one more objection. The common denominator of *olah*, *shelamim*, and *todah* is that they all can come as a *neder* or a *nedavah*, and they can exist even without the same tight tether to a living obligated owner. Perhaps it is precisely this quality — *she-hein ba'in b'neder u-vindavah* — that explains why the rule of *kasher ve-eino mirtze* applies to them, and not some general principle applicable to all *korbanot*. This returns us to the conceptual difficulty that has been present throughout: the *asham* is different in kind.

## Zot HaTorah Asham

### Rava's Zot HaTorah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה וְגוֹ׳״ – הִקִּישׁוֹ הַכָּתוּב לִשְׁלָמִים; מָה שְׁלָמִים שֶׁהֵן קֳדָשִׁים, וּשְׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן כְּשֵׁרִים וְאֵין מְרַצִּין; אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא אָשָׁם שֶׁהוּא קוֹדֶשׁ כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says: The verse: "This is the law** of the burnt offering, of the meal offering, and of the sin offering, and of the guilt offering, and of the consecration offering, and of the sacrifice of peace offerings" (Leviticus 7:37), **juxtaposes** a guilt offering **with a peace offering,** indicating that **just as peace offerings are offerings and** if one **slaughtered them not for their sake, they are fit but do not propitiate, so too, I shall include a guilt offering, as it is an offering,** and therefore if it is slaughtered not for its sake, although it is fit, it does not satisfy the obligation of its owner.

At this point Rava provides the definitive answer: *ello amar Rava zot hatorah* — Rava derives the *asham*'s intermediate status from *zot hatorah*. The *pasuk* of *zot hatorah* equates all *korbanot* to *shelamim* via *hekesh*: just as *shelamim*, which are *kodshim* and when slaughtered *shelo lishmah* are *kasher ve-einam mirtzim*, so too all *korbanot* listed in that *pasuk* — including the *asham* — share the same rule. *Hekisho ha-katuv l'shelamim*: the *Torah* connected them all to *shelamim*, and *shelamim* have this intermediate status when performed *shelo lishmah*.

### Not Connected to Chatat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חָזֵית דְּאַקֵּשְׁתְּ לִשְׁלָמִים? אַקֵּישׁ לְחַטָּאת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What did you see,** i.e., why do you think it is reasonable, **that you compared** a guilt offering **to a peace offering?** Why not **compare** it **to a sin offering,** which is disqualified in such a case?

One might object: *mai chazis de-akish l'shelamim* — why connect the *asham* to *shelamim* and conclude it has this intermediate status? Perhaps instead we should connect the *asham* to the *chatat*, which is also listed in *zot hatorah*, and conclude that an *asham* performed *shelo lishmah* is fully invalid. If the *chatat* is *pasul* when performed *shelo lishmah*, and if the *asham* is comparable to the *chatat* in its tightness of connection to the owner, perhaps the *asham* should share the *chatat*'s stringency.

### Otah Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא ״אוֹתָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The Merciful One excluded** all other offerings from the *halakha* concerning a sin offering in this matter, as derived from the verse: "And slaughter **it** for a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:33).

The *Gemara* answers: *ha mi'eit rachmana osah* — the word *osah* in the *pasuk* of *ve-shachat osah le-chatat* specifically and exclusively limits the harsher rule — invalidity *bedi'avad* — to the *chatat* alone. We cannot connect the *asham* to the *chatat*'s harsher rule because the *Torah* explicitly excluded everything other than the *chatat* from that rule via *osah*. With the *chatat* ruled out as a model, we must connect the *asham* to *shelamim* — and *shelamim* teach us the intermediate state of *kasher ve-eino mirtze*. The *chatat*'s inclusion in *zot hatorah* serves a different purpose: it establishes the *lechatchila* requirement of *lishmah* for the *chatat* along with all other *korbanot*. The *bedi'avad* consequence — *pesul* — comes from *otah*, not from *zot hatorah*.

## Asham Without Owner

### Three Rabbis Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב הוּנָא וְרַב נַחְמָן, וְיָתֵיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת גַּבַּיְיהוּ, וְיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ – אָשָׁם דְּלָא אָתֵי לְאַחַר מִיתָה; לֵימָא לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָשָׁם נָמֵי אָתֵי לְאַחַר מִיתָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna and Rav Naḥman were sitting, and Rav Sheshet was sitting with them. And they were sitting and saying: Reish Lakish raised** his **difficulty** with regard to **a guilt offering, since it is not brought after** its owner's **death. Let Rabbi Elazar say to him** that **a guilt offering is also** essentially **brought after** its owner's **death,** since it is sold once it develops a blemish, and the proceeds are used to buy a burnt offering.

A second group of *Amoraim* — Rav Huna, Rav Nachman, and Rav Sheshet, who was sitting with them — reflect on the same problem that occupied Reish Lakish: *kasha lei le-Reish Lakish, asham de-lo asi le-achar misa* — Reish Lakish was troubled that the *asham* does not come *le-achar misa*, and therefore it seemed to lack any precedent for existing in a state severed from its owner. They consider what Rabbi Elazar could have said to Reish Lakish beyond citing the *olah* after death.

### Asham Reincarnated Form

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אָשָׁם לְמַאי קָרֵב – לְמוֹתָרוֹ; חַטָּאת נָמֵי מִיקְרָב קָרְבָה מוֹתָרַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said to them:** This claim can be refuted: **What** element **of a guilt offering is sacrificed** after its owner's death? Only **its remainder,** i.e., the money from its sale that is used to purchase an animal to be sacrificed. And if so, it is no different from **a sin offering, the remainder of which is also sacrificed,** and a sin offering itself is entirely disqualified if slaughtered not for its sake.

Rabbi Elazar could have told Reish Lakish: *asham nami asi le-achar misa* — there is, in fact, a sense in which an *asham* does come *le-achar misa*. If a person designated an *asham* and then died, what happens to the animal? It is left to graze until it develops a *mum* (blemish), at which point its *kedushah* is transferred to money through redemption, and that money is used to purchase an *olah*, which is then offered on the *mizbe'ach*. The *asham* does not simply cease to exist. In a sense, its *kedushah* persists and is ultimately realized — in a reincarnated form, as an *olah* on the *mizbe'ach*. The *kedushah* migrates: the animal is redeemed, the money takes on the sanctity, and the *olah* purchased with that money is the final expression of the original *asham*'s consecration.

### Motaro Becomes Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטָּאת – אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָרְבָה מוֹתָרַהּ, מִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא ״הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna and Rav Naḥman explained: **A sin offering** that was slaughtered not for its sake is disqualified **even though its remainder is sacrificed,** because **the Merciful One** specifically **excludes** a sin offering from being a fit offering if sacrificed not for its own sake, as derived from the verse: "And he shall lay his hand upon the head of the goat, and slaughter it in the place where they slaughter the burnt offering before the Lord; **it** is a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:24). The redundant phrase "it is a sin offering" teaches that a sin offering is disqualified if sacrificed not for its own sake.

Rav Sheshet clarifies the precise mechanics: *asham le-mai karev — le-motaro*. The *asham* itself is not offered *le-achar misa*; what is offered is its *motar* — the residual value of its *kedushah* realized through the chain of redemption and repurchase as an *olah*. One might object that the same could be said of a *chatat*: a *chatat* also has a *motar* in a very specific case — when someone designated two *chataot* as mutual guarantors (*hifrish shtei chataot le-achrayut*), was atoned for through one of them, and then died. In that unusual case, the second *chatat* can still be brought in some form. The *Gemara* responds: *chatat af al gav de-karva motara, mi'eit rachmana hu* — even though a *chatat* has a *motar* in that narrow case, the word *hu* (or *hi*, in the feminine, *hia*) in the *pasuk* about the *chatat* limits and excludes, establishing that the stringency of *pesul shelo lishmah* applies uniquely to the *chatat*. The *chatat*'s special treatment is grounded in that word, not undermined by the exceptional *motar* case.

### Precedent for Middle Sta…

This discussion provides an important conceptual foundation for the *halachah* that has been under examination throughout the *sugya*. The persistent difficulty with the *asham*'s intermediate status was this: an *asham* is so bound to its owner that it seemed impossible to conceive of it existing in a middle state — *kasher* yet not *meratze* — severed from the owner who brought it. The resolution offered here is that this is not entirely without parallel even within the world of *asham* itself. The *asham* can exist *le-achar misa* in a reincarnated form: its *kedushah* migrates to money, which becomes an *olah*. This is not a full answer to the conceptual question, but it provides a *sevara* — a rational basis — for understanding that even the *asham*, though tightly bound to a living owner, is not absolutely and unconditionally annulled when that connection is severed. The *halachah* that an *asham* performed *shelo lishmah* is *kasher ve-eino mirtze* thus gains not only textual support from *otah le-chatat* and *zot hatorah*, but also a measure of conceptual grounding from the reality that the *asham*'s *kedushah* can persist and find expression on the *mizbe'ach* even after the owner has died.
