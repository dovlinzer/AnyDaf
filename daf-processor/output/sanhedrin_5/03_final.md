# Sanhedrin 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mumche L'Rabbim

### Definition of Mumche

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיָה מוּמְחֶה לְרַבִּים, דָּן אֲפִילּוּ יְחִידִי. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כְּגוֹן אֲנָא דָּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת בִּיחִידִי. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כְּגוֹן אֲנָא דָּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת בִּיחִידִי.
>
> **Translation:** **But if** one **was** a judge accepted as **an expert for the public,** then **he may judge** cases **even** as the **lone** judge. **Rav Naḥman said:** One **such as I may judge** cases of **monetary law as** the **lone** judge. **And similarly, Rabbi Ḥiyya said:** One **such as I may judge** cases of **monetary law as** the **lone** judge.

The daf opens one line from the bottom of 4b. The *baraita* states: *dinei mamonos b'shlosha* — monetary cases require three judges. It then adds: *im hayah mumche l'rabbim, dan afilu yechidi* — if the judge was a *mumche l'rabbim*, an expert recognized by the public, he may judge even alone. This is precisely the principle left unresolved at the end of the previous discussion. The *Gemara* then asks: who qualifies as a *mumche l'rabbim*? Rav Nachman answers by citing himself — *kigon ana dan dinei mamonos b'yechidi*, "someone like me, who judges monetary cases alone" — and Rabbi Chiya says the same.

### Learning & Licensing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כְּגוֹן אֲנָא דִּגְמִירְנָא וּסְבִירְנָא וּנְקִיטְנָא רְשׁוּתָא, אֲבָל לָא נְקִיט רְשׁוּתָא – דִּינֵיהּ לָא דִּינָא? אוֹ דִילְמָא: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא נָקֵיט רְשׁוּתָא – דִּינֵיהּ דִּינָא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: What is the meaning of: Such as I, in the statements of these Rabbis? Did they intend to say: **Such as I,** in **that I have studied and** have the skills to **extrapolate** and derive new rulings on the basis of earlier decisions, **and have** also **received permission** to judge as the lone judge? **But** accordingly, if one **has not received permission** to judge as the lone judge, **his judgment is not** a valid **judgment? Or perhaps** this is not the correct reading of the statements, and the *halakha* is that **even though he did not receive permission** to judge as the lone judge, **his judgment is** nevertheless a valid **judgment?**

The *Gemara* then unpacks the question further. What exactly is required? The first component is *gmurna v'savirna* — erudition in received tradition combined with analytical acuity. One has both mastered the transmitted corpus and can reason through novel problems. But is that sufficient? The second component raised is *v'naktna rishusa* — that one has also obtained formal licensing, permission from the political head of the Jewish community. The *Gemara* asks whether the absence of this licensing renders a judgment invalid: *aval lo nakt rishusa dinei lo dina*, or whether a *talmid chacham* who simply knows the law may rule without such permission and have his ruling stand nonetheless.

To understand why licensing is even conceptually relevant here, one must recall the broader framework. The requirement of three judges for *dinei mamonos* is most likely a Torah-level requirement. The institution of the *dayan yechidi*, the single judge, is thus a rabbinic allowance, motivated by the principle of *lo tinol deles bifnei lovin* — not to shut the door before borrowers by making monetary adjudication impractical. Precisely because this is a rabbinic creation, the Rabbis could attach conditions to it. They required that the single judge be a *mumche l'rabbim*, and they may additionally have required formal licensing. The concept of *rishus* — licensing — fits naturally within a rabbinic framework: if the Rabbis are the ones permitting this departure from the standard three-judge requirement, they could also stipulate that recognized authorization from the appropriate communal authority be part of what confers that permission.

### Ramban's Distinction

At this point it is important to note that the term *mumche* appears elsewhere in the *Gemara* — including in Rashi's comments back on 2b — where it referred to *smicha*, the chain of rabbinic ordination extending back to Moshe Rabbeinu. That usage of *mumche* is the status required when the word *Elohim* in the verse demands representation of divine authority, as is necessary for cases of *dinei knasos*, *malkus*, or capital punishment.

The term *mumche l'rabbim* here is categorically different, and the *Ramban* makes this distinction with characteristic precision. Counterintuitively, *mumche l'rabbim* denotes a lower status than plain *mumche*. The phrase does not mean one is an expert in some intensified or broader sense; rather, it means one is recognized by the community as an expert — *baduk lahem*, tested by them through experience. The *Ramban* draws an analogy to the *mumche* who writes amulets: that term refers to someone the community has come to rely upon, not someone who holds a formal, objectively verifiable status. He writes: *shehakol yodim bo degarim vesavir* — everyone knows he is knowledgeable — *ve'eino mumche latorah she'eino samuch berabbi*, but he does not hold the *smicha* that confers the title Rabbi in the full traditional sense. Two usages, then: *mumche* as formal ordination going back to Sinai, and *mumche l'rabbim* as communal recognition of expertise, which is what authorizes the single judge in monetary cases.

### Mar Zutra Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּמָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן דָּן דִּינָא וּטְעָה, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף. אָמַר לוֹ: אִם קִיבְּלוּךְ עֲלַיְיהוּ – לָא תְּשַׁלֵּם, וְאִי לָא – זִיל שַׁלֵּים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כִּי לָא נָקֵיט רְשׁוּתָא – דִּינֵיהּ דִּינָא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a solution to this dilemma from the following case: **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman,** once **adjudicated** a certain **case and erred** in his ruling. Upon recognizing his error, **he came before Rav Yosef** to ask what he should do. Rav Yosef **said to him: If** the litigants **accepted you upon themselves** as the lone judge, and both had agreed that they would accept your ruling, **you are not** liable to **pay** restitution to the party who lost the case due to your erroneous ruling. **But if** they did **not** accept you on themselves, but were rather compelled to be judged before you, you must **go** and **pay** restitution. And **learn from it** that even in a case **where one did not receive permission** to judge as the lone judge, **his ruling is** a valid **judgment.** The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that this is the case.

The *Gemara* now seeks to resolve whether licensing is strictly necessary. It brings the case of Mar Zutra, son of Rav Nachman, who issued a judgment — apparently without having obtained licensing from the *Reish Galuta* — and erred. He came before Rav Yosef, who ruled: *im kibluch aleiyhu lo teshalem* — if the parties had accepted his authority, he need not pay — *ve'ee lo zil shelim* — but if he imposed his authority on them without their consent, he must pay. This closely parallels what was seen in the Yerushalmi: acceptance of authority extends latitude, while imposed authority is held to a more objective standard.

What matters for the current question, however, is not the issue of payment but the status of the judgment itself. Even in the case where Mar Zutra imposed his authority — and we understand this was a case without formal licensing — the judgment stood. The payment question is secondary; the ruling was not invalidated. From this the *Gemara* concludes: *shema mina ki lo nakat reshuta dina dina* — even without licensing, the judgment is binding. That inference is accepted.

### Licensing & Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הַאי מַאן דְּבָעֵי לְמֵידַן דִּינָא, וְאִי טְעָה מִיבְעֵי לְמִיפְּטַר – לִישְׁקוֹל רְשׁוּתָא מִבֵּי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא. וְכֵן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לִשְׁקוֹל רְשׁוּתָא מִבֵּי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says: One who wants to adjudicate a case and wants to be exempt** from payment of restitution **if he errs** in his judgment **must receive permission from the Exilarch** to judge cases. **And similarly, Shmuel says:** In such a case **he must receive permission from the Exilarch.** Once he receives permission, even an erroneous decision carries halakhic force and therefore it is as if he did not err.

Rav then introduces a further practical dimension: *hai man deba'i lemidein dina ee ta'ei ba'ei lemiftera* — if one wishes to judge and also wishes to be exempt from liability in the event of error, there is a remedy: *lishkol reshuta mibei Reish Galuta*, obtain licensing from the *Reish Galuta*. Shmuel states the same ruling. The structure that emerges is therefore two-tiered. Being a *mumche l'rabbim* is sufficient for one's judgments to be binding — and perhaps even to impose authority — but it does not by itself confer immunity from liability if one errs. For that exemption, formal licensing from the *Reish Galuta* is required. One might wonder whether persistent errors would lead to revocation of that license, and who bears the authority to declare that a judge has erred in the first instance — questions the *Gemara* leaves for a later discussion.

## Jurisdiction: Bavel & EY

### Geographic Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא: מֵהָכָא לְהָכָא, וּמֵהָתָם לְהָתָם – מַהֲנֵי. וּמֵהָכָא לְהָתָם נָמֵי מַהֲנֵי, דְּהָכָא שֵׁבֶט וְהָתָם מְחוֹקֵק.
>
> **Translation:** Since the Gemara mentioned the importance of a judge receiving authorization from the Exilarch, it now discusses the scope of this authority. It is **obvious** that **from here to here,** meaning relying on permission granted by the Exilarch in Babylonia in order to adjudicate cases within Babylonia, **and from there to there,** relying on permission granted by the *Nasi* in Eretz Yisrael in order to adjudicate cases within Eretz Yisrael, the authorization **is effective. And** it is also obvious that **from here to there,** relying on permission granted by the Exilarch to adjudicate cases within Eretz Yisrael, it **is also effective,** as the authority of the Exilarch is greater than that of the *Nasi*. This is so **since** the Exilarch **here** in Babylonia may be termed **a scepter,** i.e., a ruler with actual power of governance, **and** the *Nasi* **there** in Eretz Yisrael is only **a staff,** i.e., a legislator with limited power.

Having established the role of *rishus* in conferring or protecting authority, the *Gemara* turns to the geographic scope of such authorization. The straightforward cases are clear: *me-hacha le-hacha* — authority granted in Bavel to judge in Bavel — and *me-hasam le-hasam* — authority granted in Eretz Yisrael to judge in Eretz Yisrael — these obviously work. The *Gemara* also states that authority granted in Bavel to judge in Eretz Yisrael — *me-hacha le-hasam* — likewise functions. The question is the reverse: what about authority granted in Eretz Yisrael to judge in Bavel?

### Staff & Lawgiver

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״לֹא יָסוּר שֵׁבֶט מִיהוּדָה״ – אֵלּוּ רָאשֵׁי גָלִיּוֹת שֶׁבְּבָבֶל, שֶׁרוֹדִין אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּשֵׁבֶט. ״וּמְחֹקֵק מִבֵּין רַגְלָיו״ – אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי בָּנָיו שֶׁל הִלֵּל, שֶׁמְּלַמְּדִין תּוֹרָה בָּרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "The scepter shall not depart from Judah nor the ruler's staff from between his feet until Shiloh comes" (Genesis 49:10). The term "Shiloh" is understood as a reference to the Messiah, and therefore the verse is interpreted as delineating the authority of Jewish rulers during the exile, before the Messiah comes. **"The scepter shall not depart from Judah"; these are the Exilarchs in Babylonia, who** are empowered by the government and consequently **subjugate the Jewish people** as **with a scepter. "Nor the ruler's staff from between his feet"; These are the grandchildren of Hillel** the Elder **who** hold the position of *Nasi* and **teach Torah in public,** but do not have authority to actually enforce their judgments.

The reason authority flows from Bavel to Eretz Yisrael is rooted in a *baraita* interpreting the verse *lo yasur shevet mi-Yehuda* — the staff shall not depart from Judah. The *baraita* identifies the *shevet*, the staff, with the *Roshei Galuyos* — the heads of the exile, the *Reish Galusa* — in Bavel, who exercise actual coercive power. *She-rodin es Yisrael be-shevet* — they govern Israel with a staff — meaning they had a genuine enforcement capacity backed by the surrounding government. The *mechokek*, the lawgiver, refers by contrast to the descendants of Hillel who teach Torah publicly in Eretz Yisrael. *Melamdim Torah be-rabbim* — they teach Torah to the many — but their authority is essentially one of persuasion and scholarship, not compulsion.

This distinction has direct relevance to the question of *rishus* in adjudication. The *Gemara*'s point is that since the *Reish Galusa* in Bavel holds the *shevet* — real coercive authority backed by the government — authorization from that office carries binding weight even beyond its immediate jurisdiction. This is why Bavel's authorization functions in Eretz Yisrael. There is a certain irony worth noting: the House of the *Nasi* in Eretz Yisrael, most famously Rebbe Yehuda HaNasi, combined both political and rabbinic authority in a single institution, whereas in Bavel the two were separate — political authority in the *Reish Galusa*, rabbinic authority diffused among the great Amoraim. Yet the *baraita* implies that in terms of coercive bindingness, the Babylonian political authority carried more practical weight. One might also note, as an aside, that Hillel himself was Babylonian — *Hillel HaBavli* — so that even the Torah authority of Eretz Yisrael ultimately traced its lineage to Bavel.

### Rabbah bar Chana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהָתָם לְהָכָא מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּרַבָּה בַּר חָנָה דָּן דִּינָא וּטְעָה. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי קִיבְּלוּךְ עֲלַיְיהוּ – לָא תְּשַׁלֵּם, וְאִי לָא – זִיל שַׁלֵּים. וְהָא רַבָּה בַּר חָנָה רְשׁוּתָא הֲוָה נְקִיט! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מֵהָתָם לְהָכָא לָא מַהֲנֵי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If one has permission **from there,** from the *Nasi*, and wants **to** adjudicate cases **here** in Babylonia, **what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an incident **that** occurred: **Rabba bar Ḥana adjudicated a case** in Babylonia **and erred. He came before Rabbi Ḥiyya** to ask what he should do. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: If** the litigants **accepted you upon themselves, you are not** liable to **pay** restitution to the party who unjustly lost the case, **but if not, go** and **pay. But Rabba bar Ḥana received permission** from the *Nasi* in Eretz Yisrael; therefore, **learn from** this incident that permission **from there to** adjudicate cases **here is not effective.** The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that this is the case.

The *Gemara* now seeks a proof on the key unresolved question: does authority granted in Eretz Yisrael function in Bavel? It brings the case of Rabbah bar Chana, who — as will be detailed shortly — received his authorization from Rabbi Chiya in Eretz Yisrael before traveling to Bavel, specifically with Bavel in mind. He subsequently erred in a ruling he gave there, and came before Rabbi Chiya. Rabbi Chiya told him: *ei kibluch alayhu lo teshalem* — if the parties accepted his authority, he need not pay — *ve'ei lo zil shelim* — if not, go pay.

Now, the *Gemara* observes that Rabbah bar Chana did in fact hold authorization from Rabbi Chiya. If authorization from Eretz Yisrael were valid in Bavel, he should have been automatically exempt from payment regardless of whether the parties accepted him. The fact that Rabbi Chiya subjected him to the same test — did they accept you or not? — implies that the Eretz Yisrael authorization carried no weight in Bavel. *Shema mina me-hatam le-hacha lo mahani* — authority from there to here does not help.

### Rav Huna Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא מַהֲנֵי? וְהָא רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, כִּי הֲוָה מִינְּצֵי בַּהֲדֵי דְּבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא, אָמַר: לָאו מִינַּיְיכוּ נְקִיטְנָא רְשׁוּתָא, נְקִיטְנָא רְשׁוּתָא מֵאַבָּא מָרִי, וְאַבָּא מָרִי מֵרַב, וְרַב מֵרַבִּי חִיָּיא, וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא מֵרַבִּי! בְּמִילְּתָא דְעָלְמָא הוּא דְּאוֹקֵים לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is this permission **not effective? But when Rabba bar Rav Huna was** involved **in a dispute with the** members of **the house of the Exilarch he said:** It is **not from you** that **I received permission** to judge cases. **I received permission from my father, my master,** i.e., Rav Huna, **and my father, my master,** received permission **from Rav, and Rav from Rabbi Ḥiyya, and Rabbi Ḥiyya from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi in Eretz Yisrael. Therefore, it seems that permission received in Eretz Yisrael is in fact effective in Babylonia. The Gemara rejects this proof: **He was** merely **standing up to them with words alone,** but there was no halakhic validity to his statement.

The *Gemara* challenges this conclusion by citing the behavior of Rabbah bar Rav Huna, a different figure. When Rabbah bar Rav Huna would clash with the house of the *Reish Galusa*, he would say: *lav minaichu nakitna reshuta* — do not think my authority derives from you. Rather, *nakitna reshuta mi-Abba Mari, ve-Abba Mari mi-Rav, ve-Rav mi-Rabbi Chiya, ve-Rabbi Chiya mi-Rebbi* — his authority derived from a chain going all the way back to Rebbe Yehuda HaNasi in Eretz Yisrael. This appears to be a direct claim that authorization originating in Eretz Yisrael carries weight in Bavel.

### EY Authority Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּלָא מַהֲנֵי, רַבָּה בַּר חָנָה רְשׁוּתָא דִּנְקַט לְמָה לִי? לָעֲיָירוֹת הָעוֹמְדִים עַל הַגְּבוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But since** permission to judge received in Eretz Yisrael **is not effective** in Babylonia, **why did Rabba bar Ḥana** need to **receive permission** when he left for Babylonia? What was the value of that permission? The Gemara answers: The permission is effective **for the cities that stand on the borders** of Babylonia, which are not entirely in the jurisdiction of Babylonia, so permission from Eretz Yisrael is effective there.

The *Gemara* deflects this challenge with a pointed answer: *be-milta d'alma hu d'okim le-hu* — he was merely trying to get them off his back. He was dropping the names of great authorities to deter the *Reish Galusa*'s household from pressuring him, but his claim had no actual legal foundation. Authority from Eretz Yisrael genuinely does not function to grant exemption from liability in Bavel. It is worth pausing to note that from the Bavli's perspective, the asymmetry is complete: Bavel's authorization functions in Eretz Yisrael, but Eretz Yisrael's authorization does not function in Bavel. One wonders, as the lecturer notes, whether the Yerushalmi would have reached the same conclusion.

### Border Towns Exception

This leaves a question. If authorization from Eretz Yisrael confers no benefit in Bavel, why did Rabbah bar Chana bother obtaining it before he traveled there? The *Gemara* answers: *le-ayoti omdim al ha-gevulin* — there were towns situated along the border between the two jurisdictions, towns whose affiliation was ambiguous. In those regions, the Eretz Yisrael authorization was operative and conferred at minimum the exemption from liability. In the heartland of Bavel, the authorization was without legal effect in terms of liability; but at the margins, it retained practical value. This satisfies the question of why obtaining the authorization was not pointless, even given the *Gemara*'s ruling.

## Rabbah & Rav Story

### Authority to Rabbah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רְשׁוּתָא? כִּי הֲוָה נָחֵית רַבָּה בַּר חָנָה לְבָבֶל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא לְרַבִּי: בֶּן אָחִי יוֹרֵד לְבָבֶל. ״יוֹרֶה?״ ״יוֹרֶה!״, ״יָדִין?״ ״יָדִין!״, ״יַתִּיר בְּכוֹרוֹת?״ ״יַתִּיר!״.
>
> **Translation:** § **What** is the specific nature of this **permission?** The Gemara relates: **When Rabba bar Ḥana descended to Babylonia,** his uncle **Rabbi Ḥiyya said to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **My brother's son is descending to Babylonia. May he teach** people and issue rulings with regard to what is prohibited and what is permitted? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi said to him: **He may teach.** Rabbi Ḥiyya then asked: **May he** also **adjudicate** cases of monetary law, and be absolved from payment if he errs? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi said to him: **He may adjudicate.** Rabbi Ḥiyya continued: **May he declare a firstborn** animal **permitted?** The male firstborn of a kosher animal may not be eaten, as it is supposed to be offered in the Temple. But if it acquires a permanent blemish it is unfit for an offering, and it may be eaten. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi said to him: **He may declare** such an animal **permitted.**

The *Gemara* now presents the background story that illuminates how Rabbah bar Chana received his authorization, and in doing so introduces terminology that has become the foundation of all subsequent rabbinic licensing. When Rabbah bar Chana was preparing to go down to Bavel — note the traditional usage of *yored*, descending, when one travels from Eretz Yisrael — Rabbi Chiya went to Rebbe and said: *ben achi yored le-Bavel*, my nephew, my brother's son, is going to Bavel.

He then asked three questions and received three answers. *Yoreh?* — does he have the authority to rule on questions of *issur ve-hetter*, permitted and forbidden? *Yoreh* — yes. *Yadin?* — does he have the authority to adjudicate *dinei mamonos*? *Yadin* — yes. *Yattir bechorot?* — does he have the authority to examine blemishes on firstborn animals and rule on whether they are permanent blemishes that would permit the animal to be eaten rather than offered as a sacrifice? *Yattir bechorot* — yes, he has that authority as well.

### Rav Denied Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲוָה נָחֵית רַב לְבָבֶל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא לְרַבִּי: בֶּן אֲחוֹתִי יוֹרֵד לְבָבֶל. ״יוֹרֶה?״ ״יוֹרֶה!״, ״יָדִין?״ ״יָדִין!״, ״יַתִּיר בְּכוֹרוֹת?״ ״אַל יַתִּיר!״.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **when Rav,** who was also Rabbi Ḥiyya's nephew, **descended to Babylonia, Rabbi Ḥiyya said to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **My sister's son is descending to Babylonia. May he teach** people and issue rulings with regard to what is prohibited and what is permitted? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi said to him: **He may teach.** Rabbi Ḥiyya then asked: **May he** also **adjudicate** cases of monetary law, and be absolved from payment if he errs? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi responded: **He may adjudicate.** Rabbi Ḥiyya continued: **May he declare a firstborn** animal **permitted?** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi said to him: **He may not declare** such an animal **permitted.**

When Rav was similarly preparing to go to Bavel — also a nephew of Rabbi Chiya — Rabbi Chiya went to Rebbe and asked the same sequence of questions: *ben achoti yored le-Bavel*, my sister's son is going to Bavel. *Yoreh? Yoreh. Yadin? Yadin. Yattir bechorot? Al yattir* — this authority he was not granted.

### Family Relations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא לְמָר דְּקָא קָרֵי ״בֶּן אָחִי״, וּמַאי שְׁנָא לְמָר דְּקָא קָרֵי ״בֶּן אֲחוֹתִי״? וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה, וְהָאָמַר מָר: אַיְּבוּ וְחַנָּה וְשֵׁילָא וּמָרְתָא וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא כּוּלְּהוּ בְּנֵי אַבָּא בַּר אַחָא כַּרְסַלָּא מִכַּפְרִי הֲווֹ? רַב בַּר אֲחוּהּ דַּהֲוָה בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ, רַבָּה בַּר חָנָה בַּר אֲחוּהּ דְּלָאו בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This incident raises several questions, which the Gemara asks in sequence. **What is different concerning this Sage,** Rabba bar Ḥana, **that** Rabbi Ḥiyya **called him: My brother's son, and what is different concerning that Sage,** Rav, **that** Rabbi Ḥiyya **called him: My sister's son? And if you would say that this was** the **situation:** Rabba bar Ḥana was his brother's son and Rav was his sister's son, **but doesn't the Master say: Aivu,** Rav's father, **and Ḥana,** the father of Rabba bar Ḥana, **and Sheila, and Marta, and Rabbi Ḥiyya, were all sons of Abba bar Aḥa Karsala from Kafrei?** Consequently, Rav would also be Rabbi Ḥiyya's brother's son. The Gemara answers: **Rav** was **his brother's son who was** also **his sister's son,** as Rabbi Ḥiyya's half-brother married Rabbi Ḥiyya's half-sister; while **Rabba bar Ḥana was his brother's son who was not his sister's son.** Therefore, he referred to Rav in a manner that emphasized the additional relationship.

Before examining why Rav was denied this particular authorization, the *Gemara* pauses to address an apparent inconsistency. Rabbi Chiya referred to Rabbah bar Chana as his brother's son, but to Rav as his sister's son. Were they not both his nephews through brothers? The *Gemara* confirms from a tradition that Rabbi Chiya, along with Aivo, Chana, Shila, and Marta, were all sons of Abba bar Acha Carsela of Kafri — all siblings. Chana was the father of Rabbah bar Chana, making him unambiguously a brother's son. Aivo was Rav's father, which should make Rav also a brother's son. Why then did Rabbi Chiya call Rav his sister's son?

The *Gemara* offers two answers. The first is that Rav was both his brother's son and his sister's son — that is, his parents were a half-brother and half-sister of Rabbi Chiya who were not themselves related to each other, so that Rav stood in both relationships simultaneously. Rabbah bar Chana was only a brother's son. The second answer is that Rabbi Chiya called Rav his sister's son as an expression of Rav's exceptional wisdom — *al shem chochmato* — based on the verse *emor la-chochmah achoti at*, "say to wisdom: you are my sister." By calling him the son of his sister, Rabbi Chiya was poetically indicating that Rav was a man of surpassing *chochmah*.

### Rav's Expertise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַתִּיר בְּכוֹרוֹת? אַל יַתִּיר. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא חַכִּים, הָא קָא אָמְרִינַן דְּחַכִּים טוּבָא! אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא בְּקִיעַ בְּמוּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara had related that Rabbi Ḥiyya asked Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: May Rav **declare a firstborn** animal **permitted,** and that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi had responded: **He may not declare** such an animal **permitted.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that he denied him this permission? **If we say** that it was **because** Rav **was not** sufficiently **wise** and learned, **but** that is difficult, as **we** already **said that he was exceedingly wise. Rather,** it must be that it was **because,** although he was quite knowledgeable about the *halakha*, **he was not an expert with regard to blemishes,** meaning that he lacked the practical expertise to apply the *halakha* to actual cases.

This second answer, however, immediately generates a problem. If Rav was the greater scholar, why was he denied the authorization to rule on blemishes of firstborn animals? The *Gemara* considers and rejects two obvious explanations. Perhaps he was not so learned? — but we just established he was the greater intellect. Perhaps he lacked practical familiarity with blemishes? — but the *Gemara* cites Rav's own statement: *shmonah asar chodashim gadalti etzel ro'eh behemah le-ida eizeh mum kavua v'eizeh mum over* — he spent eighteen months apprenticed to shepherds specifically to learn the difference between a permanent blemish and a transient one. He possessed both the theoretical knowledge and the practical experience.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב: שְׁמוֹנָה עָשָׂר חֳדָשִׁים גָּדַלְתִּי אֵצֶל רוֹעֵה בְהֵמָה, לֵידַע אֵיזֶה מוּם קָבוּעַ וְאֵיזֶה מוּם עוֹבֵר? אֶלָּא, לְחַלֵּק לוֹ כָּבוֹד לְרַבָּה בַּר חָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this answer. **But didn't Rav say: I apprenticed with a shepherd** for **eighteen months** in order to be able **to know which** blemish is **a permanent blemish, and which** is **a temporary blemish?** Evidently, he had a high level of practical expertise in this matter. The Gemara explains: **Rather,** it was in order **to bestow honor upon Rabba bar Ḥana.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi wanted to ensure that Rabba bar Ḥana would be treated with respect, so he made sure that there was an area of *halakha* with regard to which the people would not be able to consult with Rav and would need to consult with Rabba bar Ḥana instead.

The *Gemara* offers two answers. The first is *lachaloik lo kavod le-Rabba bar Chana* — it was done to ensure that Rabbah bar Chana would receive sufficient honor. Rav's stature was already so great that he needed no additional credential; he would command respect regardless. But Rabbah bar Chana needed something distinguishing, and by granting him an authorization withheld even from Rav, Rabbi Chiya ensured that the community would accord Rabbah bar Chana the deference he required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִשּׁוּם הָא גּוּפֵיהּ, דְּרַב בְּקִיעַ בְּמוּמֵי טְפֵי, וְשָׁרֵי מוּמֵי דְּלָא יָדְעִי אִינָשֵׁי. וְאָמְרִי: ״כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא שְׁרָא רַב״, וְאָתוּ לְמִשְׁרֵי מוּם עוֹבֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **It is due to this** fact **itself: Since Rav was a great expert with regard to blemishes, he** would **permit blemishes** that average **people do not know about. And** as a result, **they would** erroneously **say** with regard to a different blemish: In **a case like this Rav declared** the animal **permitted, and** in this way **they would come to** erroneously **permit** an animal with **a temporary blemish,** believing it to be identical to the blemish that Rav had declared permitted. Due to this concern, Rav was denied the authority to declare firstborn animals permitted on the basis of a blemish.

The second answer is more striking: *mishum ha gufa* — precisely because of Rav's expertise, he was not given the authorization. *De-Rav baki be-mumin tefei, de-sharei mumin d'lo yad'ei inshi* — Rav was so expert in blemishes that he would sometimes permit blemishes that to ordinary observers did not appear to be blemishes at all. *V'atu lemishrei mum over* — people watching would not understand the fine distinctions he was drawing, and would take his lenient rulings as precedents for cases involving transient blemishes, which are not grounds for permitting the animal. Sometimes, the *Gemara* implies, one can be too expert for the public good.

## Yoreh Yoreh

### Licensing Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹרֶה, יוֹרֶה. אִי גְּמִיר, רְשׁוּתָא לְמָה לִי לְמִישְׁקַל? מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the permission granted to Rabba bar Ḥana and Rav, the Gemara had related that Rabbi Ḥiyya asked Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **May he teach** people and issue rulings concerning what is prohibited and what is permitted? And Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi responded: **He may teach.** The Gemara asks: **If he had studied** and mastered the relevant *halakhot*, **why do I** need him **to receive permission?** The need for formal authority is understandable when it comes to serving on a court to judge cases of monetary law, but any knowledgeable person should be qualified to answer questions about ritual law. The Gemara explains: The need for such permission is **due to an incident that took place.**

Having introduced the phrase *yoreh yoreh* in the context of Rabbah bar Chana's authorization, the *Gemara* now examines the underlying rationale for requiring licensing specifically for *hora'ah* — ruling on questions of permitted and forbidden. This is counterintuitive. The case for licensing in *dinei mamonos* is clear: either to make rulings compellable, or to exempt the judge from liability. But *hora'ah* involves neither. If someone brings a chicken to a rabbi and asks whether it is *kosher*, no one is being compelled, and there is no framework of liability for an incorrect ruling. Why then should a *talmid chacham* who knows the *halacha* need any license at all?

### Dough Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: פַּעַם אַחַת הָלַךְ רַבִּי לְמָקוֹם אֶחָד, וְרָאָה בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁמְּגַבְּלִין עִיסּוֹתֵיהֶם בְּטוּמְאָה. אָמַר לָהֶם: מִפְּנֵי מָה אַתֶּם מְגַבְּלִין עִיסּוֹתֵיכֶם בְּטוּמְאָה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד בָּא לְכָאן וְהוֹרָה לָנוּ: מֵי בְצָעִים אֵין מַכְשִׁירִין. וְהוּא מֵי בֵיצִים דְּרַשׁ לְהוּ, וְאִינְהוּ סְבוּר מֵי בְצָעִים קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **once went to a certain place, and he saw people** there **kneading dough** while they were **in** a state of ritual **impurity,** and they believed that nevertheless, the dough remained ritually pure. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to them: For what** reason **are you kneading your dough in** a state of ritual **impurity? They said to him: A certain Torah scholar came here and taught us** that **water** from **swamps [*mei betza'im*] does not render** food **susceptible** to contract ritual impurity. Therefore, they would take water from swamps and knead dough with it, in the mistaken belief that such dough would not be susceptible to ritual impurity. **But** in reality, what **he taught them** was that **water of eggs [*mei beitzim*],** i.e., the albumin of eggs, does not render food susceptible to impurity, as it is not considered water. **But they thought he said: Water** from **swamps.**

The *Gemara* answers: *mishum maaseh shehayah* — because of an incident that occurred. A *baraita* relates that Rebbe once visited a certain place and found the people kneading their dough in a state of *tumah* without apparent concern. He asked them why. They explained that a certain scholar had once come and ruled for them: *mei bitzaim ein machshirin* — water from eggs does not render food susceptible to *tumah*. Since they were using water that did not constitute *mayim* in the relevant sense, they had concluded they could knead freely without concern for *tumah*.

### Misinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטָעוּ נָמֵי בְּהָא: מֵי קֵרַמְיוֹן וּמֵי פֵיגָה פְּסוּלִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן מֵי בְצָעִים. וְאִינְהוּ סְבוּר: מִדִּלְגַבֵּי חַטָּאת פְּסִילִי – אַכְשׁוֹרֵי נָמֵי לָא מַכְשְׁרִי. וְלָא הִיא: הָתָם, לְעִנְיַן חַטָּאת, בָּעֵינַן מַיִם חַיִּים; הָכָא, אַכְשׁוֹרֵי כָּל דְּהוּ מַכְשְׁרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the residents of that same place **erred also with regard to this:** It was taught in a mishna (*Para* 8:10): **The waters of** the **Keramiyyon** River **and the waters of** the **Piga** River **are not fit** for mixing with ashes of the red heifer to use as water of purification, **since they are water** from **swamps. And they** erroneously **thought: Since** this water **is not fit** for use as **water of purification,** this means it is not considered water, and therefore **it also does not render** food **susceptible** to contracting impurity. **But it is not so,** as **there, with regard to** water of **purification, we need: "Running water"** (see Numbers 19:17), and water from swamps is not running water. But **here,** with regard to **rendering** food **susceptible** to impurity, **any** water **renders** food **susceptible.**

The *Gemara* explains what had actually happened. The scholar had told them *mei beitzim darash lehu* — he had ruled about egg water, meaning that dough kneaded with eggs rather than water would not be susceptible. But the community heard *mei bitzaim* — water from marshes or ditches — and assumed that such water did not render food susceptible. Adding to the confusion, a *tanna* had indeed taught that the waters of certain rivers were invalid for use in the *parah adumah* purification process because they were like ditch water. The community reasoned: since this water is disqualified even for the *parah adumah* process, surely it cannot function as water in any halachic sense — and therefore *achshurei nami lo machshiri*, it cannot render food susceptible to *tumah* either. This reasoning is incorrect: the *parah adumah* requires *mayim chayim*, genuinely fresh water, whereas *hechsher tumah* — rendering food susceptible — is triggered by virtually any liquid. The two categories are distinct.

The core point, however, is not this particular *halachic* detail but what the incident reveals about the practical responsibilities of *hora'ah*. It is not sufficient to know the law; one must know how to communicate a ruling to a community, anticipate how it will be received, and bear responsibility for how it will be applied. The scholar in this case was apparently not irresponsible in terms of his knowledge, but his ruling was misunderstood in a way that led to widespread violation, and he had no mechanism of accountability.

### Licensing Decree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא, בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה גָּזְרוּ: תַּלְמִיד אַל יוֹרֶה אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נוֹטֵל רְשׁוּת מֵרַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught: At that time,** when Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi discovered the consequences resulting from a Torah scholar who was not precise with his terminology, the Sages **decreed: A Torah scholar may not teach** *halakha* **unless he receives permission from his teacher** to do so. The teacher should not grant him this permission if he does not know how to express himself in a clear manner.

As a result of this incident, a decree was issued: *talmid al yoreh ela im kein notal reshusa mei-rabbah* — a student may not issue rulings unless he has obtained permission from his teacher. This represents the origin of professional licensing in the rabbinic context. Unlike the *rishus* from the *Reish Galusa* discussed earlier — which was about coercive authority in *dinei mamonos* or about exemption from liability — this decree is about communal responsibility. The concern is that people should not accept a rabbi as their *posek* unless someone within the professional community has vouched for him. Just as one should not accept a physician based solely on a persuasive manner, one requires confirmation from those already within the field that the candidate has been properly trained and evaluated.

### Community Trust

This is the origin of *yoreh yoreh* as a phrase of rabbinic licensing. In the absence of *smicha* in its classical sense — the chain of ordination extending back to Moshe Rabbeinu — the phrase *yoreh yoreh* became the formulation by which a rabbi's teacher communicated to the community that this individual was authorized, responsible, and trustworthy in rendering *hora'ah*. It is not, strictly speaking, a *halachic* requirement in the sense of *d'oraisa* or even a formal rabbinic enactment in the mold of the three-judge requirement. It is a communal institution: the profession certifies one of its own, and the community is thereby assured that it can rely on his rulings.

The question of *yadin yadin* — whether the authorization for *dinei mamonos* similarly served a purpose, given that Eretz Yisrael authorization did not help in Bavel — remains open, and will be taken up in the continuation of the *sugya*.
