# Pesachim 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Raw/Roasted & Timing

### Na vs. Tzali Esh

The daf begins with a review of the previous discussion concerning the Korban Pesach. The Torah prohibits eating the *korban pesach* in a manner described as *na uvashel mevushal* — that is, raw, cooked in liquid, or in any form other than *tzali esh*, roasted over fire. The Gemara had previously interpreted *na* not as completely raw meat, but rather as undercooked or rare — a distintion from *tzali esh* proper. It is worth noting that a check of a concordance reveals this is the only instance in all of Tanakh where the word *na* appears in the sense of a manner of cooking. No other verse uses it in this context, though the concordance does not dispute the Gemara's interpretation. Beyond the prohibition of *na*, the question arises whether eating the *korban pesach* in any manner other than *tzali esh* — even if it is neither rare nor boiled — constitutes the violation of a positive commandment, a negative prohibition, or one that would incur lashes. These are the issues examined in the preceding discussion.

### Eating Before Erev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָכַל כְּזַיִת נָא מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם — פָּטוּר; כְּזַיִת נָא מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה — חַיָּיב. אָכַל כְּזַיִת צָלִי מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם — לֹא פָּסַל עַצְמוֹ מִבְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה; כְּזַיִת צָלִי מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה — פָּסַל עַצְמוֹ מִבְּנֵי הַחֲבוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If **one ate a partially roasted olive-bulk** of the Paschal lamb **while it was still day** on the fourteenth of Nisan, **he is exempt.** If he ate **a partially roasted olive-bulk after dark, he is liable** to receive lashes. If **he ate a roasted olive-bulk** of the Paschal lamb **while it was still day** on the fourteenth of Nisan, **he has not disqualified himself from** his **group.** Once he has started eating the offering, he may not leave the group he joined that arranged to partake together of a single Paschal lamb. Nevertheless, this case is different, as he began eating before the obligation to eat the Paschal lamb went into effect, and therefore he has not disqualified himself from his group by eating the Paschal lamb of another group. However, if he ate **a roasted olive-bulk after dark,** when he is obligated to eat the Paschal lamb, **he disqualifies himself from the group** he had joined.

The final matter carried over from the previous amud concerns one who ate the *korban pesach*, properly roasted as *tzali esh*, on *Erev Pesach* itself rather than at the designated time that evening. The Gemara states that such a person has transgressed. The difficulty is identifying the nature of that transgression. There is a positive commandment — *ve-achalu et habasar balaila hazeh*, they shall eat the meat on this night — which implies eating it that night and no earlier. But nowhere is there an explicit negative prohibition against eating it before that time. Negating a positive commandment is itself a violation, but it does not rise to the level of a negative prohibition. The question with which the Gemara closed on 41b is therefore: where does the negative prohibition come from?

## Rabbi Yehuda Drasha

### Blemished Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה שָׂרוּעַ וְקָלוּט נְדָבָה תַּעֲשֶׂה אֹתוֹ״. אוֹתוֹ אַתָּה מַתְפִּיס לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת, וְאִי אַתָּה מַתְפִּיס תְּמִימִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: כׇּל הַמַּתְפִּיס תְּמִימִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת עוֹבֵר בַּעֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: "Either **a bull or a lamb that has anything too long or too short, you may offer it as a free-will offering** [to the Temple treasury]; but for a vow [as a sacrifice] it shall not be accepted" (Leviticus 22:23). From here we learn that **it,** i.e., a blemished animal, **you** may **consecrate for maintaining the Temple, but you** may **not consecrate unblemished** animals **for maintaining the Temple.** In other words, any animal fit to be sacrificed as an offering may not be consecrated for maintaining the Temple but only as an offering. **From here** the Sages **stated: Whoever consecrates unblemished** animals **for maintaining the Temple transgresses a positive mitzva.**

The Gemara answers by citing *Rav Chisda*: the view that eating the *korban pesach* before its designated time involves a negative prohibition follows the opinion of *Rabbi Yehuda*. A *baraita* is brought in support. The verse states regarding a blemished ox or sheep — one that is *sarua* or *kalut*, meaning disfigured in a limb or with improperly split hooves — that it may be brought as a *nedavah* but not as a *neder*. The rabbinic interpretation of this verse is that such a blemished animal may be consecrated for *bedek habayit*, donated to the Temple treasury for its monetary value, but may not be brought as a *korban* in the full sense.

The *baraita* then draws out the parallel implication: just as a blemished animal may be donated for its proceeds but not used as a *korban*, so too an unblemished animal — *temimim* — may not be donated for its monetary value to *bedek habayit*. The reasoning is that an unblemished animal consecrated to the Temple ought to be used as a *korban* directly, since that constitutes a higher elevation of the animal itself, rather than merely selling it and using the proceeds. The verse *nedavah ta'aseh oso* — you shall make of it a donation — thus implies that this type of animal, a blemished one, may be given as a donation, but an unblemished one may not. One who consecrates an unblemished animal for the temple treasury accordingly violates a positive commandment, *over be-aseh*: the positive commandment that blemished animals are what are donated implies that unblemished ones are not.

### Vayedaber Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּעֲשֵׂה, בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַיְדַבֵּר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה לֵּאמֹר״, לִימֵּד עַל כׇּל הַפָּרָשָׁה כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי לְבַר קַפָּרָא: מַאי מַשְׁמַע? אָמַר לוֹ: דִּכְתִיב ״לֵאמֹר״ — לֹא נֶאֱמַר בַּדְּבָרִים. בֵּי רַב אָמְרִי: ״לֵאמֹר״ — לָאו אֱמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** From here **I have only** derived that he violates **a positive mitzva; from where do I** derive that he also transgresses **a prohibition? The verse states** at the beginning of that passage: **"And the Lord spoke to Moses saying"** (Leviticus 22:17). This introductory statement **teaches with regard to the entire portion that a prohibition applies to it.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** The *baraita* adds that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said to Bar Kappara: From where may it be inferred** that this is the case? How does Rabbi Yehuda derive his statement that a prohibition applies to the entire portion from the phrase "And the Lord spoke to Moses saying"? **He said to him: As it is written: "Saying [*leimor*]."** Rabbi Yehuda expounds this term as though it read: Say no [*lo emor*]. In other words, the word **no,** an expression of prohibition, **is stated with regard to** the subsequent **matters,** which means that these mitzvot are categorized as prohibitions. In **the school** of **Rav they say** a slightly different explanation: The term: Saying, can be expounded as if it were written ***lav emor*,** meaning: **Say a prohibition.** In other words, the verse indicates that Moses was instructed to inform the Jewish people of a prohibition. This teaches that any mitzva introduced by the word *leimor* should be treated as a prohibition.

Having established that one who consecrates an unblemished animal for *bedek habayit* transgresses a positive commandment, the *baraita* asks: from where do we know that a negative prohibition is also incurred? The verse introducing this entire passage begins *vayedaber Hashem el Moshe lemor* — God spoke to Moses saying. *Rabbi Yehuda* derives from this introduction that everything stated in this *parsha* carries the force of a negative prohibition as well.

When *Rabbi* pressed *Bar Kappara* — *mai mashma*, what exactly does that phrase teach? — *Bar Kappara* replied that the word *lemor* itself encodes the derivation. *Lemor* is read as *lo ne'emar*, a prohibition is stated. *Rabbi bei Rav* articulate this as *lemor lav emor* — the word *lemor* itself announces that a prohibition is being declared. Thus, according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, the introductory formula *vayedaber Hashem el Moshe lemor* converts the implications of the positive commandment in that *parsha* into a full negative prohibition as well. *Tosafot* clarifies that this does not apply to absolutely every implication, but specifically to things that are forbidden by virtue of what the positive commandment implies.

### Universal Application

The Gemara assumes that *Rabbi Yehuda*'s principle can be applied more broadly to explain why eating the *korban pesach* before its time constitutes a negative prohibition — the *parsha* of the *korban pesach* similarly opens with *vayedaber Hashem el Moshe lemor*. However, this universalization gives pause. If one takes the *drasha* globally, then any four-cornered garment worn without *tzitzit* would not merely negate a positive commandment but would constitute a full negative prohibition as well — since the passage concerning *tzitzit* also contains *lemor*. The implications, once broadly applied, become quite sweeping. The Gemara appears to assume the principle operates generally in order to answer its question about the *korban pesach*, yet limiting its application in a principled way remains a challenge. One possible constraint is that the principle applies only where the introductory formula includes *vayedaber* specifically, since *vayedaber* is *lashon kasha*, a term connoting severity, but this too remains uncertain.

## Baker's Water

### Water Disposal Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵי תַשְׁמִישׁוֹ שֶׁל נַחְתּוֹם וְכוּ׳. תָּנֵי חֲדָא: שׁוֹפְכִין בִּמְקוֹם מִדְרוֹן, וְאֵין שׁוֹפְכִין בִּמְקוֹם הָאֶישְׁבּוֹרֶן. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: שׁוֹפְכִין בִּמְקוֹם הָאֶישְׁבּוֹרֶן! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — דִּנְפִישִׁי, דִּקְווּ. הָא — דְּלָא נְפִישִׁי, דְּלָא קְווּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **Water that has been used by a baker** for cooling his hands or washing dishes must be poured out, as it contains a small, undefined quantity of leavened dough. **It was taught** in **one** *baraita*: **One** may **pour out** this water **in a place with an incline, and he** may **not pour** it **out in a** level **place where** the water **collects. And it was taught** in **another** *baraita*: **One** may even **pour out** this water **in a** level **place where** the water **collects.** The Gemara resolves this contradiction: This is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which states that it is prohibited to pour out this water in a level place, is referring **to a large** amount of water **that will collect** in one place. Since there is a large amount of water, the flour in the water will not be absorbed into the ground but will leaven. Conversely, **that** *baraita*, which states that it is permitted to pour out the water in a level place, is referring to a situation **where** there was **not a large amount** of water, so that it **will not collect.** Instead, this water will be absorbed into the ground before the dough leavens.

The final *mishnah* of the second *perek* turns to a different concern: the water that a baker uses to wash the sticky dough from his hands. This water, which contains dough residue, will itself become *chametz* and must therefore be disposed of. A conflict arises between two *baraitot*. One teaches that the water must be spilled on an incline — *bemakom midron* — so that it flows downward and is absorbed into the ground. Spilling it on flat, level ground — *bemakom ha'ishborn* — is prohibited, since it will pool and remain *chametz* in place. The other *baraita*, however, permits spilling it on level ground. The Gemara resolves the contradiction: when there is a large quantity of water that will pool and not be quickly absorbed, it must be spilled on an incline; when there is only a small amount, it will be absorbed readily even on level ground.

### Mayim Shelannu Pun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אִשָּׁה לֹא תָּלוּשׁ אֶלָּא בְּמַיִם שֶׁלָּנוּ. דַּרְשַׁהּ רַב מַתְנָה בְּפַפּוֹנְיָא. לְמָחָר אַיְיתוֹ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא חַצְבַיְיהוּ וַאֲתוֹ לְגַבֵּיהּ, וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הַב לַן מַיָּא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא בְּמַיָּא דְּבִיתוּ אֲמַרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: A woman** may **knead** *matza* dough **only with water that rested,** i.e., water that was left indoors overnight to cool. If water is added to dough immediately after it was drawn, when it is still lukewarm, the dough will leaven at a faster rate. The Gemara relates: **Rav Mattana taught** this *halakha* **in Paphunya. On the next day,** the eve of Passover, **everyone brought their jugs to him and said to him: Give us water.** They misunderstood his expression *mayim shelanu*, water that rested, as the near homonym *mayim shelanu*, our water, i.e., water that belongs to the Sage, and they therefore came to take water from his house. **He said to them: I say** and meant: **Water that rested [*devitu*]** in the house overnight.

*Rav Yehuda* rules that a woman may not knead *matzah* dough except with *mayim shelannu* — water that has slept overnight. The concern is that in the month of Nissan, at the onset of spring, well water drawn from underground tends to be warm, and warm water accelerates fermentation and the onset of *chametz*. Allowing the water to rest overnight lets it cool, making it suitable for use the following morning.

*Rav Matna* went and publicly taught this ruling in the town of Pappunya. The very next morning, he opened his door to find the entire town assembled outside his house holding their jugs, calling out *hav lan mayya* — give us your water! The townspeople had understood *mayim shelannu* not as "water that has slept," from the root *linah*, but as *our* water — water belonging to the rabbi who had taught them. *Rav Matna* was obliged to clarify: he meant water that had rested overnight, not water from his household. This stands as one of the more memorable puns in the Gemara, the Hebrew *shelannu* carrying both meanings simultaneously.

## Rava's Requirements

### Water Heat Concerns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: אִשָּׁה לֹא תָּלוּשׁ בַּחַמָּה, וְלֹא בְּחַמֵּי חַמָּה, וְלֹא בַּמַּיִם הַגְּרוּפִין מִן הַמּוּלְיָיר, וְלֹא תַּגְבִּיהַּ יָדָהּ מִן הַתַּנּוּר עַד שֶׁתִּגְמוֹר אֶת כׇּל הַפַּת. וְצָרִיךְ שְׁנֵי כֵלִים — אֶחָד שֶׁמְּקַטֶּפֶת בּוֹ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁמְּצַנֶּנֶת בּוֹ אֶת יָדֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: A woman** may **not knead** dough for *matza* **in the sun, nor with** water that has been **heated by the sun, nor with water collected [*hagerufin*] in an urn heated** by coals **[*mulyar*] And** in addition, **she** may **not remove her hand from the oven,** i.e., interrupt her baking, **until she finishes** forming **all the loaves** from the dough, so that it should not become leavened in the interim. **And** she **requires two vessels, one in which she mixes** the water into the dough **and one in which she cools her hands** so that the heat from her hands does not cause the dough to leaven.

*Rava* extends the concern about warm water into a set of stringent requirements for women kneading dough on Pesach. She may not knead dough out in direct sunlight, *bachama*, since the sun will heat the water in the vessel she is using. She may not use water already warmed by the sun, *bechamei chama*. She may not use water drawn from a *mulyar* — a metal cooking pot — since even after being removed from the fire, the metal retains heat and the water sitting at its bottom will remain warm. All of these restrictions flow from the same underlying principle: warm water speeds the fermentation process and poses a heightened risk of *chametz*. Furthermore, she may not lift her hand from work at the oven until all the dough has been formed and baked — *ad shetigmor et kol hapat* — since continuous working of the dough prevents it from having the opportunity to become *chametz*.

### Two Vessels

In addition to the above requirements, *Rava* rules that a woman kneading dough requires two separate vessels. One vessel is used for actually working the dough — for rolling and slapping it in the process of kneading. The second vessel is for cooling her hands between portions. The concern here is not merely cleanliness or hygiene: as her hands are actively pounding and working the dough, they become warm. Warm hands in contact with the dough create the same risk as warm water — accelerating fermentation. Therefore, the woman must periodically cool her hands by dipping them into the second vessel, ensuring that the heat of her hands does not contribute to the onset of *chametz*.

### Warm Water B'Diavad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: עָבְרָה וְלָשָׁה, מַהוּ? מָר זוּטְרָא אָמַר: מוּתָּר, רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אָסוּר. אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין לוֹתְתִין הַשְּׂעוֹרִין בַּפֶּסַח. וְאִם לָתַת, נִתְבַּקְּעוּ — אֲסוּרִים, לֹא נִתְבַּקְּעוּ — מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If **she transgressed and kneaded** the dough with warm water, **what is** the *halakha*? **Mar Zutra said: It is permitted** after the fact. **Rav Ashi said: It is forbidden.** **Mar Zutra said: From where do I say** my opinion on this issue? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **One** may **not soak barley on Passover, and if one soaked** barley and it **split,** the barley **is forbidden.** If it did **not split,** the barley **is permitted.** This case indicates that even if one violates the principles established by the Sages with regard to adding water to flour on Passover, the product is forbidden only after the fact if it actually leavened.

A question is then raised: what if a woman transgressed and kneaded with warm water — what is the law *b'diavad*, after the fact? *Mar Zutra* rules that the dough is permitted. His reasoning draws on a *baraita* cited earlier in the tractate: one does not remove bran from barley on Pesach using water, but if one did, the status of the result depends on observation — if the kernels cracked, the grain is forbidden as *chametz*; if not, it is permitted. This demonstrates that even where the initial act was prohibited, the resulting product may be evaluated by direct observation to determine whether *chametz* has actually formed. The same principle should apply here: if observation shows the dough has not become *chametz*, it should be permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אַטּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ חֲדָא מְחִיתָא מְחִיתִינְהוּ?! הֵיכָא דְּאִיתְּמַר — אִיתְּמַר, וְהֵיכָא דְּלָא אִיתְּמַר — לָא אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ashi said** in response: **Is that to say** that **all of them are woven in the same** act of **weaving?** In other words, is the *halakha* identical in all cases? **Where it was stated** that the Sages did not punish the violator by rendering his food prohibited, **it was stated; and where it was not stated** that they refrained from punishing the violator, **it was not stated.** It is therefore possible that the Sages rendered dough kneaded with warm water forbidden, to punish the woman who prepared it in this manner.

*Rav Ashi* disagrees. He rejects *Mar Zutra*'s analogy on the grounds that a ruling stated in one context does not automatically extend to all analogous cases: *heicha d'itmar itmar, heicha d'lo itmar lo itmar* — where it was said, it was said; where it was not said, it was not said. In the case of the barley, leniency *b'diavad* was explicitly stated; here it was not.

*Rashi*'s understanding of *Rav Ashi*'s position is particularly instructive. *Rashi* maintains that observation genuinely can determine whether dough has become *chametz* — this is not in doubt. Indeed, the Gemara elsewhere permits purchasing dough from non-Jews and evaluating it by inspection. *Rav Ashi*'s stringency is therefore not rooted in skepticism about the reliability of observation. Rather, because the woman disregarded a rabbinic ruling by using warm water, *Rav Ashi* holds that there must be consequences for that disregard — even if the dough is demonstrably not *chametz*. This understanding importantly reinforces the broader Talmudic assumption that physical inspection of dough genuinely can reveal whether *chametz* has formed.

## Third Perek Intro

### Mishnah Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ עוֹבְרִין בַּפֶּסַח: כּוּתָּח הַבַּבְלִי, וְשֵׁכָר הַמָּדִי, וְחוֹמֶץ הָאֲדוֹמִי, וְזֵיתוֹם הַמִּצְרִי, וְזוֹמָן שֶׁל צַבָּעִים, וַעֲמִילָן שֶׁל טַבָּחִים, וְקוֹלָן שֶׁל סוֹפְרִים. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַף תַּכְשִׁיטֵי נָשִׁים. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא מִמִּין דָּגָן — הֲרֵי זֶה עוֹבֵר בַּפֶּסַח. הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בָּאַזְהָרָה, וְאֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: And for possessing **these one transgresses [*overin*]** the prohibitions of: It shall not be seen, and: It shall not be found, **on Passover,** although not all of them are considered food: **Babylonian *kutaḥ*,** a dip with a sharp flavor that contains flour; **Median beer; Edomite vinegar; Egyptian *zitom*,** a type of beer; **dyers' broth [*zoman*]; bakers' well-worked dough; and *kolan of soferim*.** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The same prohibition **also** applies to **women's adornments,** i.e., cosmetics, that contain leaven. **This is the principle:** If one possesses **any** substance **that is** derived **from a type of grain** that became leavened, although it is not actually bread, **one transgresses** the prohibitions of: It shall not be seen, and: It shall not be found, **on Passover. These** substances are included **in the warning,** i.e., the biblical prohibition of possessing leaven, **but there is no element of *karet*** if one eats them.

With the conclusion of the second *perek* — *hadran alach Kol Sha'ah* — we enter the third *perek*, the last of the three chapters devoted primarily to the laws of *chametz* and *matzah* as objects, before the tractate moves to the *korban pesach* and ultimately to the *Seder* night in the tenth *perek*. The *mishnah* opens with the words *v'eilu ovrin b'Pesach* — the following things you transgress regarding on Pesach. The *mishnah* lists: *kutach ha'Bavli*, Babylonian *kutach*, a dairy-based dip containing sour milk and molded bread; *sheichar ha'Madi*, Median beer or mead, which uses barley rather than date honey as other varieties might; *chometz ha'Adomi*, Edomian vinegar, which unlike ordinary vinegar has a grain or barley basis rather than a wine basis; *zeisom ha'Mitzri*, Egyptian *zeisom*, a mixture to be described shortly; *zoman shel tzavaim*, some type of mixture used in the dyeing of leather; *amilan shel tavachim*, a dough used by butchers, placed on pot rims to absorb froth during cooking; and *kolan shel sofrim*, the glue used by scribes. *Rabbi Eliezer* adds women's ornaments — cosmetic preparations containing a flour component.

### Rabbi Eliezer Klal

*Rabbi Eliezer* states the general principle: *kol she'hu mi'min dagan*, anything that has a grain component, *harei ze over b'Pesach* — you transgress on Pesach. The concluding line of the *mishnah* then states: *harei eilu b'azhara v'ein bo mishum karet* — these carry a negative prohibition but not the full *karet* penalty.

Two significant questions arise from this *mishnah*. First, what exactly does *harei ze over b'Pesach* mean in the opening clause? *Rashi* understands it to mean that one transgresses by owning these items — the prohibition of *bal yira'eh u'bal yimatzeh*, the biblical prohibition against possessing *chametz* — and additionally transgresses a *lav* by eating them, though without incurring *karet*. A *ba'al Tosafot* goes further, reading *eilu ovrin b'Pesach* as meaning these items must be *removed* — *ma'avir* — from one's possession. *Rabbeinu Tam* disagrees with both readings. In his view, *bal yira'eh u'bal yimatzeh* applies only to *chametz* for which one would incur *karet* if eaten. These items, being of a lesser category, do not trigger the biblical ownership prohibition at all. According to *Rabbeinu Tam*, the requirement to remove them is at most rabbinic in origin, and the phrase *harei eilu ovrin* refers solely to the prohibition incurred by eating them. This dispute among the *Rishonim* — whether *bal yira'eh u'bal yimatzeh* applies to these lesser forms of *chametz* — is thus embedded in the very reading of the *mishnah*.

## Zeisom & Dyers

### Three Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים נֶאֱמָרִים בְּכוּתָּח הַבַּבְלִי: מְטַמְטֵם אֶת הַלֵּב, וּמְסַמֵּא אֶת הָעֵינַיִם, וּמַכְחִישׁ אֶת הַגּוּף. מְטַמְטֵם אֶת הַלֵּב — מִשּׁוּם נַסְיוּבֵי דַחֲלָבָא. וּמְסַמֵּא אֶת הָעֵינַיִם — מִשּׁוּם מִילְחָא. וּמַכְחִישׁ אֶת הַגּוּף — מִשּׁוּם קוֹמָנִיתָא דְאוּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Since the mishna mentions *kutaḥ*, the Gemara cites a *baraita* where *kutaḥ* is discussed. **The Sages taught** that **three things were said with regard to *kutaḥ*: It blocks the heart, it blinds the eyes, and it weakens the body.** The Gemara explains each statement: **It blocks the heart due to** the **whey.** Whey was added to *kutaḥ* and was considered to be an inferior type of food. **It blinds the eyes due to the salt** in it, which can be dangerous if it enters the eyes. **And it weakens the body due to the mold in the bread,** as one of the ingredients of *kutaḥ* was crumbs from dough that had become leavened to the point that they were nearly spoiled.

Why do these items carry a *lav* but not *karet*? There are essentially three categories of explanation. The first is *ta'aruvet* — mixture. Some of these items contain a *chametz* component but only as a minority ingredient, diluted among other substances. Normal rules of *ta'am* and *rov* might still establish a biblical prohibition in mixtures, but a minority chametz ingredient in a compound may reduce the severity from *karet* to a *lav*. The *kutach*, the beer, the vinegar, and the *zeisom* all appear to fall into this category, each containing grain or barley as only one component among others.

The second category is *chametz nuksha* — quasi-leavened product. This refers to a substance that has undergone some fermentation but not the complete process that constitutes full *chametz*. The precise definition of *chametz nuksha* requires further development as the Gemara proceeds.

The third category is *ra'ui l'kelev* — fit for a dog but not for human consumption. A substance that is not genuinely fit for human eating may still incur a *lav* if eaten but not the full penalty of *karet*, which is reserved for *chametz gamur* that a person would actually eat. The professional items at the end of the *mishnah* — the dyers' paste, butchers' dough, and scribes' glue — seem more naturally to fall into this third category or possibly the second, since they are not themselves foods consumed by people. The butchers' dough, for example, is placed on pot rims to absorb cooking froth, not eaten, and may not have fully fermented; the dyers' paste and scribes' glue are craft materials. The task remaining for the Gemara is to precisely identify which of these three rationales applies to each item.

## Kutach & Wine

### Kutach Effects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים מַרְבִּין הַזֶּבֶל, וְכוֹפְפִין אֶת הַקּוֹמָה, וְנוֹטְלִין אֶחָד מֵחֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת מִמְּאוֹר עֵינָיו שֶׁל אָדָם, אֵלּוּ הֵן: פַּת קִיבָּר, וְשֵׁכָר חָדָשׁ, וְיָרָק חַי. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים מְמַעֲטִין אֶת הַזֶּבֶל, וְזוֹקְפִין אֶת הַקּוֹמָה, וּמְאִירִין אֶת הָעֵינַיִם, אֵלּוּ הֵן: פַּת נְקִיָּיה, בָּשָׂר שָׁמֵן, וְיַיִן יָשָׁן. פַּת נְקִיָּיה — דִּסְמִידָא, בָּשָׂר שָׁמֵן — דִּצְפִירְתָּא דְּלָא אִפְּתַח, יַיִן יָשָׁן — עַתִּיק עַתִּיקֵי. כׇּל מִילֵּי דִּמְעַלֵּי לְהַאי — קָשֵׁה לְהַאי, וּדְקָשֵׁה לְהַאי — מְעַלֵּי לְהַאי, בַּר מִזַּנְגְּבִילָא רַטִּיבָא, וּפִילְפְּלֵי אֲרִיכְתָּא, וּפַת נְקִיָּיה, וּבָשָׂר שָׁמֵן, וְיַיִן יָשָׁן — דִּמְעַלֵּי לְכוּלֵּי גּוּפֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** After mentioning this *baraita*, the Gemara continues to discuss the nutritional effects of other foods. **The Sages taught: Three things increase** one's **waste, lower** one's **stature, and take one five-hundredth of a person's vision** if he eats them regularly. And **they are: Bread** from **coarse** flour, **new beer, and raw vegetables.** Similarly, **the Sages taught** in another *baraita*: **Three things decrease** one's **waste, straighten** one's **stature, and improve** one's **vision,** and **they are: Bread** from **fine** flour, **fatty meat, and aged wine.** is made **from refined flour; fatty meat** refers to meat **from a goat that has not** yet **given birth; and aged wine** refers to wine that has been **aged significantly,** for at least three years. The Gemara states a general principle: **Any** food or medical treatment **that is effective in** healing **this** sickness or this limb **is deleterious for that** one. **And** any food or treatment **that is deleterious for this** one **is effective** in healing **that** one, **except for moist ginger, long peppers, and bread** made of **refined** flour, **and fatty meat, and aged wine, which are effective to heal all** limbs **of the body.**

The Gemara then cites two parallel *baraitot* cataloguing categories of foods that affect the body. The first lists three foods that increase excretion, bend one's posture, and diminish eyesight by one five-hundredth: bran bread (*pat kibar*), new beer (*sheichar chadash*), and raw vegetables (*yerek chai*) — *Rashi* noting that this applies when one consumes them frequently and in large quantities. In direct parallel, three foods are listed that decrease excretion, straighten one's posture, and illuminate the eyes: fine white bread (*pat nekia, samnid*), fatty meat (*basar shamina*) — specifically from a young goat that has never given birth — and aged wine (*yayin yashan*). *Rashi* identifies "old" wine as three years aged, though the basis for that figure is unclear. Finally, the Gemara notes a general principle: most foods that are beneficial to one part of the body are harmful to another, and vice versa. Exceptions to this rule include moist ginger (*mizangavila retivta*), long peppers (*pilpili arichta*), fine white bread, fatty meat, and old wine — all of which benefit the entire body without corresponding harm elsewhere.

### Wine Quality Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁכָר הַמָּדִי. דְּרָמוּ בֵּיהּ מֵי שְׂעָרֵי. וְחוֹמֶץ הָאֲדוֹמִי. דְּשָׁדוּ בֵּיהּ שְׂעָרֵי. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן (בַּר יִצְחָק): בַּתְּחִלָּה כְּשֶׁהָיוּ מְבִיאִין נְסָכִים מִיְּהוּדָה, לֹא הָיָה יֵינָם שֶׁל יְהוּדָה מַחְמִיץ, עַד שֶׁנּוֹתְנִין לְתוֹכָן שְׂעוֹרִין. וְהָיוּ קוֹרִין אוֹתוֹ חוֹמֶץ סְתָם. וְעַכְשָׁיו אֵין יֵינָם שֶׁל אֲדוֹמִיִּים מַחְמִיץ עַד שֶׁנּוֹתְנִין לְתוֹכָן שְׂעוֹרִין, וְקוֹרִין אוֹתוֹ חוֹמֶץ הָאֲדוֹמִי, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִמָּלְאָה הָחֳרָבָה״, אִם מְלֵאָה זוֹ — חֲרֵבָה זוֹ, וְאִם מְלֵאָה זוֹ — חֲרֵבָה זוֹ. רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וּלְאֹם מִלְּאֹם יֶאֱמָץ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its discussion of the details mentioned in the mishna. Why is **Median beer** prohibited during Passover? It is because the Medians **place barley water into it.** **And Edomite vinegar** is prohibited because the Edomites **place barley into it.** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Initially,** when the Temple stood **and they would bring** wine **libations from Judea,** the wine would be blessed and would be preserved without any additives. **The wine of Judea would not turn to vinegar unless they placed barley into it** to achieve this effect. **And they would call** this vinegar to which barley had been added **ordinary vinegar,** since wine would not become vinegar without this additive. **And now,** after the destruction of the Temple, **Edomite wine does not turn to vinegar unless one places barley into it. This is called Edomite vinegar, to fulfill that which is stated** with regard to Tyre, and the same applies to other enemies of the Jewish people: "Because Tyre has said against Jerusalem: Aha, she is broken that was the gate of the peoples; she is turned unto me; **I shall be filled with her that is laid waste"** (Ezekiel 26:2). The Sages expound: **If this one,** Jerusalem, **is full,** then **that one,** her enemy, **is laid waste; and if this** enemy **is full,** then **she,** Jerusalem, **is laid waste.** Therefore, when the Jewish people fall, their enemies can achieve the success that was once attained by the Jews. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This notion can be derived **from here,** where the verse states regarding Esau and Jacob: **"The one people shall be stronger than the other people"** (Genesis 25:23), meaning that when one nation gains power, the other is weakened, because they cannot both be strong at the same time.

The Gemara proceeds to describe the Median beer and Edomian vinegar. The beer — *sheichar ha'Madi* — is identified as having barley water added to it, distinguishing it from varieties made purely from date honey. The Edomian vinegar — *chometz ha'Adomi* — is identified as having barley added to what would otherwise be a wine base.

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* then offers a striking teaching that illuminates why the Edomian vinegar carries this barley additive. He states that originally, when wine libations — *nesachim* — were brought from Judea at the time of the *Beis Hamikdash*, the wine of Judea was of such extraordinary quality that it would never spoil on its own and turn to vinegar. If one wished to produce vinegar from it, one had to introduce barley as a catalyst to initiate the souring process. This vinegar was simply called *chometz stam* — plain vinegar. Now, however, since the destruction of the Temple, the wine of Judea has declined in quality and spoils naturally without any additive. In its place, *Rav Nachman* notes, it is the Edomian wine that is now of this high quality, not turning to vinegar unless barley is added — and that product is therefore called *chometz ha'Adomi*, Edomian vinegar.

Looking back at the Gemara's formulation for the beer and vinegar — *deramu bei mei sa'ari, deshadu bei sa'ari* — they put into it barley water, they added to it barley — it is evident that these were not beverages made entirely of barley, but ones with a barley additive, consistent with *Rav Nachman*'s account. The Edomian vinegar had a wine base to which barley was added to induce souring.

*Rav Nachman* grounds this historical reversal in the verse *imaleh hacharava* — I will fill the desolate places — understood through the *drasha* that when one rises the other falls: *ule'om mile'om ye'ematz*, nation will prevail over nation. The Judean wine was preeminent when the Temple stood; once the *Churban Habayis* came, Judean wine declined and Edomian wine rose. *Rashi* adds that the merit of using Judean wine specifically for *nesachim* was itself what sustained its exceptional quality.

It is worth pausing over the historical identification involved here. When the *mishnah* refers to *Adomi* wine, it most plausibly refers to Idumaea — the region to which Herod's family belonged, whose inhabitants were forcibly converted by the Hasmoneans, and which was geographically in the Negev and southern regions. This is distinct from the biblical *Edom*, descendants of *Esav*, on the other side of the Jordan. The Gemara, however, maps *Adomi* onto *Edom*, which rabbinic tradition in turn identifies with Rome. There are thus two layers of historical identification at work: the general rabbinic mapping of *Edom* to Rome, and the identification of the *mishnah*'s Idumaea with that same *Edom*. Both identifications carry their own complexities.

### Temet & Separations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בִּיהוּדָה, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הַלּוֹקֵחַ חוֹמֶץ מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְעַשֵּׂר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֲזָקָה אֵינוֹ בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַתֶּמֶד. וְעַכְשָׁיו, הַלּוֹקֵחַ חוֹמֶץ מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ — צָרִיךְ לְעַשֵּׂר, שֶׁחֶזְקָתוֹ אֵינוֹ בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַיַּיִן. וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: תֶּמֶד לָאו בַּר עַשּׂוֹרֵי הוּא? וְהָא (תְּנַן): הַמְתַמֵּד וְנָתַן מַיִם בַּמִּדָּה, וּמָצָא כְּדֵי מִדָּתוֹ — פָּטוּר, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב? הָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא נֶחְשְׁדוּ עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ עַל הַתֶּמֶד. אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: נֶחְשְׁדוּ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּדִרְווֹקָא, הָא — בִּדְפוּרְצָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said: In Judea, initially, one who would purchase vinegar from an *am ha'aretz*,** i.e., one who is not scrupulous in matters of ritual purity and tithes and is therefore suspect of not having tithed his fruit properly, would **not need to tithe it due to** the fact that **it can be assumed** that ordinary vinegar **was made only from *temed*,** a liquid produced from grape remnants. After filtering the wine from the stems, seeds, and skins, water was poured over these remnants. The liquid was then drained off and allowed to ferment until it became vinegar. This liquid was called *temed*, and it is not necessary to tithe it. Vinegar was produced in this way because the wine of that time was so strong that it did not turn to vinegar on its own. **But now, one who purchases vinegar from an *am ha'aretz* must tithe it,** as the wine nowadays turns to vinegar quickly, **and the presumption is** that vinegar **comes only from wine.** The Gemara asks: **But does Rabbi Yehuda hold that *temed* is not subject to tithing? Wasn't it taught** in a mishna: With regard to **one who produces *temed* and adds a measured** amount **of water and** afterward **finds a corresponding** amount of liquid to that which **he measured, he is exempt** from tithing this *temed* because it is clear that the grape produce added only flavor and did not add to the volume of the *temed*. And **Rabbi Yehuda obligates** one to tithe the *temed* even in that case. If this is so, how can Rabbi Yehuda permit a person to purchase *temed* from an *am ha'aretz*? According to his opinion in this *baraita*, *temed* must be tithed. The Gemara answers: **This is** what Rabbi Yehuda **is saying:** One is required to tithe *temed*; however, ***amei ha'aretz* are not suspected** of failing to tithe ***temed*.** Because *temed* is so inexpensive, the assumption is that *amei ha'aretz* are not sparing with it and are willing to tithe it. **If you wish, say** instead that even if *amei ha'aretz* **are suspected** of failing to tithe *temed*, **and** this is **not difficult** for the following reason: **This** mishna is referring to a case where the *temed* was produced **with dregs** that contain some amount of wine, and therefore Rabbi Yehuda says that a person is required to tithe it. **That** *baraita* is referring to a case where the *temed* was produced **with grape pits; because** it is assumed that no wine is mixed into the *temed*, Rabbi Yehuda states that one is exempt from tithing it.

The Gemara next cites *Rav Yehuda* teaching that in Judea in former times, one who purchased vinegar from an *am ha'aretz* — an ordinary person of the field, generally suspected of not observing *ma'aser* — did not need to tithe it. The reason: since Judean wine was of such high quality, sellers would never make vinegar from actual wine. They would only make vinegar from *temed* — water in which grape lees had been soaked, producing a weak, grape-flavored liquid — and one need not tithe *temed*. Now, however, since the quality of Judean wine has declined, vinegar may well be made from actual wine, and one who purchases it from an *am ha'aretz* must indeed tithe it.

This raises a difficulty: *Rav Yehuda* himself holds elsewhere — as the *mishnah* records — that *temed* does in fact require the separation of *terumah* and *ma'aser*. How then can he say that in earlier times one need not worry about the vinegar coming from *temed*? The Gemara resolves this in two ways. First, *Rav Yehuda* may hold that even though *temed* technically requires *ma'aser*, it is of such negligible value that even *amei ha'aretz* who ordinarily neglect *ma'aser* would not bother skipping it here — the loss would be too trivial to evade. Second, the question of whether *temed* requires *ma'aser* may itself depend on how it is produced: if the grape lees are merely soaked in water, yielding only flavor with no actual juice, it is exempt; if the pressing is more thorough and actual juice is extracted along with the water, *Rav Yehuda* would require *ma'aser*. These are two distinct modes of preparation, and the rulings differ accordingly. The broader point, however, reinforces the theme that *after the Churban Habayis* the quality of our wine declined.

## Zeisom & Dyers (II)

### Zeisom Composition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֵיתוֹם הַמִּצְרִי וְכוּ׳. מַאי זֵיתוֹם הַמִּצְרִי? תָּנָא רַב יוֹסֵף: תְּלָתָא שְׂעָרֵי, תְּלָתָא קוּרְטְמֵי, וּתְלָתָא מִלְחָא. רַב פָּפָּא מַפֵּיק שְׂעָרֵי וּמְעַיֵּיל חִיטֵּי, וְסִימָנָיךְ סִיסָנֵי. תָּרוּ לְהוּ וְקָלוּ לְהוּ וְטָחֲנִי לְהוּ וְשָׁתוּ לְהוּ מִדִּיבְחָא וְעַד עֲצַרְתָּא. דִּקְמִיט מְרַפֵּי לֵיהּ, וְדִרְפֵי מְקַמֵּיט לֵיהּ, לְחוֹלֶה וּלְאִשָּׁה עוּבָּרָה סַכַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated in the mishna that **Egyptian *zitom*** is considered leavened food. The Gemara asks: **What is Egyptian *zitom*?** **Rav Yosef taught** from a *baraita*: It is **one-third barley, one-third safflower, and one-third salt.** **Rav Pappa removes barley** from the list of ingredients **and includes wheat;** he maintains that Egyptian *zitom* was made with wheat rather than barley. The Gemara comments: **Your mnemonic** to remember which Sage expressed which version is the word ***sisanei*,** meaning a twig basket. *Sisanei* contains the letter *samekh* twice, which can help one remember that Rav Yosef, whose name contains a *samekh*, says that Egyptian *zitom* is made from *se'orim*, barley, a word that contains the letter *sin*, which makes the same sound as *samekh*. The Gemara describes how Egyptian *zitom* is prepared: Those who prepare it **soak** the ingredients together, **and** then they **roast them and grind them** together. **They drink** the mixture **from Passover to *Shavuot*.** This drink **relaxes** the bowels of one who is **constipated, and it constipates** one whose bowel movements are **loose.** However, **it is dangerous for a sick person or a pregnant woman** to drink this mixture.

The Gemara now identifies the composition of *zeisom ha'Mitzri*. *Rav Yosef* teaches that it consists of one-third barley (*sa'ari*), one-third saffron (*kurtami*), and one-third salt (*milcha*). *Rav Pappa* disputes the first ingredient, substituting wheat (*chiti*) for barley. A mnemonic preserves who said which: the double-*sin* sound in *sisni* stands for *Yosef sa'ari* — Yosef's version uses barley. The preparation involves soaking the ingredients, roasting them, grinding them, and then drinking the resulting mixture — presumably after adding water to create a drinkable suspension. This was consumed from Pesach until *Shavuot*, the spring season during which it was relevant. Its principal use was medicinal: it could loosen the bowels for those who were constipated, and conversely constipate those who suffered from loose bowels. For a sick person or a pregnant woman, however, it posed a danger.

### Dyers' & Scribes'

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹמָן שֶׁל צַבָּעִים וְכוּ׳. הָכָא תַּרְגִּימוּ: מַיָּא דְּחִיוָּרֵי דְּצָבְעִי בְּהוּ לִבָּא. וַעֲמִילָן שֶׁל טַבָּחִים וְכוּ׳. פַּת תְּבוּאָה שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיאָה שְׁלִישׁ, שֶׁמַּנִּיחָהּ עַל פִּי קְדֵירָה וְשׁוֹאֶבֶת הַזּוּהֲמָא. וְקוֹלָן שֶׁל סוֹפְרִים וְכוּ׳. הָכָא תַּרְגּוּמָא: פֵּרוּרָא דְאוּשְׁכָּפֵי. רַב שִׁימִי מָחוֹזְנָאָה אָמַר: זֶה טִיפּוּלָן שֶׁל בְּנוֹת עֲשִׁירִים, שֶׁמְּשַׁיְּירוֹת אוֹתוֹ לִבְנוֹת עֲנִיִּים. אִינִי? וְהָא תָּנֵא רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אַרְבָּעָה מִינֵי מְדִינָה, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה מִינֵי אוּמָּנוּת. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ טִיפּוּלָן שֶׁל בְּנוֹת עֲשִׁירִים, מַאי מִינֵי אוּמָּנוּת אִיכָּא? וְאֶלָּא מַאי, פֵּרוּרָא דְאוּשְׁכָּפֵי? אַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ הַאי קוֹלָן שֶׁל סוֹפְרִים? קוֹלָן שֶׁל רַצְעָנִין מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: לְעוֹלָם פֵּרוּרָא דְאוּשְׁכָּפֵי, וּמַאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ קוֹלָן שֶׁל סוֹפְרִים? דְּסוֹפְרִים נָמֵי מְדַבְּקִין בְּהוּ נְיָירוֹתֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that **dyers' broth** is considered leavened. The Gemara explains: **Here,** in Babylonia, **they interpreted** that this is **bran water [*maya deḥivri*] that** people **use to dye leather.** It was further stated in the mishna that **bakers' well-worked dough** is also considered leavened. The Gemara explains the nature of this substance: It is **bread made from grain that was** harvested before it was **one-third** ripe and then made into a loaf. This loaf was placed **on top of a pot to draw out the filth** from the broth. It was further taught in the mishna that the *kolan* of *soferim*, **kolan of soferim,** is considered leavened. The Gemara explains: **Here,** in Babylonia, **they interpreted that** this expression is referring to **shoemakers' glue** that is made from flour. **Rav Shimi from Ḥozna'a said: This is the** depilatory **paste of the daughters of the wealthy, of which they would leave a remnant for the daughters of the poor.** It was the common practice for women to remove hair from different parts of their bodies by applying various pastes, some of which contained flour. The *kolan* of *soferim* mentioned in the mishna was such a substance. It was given this name because wealthy young women would give the paste [*kolan*] to poor young women whose fathers were schoolteachers [*soferim*], so that the poor women could utilize it as well. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach** a mnemonic by noting that the mishna lists **four items** that are used by the ordinary people **of the state,** i.e., *kutaḥ*, beer, vinegar, and *zitom*, **and three items of artisans,** i.e., dyers' broth, kolan of soferim, and bakers' well-worked dough? **And if you say** that kolan of soferim is **the** depilatory **paste of the daughters of the wealthy,** then **what artisanship is there in that?** According to this interpretation, that substance does not belong on the list of artisans' items. The Gemara answers with another question: **Rather what,** will you say that this is referring to **shoemakers' glue?** If so, **why did** the authors of the mishna **call it bookmakers' glue [*kolan shel soferim*]?** It should** have been called **shoemakers' glue [*kolan shel ratzanin*]. Rav Oshaya said: Actually,** one should explain that *kolan* is referring to **shoemakers' glue; and why is it called bookmakers' glue?** The reason is that **bookmakers also use it to attach their pages.** During the time of the mishna, this paste was referred to as bookmakers' glue.

*Zoman shel tzavaim* is interpreted locally in the Gemara as a kind of whitening liquid used in the dyeing or treatment of leather, though its precise composition — beyond the evident grain component — is not specified. *Kolan shel sofrim* is first explained as *perufa de'ushchafi* — a type of adhesive used by cobblers and leatherworkers. A second explanation is offered by *Rav Shimi miChozna*, who identifies it as a skin plaster used by wealthy women, with any leftover donated to the wives and daughters of scribes and *cheder* teachers — hence the name. However, *Rav Chiyya*'s *baraita* distinguishes between the first four items in the *mishnah* as *minei medina* — foodstuffs consumed by people — and the last three as *minei omanut* — items used in professional or craft contexts. If *kolan shel sofrim* were a cosmetic skin plaster used in private households, it would not qualify as a *minei omanut*. This objection rejects *Rav Shimi*'s interpretation. The Gemara then asks: if it is indeed *perufa de'ushchafi*, a craft adhesive used by leatherworkers, why is it called the *kolan* of scribes rather than of leatherworkers? *Rav Oshaya* resolves this: it is indeed the leatherworkers' adhesive, but scribes also use it to glue papers together — hence it came to be known by their name.

### Butchers' Dough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר אַף תַּכְשִׁיטֵי נָשִׁים וְכוּ׳. תַּכְשִׁיטֵי נָשִׁים סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אַף טִיפּוּלֵי נָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated in the mishna: **Rabbi Eliezer says** that **women's adornments are also** prohibited as leavened food. The Gemara asks: **Could it enter your mind** to say **that adornments** made from silver, gold, or woven materials contain leaven? **Rather, say** instead that this means: **Even women's cosmetics** or other items used by women to enhance their skin are prohibited if they contain leavened ingredients.

*Amilan shel tavachim* — the butchers' dough — is identified as bread made from grain that had not yet reached even one-third of its full ripening, *shelo hevi shlish*. This immature grain dough is placed along the rim where the pot and its cover meet, where it absorbs the froth and overflow produced during cooking. Since this dough is not a foodstuff consumed by people, the question of why it incurs a prohibition without *karet* must be understood differently from the food mixtures earlier in the *mishnah*. It is not *chametz b'ta'aruvet* — the grain here is not diluted by other ingredients in the way the *kutach* or *zeisom* are. Rather, the reason it falls short of *chametz gamur* must lie either in the fact that it did not fully ferment — since it is placed on the pot lip and not baked in the normal fashion — or in the fact that it is not fit for human consumption. The underlying rationale for this last category remains to be fully worked out as the Gemara continues.
