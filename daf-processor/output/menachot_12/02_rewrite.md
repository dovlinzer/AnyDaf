# Menachot 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kemitzah & Structure

### Opening & Parallels

Mishna 11 begins on daf 9, and with it we hope to complete the chapter. As has been the case throughout Menachot, this mishna runs remarkably parallel to the first chapters of Zvachim. We have already worked through the issues of *shelo lishma*, the various *psulim*, and a lengthy discussion about the left hand. Indeed, Menachot contains material we did not encounter in Zvachim — including a physical description of how *kemitzah* is performed and difficulties arising from taking too much or too little of the *mincha*.

### Kemitzah Technique

Regarding the physical mechanics of *kemitzah*: the straightforward reading of the *gemara*, supported by Rashi and the Rambam, is that the *kemitzah* is performed with the palm facing downward. Rashi, however, in one interpretation understands it to be performed sideways. One might note that the palm-down approach seems mechanically easier, though each reading has its difficulties.

On the related question of the *levonah*: if one has too much *levonah*, it is *pasul*. The *gemara* discussed the case of having two *kematzim* of *levonah*. Rashi, in one of his two approaches to the *gemara*, holds that anything less than double the amount — say, one extra half-unit — would not constitute "too much." Only when the *levonah* reaches double does a problem arise. This is particularly interesting given that the Torah never specifies a *shiur* for *levonah*; the measure is derived by the *gemara*, which perhaps introduces some room for leniency.

## Pigul: Place vs. Time

### Pigul Intro

Having dealt with *psulim* and *shelo lishma*, we arrive at the subject of *pigul*. The mishna establishes a fundamental distinction between two categories of invalidating thought: *chutz limkomo* — planning to perform some act in the wrong place — and *chutz lizmano* — planning to perform some act at the wrong time. The halachic consequences of these two categories differ sharply.

### Wrong Place Cases

The mishna opens: *hakometz et hamincha le'echol she'areha bachutz* — one takes the *kemitzah* intending to eat the *shirayim* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. A *mincha* is *kodshei kodashim* and may only be eaten by *kohanim* within the *Beit HaMikdash*. Planning to eat it outside is a *machshava* of *chutz limkomo*.

The mishna then enumerates the parallel cases: intending to eat even a *k'zayis* of the *shirayim* outside; intending to burn the *kometz* on a private altar; intending to burn even a *k'zayis* of the *kometz* outside; or intending to offer up the *levonah* outside. Notably, the mishna does not specify a *k'zayis* for the *levonah* — because, according to all opinions except Rabbi Meir, even the smallest amount of *levonah* suffices. The previous *stam* mishna appeared to require a full *kometz* of *levonah*, while this *stam* mishna implies no minimum *shiur*, aligning more closely with Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon. The *stam* shifts.

In all these cases of wrong-place thoughts: *pasul ve'ein bo kareit* — the *korban* is invalid, but there is no *kareit*, for this is not *pigul*.

### Wrong Time Cases

The mishna then presents the identical set of cases but oriented toward the wrong *zman*: intending to eat the *shirayim* the next day (*lemachar*), or a *k'zayis* of them; intending to burn the *kometz* or a *k'zayis* of it tomorrow; intending to offer the *levonah* tomorrow. In these cases: *pigul vechayavin alav kareit* — it is *pigul*, and one who eats it incurs *kareit*, just as eating *notar* does.

The *machshava* itself constitutes only a *lav* for the one who had the thought. *Kareit* is incurred by the one who subsequently eats the *pigul* — the same structure as *notar*.

### Pigul Definition

The mishna then presents a general principle: *ze haklal*. The same way we learned by animal *korbanos* that *pigul* can be made through any of the four *avodos* of the blood, so too here — *pigul* can be made through any of the four *avodos* from *kemitzah* through *haktarah*. These four *avodos* are: *kemitzah* (parallel to *shechita*), placing in the vessel (*noten bakli*, parallel to *kabalat hadam*), carrying it to the *mizbeach* (*hamolich*, parallel to *holacha*), and burning (*hamaktir*).

The further qualification: the thought must be directed toward *davar shedarko le'echol* or *davar shedarko lehaktir* — eating what is normally eaten, and burning what is normally burned. A thought to eat the *kometz* tomorrow is irrelevant, because the *kometz* is not meant for eating. A thought to burn the *shirayim* at the wrong time is irrelevant, because the *shirayim* are not meant for burning. Only a thought directed at the appropriate object in the appropriate mode — but for the wrong place or wrong time — produces the relevant legal consequence. *Chutz limkomo*: *pasul ve'ein bo kareit*. *Chutz lizmano*: *pigul vichayavin alav kareit*.

One might ask about the *lechem hapanim*: could one have a *pigul* thought throughout an entire week? The answer is no — *pigul* is created not by a time span but specifically during the *avodos* themselves. The relevant *avodos* of *lechem hapanim* are removing the *bazichin* from the table and the subsequent stages; the week of sitting on the table does not itself constitute an *avoda* capable of generating *pigul*.

## Hamatir K'mitzvato

### Requirement

The mishna appends a crucial qualification: *uvilvad sheyakriv hamatir k'mitzvato* — provided that the permitting act was performed correctly. The general principle is that the *korban* must be such that, setting aside the *pigul* thought, it would have been entirely *kasher*. If there was some other defect — for instance, the *kometz* was never burned, or a *zar* performed the *haktarah* — then it is not *pigul*.

More striking still, and the mishna says this explicitly: even if the *kohen* completed all the *avodos* correctly and was himself fully *kasher*, if he had any other invalidating thought alongside the *pigul* thought, the *pigul* is undermined. The *chutz lizmano* thought must be the necessary and sole reason for the invalidation. *Pigul* only applies when, were it not for the *pigul* thought, the *korban* would have been *kasher*. If it was going to be invalid regardless due to some other cause, it is *pasul* but not *pigul*.

### Pigul Success

The mishna illustrates through examples. *Keitzad karav hamatir k'mitzvato* — what does it look like when the *matir* was brought correctly? One scenario: you took the *kemitzah* in silence — the term *shtika* here, in contrast to explicit thought, is worth noting, though Rashi elsewhere in the tractate already explains that "thinking" in this context refers to articulated intent. Then you placed it in the vessel, carried it, and burned it *chutz lizmano* — or alternatively, you had the *pigul* thought during the *kemitzah* alone and completed the remaining *avodos* in silence. Both are simple cases of *pigul*.

The mishna adds: *she'kamat v'natan bakli v'holich v'hiktir chutz lizmano* — you harbored *pigul* thoughts throughout all four *avodos*. One might think this is problematic — since each individual *pigul* thought would have been "unnecessary" given the others. But this is not a concern. The logic of *karav hamatir k'mitzvato* is simply: bracket the *pigul* problem, and the *korban* would have been *kasher*. Multiple consistent *pigul* thoughts do not undermine one another.

### Pigul Fails

The mishna then asks: *keitzad lo karav hamatir k'mitzvato*? What does it look like when the *matir* was not brought correctly? The first example: you performed the *kemitzah* with a *chutz limkomo* thought, and then performed one or more of the remaining *avodos* with a *chutz lizmano* thought. Or you began with a *pigul* thought and introduced a *chutz limkomo* thought in a subsequent *avoda*. Or you had *chutz limkomo* throughout all four. In any of these combinations, where a *chutz limkomo* thought accompanies the *pigul* thought, the result is *pasul* but not *pigul* — the additional invalidating thought breaks the requirement that *pigul* be the sole operative problem.

The mishna then reminds us of another category of invalidating thought from the first mishna: *shelo lishma* by a *minchat chotei* or *minchat kanaot*. If the *kemitzah* of one of these was done *shelo lishma* — and then the remaining *avodos* had a *chutz lizmano* thought, or vice versa — this falls into the same category as *chutz limkomo*: there is an additional invalidating thought beyond *pigul*, and therefore it is *pasul* without *pigul*, as a case of *lo karav hamatir k'mitzvato*.

One might note that the debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Elazar recorded in Zvachim 13a regarding *hiluch* — whether *holacha* in a non-direct path constitutes a valid *avoda* — does not reappear here in the mishna. This is presumably because we do not rule like those positions, and therefore the compilers saw no reason to replicate them here, though the question remains a fair one.

## Compound Thoughts

### Full-Portion Mix

The mishna turns now to compound thoughts — cases where during a single *avoda*, the *kohen* harbors two different types of invalidating intent regarding two different portions. The case: *le'echol k'zayis bachutz k'zayis lemachar* — planning to eat one *k'zayis* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* and one *k'zayis* the next day. Or the reverse order — first planning the *k'zayis lemachar* and then the *k'zayis bachutz*. In both orderings, the ruling is *pasul ve'ein bo kareit*. The logic appears to rely on the principle of *lo karav hamatir k'mitzvato*: even though this is not two separate *avodos* with different thoughts, the single act contains both a *chutz limkomo* element and a *chutz lizmano* element, and the *pigul* thought alone is not sufficient to invalidate.

### Half-Portion Mix

The case of half-portions is more straightforward: *kechatzayis bachutz kechatzayis lemachar* — half a *k'zayis* outside and half a *k'zayis* tomorrow, or the reverse. These are obviously *pasul ve'ein bo kareit*: there is not even a full *k'zayis* of *pigul* thought, and a thought cannot generate *pigul* unless it is directed at a *k'zayis*-equivalent portion.

## Hamatir K'mitzvato (II)

### R. Yehuda's Principle

Rabbi Yehuda now offers his own principle — *ze haklal* — which stands in disagreement with the mishna. He holds: *im machshavas hazman kadma lemachshavas hamakom* — if the *pigul* thought preceded the *chutz limkomo* thought, it locks in *pigul* status with *kareit*. The wrong-time thought arrived first and defined the *korban* as *pigul* before the wrong-place thought appeared. Conversely, *im machshavas hamakom kadma lemachshavas hazman* — if the wrong-place thought came first, it already defined the *korban* as *pasul* before the *pigul* thought could take hold, resulting in *pasul ve'ein bo kareit*.

### Sages' Position

The Sages reject Rabbi Yehuda's sequential analysis entirely. Their position: *zeh vazeh pasul ve'ein bo kareit* — in every case where both thoughts are present, whether the *pigul* thought came first or the *chutz limkomo* thought came first, the result is *pasul* without *pigul*. *Pigul* can only function when it alone is responsible for the invalidation. The presence of any other invalidating thought, regardless of sequence, prevents *pigul* from taking hold. The *halacha* follows the Sages.

## Lost Shirayim

### Question Setup

The remainder of the *gemara* departs from the central *pigul* discussion of the mishna and takes up a theme established earlier: what happens when some *shirayim* are lost after the *kemitzah* but before the *haktarah*?

The question: *iba'ya lehu* — according to the opinion of Rabbi Yochanan, which we essentially rule like, that if some (not all) *shirayim* are lost between *kemitzah* and *haktarah* one may still burn the *kometz* — what would be the *pigul* implications?

We established earlier that Rabbi Yochanan's position carries an important qualification: *de'osin shirayim assurim be'achilah* — unlike an animal *korban* where if some of the meat is missing you simply eat the remainder, here if not all of the *shirayim* are present, you may not eat what remains. This is derived from a *pasuk*.

So the question is: if the *shirayim* are *assur be'achilah* anyway — due to this *chaser* situation — can one still make them *pigul* by harboring a *chutz lizmano* thought while burning the *kometz*? And correspondingly, does the *haktarat hakometz* in such a situation remove the *shirayim* from the category of *me'ilah*?

These are formally two separate questions — one about the parameters of *pigul*, one about the parameters of *me'ilah* — but they are united by a deeper conceptual issue: what is the nature of the prohibition against eating the *shirayim* when they are *chaser*? Is it that the *shirayim* as an object have become *pasul* — as in *yotzei* or *tamei* — or is it merely a restriction on the act of eating, without the object itself acquiring an intrinsic disqualification?

If the object is not *pasul* per se — if there is simply a halachic restriction on eating it, while the object itself retains its identity as a valid *korban* portion — then one might argue that a *pigul* thought remains relevant, and that the *haktarah* may still remove the *me'ilah* status. If, however, the *chaser* renders the object intrinsically invalid, then both the *pigul* thought and the removal of *me'ilah* status become irrelevant, because the *haktarah* cannot affect something that is fundamentally off the table.

To give this texture: ordinarily, *psulim* like *yotzei* and *tamei* render meat invalid, but they are in some sense external invalidations — the meat was taken somewhere wrong, or contracted a status from outside. *Chaser* — the very absence of part of the *mincha* — is arguably an intrinsic deficiency in the identity of the *korban* itself. The question is whether that intrinsic deficiency places it categorically beyond the reach of halachic acts like *haktarah*.

One should note that this *halacha* — that *chaser shirayim* renders the rest *assur be'achilah* — is itself unusual. Elsewhere in halacha, if some meat becomes *pasul*, the rest remains valid. Here we are told that the remainder cannot be eaten when some is missing. The *pasuk* for this is *v'hanoteret min hamincha yochlu aharon u'vanav* — "the remainder of the *mincha* shall be eaten by Aharon and his sons." The *drashah* is *v'hanoteret min hamincha*, not *v'hanoteret min hanoteret* — the remainder of the *mincha*, not the remainder of the remainder. This reads as a restriction on the circumstances under which eating is permitted, not as a pronouncement about the object's *pasul* status.

It also bears noting that if all the *shirayim* were lost — not just some — the question would not arise, because if one holds like the opinion that *zrikat hadam* cannot be performed when all the meat is gone, then the *haktarah* of the *kometz* itself could not be performed. And if no *haktarah* is performed, there is no act through which *pigul* could be generated with respect to the *shirayim*. The question is meaningful precisely in the case where some *shirayim* remain, the *haktarah* of the *kometz* can and should proceed, yet those *shirayim* which do remain cannot be eaten.

### Rav Huna

Rav Huna rules: *afilu l'Rabbi Akiva de'amar zrika mo'elet l'yotzei* — even according to Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *zrika* remains effective for meat that has become *yotzei*, in that case *yotzei* is an external invalidity — the meat is physically intact but located in the wrong place. It is *pasul* by reason of something extrinsic. *Hani milei yotzei de'ita b'einya u'pasul machmat davar acher* — that applies to *yotzei*, where the object is present and the invalidity is caused by something external.

*Aval chisaron d'psula d'gufeih hu* — but *chaser* is an intrinsic deficiency in the object itself. It is not merely in the wrong place; it is literally not fully there. *Lo mahani lei haktara* — the *haktarah* of the *kometz* cannot affect it. The *shirayim* are too fundamentally deficient to be reached by the *haktarah*. Rav Huna holds that *chaser* is a more severe invalidity than *yotzei*, and therefore even where Rabbi Akiva would apply *pigul* to *yotzei*, Rav Huna says *chaser* lies beyond even that.

### Rava's Position

Rava initially disagrees: *amar lei Rava, adra'ba* — on the contrary. He argues the opposite direction. Even according to Rabbi Eliezer, who holds *ein zrika mo'elet l'yotzei* — that *zrika* has no effect when meat is *yotzei* — that is because *yotzei* means the meat is not in the *Beit HaMikdash*. *Hani milei yotzei de'leissa bifnim* — that is *yotzei*, where the meat is literally outside. *Aval chisaron de'ita bifnim* — but *chaser*, where the remaining *shirayim* are inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, since what is there is present and inside, the *haktarah* does apply to it. *Mahani lei haktara* — and therefore there would be *pigul*, and *me'ilah* status would be removed.

Rava's conceptual framing is that *chaser* is not a *psul* at all. The *shirayim* that are there are halachically intact; there is simply a restriction on eating them that arises from the absence of some of the others. Viewed that way, the object itself remains connected to the *korban* and can be affected by the *haktarah*.

## R. Chiya's Baraita

### Rava's Reading

Rava seeks to prove his position from Rabbi Chiya's *baraita*. Rava notes: *v'sani Rabbi Chiya* — Rabbi Chiya taught a *baraita* that parallels our mishna in all respects, except that he omitted the words *o k'zayis* — the case of intending to eat or burn merely a *k'zayis*.

Why did Rabbi Chiya omit *o k'zayis*? Rava argues as follows: *kegon* — the concern was that the phrase *o k'zayis* might be understood to indicate a case where all that remains of the *shirayim* is a *k'zayis* — i.e., the rest was lost, and only a *k'zayis* is left. This is precisely the case of *chasru shirayim*. Rabbi Chiya did not want to teach that case.

Now, *o k'zayis* by the *kometz* — meaning, a thought that one would burn a *k'zayis* of the *kometz* outside or at the wrong time — would also be problematic to state, because if all that remained of the *kometz* were a *k'zayis*, the entire process would be impossible: you cannot be *maktir* the *kometz* with less than the full amount. And since one cannot speak of *o k'zayis* in that context, Rabbi Chiya wanted to be consistent and removed the phrase from the *shirayim* context as well. Even though in the *shirayim* context, halachically a *pigul* thought on a *k'zayis* of remaining *shirayim* would in principle work, Rabbi Chiya edited the text minimally and uniformly, removing *o k'zayis* throughout. And since the *baraita* concludes with *pigul vichayavin alav kareit*, Rava infers that the *haktarah* is indeed effective — otherwise Rabbi Chiya would not have taught the *pigul* ruling at all in that context.

One must acknowledge the circularity here. Rava reads Rabbi Chiya's silence on *o k'zayis* as evidence that it would have worked in the *shirayim* case but was omitted only for consistency. But Rav Huna could equally read the silence as evidence that it would not work — that the case of *chaser* is so fundamentally invalid that *pigul* does not apply. The proof is not airtight. This is presumably why the *rishonim* struggle at length with what Rava's argument actually establishes.

### Abaye's Reading

Abaye offers a cleaner and less tortured reading. *Lo* — that is not why Rabbi Chiya omitted *o k'zayis*. *Ha mani Rabbi Eliezer hi* — it is because Rabbi Chiya follows Rabbi Eliezer.

### R. Eliezer Context

The basis: *detnan* — we were taught in a mishna: *hakometz v'halevonah v'haktores v'minchat kohanim v'minchat kohen mashiach v'minchat nesachim* — in all these cases where one is obligated for offering them outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *tanna kamma* holds one is liable from a *k'zayis*. *Rabbi Eliezer poter ad she'yakriv es kulo* — Rabbi Eliezer exempts unless one offers the complete unit. A *k'zayis* does not constitute an act of *haktarah* according to Rabbi Eliezer; only the full designated amount does.

This applies equally to the *machshava*: a thought of performing *haktarah* with a *k'zayis* is not an invalidating thought according to Rabbi Eliezer, because such an act would not be a real act of *haktarah*. Therefore, *o k'zayis* in the context of *haktarah* is simply not a valid case. Rabbi Chiya, following Rabbi Eliezer, removed it.

### Consistency Logic

One might ask: even granting that *o k'zayis* cannot be stated for *haktarah* according to Rabbi Eliezer, why should it be omitted for *achilah* — eating? For eating, everyone agrees that a *k'zayis* defines the act. The *shiur* for eating is universally a *k'zayis*. Why would Rabbi Chiya not say *o k'zayis* when the thought is about eating the *shirayim* at the wrong time or wrong place?

Abaye's answer: *v'kivan de'ba'aktaras kmitza lo masni lei k'zayis* — since he cannot state the *k'zayis* case for burning the *kometz*, he does not state it for eating the *shirayim* either, so that the parallel structure is preserved. *Bishirayim nami lo matni o k'zayis*. Even though in principle, a thought of eating a *k'zayis* of *shirayim* at the wrong time would generate *pigul* (because a *k'zayis* is indeed the *shiur* for eating), Rabbi Chiya edits the text with minimal change and removes *o k'zayis* uniformly.

This reading has nothing to do with the *chaser shirayim* question. Rabbi Chiya is not addressing the case of lost *shirayim* at all. He is simply operating with Rabbi Eliezer's definition of what constitutes an act of *haktarah*, which requires the full amount, and he streamlines the text accordingly. The case where only a *k'zayis* remains of the *shirayim* — which was Rava's reading of the omission — does not enter Rabbi Chiya's calculation at all.

Rabbi Eliezer carries a further implication regarding the *levonah*. The mishna we are working with assumes *mefaglin b'chatzi matir* — that a *pigul* thought during even one of the two permitting acts (the *kometz* or the *levonah*) suffices to make *pigul*, even though both together are what permit the *shirayim* for eating. Rabbi Eliezer, however, is consistent: *hakometz v'halevonah she'hikriv echad meihen bachutz chayav; Rabbi Eliezer poter ad she'yakriv es sheneihem* — to be liable for offering outside, you must offer both the *kometz* and the *levonah* outside. A *pigul* thought on only one would correspondingly be insufficient according to Rabbi Eliezer.

So then, how can Rabbi Chiya's *baraita* mention *pigul* for a thought during just the *kemitzah* of the *kometz*, without the *levonah*? The *gemara* answers: *lo nitzrecha ela lekometz deminchat chotei* — this applies to the *kometz* of a *minchat chotei*, which has no *levonah*. By a *minchat chotei*, there is only one permitting act, so a *pigul* thought on that one act suffices even according to Rabbi Eliezer. *Ven ki ata Rav Dimi amar Rabbi Eliezer kometz deminchat chotei hu Rabbi Eliezer hi* — and when Rav Dimi came, he confirmed: it is Rabbi Eliezer's position, and it concerns the *kometz* of a *minchat chotei*.

The *gemara* then raises an objection: is it not strange that the *tanna* restructured his entire *baraita* around the special case of *minchat chotei* without explicitly identifying it as such? The *gemara* answers: *ein* — yes, this is the case. The rationale is best understood as follows: Rabbi Chiya was working from an existing text and made the least intrusive edit possible. He crossed out the *o k'zayis* throughout. The resulting text still makes sense in the context of a *minchat chotei*, even if it no longer explicitly announces that context.

## Rava Retracts

### Lechem HaPanim

*Hadar amar Rava* — Rava then retracts his initial position. *Lav ma'alya hi de'amri* — what I said before is not correct. The proof comes from a *baraita* concerning *lechem hapanim*: *she-nifresa achat mehem kulam pesulot* — if one of the loaves broke, they are all *pasul*.

### Chaser Worse

This is the case of *chaser* for *lechem hapanim* — a loaf that is broken is *chaser*. And from this *baraita*, Rava infers: *hayatzata hani de-ika gavai ksherot* — the implication is that the loaves which are inside, i.e., intact, would be valid were it not for the breakage. And the further implication: if one was *yotzei* — taken outside — the remaining loaves would be *kasher*, because only *chaser* triggers universal *psul*. Thus *chaser* is worse than *yotzei*. And since *chaser* is worse than *yotzei*, even where Rabbi Akiva says *zrika* is effective for *yotzei*, he would not say so for *chaser*. Rava retracts his position and now holds: *chaser* is intrinsically more severe than *yotzei*, and therefore the *haktarah* does not render the *shirayim* subject to *pigul* or remove *me'ilah* status.

### Abaye Responds

Abaye responds: *mi katani hayatzata?* — does the *baraita* actually say that if one was *yotzei* it would be *kasher*? *Dilma hanitmas hanach ksherot* — perhaps what the *baraita* implies is only that if one loaf became *tamei*, the rest remain valid. *Mai ta'ama? Demartze tzitz* — because the *tzitz* atones for *tumah*, so *tamei* loaves do not invalidate the rest. *Aval yatzata lo* — but if one was *yotzei*, perhaps the rest would also be *pasul*. *Ve-Rabbi Eliezer hee, de-amar ein zrika mo'elet l'yotzei* — and the *baraita* would follow Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that *zrika* is not effective for *yotzei*, meaning that *yotzei* also completely invalidates. On that reading, the *baraita* gives the example of a broken loaf simply to teach us that even *chaser*, where the loaf is still inside the *Beit HaMikdash* and is not *yotzei*, is equally invalid. *De-afillu nifresa de-itah bifnim lo mahanya lah haktara* — even a loaf that broke but remained inside is just as bad as *yotzei*. The *baraita* is thus according to Rabbi Eliezer, and the implication about *yotzei* cannot be drawn from it.

### Conclusion

In the end, the *gemara* reaches the following conclusion: whether *chaser* is subject to *pigul* and removed from *me'ilah* through *haktarah* depends on the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer. According to Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *zrika* is effective even for *yotzei*, *haktarah* would be effective for *chaser* as well — the *pigul* thought would be relevant and *me'ilah* would be removed. According to Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that *zrika* is not effective even for *yotzei*, *haktarah* would equally not be effective for *chaser* — *pigul* does not apply and *me'ilah* remains.

The original framing of the question allowed for the possibility that *chaser* might be worse than *yotzei* (Rav Huna's position), better than *yotzei* (Rava's original position), or equivalent to *yotzei*. The *gemara* concludes with them equivalent: the same *machlokes* between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer that governs *yotzei* governs *chaser* as well. Rava's retraction moves him away from his original position that *chaser* was more lenient, but Abaye successfully deflects his proof that *chaser* is more stringent. The matter remains dependent on the broader *machlokes tannaim*.
