# Shabbat 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Framework

### Opening & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּמָּה אִשָּׁה יוֹצְאָה, וּבַמָּה אֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna lists items that a woman may or may not carry into, or wear in the public domain on Shabbat. This depends on whether the particular object is considered an ornament, which she may wear, or merely a burden for the woman, which she may not. Even if it is considered an ornament, there is still concern that she might remove it and carry it in her hand in the public domain, which is prohibited by Torah law.  MISHNA: **With what** items may **a woman go out** into the public domain on Shabbat **and with what** items may **she not go out?**

We begin a new *perek* today — *perek* six, *Bameh Isha* — continuing within the sequence of chapters that address preparations for *Shabbat*. To recall the broader context: the tractate opened with concerns about *hotza'ah* and activities before *mincha*, then moved in the second *perek* to lighting candles, the third to placing food on the fire, the fourth to insulating food, and the fifth to what one's animal may carry on *Shabbat* — which is to say, what must be removed from the animal before *Shabbat* begins. That fifth *perek*, though it might initially seem remote from our daily lives given that we no longer keep farm animals, reframed what *Erev Shabbat* means: not merely tending to the home, but going down to the stable, unburdening the animal, ensuring that it too had its *Shabbat*. That is an important dimension of the preparations.

Now we arrive at what one may wear on *Shabbat* — which is both a question of what one dresses in on *Shabbat* itself and of what one puts on Friday afternoon as one enters the day. There is a pleasing structural symmetry here: we have six days of *chol* and a seventh day of *Shabbat*, and correspondingly six *prakim* of preparation before the seventh *perek* turns directly to the *melachot* of *Shabbat*. The sixth *perek* addresses what is considered clothing or jewelry and what is not.

### Rashi's Framework

Before reading the *mishnah*, it is worth examining the first *Rashi* carefully, because he lays out the conceptual framework that underlies the entire *perek*. *Rashi* identifies two distinct concerns — one biblical, one rabbinic — and both appear in the *perek*, though they are not always clearly distinguished in the *mishnah* itself.

The first concern, which *Rashi* characterizes as biblical, is captured in the phrase *de-havi tachshit ve-lo massa* — the item must constitute jewelry or clothing, not a burden. Wearing something on one's body does not automatically render it clothing; a backpack, for instance, is worn on the body but is plainly a burden. The question is whether what a woman wears qualifies as *tachshit* — jewelry or clothing — or whether it is merely something she is carrying on her person. Anything that falls outside the category of clothing or jewelry is, biblically, a burden, and carrying it constitutes a violation.

The second concern is rabbinic. *Rashi* describes it: *ve-ika de-havi tachshit* — there are items that genuinely are jewelry and therefore pose no biblical problem — *ve-gazaru beih rabbanan*, but the rabbis nonetheless prohibited wearing them. Why? *Dilma shalfa u-machavya le-chaverta le-chashivuta* — lest she remove the item to show her friend how beautiful it is. *Ve-dilma ati le-asuyei arba amot* — and then she will walk four *amot* while holding it before replacing it, thereby carrying in the public domain. This same dual structure — a biblical concern and a rabbinic concern — was present in the previous *perek* regarding animals, where loose items might fall off and come to be carried.

### Mishnah Overview

The *mishnah* does not always specify which category of concern is at play, but as the *Gemara* will make clear, this first *mishnah* is primarily addressing the second, rabbinic concern — things that are genuinely jewelry, but that the rabbis worried might be removed and carried.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא תֵּצֵא אִשָּׁה לֹא בְּחוּטֵי צֶמֶר וְלֹא בְּחוּטֵי פִּשְׁתָּן וְלֹא בִּרְצוּעוֹת שֶׁבְּרֹאשָׁהּ. וְלֹא תִּטְבּוֹל בָּהֶן, עַד שֶׁתְּרַפֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **A woman may neither go out with strings of wool, nor with strings of flax, nor with strips** of any other materials that a woman braids **in** the hair of **her head. And** a woman **may not immerse** in a ritual bath **with them** in her hair **until she loosens them.** When the strings or strips are tight, the water cannot reach her hair unobstructed, invalidating her immersion.

*Bameh isha yotza u-ve-mah eina yotza* — what may a woman go out with and what may she not go out with? The *mishnah* opens with a series of prohibitions: *lo teitzei isha lo be-chutei tzemer velo be-chutei pishtan* — she may not go out with wool strings or flax strings woven into her hair. *Velo be-retzuah she-be-roshah* — nor with a strap on her head, whether on her forehead or wrapped around her hair. There is then a parenthetical remark: *velo titbol bahem ad she-tirapaim* — she also may not go to the *mikvah* with these strings unless she first loosens them, since the water must be able to reach between the string and the hair. The *Gemara*, as we will see, treats this remark as far more than parenthetical — it is in fact the key to understanding the entire prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּ״טוֹטֶפֶת״ וְלֹא בְּסַרְבִּיטִין בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵינָן תְּפוּרִים. וְלֹא בְּכָבוּל לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And,** likewise, a woman may **neither** go out **with** the ornament called ***totefet*, nor with *sarvitin* that are not sewn** into her head covering, **nor with a *kavul* into the public domain.**

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-lo totfet ve-lo sarbitin* — she may not wear a *totfet* or *sarvitin*, which are types of head ornaments whose precise identity will be clarified in the *Gemara*. *Bi-zman she-einam tefurim* — this prohibition applies when they are not sewn into her hairnet or cap. The *Gemara* will operate on the assumption that a married woman always has her hair covered, and therefore the concern is not about the hair covering itself, which she would never remove in public, but about ornaments attached separately to it. If they are woven into her hairnet, there is no concern; if they are separate, there is.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּעִיר שֶׁל זָהָב וְלֹא בְּקַטְלָא וְלֹא בִּנְזָמִים וְלֹא בְּטַבַּעַת שֶׁאֵין עָלֶיהָ חוֹתָם וְלֹא בְּמַחַט שֶׁאֵינָהּ נְקוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And,** likewise, a woman may **neither** go out **with a city of gold** ornament, **nor with a *katla*** ornament, **nor with nose rings, nor with a ring that has no seal on it, nor with a needle that is not perforated,** which are merely for decorative purposes.

*Ve-lo be-khvul li-reshut ha-rabim* — she may not go out with a *kvul* into the public domain. The word *reshut ha-rabim* appears explicitly only in connection with the *kvul*, which raises a question: does the prohibition on all the earlier items apply only in the public domain, or more broadly? The *Gemara* will take a stricter position, ruling that all the items listed earlier are prohibited even in the *chatzer*, the courtyard — the semi-private enclosed space shared by several households. The reasoning is that if we permit her to wear these items in the courtyard, she will come to wear them into the street. This is a remarkably expansive decree: even in a space that is technically *reshut ha-yachid*, the rabbis prohibited wearing these items. Yet even under that strict view, the *kvul* forms an exception — it is permitted in the *chatzer* but not in *reshut ha-rabim*, because had the rabbis prohibited absolutely everything outdoors, it would have been an untenable burden on women.

The *mishnah* then lists additional items: *ve-lo be-ir shel zahav* — not with a "golden city," apparently some type of tiara fashioned to resemble a cityscape; *ve-lo be-katla* — not with a *katla*, a type of jewelry we will identify shortly; *ve-lo bi-nezamim* — not with earrings; *ve-lo be-taba'at she-ein aleha khotam* — not with a ring that has no seal. The absence of a seal is relevant: a ring with no seal is purely ornamental and thus rabbinicaly problematic only because she might remove it to show someone. A ring with a seal, however, might not even qualify as jewelry in the first place, since it serves a functional purpose, and could therefore be a biblical violation. *Ve-lo be-machat she-eina nekuva* — not with a needle that has no hole, which functions like a decorative pin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם יָצָאת אֵינָהּ חַיֶּיבֶת חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if she** unwittingly **went out** wearing any of these, **she is not liable** to bring **a sin-offering.** According to Torah law, a woman is permitted to go out into the public domain wearing ornaments. However, the Sages decreed that a woman may not go out wearing certain ornaments, lest she remove them to show them to another and inadvertently carry them four cubits in the public domain.

The *mishnah* concludes: *ve-im yatza eina chayabat chatat* — if she does go out with any of these items, she has not incurred biblical liability, since these are rabbinic prohibitions. Were she, by contrast, wearing a ring with a seal or a needle with a hole — items that do not qualify as jewelry — she would be biblically liable, *chayabat chatat*.

One might wonder how all of this applies today, given that most communities have an *eruv*. But one student's observation is apt and worth noting: if these items are forbidden even in a *chatzer*, which is itself a *reshut ha-yachid*, an *eruv* — which converts the public domain halachically into something resembling a *chatzer* — may not resolve the problem. If the decree reaches into the *chatzer*, it would seem to reach into an *eruv* as well.

## Wool/Linen Strings

### Tevila Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טְבִילָה מַאן דְּכַר שְׁמַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Immersion, who mentioned** anything about **that?** The mishna is dealing with the *halakhot* of Shabbat, so why did it mention the *halakhot* of immersion?

The *Gemara* opens with a striking question, bypassing the conceptual material and jumping directly to the clause about *tevila*: *tvila man d'char shemeih?* — who mentioned immersion? What does the *mikvah* have to do with the laws of *Shabbat*? *Rashi* frames this with his characteristic wit: *mai inyan tvila eitzel hilchot shabbat* — what relevance does immersion have to the laws of *Shabbat*? This is reminiscent of the rhetorical question *mai inyan shmita eitzel har sinai* — what does *shmita* have to do with Sinai? — used to highlight an apparent non sequitur. The *Gemara* raises the question because it has an answer, and the answer reframes the entire first clause of the *mishnah*.

### Nachman's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ — מַה טַּעַם קָאָמַר: מַה טַּעַם לֹא תֵּצֵא אִשָּׁה לֹא בְּחוּטֵי צֶמֶר וְלֹא בְּחוּטֵי פִּשְׁתָּן — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים בַּחוֹל לָא תִּטְבּוֹל בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁתְּרַפֵּם. וְכֵיוָן דִּבְחוֹל לָא תִּטְבּוֹל בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁתְּרַפֵּם, בְּשַׁבָּת לֹא תֵּצֵא, דִּילְמָא מִיתְרְמֵי לַהּ טְבִילָה שֶׁל מִצְוָה וְשָׁרְיָא לְהוּ, וְאָתֵי לְאֵתוֹיִינְהוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that **Rabba bar Avuh** said: When the mishna **states** the *halakha*, it employs the style known as: **What is the reason.** The mention of immersion is an explanation, not a superfluous addition. The mishna should be understood as follows: **What is the reason** that **a woman may neither go out with strings of wool nor with strings of flax?** It is **because the Sages said** that **on weekdays she may not immerse with them until she loosens them. And since on weekdays she may not immerse with them until she loosens them, on Shabbat she may not go out** with them, **lest** a situation requiring **immersion** for the purpose of a **mitzva come about, and she untie them, and come to carry them four cubits in the public domain.**

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak amar Rabba bar Avuha* — Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, citing Rabba bar Avuha, offers a reading: *ma taam kamar* — read the *mishnah* as the mention of *tevila* providing the reason for the earlier prohibition. Why may a woman not go out with wool or flax strings woven into her hair? One might think these are so minor that no one would ever remove them to show a friend. What then is the concern?

The *Gemara* explains: *mipnei she-amru chachamim* — because the rabbis have ruled that on a weekday, *lo titbol bahem ad she-terafem* — she may not immerse in the *mikvah* with these strings until she loosens them. *Ve-keivan de-bachol lo titbol bahem ad she-terafem* — and since that is the weekday rule, *be-Shabbat lo teitzei* — she should not wear them on *Shabbat*. Why? *Dilma mishtarmi lah tvila shel mitzvah* — lest an occasion for a required immersion arise on *Shabbat*, most naturally the immersion of a *niddah*.

### Mikvah Removal Fear

A woman is permitted to go to the *mikvah* on *Shabbat*. If she is wearing these strings and must immerse, *ve-sharya lehu* — she will loosen them, remove them from her hair. A brief linguistic aside is in order: the words *assur* and *mutar*, which we conventionally translate as prohibited and permitted, are in fact words about binding. *Assur* means bound, as in *beit asurim*, a prison. *Mutar* means freed, loosened. *Le-hatir* means to undo the knots. This is relevant here: the *Gemara* says she will come to *matir otam*, to loosen the strings — and then, having come out of the *mikvah* with wet hair, she will not bother rewinding the strings into her hair. *Ve-asi le-asvunehu arba amos bi-reshut ha-rabim* — and she will carry them four *amot* in the public domain. That is the concern: not that she will show them to a friend, but that the necessity of removing them for the *mikvah* will lead to carrying them on *Shabbat*.

### Tosafot Minor Items

*Tosafot* presses the obvious question: this logic seems to apply to anything a woman is wearing. If she goes to the *mikvah* on *Shabbat*, she removes all of her clothing — so why are we not afraid she will fail to replace any of them? *Tosafot* answers that major items of clothing a person will certainly replace before leaving the *mikvah*. The concern applies specifically to minor and trivial items, things so insignificant that a woman, emerging from the *mikvah* with wet hair, will not bother to weave them back in and will simply walk home carrying them. It is precisely the triviality of these items that creates the risk.

## Porous Strings

### Rav Kahana Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא מֵרַב: תִּיכֵי חֲלִילָתָא מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָרִיג קָאָמְרַתְּ? כׇּל שֶׁהוּא אָרִיג לֹא גָּזְרוּ. אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא אָרִיג לֹא גָּזְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana raised a dilemma before Rav:** With regard to strings made into **hollow chains, what is** the *halakha*? Is it permissible for women to go out into the public domain with them on Shabbat or not? It depends on whether they are considered an interposition to immersion. Rav **said to him: Woven, you say?** With regard to **anything woven, the Sages did not issue a decree.** Because water reaches the hair unobstructed, there is no need to loosen the hollow chain and there is no concern lest she carry it in the public domain. **It was also stated** that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** With regard to **anything woven, the Sages did not issue a decree.**

*Ba'ei minei Rav Kahana mei Rav* — Rav Kahana posed a question to Rav: *shikhi chalalita mai?* — what about braids or strands that are chain-like, with many gaps and openings, through which water flows freely? These are effectively porous. Does the *mikvah* concern apply to them?

Rav replied: *ari kamarta?* — are you speaking of something that is woven? *Kol she-hu arig lo gazru* — anything of that woven, porous character was never included in the prohibition. Such items do not present a *mikvah* obstruction concern, and therefore there is no reason to fear she will remove them before immersing. No concern about showing them to a friend, no concern about removing them for the *mikvah* — they are entirely permitted.

*Itmar nami* — this was also stated independently: *amar Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua, kol she-hu arig lo gazru* — anything of this woven, porous character was not subject to the decree.

### Case-by-Case Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: חֲזֵינָא לְאַחְווֹתִי דְּלָא קָפְדָן עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: I saw that my sisters are not particular about** removing **them,** and they bathe even with woven chains tied in their hair. Apparently, water reaches the hair. Therefore, the chain is not an interposition with regard to immersion.

*Ve-ika de-amri* — there is an alternative version of Rav Huna brei de-Rav Yehoshua's statement: *chazina le-achvasi de-lo kapdan alaihu* — I observed my sisters, and they were not *makpid* about these items, meaning they did not trouble themselves to remove them when going to bathe. *Rashi* notes that the reference is not only to *mikvah* immersion but to regular bathing; indeed, *Rashi* argues that one is actually more careful about removing things when washing one's hair in an ordinary bath than when immersing in a *mikvah* for halachic purposes, since for the *mikvah* one need only ensure water can flow through — if the item is porous, one need not remove it. In an ordinary bath, by contrast, one wants clean hair and will naturally remove anything that might impede washing. Rav Huna's sisters did not remove these items even when bathing, demonstrating that water passes through them freely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין הַךְ לִישָּׁנָא וּבֵין הַךְ לִישָּׁנָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** practical difference **is there between this version and that version** of the resolution of the dilemma?

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai ika bein hai lishna u-vein hai lishna?* — what practical difference exists between these two formulations? One version says that all *arig*-type items are categorically excluded from the prohibition; the other focuses on whether a woman is *makpid* — whether she would actually be bothered to remove the item.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דִּטְנִיפָן. לְהַךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא אָרִיג לֹא גָּזְרוּ — הָנֵי נָמֵי אָרִיג נִינְהוּ, וּלְהַךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם קְפִידָא — כֵּיוָן דִּטְנִיפָא מִקְפָּד קָפְדָא עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **There is** a practical difference **between them** in a case **where** the chains **are dirty. According to this version,** in **which you said:** With regard to **anything woven, the Sages did not issue a decree, these too are woven. And according to that version,** in **which you said** that it is **due to** the fact that his sisters were not **particular;** in this case, **since they are dirty, she is particular about them** and will certainly remove them when she washes. Therefore, she is required to do so when immersing in a ritual bath as well.

The difference emerges in a case where the braids or strings are soiled — dirty. *Le-hai lishna de-amar kol she-hu arig lo gazru* — according to the categorical approach, since this type of item was placed in the "not prohibited" category because water flows through it, it remains in that category regardless of circumstances. Even if the strings are dirty and a woman would naturally want to remove them when washing her hair, that particular circumstance does not change the halachic category. *Le-hai lishna de-amarta mishum kfeida* — but according to the approach that focuses on whether a woman is actually *makpid*, *keivan de-tanifa mikpad kapid alaihu* — since the strings are dirty she will certainly be *makpid* to remove them, even if not for *mikvah* reasons but simply because she does not want her wet hair getting muddied. In that case, on *Shabbat*, she should not wear them, because we can foresee that she will in fact remove them.

So the two approaches differ in their methodology: one makes categorical determinations based on the type of item; the other assesses individual circumstances to determine whether a woman will actually be *makpid* to remove the item, and restricts accordingly.

### Makpid Definition

*Rashi* presents an important clarification about the concept of *makpid*, and in doing so takes issue with his teachers. *Rashi* quotes his *Rabbaim* who argued that if the strings are dirty and a woman does not want them on her when immersing, her unwillingness itself creates a *chatzitzah* problem — that being *makpid* alone, even where water can pass through the item, renders it an obstruction. *Rashi* firmly rejects this. The criterion for *chatzitzah* requires two independent conditions: the item must physically prevent water from reaching the body, and the person must be *makpid* — unwilling to have it there. *Makpid* alone, absent a physical obstruction to water flow, cannot create a *chatzitzah*. If water gets through, the item is not an obstruction regardless of the woman's attitude toward it.

This is actually the operative ruling: we do not follow *Rashi*'s *Rabbaim* on this point. The ruling is that *makpid* without physical obstruction does not constitute *chatzitzah*. This has significant practical application: certain items on the body — bandages, braces, soft casts in some circumstances — through which water cannot pass but which one is not *makpid* about, may fall into a different category, and that determination is indeed discussed in the halachic literature.

## Chatzitzim Discussion

### Mikvaot Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: וְאֵלּוּ חוֹצְצִין בָּאָדָם: חוּטֵי צֶמֶר וְחוּטֵי פִּשְׁתָּן וְהָרְצוּעוֹת שֶׁבְּרָאשֵׁי הַבָּנוֹת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁל צֶמֶר וְשֶׁל שֵׂעָר אֵין חוֹצְצִין מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַמַּיִם בָּאִין בָּהֶן. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: וְכוּלָּן, בְּרָאשֵׁי הַבָּנוֹת שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna in tractate *Mikvaot*: **And these** are the objects that **interpose for a person: Strings of wool, and strings of flax, and the straps that are on the girls' heads. Rabbi Yehuda says:** Strings **of wool and** strands **of hair do not interpose because the water reaches** the hair **through them. Rav Huna said: And we learned all these,** strings of wool and flax, in a case where they are used to tie the hair **on the girls' heads.**

*Tnan hatam* — the *Gemara* now cites the relevant *mishnah* from *Mikvaot*: *ve-eilu chotzetzim be-adam* — the following constitute obstructions for people. The list begins precisely with the same three items as our *mishnah* in *Shabbat*: wool strings, linen strings, and the straps on the head. The parallel is not coincidental. Our *mishnah* in *Shabbat* assumes that a woman does not go to the *mikvah* with these items, and the *Gemara* has now established that this is exactly why they cannot be worn on *Shabbat*.

Rabbi Yehuda in the *mishnah* of *Mikvaot* disagrees: *shel tzemer ve-shel se'ar ein chotzetzin mipnei she-ha-mayim ba'in bahem* — wool and hair strings woven into the hair are not obstructions, because water penetrates between them. He concedes that linen, which lies tight and flat, may obstruct; but he holds that wool and hair allow water to flow through. The *tanna kamma* does not explicitly address hair strings, leaving that question open.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּצַוָּאר, וּדְמַאי: אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּצֶמֶר — הַשְׁתָּא רַךְ עַל גַּבֵּי קָשֶׁה חוֹצֵץ, רַךְ עַל גַּבֵּי רַךְ מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef strongly objects to** this *halakha* of Rav Huna: **To exclude what** other places in the body did Rav Huna say this? **If you say** that it comes **to exclude** strings tied **to the neck, and** then, strings made **of what** material does it exclude? **If you say** that it comes **to exclude** strings **of wool, now,** the mishna stated that **soft** strings of wool **on top of** hair, which is relatively **hard, interpose** and invalidates the immersion. With regard to **soft** strings **on top of** the **soft** flesh of the neck, **is it necessary** to say that they interpose?

*Amar Rav Kahana Query* — Rav Kahana posed a question to Rav: *Rav replied: ari kamarta?* — are you speaking of something that is woven? Rav Huna's statement that "all of this is in the hair" cannot be coming to exclude the neck — the neck would certainly be included.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּחוּטֵי פִשְׁתָּן. הַשְׁתָּא קָשֶׁה עַל גַּבֵּי קָשֶׁה חוֹצֵץ, קָשֶׁה עַל גַּבֵּי רַךְ מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say that Rav Huna's statement came **to exclude strings of flax.** A similar difficulty arises: **Now** the mishna stated that **hard** strings of wool **on top of** hair, which is **hard, interposes** and invalidates the immersion. If so, with regard to **hard** strings **on top of** the **soft** flesh of the neck, **is it necessary** to say that they interpose?

*Ela amar Rav Yosef* — so what then is the reasoning that Rav Huna must have had? *Lefi she-ein ishah choneket et atzmah* — because a woman does not choke herself. Whatever she wears around her neck she keeps loose. A woman will wind strings tightly into her hair, but a necklace around the neck is kept loose by self-preservation, and therefore water can get through. Thus neck coverings are not a *chatzitzah* — not because the *mishnah* excludes them, but because the physical reality is that they will always be loose.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַב הוּנָא: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אִשָּׁה חוֹנֶקֶת אֶת עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef said, this is the reason that Rav Huna** restricts the concern for interposition to strings tied in her hair and not around her neck: **Because a woman does not strangle herself** when adorning herself with a string or straps around her neck. Therefore, she never tightens the strings or straps to the extent that water cannot reach the skin.

### Choker vs. Necklace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַבָּנוֹת יוֹצְאוֹת בְּחוּטִין שֶׁבְּאׇזְנֵיהֶן, אֲבָל לֹא בַּחֲבָקִים שֶׁבְּצַוְּארֵיהֶן. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ אֵין אִשָּׁה חוֹנֶקֶת עַצְמָהּ, חֲבָקִים שֶׁבְּצַוְּארֵיהֶן אַמַּאי לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** the explanation of Rav Yosef from a *baraita*: **The girls** may **go out** into the public domain on Shabbat **with strings in their ears.** Young girls would have their ears pierced, but earrings were not placed in their ears until they were older. Instead, they inserted strings so that the holes would not close. **However,** they may **not** go out **with straps** around **their necks. And if you say** that the principle: **A woman does not strangle herself,** is halakhically valid, **why** may they **not** go out into the public domain with **straps around their necks?** They are not tied tight and do not constitute an interposition that invalidates immersion.

*Eitiveih Abaye* — Abaye challenges Rav Yosef: but the *mishnah* elsewhere states that young girls may go out on *Shabbat* with strings in their ears — worn to keep the piercing open — but *aval lo be-chabakim she-be-tzavareihen* — they may not wear chokers around their necks. If a woman always keeps things around the neck loose, why would chokers be a problem on *Shabbat*? The *Gemara* is operating on the assumption that the choker prohibition is because she would have to remove it for the *mikvah*. But if the neck is always loose, there should be no *mikvah* concern.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* amar Ravina:
>
> **Translation:** Ravina said:

*Amar Ravina* — Ravina resolves this: *hacha be-katla askinan* — we are dealing here with a *katla*. The *katla* was mentioned already in our *mishnah*. *Rashi* understands it as something like a bib or apron that includes a tight choker element at the top. There exist types of neck ornaments — true chokers — that a woman does wear tightly, precisely because a tight band around the neck is considered attractive.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּקַטְלָא עָסְקִינַן, דְּאִשָּׁה חוֹנֶקֶת אֶת עַצְמָהּ — דְּנִיחָא לַהּ שֶׁתֵּרָאֶה כְּבַעֲלַת בָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **Here we are dealing with a broad, ornamented strap [*katla*]** hanging around the neck, to which a small bib is attached. **A woman does strangle herself** with a *katla* because the strap is broad and tightening it does not cause pain. She tightens it **because it pleases her that she will appear fleshy.** It was considered beautiful to have flesh protrude from the *katla*.

*De-ishah choneket et atzmah* — in this case, the woman actually does constrict herself. Therefore, a *katla*-style choker is a genuine *chatzitzah* concern, and one would have to remove it before immersing in the *mikvah* on *Shabbat*. Since she would have to remove it before immersing in the *mikvah* on *Shabbat*, she cannot wear it on *Shabbat*. A looser necklace, by contrast, is not a *mikvah* concern and thus is not a *Shabbat* concern.

## Totafet & Sarbitin

### Totafet Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא בְּ״טוֹטֶפֶת״. מַאי ״טוֹטֶפֶת״? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: חוּמַרְתָּא דִקְטִיפְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna said that a woman may **not** go out **with** the ornament called a ***totefet*.** The Gemara asks: **What is a *totefet*? Rav Yosef said: A packet of spices** to ward off the evil eye.

*Ve-lo ve-totefes* — the *mishnah* also prohibits wearing a *totfet*. *Mai totefes?* — what is a *totfet*? *Amar Rav Yosef: chumsa de-katifta* — a small bundle of spices, which *Rashi* explains was worn to ward off the *ayin hara*.

### Amulet Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: תִּהְוֵי כְּקָמֵיעַ מוּמְחֶה, וְתִשְׁתְּרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: And let** the legal status of **this** packet **be like** that of **an effective amulet,** whose effectiveness is proven, **and** it should **be permitted,** as an effective amulet may be moved on Shabbat.

*Amar lei Abaye* — Abaye immediately presses: if this is a spice bundle worn for protection against the evil eye, the problem is not that it is a mere rabbinic issue — it should not be a problem at all. *Tehei ke-kameia mumcheh ve-tishtarei* — let it be treated like a proven amulet, which is permitted to be worn on *Shabbat*. As the *Gemara* will discuss later, amulets that have been demonstrated to be effective — attested by repeated successful use — are considered a form of protective clothing and are not subject to the *Shabbat* restriction. After all, if an amulet is doing its protective work, one has every reason to keep it on and no motivation to remove it. So why should this spice bundle be a problem?

### Amulet Deferred

The discussion of amulets, including the question of whether one may rely on their efficacy and a broader engagement with the rationalist critique — including the *Rambam*'s position — will be addressed when the *Gemara* reaches that topic directly. For now, the implication of Abaye's challenge is that if the *totfet* is a spice bundle for the *ayin hara*, it fits the model of an effective amulet and ought to be entirely permitted.

### Rabbi Yannai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: אֲפוּזְיָינֵי. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: יוֹצְאָה אִשָּׁה בִּסְבָכָה הַמּוּזְהֶבֶת, וּבְטוֹטֶפֶת וּבְסַרְבִּיטִין הַקְּבוּעִין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yehuda said in the name of Abaye:** A *totefet* is an ***appuzainu*,** an ornament worn on the forehead. This opinion **was also taught** in a *baraita*: **A woman may go out with a gilded hairnet** worn to hold the hair in place, **and with the *totefet,* and with the *sarvitin* that are fastened to the hairnet,** since a woman would not remove her head covering to show her friend those ornaments.

*Ella amar Rav Yehuda mishmeih de-Abaye: apuzaino* — Rav Yehuda, in the name of Abaye, offers a different identification: the *totfet* is an *apuzaino*, apparently some type of head ornament. The precise meaning of the term is unclear — commentators have speculated about its etymology and referent — but the word *totfet* itself, evoking *totafot*, suggests an ornament of that kind.

### Golden Hairnet

*Tanya nami hachi: yotzei isha bi-shvacha muzhevet* — it was similarly taught in a *baraita*: a woman may go out with a golden hairnet. The question naturally arises: if the concern is that she will take off attractive jewelry to show a friend, should not a golden hairnet be the prime candidate? The answer is that a woman would never remove her basic hair covering in public. She would not expose her hair in *reshut ha-rabim*. Therefore, even a magnificent golden hairnet may be worn, because the risk of removal does not exist. *U-ve-totefes u-ve-sarvitin ha-kvuim bah* — and similarly, a *totfet* and *sarvitin* that are affixed to the hairnet may be worn, since the only way to remove them would be to remove the hairnet itself, which she will not do.

### Anatomy Description

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיזוֹ טוֹטֶפֶת וְאֵיזוֹ סַרְבִּיטִין? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: טוֹטֶפֶת — הַמּוּקֶּפֶת לָהּ מֵאֹזֶן לְאֹזֶן. סַרְבִּיטִין — הַמַּגִּיעִין לָהּ עַד לְחָיֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** And they said: **Which is a *totefet* and which is *sarvitin*? Rabbi Abbahu said: *Totefet* is that which goes around her** forehead **from ear to ear. *Sarvitin* are those** attached to the net **that reach** down **to her cheeks.**

*Eizu totefes ve-eizu sarvitin? Amar Rabbi Abbahu: totefes ha-mukefet me-ozen le-ozen* — the *totfet* is something that goes from ear to ear, like a headband across the forehead. *Ve-sarvitin ha-magi'in lah ad le-chayeha* — the *sarvitin* are ornaments that hang down to the jaw. These are the temple ornaments one sees depicted in ancient Near Eastern and Egyptian headdresses: bands across the forehead, pendants hanging down the sides of the face. If woven into the hairnet, they present no problem. If worn separately, a woman might remove them to show a friend, and therefore they are forbidden.

### Poor Women Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: עֲנִיּוֹת עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָן שֶׁל מִינֵי צִבְעוֹנִין, עֲשִׁירוֹת עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָן שֶׁל כֶּסֶף וְשֶׁל זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: Poor women make** these ornaments **from** different **types of colored** materials. **Wealthy women make them of silver and of gold.**

*Amar Rav Huna: aniyot osot otan shel minei tzvu'in* — poor women would make these ornaments from colored, dyed materials rather than precious metals. This is not merely an anthropological note. The point is that the prohibition is not limited to precious or expensive ornaments. Even a beautifully colored piece fashioned from inexpensive materials can be something attractive that a woman might want to display. Within a person's socioeconomic context, even simple ornaments become objects of pride and social display. Therefore, the concern of removal applies regardless of whether the item is made of gold or of dyed fibers.

## Kvul Analysis

### Kvul Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּכָבוּל. אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כָּבוּל זֶה אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מַהוּ: אִי כַּבְלָא דְעַבְדָּא תְּנַן — אֲבָל כִּיפָּה שֶׁל צֶמֶר שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, אוֹ דִילְמָא כִּיפָּה שֶׁל צֶמֶר תְּנַן — וְכָל שֶׁכֵּן כַּבְלָא דְעַבְדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that a woman may **not** go out with **a *kavul*. Rabbi Yannai said: This *kavul*, I do not know what it is. Is it the seal of a slave,** who would have a seal on his clothing identifying him as a slave, about **which we learned** in our mishna that it is prohibited, **but a cap of wool** that a woman places on her hair, she may **well** go out wearing it? **Or, perhaps we learned** in our mishna that going out with **a cap of wool** is prohibited **and all the more so** that going out with **the seal of a slave** is prohibited.

*Ve-lo be-khvul* — the *mishnah* prohibits going out with a *kvul*. *Amar Rabbi Yannai: be-khvul zeh eini yodea mahu* — Rabbi Yannai frankly admits he does not know what the *kvul* is. Yet he is not entirely without options. He proposes two possibilities: *ee kavla de-avda tenan* — perhaps we are talking about the identifying mark worn by slaves, something like a tag or seal around the neck that identifies whose slave one is; *o dilma kippah shel tzemmer* — or perhaps it is a wool hat, an additional hair covering worn by a woman who already has a *shvacha* hairnet.

The two possibilities are not simply alternate translations — they carry different halachic implications. The question is which one represents the concern of the *mishnah*. If it is the slave's identifying collar, the concern is that the slave will remove it. If it is the wool hat, the question is whether a woman might remove an additional hair covering given that she already has a hairnet underneath — or, more precisely here, given that the hat sits atop the hairnet. One might think that any hair covering is something a woman would not remove in public, but the *Gemara* explores whether a secondary hat, which can in theory be removed without exposing the hair, is something we fear she might take off.

*Amar Rabbi Abbahu: mistabra* — it makes sense to say *kippah shel tzemmer tenan* — that the hat is what the *mishnah* is discussing. And a *baraita* is cited in support: *yotzei ishah bi-kvul* — a woman may go out with a *kvul* — *u-ve-istama le-hatzer* — and with an *istama* into the *chatzer*. So she may wear these items into the courtyard, but not into *reshut ha-rabim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר כִּיפָּה שֶׁל צֶמֶר תְּנַן. וְתַנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: יוֹצְאָה אִשָּׁה בְּכָבוּל וּבְאִיסְטָמָא לֶחָצֵר, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַף בְּכָבוּל לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַטָּה מִן הַשְּׂבָכָה — יוֹצְאִין בּוֹ, כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַעְלָה מִן הַשְּׂבָכָה — אֵין יוֹצְאִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said: It is reasonable** to say **in accordance with the one who said** that **we learned** about **a cap of wool** in the mishna. **And this** opinion **was also taught** in a *baraita*: **A woman may go out with a *kavul* and with an *istema* to the courtyard** on Shabbat. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** She may **even** go out **with the *kavul* into the public domain. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar stated a principle: Anything that is** worn **beneath the hairnet,** a woman **may go out** into the public domain **with it,** since a woman will not uncover her hair even to show off an ornament while in the public domain. **Anything that is** worn **over the hairnet,** like an ornamental hat, a woman **may not go out with it.** From the context and proximity of the *halakha* dealing with *kavul* to the statement of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, apparently a *kavul* is a wool cap worn under the net.

### Underneath Hairnet

*Klal amar Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar formulates a general rule.

### Above Below Divide

*Kol she-le-mata min ha-shvacha yotzin bo* — anything worn beneath the hairnet, one may carry, that is, wear, into *reshut ha-rabim*; we are not afraid she will remove it. *Kol she-le-mala min ha-shvacha ein yotzin bo* — anything worn on top of the hairnet, she may not go out with, because we fear she will remove it.

This is counterintuitive at first glance. One might have assumed that whatever is under the hairnet is harder to remove — the hairnet is on top, fixing it in place — while whatever is on top would be easier to take off. But the rule is about motivation, not mechanics. Anything under the hairnet that could theoretically be slipped out without fully removing the hairnet presents a concern in the view of the *tanna kamma* — she could remove it without exposing her hair. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar disagrees: anything under the hairnet is something we are confident she will not remove, since to do so requires manipulating the hairnet in a way she would not do in public. Only if it is above the hairnet — a real cap or hat worn openly on top of her hair covering — are we afraid she might remove it.

The core dispute is therefore about the intermediate case: something beneath the hairnet that could, with effort, be removed without full exposure. The *tanna kamma* in our *mishnah* says that is still a concern and limits it to the *chatzer*; Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says anything under the hairnet is safe.

### Istama Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אִיסְטָמָא״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בִּיזְיוֹנֵי. מַאי ״בִּיזְיוֹנֵי״? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי אָמַר רַב: כָּלְיָא פָּרוֹחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Since *istema* was mentioned in the *baraita*, the Gemara asks: **What is an *istema*? Rabbi Abbahu said:** *Istema* is a ***beizyunei*.** However, Rabbi Abbahu's explanation employed a term from the Aramaic dialect spoken in Eretz Yisrael, which was not understood in Babylonia. Therefore, they asked there: **What is** a ***beizyunei***? **Abaye said** that **Rav said:** It is a small hat or ribbon used to **gather hairs that protrude [*kalya paruḥei*]** from the headdress.

*Mai istama? Amar Rabbi Abbahu: bizyoni. Mai bizyoni? Amar Abaye amar Rav: kalya prukhei* — it is something that catches the sprouting hairs. A woman's hairnet covers the main body of hair, but some strands escape around the edges — in the front, at the back. She would wear a piece of cloth or felt around the perimeter to catch these stray hairs. This item is technically beyond what is strictly required for hair covering, and it is precisely because it is not strictly necessary that we fear she might remove it. Unlike the hairnet itself, which she will never remove, this peripheral cloth she might take off — and therefore she may not wear it into *reshut ha-rabim*.

### Istama Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּאִיסְטָמָא: אֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם כִּלְאַיִם, וְאֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה בִּנְגָעִים, וְאֵין יוֹצְאִין בָּהּ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta* that **three things were said with regard to an *istema*: There is no** prohibition of a mixture of **diverse kinds,** wool and linen, **in it.** Since it is made of hard felt and not woven together, the prohibition of diverse kinds does not apply to material of that kind. **And it does not become impure with** the ritual impurity of **leprosy.** Only woven garments can become impure with leprosy. **And** women **may not go out with it to the public domain** on Shabbat.

*Tanu rabbanan: gimmel devarim ne'emru be-istama* — three things were said about the *istama*. First, *ein ba mishum kilayim* — it is not subject to the prohibition of *sha'atnez*, the mixture of wool and linen. *Rashi* explains: even if the *istama* is pressed from wool and linen, they are not woven together. Moreover, *Tosafot* adds two further reasons: this material is stiff and does not conform to the shape of the body or provide warmth, and one of the criteria for *sha'atnez* to apply as a halachic "wearing" is that the garment conform and warm. The combination of being merely pressed rather than woven and of being rigid and non-warming means it does not qualify as clothing for *sha'atnez* purposes — and *Tosafot* argues this applies even rabbinically.

*Tosafot* also proposes an alternative explanation: perhaps the *istama* was not primarily clothing at all, but a support structure from which jewelry was hung. If the item exists not as clothing in its own right but as a base for jewelry, it would not be subject to *sha'atnez* since it is not worn *as* clothing.

Second, *ve-eina metamei bi-nega'im* — it does not become impure through *tzara'at* afflictions. Here too the reason is that it is not woven, and being woven is one of the criteria for garments to be susceptible to *nega'im* impurity. Third, *ve-ein yotzin bo le-reshut ha-rabim* — she may not go out with it into *reshut ha-rabim*, since she might remove it, for the same reason discussed above.

### Bride Crowns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אָמְרוּ: אַף אֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם עַטְרוֹת כַּלּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **In the name of Rabbi Shimon they said: Also,** the rabbinic decree prohibiting adorning brides with **bridal crowns** to commemorate the destruction of the Temple does **not** apply **to** an *istema*.

*Mishum Rabbi Shimon amru: af ein ba mishum atarot kallot* — in the name of Rabbi Shimon, they added a fourth ruling: this *istama* does not fall under the restriction on bridal crowns. A *baraita* in *Masechet Sotah* records that after the persecutions of Vespasian, in mourning for the destruction of Jerusalem, brides were no longer permitted to wear certain ornate crowns. The statement here is that the *istama* does not qualify as such a crown and is not subject to that restriction. Notably, this ruling lends support to *Tosafot*'s reading that the *istama* served as a support for jewelry, since the fact that this ruling is necessary at all implies that the *istama* was sufficiently jewelry-like that one might have thought it falls under the bridal crown prohibition.

## Slave Seals

### Samuel's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: כַּבְלָא דְעַבְדָּא תְּנַן. וּמִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יוֹצֵא הָעֶבֶד בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבְּצַוָּארוֹ, אֲבָל לֹא בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** Earlier, the Gemara cited Rabbi Abbahu's opinion that the *kavul* mentioned in the mishna, which one may not wear into the public domain on Shabbat, is a woolen cap. **And Shmuel said: It is the seal of a slave** that **we learned** about in the mishna. The Gemara asks: **And did Shmuel** actually **say this? Didn't Shmuel say: A slave may go out** on Shabbat **with a seal that is around his neck but not with a seal that is on his clothes?** Apparently, Shmuel holds that one may go out into the public domain with a slave's seal. How, then, could he say that *kavul* in the mishna, with which one may not go out into the public domain, is referring to the seal of a slave?

*Ve-Shmuel amar: kavla de-avda tenan* — Shmuel holds that the *mishnah*'s *kvul* refers specifically to the slave's identifying collar, not to the wool hat of a woman. The hat, under Shmuel's reading, is not a problem at all — we are only concerned about the slave's seal.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this by citing Shmuel himself: *ve-amar Shmuel: yotzei eved be-chotam she-be-tzavaro aval lo be-chotam she-bi-khsuto* — Shmuel ruled elsewhere that a slave may go out on *Shabbat* with a seal around his neck but not with a seal on his garment. If Shmuel believes the slave's seal is such a concern that it is what our *mishnah* addresses, how can he simultaneously rule that a slave may wear a seal around his neck?

### Master Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דַּעֲבַד לֵיהּ רַבֵּיהּ, הָא דַּעֲבַד אִיהוּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This,** where Shmuel said that one may go out with a slave's seal on Shabbat, **is** referring to a case **where his master made it for him.** The slave will not remove it because he fears his master. Therefore, there is no concern lest he carry it. However, **that,** where the mishna said that it is prohibited to go out with a *kavul*, which according to Shmuel is the seal of a slave, is referring to a case **where he made it for himself** to indicate to all who his master is so that he may enjoy his master's protection. In that case, since it is dependent solely upon his discretion, there is concern lest he remove the seal and carry it. Therefore, the Sages prohibited going out with it into the public domain.

*Lo kashya* — the *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *ha de-avid lei rabei* — when his master affixes the seal, requiring the slave to wear it, we are not afraid the slave will remove it. He is obligated by his master and will not dare take it off. That is the case where wearing around the neck is permitted. *Ha de-avid ihu le-nafshei* — but if the slave himself made the seal of his own accord, without his master's instruction, then we are afraid he might remove it. That is the case that our *mishnah* addresses, and that is the case the slave-seal prohibition concerns.

### Seal Breaking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקִימְתָּא לְהָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל — דַּעֲבַד לֵיהּ רַבֵּיהּ? בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ אַמַּאי לָא! דִּילְמָא מִיפְּסַק, וּמִירְתַת וּמְיקַפֵּל לֵיהּ וּמַחֵית לֵיהּ אַכַּתְפֵּיהּ. כִּדְרַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, דְּאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַיּוֹצֵא בְּטַלִּית מְקֻפֶּלֶת וּמוּנַּחַת לוֹ עַל כְּתֵפָיו בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In what** case **did you establish this** statement **of Shmuel?** It is in the case of a seal **that his master made for him.** If so, **why** may he **not** go out **with a seal on his clothes?** There too, since his master made it for him he will not remove it. The Gemara answers: There the concern is that **perhaps** the seal **will be severed, and** the slave **will fear** his master **and fold** his cloak **and place it on his shoulders** so that his master will not see that he has no seal on his clothing. That concern is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef; as Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who goes out** into the public domain **with a cloak folded and resting on his shoulders on Shabbat is liable to bring a sin-offering.** That is not the manner in which one wears a garment; it is the manner in which one carries a burden.

*Be-mai ukimta le-ha de-Shmuel* — but if the permitted case is when the master requires it, why is even the seal on the garment prohibited in that case? If his master requires him to wear it, he will not remove it from his garment either. *Dilma mifsik ve-mirta* — the concern is that the seal might break off on its own. The seal falls from the garment, and the slave is terrified that his master will think he deliberately removed it. This scenario is strikingly evocative — the fear that a visible marker of identification has been removed, and the consequences of being found without it.

*U-miflei u-machei lei a-katfei* — panicked, he takes off his outer garment and folds it over his shoulder so that no one will see he is wearing it without the seal intact. And carrying a folded garment on one's shoulder is carrying, not wearing. *Ki de-Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef amar Rabbi Yochanan: ha-yotzei be-tallis kefula u-menachat a-katfeihu be-Shabbat chayav chatat* — as Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef cited in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: one who goes out on *Shabbat* with a garment folded and placed on his shoulders is liable as one who carries, not as one who wears. A garment must be worn to be exempt; folded on the shoulder, it is simply a carried object. Therefore, even where the master requires the seal and the slave will not willfully remove it, the seal on the garment remains prohibited, because an inadvertent loss of the seal creates a cascade leading to carrying.

The neck, by contrast, does not raise this concern: if the seal around the neck falls off, there is no garment to remove and fold.

### Resh Galuta Rabbis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב חִינָּנָא בַּר שֵׁילָא: כּוּלְּהוּ רַבָּנַן דְּבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא לָא לִיפְּקוּ בְּסַרְבָּלֵי חֲתִימֵי, לְבַר מִינָּךְ — דְּלָא קָפְדִי עֲלָיךְ דְּבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **like that which Shmuel said to Rav Ḥinnana bar Sheila: All of the Sages** affiliated with **the house of the Exilarch may not go out** on Shabbat **with sealed cloaks [*sarbal*],** i.e., garments with seals on them, **except for you, since** the people **of the Exilarch's house are not particular with** regard to **you.** The Sages affiliated with the Exilarch were officially considered servants of the house and would wear the seal of the house of the Exilarch. Therefore, it was prohibited for them to go out into the public domain on Shabbat with a cloak bearing the Exilarch's seal, lest the seal break and, in fear of the Exilarch, they remove the cloak, fold it, place it on their shoulders, and carry it on Shabbat. Only Rav Ḥinnana bar Sheila was permitted to go out with this seal on Shabbat since the people of the Exilarch's house were not exacting with him. Even if he wore clothing with no seal, they would not consider it an act of insubordination against the Exilarch.

*Ve-chi ha de-amar lei Shmuel le-Rav Chinana bar Shila: kulu rabbanan de-bei resh galuta* — Shmuel said to Rav Chinana bar Shila that all the rabbis of the household of the *Resh Galuta*, the Exilarch, who wore identifying seals or insignia on their garments — *lo lipku be-sarye ve-lo chatime* — may not go out on *Shabbat* with these sealed garments. The reason is the same: if the seal falls off, the rabbit of the Exilarch's court, frantic about being caught without his mark of office, will remove his outer garment and carry it folded on his shoulder rather than be seen without the seal. *Le-bar minach* — but Rav Chinana bar Shila himself is excepted. *De-lo kefidi alach de-bei resh galuta* — the household of the Exilarch does not hold him to strict account; they will not penalize him if his insignia is missing. Since there is no consequence for him, there is no reason he would panic and remove his garment, and the concern of carrying therefore does not apply to him.

The upshot of this entire discussion: on the garment, the concern is not willful removal but the involuntary loss of the seal and the fear-driven response that follows. On the neck, that chain of events does not apply. And only those who face actual accountability to the Exilarch's household are subject to the restriction; those for whom the household will make an exception are personally exempt.
