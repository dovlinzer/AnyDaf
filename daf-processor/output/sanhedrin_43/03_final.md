# Sanhedrin 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Skilah Location

### Skilah Outside Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִגְמַר הַדִּין, מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לְסוֹקְלוֹ. בֵּית הַסְּקִילָה הָיָה חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹצֵא אֶת הַמְקַלֵּל״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: When **the trial has ended** in a guilty verdict and the condemned man has been sentenced to be stoned, **he is taken out to be stoned. The place of stoning was outside the court** and a little beyond it, **as it is stated** with regard to a blasphemer: **"Take out him who has cursed** to outside the camp, and let all that heard him lay their hands upon his head, and let all the congregation stone him" (Leviticus 24:14).

The *mishna* establishes that once a capital verdict has been rendered, the condemned is taken *chutz l'beit din* — outside the court. The *gemara* will discuss precisely what that means geographically. As we noted previously, drawing on the structure of the desert encampment, the *beit hamikdash* (corresponding to the *mishkan*) stands at the center, the *machaneh leviyim* surrounds it, and the *machaneh yisrael* occupies the outermost ring. *Skilah* takes place outside all three of these camps, at the *even hachitzon* — the place of the stone, or, as we will discuss, possibly a single stone.

### Three Camps Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הַסְּקִילָה חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין הֲוָה, וְתוּ לָא? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: בֵּית הַסְּקִילָה הָיָה חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And was the place of stoning** just **outside the court and nothing more?** Does it suffice that the place of execution is only a short distance from the court and no further? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The place of stoning was outside** the distance that is equivalent to the length of the **three camps** in the wilderness: The camp of the Divine Presence, the camp of the Levites, and the camp of the Israelites. In Jerusalem there were three corresponding camps: The Temple with its courtyard, the Temple Mount, and the rest of the city. The distance in the wilderness outside of the three camps corresponds to a place outside the city walls and limits.

This geography of the three camps — *beit hamikdash*, *machaneh leviyim*, and *machaneh yisrael* — provides the structural backdrop for the entire discussion of where capital punishment is carried out. The condemned must be taken beyond the furthest boundary, outside *machaneh yisrael* entirely.

### Rav Pappa's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: מֹשֶׁה הֵיכָא הֲוָה יָתֵיב? בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא: ״הוֹצֵא אֶת הַמְקַלֵּל אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ – חוּץ לְמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה. ״וַיּוֹצִיאוּ אֶת הַמְקַלֵּל אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ – חוּץ לְמַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** The matter of the location of the place of stoning can be derived from the verses themselves: **Where was Moses sitting** when the matter of the blasphemer was brought before him? **In the Levite camp,** as he was a Levite, and it was there that the blasphemer was brought for trial. **And the Merciful One said to** Moses: **"Take out him who has cursed to outside the camp,"** indicating that he should be taken **outside the Levite camp** into the Israelite camp. And later it says: "And Moses spoke to the children of Israel, **and they brought him that had cursed out of the camp,** and they stoned him with a stone. And the children of Israel did as the Lord commanded Moses" (Leviticus 24:23), teaching that he was taken **outside the Israelite camp** as well.

Picking up from the bottom of 42b, after the discussion of *makshir mimakshir*, Rav Pappa offers an independent source for the requirement that *skilah* takes place outside all the camps, drawing from the narrative of the *mekallel* — the blasphemer described in *Vayikra*.

The *gemara* asks: where was Moshe sitting? *B'machaneh leviyah* — in the *machaneh leviyim*, the second concentric ring. When God instructed Moshe, *hotzei et hamekallel el michutz lamachaneh* — take the blasphemer outside the camp — what Moshe understood was that he should take him outside the camp in which Moshe himself was sitting, namely the *machaneh leviyim*, placing the *mekallel* in *machaneh yisrael*. Then *bnei yisrael* subsequently took him further, *michutz lamachaneh* — outside the camp he was now in — which brought him beyond *machaneh yisrael* entirely. That outermost location is where the stoning occurred. This is derived from the verse *vayotziu et hamekallel el michutz lamachaneh* — they took the blasphemer outside the camp — meaning outside *machaneh yisrael*.

### Two Verses Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לַעֲשִׂיָּיה? עֲשִׂיָּיה בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיבָא: ״וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל עָשׂוּ כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּה ה׳ אֶת מֹשֶׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **This** additional verse **is necessary** to teach us **about the implementation** of God's instructions, i.e., that the Jewish people did in fact carry out God's command, but this verse should not be understood as referring to an additional camp. The Gemara answers: **The implementation** of God's instructions **is written explicitly,** as it is stated in the continuation of the verse: **"And the children of Israel did as the Lord commanded Moses."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״וַיִּרְגְּמוּ אֹתוֹ אָבֶן״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? הָהוּא מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וַיִּרְגְּמוּ אֹתוֹ בָּאָבֶן״ – ״אֹתוֹ״ וְלֹא בִּכְסוּתוֹ, ״אָבֶן״ שֶׁאִם מֵת בְּאֶבֶן אַחַת יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, what do they do with** the words in the verse: **"And they stoned him with a stone"?** These words appear to be superfluous, as even without them we would know that God's instructions to stone the blasphemer were implemented. What then do they serve to teach? The Gemara answers: **That** phrase is **necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And they stoned him with a stone."** The word **"him"** teaches that they stoned him alone, while he was naked, **but not** while he was **in his clothing.** The verse uses the singular term **"stone [*aven*]"** rather than the plural term stones [*avanim*] to teach **that if** the condemned man **died** after being struck **with one stone,** the court has **fulfilled** its obligation.

At this point the *gemara* raises an objection. If everything that God commanded Moshe is already accounted for by the verse *u'venei yisrael asu ka'asher tziva Hashem et Moshe* — the *bnei yisrael* did as God commanded Moshe — then we already know they carried out the execution outside the camp. The verse *vayotziu et hamekallel el michutz lamachaneh* would be redundant for the purpose of telling us the action occurred, and therefore it must be teaching us something additional: that they took him specifically outside *machaneh yisrael*, as Rav Pappa derives.

But then the *gemara* notes a further redundancy: what do we do with *vayirgemu oto ba'even* — they stoned him with a stone? If the general verse already tells us they did everything commanded, this verse too is superfluous for establishing the bare fact of execution. The *gemara* answers: *ha mibayei likde'tanya* — this verse is needed for what we learn in the following *baraita*.

## Smicha & Dechiya

### Even vs. Avanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״אָבֶן״, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״אֲבָנִים״. דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אָבֶן״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הֵיכָא דְּלָא מֵת בַּחֲדָא, לָא נַיְתֵי אַחֲרִיתִי וְנִיקְטְלֵיהּ. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֲבָנִים״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֲבָנִים״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מֵעִיקָּרָא נַיְיתֵי תַּרְתֵּי. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אָבֶן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** it **was necessary to write** with regard to the blasphemer that "they stoned him with **a stone,"** in the singular, **and** it **was necessary to write** with regard to the man who gathered sticks on Shabbat that "they stoned him with **stones"** (Numbers 15:36), in the plural. **As, had the Merciful One written** only **"stone," I would say** that **where** the condemned man **did not die** after being struck **with one** stone, **they do not bring other** stones **and kill him** with them. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes "stones." And had the Merciful One written** only **"stones," I would say** that **from the outset they should bring two** or more stones. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes "stone."**

The *baraita* derives two teachings from this verse. *Vayirgemu oto ba'even* — *oto*, "him," and not while he is clothed; and *even*, in the singular, teaches that if he died from a single stone, the obligation has been fulfilled. This raises an immediate question: in two different places the *Torah* describes the stoning of a condemned person — one instance uses *even* (singular) and another uses *avanim* (plural; the parallel verse appears in *Bamidbar*, regarding the *mekoshesh etzim*). Why does the *Torah* use both forms?

The *gemara* explains the necessity of each form. Had the *Torah* written only *even*, I might have thought that if the condemned person does not die from the first stone, no further stone may be brought to complete the execution. Therefore the *Torah* also writes *avanim* in the plural, to teach that additional stones may be brought if needed. Conversely, had the *Torah* written only *avanim*, I might have thought that *lechatchilah*, from the outset, you must bring multiple stones. Therefore the *Torah* also writes *even* to establish that you begin with one stone, and bring more only if the person has not died.

### Both Forms Needed

Both the singular *even* and the plural *avanim* are thus indispensable. The singular establishes the procedure — begin with one stone — while the plural preserves the obligation to complete the execution if the first proves insufficient. Each form addresses a possible misunderstanding that would arise from reading only the other.

### Rav Pappa vs. Tanna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הַאי תַּנָּא ״נֶאֱמַר״ קָאָמַר? ״אִילּוּ לֹא נֶאֱמַר״ קָאָמַר, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אִילּוּ לֹא נֶאֱמַר קְרָא, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה. עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר קְרָא, גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לָא צְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection to Rav Pappa's derivation: **But this *tanna*** of the *baraita* cited above **said: It is stated** here and it is stated elsewhere, thereby basing his derivation on a verbal analogy between the verse concerning the blasphemer and the verse concerning the bulls brought as sin-offerings that are burned. How, then, can Rav Pappa, an *amora*, disagree and derive the *halakha* directly from the verse dealing with the blasphemer? The Gemara answers: According to Rav Pappa, the *tanna* of the *baraita* **said: Had it not been stated, and this** is what he **is saying: Had a verse not been stated** from which it can be directly derived that the condemned man is stoned outside all three camps, **I would have said** that this can be learned by way of **a verbal analogy.** But **now that** such **a verse has been stated,** the **verbal analogy is not needed.**

The *gemara* then steps back and considers a broader difficulty. The teachings Rav Pappa derived from the *mekallel* narrative — that *skilah* takes place outside all three camps — were, at the outset of the *gemara*'s discussion, already established through *gezeirot shavot* by the *tanna'im*: the verbal analogy connecting the *mekallel* to *parim hanisrafim* and to *shochutei chutz*, all of which use the language of *michutz lamachaneh*. If the *tanna'im* already had this derivation, why did Rav Pappa need to introduce his own?

The *gemara* answers: *ne'emar ka'amar* — the *tanna* was not asserting that the *gezeirah shavah* is in fact the operative source. Rather, he was saying: *ilu lo ne'emar kra* — had the more direct verse that Rav Pappa uses not existed, I would have resorted to the *gezeirah shavah*. But *hashta de'ne'emar kra gezeirah shavah lo tzarich* — now that the direct verse exists, the *gezeirah shavah* is unnecessary. The entire exercise in *gezeirah shavah* was thus a kind of *bedi'avad* demonstration: this is what we would have done without the better source. This makes the *sugya* an instructive case study in the methodology of *gezeirah shavah* itself.

## Rav Ashi's View

### Rav Ashi's Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מֹשֶׁה הֵיכָא הֲוָה יָתֵיב? בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא: ״הוֹצֵא אֶת הַמְקַלֵּל״ – חוּץ לְמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה. ״אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ – חוּץ לְמַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל. ״וַיּוֹצִיאוּ אֶת הַמְקַלֵּל״ – לַעֲשִׂיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The location of the place of stoning can be directly derived from the verse discussing the blasphemer but in a slightly different manner. **Where was Moses sitting** when the matter of the blasphemer was brought before him? **In the Levite camp. And the Merciful One said to him: "Take out him who has cursed"** (Leviticus 24:14), indicating that he should be taken **outside the Levite camp** into the Israelite camp. And God continued in that verse: **"Outside the camp,"** which is an additional command that he should be removed even further, to **outside the Israelite camp.** And the later verse, which says: **"And they brought him that had cursed** out of the camp…and the children of Israel did as the Lord commanded Moses" (Leviticus 24:23), teaches us **about the implementation** of God's instructions, i.e., that the children of Israel did in fact carry out His command.

Rav Ashi offers yet another reading of the *mekallel* verses to establish the same geographic requirement. He begins from the same premise: Moshe was sitting in *machaneh leviyim*. God says to him, *hotzei et hamekallel chutz lamachaneh* — take the blasphemer outside the camp — which Rav Ashi understands as referring to *machaneh leviyim*.

Rav Ashi then splits the verse differently from Rav Pappa. He reads *hotzei* as commanding that the *mekallel* be removed from *machaneh leviyim*, while *el michutz lamachaneh* refers to the next boundary — outside *machaneh yisrael*. Thus the single verse itself encodes both stages of removal: out of the inner camp and then out of the outer one.

On this reading, *vayotziu et hamekallel el michutz lamachaneh* — the verse recording that they actually carried out the action — is used simply to teach that the execution took place as commanded, and does not carry the additional geographic teaching that Rav Pappa derived from it.

## Smicha & Dechiya Details

### Unexampled Actions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשִׂיָּיה בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בְּהוּ: ״וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל עָשׂוּ כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּה ה׳ אֶת מֹשֶׁה״. הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, חַד לִסְמִיכָה וְחַד לִדְחִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **The implementation** of God's instructions is **written explicitly in this** context, as it is stated in the continuation of the verse: **"And the children of Israel did as the Lord commanded Moses."** The Gemara answers: **That** verse **is necessary** to teach us that not only was the condemned man taken outside the three camps and stoned, but the rest of God's instructions were also fulfilled. These instructions relate **to the placing** of the witnesses' **hands** upon the head of the condemned man, as it is stated: "And let all that heard him place their hands upon his head" (Leviticus 24:14), **and to the** witnesses' **pushing** of the condemned man from a platform the height of two stories.

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty against Rav Ashi's approach. On his reading, the verse *u'venei yisrael asu ka'asher tziva Hashem et Moshe* — they did as God commanded — must be doing additional work, since *vayotziu* is already accounting for the bare fact that the execution occurred. What does this verse teach?

The *gemara* answers: *ha mibayei leh chad l'smicha v'chad l'dechiya* — one is needed for *smicha*, the laying of hands upon the condemned before execution, and one for *dechiya*, pushing the condemned off from a height.

One might ask: why would we need a verse to establish these actions at all? The answer is that both *smicha* and *dechiya* go beyond what is explicitly commanded. If everything commanded is already accounted for by the general verse, then the additional verse teaches that *bnei yisrael* performed even these additional acts — which requires us to explain what those acts are and where they come from.

### Smicha vs. Dechiya

As for *smicha*, it is in fact stated explicitly in the text: *v'samchu kol hashom'im et yedeihem al rosho* — all those who heard him blaspheme lay their hands upon his head. So *smicha* does appear in the commandment.

*Dechiya*, however, is not stated explicitly in the execution command at all. Where does it come from? The *gemara* answers: *dechiya lav hacha ksiva ela misinai* — it is not written here in the text but is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, derived from *yareh yareh*, the phrase in *Har Sinai* that indicates either stoning or shooting — *o sakol yisakeil o yareh yareh*. Chazal understand *yareh yareh* as teaching that one of the witnesses comes and pushes the condemned off the *beit haskilah*.

### Beit Skilah Structure

The *beit haskilah* — the stoning structure — is two stories high, *gavoha shtei komot*. One of the witnesses comes and pushes the condemned off the top, so that he falls to the ground below. This is the *dechiya*. If he does not die from the fall, then the stoning with *even* or *avanim* proceeds as described.

The *gemara* thus groups *smicha* and *dechiya* together because both represent actions that, while either stated or derived elsewhere, required confirmation that *bnei yisrael* actually performed them in the case of the *mekallel*. The general verse *u'venei yisrael asu ka'asher tziva Hashem et Moshe* provides that confirmation: they did indeed perform *smicha* and *dechiya* just as required.

### Hotzei Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ רַבָּנַן לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לְדִידָךְ, כֹּל הָנֵי ״הוֹצִיא״ דִּכְתִיבִי בְּפָרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים, מַאי דָּרְשַׁתְּ בְּהוּ? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Rav Ashi: According to you,** that the expression "take out" by itself means outside the camp, and "outside the camp" means outside an additional camp, **what do you learn from all those** instances of **"take out" that are written with regard to the bulls** brought as sin-offerings **that are burned?** According to your explanation, there are many superfluous phrases in the verses. The Gemara comments: Indeed, this is **difficult** with regard to the opinion of Rav Ashi.

The *rabbanim* raise an objection against Rav Ashi. If he is reading *hotzei* in the *mekallel* narrative as implying *dechiya* or some similar additional content, then in every case in the *Torah* where the word *hotzei* appears — including the passages concerning *parim hanisrafim*, the burned cattle — one would have to derive a *derasha* from it as well. The *gemara* concludes: *kashya* — this is indeed a strong difficulty, and Rav Ashi's reading is apparently not accepted.

## Communal Ownership

### Rav Huna's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד עוֹמֵד כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי, אֶחָד אֶבֶן שֶׁנִּסְקָל בָּהּ, וְאֶחָד עֵץ שֶׁנִּתְלֶה בּוֹ, וְאֶחָד סַיִיף שֶׁנֶּהֱרָג בּוֹ, וְאֶחָד סוּדָר שֶׁנֶּחְנָק בּוֹ – כּוּלָּן מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּמִדִּידֵיהּ לָא אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: ״זִיל וְלֵיתֵיהּ וְלִיקְטוֹל נַפְשֵׁיהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **one** man **stands** at the entrance to the court, with cloths in his hand, ready to signal to the court agents leading the condemned man to his execution that some doubt has been raised with respect to the latter's guilt. **Rav Huna says:** It is **obvious to me** that **the stone with which** the condemned man **is stoned and the tree on which** his corpse **is hung** after his execution, **or the sword with which he is killed, or the scarf with which he is strangled, all of these** come **from** the property of **the community. What is the reason** for this? **We do not tell** the condemned man to **go and bring** these items **from his own** property **and** effectively **kill himself.**

The *mishna* introduces the role of the person who stands at the entrance of the *beit din* with *sudarin* — signal flags — in hand, ready to wave them if an appeal for acquittal is raised while the condemned is being led out. Rav Huna then teaches a broader principle: it is obvious to me (*peshita li*) that the stone used for *skilah*, the tree used for hanging, the sword used for execution by *sayif*, and the cloth used for *chenek* (strangulation) must all belong to the *tzibur* — the community.

The reason is straightforward: we do not say to the condemned, *zil v'aytei veniktal nafshech* — go fetch your own stone and we will kill you with it. The instruments of execution are not the condemned person's obligation to provide. They belong to the community, whose court has decreed the execution.

### Rationale

Rav Huna then raises a question about two other items. The *sudar* used to signal — waved to halt the procession if an appeal is raised — and the horse kept ready to ride out and stop the execution if needed: whose property are these? On one hand, since both exist for the purpose of potentially saving the condemned person's life, perhaps they should be his responsibility to provide. On the other hand, since the *beit din* is obligated to halt the execution if a valid appeal arises, perhaps the *beit din* — and thus the *tzibur* — bears this responsibility. The *gemara* leaves this as an unresolved question.

### Frankincense Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַיּוֹצֵא לֵיהָרֵג מַשְׁקִין אוֹתוֹ קוֹרֶט שֶׁל לְבוֹנָה בְּכוֹס שֶׁל יַיִן כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּטָּרֵף דַּעְתּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּנוּ שֵׁכָר לְאוֹבֵד וְיַיִן לְמָרֵי נָפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** another question is raised along similar lines: With regard to **that which Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi says** that **Rav Ḥisda says:** The court **gives one who is being led out to be killed a grain [*koret*] of frankincense in a cup of wine in order to confuse his mind** and thereby minimize his suffering from the fear of his impending death, **as it is stated: "Give strong drink to him that is ready to perish, and wine to the bitter in soul"** (Proverbs 31:6).

Rabbi Chiyya bar Rav Ashi in the name of Rav Chisda teaches: *hayotzei lahareg mashkin oto koret shel levonah bekos shel yayin* — the person being led out to execution is given *levonah* (frankincense) mixed into a cup of wine. The purpose is *kedei shetitaref da'ato* — so that his mind becomes confused and disordered. As Rashi explains, *titaref da'ato velo yidag veyitmahmeha baharigato* — his mind is unsettled and he does not fully comprehend what is happening to him, sparing him the full psychological anguish of his execution. The scriptural basis offered is the verse, *tenu shechar le'oved veyayin lemarei nafesh* — give strong drink to one who is lost and wine to one who is bitter of soul.

### Women Volunteers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: נָשִׁים יְקָרוֹת שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם הָיוּ מִתְנַדְּבוֹת וּמְבִיאוֹת אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The prominent women of Jerusalem would donate** this drink **and bring** it to those being led out to be killed.

A *baraita* records that the distinguished women of Jerusalem — *nashim yekarot shebiyerushalayim* — used to voluntarily prepare and bring this mixture of their own accord, as an act of communal *chessed* toward those condemned.

### Tzibur Provides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִתְנַדְּבוּ נָשִׁים יְקָרוֹת, מִשֶּׁל מִי? הָא וַדַּאי מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר, כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיב ״תְּנוּ״ – מִדִּידְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The question is: If **these prominent women did not donate** this drink, **from whom** is it taken? The Gemara answers: With regard to **this** question, it **is certainly reasonable** that this drink should be taken **from the community, as it is written: "Give [*tenu*]** strong drink," in the plural, indicating that it should come **from them,** the community.

This prompts the question: if the precious women of Jerusalem did not volunteer, from whose resources should the mixture come? The *gemara* answers that it is clearly reasonable it should come from the *tzibur*, drawing on the fact that the scriptural verse uses the plural command *tenu* — "give" — indicating a communal obligation.

## Appeals & Silence

### Appeal Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״יֵשׁ לִי לְלַמֵּד עַל עַצְמִי זְכוּת״, מַחְזִירִין אוֹתוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ פְּעָמִים, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיֵּשׁ מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** And **even** if **he,** the condemned man himself, says: I can teach a reason to acquit myself, he is returned to the courthouse **even four or five times, provided that there is substance to his words.**

The *mishna* establishes that the condemned person may appeal his own sentence — *afillu hu omer yesh li l'lamed al atzmi zechut* — even he himself can claim to have a *limud zechut*, and he is brought back, even four or five times, *u'bilvad sheyesh mamash bidvarav* — provided there is substance to what he says.

### Silent Student

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר הוּנָא מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אָמַר אֶחָד מִן הַתַּלְמִידִים ״יֵשׁ לִי לְלַמֵּד עָלָיו זְכוּת״, וְנִשְׁתַּתֵּק, מַהוּ? מְנַפַּח רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בִּידֵיהּ. נִשְׁתַּתֵּק? אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם נָמֵי! הָתָם לָא קָאָמַר, הָכָא קָאָמַר. מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Aḥa bar Huna asked Rav Sheshet:** If **one of the students** sitting before the judges **said: I can teach** a reason to **acquit him, and he became mute** and cannot explain himself, **what is** the *halakha* in such a case? Does the court take heed of his words, or do they disregard him? **Rav Sheshet waved his hands** in scorn and said: If the student **became mute,** the court certainly does not pay attention to him, as were the court to concern themselves with what he said, they would have to be concerned **even** that perhaps there is **someone at the end of the world** who can propose an argument in the condemned man's favor. The Gemara rejects this argument: The cases are not similar. **There, no one said** that he had a reason to acquit the condemned man. **Here,** the student already **said** that he had a reason to acquit the condemned man. The question, therefore, is appropriate. **What** is the *halakha* in such a case?

This gives rise to the question posed by Rav Acha bar Huna to Rav Sheshet: what if one of the students says he has a *limud zechut* on the condemned and then goes silent — *nishtatek*? Rashi explains *nishtatek* as *ne'elam*, meaning he forgot what he was going to say. He got as far as declaring that he had an appeal but could not articulate it. Is the signal flag waved and the procession halted?

Rav Sheshet's response is pointed: he waved his hand dismissively, indicating *ruach hu zeh she'amarta* — it is merely blowing wind. *Keivan denishtatek mah ho'il* — since he went silent, what purpose does his initial claim serve? An appeal that cannot be stated is no appeal at all.

### World-Wide Objection

The *gemara* then raises a challenge to this position. If we were to treat a claim that goes silent as sufficient cause to halt proceedings, we should logically worry about everyone in the world: *afilu echad besof ha'olam nami* — perhaps someone at the ends of the earth knows a *limud zechut* on him. If the mere possibility of an unvoiced appeal is grounds for delay, no execution could ever proceed.

### Claims Only Count

The resolution is that we care only about claims actually made. *Hatam lo ka'amar hacha ka'amar* — there, nobody has said anything; here, this person did say he had an appeal. But the point of the objection stands as a reductio: the reason we do not halt proceedings for the person who *might* have a claim is that no claim has been made. By the same logic, the student who said he had an appeal but then went silent has not actually made a claim in any actionable sense. His initial statement, unaccompanied by any substantive content, is treated as nothing.

## Student Dies Mid-Appeal

### Completed Appeal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: אֶחָד מִן הַתַּלְמִידִים שֶׁזִּיכָּה וָמֵת, רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ חַי וְעוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹמוֹ. זִיכָּה – אִין, לֹא זִיכָּה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an answer: **As Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says:** In a case where there was **one of the students who** argued to **acquit** the defendant **and** then **died,** the court **views him as if** he were **alive and standing in his place** and voting to acquit the defendant. The implication is that if **he** argued to **acquit** the defendant and explained his reasoning, **yes,** the court counts his vote as if he were still alive. But if **he did not** actually argue to **acquit** the defendant, but only said that he wished to propose such an argument, his vote is **not** counted as though he were still alive.

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina teaches: if one of the students articulated a *limud zechut* — *shezika*, meaning he completed his appeal — and then died before the court could vote on it, *ro'in oto ke'ilu chai v'omed bimkomo* — we regard him as though he were alive and standing in his place.

Practically, this means we substitute another person to occupy his position for the vote. The deceased student's completed *limud zechut* retains its standing; his death does not invalidate what he articulated. A replacement voter is seated and the deliberation proceeds as if the original student were present.

### Substitute Voting

This section makes clear the direct connection back to Rav Sheshet's ruling: a student who said only that he had an appeal and then fell silent — or died before articulating anything — is treated identically. The bare statement of intention, unaccompanied by substantive argument, generates no procedural consequence.

### Censored Text

At this point in the text, there is a section that was removed by censors. The printed versions available to us preserve only a truncated *gemara*, though other printings make clear that the censored passage deals with Jesus and the eve of Passover. We will return to this if time permits.

### Unfinished Appeal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִיכָּה – פְּשִׁיטָא לִי; אָמַר – תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: If the student **argued** to **acquit** the defendant, it is **obvious to me** that he should be counted among those favoring acquittal. But if he only **says** that he wishes to propose such an argument, **let the dilemma be raised** whether or not he should be regarded as having presented a convincing argument in favor of acquittal. The question is left unresolved.

The *gemara* then distinguishes: *zika in, lo zika lo* — if he completed his *limud zechut*, yes, we place someone in his stead; if he did not complete it, no, we do not. This creates the direct connection back to Rav Sheshet's ruling: a student who said only that he had an appeal and then fell silent — or died before articulating anything — is treated identically. The bare statement of intention, unaccompanied by substantive argument, generates no procedural consequence. *Zika peshita li* — if he completed it, that is obvious; *lo zika tiba'i lach* — the question is specifically about the case where he did not complete it.

## Appeals & Silence (II)

### Baraita Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה? וְהָתַנְיָא: פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה, בֵּין שֶׁיֵּשׁ מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו בֵּין שֶׁאֵין מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו – מַחְזִירִין אוֹתוֹ. מִכָּאן וָאֵילָךְ, אִם יֵשׁ מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו – מַחְזִירִין אוֹתוֹ, אֵין מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו – אֵין מַחְזִירִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is the *halakha* that there must be substance to his words **even the first and second time** that the condemned man says that he can teach a reason to acquit himself? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The first and second times** that he says that he can teach a reason to acquit himself, **they return him** to the courthouse and consider **whether there is substance to his statement or there is no substance to his statement. From this** point **forward, if there is substance to his statement they return him** to the courthouse, but if **there is no substance to his statement, they do not return him.** This appears to contradict the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: תַּרְגּוּמַהּ מִפַּעַם שְׁנִיָּה וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Explain** that the mishna's ruling applies only **from** after **the second time forward,** that from that point on we examine whether there is substance to his words.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא יָדְעִי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דְּמָסְרִינַן לֵיהּ זוּגָא דְּרַבָּנַן. אִי אִיכָּא מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How do we know** whether or not there is substance to his words? **Abaye said:** If the condemned man has already been returned twice to the courthouse, **we send a pair of rabbis with him** to evaluate his claim. **If** they find that **there is substance to his statement, yes,** he is returned once again to the courthouse; **if not,** he is **not** returned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִימְסַר לֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא? אַגַּב דִּבְעִית, לָא מָצֵי אָמַר כֹּל מַאי דְּאִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **send** a pair of rabbis **with him from the outset,** even the first time, and have them make an initial assessment of his claim? The Gemara answers: **Since** a man facing execution **is frightened** by the thought of his impending death, **he is not able to say all that he has** to say, and perhaps out of fear he will be confused and not provide a substantial reason to overturn his verdict. Therefore, the first two times he is returned to the courthouse without an initial examination of his arguments. Once he has already been returned on two occasions, the court allows for no further delay, and they send two rabbis to evaluate his claim before returning him a third time.

Rav Pappa explains the apparent tension between our *mishna* — which says the condemned may be brought back even four or five times *provided there is substance to his claim* — and the *baraita*, which states that the first and second time he claims to have an appeal, he is brought back *whether or not* there is substance to his words. Rav Pappa resolves this by explaining that our *mishna* is speaking from the second or third time onward. The requirement of *mamash bidvarav* — substantive content — applies only from the third appeal onward; the first two times, the court brings him back unconditionally.

Abaye adds a practical dimension: from the third time onward, how does the court determine whether there is *mamashut* to the claim? A pair of rabbis accompanies the condemned and listens to what he intends to say. *Ei ika mamash bidvarav in, ei lo lo* — if there is substance, he is brought back; if not, he is not. One might ask why this procedure is not applied from the very first appeal. The *gemara* answers: *agav d'ba'it lei* — because the condemned is frightened and may not be able to fully articulate his claim under that duress, the court gives him unconditional latitude for the first two attempts.

## Pre-Execution Announcement

### Announcer Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָצְאוּ לוֹ זְכוּת – פְּטָרוּהוּ, וְאִם לָאו – יוֹצֵא לִיסָּקֵל. וְכָרוֹז יוֹצֵא לְפָנָיו: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי בֶּן פְּלוֹנִי יוֹצֵא לִיסָּקֵל עַל שֶׁעָבַר עֲבֵירָה פְּלוֹנִית, וּפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי עֵדָיו. כׇּל מִי שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לוֹ זְכוּת יָבוֹא וִילַמֵּד עָלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If, after the condemned man is returned to the courthouse, the judges **find** a reason to **acquit him, they** acquit him and **release him** immediately. **But if** they do **not** find a reason to acquit him, **he goes out to be stoned. And a crier goes out before him** and publicly proclaims: **So-and-so, son of so-and-so, is going out to be stoned because he committed such and such a transgression. And so-and-so and so-and-so are his witnesses. Anyone who knows** of a reason to **acquit him should come** forward **and teach** it **on his behalf.**

A new *mishna* teaches: *matzu lo zechut pat'ruhu* — if a meritorious claim is found, he is acquitted; if not, he is brought out to be stoned. And *charoz yotzei lefanav* — an announcer goes out before him, publicly declaring: *ish ploni ben ploni yotzei lisakel* — so-and-so is being brought out to be stoned — *al she'avar aveira ploni* — for having committed such-and-such a transgression — *u'ploni u'ploni edav* — and so-and-so are his witnesses. The announcement concludes with an open invitation: *kol mi sheyodea lo zechut yavo vyelamed alav* — anyone who knows a reason to acquit him should come forward and do so.

### Details Announced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְצָרִיךְ לְמֵימַר בְּיוֹם פְּלוֹנִי, וּבְשָׁעָה פְּלוֹנִית, וּבְמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי. דִּילְמָא אִיכָּא דְּיָדַעי וְאָתֵי וּמַזֵּים לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Abaye says: And** the crier **must** also publicly **proclaim** that the transgression was committed **on such and such a day, at such and such an hour, and at such and such a place,** as **perhaps there are those who know** that the witnesses could not have been in that place at that time, **and they will come** forward **and render** the witnesses **conspiring witnesses.**

Abaye adds that the announcer must also specify the day, the hour, and the place where the transgression occurred: *b'yom ploni u'v'sha'ah ploni u'v'makom ploni*. The reason is practical and procedurally significant — *dilma ika did'u v'atu u'mazmin lei* — perhaps there are those who know that the named witnesses were not actually present at that location at that time, and can come forward to render them *edim zomemin*, conspiring witnesses, thereby invalidating their testimony entirely.

### Why Not Earlier?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכָרוֹז יוֹצֵא לְפָנָיו. לְפָנָיו – אִין, מֵעִיקָּרָא – לָא. וְהָתַנְיָא: בְּעֶרֶב הַפֶּסַח תְּלָאוּהוּ לְיֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי, וְהַכָּרוֹז יוֹצֵא לְפָנָיו אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם: ״יֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי יוֹצֵא לִיסָּקֵל עַל שֶׁכִּישֵּׁף וְהֵסִית וְהִדִּיחַ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל. כׇּל מִי שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לוֹ זְכוּת יָבוֹא וִילַמֵּד עָלָיו״. וְלֹא מָצְאוּ לוֹ זְכוּת, וּתְלָאוּהוּ בְּעֶרֶב הַפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **a crier goes out before** the condemned man. This indicates that it is only **before him,** i.e., while he is being led to his execution, that **yes,** the crier goes out, but **from the outset,** before the accused is convicted, he does **not** go out. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **On Passover Eve they hung** the corpse of **Jesus the Nazarene** after they killed him by way of stoning. **And a crier went out before him** for **forty days,** publicly proclaiming: **Jesus the Nazarene is going out to be stoned because he practiced sorcery, incited** people to idol worship, **and led the Jewish people astray. Anyone who knows** of a reason to **acquit him should come** forward **and teach** it **on his behalf. And** the court **did not find** a reason to **acquit him, and** so **they** stoned him and **hung his** corpse **on Passover eve.**

The *gemara* then raises what should be an obvious question: the *mishna* says the announcer goes out *before him* — in front of the condemned as he is being led to execution. But does this mean that from the beginning of the trial, no such announcement was made? Surely it would make more sense to publicize the proceedings from the outset, giving potential witnesses or appellants the maximum time to come forward.

The *gemara* does not answer this question in our printed text, because this is precisely where the censor intervened. The passage continues with a *baraita*: *v'ha tanya b'erev hapesach taluhu l'Yeshu* — but we learn in a *baraita* that on *erev Pesach* they hanged Jesus. The *gemara* proceeds from there, but the text in standard printed editions is cut off. One printed version notes explicitly that what follows here was removed by the censors and added according to the Venetian printing. The *baraita* about Jesus appears to have been the *gemara*'s response to the question of why no earlier announcement was made — the implication being that in that case, announcements *were* in fact made over an extended period beforehand. We will return to this passage if time allows.

## Confession Before Death

### Yetzer Sacrifice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל הַזּוֹבֵחַ אֶת יִצְרוֹ וּמִתְוַדֶּה עָלָיו, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ כִּיבְּדוֹ לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בִּשְׁנֵי עוֹלָמִים – הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה וְהָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״זֹבֵחַ תּוֹדָה יְכַבְּדָנְנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the last verse cited in this *baraita*, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** With regard to **anyone who slaughters his** evil **inclination** after it has tempted him to sin, if he repents **and confesses** his sin, **the verse ascribes him** credit **as though he had honored the Holy One, Blessed be He, in two worlds, this world and the World-to-Come, as it is written: "Whoever slaughters a thanks-offering [*toda*] honors Me [*yekhabdaneni*]"** (Psalms 50:23), which can also be read as: Whoever slaughters his evil inclination and confesses [*mitvadeh*] honors Me, and the two instances of the letter *nun* in the word *yekhabdaneni* allude to the two worlds.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches: *kol hazove'ach et yitzro u'mitvadeh alav ma'aleh alav hakatuv ke'ilu kibdo l'Hakadosh Baruch Hu bishnei olamim* — anyone who slaughters his *yetzer hara* and confesses upon it, the verse accounts it as though he honored God in both worlds, this world and the world to come. The *drashah* is drawn from the verse *zove'ach todah yechabdaneni* — he who offers a *todah* sacrifice honors Me — and the word *todah* shares its root with *mitvadeh*, to confess. The double *nun* in *yechabdaneni*, where the simpler form *yechabdani* would have sufficed, yields one *nun* for *olam hazeh* and one for *olam haba*.

### Sacrifices & Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּם, אָדָם מַקְרִיב עוֹלָה – שְׂכַר עוֹלָה בְּיָדוֹ, מִנְחָה – שְׂכַר מִנְחָה בְּיָדוֹ. אֲבָל מִי שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ שְׁפָלָה, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ הִקְרִיב כׇּל הַקָּרְבָּנוֹת כּוּלָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זִבְחֵי אֱלֹהִים רוּחַ נִשְׁבָּרָה״. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין תְּפִלָּתוֹ נִמְאֶסֶת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֵב נִשְׁבָּר וְנִדְכֶּה אֱלֹהִים לֹא תִבְזֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** also **says: When the Temple is standing,** if **a person sacrifices a burnt-offering, he has the reward** given **for** bringing **a burnt-offering,** and if he sacrifices **a meal-offering, he has the reward** given **for** bringing **a meal-offering. But** as for **one whose spirit is humble, the verse ascribes him** credit for his prayer **as though he has sacrificed all the offerings, as it is stated: "The offerings of God are a broken spirit"** (Psalms 51:19), which teaches that a broken spirit is equivalent to the offerings to God, in the plural. **And moreover, his prayer is not rejected, as it is stated** in the continuation of that verse: **"A broken and contrite heart, God, You will not despise."**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi continues: when the *beit hamikdash* stood, a person who brought an *olah* received reward for the *olah*, and one who brought a *minchah* received reward for the *minchah*.

### Lowered Mind

But now — *aval mi sheda'ato shiflah* — whoever lowers his mind and humbles himself, *ma'aleh alav hakatuv ke'ilu hikriv kol hakorbanot kulan* — the verse accounts it as though he offered all the sacrifices combined. The prooftext is *zivchei Elohim ruach nishbarah* — the sacrifices of God are a broken spirit. And moreover, his prayer is not rejected: *lev nishbar venidkeh Elohim lo tivzeh* — a broken and contrite heart, O God, You will not despise.

### Ten Amot Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיָה רָחוֹק מִבֵּית הַסְּקִילָה כְּעֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת, אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: ״הִתְוַדֵּה״, שֶׁכֵּן דֶּרֶךְ כׇּל הַמּוּמָתִין מִתְוַדִּין. שֶׁכׇּל הַמִּתְוַדֶּה יֵשׁ לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּעָכָן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: ״בְּנִי שִׂים נָא כָבוֹד לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְתֶן לוֹ תוֹדָה״. ״וַיַּעַן עָכָן אֶת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וַיֹּאמַר אׇמְנָה אָנֹכִי חָטָאתִי וְכָזֹאת וְכָזֹאת וְגוֹ׳״. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁכִּיפֵּר לוֹ וִידּוּיוֹ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מֶה עֲכַרְתָּנוּ יַעְכֳּרְךָ ה׳ בַּיּוֹם הַזֶּה״. בְּיוֹם הַזֶּה אַתָּה עָכוּר, וְאִי אַתָּה עָכוּר לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **When** the condemned man **is at a distance of about ten cubits from the place of stoning, they say to him: Confess** your transgressions, **as the way of all who are being executed is to confess. As whoever confesses** and regrets his transgressions **has a portion in the World-to-Come. For so we find with regard to Achan, that Joshua said to him: "My son, please give glory to the Lord, God of Israel, and make confession to Him"** (Joshua 7:19). And the next verse states: **"And Achan answered Joshua, and said: Indeed I have sinned against the Lord, God of Israel, and like this and like that** have I done." **And from where** is it derived **that** Achan's **confession achieved atonement for him?** It is derived from here, **as it is stated: "And Joshua said: Why have you brought trouble on us? The Lord shall trouble you this day"** (Joshua 7:25). Joshua said to Achan as follows: **On this day** of your judgment **you are troubled, but you will not be troubled in the World-to-Come.**

The *mishna* now addresses the procedure immediately before execution. When the condemned is approximately ten *amot* from the *beit haskilah*, they say to him: confess your sins — *hitvadeh* — for it is the practice of all those who are put to death to confess, and anyone who confesses has a portion in the world to come.

The basis for this is the case of Achan, who took consecrated property from Yericho in violation of the *cherem*, committing *me'ilah*. Yehoshua said to him before his execution: *b'ni sim na kavod la'Hashem Elohei Yisrael viten lo todah* — my son, please give honor to the God of Israel and confess. Achan responded: *omnah anochi chatati v'chazot v'chazot* — truly I have sinned, and he confessed in detail. Yehoshua then said to him: *bayom hazeh ata achur v'ee ata achur l'olam haba* — today you are *achur* (the opposite of *zach*, pure — murky, blemished, unclean), but in the world to come you are not *achur*. The confessed sin was forgiven and his portion in the world to come was secured.

### Default Confession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ לְהִתְוַודּוֹ, אוֹמְרִים לוֹ, אֱמוֹר: ״תְּהֵא מִיתָתִי כַּפָּרָה עַל כׇּל עֲוֹנוֹתַי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the condemned man **does not know how to confess,** either from ignorance or out of confusion, **they say to him: Say** simply: **Let my death be an atonement for all my sins.**

For someone who does not know how to formulate a confession, the *mishna* prescribes a simple formula: *emor tehei mitati kapara al kol avonotai* — say, let my death serve as atonement for all of my sins.

### Innocent Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא מְזוּמָּם, אוֹמֵר: ״תְּהֵא מִיתָתִי כַּפָּרָה עַל כׇּל עֲוֹנוֹתַי חוּץ מֵעָוֹן זֶה״. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אִם כֵּן, יְהוּ כׇּל אָדָם אוֹמְרִין כֵּן כְּדֵי לְנַקּוֹת עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: If** the condemned man **knows that he was convicted by the testimony of conspiring witnesses,** but in fact he is innocent, **he says: Let my death be an atonement for all my sins except for this sin.** The Sages who disagreed with Rabbi Yehuda **said to him: If so, every person** who is being executed **will say that, to clear himself** in the eyes of the public. Therefore, if the condemned man does not make such a statement on his own, the court does not suggest it to him as an alternative.

Rabbi Yehuda adds a qualification: if the condemned person knows that he is *mezumam* — that he is innocent of this particular charge — he should say: *tehei mitati kapara al kol avonotai chutz me'avon zeh* — let my death be atonement for all my sins, except for this one.

### Innocence Claim

The *rabbanim* object to Rabbi Yehuda's position: *im kein yehu kol adam omer kein kedei l'nakot atzmo* — if that is the case, everyone will say this in order to appear clean before the public after their death. The force of this objection is striking. The *rabbanim* are effectively assuming that a person facing death will prioritize his posthumous reputation over the *kapara* that confession and death together could provide. One who confesses knows his death will atone for his sins; yet *rabbanim* predict that he will deny the sin anyway, apparently caring more about how he is perceived than about the spiritual benefit of honest confession. Whether this reflects a concern about wrongful conviction — the truly innocent claiming innocence — or a more cynical reading of human nature in extremis, is itself a matter worth reflection.
