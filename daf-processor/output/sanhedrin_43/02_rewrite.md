# Sanhedrin 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Skilah Location

### Skilah Outside Beit Din

The *mishna* establishes that once a capital verdict has been rendered, the condemned is taken *chutz l'beit din* — outside the court. The *gemara* will discuss precisely what that means geographically. As we noted previously, drawing on the structure of the desert encampment, the *beit hamikdash* (corresponding to the *mishkan*) stands at the center, the *machaneh leviyim* surrounds it, and the *machaneh yisrael* occupies the outermost ring. *Skilah* takes place outside all three of these camps, at the *even hachitzon* — the place of the stone, or, as we will discuss, possibly a single stone.

### Three Camps Structure

This geography of the three camps — *beit hamikdash*, *machaneh leviyim*, and *machaneh yisrael* — provides the structural backdrop for the entire discussion of where capital punishment is carried out. The condemned must be taken beyond the furthest boundary, outside *machaneh yisrael* entirely.

### Rav Pappa's Source

Picking up from the bottom of 42b, after the discussion of *makshir mimakshir*, Rav Pappa offers an independent source for the requirement that *skilah* takes place outside all the camps, drawing from the narrative of the *mekallel* — the blasphemer described in *Vayikra*.

The *gemara* asks: where was Moshe sitting? *B'machaneh leviyah* — in the *machaneh leviyim*, the second concentric ring. When God instructed Moshe, *hotzei et hamekallel el michutz lamachaneh* — take the blasphemer outside the camp — what Moshe understood was that he should take him outside the camp in which Moshe himself was sitting, namely the *machaneh leviyim*, placing the *mekallel* in *machaneh yisrael*. Then *bnei yisrael* subsequently took him further, *michutz lamachaneh* — outside the camp he was now in — which brought him beyond *machaneh yisrael* entirely. That outermost location is where the stoning occurred. This is derived from the verse *vayotziu et hamekallel el michutz lamachaneh* — they took the blasphemer outside the camp — meaning outside *machaneh yisrael*.

### Two Verses Analysis

At this point the *gemara* raises an objection. If everything that God commanded Moshe is already accounted for by the verse *u'venei yisrael asu ka'asher tziva Hashem et Moshe* — the *bnei yisrael* did as God commanded Moshe — then we already know they carried out the execution outside the camp. The verse *vayotziu et hamekallel el michutz lamachaneh* would be redundant for the purpose of telling us the action occurred, and therefore it must be teaching us something additional: that they took him specifically outside *machaneh yisrael*, as Rav Pappa derives.

But then the *gemara* notes a further redundancy: what do we do with *vayirgemu oto ba'even* — they stoned him with a stone? If the general verse already tells us they did everything commanded, this verse too is superfluous for establishing the bare fact of execution. The *gemara* answers: *ha mibayei likde'tanya* — this verse is needed for what we learn in the following *baraita*.

## Smicha & Dechiya

### Even vs. Avanim

The *baraita* derives two teachings from this verse. *Vayirgemu oto ba'even* — *oto*, "him," and not while he is clothed; and *even*, in the singular, teaches that if he died from a single stone, the obligation has been fulfilled. This raises an immediate question: in two different places the *Torah* describes the stoning of a condemned person — one instance uses *even* (singular) and another uses *avanim* (plural; the parallel verse appears in *Bamidbar*, regarding the *mekoshesh etzim*). Why does the *Torah* use both forms?

### One Stone Teaching

The *gemara* explains the necessity of each form. Had the *Torah* written only *even*, I might have thought that if the condemned person does not die from the first stone, no further stone may be brought to complete the execution. Therefore the *Torah* also writes *avanim* in the plural, to teach that additional stones may be brought if needed. Conversely, had the *Torah* written only *avanim*, I might have thought that *lechatchilah*, from the outset, you must bring multiple stones. Therefore the *Torah* also writes *even* to establish that you begin with one stone, and bring more only if the person has not died.

### Both Forms Needed

Both the singular *even* and the plural *avanim* are thus indispensable. The singular establishes the procedure — begin with one stone — while the plural preserves the obligation to complete the execution if the first proves insufficient. Each form addresses a possible misunderstanding that would arise from reading only the other.

### Rav Pappa vs. Tanna

The *gemara* then steps back and considers a broader difficulty. The teachings Rav Pappa derived from the *mekallel* narrative — that *skilah* takes place outside all three camps — were, at the outset of the *gemara*'s discussion, already established through *gezeirot shavot* by the *tanna'im*: the verbal analogy connecting the *mekallel* to *parim hanisrafim* and to *shochutei chutz*, all of which use the language of *michutz lamachaneh*. If the *tanna'im* already had this derivation, why did Rav Pappa need to introduce his own?

The *gemara* answers: *ne'emar ka'amar* — the *tanna* was not asserting that the *gezeirah shavah* is in fact the operative source. Rather, he was saying: *ilu lo ne'emar kra* — had the more direct verse that Rav Pappa uses not existed, I would have resorted to the *gezeirah shavah*. But *hashta de'ne'emar kra gezeirah shavah lo tzarich* — now that the direct verse exists, the *gezeirah shavah* is unnecessary. The entire exercise in *gezeirah shavah* was thus a kind of *bedi'avad* demonstration: this is what we would have done without the better source. This makes the *sugya* an instructive case study in the methodology of *gezeirah shavah* itself.

## Rav Ashi's View

### Rav Ashi's Setup

Rav Ashi offers yet another reading of the *mekallel* verses to establish the same geographic requirement. He begins from the same premise: Moshe was sitting in *machaneh leviyim*. God says to him, *hotzei et hamekallel chutz lamachaneh* — take the blasphemer outside the camp — which Rav Ashi understands as referring to *machaneh leviyim*.

### Verse Splitting

Rav Ashi then splits the verse differently from Rav Pappa. He reads *hotzei* as commanding that the *mekallel* be removed from *machaneh leviyim*, while *el michutz lamachaneh* refers to the next boundary — outside *machaneh yisrael*. Thus the single verse itself encodes both stages of removal: out of the inner camp and then out of the outer one.

### Action Occurred

On this reading, *vayotziu et hamekallel el michutz lamachaneh* — the verse recording that they actually carried out the action — is used simply to teach that the execution took place as commanded, and does not carry the additional geographic teaching that Rav Pappa derived from it.

## Smicha & Dechiya Details

### Unexampled Actions

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty against Rav Ashi's approach. On his reading, the verse *u'venei yisrael asu ka'asher tziva Hashem et Moshe* — they did as God commanded — must be doing additional work, since *vayotziu* is already accounting for the bare fact that the execution occurred. What does this verse teach?

The *gemara* answers: *ha mibayei leh chad l'smicha v'chad l'dechiya* — one is needed for *smicha*, the laying of hands upon the condemned before execution, and one for *dechiya*, pushing the condemned off from a height.

One might ask: why would we need a verse to establish these actions at all? The answer is that both *smicha* and *dechiya* go beyond what is explicitly commanded. If everything commanded is already accounted for by the general verse, then the additional verse teaches that *bnei yisrael* performed even these additional acts — which requires us to explain what those acts are and where they come from.

### Smicha vs. Dechiya

As for *smicha*, it is in fact stated explicitly in the text: *v'samchu kol hashom'im et yedeihem al rosho* — all those who heard him blaspheme lay their hands upon his head. So *smicha* does appear in the commandment.

*Dechiya*, however, is not stated explicitly in the execution command at all. Where does it come from? The *gemara* answers: *dechiya lav hacha ksiva ela misinai* — it is not written here in the text but is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, derived from *yareh yareh*, the phrase in *Har Sinai* that indicates either stoning or shooting — *o sakol yisakeil o yareh yareh*. Chazal understand *yareh yareh* as teaching that one of the witnesses comes and pushes the condemned off the *beit haskilah*.

### Beit Skilah Structure

The *beit haskilah* — the stoning structure — is two stories high, *gavoha shtei komot*. One of the witnesses comes and pushes the condemned off the top, so that he falls to the ground below. This is the *dechiya*. If he does not die from the fall, then the stoning with *even* or *avanim* proceeds as described.

The *gemara* thus groups *smicha* and *dechiya* together because both represent actions that, while either stated or derived elsewhere, required confirmation that *bnei yisrael* actually performed them in the case of the *mekallel*. The general verse *u'venei yisrael asu ka'asher tziva Hashem et Moshe* provides that confirmation: they did indeed perform *smicha* and *dechiya* just as required.

### Hotzei Objection

The *rabbanim* raise an objection against Rav Ashi. If he is reading *hotzei* in the *mekallel* narrative as implying *dechiya* or some similar additional content, then in every case in the *Torah* where the word *hotzei* appears — including the passages concerning *parim hanisrafim*, the burned cattle — one would have to derive a *derasha* from it as well. The *gemara* concludes: *kashya* — this is indeed a strong difficulty, and Rav Ashi's reading is apparently not accepted.

## Communal Ownership

### Rav Huna's Rule

The *mishna* introduces the role of the person who stands at the entrance of the *beit din* with *sudarin* — signal flags — in hand, ready to wave them if an appeal for acquittal is raised while the condemned is being led out. Rav Huna then teaches a broader principle: it is obvious to me (*peshita li*) that the stone used for *skilah*, the tree used for hanging, the sword used for execution by *sayif*, and the cloth used for *chenek* (strangulation) must all belong to the *tzibur* — the community.

### Rationale

The reason is straightforward: we do not say to the condemned, *zil v'aytei veniktal nafshech* — go fetch your own stone and we will kill you with it. The instruments of execution are not the condemned person's obligation to provide. They belong to the community, whose court has decreed the execution.

### Signal Cloth & Horse

Rav Huna then raises a question about two other items. The *sudar* used to signal — waved to halt the procession if an appeal is raised — and the horse kept ready to ride out and stop the execution if needed: whose property are these? On one hand, since both exist for the purpose of potentially saving the condemned person's life, perhaps they should be his responsibility to provide. On the other hand, since the *beit din* is obligated to halt the execution if a valid appeal arises, perhaps the *beit din* — and thus the *tzibur* — bears this responsibility. The *gemara* leaves this as an unresolved question.

### Frankincense Wine

Rabbi Chiyya bar Rav Ashi in the name of Rav Chisda teaches: *hayotzei lahareg mashkin oto koret shel levonah bekos shel yayin* — the person being led out to execution is given *levonah* (frankincense) mixed into a cup of wine. The purpose is *kedei shetitaref da'ato* — so that his mind becomes confused and disordered. As Rashi explains, *titaref da'ato velo yidag veyitmahmeha baharigato* — his mind is unsettled and he does not fully comprehend what is happening to him, sparing him the full psychological anguish of his execution. The scriptural basis offered is the verse, *tenu shechar le'oved veyayin lemarei nafesh* — give strong drink to one who is lost and wine to one who is bitter of soul.

### Women Volunteers

A *baraita* records that the distinguished women of Jerusalem — *nashim yekarot shebiyerushalayim* — used to voluntarily prepare and bring this mixture of their own accord, as an act of communal *chessed* toward those condemned.

### Tzibur Provides

This prompts the question: if the precious women of Jerusalem did not volunteer, from whose resources should the mixture come? The *gemara* answers that it is clearly reasonable it should come from the *tzibur*, drawing on the fact that the scriptural verse uses the plural command *tenu* — "give" — indicating a communal obligation.

## Appeals & Silence

### Appeal Rights

The *mishna* establishes that the condemned person may appeal his own sentence — *afillu hu omer yesh li l'lamed al atzmi zechut* — even he himself can claim to have a *limud zechut*, and he is brought back, even four or five times, *u'bilvad sheyesh mamash bidvarav* — provided there is substance to what he says.

### Silent Student

This gives rise to the question posed by Rav Acha bar Huna to Rav Sheshet: what if one of the students says he has a *limud zechut* on the condemned and then goes silent — *nishtatek*? Rashi explains *nishtatek* as *ne'elam*, meaning he forgot what he was going to say. He got as far as declaring that he had an appeal but could not articulate it. Is the signal flag waved and the procession halted?

### Blowing Wind

Rav Sheshet's response is pointed: he waved his hand dismissively, indicating *ruach hu zeh she'amarta* — it is merely blowing wind. *Keivan denishtatek mah ho'il* — since he went silent, what purpose does his initial claim serve? An appeal that cannot be stated is no appeal at all.

### World-Wide Objection

The *gemara* then raises a challenge to this position. If we were to treat a claim that goes silent as sufficient cause to halt proceedings, we should logically worry about everyone in the world: *afilu echad besof ha'olam nami* — perhaps someone at the ends of the earth knows a *limud zechut* on him. If the mere possibility of an unvoiced appeal is grounds for delay, no execution could ever proceed.

### Claims Only Count

The resolution is that we care only about claims actually made. *Hatam lo ka'amar hacha ka'amar* — there, nobody has said anything; here, this person did say he had an appeal. But the point of the objection stands as a reductio: the reason we do not halt proceedings for the person who *might* have a claim is that no claim has been made. By the same logic, the student who said he had an appeal but then went silent has not actually made a claim in any actionable sense. His initial statement, unaccompanied by any substantive content, is treated as nothing.

## Student Dies Mid-Appeal

### Completed Appeal

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina teaches: if one of the students articulated a *limud zechut* — *shezika*, meaning he completed his appeal — and then died before the court could vote on it, *ro'in oto ke'ilu chai v'omed bimkomo* — we regard him as though he were alive and standing in his place.

### Substitute Voting

Practically, this means we substitute another person to occupy his position for the vote. The deceased student's completed *limud zechut* retains its standing; his death does not invalidate what he articulated. A replacement voter is seated and the deliberation proceeds as if the original student were present.

### Censored Text

At this point in the text, there is a section that was removed by censors. The printed versions available to us preserve only a truncated *gemara*, though other printings make clear that the censored passage deals with Jesus and the eve of Passover. We will return to this if time permits.

### Unfinished Appeal

The *gemara* then distinguishes: *zika in, lo zika lo* — if he completed his *limud zechut*, yes, we place someone in his stead; if he did not complete it, no, we do not. This creates the direct connection back to Rav Sheshet's ruling: a student who said only that he had an appeal and then fell silent — or died before articulating anything — is treated identically. The bare statement of intention, unaccompanied by substantive argument, generates no procedural consequence. *Zika peshita li* — if he completed it, that is obvious; *lo zika tiba'i lach* — the question is specifically about the case where he did not complete it.

## Appeals & Silence (II)

### Baraita Timing

Rav Pappa explains the apparent tension between our *mishna* — which says the condemned may be brought back even four or five times *provided there is substance to his claim* — and the *baraita*, which states that the first and second time he claims to have an appeal, he is brought back *whether or not* there is substance to his words. Rav Pappa resolves this by explaining that our *mishna* is speaking from the second or third time onward. The requirement of *mamash bidvarav* — substantive content — applies only from the third appeal onward; the first two times, the court brings him back unconditionally.

Abaye adds a practical dimension: from the third time onward, how does the court determine whether there is *mamashut* to the claim? A pair of rabbis accompanies the condemned and listens to what he intends to say. *Ei ika mamash bidvarav in, ei lo lo* — if there is substance, he is brought back; if not, he is not. One might ask why this procedure is not applied from the very first appeal. The *gemara* answers: *agav d'ba'it lei* — because the condemned is frightened and may not be able to fully articulate his claim under that duress, the court gives him unconditional latitude for the first two attempts.

## Pre-Execution Announceme…

### Announcer Role

A new *mishna* teaches: *matzu lo zechut pat'ruhu* — if a meritorious claim is found, he is acquitted; if not, he is brought out to be stoned. And *charoz yotzei lefanav* — an announcer goes out before him, publicly declaring: *ish ploni ben ploni yotzei lisakel* — so-and-so is being brought out to be stoned — *al she'avar aveira ploni* — for having committed such-and-such a transgression — *u'ploni u'ploni edav* — and so-and-so are his witnesses. The announcement concludes with an open invitation: *kol mi sheyodea lo zechut yavo vyelamed alav* — anyone who knows a reason to acquit him should come forward and do so.

### Details Announced

Abaye adds that the announcer must also specify the day, the hour, and the place where the transgression occurred: *b'yom ploni u'v'sha'ah ploni u'v'makom ploni*. The reason is practical and procedurally significant — *dilma ika did'u v'atu u'mazmin lei* — perhaps there are those who know that the named witnesses were not actually present at that location at that time, and can come forward to render them *edim zomemin*, conspiring witnesses, thereby invalidating their testimony entirely.

### Why Not Earlier?

The *gemara* then raises what should be an obvious question: the *mishna* says the announcer goes out *before him* — in front of the condemned as he is being led to execution. But does this mean that from the beginning of the trial, no such announcement was made? Surely it would make more sense to publicize the proceedings from the outset, giving potential witnesses or appellants the maximum time to come forward.

### Censored Jesus Text

The *gemara* does not answer this question in our printed text, because this is precisely where the censor intervened. The passage continues with a *baraita*: *v'ha tanya b'erev hapesach taluhu l'Yeshu* — but we learn in a *baraita* that on *erev Pesach* they hanged Jesus. The *gemara* proceeds from there, but the text in standard printed editions is cut off. One printed version notes explicitly that what follows here was removed by the censors and added according to the Venetian printing. The *baraita* about Jesus appears to have been the *gemara*'s response to the question of why no earlier announcement was made — the implication being that in that case, announcements *were* in fact made over an extended period beforehand. We will return to this passage if time allows.

## Confession Before Death

### Yetzer Sacrifice

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches: *kol hazove'ach et yitzro u'mitvadeh alav ma'aleh alav hakatuv ke'ilu kibdo l'Hakadosh Baruch Hu bishnei olamim* — anyone who slaughters his *yetzer hara* and confesses upon it, the verse accounts it as though he honored God in both worlds, this world and the world to come. The *drashah* is drawn from the verse *zove'ach todah yechabdaneni* — he who offers a *todah* sacrifice honors Me — and the word *todah* shares its root with *mitvadeh*, to confess. The double *nun* in *yechabdaneni*, where the simpler form *yechabdani* would have sufficed, yields one *nun* for *olam hazeh* and one for *olam haba*.

### Sacrifices & Reward

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi continues: when the *beit hamikdash* stood, a person who brought an *olah* received reward for the *olah*, and one who brought a *minchah* received reward for the *minchah*.

### Lowered Mind

But now — *aval mi sheda'ato shiflah* — whoever lowers his mind and humbles himself, *ma'aleh alav hakatuv ke'ilu hikriv kol hakorbanot kulan* — the verse accounts it as though he offered all the sacrifices combined. The prooftext is *zivchei Elohim ruach nishbarah* — the sacrifices of God are a broken spirit. And moreover, his prayer is not rejected: *lev nishbar venidkeh Elohim lo tivzeh* — a broken and contrite heart, O God, You will not despise.

### Ten Amot Rule

The *mishna* now addresses the procedure immediately before execution. When the condemned is approximately ten *amot* from the *beit haskilah*, they say to him: confess your sins — *hitvadeh* — for it is the practice of all those who are put to death to confess, and anyone who confesses has a portion in the world to come.

### Achan Example

The basis for this is the case of Achan, who took consecrated property from Yericho in violation of the *cherem*, committing *me'ilah*. Yehoshua said to him before his execution: *b'ni sim na kavod la'Hashem Elohei Yisrael viten lo todah* — my son, please give honor to the God of Israel and confess. Achan responded: *omnah anochi chatati v'chazot v'chazot* — truly I have sinned, and he confessed in detail. Yehoshua then said to him: *bayom hazeh ata achur v'ee ata achur l'olam haba* — today you are *achur* (the opposite of *zach*, pure — murky, blemished, unclean), but in the world to come you are not *achur*. The confessed sin was forgiven and his portion in the world to come was secured.

### Default Confession

For someone who does not know how to formulate a confession, the *mishna* prescribes a simple formula: *emor tehei mitati kapara al kol avonotai* — say, let my death serve as atonement for all of my sins.

### Innocent Exception

Rabbi Yehuda adds a qualification: if the condemned person knows that he is *mezumam* — that he is innocent of this particular charge — he should say: *tehei mitati kapara al kol avonotai chutz me'avon zeh* — let my death be atonement for all my sins, except for this one.

### Innocence Claim

The *rabbanim* object to Rabbi Yehuda's position: *im kein yehu kol adam omer kein kedei l'nakot atzmo* — if that is the case, everyone will say this in order to appear clean before the public after their death. The force of this objection is striking. The *rabbanim* are effectively assuming that a person facing death will prioritize his posthumous reputation over the *kapara* that confession and death together could provide. One who confesses knows his death will atone for his sins; yet *rabbanim* predict that he will deny the sin anyway, apparently caring more about how he is perceived than about the spiritual benefit of honest confession. Whether this reflects a concern about wrongful conviction — the truly innocent claiming innocence — or a more cynical reading of human nature in extremis, is itself a matter worth reflection.
