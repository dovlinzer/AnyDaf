# Nazir 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nazir Grogrot

### Perek Sheini Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִן הַגְּרוֹגְרוֹת וּמִן הַדְּבֵילָה״, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ נָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite** and therefore will refrain **from dried figs and from cakes of dried figs, Beit Shammai say:** His statement renders him a full-fledged **nazirite,** and his addition: From dried figs, is insignificant, as this fruit is not included in the prohibitions of a nazirite, which include only products of the grapevine. **And Beit Hillel say: He is not a nazirite,** since he did not accept naziriteship upon himself.

With the opening of the second perek, *Hareini Nazir*, we enter terrain that overlaps with themes from the first perek, though with a distinctly new focus. Much of *perek* 1, as in the parallel tractate Nedarim, addressed the different formulations of language a person might use to declare *nezirut* — not only whether a particular declaration makes one a *nazir*, but also how many units of *nezirut* are created, whether we interpret the vow as binding for the standard duration or a longer period, and the special categories of *Nazir Olam* and *Nazir Shimshon*. The second perek now takes up a different kind of question: what happens when the language of the declaration is internally incoherent?

### Mishna Statement

The opening *mishna* presents the following case: *Hareini nazir min hagrogros umin hadvela* — "I am a *nazir* from dried figs and pressed dates." Neither *grogros* (dried figs) nor *dvela* (pressed dates) are grapes or wine. They have no connection to the world of *nezirut*, whose prohibitions, as the Torah specifies, all derive from *kol asher ye'aseh migefen hayayin* — everything produced from the grapevine.

### Beis Shammai/Hillel

*Beis Shammai* rules that such a person is indeed a *nazir*. *Beis Hillel* rules that he is not. The immediate difficulty, of course, bears on *Beis Shammai*: how can declaring oneself a *nazir* from something entirely outside the domain of *nezirut* — figs and dates — create *nezirut* at all?

## Rav Yehuda Reframe

### Rav Yehuda's Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אַף כְּשֶׁאָמְרוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, לֹא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא בְּאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי הֵן עָלַי קׇרְבָּן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: Even when Beit Shammai said** that this vow takes effect, **they said** that **only in** a case **where** one **said** that he meant: **They are hereby** forbidden **to me** as **an offering.** In that case it is as though he took a vow rendering the figs forbidden to him. However, Beit Shammai concede that although the vow takes effect, it is not a vow of naziriteship.

*Rav Yehuda* attempts to reframe *Beis Shammai*'s position: *ad kan she'omru Beis Shammai, lo amru ela be'omer harei hen alai korban* — "Even when *Beis Shammai* said it, they only said it in the case where one says 'these are upon me like a *korban*.'" That is, *Rav Yehuda* reads *Beis Shammai* as holding that the declaration *Hareini nazir min hagrogros* is not interpreted as *nezirut* at all, but rather as a *neder* — a vow rendering those figs forbidden. On this reading, *Beis Shammai* never actually says the person becomes a *nazir* in the conventional sense; rather, the statement is recast as a prohibition on the figs themselves, as though one said *harei hen alai ke'korban*. Alternatively, the word *nazir*, as Tosafot notes, can itself carry the meaning of *lehazir* — to separate — and thus the phrase *Hareini nazir min hagrogros* is read simply as "I hereby separate myself from these figs," a declaration of personal prohibition rather than a *kabbalat nezirut*.

### Nazir = Removal

Whether the reframing proceeds through the language of *lehazir* or through the analogy to *korban*, *Rav Yehuda*'s point is the same: *Beis Shammai* does not hold that uttering the word *nazir* in conjunction with figs actually triggers *nezirut*. The statement is translated into its most plausible halakhic meaning — a self-imposed prohibition on those items.

### Neder/Shvua Parallel

This interpretive move is familiar from the opening discussions in Nedarim. The *gemara* there explored at length the question of *neder belashon shvua, shvua belashon neder* — using the technical term for one type of vow when one clearly intended the other. Since a *neder* applies to objects and a *shvua* to actions, a person who says "a *neder* that I will not enter this house" is understood by many *rishonim* to have actually made a *shvua*, because that is the only form that fits the content. The wrong term is treated as a *yad* — a handle or indicator — pointing toward the actual intent. So too here: the word *nazir*, under *Rav Yehuda*'s reading of *Beis Shammai*, functions as a *yad* for a *neder* forbidding the figs.

### Beis Shammai Problem

The problem, however, is that the *Tanna Kamma* of the *mishna* does not read *Beis Shammai* as *Rav Yehuda* does. On the straightforward reading of the *mishna*, *Beis Shammai* holds that the person is genuinely a *nazir* — not merely forbidden from eating figs, but a full *nazir* in all its dimensions. How can a declaration that mentions only dates and figs — items wholly foreign to *nezirut* — create actual *nezirut*? This is the question the *gemara* now addresses directly.

## Rabbi Meir Principle

### Ein Adam Motzi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִן הַגְּרוֹגְרוֹת וּמִן הַדְּבֵילָה״ — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר. וְאַמַּאי? ״מִכֹּל אֲשֶׁר יֵעָשֶׂה מִגֶּפֶן הַיַּיִן״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא! בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: אֵין אָדָם מוֹצִיא דְּבָרָיו לְבַטָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It is stated in the mishna that if one says: **I am hereby a nazirite** and therefore will refrain **from dried figs and from cakes of dried figs, Beit Shammai say:** He is a **nazirite.** The Gemara asks: **But why? The Merciful One states** in the Torah in the passage dealing with naziriteship: **"From anything that is made of the grapevine**…he shall not eat" (Numbers 6:4). In naziriteship, only the fruit of the vine is prohibited. The Gemara answers: **Beit Shammai hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: A person does not utter a statement for naught.** In other words, if one utters a statement that cannot be fulfilled as stated, his statement is interpreted in a manner that renders it relevant. Here too, Beit Shammai say that he misspoke and actually intended to take a vow of naziriteship.

The *gemara* explains: *Beis Shammai savrei ke'Rabbi Meir, de'amar ein adam motzi devarav levatala* — *Beis Shammai* holds like Rabbi Meir, who says that a person does not utter his words for naught. A meaningful halakhic principle operates here: when someone makes a formal declaration in a halakhic context, we do not simply dismiss the statement as nonsense. We seek a legally coherent interpretation.

### Erech/Damim Example

The *gemara* grounds this in Rabbi Meir's classic case. The Torah provides a system of *arachin* — fixed valuations based on a person's age and sex — distinct from *dmei*, the market value of something. If someone says *erech kli alai* — "the *erech* of this vessel is upon me" — this is a problematic formulation, since *erech* as defined by the Torah applies only to human beings, not to objects. Rabbi Yehuda would say the person is not liable for anything, because the statement is simply incoherent. Rabbi Meir, invoking *ein adam motzi devarav levatala*, says we reinterpret the word *erech* as *dmei* — the person meant to say "the *value* of this vessel is upon me" — and they are obligated accordingly. The declaration, though technically misphrased, is given a coherent halakhic reading rather than being discarded.

### Why Different?

Applying this to our case, however, reveals a significant tension. When someone says *erech kli alai*, the fix is clean: substitute *dmei* for *erech*, and the entire statement makes sense. But *Hareini nazir min hagrogros* cannot be saved by a simple terminological substitution. Even if we accept *ein adam motzi devarav levatala* and refuse to dismiss the statement outright, we are still left with a nonsensical clause: *min hagrogros*. Under the *nazir* framework, figs are irrelevant. So *Beis Shammai*, in applying Rabbi Meir's principle here, is doing something more aggressive than Rabbi Meir's original example: not just reinterpreting a term, but actually discarding the second half of the statement entirely, retaining only the first word — *nazir* — as binding.

### Student Question

This raises an obvious question: why not do both? If the first part of the statement (*Hareini nazir*) is binding and the second part (*min hagrogros*) represents some additional intent, why not say the person is both a *nazir* and also forbidden from figs — treating the two halves as independent halakhic acts? The *mishna* offers no such intermediate position. *Beis Shammai*, on the *Tanna Kamma*'s reading, says the person is a *nazir* — full stop. Nobody says the person is both. The *gemara* will develop why, but the question is well-taken: logically, a "both" position is conceivable.

## Begmar Devarav

### Rabbi Yosi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: בִּגְמַר דְּבָרָיו אָדָם נִתְפָּס. וְהַאי נֶדֶר וּפִתְחוֹ עִמּוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who says: A person is** also **held accountable for the conclusion of his statement.** If one states two irreconcilable clauses, e.g., this animal is a substitute for a burnt-offering, a substitute for a peace-offering; the second clause is not disregarded, and the animal assumes both statuses. **And this is a vow with its** inherent **opening,** i.e., reason for dissolution. Although he said: I am hereby a nazirite, by adding the words: From dried figs and from cakes of dried figs, he indicated that his intention was that the naziriteship would not take effect.

*Beis Hillel*, the *gemara* explains, holds *ke'Rabbi Yosi, de'amar begmar devarav adam nitpas* — a person is "caught" — meaning bound — by the conclusion of his words. We do not freeze the analysis at the first clause of a statement; we read the statement as a whole, and the final portion of what was said carries interpretive weight for what preceded it.

### Neder Pitcho Imo

The operative concept here is *neder upitcho imo* — a vow that carries its own "opening" for dissolution within itself. Normally, dissolving a vow requires presenting a *chacham* with a *petach*, an opening — some basis for regret. Here, the very terms of the declaration themselves constitute that opening: by saying *min hagrogros*, the person has revealed that his entire concept of *nezirut* was built around abstaining from figs, which is not *nezirut* at all. The statement as a whole is self-undermining. *Beis Hillel* does not stop at *Hareini nazir* and declare it binding; they allow the speaker to finish, and what emerges is a statement that never coherently constitutes *kabbalat nezirut*. Notably, the *gemara* could have explained *Beis Hillel* more simply — as merely rejecting *ein adam motzi devarav levatala* — but instead it grounds *Beis Hillel* within Rabbi Yosi's framework, affirming that even if we accept the principle that one's words must mean something, the complete statement here, taken as a whole, produces a self-canceling result.

### Tosafot Explanation

Tosafot elaborates on how this works. The key passage, several lines into Tosafot's discussion, explains: since *begmar devarav adam nitpas*, we are "caught" by the conclusion — which is the mention of *grogros* — and since there is no *nezirut* with respect to *grogros*, the person is not a *nazir*. Even though at the outset he said *Hareini nazir*, by adding *min hagrogros* he effectively reversed himself *toch k'dei dibbur* — within the halakhic window of speech that is treated as continuous. It is as though he found a *petach* for his vow, a ground for dissolution, immediately upon completing the utterance. Since *nidro upitcho imo* — his vow and its opening came together — nothing takes effect at all.

### Tosafot Question

Tosafot then raises the precise question that arose above: the *gemara* in the context of *tmura* records that Rabbi Yosi holds that when someone says *harei zeh tmurath olah tmurath shelamim* — this animal is an exchange for both an *olah* and a *shelamim* — Rabbi Yosi does not declare the whole statement void. Rather, he says the animal has both *kedushas* and it cannot be brought as a single *korban*, but can be redeemed, with half the proceeds going to each. There, even though the two parts of the statement are logically contradictory, Rabbi Yosi says both are operative. Why, then, in our case, don't we say both parts of the statement are operative — the person is a *nazir* and also a *nadur* from figs?

## Why Not Both?

### Safa Explains Reisha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי נָמֵי, נֶדֶר וּפִתְחוֹ עִמּוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Beit Shammai too, it is a vow with its** inherent **opening,** and since they certainly agree that a vow of this kind does not take effect, why do they hold that this vow takes effect?

Tosafot's resolution is precise. In the *tmura* case, *tmurath olah* and *tmurath shelamim* are two independent, equal-weight clauses. Neither explains or qualifies the other; they simply sit side by side as separate declarations. When two parts of a statement are genuinely independent and non-overlapping, we say both are meant and both take effect. But in our case, the situation is different: *min hagrogros* is not an independent second clause. It is the speaker's own explanation of what he means by *nazir* — his conception of *nezirut* is that it consists of abstaining from *grogros*. The *safa*, the end of the statement, comes *lifaresh* the *reisha* — to explain the opening. The speaker is not making two separate declarations; he is elaborating on the one declaration, and that elaboration reveals it to be incoherent.

### End Contradicts Start

This creates a structural problem. Once we read the second half as explaining the first, the whole sentence effectively says: "I am a *nazir*, and by *nazir* I mean someone who abstains from figs." But that is not *nezirut*. The *safa* has thus not merely added a second layer; it has retroactively undermined the entire content of the first.

### One or Two Parts?

The underlying hermeneutic question is whether we are dealing with one statement or two. If they are two independent parts — as in *tmurath olah tmurath shelamim* — we say both. If the second part is an interpretation or elaboration of the first — as in *nazir min hagrogros*, where the *grogros* clause explains what the speaker thought *nazir* meant — then the *safa* is the key to the whole utterance, and since the *safa* reveals a fundamental misunderstanding, the statement as a whole produces nothing. That is what *begmar devarav adam nitpas* means in this context: we wait until the speaker is finished, and only then assess the whole.

## Multiple Versions

### Version 1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נָדוּר וְנָזִיר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נָדוּר וְאֵין נָזִיר. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר וּכְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan says** that if one says: I am hereby a nazirite and therefore will refrain from dried figs, **Beit Shammai say:** He has rendered dried figs **forbidden** to himself **by a vow, and** he is also **a nazirite; and Beit Hillel say:** He has rendered dried figs **forbidden** to himself **by a vow, but** he is **not a nazirite.** According to Rabbi Natan, **Beit Shammai hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** that a person does not utter statements for naught and that he becomes a nazirite as soon as he says: I am hereby a nazirite; **and** he explains the opinion of Beit Shammai **as Rabbi Yehuda** does, i.e., that one in this situation has vowed that dried figs are forbidden to him, since the rest of his statement also has significance. **And Beit Hillel hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** that one is held to the conclusion of his statement, and he has therefore vowed that dried figs are forbidden to him.

Having established the main conceptual framework, the *gemara* notes that the *mishna* does not align with a certain *tanna*: *matnitin delo ke'hai tanna*. A *braita* records Rabbi Natan citing a different version of the dispute. According to Rabbi Natan, *Beis Shammai* says the person is *nadur ve'nazir* — both forbidden from the figs (a *neder*) and a *nazir*. *Beis Hillel* says *nadur ve'ein nazir* — he is forbidden from the figs but is not a *nazir*. This version is notably different from the *mishna*. Here, everyone salvages the entire statement in some way. *Beis Shammai*, by saying both parts are independently binding, preserves the full declaration — the word *nazir* creates *nezirut*, and *min hagrogros* creates a *neder* on the figs. *Beis Hillel*, rather than voiding the whole statement, gives it a coherent unified meaning: *nazir min hagrogros* is read as *nadur min hagrogros* — the word *nazir* is interpreted as *neder* language, and the person is simply forbidden from figs.

### Positions Explained

The *gemara* explains the theoretical underpinnings of this version as well. *Beis Shammai savrei ke'Rabbi Meir uke'Rabbi Yehuda*. *Beis Shammai* here applies *ein adam motzi devarav levatala* (the principle of Rabbi Meir) to both halves of the statement: *nazir* means *nazir* and *min hagrogros* means *nadur min hagrogros* (as *Rav Yehuda* had originally read *Beis Shammai*). Nothing in the sentence is wasted; both parts carry independent halakhic force. *Beis Hillel savrei ke'Rabbi Yosi* — applying *begmar devarav adam nitpas* to read the statement as one coherent whole, they give the entire declaration the meaning of *nadur min hagrogros*, the only reading that renders the complete sentence meaningful.

### Version 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא אָמְרִי לַהּ: רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נָדוּר וְאֵינוֹ נָזִיר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: לֹא נָדוּר וְלֹא נָזִיר. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וּבֵית הִלֵּל כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say a different version** of the *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan says** that **Beit Shammai say:** He has rendered dried figs **forbidden** to himself **by a vow and he is not a nazirite; and Beit Hillel say:** He has **not** rendered dried figs **forbidden** to himself **by a vow, and** he is **not a nazirite.** According to this version, the opinion of **Beit Shammai is as Rabbi Yehuda** explained in the mishna, **and Beit Hillel hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who maintains that a donation not performed in the manner typical of donors is not a donation.

A second textual tradition (*lishna achrina*) within Rabbi Natan's account presents yet another variation: *Beis Shammai omrim nadur ve'eino nazir* — the person is forbidden from the figs but is not a *nazir*. This is precisely the reading *Rav Yehuda* attributed to *Beis Shammai* at the outset of the discussion. *Beis Hillel* says *lo nadur velo nazir* — neither; he is nothing at all. This version maps neatly onto *Beis Shammai* following *Rav Yehuda*'s interpretation and *Beis Hillel* following Rabbi Shimon's approach of *lo hitnadev k'derech hamitnadvim* (to which we return shortly).

### Three Options

Surveying all the material, there are three distinct positions one could occupy when applying *ein adam motzi devarav levatala* to this case. First: the person is a *nazir* and we discard the *min hagrogros* clause — the first part of the statement was binding, the second part is treated as either irrelevant or as a failed attempt at reversal. Second: the person is *nadur min hagrogros* but not a *nazir* — the whole sentence is given a single coherent meaning by translating *nazir* as *nadur*. Third: the person is both a *nazir* and *nadur min hagrogros* — both halves of the statement take independent effect. All three positions stem from the shared refusal to read the statement as meaningless, but they differ in how they distribute that meaning across the two clauses. *Beis Hillel*, for its part, is willing to look at the complete statement and conclude that it never coherently achieved anything halakhically binding.

## Mincha Parallel

### Mincha Variations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מִנְחָה לְהָבִיא מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִים״ — יָבִיא מִן הַחִיטִּים. ״קֶמַח״ — יָבִיא סוֹלֶת. ״שֶׁלֹּא בְּשֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה״ — יְבִיאֶנָּה בְּשֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה. ״חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן״ — יָבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם. ״עִשָּׂרוֹן וּמֶחֱצָה״ — יָבִיא שְׁנַיִם. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר, שֶׁלֹּא הִתְנַדֵּב כְּדֶרֶךְ הַמִּתְנַדְּבִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses a case mentioned earlier. **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Menaḥot* 103a): With regard to **one who says: It is** incumbent **upon me to bring a meal-offering from barley,** since voluntary meal-offerings are made only with wheat **he must bring** a meal-offering **from wheat.** If one vows to bring a meal-offering from **flour,** since a standard meal-offering is made from fine flour **he must bring** one from **fine flour.** If one vows to bring a meal-offering **without oil and frankincense, he must bring it with oil and frankincense,** in accordance with the *halakha*. If one vows to bring a meal-offering with **half of a tenth** of an ephah of fine flour, **he must bring** a meal-offering with **a full tenth,** since a meal-offering cannot have less than one-tenth of an ephah of flour. If one vows to bring a meal-offering with **a tenth and a half, he brings two** units of a tenth of an ephah in his meal-offering. Since half of one-tenth of an ephah is the minimum, his vow is increased to two full tenths. **Rabbi Shimon exempts** him entirely in all these cases, **as he did not donate in the manner** typical **of donors.**

The *gemara* now introduces a parallel set of cases from a *mishna* elsewhere: *Ha'omer harei alai mincha lehavi min hase'orim* — someone who says "I accept upon myself to bring a *mincha* from barley." Since there is no voluntary *mincha* brought from barley (barley *minchot* are restricted to specific obligatory cases such as *minchat sotah*), the declaration is internally inconsistent. Nevertheless, the *tanna* rules: *yavi min hachittim* — he brings it from wheat. Similarly: one who said he would bring a *mincha* from *kemach* (bran or coarse flour) must bring it from fine flour (*solet*), since there is no *mincha* from *kemach*; one who said he would bring it without oil and *levona* (frankincense) must include them; one who said *chatzi issaron* (a half-measure, which has no standing as a *mincha*) must bring a full *issaron*; and one who said one and a half *issaronot* must bring two, rounding up to the nearest valid unit. In all these cases, the *tanna kamma* holds: we strip away the invalid qualifier, identify the core intent as a *mincha*, and obligate accordingly.

### Rabbi Shimon

*Rabbi Shimon* exempts in all these cases: *shelo hitnadev k'derech hamitnadvim* — this person did not make a vow in the manner of those who normally vow. If the only halakhically coherent interpretation of the whole statement requires discarding a significant portion of what was actually said, *Rabbi Shimon* would rather declare the entire declaration non-binding than artificially reconstruct it. This is a direct and close parallel to the *Hareini nazir min hagrogros* case: just as there is no *nezirut* from *grogros*, there is no *mincha* from barley as a freewill offering, and *Rabbi Shimon* would treat both declarations identically — as generating no obligation at all.

### Tanna Kamma ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא דְּכִי אָמַר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מִנְחָה מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִים״ מֵבִיא מִן הַחִיטִּים? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: בְּמַחֲלוֹקֶת שְׁנוּיָה, וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי הִיא. לָאו אָמְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי כִּי אָמַר ״מִן הַגְּרוֹגְרוֹת וּמִן הַדְּבֵילָה״ הָוֵי נָזִיר, הָכִי נָמֵי כִּי אָמַר ״מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִין״ — מֵבִיא מִן הַחִיטִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught that when one said: It is** incumbent **upon me** to bring **a meal-offering from barley, he brings** a meal-offering **from wheat? Ḥizkiyya said** that this *halakha* **is taught as a dispute** between *tanna'im*, **and it is** the opinion of **Beit Shammai.** He explains: **Didn't Beit Shammai say that when one said:** I am hereby a nazirite and therefore will refrain **from dried figs and from cakes of dried figs, he is a nazirite,** indicating that he is held to only the first part of his statement, and the conflicting details are ignored? **So too, when he said** that he will bring a meal-offering **from barley, he brings** it **from wheat,** and the same analysis applies to the other cases listed in this mishna.

*Chizkiyah* is the first to address the question of who the *tanna kamma* is in the *mincha* case. He identifies the *tanna kamma*'s position as equivalent to that of *Beis Shammai*: just as *Beis Shammai* says *Hareini nazir min hagrogros* creates *nezirut* by ignoring the invalid *min hagrogros* clause, so too the *tanna kamma* says *mincha min hase'orim* creates an obligation to bring a *mincha* by ignoring the invalid *min hase'orim* clause. The structure of the reasoning is identical in both cases.

## Rabbi Yochanan

### Understandable Mistake

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל — בְּאוֹמֵר ״אִילּוּ הָיִיתִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין נוֹדְרִין כָּךְ, לֹא נָדַרְתִּי כָּךְ אֶלָּא כָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: You** can **even say** that **all,** including Beit Hillel, **agree** with the opinion of the Rabbis of the mishna, as the mishna may be stated **with regard to one who** later **says,** to clarify his earlier statement: **Had I known that one cannot vow in this manner,** that one cannot bring a voluntary meal-offering from barley, **I would not have vowed that way, only this way,** to bring a meal-offering from wheat. Mentioning barley was an error on his part rather than a condition, and he actually meant to bring a standard meal-offering. In this case even Beit Hillel, who maintain in the mishna here that there is no vow at all, deem him liable to bring a proper meal-offering.

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees with *Chizkiyah*'s identification. The *tanna kamma* in the *mincha* case can be understood even according to *Beis Hillel* — it need not reflect the position of *Beis Shammai* at all. Why? *Be'omer ilu hayiti yode'a she'ein nodrim kach, lo nadarti kach ella kach* — because the case is one where the person can be presumed to say: "Had I known that one cannot vow this way, I would not have said *min hase'orim*, I would have said *min hachittim*." The declaration is treated as binding not because we mechanically ignore its invalid clause, but because the mistake was an understandable one. Most people do not carefully distinguish between barley and wheat when thinking about bringing a *mincha*; the commitment to bring a *mincha* is genuine and the specification of *se'orim* was simply an error that, if caught at the time, the person would immediately have corrected. In such a case, we say the fundamental intent was to bring a *mincha*, the qualifier was mistaken but not misconceived, and therefore the obligation stands.

### Barley vs Lentils

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאָמַר ״מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִים״, אֲבָל אָמַר ״מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים״ — לָא מַיְיתֵי וְלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this ruling **Ḥizkiyya says: They taught** that he must bring a meal-offering from wheat **only if he said** that he will bring a meal-offering **from barley. But** if **he said** he will bring a meal-offering **from lentils, he does not bring anything.** The difference is that the meal-offerings of the *omer* and of a *sota* are made from barley, so if he said: From barley, he may have mistakenly believed that a voluntary meal-offering can be prepared from barley as well. As everyone knows that no meal-offering is made from lentils, his statement demonstrates that he never intended to bring a meal-offering.

This principle, *Rabbi Yochanan* implies, has a sharp limit. An "understandable mistake" is one within the normal range of confusion — where the gap between what was said and what could have been said is relatively small. *Min hase'orim* vs. *min hachittim* is a modest error; barley and wheat are both grains, both familiar, and easily confused in the mind of someone unfamiliar with the precise laws of *minchot*. But if someone says *mincha min ha'adashim* — a *mincha* from lentil beans — that is a categorically different kind of error. No one making a sincere vow about a *mincha* confuses lentil beans with wheat. Such a statement reflects not a small mistake but a fundamental misunderstanding of what a *mincha* is, analogous to someone who thinks *nezirut* means not eating figs. In that case, the speaker is not simply misspecifying a detail; the whole concept they are operating with is wrong, and we cannot say their "fundamental" intent was to bring a valid *mincha*.

### Chizkiyah Initial

*Chizkiyah* initially argues that the *mincha* dispute is parallel to the *Beis Shammai/Beis Hillel* dispute, implying that even a radical departure from the norm — like lentil beans — should be treated consistently: we drop the invalid qualifier and obligate the *mincha*. On his view, there is no halakhically meaningful distinction between small and large mistakes in this context. The principle of ignoring the nonsensical second clause operates uniformly, regardless of how far outside the realm of *minchot* the stated qualifier falls.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי חִזְקִיָּה כְּמַאן מוֹקֵים לָהּ לְמַתְנִיתִין — כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, וְהָא עֲדָשִׁים לְגַבֵּי מִנְחָה כִּגְרוֹגְרוֹת לְגַבֵּי נָזִיר דָּמוּ, וְקָאָמְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הָוֵי נָזִיר! הֲדַר בֵּיהּ חִזְקִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now** consider, **in accordance with whose** opinion **does Ḥizkiyya establish the mishna** in *Menaḥot*? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai. But lentils relative to a meal-offering are comparable to dried figs relative to a nazirite,** as everyone is likewise aware that one cannot be a nazirite and therefore refrain from figs, **and** yet **Beit Shammai say he is a nazirite.** Why not say that one who vows to bring a meal-offering from lentils is obligated to bring a standard meal-offering? The Gemara answers: **Ḥizkiyya retracted from it** and no longer holds that the mishna in *Menaḥot* is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai.

The *gemara* challenges *Chizkiyah*'s position by pressing the parallel he himself established. *Chizkiyah* had identified the *tanna kamma* by the *mincha* case as following *Beis Shammai*. But *Beis Shammai* holds — even in the case of *Hareini nazir min hagrogros* — that the person becomes a *nazir*, despite the radical absurdity of the qualifier. *Ve'adashim legabei mincha kegrogros legabei nazir damu* — lentil beans in the context of a *mincha* are like *grogros* in the context of *nezirut*: both are wildly off-base. But *Beis Shammai* rules the person is a *nazir* even in the *grogros* case. By his own analogy, *Chizkiyah* should conclude that even lentil beans would obligate a *mincha*. Yet *Chizkiyah* himself had said that the *adashim* case produces nothing. This is internally inconsistent.

### Chizkiyah Reverses

*Hadar bei Chizkiyah* — *Chizkiyah* reversed himself. Faced with this challenge, he conceded that the distinction between small and large mistakes is indeed operative, and came around to the position of *Rabbi Yochanan*: the *mincha* obligation in the case of *se'orim* rests on the fact that it was an understandable mistake, not on a blanket rule of ignoring all invalid qualifiers. Large, radical mistakes — like *adashim* — do not generate a *mincha* obligation.

## Why Only Barley?

### Rava's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי הֲדַר בֵּיהּ? אֲמַר רָבָא, מַתְנִיתִין קְשִׁיתֵיהּ: מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִים״? לִיתְנֵי ״מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים״! אֶלָּא סָבַר חִזְקִיָּה: כִּי קָאָמְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הָתָם — כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why did he** in fact **retract from it? Rava said:** The **mishna caused him difficulty** and demonstrated his error. If the mishna is in accordance with Beit Shammai's opinion with regard to a nazirite, **why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach** the case of one who says that he will bring a meal-offering **from barley? Let him teach** a greater novelty, i.e., that even one who vowed to bring a meal-offering **from lentils** is obligated to bring a meal-offering made from wheat. **Rather, Ḥizkiyya holds** that **when Beit Shammai stated** their opinion **there,** i.e., in the mishna with regard to a nazirite, their intention was **as** explained by **Rabbi Yehuda,** that one's statement is interpreted to be meaningful and it can have the meaning of creating a vow that dried figs are forbidden to him. In the case of the meal-offering, even they agree that since one's vow has no meaning, as there cannot be a meal-offering made of lentils, no vow takes effect.

*Rava* explains why *Chizkiyah* ultimately capitulated: *matnisin kashya lei* — he was troubled by the *mishna* itself. *Mai irya d'tani min hase'orim* — why did the *mishna* give specifically the example of *barley*? If the rule were that we always strip away the invalid qualifier and require a valid *mincha* regardless of how outlandish the qualifier was, the *mishna* could have — and should have — made the point more powerfully by giving an extreme case: *lisni min ha'adashim*, "even if you said lentil beans, you still bring a *mincha*." The fact that the *mishna* chose *barley* as its example demonstrates that the case is specifically calibrated to the range of understandable mistakes. *Se'orim* is at the boundary — close enough to a real *mincha* that we can say the person genuinely intended a *mincha* and simply got the grain wrong.

### Small Mistake Logic

When someone says *mincha min hase'orim*, the underlying intent is clear: this person wants to bring a *mincha*. The specification of barley was a detail they got wrong, but it does not transform their core commitment. We therefore ignore the mistaken qualifier and obligate them in a normal *mincha*.

### Big Mistake Logic

When someone says *mincha min ha'adashim*, the situation is qualitatively different. Lentil beans have nothing to do with the *mincha* as an institution. Someone who thinks they can bring a *mincha* of lentils is not merely confused about a detail; they are operating with a fundamentally different conception of what they are doing. We cannot say they "fundamentally wanted to bring a *mincha*" and the lentils were an incidental error. They were trying to do something outside the framework of *minchot* entirely, and that attempt generates no halakhic obligation.

## Rabbi Yochanan Refined

### Rabbi Yochanan Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ ״מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים״. וְהָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הוּא דְּאָמַר, בְּאוֹמֵר ״אִילּוּ הָיִיתִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין נוֹדְרִין כָּךְ, לֹא נָדַרְתִּי כָּךְ אֶלָּא כָּךְ״! לִדְבָרָיו דְּחִזְקִיָּה הוּא דְּקָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** one who vowed to bring a meal-offering **from lentils** must bring a meal-offering from wheat. The Gemara asks: **But Rabbi Yoḥanan is he who said** that the mishna is referring to a case of one **who says: Had I known that one cannot vow in this manner, I would not have vowed that way, only this way?** In the case of the lentils, it is not reasonable to say that he erred in thinking that one may vow to bring a meal-offering from lentils. The Gemara answers: **He stated** this ruling **in accordance with the statement of Ḥizkiyya.** Rabbi Yoḥanan was not stating his own opinion; he was challenging the ruling of Ḥizkiyya.

After *Chizkiyah* reverses himself and accepts *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position, the *gemara* presents *Rabbi Yochanan*'s own response to the challenge about lentil beans. Remarkably, *Rabbi Yochanan* now asserts: *afilu min ha'adashim* — even if someone says *mincha min ha'adashim*, they are still obligated to bring a *mincha*. This appears to contradict his earlier stated position. The *gemara* clarifies: *Rabbi Yochanan* is not abandoning his reasoning but rather arguing *lidvarav de'Chizkiyah* — speaking according to *Chizkiyah*'s own framework. *Rabbi Yochanan* is pressing *Chizkiyah*: you, *Chizkiyah*, should have stayed with your original position that we always drop the nonsensical qualifier. Your reason for reversing was that the *mishna* only mentioned *se'orim*; but that proves nothing, because there are other explanations for why the *mishna* chose that example.

### Reversal Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְּ מַאי טַעְמָא קָא הָדְרַתְּ בָּךְ — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא קָתָנֵי ״מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים״. דִּלְמָא לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא כִּי אָמַר ״מֵעֲדָשִׁים״, דְּמַיְיתֵי מִנְחָה מְעַלַּיְיתָא, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִיהְדָּר הוּא דַּהֲדַר בֵּיהּ, וּתְפוֹס לָשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן. אֶלָּא כִּי אָמַר ״מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִין״, וַדַּאי דְּהָכִי קָאָמַר: אִי קָדְשָׁה כַּמִּנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains his challenge: **What is the reason you retracted** your earlier opinion that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai? You did so **because it does not teach: From lentils.** That is not conclusive, as **perhaps it is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** as follows: **It is not necessary** to say that **when one says:** It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal-offering **from lentils, that he brings a proper meal-offering** of wheat. In that situation **it can be said** that by saying: From lentils, **he is retracting** his first statement and now wants to rescind his vow. **And** yet Beit Shammai hold that he is **held to the first** part of his **statement,** and they do not allow the rescinding of a vow of consecration. **But if he said:** It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal-offering **from barley,** since it is possible to explain that **certainly this is what he said: If** this meal-offering that I have vowed to bring from barley **is consecrated like the** *omer* **meal-offering,**

One such explanation: *dilma lo mibaya ka'amar* — perhaps the *mishna* is making its point *a fortiori* in the opposite direction. If someone says *min ha'adashim*, that is such an obviously impossible qualifier that it must be read as a *chazara* — a reversal. The person is effectively saying, "I'm not really committing to a *mincha*." But *tefos lashon rishon* — we hold them to the first statement. Since the second clause is so clearly absurd, we treat it as an attempted (and ineffective) retraction rather than as a genuine explanation, which means the first statement stands and the *mincha* obligation is even more obviously binding. On this reading, *adashim* is *kal vachomer* — if anything, it makes the case for obligation more compelling, not less.

### Genuine Intent Theory

A second alternative explanation for why the *mishna* used *se'orim*: when someone says *mincha min hase'orim*, it is genuinely ambiguous whether they are making a mistake or expressing a genuine and specific preference. Perhaps they specifically want to bring something like a *minchat omer* or a *minchat sotah*, which are indeed barley-based. If such a thing exists, I want to bring it; if not, I don't want to bring anything at all. If that is the case, the person has not "fundamentally wanted to bring a *mincha*" with barley as a mistaken qualifier — rather, they specifically wanted *that* type of *mincha* and nothing else. The *mishna* therefore has to affirmatively teach us that even in this ambiguous case we say they are obligated to bring a regular *mincha* from wheat, because we interpret the qualifier as a mistake rather than a precise condition.

### Intent Question

The *gemara* thus surfaces a deeper conceptual question running beneath the entire discussion: what is the nature of the mistake being made? If the error is small and within the normal range of confusion, we can confidently say the person's fundamental intent was to perform the *mitzvah* in question, and the qualifier reflects a genuine misunderstanding of the details. But if the error is large — if the qualifier reflects a radically different conception of the act itself — the question becomes whether this represents an attempted reversal, a completely different undertaking, or simply a bigger version of the same kind of mistake. These three framings lead to different conclusions, and the *amoraim* are divided on which framing correctly captures the logic of the *tanna kamma*.

## Final Synthesis

### Beis Shammai Lock

At the core of the entire sugya is a fundamental disagreement about how to evaluate a declaration that contains an incoherent element. *Beis Shammai*'s approach, as it emerges across all the versions of the debate, is essentially one of *tefisah* — locking in the first meaningful word that was uttered. Once someone says *nazir*, that word carries halakhic force, and it is exceedingly difficult to walk it back or requalify it. The subsequent clause — *min hagrogros* — does not redefine what was said; it arrives too late to undo what the first word already accomplished. Whether one explains this as an application of *ein adam motzi devarav levatala* (giving the statement force by focusing on its first viable clause), or as *ein she'eila b'hekdesh* (the principle that *nezirut*, like *hekdesh*, cannot be dissolved), or as a refusal to permit *chazara* once the declaration has taken effect, the result is the same: *Beis Shammai* is highly resistant to allowing a speaker to escape a statement they have already made.

### Beis Hillel Whole

*Beis Hillel*, by contrast, insists on reading the declaration as a whole before assigning it any binding force. *Begmar devarav adam nitpas* — a person is assessed by the entirety of what they said. When we look at the complete statement *Hareini nazir min hagrogros*, we see that the speaker's own conception of *nezirut* is built on something that has nothing to do with *nezirut*. The statement is not two parts in tension; it is one coherent (if confused) statement that, taken whole, fails to constitute any recognized halakhic act. In the *mincha* parallel, this translates to: when the qualifier is radically off, it shows that the speaker was not, at bottom, trying to bring a valid *mincha* — they were trying to do something else, something that does not exist within the halakhic framework, and therefore nothing is binding.

### Limits of Principle

The principle of *ein adam motzi devarav levatala* is thus powerful but bounded. It operates most cleanly where the statement is almost right — where a small terminological error can be corrected by reinterpretation, as in *erech kli alai* being read as *dmei kli alai*. It becomes strained when the invalid portion of the statement is not a minor terminological error but a fundamental reconception of what the *mitzvah* is — *grogros* instead of *yayin*, lentil beans instead of *solet*. In those cases, *Beis Hillel* (and ultimately *Chizkiyah* as well) concludes that the speaker simply was not doing what they thought they were doing, and no binding obligation results. *Beis Shammai*, unwilling to leave any utterance without halakhic consequence, finds a way to impose one regardless — but at the cost of effectively ignoring everything the speaker said except the very first word.
