# Nazir 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Agent & Unknown Woman

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ
>
> **Translation:** § After analyzing the mishna itself, the Gemara turns to a related issue. **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Yosef said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who says to his agent:**

The daf begins at the bottom of 11b, introducing a *sugya* that, at first glance, appears to have little connection to the topic at hand. To recall the context: the *mishnah* dealt with two men, each of whom declared, "I am a *nazir* and I accept upon myself to shave a *nazir*" — meaning, to bring another *nazir*'s *korbanot*. The *mishnah* observes that if they are clever, they will simply take care of each other's *korbanot*, a kind of mutual arrangement not unlike the practice of two poor people exchanging *Mishloach Manot* so that each fulfills his obligation for *Seudas Purim*.

### Unspecified Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צֵא וְקַדֵּשׁ לִי אִשָּׁה״ סְתָם — אָסוּר בְּכׇל הַנָּשִׁים שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם. חֲזָקָה שָׁלִיחַ עוֹשֶׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ, וְכֵיוָן דְּלָא פָּרֵישׁ לֵיהּ, הָא לָא יָדַע הֵי נִיהוּ קַדֵּישׁ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Go out and betroth a woman for me, without specifying** a particular woman; from that moment onward the one who appointed the agent is **forbidden to all the women in the world** until he finds out which woman the agent betrothed. There is a **presumption** that **an agent performs his** assigned **agency** and that he has betrothed a woman for him, **and since** the agent **did not clarify to him** which woman he chose, **he** therefore **does not know which** woman **is the one betrothed to him.** If he now betroths another woman, it is possible that she is the daughter, sister, or mother of the one his agent betrothed on his behalf and is therefore forbidden to him.

With that *mishnah* in mind, the *gemara* cites a ruling of Rav Yosef bar Yosef in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. If a man says to his *shaliach*, "*Tze ve-kadesh li isha stam*" — go out and marry any woman for me, whoever says yes first — and the *shaliach* departs but never returns with any report, then the man who sent him is forbidden to marry any woman in the entire world.

The reasoning proceeds in two steps. First, there is a presumption — *chazaka shaliach oseh shelichuto* — that an agent carries out his mission. We therefore assume the *shaliach* did in fact betroth someone. Second, since the man gave no specification as to which woman the *shaliach* should approach, he has no idea who his *shaliach* married on his behalf. Any woman he might now wish to marry could be a sister, mother, or daughter of the woman who is already, unknowingly, his wife. He is therefore forbidden to everyone.

This ruling calls to mind, in a somewhat whimsical vein, the *vort* that *Lavan* sought to destroy everything (*bi-kesh Lavan avinu la-akor et hakol*) — for if *Avraham*'s servant had married a woman without specification and never returned, *Yitzchak* would have been unable to marry anyone, since every woman in the world might be a relative of that unknown wife. The joke collapses, of course, since *Avraham* did in fact give *Eliezer* specificity — "*lo sikach isha livni mibnos Kena'ani*" — but the *gemara* here serves as its basis.

### Tosafot Fine

Tosafot immediately raises an obvious difficulty: if this man is forbidden to every woman because any one of them might be his wife's sister or mother, then by the same logic, how can any man in the entire world marry any woman? Perhaps the woman he wishes to marry is the very woman the *shaliach* betrothed?

Tosafot resolves this by invoking the position of Rabbeinu Tam that women are believed when they testify that they were not the ones who accepted *kiddushin*. So any adult woman can simply testify on her own behalf. But this does not help with *ketanot* — minor girls who would not know whether their father accepted *kiddushin* on their behalf. How, then, can anyone marry a minor?

Tosafot answers that on a *d'Oraita* level, the man can actually marry any woman. We follow the majority — *azlinan batar ruba* — since most women are not relatives of the woman the *shaliach* married. The prohibition on the man himself is not a genuine *issur* but rather a *knas b'alma* — a fine — imposed upon him for having sent a *shaliach* with no specification at all, handing him a blank check without identifying even a general pool of eligible women. Other people in the world are not subject to this fine; it is targeted solely at the one who acted irresponsibly.

### Kavua Principle

Tosafot presses further: even granting the majority principle, why should this not constitute a *kavua* situation and therefore trigger a fifty-fifty *safek* rather than a lenient *rov*? The principle *kol kavua k'mechtza al mechtza dami* holds that when a forbidden thing occupies a fixed place, we cannot simply dissolve it into percentage probabilities; we must treat it as an even doubt.

The classic illustration: if one finds a piece of meat on the ground and does not know which butcher shop it came from, and the majority of shops in the neighborhood sell kosher meat, one may follow the majority and assume it is kosher — *kol d'parish meruba parish*, what has separated itself has separated from the majority. But if one walked into a store, purchased meat, and later cannot remember which store one entered, the analysis changes. One is not simply asking about the identity of the meat in the abstract; one is asking which specific store — each standing in its fixed place — one walked into. Since each store is concrete and recognizable, not dissolved into an abstract mixture, we treat the question as fifty-fifty.

The logic is difficult to articulate precisely, and the *gemara* cites a *pasuk* as its basis. One way to approach it: when the forbidden option has a fixed, concrete identity that stands before you, you cannot simply think away from it by invoking percentages. It is not lost in a mixture. The forbidden store is right there; you cannot pretend it is only abstractly possible.

Tosafot raises the question of whether the women in this case should constitute *kavua* — the *shaliach* went to wherever the woman was, she was in her home, that is a fixed place, analogous to the store he entered. Why should we not apply *kavua* and treat every woman as a fifty-fifty doubt?

Tosafot's answer is that *kavua* applies only when the forbidden thing is distinctly recognizable as itself — *nikkar l'atzmo* — and the permitted thing is equally recognizable as itself. You know which store sells *treif*, you know which sells *kosher*; you simply do not know which one you entered. That is the scenario where you cannot ignore the concrete possibility. But in the case of the woman, it is not that you have a woman standing before you whom you know to be your wife's sister but are unsure whether you entered "that house." It is simply a question of identity in the abstract: you do not know who the *shaliach* married, and you do not know whether this woman has any relationship to that unknown person. That is closer in structure to finding meat on the floor — a question of abstract identity — not to walking into a fixed store. *Kavua*, therefore, does not apply.

## Reish Lakish Challenge

### Bird Pairs Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קֵן סְתוּמָה שֶׁפָּרְחָה גּוֹזָל אֶחָד מֵהֶן לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם, אוֹ שֶׁפָּרְחָה לְבֵין חַטָּאוֹת הַמֵּתוֹת, אוֹ שֶׁמֵּת אֶחָד מֵהֶן — יִקַּח זוּג לַשֵּׁנִי. וְאִילּוּ קֵן מְפוֹרֶשֶׁת — אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish raised an objection to Rabbi Yoḥanan** from a mishna (*Kinnim* 2:1): With regard to an impure person who comes to undergo his purification process, and for this purpose set aside **an unspecified nest,** meaning a pair of turtledoves or pigeons, to use for his offerings. One is to be a burnt-offering and one is to be a sin-offering, and he had not yet specified which bird will be used for which offering. If **one fledgling of the** pair **flew away** and escaped **to the** open **air of the world, or** if it **flew among** birds invalidated for **sin-offerings that** have been left to **die, or if one of them died,** in each of these cases the owner of the nest **purchases a partner for the second,** i.e., remaining, bird. At that point he may decide which is for a sin-offering and which is for a burnt-offering. **But** if a fledgling flew away or died from **a specified nest,** after the owner had designated which bird would be used for which offering, and it is not known which bird escaped, **it has no** means of **remedy.** This is because he does not know whether the remaining fledgling is for a burnt-offering or a sin-offering.

Reish Lakish poses a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan from a related domain. There is a category of *korban* — brought by a woman who has given birth, a *zavah*, or a *zav* — that consists of a pair of birds, one to serve as a *chatas* and one as an *olah*. One may purchase these as a unit, a *kan*, and both birds are immediately sanctified, but their respective identities — which is the *chatas* and which the *olah* — remain unspecified until a later point. This is called a *kan stuma*, an unexplicated pair.

If one bird from a *kan stuma* flies away, or flies into a group of birds that must be put down and is lost among them, or dies, the remaining bird can simply be paired with a new replacement, since no identity has yet been fixed. This is the advantage of the *stuma* arrangement. Had the pair been explicit — *kan meforeshas* — the loss of one bird would have been far more problematic, since one would not know whether the *chatas* or the *olah* had been lost.

The challenge Reish Lakish raises concerns all the *kinim* in the world. One of these birds has flown somewhere out there, carrying a sanctity — an unresolved *kedushat korban* — as it wanders. How can anyone ever bring a bird as a *korban* again? Any bird one now brings might be that escaped bird, already designated for someone else's *korban*. The parallel to Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is exact: just as any woman might be the *shaliach*'s unknown wife, any bird might be the escaped *korban* bird.

Tosafot notes that this challenge is not simply about other people. Even the person who lost the bird faces the question: when he goes to bring a new *korban* using birds, how does he know that none of these birds is his own escaped one?

### Fixed Places

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קָאָמֵינָא אֲנָא אִשָּׁה דְּלָא נָיְידָא, וְאָמְרַתְּ לִי אַתְּ אִיסּוּרָא דְּנָיֵיד? וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכָא נָמֵי נָיֵיד, אֵימוֹר בְּשׁוּקָא אַשְׁכַּח וְקַדֵּישׁ, הָתָם הָדְרָא לְנִיחוּתָא, גַּבֵּי קֵן מִי הָדְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Reish Lakish: **I state** my opinion that there is a concern for other women only with regard to **a woman, who does not move** but is fixed in her home. An unidentified item is presumed to have the same legal status as the majority of items like it, and there is no concern that it may be forbidden, even if there are some forbidden items like it. However, if this unidentified item was fixed in its place, there is an equal presumption that it may belong to the class of similar permitted items or to the class of similar forbidden items. Consequently, all women in the world are forbidden to him, as there is an equal presumption that she may or may not be a relative of the woman that the agent betrothed to him. **And you speak to me** of **a prohibition that moves** and is not fixed. Since the fledgling bird is not fixed in one spot, the majority is followed and the minority is ignored. **And if you would say: Here too,** she **moves,** since it is possible to **say that** the agent **found** the woman **in the marketplace and betrothed her** there to the one who appointed the agent, so the prohibited woman was not in a fixed location? Nevertheless, **there** she eventually **returns to her** place of **rest,** i.e., her home, and is therefore considered to be fixed. Conversely, **with regard to a nest, does** the fledgling **return** to a fixed place? Since it does not, the assumption is that any bird has the status of the majority of birds in the world, which have not been consecrated as offerings.

Rabbi Yochanan's response draws precisely the distinction Tosafot had articulated. *Kamina ana isha d'lo nayda* — I am speaking of a woman, who does not simply fly off. People have fixed places; they live somewhere, they can be found. Even if a woman goes to the marketplace, *hadar lenichusa* — she returns home. Women are therefore more analogous to the stores standing in their fixed places than to things dissolved into an anonymous mixture.

Birds, by contrast, are fundamentally mobile. A bird that has flown out into the world is genuinely lost in the mass of birds everywhere. That is a question of abstract identity — like the piece of meat found on the floor — and for such cases one simply follows the majority. There is no *kavua* concern because there is no fixed place.

### Recognizable Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ לֹא בַּת, וְלֹא בַּת בַּת, וְלֹא בַּת בֵּן, וְלֹא אֵם, וְלֹא אֵם אֵם, וְלֹא אָחוֹת. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהָיְתָה לָהּ אָחוֹת וְנִתְגָּרְשָׁה לְאַחַר מִכָּאן, דְּהָהִיא שַׁרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And Rabbi Yoḥanan concedes** that with regard to **a woman who does not have a daughter, nor a daughter of a daughter, nor a daughter of a son, nor a mother, nor a mother of a mother, nor a sister** who was single when he appointed the agent, that the one who appointed the agent may betroth her. There is no concern that the agent might have betrothed a relative that would now render such a woman forbidden. **And even though she had,** when he appointed the agent, a married **sister,** or daughter, or other female relative, **and she was later divorced,** Rabbi Yoḥanan concedes **that this** woman whom he wishes to now marry is **permitted.**

Following on from Rabbi Yochanan's response, the *gemara* cites Rava, who clarifies the limits of the position. Rabbi Yochanan concedes that if the woman one wishes to marry has no living relatives whatsoever — no daughter, granddaughter, mother, grandmother, or sister — one may marry her even under the shadow of the unknown *shaliach* marriage, since there is no relative whose identity could create a problem.

The more striking ruling is this: even if the woman did have a sister — and that sister was married to another man at the time the *shaliach* was appointed — one may marry this woman. The reasoning is *d'ba'ei sha'ata d'kamar lei havah nasuy l'gavrei*: at the moment the *shaliach* was appointed, the sister was already married to someone else. She was, at that moment, unavailable. And the principle is *ki mashvi shaliach bemilta d'kaima kamei* — one only empowers a *shaliach* in matters that stand before one as currently available options. *Bemilta d'lo kaima kamei lo mashvi shaliach* — for things not currently available, one simply does not grant the *shaliach* power.

This is a striking and counterintuitive *halacha*. The implication is that even if the sister subsequently became divorced — after the *shaliach* was appointed but before he carried out the marriage — and even if the *shaliach* then married her, it would not be binding, because he was never empowered to take that action. His authority was fixed at the moment of appointment, limited to what was then accessible. The *gemara* will develop the implications of this principle.

## Agent Empowerment

### Sister Divorced Later

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא — דִּבְהָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא דְּקָא אָמַר לֵיהּ, הֲוָה נְסִיבָן לְגַבְרֵי. כִּי מְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ — בְּמִילְּתָא דְּקָיְימָא קַמֵּיהּ. בְּמִילְּתָא דְּלָא קָיְימָא קַמֵּיהּ — לָא מְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this? It is **that at that moment, when he told** the agent to go betroth a woman on his behalf, these women **were married to men.** There is a principle that **when one appoints an agent,** he does so only **with regard to a matter present before him.** In other words, he instructs his agent to betroth a woman who is available at that time, but **he does not appoint an agent with regard to a matter that is not present before him.** Consequently, even if the agent betrothed one of the relatives of the woman after they were divorced, the betrothal would not take effect because he was not authorized to betroth them, and therefore the one who appointed the agent is permitted to the woman.

The rule that a *shaliach* is empowered only in currently available matters now connects directly to the *mishnah*. But before reaching that connection, the *gemara* pauses to clarify what the underlying principle actually is.

The case in question: I sent a *shaliach* to marry a woman for me. This woman has a sister who, at the time I appointed him, was married. Subsequently — sometime during the year I have not heard from the *shaliach* — the sister gets divorced. Can I now marry this sister?

The answer is yes, and the reason is that the *shaliach* was never empowered to marry the sister in the first place, because she was unavailable at the time of appointment. Since she could not have been the woman he married on my behalf, she poses no danger of being my wife or my wife's sister. I may therefore marry her freely.

The scenario that creates the genuine difficulty is the following: the *shaliach* departs, the sister subsequently gets divorced, and the *shaliach* — now encountering the sister in her state of availability — goes ahead and betroths her. Why should this not be binding? He is marrying a perfectly available woman, on behalf of a man who has authorized him to marry anyone.

### Thought vs. Power

One might understand the limitation as a matter of *da'at* — the man's intention at the time of appointment. When he sent the *shaliach*, he was only thinking about women who were then available; the sister simply was not in his mind. This reading suggests the limitation is cognitive: if he had explicitly said "and for any woman who becomes available later," it would work.

But the *gemara* moves toward a different and more fundamental understanding: this is not merely a question of what was in the man's mind. It is a structural limitation on the power of *shelichut* itself.

### Current Availability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא מְשַׁוֵּי אִינִישׁ שָׁלִיחַ אֶלָּא בְּמִילְּתָא דְּמָצֵי עָבֵיד הַשְׁתָּא, בְּמִילְּתָא דְּלָא מָצֵי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ הַשְׁתָּא — לָא מְשַׁוֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this comparison: The Sages **say** that there is a distinction between the two cases: **A person appoints an agent only for a matter that he** himself **can perform now,** at the time of the appointment, **but for a matter that he cannot perform now, he does not appoint** an agent. Consequently, the agent cannot betroth a woman who was married at the time of his appointment. The one who appointed the agent may therefore marry the sister of the recently divorced woman, as stated by Rava.

The *gemara* articulates the principle explicitly: *lo mashvei inish shaliach ela bemidi demati avid hashta* — a person can only empower a *shaliach* in something that he himself is currently capable of doing. *Bemidi delo mati avid lei hashta, lo mashvei* — for something outside his current capability, he simply cannot grant the power of agency.

This is a remarkable limitation. It seems to say that the scope of *shelichut* is not merely what one intends or instructs, but is bounded by what one could actually have executed at the moment of appointment. If I cannot do a given act right now — even though I will be able to do it later, and even though by the time the *shaliach* acts the situation will have changed — I never succeeded in granting the *shaliach* that power in the first place.

### Future Nazirite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר וְעָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ נָזִיר״, וְשָׁמַע חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר: ״וַאֲנִי, וְעָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ נָזִיר״, אִם הָיוּ פִּקְחִין — מְגַלְּחִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, וְאִם לָאו — מְגַלְּחִין נְזִירִים אֲחֵרִים. בִּשְׁלָמָא בָּתְרָאָה — אִיכָּא קַדְמָאָה קַמֵּיהּ. אֶלָּא קַדְמָאָה — מִי אִיכָּא בָּתְרָאָה קַמֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now proceeds to raise a difficulty against this last argument from the mishna: **We learned** in the mishna that if one says: **I am hereby a nazirite and it is** incumbent **upon me to shave a nazirite, and another heard** him **and said: And I** am hereby a nazirite, **and it is** incumbent **upon me to shave a nazirite, if they are perspicacious they shave each other; and if not, they shave other nazirites.** According to the reasoning that one takes into account only that which is possible at the time, the following difficulty arises: **Granted, the last** person **has the first one before him** and may have had in mind to volunteer to pay for the other's offerings; **but** with regard to **the first** person, **is the last one before him?** When the first one stated his vow, the other one was not yet a nazirite, and he could not have been taking him into account when he vowed to pay for the offerings of another. How can paying for the offerings of the second nazirite be considered a fulfillment of his vow?

The connection to the *mishnah* now becomes clear. The *gemara* asks: *bishlama batra* — it is understandable that the second man's vow to care for a *nazir*'s *korbanot* can include the first man, who was already a *nazir* when the second man made his declaration. *Ela kamai* — but what about the first man? When he declared that he would take care of a *nazir*'s *korbanot*, the second man had not yet become a *nazir*. How can his vow cover someone who did not yet exist as a *nazir*?

The *gemara* resolves this by reading the vow's intent: *im eshkachna dehavi nazir* — what the man meant was, whoever I find to be a *nazir* when the time comes for me to fulfill my vow. He is not committing to care for a specific, currently existing *nazir*; he is committing to care for whoever will be a *nazir* at the relevant time. This reading suggests that one's commitments naturally extend to future contingencies.

By the same logic, one might argue that when I send a *shaliach* to marry a woman, I implicitly mean: whoever is available when you get around to it, including women who become available after I send you. *Im eshkachna demigaresha* — if you find a woman who has since been divorced, go ahead and marry her for me.

But the *gemara* rejects this: *lo mashvei inish shaliach ela bemidi demati avid hashta*. Even if this is within my *da'at* — even if this is what I intended — the structural rule of *shelichut* prevents it. I cannot grant power for something I could not have done at the moment of appointment. The *mishnah* case works for a different reason: the man is not appointing a *shaliach* at all. He is making a personal vow about his own future action, and personal vows can certainly be directed at future contingencies. The limit applies to *shelichut*, not to personal undertakings.

## Vow Annulment

### Guardian Annuls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, הָאוֹמֵר לְאַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס שֶׁלּוֹ: ״כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁתִּדּוֹר אִשְׁתִּי מִכָּאן עַד שֶׁאָבוֹא מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי — הָפֵר לָהּ״, וְהֵפֵר לָהּ, יָכוֹל יְהוּ מוּפָרִין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישָׁהּ יְקִימֶנּוּ וְאִישָׁהּ יְפֵרֶנּוּ״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. וְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: מָצִינוּ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁשְּׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question on the ruling of Rava: **And** can one **not** appoint an agent to betroth a woman in this manner? **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* that indicates the contrary: **One who says to the steward [*apotropos*] of his** affairs: **All vows that my wife will vow from now until I come from such and such a place, nullify for her, and** the steward **nullified** them **for her,** one **might** have thought **they are nullified.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Her husband sustains** the vow **and her husband nullifies** the vow" (Numbers 30:14); this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.** The repetition of "husband" teaches that it is the husband alone who may nullify his wife's vows. **And Rabbi Yonatan says: We have found in all places that** the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself.** Therefore, a steward, who serves as the husband's agent, may nullify the wife's vows.

The discussion of *shelichut* and current availability raises a further question, tested against a case from the laws of vow annulment. If a man, about to depart on a journey, tells his *epitropos* — his estate manager, the one who holds his power of attorney — to annul any vow his wife makes during his absence, and the *epitropos* does indeed annul such vows upon hearing them, can those annulments stand?

### Yoshiya vs. Yonatan

Rabbi Yoshiya says no. He derives this from the verse *isha yekimenu v'isha yiferenu* — the husband himself must confirm or annul; there is a specific textual requirement that the husband perform this act personally. An agent cannot substitute for the husband here.

Rabbi Yonatan disagrees: *matzinu bechol makom she-shlucho shel adam kemoto* — we find everywhere that one's agent is as oneself. The one place in the *mishnah* where this principle is stated explicitly is in the context of a *shaliach tzibur* for *Shemoneh Esrei*, as cited in Berachot 34b. Why should the principle not apply here as well?

### Verse Obstacle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״אִישָׁהּ יְקִימֶנּוּ וְאִישָׁהּ יְפֵירֶנּוּ״, הָא לָאו הָכִי — אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס מֵיפֵר. וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי דִידֵיהּ תַּנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ ״כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁתִּדּוֹרִי מִכָּאן וְעַד שֶׁאָבֹא מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי יְהוּ קַיָּימִין״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. ״הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין״, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מוּפָר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ מוּפָר.
>
> **Translation:** Even according to Rabbi Yoshiya, who maintains that the steward cannot nullify her vows, **the reason is that the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"Her husband sustains** the vow **and her husband nullifies** the vow," **but if** it were **not so,** the **steward could nullify** even the future vows of the wife. **However, with regard to** the husband **himself** it **is taught** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 75a): **One who says to his wife: All vows that you will vow from now until I come from such and such a place shall be ratified, he has not said anything.** However, if a husband says: All vows that you will vow from now until I come from such and such a place, **they are hereby nullified, Rabbi Eliezer says: It is nullified, and the Rabbis say: It is not nullified.**

The *gemara* now uses this debate to test the principle of *shelichut* and current availability. The husband who appoints the *epitropos* cannot himself annul his wife's future vows at the moment of appointment, because those vows do not yet exist. The question is whether this constitutes an argument against appointing the *epitropos* at all.

The *gemara* reasons: if we assume that the husband himself is incapable of pre-annulling vows — that is, if we follow the *chachamim* who say in a related context that *lo matzi mefer* — then the very act of appointing an *epitropos* is a case of empowering a *shaliach* for something one cannot currently do. And yet Rabbi Yonatan says it works. This seems to directly contradict the principle that one can only empower a *shaliach* in currently available matters.

The *gemara* resolves this by suggesting that perhaps both Rabbi Yoshiya and Rabbi Yonatan are operating according to the view of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that a husband *can* pre-annul his wife's future vows. Under Rabbi Eliezer's view, there is no contradiction: the husband already has the current capability to annul future vows himself, and he therefore has the full power to delegate that capability to an *epitropos*. The reason he is appointing an *epitropos* rather than simply pre-annulling the vows himself is not inability but practicality — he might forget when he returns, or he might be angry, or distracted, and the annulment window will close.

### Pre-annul Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין כִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה — אַלִּיבָּא דְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי לָא מָצֵי מֵיפֵר, וְאִי לָאו דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״אִישָׁהּ יְקִימֶנּוּ וְאִישָׁהּ יְפֵירֶנּוּ״, אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס הֲוָה מֵיפַר. וְדִלְמָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר מָצֵי מֵיפַר! אִי הָכִי, לְמָה לִי לְשַׁוּוֹיֵי שָׁלִיחַ? לֵיפַר לַהּ אִיהוּ! קָסָבַר דִּלְמָא מִשְׁתְּלֵינָא, אוֹ רָתַחְנָא, אוֹ מִיטְּרִידְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara finishes the question: **It enters our mind** to say that **when Rabbi Yoshiya said** that the steward cannot nullify the vows, he spoke **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that the husband **is not able to nullify** her vows ahead of time, **and** yet, even according to their approach, **if the Merciful One had not stated** in the Torah: **"Her husband sustains** the vow **and her husband nullifies** the vow" the **steward would** be able to **nullify** such vows. This proves that one can appoint an agent for something he himself cannot do at the time, which contradicts the statement of Rava. The Gemara rejects this: **And perhaps** Rabbi Yoshiya spoke **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who says** the husband **can nullify** the vows ahead of time, and it is for this reason that he can appoint the steward to do the same. The Gemara rejects this: **If so, why do I** need **to make him into an agent; let him nullify** the future vows **for her** before he sets out on his journey. The Gemara answers: **He thinks, perhaps I will forget, or become angry, or be occupied** with other matters when I am about to set out on my journey. This is why he appoints an agent to nullify the vows on his behalf, and no proof can be derived from this *baraita*.

But this only pushes the question back: if Rabbi Eliezer's position allows the husband to pre-annul his wife's vows right now, why bother with the *epitropos* at all? Just do it yourself before you leave.

Tosafot addresses this. Looking at the *tosafot* discussion of *dilma mishtalyana*, he explains that a husband who pre-annuls his wife's vows explicitly — saying to her directly, "any vow you make while I am away, I hereby void" — creates a confrontation. *Tipul ketata beineihem*: it leads to a fight. It is disempowering to a wife to be told before any vow is even made that her vows mean nothing. But if the *epitropos* handles it quietly in the husband's absence, reacting to each vow as it arises, the wife's dignity is preserved and a direct confrontation is avoided. The husband prefers not to raise the issue at home before he leaves — and certainly not face-to-face — which is precisely why he makes the arrangement quietly with the *epitropos*.

The *gemara*'s own answer to this question is somewhat unsatisfying, and Tosafot's response fills the gap. In any case, the *gemara*'s conclusion is that the limitation on *shelichut* — that one can only empower an agent in currently available matters — remains intact. The *epitropos* case does not contradict it, because under Rabbi Eliezer, the husband does have current capability.

## Half Nazirite

### Two Men Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵי עָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ חֲצִי נָזִיר״, וְשָׁמַע חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר ״וַאֲנִי עָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ חֲצִי נָזִיר״ — זֶה מְגַלֵּחַ נָזִיר שָׁלֵם, וְזֶה מְגַלֵּחַ נָזִיר שָׁלֵם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זֶה מְגַלֵּחַ חֲצִי נָזִיר, וְזֶה מְגַלֵּחַ חֲצִי נָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: **It is** incumbent **upon me to shave half a nazirite,** i.e., he is vowing to pay half the costs of a nazirite's offerings, **and another heard and said: And I, it is** incumbent **upon me to shave half a nazirite, this** one **shaves a whole nazirite and that** one **shaves a whole nazirite,** i.e., each pays the full cost of a nazirite's offerings; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir,** since there is no such entity as half a nazirite. **And the Rabbis say: This** one **shaves half a nazirite and that** one **shaves half a nazirite;** they may join together to pay for the offerings of one nazirite.

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah* at hand. The next *mishnah* presents the following case: one man says *harei alai legaleiach chatzi nazir* — I accept upon myself to shave half a *nazir*, meaning to bring the *korbanot* for half a *nazir*. Another man hears and says the same thing.

### Meir vs. Sages

Rabbi Meir says: each of them is obligated to bring a full set of *korbanot* for a *nazir shaleim*. The *chachamim* say: each is only obligated to bring half.

Tosafot notes that the second man is structurally superfluous to the core debate; the real question is simply what the phrase *alai legaleiach chatzi nazir* means for a single individual. The *mishnah* introduces two men only to parallel the structure of the previous *mishnah*.

### Grammar of Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר ״חֲצִי קׇרְבָּנוֹת נָזִיר עָלַי״ — חֲצִי קׇרְבָּן מַיְיתֵי. ״קׇרְבְּנוֹת חֲצִי נָזִיר עָלַי״ — כּוּלֵּיהּ קׇרְבָּן בָּעֵי אֵיתוֹיֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּהָא לָא אַשְׁכְּחַן נְזִירוּת לְפַלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to this dispute, **Rava said: All concede** that **whenever one said: Half of the offerings of a nazirite are** incumbent **upon me, he brings half of the offerings,** since he vowed to pay only that amount. Also, everyone agrees that if he said: **The offerings of half a nazirite are** incumbent **upon me, he needs to bring all of the offerings** of a nazirite. **What is the reason** that he must bring all of the offerings of a nazirite? It is **that we have not found** such an entity as **half a naziriteship.** If one vowed to be half a nazirite, he is a full nazirite.

Rava clarifies the terms of the dispute. Everyone would agree on the following: if one explicitly says *chatzi korbanot nazir alai* — half of the *korbanot* of a *nazir* I accept upon myself — one brings half the *korbanot*. The word *chatzi* is clearly modifying *korbanot*, and half an offering is what one owes.

Conversely, if one says *korbanot chatzi nazir alai* — the *korbanot* of a half-*nazir* I accept upon myself — one brings a full set of *korbanot*. Why? Because *d'ha lo askan nezirut le-palga* — there is no such thing as a half-*nazir*. A person who is half a *nazir* is simply a *nazir*. "Half a *nazir*" is simply a *nazir*, and whoever is such a person has a full complement of *korbanot*. When one says *chatzi nazir*, the word *chatzi* attaches to the person, and that person's *korbanot* are full.

The dispute in the *mishnah* concerns the language *alai legaleiach chatzi nazir*, which reads as though *chatzi* is modifying *nazir* — in which case, following the above logic, one would owe a full *korban*. This is, on its face, Rabbi Meir's position. What requires explanation is why the *chachamim* read this differently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי פְּלִיגִי, בְּלִישָּׁנָא דְמַתְנִיתִין פְּלִיגִי. רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי״ — אִיחַיַּיב אַכּוּלֵּיהּ קׇרְבַּן נְזִירוּת, וְכִי קָאָמַר חֲצִי נְזִירוּת — לָאו כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: נֶדֶר וּפֶתַח עִמּוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And when they disagree,** it is only **in** a case of one who used **the** precise **wording of the mishna. Rabbi Meir holds** that **once he said: It is** incumbent **upon me, he is obligated in all of the naziriteship offerings, and when he** later **says: Half a naziriteship,** it **is not in his power** to uproot his first obligation. **And the Rabbis hold** that **it is a vow with** its inherent **opening.** By saying he had only half the offerings of a nazirite in mind from the outset, he has nullified his own vow.

Rabbi Meir's reasoning: *keivan de'amar harei alai*, once one has said *harei alai*, one has obligated oneself in the full *korbanot* of *nezirut*. When one then says *chatzi nazir*, that addition is simply vacuous — *lav kol kemeineih* — it cannot undo what *harei alai* already established.

The *chachamim*'s reasoning: *neder u-fetach imo hu* — there is a vow and its opening together. The latter part of the phrase explains and limits the earlier part. The entire declaration must be read as a unit, with the end clarifying the scope of the beginning.

### Tosafot Grammar

What remains puzzling is why, if the phrase *alai legaleiach chatzi nazir* looks structurally parallel to *korbanot chatzi nazir*, it should produce a different result for the *chachamim*. In both cases, *chatzi* seems to be modifying the word *nazir*, which should yield a full *korban*.

Tosafot grapples with this. The conclusion Tosafot reaches is that the presence of the word *alai* up front, before *legaleiach*, makes a structural difference. When one says *harei alai legaleiach chatzi nazir*, the *alai* — the acceptance of obligation — comes first, and then *chatzi* follows. According to the *chachamim*, *chatzi* is thus understood as modifying the *alai*, the scope of what one is accepting: I am accepting upon myself half of the obligations involved. This is equivalent in import to saying *chatzi korbanot nazir alai*, with *chatzi* clearly modifying the quantum of obligation rather than the identity of the *nazir*.

It must be admitted that this distinction is subtle, and the grammatical argument does not yield itself to clean exposition. The fundamental point, however, is that the debate turns on whether the word *chatzi* in this construction limits the person — a half-*nazir*, who is a full *nazir* — or limits the obligation — half the *korbanot* of a *nazir*.

## Conditional Birth

### Vow Upon Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר לִכְשֶׁיִּהְיֶה לִי בֵּן״, וְנוֹלָד לוֹ בֵּן — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר. נוֹלַד לוֹ בַּת, טוּמְטוּם, וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — אֵינוֹ נָזִיר. אִם אָמַר ״כְּשֶׁיִּהְיֶה לִי וָלָד״ — אֲפִילּוּ נוֹלַד לוֹ בַּת, טוּמְטוּם, וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one said: **I am hereby a nazirite when I will have a son, and a son was born to him, he is a nazirite.** If **a daughter, a *tumtum*, or a hermaphrodite [*androginos*] is born to him, he is not a nazirite,** since a son was not born to him. However, **if he says:** I am hereby a nazirite **when I will have a child,** then **even if a daughter, a *tumtum*, or a hermaphrodite is born to him, he is a nazirite.**

The final *mishnah* of the daf introduces a new type of conditional *nazir* vow. A man declares: *hareini nazir liksheyihyeh li ben* — I will be a *nazir* when I have a son. If a son is subsequently born to him, he becomes a *nazir*.

### Tumtum Question

What if the child born is a daughter, or a *tumtum* — a child whose genitalia are covered, making the sex indeterminate — or an *androgynos*, a child with both male and female genitalia? In all these cases, *eino nazir* — he is not a *nazir*. The vow specified a *ben*, a definite male child. Even a *tumtum*, who represents a *safek* as to sex, does not satisfy the condition, because when one says *ben* one means a certain, unambiguous male.

If, however, the man had said *kesheyihyeh li valad* — when I have a child — then even a daughter, a *tumtum*, or an *androgynos* triggers the *nezirus*, since the word *valad* encompasses all offspring.

If his wife miscarried, *eino nazir* — he is not a *nazir*, because the vow specified *nolad*, the child being born. A miscarriage does not satisfy *nolad*.

### Doubtful Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִפִּילָה אִשְׁתּוֹ — אֵינוֹ נָזִיר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֹאמַר ״אִם הָיָה בֶּן קַיָּימָא — הֲרֵי אֲנִי נְזִיר חוֹבָה, וְאִם לָאו — הֲרֵי אֲנִי נְזִיר נְדָבָה״.
>
> **Translation:** However, if **his wife miscarried he is not a nazirite,** since his wife did not give birth to a live child. **Rabbi Shimon says:** Since it is possible that the fetus was viable, in which case his vow of naziriteship takes effect, **he should say** the following: **If** this fetus **was viable** in terms of its development but died due to other causes, **I am hereby an obligatory nazirite** in fulfillment of my vow; **and if** it was **not** viable, **I am hereby a voluntary nazirite.** He then proceeds to observe naziriteship.

Rabbi Shimon, however, takes a different position. He holds that if the fetus miscarried but might have been viable — *ben kayama* — and might have been male, then the man is in a state of *safek nezirus*. Rabbi Shimon's reasoning is twofold: first, he considers a miscarriage of a potentially viable fetus to qualify as *leidah* for the purposes of this vow; second, he treats even a *safek* outcome as sufficient to create a *safek* vow obligation.

Being a *safek nazir* is a deeply problematic position. One must observe *nezirus* because one might be a *nazir*, but one cannot bring the concluding *korbanot* because one might not be a *nazir* at all. The situation is entirely unresolvable.

Rabbi Shimon therefore prescribes a declaration to escape the bind. The man should say: if the first child was a viable male — *im haya ben kayama* — then *harei ani nazir hova*, I am now completing the obligatory *nezirus* already triggered. And if it was not — *ve-im lav* — then *harei ani nazir nedava*, I am now taking on *nezirus* as a voluntary act. By this formula, the man commits himself either way to observing *nezirus* and may bring his *korbanot*, since regardless of which scenario obtains, he is now definitively a *nazir* under one heading or the other.

### Second Child

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזְרָה וְיָלְדָה — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֹאמַר ״אִם הָרִאשׁוֹן בֶּן קַיָּימָא — הָרִאשׁוֹן חוֹבָה וְזוֹ נְדָבָה. וְאִם לָאו — הָרִאשׁוֹן נְדָבָה וְזוֹ חוֹבָה״.
>
> **Translation:** If, subsequent to this, his wife **gave birth again, he is a nazirite,** since the unattributed opinion in the mishna holds that the condition of his vow has now been fulfilled. **Rabbi Shimon says,** following his earlier ruling: He must now accept upon himself an additional naziriteship and **he should say: If the first** fetus was **viable** then my naziriteship for **the first** child was **obligatory, and this** naziriteship is **voluntary; and if** the first child was **not** viable, then the naziriteship for **the first** one was **voluntary and this** naziriteship is **obligatory.**

If the wife then gives birth a second time — a viable child — the *chachamim*'s analysis is straightforward: the second birth is what triggers the *nezirus*, and *harei zeh nazir* — he is a *nazir*.

For Rabbi Shimon, however, the situation is more layered. Since the first child already placed the man in a state of *safek nezirus*, the second child now creates a new *safek*: perhaps the first child was the triggering event, in which case the *nezirus* has already been completed (or is in process), and the second child is irrelevant. Or perhaps the first was not triggering, and the second is the true trigger — creating yet another *safek nezirus*.

Rabbi Shimon again prescribes a two-part declaration. The man should say: if the first child was viable, *harishon hova* — the first period of *nezirus* I am now completing was the obligatory one, and *vezu nedava* — the *nezirus* I now take on for the second child is voluntary. If the first was not viable, *harishon nedava* — the first period was voluntary, and *vezu hova* — this one, for the second child, is the obligatory fulfillment of my vow.
