# Avodah Zarah 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nosein Taam Principles

### Mashechta Overview

The present daf continues in that portion of Masekhes *Avodah Zarah* that has become the classic locus for the study of *kashrus* and *bittul*. As we have seen in preceding pages, the *masechta* takes up questions of mixtures and prohibited substances with a rigor that has made it indispensable to later *posekim*. The *Mishnah* introduced the foundational concept of *nosein taam*: a prohibited substance creates a problem only if it imparts a detectable flavor to the permitted mixture in which it falls.

### Nosein Taam Lifgam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁבַּהֲנָאָתוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם — אָסוּר, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין בַּהֲנָאָתוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם — מוּתָּר, כְּגוֹן חוֹמֶץ שֶׁנָּפַל עַל גַּבֵּי גְּרִיסִין.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle: Anything that benefits** from a forbidden item **imparting flavor** to it, i.e., the forbidden item contributes a positive taste to it, is **forbidden,** and **anything that does not benefit** from a forbidden item **imparting flavor** to it is **permitted, such as** forbidden **vinegar that fell onto** split **beans,** as the flavor imparted by the vinegar does not enhance the taste of the beans.

Even within the framework of *nosein taam*, there is an important qualification. If the prohibited substance imparts a *nosein taam lifgam* — a deteriorating or off-putting taste — it is not considered to have made its presence felt in any meaningful sense, and there is no prohibition. The question that drives the *sugya* forward is precisely how to measure and characterize the taste that a prohibited substance imparts, and under what conditions its presence is or is not regarded as legally significant.

### Absorption in Porous Foods

The physical mechanism of taste transfer also requires attention. The *Mishnah* addressed cases involving porous foods such as figs or dates, whose skins allow liquid to be absorbed even without heat. Grapes present a different case: ordinarily, wine or other liquids do not penetrate their skin, and rinsing the surface suffices. *Tosafos*, however, notes that this assumes the grapes remain attached to the cluster. Once a grape is plucked from its stem, the point of detachment creates an opening through which liquid can enter. In such a case, even cold liquid can be absorbed. This is a crucial distinction, as the principle of *nosein taam* applies equally whether heat drove the absorption or the porous nature of the food allowed it naturally.

### Yayin Nesech Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַמְרָא עַתִּיקָא בְּעִנְבֵי, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם. חַמְרָא חַדְתָּא בְּעִנְבֵי, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, וְרָבָא אָמַר: בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **old wine** used for a libation that fell **onto grapes, all agree** that the grapes are forbidden only **in** a case where the wine **imparts flavor** to them. With regard to **new wine** used for a libation, i.e., wine that had just been squeezed and has not yet fermented, that fell **onto grapes, Abaye says** that **any amount** renders the grapes forbidden, **and Rava says** that they are forbidden only **in** a case where the wine **imparts flavor** to them.

With these foundations in place, the *Gemara* opens *daf* 66a by taking up the special stringency that governs *yayin nesech*. In most areas of *kashrus*, prohibited mixtures are assessed through the lens of *nosein taam*: a substance is considered present and problematic only if it can be tasted. There is, however, a well-known category of *issurim* — *yayin nesech* and *chametz b'Pesach* being the paradigmatic examples — where the standard stringency is *batel be-mashehu*: even a negligible quantity, far below the threshold of taste, renders the mixture forbidden. For *yayin nesech*, this heightened stringency applies specifically when wine is mixed *min be-mino* — wine into wine, like with like. When *yayin nesech* falls into something that is not wine — soup, for example — the ordinary rule of *nosein taam* applies.

### Min BeMinο Debate

This distinction invites an obvious question: why should wine mixed with wine be *more* problematic than wine mixed with something else? The answer lies in a foundational dispute in *halacha*. The baseline rule, which reflects the majority position, is that *min be-mino* — a prohibited substance mixed with an identical permitted substance — is *batel be-rov*: as long as a simple majority of the mixture is permitted, the prohibited minority is nullified, because it imparts no distinct taste and therefore makes no independent impression. *Min be-eino mino* — a prohibited substance mixed with something of a different kind — is a stricter case, because there the prohibited substance *can* make its presence felt through taste, and therefore a mere majority does not suffice; one requires sixty times the prohibited amount.

*Rabbi Yehuda* takes the opposite position regarding *min be-mino*: he holds that *min be-mino* is never *batel*, not even in a majority. His reasoning is the mirror image of the majority view. Precisely because the two substances are identical in taste, the prohibited substance never loses its identity — every spoonful of the mixture contains the taste of the forbidden food, even if one cannot distinguish it. It does not make its presence felt distinctively, but neither does it disappear.

In practice, the *halacha* does not follow *Rabbi Yehuda* as a general rule. We do not say that every case of *min be-mino* requires more than a majority. However, when the *Rabbanan* wish to impose a heightened stringency in a specific area — as with *yayin nesech* — they construct that stringency by adopting *Rabbi Yehuda*'s reasoning selectively. They rule, in effect: for *yayin nesech*, we will apply *Rabbi Yehuda*'s principle, and *min be-mino* will be *batel be-mashehu*. Wine falling into wine, even a single drop, is forever forbidden. When wine falls into a non-wine mixture, one returns to the ordinary rule of *nosein taam*.

## Abaye-Rava: Taste vs Name

### New Wine in Grapes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, בָּתַר טַעְמָא אָזְלִינַן, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי חַד טַעְמָא הוּא, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִין בְּמִינוֹ, וּמִין בְּמִינוֹ בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their respective opinions: **Abaye says** that **any amount** renders them forbidden because he maintains that when categorizing substances in the context of the *halakhot* of mixtures **we follow the flavor,** and **this** wine **and those** grapes **are** of **the same flavor, which renders it** a case of **a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance. And** the principle is that in a case of **a substance** in contact **with** the **same** type of **substance, any amount** of the forbidden substance renders the mixture forbidden.

This framing raises a question that the *Gemara* now confronts directly: how does one define *mino* and *eino mino*? The answer is not self-evident. Two substances might share the same name but taste quite different; conversely, two things with different names might taste nearly identical. The *Gemara* introduces its discussion with a carefully chosen case: *chamra atika be-invi* — old, fermented wine absorbed into grapes. Here there is no dispute: the case is governed by *nosein taam*. Old wine and grapes differ both in taste and in name, and therefore their interaction is assessed in the ordinary way.

The genuinely interesting case is *chamra chadta be-invi* — new wine, which is essentially unfermented grape juice, absorbed into grapes. Does this constitute *min be-mino* or *min be-eino mino*? Unfermented grape juice and grapes share a remarkably similar taste, yet they bear different names. This is precisely the question that divides *Abaye* and *Rava*.

### Abaye: Taste Determines

*Abaye amar be-mashehu* — *Abaye* holds that this is *min be-mino* and the mixture is therefore forbidden even in a negligible quantity. His principle is *batar ta'ama azlinan*: we follow taste. Because grape juice and grapes taste the same — *hai ve-idach chad ta'ama hu* — they are to be treated as identical substances. The logic connects directly to the underlying theory of *min be-mino*. The question is whether the prohibited substance makes its presence felt. If it cannot be tasted independently, it does not make its presence felt in the ordinary sense; but by the same token, it does not lose its identity either, because it is all there, tasting of grape with every bite. *Abaye* thus says: since they taste the same, they are the same, and the *mashehu* rule applies.

### Rava: Name Determines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם, בָּתַר שְׁמָא אָזְלִינַן, וְהַאי שְׁמָא לְחוּד וְהַאי שְׁמָא לְחוּד, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִין בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ, וּמִין בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says** that the grapes are forbidden only **in** a case where the wine **imparts flavor** to them because he maintains that when categorizing substances in the context of the *halakhot* of mixtures **we follow the name, and this** wine has **a discrete name, and those** grapes have their own **discrete name. And** accordingly, **this is** a case of **a substance** in contact **with a different** type of **substance. And** the principle is that in a case of **a substance** in contact **with a different** type of **substance,** the mixture is forbidden only **in** a case where the forbidden item **imparts flavor** to it.

*Rava amar be-nosein taam* — *Rava* holds that the mixture is governed by *nosein taam* and is effectively permitted once the grape juice is less than one-sixtieth of the grapes. His principle is *batar shma azlinan*: we follow the name. *Ve-hai shma le-chud ve-hai shma le-chud* — grapes and wine bear different names, and different names signal different categories. Therefore this is *min be-eino mino*, and the ordinary *nosein taam* standard applies.

One might ask whether there is something philosophically unsatisfying about *Rava*'s criterion. Categories defined by names seem like a social convention rather than an intrinsic property of the objects themselves. Yet this objection arguably misses the point: *halacha* operates with real human categories, and the names by which society classifies things are a precise reflection of the categories it recognizes. If we use different names, we are saying, as a society, that we have placed these things in different classifications. *Rava* holds that those social classifications are legally operative.

### Mishna Wine-Grape Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: יֵין נֶסֶךְ שֶׁנָּפַל עַל גַּבֵּי עֲנָבִים כּוּ׳. קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין: חַמְרָא חַדְתָּא בְּעִנְבֵי, מַאי לָאו בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם? לָא, בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna that in the case of **wine** used for **a libation that fell on** cracked **grapes,** the grapes are forbidden. It **enters our mind** that this is referring to **new wine** falling **on grapes. What, is it not** the intention of the mishna that they are forbidden only **in** a case where the wine **imparts flavor** to the grapes? If so, then it is the name that counts, as the flavor of the wine in this case is the same as the grapes. The Gemara rejects this premise: **No,** the mishna means that **any amount** of the wine renders the grapes forbidden.

Having set out the dispute, the *Gemara* attempts to resolve it by appeal to the *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* states that if *yayin nesech* fell onto grapes and the grapes had cracked skins, they are forbidden — and it states this without any explicit condition of *nosein taam*. The implication seems to be that this is a case of *min be-mino be-mashehu*, which would support *Abaye*. The *Mishnah*'s end section, however, formulates the general rule as *kol she-natan ta'am assur, kol she-lo natan ta'am mutar* — the principle is always *nosein taam*. Since the *Mishnah*'s own stated rule is *nosein taam*, the earlier case involving cracked grapes must also be understood in those terms, which seems to support *Rava*.

### Gemara Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁבַּהֲנָאָתוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם — אָסוּר, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין בַּהֲנָאָתוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם — מוּתָּר, מִכְּלָל דִּבְנוֹתֵן טַעַם עָסְקִינַן! וְאַבָּיֵי, מַתְנִיתִין בְּחַמְרָא עַתִּיקָא בְּעִנְבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation. **But from** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches** in **the last clause: This is the principle: Anything that benefits** from a forbidden item **imparting flavor** to it is **forbidden,** and **anything that does not benefit** from a forbidden item **imparting flavor** to it is **permitted, by inference, we are dealing with** a case where the forbidden item **imparts flavor** to the permitted item. **And** how does **Abaye** explain this? In his opinion, the ruling of **the mishna** is stated **with regard to old wine** that fell **onto grapes,** which are not of the same flavor, so it is reasonable that the grapes are rendered forbidden by an amount that imparts flavor.

*Abaye* responds to this challenge cleanly: *matnitin be-chamra atika be-invi* — the *Mishnah* is discussing old wine absorbed into grapes, not new wine. Old wine is fermented; it has a fundamentally different taste than the grape itself. When the *Mishnah* says the grapes are forbidden subject to the *nosein taam* rule, it is speaking of a case where the wine and the grapes are distinct in taste, just as both *Abaye* and *Rava* agree. The case of new wine — grape juice — absorbed into grapes remains unresolved by the *Mishnah*, and *Abaye*'s position that such a mixture is *mashehu* because taste determines identity still stands.

It is worth noting that *Abaye* and *Rava*'s positions are not simply a *chumra* versus *kula* in all cases. The case of new wine into grapes makes *Abaye*'s position the stricter one: since they taste the same, it is *min be-mino* and therefore *mashehu*. But the same principle can operate in reverse. If one has wine vinegar and beer vinegar — or sourdough of wheat and sourdough of barley — both examples bear the same name but taste quite differently. There *Abaye* would say they are *min be-eino mino* (different tastes, different categories, governed by *nosein taam*), while *Rava* would say they are *min be-mino* (same name, same category, governed by *mashehu*). The positions thus trade stringency and leniency depending on the case.

## Spices & Combined Issur

### Rashi's Caveat

Before proceeding, *Rashi* interjects an important qualification. The *Gemara*'s language in the line *ve-chol min be-mino be-mashehu* — "all *min be-mino* is *mashehu*" — is broader than the case of *yayin nesech* alone. It appears to assert, following *Rabbi Yehuda*'s reasoning, that in every domain of *kashrus*, *min be-mino* is forever forbidden even in a negligible amount. This is how *Rashi* reads the *sugya*, and indeed *Rashi* in various places in *Shas*, particularly in *Chullin*, rules accordingly. However, *Rashi* himself here acknowledges that the *halacha* does not actually follow this broad reading: *le-kaman paskinan de-lav hilchata hachi* — later we will rule that this is not the law. The rule of *min be-mino be-mashehu* is effectively limited to *yayin nesech*, *tevel*, and *chametz be-Pesach*. In most other areas, even *min be-mino* is governed by *nosein taam*. *Tosafos* roundly rejects *Rashi*'s broader position, and it is worth pausing to appreciate why: if every case of *min be-mino* required *mashehu*, the practical difficulties for *kashrus* observance would be considerable. *Rashi*'s inconsistency on this point — ruling one way in *Chullin* and another here — is flagged explicitly in the marginal notes.

For practical purposes, then, the *mashehu* stringency tied to *min be-mino* is primarily a *yayin nesech* concern. The broader dispute between *Abaye* and *Rava* over how to *define* *min be-mino* — taste versus name — has its main practical application there.

### Spice Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַלָּא דְּחַמְרָא וְחַלָּא דְּשִׁיכְרָא, וַחֲמִירָא דְּחִיטֵּי וַחֲמִירָא דִּשְׂעָרֵי, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם, בָּתַר טַעְמָא אָזְלִינַן, וְהַאי טַעְמָא לְחוּד וְהַאי טַעְמָא לְחוּד, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִין בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ, וּמִין בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** This dispute between Abaye and Rava applies to another case as well. With regard to the case of **wine vinegar and malt vinegar** that became mixed together, and one is permitted and the other is forbidden, **and** also the case of **leaven of wheat** flour **and leaven of barley** flour that became mixed together, and one is permitted and the other is forbidden, **Abaye says:** The forbidden substance renders the mixture forbidden **in** a case where it **imparts flavor** to the mixture, because **we follow the flavor, and this flavor is discrete and that flavor is discrete, and** therefore **it becomes** a mixture of **a type** of food mixed **with** food **not** of **its** own **type. And a type** of food mixed **with** food **not** of **its** own **type** is forbidden **in** a case where the forbidden food **imparts flavor** to the mixture.

The *Gemara* now brings *Abaye*'s proof for his own position. *Abaye* cites a *Braitah*: if you have spices of different types — some that are *orlah*, some *tevel*, some from *avodah zarah* — and they all fall into the same dish, they combine to render the dish forbidden. Furthermore, the case can involve spices that are different types entirely: pepper, cinnamon, and nutmeg, for instance, each forbidden for a different reason. Despite being different substances carrying different *issurim*, they combine. The double language of the *Braitah* — *assurin u-mitztatfin*, they are forbidden and they combine — addresses both wet and dry mixtures, but the essential point is the combination itself. If each spice individually would not constitute a sufficient quantity to render the dish forbidden, their combination together creates the requisite amount. The question *Abaye* presses is: why should fundamentally different things combine at all?

### Miney Metika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ דְּבָתַר טַעְמָא אָזְלִינַן? דְּתַנְיָא: תַּבְלִין שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה שֵׁמוֹת, וְהֵן מִין אֶחָד, אוֹ מִין שְׁלֹשָׁה — אֲסוּרִין וּמִצְטָרְפִין. וְאָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: הָכָא בְּמִינֵי מְתִיקָה עָסְקִינַן, הוֹאִיל וּרְאוּיִן לְמַתֵּק בָּהֶן אֶת הַקְּדֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: From where do I say that we follow the flavor? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: Forbidden **spices** that are known by **two or three** different **names but are** of **the same species, or** of **three** different **species, are forbidden, and combine** together to render a dish forbidden. **And Ḥizkiyya says:** The reason the three species combine to render the dish forbidden is that **here we are dealing with types of sweet** spices. **Since they are** all **fit to sweeten the dish,** they combine, as they impart the same kind of flavor.

Before *Abaye* can complete his proof, *Chizkiyah* offers a clarification that proves essential: *hacha be-meinei matika askinan, hovrin le-mateik et ha-kedeirah* — the case here involves spices that are all of the sweet-giving type, all contributing a sweetness to the pot. The combination is possible precisely because, despite their different botanical origins, they all function in the same way in the dish: they all impart sweetness. This narrows the scenario considerably. The relevant case is not just any mixture of forbidden spices, but specifically those that all contribute the same kind of flavor profile.

### Abaye: Taste Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בָּתַר טַעְמָא אָזְלִינַן — כּוּלֵּי חַד טַעְמָא הוּא, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בָּתַר שְׁמָא אָזְלִינַן — הַאי שְׁמָא לְחוּד וְהַאי שְׁמָא לְחוּד!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye explains: **Granted, if you say we follow the flavor, they are all** considered **the same flavor,** and therefore they combine. **But if you say we follow the name,** why should they combine to render the dish forbidden? **This name is discrete and that name is discrete.**

With *Chizkiyah*'s clarification established, *Abaye* presses his proof. *Ei amrat bishlama batar ta'ama azlinan, kulei chad ta'ama hu* — if you agree with me that identity is determined by taste, then the combination makes perfect sense. All these different spices ultimately produce the same sweetness; they are therefore to be regarded as a single category of substance, and they can combine to create a forbidden quantity. The proof, *Abaye* argues, is that we would not even *think* of combining things from fundamentally different categories. The fact that the *Braitah* permits combination reveals that shared taste creates a shared legal identity. That, *Abaye* says, is *batar ta'ama azlinan*.

It is true that this example involves *min be-eino mino* rather than *min be-mino*, which is where *Abaye* and *Rava* primarily disagree. The *mashehu* question does not arise here, since if you had enough of any one substance to be *nosein taam* you would not need combination. But *Abaye*'s point is structural: the very *possibility* of combination demonstrates that shared taste generates shared identity, and that is his underlying principle.

### Rava: Issur Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר לָךְ: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִנַּיִן לְכׇל אִיסּוּרִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁמִּצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תֹאכַל כׇּל תּוֹעֵבָה״, כֹּל שֶׁתִּיעַבְתִּי לָךְ — הֲרֵי הוּא בְּבַל תֹּאכַל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** could **say to you:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who maintains that all forbidden foods combine, whether or not they have the same name or the same flavor, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Meir: From where** is it derived **that any** foods **forbidden by the Torah** that fall into a mixture **combine with one another** to render a mixture forbidden? **As it is stated: "You shall not eat anything abominable"** (Deuteronomy 14:3), which indicates that **anything that I have made abominable,** i.e., forbidden, **to you, is prohibited for consumption,** and therefore all of these prohibitions are actually a single general prohibition.

*Rava* responds that the *Braitah* proves nothing of the kind. *Ha mani Rabbi Meir hi* — this source reflects the position of *Rabbi Meir*, who holds that all *issurim* in the Torah share a generic identity, as derived from the verse *lo tochal kol to'evah*: do not eat anything that is an abomination. *Kol she-ti'avti lecha harei hu be-val tochal* — anything the Torah has forbidden shares this generic designation of *toeva*, of that which is abominable. That shared generic identity — we might call it *treifus* in the broad sense — is what allows different forbidden substances to combine. They need not taste the same; they merely need to share the identity of being forbidden. The combination follows from their shared status as *toeva*, not from their shared flavor.

*Rava* thus offers a coherent account of why the combination works that makes no reference to taste whatsoever. One might then ask: if combination follows from shared *toeva* identity alone, why does the *Braitah* specify that these are *meinei matika*, sweet-giving spices? According to *Rava*, if they were being eaten outside of a mixture, combination would follow purely from their shared *toeva* identity. But when these substances are mixed into a dish, they must still make their presence felt in order not to be *batel*. And in order to make their presence felt in a mixture, they must contribute a detectable flavor. Therefore, for the purposes of the mixture context, they also need to share a taste — not to create their shared identity, but to ensure that they are not nullified. The shared *toeva* identity is what conceptually unifies them; the shared taste is what preserves their presence in the mixture. *Abaye*'s position, by contrast, makes taste do double duty: it both creates the shared identity *and* preserves their presence in the mixture.

## Wine & Vinegar Debate

### Vinegar into Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַלָּא לְגוֹ חַמְרָא — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to forbidden **vinegar** that fell **into** a barrel of **wine, all agree** that the mixture is forbidden **in** a case where the forbidden vinegar **imparts flavor** to the mixture.

The *Gemara* now takes up a new case that carries the *Abaye-Rava* debate into a domain where taste and smell diverge. *Chala le-go chamra* — vinegar falling into wine. This is straightforward: vinegar and wine have different names and different tastes. By both *Abaye*'s criterion (taste) and *Rava*'s criterion (name), this is *min be-eino mino*, and therefore *divrei hakol be-nosein taam* — all agree it is governed by the taste standard.

### Wine into Vinegar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַמְרָא לְגוֹ חַלָּא — אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, וְרָבָא אָמַר: בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, רֵיחֵיהּ חַלָּא וְטַעְמָא חַמְרָא — חַלָּא, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִין בְּמִינוֹ, וְכׇל מִין בְּמִינוֹ — בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a case of forbidden **wine** that spilled **into vinegar,** since the sharper smell of the vinegar overwhelms the smell of the wine before it mixes in, **Abaye says** that **any amount** of the wine renders the mixture forbidden, **and Rava says** that it is forbidden only **in** a case where the wine **imparts flavor** to the vinegar. The Gemara explains their respective opinions: **Abaye says** that **any amount** renders the mixture forbidden, because a liquid **whose smell is vinegar and** its **flavor is wine** is considered **vinegar,** and the wine, when it spilled into the barrel of vinegar, took on the smell of vinegar from the moment it reached the airspace of the barrel, **and it has** therefore **become a type** of food mixed **with** food of **its** own **type. And any type** of forbidden food mixed **with** food of **its** own **type** renders the mixture forbidden **by any amount.**

The reverse case is more complex. *Chamra le-go chala* — wine falling into vinegar. At first glance, this seems equally simple: different names, different tastes, and therefore *min be-eino mino* by both positions. But the physical reality of the situation introduces a complication. When a drop of *yayin nesech* hovers above a large barrel of vinegar before falling in, the powerful smell emanating from the barrel envelops the drop. By the time the drop of wine actually enters the vinegar, it already *smells* like vinegar — even though, stripped of that smell, it would taste like wine. The question therefore becomes: when something smells like vinegar and tastes like wine, which property determines its *halachic* identity?

### Abaye: Smell Rules

*Abaye amar be-mashehu* — *Abaye* holds this is *min be-mino* and governed by *mashehu*. *Reicha chala* — the smell is vinegar, and for *Abaye*, smell determines the relevant category. The drop, as it enters the barrel, is to be treated as vinegar, and therefore it is vinegar meeting vinegar: *min be-mino be-mashehu*.

This result may seem surprising, since *Abaye*'s stated general principle is *batar ta'ama azlinan* — identity follows taste. Yet here *Abaye* is applying a deeper understanding of what "taste" means. There is a well-established physiological reality that the vast majority of what we experience as flavor is in fact olfactory: smell travels through the nasal passages and constitutes the dominant component of the tasting experience. When *Abaye* says we follow taste, and then in this case follows smell, he is saying that smell is the dominant determinant of taste. The two positions are therefore consistent: *Abaye* goes by the tasting experience, and in a case where smell overwhelms, smell defines the tasting experience.

### Rava: Taste Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם, רֵיחֵיהּ חַלָּא וְטַעְמָא חַמְרָא — חַמְרָא, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִין בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ, וְכׇל מִין בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that the mixture is forbidden only **in** a case where the wine **imparts flavor** to the vinegar, because a liquid **whose smell is vinegar and** its **flavor is wine** is considered **wine, and it has** therefore **become a type** of food mixed **with** food **not** of **its** own **type. And any type** of forbidden food mixed **with** food **not** of **its** own **type** renders the mixture forbidden only **in** a case where it **imparts flavor** to the mixture.

*Rava amar be-nosein taam* — *Rava* holds this is *min be-eino mino* and governed by *nosein taam*. *Reicha chala ve-tama chamra, chamra* — if it smells like vinegar but tastes like wine, it is wine. *Ve-havei lei min be-she-eino mino* — it is therefore wine in vinegar, like with unlike, and the taste standard applies.

*Rava*'s position here connects naturally to his general criterion of *batar shma azlinan*. How would people refer to wine that has begun to smell of vinegar? Likely as "wine gone bad" or "vinegary wine" — still wine, even if deteriorating. The name has not changed, and therefore the identity has not changed. The smell does not override the category.

What makes this case especially interesting is that both the question of names and the question of taste pull in the same direction for *Rava*, yet *Abaye*, despite his general emphasis on taste, ends up calling it vinegar. The *Gemara* does not explicitly map this case onto the earlier *Abaye-Rava* debate about taste versus name, but the connection is clear. *Rava*'s position here is consistent with his broader principle; *Abaye*'s position reflects his view that smell is the dominant component of taste, and that where smell overwhelms, the tasting experience must be described in terms of that smell.

## Smell Standalone

### Bistia & Smelling Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי בַּת תִּיהָא, גּוֹי בִּדְיִשְׂרָאֵל — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּדְגוֹי — אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אָסוּר, רָבָא אָמַר: מוּתָּר. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אָסוּר, רֵיחָא מִילְּתָא הִיא. רָבָא אָמַר: מוּתָּר, רֵיחָא לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **this bunghole [*bat tiha*],** the hole in a barrel through which one can smell the wine, if **a gentile** smells **a Jew's** wine through it, the wine is **permitted,** but for **a Jew** to smell **a gentile's** wine through it, **Abaye says** that it is **prohibited,** whereas **Rava says** that it is **permitted. Abaye says** that it is **prohibited** because he holds that **a smell is** a substantial **matter,** a significant form of pleasure, and it is not permitted to derive benefit from a gentile's wine. **Rava says** it is **permitted** because he holds that **a smell is nothing;** it is insignificant.

The *Gemara* now moves to a case that fully isolates smell from taste: the *bistia*, a kind of pipette used to draw wine from a barrel and assess its quality by smell. The *Gemara* presents a two-part case. If a non-Jew smells a Jew's wine through the *bistia*, there is no issue — he has not touched the wine, has not moved it, and has not done anything that would create a problem of *yayin nesech*. *Tosafos* adds that if a Jew smells a non-Jew's wine, the assumption must be that he is doing so without payment; otherwise, there would be a separate problem of receiving benefit from *yayin nesech*. The harder question concerns the Jew smelling the non-Jew's wine itself. Has he done anything forbidden?

### Abaye: Smell is Real

*Abaye amar assur* — it is forbidden. *Reicha milta hi* — smell is a real thing. *Abaye*'s position is consistent with his earlier stance on the vinegary wine: smell is not merely an incidental property but is itself a vehicle through which one engages with and in some sense ingests the substance. To smell *yayin nesech* is, for *Abaye*, a meaningful form of engagement with it that triggers the prohibition.

### Rava: Smell Irrelevant

*Rava amar mutar* — it is permitted. *Reicha lav milta hi* — smell is nothing. *Rava* holds that if the physical wine itself does not pass down one's throat, if one has not actually drunk or eaten it, no prohibition is triggered. What precisely is the underlying dispute? The context of *yayin nesech* might suggest this is a question about *issurei hana'ah* — the prohibition of benefit. Wine that has been poured for idolatrous purposes is forbidden not only to drink but to derive benefit from, and one might ask whether inhaling its aroma constitutes benefit. However, the *Gemara* immediately connects this debate to a parallel in the laws of *terumah*, and *terumah* is not *assur be-hana'ah*. The question, as *Tosafos* explains it, is therefore not about benefit but about whether smell constitutes a form of *achilah* — ingestion or eating. When one smells something directly, tiny particles of that substance actually enter the body through the nose and mouth. *Abaye* holds that this constitutes a form of ingesting the substance itself. *Rava* holds that if the physical substance, in the form one recognizes as the food itself, does not enter the body, the act of smelling is not *achilah* and creates no prohibition.

### Terumah Bread Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, דְּרֵיחָא וְלָא כְּלוּם הוּא? דִּתְנַן: תַּנּוּר שֶׁהִסִּיקוֹ בְּכַמּוֹן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה וְאָפָה בּוֹ אֶת הַפַּת — הַפַּת מוּתֶּרֶת, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין טַעַם כַּמּוֹן אֶלָּא רֵיחָא כַּמּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: From where do I say that a smell is nothing?** It is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 10:4): With regard to **an oven that one lit with cumin** stalks **of *teruma* and baked bread in it, the bread** is **permitted because** it has **not** absorbed **the flavor of** the **cumin** stalks **but** only **the smell of** the **cumin** stalks. This indicates that smell alone does not render a food forbidden.

*Rava* supports his position from a *Mishnah* in the laws of *terumah*. *Tanur she-hisiko be-chamon shel terumah* — if someone heated an oven using cumin that was *terumah*, and then baked bread in that oven, the bread is permitted. *Lefi she-ein ta'am chamon ela reiach chamon* — the bread absorbed only the smell of the cumin, not the taste of the cumin. There is no actual cumin flavor in the bread; only the aroma permeated the baking environment. Since only the smell transferred, and smell is *lav milta*, there is no prohibition.

### Rava's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבַּיֵּי? שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּמִיקְלָא אִיסּוּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how does **Abaye** explain this? He answers that **it is different there, as the forbidden substance was burned.** The smell of the forbidden cumin stalks entered the bread after the cumin stalks themselves were consumed by the fire, and a smell that is not from an extant substance is not forbidden. By contrast, in a case where the smell is from an extant source, it is forbidden.

This, *Rava* says, is his proof: *mana amina lah de-reicha lav klum hu* — from this *Mishnah* we learn that smell alone does not constitute a halachically significant transfer. Even though the *terumah* cumin's aroma permeated the oven and the bread baked within it, the bread remains entirely permitted, because smell is nothing.

### Abaye: Direct vs. Detach…

*Abaye* responds with a distinction that saves his principle: *shani hatam de-miklei issurei* — there the cumin was burned. The cumin itself was consumed by the fire; what remained in the oven was not the cumin but only a detached aroma, the smell floating independently in the air. A detached aroma, separated from the original substance by the act of burning, is genuinely "nothing" — it is not the thing itself in any form. But in the case of smelling wine directly, the wine is physically present; the particles entering one's nose are real particles of wine. That, *Abaye* argues, is qualitatively different from an aroma left lingering in an oven after its source has been destroyed.

The distinction *Abaye* draws is between direct smell — where the original substance is present and one's nose is in immediate contact with particles emanating from it — and detached aroma, where the source is gone and only a residual smell hangs in the air. The former, for *Abaye*, is a real form of ingestion; the latter is not. This maps onto what one might describe as the physical reality of smell: to smell something is to have particles of that thing enter one's body. *Abaye* holds that this is halachically cognizable when the source is present; *Rava* holds that without the physical substance passing one's lips, no *achilah* has occurred.

## Tannaim: Bread & Barrel

### Tannaitic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי: כְּתַנָּאֵי, הָרוֹדֶה פַּת חַמָּה וּנְתָנָהּ עַל פִּי חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה — רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹסֵר, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר בְּשֶׁל חִיטִּין, וְאוֹסֵר בְּשֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַשְּׂעוֹרִים שׁוֹאֲבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari said:** This dispute between Abaye and Rava is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a mishna (*Terumot* 10:3): With regard to **one who detached a hot** loaf of **bread** from the oven **and placed it on the opening of a barrel of wine of *teruma*,** the portion of the produce designated for the priest, **Rabbi Meir deems** the bread **forbidden** to non-priests, as in his opinion the smell of the wine renders the bread forbidden to them, **and Rabbi Yehuda deems** it **permitted. Rabbi Yosei deems** it **permitted in** a case of **wheat** bread **but deems** it **forbidden in** a case of **barley** bread, **because the barley draws out** the fumes of the wine.

The *Gemara* now introduces a *Tannaitic* dispute that mirrors the *Abaye-Rava* debate and allows for a more precise mapping. The case: someone removes hot bread from an oven and places it directly on top of a barrel of *terumah* wine. Three *Tanna'im* weigh in.

### Rabbi Meir Position

*Rabbi Meir* forbids the bread. His position presupposes that the smell of the wine is absorbed into the bread and that this absorption is halachically significant — *reicha milta hi*. Because the hot bread is in direct contact with the barrel, the wine's aroma penetrates the bread, and since *Reicha milta*, that aroma renders the bread *terumah* and therefore forbidden to non-*kohanim*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

*Rabbi Yehuda* permits the bread. For him, *reicha lav milta hi* — smell does not transfer in a legally significant way, and the bread is unaffected by its proximity to the wine barrel.

### Rabbi Yossi: Wheat vs. B…

*Rabbi Yossi* takes a middle position: *mattir be-shel chittin ve-osser be-shel se'orin* — wheat bread is permitted, but barley bread is forbidden. The difference is absorption: *mipnei she-hase'orin sho'avot* — barley bread absorbs more, and therefore the wine's smell actually enters it in a physically meaningful way. Wheat bread does not absorb to the same degree, so it remains unaffected.

### Rava & Abaye Linked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: רֵיחָא מִילְּתָא הִיא, וּמָר סָבַר: רֵיחָא וְלָא כְּלוּם הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Mari explains: **What, is it not with regard to this** matter that the Sages in the mishna **disagree: That** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that **a smell is** a substantial **matter, and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **a smell is nothing?**

*Rav Mari* notes that this *Tannaitic* dispute maps directly onto the *Abaye-Rava* debate: *Rabbi Meir* corresponds to *Abaye* (*reicha milta*), and *Rabbi Yehuda* corresponds to *Rava* (*reicha lav milta*). For *Rava*, this is straightforward: he acknowledges that his position aligns with *Rabbi Yehuda*, and he concedes that other *Tanna'im* disagree. For *Abaye*, however, the situation is more nuanced.

### Reish Lakish Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרָבָא וַדַּאי תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, לְאַבָּיֵי מִי לֵימָא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? אָמַר לָךְ אַבָּיֵי: לָאו מִי אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בְּפַת חַמָּה וְחָבִית פְּתוּחָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֲסוּרָה, בְּפַת צוֹנֶנֶת וְחָבִית מְגוּפָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתֶּרֶת. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּפַת חַמָּה וְחָבִית מְגוּפָה, בְּפַת צוֹנֶנֶת וְחָבִית פְּתוּחָה. וְהָא דִּידִי נָמֵי כְּפַת חַמָּה וְחָבִית פְּתוּחָה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara remarks: **According to** the opinion of **Rava,** the dispute between him and Abaye **is certainly** parallel to a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as he must concede that Rabbi Meir holds that a smell is a substantial matter. But **according to** the opinion of **Abaye, shall we say** that **it is** parallel to a dispute between ***tanna'im*?** The Gemara answers: **Abaye** could **say to you** that Rabbi Yehuda also holds that a smell is a substantial matter. **Wasn't it stated with regard to that** mishna that **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Reish Lakish says: In** the case of **a hot** loaf of **bread and an open barrel, everyone agrees** that it is **forbidden,** as the bread certainly absorbed of the smell of the wine? Furthermore, **in** the case of **a cool** loaf of **bread and a stoppered barrel, everyone agrees** that it is **permitted. They disagree only with regard to** the case of **a hot** loaf of **bread and a stoppered barrel,** or **in** the case of **a cool** loaf of **bread and an open barrel. And this** case **of mine,** i.e., the case of the bunghole, **is also comparable to** the case of **a hot** loaf of **bread and an open barrel,** in which everyone agrees that the bread is forbidden.

*Abaye* argues that he need not read this as a fundamental dispute about the principle of *reicha*. He invokes a ruling transmitted by *Rabbah bar bar Chanah* in the name of *Reish Lakish*: *be-pat chamah ve-chavit ptuchah, divrei hakol assur* — if the bread is hot and the barrel is open, all agree the bread is forbidden, because in that case the smell is certainly absorbed. *Be-pat tzonenet ve-chavit megufah, divrei hakol muteret* — if the bread is cold and the barrel is sealed, all agree the bread is permitted, because nothing is absorbed. The debate among the *Tanna'im*, *Abaye* argues, is not about the principle but about the intermediate cases: hot bread with a sealed barrel, or cold bread with an open barrel — one factor favoring absorption and one disfavoring it.

### Abaye's Nuance

*Abaye*'s nuance is therefore this: he does not read *Rabbi Yehuda* as denying that smell is ever significant. Rather, he reads the entire *Tannaitic* dispute as operating within a shared framework that accepts *reicha milta* in principle, and debating only the threshold conditions under which smell is actually absorbed. *Ve-ha didi ke-pat chamah ve-chavit ptuchah dami* — the case of smelling wine directly, where one's face is over the barrel, is the equivalent of hot bread on an open barrel: there is no question that direct smell contact involves real absorption, and all would agree it is a problem. *Rava*, meanwhile, reads *Rabbi Yehuda* as a straightforward denial that smell matters at all, and aligns himself accordingly.

## Ovens & Pesach

### Tosafos Q on Reicha

*Tosafos* raises a question that has significant practical implications. The *Gemara* here, following *Rava*, rules *reicha lav milta hi* — smell does not create a prohibition. Yet in the *sugya* in *Pesachim* dealing with *reicha*, the conclusion appears to go in the opposite direction. Which ruling governs practical *halacha*, and do these represent the same type of scenario?

### Tosafos: Year Cooking

*Tosafos* addresses the question that arises in *Perek Keitzad Tzolin* in *Pesachim*, where *Rav* and *Levi* debate whether fatty kosher meat roasted together with *treif* meat is forbidden due to the transfer of taste through the oven. *Rav* forbids it; *Levi* permits it. The *Gemara* there brings a *Braitah* in support of *Rav*'s position. Nevertheless, *Rashi* in that chapter rules like *Levi*, permitting the practice — because *Rava* here rules *reicha lav milta hi*, and the *halacha* follows *Rava* against *Abaye*. Accordingly, *Tosafos* takes it as a baseline that during the year, cooking kosher and *treif* foods in the same oven — even simultaneously — does not violate the prohibition, because the transfer is only through smell, and smell is not a legally significant conduit of taste.

### Pesach Matzah Case

The *Pesach* context, however, changes the analysis. *Tosafos* addresses a case where *matzos* were baked and subsequently a kernel of grain was discovered in one of them, raising the concern that it may have been *chametz*. If the *matzos* were baked together in the same oven, has the smell of that potentially *chametz* kernel transferred to the other *matzos* and rendered them forbidden?

### Chametz Timing

*Tosafos* introduces a crucial timing distinction. *Chametz* is *batel be-mashehu* — forbidden even in a negligible amount — only once *Pesach* itself has actually begun. Even after midday on *Erev Pesach*, when eating *chametz* is already forbidden, *chametz* that becomes mixed into something else is still *batel be-shishim*: the *mashehu* stringency does not yet apply. Only from the night of *Pesach* onward is *chametz batel be-mashehu*. Therefore, if the *matzos* were baked on *Erev Pesach*, even after midday, the smell transfer — even if it were halachically cognizable — would still be *batel be-shishim*, and the other *matzos* would be permitted.

### Tosafos: Mashehu Issue

*Tosafos*'s critical distinction is this: ruling *reicha lav milta hi* means that smell-transferred aroma does not constitute a tastable flavor — there is no *nosein taam*. But this does not mean there is *nothing* there. Even when smell transfers, some trace particles of the substance have genuinely moved. Those particles, while insufficient to constitute *nosein taam*, still amount to *mashehu* — a negligible but real presence of the forbidden substance. During the year, when *issurim* require *nosein taam* to be operative, smell transfer is irrelevant: *reicha lav milta*. But by *chametz be-Pesach*, where even *mashehu* is forbidden, those trace particles become significant. The smell of *chametz* baking in the oven alongside *matzah* during *Pesach* itself would therefore create a *mashehu* problem.

### Final Matzah Ruling

*Tosafos* concludes: *aval im afu ha-matzos be-shiva yemei ha-Pesach ve-nimtza chita ve-chametz be-achas mehen, kulan assuros* — but if the *matzos* were baked during the seven days of *Pesach* itself and a kernel of *chametz* was found in one of them, they are all forbidden. And not only the *matzah* in which the *chametz* was found: even the other *matzos* baked in the same oven at the same time are forbidden. Though *reicha lav milta hi* — the smell carries no taste — *nehi de-ta'am leka, mi-kol makom mashehu be-alma ika* — even if there is no taste, there is still at minimum a trace quantity present, and *ve-afilu be-massa tanur* — this applies even in the context of a baking oven. The conclusion for *Pesach* practice is stark: *matzah* baked in the same oven as *chametz* during *Pesach* itself is entirely forbidden, not because smell is taste, but because *mashehu* does not require taste — and where *chametz be-Pesach* is concerned, *mashehu* is enough.
