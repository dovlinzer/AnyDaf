# Bava Batra 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Drainpipes & Chazakah

### Marzev & Mazcheila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּרְזֵב אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, וְיֵשׁ לִמְקוֹמוֹ חֲזָקָה. הַמַּזְחֵילָה יֵשׁ לָהּ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a spout** protruding from one's roof gutter draining water into another's property, its owner **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use, **but** he does **have** the means to establish **an acquired privilege with regard to its place,** as the Gemara will explain. With regard to **a gutter pipe** that traverses the length of the roof, one does **have** the means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use.

The Gemara on 59a opens with a *mishnah* that distinguishes between two different types of drainpipes extending from a roof into a neighbor's yard. In the ancient Near East — and in much of the modern Middle East — flat roofs were the norm, and the practical question of where rainwater drains inevitably became a matter of property law. The *mishnah* draws its central distinction: *ha-marzev ein lo chazakah* — a *marzev*, the more temporary type of drainpipe, does not create a *chazakah* — whereas *ha-mazcheila yesh lo chazakah* — the *mazcheila*, a more robust and permanent construction, does establish a *chazakah* and thereby confers a legally recognized right of use.

### Three Marzev Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״הַמַּרְזֵב אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, וְיֵשׁ לִמְקוֹמוֹ חֲזָקָה״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַמַּרְזֵב אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה – מֵרוּחַ אַחַת, וְיֵשׁ לִמְקוֹמוֹ חֲזָקָה – מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the mishna's statement: With regard to **a spout,** its owner **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use, **but** he does **have** the means to establish **an acquired privilege with regard to its place? Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: This** is what it **is saying:** Concerning **a spout,** its owner **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use **with regard to one side,** i.e., the owner of the field below has the right to move the spout from one place to another on one side of the roof, **but** he does **have** the means to establish **an acquired privilege concerning its place with regard to two sides,** i.e., the owner of the field below does not have the right to move it to another side of the roof.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: הַמַּרְזֵב אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה – שֶׁאִם הָיָה אָרוֹךְ מְקַצְּרוֹ, וְיֵשׁ לִמְקוֹמוֹ חֲזָקָה – שֶׁאִם בָּא לְעוֹקְרוֹ, אֵינוֹ עוֹקְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said** there is a different explanation: With regard to **a spout,** its owner **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** size, **as if it was long,** the owner of the field below may **shorten it, but** one does **have** the means to establish **an acquired privilege with regard to its place, as if** the owner of the field below **comes to remove it** entirely, **he may not remove it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: הַמַּרְזֵב אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה – שֶׁאִם רוֹצֶה לִבְנוֹת תַּחְתָּיו בּוֹנֶה, וְיֵשׁ לִמְקוֹמוֹ חֲזָקָה – שֶׁאִם בָּא לְעוֹקְרוֹ אֵינוֹ עוֹקְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said** there is a different explanation: With regard to **a spout,** its owner **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use, **as if** the owner of the field below **wishes to build beneath it,** he may **build,** and the owner of the spout may not prevent him from doing so; **but** one does **have** the means to establish **an acquired privilege with regard to its place, as if** the owner of the field below **comes to remove it** entirely, **he may not remove it.**

The Gemara's discussion depends heavily on a three-way *machlokes* introduced on the preceding daf regarding the *marzev*. Shmuel's opinion is that *marzev ein lo chazakah mi-ruach achas yesh lo makom chazakah mi-shtei ruchosav* — the *marzev* does not create a *chazakah* from one direction, but does have standing from two sides — a matter of orientation. Rav Hanina's opinion is that *marzev ein lo chazakah she-im hayah aruch mekatzro* — because if the pipe is too long it can be shortened, the length remains mutable and therefore no permanent right crystallizes. The third opinion, attributed to Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba, is that *marzev ein lo chazakah she-im ratza livnot tachtav boneh* — since one may build in the space beneath the drainpipe, the pipe can effectively be relocated, and no *chazakah* is created.

### Mazcheila Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״הַמַּזְחֵילָה יֵשׁ לָהּ חֲזָקָה״. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁאִם רָצָה לִבְנוֹת תַּחְתָּיו בּוֹנֶה, מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to **a gutter pipe,** one does **have** the means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use. **Granted, according to the one who says those** first **two** explanations, i.e., Shmuel and Rabbi Ḥanina, it is **well.** The distinction between the *halakha* with regard to a spout and that of a gutter pipe is clear: Since the gutter pipe is fixed in place, there is an acquired privilege, and it may not be moved or shortened. **But according to** Rav Yirmeya bar Abba, **the one who says** that the mishna means: **If** the owner of the field **wishes to build beneath it** he may **build, what difference does it make to** the owner of the gutter pipe if the owner of the field builds beneath it? Why would he have the right to prevent it?

Given these three opinions, the Gemara proceeds to ask a pointed question. According to the first two opinions, it makes perfect sense that the *mazcheila* creates a *chazakah*: it is more permanent than the *marzev*, cannot be as easily reoriented or shortened, and so its continued presence can be presumed to carry the neighbor's implicit consent. But according to the third opinion — that the *marzev* fails to create a *chazakah* because one may build in the space beneath it — what difference does it make whether the drainpipe is a *marzev* or a *mazcheila*? Either structure can, in principle, be displaced by construction below it. The Gemara formulates this as: *bishlama le-man de-amar hani tartei shappir ela le-man de-amar she-im ratza livnot tachtav boneh mai nafka lei mina?*

### Stone & Permanence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַזְחֵילָה שֶׁל בִּנְיָן עָסְקִינַן, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא נִיחָא לִי דְּתִתְּרַע אֲשִׁיתַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with a gutter pipe** that is made of stone and is built into the walls **of** the **building,** in a case **where** the owner of the gutter pipe **said to** the owner of the field: **It is not amenable to me** that you build beneath my gutter pipe, **as my walls will weaken** as a result.

The Gemara's answer is: *hacha be-mazcheila she-be-binyan askinan* — here we are dealing with a *mazcheila* that is built from stone, a fully permanent structure. In the case of such a construction, not only does the permanence itself support the *chazakah*, but any subsequent building that the neighbor undertakes in the vicinity — constructing a wall beneath the drainpipe or otherwise building nearby — will cause actual physical damage to the stone *mazcheila*. The neighbor may therefore legitimately say: *lo nicha li de-titra ashitei* — I do not want my foundation or structure to be compromised. The Rishonim discuss whether the critical factor is specifically stone as a material, or more broadly the permanence and susceptibility to water and structural damage; the question of how building materials interact with water absorption becomes part of that broader analysis.

### Vibration Damage

The Rashbam develops this point further. He explains: *klomar mazcheila sheli tippol le-chol makkevet ve-ha-garzen ve-chol kli barzel be-vinyancha* — my *mazcheila* will be damaged by every hammer blow, every axe stroke, and every iron tool used in your construction. The concern is not merely theoretical: the vibrations from nearby building work will compromise a protruding stone drainpipe. This is consistent with a principle encountered earlier in the second *perek*, where the Gemara was likewise concerned with the damage that construction vibrations can cause to neighboring structures. The Rashbam understands that the *mazcheila*, because it protrudes, is uniquely vulnerable to this kind of damage in a way that the rest of the house may not be, making it a discrete and recognizable subject of objection.

## Water Rights Case

### Plugging Drainpipe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: צִינּוֹר הַמְקַלֵּחַ מַיִם לַחֲצַר חֲבֵרוֹ, וּבָא בַּעַל הַגָּג לְסוֹתְמוֹ, בַּעַל הֶחָצֵר מְעַכֵּב עָלָיו, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאַתְּ קַנְיָא לָךְ חָצֵר דִּידִי לְמִשְׁדֵּא בֵּיהּ מַיָּא, לְדִידִי נָמֵי קְנֵי לִי מַיָּא דְּאִיגָּרָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** With regard to **a pipe** from **which water is draining into another's courtyard and the owner of the roof comes to seal** his drainage pipe, **the owner of the courtyard can prevent him** from doing so. **As** the owner of the field can **say to him: Just as you have acquired my courtyard for** the purpose of **throwing your water into it, I have also acquired the water of your roof,** and since I wish to use it, you may not seal the pipe.

The Gemara then presents a ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *tzinor hamekaleach mayim lachatzer chaveiro* — a pipe that collects water from a roof and channels it into a neighbor's yard. In ordinary circumstances, one might assume that such an arrangement is a burden on the yard owner — unwanted water being dumped onto his property. The surprising ruling is therefore: *uba ba'al hagag lisatmo ba'al hachatzer me'akev alav* — if the roof owner attempts to plug up the drainpipe, the yard owner can legally prevent him from doing so. The yard owner has standing to insist that the pipe remain open.

### Implicit Purchase

The explanation offered is striking: *de'amar lei ki heichi de'at kanit lach chatzer didi lemishdi bei maya lididi nami kani li maya de'agrach* — just as you effectively "purchased" the right to use my yard as a drainage site, so too I have acquired the right to the water from your roof. The Rashbam characterizes this as resembling an actual commercial transaction: *vedami lemakach umimkar* — it is like a sale. He explains: *she'ani shibadeti lach chatzeri bishvil meimecha* — I subjugated my property rights in your favor in exchange for the water. The yard owner benefits from the water — he waters his animals from it, *ulhashkot beheimtai* — and his consent to the arrangement, *she'al minat kein nitratzeiti lach*, was predicated on that benefit. Accordingly, if the roof owner now plugs the pipe, it is something akin to *gezeilah*, a wrongful deprivation: the parties had implicitly exchanged rights, and unilaterally reneging on that exchange without consent is impermissible.

### Renovation Limits

This ruling raises an important practical question: how far does this restriction on the roof owner extend? If the yard owner can prevent him from plugging the drainpipe, can he also prevent him from making other renovations — adding a second story, for instance, which might reduce the volume of water flowing from the roof? There is a *machlokes* among the Rishonim on this point. The Ra'ah appears to hold that the restriction is limited to specific and direct interference with the drainpipe itself. The Nimukei Yosef, in his commentary on the Rif, disagrees and understands the constraint much more broadly — potentially extending to renovations that would indirectly reduce or eliminate the water supply, even something as substantial as adding a second story. What makes this debate particularly striking is that the parties never explicitly agreed to anything. The Rashbam calls it *dami lemakach umimkar*, similar to a purchase, but how formal a claim this implicit arrangement can sustain in a *beit din* — especially as the hypotheticals grow more remote from the original drainpipe — is precisely what divides the authorities.

### Family Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: מְעַכֵּב. רַבִּי חָמָא, אָמַר: אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב. אֲזַל שַׁיְילֵיהּ לְרַבִּי בֵּיסָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מְעַכֵּב. קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: ״וְהַחוּט הַמְשֻׁלָּשׁ לֹא בִמְהֵרָה יִנָּתֵק״ – זֶה רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי חָמָא בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי בֵּיסָא.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** that there is a dispute with regard to this issue, as **Rabbi Oshaya says:** The owner of the courtyard **can prevent** the owner of the roof from sealing the pipe, while **Rabbi Ḥama,** Rabbi Oshaya's father, **says: He cannot prevent** it. Rabbi Oshaya **went** and **asked** Rabbi Ḥama's father, **Rabbi Bisa.** Rabbi Bisa **said to them:** He **can prevent** it. **Rami bar Ḥama read** the verse **about** him: "And if a man prevail against him that is alone, two shall withstand him; **and a threefold cord is not quickly broken"** (Ecclesiastes 4:12), saying that **this** applies to **Rabbi Oshaya, son of Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Bisa,** three generations of Torah scholars in one family who knew one another and conversed with each other with regard to matters of *halakha*.

The Gemara then records a direct dispute over this very question. Rabbi Oshaya ruled *me'akev* — the yard owner may object and compel the pipe to remain open. Rabbi Chama — who is, it should be noted, the *father* of Rabbi Oshaya, though he is quoted after him — ruled *eino me'akev*: the yard owner has no such right, presumably because Rabbi Chama does not regard this implicit mutual benefit as creating a legally binding obligation. The case then came before Rabbi Bisa, who is the son of Rabbi Chama and therefore the grandfather of Rabbi Oshaya. Rabbi Bisa ruled *me'akev*, siding with his grandson Rabbi Oshaya against the intermediate generation, his own son Rabbi Chama.

### Three Generations

Upon seeing this, Rami bar Chama applied to this family the *pasuk* from Kohelet: *ve-hachut hameshulash lo bimehera yinatek* — a threefold cord is not quickly broken. A string woven from three strands is far stronger than a doubled thread, and Rami bar Chama saw in this family the remarkable quality of three generations of Torah scholars in fruitful dialogue. The Gemara clarifies the lineage: *zeh Rabbi Oshaya beno shel Rabbi Chama beno shel Rabbi Bisa* — Rabbi Oshaya is the son of Rabbi Chama who is the son of Rabbi Bisa.

Tosafot raises a legitimate challenge: there are many families across generations with three consecutive *talmidei chachamim* — Rabban Gamliel's family, Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, and others. Why was this *pasuk* applied specifically here? The answer given is: *lefi she-shloshtan ra'u zeh et zeh* — because all three were alive and active simultaneously, engaging in direct *machloket* with one another. It was not merely that the family produced successive generations of scholarship; it was that grandfather, son, and grandson were all simultaneously issuing *psak*, learning, and debating — with Rabbi Oshaya, the grandson, ultimately agreeing with his grandfather Rabbi Bisa over his father Rabbi Chama. The beauty of that dynamic, including all its complexity, is what earned the family this particular praise.

## Ladders

### Egyptian Ladder Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוּלָּם הַמִּצְרִי אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי סוּלָּם הַמִּצְרִי? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אַרְבָּעָה חֲווֹקִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that with regard to **an Egyptian ladder,** which is small and portable, one **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use. The Gemara asks: **What is an Egyptian ladder like?** The members **of the school of Rabbi Yannai say: It** is **any** ladder that **does not have four rungs.**

The *mishnah* moves from drainpipes to ladders. The practical context is a ladder leaning against a wall and extending into a neighbor's yard — a structure whose continued presence raises questions of *chazakah*. The *mishnah* distinguishes between a *sulam hamitzri*, an Egyptian ladder, which does not create a *chazakah*, and a standard ladder, which presumably does. The Gemara asks: *heichi dami sulam hamitzri?* — what exactly is an Egyptian ladder? Rabbi Yannai answers: *kol she-ein lo arba'ah shalvikin* — any ladder with fewer than four rungs. A ladder with only three rungs or fewer is classified as Egyptian.

### Temporary Structures

The reason such a minimal ladder fails to create a *chazakah* is its inherently temporary and weak character. A ladder with so few rungs is more of a step stool than a true ladder — something portable and impermanent. Because of its transient nature, its continued presence on a neighbor's property does not carry the legal weight needed to create the presumption that the property owner has granted any kind of recognized right. The structural insignificance of the object mirrors its legal insignificance.

## Windows & Privacy

### Window Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַלּוֹן הַמִּצְרִית אֵין לָהּ חֲזָקָה, וְלַצּוֹרִית יֵשׁ לָהּ חֲזָקָה. אֵיזוֹ הִיא חַלּוֹן הַמִּצְרִית? כֹּל שֶׁאֵין רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם יָכוֹל לִיכָּנֵס לְתוֹכָהּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ לָהּ מַלְבֵּן – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם יָכוֹל לִיכָּנֵס לְתוֹכָהּ, הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **an Egyptian window,** one **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use; **but with regard to a Tyrian** window, one does **have** the means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use. **What is** the defining feature of **an Egyptian window?** It is **any** window that is so small **that a person's head is not able to fit inside it. Rabbi Yehuda says: If** a window **has a frame, even though a person's head is not able to fit inside it,** one does **have** the means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use.

The *mishnah* turns next to windows, a topic that connects directly to the opening concerns of the *masechet* about *hezek re'iyah* — the damage caused by visual intrusion upon a neighbor's privacy. The *mishnah* states: *chalon hamitzri ein lo chazakah u-l'Tzuri yesh lo chazakah* — an Egyptian window does not create a *chazakah*, while a Tyrian window does. Further, the *mishnah* itself defines the Egyptian window: *kol she-ein rosho shel adam yachol likaneis l'tochah* — any window through which a person cannot fit their head. A window so small — perhaps a *tefach* and a half in width — that a person's head cannot pass through is classified as Egyptian.

### Definition Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַלּוֹן הַמִּצְרִית אֵין לָהּ חֲזָקָה כּוּ׳. מַאי שְׁנָא גַּבֵּי סוּלָּם דְּלָא מְפָרֵשׁ, וּמַאי שְׁנָא גַּבֵּי חַלּוֹן דִּמְפָרֵשׁ? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי אִיפְּלוֹגֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּסֵיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that with regard to **an Egyptian window,** one **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use. The Gemara asks: **What is different with regard to** an Egyptian **ladder, that** the mishna **does not explain** what it is, **and what is different with regard to** an Egyptian **window, that** the mishna **does explain** what it is? The Gemara answers: It was necessary for the mishna to state the definition of an Egyptian window according to the unattributed opinion of the mishna **because** it **wants to** cite the **dissenting** opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda in the latter clause.**

The Gemara poses a sharp question: *mai shna gabei sulam d'la mefareish u'mai shna gabei chalon d'mefareish?* — why does the *mishnah* leave the Egyptian ladder undefined while it explicitly defines the Egyptian window? The Gemara answers: *mishum d'kaba'ei iplugi Rabbi Yehuda b'seifa* — because Rabbi Yehuda disputes the definition of the Egyptian window in the latter clause of the *mishnah*. Rabbi Yehuda holds: *im yesh lah malben af al pi she-ein rosho shel adam yachol likaneis l'tochah harei zeh chazakah* — even a smaller window can create a *chazakah* if it has a proper frame (*malben*). Because there is a *machlokes* about the window's definition, the *mishnah* must specify its criteria precisely enough to give Rabbi Yehuda's dissent a foothold. No comparable dispute exists regarding the ladder, so the *mishnah* has no need to define it. This is structurally similar to the Rashbam's explanation of why the Torah mentions that *Cham hu avi Chanaan* already when listing those who left the ark — because without establishing that detail in advance, the reader cannot make sense of Noah's later curse, which is directed at Canaan rather than at Cham directly. In both cases, the earlier precision is required to make subsequent disagreement intelligible.

### Height Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: לְמַטָּה מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה, וְיָכוֹל לְמַחוֹת; לְמַעְלָה מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְמַחוֹת. וְרַבִּי אִילְעָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַעְלָה מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, וְיָכוֹל לְמַחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to windows, **Rabbi Zeira says:** If one built a large window at a height that is **lower than four cubits** from the ground, he **has** the means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use, **and** therefore, his neighbor **can protest** the initial construction of the window. If one built a large window at a height that is **above four cubits** from the ground, he **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use, **and** therefore his neighbor **cannot protest** its construction. **And Rabbi Ile'a says: Even** if it is built at a height that is **above four cubits** from the ground, he **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use, **but** nevertheless, his neighbor **can protest** its construction.

Rabbi Zeira presents a position on the *chazakah* status of windows based on height: *lemata m'arba amot yesh lo chazakah* — a window below four *amot* from the ground creates a *chazakah*, while *lema'alah m'arba amot ein lo chazakah v'eino chalon* — a window above four *amot* neither creates a *chazakah* nor is even considered a window for these purposes. The reasoning flows directly from *hezek re'iyah*: a window positioned below the height of four *amot* — approximately the height of a standing person — enables a neighbor to look through it without any special effort and thereby violates the other party's privacy. Such a window is legally significant enough that the neighbor must object within three years; failure to do so results in a recognized *chazakah* and loss of the right to object. A window above four *amot*, by contrast, is effectively beyond the line of sight of a person standing at ground level. Since it poses no realistic threat of visual intrusion, there is nothing to object to — and correspondingly, nothing that could ripen into a *chazakah*. The four-*amot* threshold echoes the standard used elsewhere in discussions about building walls to block *hezek re'iyah*: a wall must reach four *amot* to prevent peeping, since that is the height above which a standing person cannot see.

One might ask whether this analysis changes if there is a second story involved — a neighbor at a higher elevation could presumably see through a high window. The Gemara's framework here appears to assume single-story construction for the baseline case; the principle that elevated positions require correspondingly elevated countermeasures would apply separately in such circumstances.

### Rabbi Ila Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כּוֹפִין, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִימְנִין דְּמוֹתְבַתְּ שַׁרְשִׁיפָא תּוּתָךְ, וְקָיְימַתְּ וְקָא חָזֵית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No, everyone** agrees that there is **coercion** concerning conduct characteristic of Sodom, **and** it **is different here,** as according to the opinion of Rabbi Ile'a this is not conduct characteristic of Sodom, **as** the neighbor can **say to** the one who constructed the window: There are **times when you place a bench beneath yourself, and** you **stand and see** into my home.

Rabbi Ila disagrees: *afilu lema'alah m'arba amot ein lo chazakah be'chalon hamitzri* — even a window above four *amot* does not create a *chazakah* in the case of an Egyptian window. According to Rabbi Ila, the neighbor always retains the right to object, regardless of the window's height.

### Midat Sodom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּכוֹפִין עַל מִדַּת סְדוֹם קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: כּוֹפִין, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין כּוֹפִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that **they disagree with regard to** whether there is **coercion concerning conduct** characteristic **of Sodom?** Perhaps their dispute is with regard to a circumstance where one will not suffer any loss while another gains some benefit, and the former desires to prevent the latter from gaining the benefit, if the former is coerced into not being evil without reason and consequently allows the latter to derive the benefit, counter to the behavior of the residents of Sodom. One **Sage,** Rabbi Zeira, **holds** that there is **coercion,** and therefore the neighbor who does not suffer any damage from a high window cannot protest, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Ile'a, **holds** that there is **no coercion.**

The Gemara raises the possibility that the dispute between Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Ila hinges on the question of *midat Sodom* — whether the court may compel someone to forgo a benefit that costs the other party nothing, or in this case, to remove a feature that causes the other party minimal harm. Perhaps Rabbi Zeira holds that the court does not enforce such claims regarding a high window, while Rabbi Ila holds that it does. The Gemara rejects this framing: *lo dekulei alma chofin* — everyone agrees that the court has authority to enforce in cases of *midat Sodom*. The real dispute, rather, is about the nature of the harm itself. The Gemara explains: *zimnin demotva sharshifa tutach vekayamt vechazit* — sometimes a person will place a *sharshifa*, a stool or low bench, beneath the high window, stand on it, and peer through. According to Rabbi Ila, this realistic possibility means that even a high window creates a genuine concern of *hezek re'iyah*, and the neighbor may therefore always object. Rabbi Zeira is less concerned about this scenario.

### Stool Peeping Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי. שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עֲבֵיד לֵיהּ כְּרַבִּי אִילְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that there was **a certain** individual **who came before Rabbi Ami** and presented this precise scenario. Rabbi Ami **sent him before Rabbi Abba bar Memel** to ask for a ruling. Rabbi Abba bar Memel **said to him: Act in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ile'a.**

The Gemara brings a practical case to illustrate the stakes: *hahu de'ata kimei deRabbi Ami* — a case came before Rabbi Ami. He sent the litigant to Rabbi Abba bar Memel and instructed him: *avi lei keRabbi Ila* — rule in this matter like Rabbi Ila. That is, even a high window presents a real concern of *hezek re'iyah* because of the possibility of the *sharshifa*, and the neighbor therefore retains the right to object. This represents the practical ruling emerging from the *sugya*.

### Light Windows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וּלְאוֹרָה – אֲפִילּוּ כָּל שֶׁהוּא יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** Since the mishna cited a dispute with regard to the conditions under which an owner has acquired the privilege to use a window, the Gemara teaches that **Shmuel says: And** if a window was built **for** the purpose of enabling **light** to enter a dark room, then the owner of the window **has** the means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use, **whatever** size **it is,** not only if it is a large window. And if the neighbor did not protest its construction, he cannot subsequently force the owner of the window to seal it.

Shmuel then offers a final, significant principle that rounds out the discussion: *ule'ora afilu kol shehu yesh lo hazakah* — if a window is made purely for the purpose of admitting light, even an extremely small window creates a *chazakah* after three years. The reasoning is that when it is contextually clear — from the structure of the building, the darkness of the space, or the location — that the window's sole function is to bring light into an otherwise dim room, the assumption that the neighbor might peer through it or stand on a stool to look out is no longer reasonable. The window is a structural and functional necessity of the building, and the neighbor should have objected within three years if he found it problematic. After that period, the *chazakah* is established and the right to object is lost.

The Rashbam notes an additional reason one might want a window beyond mere light: overseeing one's property or fields. Looking out to supervise is a very different function from admitting light, and it is worth bearing in mind that the classification of a window as being *le'ora* — for light only — depends on context and the overall assessment of the structure's purpose.

### Window Purpose

A natural question arises: if a property changes hands, does the three-year *chazakah* period restart for the new owner? The answer, in principle, is that the *chazakah* attaches to the physical structure rather than to the individual owner. If the original owner's window has already established a *chazakah* — or if it is clearly categorized as a light-only window — that status passes with the property. If, however, the new owner takes some action to change the use or expand the window, that action could potentially restart the clock. Absent any affirmative change, the new owner simply takes the property as it stands, and the *chazakah* already accumulated does not reset.

## Ledges & Air Rights

### Ledge Widths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַזִּיז; עַד טֶפַח – יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה, וְיָכוֹל לְמַחוֹת. פָּחוֹת מִטֶּפַח – אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, וְאֵין יָכוֹל לְמַחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a projection** emerging from the wall of one's house, overhanging a courtyard, one **has** the means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use if it protrudes at least **as far as a handbreadth,** **and** the owner of the courtyard **can protest** its construction. If it protrudes **less than a handbreadth,** the owner of the house **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use, **and** the owner of the courtyard **cannot protest** its construction.

The *mishnah* now turns to the *ziz* — a protrusion or ledge built from one person's wall extending into a neighbor's *chatzer*. The Rashbam describes it: *mi sheyesh lo kotel samuch lechatzer chaveiro vehotzi ziz tefach bolet mikotlo lechatzer chaveiro* — one who has a wall adjacent to his neighbor's yard and extends a ledge of one *tefach* protruding from his wall into that yard. The *mishnah* states: *haziz ad tefach yesh lo chazakah veyachol limchot* — a ledge of up to one *tefach* wide does create a *chazakah*, meaning the neighbor may object within three years, but if he fails to do so, the right is lost. The ledge owner acquires a recognized right to that protrusion. Conversely, *pachot mitefach ein lo chazakah veaino yachol limchot* — a ledge smaller than one *tefach* does not create a *chazakah*, but neither can the neighboring yard owner object to its removal. The two rules mirror each other: whatever is large enough to create a *chazakah* is also significant enough to be objected to; whatever is too small to ever create a *chazakah* is also too trivial to warrant objection.

This means that a protrusion less than one *tefach* wide remains in a kind of legal limbo: it never transfers ownership of that space to the ledge owner — the air rights always remain with the yard owner — but the yard owner also cannot compel its removal. Importantly, this does not prevent the yard owner from later objecting if the ledge owner tries to expand it; expansion into a larger structure is a new act and subject to its own rules.

### Expansion Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי מָנִי, וְאָמְרִי לָהּ אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי מָנִי: הֶחְזִיק בְּטֶפַח – הֶחְזִיק בְּאַרְבַּע. מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הֶחְזִיק רוֹחַב טֶפַח בְּמֶשֶׁךְ אַרְבַּע – הֶחְזִיק בְּרוֹחַב אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Mani says, and some say** that **Rabbi Ya'akov says** that **Rabbi Mani says:** If one **established an acquired privilege with regard to** a projection of **a handbreadth,** he has **established an acquired privilege with regard to four** handbreadths. The Gemara asks: **What is he saying? Abaye said** that **this** is what he **is saying:** If one **established an acquired privilege** with regard to a projection that measures **one handbreadth wide by four** handbreadths **long,** he has **established an acquired privilege with regard to** extending the projection to **a width** of **four** handbreadths.

Rabbi Yaakov cites a ruling in the name of Rabbi Mani: *hechezik be-tefach hechezik be-arba* — if one establishes a *chazakah* of one *tefach*, that *chazakah* confers the right to expand the ledge later up to four *tefachim* in width. The logic is that a ledge of one *tefach* is legally significant enough to constitute recognized ownership of that protrusion; once that ownership is established, it carries within it the potential for reasonable expansion to a legally meaningful size. A protrusion smaller than one *tefach*, being legally insignificant, can never serve as the basis for any such expansion.

### Shiurim

The appearance of these measurements — one *tefach* and four *tefachim* — invites reflection on how *shiurim* function across different areas of *halacha*. Four *tefachim* is the minimum dimension of a *reshut* — a domain of legal significance — in *hilchot Shabbat*. A space smaller than four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* lacks the status of an independent domain for purposes of carrying on *Shabbat* and may be classified as a *mekom patur*. The same intuition that informs the carrying laws — that below a certain size a space is simply not meaningful as a space — appears to inform property law as well, where one *tefach* serves as the threshold of legal significance for a *ziz*. This cross-contextual consistency suggests that *Chazal* were drawing on coherent underlying principles about what constitutes a legally significant physical unit, even if the precise thresholds differ between domains.

### Abaye Clarification

The Gemara notes that Rabbi Mani's statement requires clarification. Abaye explains: *hachi ka'amar hechezik rochav tefach be-meshech arba hechezik be-rochav arba* — what Rabbi Mani means is this: if one established a *chazakah* on a ledge that is one *tefach* wide and four *tefachim* long — that is, a narrow shelf running along the wall — then the *chazakah* acquired entitles the owner to expand the width up to four *tefachim*. The clarification is important: the expansion is unidirectional. One dimension — the length of four *tefachim* — is already fixed; the *chazakah* allows the expansion of the other dimension, the width, from one *tefach* to four *tefachim*. One may not expand both dimensions simultaneously, turning a tiny protrusion into a full four-by-four platform on the basis of a minimal *chazakah*. The initial *chazakah* locks in one dimension, and only the other may be expanded. At four by four *tefachim*, the ledge becomes a substantial structure — roughly the size of a large dinner plate — a genuinely usable surface.

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּחוֹת מִטֶּפַח – אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְמַחוֹת. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בַּעַל הַגָּג בְּבַעַל הֶחָצֵר, אֲבָל בַּעַל הֶחָצֵר בְּבַעַל הַגָּג – יָכוֹל לְמַחוֹת. וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל חָצֵר בְּבַעַל הַגָּג, אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְמַחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if the projection protrudes **less than a handbreadth** the owner of the house **has no** means to establish **an acquired privilege for its** use, **and** the owner of the courtyard **cannot protest. Rav Huna says: They taught only** that the **owner of the roof** cannot protest the actions **of** the **owner of the courtyard,** i.e., he may not demand that the owner of the courtyard refrain from construction that interferes with the former's use of the projection. **But** the **owner of the courtyard can protest** the actions **of** the **owner of the roof,** and demand that the latter not build a projection of any size, even less than a handbreadth. He can also demand that the owner of the roof not use an existing projection, since it leads to damage caused by sight. **And Rav Yehuda says: Even** the **owner of the courtyard cannot protest** the actions **of** the **owner of the roof.**

Rav Huna introduces an important distinction: *lo shanu ela baal hagag b'baal hachatzer* — the *mishnah*'s ruling applies only to the case where the house owner (the *baal hagag*) builds a ledge projecting into the yard owner's (the *baal hachatzer*'s) space. *Aval baal hachatzer b'baal hagag yachol limchot* — but if the *chatzer* owner builds onto the *house* owner's wall, the house owner may always object. The underlying distinction is about ownership: extending a protrusion into someone else's airspace over their yard is one thing, but physically attaching a structure to someone else's wall is categorically different, since the wall belongs to the house owner. Even if the yard owner might be able to build a freestanding structure adjacent to the wall within his own yard, he may not build something connected to and utilizing the neighbor's wall.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *afilu baal hachatzer b'baal hagag eino yachol limchot* — even when the *chatzer* owner builds a ledge projecting onto the *baal hagag*'s property, the *baal hagag* cannot object. Rabbi Yehuda permits this.

### Privacy Concerns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהֶיזֵּק רְאִיָּה קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק, וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say that** they **disagree with regard to damage** caused by **sight? As** one **Sage,** Rav Huna, **holds** that **it is considered** to be **damage,** and therefore the owner of the courtyard can protest, since the owner of the roof has the means to see into the other's courtyard when using this projection, **and** one **Sage,** Rav Yehuda, **holds** that **it is not considered** to be **damage.**

The Gemara then asks whether the dispute between Rav Huna and Rabbi Yehuda is really about *hezek re'iyah* — one position holding that *hezek re'iyah* counts as a real and actionable harm (*shemei hezek*) and the other that it does not. The Gemara rejects this framing: *d'kulei alma shemei hezek* — everyone agrees that *hezek re'iyah* is a genuine harm. The real question is a factual one about whether a small ledge — one *tefach* by four *tefachim* — is actually usable in a way that would produce *hezek re'iyah*.

### Stance Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לָא; דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק; וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: לְתַשְׁמִישְׁתָּא – לָא חֲזֵי; לְמַאי חֲזֵי – לְמִתְלֵא בֵּיהּ מִידֵּי; מַהְדַּרְנָא אַפַּאי וְתָלֵינָא בֵּיהּ. וְאִידַּךְ – אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִימְנִין דִּבְעִיתַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No, everyone** agrees that damage caused by sight **is considered** to be **damage. And** Rav Yehuda holds it **is different here, as** the owner of the roof can **say to** the owner of the courtyard: The projection **is not suitable for use,** since it is too small for me to stand upon and look into the courtyard. **For what** purpose **is it suitable? To hang items on it,** and nothing more. **I will turn my face** away **and hang** items **on it** without looking into your courtyard. **And the other** *amora*, Rav Huna, holds that the owner of the courtyard can **say to** the owner of the roof: There may be **times when you are frightened** due to the height of the projection, and you will look into my courtyard while using it.

The Gemara reasons: *l'mitala bah midi* — what can such a narrow ledge be used for? One might hang something from it. And the response is: *mehadreinan apin v'talainan bah* — we can require the person standing on the ledge to face away from the neighbor's yard while hanging things. Rabbi Yehuda therefore holds that requiring a backwards stance resolves the privacy concern, and thus no objection is warranted. Rav Huna responds: *zimnin d'ba'itet* — sometimes the person will become frightened or unsteady on the narrow ledge and instinctively turn around, at which point he will be looking directly into the neighbor's property. The very precariousness of standing on a narrow ledge makes it unrealistic to guarantee that the user will always maintain the required orientation. It is striking that the Gemara, having introduced the ledge discussion in terms of physical property rights and air rights, ultimately frames the dispute between Rav Huna and Rabbi Yehuda almost entirely in terms of *hezek re'iyah* and the realistic likelihood of visual intrusion.

## Shared Courtyards

### Window Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יִפְתַּח אָדָם חַלּוֹנוֹתָיו לַחֲצַר הַשּׁוּתָּפִין. לָקַח בַּיִת בְּחָצֵר אַחֶרֶת – לֹא יִפְתָּחֶנָּה בַּחֲצַר הַשּׁוּתָּפִין. בָּנָה עֲלִיָּיה עַל גַּבֵּי בֵּיתוֹ – לֹא יִפְתָּחֶנָּה לַחֲצַר הַשּׁוּתָּפִין; אֶלָּא אִם רָצָה – בּוֹנֶה אֶת הַחֶדֶר לְפָנִים מִבֵּיתוֹ, וּבוֹנֶה עֲלִיָּיה עַל גַּבֵּי בֵּיתוֹ, וּפוֹתְחָהּ לְתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person may not open his windows,** i.e., build an opening in a wall to use as a window, **into a courtyard belonging to partners,** i.e., a courtyard in which he is a partner. If he **purchased a house in another,** adjacent **courtyard,** he **may not open** the house **into a courtyard belonging to partners.** If he **built a loft on top of his house, he may not open it into a courtyard belonging to partners. Rather, if he desired** to build a loft, he may **build a room within his house, or** he may **build a loft on top of his house, and open it into his house,** not directly into the courtyard.

The *mishnah* now addresses the *chatzer hashutafin* — a jointly owned courtyard. The ruling is: *lo yiftach adam chalonotav lachatzar hashutafin* — a person who is a partner in a jointly owned *chatzer* may not open new windows facing into that shared space. Even though each partner has legal ownership rights in the *chatzer*, those rights do not extend to unilaterally altering the shared space in ways that compromise the other partner's privacy. The *chatzer* belongs to both; a window from one partner's house looking into the shared yard reduces the other partner's ability to move freely in that space without being observed.

### Stairwell Rule

The *mishnah* elaborates with two further applications. If a partner purchases a house in a different *chatzer* — meaning his door opens elsewhere — he also may not open a secondary entrance directly into the jointly owned *chatzer*: *lo yiftachena l'chatzar hashutafin*. Similarly, if he builds an upper story — *bana aliyah al gabei beito* — he may not route the stairwell through the jointly owned *chatzer*: *ella im ratzah boneh et hacheder lifnim mibeito* — he must instead build the stairwell internally within his own house, so that access to the upper floor does not pass through the shared space. All three cases — windows, secondary entrances, and stairwells — express the same underlying principle: even a partner with genuine ownership rights in the *chatzer* may not take actions that unilaterally increase foot traffic in or reduce the privacy of the shared space.

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי אִירְיָא לַחֲצַר הַשּׁוּתָּפִין? אֲפִילּוּ לַחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's ruling that one may not open a window into a courtyard that he co-owns, the Gemara asks: **Why** did the mishna **specifically** render it prohibited for one to open a window **into a courtyard belonging to partners?** One may **not** open a window **into another's courtyard either,** as it will lead to damage caused by sight.

The Gemara immediately raises what appears to be a devastating question: *mai irya chatzer hashutafin? Afilu chatzer chaveiro nami lo* — why is the *mishnah* addressing the special case of a jointly owned *chatzer*? Obviously you cannot open a window into someone else's privately owned yard either. The *chiddush* of the ruling seems trivially obvious, and a *mishnah* should not state what requires no teaching.

### Changed Expectations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר – לָא מִיבַּעְיָא לַחֲצַר חֲבֵרוֹ דְּלָא, אֲבָל לַחֲצַר הַשּׁוּתָּפִין, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: סוֹף סוֹף הָא קָא בָּעֵית אִצְטְנוֹעֵי מִינַּאי בֶּחָצֵר; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַד הָאִידָּנָא – בֶּחָצֵר הֲוָה בָּעֵינָא אִצְטְנוֹעֵי מִינָּךְ, הַשְׁתָּא – אֲפִילּוּ בַּבַּיִת נָמֵי בָּעֵינָא אִצְטְנוֹעֵי מִינָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies that the mishna **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** as follows: **It is not necessary** to say **that** it is **not** permitted for one to open a window **into another's courtyard,** where he is certainly not allowed to look; **but** where one wants to open a window **into a courtyard belonging to partners, where** the owner of the window can **say to** the other partner: **Ultimately,** since **you need to conceal** yourself **from me** and conduct yourself modestly **in the courtyard** where I too am a partner and have the right to be present, why does it bother you if I open a window into there? Therefore, the mishna **teaches us that** the partner may **say to him: Until now I needed to conceal** myself **from you** only when we were both **in the courtyard. Now I will need to conceal** myself **from you even in the house,** as you can see into my house from your window.

The Gemara's resolution explains what is actually novel about the *chatzer hashutafin* case. One might have thought that a partner, having ownership rights in the shared *chatzer*, could argue: since I own part of this space anyway, my adding a window or an entrance changes nothing of substance. The *mishnah* teaches otherwise by articulating what the neighbor can say: *ad ha'idna bachatzer hava ba'ina itstenu'ei minach hashta afilu lebayit nami ba'ina itstenu'ei minach* — until now, I only needed to maintain privacy from you within my house itself; I had some residual expectation of ease in the *chatzer*, since you were not generally passing through it. Now that you have added a window or entrance, I must maintain that privacy even in what was previously a more relaxed shared space. The partnership agreement, in other words, did not include the right to alter the physical parameters of the shared space in a way that fundamentally changes the other partner's privacy expectations. The *chiddush* is that even a co-owner's existing rights are circumscribed by what was implicitly agreed upon when the partnership was formed.

### Illegal Window Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁפָּתַח חַלּוֹנָיו לַחֲצַר הַשּׁוּתָּפִין; וּבָא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, אָמַר לוֹ: הֶחְזַקְתָּ בְּנִי, הֶחְזַקְתָּ; וּבָא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא, אָמַר: יָגַעְתָּ וּפָתַחְתָּ, יְגַע וּסְתוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving a person who opened his windows into a courtyard belonging to partners and came before Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosei,** who **said to him: You have established an acquired privilege, my son; you have established an acquired privilege,** and you may not be prevented from using the windows. **And he came before Rabbi Ḥiyya,** who **said** to him: **You toiled and opened** the windows; you must **toil and seal** them, as the partners have the right to prevent you from using these windows.

The Gemara then cites a case (*ma'aseh*) in which someone built a window into a jointly owned *chatzer* in violation of the *mishnah*'s ruling. The case came before Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose, who ruled: *hechzakta beni hechzakta* — you have established a *chazakah*, my friend; the window may remain. The litigant, apparently dissatisfied, brought the case before Rabbi Chiya, who ruled the opposite: *yagata upatachta yiga vistom* — you worked hard to open it, now work hard to close it. Rabbi Yishmael's ruling likely reflects a position not fully aligned with the *mishnah*'s ruling as understood by the Tannaitic tradition primarily shaped by Rabbi Akiva's students. Rabbi Chiya, a contemporary of Rebbe and well within that tradition, upholds the *mishnah*'s position straightforwardly.

### Blockage Chazakah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: וְלִסְתּוֹם – לְאַלְתַּר הָוֵי חֲזָקָה, שֶׁאֵין אָדָם עָשׂוּי שֶׁסּוֹתְמִים אוֹרוֹ בְּפָנָיו וְשׁוֹתֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** **And to seal,** i.e., if one sealed another's window in his presence, **there is an acquired privilege** established **immediately** to keep the window sealed, **as it is not common** behavior for **a person to have his** source of **light sealed in his presence and** remain **silent.** The fact that he did not immediately protest indicates that the one who sealed the window had the legal right to do so unilaterally, or that the owner of the window agreed.

Rav Nachman, the *Amora*, then introduces an important and somewhat surprising principle: *velistom la'altar havei chazakah* — if the other partner immediately blocks up the illegally opened window, that very act of immediate blockage constitutes a *chazakah* in his own right. The reasoning is: *she'ein adam asui shesotmim oro befanav veshoteik* — people do not simply stand by silently while someone boards up their light source. The assumption is that if a person allows their window to be blocked without protest, that silence itself is meaningful — which means that blocking it without any process or warning is its own kind of unilateral action that the law must account for. Even if the window was opened without permission and could therefore be closed, doing so immediately and without any warning creates a reciprocal problem: the person who opened the window is now deprived of a light source without recourse. Rav Nachman's principle therefore seeks to balance both sides — the wrong of opening an unauthorized window and the wrong of immediately and unilaterally extinguishing it. Two wrongs, as it were, do not make a right, and the law must protect both parties' interests simultaneously.
