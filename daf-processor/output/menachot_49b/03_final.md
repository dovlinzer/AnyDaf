# Menachot 49b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ein Me'akvin Korbanot

### Ein Me'akvin Intro

The overarching theme of this section of Menachot concerns the principle of *ein me'akvin* — that the absence of one korban does not prevent or invalidate another. Having dealt previously with the tight relationship between the *kivsei atzeret* and the *shtei halechem*, the Mishnah now turns to other groupings of *korbanot*.

### Tamidim Musafim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַתְּמִידִין אֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַמּוּסָפִין, וְלֹא הַמּוּסָפִין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַתְּמִידִין, וְלֹא הַמּוּסָפִין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Failure to sacrifice **the daily offerings does not prevent** sacrifice of **the additional offerings, and** likewise, failure to sacrifice **the additional offerings does not prevent** sacrifice of **the daily offerings. And** failure to sacrifice some of **the additional offerings** on a day when more than one is sacrificed, e.g., if it was both Shabbat and the New Moon, **does not prevent** sacrifice of the **other** additional offerings.

The Mishnah opens with the rule that *hatamidim ein me'akvin et hamusafim velo hamusafim me'akvin et hatamid*: the *tamidim* do not hold back the *musafim*, nor do the *musafim* hold back the *tamid*. That is, if on Shabbat one has lambs sufficient only for the *tamidim* but not for the *musaf*, one may still bring the *tamidim*; conversely, if one has animals for the *musafim* but not for the *tamid*, the *musaf* may still be offered without the *tamid*.

### Musafim Mutual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הַמּוּסָפִין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** And failure to sacrifice some of **the additional offerings does not prevent** sacrifice of the **other** additional offerings.

The principle extends further: *velo hamusafim me'akvin ze et ze* — the *musafim* do not hold one another back. If one lacks the full complement of animals required for a given day's *musafim* — which can be a substantial group, a bull, rams, and seven sheep — one may bring whatever is available. This is true even when two sets of *musafim* coincide, as on Shabbat Rosh Chodesh, when the *musafim* of Shabbat and those of Rosh Chodesh do not impede one another.

### Two Tamidim Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבּוֹקֶר – יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** If the priests **did not sacrifice a lamb in the morning** as the daily offering, nevertheless, **they should sacrifice** a lamb **in the afternoon** as the daily offering, as failure to sacrifice one daily offering does not prevent sacrifice of the other.

The Mishnah then states: *lo hikrivu keves baboker yakrivu bein ha'arbayim* — if the morning lamb was not brought, the afternoon one may still be offered. This is noteworthy: one might have assumed that the two *tamidim* form a matched pair such that one cannot stand without the other, much as we saw with the *musafim*. The Mishnah establishes that each may be brought independently.

### Rabbi Shimon Tamidim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָיוּ אֲנוּסִין אוֹ שׁוֹגְגִין, אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ מְזִידִין וְלֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבּוֹקֶר – לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: When** does this *halakha* apply? It applies **at a time when** the failure to sacrifice the daily morning offering was because **they were** prevented from sacrificing it due to **circumstances beyond their control or** they failed to sacrifice it **unwittingly. But if** the priests acted **intentionally and did not sacrifice a lamb in the morning** as the daily offering, **they should not sacrifice** a lamb **in the afternoon** as the daily offering.

*Rabbi Shimon* qualifies this ruling. *Eimatai?* — when does the above apply? *Bizman shehayu anusim o shogegim* — only when the failure to bring the morning *tamid* was due to circumstances beyond one's control, whether compulsion (*ones*) or inadvertence (*shogeg*). But *aval im hayu mezidin* — if the failure was a willful choice — *lo yakrivu bein ha'arbayim*: they may not bring the afternoon *tamid* at all.

This position reflects a broader tendency in Rabbi Shimon's thinking. Elsewhere in an earlier Mishnah, Rabbi Shimon held that if one must choose between bringing some of the *musafim* with their *nesachim* or bringing all of them without *nesachim*, it is preferable to bring fewer offerings done properly. The same logic applies here: he regards there as a meaningful relationship between the two *tamidim*, and he would rather that no *tamid* be brought at all than have the afternoon offered as if it stands entirely on its own — at least when the omission of the morning one was a deliberate choice.

### Nesachim Priority

The connecting thread is that Rabbi Shimon, in both contexts, insists that *korbanot* be performed correctly and completely. Just as he maintained the bond between each animal and its *minchat nesachim*, here he maintains the bond between the morning and afternoon *tamid*. Either bring both, or — if the omission of the morning was *meizid* — bring neither. If there was no choice, the *anusim* or *shogeg* case, the afternoon offering remains valid.

### Ketoret Morning Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִקְטִירוּ קְטוֹרֶת בַּבּוֹקֶר – יַקְטִירוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Incense was burned twice a day, half a measure in the morning and half a measure in the afternoon. If **they did not burn** the half-measure of **incense in the morning, they should burn** the half-measure **in the afternoon.**

The Mishnah continues: *lo hiktiru ketoret baboker yaktiru bein ha'arbayim* — if the *ketoret* was not offered in the morning, it may be offered in the afternoon. Recall that the full daily measure of *ketoret* — one *maneh* — was divided: half in the morning and half in the afternoon. Rabbi Shimon's position here is striking. He does not contest the legitimacy of the afternoon *ketoret* on its own, as he did with the *tamid*. However, he does add: *amar Rabbi Shimon vechulah hayeta krevah bein ha'arbayim* — if the morning *ketoret* was not offered and only the afternoon offering remains, the entire full measure should be brought in the afternoon, not merely the half that was originally designated for that time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכוּלָּהּ הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, שֶׁאֵין מְחַנְּכִים אֶת מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב אֶלָּא בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים, וְלֹא מִזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה אֶלָּא בְּתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר, וְלֹא אֶת הַשּׁוּלְחָן אֶלָּא בְּלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים בְּשַׁבָּת, וְלֹא אֶת הַמְּנוֹרָה אֶלָּא בְּשִׁבְעָה נֵרוֹתֶיהָ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: And** in such a case, **the entire** measure **was sacrificed in the afternoon.** The reason for the difference between the daily offerings and the incense is **that** the daily service on a new **golden altar is initiated only with** the burning of **the incense of the spices,** in the afternoon, at which time they would burn a full measure. **And the** daily service on a new **altar of the burnt offering,** on which the daily offerings were sacrificed, is initiated **only with the daily morning offering. And** use of a new **Table** was initiated **only with** the arrangement of **the shewbread on Shabbat, and** use of a new **Candelabrum** was initiated **only with** the kindling **of its seven lamps in the afternoon.**

The Mishnah then turns to the laws of *chinuch*, the inauguration of the vessels and altars of the Mikdash. The *mizbeach hazahav*, the golden altar, is inaugurated specifically by the burning of *ketoret*; the outer *mizbeach ha'olah* is inaugurated by the *tamid shel shachar*; the *shulchan* by the *lechem hapanim* on Shabbat; and the *menorah* by the lighting of its seven lamps in the afternoon. Each vessel is inaugurated through the performance of its characteristic *avodah*. The mention of the *tamid shel shachar* as the vehicle for inaugurating the outer altar is, as the *gemara* will develop, the key to understanding Rabbi Shimon's position regarding the primacy of the morning *tamid*.

## Precedence Tamidim

### Precedence Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: צִיבּוּר שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן תְּמִידִין וּמוּסָפִין, אֵי זֶה מֵהֶן קוֹדֵם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda:** In the case of **a community that did not have** the resources to sacrifice both **the daily offerings and the additional offerings, which of them takes precedence** over the other?

The Gemara opens this section with a question posed by *Rabbi Chiya bar Avin* to *Rav Chisda*: if the community lacks sufficient animals for both *tamidim* and *musafim*, which takes precedence? This is not a question about the order in which they are offered when all are available, but rather — when a genuine choice must be made and only one set of offerings can be brought — which one is to be given priority?

### Same vs. Different Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא תְּמִידִין דְּיוֹמֵיהּ וּמוּסָפִין דְּיוֹמֵיהּ – פְּשִׁיטָא תְּמִידִין עֲדִיפִי, דְּהָווּ לְהוּ תָּדִיר וּמְקוּדָּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** to which Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin is referring? **If we say** that he is referring to a case where the choice is between **the daily offerings of that day and the additional offerings of that** same **day,** it is **obvious** that **the daily offerings are given preference, as** the sacrifice of the daily offerings **is** more **frequent** than the sacrifice of the additional offerings, which are sacrificed only on special occasions, **and** the daily offerings are also **sanctified.**

The Gemara immediately draws a distinction. If the question is about choosing between the *tamidim* of today and the *musafim* of today — say, on Shabbat, when two lambs are needed for the *tamidim* and two for the *musaf* — that case is straightforward.

### Tadir Principle

*Pshita tmidim adifei* — obviously the *tamidim* take precedence. The reason is *tadir*: the *tamidim* are more frequent, brought every single day, and the principle *kol hatadir mechaveiro kodem et chaveiro* — the more regular precedes the less regular — is well established. Moreover, the *musaf* of Shabbat is no more *kadosh* than the *tamid* of Shabbat. When frequency and sanctity both point in the same direction, the answer is obvious.

### Tadir Mikudash

Rashi and Tosafot both note that *mikudash* here does not mean that the *tamid* is holier than the *musaf* in any absolute sense, but that it is at least as holy. Since the *tamid* of Shabbat is no less *mikudash* than the *musaf* of Shabbat, and is additionally *tadir*, it clearly prevails. The genuinely difficult case is when *tadir* and *mikudash* do not align — when one offering is more frequent but the other is more sanctified — but the same-day case presents no such tension.

### Tomorrow Tamid Today

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, תְּמִידִין דְּלִמְחַר וּמוּסָפִין דְּהָאִידָּנָא – תְּמִידִין עֲדִיפִי, שֶׁכֵּן תָּדִיר, אוֹ דִלְמָא מוּסָפִין עֲדִיפִי, דְּהָווּ לְהוּ מְקוּדָּשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin must be referring to a case where they have enough animals for the daily offerings of today and also for either **the daily offerings for tomorrow or the additional offerings for today.** In such a case, are **the daily offerings given preference since** their sacrifice is more **frequent? Or perhaps the additional offerings are given preference because they are** considered more **sanctified** because they are sacrificed on a holy day.

The hard case is the one the Gemara actually presses: *tmidim delmachar umusafim de'idna* — must one choose between tomorrow's *tamidim* and today's *musafim*? Suppose the Mikdash has four lambs and no prospect of obtaining more before the next day — since animals must be inspected four days in advance, even a last-minute delivery could not be used. The question becomes: does one seize today's opportunity, bringing today's *musaf*, or does one plan ahead and preserve the animals for tomorrow's *tamidim*?

This framing itself generates a subsidiary question. On one side, *tmidim adifei sheken tadir* — the *tamidim* warrant priority as the more frequent offering, and there is no better example of *tadir* than the daily *tamid*. On the other side, *musafim adif dehavei lemikudash* — perhaps the *musafim* are weightier, since today's offerings, whatever their character, belong to a sanctified context. Rashi offers two readings of *mikudash* here. One reading is straightforward: today is Shabbat, a sacred day, and a *korban* tied to a *kadosh* day implicitly carries greater association with sanctity than a *korban* of an ordinary weekday. The other reading is more provocative: *mikudash* does not denote greater inherent holiness at all, but rather the pressing reality of the present moment — today's obligation presents itself now and demands to be addressed now, whereas tomorrow's concern is not yet here. This second reading casts the question in terms of present urgency versus future planning, which is itself a fascinating dimension of the problem.

The Gemara attempts to resolve the question from the opening Mishnah — *hatamidim ein me'akvin et hamusafim* — arguing that it can only be making sense if it addresses the case of choosing between today's *musafim* and tomorrow's *tamidim*, and that in such a case neither prevails over the other, meaning the choice is equivalent. However, *Abaye* rejects this reading. He maintains that the Mishnah is speaking *bediavad* — about what happens after the fact when one offering was performed without the other — and not about *lechatchila* choices at all. The rule *shelo yehei davar kodem letamid shel shachar*, derived from the verse *ve'arach aleha ha'olah*, establishes that nothing may precede the *tamid lechatchila*; Rashi cites Rava's statement *ha'olah olah rishona* in support. Abaye acknowledges that this applies only *lechatchila*, and that *bediavad* the offering remains valid if done out of order. But the consequence is that the Mishnah cannot be pressed as a proof for how to choose between today's *musafim* and tomorrow's *tamidim*. The question, therefore, remains unresolved at this point in the Gemara.

## Reserve Sheep Checking

### Six Sheep Reserve

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מִשִּׁשָּׁה טְלָאִים הַמְבוּקָּרִין בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַטְּלָאִים, כְּדֵי לַשַּׁבָּת וְלִשְׁנֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin's dilemma based upon a mishna (*Arakhin* 13a): One maintains **no fewer than six lambs that** have been **inspected** and found to be unblemished **in the Chamber of the Lambs, sufficient for Shabbat and for the two festival days of Rosh HaShana** that in some years occur adjacent to it.

The Gemara introduces a *baraita*: *ein pochtin me'shisha tela'im hamevukarin belishkat hatela'im ledei Shabbat uleshnei yamim tovim shel Rosh Hashanah* — there must be no fewer than six inspected sheep in the chamber of sheep (*lishkat hatela'im*), sufficient for Shabbat and the two days of Rosh Hashanah. The straightforward reading suggests that this reserve was maintained to ensure that if Shabbat and Rosh Hashanah — the only two-day *yom tov* observed in Eretz Yisrael — arrived without opportunity to acquire new animals, the *tamidim* would still be available. Six sheep would cover two *tamidim* for Shabbat and two *tamidim* for each day of Rosh Hashanah, with the *musafim* conspicuously absent from this accounting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ תְּמִידִין וּמוּסָפִין – טוּבָא הֲווֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say that** it is referring to a case where the Temple treasury **has** enough animals for all of the offerings that should be brought, six lambs are not enough, as **there are many** lambs sacrificed for **daily offerings and additional offerings** on those three days.

The Gemara challenges this reading: if the purpose were to ensure the full complement of *korbanot* for those days, six is grossly insufficient — *tuva havu*, far more would be needed, since the *musafim* of Rosh Hashanah alone include seven lambs, two bulls, and a ram. The Gemara therefore concludes that *ela lav deleis lei* — the scenario is one where the community lacks sufficient animals and must choose, and the reserve is exclusively for the *tamidim*. This would suggest *shema minah temidin adifei* — the *tamidim* take priority even over today's *musafim*, including tomorrow's *tamidim* over today's *musafim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלֵית לֵיהּ, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּמִידִין עֲדִיפִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not that** the mishna is referring to a case where the Temple treasury **does not have** enough animals for all of the offerings, and it is teaching that the animals that he does have are utilized for the daily offerings of all three days rather than for the additional offering of Shabbat on the first of the three days? Consequently, one may **conclude from it** that the **daily offerings** of tomorrow **are given preference** over the additional offerings of today.

Rashi sharpens the inference: if Shabbat precedes Rosh Hashanah, the community would bring only two animals on Shabbat — the *tamidim* — deliberately foregoing the Shabbat *musaf*, in order to preserve the remaining four for the two days of Rosh Hashanah's *tamidim*. That is a direct prioritization of tomorrow's *tamid* over today's *musaf*.

### Ben Bag Bag Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּאִית לֵיהּ, וְהָכִי קָא אָמַר: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מִשִּׁשָּׁה טְלָאִים הַמְבוּקָּרִין בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַטְּלָאִים אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה, וּמַנִּי? בֶּן בַּג בַּג הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, בֶּן בַּג בַּג אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְתָמִיד שֶׁטָּעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תִּשְׁמְרוּ לְהַקְרִיב לִי בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה לָכֶם לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת עַד אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם״. מָה לְהַלָּן טָעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה, אַף כָּאן טָעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **No,** this is not a valid proof, as one can claim that **actually,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the Temple treasury **has** enough animals for all of the offerings of the three days, **and this is what** the mishna **is saying** when it mentions six lambs: One maintains for use as daily offerings **no fewer than six lambs that** have been **inspected** and found to be unblemished **in the Chamber of the Lambs four days prior to** their **slaughter.** Lambs sacrificed as other offerings do not have to be inspected four days prior to being slaughtered. **And** in accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **ben Bag Bag.** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **ben Bag Bag says: From where** is it derived that **the daily offering requires inspection four days prior to** its **slaughter? The verse states** with regard to the daily offering: **"You shall observe [*tishmeru*] to sacrifice to Me in its due season"** (Numbers 28:2), **and elsewhere,** with regard to the Paschal offering, the verse **states:** "On the tenth day of this month they shall take for them every man a lamb…**and you shall keep it [*mishmeret*] until the fourteenth day** of this month" (Exodus 12:3–6). Consequently, it is derived by means of a verbal analogy that **just as** in the verse **there,** the Paschal offering **requires inspection four days prior to** its **slaughter,** from the tenth of the month to the fourteenth, **so too here,** the daily offering **requires inspection four days prior to** its **slaughter.**

The Gemara then explains why the reserve addresses only *tamidim* and not *musafim*. *Ben Bag Bag* derives from a *gezerah shavah* — comparing the phrase *tishmeru lehakriv li bemo'ado* regarding the *tamid* to *vehayah lachem lemishmeret* regarding the *korban Pesach* — that the *tamid* specifically requires four days of prior inspection before *shechitah*, just as the *korban Pesach* was set aside on the tenth of Nisan and slaughtered on the fourteenth. No such requirement applies to other *korbanot*, including *musafim*. The reserve in the *lishkat hatela'im* exists precisely because of this four-day requirement: animals cannot simply be acquired at the last moment and used for a *tamid*. The *musafim*, by contrast, need no such advance preparation and are therefore not part of this reserve system.

The pairing of the *korban Pesach* and the *tamid* in this *gezerah shavah* is itself notable. The *korban Pesach* is the most singular annual event — brought once a year with enormous national significance. The *tamid*, by contrast, is brought every single day, and it is precisely the dailiness of the *tamid* that risks making it feel routine or taken for granted. That these two extremes — the once-yearly pinnacle and the daily constant — are the only two *korbanot* requiring the same four-day degree of preparation and vigilance is a meaningful statement: the care one invests in the extraordinary must equally be invested in the ordinary.

### Six vs Seven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָנֵי שִׁשָּׁה? שִׁבְעָה הָווּ, דְּהָא אִיכָּא דְּצַפְרָא דִּתְלָתָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a discussion pertaining to the mishna cited earlier. **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** With regard to **these six** inspected lambs that must always be kept in the Chamber of Lambs, there ought to **be seven, as there is** a need for another lamb for the daily offering **of the morning of the third** day **of the week,** since it cannot be inspected on Shabbat or the subsequent festival days of Rosh HaShana, and therefore should be inspected and kept beforehand.

*Ravina* raises a difficulty to *Rav Ashi*: given the purpose of the reserve, six sheep are actually insufficient — there should be seven, or perhaps even eight. If, for example, Shabbat precedes Rosh Hashanah, the six sheep in reserve will be drawn upon during Shabbat and Rosh Hashanah, but by the morning after Rosh Hashanah, all six will have been used, and no inspected sheep will remain for the *tamid* the following morning. *Ha'ika tzafra detilsa beshabbta* — there is the morning of the third day of the week, by which point the reserve is exhausted. How will the *tamid* be brought then? Conversely, Rav Ashi might respond that according to Ravina's logic one should need eight, since even the *erev Shabbat* afternoon *tamid* — *kamei detamid shel bein ha'arbayim* — requires a checked animal, and by that point there would similarly be no time to replenish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, תְּמָנְיָא הָווּ, דְּהָאִיכָּא דְּפַנְיָא דְּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi responded to Ravina: **According to your reasoning** the correct number is not seven, but rather **it is eight; as there is** also the daily offering **of the afternoon of Shabbat eve** that should also be counted.

### Mnemonic Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, דִּלְבָתַר דְּאַקְרֵיב קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina replied: **That** is **not difficult,** because the mishna **is saying that after he sacrificed** the daily offering of the afternoon there must be six inspected lambs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם שִׁבְעָה הָווּ! אֶלָּא, תַּנָּא בְּעָלְמָא קָאֵי, וּמַאי כְּדֵי לַשַּׁבָּת וּשְׁנֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה? סִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In any case,** the question stands: Why does the mishna say six lambs when, in fact the number required **is seven?** The Gemara answers: **Rather, the *tanna* is** speaking **generally,** referring to how many inspected lambs are required throughout the year. **And what** did he mean by his statement: **Sufficient for Shabbat and for the two festival days of Rosh HaShana?** It is **merely** intended as **a mnemonic,** to help one remember that there must be enough lambs for the daily offering of three days.

The Gemara resolves both objections by concluding that the *baraita* is not literally about preparing for Shabbat and Rosh Hashanah specifically. *Simana be'alma* — the mention of Shabbat and two days of Rosh Hashanah is simply a mnemonic for the number six, not a prescription for those days exactly. The actual rule is that the *lishkat hatela'im* should maintain a standing reserve of six inspected sheep as a general operational standard. Naturally, if Shabbat or Rosh Hashanah were approaching, the reserve would be augmented. The Gemara notes this can also be inferred from the *baraita*'s language: it says *kedei Shabbat*, meaning "equivalent to" or "in the amount of," rather than *leShabbat*, "for Shabbat" — a subtle linguistic indicator that Shabbat and Rosh Hashanah serve as a mnemonic frame rather than the operative purpose.

### Rotation System

The *Shittah Mekubbetzet* elaborates on the underlying operational logic. The *lishkat hatela'im* operated as a rotating reserve: two new animals were introduced into the chamber each day, each set requiring four days of inspection before use. If one traces through the rotation — animals placed on Sunday drawn for use on Thursday, and so forth — the system actually requires eight sheep to be present at any given moment, not six. The *Shittah Mekubbetzet* resolves this by noting that six is the number that remains in the chamber at the precise moment after that day's pair has been withdrawn for *shechitah* but before the day's two new animals have been added. It is admittedly a somewhat strained accounting — logically, one would have said the minimum is eight — but it explains how the figure of six emerges from the mechanics of the rotation. The central point is clear: this is a management system designed to ensure a constant supply of *tamidim* with the required four-day lead time, and it has nothing to do with choosing between *tamidim* and *musafim*.

The Gemara's attempted proof from this *baraita* — that the preference for *tamidim* in the reserve demonstrates that tomorrow's *tamid* takes precedence over today's *musaf* — therefore fails. The reserve focuses on *tamidim* not because of any comparative ranking between *tamidim* and *musafim*, but because only *tamidim* require the four-day advance inspection. The original question of which takes precedence thus remains without a definitive answer.

## Afternoon Tamid Only

### Mezid Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבּוֹקֶר – לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁלֹּא נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the mishna **is incomplete, and this is** what **it is teaching:** If **they did not sacrifice a lamb** for the daily offering **in the morning, they should not sacrifice** a lamb in **the afternoon. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** the service of a new **altar had not** yet **been initiated,** since it must first be initiated with the daily offering of the morning. **But** if the service of **the altar had** already **been initiated,** then **they should sacrifice** the lamb of the daily offering in **the afternoon** even though they did not sacrifice the lamb of the morning offering.

The Gemara now engages directly with Rabbi Shimon's position in the Mishnah. The baseline rule — that if the morning *tamid* was not brought, the afternoon one may still be offered — is read in light of the Mishnah's closing lines about *chinuch hamizbeach*. The Gemara frames it as follows: the Mishnah states *lo hikrivu keves baboker lo yakrivu bein ha'arbayim* (Rabbi Shimon's formulation for the *meizid* case). This apparently raises an acute problem: is the *mizbeach* to suffer on account of the *kohanim*'s failure?

### Chinuch Mizbeach

The Gemara introduces the device of *chisorei mechasra* — the Mishnah is missing context — and supplies it. The rule that one may not bring the afternoon *tamid* alone applies specifically *shelo nitchanech hamizbeach*, when the outer altar has not yet been inaugurated. As the Mishnah itself establishes, the *mizbeach ha'olah* is inaugurated by none other than the *tamid shel shachar*. Therefore, before inauguration, the morning offering is indispensable — one cannot begin with the afternoon. *Aval nitchanech hamizbeach yakriv bein ha'arbayim*: once the altar has been inaugurated, one may offer the afternoon *tamid* even without the morning one, provided there was no willful neglect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָיוּ אֲנוּסִין אוֹ שׁוֹגְגִין, אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ מְזִידִין – לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבּוֹקֶר, לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: When** does this *halakha* apply? It applies **at a time when** the failure to sacrifice the daily morning offering was because **they were** prevented from sacrificing it due to **circumstances beyond their control or** they failed to sacrifice it **unwittingly. But if** the priests acted **intentionally and did not sacrifice a lamb in the morning** as the daily offering, **they should not sacrifice** a lamb **in the afternoon** as the daily offering.

This also clarifies the position of the *chachamim*. They do not dispute the primacy of the *tamid shel shachar* — the Mishnah's statement about inauguration itself acknowledges it. Their disagreement with Rabbi Shimon is more limited: they accept that the morning *tamid* is foundational and that one must begin with it, but they do not extend this to a rule barring the afternoon *tamid* when the morning was not brought through *meizid*. For the *chachamim*, the weight given to the morning *tamid* is expressed through the *chinuch* requirement, not through a general restriction on the afternoon offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: ״הָעֹלָה״ – עוֹלָה רִאשׁוֹנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says** in explanation of this derivation in the *baraita* that the term **"the burnt offering,"** with the definite article, is referring to **the first burnt offering** of the day, which is the daily offering.

The verse cited — *vezeh asher ta'aseh al hamizbeach, kevasim benei shanah shenayim layom tamid* (Shemot 29:38) and following — is read not only as describing ongoing procedure but also as prescribing how the *avodah* of the *mizbeach* begins. The Torah's strong identification of the *mizbeach* as the *mizbeach ha'olah*, and the *tamid* as the foundational *korban* offered there *asuyah beHar Sinai*, both reinforce the primacy of the morning *tamid* at the moment of inauguration.

### Ketoret Afternoon OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִקְטִירוּ קְטוֹרֶת בַּבּוֹקֶר, יַקְטִירוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if the priests acted intentionally and **did not burn** the **incense in the morning,** even those same priests **may burn** it in the **afternoon.**

The Mishnah's treatment of the *ketoret* proceeds differently. *Lo hiktiru ketoret baboker yaktiru bein ha'arbayim* — if the morning *ketoret* was not offered, the afternoon one may be brought — and here Rabbi Shimon does not interpose the same *meizid* restriction that he applies to the *tamid*. The implication is that for the *ketoret*, the afternoon offering is legitimate even when the omission of the morning was willful. Rabbi Shimon adds that in such a case, the full measure of *ketoret* — the entire *maneh*, not merely the half ordinarily designated for the afternoon — should be offered then.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאֵת הַכֶּבֶשׂ הַשֵּׁנִי תַּעֲשֶׂה בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״ – שֵׁנִי בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם, וְלֹא רִאשׁוֹן בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that it is derived **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: "The one lamb you shall offer in the morning; **and the second lamb you shall offer in the afternoon"** (Exodus 29:39). This verse teaches that **the second** lamb of the daily offering is sacrificed **in the afternoon, but** if it is **the first** one to be sacrificed, it may **not** be sacrificed **in the afternoon.**

The source for restricting the afternoon *tamid* is derived from the verse: *et hakeves ha'echad ta'aseh baboker ve'et hakeves hasheni ta'aseh bein ha'arbayim* — *sheni bein ha'arbayim velo rishon bein ha'arbayim*. The afternoon offering must be the second, not the first. If nothing was offered in the morning, the afternoon offering would be the first, which is impermissible. The *baraita* then qualifies this: *bameh devarim amurim shelo nitchanech hamizbeach* — this restriction on doing the afternoon offering as a "first" applies only when the altar has not yet been inaugurated. *Aval nitchanech hamizbeach afilu rishon bein ha'arbayim* — once inaugurated, even the afternoon can serve as the first in the day, at least where the omission was beyond one's control. Rabbi Shimon then limits even the post-inauguration leniency to *anusim o shogegim*.

### Kohanim Replacement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי כֹהֲנִים חָטְאוּ, מִזְבֵּחַ בָּטֵל?! אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר – לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ הֵן, אֲבָל אֲחֵרִים יַקְרִיבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does it make sense that **because the priests sinned** by intentionally failing to sacrifice the morning daily offering, **the altar** should be entirely **idle? Rava said** that **this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying: They,** the priests who deliberately failed to sacrifice the morning daily offering, **should not sacrifice** the afternoon daily offering; **but other** priests **should sacrifice** it.

The question stands: if Rabbi Shimon's concern is rabbinic in character — a protective measure against negligence — then why should the *mizbeach* suffer because of what the *kohanim* did or failed to do? *Amar Rava hachi ka'amar*: Rabbi Shimon does not mean that no *tamid* will be offered at all. Rather, *lo hikrivu hen aval acherim yakrivu* — those particular *kohanim* who were negligent in the morning are not permitted to perform the afternoon *tamid*, but other *kohanim* may do so. The system continues; it is only the negligent *kohanim* themselves who are barred. This reframing positions Rabbi Shimon's ruling as a disciplinary measure directed at individuals, not as a withholding of *avodah* from the *mizbeach* itself.

## Tamid vs Ketoret

### Ketoret Rarity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִקְטִירוּ קְטוֹרֶת בַּבּוֹקֶר – יַקְטִירוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, דְּכֵיוָן דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא, וּמְעַתְּרָא, חֲבִיבָא לְהוּ וְלָא פָּשְׁעִי.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if the priests acted intentionally and **did not burn** the **incense in the morning,** even those same priests **may burn** it in the **afternoon.** The reason for this is **that since** burning the incense **is uncommon and causes** those who do so to become **wealthy,** it is **dear to the** priests, **and they will not be negligent** in the performance of this rite.

The Gemara now directly addresses why Rabbi Shimon treats the *ketoret* differently from the *tamid* and does not apply the same protective restriction to it. The explanation offered is: *dechivan delo shechichi um'atrah* — since the *ketoret* is rare and enriches those who perform it. This requires clarification: the *ketoret* is offered daily, so in what sense is it rare? The answer lies in the structure of its performance. While the *tamid* involved many *kohanim* — the Mishnah in Tamid describes thirteen *kohanim* engaged in various aspects of the offering — only a single *kohen* burned the *ketoret*, and the privilege was awarded by lottery. No *kohen* who had previously won the *ketoret* lottery could participate again until every other eligible *kohen* had had his turn. In practical terms, a given *kohen* might wait his entire career in the Mikdash before the opportunity came around. Furthermore, the tradition held that offering the *ketoret* brought wealth to the one who performed it. Given both its rarity and its desirability, *chaviva lehu velo pash'ei* — the *kohanim* held it dear and would not be negligent about it. A *gezeirah* protecting against morning negligence is therefore unnecessary.

The Gemara's language here also shifts subtly: whereas Rabbi Shimon spoke of *mezidin*, the Gemara frames his concern in terms of *peshi'ah*, negligence. This shift is deliberate. It signals that Rabbi Shimon's position in the *tamid* case is not a principled ruling grounded in the inherent pairing of morning and afternoon offerings, but rather a rabbinic protective measure against laxity — and where laxity is not a realistic concern, as with the *ketoret*, no such protection is needed.

### Full Ketoret Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכוּלָּהּ הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: And** in such a case, **the entire** measure **was sacrificed in the afternoon.**

Rabbi Shimon's statement that when the *ketoret* is offered only in the afternoon, the full measure — the entire *maneh* — should be offered, follows from the same logic. If the afternoon *ketoret* is legitimate on its own, it should not be treated as merely half an offering, a truncated substitute for what was missed. It stands in its own right, and it should therefore be offered in full.

### Zahav Olah Chinuch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכוּלָּהּ הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, שֶׁאֵין מְחַנְּכִים אֶת מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב אֶלָּא בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: And** in such a case, **the entire** measure **was sacrificed in the afternoon.** The reason for the difference between the daily offerings and the incense is **that** the daily service on a new **golden altar is initiated only with** the burning of **the incense of the spices of the afternoon,** at which time they would burn a full measure.

The Mishnah's statement about *chinuch* — that the *mizbeach hazahav* is inaugurated by the *ketoret* and the *mizbeach ha'olah* by the *tamid shel shachar* — is now read as part of Rabbi Shimon's continuing statement in the Gemara. Specifically, the phrase *she'ein mechanchin et hamizbeach hazahav ela biktoret samim shel bein ha'arbayim* — the golden altar is inaugurated specifically by the *afternoon ketoret* — appears in Rabbi Shimon's view with the addition *shel bein ha'arbayim*, a phrase absent from the straightforward reading of the Mishnah. This addition is significant: Rabbi Shimon holds that it is the *afternoon ketoret* that serves as the vehicle for the inaugural act on the golden altar, not the morning one.

This stands in direct contrast to the outer altar, where the inaugural act is the *tamid shel shachar*, the morning offering. The two altars thus operate on different founding orientations: the outer altar's *chinuch* belongs to the morning; the golden altar's *chinuch* belongs to the afternoon. This asymmetry may itself explain why Rabbi Shimon is more permissive about an afternoon-only *ketoret* than about an afternoon-only *tamid*: for the *mizbeach hazahav*, the afternoon is not a fallback position but the more fundamental expression of the *avodah*.

### Tannai Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל שַׁחַר! תַּנָּאֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The service of a new golden altar is initiated **with** the burning of **the incense of the spices of the morning?** The Gemara answers: The question of whether the incense of the morning or the afternoon initiates the service of a new golden altar **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.**

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *vehatanya biktores samim shel shachar?* — another *baraita* states that the *mizbeach hazahav* is inaugurated with the morning *ketoret*, not the afternoon. The Gemara acknowledges the contradiction and concludes: *tannai hi* — this is a dispute among *tannaim*. There is thus a genuine disagreement about whether the morning or the afternoon *ketoret* is the more fundamental expression that grounds the *chinuch* of the golden altar.

### Menorah Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּבֹּקֶר בַּבֹּקֶר בְּהֵיטִיבוֹ אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת יַקְטִירֶנָּה״. אִי לָאו דַּעֲבַד הַדְלָקָה מֵאוּרְתָּא, הֲטָבָה בְּצַפְרָא מֵהֵיכָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** It **stands to reason** that the *halakha* should be **in accordance with the one who says** that it was initiated **with the incense of the spices of the afternoon, as it is written** with regard to the golden altar: "And Aaron shall burn thereon incense of sweet spices; **every morning, when he dresses the lamps, he shall burn it.** And when Aaron lights the lamps at dusk, he shall burn it, a perpetual incense before the Lord throughout your generations" (Exodus 30:7–8). The fact that ashes are removed from the lamps of the Candelabrum in the morning indicates that the lamps had been lit previously, since **if** the priest had **not performed the lighting** of the lamps the previous **evening, from where** would the ashes be **removed in the morning?** This proves that the Candelabrum must have been lit for the first time in the evening. Since the verse states: "When Aaron lights the lamps at dusk, he shall burn it, a perpetual incense before the Lord," it must be that the incense was burned for the first time in the evening.

*Abaye* offers a proof that the opinion favoring the afternoon *ketoret* is more compelling. The verse states *baboker baboker behitivo et hanerot yaktirena* — every morning when the *kohen* cleans the lamps of the *menorah*, he burns the *ketoret* (Shemot 30:7). Abaye reasons: *ee lav de'avid hadlaka me'ourta hatava betzafra meheicha* — the only reason the *kohen* can clean out the *menorah* in the morning is because it was lit the evening before. The *ketoret* and the *menorah* are linked in the Torah's presentation: *ketoret* accompanies the lighting of the *menorah* in the evening and the cleaning of the *menorah* in the morning. Since the *menorah*'s cycle begins with the evening lighting — not the morning cleaning — the *ketoret* cycle similarly has its origin in the evening. By extension, the afternoon *ketoret* (which precedes the evening lighting) is the more fundamental of the two. This is, as Abaye's own formulation suggests, an indirect argument, and it may not fully convince on its own terms.

### Olah Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל שַׁחַר, גָּמַר מִמִּזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה: מָה לְהַלָּן בְּתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר – אַף כָּאן בִּקְטוֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל שַׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that the service of a new golden altar is initiated **with** the burning of **the incense of the spices of the morning,** from where is this *halakha* derived? The Gemara answers: He **derives** it **from** the initiation of **the altar of the burnt offering. Just as there,** the service of a new altar of the burnt offering is initiated **by** means of the **daily offering of the morning** rather than the afternoon, **so too here,** the service of a new golden altar is initiated **by** means of the burning of **the incense of the spices of the morning.**

The alternative derivation — supporting the view that the morning *ketoret* grounds the *chinuch* — reasons from an analogy to the outer altar: *gamar mimizbach ha'olah*. Just as the outer altar's system of morning and afternoon is anchored in the morning *tamid shel shachar*, so too the golden altar's system of morning and afternoon *ketoret* should be anchored in the morning. The two altars are structurally parallel: both involve a morning and afternoon *avodah*, both are *mezbeichot* of the Mikdash, and the governing logic of one should map onto the other.

The underlying dispute, then, is about how to categorize the *mizbeach hazahav*. Is it most naturally grouped with the *mizbeach ha'olah* — in which case the morning is primary for both — or is it grouped with the other furnishings of the *heichal*, the *menorah* and *shulchan*, which operate on a cycle that begins in the afternoon or evening? If the *mizbeach hazahav* is seen as the incense-counterpart of the *mizbeach ha'olah*, the morning is primary. If it is seen as part of the inner *heichal* complex, the afternoon is primary. Rabbi Shimon's broader permissiveness toward the afternoon-only *ketoret* — and his insistence that the full measure be offered — may itself reflect his implicit alignment of the *mizbeach hazahav* with the *heichal* complex rather than with the outer altar, making the afternoon *ketoret* not merely acceptable but foundational.
