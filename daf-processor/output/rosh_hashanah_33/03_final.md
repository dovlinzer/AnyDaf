# Rosh Hashanah 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Malchuyot/Zichronot/Shofros

### Melech Mentions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַלְכִיּוֹת, כְּגוֹן: ״חַי אָנִי נְאֻם ה׳ [אֱלֹהִים] אִם לֹא בְּיָד חֲזָקָה וּבִזְרוֹעַ נְטוּיָה וּבְחֵמָה שְׁפוּכָה אֶמְלוֹךְ עֲלֵיכֶם״, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כֹּל כִּי הַאי רִיתְחָא לִירְתַּח קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא עֲלָן וְלִיפְרוֹקִינַן, כֵּיוָן דִּבְרִיתְחָא אֲמוּר — אַדְכּוֹרֵי רִיתְחָא בְּרֵישׁ שַׁתָּא לָא מַדְכְּרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara cites examples of verses that may not be used in Rosh HaShana prayers because they deal with punishment. With regard to verses of **Kingship, for example: "As I live, says the Lord God, surely with a mighty hand, and with an outstretched arm, and with fury poured out, will I be King over you"** (Ezekiel 20:33). **And although Rav Naḥman said** about this verse: With regard to **any anger like this, let the Holy One, blessed be He, express** that **anger upon us and let Him redeem us,** if that is the process necessary for redemption, **since** the verse **was said with anger** it is not included, as **one does not mention anger on Rosh HaShana.**

The *daf* begins in the middle of *masechet* Rosh Hashanah 32b, continuing the discussion of the *psukim* of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*. The *Gemara* had been examining which verses qualify for each of the three liturgical sections of the *musaf* prayer — a matter that turns, in part, on a dispute between Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Yehuda.

### Psalm 24 Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׂאוּ שְׁעָרִים רָאשֵׁיכֶם וְהִנָּשְׂאוּ פִּתְחֵי עוֹלָם וְיָבוֹא מֶלֶךְ הַכָּבוֹד. מִי זֶה מֶלֶךְ הַכָּבוֹד ה׳ עִזּוּז וְגִבּוֹר ה׳ גִּבּוֹר מִלְחָמָה. שְׂאוּ שְׁעָרִים רָאשֵׁיכֶם וּשְׂאוּ פִּתְחֵי עוֹלָם וְיָבֹא מֶלֶךְ הַכָּבוֹד. מִי הוּא זֶה מֶלֶךְ הַכָּבוֹד ה׳ צְבָאוֹת הוּא מֶלֶךְ הַכָּבוֹד סֶלָה״. רִאשׁוֹנָה — שְׁתַּיִם, שְׁנִיָּה — שָׁלֹשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses several verses from Psalms. **"Lift up your heads, O you gates, and be lifted up, you everlasting doors, that the King of glory may come in. Who is the King of glory? The Lord strong and mighty, the Lord mighty in battle"** (Psalms 24:7–8). The psalm continues: **"Lift up your heads, O you gates, and lift them up, you everlasting doors; that the King of glory may come in. Who then is the King of glory? The Lord of hosts, He is the King of glory, Selah"** (Psalms 24:9–10). **The first** section is counted as **two** verses of Kingship, as the term king is mentioned twice, while **the second** section **is** counted as **three** verses of Kingship; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: רִאשׁוֹנָה — אַחַת, שְׁנִיָּה — שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: The first** section is counted as only **one** verse of Kingship, as the question: "Who is the King of glory," is not considered a verse of Kingship. By the same reasoning, **the second** section is counted as only **two** verses of Kingship.

The *Gemara* turns to Psalm 24, which contains the phrase *Melech Hakavod* in multiple iterations. Reading through the *perek*: *se'u she'arim rasheichem vehinas'u pitchei olam veyavo Melech Hakavod* — one mention of *Melech*. *Mi zeh Melech Hakavod Hashem izuz vegibor* — a second mention. *Hashem gibor milchama se'u she'arim rasheichem use'u pitchei olam veyavo Melech Hakavod* — a third. *Mi hu zeh Melech Hakavod* — a fourth. *Hashem tzeva'ot hu Melech Hakavod selah* — a fifth. Since only three verses from *Ktuvim* are required, Psalm 24 alone could furnish more than enough.

The *Gemara* records: *rishona shtayim shniya shalosh divrei Rabbi Yose* — Rabbi Yose holds that the first *pasuk* of the *perek* yields two counts of *Melech*, and the second *pasuk* yields three. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *rishona achas shniya shtayim* — the first *pasuk* counts for only one mention, and the second for only two. The reason, explains *Rashi*, is that the rhetorical question *mi zeh Melech Hakavod* does not yet identify God as *Melech* — it is a question, not an affirmation, and Rabbi Yehuda declines to count it. Rabbi Yose treats the question as rhetorical and counts it nonetheless.

What is striking about this dispute is the underlying principle it reveals: the count of *malchuyos* is not simply a count of *psukim* but of affirmative mentions of *Melech* within *psukim*. A single *pasuk* containing multiple qualifying mentions can count more than once.

### Universal Kingship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זַמְּרוּ אֱלֹהִים זַמֵּרוּ זַמְּרוּ לְמַלְכֵּנוּ זַמֵּרוּ. כִּי מֶלֶךְ כׇּל הָאָרֶץ אֱלֹהִים״ — שְׁתַּיִם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַחַת. וְשָׁוִין בְּ״מָלַךְ אֱלֹהִים עַל גּוֹיִם אֱלֹהִים יָשַׁב עַל כִּסֵּא קׇדְשׁוֹ״, שֶׁהִיא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the Gemara discusses the following verses: **"Sing praises to God, sing praises, sing praises to our King, sing praises. For God is the King of all the earth;** sing praises in a skillful song" (Psalms 47:7–8). These are counted as **two** verses of Kingship; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Yehuda says:** They count as only **one** verse of Kingship, as the phrase: "Sing praises to our King," is referring to God as the King of the Jewish people, not the King of the entire world. **And they** both **agree** with regard to the verse: **"God reigns over the nations, God sits upon His sacred throne"** (Psalms 47:9), **that it is** considered as only **one** verse of Kingship, as the phrase: "Sits upon His sacred throne," is not referring to God explicitly as King.

A related case makes this even sharper. The *Gemara* cites *zimru Elohim zamru zamru le'malkeinu zamru ki melech kol ha'aretz elohim* — this verse contains both *malkeinu* and *melech kol ha'aretz*. Rabbi Yose counts this as two, since two mentions of kingship appear. Rabbi Yehuda counts it as only one. *Rashi* explains that *malkeinu* — "our King" — refers to God only as King of Israel, and Rabbi Yehuda requires that a *malchuyot* verse express universal kingship, *melech al kol ha'aretz*. The verse *meloch elohim al goyim elohim yashav al kisei kodsho* counts as only one for both opinions: it speaks of God reigning over the nations, satisfying the universality requirement, but "sitting on the throne" is not the explicit language of *Melech* and therefore contributes only a single count.

### Double-Purpose Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִכְרוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ תְּרוּעָה — כְּגוֹן: ״שַׁבָּתוֹן זִכְרוֹן תְּרוּעָה מִקְרָא קֹדֶשׁ״ — אוֹמְרָהּ עִם הַזִּכְרוֹנוֹת וְאוֹמְרָהּ עִם הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ אוֹמְרָהּ אֶלָּא עִם הַזִּכְרוֹנוֹת בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a verse of **remembrance that** also **has** a mention of **sounding** the *shofar*, **for example: "A solemn rest, a memorial of blasts, a sacred convocation"** (Leviticus 23:24), **one may recite it with the** verses of **remembrances, and** one **may** also **recite it with the** verses of ***shofarot*;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Yehuda says: One may recite it only with the** verses of **remembrances alone,** as it does not explicitly mention a *shofar*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַלְכוּת שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמּוֹ תְּרוּעָה — כְּגוֹן: ״ה׳ אֱלֹהָיו עִמּוֹ וּתְרוּעַת מֶלֶךְ בּוֹ״ — אוֹמְרָהּ עִם הַמַּלְכִיּוֹת וְאוֹמְרָהּ עִם הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ אוֹמְרָהּ אֶלָּא עִם הַמַּלְכִיּוֹת בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a verse of **Kingship that** also **has** a mention of **sounding** the *shofar*, **for example: "The Lord his God is with him, and the sounding of a king is among them"** (Numbers 23:21), **one may recite it with** the verses of **Kingship and** one may also **recite it with** the verses of ***shofarot*;** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Yehuda says: One may recite it only with** the verses **of Kingship.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרוּעָה שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהּ לֹא כְּלוּם — כְּגוֹן: ״יוֹם תְּרוּעָה יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״ — אוֹמְרָהּ עִם הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ אוֹמְרָהּ כׇּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a verse that mentions **sounding** the *shofar* **that has nothing** else **with it,** i.e., no mention of remembrances, Kingship, or an actual *shofar*, **for example: "It is a day of sounding the** *shofar* **to you"** (Numbers 29:1), **one may recite it with** the verses of ***shofarot*;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Yehuda says: One may not recite it at all,** as it contains no explicit mention of a *shofar*.

The *Gemara* then addresses the possibility of using a single *pasuk* to serve double duty. A verse containing both *zichron* and *teruah* — such as *shabbaton zichron teruah mikra kodesh* — may, according to Rabbi Yose, be recited once as part of *zichronos* and again as part of *shofros*: *omrin im hazichronos v'omrin im hashofros divrei Rabbi Yose*. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *eino omer ela im hazichronos bilvad* — you may say it only in the *zichronos* section. The same dispute applies to a verse containing both *malchut* and *teruah*, such as *Hashem Elokav imo uteruas melech bo*: Rabbi Yose permits it for both *malchuyos* and *shofros*; Rabbi Yehuda limits it to *malchuyos* alone.

### Teruah vs Shofar

The reason for Rabbi Yehuda's position becomes explicit in the third case. The verse *yom teruah yiheyeh lachem* — the very *pasuk* from which the observance of Rosh Hashanah as a day of *teruah* is derived — cannot count for the *shofros* section, says Rabbi Yehuda: *eino omer kol ikar*. The word *teruah* does not specify *shofar*, and Rabbi Yehuda requires explicit mention of *shofar* for a verse to qualify for the *shofros* section. *Rashi* supports this reasoning by noting that *teruah* also appears in connection with *chatzotzros* — trumpets — and is therefore not intrinsically tied to *shofar*.

This raises a genuine question. The *Torah* itself characterizes Rosh Hashanah as a *yom teruah* and *zichron teruah*, and yet *Chazal* designated the third *bracha* of *musaf* as *shofros*, not *teruos*. One might suggest that *Chazal* chose to emphasize *shofar davka* in order to underscore that the central instrument is the *shofar*, and not the *teruah* in its broader sense. This also opens the *shofros* section to a wider range of *psukim* — including those from *Har Sinai* — that could not otherwise have been included. There is a further dimension here noted by the *Ramban* from a Kabbalistic perspective: *tekiah* is associated with *midas harachamim* while *teruah* is associated with *midas hadin* — the staccato, fragmented quality of *teruah* evoking something more like crying out or being struck. The *Torah* itself seems to confirm this distinction: *v'chi tavo'u milchamah b'artzechem vaharei'otem bachatzotzros* — in wartime, you sound *teruah* — while *uvyom simchaschem utkatem bachatzotzros* — on days of rejoicing, you sound *tekiah*. Perhaps, then, the third *bracha* was deliberately framed as *shofros* rather than *teruos* precisely to avoid overemphasizing that more severe, *din*-laden resonance, while also allowing the Sinaitic imagery of *shofar* to enrich the section.

## Torah Brackets

### End with Navi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְחִיל בַּתּוֹרָה וּמַשְׁלִים בְּנָבִיא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אִם הִשְׁלִים בַּתּוֹרָה — יָצָא. ״אִם הִשְׁלִים״, דִּיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִילָּה — לָא. וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הַמַּשְׁלִים בַּתּוֹרָה — הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח! אֵימָא: ״מַשְׁלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: When reciting the ten verses, **one begins with** verses from **the Torah and concludes with** verses from **the Prophets. Rabbi Yosei says: If he concluded with** a verse from **the Torah, he has fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yosei's formulation: **If he concluded,** indicates that **after the fact, yes,** he has fulfilled his obligation; ***ab initio*, no,** he has not fulfilled his obligation to recite the necessary verses. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says: One who concludes** the series of verses **with** a verse from **the Torah is praiseworthy?** The Gemara answers: **Say** that the text of the mishna must be modified so that it reads: Rabbi Yosei says: **He concludes** with a verse from the Torah, i.e., one should do so *ab initio*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״אִם הִשְׁלִים״ קָתָנֵי, דִּיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִילָּה — לָא! הָכִי קָאָמַר: מַתְחִיל בַּתּוֹרָה וּמַשְׁלִים בְּנָבִיא. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מַשְׁלִים בַּתּוֹרָה, וְאִם הִשְׁלִים בְּנָבִיא — יָצָא. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: ווֹתִיקִין הָיוּ מַשְׁלִימִים אוֹתָהּ בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty. **Doesn't** the mishna **teach: If he concluded?** This indicates that **after the fact, yes,** one has fulfilled his obligation; ***ab initio*, no,** he has not fulfilled his obligation. The Gemara answers that **this is what** the mishna **is saying: One begins with** verses from **the Torah and concludes with** a single verse from **the Prophets. Rabbi Yosei says: One concludes with** a single verse from **the Torah, and if he concluded with** a single verse from **the Prophets he has fulfilled** his obligation. **This is also taught** in a *baraita*. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: Pious individuals [*vatikin*],** who were scrupulous in their performance of mitzvot, **would conclude** the series **with** a single verse from **the Torah.** Presumably, Rabbi Elazar followed the opinion of his father, Rabbi Yosei.

The *Gemara* now addresses the requirement governing the structure of each of the three sections: *im hishlim matchil b'Torah umashlim b'Navi*. The *Gemara* immediately asks whether *im hishlim* implies that ending with *Torah* is only *bdi'avad* — after the fact — but *lechatchila* one would end with a verse from *Navi*.

### Torah Framing

The *Gemara* resolves the text through revision. The initial formulation is: you begin with *Torah* and end with *Navi*. Rabbi Yose says: you should end with *Torah*, and if you ended with *Navi*, you have fulfilled your obligation *bdi'avad*. In practice, the structure is: three *psukim* from *Torah*, three from *Nevi'im*, three from *Ktuvim*, and then a final *pasuk* from *Torah* to close the section. This is how we end each of the three sections — completing the ten *psukim* with a verse of *Torah*.

### Vatikin Practice

A *baraita* confirms this: Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Yose transmitted that the *vatikin* — the pious ancients, those most exacting in observance — would always conclude with a *pasuk* from *Torah*.

### Malchuyot Shortage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא זִכְרוֹנוֹת וְשׁוֹפָרוֹת אִיכָּא טוּבָא, אֶלָּא מַלְכִיּוֹת — תְּלָת הוּא דְּהָוְיָין: ״ה׳ אֱלֹהָיו עִמּוֹ וּתְרוּעַת מֶלֶךְ בּוֹ״, ״וַיְהִי בִּישׁוּרוּן מֶלֶךְ״, ״ה׳ יִמְלוֹךְ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד״, וַאֲנַן בָּעֵינַן עֶשֶׂר, וְלֵיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** it is possible to conclude **Remembrances and *Shofarot*** with a verse from the Torah, as **there are many** such verses. **However,** with regard to **Kingship, there are** only **three: "The Lord his God is with him, and the sounding of a king is among them"** (Numbers 23:21); **"And he was king in Jeshurun"** (Deuteronomy 33:5); **and: "The Lord shall reign for ever and ever"** (Exodus 15:18). **And we require ten** verses, **and** according to Rabbi Yosei **there are not** enough, as he maintains that one should recite four verses from the Torah, the first three and the concluding one.

This framework immediately creates a problem. For *zichronos* and *shofros*, there is no shortage of *psukim* from *Torah* — *ika tuvah*, there are plenty. But for *malchuyos*, only three *psukim* from *Torah* are readily available: *Hashem Elokav imo uteruas melech bo*, *vayehi biYeshurun melech*, and *Hashem yimloch l'olam va'ed*. If the structure demands three opening *Torah* verses plus a closing *Torah* verse, four *Torah* verses of *malchuyot* are required. And we need ten verses total — the *Gemara* asks: *v'anan be'inan asar v'leka*? Where is the fourth *Torah malchuyot* verse?

## Malchuyot/Zichronot/Shofros (continued)

### Fourth Malchuyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ ה׳ אֶחָד״ — מַלְכוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ מַלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: Come** and **hear** a solution from that which was taught in the *Tosefta* (2:11): The verse: **"Hear, O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is one"** (Deuteronomy 6:4), is a verse of **Kingship;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Yehuda says: It is not** a verse of **Kingship.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְיָדַעְתָּ הַיּוֹם וַהֲשֵׁבוֹתָ אֶל לְבָבֶךָ כִּי ה׳ הוּא הָאֱלֹהִים אֵין עוֹד״ — מַלְכוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ מַלְכוּת. ״אַתָּה הׇרְאֵתָ לָדַעַת כִּי ה׳ הוּא הָאֱלֹהִים אֵין עוֹד מִלְבַדּוֹ״ — מַלְכוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ מַלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **"Know this day, and lay it to your heart, that the Lord, He is God** in heaven above, and upon the earth beneath; **there is none else"** (Deuteronomy 4:39), is a verse of **Kingship;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is **not** a verse of **Kingship. "To you it was shown, that you might know that the Lord, He is God; there is none else beside Him"** (Deuteronomy 4:35), is a verse of **Kingship;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Yehuda says: It is not** a verse of **Kingship.** This shows that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei there are sufficient verses of Kingship in the Torah to recite three at the beginning and one at the end.

The *Gemara* answers with a *baraita* cited by Rav Huna: *ta shema* — Shema Yisrael.

### Shema as Malchuyot

The *baraita* proposes that *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* qualifies as a *malchut* verse. The reasoning is that the declaration of God's absolute singularity — one God over all — evokes the concept of kingship, and presumably universal kingship at that. *Divrei Rabbi Yose*. Rabbi Yehuda, characteristically more restrictive, does not accept this: *einah malchut*. The *Gemara* then offers two further candidates: *v'yadata hayom vahashevota el l'vavecha ki Hashem hu ha'Elokim ein od*, and *ata hareita ladaat ki Hashem hu ha'Elokim ein od milvado*. Again, Rabbi Yose accepts these; Rabbi Yehuda does not.

This raises a deeper conceptual question about *malchuyot* itself. Is the purpose of *malchuyot* to invoke God's absolute and exclusive dominion — such that verses affirming there is no other God would qualify? Or is *malchuyot* specifically about the imagery of kingship in its juridical, relational sense — a king who rules over his subjects and exercises power over them? If the latter, then verses like *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* may not fully evoke that dimension, even if they affirm monotheism in its most fundamental form. The question remains open, embedded in the disagreement between Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Yehuda.

### Musaf Prayer Verses

Turning to the actual text of the *musaf* prayer as it is recited today, we can trace how these *psukim* were incorporated. The *malchuyos* section opens with *Atah zachartanu* and proceeds through the *kedushas hayom*, incorporating as well the *korban musaf* verses beginning *uminchasam veniskeihem uvachodesh hashvii*. The *malchuyos psukim* themselves include *Aleinu leshabei'ach*, which introduces the theme of universal kingship, and concludes the *psukim* sequence with *kakatuv beTorasecha Hashem yimloch le'olam va'ed* — the verse on which all opinions agree — followed by *lo hibit aven beYaakov... Hashem Elokav imo utru'as melech bo*, then *vayehi biYeshurun melech behis'asef roshei am yachad shivtei Yisrael*. These are the three universally accepted *Torah* verses. The fourth, debated, verse — *ki Hashem hu ha'Elokim* — is also present in the *musaf* text, as part of the passage *veyadata hayom vehasheivosa*. Finally, *uvTorasecha kasuv leimor Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* appears as the closing *Torah* verse of the section.

The *shofros* section, similarly, opens by evoking *Har Sinai* — *Atah nigleisa ba'anan kevodecha* — and draws on Sinaitic verses: *vayehi kol shofar chazak me'od*, *vayehi kol hashofar holeich vechazeik me'od*, *vechol ha'am ro'im es hakolos ve'es halapidim ve'es kol hashofar*. What is striking is that the section concludes with the verse *uveyom simchaschem uvemoadeichem uveroshei chodsheichem uskatem bachatzotzros al olosaychem* — a verse that speaks explicitly of *chatzotzros*, not *shofar*. This is the same phenomenon, in mirror image, that we observed with the *malchuyos* section: in both cases, the final *pasuk* is not perfectly on-point, yet it was selected for inclusion. The *Gemara* does not highlight this, but it is clearly visible from the text of the prayer itself.

## No Hallel/Musaf Timing

### Shofar in Musaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעוֹבֵר לִפְנֵי הַתֵּיבָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, הַשֵּׁנִי מַתְקִיעַ. וּבִשְׁעַת הַהַלֵּל, הָרִאשׁוֹן מַקְרֵא אֶת הַהַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who is passing before the ark,** as prayer leader, **on the festival of Rosh HaShana,** it is **the second** prayer leader, i.e., the one who leads the additional prayer, who **sounds** the *shofar* on behalf of the congregation. **And** on a day **when the *hallel*** is recited, **the first** prayer leader, i.e., the one who leads the morning prayer, **recites the *hallel*** on behalf of the congregation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא שֵׁנִי מַתְקִיעַ — מִשּׁוּם דִּ״בְרוֹב עָם הַדְרַת מֶלֶךְ״, אִי הָכִי הַלֵּל נָמֵי — נֵימָא בַּשֵּׁנִי מִשּׁוּם דִּ״בְרוֹב עָם הַדְרַת מֶלֶךְ״!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is different** about the **second** prayer leader, that he **sounds** the *shofar* during the additional prayer? Is it **due to** the principle **that: "The splendor of the King is in the multitude of the people"** (Proverbs 14:28)? In other words, is the *shofar* sounded during the additional prayer because all of the congregants will have arrived by then? **If so,** with regard to ***hallel* too, let us say** that it should be read **by the second** prayer leader, **due to** the principle **that "The splendor of the King is in the multitude of the people."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מַאי שְׁנָא הַלֵּל דְּבָרִאשׁוֹן — מִשּׁוּם דִּזְרִיזִין מַקְדִּימִין לְמִצְוֹת, תְּקִיעָה נָמֵי נַעֲבֵיד בָּרִאשׁוֹן, מִשּׁוּם דִּזְרִיזִין מַקְדִּימִין לְמִצְוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is different** about ***hallel* that** it is recited **by the first** prayer leader? It is **due to** the principle **that the vigilant are early** in the performance of **mitzvot.** This is also difficult. With regard to the **sounding** of the *shofar*, **too, let us perform** it by means **of the first** prayer leader, **due to** the principle **that the vigilant are early** in the performance of **mitzvot.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּשְׁעַת הַשְּׁמָד שָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: They taught** the *halakha* that the *shofar* is sounded during the additional prayer **in a time of** religious **persecution.** The gentile authorities prohibited sounding the *shofar* and appointed guards during the morning to ensure that the Jews comply. Therefore, the Sages delayed the sounding of the *shofar* until after the guards had left. A similar decree was not imposed against the recitation of *hallel*, and therefore it was recited during the morning prayer, at the earliest possible time.

The *Gemara* cites the *Mishnah*: the *shliach tzibur* for the morning service leads *Shacharis*, but it is the second — the *ba'al musaf* — who sounds the *shofar*. The reason given is *mishum de'berov am hadrat melech* — in order to maximize the community's presence when the *shofar* is blown. The more people present, the greater the honor to God as King. The same logic would seem to require reciting *Hallel* during *musaf* as well, to ensure maximum attendance — but we do not do that. Conversely, the principle of *zrizim makdimim lemitzvot* — the zealous perform *mitzvos* at the earliest opportunity — would seem to argue for blowing *shofar* in *Shacharis*. The *Gemara* notes this tension directly: each principle seems to lead to a different conclusion for each practice.

### Historical Edict

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the matter: *be'shat gzerat hamalchut shanu* — this ruling was established at the time of a royal edict. *Rashi* explains that there was a decree forbidding *shofar* blowing, and so the community waited until the government's agents had departed — which happened to be during *musaf* — and then blew. Even after the decree was lifted, the practice was not reverted: *lo avidnan ke'meikara*, because of concern that dangerous conditions might return.

### Intrinsic Connection

*Tosafot* cites the *Yerushalmi*, which offers a more nuanced account of the historical edict and also a more intrinsic reason for the *musaf* timing. On the historical side, the *Yerushalmi* explains that the enemies of Israel misinterpreted the *shofar* as a call to war — *tru'at milchama* — and attacked the community. The solution was to wait until after the community had visibly gone through the full *tefillah* — *Kriyat Shema*, *davening*, *keriat haTorah* — at which point the authorities understood it was religious observance: *binimusei hinhu askin*, they are engaged in their own practices. The intrinsic reason the *Yerushalmi* offers is that the *teruah* verses on Rosh Hashanah appear in the context of the *mussafim* in the *Torah* — *yom teruah* and *zichron teruah* arise in the context of the communal *korbanot* of the day. If *musaf* represents the identity of the day — what makes Rosh Hashanah what it is — then the *tekios*, which are equally constitutive of that identity, properly belong there as well.

### Angels' Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָאָמַר ״בִּשְׁעַת הַלֵּל״, מִכְּלַל דִּבְרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לֵיכָּא הַלֵּל. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אָמְרוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה לְפָנֶיךָ בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וּבְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים? אָמַר לָהֶם: אֶפְשָׁר מֶלֶךְ יוֹשֵׁב עַל כִּסֵּא דִין, וְסִפְרֵי חַיִּים וְסִפְרֵי מֵתִים פְּתוּחִין לְפָנָיו — וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה?!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara comments: **From** the fact that the mishna **states: When *hallel*** is recited, one can conclude **by inference that on Rosh HaShana there is no** recitation of ***hallel*. What is the reason** that *hallel* is omitted on Rosh HaShana? **Rabbi Abbahu said: The ministering angels said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, for what** reason **don't the Jewish people recite songs** of praise, i.e., *hallel*, **before You on Rosh HaShana and on Yom Kippur? He said to them:** Is it **possible that** while **the King is sitting on the throne of judgment and the books of life and the books of death are open before Him, the Jewish people are reciting** joyous **songs** of praise? Rosh HaShana and Yom Kippur are somber days of judgment whose mood is incompatible with the recitation of *hallel*.

The *Mishnah* also teaches that the first *shliach tzibur* recites *Hallel* — not on Rosh Hashanah, but on days when *Hallel* is said, since the *ba'al Shacharis* leads directly into *Hallel* from *chazarat haShatz*. The structure of the *Mishnah* makes clear that *Hallel* is not said on Rosh Hashanah. The *Gemara* asks *mai ta'ama* — why not? — and records a remarkable passage: Rabbi Abbahu teaches that the angels said before God, *Ribono Shel Olam*, why do the people of Israel not sing *shirah* before You on Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur?

### Divine Judgment

God answers: *efshar melech yoshev al kise din v'sifrei chayim v'sifrei meitim ptuchim lefanav v'Yisrael omrim shirah?* — Is it possible that the King sits on the throne of judgment with the books of life and death open before Him, and Israel sings praises? The day is one of awe, not of *simcha*, and *Hallel* — as an expression of joyous praise — is therefore inappropriate.

### Fasting on RH

This passage raises broader questions about the character of Rosh Hashanah. How much does the *yom hadin* theme override the *yom tov* dimension? There is in fact a direct halachic consequence: whether one may fast on Rosh Hashanah. Some *poskim* permit it, reasoning that as a *yom hadin* the gravity of the day overrides the ordinary prohibition against fasting on *Yom Tov* — and the passage just cited, that *Hallel* is inappropriate because judgment hangs in the balance, would seem to support that view. Others prohibit it, citing a *midrash* that portrays Israel approaching Rosh Hashanah not in black garments and covered in ash, as one would expect of someone standing trial, but dressed in fine white clothing, confident that God will judge them favorably. The image of *kidnei maron* — the flock passing before the shepherd — and the simultaneous sense of awe and trust make the proper emotional posture of the day genuinely complex and contested.

## Shofar Restrictions

### Tchum Boundary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹפָר שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה אֵין מַעֲבִירִין עָלָיו אֶת הַתְּחוּם, וְאֵין מְפַקְּחִין עָלָיו אֶת הַגַּל. לֹא עוֹלִין בָּאִילָן, וְלֹא רוֹכְבִין עַל גַּבֵּי בְהֵמָה, וְלֹא שָׁטִין עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם, וְאֵין חוֹתְכִין אוֹתוֹ — בֵּין בְּדָבָר שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת, וּבֵין בְּדָבָר שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. אֲבָל אִם רָצָה לִיתֵּן לְתוֹכוֹ מַיִם אוֹ יַיִן — יִתֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the *shofar* of Rosh HaShana, one may not pass** the Shabbat **limit for it,** i.e., to go and hear it, **nor may one clear a pile** of rubble to uncover a buried *shofar*. **One may not climb a tree, nor may one ride on an animal, nor may one swim in water,** in order to find a *shofar* to sound.** And one may not cut** the *shofar* to prepare it for use, **neither with an object that is prohibited due to a rabbinic decree nor with an object that** may not be used **due to a prohibition** by Torah law. **However, if one wishes to place water or wine into** the *shofar* on Rosh HaShana so that it emits a clear sound, **he may place it,** as this does not constitute a prohibited labor.

The *Mishnah* now lists a series of restrictions governing the *shofar* on *Yom Tov* of Rosh Hashanah. The first: *ein ma'avirin alav et hat-chum* — one may not carry the *shofar* beyond the *tchum*, even for the purpose of enabling someone else to fulfill the *mitzvah*. This holds despite the fact that the *mitzvah* is *d'Oraisa*.

### Melacha Restrictions

Further: *v'ein mefaktin alav et hagal* — one may not move a pile of stones to uncover a buried *shofar*; *v'lo olim b'ilan* — one may not climb a tree; *v'lo rochvin al gabei behema* — one may not ride an animal; *v'lo shatin al penei hamayim* — one may not float across water. These prohibitions are recognizable from an earlier *Mishnah* in *Beitzah* that catalogues the classic list of *shevutim* — rabbinic prohibitions on *Yom Tov*. The *Gemara* explains that the *mitzvah* of *shofar* is a positive commandment (*asei*), while *Yom Tov* is governed by both a positive and a negative commandment. A positive commandment cannot override a combination of positive and negative, and therefore the biblical restrictions stand. As for the rabbinic *shevutim*: *hashta d'rabbanan amrat lo, d'Oraisa miba'aya* — once we have established that the rabbinic prohibitions are not overridden, the biblical certainly are not. The *Gemara* frames this as *zu v'ein tzarich lomar zu* — this ruling, and it goes without saying that one. Once the *mitzvah* of *shofar* cannot override *Yom Tov* at the biblical level, the rabbinic *shevutim* are equally protected.

### Cutting Shofar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין חוֹתְכִין אוֹתוֹ, בֵּין בְּדָבָר שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת וּבֵין בְּדָבָר שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת — מַגָּלָא. לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה — סַכִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: **One may not cut** the *shofar* if it needs to be prepared, **neither with an object that is prohibited due to a rabbinic decree nor with an object** that may not be used **due to** a Torah **prohibition.** The Gemara explains: An example of an object prohibited **due to a rabbinic decree** is **a sickle,** which is not ordinarily used for preparing a *shofar*; an example of an object that may not be used due to **a prohibition** by Torah law is **a knife.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת אָמְרַתְּ לָא — לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה מִיבַּעְיָא? זוֹ וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר זוֹ קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now** that **you have said** that to sound the *shofar* **one may not** perform an action that is prohibited **due to rabbinic law, is** it **necessary** to say that one may not perform an action that violates **a prohibition** by Torah law? The Gemara answers: The mishna **teaches** employing the style: **This, and it is unnecessary to say that.**

The *Mishnah* states: *v'ein chotchin oto bein bidavar shehu mishum shevut uvein bidavar shehu mishum lo ta'aseh* — one may not trim or shorten the *shofar*, whether the means of doing so is only a rabbinic violation or a biblical one. The *Gemara* identifies the rabbinic case as using a *magal* — a sickle, an unusual implement for this purpose, which constitutes a *shinui*, a deviation from the normal manner of performing a prohibited act, rendering it only rabbinically forbidden. The biblical case is using a regular *sakin* — a knife — which constitutes a normal act of *tikkun kli*, fixing a vessel, and is therefore a biblical prohibition. The same *zu v'ein tzarich lomar zu* logic applies.

### Cleaning Shofar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אִם רָצָה לִיתֵּן לְתוֹכוֹ מַיִם אוֹ יַיִן — יִתֵּן. מַיִם אוֹ יַיִן — אֵין, מֵי רַגְלַיִם — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues. **However, if one wishes to place water or wine into** the *shofar* on Rosh HaShana, so that it should emit a clear sound, **he may place it.** The Gemara infers: **Water or wine, yes,** one may insert these substances into a *shofar*. However, **urine,** whose acidity is good for the *shofar*, **no.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי — אַבָּא שָׁאוּל הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: מַיִם אוֹ יַיִן — מוּתָּר, כְּדֵי לְצַחְצְחוֹ. מֵי רַגְלַיִם — אָסוּר, מִפְּנֵי הַכָּבוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is** the *tanna* of **the mishna?** The Gemara answers: **It is Abba Shaul, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Abba Shaul says:** With regard to **water or wine,** one is **permitted** to pour these liquids into a *shofar* on Rosh HaShana **in order to make** its sound **clear.** However, with regard to **urine,** one is **prohibited** to do so **due to the respect** that must be shown to the *shofar*. Although urine is beneficial, it is disrespectful to place it in a *shofar*, which serves for a mitzva.

The *Mishnah* permits placing water or wine into the *shofar* to clean it: *aval im ratza litten letocho mayim o yayin, yitten*. Water and wine are permitted; urine is not. The *Gemara* attributes this *halacha* to Abba Shaul: *mayim o yayin mutar k'dei l'tzachzacho* — to clean and polish it — while *mei raglayim assur mipnei hakavod* — urine is forbidden because it is disrespectful to an object used for a *mitzvah*, the same principle that governs respectful handling of *ketores* and other sacred items.

## Women/Children

### Women Blowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַתִּינוֹקוֹת מִלִּתְקוֹעַ, אֲבָל מִתְעַסְּקִין עִמָּהֶם עַד שֶׁיִּלְמְדוּ. וְהַמִּתְעַסֵּק — לֹא יָצָא, וְהַשּׁוֹמֵעַ מִן הַמִּתְעַסֵּק — לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One** need **not prevent children from sounding** the *shofar* on Rosh HaShana, despite the fact that they are not obligated in mitzvot. **Rather, one occupies** himself **with them,** encouraging and instructing them, **until they learn** how to sound it properly. The mishna adds: **One who acts unawares** and sounds the *shofar* without any intention to perform the mitzva **has not fulfilled** his obligation. **And,** similarly, **one who hears** the *shofar* blasts **from one who acts unawares has not fulfilled** his obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַתִּינוֹקוֹת מִלִּתְקוֹעַ. הָא נָשִׁים — מְעַכְּבִין, וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין מְעַכְּבִין לֹא אֶת הַנָּשִׁים וְלֹא אֶת הַתִּינוֹקוֹת מִלִּתְקוֹעַ בְּיוֹם טוֹב! אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches: **One** need **not prevent children from sounding** the *shofar* on Rosh HaShana. The Gemara infers: If **women** wish to sound the *shofar*, **one** indeed **prevents** them from doing so. The Gemara asks: **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **one does not prevent women or children from sounding** the *shofar* **on a Festival?** The Gemara answers that **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult: This** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** while **that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״. בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל סוֹמְכִין, וְאֵין בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל סוֹמְכוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: נָשִׁים סוֹמְכוֹת — רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"Speak to the children of Israel…**and he shall place his hands upon the head of the burnt-offering" (Leviticus 1:2–4). The phrase "children of Israel" literally means sons of Israel, and this teaches that **the sons of Israel place** their hands upon offerings, **but the daughters of Israel do not place** their hands upon offerings; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say:** It is **optional** for **women** to **place** their hands on the head of an offering before it is slaughtered, although they are not obligated to do so. Apparently, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon, if a woman wishes to perform any mitzva that is not obligatory for her, she is permitted to do so. Here too, one does not prevent a woman from sounding the *shofar*.

The *Mishnah* states that children are not prevented from blowing the *shofar*. The *Gemara* infers that women, by contrast, are prevented — *hanashim m'akvin*. *Rashi* explains this on the basis that women are entirely exempt from positive time-bound commandments (*mitzvat asei shehazman grama hi*), and that for them to blow would raise the concern of *bal tosef* — adding to the commandment. Nearly all *rishonim* take issue with *Rashi* on this point. *Bal tosef* ordinarily refers to someone who is obligated and expands the scope of a *mitzvah* beyond its proper bounds — it is not obviously applicable to someone who is entirely exempt. Most *rishonim* prefer a different rationale: if the *mitzvah* is not incumbent upon a person, then the *shofar* might otherwise be considered *muktzeh* on *Yom Tov* and blowing it a form of prohibited music-making. On that analysis, blowing *shofar* without a *mitzvah* context generates incidental problems that would ordinarily make it inappropriate.

### Children Learning

As for children: not only does one not prevent them from blowing, but one actively engages with them — *aval mitaskin imahem ad sheyilmedu*. The *Gemara* will elaborate on what this active involvement entails and how it navigates the competing concerns of *chinuch*.

## Kavanah Issues

### Blower's Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמִּתְעַסֵּק לֹא יָצָא. הָא תּוֹקֵעַ לָשִׁיר — יָצָא. לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הַתּוֹקֵעַ לָשִׁיר — יָצָא. דִּלְמָא תּוֹקֵעַ לָשִׁיר — נָמֵי מִתְעַסֵּק קָרֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **One who acts unawares** while sounding the *shofar*, without any intention to produce a sound, **has not fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara infers: **One who sounds** a *shofar* **for music,** even if he has no intention to perform the mitzva, **has fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara asks: **Let us say** that the mishna **supports** the opinion of **Rava, as Rava said: One who sounds** a *shofar* **for music has fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara rejects this suggestion. There is no clear proof from here, as **perhaps one who sounds** a *shofar* **for music is also called** one who **acts unawares.** It is possible that the *tanna* of the mishna includes in this category anyone who sounds the *shofar* without a clear intention to fulfill the mitzva.

The *Mishnah* states: *vehamitasek lo yatza* — one who blows in an incidental, non-purposive manner has not fulfilled his obligation. This is the issue of *kavanah*: blowing the *shofar* requires intent to perform the *mitzvah*. Mere incidental production of the sounds — *misasek* — does not count.

### Listener's Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשּׁוֹמֵעַ מִן הַמִּתְעַסֵּק — לֹא יָצָא. אֲבָל הַשּׁוֹמֵעַ מִן הַמַּשְׁמִיעַ לְעַצְמוֹ, מַאי? יָצָא. לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: אִיכַּוַּון וּתְקַע לִי!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues. **And one who hears** the *shofar* blasts **from one who acts unawares has not fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara asks: **However, one who hears** the *shofar* blasts **from one who is sounding** the *shofar* **for himself,** without intention of sounding it for others, **what is** the *halakha*? The mishna apparently indicates that **he has fulfilled** his obligation. **Let us say that this is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Zeira, as Rabbi Zeira said to his attendant: Have the intention** to sound the *shofar* on my behalf **and sound** it **for me.** This statement indicates that one must have the intention to enable the one who hears it to fulfill his obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא מִתְעַסֵּק, תְּנָא סֵיפָא נָמֵי מִתְעַסֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument. **Perhaps** one can explain that **since** the **first clause** of the mishna **taught** the *halakha* with regard to **one who acts unawares, the latter clause also taught** the *halakha* with regard to **one who acts unawares.** If so, no inference may be drawn from here to the case of one who sounds the *shofar* for himself, with no intention of doing so for others.

Similarly: *vehashomea min hamitasek lo yatza* — one who hears the *shofar* from someone who was blowing without intent to perform the *mitzvah* has also not fulfilled the obligation. The chain of *kavanah* must be intact on both ends. Both rulings set the stage for the *Gemara*'s later analysis of precisely how much intent is required and what form it must take.

## Shofar Restrictions (continued)

### Positive Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? שׁוֹפָר אֲשֵׂה הוּא, וְיוֹם טוֹב אֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה — וְאֵין אֲשֵׂה דּוֹחֶה אֶת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה וַעֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: There is a principle that a positive mitzva overrides a negative mitzva. With this in mind, **what is the reason** that one may not perform a prohibited labor on Rosh HaShana to fulfill the positive mitzva of sounding the *shofar*? The Gemara answers: Sounding the ***shofar* is a positive mitzva, but** performing prohibited labor on **a Festival** violates both **the positive mitzva** to rest **and the prohibition** against performing prohibited labor, **and a positive mitzva does not override** both **a prohibition and a positive mitzva.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא עוֹלִין בָּאִילָן וְלֹא רוֹכְבִין עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה וְכוּ׳. הַשְׁתָּא דְּרַבָּנַן אָמְרַתְּ לָא, דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מִיבַּעְיָא?! זוֹ וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר זוֹ קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: One may not pass the Shabbat limit for it, i.e., to go and hear it, nor may one clear a pile of rubble to uncover a buried *shofar*. **One may not climb a tree, nor may one ride on an animal** to find a *shofar* to sound. The Gemara questions the order of these prohibitions: **Now** that **you have said** that to sound the *shofar* one **may not** perform an action that is prohibited **by rabbinic law,** i.e., passing the Shabbat limit or clearing a pile of rubble, **is** it **necessary** to say that one may not perform an action that could lead to an act prohibited **by Torah law,** i.e., climbing a tree or riding an animal? The Gemara answers: The mishna **teaches** employing the style: **This, and it is unnecessary to say that.** It begins with the more novel case before moving on to the more straightforward one.

The *Gemara* consolidates the underlying principle: *shofar asei hu ve-Yom Tov asei v'lo ta'aseh ve-ein asei docheh lo ta'aseh ve-asei* — the *mitzvah* of *shofar* is a positive commandment, while *Yom Tov* is governed by both a positive commandment and a negative commandment. The rule that a positive commandment overrides a negative does not apply when the negative is accompanied by a positive as well. Therefore, *shofar* cannot displace *Yom Tov* restrictions at the biblical level, and by extension the rabbinic *shevutim* are equally shielded.

It is worth noting that the *Gemara* does not pause to analyze whether individual *shevutim* might be overridden on other grounds — for instance, the position of the *Aruch* that a single rabbinic prohibition may be overridden for the sake of a *mitzvah* on *Shabbat* in certain circumstances. Once the *Gemara* has established that *shofar* does not override *Yom Tov* at the biblical level, it treats the *shevutim* as equally protected, without requiring independent justification for each.

## Women/Children (continued)

### Bal Tosif

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְעַכְּבִין לֹא אֶת הַנָּשִׁים וְלֹא אֶת הַתִּינוֹקוֹת מִלִּתְקוֹעַ בְּיוֹם טוֹב! אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara questions the apparent contradiction in the mishna. **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **one does not prevent women or children from sounding** the *shofar* **on a Festival?** The Gemara answers that **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult: This** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** while **that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon.**

Returning to the question of women, the *Gemara* cites a *baraita* that contradicts the *Mishnah*'s apparent prohibition: *ein m'akvin lo es hanashim v'lo es hatinokos miltkoah b'Yom Tov* — one does not prevent women *or* children from blowing on *Yom Tov*. Abaye resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — ha Rabbi Yehuda ha Rabbi Yosi v'Rabbi Shimon*. The *Mishnah*'s restrictive position reflects Rabbi Yehuda's view; the *baraita* reflects the view of Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon.

The underlying dispute concerns *smicha* — the laying of hands on a *korban*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that only *Bnei Yisrael*, males, perform *smicha*; women do not. Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon hold that women may perform *smicha* on a voluntary basis — *nashim somchot reshut*. They are not obligated, but they are permitted. This maps onto the broader question of women's relationship to positive time-bound commandments. Even granting the exemption, does that exemption also constitute exclusion? Or may women choose to perform such *mitzvos* on an optional basis?

One approach — appearing in the context of *chagigah* — is that even if there are incidental rabbinic concerns, we permit women to perform these *mitzvos* *kedei la'asot nachat ruach lenashim*, to give women a sense of inclusion. This is a meaningful statement about the value of inclusion, but it does not fully resolve the underlying *halachic* question about women's relationship to the *mitzvah* itself. A stronger approach holds that exemption is not the same as exclusion: a woman who performs a *mitzvas asei shehazman grama* is not engaged in a meaningless act — she is performing the *mitzvah*, albeit on an optional basis, and as *eino metzuveh ve'oseh* she still receives *schar*. On this view the incidental concerns about *muktzeh* or improper use of *kodshim* fall away, because the woman is actually performing a genuine act of *mitzvah*.

### Halacha Psak

According to the view of *Tosafot*, which rules in the name of Rabbeinu Tam, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yose: *af al gav desatama desifra ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — even though there is a *stam* ruling in the *Sifra* following Rabbi Yehuda — *halacha ke-Rabbi Yose*, because *denimuko imo* — Rabbi Yose's reasoning accompanies his ruling — and because of *ma'aseh rav*: Michal the daughter of Shaul wore *tefillin*, and the wife of Yonah made the *aliyah la'regel*. A further precedent involves women performing *smicha* in the *ezras nashim*. Women are therefore permitted to blow *shofar* if they choose to do so. This also overrides the secondary concerns about *muktzeh* and music-making.

### Women's Bracha

The remaining question, which occupies the bulk of *Tosafot*'s discussion, is whether women who perform a *mitzvas asei shehazman grama* make a *bracha*. This is the question that divides *Ashkenazi* and *Sefardi* practice. *Ashkenazim* rule that women do make a *bracha*, even on *mitzvos* from which they are exempt. *Sefardim*, following the *Rambam*, rule that women do not — since they were not commanded, they cannot say *vetzivanu*.

The *Ashkenazi* position rests on two possible foundations. One is that *vetzivanu* means *Klal Yisrael* — the Jewish people as a whole were commanded, and even though women individually were not, they may recite the *bracha* as members of that collective. The stronger approach is that the exemption is not equivalent to exclusion from the commandment altogether. On this reading, women were fundamentally addressed by the commandment but granted an exemption; if they choose to waive the exemption and perform the *mitzvah*, they re-enter the category of the commanded, and the *bracha* is appropriate. They cannot, however, be *motzi* men, since men retain a higher level of obligation that women cannot match.

The practical significance of the *bracha* question is considerable. When women do not make a *bracha*, the implicit message is that the act is not truly a *mitzvah* for them. When they do, the *bracha* powerfully affirms that they are performing a genuine act of religious obligation. This difference is observable in practice: in *Sefardi* communities, where the *Rambam*'s ruling prevails, women often feel considerably less connection to *mitzvos asei shehazman grama*, whereas the *Ashkenazi* practice of making a *bracha* communicates, in the most direct terms available, that the *mitzvah* is genuinely theirs. When it comes to *shofar* on Rosh Hashanah in particular, virtually all women feel deeply connected to the obligation, and many would be surprised to learn that they are technically exempt — which itself testifies to the power of the *bracha* in shaping that sense of *chiyuv*.

It is worth noting that certain *mitzvos* that are formally time-bound carry their own independent basis for women's obligation — *kiddush* is linked to *zachor* and *shamor*, and *matzah* is connected to the prohibition of *chametz* — and these are therefore distinct from the general category of *mitzvas asei shehazman grama*.

### Chinuch Age

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מִתְעַסְּקִין בָּהֶם עַד שֶׁיִּלְמְדוּ. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מִתְעַסְּקִין בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁיִּלְמְדוּ, אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת. וְאֵין מְעַכְּבִין הַתִּינוֹקוֹת מִלִּתְקוֹעַ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further states: **Rather, one occupies** himself **with them,** encouraging and instructing children, **until they learn** how to sound it properly. **Rabbi Elazar said:** This applies **even** when Rosh HaShana occurs **on Shabbat. This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **One occupies** himself **with** children **until they learn** to sound the *shofar* properly, **even on Shabbat. And one does not prevent the children from sounding** the *shofar* **on Shabbat, and needless to say** one does not prevent them **on** the **festival** of Rosh HaShana that occurs on a weekday.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא, אָמְרַתְּ: מִתְעַסְּקִין בָּהֶן עַד שֶׁיִּלְמְדוּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת — אַלְמָא לְכַתְּחִלָּה אָמְרִינַן תִּקְעוּ. וַהֲדַר תָּנָא: אֵין מְעַכְּבִין — עִכּוּבָא הוּא דְּלָא מְעַכְּבִין, הָא לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא אָמְרִינַן תִּקְעוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This matter itself is difficult,** i.e., there is an internal contradiction in the *baraita*. **You said** that **one occupies** himself **with** the children **until they learn** how to sound the *shofar*, **and** this applies **even on Shabbat. Apparently, we say to them *ab initio*: Sound** the *shofar*. **And then** the *baraita* **taught: One does not prevent** them from sounding the *shofar*, which indicates that although **one does not prevent them** from sounding it, **we do not say *ab initio*: Sound** it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּקָטָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְחִינּוּךְ, כָּאן בְּקָטָן שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ לְחִינּוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: This is **not difficult. Here,** in the first clause, the *baraita* is dealing **with a minor who has reached** the age of **training** in mitzvot. This child is taught to sound the *shofar*, as one is obligated to teach him the proper way to perform mitzvot. However, **here,** in the second clause, the *baraita* is dealing **with a minor who has not** yet **reached** the age of **training.** Although one need not prevent this child from sounding the *shofar*, one does not encourage him to do so.

The *Gemara* now turns to a contradiction within the *Mishnah* regarding children. The first clause says *mitaskin imahem ad sheyilmedu* — you actively engage with children to teach them. A *baraita* extends this even to *Shabbat*: *afilu b'Shabbat*. But the second clause says *ein me'akvin* — you merely do not interfere. The *Gemara* notes that *mitaskin* implies an active *lechatchila* encouragement, while *ein me'akvin* suggests only passive non-interference — not encouragement. *Lo kashya*: the two clauses address different cases.

### Shabbat Blowing

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between a child who has reached the age of *chinuch* and one who has not. There are two ways to read the distinction, leading to opposing conclusions.

On one reading: for a child below the age of *chinuch*, there are no *chinuch* concerns about *Shabbat* violation, so one may actively engage — even on *Shabbat*, even though no one else is performing a *mitzvah* today. For a child at the age of *chinuch*, one does not actively encourage because there is a *chinuch* obligation regarding *Shabbat* that must be respected, but one also does not interfere — particularly on Rosh Hashanah, when the *shofar* connection is itself valuable.

On the other reading: for a child below the age of *chinuch*, one does not interfere but does not actively involve; for a child at the age of *chinuch*, the *chinuch* obligation of Rosh Hashanah counterbalances the *chinuch* of *Shabbat*, and one may actively encourage blowing the *shofar*.

The *Shulchan Aruch* and the *Rama* do not adopt the *girsa* that includes *afilu b'Shabbat*; the standard *girsa* in practice reads *afilu b'Yom Tov*. The *Rama* rules clearly that for a child who has reached *chinuch*, and when it is *Yom Tov* rather than *Shabbat*, one actively involves the child — teaches, encourages, and blows the *shofar* alongside him. This is especially meaningful given that on Rosh Hashanah the child may not have reached the *beis haknesses* to hear the *tekios* himself. One may also make a *bracha* for the sake of *chinuch*, as was established in an earlier discussion.

This section also clarifies an important practical boundary for adults. The *Rama* rules that after one has fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *shofar*, blowing for mere enjoyment — without any connection to *mitzvah* or *chinuch* — is forbidden, because it constitutes making music on *Yom Tov*. The *Taz* disagrees, arguing that on Rosh Hashanah the *shofar* cannot be considered problematic in any context. The *Rama*'s ruling prevails: *lechatchila*, blowing the *shofar* for purely recreational purposes after the *mitzvah* has been fulfilled is not permitted. Blowing in the context of *chinuch* — even if the adult must demonstrate the sounds himself — remains entirely legitimate.

## Kavanah Issues (continued)

### Musical Intent

The *Gemara* engages with a fine point of *kavanah* arising from the *Mishnah*'s reference to *misasek*. The case of *hatokea lashir* — blowing the *shofar* for musical purposes — appears to suggest that the only disqualifying lack of intent is complete incidentality, *misasek* in its strictest sense. If a person deliberately produces the sounds of a *shofar* but for musical rather than *mitzvah* purposes, the implication might be that he *is* *yotzei*. The *Gemara* then cites Rava, who rules explicitly: *hatokea lashir yatza* — one who blows for music has fulfilled the obligation.

### Motzi Intent

The *Gemara* declines to confirm Rava's ruling from this passage, because *dilma tokea lashir nami misasek kari lei* — perhaps "blowing for music" is itself classified as *misasek*, and the *Mishnah* is simply saying that any blowing outside a *mitzvah* intent is disqualifying. The boundary of *misasek* is thus left uncertain.

A separate but related issue of *kavanah* emerges: does the blower need intent not only to perform the *mitzvah* himself, but specifically to *motzi* the listener? The *Mishnah* says that hearing from a *misasek* does not fulfill the obligation — implying that if the blower *did* intend to perform the *mitzvah* for himself, the listener would be *yotzei* even without the blower having intended to exempt him. This would contradict Rabbi Zeira, who instructed his attendant: *ikavein u-tkah li* — have intent specifically to blow *for me*, meaning intent to *motzi* the listener is required, not merely intent to perform the *mitzvah*.

The *Gemara* deflects: *dilma ayidi d'tani resha mitasek, tana seifa nami mitasek* — the *Mishnah* is structured around the theme of *misasek* throughout, and the *seifa* about the listener merely parallels the *reisha* about the blower. The *Mishnah* does not affirmatively rule that intent to *motzi* is unnecessary; it simply does not address that question. It remains possible that intent to exempt the listener is indeed required, as Rabbi Zeira holds.

## Sound Sequence

### Nine Sounds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ סֵדֶר תְּקִיעוֹת: שָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁל שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁלֹשׁ. שִׁיעוּר תְּקִיעָה כְּשָׁלֹשׁ תְּרוּעוֹת, שִׁיעוּר תְּרוּעָה כְּשָׁלֹשׁ יְבָבוֹת. תָּקַע בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה וּמָשַׁךְ בַּשְּׁנִיָּה כִּשְׁתַּיִם — אֵין בְּיָדוֹ אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The order of the blasts** is **three** sets **of three** blasts **each,** which are: *Tekia*, *terua*, and *tekia*. **The length of a *tekia*** is **equal to** the length of **three *teruot*,** and **the length of a *terua*** is **equal to** the length of **three whimpers.** If **one sounded the first** *tekia* of the initial series of *tekia*, *terua*, *tekia*, **and** then **extended the second** *tekia* of that series **to** the length of **two** *tekiot*, so that it should count as both the second *tekia* of the first set and the first *tekia* of the second set, **he has in his hand** the fulfillment of **only one** *tekia*, and he must begin the second set with a new *tekia*.

The *Mishnah* introduces the *seder tekios*: *shalosh shel shalosh shalosh* — three sets of three sounds each, yielding nine *kolot* in total. The biblical source for three *teruos* is that the word *teruah* appears three times in connection with the obligation: *yom teruah yiheyeh lachem*, *zichron teruah* (twice on Rosh Hashanah), and *v'ha'avarta shofar teruah* for *Yovel*. From other *psukim* it is derived that a *tekiah* precedes and follows each *teruah*, producing the structure of *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* three times — nine sounds altogether.

### Tekiah Timing

*Shiur tekiah k'shalosh teruos* — the duration of a *tekiah* equals the duration of three *teruos*. This establishes a proportional relationship: the *tekiah* is the full, sustained sound, and the *teruah* is a fragmented version of it. One standard reading is that a *teruah* — the set of broken sounds — equals one third the length of a *tekiah*, and three *teruos* together equal one *tekiah* in length. Understood this way, a *teruah* is essentially a *tekiah* that has been broken into fragments: in quantity and quality, the *teruah* is a shattered *tekiah*. The *Ramban* raises the possibility that the two sounds differ in quality as well as duration — that the *tekiah* and *teruah* are intrinsically different types of sound, one straight and one undulating — but the standard position is that the only distinction is one of length.

### Teruah Beats

*Shiur teruah k'shalosh yevavot* — the minimum duration of a *teruah* is the length of three beats, where a *yevavah* is the basic unit of sound. Thus each individual sound within the *teruah* set must last at least three beats.

## Sound Sequence (continued)

### Distinct Sounds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָתַנְיָא: שִׁיעוּר תְּקִיעָה כִּתְרוּעָה! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תַּנָּא דִּידַן קָא חָשֵׁיב תְּקִיעָה דְּכוּלְּהוּ בָּבֵי וּתְרוּעוֹת דְּכוּלְּהוּ בָּבֵי. תַּנָּא בָּרָא קָא חָשֵׁיב חַד בָּבָא וְתוּ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara raises a difficulty. Although the mishna taught that the length of a *tekia* is equal to the length of three *teruot*, **isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **the length of a** single ***tekia* is equal to** the length of an entire ***terua*,** which is comprised of several shorter sounds? **Abaye said:** There is no difficulty. **The *tanna*** of **our** mishna **counts the *tekia* of all the sets** of blasts **and the *teruot* of all the sets.** He means that the length of the three *tekiot* is equal to the length of the three *teruot*. Conversely, **the *tanna*** of **the *baraita* counts** the first *tekia* of only **one set, and no more,** and therefore he simply states that the length of one *tekia* is equal to the length of one *terua*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁיעוּר תְּרוּעָה כִּשְׁלֹשׁ יְבָבוֹת. וְהָתַנְיָא: שִׁיעוּר תְּרוּעָה כִּשְׁלֹשָׁה שְׁבָרִים!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues. **The length of a *terua*** is **equal to** the length of **three whimpers.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **the length of a *terua*** is equal **to** the length of **three *shevarim*,** i.e., broken blasts, which presumably are longer than whimpers?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּהָא וַדַּאי פְּלִיגִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״יוֹם תְּרוּעָה יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״, וּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן: ״יוֹם יַבָּבָא יְהֵא לְכוֹן״. וּכְתִיב בְּאִימֵּיהּ דְּסִיסְרָא: ״בְּעַד הַחַלּוֹן נִשְׁקְפָה וַתְּיַבֵּב אֵם סִיסְרָא״. מָר סָבַר גַּנּוֹחֵי גַּנַּח. וּמָר סָבַר יַלּוֹלֵי יַלֵּיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: In this** matter, the *tanna'im* **certainly disagree.** Although the first *baraita* can be reconciled with the mishna, this second *baraita* clearly reflects a dispute. **As it is written: "It is a day of sounding [*terua*]** the *shofar* **to you"** (Numbers 29:1), **and we translate** this verse in Aramaic as: **It is a day of *yevava* to you. And** to define a *yevava*, the Gemara quotes a verse that **is written about the mother of Sisera: "Through the window she looked forth and wailed [*vateyabev*], the mother of Sisera"** (Judges 5:28). One **Sage,** the *tanna* of the *baraita*, **holds** that this means **moanings,** broken sighs, as in the blasts called *shevarim*. **And** one **Sage,** the *tanna* of the mishna, **holds** that it means **whimpers,** as in the short blasts called *teruot*.

The *Gemara* addresses the case of *takah b'rishonah u-mashach b'shniyah k'shtayim* — if a person blew the first *tekiah* and then extended the second *tekiah* to double length, intending for it to count as both the concluding *tekiah* of one set and the opening *tekiah* of the next. The ruling is *ein b'yado ela achat* — it counts as only one. Each sound must be a distinct, discrete unit; one cannot stretch a single *tekiah* to serve as two.

### Teruah Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁבְּשׁוֹפָר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַעֲבַרְתָּ שׁוֹפַר תְּרוּעָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that** the soundings of Rosh HaShana must be performed **with a *shofar*? The verse states: "Then you shall make proclamation with the blast of the *shofar*** on the tenth day of the seventh month; on the Day of Atonement you shall make proclamation with the *shofar* throughout all your land" (Leviticus 25:9).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹבֵל, בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּחֹדֶשׁ הַשְּׁבִיעִי״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּחֹדֶשׁ הַשְּׁבִיעִי״, וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּחֹדֶשׁ הַשְּׁבִיעִי״ — שֶׁיִּהְיוּ כׇּל תְּרוּעוֹת שֶׁל חֹדֶשׁ שְׁבִיעִי זֶה כָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** From this **I have** derived the *halakha* **only** with regard to Yom Kippur **of the Jubilee Year. From where** do I derive that the soundings of **Rosh HaShana** must also be with a *shofar*? **The verse states: "Of the seventh month." Since** there is **no** need for **the verse to state: "Of the seventh month,"** as it already states: "On the Day of Atonement," **what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Of the seventh month"?** This comes to teach **that all** the obligatory **soundings of the seventh month must be similar to one another.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁפְּשׁוּטָה לְפָנֶיהָ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַעֲבַרְתָּ שׁוֹפַר תְּרוּעָה״. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁפְּשׁוּטָה לְאַחֲרֶיהָ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תַּעֲבִירוּ שׁוֹפָר״.
>
> **Translation:** This verse states: "The blast [*terua*] of the *shofar*," indicating that one must sound a *terua*. The Gemara asks: **And from where** is it derived **that** the *terua* sound **is preceded by a straight blast,** i.e., a *tekia*? **The verse states: "Then you shall make proclamation with the blast of the *shofar* [*shofar terua*]"** (Leviticus 25:9), indicating that the *terua* must be preceded by the basic sound of a *shofar*, i.e., by the straight blast of a *tekia*. **And from where** is it derived **that** the *terua* sound **is followed by a straight blast? The** same **verse states** again: **"You shall make proclamation with the *shofar*,"** which indicates that there must be another *tekia* after the *terua*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹבֵל, בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּחֹדֶשׁ הַשְּׁבִיעִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues. From this **I have** derived the *halakha* **only** that these *tekia* blasts before and after the *terua* must be sounded on Yom Kippur **of the Jubilee Year. From where** do I derive that they must be sounded **on Rosh HaShana** as well? **The verse states: "Of the seventh month."**

The *Gemara* presents a dispute about the nature of the *teruah* sound itself. The word *yom teruah* is rendered in the Aramaic *targum* as *yom yevava* — a day of wailing, derived from *vateyabev em Sisra* — the mother of Sisera wailing at the window. The question is what form that wailing takes: *mar savar genuchi ganach* — one view holds it is a moan, a sustained but broken sound, corresponding to what we call *shevarim*; *umar savar yeluli yalil* — the other view holds it is a high-pitched wail, a staccato cry, corresponding to what we call *teruah* in its technical sense. This is the source of the fundamental *machloket* that underlies our practice of sounding *shevarim*, *teruah*, and the combination *shevarim-teruah*: because it was uncertain which form of *teruah* the *Torah* intended, we perform multiple variants to satisfy both opinions.

## Missed Shofar

### Late Shofar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁבֵּירַךְ וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְמַנָּה לוֹ שׁוֹפָר — תּוֹקֵעַ וּמֵרִיעַ וְתוֹקֵעַ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who recited the blessings** of the additional prayer, **and** only **afterward a *shofar* became available to him, he sounds** a *tekia*, **sounds** a *terua*, **and sounds** a *tekia*, an order he repeats **three times.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁשְּׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר חַיָּיב, כָּךְ כׇּל יָחִיד וְיָחִיד חַיָּיב. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: שְׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר מוֹצִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים יְדֵי חוֹבָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as the prayer leader is obligated** in the prayer of Rosh HaShana, **so too, each and every individual is obligated** in these prayers. **Rabban Gamliel** disagrees and **says:** Individuals are not obligated, as **the prayer leader fulfills the obligation on behalf of the many.**

The *Mishnah* addresses the case of *mi she'berach v'achar kach nizdamen lo shofar* — a person who recited the *brachot* of *mussaf*, including the sections of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*, without having a *shofar* available, and who subsequently obtained one. In the context of the *Mishnah*, the relevant *brachot* are those of *Shmoneh Esrei* itself — not the later-developed *birkat hamitzvah* of *lishmoa kol shofar*, which arose once the *shofar* blowing was separated from the *Shmoneh Esrei* and the standalone *brachot* were required. In the original framework, *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* within *musaf* constituted the *halachic* envelope of the *shofar* obligation.

### Separate Fulfillment

The *Mishnah* rules: *tokeia u-meiri u-tokeia shalosh pe'amim* — one blows *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* three times, even without the surrounding *brachot* of *Shmoneh Esrei*. This is a significant *chiddush* in both directions: *bdi'avad*, one may recite *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* without *shofar*, and one may blow *shofar* without the enveloping *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*. Although these elements are conceptually intertwined — the *shofar* blowing occurs within and because of those *brachot* — they are not mutually indispensable.

### Individual Obligation

*K'shem she'shliach tzibur chayav kach kol yachid v'yachid chayav* — just as the *shliach tzibur* is obligated, so is every individual. This statement addresses a potential misunderstanding: since the *shliach tzibur* is the one who blows the *shofar* in the communal setting, one might think that the obligation is communal rather than personal. The *Mishnah* clarifies that the *mitzvah* belongs to each individual. In the context of *musaf*, this also raised a practical challenge: the *psukim* of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* were not widely memorized, and without *siddurim*, ordinary people had no way to recite them independently.

### Shliach Motzi

Rabban Gamliel addresses this directly: *shliach tzibur motzi et harabim yedei chovatan* — the *shliach tzibur* can exempt the community. Here Rabban Gamliel is speaking about the *Shmoneh Esrei* of *musaf* with its *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* — not about the *shofar* blowing itself. The practical issue is clear: how can people fulfill the obligation of reciting these *psukim* in *Shmoneh Esrei* if they do not know them? Rabban Gamliel's ruling — that the *shliach tzibur* can be *motzi* the community — resolves this, and the fuller implications of that ruling will be developed in the discussion that follows.
