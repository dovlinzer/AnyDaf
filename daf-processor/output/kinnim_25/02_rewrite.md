# Kinnim 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mixed Vow & Obligation

### Woman's Forgotten Vow

The final case of *Kinnim* involves a woman who took a *neder* to bring a bird offering together with her obligatory *korban yoledes*. She specified that she would bring the *neder* of a particular type — either *tor* or *bnei yonah* — but subsequently forgot which she had specified. Because she cannot recall what she vowed, she cannot verify that her obligation was discharged, and must therefore repeat the entire sequence. It should be noted that the *rishonim* debate at considerable length how to read this case, since a number of assumptions must be built into the analysis to make it cohere.

### 'Together' Requirement

The operative word in her vow is "together." As this case is understood, the togetherness requirement means that all of her *olos* must be brought simultaneously: the two *olos* of her *neder* and the *olah* of her *chovah* — the obligatory *olah* that accompanies the *korban yoledes*. Since she cannot establish what she vowed and cannot confirm what was previously performed, she must bring everything again. Because her obligatory *olah* was already offered on a prior day and she cannot retroactively join her *neder* offering to it, the only way she can now satisfy the togetherness condition is to bring an additional bird as an *olah*. This extra *olah* need not be of a specific species — she may choose whichever type she wishes.

### Single Species: Five

When everything belongs to a single species — when all the birds involved were of the same *min*, whether all *tor* or all *bnei yonah*, even though she does not remember which — the total comes to five. She must bring two *olos* of *tor* and two *olos* of *bnei yonah* on account of the *neder* she cannot identify, plus one additional *olah* to satisfy the togetherness condition with respect to her obligatory offering. That yields four plus one: five birds in all.

## Mixed Species: Six

### Chatas Determines Olah

When the offering she brought previously involved different species — that is, when she does not know what her *chatas* was — the count rises to six. The reason lies in the *halacha* that the *min* of the *chatas* determines the *min* of the matching *olah*: if the *chatas* is a *tor*, the *olah* must likewise be a *tor*; if the *chatas* is *bnei yonah*, the *olah* must be *bnei yonah*. Since the *chatas* determines which *olah* is valid, uncertainty about the *chatas* propagates directly into uncertainty about the obligatory *olah*.

### Two Olos per Species

Because she does not know what species her *chatas* was — whether it was a *tor* or *bnei yonah* — she cannot simply bring a single additional *olah* to discharge the togetherness obligation. She must bring one *olah* of *tor* and one of *bnei yonah*, ensuring that whichever species the prior *chatas* turns out to have been, the matching *olah* has been covered. Combined with the four *olos* already required for the unresolved *neder*, this produces a total of six.

## Priest's Uncertainty

### All Above Scenario

The next case introduces a further complication. The *mishna* states: she gave the birds above — to the *kohen* — and it is not known what she gave, and the *kohen* went and performed, and it is not known what he performed. On the surface this resembles everything discussed until now. The critical difference, however — which the text does not spell out explicitly — is that in this scenario the *kohen* may have performed *all* of the offerings above. In prior cases, we assumed the *kohen* performed some above and some below, simply without remembering which went where. Here, the uncertainty extends to whether anything at all was done below.

### Four + Two + One

Given this expanded uncertainty, the *mishna* requires an additional bird: four for her *neder* — two *tor* and two *bnei yonah* as before — two for her obligatory *olah*, covering both species to match whatever the *chatas* may have been, and now one *chatas* as well. That totals seven.

### New Chatas Needed

The reason a *chatas* must now be added is that since the *kohen* may have performed everything above, it is possible that no *chatas* was offered at all. She must therefore bring a *chatas* to cover that possibility. At the same time, the two *olos* remain necessary: if a *chatas* was in fact offered below on the prior day, its species would determine what the *olah* must be, and since she does not know which species that *chatas* was, she must bring an *olah* of each species. She must thus provide both the *chatas* in case nothing was offered below, and the two *olos* in case something was. Seven birds in total.

## Ben Azzai: Eight Birds

### First Determines Second

*Ben Azzai* disagrees and requires eight birds. His position, stated earlier in the tractate, is that it is not the *chatas* that categorically determines the *min* of the *olah*. Rather, whichever offering was performed *first* determines the species of the second. This principle cuts in both directions.

### Olos & Chatasim

If the *chatas* was performed first, then the *olah* must match it, and since the species of that prior *chatas* is unknown, two *olos* — one of each species — are required. But it is equally possible that the *olah* was performed first. In that case, the *chatas* must match the *olah*, and since it is not known which species the *olah* was, two *chatasim* are required. The scenario in which everything may have been done above means that both possibilities must be covered simultaneously: two *olos* to account for the case where the *chatas* came first, and two *chatasim* to account for the case where the *olah* came first. Combined with the four *olos* required for the unresolved *neder*, *Ben Azzai*'s accounting reaches eight birds. With that, all the permutations of this final case are exhausted.

## Aggadic Riddle

### One to Seven

It is characteristic of many *masechtot* that they close with material of an aggadic nature, and *Kinnim*, as the final *masechta* of *Seder Kodshim*, follows this pattern. The *Rambam* likewise closes each of his fourteen *sefarim* with an aggadic passage, not merely his individual *halachot*.

*Rabbi Yehoshua* opens by citing a saying: when something is alive it has one voice, and when it dies it has seven voices. This was evidently a familiar riddle, and *Rabbi Yehoshua* connects it to the case just concluded in our *mishna* — the progression from a single pair of birds to the seven birds required under the final scenario. The fit is admittedly imperfect: the case did not begin with one bird that grew to seven, but with four that grew to seven. Nevertheless, *Rabbi Yehoshua* intends the aphorism as a peg on which to hang the *mishna*'s climactic ruling.

### Seven Instruments

The *mishna* then offers what may be the more straightforward reading of the aphorism — one applying to a ram rather than a cow, as the text will shortly mention wool. When the animal is alive it produces one voice; when it dies its body yields seven. Its two horns become two *chatzotzerot* — whether these are understood as *shofarot* for use on *Rosh Hashanah* or as the animal-horn instruments used by the *levi'im* for musical accompaniment is itself a matter of debate. Its two leg bones become two *chalilim*, flutes fashioned from the hollow of the bone. Its hide becomes a drum. Its stomach or entrails become strings for a *nevel*, some form of lyre or plucked instrument — much as catgut, despite the name, is derived from animal intestines. And the finer intestines serve as strings for the *kinnor*, a harp-like instrument, the difference between the two sets of strings perhaps being one of quality or gauge. That accounts for seven. Some add that the ram's wool can be used to produce *techelet*, and that the *kohen gadol*'s *me'il techelet* bore bells at its hem that produced sound — though this connection is acknowledged to be somewhat strained, since it is the bells rather than the wool itself that make the sound.

### Elders & Wisdom

The *masechta* closes with a final *aggadata* from *Rabbi Shimon Hakashia*. He observes that among *ziknei am ha-aretz* — the elderly of the uneducated class — the older they become, the more their minds become disordered and confused, as the verse states: God removes speech from the trustworthy, and the discernment of elders is taken away. This describes a recognizable reality; the prevalence of dementia in advanced age is well-documented.

But the point of citing this verse is to establish a contrast. *Aval ziknei Torah* — the elders of Torah — are not like this. For them, the very opposite holds: the older they grow, the more settled and clear their thinking becomes. They accumulate wisdom rather than losing it, as the verse declares: *bishishim chochma* — with the aged is wisdom — *v'orech yamim tvunah* — and with length of days, understanding.

One might ask what this *aggadata* has to do with *Kinnim* in particular. One suggestion is that since the *masechta* has been speaking of one thing becoming seven — of something that increases with age, so to speak — the transition to elders who grow in wisdom rather than declining is a natural, if loosely associative, conclusion. A more pointed reading is that the *masechta* of *Kinnim* is itself an exercise in sustained mental acrobatics — the tracking of combinatorial permutations across multiple layers of uncertainty — and the closing passage affirms that precisely this kind of rigorous intellectual engagement is what preserves and sharpens the mind with age. There may also be a subtle wordplay at work: *zkeinim* and *ziknei* bear a resemblance to *Kinnim*, and the word *m'terefet* — meaning disordered or confused — evokes the mixing and confusing of birds that is the tractate's central subject. Whether or not those connections are intended, the thematic resonance is apt.
