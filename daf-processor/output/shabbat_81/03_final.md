# Shabbat 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Objects & Measurements

### Earth & Clay Seals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֲדָמָה — כְּחוֹתַם הַמַּרְצוּפִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כְּחוֹתַם הָאִיגְּרוֹת. זֶבֶל וָחוֹל הַדַּק — כְּדֵי לְזַבֵּל קֶלַח שֶׁל כְּרוּב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כְּדֵי לְזַבֵּל כְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **earth** on Shabbat is **equivalent to the seal of large sacks;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.** Earth was used to seal the openings of sacks so that any tampering would be evident. **And the Rabbis say:** The measure for liability is much smaller, **equivalent to the seal of letters.** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **manure and fine sand** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to fertilize** one **stalk of cabbage;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis say:** The measure that determines liability for carrying it out is **equivalent** to that which is used **to fertilize a leek,** which is less than that used for cabbage.

The eighty-first daf of Masekhta Shabbat continues the Mishnah's enumeration of minimum quantities (*shiurim*) for carrying various objects on *Shabbat*. The Mishnah opens with *adamah* — earth or clay — measured as *kechosem hamartzufim*, the size of a seal used on chests or storage containers. This is the opinion of Rabbi Akiva. The *chachamim*, however, say the standard is *kechosem ha'igaros*, the size of a seal used on letters, which is of course smaller. The Mishnah then addresses *zevel vichol hadak* — manure and fine sand — both used as fertilizer. Rabbi Akiva holds the relevant quantity is *kedei lezavel klach shel k'ruv*, enough to fertilize a single stalk of cabbage. The *chachamim* say it is enough to fertilize a *k'reisha*, a leek, which is presumably a smaller plant requiring less material.

### Sand & Plaster

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹל הַגַּס — כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן עַל מְלֹא כַּף סִיד. קָנֶה — כְּדֵי לַעֲשׂוֹת קוּלְמוֹס. וְאִם הָיָה עָבֶה אוֹ מְרוּסָּס — כְּדֵי לְבַשֵּׁל בּוֹ בֵּיצָה קַלָּה שֶׁבַּבֵּיצִים, טְרוּפָה וּנְתוּנָה בָּאִילְפָּס.
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **coarse sand** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to place on a full spoon of plaster.** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **a reed** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to make a quill. And if** the reed **was thick** and unfit for writing, **or** if it was **fragmented,** its measure for liability is **equivalent** to that which is used **to cook an egg most easily** cooked, one that is already **beaten and placed in a stew pot.**

The Mishnah continues with *chol hagass*, coarse or thick sand, measured as *kedei litein al melo kaf sid* — the amount placed on the head of a plasterer's trowel. Rashi (in his first wide comment on this passage) explains: *bigmara pireish kaf shel sayadin*, the Gemara clarifies this as the applicator used by those who plaster walls. So the *shiur* is the amount of coarse sand that would fit on a single pass of that trowel. The relevance of this measurement becomes clear once we understand why sand is added to plaster at all — a question taken up immediately by the Gemara.

Regarding reeds: *kaneh kedei la'asos kulmus*, a reed large enough to fashion a quill for writing. If the reed is too thick or cracked for use as a quill (*im hayah aveh o merusas*), the relevant *shiur* shifts to the amount sufficient to serve as firewood to cook a *beitzah kalah shebeveitzim* — the lightest, most easily cooked egg — when that egg is beaten and placed in a pan.

### Plaster Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ עַל מְלֹא כַּף סִיד. תָּנָא: כְּדֵי לִיתֵּן עַל פִּי כַּף שֶׁל סַיָּידִין. מַאן תְּנָא דְּחוֹל מְעַלֵּי לֵיהּ לְסִיד? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא יָסוּד אָדָם אֶת בֵּיתוֹ בְּסִיד, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן עֵירַב בּוֹ תֶּבֶן אוֹ חוֹל. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: תֶּבֶן מוּתָּר, חוֹל אָסוּר — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא טְרַכְסִיד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **coarse sand** is equivalent to that which is used **to place on a full spoon of plaster.** A *tanna* **taught** in a *Tosefta*: An amount **equivalent** to that which is **placed on the opening of a plasterer's trowel,** and not on a spoon used for eating. The Gemara asks: **Who is** the ***tanna*** who holds that **sand is beneficial for plaster** and is, therefore, mixed with it? **Rav Ḥisda said: It is Rabbi Yehuda, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: In mourning the destruction of the Temple, **one may not plaster his house with plaster,** which is white, **unless he mixed straw or sand in it,** which will make the color off-white and less attractive. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Straw is permitted,** but **sand is prohibited because** when mixed with plaster it forms white **cement [*teraksid*].** Apparently, Rabbi Yehuda holds that sand is typically mixed with plaster.

The Gemara asks: *man tana dechol ma'ali lei lesid* — which Tanna holds that sand is actually beneficial for plastering, and thus that it is a meaningful use by which to define the *shiur*? Rav Chisda answers: it is Rabbi Yehuda. A *baraita* is cited in support: one should not plaster one's house with *sid* (lime plaster) after the *churban* as a sign of mourning for Jerusalem — unless one has mixed in straw or sand, which diminishes the pristine beauty of the finish, thus serving as a *zecher le-churban*. Rabbi Yehuda, however, draws a distinction: straw (*teven*) is acceptable, but sand (*chol*) is prohibited — *mipnei shehu trach sid*, because sand actually strengthens the plaster, making it better rather than worse. According to Rabbi Yehuda, then, sand genuinely improves plaster, which is precisely why the Mishnah uses the plasterer's trowel as the measuring standard.

The implication is that the *chachamim*, who permit sand as a *zecher le-churban*, must hold that sand ordinarily does not improve plaster — it only serves as a symbolic degradation of it. If so, how can the Mishnah — which may reflect the *chachamim*'s view — define the *shiur* of sand by reference to the amount used in plastering?

### Kilkulo Zehu Tikuno

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן — קִילְקוּלוֹ זֶהוּ תִּיקּוּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Even if you say that** our mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda, we can say that **its ruination is its improvement.** Even though the Rabbis hold that mixing sand with plaster is not beneficial, since following the destruction of the Temple only partially ruined plaster may be used, adding sand to plaster enables its use.

Rava resolves the difficulty: *afilu teima rabanan* — even according to the *rabbanan*, sand is something one would add to plaster and is therefore a meaningful use. The key phrase is *kilkulo zehu tikuno* — its defect is precisely its correction. Because the sand makes the plaster worse aesthetically, that very deficiency is what makes the plaster permissible to use. Without the sand, the plaster would be too perfect, and using it would violate the spirit of mourning for the *churban*. With the sand, the plaster is both usable and appropriately imperfect. Therefore, even for the *rabbanan*, sand is something one would specifically want to add to plaster — not because it improves the plaster's structural quality, but because it makes the plaster *halakhically* usable in the post-*churban* world. The *shiur* thus makes sense even according to the majority opinion.

The phrase *kilkulo zehu tikuno* has wider resonance in *halakhic* literature. A well-known *teshuvah* of Rav Ettlinger in the *Binyan Tzion* employs a similar logic in addressing the status of those who violate *Shabbat* in the post-*Haskalah* era. When *mechallel Shabbat* became the norm across much of the Jewish world, he argued, the very breadth of the violation changes its *halakhic* character — what makes it worse, in a certain sense, is what moderates certain exclusionary consequences. Whether or not one accepts that application, the underlying logical structure of *kilkulo zehu tikuno* — that what degrades a thing can simultaneously constitute its functional or normative correction — is a recurring conceptual tool in *halakhic* reasoning.

### Reed Quill & Cooking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָנֶה כְּדֵי לַעֲשׂוֹת קוּלְמוֹס. תָּנָא: קוּלְמוֹס הַמַּגִּיעַ לְקִשְׁרֵי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו. בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: קֶשֶׁר הָעֶלְיוֹן אוֹ קֶשֶׁר הַתַּחְתּוֹן? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **a reed** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to make a quill.** The size of the quill was not specified. A *tanna* **taught** in a *Tosefta*: This refers to **a quill that reaches to the joints of one's fingers. Rav Ashi raised a dilemma:** Is this referring to **the upper joint** of the fingers, **or the lower joint?** No resolution was found to this dilemma, and therefore **let it stand** unresolved.

The Mishnah's standard for a reed usable as a quill is that it must be *hamagi'a le'kishrei etzbe'otav* — long enough to reach the knuckles of the fingers when held. But which knuckles? Rav Ashi raises the question: *kesher elyon o kesher tachton* — the upper knuckle (where the finger joins the hand) or the lower knuckle (in the middle of the finger)? Rashi identifies the upper knuckle as the joint connecting finger to hand and the lower as the middle joint. The Gemara leaves this question unresolved: *teiku*.

### Light Egg Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיָה עָבֶה כּוּ׳. תָּנָא: טְרוּפָה בְּשֶׁמֶן וּנְתוּנָה בָּאִילְפָּס. אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא לִבְרֵיהּ: מִי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ בֵּיצָה קַלָּה מַאי הִיא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בֵּיעֲתָא דְצִילְצְלָא. מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּזוּטְרָא? אֵימָא: דְּצִיפַּרְתָּא! אִישְׁתִּיק. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִידֵּי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ בְּהָא? [אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי] אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בֵּיצַת תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת. וּמַאי קָרוּ לַהּ ״בֵּיצָה קַלָּה״ — שִׁיעֲרוּ חֲכָמִים, אֵין לָךְ בֵּיצָה קַלָּה לְבַשֵּׁל יוֹתֵר מִבֵּיצַת תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת. וּמַאי שְׁנָא כׇּל שִׁיעוּרֵי שַׁבָּת כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת וְהָכָא כְּבֵיצָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת מִבֵּיצָה קַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And if** the reed **was thick** and unfit for writing, it is considered as fuel, and its measure for liability is equivalent to that which is used to cook a beaten egg. A *tanna* **taught** in a *Tosefta*: **Beaten** means beaten **in oil and placed in a stew pot. Mar, son of Ravina, said to his son: Have you heard what an egg** cooked **easily is? He said to him: The egg of a turtledove.** He asked his father: **What is the reason?** Is it **because** it **is small?** If so, **say** the egg **of a sparrow. He was silent** and had no explanation. He subsequently asked his father: **Have you heard** anything **about this? He said to him** that **Rav Sheshet said as follows:** This refers to **the egg of a chicken. And what** is the reason that **they call it an egg** cooked **easily?** Because **the Sages estimated** that **there is no egg easier to cook than the egg of a chicken.** He asked his father: **And what is different** about this measure? **All measures of** prohibited labors on **Shabbat** involving food are **a dried fig-bulk, and here** the measure is **like an egg** cooked easily? **He said to him** that **Rav Naḥman said as follows:** He is liable for carrying out **a dried fig-bulk from an egg** cooked **easily,** not the entire egg.

Regarding the reed too thick or cracked for use as a quill: the *shiur* shifts to the amount of firewood sufficient to cook a *beitzah kalah*. A *baraita* specifies that the egg is beaten with oil and placed in a pan (*trufah b'shemen unetunah b'ilpas*), meaning it is effectively a fried scrambled egg, which requires less heat and therefore less firewood. Mar the son of Ravina posed a question to his own son: *nishmi'a lach beitzah kalah mai* — what exactly is meant by a *beitzah kalah*, a "light egg"? The son suggested it refers to the egg of a *tziltzala* — a small bird (identified variously as a turtle dove or sparrow), reasoning that a smaller egg would require less firewood. The father pressed further: if the logic is simply that smaller is easier, why not the *tzipirta* (a sparrow, or possibly even a hummingbird), which is yet smaller? The son fell silent. He then asked his father what he had heard on the matter, and the father cited Rav Sheshet: *beitzat tarnegolet* — a regular chicken egg. Why is a chicken egg called the "light" egg? *Mishum d'kadim umevashla yatir mikol beitzat tarnegolet* — because it cooks faster and more easily than the egg of any other bird.

This raises a further question: normally in matters of food the operative measure is a *grogeret*, the size of a pressed date, which is smaller than an egg. Here we are measuring firewood, but the amount of firewood is calibrated to the amount of food being cooked. Why use an egg rather than a *grogeret* as the food standard? The answer, citing Rav Nachman, is that the Mishnah does not intend a full egg: *ke-grogeret mi-beitzah kalah* — the amount of firewood needed to cook a *grogeret*-sized portion of a chicken egg, beaten with oil in a pan. The egg is the type of food; the *grogeret* remains the measure of quantity. The *shiur* for the reed as firewood, then, is the amount needed to cook one *grogeret*-worth of scrambled chicken egg.

### Bone Spoon & Lock

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עֶצֶם כְּדֵי לַעֲשׂוֹת תַּרְווֹד. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי לַעֲשׂוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ חָף.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **a bone** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to make a spoon. Rabbi Yehuda says:** In a measure **equivalent** to that which is used **to make from it a key.**

Moving to *etzem*, bone: the Mishnah gives the *shiur* as *kedei la'asos tarvad*, enough to make a small spoon. Rabbi Yehuda says the measure is *kedei la'asos mimenu kaf*, enough to make the teeth (*chappei*) for a lock mechanism. The Gemara finds this surprising — the consistent assumption throughout this discussion is that Rabbi Yehuda is always the stricter opinion (*machmir*), meaning he gives smaller *shiurim* for carrying and thus creates a larger range of liability. If Rabbi Yehuda's measure here is the teeth of a lock, which initially seems larger than a small spoon, how is he being stricter?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמֵימְרָא דְּשִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְפִישׁ? הָא קַיְימָא לַן דְּשִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבָּנַן נְפִישׁ! אָמַר עוּלָּא: חֲפֵי פּוֹתַחַת. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲפֵי פּוֹתַחַת טְהוֹרִין, קְבָעָן בַּפּוֹתַחַת טְמֵאִין. וְשֶׁל גַּל, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחִיבְּרָן בַּדֶּלֶת וּקְבָעָן בְּמַסְמְרִים טְהוֹרִין — שֶׁכׇּל הַמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע הֲרֵי הוּא כַּקַּרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that the measure of Rabbi Yehuda is greater? Don't we maintain that the measure of the Rabbis is greater? Ulla said:** Rabbi Yehuda did not refer to the entire key, but to **the teeth of a key.** With regard to the above, the Gemara cites that which **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The teeth of a key are ritually pure,** and they cannot become impure when separate from the key, as they have no function on their own. However, if **one affixed them to a key, they** can become **ritually impure** as part of a utensil. **And** teeth **of a lock, even though one attached them to the door and affixed them with nails, are ritually pure, as anything attached to the ground has** the same legal status **as the ground** itself, which cannot become ritually impure.

Ulla resolves the difficulty: *chappei potachat* refers not to the entire lock but only to its inner teeth or pins — the mechanism through which the key operates. That is smaller than a spoon, so Rabbi Yehuda is indeed the stricter opinion. A *baraita* then adds a *tumah* dimension: the teeth of a lock by themselves, before being inserted, are *tehorim* because they are not yet functional. Once fitted into the lock (*keva'an be-potachat*), they are *teme'im*. Rashi understands this as referring to bone, but *Tosafot* argues on strong grounds that the discussion concerns metal, since bone without a *beit kibul* — a receptacle — cannot become *tamei*, while metal can. Once the metal teeth are inserted and the lock becomes functional, the mechanism renders the lock susceptible to *tumah*. However, if the same mechanism is built into a door (*shel gal*), even though physically affixed with nails, it remains *tahor* — because *kol hamechubar le-karka harei hu ke-karka*, anything attached to the ground is legally equivalent to the ground itself and cannot receive *tumah*.

### Glass & Rocks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְכוּכִית — כְּדֵי לִגְרוֹר בָּהּ רֹאשׁ הַכַּרְכֵּר. צְרוֹר אוֹ אֶבֶן — כְּדֵי לִזְרוֹק בָּעוֹף. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי לִזְרוֹק בַּבְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The measure that determines liability for carrying out **glass** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to scrape** and smooth **the top of a bobbin,** a sharpened stick used by weavers. The measure that determines liability for carrying out **a pebble or a stone** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to throw at a bird** to chase it away. **Rabbi Elazar bar Ya'akov says: Equivalent** to that which is used **to throw at an animal,** which is larger.

The Mishnah continues: *zechuchit*, glass, with a *shiur* of *kedei ligror bo rashei ha-karkar* — enough to scrape the head of a *karkar*, a wooden implement used to beat wool in a loom. A *baraita* notes an alternative spelling (*sechuchit*) and describes a related use: *kedei liftzoa bah shenei mimin ke-achat* — to separate two threads at once that have become tangled in the weaving process.

For stones — *tzror o even* — the Mishnah gives the *shiur* as the size needed to throw at a bird (*kedei lizrok be-of*) to chase it from one's crops. Rabbi Eliezer bar Yaakov holds a larger measure is needed: the size to throw at an animal (*kedei lizrok bi-vehemah*). Rabbi Yaakov in the name of Rabbi Yochanan clarifies that the standard requires a stone large enough that the animal actually feels it and responds — if the animal is unaffected, the stone is functionally insufficient. The distinction between birds and animals is also explained: one can simply shout at birds to drive them off, so stones are not practically necessary for that purpose and the relevant use is throwing at animals.

### Stone Weight Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרוֹר אוֹ אֶבֶן כְּדֵי לִזְרוֹק בָּעוֹף. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁמַּרְגֶּשֶׁת בָּהּ. וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּרוֹ? תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: מִשְׁקַל עֲשָׂרָה זוּז.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: The measure that determines liability for carrying out **a pebble or a stone** is **equivalent** to that which is used **to throw at a bird** to chase it away. **Rabbi Elazar** ben Ya'akov says: Equivalent to that which is used to throw at an animal. **Rabbi Ya'akov said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And that is** only if the stone is large enough **that** the animal **feels it. And how much is the measure** of that stone? **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar ben Ya'akov says: A weight of ten *zuz*.**

A *baraita* gives a concrete measure: Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that the stone must weigh *mishkal asara zuz* — ten *zuz*. This is offered as the operative *shiur* for a stone significant enough to carry. The Gemara here marks a natural transition into the next topic, which involves a different category of stones entirely.

## Bathroom & Dignity (II)

### Zonim Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוּנִין עַל לְבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: רַבּוֹתַי, אֲבָנִים שֶׁל בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא שִׁיעוּרָן בְּכַמָּה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: כְּזַיִת כֶּאֱגוֹז וּכְבֵיצָה. אֲמַר לְהוּ: וְכִי טוּרְטָנֵי יַכְנִיס? נִמְנוּ וְגָמְרוּ מְלֹא הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Zunin entered the study hall** and **said to** the Sages: **My teachers,** with regard to **stones** that may be moved on Shabbat for wiping in **the bathroom, how much is their measure? They said to him:** Stones of only three sizes may be moved for that purpose: **An olive-bulk, a nut-bulk, and an egg-bulk. He said to them: And will he take scales [*turtani*] into** the bathroom to weigh each stone? **They were counted and the Sages concluded** that one need not measure the stones. He simply takes **a handful** of stones.

Zonim (known elsewhere in connection with Beisus ben Zonim) came to the *beis hamidrash* and posed a question: *avanim shel beis hakisei shiuran be-khama* — what is the *shiur* for bathroom stones? In antiquity, smooth stones served the function of toilet paper, and people would carry them to the bathroom for that purpose. The question has two dimensions. First, there is a *halakhic* question about carrying: what size stone constitutes a meaningful amount for which one would be liable for carrying on *Shabbat*? Second, and more immediately operative, there is a rabbinic question: since stones that have not been pre-designated are *muktzeh*, the rabbis nonetheless permitted using bathroom stones because of *kevod habriyot* — human dignity. But that permission extends only to stones appropriate for this use. What, then, is the minimum size that renders a stone appropriate?

### Melo Hayad Standard

The rabbis answered Zonim by giving three sizes: *kezayis*, the size of an olive; *ke-egoz*, the size of a nut; and *ke-beitzah*, the size of an egg. Rashi explains these correspond to different stages of use — though the precise details of this tripartite division are somewhat obscure. Zonim pushed back: *ve-chi tortani yakhnis* — should a person bring a scale into the bathroom to weigh out exactly these three measures? This is not a functional standard. The rabbis found the objection compelling: they took a vote (*nimnu ve-gamru*) and determined the standard to be *melo hayad* — a full handful of stones.

The logic of this exchange is revealing. In virtually every other context in this chapter, the Gemara accepts precise, even counterintuitive *shiurim* without anyone complaining about practicality. One might ask: why should the bathroom be different? The answer lies in what kind of question is actually being asked. If the question were about establishing the *shiur* for liability in carrying (*chiyuv* for *hotza'ah*), the response to Zonim — "don't you have scales?" — would make little sense, since we never make that argument anywhere else. The fact that the rabbis found the argument persuasive and convened to issue a more functional ruling strongly suggests that the question here is rabbinic in nature: what *are we permitting* the person to use in the bathroom despite the *muktzeh* status of the stones? That is a leniency the rabbis are granting on account of *kevod habriyot*, and it is entirely coherent that in defining the scope of such a leniency, they would adopt a practical, functional standard rather than a set of precise measurements.

### Nut vs Egg Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כְּזַיִת כֶּאֱגוֹז וּכְבֵיצָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: מְלֹא הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei says** the measure of bathroom stones is **an olive-bulk, a nut-bulk, and an egg-bulk. Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi Yosei, says in the name of his father:** One need not measure the stones. He simply takes **a handful** of stones.

A *baraita* (cited *tanya*) records: Rabbi Yose says *ke-egoz* or *ke-beitzah* — these are the sizes. His son, Rabbi Shimon ben Rabbi Yose, transmits in his father's name: *melo hayad* — a full handful. This suggests the *tanna'im* themselves went back and forth on the question, with the later, more practical formulation attributed to Rabbi Shimon in the name of his father — consistent with the pattern we observed, where the precise sizes gave way to the more functionally accessible standard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כַּמַּחֲלוֹקֶת כָּאן כָּךְ מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּאֶתְרוֹג. הָתָם מַתְנִיתִין, הָכָא בָּרָיְיתָא! אֶלָּא: כַּמַּחֲלוֹקֶת בְּאֶתְרוֹג כָּךְ מַחֲלוֹקֶת כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rafram bar Pappa said** that **Rav Ḥisda said: Like** the **dispute here, so too,** there **is a dispute** between these Sages **with regard to** the minimum size of **a citron.** The Gemara is surprised at the comparison. Why does the Gemara cite this *baraita* as a mnemonic to recall the dispute about the size of a citron? **There,** with regard to a citron, it is **a mishna** that is known by all; **here** it is **a *baraita*,** which is more obscure and more likely to require a mnemonic and a comparison to a more popular source. **Rather,** the phrasing is reversed: **Like** the **dispute** with regard to **a citron, so too,** there **is a dispute here.**

The Gemara then cites Ravin bar Papa in the name of Rav Chisda: *kemachloket kan kach machloket b'esrog* — just as there is a debate here about whether the minimum size is a nut or an egg, there is an identical debate about the minimum size of an *esrog*. The Gemara initially objects: the *esrog* debate is a *mishnah*, while the bathroom stone debate is a *baraita* — one does not typically use a *baraita* as the organizing frame for understanding a *mishnah*. The formulation is therefore reversed: just as there is the well-known debate about the *esrog* (*ke-egoz* versus *ke-beitzah*), so too in the case of bathroom stones the same parameters apply.

At this point the Gemara steps back to note, with some implicit wonder, the sheer amount of Talmudic attention devoted to carrying. Roughly forty to forty-five *dappim* of this tractate — nearly a third of *Masechet Shabbat*, which runs to approximately 140 *daf* — address the laws of carrying, and *Masechet Eiruvin* is devoted almost entirely to the same topic. Similarly, the laws relating to the bathroom appear with surprising frequency across both this tractate and *Berachot*. This is not coincidental: for the sages, every domain of daily life fell within the scope of *halacha*, and questions of *muktzeh*, *tumah*, *kevod habriyot*, and physical health were all interrelated concerns they felt responsible to address comprehensively.

### Payas & Practical Considerations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הַפָּאיֵיס. מַאי ״פָּאיֵיס״? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כַּרְשִׁינֵי בַּבְלָיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: However,** one may **not** move **the *payis*** for use in a bathroom. The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of ***payis*? Rabbi Zeira said:** It refers to **clods of Babylonian earth,** which is soft and flaky.

The Gemara rules: Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav says one may not use *payas* — hardened clumps of earth — in the bathroom. Rav Zeira explains: *karshinei bavlita*, Babylonian clods. Rashi notes that Babylonia was a particularly moist climate and these clods would crumble in one's hands. The concern is practical: what seems solid will disintegrate, making the material both ineffective and unpleasant.

### Shinuy on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אָסוּר לְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בִּצְרוֹר בְּשַׁבָּת כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁמְּמַשְׁמֵשׁ בַּחוֹל. מַתְקִיף לַהּ מָר זוּטְרָא: לִיסְתַּכַּן? כִּלְאַחַר יָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It is prohibited to manipulate** the anus **with a stone on Shabbat** to help discharge bodily functions **in the manner** that **one manipulates** it **on weekdays. Mar Zutra strongly objected to this:** According to Rava, should one **endanger himself** by refraining from relieving himself? The Gemara explains: He meant he should do so **in an unusual manner** and not in the manner it is typically done.

Rava then rules: *assur lemashmesh betzror beShabbat* — one may not use a stone on *Shabbat* in the manner one uses it during the week. At first this seems to prohibit use entirely, which would be a hardship. Mar Zutra objects: *listaken*? Must a person endanger himself? The concern is not merely aesthetic: pressing a stone against the body in a certain way can physically assist in relieving oneself when one is having difficulty, and to prohibit this entirely would be medically harmful. The Gemara resolves the tension by reading the prohibition differently: the word *kederech* means not that one may not use a stone on *Shabbat* at all, but rather that one may not use it in exactly the ordinary weekday manner. *Kelachar yad* — in a slightly modified, unusual fashion — is permitted. The underlying concern motivating the *shinuy* will be addressed further below.

### Fixed Location & Communal Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אִם יֵשׁ מָקוֹם קָבוּעַ לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא — מְלֹא הַיָּד. אִם לָאו — כַּהֶכְרֵעַ מְדוֹכָה קְטַנָּה שֶׁל בְּשָׂמִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the size of stones, **Rabbi Yannai said: If** he has **a fixed place for a bathroom,** he may take **a handful** of stones; if he does not need them on Shabbat, he can use them on another occasion. **If** he does **not** have a fixed place he may bring in **an average size** stone, which is the size **of a small mortar** used **for** crushing **spices.**

Rav Yannai rules: *im yesh makom kavua leveit hakisei*, if there is a fixed bathroom location, one may bring *melo hayad* — a full handful of stones — to the bathroom. The rationale: if any stones are left over after use, they remain available for the next person. But if there is no fixed bathroom location and one is the only person going there, a smaller amount is permitted. Rashi identifies this smaller amount as the middle measure of the three originally cited — the size of a nut — while *Tosafot* reads the phrase as referring to the leg of a small mortar used for grinding spices, yielding a different (though similarly small) measure.

The reasoning behind the distinction between fixed and unfixed bathrooms reflects the tension inherent in the rabbinic allowance. On one hand, *kevod habriyot* justifies waiving *muktzeh* restrictions. On the other hand, the rabbis do not grant a blanket license to handle as much *muktzeh* material as one wishes. Where there is a fixed location, the excess stones are not wasted — they serve a communal purpose. Where there is no fixed location, the permissible quantity is tied more tightly to the individual's actual need.

### Kevod Habriyot & Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: יָרְדוּ עָלֶיהָ גְּשָׁמִים וְנִטַּשְׁטְשׁוּ מַהוּ? אָמַר לֵיהּ: אִם הָיָה רִישּׁוּמָן נִיכָּר — מוּתָּר. מֵיתִיבִי? עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים מְבִיאִין אֶת הָאָדָם לִידֵי תַחְתּוֹנִיּוֹת, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הָאוֹכֵל עֲלֵי קָנִים, וַעֲלֵי גְפָנִים, וְלוּלַבֵּי גְפָנִים, וּמוֹרִיגֵּי בְהֵמָה בְּלֹא מֶלַח, וְשִׁדְרוֹ שֶׁל דָּג, וְדָג מָלִיחַ שֶׁלֹּא בִּישֵּׁל כׇּל צוֹרְכּוֹ, וְהַשּׁוֹתֶה שִׁמְרֵי יַיִן, וְהַמְקַנֵּחַ בְּסִיד, וּבְחַרְסִית, וְהַמְקַנֵּחַ בִּצְרוֹר שֶׁקִּינַּח בּוֹ חֲבֵרוֹ. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף הַתּוֹלֶה עַצְמוֹ בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא. לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּלַח, הָא בְּיָבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** What is the ruling if **rain fell on** the stone **and** the indications that it had been used previously in the bathroom **were obscured?** The dilemma is: Is moving it permitted like a stone that is designated for use in the bathroom on Shabbat, or, is moving it prohibited since its indications were obscured and it might have the legal status of set-aside? Rav Yosef **said to him: If indication on them is apparent,** even though it is partially obscured, **it is permitted,** since the stone remains clear that it is designated for use in the bathroom.
>
> The Gemara **raises an objection:** Is it permitted to wipe with a stone that was already used? Didn't the Sages say: **Ten things bring a person to** suffer from **hemorrhoids and they are: One who eats the leaves of bulrushes, grape leaves, tendrils of grapevines, the palate and tongue of an animal,** as well as any other part of the animal which is not smooth and which has protrusions, **without salt, the spine of a fish, a salty fish that is not fully cooked, and one who drinks wine dregs, and one who cleans himself with lime and clay,** the materials from which earthenware is made, **and one who cleans himself with a stone with which another** person **has cleaned himself. And some say: One who suspends himself in the bathroom as well.** Apparently, using a previously used stone is dangerous to one's health. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** where it is prohibited, is referring **to** a case where the stone is still **moist. Here,** where it is permitted, is referring **to** a case where the stone is **dry. And if you wish, say** instead that **here,** where it is prohibited, is referring to **one side,** using the side that was already used; **here,** where it is permitted, is referring **to both sides,** using the other side of the same stone. **And if you wish, say** instead that **this,** where it is permitted, is referring **to one's own** stone, which he used to clean himself; **this,** where it is prohibited, is referring to **another** person's stone, which poses a danger.

The Gemara raises a further question through the voice of Ravina challenging Mareimar. Rabbi Eliezer rules that a person may take a twig lying on the ground to use as a toothpick — a *muktzeh* object — on *Shabbat*, invoking the idea that human dignity requires it. The *chachamim* disagree: one may only take a twig from the trough of an animal, since that twig was already set aside for animal consumption and is not *muktzeh*. Picking up a random twig from the ground, even to address the discomfort of food stuck in one's teeth, is not permitted. So the *chachamim* do not allow *kevod habriyot* to override *muktzeh* when one had an alternative — why then did we permit taking *muktzeh* stones to the rooftop bathroom?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבָּהּ בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: מַהוּ לְהַעֲלוֹתָם אַחֲרָיו לַגַּג? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גָּדוֹל כְּבוֹד הַבְּרִיּוֹת שֶׁדּוֹחֶה אֶת ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה״ שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה. יָתֵיב מָרִימָר וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְמָרִימָר: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר נוֹטֵל אָדָם קֵיסָם מִשֶּׁלְּפָנָיו לַחֲצוֹת בּוֹ שִׁינָּיו, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים לֹא יִטּוֹל אֶלָּא מִן הָאֵבוּס שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה! הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם — אָדָם קוֹבֵעַ מָקוֹם לִסְעוּדָה, הָכָא — אָדָם קוֹבֵעַ מָקוֹם לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Sheila raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda: What is** the *halakha* with regard **to taking** those stones **up with him to the roof** if his bathroom is there? Is it permitted or is it prohibited due to the exertion involved? **He said to him:** It is permitted; **great is human dignity as it overrides a prohibition in the Torah.** The Gemara relates: **Mareimar sat and stated this *halakha*. Ravina raised an objection to** the statement of **Mareimar** from a *baraita* where **Rabbi Eliezer says: A person may take a** wood **chip from** the ground **before him to** clean **his teeth** on Shabbat. **And the Rabbis say one may take** a wood chip **only from the animal's trough,** which is already designated for the animal's use, but not from wood on the ground, which is set-aside. Apparently, despite the fact that using the wood chip enhances human dignity, it is nevertheless prohibited due to the prohibition of set-aside. The Gemara rejects this: **How can you compare? There, a person determines** the **place for** his **meal.** Since he knows where he will eat he should have prepared toothpicks beforehand. **Here, does a person determine** the **place for a bathroom?** He relieves himself wherever he finds a discreet place to do so.

The Gemara responds with a key distinction: *hatam adam kove'a makom le-se'udato* — there, a person has a fixed place for his meal and could have designated toothpicks in advance. *Hacha adam kove'a makom le-veit hakisei* — here, a person does not know in advance exactly where he will need to relieve himself. Because the need is inherently unpredictable, we cannot fault him for failing to prepare. It is for this reason that *kevod habriyot* takes precedence and allows him to take the stones wherever he needs them.

This distinction carries practical application. The Gemara earlier cited Rabbah bar Rav Shila's question to Rav Chisda: may one carry bathroom stones up to a rooftop? Rav Chisda answered: *gadol kevod habriyot she-docheh lo ta'aseh shebaTorah* — great is human dignity, for it overrides a negative commandment of the Torah. This must not be taken too literally: as the Gemara in *Berachot* clarifies, the *lo ta'aseh* in question is primarily the rabbinic prohibition, which derives its force from the biblical *lo tasur* ("do not deviate" from rabbinic rulings), or in certain cases involves passive violations or matters *devarim shebamamon*. The principle is not that *kevod habriyot* overrides biblical prohibitions categorically, but that it can override rabbinic restrictions when those restrictions would collide directly with human dignity.

This principle appears in other areas of *halakhic* literature as well. Questions of *shalom bayit* or *kevod habriyot* sometimes permit deviation from rabbinic norms in similarly constrained circumstances — not as a wholesale repeal of those norms, but as a recognition that when human dignity is directly implicated and no practical alternative exists, the rabbinic restriction yields.

A practical extension of this discussion arises with respect to cutting toilet paper on *Shabbat*. Assuming that cutting toilet paper constitutes at minimum a *d'rabbanan* violation (a question about which there is room to discuss, given its wholly disposable nature and the role of the perforations), the principle of *kevod habriyot* would apply when one finds oneself without pre-cut paper. If the violation is genuinely only *d'rabbanan*, and particularly if performed with a *shinuy* — using the back of the hand, the teeth, or two fingers rather than the normal method — the combination of the reduced severity and *kevod habriyot* would render it permissible. One should not extend the logic of the rooftop stones too far, however: where one clearly had the opportunity to prepare in advance and failed to do so, the leniency is harder to invoke. But in genuinely unanticipated situations, the principle applies.

### Used Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אִם יֵשׁ לָהּ עֵד — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: If** the stone **has an indication on it** that it has already been used in the bathroom, **one is permitted** to move it for that purpose on Shabbat, regardless of its size.

Rav Sheshet rules: *im yesh lah ed mutar* — if a stone has a mark (*ed*) indicating previous use, it is permitted. *Tosafot* explains the principle: since the stone has been designated by actual use, it is no longer *muktzeh*, and therefore the usual size and quantity restrictions do not apply. One may use a stone larger than the standard or take more than a handful, provided the stone was already used and retains visible evidence of that use. This is in contrast to a stone that has never been used and thus carries no designation — there the restrictions on size and quantity remain in force.

Rashi reads the passage somewhat differently, connecting the statement about a previously used stone to an earlier reference to a small mortar for grinding spices that had been used in the bathroom. The scenario is, by Rashi's own account, quite unusual. *Tosafot* prefers to treat the spice mortar reference as belonging to the previous discussion and to read Rav Sheshet's ruling as an independent principle about any previously used stone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: יָרְדוּ עָלֶיהָ גְּשָׁמִים וְנִטַּשְׁטְשׁוּ מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם הָיָה רִישּׁוּמָן נִיכָּר — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** What is the ruling if **rain fell on** the stone **and** the indications that it had been used previously in the bathroom **were obscured?** The dilemma is: Is moving it permitted like a stone that is designated for use in the bathroom on Shabbat, or, is moving it prohibited since its indications were obscured and it might have the legal status of set-aside? Rav Yosef **said to him: If indication on them is apparent,** even though it is partially obscured, **it is permitted,** since the stone remains clear that it is designated for use in the bathroom.

Abaye then asks Rav Yosef: if rain fell on these stones and partially washed away the stain, what is the ruling? Rav Yosef answers: *im hayah rishumo nikar mutar* — if the mark is still recognizable, the stones retain their designation and remain permitted. Only if the mark has been completely erased does the stone revert to *muktzeh* status.

### Medical Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן בְּשַׁבָּת: שָׁלֹשׁ אֲבָנִים מְקוּרְזָלוֹת מוּתָּר לְהַכְנִיס לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא. וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּרָן? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כֶּאֱגוֹז. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּבֵיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Our Sages taught** in a *baraita* **with regard to Shabbat: Three sharpened stones may be taken into the bathroom. And what is their measure? Rabbi Meir says: A nut-bulk; Rabbi Yehuda says: An egg-bulk.**

The Gemara cites a *baraita* listing ten things that bring a person to hemorrhoids (*tachtoniyot*): eating reed leaves and grapevine leaves and vine tendrils; eating the rough or marbled flesh of an animal without salt; eating the spine of a fish; eating a salted fish that has not been fully cooked; drinking the lees of wine (*shimrei yayin*); wiping oneself with plaster (*sid*) or with pottery (*charsit*); wiping with a stone that one's friend has used (*tzror shekineach bo chaveiro*); and — according to some — squatting unsupported in the bathroom rather than sitting properly.

This passage is purely medical in nature, raising the question of why *halakhic* literature includes such material. The answer lies in the role the sages understood themselves to play. They occupied a position of communal leadership and considered it their responsibility to disseminate knowledge that would protect and promote the wellbeing of the Jewish people — not merely in the domain of *halakha*, but in all matters where reliable guidance was available to them and beneficial to share. Just as a community leader today would feel obligated to remind his congregation to check smoke detector batteries, the sages felt equally obligated to share medical knowledge. The voice in which they do so is informational and advisory, not one of *halakhic* authority; they are not issuing a *gezeirat hakatuv*. This is responsible public instruction woven into the broader discourse.

The Gemara then resolves an apparent contradiction: if wiping with a previously used stone causes hemorrhoids, why did we just say such stones are permitted? The Gemara answers (*lo kashya*): the health risk applies when the stone is still moist (*belacha*), while a dry stone (*beyaveshta*) is harmless. Alternatively, one may use the other side of the stone; or the health concern applies specifically to using someone *else's* stone, not one's own.

## Grass & Witchcraft (II)

### Fields & Grass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אָסוּר לִפָּנוֹת בִּשְׂדֵה נִיר בְּשַׁבָּת. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דַּוְושָׁא — אֲפִילּוּ בְּחוֹל נָמֵי! וְאֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם עֲשָׂבִים, וְהָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: צְרוֹר שֶׁעָלוּ בּוֹ עֲשָׂבִים — מוּתָּר לְקַנֵּחַ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: It is prohibited to defecate in a plowed field on Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for that prohibition? **If you say** it is **due to** the fact that in doing so he **treads** on the furrows and destroys them, it should be prohibited **even on weekdays. Rather,** it is **due to** the concern that he will clean himself with a clod of earth on which **grasses** have grown. **Didn't Reish Lakish say** that **it is permitted to wipe with a stone upon which grasses have grown** even though the grasses will be detached as a result?

The Gemara rules that one may not defecate in a plowed but unplanted field (*istanya*) on *Shabbat*. The initial suggestion — that the reason is *avsha* (trampling), since walking on a freshly plowed field damages it — is rejected, because that would equally prohibit doing so on a weekday. Rather, the concern is the grass growing in such a field. Specifically, Rashi explains that in a newly plowed but unplanted field, grass grows freely, and a person using a stone to wipe himself will inadvertently pull up grass — an act of *tolesh* (uprooting), one of the thirty-nine prohibited labors of *Shabbat*.

### Stone with Grass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: צְרוֹר שֶׁעָלוּ בּוֹ עֲשָׂבִים — מוּתָּר לְקַנֵּחַ בּוֹ. וְהַתּוֹלֵשׁ מִמֶּנָּה בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the matter itself, Reish Lakish said: It is permitted to wipe with a stone upon which grasses have grown. And one who detaches** grasses **from it** unwittingly **on Shabbat is liable to** bring **a sin-offering.**

Reish Lakish addresses this directly: *tzror she'alu bo asavim mutar lekanne'ach bo* — if grass has grown on a stone, one may pick it up and use it to wipe. But *vehatolesh mimenu beShabbos chayav chatas* — if one intentionally tears the grass from the stone on *Shabbat*, one is liable. The key distinction is between inadvertent separation (which may occur incidentally as one uses the stone) and deliberate tearing.

Why is there no concern that lifting the stone — thereby separating the grass from the underlying ground — constitutes *tolesh*? The Gemara implicitly treats this as not meeting the standard for *tolesh*, apparently because moss or grass growing on a stone is not considered to be drawing nutrients from the earth in the same way as rooted plants.

### Parfisa Pots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פַּפֵּי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, הַאי פַּרְפִּיסָא — שְׁרֵי לְטַלְטוֹלֵיהּ. מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא: אִם אָמְרוּ לְצוֹרֶךְ, יֹאמְרוּ שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappi said: Learn from that** which **Reish Lakish** said that **it is permitted to carry this perforated flowerpot** on Shabbat. **Rav Kahana strongly objects to this: If they said** that it is permitted to carry a stone with weeds on it **for a purpose, will they say** it is permitted to carry a flowerpot **for no purpose?**

A second concern exists in the plowed field case, however, independent of grass: Rava's principle that filling a hole in a field constitutes *choresh* (plowing). If one takes a stone from the ground and places it into a hole at the end, one inadvertently levels the earth in a way that makes it fit for planting — and that is *chayav* for *choresh*. Unlike the inadvertent tearing of grass, which is a classic case of *eino mitkavein* (unintended action), filling a hole is something one does consciously, even if one does not know it carries *halakhic* significance. The distinction is thus between an act that may or may not happen (*eino mitkavein*, acceptable) and an act one is consciously performing, even if unaware of its *halakhic* consequence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פַּרְפִּיסָא, הוֹאִיל וַאֲתָא לְיָדָן לֵימָא בֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא: הָיָה מוּנָּח עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע וְהִנִּיחוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי יְתֵידוֹת — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם תּוֹלֵשׁ. הָיָה מוּנָּח עַל גַּבֵּי יְתֵידוֹת וְהִנִּיחוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם נוֹטֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Since** the topic of **a perforated pot has come to our hands, let us say something with regard to it:** If **it** had been **placed on the ground and one** lifted it and **placed it on top of pegs** on Shabbat, **he is liable for** the labor of **detaching.** The roots of the plant could have protruded through the holes to draw sustenance from the ground, and when one lifts it he detaches it from that sustenance. Similarly, if **it** had been **placed on pegs and one placed it on the ground, he is liable for** the labor of **planting.**

Rav Pappa draws a further inference from Reish Lakish's ruling: *hai parfisa sharei letiltulei* — the *parfisa*, an object with dirt and growing plants in it, may also be carried (*tiltul*). The logic is that just as lifting a stone with grass does not constitute *tolesh*, lifting the *parfisa* from the ground does not either. Rav Kahana challenges this inference: the permission to lift the stone was granted specifically for the purpose of the bathroom (*l'tzorech*). One cannot extend that permission to carrying the *parfisa* for no particular need (*shelo l'tzorech*). There may well be a rabbinic prohibition against lifting such an object from the ground even if it does not constitute biblical *tolesh*; we allowed it only for the specific need of *kevod habriyot*.

Abaye then sets forth a more precise framework: if the *parfisa* was resting on the ground and one lifts it and places it on pegs — creating air space between the object and the earth — one is liable for *tolesh*. If it was resting on pegs and one places it on the ground, one is liable for *notei'a* (planting). Rashi and *Tosafot* both register difficulty with this ruling, pointing out that if these acts constitute full liability for *tolesh* and *notei'a*, it is unclear how the stone with grass was ever permitted. Both *rishonim* conclude that the *chiyuv* described here must be understood as rabbinic (*mid'rabbanan*) rather than biblical. This interpretation, while admittedly a stretch from the plain language, is the position of both Rashi and *Tosafot*.

One might note that the stone case and the *parfisa* case are arguably distinguishable on more fundamental grounds. Moss growing on a stone is not drawing nutrients from the earth through roots in any conventional sense; its connection to the ground through the stone is attenuated at best. By contrast, a *parfisa* — a container with soil and rooted plants actively growing in it — is more analogous to a genuine rooted plant, potentially receiving nutrients from the ground even when resting on it. This distinction might justify a real (not merely rabbinic) difference between the two cases, even if the Gemara itself equates them and the *rishonim* resolve the difficulty through the *mid'rabbanan* reading.

### Geonic Kapparot

Rashi, in connection with the *parfisa*, records a remarkable *teshuvat geonim*. The Geonim describe a practice in which containers are fashioned from palm leaves (*kapot tmarim*), filled with earth and fertilizer, and planted with legumes — one container for each child in the household — beginning approximately two weeks before Rosh Hashanah. The legumes sprout quickly in the moist soil. On *erev* Rosh Hashanah, each person takes the container designated for him or her, swings it seven times around his or her head, and recites *ze tachat ze ze chalifati v'zeh temurati* — this in place of this, this is my substitute. The container is then thrown into the river. This is apparently an early Geonic basis for *kapparot* — and, notably, the detail of casting the object into the river clearly anticipates the practice of *Tashlich*. The tradition achieves the symbolism of *kapparot* without involving any animal. How the practice evolved from this form into the later chicken-based custom is unclear, but the passage is of considerable historical and liturgical interest.

## Pottery & Witchcraft

### Pottery Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָסוּר לְקַנֵּחַ בְּחֶרֶס בְּשַׁבָּת. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם סַכָּנָה — אֲפִילּוּ בְּחוֹל נָמֵי! וְאֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם כְּשָׁפִים — אֲפִילּוּ בְּחוֹל נָמֵי לָא! וְאֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם הַשָּׁרַת נִימִין — דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין הוּא. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב נָתָן בַּר אוֹשַׁעְיָא: גַּבְרָא רַבָּה אֲמַר מִילְּתָא, נֵימָא בָּהּ טַעְמָא: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא בְּחוֹל — דְּאָסוּר, אֲבָל בְּשַׁבָּת — הוֹאִיל וְאִיכָּא תּוֹרַת כְּלִי עָלָיו, שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is prohibited to wipe with an earthenware shard on Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for that prohibition? **If you say** that it is **due to** the **danger** that he might injure himself with the sharp edges of the shard, it should be prohibited **also on weekdays. Rather,** it is **due to** the fact that it invites **witchcraft.** If so, he should **also not** do so **on weekdays. Rather,** the concern is **lest** he **remove hairs** with the earthenware shard. However, that is **an unintentional act,** which is permitted. **Rav Natan bar Oshaya said to** those who raised the question: **A great man said something, let us say a reason for it,** and explain Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement as follows: **It is not necessary** to say that it is **prohibited on a weekday** for the aforementioned reasons because he has the option of using a stone. **However, with regard to Shabbat** we would have said that **since** this shard **has the status of a utensil** and is not set-aside, he may **well** use it, as it is preferable to a stone, which is set-aside. Therefore, **he teaches us** that it is prohibited.

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *assur lekane'ach b'cheres b'Shabbat* — one may not wipe oneself with a piece of pottery on *Shabbat*. The Gemara explores the reasons. Is it because of physical danger — pottery shards can be sharp and injurious? If so, the prohibition should apply on weekdays as well, which Rabbi Yochanan does not seem to suggest. Is it because of *keshafim* — susceptibility to witchcraft? Again, if that were the reason, it should apply on weekdays too. The Gemara therefore proposes: *mishum hasharat nimin* — the concern is that the pottery may scrape off hairs from the body in the area being wiped, which would constitute *tolesh* or *gozez* on *Shabbat*. This is the concern that explains why the *shinuy* was required earlier in the discussion.

But why should this be a problem? It is *davar she'eino mitkavein* — an unintended act. Rav Natan bar Oshaya proposes a different reading entirely: since Rabbi Yochanan is a figure of great stature (*gavra raba*), if he ruled a thing forbidden, one must find a satisfying explanation (*neimru bah ta'ama*). Perhaps the ruling should be read not as *prohibiting* pottery use on *Shabbat*, but rather as saying that even on *Shabbat* — where one might think to prefer pottery because it has the status of a vessel (*torat kli*) and is therefore less problematic from a *muktzeh* standpoint — one should still not use it. *Lo mibaya b'chol d'assur*, it goes without saying that one should not use it on a weekday due to its various hazards; but one might reason that on *Shabbat*, avoiding *muktzeh* justifies choosing pottery over stones despite the risks. Rabbi Yochanan tells us: no. The hazards of pottery override any *muktzeh* consideration. Do not use it even on *Shabbat* to spare yourself the *muktzeh* concern.

This is an important ruling: one does not invoke *kevod habriyot* or *muktzeh* leniency in order to use a material that is itself hazardous. The hierarchy matters — avoiding *muktzeh* does not license endangering oneself.

### Witchcraft Practices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ מִשּׁוּם הַשָּׁרַת נִימִין, וְקַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָסוּר לְקַנֵּחַ בַּחֶרֶס בְּשַׁבָּת? אַלְמָא דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין — אָסוּר. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כִּסְתַם מִשְׁנָה. וּתְנַן: נָזִיר חוֹפֵף וּמְפַסְפֵּס, אֲבָל לֹא סוֹרֵק! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְרַב נָתָן בַּר אוֹשַׁעְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught** that Rabbi Yoḥanan ruled that it is prohibited **due to the removal of hairs, and he** raised **a difficulty** between **that** which **Rabbi Yoḥanan** said here and **that** which **Rabbi Yoḥanan** said elsewhere. **Did Rabbi Yoḥanan say it is forbidden to wipe with an earthenware shard on Shabbat? Apparently,** he holds that **an unintentional act is prohibited. Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan state** a principle: **The *halakha*** is ruled **in accordance with an unattributed mishna? And we learned** in a mishna: **A nazirite,** for whom it is prohibited to cut his hair, **may wash** his hair on a weekday with sand and natron **and separate** it with his fingers; **however, he may not comb** it, which would certainly pull out some hair. Apparently, the unintentional act of removing hair while shampooing is permitted. **Rather, it is clearly in accordance with** the explanation of **Rav Natan bar Oshaya.**

Rava offers yet another reading: Rabbi Yochanan's concern was indeed *hasharat nimin* — pulling out hairs — and he considered this a problem even though it is *eino mitkavein*. Rava notes, however, that this creates a contradiction within Rabbi Yochanan's own positions. Rabbi Yochanan rules elsewhere (*halacha ke-stam mishnah*) in accordance with an unattributed Mishnah stating that a *nazir* may run his fingers through his hair and even loosely brush it, but may not use a fine comb — because a comb will *certainly* pull out hairs (*psik reisha*), while the other methods will not necessarily do so. The Mishnah permits the less certain methods, meaning Rabbi Yochanan accepts that *eino mitkavein* which is not *psik reisha* is permitted. How then can he prohibit pottery wiping, which is merely *eino mitkavein* and not a certainty? The contradiction is genuine. The Gemara concludes: *ella mechvarta ke-Rav Natan bar Oshaya* — the correct explanation is Rav Natan bar Oshaya's. The prohibition on pottery on *Shabbat* is not about pulling out hairs; it is a ruling against using a dangerous material to circumvent a *muktzeh* concern.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי כְּשָׁפִים? כִּי הָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא הֲווֹ קָא אָזְלִי בְּאַרְבָּא, אֲמַרָה לְהוּ הָהִיא מַטְרוֹנִיתָא: אוֹתְבַן בַּהֲדַיְיכוּ! וְלָא אוֹתְבוּהָ. אֲמַרָה אִיהִי מִילְּתָא — אֲסַרְתַּהּ לְאַרְבָּא. אֲמַרוּ אִינְהוּ מִילְּתָא — שַׁרְיוּהָא. אֲמַרָה לְהוּ: מַאי אֶיעְבֵּיד לְכוּ? דְּלָא מִקַּנַּח לְכוּ בְּחַסְפָּא, וְלָא קְטִיל לְכוּ כִּינָּא אַמָּנַיְיכוּ, וְלָא שְׁלִיף לְכוּ יַרְקָא וַאֲכִיל לְכוּ מִכִּישָּׁא דְּאָסַר גִּינָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the **witchcraft** involved with wiping with an earthenware shard? The Gemara explains: It is **as that** which transpired when **Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna were going on a boat. A certain matron [*matronita*] said to them: Let me sit with you, and they did not let her sit. She said something,** an incantation of witchcraft, and **stopped the boat. They said something,** the Holy Name, and **freed it. She said to them: What will I do to you,** to enable me to harm you with witchcraft, **as you do not clean** yourselves **with an earthenware shard, and you do not kill lice on your garments, and you do not pull out a vegetable and eat it** before you untie **the bundle that was tied by the gardener?** This implies that all these actions carry with them the danger of witchcraft.

The Gemara closes the topic with the question of *keshafim*: *mai kshafim* — what is the actual concern about witchcraft? The answer is illustrated through a story. Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar Rav Huna were traveling by boat. A certain woman (*matrunita*) asked to join them; they declined. She recited an incantation and bound the boat so it could not move. They responded with a protective incantation — a *shem tahora*, a holy name according to Rashi — and freed the boat. The woman, frustrated, asked why she could not harm them. She identified three protective practices they maintained: they did not wipe themselves with pottery (*de-lo mekaneich lechu be-chaspa*); they did not kill lice on their garments (*ve-lo katlei lechu kina me-manaiku*); and they did not pull a vegetable from a bundle and eat it while the bundle remained tied (*ve-lo shaleich lechu yarka ve-achlei lechu me-kishra de-asar ginai* — they first undid the bundle before eating). Each of these three behaviors, the woman explains, makes a person susceptible to witchcraft. Because Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar Rav Huna avoided all three, she had no power over them.

## Conclusion: Summary of Principles

The discussion of pottery and its witchcraft-related concerns connects back to the broader topic of bathroom materials. The concern about *keshafim* associated with pottery explains why Rabbi Yochanan's prohibition applies even on weekdays at a baseline level — using pottery for this purpose carries independent risks regardless of *Shabbat*. The Gemara's analysis, as resolved through Rav Natan bar Oshaya, establishes that even where a material might seem preferable from a purely *muktzeh* standpoint, if it poses its own set of hazards — whether physical injury, *hasharat nimin*, or susceptibility to harm — the *halakha* does not permit its use simply to resolve a rabbinic concern. The lesson is one of proportionality: *kevod habriyot* and *muktzeh* leniency operate within limits, and the choice of materials for bathroom use must take account of all relevant considerations together.
