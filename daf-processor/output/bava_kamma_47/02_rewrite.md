# Bava Kamma 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pregnant Cow Damages

### Safek Pregnancy Intro

We open the fifth *perek* picking up at the bottom of 46b. The previous *perek* was focused heavily on *shor tam* and *shor mu'ad*; now we move into more general issues connected to the goring ox, as well as cases we will encounter in the upcoming *mishnayot*. The central concern of the first *mishnah* of this *perek* is *safek* — doubt. The case is one in which it is unknown whether a cow was pregnant at the time it was gored. Does the owner of the goring ox owe damages for the fetus? The position of Sumchos is to split the payment for the fetus, since it may or may not have been gored. The *chachamim*, by contrast, invoke *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'ayah* — the burden of proof rests on the claimant.

We are now dealing with the second case of the *mishnah*, which is less straightforward: the cow gored, and it is uncertain whether it was pregnant at the time. The *mishnah* states that one pays half damages from the cow and a quarter from the fetus — which at first glance appears to yield three-quarters total damages, a perplexing result.

### Abaye's Half Damages

Abaye explains the difficulty by positing that the fetus belongs to a different owner. Because of this ownership division, you cannot simply collect everything from the goring cow. You take half of what you should have been owed — half of the half-damages — from the goring ox, since that ox was definitively one of the perpetrators. As for the other half of the half-damages, if you knew for certain the fetus was part of the goring animal, you would collect that from the fetus. Since you do not know, you split it: you receive half of the half, which is a quarter.

Two important observations from Tosafot accompany this position. First, even if the fetus was indeed part of the cow, it presumably contributed only a small proportion of the total force of the goring. Tosafot notes that speaking of "half" is merely a representative number; the real principle is that the fetus's responsibility is proportional to its contribution, and since that is unknown, you receive only half of that proportional share. Second — and this will resurface later — even when there are multiple participants in causing damage, if you cannot collect from one party, you cannot transfer that uncollected portion onto the other. If, say, it is a ninety-ten split and the ten-percent party is unavailable, you may not collect more than your ninety percent from the other.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenges Abaye's reading directly. The *mishnah* says *chatzi nezek u-revi'a nezek* — a half and a quarter. Abaye's interpretation forces us to read "half" as meaning a quarter of the total, and "quarter" as meaning an eighth of the total. But that is not what the *mishnah* says. The *mishnah* did not say a quarter and an eighth; it said a half and a quarter.

### Same Owner Sequential

Rava therefore offers his own explanation. The cow and the fetus are owned by the same person — there is no separate ownership at all. So what is the problem? Why not simply collect everything from the cow? Rava's answer is that the *mishnah* is not describing simultaneous collection from two sources. Rather, it describes sequential collection rights: *ita le-parah mishtalem chatzi nezek mi-parah* — if the cow is available, you collect your full half-damages from the cow. The cow is definitively the *mazik*; you are entitled to collect completely from it. *Leita le-parah* — but if the cow is not available, whether because it died, fled, or is worthless, and all you have is the fetus, then since you do not know whether the fetus was part of the goring, you can only collect half of the half-damages — a quarter — from the fetus. This is a clean and straightforward reading: "half and quarter" refers not to simultaneous payments but to two alternate scenarios.

### Fetus Presence Doubt

The *gemara* then clarifies the underlying reason for this division: *ta'ama de-la yad'inan i hava valad bahadah ki nachah i lo* — the reason for the split is the uncertainty about whether the fetus was present together with the cow when it gored. But it follows from this that if it were absolutely clear the fetus was present during the goring, you would be entitled to collect your full half-damages from the fetus alone: *mishtalem kulei chatzi nezek meivlad*. Why not? It was part of the goring animal.

### Full Fetus Payment

The *gemara* notes that this last point aligns with Rava's own stated principle elsewhere: *parah shehizikah goveh meivladah* — if a pregnant cow gored, you may collect the entire damages from the fetus if there is sufficient value there to collect from. The same logic applies here: if it is known the fetus was present, you collect fully from the fetus; if not, you collect only half.

### Fetus Body Unity

The *gemara* then asks: *mai ta'ama?* — what is the reasoning behind this? The answer: *gufah hi* — it is all one body. The fetus is part of the mother's body.

Tosafot observes that the *gemara* appears to be reaching for the familiar phrase *ubar yerech imo* — the fetus is considered a limb of the mother — and discusses why it does not use that formulation, since the applicability of *ubar yerech imo* varies depending on context. But before arriving at Tosafot's analysis, it is worth pausing on what is at stake conceptually. What would the *halachah* be if we did *not* say *gufah hi*? Suppose instead we treated the fetus as a separate entity. Consider the case where two oxen gore a cow together, causing one hundred dollars of damage, each ox worth one hundred dollars, and one of the two oxen is unavailable. Could you collect the entire hundred dollars from the available ox? As we saw in yesterday's discussion: if the two oxen are owned by two different people, you divide responsibility — fifty dollars from Reuven, fifty from Shimon. If Shimon's ox is unavailable, you cannot collect the remaining fifty from Reuven. They are truly each liable only for their proportional share.

What if both oxen belong to the same person, Reuven — could you then collect the full amount from one? The *gemara*'s implicit assumption is still no: even then, each ox is only liable for its proportional portion, and if one is unavailable, you cannot recover the balance from the other. Therefore, the only reason Rava can say you are entitled to collect all of the half-damages from the fetus — when you know it was present — is because we do *not* treat the fetus as a separate animal at all. It is one body. And when part of one body dies or is removed, you are still collecting from different parts of the same animal, not from two separate *mazikim*. This is a meaningful chiddush: even under single ownership, two separate goring animals create proportional, non-transferable liability. Only the principle of *gufah hi* — treating them as one body — permits full recovery from one part of that body.

### Chicken Egg Contrast

The *gemara* now introduces a contrast. *Tarnegolet shehizikah einah govah mibeitzatah* — if a chicken caused damage, you cannot collect from the egg that was within it at the time of the damage. *Mai ta'ama? Pirsha b'alma hu* — it is merely like refuse; it is not considered part of the body.

This is interesting, because in other contexts the egg *is* considered part of the hen. Tosafot addresses this by distinguishing stages of development: while the egg is still forming within the hen, it is considered part of the hen — if the hen is a *treifah*, the egg is too. But once the egg is fully developed and merely awaiting laying, it is considered separate from the mother. At that final stage, it would not be classified as a *mazik* at all.

One might ask why we do not at least treat the egg as a partial *mazik* and collect proportionally from it. The likely answer is that a fully-formed egg is not a live being — it is not an independent entity in the way a fetus is. The fetus can be observed as its own creature within the mother; an egg at the stage of ready-to-be-laid is something categorically more distinct and inert. A fetus, even if born dead or miscarried, is recognizably its own form; the egg, perhaps because it has not been fertilized and hatched, does not achieve that independent identity. As one might note in passing: the question of when something counts as "separate" and "alive" enough to be an independent *mazik* is itself a genuinely complex one that the *gemara* does not fully resolve.

### Valuation Method

Rava now introduces a second principle: *ein shamin leparah bifnei atzmah ulevlad bifnei atzmo ela shamin levlad al gav parah* — you do not assess the cow independently and the fetus independently; rather, you assess the fetus as part of the cow. This principle is relevant not only here but will resurface when we later encounter the *sugya* of *dmei vladot*, the assessment of damages when a woman is struck and miscarries.

The question being addressed is: when an ox gores a pregnant cow and causes a miscarriage, how do we calculate the damages owed for the lost fetus? One might think to assess separately: how much is this fetus worth on its own? How much for a cow about to give birth in two weeks? Rava rules this is incorrect. Instead, we look at the cow as a whole: how much would you pay for this pregnant cow, and how much is it worth now, having lost its fetus? The fetus is evaluated as a component of the pregnant cow, not as an independent item.

The reason given is *nimtza attah machlish et hamazik* — this prevents unfairly burdening the *mazik*. The point is not merely that he would pay more with separate assessment, but that it would be fundamentally unfair, because what he actually damaged was a pregnant cow. You assess what he damaged, not its constituent parts. This is analogous to someone who destroys a field: you do not say "he destroyed one pound of grain worth such-and-such," you say "how much was the field worth before, and how much is it worth now?" The damaged object is the field as a whole, not its component grain.

## Pregnant Cow Valuation

### Parallel Damage Cases

The *gemara* supports this principle with parallel cases. *Vechein attah motzei bekotei'a yad avdo shel chaveiro* — the same approach applies if one cuts off the arm of another's slave. Rashi explains that the alternative — assessing the arm independently — would mean asking how much someone would demand to be paid to allow his slave's arm to be cut off. Obviously no one would agree to such a thing for any ordinary market price; the figure would be artificially inflated and meaningless as a legal metric. The correct assessment is: how much was the slave worth before, and how much is he worth now?

Similarly: *u'vi'er b'sadeh acheir, shamin otah al gav sadeh acheir* — if an animal enters a field and destroys grain, you assess the damage as a field before and after, not as the raw value of however many pounds of grain were consumed. The damaged object is the field. One might raise the question of *kol ha'omed liktzor k'katzur dami* — grain that is ready to be harvested might, at that late stage, be valued more like harvested grain than like field. That is a legitimate consideration and there likely is a point at which the grain has effectively separated in status from the field. But as a general principle, you look at the whole object that was damaged.

### Unified Object Principle

Rav Adda bar Abba poses the question to Rav Ashi: if the law is simply that it is fairer to assess the fetus as part of the cow, that is one thing — but why *must* we do it this way? Why not assess the fetus independently and let the *mazik* absorb the higher liability?

The answer: *mishum d'amar lei parah me'ubertah aziktech, parah me'ubertah shamna lach* — because the *mazik* can say: "I damaged a pregnant cow; assess for me a pregnant cow." What he damaged was the pregnant cow as a single object. You cannot take that object and artificially separate it into components that he never engaged with as separate items. The pregnant cow is the object of the tort, and it must be assessed as such.

### Separate Valuation Problem

This creates a practical challenge. If the cow and fetus are owned by different people — say, the cow belongs to one person and the fetus has been sold or transferred to another — we must determine how to divide the total assessed damage between the two owners. The intuitive approach would be to ask: what would the cow be worth without the fetus? The difference between that figure and the total value of the pregnant cow represents the fetus's contribution, and that amount goes to the owner of the fetus.

### Fatness Analysis

But Rashi identifies a complication. A pregnant cow may have gained weight and become *fatma* — fattier and more valuable — during the pregnancy. Does that additional value belong to the cow's owner or to the fetus's owner? *Mah she'shamnu'u she'hayah yafeh mipnei shumno, havei d'ba'al parah, d'velad machmato lo ati shumno* — the additional weight and fat belong to the cow's owner, because the weight gain, whatever its biological cause, accrues to the cow's body, not the fetus's. So when you try to imagine what the cow would be worth without the fetus, you imagine it without the fetus but *with* its current weight — not as it was before the pregnancy began.

### Bulk Appearance

Rav Acha raises a further nuance: *nafcha mai?* — what about the appearance of bulk? Even if the weight itself belongs to the cow, the visual volume of the cow — the fact that it looks larger — may be attributable to the fetus. Buyers may pay more for an animal that appears physically larger, regardless of actual weight. Does that increment in price go to the cow owner or the fetus owner?

### Bulk to Cow

Rav Papa holds that the bulk increment goes entirely to the owner of the cow. One would assess what the cow would be worth with its current weight *and* its current volume, as though no fetus were present — and that entire figure belongs to the cow owner.

### Split Bulk Value

Rav Acha brei deRav Ika disagrees: *cholkin* — you split the increment attributable to the appearance of bulk, since that visual enlargement is genuinely connected to the fetus's presence and the fetus owner has some claim to it.

### Halacha Split

*Vehilchata cholkin* — the *halachah* follows Rav Acha brei deRav Ika: the bulk-value increment is split between the two owners. With that ruling, the discussion of the pregnant cow concludes.

## Potter's Pots Case

### Pots Without Permission

We now make a significant shift. The next *mishnah* moves beyond goring cows into a broader set of questions about liability for damage that occurs in someone else's domain. *Hakadar shehichnis kedeirotav lachatzar ba'al habayit* — a potter who brought his pots into the yard of the homeowner *shelo birshut* — without permission. He is a door-to-door salesman who simply walked in. *Veshivra behemto shel ba'al habayit* — and the homeowner's animal broke the pots. *Patur* — the homeowner is exempt. This is effectively a case of *regel*, and *regel* is only *chayav* in *reshut hanizak*. Since the potter entered without permission, this is *reshut hamazik*, and there is no liability: *shurcha birshuti mai ba'ei* — what right did your pots have to be on my property?

### Animal Injury Liability

*Ve'im huzka bahen ba'al hakedeirot chayav* — if the homeowner's animal slipped on the pots and injured itself, the potter is liable. This is akin to a *bor*: he left his pots — his hazard — in a place where he had no right to leave them, and the homeowner's animal was damaged as a result.

### Permission Implies Liability

*Ve'im hichnis birshut* — but if the homeowner explicitly gave permission for the potter to enter, then *ba'al hachatzer chayav* — the homeowner is liable if his animal then tramples and breaks the pots.

### Host Liability Puzzle

Why is the homeowner liable? The most natural answer would be *regel* — his animal trampled the pots. But *regel* is only *chayav* in *reshut hanizak*. The potter's pots are in the homeowner's yard; it is at most a shared space. Can the homeowner's yard become *reshut hanizak* merely because he invited the potter in? That seems strained. The *gemara* will resolve this differently: when the homeowner says "you may come in," he is implicitly accepting responsibility for anything that happens to his guest's property. It is not a matter of domain-status; it is a matter of implicit *kibul* — acceptance of liability. As will become clear, it is as though the homeowner has declared himself an insurer of his guest's belongings.

## Fruit and Oxen Cases

### Fruit Without Permission

The *mishnah* continues with parallel cases. *Hichnis perotav lechatzer ba'al habayit shelo birshut* — the guest brings fruit into the homeowner's yard without permission. *Ve'achaltan behemato shel ba'al habayit* — the homeowner's animal eats it. *Patur* — the homeowner is exempt. Again, this is *reshut hamazik*; who gave you permission to leave fruit in my yard?

### Host Liable Shen

*Ve'im hichnis birshut* — if the homeowner gave permission and the animal then eats the fruit, *ba'al hachatzer chayav* — the homeowner is liable. This is a case of *shen*, but again, not technically *reshut hanizak* — the liability is rooted in the homeowner's implicit acceptance of responsibility upon granting permission.

### Ox Without Permission

*Hichnis shoro lechatzer ba'al habayit shelo birshut* — the guest brings his ox into the homeowner's yard without permission. *Unegacho shoro shel ba'al habayit* — the homeowner's ox gores the guest's ox, *o shenashcho kalbo shel ba'al habayit* — or the homeowner's dog bites it. *Patur* — the homeowner is exempt. This is *keren*, but even *keren*, which is ordinarily *chayav* regardless of domain, is exempt when the victim's animal was present *shelo birshut*: *shurcha birshuti mai ba'ei*.

### Well Contamination

The *mishnah* then considers the reverse. If the guest's ox gores the homeowner's ox, the guest is obviously *chayav* — it is *keren* in the homeowner's domain. *Nafal leboro* — if the guest's ox falls into the homeowner's well and *hev'ish meimav* — contaminates the well water with the stench of the animal carcass — the guest is also *chayav*. The guest's ox has no right to be here; whatever damage flows from its unauthorized presence falls on the guest.

### Kofer Question

*Hayah aviv o beno letocho* — if the homeowner's father or son was in the well and was killed by the falling ox, *meshallem et hakofer* — the guest pays *kofer*. A difficulty immediately arises: *kofer* is normally the liability of a *mu'ad*, a forewarned animal. Why is *kofer* owed here? This requires further examination, particularly in light of the position that when an animal gores in *chatzer hanizak*, it may owe full damages even as a *tam* — and whether that elevated liability extends to *kofer* is a live question that the *gemara* will need to address.

### Host Liable Keren

*Ve'im hichnis birshut* — if the homeowner gave the guest permission to bring his ox in, and then the homeowner's ox gores the guest's ox — *ba'al hachatzer chayav*. Once the ground of *shelo birshut* is removed, the homeowner's ox goring the guest's ox is straightforward *keren*, and of course the homeowner is *chayav*.

### Rabbi Disagrees

*Rabbi omer bechullan eino chayav ad sheyekabbel alav lishmor* — Rabbi holds that in all these cases, the homeowner is not liable unless he explicitly declares that he will guard and take responsibility for the guest's property. Granting mere permission to enter does not, for Rabbi, constitute an implicit acceptance of liability. Without a verbal declaration of responsibility, *shen* and *regel* remain exempt because it is not *reshut hanizak*, and there is nothing else to generate liability.

It seems, then, that the *tanna kamma* holds that granting permission constitutes implicit *kibul*, and Rabbi disagrees. Rabbi's position equally implies the converse: even the guest is not implicitly accepting liability for the host's property simply by virtue of having been granted permission. The question of what exactly "permission" generates — for whom, and in which direction — is what the *gemara* will now analyze.

## Implicit Responsibility

### Permission Reversal

The *gemara* examines the structure of the *mishnah*. *Ta'ama deshelo birshut* — when the guest is there without permission, the homeowner is exempt for damage to the guest's pots, and the guest is liable if the homeowner's animal is injured by the pots. *Ha birshut* — but if there *is* permission, the *gemara* infers, *lo mechayev ba'al hakedeirah benizkei behemto d'ba'al hachatzer* — the potter would not be liable if the homeowner's animal were injured by the pots. We do not say that because the homeowner granted the guest permission, the guest implicitly accepted liability for any damage to the homeowner. Nor — *velo amrinan kibulei kibal ba'al hakedeirah netirosa d'behemto d'ba'al hachatzer* — do we say that the guest's receipt of kindness from the host generates some reciprocal obligation to protect the host from harm.

The *gemara* notes the logical structure. When the guest has no permission and places his pots, he has created a *bor shelo birshut* — an unauthorized hazard — and is liable for the animal that slips on it. Once he has permission, his pots are a *bor birshut* — a hazard he was authorized to create — and he is not liable if the animal slips on them. But one might have thought: the homeowner was gracious enough to let the guest in; perhaps that reception generates a reciprocal implicit acceptance of liability. The *gemara* rejects this.

*Mani? Rabbi hi, de'amar kol bistama lo kibal alav netirosa* — this is Rabbi's position: there is no implicit acceptance of responsibility in either direction. Granting permission to enter says nothing about who accepts liability for damage.

But then, says the *gemara*, examine the end of the *mishnah*: *hichnis birshut, ba'al hachatzer chayav* — when the homeowner grants permission and the animal damages the guest's pots, the homeowner *is* liable. So the homeowner is impliedly accepting responsibility. *Atya k'Rabbanan, d'amri bistama nami kibulei kibal alav netirosa* — this accords with the Rabbis, who hold that granting permission to enter constitutes an implicit acceptance of liability for what happens to the guest's property. The *mishnah*'s *reisha* and *seifa* thus reflect different positions: where the homeowner is not impliedly liable, that is Rabbi; where he is, that is the Rabbis.

Rav Zeira observes: *man deshuna zo lo shuna zo* — whoever taught one clause did not teach the other; these positions are simply inconsistent and come from different authorities. But Rava offers a more coherent account: *lo kula Rabbanan hi* — the entire *mishnah*, except for Rabbi's explicit statement, reflects the Rabbanan's view. *Uvireshut, shemirat kedeirot kibal alav bechatzer* — when I invite you in, I am implicitly accepting custodial responsibility for your pots. *Va'afilu nishberu beruach* — even if they broke due to wind, with no action of mine. This is not incidental. If you are a guest in my home with permission, I am the host, and the role of host carries with it general responsibility for the wellbeing of the guest's belongings. It is not that a specific tort was committed; it is that I have taken on the position of the responsible party.

### Host-Guest Asymmetry

This generates the obvious question: why does the host accept implicit liability for the guest, but the guest does not implicitly accept liability for the host? If my granting you permission creates my implied responsibility toward you, why doesn't your receiving that permission create your implied responsibility toward me?

The answer lies in the fundamental asymmetry of the host-guest relationship. When the homeowner extends an invitation, he is, by the nature of hospitality, taking on the role of the responsible party. This is his domain, his property; welcoming someone in means placing yourself in the position of the one who oversees and bears responsibility for what occurs. The guest's role is not to take responsibility for the host — the guest is the beneficiary of the hospitality, and the host is the protector. The logic of the institution runs in one direction only.

One might push back and say this analysis fits true hospitality, but not every case of "permission" is hospitality in that sense. A door-to-door salesman who is allowed to step in with his wares is not quite a guest in the same sense as a person who is welcomed into one's home. The salesman brings his goods into your space for commercial purposes. The moral intuitions of host responsibility may not map cleanly onto every scenario involving granted permission. This is a genuinely interesting question that the *gemara* implicitly leaves open.

### Accident vs. Eating

The *gemara* introduces an important distinction. *Lo shanu* — the rule that the guest is liable when the host's animal is damaged by the guest's objects applies specifically when *shehuchleka bahen* — the animal slipped on them. *Aval achla patur* — but if the animal ate the fruit and became ill, the guest is exempt.

This creates a striking asymmetry. In *reshut harabbim*: if I leave fruit in the street and an animal slips on it, that is a *bor* and I am *chayav*. But if the animal eats the fruit and becomes sick — is that also a *bor*? In a classic *bor*, the damage occurs because the victim encounters the hazard through inattention or accident. Here, the animal actively sees the fruit and consciously consumes it. The victim is not passively injured; it participates in its own injury. Even if one might fault me for leaving the fruit there, the animal's conscious ingestion introduces a break in the causal chain that makes it very difficult to classify this as a *bor*. It becomes something closer to *grama* — indirect causation — for which one is exempt.

### Active Participation

Tosafot addresses this directly. One might be tempted to say the animal is exempt on grounds that it is unusual (*lo shechich*) for an animal to eat the fruit and injure itself — as though it were an unlikely occurrence. But Tosafot rejects that explanation; the *gemara* makes clear elsewhere that an animal eating fruit is an entirely expected event. Tosafot instead explains: *keivan shebimechaven mevi alav davar shemaziko* — the animal knowingly brings upon itself the thing that injures it. It is a form of *pi hanizak* — active participation in one's own damage. When the victim is actively complicit in the harm, you cannot make the depositor of the fruit responsible. The *avot nezikin* presuppose a structure of actor and passive victim; when the "victim" consciously acts in a way that produces the harm, the classic framework no longer applies.

This is not limited to a narrow technical point. The broader principle that Tosafot articulates is that all *avot nezikin* require a clear distinction between the perpetrator and the one upon whom the damage was perpetrated. When the damaged party is itself the agent of the damage — even if another party created the conditions — the liability framework breaks down. At most, one might be *chayav bidinei shamayim* — morally culpable — but exempt in human courts.

### Sheshet Challenge

Rav Sheshet objects: *ki nayim veshachiv Rav amrah leha shemeitata* — Rav must have been half-asleep when he said this ruling. The *gemara* brings a *beraita*: *hanosein sam hamaves lifnei behemas chaveiro* — one who places poison in front of another's animal is *patur midinei adam vechayav bidinei shamayim* — exempt in court but liable in heaven. Rav Sheshet reasons: since the *beraita* addresses poison and exempts the depositor, it implies that in the case of fruit — which an animal would naturally eat — you *should* be *chayav* in human courts. Why would Rav rule otherwise?

### Self-Inflicted Damage

The *gemara* resolves the challenge. *Sam hamaves hu, delo avida de'achla* — poison is a case where the animal would not normally eat it; normal animals avoid poison. That is precisely why the *beraita* must tell us you are still *chayav bidinei shamayim* even though the animal consumed something abnormal. But fruit, which an animal would naturally eat — surely you would be *chayav* there. The *gemara* responds: *hu hadin afilu peiros nami patur midinei adam* — the same exemption applies to fruit. You are exempt in human courts in both cases. The reason the *beraita* chose to illustrate with poison is that it carries the more surprising teaching: even with poison, where the animal is arguably even more responsible for eating something it should have recognized as dangerous, there remains *chiyuv bidinei shamayim*. This tells us that moral liability persists even in extreme cases. But in both cases — poison and fruit alike — the depositor is *patur midinei adam* because the animal brought the harm upon itself.

An alternative reading is offered as well: *ve'iba'is eima sam hamaves nami ba'afarzata dehainu peiros* — "poison" in the *beraita* might refer to a particular type of grass that is toxic but which animals would naturally eat without recognizing as harmful. In that reading, both cases involve something the animal would plausibly consume, and both are exempt in human courts for the same reason: the animal, by consuming it, was the proximate cause of its own injury.

## Grain Mill Entry

### No Permission Entry

The *gemara* introduces a further case to probe these questions. *Ha'ishah shenichnesah litchon chitin etzel ba'al habayit* — a woman entered a homeowner's mill to grind her grain. Tosafot notes that the novelty here is precisely that one might have assumed a customer has implicit permission to enter a place of business; the *gemara* is telling us that even a customer must obtain explicit permission. She entered *shelo birshut*. *Ve'achaltan behemato shel ba'al habayit* — the homeowner's animal ate the grain. *Patur* — the homeowner is exempt, because she was there without permission.

### Injury Liability Same

*Im huzak* — if the animal was injured by the grain, she is liable. This is the direct parallel to the pot-slipping case. The question arises: does *huzak* here mean the animal slipped on the grain (the *bor* scenario), or does it mean the animal ate the grain and became ill? The *gemara* notes that the word *bahen* — "by them" — was used in the pot case (explicitly indicating slipping), whereas in this case the text simply says *huzak* without specifying *bahen*. This suggests the case here might involve eating and injury from consumption, which would make the woman liable despite the general principle of *pi hanizak*.

### Rava Clarifies

Rava clarifies: the question of permission is determinative. The two scenarios — *shelo birshut* and *birshut* — are governed by entirely different logics and cannot be compared to one another. *B'reshut shelo birshut ka ramit* — you are conflating a case of permission with a case of no permission. The principles that operate in one setting do not transfer to the other.

### General Responsibility

*Birshut* — when permission has been granted, *shemirat shoro kibal alav* — the homeowner has accepted general custodial responsibility for the guest's animal. *Va'afilu chanak et atzmo* — even if the ox strangles itself. Whatever happens to the guest's animal while it is on the premises, having been welcomed in, falls within the scope of the homeowner's implicit acceptance of liability. So the case of the ox eating grain and dying in the *birshut* scenario is not an instance of *pi hanizak* canceling liability; rather, the homeowner has independently accepted liability for everything, including harms that the animal brought upon itself. The two legal logics operate independently and must not be confused.

## Third-Party Liability

### Third-Party Question

*Iba'ya lehu* — the *gemara* now raises an unresolved question. When the homeowner has accepted responsibility — implicit or explicit — for the guest's belongings, does that acceptance extend to damage caused by a third party? *Dinafshei hu dekibel alei o dilma afilu netiruta de'alma kibel alei* — does the homeowner accept liability only for things that his own property or domain causes, or does he accept responsibility even for damage inflicted by a third party's animal that happens to enter his yard?

### Third-Party Ox Case

The concrete scenario: a guest brings fruit into the homeowner's yard *birshut* — with permission. A third party's ox then enters and eats the fruit. Is the homeowner liable to reimburse the guest for the loss caused by this third party?

### Borrowed Domain

A *beraita* is cited: *hichnis peirotav lechatzer ba'al habayit shelo birshut* — fruit brought without permission, eaten by an ox from elsewhere — the homeowner is exempt. *Ve'im hichnis birshut* — with permission — *chayav*. The question is who is *chayav* and who is *patur*. One possibility is that we are speaking of the homeowner being liable for the third party's act. Another possibility is that we are speaking of whether the third party — Reuven, the owner of the ox that ate the fruit — is directly *chayav*.

### Third Party Liable

The *gemara* clarifies: if the homeowner gave permission for the fruit to be there, then the fruit has a *reshut* to be there. When Reuven's ox comes and eats it, that is *shen birshut hanizak* — the *shen* damage occurred in the domain of the victim, because the guest, having been granted permission, possesses a temporary *reshut* in that space. *Shen* is *chayav birshut hanizak*, and therefore Reuven is directly *chayav* to the guest.

### No Permission Exempt

*Shelo birshut* — if the fruit was there without permission, it has no right to be there. Reuven's ox eating it is effectively *shen birshut harabbim* — eating in a domain where the victim had no standing — and *beshen birshut harabbim paturah*. Reuven is exempt. So the question of whether the fruit had permission determines whether the direct tortfeasor — Reuven — is liable.

### Host Reimbursement

But this still leaves open the original question: even in the *birshut* case, where Reuven is directly liable, does the homeowner *also* accept liability toward the guest for third-party damage — such that the guest could collect from the homeowner instead of, or in addition to, pursuing Reuven? Does the homeowner's implicit acceptance of responsibility when welcoming the guest in extend to covering losses caused by outside parties whose actions the homeowner did not control?

This question remains unresolved — *teiku* — and we will continue examining it in the next session.
