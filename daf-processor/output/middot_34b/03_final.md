# Middot 34b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Parah Adumah Bridge

### Opening Review

We pick up at the bottom of 34a in *Middot*, having completed our treatment of the gates of *Har HaBayit* proper in the previous session. The mishnah now turns to the gates of the *Azara*, and before proceeding, a brief note from the prior discussion bears revisiting.

### Kidron Valley Bridge

The mishnah had mentioned that the eastern gate of *Har HaBayit* was used to access *Har HaZeitim* — the Mount of Olives — for the *Parah Adumah* ritual. This raises an immediate geographical difficulty: the east opens directly onto the Kidron Valley, which drops away steeply, making regular access essentially impossible. If so, how did they actually get across to *Har HaZeitim*?

The answer, as the *Mishnah* in *Parah* records explicitly, is that they built a bridge — *keves hayu lo* — spanning the Kidron Valley, connecting *Har HaBayit* to *Har HaZeitim*. Whether this was a permanent stone structure or a more temporary construction is not entirely clear from the sources. The description in *Parah* gives the impression of something substantial, perhaps great stone vaults — the kind of feat of engineering the Romans were particularly renowned for, capable of beginning a bridge from one bank alone and extending it across. Whatever its precise form, the bridge was the means by which the *Parah Adumah* procession crossed.

### Tumah & Drainage

The bridge's design was also specifically engineered to avoid *tumah*, a consideration the *Mishnah* in *Parah* addresses in some detail. The concern was not merely practical access but ritual purity: those participating in the *Parah Adumah* ceremony had to be protected from any inadvertent contact with sources of impurity along the route.

One further note about the Kidron Valley: beyond serving as the geographical barrier that necessitated the bridge, it also functioned as the drainage outlet for the *Beit HaMikdash* — the channel into which the blood and other byproducts of the *avodah* would flow out of the *Mikdash* precincts.

## Shiva Shearim Azara

### Seven Gates Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁבְעָה שְׁעָרִים הָיוּ בָעֲזָרָה, שְׁלשָׁה בַצָּפוֹן וּשְׁלשָׁה בַדָּרוֹם וְאֶחָד בַּמִּזְרָח.
>
> **Translation:** There were seven gates in the courtyard: three in the north and three in the south and one in the east.

The mishnah states: *shiva shearim hayu ba-azara* — there were seven gates in the *Azara*. This subject had already been touched upon when learning *Tamid*, in the context of the *Kohanim*'s movements through the complex. The distribution was *shlosha ba-tzafon u-shlosha ba-darom* — three in the north and three in the south — *ve-echad ba-mizrach*, and one in the east.

### Sha'ar HaDelek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבַּדָּרוֹם, שַׁעַר הַדֶּלֶק. שֵׁנִי לוֹ, שַׁעַר הַבְּכוֹרוֹת. שְׁלִישִׁי לוֹ, שַׁעַר הַמָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** In the south: the Gate of Kindling, and next to it the Gate of the First-borns, and then the Water Gate.

The southern gates are enumerated first. The first is *Sha'ar HaDelek* — literally, the Fuel Gate. *Delek* means fuel or combustible material, and the obvious implication is that this gate served as the delivery entrance for firewood and other materials needed for the *mizbe'ach*. One would not transport loads of wood through the main entrance; this was effectively the service entrance at the back.

What is striking, however, is that looking at the diagrams of the *Beit HaMikdash*, this gate appears to have had an opening only to the outside, with no corresponding opening into the *Azara* proper. If so, perhaps the wood was stored here temporarily and then brought in through a different gate — possibly via the *Ezrat Nashim* or some other route — when needed. This raises the broader question about the *lishchot* generally: the *Gemara* elsewhere establishes that the *kedushah* of any chamber was determined by the location of its opening. If these southern chambers opened only outward, they would be *chol*, non-sanctified — yet the presence of *Levi'im* standing guard on the outside seems to imply they were *kodesh*. The *mefarshim* do not appear to address this directly, and it warrants further investigation.

It is also possible that *Sha'ar HaDelek* was used for oil deliveries as well, since oil was required for the *menorah*, though the term *delek* would be an unusual way to describe lamp oil.

### Sha'ar HaBechoros

The second southern gate is given in many textual variants as *Sha'ar HaBechoros* — the Gate of the Firstborns — rather than simply *Sha'ar HaKorban*. This makes good sense functionally: rather than requiring someone who had arrived with their *bechor* to wait in the main lines, this gate served as a kind of drop-off point. Much as one might deposit books at a library return slot, a person could bring his *bechor* here and continue on, with the *Kohanim* collecting them at the appropriate time.

There is an additional reason this gate was placed in the south. *Bechoros* are *kodashim kalim*, which do not require slaughter in the north — they could be brought in the south, near the *mizbe'ach*. One might ask why this distinction was needed to separate *bechoros* from other *shelamim*-class offerings. The answer may be that since the *Kohen* eats the *bechor* — as he does with *kodshei kodashim* — there was concern about confusion, even though *bechoros* may be eaten throughout *Yerushalayim*, and so it was specifically designated to the south.

### Sha'ar HaMayim

The third southern gate was *Sha'ar HaMayim* — the Gate of Water — named for its role in the *Nisuch HaMayim* ritual on *Sukkot*. The water drawn for the libation would be brought in through this gate and then conveyed to the *mizbe'ach*. This implies, as with the other gates, that there must have been an opening on both sides — one facing outward for bringing the water in, and one facing the *Azara* to bring it forward to the *mizbe'ach*. Whether this was a full secondary opening or something more like a small door is not entirely clear; the *Gemara* makes much of where these openings were located, and the precise configuration remains somewhat uncertain.

### Sha'ar Nikanor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבַּמִּזְרָח, שַׁעַר נִקָּנוֹר, וּשְׁתֵּי לְשָׁכוֹת הָיוּ לוֹ, אַחַת מִימִינוֹ וְאַחַת מִשְּׂמֹאלוֹ, אַחַת לִשְׁכַּת פִּנְחָס הַמַּלְבִּישׁ, וְאַחַת לִשְׁכַּת עוֹשֵׂי חֲבִתִּין:
>
> **Translation:** In the east: the Gate of Nicanor. It had two chambers, one on its right and one on its left. One was the chamber of Pinchas the dresser and one the other the chamber of the griddle cake makers.

Moving to the east, the lone eastern gate was *Sha'ar Nikanor*. This was the primary entrance from the *Ezrat Nashim* into the *Azara* proper — the main door through which all *Yisrael* would pass when approaching the *avodah*. If the *Ezrat Nashim* served as the waiting area or anteroom, *Sha'ar Nikanor* was the threshold into the sacred precincts themselves.

### Nikanor's Doors

The gate was named for the man who brought its doors from Alexandria. The *Gemara* tells that *Nikanor* was transporting these great brass doors by sea when a severe storm arose, threatening to sink the ship. In an effort to lighten the load, one of the doors was thrown overboard. When the storm continued, *Nikanor* declared that they should throw him in along with the remaining door — at which point the sea calmed. When the ship arrived at port, the door that had been cast overboard was found attached to the hull of the ship, having arrived miraculously. The gate was thereafter named in his honor.

### Nikanor Chambers

Off the sides of *Sha'ar Nikanor* were two chambers — *u-shtei lishchot hayu lo*, one on each side. One was called *lishkat Pinchas ha-malbish*, the chamber of *Pinchas* the dresser — the individual who assisted the *Kohanim* in donning and removing their priestly garments. We had noted elsewhere that this function was associated with the *Lishkat HaGazit*, but here *Pinchas* may have used this chamber as a base of operations or storage. The other chamber was *lishkat osei chavitin* — the chamber of those who prepared the *chavitin*, the griddle-offering of the *Kohen Gadol*.

### Sha'ar Nitzotz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁבַּצָּפוֹן, שַׁעַר הַנִּיצוֹץ. וּכְמִין אַכְסַדְרָה הָיָה, וַעֲלִיָּה בְנוּיָה עַל גַּבָּיו, שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים שׁוֹמְרִים מִלְמַעְלָן וְהַלְוִיִּם מִלְּמַטָּן, וּפֶתַח הָיָה לוֹ לַחֵיל. שֵׁנִי לוֹ, שַׁעַר הַקָּרְבָּן. שְׁלִישִׁי לוֹ, בֵּית הַמּוֹקֵד:
>
> **Translation:** On the north was the Gate of the Sparks which was shaped like a portico. It had an upper chamber built on it, and the priests used to keep watch above and the Levites below, and it had a door opening into the Hel. Next to it was the Gate of the Sacrifice and next to that the fire chamber.

The northern gates are enumerated next, beginning from the west side moving eastward. The first is *Sha'ar Nitzotz* — the Gate of the Spark. Its name likely relates to fire: one explanation is that this was the gate through which fire was brought to supplement what was on the *mizbe'ach*, since the requirement is to bring one's own fire and not merely rely on what is already burning. The *mishnah* describes it as being *ke-min achsadra* — like a portico — with two columns supporting a roof or some kind of overhead structure, with a balcony or platform above it. The *Kohanim* would watch from above and the *Levi'im* below.

This description illuminates something worth noting across all the diagrams of the *Beit HaMikdash*: most of them show what appear to be columns at the gate structures. It is plausible that this *achsadra* configuration, highlighted here in connection with *Sha'ar Nitzotz*, was in fact a feature common to many or all of the gates, and those columns in the diagrams represent precisely this kind of portico construction.

### Opening to Chel

The *mishnah* adds a detail about *Sha'ar Nitzotz* that is not mentioned for any of the other gates: *u-pesach hayah lo la-chel* — it had a specific opening into the *chel*. The *chel* was the buffer zone between the *soreg* fence and the *Azara* proper — approximately ten *amot* wide, roughly fifteen feet — a transitional space that was itself restricted. Non-Jews, it should be noted, were forbidden from entering even the *chel*, let alone the *Azara*. The distinction between those permitted in the *chel* and those permitted in the *Ezrat Nashim* may hinge on categories such as *tevul yom* and *temei meit*.

What is puzzling is that this opening into the *chel* is mentioned only here and not for the other gates — yet all of those gates presumably also opened to the outside in some fashion, as that was their functional purpose. The *mefarshim* consulted did not resolve this question satisfactorily, and it remains open.

## Beit HaMoked Chambers

### Four Chambers Layout

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַרְבַּע לְשָׁכוֹת הָיוּ בְּבֵית הַמּוֹקֵד, כְּקִיטוֹנוֹת פְּתוּחוֹת לִטְרַקְלִין, שְׁתַּיִם בַּקֹּדֶשׁ וּשְׁתַּיִם בַּחֹל, וְרָאשֵׁי פִסְפָּסִין מַבְדִּילִין בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ לַחֹל. וּמֶה הָיוּ מְשַׁמְּשׁוֹת. מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית, הִיא הָיְתָה לִשְׁכַּת טְלָאֵי קָרְבָּן. דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית, הִיא הָיְתָה לִשְׁכַּת עוֹשֵׂי לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים. מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, בָּהּ גָּנְזוּ בְנֵי חַשְׁמוֹנַאי אֶת אַבְנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ שֶׁשִּׁקְּצוּם מַלְכֵי יָוָן. צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, בָּהּ יוֹרְדִים לְבֵית הַטְּבִילָה:
>
> **Translation:** There were four chambers inside the fire chamber, like sleeping chambers opening into a hall, two in sacred ground and two in non-holy, and there was a row of mosaic stones separating the holy from the non-holy. For what were they used? The one on the southwest was the chamber of sacrificial lambs, The one on the southeast was the chamber of the showbread. In the one to the northeast the Hasmoneans deposited the stones of the altar which the kings of Greece had defiled. Through the one on the northwest they used to go down to the bathing place.

*Arba lishchot hayu b'Beit HaMoked* — there were four chambers in the *Beit HaMoked*, arranged like small rooms opening off a great hall, *ke-kitonot ha-ptuchot la-traklin*. The *Beit HaMoked* was the central structure where the *Kohanim* would sleep, and it straddled the boundary between the *Azara* proper and the outside space, with two chambers in the sanctified area and two in the non-sanctified.

### Kodesh/Chol Division

The mishnah states: *shtayim be-kodesh u-shtayim be-chol* — two in the *kodesh* and two in the *chol*. This division arose from the fact that the *Beit HaMoked* had openings on both sides — one into the *Azara* and one to the outside — and therefore the line of sanctity passed through the structure itself. What marked that line? *Ve-roshei pispasin mavdil bein kodesh le-chol* — the tops of the *pispasin* distinguished between the *kodesh* and the *chol*.

The identity of the *pispasin* is debated. One view is that they were beams protruding from the walls at the midpoint, creating a visible marker. The Albeck offers a more visually evocative explanation: *pispeis* in modern Hebrew means mosaic, and he proposes that a different mosaic pattern on the floor — or the point at which the mosaic began — served as the dividing line between the sacred and non-sacred portions of the chamber.

### Southwest Chamber

The southwest chamber was in the *kodesh* portion — anything in the south of the structure falling within the *Azara* space. This was *lishkat korban*, or according to some versions *lishkat tela'ei ha-korban* — the chamber of the sacrificial sheep. This is where the lambs designated for the *tamid* offering were inspected for four days prior to their being brought as a *korban*.

### Southeast Chamber

The southeast chamber, also in the *kodesh*, was *lishkat lechem ha-panim* — the chamber where the *lechem ha-panim* was baked. This was a hot space, with a substantial fire maintained for the baking of the showbread.

### Northeast Chamber

Moving clockwise, the northeast chamber was now in the *chol* portion of the *Beit HaMoked*. The *mishnah* records: *bo ganezu Beit Chashmonai et avnei mizbe'ach she-shiktzum malchei Yavan* — it was here that the *Chashmonaim* stored the stones of the *mizbe'ach* that had been defiled by the Greek kings. After the Maccabean victory, those stones that the *Yevanim* had rendered unfit were dismantled and placed in storage here — a question unto itself as to what precisely constituted defilement of the stone *mizbe'ach*, but a separate discussion.

### Chotamot System

Elsewhere this same chamber is identified as *lishkat chotamot* — the chamber of receipts — which we had encountered in *Tamid*. The system worked as follows: if a person brought a *korban* and required *nesachim* — the accompanying libations of wine, oil, and *mincha* — he would go to this chamber, pay, and receive a *chotam*, a receipt or token. When he presented his *korban* to the *Kohanim*, he would hand over this receipt, and the *Kohanim* responsible for oil and libations would know that payment had been made and would provide the appropriate *nesachim*.

One might ask: why a receipt system at all? Why not simply deposit money into a box, as was done for bird offerings? The answer likely lies in the variability of the nesachim requirement. Bird offerings have a fixed price; *nesachim* varied depending on whether the animal was a sheep, goat, or ox — different quantities and therefore different costs. A simple money box would not permit accurate accounting. Beyond that, if a person brought his animal without having paid for *nesachim*, the *Kohanim* who slaughtered it would themselves be implicated in an incomplete service. The receipt system ensured accountability — the *Kohanim* could verify that the *nesachim* had been arranged before proceeding.

One picture examined in the course of this discussion suggested that the transaction may have taken place entirely within this one chamber — a person would enter on one side, pay, and receive the receipt from someone on the other side, something like a clerk's window — rather than requiring the holder to carry the receipt across the complex and present it elsewhere. Either way, the *chotam* was the operative mechanism.

### Northwest Chamber

The northwest chamber, the fourth and final, was *bah yordim le-beit ha-tvilah* — from here one descended to the *mikveh*. This served the *Kohanim* who required purification, as will be discussed momentarily in greater detail.

## Beit HaMoked Gates

### Two Gates System

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנַיִם שְׁעָרִים הָיוּ לְבֵית הַמּוֹקֵד, אֶחָד פָּתוּחַ לַחֵיל וְאֶחָד פָּתוּחַ לָעֲזָרָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, זֶה שֶׁהָיָה פָתוּחַ לָעֲזָרָה, פִּשְׁפָּשׁ קָטָן הָיָה לוֹ, שֶׁבּוֹ נִכְנָסִין לִבְלֹשׁ אֶת הָעֲזָרָה:
>
> **Translation:** The fire chamber had two gates, one opening on to the Hel and one on to the courtyard. Rabbi Judah says: the one that opened on to the courtyard had a small opening through which they went in to search the courtyard.

*Shnayim shearim hayu le-Beit HaMoked* — the *Beit HaMoked* itself had two gates: *echad patuach la-chel ve-echad patuach la-Azara* — one opening to the *chel* and one opening into the *Azara*. It is worth noting in passing that the word *chel* may be a deliberate softening of the word *chol* — one cannot quite call *Har HaBayit* profane, but this terminology emphasizes that the space is not the *Azara* proper.

### Pishpash Scouting

*Amar Rabbi Yehuda*: the gate that opened into the *Azara* had inset within it a *pishpash katan* — a small door set into the larger gate — *shebo nichnasim livlosh et ha-Azara*. Through this small door, one could enter the *Azara* to scout it out — to check that all the *keilim* had been put away and that everything was in order before the start of the new day's service. We had encountered this scouting procedure in *Tamid*, though the specific mechanism of the *pishpash* is clarified here.

## Guard & Key Storage

### Dome & Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית הַמּוֹקֵד, כִּפָּה, וּבַיִת גָּדוֹל הָיָה, מֻקָּף רוֹבָדִין שֶׁל אֶבֶן, וְזִקְנֵי בֵית אָב יְשֵׁנִים שָׁם, וּמַפְתְּחוֹת הָעֲזָרָה בְּיָדָם, וּפִרְחֵי כְהֻנָּה, אִישׁ כִּסְתּוֹ בָאָרֶץ:
>
> **Translation:** The fire chamber was vaulted and it was a large room surrounded with stone projections, and the elders of the clan [serving in the Temple] used to sleep there, with the keys of the Temple courtyard in their hands. The priestly initiates used to place their bedding on the ground.

*Beit HaMoked*, the *mishnah* now elaborates, was *kippah* — it had a dome. And it was a substantial structure: *mukaf revadim shel even*, surrounded by stone steps on the interior. These steps apparently lined the inside of the domed building, either serving as platforms on which the occupants slept, or leading to small carved-out niches in the wall where one could rest.

### Elders & Keys

*Ve-ziknei beit av yeshenim sham* — the elders of the *beit av* of that day's rotation would sleep there. We had learned in *Tamid* that the *Kohanim* on duty slept in the *Beit HaMoked*, and at least the elders among them — the senior members of that day's *mishmar* — slept up near or on the stone steps. *U-miftechot ha-Azara beyadam* — in their hands they held the keys to the *Azara*, since the *Azara* was locked from the inside at night and could only be opened by someone already within.

### Young Kohanim

*U-pirchei kehunah ish kesuto ba-aretz* — the younger *Kohanim*, by contrast, did not sleep on the elevated steps but on the ground, each man with his garment beneath him. This echoes a discussion from *Tamid* about whether the *bigdei kehunah* could be used for personal comfort — whether one could derive benefit from the priestly vestments by sleeping on them.

### Key Storage Ritual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָקוֹם הָיָה שָׁם, אַמָּה עַל אַמָּה, וְטַבְלָא שֶׁל שַׁיִשׁ וְטַבַּעַת הָיְתָה קְבוּעָה בָהּ, וְשַׁלְשֶׁלֶת שֶׁהַמַּפְתְּחוֹת הָיוּ תְלוּיוֹת בָּהּ. הִגִּיעַ זְמַן הַנְּעִילָה, הִגְבִּיהַּ אֶת הַטַּבְלָא בַּטַּבַּעַת וְנָטַל אֶת הַמַּפְתְּחוֹת מִן הַשַּׁלְשֶׁלֶת, וְנָעַל הַכֹּהֵן מִבִּפְנִים, וּבֶן לֵוִי יָשֵׁן לוֹ מִבַּחוּץ. גָּמַר מִלִּנְעֹל, הֶחֱזִיר אֶת הַמַּפְתְּחוֹת לַשַּׁלְשֶׁלֶת וְאֶת הַטַּבְלָא לִמְקוֹמָהּ, נָתַן כְּסוּתוֹ עָלֶיהָ, יָשֵׁן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** There was a place there [in the fire chamber] one cubit square on which was a slab of marble. In this was fixed a ring and a chain on which the keys were hung. When closing time came, the priest would raise the slab by the ring and take the keys from the chain. Then the priest would lock up within while the Levite was sleeping outside. When he had finished locking up, he would replace the keys on the chain and the slab in its place and put his garment on it and sleep there.

The *mishnah* now provides one of its more distinctive passages regarding the physical storage of the keys. *U-makom hayah sham amah al amah* — there was a spot measuring one *amah* by one *amah*, roughly a foot and a half square. Upon it rested *tavla shel shayish*, a marble cover, *hayeta kevuah bah* — fixed in place on this low platform. *Ve-shalshelet shel maftechot hayu tluyot bah* — a chain holding the keys was suspended from it.

The precise image is somewhat ambiguous: was this a small chamber sunk into the floor with a marble cover sealing it, beneath which the keys hung on a chain? Or was the chain attached directly to the underside of the marble slab itself, so that lifting the cover revealed the keys hanging from it? The grammar admits either reading. What is clear is that the keys were stored within or beneath this marble platform, with the chain serving as a kind of organized keyring.

Why this elaborate arrangement? One possibility is that the marble platform was not immediately obvious to someone unfamiliar with the location — a kind of concealment by obscurity, though of course the *mishnah* now publishes the secret. Another possibility is that the marble platform and chain gave the entire procedure a ceremonial character befitting the locking and unlocking of the *Azara*.

### Locking Procedure

*Higgia zman ha-neila* — when the time for locking arrived, the appointed *Kohen* would *higbia et ha-tavla* — lift the marble cover. A ring was set into the top of the cover to facilitate lifting it. He then *natal et ha-maftechot min ha-shalshelet* — removed the keys from the chain — *ve-naal* — and proceeded to lock all the doors from the inside.

Immediately there is an apparent tension with what was stated a moment ago: *mishnah 8* said the keys were *be-yadam* — in the hands — of the *ziknei beit av* sleeping on the steps above. But this passage describes keys stored in a marble box on the floor, with someone sleeping on top of it. How do we reconcile the two? One approach is to take *be-yadam* non-literally, meaning that the keys were under their authority and stewardship, rather than physically in their grip. Another is to suggest that the two references describe different sets of keys, or different stages of the same procedure. The descriptions do remain textually distinct, however.

Following the locking, the *mishnah* adds: *he-Kohen mi-bifnim u-vein Levi yashen lo mi-bachutz* — the *Kohen* was on the inside and a *Levi* slept adjacent from the outside. The word *yashen* — slept — has struck some as inappropriate for a guarding function. One variant reading substitutes *yashav*, sat, which would be more consistent with an active watch. But the *girsa* of *yashen* is attested, and may simply reflect that the guard was permitted to rest so long as he remained at his post.

Once the locking was complete: *hechzir et ha-mafteach la-shalshelet ve-et ha-tavla li-mekomah* — the key was replaced on the chain and the marble cover returned to its place. Then *natan kesuto aleha ve-yashan lo* — the one responsible for the keys placed his garment on top of the marble cover and slept upon it. The person guarding the keys thus literally slept on top of them, ensuring that no one could access them without waking him.

## Nocturnal Emission

### Nocturnal Event

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵרַע קֶרִי בְּאַחַד מֵהֶם, יוֹצֵא וְהוֹלֵךְ לוֹ בַמְּסִבָּה הַהוֹלֶכֶת תַּחַת הַבִּירָה, וְהַנֵּרוֹת דּוֹלְקִים מִכָּאן וּמִכָּאן, עַד שֶׁהוּא מַגִּיעַ לְבֵית הַטְּבִילָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר, בַּמְּסִבָּה הַהוֹלֶכֶת תַּחַת הַחֵיל יוֹצֵא וְהוֹלֵךְ לוֹ בְּטָדִי:
>
> **Translation:** If one of them had a seminal emission, he would go out by the winding stair which went under the Birah, and which was lighted with lamps on both sides, until he reached the bathing place. Rabbi Eliezer ben Jacob says: he descended by the winding stair which went under the Hel and he went out by the Taddi gate.

*Ira keri be-echad me-hem* — should one of the *Kohanim* experience a nocturnal emission, he became *tamei* and was no longer permitted to remain within the *Azara*. We had addressed this situation when learning *Tamid*.

### Underground Route

*Yotze ve-holech lo ba-misila ha-malechet tachat ha-birah* — he would leave by way of the passageway that ran beneath the *birah*, whether that term refers to the *Mikdash* as a whole or to one particular section of the complex. This passageway was lit throughout — *ve-nerot dolkim mi-kan u-mi-kan* — with lights on both sides — *ad she-yagia le-beit ha-tvilah*, until he reached the place where he could immerse in the *mikveh*.

### Sha'ar Tadi Route

*Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* offers an alternative account. In his view, the *Kohen* did not remain underground within the *Mikdash* complex but instead traveled *ba-misila ha-malechet tachat ha-chayil* — through a passageway running beneath the *chel*, effectively exiting the *Azara* precincts entirely — *ve-holech lo be-Tadi*. He would exit via *Sha'ar Tadi*, the northern gate that the *mishnah* had earlier characterized as serving no particular function.

This resolves the earlier puzzle about *Sha'ar Tadi*'s purpose: according to *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*, it was the private exit for the *Kohen* who needed to leave in the middle of the night. Some have proposed that the name *Tadi* should be read as *tari*, the Aramaic word for private, emphasizing that this was the discreet exit used for such occasions.

It is worth noting that *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* is the *Tanna* cited almost exclusively in *Middot* and *Tamid* not for *halachic* rulings but for descriptive accounts of the *Beit HaMikdash* — which is part of why both the *Bavli* and *Yerushalmi* assume that he was the primary author of tractate *Tamid*.

The divergence between this account and the version learned in *Tamid* is significant: where *Tamid* gave the impression that the *Kohen* would go to the *mikveh* and return to his post by morning, *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* here describes a scenario in which the *Kohen* was actually required to leave *Har HaBayit* immediately — going to the *mikveh* only once outside the complex — and did not remain inside waiting for dawn.

## Har HaBayit Dimensions

### Overall Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַר הַבַּיִת הָיָה חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת אַמָּה עַל חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת אַמָּה, רֻבּוֹ מִן הַדָּרוֹם, שֵׁנִי לוֹ מִן הַמִּזְרָח, שְׁלִישִׁי לוֹ מִן הַצָּפוֹן, מִעוּטוֹ מִן הַמַּעֲרָב. מְקוֹם שֶׁהָיָה רֹב מִדָּתוֹ, שָׁם הָיָה רֹב תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ:
>
> **Translation:** The Temple Mount was five hundred cubits by five hundred cubits. The greater part of it was on the south; next to that on the east; next to that on the north; and the smallest part on the west. The part which was most extensive was the part most used.

*Har HaBayit hayah chamesh me'ot amah al chamesh me'ot amah* — the entire *Har HaBayit* measured five hundred by five hundred *amot*, approximately 750 feet on each side — roughly three city blocks square. When a dimension is stated with such mathematical precision for what is ultimately a natural hilltop, one assumes this refers to the retaining walls constructed around it: the builders determined where to erect the perimeter wall and thus created a defined square of that measurement.

### Space Distribution

*Rubo min ha-darom* — the largest portion of the space was to the south. *Sheni lo min ha-mizrach* — the second largest allocation was to the east. *Shelishi lo min ha-tzafon* — third was the north, each strip progressively narrower. *Mi'uto min ha-maarav* — and the smallest allocation was to the west. *Makom she-hayah rov midato sham hayah rov tashmisho* — greater space was given where there would be greater use.

### Usage Rationale

The rationale for this distribution follows naturally from the patterns of access. The south received the most space because that is where the city of *Yerushalayim* was built and from where the slope permitted approach — the *Shaarei Chulda* were the primary entry points, and so the broad southern plaza served as the arrival and gathering area. The east received the second largest space because, once assembled in the south, the crowds would move toward the eastern section where the *Azara* was accessed through *Sha'ar Nikanor* and the *Ezrat Nashim*. Here too one might purchase *nesachim*, present one's *korban*, and spend time in the complex. The west, by contrast, was deliberately kept minimal: it lay directly behind the *Kodesh Kodashim*, and it would have been inappropriate for large numbers of people to congregate in the area most proximate to that innermost sacred space. The *Kotel* as we know it today stands along precisely this western retaining wall — the wall built closest to where the *Kodesh Kodashim* stood.

## Entry/Exit Flow

### Right Enter Left Exit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הַנִּכְנָסִין לְהַר הַבַּיִת נִכְנָסִין דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין וּמַקִּיפִין וְיוֹצְאִין דֶּרֶךְ שְׂמֹאל, חוּץ מִמִּי שֶׁאֵרְעוֹ דָבָר, שֶׁהוּא מַקִּיף לִשְׂמֹאל. מַה לְּךָ מַקִּיף לִשְׂמֹאל, שֶׁאֲנִי אָבֵל, הַשּׁוֹכֵן בַּבַּיִת הַזֶּה יְנַחֲמֶךָּ. שֶׁאֲנִי מְנֻדֶּה, הַשּׁוֹכֵן בַּבַּיִת הַזֶּה יִתֵּן בְּלִבָּם וִיקָרְבוּךָ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, עֲשִׂיתָן כְּאִלּוּ עָבְרוּ עָלָיו אֶת הַדִּין. אֶלָּא, הַשּׁוֹכֵן בַּבַּיִת הַזֶּה יִתֵּן בְּלִבְּךָ וְתִשְׁמַע לְדִבְרֵי חֲבֵרֶיךָ וִיקָרְבוּךָ:
>
> **Translation:** All who entered the Temple Mount entered by the right and went round [to the right] and went out by the left, save for one to whom something had happened, who entered and went round to the left. [He was asked]: "Why do you go round to the left?" [If he answered] "Because I am a mourner," [they said to him], "May He who dwells in this house comfort you." [If he answered] "Because I am excommunicated" [they said]: "May He who dwells in this house inspire them to draw you near again," the words of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yose to him: you make it seem as if they treated him unjustly. Rather [they should say]: "May He who dwells in this house inspire you to listen to the words of your colleagues so that they may draw you near again."

*Kol ha-nichnasim le-Har HaBayit nichnasim derech yamin* — all who entered *Har HaBayit* turned to the right upon entry. *U-makifin ve-yotzin derech smol* — and they would circle around and exit to the left. The movement was consistently counterclockwise: enter on the right, proceed around the complex, and exit via the opposite direction.

The mechanics of this circulation are worth examining. The straightforward reading is that worshippers entered the complex, moved to the right through the space — past the southern area, through the eastern sections where the *Azara* lay — completed a full circuit, and exited by the other southern gate. *Tosafot* acknowledges that this would require a complete *sivuv*, a full circuit, though this raises questions given the relatively narrow space available on the western side. An alternative reading is that the circular movement was partial — one entered from one gate and exited via another without necessarily traversing the entire perimeter.

### Avel Exception

*Chutz mi-mi she-ira bo davar she-hu makif le-smol* — except for someone to whom something has occurred, who circles to the left instead. The *mishnah* describes how such a person, moving in the reverse direction, would encounter the rest of the worshippers coming the other way. They would notice the anomaly and ask: *mah lecha makif le-smol* — why are you going in the wrong direction?

The person might answer: *she-ani avel* — I am a mourner. At first glance, this seems to describe someone who has just learned of a death and is rushing out. But on reflection — and here a sharper reading is warranted — the phrase *ira bo davar* need not indicate something that just occurred at that moment. The better understanding is that the *avel* had come to *Har HaBayit* in his state of mourning and deliberately walked in the reverse direction in order to prompt exactly this exchange. This also accounts for the parallel case of the *menudeh*, the person under *cherem*, who similarly cannot have just been excommunicated in that instant.

The purpose, in other words, was communicative and social: by moving against the flow, the mourner or excommunicate created a visible signal that invited others to ask about his situation and offer consolation or encouragement.

### Consolation Formula

Upon learning that the person was an *avel*, the response given was: *ha-shochen ba-bayit ha-ze yenachamecha* — may the One who dwells in this house console you.

The case of the *menudeh* — the excommunicated individual — generated a *machloket* between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yose*. *Rabbi Meir*'s formula was: *ha-shochen ba-bayit ha-ze yiten be-libam ve-yekarvucha* — may the One who dwells in this house place it in their hearts and bring you close again. *Rabbi Yose* objected: *asitem ke-ilu avru alav et ha-din* — by saying "may God place it in their hearts," you are implying that they were in the wrong for excommunicating him, essentially suggesting that their judgment was unjust. Rather, *Rabbi Yose* said, one should say: *yiten be-libcha ve-tishma le-divrei chaverecha ve-az ve-yekarvucha* — may God place it in your heart to heed the words of your fellows, and then they will draw you close. The consolation formula here points inward to the *menudeh* himself — you should change, and then reconciliation will follow.

## Soreg Fence

### Soreg Description

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִפְנִים מִמֶּנּוּ, סוֹרֵג, גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. וּשְׁלשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה פְרָצוֹת הָיוּ שָׁם, שֶׁפְּרָצוּם מַלְכֵי יָוָן. חָזְרוּ וּגְדָרוּם, וְגָזְרוּ כְנֶגְדָּם שְׁלשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה הִשְׁתַּחֲוָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Within it was the Soreg, ten handbreadths high. There were thirteen breaches in it, which had been originally made by the kings of Greece, and when they repaired them they enacted that thirteen prostrations should be made facing them.

*Lifnim mimenu ha-soreg* — further inward from the general *Har HaBayit* space was the *soreg*, a woven lattice fence. It was *gavo'ah yud tefachim* — ten *tefachim* high — and it created the boundary beyond which non-Jews were forbidden. It defined the outer edge of the *chel*, the buffer zone of ten *amot* width that separated the *soreg* from the *Azara* wall itself.

### Greek Breaks

*U-shlosh esreh pertzot hayu vo* — there were thirteen breaks in this fence. These were not original openings but deliberate breaches: *she-peratzum malchei Yavan* — the Greek kings had broken through in thirteen places. The symbolic intent is clear: the *soreg* was the explicit marker of the boundary beyond which non-Jews could not proceed, and so the *Yevanim*, in their conquest and desecration of the *Mikdash*, deliberately violated that fence in thirteen places as an act of defiance. *Chazru u-gedarum* — subsequently, the *Chashmonaim* repaired all thirteen breaches.

### Thirteen Prostrations

*Ve-gazru kenegdan yud gimmel hishtachavuyot* — and corresponding to those thirteen breaks, the Sages instituted thirteen prostrations. The better understanding of this ordinance is that each time a worshipper passed one of those repaired breaks, he would prostrate himself — in gratitude to God for the restoration of Jewish sovereignty over *Har HaBayit* and the expulsion of the Greeks. The number thirteen, it should be noted, carries its own significance in Jewish tradition, and it is possible that the choice was partly symbolic in that broader sense.

## Chel & Steps

### Chel Dimensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִפְנִים מִמֶּנּוּ, הַחֵיל, עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת. וּשְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה מַעֲלוֹת הָיוּ שָׁם. רוּם הַמַּעֲלָה חֲצִי אַמָּה, וְשִׁלְחָהּ חֲצִי אַמָּה. כָּל הַמַּעֲלוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם, רוּם מַעֲלָה חֲצִי אַמָּה, וְשִׁלְחָהּ חֲצִי אַמָּה, חוּץ מִשֶּׁל אוּלָם.
>
> **Translation:** Within this was the Hel, which was ten cubits [broad]. There were twelve steps there. The height of each step was half a cubit and its tread was half a cubit. All the steps in the Temple were half a cubit high with a tread of half a cubit, except those of the Porch.

*Ha-chel eser amot* — the *chel* was ten *amot* wide. Beyond it, ascending toward the *Azara*, were *shteim esrei maalot* — twelve steps. Each step was *chatzi amah* high and *chatzi amah* deep — half an *amah* of rise and half an *amah* of tread — roughly nine inches in each direction. Twelve steps of half an *amah* each account for six *amot* of total height — more than half the ten-*amah* width of the *chel* was thus consumed by the staircase, with the remaining four *amot* being level ground before the steps began.

This arrangement meant that even after entering through the *Har HaBayit* perimeter, passing through the *soreg*, crossing the *chel*, one still had to physically ascend twelve steps before reaching the *Azara* itself. There is something deeply intentional about this: one does not walk directly into the sacred space; one climbs toward it. *Maalin ba-kodesh* — we ascend in holiness — embodied quite literally in the architecture.

The mishnah generalizes: all steps throughout the complex were built to these same half-*amah* proportions — *chutz mi-shel ulam*, except for the steps leading into the *Ulam*, which were built differently, as will be discussed in a subsequent mishnah.

### Gates & Openings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הַפְּתָחִים וְהַשְּׁעָרִים שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם, גָּבְהָן עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה, וְרָחְבָּן עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת, חוּץ מִשֶּׁל אוּלָם. כָּל הַפְּתָחִים שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם, הָיוּ לָהֶן דְּלָתוֹת, חוּץ מִשֶּׁל אוּלָם. כָּל הַשְּׁעָרִים שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם, הָיוּ לָהֶן שְׁקוֹפוֹת, חוּץ מִשַּׁעַר טָדִי, שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם שְׁתֵּי אֲבָנִים מֻטּוֹת זוֹ עַל גַּב זוֹ. כָּל הַשְּׁעָרִים שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם, נִשְׁתַּנּוּ לִהְיוֹת שֶׁל זָהָב, חוּץ מִשַּׁעַר נִקָּנוֹר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה בָהֶן נֵס. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנְּחֻשְׁתָּן מַצְהִיב:
>
> **Translation:** All the doorways in the Temple were twenty cubits high and ten cubits broad except those of the Porch. All the doorways there had doors in them except those of the Porch. All the gates there had lintels except that of Taddi which had two stones inclined to one another. All the original gates were changed for gates of gold except the gates of Nicanor, because a miracle happened with them. Some say: because their copper gleamed like gold.

All gates and openings throughout the complex were *gevohim esrim amah ve-rochban eser amot* — twenty *amot* high and ten *amot* wide, approximately thirty feet in height and fifteen feet across. These are imposing dimensions that match the towering impression conveyed in every reconstruction of the *Mikdash*. All openings had doors — *hayu lahem delatot* — with the sole exception of the *Ulam*, which was closed not by doors but by a curtain. This is the curtain through which the *Kohen Gadol* would emerge after the *ketoret* service and before which the assembled *Kohanim* would prostrate themselves.

All the gates likewise had *shkofot* — lintels — with one exception: *Sha'ar Tadi*, the gate through which the *Kohen* would exit after a nocturnal emission. The other gates had simple, straight horizontal lintels. *Sha'ar Tadi* had a different structure: *shtei avanim mutot zo al gav zo* — two stones leaning one against the other, forming an arched or angled top, something closer to the triangular or voussoir-style arch of Greek and Roman construction.

### Golden Gates

*Kol ha-shearim she-hayu sham nishtanu lihyot shel zahav* — all the gates were eventually replaced with gold. This was not their original form but came later, when the resources were available. *Chutz mi-Sha'ar Nikanor* — except for *Sha'ar Nikanor*, which remained in its original brass. The reason given by the primary view: *mipnei she-naasah bahem nes* — because of the miracle associated with those doors, they were left as they were, preserved as a testament to what had occurred.

*Ve-yeish omrim* — but some say the reason was different: *mipnei she-nechushtan mazhiv* — because the copper of those doors shone like gold. According to this view, the decision to leave *Sha'ar Nikanor* unchanged was not commemorative but practical: since the brass already gleamed with a golden appearance, there was no need to replace it. While the general principle is *ein ani bi-mekom ashirut* — one does not accept lesser quality where greater quality is available — if the brass looked identical to gold, no aesthetic principle was compromised.
