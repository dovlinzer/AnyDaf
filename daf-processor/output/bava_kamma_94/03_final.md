# Bava Kamma 94 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shinuy Overview

### Opening Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹזֵל עֵצִים וַעֲשָׂאָן כֵּלִים, צֶמֶר וַעֲשָׂאָן בְּגָדִים – מְשַׁלֵּם כִּשְׁעַת הַגְּזֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who robs** another of **wood and fashions it** into **vessels,** or one who robs another of **wool and fashions it** into **garments,** he **pays** the robbery victim **according to** the value of the goods at the **time of the robbery,** but he need not return the vessels or garments. He has acquired the stolen items because they had undergone a change.

Today's daf is 94, situated within the perek of *Hagozel Etzim*, the eighth perek of Bava Kamma — sometimes called *Hagozel Kamma* or *Hagozel Batra* — as distinct from the seventh perek, which dealt with *gneiva*. The present perek concerns *gezeilah*, and a central preoccupation throughout is the question of *shinuyim*: when an object stolen by a *gazlan* undergoes change, does the *gazlan* thereby acquire it? This question was already addressed in the context of the *gneiva* perek, and the parallel treatment here invites the question of why these two discussions do not more explicitly intersect, though they are clearly related.

The *mishnah* established that a *shinuy* does transfer ownership to the *gazlan*. The *Gemara* then distinguished between two categories: a *shinuy* that is reversible — *chozer l'vriyaso* — and one that is not. A further question was raised: does the reversible *shinuy* effect acquisition only on a rabbinic level, or even on a biblical one?

### Reversible vs. Irreversible Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: עֵצִים וַעֲשָׂאָן כֵּלִים – אִין, שִׁיפָּן – לָא; צֶמֶר וַעֲשָׂאָן בְּגָדִים – אִין, לִיבְּנָן – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sages **say:** It can be inferred from the mishna that if one robbed another of **wood and fashioned it into vessels, yes,** the robber acquires the wood due to the change. If he merely **sanded it, no,** the robber does not acquire it, as this is not a significant change. Similarly, if one robbed another of **wool and fashioned it** into **garments, yes,** he has acquired the wool due to the change. If **he** merely **washed it, no,** he has not acquired it.

The significance of *chozer l'vriyaso* as a category requires careful appreciation. The *chiddush* is not merely that something can be trivially undone. Even where a substantial transformation has occurred — one that would require considerable effort to reverse — the *Gemara* suggests it may still be classified as a *shinuy hachozer l'vriyaso*. The mere theoretical possibility of reversal, however laborious, may suffice to place a *shinuy* in the weaker category that does not effect acquisition on a biblical level.

## Dyeing Wool Debate

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: גָּזַל עֵצִים וְשִׁיפָּן, אֲבָנִים וְסִיתְּתָן, צֶמֶר (וְלִיבְּנָן) [וְלִיבְּנוֹ], פִּשְׁתָּן וְנִקָּהוּ – מְשַׁלֵּם כִּשְׁעַת הַגְּזֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: If one **robbed** another of **wood and sanded it,** or **stones and smoothed them,** or **wool and washed it,** or **flax and cleaned it, he pays according to** the value of the stolen item at the **time of the robbery.** This *baraita* teaches that even a change such as sanding wood is regarded as a significant change.

The *Gemara* now engages a specific tension arising from the law of *reishis hageiz*, the obligation to give the first shearing of wool to the *kohen*. The *mishnah* or *braisa* implies that whitening wool constitutes a *shinuy*, while the case of *reishis hageiz* suggests it does not — a direct contradiction. Abaye resolved this by identifying it as a dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabanan*: Rabbi Shimon holds that whitening wool is a *shinuy*, while the *Rabanan* hold it is not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הָא רַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא: גְּזָזוֹ, טְווֹאוֹ וַאֲרָגוֹ – אֵין מִצְטָרֵף. לִבְּנוֹ – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין מִצְטָרֵף, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מִצְטָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** It is **not difficult. This** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** whereas **that** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the first of the sheared wool: If one **sheared it, spun it, and wove it,** the sheared wool **does not combine** with the wool from other sheep to constitute the minimum quantity of wool for which one is obligated to give the first of the sheared wool to the priest. If one **washed it,** then **Rabbi Shimon says** that it **does not combine** with the wool of other sheep, as washing effects a significant change, **and the Rabbis say that** it **combines** with the wool of other sheep, as washing does not effect a significant change. Their opinions correspond to the opinions in the *baraita* and mishna previously quoted.

The *Gemara* then presses further. A *braisa* concerning *reishis hageiz* records that if one shears wool and simultaneously dyes it, spins it, or weaves it, the processed wool does not combine with the remaining shearings to create the requisite quantity for the obligation to the *kohen*. Yet Rabbi Shimon Yehudah, citing Rabbi Shimon, states that even after dyeing, the wool does combine — implying that dyeing is *not* a *shinuy* in Rabbi Shimon's view. How, then, can Rabbi Shimon simultaneously hold that whitening *is* a *shinuy* while dyeing is not?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה, וְשָׁאנֵי צֶבַע – הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לְהַעֲבִירוֹ עַל יְדֵי צָפוֹן. וְכִי קָתָנֵי הָתָם: לֹא הִסְפִּיק לִיתְּנוֹ לוֹ עַד שֶׁצְּבָעוֹ – פָּטוּר, וְאוֹקִימְנָא כְּדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל; בְּקָלָא אִילָן דְּלָא עָבַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava stated** another answer: **Actually, the Rabbis do not disagree with Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda,** and they are also of the opinion that Rabbi Shimon holds that washing effects a significant change. **And** as for the apparent contradiction, **dye is different** and it does not effect a significant change, **since one is able to remove it with soap [*tzafon*]** and return the wool to its previous state. **And when it is taught there,** in the mishna cited above, that if **one did not manage to give** the sheared wool **to** the priest **before he dyed it he is exempt, and** this ruling **was established in accordance with all opinions,** it was not stated with regard to ordinary dye but **with regard to indigo, which cannot be removed** with soap and therefore effects a permanent and therefore significant change.

Abaye answers: *lo kashya* — this is not a genuine contradiction but reflects two competing traditions as to what Rabbi Shimon actually said. Rava, however, goes further: *l'olam lo pligi* — there is in fact no dispute at all. The *Rabanan* who disagree about whitening would not disagree with Rabbi Shimon Yehudah about dyeing. Why? Because dyeing, unlike bleaching with sulfur, is potentially reversible: *yachol lehachziro al yedei tzava* — the color can be removed by a dye remover. Since dyeing is an additive process, it is conceptually undoable in a way that whitening may not be. Thus, even if whitening crosses the threshold of *shinuy*, dyeing need not.

### Kalla Ilan Dye

An important qualification immediately follows. The *Gemara* notes that the case in the *braisa* where everyone agreed that dyeing constitutes a *shinuy* sufficient to create an exemption was specifically a case of *kalla ilan* — a permanent dye of the color of *techeiles* — which cannot be removed. That dye, unlike ordinary coloring, is genuinely irreversible and thus constitutes an unambiguous *shinuy*.

### Chozer L'Briyaso

The broader conceptual *chiddush* crystallizes here: *chozer l'vriyaso* does not mean the change is trivial or easily undone. A garment can theoretically be unraveled back into yarn; a complex object can be disassembled. The test is not ease of reversal but the conceptual question of whether the original identity is genuinely recoverable. Even significant transformations may fall into the category of *chozer l'vriyaso* — and that classification, as Rabbi Yochanan will later make explicit, is what drives the entire rabbinic structure of *shinuy koneh*.

## Shinuy Bamkomo Omed

### Abaye's Grouping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה, וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ: שִׁינּוּי בִּמְקוֹמוֹ עוֹמֵד. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Abaye said: Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, and Beit Shammai, and Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, and Rabbi Yishmael all hold** that despite **a change,** the changed item **remains in its place,** i.e., the changed item is still considered to have the status it had before the change. The Gemara proceeds to prove that each of these *tanna'im* holds this way: The opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda** is **that which we** just **said.** He says that, according to Rabbi Shimon, even if the wool is dyed it still combines with the wool of other animals to constitute the minimum quantity of wool for which one is obligated to give the first of the sheared wool to the priest.

Abaye now performs one of his characteristic analytical moves: grouping disparate *Tannaim* who share a common underlying orientation. He identifies Rabbi Shimon ben Yehudah, Beis Shammai, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, and Rabbi Yishmael as all sharing the view of *shinuy bamkomo omed* — that change leaves the identity of an object fundamentally intact. This is a broader formulation than the familiar *shinuy eino koneh*, which is framed specifically in terms of whether a *ganav* or *gazlan* acquires the object. Abaye deliberately casts the principle more broadly, cutting across different areas of *halacha*, because the question he is addressing is one of identity: does the object, after the change, remain the same object? This is a question of the persistence of identity — *shinuy bamkomo omed* — rather than merely a question of acquisition in the narrow sense of theft law.

Tosafos notes, and many *Rishonim* observe, that Abaye does not claim these *Tannaim* hold identical positions. Some may in principle accept *shinuy koneh* while simply requiring a very high threshold for what constitutes a qualifying *shinuy*. The common thread is that they each, in their respective domains, insist on a continuity of identity through change that others would not require.

### R. Shimon b. Yehuda

The first member of Abaye's grouping is Rabbi Shimon ben Yehudah, whose position has already been established: that dyeing wool does not constitute a *shinuy*, and thus the wool retains its identity as the same object. As just discussed, this is consistent with viewing dyeing as reversible — a *chozer l'vriyaso* — and therefore falling below the threshold of a genuine *shinuy*. It should be noted, however, that the same Rabbi Shimon ben Yehudah does treat weaving and spinning as sufficient *shinuyim*; his position is thus one of a high threshold rather than an absolute rejection of the principle.

### Beis Shammai Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: נָתַן לָהּ חִטִּין בְּאֶתְנַנָּהּ, וַעֲשָׂאָן סוֹלֶת; זֵיתִים וַעֲשָׂאָן שֶׁמֶן; עֲנָבִים וַעֲשָׂאָן יַיִן – תָּנֵי חֲדָא: אָסוּר, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the source that indicates that **Beit Shammai** maintain that an item that undergoes a change is considered to have the same status that it had before the change? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **gave** a prostitute **wheat as her payment, and she** ground it and **converted it into flour;** or if he gave her **olives, and she** squeezed them and **converted them into oil;** or if he gave her **grapes, and she** squeezed them and **converted them into wine;** and if, in any of these cases, she subsequently consecrated the final product, **it is taught** in **one** *baraita* that it is **prohibited** to sacrifice them upon the altar as a meal-offering or libation, as the Torah states: "You shall not bring the hire of a harlot or the price of a dog into the House of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 23:19). **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita* that it is **permitted.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמֵאַסְפּוֹרַק: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹסְרִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין. מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? אָמַר קְרָא: ״גַּם שְׁנֵיהֶם״ – לְרַבּוֹת שִׁינּוּיֵיהֶם. וּבֵית הִלֵּל – אָמַר קְרָא: ״הֵם״ – וְלֹא שִׁינּוּיֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **of Asporak: Beit Shammai prohibit** sacrificing these items **and Beit Hillel permit** doing so. The Gemara clarifies: **What is the reason of Beit Shammai?** The Gemara answers: **The verse states:** "You shall not bring the hire of a harlot or the price of a dog into the House of the Lord your God for any vow; for **even both of these** are an abomination unto the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 23:19). The word "even" is an amplification, which serves **to include** in the prohibition these items in **their changed** form. **And** what is the reason of **Beit Hillel? The verse states "these"** to emphasize that the prohibition applies only to these items in their initial form, **but not** in **their changed** form.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, הָא כְּתִיב ״הֵם״! הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ ״הֵם״ – וְלֹא וַלְדוֹתֵיהֶם. וּבֵית הִלֵּל – תַּרְתֵּי שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ. ״הֵם״ וְלֹא שִׁינּוּיֵיהֶם, ״הֵם״ וְלֹא וַלְדוֹתֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Beit Shammai, isn't** the word **"these" written** in the verse, indicating an exclusion? The Gemara responds: Beit Shammai **requires that** word to indicate that **"these"** items are forbidden, **but not** the **offspring** of animals given as payment to the prostitute. The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Beit Hillel,** what is the source of that *halakha*? The Gemara answers: According to Beit Hillel, **you learn two** *halakhot* **from this** word, as follows: **"These"** items are forbidden in their initial form **but not** in **their changed** form, and **"these"** items are forbidden **but not their offspring.**

The case associated with Beis Shammai concerns *esnan* — the payment given to a prostitute — and whether such payment, after transformation, may be offered as a *korban*. The *braisa* presents a dispute: one teaching prohibits offering the transformed object on the *Mizbeach*, while another permits it. Rav Yosef cites a tradition from one of the *Amoraim* that this is a dispute of Beis Hillel and Beis Shammai.

*Mai ta'ama d'Beis Shammai?* — What is Beis Shammai's reasoning? The verse states: *lo tavi esnan zona u'mechir kelev beis Hashem Elokecha l'chol neder ki to'avas Hashem Elokecha gam shneihem* — you shall not bring the payment of a prostitute or the price of a dog to the House of God for any vow, for both are an abomination to God. The word *gam* — "also" or "both" — appears seemingly superfluous alongside *shneihem*. Beis Shammai derives from this: *l'rabos shinuyehem* — to include their transformations. The similarity in sound between *shneihem* and *shinuyehem* is the basis of the *drasha*: even after a change in form, the object retains its tainted identity and cannot be brought as a *korban*.

Beis Hillel counters that *hem* — "they" — indicates only the objects themselves, *v'lo shinuyehem* — and not their transformed versions. And Beis Shammai? They derive from *hem* a different limitation: *hem v'lo voldoseihem* — the objects themselves, but not their offspring. If one gives a sheep as *esnan* and it gives birth to a lamb, that lamb may be brought; the prohibition extends only to the original object itself. Beis Hillel, for their part, derive both principles from *hem* — neither the transformed version nor the offspring is prohibited — and the word *gam* accordingly presents a difficulty for them.

### R. Eliezer b. Yaakov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי שֶׁגָּזַל סְאָה שֶׁל חִטִּין, טְחָנָהּ, לָשָׁהּ וַאֲפָאָהּ, וְהִפְרִישׁ מִמֶּנָּה חַלָּה, כֵּיצַד מְבָרֵךְ? אֵין זֶה מְבָרֵךְ אֶלָּא מְנָאֵץ! וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״בֹּצֵעַ בֵּרֵךְ נִאֵץ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** In the case of one **who robbed** another of **a *se'a* of wheat,** then **ground it, kneaded it, and baked it, and** he then **separated *ḥalla* from it,** i.e., he separated the portion of the dough that one is required to separate and then give to a priest, **how** can he **recite the blessing** over the separation of *ḥalla*? **This** individual is **not reciting a blessing,** but **rather** he is **blaspheming. And with regard to this it is stated: "The robber who recites a blessing blasphemes the Lord"** (Psalms 10:3), which is referring to a robber who recites a blessing upon performing a mitzva with an item he stole. According to Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, although this wheat has been significantly changed, it is still considered a stolen item.

The case of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov involves a *gazlan* who stole a *se'ah* of wheat, ground it into flour, kneaded it into dough, and baked it into bread. He then separated *challah* from the loaf. The question is: what *bracha* does he recite? Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says: *ein zeh mevarech ela m'naetz* — this is not a blessing but a blasphemy. Reciting a *bracha* to God while performing an act founded on theft is not an act of praise; it is an affront.

The argument works most powerfully with respect to the *challah* separation specifically. The *bracha* on *hafrashas challah* represents the owner designating a portion of his property as a gift to God. To do so with stolen dough is to presume a right of ownership one does not possess. The verse invoked is *botzea barech ni'etz Hashem* — one who breaks bread and blesses, his blessing is a cursing of God — where *botzea* carries the connotation of ill-gotten gain, making the blessing an inversion of its own intent. This scenario is the paradigmatic example of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* — a *mitzvah* enabled through sin.

### Mitzvah B'Aveira

The principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* invoked here deserves separate attention. Rava will later note that Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's ruling may be driven specifically by this principle rather than by any general position on *shinuy*. But the concept itself admits of two distinct formulations. One reading focuses on the *cheftza* — the object used for the *mitzvah* is tainted by its sinful origin: a stolen *lulav* is *pasul*, a stolen *korban* is *pasul*, an object given as *esnan* is stigmatized and cannot be used for sacred purposes. The other reading focuses on the *process*: a *mitzvah* whose performance was enabled through a transgression is not a valid fulfillment, regardless of whether the object itself retains a sinful character.

The distinction matters for the question of *shinuy*. If the concern is the *cheftza*, then the continuous identity of the stolen object is essential to the analysis. But if the concern is the process — that the *mitzvah* was enabled by a sin — then the object need not retain its original identity at all. The *mitzvah* could be performed with a transformed and technically different object, yet still be invalid because the avenue to performing it was paved by transgression. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's case may thus not require any position on *shinuy bamkomo omed*.

### R. Shimon b. Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא, כְּלָל זֶה אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל שֶׁבַח שֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחַ גַּזְלָן – יָדוֹ עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה. רָצָה נוֹטֵל שִׁבְחוֹ, רָצָה אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **What is** the source that indicates that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar** holds that an item that undergoes a change is still considered to have the same status that it had before the change? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 10:2) that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar stated this principle:** With regard to **any enhancement** to the stolen animal in **that** it **was enhanced** by the actions of the **robber, he has the advantage** when repaying the owner. If **he desires, he takes his enhancement,** i.e., when he returns the animal, the robbery victim must pay the difference between its value at the time of the robbery and its current value, and **if he desires** he can return it to the owner and **say to him: That which is yours is before you.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הִשְׁבִּיחַ – נוֹטֵל שִׁבְחוֹ. כָּחַשׁ – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״, דְּשִׁינּוּי בִּמְקוֹמוֹ עוֹמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **What is he saying?** If the robber has a right to demand compensation for the enhancement to the animal, why would he ever return it without stating this demand? **Rav Sheshet said** that **this is** what he **is saying:** If the robber **enhanced it, he takes his enhancement.** If the animal was **weakened,** the robber **says to him: That which is yours is before you,** and no further compensation is required. This is **because** despite **a change,** the changed item **remains in its place.** Since the robber has not acquired it, he simply returns the item to the robbery victim.

The *Gemara* now turns to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's ruling, which is the most directly relevant to the *gazlan* and *shinuy* discussion. The principle stated is: *kol shevach she'hishbi'ach gazlan, yado al ha'elyonah* — any improvement the *gazlan* has effected in the stolen object, he has the upper hand. He may choose either to take his improvement — *notel shivcho* — or to return the object as it stands — *harei shelcha lifanecha*.

The *Gemara* initially puzzles over this formulation, since taking the improvement and returning the object seem to be cases of different valuations rather than alternatives in the same situation. Rav Sheshet clarifies: the ruling covers two distinct scenarios. If the object has *improved*, the *gazlan* may take his enhancement — *notel shivcho* — and return the base value. If the object has *deteriorated* — *im hikchish* — he may say *harei shelcha lifanecha*, return it as it is, and owe nothing for the diminishment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ הִשְׁבִּיחַ נָמֵי! אָמְרִי: מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת הַשָּׁבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if the robber has not acquired it, then **even if he enhanced it** that should be the *halakha* **as well.** The item should still belong to the robbery victim and the robber should not be entitled to compensation. The Sages **say** in response: The fact that the robber has a right to demand compensation for the enhancement is **due to an ordinance** instituted **for the penitent.** In order to ease the burden of one who desires to repent, the Sages instituted that the robber be reimbursed for the increase in the value of the animal. Otherwise, a robber might refrain from returning a stolen item.

The second option — the blanket ability to say *harei shelcha lifanecha* under all circumstances — demonstrates the principle of *shinuy bamkomo omed*: the object is always the same object, always belonging to the original owner, who may always reclaim it regardless of what has happened to it. The *Gemara* then poses the obvious challenge: if *shinuy bamkomo omed* — if the object never changes identity — why should the *gazlan* ever be entitled to *notel shivcho*? If it remains the owner's property throughout, the owner should receive the enhanced value as well. The *Gemara* answers: *mipnei takanas hashavin* — because of the ordinance to facilitate repentance, discussed in the following section.

## Takanat Hashavin

### Improvement Example

The ruling that a *gazlan* who improved a stolen object may be compensated for his improvement is, on its face, astonishing. If *shinuy bamkomo omed* — if the object remains the owner's throughout — why should the *gazlan* receive any benefit at all? The answer is *takanas hashavin*: the rabbis recognized a practical obstacle to repentance and restitution. A *gazlan* who has invested significantly in improving a stolen object — fattening an animal, building from lumber, processing raw materials — faces a steep disincentive to return it. If he must surrender the entire enhanced value, he may well choose not to come forward at all.

The *takanah* therefore allows the *gazlan* to be compensated for the *shevach*, the increase in value attributable to his efforts. Consider the concrete example: one steals lumber worth one hundred *zuz* and fashions it into a table worth one thousand *zuz*. Biblically, the owner should receive the full table. The rabbis, however, instituted that the *gazlan* keeps the table and pays the original owner only one hundred *zuz* — the value at the time of the theft, *ki'she'as gezeilah*. The *gazlan* thus retains nine hundred *zuz* of *shevach*. This is a striking arrangement, and it invites the rueful observation that — absent fear of divine punishment or social sanction — the legal structure alone provides remarkably little disincentive to steal. A *gazlan* who is caught need only return the original value, keeping all appreciation. The *takanah*, however, is not designed to benefit the *gazlan* as such; it is designed to bring stolen property back into the legitimate economy and to facilitate the owner's compensation, even if incomplete. In that sense it serves society's interest, not merely the thief's conscience.

## Shinuy Bamkomo Omed…

### R. Yishmael Peah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: מִצְוַת פֵּאָה – לְהַפְרִישׁ מִן הַקָּמָה. לֹא הִפְרִישׁ מִן הַקָּמָה – מַפְרִישׁ מִן הָעוֹמָרִים. לֹא הִפְרִישׁ מִן הָעוֹמָרִים – מַפְרִישׁ מִן הַכְּרִי עַד שֶׁלֹּא מֵרְחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **What is** the source that indicates that **Rabbi Yishmael** holds that an item that undergoes a change is still considered to have the same status that it had before the change? The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The ideal way to fulfill the **mitzva of produce in the corner of the field, which is given to the poor [*pe'a*],** is **to separate** it **from the standing** grain, i.e., grain that has not been harvested. If **one did not separate** it **from the standing** grain, he **separates** it **from the sheaves** of grain that have already been harvested. If he **did not separate** it **from the sheaves,** he **separates** it **from the pile** of grain, **as long as** he **has not** yet **smoothed** the pile.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵרְחוֹ – מְעַשֵּׂר וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמְרוּ: אַף מַפְרִישׁ מִן הָעִיסָּה וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If he **smoothed** the pile of grain, activating the obligation to tithe the produce, he first **tithes** the grain **and** then **gives** a portion of the tithed produce **to** the poor as *pe'a*, so that the poor will not have to tithe what they receive. Additionally, **they said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael:** If he did not separate *pe'a* during any of the aforementioned stages and he made a dough from the grain, **he separates** *pe'a* **even from the dough and gives it to** the poor. This indicates that even if the grain was changed, one is not exempt from the obligation of *pe'a*. The Gemara has now clarified the sources Abaye alluded to when he listed all the *tanna'im* who hold that despite a change, the changed item remains in its place.

The final member of Abaye's grouping is Rabbi Yishmael, whose case concerns the obligation of *peah* — leaving the corner of a field for the poor. The *braisa* sets out a graduated framework: if one did not separate *peah* from the standing grain, one may do so from the sheaves; if not from the sheaves, from the threshed pile — *kari*; if not from the *kari*, one may still do so up until the *meiruach*.

The *meiruach* — the smoothing of the grain heap — is the halachic trigger for the obligation of *terumos u'maasros*. Its significance for the gifts to the poor is that all such gifts — *peah*, *leket*, *shichcha* — are normally exempt from *terumos u'maasros*, so the poor may receive them without further obligation. Once *meiruach* has occurred, however, the grain becomes obligated in *terumos u'maasros*, which changes the character of what the poor receives. From that point, one must first separate *terumos u'maasros* and then give the net amount to the poor.

Rabbi Yishmael goes further still: *af mafrish min ha'isa v'nosein lo* — even if the grain has been converted into flour and kneaded into dough, one may still separate the *peah* equivalent and give it to the poor. This is striking, because by the stage of dough the object has undergone substantial transformation. Nevertheless, Rabbi Yishmael treats it as the same entity for the purposes of the poor's entitlement — a clear expression of *shinuy bamkomo omed*.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אִיכְּפוּל כֹּל הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי קָאָמְרִי: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בְּדָבָר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Did all these *tanna'im* go to** so much trouble in **an effort [*ikhpal*] to teach us** a *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** which is presumably not accepted as normative? Abaye **said to** Rav Pappa: **This is what they are saying: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not engage in a dispute with regard to this matter.** All of the aforementioned *tanna'im* hold that even Beit Hillel agree that a change in the form of an item does not impact its status.

Rav Pappa challenges Abaye's grouping with a sharp observation: does Abaye mean to say that all of these *Tannaim* hold like Beis Shammai? It seems implausible that such diverse authorities across different areas of *halacha* would all converge on Beis Shammai's position. Abaye responds that his claim is not that they all hold *precisely* like Beis Shammai. Rather, even Beis Hillel — who disputes Beis Shammai's position on *esnan* — would not dispute the principle in all the other cases. The shared orientation of these *Tannaim* is that the threshold for recognizing a genuine *shinuy* is high; in their respective domains, each one insists that the changes under discussion fall below that threshold.

## Rava's Nuanced Reading

### Rava on Dyeing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה הָתָם, אֶלָּא בְּצֶבַע – הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לְהַעֲבִירוֹ עַל יְדֵי צָפוֹן;
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where** can it be proven that all of the aforementioned *tanna'im* hold that an item that undergoes a change is still considered to have the same status that it had before the change? One can say that the reasons for their statements are due to other factors. **Perhaps Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda states** his opinion, that it is not a significant change, **there,** in the case of the first of the sheared wool, **only with regard to dye,** which is a reversible change, **since one is able to remove it with soap.**

Rava now offers a sustained counter-reading of Abaye's grouping, arguing that none of these *Tannaim* in fact reflects a blanket principle of *shinuy bamkomo omed*. Each is responding to the specific features of his particular case. Beginning with Rabbi Shimon ben Yehudah: his ruling that dyeing wool is not a *shinuy* is not a general statement about *shinuy bamkomo omed*. It is driven entirely by the fact that dyeing, in the case at hand, is reversible — *yachol lehachziro al yedei tzava*. The dye can be removed. Remove that feature of reversibility, and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehudah's ruling disappears with it.

### Rava on Esnan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הָתָם, אֶלָּא לְגָבוֹהַּ – מִשּׁוּם דְּאִימְּאִיס;
>
> **Translation:** **And** perhaps **Beit Shammai state** their opinion **there,** in the case of a harlot, **only** with regard to an offering **to the Most High, because** of the fact **that** the item **has become repugnant,** in being used as payment for the services of a prostitute, and therefore it cannot be used for an offering even if its form has changed.

Beis Shammai's ruling that transformed *esnan* remains forbidden for the *Mizbeach* is similarly explained by specific features of that case. The *esnan* case involves objects designated for the *Mizbeach* — a domain of *gavo'ah*, the sacred. Objects offered to God are subject to heightened scrutiny, and the sense of *mi'us* — revulsion — that attaches to an *esnan* is so strong that it persists through transformation. This is a special *halacha* about sacred offerings, not a universal statement about the persistence of identity through change.

### Rava on Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב הָתָם, אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן בְּרָכָה – מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִצְוָה הַבָּאָה בַּעֲבֵירָה;
>
> **Translation:** **And** perhaps **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov states** his opinion **there,** where one robbed another of wheat, **only with regard to a blessing, because this is a mitzva** that **is performed through** commission of **a transgression,** but this does not indicate that a change is insignificant with regard to other matters.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's ruling, as already discussed, turns on *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* — not on any general principle that *shinuy* leaves identity intact. Rava makes this explicit: it is the *bracha* over stolen food, or more precisely the performance of a *mitzvah* through the fruits of theft, that drives the invalidation. One could, on this reading, hold that *shinuy koneh* as a general matter — that the stolen wheat truly became the thief's own property after the transformation — and still hold that the *mitzvah* of *challah* separation performed with it is invalid, because the avenue to that *mitzvah* was transgression. The analysis thus belongs entirely to the framework of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah* and carries no implications for *shinuy* doctrine.

### Rava on Reversibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הָתָם אֶלָּא בְּהַכְחָשָׁה דְּהָדַר;
>
> **Translation:** **And** perhaps **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar states** his opinion **there,** where the condition of the animal changed, **only with regard to weakening** of the animal **that is reversible.** Since the animal's value can be restored by fattening it, the weakening is not deemed a significant change.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's ruling that a *gazlan* may say *harei shelcha lifanecha* when the animal has weakened is, according to Rava, limited to the specific circumstance of *kachush* — emaciation — which is reversible: *dehadar*. An animal that has grown thin can grow fat again; the change is not permanent. Rava therefore reads Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar not as rejecting *shinuy koneh* across the board, but as recognizing that a reversible deterioration does not trigger *shinuy koneh*. It should also be noted that even without the reversibility argument, a thinner cow is arguably still called a cow by the same name — *shinuy hashem* has not occurred — making it doubtful that such a change would constitute a *shinuy* at all, a point acknowledged by Tosafos.

### Rava on Matanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָתָם, אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן פֵּאָה – מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״תַּעֲזֹב״ יַתִּירָא! וְכִי תֵּימָא לִיגְמַר מִינֵּיהּ, מַתְּנוֹת עֲנִיִּים שָׁאנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And** perhaps **Rabbi Yishmael states** his opinion **there,** where one separated *pe'a* at a late stage, **only with regard to *pe'a*, because it is written: "You shall leave** them for the poor" **an additional** time. It is mentioned twice, in Leviticus 19:10 and Leviticus 23:22. One of these terms is superfluous, indicating that *pe'a* must be given to the poor under all circumstances, even if the grain was changed and made into dough. **And if you would say: Let us derive from** *pe'a* that in other halakhic domains the status of an item is not affected by its undergoing a change, *pe'a* cannot function as a source because **gifts to the poor are different** from other *halakhot*.

Rabbi Yishmael's ruling that *peah* must be given even from dough is explained, according to Rava, by the specific linguistic feature of the biblical text — the extra instance of *ta'azov* (*l'ani ulager ta'azov osam*) written in the *pesukim* governing agricultural gifts to the poor. The word *ta'azov* appears more than once in the relevant passages, and the *yeseira* — the superfluous usage — teaches that the obligation to give gifts to the poor persists even after the grain has undergone transformation. This is a special *derasha* applicable specifically to *matnos aniyim*, not a universal ruling that *shinuy eino koneh*.

## Matanot Aniyim

### Yishmael Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְבָעֵי רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן – דְּבָעֵי רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר שִׁינּוּי אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה? אוֹ דִלְמָא, בְּעָלְמָא קָסָבַר שִׁינּוּי קוֹנֶה; וְהָכָא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״תַּעֲזֹב״ יַתִּירָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: Rava's claim that no definitive conclusion with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael concerning a changed item is supported by the statement of another *amora* is **like the dilemma raised by Rabbi Yonatan, as Rabbi Yonatan raises a dilemma: What is the reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Yishmael?** Is it **because he holds** that **change does not** cause one to **acquire** an item? **Or perhaps he generally holds** that **change** does cause one to **acquire** an item, **but here,** in the case of *pe'a*, it is different **because** the term **"You shall leave" is written an additional** time.

The question of what actually drives Rabbi Yishmael's ruling is the subject of a formal *ibaya* in the *Gemara*. Rabbi Yonasan asks: *mai ta'ama deRabbi Yishmael?* — what is the underlying rationale? Does Rabbi Yishmael hold as a general principle that *shinuy eino koneh* — that change never transfers ownership — and therefore the poor person's entitlement survives even after the grain becomes dough? Or does Rabbi Yishmael in principle hold *shinuy koneh*, and the ruling here is driven entirely by the extra *ta'azov*, a *limud* specific to *matnos aniyim*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל מִשּׁוּום דְּקָסָבַר שִׁינּוּי אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה, ״תַּעֲזֹב״ יַתִּירָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? וְתוּ, לְרַבָּנַן – ״תַּעֲזֹב״ יַתִּירָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** Having quoted Rabbi Yonatan's dilemma, the Gemara asks: **And if you say** that **the reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Yishmael is that he holds that change does not** cause one to **acquire** an item, **why do I** need the **additional** term **"You shall leave," which the Merciful One writes** in the Torah? **And furthermore, according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that *pe'a* may not be taken from dough, **why do I** need **the additional** term **"You shall leave" that the Merciful One writes?**

The question is not merely academic. If Rabbi Yishmael's rationale is the universal principle that *shinuy eino koneh*, then the extra *ta'azov* is superfluous — the *halacha* would follow without it. Conversely, if Rabbi Yishmael holds *shinuy koneh* as a general matter, then the *ta'azov yeseira* is doing real work, supplying an exception specific to gifts to the poor. The *Gemara* notes that for the *Rabanan* — who hold that one need not give *matnos aniyim* from dough — the question arises in the opposite direction: what do they do with the extra *ta'azov*? Since by their view *shinuy* has occurred and the obligation has lapsed, why does the Torah repeat the word?

### Hefker Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: הַמַּפְקִיר כַּרְמוֹ, וְהִשְׁכִּים לַבֹּקֶר וּבְצָרוֹ – חַיָּיב בְּפֶרֶט וּבְעוֹלֵלוֹת וּבְשִׁכְחָה וּבְפֵאָה, וּפָטוּר מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This additional term **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who renounced ownership of his vineyard and arose early in the morning** before anyone else took possession of it **and harvested it is obligated in** the mitzva of **individual fallen** grapes left for the poor **[*peret*], and in** the mitzva of **incompletely formed clusters** of grapes left for the poor **[*olelot*], and in** the mitzva of **forgotten** clusters of grapes left for the poor **and in** the mitzva of ***pe'a*.** These are the four gifts to the poor that the Torah requires one to give from a vineyard. **But he is exempt from the** mitzva to **tithe** his produce, because this requirement does not apply to an ownerless field. The obligation to give gifts to the poor in this case is derived from the additional mention of the term "You shall leave."

The *Gemara* answers by citing a *braisa* about a vineyard declared *hefker*. If one declares his vineyard *hefker* — ownerless — in the middle of the night when no one is present to take possession, and then rises early in the morning and harvests it himself, he is still obligated in *peret*, *ololos*, *shichcha*, and *peah* — all the gifts to the poor — but is exempt from *maaser*. This is because *hefker* in principle terminates the obligation of *maaser*, and even a brief period of *hefker* status suffices for that purpose. However, the obligation of *matnos aniyim* persists despite the *hefker* interlude. This, says the *Gemara*, is the force of the extra *ta'azov*: even where circumstances would normally extinguish the obligation — here, the vineyard having passed through a state of *hefker* — the gifts to the poor must still be given. This is a distinct application of the *yeseira* unrelated to *shinuy*, which explains why the *Rabanan* require the extra word even as they hold that *shinuy* does terminate the obligation.

### Stringency of Matanot

An important structural distinction emerges from this discussion. The exemption from *maaser* can be triggered by *hefker* even for a brief period; the obligation of *matnos aniyim* is more persistent and cannot be extinguished in the same way. The obligations to the poor — *peah*, *leket*, *shichcha*, and the like — are governed by the language of *ta'azov*, which signals a duty to leave, to relinquish, to let go. That language generates an especially durable claim on behalf of the poor that survives circumstances — a fleeting *hefker*, a transformation of the grain — that would be sufficient to extinguish comparable obligations. It is worth noting that *maaser ani*, which is the *maaser* given to the poor in the third and sixth years of the *shemittah* cycle, is treated as standard *maaser* in this regard — the heightened persistence applies specifically to those gifts defined by the language of *ta'azov* in the relevant *pesukim*, not to *maaser ani* as such.

## Shmuel's Ruling

### Halacha Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the discussion of a stolen item that underwent a change. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** who says that even if the stolen animal deteriorated, it is returned to the owner in its current state.

The *Gemara* records: *amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel, halacha k'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. Given the discussion above, this ruling must be understood against the backdrop of the debate between Abaye and Rava as to what Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar actually holds.

### Shamin Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי?! וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין שָׁמִין לֹא לְגַנָּב וְלֹא לְגַזְלָן, אֶלָּא לִנְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **But did Shmuel** actually **say this? But doesn't Shmuel say: One does not appraise** the change in value, **neither for a thief nor for a robber. Rather,** they keep the stolen animal and pay back the victim with their own money; one appraises the change **only** for one obligated to pay **for damage?**

The *Gemara* immediately raises a contradiction. Shmuel himself stated elsewhere: *ein shamin lo l'ganav v'lo l'gazlan ela l'nezikin* — assessment of the remaining value is done only in cases of damage, not for a *ganav* or *gazlan*. The practical import of this ruling is as follows. In cases of *nezikin*, if a damaging party causes an animal to die or breaks an object, the damaged party retains the carcass or the broken pieces, and the damager pays only the difference between the original value and what remains. But for a *ganav* or *gazlan* who steals an animal and it dies while in his possession, *shinuy koneh* applies — he has acquired it through the change — and therefore he must pay the full original value of the animal. There is no *shamin*, no offset for the residual value of what remains. This clearly presupposes that *shinuy koneh* operates in the case of a *gazlan* whose animal dies — yet Shmuel simultaneously rules like Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who appears to hold *shinuy bamkomo omed*. How are these two rulings reconciled?

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר: כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הָתָם – בְּהַכְחָשָׁה דְּהָדַר, לָא קַשְׁיָא; כִּי קָאָמַר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, דְּשִׁינּוּי בִּמְקוֹמוֹ עוֹמֵד – בְּהַכְחָשָׁה דְּהָדַר, וְכִי קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָתָם: אֵין שָׁמִין לֹא לְגַנָּב וְלֹא לְגַזְלָן, אֶלָּא לִנְזָקִין – בְּהַכְחָשָׁה דְּלָא הָדַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rava, who said** earlier in response to Abaye: **When Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar states** his opinion **there,** he stated it only **with regard to weakening** of the animal **that is reversible,** this is **not difficult: When** Shmuel **says** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** who holds **that** despite **a change,** the changed item **remains in its place,** he stated this **with regard to weakening** of the animal **which is reversible,** in which case the change is insignificant. **And when Shmuel says there: One does not appraise** the change in value, **neither for a thief nor for a robber,** but rather one appraises the change **only** for one obligated to pay **for damage,** he stated this **with regard to weakening** of the animal **that is irreversible.**

According to Rava, there is no contradiction, because Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's ruling — as Rava has already argued — applies only to reversible deterioration: *b'hachasha d'hadar*. An animal that grows thin can recover. In that case, *shinuy bamkomo omed* applies — the change does not transfer ownership — and the *gazlan* may return the weakened animal. But an animal that dies, or suffers an irreversible injury such as a broken leg that will not heal — *hachasha d'lo hadar* — that is a genuine, permanent change, and *shinuy koneh* applies. When Shmuel rules *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*, he does so specifically with respect to reversible deterioration. When Shmuel rules *ein shamin l'ganav v'lo l'gazlan*, he is addressing irreversible change. The two rulings are thus perfectly consistent.

### Abaye's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְאַבָּיֵי, דְּאָמַר: כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר – בְּהַכְחָשָׁה דְּלָא הָדַר קָאָמַר, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Abaye, who said** that **when Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar states** his opinion, **he states it** even **with regard to weakening** of the animal **which is irreversible, what can be said?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי מַתְנֵי הָכִי – אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אָמְרוּ הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר. וְלֵיהּ לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Abaye taught** the statement of Shmuel **like this: Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** Some **said** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, but** Shmuel **himself does not hold accordingly.**

Abaye, however, reads Shmuel differently. In Abaye's view, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that *shinuy* never effects acquisition — a blanket principle with no exception for irreversible change. On that reading, Shmuel's two rulings genuinely conflict, and Abaye resolves the tension by reattributing the ruling: *amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel amru* — Rav Yehuda reports in Shmuel's name that *there are those who say* the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. On Abaye's reading, Shmuel is not himself endorsing that position; he is merely transmitting it as an opinion of others. The dominant read, however — and the one that coheres far more naturally with the overall discussion — is Rava's: the *Tannaim* in Abaye's grouping are not rejecting *shinuy* as a principle but operating with a high threshold for what constitutes a qualifying *shinuy*, and Shmuel's two rulings target different categories of change accordingly.

## Rabbi Yochanan

### Biblical Gezeilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה – גְּזֵילָה הַנִּשְׁתַּנֵּית, חוֹזֶרֶת בְּעֵינֶיהָ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הַגְּזֵלָה אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: מִשְׁנָתֵנוּ! מִשּׁוּם תַּקָּנַת הַשָּׁבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the discussion of acquisition of a stolen item due to a change it underwent. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: By Torah law, a stolen item that has changed is returned as is, as it is stated: "And he shall restore that which he took by robbery"** (Leviticus 5:23). This indicates that he shall return it **in any case,** even if it has been changed. **And if you say:** In **our mishna** it is stated that if the stolen item is changed the robber gives monetary compensation rather than returning the item, that policy was instituted by the Sages **due to the ordinance** instituted **for the penitent.**

Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba cites Rabbi Yochanan: *devar Torah, gezeilah hamishtanit chozeret b'einah* — from a biblical perspective, stolen property, even after undergoing change, must be returned as it is. The proof-text is *ve-heshiv et ha-gezeilah asher gazal* — he shall return the stolen object that he stole — read as *mi-kol makom*, regardless of condition or transformation. Strikingly, this same phrase *asher gazal* is read elsewhere to mean *ka-asher gazal* — in the exact state in which it was stolen — implying that if it has changed, one need not return it. Rabbi Yochanan here reads the phrase in the diametrically opposite direction: return the object that you stole, in whatever state it now exists.

The implication runs in two directions simultaneously, and it is worth pausing to appreciate the ambiguity. On the one hand, this is a *chumra*: even if the *gazlan* has transformed the object into something far more valuable, the owner takes title to the enhanced version. On the other hand, it is a *kula*: even if the object has deteriorated drastically or died, the *gazlan* may return it as is and discharge his obligation. The *halacha* of *shinuy*, as elaborated by the *mishnah* and the rabbinic tradition, plainly recognizes change as a relevant factor. Rabbi Yochanan's statement is therefore positioned as a claim about the *Torah* level of the law — what the biblical verse mandates — not about the rabbinic superstructure built atop it.

## Takanat Hashavin…

### Purpose of Takanah

Rabbi Yochanan's position thus reframes the entire structure of the *shinuy koneh* doctrine. All of the cases in the *mishnah* and *Gemara* in which *shinuy* is recognized as transferring ownership — the *gazlan* who transformed lumber into furniture, wool into cloth, wheat into flour — are not reflections of a *d'Oraisa* principle that change effects acquisition. On the biblical level, Rabbi Yochanan insists, *gezeilah hamishntanit chozeret b'einah* — the stolen object, transformed or not, must be returned. The rabbinic recognition of *shinuy koneh* is entirely a *de-rabbanan halacha*, enacted as a *takanas hashavin* — an ordinance to clear the path to repentance and restitution.

The *takanah* operates as follows. One who stole lumber worth one hundred *zuz* and built it into a table worth one thousand should, biblically, return the table. The rabbis say: keep the table and pay the original value. The nine hundred *zuz* of *shevach* belongs to the *gazlan*. This is intended not as a reward for theft but as an inducement to come forward. If the *gazlan* must surrender the entire enhancement, the incentive to make restitution collapses. By allowing him to retain the *shevach* while paying the original value, the rabbis create conditions under which a *baal teshuvah* can actually come forward and make the victim whole — at least to the extent of the original loss.

The *takanah* is not, it should be emphasized, a sacrifice of the victim's interests on the altar of the thief's repentance. It is premised on the recognition that a system demanding full restitution of the enhanced object will produce *no* restitution at all. Partial restitution — the original value — is better for society and for the victim than none. And the prospect of *some* meaningful return, rather than complete forfeiture, is the only realistic way to bring *gazlanim* to make good on their obligations.

## Rabbi Yochanan…

### Objection to Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כִּסְתַם מִשְׁנָה, וּתְנַן: לֹא הִסְפִּיק לִיתְּנוֹ לוֹ עַד שֶׁצְּבָעוֹ – פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say that? But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with an unattributed mishna, and we learned** in a mishna with regard to first of the sheared wool (*Ḥullin* 135a): If the owner of the sheep **did not manage to give** the sheared wool **to** the priest **before he dyed it,** he is **exempt** from giving it to the priest. This indicates that dyeing the wool is a significant change.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ הַהוּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן – וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב שְׁמֵיהּ: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁגָּזַל עֵצִים מְשׁוּפִּין וַעֲשָׂאָן כֵּלִים, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ שִׁינּוּי הַחוֹזֵר לִבְרִיָּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **One of the Rabbis, whose name** was **Rabbi Ya'akov, said to them: It was explained to me** directly **by Rabbi Yoḥanan** that he was referring to a case **where he robbed** another of **sanded wood and fashioned it into vessels, which is a change** in which the item **can revert to its original state.** Consequently, the robber does not acquire the item by Torah law, but rather due to the ordinance instituted for the penitent.

The *Gemara* challenges Rabbi Yochanan's position: Rabbi Yochanan himself holds that *halacha* follows a *stam mishnah*, an unattributed ruling in the *Mishnah*. Yet the *Mishnah* regarding *reishis hageiz* states that if one did not manage to give the first shearing to the *kohen* before dyeing it, he is exempt — *patur*. This is the *mishnah* itself recognizing *shinuy* as a legally operative category, exempting the owner from his obligation to the *kohen* because the wool has been transformed. But this is not a case of a *gazlan* and certainly not a case where *takanas hashavin* is relevant — the wool belongs to the owner himself. If the *Torah* truly never recognizes *shinuy* in the case of *gezeilah*, why does the *Torah* apparently recognize *shinuy* in the context of *reishis hageiz*? Does the recognition of *shinuy* in one context not undermine the claim that it is irrelevant in the other?

Rabbi Yaakov clarifies that this difficulty was never the intent of Rabbi Yochanan's statement. Rabbi Yochanan never claimed that the *Torah* categorically refuses to recognize *shinuy*. He claimed specifically that *shinuy hachozer l'vriyaso* — a reversible change — would, on the *d'Oraisa* level, still require the return of the *gezeilah*. A change that is genuinely irreversible — transforming raw lumber into a permanent piece of furniture, or weaving wool into a garment — is recognized even by the *Torah* as effecting a transfer of ownership. What the rabbis enacted as *takanas hashavin* is limited to the case of *shinuy hachozer l'vriyaso*: where the change is theoretically reversible, the *Torah* would demand return; the rabbis, for the sake of *teshuvah*, ruled that even here the *gazlan* acquires. The *mishnah* of *reishis hageiz*, which involves true and irreversible transformation — dyeing with *kalla ilan* or similar — is thus not in tension with Rabbi Yochanan's position, because he never denied that the *Torah* recognizes genuinely irreversible change.

## Takanat Hashavin……

### Gazlanim Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַגַּזְלָנִין וּמַלְוֵי בְּרִבִּית שֶׁהֶחְזִירוּ – אֵין מְקַבְּלִין מֵהֶן. וְהַמְקַבֵּל מֵהֶן – אֵין רוּחַ חֲכָמִים נוֹחָה הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned the ordinance instituted for the penitent, the Gemara discusses other details of this ordinance. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Shevi'it* 8:11): With regard to **robbers or usurers that returned** either the stolen item or the interest to the one from whom they took it, **one** should **not accept** it **from them. And** with regard to **one who does accept** it **from them, the Sages are displeased with him,** since by doing so he discourages those who wish to repent.

The *Gemara* cites a *braisa*: *hagazlanim u'malvei b'ribbis* — professional thieves and those who lend with *ribbis* — *she-hechiziru* — who have come forward to return what they took — *ein mekablin mehen* — one does not accept it from them. Anyone who does accept it back, *ein ruach chachamim nocha heimenu* — the sages are displeased with him. Notably, Rashi interprets *ruach chachamim* here not as the sages themselves but as *ruach chochmah* — the spirit of wisdom — suggesting that one who accepts from such a person thereby distances himself from wisdom.

### Refusal Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּימֵי רַבִּי נִשְׁנֵית מִשְׁנָה זוֹ. דְּתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּשׁוּבָה, אָמְרָה לוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ: רֵיקָה! אִם אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה תְּשׁוּבָה, אֲפִילּוּ אַבְנֵט אֵינוֹ שֶׁלְּךָ! וְנִמְנַע וְלֹא עָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: הַגַּזְלָנִין וּמַלְוֵי רִבִּיּוֹת שֶׁהֶחְזִירוּ – אֵין מְקַבְּלִין מֵהֶם, וְהַמְקַבֵּל מֵהֶם – אֵין רוּחַ חֲכָמִים נוֹחָה הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This mishna,** i.e., the statement of the *Tosefta*, **was taught in the days of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident with regard to one man who desired to repent** after having been a thief for many years. **His wife said to him: Empty one [*reika*], if you repent** you will have to return all the stolen items to their rightful owners, and **even** the **belt** that you are wearing **is not yours, and he refrained and did not repent. At that time,** the Sages **said:** With regard to **robbers or usurers that returned** either the stolen item or the interest to the one from whom they took it, **one** should **not accept** it **from them. And** concerning **one who does accept** it **from them, the Sages are displeased with him.**

This ruling is initially puzzling to the point of seeming bizarre. If a *gazlan* has stolen my property and comes to return it, why should I refuse? Tosafos raises the practical objection: *maaseh bechol yom* — every day a *gazlan* comes to return stolen goods and we accept. Tosafos offers two resolutions. First, this ruling applied only to the *dor shel Rabbi* — the generation of Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi — and not to earlier or later periods. Second, and more compelling, the ruling applies only to *professionals* — those for whom the return of their stolen goods would effectively require them to liquidate all their assets, as in the story immediately following. For such a person, the awareness that return would be accepted stands as an obstacle to *teshuvah* rather than a facilitator of it. For someone who has stolen one or a few things, the dynamic is entirely different and the normal rules apply.

### Repentance Story

The *Gemara* records the *maaseh* behind this *takanah*. A certain person sought to do *teshuvah*. His wife said to him: *reika* — empty one — *im atah oseh teshuvah, afilu avnetekha shelekha* — if you do *teshuvah*, even your own belt doesn't belong to you; everything in this household has been acquired through theft. And so *venimna velo asah teshuvah* — he was deterred and did not repent.

It was at that moment that the rabbis declared: *hagazlanim u'malvei ribbis she-hechiziru, ein mekablin mehen* — one does not accept from professional *gazlanim* and *malvei b'ribbis* who come to return. The purpose is to lower the stakes sufficiently that such a person can begin the process of *teshuvah* without facing the immediate demand to surrender his entire livelihood.

The *takanah*, read carefully, is directed primarily at the *receiver* rather than the *giver*. The *gazlan* remains obligated to return — *machzirin* — and must make the effort. But the person to whom return is offered is advised not to accept. This creates a dynamic in which the *gazlan*'s obligation is preserved and his *teshuvah* is genuine, but the practical obstacles to beginning the process are reduced. Some people, knowing they have rabbinic guidance to refuse, will decline. Others may choose to accept anyway — and the *Gemara* records that while this is not the preferred course, *ein ruach chachamim nocha heimenu* is a statement of disapproval, not of prohibition. The *gazlan* still discharges his obligation by attempting return. And the fact that some will accept means the victim is not left wholly uncompensated.

## Heirs' Obligations

### Heirs Ribit Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הִנִּיחַ לָהֶם אֲבִיהֶם מָעוֹת שֶׁל רִבִּית, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהֵן יוֹדְעִין שֶׁהֵן רִבִּית – אֵין חַיָּיבִין לְהַחְזִיר. אִינְהוּ הוּא דְּלָא, הָא אֲבִיהֶם חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to children whose deceased **father left them money** paid **as interest, although they know that it is interest, they are not obligated to return** it. The Gemara infers: **They,** the children, **are** the ones **that are not** obligated to return it, **but their father** would have been **obligated to return** it, and his victims may accept his money.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּדִין הוּא דַּאֲבִיהֶם נָמֵי אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר; וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי בְּדִידְהוּ – מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִתְנֵי סֵיפָא: ״הִנִּיחַ לָהֶם אֲבִיהֶם פָּרָה וְטַלִּית וְכׇל דָּבָר הַמְסוּיָּים – חַיָּיבִין לְהַחְזִיר מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד אֲבִיהֶם״, תְּנָא רֵישָׁא נָמֵי בְּדִידְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **By right,** the *baraita* should have taught **that their father also** would **not** have been **obligated to return** it. **And** the fact **that** the *baraita* **teaches** this *halakha* **with regard to** the children is **because** of the fact **that** the *baraita* **wants to teach** a *halakha* in **the latter clause:** If **their** deceased **father left them a cow, or a garment, or any** other **specific item** he had stolen or taken as interest, **they are obligated to return** it **due to the honor of their father,** so that the item not serve as a reminder to all that their father transgressed. Since this *halakha* needs to be stated specifically with regard to the children, **the first clause** of the *baraita* **is also taught with regard to them.**

The *Gemara* raises a new case: *hiniach lahem avihem maot shel ribbis* — if a father left his children money that was collected as *ribbis* — *af al pi she'yodim she-hi ribbis, ein chayavim lehachazir* — even though they know it is interest money, the heirs are not obligated to return it. The *Gemara* clarifies: *in truth, even the father* would not have been obligated to return it — not because there is no underlying moral obligation, but because *takanas hashavin* means that we do not compel return from such persons, and the acceptance by the recipient is likewise discouraged. The reason the *braisa* frames the ruling in terms of the children rather than the father is that the *braisa* wants to introduce its contrasting conclusion in the following clause.

An important structural observation bears noting regarding the nature of *ribbis* obligations. When someone pays *ribbis*, the money paid is arguably the lender's property in a legal sense — there was a contractual obligation to pay it, even though the contract was halachically impermissible. This is unlike outright theft, where the stolen object categorically remains the owner's property. The obligation to return *ribbis* therefore falls specifically on the person who transgressed by taking it; it does not necessarily devolve upon his heirs as a matter of standard property law, which may explain why the heirs are not obligated even where they know the provenance of the money.

### Concrete Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד אֲבִיהֶם – חַיָּיבִין לְהַחְזִיר? אֶקְרֵי כָּאן: ״וְנָשִׂיא בְעַמְּךָ לֹא תָאֹר״ – בְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה עַמְּךָ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** is it true that **due to the honor of their father they are obligated to return** the item or money? **I will read here** the verse: "You shall not revile God, **nor curse a ruler of your people"** (Exodus 22:27), from which the Sages inferred that the prohibition against cursing a ruler is in effect only **with regard to** a ruler that **acts as** a member of **your people,** i.e., in accordance with Torah law. One who curses a wicked ruler does not violate this prohibition. Similarly, if one's father is wicked, the mitzva to honor him should not apply. Why would his children have to return items that he stole due to his honor?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַב פִּנְחָס: בְּשֶׁעָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה; הָכָא נָמֵי, בְּשֶׁעָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה. אִי עָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה, מַאי בָּעֵי גַּבֵּיהּ? אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי! שֶׁלֹּא הִסְפִּיק לְהַחְזִיר עַד שֶׁמֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It is **like that** which **Rav Pineḥas said** concerning a different case: This is a case **where he repented. Here too,** it is a case **where** the father **repented,** and since he is no longer wicked, his children are obligated to honor him. The Gemara asks: **If he repented, what was** the stolen item or interest **doing with him? He should have returned it** while he was still alive. The Gemara responds: It is a case **where he did not manage to return it before he died.** Consequently, the children must return the items in order to uphold their father's honor.

The contrasting case: *hiniach lahem avihem parah ve-tallit ve-chol davar mesuyam* — if the father left the children a cow, a *tallit*, or any concrete identifiable object — *chayabim lehachazir mipnei kevod avihem* — the children are obligated to return it, for the sake of their father's honor.

The *kevod avihem* rationale requires attention. The underlying verse — *venasi b'amcha lo sa'or* — prohibits cursing a *nasi* among your people, and the *drasha* reads *b'amcha* as *b'oseh ma'aseh amcha* — one who conducts himself as part of your people. The implication is that if the father behaved improperly — lending with *ribbis*, appropriating others' property — the ordinary duty of filial honor is attenuated. Yet where the father had done *teshuvah* before his death — as the *Gemara* establishes to be the case here — the children's duty to honor him is intact, and the visible presence of a stolen cow or *tallit* in their household constitutes a desecration of that honor. The *Gemara* therefore holds that they must return such concrete, identifiable objects.

### Repentance Case

The *Gemara* clarifies that the requirement of return in the case of concrete objects inherited from a repentant father applies even in a case where the father died before having the opportunity to return the object himself — *shelo hispik lehachazir ad shemes*. The *teshuvah* was genuine; the inability to complete restitution before death does not negate the obligation that devolves on the children with respect to identifiable stolen property. The irony, as noted above, is not lost: if the reason the object remains in the family's possession is precisely that the earlier *takanah* told people not to accept it, then the *takanah* itself has created the very situation it must now address.

## Shepherds/Tax Collectors

### Difficult Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָרוֹעִים וְהַגַּבָּאִין וְהַמּוֹכְסִין – תְּשׁוּבָתָן קָשָׁה, וּמַחְזִירִין לְמַכִּירִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a contradiction from another source. **Come** and **hear** the statement of another *baraita*: With regard to **shepherds** who allow their animals to graze in other people's fields, thereby stealing from the owners; **or** tax **collectors** who are hired to collect taxes on behalf of the government and collect excessive sums; **or tax collectors** who purchase the right to collect taxes themselves and collect unlawfully, **their repentance is difficult,** since they steal from the public. It is difficult for them to find every one of their victims in order to pay them restitution, **and they** must **return** what they have stolen **to whomever they recognize** as victims of their theft. This *baraita* indicates that thieves do return what they have stolen.

A *baraisa* addresses the specific situation of *roim* and *gabbaim* — shepherds and tax collectors — whose *teshuvah* is described as *kasha*, difficult. The reason is structural: these are people who, in the ordinary course of their occupations, took from many different people in small amounts over long periods. They do not know from whom they took, and even where they do know, the amounts are often too diffuse to reconstruct. The difficulty of their *teshuvah* is therefore not a moral failing but a practical reality arising from the nature of their transgressions.

### Known Victims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: מַחְזִירִין, וְאֵין מְקַבְּלִין מֵהֶם. וְאֶלָּא לָמָּה מַחְזִירִין? לָצֵאת יְדֵי שָׁמַיִם. אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי תְּשׁוּבָתָן קָשָׁה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Say** that **they return** it, **but one does not accept** it **from them.** The Gemara asks: **But why do they return** it if it will not be accepted? The Gemara responds: In order **to fulfill** their obligation to **Heaven.** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if they are not actually obligated to return what they have stolen, **why is their repentance difficult?**

With respect to those from whom they know they have taken, they attempt to return. Consistently with the *takanah* discussed throughout this section, however, those approached are instructed not to accept. The *gazlan* thus discharges his obligation of *latzeit yedei shamayim* — fulfilling his duty before Heaven — by making the effort, even where the return is declined.

### Unknown Victims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְשֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִין – יַעֲשֶׂה בָּהֶן צׇרְכֵי צִיבּוּר, וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בּוֹרוֹת, שִׁיחִין וּמְעָרוֹת! אֶלָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן קוֹדֶם תַּקָּנָה, כָּאן לְאַחַר תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **And** as for the money belonging to those **that they do not recognize** as their victims, **they should use** that money for **community needs. And Rav Ḥisda says:** This means providing **pits, ditches, and caves,** which benefit the general public. This indicates that a thief actually does pay back what he has stolen. **Rather,** this contradiction must be resolved differently. It is **not difficult: Here,** where the *baraita* states that he must actually return what he has stolen, it is referring to a time **before the ordinance** for the penitent was instituted. **There,** where the *baraita* states that one does not accept the repayment from a robber, it is referring to a time **after the ordinance** was instituted.

With respect to those from whom they have taken but cannot identify — the anonymous victims of years of overcharging or improper grazing — the mechanism of return is different. *Ya'aseh bahen tzorchei tzibbur* — they must devote the funds to community needs. Rav Chisda specifies: *borot, shichin u-mearot* — wells, cisterns, and caves for public use. By contributing to communal infrastructure that benefits the broader population from which the victims were drawn, the *baal teshuvah* makes a kind of collective restitution to those he cannot specifically locate.

### Before/After Takanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בְּשֶׁאֵין גְּזֵילָה קַיֶּימֶת – אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לְאַחַר תַּקָּנָה; וְלָא קַשְׁיָא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And now that Rav Naḥman says** that when the Sages say that he does not return what he has stolen, they refer only to a case **where the stolen item does not exist** in its initial form, and **you** can **even say** that **this and that,** both *baraitot*, are referring to a time **after the ordinance** was instituted, **and** it is **not difficult.**

The final resolution offered by the *Gemara* is that the apparent contradictions within the earlier sources — some implying that return is accepted, others that it is not — reflect a distinction between *kodem takanah* and *le-achar takanah*: before the *takanah* was enacted, return was accepted and the obligation to return was therefore actively demanding; that is why the *teshuvah* of shepherds and tax collectors was so difficult. After the *takanah*, the receivers are instructed not to accept, which changes the practical dynamic significantly and allows the *baal teshuvah* to proceed with the process even while remaining in a state of ongoing restitution effort. The *Gemara* closes here, noting that the further elaboration of how far this *takanah* reaches will continue in the sugya that follows.
