# Yoma 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Mixing Debate

### Avodah Overview

The daf opens at the very first word of 59a, picking up in the midst of discussing the *kohen gadol*'s *avodah* on Yom Kippur. To orient ourselves: after completing the sprinkling inside the *kodesh kodashim*, the *kohen gadol* slaughters the *se'ir*, performs the *se'ir*'s blood service — one and seven — and then performs the *par*'s blood service — likewise one and seven. This differs from other *korbanot* brought on the inside, such as the *par* for the collective sin of *Bnei Yisrael*, where the *kohen gadol* would stand further inward and sprinkle seven times toward the *parochet* — not one and seven — without specifically aiming at the *parochet*, though contact would be acceptable if it occurred. The *seder ha'avodah* reflects a movement from greater to lesser intensity even as it proceeds from outer to inner space. Now, the *gemara* turns to the service on the *mizbe'ach hazahav*, the golden altar.

### Mixed Blood Question

A preliminary question concerns the horns of the *mizbe'ach hazahav*: does the *kohen gadol* mix the blood of the *par* and the *se'ir* together before applying it to the horns, or does he keep them separate? The verse reads *midam hapar umidam hase'ir* — "from the blood of the bull and from the blood of the goat" — and the question is whether this phrase prescribes a mixture or merely lists the two bloods as distinct applications. Our *mishna* assumes that the bloods are mixed; later, when the *gemara* reaches the seven sprinklings on the top of the altar, it will note that the verse's phrasing of *min hadam* again clearly refers to the mixed blood.

### Corner Order Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״וְיָצָא אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ אֲשֶׁר לִפְנֵי ה׳״, זֶה מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב. הִתְחִיל מְחַטֵּא וְיוֹרֵד, מֵהֵיכָן הוּא מַתְחִיל? מִקֶּרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית, דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית. מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּא מַתְחִיל בַּחַטָּאת עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן — מִשָׁם הָיָה גּוֹמֵר עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It is stated: **"And he shall go out to the altar that is before the Lord, and make atonement for it; and he shall take of the blood of the bull, and of the blood of the goat, and place it upon the corners of the altar round about"** (Leviticus 16:18). **This** altar **is the golden altar,** since the outer altar is not before the Lord in the Sanctuary. **He began to cleanse** the altar, sprinkling the blood **downward. From where does he begin?** He begins **from the northeast corner,** and proceeds to the **northwest** corner, and then to the **southwest** corner, and finally to the **southeast** corner. A way to remember this is: At the **place** where **he begins** sprinkling the blood **for a sin-offering** sacrificed **on the outer altar,** the southeast corner, **there he finishes** sprinkling the blood **on the inner altar.**

Two issues govern the application of blood to the horns of the *mizbe'ach hazahav*. The first is whether the *kohen gadol* stands in one place and applies the blood to all four corners from where he stands — covering a space of an *amah al amah* — or whether he walks around the altar. The second, which the *gemara* is presently addressing, is the order in which he visits the four corners. Our *mishna* records that he begins at the northeast corner and proceeds counterclockwise, which is the standard movement in the *azarah*. A *braita* records that Rabbi Akiva holds he begins at the southeast corner and proceeds clockwise. The *gemara* explains that normally one would encounter the western corners first, but the verse *veyatza el hamizbe'ach* — "and he shall go out to the altar" — requires the *kohen gadol* to leave a space and begin from an outer, eastern corner. Since he originally passed by the western corner, he naturally wants to return to it first. Starting from the north means going counterclockwise — back to the northwest — and starting from the south means going clockwise — back to the southwest. The rule of *avid denafik* ("go back to the one you bypassed") thus produces a difference in clockwise versus counterclockwise direction depending on which eastern corner one begins at.

## Clockwise Explanations

### Missed Corner Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִתְחִיל מְחַטֵּא וְיוֹרֵד. מֵהֵיכָן הָיָה מַתְחִיל? מִקֶּרֶן מִזְרָחִית דְּרוֹמִית, דְּרוֹמִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מִזְרָחִית, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: מִקֶּרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית, דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **He began to cleanse downward. From where would he begin?** He would begin **from the southeast corner** of the altar, and from there he would turn to the **southwest** corner, onward to the **northwest** corner, and finally to the **northeast** corner. This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says:** He began **from the northeast corner,** from which he turned to the **northwest** corner, then to the **southwest** corner, and finished at the **southeast** corner.

The first explanation for Rabbi Akiva's clockwise circuit is precisely the practical logic just described: since one must start at an eastern corner due to *veyatza el hamizbe'ach*, the natural instinct is to then proceed first to the western corner that was bypassed. Starting from the southeast leads one to the southwest next — a clockwise movement. Starting from the northeast, as Rabbi Yose HaGlili holds, leads one to the northwest — a counterclockwise movement. The underlying debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yose HaGlili, then, is whether to begin in the south or the north, which is a question the *gemara* addresses separately.

### Walking vs. Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִי סְבִירָא לַן הַקָּפָה בָּרֶגֶל — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּיָלְפִינַן פְּנִים מִחוּץ. וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר הַקָּפָה בַּיָּד, וּמָר סָבַר הַקָּפָה בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **If we hold** that the **encircling** is performed **by foot,** i.e., the priest walks around the inner altar, **everyone agrees that we learn** the method of sprinkling **inside** from the sprinkling **outside. And here they disagree about this** matter: **One Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that the priest stands in his place and sprinkles on all the corners from there, which means his **encircling** is performed **by hand; and one Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that the **encircling** is done **by foot.**

The *gemara* offers a second explanation. *Ve'iba'it eima* — if you prefer, one may explain the clockwise movement differently. Were we to hold that *hakafa beregel* — the circuit is performed by walking around the altar — then everyone, including Rabbi Akiva, would agree that one turns counterclockwise, exactly as one does throughout the *azarah*. *Dekulei alma lo paligi depenino yemina* — there is no dispute that one turns to the right, i.e., counterclockwise. In that case, the argument between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yose HaGlili is not about clockwise versus counterclockwise at all. Rather, the dispute is *ha beha kamiflegi*: one *tanna* holds *hakafa beyad* — the circuit is performed by standing in place and moving the hand — while the other holds *hakafa beregel* — one actually walks around. If *hakafa beregel*, all agree: counterclockwise. If *hakafa beyad*, and one is simply standing and placing blood at the four corners with one's hand, there is room to deviate from the standard counterclockwise convention.

A third version follows: *ve'iba'it eima*, everyone in fact agrees it is *hakafa beyad* — no one walks around this altar, which is only an *amah al amah*. The debate is whether when performing *hakafa beyad*, one follows the same convention of moving counterclockwise that governs walking (*yalfinan miyad miregel*), or whether one does not draw that analogy (*lo yalfinan*). Rabbi Akiva may hold that standing in place and placing blood with the hand need not mirror the directionality of walking, and therefore clockwise is permitted. This does not yet explain *why* one would go clockwise — only that it is not prohibited.

### Hakafa Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הַקָּפָה בַּיָּד, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר יָלְפִינַן יָד מֵרֶגֶל, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא יָלְפִינַן. וְסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הַקָּפָה בַּיָּד? וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּמְקוֹמוֹ הָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמְחַטֵּא, מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִדְשַׁנִּינַן מֵעִיקָּרָא: מָר סָבַר הַקָּפָה בַּיָּד, וּמָר סָבַר הַקָּפָה בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **Everyone** agrees that **the encircling** was performed **by hand, and here they disagree about this** matter: **One Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that **we derive** the *halakhot* of an encircling performed by **hand from** those of an encircling by **foot,** and therefore the ritual of the inner altar is the same as that of the outer altar. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds that we do not derive** the encircling performed by hand from the encircling done by foot. § The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Yosei HaGelili hold** that **the encircling** is performed **by hand? But from** the fact **that it is taught** in **the latter clause** of the mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer says: He stood in** one **place and sprinkled** the blood from there, it can be learned **by inference** that **the first *tanna*,** whom the Gemara identified as Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, **does not maintain** that the rite is performed in this manner. **Rather, it is clear as we originally answered,** that **one Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that the **encircling** is performed **by hand; and one Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that the **encircling** is performed **by foot.**

The *gemara* now challenges the third explanation. Can it really be said that Rabbi Yose HaGlili holds *hakafa beyad*? The end of our *mishna* states: *Rabbi Eliezer omer bimkomo haya omed umechatei* — Rabbi Eliezer says he would stand in his place and apply the blood. The *gemara* notes: *michlal* — it follows implicitly — that the *tanna kamma* of the *mishna* disagrees with Rabbi Eliezer and holds that one does walk around. And that *tanna kamma*, as the *braita* reveals, is Rabbi Yose HaGlili. Therefore, Rabbi Yose HaGlili clearly holds *hakafa beregel*, and the third explanation collapses. The *gemara* concludes: *ela mechvarta kedi'shamin'ei me'ikara* — the better answer is what we said originally. Rabbi Yose HaGlili holds *hakafa beregel* and goes counterclockwise; Rabbi Akiva — on at least one account — holds *hakafa beyad* and therefore may go clockwise.

To summarize the three explanations for Rabbi Akiva's clockwise circuit: first, the law of returning to the bypassed corner, which produces a different circuit depending on where one starts; second, that Rabbi Akiva holds *hakafa beyad* and therefore is not bound by the directionality of walking; and third — though ultimately rejected — that everyone holds *hakafa beyad* and the debate is whether hand movement follows the same convention as foot movement.

## Altar Comparison

### Inner vs. Outer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר: סָבִיב דְּמִזְבֵּחַ פְּנִימִי כְּסָבִיב דְּמִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, וּמָר סָבַר: כּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ פְּנִימִי בִּמְקוֹם חֲדָא קֶרֶן דְּמִזְבֵּחַ חִיצוֹן קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **they disagree about this** matter: **One Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that the **perimeter of** the **inner altar is like the perimeter of** the **outer altar, and one Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that the **entire inner altar stands in place of one corner of** the **outer altar.** Since the entire inner altar is only one cubit by one cubit, like a single corner of the outer altar, the *halakhot* of the outer altar are not relevant to the inner altar.

The *gemara* now explains the underlying disagreement about whether one walks around the *mizbe'ach pnimi* or stands in place. *Mar savar savar de'mizbe'ach pnimi kishavrei de'mizbe'ach chitzon* — one position holds that just as the verse mandating blood application to the horns of the outer altar implies walking around, so too the parallel language regarding the inner altar implies walking around. The two are treated symmetrically.

The opposing position — that of Rabbi Eliezer in the *mishna*, or Rabbi Akiva in this discussion — holds: *kulei mizbe'ach pnimi bimkom chad karna de'mizbe'ach chitzon kai* — the entire inner *mizbe'ach* occupies the same footprint as a single horn of the outer altar, both measuring an *amah* by an *amah* (the inner altar is an *amah* by an *amah* by two *amot* in height, compared to the outer altar's horn which is an *amah* by an *amah* by an *amah*). Just as a priest applying blood to a single horn of the outer altar stands at that horn and places the blood without walking, so too the priest at the inner altar stands in place and applies the blood to all four corners from where he stands.

### Theological Implications

This structural debate opens onto a deeper conceptual question. Is the *mizbe'ach pnimi* an independent entity, to be treated in parallel with the outer altar in its own right? Or is it, when functioning in its blood-atonement capacity, more properly understood as a kind of extension or subsidiary of the outer altar — analogous to one of its horns?

The observation is apt that characterizing the entire inner altar as equivalent to a single horn of the outer altar somewhat diminishes its independent status. Yet one might refine the point: when the inner altar functions as the site of *ketoret*, it stands entirely in its own right. It is specifically in its blood-*kappara* role — where it parallels the atonement function of the outer altar — that it may be understood as an extension of that altar, like one of its horns. The deeper you penetrate into the *Mikdash*, the more you might expect independence. Yet in the specific context of blood service, the inner altar's role may be seen as a prolongation of what happens outside — a glimpsing, through the inner chamber, of what the outer altar is ultimately about. The inner altar offers, as it were, an occasional window into the blood-*kappara* function whose primary theater is the outer altar.

## Two Priests Testimony

### Two Priests Found

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: שְׁנֵי כֹהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים נִשְׁתַּיְּירוּ בְּמִקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן, זֶה אוֹמֵר: בְּיָדִי הִקַּפְתִּי, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: בְּרַגְלַי הִקַּפְתִּי. זֶה נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִדְבָרָיו, וְזֶה נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael said: Two High Priests remained from** the days of **the First Temple. This one says: I encircled by hand** and sprinkled, and I did not encircle the perimeter of the inner altar by foot. **And that one says: I encircled by foot. This one gave a reason for his statement, and that one gave a reason for his statement.**

*Tanya* — a *braita* records: *amar Rabbi Yishmael, shenei kohanim gedolim nishtayru be-Mikdash Rishon* — Rabbi Yishmael said: two *kohanim gedolim* survived to the time of the destruction of the First Temple. This itself is remarkable, since the First Temple period is associated with a stable, non-rotating *kehuna gedola*, unlike the Second Temple era with its frequent succession of high priests. That two *kohanim gedolim* remained at the *churban* is historically striking, and what follows is even more so.

### Hand vs. Foot

*Zeh omer beyadi hikafti* — one of them said: when I performed the *avodah* of Yom Kippur, I performed the circuit with my hand; I stood in place. *Ve-zeh omer beraglai hikafti* — the other said: I walked around by foot. Here we have firsthand testimony, from practitioners who actually performed the service, disagreeing on how they did it.

### Scriptural Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִדְבָרָיו: סָבִיב דְּמִזְבֵּחַ פְּנִימִי כְּסָבִיב דְּמִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, וְזֶה נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִדְבָרָיו: כּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ פְּנִימִי בִּמְקוֹם חֲדָא קֶרֶן דְּחִיצוֹן קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The one** who said that he encircled by foot **gave** the following **reason for his statement: The perimeter of the inner altar** is **like the perimeter of the outer altar,** which is encircled by foot for sprinkling. **And the one** who said that he encircled by hand **gave** the following **reason for his statement:** The **entire inner altar stands in place of one corner of** the **outer** altar. Just as for one corner of the outer altar, the priest sprinkles the blood by hand, the same applies to the entire inner altar.

What is remarkable is that neither priest appeals simply to tradition. *Zeh noten ta'am lidevrav ve-zeh noten ta'am lidevrav* — each one provides a scriptural rationale. The one who walked around cited: *saviv de-Mizbe'ach Pnimi ke-saviv de-Mizbe'ach Chitzon* — the language of encircling the inner altar is parallel to the language of encircling the outer altar; just as you walk around the outer, you walk around the inner. The one who stood in place cited: *kulei Mizbe'ach Pnimi bimkom chad karna de-Mizbe'ach Chitzon kai* — the entire inner altar is like a single horn of the outer altar, and at a single horn you stand in place.

What is striking is that these practitioners — presumably with access to living memory of the service across generations — are reasoning directly from *psukim*, not from received tradition handed down by predecessors. The *kohanim gedolim* appear to have had substantial latitude in working out the details of the *avodah* from scripture, rather than from an unbroken chain of practice.

### Pluralistic Resolution

The *Rashash* is troubled by this, because another *gemara* asserts that there was no genuine *machloket* in Israel from the time of Moshe until the death of Yosef ben Yo'ezer — the earliest recorded dispute being the question of *semicha* on *korbanot* brought on *Yom Tov*, which arose only toward the end of the Second Temple period. How then can there be a dispute between two *kohanim gedolim* at the end of the First Temple?

The *Rashash* — found in the *Tosafot Yeshanim* on 59b — offers two answers. The first: had they actually convened and taken a formal vote, they might have reached a conclusion. The fact that each reasoned independently to a different practice does not constitute a true *machloket*. The second answer, which he finds supported in the *Yerushalmi*, is the more penetrating one: *i avid hachi avid, i avid hachi avid* — either way is valid. Each priest reasoned from scripture to a legitimate practice, and neither was asserting that the other's approach was wrong. There is no true dispute here — just two plausible approaches to an unresolved question. As the *Rashash* frames it, citing the *Yerushalmi*: according to Rabbi Yishmael's understanding, they do not disagree at all.

This reframing is genuinely illuminating. Rather than reading the *braita* as a record of an ancient *machloket*, one can read it as a record of two legitimate traditions, each grounded in scripture, each reflecting a coherent understanding of the inner altar's relationship to the outer. The fact that each priest quotes *psukim* rather than tradition supports this reading: they are not asserting exclusive validity — they are each explaining the basis for their own practice.

## Finger Movement Direction

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּמְקוֹמוֹ הָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמְחַטֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The priest would not circle the altar; rather, **he stood in** one **place and sprinkled** the blood from there. Since the altar was only one square cubit, he could sprinkle the blood on all four corners without moving.

The *mishna* records: *Rabbi Eliezer omer bimkomo haya omed umechatei* — Rabbi Eliezer says the *kohen gadol* would stand in his place and apply the blood. This position, as established, reflects the view that *hakafa beyad* governs the inner altar. Standing in one place, with different corners at different distances from the priest, raises a further question: in which direction does the finger move when applying the blood — *mimala lemata* (from above to below, i.e., up-down) or *mimata lemala* (from below to above, i.e., down-up)?

The underlying concern is practical: when the corner being anointed is directly in front of the priest, a down-up movement risks causing the blood to run down onto the priest's sleeve, soiling his garments. An up-down movement is therefore the required default when facing the corner directly. When the corner is distant, however, the concern about soiling the sleeve recedes, and a down-up movement may be easier, especially when stretching across a distance.

### Rabbi Meir Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּמְקוֹמוֹ עוֹמֵד וּמְחַטֵּא, וְעַל כּוּלָּן הָיָה נוֹתֵן מִמַּעְלָה לְמַטָּה, חוּץ מֵאוֹתָהּ שֶׁבַּאֲלַכְסוֹן, שֶׁנּוֹתֵן מִמַּטָּה לְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita*, later *tanna'im* disagreed with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer. **Rabbi Meir says** that **Rabbi Eliezer says** as follows: **He stood in** one **place and sprinkled, and on all** of the corners **he presented** the blood **from above downward,** so as not to drip blood down the sleeve of his garment, **except for that** corner **on the diagonal [*alakhson*]** across from him. Since it was difficult for him to sprinkle on that corner from top to bottom, **he sprinkled from below upward.**

A *braita* records a debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda as to what exactly Rabbi Eliezer said. According to Rabbi Meir's version: *Rabbi Eliezer omer bimkomo haya omed umechatei* — he stood in place — *ve'al kulan haya nosein mimala lemata* — and for all the corners he performed the movement from above to below, up-down — *chutz me'oso sheb'alachson* — except for the one at the diagonal, the farthest corner. That one, which requires the greatest reach, he performed *mimata lemala*, from below to above, down-up. The logic: when you must stretch to the most distant corner, pulling the finger upward as it moves across the altar surface keeps the blood from falling off the tip; a downward motion at such a distance is harder to control. Consequently, Rabbi Meir's version of Rabbi Eliezer defaults to up-down everywhere, with a single exception for the most distant corner.

### Rabbi Yehuda Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּמְקוֹמוֹ עוֹמֵד וּמְחַטֵּא, וְעַל כּוּלָּן הוּא נוֹתֵן מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה, חוּץ מִזּוֹ שֶׁהָיְתָה לְפָנָיו מַמָּשׁ, שֶׁנּוֹתֵן מִמַּעְלָה לְמַטָּה, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא נִיתַּוְּוסָן מָאנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **Rabbi Eliezer says: He stood in** one **place and sprinkled, and on all of** the corners **he sprinkled from below upward,** as it is more convenient to sprinkle in that manner, **except for that one which was directly before him, on which he would present from above downward.** The reason is **so as not to dirty his garments** with blood. If he sprinkled on the corner next to him from below upward, the blood might fall on his clothes, and he would have to change garments, as dirty priestly garments may not be worn for the Temple service.

Rabbi Yehuda offers a different version of Rabbi Eliezer's position. He too agrees that Rabbi Eliezer stood in place. But: *ve'al kulan hu nosein mimata lemala* — for all corners the default movement is down-up — *chutz mizu shehayesa lefanav mamash* — except the one directly in front of him — *shenosein mimalla lemata* — that one he performs up-down — *ki heichi d'lo litsran manei* — so that his garments should not become soiled.

According to Rabbi Yehuda's version of Rabbi Eliezer, down-up is the inherently easier movement and the natural default. The sole exception is the corner directly in front, where the risk of blood running down the sleeve requires the up-down motion. According to Rabbi Meir's version, up-down is the default, with down-up permitted only where the stretch makes it necessary.

Crucially, both versions agree on two points: the corner directly in front is always up-down, and the corner farthest away is always down-up. The debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda concerns the two intermediate corners — are they governed by the default of up-down (Rabbi Meir) or the default of down-up (Rabbi Yehuda)?

By extension, when the *kohen gadol* is walking around the outer altar (*mizbe'ach hachitzon*), every horn is directly in front of him at the moment he applies the blood, and everyone agrees the motion there is up-down.

## Seven Sprinklings Site

### Tahoro Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִזָּה מִמֶּנּוּ עַל טׇהֳרוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ. מַאי ״טׇהֳרוֹ״? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: פַּלְגֵיהּ דְּמִזְבֵּחַ, כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: ״טְהַר טִיהֲרָא וְהָוֵי פַּלְגֵיהּ דְּיוֹמָא״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **He sprinkled** blood **on** the **pure** gold **[*tohoro*] of the altar.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the term ***tohoro*? Rabba bar Rav Sheila said:** It means **half** of the **altar, as people** commonly **say: *Tehar tihara*,** the light of noon shines **and it is the middle of the day.** Here, too, *tohoro* of the altar means half the altar, i.e., he sprinkled on the midpoint of the altar wall.

After completing the circuit of the four horns, the *kohen gadol* performs seven sprinklings on the top of the *mizbe'ach hazahav*. The verse governing this reads: *vehizah alav min hadam sheva pe'amim* — and he shall sprinkle upon it from the blood seven times. The verse uses the phrase *al tohoro shel mizbe'ach*, which our *mishna* echoes. But what precisely is *tohoro*? Where on the altar are these seven sprinklings to be performed?

Rabbah bar Rav Shila proposes: *palgei demizbe'acha* — the half of the altar. He invokes the linguistic parallel to *tzahornayim* — midday — where *tohar* or *tzohar* has come to mean the midpoint of the day. Thus *tohoro shel mizbe'ach* means the middle of the altar — not the top, but the midpoint on the wall. Rashi interprets this as referring to the midpoint of the wall, analogous to how blood is applied to the midpoint of the outer altar's sides.

### Clearing Ashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כְּשֶׁהוּא מַזֶּה — אֵינוֹ מַזֶּה לֹא עַל גַּבֵּי הָאֵפֶר וְלֹא עַל גַּבֵּי הַגֶּחָלִים, אֶלָּא חוֹתֶה גֶּחָלִים אֵילָךְ וְאֵילָךְ, וּמַזֶּה. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: עַל גִּלּוּיֵהּ דְּמִזְבֵּחַ, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וּכְעֶצֶם הַשָּׁמַיִם לָטוֹהַר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection: When he sprinkles** on the inner altar, **he sprinkles neither on top of the ash nor on top of the coals; rather, he rakes** and removes **the coals to both sides and sprinkles.** This indicates that this sprinkling was performed on top of the altar, not on its side. **Rather, Rabba bar Rav Sheila** retracted his previous interpretation and said: On *tohoro* of the altar means **on the exposed** area **of** the **altar, as it is written: "And the like of the very heaven for clearness [*letohar*]"** (Exodus 24:10), which shows that *tohar* is an expression of clarity.

A *Tosefta* is then cited as a challenge: *keshehu mazeh, eino mazeh lo agab ha'efer velo agab hagechalim* — when he sprinkles, he does not sprinkle on top of the ashes or on top of the coals. Rather, *choteh gechalim ilach ve'ilach* — he sweeps the coals to either side — and then performs the sprinkling. This *braita* makes clear that the sprinkling is done on the *top* of the altar. If the ashes and coals must be pushed aside to create an exposed surface for the sprinkling, then the seven are performed on the roof, not on the side wall.

In light of this, Rabbah bar Rav Shila revises his answer: *al giluyeih demizbe'acha* — on the exposed part of the altar. The coals are pushed aside, the golden surface is exposed, and the blood is sprinkled there. This is the *tahoro* — the uncovered, pure surface of the altar top. The *gemara* cites the verse *uke'etzem hashamayim latahar* — "like the very sky in its purity" — as an evocative parallel, though one cannot help but notice the resonance: the exposure of the altar's pure surface recalls that extraordinary moment at Sinai, where the elders beheld the divine presence, and the text describes the sky in its clarity. The uncovering of the altar's surface for this most intimate blood service of the year calls forth an image of radical visibility, of stripping away what ordinarily conceals. The verse at Sinai adds that they sat and ate — an act of consummate intimacy that would, of course, be entirely out of place on Yom Kippur. The blood, seven times applied to this exposed surface, occupies that liminal space between concealment and encounter.

## North-South Placement

### North vs. South

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, חֲנַנְיָא אוֹמֵר: בְּצַד צְפוֹנִי הוּא נוֹתֵן, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּצַד דְּרוֹמִי הוּא נוֹתֵן. בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: פִּיתְחָא בְּדָרוֹם קָאֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: פִּיתְחָא בְּצָפוֹן קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Ḥananya says:** The priest **presents** seven sprinklings **on the north side** of the altar, and **Rabbi Yosei says: He presents** them **on the south side.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle do **they disagree?** The Gemara explains: **One Sage,** Ḥananya, **holds** that **the entrance** was **positioned in the south,** and therefore the High Priest begins the sprinklings from that side. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that **the entrance** was **positioned in the north,** and he therefore begins to sprinkle on the altar from the north side.

A *braita* records: *Chananya omer betzad tzfoni hu noten* — Chananya says the sprinkling is done on the northern side of the *mizbe'ach*. *Rabbi Yossi omer betzad dromi hu noten* — Rabbi Yossi says it is done on the southern side. This debate is directly connected to the earlier dispute between Rabbi Yose HaGlili and Rabbi Akiva about where one begins the circuit of the horns.

### Curtain Theory

The *gemara* asks: *bemai kamifligi* — what is the underlying dispute? *Mar savar pitcha bedarom kai* — one holds the opening is in the south; *umar savar pitcha betzafon kai* — the other holds the opening is in the north. The question is how many curtains separated the *heichal* from the *kodesh kodashim*. If there were two curtains, the *kohen gadol* would exit through the space between them from a point in the south; if there was only one curtain, he exits to the north. The direction of exit determines where he stands when he begins the circuit of the horns, which in turn determines where the circuit concludes, and it is at the conclusion of the circuit that the seven sprinklings are performed.

### Circuit End Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהָא, הֵיכָא דְּגָמְרָן מַתָּנוֹת דִּקְרָנוֹת, הָתָם יָהֵיב עַל גַּגּוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְטִהֲרוֹ וְקִדְּשׁוֹ״ — מָקוֹם שֶׁקִּדְּשׁוֹ, שָׁם טִיהֲרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Everyone** agrees **in any case** that in the place **where he finishes the presentations** of the **corners, that** is **where he places** the blood **on** the altar's **top.** They disagree only about the location of the final presentation, whether it is on the south or the north side. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this agreement? The Gemara answers that **the verse states:** "And he shall sprinkle of the blood upon it with his finger seven times, **and cleanse it and sanctify it"** (Leviticus 16:19), which indicates that the **place he sanctified** by sprinkling blood, the corner of the altar where he sprinkled last, **there he shall** also begin to **cleanse** and sprinkle on top.

This is the principle: *dekulei alma meiha heicha degamrinan matnot dekarnot hatam yahiv agago* — everyone agrees that the seven sprinklings on the top of the altar are performed at the same location where the circuit of the horns concluded. If one started from the northeast (Rabbi Yose HaGlili, one curtain, exit to the north), the circuit ends in the south, and the seven are done on the southern part of the roof. If one started from the southeast (Rabbi Akiva, two curtains, exit to the south), the circuit ends in the north, and the seven are done on the northern part.

The scriptural basis is: *Vetiaro vekidesho* — you shall purify it and sanctify it. *Mekom shekidesho, sham tiaro* — where you sanctified it, where you completed the circuit (*kivuvim* being *kiddush*), there you shall purify it (the roof sprinkling being *tihur*). Notably, the word *tiaro* evokes *tohoro shel mizbe'ach*, the language of the *mishna* — suggesting that *tihur* specifically is associated with the top of the altar and the seven sprinklings there.

## Remnant Blood

### Proximate Base Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם הָיָה שׁוֹפֵךְ עַל יְסוֹד מַעֲרָבִי שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן. דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאֶת כׇּל דַּם הַפָּר יִשְׁפּוֹךְ״, וְכִי נָפֵיק — בְּהָהוּא פָּגַע בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **And he would pour the remainder of the blood on the western base of the outer altar.** The Gemara explains: The reason is **that the verse states** with regard to the sin-offering bull of the High Priest during the rest of the year: **"And he shall pour out all the blood of the bull** at the base of the altar of burnt-offering, which is at the door of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 4:7), **and when he goes out** from the Sanctuary to pour the remainder of the blood, **he first reaches that** western side of the base of the altar.

After completing the seven sprinklings, the *kohen gadol* exits and spills the remnant blood (*sheraye hadam*) at the base (*yesod*) of the outer altar. The *mishna* states: *sheraye hadam haya shofech liyesod maaravi shel mizbe'ach hachitzon* — the remnant is spilled at the western base of the outer altar. For blood of ordinary *korbanot* performed outside, the remnant is spilled at the southern base. The underlying principle, as the *gemara* develops it, is proximity: one spills at the base one encounters first. Coming out of the *heichal*, one encounters the western base; coming down from the ramp after service at the outer altar, one encounters the southern base.

The *Tosafot* note that the verse invoked — *el yesod mizbe'ach ha'olah asher petach ohel moed* — appears in Vayikra 4, in the context of the *par he'elam davar*, one of the few *korbanot* also brought inside. *Petach ohel moed* is in the west, and the verse is applied by extension to the Yom Kippur service as well. The *braita* elaborates: perhaps *yesod hamizbeiach* in that verse refers to the southern base — *atah omer yesod dromit o eino ela yesod maaravit?* The answer is derived by analogy: from the case of exiting the *heichal*, where the verse explicitly names the western base, we derive the principle of proximity — *besamuch lo*, the nearest one. Once that principle is established, it applies equally to ordinary sacrificial service at the outer altar, where the nearest base after descending the ramp is the southern one.

### Yishmael vs. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן הָיָה שׁוֹפֵךְ עַל יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִית. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יְסוֹד הַמִּזְבֵּחַ — זֶה יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִית. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִית, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא יְסוֹד מַעֲרָבִית? אָמַרְתָּ: יִלְמַד יְרִידָתוֹ מִן הַכֶּבֶשׁ לִיצִיאָתוֹ מִן הַהֵיכָל. מָה יְצִיאָתוֹ מִן הַהֵיכָל בְּסָמוּךְ לוֹ, וְאֵי זֶה — זֶה יְסוֹד מַעֲרָבִי, אַף יְרִידָתוֹ מִן הַכֶּבֶשׁ, בְּסָמוּךְ לוֹ, וְאֵי זֶה — זֶה יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִי. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: זֶה וָזֶה יְסוֹד מַעֲרָבִי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי אוֹמֵר: זֶה וָזֶה יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִי. בִּשְׁלָמָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל קָסָבַר: יִלְמַד סָתוּם מִמְּפוֹרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further taught: **And he would pour** the remaining blood after the blood of an offering was sprinkled **on the outer altar, on its southern base. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **"The base of the altar"** (Leviticus 4:30), which is mentioned with regard to pouring the remainder of the blood of an individual offering, **is the southern base.** Do **you say** it is **the southern base? Or perhaps that is not** the case, but **rather** it is **the western base?** **You said: Let his descent from the ramp** of the outer altar after sprinkling blood from the sin-offerings **be derived from his exit from the Sanctuary** with the remaining blood in his hand: **Just as** upon **his exit from the Sanctuary** he pours the remainder of the blood **on** the side **closest to him, and which is that, it is the western base; so too,** upon **his descent from the ramp** of the outer altar after sprinkling blood from a sin-offering, he pours the blood **on** the side **closest to him, and which is that? It is the southern base,** as when he descends from the ramp he turns to the right, i.e., the east, which means the southern base is the one closest to him. **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael says:** Both **this and that,** the blood of an inner sin-offering and that of an outer sin-offering, were spilled at **the western base** of the altar. **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says:** Both **this and that** blood were spilled at **the southern base.** The Gemara asks: **Granted, Rabbi Yishmael maintains** that the *halakha* of outer sin-offerings, which is **not clarified** in the Torah, **is derived from** the inner sin-offerings, whose *halakha* is **explicit:** Just as the remains of the inner sprinklings are poured at the western base, so too, the remains of the outer sprinklings are poured at the western base.

A second *braita* records divergent positions. Rabbi Yishmael says: *zeh vezeh yesod maaravi* — both the Yom Kippur blood and the ordinary sacrificial blood are spilled at the western base. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai says: *zeh vezeh yesod dromit* — both are spilled at the southern base. Rabbi Yishmael's position is comprehensible: rather than deriving the principle of proximity from the case of leaving the *heichal* and extending it to the outer altar, he derives the fixed principle of *west* from the explicit verse and applies it universally. The inner sanctuary, as it were, is *koveia* — it sets the rule. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's position, however, is initially puzzling: the verse says *petach ohel moed*, which is in the west — why would he say the southern base?

### Altar Position Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי, מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: קָסָבַר פִּתְחָא בְּדָרוֹם קָאֵי. תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בִּדְבֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: זֶה וָזֶה יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִית. וְסִימָנָיךְ: מַשְׁכוּהּ גַּבְרֵי לְגַבְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** with regard to **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai, what is the reason** that he holds that both sets of remainders of blood are spilled at the southern base? **Rav Ashi said:** Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **maintains** that the **entrance** of the Sanctuary **is positioned at the south** side of the altar, i.e., the altar is not located in the middle of the courtyard but to the north. Consequently, the southern base of the altar is closest to the High Priest's exit from the Sanctuary. **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** a different version of his opinion, which they learned **in the school of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** Both **this and that,** the blood of an inner sin-offering and that of an outer sin-offering, were presented at **the southern base.** According to this version, Rabbi Yishmael changed his opinion and agreed with Rabbi Shimon. The Gemara comments: **And your mnemonic** to remember the shift in opinion is: **The men pulled the man,** i.e., the majority overruled the individual. In this case, the numerous students of Rabbi Shimon convinced the individual Sage, Rabbi Yishmael, to accept their ruling.

Rav Ashi resolves this: *kasavar pitcha badarom kai* — Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai holds that the entrance of the *ohel moed* was in the south of the altar's position. That is, the *mizbe'ach* was positioned such that when the *kohen gadol* exited the *heichal*, the base he actually encountered first was the southern base. According to his understanding of the altar's placement in the *azarah*, the southern base was the proximate one — and therefore *both* cases yield the southern base. The debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai thus reduces to a factual disagreement about where the *mizbe'ach hachitzon* was positioned relative to the exit of the *heichal*.

An addendum: the school of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, when transmitting Rabbi Yishmael's teaching, reported it as saying *zeh vezeh yesod dromit* — the same as their own position. Rashi softens this: the school tradition was that Rabbi Yishmael had in fact retracted his position. The *gemara* preserves the observation: *mashchuha gavra legabeih* — the school drew the person's view toward their own. Both the western and southern bloods ultimately drained into a common channel, flowed out through the *azarah*, and emptied into the *Nachal Kidron*, where they were sold; the proceeds raised the question of *meilah*.

## Meila and Blood

### Meila Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ מִתְעָרְבִין בָּאַמָּה וְיוֹצְאִין וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מוֹעֲלִין בְּדָמִים — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna: **These** remainders of blood from the outer altar **and those** remainders of blood from the inner altar are **mixed in the canal** beneath the altar **and flow out** with the water used to rinse the area to the Kidron River, where they are sold to gardeners. Any blood that was not redeemed was subject to the prohibition against misuse of consecrated property. **The Sages taught:** One who takes these remainders without redeeming them **misuses** property consecrated in the Temple **by** unlawfully using **blood,** which is consecrated and is Temple property. It is prohibited to use consecrated objects for mundane purposes, and one who does so is committing the sin of misusing consecrated property. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon. And the Rabbis say:** One does **not misuse** consecrated property **by** benefiting from **these** remainders of the blood of offerings.

A *braita* teaches: *mo'alin b'damim*, said as *divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir holds that there is *meilah* by blood. *Rabbi Shimon vachachamim omrim: ein moalin bahen* — Rabbi Shimon and the Sages say there is no *meilah* by blood. The statement of the Sages is categorical — it is not restricted to blood after the *mitzvah* has been performed, but appears to apply even before the blood has been sprinkled. This is striking: of all the parts of a *korban*, the blood is the one that actually effects *kappara*, and yet it is precisely the blood that is exempt from *meilah*.

### Biblical Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן, אֲבָל מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The Rabbis **disagree only with regard to** misuse of consecrated property that applies **by rabbinic law,** as it was the Sages who prohibited the use of blood; **however,** everyone agrees that **by Torah law** one does **not misuse** consecrated property **by** benefiting from **these** remainders of blood. Clearly, the Temple treasurers would not have sold it to gardeners *ab initio* had the Torah prohibited the use of this blood (*Tosafot*).

The *gemara* asserts: *lo pligi ela miderabbanan* — even Rabbi Meir, who says there is *meilah*, is speaking only of Rabbinic *meilah*. *Ala mid'oraisa ein moalin bahem* — at the Biblical level, everyone agrees there is no *meilah* by blood. How the *gemara* derives this conclusion precisely is a matter elaborated in the *Tosafot*, which we do not develop here. The point is that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages is narrowed to the Rabbinic level, while the Biblical exemption is treated as universal.

### Blood for You

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר עוּלָּא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לָכֶם״ — שֶׁלָּכֶם יְהֵא. דְּבֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן תָּנָא: ״לְכַפֵּר״ — לְכַפָּרָה נְתַתִּיו, וְלֹא לִמְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that there is no misuse for blood, derived? **Ulla said** that **the verse states:** "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it **to you** upon the altar to make atonement for your souls" (Leviticus 17:11). "To you" indicates that **it shall be yours.** It is not the property of the Temple; rather, it belongs to all of the Jewish people. **The school** of **Rabbi Shimon** likewise **taught** that the phrase **"to make atonement"** teaches that God says: **I gave it for atonement and not for** the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated objects.

*Minani mili* — what is the scriptural source? *Amar Ulla amar kra: lachem* — Ulla says, the verse states: *va'ani natati lachem al hamizbeach lechaper* — "I have given it for you upon the altar to atone." The blood is *lachem* — for you. *Meilah* applies only to *kodshei Hashem* — that which belongs entirely to God, as the verse states: *ki timol maal mikodshei Hashem*. The part of a *korban* that is *lachem* — that the owner eats, or that effects atonement for the owner — does not fall under *meilah*. Thus, by way of example, the meat of a *shelamim* carries no *meilah* even before slaughter, since it will ultimately be eaten. Only the *cheilev* and *emurim* after the blood has been sprinkled become fully *kodshei Hashem* and subject to *meilah*. Blood, the verse tells us, is given for your sake — *lachem*, for you — and therefore, however central it is to the *avodah*, it is not characterized as something offered to God in the way that the flesh and fat are. The Torah describes the burning of fats and limbs as *reiach nichoach* and *lechem ishai* — God's sustenance, as it were. The blood achieves *kappara* but is nowhere described as a gift to God.

This is genuinely startling. The blood, which is the operative agent of atonement, the element placed on the *mizbe'ach lechaper* — is *lachem*, yours, not God's. Therefore no *meilah*.

### Before and After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״הוּא״, לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה כִּלְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה: מָה לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה אֵין בּוֹ מְעִילָה — אַף לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה אֵין בּוֹ מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that this *halakha* is derived from a different phrase. **The verse states:** "For **it is** the blood that makes atonement by reason of the life" (Leviticus 17:11), which indicates that it retains the same status **before atonement as after atonement: Just as after atonement** it is **not subject to** the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated objects, as the mitzva has been performed, **so too, before atonement it is not subject to misuse** of consecrated objects. As the Gemara states below, there is a general principle that once the mitzva involving a certain object has been performed, the object is no longer subject to misuse of consecrated objects.

Rabbi Yochanan cites yet another verse: *ki hadam hu banefesh yechaper* — for the blood is that through which, by virtue of the soul, atonement is achieved. From the word *hu* — it — Rabbi Yochanan derives: *hu hu lifnei kappara ke'achar kappara* — the status of blood before atonement is the same as after atonement. Since there is no *meilah* after the blood has been sprinkled and the *mitzvah* performed — *na'asis mitzvaso*, the *mitzvah* is done — the same is true before as well.

### Two Cases Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא, לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה כְּלִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה: מָה לִפְנֵי כַפָּרָה יֵשׁ בּוֹ מְעִילָה — אַף לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה יֵשׁ בּוֹ מְעִילָה! אֵין לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁנַּעֲשֵׂית מִצְוָתוֹ וּמוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. וְלָא? וַהֲרֵי תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the status of blood before atonement is compared to its status after atonement, one can **say** the opposite: **Just as before atonement it is subject to misuse** of consecrated objects, **so too, after atonement it is subject to misuse** of consecrated objects. The Gemara rejects this contention: This cannot be the case, as there is a general principle: **There is nothing whose mitzva has been performed that is** still **subject to misuse** of consecrated property. The Gemara asks: **And** is there **no** such case? **But there is** the instance of the **removal of the ashes** of offerings burned on the altar. These ashes require burial, and yet any benefit derived from them is misuse of consecrated property, despite the fact that their mitzva has already been performed.

The *gemara* raises the obvious challenge: *ve'eima le'achar kappara kilifnei kappara* — why not derive the opposite? Just as before atonement one might assume there is *meilah* by blood, perhaps after atonement there is likewise *meilah*. The *gemara* responds: there is no category of *davar she-na'asit mitzvaso* — something whose *mitzvah* has been completed — that retains *meilah*. Once a *mitzvah* is performed, the object no longer has the character of *kodshei Hashem* in that operative sense. Since after the blood is sprinkled there is certainly no *meilah*, the verse's *hu* — equating the two — imports that exemption to before the sprinkling as well.

But the *gemara* objects: is it really true that nothing retains a prohibition after its *mitzvah* is done? *V'harei trumas hadeshen* — what of the removal of the ashes? The ashes placed at the base of the altar (*vesamo etzel hamizbeiach*) remain forbidden for benefit even after the ritual has been completed. This appears to be a counterexample: the *mitzvah* is done, yet *issur hana'ah* — prohibition of benefit — and perhaps even *meilah* — remains. Rashi infers that since benefit is prohibited, the *kedusha* has not departed, and *meilah* applies as well. The *Tosafot* qualifies this: *issur hana'ah* does not necessarily entail *meilah*, but even granting only *issur hana'ah*, the principle that "nothing retains prohibition after its *mitzvah* is done" seems to be falsified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי תְּרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן וּבִגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִין הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד, וְכׇל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִין הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד — אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The reason is **because** the mitzva of **the removal of the ashes and the priestly vestments,** the four white garments worn by the High Priest on Yom Kippur, are both subject to the special *halakha* that misuse of consecrated objects applies to them even after their mitzva has been performed. Consequently, they are **two verses that come as one,** i.e., they share a unique *halakha* not found elsewhere. **And** there is a principle: **Any two verses that come as one do not teach,** i.e., an analogy may not be derived from these two similar cases. Instead, they are considered exceptional instances that cannot serve as models for other cases.

The *gemara* responds: *mishum de-havei trumas hadeshen u-vigdei kehuna shnei kesuvim habayim ke-echad, ve-chol shnei kesuvim habayim ke-echad ein melamdin* — because *trumas hadeshen* and *bigdei kehuna* are two verses that come as one, and whenever two verses come together to teach the same rule, they do not establish a general principle; instead, they mark off exceptions. The *bigdei kehuna* at issue are the *kohen gadol*'s Yom Kippur garments: the verse *vehinnicham sham* — "and he shall leave them there" — means they cannot be reused, remaining *assur behana'ah* even after the *avodah* is concluded. Together, *trumas hadeshen* and the Yom Kippur priestly garments form two explicit scriptural cases of post-*mitzvah* prohibition. The rule of *shnei kesuvim habayim ke'echad ein melamdin* holds that where the Torah must specify the same rule twice, it is doing so because that rule is *not* general — those two cases are the exceptions that prove the opposite rule. Everything else, once the *mitzvah* is done, becomes permitted. Blood, therefore, follows the general rule: no *meilah* after the *mitzvah* is done, and no *meilah* before it either.
