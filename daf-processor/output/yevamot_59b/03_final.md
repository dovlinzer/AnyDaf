# Yevamot 59b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Betulah Definition

### Hermeneutics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֲחוֹתוֹ אֲרוּסָה, רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמְרִים: מִטַּמֵּא לָהּ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מִטַּמֵּא לָהּ. אֲנוּסָה וּמְפוּתָּה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין מִטַּמֵּא לָהּ. וּמוּכַּת עֵץ — אֵין מִטַּמֵּא לָהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: רְאוּיָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל — מִטַּמֵּא לָהּ, שֶׁאֵין רְאוּיָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל — אֵין מִטַּמֵּא לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to a priest's **betrothed sister, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda say: He** must **become impure for her** upon her death. **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say: He** may **not become impure for her.** With regard to his sister who has been **raped or seduced, all agree** that **he may not become impure for her** upon her death. With regard to his sister **whose hymen was torn accidentally, he may not become impure for her;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon, as Rabbi Shimon would say** the following principle: If his sister was **fit for a High Priest, he** must **become impure for her,** but if she was **not fit for a High Priest, he may not become impure for her.**

The sugya at the bottom of 60a presents the position of Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon regarding the *kohen*'s obligation to become *tamei* for a sister who is a *betulah*. The relevant *pasuk* reads: *l'achoso habetulah hakerovah eilav asher lo haysah l'ish* — "for his sister the virgin who is close to him who has not been with a man." The question is how to read the dual characterization of *betulah* and *asher lo haysah l'ish*, and what each phrase independently demands.

### Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — דְּדָרְשִׁי הָכִי: ״וְלַאֲחוֹתוֹ הַבְּתוּלָה״ — פְּרָט לַאֲנוּסָה וּמְפוּתָּה. יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מוֹצִיא אַף מוּכַּת עֵץ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא הָיְתָה לְאִישׁ״. מִי שֶׁהֲוָיָיתָהּ עַל יְדֵי אִישׁ, יָצְאָה זוֹ שֶׁאֵין הֲוָיָיתָהּ עַל יְדֵי אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **What is the reason of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda? They expound** the verse **as follows:** "And for his virgin sister, who is near to him, who has had no man, for her must he defile himself" (Leviticus 21:3). **"And for his virgin sister" excludes** one who has been **raped or seduced,** as they are not virgins. One **might** have thought **that I should exclude even a woman whose hymen was torn accidentally** via a foreign object. **The verse** therefore **states: "Who has had no man,"** to include only **one whose becoming** a non-virgin was caused **by a man,** i.e., through intercourse. **This** case of a woman **whose becoming** a non-virgin **was not** caused **by a man** but rather by an object is thereby **excluded** from the category of a non-virgin, and her brother does become impure for her.

Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon read the *pasuk* as follows: *l'achoso habetulah* — *prat l'anusa u'meputah u'mukat eitz* — the word *betulah* excludes a woman who was raped, seduced, or who suffered a *mukat eitz* (a rupture of the hymen through non-sexual means). This establishes both a physical and behavioral criterion. Then *asher lo haysah l'ish* — *prat l'arusa* — the phrase "who has not been with a man" is read not as a reference to sexual intercourse but as a reference to *kiddushin*: a woman who has been *mekudeshet* to a man has in a sense "been his," even without physical relations. Therefore an *arusa* is excluded.

### Arusha Divorced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַקְּרוֹבָה״ — לְרַבּוֹת הָאֲרוּסָה. ״אֵלָיו״ — לְרַבּוֹת הַבּוֹגֶרֶת. ״הַקְּרוֹבָה״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֲרוּסָה שֶׁנִּתְגָּרְשָׁה,
>
> **Translation:** **"Who is near";** this is **to include a betrothed** sister. **"To him";** this is **to include a grown woman.** The Gemara asks: Can the term **"who is near"** come **to include a betrothed** woman **who was divorced?**

The word *hakerovah* — "who is close to him" — is read inclusively: *l'rabos arusa shenitgareshah*, to include a woman who was an *arusa* and was subsequently divorced. She is now close to him in the sense of being eligible, and since her prior *erusin* was not a physical act — *lo is'avid bah ma'aseh* — she can be restored to the category. However, since *hakerovah* can only include one case — *krova achas v'lo shtayim* — the question arises: why include the divorced *arusa* rather than the *mukat eitz*? The Gemara answers that the *mukat eitz* involves something that physically happened to her — *is'avid bah ma'aseh* — whereas the exclusion of the *arusa* was based entirely on her legal status, which can be reversed. The *mukat eitz*, by contrast, touches the definition of *betulah* itself, a category learned from the broader context of the *Kohen Gadol*, and one cannot extend the inclusion to encompass her.

### Kiddushin vs Virgin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ — דָּרְשִׁי הָכִי: ״וְלַאֲחוֹתוֹ הַבְּתוּלָה״ — פְּרָט לַאֲנוּסָה וּמְפוּתָּה וּמוּכַּת עֵץ, ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא הָיְתָה״ — פְּרָט לַאֲרוּסָה, ״הַקְּרוֹבָה״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֲרוּסָה שֶׁנִּתְגָּרְשָׁה, ״אֵלָיו״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַבּוֹגֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon, what is their reason? They expound as follows: "And for his virgin sister" excludes** a woman who was **raped or seduced and a woman whose hymen was torn accidentally,** who is also not considered a virgin. **"Who has had no** man" **excludes a betrothed** sister, although she is not yet fully married. **"Who is near";** this is **to include a betrothed** woman **who was** then **divorced,** as she is once again near to her brother. **"To him";** this is **to include a grown woman.**

At this point the Gemara raises a sharp observation: Rabbi Yosi has, by his silence on the *mukat eitz* question, parted ways with Rabbi Shimon. The *brayta* listed Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon together in holding that a *kohen* cannot become *tamei* for an *arusa*; but when Rabbi Shimon ruled that a *mukat eitz* is also excluded, Rabbi Yosi is nowhere to be found. The Gemara infers — *miklal d'mukat eitz Rabbi Meir sharei lei* — that Rabbi Yosi permits *tum'ah* for a sister who is a *mukat eitz*, aligned with Rabbi Meir's position. His textual basis is a split reading of the phrase *lo haysah l'ish*: *lo haysah* addresses the *kiddushin* dimension, excluding an *arusa*; *l'ish* addresses the sexual dimension, meaning that the definition of *betulah* here tracks whether she has had intercourse, not whether the hymen is anatomically intact.

### Alav Bogeres

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבוֹגֶרֶת — מִטַּמֵּא לָהּ, דִּבְרֵי כׇּל אָדָם. מַאי טַעְמָא — ״הַקְּרוֹבָה״ — לְרַבּוֹת בּוֹגֶרֶת, וְהָא בְּתוּלָה מִשְׁמַע בְּתוּלָה שְׁלֵימָה, הָיְכִי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן הַקְּרוֹבָה לְרַבּוֹת בּוֹגֶרֶת? אָלָא דִּמַשְׁמַע בְּתוּלָה שְׁלֵימָה, מִכְּלַל דְּאִי לָא הַקְּרוֹבָה הוּא דִּמַּיְילִי לַהּ, הָדַר בִּיְּתַר שׁוּם. וּתַנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי בְּדִבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בְּתוּלָה שְׁלֵימָה. וְתַנְנָן דִּמֶּשְׁנָתְנוּ הַלָּקוּט: תַּאֲמַר הָא נָמִי — בְּמָקוֹם אֲחֵר. תַּאֲמַר מִמַּאן, דִּמֶּשְׁנָתְנוּ הַלָּקוּט בַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And** if his sister was **a grown woman, he** must **become impure for her according to everyone.** Even those who hold that a High Priest may not marry a grown woman because her hymen is no longer whole agree that with regard to a priest becoming impure, she is considered a virgin and he must therefore become impure for her upon her death.

The Gemara then addresses the word *eilav*, which is read *l'rabos es habogeres* — to include a woman who has reached adulthood (*bogeret*) as one to whom the *kohen* may still become *tamei*. This is striking, since the word *betulah* on its own would seem to exclude a *bogeret*. The Gemara explains, in the name of Rabbi Shimon: *besulah shleima mashma* — the word *betulah* by itself connotes a fully intact state of *besulim*, even prior to any physical maturation. The very need for *eilav* to include a *bogeret* proves that without that inclusion, the word *betulah* alone would have excluded her. This, the Gemara notes, is also the source for Rabbi Shimon's general principle that *betulah* implies *besulah shleimah* — a completely intact state — which he applied in the context of the *Kohen Gadol* as well: *taamei didei nami hasam mehacha*.

What is striking in all of this is Rabbi Meir's position. In the context of the *Kohen Gadol*, Rabbi Meir defined *betulah* entirely anatomically — even a *mukat eitz* is a problem, even a *bogeret* is a problem, but *bi'ah shelo k'darkah* is not. Yet here, with respect to the *kohen*'s obligation of *tum'ah* for a sister, Rabbi Meir reverses course entirely and defines *betulah* in terms of whether she has had intercourse, not in anatomical terms. The two definitions of *betulah* — anatomical integrity and never having had intercourse — coexist, and Rabbi Meir applies them selectively: anatomical by the *Kohen Gadol*, behavioral here.

## Giyoret Status

### RS Giyoret Ruling

The Gemara transitions to a *brayta* presenting another ruling of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai, this one no longer about *tum'ah* or *Kohen Gadol* but about the permissibility of a female convert marrying a *kohen*. The baseline assumption the Gemara operates with, made explicit in the final *Mishnah* on 61a, is that a *giyoret* is categorized as a *zonah* and therefore prohibited to a *kohen hedyot*. The grounds for this categorization are a matter of significant debate among the *Rishonim* and *Tosafot*: one approach holds that we suspect a female convert of having had intercourse with a non-Jew prior to conversion, and since there can be no *kiddushin* with a non-Jew, such intercourse is defined as *bi'at znut*; a second approach holds that the legal category of *zonah* attaches to a non-Jewish woman by virtue of her identity, irrespective of actual conduct, on the assumption that non-Jews are not governed by the same sexual norms.

Against this background, Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai rules: *giyoret pechutah mibat shalosh shanim v'yom echad kesheira l'kehunah* — a convert who converted when she was less than three years and one day old is permitted to marry a *kohen*. His source is the *pasuk* from *Midyan*: *v'chol hataf banashim asher lo yadu mishkav zachar hachayu lachem* — all the young girls who had not known lying with a male, keep alive for yourselves. The Gemara notes that Pinchas, a *kohen*, was present, and the formulation *lachem* implies eligibility to marry *kohanim* as well.

### Yada Contradiction

Rav Huna raises a difficulty. If the criterion is *asher lo yadu mishkav zachar* — not having known intercourse — then why should three years matter at all? If an older woman demonstrably had not had intercourse, she should be equally permitted. Conversely, the *pasuk* seems to draw a distinction between *ishah* (adult women) and *taf* (minors), suggesting age is the operative distinction. Rav Huna shows that the two *pesukim* in the *parsha* of Midyan create a contradiction: one implies the distinction is based on having had sex, the other implies it is based on age.

### Capacity Interpretation

Rav Huna raises a difficulty. If the criterion is *asher lo yadu mishkav zachar* — not having known intercourse — then why should three years matter at all? If an older woman demonstrably had not had intercourse, she should be equally permitted. Conversely, the *pasuk* seems to draw a distinction between *ishah* (adult women) and *taf* (minors), suggesting age is the operative distinction. Rav Huna shows that the two *pesukim* in the *parsha* of Midyan create a contradiction: one implies the distinction is based on having had sex, the other implies it is based on age.

The Gemara resolves this by re-reading *asher lo yadu mishkav zachar* not as a description of actual experience but of capacity: *b're'uya l'hibba'el hakatuv medabber* — the *pasuk* speaks of a woman who is fit for intercourse, who is capable of it. The *taf* are excluded not because they demonstrably have not had sex, but because they are not yet capable of it in the *halachic* sense — which is operationalized as being under three years of age. A *brayta* confirms: *v'chol ishah yoda'at ish b're'uya l'hibba'el hakatuv medabber* — the phrase "knowing a man" refers to capacity, not experience.

### Testing Methods

This raises a practical question: how did the Israelites at *Midyan* actually determine which captive women were under three? Rav Huna bar Bizna citing Rabbi Shimon Chasida explains that they passed the women before the *tzitz* — the golden frontplate of the *Kohen Gadol*. Any woman whose face turned a sallow or bloated color was identified as fit for intercourse, i.e., over three years old. Rashi characterizes this as a *nes*. The Gemara also cites Rav Kahana's teaching about the women of *Yavesh Gilad*: there a different test was used, having each woman sit atop the opening of a wine barrel. A woman who had had intercourse — *be'ulah* — would emit a discernible scent from the wine vapors; a *betulah* would not.

### Yavesh Gilad

This raises the question: why was the *tzitz* not used for the women of *Yavesh Gilad* as it was for Midyan? The answer offered by Rav Kahana son of Rav Natan is that the *tzitz* functions for *leratzon* — for favorable purposes — but not for *pur'anut*, punitive ones. In the case of *Yavesh Gilad*, those who failed the test would be killed, and using the *tzitz* to facilitate their deaths would be using a sacred instrument for a punitive purpose. Rav Ashi then draws a distinction: *lahem leratzon v'lo lepur'anut* — for *Bnei Yisrael* the *tzitz* operates only for *leratzon*; *ule'ovdei kokhavim afilu lepur'anut* — for non-Jews it can operate even for *pur'anut*. That is why it could be used at Midyan, where the non-Jewish women who failed the test were the ones subject to the consequence, but not at *Yavesh Gilad*, where the issue concerned the *Bnei Yisrael* women.

### Halacha Ruling

Rav Yaakov bar Idi citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai* — we rule like Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai that a *giyoret pechutah mibat shalosh shanim* is permitted to a *kohen*.

### Rebbe Story

Rav Zeira asked Rav Yaakov bar Idi whether he heard this ruling explicitly from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi or whether he inferred it. The relevance of the question: Rav Zeira points to a case where Rebbe sent Rabbi Romanos to investigate a certain woman's status in a town in *Eretz Yisrael*. Rabbi Romanos found that the concern was that she was the daughter of a *giyoret* who had converted as a young child under three years. Rebbe ruled her permitted to remain married to her *kohen* husband. One might argue this was a leniency rooted solely in the fact that she was already married — analogous to the rule elsewhere (*bogeres u'mukat eitz lo yisa, im nasa nasa*) that though one should not marry certain women, if already married one need not divorce. If so, it would not constitute a ruling permitting marriage *lechatchila*.

The Gemara rejects this inference. Unlike the case of a *bogeres* or *mukat eitz*, whose status shifts over time, a *giyoret* would perpetually be in the category of *zonah* if her status were genuinely problematic — and we would never countenance a *kohen* remaining married to a *zonah*. Since Rebbe did permit it, the ruling must reflect a position that she is genuinely permitted, not merely tolerated after the fact. Rav Safra, who had learned the ruling as inferred rather than explicit, resolved the same difficulty in the same way.

## Non-Jewish Status

### Kevrei Ovdei

The Gemara continues with additional rulings of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai concerning the legal status of non-Jews. The first: *kivrei ovdei kokhavim einam mitamim be'ohel* — the graves of non-Jews do not convey *tum'ah* in an *ohel*. The source: *ve'atem tzoni tzon mar'iti adam atem* — "you are my flock, you are *adam*" — which is read *atem keruyim adam*, you are called *adam*, implying that non-Jews are not called *adam* in the relevant *halachic* sense and are therefore not subject to the *pasuk* that creates *tum'at ohel* from a human corpse.

### Metamei Bimaga

Ravina adds an important qualification: while the graves of non-Jews do not convey *tum'ah be'ohel*, non-Jews are nonetheless *metamei be'maga u've'masa* — through direct contact and carrying. This distinction has significant practical *halachic* implications: a *kohen* cannot dismiss *tum'ah* concerns entirely in contexts involving non-Jewish bodies, even if the *ohel* dimension is removed.

### Adam Definition

The ruling that non-Jews are not called *adam* in this context has generated considerable discussion, particularly given its juxtaposition with the preceding ruling about *giyoret pechutah mibat shalosh shanim*, where Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai was if anything more lenient toward non-Jews. The irony of the pairing is striking.

## Giyoret Status (II)

### RN Reversal

Against this backdrop — and after the Gemara had recorded the ruling of Rav Yaakov bar Idi in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that we rule like Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai — comes a sharp reversal. A certain *kohen* had married a *giyoret* who had converted when she was less than three years and one day old, citing that ruling. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak told him: *zil afik* — go and divorce her — and added pointedly that if he continued to invoke Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi, he would pull Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi out of his ear. The force with which Rav Nachman rejected the ruling suggests strongly that he did not regard it as settled *halacha*. The Gemara leaves the matter unresolved, but the *poskim* have understood this dramatic dismissal as a strong indication that the *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai on this point, and there is accordingly a *machlokes Rishonim* as to whether a *giyoret pechutah mibat shalosh shanim* is in fact permitted to a *kohen*.

## Non-Jewish Status (II)

### Pesukim

The Gemara raises two challenges to the principle that non-Jews are not called *adam*. First, in the narrative of *Midyan*, the *pasuk* refers to *nefesh adam* — sixteen thousand persons — and those are non-Jews. Second, the *pasuk* in Jonah regarding Nineveh states *adam asher lo yada bein yemino lismolo* — people who do not know their right from their left — and those too are non-Jews. The Gemara addresses the Midyan *pasuk* by arguing that *adam* there is used in contrast to *behemah*, the animals — the word is employed to distinguish humans from livestock, not to confer the *halachic* status of *adam*. The Nineveh *pasuk* is similarly addressed.

### Tosafot Nuance

*Tosafot* attempts to go beyond the case-by-case resolution offered by the Gemara and provide a more systematic account. One approach in *Tosafot* distinguishes between *adam* and *ha'adam* — with and without the definite article — suggesting that the form of the word carries different implications in different contexts, such that Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai's exclusion of non-Jews from the category of *adam* need not be read as a sweeping theological statement but as a technical, context-sensitive rule. This softens the categorical quality of his position and prevents his ruling from being read as a blanket denial of humanity to non-Jews.

### Rav Meshullam

*Tosafot* records a further answer in the name of Rav Meshullam that is not widely known: *d'gabei pur'anut davka ka'amar atem keruyim adam* — the term *adam* is used specifically in contexts dealing with death and *pur'anut*, and in those contexts, when the *Torah* could have said *Yisrael* explicitly, it instead said *adam*, using *adam* as a code for *Yisrael*. The reason is literary decorum: the *Torah* did not want to write *Yisrael ki yamut b'ohel* — it avoided coupling the name *Yisrael* directly with death. Therefore it used *adam* in its place. But *aval gabei ha'adam v'chai* — in contexts of life and observance, such as *asher ya'aseh osam ha'adam vachai bahem*, the word *adam* is used in its ordinary sense and includes everyone. On this reading, Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai is not making any categorical statement about the ontological status of non-Jews; he is making a narrowly technical observation about the contexts in which the word *adam* serves as a term for *Yisrael* in particular.

## KG Marriage

### Yikach Isha

The Gemara now turns to the *Mishnah* addressing the case where a *kohen* performed *erusin* with an *almana* and was subsequently appointed *Kohen Gadol*: the ruling is *yichnos* — he may proceed to *nisuin*. The question addressed in the relevant *brayta* is the textual basis for this ruling. The *drasha* proceeds from the *pasuk* *uv'tulah me'amav yikach ishah* — the word *yikach* here is read as referring to *nisuin*, meaning that once she is already *ishto*, already his wife by virtue of the prior *erusin*, he may complete the marriage through *nisuin*. The case of Yehoshua ben Gamla is cited as a *ma'aseh* corroborating this principle: he had performed *kiddushin* with Marta bat Baitos and was subsequently appointed *Kohen Gadol* by the king, whereupon he completed the marriage with *nisuin*.

### Yevama Distinction

The Gemara then distinguishes the case of a *shomeret yavam* who falls before a *kohen hedyot* who is subsequently appointed *Kohen Gadol*. Even if he performed *ma'amar* — the rabbinic form of *kiddushin* available in *yibbum* — the ruling is *lo yichnos*. The reason: *ma'amar* is only rabbinic *kiddushin*, not a full *halachic* bond. The word *isha* in the *pasuk* — once she is already *his wife* — applies only when there is a genuine prior *erusin*; it does not apply to a *shomeret yavam* where the *zika* and *ma'amar* have not created a true *ishut*.

### Yehoshua Story

The Gemara notes a nuance in the language of the *Mishnah*. In the case of Yehoshua ben Gamla it says *uminahu hamelech* — the king appointed him. In the case of *shomeret yavam* it uses the passive *nitmane*. The Gemara reads this as significant: *mineihu in, nitmane lo* — where the king appointed him actively, there is a *ma'aseh*, a demonstrable appointment; where the language suggests something more like a general consensus or selection process, it may not suffice. Rav Yosef perceives something troubling in the Yehoshua ben Gamla story — *kesher*, a kind of arrangement — and Rav Assi confirms: Marta bat Baitos, the wealthy *almana* whom Yehoshua had married by *kiddushin*, sent a basket full of *dinarim* to Yannai the king until the king installed Yehoshua ben Gamla among the senior *kohanim* as *Kohen Gadol*. The royal appointment was purchased through her wealth, which is why the *Mishnah* says *uminahu hamelech* — it was a top-down, bribed appointment — not the result of the normal selection process.

## KG Chalitzah

### Choletz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁמֵּת אָחִיו — חוֹלֵץ וְאֵינוֹ מְיַבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a High Priest's brother dies,** the **High Priest performs *chalitza* and does not perform levirate marriage.**

The *Mishnah* states: *Kohen Gadol she'mes achiv choletz v'lo meyavem* — if a *Kohen Gadol*'s brother dies, leaving a *yevama*, the *Kohen Gadol* performs *chalitzah* but does not perform *yibbum*. The Gemara notes: *pasik v'sanei lo shna min ha'erusin v'lo shna min ha'nisuim* — no distinction is drawn between whether the brother had contracted only *erusin* or full *nisuim* with the *yevama*. Both cases require *chalitzah* and prohibit *yibbum*.

### Aseh Logic

The Gemara raises the obvious question. In the case of *nisuim* — where the brother had completed the marriage — there is both a *lo sa'aseh* (the prohibition against an *almana* for the *Kohen Gadol*) and an *aseh* (the obligation of *betulah me'amav yikach ishah*). A *mitzvas aseh* cannot override a combined *lo sa'aseh* and *aseh*, so *yibbum* is clearly blocked. But in the case where there was only *erusin*, there is only a *lo sa'aseh* of *almana*; and ordinarily, a *mitzvas aseh* of *yibbum* can override a *lo sa'aseh*. Why then does the *Mishnah* prohibit *yibbum* even in that case?

### Gezeirah

The Gemara answers: *gezeirah bi'ah rishonah atu bi'ah shniyah*. The *mitzvas aseh* of *yibbum* technically applies only to the first act of *bi'ah* — the act that accomplishes the *yibbum* itself. After that first act, there is no longer an *aseh* at play. The *Kohen Gadol* would then be required to divorce her, since the ongoing marriage would be forbidden. But we are concerned that once the first act occurs, they will not separate, and the subsequent acts of intercourse would take place without any *mitzvas aseh* to legitimize the *lo sa'aseh*. Therefore *chazal* decreed that even the first act — which technically could override the *lo sa'aseh* — may not be performed.

### Tosafot Bizayon

*Tosafot* raises a further consideration. Given that the *Mishnah* states the *Kohen Gadol* must perform *chalitzah*, one might have thought that the honor of the *Kohen Gadol* would preclude such a proceeding — since *chalitzah* involves the woman spitting before him, which is a *bizayon*, a degradation. *Tosafot* notes that nonetheless the *Mishnah* requires it, since without *chalitzah* the *yevama* would remain permanently bound and unable to remarry.

## Ailonis Debate

### TK Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט לֹא יִשָּׂא אֲילוֹנִית, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים. תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: לִפְנֵי הַתּוֹרָה לֹא נִצְטַוָּה עָלָיו אִישׁ לִשְׁמֹר קֶשֶׁר עִם הָאִשָּׁה הַזּוֹ, אֲבָל הוּא לָא יִשָּׂא אוֹתָהּ כִּי אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבָנִים (בִּשְׁבִיל שׁוּם פְּרוּ וּרְבוּ שֶׁהוּא).
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A common priest may not marry a woman who is unable to conceive,** and whose physical characteristics include certain masculine features, **unless he already has a wife and children.** The *Tanna Kamma* holds that the sole concern here is the *mitzva* of *peru u'revu*: there is nothing specifically about the *kehunah* that makes such a woman prohibited; the problem is simply that a *kohen* should not foreclose his ability to fulfill the universal obligation of *peru u'revu*.

The *Mishnah* states: *kohen hedyot lo yisa ailonis ella im kein yesh lo ishah u'vanim* — a *kohen hedyot* may not marry an *ailonis* — a woman who is physically incapable of bearing children, characterized by certain masculine physical traits — unless he already has a wife or children, thereby having already fulfilled or having the means to fulfill *peru u'revu*. The *Tanna Kamma*'s position is that the sole concern here is the *mitzva* of *peru u'revu*: there is nothing specifically about the *kehunah* that makes an *ailonis* prohibited; the problem is simply that a *kohen* should not foreclose his ability to fulfill the universal obligation of *peru u'revu*.

### RY Zona

Rabbi Yehuda takes a fundamentally different position: *af al pi she'yesh lo ishah u'vanim lo yisa ailonis she'hi zonah ha'amurah ba'Torah* — even if he already has a wife and children, a *kohen* may not marry an *ailonis*, because she is a *zonah* in the sense employed by the *Torah*. On this view, the prohibition is not about *peru u'revu* at all; it is a *kehunah* prohibition, rooted in the definition of *zonah*.

### Achlu Reason

Rav Huna derives Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning from the *pasuk*: *achlu v'lo yisba'u, hiznu v'lo yifrotzu* — they ate and were not satisfied, they fornicated and did not spread forth. The Gemara reads *hiznu v'lo yifrotzu* as teaching: any act of intercourse that does not carry with it the *pirtzah* — the potential to spread forth progeny — is in its essence an act of *bi'at znut*. Since an *ailonis* is categorically incapable of childbearing, intercourse with her is permanently severed from the procreative context that would otherwise legitimize it, and therefore it constitutes *bi'lat znut*, making her a *zonah* in the *Torah*'s sense. This reflects a broader position that marital relations are legitimated not as an intrinsic value in themselves but because of the procreative framework that surrounds them — a position that stands in tension with the *Ramban*'s reading of *v'hayu l'vasar echad*, which sees the union of husband and wife as possessing intrinsic value independent of procreation.

### Chakhamim

The *Chakhamim* reject Rabbi Yehuda's expansive definition of *zonah* and restrict the category to three cases: *ein zonah ela giyoret u'meshuchreret v'sheniv'alah be'vi'at znut* — a *zonah* is only a female convert, a freed slave-woman, or a woman who has had a specific act of forbidden intercourse. Forbidden intercourse for this purpose means either an *issur kares* or a category of *bi'ah* that is prohibited specifically in the context of *kehunah* — such as that of a *gerushah* with a *kohen*, which may also render her a *challalah*. Critically, on the *Chakhamim*'s view, an *ailonis* is not a *zonah*; she is neither a convert nor a freed slave nor one who has had forbidden intercourse. The prohibition against marrying her is therefore purely a *peru u'revu* concern, and once that is satisfied, there is no further impediment.

## Ketana

### RE Ketana

A *brayta* teaches: *Rabbi Eliezer omer kohen lo yissa et hakketana* — Rabbi Eliezer rules that a *kohen* may not marry a *ketana*, a minor girl. This appears puzzling at first: we have seen that the *Mishnah* contemplates a *Kohen Gadol* marrying a *ketana* in certain contexts. The question is what drives Rabbi Eliezer's prohibition for a *kohen hedyot*.

### Combination

The Gemara's answer, worked out by Rabba in response to Rav Chisda's directive to investigate before Rav Huna's inquiry: *Rabbi Eliezer savar lah k'Rabbi Meir v'savar lah k'Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Eliezer combines two distinct positions. He holds like Rabbi Meir, who is *chayeish l'miyuta* — who takes seriously even minority-case possibilities — and he holds like Rabbi Yehuda, who rules that an *ailonis* is a *zonah* and therefore prohibited to a *kohen*.

### Ailonis Risk

Putting these two positions together: a small percentage of women who appear to be normal will turn out, upon reaching maturity, to be an *ailonis*. Rabbi Eliezer, following Rabbi Meir's general principle of *chiyush l'miyuta*, is concerned even about that small minority possibility. And following Rabbi Yehuda, he holds that an *ailonis* is prohibited to a *kohen* as a *zonah*, not merely because of *peru u'revu*. Therefore a *kohen* may not marry a *ketana* — because she might, in a small percentage of cases, prove to be an *ailonis*, which would render the marriage a prohibited union entirely. Notably, the Gemara does not suggest that the problem with a *ketana* has anything to do with *bi'at znut* on account of her being non-procreative; that framing is reserved for the *ailonis* herself. The concern here is purely the risk of a future *ailonis* status materializing.
