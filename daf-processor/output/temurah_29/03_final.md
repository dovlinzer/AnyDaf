# Temurah 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Pasul Korbanot

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר מַתְנָה, אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: מִנַּיִן לְמוּקְצֶה מִן הַתּוֹרָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "תִּשְׁמְרוּ לְהַקְרִיב לִי בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ" — כֹּל שֶׁעוֹשִׂין לוֹ שִׁימּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Tovi bar Mattana says** that **Rabbi Yoshiya says: From where** is the *halakha* of **set-aside** derived **in the Torah? As it is stated** with regard to the offerings: "My food that is presented to Me for offerings made by fire, of a pleasing aroma to Me, **you shall safeguard it to sacrifice to Me in its due season"** (Numbers 28:2), which refers to **anything that is safeguarded.**

Daf 29 picks up at the bottom of 28b, continuing the tractate's treatment of animals that are *pasul* as *korbanot*. The immediate context of the *perek* was introduced by its opening *mishnah*, which established that if *pasul* animals become mixed with valid ones, none of them are *batel*. Two reasons were offered: first, the stringency that attaches to *korbanot* means that even a single problematic animal renders all the others unfit; second, as developed in the *gemara* in Zevachim, live creatures are inherently not subject to *bitul*.

### Mixed Animals

While addressing the *bitul* question, the *gemara* has taken the opportunity to detail the various categories of animals that are unfit for *korbanot* and the textual sources for each disqualification.

### Invalid Categories

The *daf* continues down a list of *pasul* categories that the *gemara* is working through. Yesterday's discussion addressed *roveia v'nirva* — animals involved in bestiality — as well as animals that were worshipped and animals that killed a person. Today the focus shifts to two further categories: *muktzah*, an animal designated for *avodah zarah* but not actually worshipped, and *esnan*, an animal given as payment to a prostitute — a case treated explicitly in the *Torah*. These two topics will occupy the remainder of the daf.

## Muktzah Def'n

### Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיזֶהוּ מוּקְצֶה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Which is the** animal that is **set-aside?** It is an animal that is set aside **for idol worship.**

The *mishnah* raises the category of *muktzah l'avodat kochavim* — an animal set aside for idol worship. It does not, however, define precisely what constitutes *muktzah*; it simply rules that such an animal may not be offered, while its adornments (*tzippui*) do not carry the same prohibition. The definition of *muktzah* is therefore left to the *gemara*.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֵין אָסוּר אֶלָּא מוּקְצֶה לְשֶׁבַע שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: Only** an animal that has been **set aside for seven years is prohibited** to be sacrificed on the altar, i.e., one that is set aside to be used for idol worship at the end of seven years.

*Reish Lakish* opens the discussion: *ein issur ella muktzah l'shivas shanim* — the only thing that qualifies as *muktzah* for *avodah zarah* is an animal set aside for seven years. Before examining his source, it is worth pausing on what *muktzah* actually means in this context. The animal was not worshipped — that would make it *ne'evad*, a different category. What remains unclear, and appears to be a matter of debate among the *rishonim*, is the precise nature of the designation: was it set aside to become an object of worship itself, or was it designated to be offered as a *korban* to the idol, in a manner analogous to sanctifying an animal for the *Beit HaMikdash*? Both possibilities remain in play.

### Gideon Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיְהִי בַּלַּיְלָה הַהוּא וַיֹּאמֶר [לוֹ ה׳] קַח אֶת פַּר הַשּׁוֹר אֲשֶׁר לְאָבִיךָ וּפַר הַשֵּׁנִי שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים".
>
> **Translation:** **As it is stated** with regard to Gideon: **"And it came to pass the same night,** that the Lord **said** to him: **Take your father's bullock, and the second bullock of seven years,** and throw down the altar of Baal that your father has, and cut down the Asherah that is by it; and build an altar to the Lord your God…and take the second bullock, and sacrifice a burnt offering with the wood of the Asherah that you shall cut down"** (Judges 6:25–26). This indicates that it was common practice to prepare a bullock for seven years as an object of idol worship.

*Reish Lakish* derives his seven-year requirement from a *pasuk* in the Gideon narrative: *vayehi balaila hahu vayomer lo Hashem* — it was on that night that God spoke to him. The verse continues: *kach es par hashor asher l'avicha v'par hasheni sheva shanim* — take the ox belonging to your father and a second ox that has been set aside for seven years.

### Gideon Details

The context of this narrative is that God appeared to Gideon at night, selecting him as a *shofet*, and commanded him to take oxen that his father had been holding for the *Ba'al* and to use them to destroy the *Ba'al* altar and cut down the *Asherah* — the sacred tree associated with the *Ba'al* — and then to offer them up to God. Gideon executed this mission at night. The next morning, the townspeople discovered what had happened and wanted to kill him. His father, who had himself been a *Ba'al* worshipper, responded with striking logic: if the *Ba'al* is truly a god, let him defend his own honor without human assistance — an argument that evokes the *midrash* on Abraham and the smashing of the idols.

The *halachic* import of the narrative for *Reish Lakish* is as follows: God commanded Gideon to take these animals — which had been set aside for the *Ba'al* — and offer them to God. If setting aside an animal for *avodah zarah* were sufficient to constitute *muktzah*, how could God instruct that they be offered? The answer must be that the designation was not yet *muktzah* because the seven-year preparation period had not yet been completed. Hence *Reish Lakish* concludes: only once an animal has been set aside for seven years — the period after which it would be presented as a sacrifice to the idol — does it acquire the status of *muktzah*.

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מוּקְצֶה בִּלְחוּד הֲוָה? נֶעֱבָד נָמֵי הֲוָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And there, was** that bullock prohibited **merely** as **set-aside? It was also** actually **worshipped.**

The *gemara* challenges this inference: *vehasam muktzah bilchud hava?* Was the problem in the Gideon case only *muktzah*? The *gemara* asserts that those animals had in fact been worshipped — *ne'evad nami hava* — making the case compound. Rashi notes that this is not explicit in the text, but the *gemara* assumes it. If so, you cannot derive any *halacha* about the parameters of *muktzah* from this episode at all.

### Rav Acha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: מוּקְצֶה לַעֲבוֹד, וְלֹא עֲבָדוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says:** This bullock **was set aside to** be **worshipped, but** in practice, **they did not worship it.**

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* responds: in fact the animals had not been worshipped. They had been set aside to be worshipped — *muktzah la'avod* — but the actual worship had not yet taken place. On this reading, *muktzah* specifically connotes designation for future worship without the worship having occurred, and since the seven years had not elapsed, it had not yet achieved that status and was therefore valid.

### Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם עֲבָדוּהוּ, וְחִידּוּשׁ הוּא, כִּדְרַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר כָּהֲנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Actually, they did worship it,** and therefore it should have been prohibited as idolatry. **But** this case **is a novelty, in accordance with** a statement of **Rabbi Abba bar Kahana.**

*Rava* disagrees: *leolam avduhu* — the animals truly had been worshipped. Nevertheless, *vechidush hu* — that entire episode cannot serve as a source for general *halachot*, because the whole night was a *chidush*, a one-time dispensation.

### 8 Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר כָּהֲנָא: שְׁמֹנָה דְּבָרִים הִתִּירוּ בְּאוֹתוֹ לַיְלָה — חוּץ, וְלַיְלָה, וְזָרוּת, וּכְלִי שָׁרֵת, וּכְלִי אֲשֵׁירָה, וַעֲצֵי אֲשֵׁירָה, וּמוּקְצֶה, וְנֶעֱבָד.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Abba bar Kahana says: Eight** prohibited **matters were permitted** to Gideon **on that night** when he destroyed his father's idolatry (see Judges, chapter 6): The prohibition against slaughtering sacrificial animals **outside** the Tabernacle; **and** the prohibition against sacrificing offerings at **night; and** the prohibition of **non-priests** performing the service, as Gideon was not a priest; **and** the prohibition against sacrificing without **service vessels; and** the prohibition against utilizing **vessels** that had been previously used for the service of **a tree worshipped as idolatry [*asheira*]; and** the prohibition against using **the wood of an *asheira*,** as God commanded Gideon to sacrifice the burnt offering with the wood of an *asheira* that he will cut down (Judges 6:26); **and** the prohibition of sacrificing an animal **set aside** for idolatry; **and** the prohibition against using an animal that was **worshipped** as an idol.

*Abba bar Kahana* taught that eight things were permitted that night which would ordinarily be forbidden. God's directive to Gideon — analogous in spirit to Elijah's sacrifice on Mount Carmel — constituted a *hora'at sha'ah*, a temporary license granted for the purpose of striking a blow against *Ba'al* worship.

What were the eight exceptions? The offering was made *chutz*, outside the precincts of the *Mishkan* — which at that time was in Shiloh, where *bamot* (private altars) were forbidden. It was *laila*, performed at night, whereas *korbanot* are ordinarily brought only by day. *Zarut* — Gideon was a *zar*, not a *kohen*. *Klei sharet* — he lacked the sacred service vessels. *Klei Asherah* — the *gemara* understands that he used the wood of the *Asherah* to fashion the vessels. *Atzei Asherah* — he used that same wood to fuel the fire on the altar, which is explicit in the *pasuk*. *Muktzah* — the animal had been set aside for *avodah zarah*. *Ne'evad* — it had actually been worshipped. All eight departures from standard *halacha* were sanctioned that night as a collective *hora'at sha'ah*.

The conclusion from all of this is that one cannot derive any *halacha* about the permissibility or parameters of *muktzah* from the Gideon episode, since the entire night was a special exception.

## Muktzah Parameters

### R. Yoshiyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר מַתְנָה, אָמַר רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: מִנַּיִן לְמוּקְצֶה מִן הַתּוֹרָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "תִּשְׁמְרוּ לְהַקְרִיב לִי בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ" — כֹּל שֶׁעוֹשִׂין לוֹ שִׁימּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Tovi bar Mattana says** that **Rabbi Yoshiya says: From where** is the *halakha* of **set-aside** derived **in the Torah? As it is stated** with regard to the offerings: "My food that is presented to Me for offerings made by fire, of a pleasing aroma to Me, **you shall safeguard it to sacrifice to Me in its due season"** (Numbers 28:2), which refers to **anything that is safeguarded.**

The *gemara* now turns to establishing the conceptual basis for *muktzah* altogether. *Rabbi Yoshiyah* asks: *minayin l'muktzah min haTorah?* Where in the *Torah* does the category of *muktzah* find its source? He derives it from the verse *tishmeru l'hakriv li b'moado* — "you shall guard it to offer it to Me in its appointed time." The implication is that whatever is set aside and watched over for an offering is subsumed under a concept of *shimur*, preparation. By extension, *Rabbi Yoshiyah* reads *li* — "for Me" — as excluding what was prepared for another: if an animal was designated and watched over for *avodah zarah*, it falls outside the category of what may be brought to God.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אַיְיתִי אִימְּרָא דְּצוֹמָא, וְלָא עָבֵיד לֵיהּ שִׁימּוּר, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לְהַקְרָבָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye objects to this: If that is so,** then in a case where one **brought a lamb that** was **weak** as an offering, **and** its owner clearly **did not safeguard it, so too** will you say **that it is unfit for sacrifice?**

*Abaye* challenges this: if the principle is that you need *shimur* — active preparation — then consider someone who simply brought in a starving sheep from the wilderness, a sheep that had received no *shimur* whatsoever. Would you say such an animal cannot be brought as a *korban*? The obvious answer is that of course it can; the *Torah* does not require advance preparation of an animal before it is sanctified.

### Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא "תִּשְׁמְרוּ לְהַקְרִיב לִי בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ" קָאָמֵינָא — "לִי" וְלֹא לְאָדוֹן אַחֵר, וְאֵיזֶהוּ אַחֵר שֶׁמַּקְרִיבִין לוֹ? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זוֹ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoshiya said to** Abaye: You misunderstood my intention; **I say** that the verse: **"You shall safeguard it to sacrifice to Me in its due season,"** should be interpreted as follows: You shall safeguard it only **for Me, but not to another master. And which is this other** master **to which one** could **sacrifice? You must say** that **this is** an object of **idol worship.**

*Rabbi Yoshiyah* clarifies: he was never deriving a requirement that one must prepare a *korban* for God. The *drashah* on *tishmeru l'hakriv li b'moado* is about the word *li* — "for Me." What is designated for Me is valid; what is designated for *avodah zarah* is invalid. The verse is teaching not the positive requirement of *shimur* but the negative principle that designation toward another god disqualifies an animal from being a *korban*. Tosafot raises the question of why this verse is needed given that yesterday's *drashah* from *min habakar min hatzon* already established the invalidity of various categories. The answer is that those verses could only disqualify categories we already knew existed — animals that were worshipped, used in bestiality, and so forth. But where would we ever have known that mere *designation* for *avodah zarah*, short of actual worship, constitutes a recognized halakhic status? That is the novel teaching of this verse.

### Designation Def'n

The *gemara* asks: *v'eizehu acher shemakrivim lo* — what is the "other" to whom one brings offerings? *Haveh omer zo avodat kochavim* — that is *avodah zarah*. The category of *muktzah* is thus grounded.

### Work Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב אַדָּא, אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק: אֵין מוּקְצֶה אָסוּר אֶלָּא עַד שֶׁיַּעַבְדוּ [בּוֹ]. עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַד שֶׁיִּמְסְרוּהוּ לְכוּמְרֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the definition of an animal that has been set aside for idolatry. **Rava bar Rav Adda says** that **Rav Yitzḥak says:** An animal that has been **set aside** for idol worship is **prohibited,** i.e., still considered set aside, **only until it has been used for work** by the attendants of that idolatry. But once it has been used it is no longer prohibited, as it will not be sacrificed to the idol afterward. **Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says: An animal that has been set aside for idol worship is prohibited only **until it has been handed over to the attendants of idol worship** for fattening for it to be eaten, even if it has not been used for any form of work. But after it has been handed over it is not prohibited, as it will not be sacrificed.

The question then becomes: what constitutes a sufficient act of designation to establish *muktzah* status? *Rabbah bar Adda* in the name of *Rav Yitzchak* teaches: *ein muktzah assur ella ad she-ya'avdu* — it is not forbidden until you have worked it or used it in some capacity. The word *ya'avdu* is ambiguous: it could mean worship, but that would render it *ne'evad*. Rashi therefore offers two readings of the same phrase, and this turns out to be the crux of the *machloket* between the *Rambam* and the *Ra'avad*.

### Rambam v. Ra'avad

The *Rambam* holds that verbal designation alone — a non-Jew declaring "I sanctify this for the *Ba'al*" — carries no *halachic* weight, since there is no concept of *hekdesh l'avodah zarah*. To establish *muktzah* status, a physical act of designation is required: loading the animal with vessels of *avodah zarah*, using it in some capacity in service of the idol, or an equivalent act. Only such an act makes the designation legally significant.

The *Ra'avad* — and Rashi in his preferred reading — reads the phrase differently. *Ein assur ad she-ya'avdu* means not that the animal becomes forbidden only once worked, but rather that it *ceases* to be forbidden once it is put to mundane labor. If the priests of *avodah zarah*, having received the animal designated as a future offering, then use it to plow a field, that practical use removes it from the category of something properly designated for the idol's service. The animal is forbidden only *until* such labor occurs; once it is pressed into ordinary use, it can no longer be considered truly prepared and designated for an idolatrous offering. One might note that the *Rambam*'s position seems to favor the act of designation as the operative moment, while the *Ra'avad*'s position treats ordinary use as dissolving the designation — an interesting structural difference with analogues across many *halachic* discussions of when *machshavah* versus *ma'aseh* is determinative.

The *gemara* notes an important limitation: *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — a person cannot confer forbidden status on something that is not his. This raises the question of whether a renter, or the priests of *avodah zarah* who have received temporary use of an animal, can effect *muktzah* status — a question the *gemara* does not resolve.

### Ulla on Priests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַד שֶׁיִּמְסְרוּהוּ לְכוּמְרֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says: An animal that has been set aside for idol worship is prohibited only **until it has been handed over to the attendants of idol worship** for fattening for it to be eaten, even if it has not been used for any form of work. But after it has been handed over it is not prohibited, as it will not be sacrificed.

*Ulla* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* offers a further position: *ad she-yimsruha l'komrei avodat kochavim* — not until one hands the animal over to the priests of *avodah zarah*. This is an unambiguous stage of designation: the mere act of transferring the animal to the idolatrous priesthood, without any verbal formula, constitutes a sufficiently significant act to establish *muktzah* status.

Rashi, however, is drawn to read *Ulla's* statement in the same direction as the *Ra'avad*. Even handing over to the *komrei avodat kochavim* would only be problematic if those priests then receive it for their personal use — to plow, or for food. Since the animal is going not for idol worship proper but for the support of the priests, that would strip it of its sacred designation and render it no longer *muktzah*.

### Beiha Reconciles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַד שֶׁיַּאֲכִילוּהוּ כַּרְשִׁינֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְבָהָא: מִפַּלְגִיתוּ אַתּוּן אַעוּלָּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, עוּלָּא נָמֵי כִּי קָאָמַר — הוּא דְּשָׁפֵי לֵיהּ כַּרְשִׁינֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sage **Baha says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says: A set-aside animal is prohibited only **until** its owner **feeds it vetches of idolatry** after it has been given to the attendants of idolatry. **Rabbi Abba said to Baha: Do you dispute** the statement **of Ulla,** who defined set-aside differently according the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan? Baha **said to** Rabbi Abba: **No,** I do not disagree with Ulla, as **Ulla too, when he stated** his definition, also **was** referring to a case **where they fed it vetches of idolatry.**

*Beiha* — a name rarely encountered in the *gemara* — also cites *Rabbi Yochanan* with a slightly different formulation: *ad she-yachiluhu karshinei avodat kochavim* — not until one actually feeds it the special feed of *avodah zarah*. This too reflects the *Rambam*'s approach: verbal designation is insufficient; a concrete act — here, feeding the animal the specific provender prepared for idolatrous offerings, fattening it in anticipation of being sacrificed — is what creates the status. This framing also suggests that *muktzah* means designated to be brought as a *korban* to the idol, rather than designated to become an object of worship, since the animal is being fattened for eventual offering.

*Rav Abba* then asks *Beiha*: are you disputing *Ulla*? *Ulla* says handing over to the priests is the operative act; you say feeding it the special feed. *Beiha* responds: there is no dispute. When *Ulla* said handing over to the priests, he meant that one hands it over *in order that* the priests will feed it the special feed. The two statements describe a single sequence: transfer to the priests is the occasion, but what makes it definitive is that the priests then proceed to fatten the animal. *Ulla* and *Beiha* are saying the same thing.

### Beiha Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: יָדַע בְּהָא לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי שְׁמַעְתָּא, וְאִי לָא דְּסָלֵיק הָתָם — לָא הֲוָה יָדַע, דְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל גְּרַמָה לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יִצְחָק: בָּהָא מִיכָּן וּמִיכָּן הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said: Baha knows** how **to explain** this ***halakha*, but had he not ascended** from Babylonia to **there,** Eretz Yisrael, **he would not have known** this explanation, **as Eretz Yisrael caused him** to grow wise. **Rav Yitzḥak said to** Rabbi Abba: That is not the case, as **Baha was from here and there,** i.e., he became wise because he studied Torah in both places.

*Rabbi Abba* remarks: *yad Beiha lishnu'ei shma'ata* — *Beiha* knew how to answer and reconcile a talmudic discussion. The *gemara* adds: *v'ilu lo salik me-hatam, lo hava yada* — had he not come from *Eretz Yisrael*, he would not have had the insight to make this reconciliation. *Avira d'Eretz Yisrael machkim* — the air of the Land of Israel makes one wise, and *Beiha* had absorbed that clarity. *Rabbi Yitzchak* then notes that *Beiha* had in fact come from both *Eretz Yisrael* and *Bavel*, and therefore one cannot attribute his sharpness specifically to his origins in *Eretz Yisrael*. The *gemara*'s digression is notable: *Beiha* is essentially unknown elsewhere in the *gemara*, and the discussion of his provenance reads as an attempt to account for how this otherwise absent voice managed to enter — and hold his own in — the rabbinic conversation.

### Acts of Muktzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי רַב חֲנַנְיָא טְרִיטָאָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין מוּקְצֶה אָסוּר אֶלָּא עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשׂוּ בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, הוּא תָּנֵי לַהּ וְהוּא אָמַר לַהּ: מַאי "מַעֲשֶׂה"? — עַד שֶׁיִּגָּזֵז וְיַעַבְדוּ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the issue of set-aside. **Rabbi Ḥananya Terita taught before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** An animal that has been **set aside** for idolatry is **prohibited only until an action has been performed upon it. He taught it and then said its** explanation: **What** is the meaning of **an action?** It means **until it is sheared and used for labor** by the attendants of the idol.

The *gemara* then cites a *tanna* — *Rabbi Chanina Trita'ah* — who taught before *Rabbi Yochanan*: *ein muktzah assur ella ad she-ya'asu bo ma'aseh* — it is not forbidden until a concrete act is performed with it. He then explained: *ad she-yagoz v'yigzoz v'yashayer v'ya'avdu bo* — until one shears it, uses it, or works it in some capacity. This formulation once again reflects the tension between the *Rambam* and *Ra'avad*: does the act establish the *muktzah* status, or does mundane use dissolve it? Both readings remain consistent with this text. In any case, the upshot is clear: *muktzah l'avodah zarah* is forbidden for use as a *korban*, though — unlike an inanimate object of worship — it is not forbidden for personal consumption, because it is a live animal and the *halacha* does not recognize a mechanism for prohibiting an animal's flesh through verbal designation to *avodah zarah*.

## Non-Kosher Food

### Ne'evad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיזֶהוּ נֶעֱבָד כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Which is** the animal that is **worshipped** as idolatry? It is any animal that one worships. The mishna proceeds to state: The consumption of both this, the animal designated for idol worship, and that, the animal worshipped, is permitted. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived?

The *mishnah* turns to *ne'evad* — animals that were actually worshipped — and notes that as with *muktzah*, their adornments (*tzippui*) carry their own prohibition. The *gemara* now focuses on the closing line of the *mishnah*.

### Edible Status

The *mishnah* states: *v'zeh v'zeh mutar ba'achilah* — both *ne'evad* and *muktzah* animals are permitted for eating. Indeed, the *mishnah* implies that all the categories listed — *roveia, nirva, muktzah, ne'evad* — are permissible for human consumption, even though they are forbidden as *korbanot*. The *gemara* asks: how do we know this?

### Rav Papa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא "מִמַּשְׁקֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל", מִן הַמּוּתָּר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אֲסִירִי לְהֶדְיוֹט, לְמָה לִי קְרָא לְמַעֹטִינְהוּ מִגָּבוֹהַּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said that the verse states,** with regard to offerings: "And one lamb of the flock, out of two hundred, from the well-watered pastures of Israel, for a meal offering, and for a burnt offering, and for peace offerings, to make atonement for them" (Ezekiel 45:15). The expression: **"From the well-watered pastures of Israel,"** means that one brings offerings only **from that which is permitted to Israel. And if** it **enters your mind** that a set-aside animal and one worshipped as idolatry **are prohibited** in consumption **to an ordinary** person, **why do I** need **a verse to exclude them from** sacrifice to **the Most High,** as that would be obvious. Rather, they must be permitted in consumption to an ordinary person.

*Rav Papa* cites the verse in Yechezkel: *mimashkeh Yisrael* — from the drink of Israel — a verse about offerings, from which the *gemara* derives that *korbanot* may only be brought from things that are permitted to Israel for consumption. *Min hamutar le-Yisrael* — only what a Jew may eat or drink may be offered on the altar.

### Inference Logic

From this principle follows a striking inference. If any of the animals on the *mishnah*'s list were forbidden for eating, there would have been no need for a separate verse to disqualify them as *korbanot*. The general rule — that non-kosher food cannot be offered — would have excluded them automatically. The very fact that the *Torah* required explicit verses to disqualify *roveia*, *nirva*, *ne'evad*, and *muktzah* as *korbanot* demonstrates that these animals are in fact permissible for eating. Had they been forbidden food, they would have been caught by the general rule without any additional *drashah*. This is a remarkable argument from silence: the need for verses proves the permissibility of consumption.

There are specific reasons why these animals remain edible. A worshipped animal is forbidden only in the case of an inanimate idol; a live animal cannot be rendered forbidden in its flesh through designation, because one cannot make living creatures *assur* in that way. An animal used in bestiality is similarly permitted for eating as long as judgment has not been passed and executed. In neither case is there a prohibition on consumption — and the verses that exclude them from the altar confirm it.

### Treifah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹל הֵיכָא דַּאֲסִירִי לְהֶדְיוֹט — לָא בָּעֵי קְרָא? וְהָא טְרֵפָה, דַּאֲסִירָא לְהֶדְיוֹט, וּמַעֲטֵיהּ קָרָא מִגָּבוֹהַּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it true that **anywhere that** an item is **prohibited to an ordinary person, a verse is not necessary** to teach that it may not be used as a sacrifice? **But** there is the case of **an animal with a condition that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], which is prohibited** in consumption **to an ordinary** person, **and** yet **a verse excludes it** from sacrifice **to the Most High.**

The *gemara* then raises a challenge: if it is true that whatever is forbidden for eating is automatically excluded from *korbanot* without a verse, then why does the *Torah* provide an explicit verse to exclude *treifah*? A *treifah* is certainly forbidden for eating — and yet there is a *pasuk* that specifically excludes it from *korbanot*. *De-tanya*: the verse *min habakar* — a different instance of that phrase from the ones cited yesterday — comes to exclude the *treifah*. If the general rule already covers it, why is this verse needed?

### 3 Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר "מִן הַבָּקָר" לְמַטָּה, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר, אֶלָּא לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַטְּרֵפָה. אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אֲמִינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי — דְּנִטְרְפָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְקַדְּשָׁה, אֲבָל נִתְקַדְּשָׁה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִטְרְפָה — אֵימָא דְּתִישְׁתְּרֵי לְגָבוֹהַּ. וְהָא מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא, "כֹּל אֲשֶׁר יַעֲבוֹר תַּחַת הַשָּׁבֶט", פְּרָט לִטְרֵפָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עוֹבֶרֶת. הָהוּא נָמֵי אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלֹא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, דְּנוֹלְדָה טְרֵפָה מִמְּעֵי אִמָּהּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּהָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, וְיָצָאת לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִטְרְפָה — אֵימָא תִּשְׁתְּרֵי לְגָבוֹהַּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: It was previously derived from the verse: "You shall bring your offering from the cattle, even from the herd or from the flock" (Leviticus 1:2), that certain animals are prohibited to be sacrificed on the altar (see 28a). **When it says below,** in the next verse: "If his offering is a burnt offering **of the herd," there is** apparently **no need for the verse to state** this, as it was already stated earlier. **Rather,** this serves **to exclude a *tereifa*** from being brought as an offering. The Gemara answers: In this case the derivation **was necessary,** as it might **enter your mind to say: This statement,** that a *tereifa* may not be sacrificed, applies **where it became a *tereifa* and afterward was consecrated** as an offering. **But** if **it was consecrated and** only **afterward became a *tereifa*, I would say that it should be permitted to the Most High.** Therefore, the derivation from the verse is necessary. The Gemara asks: **But** isn't the *halakha*, that an animal which became a *tereifa* after it was consecrated is prohibited to be sacrificed, **derived from here,** a verse that deals with the animal tithe: "And all the tithe of the herd or the flock, **whatsoever passes under the rod,** the tenth shall be holy to the Lord" (Leviticus 27:32). The phrase: "Whatsoever passes under the rod," indicates that the *halakha* of animal tithe applies only to an animal that can pass under the rod, **excluding a *tereifa*,** e.g., if its leg was cut above the knee, **which cannot pass** under the rod. From this paradigm case of a *tereifa*, the exception is expanded to apply to all *tereifot*. The Gemara answers: **That** derivation from animal tithe **was also necessary,** as it might **enter your mind to say: This statement,** that a *tereifa* may not be sacrificed, applies **where it did not have a period of fitness** to be sacrificed before it became a *tereifa*, e.g., **where it was born a *tereifa* from its mother's womb. But** in a case **where it had a period of fitness and emerged into the air of the world and** only afterward **became a *tereifa*, I would say** that **it should be permitted to the Most High.** Therefore, the phrase: "Whatsoever passes under the rod," **teaches us** that even such an animal is prohibited to be sacrificed on the altar.

The *gemara* answers: *itztrich* — it was necessary. One might have thought that the general rule excludes only animals whose forbidden status predates their sanctification — just as most non-kosher food, whether *orlah*, *kilayim*, or *chazir*, is forbidden from the outset of its existence. In the normal case, the animal was never fit and then was sanctified. But what if it was first sanctified and only then became a *treifah*? One might have reasoned that in such a case, the general rule of non-kosher food does not apply and the animal remains a valid *korban*. Hence the verse is needed to teach that *treifah* disqualifies even when the defect occurred after sanctification.

But the *gemara* pushes further: there is yet another verse in the *Torah* concerning *treifah* and *korbanot*, derived from the passage on *ma'aser behema* — *kol asher ya'avor tachat hashavet*, anything that passes under the staff. A *treifah* that cannot walk does not pass under the staff, and from this we learn that *treifah* disqualifies for *ma'aser*. If that verse already teaches the disqualification, why is the additional verse needed? The *gemara* answers: the verse from *ma'aser* covers only an animal that was never fit — one born a *treifah*, *nolad treifah mimei imo*, which has no *sha'at hakosher*, no period of fitness. The additional verse teaches that even an animal born healthy but which later became a *treifah* is disqualified. And then yet a third application of the verse establishes that this is true even when the animal became a *treifah* after it was sanctified.

### General Rule

Three stages thus require three textual bases for *treifah*: (1) born a *treifah*, never fit; (2) became a *treifah* before sanctification but after birth; (3) became a *treifah* after sanctification. Having established all three, the *gemara* returns to its general principle: whatever is now non-kosher cannot be brought as a *korban*, regardless of when the disqualification arose. And therefore, for everything on the *mishnah*'s list, the need for explicit exclusionary verses confirms that those animals are indeed permitted for eating. The *treifah* case is unique in requiring multiple verses because of the various stages at which the disqualification can arise — but the underlying principle remains intact.

## Esnan Zonah Intro

### Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵיזֶהוּ אֶתְנַן? — הָאוֹמֵר לְזוֹנָה "הֵולִיךְ טָלֶה זֶה בִּשְׂכָרֵךְ", אֲפִילּוּ הֵן מֵאָה, כּוּלָּן אֲסוּרִין. וְכֵן הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ "הֵולָיךְ טָלֶה זֶה וְתָלִין שִׁפְחָתְךָ אֵצֶל עַבְדִּי" — רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ אֶתְנַן, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶתְנַן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And which** is the case of an animal used as **payment** to a prostitute, which is prohibited as a sacrifice? It is the case of **one who says to a prostitute: Here is this lamb as your fee. Even if they were one hundred** lambs that he gave her, **all of them are** considered as payment to a prostitute and are **prohibited. And likewise,** in the case of **one who says to another: Here is this lamb and** in return **your maidservant will lie with my slave** and engage in intercourse with him, **Rabbi Meir says:** Its halakhic status **is not** that of **payment** to a prostitute, **and the Rabbis say:** Its halakhic status is that of **payment** to a prostitute.

The *gemara* now embarks on what will occupy the remainder of the *perek*: the laws of *esnan zonah*, the animal given as payment to a prostitute, which the *Torah* explicitly forbids from the altar. This topic has no other natural home in *Shas* — *Kerisos* addresses *korban chatas* and *Meila* addresses misuse of *hekdesh* — and so *Temurah*, though an unusual venue, becomes the place where the parameters of *esnan zonah* are fully worked out.

*V'eizehu esnan?* — What constitutes an *esnan*? The *mishnah* begins: *ha'omer l'zona: heilech taleh zeh bis'chareich* — one who says to a woman, "take this lamb as your fee." The paradigmatic case evoked here is of course *Yehuda v'Tamar* — *hagedi izim*.

### Servant Case

The *mishnah* continues: *v'chen ha'omer l'chaveiro: heilech taleh zeh v'taslin shif'chatcha eitzel avdi* — similarly, if one says to his fellow, "take this lamb, and send your female slave to sleep with my male slave." This is a nuanced case: the master is legally permitted to have his slave cohabit with a female slave in order to produce slave children. The sex itself may not be a forbidden act. Yet the *mishnah* treats the payment for that arrangement as an *esnan*.

### Dispute

There is a dispute — whether attributed to *Rebbe* or *Rabbi Meir*, depending on the textual variant — according to which this case is *eino esnan*, not a valid *esnan*. The *chachamim* disagree and rule it is indeed *esnan*. This dispute will be examined in the next session.

### Multiple Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר מָר: וַאֲפִילּוּ הֵן מֵאָה, כּוּלָּן אֲסוּרִין. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּשָׁקְלָה אַגְרַאּ מֵאָה בְּהֵמוֹת — פְּשִׁיטָא דְּכוּלְּהוּ אֲסִירִי, מָה לִי חַד, מָה לִי מְאָה! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּשָׁקְלָה אַגְרַאּ חֲדָא, וְיָהֵיב לַהּ מְאָה, דְּכוּלְּהוּ מִכֹּחַ אֶתְנַן קָאָתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Master said** in the mishna: **And even if they were one hundred** lambs, **all of them are** considered as payment to a prostitute, and are **prohibited.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that this is referring to a prostitute **who takes one hundred animals as payment** for her services, it is **obvious that all** one hundred animals **are prohibited,** as **what** difference is it **to me** if she was paid **one** lamb **or** if she was paid **one hundred** lambs? What, then, is the novelty of the mishna here? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where** the prostitute **takes one** lamb as **payment, and he gave her one hundred** lambs. The mishna is teaching that in this case the extra lambs are not considered a gift. Rather, all the lambs are prohibited, **as they all come from the source of a payment** to a prostitute.

The *mishnah* states: *afilu hen me'ah, kulan asurin* — even if the payment consisted of a hundred animals, they are all forbidden. The *gemara* probes the *chiddush* here. One might have thought this was obvious: if she received one hundred animals as her fee, of course all one hundred are forbidden, since they are all payment for services. Why state it? The *gemara* answers: *lo tzricha deshaka igrah chada* — it is needed for the case where her fee was only one animal, but he gave her one hundred. One might have thought the additional animals were a gift beyond the agreed fee, not payment for services. The *mishnah* teaches that since the hundred animals come in the context of payment, they are all defined as *esnan* — *dechulu mikoach esnan ka'asi* — and all are forbidden.

## Esnan Timing

### Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נָתַן לָהּ וְלֹא בָּא עָלֶיהָ, בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְלֹא נָתַן לָהּ — אֶתְנַנָּה מוּתָּר. נָתַן לָהּ וְלֹא בָּא עָלֶיהָ, "אֶתְנַנָּה" קָרֵית לֵיהּ? וְתוּ, בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְלֹא נָתַן לָהּ, מַאי יָהֵיב לַהּ? אֶלָּא הָכִי קָתָנֵי: נָתַן לָהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּא עָלֶיהָ; בָּא עָלֶיהָ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נָתַן לָהּ — אֶתְנַנָּה מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the issue of payment to a prostitute. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If a man **gave her** an animal as payment **but did not engage in intercourse with her,** or **if he engaged in intercourse with her but did not give her** payment, **her payment is permitted.** The Gemara asks: In a case where **he gave her** an animal as payment **but did not engage in intercourse with her, do you call this her payment?** It cannot be considered payment unless the act of intercourse was actually performed. **And furthermore,** in the second case, where **he engaged in intercourse with her but did not give her** payment, **what did he give her** that it is called a permitted payment? **Rather, this** is what the *baraita* **is teaching:** If **he gave her** payment **and** only **afterward,** when some time had elapsed, **engaged in intercourse with her;** or if **he engaged in intercourse with her and** only **afterward gave her** payment, in these two cases **her payment is permitted.**

The *gemara* then takes up the *baraita*: *nasan lah v'lo ba'al aleha* — he gave her the animal but they never had sexual relations; *ba'al aleha v'lo nasan lah* — or they had relations and he never gave her anything. *Esnan mutar* — in such a case it is permitted. At first the *gemara* reads these as extreme cases: he gave it and they never had sex at all, or they had sex and he never gave her anything. Of course in those cases there is no *esnan*. The *gemara* therefore rejects this reading as trivially obvious.

The *gemara* corrects: *ela hachi katanu* — this is what the *baraita* actually means. *Nasan lah v'achar kach ba'al aleha* — he paid her in advance, and then they had relations. *Ba'al aleha v'achar kach nasan lah* — or they had relations first and then he paid. In either case, *esnan mutar*. This is arresting: it seems to limit the prohibition to an almost non-existent scenario — simultaneous payment and sex, or payment explicitly stipulated to take effect at the moment of *bi'ah*.

### Permitted Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵיחוּל עֲלֵיהּ אֶתְנַן לְמַפְרֵעַ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁקָּדְמָה וְהִקְרִיבַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** in the case where he gave her the lamb and later engaged in intercourse with her, **let** the status of **payment** to a prostitute **take effect upon it retroactively. Rabbi Elazar says:** This is referring to a case **where** the prostitute **first sacrificed** the lamb as an offering, before she engaged in intercourse with the man who gave it her.

Yet the *gemara* immediately complicates this. Regarding prepayment: if he paid and then they had relations, why is it *mutar*? Should the payment not retroactively become an *esnan* once they've had sex? *Amar Rabbi Elazar: shekadma v'hikrivasu* — the permitted case is specifically where she took the animal, brought it as a *korban*, and only *afterward* did they have relations. Since the *korban* was already offered before the *bi'ah* took place, it cannot retroactively acquire the status of *esnan*.

This raises a further question. *Heichi dami?* — what exactly is the scenario? If he transferred title to her outright before the sex, then of course it is *mutar* — it was never an *esnan*. That is obvious. And if he stipulated that it should not be hers until the moment of *bi'ah*, then she had no ownership before the sex and could not have sanctified it, since *ish ki yakdish es beiso kodesh lashem* — a person can only sanctify what is his. *Mah beiso birshuso, af kol birshuso* — ownership is a precondition for sanctification.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי נֵימָא דְּאַקְנְיֵהּ נִיהֲלַיהּ לְאַלְתַּר — פְּשִׁיטָא דְּמוּתָּר, דְּעַד כָּאן לֹא בָּא עָלֶיהָ! וְאֶלָּא, דַּאֲמַר לַהּ: "לָא נִיקְּנֵי לִיךְ עַד שְׁעַת בִּיאָה", מִי מָצְיָא מַקְרְבָה לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If we say that** the man **transferred** ownership of the lamb **to her immediately,** and only afterward engaged in intercourse with her, it is **obvious that** this lamb is **permitted** to be sacrificed on the altar, **as** it belongs to her and **up to that** point the man **had not yet engaged in intercourse with her.** Therefore, the lamb is not considered payment at all. **But rather,** this must be referring to a case **where he said to her:** The lamb **shall be acquired by you only at the time of intercourse,** and yet she went ahead and sacrificed it beforehand. But in this case, **can she** legally **sacrifice** the lamb?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "אִישׁ כִּי יַקְדִּישׁ אֶת בֵּיתוֹ קֹדֶשׁ לַה׳" אֲמַר רַחֲמָנָא, מָה בֵּיתוֹ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ, אַף כֹּל בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ! לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲמַר לַהּ: "לָא נִיקְּנֵי לָךְ עַד שְׁעַת בִּיאָה, וְאִי מִצְטְרִיךְ לָךְ נִיקְּנֵי לָךְ מֵעַכְשָׁיו".
>
> **Translation:** The Merciful One states: "And when a man shall sanctify his house to be holy to the Lord" (Leviticus 27:14), which teaches that **just as his house is in his possession, so too any** item that a person wishes to sanctify must be **in his possession.** In this case, by contrast, the lamb does not belong to her until they engage in intercourse. The Gemara responds: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where he said to her:** The lamb **shall be acquired by you only at the time of intercourse, but if you need it** in the meantime, **acquire it for yourself from now.**

*No tzricha*: the case the *gemara* has in mind is where he said: "I am not transferring ownership until the time of relations — but if you need it in the interim, you may take ownership now." His primary intent was for her to own it at the moment of *bi'ah*, but he gave her an option to take title earlier. One might therefore have thought that even if she exercised that option and brought the animal as a *korban* before they had relations, it was still tainted by the context of the arrangement and might be defined as an *esnan*. *Rabbi Elazar* teaches that this is not so: as long as the actual *bi'ah* had not yet occurred at the moment of the *korban*, the offering was valid.

### R. Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: קָדְמָה וְהִקְדִּישַׁתּוּ, מַאי? תִּפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: קָדְמָה וְהִקְרִיבַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Oshaya raises a dilemma:** If **she first consecrated** the lamb before they engaged in intercourse but had not yet sacrificed it, **what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara suggests: **Resolve** the dilemma **from that** which **Rabbi Elazar** said, **as Rabbi Elazar said** above: The *baraita* is referring to a case where the prostitute **first sacrificed** the lamb as an offering.

Building on this, the *gemara* poses a further question from *Rav Oshaya*: what if she did not bring the animal as a *korban* but merely sanctified it — *kinah v'hiktishaso mah*? He gave it to her, she sanctified it, and then they had relations. Does the sanctification protect the animal from being redefined as an *esnan* once the *bi'ah* occurs?

### Ownership Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקְרִיבַתּוּ הִיא דְּהָא לֵיתֵיהּ בִּשְׁעַת בִּיאָה, אֲבָל הִקְדִּישַׁתּוּ — אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** It can be inferred that it **is** not considered payment only if she actually **sacrificed it, as** the lamb **is not extant at the time of intercourse. But** if she merely **consecrated it** and had not yet sacrificed it, the lamb is **prohibited,** i.e., it is considered payment to a prostitute and therefore prohibited to be sacrificed upon the altar.

The *gemara* suggests resolving this from *Rabbi Elazar*'s ruling: since *Rabbi Elazar* addressed the case of *hikrivaso* — actual offering — one might infer that sanctification alone would not be sufficient protection, and that the animal's *esnan* status would attach once the relations took place. But the *gemara* rejects this inference: that may have been the obvious case for *Rabbi Elazar*, not a limit he intended to draw. Perhaps he was uncertain about the sanctification case himself.

The question thus turns on whether *amiraso l'gavoha kimesirato l'hedyot* — verbal sanctification is equivalent to physical transfer to another person — applies here. If once she sanctifies the animal it is considered as though she has already given it over to the *Beit HaMikdash*, it is no longer in her possession and cannot be redefined by subsequent events.

### Ownership Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִיא גּוּפַהּ קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: הִקְרִיבַתּוּ, דְּהָא לֵיתֵיהּ בִּשְׁעַת בִּיאָה — מוּתָּר, אֲבָל הִקְדִּישַׁתּוּ בִּשְׁעַת בִּיאָה — אָסוּר. אוֹ דִּלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דִּתְנַן "אֲמִירָתוֹ לַגָּבוֹהַּ כִּמְסִירָתוֹ לְהֶדְיוֹט", הִקְדִּישַׁתּוּ מוּתָּר, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן הִקְרִיבַתּוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rav Oshaya **raises this** inference **itself** as **a dilemma,** as follows: May one infer from Rabbi Elazar's statement that it is only if **she sacrificed it** before their intercourse **and it is no** longer in existence **at the time of intercourse** that it is **permitted; but** if she had merely **consecrated it at the time of intercourse,** the lamb is **prohibited.** **Or perhaps** one can argue otherwise: **Since we learned** in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 28b) that **a declaration to the Most High,** i.e., consecrating an item through speech, **is equivalent to transferring** an item **to an ordinary person,** therefore if she verbally **consecrated** the lamb before she engaged in intercourse with the man, it should be **permitted** to be sacrificed on the altar, as once she has dedicated the animal to God it no longer belongs to her. **And all the more so** the animal should be permitted if **she** actually **sacrificed it.** No answer is found, and the Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved. What is clear from the discussion so far is the following: prepayment does not automatically foreclose *esnan* status. If he paid in advance and then they had relations, it *does* become an *esnan* — the critical moment is the *bi'ah*, and if the animal was still available and owned by her at that moment, the *esnan* status attaches. The only protection is if the animal has already left her control — either through bringing it as a *korban* or, as the *gemara* leaves unresolved, through sanctification.

Now regarding post-*bi'ah* payment: the *gemara* turns to a *baraita* that states *ba'al aleha v'nasan lah v'afilu ad shneim asar chodesh esnan assur* — if he had relations and then paid her, even twelve months later it is forbidden. This is counterintuitive given the earlier statement that post-payment is *mutar*. *Rav Chanan bar Rav Chisda* resolves the contradiction: the distinction turns on whether the specific animal was identified at the time of *bi'ah*. If he said *hibbe'ali li b'esnan zeh* — sleep with me and I'll give you *this* sheep — then even though payment came after, the sheep had been designated at the moment of the act and therefore acquires *esnan* status when it is eventually paid. If however he said generically *hibbe'ali li* — sleep with me and I'll give you something — without identifying a specific animal, then no animal acquires *esnan* status when paid later, because at the moment of *bi'ah* nothing had been designated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְאַחַר כָּךְ נָתַן לָהּ אֶתְנַנָּה — מוּתָּר, וְהָתַנְיָא: בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְנָתַן לָהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ עַד שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ — אֶתְנַנָּה אָסוּר! אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאָמַר לַהּ "הִבָּעֲלִי לִי בְּטָלֶה זֶה", הָא דְּאָמַר לַהּ "הִבָּעֲלִי לִי בְּטָלֶה" סְתָם.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* teaches: If **he engaged in intercourse with her and afterward gave her** payment, **her payment is permitted.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: If **he engaged in intercourse with her and** afterward **gave her** payment, **her payment** is **prohibited** to be sacrificed on the altar, **and** this is the case **even** if he gave it to her as much **as twelve months** later. **Rav Ḥanan bar Rav Ḥisda said:** It is **not difficult,** as **this** second *baraita* is referring to a situation where he set aside a particular lamb and **said to her** before engaging in intercourse: **Engage in intercourse with me for this lamb.** In this case that lamb is considered hers as soon as they engage in intercourse, even if it was not actually given to her until much later. Therefore, it is prohibited to be sacrificed on the altar as payment to a prostitute. By contrast, **that** first *baraita* is referring to a case **where he said to her: Engage in intercourse with me for a lamb, without specification.** Since he did not designate a lamb for her services at the time, the lamb he gives her later is not considered payment.

The *gemara* is thus consistent: what matters above all is that at the critical moment of *bi'ah*, the specific animal be identified and — crucially — that she own it or take ownership at that moment. Hence even when payment is designated in advance, the *gemara* requires that she actually have ownership at the time of the act. In the case of a non-Jewish *zona*, *meshicha* — physical act of acquisition — is not required; she acquires through *kesef*, payment, and since the act of relations is her payment, she acquires simultaneously with the *bi'ah*. Alternatively, even for a Jewish woman, if the animal was already in her courtyard and he stipulated that it would be hers upon *bi'ah*, then the moment of *bi'ah* is the moment of *kinyan* and the *esnan* status attaches at that very instant.

## Zonah Definition

### All Arayot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אֶחָד אֶתְנַן זָכָר, וְאֶחָד אֶתְנַן כׇּל עֲרָיוֹת אָסוּר, חוּץ מֵאֶתְנַן אִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss the definition of payment to a prostitute that is prohibited to be sacrificed on the altar. **Rav says: Both the payment** given for intercourse of a male with **a male, and the payment** given for intercourse with **any** of **those with whom relations are forbidden,** have the status of payment to a prostitute and are therefore **prohibited.** Such an animal may not be used as a sacrifice, **except for payment** to **one's menstruating wife.**

*Rav Acha* in the name of *Rav* teaches: *echad esnan zachar v'echad esnan kol arayos assur* — whether it is payment for homosexual sex or payment for any category of *arayos*, the payment is forbidden. This is a striking formulation: *Rav Acha* is not saying that *esnan* applies when a woman is paid for ordinary sex, with *arayos* being a fortiori. Rather, his claim is that *zonah* in the verse means a woman with whom sex is already forbidden on another ground — *arayos*. The prohibition of *esnan* is thus predicated on the sex itself being a transgression. A simple, unmarried Jewish woman who immerses in the *mikveh* regularly and is not married would not, on the initial assumption of this position, fall into this category at all.

### Wife Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? "זוֹנָה" כְּתִיב, וְהָא לָאו זוֹנָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that payment to one's menstruating wife is permitted? The reason is that **it is written "prostitute,"** with regard to this prohibition: "You shall not bring the hire of a prostitute or the price of a dog into the House of the Lord your God, for both of them are an abomination to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 23:19), **and** this woman, his menstruating wife, **is not a prostitute.**

There is one exception: *chutz me-esnan ishto niddah* — if a husband pays his wife to have relations when she is a *niddah*, that does not constitute *esnan*, even though *niddah* is technically a form of *arayos*. *Mai ta'ama?* — why? *Zona kesiv v'ha lav zona hee* — the verse says *zonah*, and a man's wife cannot be called a *zonah*. The concept of *zenut*, illicit sexual conduct, does not apply within the marital relationship, even if the specific act is forbidden.

### Levi Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵוִי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ אִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה. מַאי טַעְמָא? "תּוֹעֵבָה" כְּתִיב, וְהָא תּוֹעֵבָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Levi says: Even** the payment for intercourse with **one's menstruating wife** is considered payment to a prostitute and is prohibited to be sacrificed on the altar. **What is the reason? It is written "abomination"** in the above verse, **and this** intercourse with one's menstruating wife **is** also called **an abomination,** as it is written with regard to those with whom relations are forbidden, including a menstruating wife: "For whoever shall do any of these abominations" (Leviticus 18:29).

*Levi* disagrees: *afilu ishto niddah* — even payment to one's wife during *niddah* is forbidden as *esnan*. *Mai ta'ama?* The verse states *ki toevat Hashem Elokecha* — because it is an abomination to God. And *toevah* is precisely the word used in *Acharei Mot* and *Kedoshim* for the entire category of forbidden sexual relationships, including *niddah*. Even if one accepts that a wife cannot be called a *zonah*, the act itself is a *toevah* — and the verse links *esnan* to the category of *toevah*, not only to the label of *zonah*.

### Gender Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵוִי נָמֵי, וְהָכְתִיב "זוֹנָה"! אָמַר לָךְ: הָהִיא "זוֹנָה", וְלֹא זוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And** according to the opinion of **Levi as well, isn't it written "prostitute,"** with regard to this payment, and one's wife is not a prostitute. The Gemara answers that Levi could have **said to you: That** term teaches that this prohibition applies only to **a female prostitute** who receives payment from a man, **and not** to **a male prostitute** who is paid by a woman.

*Levi*'s position prompts a question: what does he do with the word *zonah* in the verse, which seems to require that the woman be a *zonah*? He responds: *zonah v'lo zoneh* — the verse is using *zonah* to exclude the male equivalent. Payment for a woman paying a man for heterosexual sex is not *esnan*. The word *zonah* appears in the feminine not to restrict the category by type of relationship, but to exclude the scenario where the woman is the payer.

### Rebbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֵין אֶתְנַן אָסוּר אֶלָּא אֶתְנַן הַבָּאוֹת לוֹ בַּעֲבֵירָה, אֲבָל אֶתְנַן אִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה, וְשֶׁנָּתַן לָהּ שָׂכָר לְהַפְקָעָתָהּ, וְשֶׁנָּתְנָה לוֹ בְּאֶתְנַנּוֹ — מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that Rav **derives it from that** which **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi said. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The **only payment** to a prostitute that is **prohibited** for sacrifice on the altar **is any payment** given to one with **whom intercourse is a transgression for him** and is never permitted. **But** with regard to an animal given as **payment** by a husband to **his menstruating wife** for intercourse, **and** payment **that he gave** to a prostitute as **a fee for her neglect** of other paid labor that she could have been performing at that time, but not as payment for intercourse, **and** an animal **that** a woman **gave him as his payment,** all these are **permitted** to be sacrificed on the altar.

*Rebbi* frames the underlying principle most explicitly: *ein esnan assur ella kol esnan ha-ba al aveirah* — the payment is forbidden only when it comes in the context of a transgression. This makes clear that ordinary payment for services, where the sex itself is not otherwise forbidden, does not constitute *esnan zonah* at all. The prohibition is not about the commercial dimension but about the forbidden-sexual-act dimension.

### Payment Type

*Rebbi*'s statement also clarifies two further exclusions. If a man pays his wife *sechar l'hafka'atah* — compensating her for time she is taking off from her work, rather than paying for the sexual act itself — that payment is not *esnan*, because the payment is not *ba'al aveirah*, not the fee for the transgression. The transgression of *niddah* is happening, but the payment is for something else.

### Woman Pays

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר: "וּבְתִתֵּךְ אֶתְנָן וְאֶתְנַן לֹא נִיתַּן לָךְ וַתְּהִי לְהֶפֶךְ".
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi adds: **Even though there is no proof for the matter,** that an animal given by a woman to a man for services rendered is not considered payment to a prostitute, nevertheless there is **an allusion to the matter** in the verses: "To all prostitutes gifts are given; but you have given your gifts to all your lovers, and have bribed them to come unto you from every side in your harlotries. And the contrary is in you from other women, in that you did solicit to prostitution, and you were not solicited; **and in that you gave hire, and no hire is given to you, thus you are contrary"** (Ezekiel 16:33–34). This indicates that it is contrary to the manner of prostitution for the woman to pay the man to engage in intercourse with her.

*Ve-she-natnah lo esnannah* — and if a woman paid a man for sex, that too is *mutar*. The verse in Yechezkel, *u-ve-tittekh esnan v'esnan lo nittan lach va-tehi le-hefekh* — "when you give payment and no payment is given to you, it is all reversed" — provides the textual basis. The *pasuk* describes the reversed scenario — the woman paying rather than receiving — as a complete inversion of the norm, signaling that it is outside the classic definition of *esnan*. This applies in all circumstances, even if the sex itself involves *arayos*. A man paying for homosexual sex, by contrast, remains within the category of *esnan* when the relationship involves *arayos*.

Summarizing the exclusions: payment for time off rather than for the sex itself; payment by a wife during *niddah* (according to Rav Acha's view); and payment by a woman to a man — all are excluded from the *esnan* prohibition.

The question of a single woman who is a *niddah* — as opposed to a wife who is a *niddah* — is left open by the *gemara*. Two possible framings emerge. On Rav Acha's reading, the exclusion of *ishto niddah* rests on the marital relationship itself precluding the designation *zonah*. On *Rebbi*'s reading, the exclusion rests on the permissibility of an alternative — sex with the same woman when she is *tahor* — which means the *niddah* status is not part of her fixed identity as someone with whom the relationship is inherently and always forbidden. Whether the same logic extends to a single woman who is a *niddah* depends on which of these framings one accepts.

### Rava Post-Factum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְאַחַר כָּךְ נָתַן לָהּ אֶתְנַנָּה מוּתָּר, וְהָתַנְיָא: בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְנָתַן לָהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ עַד שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ — אֶתְנַנָּה אָסוּר! אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאָמַר לַהּ "הִבָּעֲלִי לִי בְּטָלֶה זֶה", הָא דְּאָמַר לַהּ "הִבָּעֲלִי לִי בְּטָלֶה" סְתָם.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* teaches: If **he engaged in intercourse with her and afterward gave her** payment, **her payment is permitted.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: If **he engaged in intercourse with her and** afterward **gave her** payment, **her payment** is **prohibited** to be sacrificed on the altar, **and** this is the case **even** if he gave it to her as much **as twelve months** later. **Rav Ḥanan bar Rav Ḥisda said:** It is **not difficult,** as **this** second *baraita* is referring to a situation where he set aside a particular lamb and **said to her** before engaging in intercourse: **Engage in intercourse with me for this lamb.** In this case that lamb is considered hers as soon as they engage in intercourse, even if it was not actually given to her until much later. Therefore, it is prohibited to be sacrificed on the altar as payment to a prostitute. By contrast, **that** first *baraita* is referring to a case **where he said to her: Engage in intercourse with me for a lamb, without specification.** Since he did not designate a lamb for her services at the time, the lamb he gives her later is not considered payment.

*Rava* closes the discussion with a ruling that is, as the *gemara* itself seems to recognize, genuinely surprising: *ba aleha v'achar kach nasan lah esnanah mutar* — if he had relations with her and only afterward gave her the payment, it is *mutar*. The reason: *mai ta'ama?* At the moment he gave it to her, it was no more than any other gift — *be'alata lav esnan hu*, the payment for the *bi'ah* was not the animal, and therefore the animal does not acquire the character of *esnan*.

The *gemara* then specifies that *Rava*'s ruling is necessary even in a case where he explicitly said to her: "take this as payment for that sexual act we just performed." Even with this explicit declaration, if the animal is given *after* the act, it does not become an *esnan*. This is a striking *chiddush*. Intuitively one would think that any payment explicitly designated as compensation for forbidden sex should be caught by the prohibition. Yet the *gemara* appears committed to the view that unless there is something functioning as a pre-existing or simultaneous contract — unless the animal was owned by her at the moment of the act — the post-*factum* gift, however explicitly framed, does not take on the ritual character of *esnan*. The definition is transactional in a precise, technical sense: it is the moment of *bi'ah* that is determinative, and what has not yet passed to her by that moment cannot be retroactively contaminated by the events that preceded the giving.
