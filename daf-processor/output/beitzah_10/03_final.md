# Beitzah 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chicken Designation

### Intro & Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִטּוֹל אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נִעְנַע מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עוֹמֵד וְאוֹמֵר "זֶה וָזֶה אֲנִי נוֹטֵל".
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say: One may not take** fledgling doves from a dovecote on a Festival, **unless he shook** the ones he wished to take **while it was still day,** thereby preparing them. **And Beit Hillel say:** It is not necessary to shake; rather, it is sufficient if one **stands** the day before **and says: I** will **take this** fledgling **and that** one.

Daf 10 opens in the midst of a *mishnah* discussion that continues the theme of the chicken coop from the preceding daf. While the earlier *gemara* addressed moving the ladder from one coop to another, the *mishnah* here turns to the question of designation — specifically, what happens when neither egg-laying nor slaughter has been explicitly contemplated for a given bird. The underlying premise of this *mishnah* is that most chickens in a coop are not regularly intended for *shechita*. People do not, as a matter of routine, take chickens from their coops and slaughter them casually.

### Default Muktzeh

This presumption carries a direct halachic consequence: the default status of a chicken is *muktzeh*. Since one would not ordinarily assume that a particular bird was set aside for eating, the chicken is treated as set aside for another purpose — namely, laying eggs or breeding — and may not be handled on *Yom Tov*. To render a chicken non-*muktzeh*, one must designate it before *Yom Tov*, indicating in some fashion that this is the bird intended for slaughter.

### Shammai/Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר אַמֵּי: מַחְלוֹקֶת בִּבְרִיכָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: גָּזְרִינַן דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאִמְּלוֹכֵי. וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: לָא גָּזְרִינַן. אֲבָל בִּבְרִיכָה שְׁנִיָּה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּעוֹמֵד וְאוֹמֵר "זֶה וָזֶה אֲנִי נוֹטֵל" סַגִּיא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥanan bar Ami said:** The **dispute** between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel applies **to** the **first brood,** the first pair of fledglings hatched in the first month of the year from a pair of doves. Those fledglings are usually left in the nest and are not used for food. **As Beit Shammai hold: We issue a decree** prohibiting taking them, as **perhaps** on the Festival **he will come to reconsider** after handling them and decide to leave them in their place, in which case he has moved them unnecessarily. However, once he has already shaken them, there is no further concern that he might change his mind on the Festival itself. **And Beit Hillel hold: We do not issue a decree,** as there is no concern that one might change his mind, and therefore a verbal commitment is sufficient. **However, with regard to the second brood,** the fledglings born on the second cycle in the following month, **everyone agrees** that it is **enough** for one **to stand and say: I** will **take this** one **and that** one.

The *mishnah* records the dispute between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* regarding the manner of designation. *Beis Shammai* requires physical handling: one must actually pick up and move the bird while it is still day before *Yom Tov* (*lo yitol ella im kein ni'anam miba'od yom*). *Beis Hillel*, by contrast, permits verbal designation from a distance — one may stand and point, declaring "this one and that one I shall take" (*omed ve'omer zeh vezeh ani notel*) — without physically lifting the bird. Both positions agree, however, that some act of designation is required. The default remains *muktzeh*, and without affirmative designation, the bird may not be used.

### First/Second Brood

Rav Chanan bar Ammi introduces an important qualification to this dispute. He explains that the debate between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* applies specifically to the first brood (*breicha rishonah*) — the initial set of chicks hatched by a hen, which by custom would typically be left with the mother and not slaughtered. Since these birds occupy an unusual position — they are birds one might ordinarily not take — *Beis Shammai* worries (*gazrinan dilma ati le'amluchay*) that even if one verbally designates such a bird, one might change one's mind on *Yom Tov* itself when confronted with the psychological pull of keeping it. *Beis Hillel* holds that verbal designation is sufficient even here and that we need not fear a change of heart.

By the second brood and beyond, however, where it is entirely commonplace to designate birds for slaughter, all agree (*divrei hakol*) that standing and verbally indicating "this one and that one I shall take" is sufficient. These birds occupy a more natural place in the category of food preparation, and there is no concern that the designation will be overridden by second thoughts.

## Breira Debate

### Generic vs Specific

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל — לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר "זֶה וָזֶה אֲנִי נוֹטֵל"? לֵימָא: "מִכָּאן אֲנִי נוֹטֵל לְמָחָר"?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to the opinion of **Beit Hillel, why does one need to say: I** will **take this** one **and that** one? **Let him say: From here,** this dovecote, **I will take tomorrow.** Why does one have to specify exactly which fledglings he plans to remove from the dovecote?

The *gemara* presses *Beis Hillel* on a further question. If verbal designation is sufficient, why must one specify *which* particular bird one intends to take? Why not simply declare, "I will take one of these birds tomorrow" — a generic statement that covers the entire group — without committing to a specific individual bird? This more general formulation seems to offer greater flexibility, so why does *Beis Hillel* require pointing to particular birds?

### Beira Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא בֵּית הִלֵּל לֵית לְהוּ בְּרֵירָה, וְהָתְנַן: הַמֵּת בַּבַּיִת וְלוֹ פְּתָחִים הַרְבֵּה — כּוּלָּן טְמֵאִים. נִפְתַּח אֶחָד מֵהֶן, הוּא טָמֵא, וְכוּלָּן טְהוֹרִין. חִשֵּׁב לְהוֹצִיאוֹ בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן, אוֹ בְּחַלּוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה — מַצֶּלֶת עַל כׇּל הַפְּתָחִים כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **Beit Hillel do not accept** the principle of retroactive **designation,** i.e., they reject the idea that a person's original intention can be clarified retroactively, and therefore it cannot be said that the fledglings one takes on the Festival were the ones he had in mind beforehand, **but didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Ohalot* 7:3): If **a corpse** is located **in a house, and** the house **has many entrances,** whether they are open or closed, not only is the inside of the house ritually impure as a tent of the dead, but **all** the entrances **are** likewise **ritually impure** and impart impurity to the vessels they contain? The reason is that the corpse might be removed via any of the entrances, and therefore they all contract impurity. If only **one of** the entrances **was open** after the person died, that entrance alone **is ritually impure,** as the corpse will certainly be removed through it, **and all** the other entrances remain **ritually pure.** The same applies if **one decided** from the outset **to remove** the corpse **through one of** the entrances, **or** to carry it out **through a window that is four by four** handbreadths in size, which is large enough for a corpse. This intention **spares all the** other **entrances** from ritual impurity, as it is clear that the corpse will not be removed through them.

One possible answer is that *Beis Hillel* does not accept the principle of *breira* — retroactive specification. *Breira* is the doctrine whereby a non-specific designation made at one moment becomes concretized retroactively once a particular item is selected: when you later choose a specific bird, we treat it as though that bird was always the one you had in mind. If *Beis Hillel* rejects *breira*, then generic designation can never be validated retroactively, and specific designation is therefore required from the outset.

Before accepting this answer, the *gemara* notes that the assumption underlying this entire discussion requires some examination. One might think that even without *breira*, a generic declaration like "I may take from among these ten birds" should render the entire group non-*muktzeh* — not because one has retroactively identified which bird was intended, but simply because one has affirmatively indicated that any of them might be used. The entire group, on this view, would be *muchan* (prepared for use) by virtue of having been designated as a potential pool. Yet the *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that designation must be specific — only the bird actually used is the non-*muktzeh* one — and therefore the only way a generic declaration could work is through *breira*, which would retroactively identify the selected bird as the one that was always designated.

To test whether *Beis Hillel* accepts *breira*, the *gemara* introduces a proof from a *mishnah* in *Ohalot*.

### Ohalot Case

The *mishnah* in *Ohalot* deals with the following scenario: there is a corpse inside a house, and the house has many doors, each with an overhang (*ohel*) extending outside. The relevant principle is *sof tumah latzet* — the point through which the *tumah* will eventually exit the house. Even while a door remains closed, if it is identified as the door through which the body will be carried out, that door is treated as though it were already open. Anyone standing beneath the overhang of that door becomes *tamei* even before the door is opened.

When all the doors are closed and none has been designated as the exit point, every door is considered a potential *sof tumah latzet*, and the overhang beneath each door carries *tumah*. Once one door is opened, it becomes the presumptive exit, and the overhangs beneath the remaining closed doors are rendered *tahor*. Similarly, if one consciously designates a specific door — or even a window of at least four by four *tefachim* — as the intended exit, that door alone bears the *tumah*, and the others are saved.

### Retroactive Designtn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: וְהוּא שֶׁחִשֵּׁב לְהוֹצִיאוֹ עַד שֶׁלֹּא יָמוּת הַמֵּת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף מִשֶּׁיָּמוּת הַמֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this case, **Beit Shammai say: This** *halakha* applies only **if one had decided to remove** the corpse by a particular entrance **before the person died,** so that the entrance through which the body would be removed was already determined at the time of death. **And Beit Hillel say:** This *halakha* applies **even after the person died,** as the principle of retroactive designation is invoked, which means that the entrance through which the deceased will be removed is retroactively designated. This indicates that Beit Hillel do accept the principle of retroactive designation.

*Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* disagree about when this designation must be made. *Beis Shammai* holds that designation is effective only if made before the person died. If one designates a door before death, then when death occurs, the *tumah* radiates through that designated door alone. *Beis Hillel* holds that designation is effective even after the person has died. The question is whether *Beis Hillel*'s position implies *breira*: once the person dies, all doors initially become potential *sof tumah latzet* doors and the areas beneath all overhangs are *tamei*. When one then selects a specific door, does this retroactively (*lemafrea*) save the other overhangs — as though they were never the designated exit — or does it only render them *tahor* going forward (*mikan ule'haba*)?

If *Beis Hillel* holds that the other doors are saved retroactively, this is a clear application of *breira*, demonstrating that *Beis Hillel* accepts the doctrine. The implication would be startling: someone who was standing beneath a non-designated overhang during the interval between death and designation might retroactively be revealed to have never been *tamei* — and would need to be tracked down and informed.

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבָּה: לְטַהֵר אֶת הַפְּתָחִים מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: לְטַהֵר אֶת הַפְּתָחִים מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא. מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא — אִין, לְמַפְרֵעַ — לָא. רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לְמַפְרֵעַ, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: דִּלְמָא מְטַלְטֵל וְשָׁבֵיק, מְטַלְטֵל וְשָׁבֵיק, וְקָא מְטַלְטֵל מִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: **Wasn't it stated** by *amora'im* **with regard to this** mishna that **Rabba said:** In this case, Beit Hillel referred only **to purifying the entrances from that point forward.** In other words, after one has decided on an entrance that he will use to remove the dead, any vessels in the space of the other entrances do not become ritually impure. However, vessels that were located inside the space of the other entrances before the decision was made remain impure. According to these *amora'im*, Beit Hillel do not accept the principle of retroactive designation. **And, so too, Rav Oshaya said:** This *halakha* is referring **to purifying the entrances from that point forward,** which indicates: **From here onward, yes,** the entrances are ritually pure; **retroactively, no,** they are not considered pure. **Rava said** that there is a different way to resolve the conflicting rulings of Beit Hillel: **Actually,** in the case of ritual impurity, Beit Hillel maintain that all the entrances are ritually pure **retroactively. And here,** with regard to fledglings, **this is the reason** why Beit Hillel insist that one designate which particular fledglings one plans to take on the Festival: **Perhaps one will move** fledglings, **and put** them **down,** and again **move** them, **and put** them **down** until he finds the pair he wants, thereby **moving an object that is not suitable for him,** i.e., which is *muktze*.

The *gemara* resolves this by citing Rava and Rabbi Yoshiya, both of whom clarify: *Beis Hillel* means only that the other doors are *tahor* going forward (*mikan ule'haba*), not retroactively (*lemafrea lo*). This is a striking conclusion — *Beis Hillel* does not hold of *breira* after all. This raises an obvious difficulty: if major *tannaim* such as *Beis Hillel* reject *breira*, it becomes difficult to understand how any later *tanna* could accept it — but that is a broader discussion.

The consequence for the chicken case is that *Beis Hillel* indeed requires specific designation, and the generic formulation "I will take from among these" will not work precisely because *breira* would be needed to validate it retroactively, and *Beis Hillel* does not hold of *breira*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ — בְּעוֹמֵד וְאוֹמֵר "זֶה וָזֶה אֲנִי נוֹטֵל" סַגִּיא! הָנֵי מִילֵּי מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב, אֲבָל בְּיוֹם טוֹב אָסוּר, דְּזִימְנִין דְּמִשְׁתַּכְחִי שְׁמֵנִים כְּחוּשִׁים וּכְחוּשִׁים שְׁמֵנִים, וְקָמְטַלְטֵל מִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ. אִי נָמֵי זִימְנִין דְּמִשְׁתַּכְחִי כֻּלְּהוּ כְּחוּשִׁים וְשָׁבֵיק לְהוּ, וְאָתֵי לְאִמְּנוֹעֵי מִשִּׂמְחַת יוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **But** if this is indeed the concern, **didn't you say** that **it is enough** if **one stands and says: I** will **take this** one **and that** one. In this case, too, he does not touch them. It is therefore possible that he may reconsider his choice of fledglings on the Festival after he handles them. The Gemara answers: **This applies only** when one made his choice **on the eve of the Festival.** Since he knows that he may not alter his decision on the Festival itself even if he does not like the ones he chose, he definitively decides to take those fledglings and will not handle others. **However, on the Festival** it is **prohibited, as sometimes** those that seemed to him to be **fat ones will be found** to be **thin, and thin ones** will be found to be **fat, and he** will **move an object that is not suitable for him,** thereby transgressing the prohibition against moving *muktze* objects. **Alternatively, sometimes they will all be found** to be **thin, and he will leave them** all, **and he will come to neglect the rejoicing of the Festival.** If, however, one announces the day before: I will take this one and that one, he will indeed take only those fledglings, thereby enhancing his Festival joy.

Rava then offers a second, independent answer. Even if *Beis Hillel* were to accept *breira*, generic designation would still be impermissible in the chicken case due to a practical concern. If one goes to the coop on *Yom Tov* and picks up a bird, finds it unsatisfactory, puts it down, picks up a second, rejects that one too, and finally settles on a third, *breira* would retroactively identify the third bird as the designated one. But in the meantime, the person has handled the first and second birds — which retroactively turn out to have been *muktzeh* — and has thereby violated the prohibition against handling *muktzeh*. The risk of handling *muktzeh* in the process of selecting makes generic designation unworkable, even for those who accept *breira*.

## Pre-YomTov Logic

### Visual ID Question

The *gemara* raises a further question on *Beis Hillel*'s position. *Beis Hillel* permits designation by visual identification — standing at a distance and pointing. If visual identification from a distance is sufficient to establish which bird one intends, why not permit this same visual identification to be done on *Yom Tov* itself, rather than requiring it the day before?

### Rashi's Logic

Rashi addresses this directly. The requirement to designate before *Yom Tov* is not about ensuring that the person will not *want* to change their mind — they may well want to. Rather, it is about ensuring that they *cannot* change their mind in a halachically significant way. Once a specific bird has been designated before *Yom Tov*, and only that bird is non-*muktzeh* while all others remain *muktzeh*, the person is committed to that choice regardless of their feelings on *Yom Tov*. The pre-designation locks in the decision: the person may be dissatisfied, but they are stuck with the bird they selected. Visual designation is therefore entirely adequate — but it must happen before *Yom Tov*, because only then does the resulting constraint operate.

### Rejection Risk

If, however, designation is deferred until *Yom Tov* itself, two problems arise. First, the person may pick up a bird, find it fatter or thinner than expected (*dezimnin demishtakach shemanei kchushim ukchushim shemanim*), handle it and put it down, then handle another — thereby moving *muktzeh* with respect to birds that will ultimately not be the ones chosen, since retroactively only the final selection is the designated non-*muktzeh* bird. Second, and perhaps more seriously, the person may come in on *Yom Tov*, examine every bird, find all of them wanting, abandon all of them (*v'shavik lehu*), and thereby fail entirely to have a bird for *Yom Tov* (*v'ati l'amnu'ei misimchat Yom Tov*). If, by contrast, the designation was made the day before, even a bird that turns out to be less impressive than anticipated is the committed choice — psychologically and halachically. The prior designation ensures follow-through. Waiting until *Yom Tov* risks both the violation of handling *muktzeh* and the loss of the *Yom Tov* meal entirely.

## ID Discrepancies

### Color Changes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זִמֵּן שְׁחוֹרִים וּמָצָא לְבָנִים, לְבָנִים וּמָצָא שְׁחוֹרִים, שְׁנַיִם וּמָצָא שְׁלֹשָׁה — אֲסוּרִים. שְׁלֹשָׁה וּמָצָא שְׁנַיִם — מוּתָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If, on the eve of a Festival, one **designated black** fledglings to be slaughtered, **and** on the following day **found white** ones in the dovecote, rather than the birds he had designated, or if one designated **white** ones to be slaughtered **and found black** ones, or if one designated **two** fledglings to be slaughtered **and found three, they are prohibited,** as these are not the same fledglings he had designated earlier. If, however, one designated **three** to be slaughtered **and found** only **two, they are permitted,** as it is presumed that one of the fledglings escaped.

The *mishnah* now turns to cases where designation was made but uncertainty arises about whether the birds found are actually the ones designated. If one designated black birds and found white ones (*zimmen shechorim umatza levanim*), or designated white and found black, it is clear that these are not the same birds and they are *asur*. Similarly, if two were designated and three are found, all three are forbidden — either all are new and entirely *muktzeh*, or at least one new one has mixed in and the group is compromised regardless. If three were designated and only two are found, those two are *muttar*, since we assume one left and the original two remain.

### Rava's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ פְּשִׁיטָא! אָמַר רַבָּה: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁזִּמֵּן שְׁחוֹרִים וּלְבָנִים, וְהִשְׁכִּים וּמָצָא שְׁחוֹרִים בִּמְקוֹם לְבָנִים וּלְבָנִים בִּמְקוֹם שְׁחוֹרִים. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי אִינְהוּ נִינְהוּ וְאִתְהֲפוֹכֵי אִתְהֲפוּךְ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: הָנָךְ אֲזַדוּ לְעָלְמָא, וְהָנֵי אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: But it is **obvious** that if one designated black ones to be slaughtered and found white ones, they are not the same birds. **Rabba said: With what are we dealing here?** The mishna is referring to a case **where one designated** both **black and white** ones to be slaughtered, **and he arose and found black ones in the place** where the **white ones** had been, **and white ones in** the **place** of the **black ones. Lest you say: These are those** same fledglings **and** they simply **exchanged** places, the mishna **teaches us** that this claim is not accepted. Instead, it is assumed that **these** ones that he designated **have gone to the** outside **world, and these** ones that he found **are others** that have come from elsewhere.

The *gemara* initially asks: is the color-change case so obvious as to be a *chidush*? Surely if you designated black birds and found white ones, it is self-evident that these are different birds. Rava clarifies that the *mishnah* is presenting a single case, not two alternatives. One designated black birds in one coop and white birds in another coop, and found upon returning that the positions were reversed — white birds now in the black birds' coop and black birds in the white birds' coop. One might have thought (*mah d'teima*) that these are the same birds and they simply switched locations with one another (*v'isupechi isapech*). The *chidush* of the *mishnah* is that this reasoning is rejected.

### Displacement Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: רוֹב וְקָרוֹב — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הָרוֹב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that** this mishna **supports Rabbi Ḥanina, as Rabbi Ḥanina said:** When resolving an uncertainty with regard to the identity of an item, if the **majority** indicates one ruling but the item in question is **proximate** to a source that indicates otherwise, one **follows the majority.** In this case, the majority of fledglings are prohibited, as they were not designated for use on the Festival. Consequently, the found fledglings are prohibited, despite the fact that the nearest fledglings are the designated ones.

The *gemara* explains why this assumption fails. Once we conclude that the originally designated black birds are no longer in their designated location (*hanach azdu l'alma*), we cannot then assume that the black birds found elsewhere are necessarily the designated ones. The moment a bird is dislodged from its original location and we must account for its absence, we cannot simply assume it was absorbed into another spot where designated birds of that color happen to be found. Perhaps those found elsewhere are entirely different birds (*achrinei ninhu*). Once displaced, one cannot claim identity with birds found in an adjacent designated location.

### Two→Three Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנַיִם וּמָצָא שְׁלֹשָׁה — אֲסוּרִין. מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ — הָא אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ, וְאִי לָא אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ — הָא אִיכָּא חַד דִּמְעָרַב בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that if one designated **two and found three, they are prohibited.** The Gemara explains: **Whichever** way **you** look at this matter, the fledglings are prohibited. **If these are others, they are others** that have not been designated. **And if they are not others,** i.e., two of the fledglings that one designated are still there, nevertheless **there is one** that he certainly did not designate and **that is mingled with them,** which renders the others prohibited as well.

In the case of two designated and three found (*shnayim umatza shlosha*), the *gemara* explains the ruling through a *mimma nafshach* argument: either all three are new birds — in which case all are *muktzeh* — or only one is new and the others are the original designated birds, but then at least one has mixed in and rendered the group forbidden. Either way, all three are *asur*.

### Three→Two Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה וּמָצָא שְׁנַיִם מוּתָּרִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָנֵי אִינְהוּ נִינְהוּ, וְחַד מִנַּיְיהוּ אֲזַל לְעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that if one designated **three and found two, they are permitted.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara explains: **These are the same ones, and one of them has gone to the** outside **world,** leaving two behind.

In the reverse case — three designated and two found (*shlosha umatza shnayim*) — we assume that one of the original three simply wandered off (*chad mineihu azal le'alma*) and the two remaining are from the original designated group. We do not assume that all three of the originals left and were replaced by two entirely new birds.

## Subset Debate

### Money Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי הִיא וְלָא רַבָּנַן, דְּתַנְיָא: הִנִּיחַ מָנֶה וּמָצָא מָאתַיִם — חוּלִּין וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַכֹּל חוּלִּין. הִנִּיחַ מָאתַיִם וּמָצָא מָנֶה, מָנֶה מוּנָּח וּמָנֶה מוּטָל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַכֹּל חוּלִּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and not** that of **the Rabbis, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **placed one hundred dinars** of tithe money in a safe place **and found two hundred** there, it is assumed that this is **non-sacred** money **and second-tithe** money **mingled together.** Someone must have come and added another hundred to his tithe money. Therefore, he separates one dinar from the total and states: Whatever part of this is the tithe, is fine; whatever part of it is non-tithe money, let it be used to redeem the tithe money, and this should be sanctified as tithe money in its place. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: It is all non-sacred** money. Since the money is not as he left it, the assumption is that someone took the original one hundred dinars; and he, or someone else, replaced it with two hundred dinars of regular money. Conversely, if one **placed two hundred** dinars **and found one hundred dinars,** it is presumed that **one hundred dinars has been left** in its place and is tithe money, **and one hundred dinars** was **removed.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: It is all non-sacred** money. It is assumed that someone removed all the money, and the one hundred dinars he found must have been placed there by someone else, and is not connected to the money he left. The *halakha* with regard to the case of fledglings apparently follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion in the case of second-tithe money.

The *gemara* raises the question of whether the *mishnah*'s leniency in the three-to-two case reflects the position of *Rabbi* (Rebbi) alone and not the Sages. The comparison is drawn to a parallel *baraita* concerning *ma'aser sheni* money. If one left a *maneh* (one hundred units) of *ma'aser sheni* money in a box and returned to find two hundred, the question is how to characterize what remains. *Rabbi* holds that the original hundred is still present and the second hundred is new, so there is a mixture of *ma'aser sheni* and *chulin* money. The Sages say something startling: everything is *chulin*. Their position is that since there is clearly a discrepancy — something is different — we assume that the entire original amount was removed and an entirely new sum was deposited in its place.

### Rabbi/Sages

The parallel case that maps onto our *mishnah* is the reverse: one left two hundred and found a hundred. *Rabbi* holds that one hundred remains from the original and one hundred was taken away, so what remains is *ma'aser sheni*. The Sages again say everything is *chulin* — the entire original was removed and this new hundred is unrelated to the original. If the Sages' position were applied to the chicken case, then whenever three birds were designated and only two remain, one would have to say these two are entirely new birds — and all would be *muktzeh*. Yet the *mishnah* permits them. Does this mean the *mishnah* represents only *Rabbi*'s view?

### Birds Walk Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, הָא אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: שָׁאנֵי גּוֹזָלוֹת, הוֹאִיל וַעֲשׂוּיִין לְדַדּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this claim: **Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** it can be explained, as **it was stated with regard to this** mishna that **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar both say: Fledglings are different, since they typically hop** from place to place. Therefore, it is possible that one of them escaped. By contrast, a money pouch cannot move of its own accord and must have been taken by someone.

The *gemara* rejects this conclusion. It cites Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar, who both state that *gozalot* (birds) are different (*shani gozalot*) because they are accustomed to moving about on their own (*ho'il ve'atzuyan lidatot*). When money disappears from a box, a human agent must be responsible — a person came and took it. When a person gets involved, the assumption is that they took the entire amount; if they needed only part, they might still take it all. The presence of human agency raises the suspicion that the entire original was removed. Birds, however, move by themselves. When one bird is missing from a group, there is no need to posit human intervention — the bird simply walked away. The remaining birds are therefore presumed to be part of the original group. This biological difference between money and birds is sufficient to distinguish the cases and to permit even the Sages to agree that the *mishnah*'s ruling is correct.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמָה לִי לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי עֲלַהּ שָׁאנֵי גּוֹזָלוֹת הוֹאִיל וַעֲשׂוּיִין לְדַדּוֹת? וְהָא אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ דְּהַהִיא, דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, חַד אָמַר: בִּשְׁנֵי כִיסִין מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל בְּכִיס אֶחָד — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חוּלִּין. וְחַד אָמַר: בְּכִיס אֶחָד מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל בִּשְׁנֵי כִיסִין — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מָנֶה מוּנָּח וּמָנֶה מוּטָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need **to answer with regard to it: Fledglings are different, since they typically hop? But wasn't it stated with regard to that** mishna, which deals with a money pouch, **that Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** disagree over it: **One** of them **said:** The **dispute** applies only **to** a case of **two pouches,** with one hundred dinars in each pouch. **However,** if all of the money was **in a single pouch, everyone agrees** that it is **non-sacred** money, as it is unlikely that only half of the money was removed while the other half was left in its place. **And** the other **one** of them **said:** The **dispute** applies only **to one pouch, but with regard to two pouches, everyone agrees** that **one hundred dinars** of tithe **is left and one hundred dinars** has been **removed.**

The *gemara* then asks: if this biological rationale is sufficient, why is it needed? The need to invoke "birds walk by themselves" arises specifically if the Sages' position — that a discrepancy renders everything new — applies even when the items in question are in separate, independent containers (as opposed to a single bundled unit). If the Sages only require complete replacement when all the money is in a single pouch, then birds, being naturally independent units, are obviously comparable to separate pouches, and the distinction between money and birds requires no additional argument.

### Bundling Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּשְׁנֵי כִיסִין מַחְלוֹקֶת, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי הָכָא שָׁאנֵי גּוֹזָלוֹת הוֹאִיל וַעֲשׂוּיִין לְדַדּוֹת. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּכִיס אֶחָד מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל בִּשְׁנֵי כִיסִין דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מָנֶה מוּנָּח וּמָנֶה מוּטָל — הַשְׁתָּא לְמָה לִי לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי עֲלַהּ? הָא אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁנֵי כִיסִין לָא פְּלִיגִי! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָכָא בְּגוֹזָלוֹת מְקוּשָּׁרִים וְכִיסִים מְקוּשָּׁרִים עָסְקִינַן. גּוֹזָלוֹת — מְנַתְּחִי אַהֲדָדֵי, כִּיסִין — לָא מְנַתְּחִי אַהֲדָדֵי. וְרַבִּי אָמַר לָךְ: כִּיסִין נָמֵי, זִמְנִין דְּמִתְעַכַּל קִטְרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Granted, according to the one who said** that the **dispute** deals with a case **of two pouches, this is** why **it was necessary to answer here: Fledglings are different, since they typically hop. However, according to the one who says** that the **dispute** involves a case **of one pouch, but with regard to two pouches everyone agrees** that **one hundred dinars** of tithe **is left and one hundred dinars** has been **removed, now** consider: **Why do I** need **to answer, with regard to it,** that there is a difference between that *halakha* and the case of fledglings? **You said** that **they do not disagree** even **with regard to two** separate **pouches,** and therefore they certainly do not disagree in the case of fledglings. **Rav Ashi said: Here we are dealing with fledglings tied** together **and** with money **pouches tied** together. In other words, when it is stated that the dispute applies to one pouch, this refers to two pouches that are tied together, as well. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the rabbis agree only with regard to two pouches that are entirely separate. Consequently, it is necessary to explain that there is a difference between pouches tied together and fledglings tied together: **Fledglings** can **release themselves from each other,** and therefore it is possible that only one of them escaped, whereas **pouches cannot release themselves from each other.** Therefore, it is evident that someone must have taken them. Since they were tied together, he certainly took both of them. The Gemara asks: **But** if that is indeed the case, and it is obvious that someone came and took the money, what is Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's reasoning? The Gemara explains: **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi could have **said to you: Pouches, too, sometimes** **their knot becomes worn** and untied. Consequently, it is possible that someone took only one of the two pouches.

Rav Ashi resolves this by clarifying that the debate between *Rabbi* and the Sages is not literally about whether there is one pouch or two, but about whether the items are bundled together or separate. When two pouches are tied together (*kisei mekusharin*), they function as a single bundle, and the Sages apply their stringent position. When they are entirely separate, all agree that the remaining item is from the original. Similarly, one could imagine birds tied together (*gozalot mekusharin*) — in that case too the Sages might say it is all new. But here there is an additional distinction: money pouches tied together stay bound (*kisim lo minthachei ahadadi*), whereas birds tied together naturally separate from one another (*gozalot minthachei ahadadi*) as they move about. *Rabbi* would respond to this by noting that even money pouches sometimes have their knots wear away (*zimnin demi'achel kitreihu*), so even there one might reasonably assume that what remains is from the original.

## Inside/Outside

### Basic Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּתוֹךְ הַקֵּן וּמָצָא לִפְנֵי הַקֵּן — אֲסוּרִין, וְאִם אֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא הֵם — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מוּתָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one designated them **inside the nest** and the next day he cannot find them there, **and he found** fledglings **before the nest, they are prohibited,** as they might be fledglings other than the ones he designated and left inside the nest. **But if there are only those** fledglings in the immediate vicinity, **they are permitted,** as it can be assumed that these are the ones he designated inside the nest.

The *mishnah* states that if birds were designated inside the nest and found outside (*betoch hakan umatza lifnei hakan*), they are forbidden. Intuitively, one might have supposed that the closest source for a bird found just outside the nest is the nest itself, and therefore it should be presumed to be the designated bird. The *mishnah* rules otherwise, and the *gemara* explores why proximity alone does not determine the outcome.

### Rov/Karov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: רוֹב וְקָרוֹב — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הָרוֹב!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments: **Let us say that** this mishna **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina, as Rabbi Ḥanina said:** When resolving an uncertainty with regard to the identity of an item, if the **majority** indicates one ruling but the item in question is **proximate** to a source that indicates otherwise, one **follows the majority.** Since doves from the outside world are more numerous than those that one designated, the assumption is that these fledglings are from the majority, and therefore they are prohibited.

The *gemara* proposes that this ruling supports Rabbi Chanina's principle: *rov ve'karov halach achar ha'rov* — when majority (*rov*) and proximity (*karov*) point in different directions, we follow the majority. In the nest scenario, the designated bird inside the nest is the *karov* — the closest possible source — but the majority of birds in the world are non-designated birds from elsewhere. Accordingly, one might follow *rov* and assume this bird came from the general population, not from inside the nest.

### Plank Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּדַף. רָבָא אָמַר: בִּשְׁנֵי קִנִּין זוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said,** in refutation of this claim: Here we are dealing **with a ledge** affixed to the front of the nest, where all the doves gather. Therefore, the principle pertaining to a majority and an item that is near does not apply to this case. **Rava said:** Here **we are dealing with two nests, one above the other,** i.e., adjacent nests rather than any two nests.

Abaye objects that the case discussed by the *mishnah* is specifically one where there is a *daf* — a plank — outside the nest. Pigeons from the surrounding area habitually come and perch on this plank, meaning that many other birds also regularly occupy a position immediately adjacent to the nest. In such a case, the non-designated birds from outside are also *karov*, not merely the *rov*. Accordingly, the prohibition in this case does not necessarily prove that *rov* overrides *karov* in general; it may simply reflect that here, the visiting birds are *karov* as well, and there is no clean contrast between *rov* and *karov* in the first place.

## Two Nests

### Bottom Desig

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא מִבַּעְיָא זִמֵּן בַּתַּחְתּוֹנָה וְלֹא זִמֵּן בָּעֶלְיוֹנָה, וּמָצָא בַּתַּחְתּוֹנָה וְלֹא מָצָא בָּעֶלְיוֹנָה — דַּאֲסִירָן, דְּאָמְרִינַן: הָנָךְ אֲזַלוּ לְעָלְמָא, וְהָנָךְ אִשְׁתְּרַבּוֹבֵי אִשְׁתַּרְבּוּב וּנְחוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is not necessary** to state that in the case of **one** who **designated** the fledglings in **the lower** nest **and did not designate** those **in the upper one, and he found** fledglings **in the lower one and he did not find** fledglings **in the upper one, that** the fledglings **are** all **prohibited.** The reason is **that we say: These** that were in the lower nest **went to the** outside **world, while these** still present **have dragged themselves and come down.**

Rava introduces a further scenario involving two nests arranged one above the other. The question is where a bird found outside a nest actually came from, and whether the existence of the second nest changes the analysis of *karov*.

The first case: one designated the birds in the lower nest but not the upper one (*ziman betachtonah velo ziman be'elyona*), and found a bird outside the lower nest while no bird remains in the upper nest (*matza lifnei hatachtonah velo matza be'elyona*). This is forbidden (*daasrinan*). The reason is that while one might say the bird found outside the lower nest came from within — in which case it would be the designated bird — it is equally possible that the original bird in the lower nest flew away (*hanach azlu le'alma*) and the bird from the upper nest descended and settled in front of the lower one (*ishtarvuvu venachit*). Since both sources — the interior of the lower nest and the upper nest — are equivalently *karov*, one cannot rely on proximity to validate the assumption that this is the designated bird.

### Why Not Karov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ זִמֵּן בָּעֶלְיוֹנָה וְלֹא זִמֵּן בַּתַּחְתּוֹנָה, וּבָא וּמָצָא בָּעֶלְיוֹנָה וְלֹא מָצָא בַּתַּחְתּוֹנָה — הָנָךְ נָמֵי אֲסִירִי, דְּאָמְרִינַן: הָנָךְ אֲזַלוּ לְעָלְמָא, וְהָנָךְ סָרוֹכֵי סָרוּךְ וּסְלִיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, even if one designated** fledglings **in the upper** nest **and did not designate** fledglings **in the lower** one, **and he came and found** fledglings **in the upper one and did not find** fledglings **in the lower one, those** in the upper nest **are also prohibited, as we say: Those** that he originally designated **went to the** outside **world, and those** in the lower nest **have clutched and climbed.** Therefore, there is cause for concern in both of these cases.

This case makes explicit why the *karov* argument fails in the two-nest configuration: it is not that *rov* overrides *karov*, but rather that there are multiple equally *karov* sources, only one of which is the designated bird. When *karov* points in more than one direction simultaneously, it cannot serve as a basis for leniency.

### Top Desig

The reverse scenario follows as well: one designated birds in the upper nest and not the lower (*ziman be'elyona velo ziman betachtonah*), and found a bird outside the upper nest while the lower nest is empty. Even here the ruling is that it is forbidden (*hanechu nami asiri*). Although it is physically more natural for a bird to descend from below than to climb up from below, the *gemara* acknowledges that it is still sufficiently possible that the lower bird climbed up (*sruchi sreich vesalik*) to prohibit reliance on an assumption that the found bird is the designated one from above. Both nests are *karov* in the relevant sense.

### 50-Amah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא הֵן — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מוּתָּרִין. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בִּמְפוֹרָחִין, אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: הָנָךְ אֲזַלוּ לְעָלְמָא, וְהָנֵי אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ. אֶלָּא בְּמִדַּדִּין. אִי דְּאִיכָּא קֵן בְּתוֹךְ חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה — אִדַּדּוֹיֵי אִדַּדּוֹ, וְאִי דְּלֵיכָּא קֵן בְּתוֹךְ חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה — פְּשִׁיטָא דְּמוּתָּרִין, דְּאָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: כׇּל הַמְדַדֶּה — אֵין מְדַדֶּה יוֹתֵר מֵחֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that **if there are no** others **there apart from them, they are permitted.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that the mishna is dealing **with** fledglings that **are** already **able** to **fly, it** is possible **to say** that **those** that he designated **went to the** outside **world, and the ones** that are present **are other ones.** **Rather,** the mishna must be referring **to** fledglings that can only **hop** from one place to another. However, **if** it deals with a case **where there is** another dove **nest within fifty cubits,** the fledglings might have **jumped** and come from that nest; **and if there is no nest within fifty cubits,** it is **obvious** that **they are permitted,** for from where could they have come? **As Mar Ukva bar Ḥama said:** With regard to **any** creature **that hops,** it **does not hop more than fifty cubits.**

The *mishnah* states that if there are no other birds in the area (*ein sham ela hem*), the found bird is *muttar*. The *gemara* clarifies that this ruling applies only to birds that are not yet able to fly — birds in the waddling stage (*bimdadin*). A bird that can fly could have arrived from anywhere, and the absence of nearby birds would provide no reliable inference. For a bird that can only waddle, the *gemara* further specifies that "no others in the area" means no nest within fifty *amah* — approximately seventy-five feet. If there is another nest within fifty *amah*, the waddling bird could have originated there, and one cannot assume it is the designated bird. The fifty-*amah* radius is the accepted maximum waddling distance for young pigeons, as formulated by Mar Ukva bar Chama: *kol hamadada ein madada yoter mechamishim amah* — one who waddles does not waddle more than fifty *amah*.

### Sightline Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּאִיכָּא קֵן בְּתוֹךְ חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה, וּכְגוֹן דְּקָיְימָא בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אִדַּדּוֹיֵי אִדַּדּוֹ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּמִדַּדֵּה וַהֲדַר חָזֵי לְקִנֵּיהּ — מִדַּדֵּה, וְאִי לָא — לָא מִדַּדֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it is referring to a case **where there is** another **nest within fifty cubits, and** it deals with a situation **where** the additional nest **is situated around a corner** from the first nest, rather than in a straight line from it. **Lest you say:** The fledglings **jumped** from one nest to the other, the mishna therefore **teaches us** that **anywhere that** a fledgling **hops and turns and sees its nest, it will** continue to **hop. But if** it can **no** longer see its original nest, **it will not hop** any farther.

The *gemara* asks: if there is another nest within fifty *amah* but it is around a corner (*bekeren zavit*) — out of the direct sightline of the designated nest — does the bird's potential origin in that other nest still create a problem? The *gemara* rules that this is precisely the case the *mishnah* addresses when it says that if there are no other birds in the vicinity they are permitted. The principle articulated is: a waddling bird will travel as far as it can while still being able to look back and see its home nest (*kol heicha demadada vehadar chazi lekaanei madada*). As long as the bird can see its home, it will continue to wander. But once a corner or obstacle removes its home from its sightline, it will not venture further (*ve'ee lo lo madada*). Therefore, a nest around a corner — even within fifty *amah* — does not create a concern that the bird wandered from there, because a bird originating from that nest would have lost sight of its home before reaching the designated nest and would not have continued. This elegant principle closes the discussion of the inside/outside nest scenario and the related question of how proximity and majority interact in determining the identity of designated birds.
