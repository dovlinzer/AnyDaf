# Pesachim 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabban Gamliel & Grate

### Grate Paradox

The *daf* opens two lines from the top of the page, within a *perek* that has been focused on the proper manner of roasting the *korban pesach*. The earlier discussion treated the type of wooden spit required — one that does not exude moisture, so the roasting is not akin to cooking — and the prohibition of a metal spit, which heats up and thereby roasts the meat through the heat of the metal rather than the direct heat of the fire. The *gemara* now turns to a line in the *mishna* that had prohibited roasting on a metal grate, immediately following which the *mishna* itself records a story: Rabban Gamliel instructed his servant Tavi to go and roast the *pesach* on a grate. The difficulty is glaring. The *gemara* asks: *maaseh listor?* — is this story being brought in order to contradict the very *halacha* just stated?

### Chasuri Mechasra

The *gemara* resolves the problem by invoking the principle of *chasuri mechasra vehachi katani* — the *mishna* is missing some words, and it must be read as follows: *ve'im askala menukevet mutar* — if the grate has perforations, it is permitted. A standard grate has a criss-cross pattern creating a solid surface; a *menukevet* grate, by contrast, has wide enough gaps between its bars that the meat can be suspended between them, resting over the fire directly rather than on the metal. In such a case, the meat is not genuinely roasting on the grate at all but is supported by it while the fire roasts it from below. Only then is it permissible. Rabbi Tzadok's account of Rabban Gamliel and Tavi is accordingly read as referring to this perforated variety. As with any *chasuri mechasra*, the reading is admittedly difficult, since once the meat is truly suspended between the bars, one wonders whether one is meaningfully roasting on the grate at all.

### Rashash Solution

There is, however, a far more compelling reading of the *mishna* — one that is both closer to the *pshat* and historically illuminating. The *Rashash*, a nineteenth-century commentator whose sharp and incisive notes appear in the back of the *gemara* (he was a wealthy businessman who paid to have his commentary included, a phenomenon that might appear to be a modern invention of the vanity press, though clearly it is not), offers the following observation. The Rabban Gamliel associated with Tavi the servant is, by all evidence, the Rabban Gamliel who lived after the *churban habayis*. What, then, is he doing instructing his servant to roast the *pesach*? The *Rashash* notes that in the *mishna* in *Beitzah*, this same Rabban Gamliel is recorded as permitting the preparation of a *gedi mekulas* — a fully roasted goat — on the eve of *Pesach*, and furthermore that he would refer to this dish as a *pesach*. This connects directly to the earlier story of Todos Ish Romi, who had the people of Rome prepare a *gedi mekulas* on *erev Pesach* and who similarly *v'hayu korin lo psachim* — called it *pesach* — provoking the strong objection of the Sages, who felt it resembled an actual *korban pesach* offered outside its proper context.

### Todos & Shinuy

According to the *Rashash*, Rabban Gamliel — living after the *churban* — was indeed maintaining this quasi-*pesach* ritual, preparing a fully roasted goat and calling it a *pesach*. But in order to create a *heiker*, a recognizable distinction from the actual *korban*, he deliberately introduced a *shinuy*: he had it roasted on a grate, something that would never have been done with a genuine *korban pesach*. This reading answers all of the difficulties simultaneously: the post-*churban* context of this Rabban Gamliel, the *mishna* in *Beitzah* attesting to his practice of *gedi mekulas*, the connection to the Todos Ish Romi narrative, and the purpose of the grate as a marker of difference.

This reading carries considerable weight beyond the immediate exegetical question. It speaks to the broader tension that existed after the *churban* around how much of a pseudo-*pesach* ritual should be preserved. We Ashkenazim tend to be deeply cautious — indeed, there are real questions about whether one may even eat pan-roasted chicken on *Pesach* night — yet the *gemara* makes clear that there were those who were *davka makpid* to eat fully roasted meat, and that the *minhag makom shenahagu* may have been precisely to do so. Here it was not merely the practice of Todos Ish Romi but arguably of someone as great as Rabban Gamliel himself. The *Rashash*'s reading is thus not only the better *pshat* of the *mishna* but illuminates an entire dimension of post-*churban* religious life.

A student raised the question of what exactly *chasuri mechasra vehachi katani* claims to accomplish — is it a genuine reconstruction of an alternative text, or something more modest? The answer is that it is a working assumption: given a problem, the *gemara* must conclude that something is contextually missing, without necessarily asserting that an alternative manuscript existed. Some authorities hold that even the "missing" material may refer not to actual absent words but to implicit context that was never spelled out. As for the *halachic* authority of a reading arrived at through *chasuri mechasra* — once the *gemara* adopts that reading, it carries the weight of the *bavli*'s canonization, the same weight as any other interpretation the *gemara* endorses.

## Orlah & Fire Debate

### Orlah Setup

The *gemara* continues with a new question. Rav Chanina bar Idi poses the following to Rava bar Avuh: if one heats an oven using the shells of *orlah* fruit — from which one may not derive *hana'ah* — the *halacha* is that bread baked in that oven while the fire is burning is forbidden. But what if one heats the oven with such shells, then *vegarfo* — sweeps out the oven, removing all the wood — and then bakes bread in the retained heat, without the actual prohibited wood being present? According to the authorities who forbid baking in the *orlah* fire, what is the rule when only derivative heat remains?

Rava bar Avuh answers directly: *hapat muteret* — the bread is permitted. Since the bread is not deriving benefit directly from the wood, but only from the secondary retained heat, it does not constitute forbidden *hana'ah*.

### Category Distinction

His interlocutor challenges this from a tradition in the name of Rav Chanina Saba, who cited Rabbi Yoshiyah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: if one heats an oven, sweeps it out, and then roasts a *korban pesach* in the retained heat — without any fire — *ein zeh tzli esh*, that does not constitute roasting by fire, as required by the *pasuk*, which uses the phrase *tzli esh* twice, teaching that it must literally be over an open fire. The implication is striking: *ta'ama degallei Rachmana tzli esh tzli esh shtei pe'amim* — the only reason retained heat is excluded is because the Torah repeated the phrase. Had it said *tzli esh* only once, retained heat would have sufficed, meaning that the ordinary definition of *tzli esh* would naturally encompass derivative heat. If so, derivative heat should similarly be considered as coming from the fire — and bread baked by it in an *orlah* oven should be forbidden.

### Direct Contact

The response to this challenge is twofold. The first answer: *gali Rachmana hatam v'yalfinan minah* — once the Torah teaches explicitly by the *pesach* that only direct fire counts, that principle becomes a paradigm applicable across *halachot*, and derivative heat carries no significance. The second and more analytically rigorous answer distinguishes the two categories entirely. By *pesach*, the *pasuk* says *tzli esh* twice because otherwise one would have thought that retained heat of a fire *is* fire-roasted — since the heat originates in fire. The Torah thus teaches that the heat must remain anchored to the actual fire. But by *orlah*, the operative question is entirely different: *a'atzim d'isura kapid Rachmana* — what the Torah prohibits is benefit derived from the forbidden *wood*. Once the wood is no longer present, one is at best receiving benefit at two removes — the heat came from the wood, and the bread is benefiting from the heat. The nature of forbidden *hana'ah* requires a direct relationship with the prohibited object; when the wood is absent, that directness is broken.

One might note that this is the most obvious of the distinctions: in one case the question concerns the *quality* of the cooking process, and in the other it concerns the *source* of prohibited benefit. These are simply different categories governed by different definitions.

### Fire Definition

With the *orlah* question resolved, the *gemara* transitions to a broader conceptual inquiry into what constitutes *tzli esh* as a general matter, leaving behind the issue of forbidden wood and moving toward a sustained analysis of the definition of fire itself.

## Tzli Esh Definition

### Rebbi's Coals

A *braisa* is introduced: *chatcho u'netano al gabei gechalim* — if one cut up the *korban pesach* and placed it directly on coals without an exposed flame. Before addressing the central question, it is worth noting that *Tosafot* infers from this and other *gemarot* that *bedi'avad*, even if the *pesach* was not roasted whole, it remains *kasher* and may be eaten. *Rashi*, by contrast, reads "cut" not as actual dismemberment but as partial cuts made to lay the meat flat, without fully separating the pieces. Either way, the *halacha* regarding the requirement of wholeness is that it applies *lechatchila*, not as an absolute invalidating condition.

Returning to the main question: *Rebbi omer, omer ani shezeh tzli eish* — Rebbi declares that roasting over coals, even without an exposed flame, constitutes *tzli esh*. No other authority explicitly disagrees in this *braisa*, but Rebbi's position is stated prominently enough to invite challenge.

### Tzara'as Challenge

Rav Acha bar Ami raises a contradiction to Rav Chisda: does Rebbi really maintain that coals are considered fire? A *braisa* treating the laws of *tzara'as* is cited. The *pasuk* speaks of a *michvat eish*, a burn caused by fire, and the *braisa* asks: what if the skin was burned not by a flame but by a coal (*gachelet*), by hot ashes (*remetz*), by boiling plaster (*sid roteach*), by hot tiles (*gafsit rotachat*), or by anything else that derives its heat from fire, including water heated by a fire underground? How do we know these are included in the category of *michvat eish*? The answer is that the Torah uses the word *michvah* twice, *michvah michvah ribah*, the repetition serving as an inclusion of cases beyond direct flame.

The implication is pointed: *ta'ama di-ribei Rachmana michvah michvah* — the only reason a coal-burn qualifies as *michvat eish* is the doubled word. *Ha lo ribei Rachmana michvah michvah, gechalim lav eish ninhu* — without that doubling, a coal would not be considered fire. This suggests that the baseline definition of fire requires an exposed flame, not merely glowing coals. If so, how can Rebbi say that coals constitute *tzli esh* for the *korban pesach*?

### Wood vs Metal

Rav Chisda responds: *gachelet shel etz lo itzterich lei ribuya* — when the *braisa* needed a *pasuk* to include a coal-burn under *michvat eish*, it was not speaking about a coal derived from burning wood. A wood coal is clearly fire, even without an exposed flame, and requires no special derivation. *Ki itzterich kra le-gachelet shel matechet* — the inclusion was needed only for a piece of burning-hot metal, which is categorically different.

Why is heated metal different from a wood coal? Two reasons present themselves. First, a burning piece of wood can in principle be fanned into an exposed flame; its coal-state is simply a diminished stage of fire. Metal, by contrast, can never spontaneously generate a flame. Even when metal glows incandescently — and this is directly relevant to incandescent electric lighting, which is essentially *gachelet shel matechet*, a glowing metal filament — it does not produce a flame in the sense that wood does. Second, a related consideration in *hilchot Shabbat* is that the act of *kibui*, extinguishing, is defined partly by whether what remains after the fire is extinguished constitutes a coal — a transformed residue of the burning process. Wood becomes coal; metal that cools simply returns to inert metal.

This *sugya* bears directly on the question of whether turning on an incandescent light on *Shabbat* is a *de-oraita* violation and whether turning it off constitutes a *de-oraita* act of *kibui*. Some authorities, in order to underscore that incandescent lights are genuinely *eish*, deliberately used them for *havdalah* and recited *borei meorei ha-eish* over them. The question of *kibui* remains genuinely complex, as the metal filament does not produce a coal when extinguished. Fluorescent lights, which function through excited molecules rather than a glowing filament, are generally not considered *de-oraita* by anyone. For any practical ruling on these matters, one should consult a *posek*.

Returning to the *sugya*: the answer is satisfying. Rebbi's position that wood coals constitute *tzli esh* for the *korban pesach* stands unchallenged, since the *tzara'as braisa*'s derivation concerned only the anomalous case of heated metal, not ordinary wood coals.

### Bat Kohen Case

The *gemara* now pivots in the opposite direction, raising a case that would seem to push the definition of fire *beyond* even what Rebbi claims. The *pasuk* in the Torah states that a *bat kohen* who commits adultery is put to death *ba-esh tisaref* — she shall be burned in fire. Rav Masna explains: *psila shel avar hayu osim lah* — a strip of lead was heated until molten and poured down her throat. This, then, is the method of *sereifa* — the court-imposed death by fire. Yet molten metal is clearly not fire itself; it is only heated by fire. If so, it would seem that even metal heated by fire is included in the category of *eish*, going further even than Rebbi's position on wood coals.

The *gemara* responds: *shani hatam* — that case is different. The *pasuk* states *ba-esh tisaref*, with *ba-esh* preceding *tisaref*. The word *tisaref* appearing last is the operative term: *lerabot kol srefot haba'ot min ha-eish* — it includes any burning that derives from fire, even if not fire itself. It is the word *tisaref*, not *ba-esh*, that does the work. *Ve-kol she-ken eish atzmah* — actual fire itself certainly suffices, but that is not the limiting principle.

One might then ask: if anything that derives from fire counts for *sereifa*, why not simply surround the condemned with bundles of wood and burn her on a pyre? The *gemara* answers with a *gezeirah shavah* of *sereifa-sereifa* from the death of the sons of Aharon: just as their death involved *srefat neshama ve-guf kayam* — the soul destroyed but the body left intact — so too the court's *sereifa* must leave the body intact. Only the inner burning, the life, is extinguished; the body is not consumed. This explains why actual fire is excluded and why molten lead poured into the body serves as the method.

It is worth pausing on this principle. Contemporary scholarship has debated the *halachic* and philosophical basis for *Chazal*'s insistence on this mode of execution. Yair Loberbaum, in his work *Tzelem Elokim*, argues that *Chazal* treated the physical body as bearing the image of God and therefore prohibited its mutilation. Dvorah Steinmetz, in what may be a more persuasive argument based on a consistent reading of the *arba misos beis din* in *Sanhedrin*, argues differently: the court derives its license to take human life precisely from its role as the arm of divine justice, and therefore the execution must most closely approximate a natural death — one in which the soul departs while the body remains. The mutilation of a body by a pyre would be inconsistent with this framework. Whichever reading one finds more compelling, both agree that the practical requirement is a death in which *neshama nisrefet ve-guf kayam*.

### Rav Nachman

Given the constraint that the body must remain intact, why use molten lead specifically rather than, say, boiling water or another heated liquid poured down the throat? The *gemara* answers: *mishum de-Rav Nachman* — because of the principle articulated by Rav Nachman. *D'amar Rav Nachman amar kra, ve-ahavta l'rei-acha kamocha* — the *pasuk* commands love of one's fellow as oneself — *beror lo misa yafa*, choose for him a pleasant death. Even at the moment of execution, the obligation of love toward one's fellow requires choosing the most merciful, quickest means available. Molten lead, however horrific it sounds, presumably causes nearly instantaneous death. Boiling water, by contrast, would not.

But the *gemara* then asks: if the principle of *beror lo misa yafa* already suffices to mandate the quickest method, why do we need the *gezeirah shavah* from *bnei Aharon* at all? The answer: without the *gezeirah shavah*, one would never have known that pouring molten lead constitutes *sereifa*. One might have thought that *ba-esh tisaref* required actual flame, and that to make it as quick and merciful as possible, one should simply make a very large pyre — as in the harrowing account of Rabbi Chananya ben Teradyon, who was wrapped in a Torah scroll, surrounded by bundles of wood (*chavilei zemoros*), and ignited by the Romans. The *gezeirah shavah* teaches that such a method is not the required — or permitted — form of court-imposed *sereifa*, and once we know that molten lead qualifies, *beror lo misa yafa* directs us to that method as the most merciful.

### Parim Restriction

The *gemara* now challenges from the opposite direction. The *pasuk* regarding the *parim hanisrafim* — the cows that are burned outside the *Beis HaMikdash* when their blood has been sprinkled within the *Kodesh Kodashim* — states *u-srafo oto al ha-etzim ba-esh*. A *braisa* teaches: *ba-esh ve-lo be-sid ve-votach* — this burning must be with actual fire, not with boiling plaster, hot tiles, or other materials heated by fire. So here, unlike the case of the *bat kohen*, derivative heat is excluded. How does one account for the asymmetry?

The *gemara* answers with a close reading of word order. By the *bat kohen*, the *pasuk* reads *ba-esh tisaref* — *ba-esh* first, *tisaref* last. The final word determines the operative category: *tisaref*, burning in any form, is what matters, and the word *ba-esh* merely indicates the source. By the *parim hanisrafim*, the *pasuk* reads *u-srafo oto al ha-etzim ba-esh* — the word *ba-esh* appears last. *U-le-vasof esh* — fire is the final and defining word, *de-esh in midai achrinei lo* — only fire, not derivative heat. This hermeneutical principle — that the final term in a *pasuk* carries definitional weight — is striking, though as *Tosafot* notes, one might ask why standard principles such as *klal u-frat u-klal* are not applied instead. The underlying logic, however, is intuitive: what is stated last receives the emphasis.

The *gemara* then anticipates a further question: another *pasuk* by the *parim hanisrafim* states *al shefech ha-deshen yisaref*, ending with *yisaref*, a form of *sereifa* — should this not then include derivative heat? The *gemara* responds: *hahu yisaref mibaei lei le-khid-tanya* — that second *yisaref* serves an entirely different function. A *braisa* derives two teachings from it: first, that the burning is performed even if there are no ashes currently at the designated location outside the Temple grounds; and second, *yisaref af al pi she-hitzisu or be-rubo* — even once the majority of the animal has caught fire, the obligation to actively attend to the burning continues. One may not simply walk away and allow it to finish on its own.

The overall structure of this extended *sugya* can now be summarized. Rebbi holds that wood coals constitute *tzli esh* for the *korban pesach*. The *tzara'as* case, which seemed to require an exposed flame, in fact speaks only of heated metal, not wood coals. The case of the *bat kohen*, which seemed to include even heated metal in the *eish* category, does so only through the word *tisaref*, not *ba-esh* — so it does not reflect the ordinary definition of fire. The *parim hanisrafim* requires literal fire because *ba-esh* is the final word of the relevant *pasuk*. The case of *tzara'as* requires real fire — or, at minimum, the doubling of *michvah* is needed to include derivate heat. Rebbi's position — that wood coals constitute *tzli esh* for the *korban pesach* — thus remains intact.

## Gachlei Esh

### Ravina Reorder

As a further defense of Rebbi's position, Ravina offers an alternative reading of the *tzara'as braisa* itself. Rather than saying the *braisa* was discussing heated metal, Ravina proposes *kroch u-teni* — rewrap the text, meaning reorganize the categories. In his reading, the opening clause — "I know only that one burned by fire is included" — should encompass both a flame and a coal, since a wood coal is obviously fire. The doubled *michvah* is then needed only to extend the category to the further cases: hot ashes, boiling plaster, hot tiles, and water heated by underground fire. This reading places the coal firmly in the baseline fire category without any need for a special derivation, directly supporting Rebbi. While *kroch u-teni* does not add entirely new words as *chasuri mechasra* does, it does reorganize the structure of the *braisa* — it is a recategorization rather than a wholesale reconstruction.

### Whisper Coals

Rava now raises a fresh challenge. *Mi amar Rebbe gechalim ikru eish* — did Rebbi really say that coals are fire? A *midrash halacha* is cited dealing with the *kohen*'s taking of *gechalei eish* from the outer *mizbeach* on *Yom Kippur*. This is the central act of the *Yom Kippur avoda* involving the *ketores*: the *kohen* takes fire-coals (*gechalei eish*) from the outer altar into a fire pan, carries them into the *Kodesh Kodashim*, and deposits the *ketores* on top of them, filling the inner sanctum with incense smoke. The term *gechalei eish* occupies a precise intermediate space: it is neither pure *gechalim* — coals — nor pure *eish* — fire. What exactly does it require?

The *braisa* teaches: *yachol omemos* — perhaps even smoldering coals would suffice. *Talmud lomar eish* — the word *eish* teaches that the coals must be more than merely smoldering. *I eish, yachol shalheves* — if it had only said fire, perhaps an exposed flame would be required. *Talmud lomar gechalei* — the word *gechalei* teaches that it need not be a full flame. *Ha keitzad? Meivi min ha-lochashos* — the resolution: one brings from the *lochashos*, the whispering coals, the coals that still carry the living sound of fire within them.

### Braisa Logic

The *gemara* immediately notes an internal contradiction in this *braisa*. From the *reisha*: the possibility of smoldering coals sufficing was rejected by the word *eish*, implying that *lochashos* — one step above smoldering — are themselves considered *eish*. But from the *seifa*: the possibility of an exposed flame was rejected by the word *gechalei*, implying that even *lochashos* — which are below a flame — are not in the *eish* category. So which is it? Are whispering coals *eish* or not?

The *gemara*'s difficulty is that it assumes a strictly binary system: something is either a coal or a flame, with no intermediate category. The simple reading of the *braisa* — that whispering coals occupy a middle ground — is rejected as conceptually incoherent. The *gemara* therefore demands: in which of the two binary categories do whispering coals naturally belong?

### Rav Sheshet

Rav Sheshet reads the *braisa* as follows: *gechalei* — coals — might have been taken to include both smoldering and whispering coals. *Talmud lomar eish* — the word fire then tells you that whispering coals are *not* in the coal category. *I eish*, had it only said fire, one might have required an exposed flame. *Talmud lomar gechalei* — the word coals brings you back, teaching that even whispering coals — which are below an exposed flame — count. In this reading, whispering coals are situated within the *gechalim* category as their baseline classification; they are included in the composite term *gechalei eish* only because both words together bracket them in. *Eish* alone would have meant only a flame; *gechalei* alone would have meant only fully extinguished, smoldering coals. The combination yields: whispering coals.

The critical implication is that whispering coals are, on their own, not *eish*. They are coals. This directly contradicts Rebbi, who said that roasting on coals constitutes *tzli esh*. *Mi kol makom, gechalim lo ikrei eish* — in any event, coals are not called fire; even whispering coals gain inclusion here only through the dual formulation, not because they are independently in the fire category.

### Abaye Counter

Abaye offers the opposite reading. *Yachol omemos velo lochashos* — the initial supposition was that "coals" meant only smoldering coals, not whispering coals — because whispering coals, being closer to an active fire, would naturally fall in the *eish* category. *Talmud lomar eish* — the word fire confirms: yes, even whispering coals are included. *I eish, yachol ratza shalheves yavi ratza gacheles yavi* — had the text only said fire, one might have thought there was a choice: bring either an exposed flame or whispering coals. *Talmud lomar gechalei* — the word coals tells you there is no such choice; it must be coals, not a free-burning flame.

In this reading, whispering coals are naturally in the *eish* category, and the word *gechalei* serves not to include them but to exclude the large exposed flame. This is consistent with Rebbi's position that coals — at least whispering coals — are *eish*. The *braisa*'s simple meaning, that the whispering coals occupy the intermediate space between two poles and are drawn in by the combination of both words, is thus what Abaye is also essentially recovering, though framing it in the binary terms the *gemara* demands.

### Rava Final

Rava then raises a difficulty against Abaye's reading. If the word *eish* alone would have permitted bringing either a flame or whispering coals at one's choice, that implies a flame could come *without* a coal. But *shalhevet belo gechelet heichi mashkachat lah* — how would one find a flame without any coal attached to it? Any flame burning from wood necessarily has coals.

The *gemara* answers: one might have taken a vessel, smeared it with oil, and lit it, producing a flame with no coal. Rava then dismisses even the need for a *pasuk* to exclude this: *hahu lav orach ara* — that method is simply not appropriate. Before an earthly king one would never carry a burning oil vessel to burn incense; *kol she-ken* before the King of Kings it would be unthinkable. No *pasuk* would be necessary to exclude it.

Rava therefore reformulates the reading: *gechalei* — coals — might have meant only smoldering coals, not whispering coals. *Talmud lomar eish* — fire tells you that whispering coals are included. *I eish, yachol yavi mechtza gechalim u-mechtza shalhevet* — had it only said fire, one might have thought one could bring something half flame and half coal, meaning coals with a very large and active exposed flame rather than quietly glowing coals. *Talmud lomar gechalei* — coals: it must be a coal-dominated form, not a blazing fire.

But if one might bring something with a large flame, does that not create the problem of appearing disrespectful before the King? The *gemara* resolves this: *de'aylei legavai havei kulei gechelet* — by the time one carries the coals from the outer altar into the *Kodesh Kodashim*, what began as a larger flame will have diminished to coals. One might therefore have supposed that the act of *taking* the coals from the altar (*lekicha*) could be with a larger flame, even if it subsides by the time of entry. *Talmud lomar, v'lakach melo ha-machta gechalei eish me'al ha-mizbeach* — the *pasuk* says that even at the moment of taking, it must already be *gechalei eish*, coals, not a large flame.

The bottom line of the entire *sugya*: Rebbi holds that at least whispering coals are *eish*, and roasting on such coals constitutes *tzli esh* for the *korban pesach*. Rava's final construction defends this position. Heated metal falls outside even Rebbi's definition of fire, though the *bat kohen* case extends further through a different *pasuk*. And the *yom kippur* ritual of *gechalei eish* — the very coals that the *kohen* would take from the outer *mizbeach* in the fire pan, carrying them while they were neither smoldering nor blazing but *lochashos*, whispering — illustrates precisely the category Rebbi has in mind.

## Pottery/Flour

### Oven Wall

The *gemara* now moves to the next *mishna*, which addresses practical problems that arise when roasting the *korban pesach* in an oven. One should picture not an open-air spit over a barbecue, but rather an oven with the fire burning inside and the *pesach* suspended directly over the fire within it — a more effective method, since the oven contains and concentrates the heat. The *Yerushalmi* does ask why this is permissible, since part of the cooking effect comes from the retained heat of the oven walls rather than the direct flame. The answer is that the oven walls contain but do not generate heat; they are an extension of the fire, not an independent source.

A problem nonetheless arises. As the *pesach* roasts, it may accidentally touch the pottery wall of the oven. That spot on the meat has now been roasted by the hot pottery — derivative heat — rather than directly by the fire. The ruling is: *yiklof es mekomo* — peel away the area of contact. That portion is not considered *tzli esh* and must be removed.

### Gravy Bounce

A further case: *nataf me-rutvo al ha-cheres ve-chazar alav* — drippings from the roasting meat fall onto the pottery wall or floor of the oven and then bounce back, being reabsorbed into the meat. Those juices were roasted by the pottery, not directly by the fire, and are therefore invalid. Since they have now been absorbed into the meat, peeling the outer surface is insufficient; the absorbed material must be extracted. The ruling is *yitol es mekomo* — one must dig in and remove the affected area where the juice was absorbed, not merely peel the surface.

### Flour Removal

A further complication: *nataf me-rutvo al ha-solet* — the drippings fall onto flour placed at the base of the oven. The flour, sitting in a hot oven, is itself extremely hot. When the juices hit the flour, they are roasted by the heat of the flour rather than directly by the fire. Those juices are therefore invalid, having not been subjected to *tzli esh* directly. Since one presumably wishes to eat the flour, the ruling is *yikmotz et mekomo* — one must remove a *kmitza*, a hand-grasp, of the flour at the point of contact, ensuring that none of the flour containing those juices is eaten.

### Pre-Roast Oil

Moving to a different type of problem: *sacho beshemen terumah* — one smears the *pesach* with *terumah* oil before or after roasting. Before addressing the post-roasting case, the *mishna* first treats the pre-roasting scenario. If the oil was applied while the meat was still raw, *yidichenu* — it may simply be rinsed off, since the oil has only adhered to the surface and has not been absorbed into cold raw meat. After rinsing, the group may eat it. If this is a group of *kohanim*, even without rinsing they may eat it, since they are permitted to eat *terumah*.

### Post-Roast Oil

If the oil was applied after the meat was already roasted — *im tzeli hu* — the situation is more serious. Roasted meat is absorbent, and even cold *terumah* oil applied to the surface is presumed to have been absorbed into the outer layer. *Yiklef et ha-chitzon* — one must peel off the outer layer, since that is where the oil is presumed to have penetrated. Deeper absorption is not assumed, so peeling the surface layer suffices.

### Maaser Sheni

The final case of the *mishna* shifts to a different *issur*. If one used *maaser sheni* oil rather than *terumah* oil — *sacho beshemen maaser sheni* — the group may eat the *pesach* with the absorbed oil, since *maaser sheni* is not forbidden to non-*kohanim* in the way *terumah* is; it must simply be consumed in Jerusalem. However, *lo yaasenu damim livnei chabura* — one may not charge the other members of the *chavura* for the oil, because *she'ein mochrin maaser sheni bi-Yerushalayim* — one does not sell or redeem *maaser sheni* in Jerusalem. The particulars of this prohibition will be addressed when the *gemara* treats that topic directly.

This final group of cases in the *mishna* thus represents the intersection of two bodies of law: the requirements of *tzli esh* and the proper roasting of the *korban pesach* on one side, and the laws of *kashrut* and *terumah* regarding absorption and the peeling of surfaces on the other. The practical question underlying each case is how deeply a foreign substance penetrates and how much of the meat must be removed to ensure that what is eaten has been properly subjected to *tzli esh* and contains nothing forbidden.
