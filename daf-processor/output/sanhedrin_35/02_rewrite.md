# Sanhedrin 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yerushalmi Overview

### Monetary vs Capital

The *mishnah* that opens our discussion of daf 35 is drawn from *perek* 4, *halacha* 6 of the Yerushalmi: monetary cases (*dinei mamonot*) may be concluded on the same day they begin, whether for acquittal or conviction, whereas capital cases (*dinei nefashot*) may be concluded on the same day only for acquittal — a guilty verdict requires sleeping on the judgment and carrying it to completion the following day. This rule of *halanat ha-din* — the overnight sleep on judgment — generates the corollary prohibition: we do not begin capital cases on *erev Shabbat* or *erev Yom Tov*, since the case cannot be concluded on Shabbat or Yom Tov. The question of why one cannot simply recess for Shabbat and resume on Sunday is one the Gemara will grapple with at length. For now, the Yerushalmi proceeds to examine several related questions.

### Erev Shabbat Restriction

Before returning to the central question, the Yerushalmi introduces a teaching in the name of Rabbi Chizkiyahu that even monetary cases should not be judged on *erev Shabbat*, presumably because the proceedings would interfere with preparations for Shabbat. The *mishnah* itself appears to contradict this, since it singles out only capital cases for the *erev Shabbat* restriction, implying that monetary cases may proceed. This is confirmed by a *braita* of Rabbi Chiya: monetary cases may be held on *erev Shabbat*; capital cases may not. The Yerushalmi resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between *lehalacha* and *lema'aseh* — theoretically, the law permits monetary cases on *erev Shabbat*, but in practice we refrain so as not to compromise Shabbat preparation. *Bediavad*, if proceedings were held, the ruling would stand.

### Witness Restrictions

The Yerushalmi then turns to a *tannaitic* teaching: a witness in a capital case may neither argue for acquittal nor for conviction — *ha-ed eino melamed lo zechut velo chovah*. The scriptural basis is the verse *ve-ed lo ya'aneh be-nefesh la-mut* — a witness shall not testify against a soul to put it to death — which is read expansively: the witness has no standing to argue in any direction in a capital matter.

The Gemara then asks: where do we learn that he may not argue even in the defendant's *favor*? The word *ve-hu* in the verse is taken as extra, and is read to mean that the witness has no voice — *lo ya'aneh* — in any matter of life and death, whether for conviction or acquittal.

### Ed Zomem Concern

Resh Lakish adds a striking explanation for why a witness is prohibited from arguing in the defendant's *favor* as well: *damin she-adam ro'eh et atzmo mizdamen* — sometimes a witness sees himself becoming vulnerable to the penalty of *ed zomem* — the law under which a witness who is proven to have testified falsely receives the punishment he sought to impose on the defendant. If the witness argues successfully that the defendant is innocent, there will never be a verdict against the defendant, and therefore the witness can never be found to have testified falsely about something that resulted in conviction. He thus has a personal stake — a *nogea be-edut* — in arguing for the defendant's innocence. This double self-interest, on both sides of the argument, is what renders the witness entirely ineligible to argue in either direction.

### Two Days Required

The Yerushalmi returns to the central puzzle: how do we know that the two days of deliberation must be *smuchin* — contiguous — and that there can be no recess, say, over Shabbat? Before the Yerushalmi supplies its answer, it digresses into a remarkable debate about whether capital punishment could, in principle, be carried out on Shabbat. This is introduced in the context of asking: why not judge on Friday, sleep Shabbat, and finalize the verdict on Sunday? The implicit premise is that there must be some reason the trial cannot be interrupted, and the Yerushalmi explores this by examining the feasibility of various scheduling alternatives.

One possibility would be to judge on Friday, finalize on Shabbat, and execute on *motzaei Shabbat*. But the Gemara dismisses this: once a verdict of guilt has been rendered, delaying the execution creates *inui ha-din* — the anguish of a suspended death sentence — which is prohibited. The judgment is finalized on Shabbat, but the execution must wait until Saturday night, and in the interim the convicted person suffers under the weight of the verdict. This was also raised earlier in the Yerushalmi as the reason you cannot finalize on Shabbat and execute on Sunday.

### Rishil Lakish QVH

It is at this juncture that Resh Lakish poses his *kal va-chomer*: if *avodah* — the Temple service — overrides Shabbat, and if executing a condemned criminal overrides *avodah* (as we know from the verse *me-im mizbachi tikachenu la-mut*, which requires removing even a *kohen* from the altar to execute him), then by transitivity, execution should override Shabbat as well. The argument follows the familiar structure: *avodah* is greater than Shabbat; execution is greater than *avodah*; therefore execution should be greater than Shabbat. The very language the Yerushalmi uses for court-ordered execution — *retzichat mitzvah* — is striking; ordinarily *retzicha* denotes forbidden murder, and the rabbis' discomfort with the concept seems almost to surface in the choice of terminology.

### Fire Prohibition

Rabbi Ila, in the name of Rabbi Yannai, refutes the *kal va-chomer* with a *gezeirah shava*. The verse *lo tiv'aru eish be-chol moshvoteichem* — do not kindle fire in all your dwelling places — is placed in proximity to a second verse that uses the same phrase *be-chol moshvoteichem* in the context of capital adjudication: *ve-hayu eileh lachem le-chukat mishpat le-doroteichem be-chol moshvoteichem*, referring to the laws governing the execution of murderers. Just as the latter *moshvoteichem* speaks of a *beit din*, so too the former: when the Torah prohibits fire on Shabbat, it is also addressing the *beit din* specifically — prohibiting the execution of *sereifah* on Shabbat. The verse is explicit, and the explicit prohibition overrides any *kal va-chomer* to the contrary. Thus, even though one might construct a logical argument for executing on Shabbat, the Torah itself forecloses it.

The Yerushalmi, having established that execution is prohibited on Shabbat, leaves the original question — why not recess for Shabbat during deliberations — largely without a full answer, simply assuming that such a recess is not permissible. It is the Bavli that will engage this question directly.

## Bavli: Day Requirement

### Daytime Only

The Bavli, picking up at the very bottom of 34b, establishes that capital cases must be judged and concluded exclusively in the daytime — *dinei nefashot danin ba-yom*. The question is asked: *mena hanei millei* — what is the source for this? We already know that monetary cases must begin in the daytime but may conclude at night. For capital cases, even the *gemar din* — the final verdict — must occur by day.

### Neged HaShemesh

Rav Shimi bar Chiyya derives this from the verse *ve-hoka otam la-Hashem neged ha-shemesh* — hang them before God opposite the sun — spoken in the context of those who sinned at *Baal Peor*. The *hoka'ah*, the suspension of the condemned, is the *gemar din* of a capital case, and it takes place *neged ha-shemesh*, in daylight. Thus both the beginning of the trial and its conclusion must occur during daytime hours.

### Hoka'ah Definition

Rav Chisda asks: how do we know that *hoka'ah* means suspension — hanging — as a post-mortem form of display following execution? The proof is from the episode involving the *Givonim* during a famine in the time of David, when seven of Shaul's descendants were *mukat*, suspended at Giv'at Shaul. The verse records that Ritzpah bat Ayah spread sackcloth at the rock from the beginning of the harvest — indicating that the bodies were left exposed, publicly displayed, which is the essence of *hoka'ah* as suspension and exposure.

### Moshe's Command

Having introduced the verses, the Gemara proceeds to interpret them. The verse in *Bamidbar* seems to say that God commanded Moshe to take all the heads of the people and hang them before God — yet Moshe then instructed the leaders to judge the people, not to be hanged themselves. How are we to reconcile this?

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav explains: God told Moshe *chalok lahem batei dinim* — divide them into many courts. The leaders were not to be executed; they were to serve as judges and themselves carry out the verdict of *hoka'ah* against the guilty parties. The verse is thus a command to organize a judicial process, not a death sentence against the leadership.

### Multiple Courts

One might ask: why the need for multiple courts? A single large court could hear all the cases. One might suggest it is because we do not judge two separate capital cases on the same day, but Rav Chisda clarifies: that restriction applies only when the two cases involve two distinct modes of death and distinct crimes. When the crime is identical — as with the mass sin at *Baal Peor* — multiple defendants carrying the same punishment may be heard on the same day. The reason for multiple courts, then, is not a procedural limitation but a spiritual one: as the end of the verse indicates, this was to help turn away God's anger from Israel. The sight of the entire leadership mobilizing to establish order and defend God's honor would itself serve to abate the divine wrath.

### Tzedek Yalin

With the principle established that the *gemar din* must occur by day, the Gemara turns to the rule that monetary cases may conclude on the same day while capital cases — when the verdict is guilty — require *halanat ha-din*, sleeping on the judgment. Rabbi Chanina derives this from the verse in Yeshayahu: *meleti mishpat, tzedek yalin bah, ve-ata meratzchim* — it was once filled with justice; righteousness slept in the city; and now there are murderers. The *drasha* reads this as a causal chain: it was precisely because the judges of old would let the *tzedek* — the righteous judgment — *yalin*, sleep overnight, that their court was filled with justice. Now that judges render and execute judgment on the same day, they have become like murderers, since they deprive the defendant of the time that due process demands.

### Chametz Drasha

Rava offers an alternative source: *ashru chamotz* — which can be read as "praise the judge who allows his judgment to become like *chametz*" — who lets it leaven, percolate, ferment. The imagery is of a judgment that is not rushed but given time to develop. This *drasha* also teaches the virtue of *halanat ha-din*, though it operates as praise for best practice rather than as a condemnation of those who fail to do it, and is accordingly less forceful than Rabbi Chanina's reading.

The question arises: what does Rava do with Rabbi Chanina's verse, *meleti mishpat*? And conversely, what does Rabbi Chanina do with *ashru chamotz*? Rabbi Chanina reads *ashru chamotz* according to Tosafot's explanation as a directive to hear the case of the oppressed person first — if one litigant suffered personal injury at the hands of another, who then counter-claims that the injured party owes him money, the court hears the injury claim first, because *ashru*, defend, the one who is *chamotz*, the one being squeezed and oppressed, before addressing the counter-claim.

### Tzedakah Sleeping

As for the verse *meleti mishpat*, Rava applies it via the teaching of Rabbi Elazar citing Rabbi Yitzchak: any fast day on which the *tzedaka* that was collected is allowed to sleep overnight — *malinin bah et ha-tzedaka* — is as if one shed blood, *ke-ilu shofech damim*. Rashi explains: fast days would begin in the evening, and during the preceding night people would eat and give *tzedaka* in the form of food — bread and dates — to the poor. If that food was collected but not distributed until the following morning, the poor went hungry through the night, and this delay is considered tantamount to murder by neglect. The *tzedek* — the just allocation — must not *yalin bah*, must not sleep over; if it does, the community has become like *meratzchim*.

The Gemara qualifies this: the prohibition on allowing *tzedaka* to sleep over applies specifically to things like bread and dates, which can be eaten immediately and provide immediate sustenance. But if the collection consisted of coins, wheat, or barley — items that require further processing before becoming edible — then allowing them to sit overnight is not as grave a concern, and the principle does not apply with the same force.

## No Recess in Trial

### Recess Question

The Bavli now directly addresses the question left hanging by the Yerushalmi: why not begin the capital trial on Friday, recess for Shabbat, and resume and conclude on Sunday? This appears to sidestep all the problems, since no verdict would be rendered on Shabbat. The Gemara works through the possibilities systematically.

### Halnat Din

The first possibility: begin on Friday and conclude on Friday. This is permissible if the verdict is acquittal. But if the judges are inclined toward a guilty verdict, they are required to perform *halanat ha-din* — to sleep on it — and may only finalize the conviction the following day. Thus a guilty verdict cannot be concluded on Friday; they must wait until the next day. Already this blocks the simplest resolution.

### No Execute Shabbat

The second possibility: begin on Friday, conclude on Shabbat — that is, finalize the guilty verdict on Shabbat — and execute immediately. But *ein mitah docha Shabbat*: execution does not override Shabbat. The Gemara here takes this as established; the detailed argumentation will follow momentarily.

### Inuy HaDin

The third and fourth possibilities run together: conclude the verdict on Shabbat and execute Saturday night, or conclude the verdict on Shabbat and execute Sunday morning. Both are rejected. The verse *neged ha-shemesh* requires both the *gemar din* and the execution to occur in the daytime, so Saturday night is excluded. And once a verdict of guilt has been rendered, any delay in carrying out the execution constitutes *inui ha-din* — anguishing the convicted person with the weight of his sentence. Having found him guilty on Shabbat, allowing him to linger under that verdict until Sunday morning is prohibited, since the execution must follow the verdict as promptly as possible once guilt is established. The Gemara draws a sharp contrast with modern legal systems where appeals can stretch for years; the Gemara's framework is that once guilt is determined, swift execution spares the condemned person additional suffering.

### Liba D'Inshi

Finally — the question all of this has been building toward. Why not simply begin the trial on Friday, recess for Shabbat, and resume on Sunday? The two days would be separated by only one day of rest; the judges would have a written court record to consult, since two court scribes sit before the *beit din* and record everything — every argument for acquittal and every argument for conviction.

The answer is profound: *nehi d'be-fuma katvinan, liba d'inshi inshi* — granted that we write down what was said aloud, but we cannot write down what was in the judges' hearts. There is a wordplay here: *liba d'inshi*, the heart of people, *inshi inshi* — forgets. We record words, not thoughts. During deliberation, a judge may be holding a subtle line of reasoning, a feeling of doubt, a nuanced consideration that tips toward acquittal — none of that appears in the written record. A recess, even one day, risks the loss of precisely those unspoken considerations. Since those lost thoughts are most likely to benefit the defendant — the inclination toward mercy is more fragile than the inclination toward conviction — a recess poses a genuine threat to the defendant's interests, and is therefore prohibited.

### Cannot Start Erev

The conclusion is stark: *hilkach lo efshar* — it is simply impossible to begin a capital case on *erev Shabbat*. Every scheduling option has been explored and every one is blocked. The trial cannot be interrupted; it cannot resume after Shabbat; and the verdict cannot be finalized on Shabbat itself. There is no viable path, and therefore capital cases may not commence on Friday.

## QVH: Execute on Shab (II)

### Met Mitzvah QVH

The Gemara now turns to the underlying premise that has been assumed throughout: that execution does not override Shabbat. Resh Lakish challenges Rabbi Yochanan: why not argue — through a *kal va-chomer* — that burying a *meit mitzvah* (an unattended corpse for whom no one else will perform the burial) should override Shabbat? If the *kal va-chomer* succeeds, then perhaps execution should override Shabbat as well.

### Kal Vachomer Structure

The structure of the argument: *avodah* overrides Shabbat — that is, the Temple service continues on Shabbat without interruption. And *meit mitzvah* overrides *avodah* — burying an unattended corpse takes precedence even over the Temple service. Therefore, by transitivity, *meit mitzvah* should override Shabbat.

### Nazir Tamei

The basis for the claim that *meit mitzvah* overrides *avodah* is derived from the laws of a *nazir*. Like a *kohen gadol*, a *nazir* does not become *tamei* for immediate family members. Yet the Torah specifies each family member explicitly: father, mother, brother, sister. The question is why all of them needed to be listed.

### Vachoso Drasha

The *drasha* from the word *va-achoso* teaches: each extra family member listed is there to convey that although the *nazir* cannot become *tamei* for that relative, he *can* and *must* become *tamei* for a *meit mitzvah*. Even if doing so would violate his *nezirut*, his *kedusha* as a *kohen*, his added sanctity as a *kohen gadol*, all of these yield to the imperative of burying an unattended corpse.

### Pesach & Brit

The *braita* spells this out in the most extreme terms. Suppose this *nazir kohen gadol* was on his way to slaughter his *korban Pesach* or to perform a *brit milah* for his son — the two positive commandments whose neglect incurs *kareit*, the two most weighty *mitzvot* one could be engaged in at that moment. If he then heard that a relative had died and that the relative was a *meit mitzvah*, one might think all his other obligations should take precedence. The *talmud lomar* from the extra word *va-achoso* teaches otherwise: for his sister — a relative — he does not become *tamei*. But for a *meit mitzvah* he does, even at the cost of violating his *nezirut*, his *kehuna*, and forgoing both the *korban Pesach* and the *brit milah*. This conclusively establishes that *meit mitzvah* overrides *avodah*.

With that premise in hand, the full *kal va-chomer* holds: *meit mitzvah* is greater than *avodah*, *avodah* is greater than Shabbat, therefore *meit mitzvah* should be greater than Shabbat. Resh Lakish's question stands.

---

## Shabbat Prohibition

### Exec vs Avodah

Rabbi Yochanan responds with a counter-proof: *ritzicha tochiah* — execution itself demonstrates the limit of the *kal va-chomer*. Execution overrides *avodah* — a *kohen* is taken from the altar to be executed, even if he is the only available *kohen* and the *avodah* will thereby cease — and yet execution does not override Shabbat. Here is a case of something that is weightier than *avodah* and yet does not override Shabbat. Therefore, being weightier than *avodah* does not automatically confer the power to override Shabbat. The same logic applies to *meit mitzvah*: it may override *avodah* and still not override Shabbat.

## QVH: Execute on Shab (II)

### Ritzicha QVH

Resh Lakish is not satisfied. He escalates: if the counter-proof is execution itself, then let us apply the original *kal va-chomer* directly to execution. *Ritzicha gufah tidcheh et ha-Shabbat mi-kal va-chomer* — let execution itself override Shabbat via the same *kal va-chomer*. *Avodah* overrides Shabbat; execution overrides *avodah*, as established from the verse *me-im mizbachi tikachenu la-mut*; therefore execution should override Shabbat. The counter-proof collapses into the very argument it was meant to rebut.

## Shabbat Prohibition (II)

### Ritzicha Refutes

Rabbi Yochanan's response — that execution itself refutes the *kal va-chomer* by demonstrating that overriding *avodah* does not entail overriding Shabbat — is now supplanted by a more substantive answer. Rava invokes a *tannaitic* tradition from the school of Rabbi Yishmael that resolves the question through an explicit verse.

### Lo Tiv'aru Eish

Rava states: *kvar paska tana d'bei Rabi Yishmael* — the *tana* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael already cut through this with a verse. The verse is *lo tiv'aru eish be-chol moshvoteichem* — do not kindle fire in all your dwelling places — and from it we derive an explicit prohibition against execution on Shabbat.

### Beit Din Context

But the Gemara first addresses an obvious question: what is this verse actually needed for? The prohibition against making fire on Shabbat is already known. The Gemara cites the familiar debate: according to Rabbi Yosi, the verse of *hav'arah* — kindling — was stated separately to teach that it carries only a negative prohibition and not the death penalty; according to Rabbi Natan, it was stated to teach that each *melacha* is distinct, so that one who performs multiple *melachot* inadvertently (*be-shogeg*) on Shabbat must bring a separate *korban* for each.

But Rava says the question here is not about the *hav'arah* itself — it is about the word *moshvoteichem*. Why does the verse say *be-chol moshvoteichem* — in all your dwelling places? Shabbat is *chovat ha-guf*, a personal bodily obligation, and personal obligations apply everywhere, in *Eretz Yisrael* and in the diaspora alike. The word *moshvoteichem* is thus superfluous on its face, and must be carrying an additional teaching.

### Moshvoteichem

The answer is given in the name of a student of Rabbi Yishmael. Since the verse elsewhere says *ki yihyeh be-ish cheit mishpat mavet ve-humat* — if a man is guilty of a capital offense he shall be put to death — one might think this means on any day, including Shabbat. And the verse *me-challeleha mot yumat* — one who desecrates Shabbat shall be put to death — would seem to apply only to ordinary *melachot*, not to court-ordered execution, which might be considered exempt in the same way *avodah* is exempt. One might thus argue: just as the *avodah* continues on Shabbat, so too the *beit din* should execute on Shabbat.

To counter this, the Torah adds *moshvoteichem* in the fire verse. Since fire in the context of *beit din* can mean *sereifah* — the execution of burning — the verse is understood to be addressing *beit din* directly: do not perform the execution of burning on Shabbat, wherever you dwell. Or, on the other hand: you might think the obligation to execute — *ve-humat* — applies on Shabbat as well; and what do you do with the prohibition *me-challeleha mot yumat*? Perhaps that applies only to ordinary *melachot*, while *beit din* execution is like *avodah* and is exempt. The verse therefore must come to exclude *beit din* execution from Shabbat entirely.

### Gezeirah Shava

The formal mechanism is a *gezeirah shava*: the word *moshvot* appears in the fire verse — *be-chol moshvoteichem* — and also in the verse concerning the laws of capital judgment — *be-chol moshvoteichem*. The latter verse speaks explicitly of *beit din* and capital cases. The *gezeirah shava* links the two: just as *moshvoteichem* in the second verse refers to *beit din*, so too *moshvoteichem* in the fire verse refers to *beit din*. The prohibition against fire on Shabbat, in the context of *beit din*, means: do not execute — specifically, do not perform *sereifah* — on Shabbat.

### Explicit Prohibition

The upshot: even though a compelling *kal va-chomer* could be constructed — execution overrides *avodah*, *avodah* overrides Shabbat, therefore execution should override Shabbat — the explicit verse forecloses it. The Torah anticipated this *kal va-chomer* and supplied an explicit counter-ruling. Execution does not override Shabbat. This is the answer to Resh Lakish.

## Abaye QVH

### Inverted QVH

Abaye now introduces a new challenge, inverting the direction of the *kal va-chomer*. Having established that execution does not override Shabbat, Abaye proposes: *hashta d'amarta ein retzicha docha Shabbat, ein retzicha docha avodah mi-kal va-chomer* — now that we have established that execution does not override Shabbat, let us apply that to conclude that execution should not override *avodah* either.

### Verse Contradiction

The structure: Shabbat does not yield to execution; *avodah* is weightier than Shabbat; therefore *avodah* certainly should not yield to execution. But this contradicts the explicit verse *me-im mizbachi tikachenu la-mut*, which says that a *kohen* is taken from the altar to be executed — meaning execution *does* interfere with *avodah*. There is now a direct conflict between Abaye's *kal va-chomer* and the verse.

### Yom Tov QVH

Abaye's argument can also be formulated through *Yom Tov*: *Yom Tov* does not yield to execution — if we do not execute on Shabbat, we similarly do not execute on *Yom Tov*. A private *korban* (*korban yachid*) overrides *Yom Tov* — one may bring a *nedavah* or *neder* on *Yom Tov*. Therefore, by transitivity, a private *korban* is weightier than execution, and execution should not interfere even with a private *korban*. The verse *me-im mizbachi tikachenu la-mut* is thus left without application.

### Private Korban

One proposed answer: perhaps the verse *me-im mizbachi tikachenu la-mut* speaks only of a *korban yachid* — a private sacrifice — which, according to some opinions, cannot be brought on *Yom Tov*. If a private *korban* does not override *Yom Tov*, then the *kal va-chomer* that *Yom Tov* does not yield to execution breaks down, since *korban yachid* is not demonstrated to be weightier than *Yom Tov*. Execution would then be permitted to interfere with a private *korban*, and the verse applies in precisely that case — taking the *kohen* away from a private sacrifice.

### Rava Counter

Rava challenges this resolution. Even granting that a private *korban* does not override *Yom Tov*, Rava constructs a *kal va-chomer* that prevents execution from interfering even with a private *korban*: *Yom Tov* does not yield to execution; a private *korban* overrides *Yom Tov* (according to those who hold it does); therefore a private *korban* is weightier than execution, and execution should not interfere with a private *korban* either. The verse is again left without any case to which it applies.

### All Korbonot

The difficulty compounds when we consider the range of opinions. According to the view that private *korbonot* (*nedarim u-nedavot*) *may* be brought on *Yom Tov*, both private and public *korbonot* are weightier than execution — public by virtue of overriding Shabbat directly, private by virtue of the *kal va-chomer* through *Yom Tov* — and there is no *korban* setting in which execution can legitimately be said to interfere. The verse *me-im mizbachi tikachenu* is stranded without application. This is untenable, and the analysis must be taken further.

### Mizbechi=Tamid

Rava's final resolution is elegant: *mizbechi ha-meyuchad li* — the phrase "my altar" in the verse does not refer generically to any *avodah* being performed on the altar, but specifically to the *avodah* that is most essentially *designated* to the altar, that which is its defining and primary service. And *mai nihu tamid* — what is that? The *tamid*, the daily public offering. The verse is therefore speaking of a *kohen* engaged in the public *tamid* sacrifice — a *korban tzibbur* — and even from that he must be removed to face execution.

### Public Override

This resolves the paradox. The explicit verse *me-im mizbachi tikachenu la-mut* establishes that execution interferes even with the *tamid*, the paradigmatic public *korban tzibbur*, overriding *avodah* at its most essential. No *kal va-chomer* can overturn this explicit ruling. And yet, with all that force, execution still does not override Shabbat — because the Torah supplied an explicit verse prohibiting that as well, and explicit verses govern where *kal va-chomer* arguments would otherwise lead.

The resulting framework is asymmetric in a way that demands attention: execution overrides *avodah* — even public *avodah*, even the *tamid* — but does not override Shabbat; *avodah* itself overrides Shabbat. A *kohen* performing the *tamid* can be removed and executed, but the same execution cannot take place on Shabbat. This is not a logical tension to be resolved but an expression of the Torah's own explicit rulings, which chart a course that no *kal va-chomer* alone could have predicted.
