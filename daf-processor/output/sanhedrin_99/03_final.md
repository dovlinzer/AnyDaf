# Sanhedrin 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Purpose of Creation

### World Created For

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: לָא אִבְּרִי עָלְמָא אֶלָּא לְדָוִד. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לְמֹשֶׁה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לְמָשִׁיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: The world was created only for** the sake of **David,** by virtue of his merit. **And Shmuel says:** It was created by virtue of the merit of **Moses. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It was created by virtue of the merit of the **Messiah.**

The *daf* begins by picking up at the bottom of 98b, where the *Gemara* transitions from the *aggadeta* surrounding *Techiyat Hameitim*, *Olam Haba*, and *Yemot Hamashiach* — the themes provoked by the *mishna*'s list of those who have no portion in the world to come — toward the remainder of that list. Before doing so, the *Gemara* offers a remarkable three-way debate about the very purpose of creation.

Of the three positions, *l'Moshe* is perhaps the most classically rabbinic: the world exists for *Torah*, for learning and performing *mitzvot*. *L'Mashiach* is also readily understood — the world is oriented toward a future redeemed and perfected reality. But what is the meaning of *l'David*? Rashi's answer is elegant: it is *bizchut tishbachot she'haya omer lifnei Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — on account of the praises that David would offer before God. So the three visions are *tefillah*, *Torah*, and *tikkun olam* in its deepest sense.

There is also something worth noting in the contrast between *l'David* and *l'Mashiach*. David, after all, is himself a *Mashiach* — yet he is a figure who inhabits the imperfect world, a righteous king who nonetheless sins with Bat Sheva and does *teshuvah*. This may represent a tension between two orientations: whether the purpose of existence is to strive for the highest future ideal, or to live the most perfected life possible within the flawed world we actually inhabit. This resonates with Shmuel's position elsewhere — *ein bein olam hazeh l'yemot Hamashiach ela shibud malchuyot* — the messianic era differs from this world only in that foreign domination will cease.

### Names of Mashiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה שְׁמוֹ? דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אָמְרִי: שִׁילֹה שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַד כִּי יָבֹא שִׁילֹה״. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמְרִי: יִנּוֹן שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יְהִי שְׁמוֹ לְעוֹלָם לִפְנֵי שֶׁמֶשׁ יִנּוֹן שְׁמוֹ״. דְּבֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָה אָמְרִי: חֲנִינָה שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא אֶתֵּן לָכֶם חֲנִינָה״. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: מְנַחֵם בֶּן חִזְקִיָּה שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי רָחַק מִמֶּנִּי מְנַחֵם מֵשִׁיב נַפְשִׁי״. וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: חִיוּוֹרָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָכֵן חֳלָיֵינוּ הוּא נָשָׂא וּמַכְאֹבֵינוּ סְבָלָם וַאֲנַחְנוּ חֲשַׁבְנֻהוּ נָגוּעַ מֻכֵּה אֱלֹהִים וּמְעֻנֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the Messiah, the Gemara asks: **What is his name? The school of Rabbi Sheila says: Shiloh is his name, as it is stated: "Until when Shiloh shall come"** (Genesis 49:10). **The school of Rabbi Yannai says: Yinnon is his name, as it is stated: "May his name endure forever; may his name continue [*yinnon*] as long as the sun;** and may men bless themselves by him" (Psalms 72:17). **The school of Rabbi Ḥanina says: Ḥanina is his name, as it is stated: "For I will show you no favor [*ḥanina*]"** (Jeremiah 16:13). **And some say** that **Menaḥem ben Ḥizkiyya is his name, as it is stated: "Because the comforter [*menaḥem*] that should relieve my soul is far from me"** (Lamentations 1:16). **And the Rabbis say: The leper of the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **is his name, as it is stated: "Indeed our illnesses he did bear and our pains he endured; yet we did esteem him injured, stricken by God, and afflicted"** (Isaiah 53:4).

The *Gemara* then asks: *u'mah shemo* — what is the name of *Mashiach*? What follows is a charmingly self-referential series of opinions. *Debei Rabbi Shila amri Shilo shemo* — the school of Rabbi Shila says his name is *Shilo*, deriving it from the verse *ad ki yavo Shilo*. *Debei Rabbi Yannai amri Yanun shemo* — the school of Rabbi Yannai says his name is *Yanun*, from the verse *yehi shemo l'olam lifnei shemesh Yanun shemo*. *Debei Rabbi Chanina amri Chanina shemo* — the school of Rabbi Chanina says his name is *Chanina*, from *asher lo eten lachem chanina*. And *yesh omrim*: his name is *Menachem ben Chizkiyah*, from *ki rachak mimeni menachem*.

Note that in each case, the school names *Mashiach* after its own *rebbe*. This is not mere self-promotion; rather, it reflects a genuine theological sensibility — that *Mashiach* could in theory arrive in any generation, and a figure of the stature of one's own *rebbe* might be that person. Each school is saying: the potential is real and present. This is precisely the teaching from the *daf* before — *im bekolo tishma'u*, if you listen to his voice today. So the naming exercises are less about pinpointing a historical individual and more about cultivating a living sense of messianic imminence.

*V'rabanan amri*: the rabbis say his name is *Chivara d'vei Rabbi* — the sick one, the leper of the house of *Rebbe*. This returns to the image of the suffering servant: *achen cholyenu hu nasa, umachovenu sevelam, va'anachnu chashavnuhu nagua umukeh elohim ume'uneh* — our illnesses he bears, our wounds he suffered, we considered him smitten by God and afflicted.

### Mashiach Leadership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אִי מִן חַיַּיָּא הוּא, כְּגוֹן אֲנָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה אַדִּירוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ וּמֹשְׁלוֹ מִקִּרְבּוֹ יָצָא״. אָמַר רַב: אִי מִן חַיַּיָּא הוּא, כְּגוֹן רַבֵּינוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ. אִי מִן מִתַיָּא הוּא, כְּגוֹן דָּנִיֵּאל אִישׁ חֲמוּדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: If** the Messiah **is among the living** in this generation, he is a person **such as me,** who already has dominion over the Jewish people, **as it is stated: "And their prince shall be of themselves, and their governor shall proceed from their midst"** (Jeremiah 30:21), indicating that the redeemer is already in power. **Rav says: If** the Messiah **is among the living** in this generation, he is a person **such as our saintly Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who was renowned for his sanctity, piety, and Torah knowledge. **If** the Messiah **is among the dead** he is a person **such as Daniel, the beloved man.**

On this passage, *Amar Rav Nachman*: *im min chayya hu* — if he is among the living, *kegon ana* — I would be a fitting example. Rashi offers two readings of this phrase, but the Maharsha notes that Rav Nachman was a figure of political leadership among the Jews, and *Mashiach* is, among other things, a political head. The verse he cites supports this: *vehaya adiro mimenu umoshlo mikirbo yetzei* — the glorious one will be from within them and the ruler will emerge from their midst. The tension between this image of powerful kingship and the earlier image of the leprous suffering servant is openly acknowledged — these are different visions of *Mashiach* that do not fully reconcile.

*Amar Rav*: if he is comparable to someone living, *kegon Rabbeinu Hakadosh* — like Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi, who embodied the combination of religious and political authority. If comparable to someone who has passed, *kegon Daniel ish chamudot* — Daniel, who exercised a kind of mixed authority within a non-Jewish political system while maintaining his religious identity.

### David vs. Mashiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַעֲמִיד לָהֶם דָּוִד אַחֵר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָבְדוּ אֵת ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיהֶם וְאֵת דָּוִד מַלְכָּם אֲשֶׁר אָקִים לָהֶם״. ״הֵקִים״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״אָקִים״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְדָוִד עַבְדִּי נָשִׂיא לָהֶם לְעוֹלָם״? כְּגוֹן קֵיסָר וּפַלְגֵי קֵיסָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, is destined to establish another David** for the Jewish people as the Messiah, **as it is stated: "And they shall serve the Lord their God, and David their king, whom I will establish for them"** (Jeremiah 30:9). **It is not stated: I established, but "I will establish,"** indicating that the name of the future king will be David. **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: But isn't it written: "And my servant David shall be their prince forever"** (Ezekiel 37:25), indicating that King David himself will rule over the Jewish people? Abaye said: They will rule in tandem **like an emperor and a viceroy;** the Messiah will be king and David will be second-in-command.

*Amar Rav Yehuda Amar Rav*: God will raise up for Israel another David — not merely a descendant of David, but another David. The verse reads *ve'avadu et Hashem eloheihem ve'et David malkam asher akim lahem* — and they will serve Hashem their God and David their king, whom I will raise for them. Crucially, the verse says *akim* — I will raise — not *heikim* — I have raised — pointing toward a future event.

Rav Papa then asks of Abaye: does not another verse call him *nasi* — a prince — rather than *melech*, a king? The *Gemara* resolves this through the analogy of *Keisar upalgi Keisar* — the Caesar and the half-Caesar. Rashi explains: the newly born David — whatever it means to call him a new David rather than simply *Ben David* — will be the king, while the resurrected King David himself will be second in command.

## Messianic Duration

### Length Calculations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״הוֹי הַמִּתְאַוִּים אֶת יוֹם ה׳ לָמָּה זֶּה לָכֶם יוֹם ה׳ הוּא חֹשֶׁךְ וְלֹא אוֹר״? מָשָׁל לְתַרְנְגוֹל וַעֲטַלֵּף שֶׁהָיוּ מְצַפִּין לָאוֹר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ תַּרְנְגוֹל לַעֲטַלֵּף: אֲנִי מְצַפֶּה לָאוֹרָה שֶׁאוֹרָה שֶׁלִּי הִיא, וְאַתָּה לָמָּה לְךָ אוֹרָה?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Simlai taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Woe to you who desire the day of the Lord. Why would you have this day of the Lord? It is darkness, and not light"** (Amos 5:18)? It is **comparable to a rooster and a bat who were looking forward to** the **light** of day. **The rooster said to the bat: I look forward to light, as light is** an indication of **my** time to be active. **But** as for **you, why do you** need **light?** Nighttime for you is like daytime for me.

The *Gemara* now turns to a different question that grows out of an adjacent discussion. The verse *hoi hamit'avim et yom Hashem* — woe to those who yearn for the day of God — is expounded by *Darash Rav Simlai*: why are you longing for that day? *Hu choshech velo or* — it will be a day of darkness, not light. He offers a *mashal*: a rooster and a bat were both waiting for daybreak. The rooster says to the bat — I understand why I wait for dawn, it is my time. But why do *you* wait for light? The daylight will not benefit you.

The point is not that the messianic day brings suffering to the righteous — it does not. Rather, the wicked are being warned: you are like the bat who yearns for a dawn that will only harm you. The day of *Mashiach* will be a day of judgment for those who have wronged Israel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אֵימָתַי אָתֵי מָשִׁיחַ? אָמַר לֵיהּ: לְכִי חָפֵי לְהוּ חֲשׁוֹכָא לְהָנְהוּ אִינָשֵׁי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵילָט קָא לָיְיטַתְּ לִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קְרָא כְּתִיב, ״כִּי הִנֵּה הַחֹשֶׁךְ יְכַסֶּה אֶרֶץ וַעֲרָפֶל לְאֻמִּים וְעָלַיִךְ יִזְרַח ה׳ וּכְבוֹדוֹ עָלַיִךְ יֵרָאֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And that is** the background for the following exchange, **as a certain heretic said to Rabbi Abbahu: When will** the **Messiah come?** Rabbi Abbahu **said to him:** He will come **when the darkness will enshroud these people,** i.e., you. The heretic **said to him:** Are **you cursing me** for no reason? Rabbi Abbahu **said to him,** I am merely relating to you **a verse** that **is written: "For behold, the darkness shall cover the earth, and fog the peoples; but the Lord shall shine upon you, and His glory shall be seen upon you"** (Isaiah 60:2).

This is followed by the opening of 99a: *Ve-hainu d'amar lei ha-hu mina le-Rabbi Abbahu* — a certain sectarian asked Rabbi Abbahu: when will *Mashiach* come? Rabbi Abbahu replied: when darkness covers those people — meaning you. The sectarian objected: are you cursing me? Rabbi Abbahu responded: it is a verse — *ki hinei ha-choshech yechasef eretz va-arafel le-umim, ve-alayich yizrach Hashem, u-chevodo alayich yeira-eh* — darkness will cover the earth and thick cloud the nations, but upon you God will shine forth.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, יְמוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה אָקוּט בְּדוֹר״. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר, שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְהָיָה בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא וְנִשְׁכַּחַת צֹר שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה כִּימֵי מֶלֶךְ אֶחָד״. אֵיזֶהוּ מֶלֶךְ מְיוּחָד? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה מָשִׁיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: The messianic era will be forty years** long, **as it is stated: "Forty years will I strive with the generation"** (Psalms 95:10). The forty years of strife with the gentiles will be followed by the glory days of the Messiah. **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says:** The messianic era will last **seventy years, as it is stated: "And it shall come to pass on that day, that Tyre shall be forgotten seventy years, according to the days of one king"** (Isaiah 23:15). In this context, one [*eḥad*], means unique [*meyuḥad*]. **Which is** the **unique king? You must say** that **this is** a reference to the **Messiah.**

The *Gemara* then turns to the question of how long the messianic period will last. *Tanya*: Rabbi Eliezer says *yemot ha-Mashiach* will last forty years, deriving this from the verse *arbaim shana akut be-dor* — forty years I seized the generation — read together with *samcheinu kiyemos inisanu* — make us rejoice like the days of our affliction. The days of affliction in the wilderness were forty, and those forty transfer to forty years of the messianic era. Rashi notes a variant reading of four thousand years, based on the principle that one day of God equals a thousand years.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says seventy years, from *ve-haya ba-yom ha-hu ve-nishkachat tzor shivim shana ki-ymei melech echad* — Tyre will be forgotten for seventy years, like the days of one king. The *Gemara* asks: which is the distinctive, singular king? *Hevei omer zeh Mashiach* — that must refer to *Mashiach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִירָאוּךָ עִם שָׁמֶשׁ וְלִפְנֵי יָרֵחַ דּוֹר דּוֹרִים״. רַבִּי הִילֵּל אוֹמֵר: אֵין לָהֶם מָשִׁיחַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁכְּבָר אֲכָלוּהוּ בִּימֵי חִזְקִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The messianic era will last **three generations, as it is stated: "May they fear You as long as the sun and moon endure, throughout the generations [*dor dorim*]"** (Psalms 72:5). *Dor* is singular and *dorim* is plural, for a total of three generations. **Rabbi Hillel says: There is no Messiah** coming **for the Jewish people, as they already ate** from **him,** as all the prophecies relating to the Messiah were already fulfilled, **during the days of Hezekiah.**

*Rabbi* says three generations, from the verse *yira-ucha im shemesh ve-lifnei yarei-ach dor dorim* — they will fear You with the sun and before the moon, generation of generations. Rashi explains that the moon represents the Kingdom of the House of David. *Dor dorim* — one generation plus two equals three.

This raises a question: does the messianic period lasting multiple generations imply that individuals will live for extraordinary lengths of time, or simply that the *Melech Mashiach* will die, his son will rule, and so on, with the messianic epoch continuing across natural lifespans? The *Gemara* leaves this open.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: שְׁרָא לֵיהּ מָרֵיהּ לְרַבִּי הִילֵּל. חִזְקִיָּה אֵימַת הֲוָה? בְּבַיִת רִאשׁוֹן! וְאִילּוּ זְכַרְיָה קָא מִתְנַבֵּי בְּבַיִת שֵׁנִי, וְאָמַר: ״גִּילִי מְאֹד בַּת צִיּוֹן הָרִיעִי בַּת יְרוּשָׁלִַם הִנֵּה מַלְכֵּךְ יָבוֹא לָךְ צַדִּיק וְנוֹשָׁע הוּא עָנִי וְרֹכֵב עַל חֲמוֹר וְעַל עַיִר בֶּן אֲתֹנוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says: May the Master forgive Rabbi Hillel** for stating matters with no basis. With regard to **Hezekiah, when was** his reign? It was **during the First Temple** period. **Whereas Zechariah** ben Berechiah, the prophet, **prophesied during the Second Temple** period **and said: "Rejoice greatly, daughter of Zion; shout, daughter of Jerusalem; behold, your king will come to you; he is just and victorious; lowly and riding upon a donkey and upon a colt, the foal of a donkey"** (Zechariah 9:9). In the generations after Hezekiah, there are prophecies about both redemption and the coming of the Messiah.

Now Rabbi Hillel — a minor figure in the *Gemara*, not to be confused with the great Hillel — makes a striking statement: *ein lahem mashiach l'Yisrael, she-kvar achaluhu bi-ymei Chizkiyahu* — there will be no *Mashiach* for Israel, for they already consumed that with Chizkiyahu; he was the fulfillment of all the prophetic visions. *Amar Rav Yosef*: *shrei lei mareih le-Rabbi Hillel* — may God forgive Rabbi Hillel for such an error. Chizkiyahu lived during the first *Beit Ha-Mikdash*, yet the prophet Zechariah — the one who prophesied during the second *Beit Hamikdash* — was still proclaiming *gili me'od bat Tzion, hineh malkeich yavo lach, tzadik ve-nosha hu ani ve-rochev al chamor* — rejoice greatly, daughter of Zion, your king will come to you, righteous and redeemed, poor and riding on a donkey. How could those visions have already been fulfilled in Chizkiyahu's time?

Rashi's reading of Rabbi Hillel's position is illuminating: Rabbi Hillel is not denying a future redeemed world. Rather, he holds that God Himself will reign directly — *hakadosh baruch hu yimloch b'atzmo v'yigaleim l'vado* — and there will be no human Davidic king as intermediary. It almost sounds like leaping straight to the *Olam Haba* period, a world governed directly by the divine presence without the medium of a human king.

What is theologically remarkable is how the *Gemara* responds. Rav Yosef says *shrei lei mareih* — God should forgive him — not out of horror at heresy, but essentially because the position is refuted by a *pasuk*. The *Gemara* does not react with shock that a rabbi denied the existence of *Mashiach*. This bears on the broader question of *emunah* as *psak*: can dogma be adjudicated? David Bleich, in his introduction to *In Perfect Faith*, argues that yes, *emunah* can be *paskened*, that we have *paskened* against Rabbi Hillel, and that denying *Mashiach* is now *kefirah* in a way it was not before we did so. Marc Shapiro, by contrast, argues that questions of dogma concern eternal truths — either *Mashiach* is coming or not — so the framing of *psak* is somewhat misplaced. What we can say is that Rabbi Hillel was always wrong as a matter of what will actually happen. The question of *psak* may apply more precisely to the *consequences* of holding incorrect beliefs. In any event, the *Gemara*'s relatively calm handling of Rabbi Hillel's position is striking.

### History Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, יְמוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיְעַנְּךָ וַיַּרְעִבֶךָ וַיַּאֲכִלְךָ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״שַׂמְּחֵנוּ כִּימוֹת עִנִּיתָנוּ שְׁנוֹת רָאִינוּ רָעָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: The messianic era** will be **forty years** long. **It is written here** with regard to the forty-year sojourn of the children of Israel in the wilderness: **"And He afflicted you, and suffered you to hunger and fed you** with manna" (Deuteronomy 8:3); **and it is written there: "Make us glad according to the days that You afflicted us, the years that we saw evil"** (Psalms 90:15).

*Tanya idach* — another *braita* returns to calculating the duration of *yemot ha-Mashiach*. Rabbi Eliezer again says forty years, now from the verse *vaya'anchecha vayarivcha vaya'achilcha et ha-man* — God afflicted you and made you hungry and fed you the *man*, referring to the forty years of wandering — combined again with *samcheinu kiyemos inisanu*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי דּוֹסָא אוֹמֵר: אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַעֲבָדוּם וְעִנּוּ אֹתָם אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״שַׂמְּחֵנוּ כִּימוֹת עִנִּיתָנוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Dosa says:** The messianic era will last **four hundred years. It is written here** with regard to the Covenant of the Pieces: **"And they shall serve them, and they shall afflict them four hundred years"** (Genesis 15:13); **and it is written there: "Make us glad according to the days that You afflicted us."**

Rabbi Dosa says four hundred years, from *ve-avadum ve-inu osam arba meos shanah* — they will enslave and afflict them four hundred years — again with the same rejoicing verse equating future joy with past affliction. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנָה, כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יוֹם נָקָם בְּלִבִּי וּשְׁנַת גְּאוּלַי בָּאָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The messianic era will last **365 years, corresponding to the number of days of the solar** year, **as it is stated: "For the day of vengeance is in My heart, and the year of My redeemed is come"** (Isaiah 63:4).

*Rabbi* says three hundred and sixty-five years, like the days of the solar year — *ki yom nakam b'libbi u'shnas geulai ba'ah* — a day of vengeance is in my heart, and the year of my redemption is come, reading the "day" as each day of the year and the "year" as the total. Again Rashi here carries a variant reading of three hundred and sixty-five thousand years.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״יוֹם נָקָם בְּלִבִּי״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְלִבִּי גִּלִּיתִי, לְאֵבָרַיי לֹא גִּלִּיתִי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לְלִבִּי גִּלִּיתִי, לְמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לֹא גִּלִּיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"For the day of vengeance is in My heart"? Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it means that God said: **I revealed** the day of vengeance **to My heart,** but **I did not reveal** it **to My limbs,** as it were, as I never stated it explicitly. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says** that it means that God said: **I revealed** this secret **to My heart,** but **I did not reveal** it to **the ministering angels.**

On the phrase *ki yom nakam b'libbi*, Rabbi Yochanan comments: *l'libbi galis', l'eivarai lo galis'* — I have revealed it to my heart but not to my limbs. God has not even articulated to Himself, as it were, what that day will be, so deeply hidden is it. Resh Lakish adds: *l'libbi galis', l'malachei hashareit lo galis'* — I admitted it to myself but did not tell even the angels.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי אֲבִימִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: יְמוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל שִׁבְעַת אֲלָפִים שָׁנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמְשׂוֹשׂ חָתָן עַל כַּלָּה כֵּן יָשִׂישׂ עָלַיִךְ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Avimi, son of Rabbi Abbahu, taught: The messianic era for the Jewish people** will last **seven thousand years, as it is stated: "And as the bridegroom rejoices over the bride, so shall your God rejoice over you"** (Isaiah 62:5). The bridegroom rejoices over the bride for seven days, and the day of the Holy One, Blessed be He, is one thousand years.

*Tanya Avimi brei d'Rabbi Abbahu*: *yemos hamashiach l'yisrael shivas alafim shanah* — seven thousand years. This is derived from the rejoicing of the *chatan* over the *kallah* — just as there are seven days of *sheva brachos*, so one day per thousand years yields seven thousand years.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יְמוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ כְּמִיּוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרָא הָעוֹלָם וְעַד עַכְשָׁיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּימֵי הַשָּׁמַיִם עַל הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The duration of **the messianic era is like** the duration of the period that runs **from the day the world was created until now,** i.e., the day when the Messiah will come, **as it is stated:** "That your days may be multiplied, and the days of your children, in the land that the Lord swore to your fathers to give them, **as the days of heaven upon the earth"** (Deuteronomy 11:21).

*Amar Rav Yehuda Amar Shmuel*: *yemos hamashiach k'yom she'nivra ha'olam v'ad achshav* — the messianic period will equal the entire span of world history from creation until now. The *pasuk* is *ki-ymei hashamayim al ha'aretz* — like the days of the heavens upon the earth. Whether this implies that individuals will live for that extraordinary span, or simply that the messianic epoch as a whole will endure that long, is left open.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: כִּימֵי נֹחַ עַד עַכְשָׁיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי מֵי נֹחַ זֹאת לִי אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says:** The duration of **the messianic era is like** the duration of the period that runs **from the days of Noah until now,** i.e., the day when the Messiah will come, **as it is stated** with regard to redemption: **"For this is as the seas of [*ki mei*] Noah to me; as I have sworn** that the seas of Noah shall no more go over the earth, so have I sworn that I will not be angry with you nor rebuke you" (Isaiah 54:9). The words *ki mei* can be understood as one word, *kimei*, meaning: Like the days of.

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak amar*: *ki-ymei Noach v'ad achshav* — from the days of Noah until now — citing the verse *ki mei Noach zos li asher nishbati me'avor mei Noach od al ha'aretz*. Rashi explains: God's oath after the flood was not to destroy the world; so when *Mashiach* comes, the oath covers the length from Noah until now, after which the world will be destroyed and *Olam Haba* will be recreated.

### Prophetic Visions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַנְּבִיאִים כּוּלָּן לֹא נִתְנַבְּאוּ אֶלָּא לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ, אֲבָל לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא – ״עַיִן לֹא רָאָתָה אֱלֹהִים זוּלָתְךָ אֱלֹהִים יַעֲשֶׂה לִמְחַכֵּה לוֹ״. וּפְלִיגָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין בֵּין הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ אֶלָּא שִׁעְבּוּד מַלְכִיּוֹת בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In their prophecies with regard to redemption and the end of days, **all the prophets prophesied only about the messianic era, but with regard to the World-to-Come** the reward is not quantifiable, as it states: **"No eye has seen it, God, aside from You, Who will do for those who await Him"** (Isaiah 64:3). The Gemara notes: **And** this statement **disagrees with** the opinion **of Shmuel, as Shmuel says: The** difference **between this world and the messianic era is only** with regard to **servitude to** foreign **kingdoms alone,** as they will leave Eretz Yisrael.

*Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *kol hanevi'im kulam lo nisnab'u ela l'yimos hamashiach* — all the prophets prophesied only about the messianic period. The visions of lions lying down with lambs, swords beaten into plowshares — these describe the messianic era. But when it comes to *Olam Haba* itself, *ayin lo ra'asah Elokim zulasecha* — no eye has seen it, God, other than You; *ya'aseh l'mechakeh lo* — You will do it for the one who longs for it.

### Shmuel's View

This position argues with Shmuel. *D'amar Shmuel*: *ein bein ha'olam hazeh l'yimos hamashiach ela shibud malchuyos* — there is no difference between this world and the messianic era except subjugation to foreign powers. For Shmuel, the messianic period is thoroughly this-worldly, and therefore the prophetic visions must be oriented toward the truly transcendent *Olam Haba*. For Rabbi Yochanan, the messianic period itself is miraculous — and *Olam Haba* surpasses even prophetic vision entirely.

## Hidden Reward

### Reward Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַנְּבִיאִים לֹא נִתְנַבְּאוּ אֶלָּא לְבַעֲלֵי תְשׁוּבָה, אֲבָל צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִים – ״עַיִן לֹא רָאָתָה אֱלֹהִים זוּלָתְךָ״. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַב: מָקוֹם שֶׁבַּעֲלֵי תְשׁוּבָה עוֹמְדִין שָׁם – צַדִּיקִים אֵינָן עוֹמְדִין שָׁם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שָׁלוֹם שָׁלוֹם לָרָחוֹק וְלַקָּרוֹב״. בְּרֵישָׁא רָחוֹק וַהֲדַר קָרוֹב. מַאי ״רָחוֹק״ – רָחוֹק דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא. וּמַאי ״קָרוֹב״ – קָרוֹב דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא וּדְהַשְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All of the prophets prophesied** their prophecies of consolation **only with regard to penitents, but with regard to** the reward of **the completely righteous** it is stated: **"No eye has seen it, God, aside from You."** The Gemara notes: **And** the opinion expressed in **this** statement **disagrees with** the opinion **of Rabbi Abbahu,** who holds that penitents are superior to the righteous, **as Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rav says: In the place where penitents stand,** even **the completely righteous do not stand, as it is stated: "Peace, peace upon him who is far and him who is near"** (Isaiah 57:19). Peace and greeting is extended **first** to **him who is far, and** only **thereafter** is peace extended to **him who is near. What** is the meaning of the term: "Upon him who is **far"?** It means: One who was **initially far,** i.e., the penitent. **And what** is the meaning of the term: "Him who is **near"?** It means: One who was **initially near and** continues to be near **at present,** i.e., the completely righteous individual.

The same teaching of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan is then cited in a second formulation: *kol hanevi'im lo nisnabu ela l'ba'alei teshuvah* — all the prophetic visions were only for those who sinned and repented. But for *tzaddikim gemurim* — those who never sinned at all — *ayin lo ra'asah Elokim zulasecha*: their reward is utterly beyond prophetic sight.

### Baalei Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״לָרָחוֹק״ – שֶׁהוּא רָחוֹק מֵעֲבֵירָה, ״קָרוֹב״ – שֶׁהוּא קָרוֹב מֵעֲבֵירָה וְנִתְרַחֵק מִמֶּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: "Upon him who is far"** is referring to one **who is distant from transgression,** the completely righteous individual. "Him who is **near"** is referring to one **who is near to transgression and distanced** himself **from it,** the penitent.

This claim that the *tzaddikim gemurim* occupy a higher plane than the *ba'alei teshuvah* argues with Rabbi Abbahu: *d'amar Rabbi Abbahu, makom she'ba'alei teshuvah omdim sham tzaddikim einam omdim* — the place where *ba'alei teshuvah* stand, the fully righteous cannot stand. His proof is the verse *shalom shalom l'rachok v'lakarov* — peace, peace to the distant and to the near — where peace is extended first to *rachok*, those who were once far and have drawn near, and only then to *karov*, those who have always been near. *B'reisha rachok* — first to those who started distant. So the *ba'alei teshuvah* receive precedence.

Rabbi Yochanan reads the same verse differently to support his position: *rachok* refers to one who is distant from sin — i.e., never sinned — while *karov* refers to one who had been close to sin and only now has distanced from it. The reading is admittedly strained.

### Eden Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַנְּבִיאִים כּוּלָּן לֹא נִתְנַבְּאוּ אֶלָּא לְמַשִּׂיא בִּתּוֹ לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם, וּלְעוֹשֶׂה פְּרַקְמַטְיָא לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם, וְלִמְהַנֶּה תַּלְמִיד חָכָם מִנְּכָסָיו. אֲבָל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים עַצְמָן – ״עַיִן לֹא רָאָתָה אֱלֹהִים זוּלָתְךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All of the prophets prophesied** their prophecies of consolation **only with regard to one who** values wisdom and therefore **marries his daughter to a Torah scholar, and to one who conducts business [*perakmatya*] on behalf of a Torah scholar, and to one who** utilizes his wealth **to benefit a Torah scholar from his property** in some other way. **But** the prophets did not describe the extent of the reward for **Torah scholars themselves,** whose reward is not quantifiable, as it is stated: **"No eye has seen it, God, aside from You."**

Then the *Gemara* asks directly: *mai ayin lo ra'asah* — what is this unseen reward? After all the majestic build-up — "no eye has ever seen, not even through the prophetic lens" — the *Gemara* proceeds to answer, and the answers are, perhaps deliberately, somewhat deflating. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״עַיִן לֹא רָאָתָה״? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: זֶה יַיִן הַמְשׁוּמָּר בַּעֲנָבָיו מִשֵּׁשֶׁת יְמֵי בְּרֵאשִׁית. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: זֶה עֵדֶן, שֶׁלֹּא רָאֲתָה עַיִן מֵעוֹלָם. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: אָדָם הֵיכָן דָּר? בַּגָּן. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: גַּן הוּא עֵדֶן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְנָהָר יֹצֵא מֵעֵדֶן לְהַשְׁקוֹת אֶת הַגָּן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** reward is indicated in the phrase **"no eye has seen it"? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: This is wine preserved in its grapes from the six days of Creation.** No person has drunk that wine and therefore no one can appreciate its quality. **Reish Lakish says: This is Eden,** which **no eye has ever seen. And if you say: Where did Adam** the first man **live,** if not in Eden, the answer is that he lived **in the garden. And lest you say:** The **garden is Eden** and they are indistinguishable, **the verse states: "And a river emerged from Eden to irrigate the garden"** (Genesis 2:10), indicating that they are two distinct places.

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: *zeh yayin hameshummar be'anavav misheshes yemei bereishis* — it is wine that has been stored in its grapes since the six days of creation. The unseen reward is the finest wine imaginable. This reads almost like a gentle puncturing of the preceding rhetoric.

Resh Lakish says: *zeh Eden* — it is Eden itself. The *Gemara* responds: but did not the first human being live in Eden? He lived only in the *gan*, the garden — but not Eden itself. And if you say the *gan* and *Eden* are the same, the verse teaches otherwise: *v'nahar yotzei me'Eden l'hashkot es hagan* — a river flows out of Eden to water the garden. Eden is distinct from and greater than the garden. It is in that unexplored Eden that the reward awaits.

There is, however, a deeper reading — not the material pleasures of wine or botanical paradise, but the sense of direct divine presence. As other *Gemara* passages describe *Olam Haba*: the righteous sit with crowns on their heads and bask in the radiance of the *Shechinah*. In that light, *Eden* is less a place than an encounter — an unmediated experience of the divine that no prophetic eye has glimpsed.

## Torah from Heaven

### Prooftext

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאוֹמֵר: אֵין תּוֹרָה מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי דְבַר ה׳ בָּזָה וּמִצְוָתוֹ הֵפַר הִכָּרֵת תִּכָּרֵת״ – זֶה הָאוֹמֵר: אֵין תּוֹרָה מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״כִּי דְבַר ה׳ בָּזָה״ – זֶה אֶפִּיקוֹרוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that those who have no share in the World-to-Come include: **And one who says:** The **Torah** did **not** originate **from Heaven. The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that with regard to the verse: **"Because he has despised the word of the Lord and has breached His commandment;** that soul **shall be excised;** his iniquity shall be upon him" (Numbers 15:31), **this** is a reference to **one who says:** The **Torah** did **not** originate **from Heaven. Alternatively,** one can explain: **"Because he has despised the word of the Lord"; this** is a reference to **an *epikoros*,** who treats the word of God with contempt.

Having completed the extended discussion of *Techiyat Hameitim*, *Olam Haba*, and *Yemot Hamashiach*, the *Gemara* turns to the next item on the *mishna*'s list: *Ha-Omer Ein Torah Min Ha-Shamayim* — one who says that *Torah* is not from heaven. The operative verse is *ki devar Hashem bazah* — for the word of God he has despised — followed by *hikaret tikaret ha-nefesh ha-hi avonah bah* — that person shall be surely cut off, his sin is with him. While *karet* alone attaches to many sins, the double language *hikaret tikaret* is here read as *karet* in this world and *karet* in the next — loss of a portion in *Olam Haba*.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: *ki devar Hashem bazah* — *zeh ha-omer ein Torah min hashamayim* — this is the one who says *Torah* is not from heaven. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״כִּי דְבַר ה׳ בָּזָה״ – זֶה הַמְגַלֶּה פָּנִים בַּתּוֹרָה. ״וְאֶת מִצְוָתוֹ הֵפַר״ – זֶה הַמֵּפֵר בְּרִית בָּשָׂר. ״הִכָּרֵת תִּכָּרֵת״ – ״הִכָּרֵת״ בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״תִּכָּרֵת״ לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַמּוֹדָעִי: הַמְחַלֵּל אֶת הַקֳּדָשִׁים, וְהַמְבַזֶּה אֶת הַמּוֹעֲדוֹת, וְהַמֵּפֵר בְּרִיתוֹ שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ, וְהַמְגַלֶּה פָּנִים בְּתוֹרָה שֶׁלֹּא כַּהֲלָכָה, וְהַמַּלְבִּין פְּנֵי חֲבֵירוֹ בָּרַבִּים, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדוֹ תּוֹרָה וּמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** one can explain: **"Because he has despised the word of the Lord"; this** is a reference to **one who interprets the Torah inappropriately. "And has breached His commandment"; this** is a reference to **one who breaches the covenant of flesh,** who refuses to circumcise his foreskin. **"Shall be excised [*hikkaret tikkaret*]"; "*hikkaret*"** refers to being excised **in this world,** and **"*tikkaret*"** refers to being excised **from the World-to-Come. From here Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i says:** With regard to **one who desecrates consecrated** items, e.g., intentionally rendering them impure; **and one who treats** the intermediate days of **the Festivals with contempt; and one who breaches the covenant of Abraham our forefather; and one who reveals aspects in the Torah that are not in accordance with *halakha*; and one who humiliates another in public, even if he has to his credit Torah** study **and good deeds, he has no share in the World-to-Come.**

Furthermore, even if a person says the entire *Torah* is from heaven *chutz mipasuk echad* — except for one verse — claiming Moshe said it on his own without divine instruction — *zehu ki devar Hashem bazah*. Even one word of God, denied, constitutes despising God's word. The same applies to one who denies a single rabbinic inference: *chutz midekduk zeh, chutz migezeirah shavah zo* — everything except this one *kal vachomer*, everything except this one *gezeirah shavah* — even that partial denial is *ki devar Hashem bazah*. The Maharsha notes that the emphasis here is on *davar* — speech — suggesting that even the oral dimension, not only the written text, falls within the scope of this prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״כִּי דְבַר ה׳ בָּזָה״ – זֶה הָאוֹמֵר אֵין תּוֹרָה מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם. וַאֲפִילּוּ אָמַר כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, חוּץ מִפָּסוּק זֶה שֶׁלֹּא אֲמָרוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֶלָּא מֹשֶׁה מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ – זֶהוּ ״כִּי דְבַר ה׳ בָּזָה״. וַאֲפִילּוּ אָמַר כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, חוּץ מִדִּקְדּוּק זֶה, מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר זֶה, מִגְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה זוֹ – זֶה הוּא ״כִּי דְבַר ה׳ בָּזָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **"Because he has despised the word of the Lord"; this is** a reference to **one who says** the **Torah** did **not** originate **from Heaven. And even** if **one says the entire Torah** originated **from Heaven except for this verse,** i.e., any one verse, claiming **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, did not say it but Moses** himself said it **on his own, this is** included in the category of: **"Because he has despised the word of the Lord." And even** if **one says the entire Torah** originated **from Heaven except for this inference** inferred by the Sages, or except **for this *a fortiori*** inference, or except **for this verbal analogy, this is** included in the category of: **"Because he has despised the word of the Lord."**

### Apikoros Definition

*Davar acher*: *ki devar Hashem bazah* — *zeh apikoros*. This demands careful attention. There is a widespread assumption that *apikoros* means heretic — specifically, one who rejects articles of Jewish dogma — and this assumption derives from the Rambam, who systematized the *ikarei emunah* and used *apikoros* as his hook for the category of those who deny fundamental beliefs. But that is not what the *Gemara* means by the term.

From the *Gemara*'s perspective, an *apikoros* is someone who acts in a disrespectful and demeaning manner toward *Torah* or toward *Torah* scholars. The classic *Gemara* definition is *mevazeh talmidei chachamim* — one who demeans scholars of *Torah*. This is precisely the language of *ki devar Hashem bazah* — *mevazeh*, to demean — demonstrating that treating those who embody and represent *Devar Hashem* in a contemptuous manner is itself a form of despising God's word.

So the two entries from the *mishna* — *ein Torah min hashamayim* and *apikoros* — are both expressions of the same root: treating *Devar Hashem* with contempt, whether by denying it is divine or by demeaning its human representatives.

*Davar acher*: *ki devar Hashem bazah* — *zeh megaleh panim ba-Torah*. This phrase is often encountered with the addition *shelo ke-halacha* — one who explicates *Torah* contrary to *halacha* — and read as referring to heretical or deviant interpretation. But the *Gemara* does not use the phrase *shelo ke-halacha* here; that is a later gloss. *Megaleh panim ba-Torah* means what it literally says: *gillui panim* — to bare one's face, to act brazenly and insolently toward the *Torah*. It describes an attitude of brazen disrespect, not an act of textual misinterpretation. The gloss *shelo ke-halacha* appears in only one place, and even there it is clearly secondary.

Continuing: *zeh meifer brit basar* — one who has reversed the covenant of the flesh, undoing circumcision surgically. *Hikaret tikaret* — cut off in this world and cut off in the next.

What emerges from this entire passage is significant: what is being catalogued here is not primarily a list of incorrect dogmas. It is a collection of attitudes and actions — disrespect toward *Torah*, toward *talmidei chachamim*, toward one's covenantal relationship with God. It is the Rambam who transforms this material into a system of dogma with formal consequences for unbelief. The *Gemara* itself is far more interested in relationship and orientation than in propositional correctness. Even the *Chatam Sofer*, it should be noted, was troubled by the Rambam's systematization of thirteen *ikarei emunah*, arguing that fundamentally there are only two — God exists, and God gave the *Torah* — from which everything else follows.

### Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: הַלּוֹמֵד תּוֹרָה וְאֵינוֹ מְלַמְּדָהּ – זֶה הוּא ״דְּבַר ה׳ בָּזָה״. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַשְׁגִּיחַ עַל הַמִּשְׁנָה. רַבִּי נְהוֹרַאי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לַעֲסוֹק בַּתּוֹרָה וְאֵינוֹ עוֹסֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say:** With regard to **one who studies Torah and does not teach it** to others, **this** person **is** included in the category of: **"He has despised the word of the Lord,"** as his conduct indicates that he does not consider the word of God significant enough to teach others. **Rabbi Natan says: Anyone who does not pay** the requisite **attention to the Mishna** and does not consider it essential *halakha* is included in the category of: "Because he has despised the word of the Lord." **Rabbi Nehorai says: Anyone** for **whom** it is **possible to engage in Torah** study **and** who nevertheless **does not engage** in its study is included in the category of: "Because he has despised the word of the Lord."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: זֶה הָעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. מַאי מַשְׁמָעָה? דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״כִּי דְבַר ה׳ בָּזָה״ – זֶה הַמְבַזֶּה דִּבּוּר שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי: ״אָנֹכִי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, ״לֹא יִהְיֶה לְךָ אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael says: This** verse: "Because he has despised the word of the Lord," is a reference to **an idol worshipper.** The Gemara asks: From **where** in the verse is this **inferred?** The Gemara explains: It is derived from a verse, **as the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: "Because he has despised the word [*devar*] of the Lord"; this** is a reference to **one who treats with contempt a statement [*dibbur*] that was stated to Moses at Sinai** and heard by all of the Jewish people: **"I am the Lord your God…You shall have no other gods** beside me" (Exodus 20:2–3).

*Mikan amar Rabbi Eliezer HaModai* in *Pirkei Avot*: one who desecrates the sacrifices, one who demeans the *mo'adot* — Rashi says this refers to *Chol HaMoed*, the more vulnerable liminal period — one who undoes *brit milah*, one who acts brazenly toward *Torah*, and one who shames his friend in public — *af al pi sheyesh beyado Torah uma'asim tovim ein lo chelek l'Olam Haba* — even with much *Torah* and good deeds, has no portion in the world to come. Whether all of these are derived from the single verse *ki devar Hashem bazah* involves some creative reading: the sacrifices are things sanctified by God; the *mo'adim* are time sanctified by God; *malbish pnei chaveiro* may relate to human beings being created *betzelem Elokim*. Rashi seems to read it roughly this way.

*Tanya idach*: another *braita* adds that even one who says the entire *Torah* is from heaven except for this one *pasuk*, or this one inferential derivation, or this one *gezeirah shavah* — each of these is *ki devar Hashem bazah*.

## Attitudes Toward Torah

### Learn and Teach

*Tanya*: *Rebbi Meir omer* — whoever learns *Torah* and does not teach it, *zehu ki devar Hashem bazah*. The Maharsha explains the link to the verse: teaching requires verbalization — *dibbur* — while learning alone does not necessarily. To refuse to transmit is to be *mevaze* the *davar* of God, demeaning the verbal, living quality of *Devar Hashem*. Once again, the *Gemara* slides naturally between matters of faith, matters of attitude, and matters of action — without privileging the dogmatic over the relational.

*Rebbi Nasan omer*: whoever is not careful to repeat the *mishna* — to go over it again and again — is in a sense despising God's word, because the *mishna* is what makes the *pesukim* meaningful.

*Rebbi Nehorai omer*: whoever can engage in *Torah* and chooses not to — whoever sets other priorities above *Devar Hashem* — that too is *ki devar Hashem bazah*.

*Rebbi Yishmael omer*: this designation properly belongs to *oved avodah zarah*. His point seems to be that if we are going to be reading into this verse the severe consequence of having no *chelek l'Olam Haba*, we should reserve that for genuinely weighty acts. The *Gemara* asks how *avodah zarah* is derived from *ki devar Hashem bazah*. *Detani Debei Rebbi Yishmael*: *ki devar Hashem bazah* — *zeh hamevaze dibbur shene'emar lo l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — one who rejects the prime *dibbur* given at Sinai: *Anochi Hashem Elokecha* and *Lo yihiyeh lecha elohim acheirim* — the very words all of Israel heard directly. Rejecting *avodah zarah* is rejecting the foundational speech-act of revelation.

### Chazarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַלּוֹמֵד תּוֹרָה וְאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר עָלֶיהָ, דּוֹמֶה לְאָדָם שֶׁזּוֹרֵעַ וְאֵינוֹ קוֹצֵר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַלּוֹמֵד תּוֹרָה וּמְשַׁכְּחָהּ, דּוֹמֶה לְאִשָּׁה שֶׁיּוֹלֶדֶת וְקוֹבֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Torah study, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: Anyone who studies Torah and does not review it is comparable to a person who sows and does not reap. Rabbi Yehoshua says: Anyone who studies Torah and causes** himself **to forget it is similar to a woman who gives birth and buries** her newborn child.

*Rebbi Yehoshua ben Korcha omer*: whoever learns *Torah* and does not review it — *eino chozer aleha* — is likened to one who plants without harvesting. There is no point in the planting if you are not going to follow through with the harvest. The seed will simply rot in the ground. Doing *chazarah* is the act of cultivation that allows what was planted to take root, grow, and become something lasting. Failing to do so may itself be a form of *ki devar Hashem bazah* — it signals that you do not value it enough to follow through.

### Forgetting

*Rebbi Yehoshua omer*: whoever learns *Torah* and actually forgets it — *kol halomed Torah u'meshach'cha* — is likened to *ishah she'yoledes ve'koveres* — a woman who gives birth and then buries her child. The image is devastating. The previous metaphor allowed for some hope — perhaps something will grow despite the lack of harvesting — but this metaphor allows for none. To have invested all that effort in bringing something to life, only to watch it die — *why did you go through all this?* There is something almost like the concept of *chevlei leidah shel Mashiach* echoing here: you endure the labor pains of birth only to have the child taken from you. The follow-through — the *chazarah* — requires less effort than the initial learning, and yet it is the thing that sustains everything.

### Torah Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: זַמֵּר בְּכׇל יוֹם, זַמֵּר בְּכׇל יוֹם. אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אֲבוּדִימִי: מַאי קְרָא? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נֶפֶשׁ עָמֵל עָמְלָה לּוֹ כִּי אָכַף עָלָיו פִּיהוּ״. הוּא עָמֵל בִּמְקוֹם זֶה, וְתוֹרָתוֹ עוֹמֶלֶת לוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Sing every day, sing every day,** i.e., review your studies like a song that one sings over and over. **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avudimi says:** From **what verse** is this derived? It is **as it is stated: "The hunger of the laborer labors for him; for his mouth presses upon him"** (Proverbs 16:26), i.e., he exhausts his mouth through constant review and study. **He labors** in Torah **in this place,** this world, **and his Torah labors for him in another place,** the World-to-Come.

*Rebbi Akiva omer*: *zemer bechol yom zemer bechol yom* — your *Torah* should be a song every day. The Maharsha suggests that *zemer* here might mean *zomer* — to prune, to tend to what you have planted — but the more natural reading is musical. The way to keep *Torah* alive is to make it into a song: something you repeat again and again, something you return to naturally. Memory attaches powerfully to music; the children in the old *chadarim* had a *niggun* to the learning, and we remember melodies with a tenacity we rarely achieve for ordinary words. Rashi confirms: *haya mesader limudcha af al pi shesedurah beficha ke-zemer* — arrange your learning even when it is already arranged in your mouth, like a song. And the reward? *Vehu yigrom lecha shetehe l'Olam Haba be-simcha u-ve-shirim* — it will bring you to *Olam Haba* with joy and with song.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל אָדָם לְעָמָל נִבְרָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כִּי אָדָם לְעָמָל יוּלָּד״. אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם לַעֲמַל פֶּה נִבְרָא אִם לַעֲמַל מְלָאכָה נִבְרָא. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״כִּי אָכַף עָלָיו פִּיהוּ״, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר לַעֲמַל פֶּה נִבְרָא. וַעֲדַיִין אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם לַעֲמַל תּוֹרָה אִם לַעֲמַל שִׂיחָה. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא יָמוּשׁ סֵפֶר הַתּוֹרָה הַזֶּה מִפִּיךָ״, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר לַעֲמַל תּוֹרָה נִבְרָא. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רָבָא: כּוּלְּהוּ גּוּפֵי דְּרוּפְתְּקֵי נִינְהוּ. טוּבֵיהּ לִדְזָכֵי דְּהָוֵי דְּרוּפְתְּקֵי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Every man was created for labor, as it is stated: "Man is born for toil"** (Job 5:7). Based on this verse, **I do not know whether he was created for toil of the mouth,** speech, or **whether he was created for the toil of labor. When** the verse **states: "For his mouth presses upon him"** (Proverbs 16:26), **you must say** that **he was created for toil of the mouth. And still I do not know** with regard to the toil of the mouth **whether it is for the toil of Torah or for the toil of conversation. When** the verse **states: "This Torah scroll shall not depart from your mouth"** (Joshua 1:8), **you must say** that **he was created for the toil of Torah. And that is** the meaning of **what Rava said: All bodies are like receptacles** to store items until use. **Happy is one who is privileged, who is a receptacle for Torah.**

*Amar Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi*: what is the verse? *Nefesh amal amla lo ki akaf alav pihu* — if you labor, it will labor for you, because his mouth has devoted itself to it. Rashi: *mipnei she-amal ba-Torah, Torah omelet lo* — because you labored in *Torah*, *Torah* labors for you. The Maharsha reads this as the hidden secrets of *Torah* being revealed to you in *Olam Haba*. But there is also a more immediate reading: there is a percolating effect in deep learning. You work at something, you cannot quite reach it, you step away — and then the understanding arrives, because something in your mind was still processing it. If you have made it into a song and keep returning to it, then even when you are not consciously engaged, the *Torah* continues to work within you. *Hu amal be-makom zeh ve-Torato amla lo be-makom acher* — you labor here, and your *Torah* labors for you somewhere else.

*Amar Rabbi Eliezer kol adam le-amal nivra* — every person is created for labor. The verse in *Iyov*: *ki adam le-amal yulad*. The *Gemara* asks: does this mean labor of the mouth or physical labor? Refining further: if it means the mouth, does it mean *Torah* or ordinary speech? The answer builds through a chain: *lo yamush sefer ha-Torah ha-zeh mipicha* — let this *sefer Torah* not depart from your mouth — resolving that the labor of the mouth means *Torah*. And *Rava* adds: *kulhu gufei deruftaki ninhu* — all people are laborers in some sense. *Tovei le-dizakei de-havei deruftaki d'Oraisa* — fortunate are those who merit that they become laborers in *Torah*.

### Lishmah

It is striking that a *sugya* ostensibly about *Torah min ha-shamayim* has given so much space to *chazarah*, forgetting, the labor of *Torah*, and singing. The *Gemara* is less interested in dogma as a propositional category and more in the lived relationship one cultivates with *Torah* — whether one values it enough to review it, invest in it, and let it permeate one's life.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נֹאֵף אִשָּׁה חֲסַר לֵב״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: זֶה הַלּוֹמֵד תּוֹרָה לִפְרָקִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כִּי נָעִים כִּי תִשְׁמְרֵם בְּבִטְנֶךָ יִכֹּנוּ יַחְדָּו עַל שְׂפָתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"He who commits adultery with a woman lacks understanding"** (Proverbs 6:32). **Reish Lakish says: This is** a reference to **one who studies Torah intermittently,** who is like an adulterer, who sins with the other woman intermittently, **as it is stated** about words of Torah: **"For it is a pleasant thing if you keep them within your belly; let them be established on your lips"** (Proverbs 22:18) and keep the Torah always available.

*Ve-no'ef isha chaser lev, amar Reish Lakish*: *zeh ha-lomed Torah l'prakim* — this refers to one who learns *Torah* intermittently, when convenient, every now and then. The verse is about the adulterer who lacks heart, and Resh Lakish maps it onto the person whose relationship to *Torah* is not like a marriage — ongoing, committed, primary — but like an affair: occasional, disconnected, without depth. A deep and sustaining relationship with *Torah* requires constancy. *Ki na'im ki tishmerem be-vitnecha yikonu yachdav al sfatecha* — they are pleasant when you keep them in your belly; they will be established well on your lips.

## Menashe Example

### Menashe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשֶׂה בְּיָד רָמָה״ – זֶה מְנַשֶּׁה בֶּן חִזְקִיָּה, שֶׁהָיָה יוֹשֵׁב וְדוֹרֵשׁ בְּהַגָּדוֹת שֶׁל דּוֹפִי.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that with regard to the verse: **"But the person who acts high-handedly,** whether he is born in the land, or a stranger, that person blasphemes the Lord" (Numbers 15:30), **this** is a reference to **Manasseh ben Hezekiah,** king of Israel, **who would sit and teach flawed** interpretations of Torah **narratives.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: וְכִי לֹא הָיָה לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה לִכְתּוֹב אֶלָּא ״וַאֲחוֹת לוֹטָן תִּמְנָע״, ״וְתִמְנַע הָיְתָה פִילֶגֶשׁ לֶאֱלִיפַז״, ״וַיֵּלֶךְ רְאוּבֵן בִּימֵי קְצִיר חִטִּים וַיִּמְצָא דוּדָאִים בַּשָּׂדֶה״? יָצְאָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: ״תֵּשֵׁב בְּאָחִיךָ תְדַבֵּר בְּבֶן אִמְּךָ תִּתֶּן דֹּפִי. אֵלֶּה עָשִׂיתָ וְהֶחֱרַשְׁתִּי דִּמִּיתָ הֱיוֹת אֶהְיֶה כָמוֹךְ אוֹכִיחֲךָ וְאֶעֶרְכָה לְעֵינֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Manasseh **said: But did Moses need to write only** insignificant matters that teach nothing, for example: **"And Lotan's sister was Timna"** (Genesis 36:22), or: **"And Timna was concubine to Eliphaz,** son of Esau" (Genesis 36:12), or: **"And Reuben went in the days of the wheat harvest and found *duda'im* in the field"** (Genesis 30:14)? **A Divine Voice emerged and said to him: "You sit and speak against your brother; you slander your own mother's son. These things you have done, and should I have kept silence, you would imagine that I was like you, but I will reprove you, and set the matter before your eyes"** (Psalms 50:20–21). The verses in the Torah are not empty matters, with regard to which you can decide their import.

*Ve-ha-nefesh asher ta'aseh be-yad rama* — and the soul that acts with a high hand. *Zeh Menashe ben Chizkiyah* — this refers to Menashe ben Chizkiyah. *She-haya yoshev ve-doresh b'agadot shel dofi* — he would sit and expound on teachings that he considered foolish or degraded. He would say: did Moshe have nothing better to write than *va-achot Lotan Timna ve-Timna hayta pilegesh l'Elifaz* — that Lotan's sister was Timna and Timna was the concubine of Elifaz? What is the need for all these genealogies?

There is a question as to exactly which verses bother him. Perhaps it is the entire genealogical section of Esav's descendants. The common thread between the Timna passage and the mandrakes — *vayeilech Reuven bimei ketzir chitim vayimtza dudaim basadeh* — may be a concern about passages that seem to deal with sexual or carnal themes (*pilegesh*, the aphrodisiac properties attributed to mandrakes). But whether his complaint is about irrelevance, impropriety, or simply the apparent mundaneness of certain passages, his essential posture is dismissive: why does this belong in the *Torah*?

### Divine Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעָלָיו מְפוֹרָשׁ בַּקַּבָּלָה: ״הוֹי מֹשְׁכֵי הֶעָוֹן בְּחַבְלֵי הַשָּׁוְא וְכַעֲבוֹת הָעֲגָלָה חַטָּאָה״. מַאי ״כַּעֲבוֹת הָעֲגָלָה״? אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: יֵצֶר הָרָע בַּתְּחִלָּה דּוֹמֶה לְחוּט שֶׁל כּוּבְיָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף דּוֹמֶה לַעֲבוֹת הָעֲגָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And about** Manasseh ben Hezekiah **it is stated explicitly in the** texts of **tradition,** the Prophets: **"Woe unto them who draw iniquity with cords of vanity, and sin as with a cart rope"** (Isaiah 5:18). **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"as with a cart rope"? Rabbi Asi says:** This is a reference to **the evil inclination. Initially, it seems like** a flimsy **spinning [*kuveya*] thread and ultimately it seems like** a sturdy **cart rope.**

*Yatzta bat kol ve'amra lo* — a heavenly voice came out and said to him: *teshev be-achicha tedaber, beven imcha titen dofi* — will you sit and speak against your brother, will you ascribe foolishness to your mother's son? *Eileh asita ve-hecherashti, dimita hayot eheyeh chamocha* — you have done this and I was silent, and you thought that my silence meant I was with you. *Ochichecha* — I will now rebuke you, and *ve-erecha l'einecha* — I will lay my argument before your eyes.

The *bat kol*'s rebuke carries a powerful point about silence as perceived complicity. Because God did not immediately punish Menashe for his dismissiveness, Menashe assumed endorsement. The verse captures the danger of interpreting divine patience as divine agreement.

*Ve-alav meforash ba-kabalah*: the prophets make explicit what happens to such a person — *hoi moshchei he'avon be-chavlei hashav uche'avot ha'agalah chata'ah* — woe to those who drag iniquity with ropes of nothingness and like the cords of a wagon drag sin. *Amar Rabbi Assi*: *yeitzer hara betichilah domeh la-chut shel kuvya* — the evil inclination initially is like a spider's thread. *Ul'vasof domeh ke'avot ha'agalah* — but if allowed to continue, it thickens into ropes as heavy as a wagon's cord.

The principle illustrated is: Menashe began with what might seem like a relatively modest position — asking why certain *pesukim* are necessary. But that attitude, if unchecked, grows. It is one thing to ask, in genuine curiosity, what the purpose is of a particular passage. It is something else entirely to declare it foolish or beneath inclusion in the *Torah*. That posture of dismissal can metastasize into something far darker. Small disrespect, left unchallenged, becomes deep rejection.

### Timna Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּאֲתַן עֲלַהּ, מִיהַת, ״אֲחוֹת לוֹטָן תִּמְנָע״ מַאי הִיא? תִּמְנַע בַּת מְלָכִים הֲוַאי, דִּכְתִיב: ״אַלּוּף לוֹטָן״, ״אַלּוּף תִּמְנָע״, וְכׇל אַלּוּף – מַלְכוּתָא בְּלָא תָּאגָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Manasseh began by mocking a few verses and ultimately violated the entire Torah. The Gemara asks: With regard to that verse **that we came to** discuss, **in any event, what is** the significance of the phrase in the verse **"And Lotan's sister was Timna"?** The Gemara explains: **Timna was the daughter of kings, as it is written: "The chief of Lotan"** (Genesis 36:29), and: **"The chief of Timna"** (Genesis 36:40), **and each chief is** a member of **a monarchy,** albeit **without a crown.** That is why they are called chief and not king.

*De'ata le-mai achot Lotan Timna, mai hi?* The *Gemara* now explains the *purpose* of those verses that Menashe mocked. *Timna bat melachim havaia* — Timna was a daughter of kings: *aluf Lotan, aluf Timna* — and every *aluf* is a form of royalty, a king without a crown. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֲיָא לְאִיגַּיּוֹרֵי. בָּאתָה אֵצֶל אַבְרָהָם, יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב, וְלֹא קִבְּלוּהָ. הָלְכָה וְהָיְתָה פִילֶגֶשׁ לֶאֱלִיפַז בֶּן עֵשָׂו. אָמְרָה: מוּטָב תְּהֵא שִׁפְחָה לְאוּמָּה זוֹ וְלֹא תְּהֵא גְּבִירָה לְאוּמָּה אַחֶרֶת. נְפַק מִינַּהּ עֲמָלֵק, דְּצַעֲרִינְהוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּלָא אִיבְּעִי לְהוּ לְרַחוֹקַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Timna **sought to convert. She came before Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and they did not accept her. She went and became a concubine of Eliphaz, son of Esau, and said,** referring to herself: **It is preferable that she will be a maidservant for this nation, and she will not be a noblewoman for another nation.** Ultimately, **Amalek,** son of Eliphaz, **emerged from her,** and that tribe **afflicted the Jewish people. What is the reason** that the Jewish people were punished by suffering at the hand of Amalek? It is due to the fact **that they should not have rejected her** when she sought to convert. Therefore, the verse is significant.

*Ba'aya l'igurei* — she wished to convert. *Atai eitzel Avraham, Yitzchak ve-Yaakov, ve-lo kibluha* — she came to Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, and they refused to accept her.

*Halacha ve-hayta pilegesh l'Elifaz ben Esau* — she then became a concubine to Elifaz ben Esav. *Amra mutav shetehei shifchah l'umah zo ve-lo tehei gvirah l'umah acheret* — better to be a maidservant to this nation than a great woman in any other nation. *Nafak minah Amalek* — and from her came Amalek, the nation that attacked Israel and represents the archetype of evil in Jewish history.

*Mai ta'ama? De-lo ibei lehu le-rachakah* — why did Amalek emerge from her? Because the Avot should not have pushed her away. Rashi is explicit: *she-haya lahem le-gayera* — they should have converted her. This is a striking and oft-cited *Gemara* on the consequences of turning away sincere converts. Timna had no malice; she sought to attach herself to the Jewish people with great devotion. The responsibility for what emerged from her rejection lies, the *Gemara* says, with those who sent her away.

### Mandrakes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֵּלֶךְ רְאוּבֵן בִּימֵי קְצִיר חִטִּים״. אָמַר רָבָא בְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַב: מִכָּאן לְצַדִּיקִים שֶׁאֵין פּוֹשְׁטִין יְדֵיהֶן בְּגָזֵל. ״וַיִּמְצָא דּוּדָאִים בַּשָּׂדֶה״. מַאי דּוּדָאִים? אָמַר רַב: יַבְרוּחֵי. לֵוִי אָמַר: סִיגְלֵי. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אָמַר: סְבִיסְקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **"And Reuben went in the days of the wheat harvest"** (Genesis 30:14). **Rava, son of Rabbi Yitzḥak, says** that **Rav says: From here** it can be seen **that the righteous do not extend their hands** to engage **in robbery** even of small items, as rather than taking wheat, Reuben took only the ownerless *duda'im*. The verse continues: **"And he found *duda'im* in the field."** The Gemara asks: **What are *duda'im*? Rav says:** They are a plant called ***yavruḥei*. Levi says:** They are **violets. Rabbi Yonatan says:** They are ***seviskei*.**

*Vayelech Reuven bimei ketzir chitim* — and Reuven went during the wheat harvest. *Amar Rava bar Rav Yitzchak Amar Rav*: the point of this verse is to show us the virtue of the righteous — *mitovatan shel tzadikim she'ein poshtim yedeihem be-gazal* — they do not extend their hands in theft. Rashi explains: after the field had been harvested, it was customary and acceptable for others to enter. Reuven went precisely at that time, making clear that he was not trespassing or taking what was not his.

*Vayimtza dudaim ba-sadeh* — and he found *dudaim* in the field. *Mai dudaim?* Rav says *yavrochei*. Levi says *sigli* — Rashi identifies this as a violet. Rabbi Yonatan says *savisky*, some type of spice or flower. The exact botanical identification remains uncertain.

## Torah Power

### Peace and Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִי: כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בְּתוֹרָה לִשְׁמָהּ מֵשִׂים שָׁלוֹם בְּפָמַלְיָא שֶׁל מַעְלָה וּבְפָמַלְיָא שֶׁל מַטָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אוֹ יַחֲזֵק בְּמָעֻזִּי יַעֲשֶׂה שָׁלוֹם לִי שָׁלוֹם יַעֲשֶׂה לִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the significance of Torah study, **Rabbi Alexandri says: Anyone who engages in** the study of **Torah for its own sake introduces peace into the** heavenly **entourage above and into the** earthly **entourage below, as it is stated: "Or let him take hold of My stronghold [*ma'uzi*], that he may make peace with Me; and he shall make peace with Me"** (Isaiah 27:5). One who observes the Torah, which is called *oz*, introduces peace, even before the presence of God, as it were.

*Amar Rabbi Alexandri*: *kol ha-osek ba-Torah lishmah* — whoever occupies himself with *Torah* for its own sake, *mashim shalom be-pamalia shel ma'ala u-ve-pamalia shel mata* — makes peace in the family above and the family below, in heaven and on earth. The verse: *o yachazek be-maozi ya'aseh shalom li shalom ya'aseh li* — two instances of peace, one above and one below.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: כְּאִילּוּ בָּנָה פַּלְטֵרִין שֶׁל מַעְלָה וְשֶׁל מַטָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאָשִׂים דְּבָרַי בְּפִיךָ וּבְצֵל יָדִי כִּסִּיתִיךָ לִנְטֹעַ שָׁמַיִם וְלִיסֹד אָרֶץ״. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף מֵגֵין עַל כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְצֵל יָדִי כִּסִּיתִיךָ״. וְלֵוִי אָמַר: אַף מְקָרֵב אֶת הַגְּאוּלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֵאמֹר לְצִיּוֹן עַמִּי אָתָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** It is **as though he built a palace of** heaven **above and of** earth **below, as it is stated: "And I have placed My words in your mouth, and I have covered you in the shadow of My hand, to plant the heavens and lay the foundations of the earth,** and say to Zion, you are My people" (Isaiah 51:16). One who has the word of God placed in his mouth through Torah study has established heaven and earth. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** One who engages in Torah study **also protects the entire world, as it is stated: "And I have covered you in the shadow of My hand." And Levi says: He also advances** the coming of **the redemption, as it is stated: "And say to Zion, you are My people."**

*Rava amar*: *ke-ilu bana paltarin shel ma'ala ve-shel mata* — it is as if he built a palace above and below. The verse: *va-asim devarai be-ficha, ub-tzel yadi kisiticha linto'a shamayim ve-lisod aretz* — I have placed My words in your mouth and covered you in the shadow of My hand, to plant the heavens and to found the earth. True love and connection to *Torah* simultaneously serves God and sustains this world.

*Rabbi Yochanan amar*: *af megin al kol ha-olam kulo* — moreover, it protects the entire world. *Ub-tzel yadi kisiticha* — in the shadow of My hand I have covered you. The learning of *Torah* is the opposite of *mevaze Devar Hashem*; it is what keeps the world protected and sustained.

*Levi amar*: *af me-karev et ha-geulah* — it brings the redemption closer. *Ve-lomar le-Tzion ami atah* — and to say to Zion: you are My people.

### Teaching Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הַמְלַמֵּד אֶת בֶּן חֲבֵירוֹ תּוֹרָה, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ עֲשָׂאוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵת הַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר עָשׂוּ בְחָרָן״. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כְּאִילּוּ עֲשָׂאָן לְדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם אֶת דִּבְרֵי הַבְּרִית הַזֹּאת וַעֲשִׂיתֶם אֹתָם״. רָבָא אָמַר: כְּאִילּוּ עֲשָׂאוֹ לְעַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם אֹתָם״ – אַל תִּקְרֵי ״אֹתָם״ אֶלָּא ״אַתֶּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said:** With regard to **anyone who teaches Torah to the son of another, the verse ascribes him** credit **as though he formed** that student, **as it is stated:** "And Abram took Sarai his wife…**and the souls that they formed in Haran"** (Genesis 12:5). They are given credit for forming the students to whom they taught Torah. **Rabbi Elazar says:** It is **as though he fashioned [*asa'an*] the words of Torah** themselves, **as it is stated: "Observe the words of this covenant, *va'asitem otam*"** (Deuteronomy 29:8), indicating that studying the Torah is like fashioning it. **Rava says:** It is **as though he fashioned himself, as it is stated: "*Va'asitem otam*." Do not read** "*va'asitem **otam*****"** as: And you shall fashion them; **rather,** read it as *va'asitem **atem*****,** meaning: You shall fashion yourself.

*Amar Resh Lakish*: *kol ha-melamed et ben chavero Torah* — whoever teaches his friend's son *Torah* — *ma'ala alav ha-katuv ke-ilu asa'o* — the verse treats him as though he created that person, giving him a spiritual life. The verse: *ve-et ha-nefesh asher asu ve-Charan* — the souls that they made in Charan. By spreading God's word and drawing a person into *Torah*, one participates in a kind of spiritual birth.

*Rabbi Eleazar amar*: *ke-ilu asan l'divrei Torah* — it is as if you made the words of *Torah* themselves. By loving *Torah* enough to spread it, you help bring *Torah* into actual existence in the world.

*Ve-Rava amar*: *ke-ilu asa'o l'atzmo* — it is as if you made yourself. In teaching and spreading *Torah*, you transform yourself in the process. *Va-asitem otam — al tikrei otam ela atem* — do not read "them" but "yourselves."

### Encouraging Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: כׇּל הַמְעַשֶּׂה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ לִדְבַר מִצְוָה, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ עֲשָׂאָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמַטְּךָ אֲשֶׁר הִכִּיתָ בּוֹ אֶת הַיְאֹר״. וְכִי מֹשֶׁה הִכָּהוּ? וַהֲלֹא אַהֲרֹן הִכָּהוּ! אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ: כׇּל הַמְעַשֶּׂה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ לִדְבַר מִצְוָה, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ עֲשָׂאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says:** With regard to **anyone who causes another to** engage in **a matter of a mitzva, the verse ascribes him** credit **as though he performed it** himself, **as it is stated:** "And the Lord said to Moses…**and your rod, with which you struck the river,** take in your hand and go" (Exodus 17:5). **And** was it **Moses** who **struck** the river? **But isn't** it written explicitly (see Exodus 7:19–20) that **Aaron struck** the river? **Rather,** that verse serves **to say to you: Anyone who causes another to** engage in **a matter of a mitzva, the verse ascribes him** credit **as though he performed it** himself.

*Amar Rabbi Abbahu*: *kol ha-ma'aseh et chavero le-dvar mitzvah* — whoever encourages or compels his friend to perform a *mitzvah* — *ma'ala alav ha-katuv ke-ilu asa'o* — it is as though he himself performed it. The proof: *u-matcha asher hikita bo et ha-Ye'or* — your staff with which you struck the Nile. But did *Moshe* strike the Nile? It was *Aharon* who struck! Rather, this teaches that because *Moshe* directed and enabled *Aharon*, the act is attributed to him. The one who inspires another to do a *mitzvah* receives the credit as though he had done it himself.

The *daf* closes here. That transformation of the term belongs to the Rambam, and it is a transformation the *Gemara* itself does not recognize.
