# Bava Batra 116 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bnot Tzelofchad & Binyam…

### Yerushah & Binyamin

The daf opens with a *pasuk* from *Shoftim*: *vayomeru yerushat pleitah l'Vinyamin v'lo yimacheh shevet miYisrael* — "And they said: an inheritance for the remnant of Binyamin, so that a tribe shall not be wiped out from Israel." The historical backdrop is the aftermath of *Pilegesh b'Givah*, when only six hundred men remained of the tribe of Binyamin. The other tribes arranged for their daughters to be taken by the men of Binyamin so that the tribe could repopulate. This episode also echoes the principle stated on the previous *daf* — *d'lo khalah shivta* — that no tribe will ever be completely destroyed. Here it nearly happened, and the *pasuk* records their determination to prevent it.

The word *yerushah* — inheritance — is striking given the tractate's subject matter. What does it mean to speak of an "inheritance" for the remnant of Binyamin?

### Takanah: Daughters & Sons

Rabbi Yitzchak of the school of Rabbi Ami explains: *melamed she-hitnu al shevet Binyamin she-lo tirash bat haben im ha'ahim* — this teaches that they enacted a *takanah* for the tribe of Binyamin that a son's daughter would not inherit together with his brothers.

To appreciate the force of this enactment, it is necessary to recall the general rule of inheritance: if a son has died, his children — even daughters — step into his place and inherit together with the surviving brothers. That is the case of *Bnot Tzelofchad*, which the Gemara has already established. The *takanah* for Binyamin was a suspension of that principle. Since only six hundred men survived, any portion of tribal land that passed to a woman risked leaving the tribe's holdings, as a woman who married outside the tribe would take her inheritance with her. Rather than allowing a son's daughter to inherit alongside her uncles, the *takanah* directed that only sons — not daughters — would step into a deceased father's place.

This was not a permanent abolition of daughters' rights, but a temporary measure to slow the potential outflow of property from the tribe until it could repopulate. Even so, the *takanah* is of limited effectiveness, since property could pass to a son who then dies leaving only a daughter, at which point the same problem recurs a generation later. The Gemara acknowledges this and treats the enactment as more modest in scope: it did not say that property would *never* pass to a female heir in any circumstances, but only that, where brothers exist to receive it, a son's daughter would not displace them.

### Tzelofchad's Daughters

The general rule, against which this *takanah* stands in contrast, is the inheritance of the daughters of Tzelofchad. Tzelofchad was a son of Chefer, and Chefer had other sons as well. When Tzelofchad died without male heirs, one might have thought his portion would go only to his surviving brothers — after all, they are alive and at the same generational level. One might further suppose that descending a generation to find an heir is appropriate only when there is *no one* remaining at the current level.

The Gemara rejects this. Even in the presence of the brothers, Tzelofchad's daughters step into their father's place and inherit alongside their uncles. His daughters are not competing with the brothers as daughters; they are representing their father. They inherit *as* Tzelofchad, taking the portion that would have been his had he lived.

### Sons' Descendants Priori…

This leads to a broader principle: a son's descendants — even daughters — take priority over a daughter. The reason is that before passing to a daughter in the inheritance chart, one must first exhaust all descendants of the sons. The male line is followed as far down as necessary, and it is only when there are truly no descendants to be found anywhere in that line that the inheritance moves to a daughter.

Furthermore, the *takanah* of Binyamin also sheds light on the reverse concern. Normally, even if those descendants are female, they precede the daughters of the original owner. The Binyamin *takanah* temporarily overrode this to prevent property from leaving the tribe — but in doing so, it implicitly confirmed that under ordinary law, daughters-replacing-sons would indeed inherit even in the presence of uncles. That is the ordinary rule; the Binyamin enactment was an acknowledged departure from it, sensitive to the reality that when property passes to a woman it is liable to leave the patrilineal tribe.

## No Son = God's Anger

### Kol she'eino maniach ben

Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai: *kol she'eino maniach ben leyoresho, HaKadosh Baruch Hu malei alav evrah* — whoever does not leave behind a son to inherit him, the Holy One, Blessed be He, is filled with wrath against that person. The Gemara derives this from the language of the *pesukim*: the word *havartem* — "you shall transfer" the *nachala* to his daughter — is read as *evrah*, wrath. And a further verse reinforces this: *asher ein chalifot lamo* — there is no substitute or exchange for him — meaning he has left no son who serves as his continuation, *velo yiru Elohim* — and they will not see God.

This is a striking statement. There is an obvious tension: the person who died without a son presumably did not choose that fate. And if one views dying without a son as a mark of divine anger, it creates a troubling implication for those who mourn. Yet the Gemara's tone is not simply punitive; the language of *evrah* seems to be expressing how deeply disruptive — how contrary to the proper order of things — it is when there is no continuation of the person. In a profoundly patriarchal context, the absence of a son meant that the name, the property, and the identity of the father came to an end.

### Son vs. Student Dispute

The Gemara records a dispute between Rav Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi about the precise meaning of this teaching. *Chad amar*: it refers to one who does not leave behind a son — a biological heir. *Vechad amar*: it refers to one who does not leave behind a *talmid* — a student, a spiritual heir. The concept of the *talmid* as a form of son is well-established: *velimadetem et beneichem* — *beneichem elu talmideichem* — "your sons" refers to your students. A *rav* functions as a father and a student as a son, and there are practical *halachot* that reflect this, such as the principle that returning a lost object to one's *rav* takes precedence over returning it to one's father.

The Gemara then attempts to determine which sage held which position: *tistayeim* — let us conclude — that Rav Yochanan is the one who says the teaching refers to a *talmid*.

### Resolution & Rav Yochanan

The proof is from a well-known story in *Berachot*: Rav Yochanan would carry with him a small bone — the *Rashbam* identifies it as a tooth, since a detached tooth does not convey *tumah*, which would have been a concern for *kohanim* — and would use it to console those who had lost a child. He would say: know how much I myself have suffered — this is the bone of my tenth son who died. All ten of Rav Yochanan's sons predeceased him.

Now, Rav Yochanan did not leave any surviving sons. If he were the one who taught that God is filled with wrath toward one who does not leave a son, he would hardly use that teaching to comfort mourners — it would compound their grief rather than console them. The *Rashbam* explains that the proof lies not just in the fact that it happened to him, but that he specifically employed this as a source of comfort. Therefore, *tistayeim de-amar talmid* — Rav Yochanan is the one who says the teaching is about failing to leave behind a *talmid*. God's wrath is directed at one who has not transmitted Torah to a spiritual heir. Dying without a biological son, by contrast, is a tragedy — but not necessarily a mark of divine anger.

This then reverses the attribution: it is Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi who says the teaching refers to a biological son. And indeed, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi would visit a *shiva* house only when a person had died leaving no sons — *ela lebei ma deshachiv bela benei* — because he considered that the true tragedy warranting special mourning. The verse he cites: *becho vacho leholech* — weep for the one who goes — and Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *leholech bela ben zachar* — for the one who departs without leaving a male son.

Here is where the reasoning becomes subtle. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi frames dying without a son as a tragedy, not as something that invites blame. Precisely because he views it this way, he would *not* say that God is angry at such a person — anger implies fault, and he does not see fault here. Therefore it is he who says God's anger is reserved for one who leaves no *talmid*, where there may be an element of personal failing. Rav Yochanan, then, is the one who says God is angry at the one who leaves no son, since his formulation — transmitted in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai — ties the phrase *ben leyoresho* to the language of *evrah*.

This creates an apparent contradiction within Rav Yochanan himself: he both teaches this statement in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai and comforts mourners using his own experience of having no sons. The Gemara resolves it: *lo kashya — ha didei, ha d'rabbih* — there is no contradiction. His own view (*didei*) accords with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, that God is angry only at one who leaves no *talmid*. What he transmitted in the name of his teacher, Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai, is a different, stricter position — that God's wrath applies to one who leaves no son. It remains striking that Rav Yochanan would transmit in his teacher's name a ruling of such personal poignancy, yet that is the nature of faithfully reporting received tradition.

The conclusion is that Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai held that God is angry at one who does not leave a son; later *amoraim* — both Rav Yochanan in his own view and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — somewhat softened the emphasis, directing the concept of divine anger toward one who leaves no spiritual heir.

## Midrash: David & Yoav

### David & Yoav Drasha

The Gemara turns from *halacha* to *aggadata*, though still within the thematic orbit of leaving behind an heir. Rabbi Pinchas ben Chama expounded the verse from *Melachim*: *V'Hadad shama b'Mitzrayim ki shakhav David im avotav* — Hadad heard in Egypt that David had lain with his fathers — *v'ki met Yoav sar hatzava* — and that Yoav the general had died. The *drasha* notes an asymmetry in language: regarding David it says *shakhav* — he lay down — a softer, more euphemistic expression; regarding Yoav it says *met* — he died, plainly. Why the difference?

*David she'heni'ach ben ne'emar bo shkhivah* — David left behind a son, so the text uses the language of lying down; he is not truly gone, his line and name continue. *Yoav shelo heni'ach ben ne'emar bo mitah* — Yoav left no son, so the text says he died; in a real sense, that name died with him.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: did Yoav not leave a son? Is it not written that there were *bnei Yoav*? The answer leads to a refinement of the *drasha*.

### Ben Kamoso Concept

The resolution is: *ela David she'hini'ach ben kamoso ne'emar bo shkhivah* — it is not merely leaving a biological son, but leaving *a son like oneself* — *ben kamoso* — a son who embodies one's values and continues one's spiritual legacy. David left a son who was in some meaningful sense a continuation of what David stood for. Yoav had biological descendants but not a *ben kamoso*, and so the text uses the language of death.

This formulation elegantly synthesizes the two sides of the earlier dispute. A *ben kamoso* is simultaneously a biological heir and a spiritual one — the one who continues the name, the values, and the path of the father. The earlier discussion about *talmid* as heir and the concept of *ולימדתם את בניכם — בניכם אלו תלמידיכם* both converge here. It is not enough to have sired a son, and it is not enough to have taught a student; the ideal is a son who is truly like you in what matters, who will carry forward both your identity and your substance.

### Poverty & Wisdom

Rabbi Pinchas bar Chama continues with additional *drashos*. He taught: *kashe aniyut b'soch beiso shel adam p'amim yoter mikhamishim makah* — poverty in a person's home is more difficult than fifty plagues. The derivation follows the method familiar from the *Haggadah*: the verse in *Iyov* says *ki yad Eloh nig'ah bi* — the hand of God has struck me — and since a hand is equivalent to five times what a finger (*etzba*) represents, and *etzba* equals ten plagues, therefore a *yad* equals fifty. His companions then say to him: *ki al zeh bacharta mei'oni* — this is preferable to poverty — meaning even all that Job suffered was considered preferable to destitution.

Rabbi Pinchas bar Chama also taught: if there is a sick person in one's house who may be dying, one should go to a wise man and ask him to pray for mercy on that person's behalf — *ish chacham yikhaprena* — a wise man will atone for it or intercede. The verse is from *Mishlei*: *chamat melech malakhei mavet* — the king's wrath brings angels of death — but a wise man can avert it. It is notable that the text here specifies a *chacham* — a wise person — rather than a *chasid* or a particularly righteous individual, suggesting that wisdom itself, in the Talmudic conception, confers a special proximity to divine favor.

## General Inheritance Rules

### Son, Daughter, Father

The Gemara now returns to the substance of the *mishnah* and its general formulation of inheritance order. The *klal* stated is: *kol hakodem benachala, yotzei yerecho kodmin, v'av kodem lechol yotzei yerecho* — whoever takes precedence in inheritance, his descendants take precedence before him; and the father takes precedence over all of his descendants.

At the most basic level, the order is: sons first, then daughters, then the father. That is the structure of the entire chart, and everything else derives from it.

### Heir Depth Question

A natural question arises: how far down a generational line must one search before concluding that there are no descendants and moving to the next category? One might have thought that the rule of replacing a deceased heir with his own children applies only within the first generation. But the *Rambam* and the *Shulchan Aruch* treat the principle as extending without limit, and the *Rashbam* in his comprehensive summary at the end of the *daf* confirms this explicitly, taking it as an established rule that one always follows the male line downward before moving laterally or upward in the chart.

### Males Over Females

At every level, males are preferred over females. The Gemara establishes this through the principle of *maki yerusha shniya liyerusha rishona* — the second inheritance is compared to the first. Just as at the primary level men inherit before women, so too at any subsequent level where descendants are standing in for their parent, male descendants are preferred over female descendants.

The concrete case is *bnei achot velo bnot achot* — nephews inherit before nieces. One might have thought that since they are all standing in the same position — children of the deceased's sister, for instance — sex would not be a differentiating factor. But the Gemara says no: the preference for males applies at every level of descent, by analogy to the primary level.

This means that, in a case where a man dies and the inheritance passes through multiple generations, a great-great-granddaughter in the male line may inherit together with a surviving daughter of the deceased — or even before other women who are closer in generation — because she is taking her male ancestor's place, and that place carries priority over the female line at the same or even higher generational level. The *Rashbam* states this explicitly: *kach seder nachala* — this is the order of inheritance. The rule that males precede females is applied at every node in the descent, not only at the top.

## Grandfather vs. Uncle

### Avi ha'av Question

Rami bar Chama posed a question: *avi ha'av ve'achei ha'av* — the grandfather versus the paternal uncles. He framed it using the biblical cast: *Avraham veYishmael benichsei Eisav* — Avraham and Yishmael with respect to the property of Eisav. Eisav is dead, Yitzchak is dead, Yaakov is dead. Who inherits? Does it go to Avraham, Eisav's grandfather, or to Yishmael, Eisav's uncle?

The question presupposes that Eisav had no children and no direct descendants, so the inheritance must move elsewhere on the chart. Once we are past the direct descendants of the deceased, does the principle *av kodem lechol yotzei yerecho* — the father takes precedence over all of his descendants — mean that the grandfather takes precedence even over the uncle?

### Rava vs. Rami Answer

Rava initially answered: *ta shma — ha'av kodem lechol yotzei yerecho* — the father takes precedence over all his descendants. He read this phrase to mean that Avraham, as grandfather, would come before Yishmael. On a literal reading, *av* who is higher in the chart should precede any of his own descendants — and Yishmael is after all a descendant of Avraham.

Rami bar Chama then refined the question: *Avraham veYaakov benichsei Eisav* — replacing Yishmael with Yaakov. Now the question becomes whether the grandfather Avraham takes precedence over his grandchild Yaakov. Rava's answer of *av kodem lechol yotzei yerecho* would, taken literally, give it to Avraham. But the Gemara comments: *Rami bar Chama agav churpei lo ayin bah* — because of Rami bar Chama's characteristic sharpness, he posed a speculative question without noticing that it was already essentially answered by the *mishnah*. And indeed, once the Gemara presses on the meaning of the phrase, a problem emerges.

If *av kodem lechol yotzei yerecho* means the father precedes *all* descendants at every remove, it would produce an absurd result: Eisav's own children could never inherit before Yitzchak, since Yitzchak — as father — would always come first. But clearly the children of the deceased are the *first* heirs. The phrase cannot be read that absolutely.

### Direct Descendants First

Rami bar Chama therefore explains: *yotzei yerecho shelo velo yotzei yerecho shel beno* — *yotzei yerecho* means only the father's own first-generation descendants, not the descendants of his son. The father comes before his own children — that is the content of *av kodem lechol yotzei yerecho*. But the father does not come before his son's descendants.

The Gemara confirms that this reading is the more sensible one: *hachi nami mistavra*. The *klal* in the *mishnah* says: *kol hakodem benachala yotzei yerecho kodmin* — whoever takes precedence, his descendants take precedence. If Yitzchak were alive, he would precede Yaakov. Since Yitzchak is not alive, the rule kicks in: before moving elsewhere on the chart, look for Yitzchak's descendants. Yaakov is Yitzchak's descendant, so Yaakov inherits — taking Yitzchak's place — before the chart moves up to Avraham.

The governing logic is now clear: before you move from one position on the chart to another, you must first exhaust all direct descendants of the person currently at that position. So if Yitzchak would have inherited but is not alive, you look for Yitzchak's children, then Yitzchak's grandchildren, and so forth, before ever moving up to Avraham. Only when Yitzchak has no descendants at all does the inheritance rise to Avraham; and then, if Avraham is not alive, one looks for Avraham's descendants before moving further.

Interestingly, the underlying structure here parallels the logic of *yibum*: there is an idea of a person standing in for another, of continuity and replacement, of a living individual taking the place of one who is gone so that the name and portion are not lost. The *drasha* that the daughters of Tzelofchad inherit because they replace their father — derived from the phrase *va'avatem et nachalato levito* — uses similar interpretive moves.

## Rules Summary & Examples

### Three Essential Rules

The Gemara's extended discussion collapses into a compact set of rules. The first and most fundamental is the chart itself: *ben, bat, av* — son, daughter, father. Those three positions and that order are all one needs to know; everything else is derived. If a man dies, his estate goes to his sons. If there are no sons, it goes to his daughters. If there are no daughters, it goes to his father. If the father is gone, the process iterates: it goes to the father's sons (brothers of the deceased), then daughters of the father (sisters of the deceased), then the father's father, and so on up.

The second rule is that before moving from any position on the chart to the next, one must look for direct descendants of the person who holds that position. If a son would have inherited but is dead, his children — not the daughter of the deceased — take his place. And that process continues: if those children are dead, their children substitute, and so on without limit.

The third rule is that males are always preferred over females at any given level of descent, by the principle of *maki yerusha shniya liyerusha rishona*.

Together, these three rules generate the entire inheritance system.

### Edge Cases & Exceptions

Several unusual cases are worth noting. First, the mother never inherits. She is simply absent from the chart entirely. In a case where a man dies leaving no children and no known or halachically cognizable father — for instance, where the father is a gentile, or where paternity is unknown — one might expect the estate to pass to the mother. The *Rama* rules otherwise: in such a case, the property becomes *hefker*, ownerless, as in the death of a *ger* without heirs. This is a striking result: the biological mother is passed over entirely, and the estate becomes ownerless rather than devolve to her.

Second, one cannot waive inheritance rights. The *poskim* address a case where a son had a bitter dispute with his mother and, before communal witnesses, performed a *kinyan* and formally declared that he renounced any claim to her estate. When the mother later died, he sought to inherit. The *poskim* held that the renunciation was void: the laws of inheritance are not *dinei mamonot* in the ordinary sense of a bilateral arrangement between parties, but a *chukat mishpat*, a fixed legal rule that operates automatically. One cannot contract out of it any more than one can contract out of secular inheritance law by private oath. The correct way to disinherit an heir is to write a will transferring property to another party before death; absent such a will, inheritance follows the fixed rules regardless of prior declarations.

In this particular case, however, because the community had imposed the renunciation as a form of fine or communal sanction, it was enforceable for that distinct reason — not because one can generally waive inheritance rights, but because communal authority to impose penalties is independent of the ordinary inheritance rules.

## Tzelofchad's Three Porti…

### Yotzei Mitzrayim Division

The *mishnah* states: *Bnot Tzelofchad natlu gimel chalakim benachala* — the daughters of Tzelofchad received three portions in the inheritance. To understand why, the *mishnah* invokes the first of several key principles: *leyotzei mitzrayim nechlekah ha'aretz* — the land of Israel was divided according to those who left Egypt, not according to those who entered the land.

The plain meaning of the verse *le'eileh techalek ha'aretz benachala* — "to these shall the land be apportioned as an inheritance" — might suggest that the division is determined by the generation that actually entered *Eretz Yisrael*. But the *halacha* as the Gemara presents it is that conceptually the division was structured around those who left Egypt. Every adult male who left Egypt received, as it were, a foundational share; those shares then passed through the rules of inheritance to whoever survived to enter the land and to their descendants.

The practical implication is significant. Two men leave Egypt, one with no children and one with many. In the *midbar*, the first man sires ten children and the second only one. When *Eretz Yisrael* is divided, the ten children share one portion between them and the one child receives an equal single portion — because the entitlement was fixed at the moment of the Exodus, not at the moment of entry. It is a retroactive conceptual inheritance: those who left Egypt are treated as having already acquired the land, and the physical division at the time of Yehoshua merely realizes that prior entitlement.

### Tzelofchad's Portions

Applying this principle: both Chefer and Tzelofchad were among *yotzei Mitzrayim* and therefore each received one foundational portion in the land. Tzelofchad's own portion — the one share he was entitled to as one who left Egypt — is the first of the three portions his daughters ultimately received.

When Chefer died, his estate — including his own *yotzei Mitzrayim* share — was divided among his sons. Tzelofchad was a *bechor*, a firstborn, and therefore received *pishnayim* — a double portion — from his father Chefer's estate. That means Tzelofchad received two portions of Chefer's land, while the remaining brothers divided the rest. These two portions constitute the second and third of the three portions that the daughters of Tzelofchad inherited.

### Daughters Get Bechor

This analysis contains an important *chidush*: the daughters of Tzelofchad inherited not only their father's personal *yotzei Mitzrayim* share but also his double portion as *bechor*. One might have thought that the right of *bekorah* — the double portion of the firstborn — would not pass through daughters, or that it would be lost when the firstborn dies before the division occurs. The *mishnah* teaches otherwise: since the daughters step into their father's place entirely, they receive everything he would have received, including the double portion of the firstborn.

This is the *chidush* on the law of *bekorah* specifically: even when the firstborn is no longer alive at the time of the division, his heirs — here, his daughters — receive the double portion that would have been his. The right of *bekorah* vests in the firstborn and passes through his estate to his heirs.

### Muchzekim Principle

This in turn requires the resolution of a further difficulty. The law states that a *bechor* does not take a double portion in property that is *ra'uy* — expected but not yet in the father's possession — only in property that is *muchzak* — already in the father's possession. *Eretz Yisrael* was never physically in Chefer's possession; he left Egypt and died in the desert. How then could Tzelofchad, as *bechor*, receive a double portion from Chefer's share of *Eretz Yisrael*?

The answer the Gemara asserts is: *Eretz Yisrael muchzeket hi* — the land of Israel is considered as already in possession. The entire framework of *yotzei Mitzrayim* implies not merely that the generation of the Exodus was *entitled* to the land, but that they are treated as having already acquired it, as already being *muchzak* in it. Chefer's portion in *Eretz Yisrael* was therefore considered to be in his possession from the moment of the Exodus conceptually, even though he died before physical entry. As a result, the laws of *bekorah* apply: Tzelofchad received a double portion, and that double portion passed to his daughters, giving them in total three portions — his own share, and a double share from his father Chefer's estate.
