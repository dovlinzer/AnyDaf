# Avodah Zarah 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Akiva's Three Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אִיקְּלַע לְגִינְזַק, בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ: מִתְעַנִּין לְשָׁעוֹת אוֹ אֵין מִתְעַנִּין לְשָׁעוֹת? לָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ. קַנְקַנִּים שֶׁל גּוֹיִם אֲסוּרִין אוֹ מוּתָּרִין? לָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ. בַּמֶּה שִׁימֵּשׁ מֹשֶׁה כׇּל שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הַמִּלּוּאִים? לָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Akiva happened to** come to the city of **Ginzak,** whose residents **asked him** three questions to which he did not know the answer: First, **does one fast for hours, or does one not fast for hours?** The answer to the question **was not available to** Rabbi Akiva. Second, are clay **jars** that **belong to gentiles** permanently **prohibited, or** can they be rendered **permitted?** The answer **was not available to him.** Third, **in what** garments **did Moses serve all seven days of** the Tabernacle's **inauguration,** as acting priest when Aaron and his sons were initiated into the priesthood? Moses presumably did not wear the priestly vestments, as he himself was not a priest. Once again, the answer **was not available to him.**

### Fasting for Hours

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא שְׁאֵל בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא, אָמְרִי: הִלְכְתָא מִתְעַנִּין לְשָׁעוֹת, וְאִם הִשְׁלִים — מִתְפַּלֵּל תְּפִלַּת תַּעֲנִית. וְהִלְכְתָא קַנְקַנִּים שֶׁל גּוֹיִם לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ מוּתָּרִין. בַּמֶּה שִׁימֵּשׁ מֹשֶׁה שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הַמִּלּוּאִים? בְּחָלוּק לָבָן. רַב כָּהֲנָא מַתְנֵי: בְּחָלוּק לָבָן שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אִימְּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **came** and **asked** these questions in the **study hall. They said to him:** The ***halakha*** is that **one fasts for hours, and if he completed** the fast **he prays the prayer of a fast. And** the ***halakha*** is that the **jars** that **belong to gentiles are permitted after** they have not been used for **twelve months.** Finally, **in what** garments **did Moses serve** during **the seven days of inauguration?** He did not serve in his own clothes, nor in the regular priestly vestments, but **in** a special **white cloak. Rav Kahana teaches:** Moses served **in a white cloak without a hem.**

The daf opens mid-discussion, picking up a few lines into 34a with the story of *Rabbi Akiva ikla l'Ginzak* — Rabbi Akiva's visit to Ginzak, where he was posed three questions. In each case, *lo hava b'yadei* — he did not hold the material and could not supply the *halacha* on the spot. The image is evocative: it would not have been sufficient for Rabbi Akiva simply to hazard an opinion; he would need to have the ruling firmly in hand, as a matter of received tradition.

The first question was *matnin l'shaot o ein mitanin l'shaot* — can one fast for hours, or must a fast be a full day? Rashi understands this as a question about whether one who incidentally did not eat in the morning may, upon reaching midday, formally accept a fast upon himself, thereby counting the entire day. There is, however, reason to read the question differently — in line with the *mishnayot* in Masechet Ta'anit concerning fast days that were called in the hope of rain. If rain fell during the afternoon, was one permitted to discontinue the fast at that point? The phrase *ve'im hishlim* — "and if he completed it" — appears to describe the scenario in which one might have terminated the fast early but chose not to, suggesting that the question concerns ending the fast before its conclusion rather than beginning it late. In any case, the *beit midrash* ruled *mitanin l'shaot*, and added that *ve'im hishlim yitpallel tefillat ta'anit* — if one did complete the fast, one recites the designated fast-day prayer, which in Tannaitic times meant the inclusion of additional *brachot* in the *Amidah*. Today this is reflected in the *Aneinu* paragraph. Conversely, one who does not complete the fast does not add those prayers.

This ruling opens an interesting side question regarding *Yom Kippur*: the *Shacharit* Torah reading on *Yom Kippur* is essentially a *Yom Tov* reading, reflecting the *kedushat hayom*, and there is general consensus that even one who is not fasting could receive an *aliyah* at *Shacharit*. The *Mincha* Torah reading, however, is more ambiguous — is it like a fast-day *Mincha* reading, which would require fasting, or like a Shabbat *Mincha* reading, which would not? This is a *machloket* between the *Shulchan Aruch* and the *Rema*, and Rabbi Akiva Eiger weighs in on the matter as well.

### Wine Jars (Kankanim)

The second question put to Rabbi Akiva was *kankanin shel ovdei kochavim asurim o mutarim* — are wine jars owned by idolaters prohibited or permitted? The question is understood to concern not their present status but whether they can be *kashered* and rendered permissible at all. Rabbi Akiva did not have the answer to this question either. The *beit midrash* ruled: *v'hilchta kankanin shel ovdei kochavim l'achar yud-bet chodesh mutarin* — the law is that such jars become permissible after twelve months of disuse, because whatever wine flavor was absorbed into the pottery becomes inconsequential over that span of time.

This ruling has broader implications. There is a lengthy discussion in *Yoreh Deah*, around simanim 121–122, and treated in the *Pitchei Teshuvah*, about whether porcelain and other ceramic vessels might similarly be *kashered* after twelve months, given that ordinarily we hold earthenware cannot be *kashered* by any means. The argument would be that after twelve months any absorbed flavor has fully degraded — through exposure to air, oxygen, or other environmental factors — such that there is simply nothing remaining. Of course, this remains a contested position and is not accepted *l'halacha* by many *poskim*. Moreover, for materials that can be *kashered* more readily — such as metal utensils, which require only immersion in boiling water — there would be little reason to resort to this more tenuous *kula*. The logic of the twelve-month leniency applies most naturally to cases where no other form of *kashering* is available, and even then, particularly given that the original prohibition of *stam yeinam* is itself *d'rabanan*, there are multiple layers of leniency operating simultaneously.

### Moshe's Clothing

The third question — *bameh shimmesh Moshe kol shivat yemei hamiluim*, what did Moshe wear throughout the seven days of the *miluim* — carries particular resonance given that Parashat Tetzaveh, which describes the inauguration of the *Kohanim*, was the week's *parashah*. During those seven days, Moshe Rabbeinu served as the *Kohen Gadol*, presiding over the induction of Aharon and his sons into the *Kehunah*. What he wore mattered, because it had to be clearly distinguishable from the *bigdei kehunah* worn by Aharon and his sons.

### Answers from Beit Midrash

The *beit midrash* answered: *b'chaluk lavan* — a plain white garment. And Rav Kahana later clarified, in the Amoraic period, that it was a white smock *she'ein bo imra* — one that lacked even an embroidered hem, so that no one would confuse it with the elaborate vestments of the *Kohen Gadol*. Thus the three questions encompassed an *Orach Chaim* matter, a *Yoreh Deah* matter, and a historical question about the *Beit Hamikdash* — none of which Rabbi Akiva could answer from his own knowledge, and all of which he resolved by consulting the *beit midrash*.

## Twelve-Month Drying Rule

### Grape Skins Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַחַרְצַנִּים וְהַזַּגִּים שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, לַחִין — אֲסוּרִין, יְבֵשִׁים — מוּתָּרִים. הֵי נִינְהוּ לַחִין וְהֵי נִינְהוּ יְבֵשִׁין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לַחִין — כׇּל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, יְבֵשִׁים — לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** Moist grape **seeds and grape skins** that **belong to gentiles** are **prohibited,** but **dry ones are permitted.** The Gemara asks: **Which are** considered **moist and which are** considered **dry? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** Grape residues are considered **moist** for **all** of the first **twelve months** after the grapes were pressed, and **dry after** the first **twelve months.**

The *Gemara* now presents a *Baraita*: *tanu rabbanan hachartzanim vehazagim shel ovdei kochavim* — the grape seeds and grape peels belonging to idolaters. *Lachim assurim* — while moist, they are prohibited, because any residual grape liquid absorbed in them carries the status of *stam yeinam*. *Yeveshim mutarim* — once dry, they are permitted. The question the *Baraita* leaves unresolved is: *heichi dami lachim heichi dami yeveshim* — precisely what counts as moist and what counts as dry?

### Twelve Months Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּשֶׁהֵן אֲסוּרִין — אֲסוּרִין אֲפִילּוּ בַּהֲנָאָה, כְּשֶׁהֵן מוּתָּרִין — מוּתָּרִין אֲפִילּוּ בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: When** these grape residues **are prohibited,** one is **prohibited even from** deriving **benefit** from them. **When they are permitted, they are permitted even with regard to consumption.**

The Amoraic clarification comes from *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: *lachim kol yud-bet chodesh, yeveshim le'achar yud-bet chodesh* — they are considered moist for the entirety of the first twelve months, and only after twelve months are they considered dry.

One might ask why twelve months is necessary — surely grape peels dry out more quickly than that. The answer lies in the realities of wine production: the skins and seeds are not laid out individually to air-dry but are discarded in large heaps together with all the waste from the winery. Clumped together in a mass, they retain their moisture for far longer than one might expect. One could also draw a suggestive parallel to the year-long mourning period, in which twelve months similarly marks the threshold at which something that was moist with connection begins to be regarded as fully separated.

A related procedural question arises: in a leap year, does "twelve months" mean twelve calendar months, or a full calendar year? Similar ambiguities arise with *kaddish*, *avelut*, and *yahrtzeit* observance — particularly in *Adar* years. The *halacha* regarding twelve months would seem to mean twelve months as such, though the question is not trivial.

It is also worth clarifying the distinction between *stam yeinam* and *yayin nesech*. *Yayin nesech* technically refers to wine actually used for libation to an idol, which is *assur behana'ah* by Torah law. *Stam yeinam* refers to wine touched or produced by non-Jews and is prohibited by rabbinic decree — a *gezeirah* that treats it as if it were *yayin nesech*. The difficulty is that throughout the *Gemara* and throughout the *Rishonim*, the terms are frequently conflated, and *yayin nesech* is often used loosely even where the prohibition is only *d'rabanan*. Keeping the distinction in view is essential for correctly parsing the level of prohibition being discussed.

### Wet/Dry Status Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: הַאי דּוּרְדְּיָּא דְּחַמְרָא דַּאֲרַמָּאֵי, בָּתַר תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא שָׁרֵי. אָמַר רַב חֲבִיבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָנֵי גּוּלְפֵי, בָּתַר תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא שָׁרֵי. אָמַר רַב חֲבִיבָא: הָנֵי אֲבַטָּא דְּטַיָּיעֵי, בָּתַר תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא שְׁרֵי. אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: הָנֵי פּוּרְצָנֵי דַּאֲרַמָּאֵי, בָּתַר תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא שָׁרֵי. אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָנֵי גּוּלְפֵי שְׁחִימֵי וְאוּכָּמֵי, בָּתַר תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid says:** With regard to **these yeasts** produced from **the wine of Arameans, after twelve months** of **the year they are permitted. Rav Ḥaviva, son of Rava, says:** With regard to **these jugs** that belong to gentiles, **after twelve months** of **the year they are permitted. Rav Ḥaviva says:** With regard to **these** **canteens** belonging **to Arabs, after twelve months** of **the year they are permitted. Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, says:** With regard to **these Aramean grape pits, after twelve months** of **the year they are permitted. Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, says:** With regard to **these brown and black jugs, after twelve months** of **the year they are permitted.**

*Amar Rabbah bar bar Channah amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the Gemara now records a *memra* that sharpens the rule considerably. *Keshen asurin asurin afilu behana'ah* — when the grape seeds and peels are in their moist state, they are prohibited even for benefit, not merely for consumption, because the liquid absorbed in them carries the full prohibition of wine that is *assur behana'ah*. *Keshen mutarin mutarin afilu be'achilah* — and once they become permitted after twelve months, they are permitted even to be eaten outright.

This formulation has potentially significant implications for the *kashrut* of raisins. If a non-Jew presses grapes or otherwise handles them in a manner that creates *stam yeinam* concerns, but the grapes are subsequently dried and allowed to remain dry for twelve months, they would become fully permissible. In practice, the production of raisins typically involves no pressing of juice at all, so the *stam yeinam* concern may not arise in the first place — though incidental dripping of juice during processing might still pose questions. The *halacha* regarding industrial grape and raisin production is complex and depends heavily on the specifics of each production process.

### Sediment & Vessel Types

The *Gemara* then records a series of Amoraic statements, each extending the twelve-month rule to a different substance or vessel type. *Amar Rav Zevid*: *hai dardaya dechamra de'armaye* — the wine sediment, the *shmarim*, the yeast-bearing dregs that settle at the bottom of a barrel of non-Jewish wine, also become permissible after twelve months, once whatever wine was in them has evaporated and only dry residue remains.

*Amar Rav Chaviva brei deRava*: *hani gulfi* — a different type of jug — are likewise permissible after twelve months. He adds a further ruling regarding *hani avata detayei* — the leather wine skins used by desert travelers, made of thick leather as Rashi describes. *Amar Rav Acha brei deRav Ika*: *hani purtzanei de'armai* — yet another category of vessels or pits and seeds — similarly follow the twelve-month rule. And *Amar Rav Acha brei deRava*: *hani gulfi dechimei ve'uchmei*, the black and brown pottery-type jars, also become permissible after twelve months. The cumulative effect of these statements is to establish a single overarching principle: wherever the concern is that a vessel or substance has been permeated with wine that carries a prohibition, twelve months of dryness renders it permissible. Each Amora addresses a slightly different case, but they all reinforce the same *psak*.

## Muryas & Fish Sauce

### Muryas Background

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמּוּרְיָיס. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מוּרְיָיס אוּמָּן — מוּתָּר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: אַף חִילָק אוּמָּן — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **fish stew** is prohibited. **The Sages taught: Fish stew** prepared by **an expert is permitted,** as professionals do not mix wine in it. **Rabbi Yehuda ben Gamliel says in the name of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel: *Ḥilak*** prepared by **an expert is also permitted.**

The *Gemara* turns to a discussion of *muryas*, a fermented fish sauce that was a staple of the Roman world, known in Latin as *garum*. The production process involved packing fish in salt and allowing them to ferment over an extended period. The liquid that resulted was the *garum* itself; the fish solids that sank to the bottom — corresponding to the Latin *hallex*, perhaps derived from *halos*, meaning salt — became what the *Gemara* calls *chilek*.

Archaeological evidence illuminates this world considerably. Jars found at Pompeii bear inscriptions identifying their contents as *garum castum* — pure *garum* — and at least one scholar has proposed that these were produced specifically from *kosher* fish for the Jewish inhabitants of Pompeii, though this theory is debated. The overwhelming majority of commercially produced *garum* appears to have been made from mackerel, which is a *kosher* fish, though cost pressures or availability could lead producers to substitute other species.

The *mishnah* — back on 29b — lists *muryas* as one of the items prohibited with an *issur hana'ah*, which points toward wine as the underlying concern, since a *kashrut* problem with non-*kosher* fish would ordinarily produce a prohibition on eating but not a prohibition on benefit. The role of wine in the production process will become clearer as the *Gemara* proceeds.

### Professional Production

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי אֲבִימִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מוּרְיָיס אוּמָּן מוּתָּר. הוּא תָּנֵי לַהּ וְהוּא אָמַר לַהּ: פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי מוּתָּר, שְׁלִישִׁי אָסוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי דִּנְפִישׁ שׁוּמְנַיְיהוּ — לָא צְרִיךְ לְמִירְמֵי בְּהוּ חַמְרָא, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ רָמוּ בְּהוּ חַמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Avimi, son of Rabbi Abbahu,** would **teach: Fish stew** prepared by **an expert is permitted. He** would **teach** this *baraita* that he received through tradition, **and** then **he** would **say its** explanation: The **first time and** the **second** time that fish stew is prepared from a fish, it is **permitted,** but the **third** time it is **prohibited. What is the reason?** With regard to the **first time and** the **second** time, **as** the fish's **oil is plentiful,** there is **no need to place wine in it. From this** point **forward,** one might **place wine in it** to compensate for the insufficient fish oil.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: the *Baraita* states *muryas uman mutar* — *muryas* produced by a professional craftsman is permissible. The presumption is that a professional producer adheres to standard practice and does not adulterate his product with non-*kosher* fish or with wine — at least not in the initial stages of production.

The key distinction here concerns the stages of pressing. The first and second pressings of the fish are rich in natural oils and require no additives; the flavor is strong on its own. It is only from the third pressing onward — when the fish have been pressed multiple times and the resulting sauce is weak — that wine would be added to compensate for the diminished flavor. The price differential is decisive: where fish is inexpensive relative to wine, there is no economic incentive to adulterate the sauce with wine, and so the first-quality *muryas* from a professional can be assumed to be free of wine. This is analogous in structure to extra-virgin olive oil, where successive pressings yield progressively lower-quality product.

*Avimi brei deRabbi Abahu* taught: *muryas uman mutar*, and he himself both taught this ruling and applied it. He specified: *pa'am rishon vesheni denafish shumnayhu lo tzarich lemarmeihu chamra* — the first and second pressings, which are rich in fat and oil, require no wine addition. *Mikan ve'eilach remu behu chamra* — from the third pressing onward, wine would be mixed in, and therefore *shlishi assur* — the third pressing is prohibited.

### Chilek Permissibility

The *Gemara* continues with the teaching of *Rabbi Yehuda ben Gamliel*: *af chilek uman mutar* — even the *chilek*, the fish residue from the bottom of the production vessel, is permissible when it comes from a professional producer. The reasoning parallels that for *muryas* itself: there is a *chazakah* that a professional does not adulterate his high-quality product with non-*kosher* fish, and this presumption extends to the *chilek* as well.

One might raise the concern that after extracting the sauce, a producer could increase the volume of the *chilek* by mixing in other, cheaper fish — possibly non-*kosher* species — to bulk it up. Rashi raises a similar point, noting that *chilek* of non-Jews is prohibited when it is made from small fish mixed together in large quantities, since there is concern that non-*kosher* fish may have been included. The *Tannaim* nonetheless rule it permissible when coming from a professional, on the basis of the same presumption of honest practice. The parallel to contemporary *kashrut* supervision is worth noting: the presumption underlying these rulings — that a major commercial producer would not risk its reputation and business by adulterating its product — is structurally similar to arguments made about modern industrial food production.

### Akko Port Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא אַרְבָּא דְּמוּרְיְיסָא דַּאֲתַי לִנְמֵילָא דְּעַכּוֹ, אוֹתֵיב רַבִּי אַבָּא דְּמִן עַכּוֹ נָטוֹרֵי בַּהֲדַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: עַד הָאִידָּנָא מַאן נַטְרַהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַד הָאִידָּנָא (לְמַאן) [לְמַאי] נֵיחוּשׁ לַהּ? אִי מִשּׁוּם דִּמְעָרְבִי בֵּיהּ חַמְרָא — קִיסְתָּא דְּמוּרְיָיס בְּלוּמָא, קִיסְתָּא דְּחַמְרָא בְּאַרְבְּעָה לוּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that there was **a certain boat** carrying **fish stew which came to the port of Akko,** and **Rabbi Abba from Akko placed guards over it** to ensure that no wine would be added to the fish stew. **Rava said to him: Until now, who guarded it?** Rabbi Abba **said to him: Until now, for what should we be concerned? If** the problem is **due to** the concern **that they mix wine in it,** that concern is unfounded, as in the place where this fish stew was produced, **a *kista* of fish stew** sells **for one *luma*** while **a *kista* of wine** sells **for four *luma*.** Since wine was more expensive than fish stew, there is no reason to suspect that wine was added to the stew before it reached Akko, where fish stew is sold at a higher price than wine.

The *Gemara* relates a case: *hahu arba demuryasa de'atu lenamlya de'Akko* — there was a boat laden with *muryas* that docked at the port of Akko. Rabbi Abba of Akko went to inspect it. The question was whether this particular *muryas* could be presumed to be of the permissible variety.

Rava articulated the relevant concern: *ad ha'idna nichosh lah mishum demervei bah chamra* — the concern up to this point has been whether wine was mixed into it. The critical factor is the local price differential: *kista demuryas beluma* — if a measure of *muryas* costs one *luma*, and *kista dechamra bedaled luma'ei* — the same measure of wine costs four times as much, then there is no economic motivation to add wine to the *muryas*, and it can be presumed clean.

But Rabbi Yirmeya raised a complication to Rabbi Zeira: *dilma eidi deTzor atu deshavo chamra* — perhaps the boat came from Tyre, where the price ratio was inverted and wine was cheaper relative to *muryas*, making adulteration economically attractive. If it was produced in Tyre and then transported to Akko, the different price structure at the point of production would change the analysis entirely. The *Gemara* responds that sailing conditions near Tyre were poor, making such a route uncommon, so this scenario would be exceptional. The underlying principle, however, is clear: the permissibility of *muryas* is tied not only to whether it came from a professional producer but to the economic circumstances at the place of production, since those circumstances determine whether there was any incentive to adulterate the product with wine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: דִּלְמָא אַיְּידֵי דְּצוֹר אֲתוֹ דְּשָׁוֵי חַמְרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם עִיקּוּלֵי וּפָשׁוֹרֵי אִיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this, **Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Zeira:** But **perhaps they came by means of Tyre, where wine is inexpensive** and therefore likely to be added to the fish stew to increase its volume. Rabbi Zeira **said to him: There,** by way of Tyre, **there are impediments and melted** snow, which make travel very difficult, and the boat would not have sailed through that route.

## Gevinat Beit Unaiki

### Reason for Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּגְבִינַת בֵּית אוּנְיָיקֵי. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָסְרוּ גְּבִינַת אוּנְיָיקֵי? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרוֹב עֲגָלִים שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ עִיר נִשְׁחָטִין לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. מַאי אִירְיָא רוֹב עֲגָלִים? אֲפִילּוּ מִיעוּט נָמֵי, דְּהָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר חָיֵישׁ לְמִיעוּטָא!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And cheese of Beit Unyaki** is prohibited. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: For what** reason **did they prohibit** deriving benefit from **the cheese of Beit Unyaki?** It is **because most** of the **calves of that city are slaughtered for** the sake of **idol worship,** and the milk curdled in their stomach contents is prohibited. The Gemara asks: **Why** does this explanation **specifically** mention **most calves? Even** if **a minority** of calves were slaughtered for idol worship, this would **also** be reason enough, **as Rabbi Meir,** who is the *tanna* of unattributed opinions in a mishna, **is** generally **concerned about a minority.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the cheese of *Beit Unaiki*, which the *mishnah* (on 29b) listed as prohibited with an *issur hana'ah*. This is an *avodah zarah* question, distinct from the general *kashrut* questions about non-Jewish cheese that will be addressed later in the tractate.

*Amar Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish* — Reish Lakish — *mipnei mah asuru Gevinat Unaiki*: why did the Sages prohibit this cheese? *Mipnei she'rov agalim shel otah ir nishchatin l'avodah kochavim* — because the majority of the calves in that city are slaughtered for *avodah zarah*.

To understand why this matters, one must understand how cheese was made in Talmudic times. Cheese production required *or keva* — the stomach lining of a calf. The lining was fashioned into a bag, filled with milk, and the enzymes in the stomach lining caused the milk to curdle and become cheese. If the calf was slaughtered for idolatrous purposes, then the physical substance of the stomach lining carries the prohibition of *avodah zarah*, and any cheese made with it is prohibited even for benefit. That is why this is an *issur hana'ah* rather than merely a prohibition on consumption.

One might wonder: if a calf is slaughtered entirely for *avodah zarah*, would any part of it remain for sale in the market at all? The answer is that non-Jewish idolatrous sacrifice typically did not consume the whole animal. Just as in Greek mythology Prometheus teaches humans to offer the skin, bones, and fats to the gods while keeping the meat — a construction not entirely unlike the *korban shelamim* in its structure, though with the crucial difference that in our tradition the division is commanded by God rather than devised as a deception — it is reasonable that idolaters would dedicate specific portions of the animal to their worship while the remainder, including the stomach, entered commerce.

### Majority/Minority Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ רוֹב — אִיכָּא מִיעוּט. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִיעוּט, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא רוֹב עֲגָלִים דְּאֵין נִשְׁחָטִין לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאִיכָּא נָמֵי שְׁאָר בְּהֵמוֹת דְּאֵין נִשְׁחָטִין לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — הָוֵה לֵיהּ מִיעוּטָא דְּמִיעוּטָא, וּמִיעוּטָא דְּמִיעוּטָא לָא חָיֵישׁ רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **If you say** that the reason for the prohibition is due to **a majority** of calves slaughtered for idol worship, then despite the fact that the majority of animals in general used to curdle cheese are not slaughtered for idolatrous purposes, **there are** nevertheless **a minority** of animals altogether, i.e., the majority of calves, that are, and this minority of calves are cause for concern according to Rabbi Meir. The Gemara continues: **But if you say** that Rabbi Meir prohibits the cheese due to **a minority** of calves, **since there are a majority of calves that are not slaughtered for idol worship, and there are also** a wider majority of the **remaining animals** used to curdle milk **that are not slaughtered for idol worship** at all, **this** would constitute **a minority of a minority, and Rabbi Meir is not concerned for a minority of a minority.** Since only a particular minority of animals used to curdle cheese, i.e., calves, are ever slaughtered for idolatry, and even within that group, only a minority are actually slaughtered, even Rabbi Meir would not be concerned.

The *Gemara* presses further: *mai irya rov agalim* — why does it matter that it is the majority? *Afilu miyut nami* — even if it were a minority of calves being slaughtered for *avodah zarah*, shouldn't there still be a concern?

The answer invokes Rabbi Meir: *de'ha Rabbi Meir chayish l'miyuta* — Rabbi Meir, who is understood to be the author of our *mishnah*, is the opinion that is concerned even for a *miyut*, a minority. But a *miyut de'miyut* — a double minority — is another matter. If one says that the concern applies even to a minority, then one encounters the following analysis: consider all the animals in the city. Calves are a minority of all animals. Even if calves slaughtered for *avodah zarah* constituted the majority of calves, they would still be a minority of all animals — and their stomach linings, available in the market, would be a minority of all available stomach linings. That would make the problematic ones a *miyut de'miyut*, a minority of a minority.

### Rabbi Meir's Position

*Miuta de'miuta lo chayish Rabbi Meir* — even Rabbi Meir, who is concerned for a simple minority, is not concerned for a double minority. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the *mishnah*'s ruling about *Beit Unaiki* specifically must rest on the premise that the calves slaughtered for *avodah zarah* constitute an actual majority of the calves in that city — not merely a majority of all animals. That way, the stomach linings from idolatrous slaughter are the majority among calf stomach linings available for cheese-making, while being a minority among all animal stomachs in general. This is a single *miyut* — a minority of all animals — which is exactly the kind of case Rabbi Meir does consider, and which the majority among calves makes sufficient to warrant prohibition. This also explains why the prohibition was specific to that one city: it was a local condition in which the majority of calves specifically, not animals in general, were dedicated to idolatrous slaughter.

### Intent Transfer Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר אֶלְיָקִים לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כִּי נִשְׁחָטִין לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מַאי הָוֵי? וְהָא אַתְּ הוּא דְּשָׁרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon bar Elyakim said to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish:** Even **when** calves **are slaughtered for** the sake of **idol worship, what of it? But are you** not **the one who permits** animals slaughtered with idolatrous intentions?

The *Gemara* introduces a further question from Rabbi Shimon bar Elyakim directed at Reish Lakish: *ki nishchatin le'avodah kochavim mai havei* — even granting that most calves are slaughtered for *avodah zarah*, why should that render the stomach lining prohibited? Shouldn't Reish Lakish himself hold that there is no problem?

This question draws on a recorded *machloket* between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. The case: *ha'shochet et ha-beheimah lizrok damah le'avodah kochavim ve'hiktir chalavah le'avodah kochavim* — one who slaughters an animal with the intention of subsequently sprinkling its blood for idolatrous worship, or burning its fats as an idolatrous offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִתְּמַר: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה לִזְרוֹק דָּמָהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, לְהַקְטִיר חֶלְבָּהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲסוּרָה, קָסָבַר מְחַשְּׁבִין מֵעֲבוֹדָה לַעֲבוֹדָה, וְיָלְפִינַן חוּץ מִפְּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was stated:** The *halakha* with regard to **one who slaughters an animal** in order **to sprinkle its blood for** the sake of **idol worship,** or **to burn its** prohibited **fat for idol worship,** is subject to a dispute between *amora'im*. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Deriving benefit from the animal is **prohibited,** as **he holds** that **one can intend from** one **rite to** another **rite.** In other words, idolatrous intent while slaughtering an animal renders it prohibited, even if the intention pertains not to the slaughter itself but to sprinkling the blood or burning the fat. **And** he maintains that **we derive** *halakhot* concerning intent **outside** the Temple **from** the *halakhot* concerning intent **inside** the Temple. Since such intentions at the time of slaughter render an animal prohibited within the Temple, they render it prohibited outside the Temple as well, with regard to idol worship.

*Rabbi Yochanan amar asurah* — Rabbi Yochanan says the animal is prohibited. His reasoning: *kasavar machshivin me'avodah le'avodah* — the intent transfers from one act of worship to another. Even if what was ultimately performed differs from what was originally planned, the fact that the entire chain of actions was conceived as *avodah zarah* worship is sufficient to render the animal prohibited. Rabbi Yochanan derives this principle by analogy to the *Beit HaMikdash*: just as within the Temple the *machshava* — the mental intention during one *avodah* — can transfer to and affect another *avodah*, the same principle applies to *avodah zarah*. *Ve'alfinan chutz mi'pnim* — we extrapolate the definitions of *avodah zarah* from the definitions of Temple service.

The practical upshot for the *Beit HaMikdash* analogy is significant: *zerikat hadam* — sprinkling the blood — and *hekter chalavim* — burning the fats on the altar — are both recognized *avodot* in the Temple. The principle of *piggul* and related concepts in Masechet Zevachim turn precisely on how a disqualifying intent during one *avodah* can travel across the sequence of sacrificial acts. Here the same logic is applied to the realm of *avodah zarah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מוּתֶּרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** Deriving benefit from the animal is **permitted.** Apparently, Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish holds that slaughtering an animal for the sake of idol worship does not render it prohibited. This contradicts his previous assertion that it is prohibited to derive benefit from calves that have been slaughtered for the sake of idol worship.

Reish Lakish says *muteret* — the animal remains permissible — because he does not accept this transfer of intent, at least not in all configurations. Rashi explains that Reish Lakish holds *lo yalfinan mipnim* — we do not extrapolate the laws of *avodah zarah* from the laws of the *Beit HaMikdash*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תִּרְמִינָךְ שַׁעְתָּךְ, בְּאוֹמֵר: בִּגְמַר זְבִיחָה הוּא עוֹבְדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **said to** Rabbi Shimon bar Elyakim: **May your fortune be auspicious;** the case here is **where one says** that **he is worshipping** the idol **at the conclusion of the slaughter.** Since the act of slaughtering itself is an act of worship, the calf is rendered prohibited immediately.

When Rabbi Shimon bar Elyakim challenges Reish Lakish — if you say it is permitted, then how can our *mishnah* prohibit this cheese? — Reish Lakish responds: *tarmnach shatach*, a blessing upon you for raising it, and then clarifies: *uv'omer bigmar zevichah hu uvda* — the case of the *mishnah* is specifically where the person explicitly dedicated the *shechitah* itself as an act of *avodah zarah* worship. If the *shechitah* is itself the act of worship, then Reish Lakish agrees the animal is prohibited. What he disputes is only the case where the *shechitah* is neutral but the slaughterer plans to use the blood or fats for *avodah zarah* afterward — there, Reish Lakish holds that the subsequent *avodah zarah* does not retroactively taint the animal through the *shechitah*.

In other words, Reish Lakish does consider *shechitah* a significant *avodah* in the relevant sense — *dezevichah havei avoda chashuva kezrikah* — such that if the *shechitah* itself is performed with explicit *avodah zarah* intent, the animal is prohibited. He simply disagrees with Rabbi Yochanan about whether intent aimed at a *subsequent* act of worship can transfer back through the *shechitah* to prohibit the animal when the *shechitah* itself was not dedicated to *avodah zarah*.

This distinction allows Reish Lakish to uphold the *mishnah*'s prohibition on the cheese of *Beit Unaiki* — where the calves are slaughtered with explicit *avodah zarah* intent at the moment of *shechitah* — while maintaining his position in the broader debate with Rabbi Yochanan about the limits of transferred intent across distinct acts of worship.
