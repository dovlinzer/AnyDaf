# Shevuot 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shvuos Ha'eidus Basics

### Opening & Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: רָאוּהוּ שֶׁרָץ אַחֲרֵיהֶן, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״מָה אַתָּה רָץ אַחֲרֵינוּ? שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לָךְ עֵדוּת״ – פְּטוּרִין, עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁמְעוּ מִפִּיו.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel says:** If the witnesses **saw** the plaintiff **pursuing them,** and **they said to him:** For **what** reason **are you pursuing us?** On our **oath we do not know** any **testimony on your** behalf, they are **exempt, until they will hear** a demand to testify directly **from his mouth.**

The present daf, Shevuot 32a, continues from the bottom third of 31b, where the Gemara begins transitioning from a theoretical discussion of the parameters and criteria of *shvuos ha'eidus* to the practical scenario of how the *chiyuv* actually arises and what circumstances generate an obligation to bring a *korban*.

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁבוּעַת הָעֵדוּת כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לִשְׁנַיִם: ״בּוֹאוּ וְהַעִידוּנִי״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לְךָ עֵדוּת״, אוֹ שֶׁאָמְרוּ לוֹ ״אֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לְךָ עֵדוּת״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם״, וְאָמְרוּ: ״אָמֵן״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Liability to bring a sliding-scale offering for taking a false **oath of testimony, how so?** In a case where the plaintiff **said to two** individuals: **Come and testify** on **my** behalf, and they replied: On our **oath we do not know any testimony on your** behalf, i.e., we do not have any knowledge of the matter you speak of, **or** in a case where **they said to him: We do not know** any **testimony on your** behalf, and he said to them: **I administer an oath to you, and they said: Amen;** if it was determined that they lied, **these** two witnesses **are liable.**

The *mishnah* opens with the question *shvuos ha'eidus keitzad* — what is the scenario that generates a *shvuos ha'eidus* obligating a *korban*? The classic case is that a litigant approaches two individuals who are *kasher l'eidus* and says: "Come and testify on my behalf." The requirement that he address two witnesses — and not merely one — reflects the principle that *shvuos ha'eidus* applies only when the testimony in question could actually have been used to extract *mamon*; a single *eid* cannot independently accomplish that. The witnesses then respond with a false *shvuah*: "We swear we have no testimony on your behalf." That is the paradigmatic case that obligates a *korban*.

### Oath vs. Amen

The *mishnah* presents a second scenario as well. The witnesses first declare — without an oath — that they have no testimony. The litigant then responds: *mashpia ani aleichem*, "I impose a *shvuah* upon you," and the witnesses answer *amen*. The *mishnah* rules that they are likewise *chayavim*. The halachic basis for this is the principle that *kol ha'omer amen k'motzi shvuah mipiv dami* — answering *amen* is considered as though one personally uttered the oath. Accordingly, both scenarios in the *mishnah* are, in fact, cases of *mushpa mipi atzmo*.

### Self vs. Other Oath

This stands in contrast to a true case of *mushpa mipi acherim*, which would occur if the litigant declared *mashpia ani aleichem* that you have no testimony, and the witnesses responded only by reaffirming their position — "We're telling you we don't know" — without themselves articulating or accepting a *shvuah*. They were merely standing by their denial after having the *shvuah* placed upon them. Both scenarios in our *mishnah*, by contrast, involve the witnesses actively taking the oath themselves, through explicit utterance or through *amen*.

## Denial in Beis Din

### Outside BD Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִשְׁבִּיעַ עֲלֵיהֶם חָמֵשׁ פְּעָמִים חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין, וּבָאוּ לְבֵית דִּין וְהוֹדוּ – פְּטוּרִין. כָּפְרוּ – חַיָּיבִין עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** If **he administered an oath to them five times outside the court, and they came to court and admitted** that they had knowledge of the incident in question and testified, they are **exempt.** But if **they denied** knowledge of the incident in court as well, they are **liable for each and every one** of the oaths administered to them outside the court.

The *mishnah* then addresses a case where the litigant administered the oath to the witnesses five times outside of *beis din*, and when they subsequently appeared before *beis din* they admitted to knowing testimony — they are *patur*. Two distinct principles underlie this ruling, both of which the *Gemara* will develop.

### Kfira Triggers Chiyuv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן דְּאַכְּפִירָה בְּבֵית דִּין הוּא דִּמְחַיְּיבִי, אַחוּץ לְבֵית דִּין לָא מִחַיְּיבִי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״אִם לוֹא יַגִּיד וְנָשָׂא עֲוֹנוֹ״ – לֹא אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאִילּוּ מַגִּיד זֶה, מִתְחַיֵּיב זֶה מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that it is** specifically **for denial in court that they are liable,** and **they are not liable for** denial **outside court?** **Abaye said:** It is derived as **the verse states** with regard to the oath of testimony: **"If he does not utter it, he shall bear his iniquity"** (Leviticus 5:1), from which it is derived: **I said** this *halakha* **to you only in a place where, were this** witness **to utter** his testimony, **that** other individual **becomes liable** to make **a monetary** payment, i.e., in court. He is not liable for denial in a place where his testimony would not render one liable to pay.

The first principle is that the *kfira* — the denial of testimony — must occur in *beis din* in order to trigger the *chiyuv*. Outside of *beis din*, the witnesses have not yet testified to anything in a binding sense. They retain the ability to come before *beis din* and testify truthfully at any moment. Their repeated false oaths outside of *beis din* therefore remain open-ended and do not yet crystallize into a *chiyuv*.

### No Retraction Rule

The second principle is *kivan she'higid shuv eino chozer u'maggid*: once testimony — or a denial of testimony — has been given before *beis din*, one cannot retract it. Outside of *beis din*, by contrast, the situation remains fluid. Since the witnesses can always choose to testify truthfully when they arrive before *beis din*, the issue is still live, and the prior oaths carry no irreversible force.

### Multiple Oaths BD

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִשְׁבִּיעַ עֲלֵיהֶן חָמֵשׁ פְּעָמִים בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וְכָפְרוּ – אֵינָן חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם? הוֹאִיל וְאֵינָם יְכוֹלִין לַחְזוֹר וּלְהוֹדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** If **he administered an oath to them five times before the court, and they denied** knowledge of any testimony relating to the incident, **they are liable** for taking **only one** false oath. **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason** for this ruling? **Since** once they denied that they had any knowledge of the matter **they can no** longer **retract** that denial **and admit** that they have knowledge of the matter.

The *mishnah* then considers a different case: the litigant administered the oath to them five times *before beis din*, and they denied. Here one might expect that each of the five oaths generates a separate *chiyuv*, since everything occurred in the very place where testimony could have been given. Yet the *mishnah* rules that they are *chayav* only once — *ein chayav ela achas*.

### Posul After First

Rabbi Shimon explains: *ho'il ve'einam yecholim lachzor lehodos* — once the witnesses make their first denial in *beis din*, they are no longer in a position to testify at all. *Kivan she'higid shuv eino chozer u'maggid* applies from the moment of the first *kfira* in *beis din*. After that point, every subsequent oath is meaningless as a *shvuos ha'eidus*, because the witnesses are no longer eligible to give testimony — they are, in effect, like *psulei eidus* the moment they deny in *beis din*. The first denial activates the *chiyuv* for the first *shvuah* and simultaneously forecloses any *chiyuv* for those that follow.

## Two Witnesses

### Simultaneous Denial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּפְרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן כְּאֶחָד – שְׁנֵיהֶן חַיָּיבִין. בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – הָרִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב וְהַשֵּׁנִי פָּטוּר. כָּפַר אֶחָד וְהוֹדָה אֶחָד – הַכּוֹפֵר חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If **both of** the witnesses **denied** knowledge of the incident **together, both of them are liable.** If they denied knowledge **one after the other, the first** who denied knowledge is **liable, and the second** is **exempt,** as once the first witness denies knowledge of the incident, the second is an individual witness, whose testimony is not decisive. If **one** of the two witnesses **denied** knowledge of the incident, **and** the other **one admitted** that he had knowledge and proceeded to testify, **the one who denies** knowledge of the incident is **liable.**

The *mishnah* then turns to the structure of *chiyuv* when two *eidim* are involved. If *kafru shneihem k'echad* — both witnesses denied at the same moment — both are *chayav*. Each one withheld testimony that, in combination with the other's, could have established the case and extracted *mamon* for the litigant.

### Sequential Denial

If, however, they denied sequentially — *zeh achar zeh* — the first is *chayav* and the second is *patur*. When the first witness denies before *beis din*, his *kfira* takes immediate effect: *kivan she'higid shuv eino chozer u'maggid* renders him no longer eligible to testify. By the time the second witness denies, he is standing alone. He is only an *ed echad*, and a single witness cannot independently support the litigant's case to extract *mamon*. Therefore the second witness is *patur* for *shvuos ha'eidus*.

### First Denies

Similarly, if the first witness denied and the second witness admitted — *kafar echad ve'hodeh echad, hakofar chayav* — the first witness is *chayav*. At the time of his denial, his testimony could have joined with that of the second witness to fully establish the case. He withheld evidence that was potentially decisive.

### Two Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים, כָּפְרָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ כָּפְרָה הַשְּׁנִיָּה – שְׁתֵּיהֶן חַיָּיבוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהָעֵדוּת יְכוֹלָה לְהִתְקַיֵּים בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** If **there were two sets of witnesses** that took the oath of testimony, and **the first** set **denied** knowledge of the matter **and then the second** set **denied** knowledge of the matter, **both of** the sets **are liable, because the testimony can exist with either of them,** as even after the first set denies knowledge of the incident, the second remains capable of providing decisive testimony.

The *mishnah* then addresses a case involving two separate groups of witnesses — *shtei kitei eidim* — both of which the litigant approached for testimony. Even if the first group denied before *beis din* and was subsequently rendered ineligible, the second group can still independently establish the testimony. Since each group of two witnesses constitutes a complete set of *eidus* on its own — *ha'eidus yecholah lehiskayem bishteihen* — the *chiyuv* applies to both groups even if their denials were sequential.

## Direct Demand Req.

### Shmuel's Ruling

The teaching of Shmuel that opens 32a establishes a critical requirement: if the witnesses saw the litigant running after them, clearly intending to ask them to testify, and they preemptively swore that they had no testimony on his behalf, they are *patur*. Why? *Ad sheyishme'u mipi* — they must actually hear the demand verbalized directly from him. Merely observing his behavior does not suffice.

### Running After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָץ אַחֲרֵיהֶן אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ; סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כֵּיוָן דְּרָץ אַחֲרֵיהֶן – כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לְהוּ דָּמֵי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Mentioning the case of the plaintiff **pursuing them was necessary for** Shmuel, as otherwise it could **enter your mind to say: Since** the plaintiff **is pursuing them,** it is **like** the case of **one who says to them** directly to testify. Therefore, Shmuel **teaches us** that although the intent of the plaintiff is for them to testify, the witnesses are liable only if he tells them so explicitly.

The concern that Shmuel is addressing is whether implicit communication — specifically, running after witnesses in a context that makes the purpose unmistakable — could constitute a sufficient demand. One might have thought: *kivan de'ratz achareihem ke'man de'amar lehu dami* — since he is visibly running after them and the intent is obvious, it is as though he has made an explicit verbal demand. Shmuel's ruling forecloses that reasoning: *ka mashma lan*, verbal articulation of the demand is required.

### Necessity Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: שְׁבוּעַת הָעֵדוּת כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לְעֵדִים ״בּוֹאוּ וְהַעִידוּנִי״, ״שְׁבוּעָה״ כּוּ׳ – אָמַר אִין, לָא אָמַר לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But this too, we learn** in the mishna: Liability to bring a sliding-scale offering for taking a false **oath of testimony, how so?** In a case where the plaintiff **said to** two **witnesses: Come and testify** on **my** behalf, and they replied: On our **oath, etc.,** from which it can be inferred that if the plaintiff **said** this to the witnesses, **yes,** they are liable, and if he **did not say** this to the witnesses, **no,** they are not liable.

The *Gemara* challenges whether Shmuel's teaching is even necessary. A later *mishnah* already states that if the litigant sent his servant, or if the opposing party imposed the *shvuah*, the witnesses are *patur* — *ad sheyishme'u mipi hatoveia* — because they must hear the demand from the litigant himself. Doesn't that *mishnah* already establish that a direct verbal demand is required?

### Verbal Essential

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״אָמַר״ לָאו דַּוְקָא; הָכָא נָמֵי לָאו דַּוְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that when the *tanna* says with regard to the oath on a deposit: In a case where the owner **said** to the bailee, he did **not** mean that this is the *halakha* only in a case where he **specifically** verbalized his demand. **Here too,** with regard to the oath of testimony, the *tanna* did **not** mean that this is the *halakha* only in a case where the plaintiff **specifically** verbalized his demand.

The *Gemara* responds: the case of running after them is qualitatively different. There it is the litigant himself who is approaching — only the verbalization is absent. One might have thought that his physical pursuit, which clearly communicates his intent, suffices. It is therefore necessary for Shmuel to inform us that it does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״אָמַר״ דְּהָכָא דַּוְקָא – תְּנָא הָתָם אַטּוּ הָכָא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לָא ״אָמַר״ דְּהָתָם דַּוְקָא וְלָא ״אָמַר״ דְּהָכָא דַּוְקָא – ״אָמַר״ ״אָמַר״ לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנְיַיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is this** comparison? **Granted, if you say that here,** when the *tanna* says in the mishna with regard to the oath of testimony: In a case where the plaintiff **said** to two individuals, it is **specifically** in a case where the plaintiff verbalized his demand, one could explain that the *tanna* **taught there,** with regard to the oath on a deposit: In a case where the owner said, **due to** the fact that he employed that formulation **here** in the mishna. *Tanna'im* frequently employ uniform language in different cases, even though there are halakhic differences between them. **But if you say** that **neither there** with regard to the oath on a deposit is it **specifically** in a case where the owner verbally **said, nor here** with regard to the oath of testimony is it **specifically** in a case where the plaintiff verbally **said, why do I** need **to teach: Said, said,** in both instances?

The *Gemara* attempts a counter-argument: perhaps *amar* in the context of *shvuos ha'eidus* is *davka*, a genuine requirement, and the *pikadon mishnah* adopted the same language only by association — *tana hatam atu hakha* — patterning itself on our *mishnah* even though in that case the requirement does not apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא אוֹרְחָא דְּמִילְּתָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Perhaps** the *tanna* **is teaching us the matter** in **the manner in which** it typically occurs, as both a plaintiff and the owner of a deposit typically articulate their claims verbally. It is therefore entirely possible that I would have read *amar* in our *mishnah* as non-*davka*, and would have assumed that a non-verbal demand also suffices for *shvuos ha'eidus*. Shmuel comes to clarify that this is not the case — a verbalized demand is essential.

### Braisa Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: רָאוּהוּ שֶׁבָּא אַחֲרֵיהֶן, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״מָה אַתָּה בָּא אַחֲרֵינוּ? שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לָךְ עֵדוּת״ – פְּטוּרִין. וְאִם בְּפִקָּדוֹן – חַיָּיבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **it is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel:** In a case where the witnesses **saw that** the plaintiff **was pursuing them,** and **they said to him:** For **what** reason **are you pursuing us;** on our **oath we do not know testimony on your** behalf, **they are exempt. And if** it is **with regard to** an oath on **a deposit,** in a case where the owner is pursuing the bailee and he denies that the deposit is in his possession, the bailees **are liable,** as they are liable for any denial of the deposit at all, irrespective of the nature of the claim raised by the owner of the item.

A *braisa* corroborates Shmuel's position almost word for word: witnesses who see the litigant pursuing them, preemptively declare a *shvuah* that they have no testimony on his behalf, and are ruled *paturim* — exempt — because they never heard him verbally demand their testimony. The *braisa* adds: *ve'im be'pikadon chayavin* — but if this were a case of *shvuas ha'pikadon*, they would be *chayavin*, since the direct verbal demand is not a requirement in that context.

## Scriptural Basis

### Im Lo Yagid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁמְעוּ מִפִּי הַתּוֹבֵעַ!
>
> **Translation:** **until they hear** a demand to testify directly **from the mouth of the plaintiff.**

The *Gemara* now derives the requirement that *kfira* occur specifically in *beis din* from a *pasuk*. The verse states: *im lo yagid ve'nasa avono* — "if he does not testify, he bears his sin."

### Abaye's Logic

The *pasuk* is interpreted as establishing that the denial must occur in a place where, had the witnesses chosen to testify, their testimony would at that moment create a monetary obligation for the opposing party. *Lo amarti lach ela be'makom she'ilu maggid ze mis'chayev ze mamon* — I am speaking only of a context in which actual testimony could be given and would be binding. That context is *beis din*. The phrase *im lo yagid* implies a setting in which *hagada* — giving testimony — is possible and operative.

### Papa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא שְׁבוּעָה גּוּפַאּ – בְּבֵית דִּין אֵין, וְשֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית דִּין לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: If so, say** that it is not the denial but **the oath itself;** if it is taken **in court, yes,** he is liable, **and** if it is **that** which is **not** taken **in court, no,** he is not liable.

Rav Papa immediately raises an obvious objection to Abaye: if the *pasuk* establishes a *beis din* context for the entire *parasha* of *shvuos ha'eidus*, does this mean that the *shvuah* itself must also be taken in *beis din*? Should we rule that a *shvuah* administered outside of *beis din* falls entirely outside the *parasha* and generates no *chiyuv* whatsoever?

### Multiple Chiyuvim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: ״לְאַחַת״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בְּבֵית דִּין, מִי מְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת?! וְהָתְנַן: הִשְׁבִּיעַ עֲלֵיהֶן חֲמִשָּׁה פְּעָמִים בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וְכָפְרוּ – אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם? הוֹאִיל וְאֵינָם יְכוֹלִין לַחְזוֹר וּלְהוֹדוֹת. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: שְׁבוּעָה – חוּץ לְבֵית דִּין, כְּפִירָה – בְּבֵית דִּין?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said to Rav Pappa: That should **not enter your mind, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse: "And it will be when he is guilty **of any one** of these" (Leviticus 5:5), serves **to render** one **liable** to bring an offering **for each and every** instance when one repeatedly performs the transgressions for which one is liable to bring a sliding-scale offering. **And if it enters your mind** that one is liable only for an oath taken **in court, is one liable for each and every** oath? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: If **he administered an oath to them five times before the court, and they denied** knowledge of any testimony relating to the incident, **they are liable** for taking **only one** false oath. **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason** for this ruling? **Since** once they denied that they had any knowledge of the matter **they can no** longer **retract** that denial **and admit** that they have knowledge of the matter. Abaye explains: **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that one is liable for each and every **oath** taken **outside court;** but one is liable only if the **denial** is **in court?**

The *Gemara* responds by invoking the *pasuk*: *ve'haya ki yesham le'achas me'eila* — "and it shall be when he incurs guilt for one of these." While the *peshat* of this phrase refers to liability for one of the three categories of *oleh ve'yored*, the *Gemara* reads it to teach *lechayyev al kol achas ve'achas* — that one can be *chayav* separately for each individual *shvuah* in a single case. This is precisely the scenario in our *mishnah*: five oaths taken outside of *beis din*, followed by a single *kfira* in *beis din*, generate five separate *korbanot*.

### Reconciliation

This produces a revealing proof. If we were to require that the *shvuah* itself also be taken in *beis din*, it would be impossible to ever be *chayav* for multiple *korbanot* in one case. Why? Because as soon as the witnesses make their first denial in *beis din*, *kivan she'higid shuv eino chozer u'maggid* applies — they are no longer eligible to testify, and any subsequent *shvuah* in *beis din* is irrelevant. The only way to accumulate multiple *chiyuvim* is to take multiple oaths *outside* of *beis din* — where each oath is still meaningful, since at every prior moment the witnesses retained the ability to come and testify — and then trigger those *chiyuvim* through a single *kfira* inside *beis din*. The *Gemara* concludes: *shma mina shvuah chutz la'beis din, kfira be'beis din* — the oaths must occur outside *beis din*, and the *kfira* that activates them must occur inside. This conclusion is arrived at not by a simple reading of the *pasuk* but by working backwards from two established principles: that multiple *chiyuvim* are possible, and that the context of *im lo yagid* demands *beis din*.

One important caveat: in all the cases where the witnesses fall short of the criteria for *shvuos ha'eidus* — whether because the *kfira* occurred outside *beis din*, or because five oaths were taken within *beis din* rendering subsequent ones irrelevant — the witnesses may still have violated *shvuas sheker*. The *Gemara* had discussed earlier whether such cases generate a *chiyuv* for *shvuas bitui*; that discussion, however, lies beyond the scope of the present analysis. The focus here is solely on whether the parameters of *shvuos ha'eidus* have been met.

## Two Witnesses…

### K'dei Dibur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּפְרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן כְּאַחַת – חַיָּיבִין. הָא אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **both of** the witnesses **denied** knowledge of the incident **together,** both of them **are liable.** The Gemara asks: **But** isn't it **impossible** for two events **to coincide precisely?** By necessity, one denial must have preceded the other.

The *Gemara* returns to the *mishnah*'s ruling that when both witnesses deny *k'echad* — simultaneously — they are both *chayav*. A conceptual difficulty immediately presents itself: is it ever truly possible to say that two people did something at precisely the same moment? If one always precedes the other by even a fraction of a second, then the second witness will always be rendering himself a lone *ed echad* after the first's denial, and will always be *patur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הִיא, דְּאָמַר אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says:** It is **possible** for two events **to coincide precisely.**

Rav Chisda identifies this *mishnah* as following the position of Rabbi Yosi ha-Glili, who holds *efshar le'tzamtzem* — that genuine simultaneity is a coherent and legally recognized possibility. The underlying dispute surfaces in Bechorot, in the case of twin firstborn lambs that emerged simultaneously: the *chachamim* treat each as a doubtful *bechor*, while Rabbi Yosi ha-Glili holds that both are *bechoros* — both literally emerged first.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁכָּפְרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן בְּתוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר – וְתוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר כְּדִיבּוּר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, the mishna can be interpreted in a case **where both of them denied** knowledge of relevant testimony **within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase, **and** the halakhic status of a pause or retraction **within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase **is like** that of continuous **speech.** Although the two statements did not coincide precisely, their halakhic status is as if they did.

Rabbi Yochanan, however, offers a simpler resolution: the ruling in our *mishnah* can be explained even according to the *rabanan* who reject *efshar le'tzamtzem*. The *mishnah* does not require literal simultaneity; it is sufficient that both witnesses denied *betoch k'dei dibur* — within the span of time it takes to utter a brief utterance. The principle *betoch k'dei dibur ke'dibur dami* — that what occurs within this interval is treated as contemporaneous — is a well-established *halachic* concept, operative across many domains. In the context of speech acts in particular, actions taken within this brief window are considered to have occurred at the same time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: מִכְּדִי תּוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר כַּמָּה הָוֵי – כְּדֵי שְׁאֵילַת תַּלְמִיד לָרַב, אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: כְּדֵי שְׁאֵילַת הָרַב לַתַּלְמִיד; עַד דְּאָמְרִי ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לָךְ עֵדוּת״, טוּבָא הָוֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד תּוֹךְ דִּיבּוּרוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: After all, how long is** the duration of: **Within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase? It is an interval **equivalent to** the duration of **the** three-word **greeting of a student to** his **teacher:** *Shalom alekha rabbi*. **Some say** that it is a briefer interval, **equivalent to** the duration of **the** two-word **greeting of a teacher to** his **student:** *Shalom alekha*. According to either opinion, in the time that elapses **until** the two of them **say:** On my **oath we do not know** any **testimony for you, it is** an interval **greater** than the time required to utter those words. How, then, can the mishna be interpreted as referring to a case where they stated their denials within the time required for speaking those words? Ravina **said to** Rav Aḥa of Difti: The case in the mishna is one where **each and every** potential witness will issue his denial **within** the time required **for speaking,** starting from the end of the statement **of the other.**

Rashi here specifies that *k'dei dibur* corresponds to the time it takes a student to greet his teacher: *shalom alecha Rabi* — three words. Rav Acha mi'Difti raises a pointed question to Ravina: the full declaration *shvuah she'eini yode'a lach eidus* is itself considerably longer than *k'dei dibur*. If the second witness must begin his oath within *k'dei dibur* of the first witness's beginning, it would always take too long. Ravina responds: the measure of *k'dei dibur* is not calculated from the start of the first witness's speech, but from the moment he finishes. Each subsequent speaker need only begin within *k'dei dibur* of when his fellow completed his utterance — *kol echad ve'echad toch k'dei dibur shel chaveiro*. One might add that even without this framework, one could envision the two witnesses beginning their declarations in an overlapping fashion; but at minimum, the *Gemara's* formulation is as stated.

## Ed Echad Debate

### Concept Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – הָרִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב וְהַשֵּׁנִי פָּטוּר. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא – דְּתַנְיָא: מַשְׁבִּיעַ עֵד אֶחָד – פָּטוּר, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If they denied knowledge **one after the other, the first** one who denied knowledge is **liable, and the second** one is **exempt,** as once the first witness denies knowledge of the incident, the second is an individual witness, whose testimony is not decisive, and he is exempt from the oath of testimony. The Gemara notes: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who administers an oath to one witness,** the witness is **exempt** from bringing an offering for taking a false oath of testimony; **and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, deems** the witness **liable** to bring an offering. In his opinion, the second witness in the case in the mishna would be liable, not exempt.

The *Gemara* notes that our *mishnah* — in ruling that the second witness who denies after the first is *patur* — does not follow the position of a certain *tanna*. A *braisa* teaches: *mashpia eid echad patur* — imposing a *shvuah* on a single witness generates no *chiyuv* for *shvuos ha'eidus*. This is the implied assumption of our *mishnah*. However, Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon disagrees and holds him *chayav*. The *Gemara* seeks to identify the conceptual root of this dispute.

### Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: עֵד אֶחָד כִּי אָתֵא – לִשְׁבוּעָה הוּא דְּקָא אָתֵא; וּמָר סָבַר: עֵד אֶחָד כִּי אֲתָא – לְמָמוֹנָא קָא אָתֵא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **they disagree about this:** One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds: When one witness comes** to testify, **it is to** render the one against whom he is testifying liable to take **an oath,** and that is the reason **that he is coming,** as one witness cannot render him liable to make a monetary payment. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **holds: When one witness comes** to testify, **it is to** render the one against whom he is testifying liable to make **a monetary** payment, and that is the reason that **he is coming.** The *tanna'im* disagree whether denial by one witness constitutes a denial with regard to monetary matters.

The initial proposal is that the debate turns on the fundamental nature of *eid echad*: does the testimony of a single witness *come for shvuah* — its only halachic function is to impose a *shvuah* on the opposing party — or does it *come for mamon* — its fundamental legal import is monetary, with the *shvuah* serving merely as a mechanism that can neutralize that monetary force? If an *eid echad* is understood as coming for *mamon*, one might argue that withholding such testimony is as consequential as withholding the testimony of two witnesses, and the *chiyuv* of *shvuos ha'eidus* should apply. The classic *nafka mina* of this conceptual question is the rule *mitoch she'eino yachol lishava meshalem* — when the party who would normally take the *shvuah* is unable to do so, he must pay — which would make sense if we understand the *eid echad* as fundamentally generating a monetary obligation that the *shvuah* was merely suspending.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיסְבְּרָא?! הָאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד סוֹטָה, וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵדֵי סוֹטָה, וּמַחְלוֹקֶת בְּעֵדֵי סוֹטָה; הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד אֶחָד, וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד שֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ חָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **And** how can **you understand** their dispute in that way? **Doesn't Abaye say: All concede with regard to a witness** in the case **of a *sota*** that he is liable for taking a false oath of testimony; **and all concede with regard to witnesses** in the case **of a *sota*; and** there is **a dispute with regard to witnesses** in the case **of a *sota*. All concede with regard to one witness** that he is not liable for taking a false oath of testimony because he cannot render another liable to make a monetary payment; **and all concede with regard to a witness** testifying on behalf of the claimant **when his counterpart,** the defendant, **is suspect about the oath.**

The *Gemara* rejects this initial framework.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא עֵד אֶחָד כִּי אָתֵי – לִשְׁבוּעָה קָא אָתֵי; וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר דָּבָר הַגּוֹרֵם לְמָמוֹן – כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי, וּמָר סָבַר לָאו כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather, all concede** that **when one witness comes** to testify, it is to render the one against whom he is testifying liable **to** take **an oath,** and that is the reason **he is coming. And here they disagree about this:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **holds: A matter that causes financial** loss **is considered** to have **monetary** value. Although the testimony of one witness does not render one liable for monetary payment, occasionally the party against whom he testified will prefer to pay rather than take the oath that the witness rendered him liable to take. In those cases, the testimony of one witness does in fact cause money to be paid. **And** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds:** A matter that causes financial loss **is not considered** to have **monetary** value.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon's position, ultimately, is not that an *eid echad* comes for *mamon* directly. Rather, it is founded on the principle of *davar ha'gorem l'mamon k'mamon dami* — something that causes money to be paid is treated as equivalent to money itself. Since the testimony of a single *eid* will, in the typical case, lead the opposing party to take a *shvuah* — and most people will not take a false *shvuah*, or at minimum there is a real possibility they will not — the testimony of an *eid echad* effectively has the power to bring about the payment of the debt. Because it can generate this monetary consequence, Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon treats it as *eidus* that relates to *mamon*, and therefore imposes the *chiyuv* of *shvuos ha'eidus*.

### Chachamim Position

The *chachamim*, by contrast, hold *lav k'mamon dami* — what only indirectly leads to monetary consequence is not treated as monetary in itself. Unless the testimony of the *eid* would directly and immediately obligate the opposing party to pay, it does not qualify as the type of *eidus* that triggers *shvuos ha'eidus*. An *eid echad* whose testimony requires the intermediate step of a *shvuah* — which may or may not be taken — before any monetary consequence materializes, does not meet this threshold.

### Abaye's Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד סוֹטָה, וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵדֵי סוֹטָה, וּמַחְלוֹקֶת בְּעֵדֵי סוֹטָה; הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד אֶחָד, וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד שֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ חָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **the** matter **itself, Abaye says: All concede with regard to a witness** in the case **of a *sota*** that he is liable for taking a false oath of testimony; **and all concede with regard to witnesses** in the case **of a *sota*; and** there is **a dispute with regard to witnesses** in the case **of a *sota*. All concede with regard to one witness, and all concede with regard to a witness** testifying on behalf of the claimant **when his counterpart is suspect about the oath.**

Abaye offers a nuanced statement identifying the precise contours of the dispute: *hakol modim be'eit sotah, ve'hakol modim be'eid sotah, u'machlokes be'eid sotah, hakol modim be'eid echad, ve'hakol modim be'eid she'kenegdo chashud al ha'shvuah*. This cryptic formulation contains five cases — two on which everyone agrees the witness is *chayav*, two on which everyone agrees he is *patur*, and one that falls within the active *machlokes*. We will unpack each in turn.

### Sotah Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד סוֹטָה – שֶׁחַיָּיב, בְּעֵד טוּמְאָה; דְּרַחֲמָנָא הֵימְנֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב ״וְעֵד אֵין בָּהּ״ – כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **All concede with regard to a witness** in the case **of a *sota* that** he is **liable** for taking a false oath of testimony **in** the case of **a witness of impurity.** This is referring to a case where the husband issues a warning to his wife in the presence of two witnesses that she may not enter into seclusion with a certain man and witnesses testify that she entered into seclusion with him, and one witness testifies that she engaged in intercourse with that man, **as** in that case **the Merciful One accorded credibility to** the witness, **as it is written** with regard to a *sota*: **"And there is no witness against her"** (Numbers 5:13), that she engaged in intercourse. From that verse it is derived that **any** witness **that there is against her** is sufficient to render her forbidden to her husband and enable him to divorce her without paying the sum stipulated in the marriage contract. Therefore, the witness who testified that she engaged in intercourse with that man is for all intents and purposes a witness to a monetary matter.

The first case of universal agreement: *hakol modim be'eit sotah she'chayav be'eid tumah* — everyone agrees that a witness is *chayav* in the case of the *eid tumah* of a *sotah*. Recall that the *sotah* process involves multiple stages: the husband's warning (*kinuy*), the witness to the wife's seclusion with the warned man (*stirah*), and potentially a witness who actually observed the act of adultery itself (*eid tumah*). Regarding *eid tumah*, the *Torah* explicitly believed even a single witness — *ve'eid ein bah*, from which we derive *yesh bah* — meaning that one witness who testifies to the act of adultery is credited with the status of two witnesses for purposes of the monetary consequence: the wife is divorced and loses her *kesubah*. Since the *eid tumah* directly generates a monetary consequence in the manner of two witnesses, everyone agrees that withholding such testimony while swearing falsely constitutes a full violation of *shvuos ha'eidus*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵדֵי סוֹטָה – שֶׁפָּטוּר, בְּעֵדֵי קִינּוּי; דְּהָוֵה גּוֹרֵם דְּגוֹרֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **And all concede with regard to witnesses** in the case **of a *sota* that** each witness is **exempt** from liability due to a false oath of testimony. This is referring **to** the case of **witnesses of warning,** who testify that the jealous husband warned his wife not to enter into seclusion with a certain man, **as** each witness **is** the **cause of a cause** of financial loss, not a direct cause of that loss. In order to lose payment of her marriage contract, in addition to the witnesses of warning, witnesses of seclusion would also be required, after which either a witness would testify that she engaged in intercourse or she would be required to drink the bitter water of a *sota*, either of which would confirm that she engaged in an adulterous relationship.

The second case of universal agreement runs in the opposite direction: *hakol modim be'eidei kinuy she'patur* — everyone agrees that the witnesses to the husband's warning are *patur* from *shvuos ha'eidus*. Their testimony is twice removed from any monetary consequence. First, the warning witnesses must testify; then, a witness to the seclusion must testify; then, the outcome of the *sotah* ritual must unfold before there is any monetary consequence. This is not merely *davar ha'gorem l'mamon* but *gorem de'gorem* — a cause of a cause — and everyone agrees that this is too attenuated to qualify.

### Sotah Stirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַחְלוֹקֶת בְּעֵדֵי סוֹטָה – בְּעֵדֵי סְתִירָה; מָר סָבַר: דָּבָר הַגּוֹרֵם לְמָמוֹן כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי – וְחַיָּיב, וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו כְּמָמוֹן דָּמֵי – וּפָטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** there is **a dispute with regard to witnesses** in the case **of a *sota*;** this is referring **to witnesses of seclusion** who testify that the woman who was warned by her husband in fact entered into seclusion with the man in question. One **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **a matter that causes financial** loss **is considered** to have **monetary** value, **and** each witness is **liable** for taking a false oath of testimony, as had they testified the woman would lose her marriage contract. **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **hold** that a matter that causes financial loss **is not considered** to have **monetary** value, **and he is exempt.**

The debated case is *eidei stirah* — the witness or witnesses who testify that the wife entered seclusion with the warned man. Even granting for the moment that two witnesses could testify to the *stirah*, their testimony does not directly obligate the wife to lose her *kesubah*. What follows from their testimony is the *sotah* process: she is brought before *beis din*, given the opportunity to drink the *sotah* waters, and only if she refuses — or if she is found guilty — does she lose her *kesubah*. For Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon, since this sequence *could* lead to monetary consequence, *davar ha'gorem l'mamon k'mamon dami* applies and the *chiyuv* exists. For the *chachamim*, since there is no direct monetary consequence — too many intervening steps remain — *lav k'mamon dami* and the witnesses are *patur*. The *Gemara* notes that even in the case of two witnesses to the *stirah*, the *chachamim* maintain their position, since the indirectness of the monetary consequence is not resolved by having two witnesses rather than one.

### Shvua Impossible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּשֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ חָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבוּעָה, הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד אֶחָד דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated above further: **All concede with regard to** a witness testifying on behalf of the claimant **when his counterpart is suspect about the oath. All concede in** the case of **one witness,** as in the incident **with Rabbi Abba.**

Abaye also states that everyone agrees a *chiyuv* exists in cases where the party who would have been obligated to take the *shvuah* cannot do so. The mechanism here is *mitoch she'eino yachol lishava meshalem*: when the *shvuah* cannot be taken, the obligation reverts directly to payment. In such a case, the testimony of even an *eid echad* would have an immediate, direct monetary consequence, since no *shvuah* stands between the testimony and the payment. Everyone — including the *chachamim* — agrees that this qualifies as *eidus* relating to *mamon*, and that withholding it constitutes *shvuos ha'eidus*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּשֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ חָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבוּעָה – דַּחֲשִׁיד מַאן? אִילֵימָא דַּחֲשִׁיד לֹוֶה, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ מַלְוֶה: אִי אֲתֵית אַסְהֵדְתְּ לִי, הֲוָה מִשְׁתְּבַעְנָא וְשָׁקֵילְנָא; וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: מִי יֵימַר דְּמִשְׁתְּבַעְתְּ? אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶן חֲשׁוּדֵין, דְּאָמַר מָר: חָזְרָה שְׁבוּעָה לַמְחוּיָּב לָהּ, וּמִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **All concede with regard to** a witness testifying on behalf of the claimant **when his counterpart is suspect about the oath.** The testimony of one witness renders the borrower liable to take an oath that he does not owe money. The Gemara asks: In a case **where who is suspect? If we say that the borrower** who is denying the debt **is suspect,** and it is a case **where the lender says to** the witness: **If you came** and **testified on my** behalf **I would have taken an oath and taken** the sum owed me from the borrower, since he is suspect with regard to oaths, why would the witness be liable for taking a false oath of testimony? **Let** the witness **say to** the lender: **Who** could **say that you** would have **taken an oath?** Since there is no certainty that he would have taken the oath, the witness is merely the cause of a cause of financial loss. **Rather,** it is a case **where both** the borrower and the lender **are suspect, as the Master says:** Since both are suspect, the **oath reverts to the one who is** initially **obligated to take it,** i.e., the borrower, **and since he is unable to take the oath** because he is suspect, **he pays** the entire claim to the lender. One witness rendered the borrower liable to pay the debt.

The scenario Abaye identifies is where *she'kenegdo chashud al ha'shvuah* — the opposing party is disqualified from taking a *shvuah* due to prior misconduct. The *Gemara* clarifies: if only the *loveh* (borrower) is suspect and cannot take a *shvuah*, the *shvuah* would transfer to the *malveh* (lender), who could then swear and collect. There remains the intermediate step of the *malveh* actually taking the *shvuah*, which the *malveh* might decline to do — so the testimony still does not *directly* obligate payment, and the *chachamim* would not necessarily agree. The relevant case is where *both* parties are suspect and neither can take a *shvuah*. In that event, the *shvuah* reverts to the *loveh*, who cannot take it, and he must pay directly. The *eid echad's* testimony would thus have immediate monetary consequence, and everyone agrees he is *chayav*.

### Abba Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד אֶחָד דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא – דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּחֲטַף נְסָכָא מֵחַבְרֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, הֲוָה יָתֵיב רַבִּי אַבָּא קַמֵּיהּ; אֲזַל אַיְיתִי חַד סָהֲדָא דְּמִיחְטָף חַטְפַהּ מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אִין, חֲטַפִי – וְדִידִי חֲטַפִי״.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said: **All concede in** the case of **one witness,** as in the incident **with Rabbi Abba.** What are the circumstances? **As** there was **a certain man who snatched** a silver **ingot from another.** The one from whom it was taken **came before Rabbi Ami** while **Rabbi Abba was sitting before him,** and he **went** and **brought one witness** who testified **that** the defendant indeed **snatched it from him.** The one who snatched it **said to him: Yes,** it is true that **I snatched** it, **but I** merely **snatched** that which was **mine.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הֵיכִי (לִדַיְּינֵי) [לִידַיְּינוּהּ] דַּיָּינֵי לְהַאי דִּינָא? לִישַׁלֵּם – לֵיכָּא תְּרֵי סָהֲדִי! לִיפְטְרֵיהּ – הָא אִיכָּא חַד סָהֲדָא דְּמִחְטָף חֲטַף! לִישְׁתְּבַע – כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״אֵין, חֲטַפִי – וְדִידִי חֲטַפִי״, הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּגַזְלָן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא: הָוֵה לֵיהּ מְחוּיָּב שְׁבוּעָה וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע, וְכׇל הַמְחוּיָּב שְׁבוּעָה וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said: How shall judges rule in this case? Let** the judges require him to **pay.** But **there are not two witnesses** who witnessed the robbery. **Let** the judges rule to **exempt him** from payment. **But there is one witness that he snatched** the ingot. Based on the testimony of that witness, **let** the alleged robber **take an oath** that he did not snatch the ingot. But **once he said: Yes, I snatched** it, **but I** merely **snatched** that which was **mine,** his halakhic status **is like** that of **a robber,** who is disqualified from taking an oath. **Rabbi Abba said to him: He is** one who is **obligated** to take **an oath but is unable to take an oath, and anyone who is obligated** to take **an oath but is unable to take an oath** is liable to **pay.** In that case, were that witness to take a false oath and deny knowledge of the matter, he would be liable.

The final case where everyone agrees — *hakol modim be'eid echad de'Rabbi Abba* — involves a striking incident adjudicated by Rabbi Ami in which Rabbi Abba was present. A man had seized a chunk of metal (*naska*) from his fellow. The aggrieved party brought a single witness who testified to the seizure. The accused did not deny the act: he admitted that he took it — *chatafi vedidi chatafi*, "I grabbed it, and what I grabbed was mine." He claimed he was merely reclaiming his own property.

Rabbi Ami was uncertain how to rule. The case could not simply result in payment, since there were not two witnesses. Nor could the accused simply be released, since there was one witness whose testimony had to be addressed. Normally, one witness obligates the opposing party to take a *shvuah*. But here lies the difficulty: the *shvuah* the *Torah* would have him take — denying that he took the object — is a *shvuah* he cannot take, since he has already admitted to taking it. Rabbi Ami asked whether he might take a different *shvuah* — one affirming that he took only what was rightfully his — in lieu of the *shvuah* he cannot take.

Rabbi Abba ruled: *hava leh mechuyav shvuah ve'eino yachol lishava, ve'chol hamechuyav shvuah ve'eino yachol lishava meshalem* — he has an obligation to take a *shvuah* and is unable to do so, and in such a case he must pay. The fact that he has an explanation for his conduct does not alter the analysis. The *shvuah* the *Torah* obligates him to take is the *shvuah* that would contradict the *eid*, and that *shvuah* he cannot make. He therefore must pay.

This case parallels the preceding one: just as when both parties are suspect and the *shvuah* cannot be taken by anyone, the *eid echad's* testimony translates directly into a monetary obligation — so too here. The witness who denied knowledge of this seizure was withholding testimony that would have immediately led to payment, and therefore everyone agrees he is *chayav* for *shvuos ha'eidus*.

## Death Testimony

### Death Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד מִיתָה שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב, וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד מִיתָה שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa says: All concede with regard to** the **witness of a death that he is liable** for taking an oath of testimony if he took a false oath and denied knowledge of the incident, **and all concede with regard to** the **witness of a death that he is exempt** in that case.

Rav Papa constructs a parallel framework using the testimony of witnesses regarding a husband's death. The halachic backdrop is the leniency of *chazal* to permit a woman to remarry — and to collect her *kesubah* — based on the testimony of even a single witness, or even hearsay (*eid mipi eid*), in circumstances where testimony about death is otherwise unavailable. Since there are also monetary consequences to this testimony — the woman collects her *kesubah* — Rav Papa identifies scenarios that map precisely onto the structure of Abaye's statement: cases where everyone agrees the witness is *chayav*, cases where everyone agrees he is *patur*, and implicitly, cases falling within the active *machlokes*.

### Private Tell

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד מִיתָה שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר – דַּאֲמַר לַהּ לְדִידַהּ וְלָא אֲמַר לְהוּ לְבֵית דִּין, דִּתְנַן: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁאָמְרָה ״מֵת בַּעְלִי״ – תִּנָּשֵׂא, ״מֵת בַּעְלִי״ – תִּתְיַבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **All concede with regard to** the **witness of a death that he is exempt where he said to** the wife **herself** that her husband is dead, **but he did not tell** it **to the court,** and now he denies his earlier statement, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Eduyyot* 1:12): **The woman who said: My husband died, shall remarry** on the basis of her own testimony. Likewise, if she says: **My husband died, she shall enter into levirate marriage** with her brother-in-law on the basis of her own testimony. The fact that the witness subsequently denied knowledge of the matter does not cause her to lose her marriage contract, since she can come to the court and testify based on the statement of the witness and collect her marriage contract.

The first case where everyone agrees the witness is *patur*: *d'amar la l'didah ve'lo amar lehu l'vei dina* — he told the woman privately that her husband had died, but when called before *beis din* he denied it and took a false *shvuah*. In this scenario, his testimony before *beis din* was no longer needed. The *mishnah* teaches: *ha'isha she'amra meis ba'ali tinasei* — a woman who declares her husband has died may remarry based on her own statement if she heard it from a reliable source, including a single witness. Once he told her directly, his testimony accomplished its full effect. His subsequent denial in *beis din* is therefore inconsequential, and he is *patur*.

### Refuses Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֵד מִיתָה שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב – דְּלָא אָמַר לְדִידַהּ וְלָא אֲמַר לְהוּ לְבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **All concede with regard to** the **witness of a death that he is liable** in a case **where he did not say** that the husband is dead **to** the wife **herself nor did he say** it **to the court.** In that case, his denial of knowledge of the matter causes the wife to lose payment of her marriage contract.

The second case where everyone agrees the witness is *chayav*: *d'lo amar la l'didah ve'lo amar lehu l'vei dina* — he refused to tell the woman privately and refused to testify before *beis din*. In this scenario, his testimony — had he given it either to her or to *beis din* — would have had immediate monetary consequences. The woman would have been permitted to remarry and to collect her *kesubah*. This is analogous to the *eid tumah* in the *sotah* case: the testimony of a single witness, credited with the weight necessary to generate an immediate monetary consequence, and withheld through a false oath, constitutes a full *chiyuv* of *shvuos ha'eidus*.

### Monetary Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מַשְׁבִּיעַ עֵדֵי קַרְקַע חַיָּיב? דִּלְמָא דִּתְפִישָׂא מִטַּלְטְלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Shall we **conclude from** here that according to Rav Pappa, in the case of **one who administers an oath to witnesses** with regard to **land,** the witnesses are **liable** for taking a false oath of testimony, given that the marriage contract here is collected from land belonging to the husband? This matter is subject to a dispute between the Sages (37b). The Gemara answers: No proof may be cited from here, as **perhaps** Rav Pappa is referring to a case **where she seizes** her husband's **movable property** as payment for her marriage contract. Therefore, this is not considered testimony with regard to land.

The *Gemara* closes with a brief observation. If the woman's *kesubah* is secured by liens on land (*karka*), one might ask whether the *chiyuv* of *shvuos ha'eidus* still applies — since the normal rule is that there is no *shvuah* with respect to *karka*. The *Gemara* rejects any inference from this case: *dilma d'tfisa metaltalei* — perhaps in the case under discussion the woman was holding chattel in escrow as security for her *kesubah*, not land. No proof can therefore be adduced here regarding the question of *shvuah* where liens on land are involved.

The underlying principle that emerges from the entire discussion is that *shvuos ha'eidus* requires that the withheld testimony have a genuine and direct monetary effect. Where that monetary connection is immediate, whether for two witnesses or for an *eid echad* in those particular circumstances recognized by all, the *chiyuv* is clear. Where the monetary consequence is indirect, attenuated, or contingent on further steps, it is precisely this point that divides Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Shimon and the *chachamim*.
