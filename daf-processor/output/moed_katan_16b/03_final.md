# Moed Katan 16b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nidui/Nezifa Durations

### Nidui: 30 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין נִידּוּי פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וְאֵין נְזִיפָה פָּחוֹת מִשִּׁבְעָה יָמִים. וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָבִיהָ יָרֹק יָרַק בְּפָנֶיהָ הֲלֹא תִכָּלֵם שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught the following *baraita*: **Ostracism does not** apply **for less than** a period of **thirty days, and admonition,** which is less severe than ostracism, **does not** apply **for less than** a period of **seven days. And although there is no proof with regard to the matter,** i.e., the standard duration of admonition, there is **an allusion to the matter, as it is stated: "If her father had but spit in her face, should she not be ashamed for seven days?"** (Numbers 12:14). This implies that admonition lasts for seven days.

The Gemara opens with a *baraita*: *ein nidui pachot mi-shloshim yom* — *nidui* carries a minimum duration of thirty days. As we have discussed previously, while it is theoretically possible to reverse a *nidui* earlier, the standard *nidui* runs for thirty days.

### Nezifa: 7 Days Min.

*Nezifa* is a lighter form of censure than *nidui*. The Gemara never fully explains what the practical observances of *nezifa* consist of — the only thing we can discern from the sugya is that it is of shorter duration, and perhaps that it involves the same practices but for a briefer period. The *baraita* establishes: *ein nezifa pachot mi-shiva yamim* — there is no *nezifa* of less than seven days. The Gemara adds that while there is no formal proof for this, there is a *zecher la-davar*, a hint: *ve-aviha yarok yarak be-faneha halo tikkalem shivat yamim* — if her father had spat in her face, would she not be in a state of abeyance for seven days? This is actually a compelling indication, since God says to Moses that there must be some minimum period appropriate to the state of being censured, and that minimum is seven days.

### Bavel vs. Eretz Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: נִידּוּי שֶׁלָּנוּ כִּנְזִיפָה שֶׁלָּהֶן, וּנְזִיפָה דִּידְהוּ שִׁבְעָה וְתוּ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: Our** decree of **ostracism** in Babylonia has the power **of their admonition** in Eretz Yisrael. Since the authorities in Eretz Yisrael are ordained with the title Rabbi, their admonition carries more weight than a decree of ostracism issued in Babylonia.

Rav Chisda states: *nidui she-lanu ke-nezifa she-lahen* — our *nidui* in Bavel is equivalent to their *nezifa* in Eretz Yisrael, which presumably means that the standard *nidui* in Bavel was of shorter duration than in Eretz Yisrael. This is a striking observation. Why would that be? Perhaps the rabbinic authorities in Bavel had adopted a less severe approach, or perhaps they exercised a somewhat different degree of institutional authority — one less insulated from the surrounding secular society and its norms.

### Ashkenazi vs. Sephardi

This difference between Bavel and Eretz Yisrael invites a historical parallel to the later distinction between *Ashkenazi* and *Sephardi* communities. *Ashkenazi* communities were by and large more cloistered, and as a result rabbinic authority tended to be stronger within them. *Sephardi* communities were more cosmopolitan, more integrated into the broader surrounding society, and correspondingly the exercise of rabbinic authority may have been somewhat attenuated. Interestingly, *Ashkenazi* culture traces more directly from Eretz Yisrael, while *Sephardi* culture draws more from Bavel — which makes the parallel all the more suggestive when thinking about why the forms of censure were less severe in Bavel than in Eretz Yisrael.

## Self-Imposed Nezifa

### Difficult Sugya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי וּבַר קַפָּרָא הֲווֹ יָתְבִי וְקָא גָרְסִי, קַשְׁיָא לְהוּ שְׁמַעְתָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְבַר קַפָּרָא: דָּבָר זֶה צָרִיךְ רַבִּי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ בַּר קַפָּרָא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וּמָה רַבִּי אוֹמֵר בְּדָבָר זֶה?
>
> **Translation:** But isn't it related that **Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and bar Kappara were sitting and studying,** and they posed **a difficulty with** a certain ***halakha*. Rabbi Shimon said to bar Kappara: This issue requires** my father, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, to explain it. **Bar Kappara said to Rabbi Shimon,** somewhat mockingly: **And what can** your father, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **say about this** issue? What can he add and teach us about it?

The Gemara now challenges the notion that *nezifa* in Eretz Yisrael is only seven days. It relates that Rav Shimon, the son of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and Bar Kappara were sitting together and studying, when they encountered a *shemat'ta* — a passage — that was extremely difficult for them. Rav Shimon said to Bar Kappara: *davar zeh tzarich Rabbi* — this matter requires Rabbi, meaning his father. The implication was: this *sugya* is so hard that only Rabbi could resolve it. Bar Kappara responded: *u-mah Rabbi omer be-davar zeh* — what could Rabbi possibly say about this? Rabbeinu Gershom reads Bar Kappara's words as: there is no rabbi in the world who could understand this, the *sugya* is simply too difficult.

### Bar Kappara's Nezifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל אֲמַר לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּהּ, אִיקְּפַד. אֲתָא בַּר קַפָּרָא לְאִיתְחֲזוֹיֵי לֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּר קַפָּרָא, אֵינִי מַכִּירְךָ מֵעוֹלָם! יְדַע דִּנְקַט מִילְּתָא בְּדַעְתֵּיהּ, נְהַג נְזִיפוּתָא בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon **went and told his father,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, what bar Kappara had said, and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **became angry** with him. When **bar Kappara came** at some later point **to visit,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Bar Kappara, I do not know you at all.** Bar Kappara **understood that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **had taken** his **statement to heart,** i.e., was insulted. He subsequently **behaved** as if he had been **admonished,** as a self-imposed punishment, **for thirty days.**

Rav Shimon went and relayed this exchange to his father. Rabbeinu Gershom notes that this was not an act of telling tales — he was simply sharing with his father what had transpired. Nevertheless, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was offended. When Bar Kappara subsequently came to appear before Rabbi — perhaps to visit him during an illness, as Rabbeinu Gershom suggests — Rabbi said to him: *eini makircha*, I do not recognize you, I have never known who you are. He refused to acknowledge him in any way.

Bar Kappara understood from this that Rabbi was holding something against him. And so *nahag nezifuta b'nafshei tlatin yomin* — he imposed upon himself a state of *nezifuta* for thirty days. Two things are worth noting here. First, the practice of imposing this state upon oneself: we will see shortly that this applies not only to *nezifa* but also to *nidui* — one can place oneself into that status. Second, the duration of thirty days raises a question: if *nezifa* and *nidui* differ only in length, and *nidui* is also thirty days, then there must be a qualitative difference between them in terms of the actual practices involved, even if the Gemara never spells out what those practices are. Just as *nidui* and *cherem* differ in the intensity of their observances rather than merely in duration, presumably *nezifa* represents a lesser degree of restriction than *nidui* — we simply do not know precisely what that set of practices entails.

### Torah in Marketplace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוּב פַּעַם אֶחָד גָּזַר רַבִּי שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁנוּ לַתַּלְמִידִים בַּשּׁוּק, מַאי דְּרַשׁ — ״חַמּוּקֵי יְרֵכַיִךְ כְּמוֹ חֲלָאִים״. מָה יָרֵךְ בַּסֵּתֶר, אַף דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה בַּסֵּתֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Once again, on another occasion, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **decreed that students not be taught in the marketplace** but only in a study hall. **What** verse did he **expound** to serve as the basis for this decree? The verse states: **"Your rounded thighs are like jewels,** the work of the hands of an artist" (Song of Songs 7:2). **Just** as **a thigh is** ordinarily **hidden** and kept covered with clothes, **so too, the words of Torah,** which are "the work of the hands of an artist," i.e., God, must remain **hidden** in the study hall.

Subsequently, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi issued a *gezeira* — a decree — that students should not study Torah in the marketplace. This was not simply a matter of the *shuk* being noisy or malodorous; there was also a principled basis for the decree. The Gemara asks: *mai darish* — what verse did Rabbi expound to support this? He derived it from the verse in Shir HaShirim, *chamukei yerachayich* — the hidden curves of the thigh. Just as the thigh is kept private and unseen, *af divrei Torah b'seter* — so too words of Torah must be kept in private. There is an aspect of elitism here, a sense that Torah is something to be kept away from those who do not appreciate it, and there is also a dimension of intimacy: the deeply personal and even relational metaphors used throughout Shir HaShirim for the relationship between the learner and the Torah suggest that Torah study is something whose very nature calls for privacy.

### Rabbi Chiya's Nezifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְשָׁנָה לִשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אֶחָיו בַּשּׁוּק, לְרַב וּלְרַבָּה בַּר (בַּר) חָנָה. שְׁמַע רַבִּי, אִיקְּפַד. אֲתָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא לְאִיתְחֲזוֹיֵי לֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עִיָּיא, מִי קוֹרֵא לְךָ בַּחוּץ! יְדַע דִּנְקַט מִילְּתָא בְּדַעְתֵּיהּ, נְהַג נְזִיפוּתָא בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין.
>
> **Translation:** Despite Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's decree, **Rabbi Ḥiyya went out and taught his two nephews, Rav and Rabba bar bar Ḥana, in the marketplace. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **heard** what he had done and **became angry** with him. When **Rabbi Ḥiyya came** at some later date **to visit him,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi mockingly **said to him: Iyya, who is calling you outside?** By asking this question Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was intimating that Rabbi Ḥiyya should leave his house. Rabbi Ḥiyya **understood that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **had taken the matter to heart** and was insulted, and so **he conducted** himself as if he had been **admonished,** as a self-imposed punishment, **for thirty days.**

Despite this decree, Rabbi Chiyya went out and gave a *shiur* to his two nephews — Rav and Rabbah bar bar Chana — in the marketplace. Rabbi heard of this and was offended. When Rabbi Chiyya came to appear before him, Rabbi said: *Iyya* — mocking his name by dropping the *chet* — *mi kore l'cha bachutz*, I believe someone is calling you outside. This was Rabbi's way of indicating that he refused to recognize Rabbi Chiyya, though the form was slightly more nuanced than his treatment of Bar Kappara — he did not say outright "I do not know you." One might also note the pointed irony: the reprimand itself contained the word *bachutz*, outside, given that the offense was teaching Torah *bachutz*.

Rabbi Chiyya understood that Rabbi was angry with him, and so *nahag nezifuta b'nafshei tlatin yomin* — he too imposed upon himself this state of *nezifuta* for thirty days.

### Measuring Nezifa Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּיוֹם תְּלָתִין שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: תָּא. הֲדַר שְׁלַח לֵיהּ דְּלָא לֵיתֵי. מֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי סְבַר וּלְבַסּוֹף מַאי סְבַר? מֵעִיקָּרָא סָבַר: מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ. וּלְבַסּוֹף סָבַר: לָא אָמְרִינַן מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **On the thirtieth day,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **sent him** a message, saying: **Come** and visit me. However, **he later** reversed his opinion and **sent him** another message, telling him **not to come.** The Gemara asks: **At the outset what did he hold, and ultimately what did he hold? Initially,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **held that** the legal status of **part of the day is like** that **of an entire** day, and since the thirtieth day already begun, Rabbi Ḥiyya's time of admonition had ended. **But ultimately he held** that with regard to this issue **we do not say** that the legal status of **part of the day is like** that **of an entire** day.

On day thirty, Rabbi sent for Rabbi Chiyya and called him to come. Then Rabbi sent a second messenger telling him not to come. The Gemara asks: *me'ikara mai savar u'l'vasof mai savar* — what did Rabbi think initially, and why did he reverse himself? Initially he held *miktzat hayom k'kulo* — that part of the day counts as the whole, and since it was already day thirty, even the beginning of that day was sufficient to conclude the *nezifuta*. In the end, he decided not to apply that principle to *nezifuta*, and so he sent the second message. This is an interesting parallel to the laws of *aveilus*, where the same question of *miktzat hayom k'kulo* arises regarding the seventh and thirtieth days of mourning.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף אֲתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי אֲתֵית? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּשָׁלַח לִי מָר דְּלֵיתֵי. וְהָא שְׁלַחִי לָךְ דְּלָא תֵּיתֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זֶה רָאִיתִי, וְזֶה לֹא רָאִיתִי. קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ: ״בִּרְצוֹת ה׳ דַּרְכֵי אִישׁ גַּם אוֹיְבָיו יַשְׁלִים אִתּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **In the end** Rabbi Ḥiyya **came** on that same day. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **asked him: Why have you come?** Rabbi Ḥiyya **responded: Because** you, **Master, sent** me a message that **I should come.** He said to him: **But I sent** you a second message **that you should not come. He responded: This** messenger that you sent, i.e., the first one, **I saw** him and I did as he said, **but that** messenger, i.e., the second one, **I did not see.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **read** the verse **about** Rabbi Ḥiyya: **"When a man's ways please the Lord, He makes even his enemies to be at peace with him"** (Proverbs 16:7), as it was clear to him that Rabbi Ḥiyya had merited divine assistance.

Rabbi Chiyya received the first message but not the second, and so he came. Rabbi asked him: *amai atit* — why did you come? Rabbi Chiyya answered: because you called me. Rabbi replied that he had subsequently sent word not to come. Rabbi Chiyya responded: *da chaziti v'da lo chaziti* — I received the first message, I did not receive the second.

Rabbi then recited the verse about him: *birtzot Hashem darchai ish, gam oyvav yashlim lo* — when God desires the ways of a man, even his enemies will make peace with him. Here there was a measure of *hashgacha pratit*: the second message did not reach Rabbi Chiyya, and so reconciliation came today rather than tomorrow, through divine providence rather than formal process.

### Torah Accessibility Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא עֲבַד מָר הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״חׇכְמוֹת בַּחוּץ תָּרוֹנָּה״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם קָרִיתָ — לֹא שָׁנִיתָ, וְאִם שָׁנִיתָ — לֹא שִׁילַּשְׁתָּ, וְאִם שִׁילַּשְׁתָּ — לֹא פֵּירְשׁוּ לְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** § Concerning the issue with which the entire incident had begun, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi asked Rabbi Ḥiyya: **What is the reason that** you, **the Master, acted as** you did, ignoring my instructions not to teach Torah in the marketplace? Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: As it is written: "Wisdom cries aloud in the streets"** (Proverbs 1:20), which implies that Torah should be publicized in the streets. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: If you read** this verse once, **you** certainly **did not read** it **a second time** in greater depth; **and if you read** it **a second time, you** certainly **did not read** it **a third time;** and **if you read** it **a third time,** then **it was not** adequately **explained to you,** as it is clear that you do not understand it properly.

Rabbi then said: now that thirty days have passed and you have appropriately demonstrated that you acted improperly, let us discuss it — *mai ta'ama amart lah hachi*, why did you violate my ruling? Rabbi Chiyya cited the verse *chachmot bachutz taronah* — wisdom should sing out in the open, Torah should be public. We should not be keeping it secluded.

### Rabbi's Counter-Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חׇכְמוֹת בַּחוּץ תָּרוֹנָּה״, כִּדְרָבָא. דְּאָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה מִבִּפְנִים, תּוֹרָתוֹ מַכְרֶזֶת עָלָיו מִבַּחוּץ. וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״לֹא מֵרֹאשׁ בַּסֵּתֶר דִּבַּרְתִּי״! הָהוּא בְּיוֹמֵי דְכַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The words: **"Wisdom cries aloud in the streets,"** should be understood **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava. As Rava said:** With regard to **everyone who occupies himself with Torah** study **inside** the privacy of his home, **his Torah** knowledge **will proclaim his** greatness **outside,** as it will be revealed to the masses and they will see his greatness. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written: "From the beginning I have not spoken in secret"** (Isaiah 48:16), implying that the Torah should be taught and proclaimed in public? The Gemara answers: **That** verse is referring to **the days of the *kalla*,** the gathering for Torah study held during Elul and Adar, when many people come to listen to Torah discourses. During this time, it is not only permitted but even recommended to teach Torah to the masses. In this way, the verse can be explained in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

Rabbi replied: *im karita lo shanita* — if you read the verse, you did not study it sufficiently. *Ve-im shanita lo shilashta* — and if you studied it twice, you did not do it a third time. *Ve-im shilashta lo pirshu lach* — and if you did it three times, it was not properly explained to you. How should the verse be understood? Like Rava, who — anachronistically — explained: *kol ha'osek ba-Torah mibifnim Torato machrezet alav mibachutz*. It does not mean that you go out and proclaim your Torah in the street. It means that if you study Torah in private, as Rabbi had decreed, your wisdom will nevertheless announce itself outward. The internal devotion radiates outward — you do not bring the Torah outside, rather it emerges on its own.

The Gemara then raises a challenge: does the verse not say *lo me-rosh be-seter dibarti* — I did not speak initially in secret? This would seem to imply that Torah is not supposed to be kept private. The answer is: *ahu be-yomei de-kalla* — that refers to the days of the *kalla*, when everyone gathers before the *yamim tovim* for a large public *shiur*. There are specific occasions for public teaching, but that does not mean one is always to be teaching Torah in public settings.

### Charity Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא, הַאי ״חַמּוּקֵי יְרֵכַיִךְ״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵהּ? מוֹקֵי לֵהּ בִּצְדָקָה וּבִגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did Rabbi Ḥiyya do with this** verse: **"Your rounded thighs are like jewels"?** How did he understand it? This verse implies that the Torah must be kept hidden in the study hall and not publicized in the marketplace. The Gemara explains: **He interprets it** not as a reference to Torah, but as referring **to** acts of **charity and loving-kindness,** which should certainly be performed in private.

The Gemara then addresses what Rabbi Chiyya does with the verse *chamukei yerachayich* — the one about the hidden thigh that Rabbi had expounded. Rabbi Chiyya's response is: *muki leh bi-tzedakah u-gemilut chasadim* — he applies that verse to acts of *tzedakah* and *gemilut chasadim*, which indeed should be kept private, both out of respect for the recipient and to avoid any self-congratulatory display of one's own generosity. But Torah, Rabbi Chiyya maintains, is something that should be spread and made public.

### Proportional Calculations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא נְזִיפָה דִּידְהוּ תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין! נְזִיפַת נָשִׂיא שָׁאנֵי. וּנְזִיפָה דִּידַן כַּמָּה הָוֵי? חַד יוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** This incident demonstrates **that, apparently, admonition of those** who live in Eretz Yisrael lasts for **thirty days** and not for seven days. The Gemara answers that this is not a conclusive proof, since Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was the *Nasi*. **The admonition of the *Nasi*** of the Sanhedrin **is different** i.e., more severe, than the admonition of anyone else. **And how** long **is our admonition** in Babylonia? The Gemara answers: It is only **one day,**

At this point the Gemara raises the question: does this not demonstrate that their *nezifa* in Eretz Yisrael was thirty days — *nezifa d'nasi shani* — the *nezifa* arising from the censure of a *nasi* carries different weight. It was not Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi himself who placed them in *nezifa*; they imposed the practice on themselves. But in effect, they had been censured — one might say they had been "spat in the face," to use the Gemara's earlier metaphor — by Rabbi, and they responded by adopting the *nezifa* practices accordingly.

One might speculate about what *nezifa* actually consists of in practice. In every one of these stories, the *nezifa* involves not appearing before the person one has offended. In the case of Miriam, she is *michutz la-machaneh*, outside the camp. Perhaps *nezifa* simply means refraining from appearing before the person who has censured you for the duration of the period. The Gemara never specifies anything further, and this may in fact be the entirety of the practice.

The Gemara then works out the proportional logic: their *nezifa* in Eretz Yisrael is seven days; our *nidui* in Bavel equals their *nezifa*, so our *nidui* is seven days; and our *nezifa* — *kama havei* — would then be only *chad yoma*, one day. Everything in Bavel is of shorter duration and lesser severity than in Eretz Yisrael.

### Shmuel and Mar Ukva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל וּמָר עוּקְבָא, כִּי הֲווֹ יָתְבִי גָּרְסִי שְׁמַעְתָּא, הֲוָה יָתֵיב מָר עוּקְבָא קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל בְּרָחוֹק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. וְכִי הֲווֹ יָתְבִי בְּדִינָא, הֲוָה יָתֵיב שְׁמוּאֵל קַמֵּיהּ דְּמָר עוּקְבָא בְּרָחוֹק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. וַהֲווֹ חָיְיקִי לֵיהּ דּוּכְתָּא לְמָר עוּקְבָא בְּצִיפְּתָא, וְיָתֵיב עִילָּוֵיהּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִישְׁתַּמְעָן מִילֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** as in the case involving **Shmuel and** the Exilarch **Mar Ukva. When they would sit and study *halakha*, Mar Ukva would sit before Shmuel at a distance of four cubits** as a sign of respect. Mar Ukva would conduct himself as though Shmuel were his teacher because Shmuel was much greater than him in Torah matters. **And when they would sit** together **in judgment, Shmuel would sit before Mar Ukva at a distance of four cubits** because Mar Ukva was the Exilarch and the chief judge. **But they would lower a place for Mar Ukva in the matting** upon which he sat, **and he would sit on it so that he could hear** Shmuel's **words** of Torah even when they were engaged in judgment.

This is illustrated by the story of Shmuel and Mar Ukva. They would sit together, with Mar Ukva positioned *be-richuk arba amos* — four *amos* — from Shmuel, as a demonstration of deference to Shmuel's greater Torah authority. When they sat in *din*, the relationship reversed: Shmuel sat *be-richuk arba amos* from Mar Ukva, because Mar Ukva — who was the *Reish Galusa*, or at least held some equivalent political role — was the greater judicial authority. Each of them was always four *amos* from the other, but the question of who sat "before" the other determined who was playing the secondary role.

### Mar Ukva's Single-Day Nezifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל יוֹמָא הֲוָה מְלַוִי לֵיהּ מָר עוּקְבָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל עַד אוּשְׁפִּיזֵיהּ. יוֹמָא חַד אִיטְּרִיד בְּדִינֵיהּ הֲוָה אָזֵיל שְׁמוּאֵל בָּתְרֵיהּ. כִּי מְטָא לְבֵיתֵיהּ, אָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא נְגַהּ לָךְ? לִישְׁרֵי לִי מָר בְּתִיגְרֵיהּ! יְדַע דִּנְקַט מִילְּתָא בְּדַעְתֵּיהּ, נְהַג נְזִיפוּתָא בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ חַד יוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Every day, Mar Ukva would accompany Shmuel to his lodgings,** in the manner that a student would show honor toward his teacher. **One day,** Mar Ukva **was** so heavily **preoccupied with a case** that had been brought before him for judgment that he did not realize that **Shmuel was walking behind him** to show him respect due to his position as the Exilarch. **When** Mar Ukva **reached his home,** Shmuel **said to him: Is it not enough for you** that I accompanied you until here? **Release me, Master, from my obligation,** so that I may return home. Mar Ukva **understood that** Shmuel **had taken the matter to heart** and was insulted. Therefore, **he conducted** himself as if he had been **admonished, for one day** as a self-imposed punishment.

Every day Mar Ukva would accompany Shmuel to his lodgings. One day, absorbed in a complex court case, Mar Ukva forgot his usual custom and went home by himself, whereupon Shmuel followed him. When they arrived at Mar Ukva's door, Shmuel said: *lo naga lei le-mar le-mishrei li be-tigra* — is it not enough that I have followed you this far? Can I not be excused to go home? Mar Ukva, preoccupied with his own thoughts, had not even noticed that Shmuel had been following him and was still standing outside waiting to be dismissed. When Mar Ukva realized what had happened, he understood that Shmuel was displeased with him. And so *nahag nezifuta b'nafshei chad yoma* — he imposed upon himself this state of *nezifuta* for one day. This confirms that *nezifuta* in Bavel is one day.

### Woman Sifting Barley

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּהֲווֹ יָתְבָה בִּשְׁבִילָא, הֲווֹת פָּשְׁטָה כַּרְעַהּ וְקָא מְנִיפָה חוּשְׁלָאֵי, וַהֲוָה חָלֵיף וְאָזֵיל צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן וְלָא אִיכַּנְעָה מִקַּמֵּיהּ. אֲמַר: כַּמָּה חַצִּיפָא הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא! אֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן. אֲמַר לַהּ: מִי שְׁמַעְתְּ שַׁמְתָּא מִפּוּמֵּיהּ? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: זִילִי נְהוּגִי נְזִיפוּתָא חַד יוֹמָא בְּנַפְשִׁיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** It was related that **a certain woman was sitting alongside a path** with **her leg extended** while **she was sifting barley. A Torah scholar passed** by her on this path, **but she did not yield to him** and move her leg to make room for him. **He said: How rude is that woman!** The woman **came before Rav Naḥman** to ask if this statement should be deemed as excommunication. **He said to her: Did you hear** the word **excommunication** explicitly issue **from his mouth? She said to him: No. He said to her:** If this is the case, then **go and observe an admonition for one day,** as it appears that the Torah scholar sought only to admonish you.

The story of the woman sifting barley on the path adds another dimension to how *nezifa* operates. A *tzurba me-rabbanan* passed by; she was sitting with her legs stretched out and did not show him deference. He said publicly: *kama chatzipa hi iteta* — how brazen is this woman. She came before Rav Nachman and complained that he had publicly shamed her. Rav Nachman asked her: *mi shamas shamta mi-pumei* — did he actually utter a formal declaration of *nidui*? She said no. He told her: *zili nehog nezifuta chad yoma be-nafshach* — go and practice *nezifuta* on yourself for one day.

What is striking across all of these cases is that in none of them does the rabbi formally issue a *nezifa*. Rather, the *nezifuta* is always self-imposed: the person perceives from the rabbi's reaction — a refusal to recognize him, a mocking comment, an expression of displeasure — that he has given offense and has been censured, and then adopts the practice on his own. The rabbi never formally declares it. Perhaps this is the essence of *nezifuta*: it is your own internalized recognition that you have offended a Torah authority and your consequent response to that recognition. What the practice concretely involves remains unstated in the Gemara, but in every instance it seems to amount to refraining from appearing before the person one has offended.

## Zutra bar Tuvia

### Pesach Exegesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה הֲוָה קָפָסֵיק סִידְרָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, כִּי מְטָא לְהַאי פְּסוּקָא ״וְאֵלֶּה דִּבְרֵי דָוִד הָאַחֲרוֹנִים״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אַחֲרוֹנִים״ — מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא רִאשׁוֹנִים, רִאשׁוֹנִים מַאי נִינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Zutra bar Toviyya was** once **reading the portion** of the Bible **before Rav Yehuda. When he reached the verse: "Now these are the last words of David"** (II Samuel 23:1), Zutra bar Toviyya **said to** Rav Yehuda: If it is written that these are the **last** of David's words, **by inference there are first** words as well. If this is the case, **what are these first** words of David? Prior to this, it mentions only David's song, but not his words.

Zutra bar Tuvia was teaching the *parshiyot* — going through *pesukim* — before Rav Yehuda. When he arrived at the verse *ve-eileh divrei David ha-achronim* — these are the last words of David — he asked: *achronim mikhlal d'ika rishonim, rishonim mai ninhu* — the fact that it says "last" implies there are earlier words; where are those earlier words? As Rabbeinu Gershom notes, *divrei* in this context means *nevuah*, prophecy — there is only one other place where the phrase *divrei David* appears with that specific meaning. So the question was: where in Scripture does an earlier *divrei David* appear?

### Zutra's Nezifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵיק וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי, הֲדַר אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אַחֲרוֹנִים״, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא רִאשׁוֹנִים, רִאשׁוֹנִים מַאי הִיא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּלָא יָדַע פֵּירוּשָׁא דְּהַאי קְרָא לָאו גַּבְרָא רַבָּה הוּא? יְדַע דִּנְקַט מִילְּתָא בְּדַעְתֵּיהּ, נְהַג נְזִיפוּתָא בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ חַד יוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda **remained silent and said nothing to him.** Zutra bar Toviyya thought that Rav Yehuda did not hear what he had said, so he **then said to him** a second time: If it is written that these are the **last** of David's words, **by inference there are first** words as well. If this is the case, **what are these first** words of David? **He said to him: What do you think?** Do you think that anyone **who does not know the meaning of this verse is not a great man?** Why are you stressing the fact that I do not know the answer to your question? Zutra bar Toviyya **understood that** Rav Yehuda **had taken the matter to heart** and was insulted. Therefore, **he conducted** himself as if had been **admonished for one day** as a self-imposed punishment.

Rav Yehuda was silent and did not respond. Zutra bar Tuvia asked again. This time Rav Yehuda replied: *ma da'atach d'lo yada perusha d'hai kra* — do you think I do not know the meaning of this verse? *Lav gavra raba hu* — am I not a great authority? He spoke in the third person, which itself underscores the emphasis he was placing on the *kavod* owed to him. Notably, the very fact that Rav Yehuda refused to answer twice — and then, when pressed, made clear it was not ignorance but a statement about the respect owed to him — reveals the depth of his offense.

Zutra bar Tuvia understood from this that Rav Yehuda was angry with him, and *nahag nezifuta b'nafshei chad yoma* — he imposed upon himself a state of *nezifuta* for one day. This is another case of Babylonian *nezifuta* lasting a single day.

What is remarkable about all these stories taken together is the picture they paint: the *amoraim* were acutely attuned to the signals a rabbi gave when he was displeased, and the expected response was to independently adopt this practice without waiting for a formal declaration. In a sense, as one might observe, this actually inverts the dynamic of coercive rabbinic authority — rather than power being imposed from above, the recognition of wrongdoing and the consequent response comes from within the offending party himself. And if one can get people to genuinely internalize that sensitivity and deference, that is far more powerful than any formal mechanism of *kefiyah*.

### Resolution of the Exegetical Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדַאֲתָן עֲלַהּ, מִיהָא ״אַחֲרוֹנִים״ — מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא רִאשׁוֹנִים, רִאשׁוֹנִים מַאי הִיא? ״וַיְדַבֵּר דָּוִד לַה׳ אֶת דִּבְרֵי הַשִּׁירָה הַזֹּאת בְּיוֹם הִצִּיל ה׳ אוֹתוֹ מִכַּף כׇּל אוֹיְבָיו וּמִכַּף שָׁאוּל״. אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְדָוִד: דָּוִד, שִׁירָה אַתָּה אוֹמֵר עַל מַפַּלְתּוֹ שֶׁל שָׁאוּל? אִלְמָלֵי אַתָּה שָׁאוּל וְהוּא דָּוִד — אִיבַּדְתִּי כַּמָּה דָּוִד מִפָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** now **that we have come** to discuss this issue, since the verse mentions David's **last** words, **by inference there are** also **first** words. **What** then **are these first** words of David? The Gemara answers: The first words are: **"And David spoke to the Lord the words of this song in the day that the Lord delivered him out of the hand of his enemies, and out of the hand of Saul"** (II Samuel 22:1), as that song is also referred to as words. The Gemara elaborates: **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to David: David, do you recite a song over the fall of Saul? Had you been Saul and he were David,** then **I would have destroyed many Davids before him.** Although I decreed that Saul's kingdom would not continue, as an individual he was far greater and far more important than you.

The Gemara then returns to the substantive question that Zutra had raised: *ve-da-atina l'ha* — now that we have arrived at this topic, what does the verse actually mean? Where do the *rishonim* appear? The answer is that the verse *va-yedaber David la-Hashem et divrei ha-shirah ha-zot* uses *divrei ha-shirah*, words of the song — not specifically *divrei David*. And God said to David: *shirah atah omer al mapalaso shel Shaul* — you are singing a song over the fall of Shaul? *Ilmaleh atah Shaul ve-hu David, ibadti kamah Davidim mipanav* — had you been Shaul and he had been David, I would have destroyed many Davids for his sake. As Rabbeinu Gershom explains: do not think yourself so superior. Shaul was actually more righteous; it is simply that circumstances and *mazal* brought things to where they did. And this is what is meant by *shigayon l'David asher shar la-Shem al divrei Kush ben Yemini* — the word *shigayon* is read as *shigagah*, an error, and *Kush ben Yemini* refers to Shaul the *Binyamini*: *mah Kushi meshuneh b'oro, af Shaul meshuneh b'maasav* — just as a Kushite is exceptional in his skin, so Shaul was exceptional in his deeds.

### The Kush Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״שִׁגָּיוֹן לְדָוִד אֲשֶׁר שָׁר לַה׳ עַל דִּבְרֵי כוּשׁ בֶּן יְמִינִי״, וְכִי כּוּשׁ שְׁמוֹ? וַהֲלֹא שָׁאוּל שְׁמוֹ! אֶלָּא: מָה כּוּשִׁי מְשׁוּנֶּה בְּעוֹרוֹ — אַף שָׁאוּל מְשׁוּנֶּה בְּמַעֲשָׂיו. כַּיּוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״עַל אוֹדוֹת הָאִשָּׁה הַכּוּשִׁית אֲשֶׁר לָקָח״, וְכִי כּוּשִׁית שְׁמָהּ? וַהֲלֹא צִיפּוֹרָה שְׁמָהּ! אֶלָּא: מָה כּוּשִׁית מְשׁוּנָּה בְּעוֹרָהּ — אַף צִיפּוֹרָה מְשׁוּנָּה בְּמַעֲשֶׂיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The response to this admonishment **is** found in the verse, **as it is written: "Shiggaion of David, which he sang to the Lord, concerning the words of Cush the Benjaminite"** (Psalms 7:1). **Is Cush his name? Saul is his name. Rather,** this is a designation that indicates: **Just as a Cushite,** a native of the ancient kingdom of Cush in eastern Africa, **is distinguished by his** dark **skin, so too, Saul was distinguished by his actions,** as he was absolutely righteous and performed many good deeds. Therefore, David uses the word *shiggaion* as an allusion to the error [*shegia*] that he had made when he sang a song of praise over Saul's downfall. The Gemara notes: **Similarly, you can explain** the verse: "And Miriam and Aaron spoke against Moses **due to the Cushite woman whom he had married,** for he had taken a Cushite woman" (Numbers 12:1). **But is her name Cushite? Zipporah is her name. Rather, just as a Cushite is distinguished by his** dark **skin, so too, Zipporah was distinguished by her actions.**

The Gemara then draws the same *kush* analogy to Moshe's wife Tziporah — *al odot ha-ishah ha-Kushit asher lakach* — she is called *Kushis* not because that was her name (her name was Tziporah) but because she was exceptional in her deeds. The same applies to Eved Melech ha-Kushi in Yirmiyahu, who likewise was not named Kushi but is called so because he was exceptional, and whose name was Tzidkiyahu. And the verse in Amos — *halo kivnei Kushiyim atem li, bnei Yisrael* — Israel is compared to *bnei Kushiyim* not because that is their name but because they are exceptional among all the nations, which depending on context can be either a compliment or a rebuke.

## King David Narrative

### David's Humility and Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״נְאֻם דָּוִד בֶּן יִשַׁי וּנְאֻם הַגֶּבֶר הוּקַם עָל״ — נְאֻם דָּוִד בֶּן יִשַׁי שֶׁהֵקִים עוּלָּהּ שֶׁל תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned the last words of David, the Gemara continues to explain other expressions in that passage. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "The saying of David, son of Yishai, and the saying of the man who was raised up on high [*al* ]"** (II Samuel 23:1)? It means as follows: **The saying of David, son of Yishai, who raised the yoke of [*ulla*] repentance,** as through his actions he taught the power of repentance. The word *al*, on high, and the word *ulla* are comprised of the same consonants in Hebrew.

Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonasan: what is meant by *neum David ben Yishai u-neum ha-gever hukam al* — the utterance of David son of Yishai, the utterance of the man raised up high? *Neum David ben Yishai she-hekim ol ha-teshuvah* — that David raised up, established, the yoke of *teshuvah*. Through the entire episode involving Bat Sheva, he demonstrated to the world the process of *teshuvah*. Rabbeinu Gershom cites the *Gemara* in Avodah Zarah: *lo David rauy l'oto ma'aseh* — David was not really at the level at which he should have sinned in that way, but it came about so as to serve as a lesson for all others. This is an interesting parallel to the story of Shaul: just as what happened to Shaul does not necessarily reflect his righteousness or lack thereof, so too what happened to David does not necessarily reflect his.

### Tzaddik Gozer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָמַר אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לִי דִבֶּר צוּר יִשְׂרָאֵל מוֹשֵׁל בָּאָדָם צַדִּיק מוֹשֵׁל יִרְאַת אֱלֹהִים״. מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אָמַר אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, לִי דִבֶּר צוּר יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲנִי מוֹשֵׁל בָּאָדָם, מִי מוֹשֵׁל בִּי — צַדִּיק, שֶׁאֲנִי גּוֹזֵר גְּזֵרָה וּמְבַטְּלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The passage continues: **"The God of Israel said, the Rock of Israel spoke to me, He that rules over men must be righteous, ruling in the fear of God"** (II Samuel 23:3). The Gemara asks: **What is** this verse **saying?** What does it mean? **Rabbi Abbahu said: This is what** the verse **is saying: The God of Israel said, the Rock of Israel spoke to me:** Although **I rule over man, who rules over Me?** It is **a righteous person.** How is it possible to say that a righteous person rules over God, as it were? **As I,** God, **issue a decree** and the righteous person **nullifies it.**

The verse then says: *amar Elohei Yisrael li diber tzur Yisrael moshel ba-adam tzaddik, moshel yiras Elokim*. The Gemara asks: *mai ka'amar* — what does this verse mean? Rabbi Abbahu explains: *ani moshel ba-adam* — I rule over humanity. *Mi moshel bi* — but who rules over Me? *Tzaddik* — the righteous person. *She-ani gozer gezeirah u-mevattelah* — I issue a divine decree, and the *tzaddik* cancels it. This goes even further than the familiar formula of *tzaddik gozer ve-haKadosh Baruch Hu mekayem* — here it is God who issues the decree and the *tzaddik* who overrides it.

### David as Rosh Yeshiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֵלֶּה שְׁמוֹת הַגִּבּוֹרִים אֲשֶׁר לְדָוִד יוֹשֵׁב בַּשֶּׁבֶת וְגוֹ׳״. מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְאֵלֶּה שְׁמוֹת גְּבוּרוֹתָיו שֶׁל דָּוִד. ״יוֹשֵׁב בַּשֶּׁבֶת״ — בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהָיָה יוֹשֵׁב בִּישִׁיבָה, לֹא הָיָה יוֹשֵׁב עַל גַּבֵּי כָּרִים וּכְסָתוֹת אֶלָּא עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע. דְּכֹל כַּמָּה דַּהֲוָה רַבֵּיהּ עִירָא הַיָּאִירִי קַיָּים, הֲוָה מַתְנֵי לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן עַל גַּבֵּי כָּרִים וּכְסָתוֹת. כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ, הֲוָה מַתְנִי דָּוִד לְרַבָּנַן וַהֲוָה יָתֵיב עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לִיתֵּיב מָר אַכָּרִים וּכְסָתוֹת! לָא קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the verse states there: **"These are the names of David's warriors; Josheb-Basshebeth** a Tahchemonite, chief of the captains; the same was Adino the Eznite; he raised his spear against eight hundred, whom he slew at one time" (II Samuel 23:8). The Gemara asks: **What is** this verse **saying? Rabbi Abbahu said: This is what** the verse **is saying: These are the names of the mighty actions of David.** These expressions should not be read as names of people but instead as descriptions of David's good deeds. **Josheb-Basshebeth [*yoshev bashevet*]** indicates that **when** David **would sit [*yoshev*] in the study hall, he would not sit upon pillows and cushions,** as an important person ordinarily would. **Rather,** he would sit **on the ground** like one of the students. **For as long as** David's **teacher, Ira the Jairite, was alive,** Ira **would teach the Sages** while sitting **on pillows and cushions. When** Ira **passed away, David would teach the Sages, and he** did this while **sitting on the ground. They said to him: Master,** you **should sit upon pillows and blankets. He did not accept** their suggestions, since in his humility he did not wish to appear as the teacher of the Jewish people.

The Gemara then expounds the verse *eileh shemot ha-giborim asher le-David, yoshev ba-shevet*. Rav Elazar, citing Rabbi Abbahu, explains: these are the mighty deeds of David, *yoshev ba-shevet* — sitting in the *yeshivah*. Specifically: *b'sha'ah she-hayah yoshev ba-yeshivah, lo yoshev al gabei karim u-khistot* — when David sat in the *yeshivah*, he did not sit on cushions and pillows, but rather *al gabei karka* — directly on the ground.

Moreover, as long as his teacher Ira haYa'iri was alive, Ira haYa'iri would teach the *rabbanan* while seated on cushions — a mark of his status and authority. David, the student, sat on the ground.

*Ki nach nafsheih Ira haYa'iri* — when Ira haYa'iri died, David became the one to teach the *rabbanan*. He became, in effect, the *Rosh Yeshivah*. This is a fascinating projection, for Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is understood to be a descendant of David, and here we see the combination of political leadership and Torah leadership being projected back onto David himself. The rabbis then urged David: *leitiv mar al karim u-khistot* — Master, sit on the cushions. *Lo kibeil aleih* — he would not accept it.

### David's Reward for Humility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תַּחְכְּמוֹנִי״, אָמַר רַב: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, הוֹאִיל וְהִשְׁפַּלְתָּ עַצְמְךָ תְּהֵא כָּמוֹנִי. שֶׁאֲנִי גּוֹזֵר גְּזֵרָה, וְאַתָּה מְבַטְּלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In this verse, David is described as **"a Tahchemonite [*taḥkemoni*]." Rav said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: Since you have humbled yourself, be you** now **like Me [*tehe kamoni*].** How so? **As I issue a decree, and you,** owing to your righteousness, **may nullify it.**

The Gemara then reads the word *tachkemoni* — which should mean "he will make me wise" — as *tehei cha-moni*, you shall be like Me. God said to David: *ho'il ve-hishpalta atzmecha, tehei cha-moni* — since you have lowered yourself, you shall be like Me. The *mida ke-neged mida*: David lowered himself from his high position, and as a result he received the power to be comparable to God — *she-ani gozer gezeirah ve-atah mevatlah* — that if I were to issue a decree, you have the power to negate it.

### David's Military Prowess and Mourning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הוּא עֲדִינוֹ הָעֶצְנִי״, כְּשֶׁהָיָה יוֹשֵׁב וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה — הָיָה מְעַדֵּן עַצְמוֹ כְּתוֹלַעַת, וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁיּוֹצֵא לַמִּלְחָמָה — הָיָה מַקְשֶׁה עַצְמוֹ כְּעֵץ. ״עַל שְׁמוֹנֶה מֵאוֹת חָלָל בְּפַעַם אֶחָת״. שֶׁהָיָה זוֹרֵק חֵץ, וּמַפִּיל שְׁמוֹנֶה מֵאוֹת חָלָל בְּפַעַם אֶחָת, וְהָיָה מִתְאַנֵּחַ עַל מָאתַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֵיכָה יִרְדֹּף אֶחָד אֶלֶף״. יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: ״רַק בִּדְבַר אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **The same was Adino the Eznite**; this alludes to the fact that **when** David **would sit and occupy himself with Torah, he would make himself soft [*me'aden*] as a worm, and when he would go out to war, he would make himself hard** and strong **as a tree [*etz*].** The expression: **"Against eight hundred people, which he slew at one time,"** means **that he would throw an arrow** in the air **and** with it **kill eight hundred people at one time. And** David **would sigh over the two hundred** who were missing from fulfillment of the Torah's promise, **as it is written: "How should one man chase a thousand"** (Deuteronomy 32:30). **A Divine Voice issued forth and said** by way of explanation as to why the promise was not entirely fulfilled: "Because David did that which was right in the eyes of the Lord, and turned not aside from anything that He commanded him all the days of his life, **save only the matter of Uriah the Hittite"** (I Kings 15:5). Had David not committed this sin, then all of the promises mentioned in the Torah would have been fulfilled in their entirety through him.

The Gemara continues with *rosh ha-shalishim*: *tehei rosh le-shalshelet avot* — you shall be head of the chain of the three *avot*, preceding them in *Olam HaBa*, as Rabbeinu Gershom explains.

The verse then continues with *hu Adino ha-Etzni*. *Ke-she-hayah yoshev ve-osek ba-Torah, hayah me-aden atzmo ka-tola'at* — when he sat and engaged in Torah study, he would make himself soft, gentle, lowly as a worm. *U-v'sha'ah she-yotzei la-milchamah, hayah maksheh atzmo ke-eitz* — but when he went out to war, he would make himself hard as wood. *Al shmoneh meiot chalal b'fa'am echat* — he would shoot one arrow and fell 800 enemies at once. And yet *hayah mit'ane'ach al matayim* — he would bemoan the 200 of his own men who fell. *Eichah yirdof echad elef* — one should chase a thousand, yet the prophecy was not completely fulfilled. A heavenly voice declared: *rak bi-dvar Uriyah ha-Chitti* — it is on account of the matter of Uriah ha-Chitti that the full measure was not realized.

This digression about David is worth pausing over before moving on. What we have witnessed throughout this section is David's consistent refusal to stand on his own *kavod*: he sat on the floor in the *yeshivah*, he made himself soft as a worm in Torah study, he would not accept the cushions even when offered. And the divine response was: because you lowered yourself, you receive the power to override My decrees. This stands in a certain tension — or perhaps as a counter-voice — to the earlier discussions of rabbis who were quite protective of their *kavod* and their authority. One might observe that even the stories of *nezifa* represent a softening compared to the harsher *nidui*, and now here is David Melech Yisrael who is entirely *mochel* on his *kavod* and receives a greater reward in consequence. The parallel to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who was also *mochel* on his *kavod* in the stories we have seen, is difficult to miss. This whole section of the *daf* seems to be in conversation with itself, offering a counter-perspective to the exercises of rabbinic authority we have observed.

## Nidui Lichvodo

### Student's Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמִיד שֶׁנִּדָּה לִכְבוֹדוֹ — נִדּוּיוֹ נִידּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** If **a student ostracized** someone else **due to** an insult to **his dignity,** and not because the ostracized person was guilty of some transgression, **his** decree of **ostracism is valid.**

The Gemara now states: *talmid she-nidah li-chevodo niduyo nidui* — a student who placed someone in *nidui* on account of his personal honor, that *nidui* is valid. We had earlier encountered the concept of *menudah le-rav menudah le-talmid* — but here we are dealing with *nidui li-chevodo*, a *nidui* issued not because of a general religious offense but because of a personal affront to this particular person. Even a student — one who has not yet received full *semicha* and is not a fully ordained *rav* — has standing to place someone in *nidui* if that person has treated him disrespectfully.

### Universal vs. Personal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מְנוּדֶּה לָרַב — מְנוּדֶּה לַתַּלְמִיד, מְנוּדֶּה לַתַּלְמִיד — אֵינוֹ מְנוּדֶּה לָרַב. לָרַב הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ מְנוּדֶּה, הָא לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא — מְנוּדֶּה. לְמַאי? אִי בְּמִילֵּי דִשְׁמַיָּא — ״אֵין חׇכְמָה וְאֵין תְּבוּנָה וְאֵין עֵצָה לְנֶגֶד ה׳״! אֶלָּא לָאו, לִכְבוֹד עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* teaches: **One who was ostracized by the teacher is** considered **ostracized with regard to the student.** However, **one who was ostracized by the student is not** considered **ostracized with regard to the teacher.** The Gemara attempts to draw an inference from a careful reading of this *baraita*: **He is not** considered **ostracized with regard to the teacher,** which implies **that he is** considered **ostracized with regard to everyone** else. The Gemara clarifies the case: **For what** reason was he ostracized? **If** it was **for a matter relating to Heaven,** i.e., because he sinned, then why, if he was ostracized by the student, should he not be considered ostracized with regard to the teacher? Doesn't the verse state: **"There is no wisdom or understanding or counsel against the Lord"** (Proverbs 21:30)? This means that when a sin is committed and God's name is desecrated, all other considerations are ignored, even the eminence and knowledge of the teacher, and therefore he too must treat the offender as ostracized. **Rather, is it not** that the Gemara is referring to a case where the student ostracized the other person due to an insult **to his** own **dignity?** Therefore, it is apparent that his decree of ostracism is valid and binding upon all, with the exception of his teacher.

A *baraita* teaches: *menudah le-rav menudah le-talmid; menudah le-talmid eino menudah le-rav* — if the *rav* places someone in *nidui*, the *talmidim* observe it; but if the *talmid* places someone in *nidui*, the *rav* is not obligated to observe it. The *rav* need not treat this person as if in *nidui* — that requirement applies to everyone else, *le-chulei alma*, but not to the *rav* himself.

The Gemara then asks: for what type of offense did the *talmid* issue this *nidui*? If it was for *millei di-shmaya* — matters of heaven, meaning this person was acting in a generally contemptuous and dismissive way toward rabbinic authority or Torah — then the principle of *ein chochmah ve-ein tevunah ve-ein eitza le-neged Hashem* applies: there is no status or wisdom that counts against God's concerns, and everyone — even a *talmid* — has standing to issue such a *nidui*, and everyone must observe it. *Ela lav li-chevod atzmo* — rather, the case must be where this was not a broader religious offense but a particular personal offense to this individual. The person generally respects rabbis; he just does not respect this one person. That produces a *nidui* of a much more limited and subjective character, which is why the *rav* need not observe it — it is not a universal decree.

### Ruling for Oneself

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן עָבֵיד דִּינָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּמִילְּתָא דִּפְסִיקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: A Torah scholar may execute judgment for himself with regard to a matter about which he is certain,** and he is not required to first go to court and have the case decided for him. The same applies when another person behaves in a disrespectful manner toward him; he is permitted to go ahead on his own and ostracize him.

Rav Yosef states: *tzurba me-rabbanan avid dina le-nafshei be-milei di-psika lei* — a member of the academy may make a ruling for himself in a matter that is clear-cut and unambiguous to him. This statement requires careful handling, because the phrase *avid dina le-nafshei* is well known from Bava Kamma in the context of taking the law into one's own hands — a practice that is in general strongly discouraged. The obvious danger is that a person might convince himself that the *halacha* is clear, that he is owed money, and simply take it.

The Rosh addresses precisely this concern, and his language is striking: *ve-kashe ha-davar le-pareish kein* — it is very difficult to explain this *Gemara* in that way. He gives two reasons. First: *im ha-din barur lo, eino barur le-acherim ve-chilul Hashem ha-davar* — even if the law is clear to you, it is not clear to others; people will simply see you taking something from someone else and assume impropriety. Second: *ein adam dan dino shel krovo, ve-chol she-ken shel atzmo* — a person may not judge the case of a relative, and certainly not his own case. No matter how well you know the *halacha*, you cannot be fully objective about the facts as they apply to yourself.

The Rosh therefore reads this statement of Rav Yosef very narrowly, in the specific context of what we have been discussing: *nidui li-chevodo*. A *tzurba me-rabbanan* may act on his own behalf in placing someone who has personally offended him in *nidui*, when the matter is clear-cut. Reading it as a broad license for self-help in monetary disputes, the Rosh says, is extremely disturbing and difficult to sustain.

## Bad Reputation Case

### Need vs. Accountability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן דַּהֲווֹ סְנוּ שׁוּמְעָנֵיהּ, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הֵיכִי לֶיעְבֵּיד? לְשַׁמְּתֵיהּ — צְרִיכִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן, לָא לְשַׁמְּתֵיהּ — קָא מִיתְּחִיל שְׁמָא דִשְׁמַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **There was a certain Torah scholar who gained a bad reputation** due to rumors about his conduct. **Rav Yehuda said: What should be done? To excommunicate him** is not an option. **The Sages need him,** as he is a great Torah authority. **Not to excommunicate him** is also not an option, as then **the name of Heaven would be desecrated.**

The Gemara now presents a difficult case. There was a *tzurba me-rabbanan* — a member of the rabbinic academy — who had a bad reputation, *de-havu sanu shumanei*. Rav Yehuda asked: *heichi di-na'avid* — what should we do here? Should we place him in *nidui*? On one hand: *tzrichi lei rabbanan* — the other rabbis need him. He apparently served as a teacher or in some role of communal influence, and this created reluctance to act — as is so often the case when a figure with communal impact is acting improperly. On the other hand: *lo neshamtei, ka mitchell shma di-shmaya* — if we do nothing, there is a *chilul Hashem*, since this person is acting improperly with impunity and nothing is done about it. This tension — between the communal good a person does and the *chilul shem shamayim* resulting from inaction — is one that has never ceased to confront rabbinic leadership.

### Rabbinic Character

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: מִידֵּי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ בְּהָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי שִׂפְתֵי כֹהֵן יִשְׁמְרוּ דַעַת וְתוֹרָה יְבַקְּשׁוּ מִפִּיהוּ כִּי מַלְאַךְ ה׳ צְבָאוֹת הוּא״. אִם דּוֹמֶה הָרַב לְמַלְאַךְ ה׳ — יְבַקְּשׁוּ תּוֹרָה מִפִּיו, וְאִם לָאו — אַל יְבַקְּשׁוּ תּוֹרָה מִפִּיו.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda **said to Rabba bar bar Ḥana:** Have **you heard anything with regard to this** issue? **He said to him: Rabbi Yoḥanan said as follows: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "For the priest's lips should keep knowledge, and they should seek Torah at his mouth; for he is a messenger [*malakh*] of the Lord of hosts"** (Malachi 2:7)? This verse teaches: **If the teacher is similar to an angel [*malakh*] of the Lord,** then **seek Torah from his mouth,** but if he is **not** pure and upright, then **do not seek Torah from his mouth;** even if he is knowledgeable about Torah, do not learn from him.

Rami bar Abba conveyed in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ki siftei kohen yishmeru da'at, ve-Torah yivakshu mi-pihu, ki malach Hashem tzeva'ot hu* — the lips of the *kohen* shall guard knowledge, and they shall seek Torah instruction from his mouth, for he is the angel of God. *Im domeh ha-rav le-malach Hashem, yivakshu Torah mi-pihu; ve-im lav, al yivakshu Torah mi-pihu* — if the rabbi is like an angel of God, one should seek Torah from him; and if not, one should not. This is not a minor qualification: it is a direct statement that a rabbi's character is not separable from his Torah. You cannot bracket his problematic conduct and focus only on the quality of his *shiurim* or sermons. A rabbi must embody what he teaches; otherwise, one should not be seeking Torah instruction from him at all. And if that is the standard, then certainly the improper conduct cannot be allowed to stand unchallenged.

### Rav Yehuda Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁמְּתֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה. לְסוֹף אִיחֲלַשׁ רַב יְהוּדָה. אֲתוֹ רַבָּנַן לְשַׁיּוֹלֵי בֵּיהּ וַאֲתָא אִיהוּ נָמֵי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, כַּד חַזְיֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה, חַיֵּיךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא מִסָּתְיֵיהּ דְּשַׁמְּתֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא, אֶלָּא אַחוֹכֵי נָמֵי חַיֵּיךְ בִּי?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו בְּדִידָךְ מְחַיֵּיכְנָא, אֶלָּא דְּכִי אָזֵילְנָא לְהָהוּא עָלְמָא בְּדִיחָא דַּעְתַּאי, דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְגַבְרָא כְּווֹתָךְ לָא חַנֵּיפִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Based on this statement, **Rav Yehuda ostracized** that Torah scholar. **In the end,** after some time had passed, **Rav Yehuda took ill** and was on the verge of death. **The Sages came to inquire about his well-being, and** the ostracized scholar **came** along **with them as well. When Rav Yehuda saw him,** that scholar, **he laughed.** The ostracized scholar **said to him: Was it not enough that you excommunicated that man,** i.e., me, **but** now **you even laugh at me?** Rav Yehuda **said to him: I was not laughing at you; rather, I am happy as I go to that** other **world that I did not flatter even** a great **man like you,** but instead I treated you fairly in accordance with the *halakha*.

Rav Yehuda placed him in *nidui* — *shamtei Rav Yehuda*. Subsequently Rav Yehuda fell ill, and the *rabbanan* — the rabbinic students — came to pay a *bikur cholim* call. The rabbi who had been placed in *nidui* came among the group. When Rav Yehuda saw him, he laughed. The rabbi in *nidui* said: *lo mistayecha de-shamtei le-hahu gavra* — is it not enough that you placed me in *nidui*, *ve-achuchuy nami mechayechat bi* — and now you laugh at me? Rav Yehuda replied: *lav bi-didach mechayechana* — I am not laughing at you. Rather: *deki azlina le-hahu alma, bidicha da'tai* — as I am going to that future world, I am in a joyful state of mind. Why? *De-afilu le-gavra ke-vasach, lo chanif lei* — even for a man of your stature and influence, I did not flatter him, I did not give inappropriate deference to your status when the situation demanded that I stand up and speak. That integrity is something that brings him great satisfaction as he approaches death.

### Maidservant Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה. אֲתָא לְבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: שְׁרוֹ לִי! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: גַּבְרָא דַּחֲשִׁיב כְּרַב יְהוּדָה לֵיכָּא הָכָא דְּלִישְׁרֵי לָךְ, אֶלָּא זִיל לְגַבֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה דְּלִישְׁרֵי לָךְ. אֲזַל לְקַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי: פּוֹק עַיֵּין בְּדִינֵיהּ, אִי מִיבְּעֵי לְמִישְׁרֵא לֵיהּ — שְׁרִי לֵיהּ. עַיֵּין רַבִּי אַמֵּי בְּדִינֵיהּ, סְבַר לְמִישְׁרֵא לֵיהּ. עָמַד רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי עַל רַגְלָיו וְאָמַר: וּמָה שִׁפְחָה שֶׁל בֵּית רַבִּי לֹא נָהֲגוּ חֲכָמִים קַלּוּת רֹאשׁ בְּנִידּוּיָהּ שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, יְהוּדָה חֲבֵירֵינוּ עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda died.** The ostracized scholar **came to the study hall and said** to the Sages: **Release me** from the decree of ostracism. **The Sages said to him: There is no man here as eminent as Rav Yehuda who can release you** from the ostracism. **Rather, go to Rabbi Yehuda Nesia** in Eretz Yisrael, **as** only **he can release you.** That scholar **came before** Rabbi Yehuda Nesia. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said to Rabbi Ami: Go and examine his case. If it is necessary to release him** from his decree of ostracism, **release him** on my behalf. **Rabbi Ami examined his case** and **thought** at first **to release him** from his ostracism. But **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani rose up on his feet and said: If the maidservant in the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi once ostracized another person, and **the Sages did not relate frivolously to her** decree of **ostracism** and did not revoke it until **three years** had passed, **all the more so, with regard to** a decree of ostracism placed by **Yehuda our colleague,** we must take it seriously and not release this scholar.

After Rav Yehuda died, the rabbi who had been placed in *nidui* came to the *Beis Medrash* and asked to be released: *sharu li*. The *rabbanan* replied: *gavra de-chashiv ke-Rav Yehuda leka hacha de-lishrei lach* — there is no one here of equivalent weight to Rav Yehuda who can release you. They sent him to Rav Yehuda Nesia, the grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the *nasi*, with the authority to release him. Rav Yehuda Nesia instructed Rabbi Ami to look into the matter and, if appropriate, to release him.

Rabbi Ami considered the case and was inclined to release him. At that moment, Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani rose and said: *umah shifcha she-beis Rebbe* — and the *shifcha*, the maidservant, of Rabbi's household: *lo nahagu chachamim kal rosh be-niduyyah* — when she placed someone in *nidui*, the *Sages* did not treat that lightly. *Shelosh shanim* — for three years that *nidui* stood. *Yehuda chavreinu — al achat kama ve-chamma* — if even the maidservant of Rabbi's house, our colleague Yehuda's *nidui* should certainly not be dissolved hastily.

### Heaven's Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: מַאי דְּקַמַּן דַּאֲתָא הָאִידָּנָא הַאי סָבָא בְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא, דְּהָא כַּמָּה שְׁנֵי לָא אֲתָא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָא מִיבְּעֵי לְמִישְׁרֵא לֵיהּ. לָא שְׁרָא לֵיהּ, נְפַק כִּי קָא בָכֵי וְאָזֵיל,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: What** caused **this Elder,** Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani, **to come before us in the study hall** today **though for many years he did not come,** and now he comes precisely during this discussion. **Learn from this** that **it is not necessary to release him** from his decree of ostracism, as this combination of events is certainly not a coincidence. Rather, it should be viewed as an instructive sign from Heaven. Consequently, Rabbi Ami **did not release him** from the ostracism, and the ostracized scholar **left in tears.**

Rabbi Zeira then observed: *mai de-kama de-ata idna hai sava le-vei midrasha, de-lo ata le-vei midrasha ha kamma shani* — what is it that *davka* today this elder, Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani, appeared in the *Beis Medrash*, when he had not come to the *Beis Medrash* in many years? *Davka* today he appeared and lodged his protest at the very moment this question was being deliberated. *Shema mina lo miba'ei le-mishrei lei* — this is clearly a *siman min ha-shamayim* that it is not appropriate to release him. And so they did not release him.

### Death and Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא זִיבּוּרָא וְטַרְקֵיהּ אַאַמְּתֵיהּ וּשְׁכֵיב. עַיְּילוּהּ לִמְעָרְתָּא דַּחֲסִידֵי וְלָא קַיבְּלוּהּ, עַיְּילוּהּ לִמְעָרְתָּא דְּדַיָּינֵי וְקַיבְּלוּהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? דַּעֲבַד כְּרַבִּי אִילְעַאי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אִילְעַאי אוֹמֵר: אִם רוֹאֶה אָדָם שֶׁיִּצְרוֹ מִתְגַּבֵּר עָלָיו — יֵלֵךְ לְמָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִין אוֹתוֹ, וְיִלְבַּשׁ שְׁחוֹרִים וְיִתְעַטֵּף שְׁחוֹרִים, וְיַעֲשֶׂה מַה שֶּׁלִּבּוֹ חָפֵץ, וְאַל יְחַלֵּל שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **A wasp came and stung** the ostracized scholar **on his penis and he died.** Because he was a great Torah scholar, **they took him into the caves in which the pious** are interred in order to bury him there, **but** the caves **did not accept him.** A snake stood at the entrance of the caves and did not let them pass. **They** then **took him into the caves of the judges, and they accepted him.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that he was accepted there? The Gemara answers: Even though he sinned, **he** still **acted in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ilai, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Ilai says: If a person sees that his** evil **inclination is gaining control over him** and he cannot overcome it, then **he should go to a place where he is not known. He should wear black, and he should wrap his** head **in black,** as if he were a mourner. Perhaps these changes will influence him, so that he not sin. Even if these actions do not help, **he should** at least **do as his heart desires** in private **and not desecrate the name of Heaven in public.** Although this person had sinned, he did so in private and in a manner that did not publicly desecrate God's name, and therefore it was fitting that he be given an honorable burial.

He left the *Beis Medrash* weeping. *Ata zibura ve-tarkeih a-amateih* — a wasp came and stung him on his genitals, and he died. When they sought to bring him into the *maarat ha-chasidim* — the cave of the *chasidim* — the cave did not receive him. They brought him instead to the *maarat ha-dayanim* — the cave of the judges — and there he was accepted. He was a judge, but evidently not a *chasid*.

### Rabbi Ilai's Teaching

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama de-avid ke-Rabbi Ilai* — what is the reason he was accepted in the cave of judges? Because he acted like Rabbi Ilai. A *baraita* teaches: Rabbi Ilai says — *im ra'ah adam she-yitzro misgaber alav* — if a person sees that his *yetzer hara*, specifically in the context of sexual desire, is overpowering him and he will not be able to subdue it, *yeilech le-makom she-ein makirim oto* — let him go to a place where no one recognizes him, *ve-yilbash shechorim ve-yit'atef shechorim* — dress in black and wrap himself in black, so that he is unrecognized, and perhaps the humbling effect will cause him to reconsider. *Ve-ya'aseh mah she-libo chafetz* — and if he still must act, let him act according to his desire. *Ve-al yechalel shem shamayim be-farhesya* — but he should not create a public *chilul Hashem*.

*Tosfos* presents two versions of the *girsa* here. One version reads *de-avid ke-Rabbi Ilai* — he acted in accordance with Rabbi Ilai's teaching, meaning even though he sinned sexually, he did so in a way that was at least partially protective of *shem shamayim*, conducting himself in private, which is why he was accepted into the cave of judges despite his failings. The other version reads *de-avar al Rabbi Ilai* — he violated Rabbi Ilai's teaching, meaning he did not go to a foreign place and conduct himself in disguise but rather acted in a way that became publicly known — which is why he was not accepted into the cave of *chasidim*.

This raises a genuine tension. There is something troubling about a person of public rabbinic standing conducting himself improperly while hiding it, maintaining a public persona of respectability. On the other hand, there is a real argument to be made for the value of not doing so in public: it preserves the perception of Torah's honor, and perhaps more importantly, it sends a continuous signal to oneself that the act is a transgression. Rabbi Yisrael Salanter observed that if a person commits a sin once it becomes familiar; if twice it begins to feel permitted; if three times it begins to feel obligatory — *na'aseh lo ke-mitzva*. Doing something in hiding, with the self-consciousness that accompanies it, may maintain an awareness of wrongdoing that public rationalization would erode.

The question was in fact addressed by Rav Moshe Feinstein in the context of the era when attending a pornographic theater required physically entering a public venue. When asked whether someone who could not control his *yetzer hara* should remove his *yarmulke* before entering, Rav Moshe's response, as recalled, was no — because however problematic the *chilul Hashem* of being seen, the shame of the *yarmulke* might itself serve to deter him.

A final observation on this story: even though the rabbi had been placed in *nidui*, he joined the group of *rabbanan* coming to perform *bikur cholim* for Rav Yehuda. One would expect that a person in *nidui* — who may not be approached within *arba amos*, who is not to be included in a group — would be excluded from such a communal visit. His presence among them raises a question about the precise limits of how much separation is maintained in practice, and what the boundaries of *nidui* actually require in situations of this kind.
