# Shabbat 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Insulation Before Shabbat

### Heat Retention Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא כִּסָּהוּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם — לֹא יְכַסֶּנּוּ מִשֶּׁתֶּחְשַׁךְ. כִּסָּהוּ וְנִתְגַּלָּה, מוּתָּר לְכַסּוֹתוֹ. מְמַלֵּא אֶת הַקִּיתוֹן וְנוֹתֵן לְתַחַת הַכַּר אוֹ תַּחַת הַכֶּסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one did not cover** a pot of cooked food on Shabbat eve **while it was still day, he may not cover it after dark.** However, if **one covered it** while it was still day **and it was uncovered** on Shabbat, **he is permitted to cover it** even on Shabbat. **One may fill a jug** with cold water on Shabbat **and place** it **beneath a pillow or a cushion** to prevent it from getting warm.

The present daf opens a new *mishna* within this *perek*, which has been devoted throughout to the laws of *hatmana* — insulating food before *Shabbat*. The governing framework is familiar: one may not insulate food in a material that adds heat (*davar hamosif hevel*), but one may insulate it in a material that merely retains heat (*davar she'eino mosif hevel*). That much has been established. What the new *mishna* now introduces is a further distinction — between insulating *before Shabbat* begins and doing so *on Shabbat* itself.

### Mishna: When Insulation…

The *mishna* states: *lo hitmanu m'bo yom, lo yitmi'nenu m'shetechshach* — if one did not insulate the pot from before nightfall, one may not insulate it once *Shabbat* has begun, even in a material that only retains heat and does not add to it. This creates an asymmetry that requires explanation: if the material in question adds no heat, what possible concern could arise from insulating on *Shabbat* itself?

### Fear of Heating

Rashi addresses this directly, noting that the explanation was already given in the second chapter — roughly twenty *dappim* earlier. The relevant principle is *g'zeira shema yartiyach*: a rabbinic decree born of the fear that one might come to heat the food. The application here is a striking one. Throughout the earlier chapters, the paradigmatic concern was inadvertent action — a pot sitting in hot coals and the person absentmindedly stirring them. Here, the feared scenario is different. If it is *Shabbat* itself and the food has not been set up in any insulation from before *Shabbat*, the food will by now have cooled somewhat. A person wishing to insulate it in heat-retaining material will recognise that the effort is only worthwhile if the pot is sufficiently hot. The concern is that he will first place it on the fire to reheat it — and in doing so, inadvertently forget the larger context of *Shabbat*. One might think this scenario too deliberate to constitute inadvertent transgression, but the *g'zeira* accounts for the very human experience of becoming so absorbed in a task — heating the pot, preparing for guests — that one loses sight of the fact that it is *Shabbat*.

### Removing & Restoring Pots

The *mishna* continues: *kiseihu v'nitgaleh, mutar l'chasoto* — if one covered the pot before *Shabbat* and it became exposed, one may re-cover it. As Tosafot notes, and as the Gemara's earlier discussion implied, this extends beyond accidental uncovering: one may actively remove the pot from its insulation and restore it. The permissibility is one of *continuation* — restoring a state of insulation that already existed before *Shabbat* — as opposed to initiating a new act of *hatmana* from scratch on *Shabbat* itself.

### Filling Water Vessels

The *mishna* adds another case: *memalei et ha'kiton v'noten l'toch ha'kar o l'toch ha'keset* — one may fill a flask with water and place it under a pillow or blanket. The natural question is what purpose this serves: water would not normally be something one wants to keep warm. From the outset the Gemara understands this as a case of insulating something cold — protecting it from external heat, not preserving warmth within it.

## Insulating Cold Things

### Shmuel Permits Cold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מוּתָּר לְהַטְמִין אֶת הַצּוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: It is permitted to insulate the cold** food on Shabbat to keep it cold.

Rashi explains the purpose as *kedei she'lo yeichamu ba'chama* — so that they do not become warm in the sun.

### Rav Yosef's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי קָמַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: מְמַלֵּא אָדָם קִיתוֹן וְנוֹתֵן תַּחַת הַכַּר אוֹ תַּחַת הַכֶּסֶת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: What is** Shmuel **teaching us** with this statement? **We** already **learned** in our mishna: **One may fill a jug** with cold water on Shabbat **and place** it **beneath a pillow or a mattress** to prevent it from getting warm.

Rav Yosef immediately challenges: *mai k'mashma lan?* — what is this teaching us? The *mishna* already stated that one may fill a flask and place it under pillows or blankets, which is manifestly a case of insulating something cold. The ruling of Shmuel seems redundant.

### Abaye's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: טוּבָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּאִי מִמַּתְנִי׳ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי — דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לְהַטְמִין, אֲבָל דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְהַטְמִין לָא — קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: He teaches us a great deal. As, if** it had been learned **from the mishna** alone, **I would have said** that the ruling that one is permitted to insulate cold food applies only to **something that is not ordinarily insulated** when it is hot. **However, something** that is **commonly insulated** when it is hot, **no,** it may not be insulated even when it is cold. Therefore, Shmuel **teaches us** that this is allowed even in the case of something which is commonly insulated when it is hot.

Abaye responds: *tuva k'mashma lan* — it is in fact telling us considerably more than the *mishna* alone conveys. For had we only the *mishna*, we might have reasoned as follows.

### Normal vs. Abnormal Use

The *mishna*'s case involves water — *davar she'ein darko lehatmin*, something one does not ordinarily insulate for the purpose of keeping warm. Cold water placed under a pillow to keep cool presents no confusion: nobody would normally insulate cold water for warmth. But what of something that under ordinary circumstances *would* be insulated to preserve heat — the same object, the same act, the only difference being the intention? In that case, one might have distinguished: where the act of insulating is identical in form to what one might do in a heat-preserving context, perhaps we forbid it even when the purpose is to keep something cold, lest the two be confused. Shmuel's ruling tells us that this distinction is not made. As long as one is insulating for the purpose of keeping something cold, it is permitted — even for objects that in another context one would insulate for warmth. This is how Rashi reads the Gemara.

An alternative reading, which has some merit, is that the concern is not about purpose at all but about temperature: if the item is already quite cold, we are simply not worried that one will come to heat it up, and the leniency follows from that practical reality rather than from the declared intention. Rashi, however, does not read the Gemara that way. His formulation is explicitly that if one is doing it *for the sake of keeping it cold*, it is permitted; if for the sake of keeping it warm, it is not.

### Rav Huna vs. Rabbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לְהַטְמִין אֶת הַצּוֹנֵן. וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי הִתִּיר לְהַטְמִין אֶת הַצּוֹנֵן! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא מִקַּמֵּיהּ דְּלִישְׁמְעֵיהּ מֵרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הָא לְבָתַר דְּלִישְׁמְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: It is prohibited to insulate cold** food on Shabbat to keep it cold. The Gemara raises an objection: **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **permitted cold** food **to be insulated** on Shabbat? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** statement **was** made **before he heard** the ruling **of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei; that** statement in the *baraita* **was** made **after he heard it.**

Against this, the Gemara cites Rav Huna in the name of Rabbi: *assur l'hatmin es ha'tzonin* — it is forbidden to insulate cold things. This immediately conflicts with another statement attributed to Rabbi in a *braita* — *Rebbe hitir l'hatmin es ha'tzonin* — that Rabbi permitted insulating cold things. The resolution is that these are not contradictory positions but sequential ones.

### Rabbi's Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּיָתֵיב רַבִּי וְאָמַר: אָסוּר לְהַטְמִין אֶת הַצּוֹנֵן. אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אַבָּא הִתִּיר לְהַטְמִין אֶת הַצּוֹנֵן. אָמַר: כְּבָר הוֹרָה זָקֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **As** in **that** incident **where Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **sat and said: It is prohibited to insulate cold** food on Shabbat to keep it cold, **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said before him: Father permitted insulating cold** food on Shabbat. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said:** I retract my previous statement, as **the Elder,** Rabbi Yosei, **has already issued a ruling** on this topic, and I defer to his ruling.

The Gemara explains: one statement is from before Rabbi heard the teaching from Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi, and one is from after. A *braita* narrates the scene directly: Rabbi was sitting and declared that it is forbidden to insulate cold things. Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi said before him: *Abba* — my father — *permitted insulating cold things*. Rabbi immediately responded: *kvar hora zaken* — the elder has already ruled. He retracted his prohibition.

### Respect Between Scholars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה מְחַבְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. שֶׁאִילּוּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי קַיָּים, הָיָה כָּפוּף וְיוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבִּי. דְּהָא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי דִּמְמַלֵּא מְקוֹם אֲבוֹתָיו הֲוָה, וְכָפוּף וְיוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבִּי, וְקָאָמַר: כְּבָר הוֹרָה זָקֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Come and see how much they loved each other. Had Rabbi Yosei** still been **alive, he would** have been **subordinate** to **and sitting before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi as his student, **as Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, who took his father's place** and was as great a Torah scholar as his father, was **subordinate** to **and sitting before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi as his student. **And,** nevertheless, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **says: The Elder has already issued a ruling** on this topic, and he deferred to Rabbi Yosei's ruling.

Rav Pappa pauses to draw a remarkable observation from this exchange: *bo re'ei kama mechabevin ze la-ze* — come and see how much the sages cherished one another. Were Rabbi Yosi still alive, he would have sat in a posture of deference before Rabbi — *kofuf v'yoshev lifnei Rebbe* — because Rabbi was the *nasi* and the position of authority commanded that deference, irrespective of relative scholarly stature. And Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi is known to have been a scholar of equal caliber to his father, *memale makom avosav*, yet he too sat in that subservient posture before Rabbi. And nevertheless, when Rabbi heard what Rabbi Yosi had said, he stepped aside entirely and accepted his ruling.

Rashi explains the logic: Rabbi Yosi adopted a subservient posture out of respect for the institutional authority of the *nasi*. Rabbi himself, however, who privately acknowledged Rabbi Yosi as the greater *talmid chacham*, was willing — once Rabbi Yosi had passed from the world — to step aside in honor of his ruling. The whole exchange is introduced by the Gemara with no halachic necessity: the *halacha* was already resolved by the reversal itself. The digression exists solely to illuminate the character of the sages — and the Gemara evidently considers that worth noting.

## Adam Chashuv Standards

### Rav Nachman's Instruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְדָרוּ עַבְדֵּיהּ: אַטְמֵין לִי צוֹנֵן, וְאַיְיתִי לִי מַיָּא דְּאַחֵים קַפִּילָא אֲרַמָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to Daru, his slave: Insulate cold** food **for me** on Shabbat, so that it will not become warm, **and bring me water that a gentile cook [*kappeila*] heated** on a weekday, as the prohibition to eat food cooked by a gentile does not apply to water.

The Gemara now presents a practical narrative. Rav Nachman instructed his servant: *atmin li tzonei* — go insulate this cold water for me. Additionally, he requested: *v'aisei li maya de'achim kefela armayei* — bring me water that has been heated by a certain non-Jewish cook.

### Ammi Objects, Yosef Defe…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְאִיקְּפַד. אֲמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי טַעְמָא אִיקְּפַד? כְּרַבְּווֹתֵיהּ עָבֵיד — חֲדָא כְּרַב, וַחֲדָא כִּשְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** When **Rabbi Ami heard** this, **he became angry. Rav Yosef said: What** is **the reason** that Rabbi Ami **become angry?** Rav Naḥman **acted in accordance with** the rulings of **his teachers;** in **one** matter **in accordance with** the ruling of **Rav, and** in **one** matter **in accordance with** the ruling of **Shmuel.**

Rabbi Ammi heard of this and was offended. Rav Yosef immediately came to Rav Nachman's defense: *mai ta'ama ikpid?* — what is the basis for objecting? *Ke'rabosei avid* — Rav Nachman acted in accordance with the rulings of his own teachers.

### Following Teachers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּשְׁמוּאֵל — דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מוּתָּר לְהַטְמִין אֶת הַצּוֹנֵן. כְּרַב — דְּאָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק אָמַר רַב: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא נֶאֱכָל כְּמוֹת שֶׁהוּא חַי — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם בִּשּׁוּלֵי גּוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In one matter **in accordance with** the ruling of **Shmuel, as Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: It is permitted to insulate cold** food on Shabbat to keep it cold. In one matter **in accordance with** the ruling of **Rav, as Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak said** that **Rav said: Anything that is eaten as it is, raw,** and cooking it is unnecessary, even if it was cooked **it is not** subject **to** the prohibition of food **cooked by gentiles.** Since water is commonly drunk uncooked, one may drink it even if it was boiled by a gentile.

Specifically, Rav Yosef explains: *chada ke'Rav v'chada ke'Shmuel* — one ruling follows Rav and one follows Shmuel. The insulation of cold water follows Shmuel, who, as quoted by Rav Yehuda, ruled that *mutar l'hatmin et ha'tzonin*.

### Bishul Akum Issue

The heated water introduced a separate question entirely — not one of *Shabbat* but of *bishul akum*. The concern of *bishulei goyim* — food cooked by a non-Jew — arises in its own right. Rav Nachman's second ruling follows Rav: *kol she'ne'echal k'mo shehu chai ein bo mishum bishulei nochrim* — anything that can be eaten in its raw state is not subject to the prohibition of *bishul akum*. Water can be drunk as it is; therefore, heating it does not create a *bishul akum* problem, and consuming such water is permitted.

### Adam Chashuv Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (הוּא) סָבַר אָדָם חָשׁוּב שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Ami became angry because **he held** that **an important person is different.** A distinguished person like Rav Naḥman should be stringent and distance himself from conduct that could be perceived, even mistakenly, as a prohibited act.

The Gemara then explains why Rabbi Ammi objected: *adam chashuv shani* — an important person is held to a different standard. The concern is not that Rav Nachman erred in *halacha* — he did not — but that a person of stature must be conscious of how his conduct is perceived and what precedents it sets. People do not always follow the strict *halacha* of an action; they follow their impression of what respected figures do, and from that draw conclusions — sometimes incorrect ones — about what is permitted.

### Marit Ayin & Standards

This raises a broader question about *marit ayin* — the concern for appearances. A distinction is worth drawing: *marit ayin*, properly understood, applies when everyone is aware of the relevant *halacha* and there is only a confusion about the facts. If one is eating fake bacon bits or a hamburger with soy cheese, the prohibition is universally known; only the facts of the particular situation are unclear, and one does not want to appear to be transgressing. That is the domain of *marit ayin*. Where people simply misunderstand what is permitted, the answer is not to capitulate to the misunderstanding but to educate. Rav Moshe Feinstein's approach to *marit ayin* follows this line: the obligation does not extend to avoiding actions that are in fact permitted, simply because some people incorrectly believe them to be forbidden.

The *adam chashuv* concern, however, operates somewhat differently. It is not merely about factual confusion but about community expectations and the downstream effects of conduct. A person of standing — a posek, a teacher, a communal figure — must weigh how his actions will be interpreted and what norms they will reinforce or erode. This is true even in domains that are not formally *halachic*: public comportment, communal norms, the general expectations that attach to those who represent *Torah*. The *adam chashuv* category captures the sense that strict *halacha* does not exhaust the full set of considerations relevant to how a prominent figure conducts himself.

Whether we in fact rule that *adam chashuv shani* as a binding *halachic* standard is a separate question. What this Gemara shows is that Rabbi Ammi understood the category to be operative and that Rav Yosef's defense — he was acting like his teachers — did not fully resolve the concern in Rabbi Ammi's view.

## Adding & Transferring

### Braita Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ אֵין טוֹמְנִין אֲפִילּוּ בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹסִיף הֶבֶל מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁכָה, אִם בָּא לְהוֹסִיף — מוֹסִיף. כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: נוֹטֵל אֶת הַסְּדִינִין וּמַנִּיחַ אֶת הַגְּלוּפְקְרִין, אוֹ נוֹטֵל אֶת הַגְּלוּפְקְרִין וּמַנִּיחַ אֶת הַסְּדִינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: **Although** the Sages **said** that **one may not insulate** hot food, **even in something that does not add heat after nightfall** on Shabbat, **if he comes to add** to the material in which he insulated the food on Shabbat eve, **he may add** to it even on Shabbat. **How** should **he do** it? **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: He takes the sheets** with which he insulated a pot **and places the heavy blankets,** which provide better insulation, in their place. **Or,** if he is concerned about excessive heat, **he takes the heavy blankets** in which the pot had been insulated **and places the** lighter **sheets** in their place.

The Gemara now cites a *braita* that reviews the rules for *hatmana* on *Shabbat* itself. The principle stated is: although one may not perform a new *hatmana* on *Shabbat* even in material that does not add heat, nonetheless *im ba lehosif, mosif* — if the pot is already insulated from before *Shabbat* and one wishes to add further insulation on *Shabbat*, that is permitted. Adding to an existing insulation is not equivalent to initiating a new one.

### Exchanging Coverings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הָיָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא אָסְרוּ אֶלָּא אוֹתוֹ מֵיחַם, אֲבָל פִּינָּה מִמֵּיחַם לְמֵיחַם — מוּתָּר. הַשְׁתָּא אוֹקוֹרֵי קָא מֵקֵיר לְהוּ, אַרְתּוֹחֵי קָא מַרְתַּח לְהוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** in teaching an additional leniency, **said: They prohibited** insulating a pot on Shabbat to keep its contents warm when the food remains **only** in **the same urn** in which the water was boiled. **However, if one emptied** the water **from that urn into** another **urn, it is permitted** to insulate the second urn to keep the water warm. The reason for the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is: It is prohibited to insulate a pot on Shabbat, due to concern lest one heat the food beforehand. **Now** that **he has** already taken steps to **cool** the water by pouring it from one urn to another, is there concern that he will **boil it** again on Shabbat?

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel extends this: *notel et ha'sadinim u'meniach saglofkarin, o notel et ha'saglofkarin u'meniach et ha'sadinim* — one may remove a lighter covering and replace it with a heavier one, or remove a heavier covering and replace it with a lighter one. *Sadinim* are sheets; *saglofkarin* is a thicker covering that does a better job of retaining heat.

Tosafot notes a significant point of interpretation: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel may in fact be arguing with the *Tanna Kamma*, not merely clarifying. The *Tanna Kamma* permits only *adding* to existing insulation — the original setup remains in place and one simply builds on it. But Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permits *exchanging* — actively removing one covering and replacing it with another. During the act of removal, the pot is momentarily exposed, which might be construed as a return to an uninsulated state, making the subsequent covering a new *hatmana* from the outset. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that this does not constitute a new *hatmana* — it is an adjustment of an existing arrangement. The parallel to *chazara* — the permission to remove a pot from a flame and return it, provided the right conditions are met — is apt: in both contexts, continuity of process distinguishes adjustment from initial action.

### Transfer to 2nd Vessel

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel offers a further leniency: *lo asru ela oto meicham, aval pina mimeicham l'meicham mutar* — the prohibition applies only to the original vessel in which the food was heated. If one transfers the contents to a second vessel and then insulates *that*, it is permitted.

### Cooling Logic

The reasoning is stated explicitly in the Gemara: *hashta akurei k'meiker lei, artuchei k'mirtech lei?* — right now you are cooling it off; are you going to heat it up? The act of transferring food to a second vessel accelerates its cooling. Given that the underlying concern of the prohibition is *shema yartiyach* — lest one come to reheat it — that concern is substantially diminished when the person is actively cooling the food by pouring it into a fresh vessel. Rashi adds that it is not merely a factual observation that the food happens to be cooling; rather, the act of transfer itself constitutes an intentional step toward cooling, which removes the basis for the decree.

## Muktzeh Issues

### Muktzeh Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָמַן וְכִיסָּה בְּדָבָר הַנִּיטָּל בְּשַׁבָּת, אוֹ טָמַן בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיטָּל בְּשַׁבָּת וְכִיסָּה בְּדָבָר הַנִּיטָּל בְּשַׁבָּת — הֲרֵי זֶה נוֹטֵל וּמַחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: If **he insulated** the pot **and covered** it **with something that may be moved on Shabbat, or if he insulated** it **with something that may not be moved on Shabbat** because it is set-aside **and covered** it **with something that may be moved on Shabbat, he may take** the pot to remove food **and return** it to its place and not be concerned.

The Gemara continues with a *Tosefta* that surveys a range of cases, several of which involve *muktzeh*. If one insulated and covered a pot with materials that are not *muktzeh* — *davar hanittal b'Shabbat* — one may remove the pot and restore it. Similarly, if the surrounding insulation is *muktzeh* but the top covering is not, one still has unobstructed access to the pot and may remove and restore it without handling *muktzeh*. The rule is: *harei zeh notel u'machzir* — it is permitted.

### Access Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָמַן וְכִיסָּה בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיטָּל בְּשַׁבָּת אוֹ שֶׁטָּמַן בְּדָבָר הַנִּיטָּל בְּשַׁבָּת, וְכִיסָּה בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיטָּל בְּשַׁבָּת, אִם הָיָה מְגוּלֶּה מִקְצָתוֹ נוֹטֵל וּמַחֲזִיר. וְאִם לָאו — אֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל וּמַחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** However, if **he** both **insulated** it **and covered** it **with something that may not be moved on Shabbat, or if he insulated** it **in something that may be moved on Shabbat and covered it with something that may not be moved on Shabbat, if** the pot **was partially exposed, he may remove** the pot and the cover will fall on its own **and** then **return** it to its place. **And if** the pot was **not** partially exposed, **he may not remove** the pot **and** then **return** it to its place.

However, if the covering on top is *muktzeh* — whether or not the surrounding insulation is — there is a problem of access. Rashi initially suggests that even with a *muktzeh* top layer, one might move the material on the sides and reach the pot that way. But this explanation runs into difficulties.

Tosafot offers the cleaner solution: the case discussed is one where the pot is inside a box, filled with insulating material. In that configuration, the only access to the pot is from above — the sides are enclosed. Therefore, whether the *top* layer is *muktzeh* or not is the only question that matters.

Given this, the rule follows: *im hayah meguleh miktzato, notel u'machzir* — if part of the pot protrudes above the *muktzeh* material, one may grab onto the exposed portion; the *muktzeh* falls away incidentally, and since this is *tiltul min hatzad*, it is permitted. *Ve'im lav, eino notel u'machzir* — if none of the pot is exposed, one has no access without directly moving the *muktzeh*, and that is forbidden.

The distinction is between incidental displacement — where one grabs the pot and the *muktzeh* falls away as a byproduct — and direct handling of *muktzeh* in order to clear a path to the pot. The former is permitted; the latter is not.

## Misc. Rules

### Flax Remnants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נְעוֹרֶת שֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן דַּקָּה הֲרֵי הִיא כְּזֶבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: The chaff of fine flax is like manure.** Therefore, one may not insulate food in it even on Shabbat eve.

The *Tosefta* continues with a series of rapid rulings that recap material from the *perek*. Rabbi Yehuda rules that thin remnants of flax — *nesoret shel pishtan dakah* — may not be used for insulation. *Harei hu ke'zevel* — it is like excrement: it adds heat, and one may not insulate with a heat-adding material.

### Stacking Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּיחִין מֵיחַם עַל גַּבֵּי מֵיחַם וּקְדֵרָה עַל גַּבֵּי קְדֵרָה, אֲבָל לֹא קְדֵרָה עַל גַּבֵּי מֵיחַם, וּמֵיחַם עַל גַּבֵּי קְדֵרָה. וְטָח אֶת פִּיהָ בְּבָצֵק, וְלֹא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיֵּחַמּוּ, אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיִּהְיוּ מְשׁוּמָּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **One may place a** copper **urn upon a** copper **urn, and** one may place **an** earthenware **pot upon an** earthenware **pot** because the lower utensil will not heat the upper one. **However,** one may **not** place **an** earthenware **pot upon a** copper **urn, or a** copper **urn upon an** earthenware **pot,** as in that case there is concern that the upper utensil will be heated by the lower one. **And one may seal the mouth** of a pot **with dough.** All of the above may **not** be undertaken **in order to heat** the water, **but only so that** its heat **will be maintained** and it will not cool down.

One may place a hot *meicham* — a metal hot-water vessel — on top of another *meicham*, and a *kdeira* — a pottery pot — on top of another *kdeira*. According to Rashi's preferred reading of the text, and the way we *poskin*, it is in fact permitted to stack a *meicham* on a *kdeira* and vice versa as well. The point here is that placing one vessel on top of another does not constitute *hatmana b'davar hamosif hevel*, since the lower vessel is not surrounding the upper one as insulation. Nor does it raise a *bishul* concern, provided the upper vessel will not reach *yad soledet bo* — the threshold temperature for cooking. The Gemara's earlier discussion established that when the purpose is to prevent cooling rather than to heat, and when in practice the food will not reach *yad soledet bo*, the arrangement is permitted. The *poskim* interpret that condition not merely as a statement of intention but as a practical requirement: the upper vessel must not in fact reach *yad soledet bo*.

### Sealing with Dough

One may take pre-existing dough and seal the gap between the pot and its lid in order to prevent heat loss. This raises no *hatmana* concern, since the dough is not surrounding the pot. Nor does it raise a *bishul* concern, again with the qualifier that the pot will not reach *yad soledet bo*. The *Tosefta* states the purpose explicitly: *velo bishvil sheyachamu, ela bishvil sheyiheyu meshumarin* — not to increase the heat but to preserve what is already there.

### Snow & Hail

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְרַזְּקִין לֹא אֶת הַשֶּׁלֶג וְלֹא אֶת הַבָּרָד בְּשַׁבָּת בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיָּזוּבוּ מֵימָיו, אֲבָל נוֹתֵן הוּא לְתוֹךְ הַכּוֹס אוֹ לְתוֹךְ הַקְּעָרָה וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may neither crush snow nor hail on Shabbat so that its water will flow** and he will be able to drink it. That act involves creation of a new entity, water from ice, on Shabbat, which is prohibited. **However, he may place** the snow or the hail **into a cup or a dish** and allow it to melt on its own, **and he need not be concerned.**

The *Tosefta* adds: *ein merazkin lo et ha'sheleg velo et ha'barad b'Shabbat bishvil sheyizovu meimav* — one may not squeeze snow or hail on *Shabbat* in order to produce water from it. This is prohibited. However, *aval notein hu l'toch ha'kos o l'toch ha'kiton v'eino choshesh* — one may place snow or ice into a cup or flask and allow it to melt on its own, and there is no concern.

### Nolad Debate

The question of precisely *why* squeezing snow is forbidden, and why passive melting is permitted, has significant implications — particularly for the laws of *muktzeh* and the category of *nolad*. Two approaches present themselves.

The first, which is the approach adopted by the *Shulchan Aruch*, holds that the problem is the *act* of squeezing itself. Actively squeezing snow to extract water resembles the prohibited act of squeezing fruits to extract juice. The issue is what one *does*, not what results. If the transformation occurs on its own — the ice melts, the solid becomes liquid — there is no prohibited act and therefore no problem.

The second approach, advanced by one of the *Ba'alei Tosafot*, holds that the problem is with the *result*. The transformation of ice into water constitutes *nolad* — the emergence of something new — which is itself a category of *muktzeh*. On this reading, actively squeezing ice is problematic not only because of the act but because it produces a substance that is *muktzeh*. And even passive melting could potentially be prohibited *to use* the resulting water, since that water is *nolad*.

The resolution — why passive melting into a cup is nevertheless permitted even on this stricter view — is that when ice melts directly into an existing liquid, it never achieves independent identity. The water produced by the melting ice merges immediately into the liquid already in the cup; it never exists as a distinct, newly-formed thing. *Nolad* requires that something new come into independent existence. If the ice melts into a cup of water and immediately becomes part of that water, the *nolad* category is never triggered.

The broader implications of the *nolad* position are substantial: they extend to any transformation of state — liquid to solid, solid to liquid. Making ice in a freezer, making ice pops, the freezing of water — all of these potentially implicate *nolad* on the stricter reading. The *Shulchan Aruch* does not rule this way; the *Rema* suggests one may wish to be stringent without formally ruling that way. The frank assessment is that the simpler reading of the Gemara, and the basic *psak* of the *Shulchan Aruch*, does not support treating ordinary state changes as *nolad* — though one of the *Ba'alei Tosafot* does indeed read it that way, and that view has found expression in much of the later literature.

## Animals on Shabbat

### Intro to Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הדרן עלך במה טומנין
>
> **Translation:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּמֶּה בְּהֵמָה יוֹצְאָה וּבַמָּה אֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה? — יוֹצֵא הַגָּמָל בְּאַפְסָר, וְנָאֲקָה בַּחֲטָם, וְלוּבְדְּקִים בִּפְרוּמְבְּיָא, וְסוּס בְּשֵׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** Due to the mitzva to rest one's animals on Shabbat, one's animal may not go out into the public domain bearing a burden. However, an object designated to protect the animal or to prevent it from fleeing is not considered a burden; therefore, an animal bearing objects that serve that purpose may go out into the public domain. MISHNA: The mishna asks: **With what may an animal go out** into the public domain on Shabbat **and with what may it not go out? A camel may go out** on Shabbat **with an *afsar*, and a *naka*** may go out **with a *ḥatam*, and a *luvdekim*** may go out **with a *perumbiya*.** All these terms will be defined in the Gemara. **And a horse** may go out **with a chain** around its neck.

With the conclusion of this *perek* — *hadran alach bameh tomnin* — the Gemara transitions to a new subject. Two full *perakim* of *masechet Shabbat* have now been devoted to cooking, placing food on the fire before *Shabbat*, returning food to the fire, insulating food, and *muktzeh*. The next two *perakim*, before the seventh chapter's treatment of the *lamed-tet melachot*, are devoted to carrying. Notably, *muktzeh* is not itself one of the thirty-nine *melachot*, nor is much of the rabbinic architecture around cooking and *hatmana* biblical in character — yet these have occupied the better part of the opening *dappim* of the *masechta*.

The new *mishna* asks: *bameh behema yotza u'vameh einah yotza* — what may an animal go out with on *Shabbat*, and what may it not? The underlying principle is that just as a Jew may not perform *melacha* on *Shabbat*, neither may his animal. The verse states explicitly *lo ta'aseh kol melacha, ata u'vehemtecha* — you shall do no manner of work, you or your animal. There are in fact two distinct concerns: the direct prohibition of using one's animal as an instrument of *melacha* — as when an animal pulls a plow — and the broader positive obligation to allow one's animal to rest, which applies even when the animal's activity does not constitute *melacha* performed *for* the owner. The *mishna* in *Avodah Zarah*, for instance, prohibits selling animals to non-Jews before *Shabbat* out of concern that the animal, still under the Jew's ownership, will perform *melacha* on *Shabbat* — not for the owner's benefit, but still in violation of the rest obligation.

The governing distinction the *mishna* establishes is between *levush* — clothing, something the animal wears for its protection — and *masui* — a burden, something placed on the animal beyond what it needs. Just as a person may go out wearing clothing on *Shabbat* without violating the prohibition of carrying in a public domain, an animal may go out wearing items that constitute its normal protective gear. Anything that is genuinely there to protect the animal counts as a type of clothing and is permitted. Anything that exceeds that function, serving no protective purpose, is considered a burden and is forbidden even if the animal is not performing any work.

## Animals on Shabbat (II)

### Specific Accessories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכָל בַּעֲלֵי הַשֵּׁיר יוֹצְאִין בְּשֵׁיר וְנִמְשָׁכִין בְּשֵׁיר. וּמַזִּין עֲלֵיהֶן, וְטוֹבְלָן בִּמְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And,** in general, **all** animals that typically **have a chain** around their necks when they go out to the public domain **may go out with a chain** on Shabbat **and may be pulled by the chain.** If these chains contracted ritual impurity, **one may sprinkle** waters of purification **on them and immerse them in their place** on the animal, and they need not first be removed.

The *mishna* enumerates: *yotzeh ha'gamal be'afsar* — a camel may go out with an *afsar*, a halter or bit. *Ve'naaka b'chotam* — a *naaka*, which we shall identify shortly, with a *chotam*. *Ve'ha'luvdikim b'prumbiya* — Libyan donkeys with a *prumbiya*. *Ve'sus ba'shir* — a horse with a *shir*, a collar. *Ve'chol ba'alei ha'shir yotzim ba'shir ve'nimshachim ba'shir* — and all animals that normally wear collars may go out with them and may be led by them. The *mishna* adds that if such a collar became *tamei*, one may perform the requisite sprinkling of *parah adumah* ashes or even immerse it in a *mikvah* while it remains on the animal.

## Harness Details

### Naaka & Nose Ring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״נָאקָה בַּחֲטָם״? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: נָאקְתָּא חִיוָּרְתִּי בִּזְמָמָא דְפַרְזְלָא. ״וְלוּבְדְּקִים בִּפְרוּמְבְּיָא״ — אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חֲמָרָא לוּבָא בְּפַגֵּי דְפַרְזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Several terms in the mishna were not clear to the Sages, and the Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of ***naka* with a *ḥatam*? Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: A white female camel** (*ge'onim*) **with an iron nose ring.** And what is the meaning of ***luvdekim* with a *perumbiya*? Rav Huna said: A Libyan donkey with an iron halter.**

The Gemara asks: *mai naaka b'chotam?* — what exactly is a *naaka* with a *chotam*? Rabbah bar bar Chana explains: a *naaka* is a *naakasa chivarti* — a white female camel. The *chotam* with which it goes out is a *zmama deparzala* — an iron *zmama*. Rashi identifies the *zmama* with a word in *la'az* that sounds like "muzzle," though the description — *nokev et chotamo u'machniso betocho ve'hu kemin tabaat* — makes clear that it is more like a nose ring: something that pierces the camel's nose and sits within it, ring-shaped. The implication for our purposes is that this white female camel requires a more robust control mechanism than an ordinary camel — presumably because it is harder to manage — and this iron nose ring serves that function.

### Libyan Donkeys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵוִי שַׁדַּר זוּזֵי לְבֵי חוֹזָאֵי לְמִיזְבַּן לֵיהּ חֲמָרָא לוּבָא. צָרוּ שַׁדַּרוּ לֵיהּ שְׂעָרֵי, לְמֵימַר דְּנִיגְרֵי דַחֲמָרָא שְׂעָרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned a Libyan donkey, the Gemara relates that **Levi** once **sent money to Bei Ḥozai to procure for himself a Libyan donkey,** which is reputed to be of superior quality. **They bound** his money, returned it, and **sent him barley, to say that the strides of a donkey** depend **on the barley** that it eats. If one provides his donkey with better feed, its performance will be as good as that of a Libyan donkey.

For the Libyan donkeys, the *mishna* states that they go out with a *prumbiya*. Rav Huna explains: *chamra luva be'pagi deparzala* — a Libyan donkey with an iron *pagi*. Rashi describes the *pagi* as something that goes around the face — a kind of iron halter. The Libyan donkey, like the white female camel, apparently requires stronger control equipment than its domestic counterpart.

The Gemara briefly notes that Levi once sent money to agents in Chuzei to purchase Libyan donkeys for him. They returned the money and sent back barley. The message, as Rashi explains, was: *denigrei dechamra sha'arei* — the stepping of a donkey is barley, meaning: do not go to the trouble and expense of acquiring fancy Libyan donkeys; buy donkeys locally, feed them well, and save yourself the difficulty. This digression highlights, incidentally, that these Libyan donkeys were a significant investment — possibly wilder or less tractable — which contextualizes why they required the more substantial *pagi*.

## Sufficient Protection

### Switching Accessories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַחְלִיפִין לִפְנֵי רַבִּי, שֶׁל זוֹ בָּזוֹ מַהוּ? נָאקָה בְּאַפְסָר לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מִינַּטְרָא בֵּיהּ — מַשּׂאוֹי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The students **switched** the details in the mishna **before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi and asked: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **this** animal going out into the public domain **with** that which is permitted **for that** animal? For example, may a white female camel go out with a bit or a camel with an iron nose ring? The Gemara explains: The case of **a white female camel** going out **with a bit should not be a dilemma for you; since it is not** sufficiently **secured by** a bit, **it is** regarded as **a burden** with which the animal may not go out.

In the name of Shmuel, Rav Yehuda raises the question of whether the accessories specified for particular animals may be switched — whether animal A may go out with the equipment designated for animal B and vice versa.

### Four Animals Bit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ גָּמָל בַּחֲטָם מַאי? כֵּיוָן דְּסַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּאַפְסָר — מַשּׂאוֹי הוּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא נְטִירוּתָא יַתִּירְתָּא לָא אָמְרִינַן מַשּׂאוֹי הוּא? אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כָּךְ אָמַר אַבָּא: אַרְבַּע בְּהֵמוֹת יוֹצְאוֹת בְּאַפְסָר, הַסּוּס וְהַפֶּרֶד וְהַגָּמָל וְהַחֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The case **where** there **should be a dilemma for you** is that of **a camel** going out to the public domain **with a nose ring. What is** the *halakha* in that case? The Gemara explains the dilemma: Is the *halakha* that **since a bit** alone **suffices** to secure a camel, an iron nose ring **is** considered **a burden? Or,** is the *halakha* **perhaps** that with regard to a device that provides **excessive security we do not say** that **it is a burden?** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said before** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi that **so said father,** Rabbi Yosei: **Four animals** may **go out with a bit: The horse, and the mule, and the camel, and the donkey.**

Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi responds in the name of his father: *arba behemot yotzot be'afsa* — four animals go out with a bit: the horse, the mule, the camel, and the donkey.

The question is what this statement excludes. It does not seem to limit the equipment used for these four animals — it does not say these animals may *only* use a bit and nothing more. Rather, it limits which animals a bit is *sufficient* for. A *naaka* — a white female camel — cannot go out with merely a bit. *Keivan d'la mintra bei, masui hu* — since the bit does not adequately protect or control it, putting a bit on this animal is considered a *masui*, a burden with no protective function. It is as if one placed a random object on the animal.

### Braita Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לַָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי גָּמָל בַּחֲטָם! לָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי נָאקָה בְּאַפְסָר. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: לוּבְדְּקִים וְגָמָל יוֹצְאִין בְּאַפְסָר. כְּתַנָּאֵי: אֵין חַיָּה יוֹצְאָה בְּסוּגַר, חֲנַנְיָה אוֹמֵר: יוֹצְאָה בְּסוּגַר וּבְכׇל דָּבָר הַמִּשְׁתַּמֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **What** does this list come **to exclude?** Is it **not** coming **to exclude a camel** going out **with a nose ring?** Apparently, the dilemma is resolved. The camel may go out only with a bit. The Gemara rejects this proof: **No**, the list comes **to exclude a white female camel** going out **with a bit.** **It was taught in a *baraita*: A Libyan donkey and a camel may go out with a bit.** The Gemara notes that the question whether or not an animal may go out into the public domain with excessive security is **parallel** to a dispute between **the *tanna'im*,** as it was taught in a *baraita*: **A non-domesticated animal may not go out with a collar. Ḥananya says: It may go out with a collar and with anything that secures it.**

The deeper question is raised by the *braita*: *ein chaya yotza be'soger* — an animal may not go out with a *soger*, a collar. *Chananya omer: yotza be'soger u'v'chol davar ha'mishtamer* — Chananya rules that it may go out with a collar and with anything else that protects it.

What is the case under discussion? It is not a large, dangerous animal — a collar would obviously be insufficient for that, and the *Tanna Kamma* would not need to prohibit it on grounds of being a burden. Rashi suggests it is a small animal. But if it is a small animal, a collar is exactly the right control mechanism. The Gemara concludes that the case is a cat. Rashi notes that cats *she'einan ta'avim livroa'ch kol kach* — are not particularly inclined to run away — and therefore a simple string or cord would suffice. Given that, a full collar is *netiruta yeteira* — excessive protection — and the *Tanna Kamma* holds that excessive protection is in the category of *masui*: it has no necessary protective function relative to the animal's actual needs, and is therefore treated as a burden.

### Excess = Burden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵּימָא בְּחַיָּה גְּדוֹלָה — מִי סַגִּי לַהּ סוּגַר? וְאֶלָּא בְּחַיָּה קְטַנָּה — מִי לָא סַגִּי לַהּ סוּגַר? אֶלָּא לָאו חָתוּל אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ: תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר כֵּיוָן דְּסַגִּי לַהּ בְּמִיתְנָא בְּעָלְמָא מַשּׂאוֹי הוּא, וַחֲנַנְיָה סָבַר כׇּל נְטִירוּתָא יַתִּירְתָּא לָא אָמְרִינַן מַשּׂאוֹי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the case: **With what are we dealing** here? **If you say** that we are dealing **with a large non-domesticated animal, does a collar suffice for it?** Since it does not sufficiently secure the animal, it is considered a burden, and it is prohibited for the animal to go out with it on Shabbat. **Rather,** it must be dealing **with a small non-domesticated animal.** In that case, **doesn't a collar suffice for it?** Why then does the anonymous first *tanna* hold that the animal may not go out with it? **Rather, is it not** that **the** practical **difference between their** opinions **is** with regard to **a cat? The** anonymous **first *tanna*** of the *baraita* **holds** that **since a small rope suffices** for the cat, a collar **is** considered **a burden** with which the cat may not go out into the public domain. **And Ḥananya holds** that with regard to a device that provides **excessive security, we do not say that it is a burden.**

### Chananya View

Chananya disagrees: *kol netirot yeteira lo amrinan masui hu* — even excessive protection does not constitute a burden. The item is still *doing something* — it is genuinely protective, albeit more than strictly necessary. The fact that a simpler measure would have sufficed does not convert the more robust measure into something categorically different from protection.

### Cat Case

The Gemara thus understands the dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Chananya to hinge precisely on *netiruta yeteira*: the *Tanna Kamma* holds that where something simpler would suffice, the more elaborate protective measure crosses the line into *masui*; Chananya holds that any genuinely protective measure, regardless of whether a lesser one would do, retains its character as *levush* and is not a burden.

## Halakha & Example

### Halakha = Chananya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כַּחֲנַנְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Ḥananya.** A device that provides excessive security is not considered a burden.

Rav Huna bar Chiya rules in the name of Shmuel: *halacha ke'Chananya* — the *halacha* follows Chananya. Even *netiruta yeteira*, protection that exceeds what is strictly necessary, does not constitute a *masui*.

### Donkey Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵוִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא הֲווֹ קָאָזְלִי בְּאוּרְחָא. קַדְמֵיהּ חֲמָרָא דְלֵוִי לַחֲמָרָא דְּרַבָּהּ בַּר רַב הוּנָא. חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, אֲמַר: אֵימָא לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתִיתּוֹתַב דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Levi, son of Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya, and Rabba bar Rav Huna were** once **going** together **on a road. Levi's donkey** on its own initiative **went ahead of the donkey of Rabba bar Rav Huna. Rabba bar Rav Huna was offended** because he was the greater Torah scholar, and he thought that Levi went first to assert that he considered himself the greater scholar. Levi **said** to himself: **I will say something to him, so that** he will be placated and will understand that it was not my intention to disrespect him.

The Gemara closes with a charming narrative. Levi the son of Rav Huna bar Chiya and Rabba bar Rav Huna were traveling together on the road. Levi's donkey moved ahead of Rabba bar Rav Huna's donkey. Rabba bar Rav Huna was clearly the senior figure, and the donkey's moving ahead was perceived as a slight — as if Levi were not according Rabba bar Rav Huna the proper deference. *Chalash daiteih de'Rabba bar Rav Huna* — Rabba bar Rav Huna was hurt.

Levi perceived this and wished to set things right without making the discomfort more pronounced. His solution was elegant. He turned to Rabba bar Rav Huna and posed a *halachic* question: if one has a rebellious donkey — *chamor she'askav ra'in* — one that does not behave, one that moves ahead on its own as this one just did, *mahu litzet be'pumbiya b'Shabbat* — may such a donkey go out on *Shabbat* with a *pumbiya*, a halter, even though an ordinary donkey might not require one?

### Clever Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲמוֹר שֶׁעֲסָקָיו רָעִים כְּגוֹן זֶה, מַהוּ לָצֵאת בִּפְרוּמְבְּיָא בְּשַׁבָּת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אֲמַר אֲבוּךְ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כַּחֲנַנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: An** undisciplined **donkey whose conduct is wicked like this** one that I am riding, **what** is the ruling with regard **to** having it **go out with a halter on Shabbat?** Typically, in order to secure a donkey, a bit suffices and it does not require a halter. A halter constitutes excessive security. However, the question is whether or not a halter that provides excessive security for a wild donkey like this one is considered a burden with which it is prohibited to go out to the public domain on Shabbat. Rabba bar Rav Huna **said to him**: Even if the security is considered extraneous, **your father said the following in the name of Shmuel: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Ḥananya,** who said that a device that provides excessive security is not considered a burden.

The move accomplished two things at once. It made clear that the donkey's behavior was not intentional on Levi's part — the animal was, as he put it, a rebellious donkey that moved of its own accord. And it placed Levi in the position of *talmid* asking a *shaila* of his *rav*, making the hierarchy visible and respectful.

Rabba bar Rav Huna responded: *hachi amar avuch mishmei de'Shmuel* — this is what your father said in the name of Shmuel — *halacha ke'Chananya*. The ruling follows Chananya, and therefore a rebellious donkey that requires stronger control may indeed go out with a halter on *Shabbat*, since even a more-than-necessary protective measure is not a *masui*. Whether Rabba bar Rav Huna's mood fully lifted the text does not say — but presumably it did.
