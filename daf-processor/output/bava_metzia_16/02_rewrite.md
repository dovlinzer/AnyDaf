# Bava Metzia 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Knowing field stolen

### Case setup

The Gemara turns to a case more extreme than the standard *mekach ta'ut*: what happens when the purchaser knows from the outset that the field is stolen? In our running scenario, the seller — call him Dov — steals a field from Eli and then sells it to Michael. But unlike the previous cases, Michael is fully aware that the field is not Dov's to sell. He proceeds with the purchase anyway, calculating that he will at least enjoy several years of produce before the original owner can reclaim it.

### Rav's ruling

Rav rules: *ma'ot yesh lo, shevach ein lo* — Michael can recover his principal but cannot recover the value of any improvements he made to the field. Since he entered the transaction with open eyes, the *shevach* is beyond his claim.

### Shmuel's ruling

Shmuel goes further: *afilu ma'ot ein lo* — Michael cannot recover even the principal.

### Debate foundation

The Gemara asks: *bemai kamiflagi* — what is the underlying dispute? Both Rav and Shmuel agree that Michael knew perfectly well that Dov had no legal title to the field. Where they diverge is in how to characterize the money Michael handed over, given that no valid transaction occurred.

### Rav's logic

Rav holds: *adam yode'a shekarka ein lo* — Michael knew Dov had no title. Since the transaction was legally void, what was the money? *Vegamar venatan leshum pikadon* — Michael must have intended the payment as a kind of deposit, entrusting the money to Dov for safekeeping. The Gemara immediately presses the obvious difficulty: if Michael merely wanted Dov to hold his money, why not simply say so? The answer is that Michael assumed Dov would not agree to hold the money as a straightforward bailment — that would be asking a favor. By framing it as a purchase, Michael created a pretext under which Dov would accept the funds. On Rav's view, then, since the money functioned as a *pikadon*, Michael is entitled to it back.

The Gemara is candid about the strangeness of this framing. It is not at all obvious that anyone's actual subjective intent was to create a *pikadon*. More naturally, Michael gave the money because doing so was the practical mechanism by which Dov would allow him to use the field — and perhaps because Michael hoped, however naively, that some legal right actually attached. The Gemara seems to recognize this tension but presses forward with the question of how we legally categorize money tendered in a transaction that is void, rather than treating it purely as a matter of subjective intent.

### Shmuel's logic

Shmuel holds: *vegamar venatan leshum matana* — Michael gave the money as a gift. He knew Dov had no title, so the money was not compensation for a real transfer; it was simply given to Dov. And here too the Gemara poses the same challenge: why not just announce it as a gift? The answer mirrors Rav's: *kisfah lei milta* — Dov would have been embarrassed to receive an outright gift. The purchase-frame allowed Dov to accept the money without awkwardness. On Shmuel's view, Michael freely gave that money away, and there is no basis to reclaim it.

## Sister marriage case

### Sister case intro

The Gemara notes that an identical dispute between Rav and Shmuel appears elsewhere, in the context of *mekadesh achoto* — a man who betroths his sister, giving her a ring of significant value with the declaration *harei at mekudeshet li*. The betrothal is obviously void; the question is what becomes of the ring. Rav rules *ma'ot chozrin* — the ring must be returned. Shmuel rules *ma'ot matana* — she may keep it. The same logical structure applies: both parties knew the transaction was legally inoperative, so what was the money given for — a *pikadon* or a *matana*?

### Why both cases needed

The Gemara asks: since the reasoning is identical, why must the debate be stated in both cases? Rava answers *tzricha* — both are necessary.

### Difference in recipients

The reason each case independently needs to be stated is that the two scenarios pull in opposite directions. In the field case, one might think Rav's position is clearly correct: people do not ordinarily give gifts to strangers, so *de-lo avid inish de-shadi ma'osav le-nochra* — it is implausible that Michael would simply gift money to Dov, a relative stranger, and therefore Shmuel might concede the money is a *pikadon*. But by a sister, one might argue that family relationships make gifts more natural, and Shmuel's *matana* ruling is more intuitive — perhaps Rav would concede there.

Conversely, in the sister case alone one might think Shmuel is obviously right because of the familial warmth involved, while in the field case, involving a stranger, perhaps even Shmuel would agree with Rav. Since each case might seem to favor one side, both rulings must be explicitly stated for each scenario to establish that the dispute is consistent across contexts.

## Michael's use issue

### Harvesting problem

Having established the theoretical framework of *pikadon* versus *matana*, the Gemara confronts a practical difficulty. If Michael truly gave the money merely as a deposit or a gift — in other words, if the money had no transactional connection to the field — on what basis does Michael then enter the field, plow it, plant crops, and eat the produce? The very behavior that follows the "purchase" reveals that Michael views himself as having acquired some form of right. *Hai le-ara bemai ka nachis u-feiros heichi achil* — how is he descending to the field and how is he eating its fruit?

### Resolution offered

The Gemara's answer is somewhat surprising. Michael reasons as follows: whatever basis Dov was using to occupy Eli's field, Michael will do the same — *ana eichus le-ara ve-avid ve-achul ke-heichi de-hava ka avid ihu*. He does not premise his use of the field on any legal right conveyed by the money he paid. He simply observes that Dov was working the field and assumes he can do likewise. When Eli the original owner eventually appears to reclaim it, Michael will hand it back. His physical occupation of the field is, on this account, entirely independent of the money he gave Dov.

### Money status timing

The question of what happens to the money — whether it is a *pikadon* recoverable by Michael or a *matana* belonging to Dov — only becomes live, on this reading, once the owner actually reclaims the field. At that point, depending on whether one follows Rav or Shmuel, Michael will either recover his money or not.

## Halacha protection

### First protection

Having laid out the full range of debates, the Gemara now delivers a series of practical rulings, all of which protect the purchaser and all of which rule against Shmuel. Although *halacha* generally follows Shmuel in monetary matters, in this cluster of cases the outcome consistently favors Rav.

The first ruling: *yesh lo ma'ot ve-yesh lo shevach* — in the standard case where Michael did not know the field was stolen (*lo hikir*) and the field is subsequently reclaimed, Michael is reimbursed not only for his principal but also for the *shevach*, the appreciation and improvements. This is against Shmuel, who had raised concerns about the *shevach* resembling *ribbit*. Moreover — *af al pi shelo perash* — this protection applies even if the seller did not explicitly write a guarantee of *shevach* into the sale document. Shmuel had wanted the *sofer* to explicitly ask the seller whether he was insuring the *shevach*; the halachic ruling dispenses with that requirement.

### Second protection

The second ruling addresses the *hikir bah she'eina shelo* case just analyzed: even when Michael knew the field was stolen and purchased it anyway, *ma'ot yesh lo* — he still recovers his principal. The ruling follows Rav against Shmuel.

### Lien presumption

The third ruling: *achrayut ta'ut sofer hu* — if the *sofer* neglected to write that there was a lien (*achrayut*) on the field, this is treated as a scribal oversight, and the lien is presumed to exist regardless. This applies both to loan documents — *bein bi-shitrei halva'ah* — and to sale documents — *bein bi-shitrei mekach u-mimkar*. This is significant because one might have argued that in a sale document, unlike a loan document, the purchaser's primary intent is to own the field rather than to be reimbursed, and perhaps no lien is presumed. The ruling rejects this: comprehensive protection applies in both contexts.

### Complete protection

The cumulative effect is a consistent set of rulings in favor of the purchaser. Whether he purchased in good faith and the field turned out to be stolen, or whether he purchased with knowledge that it was stolen, or whether the sale document was deficient on its face — in all scenarios the purchaser is protected: he recovers his principal and his *shevach*, and the lien is presumed written even where it was not.

## Legitimizing sale

### Case posed

The Gemara now introduces a new and intricate question, posed by Shmuel to Rav: *chazar ve-lakach mi-ba'alim harishonim mahu* — what is the law if, after selling the field to Michael, the seller Dov goes back and purchases the field from Eli, the original owner? Can Dov now demand the field back from Michael, on the grounds that he now legitimately owns it? Or do we say that Dov's purchase from Eli was made precisely in order to retroactively validate the original sale to Michael?

Underlying this question is a further puzzle we will address shortly: even if Dov's intent was to validate Michael's purchase, by what formal act of *kinyan* does Michael acquire the field? The sale document from January, when Dov had no title, was legally worthless — *chaspa be-alma hu*, a mere shard of pottery. When Dov buys from Eli in July, what mechanism transfers the field to Michael?

### Rav's answer

Rav responds: *mah machar rishon le-sheni* — what did the seller convey to Michael in the original transaction? *Kol zechus she-tavo le-yado* — all rights that would ever come into Dov's hands regarding that field. In other words, the original "sale," although defective at the time, is retroactively read as a transfer of every future entitlement Dov might acquire. Since Dov subsequently acquires full title from Eli, that title flows automatically to Michael by virtue of the earlier transaction.

### Ganav reasoning

The Gemara then asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the underlying rationale? Why do we assume Dov bought from Eli in order to benefit Michael rather than to reclaim the field for himself? Mar Zutra explains: *nicha lei delo likreyei gazlana* — Dov does not want to be called a *gazlan*. If Eli reclaims the field from Michael and Michael then comes after Dov, Dov will have a reputation as a robber. To avoid this, when Dov purchases from Eli, we assume his motivation is to ensure Michael's sale stands.

### Trustworthiness reasoning

Rav Ashi offers a distinct formulation: *nicha lei de-leiku be-heimanutei* — Dov wants his word to be reliable, his deeds to be seen as trustworthy. It is not merely the negative desire to avoid being called a *gazlan*; it is the positive aspiration to be known as a person of integrity who stands behind his transactions. The Gemara explores whether this difference in framing yields any practical difference (*mai beineihu*), and the discussion turns on edge cases such as whether the purchaser has already died. On the *gazlan*-avoidance rationale, if Michael has died and cannot call Dov a *gazlan*, the motivation disappears. But on the *heimanut* rationale, Dov still wants to be seen as trustworthy in society's eyes, and the motivation persists. The Gemara ultimately notes, however, that even on the *gazlan* rationale, Michael's heirs would call Dov the son of a *gazlan*, effectively still imputing the label to Dov — so the practical difference between the two rationales may reduce to the case where Dov himself has died: on the *gazlan* rationale, a dead man is indifferent to reputation; on the *heimanut* rationale, there is still reason to assume even a dying man wants his transactions validated.

## Validation conditions

### Multiple sales

The presumption that Dov's repurchase from Eli validates Michael's sale is not absolute. The Gemara now delineates the conditions under which it applies and those under which it does not. If, after selling to Michael, Dov also sold the field to additional people — *zavna* — or bequeathed it in his will — *orsa* — or gave it as a gift to someone else — *yahavta be-matana* — and only thereafter purchased from Eli, we do not say the repurchase was intended to validate Michael's sale. *Lav bi-mekom loke'ach kavai* — in such a case Dov has clearly not been consistent in trying to establish the original sale, and his repurchase is not treated as aimed at benefiting Michael.

### Inheritance case

Similarly, if the field simply fell to Dov as an inheritance — *nafla lei be-yerusha* — because Eli happened to be a relative of Dov's and died in the interim, we do not treat Dov's acquisition as a deliberate attempt to validate Michael's sale. *Yerusha mimmila hi* — an inheritance comes of itself, automatically. *Ve-lav ihu katarach avasrah* — Dov did not exert effort to obtain it. Since the acquisition was passive, there is no basis for inferring that it was motivated by a desire to establish the earlier transaction with Michael. The field therefore reverts to Dov and Michael's sale is not validated.

### Debt collection

A subtler case arises when it turns out that Eli actually owed Dov money, and Dov collects that debt by taking the contested field as payment. Here the analysis is more nuanced.

### Specific choice

If Eli had other assets — other plots of land, other property — and Dov specifically insisted on *this* field as payment for the debt, we infer *le-ukme kamei loke'ach kavai* — Dov's insistence on choosing precisely the field he had sold to Michael indicates that his purpose was to validate that earlier sale. The ruling in that case favors Michael.

### Only option

If, however, Eli had no other assets and this field was the only available means of payment, we cannot draw the same inference. *Zuzei hu de-vaei iparuei* — Dov was simply trying to get paid. The choice of this particular field was not meaningful; it was the only option. In that case, the acquisition is treated like an inheritance — it happened by necessity, not by purposeful action — and Michael's sale is not validated.

The common thread across these distinctions is clear: where Dov demonstrably went out of his way to acquire specifically this field after having sold it to Michael, we attribute his motivation to *heimanut* — the desire to make good on his word. Where the acquisition was passive, accidental, forced by circumstance, or contaminated by subsequent inconsistent acts, no such inference is drawn.

## Gift from owner

### Gift status

The case now examined is where Eli gives the field to Dov as a gift after Dov has sold it to Michael. Does Dov's receipt of this gift validate the original sale to Michael?

### Gift like inheritance

One view holds that a gift is like an inheritance: it comes by chance, without the recipient necessarily exerting effort to obtain it. *Ve-mimmila* — it happens of itself. On this view, receiving a gift from Eli does not indicate any particular intent on Dov's part to validate Michael's sale, and Michael does not benefit.

### Gift like sale

The opposing view holds that a gift is more like a sale: *de-ilav de-tarach ve-artzi kamei, lo yahavu lei matana* — had Dov not gone to effort, had he not cultivated Eli's goodwill and worked to endear himself to Eli, Eli would not have given the gift. The very receipt of the gift implies that Dov invested social effort — *tarach ve-artzi kamei* — specifically in order to secure the field and thereby validate the earlier sale to Michael. On this view, *ki heichi di-leikum be-heimanutei* — just as Dov might go to the trouble of purchasing the field precisely to stand behind his word, so too the effort invested in obtaining a gift reflects the same motivation.

This disagreement — between Rav Acha and Ravina — touches on a broader question in halachic thinking about whether gifts are functionally analogous to purchases. Even without the cynical assumption that all gifts are transactional, one can observe that gifts typically arise in contexts of reciprocity: a person extends goodwill to the giver, and the gift flows from that relationship. The timing here — after Dov had already sold the field to Michael — lends particular weight to the inference that Dov's cultivation of Eli's goodwill was deliberate and purposeful.

## Time limits

### Rav Huna

Even granting that Dov's repurchase of the field (or his obtaining it by gift) is motivated by the desire to legitimate Michael's sale, this presumption does not extend indefinitely. Rav Huna sets the outer limit at *she'at ha'amada be-din* — the moment the matter reaches court. Once Eli has brought Michael to *beit din* to reclaim his field, and the litigation is underway, we can no longer attribute Dov's subsequent acquisition to a voluntary desire to stand behind his transaction.

### Court proceedings

The logic is analogous to a person who confesses to a fine only after witnesses have already come forward: when someone acts only because they have been cornered, we do not credit them with the positive motivation of wanting to honor their commitments. Once Eli takes the matter to court and demands the field back from Michael, and Michael in turn demands reimbursement from Dov, and *beit din* is preparing to issue a *adrach'ta* — a writ authorizing Michael to seize Dov's property in payment — we are no longer in the domain of voluntary rectification. We have moved to a different stage entirely: the original sale is done, the field has been returned to Eli, and the proceedings are now about Michael being compensated. There is no longer a "sale to Michael" to validate. Dov's acquiring the field at this point, even if he could, would not be interpreted as an attempt to make Michael whole.

### Rav Papa

Rav Papa refines the cutoff further: even before the *adrach'ta* itself is issued, once *beit din* has begun making public auction announcements — *yomei achraza* — advertising Dov's property for sale in order to raise funds to pay Michael, the process has crossed a threshold. At that stage too, any acquisition by Dov is no longer presumed to be in service of validating the original sale.

## Acquisition mechanism

### Challenge posed

A fundamental question underlies the entire preceding discussion: how does Michael actually *acquire* the field when Dov buys it from Eli after the fact? Rami bar Chama presses this sharply: *michdi, hai loke'ach bemai kani le-hai ara* — by what mechanism did Michael acquire this field? The document Dov gave him in January, when Dov had no title, is *chaspa be-alma* — legally worthless, a piece of pottery. What is the operative act of *kinyan* that makes Michael the owner at the moment Dov purchases from Eli, even if Michael is elsewhere and the field sits idle?

### Rava's answer

Rava answers: *tehei be-ma'amino* — it operates through trust. Because Michael trusted Dov — *samcha da'ateih* — his reliance on Dov's implicit commitment to make the sale good constitutes a sufficient *gemirut da'at*, a completeness of intention, to effect a transfer. The standard framework for *kinyanim* requires two elements: a formal act of transfer and the mutual *da'at* of both parties. Rava's position is that in extraordinary circumstances, where the *da'at* on both sides is so thoroughly settled that no ambiguity remains, the formal act becomes dispensable. Here, both Dov and Michael knew the field was stolen. Michael handed over money anyway, asking nothing explicit in return — *ve-lo ka-amar lei ve-lo midi* — demanding no guarantee, lodging no formal protest. That silence, born of complete trust, generated the *gemirut da'at* that substituted for a formal *kinyan*.

### Trust paradox

The irony is striking and the Gemara does not shy away from it. It is precisely *because* the field was known to be stolen that the trust between the parties was so complete. Michael bought from Dov knowing the title was defective; there could be no pretense of a normal transaction. His decision to hand over the money in silence, under those circumstances, was an act of absolute reliance — an implicit understanding that Dov would find a way to make the sale legitimate. And it is that very trust, born of mutual awareness of the irregularity, that generates the *gemirut da'at* sufficient to effect the eventual transfer.

## Selling futures

### Futures cases

Rav Sheshes raises a challenge from an explicit Tannaic ruling bearing on the sale of futures. If a person says to another: *ma she-irash me-ava makur lach* — what I will eventually inherit from my father, I am selling you now — the Gemara rules *lo amar klum* — he has said nothing, the sale is void. Similarly: *ma she-ta'aleh metzudati makur lach* — whatever my net will catch, I am selling you — this too is void. These are classic cases of *davar shelo ba le'olam* — something not yet in the world — and they simply do not work as sales. The contrast case: if the person says *ma she-irash me-aba hayom* — what I will inherit today (with the father on his deathbed) — *devarav kayamim*, the sale is valid. And similarly, *ma she-ta'aleh metzudati hayom* — what my net catches today — is also valid.

Rav Sheshes argues that these rulings undermine the case for the field: if selling a future asset is generally void — even when the seller will obtain it through his own active effort, as with the fish — how can we say that Dov's eventual purchase from Eli validates his earlier "sale" to Michael?

## Selling futures…

### Opinions differ

Rami bar Chama praised Rav Sheshes's challenge as a genuine difficulty, calling it a sharp question from a *talmid chacham*. Rava disagreed: *gavra kachazeina, tiyufta lo kachazeina* — I see the scholar, but I do not see the difficulty. When Rabbi Abba bar Zavda was sent this question, he responded that it required no investigation — *zu einah tzricha lifnim* — either because the answer was obviously available or (as Rashi notes is the more common usage) because the question was trivially easy. Rava, by contrast, insisted: *zu tzricha lifnim ve-lifnei ve-lifnim* — this requires deep investigation. Yet there is an answer.

### Urgency exception

The answer in both the inheritance case and the fish case turns on the concept of urgency rather than *gemirut da'at*. Rav Yochanan explains: in the inheritance case — *ma she-irash me-aba hayom* — the reason the sale works on the day of the father's death is *mishum kevod aviv*, on account of honor for his father, meaning the son urgently needs funds to cover funeral expenses. The situation is so pressing that a special dispensation is in effect. Similarly, *ma she-ta'aleh metzudati hayom* — the sale of today's catch works *mishum ke-dei chayav*, because the fisherman's livelihood is at stake and the urgency of his immediate need is apparent from the fact that he cannot even wait until later in the day to sell his catch. In both instances, the validation of the future sale is rooted in a *takanah*-like dispensation for emergency circumstances, not in a general principle that selling futures works.

## Futures doctrine

### Unspecified field

Against this backdrop, the Gemara introduces Rav's broader ruling: *ha-omer le-chavero, sadeh she-ani loke'ach, uke-she-ekachenah knuyah lecha me-achshav* — if one says to another, "this field I intend to purchase, once I purchase it, it shall be yours as of now" — *kanah*, the sale works. This appears to endorse selling a future on a broad basis — precisely what the fish case seemed to deny.

### Rava's refinement

Rava attempts to confine Rav's ruling: *mistabra milta de-Rav be-sadeh stam* — Rav's ruling makes sense when the person says he will purchase *a* field, without specifying which one, because there are always fields available on the market. Since the seller can always find and purchase some field, the sale is not truly contingent on an uncertain future event; it is as good as done. But *aval be-sadeh zu* — if the person specifies *this particular field* — *mi yeimar de-mazbin lei nihalei* — who says he will actually be able to purchase it? The original owner might refuse. On this reading, Rav's ruling is limited to unspecified, market-available commodities.

### Rabbi Meir view

The Gemara immediately overturns Rava's limitation. *Ve-ha'Elohim* — in truth, *amar Rav afilu be-sadeh zu* — Rav meant even a specific field. Why? Because Rav *paskens* like Rabbi Meir. *Mi-chdi Rav ke-man amra li-shmatei* — following whom does Rav issue this ruling? Like Rabbi Meir. *De-amar adam makneh davar shelo ba le'olam* — Rabbi Meir holds that a person can transfer something not yet in the world.

### Betrothal proofs

The Gemara cites the *beraita* establishing Rabbi Meir's position. If a man says to a woman: *hitkadshi li le-achar she-etgayer* — be betrothed to me after I convert; *le-achar she-tisgayeri* — after you convert; *le-achar she-eshtachrer* — after I am freed from slavery; *le-achar she-tishtachreri* — after you are freed; *le-achar she-yamut ba'aleich* — after your husband dies; *le-achar she-yachlots lach yevameich* — after your *yavam* performs *chalitza* for you; *le-achar she-nitgarshah* — after your sister whom I am married to is divorced — in all these cases, the *Chachamim* rule *einah mekudeshet*, the betrothal does not take effect. *Rabbi Meir omer: mekudeshet* — it does. In nearly all of these cases, the condition is beyond the man's control: he cannot compel the husband to die, cannot compel the *yavam* to perform *chalitza*, and so on. Rabbi Meir nevertheless holds the conditional betrothal valid — because for Rabbi Meir, *davar shelo ba le'olam* is transferable. *Behai isha ke-sadeh zu damya* — the woman in these conditional betrothals is analogous to the case of a specific field: in both, the future acquisition is uncertain and beyond the seller's unilateral control, yet Rabbi Meir validates the transaction.

### Rambam synthesis

The Gemara has thus generated at least three distinct categories governing the sale of futures. First, the case of the stolen field: validated retroactively through *gemirut da'at*, rooted in mutual trust and the seller's desire not to be called a *gazlan* or to establish his *heimanut*. Second, the emergency cases — inheritance on the day of the father's death, today's catch — validated by a kind of *takanah* for urgent need, not by a general principle. Third, the general question of *davar shelo ba le'olam*: on Rabbi Meir's view, such a sale works categorically; on the *Chachamim*'s view, it does not.

The Rambam draws a further distinction that emerges from Rava's earlier attempt to limit Rav's ruling: even according to the *Chachamim*, who hold that *davar shelo ba le'olam* does not generally work, a sale of something readily available on the market — something the seller can simply go out and acquire — may function differently. Since the seller's obtaining the item is not genuinely contingent, such a transaction is not truly a sale of something not yet in the world. The Rambam *paskens* accordingly: where a commodity is readily available and accessible, the general prohibition on selling *davar shelo ba le'olam* does not apply. This distinction, raised in the Gemara in the contrast between *sadeh stam* and *sadeh zu*, carries direct implications for the modern futures market — the sale of standardized, fungible commodities that are always available for purchase — and represents a live halachic question of considerable practical significance.
