# Niddah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yalta & Override

### Yalta intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַלְתָּא (אייתא) [אַיְיתַאי] דְּמָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה, וְטַמִּי לַהּ. הֲדַר (אייתא) [אַיְיתַאי] לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וְדַכִּי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Yalta,** Rav Naḥman's wife, **brought blood before Rabba bar bar Ḥana, and he deemed her ritually impure. She then brought** it **before Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, and he deemed her pure.**

We resume at the bottom of 21b, returning to the case of *Yalta*, who had previously brought a bloodstain for examination, received a ruling she found questionable — though technically correct — and then obtained a revised ruling from a second authority. The *Gemara* raises the question of how that second ruling was permissible, and provides its own resolution: she reported that the first rabbi normally would have permitted stains of this type, and that he had been suffering from an eye problem at the time. But before the *Gemara*'s own answer is examined, it is worth pausing on a remarkable comment by *Tosafot*.

### Tosafot dialectic

*Tosafot* asks a question the *Gemara* itself does not bother to raise: how was *Yalta* permitted to seek a second opinion in the first place? The *Gemara*'s answer, once given, makes the question seem obvious — she could see the ruling was erroneous — but *Tosafot* treats it as a genuine methodological problem. At the bottom of *Tosafot* on this *sugya*, citing *Gemara* 60, the text reads: *v'yesh lomar d'shelo haya hashoel ela lachacham v'hashoel yishal kol mah sheyirtzeh umitoch kach nedakdek badavar upe'amim sheharishon to'eh v'yit'hadar hadavar l'ora* — "one may say that the questioner is only asking a *chacham*, and the questioner may ask whomever they wish, and through this the matter is refined, and sometimes the first authority errs and the matter is brought to light."

This is a striking *Tosafot*. In a number of places where the *Gemara* warns against seeking multiple opinions or overriding a prior ruling, *Tosafot* presents a counter-voice that emphasizes the importance of dialectic and multiple perspectives as a path toward greater truth. Even if, after receiving a *psak*, one is bound by it and may not simply shop around, the underlying process of seeking out and hearing different opinions is central to *Tosafot*'s entire approach to *halacha*. Furthermore, as *Tosafot* notes elsewhere, a healthy process exists by which one may return to the original *posek* and, through reasoned argument, persuade him to reverse his own ruling. All of this is, according to *Tosafot*, not merely tolerated but positively valuable.

## Yalta & Override (II)

### Yalta stain case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַלְתָּא אַיְיתַאי דְּמָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה וְטַמִּי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Yalta brought her blood before Rabba bar bar Ḥana and he ruled it tamei.**

The *Gemara* now frames the *Yalta* episode precisely, and does so in order to pose a legal challenge. The text reads: *Yalta ayisi dama l'kamei d'Rabbah bar bar Chana v'tamei lei* — *Yalta* brought her blood before *Rabbah bar bar Chana* and he ruled it *tamei*. She then brought it before *Rabbi Yitzchak brei d'Rav Yehudah*, and *v'dachi lei* — he ruled it *tahor*.

### Override challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: חָכָם שֶׁטִּימֵּא — אֵין חֲבֵרוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְטַהֵר, אָסַר — אֵין חֲבֵירוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְהַתִּיר!
>
> **Translation:** **But how could** Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, **act in this manner? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a halakhic authority who deemed** an item **impure, another** halakhic authority **is not allowed to deem** it **pure;** if one halakhic authority **deemed** a matter **prohibited, another** halakhic authority **is not allowed to deem** it **permitted?**

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *v'heichi avid hachi?* How could he have done this? The rule is explicit — *chacham she'tima ein chaveiro rashai letaher* — once a *chacham* has ruled something *tamei*, a colleague may not override that ruling and declare it *tahor*.

### Resolution via context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיקָּרָא טַמּוֹיֵי הֲוָה מְטַמֵּי לַהּ, כֵּיוָן דַּאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ דְּכֹל יוֹמָא הֲוָה מְדַכֵּי לִי כִּי הַאי גַוְנָא, וְהָאִידָּנָא הוּא דְּחַשׁ בְּעֵינֵיהּ — דַּכִּי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Initially** Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, **deemed her impure,** but he changed his mind **when** Yalta **said to him: Every day** that I bring blood **of this kind** of color to Rabba bar bar Ḥana **he deems me pure, and specifically now** he issued a different ruling, **as he feels** pain **in his eye.** Upon hearing this, Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, **deemed her pure.**

The *Gemara* resolves this: *me'ikara hava matmei lei* — *Rabbi Yitzchak brei d'Rav Yehudah* had initially intended to follow the first ruling out of collegial respect. But then *keivan d'amrah lei d'chol yoma madki li k'hai gavna* — she told him that *Rabbah bar bar Chana* would normally rule such stains *tahor* for her, and that the problem today was only *v'haidna d'chash b'eineih* — he was suffering from an eye ailment. At that point, *Rabbi Yitzchak* had grounds to issue his own independent ruling, and *v'dachi lei* — he declared it *tahor*.

### Yalta expertise Q&A

A natural question arises: if *Yalta* possessed sufficient expertise to recognize that the first ruling was mistaken, and sufficient sophistication to report accurately on *Rabbah bar bar Chana*'s customary practice, why did she need to consult any rabbi at all? Part of the *Gemara*'s implicit answer is that she may not have had full expertise to rule autonomously, yet she clearly had enough knowledge to identify when something had gone wrong and to provide the contextual information needed to correct it. But there is also a social dimension at work. The rabbinic establishment, particularly in matters of blood examination, was structured around male rabbinic authority, and *Yalta* appears to have navigated that structure with considerable skill — challenging it, correcting it, and ultimately getting the right answer, while never overtly defying it. The other *Yalta* stories in the *Gemara* suggest a similar pattern: working within and against authority simultaneously. One is reminded of a scenario in which a rabbi is unavailable but his wife, deeply knowledgeable in the relevant *halacha*, tells the caller what her husband would have ruled — and everyone proceeds accordingly. It is a way of transmitting the correct ruling while respecting the formal structure of authority.

## Evidence vs Decision

### Lost stain baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמֶנֶת אִשָּׁה לוֹמַר ״כָּזֶה רָאִיתִי וְאִבַּדְתִּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **A woman is deemed credible** if she **says: I saw** blood **like this** color, **but I lost it** before it could be examined.

The *Gemara* now pivots to a broader question prompted by the *Yalta* case. The episode in which *Yalta* reported *Rabbah bar bar Chana*'s customary practice might seem to suggest that a woman — or anyone — can relay a rabbinic ruling to others and thereby serve as a conduit for *psak*. The *Gemara* attempts to test this through a *baraita*: *ne'emenet ishah lomar kaze ra'iti v'ibadtiv* — a woman is believed to say "I saw a stain that looked just like this one, though I have lost the original cloth." In other words, even without the physical specimen, she may describe it by reference to another stain in front of the *rav*, and he may rule on that basis.

### Facts vs rulings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּלֵיתֵיהּ לְקַמֵּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There** it **is different, as** in that case the blood **is not before her,** and therefore the Sages were lenient. But here, the woman's friend can take her blood to a halakhic authority for examination.

The *Gemara* rejects this as a proof for the broader question, explaining: *shani hasam d'leika l'kamei* — that case is different, because the cloth is no longer available. In that situation, the woman's testimony about the stain's appearance is the only evidence available, and moreover it is testimony about *facts* — what the stain looked like — not a report of someone else's *halachic* decision. This distinction is crucial. Reporting the appearance of a stain is simply conveying evidence. Reporting that "Rabbi X ruled this *tahor*" is something qualitatively different: it involves transmitting a legal conclusion, and that requires knowing which factors were critical to the ruling.

### Non-expert limits

The *Gemara* makes this explicit: a non-expert cannot reliably relay another expert's ruling because she does not necessarily understand what elements were decisive in the original determination. She may know the conclusion without understanding its basis — and without that understanding, applying the ruling to even a superficially similar case may be entirely invalid. The concern here, as all the *Rishonim* understood it, is never about the gender of the reporter but entirely about the level of expertise. One patient telling another what a doctor prescribed, based on an apparently similar set of symptoms, is not the same as the second patient consulting the doctor directly — the relevant variables may differ in ways the untrained observer cannot detect.

## Women paskening

### Gender vs expertise

The *Gemara* at no point raises a concern about a *woman* rendering a *psak* as such. The question throughout is whether the person rendering or transmitting a ruling possesses the necessary expertise to do so reliably. This point deserves emphasis because it has sometimes been obscured in later discussions. The *Gemara*'s concern is epistemic, not gendered.

### Doctor analogy

The analogy to a physician is instructive. If a doctor prescribed a course of treatment for one patient, and that patient then advises a friend with apparently identical symptoms to follow the same regimen, the friend is not receiving medical advice — she is receiving a secondhand account of a judgment made in different circumstances, by someone who understood which variables mattered and which did not. The same logic governs the question of relaying a *halachic* ruling in matters of blood examination: the non-expert cannot know which features of the stain were decisive, and therefore cannot reliably apply the previous ruling to a new case.

## Women paskening (II)

### Deaf woman baraita

Earlier in the *sugya*, a *baraita* was cited about a deaf woman in a certain neighborhood who would examine blood stains and issue rulings for all the women in the community. This example makes the *Gemara*'s position clear: a woman who possesses the requisite expertise can absolutely serve as a *poseket* in these matters. The question was never one of gender; it was always one of training and competence.

### Yoetzet halacha

This has direct bearing on the modern institution of the *yoetzet halacha* — a woman who has undergone rigorous training in the laws of *niddah* and is certified to issue rulings in these areas. From the *Gemara*'s perspective, once the expertise question is resolved by formal training and certification, there is no remaining objection. The concern the *Gemara* expressed was always and only about the non-expert; the trained *yoetzet* does not fall into that category at all.

## Rabbi viewing stains

### Night vs day viewing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי רָאָה דָּם בַּלַּיְלָה וְטִימֵּא, רָאָה בַּיּוֹם וְטִיהֵר, הִמְתִּין שָׁעָה אַחַת חָזַר וְטִימֵּא, אָמַר: אוֹי לִי שֶׁמָּא טָעִיתִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi once **saw** a woman's **blood at night and deemed** it **impure. He** again **saw** that blood **in the day,** after it had dried, **and deemed** it **pure. He** **waited one hour** and then **deemed** it **impure again.** It is assumed that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi did not conduct another examination at this point; rather, he reasoned that the previous night's examination had been correct, and the blood's color should be deemed impure because of how it had looked when it was moist. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi then **said: Woe is me! Perhaps I erred** by declaring the blood impure, as based on its color it should be pure.

The *Gemara* now records a fascinating sequence involving *Rabbi* himself — Rebbi *Yehudah HaNasi* — examining a blood stain under varying lighting conditions. *Rabbi ra'ah dam balaylah v'timei* — *Rabbi* saw blood at night and ruled it *tamei*. *Ra'ah bayom v'tihar* — then he examined it again by day and ruled it *tahor*. This inversion already captures the essential problem with nighttime examination: candlelight not only provides inadequate illumination but also introduces a reddish cast that can make stains appear more severe than they are.

### Color change observation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמָּא טָעִיתִי? וַדַּאי טָעָה! דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא יֹאמַר חָכָם ״אִילּוּ הָיָה לַח — הָיָה וַדַּאי טָמֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this statement: **Perhaps I erred? He certainly erred, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **a halakhic authority may not say: If** the blood **were moist it would certainly have been impure,** and yet here, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi deemed the blood impure based on that type of reasoning.

The story continues: *hamten sha'ah achat, chazar v'timei* — after waiting an additional hour, *Rabbi* reversed himself again and ruled *tamei*. The passage recalls an earlier episode in which *Rabbi* revised a ruling connected to the position of *Rabbi Eliezer*, and reflects a characteristic quality: constant reflection and willingness to reconsider.

### Rabbi's doubt

Following this second reversal, *Rabbi* expressed misgiving: *amar oy li shema taiti* — "woe to me, perhaps I have made a mistake." The *Gemara* responds: *vadai taita* — you have certainly made a mistake.

### Night ruling tentative

The *Gemara*'s initial understanding is that *Rabbi*'s nighttime ruling was inherently tentative, since examination by candlelight is acknowledged to be unreliable. On that reading, when he examined the stain by daylight and found it *tahor*, that was the authoritative ruling — clear, well-lit, and definitive — and reverting to the nighttime ruling constituted the error.

### Judge by clear sight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵין לוֹ לַדַּיָּן אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁעֵינָיו רוֹאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that the incident did not unfold as initially assumed. **Rather,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi examined the blood three times, as he **said: A judge has only what his eyes see** as the basis for his ruling.

This is reinforced by a *baraita*: a *chacham* should not say, "had you shown me the stain when it was fresh and moist, I surely would have ruled it *tamei*." The rule is *ein lo l'dayan ela mah she'einav ro'os* — the judge has only what his eyes see. In this context the phrase is not merely metaphorical; it is literally about the visual conditions of examination. What cannot be seen clearly cannot be ruled upon. A woman often performs a *bedikah* and notices something that appears problematic, but by the time the cloth reaches the rabbi it has dried and changed appearance. The rabbi rules on what is before him, not on what it might have been when still moist — unless the woman herself states definitively that it was problematic at the time, in which case her own assessment stands as the ruling. Otherwise, the present visual reality governs entirely.

## Rabbi viewing stains (II)

### Definite vs tentative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיקָּרָא אַחְזְקֵיהּ בְּטָמֵא, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזָא לְצַפְרָא דְּאִשְׁתַּנִּי אֲמַר (ליה): וַדַּאי טָהוֹר הֲוָה, וּבַלַּיְלָה הוּא דְּלָא אִתְחֲזִי שַׁפִּיר. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזָא דַּהֲדַר אִשְׁתַּנִּי אָמַר: הַאי טָמֵא הוּא, וּמִפְכָּח הוּא דְּקָא (מפכח) [פָכַח] וְאָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Initially,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **established the presumptive status** of the blood **as ritually impure,** but **when he saw in the morning that** its color had **changed, he said: It was definitely pure** last night as well, **and only** because it was **at night** I thought that it was impure, **because it could not be seen well.** Subsequently, **when he saw** after a short while **that** its color **again changed,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said: This** blood **is impure, and it is gradually becoming lighter** as its color fades.

The *Gemara* then revisits and corrects the previous reading. In fact, *Rabbi*'s original nighttime ruling was not tentative — *me'ikara achzukei b'tamei*, he established it as a definite ruling of *tamei*. This makes the subsequent reversal harder to explain. If it was a definite ruling, how did he allow himself to reverse it upon seeing it in daylight?

### Changing colors insight

The *Gemara* explains: *u'keivan d'chaza d'tzafra ishtani* — when he saw it in the morning it appeared to have changed. He assumed he had simply not seen it well at night, and on that basis reversed to *tahor*. But then *u'keivan d'chaza d'hadar ishtani* — when he saw it again later in the same day, it appeared to have changed again. At that point, *Rabbi* understood that the phenomenon was not one of poor nighttime vision but of a stain that was continuously and inherently changing color. *U'mifachach hu d'ka mifachach v'azil* — it is simply changing on its own. That being the case, he restored his original ruling: *amar vadai tamei hu* — it is genuinely *tamei*, and what he saw in the morning was not evidence that he had seen it incorrectly at night, but simply another moment in an ongoing process of color change. The last time he thought he had made a mistake, he had not.

## Lighting for examination

### Candlelight vs shade

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי בָּדֵיק לְאוֹר הַנֵּר. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵף בָּדֵיק בְּיוֹם הַמְעוּנָּן בֵּינֵי עַמּוּדֵי. אָמַר רַב אַמֵּי בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְכוּלָּן אֵין בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתָן אֶלָּא בֵּין חַמָּה לְצֵל. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: בְּחַמָּה וּבְצֵל יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the manner in which the Sages would examine blood, the Gemara relates that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **would examine** blood **by candlelight. Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosef, would examine** blood **between the pillars** of the study hall even **on a cloudy day,** despite the fact that it was not very light there. **Rav Ami bar Shmuel says: And** in **all these** cases, **one examines** blood **only between sunlight and shade. Rav Naḥman** says that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** One stands **in** a place lit by the **sun, and** he conducts the examination **under the shadow of his hand,** i.e., he places his hand over the blood. In this manner the color of the blood can be best discerned.

Having established the unreliability of nighttime viewing, the *Gemara* turns to a discussion of optimal lighting conditions. *Rabbi badik l'or haner* — *Rabbi* himself would examine stains by candlelight, which from the preceding discussion is understood to be suboptimal but permissible in certain circumstances. *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yosi badik b'yom hame'unan bein ha'amudim* — *Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi* would examine on a cloudy day between pillars, effectively placing the stain in indirect sunlight between zones of shade.

### Sun and shade ideal

*Rav Ami bar Shmuel* states this principle succinctly and comprehensively: *bekullan ein bodkin otan ela bein hamma latzel* — in all these cases, stains should be examined only between direct sunlight and shade, that is, in indirect sunlight. Not fully in shade, not in direct sunlight, but in the intermediate zone.

### Hand shade method

*Rav Nachman* citing *Rabbah bar Avuha* adds a practical refinement: *behamma uvetzel yado* — one may examine in direct sunlight while using one's own hand to create a zone of shade over the stain. *Rashi* reads this as an acceptable alternative, though the *sugya* suggests it may in fact be the preferred method.

### Why indirect works

One might ask why indirect sunlight is superior to direct sunlight, given that the latter provides more light. The answer appears to be twofold: direct sunlight is harsh on the eyes, leading to visual strain that compromises accurate color perception, and — analogously to the problem with candlelight — it may affect the perceived color spectrum of what is being examined. Just as candlelight renders colors more red, extremely direct sunlight may distort in its own way. The concern is not merely the quantity of light but the quality and neutrality of the illumination.

## Wine color standards

### Sharon wine ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשָּׁרוֹנִי נִידּוֹן כַּכַּרְמְלִי, חַי וְלֹא מָזוּג, חָדָשׁ וְלֹא יָשָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** For the purposes of the examination of blood, **the** wine **of the Sharon** region in Eretz Yisrael **has the same status as undiluted Carmelite** wine **and not diluted** Carmelite wine, **new** Carmelite wine **and not old** Carmelite wine.

The *Gemara* concludes the *perek*'s discussion of color by addressing the standard of comparison used in blood examination. The *mareh* of blood is compared to wine, and a *baraita* establishes: *Hasharoni nidon ke-Charmeli chai* — Sharon wine mixed two parts water to one part wine has the same color, for purposes of comparison, as undiluted wine from the Carmel region. This allows for standardized comparison across wines of different strengths.

### Wine aging requirement

The wine used for comparison should be neither freshly pressed nor very old — neither *chadash* nor *yashan* — but medium-aged, so that its color falls in the relevant range.

### Tiberian glass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אֲבוּדִימִי: וְכוּלָּן, אֵין בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתָן אֶלָּא בְּכוֹס טְבֶרְיָא פָּשׁוּט. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁל כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ מַחְזִיק לוֹג — עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ מִמָּנֶה, שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין — עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ מִמָּאתַיִם. כּוֹס טְבֶרְיָא פָּשׁוּט, אֲפִילּוּ מַחֲזִיק שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין — עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ מִמָּנֶה, וְאַיְּידֵי דִּקְלִישׁ — יְדִיעַ בֵּיהּ טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avudimi says: And** in **all** cases of blood that has the color of diluted wine, **one examines** blood **only with a simple Tiberian cup.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason? Abaye says:** Containers are designed according to **a universal** standard, such that a cup that can **contain a *log*** of wine, **one fashions it from** material weighing **one hundred dinars,** whereas a cup that can contain **two *log*, one fashions it from** material weighing **two hundred dinars.** By contrast, in the case of **a simple Tiberian cup, even** one that can **contain two *log*, one fashions it from** material weighing **one hundred dinars. And since** the material from which the cup is made **is weak,** it is more transparent and therefore the redness of the wine inside is **more noticeable.** Consequently, one must compare the blood to wine in a cup of this kind.

*Rav Yosef bar Avdumi* adds a further specification: *vekullan ein bodkin otan ela bekos Tverya pashut* — all of these comparisons should be made using a simple Tiberian glass. The choice of container affects the perceived color of the wine within it, and a standard must be established.

### Thin glass reason

*Abaye* explains the reason: ordinary cups, however large, are made with a fixed ratio of material — a vessel holding one *log* is made from one unit of copper or glass, a vessel holding two *log* from two units. A simple Tiberian cup, even if it holds two *log*, is made from only one unit of material — *aidi dekallish yedei bei tefei* — and because the glass is very thin, the color of what it contains can be seen more clearly through it. With that, *Hadran alach Kol Hayad* — the *perek* concludes.

## Yoledet tumah intro

### Yoledet periods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הַמַּפֶּלֶת חֲתִיכָה, אִם יֵשׁ עִמָּהּ דָּם — טְמֵאָה, וְאִם לָאו — טְהוֹרָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ — טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a woman **who discharges** an amorphous **piece** of tissue, **if there is blood** that emerges **with it,** the woman is **ritually impure** with the impurity of a menstruating woman. **And if not, she is pure,** as she is neither a menstruating woman nor a woman after childbirth. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** In **both this** case, where blood emerged, **and that** case, where no blood emerged, the woman **is impure** with the impurity of a menstruating woman, as there was certainly undetected blood that emerged with the flesh.

The *Gemara* now turns to the *tumah* of the *yoledet* — a woman who has given birth — which is the subject of the next *perek*. The basic framework, derived from the *Torah* (*Vayikra* 12), is as follows: a woman who gives birth to a male is *temeiah* for seven days as a *niddah*, followed by thirty-three days of *demei tohar*; a woman who gives birth to a female is *temeiah* for fourteen days, followed by sixty-six days of *demei tohar*. These are vaginal births; a Cesarean birth, as will be noted later, has a different status.

### Demei tohar blood

During the *demei tohar* period, even if the woman sees blood, it does not render her a *niddah*. This is a unique category — the blood is *tahor* by virtue of its timing within the post-birth period.

### Miscarriage 40 days

Crucially, *tumas yoledet* applies not only to full-term births but to miscarriages as well, provided the fetus was at least forty days developed. A miscarriage of a fetus that meets this threshold triggers the full *yoledet* framework.

### Non-human form exception

The exception is a miscarriage that does not have human form — whether because it is insufficiently developed or because it is deformed in such a way that it lacks a recognizable human appearance. Such a miscarriage does not confer *yoledet* status, and the question of whether a given discharge meets this threshold occupies much of the *perek*.

## Miscarriage cases

### Small chunk rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפֶּלֶת חֲתִיכָה, אִם יֵשׁ עִמָּהּ דָּם תְמֵאִה, וְאִם לַיְו תְהוֹרָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ תְמֵאִה. הַמַּפֶּלֶת כְּמִין קְלִיפָּה, כְּמִין שַׂעֲרָה, כְּמִין עָפָר, כְּמִין יַבְחוּשִׁין אֲדוּמִּים — תָּטִיל לַמַּיִם: אִם נִמּוֹחוּ תְמֵאִה, וְאִם לַיְו תְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a woman **who discharges** an amorphous **piece** of tissue, **if there is blood** that emerges **with it,** the woman is **ritually impure** with the impurity of a menstruating woman. **And if not, she is pure,** as she is neither a menstruating woman nor a woman after childbirth. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** In **both this** case, where blood emerged, **and that** case, where no blood emerged, the woman **is impure.** [Additional clause:] In the case of a woman **who discharges** an item **similar to a shell,** or **similar to a hair,** or **similar to soil,** or **similar to mosquitoes,** if such items are **red, she should cast** them **into water** to ascertain their nature: **If they dissolved,** it is blood, and the woman **is impure** with the impurity of a menstruating woman; **and if not, she is pure.**

The *Mishnah* opens with the case of a woman who discharges a small chunk — a *chatichah*. *Im yesh imah dam temeiah* — if blood accompanies it, the blood renders her a *niddah*. *Ve-im lav tehorah* — if there is no blood, she is *tehorah*, because the chunk itself does not constitute a fetus and does not independently render her a *niddah*.

### Rabbi Yehudah strict

*Rabbi Yehudah* disagrees: *bein kach u-vein kach temeiah* — whether blood accompanies it or not, she is *temeiah*. The basis for this position will be the focus of the *Gemara*'s analysis.

### Tiny fragment test

The *Mishnah* then addresses cases where what was discharged is something even smaller and less defined — something resembling a shell, a hair, a barleycorn, dust, or a red gnat. These tiny fragments clearly do not constitute a fetus. The only question is whether they represent dried blood. The procedure: *hatilu le-mayim* — place it in water. *Im nimochu temeiah* — if it dissolves, it is dried blood and she is *temeiah*. *Ve-im lav tehorah* — if it does not dissolve, it is something else and she is *tehorah*.

### Non-human animal forms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפֶּלֶת כְּמִין דָּגִים, חֲגָבִים, שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים — אִם יֵשׁ עִמָּהֶם דָּם טְמֵאָה, וְאִם לָאו — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of a woman **who discharges** an item **similar to fish** or to **grasshoppers, repugnant creatures, or creeping animals, if there is blood** that emerges **with them,** the woman **is impure** with the impurity of a menstruating woman. **And if not, she is pure.**

If the discharge resembles a non-human creature — a fish, grasshopper, rodent, or other creeping thing — the rule is again tied to accompanying blood: *im yesh imahem dam temeiah, ve-im lav tehorah*. It is not considered a fetus, and only actual blood triggers *niddah* status.

### Animal form fetus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפֶּלֶת מִין בְּהֵמָה, חַיָּה וָעוֹף, בֵּין טְמֵאִין בֵּין טְהוֹרִין — אִם זָכָר — תֵּשֵׁב לְזָכָר, וְאִם נְקֵבָה — תֵּשֵׁב לִנְקֵבָה. וְאִם אֵין יָדוּעַ — תֵּשֵׁב לְזָכָר וְלַנְּקֵבָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מִצּוּרַת אָדָם אֵינוֹ וָלָד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a woman **who discharges** tissue in the form of **a type of domesticated animal, undomesticated animal, or bird, whether** it had the form of **a non-kosher** species or **a kosher** species, **if** it was **a male** fetus, then **she observes** the periods of impurity, seven days, and purity, thirty-three days, established in the Torah (see Leviticus 12:2–5) **for** a woman who gives birth to **a male. And if** the fetus was **a female,** the woman **observes** the periods of impurity, fourteen days, and purity, sixty-six days, established in the Torah **for** a woman who gives birth to **a female.** **And if** the sex of the fetus **is unknown, she observes** the strictures that apply to a woman who gave birth both **to a male and to a female.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: Any** fetus **that is not of human form is not** regarded as **an offspring** with regard to observance of these periods, and she is permitted to engage in intercourse provided that she does not experience a discharge of uterine blood.

However, if the discharge resembles a mammal, wild animal, or bird — whether of a *kosher* species or not — that *does* carry the status of a fetus, and the *yoledet* framework applies. The question then becomes whether the birth was of a male or female. If male genitalia are visible, *teshev le-zachar* — she observes seven days *tamei* and thirty-three days *tahor*. If female, *teshev le-nekeivah* — fourteen and sixty-six. If the sex cannot be determined, *Rabbi Meir* rules that she must be strict in both directions: fourteen days *temeiah*, and only the remaining days of the thirty-three-day *tahor* period that fall after the fourteen days have elapsed. The *Chachamim*, however, hold that *kol she-ein bo mi-tzuras adam eino valad* — anything lacking human form does not have the status of a fetus at all, and none of the *yoledet* rules apply.

## Yehudah chunk debate

### Red color limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא טִימֵּא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֶלָּא בַּחֲתִיכָה שֶׁל אַרְבַּעַת מִינֵי דָמִים, אֲבָל שֶׁל שְׁאָר מִינֵי דָמִים — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if a woman discharges an amorphous piece of tissue and no blood emerges with it, the Rabbis say that she is pure, whereas Rabbi Yehuda says that she is impure. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Rabbi Yehuda deemed** the woman **impure,** despite the fact that no blood emerged, **only in** the case of **a piece** of tissue that has the appearance **of** one of **the four types of** ritually impure **blood,** as in such a case the piece has the status of blood. **But** if it has the appearance **of other types of blood,** the woman **is pure.**

The *Gemara* focuses first on *Rabbi Yehudah*'s ruling that a chunk renders the woman *temeiah* even without accompanying blood. *Rav Yehudah* citing *Shmuel* limits *Rabbi Yehudah*'s ruling: *lo timah Rabbi Yehudah ela b'chatichah shel arba'ah minei damim* — *Rabbi Yehudah* only declared *tamei* when the chunk bore one of the four problematic colors associated with *niddah* blood. If the chunk's color was one of the acceptable non-blood colors, *Rabbi Yehudah* would agree it is *tehorah*. On this reading, the debate between *Rabbi Yehudah* and the *Chachamim* concerns whether a red-colored chunk can be presumed to constitute dried blood even without the dissolving test.

### Color consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: שֶׁל אַרְבַּעַת מִינֵי דָמִים — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל טְמֵאָה, שֶׁל שְׁאָר מִינֵי דָמִים — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל טְהוֹרָה. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁהִפִּילָה, וְאֵינָהּ יוֹדַעַת מָה הִפִּילָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: זִיל בָּתַר רוֹב חֲתִיכוֹת, וְרוֹב חֲתִיכוֹת שֶׁל (מִינֵי) אַרְבַּעַת מִינֵי דָמִים הָוְיָין. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן רוֹב חֲתִיכוֹת שֶׁל אַרְבַּעַת מִינֵי דָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says** there is a different explanation of the dispute: If a woman discharges a piece of tissue that has the appearance **of** one of **the four types of** ritually impure **blood, everyone,** i.e., Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis, **agrees** that she **is impure.** Likewise, if it has the appearance **of other types of blood, everyone agrees** that **she is pure.** **They disagree only** with regard to a case **where** the woman **discharged** an amorphous piece of tissue, **and she** herself **does not know** exactly **what** was the appearance of the piece of tissue that **she discharged,** e.g., if it was lost. **Rabbi Yehuda holds: Follow the majority of** discharges of amorphous **pieces** of tissue, **and the majority of pieces** of tissue **are** of the appearance **of** one of the **four types of** impure **blood. And the Rabbis hold: We do not say** that **the majority of pieces** of flesh have the appearance **of** one of the **four types of** impure **blood.**

*Rabbi Yochanan* offers a different framing entirely: *shel arba'ah minei damim divrei hakol teme'ah* — if a chunk bears the four problematic blood colors, everyone agrees it is *temeiah*, since it is manifestly dried blood. *Shel she'ar minei damim divrei hakol tehorah* — if it bears acceptable colors, everyone agrees she is *tehorah*. Where then is the debate? *Lo necheluku ela she-lo yadah mah hepila* — the dispute is limited to the case where she did not see what was discharged, the chunk was lost.

## Yehudah chunk debate (II)

### Rov presumption

*Rabbi Yochanan* continues: in the case where the chunk is not seen, *Rabbi Yehudah savar azil batar rov chatichot* — *Rabbi Yehudah* holds that we follow the majority: most discharges from the uterus are the four problematic blood colors, and therefore even an unseen chunk is presumed to have been of that type.

### Chezkat tahorah

The *Chachamim* reject this application of *rov*. Either they disagree with the statistical claim, or — more likely — they hold that the woman's *chezkat tahorah*, her presumptive status of purity, cannot be overridden by a statistical inference alone. She retains her *chezkat tahorah* unless actual blood is reported or observed.

## Oshaya baraita

### Color listing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא כִּי אֲתָא רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא, אֲתָא וְאַיְיתִי מַתְנִיתָא בִּידֵיהּ: הַמַּפֶּלֶת חֲתִיכָה אֲדוּמָּה, שְׁחוֹרָה, יְרוּקָּה, וּלְבָנָה — אִם יֵשׁ עִמָּהּ דָּם טְמֵאָה, וְאִם לָאו — טְהוֹרָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ טְמֵאָה. קַשְׁיָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל בַּחֲדָא, וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּתַרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But when Rav Hoshaya came from Neharde'a, he came and brought a *baraita* with him** that states: In the case of a woman **who discharges a piece** of tissue that is **red, black, green, or white, if there is blood** that emerges **with it,** the woman **is impure, and if not, she is pure. Rabbi Yehuda says: Both** in **this** case, where blood emerged, **and** in **that** case, where no blood emerged, the woman **is impure.** This *baraita* poses **a difficulty to** the statement of **Shmuel with regard to one** aspect of his opinion, **and** it poses a difficulty **to** the statement of **Rabbi Yoḥanan with regard to two** aspects of his opinion.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from a *baraita* brought by *Rav Oshaya* from *Nehardea*: *hamapeles chatichah adumah, shechorah, yerukkah u-levana* — a woman who discharges a chunk that is red, black, green, or white. *Im yesh imah dam teme'ah, ve-im lav tehorah* — the *Chachamim* say: if blood accompanies it she is *temeiah*, if not she is *tehorah*.

### Yehudah either way

*Rabbi Yehudah omer bein kach u-vein kach teme'ah* — *Rabbi Yehudah* rules *temeiah* regardless, whether or not blood is present.

### Shmuel contradiction

This *baraita* contradicts *Shmuel*'s limiting principle. *Shmuel* said *Rabbi Yehudah* only invoked *tum'ah* for chunks bearing the four problematic blood colors, yet this *baraita* explicitly includes green and white — non-blood colors — in the list for which *Rabbi Yehudah* says *temeiah*. One cannot deflect this by saying *Rabbi Yehudah* is only arguing about the red and black cases, since the *baraita* would then have had no reason to mention green and white at all. The mention of green and white serves precisely to tell us that *Rabbi Yehudah*'s ruling extends even there.

### Yochanan contradiction

The same *baraita* contradicts *Rabbi Yochanan* on two counts. *Rabbi Yochanan* held that a red chunk is *temeiah* by universal consensus. But the *baraita* opens with a case of a red chunk and explicitly states that the *Chachamim* say *tehorah* if there is no accompanying blood — meaning the *Chachamim* dispute even the red case. One cannot rescue *Rabbi Yochanan* by suggesting the *Chachamim*'s leniency applies only to green and white, since the *baraita* would have had no reason to mention the red case unless it was telling us that even the *Chachamim* permit it.

## Oshaya baraita (II)

### Blood assumption basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בְּאֶפְשָׁר לִפְתִיחַת הַקֶּבֶר בְּלֹא דָּם קָמִיפַּלְגִי, וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְהָנֵי תַנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: קִשְּׁתָה שְׁנַיִם, וְלַשְּׁלִישִׁי הִפִּילָה, וְאֵינָהּ יוֹדַעַת מָה הִפִּילָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ סְפֵק לֵידָה סְפֵק זִיבָה, מְבִיאָה קׇרְבָּן וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מְבִיאָה קׇרְבָּן וְנֶאֱכָל, לְפִי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לִפְתִיחַת הַקֶּבֶר בְּלֹא דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yehuda says** there is a different explanation of the dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda: **They disagree with regard to** whether or not **opening of the womb is possible without** a discharge of **blood. And** they disagree **with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute between these *tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a woman experienced **difficulty in labor** during which blood emerged on **two** consecutive days, **and on the third** day **she discharged, but she does not know what she discharged, it is uncertain** whether she has the status of a woman who gave **birth,** and it is **uncertain** whether she has the status of a woman who experienced ***ziva*.** Therefore **she brings an offering, but it is not eaten** by the priests. The reason is that perhaps she neither gave birth nor experienced *ziva*, and is therefore exempt from bringing an offering. Consequently, her bird sin offering is disqualified, and is forbidden in consumption, as a bird offering is killed by pinching its neck, which is not the valid manner of slaughtering a non-sacred bird. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The woman **brings an offering, and it is eaten.** The reason is that she is certainly either a woman after childbirth or a *zava*, **as opening of the womb is not possible without** a discharge of **blood.**

The *Gemara* concludes: the true basis of the dispute has nothing to do with the color of the chunk. *Rabbi Yehudah* holds that whenever something is discharged from the uterus, blood will invariably accompany it — the expulsion itself causes some of the uterine lining to shed, producing blood. This is so regardless of what the discharged material looks like. The *Chachamim* reject this assumption: it is possible for something to be discharged without blood coming with it. That is the real *machloket* — a factual-physiological assumption about whether uterine discharge is always accompanied by blood.

## Pesichat ha-kever

### Grave opening concept

The *Gemara* does not explicitly provide a new Sefaria segment at this point in the essay, so we continue without a blockquote.

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* formulates the dispute sharply: *ba-efshar le-ftihat ha-kever be-lo dam ka-mifalgi* — the question upon which they dispute is whether it is possible to have an *opening of the kever* without blood. This is the principle of *pesichat ha-kever*, the opening of the womb.

### Kever as womb

*Rashi* clarifies immediately: *ftihat ha-kever* — *ha-rechem*, the womb. The term *kever*, literally "grave," is here used as a synonym for *rechem*. This is a striking terminological choice. The *Gemara* has used various metaphors for the uterus — *rechem*, with its connotations of *rachamim* and life; *makor*, a spring or source; *bayit* and *cheder*, relatively neutral spatial images. The word *kever* is jarring. The reason the *Gemara* employs it here, however, is contextually coherent: this entire discussion concerns miscarriage, cases where a pregnancy has ended without a live birth. In that specific context, the womb has literally functioned as a grave.

### Womb grave metaphor

What is more troubling is that later *poskim*, relying on the *Gemara*'s language, carry the term *kever* into discussions of entirely different scenarios — gynecological examinations, surgical procedures, any situation in which an instrument passes through the cervix — and apply the principle of *pesichat ha-kever belo dam* in those contexts as well. In doing so, the terminology of the grave becomes attached to the living, functioning uterus in circumstances entirely unrelated to miscarriage. It is important to recognize that the *Gemara*'s original use of *kever* was specifically tied to these cases of fetal loss, and that its extension to other contexts by later authorities is a *chiddush* with significant implications.

### Noda B'Yehudah app

The *Noda Bi-Yehudah* represents the most prominent and far-reaching application of this principle. He rules that any insertion of an instrument through the cervix — any gynecological procedure that penetrates the *os* — constitutes a *ftihat ha-kever*, and that since *ee efshar le-ftihat ha-kever belo dam*, blood will inevitably be released from the uterine lining, rendering the woman a *niddah*. This is a major *chiddush*: the principle, which in the *Gemara* applies exclusively to cases of miscarriage where something is being expelled from the uterus outward, is applied by the *Noda Bi-Yehudah* in the reverse direction — to something being inserted from outside inward. The assumption is that any such insertion disturbs the uterine lining and produces uterine blood, not merely the blood of a wound (*dam makka*), and thereby triggers *niddah* status.

## Kashta shnayyim

### Unclear miscarriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִשְּׁתָה שְׁנַיִם, וְלַשְּׁלִישִׁי הִפִּילָה, וְאֵינָהּ יוֹדַעַת מָה הִפִּילָה. הֲרֵי זוֹ סְפֵק לֵידָה סְפֵק זִיבָה, מְבִיאָה קׇרְבָּן וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מְבִיאָה קׇרְבָּן וְנֶאֱכָל, שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לִפְתִיחַת הַקֶּבֶר בְּלֹא דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** If a woman experienced **difficulty in labor** during which blood emerged on **two** consecutive days, **and on the third** day **she discharged, but she does not know what she discharged, it is uncertain** whether she has the status of a woman who gave **birth,** and it is **uncertain** whether she has the status of a woman who experienced ***ziva*.** Therefore **she brings an offering, but it is not eaten** by the priests. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The woman **brings an offering, and it is eaten, as opening of the womb is not possible without** a discharge of **blood.**

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* that directly applies the *pesichat ha-kever* principle. A woman has *kashta* — labor pains, accompanied by bleeding — for two days. On the third day she miscarries, but does not know the nature of what she miscarried. The uncertainty is double: she does not know whether the discharge constituted a *halachic* fetus, and she does not know whether blood accompanied the third-day expulsion.

### If yoledet status

If what she expelled on the third day is considered a *halachic* fetus, she is a *yoledet*. Critically, in that case, the bleeding of the first two days is attributed to the birth process and does not constitute *zavah* bleeding — the blood of a woman in *yoledet* status during labor is categorically different from *zavah* blood. The consequence is that she is not a *zavah gedola*.

### If not yoledet

If what she expelled is not considered a fetus, then the two days of bleeding fall in the *zavah* period without the exemption of childbirth. Combined with the third day's blood, she has bled for three consecutive days in the *zavah* period. She is a *zavah gedola*, requiring seven clean days before she can immerse.

### Safek status

The baseline ruling: *harei zu safek leida safek ziva* — she is of uncertain status, possibly a *yoledet*, possibly a *zavah*. There is also a third possibility — she is neither — because if the discharge was not a *halachic* fetus and no blood came out on the third day, she would not meet the criteria for either status. Given this uncertainty, she brings a *korban* — as both a *yoledet* and a *zavah gedola* are obligated to bring one — but *ve-eino ne'echal*, it is not eaten. One cannot eat a *korban* when it is uncertain whether the obligation existed, because if she is neither *yoledet* nor *zavah* the animal was never validly consecrated for this purpose.

### Yehoshua position

*Rabbi Yehoshua* disagrees: *mevia korban ve-ne'echal* — she brings a *korban* and it is eaten. His reasoning: *she'ee efshar le-ftihat ha-kever belo dam* — it is impossible for the womb to open without blood. Whenever a miscarriage occurs, blood invariably accompanies the expulsion. Therefore the third day definitively involved blood, eliminating the "neither" scenario. She is certainly either a *yoledet* or a *zavah*, and either way she brings and eats the *korban*. The case thus provides an explicit *Tannaitic* statement of the *pesichat ha-kever* principle — and it is clear that the debate between *Rabbi Yehoshua* and the *Tanna Kamma* is precisely the same as the debate between *Rabbi Yehudah* and the *Chachamim* about the chunk: can there be an opening of the uterus without blood?

## Pesichat ha-kever (II)

### Feinstein limitation

*Rav Moshe Feinstein* substantially limits the scope of the *Noda Bi-Yehudah*'s ruling. His argument is that the principle of *pesichat ha-kever belo dam* — the assumption that any opening of the uterus produces blood — applies only when the instrument or object involved is sufficiently large to genuinely distend the cervix: approximately three-quarters of an inch in diameter or larger. Below that threshold, he contends, there is no *ftihat ha-kever* in the *halachic* sense, and the *pesichat ha-kever* concern does not arise. In practice, *Rav Moshe* notes, virtually no standard gynecological instrument employed in routine examination reaches that size. The effect of his ruling is to render the *Noda Bi-Yehudah*'s position nearly inapplicable in contemporary medical contexts. This remains one of the major practical disputes in the application of this *sugya* to modern gynecological *sha'elot*.

## Opening chunk debate

### Sumachus view

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּפֶּלֶת חֲתִיכָה, סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר כִּדְבָרָיו: קוֹרְעָהּ, אִם יֵשׁ דָּם בְּתוֹכָהּ — טְמֵאָה, וְאִם לָאו — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of a woman **who discharges** an amorphous **piece** of tissue, **Sumakhos says in the name of Rabbi Meir, and likewise Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya would say in accordance with his statement:** A Sage who is presented with this piece of tissue should **tear it** to examine it. **If there is blood inside it,** the woman is **ritually impure, and if not, she is pure.**

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* offering several distinct opinions on how to adjudicate the status of a discharged chunk. *Sumchos* says in the name of *Rabbi Meir*, and *Rabbi Shimon ben Nanas* concurred: *kor'ah* — tear the chunk open. *Im yesh dam betochah teme'ah* — if blood is found inside it, she is *temeiah*. *Ve-im lav tehorah* — if not, she is *tehorah*. The ruling focuses on whether the chunk is itself filled with blood: whether it is, so to speak, a blood-soaked mass.

### Stricter than chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבָּנַן, וַעֲדִיפָא מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** This statement is basically **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** in the mishna, **but it is** more **far-reaching,** i.e., more stringent, **than that** ruling of **the Rabbis.**

The *Gemara* observes that *Sumchos* is stricter than the *Chachamim* in one respect but does not adopt *Rabbi Yehudah*'s position either. The *Chachamim* hold that blood visible on the outside of a chunk triggers *tum'ah*, but they do not extend concern to blood contained within the interior of the chunk — blood that has not made contact with the vaginal canal. *Sumchos* goes further: even internal blood matters. At the same time, *Sumchos* does not hold *pesichat ha-kever* as *Rabbi Yehudah* does; he does not say that blood must automatically be assumed. He requires actual blood to be found — just that it may be found internally rather than only externally.

### Akiva view

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הַמַּפֶּלֶת חֲתִיכָה, רַבִּי אַחָא אוֹמֵר: קוֹרְעָהּ, אִם תּוֹכָהּ מַאֲדִים — טְמֵאָה, וְאִם לָאו — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita* with regard to a woman **who discharges** an amorphous **piece** of tissue, that **Rabbi Aḥa says:** One **tears it** open, and **if its interior looks red,** even if it contains no blood, the woman **is impure; and if** it does **not** have a red appearance, **she is pure.**

A further *baraita* records *Rabbi Akiva*'s position: *kor'ah* — open it. *Im tocho ma'adim teme'ah* — if the interior is *reddish*, she is *temeiah*, even if the redness does not constitute identifiable blood. *Ve-im lav tehorah* — if not, she is *tehorah*. *Rabbi Akiva*'s standard is thus stricter than *Sumchos* in one direction: he does not require actual blood, only a reddish coloration. He is similarly unmoved by the *pesichat ha-kever* concern, requiring actual examination rather than assumption.

### Binyamin bone test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הַמַּפֶּלֶת חֲתִיכָה, רַבִּי בִּנְיָמִין אוֹמֵר: קוֹרְעָהּ, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ עֶצֶם — אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וּבַחֲתִיכָה לְבָנָה. וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא זוּגָא דְּמִן חַדְיָיב, אֲתָא וְאַיְיתִי מַתְנִיתָא בִּידֵיהּ: הַמַּפֶּלֶת חֲתִיכָה לְבָנָה — קוֹרְעָהּ, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ עֶצֶם — אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita* with regard to a woman **who discharges** an amorphous **piece** of tissue, that **Rabbi Binyamin says** one **tears it** open to examine it. **If it contains a bone,** it is considered a fetus, and **its mother is impure** with the impurity of a woman who gave **birth. Rav Ḥisda says: And** this applies **in** the case of **a white piece** of flesh; only in such a situation does the existence of a bone render it a fetus. **And likewise, when a pair** of Torah scholars **came from Ḥadyab, they came and brought a *baraita* with them:** In the case of a woman **who discharges a white piece** of tissue, one **tears it** open to examine it, and **if it contains a bone, its mother is impure** with the impurity of a woman who gave **birth.**

A third *baraita* presents *Rabbi Binyamin*'s view: *kor'ah* — open it. *Im yesh bah etzem, imo teme'ah leidah* — if there is a bone inside, the mother is *teme'ah leidah*, a *yoledet*. Here the focus shifts entirely. *Rabbi Binyamin* is not asking about blood at all. He is asking about the status of the chunk as a fetus. The presence of a bone — a *etzem* — indicates that this was indeed a fetus, however rudimentary, and thus the *yoledet* framework applies. This is a different inquiry from all the preceding opinions: not "was there blood?" but "was there a fetus?"

### Pappa white chunk

*Rav Pappa* adds an important practical limitation: *davka chatichah levana* — the bone-test procedure of *Rabbi Binyamin* is appropriate specifically when the chunk is white or clear in appearance. A whitish, clear chunk is the type that might plausibly contain a developing fetus. A reddish or blood-soaked chunk is not the type that calls for this analysis; for that, the blood-based inquiry is more appropriate.

### Chadyav baraita

A *baraita* from scholars of *Chadyav* corroborates *Rav Pappa*'s limitation and *Rabbi Binyamin*'s method in combination: *hamapeles chatichah levana* — a woman who discharges a white chunk — *kor'ah* — tear it open — *im yesh bah etzem, imo teme'ah leidah* — and if there is a bone inside, the mother is *teme'ah leidah*.

## Shimon bar Yochai

### Agur blood standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: הַמַּפֶּלֶת חֲתִיכָה — קוֹרְעָהּ, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ דָּם אָגוּר — טְמֵאָה, וְאִם לָאו — טְהוֹרָה, כְּסוֹמְכוֹס, וְקִילָּא מִכּוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** With regard to a woman **who discharges** an amorphous **piece** of tissue, one **tears it** open to examine it. **If it contains** a quantity of **accumulated blood,** the woman **is impure; and if not, she is pure.** The Gemara comments: This is basically **in accordance with** the opinion of **Sumakhos,** that blood found inside the piece of tissue renders the woman impure, **but it is more lenient than all** the previous opinions, i.e., Sumakhos and Rabbi Aḥa, as according to Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai the woman is rendered impure only if there is a quantity of accumulated blood.

The *baraita* concludes with the position of *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*, transmitted by *Rabbi Yochanan*: *hamapeles chatichah, kor'ah* — one opens the chunk — *im yesh bah dam agur teme'ah* — if there is *dam agur*, blood that is collected in significant quantity, she is *teme'ah*. *Ve-im lav tehorah* — if not, she is *tehorah*.

### Most lenient view

*Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*'s position is the most lenient of all those presented. He does not accept the *pesichat ha-kever* presumption that blood automatically accompanied the expulsion. He does not rule *teme'ah* on the basis of any blood found inside; he requires *dam agur* — blood that has pooled and collected in a meaningful amount. A trace of redness or a small amount of blood is insufficient. Only a significant collection triggers *tum'ah*.

### Internal vs external

This *sugya* raises a conceptual question — which the *Gemara* will pursue further — about the distinction between blood found inside a chunk and blood found on its exterior. The *Chachamim* in their baseline ruling attend only to external blood, blood that has made contact with the vaginal canal. *Sumchos* extends concern to internal blood. *Rabbi Akiva* looks at internal redness. *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* requires substantial internal pooling. The conceptual and practical significance of this internal-versus-external distinction, particularly in light of contemporary gynecological *sha'elot*, will be taken up in the following session.
