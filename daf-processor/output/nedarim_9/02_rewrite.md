# Nedarim 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Knidrei Reshaim

### Opening & Mishnah Text

The ninth daf of Nedarim opens with a new mishnah — one that is, from the outset, slightly peculiar. The mishnah introduces a category of vow phrased as *knidrei reshaim*, "like the vows of the wicked," and proceeds to establish whether such a formulation creates a binding *neder*, *nezirut*, or *shvuah*. The broader context is that of *yadot* — the continuation of the discussion about partial or indirect formulations that may or may not create binding vows. The question is always: when someone leaves part of the sentence unspoken, do we know how to fill in the blank, and is that incomplete formulation effective?

The full range of the mishnah covers three categories: the person who says something *knidrei reshaim* may be intending *nezirut*, may be intending an obligation regarding a *korban*, or may be making a *shvuah*. The opening clause — *knidrei reshaim* — is what drives the entire analysis.

### Three Categories

The mishnah's statement is *nadar binazir u-vekorban u-vishvuah*: one who vows regarding *nazir*, *korban*, and *shvuah*. The basic scenario is that someone says *knidrei reshaim hareini* — "like the vows of the wicked, behold I am" — and leaves the end of the sentence incomplete. Depending on context, *hareini* might be followed by *nazir*; or one might say *alai* ("upon me"), suggesting a *korban*; or one might say *heimenu* ("from it"), gesturing toward a *shvuah* not to eat from something. In each case, part of the critical content of the statement is missing.

This is the standard *yad* problem: a *yad mochiach* — a probative indication — suffices, but a *yad she'einah mochiach* — an unclear indication — does not, and the question is always whether there is enough context to determine what was meant.

### Chiddush Analysis

The *chiddush* of the mishnah, however, lies not in the incomplete end of the sentence but in the opening qualifier. The mishnah tells us: *knidrei reshaim* works. *Knidrei kesherim lo amar klum* — if someone says "like the vows of *kesherim*," righteous people, they have said nothing, because *kesherim* do not make *nedarim*. *Knidvosam*, however — if someone says "like the *nedavah* of *kesherim*" — that does work. And by implication, *knidvos reshaim* would not work either.

There is also an item conspicuously absent from the end of the mishnah: *shvuah* does not appear in the closing formulation. The mishnah says *nadar binazir u-vekorban*, but does not add *u-vishvuah*. This asymmetry will require explanation.

What the mishnah is fundamentally communicating, then, is that *reshaim* make *nedarim* and *kesherim* make *nedavos* — and not vice versa. This sets up a crucial discussion about the intrinsic religious value, or disvalue, of making *nedarim* in the first place.

## Neder vs. Nedava

### Ran's Framework

The Ran explains that the word *nedava* — freely given donation — carries the connotation expressed in the verse *v'chol nediv lev asher yidvenu libo*: the generosity of the heart. A *nedava*, then, refers to an act performed out of sincere, uncomplicated religious motivation — a pure desire to serve God or to give something to God. A *neder*, by contrast, reflects a more complex motivational landscape. A person making a *neder* may have religious intentions, but there are other things operating in the background: guilt, shame, an impetuous desire to correct some failing, a wish to appear more *frum*, or simply complicated feelings that have been channeled into a religious act. The religious component is present, but it is not the clean, freely flowing, uncomplicated motivation that a *nedava* represents.

It follows, then, that calling someone who makes a *neder* a *rasha* does not mean they are wicked in all their ways. The Ran qualifies this as *bedavar zeh* — in this particular act. Within the narrow scope of making *nedarim* and *nedavos*, a *neder* reflects a lesser or at least more problematic act.

This distinction, it should be noted, is not a formal legal one. It is not about the mechanics of how you produce the *neder* or *nedava*. It is about describing the religious mindset and motivation of the person making it. Therefore: *nidrei reshaim* is a meaningful phrase — it refers to vows made from complicated motivation. *Nidrei kesherim* is not meaningful — *kesherim* simply do not operate that way. *Nidvos kesherim* is meaningful — that is precisely what a *kasher* person does. And *nidvos reshaim* is not meaningful — the *nedava* of a *rasha* is not a coherent category.

### Korban Analysis

The Ran then maps this distinction onto the technical language of *korban* vows. A classic *neder* of a *korban* is *harei alai* — "it is upon me," creating a personal obligation. A classic *nedava* of a *korban* is *harei zo* — "behold this [animal]," sanctifying the object itself rather than creating an obligation on the person. The Ran suggests that this technical legal distinction reflects the underlying motivational one: *harei alai* shows a certain hesitation — you are not yet giving it, you are promising to give it in the future. *Harei zo* is more wholehearted, because you are giving it over immediately. The act of *neder* thus maps onto the more complex, holding-back motivation, while *nedava* maps onto the fully generous one.

There is, however, a debate between the Ran and Tosafot about what *bekorban* means within the mishnah's context. The Ran understands it as a literal *korban* — a sacrificial offering, with the *neder/nedava* distinction playing out as just described. Tosafot reads *bekorban* as *konam* — the classic formulation of *nidrei issur* — meaning it is a standard prohibition vow, where the object is treated as though it were a *korban* and therefore forbidden. Tosafot's reading fits better with the three classic types of *hafla'ah* under discussion in this tractate: *neder*, *nazir*, and *shvuah* — with the *neder* category being the *konam* prohibition.

### Nazirut Analysis

The Ran then extends this framework to *nezirut*. Technically, there is only one way to become a *nazir* — you say *hareini nazir* — so there is no formal legal parallel to *harei alai* versus *harei zo*. Nevertheless, the Ran explains that someone whose *nezirut* flows from an uncomplicated, purely religious motivation — a true gift of the self to God — has made a *nezirut* of the *nedava* type. Someone whose *nezirut* flows from more complex, troubled, or impetuous motivations has made a *nezirut* of the *neder* type. This is not a legal distinction; it is a characterization of the act's religious character.

## Knidrei Reshaim…

### Shvuah Exclusion

Why does the mishnah omit *shvuah* from its closing list? The basic answer is that *kesherim* never take *shvuos* at all. The Ran states this directly. The underlying concern is the danger of *shem shamayim l'vatalah* — the non-weighty invocation of God's name. And we have already seen earlier in the tractate that someone who hears *shem shamayim l'vatalah* must put the offender in *cherem*.

There is a rich tradition on this point. Rashi on the *pasuk* *es Hashem Elokecha tira v'oso sa'avod u'vo sidbak u'vishmo tishaveia* says that only someone who possesses all of those qualities — fear of God, service, cleaving — is at the appropriate *madreiga* to take a *shvuah b'shem Hashem*. The Ramban there appears to disagree and reads *vishmo tishaveia* as meaning that if one is going to take a *shvuah* it must be in God's name, as opposed to the name of another deity — not that there is ever a positive circumstance for taking it.

Either way, the practical conclusion is clear. Since taking a *shvuah* in God's name is intrinsically fraught — *lo yenakeh Hashem es asher yisa es shemo lashav* — a *kasher* person would simply not do it. Therefore, one cannot invoke the concept of *knidvos kesherim* to validate a *shvuah*, because there is no such thing as a *shvuah* of the *kesherim*. Hence, *shvuah* is absent from the closing clause of the mishnah.

## Gemara Questions

### Shmuel's Three Ways

With the *chiddush* of the opening qualifier established, the Gemara turns to the question of the incomplete end of the sentence. *V'dilma hachi ka'amar* — perhaps what the speaker meant was "like the vows of *reshaim*, I will not vow" — in other words, they were disavowing *nedarim*, not making one. How do we know the speaker intended to become a *nazir*, or to obligate a *korban*, or to make a *shvuah*?

Shmuel answers: *b'omer k'nidrei reshaim hareini alai v'heimenu* — we are dealing with three distinct formulations. *Hareini*, behold I am, points toward *nezirut*. *Alai*, upon me, points toward a *korban* obligation. *Heimenu*, from it, points toward a *shvuah* not to eat. Each of these formulations provides a partial indication of what was meant, which is why the question of *yad* arises.

### Yad Sheeinah

The Gemara immediately presses: for *hareini* specifically, how does it point toward *nezirut*? *Dilma hareini betaanit ka'amar* — perhaps the speaker meant to be in a fast. Or any number of other things. At best this is a *yad she'einah mochiach*, an unclear indication, and Shmuel is on record holding that a *yad she'einah mochiach* is not effective. One might even say it is not a *yad* at all, since *hareini* is so vague that there is no way to determine what was intended.

### Okimta: Nazir Present

Shmuel's response is an *okimta* — a recontextualization: *keshehayah nazir over lefanav* — when there was a *nazir* walking in front of the speaker. In that context, when the speaker said *hareini*, it was obvious that he meant *hareini nazir*. The surrounding circumstance provided the necessary clarity.

This *okimta* reads a great deal into the mishnah — both the triggering word *hareini* and the contextual fact of the passing *nazir* — but the point is that these are not really where the mishnah's weight lies. The mishnah is not primarily interested in how we fill in the end of the sentence. The end of the sentence is assumed to be clear enough by whatever means — a *nazir* walking by, an explicit continuation, or some other contextual indicator. The *chiddush* of the mishnah is entirely concentrated in the opening qualifier.

### Ran vs. Tosafot

This is precisely where the Ran and Tosafot diverge. The Ran holds that the *chiddush* of the mishnah operates even if the end of the sentence is completely explicit. If someone says *knidrei reshaim kikar zeh alai konam* — "like the vows of *reshaim*, this loaf is forbidden to me as a *konam*" — the opening qualifier *knidrei reshaim* makes the vow effective, because that is a meaningful thing for a *rasha* to say. And if someone says *knidrei kesherim kikar zeh alai konam*, the opening qualifier *knidrei kesherim* prevents it from taking effect, because *kesherim* do not make *nedarim*, and therefore that qualifier signals that the vow is not seriously intended.

Tosafot disagrees. According to Tosafot, the qualifier at the beginning matters only because the end of the sentence is somewhat ambiguous — there is an element of *yad* in play. Given that ambiguity, if the opening qualifier is one that would not normally "work" — like *knidrei kesherim* — we do not resolve the ambiguity in favor of effectiveness. But if the end of the sentence were completely explicit, the opening qualifier would be irrelevant: *knidrei kesherim kikar zeh alai konam* would in that case be binding, because the commitment is unambiguous.

The Ran's reading, in a sense, is simpler. There was a *nazir* walking by; the speaker said *hareini*; we know what was meant. The entire force of the mishnah lies in the opening clause, regardless. One might ask why anyone would ever say *knidrei reshaim* when trying to create an effective vow — and this is genuinely curious. It may be that the mishnah is using this formulation to express a certain displeasure with the whole phenomenon of *nedarim* and *nezirut*, communicating that disapproval through the very *halacha* it is teaching. The phrase *knidrei kesherim*, for its part, has come down in popular usage precisely as that sort of hedge — an acknowledgment, very much like the contemporary *bli neder*, that the speaker is not fully committing.

### Raba: Hemenu

Turning to the third formulation — *heimenu*, "from it" — the Gemara asks: *dilma heimenu d'achila ka'amar*, perhaps the speaker meant "I will eat from it," not "I will not eat from it." There is simply no *shvuah* implied at all. Raba clarifies: the case is where the speaker said *heimenu shelo ochal* — "from it I will not eat." That makes the content explicit. The Gemara then asks: if so, what is the *chiddush*? The speaker said everything! The answer is: *kamah shmalan* — the speaker did not say the word *shvuah*. The *chiddush* is that even without explicitly pronouncing the word *shvuah*, saying "I will not eat from it" functions as a *yad lishvuah* and constitutes a binding *shvuah*. The significance of the end of the sentence, in other words, is also partly that it is incomplete — the keyword is absent — and nevertheless it is effective.

So the Gemara recognizes two layers of *chiddush* in the mishnah: the opening qualifier determines whether the statement is the kind of thing that would or would not take effect, and the remainder of the statement demonstrates that a *yad* without the explicit keyword is sufficient.

## Neder Values Debate

### Rabbi Meir's View

The Gemara now pivots to a broader question: who is the *tanna* of this mishnah that distinguishes between *neder* and *nedava*? The Gemara suggests it fits neither Rabbi Meir nor Rabbi Yehudah — and uses this as an entry point to discuss a fundamental *machlokes* about the religious value of making *nedarim*. *D'tanya*: the verse says *tov asher lo tidor mishetiddor v'lo tishalem* — better not to vow than to vow and not fulfill. *Tov mizeh umizeh she'eino noder kol ikar divrei Rabbi Meir*. Rabbi Meir says: best of all is simply not to vow at all.

### Risk & Non-Fulfillment

Rabbi Meir's position can be understood on two levels. The more pragmatic reading is that making a *neder* is inherently risky: you have now created a personal obligation, and if you fail to fulfill it — *ki tidor neder l'Hashem Elokecha lo te'acher l'shalmo* — you have transgressed. People may feel that the very act of raising their hand at a communal appeal has intrinsic religious value, but if they do not follow through, it would have been far better not to have made the commitment at all. The risk of non-fulfillment is simply too great.

But there is a deeper theological reading as well. One's *avodat Hashem* should consist of what God demands of a person. The obligations are divinely imposed — heteronomous, in the philosophical sense. When a person takes it upon themselves to create new religious obligations, they are in a sense serving their own religious impulses rather than the divine will. That is not true *avodat Hashem*. This may be the underlying concern: *kol hanoder k'ilu banah bamah v'chol hamkaymo k'ilu hikriv aleha korban* — every one who vows is as though he built a *bamah*, and one who fulfills it is as though he brought a *korban* upon it. A *bamah* may be a religious act, but unauthorized, individual religious construction is not self-evidently a positive phenomenon.

### Rabbi Yehudah View

Rabbi Yehudah takes a different position: *tov mizeh umizeh noder umshalem* — best of all is to vow and fulfill it. Rabbi Yehudah appears to regard the making of a *neder* as a genuine religious act, provided one sees it through. The vow, on this view, is not inherently problematic; it is the failure to fulfill it that is the danger.

### Mishnah & Meir

Returning to our mishnah: the mishnah seems to distinguish between *neder* (associated with *reshaim*) and *nedava* (associated with *kesherim*). This fits uncomfortably with Rabbi Yehudah, who seems to regard *nedarim* positively. But it also seems uncomfortable with Rabbi Meir — because Rabbi Meir, who says it is better not to vow at all, apparently allows for the positive phenomenon of a *nedava*, whereas the mishnah seems to treat *nedavos* as the clearly superior category.

### Meir Consistency

The Gemara resolves this: *afilu teima Rabbi Meir* — it can still be Rabbi Meir. *Ki ka'amar Rabbi Meir b'neder, b'nedava lo ka'amar* — when Rabbi Meir objects to vowing, he objects to the *neder*, not to the *nedava*. A *nedava* he does not include in his critique. This distinction has conceptual grounding beyond the risk of non-fulfillment: a *neder* creates a personal religious obligation that the person has imposed upon himself — something other than divinely mandated duty. A *nedava* merely sanctifies an object; it does not generate a personal obligation in the same way. One can thus argue that the concern about self-generated religious heteronomy applies to *neder* but not to *nedava*.

But the Gemara presses: a *nedava* also carries risks. *Dilma ati lidei takala* — what if the *nedava* also leads to stumbling? If you sanctify an animal and it wanders around your flock for months, you might come to misuse it, violating *meila*.

## Hillel Practice

### Hillel's Method

The Gemara answers with a historical precedent: *k'Hillel HaZaken* — like the practice of Hillel the Elder. *Amru alav al Hillel HaZaken shelo ma'al adam b'olato kol yamav* — they said of Hillel that no one ever committed *meila* with his *olah* throughout his lifetime. How did he accomplish this? *Meivi keshehei chulin la'azara, umakdisha, v'someich aleha, v'shochta* — he would bring his animal unsanctified to the outer precincts of the *azara*, sanctify it there and then, perform *semichah*, and immediately bring it for *shechita*. There was no window of time between sanctification and slaughter during which anything could go wrong.

### Nedava Safety

One might ask: why not do the same with a *neder*? Why not walk to the *beis hamikdash*, make a *neder* right at the entrance, and immediately bring the *korban*? The answer is that a *neder* creates a personal obligation — that step is simply unnecessary. Since you are going to be there and sanctify the animal anyway, why first take a *neder* that creates an obligation on your person? You can go straight to the *nedava*, sanctify the animal on the spot, and bring it immediately. The *nedava* allows a person to give a *korban* in a manner that involves no personal obligation, no window for *meila*, and no possibility of non-fulfillment. That is the paradigm of a *nedavat kesherim* — a *nedava* of the righteous.

### Tosafot Solution

This raises a question raised by both Tosafot and the Ran: does this mean that the mishnah's phrase *knidvat kesherim* can only take effect if the person is standing at the entrance to the *beis hamikdash*? That seems forced. Tosafot resolves this cleanly: the phrase *knidvat kesherim* works because the concept of a *nedavat kesherim* — a *nedava* in a fully unproblematic mode — genuinely exists. There is a real phenomenon that fits that description, even if it requires a particular set of circumstances. Because such a phenomenon exists, the phrase is meaningful and the vow takes effect. By contrast, there is no such thing as a *nidrei kesherim* — righteous people do not make *nedarim* — and therefore that phrase is not meaningful and the vow does not take effect. The mishnah is not requiring that one literally be at the gates of the *beis hamikdash*; it is simply invoking a concept that has a genuine referent.

## Reality of Nedava

### Nazir Nedava Q

Having established that *nedavat kesherim* is a real and achievable phenomenon when it comes to *korbanos*, the Gemara turns to *nezirut*: *nedava d'nezirus mai ika l'meimar?* Is there truly such a thing as an uncomplicated, purely motivated *nedavah*-type *nezirut*? The question is pressing precisely because the Gemara elsewhere associates *nezirut* with the expression *asher chata al hanefesh* — the *nazir* is said to have "sinned against the soul" — implying that something inherently problematic attaches to the *nazir*'s situation. Can *nezirut* ever truly be a pure religious gift?

The Gemara answers: *savar lah k'Shimon HaTzadik* — this view follows that of Shimon HaTzadik.

### Shimon HaTzadik

A *braisa* is introduced: *amar Shimon HaTzadik, miyamai lo achalti asham Nazir tamei* — Shimon HaTzadik said, "In all my days I never ate from the *asham* of an impure *nazir*." The *asham* of a *nazir tamei* is an offering brought when a *nazir* has become *tamei* and must begin the *nezirut* again. Shimon HaTzadik, who was the *kohen gadol* and entitled to eat from such offerings, consistently refused to do so in the case of a *nazir tamei*. Only one time did he consent to eat, and that occasion he describes in detail.

### Beautiful Nazir Story

*Ba adam echad Nazir min hadarom* — once a man came from the south, a *nazir*, and Shimon HaTzadik observed that he had beautiful eyes, was strikingly handsome, and had his hair arranged in flowing locks. Shimon asked him: what led you to accept upon yourself *nezirut* and thereby commit to shaving off this beautiful hair? The man replied: I was a shepherd for my father in my village. I went to draw water from the spring, and I saw my reflection. Suddenly my *yeitzer hara* seized me — *v'pachaz alai yitzri* — and it sought to take me away from this world, to lead me toward a life dominated by physical indulgence and vanity.

In response to his *yeitzer hara*, he said to himself: *lamah atah mitga'eh b'olam she'eino shelcha* — why do you glorify yourself in a world that is not yours? *B'mi she'asid lihayos rimah v'tole'ah* — in this body that will ultimately become food for worms. *She'agalechacha l'shamayim* — I will shave you for the sake of Heaven. Immediately he arose, kissed the young man on the head, and said: *bni, kamochah yirbu nozrei nezirus b'Yisrael* — my son, may those who take *nezirut* in Israel multiply like you. Regarding such a person the verse speaks: *ish ki yaifli lindor neder Nazir l'hazir l'Hashem* — the emphasis falls on *l'Hashem*, for God's sake.

## Nazir Motivation

### Tamei Nazir Issue

The Gemara now interrogates Shimon HaTzadik's practice more carefully. *Matkif leh Rav Mani: mai shna asham nazir tamei de-lo achal?* — Rav Mani presses: what is the particular reason Shimon HaTzadik refused to eat the *asham* of a *nazir tamei*? The stated rationale would seem to be that the offering comes as a result of sin — the *nazir* became *tamei*. But then *kol asham nami lo leachul* — he should refuse to eat any *asham* offering at all, since every *asham* comes to atone for some sin!

### Rav Yonah Regret

Rav Yonah responds: *hainu ta'ama* — this is the real reason. *Kishe'hen tohin, nozrin* — when people are in a state of being troubled or agitated about their own moral condition, filled with guilt and a desire to correct themselves, they impulsively accept *nezirut*. *Ukshe'hen mitamein, v'rabbim aleihem yemei nezirot, mitchartin bahen* — but when they then become *tamei* and the period of *nezirut* extends far longer than they had anticipated, they regret it. They wish they had never begun. *Venimtze'u mevi'in chullin la'azara* — and it turns out, in a sense, as though they are bringing *chullin* — non-sacred items — into the *azara*.

### Motivation Types

The insight here is significant. The *nazir tamei* reveals a person whose original motivation was impetuous rather than deeply rooted. The guilt or shame that drove them to accept *nezirut* was real, but it was not the product of a profound, settled conviction. When the *nezirut* becomes difficult — when it extends due to *tumah* — the depth of that commitment is exposed. They never had the endurance for it, because the conviction was never truly deep.

Shimon HaTzadik, seeing this dynamic, would not eat the *asham* of a *nazir tamei*, because such a person's *nezirut* was almost certainly of the *neder* type — complex, guilt-driven, impetuous — not the *nedava* type. The *nazir tahor*, whose period ended without interruption, remains at least formally in the category of someone who may have had genuine conviction. Perhaps the conviction was complicated, but there was no opportunity for regret to manifest. The *nazir tamei*, by contrast, had that opportunity, and the regret demonstrated what the conviction had really been.

The Gemara pushes: *i hachi afilu nazir tahor nami* — if the logic is impetuous motivation leading to eventual regret, should we not worry about even a *nazir tahor*? The answer is: *lo, de-amudei amei nafshia de-achul lemidor* — no, because the *nazir tahor* had assessed that he could endure the standard thirty days, and he did. The absence of demonstrated regret allows us to take his *nezirut* at face value, even if we remain somewhat skeptical. The *nazir tamei*'s regret, by contrast, has been publicly demonstrated.

## Yehudah Nedava

### Yehudah Reread

Having established all of this for Rabbi Meir's position, the Gemara now constructs a parallel reading for Rabbi Yehudah. *Afilu teima Rabbi Yehuda* — even according to Rabbi Yehudah, the mishnah can be explained consistently. *Ki amar Rabbi Yehuda* when he says it is best to vow and fulfill — *b'nedavah*, he really meant *nedavah*. His objection was only to *neder*, not to *nedavah*. The Gemara then reads back Rabbi Yehudah's statement in the *braisa* — *tov mizeh umizeh noder umekayeim* — and says we should actually read it as *tani nodev umekayeim* — he was speaking of *nedava*, not *neder*. This is explicitly not the plain sense of Rabbi Yehudah's words, but the Gemara is working to show that the mishnah can fit Rabbi Yehudah's view on this reinterpretation.

The Gemara then replicates the earlier analysis: *mai shna noder d'lo — dilma ati lidei takala*, same concern about stumbling. *Nedava nami dilma ati lidei takala* — so why is *nedava* any better? And here, unlike the case of Rabbi Meir where we needed to bring proofs from Hillel HaZaken, we have Rabbi Yehudah's own statements. *Rabbi Yehuda l'taameih — adam meivi kivshato l'azara, umakdisha, v'someich aleha v'shochta* — Rabbi Yehudah himself describes the practice of bringing one's animal unsanctified to the *azara*, sanctifying it there, performing *semichah*, and bringing it for *shechita* immediately. This is exactly what we said of Hillel, now attributed directly to Rabbi Yehudah's own teaching. And so the same analysis follows: *neder* is problematic because it creates a personal obligation at risk of non-fulfillment; *nedava* performed in this manner is entirely safe.

### Righteous Nazir

But the same question reemerges: *teinach nedava d'korbanos* — fine for *korbanos*, what about *nezirut*? Here Rabbi Yehudah also speaks from his own tradition. *Rabbi Yehuda omer: chasidim harishonim hayu mis'avim l'havi korban chatas* — the great righteous ones of earlier generations longed to bring a *korban chatas*, but they faced a fundamental obstacle: *lefi she'ein HaKadosh Baruch Hu meivi takala al yadeihem* — God would never bring them to unintentional sin, because that is the only circumstance under which a *chatas* may be brought. They could not sin intentionally — that would disqualify the offering — and God would not allow them to sin inadvertently. So how could they bring a *chatas*?

*Omdim umisnadbim nezirus l'Makom* — they would accept *nezirut* upon themselves, not for its own sake, but as a vehicle: *k'dei sheyischayeiv korban chatas l'Makom* — so that they would incur the obligation of a *korban chatas* for God. The *chatas* of a *nazir* who becomes *tamei* is the mechanism. They accepted *nezirut* knowing that they might become *tamei* and thereby be required to bring a *chatas*, which is what they ultimately sought to offer. This, according to Rabbi Yehudah, is the example of a genuinely righteous, purely motivated *nezirut* — a *nedavat nezirut* in the fullest sense.

## Shimon Objects

### Shimon Objection

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *lo nadru b'nazir* — they would not have done this. They would not have accepted *nezirut* upon themselves even to obtain the ability to bring a *chatas*.

### Alternatives

Instead, Rabbi Shimon says, those who wished to bring *korbanos* freely would do so through other means. One who wanted to bring an *olah* would make a *nedava* of an *olah* and bring it. One who wished to bring *shlamim* would make a *nedava* of *shlamim*. One who wished to bring a *todah* with its four kinds of bread could do so as a *nedavah* as well. All of these could be freely offered as *nedavot* without *nezirut* being involved at all.

### Sinner Status

As for *nezirut*, *aval binezirus lo hisnadvu* — they would not accept *nezirut* even for this purpose. The reason: *k'dei shelo yikar'u chotim* — so that they not be called sinners. For even a *nazir* who has committed no wrong is described in the Torah's language as one who has *asher chata al hanefesh* — who sinned against the soul. The verse speaks in the context of a *nazir tamei*, and the Ran and others note that this refers specifically to that case, but Rabbi Shimon learns from it a broader principle about the nature of *nezirut* itself.

### Denial Problem

The *asher chata al hanefesh* points to the ascetic dimension of *nezirut*. A *nazir* separates himself from wine and certain aspects of ordinary life. He marks himself visibly as different from the rest of society. Rabbi Shimon sees this self-denial as intrinsically a form of sin — against the soul, against the self. *Nezirut* does not merely risk impure motivation from the outside; it contains within itself an element that is religiously problematic, regardless of motivation.

## Asceticism Debate

### Kadosh vs. Choteh

Rabbi Shimon's objection opens onto a profound theological question: is the ascetic a *kadosh* or a *choteh*? Is the person who sits in a *taanis* — a fast — holy, or a sinner? This is not merely a question of motivation, though motivation is certainly at issue. It is a question about the nature of religious life itself. Is the model of the religious ideal one who withdraws from the physical world, denies bodily pleasures, and focuses entirely on the spiritual? Or is that very withdrawal itself a kind of failure — a rejection of the world that God created and declared good?

### Embodied Religion

Judaism has, by and large, been understood as an embodied religion — one in which the physical world is not the enemy of the spirit but the medium through which God is served. We are not at war with the physical world. The pleasures of *Shabbos*, the joy of the *yom tov*, the *mitzvos* that engage the body and the senses — all of these reflect a fundamentally different orientation than that of a religion that sees the soul as imprisoned in the body, longing to escape. Yet the tension has always existed. Figures of *prishus* — separation from worldly pleasure — appear throughout the tradition, and the question of when separation is *kedushah* and when it is a betrayal of the world God made is genuinely unresolved.

### Rambam Critique

The Rambam addresses this tension directly in his *Peirush Mishnayos*. He criticizes at length the approach of the *Chasidei Ashkenaz* — the medieval German pietists — who practiced extensive self-denial and asceticism. The Rambam argues that this entire orientation was absorbed from Christian religious culture and has no authentic Jewish foundation. *Lo dayecha mah she'asra lacha Torah* — the prohibitions of the Torah are sufficient; one need not add to them by denying oneself what the Torah permits. To do so is not *kedushah* but a kind of unhealthy religious distortion.

Yet even acknowledging the Rambam's sharp critique, the tension remains. The debate between Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Shimon about whether the *chasidim harishonim* would have accepted *nezirut* in order to bring a *chatas* is one expression of that tension. Is *nezirut* a form of *nedavah* — a gift of the self — or is it always, by definition, a *chatas al hanefesh*, a sin against the soul? The question of whether a *nazir* is *kadosh* or *choteh*, and whether taking a *taanis* is holy or sinful, maps directly onto this larger question about the place of asceticism within Jewish religious life — a question that the Gemara introduces here and does not fully resolve.
