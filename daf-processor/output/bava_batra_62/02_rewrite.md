# Bava Batra 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Long & Short Boundary

### Rav: Short Boundary

The daf opens in the context of a broader discussion about boundary language in land sales — specifically the principle of *metzarim hirchiv lo*, whereby a seller may have drawn wider boundaries than the plot he actually intended to convey, perhaps because the most convenient landmarks happened to extend beyond the property in question. Against this background, the Gemara now introduces several scenarios involving irregular or incomplete boundary descriptions and asks what is actually acquired in each case.

The first case concerns a field whose boundaries are described unevenly: *metzar u-metzar echad aroch u-metzar echad katzar* — one boundary drawn long and one boundary drawn short. As pictured in the Rashbam, imagine that the northern and southern edges of the field are fully drawn, but the eastern boundary extends only halfway across the width while the western boundary spans the full width. The question becomes: what shape of plot has the seller actually conveyed?

Rav rules *lo kana ela keneged ha-katzar* — the buyer acquires only as much as corresponds to the shorter boundary. Since the eastern line ran only halfway across, that half-width represents the true extent of the plot. The western boundary, though drawn the full width, should not be read as extending the conveyance; perhaps the seller mentioned a neighbor's plot on the west — a neighbor whose land happened to span the full width — simply as a convenient landmark. This is *metzarim hirchiv lo* applied to the shorter boundary as the controlling measure. Rav takes the more restrictive interpretation.

### Kahana & Assi: Rosh Tor

Rav Kahana and Rav Assi challenged Rav's position: *veyikne keneged rosh tor* — why not connect the endpoints of both boundaries and include everything in between? By drawing a line from the terminus of the shorter eastern boundary to the far end of the wider western boundary, the result is a trapezoid — described by the Gemara as a *rosh tor*, the head of an ox, an angular, tapered shape. This reading honors both boundary lines as meaningful markers of the plot's extent. It is a reasonable interpretation, and one that tries to give maximum effect to both the long and the short boundary.

Rav was silent — *shatik Rav*. The question of what that silence means is worth pausing on. Does it indicate concession, or merely that Rav declined to press the debate further? The subsequent line suggests that Rav had not actually conceded, since the Gemara goes on to specify a separate case in which he *would* concede — implying that without that special circumstance, he maintained his original position.

### Rav's Concession

There is, however, one configuration in which Rav concedes. If the eastern side is shared between two neighbors — Reuven occupying one half and Shimon the other half — and on the western side the boundary is shared between Levi and Yehuda, and the document listed both Levi and Yehuda as the western boundary, then Rav would agree that the full width on the west is intended. The argument: *mide-hava leh le-michatav leh de-Reuven keneged Levi* — if the seller truly only meant to convey the portion opposite Reuven on the east, which aligns with Levi on the west, he should have written simply "Reuven on the east and Levi on the west." By mentioning Yehuda as well, the seller has added something beyond what was necessary. That surplus language is telling: it demonstrates that the full western width — both Levi's portion and Yehuda's portion — was intentionally included. From this we infer, *shma mina kineged rosh tor hu d'amar lei*, that the acquisition is in fact the trapezoidal *rosh tor* shape, connecting both endpoints. That case Rav concedes.

## Perpendicular Boundaries

### Specifying Both Sides

The Gemara shifts to a different scenario. Suppose a seller identifies two different neighbors as the anchoring boundaries: Reuven's land sits to both the east and west, while Shimon's land sits to both the north and south. The Gemara rules that one must not simply say "bounded by Reuven on one side and Shimon on the other." Rather, *tzarich lemichtav lei metzar Reuven ruchin trein u-metzar Shimon ruchin trein* — the document must specify that Reuven's land forms two of the boundaries and Shimon's land forms two of the boundaries. In other words, the language must make explicit that both the east and the west belong to Reuven, and that both the north and south belong to Shimon.

### Ambiguity Risk

Why is this precision necessary? Because without it, the description is dangerously ambiguous. If one merely writes that the field is "between Reuven and Shimon," a diagonal strip running from one corner of Reuven's plot to the opposite corner of Shimon's plot technically satisfies that description. Such a strip would touch the corners of both neighbors' holdings and would be entirely contained within them. One might then ask: why not interpret it as a small square inscribed within the larger field, also entirely contained within Reuven's and Shimon's boundaries? The answer is that the diagonal strip, unlike the inscribed square, actually reaches the edges of both neighbors' plots at its corners — making it a defensible reading of "between" them. The inscribed square would be fully contained but would not abut their borders at any point. To eliminate this ambiguity and make clear that a full rectangular plot is intended — bounded on two sides by Reuven and two sides by Shimon — both pairs of boundaries must be explicitly named.

## Kranot & Gamma Marks

### Corner Points Sufficiency

A series of unresolved questions follows. The first: *tzimam lo ha-kranot mahu* — if, instead of specifying entire boundary lines, the seller simply defined the four corner points of the field, is that sufficient to establish acquisition of the full square? One might think so, since four corner points logically define a quadrilateral. But the Gemara entertains the possibility that even this is ambiguous: the four defined corners could equally describe an X-shaped figure, where only the corners themselves are transferred, not the full interior square. The question is left open.

### Gamma Markings

The second question is *kemin gam mahu*: what if, at each of two diagonal corners, the seller drew a small mark indicating a bit of two adjacent boundary lines — like a Greek gamma or the letter *lamed*, gesturing toward both sides of that corner? Does this partial marking of each side convey enough information that the reader should extrapolate the full square? Or might even such marks be interpreted as defining the corners of a smaller, irregular figure — a trapezoid, perhaps — that happens to have those marked segments as its edges? Once again the Gemara does not resolve this question.

## Alternating Boundaries

### Partial Edges Question

The final unresolved question in this series concerns the case of *beserugin* — alternating boundaries. Imagine the field is surrounded by eight neighbors: Reuven and Shimon to the east, Levi and Yehuda to the north, Zevulun and Yissachar to the west, and Yosef and Binyamin to the south. The seller specifies, alternating around the field, one neighbor from each side — say Reuven to the east, Levi to the north, Yissachar to the west, and Yosef to the south. Each of the four sides is partially identified, but only one half of each side is named. The question is whether specifying a portion of each side is sufficient to establish that the full square is being conveyed, or whether one should instead try to construct the smallest possible figure consistent with those partial boundary references.

All three of these questions — *kranot*, *kemin gam*, and *beserugin* — remain unresolved. *Teiku.*

## Omitted 4th Boundary

### Rav: Except Final Furrow

The Gemara now moves to a case that at first glance appears simple: a seller drew three of the four boundaries — say the northern, eastern, and southern — but did not draw the western boundary at all. Three full sides are defined. What is acquired?

Rav rules *kana hakol chutz mi-metzar revi'i* — the buyer acquires everything except that last boundary. More precisely, since the fourth side was never drawn, the final furrow's width at that western edge is excluded. The buyer gets essentially the full square minus one furrow's depth along the missing fourth side. This is a notable nuance: Rav here takes a position that at first seems more expansive than his earlier ruling in the *rosh tor* case, but may reflect a situation where Rav had already reconsidered his earlier position, or where the difference lies in the structure of what is missing.

### Shmuel: All Included

Shmuel disagrees in the opposite direction: *afilu metzar revi'i* — even the fourth boundary, including that final furrow, is acquired. In Shmuel's view, if three sides are fully specified, no reasonable reading of the document would exclude the last edge. The full square is conveyed.

### Rav Assi: One Furrow

Rav Assi takes the most restrictive position of all: *lo kana ela telem echad al pnei kula* — the buyer acquires nothing more than a single furrow running along each of the three specified boundaries. This is extraordinarily limiting. The logic, as the Gemara explains, combines two principles: Rav Assi holds that the failure to draw the fourth boundary should be read like Rav's principle of *shura shura* — that something has been held back — and then extends this further with the rule *mid-shayar b'metzar shayar nami b'kulah*: if something is withheld at the boundary, one should assume a great deal is withheld throughout. The result is a near-total contraction: the buyer receives only the outermost furrow of each of the three sides, nothing more.

### Rava: Conditional Exclus…

Rava rules as the *halakha*: *kana hakol chutz mi-metzar revi'i* — in line with Rav, the buyer receives everything except the fourth boundary. But Rava immediately introduces a series of conditions that significantly complicate what "except the fourth boundary" means.

### Mivla'ut & Independence

The critical concept is *mivla'ut* — absorption. If the fourth boundary is *mivla*, meaning it is geometrically absorbed within the three boundaries already drawn — in the illustration in the Rashbam, the fourth side's extent is already partially enclosed by the edges of the other three — then the buyer acquires even the fourth boundary. The absorption itself is the decisive factor: when that undescribed edge is caught within the figure you drew, it comes along.

But when the fourth boundary is *lo mivla* — it stands fully outside the three drawn lines, as in the left-hand diagram in the Rashbam — then in principle it is excluded. However, even this exclusion is not absolute. The fourth boundary is only excluded if it also possesses independent significance: either it has a planting of palm trees upon it (*richva de-dikla*) or it is a sufficiently large area, at least nine *kavin* (*tisha kavin*). If the fourth boundary is outside the three lines but has neither of these characteristics — no planting and not large enough — then it is still included.

### Rava Versions Compared

The Gemara presents a second version of Rava's ruling that inverts the structure in a telling way. In this second version, Rava rules *kana hakol v'afilu metzar revi'i* — everything is acquired, including the fourth boundary — and then specifies the conditions under which the fourth boundary is included. It is included *ella de-mivla*: only when it is absorbed. If it is not absorbed, *lo kanei* — it is not acquired at all. The nuance within the absorbed case: even when absorbed, the fourth boundary is acquired *only* if it lacks both *richva de-dikla* and the area of *tisha kavin*. If it is absorbed but has trees planted or is a large enough area, it is not acquired.

The two versions of Rava thus differ in an important structural way. In the first version, the primary case is exclusion of the fourth boundary, with absorption and lack of significance serving as grounds to *include* it. In the second version, the primary case is inclusion of the fourth boundary, with non-absorption serving as grounds to *exclude* it, and within the absorbed case, significance (*richva de-dikla* or *tisha kavin*) serving as a further basis for exclusion.

### Consensus & Dispute

The Gemara maps out which outcomes are agreed upon by both versions of Rava and which are disputed. Both versions agree on two extreme cases. First, when the fourth boundary is absorbed and is also small and insignificant — lacking trees and below *tisha kavin* — both agree it is acquired. Second, when it is not absorbed and has both markers of significance — it is outside the drawn lines and has *richva de-dikla* or *tisha kavin* — both versions agree it is not acquired.

The dispute concerns the middle cases: when the fourth boundary is absorbed but possesses markers of significance, or when it is not absorbed but is also small and without plantings. In those mixed cases, the two readings of Rava's position diverge, one treating the boundary as included and the other as excluded. The *halakha* in such cases is *shuda dedaynei* — it is left to the discretion of the judges, who must assess what the seller most plausibly intended. The Rashbam's explanation of this ruling is unusually long, as is the *Tosafot*, reflecting the complexity of the analysis. Notably, the Gemara does not resort to the ordinary default rules — neither *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* nor *kol dalim gavar* — but assigns the determination to judicial judgment about the seller's intent.

## Partnership Language

### Half vs. Half Of

The Gemara now turns to a distinct but related question about the precision of language in the sale of partnership land. Raba identifies a critical linguistic distinction. When Reuven and Shimon are partners in a field and Reuven wishes to sell his interest to Levi, there are two ways he might phrase the transaction. If Reuven says *palga de'is li be'ara* — "the half that I have in this land" — he has sold his entire half-interest. That is, he conveyed the half of the field that he owns. But if Reuven says *palga be'ara de'is li* — "half of the land that I have" — he has sold only half of his share, which is a quarter of the whole field, *riva*. The distinction is subtle: in the first formulation, Reuven identifies his *palga* — the half that is his — as the object of the sale. In the second formulation, he sells *palga* of what he has, which itself is already only a half.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenged Raba directly: *mai shna hai u-mai shna hai* — what is really the difference? Abaye's view was that people do not speak with such precision, and in both phrasings Reuven means to convey his half-share. Abaye further reported that when Raba fell silent in response, he assumed that silence constituted concession: *ana savri mide'ishtik kibulei kibla* — I thought that since Raba was silent, he had accepted my argument. But Abaye was mistaken.

### Documentary Evidence

*Velo hi* — it was not so. The evidence came from Raba's own *beit din*. Documents processed there (*shtarei dinfaku mibei Mar*) demonstrated that Raba consistently maintained the distinction in practice. A document reading *palga de'is li be'ara* was adjudicated as conveying the full half-interest; a document reading *palga be'ara de'is li* was adjudicated as conveying only a quarter. Raba's silence was not concession — he simply did not feel it necessary to press the argument further. The documents from his court prove he never abandoned the distinction.

## Portion in Partnership

### My Half vs. My Portion

Raba extends his analysis to a related scenario, this time involving the description of the boundary in a sale where the seller is retaining a portion. Reuven is selling a plot to Shimon and has drawn the northern, eastern, and western boundaries. The southern boundary, however, he is keeping for himself. If Reuven says *metzar ara demina palga* — the boundary on the south is from the half that is mine — then the word *palga* is interpreted precisely: Reuven is retaining a full half of the land for himself. But if Reuven says *metzar ara demina psika* — the boundary on the south is from a portion that is mine — then he is retaining only a small sliver, specifically *tisha kavin*, the minimum area of independent significance we have seen throughout this discussion.

### Abaye Disputes Again

Again, Abaye objects: *mai shena hachi u-mai shena hachi* — what is the difference? Both formulations, he argues, should be understood as meaning a half, or alternatively both as meaning only a portion. Raba did not respond — *ishtik*. And again, Abaye believed the silence to be concession: *savri minei adei ve-adei palga* — he thought Raba meant that in both formulations, the retained piece is a full half.

### Boundaries Language Role

But this too was a misreading of Raba's silence. Rav Yeimar bar Shlimya clarified in Abaye's own name what the true position was: *bein metzar ara demina palga bein metzar ara demina psika* — whether Reuven said the boundary is from the half that is mine or from the portion that is mine — *ei amar lei ilin metzrani nahu palga*: if Reuven added the phrase "and these are the boundaries," then in either case he retains a full half. But *lo amar lei ilin metzrani nahu tisha kavin*: if he did not add that phrase, then in either case he retains only *tisha kavin*.

The Rashbam explains this additional language as *yeter lashon liyafos kocho* — an extra formulation intended to strengthen and clarify the force of the conveyance. When the seller says "and these are the boundaries," he signals that the boundary line he is describing is a fully defined border, and whether he uses the word "half" or "portion" in that context, he means a genuine half. Without that reinforcing phrase, neither the word "half" nor "portion" alone is sufficient to convey a full half-share; the retained piece is interpreted as no more than the minimum significant area of *tisha kavin*. The distinction is subtle in conceptual terms, but the practical consequence — whether one retains a half or merely a small strip — is significant, and the Gemara takes pains to establish which language achieves which result.
