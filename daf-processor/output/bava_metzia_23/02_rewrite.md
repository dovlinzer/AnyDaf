# Bava Metzia 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ye'ush Debate & Metzia

### Ye'ush Consciousness

We pick up in the middle of 22b, having just concluded the debate between Abaye and Rava. The Gemara rules like Abaye — and it must be said that if anything, the weight of the textual evidence seemed to favor Rava — establishing that *ye'ush shelo mida'at lo havei ye'ush*: the owner's implicit despair, had he known of the loss, does not constitute legally operative *ye'ush*. According to Rava, *ye'ush shelo mida'at havei ye'ush*: since all of the indications support the conclusion that one may keep a *metzia* lacking a *siman*, the circumstances themselves suffice. But the Gemara chose to rule like Abaye, requiring that *ye'ush* be conscious and deliberate. The owner must actually become aware that he has lost the object and must actively despair of its return. Furthermore, as the Gemara's proofs suggest, this principle extends beyond *ye'ush* alone to other areas of *halacha* where a person's frame of mind is legally relevant.

### Dates from Trees

With this ruling in place, the Gemara poses an immediate practical problem. Rav Aha brei deRava said to Rav Ashi: now that Rava has been definitively contradicted and we rule like Abaye, what of *hani timre de-zikka* — dates blown off palm trees by the wind and found by the roadside? The Gemara had earlier distinguished figs, which owners anticipate will fall and have already despaired of, from other fruits, which the owner does not necessarily anticipate losing. If an owner of dates does not know his dates have fallen, and may not have anticipated it, how can we eat them?

Rav Ashi replied: *keivan de-ika shkatzim u'remasim deka achli lehu, me-ikara ye'ush miya'esh minaihu* — since there are insects and rodents on the ground that will eat or ruin the fallen fruit, the owner despairs from the very outset. This answer bothers Rashi somewhat, because the earlier Gemara seemed to restrict anticipatory *ye'ush* to figs. What the answer must implicitly add is that the owner is aware that some dates will fall from the tree — not necessarily which dates, but some — and further, that whatever falls will be rendered disgusting and not worth recovering. There are thus two components to any valid anticipatory *ye'ush*: that the owner could foresee the loss occurring, and that he would have no realistic expectation of recovering the object even if he learned of it. This case answers the second question — the dates will be fouled — while the first requires the additional assumption that date-fall, at some level, is anticipated.

### Policy & Liberties

One might ask what ultimately drove the Gemara to rule like Abaye when the evidence seemed to cut the other way. The honest answer is that it is not entirely clear from the text itself. But conceptually, Abaye's position reflects an important policy principle. Tosafot, in the preceding discussion, extended the logic to a familiar scenario: even if you are confident your friend would not mind your helping yourself to their property, you cannot act on that assumption without explicit permission. Their hypothetical consent does not constitute actual permission. The fact that they *would* be *meyaiesh* were they to find out does not mean they *are* presently *meyaiesh*, and therefore it is still *shelo mida'at*. This is a significant ruling about not overstepping boundaries to which one is not entitled.

At the same time, ruling like Abaye makes *halacha* practically difficult. How is a finder ever supposed to determine whether the owner is aware of the loss, or could have anticipated it from the outset? These are not easily ascertainable facts. Nonetheless, that is the *psak*.

### Orphans & Fenced Land

The Gemara presses further with additional cases. *Yasmi de-lav benei mechila ninhu* — what about dates found near trees belonging to orphans, that is, minors, whose *da'at* carries no legal weight? Even if the circumstances would otherwise support *ye'ush*, their despair is legally meaningless. The response is practical: *baga be-ara de-yasmi lo machzikinan* — we do not presume that a given valley or plot belongs to orphans, and therefore one need not be concerned.

But what if the land is *muchzak ve-omed*, established as belonging to minors? Then it is *asurin* — forbidden to keep the fallen fruit. And what of land that is *kirchasa*, surrounded by a fence? There the owner clearly anticipates that fallen dates will remain within the protected enclosure, safe from insects. He expects to recover them. If the dates fall outside the fence, the owner would presumably despair — but since his stated expectation is that they remain within, and the land is protected, there is no original *ye'ush*. The answer here too is *asurin*.

Tosafot adds an important observation about what is implied in this Gemara. The phrase *bishlama le-Rava keivan de-lichi gadli miya'esh hashta nami mikrei ye'ush* reveals something striking: according to Rava, even where an owner is a seven-year-old child whose *ye'ush* will not carry legal weight for another six years, if the circumstances are such that a legally competent person would be *meyaiesh*, that is sufficient. This is a considerable conceptual leap. It is not simply that we extrapolate what the owner's state of mind *would* be were he aware of the loss — we are now applying the principle even where the owner's *da'at* will not become legally meaningful for years. This suggests that what really drives Rava's position is not a legal fiction about implicit mental states, but rather the circumstances themselves: where conditions are such that any normal person would despair, the finder may keep the object. That framing sits better with the practical implications. Regardless, *le-ma'aseh* we rule like Abaye, which means that to keep a *metzia* one must be able to establish either that the owner could have anticipated the loss from the outset, making it more akin to *ye'ush mida'at*, or that the owner is by now aware that the object has been lost — and in either case, the owner must be an adult with legally operative *da'at*.

## Siman Trampled

### Krichos & Domains

The Gemara now turns to the technical analysis of the *mishnah*'s various cases, beginning with: *krichos b'reshut harabim harei elu shelo* — small bundles of wheat found in a public domain may be kept, while in a private domain one must announce the find. What is it about these small bundles that makes the distinction between public and private domain operative?

Rabba states: *v'afilu b'davar she'yesh bo siman* — the rule permitting one to keep them applies even if the bundle carries an identifying mark. Rabba's reasoning is that *siman ha'asuy liddares lo havei siman* — a *siman* that is liable to be trampled is not a valid *siman*. Bundles, it is assumed, typically have some identifying feature: the manner of the knot, a piece of paper inserted in the bundle, or some similar mark. In a private domain, that *siman* remains intact and must be used to return the object. In a public domain, however, passersby will step on the bundle and destroy the mark. Critically, Rabba holds that even if the *siman* has not yet been trampled, if it is the kind of thing that *could* be trampled, the owners will already have despaired of identification. They assume it has been destroyed and will not hope to recover the object through it. Hence in a public domain, even a bundle with a *siman* may be kept.

### Rava on Makom

Rava disagrees entirely with Rabba's framing. *Lo shanu ela b'davar she'ein bo siman aval davar she'yesh bo siman chayav l'hachriz*: the distinction between public and private domain applies only to objects without a *siman*; if there is a *siman*, even in the public domain one must announce and return the find. Rava holds that *siman ha'asuy liddares havei siman* — a *siman* that could be trampled remains valid, because the owner will still hope that it has not been trampled and will expect to recover his property through it.

Rava then offers an entirely different explanation of why the *mishnah* distinguishes between public and private domains in the case of *krichos*. The Gemara challenges this by asking: if there is no *siman*, what can possibly be announced in a private domain that would allow an owner to reclaim the bundle? *Machriz makom* — location is announced. Rava holds that *makom* (location) constitutes a valid *siman*, at least in certain circumstances. As Tosafot explains, location works as an identifier specifically when the object was placed and forgotten: the owner set the bundle down in a particular spot and left without it, and therefore knows exactly where he left it. This is distinct from an object that fell accidentally from one's possession, in which case the owner typically cannot identify the location.

Rabba, by contrast, holds *makom lo havei siman* — location is never a valid identifier, regardless of context.

The Gemara now makes this debate explicit. *De'itmar makom: Rabbah amar lo havei siman, ve-Rava amar havei siman.* This is the true axis of the dispute, and it is what the *mishnah*'s distinction between public and private domains reflects. According to Rava, *krichos* in a private domain must be announced because *makom* works as a *siman* when the bundle was left behind and not moved. In a public domain, even if it was left in a particular spot, it gets kicked around — *deminshpa*, as Rashi explains, meaning they roll — and therefore *makom* ceases to be a reliable identifier. Rabba explains the same *mishnah* entirely through the lens of *siman ha'asuy liddares*: in a private domain the *siman* is not destroyed; in a public domain it would be trampled and is therefore invalid.

### Sheaves & Domains

The Gemara then introduces *alummot* — large sheaves — which the *mishnah* requires to be announced whether found in a public or private domain. How does each position explain this?

Rabba explains it through *siman*: large sheaves, being tall, do not get trampled — *lo middarsah* — and therefore the *siman* remains valid in both domains. Rava explains it through *makom*: large sheaves are heavy — *de'ikiri* — and therefore they do not roll or shift even if bumped. Where Rabba's reason is their height (one cannot trample something tall), Rava's reason is their weight (something heavy does not get displaced). The location therefore remains a reliable identifier even in a public domain, and thus must be announced wherever they are found.

### What's Announced

A question arises as to precisely what is being announced when *machriz makom* is the operative principle. The phrasing of the Gemara — *mai machriz? machriz makom* — admits two readings, and Rashi and Tosafot disagree.

According to Rashi, the finder announces the location: "I found something at such-and-such a corner; if you left something there, come forward and tell me what it was." The identifying mark the claimant must produce is the object itself — its description. Tosafot's reading is more intuitive and, it must be said, more convincing. The finder announces what was found: "I found a sack of grain." What the claimant must then produce is the *makom* — the specific location where he left it. The finder asks for location as the identifying mark, not the object.

Rashi's reading presents difficulties. If the finder announces only the location, there is no particular reason why a genuine owner would be more likely to come forward than an opportunistic fabricator. People think about the objects they have lost, not the locations where they may have been left. A person who lost his bundle is waiting to hear an announcement about that bundle; he is not scanning announcements of locations and trying to recall whether he left something somewhere nearby. Furthermore, the Gemara never suggests that naming an object by type alone suffices as a *siman* — there is always some identifying detail required of the claimant. The system the Gemara envisions works most naturally if the finder publicizes the *object* and the owner produces the *makom* as proof of ownership.

## Home-Baked Bread

### Bread Identification

The *mishnah* distinguishes between baker's bread (*kikaros shel nachtom*), which is standardized and therefore carries no identifying marks and may be kept, and home-baked bread (*shel ba'al habayit*), which requires announcement regardless of where it is found. The reason, as the Gemara explains, is *keivan de'is beih siman dimyada yada rifta de'inish inish*: people recognize home-baked bread. Each household's bread has its own characteristic shape, braid, or appearance, and even if one cannot fully articulate the distinguishing features, they are recognizable. The *mishnah* specifies that this applies in both a public and private domain, without distinction.

The Gemara draws a significant inference: *alma siman ha'asuy liddares havei siman* — this proves that even a *siman* susceptible to trampling remains valid, since there is no domain-based distinction made for home-baked bread even though it could be stepped on in a public domain.

### Proof Against Rabba

This appears to be a *tiyuvta* — a decisive refutation — against Rabba's position that *siman ha'asuy liddares lo havei siman*. Rabba responds that this is no proof, because the home-baked bread case rests on a different principle entirely: *ein ma'avirin al ha'ochlin* — one does not step over food. Since people naturally step around food rather than on it, bread in a public domain is not truly *asuy liddares*; the concern about trampling does not apply to foodstuffs.

The Gemara presses: but what of non-Jews, who have no such hesitation? The reply is *nochrim chaishi l'chshafim* — non-Jews are fearful of witchcraft and believe that stepping on food renders them susceptible to it, so they too will not trample it. And what of animals and dogs? *Be-asra de-lo shchichi behemah ve-klavim* — the case is one where such animals are not commonly found. These are admittedly circumstantial answers, and the Gemara cycles through them somewhat rapidly. What matters is that Rabba's position is defended, however tenuously, by insisting that food is categorically exempt from the *siman ha'asuy liddares* concern.

## Hidden Simanim

### Money in Bread

The Gemara now cites a *mishnah* in which Rabbi Yehuda states: *kol davar she'yesh bo shinuy chayav l'hachriz* — anything that has an unusual feature must be announced. The example he gives: one who finds a round pressed-fig cake with a potsherd inside it (*matza igul uvesocho cheres*), or a loaf of bread with money baked inside it (*kikar uvesocho ma'os*), must announce the find. By implication, the *Tanna Kamma* holds that one need not announce — one may keep — these objects.

The Gemara initially assumes that the debate is about *siman ha'asuy liddares*, and frames it accordingly: the *Tanna Kamma* holds *lo havei siman*, Rabbi Yehuda holds *havei siman*. But this framing is quickly set aside. The real question, as the Gemara clarifies, is something different: *siman ha'ba me'elav* — a naturally-occurring or internally-generated identifying mark.

### Rashi on Intent

Rashi explains the dispute in terms of intent. A potsherd might fall into a fig cake on its own, or money might somehow end up baked inside bread. If it happened by itself — accidentally — the owner may not even know it is there, and therefore it cannot function as an identifying mark the owner can produce. If, on the other hand, the owner placed it there deliberately as a mark, then of course it is a valid *siman*. The debate between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda thus concerns what we assume: did this happen accidentally, rendering it an unreliable *siman*, or was it placed intentionally, making it valid?

### Tosafot on Awareness

Tosafot takes a different and more nuanced approach. Tosafot notes that Rashi's framing is somewhat puzzling — it is not at all obvious that someone would intentionally place money inside a loaf of bread as a *siman*. Tosafot therefore assumes the mark was clearly accidental. The question is not whether it was intentional, but whether the owner was *aware* of it. Even if something happened by accident, if I subsequently noticed it, I can use it as an identifying mark. What makes *siman ha'ba me'elav* potentially invalid is precisely this: since it happened by chance, *mistama lo yada* — we presume the owner probably does not know about it. The assumption of ignorance is what undermines its reliability.

### Hidden vs. Visible

Tosafot makes a further critical distinction: the entire category of *siman ha'ba me'elav* as a questionable identifier applies only to internal, non-visible marks. If a siman occurs naturally but is externally visible — a splotch on a sheep's flank, a marking on the surface of an object — there is no question that *hakol havei siman ha'ba me'elav*: any naturally occurring but externally visible mark is a valid *siman*, and the owner may be presumed to be aware of it. What generates the doubt is specifically the combination of accidental occurrence and invisibility from the outside. The money is baked inside the bread; the potsherd is pressed inside the fig cake. The owner may never have known it was there. That is why these cases are questionable, and that is the debate between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda.

## Home-Baked Bread…

### Food Protection

The Gemara now works through multiple versions of the same logical structure to resolve the interaction between *siman ha'asuy liddares*, *ein ma'avirin al ha'ochlin*, and *siman ha'ba me'elav*. The key statement comes from Rav Zevid in the name of Rava. The Gemara has been testing whether the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda are debating *siman ha'asuy liddares* or *ma'avirin al ha'ochlin*, and Rav Zevid demonstrates that neither framing works as a general debate, because in either case the obligation to announce home-baked bread would be left without foundation.

The argument runs as follows: if the *Tanna Kamma* held both that *siman ha'asuy liddares lo havei siman* and that *ma'avirin al ha'ochlin* — that one may pass by and thereby trample food — then the combination would mean food could be trampled and trampled *simanim* are invalid, which would mean you could never be obligated to return home-baked bread. Since we know home-baked bread must be returned, this combination is impossible.

### Both Valid

From this analysis, the Gemara arrives at the following resolution, stated twice over from two directions. First iteration — attributed to Rav Zevid in the name of Rava: *dechulei alma savri siman ha'asuy liddares havei siman* — everyone agrees that a *siman* that could be trampled remains valid. And it may further be true that one can walk by or step on food. Neither point undermines the obligation to return home-baked bread, because even a trampleable *siman* is still valid. The debate in the *mishnah* is therefore about *siman ha'ba me'elav*: the *Tanna Kamma* holds *lo havei siman* and Rabbi Yehuda holds *havei siman*.

Second iteration — again from Rav Zevid in the name of Rava, representing Rava's own position: *dechulei alma savar siman ha'asuy liddares lo havei siman* — everyone agrees that a tramplable *siman* is not valid (consistent with Rava's ruling against Rabba). And further, *ein ma'avirin al ha'ochlin* — one cannot step on food. This means food is never truly *asuy liddares*, which is why home-baked bread must always be returned. And again, the debate in the *mishnah* is about *siman ha'ba me'elav*, with the same assignment of positions.

The net result is identical regardless of which approach one takes: one way or another, food retains its *siman* and must be returned — either because tramplable *simanim* remain valid in general (first approach), or because food specifically is not subject to trampling (second approach). And in either case, the *mishnah*'s dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda is about the distinct question of naturally-occurring, hidden, internal identifying marks.

## Practical Rulings

### Krichos Kept

The Gemara now turns to practical rulings, introduced by Rav Zevid in the name of Rava, concluding the earlier debate with a statement of *hilchesa*. The basic ruling of the *mishnah* stands as the starting point: *krichos b'reshut harabim harei elu shelo* — small bundles found in the public domain may be kept. This is the baseline rule; the further analysis will explain exactly why and under what conditions.

### Left vs. Fallen

In a private domain the analysis becomes more refined, and here Rava's position diverges clearly from Rabba's. *Ai derech nefilah harei elu shelo* — if the bundles appear to have fallen accidentally from the owner's possession, one may keep them even in a private domain. The reason follows directly from Rava's position that *makom* is the operative *siman* for *krichos*: *makom* works only when an item was deliberately placed and forgotten. If the item fell, the owner has no specific location to identify and *makom* fails as a *siman*, leaving nothing by which the object can be reclaimed.

Conversely, *ai derech hanachah notel umachriz* — if the bundle appears to have been placed and left behind, one must announce it, because the owner knows where he left it and *makom* functions as a valid identifier. This distinction between *derech nefilah* and *derech hanachah* is thus central to the ruling, and the finder must assess from context which situation obtains.

It should be noted that *derech hanachah* encompasses two distinct scenarios. One is where something was clearly forgotten in an exposed place — a bag of groceries left on a public bench. The other is where an item was left in a more protected location and the owner may actually intend to return for it. The Gemara elsewhere discusses this distinction — *hunach* versus *safeik hunach* — and it is important not to collapse the two. Only in the first case, where it is clear the owner forgot the item, does *makom* function as a *siman* and an obligation to announce arise.

### With Siman

If the object carries any *siman* other than *makom*, the analysis changes entirely. *Aval b'davar she'yesh bo siman: lo sh'na reshut harabim velo sh'na reshut hayachid, bein derech nefilah bein derech hanachah, chayav l'hachriz* — if there is a proper identifying mark, domain is irrelevant, manner of loss is irrelevant, and one must always announce and return. This follows directly from rejecting Rabba and ruling like Rava: since a *siman* remains valid even if it could be trampled, the concern that drove Rabba's distinction between domains no longer applies. A *siman* is a *siman* wherever the object is found and however it came to rest there.

The practical upshot is that Rava's rulings expand the finder's obligation relative to Rabba's on two fronts: objects with a *siman* must be returned even in a public domain (where Rabba would have permitted keeping them), and objects without a *siman* but left behind in a private domain must be announced (because *makom* is a valid *siman* under those circumstances, which Rabba would have denied).

## Types of Simanim

### Fish on String

The Gemara works through the *mishnah*'s individual cases to illustrate the principles governing what constitutes a valid *siman*. A *machrozet shel dagim* — a string of fish — may be kept. Two potential *simanim* are proposed and dismissed. First: should the knot by which the fish are strung serve as a *siman*? No — *bekitra detzaida*, it is a fisherman's knot, the standard tie used universally in that trade, and what is universal cannot identify. Second: should the number of fish on the string serve as a *siman*? No — *beminyana deshavin*, there is a standard number, and standard quantities do not identify.

On the question of whether number can serve as a *siman* in principle, the Gemara reports that the question was in fact posed to Rav Sheshet, who replies that the answer can be found in the following teaching: *matza kesef nechoshet* (the Gra removes the word *kli* here as Rashi notes a vessel already has identifying marks, and reads the case as referring to raw ingots), or ingots of various metals — *u'mishkala havei siman, mida u'minyan nami havei siman*. Since weight is established as a valid *siman*, measurement and number are likewise valid.

### Metals & Measure

The practical force of this ruling is that for ingots and unworked metals, precision of description — by weight, dimension, or count — constitutes a valid identifying mark. This is because these objects typically lack surface markings, and quantitative precision is the most available form of identification.

### Precision Debate

Tosafot raises a subtle point about the relative reliability of different quantitative *simanim*. The Gemara elsewhere implies that if two claimants come forward for a garment and one gives the dimensions while the other gives the weight, the one who gives dimensions is treated as the more credible claimant. This seems to suggest that measurement is a more precise identifier than weight. Yet the Gemara's language here — moving from weight to measurement — seems to imply the opposite, that measurement is the derivative inference from weight rather than the superior category.

Tosafot resolves this: *dehanni millei be-beged d'ein derech lishkol, hasam vaddai mishkalo adif* — for a garment, which is not normally weighed, knowing the weight is the more impressive piece of knowledge and therefore the better *siman*. Saying a garment is a size 42 regular requires far less precision than knowing it weighs exactly two pounds, three ounces. The hierarchy depends on what is and is not normally known about the type of object in question.

This points to a broader principle worth noting: the Gemara's statement that weight, measurement, and number are valid *simanim* does not mean all quantities are equally reliable. There is a range between a completely standard quantity (eight fish on every rope) — which is clearly not a *siman* — and a highly unusual or precise quantity that could only be known by the actual owner. Quantities that fall in the middle — not standard, but easily guessable within a narrow range — present genuine uncertainty about whether they qualify. The Gemara establishes the principle without fully mapping its limits.

### Meat Cuts

For cuts of meat (*chatichos shel basar*), the Gemara similarly dismisses standard potential *simanim*. Weight is not sufficient — *bemishkala deshavin*, cuts come in standard weights. The cut itself — whether it is a side piece, a shoulder, a neck — is also not sufficient, as these are standard categories of butchery. The question then is what *would* constitute a valid *siman* for a piece of meat.

The answer is: an unusual cut. Rashi, in a celebrated observation, notes that the Gemara mentions *dafka* as one standard cut type, and cites in Rashi's name a tradition that *dafka* means *flanken* — a specific well-known cut. The standard nomenclature of cuts does not identify. What identifies is when the butcher — or the owner — cuts the meat in an unusual way. The Gemara offers the example of Rabbah bar bar Hana, who would cut his meat into a triangle (*tlata karnata* — three corners). Such an unusual cut is a valid *siman*.

This is confirmed, the Gemara notes, by the *mishnah*'s own language: the obligation to announce cuts of fish derives from the case of *dag nashuch* — a bitten fish — which is inherently unusual. *Dumya de-dag nashuch*: just as a bitten fish is unusual and therefore identifiable, so too any cut of meat that departs from the standard is a valid *siman*; no standard cut is.

The practical question of whether combining multiple non-*siman* identifiers — species of animal, type of cut, approximate weight — might together constitute a valid *siman* is raised but left open by the Gemara. Intuitively, if no single feature is distinctive enough, the conjunction of several might be; the Gemara does not resolve this.

### Barrels & Pitchers

The *mishnah* lists several types of barrels — *chaviyos* of wine, oil, grain, dried figs, and olives — that may be kept. These are standard, sealed barrels with no external identifying marks, and there is nothing to distinguish one from another. However, a separate source teaches that *kadei yayin ve-kadei shemen chayav l'hachriz* — pitchers of wine and oil must be announced. The Gemara often treats pitchers and barrels as equivalent containers, so the distinction requires explanation.

Rav Zevid said in the name of Rav: *matnisin b'rashum* — the source requiring announcement refers to marked pitchers, meaning those that have been sealed again after opening and bear the owner's personal stamp on the seal. The stamp is the *siman*, and it obligates announcement.

### Supplier's Mark

But this raises a further question: if the barrel bears a supplier's mark, doesn't that always constitute a valid *siman*? The Gemara, following Abaye, resolves this through a temporal distinction: *kan kodem sheniftchu ha-otzaros, kan l'achar sheniftchu ha-otzaros* — before the warehouses have been opened and the barrels shipped to retailers, the supplier's mark is a unique identifier. You know whose it is and whom to return it to. After the warehouses have opened and the barrels have been distributed to many different retailers, the supplier's mark no longer identifies, because any of numerous retailers may have had barrels bearing that same mark.

The Gemara illustrates this with a practical ruling: Rav Yaakov bar Abba found a barrel of wine after the warehouses had been opened and came to Abaye. Abaye told him: *zil shkol l'nafshach* — go keep it for yourself. Once the barrels have been distributed, the mark on the seal no longer points to a single identifiable owner, and therefore there is no *siman* and no obligation to return it.
