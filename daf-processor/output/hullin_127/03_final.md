# Hullin 127 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sheratzim Overview

### Opening & Prior Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הַטְּמֵאִים״ – לְרַבּוֹת בֵּיצַת הַשֶּׁרֶץ וְקוּלִית הַשֶּׁרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that in the case of an egg of a creeping animal in which tissue of an embryo developed, if it is perforated then one who touches it is ritually impure. With regard to this matter **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "These are the impure ones to you among all that creep; whoever touches them when they are dead shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 11:31). The term **"the impure ones"** is interpreted as **including the egg of a creeping animal and the thigh bone of a creeping animal** in the category of sources of impurity.

Daf 127 opens at the very bottom of 126b. Having spent considerable time on questions of skin and bone serving as a *shomer* — and, before that, on the laws of *tumas meis* and how the *tumah* of a corpse rises through the air, whether by contact or by *ohel* — we now turn to a new *mishna* treating an entirely different domain of impurity.

### Eight Sheratzim

The new *mishna* concerns *sheratzim* — the eight specific types of rodents and reptiles enumerated in the *Torah* that convey *tumah* upon death. These creatures, both small rodents and reptiles, are among the most familiar sources of *tumat maga* in the laws of purity, and the *mishna* opens up a series of questions about their precise scope and application.

### Reptile Eggs & Spontaneous Generation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״עַכְבָּר״, שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי אֲפִילּוּ עַכְבָּר שֶׁבַּיָּם, שֶׁשְּׁמוֹ עַכְבָּר. וְדִין הוּא: טִימֵּא בְּחוּלְדָּה וְטִימֵּא בְּעַכְבָּר, מָה חוּלְדָּה מִין הַגָּדֵל עַל הָאָרֶץ, אַף עַכְבָּר מִין הַגָּדֵל עַל הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** Since "mouse" is stated among the creeping animals that impart impurity, **I** would **derive** that **even a sea mouse,** i.e., a sea creature that has an appearance similar to a mouse, imparts impurity **because its name is** also **mouse. But** ostensibly, the opposite conclusion could be derived through **logical inference:** The verse **deems a weasel impure and deems a mouse impure.** Therefore, **just as a weasel is a species that grows on land, so too** the **mouse** to which the verse is referring is **a species that grows on land;** a sea mouse does not impart impurity.

The *mishna* also addresses the eggs of reptiles: because one does not have direct access to the developing fetus within, touching the egg renders one *tahor*, but if there is a hole in the shell, one becomes *tamei*. A striking and genuinely fascinating case then follows — one that can only be understood against the backdrop of ancient scientific belief. The rabbis understood that a certain *achbar*, a rat or mouse, could arise through spontaneous generation from the earth itself. At a particular stage of its development, the creature might be half soil and half flesh as it emerged from the ground. The *mishna* raises the question of whether that half-ground portion shares the *tumah* status of the rat itself. It bears emphasizing that spontaneous generation was not disproven until the seventeenth century, and the rabbis dealt with the best scientific understanding available to them.

## Sea Rat Dispute

### Tanu Rabbanan Sea Rat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: טִימֵּא בְּחוּלְדָּה וְטִימֵּא בְּעַכְבָּר, מָה חוּלְדָּה כֹּל שֶׁשְּׁמָהּ ״חוּלְדָּה״, אַף עַכְבָּר כֹּל שֶׁשְּׁמוֹ ״עַכְבָּר״, אֲפִילּוּ עַכְבָּר שֶׁבַּיָּם שֶׁשְּׁמוֹ ״עַכְבָּר״. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עַל הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Or,** perhaps **go this way:** The verse **deems a weasel impure and deems a mouse impure;** accordingly, **just as "weasel"** is referring to **any** animal **whose name is weasel, so too, "mouse"** is referring to **any** animal **whose name is mouse, even a sea mouse, as its name is** also **mouse.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Upon the earth,"** indicating that a land mouse imparts impurity, but a sea mouse does not.

The *Gemara* on 126b presents a *baraita*: *Tanu Rabbanan* — our rabbis taught. Since the *Torah* mentions *achbar* as one of the eight *sheratzim* that are *tamei* upon death, one might have thought this includes the sea *achbar* as well. There was a creature known as the sea rat — *achbar shebayam* — called by the same name. A *kal vachomer* or analogy might seem to support its inclusion. The *baraita* presents the argument as follows: the *Torah* states that both *chulda* (the weasel) and *achbar* are *tamei*. Just as the *chulda* is a creature of the land — *min hagadel al ha'aretz* — so too the *achbar* should be understood as referring only to land-dwelling animals, thereby excluding the sea rat.

### Chulda Analogies

The *baraita* immediately offers the counter-reading: *o kalech laderech zu* — or consider the alternative. The same analogy to *chulda* can be pressed in the opposite direction. One might argue that the term *chulda* applies to any creature called by that name — *kol sheshmo chulda* — without geographic restriction. The sea rat, called *achbar*, would then be included precisely because its name is *achbar*. The analogy from *chulda* thus cuts both ways, and neither direction is self-evidently correct.

### 'Al Ha'aretz Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי ״עַל הָאָרֶץ״, יָכוֹל עַל הָאָרֶץ – יְטַמֵּא, יָרַד לְיָם – לֹא יְטַמֵּא,
>
> **Translation:** **If** this *halakha* is derived only from the phrase **"upon the earth,"** one **might** have thought that the verse means that any mouse, whether a land mouse or a sea mouse, **imparts impurity** when it is **upon the earth,** but if **it descended to the sea it does not impart impurity.**

The *baraita* therefore turns to the verse itself: *talmud lomar* — *hasheretz hashoretz al ha'aretz*, that which creeps upon the ground. This phrase, the *baraita* argues, restricts *tumah* to land animals, not sea animals. But even this phrase admits of a further ambiguity: perhaps *al ha'aretz* refers not to the *type* of animal but to its *location* at the moment of contact. On that reading, if one touches a land rat while it is in the sea — say, a drowned rat floating in the ocean — one would be *tahor*, since the rat is not currently on the ground.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַשּׁוֹרֵץ״ – כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁשּׁוֹרֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "That creep,"** indicating that creeping animals impart impurity **anywhere that** they **creep,** including the sea, as these animals can float in the sea. Consequently, the phrase "upon the earth" is understood as indicating that a sea mouse does not impart impurity.

The *baraita* rejects this reading by adding *hashoretz — mikol makom sheshoretz*, wherever it creeps. This phrase comes to include even a land rat found in the sea, thus clarifying that *al ha'aretz* defines the *species*, not the *circumstance*. The phrase "on the land" tells us which type of creature is subject to the laws of *tumah*, not where that creature must physically be found when touched.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא ״הַשּׁוֹרֵץ״ – יָכוֹל כׇּל הַמַּשְׁרִיץ יְטַמֵּא, שֶׁאֵין מַשְׁרִיץ לֹא יְטַמֵּא? אוֹצִיא עַכְבָּר שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ בָּשָׂר וְחֶצְיוֹ אֲדָמָה, שֶׁאֵין פָּרֶה וְרָבֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* raises an alternative interpretation: **Or** perhaps the term **"that creep [*hashoretz*]"** should **not** be interpreted in this manner, as it **could rather** be interpreted to mean that **any** creeping animal **that breeds [*hammashritz*] imparts impurity,** but a creeping animal **that does not breed does not impart impurity. I shall** therefore **exclude a mouse that is half**-**flesh half-earth,** i.e., that generates spontaneously from the earth, **as it does not breed** and therefore does not impart impurity.

A further issue arises from the word *sheretz* itself. The verse could be read to mean that only a creature that procreates — *mashritz* — is subject to *tumah*, drawing on the related usage in *Shemot* where *vayishretzu* describes the Israelites multiplying prolifically. If so, the spontaneously generated rat, which does not procreate, might be excluded.

### Spontaneous Generation Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הוּא: טִימֵּא בְּחוּלְדָּה וְטִימֵּא בְּעַכְבָּר, מָה חוּלְדָּה פָּרָה וְרָבָה, אַף עַכְבָּר פָּרֶה וְרָבֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּשֶּׁרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **But** ostensibly, the *halakha* of a mouse that is half-flesh half-earth is subject to **logical inference:** Since the verse **deems a weasel impure and deems a mouse impure,** then **just as "weasel"** is referring to **any** animal **whose name is weasel, so too, "mouse"** is referring to **any** animal **whose name is mouse, even a mouse that is half-flesh half-earth.**

The *baraita* counters this by reading an inclusive *bet* in the word *basheretz* as extending the category to any creature called a *sheretz*, even one that does not procreate naturally. Through this exegetical move — somewhat opaque in its precise mechanism, as the lecturer notes — the spontaneously generated rat is brought within the scope of the verse's impurity laws.

### Analogy Reversal Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: אֵימָא ״בַּשֶּׁרֶץ״, לְאֵתוֹיֵי עַכְבָּר שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ בָּשָׂר וְחֶצְיוֹ אֲדָמָה, ״הַשּׁוֹרֵץ״ – כֹּל שֶׁהוּא שׁוֹרֵץ, וַאֲפִילּוּ עַכְבָּר שֶׁבַּיָּם, וְאִי מִשּׁוּם ״עַל הָאָרֶץ״ – עַל הָאָרֶץ יְטַמֵּא, יָרַד לַיָּם – לֹא יְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **One of the Sages said to Rava: Say** the interpretation of the verse differently. The term **"among the creeping animals"** serves **to include a mouse that is half-flesh half-earth** among those that impart impurity. The term **"that creep"** teaches that **any** animal **that creeps** imparts impurity, **and even a sea mouse. And if** one should reject this interpretation **due to** the phrase **"upon the earth,"** which seems to indicate that a sea mouse does not impart impurity, that phrase teaches that a creeping animal **imparts impurity** only when it is **on land,** but if it **descended to the sea** it **does not impart impurity.**

At this point the *Gemara* on 127a records a challenge from one of the rabbinic students to *Rava*. The student asks: why not reverse the two rulings? Why not use *basheretz* to include the sea rat — interpreting it as applying to any creature that creeps, wherever found — and then use *al ha'aretz* to restrict *tumah* to cases where the creature is physically on land, thereby excluding both the sea rat in the sea and the land rat encountered in the sea?

This formulation would yield: sea rats are *tamei*, land rats only when on land, and spontaneously generated rats included by the name-based analogy. The student's question is whether the *baraita*'s chosen allocation of the exegetical resources is the only defensible one.

### Rava's Location Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמֵאַחַר דְּשַׁוִּיתֵיהּ לְיָם מְקוֹם טוּמְאָה, מָה לִי הָכָא, מָה לִי הָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** Your suggestion is not logical. According to your opinion, a sea mouse, which is in the sea, imparts impurity. **And since you consider the sea a location of impurity,** it is impossible to suggest that a mouse does not impart impurity when it is located in the sea. Since both land and sea are places of impurity, **what** difference does it make **for me** if the mouse is located **here** on land, and **what** difference does it make **for me** if it is located **there** in the sea?

*Rava* responds with a sharp logical observation: *me'achar deshaviteih layam mekom tumah* — once you have made the ocean a place where *tumah* can reside at all, by including sea rats, why would it make any sense to then exclude land rats found there? If the sea rat is *tamei* in the sea, then you have already conceded that the ocean is a venue where *tumah* operates. There is then no grounds to say a land rat found in the sea is *tahor*. The two positions are incoherent together. If, on the other hand, *al ha'aretz* defines species, then everything is consistent: land-type creatures are *tamei* wherever found, and sea-type creatures are excluded entirely.

## Safek Tumah Tzafa

### Floating Doubt Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״עַל הָאָרֶץ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְהוֹצִיא סְפֵק טוּמְאָה צָפָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: ״עַל הָאָרֶץ״ – לְהוֹצִיא סְפֵק טוּמְאָה צָפָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can the *baraita* interpret the phrase "upon the earth" as teaching that a sea mouse does not impart impurity? Isn't **this** phrase: **"Upon the earth," necessary to exclude** a case of **uncertainty** involving **a floating** source of **impurity?** If a person is uncertain whether he touched a source of impurity that is floating in the water, he remains pure even if the incident took place in a private domain, where a case of uncertain impurity is generally deemed impure. **As Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi said:** The phrase **"upon the earth"** is written **to exclude** a case of **uncertainty** involving **a floating** source of **impurity.**

The *Gemara* then addresses a further implication of the phrase *al ha'aretz*: *vehai al ha'aretz miba'ei lei lehotzi safek tumah tzafa* — the verse is needed to exclude a case of *doubtful tumah* that is *floating* in the sea. *Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi* teaches: *al ha'aretz* — referring to what creeps upon the ground — comes to exclude *safek tumah tzafa*, a case of doubtful *tumah* floating in the ocean. In that case, one is *tahor*. Since the verse is needed for this purpose as well, the *Gemara* notes that *hasheretz al ha'aretz* is written twice, and one instance addresses the species question while the other addresses this special *safek* ruling.

### Tumah Domains & Ocean

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְתֵּי ״עַל הָאָרֶץ״ כְּתִיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The phrase **"upon the earth" is written two** times in the passage. One instance is written to exclude a case of uncertainty involving a source of impurity that is floating, and the other instance teaches that a sea mouse does not impart impurity.

This is one of the more strikingly arbitrary-seeming rules in the laws of *tumah*. The basic framework is familiar: if one has a doubt about *tumah* in a *reshut hayachid*, one is ruled *tamei*; in a *reshut harabim*, one is ruled *tahor*. Here, floating in the ocean constitutes its own halakhic category. If one touched an unidentified decomposed object in the ocean and cannot determine whether it was a dead *sheretz* or something else, one is *tahor* — a leniency that would not apply had the identical doubt arisen on land in a private domain.

The *tumah* of the *sheretz* itself, it should be noted, only applies to the creature after death — *asher yipol mehem bemotam yitma*, the *Torah* states explicitly. The special rule here concerns doubt: even where a land rat found in the sea would create *tumah* if one *definitely* touched it, the doubtful case is treated more leniently when the object is floating in the ocean. The precise logic underlying this leniency may be compared to the leniency in a *reshut harabim*, where the probability of inadvertent contact with *tumah* in a vast public space argues for leniency. The ocean, similarly, may function as a domain in which such doubts are resolved favorably. Whether this principle would extend to a private body of water — a private lake, for instance — is a question the *Gemara* does not resolve here.

## Tzav Species & Creation

### Reptile Species List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הַצָּב לְמִינֵהוּ״ – לְהָבִיא הֶעָרוֹד, וְכֵן הַנְּפִילִים וְסָלָמַנְדְּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse: **"The great lizard after its kinds"** (Leviticus 11:29) **includes** in the category of creeping animals **the *arvad*,** a type of snake, **and also** the creeping animals called ***nefilim* and salamander [*salamandera*].**

*Tanu Rabbanan* — the verse states *hatzav leminehu*, the *tzav* according to its kind. The *tzav* appears to be a type of lizard or chameleon. The phrase *leminehu* — "according to its kind" — is understood to include additional species within this category. These are: the *arod*, a type of lethal reptile (distinguished here from the wild donkey also called *arod* in some contexts, as noted by the *Gemara*); the *nifilim* (some versions read *uben hanifilim*); and the *salamandra*, the salamander.

The *arod* is the creature associated with the famous account in *Gemara Brachos* of Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa: while he was in the midst of prayer at a hole in the ground, an *arod* bit him and died, since his prayer was so holy that the deadly creature could not harm him. The lesson drawn there was that it is not the *arod* that kills but sin.

### Rabbi Akiva's Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַגִּיעַ לְפָסוּק זֶה, אוֹמֵר: ״מָה רַבּוּ מַעֲשֶׂיךָ ה׳״! יֵשׁ לְךָ בְּרִיּוֹת גְּדֵלוֹת בַּיָּם, וְיֵשׁ לְךָ בְּרִיּוֹת גְּדֵלוֹת בַּיַּבָּשָׁה. שֶׁבַּיָּם – אִילְמָלֵי עוֹלוֹת (בַּיַּבָּשָׁה) [לַיַּבָּשָׁה] מִיָּד מֵתוֹת, שֶׁבַּיַּבָּשָׁה – אִילְמָלֵי יוֹרְדוֹת לַיָּם מִיָּד מֵתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the salamander, which was thought to generate from fire, the *baraita* continues: **When Rabbi Akiva would reach this verse** in Leviticus, he would **say** in exclamation: **"How great are Your works, O Lord"** (Psalms 104:24). **You have creatures** that **grow in the sea and you have creatures** that **grow on land. If those in the sea would ascend to the land they would immediately die. If those that are on land would descend to the sea they would immediately die.**

*Ubshaeh Rabbi Akiva megia lapasuk hazeh* — when Rabbi Akiva would reach the verse *hatzav leminehu* and reflect on the astonishing range of creatures included within it, he would say: *mah rabu maasecha Hashem* — how great and numerous are Your creations, O God. He used this verse as an occasion to reflect on the diversity of creation and on the specific ways in which each creature is fashioned to thrive within its particular environment.

### Environmental Adaptation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ לְךָ בְּרִיּוֹת גְּדֵלוֹת בָּאוּר, וְיֵשׁ לְךָ בְּרִיּוֹת גְּדֵלוֹת בָּאֲוִיר. שֶׁבָּאוּר – אִילְמָלֵי עוֹלוֹת לָאֲוִיר מִיָּד מֵתוֹת, שֶׁבָּאֲוִיר – אִילְמָלֵי יוֹרְדוֹת לָאוּר מִיָּד מֵתוֹת. ״מָה רַבּוּ מַעֲשֶׂיךָ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **you have creatures** that **grow in the fire and you have creatures** that **grow in the air. If those in the fire would ascend to the air they would immediately die. If those in the air would descend to the fire they would immediately die.** Therefore, **"how great are Your works, O Lord."**

Rabbi Akiva's reflection unfolds as follows. *Yesh lecha briyot gedolot bayam v'yesh lecha briyot gedolot bayabasha* — there are enormous creatures in the sea and enormous creatures on the land. The sea creatures, if brought onto dry land, would immediately die. The land creatures — no matter how powerful, whether a lion or an elephant — if placed in the sea, would immediately perish. Similarly, there are great creatures of fire and great creatures of the air. The fire creatures, if brought into the air, would die immediately; the air creatures, if brought into fire, would immediately die.

*Mah rabu maasecha Hashem* — how great are Your creations. The wonder Rabbi Akiva is expressing is not merely the diversity of creation, but the exquisite specificity with which each creature is fashioned for its particular domain. The greatness lies not in universality but in the precise fit between creature and environment. The observation invites a question about amphibians and creatures that inhabit multiple domains, but the point of the *derasha* is the mutual incompatibility of the domains — and the marvel of the fact that each creature is made entirely to belong where it belongs.

### Salamander & Fire

The fire creatures Rabbi Akiva mentions are the salamanders. The rabbis believed that the salamander was a creature of fire — created from fire, according to *Rashi*, from fire and myrtle branches through some kind of process — and that anointing oneself with the salamander's blood would confer fireproof qualities. This is the creature included in the verse *hatzav leminehu*, which prompted Rabbi Akiva's exclamation of wonder.

## Regional Warnings

### Land-Sea Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בַּיַּבָּשָׁה יֵשׁ בַּיָּם, חוּץ מִן הַחוּלְדָּה. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: מַאי קְרָאָה? ״הַאֲזִינוּ כׇּל יֹשְׁבֵי חָלֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss creatures living in a particular environment. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: For **every** animal **that exists on land there is** an equivalent animal **in the sea, except for the weasel,** which exists only on land. **Rabbi Zeira said: What is the verse** from which it is derived? It is written: **"Listen all you inhabitants of the world [*ḥeled*]"** (Psalms 49:2). Dry land is called *ḥeled* because it is the sole habitat for the weasel [*ḥulda*].

*Tanu Rabbanan* — our rabbis taught: every land animal has a sea counterpart, with the exception of the *chulda*, the weasel. Rabbi Zeira asks: *mai kra* — what is the scriptural basis for this? He cites the verse *ha'azinu kol yoshvei chaled* — hear, all who dwell in *chaled*. The word *chaled* is etymologically connected to *chulda*, and its usage in reference to dry land indicates that the *chulda* is specifically and exclusively a creature of dry land, with no sea counterpart.

### Narsh & Cherem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: בִּיבְרֵי דְּנָרֶשׁ אֵינָן מִן הַיִּשּׁוּב.
>
> **Translation:** In continuation of the discussion of creatures living in a particular environment, **Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua said: The beavers** of the region **of Neresh are not from the settled area,** because they live only in the water and not on dry land. Consequently, one who eats their meat is not liable to receive lashes for violating the prohibition: "And every creeping animal that creeps upon the earth is a detestable thing; it shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 11:41).

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua then states: *bivrei deNarsh einam min hayishuv* — the people of the place called Narsh are not from the *yishuv*, not from civilized society. While *Rashi* interprets *bivrei deNarsh* as referring to the animals of Narsh being primarily sea creatures, *Tosafot* prefers a reading that fits the context better: these are people, and *einam min hayishuv* means that they are simply uncivilized. This sets the stage for a series of characterizations of various locales.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּשַׁמְתָּא נָרֶשׁ, תַּרְבֵּיהּ מַשְׁכֵּיהּ וְאַלְיְתֵיהּ. ״אֶרֶץ אֶרֶץ אָרֶץ שִׁמְעִי דְּבַר ה׳״ – אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֹא אָבָה נָרֶשׁ שְׁמוֹעַ דְּבַר ה׳.
>
> **Translation:** §Apropos the region surrounding Neresh, **Rav Pappa said:** The people of the city of **Neresh** shall be placed **under excommunication,** as they are all wicked, including **its fat, its hide, and its tail,** i.e., all types of people, both old and young. The Gemara continues to discuss Neresh. The verse states: **"Oh land, land, land hear the word of the Lord"** (Jeremiah 22:29). **Rav Pappa said:** This verse is appropriate with regard to the inhabitants of Neresh, as **Neresh does not want to listen to the word of the Lord.**

Rav Papa, whose remark coheres with this reading, declares: *beshamta Narsh — tarbei mashchei ve'alifta* — let there be a *cherem* over Narsh, the fat, the skin, the fat tail — meaning all of them, every last one. The city is populated entirely by wicked people.

### Geographic Warnings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל, אָמַר רַב: נַרְשָׁאָה נַשְּׁקָיךְ – מָנֵי כַּכָּיךְ; נְהַר פְּקוֹדָאָה לַוְיָיךְ – מִגְּלִימָא שַׁפִּירָא דְּחָזֵי עֲלָךְ; פּוּמְבְּדִיתָאָה לַוְיָיךְ – אַשְׁנִי אוּשְׁפִּיזָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said:** If **a resident of Neresh kisses you, count your teeth** to make sure he did not steal one. And if **a resident of** the city of **Nehar Pekod accompanies you** on a journey, it is **because of the beautiful jacket that he sees on you** and wants to steal from you. If **a resident of Pumbedita accompanies you** on a journey, **change your lodging place** because there is a concern that he will rob you.

The passage continues with a series of terse warnings keyed to specific towns. *Eretz eretz eretz shimi devar Hashem* — land, land, land, hear the word of God — Rav Papa interprets this prophetic cry as addressed to the people of Narsh, who refused to listen. Rav Gidal in the name of Rav adds: if a person from Narsh kisses you, count your teeth — meaning, verify that your possessions are intact. If a person from Nahar Pekoda accompanies you, it is because he has noticed your fine cloak — watch your belongings. And if a person from Pumbedita accompanies you, change your inn immediately — he is trying to discover where you are staying in order to rob you at night. One wonders whether these characterizations applied to the non-Jewish inhabitants of these towns, or to the non-scholarly classes, rather than to the rabbinic community. In any event, the passages function as practical ethical warnings about certain locales.

## Arod Cross-Breeding

### Snake-Lizard Mating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר תּוֹרְתָּא: פַּעַם אַחַת הָלַכְתִּי לַוַּועַד, וְרָאִיתִי נָחָשׁ שֶׁהוּא כָּרוּךְ עַל הַצָּב, לְיָמִים יָצָא עַרְוָד מִבֵּינֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** §The Gemara returns to discussing different types of creatures. **Rav Huna bar Torta said: Once I went to** the city of **Va'ad and I saw** that the locals were in the practice of placing **a snake wrapped around a great lizard** in order to breed the two. **After a period of time, an *arvad*,** a snake that bites and kills people, **emerged from between them.**

The *Gemara* returns to the *arod* and to a remarkable firsthand account. Rav Huna bar Turta said: *paam achas hayisi l'vad* — once I attended a gathering (which *Rashi* identifies as a gathering where cross-breeding was practiced), *v'raisi nachash sheu karuch al hatzav* — and I saw a snake entwined with a *tzav*, a type of lizard. They were mating. *L'yamim yatza arod mibeineihem* — in time, an *arod* emerged from them — this lethal creature was the product of their cross-breeding.

### Divine Response Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁבָּאתִי לִפְנֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הֶחָסִיד, אָמַר לִי: אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: הֵם הֵבִיאוּ בְּרִיָּה שֶׁלֹּא בָּרָאתִי בְּעוֹלָמִי, אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא עֲלֵיהֶם בְּרִיָּה שֶׁלֹּא בָּרָאתִי בְּעוֹלָמִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And when I came before Rabbi Shimon the Righteous, he** explained why this crossbreeding created an *arvad* and **said to me: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said:** These residents of Va'ad **caused the emergence of a creature that I did not create in My world** by crossbreeding a snake and a great lizard; **so too, I will bring upon them** a punishment, the hazard of this uniquely dangerous **creature that I did not create in My world,** i.e., an *arvad*.

When Rav Huna bar Turta brought this account to Rabbi Shimon HaChasid — a *Tanna* not often encountered in this form of address — the latter interpreted it theologically. *Amar HaKadosh Baruch Hu: heim heiviu birya shelo barasi b'olami* — they brought into existence a creature that I had not created in my world. *Af ani avia aleihem birya shelo barasi b'olami* — so I will bring upon them a creature that I had not created, meaning this dangerous *arod* is itself the divine response. Humanity tried to create something new and unnatural; the Holy One brought something equally unprecedented — but destructive — as the punishment.

### Cross-Breeding Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר מָר: כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישָׁן וְעִיבּוּרָן שָׁוֶה – יוֹלְדִין וּמְגַדְּלִין זֶה מִזֶּה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין תַּשְׁמִישָׁן וְעִיבּוּרָן שָׁוֶה – אֵין יוֹלְדִין וּמְגַדְּלִין זֶה מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But didn't the Master say: All** different animals **whose** method of **procreation and** period of **gestation are the same** are able to **reproduce and raise offspring together. But all** animals **whose** method of **procreation and** period of **gestation are not the same cannot reproduce and raise offspring together.** And the gestation period for a great lizard and a snake are not equal.

The *Gemara* raises a pointed objection. There is a principle: *kol she'ein tashmishan v'iburatan shaveh ein yoldin zeh mizeh* — any animals whose manner of copulation differs, whether front-to-front or front-to-back, or whose gestation periods differ, cannot produce offspring together. *V'kol she'ein tashmishan v'iburatan shaveh ein yoldin umigadlin zeh mizeh* — and they cannot nurse each other's young. A snake and a *tzav* differ dramatically in gestation period. How then could they have produced offspring at all?

### Double Miracle Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: נֵס בְּתוֹךְ נֵס! הַאי פּוּרְעֲנוּתָא הוּא? מַאי נֵס בְּתוֹךְ נֵס – לְפוּרְעָנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** It was **a miracle within a miracle** that they were able to reproduce and a new creature was born. The Gemara asks: Why is **this** considered a miracle? **It was a calamity** because an *arvad* was born. The Gemara answers: **What** is meant by **a miracle within a miracle?** It was a miraculous **calamity** for the wicked people, to punish them for their actions.

The answer: *Rav nes b'toch nes* — it was a double miracle. First, a new creature came into being at all. Second, the cross-breeding succeeded despite the animals' biological incompatibility. The *Gemara* then asks: this is a negative event, a punishment — in what sense is it a *miracle*? The answer is: *mai nes b'toch nes l'puranusa* — it is indeed a miracle within a miracle, but a miracle administered as punishment. It is precisely as an expression of divine response that this impossible cross-breeding produced the dangerous *arod*.

## Genetic Engineering Ethics

### Torah Prohibitions

This passage opens into one of the more substantive halakhic-philosophical discussions of the tractate. There are explicit biblical prohibitions against cross-breeding animals — *kilayim* — and against cross-breeding seeds, as well as the prohibition of mixing wool and linen. These are grounded in specific verses. But the *Torah* contains no explicit prohibition of genetic engineering, cloning, or the contemporary forms of biotechnological intervention. The question then becomes: to what extent do the implicit values embedded in this *Gemara* inform those areas?

### Implicit Values Analysis

The language used by Rabbi Shimon HaChasid is striking: *birya shelo barasi b'olami* — a creature that I had not created in my world. This formulation seems to articulate a value of not fabricating new organisms that God has not brought into existence, at least within the animal kingdom. The story suggests that transgressing this boundary carries real moral weight, severe enough to warrant divine punishment through a new and dangerous creature.

### Innovation vs. Species Limits

And yet this sits in tension with other foundational values. The opening chapters of *Bereishit* command *v'chivshuha u'redu b'digat hayam* — human dominion over the natural world — and the opening of creation is marked throughout by the phrase *leminehu*, each species according to its kind. Human innovation is built into the structure of the world; keeping natural species distinct is equally built in. The challenge is that contemporary biotechnology often does not operate by crossing species in the traditional sense, which complicates any straightforward application of the *kilayim* prohibitions.

### Novel Applications Assessment

In areas not directly addressed by the *Torah*, the halakhic response must be case by case. This is not unique to genetic engineering: it is the general challenge of applying traditional halakhic categories to genuinely novel situations. Some *poskim* will seek to find the implicit values of the *pesukim* informative even where no explicit prohibition exists; others will resist ruling stringently in the absence of a direct source.

### Mule Precedent

The mule offers an instructive precedent. The act of cross-breeding a horse and a donkey is prohibited. Yet the mule itself is permissible — beneficial and indeed critical to pre-modern civilization. The *Torah* prohibits the act while not prohibiting the result. This asymmetry is important: the harm is not in the mule's existence but in the human act of deliberate species transgression that produced it.

### Shita Mekubetzet Reading

The *Shita Mekubetzet* offers an alternative reading of the *Gemara* that sheds further light on this. Rather than understanding the story as being about this particular act, the *Shita Mekubetzet* reads it more broadly. He cites the *pesukim* in the *Torah* that are understood to refer to the first instances of cross-breeding leading to mules — *hu matza et hayeimim bamidbar* — and suggests that from the time humans began to cross-breed at all, God decreed that this capacity could produce dangerous as well as beneficial outcomes. Read this way, the theological point is not a condemnation of any specific act, but a statement about the power that human beings unlock when they begin to manipulate natural boundaries. The potential for tremendous good and the potential for tremendous evil are released together.

### Human Responsibility

This reading speaks directly to the question of human responsibility in the face of novel creation. Once one has the power to produce something new — whether through cross-breeding, genetic engineering, or other biotechnological means — one cannot always anticipate what dangers may follow. The parallel to nuclear energy is instructive: the release of an enormously powerful force carries with it a corresponding magnitude of responsibility, and the inability to fully foresee consequences does not diminish that responsibility. The *Gemara* seems to say: you are playing with fire, and you must understand that even when the intent is constructive, the outcome may not be.

## Dangling Limb Status

### Dangling Limb Tuma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאֵבֶר וְהַבָּשָׂר הַמְדוּלְדָּלִין בַּבְּהֵמָה, מְטַמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִים בִּמְקוֹמָן, וּצְרִיכִין הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The limb** of an animal, with flesh, sinews, and bones, **and the flesh** of an animal, **that were** partially severed and remain **hanging from the animal** do not have the halakhic status of a limb severed from a living animal, which imparts impurity like an unslaughtered carcass, or of flesh severed from a living animal, which is ritually pure, respectively. If one had intent to eat the limb or the flesh, the limb or flesh becomes impure if it comes in contact with a source of impurity, and they **impart impurity as food** to other foods and liquids, although they remain **in their place** attached to the animal. **But** in order for them to become impure, **they need** to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity through contact with one of the seven liquids that facilitate susceptibility.

The *Gemara* transitions to the *mishna* governing the *eiver hamedulal* — the dangling limb or flesh barely connected to a living animal. The *mishna* states: *ha'eiver vehabasar hameduldalim bibehema — metam'in tumat ochlin bimkoman* — a dangling limb or dangling flesh from an animal conveys *tumat ochlin*, the impurity of food, while still in place. This is a case encountered earlier in the tractate: something that is in a middle status, not fully part of the living animal yet not fully detached, can already acquire the *tumah* classification of food. And yet — *u'tzrichin hechshier* — it requires *hechshier*, contact with a liquid, to become susceptible to *tumah*, since all foodstuffs require prior exposure to one of seven liquids before they can become *tamei*.

### Shechita as Hechshier

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁחֲטָה הַבְּהֵמָה – הוּכְשְׁרוּ בְּדָמֶיהָ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא הוּכְשְׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **the animal was slaughtered,** although this act of slaughter does not render it permitted for consumption by a Jew (see 73b), the limb and the flesh **were** thereby **rendered susceptible** to impurity by coming in contact **with the blood of** the slaughtered animal, as blood is one of the seven liquids; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Shimon says: They were not rendered susceptible** to impurity through the animal's own blood; they are rendered susceptible only once they have been wet with another liquid.

If the animal was subsequently slaughtered: *nishcheta habeheima huchshiru bedameha* — the act of *shechita* itself serves as *hechshier* for this dangling limb. This parallels a *mishna* treated earlier: the act of *shechita*, not merely the blood of *shechita*, functions as a *hechshier* that renders the animal's meat susceptible to *tumah* without any separate exposure to liquid. That principle applies equally to the dangling limb. Notably, one remains prohibited from eating this limb even after *shechita* — a prohibition derived from a verse, though some hold it to be rabbinic — yet the limb is considered edible enough to become susceptible to *tumah* through the *shechita*.

### Meir vs. Shimon

This is the position of Rabbi Meir: *divrei Rabbi Meir*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees — *Rabbi Shimon omer lo huchshiru* — the *shechita* does not render this dangling limb susceptible. Since the dangling limb cannot be eaten, the *shechita* does not extend to it for purposes of *hechshier*, and it would still require contact with a liquid.

### Death of Animal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתָה הַבְּהֵמָה – הַבָּשָׂר צָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר, הָאֵבֶר מְטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶר נְבֵלָה – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** If **the animal died** without slaughter, **the** hanging **flesh needs** to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity in order to become impure, as its halakhic status is that of flesh severed from a living animal, which is ritually pure and does not have the status of an unslaughtered carcass. **The** hanging **limb imparts impurity as a limb** severed **from a living** animal **but does not impart impurity as the limb of** an unslaughtered **carcass;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Shimon deems** the limb **ritually pure.**

The *mishna* then addresses the case where the animal simply dies rather than being slaughtered: *meta habeheima*. Here, the *mishna* distinguishes between the dangling meat and the dangling limb. Regarding the meat: *habasar tzarich hechshier* — the dangling flesh requires *hechshier* to become susceptible. The reason is notable. One might have expected the dangling flesh to become *nevela* along with the rest of the animal, but because it was not fully attached, it is not swept into *nevela* status with the animal's death. It is as though it had already fallen off before the animal died — and flesh that falls from a living animal is *basar min hachai*, which does not independently convey *tumah*. Therefore this flesh, once separated conceptually from the *nevela*, is simply detached meat that needs *hechshier*.

The limb presents a different issue. Rabbi Meir says: *ha'eiver metamei mishum eiver min hachai ve'eino metamei mishum eiver nevela* — the dangling limb conveys *tumah* as *eiver min hachai*, not as *eiver nevela*. The limb is conceptually understood as having fallen off at the moment of the animal's death — since it was never fully integrated, its separation is deemed to coincide with the animal's dying. Because it falls off in the course of the animal's dying, it is *eiver min hachai*, which carries its own *tumah*. The distinction between *basar min hachai* and *eiver min hachai* is that detached flesh does not independently convey *tumah*, whereas a detached limb does. Rabbi Meir therefore holds that the dangling limb conveys the *tumah* of *eiver min hachai*, not of *nevela*.

### Shimon: Entirely Tahor

Rabbi Shimon holds the more radical position: *Rabbi Shimon metaher* — the dangling limb is entirely *tahor*. It is neither *nevela* nor *eiver min hachai*. As the *Gemara* will examine, Rabbi Shimon's position apparently flows from his view that *shechita* does not apply to this limb at all, which has implications for how the limb's status is understood throughout.

## Gray Zone Detachment

### Tumat Ochlin Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִים – אִין, טוּמְאַת נְבֵלָה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that the limb of an animal that was partially severed and remains hanging from the animal imparts impurity as food if one had intent to eat it. The Gemara infers: It imparts **impurity as food, yes,** but it does **not** impart **the impurity of a carcass,** which can be transmitted to people and utensils in addition to food.

The *Gemara* opens its analysis of the *mishna*: *tumat ochlin ika, tumat nevela leika* — the dangling limb has *tumat ochlin* but not *tumat nevela*. A note is in order: *Tosafot* clarifies that *tumat nevela* as used here is not literal *nevela* — obviously the animal is still alive — but rather refers to *tumat eiver min hachai*, the *tumah* of a limb from a living animal. So: the dangling limb has the *tumah* of food, but not of *eiver min hachai*.

### Healing Capacity Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּמַעֲלִין אֲרוּכָה – אֲפִילּוּ טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִים נָמֵי לָא לִיטַּמּוּ, וְאִי דְּאֵין מַעֲלִין אֲרוּכָה – טוּמְאַת נְבֵלָה נָמֵי לִיטַּמּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** the limb **can heal** and reattach to the animal's body then **it should not be susceptible even to impurity as food. And if it cannot heal, it should impart the impurity of a carcass as well.**

*Heichi dami* — what precisely is the case being described? If the dangling limb could still heal and reattach — *ei demalina arucha* — it should not even be considered food; it is going to become integrated back into the animal. *Ve'ei de'ein malina arucha* — if it cannot heal, one would expect it to be treated as fully detached and therefore to convey *tumat eiver min hachai*. What kind of case yields the intermediate status of *tumat ochlin* only, without *tumat eiver min hachai*?

### Full Detachment Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם, דְּאֵין מַעֲלִין אֲרוּכָה, וְשָׁאנֵי טוּמְאַת נְבֵלָה, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר ״כִּי יִפּוֹל״, עַד שֶׁיִּפּוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna is discussing a case **where** the limb **will not heal, and** the reason that the limb does not impart impurity of a carcass is that **the impurity of a carcass is different** and unique, **as the Merciful One states** with regard to the impurity of a carcass: "And **if** any of their carcass **fall** upon any sowing seed" (Leviticus 11:37), indicating that the severed limb of an animal is not considered a carcass **until it** completely **falls** from the animal.

The answer: *le'olam de'ein malina arucha* — we are speaking of a case where it cannot heal. *V'shani tumat nevela* — but *tumat eiver min hachai* is different. *Rachmana amar ki yipol — ad sheyipol*: the *Torah* requires that something actually *fall off* before it becomes *eiver min hachai*. Because there is still a point of connection, the limb has not yet fully fallen. This is the halakhically meaningful gray zone: the limb is detached enough from the living animal to be no longer considered part of it — and therefore can be categorized as food — but it has not yet physically fallen off, and therefore it has not crossed the threshold into *eiver min hachai* status.

### Hair Strand Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הָאֵבֶר וְהַבָּשָׂר הַמְדוּלְדָּלִין בִּבְהֵמָה וּמְעוֹרִין בְּחוּט הַשַּׂעֲרָה, יָכוֹל יְטַמְּאוּ טוּמְאַת נְבֵלָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יִפּוֹל״ – עַד שֶׁיִּפּוֹל, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִים מְטַמּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **This** explanation **is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **the limb and the flesh** of an animal **that** were partially severed and remain **hanging from the animal and are connected** to the animal **by** a connector the size of **a strand of hair,** one **might** have thought that **they impart the impurity of a carcass.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And if any of the carcass **fall,"** indicating that a severed limb does not impart the impurity of a carcass **until it** completely **falls** from the animal. **And nevertheless,** despite the fact that it is not considered severed with regard to the impurity of a carcass, such a limb is considered severed with regard to **being susceptible to impurity as food.**

*Tanya nami hachi* — a *baraita* confirms this: if a dangling limb or flesh is still intertwined with the animal by even a single hair-strand — *ume'urin bechut hasa'ara* — one might think they already constitute *tumah* as a detached limb. The verse teaches: *yipol — ad sheyipol* — only when it actually falls off. The connection, however tenuous, prevents the full legal detachment needed for *tumat eiver min hachai*.

### Quasi-Food Status

Yet even so — *afilu hachi tumat ochlin metamei* — this limb, despite remaining connected, still conveys the *tumah* of food. This is the middle status: it is quasi-food, because it is regarded as no longer truly integral to the living animal; but it is not yet *eiver min hachai*, because it has not fully separated. The *Gemara* finds an analogy to support this principle.

## Dried Figs Analogy

### Rabbi Chiya Dried Figs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי, דְּאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תְּאֵנִים שֶׁצָּמְקוּ בְּאִיבֵּיהֶן – מְטַמְּאוֹת טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִים, וְהַתּוֹלֵשׁ מֵהֶן בְּשַׁבָּת חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** This explanation **supports** the opinion of **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi, as Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **figs that dried** while still attached to **their tree,** despite the fact that they are still attached, they are considered as if they have been picked and **are susceptible to impurity as food. But** with regard to **one who picks them on Shabbat** they are considered attached, and **he is liable** to bring **a sin offering.** Just as a partially severed limb of an animal is considered both attached and severed with regard to different *halakhot*, so too this dried fruit is considered both attached and detached with regard to different *halakhot*.

*Mesayeia lei leRabbi Chiya bar Ashi* — the *Gemara* notes that this point provides support for a teaching of Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi. *De'amar Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi amar Shmuel*: Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi said in the name of Shmuel — *te'einim shetzamku be'avihen* — figs that have dried out on their stem while still on the tree. Here we have an analogous situation in the plant world. The fruit is dried out — no longer truly integrated into the tree as a growing, living part of it — and yet the stem connecting it to the tree has not dried out. The fruit is therefore in a middle state. The ruling: *hametamei tumat ochlin* — such a fig conveys the *tumah* of food; it is independent enough from the tree to be considered a food item. *V'hatolesh mehem beShabbat chayav chatat* — and yet, one who plucks such a fig on *Shabbat* is liable to bring a *chatat*, a sin-offering, because the act of *tolesh* — detaching from a living plant — still applies. The fig is still connected.

### Parallel Middle Statuses

The parallel between the two cases is precise. In both, something has reached a stage of quasi-independence: it is treated as food for the purposes of *tumat ochlin*, because it is no longer viewed as fully integrated into the parent body. Yet the remaining physical connection is halakhically real: in the animal case, the connection prevents the limb from being *eiver min hachai*; in the plant case, the connection means that removing the fruit on *Shabbat* remains a violation. The gray zone — between full attachment and full detachment — operates in the same way across both domains.

## Drying & Food Status

### Inedible When Dried

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: יְרָקוֹת שֶׁצָּמְקוּ בְּאִיבֵּיהֶן, כְּגוֹן הַכְּרוּב וְהַדַּלַּעַת – אֵין מְטַמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִים; קְצָצָן וְיִבְּשָׁן – מְטַמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say** that a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Okatzin* 2:11) **supports** the opinion of Shmuel, who holds that dried figs still attached to the tree are considered as if they are detached with regard to susceptibility to impurity as food: **Vegetables that dried** while they are attached to **their plant, such as cabbage and gourd,** which become hard as wood and inedible when dried, **are not susceptible to impurity as food.** But if **one cut them** when they were still moist **and** then **dried them** in order to use them for fuel, or, in the case of gourds, to make utensils out of them, **they are susceptible to impurity as food.**

The *Gemara* now tests the boundaries of this principle through a series of cases. *Yerakot shetzimku be'iveihem* — vegetables that have dried out on their vine: specifically, *hakruv vehadilaat*, cabbage and gourd. *Ein mitamin tumas ochlim* — these do not convey *tumat ochlin*. The reason: a dried gourd becomes essentially wooden, and dried cabbage is similarly no longer edible. Since they are no longer fit to eat, they have lost their status as food entirely.

### Intent to Dry Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְצָצָן וְיִבְּשָׁן, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! עֵץ בְּעָלְמָא הוּא! וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: בְּעַל מְנָת לְיַבְּשָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does it enter your mind** that if **one cut them and dried them** they are susceptible to impurity as food? Such a vegetable **is merely wood,** and it is inedible. **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** The *baraita* is discussing a case **where** one cut the vegetables when they were still moist **in order to dry them.** The novelty of the *baraita* is that even though one intends to dry the vegetables and render them inedible, as long as they are still moist they are susceptible to impurity as food.

However: *katzatzan beyamshan* — if one cut them while still fresh with the *intention* of drying them, *mitamin tumas ochlim* — they do convey *tumat ochlin*. But the *Gemara* immediately clarifies: *de'amar Rabbi Yitzchak — be'almenas leyabshan* — this does not mean one literally cut them already dried out, which would be meaningless since dried gourd is essentially wood and not food. What it means is: one cut them while fresh, with the intention of drying them. At the moment of cutting they are still edible and therefore still food; the intention to dry them does not retroactively strip them of their food status. Since they are not yet dry, they remain food.

### Other Fruits Remain Food

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דִּכְרוּב וְדַלַּעַת הוּא, כֵּיוָן דְּיִבְּשָׁן לָאו בְּנֵי אֲכִילָה נִינְהוּ, הָא שְׁאָר פֵּירוֹת מְטַמְּאִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **The reason** for this *halakha* in the *baraita* is **that it is** discussing **cabbage and gourd: Since one dried them, they are inedible** and consequently are not susceptible to impurity as food. **But other** types of **produce,** which are edible when dried, **are susceptible to impurity.**

The implicit reasoning here — *hashar peiros mitamei* — is that other fruits, which retain their edibility even when they dry out (unlike gourds or cabbage), would still be considered food even when dried on the vine and therefore still susceptible to *tumah*. A dried fig, a raisin — these remain edible even after drying, and therefore retain their food status.

### Twig Connection Implication

This raises a further question: if other fruits dried on the vine are still food, what does that mean for their connection to the tree? The *Gemara* asks whether the implication extends to saying that such dried fruit, because still connected by the twig, remains fully *mechubarin* — connected — for all purposes, including *Shabbat*. Would picking such dried fruit on *Shabbat* still constitute *tolesh*? And conversely, if we consider it detached enough for *tumah*, might we also consider it detached enough that picking it on *Shabbat* would not be a violation?

### Mishna's Main Point

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם הֵן וְעוּקְצֵיהֶן, וּקְצָצָן עַל מְנָת לְיַבְּשָׁן אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this interpretation: The *baraita* is not necessarily discussing that case. **Actually,** the *baraita* is discussing a case where both the produce **itself and its stems** were dried. **And** although it appears that the *halakha* is obvious in such a case, **it was necessary for** the *baraita* to mention it in order to teach the latter clause of the *baraita*: In a case where one **cut** the cabbage and gourd when they were still moist **in order to dry them,** they are susceptible to impurity as long as they are still moist.

The *Gemara* resolves the uncertainty by drawing back: *dechi katani amansi yavesh itztricha lei* — the point of the *mishna* is not the implied alternative at all. The *mishna*'s purpose is to state the case of the gourd cut while fresh with intent to dry: since it has not yet dried, it remains food and is susceptible to *tumah*. The *mishna* is not making any broader statement about other fruits dried on the vine, and we cannot draw halakhic conclusions about *Shabbat* or *tumah* from what might appear to be the implied alternative.

## Branch Severance

### Fruit on Cut Branch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אִילָן שֶׁנִּפְשַׁח וּבוֹ פֵּירוֹת – הֲרֵי הֵן כִּתְלוּשִׁין, יָבְשׁוּ – הֲרֵי הֵן כִּמְחוּבָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a challenge to the opinion of Shmuel from a *baraita*: In the case of **a tree from which a branch broke off, and** the branch **has fruit** attached to it, even if the fruit is still moist **it is considered detached** from the tree. But if the branch did not break off, and the fruit **dried** on the tree, **it is considered attached.**

A *baraita* is cited to clarify the distinction between severing a branch and drying fruit on a connected branch: *ilan shenifshach ubo peirot — harei hen ketlushin* — if a branch is broken off from a tree and it has fruit hanging from it, those fruits are considered *ketlushin*, fully detached, and are susceptible to *tumah* as food items. Cutting off an apple branch with apples attached renders those apples legally independent from the tree.

### Dried Fruit, Connected Branch

Conversely: *yaveshu — harei hen kemechubarin* — if the fruit dried out but the branch remains connected to the tree, they are still considered *mechubarin*, connected, for all purposes. The branch's continued connection to the tree preserves the fruit's legal status as attached, even if the fruit itself has dried.

### Categories Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי אִירְיָא? הָא כִּדְאִיתָא, וְהָא כִּדְאִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this challenge: **Are the cases comparable? This** case is **as it is, and that** case is **as it is.** In the first clause of the *baraita*, the fruit on the detached branch is considered detached with regard to all matters. In the latter clause of the *baraita*, the dried fruit on the tree is considered attached with regard to Shabbat but detached with regard to impurity.

*Midi irya — ha kide'ita veha kide'ita* — these cases do not contradict one another; each operates according to its own category. When a branch is severed, the severing of the branch creates full legal detachment for the fruit, and this applies across all halakhic domains — both for *tumah* susceptibility and for *Shabbat*. When fruit dries on a connected branch, the branch's connection to the tree remains legally operative for all purposes. The two cases differ not by degree but by category: branch severance is a different halakhic event from fruit drying, and each carries its own complete set of consequences.

## Gray Zone Synthesis

### Middle Status Principle

What the *Gemara*'s extended discussion demonstrates — across the dangling limb, the dried fig, the gourd, and the severed branch — is that the category of *middle status* between connection and detachment is halakhically meaningful and not merely a transitional curiosity. The dangling limb that is no longer truly part of the animal but has not yet fallen off; the fig that has dried sufficiently to be food but is still connected enough that removing it on *Shabbat* is *tolesh* — these represent a genuine halakhic category, not a gap in the law. The *Gemara* is fascinated by precisely this gray zone, and the parallel between the animal and plant cases is deliberate.

### Impurity Despite Prohibition

A final, striking observation emerges. The dangling limb — whether *eiver min hachai* or simply dangling flesh — is prohibited for consumption by everyone. It is *eiver min hachai* or *basar min hachai*, forbidden not only to Jews but to *bnei Noach* as well. And yet it can be *tamei* with *tumat ochlin* — the *tumah* of food. The category of food susceptible to impurity does not require that the food be permitted to any human being. Something can be, for purposes of *tumah*, a food item — and therefore subject to all the rules of *tumat ochlin* — even when there is no one for whom it is *mutar* to eat it. This is, as the lecturer notes, quite a remarkable halakhic result.
