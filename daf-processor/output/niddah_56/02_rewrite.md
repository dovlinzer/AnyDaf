# Niddah 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zav Fluids

### Dam Ha-Yotzei

The daf opens five lines from the top of 56a, at the words *dam ha-yotzei*. The broader context is the *mishnah* that surveys various substances — primarily, though not exclusively, bodily fluids — that are *tamei*, and addresses whether they convey *tumah* only when moist or even when dried. At this juncture, the *Gemara* is examining the fluids of a *zav*.

The *Torah* mentions spit explicitly as a source of *tumah* conveyed by a *zav*. By means of an additional inclusive word in the *pasuk*, the category is extended to include phlegm and nasal mucus. Urine is then included through an analogical derivation: first phlegm and mucus are compared to spit, and then spit is compared to the primary flow of *zivah* from the penis, thereby drawing urine into the same category. Blood, however, even if it were to emerge from the penis, is excluded and is *tahor*. The *Gemara* now sets out to explain the conceptual basis for these distinctions.

### Inclusion Rules

The *Gemara* asks: how do we know that blood emerging from the mouth or from the penis is *tahor*? A *baraita* provides the answer. One might have thought — *yakhol yehei dam ha-yotzei mi-piv u-mi-pi ha-amah tamei* — that blood from either location should be *tamei*, given that other fluids from those same locations are. The verse therefore specifies: *zovo tamei hu* — *hu*, "it," a limiting particle: only the flow itself is *tamei*, to the exclusion of blood emerging from mouth or penis. That exclusion is established by the word *hu*.

But this immediately raises the inverse question: *ve-eippukh ana* — why not reverse the derivation? Why not say that blood is *tamei* and the other fluids — phlegm, mucus, urine — are *tahor*? How do we know which items to include and which to exclude?

### Blood Exclusion

The *Gemara* cites a principle stated by Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai: the operative criterion is *dumya de-rok* — analogy to spit, which the *Torah* explicitly designates as a source of *tumah* for a *zav*. From spit we derive the defining characteristic: *mah rok she-mit'agel ve-yotzei* — spit is a fluid that collects within the body before it exits. Accordingly, *af kol she-mit'agel ve-yotzei* — any fluid that similarly collects before coming out is included. Blood, by contrast, does not operate this way: when the body bleeds, whether internally or from a surface wound, the blood does not gather and pool before emerging. Hence *yatza dam* — blood is excluded.

This is the conceptual framework. The *Torah* explicitly identifies *zivah* and spit as sources of *tumah*, and additional *pesukim* extend the category. But the abstracted criterion — collection prior to emission — is what determines which further substances fall within the category and which fall outside it.

### Spit Analogy

The *Gemara* immediately presses: granted that blood is excluded because it does not collect, what of a nursing woman's milk — *chalav she-be-ishah*? Milk certainly collects before it emerges. Yet we know from an earlier teaching — *ve-amar mar* — that mother's milk does not carry the severe *tumah* of a primary fluid of a *zavah*. It is *tamei* only as a liquid that has received *tumah* from a *zavah* (*tumat mashkin*), but it is not itself an *av ha-tumah* in the manner of spit. The challenge stands: if the criterion is simply *mit'agel ve-yotzei*, milk should be included.

### Milk Problem

Rabbi Yochanan, still citing Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai, accordingly refines the criterion. The analogy to spit is more demanding than first stated. Spit has two defining characteristics: first, it collects before being expelled (*mit'agel ve-yotzei*); and second, it can be swallowed back — *ve-chozer ve-nivla* — it is capable of reabsorption into the body if not expelled. This second criterion is what excludes mother's milk. Although milk collects before it emerges (*she-af al pi she-mit'agel ve-yotzei*), it cannot be reabsorbed back into the body (*eino chozer ve-nivla*). Hence *yatza chalav she-be-ishah* — mother's milk is excluded.

### Two Criteria

This dual criterion — collection and reabsorption — resolves the case of milk, but it seems to create a problem with urine. Urine is included within the category of *tamei* fluids of a *zav*, yet can urine really be said to be reabsorbed if not expelled? Rashi addresses this directly, noting in the narrow column that *silon shel mei raglayim chozer ve-nivla* — the stream of urine can be reabsorbed. The lecturer acknowledges this is difficult to understand physiologically. When urination is interrupted, the urine does not literally return into the body; one simply stops urinating. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* appears to accept this distinction.

The harder conceptual question is why urine is treated differently from milk. The *Gemara* never offers an explicit account of this, and the lecturer notes that the *meiтav* commentary does not resolve the asymmetry either. What the *Gemara* does presuppose is that both criteria — collection and potential reabsorption — apply to urine but not to milk, even if the physiological basis for that distinction is not entirely transparent.

A further question arises about *tzichtzuchei zivah* — drops or secretions associated with the flow. The *Gemara* elsewhere states *ee efshar belo tzichtzuchei zivah* — one cannot have a flow of *zivah* without accompanying droplets. Whether *tzichtzuchei* are themselves considered one of the primary *ma'ayanot* of a *zav* — with the consequence of being *tamei be-masa* and *tamei le-vigadim* — or whether they are *tamei* in a lesser sense only, is left as a question requiring further investigation.

There is also a broader hermeneutic point worth noting. The *Gemara* never returns to discuss nasal mucus with full rigor. Whether mucus is included purely through the abstract category of *mit'agel ve-yotzei*, or whether it requires its own independent *pasuk*, remains somewhat open. The underlying tension is between how much work the abstract criterion can do on its own and how much independent textual support each inclusion requires.

One might further ask: why is "natural secretion" not the defining criterion, rather than collection and reabsorption? Spit, phlegm, and urine are natural; blood from the penis would be pathological; and the very *zivah* that makes one a *zav* is itself unnatural. This observation actually reinforces why the *zivah* itself cannot be used as the model for abstraction — which leads directly to the next sub-discussion.

### Flow Challenge

The *Gemara* raises a pointed objection: *ve-nilef mi-zovo* — why not derive the criterion from the *zivah* itself, the primary flow that makes the person a *zav*? The *zivah* does not possess the characteristic of *mit'agel ve-yotzei* — it does not collect before coming out. If we were to abstract from the *zivah*, we would derive a criterion that is the opposite of the one derived from spit, and we would wind up including things that do not collect. This seems to undermine the entire framework.

### Rava Response

Rava resolves the difficulty decisively: *mi-zovo leika le-meylaf* — there is no valid derivation from the *zivah* flow itself, because *she-kein gorem tumah le-achreim* — the *zivah* is what creates the person's status as a *zav* in the first place. It is a categorically different phenomenon: it is the source of his *tumah* identity, not a fluid he conveys *after* becoming a *zav*. When we ask which fluids an already-established *zav* transmits as an *av ha-tumah*, the model must be spit — the fluid the *Torah* explicitly designates for that purpose — not the *zivah* itself, which operates in a different register entirely.

This resolution, as the lecturer notes, actually lends further support to the question about "natural secretion" as a potential criterion. Once the *zivah* is set aside precisely because it is the act that constitutes the *zav* — not merely a fluid he conveys — we are left with spit as the exclusive model. And spit is a natural secretion. That "natural" is not, in the end, treated as the operative criterion remains something of an open question.

## Sheretz Status

### Reish Lakish

The *Gemara* now turns to the case of a dead rodent — *sheretz* — which the *Torah* identifies as a source of *tumah*. The *mishnah*'s point is not simply that it conveys *tumah* but that this applies only when the rodent is moist, not when it has dried out. Against the plain reading of the *mishnah*, Reish Lakish states: *sheretz she-yavesh ve-shildo kayemet tamei* — a rodent that has completely desiccated, if its form is still intact, is still *tamei*.

This position invites a conceptual question. Why should drying remove *tumah* at all? Two frameworks are possible. One might hold that a dried-out substance is simply no longer the same entity — it has lost its identity as a rodent. Alternatively, one might hold that it remains the same entity but loses its *significance* — its halachic weight — once dried. The fact that the *mishnah* uses the test of reconstitution in water (if the substance can be refreshed, it is not considered fully dried) might suggest the first framework: it is a question of identity. But Reish Lakish's ruling — that retaining the *shape* is sufficient even when the substance is desiccated — seems clearly to operate within the second framework. A dried rodent that still looks like a rodent has not lost its significance, even though it has lost moisture. It is not "dirt in the shape of a rodent," but a rodent that has retained enough of its form to retain its halachic weight.

### Whole vs Part

This significance-based framework is reinforced by the *Gemara*'s next step. The *mishnah* is cited: *metamin lachim ve-ein metamin yeveshim* — moist rodents convey *tumah*, dried ones do not. Rabbi Zeira resolves the tension with Reish Lakish: *lo kashya — ha be-kullan, ha be-miktzatan* — there is no contradiction. The distinction turns on whether one has the entire rodent or only a part of it.

The minimum quantity required for a *sheretz* to convey *tumah* is normally *ke-adasha* — the size of a lentil (or bean). If one has only a piece of a dried rodent that meets this minimum, it no longer conveys *tumah*. But if one has the entire rodent, even dried out, it still conveys *tumah* — provided its shape is retained.

This distinction parallels a principle known elsewhere in *hilchot tumah*: a *beriah* — an entire creature — conveys *tumah* even if it is smaller than the standard minimum quantity. The qualitative significance of wholeness can substitute for the quantitative threshold. Here too, the conceptual point is that significance can be conveyed either quantitatively (a piece that meets the minimum size) or qualitatively (the entirety of the creature retaining its shape). When a piece dries out, it loses the quantitative significance it once had — it is now just a small fragment of dried matter. But the whole creature, shape intact, retains the qualitative significance that marks it as a *sheretz*.

### Baraita

A *baraita* lends textual support to this framework. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Rabbi Bisni cites Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: the *Torah* uses two formulations — *bahem* ("in them," meaning the entirety) and *mehem* ("from them," meaning a portion). A *talmud lomar* argument resolves the tension: *kan be-lach, kan be-yavesh* — one verse addresses the moist case (where a minimum-sized piece suffices) and the other addresses the dried case (where the entirety is required). This textual derivation anchors Reish Lakish's ruling in the *Torah* itself.

### Shape Significance

Rava applies the principle concretely. He observes that the *zivugim* of Machuza — a species of rodent common in the Babylonian city where Rava resided, and which apparently were frequently found dried out in public spaces — *ke-shildan kayemet, tme'im*: as long as their form is retained, they are *tamei*. Rava is simply applying the theoretical principle to a practical, everyday case.

### Burned Sheretz

Reish Lakish then extends the ruling in a striking direction: *sheretz she-nisraf, ve-shildon kayemet, tme'im* — a rodent that has been burned, if its form is still recognizable, is still *tamei*. The more natural reading of "burned" here refers to charring rather than complete incineration (which would reduce the object to ash and destroy any recognizable form), but the case is nonetheless arresting. A charred rodent whose skeletal shape remains intact retains its *tumah*.

### Current State

An objection is raised from a *mishnah* in *Taharos*: if a burned rodent is found lying on olives (of *terumah* or other *tahara*-sensitive produce), the olives are *tahor* — *kol ha-tme'im ke-sha'at metzi'atan*, things are assessed according to their state at the time of discovery. Since the rodent is found already burned, we assume it was already burned when it fell, and therefore did not convey *tumah*. Does this not suggest that a burned rodent is *tahor*? Similarly, a tattered garment that was once *tamei* but has deteriorated beyond halachic recognition — *matlis ha-muha* — is treated as *tahor* at the time of discovery.

### Entirety Rule

Rav Zeira resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya — ha be-kulah, ha be-miktzatah* — the same whole-versus-part distinction applies. The *mishnah* in *Taharos* speaks of a burned rodent where only a portion remains, which is insufficient once dried or charred; Reish Lakish speaks of the entire rodent with its shape intact. A *baraita* confirms this by presenting the same *bahem/mehem* textual derivation applied now to *saruf* versus *she-eino saruf* — burned versus unburned — rather than to dried versus moist.

The conceptual unity is clear. Whether the diminishment is drying or burning, the operative question is whether halachic significance is retained. Significance can persist even through drying or charring, but only when the entire creature's form is present. A portion, once dried or charred, is simply insignificant matter — neither quantitatively sufficient nor qualitatively whole.

## Freshness Rule

### Torah Sources

The *Gemara* now surveys the textual sources establishing that all these substances — *zav* fluid, spit, rodent, semen — convey *tumah* only when fresh, not when dried out. For the *zav* flow, the verse states *rar besaro* — it flows in his flesh, implying a liquid state. For phlegm and spit, the verse says *ki yarok ha-zav ke-eyn rok* — when the *zav* spits, it must be *ke-eyn rok*, resembling actual spit — that is, in liquid form. For the *sheretz*, the verse says *be-motam* — "in their death" — and the *Gemara* reads this as requiring the substance to be *ke-eyn mitah*, as it is at the moment of death — still relatively fresh.

### Semen Fitness

For semen, the source is the term *shichvat zera* itself, understood as *ha-re'uya le-hazria* — that which is capable of causing conception. Dried semen cannot cause conception, and therefore it is no longer *tamei*. This raises an interesting contemporary question: is dried semen truly incapable of causing conception? The lecturer notes that the *Gemara* did not have knowledge of sperm as a distinct biological entity. The *Gemara* elsewhere considers whether semen that emerges in a powerful stream is presumed more capable of causing conception, and *Tosafot* addresses this, arguing that *ra'uya le-hazria* refers to the intrinsic quality of the substance rather than the manner of its propulsion. The broader point, as the lecturer notes, is one the *Gemara* itself implicitly raises throughout: the selection of defining criteria is always an act of interpretation. The question of which properties of semen make it *ra'uya le-hazria* — for purposes of *tumah* — is exactly the same kind of question as which properties of spit define the category of *zav* fluids.

### Reconstitution

The *mishnah* offers a practical test for determining whether a substance has dried sufficiently to lose *tumah*: if it can be soaked in lukewarm water (*poshrin*) and reconstituted, it is not considered dried. This is the test for semen, *sheretz*, and related substances.

### Lukewarm Duration

A question is posed by Rami bar Chama: must the water remain lukewarm for the entire twenty-four-hour soaking period, or is it sufficient that it began as lukewarm even if it cooled subsequently? The *baraita* resolves this as a *tannaitic* dispute. Yehuda ben Nekusa holds: twenty-four hours is required, but it suffices that the water *began* as lukewarm — *techilato af al pi she-ein sofo*. Rabbi Akiva holds more stringently: the water must remain lukewarm throughout the full twenty-four hours — *tzarich she-yehu poshrin me'et le-et*.

### Corpse Flesh

The *tanna kamma* holds that flesh of a corpse — *basar ha-met* — conveys *tumah* even when dried. Rabbi Yosi dissents, ruling that dried corpse flesh is *tahor*.

### Shmuel's View

Shmuel clarifies Rabbi Yosi's position. Rabbi Yosi does not hold that dried corpse flesh is entirely *tahor* in all respects. Rather, *tahor mil-tamei be-khezayit* — it no longer conveys the standard *tumah* of a *kezayit* of corpse flesh, which is what the *tanna kamma* maintained. But *mitamei tum'at rekev* — it does still convey *tumah* as *rekev*, the specially defined category of decomposed corpse matter, which operates under its own parameters (requiring isolation in a sealed marble casket and measured in a distinct quantity). A *baraita* confirms this reading of Rabbi Yosi: the dried flesh is *tahor mil-tamei be-khezayit, aval mitamei tum'at rekev*.

## Stains Overview

### Rodent Timeline

The *Gemara* now pivots to the *mishnah* that will dominate the discussion for some time: the question of stains found on women's garments and the retroactive *tumah* they generate. A parallel case introduces the principle: *sheretz she-nimtza ba-mavoi* — a dead rodent found in an alleyway. If a person passed through and wishes to know whether they became *tamei* by stepping on it, the ruling is *tamei le-mafrea* — *tamei* retroactively. How far back? Until the person can say: *badakti et ha-mavoi ha-zeh ve-lo hayah bo sheretz* — I checked this alleyway and found no rodent — or *ad she-kibbud* — until the last time the alleyway was swept. Either a positive check or an act of sweeping constitutes the terminal point beyond which the rodent is not assumed to have been present.

### Stain Timeline

The parallel rule for stains: *ve-chen ketem she-nimtza be-chaluk* — a stain found on a woman's garment — *metamei le-mafrea* — conveys *tumah* retroactively. The terminal point is either her stating *badakti et ha-chaluk ha-zeh ve-lo hayah bo ketem* — I checked this garment and it was clean — or *ad she-kibbud* — until the last laundering. The *mishnah* was cited earlier in the tractate; here is where it originally appears.

A practical note: these rules raise the difficult question of what precisely becomes *tamei*. The garment itself, anyone who touches it, and, most significantly, the woman herself — who must assume she was a *niddah* from as far back as the terminal point. This retroactive personal status is the gravest consequence of the rule.

### Niddah Blood

The *mishnah* further states: *u-metamei bein lach bein yavesh* — a stain of *niddah* blood conveys *tumah* whether moist or dried. This, as established earlier in the *daf*, is the distinctive rule for *niddah* blood: unlike the other substances surveyed in this *mishnah*, its *tumah* is not conditioned on freshness.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon offers a compelling qualification: *ha-yavesh metamei le-mafrea, ve-ha-lach eino metamei ella ad sheyehei yakhol lihyot lach* — the dried stain conveys *tumah* retroactively to the full extent; but the fresh (moist) stain conveys *tumah* only as far back as it is plausible that it could still be fresh at the point of discovery. A dried stain could have been sitting there for weeks — back to the last check or laundering. But a fresh stain, by its very freshness, limits the temporal window: it cannot have been there longer than the time during which it could remain fresh. Rabbi Shimon's position is straightforwardly logical: the physical state of the stain itself serves as evidence of timing.

The converse implication is also noted: if a stain appears dried, one might reason that it *could* have been more recent. But this matters less, because even a dried stain from some time ago is still *tamei* — as established, *niddah* blood conveys *tumah* even dried. The *Rashash* clarifies that "dried" in this context does not necessarily mean dried to the extreme point of *taharah*; it simply means no longer fresh.

## Check Debate

### Sweep Mechanism

The *Gemara* now analyzes the mechanism behind the rule that sweeping serves as a terminal point. *Ad she-kibbud — chazkato baduk*: why does sweeping establish the endpoint? Two possibilities are raised: either because *chazkato baduk* — we presume that a person conducting a sweep is also actively inspecting; or because *chazkato mitkabed* — the act of sweeping itself naturally dislodges and removes anything in the *mavoi*, irrespective of whether the sweeper was looking.

### Hole Scenario

The practical difference between these two rationales is significant. If someone says *kibbadti ve-lo badakti* — I swept but I was not looking — the outcome depends on which rationale applies. If the terminal point rests on the presumption of active inspection, then an admission of non-inspection eliminates the terminal point, and *tumah* must be assumed retroactively from an earlier time. If the terminal point rests on the mechanical effect of sweeping itself, the admission does not matter.

A second *nafka mina* is the case of *de-ishtakach be-guma* — the rodent was found in a hole or crevice. If the rationale is active inspection (*chazkato baduk*), we presume the inspector checked even the crevices, and finding the rodent there now means it arrived after the sweep. If the rationale is mechanical displacement (*chazkato mitkabed*), a sweeping broom does not reach into a hole — *guma lo mitkabed* — and the rodent in the hole could have been there before the sweep without being dislodged.

### Laundry Mechanism

The identical question is applied to laundering as a terminal point for stains. Does laundering serve as a terminal point because *chazkato baduk* — we presume the woman inspects the garment at the time of laundering — or because *chazkato mitkabes* — the laundering process itself will naturally remove any stain that is present, irrespective of inspection?

### Fold Scenario

Again, two practical differences emerge. First: if the woman says *kibasti ve-lo badakti* — I laundered but did not inspect — does she lose the terminal point? If the rationale is active inspection, yes; if the rationale is automatic removal through laundering, no. Second: *de-ishtakach be-sitra* — if the stain is found in a fold of the garment. If the rationale is inspection (*chazkato baduk*), we presume the inspector checked even the folds — *man de-baduk be-sitra nami baduk* — and a stain found there now must have arrived post-laundering. If the rationale is automatic removal (*chazkato mitkabes*), a fold does not necessarily get laundered through — *be-sitra lo mitkabes* — and the stain could have persisted there through the laundering undetected.

### Rabbi Meir

The *Gemara* resolves the question from a *baraita* citing Rabbi Meir. He asks: *mipnei mah amru ha-sheretz she-nimtza ba-mavoi metamei le-mafrea* — why is it that we declare retroactive *tumah* from the time of the sweep? His answer: *mipnei she-chazkat bnei Yisrael bodkin mavo'oteihen bi-sh'at kibudan* — because Jews, as a matter of established practice (*chazakah*), inspect their courtyards as they sweep. The rationale is explicitly inspection, not mechanical displacement. The same reasoning applies to stains: *mipnei she-chazkat bnot Yisrael bodkot chalukeihen bi-sh'at kibuseihen* — Jewish women inspect their garments at the time of laundering.

### No Checking Loss

The consequence follows directly: *ve-im lo badku hifsidu le-mafrea* — if they concede that they did not inspect during the sweeping or laundering, they forfeit the protection of that terminal point and must consider *tumah* as potentially reaching back indefinitely to the prior check. Rabbi Meir's position is unambiguous: it is the active inspection, not the mechanical act, that creates the terminal point.

## Stain Age

### Rav Acha

Even granting that an active inspection must have taken place for the terminal point to apply, the *Gemara* now offers practical tools for assessing a stain's age independent of whether inspection occurred. Rav Acha states: *tachzor ve-tis-chabsenu* — re-launder the garment. *Im nidchah mar'ito* — if the stain fades upon second laundering, *be-yadu'a she-le'achar kibus* — it is established that this is a fresh stain, one that arrived after the last laundering, because a fresh stain will visibly fade when first washed. *Ve-im lav* — if it does not fade further, *be-yadu'a she-lifnei ha-kibus* — it is established that the stain predates the laundering, having already been subjected to laundering once before.

This is a reasonable empirical test. A stain that has already been laundered acquires a characteristically faded, set appearance; further laundering changes it little. A fresh stain, by contrast, will fade noticeably on first washing.

## Stain Age…

### Rebbi View

Rebbi offers a refinement that does not even require re-laundering. *Eino domeh ketem she-le'achar ha-kibus le-ketem she-lifnei ha-kibus* — a post-laundering stain does not resemble a pre-laundering stain to the trained eye. The distinction: *zeh makdir ve-zeh maglid* — one penetrates into the fabric (a stain that has set through previous laundering is embedded into the fibers); the other forms a crust (*maglid*), sitting on the surface with visible substance. A fresh stain has not yet been worked into the garment and retains surface texture; a previously laundered stain has lost that and appears only as a deep coloring of the fabric.

### Inference

From these testing methods, the *Gemara* draws an inference confirming the earlier resolution: *chazkato baduk — shema mina*. The very existence of methods for distinguishing fresh from previously laundered stains presupposes that the operative reason for laundering serving as a terminal point is inspection (*chazkato baduk*). If the terminal point depended merely on the mechanical act of laundering, there would be no need to distinguish the age of the stain — the act itself would have resolved the matter. The fact that we need to test whether the stain is pre- or post-laundering confirms that the terminal point is grounded in the presumption of active inspection.

## Origin Analysis

### Rekem Rule

A new *mishnah* opens the discussion that will dominate this section: *kol ha-ketamim ha-ba'im mi-Rekem tehorim* — all stains originating from Rekem, a particular city in Israel, are *tahor*. Rekem was a city whose population was predominantly non-Jewish, and stains belonging to non-Jews do not carry the *halachic tumah* of *niddah* stains, which applies — both as *Torah* law and rabbinically — to Jewish women. The stains found in Rekem are presumed to come from non-Jews and are therefore *tahor*.

### Yehuda Exception

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *metamei* — the stains are *tamei*. His reasoning: *mipnei she-hen gerim ve-toim* — because they are converts who err. The converts in Rekem are technically Jews but are not sufficiently careful about the laws of *tumah* and *taharah* to put away stained garments properly. Therefore, one cannot presume that all freely circulating stains in that city come from non-Jews; there are careless Jewish converts whose stains may be found. Accordingly, Rabbi Yehuda rules those stains *tamei*.

### General Rule

The general principle underlying the *mishnah*: stains from among non-Jews are *tahor*; stains attributed to Jews are *tamei*. But the determination of which stains one encounters in practice depends on where one finds them and who inhabits the area.

### Mixed Cities

For cities with a mixed population of Jews and non-Jews, the *mishnah* addresses the question of attribution. The general rule is that one follows the majority population, or considers the particular characteristics of the inhabitants. The critical variable, as the *mishnah* frames it, is not merely numerical majority but the *carefulness* of the Jewish residents: Jews who are careful with their stained garments — who put them away and do not allow them to circulate — effectively remove their stains from the pool of stains one might encounter. The presumption that careful Jews have removed their potentially *tamei* stains from public circulation means that stains found in mixed cities can often be attributed to the non-Jewish population.

### Convert Status

The *mishnah*'s closing section addresses Samaritans (*Kutim*). The *Gemara* reconstructs the *mishnah* through a *chisuri mechsara*: the text is incomplete and requires supplement. The full reading: if a stain is known to come from a Jew, it is *tamei*; if it is known to come from a Samaritan, the question depends on the disputed status of Samaritans. Rabbi Meir rules it *tamei* — *d'Kutim gerei emet hein* — Samaritans are genuine converts and therefore Jews in all respects, making their stains *tamei*. The *Chachamim* rule it *tahor* — *d'Kutim gerei arayot hein* — Samaritans converted only out of fear of lions sent by God, not from genuine religious conviction, and are therefore not considered Jews; their stains are *tahor* as the stains of non-Jews.

### Carefulness Rule

The *Gemara* notes that if the dispute were purely about Samaritan legal status — Jew versus non-Jew — the *mishnah*'s phrasing, *she-lo nechshdu al kitmeihen* (they are not suspect of mishandling their stains), would be irrelevant. The question of status and the question of carefulness are distinct. The *Gemara* therefore re-reads the *mishnah*: when the stain is *known* to belong to a Jew or Samaritan, it is *tamei* in either case, since even the *Chachamim* agree Samaritans are *gerei emet* for purposes of establishing identity of ownership. The real question in the latter part of the *mishnah* is about stains *found* in various locations without known attribution. In a Jewish city, even found stains are *tahor* — *she-lo nechshdu al kitmeihen ve-tzni'ei matni lehu* — because Jews are careful with their stains and put them away, so any stain encountered in the street is presumed to come from a non-Jewish passerby or from some non-*niddah* source.

### Samaritans

For stains found in Samaritan cities — *ha-nimtza'im be-arei Kutim* — Rabbi Meir rules *tamei*: *d'nechshdu al kitmeihen* — Samaritans are not careful with their stains and allow *tamei* stains to circulate. The *Chachamim* rule *tahor*: *she-lo nechshdu al kitmeihen* — Samaritans are as careful with their stains as Jews.

The principle that emerges from the entire discussion: the *tumah* of a found stain is not determined solely by the *halachic* status of who produced it (Jew versus non-Jew), but by the combination of status and carefulness. A *tamei* stain must be both from a Jew (or legal equivalent) *and* from someone who would allow such a stain to circulate. Because careful Jews put their stained garments away, one cannot infer from the mere discovery of a stain in a Jewish environment that it comes from a negligent Jewish woman. The special cases — Rekem's converts, Samaritan cities — are the cases where this combination of Jewish status and lack of carefulness may coexist, and those are precisely the cases the *mishnah* singles out for attention.
