# Bava Metzia 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mari bar Isak Case

### Brother's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָרִי בַּר אִיסַק אֲתָא לֵיהּ אַחָא מִבֵּי חוֹזָאֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּלוֹג לִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָדַעְנָא לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Mari bar Isak, who was a wealthy and powerful man, had a brother whom he did not previously know, come to him from Bei Ḥozai, which was distant from central Babylonia. His brother said to him: Divide the property that you inherited from our father and give half to me, as I am your brother. Mari said to him: I do not know who you are.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר לָךְ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּכֵּר יוֹסֵף אֶת אֶחָיו וְהֵם לֹא הִכִּרֻהוּ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁיָּצָא בְּלֹא חֲתִימַת זָקָן וּבָא בַּחֲתִימַת זָקָן.
>
> **Translation:** The case came before Rav Ḥisda. He said to the brother: Mari bar Isak spoke well to you, as it is stated: "And Joseph knew his brothers and they knew him not" (Genesis 42:8). This teaches that Joseph left Eretz Yisrael without the trace of a beard, and he came with the trace of a beard. This proves that it is possible for brothers not to recognize each other. Mari bar Isak may be telling the truth when he claims he does not recognize you.

The daf begins toward the bottom of 39b with the story of Mari bar Isak. A man arrived from Bei Chuzai claiming to be Mari bar Isak's brother and demanding half the estate. Mari bar Isak responded simply: *la yadeina lach* — I don't know who you are.

The case came before Rav Chisda, who said to the claimant that Mari bar Isak's denial was not necessarily dishonest. He cited the *pasuk*: *Vayaker Yosef et echav vehem lo hikiruhu* — Yosef recognized his brothers, but they did not recognize him. The Gemara explains: *melamed sheyatza belo chatimat zakan uva bechatimat zakan* — he left without a beard and returned with one. So too here, perhaps Mari bar Isak left when this supposed brother was very young, or perhaps he did not even know his father had a child elsewhere. His denial is therefore not proof of bad faith.

Tosafot notes that this point is not meant to suggest that anyone claiming to be a brother is automatically to be believed. Obviously a stranger cannot collect on a bare assertion of kinship. Rather, the Gemara is clarifying that Mari bar Isak's denial does not itself disprove the claim — it simply means the claimant must bring proof.

### Burden of Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי סָהֲדִי דַּאֲחוּהּ אַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda said to the brother: Go bring witnesses that you are his brother.

Rav Chisda therefore told the claimant: go bring witnesses that you are indeed his brother. This is entirely reasonable — the principle of *hamotzi me-chavero alav ha-rayah* places the burden of proof on the one seeking to extract from another's possession.

### Witness Coercion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִית לִי סָהֲדִי, וְדָחֲלִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּגַבְרָא אַלִּימָא הוּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ: זִיל אַנְתְּ אַיְיתִי סָהֲדֵי דְּלָאו אֲחוּךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The brother said to him: I have witnesses, but they fear Mari bar Isak because he is a violent man. Rav Ḥisda said to Mari bar Isak: You go bring witnesses that he is not your brother.

The claimant responded: *it li sahedi u-dechali minei* — I have witnesses, but they are afraid of Mari bar Isak, who is *gavra alma hu*, a violent and powerful man. Because of his reputation for intimidation, witnesses will not come forward.

Rav Chisda then turned to Mari bar Isak and said: in that case, *you* bring witnesses that this man is *not* your brother. This is a striking move. Proving a negative is inherently difficult — what witnesses can establish with certainty that this claimant was never known as the son of the same father? One could imagine bringing people from the claimant's place of origin who could establish that he was never known to have this man as his father, or that he never claimed this ancestry, which would at least create a strong presumption against the claim.

### Judicial Discretion and Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּינָא הָכִי?! הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי דָּיְינִינָא לָךְ וּלְכֹל אַלִּימֵי דְּחַבְרָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Mari bar Isak said to him: Is this the *halakha*? Isn't there a principle in these cases that the burden of proof rests upon the claimant? Rav Ḥisda said to him: This is the way I judge you and all of your fellow violent people.

Mari bar Isak challenged this ruling: *dina hachi?* — is this really the law? Is not *hamotzi me-chavero alav ha-rayah* the governing principle? Why must he disprove the claim? Rav Chisda answered: *hachi daina lach u-lechol alimei de-chavrach* — yes, this is how I will judge you and all men of violence like you.

The issue was not simply that witnesses were unavailable — it was that the claimant asserted they existed but were being silenced through intimidation. This gave some credibility to the claim. Rav Chisda was exercising judicial discretion in response to what appeared to be witness suppression. Tosafot emphasizes, however, that this cannot become a general rule permitting anyone to sue a powerful man simply by claiming to have frightened witnesses — the specific circumstances here lent the claim particular weight.

### False Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סוֹף סוֹף אָתוּ סָהֲדִי וְלָא מַסְהֲדִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תַּרְתֵּי לָא עָבְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** Mari bar Isak said to him: Ultimately, if that is your concern, witnesses will come, and they will not testify in his favor. They will lie and testify in my favor. Rav Ḥisda said to him: They will not perform two wrongs; they will not refrain from telling the truth and also testify falsely.

Mari bar Isak then raised a further objection: *sof sof, ati sahadi velo mesahadi* — even if he brings witnesses, what good will it do? If Rav Chisda suspects him of intimidating witnesses into silence, won't those witnesses be equally suspect? Rav Chisda responded: *tarti lo avdi* — witnesses will not do two things. They might be cowed into silence, but witnesses will not actively testify *falsely* out of fear. If witnesses come and testify, their testimony can be trusted.

### Witnesses Confirm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף אֲתוֹ סָהֲדִי דַּאֲחוּהּ הוּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לִפְלוֹג לִי נָמֵי מִפַּרְדֵּיסֵי וּבוּסְתָּנִי דִּשְׁתַל.
>
> **Translation:** Ultimately, witnesses came and testified that the person from Bei Ḥozai was his brother. At that point, the brother said to Mari bar Isak: Divide and give me half of the orchards and the gardens that you planted since the death of our father as well.

The story resolves itself cleanly: *lesof, asu sahadi de-achuh hu* — in the end, witnesses did come forward and testified that this person was indeed Mari bar Isak's brother. The entire dramatic back-and-forth ended with the brother's identity confirmed through witness testimony.

## Property Improvements

### Orchards Claim

Once the brother's identity was established, he pressed a further claim: *liflog li nami mipardisai uvestinai deshatal* — give me half of the orchards and gardens that you planted. Mari bar Isak had invested considerable resources and effort — fertilizer, saplings, labor — into improving the inherited property, believing it to be entirely his. Now this brother claimed half the improvements as well.

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר לָךְ, דִּתְנַן: הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים גְּדוֹלִים וּקְטַנִּים, וְהִשְׁבִּיחוּ גְּדוֹלִים אֶת הַנְּכָסִים – הִשְׁבִּיחוּ לָאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda said to Mari bar Isak: He spoke well to you, as we learned in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 143b): If one died and left adult and minor sons, and the adult sons enhanced the property, they enhanced the property, and the profit goes to the middle, i.e., it is divided between the adult sons and the minor sons.

Rav Chisda told Mari bar Isak that this claim was well-founded. He cited the *mishnah*: *hiniach banim gedolim uketanim vehishpichu gedolim et hanechasim, hishpichu le'emtsa* — if a father dies leaving adult and minor children behind, and the adult children improve the property, those improvements are divided equally among all the heirs, including the minors who did no work. And Rava similarly ruled: *hishpichu le'emtsa* — improvements go to the middle, meaning they are shared.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה: הִשְׁבִּיחוּ לָאֶמְצַע. אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם, גְּדוֹלִים גַּבֵּי קְטַנִּים יָדְעִי וְקָא מָחֲלִי, הָכָא מִי יָדַע דְּלֵיחִיל?
>
> **Translation:** And likewise, Rabba says: They enhanced the property, and the profit goes to the middle. Abaye said to him: Are these matters comparable? There, in the case that the adult and minor brothers were together, the adults are aware that the minors exist and forgo payment for their effort on behalf of their younger brothers. Here, in the case of Mari bar Isak, was the older brother aware of the existence of the younger brother so that he could forgo payment for his labor?

Abaye challenged this comparison: *mi dami?* — how can you compare the two cases? In the case of the *mishnah*, the adult heirs know that minor heirs exist. By choosing to improve the property without stipulating otherwise, they implicitly waive their exclusive claim to the improvements. They are understood to be working on behalf of all the heirs. But here — *hacha mi yadi de'itkha?* — Mari bar Isak had no idea this brother existed. How can his unilateral effort, undertaken in complete ignorance of his co-heir, be said to benefit that co-heir?

### Rabbi Ami's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִגַּלְגַּל מִלְּתָא וּמְטָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: גְּדוֹלָה מִזּוֹ אָמְרוּ: שָׁמִין לָהֶם כְּאָרִיס, הַשְׁתָּא דִּידֵיהּ לָא יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The matter continued to develop and came before Rabbi Ami. He said to those who reported Rav Ḥisda's ruling: The Sages stated a more far-reaching *halakha* than that: In the case of relatives who tend to the property of a captive, the court appraises their work as one would appraise the work of a sharecropper. Although the property they tended did not belong to them at all, they receive wages for their labor. Why, then, is the ruling now, in the case of Mari bar Isak, that payment for labor on property that is his, we do not give him? Mari bar Isak should be reimbursed for his expenditures.

The case was brought before Rabbi Ami, who made an argument in the opposite direction. He said: *gedola mizu amru, shamin lahen ke-aris* — they have said something even greater than this. In the case of *karov lenichsei shavuy*, where a relative is permitted to work the fields of an absent person, the relative is compensated as an *aris* — receiving a share of the produce (a half, a third, or a quarter) in exchange for his labor. If someone working another's field entirely is compensated in this way, then certainly when Mari bar Isak worked what he believed to be his own property, he should at least be entitled to compensation like an *aris* for whatever portion of the estate turned out to belong to his brother.

### Rav Chisda Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהְדְּרוּהָ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם בִּרְשׁוּת נָחֵית, הָכָא לָאו בִּרְשׁוּת נָחֵית. וְעוֹד: קָטָן הוּא, וְאֵין מוֹרִידִין קָרוֹב לְנִכְסֵי קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** They returned and related this matter before Rav Ḥisda. Rav Ḥisda said to them: Are these matters comparable? There, in the case of the captive's property, it was with authorization from the court that the relative descended to tend to the property. Here, it was without authorization that Mari bar Isak descended to tend to the property of his brother. And furthermore, Mari bar Isak's brother was a minor when Mari inherited the property, and the court does not authorize a relative to descend and manage the property of a minor.

The case came back to Rav Chisda, who rejected Rabbi Ami's argument on two grounds. First: *hatam birshut nachit, hacha lav birshut nachit* — in the case of *karov lenichsei shavuy*, the relative acts with explicit authorization. The *beit din* permitted him to work the field; there was even a *takana* establishing this arrangement. Therefore he is entitled to the benefits of an *aris*. Mari bar Isak, by contrast, had no such authorization from *beit din*; he simply believed the property was his.

Second, and crucially: *ve-od katan hu* — this brother was a minor. When Mari bar Isak was working the property, the brother was not yet an adult. This makes the situation one of *karov lenichsei katan* — a relative working the estate of a minor — which is a category the Talmud does not sanction. Even though Mari bar Isak did nothing wrong and acted in good faith, the halachic framework under which he was operating was one that would have been disapproved had anyone known about it. On that basis, Rav Chisda ruled that he was not entitled to any compensation for his efforts.

### Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהְדְּרוּהָ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא סַיְּימוּהָ קַמַּי דְּקָטָן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** They returned and related this response before Rabbi Ami. Rabbi Ami said to them: They did not complete conveying all the details of the case before me, and I was unaware that Mari's brother was a minor. Rav Ḥisda is correct.

The case returned to Rabbi Ami. His response was direct: *lo simu kamei de-katan hu* — when you presented the case to me, you did not tell me that the brother was a minor. Had I known that critical fact, I would have agreed with Rav Chisda that Mari bar Isak is not entitled to compensation for his efforts. The missing detail — that the absent co-heir was a *katan* — changed the entire analysis.

This story thus serves as a practical illustration of several principles discussed earlier in the tractate: the laws of *karov lenichsei shavuy*, the distinction between authorized and unauthorized work on another's property, and the conditions under which *karov lenichsei katan* applies.

## Grain Deposits

### Mishnah Deductions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְקִיד פֵּירוֹת אֵצֶל חֲבֵירוֹ, הֲרֵי זֶה יוֹצִיא לוֹ חֶסְרוֹנוֹת: לְחִטִּים וּלְאוֹרֶז – תִּשְׁעָה חֲצָאֵי קַבִּין לְכוֹר, לִשְׂעוֹרִין וּלְדוֹחַן – תִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין לְכוֹר, לְכוּסְּמִין וּלְזֶרַע פִּשְׁתָּן – שָׁלֹשׁ סְאִין לְכוֹר, הַכֹּל לְפִי הַמִּדָּה וְהַכֹּל לְפִי הַזְּמַן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of one who deposits produce with another, and the bailee provides him with different produce in return, that bailee deducts from the produce that he returns an amount equal to the standard decrease of the produce. The decrease is calculated according to this formula: For wheat and for rice, he deducts nine half-*kav* per *kor*, which is 180 *kav*; for barley and millet, he deducts nine *kav* per *kor*; for spelt and flaxseed, he deducts three *se'a*, which total eighteen *kav*, per *kor*. The entire calculation is according to the measure, and the entire calculation is according to the time elapsed. This is the amount of produce that the bailee deducts per one *kor* of produce over the course of one year.

The *sugya* now turns to a new *mishnah*: *hamafkid peirot etzel chaveiro* — if someone deposits produce or grain with a friend to watch. The Gemara will clarify that the operative scenario is one where the depositary mixed the deposited grain with his own. In such a case, the *mishnah* rules that the depositary need not return the full original quantity, because a measurable amount of grain will inevitably have been lost over time — whether consumed by rats, crumbled husks, mold, or other sources of attrition.

The *mishnah* specifies the applicable deductions by grain type. For wheat and rice: nine half-*kabin* per *kor*. Since a *kor* contains thirty *se'ah* and each *se'ah* contains six *kav*, a *kor* equals 180 *kav*. Nine half-*kabin* amounts to 4.5 *kav*, which is 2.5%. For barley and *dochen* (millet): nine *kabin* per *kor*, which is 5%. For *kusmin* (spelt) and flaxseed: three *se'in* per *kor*, which is 10%. The *mishnah* then states *hakol lepi hamidah vehakol lepi hazman* — all amounts are proportional, both to the quantity stored and to the duration of storage. If ten *kor* were deposited, multiply by ten; if stored for three years, multiply by three.

### Fixed Rat Losses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: וְכִי מָה אִכְפַּת לָהֶן לָעַכְבָּרִין? וַהֲלֹא אוֹכְלוֹת בֵּין מֵהַרְבֵּה וּבֵין מִקִּמְעָה! אֶלָּא אֵינוֹ יוֹצִיא לוֹ חֶסְרוֹנוֹת אֶלָּא לְכוֹר אֶחָד בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: And what do the mice care how much produce the bailee is safeguarding? Don't they eat the same amount whether it is from much produce and whether it is from little produce? Rather, he deducts an amount equal to the standard decrease of just one *kor* of produce.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri disputes this proportional approach: *vechi mah ichpas lahen le'achbarin* — what do the rats care about the size of the deposit? Rats do not eat proportionally to the quantity of available food; they eat a fixed amount regardless. Therefore, he argues, one should deduct only the amount one would deduct for a single *kor* — the fixed quantity the rats will consume — regardless of how large the deposit actually is. Tosafot notes that for a deposit smaller than a *kor*, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri would agree the deduction would be proportionally smaller; his point is that once you reach a *kor*, the rat-consumption figure does not scale upward.

### Clarification of Grain Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אוֹרֶז טוּבָא חָסֵר! אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּאוֹרֶז קָלוּף שָׁנוּ. לְכוּסְּמִין וּלְזֶרַע פִּשְׁתָּן שְׁלֹשָׁה סְאִין לְכוֹר וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: זֶרַע פִּשְׁתָּן בְּגִבְעוֹלִין שָׁנוּ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: לְכוּסְּמִין וּלְזֶרַע פִּשְׁתָּן בַּגִּבְעוֹלִין, וּלְאוֹרֶז שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָלוּף – שְׁלֹשָׁה סְאִין לְכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara challenges: After decrease, rice is lacking a greater amount than what is recorded in the mishna. Rabba bar bar Ḥana says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: It is with regard to shelled rice that the *tanna'im* taught the mishna. The mishna teaches: For spelt and flaxseed, he deducts three *se'a* per *kor*. Rabbi Yoḥanan says that Rabbi Ḥiyya says: It is with regard to flaxseed on its stalks that the *tanna'im* taught the mishna, and that is why the rate of decrease is so great. The Gemara comments: That is also taught in a *baraita*: For spelt and for flaxseed on its stalks and for unshelled rice, he deducts three *se'a* per *kor*.

The Gemara then sets out the specific deductions in greater detail. Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the *mishnah*'s figures for rice apply to *orez kaluf* — peeled rice — which loses relatively little. Unpeeled rice, whose husks crumble and fall away, would lose considerably more. Similarly, Rabbi Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Hiyya that the high 10% figure for flaxseed applies to *zera pishtan begiv'olin* — flaxseed still in the husks, which crumble significantly. A *baraita* confirms: for *kusmin*, flaxseed in husks, and unpeeled rice, the deduction is three *se'in* per *kor* — 10%. The *baraita* thereby clarifies that the *mishnah* addresses peeled rice on the one hand and husked flaxseed on the other, and the *gemara* notes Tosafot's discussion of why the *mishnah* combined these particular cases.

### Multiple Loss Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא, אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַרְבֵּה אוֹבְדוֹת מֵהֶן, הַרְבֵּה מִתְפַּזְּרוֹת מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said: And what do the mice care how much produce the bailee is safeguarding? It is taught in a *baraita* that the Sages said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: The reduction is due not only to mice eating the produce. Much of the produce is lost, and much of the produce is scattered.

The *chachamim* respond to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri with a *baraita*: *harbeh ovdot mehen, harbeh mitpazrot mehen* — many things are lost from grain, many things scatter. The losses are not solely attributable to rats. Grain crumbles, scatters, and deteriorates through various means, and all of these losses are proportional to the quantity of grain stored. Therefore, the proportional approach of the *chachamim* is correct.

### Rats' Behavior

The Tosafot cites an interesting passage from the Yerushalmi that addresses Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's reasoning from a different angle. The *chachamim*'s rationale is explained there as follows: *hanei achbarim rishi'ei ninhu* — these rats are *resha'im*. *Kad chamyan ibur, lo mistaye'u de'achli, ela karun le'chavreihon de'achlin imehon* — when they find grain, it is not enough that they eat it themselves; they call their friends to come and eat with them. The more grain there is, the more widely word spreads, and a greater number of rats arrives to consume it. On this view, rat consumption *is* proportional to the quantity of food — not because each rat eats more, but because more rats are recruited when more food is available. This is a different answer than the one our Gemara gives, and Tosafot notes the distinction.

## When Deductions Apply

### Mixing Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁמָּדַד לוֹ מִתּוֹךְ גּוֹרְנוֹ וְהֶחְזִיר לוֹ מִתּוֹךְ גּוֹרְנוֹ. אֲבָל מָדַד לוֹ מִתּוֹךְ גּוֹרְנוֹ וְהֶחְזִיר לוֹ מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – אֵינוֹ יוֹצִיא לוֹ חֶסְרוֹנוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמּוֹתִירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* who recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* taught before Rav Naḥman: In what case is this statement said, that the bailee deducts the decrease from the produce he returns? It is in a case where the owner of the produce measured the produce for the bailee from his own threshing floor, and the bailee returned the produce to him from his own threshing floor. The measures used in all threshing floors were equal, and tended to err on the side of increasing the amount measured. But in a case where the owner measured the produce for the bailee from his own threshing floor and the bailee returned the produce to him as measured by a measure from his own house, which were more precise than those used on the threshing floor, he does not deduct the decrease when returning the produce. This is because the produce the owner deposited was measured with the increased measure of the threshing floor, and that offsets the decrease.

A *baraita* clarifies the conditions under which the *mishnah*'s deductions apply: *bamah devarim amurim, she'eirev mi'peirotav* — when does one apply these percentage deductions? Only when the depositary mixed the deposited grain with his own. In that case, one cannot identify and return the specific grain that was deposited, so the *mishnah*'s fixed rates govern.

But if the depositary kept the grain separate — *yichad lo keren zavit*, he designated a particular corner for it — then he simply returns it as it is: *harei shelcha lefanecha*, here is yours, untouched.

### Empirical Measure

This raises an obvious question. Even when the grain is mixed, why not simply measure how much the depositary's own grain shrunk over the period and apply that same proportional loss to the deposited grain? That would provide actual empirical data rather than fixed rabbinic estimates.

### Unknown Usage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בְּמִסְתַּפֵּק מֵהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Where the bailee took supplies from that produce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּלָא יָדְעִי כַּמָּה אִסְתַּפַּק.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* is referring to a case where the bailee does not know with how much he took as supplies.

The Gemara answers: *be'mistapek mehen* — because the depositary has been eating from the grain himself throughout the period. He cannot isolate how much of the decrease in his stores is due to natural shrinkage and how much he consumed. And when asked why he does not simply track how much he took out — the Gemara responds: *dela yada'ei kamah istapak* — he does not know. He has been drawing from the stores regularly without keeping records of each withdrawal. Since neither the natural shrinkage nor his own usage can be precisely determined, the fixed *mishnah* rates are applied.

## Seasonal Expansion

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיְתָה וְכוּ׳. כַּמָּה מִדָּה מְרוּבָּה? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֲשָׂרָה כּוֹרִין. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: כַּמָּה מִדָּה מְרוּבָּה? עֲשָׂרָה כּוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that Rabbi Yehuda says: If the deposit was a large measure, the bailee does not deduct the decrease from it. The Gemara asks: How much is a large measure? Rabba bar bar Ḥana says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: It is ten *kor*. This is also taught in a *baraita*: How much is a large measure? It is ten *kor*.

Rabbi Yehuda adds a further qualification: if grain was deposited in the dry season and returned in the rainy season, the depositary need not deduct anything. Grain expands in the presence of moisture. Returning the same *measure* of grain in the rainy season as was originally deposited already constitutes giving back slightly less grain by weight and density, because the expanded grain takes up more volume. That differential effectively offsets what would otherwise be the required deduction.

Rabbi Yehuda's exemption applies only when the deposit was *midah merubah* — a large quantity. Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, and a *baraita* confirms: the threshold is ten *kor*.

### Rashi's Logic

This raises a question: why should the size of the deposit matter for the seasonal expansion argument? Does grain not expand regardless of quantity? Rashi explains by returning to the logic of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri. For a very large quantity of grain, rats consume only a small and fixed proportion of it — they cannot eat unlimited amounts. As the deposit grows, the rat-consumption figure becomes a progressively smaller percentage of the total. At ten *kor*, the percentage lost to rats and other attrition is small enough that the volumetric expansion of the grain in the rainy season compensates for it. But for a smaller deposit, the proportional loss to rats is a larger percentage, and the expansion of the grain in the wet season does not fully offset it. Hence Rabbi Yehuda's exemption is limited to large quantities.

### Burst Jug

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי בְּשׁוּפְטָנֵי עָסְקִינַן דְּיָהֲבִי בִּכְיָילָא רַבָּא וְשָׁקְלִי בִּכְיָילָא זוּטָא? דִּלְמָא בִּימוֹת הַגּוֹרֶן קָאָמְרַתְּ. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁמָּדַד לוֹ בִּימוֹת הַגּוֹרֶן וְהֶחְזִיר לוֹ בִּימוֹת הַגּוֹרֶן. אֲבָל מָדַד לוֹ בִּימוֹת הַגּוֹרֶן וְהֶחְזִיר לוֹ בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים – אֵינוֹ יוֹצִיא לוֹ חֶסְרוֹן מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמּוֹתִירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman said to him: And are we dealing with fools, who give the deposit with a large measure and take the produce back with a small measure? Clearly, the same measure was used in both cases. Perhaps you are stating a ruling about the season of the threshing floor, and this is what it means: In what case is this statement said? It is said in a case where he measured the produce for the bailee during the season of the threshing floor and the bailee returned the produce to him during the season of the threshing floor, i.e., in the same period. But in a case where he measured the produce for the bailee during the season of the threshing floor and the bailee returned the produce to him during the rainy season, he does not deduct the decrease when returning the produce, because the produce that he received absorbed moisture and expanded, so that he ultimately returns the same measure.

A *tanna* then taught before Rav Nachman a related point about where the grain is measured. When do the *mishnah*'s deduction figures apply? When grain was measured in the granary and returned at the granary. But if it was measured at the granary and returned in the home, the depositary need not make deductions, because the home measures compensate for the difference.

Rav Nachman objected: *vechi b'shuftani askinan?* — are we dealing with fools? Would anyone give grain in large granary measures and accept it back in small household measures? No sensible person would do that. So the *tanna* clarified: the distinction is not between granary and home as *locations*, but between the *granary season* — *yemot hagoren*, the dry summer — and the *rainy season* — *yemot hageshamim*. When grain is deposited in the dry season and returned in the dry season, deductions apply. But when deposited in the dry season and returned in the rainy season — *ein motzi lo chisaron mipney shemosiros* — no deductions are required, because the expanded grain already compensates.

### Jug Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אִם כֵּן, לִפְקַע כַּדָּא! הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וּפְקַע כַּדָּא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, מִשּׁוּם אִיצְצָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa said to Abaye: If so, if the volume of the grain expands during the rainy season, the jug in which the grain is placed should burst due to that expansion. The Gemara relates: There was an incident and the jug burst. If you wish, say instead that the volume contracted due to compression. When the produce was deposited it was loose and had greater volume. When the bailee returned it, the produce was tightly packed in the jug, resulting in lesser volume.

This prompted Rav Papa to ask Abaye: if grain truly expands in the rainy season, why does a jug filled with grain not burst as the grain expands? Two answers are offered. First: *hava uvda upaka kada* — it has in fact happened; jugs do burst if no space is left for expansion. Second: *mishum itzitza* — when grain is completely compressed with no room to expand, it may not expand at all. The expansion occurs precisely because there *is* room.

## Wine & Oil

### Wine Sixth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יוֹצִיא לוֹ שְׁתוּת לְיַיִן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חוֹמֶשׁ. יוֹצִיא לוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין שֶׁמֶן לְמֵאָה: לוֹג וּמֶחֱצָה שְׁמָרִים, לוֹג וּמֶחֱצָה בָּלַע. אִם הָיָה שֶׁמֶן מְזוּקָּק – אֵינוֹ יוֹצִיא לוֹ שְׁמָרִים. אִם הָיוּ קַנְקַנִּים יְשָׁנִים – אֵינוֹ יוֹצִיא לוֹ בֶּלַע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: When the bailee returns liquids that were deposited with him, he deducts one-sixth of the amount for wine, to offset the decrease in volume due to absorption into the cask and evaporation. Rabbi Yehuda says: He deducts one-fifth. He deducts three *log* of oil for one hundred *log*: A *log* and a half for sediment that sinks to the bottom of the cask, and a *log* and a half for absorption into the cask. If it was refined oil, he does not deduct any of the oil for sediment because it was filtered. If the oil was stored in old casks that are already saturated, he does not deduct any of the oil for absorption.

The *mishnah* now turns to wine and oil deposits. For wine, the depositary deducts a sixth — approximately 16%. This large deduction reflects the fact that pottery jugs absorb wine. If a depositor left wine in a six-unit jug, only five units would remain by the time it is returned, because one sixth would have been absorbed into the porous walls of the vessel. If the original jug can be identified and returned, no calculation is needed. But when the wine was mixed with the depositary's own stores, one deducts a sixth and returns the remainder as the calculated equivalent of what was deposited.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: the deduction for wine should be a fifth — *chomesh* — not a sixth.

For oil, the deduction structure is different. Oil absorbs into pottery at a much lower rate: three *log* per hundred, which is 3%. Of that 3%, one and a half *log* per hundred represents absorption into the jug walls, and one and a half *log* per hundred represents lees — *shemarim* — that settle at the bottom. So when a depositary returns oil from mixed stores, he returns pure oil from the top and deducts 3%: one and a half percent for absorption and one and a half percent for lees.

### Oil Refinements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמּוֹכֵר שֶׁמֶן מְזוּקָּק לַחֲבֵירוֹ כׇּל יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה – הֲרֵי זֶה מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו לוֹג וּמֶחֱצָה שְׁמָרִים לְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda says: Even in a case of one who sells refined oil to another all the days of the year, this buyer accepts upon himself that the seller will deduct a *log* and a half of sediment for one hundred *log*, as that is the standard measure of sediment.

Two further refinements follow. If the depositor entrusted *shemen mezukak* — oil from which the lees had already been removed — the depositary deducts only one and a half percent, since there are no lees to account for. And if the jugs were *kankanin yeshanim* — old jugs that had already absorbed their capacity of oil — then no deduction for absorption is made, since old, saturated jugs take in nothing further.

Rabbi Yehuda adds a ruling about ongoing commercial sales: *af hamocher shemen mezukak le'chaveiro kol yemot hashana* — even a merchant who sells purified oil to a customer in regular installments throughout the year must accept upon himself one and a half *log* of lees per hundred. The case is where a customer purchases a barrel of oil and the merchant delivers pure oil from it throughout the year. The customer has bought the barrel with its natural lees included; the merchant delivers refined oil and is therefore entitled to retain one and a half percent as the lees portion.

## Regional & Pricing

### Coatings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: מָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ וּמָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ. בְּאַתְרֵיהּ דְּמָר חָפוּ בְּקִירָא וְלָא מָיֵיץ טְפֵי, בְּאַתְרֵיהּ דְּמָר חָפוּ בְּכוּפְרָא וּמָיֵיץ טְפֵי. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מִשּׁוּם גַּרְגִּישְׁתָּא, הָא מָיְיצָא טְפֵי וְהָא לָא מָיְיצָא טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: And the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yehuda do not disagree with regard to the *halakha*. Rather, this Sage ruled in accordance with the custom of his locale, and this Sage ruled in accordance with the custom of his locale. In the place of one Sage, i.e., the first *tanna*, they coat the casks with wax [*bekira*] and it does not absorb much. In the place of the other Sage, i.e., Rabbi Yehuda, they coat the casks with pitch and it absorbs much. If you wish, say instead that it is due to the quality of earth [*gargishta*] from which they make the casks. Barrels made from this earth absorb much, and barrels made from that earth do not absorb much.

The Gemara explains that the disagreement between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda over whether the wine deduction is a sixth or a fifth does not necessarily reflect a substantive halachic dispute. Rather, *mar ki atreh umar ki atreh* — each authority speaks according to the practice of his locale. In the region of the *tanna kamma*, barrels were coated with wax (*keri*), which is less absorbent, so only a sixth of the wine was lost. In Rabbi Yehuda's region, barrels were coated with pitch (*chufra*), which is more absorbent, and therefore a fifth was lost. An alternative explanation: *mishum gargishta* — because of the clay used in making the jugs. In some regions the clay itself is more porous than in others, accounting for the different absorption rates.

### Merchant Profits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה רָמוּ אַרְבָּעִים וְתַמְנֵי כּוּזֵי בְּדַנָּא. אָזֵיל דַּנָּא בְּשִׁיתָּא זוּזֵי, פָּרֵיס רַב יְהוּדָה שִׁיתָּא שִׁיתָּא בְּזוּזָא, דַּל תְּלָתִין וְשִׁיתָּא בְּשִׁיתָּא, פָּשׁוּ לֵיהּ תְּרֵיסַר. דַּל תְּמָנְיָא שְׁתוּתֵי, פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ אַרְבְּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: In Rav Yehuda's place they would place the contents of forty-eight pitchers of wine into a barrel, as that was the standard size of barrels there. The barrel went for six dinars, and Rav Yehuda divided the oil and sold it at six pitchersful for one dinar. The Gemara now analyzes Rav Yehuda's calculation: Subtract thirty-six pitchersful that were sold for six dinars each, with which he recoups the purchase price of the barrel. Twelve pitchersful remained for him. Subtract eight pitchers full, which is one-sixth of the total amount, as that is the measure absorbed in the barrels. Four pitchersful remained as profit for Rav Yehuda.

The Gemara then illustrates the wine deduction in practice with a case drawn from Rav Yehuda's locale. There, 48 units of a particular measure (*kuza*) filled a barrel (*dana*). A full barrel sold for six *zuz*. Rav Yehuda sold the wine from such a barrel at six *kuzei* per *zuz*. Since the barrel held 48 units, dividing by 6 units per *zuz* yields 8 *zuz* as the maximum possible revenue.

The Gemara then works through the arithmetic across the top of 40b. He paid six *zuz* for the barrel. Selling 36 units at six per *zuz* recoups exactly his six *zuz* cost. That leaves 12 units remaining — which at six per *zuz* represents two additional *zuz* of gross revenue. But one must subtract the wine absorbed by the barrel: a sixth of 48 is 8 units. Those 8 units are gone. So he is left with 12 minus 8 = 4 units of actual saleable wine beyond what he paid for, which at the rate of 6 per *zuz* equals two-thirds of a *zuz* in net revenue above his cost.

### Price Control

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמִּשְׂתַּכֵּר אַל יִשְׂתַּכֵּר יוֹתֵר עַל שְׁתוּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But doesn't Shmuel say that one who profits from the sale of matters related to one's existence may not profit more than one-sixth? One can infer that it is permitted for one to profit up to one-sixth. But according to the calculation, Rav Yehuda's profit was much lower. Why did he not sell the oil at a higher price?

The Gemara now raises an apparent difficulty. Shmuel ruled that a merchant selling staple goods may not profit by more than one sixth of his purchase price — *hamishtaker al yistaker yoter al shtut*. Rashi emphasizes that this price control applies to staples that the community depends on. Rav Yehuda paid six *zuz* for the barrel, so his maximum permitted profit under Shmuel's ruling would be one *zuz*. But his actual net gain came to only two-thirds of a *zuz* — less than what he was allowed. Why did he not charge more to reach his permitted profit?

### Profit Justification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא גּוּלְפֵי וּשְׁמָרַיָּא. אִי הָכִי נְפִישׁ לֵיהּ טְפֵי מִשְּׁתוּת? אִיכָּא טִרְחֵיהּ וּדְמֵי בַּרְזַנְיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: There are the barrel and the sediment to account for. These remain in his possession, as he purchased the barrel and all its contents for six dinars, and they supplement the profit. The Gemara challenges: If so, once the barrel and sediment are taken into account, the profit is greater than one-sixth. How did Rav Yehuda profit beyond the permitted amount? The Gemara answers: There is the payment for his exertion, as he sold the oil, and there is the payment for tapping, as a craftsman is needed to install a tap in the barrel. When those payments are included in the calculation, the profit is precisely one-sixth.

The Gemara answers: *ika gufa ve'shmarya* — he also received the barrel itself and the lees at the bottom. He did not merely profit from selling wine; he gained the physical barrel (*gufa*) and the settled lees (*shmarya*), both of which have value. So his total gain — two-thirds of a *zuz* from wine sales plus the value of the barrel and lees — may well represent more than a sixth above his cost.

The Gemara immediately raises the opposite concern: if we add in the barrel and the lees, does his total profit not *exceed* one sixth, in violation of Shmuel's ruling? The Gemara responds: *ika tircha ve'dmei barzinayta* — there is also his labor and the cost of the spigot (or, more broadly, the skill and equipment involved in dispensing the wine). Shmuel's one-sixth cap applies to profit *above and beyond* reimbursement for labor and direct expenses. Once one accounts for the merchant's effort and the cost of the *barzinayta*, the remaining profit falls within the permitted sixth. The system is internally coherent: the merchant is compensated for his labor and expenses, and his markup beyond that remains within the halachically sanctioned limit.
