# Bava Batra 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Banna'ah & Grave (II)

### Marking Graves

The daf opens four lines into 58a, continuing the series of episodes featuring Rav Banna'ah, whose earlier statements quoted in the name of Rabbi Yochanan touched on matters of *tzniut* — though as has been noted repeatedly, the Gemara's emphasis in those passages falls not on the women but on how men comport themselves. We also encountered there a discussion about how a *talmid chacham* presents his own person, with the reminder that norms we typically associate with women's modesty were applied there to men.

The new story likewise intersects with *tzniut* in a fascinating way. The Gemara introduces Rav Banna'ah's practice of marking grave sites: *rav banna'ah hava ka metzayen me'arata* — Rav Banna'ah would go into burial caves, locate precisely where the bodies were interred, and then mark on the ground above the spots that would convey *tumah*. In that way, *kohanim* would know which areas to avoid walking over. The practical mechanism is that when a grave is sealed without an air gap between the coffin and the surrounding earth, *tumah boke'ah ve'olah* — the *tumah* breaks straight through upward — and therefore the exact location must be known and marked.

### Abraham's Cave

*Ki mata lime'arata de-Avraham Avinu* — when he arrived at the cave of Abraham, that is, the *Me'arat HaMachpelah* in *Kiryat Arba*, his task was to determine which areas above the cave one could traverse without incurring *tumah*. How the site had been navigated for the preceding two thousand years we cannot say — perhaps people avoided the entire region, or perhaps the exact layout was simply unknown. In any case, Rav Banna'ah now sought to map it.

*Ashkechei le-Eliezer eved Avraham de-ka'i kamei vava* — he found Eliezer, the servant of Abraham, standing at the entrance of the cave, guarding it. Rav Banna'ah asked: *mai ka avid Avraham* — what is Abraham doing inside? The Maharsha explains that Rav Banna'ah was not merely making idle conversation; having found Eliezer posted at the door, he reasoned that Abraham must have sent him there to ensure privacy, and he therefore asked what manner of private activity was taking place.

Eliezer replied: *ganei be-kanfei de-Sarah* — he is lying in the arms, or wings, of Sarah. The Rashbam explains *kanfei* as the arms; one might understand it as the lap, though the Rashbam specifies the arms — that is, his head resting in her embrace. *Ve-ka me'ayina lei be-reisha* — and she is gazing at his head, or attending to his hair. Many of the *meforshim* understand this as a gesture of tender affection, though some note that when similar language is cited in *halachic* contexts it refers to checking for lice — yet that plainly is not the primary meaning of the passage here.

Rav Banna'ah said to Eliezer: *zil eima lei Banna'ah ka'i be-vava* — please go tell Abraham that Banna'ah is at the door and wishes to enter. Eliezer responded that there was no need to ask: *le'ul* — you may go in. *Midei yedia de-yeitzer be-hai alma leika* — it is well known that there is no *yetzer* in the World to Come, and therefore entering at this moment would not constitute an impropriety.

Rav Banna'ah entered, surveyed the precise parameters of the burial space, and departed. The obvious irony, of course, is that Abraham appears to be very much present and alive, yet Rav Banna'ah is mapping out the coffin's location to determine the *tumah*. This tension between the dead being buried and yet somehow present will recur throughout these stories, and we will return to it.

After completing his survey of the Patriarchs' and Matriarchs' section of the cave — where Abraham and Sarah, Isaac and Rebecca, Jacob and Leah are all buried — he proceeded toward the cave of Adam. Apparently Adam was interred in a distinct section, and the Gemara will shortly explain why Rav Banna'ah could not approach it in the same way.

*Ki mata lime'arta de-Adam HaRishon* — when he arrived at the cave of Adam, *yatzta bas kol ve'amra* — a heavenly voice issued forth and declared: *nistakalta bidmus diyukni* — you have already gazed upon the likeness of my image. The word *diyukan* derives from the Greek *eikon*, meaning image or icon. Abraham — or, according to the Rashbam, Jacob, in anticipation of a statement the Gemara will cite shortly about Jacob's image — bears a resemblance to Adam HaRishon, who was created directly in the divine image, and is thus in some sense an image of an image. *Bidiyukni atzmah al tistakel* — but you may not look upon Adam HaRishon himself, who was fashioned directly in God's own image.

Rav Banna'ah pushed back at the *bas kol*: *avina letziyunei me'arta* — but I need to mark the graves, this is a practical *halachic* necessity. The *bas kol* responded: *kemiddas hachitzonah kach middas hapenimis* — the dimensions of the outer cave and the inner cave are identical. If Adam is buried in the inner chamber and the Patriarchs in the outer, simply replicate the measurements. And according to the alternative view — *uleman de'amar shenei vattim zeh lemalah mizeh* — that the cave is doubled vertically rather than horizontally, with an upper and lower chamber, then *kemiddas elyonah kach middas hatachtonah*: the upper and lower chambers are exactly parallel, meaning that once you have marked the upper level, the lower level is automatically accounted for, since the *tumah* rises straight up and the two chambers share the same horizontal footprint.

One can observe a certain resonance here with Plato's allegory of the cave — we are in a cave, encountering images of images, glimpsing echoes of ultimate truth — a parallel worth noting, even if it was not the Gemara's intent.

### Adam's Heels

Despite the heavenly prohibition, Rav Banna'ah reports: *nistakalti bishnei akeivav* — I caught a glimpse of his two heels. Presumably this occurred as he was approaching and the *bas kol* halted him, and he managed a brief look before turning away. *Ve-domin lishnei galgalei chamah* — and they appeared like two orbs of the sun: luminous, radiant, almost overwhelming in their splendor. If even the heels of Adam HaRishon shone with such intensity, one can only imagine the totality of his appearance.

The Maharsha raises an obvious question: why did Adam's contemporaries not find his appearance so startling? He answers that those who lived in proximity to the creation of Adam were themselves so close to the source of divine radiance that the difference was not as jarring. Only for a later generation, standing at a far remove from that original glory, would encountering it be so overwhelming.

A brief academic observation: Rav Banna'ah's statement — *nistakalti bishnei akeivav* and what follows — is in Hebrew, whereas the surrounding narrative frame is in Aramaic. This suggests that this statement may represent an authentic, original pronouncement of Rav Banna'ah himself, while the Aramaic narrative is the *stam* Gemara's elaboration providing the context for that statement. One wonders whether the story of his entering the cave and receiving the *bas kol* was developed specifically to frame this original dictum.

## Tzaddikim & Tumah

### Status of Avot

The narrative raises a question that was discussed in the room: according to certain *midrashic* or *aggadic* sources, *tzaddikim* do not convey *tumah* after death. This question is historically significant — when great *rabbinic* figures passed away, there are accounts of *kohanim* attending their funerals directly. In the case of the Ra'avad of Posquières, it is reported that all the *kohanim* of his city participated in his funeral, relying on the principle of *tzaddikim einam metamei*, or alternatively on the grounds that he was a *nasi b'doro*. Whether such statements carry genuine *halachic* weight is itself a serious question that this sugya leaves unresolved.

### Tosafot Query

Tosafot, however, raises an even more striking difficulty. The technical basis for *tumah* conveyed by a grave is the verse *adam ki yamut be'ohel* — when a man (*adam*) dies in a tent. From this, the Gemara elsewhere derives that the graves of non-Jews do not convey *tumat ohel* — the *tumah* of the airspace above — because non-Jews are not included in the term *adam*. They may convey *tumah* through direct contact, but not through *ohel*.

Tosafot's question is: who exactly is the non-Jew here? *Adam HaRishon* himself, obviously, was not Jewish in any formal sense. And what of Abraham? The *Mishneh LaMelech* — the commentator on the Rambam, in his work *Parshat Derachim* — devotes an entire volume to reading the early biblical narratives through the lens of an ongoing dispute about whether the *Avot* had the status of Jews or of non-Jews from a *halachic* perspective. This question animated many of the disputes between Joseph and his brothers as well.

Tosafot's underlying assumption, then, is that Abraham — and certainly Adam — might be categorized as non-Jews, and therefore their graves might not convey *tumat ohel* at all. If so, why was Rav Banna'ah marking their graves in the first place?

### Pre-Halacha Graves

Tosafot raises a second, independent resolution: *be-kever she-lifnei ha-dibbur lo amru* — even granting that a non-Jewish grave can convey *tumat ohel* according to some opinions, those opinions might not apply to burials that predate the giving of the Torah. The *halacha* of *tumat ohel* was transmitted at Sinai; those buried before that moment are, in a sense, grandfathered out of its application. The only *tumah* applicable to pre-Sinaitic graves might be through direct contact or proximity to the grave itself, derived from the verse *o be-kever* — or a grave — which the Gemara uses to extend coverage to graves that predate the *dibbur*.

Tosafot then offers a resolution to his own question: *ve-yesh lomar de-Avraham nikra Adam* — Abraham is explicitly called *adam* in Tanakh. The verse in Yehoshua 14:15 refers to him as *ha-Adam ha-gadol*, and similarly Adam HaRishon is also designated *adam* in ways that bring both figures under the biblical category of *adam*. Therefore, even according to those who say non-Jewish graves do not convey *tumat ohel* — because non-Jews are excluded from the word *adam* — these particular figures, Abraham and Adam, cannot be excluded on those grounds, because Scripture explicitly applies the term to them. And thus, the earlier resolution about pre-Sinaitic burials may be unnecessary, since both figures are categorized as *adam* regardless of their formal ethnic or covenantal status. It is a remarkable question — that Adam himself might initially not qualify under the term *adam* — and a fascinating answer.

## Intimacy & Privacy

### Privacy vs Display

Returning now to the story of Abraham and Sarah: from this narrative, one late *acharon* — drawing on a reading that the Rama appears to cite in the name of the Ran — derives a *halachic* or quasi-*halachic* principle that public displays of affection are contrary to *tzniut*. This reading has given rise to a broader discussion about what constitutes an inappropriately public intimate gesture, how overtly sexual it must be to present a problem, and whether this narrative carries genuine *halachic* weight at all — the Rama himself frames it as a matter of impropriety rather than issuing a formal ruling.

But there is a more fundamental question about what the story is actually teaching. One must ask: in the scenario Rav Banna'ah encountered, who precisely would have been doing something wrong had he entered without sending Eliezer first? Clearly the problem falls on Rav Banna'ah, not on Abraham and Sarah. And the wrong in question is not that Abraham and Sarah were displaying their affection publicly — they were in a private cave, entirely secluded. The wrong would have been that he would have been *invading their intimate space*. That is a very different moral lesson from a prohibition on public displays of affection. The case of public affection involves no expectation of privacy, no presumption of a protected intimate space — the wrong is of a different character entirely.

### Responsum

This distinction is borne out by the language of the Rashbam on the passage. He writes that Eliezer told Rav Banna'ah: *de-ein kan chutzpah* — it would not be *chutzpah* — and adds *ve-lo tzerichinah le-iztanuei minei* — I do not need to hide or withdraw from his entering. Read carefully, the Rashbam is framing the question as one of *chutzpah*, of uninvited intrusion into a private space, not as a question of whether the couple were conducting themselves immodestly. The lesson of the story, properly understood, concerns the sanctity of private intimate space and the impropriety of invading it — an ethos of a rather different kind from the blanket restriction on public displays of affection that some have read into it. This question has been addressed at length in a *teshuvah* devoted to the subject, and interested readers may consult it.

## Rav Banna'ah & Grave (II)

### Beauty Hierarchy

Returning now to the Gemara's continuation: *hakol bifnei Sarah kekof bifnei adam* — all human beings, in comparison to Sarah, are like monkeys compared to humans, in terms of that quality — call it beauty, but beauty here in a sense that carries overtones of divine radiance rather than mere physical attractiveness.

*Sarah bifnei Chavah kekof bifnei adam* — but Sarah herself, compared to Chavah, is like a monkey compared to a human. And *Chavah bifnei Adam kekof bifnei adam* — Chavah compared to Adam HaRishon is likewise a monkey to a human. This last statement is almost certainly a continuation of Rav Banna'ah's own statement rather than a separate tradition, since the chain of comparisons flows directly from his experience at the cave.

What is striking is who appears at the top of this hierarchy on the female side: not the Matriarchs collectively, not Eve alone, but specifically Sarah. The *avot* are curiously bypassed in the female chain of comparison. Then *Adam bifnei Shechinah kekof bifnei adam* — and Adam HaRishon himself, compared to the *Shechinah*, is like a monkey to a human. One should note the precision of the term *Shechinah* here — the divine indwelling, the immanent divine presence — rather than God directly, which would push the anthropomorphism even further.

The Gemara then extends the chain forward through history: *shufrei de-Rav Kahana me'ein shufrei de-Rav* — the beauty of Rav Kahana was an echo, a glimmer, of the beauty of Rav; *shufrei de-Rav me'ein shufrei de-Rabbi Abbahu* — the beauty of Rav was an echo of that of Rabbi Abbahu; *shufrei de-Rabbi Abbahu me'ein shufrei de-Yaakov Avinu* — Rabbi Abbahu's was an echo of Jacob's; and *shufrei de-Yaakov Avinu me'ein shufrei de-Adam HaRishon* — and Jacob's was some trace, some distillation, of the beauty of Adam HaRishon. Here Jacob is specifically highlighted, corresponding to the tradition of *Chazal* that it was Jacob's image that was engraved on the *Kisei HaKavod* — when Yechezkel saw the figure of a man among the angelic beings, that figure is understood to have borne Jacob's face.

It is noteworthy that Rabbi Yochanan — who elsewhere in the Gemara is described in striking terms of physical beauty — does not appear in this chain. The Rashbam distinguishes between ordinary physical beauty, however remarkable, and this other quality: an almost numinous radiance, something approaching a halo or divine luminosity, which is what the chain here traces.

## Magician Story

### Grave Desecration

The Gemara now introduces another figure: *hahu amgosha* — a certain magician, the word *amgosha* being related to "magus." *De-hava chati shichvei* — he was digging up graves. The Rashbam understands him as a grave robber, stripping buried bodies of their clothes. The Maharsha, more naturally given that he is identified as a magician, suggests he was a necromancer seeking body parts for his craft. But the Soncino commentary, drawing on scholarly notes not always found in other editions, points out that within Persian Zoroastrian belief, burying bodies in the earth was considered a defilement of the sacred earth; bodies were therefore traditionally exposed in open pits for vultures to consume. The magician was likely acting from this religious impulse — attempting to expose the buried dead so that they could be disposed of in the manner his tradition required. In any case, he was violating the graves.

*Ki mata amrata de-Rav ve-Rav Tuvi bar Masna* — when he came to the grave of Rav Tuvi bar Masna, *tafsei bediknei* — the buried man seized him by the beard and would not release him. Here again, as with Abraham and Sarah, the dead are in some sense present and active. The magician, beard in hand, was trapped.

### Beard Cutting

*Ata Abaye* — Abaye came, and appealed: *bematusa minach shavkeih* — please, at your leave, let him go. The Rashbam suggests this magician was a friend of Abaye's, which is why Abaye interceded. Rav Tuvi released him.

*Le-shana achrisita* — a year later, the magician returned. Perhaps he assumed Abaye had forgotten, or perhaps he reasoned his luck would hold. He attempted to disturb the same grave, and Rav Tuvi seized him by the beard again. Abaye came once more to intercede, but this time *lo shavkeih* — Rav Tuvi bar Masna would not release him: fool me once, shame on you. *Ad de-ata mispara ve-gazei lediknei* — until Abaye brought scissors and cut off the magician's beard in order to free him from the grip of the deceased. The deeper significance of this story, if any, one may supply for oneself.

## Deathbed Riddle

### Cryptic Will

A man on his deathbed said to his family: *chavita de-afra le-chad berai* — a barrel of dirt to one son; *chavita de-garmi le-chad berai* — a barrel of bones to a second son; *ve-chavita de-udra le-chad berai* — and a barrel of cloth scraps to a third. *Lo havu yad'i mai ka-amar lehu* — they had no idea what he meant. They came to Rabbi Bana'ah, who asked three questions: do you own land? Yes. Do you own animals? Yes. Do you own clothes or cushions? Yes. *Ee hachi hachi ka-amar lechu* — that is precisely what he was saying. The land is the dirt, the animals are the bones, and the cloth or cushioning is the scraps of fabric.

### Mortality Lesson

But why did he not simply say so directly? The Maharsha explains that this wealthy man, dividing up his estate among his sons, wanted simultaneously to impart a measure of *mussar*. Rather than allowing his children to focus entirely on how much they would inherit, he framed his bequests in the language of decay and mortality: we all return to the dirt, we all become a pile of bones, we all dissolve like cloth left to rot in a grave. The coded language was a reminder of impermanence, delivered precisely at the moment of distributing material wealth.

## Inheritance Dispute

### Wife's Betrayal

The Gemara introduces another case that came before Rabbi Bana'ah. A certain man overheard his wife instructing their daughter: *amai lo tzeni'at be-issura* — why are you not more discreet in your sinful conduct? And the wife then disclosed about herself: *asara benei it li, ve-leis li me-avuch ela chad* — I have ten sons, and only one of them is your father's child. On his deathbed, this man declared: *kol nichsai le-chad bera* — all my property goes to one son. He clearly meant his biological son, but he specified no name, leaving it for others to determine — or perhaps for a *beis din* to sort out.

*Lo yediah le-hai mineihu ve-lo le-hai mineihu* — they did not know to which son to award the estate. They came to Rabbi Bana'ah.

### Grave Test

Rabbi Bana'ah instructed them: *zilu chavutu kivra de-avuchon* — go bang on your father's grave, *ad de-kai* — until he rises, as we have seen the dead do in these stories, *u-meimar lachu le-hai mineichu shavki* — and he will tell you which son he left it to. Could anyone anticipate where this is heading?

### Character Proof

*Azlu kuleihu* — they all went to bang on the grave. *Ve-hu de-vrei hava lo azal* — the one who was genuinely the man's son refused to go. He would not bang on his father's grave. *Amar lehu kuleihu nichsai de-hai* — Rabbi Bana'ah ruled that the entire estate belongs to this one.

Now, did the legitimate son know that he was the biological son? The Rashbam says he did not. The reasoning must therefore rest on a deeper premise: since all the other sons were *mamzerim*, they evidently lacked the quality of character and *neshama* that would have restrained them from desecrating their father's grave. The legitimate son, whatever he knew about his own parentage, possessed the character that made such an act unthinkable. The Gemara is implicitly assuming that the quality of one's birth shapes one's fundamental character in ways that express themselves in moments exactly like this.

### Court & Prison

The other sons were not pleased. *Azlu achalu kurtza be-malka* — they all went and slandered him before the king. *Achalu kurtza* is an idiomatic expression for slander; the precise etymology of *kurtza* is unclear, though it may evoke the image of speaking while eating privately. They told the court: *ika gavra chad bi-Yehudai de-ka mapik mamona me-inshei bela sahadei u-vela midi* — there is one man among the Jews who extracts people's money without witnesses and without any proper evidence, effectively robbing them of their inheritance. *Atiyuhu ve-chavshuhu* — they brought Rabbi Bana'ah before the court and threw him in prison.

## Wife's Parable

### Slave Riddle

His wife came to intercede. She presented the following riddle to the royal court: *avda chad hava li* — I had a certain slave. *Pasku le-reisheih* — they cut off his head. *U-fashtu le-mascheih* — they stripped his skin. *Ve-achalu bisreih* — they ate his flesh. *Ve-kamlu beih maya* — they filled his skin with water. *U-mashku beih le-chavraya* — and they gave others to drink from it. *Ve-lo kaheivi li dameih ve-lo agreih* — and they have not paid me his price or his hire.

*Lo yadei mai kamra lehu* — the court had no idea what she was talking about. They said: *neito le-chakima de-Yehudai ve-lima* — let us bring the Jewish sage and have him explain. This sequence echoes the story of Joseph: a mysterious riddle, perplexity in the royal court, and a Hebrew sage summoned from confinement to interpret it. There is also a dimension of *dina de-malchuta dina* — the engagement with the non-Jewish legal system — present in this episode.

### Goat Solution

*Kariyuhu le-Rabbi Bana'ah* — they summoned Rabbi Bana'ah. *Amar lehu zarnuka amra lachu* — he said to them: she is talking about her goat. The goat served her; they slaughtered it, ate its meat, filled its skin to use as a water vessel, and have not compensated her. With that single substitution — slave means goat — the riddle dissolved.

### Deeper Meaning

The Maharsha, however, finds this resolution somewhat unsatisfying on its own. Is it so brilliant merely to say that "slave" means "goat"? The Maharsha reads the wife's parable on a second, parallel level: she was also describing her husband. *My slave* — my husband, who labored and provided for the family. *They cut off his head* — they effectively decapitated him by throwing him in prison. He then traces the remaining elements of the parable, showing how each detail maps onto what was done to Rabbi Bana'ah. In this reading, she was simultaneously presenting a riddle that secured his release and filing a covert complaint before the court about the injustice perpetrated against her husband — with Rabbi Bana'ah's answer of "goat" providing just enough of a surface explanation to satisfy the court without exposing the deeper meaning.

## Judge & Gates

### Judge Rule

*Hoil ve-chakim kulei hai* — since he is so wise, the court declared, *letvei abava ve-nidun dina* — let him sit at the gate and serve as a judge. Courts in the ancient world conducted their proceedings at the city gates — echoing the *lo yoshev be-sha'ar Sodom* and similar biblical imagery.

Observing what was inscribed on the gate of the court, Rabbi Bana'ah read: *kol dayan d'mitkari l'din lo shmei dayan* — any judge who has ever been brought before a court as a litigant is not truly called a judge. A person against whom charges have been brought, who has been subjected to litigation, lacks the moral authority to serve as a judge. There is something beautiful about this principle: the judge must stand genuinely above suspicion.

### Refinement

Rabbi Bana'ah objected. *Ela me'ata atei inish me-alma u-mashkhei l'dina batrei* — what is to stop someone from simply fabricating a charge against a judge he dislikes, thereby disqualifying him? Under this rule, any litigant could neutralize an inconveniently honest judge by the simple expedient of suing him for something trivial. It is worth noting, as the Maharsha observes, that there was likely a subtext here: the court may have been mocking Rabbi Bana'ah — yes, we'll make you a judge, even though you yourself have just been a defendant. Rabbi Bana'ah was responding to both the logical flaw and the implicit taunt simultaneously.

The refined formulation, Rabbi Bana'ah said, should read: *kol dayan d'mitkari l'din u-mapki minei mamona be-din lav shmei dayan* — only a judge against whom not merely a charge was brought, but against whom money was actually extracted by force of judgment — who was found liable, whose refusal to pay had to be overcome by compulsion of the court — only that person is disqualified. Such a person has demonstrated a willingness to compromise his integrity for money, and cannot be trusted on the bench.

### Tosafot Detail

Tosafot refines the ruling further. *Ke-gon im kafar ve-hotzi'u mimenu be-eidim* — the disqualification applies specifically when the judge denied the claim and witnesses then proved him wrong, establishing that he had lied. *Aval im chiyavuhu shevua ve-lo ratza le-hishava ve-shileim* — but if he was required to take an oath to discharge himself, and instead of swearing he simply chose to pay, that does not disqualify him. Not every such payment proves dishonesty; a person who refrains from swearing even a true oath, out of particular reverence for oaths generally — a characteristically Jewish scruple — and therefore opts to pay rather than invoke God's name, cannot be said to have proven himself willing to lie. That would not bar him from serving as a judge.

### Amended Rule

The court, rather than erasing its original inscription, added an asterisk: *bram savvei d'Yehudai amri* — however, the elders of Israel say — *kol dayan d'mitkari l'din u-mapki minei mamona be-din lav shmei dayan*. The Jewish qualification was appended alongside the original rule without replacing it. This leaves the court's own rule looking somewhat foolish by implication.

One can appreciate the underlying logic of the original rule, even so. There is a meaningful argument that a judge who has been publicly charged — even if ultimately innocent — loses a certain moral authority in the eyes of the community. Public perception, as has been famously observed, operates independently of fact; it may be easier to change reality than to change perception. But Rabbi Bana'ah's practical objection — that the rule as stated is too easily weaponized — was compelling enough that it had to be incorporated.

## Medical Wisdom

### Blood Death

Rabbi Bana'ah also encountered another inscription — apparently somewhere near the gate, though possibly elsewhere — reading: *berosh kol mossa ana dam* — at the head of all deaths am I, blood. The Rashbam understands this as a reference to excess blood in the body — the medical theory underlying bloodletting — as the primary cause of death. Interestingly, by a curious coincidence, the leading causes of death in the contemporary developed world are cardiovascular diseases: heart attacks and strokes, all of which are indeed blood-related in various ways.

### Objections

Rabbi Bana'ah challenged this claim. *D'naflu me-igra u-mis* — someone who falls from a roof and dies, *d'naflu mi-dikla u-mis* — someone who falls from a palm tree and dies: *damma katleh* — did blood kill him? Clearly not; the impact did. And *man d'darchei le-memis u-mashkecha chammar ve-chayeh* — someone who is dying a natural death, can you simply give him wine and revive him? The inscription claimed wine was the supreme life-giving force, but that is plainly not how death works.

### Illness Correction

The corrections Rabbi Bana'ah proposed: *berosh kol mar'in ana dam* — at the head of all *illnesses* am I, blood. Violent and accidental deaths have their own causes, but the predominant cause of disease is blood — whether through excess or disorder. Similarly: *berosh kol asvin ana chammar* — at the head of all healthful things am I, wine. Wine does not conquer death, but as a regimen for health it stands paramount.

### Wine & Health

*Kasevu hachi* — they wrote accordingly, adding the qualified formulation with an asterisk attributing it to the Jewish sages, alongside their own original version. And they appended one further line: *be-asra de-lais chammar tamman mitba'u samanin* — in a place where wine is unavailable, that is where one must resort to medicines. The clear implication is that where wine is regularly consumed, the need for medical intervention diminishes substantially — a proposition that modern studies of moderate red wine consumption have not entirely refuted.

The Gemara then notes an inscription at the gate of Cappadocia — a major metropolitan center. *Anpik anvag antol* — three terms the Rashbam identifies as three equivalent measurements, the point being that different localities use different words for the same unit. Tosafot takes a different approach, reading *anpik* and *anvag* as referring to some type of healthful food or liquid, with *antol* being a unit of measurement specifying how much one should consume. *Ve-eizu antol? Zehu reyi'is* — and how much is an *antol*? It equals a *revi'it*, the standard quarter-*log* measure of approximately three and a half ounces — the minimum *shiur* of drinking used across most *halachic* contexts, including *kiddush*.

## Chazaka Intro

### Usage Rights

The Gemara now transitions from the stories of Rav Bana'ah to the *mishna* on *chazaka* — specifically, a different type of *chazaka* from the one discussed in the earlier portion of the tractate. When we previously discussed a neighbor using someone else's yard for chickens or fencing, the *chazaka* there served as *evidence of ownership*: my three years of undisturbed use prove that you must have sold me this yard, for otherwise you would have protested.

The Rambam, however, cites that earlier *mishna* in two distinct ways. In the first, one is claiming outright ownership: I assert that David sold me his yard, and my three years of storing chickens there without objection proves it. In the second — relevant to a *shutfin* arrangement, a shared courtyard — one is not claiming ownership at all. Rather, the claim is that David has implicitly granted me the ongoing *right* to use the shared space for my chickens in a manner he might otherwise find objectionable, and that by his continued silence he has waived any right to object going forward.

This second category — *chazaka* not as evidence of sale but as establishment of a usage right through acquiescence — is what our *mishna* addresses. Questions remain: does this right accrue immediately upon the neighbor's silence, or only after three years? And must one positively claim that the right was explicitly granted — *chazaka she-yesh imah ta'ana* — or is the silence itself interpreted as the granting of the right? These questions animate the discussion ahead.

### Spout Rule

*Ha-marzev ein lo chazakah* — a rain spout does not acquire *chazaka*. The scenario is this: my roof ends where your yard begins; my rain gutter channels water to a spout that overhangs your yard. You have not objected — perhaps you even benefit from the water. Does my undisturbed use give me an established right to keep the spout exactly where it is, such that you can no longer demand I remove it?

The *mishna* answers: *ein lo chazakah* — no such *chazaka* attaches to the spout itself, meaning you may demand that I move or adjust it. *Ve-yesh limkomo chazakah* — but its location does have a *chazaka*. This formulation is puzzling: the spout has no *chazaka* but its location does? The Gemara will offer three distinct explanations of this phrase, which we will examine in a moment.

### Permanence

*Ha-mazchila yesh la chazakah* — the rain gutter itself, however, does acquire *chazaka*. The gutter is a more permanent structure than the spout; if the gutter projects over your yard from the roofline and you have not objected, that silence constitutes a waiver and you may no longer demand its removal.

*Sulam ha-mitzri ein lo chazakah* — an Egyptian-style ladder, which is a light, portable stepladder, does not acquire *chazaka*. The scenario: the edge of my roof is accessible only from your yard, and I have been keeping such a ladder in your yard to service my roof. You have been generous and said nothing. Can I now claim an established right to keep my ladder there indefinitely? No — because a temporary, easily moved object, however long it has been present, does not sufficiently provoke objection. Your failure to object cannot be taken as an implicit waiver because there was never sufficient cause to demand removal of something so transitory.

*Tzuri yesh lo chazakah* — a Tyrian ladder, from *Tzor*, is an entirely different matter. This is a massive, heavy structure requiring multiple people to move. If such a ladder has been sitting in your yard for an extended period without objection, that silence speaks volumes: you clearly have acquiesced to an ongoing right, because a structure of such size and permanence would obviously provoke any reasonable person to object.

### Windows

*Chalon ha-mitzrit ein la chazakah* — a small window — the *mitzrit* variety being the smaller type — does not acquire *chazaka*. If I open a small window in my wall that theoretically allows me to look into your yard, your failure to object does not establish my right to maintain it, because a small window provides only occasional access to your space and is not the kind of provocation that demands a response. Your silence cannot be read as a waiver of your privacy rights.

*Ve-tzuri yesh lecha chazakah* — but a large window does establish *chazaka*. The larger the window, the more easily and regularly I can look into your yard with every casual glance. If you have tolerated that for three years, your silence becomes significant. The counterintuitive principle is this: the greater the invasion, the more your silence proves implicit consent, because the provocation was sufficiently obvious that any objection would naturally have been forthcoming.

*Eizehu chalon ha-tzuri?* The *mishna* defines the small variety as one through which the head of a person cannot pass.

### Window Frame

*Rabbi Yehuda omer* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that *im yesh lo malben* — if the window has a proper frame around it, *af al pi she-ein rosho shel adam yachol le-hikanes le-tocho* — even though a person's head cannot fit through it, *harei zeh chazakah* — it nonetheless acquires *chazakah*. The reasoning is that affixing a frame around a window demonstrates an intent of permanence; it is no longer a casual opening but a finished installation. Whether that alone is sufficient provocation to trigger *chazakah* — even absent the ability to look through it fully — is a question we will see the Gemara address.

The underlying principle running through all these cases is consistent: a temporary or trivially intrusive use of another's property does not establish any right, because the neighbor's inaction cannot be fairly construed as consent. But where the use is substantial, permanent, and obvious enough that any normal person would have objected, continued silence implies acquiescence — and that acquiescence itself either constitutes the granting of the right or, as the Rashbam frames it, proves that the right must have been formally purchased at some point.

## Gemara Marzev

### Two Ends

*Mai marzev ein lo chazakah ve-yesh limkomo chazakah* — what does it mean that the spout lacks *chazakah* but its location retains one? *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — the explanation is this: *ha-marzev ein lo chazakah me-ruach achat ve-yesh limkomo chazakah mi-shtei ruchot*. A gutter with one stopped end and one open end can have its spout positioned at either side. Your undisturbed use of the spout at the current end gives you an implicit right to maintain *a* spout somewhere on the gutter — that is the *chazakah* of its location — but I can demand that you move the spout to the *other* end if it suits me, since it is not fixed in its precise placement. *Ein lo chazakah* in its exact spot, *yesh limkomo chazakah* in one of the two available positions.

Tosafot raises the obvious mechanical question: since a gutter must be pitched in one direction for the water to drain, can the spout truly be functional at either end? This is a legitimate difficulty that Tosafot pursues, though we proceed with the other explanations for now.

### Shorten Length

*Amar Rav Chanina* — here the explanation turns on length rather than position: *ha-marzev ein lo chazakah she-im hayah aroch me-katzro* — I can insist that a spout protruding three feet into my yard be shortened to three inches, the minimum necessary to channel water away from the wall. What you cannot do is eliminate the spout entirely: *ve-yesh limkomo chazakah she-im ba la-akro eino okro* — if you come to uproot it completely, you cannot, since clearly you need some spout to drain the roof, and your undisturbed use has established an implicit right to maintain it. The *chazakah* of its location means: the spout stays, but not necessarily at its current length.

### Build Below

*Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba amar* — the third explanation approaches the question from below: *ha-marzev ein lo chazakah she-im rotzeh livnot tachtav boneh* — I, the yard owner, am free to build beneath the spout. The fact that I have not objected to the spout's presence does not mean I have waived control over the airspace beneath it. I may build right up under it, even if the structure will interfere with the spout's operation. *Ve-yesh limkomo chazakah she-im ba la-akro eino okro* — but the one right I cannot demand is that the spout be removed entirely from where it sits. Your *chazakah* is in the continued existence of the spout at its location; it is not a *chazakah* over the airspace below it, and I retain the right to build in that space independently.
