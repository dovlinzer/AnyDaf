# Bekhorot 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Judicial Liability Review

### Bad vs. No Judgment

The present daf opens by returning briefly to the major themes of daf 28, which centered on the question of judicial liability for legal malpractice. The discussion there explored a fundamental distinction: on one end of the spectrum, a ruling so defective that it cannot even be characterized as a *din* at all — provided the judge did not physically transfer funds — carries no liability; on the other end, a *mumche l'din*, a licensed and acknowledged expert judge, is likewise free from liability. These two exemptions bracket the middle ground where liability actually attaches.

### Kiblu Aleihu & Din Torah

This framework connects to a broader discussion found in Sanhedrin concerning the role of *kiblu aleihu* — the parties' acceptance of a particular individual as their adjudicator. Where the parties accepted the judge unconditionally, with no further stipulations, there would be no liability, as the ruling would not be characterized as a *to'eh* in any meaningful sense. However, where the acceptance was contingent — for instance, where the parties normally could not have gone before a single judge or a relative, but agreed to do so on the condition that the judge issue a genuine *din torah*, a binding halachic ruling — the full framework of error and liability still applies. That discussion, however, belongs to Sanhedrin and is not taken up here.

### Liability: Error vs. Aut…

A further ambiguity in the *mishnah* is worth noting before moving on. The case of the *bechor* established that a person who was not a recognized expert — an *eino mumche* — bore liability when his ruling led to an improper *shechita*. But the subsequent *mishnah*, which simply states that one who judges and errs must pay, does not specify that a mistake was actually made. By juxtaposition, the *mishnah* seems to suggest that even a correct ruling by an unauthorized judge triggers liability — as though the lack of authorization is itself the disqualifying factor, regardless of outcome. Yet the *gemara* here, like the *gemara* in Sanhedrin, engages at length with the question of *to'eh b'dvar mishna* versus *to'eh b'shikul hadaas* — error in black-letter law versus error in judgment — and the case of Rabbi Tarfon cited there is a clear instance of an actual mistake. So there remains some ambiguity: does liability follow from demonstrable error, or does the *mishnah* treat the absence of authorization as constructively equivalent to error, regardless of whether the ruling itself was correct?

## Ilo & Fee Structure

### Ilo Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּאִילָא בְּיַבְנֶה, שֶׁהִתִּירוּ לוֹ (בְּיַבְנֶה) חֲכָמִים לִהְיוֹת נוֹטֵל אַרְבַּע אִיסָּרוֹת לִבְהֵמָה דַּקָּה, וְשִׁשָּׁה לְגַסָּה, בֵּין תָּם וּבֵין בַּעַל מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **like Ila in Yavne, whom the Sages in Yavne permitted to take** a wage of **four *issar* for** issuing a ruling concerning **a small animal and six** *issar* **for** issuing a ruling concerning **a large animal.** They permitted this provided that he would be paid **whether** it turned out that the firstborn was **unblemished or whether** it was **blemished.**

With those questions noted, the *gemara* turns to an entirely different domain — the question of accepting payment for the performance of religious functions. The *mishnah* at the bottom of 28b states: *hanotel sachar lirot es habechorot* — one who accepts a fee for inspecting *bechorot* — *ein shochtin al piv*, one may not *shecht* on his ruling. The very fact that payment is involved renders his determinations suspect, even if he is otherwise expert. One might ask whether expertise alone is not sufficient to validate the ruling; but the *mishnah* makes clear that financial interest overrides the presumption of competence.

The *mishnah* then introduces an exception: *ke'ilo b'yavne* — like Ilo, the recognized *bechor* inspector in Yavne, whom the *chachamim* permitted to take four *issarot* for a small animal and six for a large animal, whether he ruled it *tam* or *ba'al mum*.

### Ilo's Piety

Rashi explains the exception briefly but pointedly: *ke'ilo hadar b'yavne dechasid hava v'lo nechshad bekach* — Ilo was so extraordinarily pious that there was no objective ground for suspicion that payment would influence his rulings. His personal character removed the concern that animates the general prohibition.

### Flat Fee Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? הַאי נְפִישׁ טִירְחֵיהּ, וְהַאי לָא נְפִישׁ טִירְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that the Sages in Yavne said that Ila could take a wage of four *issar* for issuing a ruling concerning a small animal but six *issar* for issuing a ruling concerning a large animal? The Gemara answers: Examining **this** one, a firstborn of a large animal, for blemishes involves **great effort; but** examining **that** one, the firstborn of a small animal, does **not** involve such **great effort.**

The *gemara* first asks why the fee for a large animal exceeds that for a small one. The answer is straightforward: *hai nafish tirchei v'hai lo nafish tirchei* — one involves considerably more effort than the other. One might have assumed that since the inspection focuses on a specific suspected blemish rather than the whole animal, the size difference is irrelevant. Rashi explains otherwise: *gassa nafish tirchaihu l'hashlicha l'aretz l'chpota v'liros muma* — a cow must be brought to the ground and held down in order to examine the blemish properly, which is a substantially more demanding physical task. The differential fee reflects that differential effort.

The *gemara* then probes the logic of the flat fee structure. The payment seems justified when the ruling is *ba'al mum* — the owner gains a tangible benefit when the animal is declared blemished and permitted for slaughter. But why should the owner pay when the ruling goes against him, when the animal is declared *tam*? The *gemara* answers: *de'im ken ati l'michshdei* — if payment were only made upon a favorable ruling, people would suspect the inspector. They would say: *hai ba'al mum tam hu v'hai deka shari lei mishum agra* — this animal is really unblemished, but he declared it *ba'al mum* because he was being paid for the result.

### One Payment Takana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין תָּם וּבֵין בַּעַל מוּם. בִּשְׁלָמָא בַּעַל מוּם — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא שָׁרֵי לֵיהּ, אֶלָּא תָּם אַמַּאי? דְּאִם כֵּן, אָתֵי לְמִיחְשְׁדֵיהּ, וְאָמְרִי: הַאי בַּעַל מוּם תָּם הוּא, וְהַאי דְּקָא שָׁרֵי לֵיהּ — מִשּׁוּם אַגְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one like Ila may take his wages provided that he would be paid **whether** it turns out that the firstborn is **unblemished or whether** it is **blemished.** The Gemara inquires: **Granted,** if the animal is **blemished** he takes his wages, **because he deems** the animal **permitted** to be slaughtered and eaten; **but** if he examined **an unblemished** animal, **why** does he take his wages? The Gemara answers **that if so,** i.e., if he would be paid for examining only a blemished animal, people **would come to suspect him and say** that **this** animal that he declared to be **blemished** is in fact **unblemished, and** he declared it blemished and thereby **deemed it permitted** to be eaten only **due to** the fact that he wanted to claim his **wages.**

The *gemara* presses further: even with a flat fee, suspicion has merely shifted direction. Now one might say that when the inspector rules *tam*, people will suspect he is prolonging the process — that he declared it unblemished precisely so that the owner will bring the animal back repeatedly, generating additional inspection fees. The *gemara* responds: *chada zimna takinu lei rabanan, tarti zimnei lo takinu lei rabanan* — the same rabbinic institution that authorized Ilo's fee structure also stipulated that he may only be paid once per animal. The inspector receives one payment, regardless of how many subsequent inspections occur. With repeat-business incentive eliminated, the suspicion of ruling *tam* for financial gain collapses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תָּם נָמֵי אָמְרִי: בַּעַל מוּם הוּא, וְהַאי דְּלָא קָשָׁרֵי לֵיהּ סָבַר כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִשְׁקוֹל אַגְרֵיהּ זִימְנָא אַחֲרִיתִי! חַד זִימְנָא תַּקִּינוּ לֵיה רַבָּנַן, תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי לָא תַּקִּינוּ לֵיה רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then in a situation where the examiner is paid either way, people might **also state** a similar claim with regard to one who rules that it is **an unblemished** animal. They may suspect that **this** animal is really **blemished, and** the reason that **this** expert **did not deem it permitted** is that **he holds** that it is preferable for him to declare it unblemished **so that he can collect his wages another time,** when he examines it later and declares it blemished. The Gemara answers: **The Sages** of Yavne **instituted** an ordinance that he may take his wages for examining a firstborn **once,** but **the Sages did not institute** that he may take his wages **twice** for examining the same animal. Since he will not get paid a second time for the same animal, he has no incentive to declare it unblemished if it has a blemish.

### Blemish Incentive

Yet this resolution creates its own paradox. If the inspector receives only one payment per animal and cannot be compensated for future inspections of the same animal, the incentive now runs in the opposite direction: he has every reason to declare *ba'al mum* on the first inspection, collect his single fee, and be done with it. Why bother ruling *tam* and being called back repeatedly, knowing that no further payment is forthcoming?

### Right of Refusal

Tosafot addresses this directly. The concern would be that even with one-time payment, people would suspect the *ba'al mum* ruling was motivated by the desire to avoid the burden of repeated inspections. Tosafot answers: *shelo chiyu hu chachamim lirot pa'am acheret* — the *chachamim* never obligated the inspector to examine the same animal a second time. The right of refusal always remains with the inspector. *Ela d'im ra'ahu hu d'ein yachol litol alav sachar* — only if he voluntarily agrees to inspect again is he barred from collecting an additional fee, but he is never compelled to do so. Therefore, no one can claim his *ba'al mum* ruling was motivated by the desire to avoid future effort, since he could simply decline any future inspection without consequence. Tosafot adds a second answer: we would not carry the suspicion that far in any case.

The broader point is that this entire carefully constructed system — flat fee, equal payment for *tam* and *ba'al mum*, one payment per animal — was in place only for Ilo, as a sanctioned exception. The general principle remains that accepting any fee for this function, however structured, is prohibited, because financial interest inevitably raises questions of objectivity.

## Payment Prohibitions

### Judges & Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנּוֹטֵל שְׂכָרוֹ לָדוּן — דִּינָיו בְּטֵילִים, לְהָעִיד — עֵדוֹתָיו בְּטֵילִין, לְהַזּוֹת וּלְקַדֵּשׁ — מֵימָיו מֵי מְעָרָה, אֶפְרוֹ אֵפֶר מִקְלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who takes his wages to judge** cases, **his rulings are void.** In the case of one who takes wages **to testify, his testimonies are void.** With regard to one who takes wages **to sprinkle** the purification waters of the red heifer upon one who contracted impurity imparted by a corpse, **and** one who takes wages **to sanctify** those waters, the halakhic status of **his water is** that of **cave water, and** the status of **his ashes is** that of mere **burnt ashes.**

The *mishnah* now extends this principle well beyond the specific case of *bechor* inspection. *Ha'notel sacharo ladun* — a judge who accepts payment for his rulings — *dinav betelin*, his judgments are nullified. *Leha'id* — if a witness accepts payment for testifying — *eduto betela*, his testimony is thrown out. The stakes here are plain: the entire institution of expert witness testimony — an enormously lucrative field in contemporary legal practice, where witnesses are retained precisely because they are expected to perform in a particular direction — is directly implicated by this principle.

### Parah Adumah Payment

The *mishnah* continues beyond the realm of adjudication and testimony: *lehazot ulekadesh* — if one accepts payment for sprinkling the ashes of *parah adumah* or for sanctifying the ashes by mixing them with well water — *meimav mei me'arah* — his water becomes like the water of a cave, i.e., it is not valid *mayim chayim* — and *afro efer mikleh* — his ashes are like ordinary fireplace ash. The purification process is entirely annulled.

### Subjective Functions

This is a striking expansion. The concern about payment for judgment and testimony can be understood through the lens of integrity and objectivity — money compromises the subjective determinations of judges and witnesses. But the preparation of *parah adumah* involves no such subjective determination. The functionary is simply performing a physical act of preparation; there is no ruling to be corrupted, no testimony to be distorted. Yet payment for this act is equally disqualifying. The *gemara* will generalize this principle across *mitzvot* broadly: one is never to accept payment for the performance of a *mitzvah*. The inclusion of *parah adumah* in the *mishnah* can be read as establishing precisely this point — that the prohibition is not limited to contexts where money might compromise judgment, but extends to the very mixing of *mitzvah* performance with monetary gain.

One might alternatively read the *mishnah* as *davka* these cases — judgment, testimony, and *parah adumah* — because they each involve a situation of dependency and power differential. In the case of *parah adumah*, one who requires purification is entirely dependent on another to perform the act; one cannot sprinkle the ashes on oneself. This power asymmetry, combined with the enormous rarity and value of *parah adumah* itself, creates conditions where financial incentive could be particularly corrupting or at least suspicious. That reading has considerable force. It is worth noting that the *gemara* does in fact generalize the principle to all *mitzvot*, but the more limited reading of the *mishnah* itself remains textually defensible.

### Kohen Expenses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה כֹּהֵן, מְטַמְּאֵהוּ מִתְּרוּמָתוֹ — מַאֲכִילוֹ וּמַשְׁקוֹ וְסָכוֹ, וְאִם הָיָה זָקֵן — מַרְכִּיבוֹ עַל הַחֲמוֹר, וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ כְּפוֹעֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Although taking actual wages is prohibited, **if** the one examining the firstborn, or the judge, or the witness, **was a priest,** and the one who requires his services **rendered him impure** and prevented him **from** partaking of **his *teruma*,** that person **must provide** the priest **with food, drink, and** oil for **smearing** on his body from his own non-sacred property. **And likewise if** the one examining the firstborn, or the judge, or the witness, **was an elderly person,** the one who requires his services **transports him on a donkey. And** in all these cases, although it is prohibited to take wages, the one who requires his services **gives him his wages like** the wages of **a laborer,** as he was unable to perform his usual labor that day.

Before turning to the *gemara*'s discussion of the Scriptural source, the *mishnah* introduces the case of a *kohen* who became *tamei* in the course of traveling to fulfill the *mitzvah* of testimony or adjudication. As a result, he can no longer eat his *terumah* and must purchase the more expensive *chulin* food instead. May he be reimbursed for this additional expense?

---

## Kohen Tamei Case

### Food Compensation

The *mishnah* rules that he may be fed and given drink and oil — that is, compensated for the additional costs incurred as a result of his *tumah*. The reimbursement covers only the differential expense caused by becoming *tamei* — the cost of *chulin* food in place of *terumah* — and not a general food allowance.

## Payment Prohibitions…

### Sechar Batel

The *mishnah* adds: if the witness or judge was elderly and could not travel unassisted, one pays for his transportation — *markivo al hachamor*, the donkey, equivalent to a car service today. And finally: *venosein lo secharo kepo'el* — one pays him his fee as a worker. The *gemara* will clarify that this means *sechar batelah* — payment for lost opportunity, for the time he took away from his regular occupation — not a fee for the service of judging or testifying itself. The distinction between time and service will occupy the *gemara* at length.

## Torah Teaching Source

### Moshe's Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״רְאֵה לִמַּדְתִּי אֶתְכֶם וְגוֹ׳״, מָה אֲנִי בְּחִנָּם — אַף אַתֶּם בְּחִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the rulings of one who takes wages to judge cases are void. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter,** that a judge may not be paid for his rulings, derived? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says** that it is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "Behold, I have taught you** statutes and ordinances, as the Lord my God commanded me, that you should do so in the midst of the land that you are going in to possess" (Deuteronomy 4:5). This teaches that **just as I,** Moses, learned the word of God **for free, so too, you** learned it from me **for free,** and this is also how you should act with all future generations.

The *gemara* asks: *mina hani mili* — what is the Scriptural source for the prohibition against accepting payment for these functions? Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: the verse states, *Re'eh limadeti etchem chukim umishpatim ka'asher tzivani Hashem Elokai* — Behold I have taught you statutes and judgments just as Hashem my God commanded me. The *gemara* derives: *ma ani bechinam af atem bechinam* — just as I, Moshe, taught you without payment, so too you must teach others without payment. The same way God taught Moshe freely, Moshe transmitted that teaching freely, and the chain must continue unbroken.

### Emet Kneh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוַּנִי ה׳ אֱלֹהָי״ — מָה אֲנִי בְּחִנָּם, אַף אַתֶּם בְּחִנָּם. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם לֹא מָצָא בְּחִנָּם שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּשָׂכָר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֱמֶת קְנֵה״. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמַר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁלְּמַדְתִּיהָ בְּשָׂכָר כָּךְ אֲלַמְּדֶנָּה בְּשָׂכָר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֱמֶת קְנֵה וְאַל תִּמְכֹּר״.
>
> **Translation:** **This** *halakha* **is also taught** in a *baraita*: "Behold, I have taught you statutes and ordinances, **as the Lord my God commanded me"** (Deuteronomy 4:5). **Just as I** learned from God **for free, so too, you** learned from me **for free. And from where** is it derived **that if** one **cannot find** someone to teach him **for free that he should learn** even **by** giving **payment? The verse states: "Buy the truth,** and do not sell it; also wisdom, and instruction, and understanding" (Proverbs 23:23). **And from where** is it derived **that** one **should not say: Just as I learned by** giving **payment, so too, I will teach it by** receiving **payment? The verse states: "Buy the truth, and do not sell it."**

A *baraita* corroborates: just as I teach *bechinam*, so must you teach *bechinam*.

The *baraita* immediately asks: how do we know that if one cannot find a teacher who will teach for free, one should nonetheless pay for learning? The answer: *talmud lomar emet kneh* — the verse says, "purchase truth." If the only available teacher demands a fee, one should pay. The supreme value of Torah study overrides the impropriety of the teacher's conduct; one does not decline an education because the teacher is behaving wrongly.

And conversely: how do we know that once one has paid for one's own learning, one should not argue that since one's own teachers charged, one is entitled to do the same? The verse continues: *emet kneh ve'al timkor* — purchase truth, but do not sell it. One may spend freely to acquire Torah; one may not charge to transmit it.

This formulation illuminates an important structural point. One might have thought that the principle against payment should generate a *takkanah* parallel to the one governing judgment and testimony — that Torah transmitted for payment is somehow *batel*, invalidated. Yet no such consequence is applied. Instead, the student is instructed to seek out free teaching and, when unavoidable, to pay without thereby legitimizing the practice. This asymmetry reflects the *gemara*'s recognition that *talmud torah* occupies a position of such supreme value that the normal enforcement mechanism — nullification — is suspended in order to preserve the institution of Torah study itself.

## Teachers & Officials

### Sechar Batel Principle

This raises the obvious question: how do we justify paying teachers, *rabbanim*, and full-time *dayanim* in practice? The answer turns on *sechar batelah* — compensation for lost opportunity, for the time one is not pursuing other work. The principle is that one may pay a judge, witness, or teacher not for the service itself but for the opportunity cost of their time. This is symbolized, however inadequately, by the nominal daily fee paid to jurors in civil courts, which is meant to represent time rather than expertise.

The *sechar batelah* framework has a significant structural implication: it would mean, in principle, that teachers and *dayanim* should be paid not according to the quality of their service, nor according to their seniority in their current role, but according to what they could have earned doing something else. A person who left a career in law or medicine to become a teacher would, on this model, receive a substantially higher salary than someone with no other marketable skills — not because the former is a better teacher, but because the value of what they gave up is greater.

### Community Funds

Tosafot addresses the problem of full-time judges and officials. The *gemara* in other contexts records that *dayanei gezeirot* — the permanent judges in Jerusalem — received salaries from the Temple treasury. How could this be, given the prohibition? Tosafot writes: *vein litmoah al dayanei gezeirot shebishchar hayu notlin schar mitrumat halishka* — one should not be astonished that these judges received payment from communal funds. The reason, Tosafot explains, is: *dekivan dechol askam haya al ze velo hayu oskim beshum melacha* — since this was their entire occupation and they had no other livelihood, *al korcham tzrichim lehitparnes* — they necessarily had to be supported. When someone devotes their entire professional life to a communal function and has no other income, the payment is not framed as a fee for services but as a living stipend enabling their dedication. The community's need for that institution overrides the formal concern about payment for *mitzvah* performance.

## Payment & Mitzvah

### Talmud Torah Exception

A further question arises from the structure of the *mishnah*: we throw out the judgment, nullify the testimony, invalidate the *parah adumah* when payment is involved. Why do we not apply the same consequence to *talmud torah*? The *gemara* permits paying for Torah study when no free option is available, and does not say the Torah learned from a paid teacher is *batel*. This asymmetry demands explanation.

### Supreme Value

The answer lies in the supreme value of *talmud torah* itself. The concern that money might corrupt the integrity of a religious function is always present in principle, but the institution of Torah learning is so foundational that the normal enforcement mechanism — nullification of the act — is suspended. We allow what we do not approve of, because *talmud torah* transcends the ordinary calculus.

### Communal Necessity

The same logic applies, by extension, to supporting full-time teachers, *rabbis*, and *dayanim*. The societal need for dedicated, expert transmission of Torah and *halacha* is itself an overriding value, one that justifies departure from the strict theoretical framework. This is not merely *sechar batelah* — it is recognition that when someone devotes their entire working life to communal religious service, the community must sustain that person. As Tosafot frames it, this is not payment for doing a *mitzvah*; it is support for a person who has placed themselves entirely in service of the community's spiritual needs.

---

## Modern Application

### Rambam Critique

The Rambam has a well-known and sharp critique of those who draw on communal funds while engaged in full-time Torah study without performing direct communal service. The question of *kollel* stipends — whether Torah study alone, without a functional communal role, justifies communal support — is a distinct and contested matter. The Rambam's critique focuses specifically on this scenario and does not directly undermine the legitimacy of supporting those who perform actual teaching, rabbinic, or judicial functions for the community.

---

## Torah Teaching Source…

### Rashi's Limitation

Rashi, in his comment on *mina hani mili*, specifies that the Scriptural source and its derived prohibition apply to *hora'ah* — the giving of rulings — and to *talmud torah*, the transmission of Torah. This connects to the verse's subject matter: Moshe is speaking not abstractly but specifically about *chukim umishpatim*, statutes and legal norms, and teaching what the law requires. The link between *hora'ah* and *talmud torah* in this context is natural — both are transmission of the substance of *halacha* — and from there the *gemara* extends the principle to the full range of *mitzvot*. Whether this extension is logically necessary or whether it is better read as a *gezerat hakasuv* — a Scriptural decree whose extension is learned rather than derived — is a point of analysis, but the *gemara*'s practical application of the principle is not limited to teaching and ruling.

## Invalidation Gezerah

### vs. Shochad

The obvious question is: why is a separate principle needed at all? Is this not simply a case of *shochad* — bribery? The Torah already prohibits *shochad* and states explicitly that *ki hashochad ye'aver einei* — bribery blinds the eyes of the wise. Why is a distinct source necessary to invalidate paid judgments?

Tosafot responds that *shochad* is specifically payment from only one side, with an implicit or explicit expectation that the judge will exert himself on behalf of the paying party. The Rema notes that *shochad* is forbidden even when the judge takes it with the intent to remain objective, because the giving party's intent is that the judge will work harder for them. The cases in our *mishnah*, however, may involve payment from communal funds, payment shared equally by both parties, or a straightforward fee-for-services arrangement with no directional expectation. None of these constitute *shochad* in the technical sense. Yet the *mishnah* still invalidates the judgment.

### Why Throw Out

The question then is sharper: if it is not *shochad*, why throw out the judgment? The person may have ruled entirely correctly. The testimony may have been perfectly accurate. Why do we negate the result?

### Prevention Takana

The answer is that the invalidation is a rabbinic *gezerah* or *takkanah*, not a biblical consequence. The rabbis determined that no money should be involved in the processes of judgment, testimony, and — by extension — *parah adumah* preparation, and they established the nullification of the result as the enforcement mechanism. For *talmud torah*, they deliberately chose not to apply this enforcement, as the value of Torah study is too great to allow the prohibition to function as a barrier to learning. For the full-time judges supported by communal funds, they similarly allowed exceptions where the structural concern — that money might compromise integrity — does not in fact apply in the same way. The *gezerah* is not a response to demonstrated corruption but a prophylactic institution designed to keep money entirely out of these domains.

---

## Teachers & Officials…

### Rosh's Position

Returning to the case of Ilo: Rashi explained Ilo's exception on the basis of his extraordinary personal piety — *dechasid hava v'lo nechshad bekach* — he was so pious that no suspicion could attach to him. The Rosh offers a structurally different reading. The Rosh argues that Ilo was permitted to receive fees not because of his personal character, but because he was a full-time *bechor* inspector. His situation parallels the *dayanei gezeirot* of Jerusalem — people who devoted their entire professional lives to this function and had no other livelihood. On this reading, the Rosh wants to connect the Ilo exception directly to the principle that Tosafot articulates about full-time communal officials, rather than making it a personal exception grounded in Ilo's *chasidus*.

---

## Payment & Mitzvah…

### Kohanim Model

A useful analogy for this entire structure is the *kohanim* themselves. The *kohanim* were designated teachers of Torah for Israel — this was among their primary roles — and they were supported by the entire community through *terumah* and other designated portions. They were not paid per class or per ruling; rather, the community sustained them so they could devote themselves to service. This is precisely the model that Tosafot's principle invokes: not *sechar batelah* in the narrow sense of compensating for a specific missed opportunity, but communal support for a life devoted to religious function. The payment is, in essence, a living stipend enabling dedication, not a fee for services rendered.

## Parah Adumah Details

### Preparation Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַזּוֹת וּלְקַדֵּשׁ — מֵימָיו מֵי מְעָרָה, אֶפְרוֹ אֵפֶר מִקְלֶה. וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמֵי חַטָּאת בְּאֵפֶר חַטָּאת — הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא יִשְׂרָאֵל!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: With regard to one who takes wages **to sprinkle** the purification waters of the red heifer, **and** one who takes wages **to sanctify** those waters, the halakhic status of **his water is** that of **cave water, and** the status of **his ashes is** that of mere **burnt ashes.** This indicates that one may not derive any benefit from the water or ashes of the red heifer. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Kiddushin* 58a): With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with the water of purification, or with the ashes of purification, she is betrothed, and** this is the *halakha* **even if** the man betrothing her is **an Israelite,** not a priest or a Levite. This indicates that one may derive benefit from the waters or ashes of purification.

Having established the general principle, the *gemara* now engages a tension. The *mishnah* says that if one is paid to sprinkle or sanctify the *parah adumah* waters, the waters and ashes are invalidated. But a separate *mishnah* elsewhere states: *ha'mekadesh b'mei chatat u've'efer chatat harei zu mekudeshet* — one who gives the water or ashes of *parah adumah* as *kesef kiddushin* for betrothal, it works, even if he is a non-*kohen* Israelite. If one cannot derive benefit from these materials, how can they function as a medium of *kiddushin*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא קַשְׁיָא — כָּאן בִּשְׂכַר הֲבָאָה וּמִלּוּי, כָּאן בִּשְׂכַר הַזָּאָה וְקִידּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult,** since **there,** the mishna in *Kiddushin* is referring to one who betroths a woman **with** the value of **the payment for bringing** the ashes from afar **and filling** the vessel with the purification water, for which it is permitted to accept payment. Performing this act for the woman is comparable to giving her an item of value, since she will not have to pay someone to bring and fill the vessel for her. **Here,** the mishna is referring **to payment for** the actual **sprinkling and sanctifying** of the ashes.

Abaye resolves the tension: *kan bischar hava'ah u'milui, kan bischar haza'ah v'kiddush* — in one case the payment is for bringing the ashes or filling the containers, acts of preparation and effort; in the other case the payment is for the actual act of sprinkling or sanctification, the *mitzvah* act itself. Payment for preparatory effort — transporting the ashes, drawing and carrying the water — is entirely permissible; the *mitzvah* has not yet been performed and one is simply being compensated for physical labor. But payment for the *mitzvah* act itself — the sprinkling, the mixing — is what invalidates. The water used in *kiddushin* represents the value of the preparatory effort involved, which is a legitimate commodity.

### Teacher Preparation

This distinction maps naturally onto the case of teachers and *dayanim*. We are not paying them for the moment of teaching or the moment of ruling — which is the *mitzvah* act itself — but for the enormous investment of preparation that precedes it: the years of study, the preparation of lessons, the time spent in research, the effort of grading and review. That preparatory work is compensable, even if the act of transmission itself is not.

### Language Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי הָכָא ״לְהַזּוֹת וּלְקַדֵּשׁ״, וְהָתָם קָתָנֵי ״הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמֵי חַטָּאת וּבְאֵפֶר חַטָּאת״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: According to this answer, the language of the two *mishnayot* is **also precise, as it teaches here: To sprinkle, or: To sanctify,** indicating that he receives payment for the actual sprinkling and sanctification; **and it teaches there,** in the mishna in *Kiddushin*, that she is betrothed **with the water of purification or with the ashes of purification,** which indicates that the water and ashes have not yet been mixed together. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that this is the correct explanation of the *mishnayot*.

The *gemara* notes that the resolution is supported by the precise language of the *mishnah*. The prohibitory clause says *lehazot ulekadesh* — to sprinkle and to sanctify — referring to the acts themselves. The permissive clause in the other *mishnah* refers to *mei chatat v'efer chatat* — the water and ash as objects, which implies their value derives from the effort involved in preparing and transporting them, not from the *mitzvah* act itself. *Shema mina* — this is a sound inference.

## Kohen Tamei Case…

### Tamei Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה כֹּהֵן, מְטַמְּאֵהוּ מִתְּרוּמָתוֹ כּוּ׳. אִיהוּ גּוּפֵיהּ הֵיכִי אָזֵיל?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Although taking wages is prohibited, **if** the one examining the firstborn, or the judge, or the witness, **was a priest,** and the one who required his services **rendered him impure** and prevented him **from** partaking of **his *teruma*,** he must provide the priest with food, drink, and oil. The Gemara asks: But **he himself,** the priest, **how** was he permitted to **go** to a place that caused him to become impure? It is prohibited for a priest to render himself ritually impure.

Returning to the case of the *kohen* who became *tamei*: the *gemara* asks directly, *ihu gufeh heichi azil* — how was the *kohen* permitted to go and become *tamei* in the first place? A *kohen* is biblically prohibited from contracting corpse impurity. How did he incur *tumah* in the course of coming to testify?

### Blowing Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְבֵית הַפְּרָס דְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מְנַפֵּחַ אָדָם בֵּית הַפְּרָס וְהוֹלֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this is referring to a case where he went **to an area in which uncertainty exists concerning the location of a grave or a corpse [*beit haperas*],** into which it is prohibited **by rabbinic law** for a priest to enter. **As Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A person** who passes through **a *beit haperas* may blow** on the dust before taking each step, which would expose any bone beneath the dust, **and walk.** A priest may rely on that method of examination and enter the field.

The *gemara* answers: *l'Beis HaPras d'rabbanan* — we are discussing a *beis ha'pras*, a field where a grave has been lost and the concern is only rabbinic. And as Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *minapeach adam Beis HaPras v'holech* — a person may blow in front of himself while walking through a *beis ha'pras*, sifting the dirt to check for chips of bone, and proceed step by step.

Tosafot identifies two ways to read this *gemara*. One reading: the fact that blowing allows one to traverse a *beis ha'pras* proves that the *tumah* concern there is only rabbinic. Since it is only rabbinic, a *kohen* may traverse it — even without blowing — for the sake of a *mitzvah*, specifically the *mitzvah* of testifying. He still contracts *tumah*, but since the prohibition is rabbinic rather than biblical, it yields to the *mitzvah* obligation. Alternatively, the *gemara* may mean that the *kohen* did in fact employ the blowing technique and traversed in that manner, and even so he became *tamei* — because while the blowing makes traversal permissible, it does not guarantee absolute purity; he still attains *tumah* status and therefore requires reimbursement for the additional food costs.

Either way, the key structural point is the same: *tumah* incurred via *beis ha'pras* is rabbinic, and it is therefore overridden by the *mitzvah* of testimony, even though the *kohen* will still be regarded as *tamei* thereafter for the purpose of his *terumah*.

### Different Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יְהוּדָה בַּר אַמֵּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: בֵּית הַפְּרָס שֶׁנִּידַּשׁ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda bar Ami says in the name of Rav Yehuda: A *beit haperas* that has been trodden** underfoot, thereby creating a path, is **pure,** and one no longer needs to be concerned about bones, as the entire prohibition is a stringency by rabbinic law. Nevertheless, a priest who walks through such a field may not partake of *teruma* until he has purified himself.

The *gemara* then offers Rav Yehuda bar Ami's position: *Beis HaPras shenidosh tahor* — a *beis ha'pras* that has been trodden over is *tahor*, because any bone chips would have been pulverized by the foot traffic. This might suggest that in such a case the *kohen* would remain fully *tahor*, which would then undercut our *mishnah*'s premise that he became *tamei*. The resolution is that this statement proves the rabbinicity of the concern rather than establishing what happened in the *mishnah*'s case. The *mishnah*'s *kohen* traversed an untrodden area, became *tamei*, but was allowed to do so because the prohibition was only rabbinic and was overridden by the *mitzvah* of testimony.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי: בִּשְׁאָר טוּמְאוֹת, דְּלָא מַזְהַר עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the Gemara suggests that the mishna is referring to a case where the priest becomes impure **with other** forms of **impurity,** with regard to **which** priests **are not admonished.** A priest is prohibited from becoming impure only through contact with a corpse. Yet, he may not partake of *teruma* if he becomes impure even in any other manner.

The *gemara* adds a simpler alternative: *bish'ar tumos d'lo muzhar aleihu* — perhaps the *kohen* became *tamei* through a different type of *tumah* altogether, one that does not implicate the special prohibition against a *kohen* contracting corpse impurity. Contact with a *neveilah* or a *sheretz*, for instance, renders one *tamei* and prohibits eating *terumah*, but does not involve the specific biblical prohibition that applies to a *kohen* regarding the dead. In such a case, the *kohen* may have become *tamei* in a manner that was entirely permissible for him, yet still incurred the consequential expense of having to purchase *chulin* food.

## Sechar Batel Deep

### Rashi's Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה זָקֵן — מַרְכִּיבוֹ עַל הַחֲמוֹר. תְּנָא: כְּפוֹעֵל בָּטֵל. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּפוֹעֵל בָּטֵל שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **If** the one examining the firstborn, or the judge, or the witness, **was an elderly person,** the one who required his services **transports him on a donkey.** And in all these cases, although it is prohibited to take wages, the one who required his services gives him his wages like the wages of a laborer, as he was unable to perform his usual labor that day. The Sages **taught** (*Tosefta*, *Bava Metzia* 4:11): The owner **gives him his wage as** if he were **an idle laborer. Abaye says:** It means that he is paid **as a laborer** who is **idle from that** typical **labor** of his from which he is kept idle. In other words, he must receive the amount of money a person would be willing to accept to refrain from his current occupation and engage in the actions discussed in the mishna. This calculation accounts for both the degree of difficulty of his steady employment and the amount of his remuneration.

The *mishnah*'s phrase *kepo'el batel* — as a worker who is idle — is amplified by the *gemara* as *k'poel batel shel osa melacha*, and Rashi provides the practical definition: how much would a person accept to take a day off from his job and not work? That amount — not the wage he would have earned working, but the lesser sum he would accept as sufficient compensation for the pleasure or inconvenience of a day of rest — is what he receives.

---

## Sechar Batel Deep…

### Batel Variables

This definition contains several variables that make it structurally complex. The amount a person would accept to take a day off depends on both how much they earn and how demanding their work is. As Rashi himself illustrates — using the example of someone who cuts precious stones — some of the highest-earning occupations are also the most refined and least physically burdensome. For such a person, the incentive to take a day off is low on both dimensions: the job pays very well and is relatively pleasant, so the threshold for accepting a day off is close to the full daily wage. By contrast, a laborer doing heavy construction work earns much less and would welcome the relief of a day off, so his *sechar batelah* would be substantially lower.

### Ethical Problem

The result is a structurally inequitable compensation system: those who could earn more elsewhere receive higher compensation for their time as judges or witnesses, while those at the lower end of the economic spectrum — who may actually need every dollar and cannot afford to take a day off — receive less. The system pays for the economic position of the individual rather than for any intrinsic value of their testimony or judgment.

### Tosafot Critique

Tosafot raises a pointed objection to Rashi's formulation. If one asks how much this person would accept to take a day off from their job and spend the day as a judge or witness, one is already partially paying for the act of judging or testifying — because being a judge involves effort, and that effort is factored into the amount the person demands. Tosafot insists that the correct baseline is how much the person would accept to take a day off and spend it on the beach — pure leisure, with no effort involved. Any amount above that baseline reflects compensation for the work of judging itself, which is forbidden. Therefore the only permissible payment is the leisure-value of a day away from work, not the cost of the day's labor spent in a different capacity.

---

## Teachers & Officials……

### Sechar Batel Issues

The structural inequity of *sechar batelah* is worth dwelling on. The system as described means that a highly-paid professional who takes a day to testify receives far more compensation than a low-wage worker who does the same. Moreover, someone who is independently wealthy or whose other income does not depend on daily attendance — say, an investor who earns passively at night — faces the question of whether they are entitled to any *sechar batelah* at all. Prima facie, such a person may have no legitimate claim to compensation for time taken from work. The converse side of this — *emet kneh* — suggests that the community should not refuse to engage such a person if they will not perform the service without payment, since acquiring Torah and justice is worth any price. But whether that person is entitled to take the payment is a separate question.

### Beach Day Debate

Tosafot's position — that one must calculate *sechar batelah* relative to a day of pure leisure rather than a day of alternative work — carries significant practical implications. It would mean that the amount paid to any judge or witness should reflect only the value they place on leisure, stripped of any component that could be characterized as paying for the act of judging. This is a genuinely demanding standard, and it is one of the central debates in the application of *sechar batelah* as a compensatory concept.

---

## Payment & Mitzvah……

### Doctors & Services

All of this bears directly on the question of how doctors can be paid. The Ramban — himself a physician — grapples with the question of whether a doctor performing the *mitzvah* of healing (*ve'rappo yerape*) is subject to the same prohibition against payment for *mitzvah* performance. The analysis mirrors the *parah adumah* distinction: one might argue that the physician is paid not for the act of healing itself but for all of the years of preparation, training, and expertise that preceded it. But that framework is strained when fees are calibrated to the complexity and duration of specific procedures — a three-hour surgery commands a different fee than a thirty-hour surgery — suggesting that the payment does in fact track the service itself, not merely the preparation.

---

## Modern Application…

### Doctor Dilemma

The doctor's situation illustrates the tension between the ideal of *mitzvah* performance without payment and the practical necessity of sustaining a healthcare system. The *gemara* in Bava Kamma is itself instructive: a doctor who heals for nothing is worth nothing — meaning that the absence of any fee structure can itself undermine the quality and seriousness of the service. The market dynamics of payment, however uncomfortable from a *halacha* perspective, may in fact serve the underlying goal of *pikuach nefesh* better than a purely volunteer model.

### Prof Salaries

The phenomenon observed in universities — where law and business professors command substantially higher salaries than professors of English literature or history — maps directly onto the *sechar batelah* framework. The differential does not necessarily reflect a judgment that legal and business expertise is more inherently valuable as scholarship. Rather, it reflects the market alternative: a law professor could be earning partner-track compensation at a law firm; a business professor could be in consulting. The university compensates for the opportunity cost of *not* being in those fields. An English professor has no such lucrative alternative, so the opportunity cost is lower. The *gemara*'s principle of *sechar batelah* produces exactly this outcome — and both its logic and its discomforts are on full display in contemporary institutional compensation structures.

## Suspicious Trade

### Deer & Leather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַבְּכוֹרוֹת, אֵין לוֹקְחִין מִמֶּנּוּ בְּשַׂר צְבָאִים, וְלֹא עוֹרוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן עֲבוּדִים. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לוֹקְחִין מִמֶּנּוּ עוֹרוֹת שֶׁל נְקֵבָה, וְאֵין לוֹקְחִין מִמֶּנּוּ צֶמֶר מְלוּבָּן וְצוֹאִי, אֲבָל לוֹקְחִין מִמֶּנּוּ טְוִי וּבְגָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who is suspect with regard to firstborn** animals of slaughtering them and selling their meat when it is prohibited to do so, **one may neither purchase** meat **from him,** including **even deer meat, nor** may one purchase from him **hides that are not tanned. Rabbi Eliezer says: One** may **purchase hides of female animals from him,** as the *halakhot* of firstborn animals are in effect only with regard to males. **And one** may **not purchase bleached or dirty wool from him. But one** may **purchase spun** thread **from him, and** all the more so may one purchase **garments** from him.

The *mishnah* turns to a different but related set of concerns: *hachashud al habechorot ein lokchin mimenu basar tzeva'im velo orot she'einam avudim* — if someone is suspected of slaughtering *bechorot* without proper blemish authority, one may not purchase from him deer meat or untanned leather. The concern is practical: deer meat resembles calf meat closely enough — both have a gamier quality — that a seller could pass *bechor* meat off as deer meat. Similarly, raw, untanned leather might come from an improperly slaughtered *bechor*, and the buyer could unknowingly be purchasing and deriving benefit from a forbidden animal.

### Female Leather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשַׂר צְבָאִים. דְּמִיחַלַּף בִּדְעֶגְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that one may not purchase from him even **deer meat.** The Gemara explains that the reason is **that** it could be **confused with** meat **of a calf,** as they are similar in appearance.

Rabbi Eliezer introduces an exception: *lokchin mimenu or shel nekeva* — one may purchase female leather from a suspected seller, because the sex of the animal is verifiable. When one receives a whole skin, the genital area is visible and female identification is reliable. A *bechor* is necessarily male, so a verifiably female skin cannot have come from an improperly slaughtered *bechor*.

## Effort-Based Trust

### Investment Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא עוֹרוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן עֲבוּדִים. הָא עֲבוּדִים זָבְנִינַן. אִם אִיתָא דִּבְכוֹר הֲוָה — לָא הֲוָה טָרַח בֵּיהּ, סָבַר: אִי שָׁמְעוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן מַפְסְדוּ לֵיהּ מִינַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Nor** may one purchase from him **hides that are not tanned.** The Gemara infers from here that **we** may **purchase tanned** hides from one who is suspect with regard to firstborn animals. The reason is that **if it is so that it is** a hide **of a firstborn, he would not make** such **an effort with it** to tan it. This is because he would **think: If the Sages hear that** this hide is from a firstborn, they will **cause me a loss** by confiscating it **from me.**

The *mishnah*'s allowance of tanned leather rests on a general principle of considerable elegance: *im ita debechor hava lo hava tarach bei* — if this leather were really from a *bechor*, the seller would never have invested the effort to tan it. Why? *Savar ei sham'u bei rabanan mafsidu li minai* — he reasons that if the rabbis find out and can prove the leather came from an improperly slaughtered *bechor*, they will seize it or declare it forbidden, and all of his tanning effort will have been wasted. A person does not invest substantial labor in material that he fears may be confiscated. The willingness to invest that effort is therefore itself a signal — not perfect, but meaningful — that the seller is confident the material is permitted.

### Tanned Leather

Untanned leather, by contrast, represents minimal effort: the seller simply strips the hide. Even if it is seized, he has lost little. But once the seller subjects the leather to the tanning process — a labor-intensive and time-consuming procedure — the calculus changes. No rational actor invests that degree of effort in material he believes is vulnerable to seizure. The effort itself becomes a form of testimony to the seller's confidence in the material's permissibility.

## Suspicious Trade…

### Rat Damage Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לוֹקְחִין מִמֶּנּוּ עוֹרוֹת שֶׁל נְקֵבָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? מִידָּע יְדִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Eliezer says: One** may **purchase hides of female animals from him,** as the *halakhot* of firstborn animals are in effect only with regard to males. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason?** People **know** the difference between the hide of a male animal and the hide of a female animal, due to the differences in anatomy.

A challenge is raised against Rabbi Eliezer's position. Granted, a female skin with an intact genital area is clearly identifiable. But what prevents a dishonest seller from cutting off the genital area of a male skin and claiming the missing portion was eaten by rats? He could then present the skin as a female — or simply as unknown sex — and sell it despite its *bechor* origin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא, אִם כֵּן, זָכָר נָמֵי חָיֵיק לְזַכְרוּתֵיהּ וְאָמַר ״עַכְבָּרִים אַכְלוּהּ״, וְאִידַּךְ — בֵּי עַכְבָּרִים מִידָּע יְדִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, according to **the first *tanna*,** why is it prohibited to purchase hides of female animals from one who is suspect with regard to firstborn animals? The Gemara answers that the first *tanna* claims as follows: **If so,** i.e., if it is permitted to purchase hides of female animals, one might **also** come to sell hides of **male** animals, as he will **sever the male** reproductive organ **and say** that **mice ate** this part of the hide. The Gemara asks: **And the other,** Rabbi Eliezer, why is he not concerned that one might cut off that part of the hide and sell it as the hide of a female? The Gemara answers that Rabbi Eliezer maintains that the **place of** the **mice is known,** i.e., one can tell by looking at the hide whether it was eaten by mice or cut with a knife.

The *gemara* dismisses this concern: *be'achbarim midei yedia* — rat damage is visibly identifiable. The pattern of gnawing, the irregular edges, the nature of the damage — all of these are perceptible upon inspection, and a deliberately cut area does not resemble genuine rat damage. Therefore, if the skin presents as female and there is no suspicious damage, the identification is reliable. The concern about fraudulent alteration does not undermine Rabbi Eliezer's position.

## Effort-Based Trust…

### Carded Wool

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לוֹקְחִין מִמֶּנּוּ צֶמֶר מְלוּבָּן וְצוֹאִי, הַשְׁתָּא צֶמֶר מְלוּבָּן לָא זָבְנִינַן, צוֹאִי מִיבַּעְיָא? אֶלָּא, חֲדָא קָתָנֵי: צֶמֶר מְלוּבָּן מִצּוֹאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And one** may **not purchase bleached or dirty wool from him.** The Gemara asks: **Now** that the mishna has taught that **we** may **not purchase bleached** wool **from him,** despite the fact he has expended effort in bleaching it, which indicates that it is not from a firstborn, **is** it **necessary** to teach that one may not purchase **dirty** wool from him? The Gemara answers: **Rather,** the *tanna* **is teaching one** case here, that of **wool** that was **bleached** and cleaned **from its dirt.**

The *mishnah* states that one may not purchase cleaned or dirty wool — *tzemer meluban vetzoa'i* — from a suspected seller. The *gemara* notes the apparent redundancy: if cleaned white wool remains suspect, is there any question about dirty raw wool? The answer is that the *mishnah* is expressing a single unified case: *tzemer meluban mitzo'ato* — wool that has been whitened from its dirty state. Even this modest degree of processing — cleaning — does not rise to the level of effort that would invoke the effort-based trust principle.

### Effort Logic

The underlying logic is consistent throughout: the threshold for invoking the effort-based trust is not any effort, but *sufficient* effort — the kind of investment that a rational person would not risk losing. Cleaning wool is relatively simple work; even if it were seized, the seller's loss would be minimal. Therefore it does not generate the inference of confidence that tanning leather would.

### Spun Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לוֹקְחִין מִמֶּנּוּ טְוִי וּבְגָדִים. הַשְׁתָּא טְוִי זָבְנִינַן, בְּגָדִים מִיבַּעְיָא? מַאי בְּגָדִים? נַמְטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **But one** may **purchase spun** thread **from him, and** all the more so one may purchase **garments** from him. The Gemara asks: **Now** that the mishna has taught that **we may purchase spun** thread from one who is suspect with regard to firstborn, **is** it **necessary** to teach that one may purchase **garments?** The Gemara answers: **What** does the mishna mean which it says: **Garments?** It is referring to **felt [*namtei*]** garments, which are not made of spun thread.

By contrast, spun thread and finished garments are permissible to purchase from the suspected seller. The *gemara* observes: *hashta tavi zavninan bgadim mibaya* — if we can buy spun thread, of course we can buy garments made of it. The *gemara* resolves that *bgadim* here refers to felt — pressed, non-spun wool constructed by compression. Even this materially different construction method, which does not involve spinning, still requires substantial effort and investment, and therefore triggers the same inference. The effort of constructing felt would not be risked on material the seller believed to be forbidden.

## Shevi'it Trade

### Flax Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבִיעִית — אֵין לוֹקְחִין מִמֶּנּוּ פִּשְׁתָּן, וַאֲפִילּוּ סְרָק, אֲבָל לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶם טְוִי וְאָרִיג.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who is suspect with regard to the Sabbatical** Year, i.e., of sowing or engaging in commerce with Sabbatical-Year produce, **one may not purchase flax from him, and** this applies **even** to **combed** flax, in which much labor and exertion was invested. **But one** may **purchase spun** thread **and woven** fabric **from** such individuals.

A parallel case: *hachashud al hashevi'it ein lokchin mimenu pishtan* — if someone is suspected of shevi'it violations — whether by failing to declare his *shevi'it* produce *hefker* or by conducting prohibited business in *shevi'it* produce — one may not purchase flax from him. The concern is that the flax may carry *shevi'it* sanctity, which would restrict its permitted uses.

### Spun Flax

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַשְׁתָּא טְוִי זָבְנִינַן, אָרִיג מִיבַּעְיָא? מַאי ״אָרִיג״? תִּיכֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Now** that the mishna has taught that **we may purchase spun** thread from one who is suspect with regard to the Sabbatical Year, **is** it **necessary** to teach the case of **woven fabric?** The Gemara answers: **What** does the mishna mean when it says: **Woven?** It means wool woven into **chains,** which is not spun.

*Ve'afilu saruk* — even combed flax remains suspect: the effort of combing is insufficient to trigger the investment-confidence inference. But *aval lokchin mimenu tavi ve'arig* — spun and woven flax may be purchased. Once the flax has been processed into thread and fabric, the seller has invested enough that he would not risk producing a product he feared would be declared forbidden.

### Confidence Indicator

The *gemara* again applies the inversion: *hashta tavi zavninan arig mibaya* — if spun thread is permissible, woven fabric obviously is as well. The *gemara* resolves that *arig* here refers to unspun flax pressed into fabric through a chain-linking technique — a different construction method that nonetheless requires substantial effort, and thus falls within the same principle. The criterion throughout is not the specific technique but the magnitude of the investment: any process demanding enough effort that a rational seller would not undertake it on forbidden material provides a basis for trust, even in the face of general suspicion.
