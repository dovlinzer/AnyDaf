# Sotah 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meit Tofes Arba Amot

### Opening Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת תּוֹפֵס אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לְטוּמְאָה
>
> **Translation:** **A corpse occupies four cubits with** regard to **impurity,**

The daf opens in the middle of a statement transmitted by Rabbi Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Eleazar ben Yaakov: *meit tofes arba amot letumah* — that within four *amot* of a corpse, a person is considered *tamei*, this rule functioning as a mechanism to compel people to maintain distance from the dead.

### Chatzar HaKever

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא תּוּנָא: חֲצַר הַקֶּבֶר, הָעוֹמֵד בְּתוֹכָהּ — טָהוֹר, וְהוּא שֶׁיְּהוּ בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, דִּבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי. בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And the *tanna*** of the mishna **taught** in another mishna as well (*Oholot* 15:8): With regard to **a courtyard** adjacent to **a grave, one who stands within it is ritually pure, provided that there are four cubits** by four cubits **within it.** A courtyard this size constitutes its own space, according to **the statement of Beit Shammai. Beit Hillel say:** A space of **four handbreadths** by four handbreadths is enough.

The Gemara marshals a corroborating teaching: *chatzar hakever* — the courtyard situated in front of a burial cave. If one stands in such a courtyard, *ha'omed betochah tahor* — he is *tahor*. The underlying rationale, as Rashi explains, is that the wall of the cave serves as a clear demarcation: a person standing in the courtyard can see exactly where the cave begins and therefore will not inadvertently enter the space of the dead. The qualifying condition is *vehu sheyiyu bah arba amot* — the courtyard must measure four *amot*. This minimum is not a distance requirement from the graves themselves but a size requirement for the courtyard as an independent space. If the courtyard is too small, it cannot be considered a genuinely separate domain, and one standing in it would effectively be considered co-located with the interior of the cave. This is the position of Beit Shammai.

### Beit Shammai v. Hillel

Beit Hillel disagrees, holding that *arbaah tefachim* suffice. Even a very small courtyard — provided it affords a person some place to stand — is adequate, since the wall itself provides the meaningful separation. The presence of the wall, on this view, does the primary halachic work, and the minimum size threshold can therefore be set much lower.

### Access Orientation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁפִּתְחָהּ מִלְּמַעְלָה, אֲבָל פִּתְחָהּ מִן הַצַּד — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement said?** Four handbreadths suffice **when the entrance** to the courtyard is **from above.** In such a case there is no concern that one will cover the grave with his body, and thereby become ritually impure, when entering the courtyard. **However,** if **its entrance** is **from the side, everyone agrees** the courtyard must be at least **four cubits** by four cubits in size.

The Mishnah then introduces a distinction based on how one enters the courtyard: *bameh devarim amurim shepitchah milamalah* — when does the four-*amah* requirement apply? When the entrance to the courtyard is from above, meaning one must climb or drop down into the space. *Aval pitchah min hatzad, divrei hakol arba amot* — but if the entrance is from the side, then all opinions agree the courtyard must be four *amot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלַפֵּי לְיָיא?! אַדְּרַבָּה: מִן הַצַּד — מִידְּרִיד וְנָפֵיק, מִלְּמַעְלָה — אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא מַאֲהִיל. אֶלָּא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים שֶׁפִּתְחָהּ מִן הַצַּד, אֲבָל פִּתְחָהּ מִלְּמַעְלָה, אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the preceding resolution: **In which direction [*kelapei layya*]** is there a distinction between the positions of the entrance? **On the contrary,** if one enters **from the side,** he can **slip away** and **exit** without becoming impure. However, if one enters **from above it is impossible that** he will **not overlie the grave** over the course of climbing in and out of the courtyard. **Rather,** it should say: **In what** case **is this statement** of Beit Hillel, according to which four handbreadths by four handbreadths are enough, **said?** It is **when the entrance is from the side. However,** if **the entrance is from above,** then the courtyard must be at least **four cubits** by four cubits according to all opinions.

The Gemara immediately objects: *klapi laya* — this seems backwards. If entry is from the side — *madrik venafik* — one can navigate in and out smoothly without any part of the body passing over the cave. It is entry from above that is genuinely dangerous, since *iy efshar delo mayei* — it is virtually impossible to jump in and out without some part of one's body hovering over the burial space. The Gemara therefore reverses the ruling: the smaller size (*tefachim*) suffices when entry is from the side, because the risk of overhang is minimal; when entry is from above, the larger four-*amah* dimension is required so as to ensure the person can remain safely clear of the cave's interior space.

### Wall Demarcation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי, חֲצַר הַקֶּבֶר דִּמְסַיְּימָא מְחִיצָתַהּ, אֲבָל מֵת בְּעָלְמָא — תָּפֵיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further qualifies: **And this statement** applies in **a courtyard** adjacent to **a grave whose partitions** are **delineated, but** if there is **only a corpse** lying about without any enclosures, it certainly **occupies** and affects a surrounding area of four cubits.

The Gemara draws the larger point together: *chatzar hakever, demisayma mechitzta* — the courtyard of a burial cave works because the wall is clearly delineated. One can see exactly where the boundary lies and stay clear of it. *Aval meit dealma, tefis* — but in the case of an ordinary corpse lying in an open space, there is no such demarcation, and the four-*amah* *tumah* zone takes hold automatically, compelling people to keep their distance. The proof from *chatzar hakever* is admittedly indirect — one might question whether the existence of a wall truly maps onto the rule of *meit tofes arba amot* — but the Gemara presents it as consistent with the principle: the extension of *tumah* to four *amot* functions precisely as a distancing mechanism in the absence of physical demarcation.

## Expansion Hermeneutics

### Who Betroths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר אֵרַשׂ אִשָּׁה כּוּ׳״, תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֲשֶׁר אֵרַס״, אֶחָד הַמְאָרֵס אֶת הַבְּתוּלָה, וְאֶחָד הַמְאָרֵס אֶת הָאַלְמָנָה, וְאֶחָד שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם. וַאֲפִילּוּ חֲמִשָּׁה אַחִים, וּמֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶם בַּמִּלְחָמָה — כּוּלָּן חוֹזְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches among the statements that were said before battle: **"What man is there that has betrothed a wife** and has not taken her? Let him go and return to his house" (Deuteronomy 20:7). **The Sages taught: "That has betrothed"** is referring to **one who betroths a virgin, and** to **one who betroths a widow, and** to **one** whose *yevama* is **a widow awaiting her *yavam*,** i.e., this man in the military ranks, to perform levirate marriage. **And even** if there are **five brothers, and one of them dies in the war, they all return** for the widow.

Returning now to the *meshuach milchama* and the exemptions from military service, the Gemara takes up the phrase *mi ha'ish asher eiras isha* — "who is the man who betroths a woman." Just as the earlier sections expanded the categories of houses and vineyards beyond their most straightforward applications, so too the category of betrothal is subject to interpretive expansion.

### Betrothal Categories

*Tanu rabbanan*: *asher eiras* — "who betroths" — includes not only one who betroths a virgin (*betulah*), but also one who betroths a widow or divorcée, and even one whose connection to the woman is not through a formal act of *erusin* at all but through the bond of *yibum* — where a brother died and left a widow, making the surviving brother a *yavam* with an obligation to that woman.

### Multiple Brothers

Even more expansively: if five brothers fought together and one died in battle, having left a childless widow, all the remaining brothers return home — even though only one of them will ultimately perform *yibum*. Since at the time of departure any one of them might be the one to take her, all of them share the exemption. This holding is notable: the exemption attaches not to the certainty of the obligation but to the possibility of it.

### Forbidden Marriages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֹא לָקַח״, ״וְלֹא לְקָחָהּ״ — פְּרָט לְאַלְמָנָה לַכֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, מַמְזֶרֶת וּנְתִינָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַמְזֵר וּלְנָתִין.
>
> **Translation:** In addition, the verse could have singled out one who has **not taken** a wife. When the verse specifies: **"and has not taken her,"** this **excludes** men in forbidden marriages: **A widow** who is betrothed **to a High Priest;** either **a divorcée or a *yevama* who performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*],** who is betrothed **to a common priest; a *mamzeret* or a Gibeonite woman** who is betrothed **to an Israelite;** or **an Israelite woman** who is betrothed **to a *mamzer* or to a Gibeonite.**

The phrase *velo lekacha* — "and did not take her" — functions as an exclusion. Just as the analogous phrases *velo chanacho* and *velo chilalo* excluded cases of *gezelah* or *issur* in earlier applications, so here the Torah excludes marriages that are halachically forbidden even while technically binding. The list includes: a widow (*almanah*) married to a *kohen gadol*, a divorced woman or *chalutzah* married to a regular *kohen*, a *mamzeret* or *netinah* married to an Israelite, and a daughter of Israel married to a *mamzer* or *natin*. In all these cases — unions that constitute a *lav* but where *kiddushin* nevertheless takes effect — the betrothed man does not receive the military exemption.

### Rabba's Transgression Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramiac:** לֵימָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, דְּאִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, הָא אָמַר ״הַיָּרֵא וְרַךְ הַלֵּבָב״ — זֶה הַמִּתְיָירֵא מֵעֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that this opinion is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili? As, if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, he says:** When the verse singles out **"fearful and fainthearted"** (Deuteronomy 20:8), **this** is referring to **one who is afraid because of sins that he has.** According to this interpretation, one who marries a woman forbidden to him should return home from war because of his guilt, which seems to contradict the opinion mentioned above that such a man does not return from the ranks.

This exclusion generates a *kushya*: given that Rabbi Yose HaGlili will say later that the *yarei verach halevav* — the fearful-hearted one — refers to someone who fears on account of *aveirot she'beyado*, sins presently in his possession, why do we need a special *pasuk* to exclude the man in a forbidden marriage? He has an *aveirah she'beyado* already! In the case of *gezelah* discussed earlier, the Gemara could say the person did *teshuvah* and repaid the theft, thereby clearing his slate. But here the man is still in the relationship — he cannot have rectified it without having divorced her. The *aveirah* should still be *beyado*. And yet the Gemara answers: *kiderabba* — this follows the view of Rabba.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, כִּדְרַבָּה. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּבְעוֹל, מָה טַעַם: ״לֹא יִקָּח״ מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא יְחַלֵּל״ — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה עַד שֶׁיִּבְעוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Even** if **you say** that the ruling is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** such a man does not return, **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabba. As Rabba said:** A man is **never liable** for a forbidden marriage **until he engages in sexual intercourse** with his wife. With regard to the forbidden marriages of a High Priest, the Torah states: "A widow, or a divorcée, or a profaned woman, or a harlot, these he shall not take…And he shall not profane his seed among his people" (Leviticus 21:14–15). **What is the reason** that **"he shall not take"** one of these women as a wife? It is **due to: "He shall not profane** his seed" by engaging in sexual intercourse. **Due to that** reason, **he is not flogged until he engages in sexual intercourse** with a woman who is forbidden to him. Therefore, even according to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, one who merely betroths a forbidden wife might not return home from the ranks.

Rabba holds: *le'olam eino chayav ad she'yiv'ol* — the man does not transgress at all until he actually consummates the union through *biah*. Although the *pesukim* governing *issurei kehunah* speak in the language of *kicha* — "taking," which sounds like *kiddushin* — Rabba maintains that the actual prohibition is only violated through the act of intercourse. His proof: the *pasuk* links *lo yikach* to *lo yechallel zaro be'amav* — the prohibition against "taking" is connected to the concern about desecrating his seed and altering her status. Neither of those consequences is possible without *biah*. Therefore: *mishum hachi eino lokeh ad she'yivol* — he is not flogged until *biah* has occurred. Prior to that act, whatever groundwork is being laid, no formal *aveirah* has yet been committed. This is why the *pasuk* of *velo lekacha* must explicitly exclude these cases — without it, since no *aveirah she'beyado* exists yet under Rabba's framework, they might otherwise qualify for the exemption.

Rashi notes that this point applies with even greater force to the case of *mamzer*, since the *pasuk lo yavo mamzer bikhal hashem* does not even employ the language of *kicha*. All of the *arayot* — the most severe forbidden relations, those carrying *karet* — are formulated exclusively in terms of *biah*, not *kiddushin*. It is specifically the *issurei kehunah* where the *pasuk* unusually employs the language of marriage-taking, and it is for that reason that Rabba's ruling is most needed there: even with language that sounds like *kiddushin*, the transgression only attaches at the point of *biah*.

A further point of interest arises in this context: there is a *machloket rishonim* about the converse scenario — a *kohen* who cohabits with a *gerushah* outside the context of *kiddushin* entirely. According to the Rambam, such a *kohen* is not *over* on *lo yikach*, since the prohibition technically requires both *kiddushin* and *biah* to be fully constituted. One might ask, finally, why a *kohen* is fighting in the army at all — a genuinely interesting question that warrants further analysis.

## Order of Life

### Torah's Derech Eretz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה״, ״אֲשֶׁר נָטַע״, ״אֲשֶׁר אֵרַשׂ״, לִימְּדָה תּוֹרָה דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ: שֶׁיִּבְנֶה אָדָם בַּיִת, וְיִטַּע כֶּרֶם, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִשָּׂא אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 7:20-21): The Torah states: "What man is there **that has built"** (Deuteronomy 20:5), and then **"that has planted"** (Deuteronomy 20:6), and finally **"that has betrothed"** (Deuteronomy 20:7). **The Torah** has **taught** a person **the desired mode of behavior: A person should build a house,** then **plant a vineyard, and afterward marry a woman.**

With the *halachot* of all three exemptions having been treated, the Gemara turns to a thematic reflection on the sequence in which the Torah presents them: *asher bana, asher nata, asher eiras* — who built, who planted, who betrothed. *Limdah Torah derech eretz* — the Torah is teaching the proper order of human life: first build a house, then plant a vineyard, then marry. The principle is that one should first establish the material capacity to support a family before undertaking the responsibility of one.

### Solomon's Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף שְׁלֹמֹה אָמַר בְּחׇכְמָתוֹ: ״הָכֵן בַּחוּץ מְלַאכְתֶּךָ וְעַתְּדָהּ בַּשָּׂדֶה לָךְ אַחַר וּבָנִיתָ בֵיתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And even** King **Solomon said in his wisdom: "Prepare your work outside, and make it fit for yourself in the field; and afterward build your house"** (Proverbs 24:27).

Shlomo expressed the same idea in his wisdom (Proverbs 24): *hachein bachutz melachtecha ve'atadah basadeh lach, achar u'vanita beisecha* — "Prepare your labor outside, ready it in the field, and afterward build your house." The peshat appears to counsel: first secure your livelihood and prepare your field, then establish your home. The Gemara proceeds to offer two *derashot* on this verse.

### Learning Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָכֵן בַּחוּץ מְלַאכְתֶּךָ״ — זֶה בַּיִת, ״וְעַתְּדָהּ בַּשָּׂדֶה לָךְ״ — זֶה כֶּרֶם, ״אַחַר וּבָנִיתָ בֵיתֶךָ״ — זוֹ אִשָּׁה. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״הָכֵן בַּחוּץ מְלַאכְתֶּךָ״ — זֶה מִקְרָא, ״וְעַתְּדָהּ בַּשָּׂדֶה לָךְ״ — זֶה מִשְׁנָה, ״אַחַר וּבָנִיתָ בֵיתֶךָ״ — זֶה גְּמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages explained: **"Prepare your work outside"; this** is **a house. "And make it fit for yourself in the field"; this** is **a vineyard. "And afterward you shall build your house"; this** is **a wife.** **Alternatively,** this verse may be understood as relating to Torah study: **"Prepare your work outside"; this** is the study of **Bible. "And make it fit for yourself in the field"; this** is the study of **Mishna. "Afterward you shall build your house"; this** is the study of **Gemara,** the analysis of and deliberation over the statements of the Sages.

In the first interpretation: *hachein bachutz melachtecha* — prepare your labor outside — *zeh bayis*, this refers to building a house. *Ve'atadah basadeh lach* — prepare it in the field for yourself — *zeh karem*, this is the vineyard, one's source of livelihood. *Achar u'vanita beisecha* — afterward build your house — *zu isha*, this signifies marriage, since the Gemara equates *bayit* with *ishah*.

In the second interpretation — *davar acher* — the verse is read as a curriculum of Torah study: *hachein bachutz melachtecha* — *zeh mikra*, this is Scripture; *ve'atadah basadeh lach* — *zeh mishnah*, this is Mishnah; *achar u'vanita beisecha* — *zeh Gemara*, this is the study of Gemara. The point, made explicit by the Maharsha but already implicit in the Gemara, is that learning *mikra* and *mishnah* is preparatory groundwork — necessary stages, but ultimately in service of the genuine intellectual construction that is *talmud Gemara*. Just as one must first secure a livelihood and a house before starting a family, so one must traverse the prior stages of learning before engaging in the ultimate building, which is the *beit ne'eman* of Gemara study.

### Three Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״הָכֵן בַּחוּץ מְלַאכְתֶּךָ״ — זֶה מִקְרָא וּמִשְׁנָה, ״וְעַתְּדָהּ בַּשָּׂדֶה לָךְ״ — זֶה גְּמָרָא, ״אַחַר וּבָנִית בֵיתֶךָ״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively: "Prepare your work outside"; this** is the study of **Bible and Mishna. "And make it fit for yourself in the field"; this** is the study of **Gemara. "Afterward you shall build your house"; these** are **good deeds.**

A third reading — another *davar acher* — reframes the structure: *hachein bachutz melachtecha* — *zeh mikra u'mishnah*, Scripture and Mishnah together constitute stage one; *ve'atadah basadeh lach* — *zeh Gemara*, the study of Gemara is stage two; *achar u'vanita beisecha* — *eilu ma'asim tovim*, this is good deeds. On this reading, the ultimate goal beyond even the study of Gemara is the *ma'asim tovim* to which it should give rise — the classic question of whether the telos of Torah learning is the learning itself or the action it generates.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: ״הָכֵן בַּחוּץ מְלַאכְתֶּךָ״ — זֶה מִקְרָא וּמִשְׁנָה וּגְמָרָא, ״וְעַתְּדָהּ בַּשָּׂדֶה לָךְ״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים, ״אַחַר וּבָנִיתָ בֵיתֶךָ״ — דְּרוֹשׁ וְקַבֵּל שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, says: "Prepare your work outside"; this** is the study of **Bible, and Mishna, and Gemara. "And make it fit for yourself in the field"; these** are **good deeds. "Afterward you shall build your house"; expound** upon new understandings of Torah **and receive reward,** which is possible only after the initial steps.

Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yose HaGlili, offers yet a fourth reading. For him, all of *mikra*, *mishnah*, and *Gemara* together constitute stage one — *hachein bachutz melachtecha*. Stage two — *ve'atadah basadeh lach* — is *ma'asim tovim*. And the culminating stage three — *achar u'vanita beisecha* — is *derosh u'kabel sachar*: theoretical analysis, for which one receives reward. This phrase, *derosh u'kabel sachar*, is typically invoked in contexts where a *pasuk* is being explained as existing purely for theoretical derivation without practical application — such as the case of *ben sorer u'moreh*. What does it mean here as the highest stage? The Maharsha understands it as *chochmah iyunit* — speculative philosophical wisdom, as distinct from the *chochmah* of Gemara study per se. The Rambam's *Moreh Nevuchim* offers the clearest framework: the entire structure of *mitzvot*, learning, and *ma'asim tovim* exists to cultivate sound character, a stable society, and disciplined intellect — all so that one may ultimately dedicate oneself to pure theoretical knowledge of God. That is the *derosh* — the pure contemplation — that constitutes the final, highest *bayit*.

It is worth noting that the three things invoked in these interpretations — Torah, *chuppah*, and *ma'asim tovim* — are the very same trio mentioned at a *brit milah*: *keshem she'nichnas la'brit kein yikanes le'Torah u'le'chupah u'le'ma'asim tovim*.

## Non-Returners

### Gatehouse Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵינָן חוֹזְרִין: הַבּוֹנֶה בֵּית שַׁעַר כּוּ׳. תָּנָא: אִם הוֹסִיף בּוֹ דִּימוֹס אֶחָד — חוֹזֵר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַף הַבּוֹנֶה בֵּית לְבֵנִים בַּשָּׁרוֹן — לֹא הָיָה חוֹזֵר. תָּנָא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּחַדְּשִׁין אוֹתוֹ פַּעֲמַיִם בְּשָׁבוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And these** are the men **who do not return** from the ranks: **One who builds a gateway,** or an enclosed veranda, or a balcony…Rabbi Yehuda says: Even one who rebuilds a house as it stood originally would not return. A Sage taught (*Tosefta* 7:18): **If** one **adds one** additional **row of stones [*dimos*] to** the original structure, **he returns** from the ranks. That is enough to render it a new building. The mishna further teaches: **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even one who builds a** new **brick house in the Sharon would not return.** A Sage **taught:** This is **because** the owners **renew it twice in** a period of **seven years,** and it is therefore not considered a permanent structure.

The Mishnah turns to those who do *not* return from the front lines: among them is one who built only a *beit sha'ar* — a gatehouse — which does not constitute a true dwelling. The *Tanna* adds: if a man tore down his house and rebuilt it according to its original specifications, he does not return. However, *im hosif bo dimus echad, chozer* — if he added even a single row of bricks, departing even slightly from the original plan, he does return. Rabbi Eliezer adds: even one who built a *beit levenim* — a house of bricks — in the Sharon region would not return, because *mipnei shemechadeshin oto paamayim beshavua* — such structures require rebuilding twice every seven years, rendering the house insufficiently permanent to qualify as a stable dwelling.

### Full Completion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין זָזִין מִמְּקוֹמָן: בָּנָה בַּיִת חָדָשׁ וַחֲנָכוֹ וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **These** are the men **who do not** even **move from their places:** One who **built a house and dedicated it** within the year; one who planted a vineyard and used its fruit for less than a year; one who marries his betrothed and one who marries his *yevama*, as it is stated: "When a man takes a new wife, he shall not go out with the army, neither shall he be charged with any business; he shall be free for his house one year, and shall cheer his wife whom he has taken" (Deuteronomy 24:5).

A separate and stronger category is *ve'elu she'einam zazim mimkomam* — those who do not even move from their position to provide support services. This category is derived from the *pasuk* concerning the newly married man: *lo yeitzei batzava velo yaavor alav lechol davar* — he shall not go out to the army and shall not be tasked with any matter. The expansion of this complete exemption to the cases of the house and vineyard requires that those cases have reached culmination: the house must have been dedicated (*chancho*), the vineyard must have reached its fourth year (*chillelo*), and the wife must have been fully married (*lekachah*).

## Complete Exemption

### New Wife Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִשָּׁה חֲדָשָׁה״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִשָּׁה חֲדָשָׁה, אַלְמָנָה וּגְרוּשָׁה מִנַּיִן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִשָּׁה״ מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִשָּׁה חֲדָשָׁה״? מִי שֶׁחֲדָשָׁה לוֹ, יָצָא מַחֲזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ שֶׁאֵין חֲדָשָׁה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With respect to a new husband's exemption, **the Sages taught** a number of *halakhot* from this verse. From the term: **"A new wife" I have** derived **only** that a man returns only for **a new** virgin **wife. From where** do I derive that it applies to **a widow or a divorcée? The verse states: "A wife," in any case. If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "A new wife?"** It is referring to **one who is new for him, excluding one who remarries his divorcée, who is not new to him.**

*Tanu rabbanan*: the *pasuk* states *ki yikach ish ishah chadashah* — when a man takes a new wife — *lo yeitzei batzava velo yaavor alav lechol davar, naki yihyeh leveiso shanah achat veshimach et ishto asher lakach* — he shall be free for his home for one year and shall gladden the wife he has taken. The Gemara asks: *ein li ela ishah chadashah* — I only know this applies to a new wife. What of a widow or divorcée who has been married before? *Talmud lomar ishah, mikol makom* — the broader word *ishah* includes all women. Then: *im kein mah talmud lomar ishah chadashah* — if so, what is the point of the word *chadashah*? The answer: *ishah chadashah lo* — new to *him*. This serves to exclude *perat lemachazir gerushato* — a man who remarries his own divorcée; since she is not new to him, the full exemption does not apply.

### No Support Services

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא יֵצֵא בַּצָּבָא״. יָכוֹל בַּצָּבָא הוּא דְּלֹא יֵצֵא, אֲבָל יַסְפִּיק מַיִם וּמָזוֹן וִיתַקֵּן הַדְּרָכִים — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלֹא יַעֲבֹר עָלָיו לְכׇל דָּבָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: **"He shall not go out with the army"** (Deuteronomy 24:5); one **might** have thought it is **with the army that he does not go out, but** he does go to **supply water and food** to the army **and to repair the roads** for them. Therefore, **the verse states: "Neither shall he be charged with any business."**

*Lo yaavor alav lechol davar* — he shall not be put to any service whatsoever. One might have thought the exemption is only from frontline combat while logistical support duties — supplying water, food, and maintaining roads — remain obligatory. The *pasuk* therefore adds *lechol davar* to indicate that not even auxiliary military service may be demanded of him.

### Culmination Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אַף הַבּוֹנֶה בַּיִת וְלֹא חֲנָכוֹ, נָטַע כֶּרֶם וְלֹא חִלְּלוֹ, אֵרַס אִשָּׁה וְלֹא לְקָחָהּ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עָלָיו״ — עָלָיו אִי אַתָּה מַעֲבִיר, אֲבָל אַתָּה מַעֲבִיר עַל אֲחֵרִים. וּמֵאַחַר דִּכְתַב ״לֹא יַעֲבֹר״, ״לֹא יֵצֵא בַּצָּבָא״ לְמָה לִי? לַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בִּשְׁנֵי לָאוִין.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought **that I include even one who has built a house and has not dedicated it,** or **one who has planted a vineyard and has not used its fruit,** or **one who has betrothed a woman and has not taken her** as his wife? **The verse states:** "Neither shall **he** be charged with any business"; **you do not charge him with any** responsibilities, **but you do charge others.** The Gemara asks: **And since** the Torah **states: "Neither shall he be charged** with any business," **why do I** need to be taught: **"He shall not go out with the army"?** The Gemara answers: The Torah adds this clause **so that he will violate two prohibitions** if he goes out to war: "He shall not go out with the army," and: "Neither shall he be charged."

Conversely, one might think the complete exemption — release from all army-related duty — should extend also to the person who built a house but did not yet dedicate it, or who planted a vineyard but did not yet reach the fourth year, or who betrothed a woman but did not yet marry her. The *pasuk* responds: *alav* — *velo yaavor alav lechol davar* — *alav iy atah maavir*, only upon *him*, upon the one who has reached full culmination, do you impose no obligation whatsoever. *Aval atah maavir al acherim* — but those in the preliminary stages are subject to support duties, even though they are exempt from frontline combat.

### Two Lavin

Why does the *pasuk* state both *lo yeitzei batzava* and *velo yaavor alav lechol davar*, given that once any obligation to support is removed, the prohibition against frontline service should be self-evident? The answer: *laavor alav bishnei lavin* — to teach that if such a man does go out to fight, he has transgressed two separate *lavin*. Whether this prohibition runs against the army authorities for sending him or against the man himself for going is an interesting question — though the language of *lo yeitzei* and *lo yaavor* does suggest personal prohibition, not merely entitlement. The Rambam's formulation in *Hilchot Melachim* (7:11) confirms: if the man provides water, food, or road service, or stands guard at the wall, or contributes in any capacity to the army's function throughout that year, *lo ya'avor alav shum davar ba'olam* — he violates *lo yeitzei* and *lo yaavor*, two *lavin*.

Two additional questions merit further attention: whether the man in the preliminary categories (*lo chancho*, *lo chillelo*, *lo lekachah*) can waive his exemption from frontline duty, and the underlying question of why a *kohen* would be fighting in the army at all — a point flagged earlier that remains unresolved and worth examining in the *Sefer Minchat Chinuch*.

## Hayrei Verach Halevav

### Rabbi Akiva Simple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״וְיָסְפוּ הַשֹּׁטְרִים לְדַבֵּר אֶל הָעָם וְגוֹ׳״, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״הַיָּרֵא וְרַךְ הַלֵּבָב״ — כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד בְּקִשְׁרֵי הַמִּלְחָמָה וְלִרְאוֹת חֶרֶב שְׁלוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna continues its discussion of the speech given before battle. **"And the officers shall speak further to the people,** and they shall say: What man is there that is fearful and fainthearted? Let him go and return unto his house" (Deuteronomy 20:8). **Rabbi Akiva says: "That is fearful and fainthearted"** is to be understood **as it indicates, that** the man is **unable to stand in the battle ranks and to see a drawn sword** because it will terrify him.

The Torah commands: *veyasfu hashotrim ledaber el ha'am* — and the officers shall continue to speak to the people — and then introduces the case of *ha'yarei verach halevav*, the fearful and tender-hearted. Rabbi Akiva reads this straightforwardly, *kemashmao* — as it sounds: a person who is genuinely afraid, *she'eino yachol la'amod bekishrei milchama* — who cannot hold his place in the battle formation when soldiers bind together into a fighting unit — *velirot cherev shlufa* — and who cannot bear to face a drawn sword. According to Rashi, *kishrei milchama* refers to the moment when soldiers lock together to form a human wall or phalanx.

### R. Yose HaGlili Sin-Based

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: ״הַיָּרֵא וְרַךְ הַלֵּבָב״ — זֶהוּ הַמִּתְיָירֵא מִן הָעֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ, לְפִיכָךְ תָּלְתָה לוֹ הַתּוֹרָה אֶת כׇּל אֵלּוּ, שֶׁיַּחְזוֹר בִּגְלָלָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: "That is fearful and fainthearted"; this is one who is afraid because of the sins that he has;** he, too, returns. **Therefore, the Torah provided him** with **all these** additional reasons for exemption from the army so he can ascribe **his leaving to** one of **them.** In this way, the sinner may leave the ranks without having to publicly acknowledge that he is a sinner.

Rabbi Yose HaGlili disagrees, interpreting *ha'yarei verach halevav* as *ze hamityare me'aveirot she'beyado* — the one who fears on account of the sins presently in his possession. It is not physical fear of battle that the Torah addresses here but a consciousness of moral vulnerability: the awareness that one's sins have diminished one's merit and left one exposed on the battlefield.

### Divine Cover

*Ulfichach taltah lo hatorah et kol elu sheyachazor biglalan* — it is for this reason that the Torah provided all these other exemptions — the house, the vineyard, the wife — so that the person who must return on account of his *aveirot she'beyado* could do so without publicly disclosing the real reason. He can go back in the company of those who have built houses or betrothed wives, and no one need know that his departure is motivated by sin-fear rather than any qualifying material circumstance. This raises a striking implication embedded in the word *lefichach* — as if to say the Torah's entire framework of practical exemptions exists, at least in part, to provide cover for the *aveira*-bearing soldier. On this reading, there is no misrepresentation involved in availing oneself of a legitimate exemption even when one's actual motivation is different.

Rabbi Akiva, notably, is the one advancing the *pshat* reading here.

### R. Yose Addition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, מַמְזֶרֶת וּנְתִינָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַמְזֵר וּלְנָתִין — הֲרֵי הוּא הַיָּרֵא וְרַךְ הַלֵּבָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to one who has betrothed a woman forbidden to him, including **a widow** betrothed **to a High Priest; a divorcée or a *yevama* who performed *ḥalitza*** [***ḥalutza***] betrothed **to a common priest; a *mamzeret* or a Gibeonite woman** betrothed **to an Israelite;** or **a daughter of an Israelite** betrothed **to a *mamzer* or a Gibeonite; this** man **is he** whom the verse calls **"fearful and fainthearted."** He fears that his sin will jeopardize his safety in the war.

Rabbi Yose adds: *almanah lekohen gadol, gerushah vechalutzah lekohen hedyot, mamzeret unetinah leyisrael, bat yisrael lemamzer ulenatin* — all the cases of forbidden marriage discussed above — *harei hu ha'yarei verach halevav*. These men, too, fall under the category of the fearful-hearted, since their sin-laden situation makes them spiritually vulnerable. The Gemara had noted earlier that these cases seemingly present no *aveira she'beyado* under Rabba's framework (since the transgression only attaches at *biah*); Rabbi Yose is apparently disagreeing and treating the forbidden betrothal itself as an ongoing *aveirah she'beyado*.

The question of how Rabbi Yose differs from Rabbi Yose HaGlili in this regard is taken up directly: *ika beiniyahu aveira de-rabbanan* — the distinction lies in whether Rabbinic violations also qualify. Rabbi Yose HaGlili, who spoke in general terms of any *aveirot she'beyado*, would include even Rabbinic transgressions. Rabbi Yose gave specific examples that are all Biblical prohibitions, which suggests he would not extend the category to *aveirot de-rabbanan*. The Gemara confirms this by asking *keman azla* — according to whom is the *baraita* that teaches: one who speaks between the *tefillin shel yad* and the *tefillin shel rosh* has an *aveirah beyado* and returns from the battle array? The answer: this goes according to Rabbi Yose HaGlili, who extends the principle even to Rabbinic violations. Similarly, the Gemara asks the same question regarding the *baraita* that teaches: one who heard the sound of trumpets and trembled, heard the clashing of shields and trembled, heard the clashing of swords, and lost control of his bladder — *chozer* — he too must return. *Leima Rabbi Akiva hi* — perhaps this goes only according to Rabbi Akiva, who makes the category about simple physical fear? The Gemara answers: no, even Rabbi Yose HaGlili would agree in that extreme case, *mishum de'ksiv velo yimas levav achiv k'levavo* — because the Torah explicitly prohibits the melting of one's fellow soldier's heart; at that degree of visible fear, even Rabbi Yose HaGlili concedes the man must depart lest he infect the rest of the army.

## Officers & Deterrence

### Officer Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָיָה כְּכַלֹּת הַשֹּׁטְרִים לְדַבֵּר אֶל הָעָם וּפָקְדוּ שָׂרֵי צְבָאוֹת בְּרֹאשׁ הָעָם״. וּבַעֲקֵיבוֹ שֶׁל עָם מַעֲמִידִין זְקִיפִין לִפְנֵיהֶם, וַאֲחֵרִים מֵאֲחוֹרֵיהֶם, וְכַשִּׁילִין שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל בִּידֵיהֶן, וְכׇל הַמְבַקֵּשׁ לַחְזוֹר — הָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ לְקַפֵּחַ אֶת שׁוֹקָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues its discussion. The verse states: **"And it shall be, when the officers conclude speaking to the people, that captains of legions shall be appointed at the head of the people"** (Deuteronomy 20:9). The mishna adds: **As well as at the rear of the people.** The officers **station guards [*zekifin*] in front of them, and other** guards **behind them, and** they have **iron rods [*kashilin*] in their hands. And** with regard to **anyone who attempts to turn back** and flee from the war, the guard **has license to beat [*lekape'aḥ*] his legs**

*Vahaya kechalot hashotrim ledaber el ha'am* — after the officers finish speaking — *upakdu sarei tzevaot berosh ha'am* — officers are appointed at the head of the force. The Gemara adds *ube'akeivo* — and also at the rear: *ma'amidin zekifim lifnehem ve'acherim me'achareihem* — they station strong officers both in front of the troops and behind them.

### Iron Staffs

*Vekashilin shel barzel beyadeihem* — and in their hands are iron implements — whether staffs, blades, or some form of weapon. The purpose: *vechol hamevakesh lachazor, hareshut beyado lekapeach et shokav* — anyone who seeks to retreat, it is within the officers' authority to cut him down at the knees. The rationale is the principle: *techilat nifila nisa* — the beginning of falling in battle is flight. The first instinct under fire is to turn and run; armies throughout history have recognized that the most dangerous moment is when soldiers begin to flee. The fear of the consequences of desertion must outweigh the fear of advancing into battle.

### Philistine Wars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁתְּחִילַּת נִיסָה נְפִילָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נָס יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי פְלִשְׁתִּים וְגַם מַגֵּפָה גְדוֹלָה הָיְתָה בָעָם״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיָּנֻסוּ אַנְשֵׁי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִפְּנֵי פְלִשְׁתִּים וַיִּפְּלוּ חֲלָלִים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **because the beginning of fleeing** is **a downfall** on the battlefield, **as it is stated: "Israel has fled before the Philistines, and there has been also a great slaughter among the people"** (I Samuel 4:17), **and** likewise **it says further on: "And the men of Israel fled from before the Philistines, and fell down slain** in Mount Gilboa" (I Samuel 31:1).

The scriptural proof comes from two *pesukim* juxtaposed in the Gemara: *nass Yisrael lifnei Plishtim vegam mageifa gedolah haytah ba'am* — Israel fled before the Philistines and there was a great blow among the people; and *vayanusu anshei Yisrael mipnei Plishtim vayiplu chalalim* — they fled before the Philistines and fell as corpses. The pairing demonstrates the causal chain: flight *is* the beginning of slaughter.

## War Categories

### Discretionary v. Obligatory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּמִלְחֲמוֹת הָרְשׁוּת, אֲבָל בְּמִלְחֲמוֹת מִצְוָה — הַכֹּל יוֹצְאִין, אֲפִילּוּ חָתָן מֵחֶדְרוֹ וְכַלָּה מֵחוּפָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna adds: **In what** case **are** all of **these statements,** with regard to the various exemptions from war, **said?** They are said **with regard to elective wars. But in wars** whose mandate is **a mitzva, everyone goes, even a groom from his room and a bride from her wedding canopy.**

*Bameh devarim amurim* — when do all these exemptions apply? According to Rabbi Yose HaGlili: in a *milchemet reshut*, a discretionary war. *Aval bemilchemet mitzvah, hakol yotzim* — but in an obligatory *mitzvah* war, all go out, without exemption. The paradigmatic example Rashi gives of a *milchemet mitzvah* is *kibush Eretz Yisrael bimei Yehoshua* — the conquest of the Land in the days of Joshua. The Rambam includes also *milchemet Amalek* and defensive wars — *lehoshia et Yisrael miyad tzar* — wars to rescue Israel from an attacking enemy.

### R. Y. HaGlili Discretion...

In a *milchemet reshut*, the exemptions operate fully, and those with unfinished houses, undedicated vineyards, and recent betrothals return home.

### Rabbi Yehuda Obligatory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּמִלְחֲמוֹת מִצְוָה, אֲבָל בְּמִלְחֲמוֹת חוֹבָה — הַכֹּל יוֹצְאִין, אֲפִילּוּ חָתָן מֵחֶדְרוֹ וְכַלָּה מֵחוּפָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: In what** case **are** all of **these statements,** with regard to the various exemptions from war, **said?** They are said **with regard to wars** whose mandate is **a mitzva. But in obligatory wars, everyone goes, even a groom from his room and a bride from her wedding canopy.**

Rabbi Yehuda frames the same dichotomy with different terminology: *bameh devarim amurim bemilchemet mitzvah* — when do the exemptions apply? In a *mitzvah* war. *Aval bemilchemet chovah, hakol yotzim afilu chatan mechedro vechallah mechupatah* — but in a *chovah* war, all go out, even the groom from his chamber and the bride from her canopy.

### Terminology Equivalence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ עֲבֵירָה דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: With regard to their understanding that the "fearful and fainthearted" is referring to one harboring sins, **what** difference **is there between** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei and** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili?** The Gemara answers: There is a practical difference **between them** with regard to **a sin** which violates a prohibition **by rabbinic law.** According to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, one who has violated a rabbinic law returns home, whereas Rabbi Yosei maintains that one returns home only if he has violated a Torah law, as in the case of a priest who has married a divorcée.

The Gemara clarifies: are these two *tannaim* actually debating, or merely using different vocabulary for the same categories? Rabbi Yochanan states: *reshut de'rabbanan zu hi mitzvah de'Rabbi Yehuda* — what the *Rabbanan* call *reshut* is what Rabbi Yehuda calls *mitzvah*. *Mitzvah de'rabbanan zu hi chovah de'Rabbi Yehuda* — what the *Rabbanan* call *mitzvah* is what Rabbi Yehuda calls *chovah*. On this reading, the categories are substantively identical; the dispute is purely terminological.

### Three War Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִלְחֲמוֹת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְכַבֵּשׁ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חוֹבָה, מִלְחֲמוֹת בֵּית דָּוִד לִרְוָוחָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל רְשׁוּת. כִּי פְּלִיגִי לְמַעוֹטֵי נׇכְרִים דְּלָא לַיְתֵי עֲלַיְיהוּ. מָר קָרֵי לַהּ מִצְוָה וּמָר קָרֵי רְשׁוּת. נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ לְעוֹסֵק בְּמִצְוָה שֶׁפָּטוּר מִן הַמִּצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** With respect to **the wars** that **Joshua** waged **to conquer** Eretz Yisrael, **all agree** that they were **obligatory.** With respect to **the wars** waged by **the House of** King **David for** the sake of **territorial expansion, all agree** that they were **elective** wars. **When they disagree,** it is with regard to preventative wars that are waged **to reduce the gentiles so** that they will **not come** and wage war **against them.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **called this** type of war **a mitzva, and** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **called** it **an elective** war. There is **a practical difference** between these opinions with respect **to** the principle: **One who is engaged in a mitzva is exempt from** performing another **mitzva.** According to Rabbi Yehuda, one fighting in this kind of war is exempt from performing another mitzva.

Rava sharpens the analysis by introducing a tripartite framework. *Milchemet Yehoshua lichbosh — divrei hakol chovah*: the war of Joshua to conquer the Land is universally placed in the highest category. *Milchemet beit David l'rivachah — divrei hakol reshut*: expansionary wars fought by David and his successors for enlargement of territory are universally placed in the lowest category. The contested middle ground is preemptive war — *lema'utei ovdei kochavim delo leisu aleihu* — striking against enemy nations before they launch an attack. For such a war, *mar kari lei mitzvah umar kari lei reshut*: one authority calls it *mitzvah*, the other calls it *reshut*.

This is a striking Gemara. In terms of halachic outcome — whether the exemptions apply — both opinions agree: a preemptive war is not in the highest (*chovah*) category, so the exemptions for the newly built house, the vineyard, and the betrothed woman are all operative. The argument between them is not about the exemptions at all. Both the *Tanna Kamma* calling it *reshut* and Rabbi Yehuda calling it *mitzvah* agree that it is not *chovah* and thus the exemptions stand. This can be laid out concisely:

The *Tanna Kamma* operates with two levels — *reshut* and *mitzvah*. Rabbi Yehuda operates with two levels — *mitzvah* and *chovah*. Rabbi Yochanan equates these: the *Tanna Kamma*'s *reshut* = Rabbi Yehuda's *mitzvah*; the *Tanna Kamma*'s *mitzvah* = Rabbi Yehuda's *chovah*. In both systems, the lower level carries the exemptions; the upper level sends everyone to battle. Everyone agrees that Joshua's conquest belongs in the upper level and expansionary wars belong in the lower level. The preemptive war falls in the lower level for both — they simply name it differently.

### Nifka Mina

The practical implication of calling a preemptive war a *mitzvah* rather than a *reshut* is not about the exemptions but about an entirely different halachic principle: *osek bemitzvah patur min hamitzvah* — one engaged in a *mitzvah* is exempt from other *mitzvot*. If a preemptive war is classified as a *mitzvah*, then a soldier engaged in it need not interrupt his duties to perform other obligations — he would not be required to don *tefillin*, take a *lulav*, or fulfill other *mitzvot* during the campaign. If it is merely *reshut*, no such exemption applies. The *nafka mina* between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda on the question of preemptive war thus surfaces here: not in who fights, but in what obligations are suspended for those who do.

## Eglah Arufah Intro

### Opening Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה בִּלְשׁוֹן הַקּוֹדֶשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא חָלָל בָּאֲדָמָה ... וְיָצְאוּ זְקֵנֶיךָ וְשֹׁפְטֶיךָ״. שְׁלֹשָׁה מִבֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם הָיוּ יוֹצְאִין, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In certain cases of unsolved murder, the Torah prescribes a ritual performed with **a heifer whose neck is broken.** During the course of this ritual, the judges say a confession **in the sacred tongue,** Hebrew, **as it is stated** in the verse: **"If one be found slain in the land** which the Lord your God has given you to possess it, lying in the field, and it is not known who has smitten him; **then your Elders and your judges shall come forth"** (Deuteronomy 21:1–2). What is the procedure for this ritual? **Three members of the High Court [Sanhedrin] that is in Jerusalem would go out** to see the corpse. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Five** would go out.

With the conclusion of the discussion of *milchama*, the Gemara opens the final *perek* of the tractate with the *mitzvah* of *eglah arufah* — the decapitated heifer performed in response to an unsolved murder. When a corpse is discovered and the killer is unknown, the Torah prescribes a ritual in which the *zekanim* come forth and make the declaration: *yadeinu lo shafchu et hadam hazeh* — our hands did not shed this blood. This declaration is in *lashon hakodesh*, as the Gemara will address. The governing *pasuk* is *ki yimatze challal ba'adamah... veyatzu zekenecha veshoftecha*.

### Three v. Five

*Sheloshah mibeit din hagadol shebiyerushalayim hayu yotzin* — three representatives from the Great Sanhedrin of Jerusalem would go out. The *zekanim* referred to in the *pasuk* are not merely the elders of the local city but the elders of the entire nation — representatives of the central court. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *chamishah* — five are required. His derivation: *zekenecha* — your elders (plural, implying at least two) — *veshoftecha* — your judges (plural, implying at least two more) — totals four. *Ve'ein beit din shakul* — and since a *beit din* must have an odd number to avoid a deadlock, *mosifin aleihem od echad* — one more is added, yielding five.

### Disqualifying Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא טָמוּן בַּגַּל אוֹ תָּלוּי בָּאִילָן אוֹ צָף עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם — לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּאֲדָמָה״ — וְלֹא טָמוּן בַּגַּל, ״נֹפֵל״ — וְלֹא תָּלוּי בָּאִילָן, ״בַּשָּׂדֶה״ — וְלֹא צָף עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** If the corpse **was found concealed in a pile** of stones, **or hanging on a tree, or floating on the surface of the water,** then the judges **would not break the neck** of the heifer, **as it is stated:** "If one be found slain **in the land"** (Deuteronomy 21:1), **and not concealed in a pile** of stones; **"lying"** on the ground **and not hanging on a tree; "in the field," and not floating on the surface of the water.**

Certain circumstances disqualify the *eglah arufah* ritual altogether. *Nimtza tamun begal* — if the corpse was found buried under a heap of stones; *o talui be'ilan* — or hanging from a tree; *o tzaf al penei hamayim* — or floating on the surface of water — *lo hayu orfin*, the ritual is not performed. The *pasuk* requires *challal ba'adamah* — fallen on the ground: exposed on the ground (*lo tamun begal*), fallen rather than suspended (*lo talui be'ilan*), and on the ground rather than floating (*lo tzaf al penei hamayim*).

### Proximity & Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא סָמוּךְ לַסְּפָר אוֹ לְעִיר שֶׁרוּבָּהּ נׇכְרִים אוֹ לְעִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ בֵּית דִּין — לֹא הָיוּ עוֹרְפִין. אֵין מוֹדְדִין אֶלָּא לְעִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ בֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** If a corpse **was found close to the border** of the country, **or** close **to a city in which the majority of its** inhabitants **are gentiles, or** close **to a city that is without** a rabbinical **court** of twenty-three judges, then the judges **would not break the** heifer's **neck.** Additionally, the Elders **measure** the distance from the corpse **only to a city that has** a rabbinical **court** with twenty-three judges.

Additionally, if the corpse was found near a border city (*samuch le'sfar*), or near a city whose majority population is non-Jewish (*rubah ovdei kochavim*), or near a city that lacks a functioning *beit din* (*she'ein bah beit din*) — *lo hayu orfin*, the ritual is not performed. The measurement to determine which city is closest is conducted only with respect to cities that have a *beit din*: *ein modedin ela le'ir sheyesh bah beit din*. The significance of the local *beit din* here — distinct from the Great Sanhedrin representatives who come from Jerusalem — reflects the role of *ziknei ha'ir hahi*, the elders of that particular city, as participants in the ritual alongside the central court emissaries. The Gemara will elaborate on all of these requirements, and the discussion of *eglah arufah* will continue in the next session.
