# Avodah Zarah 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bittul & Shutfus

### Opening & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי וָולֶס: לֹא נִצְרְכָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בָּהּ שׁוּתָּפוּת, וְקָא מַשְׁמַע לַן יִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא דְּלָא מְבַטֵּל דְּנׇכְרִי, אֲבָל נׇכְרִי דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ מְבַטֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **of Rabbi Volas: No,** this *halakha* is **necessary** in a case **where** the gentile **has partnership in** the idol, **and** it **teaches us** that **it is** only **a Jew who cannot revoke** the status of **a gentile's** object of idol worship. **But a gentile** can **revoke** the status of **his own** object of idol worship.

Daf 53 picks up at the bottom of 52b, where the Gemara has been analyzing the *mishna* on *bittul* of *avoda zara*. The preceding discussion surfaced a striking dispute involving Rabbi Shimon and his father Rebbi — the editor of the *mishna* — concerning an early version of the *mishna* that appeared to permit a non-Jew to effect *bittul* of a Jew's *avodas kochavim*. This seemed extraordinary; we have no other source suggesting such a possibility. The Gemara resolved that the case involved *shutfus* — joint ownership — and that the Jew's worship was understood as secondary, an adjunct to the non-Jew's primary worship. On that reading, when the non-Jew nullified his portion, the entire object's status as *avoda zara* dissolved along with it. With that foundation established, the Gemara now presents an alternative version of the same discussion.

### Non-Jew Nullifying Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵינוֹ מְבַטֵּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל נׇכְרִי. פְּשִׁיטָא! אָמַר רַבִּי הִילֵּל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי וָולֶס: לֹא נִצְרְכָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בָּהּ שׁוּתָּפוּת, וְקָא מַשְׁמַע לַן יִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא דְּלָא מְבַטֵּל דְּנׇכְרִי, אֲבָל נׇכְרִי דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ מְבַטֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **There are those who teach** Rabbi Hillel's statement **with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna: **A Jew cannot revoke** the status of the object of **idol worship of a gentile.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** **Said Rabbi Hillel, son of Rabbi Volas: No,** this *halakha* is **necessary** in a case **where** the gentile **has partnership in** the idol, **and** it **teaches us** that **it is** only **a Jew who cannot revoke** the status of **a gentile's** object of idol worship. **But a gentile** can **revoke** the status of **his own** object of idol worship.

*Ika d'masni la a'seifa*: some transmit this *shutfus* analysis not as a commentary on the early reading of the *mishna* — the reading that permitted a non-Jew to be *mevatel* the *avoda zara* of a Jew — but rather as a commentary on the *seifa* of the *mishna* as it stands: *Yisrael eino mevatel avodas kochavim shel oved kochavim* — a Jew cannot be *mevatel* the idol of a non-Jew. This seems obvious, so the Gemara asks: what is the *chiddush*? One would never imagine that a Jew could go around nullifying every idol in sight; if that were possible, there would never be a *mitzvah* to destroy *avoda zara*.

### Mishna Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַבָּרַיְיתָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין לָהּ בְּטִילָה עוֹלָמִית. מַאי ״עוֹלָמִית״? אָמַר רַבִּי הִילֵּל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי וָולֶס: לֹא נִצְרְכָה, אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לְגוֹי בָּהּ שׁוּתָּפוּת, וְקָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל אַדַּעְתָּא דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ פָּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** **There are those who teach** Rabbi Hillel's statement **with regard to a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says:** The status of **a Jew's** object of **idol worship can never be revoked. What** is the reason for the additional emphasis of the term **never? Rabbi Hillel, son of Rabbi Volas, says:** The emphasis **is necessary only** for a case **where the gentile has partnership in** the idol, **and** it **teaches us that** the **Jew worships** the idol **based on his own intentions,** and therefore although the gentile revokes the status of his share, the Jew's share remains forbidden.

Rav Hillel son of Rav Vallas answers: *lo nitzrecha ela sheyesh bo bashutfus* — the *chiddush* applies to a case of joint ownership. But even then, as the Gemara presses, why would anyone think a Jew could be *mevatel* the non-Jew's idol? He cannot even be *mevatel* his own.

### Shutfus Framework

The *chiddush*, the Gemara explains, operates in the other direction: *Yisrael hu d'lo mevatel d'oved kochavim* — in a *shutfus* case, the Jew cannot be *mevatel* the non-Jew's portion, because he is incapable of performing *bittul* even on his own portion. However, *aval oved kochavim d'nafshei mevatel* — the non-Jew can be *mevatel* his own half. The precise practical implication of this asymmetry is not entirely clear, since *ein breira* would normally prevent retroactive allocation of the portions. One might imagine a scenario in which each partner directed worship at a distinct face of the object — something like the two-faced god Janus — but otherwise the halakhic mechanics remain somewhat opaque.

### Braita Parallel

A third version: *ika d'masni la a'braisa* — some transmit the *shutfus* discussion as a commentary on a *braisa*. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya states: *avodas kochavim shel Yisrael ein la biteila olamis* — an idol that belongs to a Jew can never be brought down, *olamit*, meaning ever. Rav Hillel son of Rav Vallas again supplies the application: the case is one of *shutfus*, and the *chiddush* is that even if the non-Jew performs *bittul* on his portion, the Jewish portion remains permanently non-*bateil*.

### Jewish Worship Binding

The Gemara explains: *v'kamashmalan d'Yisrael ada'atei d'nafshei palach* — the Jew worshipped the object on his own account, with his own intent. We do not say that the Jewish worship in a *shutfus* arrangement was merely an adjunct of the non-Jew's worship. Therefore, even after the non-Jew nullifies his portion, the Jewish portion retains its full status as *avoda zara* and admits of no *bittul* whatsoever. This conclusion is identical to what emerged from the earlier analysis of the first version of the debate; the difference is only that this version grounds the conclusion in a *braisa* rather than in Rebbi's earlier and subsequently revised draft of the *mishna*.

## Physical Bittul

### Cutting & Breaking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד מְבַטְּלָהּ? קָטַע רֹאשׁ אׇזְנָהּ, רֹאשׁ חוֹטְמָהּ, רֹאשׁ אֶצְבָּעָהּ, פְּחָסָהּ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא חִיסְּרָהּ — בִּיטְּלָהּ; רָק בְּפָנֶיהָ, הִשְׁתִּין בְּפָנֶיהָ, גֵּרְרָהּ, זָרַק בָּהּ אֶת הַצּוֹאָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֵינָהּ בְּטֵילָה; מְכָרָהּ אוֹ מִשְׁכְּנָהּ — רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בִּיטֵּל, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא בִּיטֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How does** a gentile **revoke** the status of an object of idol worship? If **he cut off the tip of its ear,** or **the tip of its nose,** or **its fingertip;** or if **he crushed it, even though he did not remove** any **part of it,** in all these cases he thereby **revoked** its status as an object of idol worship. If he **spat before** the idol, **urinated before it, dragged it** on the ground, or **threw excrement at it,** the status of **this** idol **is not revoked,** as this is only a temporary display of scorn, and afterward the gentile might continue to worship the idol. If the gentile **sold it or mortgaged it, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** He thereby **revoked** its status. **And the Rabbis say** that he **did not revoke** its status.

The *mishna* now addresses the mechanics of *bittul*: *keitzad mevatla* — how does one actually nullify an idol? The *mishna* specifies: if one cuts off its head, ear, nose, or finger — *kata rosho ozno rosh chotmo rosh etzba* — that constitutes *bittul*. Alternatively, if one disfigures it — *pichasa* — even if nothing is physically removed, *af al pi shelo chisra*, that too is a valid *bittul*. The visual impression is striking: the Steinsaltz edition of Gemara Avoda Zara famously features an image of an idol on its cover with the nose chipped off — an apt illustration of precisely this *mishna*.

### Non-Nullifying Acts

By contrast, the *mishna* enumerates acts of contempt that do not constitute *bittul*: spitting in its face (*rakak befaneya*), urinating before it (*hishtin befaneya*), dragging it through the mud (*gerara*), throwing excrement at it (*zarak batzo'a*) — *harei zo eina betela*. Despite being obvious expressions of disrespect, none of these effects *bittul*.

### Physical Change Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי לֹא חִיסְּרָהּ, בְּמַאי בִּיטְּלָהּ? אָמַר רַב זֵירָא: שֶׁפְּחָסָהּ בְּפָנֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if the gentile crushed the idol without removing any part of it, the status of the idol is revoked. The Gemara asks: In a case **where he did not remove** any **part of it, by what** action **did he revoke** its status? **Rav Zeira says:** The mishna is referring to a case **where he crushed its face** with a hammer, destroying its form, even though none of its stone was removed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָקַק בְּפָנֶיהָ, וְהִשְׁתִּין בְּפָנֶיהָ. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If he **spat before** the idol **or urinated before it,** the status of this idol is not revoked, as this is only a temporary display of scorn. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיָה כִי יִרְעַב וְהִתְקַצַּף וְקִלֵּל בְּמַלְכּוֹ וּבֵאלֹהָיו וּפָנָה לְמַעְלָה״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״וְאֶל אֶרֶץ יַבִּיט וְהִנֵּה צָרָה וַחֲשֵׁכָה וְגוֹ׳״, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּקִלֵּל מַלְכּוֹ וֵאלֹהָיו וּפָנָה לְמַעְלָה, אֶל אֶרֶץ יַבִּיט.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya says:** This is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "And it shall come to pass that, when he shall be hungry, he shall fret, and curse his king and his god, and turn his face upward"** (Isaiah 8:21). **And it is written after** this verse: **"And he shall look to the earth, and behold distress and darkness,** the gloom of anguish, and outspread thick darkness" (Isaiah 8:22). This indicates **that even though he cursed his king and his** idolatrous **god, and** he **turned his face upward** to God, nevertheless, **he** subsequently **looks to the earth** and beholds distress and darkness, since he returns to his idol worship.

The Gemara asks: in the case of *pichasa*, where the idol was struck but nothing was removed, what exactly constitutes the *bittul*? Rav Zeira answers: *shepichsa befaneya* — the face itself was disfigured. The point seems to be that the physical change must be conspicuous and visible, something that substantially alters the object's appearance, not merely an imperceptible dent. The broader principle taking shape is that *bittul* requires a physical change to the object itself.

As for spitting and urinating: *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for excluding these? Rav Chizkiya cites a verse: *ve-haya ki yir'av ve-yitkatzeif ve-kileil be-malko uve-elohav u-fana lema'alah* (Yeshayahu 8:21–22). The verse describes an idolater who, in his hunger and frustration, curses his king and his gods, looks upward toward God as though repenting — and then turns right back to idolatry: *ve-el aretz yabit ve-hinei tzara va-chasheicha*. The Gemara reads this as evidence that even cursing one's god, even performing acts of contempt and degradation, does not reflect a genuine abandonment of the deity. The worshipper may be furious, may do disgusting things to the idol, and yet remain fundamentally in the relationship.

This is a psychologically astute observation. The concept of *aveira l'hachis* — sinning in order to provoke — illustrates the point: when a child shouts "I hate you, you're the worst parent in the world," the very intensity of the outburst confirms that the relationship is very much intact. Throwing excrement at one's idol is, paradoxically, still a form of engagement. What is required for *bittul* is not an emotional display of anger or contempt but a definitive legal act that severs the relationship entirely — and from the Gemara's perspective, that act must leave a permanent mark on the object itself.

A natural question arises: what if the object of the *avoda zara* were a person? The Gemara does not address this, but the implication would seem to be that one cannot render a human being *assur b'hana'ah* in the same way one cannot render the landscape *assur b'hana'ah*. By the same logic, one cannot make an animal *assur b'hana'ah* — and that would apply all the more to a human being.

## Sale & Transfer

### Mechira Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכָרָהּ אוֹ מִשְׁכְּנָהּ, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בִּיטֵּל וְכוּ׳. זְעֵירִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב, חַד אָמַר: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּצוֹרֵף גּוֹי, אֲבָל בְּצוֹרֵף יִשְׂרָאֵל — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בִּיטֵּל, וְחַד אָמַר: בְּצוֹרֵף יִשְׂרָאֵל מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the gentile **sold it or mortgaged it, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** He thereby **revoked** its status. And the Rabbis say that he did not revoke its status. The Gemara cites a dispute between that which **Ze'eiri** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and** that which **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba** says that **Rav says. One says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis applies only **when** the gentile sold his idol to **a gentile smith. But when** he sold it to **a Jewish smith everyone agrees** that by selling the idol the gentile **revoked** its status, as he knows that the Jewish smith will certainly melt it down. **And one says:** The **dispute** applies **to** the case where he sold the idol to **a Jewish smith.**

The *mishna* continues: *machara o mashkana* — if a non-Jew sold the idol or gave it as *mashkon* (collateral for a loan), what is the status? *Rebbi omer bitel* — Rebbi holds this constitutes *bittul*. *Va-chachamim omrim lo bitel* — the Sages disagree. This dispute is structurally important. For Rebbi, *bittul* clearly does not require a physical change to the object; a permanent, decisive act of transfer — placing the idol beyond one's control — is sufficient. For the *chachamim*, the question remains open: are they insisting categorically that only physical change suffices, or are there acts short of physical change that could, under the right circumstances, still constitute *bittul*?

One might observe that selling an idol and spitting at it differ in a critical respect: spitting leaves no lasting mark and can be undone the very next day, whereas a sale transfers control permanently. Once sold, the object is out of the seller's hands. This distinction — permanence rather than physical change — appears to be the animating principle behind Rebbi's position.

### Two Version Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּצוֹרֵף יִשְׂרָאֵל מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל צוֹרֵף גּוֹי דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לֹא בִּיטֵּל, אוֹ דִלְמָא בֵּין בָּזֶה וּבֵין בָּזֶה מַחְלוֹקֶת?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: According to the second opinion, does the **dispute** apply only **to** the case where he sold the idol to **a Jewish smith; but** if he sold it to **a gentile smith everyone agrees** that he **did not revoke** its status by selling it? **Or perhaps both in this** case **and in that** case there is **a dispute.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דְּבָרַיי כְּשֶׁמְּכָרָהּ לְחַבְּלָהּ, וְדִבְרֵי חֲבֵירַיי שֶׁמְּכָרָהּ לְעוֹבְדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*, **as Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: My statement** that by selling the idol the gentile revokes its status **appears** correct in a case **where he sold it for** the purpose of **destruction, and the statement of my colleagues** that its status is not revoked appears correct in a case **where he sold it for** the purpose of **worship.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לְחַבְּלָהּ״ וּמַאי ״לְעוֹבְדָהּ״? אִילֵּימָא לְחַבְּלָהּ — לְחַבְּלָהּ מַמָּשׁ, לְעוֹבְדָהּ — לְעוֹבְדָהּ מַמָּשׁ, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּיטֵּל, וּמַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לֹא בִּיטֵּל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the *baraita*: **What** is the meaning of selling the idol **for destruction, and what** is the meaning of selling it **for worship? If we say** that selling it **for destruction** means **literally** that he knew that it was being bought **for** the purpose of **destruction,** and that selling it **for worship** means **literally** that it was bought **for** the purpose of **worship,** this is difficult. **What is the reasoning of the one who says** that the gentile **revoked** the idol's status even though he knew that the buyer intended to worship it, **and what is the reasoning of the one who says** that he **did not revoke** its status even though he knew that the buyer intended to destroy it?

The Gemara then poses the question sharply: according to the version that the dispute concerns only the Jewish smelter — does Rebbi agree that selling to a non-Jewish smelter is not *bittul*? Or does Rebbi hold that even a non-Jewish sale is *bittul* in all cases? The Gemara invokes a *braisa*: *nirin devarai k'she-machra l'chavalah v'divrei chaverai k'she-machra l'ovdah* — Rebbi says his position seems correct when the sale is for destruction, and his colleagues' position seems correct when the sale is for eventual re-worship.

### Intent & Tradition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו לְחַבְּלָהּ — לְמִי שֶׁעָתִיד לְחַבְּלָהּ, וּמַנּוּ? צוֹרֵף יִשְׂרָאֵל. לְעוֹבְדָהּ — לְמִי שֶׁעָתִיד לְעוֹבְדָהּ, וּמַנּוּ? צוֹרֵף גּוֹי. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בֵּין בָּזֶה וּבֵין בָּזֶה מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** referring to a case where the buyer's intentions were not known with certainty? And accordingly, selling the idol **for destruction** means selling it **to one who will** presumably **destroy it in the future. And who is that** buyer? This is referring to **a Jewish smith.** Similarly, selling the idol **for worship** means selling it **to one who will** presumably **worship it in the future. And who is that** buyer? This is referring to **a gentile smith.** Since Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi states that his opinion appears correct in the case of a Jewish smith and the opinion of his colleagues appears correct in the case of a gentile smith, one **may conclude from** the *baraita* that there is **a dispute both in this** case **and in that** case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דְּבָרַיי לַחֲבֵירַיי כְּשֶׁמְּכָרָהּ לְחַבְּלָהּ. וּמַנּוּ? צוֹרֵף יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁאַף חֲבֵירַיי לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ עָלַי אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁמְּכָרָהּ לְעוֹבְדָהּ, אֲבָל לְחַבְּלָהּ — מוֹדוּ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: My statement** that the idol's status is revoked **appears to my colleagues** correct in a case **where he sold it for** the purpose of **destruction. And who is it** who buys the idol with the intent of destroying it? This is referring to **a Jewish smith.** This is **because even my colleagues disagreed with me only** in a case **where he sold it for** the purpose of **worship; but** when he sold it to a Jewish smith **for** the purpose of **destruction, they concede to my** opinion.

Rashi's reading of this *braisa* is particularly striking. Rashi explains that Rebbi and the *chachamim* are each transmitting traditions they received. Rebbi's tradition holds that selling constitutes *bittul* in all cases; the *chachamim*'s tradition holds that it never does. What Rebbi adds — *nirin devarai*, my position seems correct — is a retrospective comment on the intellectual logic of each position, not a modification of the tradition itself. Stepping back from the received tradition, Rebbi observes: my tradition makes better sense in a case where the idol is sold for destruction; their tradition makes better sense in a case where re-worship is the likely outcome. This framing is remarkable: *posekim* present their traditions authoritatively, and only afterwards may reflect analytically on the conditions under which each tradition finds its most natural application.

The Gemara then works through the possible readings of *l'chavalah* and *l'ovdah* — whether these refer to explicitly stated intentions or to likely outcomes based on the identity of the buyer. The conclusion: *l'chavalah* means sold to someone who will most likely destroy it — a Jewish smelter — and *l'ovdah* means sold to someone who will most likely resell it for worship — a non-Jewish smelter. On this reading, Rebbi is saying: even my colleagues agree with me in the Jewish smelter case; we only dispute the non-Jewish smelter case. This aligns with the first version of the dispute presented earlier — that by a *tzoref Yisrael* everyone agrees it is *bittul*, and the debate concerns the non-Jewish smelter.

### Mekach Taos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַלּוֹקֵחַ גְּרוּטָאוֹת מִן הַגּוֹיִם וּמָצָא בָּהֶן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן מָעוֹת מָשַׁךְ — יַחֲזִיר, אִם מִשֶּׁנָּתַן מָעוֹת מָשַׁךְ — יוֹלִיךְ לְיָם הַמֶּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **one who buys broken vessels** made from gold or silver **from the gentiles and finds among them** an object of **idol worship, if he pulled** the object of idol worship, thereby performing an act of acquisition, **before he gave** the **money** to the gentile, **he may return** the object of idol worship to the gentile. But **if he pulled** it **after he gave** the **money** to the gentile he may not return it. Since the idol's status was not revoked, he must **take** it and cast it **into the Dead Sea.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּצוֹרֵף יִשְׂרָאֵל מַחְלוֹקֶת, הָא מַנִּי? רַבָּנַן הִיא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּצוֹרֵף גּוֹי מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל בְּצוֹרֵף יִשְׂרָאֵל דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בִּיטֵּל, הָא מַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **Granted, if you say** that **in** the case of a gentile who sells an object of idol worship to **a Jewish smith** there is **a dispute** between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis, the *baraita* is not difficult. In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that when a gentile sells an idol to a Jewish smith he does not thereby revoke its status. **But if you say** that the **dispute** between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis applies only **when** the idol is sold to **a gentile smith, but in** the case of **a Jewish smith everyone agrees** that the gentile **revoked** the idol's status, then in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאַדַּעְתָּא דִּגְרוּטָאוֹת זַבֵּין, אַדַּעְתָּא דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לָא זַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **is different there, as** the gentile **sold** the metal **with the understanding that** he was selling **broken vessels,** and **he did not sell** the metal **with the understanding that** he was selling an object of **idol worship.** He therefore had no intention of revoking its status.

The Gemara also addresses a practical question: if a trader purchases a batch of scrap metal from a non-Jew and discovers an idol mixed in — *halokeach grutaos min ovdei kochavim u-matza vahen avodas kochavim* — why is there any problem at all? The non-Jew, after all, sold it to the Jew. Should that sale not constitute *bittul* and render the idol permitted? The answer: *shani hasam de-ada'ata de-grutaos zavin, ada'ata de-avodas kochavim lo zavin* — it was a *ta'os*, a mistake, on the part of the non-Jew as well. He did not knowingly sell the idol; had he known it was there, he would not have sold it. An unknowing sale cannot constitute *bittul* precisely because *bittul* requires a deliberate act of abandonment. A sale made without awareness of the idol's presence reflects no intent whatsoever regarding its status.

This ruling generates its own subsidiary discussion. *Im ad shelo nasan ma'os mashach, yachzir* — if the buyer performed *meshicha* before paying, he may return the idol and pay only for the remaining metal. According to Rashi, *meshicha* does effect *kinyan* even from a non-Jew, but the transaction is a *mekach ta'os* — a mistaken purchase — since the buyer never intended to acquire an idol. He is therefore entitled to treat the idol as never having been purchased. However, *im mishenasan ma'os mashach, yolich l'yam hamelach* — if *meshicha* was performed after payment, the buyer must dispose of it in the Dead Sea rather than return it, because returning it while receiving money would create the appearance of selling an idol for *hana'ah*, even though technically he is entitled to a refund.

## Abandonment

### Hidden Idol Discovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לָוָה עָלֶיהָ, אוֹ שֶׁנָּפְלָה עָלֶיהָ מַפּוֹלֶת, אוֹ שֶׁגְּנָבוּהָ לִיסְטִין, אוֹ שֶׁהִנִּיחוּהָ הַבְּעָלִים וְהָלְכוּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם,
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If a gentile **borrowed** money **against** an object of idol worship, using it as collateral, **or** with regard to another case **where a rockslide fell on it, or** a case **where robbers stole it, or** a case **where the owners abandoned it and went overseas,** the following *halakha* applies:

### Mekach Taos

As noted above, when a buyer discovers an idol within a batch of scrap metal purchased from a non-Jew, the governing principle is *mekach ta'os*: the purchase was a mistaken one. If *meshicha* preceded payment, the buyer may simply return the idol without further consequence, since he never genuinely acquired it. This is distinguished from a case where the non-Jew consciously sold the idol: in a knowing sale, the circumstances differ entirely, and the idol may well have been *bateil* through that deliberate act.

### Dead Sea Disposal

Where payment preceded *meshicha*, however, the prohibition against appearing to derive *hana'ah* from the sale of *avoda zara* prevents a straightforward return. The buyer must instead dispose of the idol in the Dead Sea — *yolich l'yam hamelach*. There is a broader question in the Gemara in Pesachim, with a significant *Tosafot*, about whether the Dead Sea here is meant literally or functions as an idiom for permanent disposal, but the operative rule is that the idol must be eliminated in a manner that cannot be confused with a sale.

### Non-Actions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם עֲתִידִין לַחְזוֹר כְּמִלְחֶמֶת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ — אֵינָהּ בְּטֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** In any of these cases, **if** the owners **will return in the future, as** was the case in the **war of Joshua** when he conquered Eretz Yisrael, then the status of the object of idol worship **is not revoked.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי תְּנָא לָוָה עָלֶיהָ — מִדְּלָא זַבְּנַהּ, לָא בַּטְּלַהּ; אֲבָל נָפְלָה עָלֶיהָ מַפּוֹלֶת — מִדְּלָא קָא מְפַנֵּי לַהּ, אֵימָא בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלַהּ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** for the *baraita* to state all of these cases. **As, had** the *baraita* **taught** only the case where the gentile **borrowed** money **against** the object of idol worship, one would say that in that case the gentile **did not revoke** its status, **as he did not sell it,** and he did not indicate that he intended to relinquish it. **But** in the case where **a rockslide fell on it, since he did not clear** the rocks, **say** that **he revoked** the status of the object of idol worship. It is therefore **necessary** to teach this case as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא נָפְלָה עָלֶיהָ מַפּוֹלֶת, מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר: הָא מַנְּחָה, כֹּל אֵימַת דְּבָעֵינָא לַהּ שָׁקֵילְנָא לַהּ, אֲבָל גְּנָבוּהָ לִסְטִים, מִדְּלָא קָא מַהְדַּר אַבָּתְרַהּ — בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלַהּ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the *baraita* **taught** only the case where **a rockslide fell on** the idol, one would say that in that case its status is not revoked **because** the owner **thinks** to himself: The idol **lies** under the rocks safely; **whenever I want it, I shall take it,** and he feels no need to clear the rockslide immediately. **But** in the case where **robbers stole it, since he is not searching after it,** this indicates that **he revoked** its status. It is therefore **necessary** to teach this case as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא גְּנָבוּהָ לִסְטִין, מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר: אִי גּוֹי שָׁקֵיל לַהּ — מִפְלָח פָּלַח לַהּ, אִי יִשְׂרָאֵל שָׁקְלָה — אַיְּידֵי דְּדָמֶיהָ יְקָרִין מְזַבֵּין לַהּ לְגוֹי וּפָלַח לַהּ, אֲבָל הִנִּיחוּהָ הַבְּעָלִים וְהָלְכוּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, מִדְּלָא שָׁקְלוּ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ — בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלוּהָ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the *baraita* **taught** only the case where **robbers stole** the idol, one would say that in that case its status is not revoked **because** the owner **thinks** to himself: **If a gentile took it, he will worship it. If a Jew took it, since it is of great monetary** value, **he will sell it to a gentile and** the buyer **will worship it.** There is therefore no indication that the gentile intends to revoke its status. **But** in the case where **the owners abandoned** the idol **and went overseas, since they did not take it with them,** this indicates that **they revoked** its status. It is therefore **necessary** to teach this case as well.

The *braisa* enumerates a series of passive situations in which an owner fails to act on behalf of his idol and the question is whether that inaction constitutes *bittul*. The discussion dovetails with what was established regarding the sale cases above, but the emphasis now shifts from active transfer to passive non-engagement.

### Rubble Example

If a building fell on the idol and the owner simply does not bother to excavate it, one might have thought that the failure to dig it out signals complete indifference — and that indifference constitutes *bittul*. The Gemara dismisses this: *mishum desavar, hamancha, kol eimat d'beina lah shakilna lah* — the owner reasons: the idol is not going anywhere; it is still there; when I need it again, I will dig it out. Not bothering to excavate something is not the same as abandoning it. The idol's continued presence under the rubble, accessible whenever the owner chooses to retrieve it, means no act of renunciation has taken place.

This contrasts interestingly with the earlier cases. Even Rebbi, who permits *bittul* through sale and through actual transfer as *mashkon*, concedes that passively failing to retrieve one's idol from rubble is not *bittul*. The same logic applies to borrowing money against it without physically handing it over: since the idol remains in the owner's home and is not actually transferred, it is far below even the threshold Rebbi set for *bittul* through *mashkena*.

This *braisa* is in fact most naturally consistent with Rebbi's position: the *chiddush* is aimed primarily at the reader who might have thought that these non-actions — especially the more dramatic ones like not pursuing a stolen idol — constitute *bittul* even within Rebbi's more expansive framework. Within the *chachamim*'s position, which demands physical change, the conclusion is obvious. For Rebbi, it is genuinely instructive.

### Theft & Belief

If bandits steal the idol and the owner does not pursue it, why does non-pursuit not constitute *bittul*? After all, the owner effectively has no way of recovering it, and his failure to chase after it might seem equivalent to the permanent loss of control that characterizes a sale. The Gemara explains: *mishum desavar oved kochavim shakil miplach palach lah* — the owner reasons that a non-Jewish thief will worship the idol himself. Just as one might not bother filing a police report for a stolen item because one knows nothing will come of it — not because one no longer values the item — so here the owner does not pursue the idol because he doubts he will recover it, not because he has ceased to regard it as divine. Someone else has it; let them worship it in good health.

A further nuance: what if a *Jew* stole the idol? One might think the owner would be indifferent because he knows the Jew will melt it down. But the Gemara says: *adei dedamei yikarin mazbin lei l'oved kochavim u-falach lei* — the Jew, being a good businessman, will find it more profitable to resell the idol than to smelt it. The idol is worth more intact than as raw metal. Therefore, even a theft by a Jew does not render the owner's non-pursuit an act of *bittul*: he still anticipates the idol will be worshipped by whoever eventually acquires it.

### Yehoshua War

If the owners left on a trip and did not take the idol with them, one might think — since the idol is portable and they will be away for a year — that their failure to bring it along signals genuine abandonment. After all, if they truly believed in this deity, they would want it present in Hawaii with them. The Gemara says: *bitulei batla* — perhaps this should indeed be considered *bittul*. The answer: *tzricha* — it is necessary to teach us otherwise. Even leaving for an extended period does not constitute *bittul* if one is planning to return — *im atidim lachazor*, *eina betela*. The owner simply does not need the idol at this moment; when he comes back, he will worship it at home and find other gods to worship in the interim.

The *braisa* invokes *milchemet Yehoshua* as the paradigm: the Canaanites who fled before the advancing Israelite armies intended to return after a hoped-for victory. Their flight did not constitute *bittul* because they anticipated coming back. This reference seems oddly specific, but as the Gemara will shortly clarify, the mention of *milchemet Yehoshua* is not incidental — it is signaling a much deeper doctrinal point about *avoda zara* in *Eretz Yisrael* that will shortly come into focus.

## Golden Calf Doctrine

### No Bittul in EY

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן דַּאֲסָרָהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כַּתְּחִילָּה שֶׁל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״וַאֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ״, מִכְּדֵי יְרוּשָּׁה הִיא לָהֶם מֵאֲבוֹתֵיהֶם, וְאֵין אָדָם אוֹסֵר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **From where do we** derive **that he rendered it prohibited? Rabbi Elazar says:** This *halakha* is **like** the *halakha* that applied at the **outset of** the Jewish people's conquest of **Eretz Yisrael,** when the Torah commanded them to destroy any trees that were used as part of idolatrous rites [*asherim*], **as the Merciful One states:** "And you shall break down their altars…**and you shall burn their *asherim* with fire"** (Deuteronomy 12:3). **Now,** Eretz Yisrael **is** the **inheritance of** the Jewish people **from their ancestors, and a person does not render forbidden an item that is not his.** If so, how could the gentiles render the trees forbidden, as the land was not theirs?

### Brick Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מִשּׁוּם הָנָךְ דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא, בְּבִיטּוּלָא בְּעָלְמָא סַגִּי לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **If** the trees were forbidden **because** some of them might have been **those** trees **that** were worshipped **initially,** before God gave the land to Abraham, it would not have been necessary to destroy them. Rather, the Jews could have forced the gentiles to revoke their status, and since the *asherim* were objects of gentiles' idol worship, **a mere revocation** would be **sufficient to** render **them** permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִדִּפְלַחוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְעֵגֶל, גַּלּוֹ אַדַּעְתַּיְיהוּ דְּנִיחָא לְהוּ בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְכִי אֲתוֹ גּוֹיִם, שְׁלִיחוּתָא דִּידְהוּ עָבְדִי. הָכִי נָמֵי, יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁזָּקַף לְבֵינָה, גַּלִּיא דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְכִי אֲתָא גּוֹי וּפְלַח לַהּ, שְׁלִיחוּתָא דִּידֵיהּ קָעָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, since the Jewish people worshipped the** Golden **Calf, they revealed their intentions** and indicated **that they were amenable to idol worship. And when** the **gentiles came** and engaged in idol worship, they were, in effect, **carrying out** their **agency** on behalf **of** the Jewish people. The *asherim* were therefore considered objects of Jews' idol worship, whose status cannot be revoked. **So too,** in the case of **a Jew who set a brick** upright in order to bow to it, **he** thereby **revealed his intentions** and indicated **that he is amenable to idol worship. And when a gentile came and worshipped it, he was carrying out the agency** on behalf **of** the Jew.

The mention of *milchemet Yehoshua* in the *braisa* prompts a fundamental question: why were the Israelites required to destroy all the *avoda zara* in the land of Canaan at all? If *bittul* is a functional legal mechanism, the simplest approach would have been to compel a single surviving Canaanite to perform *bittul* on every idolatrous site in the land. The Torah commands *va-asheireihem tisrefun ba-eish* — you shall burn their *asheirot* in fire — but within the *halachic* framework the Gemara has developed, one might ask why physical destruction was necessary when *bittul* by a non-Jew could have accomplished the same result far more efficiently.

Before reaching this question, the Gemara introduces a ruling of Rav Yehuda citing Rav that clarifies a precondition: *Yisrael she-zakaf leveina ve-hishtachavah lah, u-va oved kochavim ve-hishtachavah lah, asrah* — a Jew who erected a brick with the intention that it be worshipped, and a non-Jew who then came and worshipped it, renders the brick forbidden. Ordinarily a non-Jew cannot make forbidden something he does not own. But here the Jew's act of erection signaled his desire for the brick to be worshipped, and the non-Jew's worship is therefore viewed as fulfilling the Jew's intention — as though acting as the Jew's *shaliach*. The non-Jew's worship is the Jew's worship, and what results is *avoda zara shel Yisrael*, which, as established, admits of no *bittul* at all.

### Land Ownership

The source for this principle is Rabbi Elazar's statement: *k'techilah shel Eretz Yisrael* — like the original status of the Land of Israel. The Gemara explains: *yerushah hi lahem me-avoteihem* — the land was an inheritance for the Jewish people from their forefathers, from the time of Avraham. The entire land of Israel always belonged to the Jews. The corollary follows: *v'ein adam oser davar she-eino shelo* — no one can render forbidden something that is not his. Therefore, when the Canaanites worshipped trees and stones in the land, they were worshipping things that legally belonged to the Jewish people. This would mean the Canaanites should not have been able to render the land's objects forbidden at all. But this generates an earlier difficulty: if the land was always ours, why could the Canaanites make *avoda zara* at all? And conversely, regarding the older shrines — those worshipped before Avraham — even if the Canaanites could make them forbidden, a simple *bittul* by a non-Jew should have sufficed: *bevitula b'alma sagi lehu*. Why burn everything?

### Signaling Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא בְּעֵגֶל הוּא דְּנִיחָא לְהוּ, בְּמִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֵלֶּה אֱלֹהֶיךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאִיוּוּ לֶאֱלוֹהוֹת הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps it is** only **with regard to the** Golden **Calf that** the Jewish people **were amenable** to worshipping it, but **not with regard to** any **other** type of idol worship. The Gemara counters: **The verse states** with regard to the Golden Calf: "And they said: **These are your gods, O Israel"** (Exodus 32:4), in the plural. This **teaches that they desired many gods,** and they did not desire to worship only the Golden Calf.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: כֹּל דְּבַהֲדֵי עֵגֶל נִיתַּסְרוּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ נִישְׁתְּרֵי? מַאן מוֹכַח?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Say** that **whatever** *asherim* **were** worshipped by the gentiles simultaneously **with** the Jewish people's worship of the Golden **Calf should be prohibited,** because at that time they acted as agents of the Jewish people. But any *ashera* that was worshipped **from that** point **forward,** after the Jewish people repented and no longer engaged in idol worship, **should be permitted.** The Gemara answers: **Who can prove** when each *ashera* was worshipped? Since it is impossible to determine which *asherim* were worshipped at the time of the Golden Calf, they are all forbidden.

The answer is the *eigel*: *mide-palchu Yisrael la-eigel, galu ada'ataiyu d'nicha lehu ba-avodat kochavim* — when the Jews worshipped the golden calf, they publicly signaled that they are comfortable with *avoda zara*. This is *hashkalta* — signaling intent. That moment of worship was not merely a sin against God; it was a declaration of preference, an advertisement of desire for idolatry of all kinds.

The consequence is halakhically devastating: *ve-chi avdi ovdei kochavim shelichuta didhu avdi* — any *avoda zara* worship that subsequently took place in the land of Canaan is considered as having been performed on behalf of the Jews, as their *shlichim*. The Jews desired *avoda zara*; the land was theirs; any worship of objects within that land was therefore the Jews' worship. Everything in the land became *avoda zara shel Yisrael*.

This is a remarkable and — as the Gemara itself acknowledges — somewhat extraordinary construction. Two difficulties immediately present themselves. First: even granting that the Jews desired *avoda zara* through the golden calf, why should their desire for *this* idol translate into endorsement of *every* form of idolatry throughout the land? Perhaps the Jewish desire was specific to the calf, not to Canaanite worship generally.

The Gemara answers from the verse *eileh elohecha Yisrael* — the plural *elohim* in the golden calf episode demonstrates that the people desired multiple forms of divine worship simultaneously: *melamed she-ivu le-elohut harbeh* — they craved a multiplicity of gods. Their intent was not narrowly focused on one idol; they were signaling a broad embrace of idolatry in all its forms.

Second difficulty: why does the land connection matter? If I worship a golden calf in the wilderness and thereby signal my love of *avoda zara*, why does that make a Canaanite worshipping his own idol *in his own land* into *avoda zara shel Yisrael*? The answer lies precisely in the *yerushah* principle: the land of Israel was never genuinely Canaanite. Since it was always the Jewish inheritance, anything worshipped on that land is on Jewish property, and Jewish property combined with Jewish desire for idolatry means Jewish worship. The logic is confined to *Eretz Yisrael*; idolatry happening in France or India, even after the *eigel*, remains the worship of those nations and does not become the Jews' responsibility.

The theological weight of this argument is striking. One tends to read the conquest narratives in terms of Israel purifying a foreign land, wiping away alien worship so that the land of God can be inhabited by God's people. What the Gemara reveals is nearly the inverse: the *passion* with which all Canaanite *avoda zara* had to be destroyed was because it was all, ultimately, *ours* — an extension of *cheit ha-eigel*. The Israelites were not destroying foreign gods; they were dismantling an enormous complex of idolatry that their own sin had made into *avoda zara shel Yisrael*. That is why it admits of no *bittul*, and that is why *milchemet Yehoshua* had to be so thoroughgoing.

The Gemara further notes: *man mochi'ach* — who can prove which objects in the land were worshipped at the precise moment of the *eigel* and which only afterward, after *teshuva* had been done? Since it is impossible to distinguish, the entire category must be treated as permanently forbidden.

### Olamit Status

This now clarifies the force of the word *olamit* in the earlier *braisa* of Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, and clarifies the reference to *milchemet Yehoshua* in the *braisa* about abandonment. The word *olamit* — never, ever — signals not merely that this particular idol is stubbornly resistant to *bittul*, but that the category of *avoda zara shel Yisrael* carries a fundamental, permanent, irremediable status. The mention of *milchemet Yehoshua* is a shorthand for this entire doctrine: the *avoda zara* of that war was *avoda zara* of *Yisrael*, rooted in *cheit ha-eigel*, and it is therefore permanently beyond the reach of *bittul*, *olamit*.

## Abandonment Categories

### Peace vs. War

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁהִנִּיחוּהָ עוֹבְדֶיהָ — בִּשְׁעַת שָׁלוֹם מוּתֶּרֶת, בִּשְׁעַת מִלְחָמָה אֲסוּרָה. בִּימוֹסְיָאוֹת שֶׁל מְלָכִים — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מוּתָּרוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַמְּלָכִים עוֹבְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to an object of **idol worship that was abandoned by its worshippers,** if it was abandoned **in peacetime,** it is **permitted,** as it was evidently abandoned by choice and this constitutes an implicit revocation of its status as an object of idol worship. If it was abandoned **in wartime,** it is **prohibited,** as it was not abandoned by choice. With regard to the stone **platforms of kings** upon which idols are placed in honor of the kings, **these are permitted, due to** the fact **that** the idol is **placed** on these platforms only **at the time that the kings pass by.**

The next *mishna* addresses a different mode of abandonment: *avodat kochavim she-henichu ovdeiha bishat shalom* — an idol that its worshippers left behind during a time of peace. Such an idol is *muteret* — permitted. *Bishat milchama* — if left during wartime, it remains *assurah* — forbidden. The rationale is that people fleeing from an invading army have not chosen to abandon their god; they were forced out. The desertion of an idol at a moment of existential danger says nothing about the worshipper's ongoing relationship to it. But leaving it behind in a time of peace — when nothing compels the departure — is a genuine act of abandonment.

### Royal Platforms

*Bimasot shel melachim* — platforms erected for idols along the route kings are expected to travel — *harei elu mutarot*, these are permitted. The reason given by the *mishna* is that they are erected only for the duration of a king's passage. Why temporary erection should automatically render them *muttar* is not immediately clear, and the Gemara will address this directly.

### Babel Tower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: בֵּית נִמְרוֹד הֲרֵי הִיא כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁהִנִּיחוּהָ עוֹבְדֶיהָ בִּשְׁעַת שָׁלוֹם, וּמוּתָּר. אַף עַל גַּב דְּכִי בַּדְּרִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא כִּשְׁעַת מִלְחָמָה דָּמֵי, אִי (בָּעֲיָא) [בְּעוֹ] לְמִיהְדָּר הֲדוּר, מִדְּלָא הֲדוּר בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלֻהָ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says** that **Rav says: The temple of Nimrod,** i.e., the remnants of the tower of Babel (see Genesis 11:1–9), **is considered** a place of **idol worship whose worshippers abandoned it in peacetime, and** it is therefore **permitted.** This is **despite** the fact **that when the Merciful One scattered** the builders of the tower, the situation **resembled wartime,** as they were compelled to leave. Nevertheless, **if they had desired to return, they** could have **returned. Since they did not return,** they evidently chose to abandon the place of idol worship and thereby **revoked** its status.

Rav Yehuda citing Rav teaches: *Beis Nimrod* — which Rashi identifies as the *Migdal Bavel* — has the status of *avodat kochavim she-henichu ovdeiha bishat shalom*. One might have assumed the opposite: God scattered the builders against their will, which is analogous to wartime flight, and therefore the tower should remain *assur*. But Rav holds it is treated as a peaceful abandonment and is therefore *muttar*.

The reasoning: *af al gav deki baddarnehu Rachmana, keshat milchama dami* — even though the divine scattering was in some sense like a military expulsion, *ei ba'u lemehedar haduru* — those who were scattered could always have returned afterward. Nothing physically prevented them from coming back to Babylon and returning to the tower. *Mi-dela haduru bitulei bitelah* — since they chose not to return, that passive choice constitutes *bittul*. The act of not returning, when one had every opportunity to return, is itself a statement of abandonment.

This stands in apparent tension with the earlier ruling that if one's idol is stolen and one does not pursue it, that non-pursuit is not *bittul*. The difference may be that in the theft case, the owner cannot recover the idol regardless; his passivity reflects practical helplessness rather than genuine renunciation. In the *Migdal Bavel* case, there was nothing stopping the builders from returning — and yet they did not. That voluntary non-return, freely chosen over an extended period, carries the force of a deliberate decision to abandon.

The Gemara draws an analogy to the *Iggeret HaShmad* of the Rambam, who addressed Jews who had converted under threat of death and were later in circumstances where they could safely return to Jewish practice. Rambam was deeply sympathetic to *anusim*, but he noted that once the immediate threat had passed and a genuine opportunity to return existed, continued non-return could no longer be called pure *ones*. The parallel is instructive: voluntary non-return, when return is available, becomes a meaningful act.

### Route Uncertainty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּימוֹסְיָאוֹת שֶׁל מְלָכִים — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מוּתָּרוֹת. וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַמְּלָכִים עוֹבְרִין מוּתָּרִין?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to the stone **platforms of kings, these are permitted,** due to the fact that the idol is placed on these platforms only at the time that the kings pass by. The Gemara asks: **But** should the platforms be **permitted because** idols are **placed on them at the time that the kings pass by?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָן בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַמְּלָכִים עוֹבְרִין, וּמְלָכִים מַנִּיחִין דֶּרֶךְ זוֹ וְהוֹלְכִין בְּדֶרֶךְ אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: This** is what the mishna **is saying:** The platforms are permitted **because** the idol is **placed** on **them at the time that the kings pass by, and** sometimes the **kings abandon this path** where the platform has been placed **and** choose to **walk on a different path.** Since the kings do not impart any importance to the platforms, they are not considered accessories of idol worship.

Returning to the *bimasot shel melachim*: the Gemara asks how these platforms are permitted at all. Rabbah bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan explains: *mipnei she-ma'amidun otah bishat she-melachim ovrim u-melachim manichin derech zo v'holchim b'derech acheret* — the platforms are erected along the anticipated royal route, but kings frequently choose an alternative route. Since those who erected the platforms always knew the king might not pass this way, the idol was erected from the outset under conditions of uncertainty. Rashi explains that since the kings ultimately do not care about these idols — having taken a different route — they are not considered meaningful *tashmishei avoda zara*. Tosafot takes a somewhat different approach: since the erection was always contingent on the king's passage, and since the king did not in fact pass, the object was never truly consecrated as an idol at all. Either way, the element of uncertainty in the original intention removes the standard status that would otherwise attach to an idol or its accessories.

## Tashmish Status

### Platform Breakage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא עוּלָּא, יָתֵיב אַבִּימְסָא פְּגִימָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְעוּלָּא: וְהָא רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בִּימוֹס שֶׁנִּפְגַּם אָסוּר! וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אֵין עוֹבְדִים לִשְׁבָרִים — הָנֵי מִילֵּי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דְּזִילָא בֵּיהּ מִלְּתָא לְמִפְלַח לִשְׁבָרִים, אֲבָל הַאי לָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** § **When Ulla came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he sat on a damaged platform** of idol worship. **Rav Yehuda said to Ulla: But don't Rav and Shmuel both say: A platform that was damaged** remains **prohibited? And even according to the one who says** that idol worshippers **do not worship fragments** of idols, **that statement** applies only to an object of **idol worship, as** it is **a degrading matter to** a person **to worship fragments. But** in **this** case, with regard to the platform, it **does not matter to him** if it is damaged, as it is still fit for use.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאן יָהֵיב לַן מֵעַפְרָא דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל וּמָלִינַן עַיְינִין? הָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בִּימוֹס שֶׁנִּפְגַּם — מוּתָּר, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר עוֹבְדִין לִשְׁבָרִים, הָנֵי מִילֵּי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דְּכֵיוָן דְּפַלְחַהּ — זִילָא בֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא לְבַטּוֹלַהּ, אֲבָל הָנֵי שָׁקְלִי לְהַאי וּמַיְיתוּ בִּימוֹס אַחֲרִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla **said to** Rav Yehuda: **Who shall give us of the dust** of the graves covering **Rav and Shmuel? We** would **fill our eyes** with that dust, as they were great and holy men. Nevertheless, with regard to the *halakha* in this case, **don't Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish both say: A platform that was damaged** is **permitted? And even according to the one who says** that idol worshippers **worship fragments** of idols, **that statement** applies only to an object of **idol worship, due to** the fact that **since he** already **worshipped it,** it is **a degrading matter to him to revoke** its status. **But** in the case of **these** platforms, idol worshippers **take this** platform and throw it away **and bring another platform** that is not defective to replace it.

When Ulla arrived and sat on a chipped platform — *abimtza pagima* — Rav Yehuda challenged him: do not Rav and Shmuel both hold that *bimus she-nifgam assur* — a platform that has been chipped remains forbidden? Even according to the opinion that a broken idol is permitted — *ein ovdim l'shvarim*, that people do not worship broken pieces, so a self-broken idol may be considered automatically *batel* — that logic applies to the idol itself. The reasoning there is that it is considered disgraceful to worship broken pieces: *d'zila bah milta l'miflach l'shvarim*. But a *tashmish*, a platform that serves the idol, is different: *lo ichpat lei* — a small chip does not stop a platform from functioning as a platform. The idol's worshippers will continue to use it regardless of the chip, and therefore no *bittul* has taken place.

### Platform Nullification

Ulla responds — *man yahiv lan me-afra d'Rav ve-Shmuel u-mal'inan einan* — a pointed expression meaning: I would sooner fill my eyes with their dust and be blinded than accept their approach as illuminating. He then invokes the opposing ruling: Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish both hold *bimus she-nifgam muttar* — a chipped platform is permitted — and this holds *even* according to the opinion that *ovdim l'shvarim*, that worshippers still regard broken pieces as retaining divine status. The logic runs in the opposite direction from Rav and Shmuel's. Even if a broken idol itself is not automatically *batel* — because *d'keivan d'palcha, zila ba milsa levatulei*, having once worshipped it they are loath to nullify it and will continue worshipping even the broken pieces — a mere platform is different: *shaklei lehai u-maisei bimus achrina* — if the platform has a chip, they discard it and obtain a new stone. A platform is more readily exchanged than an idol. Therefore, even a minor chip constitutes *bittul* for a *tashmish*, unlike for the idol itself.

### Altar Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בִּימוֹס שֶׁנִּפְגַּם — מוּתָּר, מִזְבֵּחַ שֶׁנִּפְגַּם — אָסוּר עַד שֶׁיִּנָּתֵץ רוּבּוֹ. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי בִּימוֹס, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי מִזְבֵּחַ? אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּימוֹס — אֶבֶן אַחַת, מִזְבֵּחַ — אֲבָנִים הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish: A platform that was damaged** is **permitted.** Conversely, **an altar** of idol worship **that was damaged** remains **prohibited until most of it is destroyed. What is considered a platform,** and **what is considered an altar? Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A platform** consists of **one stone; an altar** consists of **many stones.**

A *braisa* confirms Rabbi Yochanan's position and simultaneously draws a distinction: *bimus she-nifgam muttar, mizbeach she-nifgam assur* — a chipped platform is permitted, but a chipped altar remains forbidden. The altar is not *bateil* until *ad she-yinateitz rubo* — until the majority of it has been demolished. The reason: although a *mizbeach* is also a *tashmish avoda zara*, it requires substantially more labor and materials to construct than a simple platform stone. Therefore, worshippers do not discard an altar the moment it sustains minor damage. A small chip does not signal any intention to replace the altar, and consequently it cannot constitute *bittul*. The threshold of *bittul* for a *mizbeach* is therefore much higher than for a *bimus*.

### Definitions

What precisely is the difference between a *bimus* and a *mizbeach*? Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi citing Rabbi Yochanan provides the definition: *bimus even achas* — a *bimus* is a single stone, *mizbeach avanim harbei* — a *mizbeach* is constructed from multiple stones. The distinction is practical and structural: a single stone can be swapped out effortlessly when it sustains damage, whereas an altar built from many stones represents a substantial investment. That investment explains the different thresholds for *bittul*, and the daf concludes at this point.
