# Nedarim 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pre-Marital Vow Annulment

### Daf Overview & Structure

We pick up on 72b, *amud bet*, with a new *mishna*. The *mishna* continues in the theme of the father and groom who together annul vows in partnership, but in doing so it raises — somewhat in passing — a central question that the *gemara* will open up and explore in depth.

It is worth noting that our *perek* in Nedarim follows a pattern common to many *masechtos* and *perakim*: it begins with the more obscure and in some ways more fascinating *halacha* — the intricate details of the partnership between father and groom, who is the *ikar*, how the combination functions, and so on — rather than starting from first principles. A more straightforward pedagogical approach would have first explained how *hafara* works, what the twenty-four-hour period entails, what *hakama* is, and so forth. Instead, all of that gets addressed along the way, as the *gemara* works through the more complex and nuanced cases. This is simply further confirmation that the *gemara* and the *mishnayos* were organized for those already in the conversation — not as Nedarim for beginners.

### Father & Groom Partnership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דֶּרֶךְ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, עַד שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה בִּתּוֹ יוֹצְאָה מֵאֶצְלוֹ, אוֹמֵר לָהּ: כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁנָּדַרְתְּ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתִי — הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין. וְכֵן הַבַּעַל, עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּכָּנֵס לִרְשׁוּתוֹ, אוֹמֵר לָהּ: כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁנָּדַרְתְּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּכָּנְסִי לִרְשׁוּתִי — הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין. שֶׁמִּשֶּׁתִּכָּנֵס לִרְשׁוּתוֹ — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהָפֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **The practice of Torah scholars** is to ensure that a woman about to be married should not be encumbered by any vows. A father, **before his daughter would leave him** through marriage, **would say to her: All vows that you vowed in my house are hereby nullified. And similarly, the husband, before she would enter his jurisdiction,** i.e., while they were still betrothed, **would say to her: All vows that you vowed before you entered my jurisdiction are hereby nullified.** This was necessary **because once she enters his jurisdiction he cannot nullify** the vows she made before that.

The *mishna* at the top of 72b opens with a striking phrase: *derech talmidei chachamim* — it is the way of Torah sages. It is worth pausing on this, as it is unusual for a *mishna* to invoke the category of *talmidei chachamim* so directly. The *mishna* then describes the following practice: before a man's daughter is about to leave his home — and *yotzes* here means *nissu'in*, marriage proper, since *kiddushin* and *erusin* leave her still legally in her father's house — a conscientious father would ensure that any outstanding vows were addressed. Once she becomes *nesu'ah*, the father exits the picture entirely: he can no longer annul, and her husband cannot annul vows that predate the *nissu'in* because they are *kodmim* — they preceded his jurisdiction. The only window for annulling such prior vows is before the *chuppah*.

So the *mishna* tells us: *derech talmidei chachamim*, before his daughter departs his home, a father would say to her, *kol nedarim she-nadart betoch beisi harei hein mefurim* — any vows you have taken in my house are hereby annulled. This is a blanket annulment, reminiscent in structure of *hataras nedarim* on *erev Rosh Hashana*. Clearly, he does not know the specific content of the vows; if he did, the situation would have played out differently, and the urgency of doing it now, before the window closes, would not have the same character. The *mishna* then adds: *vechain haba'al*, and so too the groom, before she enters his domain — since once she is *mekudeshes* his participation is already required — would similarly say: *kol nedarim she-nadart ad shelo tikanesi lirshusi harei hein mufarim* — any vows you may have taken before the *chuppah* are annulled. Because once the *chuppah* occurs and she is *nesu'ah*, he can no longer address vows that preceded his jurisdiction.

### Blanket Annulment Practice

The implication of the *mishna* is immediately apparent: both the father and the groom are issuing blanket annulments of vows they have not heard. The framing could not be clearer — they say "any vows you may have taken," which indicates they are unaware of any specific vow. And crucially, there is no sense of a clock running out that would require them to speak now rather than later. The only urgency is the impending *nissu'in* after which the window closes entirely.

This raises an obvious question rooted in the *pesukim* in *Matos*: the Torah says *min hayom shema'o* — from the day he heard. It sounds as though hearing is a prerequisite for annulment. And one might add: if a husband can issue a standing blanket annulment without hearing, then presumably he could do so every night as a matter of course, and his wife's vows would always be pre-annulled. These are precisely the questions the *gemara* will now explore.

## Hearing Requirement Debate

### Rami Bar Chama's Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: בַּעַל, מַהוּ שֶׁיָּפֵר בְּלֹא שְׁמִיעָה? ״וְשָׁמַע אִישָׁהּ״ דַּוְקָא הוּא, אוֹ לָאו דַּוְקָא הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama asks:** Concerning **a husband, what is** the *halakha* **with regard** to his **nullifying** a vow **without hearing** it? In other words, can a husband state a general nullification of his wife's vows without being aware of any particular vow? When the verse states: **"And her husband hears** it, on the day that he hears it, and holds his peace at her, then her vows shall be ratified" (Numbers 30:8), **is** that referring **specifically** to a situation where he actually heard of a vow, and only then he can nullify it? **Or is** it **not specifically** referring to such a situation, and the mention of hearing is merely because the ordinary situation is that the husband nullifies a vow once he hears it?

*Ba'i Rami Bar Chama* — Rami Bar Chama poses the question: *ba'al mahu sheyafer belo shmiya?* Can a husband — and the same question applies equally to a father, there is no distinction here — annul a vow without having heard it? *V'shama isha gabeyhu* — does the requirement that the husband hear function as the trigger that empowers him to annul? *O lav davka hu* — or is that phrase not to be read literally as a strict requirement?

It is almost striking to take a *pasuk* from the Torah and say *lav davka* — as if to say, the phrase doesn't really mean what it seems to say. But that is precisely what is at stake.

### Lav Davka Interpretation

What *lav davka* must mean here requires some unpacking. It would not be a wholesale dismissal of the phrase as meaningless. Rather, the more precise reading would be: the husband has twenty-four hours from the time he heard. He need not hear in order to perform the act of annulment, but once he has heard, he has only twenty-four hours remaining. Failure to annul within twenty-four hours of hearing is treated as *hakama* — tacit confirmation of the vow — since his silence upon learning of it for an entire day is construed as acquiescence. So *lav davka* would not mean hearing is irrelevant; it would mean that hearing is not a precondition for annulling, but it is the event that starts the twenty-four-hour clock. The *gemara* does not state this explicitly, but it appears to be the necessary *pshat*.

We should not simply say *lav davka* without accounting for the word's role at all. Rather, *b'yom shama'o* informs us of the consequences of hearing — the twenty-four-hour window — without making hearing itself a precondition for the act of annulment.

### Opening Conversation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: דֶּרֶךְ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, עַד שֶׁלֹּא יָצָאת בִּתּוֹ מֵאֶצְלוֹ, אוֹמֵר לָהּ: כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁנָּדַרְתְּ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתִי — הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין, וְהָא לָא שְׁמַע!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Come** and **hear** the mishna: **The practice of Torah scholars** is that a father, **before his daughter would leave him** through marriage, **would say to her: All vows that you vowed in my house are hereby nullified.** Rava points out: **But** the father **did not hear** her vows, so it must be that one can nullify vows without knowledge that they were actually made.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכִי שָׁמַע הוּא דְּמֵיפַר. אִם כֵּן, כִּי לָא שְׁמַע, לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר? הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן: אוֹרְחֵיהּ דְּצוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן לְהַדּוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: The mishna means that the father states a preemptive nullification that **when he** will **hear** a particular vow **is when he nullifies** it. The vow is not actually nullified until he hears it. The Gemara asks: **If so, when he has not** actually **heard** those vows yet, **why** is it necessary **for him to state** preemptively that the vows will be nullified; why not wait until he actually hears the vow? The Gemara answers: **This teaches us** that it is **the practice of a Torah scholar to pursue** such matters, in order to prompt his daughter or his betrothed to inform him of vows she took, which will then be nullified when he hears of them.

*Amar Rava ta shma* — Rava says, come and hear from our *mishna* itself. The *mishna* says *derech talmidei chachamim* — and it is interesting to note that the *mishna* identifies this practice specifically with *talmidei chachamim*, perhaps because knowing that a blanket annulment of unknown vows is valid, and that hearing is not strictly required to perform it, itself requires significant *halachic* awareness. The *mishna* describes the father giving a comprehensive annulment of any vows the daughter may have taken — *ve-ha lo shama?* But he didn't hear them. So how does this work?

The *gemara* responds: *v'chi shama hu demifer* — no, what the *mishna* means is that he is not actually annulling now. Rather, he is opening the conversation with her. By saying "any vows you may have taken are hereby annulled," he is prompting her to disclose whatever vows she may have made, so that he can then perform the actual annulment upon hearing. So the *mishna*'s case involves a father who initiates the discussion precisely in order to get his daughter to articulate her vows — and only then does the formal annulment occur.

This is admittedly a significant reading into the *mishna*, which on its face sounds like a genuine blanket annulment of unspecified vows. The *gemara* then turns to the second half of the *mishna*: *vechain haba'al*, the groom likewise says this before the *nissu'in*. And here too the *gemara* applies the same logic — *hacha nami de-amar lah lechishe'eshma'enah* — he is saying that when he hears, the annulment will take effect. This is how the *gemara* reads the case: the act of annulment can be performed before hearing, but its effect is contingent on hearing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע מִסֵּיפָא: וְכֵן הַבַּעַל, עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּכָּנֵס לִרְשׁוּתוֹ, אוֹמֵר לָהּ. הָכָא נָמֵי, דְּאָמַר לַהּ: ״לְכִי שָׁמַעְנָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear from the latter clause** of the mishna: **And similarly, the husband, before she would enter his jurisdiction,** i.e., while they were still betrothed, **would say to her:** All vows that you vowed before you entered my jurisdiction are hereby nullified. This implies that he can nullify vows without hearing them. The Gemara responds: **Here too,** it means **that he says to her: When I hear** the particular vow, then it will be nullified.

Now this is a genuinely interesting middle position. One might have thought: if hearing is required for the effect, surely the very act of annulment cannot be initiated before that threshold is met — much like the case of *davar shelo ba le'olam*, where one cannot perform a legal act regarding something that does not yet exist. And yet the *gemara* here seems to carve out an intermediate stance: the act can be initiated prior to hearing, but the effect can only take hold once hearing has occurred.

### Father vs. Husband Timing

The Ran raises a pointed question: why is this resolution stated specifically regarding the husband, and not the father? The distinction is significant. For the father, if he performs the act of annulment now and hearing only occurs later, that "later" might well be after the *chuppah* — after which the daughter is entirely outside his domain. Since neither the moment of the act nor the moment of the effect corresponds to a time when he is genuinely empowered to annul, it is difficult to see how the two could combine into a valid *hafara*.

For the husband the situation is different. He performs the act now as an *arus* — he already holds the power to annul in partnership with the father. And when he later hears, he will hear as a *ba'al*, a fully married husband, at which point he has comprehensive authority over her vows. So the act performed at one valid stage and the effect triggered at another valid stage can indeed combine. The father, by contrast, must work within the *gemara*'s first reading: he initiates conversation, she discloses, and he annuls before the *chuppah* while he still can.

## Annulment Before Hearing

### Annulment of Future Vows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ ״כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁתִּדּוֹרִי עַד שֶׁאָבֹא מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי הֲרֵי הֵן קַיָּימִין״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. ״הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין״, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מוּפָר. וְהָא לָא שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another mishna to answer the question (*Nedarim* 75a): **One who says to his wife: All vows that you vow until I arrive from such and such a place are hereby ratified, has not said anything,** i.e., the vows are not ratified. If he says: All vows that you vow until then **are hereby nullified, Rabbi Eliezer says:** They are **nullified.** The Gemara comments: **But he did not** actually **hear** the particular vows, so one can infer from this that he need not hear her vows in order to nullify them.

*Ta shma* — come and hear. The *gemara* now introduces a new *mishna* — which we find at 75a — that takes the question even further. The case: a man says to his wife before departing on a journey, *kol nedarim she-tidori mi-kan ad she-avo mi-makom ploni* — any vows that you make from now until I return from such-and-such a place.

### Non-Existent Vows Debate

These are not vows that exist and have merely not been heard — these are vows that do not yet exist at all. The *mishna* addresses two scenarios. If the husband says *harei hen kayamin* — I am confirming them, I am being *mekayem* — *lo amar klum*, his declaration is null. One cannot strengthen something that does not yet exist; *kiyum* requires the vow to already have some footing before it can be anchored. On this everyone agrees.

However, if he says *harei hen mufarin* — I am annulling them — then Rabbi Eliezer says it works, while the *Chachamim* say it does not.

### Prevention Logic

*Amar Rabbi Eliezer*: why should pre-emptive annulment be less effective than after-the-fact annulment? *Im hefer nedarim she-bau li-khlal issur, lo yafer nedarim she-lo bau li-khlal issur?* If he can annul a vow once it has already entered the realm of prohibition, surely he can act to prevent one from entering that realm altogether. Prevention is easier than cure; an ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure. This is also why the cases are not symmetrical: *kiyum* means strengthening, and you cannot strengthen what is not yet there. But *hafara* means preventing or removing — and surely preventing is even more achievable than curing.

### Establishment Requirement

The *Chachamim* respond from the *pasuk* itself: *ishah yekimenu ve-ishah yefirenu* — her husband shall establish it and her husband shall annul it. *Kol she-ba li-khlal hakam ba li-khlal hafer* — only something that can be subject to *hakama* can be subject to *hafara*. If a vow cannot be established — because it does not yet exist — it cannot be annulled either. This may reflect the mechanics of the annulment process, or it may simply be a *gezeirat ha-katuv*. Rabbi Eliezer's logic is conceptually appealing, but the *Chachamim* hold that the Torah's mechanism of annulment operates only on something already in existence.

One might note the irony: Rabbi Eliezer concedes that *kiyum* of a non-existent vow is impossible — but this is precisely the assumption that undermines his own *kal va-chomer*, since if *kiyum* cannot apply, and *kiyum* and *hafara* are treated as parallel categories by the *Chachamim*, then the same limitation applies to both.

In any case, this debate concerns only whether the act of annulment can precede the vow's existence. Nobody in the *mishna* raises the objection that the husband failed to hear the vow — neither Rabbi Eliezer nor the *Chachamim* flag hearing as a problem. This silence is itself informative.

### Effect Upon Hearing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, דְּאָמַר: ״לְכִי שָׁמַעְנָא״. וּלְמָה לִי מִן הַשְׁתָּא? לְכִי שָׁמַע לֵיפַר לַהּ! קָסָבַר: דִּלְמָא מִטְּרִידְנָא הָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Here too,** one can understand the situation to be **that he says: When I hear** the particular vow, it will be nullified. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need, i.e., why must the husband state his nullification, **from now; let him nullify** them **for her when he** actually **hears** them. The Gemara answers: **He reasons: Perhaps I will be preoccupied at that moment** and will forget to nullify them. He therefore nullifies the vows beforehand, so that the nullification will take effect automatically when he hears them.

*Ve-ha lo shama* — but he didn't hear these vows. This too doesn't seem to trouble either opinion in the *mishna*. The *gemara* once again resolves this: *hacha nami de-amar le-chi-ishmana* — here too, we read it as if he said the annulment would take effect when he hears about the vows. So again: the act is performed now, but the effect is held in abeyance until hearing occurs.

The *gemara* then asks: if that is the case, why does he need to make the declaration before he leaves? *Lechi yishma yeifer lah* — when he gets back and hears about any vow she made, let him annul it then. The answer: *kasvar dilma matreeda ha-hee sha'ata* — he is worried that upon returning from his trip, in the bustle of arriving home, he might be distracted and forget to annul in time. The twenty-four-hour window could expire. So he sets things in motion beforehand to ensure the annulment will take effect automatically once he hears, without relying on his future composure.

The *pshat* of the *mishna* clearly reads as genuine pre-emptive annulment. But the *gemara*, in order to preserve the requirement of hearing, consistently reads all such cases as: the act is permitted before hearing, the effect requires hearing. This is the framework the *gemara* applies throughout this *sugya*.

## Shaliach (Agent) Authority

### Appointed Manager Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָאוֹמֵר לְאַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס ״כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁנּוֹדֶרֶת אִשְׁתִּי מִכָּאן וְעַד שֶׁאָבֹא מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי הָפֵר״, וְהֵפֵר לֵהּ, יָכוֹל יְהוּ מוּפָרִין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישָׁהּ יְקִימֶנּוּ וְאִישָׁהּ יְפֵרֶנּוּ״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: In the case of **one who says to a steward [*apotropos*]** appointed to manage his affairs in his absence: **All vows that my wife vows from now until I arrive from such and such a place** you should **nullify, and** the steward **nullified** the vows **for her,** one **might** have thought that **they would be nullified.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Her husband may ratify it, or her husband may nullify it"** (Numbers 30:14). The repetition of "her husband" teaches that it is the husband alone who may nullify his wife's vows; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.**

*Ta shma* — the *gemara* brings a further case. A man about to leave on a trip says to his *apotropus* — his household manager or appointed agent — *kol nedarim she-tidor ishti mi-kan ad she-avo mi-makom ploni*, hafer lah: any vows my wife makes from now until I return, go ahead and annul them. The manager does so — *yachol yihiyu mufarim?* Could such an annulment be valid?

### Non-Delegable Power

*Talmud lomar isha yekimenu ve-ishah yefirenu* — the verse specifies the husband. The power of *hafara*, says Rabbi Yoshiah, cannot be transferred to an agent. It belongs exclusively to the husband himself.

### Shaliach Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מָצִינוּ בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ שְׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yonatan said to him: We have found everywhere in the Torah** that the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself.** Therefore, a steward can nullify the vows on the husband's behalf.

Rabbi Yonatan objects: *matzinu be-chol ha-Torah kulo shlucho shel adam k'moto* — throughout the entire Torah, an agent stands in the place of the one who appointed him. Why should *hafara* be any different? Why can the husband not empower a *shaliach* to annul on his behalf?

### Hearing vs. Legal Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה לָא קָאָמַר אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דִּגְזֵירַת הַכָּתוּב הוּא ״אִישָׁהּ יְקִימֶנּוּ וְאִישָׁהּ יְפֵרֶנּוּ״, אֲבָל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שְׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ. וְהָא לָא שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out: **And even Rabbi Yoshiya says** that a steward cannot nullify the wife's vows **only because it is a Torah edict,** based upon the words **"her husband may ratify it, or her husband may nullify it"** (Numbers 30:14). **But** according **to everyone,** the principle that the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself** is generally valid. The only objection to the steward nullifying the vows is the Torah edict. The Gemara asks: **But** these vows **were not heard by** the steward? This indicates that not having heard the vows is not an obstacle to nullification.

The *gemara* then notes a deeper puzzle: even if the principle of *shaliach* could in theory apply, there is a conceptual difficulty. The *shaliach* would be the one who hears the vow — but the husband would not have heard it. If hearing is what empowers the husband to annul, then how could his *shaliach*'s hearing translate into the husband's ability to act?

The *gemara*'s answer — and this is spelled out more fully in the Ran and in Tosafot — is that hearing is not a legal act that can be delegated. One can transfer a legal power to a *shaliach*; one cannot make someone else do one's hearing. Hearing is circumstances, not agency. The circumstances of hearing create the empowerment; but those circumstances must attach to the person himself. As Tosafot states concisely: *inyan shmiah eino na'aseh shaliach* — the matter of hearing cannot be made into an act of agency.

### Shaliach Hears & Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: לְכִי שָׁמַעְנָא מֵיפַר לַהּ. לְכִי שָׁמַע לֵיפַר לַהּ? הוּא סָבַר: דִּלְמָא מִטְּרִידְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion as well: **Here too,** it is a case **in which** the husband **says to** the steward: **When I hear** the vow, then it **will be nullified for her.** The Gemara asks: **Let him nullify** the vows **for her when he** actually **hears** them. Why do so earlier? The Gemara answers: **He reasons: Perhaps I will be preoccupied** at that moment and will forget to nullify them. The question pertaining to nullification of vows without hearing them is left unresolved.

Given all of this, the *gemara* concludes: even Rabbi Yoshiah, who holds that the power of *hafara* cannot be transferred, acknowledges that the reason is specifically *gezeirat ha-katuv* — the verse *isha yekimenu ve-ishah yefirenu* specifies the husband. Were it not for that verse, everyone would agree the power could in principle be transferred. *Aval d'chulei alma shlucho shel adam k'moto* — the baseline principle of *shaliach* would apply.

The *gemara* then works out how this case would function if the *shaliach* mechanism were valid: *de-amar lah le-chi shamana meifer lah* — the husband says to the *shaliach*, when you hear, annul it, and it will take effect when I hear. *Savar dilma matridna* — the husband anticipates being distracted upon his return, so he sets the *shaliach* in motion ahead of time. The *shaliach* performs the act upon hearing; the effect activates once the husband himself hears. In this way the *gemara* accommodates the case within its consistent framework: the act of annulment can precede hearing, but the effect requires the husband personally to have heard.

This entire discussion reinforces once more that the *pshat* of the *mishna* and *braita* is that hearing is not required to perform *hafara*. The *gemara*'s repeated accommodation — reading every case as a deferred effect contingent on future hearing — is the only mechanism by which it preserves any residual role for *b'yom shama'o* at all.

## Deaf Person Annulment

### Cheresh Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: חֵרֵשׁ, מַהוּ שֶׁיָּפֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ? אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר בַּעַל מֵיפֵר בְּלֹא שְׁמִיעָה — מִשּׁוּם דְּבַר מִישְׁמַע הוּא, אֲבָל חֵרֵשׁ דְּלָאו בַּר מִישְׁמַע הוּא, הַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rami bar Ḥama asks:** With regard to **a deaf man, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to his nullifying** vows **for his wife? If you say** that **a husband** who is not deaf **can nullify** a vow **without hearing** it, then perhaps this is **because he is capable of hearing. But** with regard to **a deaf man, who is not capable of hearing,** perhaps **this is** an application of the principle derived from the statement **of Rabbi Zeira.**

*Ba'ei Rami Bar Chama* — Rami Bar Chama now poses a second question: *cheresh mahu sheyafer le'ishto?* Can a deaf person annul the vows of his wife? The question presupposes the conclusion of the previous discussion: if we have established — or are at least working with the assumption — that one need not actually hear a vow in order to annul it, then a deaf person's disability does not categorically prevent him from performing *hafara*. So the question must be sharper than that.

### Category of Hearer

*Im timtzei lomar ba'al meifer belo shemia* — if we say a hearing husband can annul without actually having heard a particular vow, perhaps the reason is *mishum debar shemia hu* — because he at least belongs to the category of one capable of hearing. *Aval cheresh de-lav bar shemia hu* — a deaf person, however, is not in that category at all. Perhaps that categorical exclusion disqualifies him from *hafara* altogether.

### Rabbi Zeira's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לְבִילָּה — אֵין בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לְבִילָּה — בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Zeira said:** For **any** amount of flour **suitable for mingling** with oil in a meal-offering, **mingling is not indispensable for it.** Even though it is a mitzva to mingle the flour and oil *ab initio*, if they were not mingled, the meal-offering is still valid. **But** for **any** amount of flour **not suitable for mingling, mingling is indispensable for it,** and such a meal-offering is invalid. The principle is: *Ab initio* requirements prevent the fulfillment of a mitzva in situations where they are not merely absent but impossible. In this case, the deaf man does not merely not hear the vow, it is impossible for him to do so.

This question connects to a broader principle articulated by Rabbi Zeira: *kol ha-ra'ui le-vila ein bila me'akevet bo, ve-chol she-eino ra'ui le-vila bila me'akevet bo* — whatever is fit to be mixed, the mixing is not actually indispensable; but if it is not fit to be mixed, then the mixing is determinative. The reference is to a *mincha* offering: the Torah requires the flour and oil to be mixed (*bila*), yet if one failed to mix them in a case where mixing was possible, the offering remains valid. But if the offering is of such a size that mixing is physically impossible — once the quantity exceeds what can be practically mixed — then its lack of fitness for *bila* disqualifies it entirely.

Tosafot flags the broader question this principle raises: how does one know when the *halacha* requires merely the potential for an act, and when it requires the act itself? The answer is not always clear. But the *gemara* here applies Rabbi Zeira's principle to suggest: perhaps *b'yom shama'o* is not eliminated entirely by our conclusion that hearing need not actually occur — perhaps it is reduced to the requirement of being in the *category* of a hearer. A hearing person who happens not to have heard a specific vow retains that potential; a deaf person has no such potential at all. *O dilma ve-shama isha lav me'akev* — or perhaps *ve-shama isha* plays no role whatsoever, not even as a categorical requirement.

### Braita Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא: ״וְשָׁמַע אִישָׁהּ״ לָא מְעַכֵּב. אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע ״וְשָׁמַע אִישָׁהּ״ — פְּרָט לְאֵשֶׁת חֵרֵשׁ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** the phrase **"and her husband hears it"** (Numbers 30:8) **does not** mean that hearing **is indispensable** to the nullification of a vow, so that even a deaf man can nullify his wife's vows. **Rava said: Come** and **hear** a *baraita* interpreting that verse: **"And her husband hears it";** this **excludes the wife of a deaf man.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from** this *baraita* that a deaf man cannot nullify his wife's vows.

*Amar Rava ta shma* — Rava resolves the question by citing a *braita*. The *braita* expounds *ve-shama isha* — her husband shall hear — and adds: *prat le-eshet cheresh*, to exclude the wife of a deaf person. The exclusion is explicit. A deaf man cannot annul his wife's vows.

From here, many *rishonim* draw the further implication: since the *braita* finds it necessary to explicitly exclude the case of the *cheresh*, it implies that merely not having heard a particular vow is insufficient to constitute a problem. The only disqualifying circumstance is being fully outside the category of hearing. This reinforces the position — consistent with everything seen in this *sugya* — that a hearing person need not actually have heard the vow in order to perform *hafara*. As this is a striking conclusion given the Torah's explicit language of *b'yom shama'o*, it bears emphasis: according to most *rishonim* and *poskim*, the *pshat* of the *sugya* is that actual hearing of the specific vow is not required for annulment to be valid.

## Multiple Wives Vows

### Simultaneous Annulment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בַּעַל, מַהוּ שֶׁיָּפֵר לִשְׁתֵּי נָשָׁיו בְּבַת אַחַת? ״אוֹתָהּ״ דַּוְקָא, אוֹ לָאו דַּוְקָא?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Concerning **a husband, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to nullifying** vows **for his two wives simultaneously?** Do the words "but if her husband disallows **her** on the day that he hears it, and he nullifies her vow which is upon her" (Numbers 30:9), stated in the singular, refer **specifically** to one wife? **Or,** perhaps it does **not** refer **specifically** to one wife, and a husband can nullify the vows of more than one wife simultaneously.

*Iba'ya lehu* — a further question is raised: *ba'al mahu sheyafer li-sh'tei nashim be-vat achat?* Can a husband annul the vows of two of his wives simultaneously? He has vows from two wives on the same day and declares *mufar lachem* — it is annulled for both of you. Is that valid?

The question parallels the earlier inquiry into *ve-shama isha*: just as we asked whether *isha* — singular — is *davka*, implying the husband must address one wife at a time, so too we might ask about *yafer otah* — the annulment is of *her* vow. One might note that in the *pesukim* in *Matos*, the verb of *hafara* appears in the context of *kol nedareha ve-chol issar asher asra al nafsha* — her vows in their entirety — and the *hafara* there is on the *neder*, not on the person. The word *otah* in the sense of disallowing her personally (*yani otah*) is distinct from the *hafara* of the vow. So the textual basis for requiring individual treatment is not straightforwardly clear.

### Sotah Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין מַשְׁקִין שְׁתֵּי סוֹטוֹת כְּאַחַת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלִּבָּהּ גַּס בַּחֲבֶירְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: In the *sota* ritual, performed by women suspected by their husbands of having committed adultery, **two *sota* women are not given to drink** the bitter waters **as one.** This is **because the heart** of each becomes **emboldened [*gas*] in the** presence of **the other** woman, and if one is guilty she will lack the humility to confess.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִן הַשֵּׁם הוּא זֶה, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְהִשְׁקָהּ״ — אוֹתָהּ, לְבַדָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: This is not for** that **reason [*lo min hashem hu zeh*], but because it is stated: "And he shall make her drink"** (Numbers 5:27), which indicates **her by herself.** Similarly, the words "disallows her" (Numbers 30:9) should be read as referring specifically to a single woman, indicating that a man cannot nullify the vows of two wives simultaneously.

*Amar Rav Aina ta shma* — come and hear from the laws of *sotah*. *Ein mashkin sh'tei sotos ke'achat* — one does not administer the *sotah* waters to two suspected wives at the same time. Even if a man suspects both his wives of infidelity, the *sotah* process must be conducted separately for each. The reason given by the *tanna kama* is *mipnei she-liba gas ba-chaverta* — because one woman's heart becomes emboldened by her companion. Seeing her fellow undergo the drinking process without immediate consequence, she may not take the matter seriously, failing to appreciate that her companion may be innocent while she is guilty.

### Individual Requirement

Rabbi Judah disagrees with this rationale and offers instead a textual basis: *ela mishum she-ne'emar ve-hishka otah levadah* — because the verse says "and he shall make her drink *alone*," *levadah*, implying individual treatment is mandated by the *pasuk* itself. There is some discussion among the *rishonim* as to whether the *tanna kama* disputes Rabbi Judah's *derashah* entirely, or whether he agrees with the *pasuk* but is merely supplying the underlying reason behind it — in the manner of Rabbi Shimon who *doresh ta'ama di-kra*. The *gemara*'s conclusion on the question of simultaneous annulment for two wives appears to be that it must be done individually, though the precise resolution remains somewhat open.

## Bogeret & Widow Cases

### Ready-for-Marriage Cases

The *mishna* now addresses a distinct set of cases: a *bogeret* — a woman who has reached full maturity — who was betrothed (*mekudeshes*) and is now in the period that would naturally precede *nissu'in*, and similarly a widow who is betrothed for a second marriage. These are women who are fully ready to be married but have not yet entered the *chuppah*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

*Rabbi Eliezer omer*: since the *Sages* have ruled that the husband is now obligated in her *mezonos* — her financial support — he may annul her vows unilaterally, without the father's partnership, as though she were already *nesu'ah*.

This is a striking ruling. *De'oraisa* she is not yet *nesu'ah*, and the annulment would be *le-kula* — a leniency. How can the husband be granted unilateral authority that normally requires full *nissu'in*?

### Chachamim Position

*Chachamim omrim*: the husband cannot perform full *hafara* until she has actually entered his domain — until there is a real *chuppah* and genuine *nissu'in*.

### Rava's Comparison

*Amar Rava*: Rabbi Eliezer and the *mishna rishona* — the earlier teaching cited in Ketubot — express the same position. As the *mishna* in Ketubot teaches: *notnin li-vtula yod bet chodesh le-parnes atzma* — a virgin bride is given twelve months to prepare her trousseau and arrange for the wedding. *Higia yod bet chodesh* and the husband has not yet married her — his financial obligations kick in. *Ocheles mi-shelo* — he bears her food expenses.

### Truma Parallel

And further: *ocheles bi-truma* — if the husband is a *kohen*, she may even eat *truma*, despite the fact that ordinarily a woman must be genuinely *nesu'ah* to a *kohen* to be permitted *truma*. So there is a ritual-legal parallel: just as once twelve months have elapsed she is treated as *nesu'ah* for the purpose of eating *truma*, Rabbi Eliezer holds that she is treated as *nesu'ah* for the purpose of *hafara* as well.

By contrast, the *mishna* in Ketubot distinguishes the case of *yibum* — the *yavam*, the brother-in-law awaiting *yibum*. Even if six months passed under the deceased husband and six more under the *yavam* — or all twelve months in either configuration — they do not combine, and she may not eat *truma* by virtue of the *yavam*'s obligation. The later *beit din*, the *mishna rishona*'s successor ruling, went further and said: *ein ha-isha ocheles bi-truma ad she-tikanes la-chuppah* — she does not eat *truma* until she actually enters the *chuppah*. So even the *truma* leniency was subsequently restricted, reflecting the debate about how far we are prepared to treat her as *nesu'ah* for *issur* purposes before she formally is.

## Truma vs. Nedarim

### Rabbanic vs. Torah

*Amar lei Abaye* — Abaye challenges Rava's equation. Perhaps, says Abaye, the parallel between Rabbi Eliezer and the *mishna rishona* is not so simple. *Ad kan lo ashmian mishna rishona ela meichal bi-truma d'rabbanan* — the *mishna rishona* permitted her to eat *truma*, but *truma* in this context is a *rabbinic* matter. *Aval nedarim d'oraisa eima lo* — but *nedarim* is Torah law, and one might say Rabbi Eliezer would never have extended his position to allow a Torah-level *hafara* before actual *nissu'in*.

### Truma Sources

What does Abaye mean when he says *truma* here is *de-rabbanan*? This requires some background. *Mideoraita*, why may the wife of a *kohen* eat *truma* at all? Two potential *pesukim* are relevant. One is *anshei beiso heim yochlu be-lachmo* — the people of his household eat from his bread — pointing toward a *nesu'ah*, a woman established in his home. The other is *kinyan kaspo hu yochal be-lachmo* — that which he has acquired with his money, referring to *kiddushin* — pointing toward an *arusa*, a betrothed woman. The Yerushalmi understands the basis as *beiso*, requiring full *nissu'in* even *de'oraita*. The Bavli understands it as *kinyan kaspo*, so that *de'oraita* even an *arusa* may eat *truma*.

### Nesu'ah Requirement

If so, why did the *Sages* require *nissu'in* before she may eat *truma*? The reason is practical: she is still living in her father's house, among family members who are not *kohanim*, and would likely share the *truma* with them — which is forbidden. The *rabbinic* restriction is therefore a protective measure. Since her inability to eat *truma* before *nissu'in* is only *de-rabbanan*, it is not so significant to say: once twelve months have elapsed and it is truly time for the *nissu'in*, we will treat her as *nesu'ah* and allow her to eat. *Mah she-ein kein nedarim* — but for *nedarim*, which are *de'oraita*, how could the husband annul vows if she is not formally his *nesu'ah*? That is a much more serious claim, and Abaye holds that the parallel to *truma* therefore does not carry.

## Vows with Spousal Consent

### Spousal Presumption

The *gemara* introduces the position of Rav Pinchas in the name of Rava: *kol ha-noderet al da'as ba'alah hi noderet* — any woman who takes a vow does so with the presumption that her husband will agree. This is the conceptual key to Rabbi Eliezer's position. The woman's vow is not a unilateral declaration issued in disregard of her husband; it is made against the backdrop of her relationship with him, implicitly conditional on his acceptance. Since that implicit conditionality is built into the vow itself, the husband's annulment does not need to await formal *nissu'in* in the same way as a standard *hafara* would — because the vow's very force depends on his concurrence.

### Rabbi Eliezer's Logic

This explains why Rabbi Eliezer holds that once twelve months have elapsed and the husband is already obligated in her maintenance, the relationship has already reached a stage where her psychological orientation is effectively that of a *nesu'ah*. She is already thinking about him as her husband, and her vows are already being made *al da'at* him. The formal *nissu'in* has not yet occurred, but the relevant dynamic — the one the Torah was addressing when it invested the husband with the power of *hafara* — is already present. This is not merely a financial or technical marker; it reflects the actual state of the relationship.

### Implicit Condition

The Ran is careful to note, however, that one should not conclude from this that all formal requirements of *hafara* are suspended in such cases. The principle that a woman vows *al da'as ba'alah* does not mean that the husband's silent non-objection constitutes *hafara*, or that any expression of reluctance will do. The Ran writes explicitly: even granting that she vows on the presumption of his agreement, the husband must still perform a formal act of *hafara* using the language designated by the Torah. This is *gezeirat ha-katuv* — the Torah prescribed a formal mechanism with specific rules: a twenty-four-hour window, particular language, and so on. *Al da'as* explains the *reason* the Torah invested the husband with this power; it does not dissolve the formal requirements of how that power must be exercised.

One might think, for example, that if someone vows explicitly *al da'as* a specific individual — say, "I am taking this vow subject to So-and-so's approval" — that person could annul the vow without formal *hafara* language, simply by withholding consent. The Ran firmly rejects this: *vazu hi shegagas hora'ah* — this would be a genuine error in *psak*. The mechanism of *hafara* is formal and its rules are fixed; *al da'as* informs the underlying rationale but does not replace the formal act.

### Husband Category Expansion

The Ran develops the point further in a direction that is conceptually illuminating. If *al da'as ba'alah* is the *reason* the Torah gave the husband power over his wife's vows, then we are essentially engaged in *darshinan ta'ama di-kra* — deriving the law from the reasoning behind the *pasuk*. And if so, when the Torah says *ba'al* — husband — we need to ask: what does the Torah mean by husband in this context? The Torah means the person standing in that relationship of presumptive agreement with her vows.

Once we frame it this way, Rabbi Eliezer's extension becomes intelligible. An *arus* who has been in a committed relationship with his betrothed for twelve months, who is already providing her with financial support, and in whose eyes she is already conducting herself as though married — such a person fits the Torah's category of *ba'al* for purposes of *hafara*, even though the formal *chuppah* has not yet occurred. The Torah's category of "husband" is not strictly limited to post-*chuppah* status; it encompasses whoever stands in the relevant relationship. At the twelve-month mark, with the *arus* already obligated in *mezonos*, the relationship has effectively reached that threshold.

This is not an unlimited expansion. The Ran is explicit: it would not extend to a stranger, a neighbor, or even a close friend, regardless of how much the woman trusted him or made her vows with him in mind. It extends only to someone who already fits within some recognizable form of the *ba'al* category — an *arus* at an advanced stage of betrothal. For such a person, the Torah's language of *ba'al* already includes him, given the reasoning that underlies the entire institution of *hafarat nedarim*.
