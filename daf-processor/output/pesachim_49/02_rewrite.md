# Pesachim 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arba'asar b'Shabbat

### Intro: 14th on Shabbat

The third *perek* draws toward its close, and we open daf 49a with the *mishnah* addressing *arba'asar she'chal lihyot b'Shabbat* — the 14th of Nisan falling on *Shabbat*. This is a recognized subspecialty within *hilchot Pesach*, significant enough that entire *sefarim* have been written on *arba'asar she'chal lihyot b'Shabbat* and *erev Pesach she'chal lihyot b'erev Shabbat*. The core difficulty is layered: you cannot perform *bi'ur* on *erev Pesach* itself in the usual way, and there are additional questions the *mishnah* does not address — how to fulfill *shalosh seudot*, how to handle the prohibition against eating *matzah* on *erev Pesach*, and whether one may make *Hamotzi* on *matzah ashirah*. These are questions that stand, as the expression goes, *b'rumo shel olam*. The *mishnah* addresses what it addresses, and the *Gemara*'s treatment is worth examining closely.

### Rabbi Meir: Erev Shabbat

*Arba'asar she'chal lihyot b'Shabbat* — if the 14th falls on *Shabbat*, *m'va'arin et hakol milifnei haShabbat*, we destroy everything the day before *Shabbat*. These are the words of Rabbi Meir. The concern motivating his position is real: one cannot be certain an opportunity to destroy the *chametz* properly will present itself on *Shabbat*. The complication is that some of what must be destroyed may be *terumah*, which ordinarily could be consumed. Destroying *terumah* before absolutely necessary involves a genuine waste, yet Rabbi Meir holds that the concern about *chametz* overrides. It is also worth noting that Rabbi Yehudah, who holds *bi'ur chametz* must be specifically through burning — *sereifah* — would presumably agree with Rabbi Meir's conclusion, since waiting until *Shabbat* forecloses the option of *sereifah* altogether. This raises an interesting question: who are the *chachamim* who disagree with Rabbi Meir? It cannot be Rabbi Yehudah, precisely because of the *sereifah* requirement. Rabbi Meir's insistence on doing everything *erev Shabbat* is thus particularly pointed.

### Chachamim: Bizmano

The *chachamim* say *bizmano* — wait and do everything on the 14th, on *Shabbat* itself. The assumption is that one will find some way to handle it, some person available to consume the *terumah*, some arrangement. The difficulty, of course, is that waiting until *Shabbat* both risks *chilul Shabbat* and makes destruction more difficult — one cannot burn on *Shabbat*, and more indirect means of destruction are harder to arrange than simply throwing *chametz* into a fire.

### R. Eliezer b. Tzadok

Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok offers a differentiated position: *terumah milifnei haShabbat v'chullin bizmana* — destroy the *terumah* before *Shabbat*, but the *chullin* on *Shabbat* itself. This is initially counterintuitive. One might expect the reverse — surely *terumah* is the more precious item we wish to preserve as long as possible. But the logic is the opposite: *terumah* can only be eaten by a *kohen*, so the pool of people available to consume it before it must be destroyed is small. *Chullin*, by contrast, can be eaten by anyone, so there is far greater confidence that the *chametz chullin* can be handled on *Shabbat* itself — whether by eating or by some other means. *Terumah* presents the greater practical difficulty, and therefore should be dealt with *erev Shabbat*.

One might add a qualification: the calculus would shift depending on one's circumstances. If one is a *kohen* with many *kohanim* in the household, or has relatively little *terumah* and much *chullin*, the picture changes accordingly — *lo kol anashim shavot*.

### Yavneh Precedent

A *tanna* taught in support of Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok's position: *pa'am achat shabat Abba b'Yavneh* — once my father spent *Shabbat* in Yavneh, and that *Shabbat* happened to coincide with *erev Pesach*, the 14th falling on *Shabbat*. Yavneh was at that time the seat of the *nasi*. *Zonin*, an officer of Rabban Gamliel, came and made an announcement throughout the city: *higia et l'va'er et hachametz* — the time to destroy the *chametz* has arrived. The son relates: *v'halachti achar Abba u'viarnu et hachametz* — I followed my father and we destroyed the *chametz*.

The phrase *biarnu et hachametz* is admittedly a bit strange in a *Shabbat* context, since it conjures the image of burning, which one cannot do on *Shabbat*. Perhaps the more accurate way to have told the story would have been *v'achalnu et hachametz*. The *mishnah* itself uses the verb *mevaarim* even when describing what happens on *Shabbat*, and presumably *mevaarim b'Shabbat* in that context means consuming it. The precedent is thus cited to support Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok's view that *chullin bizmanan* — destroying the *chullin* on *Shabbat* itself — is the correct approach, though one would have hoped for greater specificity in how that destruction was accomplished.

## Holech Lishchot

### Multiple Mitzvot Crisis

The next *mishnah* shifts to a different scenario: *haholech lishchot et hapesach* — a person is on his way to perform *shechitah* of the *korban Pesach* on the 14th. The *mishnah* compounds the picture: *u'lamol et bno* — perhaps his son's *bris milah* is also today; *v'le'echol seudat eirusin b'veit chamiv* — or he is going to his father-in-law's house for a *seudat eirusin*. Some context is needed here: *eirusin* in the Talmudic sense means *kiddushin* — one is legally married but has not yet entered into *nissuin* and the couple is not yet living together. The post-*kiddushin* celebration at the father-in-law's house was a recognized practice with legal standing in other areas of *halachah*.

A brief digression: while *ein mearvin simcha b'simcha* applies to *nissuin* on *Chol HaMoed*, *kiddushin* is permitted on *Chol HaMoed*, partly because *shema yikadmenu acher* — one fears another may come and take her — and partly because *kiddushin* technically does not yet carry the quality of lived *simcha* that *sheva brachot* celebrate. The *seudas eirusin* at the father-in-law's house, however, was treated as carrying a special character of *simcha* and *mitzvah* in its own right.

Now comes the complication: *v'nizkar she-yesh lo chametz b'toch beito* — he suddenly remembers he has *chametz* in his house that he forgot to destroy. What should he do?

### Bittul: Last Resort

If he can return and destroy the *chametz* and still return to perform his *mitzvah* — *lachzor u'lvaer v'lachzor l'mitzvato* — then *yachzor v'yvaer*, he goes back and destroys it. If not, *mevatlo b'libo* — he nullifies it in his heart, performing *bittul*.

This is a significant moment in the *mishnah*: for anyone who has been following since the beginning of the *perek*, this is the first appearance of *bittul chametz* in the *mishnah*. Throughout the earlier *mishnayot*, the *mishnah* discusses only burning and physical destruction of *chametz*. *Bittul* appears here, at the close of the third *perek*, as a last resort in extenuating circumstances.

### Osek b'Mitzvah

This connects to a broader principle worth examining: *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah*. One might ask how that principle applies here. The complication is that this is not simply a conflict between two *mitzvot*, where we resolve that one who is engaged in a *mitzvah* is exempt from another. Here, owning *chametz* involves a *lav* — the prohibitions of *bal yira'eh uval yimatzei* — and a *lo ta'aseh* carries more serious implications than simply not performing a positive commandment. The *mishnah* therefore frames the question as: can you return and still do your *mitzvah*, or not? If you can, go back. If you cannot, *bittul* is available.

This context illuminates the broader development of *bittul* as a halachic tool. The first *Rashi* in the *masechet* explains that *bedikat chametz* is performed to avoid violating *bal yira'eh u'val yimatzei*. Tosafot objects: the *Rav* rules that one who performs *bedikah* must also do *bittul*, and if *bittul* is effective, how can the *bedikah* alone be serving the function of preventing the violation? Tosafot's question, however, reflects the *amora'ic* period. In the era of the *mishnah*, *bittul* was not routinely practiced — precisely because allowing it universally would turn the whole enterprise into a farce. One could simply declare mental nullification and never actually rid oneself of *chametz*. So *chazal* in the *mishnaic* period permitted *bittul* only in exceptional circumstances, while the standard obligation was to perform *bedikah* and *bi'ur*. The rule *habodek tzarich sheyivatel* — that one who searches must also nullify — was introduced in the *amora'ic* period, adding *bittul* as a backup measure even for one who performs a thorough search, in case something was missed. That later development then raised Tosafot's question, but the historical progression clarifies why the *mishnah* presents *bittul* as the exceptional last resort rather than the primary tool.

### Pikuach Nefesh

The *mishnah* then considers a different kind of situation: *lehatzil min hanochri, umin hanahar, umin halistin, umin hadeleikah, umin hamapolet* — one is going not to perform a standard *mitzvah* but to save lives: people are being attacked by hostile forces, a river threatens to flood, bandits are present, a fire is spreading, or a building has collapsed. In such cases, *yevatel belibo* — simply do *bittul* immediately, without any calculation about whether one might return in time. There is no *cheshbon* about whether the fire might not have spread too far, or whether the river will have risen too much. When lives are at stake, one does not weigh the *mitzvah* of *bi'ur* against the urgent need for rescue. Act immediately on the life-threatening situation, and rely on *bittul*.

### Reshut: Must Return

The final case in this sequence inverts the logic entirely: *v'lishbot shevitat hareshut* — one is traveling not to perform a *mitzvah* at all, but simply to spend *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* somewhere for discretionary reasons. Rashi understands this as placing an *eruv techumin*; Tosafot reads it more simply as going to spend *Yom Tov* with family — at one's parents' home, or visiting one's children. One is already on the road, already packed and gone, and one suddenly remembers: *bi'ur chametz* was not done. In that case, *yachzor miyad* — turn around immediately and go back. There is no calculation about whether you can still make it to your destination in time. Since what you were doing is only a *reshut*, the obligation of *bi'ur chametz* takes full precedence. Even *bittul*, which is technically available, is not sufficient to excuse you from returning when what you were pursuing was discretionary.

This is a striking feature of the *mishnah*: it demonstrates how seriously the physical destruction of *chametz* was demanded at a time when *bittul* was viewed as a true last resort. The obligation to actually perform *bi'ur* was pressed even at real personal cost, set aside only when a *mitzvah* or danger to life made return genuinely impossible.

## Yetza mi-Yerushalayim

### Meat Spoilage Case

The *mishnah* continues: *mi she-yatza mi-Yerushalayim v'nizkar she-yesh b'yado basar kodesh* — someone who left Jerusalem and realized he was still carrying meat from *shelamim* that had not been consumed in time. *Kodshim* that leave Jerusalem become *pasul* and must be destroyed. The question is: must one go back to Jerusalem to burn it there, or may one burn it wherever one finds oneself?

### Tzofim Boundary

*Im avar Tzofim* — if one has passed Tzofim, one burns it *bimkomo*, wherever one stands. Rashi understands *Tzofim* as a proper place-name. Tosafot, however, understands it as a descriptive term: any point outside Jerusalem from which Jerusalem is no longer within line of sight. According to Tosafot, the operative moment is when one can no longer see Jerusalem. If one has not yet passed that boundary — if one is still within line of sight — one must turn back.

### Line of Sight Rule

If one is still within the *Tzofim* boundary — still within line of sight of Jerusalem — *chozer v'sorfo lifnei ha-birah* — one returns and burns the meat in the presence of the *birah*, the royal court, presumably on or near the *Beit HaMikdash*, using wood dedicated to the *ma'arachah*, the altar's woodpile.

Tosafot raises an important difficulty here. Rashi, citing an earlier *Gemara*, notes that *kodesh she-psulah b'kodesh teunah sereifah* — consecrated items that become *pasul* must be burned in a sacred place. If so, how could the *mishnah* ever permit burning the meat outside Jerusalem? At least for *chametz* there is a backup — *bittul* — but if there is a genuine obligation to burn the *kodesh* within the *Beit HaMikdash*, how can we release someone from that obligation simply because of the distance?

Tosafot resolves this by distinguishing categories: the requirement to burn *kodesh* within the *Beit HaMikdash* applies specifically to *kodshei kodoshim*, the most sacred class of offerings. *Shelamim* are *kodshim kalim* — offerings of lesser sanctity, which may be eaten by any Jew throughout Jerusalem. *Kodshim kalim* that are taken outside Jerusalem do not technically carry the same requirement to be burned within the *Beit HaMikdash*. Therefore, *chazal* instituted the practice of returning to burn them near the *Beit HaMikdash* so as not to generate confusion, but when the burden of returning becomes excessive, they were lenient.

### Size Thresholds

For how large a quantity must one return — whether to destroy *chametz* or to return meat that has become *pasul*? Rabbi Meir says *zeh v'zeh k'beitzah* — both require returning if the amount is at least a *beitzah* (an egg's bulk). Rabbi Yehudah says *zeh v'zeh k'zayit* — both require returning for an olive's bulk. The *chachamim* distinguish: *basar kodesh b'khazayit v'chametz k'beitzah* — for the sacred meat the threshold is a *zayit*, but for *chametz* it is a *beitzah*.

The *Gemara* explains the positions of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah but does not explain the *chachamim*. Tosafot supplies the reasoning: *chametz* has the backup mechanism of *bittul*, and because of that backup option, the *chachamim* set the threshold at the more lenient *beitzah* rather than the *zayit*. For *basar kodesh*, no comparable backup exists, so the stricter *zayit* threshold applies.

One sharp observation deserves mention: in the *chametz* case, when one is leaving for a purely discretionary purpose (*reshut*), one is always required to return regardless of how far one has gone. Yet in the *basar kodesh* case — where one is also presumably leaving for a *reshut* — the *mishnah* allows leniency depending on how burdensome the return would be. The apparent asymmetry is striking. One might have expected the *chametz* case, given the availability of *bittul*, to be treated more leniently — and yet it is precisely there that the *mishnah* demands return even when what one was doing was purely discretionary. The resolution may be that the two cases operate by different considerations, though this question deserves further analysis.

## Marriage Hierarchy

### Seudas Eirusin Debate

The *Gemara* now raises a difficulty against the *mishnah*'s classification of the *seudat eirusin* as a *mitzvah*. A *baraita* is cited: *haholech le'echol seudat eirusin b'veit chamiv v'lishbot shevitat hareshut* — one who goes to eat the *seudat eirusin* at his father-in-law's house, or who travels merely for discretionary purposes — in both cases *yachzor miyad*, one must turn back immediately. This *baraita* groups the *seudat eirusin* together with *reshut* — apparently treating it as not a *mitzvah* at all. This stands in apparent contradiction to the *mishnah*, which presents the *seudat eirusin* as a *mitzvah* that affects whether one must or need not interrupt to return.

### First vs Second Meal

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the contradiction: it is a *machlokes* between Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Yosei. A *baraita* teaches — *seudat eirusin reshut divrei Rabbi Yehudah*: Rabbi Yehudah holds it is purely discretionary. *Rabbi Yosei omer mitzvah*: Rabbi Yosei holds it is a *mitzvah*. This is a somewhat curious debate — in what precise sense is such a celebration a *mitzvah*? Presumably in the *rabbinic* sense, like many institutions that *chazal* elevated and formalized.

Rav Chisda sharpens the picture: the debate between Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Yosei is specifically about the *seuda shniya* — the second meal — which involved the sending of *sivlonot*, gifts, to the father-in-law's house, followed by a second celebratory meal in which those gifts were consumed. *Aval b'seuda rishona divrei hakol mitzvah* — but regarding the first meal, everyone agrees it is a *mitzvah*. The *mishnah* refers to the first meal; the *baraita* refers to the second. And even holding by Rabbi Yehudah, one can resolve the apparent contradiction: the *mishnah* speaks of the first meal (*mitzvah*), the *baraita* of the second (*reshut*).

A *baraita* is then cited in direct support of Rav Chisda's clarification: *amar Rabbi Yehudah ani lo shamati ela seudat eirusin* — I have only heard of the first *seudat eirusin* as having this status, *aval lo sivlonot* — but not the *sivlonot* meal. Rabbi Yosei disagrees: *ani shamati seudat eirusin v'sivlonot* — I heard that both carry the status of *seudat mitzvah*.

### Talmid Chacham Restricti…

The discussion of *seudat eirusin* transitions into a broader teaching. A *baraita* cites Rabbi Shimon: *kol seudah she-einah shel mitzvah, ein talmid chacham rashai l'henot mimena* — any meal that is not a *seudat mitzvah*, a *talmid chacham* may not derive pleasure from it. The scope of this prohibition requires clarification. Presumably it does not apply to ordinary meals shared with friends or to the ordinary rhythms of daily life. It seems to address more specifically meals to which the *talmid chacham* is invited by virtue of his status, or grand celebrations in which his presence lends a certain cachet. Two concerns may be at play: the danger of personal indulgence in physical pleasure, and the more specific concern about the *talmid chacham* allowing his Torah standing to function as social currency — a kind of *kardom lachpor bah*, leveraging one's Torah status for personal benefit. On the other hand, there are *Gemarot* that speak strongly about the responsibility of wealthy people to support *talmidei chachamim*, so the concern here seems to be about something more than simple hospitality.

The *Gemara* asks: *kegon mai* — what would be a concrete example of a meal that is *not* a *seudat mitzvah* in this sense? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *kegon bat kohen le-Yisrael u'vat talmid chacham le-am ha'aretz* — for example, the wedding feast of a *bat kohen* marrying a *Yisrael*, or the daughter of a *talmid chacham* marrying an *am ha'aretz*. The *Gemara* thus identifies these specific matches as the paradigmatic cases of a *seudah she-einah shel mitzvah*. Technically, *kiddushin* is a *mitzvah* — but it is not something the *Rabbanan* view as appropriate, and therefore the celebration does not carry the character of a true *seudat mitzvah*. The word *mitzvah* is being used normatively, not technically. One sees this appropriation of terminology in other contexts as well.

### Bad Match Examples

Rabbi Yochanan further teaches: *bat kohen le-Yisrael ein zivugan oleh yafeh* — the marriage of a *bat kohen* to a *Yisrael* does not turn out well. Rav Chisda specifies what this means, drawing on adjacent *psukim* in the Torah: *o almanah o gerushah o zera ein lah* — either she becomes a widow, or divorced, or they have no children. The *baraita* adds even more starkly: *kovrah o koverato* — either he buries her or she buries him. Rashi explains this means one of them dies before their time. Or, the *Gemara* adds, *mevi'ato lidei aniyut* — it brings him to poverty. One practical explanation for the poverty concern: the *kohanim* represented the aristocracy, and a *bat kohen* accustomed to that standard of living may strain the household's resources.

The *Gemara* challenges this with a statement also attributed to Rabbi Yochanan: *ha-rotzeh she-yitasher yidbak b'zaro shel Aharon* — whoever wishes to become wealthy should cleave to the descendants of Aharon. Indeed, *kol she-ken she-Torah u'khunah measheran* — all the more so if one combines Torah learning with a priestly lineage through marriage, both will contribute to wealth. Does this not contradict the earlier pessimism about such marriages?

The *Gemara* resolves: *lo kashya — ha b'talmid chacham, ha b'am ha'aretz*. The positive outcome attaches to a *talmid chacham* marrying a *bat kohen*; the negative outcome describes an *am ha'aretz* doing so. The two aristocracies — priestly lineage and Torah scholarship — are natural partners. The match becomes problematic when someone crosses out of both aristocratic categories: a *bat kohen* marrying an *am ha'aretz*, or a *bat talmid chacham* marrying an *am ha'aretz*. The original formulation of *bat kohen le-Yisrael* must therefore be understood as *bat kohen le-am ha'aretz* — a *Yisrael* who is an *am ha'aretz*.

### Good Match Examples

The *Gemara* proceeds to positive examples. Rabbi Yehoshua married a *kohenet* — note the shift from the construct *bat kohen* (a daughter defined by her father) to the feminized Aramaic *kohenet*. He fell ill and feared this was divine displeasure — *lo nicha leh l'Aharon d'idbak b'zarei d'havei leh chasna ki ana?* — Does Aharon not want me to cleave to his descendants? Am I not worthy to be his son-in-law? The complaint itself implies that a *talmid chacham* marrying a *bat kohen* is considered a proper match.

Rav Idi bar Avin married a *kohenet*, and the *Gemara* records: *nafak minei trei b'nei smichi* — from him came two sons who achieved full *semikhah*, two prominent rabbinic authorities: Rav Sheshet b'rei d'Rav Idi and Rav Yehoshua b'rei d'Rav Idi. Rav Pappa states: *ilu lo nasivna kahanta, lo itat'ri* — had I not married a *kohenet*, I would not have become wealthy. Rav Kahana, by contrast: *ilu lo nasivna kahanta, lo galai* — had I not married a *kohenet*, I would not have gone into exile. His going into *galut* from Bavel to Eretz Yisrael was not voluntary; he was forced to flee. When his students pointed out that he went to a place of Torah, he acknowledged it, but insisted this was not a *galut* in the ordinary sense — *lo galai k'degali inshi* — it was not the exile one chooses.

Tosafot makes an interesting observation: from the fact that Rav Kahana attributes his exile to having married a *kohenet*, it seems he was not himself a *kohen* — otherwise one would not expect punishment for marrying within the *kehunah*. This is corroborated by the linguistic point that *Kahana* is a first name in the Talmudic period, not a patronym or family designation, so bearing it carries no presumption of priestly lineage.

### Marriage Obligation

The *Gemara* now states the positive obligation more explicitly. *Tanu Rabbanan: le'olam yimkor adam kol mah she-yesh lo v'yisa bat talmid chacham* — a person should always sell everything he owns and marry the daughter of a *talmid chacham*. The reasoning: *she-im met o goleh yafeh* — if he dies or is compelled to travel, who will ensure the Torah education of his children? A *bat talmid chacham* carries those values as core commitments, not merely as external aspirations. *Im asah ken muvtach lo she-banav talmidei chachamim* — if he does so, he can be confident his children will carry those values forward. Conversely: *v'al yisa bat am ha'aretz* — do not marry the daughter of an *am ha'aretz*, for *banav amei ha'aretz* — if you are not present to invest in their education, no one else will ensure it, and they will become *amei ha'aretz*.

The *Gemara* then introduces the complementary principle: not only should one seek a *bat talmid chacham* for oneself, but *v'yasi bito l'talmid chacham* — one should also ensure one's daughter marries a *talmid chacham*. The parable given captures the ideal: *mashal l'invei hagefen b'invei hagefen davar na'eh u'mitkabel* — it is like grapes of the vine with grapes of the vine, a beautiful and pleasing combination. By contrast, *l'invei hagefen b'invei hasneh, davar ka'ur v'eino mitkabel* — grapes of the vine with berries of the thornbush is ugly and finds no favor.

A hierarchy is then laid out for cases where no *bat talmid chacham* is available. After a *talmid chacham*, one should seek *bat gedolei hador* — the daughter of the prominent leaders of the generation, presumably figures of civil authority. If not them, then *bat rashei kenesiyot* — the daughter of synagogue heads, a position of recognized communal prestige. Failing that, *bat gaba'ei tzedakah* — overseers of charitable funds, positions of both rectitude and public trust. Then *bat melamdei tinokot* — the daughter of those who teach children in the schools, people deeply invested in Torah transmission even if not themselves great scholars. The hierarchy reflects a combination of Torah commitment, public responsibility, and communal standing. Notably, the *melamdei tinokot* appear at the bottom rather than the top of the list even though the practical concern about ensuring children's education might seem to favor them — this reflects that the *Gemara* is tracking a prestige hierarchy alongside the purely educational concern. In every case, the clear bottom line is: *v'lo yisa bat am ha'aretz*.

It is worth stepping back to appreciate what motivates this passage. The language of selling everything one owns to ensure the right match is not mere rhetoric. It is an acknowledgment of what the right match costs — financially, logistically, and personally — and a statement that ensuring Torah continuity across generations is worth that cost. The concern is not primarily about social hierarchy for its own sake, but about the concrete transmission of Torah values within the home.

## Am Ha'aretz Rhetoric

### Sheketz Designation

Now comes the most challenging portion of the daf. The *Gemara* assigns stark characterizations to the *am ha'aretz* and his household: *mipnei she-hein sheketz v'nshoseihem sheretz* — because they are an abomination, and their wives are like vermin. And regarding their daughters the verse is invoked: *arur shochev im kol behemah* — cursed is one who lies with any animal. Rashi understands this as referring to their ignorance — they are treated as animals, as beings devoid of intellectual or spiritual distinction. But the verse is applied broadly, and as the *Gemara* continues the language only intensifies.

### Extreme Violence

Rabbi Elazar states: *am ha'aretz mutar l'nochro b'Yom HaKippurim she'chal l'hiyot b'Shabbat* — it is permitted to stab an *am ha'aretz* even on Yom Kippur that falls on *Shabbat*, meaning one may violate both Yom Kippur and *Shabbat* to do so. His students question the choice of language: why *nochro* — stabbing? Should he not say *shochto* — slaughtering, *shechitah*? He responds: *zeh ta'un berachah v'zeh eino ta'un berachah* — *shechitah* requires a *berachah*, and he does not wish any *berachah* to be associated with killing a person. This is an extraordinarily extreme statement. We can hope it is hyperbolic in the extreme, but it is in the text.

Rabbi Eliezer teaches: *am ha'aretz assur l'hitlavot imo baderech* — one may not accompany an *am ha'aretz* on a journey, as the verse says *ki hi chayecha v'orech yamecha* — Torah is your life and the length of your days. The reasoning: *al chayav lo chas* — he does not care about his own life, since he never invests in Torah learning; *al chayei chaveiro lo kol sheken* — all the more so will he not care about yours. Rabbi Yochanan adds: *am ha'aretz mutar l'kor'o k'dag* — one may tear him like a fish. Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak specifies: *u'migavo* — from his back, as one guts a fish by cutting along the spine, quickly and without the fish resisting.

### R. Akiva Confession

*Tanya*: Rabbi Akiva himself testifies — *k'she-hayiti am ha'aretz, amarti: mi yiten li talmid chacham v'anshchenu k'chamor* — when I was an *am ha'aretz*, I said: if only I could get hold of a *talmid chacham*, I would bite him like a donkey. His students ask: why a donkey rather than a dog? He explains: *zeh noshech v'shover etzem v'zeh noshech v'eino shover etzem* — a donkey bites and breaks the bone, while a dog bites but does not break bones. So fierce was his hatred. Rabbi Akiva's testimony here is remarkable: the violent animus was not one-directional. The *am ha'aretz* harbored equivalent — or greater — hatred toward *talmidei chachamim*, at least as reported here. This mutual contempt is a cycle that the *Gemara* clearly regards as a communal pathology even as it participates in it.

### Behavioral Claims

The *Gemara* continues with a teaching of Rabbi Meir: *kol hamashi bito l'am ha'aretz k'ilu kaftah u'minichtah lifnei ari* — whoever marries his daughter to an *am ha'aretz* is as if he bound her and left her before a lion. The *Gemara* explains the analogy: *mah ari dores v'ochel v'ein lo boshes panim, af am ha'aretz makeh u'bo'el v'ein lo boshes panim* — just as a lion tramples and eats without any shame (and Tosafot notes without even waiting for the prey to die), so too an *am ha'aretz* will smite and engage in sexual relations without any sense of privacy or restraint. The characterization is that an *am ha'aretz*'s approach to sexuality is violent and animalistic, without the modesty and self-restraint that Torah cultivation produces.

A *baraita* teaches: *Rebbi omer, am ha'aretz assur l'echol basar* — an *am ha'aretz* is forbidden to eat meat, because the verse says *zot torat habehemah v'ha'of* — this is the Torah of animal and bird. Torah is connected to animal and bird; therefore, *kol ha'osek baTorah mutar l'echol basar behemah v'of* — one who engages in Torah may eat meat, while *kol she'eino osek baTorah assur l'echol basar behemah v'of* — one who does not engage in Torah is forbidden to eat it. The implication: consuming animal flesh is a privilege granted to those whose Torah study elevates them above the merely animal. Eating meat without that spiritual framework may only deepen the animalistic orientation.

### Mutual Hatred

*Amar Rebbi: gedolah sinah she'sonim amei ha'aretz l'talmid chacham yoser mi'sinah she'sonim ovdei kochavim et Yisrael* — the hatred that *amei ha'aretz* bear toward *talmidei chachamim* is greater than the hatred non-Jews bear toward Jews. *V'neshoseihem yoter mehem* — and their wives hate even more virulently than the men. One might observe that at least part of this reflects projection — the *Gemara* itself has expressed extraordinarily hostile attitudes toward the *am ha'aretz*, and it should not surprise us that those attitudes generated reciprocal hostility.

The *Gemara* adds: *shana u'peireish yoter mi'kulam* — one who studied Torah and then left that world is worse than all of them, more virulent in his hatred than someone raised entirely as an *am ha'aretz*. Rashi's explanation is psychologically acute: he has heard everything that *chazal* say about *amei ha'aretz*, and therefore he hates the rabbinic community with particular intensity born of intimate knowledge.

### Practical Restrictions

*Tanu Rabbanan: shisha devarim ne'emru b'amei ha'aretz* — six practical rules were stated regarding an *am ha'aretz*. First, *ein mosrim lahem edut* — one does not call them as witnesses for a document. Second, *ein mekabelim mimenu edut* — one does not accept testimony from them when they come forward. The distinction between these two: the first is that you would not invite them to serve as witnesses for a transaction; the second is that even when they volunteer testimony — saying they witnessed Reuven lend money to Shimon — their testimony is not accepted. Third, *v'ein megalim lahem sod* — one does not share secrets with them. Fourth, *v'ein memanim osam apitropus al yesomim* — one does not appoint them as guardians over orphans' estates. Fifth, *v'ein memanim osam apitropus shel kupas tzedakah* — one does not appoint them as overseers of charitable funds. Sixth, *v'ein mitlavim imahem baderech* — one does not accompany them on the road. The common thread is untrustworthiness in matters of money and personal safety.

Some add a seventh: *af ein machrizim al aveidato* — one does not even announce their lost object so as to return it. Tosafot notes the practical question: how would one even know whose lost object it is? Tosafot answers: one found the object in a neighborhood of *amei ha'aretz*, so it is presumable that the owner is an *am ha'aretz*. The basis for not returning it seems to relate to a general suspicion that their property may not be honestly acquired.

The first *tanna* who holds that one does return the *aveida* of an *am ha'aretz* gives the following reason: *zimnin d'nafik minei zera ma'alya v'achal leh* — sometimes from him will come worthy offspring who will deserve to benefit from the wealth. As the verse says: *yachin tzaddik yilbash* — the righteous man prepares, and another righteous man will wear it.

### Tosafot Nuance

Tosafot registers an important qualification regarding the entire arc of this *Gemara*. Elsewhere in the Talmud there are many passages that take a far more measured, accepting attitude toward *amei ha'aretz* — far less harsh, far more nuanced. Tosafot explains that there are distinct categories of *am ha'aretz*, and the most extreme rhetoric in this *Gemara* addresses a specific category: those who were actively and even violently hostile to rabbinic authority. Tosafot invokes the ruling *ein machzirin lo aveidato* — not returning his lost object — and clarifies in that context: *v'nir'eh li d'la mairi b'makir v'kofer l'hach'is d'havei lei k'mo pikuach nefesh d'listim v'chashud al hadamim* — this refers specifically to someone who consciously rejects rabbinic authority in a provocative way, making him like a bandit, suspect regarding bloodshed. Tosafot further notes: *d'ika harbe inyanei am ha'aretz* — there are many different categories of *am ha'aretz*, and by later periods, testimony from an *am ha'aretz* was accepted in practice.

By the *Gemara*'s own close, the rhetoric has moderated. The final concern is no longer murder or gutting like a fish but questions of trust in monetary matters and whether one returns a found object. Even there, a voice within the *Gemara* itself insists one should return the *aveida*, because worthy descendants may benefit from it. This trajectory — from extreme violence to measured practical restrictions, with a dissenting voice arguing even for the return of lost property — offers some evidence that we are dealing throughout with hyperbole, with a specific and hostile sub-category of *am ha'aretz*, and with a historical moment of sharp communal conflict that must be read carefully and not generalized. Tosafot makes exactly this point, and it remains an essential hermeneutic for approaching this *Gemara*.
