# Zevachim 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Order of Korbanot

### Opening Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּר וְשָׂעִיר שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים כּוּ׳. מִכְּדֵי צָפוֹן בְּעוֹלָה כְּתִיב, נִיתְנֵי עוֹלָה בְּרֵישָׁא!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **the bull and the goat of Yom Kippur,** their slaughter is in the north and the collection of their blood in a service vessel is in the north. The Gemara asks: Why does the mishna list these sin offerings first? **After all,** while the *halakha* that slaughter must be in the **north** of the Temple courtyard **is written** in the Torah **with regard to a burnt offering** (Leviticus 1:11), the Torah does not explicitly state that the other offerings must be slaughtered in the north. Therefore, **let** the *tanna* of the mishna **teach** the *halakha* of **a burnt offering first.**

Daf 48 opens at the very first line of the *gemara*'s analysis of the *mishnah*, which catalogues the procedural requirements for the various *korbanot*. The *mishnah* begins with the category of *kodshei kodashim she-shachtan ba-tzafon* — the highest-sanctity offerings slaughtered in the north — and then proceeds to specify for each individual offering that the blood must likewise be received in the north, rather than stating this as a single global rule. The preceding daf had already addressed why the *gemara* needed to handle the *asham* separately, given that a portion of its blood is caught directly by hand. Now the *gemara* turns to the specifics, beginning with the *parim hanisrafim* — the offerings whose blood is brought into the inner sanctuary and whose flesh is burned outside the *Beis HaMikdash* — and, within that category, with the *par v-se'ir* of *Yom Kippur*.

### Chatat Before Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטָּאת – אַיְּידֵי דְּאָתֵי מִדְּרָשָׁא, חַבִּיבָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since** the location for slaughtering the **sin offering is derived through interpretation, it is dear to** the *tanna*, and therefore he gives it precedence. The verse states: "Speak to Aaron and to his sons, saying: This is the law of the sin offering: In the place where the burnt offering is slaughtered shall the sin offering be slaughtered before the Lord; it is most holy" (Leviticus 6:18). The Gemara (55a) derives from this verse that the sin offering must be slaughtered in the same place as the burnt offering, i.e., in the north of the Temple courtyard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי חַטָּאוֹת הַחִיצוֹנוֹת! אַיְּידֵי דְּנִכְנַס דָּמָן לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים, חַבִּיבָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let** the *tanna* of the mishna **teach** first the *halakha* of **the external sin offerings,** since those are the offerings to which the verse is referring. The Gemara explains: **Since** the **blood** of the Yom Kippur sin offerings **enters the innermost sanctum,** these offerings are **dear to** the *tanna*, and he taught them first.

Before engaging the substance of the laws, the *gemara* pauses to address the order of the *mishnah* itself. *Tzafon* is first mentioned in the Torah in connection with the *olah*, and all subsequent references to *tzafon* in other *korbanot* are framed in relation to it — "in the place where you slaughter the *olah*." This raises an obvious question: *nistni olah ba-reisha* — why does our *mishnah* not list the *olah* first? The *gemara* answers: *chatas idi d'asi midrasha chaviva lei* — since the *chatat* is derived through a *derasha*, it has a certain special status in the rabbinic enterprise and was therefore placed first. Even though the verse *bimkom asher tishchat ha-olah tishchat hachatat* is fairly explicit, the fact that it requires derivation at all gives it a kind of prominence in the *mishnah*'s ordering.

### Inner vs Outer Chataot

A further question follows: even granting that the *chatat* comes first, why do the *inner chataot* — those whose blood is brought into the inner sanctuary — take priority over the *outer chataot*, which appear earlier in the Torah? The *gemara* answers: *idi d'nichnas daman lifnai v'lifnim chaviva lei* — since their blood enters *lifnai v'lifnim*, the innermost sanctum, they receive priority. And *lifnai v'lifnim* here refers specifically to the *chatat* of *Yom Kippur*, whose blood enters the *Kodesh Kodashim* itself. This detail is notable: the ordering of the *mishnah* appears to reflect a hierarchy of sanctity, in which the power of the *chatat* to bring its blood even into the *Kodesh Kodashim* — something no *olah* does — grants it a certain pride of place that the *olah*, as the paradigmatic offering, nonetheless does not possess.

## Tzafon in Olah & Vav Mos…

### Tzafon Source in Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְצָפוֹנָה בְּעוֹלָה הֵיכָא כְּתִיבָא? ״וְשָׁחַט אֹתוֹ עַל יֶרֶךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ צָפֹנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where** is it **written** that **a burnt offering** must be slaughtered in the **north** of the Temple courtyard? The Gemara answers that with regard to a sheep that is brought as a burnt offering the Torah states: **"And he shall slaughter it on the side of the altar northward** before the Lord; and Aaron's sons, the priests, shall dash its blood against the altar round about" (Leviticus 1:11).

With the order of the *mishnah* accounted for, the *gemara* proceeds to its main inquiry: *v'tzafona ba-olah heicha ksiv* — where exactly does the Torah state that the *olah* is slaughtered in the north? The answer: *v'shachat oso al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafonah lifnei Hashem* — "and he shall slaughter it on the north side of the altar before God." That is the source for *tzafon* by the *olah*.

### Bakar vs Tzon Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן בֶּן צֹאן, בֶּן בָּקָר מְנָא לַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִם מִן הַצֹּאן״ – וָי״ו מוֹסִיף עַל עִנְיָן רִאשׁוֹן, וְיִלְמַד עֶלְיוֹן מִתַּחְתּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **We have found** that this verse provides a source that **a young sheep** burnt offering must be slaughtered in the north. **From where do we** derive that **a young bull** burnt offering must also be slaughtered in the north? The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "And if** his offering be **of the flock,** whether of the sheep, or of the goats, for a burnt offering, he shall offer it a male without blemish" (Leviticus 1:10). The conjunctive "and" represented by the letter ***vav* adds to the previous matter.** The previous passage addresses cattle offerings. **And let** the **upper** passage, the place of the slaughter of a bull, **be learned from** the **lower** passage, the place of slaughtering a sheep.

This immediately generates a further question. The opening of *Vayikra* introduces the *olah* in two stages: first the *olah* from *bakar* (cattle), in *Vayikra* 1:5, and then the *olah* from *tzon* (sheep or goat), in *Vayikra* 1:10–11. In the first case, concerning the *bakar*, the verse says only *v'shachat et ben habakar lifnei Hashem* — "and he shall slaughter the cattle before God" — with no mention of *tzafon*. Only when the Torah reaches the *tzon* does it add *al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafonah* — "on the north side of the altar." So: *ashkechan ben tzon, ben bakar minalan* — we know that the *tzon* is slaughtered in the north, but where do we learn this for the *bakar*?

The *gemara* answers: *amar kra v'im min hatzon* — "and if the offering is from the flock." Because the second subsection opens with *v'im*, "and if," the connecting *vav* links the two passages. This is the principle of *vav mosif al inyan rishon*: the *vav* adds to what was stated above.

### Vav Mosif Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְלַמְּדִין; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מְלַמְּדִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This works out well according to the one who says** that **we learn** *halakhot* in this manner. **But according to the one who says** that **we do not learn** *halakhot* in this manner, **what is there to say?**

Here lies the hermeneutical challenge. Normally, a linking *vav* flows downward — something stated in the first clause is applied to the second. But here the *gemara* is applying a detail stated in the *second* clause back to the *first*. The rule invoked is *velamed elyon mitachton* — the upper (earlier) passage is taught by the lower (later) one. Since the *tzon* passage specifies *tzafon*, and the *vav* links it to the *bakar* passage above, we apply *tzafon* to the *bakar* as well.

This principle, however, is not universally accepted. The *gemara* raises the position *ein melamdim* — that the lower passage cannot literally teach the upper one. If that view is adopted, we cannot learn that *bakar* is slaughtered in the north from the *tzon* passage, and a different source must be found. This question leads the *gemara* into an extended discussion about the *asham*, which itself turns on the same hermeneutical debate.

## Asham Taluy Debate

### Ve'im Nefesh Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ וְגוֹ׳״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל סְפֵק מְעִילוֹת אָשָׁם תָּלוּי. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין. מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר לְמֵידִין, וּמַר סָבַר אֵין לְמֵידִין?
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: Immediately following the passage in the Torah addressing a guilt offering for misuse of consecrated property, the Torah discusses the *halakhot* of a provisional guilt offering, brought by one who is uncertain as to whether he committed a sin that requires a sin offering. The verse states: **"And if anyone** sin, and does any of the commandments which the Lord has commanded not to be done, though he did not know it, yet is he guilty, and shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 5:17). This serves **to render** him **liable** to bring **a provisional guilt offering for uncertain misuse** of consecrated property; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis deem** him **exempt** in such a case. The Gemara suggests: **What, is it not** that **they disagree with regard to this:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that **we learn** *halakhot* of the upper passage from the lower passage, **and** the other **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that **we do not learn** *halakhot* in this manner?

The *gemara* introduces two adjacent passages from *Vayikra* 5. The first, 5:15, opens *nefesh ki tim'al ma'al* — "a person who commits a trespass" — and describes the *asham me'ilot*, the guilt offering brought for misuse of sanctified property. It specifies: *vehevi et ashamo l'Hashem ayil tamim min hatzon b'erkecha kesef shekalim b'shekel hakodesh l'asham* — "and he shall bring his guilt offering to God, an unblemished ram from the flock, at your valuation in silver shekels by the sanctuary standard, as a guilt offering." The phrase *kesef shekalim* establishes a minimum monetary requirement for an *asham*, a detail to which the *gemara* will return.

Immediately following, 5:17–18, the Torah states *v'im nefesh ki techeta velo yada v'ashem* — "and if a person sins and does not know, and incurs guilt." This is the *asham safek* (also called *asham taluy*): the conditional guilt offering brought when one is uncertain whether one has committed a sin requiring a *chatat*. The new subsection opens with *v'im*, the same construction as the *tzon* passage — an "and if" that links back to the *asham me'ilot* above via a connecting *vav*.

The question follows naturally: does the *vav* here operate as *vav mosif al inyan rishon*, applying the law of *asham taluy* upward to the *asham me'ilot* as well? That is, if one is uncertain whether one committed *me'ilah*, does one bring an *asham taluy*? This is precisely the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְמֵידִין; וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״מִצְוֹת״, וְנֶאֱמַר בְּחַטַּאת חֵלֶב ״מִצְוֹת״; מָה לְהַלָּן – דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, אַף כָּאן – שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** This is not correct, **as everyone** holds that **we learn** *halakhot* of the upper passage from the lower passage. **And this is** the **reason that the Rabbis** exempt from bringing an offering one who is uncertain whether he misused consecrated property: They learn a verbal analogy. **It is stated here:** "And if anyone sin, and does any of the **commandments** which the Lord has commanded not to be done" (Leviticus 5:17). **And it is stated with regard to the sin offering for** eating forbidden **fat:** "And if any one of the common people sin through error, in doing any of the **commandments** which the Lord has commanded not to be done, and be guilty" (Leviticus 4:27). **Just as there,** the sin offering is brought only for an act **that for its intentional** violation **one is liable** to be punished with ***karet*, and for its unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin offering, so too here,** one brings a provisional guilt offering only for an act **that for its intentional** violation **one is liable** to be punished with ***karet*, and for its unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin offering,** which is not the case concerning misuse of consecrated property.

A *beraita* states: *v'im nefesh* — this teaches that even doubt regarding *me'ilah* obligates an *asham taluy* — *divrei Rabbi Akiva*. The Sages, by contrast, exempt one in such a case. The *gemara* initially proposes that this dispute maps onto the debate about *melamdim* versus *ein melamdim*: Rabbi Akiva holds that the lower passage can teach the upper one via the *vav*, while the Sages hold it cannot. If this were the case, however, then the Sages' position would leave us without a source for the *bakar* being slaughtered in the north, since that derivation also relies on learning upward from the *tzon* passage.

Rav Pappa therefore rejects this framing: *dechulei alma melamdim* — everyone agrees that the *vav* can teach upward. This allows all opinions to affirm that *bakar* is slaughtered in the north. The debate between Rabbi Akiva and the Sages is on different grounds entirely.

### Rabbis' Mitzvot Limitati…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – מָה לְהַלָּן בִּקְבוּעָה, אַף כָּאן בִּקְבוּעָה; לְאַפּוֹקֵי חַטָּאת דְּטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, דְּעוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what does **Rabbi Akiva** derive from this verbal analogy? He learns that **just as there** the verse obligates one to bring **a fixed** sin offering, **so too here,** with regard to the provisional guilt offering, one brings it for a case of uncertain transgression of a prohibition for which one would be liable to bring **a fixed** sin offering, **to exclude a sin offering** brought for uncertain transgression **of** the **defiling of** the **Temple or sacrificial** foods, **as** the sin offering for that transgression is not fixed, but **is a sliding-scale** offering. If the sinner is poor he brings a meal offering or a bird offering; if he is rich he brings an animal offering. In a case of uncertainty one does not bring a provisional guilt offering.

The Sages' actual position rests on a *gezeirah shavah*. The passage concerning *asham safek* uses the phrase *mikol mitzvot Hashem asher lo te'asenah* — "from any of the commandments of God that may not be done." The same word *mitzvot* appears in the context of the *chatat* for *cheilev* (forbidden fat). This verbal link teaches that *asham taluy* applies only to sins of the same type as *cheilev* — sins for which one would otherwise bring a fixed *chatat*: specifically, those punishable by *karet* for deliberate violation and requiring a *chatat* for inadvertent violation.

The Sages read this linkage as exclusive: the *asham taluy* is tethered to the *chatat*, and to the *chatat* alone. Because *asham me'ilot* is not a *chatat* but an *asham*, and a fixed *asham* rather than a variable-cost offering, the *gezeirah shavah* to *mitzvot* locks the *asham taluy* to *chatat* and prevents the *vav* from carrying it upward to *asham me'ilot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן – אֵין גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לְמֶחֱצָה. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא נָמֵי, אֵין גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לְמֶחֱצָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **the Rabbis,** who derived a different *halakha* from the verbal analogy, they hold that **there is no verbal analogy for half** of a matter. Once a provisional guilt offering is compared to a sin offering, it must be completely similar, and both matters are derived from the verbal analogy. The Gemara asks: **But Rabbi Akiva also** must hold that **there is no verbal analogy for half** of a matter, so why does he not agree with the derivation of the Rabbis?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין הָכִי נָמֵי; וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ״ כְּתִיב – וָי״ו מוֹסִיף עַל עִנְיָן רִאשׁוֹן;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reconsiders: **Yes,** this **is indeed so. And here they disagree with regard to this: Rabbi Akiva holds** that **it is written** with regard to the provisional guilt offering: **"And if anyone** sin, and does any of the commandments which the Lord has commanded not to be done, though he did not know it, yet is he guilty, and shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 5:17). The word "and" represented by the letter ***vav* adds to the previous matter.** When a phrase begins with the conjunction *vav*, it is a continuation of the previous matter, and the *halakhot* of the previous passage can be learned from the subsequent passage. Therefore one brings a provisional guilt offering for uncertain misuse of consecrated property.

### Akiva: Fixed Korbanot

Rabbi Akiva, facing this objection, offers a nuanced response. He agrees that the word *mitzvot* creates a link to a *chatat*. But he reads that link more narrowly: it tells you that *asham taluy* applies only to sins requiring a *fixed* offering — *bikvuah* — and excludes sins where the *korban* is variable, *oleh veyored*, such as *chatat tumat mikdash v'kodashav*, whose cost varies with the offerer's financial means. What *mitzvot* does not do, in Rabbi Akiva's view, is limit the *asham taluy* exclusively to *chatat* as opposed to *asham*. A fixed *asham*, such as *asham me'ilot*, satisfies the criterion of *kvuah* and therefore falls within the scope of *asham taluy*.

The Sages respond with a powerful counterargument: *ein gezeirah shavah lemachetza* — you cannot use a *gezeirah shavah* in half-measures. Once the *asham taluy* has been linked to the *chatat* through *mitzvot*, that linkage is total. You cannot say "it's linked to the *chatat* in one respect but extends beyond it in another." The link defines the entire scope. The *gemara* acknowledges this as a compelling point.

### Heikesh vs Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ״! לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר הֶיקֵּשׁ עֲדִיף, וּמָר סָבַר גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה עֲדִיף?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to the opinion of **the Rabbis also, isn't it written: "And if anyone** sin"? **Let us say** that **they disagree about this: As** one **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that a derivation from **a juxtaposition is preferable,** and derives from the juxtaposition of the *halakhot* of misuse of consecrated property to the *halakhot* of a provisional guilt offering that one is liable to bring a provisional guilt offering if he is not certain whether he misused consecrated property. **And** one **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that a derivation from **a verbal analogy is preferable,** and therefore derives from a verbal analogy between the passage of misuse of consecrated property and the passage of a sin offering for eating forbidden fat that one is not liable to bring a provisional guilt offering if he is not certain whether he misused consecrated property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – דְּהֶיקֵּשׁ עֲדִיף; וְאָמְרִי לָךְ רַבָּנַן: תַּחְתּוֹן הוּא דְּגָמַר מֵעֶלְיוֹן –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it may be **that everyone** agrees **that** a derivation from **a juxtaposition is preferable. And the Rabbis would say to you** that the reason one is exempt from bringing a provisional guilt offering in a case of uncertain misuse of consecrated property is that the juxtaposition should be understood in the opposite manner. **It is** the **lower** passage **that** is **derived from** the **upper** passage.

The *gemara* then explores a reformulation: perhaps the debate is not about *melamdim* but about which hermeneutical tool takes priority — *heikesh* (the juxtaposition created by the *vav*) or *gezeirah shavah* (the verbal analogy through *mitzvot*). Perhaps Rabbi Akiva holds *heikesh adif* — the juxtaposition is the stronger tool — while the Sages hold *gezeirah shavah adif*. But the *gemara* rejects this framing as well: *lo dechulei alma heikesh adif* — everyone agrees the *heikesh* takes priority.

The resolution is more subtle. The Sages agree to prioritize the *heikesh*, but they apply it in the opposite direction. Rather than using the *vav* to carry *asham taluy* upward to *asham me'ilot*, they use the *heikesh* to carry the law of *kesef shekalim* downward from *asham me'ilot* to *asham taluy*.

### Kessef Shekalim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים. שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאמַר: לֹא יְהֵא סְפֵיקוֹ חָמוּר מִוַּדָּאוֹ; מָה וַדָּאוֹ חַטָּאת בַּת דַּנְקָא, אַף סְפֵיקוֹ אָשָׁם בַּר דַּנְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The juxtaposition teaches that a provisional **guilt offering** must be a ram **worth** a minimum of two **silver shekels,** as is the *halakha* with regard to the offering brought for misuse of consecrated property. This derivation is needed **so that you should not say** that a provisional guilt offering, brought for **his uncertain** transgression, **should not be** more **stringent than** the offering one brings in a case of **his definite** transgression. According to that claim, one would say that **just as** for **his definite** transgression one may bring **a sin offering worth** only **one-sixth [*danka*]** of a dinar, **so too,** for **his uncertain** transgression one may bring a provisional **guilt offering worth** only **one-sixth** of a dinar.

This is a striking point. The *asham me'ilot* passage specifies *kesef shekalim* — a minimum value of two *shekalim* — as the cost threshold for that offering. An *asham taluy*, by contrast, is brought in a case of doubt. One might reason as follows: if the sin actually occurred, one would bring a *chatat*, which has no minimum cost (*bedanka* — even a trivially small sum suffices). If the *chatat* can be brought for pennies, surely the *asham taluy*, which addresses mere uncertainty, should have no minimum either. This *hava amina* is intuitively appealing.

The Sages, however, say the *heikesh* teaches the opposite: the *asham taluy* must also meet the minimum of *kesef shekalim*. One rabbinic thinker — Rabbeinu Yonah, it is noted — makes a morally significant observation here. When one knows with certainty that one has sinned, the path to *teshuvah* is more direct; one owns the failure concretely. When the sin is merely possible, one is more susceptible to rationalization and avoidance. The Torah, by requiring a significant minimum expenditure for the *asham taluy*, forces a genuine reckoning even in the face of doubt.

### Cost Requirement Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָא סְבָרָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״זֹּאת תּוֹרַת הָאָשָׁם״ – תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל הָאֲשָׁמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where** does **Rabbi Akiva** derive **this conclusion?** The Gemara answers: **He derives** it **from** the verse that states: **"And this is the law of the guilt offering"** (Leviticus 7:1), which teaches that there is **one law for all of the guilt offerings** and they must all be worth at least two shekels, provisional guilt offerings included.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח מַאן דְּאִית לֵיהּ ״תּוֹרַת״; מַאן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ ״תּוֹרַת״ מֵהֵיכָא גָּמַר? גָּמַר ״בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״–״בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This **works out well** according to **one who holds** that there is a derivation from the word **"law,"** but according to the **one who does not hold** that there is a derivation from the word **"law," from where does he learn** that all guilt offerings must have the same minimum value? The Gemara answers: **He learns** it from a verbal analogy between the term **"according to your valuation"** stated with regard to a guilt offering for misuse of consecrated property (Leviticus 5:15) and the term **"according to your valuation"** stated with regard to a provisional guilt offering (Leviticus 5:18) and a guilt offering for robbery (Leviticus 5:25).

A further question: where does Rabbi Akiva himself derive the minimum cost for the *asham*, given that he reads the *heikesh* as extending *asham taluy* to *asham me'ilot* rather than importing *kesef shekalim* downward? The *gemara* answers: *nafka lei mizot Torat haAsham* — he derives it from the phrase *zot Torat haAsham*, "this is the law of the *asham*," which teaches a single law applicable to all *ashamot*, thereby globalizing the minimum cost requirement. The *gemara* then addresses the view that does not *darshan* the word *Torat* in this way: that opinion derives the linkage from the repeated word *b'erkecha* ("at your valuation"), which appears in both the *asham me'ilot* and *asham taluy* passages, establishing the minimum cost through a *gezeirah shavah* of *b'erkecha–b'erkecha*.

### Asham Shifchah Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח הֵיכָא דִּכְתִיב ״בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״; אֲשַׁם שִׁפְחָה חֲרוּפָה דְּלָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This **works out well** concerning guilt offerings **where it is written "according to your valuation,"** but with regard to **a guilt offering** brought for engaging in sexual intercourse with **an espoused maidservant, concerning which** it is **not written** in the Torah **"according to your valuation," what is there to say?** How does one derive that the guilt offering brought for engaging in sexual intercourse with an espoused maidservant must be worth a minimum of two silver shekels?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּמַר ״בְּאֵיל״–״בְּאֵיל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that it is **derived** from a verbal analogy between the term **"with the ram"** stated with regard to a guilt offering for misuse of consecrated property (Leviticus 5:16) and the term **"with the ram"** stated with regard to the guilt offering for engaging in sexual intercourse with an espoused maidservant (Leviticus 19:22).

The *gemara* then addresses a further case: *asham shifchah charufah*, the guilt offering brought in connection with a *shifchah charufah*. Unlike the *asham taluy* and *asham me'ilot*, this offering does not carry the word *b'erkecha*. If one is deriving the minimum cost only from that word, how does one extend the rule to *asham shifchah charufah*? The answer: *gamar b'ayil b'ayil* — the word *ayil* ("ram"), common to multiple *asham* passages, serves as the linking term to extend the minimum cost requirement more broadly. The *gemara* also notes that this globalization is not perfectly uniform: *asham nazir* and *asham metzora*, which require a *keves* (a young ram of one year rather than a full two-year-old *ayil*), carry a minimum of only one *shekel* rather than two. The upshot of the entire discussion is that while the minimum cost requirement does apply to the relevant *ashamot*, it is calibrated to the specific animal required by each.

Returning to the original question: everyone agrees that the *vav* can teach upward (*melamdim*), and this is how all opinions derive that the *bakar* of the *olah* is slaughtered in the north. The debate between Rabbi Akiva and the Sages concerns the specific interaction of the *vav* and the *gezeirah shavah* in the *asham taluy* context, not the general principle of *melamdim*.

## Tzafon in Chatat

### Torah Sources Chatat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטָּאת מְנָא לַן דְּבָעֲיָא צָפוֹן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת הַחַטָּאת בִּמְקוֹם הָעוֹלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss the mishna. **From where do we** derive that **a sin offering requires** slaughter in the **north** of the Temple courtyard? The Gemara answers: **As it is written** with regard to an individual sin offering: "And he shall place his hand upon the head of the sin offering, **and slaughter the sin offering in the place of the burnt offering"** (Leviticus 4:29). Just as a burnt offering must be slaughtered in the north of the Temple courtyard, so too, a sin offering must also be slaughtered in the north.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שְׁחִיטָה, קַבָּלָה מְנָא לַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן מִדַּם הַחַטָּאת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We have found** from this verse that the **slaughter** must be in the north. **From where do we** derive that **collection** of the blood must also be in the north? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering** with his finger and place it upon the corners of the altar of burnt offering, and all its remaining blood he shall pour out at the base of the altar" (Leviticus 4:34). As this verse immediately follows the verse discussing the slaughter of a sin offering, evidently the taking of the blood is performed in the same place as the slaughter.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְקַבֵּל עַצְמוֹ מְנָא לַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְלָקַח״ – ״לוֹ יִקַּח״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that the one collecting the blood must **himself** stand in the north of the Temple courtyard? Perhaps he may stand near the north and extend his arm to collect the blood. The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "And** the priest **shall take [*velakaḥ*]"** (Leviticus 4:34), which can be read, as: **He will take himself [*lo yikkaḥ*].**

Having established the source for *tzafon* in the *olah*, the *gemara* turns to the *chatat*. The inquiry is: *chatat minalan d'ba'ya tzafon* — how do we know that the *chatat* requires the north?

Two distinct verses are at play. The first, from *Vayikra* 4:27–29, describes the *chatat yachid* — the sin offering of an ordinary person — and concludes: *v'shachat et hachatat bimkom ha-olah* — "and he shall slaughter the *chatat* in the place of the *olah*." A second verse, from *Vayikra* 4:22–24, describes the *chatat nasi* — the sin offering of a prince — and states: *v'shachat oto bimkom asher yishchat et ha-olah* — "and he shall slaughter *it* in the place where the *olah* is slaughtered." A third formulation appears in *Vayikra* 6:18 (*parashat Tzav*): *zot Torat hachatat bimkom asher tishchat ha-olah tishchat hachatat lifnei Hashem* — "this is the law of the *chatat*: in the place where the *olah* is slaughtered, the *chatat* shall be slaughtered before God." This last verse functions as a global statement — *zot Torat hachatat* announces a general principle applicable to all *chataot*.

From *v'shachat et hachatat bimkom ha-olah* the *gemara* derives that *shechita* must occur in the north. From the following verse, *velakach hakohen midam hachatat* — "and the priest shall take from the blood of the *chatat*" — the *gemara* derives that *kabbalah* (the catching of the blood) must also occur in the north: the blood is taken *from where the chatat is*, i.e., in the north. The requirement that the *mekabel* himself (the one catching the blood) must stand in the north is derived separately: reading *velakach* as *lo yikach* — rearranging the letters of *vav* and *lamed* — the verse is read as commanding the *kohen* to place *himself* at the location where the blood is caught.

### Me'akev Default Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן לְמִצְוָה, לְעַכֵּב מִנַּיִן? קְרָא אַחְרִינָא כְּתִיב: ״וְשָׁחַט אוֹתוֹ בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁחַט אֶת הָעוֹלָה״, וְתַנְיָא: הֵיכָן עוֹלָה [נִשְׁחֶטֶת] – בַּצָּפוֹן; אַף זֶה בַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We have found** a verse teaching that in order **to** perform the **mitzva** in the optimal manner, the slaughtering of the sin offering is in the north. **From where do we** derive **to disqualify** after the fact an offering slaughtered elsewhere, not in the north? The Gemara answers: **It is written** in **another verse** which speaks of a goat sin offering brought by a king who sins: "And he shall place his hand upon the head of the goat **and slaughter it in the place where they slaughter the burnt offering** before the Lord; it is a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:24). **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Where is the burnt offering slaughtered? In the north. This** sin offering of a king must **also** be slaughtered **in the north** of the Temple courtyard.

Having established that the *chatat* must be slaughtered in the north and that the blood must be caught in the north, the *gemara* poses a further question: *ashkechan lemitzvah, le'akev minalan*? We know that slaughtering in the north is a *mitzvah* — but do we know that failure to do so *invalidates* the offering?

This question reflects a striking general assumption about the laws of *korbanot*: unlike most areas of *halacha*, where a positive command is presumed to be indispensable, in the context of *korbanot* the *gemara* assumes that no particular procedural requirement is *me'akev* — invalidating — unless it is stated twice. The logic appears to be that the sheer density of procedural detail in the *avodah* would render too many offerings invalid if every requirement were automatically *me'akev*. The principle therefore requires a doubling of the relevant law before it becomes indispensable.

### Double Mention Doubling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מִכָּאן אַתָּה לָמֵד?! וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר תִּשָּׁחֵט הָעוֹלָה תִּשָּׁחֵט הַחַטָּאת״! הָא לְמָה יָצָא – לִקְבּוֹעַ לוֹ מָקוֹם, שֶׁאִם לֹא שְׁחָטָהּ בַּצָּפוֹן פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do you learn** this *halakha* **from here? But isn't it already stated:** "Speak to Aaron and to his sons, saying: This is the law of the sin offering: **In the place where the burnt offering is slaughtered shall the sin offering be slaughtered** before the Lord; it is most holy" (Leviticus 6:18)? If so, **to what** purpose **was this singled out?** Why did the Torah state explicitly that the sin offering of the king requires slaughter in the north? The Gemara answers: **To fix a place for it,** that this is the only place where a sin offering may be slaughtered, teaching **that if it was not slaughtered in the north** of the Temple courtyard, the offering is **disqualified** even after the fact.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לְכָךְ יָצָא; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא שֶׁזֶּה טָעוּן צָפוֹן, וְאֵין אַחֵר טָעוּן צָפוֹן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת הַחַטָּאת בִּמְקוֹם הָעוֹלָה״ – זֶה בָּנָה אָב לְכׇל חַטָּאוֹת, שֶׁיְּהוּ טְעוּנוֹת צָפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Do **you say** that it is **singled out for this** purpose, to teach that even after the fact a sin offering slaughtered anywhere other than in the north is disqualified? **Or** perhaps **it is only** to teach **that this** goat sin offering **requires** slaughter in the **north but no other** goat sin offering **requires** slaughter in the **north.** The Gemara answers: **The verse states:** "And he shall place his hand upon the head of the sin offering, **and slaughter the sin offering in the place of the burnt offering"** (Leviticus 4:29). **This established a paradigm for all sin offerings,** teaching **that they require** slaughter in the **north.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא – בֵּין לְמִצְוָה בֵּין לְעַכֵּב; שְׁאָר חַטָּאוֹת נָמֵי – אַשְׁכְּחַן לְמִצְוָה; לְעַכֵּב מְנָא לַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its explanation: **We have found** that the Torah writes with regard to the **goat** sin offering **of a king** that it requires slaughter in the north **both to** perform the **mitzva** in the optimal manner and **to disqualify** the offering even after the fact. **We have also found** with regard to the **other sin offerings** that the Torah states that **to** perform the **mitzva** in the optimal manner they must be slaughtered in the north of the Temple courtyard. **From where do we** derive the *halakha* **to disqualify** other sin offerings if they were not slaughtered in the north?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב בְּכִשְׂבָּה וּכְתִיב בִּשְׂעִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **As it is written with regard to a lamb** sin offering that it must be slaughtered in the north: "And slaughter the sin offering in the place of the burnt offering" (Leviticus 4:33) **and** it is also **written with regard to a she-goat** sin offering: "And he shall place his hand upon the head of the sin offering, and slaughter it for a sin offering in the place of the burnt offering" (Leviticus 4:29). This repetition teaches that a sin offering is disqualified if it is not slaughtered in the north.

How, then, is the requirement of *tzafon* for the *chatat* doubled? The *gemara* turns to the verse concerning *chatat nasi*: *v'shachat oto bimkom asher yishchat et ha-olah*. A *beraita* raises the possibility that the word *oto* ("it") in this verse comes to *limit* the scope of *tzafon* — only this *se'ir nasi* requires the north; nothing else does. The *beraita* rejects this reading: *talmud lomar v'shachat et hachatat bimkom ha-olah* — the general verse in *parashat Tzav* teaches that all *chataot* require the north. The verse about *chatat nasi* is therefore not restricting, but rather *doubling* — its repetition of the *tzafon* requirement establishes that this is *me'akev*. This doubling is explicitly stated: the verse concerning *chatat nasi* is singled out *likvo'a lo makom* — to establish its place as a binding location requirement — such that *she-im lo shachatah b'tzafon, pasul* — if the slaughter did not occur in the north, it is invalid.

### Doubling Extension Logic

The doubling that establishes *me'akev* status appears in the context of the *se'ir nasi*. How do we know that the remaining *chataot* are also *me'akev* with respect to *tzafon*? The *gemara* answers: *d'chtiv b'chesevah v'chtiv b'se'irah* — the Torah states the requirement of *bimkom ha-olah* both for the case of a *kesevah* (female sheep) and for the case of a *se'irah* (female goat) brought as a *chatat* by an ordinary person. This internal doubling within the passage concerning *chataot* of ordinary individuals establishes that *tzafon* is *me'akev* for those offerings as well. The *me'akev* status for *se'ir nasi* is established by the doubling between the two separate verses; the *me'akev* status for the other *chataot* is established by the doubling within their own passage.

## Oto: Animal vs Shochet

### Se'ir Nachshon Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״אוֹתוֹ״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** § Having derived from these verses that all sin offerings are disqualified if they are slaughtered not in the north, the Gemara questions its earlier explanation. **Rather, why do I** need the term **"it"** stated with regard to the sin offering of a king in the verse: "And he shall place his hand upon the head of the goat, and slaughter it in the place where they slaughter the burnt offering before the Lord; it is a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:24)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״אוֹתוֹ בַּצָּפוֹן״ – וְאֵין שְׂעִיר נַחְשׁוֹן בַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is required for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **It,** the goat sin offering of a king, is slaughtered **in the north** of the Tabernacle, **but** the **goat** offered by **Nahshon** and the other princes was **not** slaughtered **in the north.** Nahshon was the prince of the tribe of Judah. He, along with all the other princes of the tribes, brought offerings to inaugurate the altar and the Tabernacle, as recorded in the Torah (Numbers, chapter 7). Although the offerings were classified as sin offerings because they shared some characteristics of sin offerings, they were not brought to atone for any particular sin. Therefore, the term "it" teaches that the offerings of the princes did not require slaughter in the north.

With the *me'akev* character of *tzafon* for the *chatat* established, the *gemara* turns to the word *oto* — "it" — that appears in the verse concerning *chatat nasi*. If the *oto* is not needed to restrict the scope of *tzafon* to only the *se'ir nasi* (since the subsequent verse broadens it to all *chataot*), what is the *oto* doing?

Before addressing that question, the *gemara* first considers what the *oto* might initially have seemed to teach. A *beraita* states: *oso b'tzafon v'ein se'ir nachshon b'tzafon* — "this" (the *chatat* of the *nasi*) is slaughtered in the north, but the *se'ir Nachshon* is not. What is the *se'ir Nachshon*? Ordinarily, an individual *chatat* takes a female animal. The one case in which an individual's *chatat* takes a male *se'ir* (goat) is the *nasi* — the prince who sins. But at the end of *parashat Naso*, the heads of each tribe (*nesi'im*) each brought a voluntary offering that included *se'ir izim echad l'chatas* — a male goat as a free-will offering designated as a *chatat*. This is an anomaly: a free-will *chatat*, something almost without parallel, brought under the unique circumstances of the *midbar*. Because *Nachshon ben Amminadav* was the first to bring, the whole series bears his name.

The *gemara* derives that the *se'ir Nachshon* does not require *tzafon*, since its status differs fundamentally from a standard obligatory *chatat nasi*.

### Se'ir Smicha Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: ״וְסָמַךְ יָדוֹ עַל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׂעִיר״ – לְרַבּוֹת שָׂעִיר נַחְשׁוֹן לִסְמִיכָה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְרַבּוֹת שְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לִסְמִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the reason that it is necessary for the Torah to exclude Nahshon's sin offering from the requirement of slaughter in the north is because **it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the sin offering of a king: **"And he shall place his hand upon the head of the goat,** and slaughter it in the place where they slaughter the burnt offering before the Lord; it is a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:24). The verse could have stated: Upon its head. The reason it adds "of the goat" is **to include** the **goat** brought as a sin offering by **Nahshon in** the requirement of **placing hands** on the head of an offering. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says:** The term "of the goat" serves **to include** the **goats** brought as sin offerings for communal **idol worship in** the requirement of **placing hands** on the head of an offering.

A further *beraita* introduces a related question. The verse says *v'samach yado al rosh hase'ir* — "and he shall lean his hand on the head of the *se'ir*." The extra word *hase'ir* (the definite article on the goat) rather than simply *rosho* (its head) is taken to include an additional category of *se'ir* under the requirement of *smicha* (the laying on of hands). Rabbi Yehuda holds that this inclusion is for the *se'ir Nachshon*: even though it does not require the north, it does require *smicha*. Rabbi Shimon holds that the inclusion is instead for the *se'ir* brought as a communal *chatat* when the *Sanhedrin* rules incorrectly in a matter involving *avoda zara*.

### Ravina Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִיתְרַבּוֹ לִסְמִיכָה – אִיתְרַבּוֹ נָמֵי לְצָפוֹן; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: It might **enter your mind to say** that **since** the sin offerings of the princes **are included in** the requirement of **placing hands, they are also included in** the requirement of being slaughtered in the **north.** Therefore, the term "it" **teaches us** that for the goat brought as a sin offering by Nahshon and the other princes there was no requirement of slaughter in the north.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא: הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה; לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina objects to this** interpretation: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who says that the offering of Nahshon required him to place his hands on the head of the animal. But **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, what is there to say?** Why should the Torah write the term "it," since there is no reason to assume that it would require slaughter in the north?

Ravina challenges the structure of the argument. The *gemara* has explained that we need the word *oto* to prevent us from assuming that the *se'ir Nachshon*, having been brought into the normal *chatat* framework for *smicha* (according to Rabbi Yehuda), should also be brought in for *tzafon*. Fine — this works for Rabbi Yehuda, who explicitly includes the *se'ir Nachshon* in *smicha* and therefore might have assumed the full set of *chatat* rules apply. But for Rabbi Shimon, who never includes the *se'ir Nachshon* in any standard procedure, there would have been no basis to assume *tzafon* applies in the first place — and therefore no need for *oto* to exclude it.

### Medorot Lo Gamrinan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב טָבִי לְרָבִינָא: וּלְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִי נִיחָא?! לְמַאי דְּאִיתְרַבִּי אִיתְרַבִּי, וּמַאי דְּלָא אִיתְרַבִּי לָא אִיתְרַבִּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Tavi, said to Ravina: And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, does it work out well?** Why is it necessary for the Torah to specifically exclude the offerings of the princes from the requirement of slaughter in the north? Why not say that **for that** for **which it was included,** i.e., placing hands on the head of an animal, **it was included, and** for **that which it was not included,** i.e., slaughter in the north, **it was not included.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, אִי לָא מַעֲטֵיהּ קְרָא – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא תֵּיתֵי בְּבִנְיַן אָב; אִם כֵּן, סְמִיכָה גּוּפַהּ תֵּיתֵי מִבִּנְיַן אָב! אֶלָּא מִדּוֹרוֹת לָא גָּמְרִינַן; הָכָא נָמֵי מִדּוֹרוֹת לָא גָּמְרִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **if the verse had not excluded** the offerings of the princes **I would say** that one could **derive** the requirement for slaughter in the north **via a paradigm** from all other sin offerings, **if so,** one could also **derive** the requirement for **placing hands** on the head of an animal **itself via** the same **paradigm. Rather,** the reason that the requirement of placing hands cannot be derived via a paradigm is that **we do not learn** the requirements of the sin offering of Nahshon, which was only for the time of the inauguration of the Tabernacle alone, **from** the requirement of sin offerings applicable to all **generations. So too,** the requirement of slaughter in the north cannot be derived via a paradigm because **we do not learn** the requirements of the sin offering of Nahshon **from** the requirement of sin offerings applicable to all **generations.**

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Tavi presses the challenge even further. Even according to Rabbi Yehuda, why would including the *se'ir Nachshon* for *smicha* create any implication that it requires *tzafon*? *L'mai d'israbu israbu, v'l'mai d'lo israbu lo israbu* — whatever is included is included, and whatever is not included is not. The inclusion for *smicha* is explicitly stated; there is no basis to extend it further. And if one argues that without the *oto*, we might have extended the rules via *binyan av*, Mar Zutra answers: *im kein smicha gufah tesei binyan av* — if ordinary inference could apply, we would not have needed a special verse to teach *smicha* in the first place. The very fact that a verse is required for *smicha* demonstrates that the *se'ir Nachshon* is its own category to which normal rules do not extend absent explicit inclusion.

The *gemara* resolves: *madoros lo gamrinan* — we do not derive laws for temporary offerings from laws that apply to offerings throughout the generations. Since the *se'ir Nachshon* was brought only as a *hora'as sha'ah* — a one-time ruling for that singular moment in the *midbar* — it constitutes its own category. The ordinary rules of *chatat* do not automatically apply, and nothing applies to it unless explicitly stated. This explains why a verse was needed for *smicha*, and equally, why no verse for *tzafon* means no *tzafon* is required.

### Oto Animal Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״אוֹתוֹ בַּצָּפוֹן״ – וְאֵין שׁוֹחֵט בַּצָּפוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the term "it" stated with regard to the sin offering of a king serves to teach that **it,** i.e., the animal, must be slaughtered **in the north** of the Temple courtyard, **but the one who slaughters** it does **not** need to stand **in the north** when he slaughters it.

The question thus returns: what does the word *oto* actually come to teach? The *gemara*'s answer: *oto b'tzafon v'ein shochet b'tzafon* — the *animal* must be in the north, but the person performing the *shechita* need not stand in the north. The *shochet* may stand in the south and slaughter across the line, provided the animal itself is in the northern section. This is an important practical *halacha* regarding the *avodah*.

### Rabbi Chiya Shochet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹחֵט – מִדְּרַבִּי אַחִיָּא נָפְקָא! דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אַחִיָּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְשָׁחַט אֹתוֹ עַל יֶרֶךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ צָפוֹנָה״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: The *halakha* of the one who **slaughters** the offering has already been **derived from** the statement **of Rabbi Aḥiyya, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Aḥiyya says:** The verse states with regard to the burnt offering: **"And he shall slaughter it on the side of the altar northward** before the Lord" (Leviticus 1:11). **Why** must **the verse state** the exclusionary term "it"?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ עוֹמֵד בַּצָּפוֹן וּמְקַבֵּל בַּצָּפוֹן, וְאִם עָמַד בַּדָּרוֹם וְקִיבֵּל בַּצָּפוֹן פָּסוּל; יָכוֹל אַף זֶה כֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֹתוֹ״ – אוֹתוֹ בַּצָּפוֹן, וְאֵין הַשּׁוֹחֵט בַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** He explains: **Since we have found** that the priest **stands in the north and collects** the blood from the neck of the animal **in the north, and if he stood in the south and collected** the blood **in the north** the offering is **disqualified,** one **might** have thought that this is **so also** with regard to **this** one who slaughters the offering. Therefore, **the verse states:** "And he shall slaughter **it,"** to teach that **it,** the animal, must be **in the north, but the one who slaughters** it is **not** required to be standing **in the north** of the Temple courtyard when he slaughters the animal. Therefore, the question must be addressed: What is derived from the exclusionary term "it" stated with regard to the sin offering of a king?

The *gemara* challenges this reading. The principle that the *shochet* need not stand in the north is already established through the *derasha* of Rabbi Chiya, derived from the *olah* passage: *v'shachat oso al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafonah* — the word *oso* by the *olah* teaches that the animal is in the north, but not necessarily the one slaughtering it. Rabbi Chiya's *beraita* articulates this explicitly: we might have thought that since the *kohen* must stand in the north for *kabbalah*, the same should apply to *shechita*; the word *oso* comes to exclude this. Since this is already known from the *olah*, and all *chatat* laws are derived from or linked to the *olah*, it is redundant to learn it again from the *oto* here.

### Ben Ohf Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, ״אוֹתוֹ בַּצָּפוֹן״ – וְאֵין בֶּן עוֹף בַּצָּפוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יְהֵא בֶּן עוֹף טָעוּן צָפוֹן? וְדִין הוּא; וּמָה בֶּן צֹאן, שֶׁלֹּא קָבַע לוֹ כֹּהֵן – קָבַע לוֹ צָפוֹן; בֶּן עוֹף, שֶׁקָּבַע לוֹ כֹּהֵן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּקְבַּע לוֹ צָפוֹן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֹתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather,** the term "it" stated with regard to the sin offering of a king serves to teach that **it,** a goat brought as a sin offering, must be slaughtered **in the north, but a young bird** brought as an offering does **not** need to be killed **in the north.** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that **a male bird requires** pinching of the nape in the **north** of the Temple courtyard. **And this** can be derived through **a logical inference: Just as a young sheep** brought as a burnt offering is an offering for **which** the Torah **did not fix** that **its** slaughter must be performed by **a priest** but nevertheless **fixed** that **its** slaughter must be in the **north,** with regard to **a young bird** brought as an offering, for **which** the Torah **did fix** that **its** killing must be performed by **a priest, is it not logical that** the Torah **should** also **fix its** slaughter in the **north?** Therefore, **the verse states "it,"** to exclude a male bird from the requirement of being killed in the north.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְבֶן צֹאן, שֶׁכֵּן קָבַע לוֹ כְּלִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the logical inference. One cannot derive the *halakha* of a bird offering from the *halakha* of a sheep offering, as **what** is notable **about a young** sheep offering? It is notable **in that** the Torah **fixed** the requirement that **it** be slaughtered with **a utensil,** i.e., a knife. A bird, by contrast, is killed by the priest using his fingernail. Therefore, the term "it" cannot serve to counter this derivation.

The *gemara* therefore suggests another function for *oto*: *oto b'tzafon v'ein ben ohf b'tzafon* — the *chatat* animal is in the north, but not a bird offering. One might have reasoned by *kal vachomer*: if a *ben tzon* (sheep), which does not involve the priest's body in slaughter but rather a knife, requires the north — surely a *ben ohf* (bird offering), whose *melika* does involve the priest's body, should require the north all the more. The word *oto* excludes this: only this category of *chatat* requires the north, not a bird. But the *gemara* rejects this too: the *kal vachomer* could equally be reversed — precisely because the bird requires the priest's body and not a knife, one might say it is less analogous to the standard slaughter requirements. The *hava amina* is not strong enough to require a verse to exclude it.

### Pesach Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, ״אוֹתוֹ בַּצָּפוֹן״ – וְאֵין פֶּסַח בַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather,** the term "it" stated with regard to the sin offering of a king serves to teach that **it,** the goat of the king, is slaughtered **in the north, but** the **Paschal offering** is **not** slaughtered **in the north.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יְהֵא פֶּסַח טָעוּן צָפוֹן? וְדִין הוּא; וּמָה עוֹלָה, שֶׁכֵּן לָא קָבַע לוֹ זְמַן לִשְׁחִיטָתוֹ – קָבַע לוֹ צָפוֹן; פֶּסַח, שֶׁקָּבַע לוֹ זְמַן לִשְׁחִיטָתוֹ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּקְבַּע לוֹ צָפוֹן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֹתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says:** One **might** have thought that **a Paschal offering requires** slaughter in the **north. And this** can be derived through **a logical inference: Just as a burnt offering** is an offering for **which** the Torah **did not fix a time for its slaughter** but **fixed** that **it** requires slaughter in the **north,** with regard to **a Paschal offering,** for **which** the Torah **fixed a time for its slaughter,** as it must be slaughtered in the afternoon of the fourteenth day of Nisan, **is it not logical that** the Torah **would fix** that **it** must be slaughtered in the **north?** Therefore, **the verse states "it,"** to exclude the Paschal offering from the requirement of slaughter in the north.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְעוֹלָה, שֶׁכֵּן כָּלִיל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the logical inference. One cannot derive the *halakha* of a Paschal offering from the *halakha* of a burnt offering, as **what** is notable **about a burnt offering?** It is notable **in that** the Torah teaches that **it is entirely** burned on the altar. This is not so with regard to a Paschal offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵחַטָּאת – מָה לְחַטָּאת, שֶׁכֵּן מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: If you would suggest a logical inference **from** the *halakha* of **a sin offering,** which is not entirely burned upon the altar but is slaughtered only in the north, this too can be refuted. As **what** is notable **about a sin offering?** It is notable **in that it atones for** those sins that render one **liable for** punishment by ***karet*,** which is not so with regard to a Paschal offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאָשָׁם – מָה לְאָשָׁם, שֶׁכֵּן קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים! מִכּוּלָּן נָמֵי – שֶׁכֵּן קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: If you would suggest a logical inference **from** the *halakha* of **a guilt offering,** which is not entirely burned, which does not atone for those sins punishable by *karet*, and which is slaughtered only in the north, this too can be refuted. As **what** is notable **about a guilt offering?** It is notable **in that** it has the status of **an offering of the most sacred order,** which is not so with regard to a Paschal offering. The Gemara adds: Having noted this distinction between a guilt offering and a Paschal offering, one can say that for **all of** the three offerings **as well,** the *halakha* of a Paschal offering cannot be derived **from them, since** they all have the status of **offerings of the most sacred order.**

Another suggestion: *oto b'tzafon v'ein pesach b'tzafon* — the *chatat* is in the north, but the *korban Pesach* is not. A *beraita* cited in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov constructs a *kal vachomer*: an *olah* has no fixed time for its slaughter, yet requires the north; the *korban Pesach*, which does have a fixed and obligatory time, should certainly require the north. The *gemara* again challenges: could the *korban Pesach* really have been thought to require *tzafon*? The *olah* is entirely burnt (*kalil*), so one cannot simply learn from it to a *Pesach* that is eaten by the owners. And one cannot learn from a *chatat* — it atones for sins punishable by *karet*. And the *asham* is *kodshei kodashim*. In each case, the *korban Pesach*, as *kodashim kalim*, is structurally different. Even learning from all of them together — *mikulan* — still does not overcome the fundamental distinction: everything that must be in the north is *kodshei kodashim*, whereas the *korban Pesach* is *kodashim kalim*. There was therefore never a strong enough basis to think *Pesach* required *tzafon*, and thus no verse is needed to exclude it.

### Oto Mekabel Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם כְּדַאֲמַרַן מֵעִיקָּרָא: ״אוֹתוֹ בַּצָּפוֹן״ – וְאֵין שׁוֹחֵט בַּצָּפוֹן. וּדְקַשְׁיָא לָךְ מִדְּרַבִּי אַחִיָּא נָפְקָא לַן – לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי שׁוֹחֵט בַּצָּפוֹן; אֶלָּא אֵין שׁוֹחֵט בַּצָּפוֹן, אֲבָל מְקַבֵּל בַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the earlier inference: **Actually,** the term "it" teaches **as we said initially: It,** i.e., the animal, must be standing **in the north, but** the **one who slaughters** the animal does **not** have to stand **in the north. And that** which is **difficult for you,** that **we derive** this *halakha* **from** the statement **of Rabbi Aḥiyya,** is in fact not difficult. The derivation from the term "it" is **not to exclude** one who **slaughters** from the requirement to slaughter **in the north,** since that *halakha* is known already from the statement of Rabbi Aḥiyya. **Rather,** the derivation is that it is only one who **slaughters** the animal who does **not** have to stand **in the north, but** by inference, the one who **collects** the blood from the neck of the animal must stand **in the north.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְקַבֵּל מִ״לָּקַח״–״וְלָקַח״ נָפְקָא! ״לָקַח״–״וְלָקַח״ לָא מַשְׁמַע לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this inference: The *halakha* that the one who **collects** the blood from the neck of the animal must stand in the north is derived **from** the fact that the Torah could have written: The priest **shall take,** and instead writes: **"And** the priest **shall take" [*velakaḥ*]** (Leviticus 4:34), which may be read as: He will take himself [*lo yikaḥ*], as explained on the previous *amud*. The Gemara explains: This *tanna* **does not learn** anything from this distinction between: The priest **shall take,** and: **"And** the priest **shall take."** He does not agree with this derivation, and therefore he must derive the requirement to collect the blood while standing in the north from a different verse.

The *gemara* therefore returns to its original answer: *le'olam k'de'amran me'ikara oto b'tzafon v'ein shochet b'tzafon* — the animal must be in the north, but not the *shochet*. And when the *gemara* objected that this is already known from the *olah* via Rabbi Chiya's *derasha*, the *gemara* clarifies: that objection is resolved. The *oto* here is not *primarily* teaching about the *shochet*. Rather — *ela ein shochet b'tzafon aval mekabel b'tzafon* — it is teaching a distinction between the two acts of the *avodah*: the exemption of the *shochet* from standing in the north does not extend to the *mekabel*. The *kohen* who receives the blood must stand in the north.

This is the final *halacha*: the *shochet* does not have to stand in the north (already known from the *olah*), and the *oto* here reinforces this to sharpen the contrast — namely, that this exemption applies *only* to *shechita*, not to *kabbalah*. For *kabbalah*, the *kohen* must stand in the north. The *gemara* notes that some derive the *mekabel* requirement from the *derasha* of *velakach* as *lo yikach*, but the *amora* here does not accept that reading, and instead derives it from the *oto*.

## Olah Tzafon Me'akev

### Olah Doubling Search

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שְׁחִיטָה בְּעוֹלָה – לְמִצְוָה, קַבָּלָה נָמֵי – לְמִצְוָה אַשְׁכְּחַן; שְׁחִיטָה וְקַבָּלָה לְעַכֵּב מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the requirement of **slaughter** in the north **with regard to a burnt offering** in order **to** perform the **mitzva** in the optimal manner. **We also found** a source for the requirement of **collecting** the blood while standing in the north in order **to** perform the **mitzva** in the optimal manner. **From where do we** derive that **slaughter and collection** of the blood are **disqualified** if they are not performed in the north?

The *gemara* now turns back to the *olah* itself. We have shown that *tzafon* is a *mitzvah* for the *olah*, and we have shown that *tzafon* is *me'akev* for the *chatat*. But is *tzafon* also *me'akev* for the *olah*? Specifically: *ashkechan shechita b'ola l'mitzvah, kabala nami l'mitzvah ashkechan* — we have found *shechita* and *kabbalah* in the north as a *mitzvah* for the *olah*. But where is the *doubling* that would render them *me'akev* for the *olah* as well?

### Kal Vachomer Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא: קַל וָחוֹמֶר; וּמָה חַטָּאת, הַבָּאָה מִכֹּחַ עוֹלָה – מְעַכֶּבֶת; עוֹלָה, שֶׁבָּאָה חַטָּאת מִכֹּחָהּ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמְּעַכֶּבֶת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava says, and some say** it was **Rabba bar Sheila** who says: It is derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as** the *halakha* that the slaughter and blood collection of **a sin offering** be performed in the north **comes by virtue of** a comparison to the *halakha* of **a burnt offering,** as the verse states: "And slaughter the sin offering in the place of the burnt offering" (Leviticus 4:29), yet if the animal was not slaughtered or its blood was not collected in the north it **disqualifies** the offering, with regard to the **burnt offering** itself, **as** the *halakha* that the slaughter and blood collection of **a sin offering** be in the north **comes by virtue of it, is it not logical that** if it was not slaughtered or its blood was not collected in the north, this should **disqualify** the offering?

The *gemara* proposes an answer in the name of Abba bar Abba, or perhaps Rava bar Shila: a *kal vachomer*. If the *chatat*, which derives its *tzafon* requirement from the *olah* — its entire rule about location is formulated as "slaughter it where you slaughter the *olah*" — if even the *chatat* has *tzafon* as *me'akev*, then certainly the *olah*, which is the source from which the *chatat* learns, must have *tzafon* as *me'akev*. The argument is structurally elegant: the derivative cannot exceed the source; if the derivative's *tzafon* is *me'akev*, the source's must be as well.

### Chatat Weightier Objecti…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְחַטָּאת, שֶׁכֵּן מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this logical inference: **What** is notable **about a sin offering?** It is notable **in that it atones for** those sins that render one **liable for** punishment by ***karet*,** which is not so with regard to a burnt offering.

The *gemara* challenges: *mah l'chatat shekein mechaperet al chayavei kiritot* — perhaps the *chatat* is weightier than the *olah* in this respect because it atones for sins punishable by *karet*, which is a weightier form of atonement than the *olah* provides. If the *chatat* is weightier, one cannot run the *kal vachomer* from *chatat* to *olah* so straightforwardly.

### Derivative Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה – כְּלוּם מָצִינוּ טָפֵל חָמוּר מִן הָעִיקָּל?!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: This** is what is **difficult for Rav Adda bar Ahava.** In other words, regardless of the notable characteristic of a sin offering, he still is of the opinion that his logical inference stands. **Have we ever found** that **a secondary** prohibition is more **stringent than a primary** prohibition? Since the *halakha* of a sin offering is derived via a comparison to the *halakha* of a burnt offering, the former cannot have stringencies that the latter lacks.

Rav Adda bar Ahava responds to the objection by sharpening the argument. He was not making an ordinary *kal vachomer* based on relative weightiness. He was making a structural claim: *klum matzinu tafel chamur min ha'ikar* — can something derived ever exceed the thing from which it was derived? This is not a question of which is more stringent in some independent comparison; it is a question of the nature of derivation itself. A *halakha* that is learned *from* another cannot, by the logic of derivation, impose more demanding conditions than its source. If the *olah* were not required to be in the north *me'akev*, the *chatat*, which is defined as "slaughter it where you slaughter the *olah*," could not have a *me'akev* requirement either. The *me'akev* character of the source must therefore hold.

### Ma'aser Sheni Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: וְלָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina: But** have we **not** found a secondary prohibition that is more stringent than the primary one?

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Mari challenges Rav Adda bar Ahava's principle with a counterexample from *ma'aser sheni*. *Ma'aser sheni* that has become *tamei* in Jerusalem may be redeemed. But if one has redeemed the *ma'aser sheni* onto money and then purchased food with that money in Jerusalem, and that food becomes *tamei*, it cannot be redeemed. According to Rabbi Yehuda it must be buried. Here, the derivative — the food purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money — is treated *more stringently* than the original *ma'aser sheni* itself. This appears to contradict the principle that a derivative cannot be weightier than its source.

### Stringency vs Weakness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָא אַלִּימָא קְדוּשָּׁתֵיהּ לְמִיתְפַּס פִּדְיוֹנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** the reason one may not redeem food that was purchased with second-tithe money and that then became ritually impure is not that the *halakha* is more stringent. Rather, there is a different reason. Since this food was merely purchased with second-tithe money, its **sanctity is not** as **strong** as the tithe itself and is unable **to grasp its redemption** money. Its status as second tithe is weak, and cannot be transferred to a third item. Consequently, if it becomes impure, according to Rabbi Yehuda it cannot be redeemed for money but must be buried, as an item that one is prohibited to derive benefit from.

The *gemara* distinguishes. The case of *ma'aser sheni* money does not represent a genuine stringency of the derivative. It represents a *weakness* of the derived *kedushah*. *Lo alima kedushatei l'mitfas pidyonah* — the *kedushah* of the purchased food is not strong enough to transfer to a further object through redemption. It is not that the purchased food is *more* sacred and therefore more restricted; it is that its *kedushah* is *weaker* — being a second-degree *kedushah* derived from derived money — and therefore lacks the power to extend itself further. The apparent stringency is in fact a manifestation of diminished sanctity.

By contrast, in the case of the *olah*, the question of *tzafon* being *me'akev* reflects the positive weight of the *kedushah* and the demands of the *avodah*. The *chatat*'s *me'akev* requirement for *tzafon* is a genuine expression of the substantive laws of *korbanot*, not a symptom of attenuated sanctity. Accordingly, Rav Adda bar Ahava's principle holds: a derivative cannot exhibit greater weight of this kind than its source. If the *chatat*'s *tzafon* is *me'akev*, the *olah*'s must be as well.
