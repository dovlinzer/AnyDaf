# Pesachim 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Levels of Tumah

### Six Levels of Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים אוֹמֵר: מִימֵיהֶם שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים לֹא נִמְנְעוּ מִלִּשְׂרוֹף אֶת הַבָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּטְמָא בִּוְולַד הַטּוּמְאָה עִם הַבָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּטְמָא בְּאַב הַטּוּמְאָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמּוֹסִיפִין טוּמְאָה עַל טוּמְאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Apropos the removal of leaven on Passover eve, including the consecrated loaves of thanks-offerings and *teruma*, the mishna cites a related *halakha*. **Rabbi Ḥanina the deputy High Priest says: In** all **the days of the priests, they did not refrain from burning meat that became ritually impure by** coming into contact with **a secondary source of ritual impurity,** i.e., an object that had come into contact with a primary source of impurity, together **with meat that became ritually impure by** contact with **a primary source of impurity.** They would do so **even though they** would thereby **add** a degree of **impurity to** the **impurity** of the first piece of meat, which was previously impure to a lesser degree.

The *mishnah* at the top of 14a opens a discussion about the various levels of *tuma* and whether one may mix items of different *tuma* levels when burning them. Before turning to the specific cases, it is worth reviewing the structure of *tuma* transmission. The highest level is the *avi avot hatuma* — the granddaddy of *tuma* — which is the dead body itself. There is a special extension of this category: metal that comes into contact with a corpse attains the same status as the corpse itself, as we will see later. Something that touches the dead body becomes an *av hatuma*. Something that touches the *av hatuma* becomes a *rishon letuma* — also called by the *mishnah* a *vlad hatuma*, the "child" of the higher level. From there the chain continues: *sheni*, *shlishi*, and *revi'i*. Not everything, however, can reach each of these lower levels. Generally speaking, only *ochlin u'mashkin* — food and liquids — transmit *tuma* at these lower levels, and at the further degrees perhaps only *teruma* or *kodshim* are affected at all.

The overarching problem the *mishnah* is addressing is this: when *kodshim*, *teruma*, or *chametz* must be burned and destroyed, is it permitted to mix items of different *tuma* levels together — thereby either increasing the *tuma* of one item or, in some cases, conveying *tuma* to something *tahor* in the first place? Because these items are being destroyed, perhaps the usual concern about increasing *tuma* does not apply.

### Rabbi Chanina Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹסִיף רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְאָמַר: מִימֵיהֶם שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים לֹא נִמְנְעוּ מִלְּהַדְלִיק אֶת הַשֶּׁמֶן שֶׁנִּפְסַל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם בְּנֵר שֶׁנִּטְמָא בִּטְמֵא מֵת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמּוֹסִיפִין טוּמְאָה עַל טוּמְאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva added** to the statement of Rabbi Ḥanina the deputy High Priest **and said: In** all **the days of the priests, they did not refrain from lighting** *teruma* **oil that was** ritually **disqualified by** coming into contact with **one who immersed himself during that day** and who does not become completely purified until nightfall **in a lamp that became ritually impure** with first-degree impurity **through** contact with one who became **ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse.** They did so **even though they** would thereby **add impurity to** the **impurity** of the oil. A person who immersed himself during that day assumes the status of second-degree ritual impurity. His contact renders the oil ritually impure with third-degree ritual impurity. The lamp with first-degree ritual impurity renders the oil ritually impure with second-degree impurity.

Rabbi Chanina *Sgan HaKohanim* — the vice *kohen gadol* — states: *mi-yemeihem shel kohanim lo nimneu milisrof et habasar she-nitma bivlad hatuma im habasar she-nitma be-av hatuma* — in all the days of the *kohanim*, they never refrained from burning meat that became *tamei* through a *vlad hatuma* (i.e., a *rishon letuma*, rendering it a *sheni letuma*) together with meat that became *tamei* through an *av hatuma* (i.e., a *rishon letuma*). This is permitted *af al pi she-mosifin tuma al tumato* — even though one is thereby increasing the *tuma* level — because the items are in any case being destroyed.

### Rabbi Akiva Case

Rabbi Akiva added a further case: *mi-yemeihem shel kohanim lo nimneu milahadlik et hashemen she-nifsal bitvul yom bener she-nitma betmei meit* — they also never refrained from lighting oil that had become *nifsal* through contact with a *tvul yom* together with a lamp that had become *tamei* through a person who was *tamei* through the dead body.

The word *pasul* rather than *tamei* is used for levels below *rishon letuma* because at those levels *tuma* is generally not transmitted onward to other objects. The oil that touched a *tvul yom* — a person who has immersed in a *mikvah* and is awaiting nightfall — is a *shlishi letuma*. The lamp that was made *tamei* by contact with a person who had touched a *tamei meit* is a *sheni letuma*. Rabbi Akiva similarly permits mixing these while burning, *af al pi she-mosifin tuma al tumato*.

## Rabbi Meir Inference

### Meir's Deduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם לָמַדְנוּ שֶׁשּׂוֹרְפִין תְּרוּמָה טְהוֹרָה עִם הַטְּמֵאָה בַּפֶּסַח. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֵינָהּ הִיא הַמִּדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir said: From their statements we learned that one** may **burn ritually pure *teruma* with impure** *teruma* when removing leaven **on Passover** eve. The rationale that applies to the two previous cases applies here as well. Since both items are being burned, one may disregard the fact that one item will assume a higher degree of ritual impurity in the process. **Rabbi Yosei said: That is not the inference** from which the *halakha* in the case of ritually pure and ritually impure *teruma* can be learned. In those first two cases, the two items are both ritually impure, albeit at different degrees of ritual impurity. Rabbi Meir is referring to the combination of impure *teruma* with pure *teruma*, which would render pure *teruma* ritually impure.

From the practices described by Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Akiva, Rabbi Meir draws a practical ruling: *she-sorfin teruma tehora im hatmeia b'Pesach* — on *Erev Pesach*, when *chametz* that is *teruma* must be burned, one may burn the *tahor* portion together with the *tamei* portion, without concern about conveying *tuma* to the *tahor teruma* in the process. The rationale is that since the items are being burned, we are unconcerned with increasing or transmitting *tuma*.

### Yossi Objection

Rabbi Yosei disagrees: *ein hi hamidda* — this is not a valid analogy. One cannot derive the rules governing *chametz* from the rules governing the burning of *teruma* or *kodshim*. The *gemara* will elaborate on why the two contexts are not comparable.

### Eliezer/Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדִים רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ שֶׁשּׂוֹרְפִין זוֹ לְעַצְמָהּ וְזוֹ לְעַצְמָהּ. עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — עַל הַתְּלוּיָה וְעַל הַטְּמֵאָה. שֶׁרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: תִּשָּׂרֵף זוֹ לְעַצְמָהּ וְזוֹ לְעַצְמָהּ, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: שְׁתֵּיהֶן כְּאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in fact **Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua,** who disagree with regard to the burning of leavened *teruma*, nevertheless **concede that one burns this** ritually pure *teruma* **by itself and that** impure *teruma* **by itself. With regard to what did they disagree?** They disagreed **with regard to** whether one may burn *teruma* **in abeyance,** i.e., *teruma* whose purity is uncertain, **and** definitely **impure** *teruma* together, **as Rabbi Eliezer says: This** *teruma* in abeyance **should be burned by itself, and that** impure *teruma* **should be burned by itself; and Rabbi Yehoshua says:** In that case, **both of them** may be burned **as one.**

The *mishnah* notes that despite their broader dispute, both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua agree with Rabbi Yosei's position that one must burn the *tamei* portion of *chametz teruma* separately from the *tahor* portion — *sorfin zo le'atzmah vezu le'atzmah*. Where do Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua themselves disagree? Their dispute concerns *teruma* in a state of *safek tumah* — doubtful *tuma* — and whether it may be burned together with *teruma* that is definitely *tamei*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even the *safek* must be burned separately; Rabbi Yehoshua holds that the *safek* and the definitely *tamei* may be burned together.

## Levels Analysis

### First Case Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִכְּדֵי בָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּטְמָא בִּוְולַד הַטּוּמְאָה מַאי הָוֵי — שֵׁנִי, כִּי שָׂרֵיף לֵיהּ בַּהֲדֵי בָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּטְמָא בְּאַב הַטּוּמְאָה מַאי הָוֵי — שֵׁנִי,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara analyzes the mishna's first statement: **Now** consider, **what is** the status of **meat that became ritually impure by** coming into contact with **a secondary source of impurity?** It assumes **second-**degree ritual impurity status. **When one burns** that meat together **with meat that became ritually impure by** coming into contact with **a primary source of ritual impurity, what is** the status of that first piece of meat? It assumes **second-**degree ritual impurity status. Meat that touches a primary source of impurity assumes first-degree ritual impurity status, which transmits second-degree impurity to other meat.

The *gemara* now proceeds to analyze the precise *tuma* levels in each case of the *mishnah*. Regarding the first case — meat that became *tamei* through a *vlad hatuma* — the *gemara* asks: *mah havei*? What level is that? It is a *sheni letuma*. And the meat that became *tamei* through an *av hatuma* — what is that? That is a *rishon letuma*. When you burn them together, the *rishon* makes the *sheni* — a *sheni*. But the *sheni* was already a *sheni*. So in what sense is one adding *tuma* to the other? The novelty the *mishnah* is teaching appears elusive.

### Sheni Transmission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁנִי וְשֵׁנִי הוּא. מַאי מוֹסִיף לוֹ טוּמְאָה עַל טוּמְאָתוֹ אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: Since when the first piece of meat is placed next to the meat that came into contact with a primary source it assumes second-degree impurity, this **is** a case where the meat is with **second-**degree status, **and** through contact with the primary source it would assume **second-**degree status. In **what** sense **is there** a case of **adding impurity to its impurity** here? There is no change in the status of the first piece of meat at all.

One might wonder whether the problem the *mishnah* is addressing concerns the *rishon* being elevated in some way by contact with the *sheni*, or vice versa. The deeper issue the *gemara* is probing is whether *sheni letuma* can transmit *tuma* to other food at all — and if it cannot, then what precisely is the *chiddush* of burning them together?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָכָא בִּוְולַד וְולַד עָסְקִינַן, דְּהָוֵי לֵיהּ שְׁלִישִׁי, וְקָסָבַר שְׁלִישִׁי מוּתָּר לַעֲשׂוֹתוֹ שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** The above interpretation is incorrect, as **here we are dealing with** the **secondary source of a secondary source of ritual impurity,** i.e., meat that came in contact with second-degree ritual impurity. The statement in the mishna: That became ritually impure by coming into contact with a secondary source of ritual impurity, should not be understood as saying that it came into contact with meat with first-degree ritual impurity status, **as** in this case, the meat came into contact with meat with second-degree ritual impurity status and **is** impure with **third-**degree ritual impurity. **And** Rabbi Ḥanina the deputy High Priest **maintains** that **it is permitted to render** impure with **second-**degree impurity an object with **third-**degree ritual impurity by burning it with meat that came into contact with a primary source of ritual impurity.

A *baraita* is cited: *yakhol yiheyeh ochel metamei ochel* — one might think that at the level of *sheni letuma* food can make other food *tamei*. The *pasuk* teaches: *ve-chi yutan mayim al zera ve-nafal mi-nivlatam alav tamei hu* — when water falls on a seed and a *neveilah* comes in contact with it, it becomes *tamei*. The *derasha* drawn from the word *hu* is: *hu tamei ve-ein oseh kayotzei bo tamei* — that food itself becomes *tamei* but does not transmit its *tuma* to other food. Rather, at that level it can only make liquids *tamei*.

Rav Yehudah offers an emendation to the *mishnah*: *hacha bivlad vlad askinan de-havei lei shlishi* — the case actually involves not a *vlad hatuma* but a *vlad vlad hatuma*, meaning a *shlishi letuma*, and the ruling is that *shlishi mutar la'asoto sheni* — it is permitted to make a *shlishi* into a *sheni* when burning, since these items are being destroyed in any case.

### Ochel Transmission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֵין אוֹכֶל מְטַמֵּא אוֹכֶל! דְּתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יְהֵא אוֹכֶל מְטַמֵּא אוֹכֶל, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכִי יֻתַּן מַיִם עַל זֶרַע וְנָפַל מִנִּבְלָתָם עָלָיו טָמֵא הוּא״ — הוּא טָמֵא, וְאֵין עוֹשֶׂה כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Isn't** there a principle that **food does not transmit ritual impurity to** other **food, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: I **might** have thought that **food transmits impurity to** other **food;** therefore, **the verse states: "And if water is placed upon the seed, and any** part **of a carcass falls upon it, it is impure"** (Leviticus 11:38). The Sages derived from this verse: **It,** the food exposed to the source of impurity, **is impure, but it does not render similar** foods **impure.** Apparently, food does not transmit impurity to other food.

The *gemara* challenges this: *veha ein ochel metamei ochel* — but does not the principle that food does not transmit *tuma* to food apply here? At the level of *shlishi letuma*, food cannot make other food *tamei* — it can only affect liquids.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְאַבָּיֵי דְּאָמַר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּחוּלִּין, אֲבָל בִּתְרוּמָה וְקׇדָשִׁים — עוֹשֶׂה כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **This** works out **well according to** the opinion of **Abaye, who said: They taught** this principle that food does not transmit ritual impurity to other food **only with regard to non-sacred** food; **however, with regard to *teruma* and consecrated** food, food **transmits** impurity to other foods it touches, and **it renders** the *teruma* or consecrated food **similar to it** in terms of impurity.

Two Amoraic positions address this difficulty. Abaye holds: *lo shanu ela be-chulin* — the rule that food does not transmit *tuma* to food applies only to *chulin*. But with *teruma* and *kodshim*, food does transmit *tuma* to food. According to Abaye's view, the *mishnah* is discussing *kodshim*, and the *chiddush* is that even in this context — where the transmission is real and significant — one is permitted to burn them together. Rav Adda bar Ahava (citing Rava) goes one step further: this limitation applies to *chulin* and *teruma*, but *kodshim* food can transmit *tuma* to other *kodshim* food. Either way, the *mishnah* is dealing with *kodshim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרָבִינָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא דְּאָמַר: מִקְרָא מָלֵא דִּיבֵּר הַכָּתוּב, לָא שְׁנָא חוּלִּין, לָא שְׁנָא תְּרוּמָה, לָא שְׁנָא קָדָשִׁים — אֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ. מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **However,** this is not the case **according to** the opinion stated by **Ravina in the name of Rava, who said: The Torah stated** this principle in **a categorical verse,** without any exceptions, meaning it is **no different** with regard to **non-sacred** food, and it is **no different** with regard to ***teruma*, and** it is **no different** with regard to **consecrated** food, as in all of these cases one type of food **does not render** other food **similar to it** in terms of impurity. According to this opinion, **what can be said** in terms of understanding the statement in the mishna: Even though they thereby add impurity to its impurity?

A further position is attributed to Ravina, also citing Rava: *mikra malei divrei ha-kasuv* — the verse is comprehensive, and food does not transmit *tuma* to food regardless of category — whether *chulin*, *teruma*, or *kodshim*. According to Ravina, the *mishnah* cannot be explained by reference to food-to-food transmission at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן? דְּאִיכָּא מַשְׁקִין בַּהֲדֵי בָּשָׂר, דְּקָא מִיטַּמֵּא מֵחֲמַת מַשְׁקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers in defense of this opinion: **With what are we dealing here?** It is with a case **where there are liquids with the meat** when it comes into contact with the primary source of ritual impurity. Since the other piece of meat comes into contact with the liquid on that meat, **it becomes impure due to** contact with **the liquid.** Although food does not transmit impurity to food, liquid transmits impurity to food.

The *gemara* answers: *hacha mai askinan de-ika mashkin bahadi basar* — the *mishnah* is discussing meat that has liquid on its surface. The rule that food does not transmit *tuma* to food does not apply to liquids; food transmits *tuma* to liquids readily. Therefore, the *chiddush* is that even though by burning the meat together one is making the liquid *tamei* at a higher level, it is permitted because they are being destroyed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַאי ״עִם הַבָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּטְמָא בְּאַב הַטּוּמְאָה״ — ״עִם הַבָּשָׂר וּמַשְׁקִין״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא: נְהִי דְּאֵין אוֹכֶל מְטַמֵּא אוֹכֶל מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מִדְּרַבָּנַן מִיהוּ מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so, this** phrase: **With meat that became ritually impure by** contact with **a primary source of impurity,** is imprecise. The *tanna* **should have** said: **With meat and liquids,** as the liquids are essential for the transmission of impurity. **Rather,** the Gemara explains: **Although food does not transmit impurity to** other **food by Torah law, in any event, by rabbinic law,** food **transmits impurity** to other food. The mishna is based on the rabbinic decree that food transmits impurity to other food.

The *gemara* raises a stylistic question: if the case depends on the presence of liquid on the meat's surface, why does the *mishnah* not mention the liquid explicitly? The *gemara* ultimately resolves this by proposing: *ella nehi de-ein ochel mitamei ochel mi-de-oraita, aval mi-derabbanan mihu mitamei* — granted that *mi-de-oraita* food does not transmit *tuma* to food at the *sheni* level, nonetheless *mi-derabbanan* such transmission does exist. It is this *de-rabbanan* concern that the *mishnah* is addressing, and it is telling us that because these items are being burned, we need not be concerned even with that *de-rabbanan* transmission.

## Oil & Lamp Case

### Oil/Lamp Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹסִיף רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִימֵיהֶן שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים לֹא נִמְנְעוּ מִלְּהַדְלִיק כּוּ׳. מִכְּדֵי שֶׁמֶן שֶׁנִּפְסַל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם מַאי הָוֵי — שְׁלִישִׁי. וְכִי מַדְלֵיק לֵיהּ בְּנֵר שֶׁנִּטְמָא בִּטְמֵא מֵת מַאי הָוֵי — שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the mishna that **Rabbi Akiva added: In** all **the days of the priests, they did not refrain from lighting** *teruma* oil that was ritually disqualified by coming into contact with one who immersed himself during that day, in a lamp that was rendered ritually impure with first-degree impurity through contact with one who became ritually impure with impurity imparted by a corpse. The Gemara asks: **Now** consider, **what is** the status of **oil that was disqualified by one who immersed himself during that day?** As one who immersed himself during that day assumes second-degree impurity, the oil that he touches assumes **third-**degree ritual impurity status. **And when he lights it in a lamp that was rendered ritually impure through** contact with one who became **ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse,** who has first-degree impurity status, **what is** the impurity status of the oil? It assumes **second-**degree ritual impurity status.

The *gemara* now turns to analyze the second case in the *mishnah* — that of Rabbi Akiva. The oil that became *nifsal* through a *tvul yom*: *mah havei*? It is a *shlishi letuma*. The *tvul yom* is himself a *sheni letuma*, and what he touches becomes a *shlishi*. And the lamp *she-nitma betmei meis* — that became *tamei* through a person who had contact with the dead: *mah havei*? Here the *mishnah* means that the lamp touched someone who had himself touched a *tamei meit* — that person is a *rishon letuma*, and the lamp he touched is a *sheni letuma*. So Rabbi Akiva's case involves oil that is a *shlishi* and a lamp that is a *sheni*. The apparent *chiddush* would be that *shlishi mutar la'asoto sheni* — one may elevate a *shlishi* to a *sheni* in the process of burning. But the *gemara* asks: *hainu hach* — is this not the same as what we already learned from the meat case? What does Rabbi Akiva add?

### Metal Lamp Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן — שְׁלִישִׁי מוּתָּר לַעֲשׂוֹתוֹ שֵׁנִי, הַיְינוּ הָךְ?! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָכָא בְּנֵר שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת עָסְקִינַן, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר:
>
> **Translation:** If so, **what is** Rabbi Akiva **teaching us** by this *halakha*? This statement apparently teaches us that with regard to an object that is ritually impure with **third-**degree impurity status, **it is permitted to render it** impure with **second-**degree impurity status. Yet **this is** the same *halakha* as **that** which was taught by Rabbi Ḥanina the deputy High Priest. What is novel about the *halakha* taught by Rabbi Akiva? **Rav Yehuda said: Here, we are dealing with a metal lamp,** which has a unique halakhic status. **As the Merciful One states:**

Rabbi Yehudah explains: *hacha bener shel matechet askinan* — here we are dealing with a metal lamp. And metal has a special halakhic status that changes the calculus entirely.

### Metal Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* ״בַּחֲלַל חֶרֶב״. חֶרֶב הֲרֵי הוּא כֶּחָלָל. וְהָוְיָא לַיהּ אַב הַטּוּמְאָה, וְקָסָבַר שְׁלִישִׁי מוּתָּר לַעֲשׂוֹתוֹ רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** "And whoever touches **one who is slain with a sword** in the open field, or one who dies on his own, or a bone of a man, or a grave, shall be unclean seven days" (Numbers 19:16). The Sages derived from the phrase: One who is slain with a sword, that the legal status of a metal **sword** in terms of its degree of impurity **is like** that of **one who is slain.** Any metal vessel that becomes impure through contact with a corpse assumes the impurity status of a corpse, the ultimate primary source of ritual impurity. The same is true with regard to a metal vessel that came into contact with a person or vessel that became impure with impurity imparted by a corpse. In that case the metal vessel assumes the impurity status of that person or vessel, **and** therefore, **this** metal lamp **is a primary source of impurity. And** yet Rabbi Akiva **maintains** that **it is permitted to render** this oil, which is impure with **third-**degree impurity, impure with **first-**degree impurity through contact with the metal lamp.

The *gemara* explains immediately: *drachmana amar bechalal cherev, cherev harei hu kechalal* — the Torah, in describing the *tuma* of the dead, uses the phrase "through a sword," and from this we derive that the sword and the *chalal* (the corpse) are on the same level. Metal that touches a corpse does not descend a level — it attains *av hatuma* status in its own right. By extension, this principle is generalized to all metal: metal attains the same *tuma* level as what it touches, rather than descending one level as food or vessels ordinarily do.

In Rabbi Akiva's case, a person who touched the dead body is a *rishon letuma*. The metal lamp that this *rishon* touched becomes — not a *sheni* as one might expect — but itself a *rishon letuma*, because metal jumps up a category. With the oil being a *shlishi letuma*, burning the two together does not merely jump one level — it jumps two levels. That is the *chiddush* Rabbi Akiva adds over Rabbi Chanina's case.

### Rava Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי דּוּחְקֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה לְאוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּנֵר שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת, נוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּנֵר שֶׁל חֶרֶס, וּמַאי ״הוֹסִיף״ — דְּאִילּוּ הָתָם טָמֵא וְטָמֵא, וְאִילּוּ הָכָא פָּסוּל וְטָמֵא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what impelled Rav Yehuda to establish** the mishna as referring specifically to the case **of a metal lamp? Let him establish it** as referring specifically to the case **of an earthenware lamp.** **And** if so, **what does** Rabbi Akiva's statement **add?** The Gemara answers: **Whereas there,** in Rabbi Ḥanina's testimony, he is referring to a case where one piece of **ritually impure** meat came into contact with another piece of **impure** meat, **here,** in Rabbi Akiva's testimony, he is referring to a case where oil that is **disqualified** came into contact with a lamp with first-degree impurity status, rendering the oil **impure.** Oil with second-degree ritual impurity status disqualifies *teruma*, as *teruma* with third-degree ritual impurity status does not transmit ritual impurity to other *teruma*. In that case, the novelty in Rabbi Akiva's statement is that a disqualified item is burned together with an impure item even though it is thereby rendered impure.

Rava proposes an alternative approach that does not require specifying a metal lamp. One could explain the *mishnah* as dealing with an ordinary clay lamp, and the *chiddush* of Rabbi Akiva would then be different: *deilu hatam tamei vetamei* — in Rabbi Chanina's first case, both items are in the category of *tamei* — one is a *rishon* and the other is a *sheni*. *Veilu hacha pasul vetamei* — but here in Rabbi Akiva's case, one is *pasul* (the *shlishi*) and one is *tamei* (the *sheni*). Even though the jump is only one level, it crosses a qualitative category — from the realm of *pasul*, which does not transmit *tuma* to other things, into the realm of *tamei*, which does. That categorical crossing is itself a *chiddush*.

### Meis Specification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, מַתְנִיתִין קְשִׁיתֵיהּ: מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי נֵר שֶׁנִּטְמָא בִּטְמֵא מֵת? נִיתְנֵי שֶׁנִּטְמָא בְּשֶׁרֶץ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The mishna was difficult for** Rav Yehuda: **Why** did the *tanna* **specifically teach** the case of **a lamp that became ritually impure** with first-degree impurity **through** contact with one who became **ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse? Let it teach that** the lamp **became impure by** contact with **a creeping animal,** which is a much more common primary source of impurity.

However, Rava notes that this alternative reading runs into a difficulty from the *mishnah* itself: *matnitin kashya lei*. The *mishnah* specifies *ner she-nitma betamei meis* — a lamp that became *tamei* through a *tamei meit*. Why specify *tumas meit* in particular? Let the *mishnah* have used *tumas sheretz* — impurity from a creeping creature — which would work equally well if we were only talking about a one-level categorical jump. The fact that the *mishnah* specifically chooses *tumas meit* indicates that something unique about *tumas meit* is at play. And what is uniquely different about *tumas meit* as opposed to *tumas sheretz*? Metal — since metal uniquely attains *av hatuma* status when it touches a corpse, but not when it touches a *sheretz*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: אֵיזֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁחֲלוּקָה טוּמְאָתוֹ בֵּין טוּמְאַת מֵת לְשֶׁרֶץ — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה מַתֶּכֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is the substance** with regard to **which** there is **a distinction between its impurity** when exposed to **impurity** imparted by **a corpse** and its impurity when exposed to impurity imparted by a **creeping animal? You must say** that the substance **is metal.** A metal vessel that comes into contact with a creeping animal assumes first-degree ritual impurity status, whereas if it comes into contact with a person or a vessel that came into contact with a corpse, it becomes a primary source of impurity.

Therefore, *ela eizehu davar she-chaluka tumato bein tumas meit le-sheretz hevei omer zeh mateschet* — the distinction between *tumas meit* and *tumas sheretz* points to metal. This confirms Rabbi Yehudah's reading: the *mishnah* is discussing a metal lamp, and Rabbi Akiva's *chiddush* is the two-level jump.

### Liquids Transmission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קָסָבַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין לְטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּרַבָּנַן, מִכְּדִי הַאי נֵר מַאי קָא מַהְנְיָא לְהַאי שֶׁמֶן, אִי לְאִיפְּסוֹלֵי גּוּפֵיהּ — הָא פְּסִיל וְקָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Learn from** this statement that **Rabbi Akiva holds: The ritual impurity of liquids** with regard **to transmitting impurity to other** objects is **by Torah law,** contrary to those *tanna'im* who hold that liquids transmit impurity only by rabbinic decree. **As, if it enters your mind** that this type of impurity is **by rabbinic law, now, this lamp, what effect does** this lamp **have on that oil? If** it is to **disqualify** the oil **itself, it is** already **disqualified from the outset.** Rather, Rabbi Akiva evidently maintains that through contact with the lamp this oil becomes impure and transmits impurity to food by Torah law.

Rava draws a further inference from this: *shma mina kasvar Rabbi Akiva tumas mashkin letamei acherim de-oraita* — from this case we can conclude that Rabbi Akiva holds the *tuma* of liquids to transmit *tuma* to other objects is *de-oraita*. The reasoning: what exactly is the metal lamp doing to the oil in this case? If the lamp's function were merely to render the oil *pasul* — unfit for temple use — the oil is already *pasul* and the lamp adds nothing. The lamp must therefore be conveying genuine *tuma* to the oil, elevating it from *shlishi* to *sheni*. This transmission would have to pass through the liquid surface of the oil, and for that to be significant it must be *de-oraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא לְטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים מִדְּרַבָּנַן. אִי מִדְּרַבָּנַן, מַאי אִירְיָא בְּאַב הַטּוּמְאָה? אֲפִילּוּ בְּרִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי נָמֵי תְּחִלָּה הָוֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **From where** do you know that this is Rabbi Akiva's opinion? **Perhaps** Rabbi Akiva holds that through contact with the lamp, the oil will be able **to transmit ritual impurity to other** objects **by rabbinic law.** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **If** the oil confers impurity **by rabbinic law, why** does Rabbi Akiva refer **particularly** to a case where the oil became impure **by** contact with **a primary source of impurity?** If Rabbi Akiva sought to cite an example of rabbinic impurity, he could have cited **even** a case where the oil came into contact **with** an object with **first-**degree impurity status, **or** an item with **second-**degree impurity status. By rabbinic law, in those cases **too,** the oil **is** impure with **first-**degree ritual impurity and transmits impurity to food.

The *gemara* interjects: perhaps the transmission is only *de-rabbanan*, not *de-oraita*? The response: *im miderabbanan mai irya be'av hatumah afilu berishon uvesheni nami* — if we were dealing entirely with *de-rabbanan* concerns, why would the *mishnah* need to specify a case involving the *av hatuma* derived from *tumas meit*? The entire structure of *rishon* and *sheni* is *de-rabbanan* in the first place, so if Rabbi Akiva's case were also *de-rabbanan*, the specific choice of *av hatuma* would be unmotivated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַפּוֹסֵל אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה — מְטַמֵּא מַשְׁקִין לִהְיוֹת תְּחִלָּה, חוּץ מִטְּבוּל יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the source for that *halakha*. **As we learned** in a mishna: **Any** item that **disqualifies *teruma*,** e.g., anything with second-degree ritual impurity status, **transmits impurity to liquids,** conferring upon them **first-**degree ritual impurity status. These liquids assume a higher degree of impurity than the item that rendered them impure. This rabbinic decree applies to anything with second-degree ritual impurity status **except** for one **who** was impure and **immersed himself during that day** and the sun has not yet set. If such a person touches liquids, he does not confer upon them first-degree impurity status. Instead, that case conforms to the standard process of transmission of ritual impurity, and he confers upon them third-degree ritual impurity status and invalidates them.

The *gemara* confirms this from a *mishnah* elsewhere: *kol ha-posel et ha-teruma metamei mashkin lihyot techila* — anything that renders *teruma* unfit can make liquids *tamei* as a *rishon*, *chutz mi-tvul yom* — except for a *tvul yom* which is an exception.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Rather, learn from** the fact that Rabbi Akiva did not cite the example of oil that became impure through contact with an item with first or second-degree ritual impurity that Rabbi Akiva holds that the *halakha* that liquids transmit impurity to other items **is by Torah law.**

Therefore, we must be speaking of *de-oraita* transmission, and Rava's conclusion stands.

## Chametz Learning

### Meir from Chanina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם לָמַדְנוּ וְכוּ׳. מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא מִדִּבְרֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים, מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם טָמֵא וְטָמֵא, הָכָא טָהוֹר וְטָמֵא!
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that **Rabbi Meir said: From their statements we learned** that one may burn ritually pure *teruma* with impure *teruma* when removing leaven on Passover eve. The Gemara asks: **From whose statements** was this conclusion inferred? **If you say** that this conclusion is inferred **from the statement of Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, is** Rabbi Meir's statement **comparable** to that case? **There,** Rabbi Ḥanina said that one may burn one **ritually impure** item **and** another **ritually impure** item together, whereas **here,** Rabbi Meir is referring to burning **pure and impure** *teruma* together.

The *gemara* now returns to Rabbi Meir's attempt to derive a ruling about *chametz* from the cases of Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Akiva. From which case does Rabbi Meir learn? If one suggests it is from Rabbi Chanina's case of the meat — *mi domei*? — is it analogous? *Hasam tamei vetamei, hacha tahor vetamei* — there, both items were *tamei*, merely at different levels; here, one item is *tahor* entirely and one is *tamei*. The case of the meat cannot serve as the source, because Rabbi Chanina speaks only of changing *tuma* levels, not of conveying *tuma* to something *tahor* in the first place.

### Meir from Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מִדִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם פָּסוּל וְטָמֵא, הָכָא טָהוֹר וְטָמֵא!
>
> **Translation:** **But rather,** Rabbi Meir's conclusion is inferred **from the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Is it comparable** to that case? **There,** Rabbi Akiva said that a **disqualified** item and an **impure** item may be burned together, whereas **here,** Rabbi Meir is referring to burning a **pure** item and an **impure** item together.

What then of Rabbi Akiva's case of the oil and lamp? Again — *mi domei*? *Hasam pasul vetamei, hacha tahor vetamei*. Rabbi Akiva's case similarly involves changing the degree of *tuma* — from *pasul* to *tamei* — not the introduction of *tuma* to something that was *tahor*. That too cannot directly prove Rabbi Meir's point.

### Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא קָסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר מַתְנִיתִין בְּאַב הַטּוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא וְולַד הַטּוּמְאָה דְּרַבָּנַן, דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא טָהוֹר מְעַלְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **Rabbi Meir maintains** that **the mishna** is referring **to** an object that is **a primary source of impurity by Torah law and** an object that is **a secondary source of impurity by rabbinic law, which by Torah law is entirely pure.** Since the *teruma* is pure by Torah law, the novelty of Rabbi Meir's statement is that although by Torah law one of the foods is pure and the other is impure, due to the rabbinic decree of impurity, one may burn the two items together.

The *gemara* therefore proposes a way to salvage Rabbi Meir's inference: *neima kasavar Rabbi Meir matnitin be-avi tumah de-oraita ve-vlad ha-tumah de-rabbanan* — perhaps Rabbi Meir holds that the *av hatuma* discussed in the *mishnah* is a genuine *de-oraita* level of *tuma*, while the *vlad hatuma* — the *rishon letuma* — is only *de-rabbanan*. On this reading, *de-midoraita* the corresponding piece of *teruma chametz* that is a *vlad hatuma* is essentially *tahor* — the *de-rabbanan* status does not constitute real *tuma* in a *de-oraita* sense. And it is from Rabbi Chanina's case that Rabbi Meir learns: just as the *kohanim* would burn meat that is *av hatuma* together with what is, in *de-oraita* terms, *tahor* (*vlad hatuma* being only *de-rabbanan*), so too one may burn *chametz teruma* that is genuinely *tamei* together with *chametz teruma* whose *tuma* is only *de-rabbanan* — and in *de-oraita* terms is *tahor*.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם בַּר קַפָּרָא: מַתְנִיתִין בְּאַב הַטּוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא וְולַד הַטּוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וּמַאי ״מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם״, מִדִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said** another explanation of the mishna **in the name of bar Kappara: The** case in the **mishna** is one involving **a primary source of ritual impurity by Torah law and a secondary source of impurity by Torah law. And what** did Rabbi Meir mean by the phrase: **From their statements?** He was not referring to the *tanna'im* in this mishna, but rather: **From the statements of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua** cited elsewhere.

Reish Lakish, citing Bar Kappara, takes a different position: *matnitin be-avi tumah de-oraita ve-vlad ha-tumah de-oraita* — the *mishnah* discusses levels of *tuma* that are both *de-oraita*, and Rabbi Meir learns from the end of the *mishnah* — from the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. Those *tanna'im* agree that definitely *tahor* and definitely *tamei* *teruma* must be burned separately on *Erev Pesach*; their dispute is only about *safek tumah* burned together with definite *tamei*. Reish Lakish reads Rabbi Meir as learning from the position adopted by Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua.

### Which Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ? אִילֵּימָא הָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דִּתְנַן: חָבִית שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה שֶׁנּוֹלַד לָהּ סְפֵק טוּמְאָה, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיְתָה מוּנַּחַת בִּמְקוֹם הַתּוּרְפָּה, יַנִּיחֶנָּה בְּמָקוֹם הַמּוּצְנָע, וְאִם הָיְתָה מְגוּלָּה — יְכַסֶּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: To **which** statement of **Rabbi Yehoshua** is Rabbi Meir referring? **If you say** he is referring to **this** statement of **Rabbi Yehoshua, as we learned** in a mishna: In the case of **a barrel of *teruma*** produce **with regard to which uncertainty developed** with regard to its **impurity,** and which therefore may not be eaten, **Rabbi Eliezer says** that one must nevertheless safeguard the *teruma* from ritual impurity. Therefore, he maintains: **If** the barrel **was resting in a vulnerable place,** where it may come into contact with impurity, **one** should **place it in a concealed place, and if it was exposed, he** should **cover it.**

Which Rabbi Yehoshua does Reish Lakish have in mind? The *gemara* raises the possibility that it is the Rabbi Yehoshua of a *mishnah* elsewhere, who rules that if one has a barrel of *teruma* in a state of *safek tumah*, one should place it in the open and allow it to become definitely *tamei* — rather than continuing to protect it. This implies that it is better to give the *teruma* a definite *tuma* status so that it can be properly burned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיְתָה מוּנַּחַת בְּמָקוֹם הַמּוּצְנָע — יַנִּיחֶנָּה בִּמְקוֹם הַתּוּרְפָּה, וְאִם הָיְתָה מְכוּסָּה — יְגַלֶּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** That is not necessary. Rather, even **if it was placed in a concealed place,** he may **place it in a vulnerable place** if he chooses. **And if it was covered, he** may **expose it,** as he need no longer safeguard this *teruma* from impurity. According to Rabbi Yehoshua, as *teruma* whose impurity status is uncertain, may be used only for lighting a fire, there is no requirement to prevent it from contact with ritual impurity. The same reasoning applies to pure leaven: One is not required to safeguard it from impurity in the process of its removal.

However, the *gemara* objects: *hasam grama b'alma* — there, Rabbi Yehoshua is speaking of only indirect causation, placing the barrel where it is likely to contract *tuma* rather than actively conveying *tuma*. *Hacha b'yadayim* — here we are speaking of actively mixing *tahor* and *tamei* together by hand. The two cases are not analogous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם — גְּרָמָא בְּעָלְמָא. הָכָא — בְּיָדַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the comparison: **Is** this dispute with regard to the placement of doubtfully impure *teruma* **comparable** to the case of burning ritually pure and impure items together? **There,** Rabbi Yehoshua permits **mere** passive **causation** of impurity; however, he does not permit one to actively render *teruma* whose impurity status is uncertain, impure. **Here,** however, in the statement of Rabbi Meir, he actively renders leavened *teruma* **impure with** his **hands.**

The question of precisely which statement of Rabbi Yehoshua Reish Lakish intends will require further analysis in the continuation of the *gemara*.
