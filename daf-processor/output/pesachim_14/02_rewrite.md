# Pesachim 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Levels of Tumah

### Six Levels of Tumah

The *mishnah* at the top of 14a opens a discussion about the various levels of *tuma* and whether one may mix items of different *tuma* levels when burning them. Before turning to the specific cases, it is worth reviewing the structure of *tuma* transmission. The highest level is the *avi avot hatuma* — the granddaddy of *tuma* — which is the dead body itself. There is a special extension of this category: metal that comes into contact with a corpse attains the same status as the corpse itself, as we will see later. Something that touches the dead body becomes an *av hatuma*. Something that touches the *av hatuma* becomes a *rishon letuma* — also called by the *mishnah* a *vlad hatuma*, the "child" of the higher level. From there the chain continues: *sheni*, *shlishi*, and *revi'i*. Not everything, however, can reach each of these lower levels. Generally speaking, only *ochlin u'mashkin* — food and liquids — transmit *tuma* at these lower levels, and at the further degrees perhaps only *teruma* or *kodshim* are affected at all.

The overarching problem the *mishnah* is addressing is this: when *kodshim*, *teruma*, or *chametz* must be burned and destroyed, is it permitted to mix items of different *tuma* levels together — thereby either increasing the *tuma* of one item or, in some cases, conveying *tuma* to something *tahor* in the first place? Because these items are being destroyed, perhaps the usual concern about increasing *tuma* does not apply.

### Rabbi Chanina Case

Rabbi Chanina *Sgan HaKohanim* — the vice *kohen gadol* — states: *mi-yemeihem shel kohanim lo nimneu milisrof et habasar she-nitma bivlad hatuma im habasar she-nitma be-av hatuma* — in all the days of the *kohanim*, they never refrained from burning meat that became *tamei* through a *vlad hatuma* (i.e., a *rishon letuma*, rendering it a *sheni letuma*) together with meat that became *tamei* through an *av hatuma* (i.e., a *rishon letuma*). This is permitted *af al pi she-mosifin tuma al tumato* — even though one is thereby increasing the *tuma* level — because the items are in any case being destroyed.

### Rabbi Akiva Case

Rabbi Akiva added a further case: *mi-yemeihem shel kohanim lo nimneu milahadlik et hashemen she-nifsal bitvul yom bener she-nitma betmei meit* — they also never refrained from lighting oil that had become *nifsal* through contact with a *tvul yom* together with a lamp that had become *tamei* through a person who was *tamei* through the dead body.

The word *pasul* rather than *tamei* is used for levels below *rishon letuma* because at those levels *tuma* is generally not transmitted onward to other objects. The oil that touched a *tvul yom* — a person who has immersed in a *mikvah* and is awaiting nightfall — is a *shlishi letuma*. The lamp that was made *tamei* by contact with a person who had touched a *tamei meit* is a *sheni letuma*. Rabbi Akiva similarly permits mixing these while burning, *af al pi she-mosifin tuma al tumato*.

## Rabbi Meir Inference

### Meir's Deduction

From the practices described by Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Akiva, Rabbi Meir draws a practical ruling: *she-sorfin teruma tehora im hatmeia b'Pesach* — on *Erev Pesach*, when *chametz* that is *teruma* must be burned, one may burn the *tahor* portion together with the *tamei* portion, without concern about conveying *tuma* to the *tahor teruma* in the process. The rationale is that since the items are being burned, we are unconcerned with increasing or transmitting *tuma*.

### Yossi Objection

Rabbi Yossi disagrees: *ein hi hamidda* — this is not a valid analogy. One cannot derive the rules governing *chametz* from the rules governing the burning of *teruma* or *kodshim*. The *gemara* will elaborate on why the two contexts are not comparable.

### Eliezer/Yehoshua

The *mishnah* notes that despite their broader dispute, both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua agree with Rabbi Yossi's position that one must burn the *tamei* portion of *chametz teruma* separately from the *tahor* portion — *sorfin zo le'atzmah vezu le'atzmah*. Where do Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua themselves disagree? Their dispute concerns *teruma* in a state of *safek tumah* — doubtful *tuma* — and whether it may be burned together with *teruma* that is definitely *tamei*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even the *safek* must be burned separately; Rabbi Yehoshua holds that the *safek* and the definitely *tamei* may be burned together.

## Levels Analysis

### First Case Levels

The *gemara* now proceeds to analyze the precise *tuma* levels in each case of the *mishnah*. Regarding the first case — meat that became *tamei* through a *vlad hatuma* — the *gemara* asks: *mah havei*? What level is that? It is a *sheni letuma*. And the meat that became *tamei* through an *av hatuma* — what is that? That is a *rishon letuma*. When you burn them together, the *rishon* makes the *sheni* — a *sheni*. But the *sheni* was already a *sheni*. So in what sense is one adding *tuma* to the other? The novelty the *mishnah* is teaching appears elusive.

### Sheni Transmission

One might wonder whether the problem the *mishnah* is addressing concerns the *rishon* being elevated in some way by contact with the *sheni*, or vice versa. The deeper issue the *gemara* is probing is whether *sheni letuma* can transmit *tuma* to other food at all — and if it cannot, then what precisely is the *chiddush* of burning them together?

A *baraita* is cited: *yakhol yiheyeh ochel metamei ochel* — one might think that at the level of *sheni letuma* food can make other food *tamei*. The *pasuk* teaches: *ve-chi yutan mayim al zera ve-nafal mi-nivlatam alav tamei hu* — when water falls on a seed and a *neveilah* comes in contact with it, it becomes *tamei*. The *derasha* drawn from the word *hu* is: *hu tamei ve-ein oseh kayotzei bo tamei* — that food itself becomes *tamei* but does not transmit its *tuma* to other food. Rather, at that level it can only make liquids *tamei*.

### Rav Yehudah View

Rav Yehudah offers an emendation to the *mishnah*: *hacha bivlad vlad askinan de-havei lei shlishi* — the case actually involves not a *vlad hatuma* but a *vlad vlad hatuma*, meaning a *shlishi letuma*, and the ruling is that *shlishi mutar la'asoto sheni* — it is permitted to make a *shlishi* into a *sheni* when burning, since these items are being destroyed in any case.

The *gemara* challenges this: *veha ein ochel metamei ochel* — but does not the principle that food does not transmit *tuma* to food apply here? At the level of *shlishi letuma*, food cannot make other food *tamei* — it can only affect liquids.

### Ochel Transmission

Two Amoraic positions address this difficulty. Abaye holds: *lo shanu ela be-chulin* — the rule that food does not transmit *tuma* to food applies only to *chulin*. But with *teruma* and *kodshim*, food does transmit *tuma* to food. According to Abaye's view, the *mishnah* is discussing *kodshim*, and the *chiddush* is that even in this context — where the transmission is real and significant — one is permitted to burn them together. Rav Adda bar Ahava (citing Rava) goes one step further: this limitation applies to *chulin* and *teruma*, but *kodshim* food can transmit *tuma* to other *kodshim* food. Either way, the *mishnah* is dealing with *kodshim*.

### Liquid Resolution

A further position is attributed to Ravina, also citing Rava: *mikra malei divrei ha-kasuv* — the verse is comprehensive, and food does not transmit *tuma* to food regardless of category — whether *chulin*, *teruma*, or *kodshim*. According to Ravina, the *mishnah* cannot be explained by reference to food-to-food transmission at all. The *gemara* answers: *hacha mai askinan de-ika mashkin bahadi basar* — the *mishnah* is discussing meat that has liquid on its surface. The rule that food does not transmit *tuma* to food does not apply to liquids; food transmits *tuma* to liquids readily. Therefore, the *chiddush* is that even though by burning the meat together one is making the liquid *tamei* at a higher level, it is permitted because they are being destroyed.

The *gemara* raises a stylistic question: if the case depends on the presence of liquid on the meat's surface, why does the *mishnah* not mention the liquid explicitly? The *gemara* ultimately resolves this by proposing: *ella nehi de-ein ochel mitamei ochel mi-de-oraita, aval mi-derabbanan mihu mitamei* — granted that *mi-de-oraita* food does not transmit *tuma* to food at the *sheni* level, nonetheless *mi-derabbanan* such transmission does exist. It is this *de-rabbanan* concern that the *mishnah* is addressing, and it is telling us that because these items are being burned, we need not be concerned even with that *de-rabbanan* transmission.

## Oil & Lamp Case

### Oil/Lamp Levels

The *gemara* now turns to analyze the second case in the *mishnah* — that of Rabbi Akiva. The oil that became *nifsal* through a *tvul yom*: *mah havei*? It is a *shlishi letuma*. The *tvul yom* is himself a *sheni letuma*, and what he touches becomes a *shlishi*. And the lamp *she-nitma betmei meis* — that became *tamei* through a person who had contact with the dead: *mah havei*? Here the *mishnah* means that the lamp touched someone who had himself touched a *tamei meit* — that person is a *rishon letuma*, and the lamp he touched is a *sheni letuma*. So Rabbi Akiva's case involves oil that is a *shlishi* and a lamp that is a *sheni*. The apparent *chiddush* would be that *shlishi mutar la'asoto sheni* — one may elevate a *shlishi* to a *sheni* in the process of burning. But the *gemara* asks: *hainu hach* — is this not the same as what we already learned from the meat case? What does Rabbi Akiva add?

### Metal Lamp Status

Rabbi Yehudah explains: *hacha bener shel matechet askinan* — here we are dealing with a metal lamp. And metal has a special halakhic status that changes the calculus entirely.

### Metal Rule

The *gemara* explains immediately: *drachmana amar bechalal cherev, cherev harei hu kechalal* — the Torah, in describing the *tuma* of the dead, uses the phrase "through a sword," and from this we derive that the sword and the *chalal* (the corpse) are on the same level. Metal that touches a corpse does not descend a level — it attains *av hatuma* status in its own right. By extension, this principle is generalized to all metal: metal attains the same *tuma* level as what it touches, rather than descending one level as food or vessels ordinarily do.

In Rabbi Akiva's case, a person who touched the dead body is a *rishon letuma*. The metal lamp that this *rishon* touched becomes — not a *sheni* as one might expect — but itself a *rishon letuma*, because metal jumps up a category. With the oil being a *shlishi letuma*, burning the two together does not merely jump one level — it jumps two levels. That is the *chiddush* Rabbi Akiva adds over Rabbi Chanina's case.

### Rava Alternative

Rava proposes an alternative approach that does not require specifying a metal lamp. One could explain the *mishnah* as dealing with an ordinary clay lamp, and the *chiddush* of Rabbi Akiva would then be different: *deilu hatam tamei vetamei* — in Rabbi Chanina's first case, both items are in the category of *tamei* — one is a *rishon* and the other is a *sheni*. *Veilu hacha pasul vetamei* — but here in Rabbi Akiva's case, one is *pasul* (the *shlishi*) and one is *tamei* (the *sheni*). Even though the jump is only one level, it crosses a qualitative category — from the realm of *pasul*, which does not transmit *tuma* to other things, into the realm of *tamei*, which does. That categorical crossing is itself a *chiddush*.

### Meis Specification

However, Rava notes that this alternative reading runs into a difficulty from the *mishnah* itself: *matnitin kashya lei*. The *mishnah* specifies *ner she-nitma betamei meis* — a lamp that became *tamei* through a *tamei meit*. Why specify *tumas meit* in particular? Let the *mishnah* have used *tumas sheretz* — impurity from a creeping creature — which would work equally well if we were only talking about a one-level categorical jump. The fact that the *mishnah* specifically chooses *tumas meit* indicates that something unique about *tumas meit* is at play. And what is uniquely different about *tumas meit* as opposed to *tumas sheretz*? Metal — since metal uniquely attains *av hatuma* status when it touches a corpse, but not when it touches a *sheretz*. Therefore, *ela eizehu davar she-chaluka tumato bein tumas meit le-sheretz hevei omer zeh mateschet* — the distinction between *tumas meit* and *tumas sheretz* points to metal. This confirms Rabbi Yehudah's reading: the *mishnah* is discussing a metal lamp, and Rabbi Akiva's *chiddush* is the two-level jump.

### Liquids Transmission

Rava draws a further inference from this: *shma mina kasvar Rabbi Akiva tumas mashkin letamei acherim de-oraita* — from this case we can conclude that Rabbi Akiva holds the *tuma* of liquids to transmit *tuma* to other objects is *de-oraita*. The reasoning: what exactly is the metal lamp doing to the oil in this case? If the lamp's function were merely to render the oil *pasul* — unfit for temple use — the oil is already *pasul* and the lamp adds nothing. The lamp must therefore be conveying genuine *tuma* to the oil, elevating it from *shlishi* to *sheni*. This transmission would have to pass through the liquid surface of the oil, and for that to be significant it must be *de-oraita*.

The *gemara* interjects: perhaps the transmission is only *de-rabbanan*, not *de-oraita*? The response: *im miderabbanan mai irya be'av hatumah afilu berishon uvesheni nami* — if we were dealing entirely with *de-rabbanan* concerns, why would the *mishnah* need to specify a case involving the *av hatuma* derived from *tumas meit*? The entire structure of *rishon* and *sheni* is *de-rabbanan* in the first place, so if Rabbi Akiva's case were also *de-rabbanan*, the specific choice of *av hatuma* would be unmotivated. The *gemara* confirms this from a *mishnah* elsewhere: *kol ha-posel et ha-teruma metamei mashkin lihyot techila* — anything that renders *teruma* unfit can make liquids *tamei* as a *rishon*, *chutz mi-tvul yom* — except for a *tvul yom* which is an exception. Therefore, we must be speaking of *de-oraita* transmission, and Rava's conclusion stands.

## Chametz Learning

### Meir from Chanina

The *gemara* now returns to Rabbi Meir's attempt to derive a ruling about *chametz* from the cases of Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Akiva. From which case does Rabbi Meir learn? If one suggests it is from Rabbi Chanina's case of the meat — *mi domei*? — is it analogous? *Hasam tamei vetamei, hacha tahor vetamei* — there, both items were *tamei*, merely at different levels; here, one item is *tahor* entirely and one is *tamei*. The case of the meat cannot serve as the source, because Rabbi Chanina speaks only of changing *tuma* levels, not of conveying *tuma* to something *tahor* in the first place.

### Meir from Akiva

What then of Rabbi Akiva's case of the oil and lamp? Again — *mi domei*? *Hasam pasul vetamei, hacha tahor vetamei*. Rabbi Akiva's case similarly involves changing the degree of *tuma* — from *pasul* to *tamei* — not the introduction of *tuma* to something that was *tahor*. That too cannot directly prove Rabbi Meir's point.

### Meir Position

The *gemara* therefore proposes a way to salvage Rabbi Meir's inference: *neima kasavar Rabbi Meir matnitin be-avi tumah de-oraita ve-vlad ha-tumah de-rabbanan* — perhaps Rabbi Meir holds that the *av hatuma* discussed in the *mishnah* is a genuine *de-oraita* level of *tuma*, while the *vlad hatuma* — the *rishon letuma* — is only *de-rabbanan*. On this reading, *de-midoraita* the corresponding piece of *teruma chametz* that is a *vlad hatuma* is essentially *tahor* — the *de-rabbanan* status does not constitute real *tuma* in a *de-oraita* sense. And it is from Rabbi Chanina's case that Rabbi Meir learns: just as the *kohanim* would burn meat that is *av hatuma* together with what is, in *de-oraita* terms, *tahor* (*vlad hatuma* being only *de-rabbanan*), so too one may burn *chametz teruma* that is genuinely *tamei* together with *chametz teruma* whose *tuma* is only *de-rabbanan* — and in *de-oraita* terms is *tahor*.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish, citing Bar Kappara, takes a different position: *matnitin be-avi tumah de-oraita ve-vlad ha-tumah de-oraita* — the *mishnah* discusses levels of *tuma* that are both *de-oraita*, and Rabbi Meir learns from the end of the *mishnah* — from the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. Those *tanna'im* agree that definitely *tahor* and definitely *tamei* *teruma* must be burned separately on *Erev Pesach*; their dispute is only about *safek tumah* burned together with definite *tamei*. Reish Lakish reads Rabbi Meir as learning from the position adopted by Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua.

### Which Yehoshua

Which Rabbi Yehoshua does Reish Lakish have in mind? The *gemara* raises the possibility that it is the Rabbi Yehoshua of a *mishnah* elsewhere, who rules that if one has a barrel of *teruma* in a state of *safek tumah*, one should place it in the open and allow it to become definitely *tamei* — rather than continuing to protect it. This implies that it is better to give the *teruma* a definite *tuma* status so that it can be properly burned. However, the *gemara* objects: *hasam grama b'alma* — there, Rabbi Yehoshua is speaking of only indirect causation, placing the barrel where it is likely to contract *tuma* rather than actively conveying *tuma*. *Hacha b'yadayim* — here we are speaking of actively mixing *tahor* and *tamei* together by hand. The two cases are not analogous. The question of precisely which statement of Rabbi Yehoshua Reish Lakish intends will require further analysis in the continuation of the *gemara*.
