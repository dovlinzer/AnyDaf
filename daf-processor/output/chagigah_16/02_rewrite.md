# Chagigah 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Acher's Daughter & Honor

### Daughter's Request

The daf opens on 15b, in the final section of the story of Elisha ben Abuyah — known as *Acher*. The passage begins: *bito shel Acher atya l'kamei d'Rabbi* — the daughter of *Acher* came before Rabbi, the next generation, seeking *parnasah*, material support. The scene itself raises pointed questions. If *Acher* had adopted a hedonistic lifestyle, did he abandon his family entirely? The *gemara* does not say so explicitly, but the subtext is difficult to avoid.

Rabbi did not recognize her. When she identified herself as *Acher*'s daughter, his first reaction was one of genuine surprise.

### No Remnant Verse

Rabbi responded with a citation: *lo nin lo velo neched be'amo, v'ein sarid bimgurav* — he has no child or grandchild among his people, no remnant in his dwelling place. If *Acher* was a *rasha*, how could descendants of his still be present in this world? The verse seemed to foreclose the very possibility.

### Remember Torah

She answered him: *zechor l'Torato v'al tizkor ma'asav* — remember his Torah, do not remember his deeds. The response was not merely a personal plea but a theological statement about the separability of Torah and conduct. Immediately, *miyad yardah esh v'sichsechah safsalo shel Rabbi* — a fire descended and singed Rabbi's chair.

### Torah Praise & Honor

Rabbi wept and said: *umah l'misganin bah kach* — if those who denigrate Torah are treated with such care that even heaven comes down to defend their honor and their children, then *l'mishtabchin bah, al achat kamah v'khamah* — how much more so is the honor owed to those who sustain and praise Torah. The ambivalence is palpable. Rabbi had initially felt that *Acher* deserved no living descendants; by the end of the scene, even the heavens had intervened on his behalf — and this, despite his having abandoned Torah. The conflicted attitudes surrounding *Acher* run through the entire closing portion of this narrative.

## Rabbi Meir & Acher

### Angel Standard

The *gemara* now shifts to a distinct but related question: *Rabbi Meir heichi gmar Torah mipumei d'Acher?* — how could Rabbi Meir learn Torah from the mouth of *Acher*? Rabbah bar bar Chanah, citing Rabbi Yochanan, invokes the verse: *ki siftei kohen yishmeru da'at v'Torah yevakshu mipihu, ki malach Hashem Tzevaot hu* — because the lips of the *kohen* preserve knowledge, and Torah instruction is sought from his mouth, for he is like an angel of God. The implication is clear: *im domeh harav l'malach Hashem Tzevaot yevakshu Torah mipihu* — only if the teacher resembles an angel of God should one seek Torah from him. If not — *al yevakshu Torah mipihu* — one should not be pursuing instruction from his mouth. Rabbi Meir's conduct with *Acher* would seem to violate this standard directly.

### Intent Not Theirs

Resh Lakish responds: Rabbi Meir found a verse and expounded it to justify his approach. *Hatei oznecha u'shma divrei chachamim v'libcha tashit l'da'ati* — incline your ear and listen to the words of the sages, but set your heart to *my* intent. Crucially, the verse says *l'da'ati* — to my knowledge — and not *l'da'atam* — to their knowledge. This means one can listen to a sage while remaining selectively focused on the divine content of what is being transmitted, without being wholly drawn after the teacher's attitudes and lifestyle.

### Forget Deeds

Rav Chanina offers an alternative verse: *shimi bat u're'i, ve-hati oznekh ve-shikhchi amekh u-veit avich* — listen my daughter and see, incline your ear and forget your nation and your father's house. Rashi explains *ve-ma'aseihem shikhchi* — forget their deeds. The idea is parallel: one can isolate the *dvar Hashem* and set aside everything extraneous to that transmission.

### Adult Discernment

But this creates an apparent contradiction: these verses seem to sanction learning from a teacher of flawed character, while the *malach* verse seems to prohibit it. The *gemara* resolves the tension: *lo kashya, ha b'gadol ha b'katan* — there is no contradiction; one ruling applies to an adult and the other to a child. A child would be too readily seduced by and drawn after the teacher as a whole personality, whereas a discerning adult can be more selective. When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported: *Rabbi Meir achal tichla ve-shada shichla l'bar* — Rabbi Meir ate the date and threw the pit outside. He was able to take what was of value and discard what was not.

## Date & Pomegranate

### Selective Eating

The image of Rabbi Meir eating the date and discarding the pit captures something essential about his approach to *Acher*: the capacity to be selectively discerning, to receive genuine Torah content while not being swept along by the teacher's broader conduct and worldview.

### Nut Parable

Rava then expounds a verse: *el ginat egoz yaraditi lirot b'ibei ha-nachal* — I went down into the nut garden. Why are *talmidei chachamim* compared to a nut? *Mah egoz zeh af al pi she-meluchlach b'tit u-v'tzoa ein mah she-betocho nim'as* — just as a nut, even if its outer husk has become covered with filth and excrement, the nut itself inside remains intact and is not ruined.

### Torah Preserved

The application follows: *af talmid chacham af al pi she-sarach ein torato nim'eset* — a *talmid chacham*, even after he has gone off the *derech*, his Torah remains preserved within him. This is admittedly a dangerous statement. One might think that a scholar who has abandoned observance would be among the most pernicious influences — knowledgeable enough to be seductive, yet no longer anchored by the very Torah he transmits. And yet the *gemara* says no: even there, the Torah inside may be intact. This ambivalence is central to the entire *Acher* narrative and to the question of how Rabbi Meir is to be understood.

### Chananel Objection

Rabbeinu Chananel is deeply troubled by the implicit permission the *gemara* seems to extend here. He presents the *gemara*'s reasoning in full — that Rabbi Meir found a verse, that the contradiction between verses is resolved by the adult-child distinction — and then notes that a later passage will qualify the entire discussion. Elijah, Rabbeinu Chananel cites, tells us what happens in the heavenly *yeshiva*: *b'mivta d'raki'a shema'iti mikulhu rabbanan* — in the heavenly academy, the teachings of all the rabbis are taught, *ve-shema'iti shema'ata d'Rebbi Meir mishum d'gmar me-Acher lo amar* — but the teachings of Rabbi Meir are not recited there, because he learned from *Acher*. This would seem to vindicate the stricter reading. Yet Rabbeinu Chananel, even after building this argument toward a prohibition, ultimately concludes only: *hilchata afilu gadol lilmote torah mi-chavero yerei shamayim mipi adif lei* — even for an adult, learning Torah from an observant colleague is preferable. This is striking. After all the framing that suggests an outright prohibition, Rabbeinu Chananel lands on the softer formulation: it is merely *better*. The ambivalence surrounding *Acher* and Rabbi Meir's relationship to him resists clean resolution, even in the *rishonim*.

## Date & Pomegranate (II)

### Heaven Rejects

The *gemara* now presents the scene that Rabbeinu Chananel anticipated. *Rabba bar Shila* encountered Elijah the prophet and asked him: *mai ka-avid Hakadosh Baruch Hu?* — what is God doing? Elijah responded: *ka'amar shmaattah mipumaihu de'chul-hu rabbanan* — God is reciting teachings from the mouths of all the rabbis. *U'mipumei de'Rebbi Meir lo ka'amar* — but not from the mouth of Rabbi Meir. The implication appears to extend further than merely the Torah that Rabbi Meir received from *Acher*: because he learned from *Acher*, his entire corpus is kept out of the heavenly *yeshiva*. It did not invalidate the teachings; it excluded them.

## Pomegranate Seeds

### God Teaching

To appreciate the full arc of this passage, it is worth pausing on the image of God teaching in heaven from the mouths of all the rabbis. The *gemara* here presents something close to a *lo bashamayim hi* moment in reverse: the Torah that the rabbis developed below is recited above. That Rabbi Meir's teachings are withheld from this recitation is thus a pointed exclusion.

### Why Teach Meir?

Rabba bar Shila pressed Elijah: *amai?* — why? Elijah explained: *mishum de'ka'gamar shmaattah mipumei de'Acher* — because he learned teachings from the mouth of *Acher*. One might ask: does this not extend the problem to the *stam Mishnah* as well, given that Rabbi Meir is understood as the authority behind anonymous *mishnayot*? The answer lies perhaps in a distinction between what is addressed down here and what takes place above. But the irony is real: the *stam Mishnah* preserves Rabbi Meir without naming him, while in heaven his name is withheld. The two forms of anonymity move in opposite directions. Another dimension worth noting is the nature of *Torah* and *mesorah* itself. If a *talmid chacham* is essentially a vehicle — transmitting what he received — then what he teaches remains intact regardless of the vehicle's flaws. But if Torah scholarship involves creative engagement, then the personality of the scholar is more deeply implicated in the product, and the *Ribono Shel Olam*'s refusal to quote Rabbi Meir becomes more theologically coherent.

### Pomegranate Defense

Rabba bar Shila challenged Elijah: *Rabbi Meir rimon matza, tocho achal, klipato zarak* — Rabbi Meir found a pomegranate, ate the seeds, and discarded the rind. The pomegranate is a richer image than the date: it is filled with seeds, each one dense with content, and requires careful effort to extract them from the surrounding rind. It is also laden with associations of *mitzvot*. Rabba bar Shila's argument is that Rabbi Meir's selective extraction from *Acher* was an act of genuine discernment — not passive consumption but active, careful separation of content from vessel. The response is immediate. Elijah says: *hasha kamar* — now they are saying, *Meir bni omer* — "here is what Meir my son says." The heavenly academy has reversed its position on the strength of this single point, and Rabbi Meir's teachings are now quoted above.

### Shared Suffering

The teaching that God chooses to quote in Rabbi Meir's name is this: *bizman she'adam mitzta'er, Shechina mah lashon omeret? Kalani me-roshi kalani me-zero-i* — when a person is in pain, the *Shechina* says, "I am made light from my head, I am made light from my arm" — a euphemism indicating that God himself suffers. *Im kacha Hakadosh Baruch Hu mitzta'er al damam shel resha'im, kal vachomer al damam shel tzaddikim shenispach* — if God suffers even over the blood of the wicked, how much more so over the blood of the righteous who are killed. This is the teaching selected to represent Rabbi Meir in heaven — one about empathy, about God's identification with human suffering, including the suffering of those who are not *tzaddikim*. The allusion to *Acher* is difficult to avoid. Even he, who abandoned Torah, is not simply to be dismissed. God feels the pain of the wicked, and therefore *Acher*'s fate cannot be a matter of complete indifference. The teaching that rehabilitates Rabbi Meir in the heavenly academy is precisely the one most relevant to *Acher* himself.

## Corruption & Culture

### Weeping Question

The *gemara* now introduces a new scene. Samuel encountered Rav Yehuda leaning against the bolt of a gate, weeping. Samuel asked him — addressing him as *shinina*, sharp one — why he was crying. Rav Yehuda replied: *mi zutra mai d'chtiv u'rabbanan?* — is it a small matter, what is written by the rabbis? He then cites the verse from Isaiah: *ayei sofer, ayei shokel, ayei sofer tamigdalim* — where is the scribe, where is the weigher, where is the one who counts the towers?

### Doeg & Achitofel

The verse is interpreted to evoke three categories of scholarship that once existed and have been lost. *Ayei sofer* — those who counted every letter of the Torah. *Ayei shokel* — those who weighed the *kal va'chomer* arguments, who knew which legal principles were light and which were heavy. *Ayei sofer tamigdalim* — those who knew 300 *halachot* about the *migdal haporei'ach ba'avir*, the tower that floats in the air. Rashi explains this as a reference to the *mishnah* in *Ohalot* dealing with a chest half inside and half outside a house and the transfer of *tumah* — not a literally floating tower, but the evocative imagery of *porchim ba'avir* seemed appropriate here regardless. Rabbi Ami adds: Doeg and Achitofel — who represent great *talmidei chachamim* who were nonetheless *resha'im* — themselves raised 300 questions about this subject. And yet: *gimel melachim v'arba hedyotot ein lahem chelek l'olam haba* — three kings and four commoners have no portion in the world to come, among whom Doeg and Achitofel are counted.

### Our Hope?

This is Rav Yehuda's cry: *anan ma te'hei alan?* — if such figures, with their extraordinary Torah knowledge, could go off the *derech*, what will become of us? If *Acher*, if Doeg, if Achitofel — people of such immense scholarship — were not preserved by that Torah, what hope do we have?

### Heart Corruption

Samuel's answer is pointed: *tina hayisa belibam* — there was already corruption in their hearts from the beginning. The problem was not that their Torah failed to protect them from something that attacked them from outside. Rather, they had their own lusts, their own desires, their own internal drives — and those were present from the very start. Torah did not fail these people; rather, they were not people whose raw material was suited to be fully transformed by it.

### Greek Song

The *gemara* then asks specifically about *Acher*: what was his *tina*? *Zemer Yavani lo fasak mipumei* — the Greek song never stopped from his mouth. He was deeply and persistently drawn to Hellenistic culture. The phrase *tarbus ra'ah* — bad culture — is resonant here; *tarbus* in modern Hebrew simply means culture, and that is precisely the point. It was not merely intellectual temptation that pulled *Acher* away. It began with cultural attraction: the music, the sensibility, the entire world that Greek civilization represented. Rashi's comment here is notable, if somewhat reductive — he emphasizes *lo fasak*, the unceasing quality of the singing, framing the problem as a failure to mourn the *churban* properly, citing the verse that one should not drink wine accompanied by song. But the real weight of the phrase seems to fall on *Yavani* — it was specifically Greek song, representing a cultural seduction that Rashi's reading somewhat softens.

### Minim Books

The *gemara* continues: *besha'ah shehayah omeid mibeis hamedrash, harbei sifrei minim noshrim micheiko* — when *Acher* would rise from the *beis medrash*, books of *minim* — whether Greek philosophy, or perhaps Jewish-Christian literature — would fall from his lap. One pictures him hiding them beneath his texts in the study hall, carrying two worlds simultaneously. This passage presents a very different explanation for *Acher*'s fall than the mystical account of his experience in *Pardes*. There, it was a theological error arising from an encounter he was not equipped to properly understand. Here, it is something far more mundane and recognizable: cultural attraction that preceded and prepared the ground for intellectual apostasy. The two explanations are not necessarily incompatible. The Greek song created a disposition; the philosophical and theological seduction followed. The question about what leads a person away from a life of observance — whether it begins with genuine intellectual challenge or with the pull of the outside world's broader attractions — is one the *gemara* addresses with real psychological sophistication here. The conflict between them is often not as clean as it appears. Sometimes what presents itself as a philosophical crisis is, at its root, a desire for everything else that comes with abandoning the restrictions of observance. Sometimes, as the *Vilna Gaon* is said to have observed, what looks like questions are really answers in disguise — conclusions already reached in search of justification.

## Wool & Dye

### Dye Question

A weaver named Nimus HaGardi — identified by *Tosafot* as a Greek philosopher who opposed paganism — posed a question to Rabbi Meir: *kol amar denachis leyoreh salik?* — does all wool that goes into the dyeing vat come out well-dyed?

### Clean Wool

Rabbi Meir answered: *kol ma dahavei naki agav imei, salik* — anything that was clean when it was shorn from the animal will hold the dye when it emerges from the vat. *Kol delo havei naki agav imei, lo salik* — anything that was not clean to begin with will not hold the dye. The premise of good dyeing is good raw material.

### Raw Material

The application is transparent: if Torah is the dye, then the raw material of a person's character determines whether the Torah will hold. This is the explanation for the *tina hayisa belibam* — if the raw material was flawed from the outset, Torah cannot always be expected to produce a complete transformation. This is a remarkable admission. Torah can be transformative, but it is not magic. Religion can enrich and shape a person of good character, help them grow, deepen their commitment. But it is unrealistic to expect it to wholly reconstitute someone whose underlying character is significantly compromised. People are still people. The future promise of *vahasiroti es lev ha'even* — I will remove the heart of stone — belongs to a world that has not yet arrived. In this world, transformation is incremental, and the raw material matters enormously. What is being explained here is not a reason to abandon *Acher*, as Rabbi Meir's own persistent attempts to bring him back demonstrate. It is rather an explanation for why enormous Torah scholarship did not, in *Acher*'s case, prove sufficient to keep him on the *derech* — and why that need not be taken as a theological indictment of Torah itself.

## Rabbi Akiva

### Up & Down Peace

The *gemara* now returns to the *Pardes* narrative, turning at last to Rabbi Akiva. *Rabbi Akiva ala beshalom veyarad beshalom* — Rabbi Akiva went up in peace and came down in peace. This formulation is notable: the addition of *ala beshalom* is new. The others who entered did not emerge whole; Rabbi Akiva entered whole and exited the same way. The verse applied to him is from *Shir HaShirim*: *moshcheini acharecha narutza* — draw me after you and I will run. Rabbi Akiva was famously the one who declared *kol hashirim kodesh ve-Shir HaShirim kodesh kodashim*, understanding *Shir HaShirim* as a *mashal* of divine encounter. For him, the image is apt: God needed only to draw him slightly near, and he was ready to run — because he was already prepared for the encounter.

### Angels' Objection

Even so, *bikshu malachei hashareis lidoffo* — the ministering angels sought to push him away. God intervened: *hanichu lazaken zeh, she-raui le-hishtamesh bichvodi* — let this elder be; he is fit to use my honor. The phrase *le-hishtamesh bichvodi* is unusual — to make use of God's honor — suggesting some form of intimate access or participation in the divine presence.

### Gaze Divine

Rabbeinu Chananel's *girsa* reads differently: *raui hu le-histakel bichvodi* — he is fit to gaze upon my *kavod*. In this reading, the contrast with Ben Zoma is made explicit: Ben Zoma *hetzitz venifga* — he peered and was struck. Rabbi Akiva was fit to have the vision that Ben Zoma was not. The *kavod Hashem*, elsewhere described as *kvod Hashem nireh be'anan*, is understood as a tangible manifestation of the divine presence, and Rabbi Akiva's qualification to perceive it sets him apart from the others who entered *Pardes*.

## Distinguish God

### The Sign

*Mai darash?* — what did Rabbi Akiva understand that protected him from the error into which *Acher* fell? Rabbah bar bar Chanah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan cites the verse: *ve-ata meirevivot kodesh*. The word *ata* — you — contains within it an *ot*, a sign: *ot hu berivavo shelo* — God is marked out, distinguishable, within his own myriads. The implication, as Rashi reads it, is that Rabbi Akiva, when encountering the divine retinue, was able to discern God from the angels. *Acher* saw Metatron and, catastrophically, mistook him for a second divine power. Rabbi Akiva would not have made that error because he knew that God is always recognizably distinct from the angels — even from the greatest among them.

### God as Example

Rabbi Abbahu offers a reading of the same idea through a different term: *dugma hu be-revava shelo* — God is the *dugma*, the original, within his myriad. The word *dugma* usually refers to a model after which others are patterned, though here it functions to mark God's distinctiveness within the divine retinue.

### Master of Hosts

Resh Lakish adds: *Hashem Tzeva'ot Shemo, adon hu betzeva shelo* — God's name is *Hashem Tzeva'ot*, meaning he is the master within his own hosts. The very name of God encodes his supremacy over and distinction from the angelic beings that surround him.

### Beyond Elements

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, citing Rabbi Yochanan, then invokes the theophany from Elijah's cave: *lo veruach Hashem, ve-achar haruach ra'ash, lo beraash Hashem, ve-achar hara'ash esh, lo va'esh Hashem, ve-achar ha'esh kol demama daka* — God was not in the wind, not in the earthquake, not in the fire; after the fire, a still small voice. The *Maharsha* notes that the angels of wind, earthquake, and fire are each powerful in their own right — but God is encountered in something altogether different. Rabbi Akiva understood that all the dramatic manifestations of the angelic realm were precisely *not* God; God had to be sought elsewhere, in a qualitatively different mode of encounter.

### Acher in Verse

It would be impossible to cite this verse — *ve-achar haruach ra'ash* — without noticing the word *acher* embedded within it. The linguistic echo seems almost too apt. *Acher* is the one who encountered precisely the wind, the earthquake, the fire — the angelic beings — and could not see past them to the God who lay beyond. Rabbi Akiva could. That discernment was the difference.

## Shedim & Humans

### Shedim Angelic

The *gemara* now presents a *baraita* that at first seems unrelated to what preceded. *Tanu Rabbanan: shisha devarim neemru bashedim* — the rabbis taught: six things were said about *shedim*. Three of them resemble the ministering angels: *yesh lahem knafayim kemalachei hashareis* — they have wings; *vetasin misof ha'olam ve'ad sofo* — they fly from one end of the world to the other; *veyodin mah she'asid lihyot kemalachei hashareis* — and they know what is destined to occur. A qualification immediately follows: surely they cannot know the future intrinsically? Rather, *ela shom'in me'achorei hapargod* — they hear from behind the divine curtain, just as the ministering angels do. Their knowledge of the future is derivative, not inherent.

### Shedim Human

The other three characteristics of *shedim* resemble human beings: *ochlin veshotin kivnei adam* — they eat and drink; *parin veravin kivnei adam* — they procreate; *umesim kivnei adam* — and they die. Angels do not die; *shedim* do. This last detail is particularly significant, placing *shedim* closer to the human end of the spectrum than to the angelic.

### Humans Angelic

Six things are then said about human beings, three of which resemble the ministering angels. *Yesh lahem da'at kemalachei hashareis* — they possess intellect. *Umehalchin bekomah zkufah kemalachei hashareis* — they walk upright, an expression of human dignity. *Umesaprim belashon hakodesh kemalachei hashareis* — they speak in the holy tongue, or more broadly, they possess spoken language, which is the province of the angels.

### Humans Animal

The remaining three characteristics of humans resemble animals: *ochlin veshotin keveheimah* — they eat and drink; *parin veravin keveheimah* — they procreate; *umotzi'in re'i keveheimah* — and they excrete waste. The physical, appetitive, and reproductive dimensions of human life are shared with the animal kingdom.

### Human Paradox

Taken together, the portrait that emerges is of the human being as a genuinely hybrid creature — partly angelic, partly animal, with intellect and upright bearing and language on one side, and digestion and reproduction and death on the other. This connects, in a profound way, to the broader discussion of *ma'aseh merkavah* and the challenges of mystical ascent. The fundamental tension of *Pardes* — who can enter, who is damaged, who comes out whole — is in part a tension about what kind of being a human being is. Can a physical creature, constituted partly like an animal, really rise to encounter the divine? The story of Rabbi Akiva says yes, but only for one who is truly complete — *shalom* from the start. The story of *Acher*, Doeg, Achitofel, and Ben Zoma all illuminate, in different ways, what happens when that completeness is absent.

## God's Honor

### Four Unknowns

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* about what one may not investigate: *kol hamistakel be'arba'ah devarim, raui lo shelo ba la'olam* — whoever looks into four things, it would have been better for him not to have come into the world: what is above, what is below, what was before, and what will be after.

### Why Past OK?

The *gemara* finds the formulation puzzling. *Bishlama l'ma'alah l'matah* — I understand the prohibition on investigating what lies above or below, or *l'achor*, the future. *Ela lefanim* — but regarding what came before, *mah dehava hava* — what was, was. Why should inquiry into the primordial past be any more problematic than inquiry into what has already transpired? The underlying subject is clearly questions about the nature of creation itself — what existed above and below the world, what lies beyond the heavens, what the future of the world will be, and what the origins of the world were before it came to be.

### Dung Heap

Rabbi Yochanan bar Eleazar answers with a parable: *mashal l'melech basar v'dam she'amar l'avadav bnu li paltarin gedolin* — it is like a king who instructed his servants to build him a great palace. The best available land happened to be a dung heap. They built the palace, filled in and covered over the dung heap, and now the palace stands in all its beauty. *Ein ritzono shel hamelech lehazkir shem ashpah* — the king does not want anyone to mention that the site was once a dung heap.

### Tohu v'Vohu

The application: God made the world out of *tohu v'vohu* — primordial chaos and formlessness. Raising questions about those origins — about the mess from which creation emerged — is to undermine the dignity and beauty of the creation that now exists. It is, in that sense, disrespectful of *kvod kono*, God's honor. One need not claim to fully understand the theology here; the *gemara* itself presents this as a *mashal* rather than a direct explanation.

## Three Gazes

### Rainbow Glory

The *gemara* then cites the verse: *k'mareh hakeshet asher yihyeh be'anan b'yom hageshem, ken mareh hanogah saviv, hu mareh dmut kvod Hashem* — like the appearance of the rainbow in the cloud on a rainy day, so was the appearance of the radiance surrounding it; that is the appearance of the likeness of the *kvod Hashem*. To gaze directly at the rainbow is, in some sense, to gaze at this manifestation of the divine presence.

### King's Glory

Similarly, one should not gaze at a king. The verse *ve-natata me-hodcha alav* — you shall give of your glory to him — refers to Moshe transmitting something of his own radiance to Yehoshua, a radiance that derived ultimately from proximity to God. A king bears something of that divine glory as well.

### Kohanim's Presence

And one should not gaze at the *kohanim* when they stand on the *duchan* during the time of the *Beis HaMikdash*, reciting the priestly blessing with God's explicit name. At that moment, the *Shechina* rests upon their fingers. Gazing at them is gazing at the divine presence itself.

### Not Really Glory

Rabbeinu Chananel offers a distinctive reading of the rainbow prohibition. The problem is not that the rainbow *is* the *kvod Hashem* and one must not look. Rather, the problem is that one *says* it is the likeness of God's glory — when in fact it is not. The failure to be *chas al kvod kono* lies in the error of identification, in conflating a natural phenomenon with the divine presence itself.

### Mystical Rainbow

In *kabbalistic* interpretation, by contrast, the rainbow is treated with considerable literalness as a mystical symbol, and being *mistakel bakeshet* is a genuinely grave matter. The association of the rainbow — its form, its emergence from behind the clouds — with the *kvod Hashem* is developed in ways that take the verse more directly than Rabbeinu Chananel's rationalist reframing allows. The *gemara*'s concern may be precisely that gazing carries with it the risk of confusing the symbol for the reality — a version, in fact, of the same error that destroyed *Acher* in *Pardes*.

## Private vs. Public

### Push Away God

The *gemara* cites Rabbi Yosef: *zeh ha'over aveirah b'seter* — the one who transgresses in private also belongs in the category of *kol shelo chas al kvod kono*. The verse *koh amar Hashem, hashamayim kisei veha'aretz hadom raglai* — heaven is my throne and the earth is my footstool — grounds the concern: sinning in private amounts to pushing away God's footstool, to behaving as though God's presence does not extend into private space.

### God Sees All

Rabbi Yitzchak sharpens the point: *kol ha'over aveirah b'seter k'ilu dochek raglei Shechinah* — whoever transgresses in private acts as though pushing away the feet of the *Shechina*. The theological failure of private sin is the implicit claim, whether conscious or not, that God does not see what is done when no human observer is present. Sinning in public at least involves the acknowledgment, however defiant, that one's actions are visible. Sinning in private carries the deeper distortion of treating God's presence as bounded by human visibility.

### Illai's Leniency

And yet the *gemara* immediately introduces a counterpoint. Rabbi Illai the Elder taught: *im ro'eh adam she'yitzro mitgaber alav, yelech lemakom she'ein makkirin oto, ve-yilbash shechorim, ve-yitatef shechorim, ve-ya'aseh mah she-libo chafetz, ve-al yechalel shem shamayim befarhesya* — if a person sees that his *yetzer hara* is overcoming him, he should go to a place where no one recognizes him, dress in black, and do what his heart desires — rather than desecrate God's name publicly. This appears, on its surface, to directly endorse private sin as preferable to public sin, at least when the desire is overwhelming.

### Control Desire

The *gemara* reconciles the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, ha de-matikif lei yitzrei, ha de-lo matikif lei yitzrei* — there is no contradiction; the cases differ based on whether the *yetzer hara* overwhelms the person or does not. If one's desire is truly overwhelming and the sin will occur regardless, then it is preferable for it to occur privately rather than publicly, avoiding *chillul Hashem*. If, however, one could resist but chooses to sin in private precisely because no one will see — then that person is *dochek raglei ha-Shechina*, sinning against the very fabric of the divine presence in the world.

### Sin Hierarchy

The practical conclusion that emerges is: if one must sin, private is better than public. If one can avoid sin, one should avoid it entirely. The difficulty, of course, is that this reasoning can quickly become self-serving: once one tells oneself that the sin will occur regardless, going somewhere private becomes a form of permission rather than a last resort.

### Rishonim Reject

This is precisely what troubled many *rishonim* deeply. They were unwilling to read the *gemara* as licensing private sin even in extremis. Their reading: going to a place where one is unknown, dressing in black mourning clothes — these are acts designed not to facilitate the sin but to create humility and discomfort, to make the person feel so out of place and burdened that the desire itself dissipates. *Ve-ya'aseh mah she-libo chafetz* — "do what your heart desires" — means, in their reading, that by that point the heart no longer desires to sin. The concern is real and practical. Once a person tells himself that the sin is inevitable and it is therefore permitted to pursue it privately — and perhaps even remove outward markers of identity to facilitate that — a far greater license has been extended than the *gemara* intended. The question asked of Rav Moshe Feinstein about wearing a *kippah* while attending improper films illustrates the same problem: does removing the *kippah* prevent *chillul Hashem*, or does it simply grant a far more comfortable path to transgression?

## Yetzer Hara Warnings

### Believe Not

The *gemara* concludes with a *derashah* by Rabbi Yehuda b'Rebbe Nachmani, the *turgeman* of Reish Lakish. The verse *al ta'aminu b'rei, al tivtechu b'aluf* — do not trust in a friend (*rei*), do not put your faith in an officer (*aluf*) — is read as a warning against two distinct forms of false confidence. *Rei* is re-read as *ra* — evil, the *yetzer hara*. *V'ein ra ela yetzer hara, shene'emar ki yetzer lev ha'adam ra mine'urav* — there is no *ra* other than the *yetzer hara*, as the verse says, for the inclination of a man's heart is evil from his youth.

### Forgive Argument

The precise seduction the *gemara* has in mind is this: *im yomar lecha yetzer hara, chata, ve-Hakadosh Baruch Hu mochel* — if the *yetzer hara* tells you, go ahead and sin, and God will forgive — this is what one must not believe. The argument is seasonally pointed. As *Rosh Hashanah* approaches and *Yom Kippur* looms, the *yetzer hara*'s most effective tool is precisely this: transgress now, repent later. The machinery of atonement becomes, in the *yetzer hara*'s hands, a license to sin.

### Trust God

*Al tivtechu b'aluf* — do not put your trust in the *aluf*. And who is the *aluf* here? *V'ein aluf ela Hakadosh Baruch Hu, shene'emar aluf ne'urai atah* — there is no *aluf* other than God. The verse "do not trust in the dignitary" becomes, in this reading, a warning not to presume on God's forgiveness. Do not trust that God will simply absorb whatever you do because *Yom Kippur* is coming.

### Bricks Testify

And if one objects: who will even know? *Shema tomar mi me'idi bi* — what if you say, who will testify against me? *Avnei beiso v'koros beiso shel adam hem me'idim bo* — the very bricks and beams of a person's house will testify against him. *Shene'emar ki even mikir tiz'ak ve-chaphis mei-eitz ya'anena* — for the stone cries out from the wall and the beam of wood answers it.

### Multiple Witnesses

The *gemara* then presents additional witnesses: the sages say that *nishmato shel adam me'idah bo* — a person's own soul testifies against him, as the verse *mishkavas cheikecha shemor pischei ficha* indicates, for what lies in the bosom of a person is the *neshama* itself. Rabbi Zreika adds: *shenei malachei hashareis ha-malvin oto hem me'idim bo* — the two ministering angels who accompany every person will testify against him, as the verse says, *ki malachav yetzaveh lach lishmorcha bechol derachecha* — for he will command his angels to watch over you in all your ways. They are watching. And others say: *evarav shel adam me'idim bo* — a person's own limbs testify against him, as it says, *ve-atem eidai ne'um Hashem va'ani el* — you yourselves are my witnesses, says God.

### Real Consequences

These sources need not be read as purely metaphysical accounts of some future heavenly tribunal. There is something deeply real in each of them. The bricks and beams of one's home testify — one's environment reflects one's values and conduct, shaped visibly by the choices one makes there. The *neshama* testifies — one's character is formed and deformed by one's actions; the soul bears the imprint of what a person does with it. The limbs testify — the body itself is shaped by how it is used, trained or neglected, directed toward service or degraded by misuse. In each case, our actions do not remain private. They leave their mark — on our homes, our characters, our bodies — in ways that are entirely real, even before any metaphysical accounting takes place. With that, and in the spirit of the season, may the year ahead be one in which all our actions reflect for us in wholly positive ways. *Ksiva ve-chasima tova*.
