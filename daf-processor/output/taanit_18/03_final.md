# Taanit 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Megillat Ta'anit Cat (II)

### Intro to Megillat Ta'anit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בִּמְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית ״דְּלָא לְמִיסְפַּד״ — לְפָנָיו אָסוּר, לְאַחֲרָיו מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Any** day concerning which **it is written in *Megillat Ta'anit* not to eulogize** on that day, it is also **prohibited** to eulogize **on the day before,** but it is **permitted** to do so **on the following day.**

The *sugya* opens at the bottom of 17b, approximately three lines before the text widens, picking up on the point in the *mishnah* that addresses *Megillat Ta'anit*. The relevant formulation is *kol hakatuv b'Megillat Ta'anit d'lo l'mispad* — anything written in *Megillat Ta'anit* designating days on which one may not deliver a *hesped*: *lefanav assur v'acharav mutar*, beforehand is forbidden, but afterwards is permitted.

### Two Day Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִלֵּין יוֹמַיָּא דְּלָא לְהִתְעַנָּאָה בְּהוֹן, וּמִקְצָתְהוֹן דְּלָא לְמִיסְפַּד בְּהוֹן
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in *Megillat Ta'anit*: **These are the days on which fasting is prohibited, and on some of them eulogizing is prohibited** as well

There are, in fact, two distinct categories of days recorded in *Megillat Ta'anit*. The first and less weighty category consists of days designated *d'lo l'hityana behon* — days on which fasting is prohibited. These represent the most basic level of holiday observance, since fasting is the most serious violation of a day's festive character. The second and more stringent category consists of days designated *d'lo l'mispad behon* — days on which even a *hesped* is forbidden. Delivering a *hesped*, while less inherently disruptive to a holiday than fasting, is nonetheless prohibited on these weightier days.

The question then arises: what is the status of the days surrounding these holidays? Do these days cast a kind of halachic shadow over adjacent dates, prohibiting fasting or *hespedim* on the days immediately before or after?

### Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi Yossi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַיָּמִים הָאֵלֶּה הַכְּתוּבִין בִּמְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית — לִפְנֵיהֶם וּלְאַחֲרֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִין, שַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים, הֵן — אֲסוּרִין, לִפְנֵיהֶן וּלְאַחֲרֵיהֶן — מוּתָּרִין. וּמָה הֶפְרֵשׁ בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה? הַלָּלוּ — דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, וְדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה אֵין צְרִיכִין חִיזּוּק, הַלָּלוּ — דִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים, וְדִבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים צְרִיכִין חִיזּוּק.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **these days that are written in *Megillat Ta'anit*, it is prohibited to fast before them and after them.** However, concerning ***Shabbatot* and Festivals,** fasting on those days **is prohibited,** but **before them and after them** fasting is **permitted. And what is the difference between this and that? These,** Shabbat and Festivals, are **statements of Torah, and statements of Torah do not require reinforcement,** whereas **these** days mentioned in *Megillat Ta'anit* are statements of **rabbinic law,** and statements of **rabbinic law require reinforcement.**

This question is the subject of a dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yossi. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that days designated only *d'lo l'hityana behon* carry no restriction whatsoever on the days before or after. Days designated *d'lo l'mispad behon* — the weightier category — do prohibit the day before, since anticipation of the holiday is considered significant, but impose no restriction on the day after, since once the day has passed, it has passed.

Rabbi Yossi shifts the entire framework one level stricter. According to him, days of *d'lo l'hityana behon* do carry a restriction on the day before. And days of *d'lo l'mispad behon* carry a restriction on both the day before and the day after. This is the fundamental dispute that will govern the analysis throughout the *sugya*.

## Rosh Chodesh Nisan (II)

### Why Rosh Chodesh?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵרֵישׁ יַרְחָא דְנִיסָן וְעַד תְּמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ אִיתּוֹקַם תְּמִידָא, דְּלָא לְמִיסְפַּד בְּהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **From the New Moon of Nisan until the eighth of** the month, the proper sacrifice of **the daily offering was established,** and therefore it was **decreed not to eulogize on these** dates.

The *Gemara* proceeds to quote from *Megillat Ta'anit* by way of a *Tanna Rabbanan* — introduced with the language of a *braita* or *tosefta*, though the passage is in fact a quotation from *Megillat Ta'anit* itself, which the *Gemara* elsewhere introduces with the formula *d'chtiv*. The text states: *elu yamaya d'lo l'hityana behon umiktzathon d'lo l'mispad behon* — these are the days on which one may not fast, and some of them on which one may not even deliver a *hesped*.

The first period listed runs *me-reish yarcha d'Nissan v'ad tamanya bei itokam tamida d'lo l'mispad behon* — from the first of Nissan through the eighth, a period during which the *tamid* offering was established. The background was a dispute between the *Tzadukim*, who held that an individual could donate a *tamid*, and the *Perushim*, who maintained it could only be a communal sacrifice. The victory of the *Perushim* was commemorated as a minor holiday of sufficient weight that even a *hesped* was forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מֵרֵישׁ יַרְחָא דְנִיסָן עַד תְּמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ אִיתּוֹקַם תְּמִידָא, דְּלָא לְמִיסְפַּד. לְמָה לִי מֵרֵישׁ יַרְחָא? לֵימָא מִתְּרֵי בְּנִיסָן, וְרֹאשׁ חוֹדֶשׁ גּוּפֵיהּ יוֹם טוֹב הוּא וְאָסוּר! אָמַר רַב: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לֶאֱסוֹר יוֹם שֶׁלְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify these statements by comparing them to the ruling of the mishna. **The Master said** above: **From the New Moon of Nisan until the eighth of** the month, **the daily offering was established,** and therefore it was **decreed not to eulogize on these** dates. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need *Megillat Ta'anit* to say: **From the New Moon? Let it say: From the second of Nisan, as the New Moon is itself a holiday, and** it is already **prohibited** to eulogize on that day. **Rav said: It is necessary** to mention the New Moon of Nisan **only to prohibit** eulogizing on **the day before,** in accordance with the statement in *Megillat Ta'anit* that fasting on the day before any of the specified commemorative days is also prohibited.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *lama li me-reish yarcha?* Why does *Megillat Ta'anit* need to say the period begins from *Rosh Chodesh*? *Lima mi-trei be-Nissan* — just say it begins from the second of Nissan! After all, *Rosh Chodesh gufay yom tov hu* — Rosh Chodesh is itself already a minor *yom tov*, and the prohibition against fasting or delivering a *hesped* on Rosh Chodesh is already known independently of *Megillat Ta'anit*.

## Megillat Ta'anit Cat (II)

### Purpose of Eulogies

Before proceeding to the *Gemara*'s answer, it is worth pausing on the nature of the *hesped* prohibition itself — a point that is directly relevant to contemporary practice. The *Gemara*'s conception of a *hesped* is one specifically designed to generate intense communal mourning: hiring *mekonenot*, professional mourning women, to stir up weeping and wailing and to evoke an overwhelming sense of tragic loss. That kind of experience is, of course, a genuine violation of the spirit of any festive day.

The practice of delivering *hespedim* today is, by contrast, oriented quite differently. Modern eulogies tend to celebrate the life of the deceased, speak to the person's positive qualities, and are designed to leave those present with an affirming, uplifting sense of who that person was. People may become emotional, but the goal is rarely to produce the kind of communal outpouring of grief that the *Gemara* had in mind. It is therefore a legitimate question whether contemporary *hespedim* carry the same halachic weight as the *hesped* the *Gemara* was addressing. In practice, the prohibition on *hespedim* during Nissan is widely observed in a somewhat attenuated fashion — a few words are said, something is mentioned about the deceased — and while one should not treat this casually, the underlying rationale of the prohibition may genuinely apply with less force given the very different character of the modern *hesped*.

## Rosh Chodesh Nisan (II)

### Rav's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֹאשׁ חוֹדֶשׁ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא, וּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא לָא בָּעֵי חִיזּוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that as **the New Moon is by Torah law and a Torah law requires no reinforcement,** it is permitted to fast on the previous day.

Returning to the *Gemara*'s question — why does *Megillat Ta'anit* mention the period as beginning from Rosh Chodesh if Rosh Chodesh is already forbidden independently? — Rav answers: *lo nitzrecha ela le-assur yom she-lifanav*, the mention of Rosh Chodesh as part of this rabbinic holiday period is needed precisely to render the day before Rosh Chodesh — the twenty-ninth of Adar — forbidden. If *Megillat Ta'anit* had simply stated that the *tamid* holiday runs from the second through the eighth of Nissan, then Rosh Chodesh would have remained covered by its own independent status — but the twenty-ninth of Adar would have been entirely unrestricted. By including Rosh Chodesh within the *tamid* holiday period, *Megillat Ta'anit* brings the rabbinic holiday machinery to bear on the first of Nissan, and since this is a day of *d'lo l'mispad* — on which even the *Chachamim* agree the day before is forbidden — the twenty-ninth is thereby rendered *assur*.

The *Gemara* immediately presses further: but couldn't the independent status of Rosh Chodesh itself accomplish the same thing? If Rosh Chodesh is already forbidden, shouldn't it also have the effect of prohibiting the day before it? The *Gemara* answers: *Rosh Chodesh de-Oraita hu ve-de-Oraita lo ba'ei chizuk* — Rosh Chodesh is a biblical institution, and biblical institutions do not require reinforcement. Their halachic weight is self-evident; no one doubts their authority. The mechanism of prohibiting adjacent days is a feature specific to these new minor rabbinic holidays, precisely because they need strengthening.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

The *braita* makes this principle explicit: *ha-yamim ha-eleh ha-ketuvim be-Megillat Taanit lifneihem ve-achareihem assurim* — the days recorded in *Megillat Ta'anit*, both the days before and after them are forbidden. That reflects the position of Rabbi Yossi. But *Shabbatot ve-yamim tovim hen assurim lifneihem ve-achareihem mutarim* — for *Shabbatot* and biblical *yamim tovim*, the days before and after are entirely permitted. The *braita* itself explains the distinction: *hallalu divrei Torah ve-divrei Torah einam tzrichim chizuk* — the latter are biblical, and biblical law does not require reinforcement. *Hallalu divrei sofrim ve-divrei sofrim tzrichim chizuk* — the former are rabbinic enactments, and *divrei sofrim* require strengthening.

The practical upshot: by including Rosh Chodesh Nissan within the *tamid* rabbinic holiday period rather than allowing it to stand solely on its biblical footing, *Megillat Ta'anit* activates the "*d'lo l'mispad*" machinery for that day — and since everyone agrees the day before a *d'lo l'mispad* day is forbidden, the twenty-ninth of Adar is rendered *assur*.

## End of Pesach Period

### Why Through End?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִתְּמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ עַד סוֹף מוֹעֲדָא אִיתּוֹתַב חַגָּא דְשָׁבוּעַיָּא, דְּלָא לְמִיסְפַּד בְּהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **From the eighth of** Nisan **until the end of the festival** of Passover, **the festival of *Shavuot* was restored** and it was likewise decreed **not to eulogize** during this period.

The *Gemara* turns to the second holiday period recorded in the same passage of *Megillat Ta'anit*: *mi-tamanya bei v'ad sof mo'ada itoseif chaga d'shavuaya d'la l'mispad behon* — from the eighth of Nissan through the end of Pesach, the holiday of Shavuot was established, in the sense that the *Perushim* were victorious in their dispute against the *Baitusim* regarding when to begin counting the fifty days — whether from Sunday or from the day after the first day of Pesach. That entire debate and its resolution is commemorated over the period from the eighth of Nissan through the end of Pesach, and *hespedim* are forbidden throughout.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי עַד סוֹף מוֹעֵד, לֵימָא עַד הַמּוֹעֵד, וּמוֹעֵד גּוּפֵיהּ יוֹם טוֹב הוּא וְאָסוּר! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כִּדְאָמַר רַב — לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לֶאֱסוֹר יוֹם שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need *Megillat Ta'anit* to say: **Until the end of the Festival? Let it say: Until the Festival,** as it is anyway **prohibited** to eulogize on the festival of Passover. Rav Pappa said that this, too, should be explained **as Rav said: It is necessary** to mention Passover **only to prohibit** eulogizing on the **following day.**

The *Gemara* asks the parallel question: *lama li ad sof mo'eid?* Why does *Megillat Ta'anit* need to extend this period all the way through the end of Pesach? Say simply: from the eighth of Nissan until Pesach begins. *U-mo'eid gufei yom tov hu va-assur* — Pesach itself is already a *yom tov* and is obviously forbidden.

### Rav Papa's Answer

Rav Papa answers, applying the same logic Rav applied to the first case: *lo nitzrecha ela le-assur yom shele-achrav* — the mention of Pesach as included within this rabbinic holiday period is needed to render the day after Pesach — *Isru Chag* — forbidden. If Pesach stood only on its biblical footing as a *yom tov*, the day after would carry no restriction, since biblical holidays do not prohibit adjacent days. But by including Pesach within the scope of this rabbinic commemoration, the rabbinic holiday now extends through the end of Pesach, and *Isru Chag* becomes forbidden as the day after a *d'lo l'mispad* rabbinic holiday.

### Rabbi Yossi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן, כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי דְּאָמַר: בֵּין לְפָנָיו וּבֵין לְאַחֲרָיו אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this ruling? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said** that eulogizing is **prohibited both** on the day **before** the date recorded in *Megillat Ta'anit* **and** on the **following day.**

At this point, the *Gemara* raises a pointed observation: this entire analysis depends on adopting the position of Rabbi Yossi. Only Rabbi Yossi holds that *d'lo l'mispad* days render even the day *after* forbidden. The *Chachamim* restrict the prohibition to the day before. If *Megillat Ta'anit* is being read as rendering *Isru Chag* forbidden, that necessarily reflects Rabbi Yossi's view — *ke-Rebbi Yosi d'amar bein lefanav bein le-acharav assur*. The *Gemara* thus arrives at the conclusion that *Megillat Ta'anit*, as understood here, was authored in accordance with Rabbi Yossi's position.

## 29th of Adar Problem

### 29th Coverage Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּתְמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ, אֲתָת בְּשׂוֹרְתָּא טָבְתָּא לִיהוּדָאֵי דְּלָא יְעִידוֹן מִן אוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in *Megillat Ta'anit*: **On the twenty-eighth of** Adar **good tidings came to the Jews, that they would not be restricted from Torah** study

This conclusion generates a new difficulty. If *Megillat Ta'anit* follows Rabbi Yossi, then the twenty-eighth of Adar — which is already an independent holiday in *Megillat Ta'anit* (*be-asrin ve-tamnya beih atat besorta tavta le-Yehudai*, a day commemorating the annulment of a decree against the Jewish people that would have prevented observance of Torah, *milah*, and *Shabbat*) — would already render the twenty-ninth of Adar forbidden, since according to Rabbi Yossi even the day *after* a *d'lo l'mispad* day is forbidden. If the twenty-ninth is thus already covered by being the day after the twenty-eighth, what is the purpose of including Rosh Chodesh Nissan within the *tamid* holiday — which was said to be needed precisely to forbid the twenty-ninth?

The *Gemara* is making an assumption that deserves comment. The question implicitly treats *Megillat Ta'anit* as a purely halachic document, as though the only purpose of mentioning a date is to generate a *nafka mina*. In reality, *Megillat Ta'anit* is a historical record: it commemorates specific events that occurred on specific dates, and those dates would be recorded regardless of whether there is any independent halachic reason to mention them. The *Gemara*, however, proceeds on the assumption that redundancy must have purpose, and presses the question accordingly.

### Abaye: 30-Day Month

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְתִשְׁעָה נָמֵי — מַאי אִירְיָא דְּהָוֵי יוֹמָא דְּמִקַּמֵּי יוֹמָא דְּמִיתּוֹקַם תְּמִידָא? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ יוֹמָא דְּבָתַר עֶשְׂרִין וּתְמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** with regard to **the twenty-ninth** of Adar **too, why** state **specifically** that eulogizing is prohibited then **because it is the day before the day on which the daily offering was established? Let him derive** this prohibition from the fact **that it is the day after the twenty-eighth of** Adar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְחֹדֶשׁ מְעוּבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: It is necessary** to include the New Moon of Nisan **only for** the case of **a full,** thirty-day **month.**

Abaye answers: *lo nitzrecha ela l'chodesh meubar* — the mention of Rosh Chodesh Nissan is necessary for a case where Adar is a full thirty-day month. In a twenty-nine-day Adar, the twenty-eighth generates, via Rabbi Yossi's rule, a prohibition on the twenty-ninth — and Rosh Chodesh Nissan then forbids that same twenty-ninth as the day before it. But in a thirty-day Adar, the twenty-eighth generates a prohibition on the twenty-ninth, which is itself the twenty-ninth of Adar, and the thirtieth — which functions as Rosh Chodesh — still requires separate coverage. That is provided by including Rosh Chodesh Nissan within the rabbinic holiday, which thereby prohibits the thirtieth as the day before it.

### Rav Ashi: Double Coverage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא לְחוֹדֶשׁ חָסֵר, כׇּל שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו — בְּתַעֲנִית אָסוּר, בְּהֶסְפֵּד מוּתָּר. וְזֶה, הוֹאִיל וּמוּטָל בֵּין שְׁנֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים — עֲשָׂאוּהוּ כְּיוֹם טוֹב עַצְמוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּהֶסְפֵּד נָמֵי אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Even** if **you say** that we are dealing **with a deficient month,** with twenty-nine days, the inclusion of the New Moon of Nisan can still be explained. The reason is that with regard to **all** days **that follow** the dates listed in *Megillat Ta'anit*, **fasting is prohibited but eulogizing is permitted. But** in **this** case, **since** the twenty-ninth of Adar **is positioned between two** commemorative **holidays,** the twenty-eighth of Adar and the New Moon of Nisan, the Sages **made it like** a commemorative **holiday in its own right, and** it is therefore **prohibited even to eulogize** on this date.

Rav Ashi offers a further qualification, one that introduces a significant conceptual refinement: *afilu teima l'chodesh chaser* — the point holds even for a regular twenty-nine-day month. The reason is that the prohibition generated by a day before or after a holiday does not carry the same weight as the holiday itself. The day itself, being *d'lo l'mispad*, is forbidden even with respect to a *hesped*. But the day before or after — even according to Rabbi Yossi — is forbidden only with respect to fasting; a *hesped* on an adjacent day is permitted. *Ve-zeh ho'il u-mutav bein shenei yamim tovim asu'uhu ke-yom tov atzmo ve-afilu behesped nami assur* — but where a particular day falls between two holiday periods and is thus doubly covered — forbidden both as the day after one holiday and as the day before the next — that day is treated with the full weight of the holiday itself, and even a *hesped* is forbidden on it.

### Overlap & Contingency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מִתְּמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ וְעַד סוֹף מוֹעֲדָא אִיתּוֹתַב חַגָּא דְשָׁבוּעַיָּא, דְּלָא לְמִיסְפַּד. לְמָה לִי לְמֵימַר מִתְּמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ? לֵימָא מִתִּשְׁעָה בֵּיהּ, וּתְמָנְיָא גּוּפֵיהּ אָסוּר, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ יוֹמָא דְּאִיתּוֹקַם בֵּיהּ תְּמִידָא!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above, in *Megillat Ta'anit*: **From the eighth of** Nisan **until the end of the festival** of Passover, **the festival of *Shavuot* was restored,** and it was decreed **not to eulogize** during this period. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need it **to say: From the eighth of** Nisan? **Let** the *tanna* **say: From the ninth of** Nisan, **and the eighth itself** will still be prohibited because, **as** stated earlier, **it is the day on which the daily offering was established.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאִילּוּ מִקְּלַע לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא וּבַטְּלִינֵּיהּ לְשִׁבְעָה — תְּמָנְיָא גּוּפֵיהּ אָסוּר, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ יוֹמָא קַמָּא דְּאִיתּוֹתַב בֵּיהּ חַגָּא דְשָׁבוּעַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since if** a calamitous **event happened and they canceled the seven** days commemorating the establishment of the daily offering, **the eighth** day **itself** will remain **prohibited, as it is the first day on which the festival of *Shavuot* was restored.** Since this date is not merely the last of the series for the daily offering, but it also commemorates the restoration of *Shavuot*, it is not affected by the cancellation of the previous seven days.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, עֶשְׂרִים וְתִשְׁעָה נָמֵי: כֵּיוָן דְּאִילּוּ מִיקְּלַע מִילְּתָא וּבַטְּלִינֵּיהּ לְעֶשְׂרִים וּתְמָנְיָא — עֶשְׂרִין וְתִשְׁעָה גּוּפֵיהּ אָסוּר, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ יוֹמָא דְּמִקַּמֵּי יוֹמָא דְּאִיתּוֹקַם תְּמִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Now that you have arrived at this** conclusion, the same logic can be applied to **the twenty-ninth** of Adar **as well: Since if** a calamitous **event happened and they canceled** the commemoration of **the twenty-eighth** of Adar, nevertheless, **the twenty-ninth** day **itself** will remain **prohibited, as it is the first day on which the daily offering was established.**

This leads to a further and perhaps surprising explanation for the apparent redundancy in the text. The *Gemara* continues: *hashata de-atit le-hachi* — now that we have reached this understanding — namely, that overlap between holiday periods serves a meaningful halachic function — we can explain another feature of the text. The eighth of Nissan appears in both the first holiday (which runs from the first through the eighth) and the second holiday (which runs from the eighth through the end of Pesach). Why mention the eighth twice? The *Gemara* answers: *kivan de-ilu mikla milta u-vatlinei le-shiva* — if circumstances were to arise that caused the first holiday period to be stricken from the calendar due to some later tragedy occurring during those days, *tamanya gufei assur de-havah lei yoma kama de-itotav bah chaga de-shavu'aya* — the eighth would still be forbidden, since it is the first day of the second holiday period.

The same logic resolves the question about the twenty-ninth of Adar. Even following Rabbi Yossi, if some tragedy were to occur on the twenty-eighth of Adar and that date were removed from the holiday calendar, the twenty-ninth would still be covered — as the day before Rosh Chodesh Nissan, which remains part of the *tamid* holiday. The apparent redundancy in these dates is thus deliberate: it is a form of built-in resilience, anticipating the possibility that individual dates might be removed from the calendar while others remain. The overlap ensures that no date is left uncovered simply because one of its two sources of protection has been nullified.

This is a striking feature of the *sugya*. The *Gemara* here envisions *Megillat Ta'anit* as a living document, one whose calendar of minor holidays was understood from the outset to be subject to later historical revision. Certain events, if sufficiently tragic, could override individual commemorations. The careful overlapping of dates was designed to maintain coverage even in that eventuality.

## Halachic Status Disputes

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַסִּי אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is a dispute between *amora'im*: **Rav Ḥiyya bar Asi said** that **Rav said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,**

The *Gemara* now turns to the recorded opinions of the *Amoraim* on the underlying halachic question. Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi said in the name of Rav: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yose* — the law follows Rabbi Yossi, meaning that both the day before and the day after a *d'lo l'mispad* day are forbidden.

### Shmuel's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר. וּמִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בְּהוֹן״ ״בְּהוֹן״ שְׁתֵּי פְעָמִים — לוֹמַר לָךְ שֶׁהֵן אֲסוּרִין, לִפְנֵיהֶן וּלְאַחֲרֵיהֶן מוּתָּרִין. וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** the *tanna* of the unattributed mishna, who said that although it is prohibited to eulogize on the day before, it is permitted on the day after. The Gemara asks: **And did Shmuel** actually **say this? But isn't it** taught in a *baraita* that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: And what** is the meaning when *Megillat Ta'anit* **states: On them, on them, twice,** in the phrases: Not to eulogize on them, and: Not to fast on them. This phrase is repeated **to say to you** that fasting and eulogizing **on these** days themselves **is prohibited,** but on the days **before** and on the **following** days it is **permitted. And Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** How, then, can it be said that Shmuel ruled in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיקָּרָא סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דְּלֵיכָּא תַּנָּא דְּמֵיקֵל כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, אָמַר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר. כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁמְעֵיהּ לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן דְּמֵיקֵל טְפֵי, אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Initially,** Shmuel **maintained** that **since there is no** other ***tanna* as lenient as Rabbi Meir, he said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir. When he heard that** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon** ben Gamliel **was more lenient,** he said that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** Shmuel consistently ruled in the most lenient manner possible on this issue.

Shmuel's position is more complex. Initially, the *Gemara* states that Shmuel ruled: *halacha ke-Rabbi Meir* — the law follows Rabbi Meir, under whose view the day after is not forbidden but the day before is. The *Gemara* then cites a *braita* in which Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel offers a *derashah* on the double use of the word *behon* in *Megillat Ta'anit* — *behon behon shtei pe'amim* — interpreting the repetition as indicating that only those days *themselves* are forbidden, and that both before and after are entirely permitted. This is the most *mekil* position of all.

The *Gemara* then reports that Shmuel changed his ruling: *me-ikara savar keivan de-leika tanna de-mekal ke-Rebbi Meir*, originally, since no *tanna* was more lenient than Rabbi Meir on this point, Shmuel ruled accordingly. But *keivan de-shamiah le-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel de-mekal tefei*, once he heard that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel took an even more lenient position, *amar halacha ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — he ruled like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

This is, frankly, a striking *Gemara*. The *Gemara* itself uses the language of *mekal* — Shmuel is explicitly presented as searching for and seizing upon the most lenient available opinion. This is the kind of *posik* behavior that rabbinic literature usually cautions against. One way to understand Shmuel's approach is in the context of the broader trajectory of *Megillat Ta'anit* as a whole. Given that there was already a position of *batla Megillat Taanit* — that these minor holidays had lost their force entirely, particularly once the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed — Shmuel may have been attempting to preserve *Megillat Ta'anit* in a more limited form: acknowledging the holidays themselves while insisting that they cast no shadow on adjacent days. This would be a more sustainable and realistic *kiyum* of *Megillat Ta'anit* in the post-Temple period than the maximalist position.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר בָּאלִי אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא לְבָאלִי: אַסְבְּרַהּ לָךְ, כִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — אַדְּלָא לְהִתְעַנָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly,** the Sage **Bali said** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Ḥiyya** bar Abba **said to Bali: I will explain** this ruling **to you. When Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** he was not referring to all matters. Rather, he spoke specifically **with regard to** the day before those dates concerning which *Megillat Ta'anit* said: **Fasting is prohibited.** However, with regard to those days on which it is prohibited to eulogize, he did not rule in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, as Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that eulogizing on the following day is permitted.

Rabbi Yochanan's position is transmitted through a chain: Bali said in the name of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, *halacha ke-Rebbi Yossi* — the law follows Rabbi Yossi. Rabbi Chiya then clarified to Bali precisely what this means: *asber lach ki amar Rabbi Yochanan halacha ke-Rebbi Yossi* — when Rabbi Yochanan ruled like Rabbi Yossi, he meant only *adela le-itana* — with respect to the *d'lo l'hityana* days. For those days, where the *Chachamim* said the day before is entirely unrestricted, Rabbi Yochanan rules that Rabbi Yossi is correct and the day before is forbidden. But regarding *d'lo l'mispad* days — where Rabbi Yossi goes so far as to forbid even the day after — Rabbi Yochanan does not rule like Rabbi Yossi. The practical result is that, according to Rabbi Yochanan, both categories of days in *Megillat Ta'anit* prohibit the day before, but neither category prohibits the day after.

It is worth noting the geographic dimension here, even if speculatively. Shmuel, in Bavel, sought the most lenient position on these minor holidays, perhaps reflecting the distance — physical and experiential — from the *Mikdash*-centered events those holidays commemorated. Rabbi Yochanan, in Eretz Yisrael, adopted the more stringent position, at least as originally transmitted. Whether that difference in orientation is itself informative is left for the reader to consider.

### Reading Megilla Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כִּסְתַם מִשְׁנָה. וּתְנַן: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ מַקְדִּימִין וְלֹא מְאַחֲרִין, מוּתָּרִין בְּהֶסְפֵּד וְתַעֲנִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say this? But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say** as a principle that **the *halakha*** is always in accordance with **an unattributed mishna. And we learned** in a mishna: **Although** the Sages **said,** with regard to reading of the Scroll of Esther, that **one** may read it **earlier but one** may **not** read it **later,** it is **permitted to eulogize and fast** on these days.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא בְּנֵי חֲמֵיסַר וְקָא קָרוּ לֵיהּ בְּאַרְבֵּיסַר, וּמִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָכְתִיב בִּמְגִילַּת תַּעֲנִית: יוֹם אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר בּוֹ וְיוֹם חֲמִישָׁה עָשָׁר בּוֹ, יוֹמֵי פּוּרַיָּא אִינּוּן דְּלָא לְמִיסְפַּד בְּהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **When** does this ruling apply? **If we say** that it applies to **those** in walled cities, who normally read the scroll on the **fifteenth** of Adar **and** yet this year **they read it on the fourteenth,** a day on which they normally are permitted to fast and eulogize, **but** this cannot be the case, as **are they permitted** to fast and eulogize at all on these days? **But isn't it written in *Megillat Ta'anit*: The day of the fourteenth of** Adar **and the day of the fifteenth of** Adar **are the days of Purim, on which eulogizing is prohibited.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא, אֶלָּא לֶאֱסוֹר אֶת שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה וְאֶת שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה. וְאֶלָּא, בְּנֵי אַרְבֵּיסַר וְקָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ בִּתְלֵיסַר — יוֹם נִיקָנוֹר הוּא! וְאֶלָּא, בְּנֵי אַרְבֵּיסַר וְקָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ בִּתְרֵיסַר — יוֹם טוּרְיָינוֹס הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said:** Since these days are already mentioned in the Bible (Esther 9:18–19), it **is necessary** to state this *halakha* in *Megillat Ta'anit* **only to prohibit** those living **in these** walled cities from fasting and eulogizing **on this** date, the fourteenth, and those living **in these** non-walled cities from fasting and eulogizing **on this date,** the fifteenth. The Gemara continues its explanation of the difficulty. **But rather,** the mishna must be referring to **those** who normally read on the **fourteenth** of Adar, **but who read** the Scroll of Esther early, **on the thirteenth.** However, it is already prohibited to fast on the thirteenth, as **it is Nicanor's Day,** which is a commemorative day in its own right. **But rather,** you will say that the mishna is referring to **those** residents of cities who normally read on **the fourteenth, but who read it** early that year, **on the twelfth;** however, the twelfth of Adar is also a commemorative day, as **it is Trajan's Day.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו, דְּקָא קָרוּ לֵיהּ בַּחֲדֵיסַר, וְקָתָנֵי מוּתָּר בְּהֶסְפֵּד וּבְתַעֲנִית! לָא, בְּנֵי אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, וְקָא קָרוּ לֵיהּ בִּתְרֵיסַר. וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ יוֹם טֻרְיָינוֹס הוּא — יוֹם טֻרְיָינוֹס גּוּפֵיהּ בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלוּהּ, הוֹאִיל וְנֶהֶרְגוּ בּוֹ שְׁמַעְיָה וַאֲחִיָּה אֶחָיו. כִּי הָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן גְּזַר תַּעֲנִיתָא בִּתְרֵיסַר, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: יוֹם טוּרְיָינוֹס הוּא! אֲמַר לְהוּ: יוֹם טוּרְיָינוֹס גּוּפֵיהּ בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלוּהּ, הוֹאִיל וְנֶהֶרְגוּ בּוֹ שְׁמַעְיָה וַאֲחִיָּה אָחִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, isn't** the mishna referring to a case **where they read** the Scroll of Esther **on the eleventh** of Adar, **and** nevertheless that mishna **teaches** that it is **permitted to eulogize and fast** on this day, despite the fact that it is the day before Trajan's Day? The opinion in this unattributed mishna is not in accordance with that of Rabbi Yosei, which means that there is a contradiction between the two statements of Rabbi Yoḥanan. The Gemara answers: **No;** the mishna is actually referring to **those** who normally read **on the fourteenth, but who read it** that year **on the twelfth** of Adar. **And** with regard to **that which you said,** that **it is Trajan's Day, Trajan's Day itself was annulled** and is no longer celebrated, **since Shemaya and his brother Aḥiya were killed on that** day. We learn this **as in** the incident **when Rav Naḥman decreed a fast on the twelfth** of Adar and **the Sages said to him: It is Trajan's Day. He said to them: Trajan's Day itself was annulled, since Shemaya and his brother Aḥiya were killed on that** day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ יוֹם שֶׁלִּפְנֵי נִיקָנוֹר! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הַשְׁתָּא אִיהוּ גּוּפֵיהּ בַּטְּלוּהּ, מִשּׁוּם יוֹם נִיקָנוֹר נֵיקוּם וְנִגְזֹר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let him derive** that fasting on the twelfth is prohibited in any case, **as it is the day before Nicanor's** Day. **Rav Ashi said: Now** that with regard to Trajan's Day **itself, they annulled it, will we** then **arise and issue a decree** not to fast on this date **due to** the following day, **Nicanor's** Day?

The *Gemara* then questions whether Rabbi Yochanan truly held that the day before a *Megillat Ta'anit* holiday is forbidden, citing an apparently conflicting ruling: *halacha ke-stam mishna*. The *mishnah* teaches that although the *megilla* may be read early — on the eleventh, twelfth, or thirteenth of Adar for villages that advance the reading to market days — *mutar be-hesped uv-ta'anit*: the day on which the *megilla* is read early remains a day on which *hespedim* and fasting are permitted. If the day before a *Megillat Ta'anit* holiday were forbidden, there should be no day in Adar on which both early *megilla* reading and *hesped* or *ta'anit* are permitted.

The *Gemara* works through the options. The fourteenth is obviously excluded — it is itself listed in *Megillat Ta'anit* as a *d'lo l'mispad* day. The thirteenth is *Yom Nikanor*, a holiday in *Megillat Ta'anit*. The twelfth is *Yom Turaynus*, another holiday. That leaves the eleventh of Adar — but the eleventh is the day before *Yom Turaynus* on the twelfth, and if the day before is forbidden according to Rabbi Yochanan, the eleventh should also be off limits.

The resolution is that *Yom Turaynus gufei bitulei batluhu* — the holiday of Yom Turaynus was itself nullified, overridden by a later tragedy. *Hoeil venhergu bo Shmaya ve-Achya achiv* — since Shmaya and his brother Achya were killed on that day. Rashi notes that we do not know precisely who these individuals were; the *Aruch* identifies them with those killed in Lod. In any case, the twelfth of Adar lost its status as a holiday because of this subsequent tragedy. The *Gemara* confirms this with the story of Rav Nachman, who decreed a public fast on the twelfth of Adar; when his students objected that it was *Yom Turaynus*, he replied: *Yom Turaynus gufei batulei batluhu hoeil venhergu bo Shmaya ve-Achya achiv* — they nullified it because of the killing of those individuals on that day.

With the twelfth no longer a holiday, the eleventh is no longer the day before a holiday, and thus reading the *megilla* on the eleventh combined with a *hesped* or *ta'anit* is entirely permissible — consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's ruling. One final technical point: the *Gemara* asks whether the eleventh might still be forbidden as the day before Yom Nikanor on the thirteenth. Rav Ashi answers: *hashta ihu gufei batul mishum yom Nikanor nekum ve-nigzar?* If the twelfth, which was itself an established *yom tov*, was overridden because of a later tragedy, surely the residual effect of the thirteenth is not going to revive a prohibition on the eleventh. The overriding event on the twelfth effectively removes it entirely from the halachic landscape, and the eleventh is free of any restriction from that direction as well.

## Nikanor & Turaynus

### Nikanor Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי נִיקָנוֹר וּמַאי טוּרְיָינוֹס? דְּתַנְיָא: נִיקָנוֹר, אֶחָד מֵאִפַּרְכֵי יְוָונִים הָיָה, וּבְכׇל יוֹם וָיוֹם הָיָה מֵנִיף יָדוֹ עַל יְהוּדָה וִירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְאוֹמֵר: אֵימָתַי תִּפּוֹל בְּיָדִי וְאֶרְמְסֶנָּה. וּכְשֶׁגָּבְרָה מַלְכוּת בֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי וְנִצְּחוּם, קָצְצוּ בְּהוֹנוֹת יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו וּתְלָאוּם בְּשַׁעֲרֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, וְאָמְרוּ: פֶּה שֶׁהָיָה מְדַבֵּר בְּגַאֲוָה, וְיָדַיִים שֶׁהָיוּ מְנִיפוֹת עַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם — תֵּעָשֶׂה בָּהֶם נְקָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** In relation to the above, the Gemara inquires: **What is** the origin of **Nicanor's** Day **and what is** the origin of **Trajan's** Day? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Nicanor was one of the generals [*iparkhei*]** in the **Greek** army, **and each and every day he would wave his hand over Judea and Jerusalem and say: When will** this city **fall into my hands, and I shall trample it? And when the Hasmonean monarchy overcame** the Greeks **and emerged victorious over them,** they killed Nicanor in battle, **cut off his thumbs and big toes, and hung them on the gates of Jerusalem, saying: The mouth that spoke with pride, and the hands that waved over Jerusalem, may vengeance be taken against them.** This occurred on the thirteenth of Adar.

The *Gemara* now asks: *umai Nikanor umai Turaynus* — what are these commemorated events? The passage that follows, introduced as a *braita* (though much of the material derives from the *Scolion* of *Megillat Ta'anit*, the traditional gloss accompanying that text), describes the background of each holiday.

Nikanor, *echad me-afarkei Yevanim* — one of the district governors of the Hellenistic regime — would daily wave his hand menacingly toward Judea and Jerusalem and declare: *eimatai tipol be-yadi ve-eremsena* — when will it fall into my hand, that I may trample it? When the Chashmonai kingdom grew powerful and went to war against him and was victorious, *katzetzu behonot yadav ve-raglav ve-tla'um be-sha'arei Yerushalayim* — they cut off his thumbs and big toes and hung them at the gates of Jerusalem. They declared: *peh she-haya medaber be-ga'ava ve-yadayim she-hayu menifot al Yerushalayim te'aseh bahem nekama* — the mouth that spoke with arrogance and the hands that were waved against Jerusalem have had vengeance visited upon them. That the accounts also indicate his head was severed adds to the graphic character of the episode. This is not the Nikanor of the famous gates of the *Beit HaMikdash* — that is a different individual; this Nikanor was a military adversary of the *Chashmonaim*, and his defeat was considered worthy of commemoration in *Megillat Ta'anit*.

### Turaynus Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טוּרְיָינוֹס? אָמְרוּ: כְּשֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ טוּרְיָינוֹס לַהֲרוֹג אֶת לוּלְיָנוּס וּפַפּוּס אָחִיו בְּלוּדְקִיָּא, אָמַר לָהֶם: אִם מֵעַמּוֹ שֶׁל חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה אַתֶּם — יָבֹא אֱלֹהֵיכֶם וְיַצִּיל אֶתְכֶם מִיָּדִי, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִצִּיל אֶת חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה מִיַּד נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִין הָיוּ, וּרְאוּיִים הָיוּ לֵיעָשׂוֹת לָהֶם נֵס. וּנְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר מֶלֶךְ הָגוּן הָיָה, וְרָאוּי לַעֲשׂוֹת נֵס עַל יָדוֹ. וְאוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע, הֶדְיוֹט הוּא, וְאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לַעֲשׂוֹת נֵס עַל יָדוֹ. וְאָנוּ נִתְחַיַּיבְנוּ כְּלָיָה לַמָּקוֹם, וְאִם אֵין אַתָּה הוֹרְגֵנוּ — הַרְבֵּה הוֹרְגִים יֵשׁ לוֹ לַמָּקוֹם, וְהַרְבֵּה דּוּבִּין וַאֲרָיוֹת יֵשׁ לוֹ לַמָּקוֹם בְּעוֹלָמוֹ שֶׁפּוֹגְעִין בָּנוּ וְהוֹרְגִין אוֹתָנוּ. אֶלָּא, לֹא מְסָרָנוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּיָדְךָ, אֶלָּא שֶׁעָתִיד לִיפָּרַע דָּמֵינוּ מִיָּדֶךָ. אַף עַל פִּי כֵן הֲרָגָן מִיָּד. אָמְרוּ: לֹא זָזוּ מִשָּׁם, עַד שֶׁבָּאוּ דְּיוֹפָּלֵי מֵרוֹמִי וּפָצְעוּ אֶת מוֹחוֹ בְּגִיזְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the origin of **Trajan's** Day? **They said** in explanation: **When Trajan sought to kill** the important leaders **Luleyanus and his brother Pappas in Laodicea, he said to them: If you are from the nation of Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, let your God come and save you from my hand, just as He saved Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah from the hand of Nebuchadnezzar.** Luleyanus and Pappas **said to him: Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah were full-fledged righteous** people, **and they were worthy that a miracle** should be **performed for them, and Nebuchadnezzar was a legitimate king** who rose to power through his merit, **and it is fitting that a miracle** be performed **through him. But this wicked** man, Trajan, **is a commoner,** not a real king, **and it is not fitting that a miracle** be performed **through him.** Luleyanus and Pappas continued: **And** we are not wholly righteous, **and have been condemned to destruction by the Omnipresent** for our sins. **And if you do not kill us, the Omnipresent has many other executioners. And** if men do not kill us, **the Omnipresent has many bears and lions in His world that** can **hurt us and kill us. Instead, the Holy One, Blessed be He, placed us into your hands only so that He will avenge our blood in the future. Even so,** Trajan remained unmoved by their response and **killed them immediately. It is said** that **they had not moved from** the place of execution **when two officials [*diyoflei*] arrived from Rome** with permission to remove Trajan from power, **and they split his skull with clubs.** This was viewed as an act of divine retribution and was established as a commemorative day.

Turaynus (*Turnus Rufus* in other sources) was a Roman general who sought to execute Lulianos and Pappus, two brothers, in Laodicea. He taunted them: *im me-amo shel Chananya Mishael ve-Azarya atem yavo Eloheichem ve-yatzil etchem mi-yadi* — if you are truly of the people of Chananya, Mishael, and Azarya, let your God come and save you from my hand, as He saved them from the hand of Nevuchadnezzar.

Their response is remarkable. They told him: Chananya, Mishael, and Azarya were *tzaddikim gemurim* — entirely righteous — and were *rauyim lehe-asot lahem nes*, deserving of a miracle. Furthermore, Nevuchadnezzar was a *melech hagun*, a fitting king — worthy of having a miracle wrought against him, since God reserves such direct interventions for historically consequential moments and figures. *Ve-oto rasha attah hedyot* — but you, you are a wicked commoner. *Ve-eino raui lehe-asot nes al yadecha* — God will not trouble Himself to perform a miracle through you or against you. *Ve-anu nitchayavnu kelayah la-Makom* — as for us, we are evidently deserving of death in the divine accounting. *Ve-im ein attah horegenu harbe horegim yesh lo la-Makom* — and if you do not kill us, God has many other agents: *harbe dubbim va-arayot yesh lo la-Makom she-pog'im banu ve-horegim otanu*. You are entirely inconsequential to this. Rather, *she-atid lippara damenu mi-yadecha* — God is bringing you into this position so that He may exact vengeance from you for our blood. *Af al pi chen haragan miyad* — nevertheless, he killed them immediately.

But *lo zazu mi-sham ad sheba'u diyufni me-Romi ve-patzu et mocho be-Gizarina* — officers arrived from Rome and struck him down. God's justice was exacted in the end, and the day was commemorated accordingly.

### Holiday Nullification

As noted in the preceding section, *Yom Turaynus* — the twelfth of Adar — was subsequently nullified as a holiday because of the tragedy of Shmaya and his brother Achya, who were killed on that date. This is confirmed by the narrative of Rav Nachman, who decreed a communal fast on the twelfth of Adar, and when his students invoked *Yom Turaynus*, replied that it had been abolished: *Yom Turaynus gufei batulei batluhu hoeil venhergu bo Shmaya ve-Achya achiv*. This episode exemplifies the broader pattern the *Gemara* had earlier described in the abstract — that later tragedies could override the commemorative status of earlier celebrations.

## Overriding Megillat Ta'anit Holidays

### Override Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין גּוֹזְרִין תַּעֲנִית עַל הַצִּבּוּר בַּתְּחִלָּה בַּחֲמִישִׁי, אֵין גּוֹזְרִין תַּעֲנִית בְּרָאשֵׁי חֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught: **One may not decree a fast on the community starting on a Thursday,** so as not to cause prices to rise. Furthermore, **one may not decree a fast on New Moons,**

The *perek* concludes with a series of rulings regarding the relationship between a declared communal fast series and the holidays of *Megillat Ta'anit*. The basic principle is: one does not initially decree a communal fast on days listed in *Megillat Ta'anit*, or on *Roshei Chodashim*. However, if a series of communal fasts has already begun — typically a series of three or seven fasts in response to drought or communal distress — and one of the days of *Megillat Ta'anit* falls within that series, the fast is not interrupted. *Megillat Ta'anit* is overridden, and the fast continues.

### When Series Begins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה הָוְיָא הַתְחָלָה? רַב אַחָא אָמַר: שָׁלֹשׁ. רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how many** fasts **are** considered **a beginning? Rav Aḥa said:** If one fasted **three** fasts before the festive day. **Rabbi Asi said:** Even if one fasted **one** fast before it.

The question then arises: *ve-kama havei hatchalah* — how much of the series must have been observed to constitute a "beginning" sufficient to override *Megillat Ta'anit*? Rav Acha says three fasts must have been observed — this is only relevant to a series of seven, since once three have been completed the series has clearly begun. Rav Ashi says even one fast is sufficient; having begun the series at all permits its completion even through a *Megillat Ta'anit* holiday. Rav Yehudah said in the name of Rav: this entire question — how much of a series is needed and whether it overrides *Megillat Ta'anit* — represents the position of Rabbi Meir, who said it in the name of Rabban Gamliel.

### Ending the Fast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מִתְעַנֶּה וּמַשְׁלִים. דָּרֵשׁ מָר זוּטְרָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא: הֲלָכָה מִתְעַנֶּה וּמַשְׁלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: This** *halakha* of the mishna that a fast that occurs on a festival is not observed, **is the statement of Rabbi Meir, who said** it **in the name of Rabban Gamliel. However, the Rabbis say:** If a communal fast occurs on one of these days, one must **fast and complete** the fast until nightfall. **Mar Zutra taught in the name of Rav Huna:** The practical ***halakha*** is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, that one **fasts and completes** his fast until nightfall.

Rabbi Meir's position in the *mishnah* is that when the series overrides *Megillat Ta'anit*, the community does not conclude the fast in the ordinary sense — that is, one fasts for most of the day but does not fully complete it, in deference to the holiday status of the day. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, say *mitaneh u-mashlim* — one fasts and completes the fast to the end of the day, treating the override as complete.

### Final Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ סֵדֶר תַּעֲנִיּוֹת כֵּיצַד
>
> **Translation:** [conclusion of tractate]

Mar Zutra taught in the name of Rav Huna: *halacha mitaneh u-mashlim* — the law follows the *Chachamim*, and when a communal fast series overrides *Megillat Ta'anit*, the fast is completed to the end of the day. This ruling is itself another sign of the diminished standing of *Megillat Ta'anit* in the post-Temple era: the holiday can be overridden entirely and without qualification by a communal fast that has already begun.

*Hadran lach Seder Taaniyot Keitzad.*
