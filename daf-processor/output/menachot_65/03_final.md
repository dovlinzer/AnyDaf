# Menachot 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Omer from Nearby

### Omer Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִצְוַת הָעוֹמֶר לְהָבִיא מִן הַקָּרוֹב, לֹא בִּיכֵּר הַקָּרוֹב לִירוּשָׁלַיִם – מְבִיאִין אוֹתוֹ מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבָּא הָעוֹמֶר מִגַּגּוֹת צְרִיפִין, וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם מִבִּקְעַת עֵין סוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The mitzva of the *omer* is to bring** the barley reaped for the meal offering **from** fields **proximate** to Jerusalem. If the barley **did not ripen** in **the** fields **proximate to Jerusalem, one brings it from any place** in Eretz Yisrael. There was **an incident where the *omer* came from Gaggot Tzerifin and** the wheat for **the two loaves** on *Shavuot* came **from the valley of Ein Sokher.**

The *mishnah* opens with the rule that the *mitzvah* of the *omer* is to bring it from grain that is near Jerusalem — *mitzvas ha'omer lehavi min hakarov*. If the grain nearest to Jerusalem had not yet ripened, one may bring it from anywhere else in the land of Israel. The *gemara* had already established in an explicit *mishnah* that the *omer* may only come from grain grown in Israel, but the ideal is grain grown as close to Jerusalem as possible.

### Karmel & Avir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מִשּׁוּם ״כַּרְמֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that the barley reaped for the *omer* meal offering should ideally be brought from fields proximate to Jerusalem? The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that it is **because** the verse states: "And if you bring a meal offering of first fruits to the Lord, you shall bring for the meal offering of your first fruits grain in the ear parched with fire, even groats of the **fresh ear [*karmel*]"** (Leviticus 2:14). This indicates that the grain should be soft and fresh. Consequently it should be brought from close by, not from a place where it might become stale and hardened during a long journey.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין מַעֲבִירִין עַל הַמִּצְווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the reason is **due to** the principle that **one does not postpone** performance of **the mitzvot.** When presented with the opportunity to perform a mitzva, one should perform it immediately. Therefore, the barley for the mitzva of the *omer* meal offering in the Temple should be brought from the first crop encountered outside of Jerusalem.

*Tosafos* dedicates considerable discussion to delineating what exactly constitutes *ma'avir al hamitzvos*. The classic illustration from elsewhere in Menachot involves *tefillin*: the *tefillin shel yad* is put on before the *tefillin shel rosh*, but if one reaches into one's bag and inadvertently grasps the *tefillin shel rosh* first, the principle of *ein ma'avirin* would seem to require placing it on immediately. Yet in practice one does set it aside to first don the *tefillin shel yad*, because the correct order itself is a competing *halachic* priority — so the two principles come into tension.

The application here, however, is somewhat unusual. The ordinary understanding of *ein ma'avirin* involves literally passing over a *mitzvah* object that one has already encountered. Here, one need not have encountered the nearby grain at all — the principle seems to extend even to a case where one is simply choosing a direction: going east toward closer grain rather than west toward more distant grain. This broader application — choosing the more proximate opportunity even before physically encountering it — appears to be included within the rubric of *ein ma'avirin al hamitzvos*, though it is somewhat surprising that it reaches that far. One might note, as was suggested, that the *mishnah* likely means this only when the nearby grain satisfies all the required criteria equally; if all conditions are equal, naturally one goes closer. Whether the principle applies even when the more distant grain is of appreciably better quality is a genuine question.

### Hashmonean Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבָּא [הָעוֹמֶר] מִגַּגּוֹת צְרִיפִין. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁצָּרוּ מַלְכֵי בֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי זֶה עַל זֶה, וְהָיָה הוּרְקָנוֹס מִבַּחוּץ וַאֲרִיסְטוֹבְּלוּס מִבִּפְנִים, בְּכׇל יוֹם וָיוֹם הָיוּ מְשַׁלְשְׁלִין לָהֶן דִּינָרִין בְּקוּפָּה וּמַעֲלִין לָהֶן תְּמִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: There was **an incident where the *omer* came from Gaggot Tzerifin** and the two loaves on *Shavuot* came from the valley of Ein Sokher. **The Sages taught** a *baraita* that provides the background of this event: **When the kings of the Hasmonean monarchy besieged each other** in their civil war, **Hyrcanus was outside of** Jerusalem, besieging it, **and Aristoblus was inside. On each and every day they would lower dinars in a box** from inside the city, **and** those on the outside **would send up** animals for them to bring the **daily offerings** in the Temple.

The *mishnah* then notes a historical anomaly: *maaseh sheba omer migagot tzerifin* — there was an occasion when the *omer* had to be brought from a place called *Gagot Tzerifin*, the rooftops of *Tzerifin* (referring to a village whose name evokes teepee-like structures), and when the *shtei ha'lechem* had to come from a valley called *Ein Socher*. The *gemara* will now explain why this was so, and the explanation involves one of the most striking and historically resonant narratives in this entire *sugya*.

## Siege Narrative (II)

### Greek Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה שָׁם זָקֵן אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מַכִּיר בְּחׇכְמַת יְוָונִית, לָעַז לָהֶם בְּחׇכְמַת יְוָונִית, אָמַר לָהֶן: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁעֲסוּקִין בַּעֲבוֹדָה אֵין נִמְסָרִין בְּיֶדְכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain elderly man was there,** in Jerusalem, **who was familiar with Greek wisdom. He communicated to** those on the outside **by** using words understood only by those proficient in **Greek wisdom. The elderly man said to them: As long as they are engaged with the** Temple **service, they will not be delivered into your hands.**

In this moment, with Hurkanus outside and Aristobulus inside, a remarkable arrangement existed: every day those inside the walls would lower a basket with *dinarim*, and in return those outside — despite being enemy troops prosecuting a siege — would send up lambs to be used as *temidim*. Whatever their quarrel over kingship, the Temple service was treated as a separate matter entirely: at least for the moment, both sides acknowledged the sanctity of the *avodah* and cooperated to maintain it.

There was at this time a certain elder inside Jerusalem who was *makkir be'chochmat Yevanit* — he was conversant in what the *gemara* calls Greek wisdom. From context it is clear this refers to some form of communication that could not be easily detected by others — perhaps a kind of code, perhaps the Greek language itself, perhaps a system of signals analogous to Morse code. *Tosafos* suggests it may have been a system of signs or signals rather than language per se. Whatever its precise nature, *chochmat Yevanit* here functions as a covert means of communication.

### Betrayal Message

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָחָר שִׁלְשְׁלוּ לָהֶן דִּינָרִין בְּקוּפָּה, וְהֶעֱלוּ לָהֶן חֲזִיר. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לַחֲצִי חוֹמָה, נָעַץ צִפׇּרְנָיו בַּחוֹמָה, וְנִזְדַּעְזְעָה אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת פַּרְסָה עַל אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת פַּרְסָה.
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this, **on the following day,** when **they lowered dinars in a box, they sent up a pig to them. Once** the pig **reached halfway** up the **wall, it inserted its hooves** into the wall and **Eretz Yisrael shuddered four hundred parasangs by four hundred parasangs.**

This elder was in fact a traitor. He used *chochmat Yevanit* to communicate from inside Jerusalem to Hurkanus's troops on the outside: *kol zeman she'asukim ba'avodah ein nimsarim be'yedekhem* — as long as those inside are engaged in the *avodah*, they will not be delivered into your hands. The oddity of this intelligence is worth noting: this is hardly a military secret. The idea that the protection of the Temple service conferred divine protection upon the besieged would have been known to all. One compelling reading of this passage is that the message was about morale rather than theology — that the *avodah* sustained the resolve and morale of the defenders, and that disrupting it would collapse their will to resist.

### Pig Sent

### Curse & Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: אָרוּר שֶׁיְּגַדֵּל חֲזִיר, וְאָרוּר שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּנוֹ חׇכְמַת יְוָונִית. וְעַל אוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה שָׁנִינוּ: מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבָּא עוֹמֶר מִגַּגּוֹת צְרִיפִין, וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם מִבִּקְעַת עֵין סוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** When the Sages saw this, **they said** at **that time: Cursed is he who raises pigs, and cursed is he who teaches his son Greek wisdom. And** it is **with regard to that time** of civil war, in which the land was destroyed, that **we learned: An incident** occurred **in which the *omer*,** the measure of barley brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of Nisan, **came from Gaggot Tzerifim, and the two loaves** offered on *Shavuot* came **from the valley of Ein Sokher.** Since no fresh barley grain was found in the fields immediately surrounding Jerusalem, it had to be brought from these outlying areas.

*Be'otah sha'ah amru* — at that moment they declared: *arur she'yegadel chazirim* — cursed is the one who raises pigs; *ve'arur she'yelamed beno chochmat Yevanit* — and cursed is the one who teaches his son Greek wisdom. These dual curses, emerging from a single catastrophic moment, will each require further analysis.

The connection to the *mishnah* is now explained: *rashi* notes that when an army lays siege to a city, it employs a scorched-earth strategy, burning the surrounding fields to cut off the besieged from any source of sustenance. As a result, all the grain fields near Jerusalem were destroyed. Even if the troops outside may have continued to assist with *korbanot* for a time, there was simply no nearby grain available for the *omer* or the *shtei ha'lechem*. It was during this period that the *omer* was brought from the distant *Gagot Tzerifin*, and the *shtei ha'lechem* from the valley of *Ein Socher*. Alternatively, it is possible that this occurred after the city was conquered — once the siege ended, those who had just won would have needed to bring these offerings, but found that the surrounding fields had already been destroyed.

## Mute's Signal (II)

### Gagot Tzerifin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מְטָא עוֹמֶר, לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי מֵהֵיכָא אַיְיתִי עוֹמֶר. אַכְרֻזוֹ, אֲתָא הָהוּא חַרְשָׁא, אוֹתֵיב חֲדָא יְדָא אַאִיגָּרָא וַחֲדָא יְדֵיהּ אַצְּרִיפָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ מׇרְדֳּכַי: מִי אִיכָּא דּוּכְתָּא דִּשְׁמֵהּ ״גַּגּוֹת צְרִיפִין״ אוֹ ״צְרִיפִין גַּגּוֹת״? בְּדַקוּ וְאַשְׁכְּחוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates another tradition with regard to that occasion when the *omer* came from Gaggot Tzerifin and the two loaves from the valley of Ein Sokher: **When it came** time to bring the ***omer*** meal offering, **they did not know from where** they could **bring** the ***omer*** grain, as all the surrounding fields were looted and ruined. The court publicly **proclaimed** their difficulty. **A certain deaf-mute [*ḥersha*] came** forward and **stretched out one hand toward a roof,** *gag* in Hebrew, **and one hand toward a hut [*atzerifa*]. Mordekhai said to** the Sages: **Is there a place that is called Gaggot Tzerifin or Tzerifin Gaggot? They checked and found** that there was such a place, and it contained fields of barley from which they were able to bring the *omer* meal offering.

*Ki mata omer* — when it was time to bring the *omer*, *lo havu yadei me'heicha le'ityei* — they did not know where to obtain barley. An announcement was made: does anyone know of a suitable field? *Ata hahu charasha* — a certain *cheresh* arrived. While *cheresh* typically denotes a deaf-mute, the emphasis in this context is on his muteness; presumably he heard the announcement through some other means or was informed by others. This mute person placed one hand on a roof of a building — *igra* — and the other hand on a teepee-like structure — *tzerifa*. He gestured simultaneously toward a rooftop and something resembling a pointed hut.

*Amar lehu Mordechai* — Mordechai, who was present, addressed those around him: *mi ika duchta de'shama Gagot Tzerifin o Tzerifin Gagot?* — is there a place called Gagot Tzerifin, or Tzerifin Gagot? For the mute had placed one hand on the *gagot* (roofs) and one on the *tzerifin* (the huts). They investigated and found that indeed such a place existed, *Gagot Tzerifin*, and from there they brought the barley for the *omer*.

*Rashi* identifies this Mordechai as the original Mordechai of the Purim story; *Tosafos* demurs, noting the chronological difficulty this creates, and suggests it was a different person who bore the same name and was similarly distinguished for wisdom and leadership. The *gemara* itself will ultimately weigh in on this identification.

### Ein Socher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי בָּעֵי לְאֵתוֹיֵי שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, לָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי מֵהֵיכָא לְאֵתוֹיֵי, אַכְרוּז. אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא חַרְשָׁא, אוֹתֵיב יְדֵיהּ אַעֵינֵיהּ וַחֲדָא יְדָא אַסִּיכְרָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ מׇרְדֳּכַי: וּמִי אִיכָּא דּוּכְתָּא דִּשְׁמֵהּ ״עֵין סוֹכֵר״ אוֹ ״סוֹכֵר עַיִן״? בְּדַקוּ וְאַשְׁכַּחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** A similar incident occurred **when they needed to bring the two loaves,** and **they did not know from where to bring** the grain. Again the court publicly **proclaimed** their difficulty, and **a certain deaf-mute came** forward and **stretched out** one **hand toward his eye [*a'eineih*] and one hand toward a door latch [*assikhera*]. Mordekhai said to** the Sages: **And is there a place that is called: Ein Sokher, or Sokher Ayin? They checked and found** that there was such a place, and it contained fields of wheat from which they were able to bring the two loaves.

When it came time for the *shtei ha'lechem*, they again did not know from where to bring wheat. Another announcement was made. Again a mute arrived — *ata hahu charasha*. This time, he placed one hand over his eye and the other on a *sichra*, a bolt-hole, the aperture through which a door-bolt passes, which in Hebrew is called a *socher*. Mordechai again interpreted: *mi ika duchta de'shama Ein Socher o Socher Ein?* — is there a place called Ein Socher, or Socher Ein? For the mute had placed his hand over his *ein* (eye) and on the *socher* (bolt-hole). They investigated and found the place called *Ein Socher*, and from there they brought the wheat for the *shtei ha'lechem*.

There is a striking structural parallel running through this whole passage: an elder uses covert signaling — *chochmat Yevanit* — to communicate the means by which the city's defenders could be undermined, leading to the abomination of the pig being sent as a *korban*. Now, a mute uses non-verbal physical gestures to communicate the means by which the proper *korbanot* can be restored. The misuse of cryptic communication brought catastrophe; the correct use of cryptic communication brings renewal. The theme of coded and interpreted messages — their use and misuse — runs through the entire narrative.

## Mordechai's Wisdom

### Three Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ שָׁלֹשׁ נָשִׁים דְּאַיְיתוֹ שָׁלֹשׁ קִינִּין, חֲדָא אָמְרָה: ״לְזִיבָתִי״, וַחֲדָא אָמְרָה: ״לְיַמָּתִי״, וַחֲדָא אָמְרָה: ״לְעוֹנָתִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another story that demonstrates Mordekhai's wisdom: Once, **a certain three women brought three nests** for their obligatory offerings of pairs of pigeons or doves (see Leviticus 15:29). **One** of them **said:** This offering is **for my *ziva*; and one said:** This if **for my *yamma*; and** the last **one said:** This is **for my *ona*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבוּר מִינָּה ״זִיבָתִי״ – זָבָה מַמָּשׁ, ״לְיַמָּתִי״ – לְיַמָּתִי מַמָּשׁ, ״לְעוֹנָתִי״ – לְעוֹנָתָהּ, דְּכוּלְּהוּ חֲדָא חַטָּאת וַחֲדָא עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **understood from** the first woman's statement: **For my *ziva*,** that she had experienced a discharge of uterine blood when not expecting her menstrual period, which would give her the status of **an actual *zava*.** From the second woman's statement: **For my *yamma*,** they understood: **My actual *yamma*,** i.e., she was also a *zava*, as *yam* can mean: Sea, or a flow of blood. From the third woman's statement: **For my *ona*,** they came to the conclusion that she needed to bring a sacrifice **for her time [*ona*]** of completing her purification process from being a *zava*. Accordingly, they understood **that all** these women were obligated to bring **one sin offering and one burnt offering.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ מׇרְדֳּכַי: שֶׁמָּא בְּזוֹב סִיכְּנָה, שֶׁמָּא בַּיָּם סִיכְּנָה, שֶׁמָּא בְּעֵינָהּ סִיכְּנָה, דְּכוּלְּהוּ עוֹלוֹת נִינְהוּ. בְּדוּק וְאַשְׁכֻּח.
>
> **Translation:** **Mordekhai said to** the other Sages: **Perhaps** the first woman **was endangered** in the course **of** her menstrual **flow [*zov*].** Similarly, **perhaps** the second woman **was endangered at sea [*yam*].** Finally, **perhaps** the third woman **was endangered through her eye [*ayin*],** as *ayin* is phonetically similar to *ona*. According to these explanations, each woman sought to bring a voluntary offering to give thanks to God for being saved from danger. If so, the appropriate offering in each case is not a sin offering, **as they are all burnt offerings.** It **was checked and** they **found** that Mordekhai's interpretation was in fact correct.

The natural reading of all three statements was that each woman was a *zava* — a woman who had experienced abnormal uterine bleeding outside the normal menstrual cycle — and was now bringing the required *korban* upon completion of her clean days: one bird as a *chatat* and one as an *olah*.

Mordechai, however, intervened. He suggested an alternative reading of each statement: *shema be'zov sachna* — perhaps she means that because of her flow she had been in mortal danger, not that her flow rendered her *tamei* in the sense of *zivah*; and similarly for the others. If so, none of the three were bringing *korbanot* as *zavot* at all; rather, each had survived a health crisis connected to her flow and was now bringing a *korban toda*-style *olah* in gratitude. In that case, *kulhu olot ninhu* — all the birds are *olot*, not the combination of *chatat* and *olah* required for a *zava*. They investigated and found that Mordechai was correct.

### Mordechai ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּתְנַן: פְּתַחְיָה עַל הַקִּינִּין זֶה מָרְדֳּכַי, לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ פְּתַחְיָה – שֶׁפּוֹתֵחַ דְּבָרִים וְדוֹרְשָׁן, וְיוֹדֵעַ בְּשִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is as we learned** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 13b): **Petaḥya** was responsible **for the nests** of birds, i.e., the doves or pigeons brought by a *zav*, a *zava*, a woman after childbirth, and a leper. These individuals would place the appropriate sum of money into the horn designated for this purpose, and each day Petaḥya oversaw the purchase of birds from that money and their sacrifice in the proper manner. **This** Sage **is Mordekhai;** and **why was he called Petaḥya,** which resembles the word for opening [*petaḥ*]? The reason is **that he would open,** i.e., elucidate, difficult **topics and interpret them** to the people, **and** because **he knew** all **seventy languages** known in that region at the time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלְּהוּ סַנְהֶדְרִין נָמֵי יָדְעִי שִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין מוֹשִׁיבִים בַּסַּנְהֶדְרִין אֶלָּא בַּעֲלֵי חׇכְמָה, בַּעֲלֵי מַרְאֶה, בַּעֲלֵי קוֹמָה, בַּעֲלֵי זִקְנָה, בַּעֲלֵי כְשָׁפִים, וְיוֹדְעִים שִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן, שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא סַנְהֶדְרִין שׁוֹמַעַת מִפִּי הַתּוּרְגְּמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What was unique about Petaḥya? **All** of the members of the **Sanhedrin also know** all **seventy languages. As Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** They **place on the** Great **Sanhedrin only** men **of wisdom, and of** pleasant **appearance, and of** high **stature, and of** suitable **age** so that they will be respected. **And** they must also be **masters of sorcery,** i.e., they know the nature of sorcery, so that they can judge sorcerers, **and** they must **know** all **seventy languages** in order **that the Sanhedrin will not** need to **hear** testimony **from the mouth of a translator** in a case where a witness speaks a different language.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דַּהֲוָה בָּיֵיל לִישָּׁנֵי וְדָרֵישׁ, וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב בְּמׇרְדֳּכַי ״בִּלְשָׁן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** Petaḥya was unique **as** he not only knew all seventy languages, but also had the ability to **combine** various **languages and interpret** them. **This is** the meaning of that **which is written with regard to Mordekhai: "Bilshan"** (Nehemiah 7:7). Bilshan is interpreted as another name for Mordekhai, as he would combine [*balil*] languages [*lashon*].

This episode is the basis for identifying Mordechai with the figure named *Ptachya* in the *mishnah* (Menachot 62a): *Ptachya al ha'kinin* — Ptachya oversaw the *kinin* sacrifices. *Zeh Mordechai* — this was Mordechai. *Ve'lamah nikra shemo Ptachya?* — why would Mordechai be called Ptachya? *She'poteach devarim ve'doresham* — because he was able to open up words and interpret them, to discern what people meant and to articulate what others could not. The name *Ptachya* — *poteach*, opener — captures precisely the interpretive gift we have just seen demonstrated three times over.

The *gemara* adds that Mordechai *yodea be'shivim lashon* — he knew all seventy languages. This prompts an immediate objection: the entire *Sanhedrin* was required to know all seventy languages, as *Rabbi Yochanan* taught — *ein moshivin be'Sanhedrin ela ba'alei chochma, ba'alei mareh, ba'alei komah, ba'alei ziknah, ba'alei keshafim* — only those possessing wisdom, fine appearance, stature, seniority, and knowledge of witchcraft may sit on the *Sanhedrin*. *Rashi* explains the requirement of witchcraft knowledge as enabling the judges to neutralize any attempt by an accused sorcerer to escape judgment through mystical means; *Tosafos* takes a more prosaic view, that judges must simply be conversant enough to evaluate testimony in such cases. And crucially, *she'lo tihei Sanhedrin shoma'at mipi ha'meturgeman* — so that the *Sanhedrin* need not rely on a translator, whose rendering might distort, whether intentionally or otherwise, what was actually said. *Tosafos* notes, realistically, that the requirement may have been satisfied if even one or two members possessed this knowledge and could be trusted to relay accurately what was said to the others.

But given that the entire *Sanhedrin* knew seventy languages, what was distinctive about Mordechai? The *gemara* responds: *de'hava bala lishnei ve'dorash* — he would blend languages together and interpret the mixture. That is, he possessed not merely passive competence in multiple languages but the active capacity to draw meaning from across linguistic boundaries, to hear something expressed obliquely in one language mixed with another and still extract its intent. *De'hainu de'ketiv: Mordechai Bilshan* — this is why Mordechai is called *Bilshan* in the Book of Nechemia. *Bilshan* is *bolela lashon* — one who blends or mixes language. The identification with the original Mordechai of the Purim narrative becomes more plausible, since the figure of *Bilshan* in Nechemia belongs to a period that could chronologically accommodate that identification.

The larger theme drawing together all of these episodes — the traitor using *chochmat Yevanit*, the mute using physical gestures, and Mordechai interpreting the ambiguous words of three women — is the question of communication itself: how meaning is encoded, how it is decoded, and whether the capacity to work across the boundaries of ordinary language is put to use in service of *kedusha* or in its subversion. Mordechai's gift, his ability to *bolel lashon*, to penetrate the surface of speech and find what lies beneath, is the constructive counterpart to the elder's treachery. There is nothing inherently wrong with mastery of indirect communication — everything depends on whether it is directed toward bringing the right *korbanot* or toward introducing the pig.

## Omer Harvest

### Pre-Yom Tov Mark

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד הֵן עוֹשִׂין שְׁלוּחֵי בֵּית דִּין יוֹצְאִין מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב, וְעוֹשִׂין אוֹתָן כְּרִיכוֹת בִּמְחוּבָּר לְקַרְקַע, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא נוֹחַ לִקְצוֹר. כׇּל הָעֲיָירוֹת הַסְּמוּכוֹת לְשָׁם מִתְכַּנְּסוֹת לְשָׁם, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא נִקְצָר בְּעֵסֶק גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How would they perform** the rite of the harvest of the *omer*? **Emissaries of the court** would **emerge on the eve of the festival** of Passover **and fashion** the stalks of barley into **sheaves while** the stalks were still **attached to the ground, so that it would be convenient to reap** them. The residents of **all the towns adjacent to** the site of the harvest **would assemble there, so that it would be harvested with great fanfare.**

The next *mishnah* turns to the practical procedure for harvesting the barley of the *omer*: *keitzad hein osim?* — how was it actually done? *Shluchei Beit Din yotzin me'erev Yom Tov* — emissaries of the *Beit Din* would go out on *erev Yom Tov*, the fourteenth of Nissan, and *ve'osim otan krichot* — they would bind bands around stalks of standing grain, wrapping together the barley they intended to harvest the following night. This was done while the grain was still attached to the ground.

The reason is this: the *halacha* requires that the actual cutting of the *omer* take place on the day of the bringing itself, derived from the relevant *pasuk*. But one need not squander time searching and selecting grain in the darkness. So the *shluchei Beit Din* would invest that preparatory effort on *erev Yom Tov*, marking and binding the grain they had chosen, so that when the time came — immediately after nightfall on the night of the sixteenth — they could proceed directly to cutting without delay. The analogy sometimes drawn is to the practice of scoring the *challah* before making *hamotzi* so that the blessing is not interrupted by searching for where to cut, though in practice the mark is often forgotten by the time the blessing concludes.

### Public Ceremony

### Question Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁהֶחְשִׁיכָה, אוֹמֵר לָהֶן: ״בָּא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ?״ אוֹמֵר: ״הֵין״. ״בָּא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ?״ אוֹמֵר: ״הֵין״. ״מַגָּל זוֹ?״ אוֹמֵר: ״הֵין״. ״מַגָּל זוֹ?״ אוֹמֵר: ״הֵין״. ״קוּפָּה זוֹ?״ אוֹמֵר: ״הֵין״. ״קוּפָּה זוֹ?״ אוֹמֵר: ״הֵין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Once it grew dark,** the court emissary **says to** those assembled: **Did the sun set?** The assembly **says** in response: **Yes.** The emissary repeats: **Did the sun set?** They again **say: Yes.** The court emissary next says to those assembled: Shall I reap the sheaves with **this sickle?** The assembly **says** in response: **Yes.** The emissary repeats: With **this sickle?** The assembly **says: Yes.** The court emissary then says to those assembled: Shall I place the gathered sheaves in **this basket?** The assembly **says** in response: **Yes.** The emissary repeats: In **this basket?** The assembly **says: Yes.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּשַּׁבָּת, אוֹמֵר לָהֶן: ״שַׁבָּת זוֹ?״ אֹמֵר: ״הֵין״. ״שַׁבָּת זוֹ?״ אֹמֵר: ״הֵין״. ״אֶקְצוֹר?״ וְהֵם אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: ״קְצוֹר״. ״אֶקְצוֹר?״ וְהֵם אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: ״קְצוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** If the sixteenth of Nisan occurs **on Shabbat,** the court emissary **says to** the assembled: Shall I cut the sheaves on **this Shabbat?** The assembly **says** in response: **Yes.** The emissary repeats: On **this Shabbat?** The assembly **says: Yes.** The court emissary says to those assembled: **Shall I cut** the sheaves? **And they say to him** in response: **Cut.** The emissary repeats: **Shall I cut** the sheaves? **And they say** to him: **Cut.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים עַל כׇּל דָּבָר וְדָבָר, וְהֵן אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: הֵין, הֵין, הֵין. כׇּל כָּךְ לָמָּה לִי? מִפְּנֵי הַבַּיְיתּוֹסִים, שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: אֵין קְצִירַת הָעוֹמֶר בְּמוֹצָאֵי יוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The emissary asks **three times with regard to each and every matter, and** the assembly **says to him: Yes, yes, yes.** The mishna asks: **Why do I** need those involved to publicize each stage of the rite **to that extent?** The mishna answers: It is **due to the Boethusians, as they** deny the validity of the Oral Law and **would say: There is no harvest of the *omer* at the conclusion of the** first **Festival** day of Passover unless it occurs at the conclusion of Shabbat. The publicity was to underscore that the sixteenth of Nisan was the proper time for the *omer* harvest.

*Kol ha'ayaros ha'smuchos l'sham miskansos l'sham* — all the villages in proximity to that field would gather there. This was done *k'dei she'yehei niktzar be'esek gadol* — so that the harvesting would take place amid great public commotion, a large visible gathering. The reason, as the *mishnah* will shortly make explicit, was to publicize the correct day of the *omer* harvest and thereby the day from which the *sefirah* begins and, by extension, the day on which *Shavuot* would fall fifty days hence. This question was bitterly contested, and the public gathering was a deliberate act of declaration — not merely announcing the date, but staging the consensus, presenting the normative practice as the obvious, universally shared position, and marginalizing the opposing view as the eccentric aberration.

*Keivan she'chashecha* — when nightfall came on the night of the sixteenth, the one in charge would address the assembled crowd in a carefully choreographed series of questions. Has the sun set? — *hen*, yes. Has the sun set? — *hen*, yes. Should I use this scythe? — *hen*, yes. Should I use this scythe? — *hen*, yes. Should I use this basket? — *hen*, yes. Should I use this basket? — *hen*, yes. If the night happened to fall on *Shabbat*, he would add: should I do this even on this *Shabbat*? — *hen*, yes. Should I cut? — *ketzor*, cut. Should I cut? — *ketzor*, cut. Each element was repeated, and the procedure was performed three times over, with the crowd affirming each step with *hen, hen, hen*.

### Anti-Baitusim

*Kol kach lamah? Mipnei ha'Baitusin* — why all of this? Because of the *Baitusin*, who maintained that the *omer* was not cut on *motzei Yom Tov* — the evening after the first day of Pesach — but on *motzei Shabbat*, *mimacharas ha'Shabbat*, on a Saturday night, so that the *omer* would always begin on a Sunday. By staging this elaborate, publicly confirmed, consensual ceremony, the *Beit Din* was making a pointed statement: not merely *this* is what we do, but *everyone here knows* this is what we do, and agreement is total and universal. The form of the questioning — calling for affirmation at every step — shapes the practice as common knowledge rather than contested position.

## Tamid Debate

### Tamid Victory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִלֵּין יוֹמַיָּא דְּלָא לְהִתְעַנָּאָה בְּהוֹן, וּמִקְצָתְהוֹן דְּלָא לְמִסְפַּד בְּהוֹן. מֵרֵישׁ יַרְחָא דְּנִיסָן עַד תְּמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ – אִיתּוֹקַם תְּמִידָא דְּלָא לְמִסְפַּד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **These are the days on which fasting is prohibited, and on some of them eulogizing is prohibited** as well: **From the New Moon of Nisan until the eighth of** the month, the proper sacrifice of **the daily offering was established,** and therefore it was decreed **not to eulogize** on these dates.

The *gemara* quotes from *Megillat Ta'anit* — the ancient register composed during the Second Temple period listing the days on which fasting, and in some cases even eulogizing, is forbidden, in commemoration of various historical victories. *Megillat Ta'anit* is also the source from which we learn of the eight days of Chanukah as days on which fasting and eulogizing are prohibited. Among its entries: from *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* until the eighth of Nissan, *it'ukom tamida d'lo le'mispid* — the *tamid* offering was established, and therefore one may not eulogize on those days.

### Tzadok Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵרֵישׁ יַרְחָא דְּנִיסָן וְעַד תְּמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ אִיתּוֹקַם תְּמִידָא, דְּלָא לְמִסְפַּד – שֶׁהָיוּ צַדּוּקִים אוֹמְרִים: יָחִיד מִתְנַדֵּב וּמֵבִיא תָּמִיד. מַאי דְּרוּשׁ? ״אֶת הַכֶּבֶשׂ הָאֶחָד תַּעֲשֶׂה בַבֹּקֶר וְאֵת הַכֶּבֶשׂ הַשֵּׁנִי תַּעֲשֶׂה בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the *baraita*: **From the New Moon of Nisan until the eighth of** the month the proper sacrifice of **the daily offering was established,** and therefore it was decreed **not to eulogize** on these dates. The Gemara explains **that the Sadducees would say: An individual may donate and bring** the **daily offering,** in opposition to the accepted tradition that the daily offering must be brought from communal funds. **What** verse did the Sadducees **expound? "The one lamb shall you offer [*ta'aseh*] in the morning, and the other lamb shall you offer in the afternoon"** (Numbers 28:4). Since the verse is in the singular form, the Sadducees maintained that even an individual may donate the daily offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אַהְדַּרוּ? ״אֶת קׇרְבָּנִי לַחְמִי לְאִשַּׁי תִּשְׁמְרוּ״, שֶׁיִּהְיוּ כּוּלָּן בָּאִים מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** did the Sages **reply** to refute the argument of the Sadducees? They cited the verse: "Command the children of Israel, and say to them: **My food that is presented to Me for offerings made by fire,** of a pleasing aroma unto Me, **you shall observe [*tishmeru*]** to offer to Me in its due season" (Numbers 28:2). The term: "You shall observe" is in the plural form, which indicates that **all of the** daily offerings **should come from the collection of the** Temple treasury **chamber.** Since during that period, between the New Moon of Nisan and the eighth of Nisan, the Sages overruled the Sadducees, it was established as a period of rejoicing, and it was prohibited to eulogize on those dates.

The underlying debate: *she'hayu Tzedukim omrim, yachid mitnadev u'mevi tamid* — the Sadducees held that an individual could privately sponsor and donate the daily *tamid* offering. Their *drasha* derived from the *pasuk* — *et ha'keves ha'echad ta'ase ba'boker ve'et ha'keves ha'sheni ta'ase bein ha'arbayim* — which addresses the reader in the second person singular, implying that an individual, rather than the community as a whole, could be the donor. In practical terms, this would mean that any given day's *tamid* could be underwritten by a private individual's pledge.

### Pharisaic Refutation

The response: the *pasuk* says *et korbani lachmi le'ishai tishmeru* — you (plural) shall observe my offerings — which indicates that the *korbanot* are communal obligations, and the verse *she'kulam ba'im mi'trumat ha'lishka* — they must all come from the communal *lishka* funds collected through the half-*shekel* levy. Beyond the textual argument, there is a broader subtext here: the Sadducees were framing the *tamid* as something an individual could claim and control, wresting the most central act of Temple worship from its communal foundation. *Chazal*, from their perspective, were defending not merely a *halakhic* ruling but the very communal nature of the *avodah*. That this debate lasted eight days before being resolved likely reflects the reality that such disputes are rarely settled by the force of textual argument alone.

## Shavuot Debate

### Baitusim Shavuot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִתְּמָנְיָא בֵּיהּ וְעַד סוֹף מוֹעֲדָא – אִיתּוֹתַב חַגָּא דְשָׁבוּעַיָּא דְּלָא לְמִסְפַּד, שֶׁהָיוּ בַּיְיתּוֹסִין אוֹמְרִים: עֲצֶרֶת אַחַר הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the next period listed in the *baraita*: **From the eighth of** Nisan **until the end of the festival** of Passover, the correct date for the **festival of *Shavuot* was restored,** and it was similarly decreed **not to eulogize** during this period. **As the Boethusians would say** that the festival of ***Shavuot*** always occurs **after Shabbat,** on a Sunday. Their reasoning was that the verse states, with regard to the *omer* offering and the festival of *Shavuot* that follows seven weeks later: "And you shall count for you from the morrow after the day of rest [*hashabbat*], from the day that you brought the sheaf [*omer*] of the waving; seven weeks shall there be complete" (Leviticus 23:15). Disregarding the oral tradition, the Boethusians interpreted the phrase "from the morrow after the day of rest [*hashabbat*]" literally, as referring to Shabbat, not the Festival day.

The next entry in *Megillat Ta'anit*: from the eighth of Nissan until the end of *Pesach*, *itotav chaga de'shavuaya* — the festival of *Shavuot* was established as it should be. *She'hayu Baitusin omrim, Atzeret achar ha'Shabbat* — the *Baitusin* maintained that *Shavuot* always falls on a Sunday because the *omer* begins on a Sunday — specifically, on the first Sunday following the beginning of *Pesach*, reading *mimacharas ha'Shabbat* to mean literally the day after the weekly *Shabbat*.

### Yochanan Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיטְפַּל לָהֶם רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, וְאָמַר לָהֶם: שׁוֹטִים, מִנַּיִן לָכֶם?
>
> **Translation:** At the time, **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai joined** the discussion with the Boethusians **and said to them: Fools! From where** have **you** derived this?

*Nitpal lahem Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* — Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was dispatched to engage them. The verb *nitpal* is pointed: it derives from *tafel*, something secondary or trivial, signaling from the outset that he was not treating this as a serious intellectual confrontation requiring genuine engagement. He opened: *shotim* — idiots. *Minayin lachem* — where do you get this idea from? No one initially stepped forward to respond. The rhetorical framing had already loaded the debate heavily against the *Baitusin* before a single substantive argument was made.

### Elder's Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הָיָה אָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מְשִׁיבוֹ, חוּץ מִזָּקֵן אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מְפַטְפֵּט כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, וְאָמַר: מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ אוֹהֵב יִשְׂרָאֵל הָיָה, וְיוֹדֵעַ שֶׁעֲצֶרֶת יוֹם אֶחָד הוּא, עָמַד וְתִקְּנָהּ אַחַר שַׁבָּת כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִתְעַנְּגִין שְׁנֵי יָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And there was no man who answered him, except for one elderly man who was prattling [*mefatpet*] at him, and he said: Moses, our teacher, was a lover of the Jewish people and he knew that *Shavuot* is** only **one day.** Therefore, **he arose and established it after Shabbat, in order that the Jewish people would enjoy themselves for two days.**

*Chutz mi'zaken echad she'haya mepatpet knegdo* — except for one elder who began to sputter, or chatter, against him. The verb *mepatpet* is deliberately deprecating — it conveys not reasoned argument but babbling or sputtering. And indeed, the elder's argument is striking precisely for what it does *not* do: he makes no textual argument whatsoever. Instead: *amar, Moshe Rabbeinu ohev Yisrael haya* — he declared that Moses loved the Jewish people; *ve'yodea she'Shavuot yom echad hu* — and knowing that *Shavuot* is only a one-day holiday, he arranged it to fall on a Sunday, so that Israel could enjoy two consecutive days of celebration — the Sunday *Shavuot* combined with whatever immediately preceded or followed. The pastoral concern for Israel's enjoyment of a two-day celebration is the entirety of the argument.

### Yochanan Refute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרָא עָלָיו מִקְרָא זֶה: ״אַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם מֵחוֹרֵב דֶּרֶךְ הַר שֵׂעִיר״. וְאִם מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ אוֹהֵב יִשְׂרָאֵל הָיָה, לָמָּה אִיחֲרָן בְּמִדְבָּר אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה?
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **recited this verse** in response **to** that old man: **"It is eleven days' journey from Horeb to Kadesh Barnea by the way of Mount Seir"** (Deuteronomy 1:2). **And if Moses, our teacher, was a lover of the Jewish people, why did he delay them in the wilderness forty years?**

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai replied with the *pasuk*: *achad asar yom me'Chorev derech Har Seir* — the journey from Sinai to the Land of Israel was eleven days. *Im Moshe Rabbeinu ohev Yisrael haya, lama icharam ba'midbar arbaim shana?* — if Moses loved Israel so much as to arrange the calendar for their pleasure, why did he keep them wandering in the desert for forty years when they were eleven days from the Land? The argument is not so much a refutation as a reductio: if you invoke Moses's love for Israel to explain any particular arrangement, you must contend with the forty years of wandering, which sits poorly with the premise.

### Debate End

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, בְּכָךְ אַתָּה פּוֹטְרֵנִי? אָמַר לוֹ: שׁוֹטֶה! וְלֹא תְּהֵא תּוֹרָה שְׁלֵמָה שֶׁלָּנוּ כְּשִׂיחָה בְּטֵילָה שֶׁלָּכֶם!
>
> **Translation:** The elderly man **said to him: My teacher, you dismiss me with this** retort? Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to him: Fool! And will our perfect Torah not be** as worthy **as your frivolous speech?** Your claim can easily be refuted.

The elder responded: *bakifach ata potrani* — is this really how you dismiss me, with that kind of response? Give me a serious answer. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai replied: *bakhshum shoteh* — idiot. *She'lo tehei Torah shelemah shelanu ke'sicha batela shelachem* — our complete Torah cannot be equated with your idle chatter. We cannot have a debate on equal terms; we possess the truth, and I will not dignify your position by engaging it substantively.

This rhetorical strategy is worth reflecting upon. The *peshat* of the *pesukim* is not obviously on the Pharisaic side — *mimacharas ha'Shabbat* reads quite naturally, in its literal sense, as meaning the day after the weekly *Shabbat*. If you believe your tradition is correct but recognize that the opposing side has a strong textual argument, it may be that the only path to rhetorical victory is to reframe the debate entirely — to declare the other side absurd before the argument begins, to deny them a legitimate standing to engage. That seems to be what is happening here. *Rashi* suggests that the victory over the *Baitusin* in the *Shavuot* debate was commemorated with a lesser status on *Megillat Ta'anit* — only *d'lo le'itana*, not to fast, rather than the stricter *d'lo le'mispid*, not to eulogize — because it was such an easy victory, the *proofs* being so overwhelming. *Tosafos* takes the opposite view: the lesser commemoration reflects not an easy victory but a less conclusive one, which is precisely why the *gemara* now proceeds to marshal those proofs.

## Textual Proofs

### Two Pesukim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״תִּסְפְּרוּ חֲמִשִּׁים יוֹם״, וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״שֶׁבַע שַׁבָּתוֹת תְּמִימֹת תִּהְיֶינָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Zakkai cites a proof that *Shavuot* does not need to occur specifically on a Sunday. **One verse states:** "Even to the morrow after the seventh week **you shall number fifty days;** and you shall present a new meal offering to the Lord" (Leviticus 23:16), **and one verse,** the preceding one, apparently contradicts this when it **states:** "And you shall count for you from the morrow after the day of rest, from the day that you brought the sheaf of the waving; **seven weeks shall there be complete."** Is the festival of *Shavuot* seven full weeks after Passover, i.e., counting from Sunday through Shabbat seven times; or is it fifty days after Passover?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? כָּאן – בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת, כָּאן – בְּיוֹם טוֹב שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּאֶמְצַע שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **How so,** i.e., how can one reconcile these two verses? **Here,** the verse that mentions seven complete weeks, is referring to a year **when the festival** of Passover **occurs on Shabbat.** In such a year, the fifty-day period between Passover and *Shavuot* contains seven complete weeks, from Sunday through Shabbat. **There,** the verse that defines the period as fifty days, is referring to a year **when the festival** of Passover **occurs in the middle of the week.**

The remainder of the *daf* is devoted to the very proofs that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai declined to engage with — demonstrating that the counting of the *omer* begins the day after *Yom Tov*, not the day after the weekly *Shabbat*. The *gemara* opens by confronting an apparent contradiction between two *pesukim*. One *pasuk* says *tispru chamishim yom* — you shall count fifty days; another says *sheva shabbatot temimot tihyena* — there shall be seven complete weeks. How can both be true simultaneously?

*Kan be'Yom Tov she'chal lihyot be'Shabbat, kan be'Yom Tov she'chal lihyot be'emtza Shabbat* — one *pasuk* describes the case where *Yom Tov* falls on *Shabbat*, and the other describes the case where *Yom Tov* falls during the week. When *Yom Tov* falls on *Shabbat*, the counting begins on Sunday, producing seven perfectly complete calendar weeks — *sheva shabbatot temimot*. But when *Yom Tov* falls on any other day of the week, the counting begins mid-week, and the weeks are not complete in the sense of running Sunday through Saturday. This is consistent only if *Shabbat* in the phrase *mimacharas ha'Shabbat* means *Yom Tov* and not the weekly *Shabbat* — for the weekly *Shabbat* would always produce a Sunday start and always yield complete weeks, making the reference to *chamishim yom* in the other *pasuk* inexplicable.

One might ask: why does one *pasuk* say fifty days if the count is forty-nine? *Tosafos* offers two answers. First, *tispru chamishim yom* means you count *until* the fiftieth day, with the fiftieth day itself — *Shavuot* — not included in the count: *ad ve'lo ad bi'khlal*. Alternatively, the verse *ad mimacharas ha'shabbat ha'shvi'it tispru chamishim yom ve'hikravtem mincha chadasha la'Hashem* means that day 50 is *Shavuot*, the day the *mincha chadasha* is brought, but *tispru chamishim yom* does not mean you count fifty full days of *sefirah*. The *Rosh* offers a striking third answer: the Torah rounds numbers, just as the Torah says forty lashes and *Chazal* interpret this as thirty-nine — so too, fifty here means forty-nine. It is notable that this question of why the Torah says fifty when we count forty-nine is not addressed in the *gemara* itself but left to the *rishonim*.

### Eliezer Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״תִּסְפׇּר לָךְ״, סְפִירָה תְּלוּיָה בְּבֵית דִּין, שֶׁהֵם יוֹדְעִים לְחַדֵּשׁ. ״מִמָּחֳרַת הַשַּׁבָּת״ – מָחֳרַת יוֹם טוֹב, יָצָאת שַׁבַּת בְּרֵאשִׁית שֶׁסְּפִירָתָהּ בְּכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The previous proof **is not necessary, as** the verse **states:** "Seven weeks **you shall number for you;** from the time the sickle is first put to the standing grain you shall begin to number seven weeks" (Deuteronomy 16:9). The term "for you" indicates that the **counting** of the weeks **is dependent upon** the decision of the **court, as they know** how to **calculate the new** months, upon which the date of the Festival depends. Therefore, when the verse states: **"The morrow after the day of rest [*hashabbat*]"** (Leviticus 23:16), it means: **The morrow after the Festival,** as the determination of Festivals is by the court. This serves to **exclude** the interpretation that the counting starts after the **Shabbat of Creation,** i.e., a regular weekly Shabbat, **whose counting** can be performed **by every person,** not exclusively by the court.

*Rabbi Eliezer* offers an independent proof. He says *eino tzarich* — there is no need for the previous argument; a different *pasuk* suffices. The verse says *tispor lach* — count for yourself, in the singular. The general approach of *Chazal* when confronted with singular language in the *Torah* is to read it as addressing the collective of Israel as a single corporate entity. Here, that singular *tispor lach* implies that the counting is not an act performed by separate individuals but an act tied to a collective determination. What collective act is tied to this count? The sanctification of the new month by *Beit Din*, which determines when *Pesach* falls.

### Pesach Depends

*Sefira tluyah be'Beit Din* — the counting depends upon *Beit Din*. Elsewhere, the verse says *u'sfartem lachem* — count for yourselves, in the plural — from which it is derived that each individual counts for himself, the *sefirah* being an obligation upon every person. The singular *tispor lach*, in contrast, refers to a counting that is corporate, dependent on a determination made by *Beit Din* representing *Klal Yisrael*. Now, the weekly *Shabbat* is not determined by *Beit Din* — it comes and goes on its fixed natural cycle regardless of any judicial act. But *Pesach* is determined by *Beit Din*, since it depends on the *sanctification of the new month*: if *Beit Din* establishes *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* on a particular day, *Pesach* follows fifteen days later, and the *omer* begins the next day. *Yatzat Shabbat bereishit* — the ordinary weekly *Shabbat* is thereby excluded from being the referent, since the *sefirah* tied to *Beit Din*'s determination — *she'sfira be'chol adam*, the individual counting — is a separate matter. The counting, in other words, is anchored not in the recurring natural rhythm of the week but in the juridical act of *Beit Din* that establishes the calendar. It therefore begins the day after *Yom Tov* — which is determined by *Beit Din* — and not the day after the weekly *Shabbat*, which is not.

The *gemara* will continue with further proofs on the following *daf*, building the case that *Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* dismissed as too obvious to require demonstration — a dismissal that the *gemara*'s own careful reasoning suggests was rather less straightforward than his rhetoric implied.
