# Hullin 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oref & Melika Location

### Shechita vs. Melika Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט מִן הַצְּדָדִין – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, הַמּוֹלֵק מִן הַצְּדָדִין – מְלִיקָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה. הַשּׁוֹחֵט מִן הָעוֹרֶף – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה, הַמּוֹלֵק מִן הָעוֹרֶף – מְלִיקָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה. הַשּׁוֹחֵט מִן הַצַּוָּאר – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, הַמּוֹלֵק מִן הַצַּוָּאר – מְלִיקָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁכׇּל הָעוֹרֶף כָּשֵׁר לִמְלִיקָה, וְכׇל הַצַּוָּאר כָּשֵׁר לִשְׁחִיטָה. נִמְצָא, כָּשֵׁר בִּשְׁחִיטָה – פָּסוּל בִּמְלִיקָה, כָּשֵׁר בִּמְלִיקָה – פָּסוּל בִּשְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who slaughters from the sides** of the throat, **his slaughter is valid. One who pinches** the neck of a bird offering **from the sides, his pinching is not valid.One who slaughters from the nape [*oref*]** of the neck, **his slaughter is not valid. One who pinches** a bird offering **from the nape** of the neck, **his pinching is valid. One who slaughters from the throat, his slaughter is valid. One who pinches** a bird offering **from the throat, his pinching is not valid, as the entire nape is valid for pinching and the entire throat is valid for slaughter. It is found that** that which is **valid for slaughter** is **not valid for pinching** and that which is **valid for pinching** is **not valid for slaughter.**

The Mishnah on 19b contrasts the act of shechita with the act of melika. Melika is valid only from the back of the neck, while shechita is valid from the throat — whether the front or the sides. The basic reason is that there is no topographically specific location required for shechita in terms of the throat; the trachea and esophagus must be cut within a certain space, but shechita is not restricted to any particular side of the neck. The reason shechita cannot be performed from the back is that doing so would break the backbone and sever the spinal cord, rendering the animal a treifa before the simanim are cut. Melika, by contrast, is specifically required to be performed from the back.

### What Is Oref?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי עוֹרֶף? אִילֵימָא עוֹרֶף מַמָּשׁ, מַאי אִרְיָא שׁוֹחֵט? אֲפִילּוּ מוֹלֵק נָמֵי! ״מִמּוּל עׇרְפּוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא עׇרְפּוֹ. אֶלָּא מַאי עוֹרֶף? מִמּוּל עוֹרֶף, כִּדְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: כׇּל הָעוֹרֶף כָּשֵׁר לִמְלִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the statement in the mishna: One who slaughters an animal from the *oref*, its slaughter is not valid, the Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***oref*? If we say** that the reference is to the **actual occipital bone** at the rear of the skull, **why** does this *halakha* apply **specifically** to one who **slaughters** from the *oref*? **Even** if one **pinches** a bird offering from the *oref* it would **also** not be valid, as **the Merciful One states:** "Pinch off its head **adjacent to its *oref*"** (Leviticus 5:8), at the nape beneath the occipital bone, **and not its *oref*. Rather, what** is the ***oref*** mentioned in the mishna? It is **adjacent to the *oref*,** the back of the neck below the occipital bone, **as it is taught** in **the latter clause** of the mishna: **The entire nape is valid for pinching.**

The Gemara now opens by asking: mai oref — what does "oref" mean as used in the Mishnah? The Gemara entertains the possibility that oref means literally the back of the head — that small area at the posterior of the skull where the head meets the neck. If so, then ileima oref mamash, mai irya shocheit — why would only shechita be invalid from there? Afilu moleik nami — even melika would be invalid. Why? Because the pasuk says mimul orpo — opposite the oref — not the oref itself. Hence the Mishnah's use of "oref" must mean not the back of the skull but rather its opposite: the back of the neck. The Mishnah is therefore using the term loosely, and this is confirmed by the continuation: kol ha-oref kasher le-melika — the entire oref is valid for melika. The phrase "the entire oref" implies a length, a span — something that would not apply to the small area at the base of the skull. Oref in the Mishnah, therefore, refers to the back of the neck proper.

### Kol Ha-Oref Kasher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי, דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מִמּוּל עׇרְפּוֹ״ – מוּל הָרוֹאֶה אֶת הָעוֹרֶף, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהוּא יֹשֵׁב מִמֻּלִי״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״כִּי פָנוּ אֵלַי עֹרֶף וְלֹא פָנִים״. מַאי וְאוֹמֵר? וְכִי תֵּימָא: עוֹרֶף גּוּפֵיהּ לָא יָדְעִינַן הֵיכָא, דְּנִדַּע מוּל דִּידֵיהּ הֵיכָא – תָּא שְׁמַע: ״כִּי פָנוּ אֵלַי עֹרֶף וְלֹא פָנִים״, מִכְּלָל דְּעוֹרֶף לַהֲדֵי פָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the phrase **"adjacent to [*mimmul*] its *oref*," adjacent** means a place **that sees the *oref***  and not the *oref* itself. **And likewise,** the verse **states: "And they reside adjacent to me [*mimmuli*]"** (Numbers 22:5); **and** the verse **states: "For they have turned their *oref* unto Me, and not their face"** (Jeremiah 2:27). The Gemara asks: **What** is added by the latter proof, introduced with the term: **And** the verse **states?** The Gemara answers: **And if you would say, we do not know where the *oref* itself** is, so **that we will know where adjacent to it is, come** and **hear: "For they have turned their *oref* unto Me, and not their face,"** from which it may be ascertained **by inference that the *oref* is opposite the face,** at the rear of the head.

The Gemara anchors this in the relevant pasuk: u-malako mimul orpo — the kohen performs melika from what is mimul orpo, opposite the oref. The Mishnah is describing the offering of a bird korban, specifically the act by which a bird is slaughtered for the altar. The pasuk itself is understood to refer to the oref as the back of the head, and mimul orpo accordingly refers to the back of the neck — the place that faces, or is in the direction of, the oref.

### Mimul Orpo Drasha


The Gemara then asks: minah haini milei — how do we know this location? The Baraita teaches: mimul orpo — mul ha-ro'eh es ha-oref — facing what sees the oref. That is, the melika must be done at the place that is, as it were, facing the oref from below: the back of the neck. This is supported by the verse *ve-hu yosheiv mimuli* — "he sits opposite me" — where mimul clearly means facing, or in front of. So if the oref is the lower back of the skull, then mimul orpo is the back of the neck, the area immediately beneath it and oriented toward it.

### Oref Opposite the Face


The Gemara then cites a second verse to establish the meaning of oref itself — for without knowing where the oref is, we cannot know what is mimul it. Ve-chi teima oref gufei lo yadnan heicha — we don't know where the oref itself is located. Ta shema: ki fanu eilai oref velo fanim — "they turned to me their oref and not their face." *miklal de-oref la-hadei fanim* — this shows that the oref is directly opposite the face. If the face is the front, the oref is the back of the head, and mimul oref — opposite or facing the oref — is the back of the neck.

### Am Keshei Oref


This prompts a brief observation: the classic phrase am keshei oref — the stiff-necked people — takes on an unexpected dimension. If oref itself refers to the back of the head rather than the neck, what does it mean to have a stiff oref? A stiff neck suggests an unwillingness to turn, to do teshuvah — but a stiff back of the head is a less intuitive image. The question is noted without a clear resolution.

## Machzir Simanim Debate

### Bnei R' Chiya Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: מִצְוַת מְלִיקָה מַחֲזִיר סִימָנִים לַאֲחוֹרֵי הָעוֹרֶף וּמוֹלֵק. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אַף מַחְזִיר, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מַחְזִיר דַּוְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **The sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya say:** How is **the mitzva of pinching** of a bird offering performed? **One moves** the two ***simanim*** that must be severed in ritual slaughter, i.e., the windpipe and the gullet, **behind the nape and pinches.** There is a dispute among the Sages with regard to the meaning of the statement. **There are** those **who say:** The mitzva *ab initio* is to pinch through the spinal column first and then pinch the windpipe and the gullet, and one may **even move** the *simanim* to behind the nape and pinch. **And there are** those **who say:** The mitzva is **specifically** to **move** the *simanim* behind the nape and pinch.

Amari bnei Rabbi Chiya — the sons of Rabbi Chiya, Yehuda and Chizkiya, stated: *mitzvas melika* — the proper performance of melika — is accomplished by machazir simanim achorei ha-oref u-molayk: moving the trachea and esophagus to behind the spinal cord and then performing the melika. The concern is the same problem that makes shechita from the back invalid — one would break the backbone and the spinal cord before reaching the simanim, rendering the animal a treifa first. To avoid this, the bnei Rabbi Chiya hold that one physically manipulates the simanim, relocating them to the posterior of the spinal cord so that the melika cuts through the trachea and esophagus before encountering the backbone. In the case of a bird olah, only one siman need be cut, while for a chattas both must be severed — and the use of the hand to reposition the simanim may be understood accordingly.

### Davka vs. Af Machzir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אַף מַחְזִיר, מִמַּאי? מִדְּקָתָנֵי: הַשּׁוֹחֵט מִן הָעוֹרֶף – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה, הַמּוֹלֵק מִן הָעוֹרֶף – מְלִיקָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And it is reasonable according to the one who says: One may even move** the *simanim* behind the nape. **From where** does one draw that conclusion? It is **from** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches: One who slaughters from the nape** of the neck, **his slaughter is not valid. One who pinches from the nape** of the neck, **his pinching is valid.**

Two versions of this position are transmitted. Ika de'amrei: af machazir — even moving the simanim is acceptable, but it is not required. Ve-ika de'amrei: *machazir davka* — according to others, moving the simanim is the exclusive valid method of melika according to the bnei Rabbi Chiya, because only this prevents the bird from becoming a treifa midway through the act. U-mistabra ke-man de'amar af machazir — the Gemara registers that the af machazir reading seems more plausible. The question is how to prove this.

### Proof from Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מַחְזִיר דַּוְקָא, מַאי אִירְיָא מוֹלֵק? אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹחֵט נָמֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַף מַחְזִיר, וּמַתְנִיתִין בִּדְלָא אַהְדַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind that** the mitzva is **specifically** to **move** the *simanim* behind the nape and pinch them, **why** did the *tanna* say **specifically** that if one **pinches** in this manner it is valid? **Even** if **one slaughters** from the nape in this manner the slaughter would be valid. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that the proper understanding is: One may **even move** the *simanim* behind the nape and pinch, **and the mishna is** referring to a case **where one did not move** the *simanim* behind the nape.

The proof comes from a reading of our Mishnah: ha-shochet min ha-oref shechitaso pesulah — one who performs shechita from the back of the neck, the shechita is invalid. Ve-ha-moleik min ha-oref melikaso kesheirah — but one who performs melika from the back, the melika is valid. On daf 20a the argument proceeds: ve-i salka da'atach *machazir davka* — if the only valid form of melika requires first relocating the simanim behind the spinal cord, such that the knife never contacts the backbone before the simanim, then mai irya moleik — why would only melika be valid from the back? Afilu shocheit nami — shechita from the back should equally be valid under those conditions. The Gemara takes as axiomatic that if the simanim are repositioned, there is no intrinsic reason why shechita from the back would be prohibited — the trachea and esophagus are encountered before the spinal cord regardless of the direction of approach. Since the Mishnah declares shechita from the back invalid while allowing melika, it must be assuming the standard case in which the simanim have not been relocated. This in turn demonstrates that the repositioning is not the required and exclusive mode of melika, but merely an additional acceptable option — ella lav shema minah af machazir. The Mishnah's ruling applies to a normal melika without any relocation of the simanim.

It is worth noting that melika is performed exclusively in the Beit HaMikdash as part of the korban service — the olah is not eaten while the chattas is. Outside the context of a valid bird korban, melika would render the bird a neveilah or treifa; it is only recognized as a legitimate act of shechita within the framework of a korban offering.

### Rav Yannai's Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: יְקַבְּלוּ הָרוֹבִין אֶת תְּשׁוּבָתָן, דְּקָתָנֵי: נִמְצָא כָּשֵׁר בִּשְׁחִיטָה – פָּסוּל בִּמְלִיקָה, כָּשֵׁר בִּמְלִיקָה – פָּסוּל בִּשְׁחִיטָה. לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי מַחֲזִיר סִימָנִין לַאֲחוֹרֵי הָעוֹרֶף, דְּלָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai says: The young ones [*rovin*],** the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya, **shall receive their response** that rejects their statement from **that** which **is taught** in the mishna: **It is found that** that which is **valid for slaughter** is **not valid for pinching and** that which is **valid for pinching** is **not valid for slaughter. What** does this statement serve **to exclude?** Does it **not** serve **to exclude** the case where **one moves the *simanim* behind the nape,** teaching **that** it is valid only for slaughter and **not** for pinching?

Rav Yannai is unwilling to accept even this concession. Yekablu ha-Rubim et teshuvatan — let the young men receive their response, he says, addressing the bnei Rabbi Chiya with pointed directness. He cites the end of the Mishnah: nimtza *kasher bishchita* pasul bi-melika, *kasher bi-melika* pasul bishchita — what is valid for shechita is invalid for melika and vice versa. Although on its surface this seems to merely summarize what came before — the throat is good for shechita but not for melika; the back of the neck is good for melika but not for shechita — the Gemara assumes this closing formulation is not merely recapitulating but extending to other cases as well. *l'atuyei mai*? What new case does it add? The Gemara takes it as given that relocating the simanim to the back would be valid for shechita — if the trachea and esophagus are moved to the posterior, there is no halakhic reason shechita performed from the back would be invalid. Therefore, what is valid for shechita is not valid for melika — and since *machzir simanim* would be valid for shechita, it follows that it is not valid for melika either. Rav Yannai thus rejects not merely the claim that *machzir simanim* is required for melika but the claim that it is even acceptable.

### Shen ve-Tzipporen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: לָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי שֵׁן וְצִפּוֹרֶן. שֵׁן וְצִפּוֹרֶן בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: No,** perhaps it serves **to exclude** one who uses **a tooth or a fingernail** that is not detached, which are valid for pinching and not valid for slaughter. The Gemara objects: That could not be, as the *tanna* **teaches explicitly** the case of **a tooth and a fingernail** in a mishna (15b), and there was no need to repeat it.

Amar Rabbah bar bar Chana — Rabbah bar bar Chana offers a different reading entirely. Perhaps the Mishnah's concluding formulation is not addressing the location of the simanim at all but the instrument of the act. L'atuyei shen ve-tzipporen — it comes to include the case of a tooth and a fingernail. The reasoning: what is valid for melika — using one's own fingernail — is invalid for shechita, since an attached fingernail cannot serve as a valid shechita implement according to Rabbi (who holds that attached objects, whether attached to the ground, to a person, or to an animal, cannot be used for shechita). The reverse applies as well. So the Mishnah's summary may be teaching about the instrument rather than the location.

This reading is admittedly somewhat forced, since the plain sense of the Mishnah is clearly focused on the anatomical location of the act rather than the instrument. And one might ask why the phrase includes both shen and tzipporen together — the tooth alongside the fingernail.

### Melika With a Tooth?


Tosafot takes this seriously. The basis for using a fingernail for melika derives from the verse u-malako, which is expounded as be-atzmo shel kohen — the melika must be performed with the body of the kohen himself. If so, Tosafot reasons, perhaps the kohen's tooth is equally valid — after all, the requirement is that it be part of his own body, and the tooth qualifies. The only question Tosafot raises is whether the right-side requirement applies: every act performed by the kohen in the avodah must be done with the right hand. But a tooth does not have a right and a left, and Tosafot suggests that the requirement of using the right may apply only to a limb or appendage that possesses a right-left distinction. Where no such distinction applies, perhaps the right-side requirement does not operate. Tosafot therefore reads this Gemara as allowing, in principle, for melika to be performed with the kohen's tooth.

This raises in passing a further question: if melika can be performed with any part of the kohen's body, perhaps even his toenail is valid. The question of blood ingestion is noted as well — but the practical concern would be to spit it out, since the act of melika, as will be discussed, is a piercing rather than a cutting motion, and could in theory be executed with sharp teeth biting through the neck. Rashi, for his part, consistently emphasizes the fingernail and reads the Mishnah's reference to shen as part of a paired citation rather than as an independent endorsement of the tooth as a valid melika instrument. The word kegon in Rashi appears in the context of what is pasul bishchita — the tooth is included among those things — but when Rashi gets to *kasher bi-melika*, he mentions only the fingernail. Tosafot, however, is willing to read the Gemara literally and remains open to the tooth as a valid option.

### Molich u-Mevi Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לְמַעוֹטֵי מוֹלִיךְ וּמֵבִיא. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מוֹלִיךְ וּמֵבִיא בִּמְלִיקָה – פָּסוּל, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא סָבְרִי לַהּ כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר מוֹלִיךְ וּמֵבִיא בִּמְלִיקָה – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yirmeya said:** The statement of the mishna: That which is valid for slaughter is not valid for pinching, serves **to exclude drawing back and forth.** One who pinches may not cut the *simanim* by drawing his fingernail back and forth. Rather, he must press and cut them in one motion. The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says: Drawing back and forth for pinching is not valid, but according to the one who says:** It is **valid, what is there to say?** The Gemara answers: **The sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya hold in accordance with the one who says: Drawing back and forth for pinching is not valid.**

The Gemara objects to the Rabbah bar bar Chana reading as well: shen ve-tzipporen be-hedya katani le-hu — the tooth and the fingernail are stated explicitly in the earlier Mishnah, so the concluding formulation would not be needed to convey that point. Ella amar Rabbi Yirmiya: lema'utei molich u-mevi — rather, the Mishnah's concluding statement comes to exclude the back-and-forth motion. By shechita, the valid act is performed molich u-mevi — a horizontal, sawing, back-and-forth motion. By melika, the valid act is a piercing, downward cut — not a horizontal motion. The horizontal motion by melika would constitute hadrassa, a form of pressure, which is invalid for shechita precisely because it does not involve the sharp cutting action required. Conversely, the piercing motion that is valid for melika is invalid for shechita. This formulation — what is valid for shechita is invalid for melika and vice versa — cleanly captures the distinction between these two modes of motion.

### Bnei Chiya & Molich u-Me…


The Gemara then reasons: henicha le-man de'amar molich u-mevi bi-melika pasul — this reading of the Mishnah works perfectly if we hold that the back-and-forth motion is invalid for melika. But ela le-man de'amar kasher — if we hold that the back-and-forth motion is actually valid for melika, then Rabbi Yirmiya's reading collapses, and we would need to revert to the reading that the Mishnah is excluding *machzir simanim*. Benei Rabbi Chiya savrei lah ke-man de'amar molich u-mevi bi-melika pasul — the bnei Rabbi Chiya would hold that the horizontal sawing motion is invalid for melika. Therefore, from their perspective, the Mishnah's concluding formulation is not addressing the *machzir simanim* case at all; it is addressing the motion — what is valid for shechita (the horizontal cut) is invalid for melika, and what is valid for melika (the piercing motion) is invalid for shechita. This allows their position on *machzir simanim* to remain technically intact, even if Rav Yannai has largely dismantled it from another direction.

### Melika vs. Shechita Summ…


At this point it is worth summarizing the several dimensions in which melika differs from shechita. First, location: melika must be performed from the back of the neck, which by shechita would render the animal a treifa or a neveilah by breaking the spinal cord before reaching the simanim. Second, motion: shechita requires the horizontal sawing motion of molich u-mevi, while melika requires the piercing, downward kotzetz ve-yored motion; these motions are mutually exclusive as valid acts for their respective procedures. Third, instrument: shechita requires a detached knife, and according to Rabbi, an attached object cannot serve; melika by contrast is performed with the kohen's own fingernail — or, according to Tosafot's reading, potentially with his tooth — which is precisely what would be invalid for shechita.

The open questions are whether melika could theoretically become *kasher shechita* if both problematic variables were corrected simultaneously — both relocating the simanim and performing a horizontal cut. The Gemara treats the two approaches as alternatives rather than as cumulative requirements, noting that whichever approach one takes, some element precludes melika from counting as *kasher shechita*. The conclusion is that standard melika — from the back, without relocating the simanim, using the piercing motion and the kohen's fingernail — is always what would otherwise constitute a neveilah, and its validity is entirely anchored in its unique status as a korban act.

## Kotzetz ve-Yored

### Rav Kahana's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מִצְוַת מְלִיקָה קוֹצֵץ וְיוֹרֵד, וְזוֹ הִיא מִצְוָתָהּ. סָבַר רַבִּי אָבִין לְמֵימַר: קוֹצֵץ וְיוֹרֵד – אִין, מוֹלִיךְ וּמֵבִיא – לָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּמוֹלִיךְ וּמֵבִיא בִּמְלִיקָה כָּשֵׁר, וּמַאי זוֹ הִיא מִצְוָתָהּ? אֵימָא: אַף זוֹ הִיא מִצְוָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says: The mitzva of pinching** is that **one cuts** with his fingernail from the nape **and continues downward, and that is its mitzva. Rabbi Avin thought to say: Cuts and continues downward, yes; draws back and forth, no. Rabbi Yirmeya said to him: All the more so that drawing back and forth for pinching is valid.** The Gemara asks: **And what** is the meaning of the phrase: **That is its mitzva,** which indicates that it is specifically in that manner? The Gemara answers: **Say** that it means: **That too is its mitzva.**

Amar Rav Kahana: *mitzvas melika* — kotzetz ve-yored. Rav Kahana states that the prescribed method of melika is cutting downward, a piercing motion. Zu hi mitzvasam — this is the way the mitzvah is performed.

### Rav Ami vs. R' Yirmiya


Sevar Rav Ami lemeimar: kotzetz ve-yored — in, molich u-mevi — lo. Rav Ami assumed that Rav Kahana's formulation was exclusive: the piercing downward cut is valid, the horizontal back-and-forth motion is not. Amar lei Rabbi Yirmiya: kol shekein de-molich u-mevi bi-melika kasher — Rabbi Yirmiya pushes back, arguing that certainly the horizontal motion should be valid for melika. If the downward piercing is valid, the conventional cutting motion should be at least equally acceptable. U-mah zu hi mitzvasah — eima af zu hi mitzvasah: the statement "this is its mitzvah" means only that this is also an acceptable method, not that it is the exclusive one. This is a direct parallel to the earlier debate about *machzir simanim* — the same question of whether a formulation stating a preferred method implies that other methods are excluded (davka) or merely that they are also acceptable (af).

### Shmuel's Kasher Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַכָּשֵׁר בִּשְׁחִיטָה – כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בָּעוֹרֶף כָּשֵׁר בִּמְלִיקָה, הָא פָּסוּל בִּשְׁחִיטָה – פָּסוּל בִּמְלִיקָה. לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי עִיקּוּר סִימָנִין, וְהָא תָּנֵי רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל: אֵין עִיקּוּר סִימָנִין בָּעוֹף!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yirmeya says** that **Shmuel says: Any** place **that is valid for slaughter** on the throat **is correspondingly valid for pinching** on the nape. By inference, any place on the throat that **is not valid for slaughter is not valid for pinching.** The Gemara asks: **What** does this statement serve **to exclude? If we say** that it serves **to exclude ripping** the ***simanim*** from their place before cutting them, which is invalid with regard to pinching just as with regard to slaughter, **but didn't Rami bar Yeḥezkel teach:** There is **no** disqualification of **ripping** the ***simanim* in** the case of **a bird?**

Amar Rabbi Yirmiya amar Shmuel: kol ha-kasher bi-shchita, kenegdo ba-oref *kasher bi-melika* — anything that is valid for shechita, its counterpart at the back of the neck is valid for melika. The emphasis here is not on the instrument or the motion per se, but on the location within the animal's throat and neck. Wherever the anatomy of the throat would make shechita valid, the corresponding location at the back of the neck makes melika valid. What Shmuel is coming to include is that a range of positions along the length of the valid shechita zone, when transposed to the back of the neck, will each be valid for melika.

### Pasul Shechita = Pasul M…


The Gemara then extrapolates the inverse: ha-pasul bi-shchita pasul bi-melika — what is invalid for shechita is invalid for melika. *lema'utei mai* — what is this coming to exclude? Ileima lema'utei ikur simanim — perhaps it comes to exclude the case where the simanim have been uprooted. The psul of ikur simanim is one of the invalidating conditions of shechita: if the trachea or esophagus has somehow been wrenched or detached from its natural embedded position within the flesh of the animal's throat — not merely torn through the cut, but physically displaced from where it sits — the shechita is invalid. Rashi in several places describes this as rendering the animal a treifa, though Tosafot argues cogently that we never find ikur simanim listed as a treifa condition, and that it is better understood as a psul she-chitah — a defect in the act of shechita itself, requiring that the simanim be in their proper anatomical position for the shechita to be valid. The question, then, is whether ikur simanim similarly invalidates melika.

### Ikur Simanim ba-Of

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנֵי רָמִי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל – אֵין עִיקּוּר סִימָנִין בָּעוֹף!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But the *baraita* taught by Rami bar Yeḥezkel: There is no ripping** of the ***simanim* in** the case of **a bird!**

The Gemara rejects this: ve-hatanya Rami bar Yechezkel — ein ikur simanim ba-of — the uprooting of the simanim is not a problem by birds at all. If there is no psul of ikur simanim for shechita of birds, one cannot claim that Shmuel's principle is coming to extend this problem to melika, since the problem does not apply in this context to begin with.

### Hagrama in Melika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְמַעוֹטֵי רֹאשׁוֹ. רֹאשׁוֹ פְּשִׁיטָא? ״מִמּוּל עׇרְפּוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא בְּרֹאשׁוֹ! מַאי ״רֹאשׁוֹ״? שִׁיפּוּי רֹאשׁוֹ, כְּגוֹן דְּנָקֵט מִשִּׁיפּוּי רֹאשׁוֹ וְהִגְרִים וַאֲזַל עַד דִּמְטָא תַּתַּאי, וְכִדְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: הִגְרִים שְׁלִישׁ וְשָׁחַט שְׁנֵי שְׁלִישׁ – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** It serves **to exclude** pinching the occipital bone at the back of **its head;** just as it is not the place of slaughter, it is not the place of pinching. The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that pinching at the back of **its head** is not valid? **The Merciful One states: "Adjacent to its nape," and not at its head.** The Gemara answers: **What** is **its head** that is not the place for pinching? It is **the incline of its head,** e.g., in a case **where one began at the incline of its head and diverted and continued until he reached below** to the place of the *simanim*, where he completed the pinching. Since he began the process in the incorrect location, it is invalid, similar to slaughter. **And** this is **in accordance with** the opinion **that Rav Huna** says that **Rav Asi says, as Rav Huna says** that **Rav Asi says:** If **one diverted** the knife upward and cut **one-third** of the windpipe **and** then **cut two-thirds** within the ring, the slaughter is **not valid.**

Amar Rav Pappa: lema'utei rosho — the head. The Gemara asks peshita — is that not obvious? The Torah says mimul orpo — opposite the oref — which is the back of the neck, velo ve-rosho — and not the head itself. The Gemara clarifies: what Rav Pappa means to exclude is shefui rosho — the slope of the head, the case of hagrama. If one begins the melika too high up, starting at the downward slope of the skull, and works downward until reaching the proper zone of the back of the neck, that is invalid — just as hagrama is a psul shechita when one begins the cut outside the valid zone and finishes inside it. This is confirmed by the rule transmitted by Rav Huna in the name of Rav Assi and discussed in the preceding daf: higram shelish ve-shachat shenei shelishim — if one performed the first third of the cut in the wrong zone and the final two thirds in the correct zone, the shechita is invalid. Rav Pappa holds that the same principle of hagrama applies to melika.

## Ikur Simanim & Birds

### Ikur if Shechita Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָא דְּתָנֵי רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל אֵין עִיקּוּר סִימָנִין בָּעוֹף, לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין שְׁחִיטָה לָעוֹף מִן הַתּוֹרָה,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** With regard to **that which Rami bar Yeḥezkel teaches:** There is **no** disqualification for **ripping *simanim* in a bird, we say** it **only according to the one who says: There is no** source for the **slaughter of a bird in the Torah.**

Amar Rav Acha bereih de-Rava le-Rav Ashi: ha de-tani Rami bar Yechezkel ein ikur simanim ba-of — the statement that ikur simanim is not a problem by birds — lo amran ela le-man de'amar ein shechita la-of min ha-Torah. This principle applies only according to the view that shechita of birds carries no biblical requirement. The reasoning is clear: if shechita of birds is entirely rabbinic, the rabbis may well have constructed a more lenient framework, one that does not require the simanim to be in their anatomically proper position. The concept of ikur simanim as a psul shechita may simply not have been applied to birds because there is no biblical shechita mandate for them to begin with.

### Biblical Shechita & Ikur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ שְׁחִיטָה לָעוֹף מִן הַתּוֹרָה, יֵשׁ עִיקּוּר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא! לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ שְׁחִיטָה לָעוֹף מִן הַתּוֹרָה, אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר דְּהָכִי אַגְמְרֵיהּ דְּאֵין עִיקּוּר, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כִּבְהֵמָה, לְעִנְיַן עִיקּוּר לָא לֶיהֱוֵי כִּבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says: There is** a source for the **slaughter of a bird in the Torah, there is** disqualification for **ripping** *simanim* in a bird as well. Rav Ashi **said to him: On the contrary, the opposite is reasonable. According to the one who says: There is** a source for the **slaughter of a bird in the Torah,** the *halakhot* of slaughter are not explicit and were transmitted to Moses orally, and **it can be said that this is what** God **taught him, that there is no** disqualification for **ripping** *simanim*. **And even according to the one who says** that the halakhic status of a bird is **like** that of **an animal,** as the *halakhot* of the slaughter of a bird are derived from the *halakhot* of the slaughter of an animal, perhaps God taught Moses that with regard **to the matter of ripping** *simanim* **it will not be like an animal.**

The counterargument is stated: amar lei — adaraba, *ipcha mistabra* — the opposite is more logical. Le-man de'amar yesh shechita la-of min ha-Torah — if the requirement of shechita for birds is biblical, icha lemeimar de-hachi agmareih — this may be precisely what God taught Moshe: de-ein ikur — that ikur simanim is not a problem. The Torah gave birds a unique set of shechita laws distinct from those of beheimah. Just as birds require only one siman while animals require two, so too the Torah may have established that ikur simanim does not apply to birds as part of this independently transmitted framework. The phrase agmareih here evokes the image of halakhah le-Moshe mi-Sinai — a tradition given directly to Moshe rather than derived from a verse.

The reason this framing becomes significant is the very basis upon which biblical shechita of birds is claimed. The Torah never uses the word veshachatta in the context of birds. The word vezavachta, from which the requirement of shechita is derived for beheimah, does not appear in any pasuk about birds. The one passage where birds are mentioned in the context of eating — the covering of blood — speaks only of shefichat dam, not of shechita. Therefore, any claim of biblical shechita for birds cannot rest on a standard textual derivation but must rely on a tradition, or be learned from the verse zot torat ha-beheimah ve-ha-of, which links birds to the same legal framework as animals. Given this, the view that shechita of birds is biblical can accommodate birds having their own distinctive rules — including the absence of an ikur problem — because the very content of that tradition may include such distinctions.

### Rabbinic = Modeled on Be…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אֵין שְׁחִיטָה לָעוֹף מִן הַתּוֹרָה אֶלָּא מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים, מֵהֵיכָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ? מִבְּהֵמָה, כּוּלַּהּ מִילְּתָא כִּבְהֵמָה!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says: There is no** source for the **slaughter of a bird in the Torah,** but **rather** it is **by rabbinic law, from where are** the *halakhot* of the slaughter of a bird **learned?** They are learned **from** the *halakhot* of the slaughter of **an animal;** consequently, **the entire matter** of the slaughter of a bird is **like** that of **an animal.**

Ve-afilu le-man de'amar ke-beheimah — even according to the view that the biblical basis for bird shechita is derived from the verse linking birds to animals, it does not follow that every detail of shechita law is fully equated. The ikur principle might still not apply to birds even if the general shechita requirement is biblical. However, the inverse argument has force: ela le-man de'amar ein shechita la-of min ha-Torah, ella mi-divrei sofrim — if shechita of birds is only rabbinic, me-heicha gemiri lah — where would the rabbis have derived their model? From beheimah. Me-heicha gamiri lah mi-beheimah — they would have taken the laws of beheimah as their template and applied them to birds. If so, kuleihu nuscha ke-beheimah — all the details should follow the model of beheimah, including the psul of ikur simanim. Under this view, the lenient ruling of ein ikur simanim ba-of is harder to explain if shechita of birds is rabbinic, because a rabbinic ordinance modeled on biblical precedent would typically import all the attendant stringencies of that precedent.

### Ikur Only by Melika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, אָמַר לִי רָבִין בַּר קִיסִי: הָא דְּתָנֵי רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל, אֵין עִיקּוּר סִימָנִין בָּעוֹף – לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בִּמְלִיקָה, אֲבָל בִּשְׁחִיטָה יֵשׁ עִיקּוּר. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַכָּשֵׁר בִּשְׁחִיטָה כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בָּעוֹרֶף כָּשֵׁר בִּמְלִיקָה, הָא פָּסוּל – פָּסוּל! הָהִיא פְּלִיגָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Rabin bar Kisi said to me:** With regard to **that which Rami bar Yeḥezkel teaches,** i.e., that **there is no** disqualification for **ripping *simanim* in a bird, we say** it **only with regard to pinching, but with regard to slaughter, there is** disqualification for **ripping** *simanim*. The Gemara objects: **But doesn't Rabbi Yirmeya say** that **Shmuel says: Any** place **that is valid for slaughter** on the throat **is correspondingly valid for pinching on the nape, but** that which is **not valid** for slaughter is **not valid** for pinching. The Gemara explains: **That** *halakha* **disagrees** with this statement of Shmuel.

Amar Ravina amar li Rabin bar Kisi: ha de-tani Rami bar Yechezkel ein ikur simanim ba-of — lo amran ela bi-mlikah. The statement that ikur simanim is not a problem by birds should not be read as a general ruling about bird shechita at all. It applies exclusively to melika. Since melika already operates by a set of anomalous rules — it breaks the spinal cord, it uses the kohen's fingernail, it is performed from the back — it is consistent that ikur simanim similarly presents no problem within the framework of melika. But for ordinary shechita of birds — aval bi-shchita, yesh ikur — the standard psul of ikur simanim applies, just as it does by beheimah.

### Conflicts with Shmuel


This position, however, stands in tension with the principle stated earlier by Shmuel: kol ha-kasher bi-shchita kenegdo ba-oref *kasher bi-melika*, ha poslo poslo — what is valid for shechita is valid for melika, and what is invalid for shechita is invalid for melika. If ikur simanim is a psul for shechita of birds but not for melika, this directly contradicts Shmuel's symmetry principle. Ha pligu — yes, Ravina's position is in explicit disagreement with Shmuel's rule. The two positions reflect genuinely competing frameworks for understanding the relationship between shechita and melika.

## Nishbera Mafreket & Neve…

### Shechita vs. Death Intro


A broader question lurks beneath all of these discussions: is shechita a legal definition of an act, or is it a definition of the moment of death? When the Gemara speaks of shechita and the categories that follow from it, is it determining only whether the required ritual act has been performed, or is it also determining when, halachically, the animal has died? This question has bearing on fundamental issues far beyond the laws of korbanot.

### Ben Noach & Eiver Min Ha…


An illuminating example is the following case discussed in the Gemara: if one performs shechita on an animal and the animal continues to thrash — which it will do for some time after a valid shechita — a Jew may take a piece of meat from that animal and eat it, because the animal is halachically shechted. However, if a *ben noach* were to take a piece of meat from that same animal while it is still thrashing, he would be liable for eiver min ha-chai — the prohibition against taking a limb from a living animal. The Jew is exempt because he operates within a halachic category of shechita; once shechita is performed, the meat is permissible. The *ben noach* has no such category; he has only the concepts of alive and dead, and from his perspective the animal is still alive.

This case raises a profound question: does shechita halachically constitute the death of the animal, such that for the Jew the animal is considered dead even while it thrashes? Or does the halachic category of shechita operate independently of life and death, rendering the meat permissible even if the animal is still technically alive? These are two very different frameworks. One says that death is a legal definition — and the Jew's definition of when death occurs differs from the non-Jew's. The other says that life and death are not the operative categories here; rather, shechita permits the meat regardless of the animal's biological status.

This bears directly on the brain death discussion. The question of when death occurs is not simply one of uncovering a metaphysical reality. In the gray zones — where some biological systems continue to function and others do not — it is a legal question: when does the law consider the person to have died? Just as the question of when life begins (in discussions of abortion) or when death occurs is, in those gray zones, fundamentally a legal determination rather than the identification of a pre-existing metaphysical fact, so too the halachic question of brain death requires establishing how the law defines death at its boundary — not merely reading off a biological datum.

### Zeiri's Neveila Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר זְעֵירִי: נִשְׁבְּרָה מַפְרֶקֶת וְרוֹב בָּשָׂר עִמָּהּ – נְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ze'eiri says:** If **the neck bone** of an animal or a bird **was broken and most of the** surrounding **flesh** was cut **with it,** the status of the animal or the bird is that of **an unslaughtered carcass.** It is dead and can no longer be rendered fit by slaughter.

Amar Zeiri: nishbera mafreket ve-rov basar imah — neveilah. If the mafreket — the upper portion of the spine, at the nape of the neck — is broken, and most of the flesh around it is severed as well, the animal is a neveilah. This means that even if one subsequently performs shechita, it is too late; the animal is not merely a treifa — an animal mortally wounded but halachically alive — but a neveilah, an animal killed without a valid shechita act.

This is a striking ruling. The expected category for a mortal wound prior to shechita would be treifa. Zeiri goes further: the animal is a neveilah, which halachically means it has already been killed — without shechita — before any shechita act was performed. And this is the case even though the trachea and esophagus have not been reached, and the animal may still be breathing.

### Melika b'Sakin = Neveila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, מָלַק בְּסַכִּין – מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ טְרֵפָה הָוְיָא, מְלִיקָתָהּ זוֹ הִיא שְׁחִיטָתָהּ – תַּהֲנֵי לַהּ סַכִּין לְטַהֲרָהּ מִידֵי נְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** that **we learn** this in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 68a) as well: If **one pinched** a bird offering **with a knife** and not with his thumbnail, the bird **renders the garments** of one who swallows it **impure** when it is **in the throat,** which is the *halakha* in the case of an unslaughtered carcass of a kosher bird. **And if you would say** that if the neck bone of an animal or a bird was broken and most of the surrounding flesh was cut with it, the bird is not an unslaughtered carcass but **it is a *tereifa*,** then since with regard to a bird offering **its pinching is its slaughter, let** pinching with **a knife be effective to purify** the bird **from** the impurity of **an unslaughtered carcass,** as a *tereifa* does not transmit impurity when slaughtered properly. From the *halakha* that pinching with a knife does not render the bird pure it is evident that when its neck bone is broken the bird is rendered an unslaughtered carcass.

Amar Rav Chisda af anan nami tanina — Rav Chisda finds a corroborating proof from the Mishnah. Malak be-sakin — if a kohen performed melika using a knife rather than his fingernail, the melika is invalid. The consequence: metame begadim be-veit ha-bliah — the bird imparts the tumah of a neveilah when swallowed, which is how a valid bird neveilah transmits its impurity. The bird is thus a neveilah, not a treifa. And what has happened here? The kohen has approached from the back of the neck and cut through, as one would in melika, but using a knife. Rav Chisda reasons: why does this produce the status of neveilah and not treifa? If the cutting through the back of the neck produced only a fatal wound — a treifa — and the subsequent cutting of the trachea and esophagus constituted a valid shechita of a treifa animal, then the animal should be a treifa, not a neveilah. The fact that it is a neveilah shows that by the time the knife reaches the trachea and esophagus, the animal has already been halachically killed — it is a neveilah — and the subsequent cutting of the simanim cannot serve as *shechita because* one cannot perform shechita on a neveilah. This supports Zeiri's ruling: cutting through the back of the neck constitutes killing the animal in a halachic sense, not merely mortally wounding it.

### Dores vs. Machlid Answers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָתָם מִשּׁוּם דְּלָאו שְׁחִיטָה הִיא כְּלָל. מַאי טַעְמָא? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַחְלִיד, רָבָא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא דּוֹרֵס. מַאן דְּאָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַחְלִיד, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא דּוֹרֵס? קָסָבַר: מוֹלִיךְ וּמֵבִיא בִּמְלִיקָה כָּשֵׁר. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא דּוֹרֵס, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַחְלִיד? אָמַר לָךְ: חֲלָדָה הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּחוּלְדָּה הַדָּרָה בְּעִיקְּרֵי בָתִּים דְּמִכַּסְּיָא, הָכָא הָא מִיגַּלְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **There,** pinching with a knife is ineffective in rendering it pure not because the breaking of the neck bone renders the bird an unslaughtered carcass. Rather, it is **because it is not slaughter at all.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason? Rav Huna says:** It is **because he conceals** the knife and performs an inverted slaughter, which invalidates the slaughter. **Rava says:** It is **because he presses** the knife. The Gemara asks: With regard to **the one who says: Because he conceals** the knife and performs an inverted slaughter, **what is the reason** that **he does not say: Because he presses** the knife? The Gemara answers: It is because **he holds** that **drawing back and forth in pinching is valid.** The Gemara asks: **And the one who says: Because he presses** the knife, **what is the reason** that **he does not say: Because he conceals [*maḥlid*]** the knife? The Gemara answers that **he** could have **said to you: What are the circumstances of concealing** the knife? It is **like a rat [*ḥulda*] that resides in the foundations of houses that are concealed. Here,** when he begins cutting from the nape of the neck, **that** knife **is exposed.**

The Gemara rejects this proof. Amri — hatam mishum de-lav shechita hi klal. When the kohen performs melika with a knife from the back of the neck, the reason the animal is a neveilah is not that it was already halachically killed before the simanim were cut. Rather, even when the knife reaches the trachea and esophagus, the act performed is not a valid shechita. Two explanations are offered.

Rav says: mipnei she-hu machlid — because the act involves machlid, burrowing. What is the psul of machlid? In its standard form, this psul occurs when one inserts a knife between the two simanim and cuts them from the middle outward, rather than cutting from the outside in. Rashi explains machlid in this context as follows: when the knife has already cut through the backbone, the bone now overlays the knife as it continues forward, causing the knife to be buried, hidden inside the neck, as it proceeds toward the simanim. The knife is not cutting cleanly from the outside in; it is burrowing within the neck, with the bone covering over it. This constitutes the psul of machlid. Tosafot explains machlid differently — as a problem of reversed order, cutting from top to bottom rather than in the proper direction — and reads the problem as one of procedure rather than of the knife's visual exposure.

Rava says: mipnei she-hu dores — because the act involves dores, pressure or stabbing. Melika requires a piercing motion; that piercing motion, when performed with a knife, constitutes not a valid shechita but the psul of dores — applying pressure rather than using the sharpness of a cutting blade. Therefore the bird is a neveilah not because it was already killed before the simanim were reached, but because the act of cutting the simanim itself was not a valid shechita. And if this is the explanation, one cannot use the case of malak be-sakin as proof that cutting through the back of the neck halachically constitutes killing the animal.

The Gemara then notes: man de-amar mishum machlid, mai ta'ama lo amar mishum dores? The one who says the problem is machlid — why does he not say the problem is dores? Kasavar molich u-mevi bi-melika kasher — he holds that the back-and-forth horizontal motion is valid for melika. If so, he cannot say that every stabbing motion in the context of melika with a knife constitutes the psul of dores, since he allows for the horizontal cutting motion as valid. Therefore he must locate the problem elsewhere — in machlid. U-man de-amar mishum dores, mai ta'ama lo amar mishum machlid? The one who says the problem is dores — why not machlid? Amar lach: *chaluda heichi dami*? The psul of machlid is like a weasel (chulda) burrowing under the foundation of a house — de-michasya, the knife is genuinely hidden, tunneling from the inside. Here — ha hacha migalya — the knife is exposed; it entered from the outside in, cutting through the back of the neck in a visible manner. The mere fact that the bone lies over the knife as it proceeds does not constitute the concealed burrowing that defines machlid. Therefore this opinion locates the entire problem in dores alone.

In either case, the proof from malak be-sakin fails: one cannot demonstrate from it that cutting through the back of the neck constitutes halachic death. The neveilah status results from a defect in the shechita act itself, not from prior death of the animal.

### Rava: Melika on Mesa?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִי קַשְׁיָא לִי הָא קַשְׁיָא לִי, וְכִי מֵתָה עוֹמֵד וּמוֹלֵק?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: If** that which Ze'eiri said: If the neck bone of an animal or a bird was broken and most of the surrounding flesh was cut with it, the status of the animal or the bird is that of an unslaughtered carcass, is **difficult for me, this** is **difficult for me:** How does pinching a bird offering prepare it for sacrifice? Since pinching involves breaking the neck bone and cutting most of the surrounding flesh with it before cutting the *simanim*, what significance is there to pinching the *simanim*? **And does he stand and pinch a dead** bird? If it is dead, of what use is the pinching?

Amar Rava: i kashya li, ha kashya li — if there is something in this sugya that troubles me, this is it. Veci meisa omeid u-molayk? And one stands and performs melika on a bird that is already dead (meisah)? Rava's question cuts to the heart of the issue. If we accept Zeiri's ruling — that breaking through the back of the neck and most of the surrounding flesh renders the animal a neveilah — then by the time the kohen reaches the trachea and esophagus to perform the melika, he is performing melika on an animal that is halachically meisah. How can melika be valid if the bird is already dead before the simanim are cut? And significantly, Rava uses the word meisah — dead — not merely neveilah, which underscores that in his framing the concern is not merely a formal halachic category but the actual status of the animal as living or dead at the moment the melika is performed.

### Abbaye's Olah Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְתִקְשֵׁי לָךְ עוֹלַת הָעוֹף, דְּבָעֲיָא שְׁנֵי סִימָנִין, וְכִי מֵתָה עוֹמֵד וּמוֹלֵק? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם כְּדֵי לְקַיֵּים בָּהּ מִצְוַת הַבְדָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: And** even without the statement of Ze'eiri, **let** the case of **a bird burnt offering be difficult for you, as** it **requires** cutting of **two *simanim*.** Since slaughter of a non-sacred bird requires cutting of one *siman*, once one *siman* is cut the bird is considered dead for all intents and purposes, **and does he stand and pinch a dead** bird? Rava **said to him: There,** he continues pinching **in order to fulfill through it the mitzva of separation** between the head and the body in the bird burnt offering.

Amar lei Abbaye: ve-tukshei lach olat ha-of *de-baya shenei simanim* — and this question should trouble you equally with respect to an olat ha-of. A bird olah requires both simanim to be cut for the melika to be complete, even though ordinary shechita of a bird requires only one. Veci meisah omeid u-molayk — and one stands and performs melika on a bird that is already meisah? After the first siman is cut, the bird is halachically dead — so how can one proceed to cut the second siman as a valid continuation of the melika? Abbaye implies that this is a built-in feature of melika: you perform it even as the animal is halachically dying through the very act. That is simply the nature of melika as a chiddush, and the question of performing melika on an animal that has become meisah mid-act is not a disqualifying problem.

### Havdalah Not Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: הָשָׁם קְדֵי לְקַיֵּים בָּהּ מִצְוַת הַבְדָּלָה. אִי הָכִי, עוֹר נָמֵי! כׇּל הַמְעַכֵּב בִּשְׁחִיטָה מְעַכֵּב בְּהַבְדָּלָה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב בִּשְׁחִיטָה אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב בְּהַבְדָּלָה. וְהָא מִיעוּט סִימָנִין לְרַבָּנַן, דְּלָא מִעַכְּבִי בִּשְׁחִיטָה, וּמְעַכְּבִי בְּהַבְדָּלָה! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בִּשְׁחִיטָה יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּהַבְדָּלָה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בִּשְׁחִיטָה אֵינוֹ בְּהַבְדָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: There,** he continues pinching **in order to fulfill through it the mitzva of separation** between the head and the body in the bird burnt offering. The Gemara asks: **If so,** there should be an obligation to cut the **skin** of the bird **as well** in order to fulfill the mitzva of separation. Abaye answers: **Any** element **that invalidates slaughter invalidates separation, and any** element **that does not invalidate slaughter does not invalidate separation.** Failure to cut the skin does not invalidate slaughter. The Gemara objects: **But isn't** there **the minority of the *simanim* according to the Rabbis, which do not invalidate slaughter,** as, if one slaughtered a majority of the *simanim* and a minority remained uncut, the slaughter is valid, **and** they hold that **they invalidate separation?** The Gemara clarifies: **Rather, say: Any** element **that is** in effect **with regard to slaughter is** in effect **with regard to separation, and any** element **that is not** in effect **with regard to slaughter is not** in effect **with regard to separation.** The two *simanim*, although they do not invalidate slaughter, are part of the mitzva of slaughter, while the skin is not part of the mitzva of slaughter.

Amar Rava: hasam kedei lekayem bah mitzvas havdalah — no, the second siman of the olat ha-of is not being cut as a shechita act at all. The Torah requires that for an olat ha-of the kohen perform havdalah — severing the head from the body. The verse by chattas says velo yavdil; by olah the requirement is the positive act of separating the head. Accordingly, cutting the second siman is not an attempt to perform melika on a dead bird; it is the performance of an independent mitzva of havdalah, the physical separation of the head. You are cutting a bird that is already dead — and that presents no problem, because the act is not shechita but the completion of the korban's procedure.

The Gemara then asks: if so, one should have to cut the outer skin of the neck as well for the havdalah to be complete. The answer: kol ha-me'akev bi-shchita me'akev be-havdalah — anything that is involved in the act of shechita, that is what must be severed for the havdalah requirement to be satisfied. Ve-chol she-eino me'akev bi-shchita eino me'akev be-havdalah — what is not part of the shechita act — such as the outer skin — is not required for havdalah. The Gemara then refines this formulation: kol she-yeshno bi-shchita yeshno be-havdalah — anything that can be part of a shechita act is part of the havdalah requirement — ve-chol she-eino bi-shchita eino be-havdalah. This reformulation accounts for the Rabbinically required complete severance of the trachea and esophagus for the bird olah, even though for shechita only a rov is needed — the potential inclusion of each part of those simanim in the shechita act means they are all required for havdalah as well.

### Rava's Melika Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא! אָמַר רָבָא, אֵימָא: וְכֵן הוּא עוֹשֶׂה – חוֹתֵךְ שִׁדְרָה וּמַפְרֶקֶת בְּלֹא רוֹב בָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **In any case,** the statement of Ze'eiri remains **difficult.** What is the significance of pinching a dead bird? **Rava said: Say** in explanation: **And likewise he does** when he pinches, **he cuts** the spinal **column and** the **neck bone without a majority of** the surrounding **flesh** and then he pinches the *simanim*.

But we remain with Rava's original question about standard melika: if the bird becomes meisah once the back of the neck is broken through and most of the flesh severed, how can the kohen then perform melika on a dead bird? Miklal makom kashya. Amar Rava: eima ve-chen hu oseh — we must say that this is precisely what the correct procedure of melika requires. Rather than rejecting Zeiri's ruling that nishbera mafreket ve-rov basar is a neveilah, Rava instead revises our understanding of how melika is to be performed.

Chotech shidrah u-mafreket, belo rov basar — the kohen cuts through the spinal cord and the nape, but without severing most of the surrounding flesh. The melika must be performed as a precise, targeted piercing that gets through the backbone to the trachea and esophagus while carefully not tearing away the majority of the flesh that holds the neck together. If most of the flesh is severed, the bird becomes a neveilah before the simanim are reached, and the melika is invalid. But if the kohen cuts through the backbone without severing most of the flesh, the bird remains halachically alive — not yet a neveilah — until the simanim are cut. The act of melika is completed on a living bird, and the cutting of the simanim constitutes a valid melika.

This is a demanding halakhic requirement: a piercing cut through the backbone that reaches the trachea and esophagus without cutting away the majority of the surrounding flesh. The precise anatomical mechanics — whether the critical flesh surrounds the spinal cord or occupies a different part of the neck — remain somewhat unclear. But the principle is halakhically clear: the bird must be kept alive in a halachic sense until the moment of melika, and the procedure must be calibrated accordingly.

### Treifa Midway Is Normal


Rashi takes care to address an obvious question: the procedure just described involves cutting through the backbone, which creates a fatal wound — rendering the bird a treifa — before the simanim are cut. Why is that acceptable? Rashi explains that this is not a conceptual problem at all, because every act of shechita works this way. At any moment during a valid shechita, if one were to stop halfway, the animal would be a treifa. Part of shechita is necessarily passing through a phase in which the animal has a mortal wound but is not yet fully shechted. The act of shechita itself creates a treifa as it proceeds and then completes the act. What is unacceptable is performing shechita on an animal that is already a neveilah before the act begins. Being in a state of treifa midway through the act is inherent to shechita and does not invalidate it. The problem arises only if the animal has already been halachically killed — made a neveilah — before the melika reaches the simanim.

### Brain Death & Neveila


This sugya is cited by some in the context of the halachic debate over brain death, and it is worth examining both what it potentially establishes and where the argument has limits.

The scenario described by Zeiri — the spinal cord at the nape of the neck is broken, the brain is functionally disconnected from the body — is structurally analogous to brain death in one important respect: the brain is no longer communicating with the rest of the body. The animal's trachea and esophagus may still be intact; its lungs may still be moving. Yet the Gemara, following Zeiri, treats this as constituting a neveilah — and Rava himself uses the word meisah. Those who invoke this source in the brain death discussion point to the fact that the Gemara appears to define the severing of brain-body communication as death, even prior to cessation of breathing or heartbeat.

However, there are significant difficulties with this argument. First, the case requires not only the severing of the spinal cord but also the tearing away of most of the surrounding flesh — rov basar. The question of why that additional element is required, and why severing the spinal cord alone is insufficient to constitute a neveilah, is not easily answered if the theory is that brain disconnection from the body defines death. Severing the spinal cord without rov basar does not produce neveilah status; why not?

Second, and perhaps more fundamentally, there is the question of whether neveilah is the same thing as meisah. The Gemara uses the two terms in proximity and, at points, seemingly interchangeably. But when the Rambam codifies Zeiri's ruling, his language is notably more cautious. He writes: harei zo ki-neveilah le-chol davar — it is like a neveilah for all purposes. He does not say it is a neveilah in the strict sense; he says it is ke-neveilah. And earlier he writes af al pi she-lo meisah in parallel contexts — even though it has not died — which suggests that for the Rambam, the category of neveilah and the factual state of meisah may not be fully coextensive. Something could be ke-neveilah for halachic purposes without being considered an animal that has factually died.

This distinction is at the heart of the brain death question. Is neveilah a halachic definition of death — meaning the law treats this as death, period? Or is it a category that tracks death but can apply even in cases where the law recognizes that factual death has not yet occurred, as the Rambam's hedged language implies? If the latter, the Gemara's use of meisah alongside neveilah may be a somewhat loose application of the term rather than a precise halachic equation. The question of whether this source can ground a definition of brain death as halachic death thus remains genuinely open — the source is relevant and the structural parallel is real, but the equation of neveilah with meisah cannot be assumed without further analysis.
