# Bava Metzia 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hekdesh Transfer Rules

### R. Yehoshua ben Levi

The daf opens in the midst of a discussion about the rules governing the addition of a *chomesh* (a one-fifth surcharge) when redeeming *hekdesh* and *ma'aser sheni*. The Gemara cites a *tanya k'vatei d'Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — a *braita* that corroborates the position of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — establishing the foundational principle that the obligation to add a *chomesh* applies only to a first-generation transfer out of sanctified property, but not to a second-generation transfer. That is, when one redeems the original *hekdesh*, a *chomesh* is added; but when one subsequently redeems the object or money that replaced it, no *chomesh* is required.

### Cow Exchange Rules

The *braita* states: *para zo tachat para shel hekdesh, tallis zo tachat tallis shel hekdesh, hekdesho paduy v'yad hekdesh al ha'elyona* — if one declares "this cow in exchange for this *hekdesh* cow," or "this *tallis* in exchange for this *hekdesh tallis*," the transfer is effective. Significantly, the transfer need not be conducted in money; objects may be exchanged directly for *hekdesh* objects. And *hekdesh* retains the upper hand: if the second object is worth less than the first, one remains obligated to pay *hekdesh* the difference. If the second object is worth more, *hekdesh* receives the full object.

This asymmetry reflects an important principle. As Tosafot notes, there is a famous ruling of Shmuel that even if one transferred *hekdesh* worth a thousand *dinar* onto an object worth a mere *prutah*, the transfer is technically valid *b'dieved* — because one can conceptualize the transfer as moving an abstract status of *kedushah* from one object to another, independent of monetary value. However, that is plainly not the default intent when one uses simple object-for-object language. When one says "this cow for this cow," the language implies an intent to exchange equal value. Accordingly, if the second cow is worth less, the balance is owed to *hekdesh*; if more, *hekdesh* receives the full benefit.

### Explicit Valuation

The *braita* continues with a second case: *para zo b'chamesh sela'im tachat para shel hekdesh* — "this cow, which I know is worth five *sela'im*, in exchange for this *hekdesh* cow." Here the speaker explicitly names the value of the second object. In this scenario, *hekdesho paduy* — the redemption is valid — but the rule of *yad hekdesh al ha'elyona* does not apply. The *braita* concludes by restating the core teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *al hekdesh rishon mosif chomesh, al hekdesh sheni ein mosif chomesh* — the *chomesh* is added when transferring out of the original *hekdesh*, but not when transferring out of the replacement.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot spells out precisely why explicit valuation changes the legal calculus. In the case of *tallis zo b'chamesh sela'im hekdesho paduy*, Tosafot writes: *hacha lo garsinan yad hekdesh al ha'elyona, d'keivan she'nitkavein l'challel hekdesh, afilu shaveh harbeh al davar muat, mechullal* — here we do not apply the rule that *hekdesh* holds the upper hand, because by explicitly stating a value, the speaker has expressed a clear intent to transfer the *kedushah* onto that specific monetary amount, even if the original *hekdesh* was worth considerably more. This parallels Shmuel's ruling, cited by Tosafot as *k'dikmar Shmuel lekaman*.

By contrast, in the first case — where no value is stated and one simply says "this for this" — *aval b'reisha shelo hizkir shum shaveh, lo haya b'daato liknot hekdesh*: the language implies an intent to exchange equivalent value, and therefore if the second object falls short, the shortfall remains owed to *hekdesh*. Tosafot adds that the same logic applies in the upward direction: *ve'im hotir yad hekdesh al ha'elyona, lekach lo eirev leshon sheneihem ke'echad* — in the first case, if the second object is worth more than the first, *hekdesh* receives the full object, whereas when a specific value has been named, the transfer is limited to precisely that stated amount. Thus, the formula *b'chamesh sela'im* fixes the exchange in both directions, at once releasing *hekdesh* from demanding more and releasing the redeemer from offering more.

### Temura Exception

A natural question arises: how can one transfer *hekdesh* by exchanging one object for another directly? Does not the Torah explicitly prohibit *temura* — as stated *ve'haya hu u'tmurato yiheyeh kodesh* — meaning that attempting to substitute one animal for a consecrated one causes *both* to become sanctified, without actually effecting the intended transfer?

Tosafot addresses this directly: *yesh lomar de'hacha be'mai askinan be'kodshei bedek ha'bayit* — the cases in our *braita* concern *kodshei bedek ha'bayit*, donations made to the Temple treasury, as distinct from *korbanot*. The prohibition of *temura* and its extraordinary consequence of dual sanctification apply specifically to the *kedushah* of an animal designated as a *korban* — an offering whose intrinsic sanctity is bound up with its purpose of being brought on the altar. Such an animal cannot simply be transferred onto another object, and even if it bears a *mum* and must be redeemed, the redemption must be onto money rather than another object. But *kodshei bedek ha'bayit* — property consecrated for the Temple's maintenance fund — is treated essentially as a matter of monetary value. Its *kedushah* is not a deep intrinsic sanctity tied to being offered, but rather a sanctity of ownership and value. Therefore, a direct object-for-object exchange is entirely valid for *kodshei bedek ha'bayit*, and the laws of *temura* simply do not apply.

## Ona'ah Thresholds

### One-Sixth Rule

The Gemara now transitions to the *mishnah* that opens the discussion of *ona'ah* (overcharging). The *mishnah* states: *ha'ona'ah arba'ah kessef* — the threshold for *ona'ah* is four *kessef* per *sela*, which, since a *sela* equals twenty-four *ma'ah*, works out to exactly one-sixth of the purchase price. This is the familiar rule of *shtus lameikach*. The *mishnah* then states *ve'ha'ta'anah shtei kessef* — that for the law of *modeh bemiktzat* to apply, the original claim must amount to at least *shtei ma'ah*.

### Modeh Bemiktzat

The law of *modeh bemiktzat* requires three elements: a claim of at least *shtei ma'ah*, an admission of at least a *prutah*, and a denial of at least a *prutah*. If one demands payment of *shtei ma'ah* from another, and the defendant admits to part while denying the rest, a *shevuah* is imposed — provided that both the admitted portion and the denied portion each reach the threshold of a *prutah*. The *mishnah* concludes: *ve'ha'hoda'ah shaveh prutah* — the admission must be worth a *prutah*. The *mishnah* then declares *chamesh perutot hen* — there are five areas of law in which the *prutah* serves as the minimum monetary threshold.

The Gemara asks why the *mishnah* needs to teach the *ona'ah* rule at all, since it was already established in an earlier *mishnah* in this same *perek*. The answer is that the *mishnah* needed the *ona'ah* rule as a hook to introduce the two-*ma'ah* threshold for *ta'anah*, which in turn was needed as a hook to introduce the concept of *prutah* in the admission, which finally led to the main purpose: the enumeration of the five *perutot*.

The Gemara similarly asks whether the *ta'anah* of *shtei ma'ah* and the *hoda'ah* of *prutah* needed to be stated here, since they already appear in tractate *Shevuot* in the context of *shevuot hadayanim*. The Gemara explains: *seifa itztricha lei* — each element in the chain was needed to arrive at the final teaching. The first rule brought in the second, the second introduced the *prutah*, and the *prutah* enabled the enumeration of the five categories.

### No Ona'ah Below Isar

The Gemara then raises a pointed question: *velisni nami ha'ona'ah perutah* — why does the *mishnah* not add a sixth category, namely that *ona'ah* also has a minimum threshold of a *prutah*? After all, if one overcharges by less than a *prutah*, that too would seem to qualify as *ona'ah* on a proportional basis.

Rav Kahana's answer is striking: *zot omeret ein ona'ah liprutos* — this omission teaches that there is in fact no *ona'ah* for trivially small amounts. *Ona'ah* requires not only a one-sixth overcharge proportionally, but also a minimum absolute amount. According to Rashi, that minimum is an *isar* — a denomination worth eight *perutot* — since the *isar* represents the lowest denomination still considered silver coinage, while anything below that has entered the realm of copper and is too trivial to sustain a claim of *ona'ah*.

The conceptual point is important. *Ona'ah* is not simply a matter of taking more than one is entitled to in a proportional sense; it involves a meaningful monetary wrong. A few extra pennies, even if they represent a large proportion of the item's value, do not constitute the kind of commercial harm the Torah was addressing. This is in contrast to *gezel*, where stealing even a *prutah* is a formal transgression — because in *gezel* one is taking an object that belongs to another, while in *ona'ah* the buyer did receive what they purchased, merely at a somewhat inflated price.

### Levi Disputes Kahana

Levi disagrees with Rav Kahana and maintains *yeish ona'ah liprutos* — that even an overcharge of a *prutah* constitutes *ona'ah* if it represents one-sixth of the item's value. Levi further corroborated this position in his own *baraita*, which offered an alternative version of the five *perutot* list. In Levi's list, *ona'ah* itself appears as one of the five: *ha'ona'ah prutah, v'hoda'ah prutah, v'kiddushi ishah prutah, v'gezel b'prutah*. However, Levi omits *metziah* (found objects) and replaces it with *ha'yeshivas hadayanim prutah* — the rule that a court will convene to adjudicate a claim worth a *prutah*. According to our *mishnah*, which does not include *ona'ah* among the five, Rav Kahana's position prevails: *ona'ah* does not apply below the *isar* threshold.

## Five Prutah Categories

### The Five Prutot

The *mishnah* enumerates five domains in which the *prutah* functions as the operative minimum unit of legal monetary recognition: admission in a *modeh bemiktzat* context (*hoda'ah shaveh prutah*); betrothal of a woman (*ha'ishah mitkadeshet bishvei prutah*, in accordance with Beit Hillel); deriving benefit from *hekdesh* (*ha'neheneh bishvei prutah min hahekdesh ma'al*, this constituting *me'ilah*); the obligation to announce a found object (*ha'motzei shaveh prutah chayav lehachriz*); and stealing from another with a false oath (*ha'gozel chaveiro shaveh prutah ve'nishba lo yolichenu acharav afilu leMadai* — one must pursue him to return it even to the farthest regions).

The case of *me'ilah* is worth pausing over. One might ask whether *me'ilah* is properly conceptualized as a form of theft from *hekdesh* — in which case the *prutah* threshold follows naturally as the minimum definition of money — or whether it is better understood as a category of deriving prohibited *hana'ah* from *hekdesh*, in which case the relevant standard might be a *kezayit* rather than a *prutah*. Here the *halacha* is clear that the threshold is a *prutah*, but a related debate does arise among the *acharonim* in analogous contexts — for instance, regarding things that are *assur behana'ah*, such as *basar bechalav* or *avodah zarah*, where there is a dispute whether the minimum for *malkut* is a *kezayit* or a *prutah*'s worth of benefit, depending on whether one frames *hana'ah* as a form of *achilah* or as a form of monetary gain.

### Rav Katina Exception

The Gemara explains why Levi listed *yeshivas hadayanim* — the adjudication threshold — rather than *metziah*, and it does so by revealing that this inclusion is not merely redundant with *gezel*. The *chidush* of *yeshivas hadayanim perutah* is specifically *laapokei mid'Rav Katina* — to exclude the position of Rav Katina. Rav Katina held *beit din nizkakin afilu lefachos mishaveh prutah* — that a court will sit and adjudicate even for a claim worth less than a *prutah*. Levi's enumeration of *yeshivas hadayanim* among the five *perutot* thus serves to establish the opposite: a *beit din* will hear cases starting from a *prutah*, but not for amounts below that threshold.

### Meila Below Prutah

A related question emerges from the *pasuk* in Vayikra 5:16: *ve'et asher chata min ha'kodesh yeshallem v'chamishito yosef alav*. The Gemara reads *ve'et* as a *ribbui* extending the repayment obligation even to benefit from *hekdesh* worth less than a *prutah* — *lerabos pachos mishaveh prutah lehishavon*. How does this square with the *mishnah*'s statement that *me'ilah* requires a *prutah*?

The resolution is that the *prutah* threshold in *me'ilah* applies to the obligation to bring a *korban me'ilah*, not to the obligation of repayment itself. As the Rambam states in *Hilchot Me'ilah* (chapter 7, *halacha* 8): one who derives benefit from *hekdesh* worth less than a *prutah*, whether *b'meizid* or *b'shogeg*, pays back the principal but is not liable for a *chomesh* or a *korban*. Thus, the return obligation extends even below a *prutah* when it comes to *hekdesh*, unlike the analogous case between two private parties — *lekodesh in, aval lehedyot lo* — where no legal obligation to return amounts below a *prutah* exists at all.

### Opening vs. Ending Case

This asymmetry between *hekdesh* and *hedyot* raises a further difficulty: if there is no legal obligation to return less than a *prutah* to a private individual, how can a *beit din* rule on such a claim? The Gemara resolves this with a refined formulation: *amar Rav Katina, huchzeku beit din leshaveh prutah, gomrin afilu pachos mishvei prutah* — a court will open a case only for a claim worth at least a *prutah*, but once the case has been opened on the basis of a *prutah*-valued claim, they will complete it even if the outstanding balance at the end of litigation turns out to be less than a *prutah*. The principle is: *techilas din be'inan prutah, gemar din lo be'inan prutah*. To begin litigation requires a *prutah*; to conclude it does not. This presumably reflects the principle that once a process of adjudication is underway, seeing it through to a conclusion — even for a small residual balance — is required to avoid a perversion of justice, even where no independent legal obligation to pay would have attached.

## Five Chomeshim

### Sacred Foods

The *mishnah* now enumerates *chamisha chumshin hen* — five categories in which one is obligated to add a *chomesh*, a one-fifth surcharge, upon repayment or redemption. The first category is *ha'ochel trumah* — one who consumes *terumah* without authorization, *b'shogeg*, and must repay the *kohen* the value plus a fifth. Included within this single category, because they share the same status, are *terumat ma'aser* (the *kohen*'s portion of the Levite's tithe), *terumat ma'aser shel demai*, *challah* (the dough-portion given to the *kohen*), and *bikurim* (first fruits brought to the Temple). All of these are treated as forms of *terumah*, all go to the *kohen*, and consuming any of them *b'shogeg* triggers the same obligation to repay plus a fifth.

### Neta Revai Rules

The second category is *ha'podeh neta revai u'ma'aser sheni shelo* — one who redeems his own *neta revai* (fourth-year fruit, which carries the same sanctity as *ma'aser sheni*) or his own *ma'aser sheni* adds a *chomesh*. The word *shelo* is critical: the *chomesh* applies when the *owner* redeems his own consecrated property. If someone else — a stranger — redeems another's *ma'aser sheni* or *neta revai*, no *chomesh* is added. This parallel structure — owner adds, stranger does not — will recur in the next category.

### Meila & Oath

The fifth category in the *mishnah* is *ha'gozel chaveiro shaveh prutah ve'nishba lo* — one who steals from another and swears falsely about the theft. When the thief eventually confesses and makes restitution, he must return the principal, add a *chomesh*, and bring a *korban asham*. This combines the earlier theme of the five *perutot* (stealing a *prutah* plus a false oath) with the present discussion of *chomesh*.

### Hekdesh Redemption

The fourth category is *ha'podeh hekdesho mosif chomesh* — one who redeems his own *hekdesh* adds a *chomesh*. As with *ma'aser sheni*, this privilege and obligation belongs to the owner: when a stranger redeems *hekdesh*, purchasing it from the Temple treasury, no *chomesh* is added. Only the original owner who redeems his own consecrated property is required to pay the additional fifth. The third category, which completes the list, is *ha'neheneh bishvei prutah min hahekdesh* — deriving benefit worth a *prutah* from *hekdesh*, which constitutes *me'ilah* and obligates payment of the principal plus a *chomesh*; here the minimum threshold of a *prutah* for this obligation cross-references the discussion in the previous *mishnah*.

## Terumas Ma'aser Demai

### R. Elazar Question

The Gemara reports that Rava conveyed the following difficulty in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *terumat ma'aser shel demai* — why should consuming *terumat ma'aser* that derives from *demai* produce an obligation to add a *chomesh*? *Ve'chi asu chachamim chizuk l'divreihem k'shel Torah?* — did the Rabbis really strengthen their own enactments to the level of Torah law in this regard? *Demai* is produce purchased from an *am ha'aretz* whose tithing practices are uncertain; the obligation to tithe it and to separate *terumat ma'aser* from it is rabbinic. Would the Rabbis truly go so far as to impose the *chomesh* — a penalty associated specifically with the violation of *terumah* of Torah status — upon one who mistakenly eats *terumat ma'aser* of *demai*?

Tosafot sharpens the question further. While it is common for rabbinic enactments to be treated with great stringency, and sometimes even stricter than Torah law, that is typically when there is a logical reason to build additional safeguards — *heicha d'shayach l'miknos*. But here, the entire concept of adding a *chomesh* is itself a *kenas* (penalty) that the Torah imposes; it is not an intrinsic property of the prohibited act. Why would the Rabbis extend this penalty to a case that is rabbinic in origin? Since the *chomesh* is itself framed as an extrinsic fine, not an organic consequence of the violation, it is difficult to understand why the Rabbis would replicate it in a domain where the underlying prohibition is only of rabbinic force.

### R. Meir Stringency

Rav Nahman, citing Shmuel, answers: *ha mani, Rabbi Meir hi* — this ruling in the *mishnah* follows the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who holds that *asu chachamim chizuk l'divreihem k'shel Torah* — the Rabbis do indeed treat their enactments with the full weight of Torah law, without requiring a case-by-case logical justification for each stringency.

### Get Precedent

The *braita* adduced in support of this characterization concerns the laws of *gittin*. If an agent bringing a *get* from abroad delivers it to the woman without reciting the declaration *bifnai nichtav u'vifnai nechtam* — "it was written and signed in my presence" — Rabbi Meir rules *yotzi ve'ha'valad mamzer* (she must leave the second husband and the children are *mamzerim*), even though the only failure was a rabbinic procedural requirement. The *chachamim* disagree and rule *ein ha'valad mamzer*, although Tosafot notes that the *chachamim*'s formulation — which does not say *lo yotzi* but only *ein ha'valad mamzer* — implies that even they may agree the marriage requires dissolution; they merely decline to declare the children *mamzerim*.

To remedy the defect, the *braita* prescribes that the *shaliach* should *yitlenu himena v'yachzor v'yitnenu lah bifnai shenayim* — take the *get* back from the woman and re-deliver it with the proper declaration in front of two witnesses.

The Gemara identifies this as an expression of *Rabbi Meir l'ta'amei* — his consistent approach across legal domains. As Rav Hamnuna reported in the name of Ulla: *kol ha'meshaneh mi'matbe'a she'tav'u chachamim b'gittin yotzi ve'ha'valad mamzer* — any deviation from the precise formula the Sages established for *gittin* renders the *get* invalid and the children *mamzerim*, according to Rabbi Meir. The underlying principle is that once the Rabbis have established a formal requirement, Rabbi Meir treats any deviation as a complete failure — not as a minor procedural irregularity — regardless of whether the consequence (such as *mamzerut*) seems proportionate to the offense.

Tosafot notes that this extreme position in the *get* case — *Rabbi Meir* declaring children *mamzerim* over a purely procedural rabbinic failure — is the template for his approach here. Even where there is *af al pi she'ein sevara* — no compelling substantive logic — linking the stringency to the concern being addressed, Rabbi Meir imposes the full Torah consequence because, in his view, once a rabbinic enactment is in place, it is treated as if it were Torah law in every respect.

### Rav Sheshet Query

Rav Sheshet now challenges the attribution of the *mishnah*'s stringency to Rabbi Meir. The challenge comes from a *braita* dealing with *ma'aser sheni shel demai*: *mechalelo kesef al kesef, nechoshet al nechoshet, kesef al nechoshet, v'nechoshet al peirot, v'yachzor v'yifdeh et ha'peirot* — one may transfer the sanctity of *ma'aser sheni* of *demai* from silver coins to silver, copper to copper, silver to copper, copper to fruit, and then the fruit may be redeemed back onto coins. This last step — transferring from fruit back to coins — is what Rabbi Meir permits according to the *braita*. The *chachamim*, by contrast, rule *ya'alu peirot v'yei'achlu b'Yerushalayim* — once the sanctity is on fruit, the fruit must be brought to Jerusalem and eaten there; they may not be re-redeemed onto money.

The apparent implication is that Rabbi Meir is actually more *lenient* regarding *ma'aser sheni shel demai* than the *chachamim*. How, then, can the Gemara attribute the *mishnah*'s stringency — adding a *chomesh* to *terumat ma'aser shel demai* — to Rabbi Meir on the grounds that he always treats rabbinic enactments with maximal stringency?

### Mixed Coin Transfer

Before resolving this tension, the Gemara establishes that Rabbi Meir's leniency in the *braita* is not simply a reflection of his general approach to monetary transfers, but is specifically calibrated to the rabbinic status of *demai*. This is demonstrated by contrasting the *demai* rules with the rules for *ma'aser sheni vadai* — ordinary, certain *ma'aser sheni*.

The Gemara cites a *mishnah* dealing with a case where a *sela* of *ma'aser sheni* became mixed with a *sela* of ordinary *chullin* money. To resolve the situation without spending both coins as *ma'aser sheni*, one brings copper coins (*maot*) and declares: wherever the coin of *ma'aser sheni* is, its sanctity is hereby transferred to these copper coins. Then, from the two original silver coins — now both free of *ma'aser sheni* sanctity — one selects the nicer one and transfers the sanctity of the copper coins back onto it. This roundabout procedure is necessary because one cannot redeem silver *ma'aser sheni* directly onto silver of equal denomination: *mipnei she'amru, mechalelin oto kesef al nechoshet midochek* — the Sages permitted going from silver to copper only in a pressing situation (*midochek*), and even then only provisionally, with the expectation that *v'lo she'itkayem ken, ela she'yachzor v'yechalel al kesef* — it should not remain in copper form but should be redeemed back onto silver.

The principle established is: *midochek in, d'lo midochek klal* — redeeming from silver to copper is permitted only under pressing circumstances, and never as a standard practice. This stands in sharp contrast to the *demai* rules in the earlier *braita*, where Rabbi Meir freely permits transfers between silver and copper and from copper to fruit and back — without any requirement of *dochek*. The freer rules for *demai* are thus clearly a function of its rabbinic status, not a reflection of Rabbi Meir's general approach to the *pidyon* process.

### R. Meir Strict/Lenient

This now sharpens Rav Sheshet's challenge. If Rabbi Meir is demonstrably *more lenient* than the *chachamim* when it comes to the *pidyon* of *ma'aser sheni shel demai*, how can one attribute to him a *stringency* regarding *terumat ma'aser shel demai*?

### Eating vs. Pidyon

Rav Yosef resolves the apparent contradiction: *af al pi she'meikel Rabbi Meir b'pidyono, machmir hu b'achilato* — even though Rabbi Meir is lenient regarding the procedural rules of *redeeming* demai *ma'aser sheni*, he is strict when it comes to the act of *eating* improperly. When a non-*kohen* mistakenly eats *terumat ma'aser shel demai*, he has committed a transgression — a violation of the sanctity of *terumah*, even if it is only rabbinic in force. In Rabbi Meir's framework, this transgression is treated with the full severity of its Torah analogue, including the obligation to add a *chomesh*. But when the question is merely one of how to *perform the pidyon* — the technical procedure for redeeming *ma'aser sheni shel demai* — no transgression is involved, and there Rabbi Meir is content to be lenient.

This distinction is further supported by a *braita*: *lo hitiru limkor demai ela l'siton bilvad* — they permitted selling *demai* without first separating *terumos u'ma'asros* only to a wholesaler (*siton*), since everyone understands that a wholesaler is simply reselling without having personally supervised the tithing. A homeowner (*ba'al habayit*), however, must separate *terumos u'ma'asros* before reselling — *bein kach u'vein kach tzarich l'aser, divrei Rabbi Meir*. The *chachamim* are more lenient and permit even a homeowner to resell or give as a gift without separating, when it is clear from the context that no personal supervision of tithing was involved.

The relevance is this: Rabbi Meir's stringency in the *baraita* is directed at preventing people from *eating* improperly — the concern is that buyers will assume that the homeowner, being a *chaver*, has already tithed, and will therefore consume produce that has not properly had its *terumos u'ma'asros* taken. So when the concern is *eating a violation*, Rabbi Meir is strict. When the question is *how to perform the pidyon* of *demai*, Rabbi Meir is lenient.

Thus the *mishnah*'s ruling — that one who eats *terumat ma'aser shel demai* must add a *chomesh* — is entirely consistent with Rabbi Meir's overall approach: strict with respect to violations of eating sanctified produce, even when those violations are only rabbinic in character, but lenient with respect to the procedural mechanics of *pidyon* when no transgression is at stake.
