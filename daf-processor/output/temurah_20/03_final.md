# Temurah 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Female Asham

### Opening & Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְאָשָׁם, תִּרְעֶה עַד שֶׁתִּסְתָּאֵב, וְתִימָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא בְּדָמֶיהָ אָשָׁם. וְאִם קָרַב אֲשָׁמוֹ, יִפְּלוּ דָּמָיו לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who designates a female** animal **for a guilt offering,** which may be brought only from males, **it** is left to **graze until it becomes blemished and** then **it is sold, and he brings a guilt offering with** the **money** received for **its** sale. **And if** in the interim, he designated a male animal and **his guilt offering was** already **sacrificed,** so that a guilt offering is no longer needed, the **money** received for the sale of the blemished female **is allocated for** communal **gift offerings.**

Daf 20 opens in the middle of the *mishnah* on 19b, which deals with *temurah* and *vladot* — the offspring — of various *korbanot*. The preceding discussion had already covered *zevach todah*, *shelamim*, and *olah*. By *shelamim*, the offspring follows the same *halacha* as the parent animal. By *todah*, things are slightly different: the *lechem* is not brought for the offspring. And by *olah*, the interesting complication is that an *olah* itself cannot directly produce a *vlad* in the ordinary sense, so the discussion there concerned the *vlad* of a *temurah* of an *olah* — whether a male offspring in that position could itself be brought, or whether it follows the original animal's status.

The question turned on whether the animal that originated the chain could itself be brought. If one was *makdish* a female *olah*, the calculus shifts, since the original could not be brought at all. With that background established, the *mishnah* now turns to the *asham* — specifically, a female *asham*.

### Female Asham Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: תִּימָּכֵר שֶׁלֹּא בְּמוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** Since a female is unfit to be sacrificed as a guilt offering, its halakhic status is like that of a blemished animal in the sense that it does not become inherently sacred; rather, its value alone becomes sacred. Therefore, **it may be sold without a blemish,** and a guilt offering is purchased with the money received for its sale.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּלְמָה לִי תִּסְתָּאֵב? תִּימָּכֵר! כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חַזְיָא לְמִילְּתָא — הַיְינוּ מוּמָא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need to wait until the female animal that was designated as a guilt offering **becomes blemished** before it can be sold? **Let it be sold** immediately even without a blemish: **Since it is** a female and therefore **unfit for the matter** for which it was designated, **this is** the same thing as **a blemish.**

The question posed is: what does one do with a female animal designated as an *asham*? An *asham* must be male. This animal is not one of the five *chataot* that die when they cannot be brought; it simply cannot be brought because it is the wrong gender. The *halacha* is that one waits until it acquires a *mum* — a blemish — and only then redeems it, transferring the funds toward a proper *asham*.

The *gemara* presses: why wait for a *mum* at all? The animal cannot be used — *kivan d'lo chazi l'miti haynu mum*, the very fact that it cannot be brought should itself be equivalent to a *mum*, and it should be redeemable immediately. One might add that the question is even sharper here than in a case like a *treifah* that became unfit after sanctification. Here, the animal was never fit from the moment of consecration — it could never have received *kedushat haguf* in the first place.

### Transform Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: הַיְינוּ טַעַם דְּאָמְרִינַן, מִיגּוֹ דְּנָחֲתָא לַהּ קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים — נָחֲתָא נָמֵי קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: This is the reason** that the female animal may not be sold before it becomes blemished, **for we say** that **as sanctity** that inheres in its **value has descended on it,** therefore **inherent sanctity has descended on it as well.** Although it may not be sacrificed in any event, its inherent sanctity still mandates that it may not be sold until it becomes blemished.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, הִקְדִּישׁ זָכָר לְדָמָיו — קָדוֹשׁ קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: That is to say** that even if one **consecrated a male** animal with the intention of selling it and bringing a burnt offering or guilt offering with **the money** received for **its** sale, as it becomes sanctified with sanctity that inheres in its value, it also becomes **sanctified** with **inherent sanctity.** And as the animal is fit to be brought as a burnt offering or as a guilt offering, it is sacrificed.

Rav Yehuda amar Rav provides the answer: *migu d'nachit lei kedushat damim nachit nami kedushat haguf* — since it descended into *kedushat damim*, it also descended into *kedushat haguf*. The *gemara* takes for granted that because this female animal cannot be brought, its basic *kedusha* is *kedushat damim* — the sanctity of its monetary value — rather than *kedushat haguf*, the sanctity of the body itself that would allow it to be offered on the *mizbe'ach*. *Kedushat damim* means one uses the proceeds; the animal itself is not a *korban*.

If that is so, why does one need a *mum* to redeem it? The answer is that although it starts as *kedushat damim*, it transforms and rises to *kedushat haguf* — and since it has *kedushat haguf*, it requires a *mum* for redemption. One senses a certain circularity in this reasoning: the whole question was why it should need a *mum* if it can never be brought, and the answer essentially says that because it is fit enough, it gets upgraded — but the explanation of why the upgrade occurs in this case is not fully elaborated.

Rashi frames this carefully: the upgrade to *kedushat haguf* is only *l'milsa d'ba'ei mum* — to the degree that it cannot be redeemed without a *mum* — not to the degree that it could actually be offered. So the *kedushat haguf* here is a partial one. The implicit logic seems to be that while a *mum* renders an animal automatically redeemable by Torah law, preventing it from ever reaching *kedushat haguf*, wrong gender is not treated with the same severity. The animal is otherwise fit; it lacks only the required gender. Therefore, although it enters with *kedushat damim*, because it is close enough — being an unblemished animal of the right species — it transforms into *kedushat haguf*. The question of why wrong gender is treated differently from *mum* is, as the lecturer acknowledges, not fully resolved by the *gemara*; it is, to some degree, simply stated as the operative rule.

### Rava's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הִקְדִּישׁ זָכָר לְדָמָיו, רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: קָדוֹשׁ קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף, רָבָא אָמַר: אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף. וַהֲדַר בֵּיהּ רָבָא לִדְרַב כָּהֲנָא, מִדְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** that this issue is subject to a dispute of *amora'im*: If one **consecrated a male** animal with the intention of selling it and bringing a burnt offering or guilt offering with **the money** received for **its** sale, **Rav Kahana says** that it is **sanctified** with **inherent sanctity,** whereas **Rava says** that it **is not sanctified** with **inherent sanctity. But Rava** later **retracted** his statement and agreed with the opinion **of Rav Kahana, due to** the aforementioned statement of **Rav Yehuda** citing that which **Rav said,** that as the animal becomes sanctified with sanctity that inheres in its value, inherent sanctity also takes effect.

From this principle, Rava draws a further inference: *hamakdish zachar lidamav kadosh kedushat haguf* — if one consecrates a male animal for its *damim*, intending to use the proceeds toward an *asham* or *olah* rather than bringing the animal itself, the animal automatically acquires *kedushat haguf*. This statement has appeared before in the tractate. The logic seems even stronger here than in the female *asham* case: if you are consecrating a male animal specifically so that its proceeds fund this type of *korban*, and the animal itself is perfectly suited to serve as that *korban*, why would we honor the artificial distinction and not treat the animal as already having *kedushat haguf*?

Yet one might question whether this inference flows naturally from the female *asham* case. There, the *kedushat haguf* arose despite the animal never being fit for the designated use. Here, the animal is entirely fit — the person simply tried to give it the lesser consecration of *kedushat damim*. The *gemara* acknowledges this is something of a logical leap: in the previous case, *kedushat haguf* arose even where the upgrade made the animal no more eligible to be brought; here the upgrade is more intuitive since the animal actually can be brought.

### Rav Kahana vs. Rava

The *gemara* then reveals that this *hamakdish zachar lidamav* principle was actually the subject of a debate. Rav Kahana held: *kadosh kedushat haguf* — it does receive *kedushat haguf*. Rava originally held: *eino kadosh kedushat haguf* — it does not automatically receive *kedushat haguf*. Ultimately, *v'hadar bei Rava legabei d'Rav Kahana mi'd'Rav Yehuda amar Rav* — Rava retracted his position in favor of Rav Kahana's, based on the very teaching of Rav Yehuda amar Rav about the female *asham* and the transformation from *kedushat damim* to *kedushat haguf*.

The underlying difficulty remains: once you grant the upgrade in the female *asham* case, the upgrade in the *hamakdish zachar lidamav* case follows even more naturally. But neither the female *asham* case nor the male *damim* case fully explains, from first principles, *why* a *kedushat damim* that was placed on a fit animal automatically upgrades. The *gemara* simply establishes that it does.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: תִּימָּכֵר שֶׁלֹּא בְּמוּם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִיגּוֹ דְּנָחֲתָא לֵיהּ קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים, תֵּיחוֹת לֵיהּ נָמֵי קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon** disagrees with the opinion of the Rabbis and **says** that a female animal that was designated as a guilt offering **may be sold without a blemish,** as it is unfit to be sacrificed as the offering for which it was designated, and this itself is considered a blemish. **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin said to Rabbi Yoḥanan:** Why doesn't Rabbi Shimon say that **as sanctity** that inheres in its **value has descended on it, inherent sanctity should descend on it as well,** and therefore it may not be sold until it becomes blemished?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: כֹּל מִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ — לָא נָחֲתָא לֵיהּ קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף, דְּתַנְיָא: אָשָׁם בֶּן שָׁנָה וֶהֱבִיאוֹ בֶּן שְׁתַּיִם, בֶּן שְׁתַּיִם וֶהֱבִיאוֹ בֶּן שָׁנָה — כְּשֵׁירָה, וְלֹא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin: **Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** with regard to **anything that is not fit itself** to be sacrificed upon the altar, that **inherent sanctity does not descend upon it. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a guilt offering** that should be sacrificed when it is **in its** first **year,** such as a guilt offering of a nazirite or of a leper, **but** the owner **brought it** when it was **in its second** year, or a guilt offering that should be sacrificed when it is **in its second** year, such as a guilt offering for robbery, for misuse of consecrated property, or for a designated maidservant, **and** the owner **brought it** when it was **in its** first **year,** the offering **is fit, but it does not satisfy the obligation of the owner** to bring a guilt offering, and he must bring another one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל עַצְמָן אֵינָן קְדוֹשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says** that the offering is disqualified, as these offerings **themselves are not consecrated,** due to the fact that the proper time of their offering has either not yet arrived or has already passed. This indicates that Rabbi Shimon maintains that in such a case there is no inherent sanctity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי מְחוּסַּר זְמַן, דְּלָא חֲזֵי, וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּקָדוֹשׁ, שָׁאנֵי מְחוּסַּר זְמַן, דַּחֲזֵי לִמְחַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** consider the case of one who consecrates an animal **whose time has not yet** arrived, e.g., an animal that is less than eight days old, **which is unfit** to be sacrificed, **and** yet **Rabbi Shimon said that it is sanctified** with regard to the prohibition against the slaughter of sacrificial animals outside the Temple courtyard. This proves that according to Rabbi Shimon, even an offering that is not fit to be sacrificed has inherent sanctity. The Gemara explains that an animal **whose time has not yet** arrived **is different, as it is fit** to be offered **tomorrow,** i.e., automatically at a later stage, and is therefore considered to be fit for an offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אָשָׁם בֶּן שְׁתַּיִם וֶהֱבִיאוֹ בֶּן שָׁנָה, הָא חֲזֵי לְשָׁנָה, אֶלָּא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּמְחוּסַּר זְמַן, דְּיָלֵיף לֵיהּ מִבְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so,** that any animal which will be fit to be sacrificed when its time arrives is sanctified with inherent sanctity, the same should also apply to **a guilt offering** that should be offered when it is **in its second** year **but** the owner **brought it** to be sacrificed when it was **in its** first **year,** as it will be **fit** for sacrifice **in** another **year.** Why, then, doesn't it have inherent sanctity according to Rabbi Shimon? **Rather, this is the reason of Rabbi Shimon in** the case of an animal **whose time has not yet** arrived, **as he derives it from** the *halakha* of **a firstborn.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מְחוּסַּר זְמַן נִכְנָס לַדִּיר לְהִתְעַשֵּׂר, וַהֲרֵי הוּא כִּבְכוֹר, מָה בְּכוֹר קָדוֹשׁ לִפְנֵי זְמַנּוֹ וְקָרֵב לְאַחַר זְמַנּוֹ, אַף מְחוּסַּר זְמַן קָדוֹשׁ לִפְנֵי זְמַנּוֹ וְקָרֵב לְאַחַר זְמַנּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon:** An animal **whose time has not yet** arrived **enters the pen to be tithed** together with the other animals. **And it is** considered in this regard to be **like a firstborn: Just as a firstborn is sanctified before the time** when **it** is fit to be sacrificed has arrived, i.e., immediately after birth, **and it is sacrificed after its time,** following the eighth day, **so too,** an animal **whose time has not yet** arrived **is sanctified before its time** has arrived **and is sacrificed after its time.**

The *gemara* now challenges Rabbi Shimon's position. If his principle is that anything unfit for its designated purpose never receives *kedushat haguf*, how does he handle the case of *mechusar zman* — an animal less than eight days old, which cannot yet be offered? In another context, Rabbi Shimon himself holds that if one consecrated such an animal and then slaughtered it outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, one is liable for *shechutei chutz* — a liability that only applies to animals with *kedushat korban*. This implies that even a *mechusar zman* has *kedushat haguf* according to Rabbi Shimon — so why is it any different from a wrong-gender animal?

The *gemara* answers: *shani mechusar zman dechazi lemachar* — a *mechusar zman* is different because it will naturally become fit in a matter of days. It is not truly outside the category; it will come into the category on its own.

The *gemara* immediately presses: if so, then an *asham ben shtayim* that was supposed to be *ben shana* will also naturally become fit in the future — why should Rabbi Shimon say it lacks *kedushat haguf*? The *gemara* responds: the logic of *mechusar zman* is not a general principle about future fitness; it derives from a specific *pasuk* learned from *bechor*. As the *braita* states, Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda said in the name of Rabbi Shimon: a *mechusar zman* can be included in one's flock when counting *maaser behemah* — just as a *bechor* is sanctified from birth but can only be brought after eight days, so too any animal can be consecrated before it is eight days old. This is a special scriptural exception for *mechusar zman*, not a general principle. Absent that exception, if it cannot presently be brought, it would only receive *kedushat damim*.

The *gemara* emphasizes a crucial point in its framing of Rabbi Shimon's position: nowhere does it treat wrong gender as a *mum*. The framework throughout is that if the thing cannot be brought, it is *like* consecrating an inanimate object — it receives only *kedushat damim*, not *kedushat haguf*. The *chachamim*, by contrast, hold that it always receives *kedushat haguf*. The most satisfying way to understand the *chachamim*'s position — though the *gemara* does not spell this out — is probably that since the animal is otherwise unblemished and could be used for some type of *korban*, it remains sufficiently within the world of *korbanot* to receive *kedushat haguf*. A car, or a *baal mum*, or perhaps a *treifah*, is categorically outside. A female sheep, or a two-year-old when a one-year-old is needed, is not categorically outside — it simply does not fit this particular slot, and that, according to the *chachamim*, is enough for the upgrade.

## Gender & Category

### Challenge to Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְקֵבָה לְעוֹלָתוֹ וּלְפִסְחוֹ וְלַאֲשָׁמוֹ — עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss this dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis: **The Sages taught** that **one who consecrates a female** animal **for his burnt offering** **or for his Paschal offering or for his guilt offering,** although these offerings are brought only from male animals, it is still consecrated with inherent sanctity. Therefore, if one exchanges for it a non-sacred animal, he **renders** that animal **a substitute,** which is consecrated with the same sanctity as the original animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָתוֹ — עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה, לְפִסְחוֹ וְלַאֲשָׁמוֹ — אֵין עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon** disagrees with the Sages and **says:** The *halakha* is not the same in all these cases. Granted, if he consecrates a female animal **for his burnt offering, it renders** the non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it **a substitute,** as there is burnt offering status for female birds. For this reason it is consecrated with inherent sanctity and can be sold only after it has become blemished. But if he consecrates a female animal **for his Paschal offering or for his guilt offering** it is not consecrated with inherent sanctity, and it may be sold even without a blemish. Therefore, **it does not render** the non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it **a substitute.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֵין לְךָ דָּבָר עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה, אֶלָּא הָרוֹעֶה לְהִסְתָּאֵב.
>
> **Translation:** This ruling is based upon the principle that **no** animal **renders** the non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it **a substitute except** for an animal that has inherent sanctity, which means that even if it may not be sacrificed, it must still be left to **graze** in order **to become blemished,** after which it is sold, and the proceeds from the sale are used to purchase a fit offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי: אֵין אֲנִי רוֹאֶה דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּפֶּסַח, הוֹאִיל וּמוֹתַר הַפֶּסַח קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says,** with regard to this dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the first *tanna*: **I do not agree with the statement of Rabbi Shimon with** regard to the **Paschal offering,** that a non-sacred animal exchanged for a female which was designated for a Paschal offering does not become consecrated as a substitute. I disagree, **since a leftover Paschal** offering **is sacrificed** after Passover as **a peace offering,** and a peace offering is brought even from female animals. Therefore, a female animal that was designated for a Paschal offering should be consecrated with inherent sanctity, as there is peace offering status for female animals. It should not be sold unless it has a blemish, and it should render a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it a substitute.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא: אֵין אֲנִי רוֹאֶה דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּאָשָׁם, הוֹאִיל וּמוֹתַר אָשָׁם קָרֵב עוֹלָה! רַבִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: מוֹתָרוֹת לְנִדְבַת צִיבּוּר אָזְלִי, וְאֵין תְּמוּרָה בְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **And let him** also **say: I do not agree with the statement of Rabbi Shimon** with regard **to a guilt offering,** for the same reason that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi disagrees with regard to the Paschal offering, **since a leftover guilt offering is sacrificed as a burnt offering,** and a bird burnt offering is brought even from female animals. Therefore, a female animal designated as a guilt offering should be consecrated with inherent sanctity, and it should render a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it a substitute. The Gemara explains: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis, who say: Leftover** guilt offerings **are used for communal gift offerings,** i.e., they are sold and the money is used to purchase communal gift offerings, **and a substitute cannot** be designated **for a communal** offering.

The *gemara* now challenges Rabbi Shimon's position. If his principle is that anything unfit for its designated purpose never receives *kedushat haguf*, how does he handle the case of *mechusar zman* — an animal less than eight days old, which cannot yet be offered? In another context, Rabbi Shimon himself holds that if one consecrated such an animal and then slaughtered it outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, one is liable for *shechutei chutz* — a liability that only applies to animals with *kedushat korban*. This implies that even a *mechusar zman* has *kedushat haguf* according to Rabbi Shimon — so why is it any different from a wrong-gender animal?

The *gemara* answers: *shani mechusar zman dechazi lemachar* — a *mechusar zman* is different because it will naturally become fit in a matter of days. It is not truly outside the category; it will come into the category on its own.

The *gemara* immediately presses: if so, then an *asham ben shtayim* that was supposed to be *ben shana* will also naturally become fit in the future — why should Rabbi Shimon say it lacks *kedushat haguf*? The *gemara* responds: the logic of *mechusar zman* is not a general principle about future fitness; it derives from a specific *pasuk* learned from *bechor*. As the *braita* states, Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda said in the name of Rabbi Shimon: a *mechusar zman* can be included in one's flock when counting *maaser behemah* — just as a *bechor* is sanctified from birth but can only be brought after eight days, so too any animal can be consecrated before it is eight days old. This is a special scriptural exception for *mechusar zman*, not a general principle. Absent that exception, if it cannot presently be brought, it would only receive *kedushat damim*.

The *gemara* emphasizes a crucial point in its framing of Rabbi Shimon's position: nowhere does it treat wrong gender as a *mum*. The framework throughout is that if the thing cannot be brought, it is *like* consecrating an inanimate object — it receives only *kedushat damim*, not *kedushat haguf*. The *chachamim*, by contrast, hold that it always receives *kedushat haguf*. The most satisfying way to understand the *chachamim*'s position — though the *gemara* does not spell this out — is probably that since the animal is otherwise unblemished and could be used for some type of *korban*, it remains sufficiently within the world of *korbanot* to receive *kedushat haguf*. A car, or a *baal mum*, or perhaps a *treifah*, is categorically outside. A female sheep, or a two-year-old when a one-year-old is needed, is not categorically outside — it simply does not fit this particular slot, and that, according to the *chachamim*, is enough for the upgrade.

### Pesach Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן גַּבֵּי מַפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְעוֹלָתוֹ, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה, דְּהָאִיכָּא שֵׁם עוֹלֶה עָלֶיהָ גַּבֵּי עוֹלַת הָעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes the opinion of Rabbi Shimon in the *baraita*: It might **enter your mind** to say that **this is the reason for** the ruling of **Rabbi Shimon with regard to** one who **designates a female** animal **for his burnt offering:** One **renders** a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it **a substitute due to that** reason, **that it has burnt offering status** because of the case **of a bird burnt offering,** which may be brought as a female.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁהִפְרִישׁ פָּרָה לְפָרוֹ — תִּיקְדּוֹשׁ, דְּהָאִיכָּא פָּרַת חַטָּאת! קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת הוּא, וְקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת לָא עָבְדִי תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** But **if that is so,** in the case **of a High Priest who designated a female cow** instead **of his** male **bull** that he is obligated to bring as a sin offering on Yom Kippur, **it should be sanctified** with regard to rendering a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it a substitute. Here too, the status of a sin offering **is** upon it, as **the** red **heifer of purification** is similar to a sin offering and is female. The Gemara answers: The red heifer is not consecrated with inherent sanctity, as it is not sacrificed upon the altar; rather, **it is consecrated** for **Temple maintenance, and** items **consecrated** for **Temple maintenance do not render** a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for them **a substitute.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָחִיד שֶׁהִפְרִישׁ שָׂעִיר לִשְׂעִירָתוֹ — תִּיקְדּוֹשׁ, דְּהָאִיכָּא שְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא! אִי נָמֵי, נָשִׂיא שֶׁהִפְרִישׁ שְׂעִירָה לִשְׂעִירוֹ — תִּיקְדּוֹשׁ, דְּהָא יָחִיד מַפְרִישׁ שְׂעִירָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: According to Rabbi Shimon, if **an individual, who** is obligated to bring a female animal as a sin offering, **designated** a male **goat** instead **of his female goat, it should be sanctified** with inherent sanctity with regard to rendering a substitute, **as it has** sin offering status, due to the case of the male **goat** brought as a sin offering by **the king. Alternatively,** if **a king, who** is obligated to bring a male animal as a sin offering, **designated a female goat** instead of **his** male **goat, it should be sanctified** with inherent sanctity with regard to rendering a substitute, since here too it has sin offering status, **as an individual designates a female goat** as a sin offering. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon should have stated that these animals render non-sacred animals exchanged for them consecrated as substitutes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי תְּרֵי גּוּפֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Neither a female goat brought as a sin offering by a king nor a male goat brought as a sin offering by an individual are considered to have sin offering status. The reason is that **these,** the king and an individual, **are two** distinct **bodies,** and the status of an offering can be conferred only when such an offering is brought by people of the same status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָטְאוּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִתְמַנּוּ, כִּי מַפְרֵישׁ שָׂעִיר לִשְׂעִירָתוֹ — תִּיקְדּוֹשׁ, דְּהָא אִילּוּ חָטָא הַשְׁתָּא — בַּר אֵיתוֹיֵי שָׂעִיר הוּא! הָא לָא חָטָא — לָא אִיחַיַּיב בְּשָׂעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: If so, then in a case where an individual **sinned** and is obligated to bring a female goat as a sin offering **prior to being appointed** as king, and he did not designate an animal as his offering before his appointment, **if he designates** a male **goat** instead **of** his **female goat** following his appointment, **it should be sanctified** with inherent sanctity with regard to substitution. In this situation, it should have sin offering status, **as, if he sinned now, he is obligated to bring** a male **goat.** The Gemara explains: This is not correct, **as** he **did not sin** when he was a king, and therefore **was not** actually **obligated to** bring a male **goat,** but a female goat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הָכָא נָמֵי, הָא לָאו עוֹלַת הָעוֹף קָמַיְיתֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that the individual's appointment as king does not give sin offering status to the male goat designated as a sin offering for a sin committed prior to his appointment, **here too,** one should say that a female animal designated for a burnt offering does not have burnt offering status, as the person is not a poor leper, and therefore he **does not bring a bird burnt offering.** Why, then, does Rabbi Shimon maintain that such an animal renders a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it a substitute?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, דִּתְנַן: ״הֲרֵי עָלַי עוֹלָה״ — יָבִיא כֶּבֶשׂ. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: אוֹ תּוֹר אוֹ בְּנֵי יוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The *baraita* is not referring to an obligatory burnt offering but rather to a voluntary burnt offering, and **Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya,** who says that anyone, even a wealthy man, can bring a bird if he vows to bring a burnt offering. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Menaḥot* 107a) that one who says: **It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a burnt offering,** without specifying which animal, **brings a lamb,** which is the smallest animal that a wealthy man can bring as a burnt offering. **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says:** He may even bring **a dove or pigeons** as a bird burnt offering.

### Testing Limits

The *gemara* continues to probe. If one takes Rabbi Shimon's logic to its endpoint — that any case where the alternate gender might be personally relevant gives the animal *kedushat haguf* — should not a male animal consecrated for a female *chatat* also be granted *kedushat haguf*, since a *nasi* who sinned brings a male goat (*se'ir*), and female individuals bring female goats (*se'irah*)? Might a male goat be considered relevant to the *chatat* category?

The *gemara* goes through several permutations. What if the *nasi* sinned before his appointment? He must bring a female *se'irah* for that sin, since at the time of sin he was a commoner. If he accidentally designated a male instead, should Rabbi Shimon say it has *kedushat haguf* because now, as *nasi*, he would bring a male? The *gemara* rejects this: *ha lo cheit lo chayav b'se'ir* — he has not sinned as a *nasi*, and the male is not relevant to the sin at hand. What makes something "relevant" in Rabbi Shimon's calculus is whether the alternate gender is relevant specifically to this person for this particular obligation — not whether there exists some other circumstance under which this person might bring the other gender.

### Bird Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו, וְהָיָה בָּהֶן בְּהֵמָה רְאוּיָה לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss Rabbi Shimon's opinion in the *baraita*: **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Shekalim* 4:7): In the case of **one who consecrates** all **his possessions** without specifying for what purpose, **and among them there is an animal that is suitable** to be sacrificed **on the altar,** or multiple such animals that are **males and females,** what should be done with them?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: זְכָרִים יִמָּכְרוּ לְצׇרְכֵי עוֹלוֹת, וּנְקֵבוֹת יִמָּכְרוּ לְצׇרְכֵי שְׁלָמִים, וּדְמֵיהֶן יִפְּלוּ עִם שְׁאָר נְכָסִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** Since he did not specify otherwise, everything is consecrated for Temple maintenance. Therefore, any **males** should **be sold for the needs of burnt offerings,** i.e., to individuals who will sacrifice them as burnt offerings. **And** any **females,** as they cannot be brought as burnt offerings, should **be sold for the needs of peace offerings,** i.e., to individuals who will sacrifice them as such. **And their monetary value** that is received from their sale is **allocated with the rest of his property for Temple maintenance.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: זְכָרִים עַצְמָן יִקְרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת, וּנְקֵבוֹת יִמָּכְרוּ לְצׇרְכֵי שְׁלָמִים, וְיָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהֶן עוֹלוֹת, וּשְׁאָר נְכָסִים יִפְּלוּ לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** Although he did not specify for what purpose he consecrated his possessions, it may be assumed that he intended the animals to be consecrated as burnt offerings. Therefore, any **males** should **themselves be sacrificed as burnt offerings, and** any **females,** since they cannot be brought as burnt offerings, should **be sold for the needs of peace offerings,** i.e., to individuals who will sacrifice them as such, **and their monetary value** that is received from their sale should be used to purchase and **bring burnt offerings. And** according to both opinions, **the rest of the property,** which is not suitable for sacrificial use, **is allocated for Temple maintenance.**

This principle of personal relevance is what resolves the *olah* case. The *gemara* asks: even granting that a bird *olah* can be female, when this person consecrates a sheep as an *olah*, he is not bringing a bird. Why should a female *olat ha'of* be considered relevant to him?

The *gemara* answers: *Rabbi Shimon savar lah k'Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah*. As a *mishnah* elsewhere records, Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah holds that if one says *harei alai olah* — "I am obligating myself in an *olah*" — one may discharge that obligation by bringing *tor o bnei yonah*, pigeons or turtledoves. Even a person of means, when undertaking a voluntary *olah* obligation, retains the option of fulfilling it with a bird. Therefore, the *olat ha'of* is not an irrelevant category for this person — it is a genuine alternative available to him. A female is relevant to his *olah* obligation because the bird option, which has no gender restriction, is always open to him. This is sufficient for Rabbi Shimon to grant *kedushat haguf* for a female *olah*.

This brings the entire analysis into focus. Rabbi Shimon's position is coherent and consistent: if the animal is not fit for what was intended, it receives only *kedushat damim*. The one case where he concedes is where, for this person in this context, the alternate gender is genuinely relevant — as it is for *olah*, since a female bird *olah* is always an option. The *tanna kamma* holds that any animal within the general category of *korbanot* (not a *baal mum*, not an inanimate object) always receives *kedushat haguf*. The *gemara* never fully articulates why the *chachamim* grant the upgrade, though the implication is that animals which could be used as some type of *korban* — even if not the intended one — retain sufficient connection to the world of *korbanot* to receive *kedushat haguf*.

## Property Sanctification

### Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דְּאָמַר זְכָרִים עַצְמָן יִקְרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת, וּנְקֵבוֹת הֵיכִי (מקרבן) [קָרְבָן] שְׁלָמִים? הָא מִכֹּחַ קְדוּשָּׁה דְּחוּיָּיה קָאָתְיָין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, who said** that any **males** should **themselves be sacrificed as burnt offerings,** as that was the intention of the one who consecrated them, **how can** the buyers **sacrifice the females** as **peace offerings?** He explains the difficulty: **Their** status **stems from deferred sanctity,** as they were consecrated to be burnt offerings, and a female that was designated as a burnt offering is not sacrificed upon the altar, but instead is left to graze until it becomes blemished and is then sold.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לִישָּׁנָא אַחְרִינָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִדְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ זְכָרִים עַצְמָן יִקְרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת, לְמֵימְרָא דִּקְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף אַקְדְּשִׁינְהוּ? אִי הָכִי, נְקֵבוֹת אַמַּאי יִמָּכְרוּ לְצׇרְכֵי שְׁלָמִים? בָּעֲיָא רְעִיָּיה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites **another version of** this statement: **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said to Rabbi Yoḥanan:** From the fact **that Rabbi Yehoshua said** that any **males** should **themselves be sacrificed as burnt offerings, is this to say that he consecrated them** with **inherent sanctity? If so, why are the females sold for the needs of peace offerings?** Since they were consecrated as burnt offerings, it is **required** that they be left to **graze** until they become blemished.

### Yehoshua View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: כֹּל מִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ — לָא נָחֲתָא לֵיהּ קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: תִּמָּכֵר שֶׁלֹּא בְּמוּם, וְאָמְרִינַן דְּטַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חַזְיָא נְקֵבָה לְאָשָׁם, לָא נָחֲתָא לֵיהּ קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: **Rabbi Yehoshua holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** with regard to **anything that is not fit** to be sacrificed **itself** upon the altar that **inherent sanctity does not descend upon it. As we learned** in the mishna that **Rabbi Shimon says:** If one designated a female for a guilt offering, for which one must bring a male, **it may be sold without a blemish,** and a guilt offering is purchased with the money received for its sale. **And we say that the reason of Rabbi Shimon** is that **as a female** animal **is not fit** to be sacrificed **as a guilt offering, inherent sanctity does not descend upon it.**

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees. When a person consecrates animals along with his property, the implicit *kedusha* he places on unblemished animals fit for the *mizbe'ach* is *kedushat korban*, not *kedushat bedek habayit*. People naturally associate animals with *korbanot*. Moreover, since an *olah* represents the fullest form of giving to the *beis hamikdash* — unlike a *shelamim*, where the owner retakes most of the meat — the translation of this consecration is that the animals are treated as *olot*. Therefore, *zcharim atzman yikrevu olot* — the males are brought directly as *olot*. As for the females, they are sold to people bringing *shelamim*, and those proceeds are used to purchase an *olah*.

### Female Olah Issue

This immediately raises a difficulty. If the males are brought directly with *kedushat haguf* as *olot*, then the females, by the same logic, also have *kedushat haguf* as *olot*. But if so, how can they be sold without a *mum*? Rabbi Shimon — and even the *tanna kamma* — required a *mum* before redeeming a female *olah*. The *gemara* presses: *ba'aya re'iya* — it needs to graze, it needs to acquire a *mum*. You cannot simply sell an unblemished female with *kedushat haguf* and use the proceeds for something else.

### Yehoshua Agrees Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן גַּבֵּי נְקֵבָה לְאָשָׁם, דְּלֵיכָּא שֵׁם אָשָׁם עַל אִמּוֹ, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי נְקֵבָה לְעוֹלָה דְּאִיכָּא שֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ — אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מוֹדֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **You can** say that **Rabbi Shimon** expressed his opinion specifically **with regard to** one who consecrates **a female for a guilt offering,** **where there is no guilt offering status** for an animal that is the same sex **as its mother,** as a female guilt offering is never brought. **But with regard to** one who designates **a female** animal **as a burnt offering, where there is burnt offering status** for a bird that is the same sex **as its mother, even Rabbi Shimon concedes** that it has inherent sanctity, and therefore it should not be sold for the needs of a peace offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לְעוֹלָתוֹ עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **In addition,** another objection can be raised against the explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as **we have heard that Rabbi Shimon** maintains that one who designates a female animal **as a burnt offering renders** a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it **a substitute.** This indicates that it does have inherent sanctity with regard to this issue. Therefore, the question remains: How can the buyers sacrifice the females as peace offerings, when their status stems from deferred sanctity?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר לֵיהּ כְּאִידַּךְ תַּנָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אַף לְעוֹלָתוֹ אֵין עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba in response: I meant that **Rabbi Yehoshua holds in accordance with** the interpretation of **the other *tanna* with regard** to the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: Even** one who consecrates a female **as his burnt offering does not render** a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it **a substitute.** Even though there is burnt offering status for female birds, a female animal consecrated as a burnt offering does not have inherent sanctity. Therefore, it is sold and sacrificed as a peace offering, and its status is not considered to stem from deferred sanctity.

The *gemara* resolves this: *Rabbi Yehoshua savar lei k'Rabbi Shimon* — Rabbi Yehoshua holds in accordance with Rabbi Shimon's opinion in our *mishnah*, that *kol midi d'lo chazi lei l'gufei lo nachsi lei kedushat haguf* — whatever cannot be brought itself does not receive *kedushat haguf*, only *kedushat damim*. Therefore, the female animals, though consecrated as *olot*, never receive *kedushat haguf* — they have only *kedushat damim* — and can be sold without a *mum*. The proceeds carry the *kedusha* that was on the females and are used to purchase an *olah*. Rabbi Yehoshua thus aligns himself with a particular reading of Rabbi Shimon — specifically the reading that a female *olah* does not receive *kedushat haguf* — which corresponds to the dissenting *tanna* cited earlier: *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda omer mishum Rabbi Shimon: af le-olah eino oseh temurah*.

## Temurah & Vlad

### Mishna Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ תְּמוּרַת אָשָׁם, וְלַד תְּמוּרָה, וְלָדָן וְלַד וְלָדָן עַד סוֹף כׇּל הָעוֹלָם — יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיִמָּכְרוּ, וְיִפְּלוּ (דָּמָיו) [דְּמֵיהֶן] לִנְדָבָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יָמוּתוּ. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: יָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהּ עוֹלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the substitute of a guilt offering, the offspring of** that **substitute, their offspring** and **the offspring of their offspring, until the end of all time,** they are all left to **graze until they become unfit, and** then **they are sold, and** the **money** received for the sale **is allocated for** communal **gift offerings. Rabbi Eliezer says:** These animals are not left to graze; rather **they** are left to **die. And Rabbi Elazar says:** Communal gift offerings are not purchased with the money from the sale; rather, the owner should **bring** an individual **burnt offering with the money** received for **its** sale.

The *mishnah* now addresses directly what happens to a *temurah* of an *asham* and its offspring. If one has a *temurah* of an *asham* — or the *vlad* of that *temurah*, or the *vlad* of a *vlad*, down through all generations — the rule is *yiru ad she'yista'avu v'yimachru v'yipelu dmeihem l'nedava*: they graze until they acquire a *mum*, are sold, and the proceeds go to *nedava* — a communal *olah*. This parallels the general treatment of a *temurah* of an *asham*: whereas a *temurah* of a *chatat* is left to die, a *temurah* of an *asham* grazes and the money is used for an *olah*.

### Eliezer Dissent

Rabbi Eliezer dissents: *yamutu* — they die. His position identifies the *asham* with the *chatat* in this respect. The *gemara* will clarify that his reasoning is not a fundamental equation of *asham* and *chatat* but a rabbinic precaution, as we will see shortly.

### Elazar Dissent

Rabbi Elazar dissents in the opposite direction: *yavi bidmei olot* — one brings an *olah* with their proceeds. This, as the *mishnah* itself will immediately clarify, means a private *olah* rather than a communal one.

### Five Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָשָׁם שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלָיו, וְשֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ בְּעָלָיו — יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיִמָּכְרוּ, וְיִפְּלוּ דָּמָיו לִנְדָבָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יָמוּתוּ. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: יָבִיא בְּדָמֶיהָ עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** These *tanna'im* similarly disagree about the following case: **A guilt offering whose owner died, and** a guilt offering **that** was lost and **its owner gained atonement** with another animal, **graze until they become unfit, and** then **they are sold, and** the **money** received for the sale **is allocated** for communal **gift offerings. Rabbi Eliezer says:** These animals are left to **die. Rabbi Elazar says:** The owner must **bring** an individual **burnt offering with the money** received for **its** sale.

The *mishnah* then widens the discussion to all the cases in which an *asham* becomes unavailable for its intended purpose — cases that parallel the *chatat* that dies. These include: *asham she'meitu ba'alav* — the owners died; *she'nitkapru ba'alav ba'acher* — they were atoned for by another animal; *temurah*; *vlad*; and an animal that has exceeded its valid age. These five situations present the question of what becomes of a derailed *asham*.

### Default Halacha

The default ruling: *yiru ad she'yista'avu v'yimachru v'yipelu dmeihem l'nedava* — they graze until blemished, are sold, and the proceeds go to a communal *olah* (*nedavat tzibur*). Rabbi Eliezer says they die. Rabbi Elazar says they are used for a private *olah*.

### Private vs. Communal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא אַף נְדָבָה עוֹלָה הִיא, וּמָה בֵּין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים? אֶלָּא, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהִיא בָּאָה עוֹלָה — סוֹמֵךְ עָלֶיהָ, וּמֵבִיא נְסָכִים, וּנְסָכֶיהָ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ. אִם הָיָה כֹּהֵן — עֲבוֹדָתָהּ וְעוֹרָהּ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna objects: **But** even according to the Rabbis, **isn't a gift offering** also **a burnt offering? And what** then **is** the difference **between the statement of Rabbi Elazar and the statement of the Rabbis? Rather,** the Rabbis are referring to a communal burnt offering and Rabbi Elazar is referring to an individual burnt offering, and there are several differences between these two offerings: **When** the animal **comes** as an individual **burnt offering,** the owner **places** his **hands upon it and brings** the accompanying meal offering and **libations, and its libations** come **from his** own property. **If** the owner of the animal **was a priest,** the right to perform **its Temple service and** the right to **its hide are his.**

The *mishnah* itself poses the question: given that both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Elazar are directing the animal or its proceeds toward an *olah*, what is the practical difference between them?

### Private Olah Details

If it is a private *olah*, as Rabbi Elazar holds: the owner performs *semicha* on it; he must supply the *nesachim* — the wine, oil, and flour libations — at his own expense; if he is a *kohen* and brings his own *korban*, he may perform the *avoda* that day regardless of which *mishmar* is on duty, and the hide of the animal belongs to him.

### Communal Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִזְמַן שֶׁהִיא נְדָבָה — אֵינוֹ סוֹמֵךְ עָלֶיהָ, וְאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא עָלֶיהָ נְסָכִים, וּנְסָכֶיהָ מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא כֹּהֵן — עֲבוֹדָתָהּ וְעוֹרָהּ מִשֶּׁל אַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And when it is a** communal **gift offering,** the owner of the animal that was sold **does not place** his **hands upon it,** as there is no placing of hands for communal offerings, **and he does not bring its libations;** rather, **its libations** are brought **from** the property **of the community.** Furthermore, **although** the owner of the animal that was sold **is a priest,** the right to perform **its Temple service and** the right to **its hide** are divided **among the members of the priestly watch** serving in the Temple that week.

If it is a communal *olah* (*nedava*), as the *tanna kamma* holds: there is no *semicha*, since *semicha* applies only to private *korbanot* brought by an individual owner; the *nesachim* are funded from communal resources, not the individual's; and even if the person happens to be a *kohen*, he has no special right to perform the *avoda* — the *kohanim* of the assigned *mishmar* for that week perform the service and retain the hide.

## Timing Doctrine

### Eliezer's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּצְרִיכָא: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן אָשָׁם, בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר יָמוּתוּ, מִשּׁוּם דְּגָזַר לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה אַטּוּ לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages disagreed about two cases, the substitute of a guilt offering and the *halakha* of a guilt offering whose owner died. The Gemara explains that both disputes are **necessary.** **As, had** the *tanna* **taught us** the dispute only in the case of **the guilt offering** where the owner achieved atonement through another animal, I might have said that perhaps it is **in this** case alone that **Rabbi Eliezer says that** the animals are left to **die.** This would be **because he** holds that there is a rabbinic **decree concerning** what to do with a guilt offering **following** the owner having achieved **atonement, due to** the case of a guilt offering **before** the owner achieved **atonement.** If burnt offerings were brought with the money from the sale in a case where the owner already achieved atonement by means of another offering, people might mistakenly say that if a guilt offering was lost and another was designated in its place, there too the other animal is sold and burnt offerings are brought from the proceeds. In fact, in that case, as atonement has not yet been achieved, that money must actually be used for a guilt offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל גַּבֵּי תְּמוּרַת אָשָׁם, וְלַד תְּמוּרָתָהּ, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to the substitute of a guilt offering** and **the offspring of its substitute,** which in any case are sent to graze, even if the owner did not achieve atonement with another animal, one might **say** that Rabbi Eliezer **concedes to the Rabbis,** as there is no need for such a decree.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָתָם, בְּהָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי אָשָׁם מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר — צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the *tanna* **taught us** the dispute only **there,** in the case of the substitute of a guilt offering and the offspring of the substitute, I might have said that perhaps it is only **in this** case that **the Rabbis say** the animals are sent to graze, as there is no reason for a decree. **But with regard to** the case of **a guilt offering** where the owners achieved atonement, one might say that **they concede to Rabbi Eliezer** that the animals are left to die, as a decree. Therefore, both cases **are necessary.**

The *gemara* now returns to Rabbi Eliezer's position that the *temurah* and *vlad* die, and clarifies that the *mishnah* needed to state his ruling in both contexts — both in the case of *temurah* and *vlad*, and in the case of *meitu ba'alav* and *nitkapru b'acher*.

Had the *mishnah* stated his ruling only in the case where owners died or were atoned for elsewhere, one might have thought that Rabbi Eliezer's concern there is specifically: *mishum degazar achar kapara atu lifnei kapara* — he decrees that the animal must die after atonement as a precaution against doing so before atonement. If we were to permit the original *asham* to be redeemed and its proceeds used for an *olah* after the special circumstances arise, people might come to redeem the original *asham* even before those circumstances arise, conflating "after atonement" with "before atonement." But this concern applies only to the original *asham*. For the *temurah* and *vlad* — which are not the original — one might think Rabbi Eliezer agrees with the *rabbanan* and permits them to graze, since nobody will make any mistake there. Had the *mishnah* stated his ruling only for *temurah* and *vlad*, one might think that there the *rabbanan* perhaps also concede that the original *asham* should die, lest we come to redeem it prematurely. Therefore, *tzericha* — both cases are necessary.

### After Atonement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: מַחְלוֹקֶת לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה, אֲבָל לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הוּא עַצְמוֹ יִקְרַב אָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** This **dispute** between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis with regard to the offspring of a substitute applies only **after atonement** has been achieved, i.e., after the guilt offering has been sacrificed. **But before atonement** is achieved, and the consecrated animal and the substitute are both present, **everyone agrees** that even the offspring **itself is sacrificed** as **a guilt offering,** if the owner wishes.

The *gemara* records a teaching from Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha: *zu machlokes le-achar kaparah* — the three-way debate of the *mishnah* (die, communal *olah*, private *olah*) applies only after the owner has already received his atonement. *Aval lifnei kaparah* — but before the owner has achieved atonement, using the original first *asham* — *divrei hakol hu atzmo yikarev asham*: everyone agrees that the *temurah* or *vlad* itself can be brought as an *asham*.

### Rava Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁתֵּי תְּשׁוּבוֹת בַּדָּבָר, חֲדָא דְּאֵין אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּדָבָר הַבָּא בַּעֲבֵירָה, וְעוֹד, הָתָנֵי רַב חֲנַנְיָא לְסַיּוֹעֵי לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי דְּאָמַר וָלָד רִאשׁוֹן קָרֵב, וְלַד שֵׁנִי אֵינוֹ קָרֵב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There are **two refutations of** this **statement: One** is **that a person cannot achieve atonement through an item that comes from a transgression,** and this offspring comes from a transgression, as its mother offspring was rendered a substitute, which is prohibited. **And in addition, didn't Rav Ḥananya teach** a *baraita* **in support of** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who said** that only **the first offspring** of a peace offering **is sacrificed,** but **the second offspring,** i.e., the offspring of the offspring, **is not sacrificed?** Here too, the offspring of a substitute is considered like the second offspring, as it is two stages removed from the original offering.

Rava rejects this emphatically: *shtei teshuvos badavar* — two responses. First: *ein adam miskaper bedavar haba ba-aveira* — one cannot achieve atonement through something that itself came through a transgression. Performing a *temurah* is itself an *aveira*; the *temurah* is a product of sin. To suggest that this product of sin can serve as a proper *asham* to atone for sin is inconceivable. An *asham*'s entire purpose is atonement, and something produced through transgression cannot serve that purpose.

Second, Rava cites the teaching of Rabbi Chanina, brought in support of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who held that a *vlad rishon* — the first-generation offspring — can be brought as a *korban*, but a *vlad sheni* — a second-generation offspring — cannot. Since one of the cases in the *mishnah* involves the *vlad* of the *temurah* — which is two degrees removed from the original — the suggestion that this animal could itself be brought as an *asham* is ruled out on this ground as well.

### Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִי אִיתְּמַר, הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: מַחְלוֹקֶת קוֹדֶם כַּפָּרָה, אֲבָל לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה — הוּא עַצְמוֹ קָרֵב עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** it **was stated,** it **was stated like this: Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis with regard to the offspring of a substitute applies only **before atonement** has been achieved,as Rabbi Eliezer holds that it is left to die, due to a concern that it might be sacrificed as a guilt offering, whereas the Rabbis say that it is left to graze, as there is no concern that it will be brought as a guilt offering. **But after** the original guilt offering is sacrificed and **atonement** has been achieved, everyone agrees that even the offspring **itself is sacrificed** as **a burnt offering,** as there is no further concern that it might be sacrificed as a guilt offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָנֵי רַב חֲנַנְיָא לְסַיּוֹעֵי לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav Ḥananya teach** a *baraita* **in support of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** that the second offspring is not sacrificed? How, then, can the offspring of the substitute be sacrificed, as it too should be treated like the second offspring? The Gemara concedes: That **is difficult.**

The *gemara* therefore corrects the formulation: *ela i itmar hachi itmar* — if Rav Nachman's teaching was indeed transmitted, here is how it should be understood. The three-way debate — die, communal *olah*, private *olah* — applies *kodem kapara*, before the owner has used the original *asham* and received atonement. *Aval le-achar kapara* — but once the owner has already used the original *asham* and been atoned for, *hu atzmo karev olah* — the *temurah* or *vlad* itself may be brought directly as an *olah*, rather than the roundabout procedure of grazing until blemished, selling, and using the proceeds.

The reasoning: once the original *asham* has been brought, the animal is no longer under the shadow of an *asham* obligation. The *asham* identity has been discharged. This remaining animal is heading toward an *olah* one way or another — why not use it directly? Before the original *asham* was brought, the animal still exists in an *asham* context and the identity of *temurah* or *vlad* of an *asham* constrains it. But once the original has been offered and the *asham* obligation is fully resolved, the animal can proceed directly to be brought as an *olah*, at least if it is male and otherwise fit.

The *gemara* concludes with *kasha* — a difficulty remains. If the case involves a *vlad* of the *temurah*, that is two degrees removed, and the teaching of Rabbi Chanina cited above indicates that a second-generation offspring cannot be brought. This difficulty stands unresolved.
