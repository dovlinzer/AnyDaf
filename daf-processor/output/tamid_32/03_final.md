# Tamid 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Daily Offering Location

### Binding Legs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָד וָרֶגֶל, כַּעֲקֵידַת יִצְחָק בֶּן אַבְרָהָם. לֹא הָיוּ כּוֹפְתִין אֶת הַטָּלֶה. מַאי טַעְמָא? רַב הוּנָא וְרַב חִסְדָּא, חַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם בִּזְיוֹן קֳדָשִׁים. וְחַד אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם דִּמְהַלֵּךְ בְּחוּקֵּי הָעַמִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: The animal's **foreleg and** hind **leg** are bound together, **as** in the **binding of Isaac, son of Abraham.** The mishna teaches that the priests **would not tie the lamb** by fastening all four of its legs together. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara answers: This is a matter of dispute between **Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda. One** of these Sages **said:** The animal is not tied **because** this would constitute **degradation of sacred items; and** the other **one said** that the animal is not tied **because that** method is the one adopted in pagan worship, and is therefore considered to be **walking in the statutes of the nations,** and the verse states: "You shall not walk in their statutes" (Leviticus 18:3).

The daf begins near the bottom of 31b, continuing into 32a, with the *gemara*'s inquiry into the source for the placement of the *tamid* offering: *shachar haya nishchat al keren mizrachit tzfonit* — the morning offering was slaughtered at the northeastern corner. Before examining the positioning directly, it is worth noting a point raised in the previous session: the Rambam's distinctive understanding of the binding of the animal's legs. Most authorities hold that the front leg and front foot were bound together on the left side, and similarly on the right — that is, each leg bound to its corresponding foot — before the animal was placed on the hooks for *shechita*. The Rambam, however, understands that no binding in the conventional sense took place at all. Rather, the leg and foot were threaded together through the ring itself, which served to hold the animal steady during slaughter. Virtually all other *mefarshim* reject this view, but it is the Rambam's characteristic position.

### Ring Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל שַׁחַר הָיָה נִשְׁחָט עַל קֶרֶן צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״שְׁנַיִם לַיּוֹם״, כְּנֶגֶד הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the daily offering **of the morning was slaughtered at the northwest corner** of the altar, in the first ring of the second row from the south, which is called the second ring, whereas the daily offering of the afternoon was slaughtered at the northeast corner of the altar, at the second ring. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Ḥisda said: As the verse states,** with regard to the daily offering: "This is the offering made by fire that you shall bring to the Lord: Lambs of the first year without blemish, **two by day,** for a continual burnt offering" (Numbers 28:3). The phrase "two by day" indicates that the lamb must be slaughtered **opposite** the light of **the day.** Since Eretz Yisrael is north of the equator, the sun is always in the southern part of the sky. The first ring, then, is always in the long shadow of the altar, and only the second ring falls under direct sunlight.

Most *mefarshim* understand "northwest" and "northeast" to refer to the rings near the northwest and northeast corners of the *mizbe'ach* itself, not of the entire Temple precinct. However, the Yachin u'Voaz — that is, the *Tiferet Yisrael*'s classic commentary on the *mishnah* — argues, and suggests that the Rambam concurs, that "northeast" and "northwest" refer to the northeast and northwest of the entire space, not merely of the *mizbe'ach* corners. Under that reading, the rings in question would be the further row. This remains a point of discussion among the commentators.

### Facing the Sun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״שְׁנַיִם לַיּוֹם״ – כְּנֶגֶד הַיּוֹם. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר נֶגֶד הַיּוֹם, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא חוֹבַת הַיּוֹם? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֶת הַכֶּבֶשׂ אֶחָד תַּעֲשֶׂה בַבֹּקֶר וְאֵת הַכֶּבֶשׂ הַשֵּׁנִי תַּעֲשֶׂה בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״ – הֲרֵי חוֹבַת הַיּוֹם אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״שְׁנַיִם לַיּוֹם״ – נֶגֶד הַיּוֹם. הָא כֵיצַד? תָּמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר – הָיָה נִשְׁחָט עַל קֶרֶן צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, עַל טַבַּעַת שְׁנִיָּה, וְשֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם – הָיָה נִשְׁחָט עַל קֶרֶן צְפוֹנִית מִזְרָחִית, עַל טַבַּעַת שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*, that the phrase **"two by day"** teaches that the lamb must be slaughtered **opposite** the light of **the day.** The *baraita* asks: **Do you say** that this means **opposite** the light of **the day, or** does it **only** mean that two lambs must be sacrificed for the **obligation of** each **day?** The *baraita* answers that **when** the verse **states: "One lamb you shall sacrifice in the morning, and the other lamb you shall sacrifice in the afternoon"** (Numbers 28:4), **the obligation of** each **day is** thereby **stated** explicitly. **How,** then, **do I realize** the meaning of **"two by day"?** This teaches that the lamb must be slaughtered **opposite** the light of **the day.** The *baraita* concludes: **How so,** i.e., how can this principle be applied to both the morning and the afternoon offerings? The **daily offering of the morning was slaughtered opposite** the **northwest corner** of the altar, **on** the **second ring,** across from the sun, which rises in the east. **And** the daily offering **of the afternoon was slaughtered opposite** the **northeast corner** of the altar, **on** the fourth ring of the second row, also called the **second ring,** again across from the sun, which is located in the west in the afternoon.

The *gemara* then asks: *mina hanei mili* — what is the source for this positioning? Rav Chisda cites the verse *shnayim layom kineged hayom* — "two each day, facing the sun" — where *yom* is understood to mean the sun itself. A *baraita* then confirms this reading. The verse already states explicitly that one lamb is brought in the morning and one in the afternoon — *chovat hayom*, the daily obligation, is thus already established. Why then does the Torah again say *shnayim layom*? To teach *kineged hayom* — that the positioning must face the sun. The *baraita* then elaborates: *tamid shel shachar haya nishchat al keren tzfonit ma'aravit* — the morning offering at the northwest corner, *al taba'at shniya* — on the second ring; and the afternoon offering at the northeast corner, again on the second ring.

The reasoning is geometric and practical. In the morning, the sun rises in the east, casting shadows from the eastern wall westward. To have the animal positioned in direct sunlight — *kineged hayom* — one places it at the northwest, away from those shadows. In the afternoon, the sun is in the west, and shadows fall in the opposite direction; accordingly, the afternoon offering is placed at the northeast. One might have thought the Temple service would specifically *avoid* any appearance of solar orientation, but the *gemara* takes the requirement at face value: the positioning is designed to ensure the animal stands fully exposed to sunlight.

## Alexander & Ten Sages

### Ten Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים שָׁאַל אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס מוֹקְדוֹן אֶת זִקְנֵי הַנֶּגֶב, אָמַר לָהֶן:
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the position of the sun, the Gemara relates that **Alexander of Macedon asked the Elders of the Negev** about **ten matters. He said to them:**

The *gemara* now introduces a lengthy and remarkable *aggadata*: *asara devarim she'al Alexandros Mokdon et ziknei ha-Negev* — ten things that Alexander the Great, Alexander of Macedon, asked the elders of the South. The connection to the preceding discussion of east, west, and the sun will become apparent as we proceed.

### Heaven/Earth vs East/West

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם לָאָרֶץ רָחוֹק, אוֹ מִמִּזְרָח לַמַּעֲרָב?
>
> **Translation:** Is the distance **from the heavens to the earth further, or** is the distance **from east to west** further?

The first question: *min shamayim la-aretz rachok o mi-mizrach le-ma'arav* — which is the greater distance, from earth to the heavens or from east to west?

### Distance Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִמִּזְרָח לַמַּעֲרָב. תֵּדַע, שֶׁהֲרֵי חַמָּה בַּמִּזְרָח – הַכֹּל מִסְתַּכְּלִין בָּהּ, חַמָּה בַּמַּעֲרָב – הַכֹּל מִסְתַּכְּלִין בָּהּ, חַמָּה בְּאֶמְצַע רָקִיעַ – אֵין הַכֹּל מִסְתַּכְּלִין בָּהּ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זֶה וָזֶה כְּאֶחָד שָׁוִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּגְבוֹהַּ שָׁמַיִם עַל הָאָרֶץ [וְגוֹ׳] כִּרְחוֹק מִזְרָח מִמַּעֲרָב״. וְאִי חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ נְפִישׁ, נִכְתּוֹב תַּרְוַויְיהוּ כִּי הָהוּא דִּנְפִישׁ! וְאֶלָּא, חַמָּה בְּאֶמְצַע רָקִיעַ מַאי טַעְמָא אֵין הַכֹּל מִסְתַּכְּלִין בָּהּ – מִשּׁוּם דְּקָאֵי לְהֶדְיָא, וְלָא כָּסֵי לֵיהּ מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: From east to west** is a greater distance. **Know** that this is so, **as** when the **sun is in the east, everyone looks at it** without hurting their eyes, and when the **sun is in the west, everyone looks at it** without hurting their eyes. By contrast, when the **sun is in** the **middle of** the **sky, no one looks at it,** as it would hurt their eyes. This shows that the sun's place in the middle of the sky is not as far from the earth as its remote positions in the extreme east and west. **But the Sages say: This** distance **and that** distance **are equal, as it is stated: "For as the heaven is high above the earth,** so great is His kindness toward them that fear Him. **As far as the east is from the west,** so far has He removed our transgressions from us" (Psalms 103:11–12). The verses compare the extent of God's kindness and His removal of transgressions to vast expanses. **And if one of the** distances **is greater** than the other, **let** the verse **write** that **both of** God's enumerated attributes are **like** the measure **that is greater. But** if so, with regard to the **sun in** the **middle of** the **sky, what is the reason** that **no one looks at it?** It is **because it stands exposed and nothing covers it,** whereas it is partially screened when it is in the east or the west.

The sages answered: *mi-mizrach le-ma'arav* — from east to west is the greater distance. Their proof: *chama be-mizrach ha-kol mistaklin bah* — when the sun is in the east at sunrise, everyone can look directly at it; *chama be-ma'arav ha-kol mistaklin bah* — when the sun is in the west at sunset, everyone can look at it. But *chama be-emtza raki'a ein ha-kol mistaklin bah* — at noon, when the sun is directly overhead, no one can look at it. The sages inferred from this that the sun must be *closer* to the earth at midday, and its greater intensity at that distance makes it impossible to view directly. If east to west were a shorter distance than the distance from earth to sky, the sun would be further from us at noon than at sunrise or sunset — but the evidence suggests the opposite.

This reasoning is, of course, scientifically incorrect, and the *gemara* itself immediately qualifies it. *Ve-chachamim omrim: zeh va-zeh ke-echad shavin* — the Sages say both distances are actually equal. The proof is from the verse in Psalms, which in a single metaphor compares something to *ki-gevo'ah shamayim al ha-aretz* — the height of the heavens over the earth — and then to *ki-rachok mizrach mi-ma'arav* — the distance from east to west. If one were greater than the other, the verse would have cited only the larger distance for its comparison. The fact that both are used side by side implies they are equal.

### Sun Brightness

If the two distances are equal, however, what is the actual explanation for the sun appearing more intense at midday? The *gemara* answers: *meshum de-kai le-hedya* — because at noon it stands directly overhead, *ve-lo kasi lei midi* — and nothing screens it. The *mefarshim* explain that when the sun is low on the horizon, its rays must travel through the terrain and geography lying between the observer and the sun — mountains, surface features, and the like — which diffract or attenuate the light. When the sun is directly overhead, nothing interposes. The scientific explanation, which is closely related, involves the angle at which sunlight strikes the earth's surface and the atmosphere it must traverse. When the sun is at a low angle, a given unit of solar energy is spread across a larger surface area and passes through a thicker cross-section of atmosphere; when the sun is directly overhead, that same unit strikes a smaller area perpendicularly, concentrating its intensity. This angle-of-incidence effect is also related to the well-known phenomenon of the moon appearing larger near the horizon than it does high in the sky. The sages of the *gemara*, working within a cosmological framework that imagined the sky as a solid dome and the sun as traveling beneath it by day and above it by night, arrived at their answer through a different mode of reasoning — but they were asking the right question.

### Light vs Darkness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: אוֹר נִבְרָא תְּחִלָּה, אוֹ חֹשֶׁךְ? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִילְּתָא דָּא אֵין לַהּ פָּתַר.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them:** Was the **light created first, or** was the **darkness** created first? **They said to him: This matter has no solution,** as the verses do not indicate an answer.

The next question: *or nivra techila o choshech* — was light created first, or darkness?

### Strategic Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימְרוּ לֵיהּ חֹשֶׁךְ נִבְרָא תְּחִלָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָאָרֶץ הָיְתָה תֹהוּ וָבֹהוּ וְחֹשֶׁךְ״, וַהֲדַר ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֱלֹהִים יְהִי אוֹר וַיְהִי אוֹר״! סָבְרִי, דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְשַׁיּוֹלֵי מָה לְמַעְלָה וּמָה לְמַטָּה, מָה לְפָנִים וּמָה לְאָחוֹר. אִי הָכִי, שָׁמַיִם נָמֵי לָא נֵימְרוּ לֵיהּ?! מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר אַקְרַאי בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּקָא שָׁאֵיל, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּקָהָדַר שָׁאֵיל, סָבְרִי לָא נֵימָא לֵיהּ, דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְשַׁיּוֹלֵי מָה לְמַעְלָה מָה לְמַטָּה, מָה לְפָנִים וּמָה לְאָחוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let them say to him** that the **darkness was created first, as it is written: "Now the earth was unformed and void, and darkness** was upon the face of the deep" (Genesis 1:2), **and** only **then** does it state: **"And God said: Let there be light. And there was light"** (Genesis 1:3). Why did they not say that the darkness was created first? The Gemara answers that the Elders **maintained:** We must not answer this question, **lest** he **come to ask** questions about Creation that may not be discussed, i.e., **what** is **above** the firmament **and what is below** the earth, **what was before** Creation, **and what** will be **after** the end of the world (see *Ḥagiga* 11b). The Gemara asks: **If so,** if the Elders were concerned about such proscribed questions, then with regard to the creation of **heaven as well, they should not** have **said** anything **to him.** Why did they answer the question about heaven, but not the one about darkness? The Gemara answers that **initially** they **assumed:** It is **merely incidental** that **he is asking** about the creation of the universe, and therefore there is no need for caution. But **once they saw that he again asked** about the same general matter, **they maintained: Let us not tell him** an answer, **lest** he **come to ask: What** is **above** the firmament and **what is below** the earth, **what was before** Creation, **and what** will be **after** the end of the world?

Here the sages refused to answer — *milta da ein lah petar*, there is no resolution to this matter. The *gemara* challenges this: surely they could have said darkness was created first, since the verse in *Bereishit* describes *tohu vavohu* and *choshech* — formless void and darkness — and only afterward does God say *yehi or*. Why the silence?

The *gemara* explains: *savri dilma asi leshiyuleina* — they were concerned that once they began engaging with cosmological questions, Alexander would proceed to ask *mah lema'ala mah lemata mah lefanim mah leachor* — what exists above, what below, what before creation, what after — precisely the matters enumerated in the famous *mishnah* in Chagigah as subjects not to be expounded in public. After all, when Alexander asked about heaven and earth, they answered readily, thinking it was a one-off question. But when he returned immediately with a question about light and darkness, they recognized a pattern: he was systematically probing the secrets of cosmological origins. So they shut it down.

The question of light and darkness is itself theologically weighty. There is a real dispute among commentators as to whether darkness is an independent created entity or merely the absence of light — a distinction with significant implications for how one understands the origin of evil. The morning liturgy recites *yotzer or u'vorei choshech* — "who creates light and creates darkness" — but the concluding phrase of that verse, *oseh shalom u'vorei ra*, "who makes peace and creates evil," is modified in prayer to *u'vorei et hakol*, "who creates everything," out of discomfort with the plain meaning. The sages' reticence here may thus reflect not only a desire to avoid cosmological disclosure but also the genuine sensitivity of the question itself.

Why would the sages withhold such knowledge even from a sincere questioner? This connects to the broader framework in *Chagigah* around *ma'aseh bereishit* and esoteric learning generally — the sense that certain knowledge carries dangerous power, and that not every questioner, however brilliant or well-intentioned, is a fit recipient.

### Wisdom & Virtue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: אֵידֵין מִתְקְרֵי חַכִּים? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵיזֶהוּ חָכָם – הָרוֹאֶה אֶת הַנּוֹלָד. אָמַר לָהֶם: אֵידֵין מִתְקְרֵי גִּבּוֹר? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵיזֶהוּ גִּבּוֹר – הַכּוֹבֵשׁ אֶת יִצְרוֹ. אָמַר לָהֶן: אֵידֵין מִתְקְרֵי עָשִׁיר? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵיזֶהוּ עָשִׁיר – הַשָּׂמֵחַ בְּחֶלְקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Alexander **said to** the Elders: **Who is** truly worthy of being **called wise? They said to him,** citing a tradition (see *Avot* 2:9): **Who is** the **wise** person? **The one who sees** and anticipates **the consequences** of his behavior. **He said to them: Who is** truly worthy of being **called mighty? They said to him,** again citing a tradition (see *Avot* 4:1): **Who is** the **mighty** person? **The one who masters his desire. He said to them: Who is** worthy of being **called wealthy? They said to him: Who is** the **wealthy** person? **The one who is pleased with his** own **portion** (see *Avot* 4:1).

Alexander then pivots to a series of questions that seem, on the surface, far more straightforward: *idin mitkari chacham* — who is called wise? *Idin mitkari gibor* — who is called mighty? *Idin mitkari ashir* — who is called wealthy?

### Sages' Answers

The sages' answers are drawn directly from *Pirkei Avot*: *eizehu chacham ha-roeh et ha-nolad* — the wise person is one who anticipates the future; *eizehu gibor ha-kovesh et yitzro* — the truly mighty person is one who conquers his own *yetzer*; *eizehu ashir ha-samei'ach be-chelko* — the truly wealthy person is one who is content with his portion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהֹן: מָה יַעֲבֵיד אִינִישׁ וְיִחְיֶה? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: יָמִית עַצְמוֹ. מָה יַעֲבֵיד אִינִישׁ וְיָמוּת? יְחַיֶּה אֶת עַצְמוֹ. אֲמַר לְהֹן: מָה יַעֲבֵיד אִינִישׁ וְיִתְקַבֵּל עַל בְּרִיָּיתָא? אֲמַרוּ: יִסְנֵי מַלְכוּ וְשׇׁלְטָן. אֲמַר לְהוּ: דִּידִי טָבָא מִדִּידְכוּ, יִרְחַם מַלְכוּ וְשׇׁלְטָן, וַיַּעֲבֵד טֵיבוּ עִם בְּנֵי אִינָשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: What must a man do and** thereby ensure that **he will live? They said to him:** Such a man **must** figuratively **kill himself,** by living moderately. Alexander further inquired: **What must a man do and** ensure that **he will die? They said to him:** Such a man **must keep himself alive,** i.e., lead an extravagant and indulgent life. **He said to them: What must a man do and** ensure that **he will be accepted by people? They said** to Alexander: **He must hate the king and the authorities** and avoid becoming too close to those in power. Alexander rejected their answer and **said to them: My** advice for gaining people's favor **is better than yours.** One who wants to be accepted **must love the king and the authorities, but he must** use his connections to **perform beneficial** acts **for people.**

Alexander then asked: *ma ya'avid inish v'yichyeh* — what should a person do in order to live? *Yamit atzmo*, the sages replied — let him "kill himself." The *mefarshim* offer several interpretations: self-denial of physical pleasures, hard honest labor, or — most evocatively — the formulation from the *midrash* that *ein ha-Torah mitkayemet ela bemi she-meimit atzmo aleha*, Torah endures only with those who sacrifice themselves for it. The counterpart follows immediately: *ma ya'avid inish ve-yamut* — what leads a person to death? *Yechaye et atzmo* — let him "give himself life," meaning: indulge the bodily self, pursue comfort and pleasure, inflate the ego.

### Authority & Favor

The next question: *ma ya'avid inish ve-yitkabel al ha-briyot* — what should a person do to find favor with people? *Yisne malchut ve-shilton*, the sages answered — despise kingship and rule. This is, of course, a pointed remark to make in the presence of a world conqueror. One interpretation takes it personally: avoid positions of authority yourself, since public office earns more resentment than gratitude. Another reading invokes the *Pirkei Avot* counsel of *tisrachak min ha-reshut* — keep your distance from government — not because authority is inherently wrong, but because proximity to power inevitably entangles one in its conflicts and costs.

### Alexander's Counter

Alexander was not persuaded. *Didi tava midichon*, he said — my answer is better than yours. Rather than *yisne malchut*, one should *yerachem malchut ve-shilton* — embrace and love kingship and rule, because power wielded rightly enables one *le-maheni livnei insha*, to benefit others. This is the one moment in the exchange where Alexander pushes back with a substantive counter-argument rather than simply receiving the sages' teaching.

### Ocean vs Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהֹן: בְּיַמָּא יָאֵי לְמֵידַר, אוֹ בְּיַבִּשְׁתָּא יָאֵי לְמֵידַר? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: בְּיַבִּשְׁתָּא יָאֵי לְמֵידַר, דְּהָא כֹּל נָחוֹתֵי יַמָּא לָא מִיַּתְּבָא דַּעְתֵּיהוֹן עַד דְּסָלְקִין לְיַבִּשְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them:** Is it **better** for a person **to live at sea, or** is it **better to live on dry land? They said to him:** It is **better to live on dry land, as all seafarers, their minds are** constantly **unsettled until they reach the dry land.**

Another question: *beyama yae lmeida o be-yabeshta yae lmeida* — is it better to live on the sea or on dry land? The sages answered without hesitation: dry land is better, and their proof is behavioral — *de-kol nechutei yamma*, all sailors, *lo mitba daitaihu ad de-salki le-yabeshta*, they have no peace of mind until they are back on solid ground. The very longing of seafarers for land demonstrates that land is the natural and proper home of human beings.

### Who is Smartest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: אֵידֵין מִנְּכוֹן חַכִּים יַתִּיר? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: כּוּלָּנָא כַּחֲדָא שָׁוַויִין, דְּהָא כֹּל מִילְּתָא דַּאֲמַרְתְּ לַנָא – בְּחַד פְּתַרְנָא לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: Who among you is wiser** than the others? **They said to him: We are all equal** in wisdom, **as every matter that you say to us, we solve for you unanimously.**

*Idi minnechon chakim yatir* — which among you is the wisest? *Kulanu ke-chada shavinan*, came the reply — we are all equally wise. *De-hachol milta de-amarta lan be-chada patanu lach* — every question you posed to us, we answered as one.

### Opposition & Satan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְהֹן: מָה דֵין אַתְרֵיסְתּוּן לְקִבְלִי! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: סִטְנָא נָצַח.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: What is** the substance of **this** stance, by which **you,** the Jewish people, **oppose me?** Since you are my subjects, and my people constitute the majority, you should concede that our lifestyle is superior. **They said to him:** Your dominance is inconsequential, as sometimes even **the Satan is victorious,** by convincing people to act in a manner that is clearly incorrect.

Alexander then pressed: *mah beini Aristom le-kabli* — why do you oppose me? Many *mefarshim* understand this as a challenge to their refusal to accept his religion or acknowledge his supremacy. The sages replied: *stitna natzach* — the Satan has won. As the Soncino notes, this was a double entendre: framed as a concession — "the evil inclination has gotten the better of us" — it actually carried the sharper meaning that the adversarial force, the *stitna*, is currently in ascendancy. Power does not confer righteousness.

### Threat & Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהֹן: הָא אֲנָא מְקַטֵּילְנָא יָתְכוֹן בִּגְזֵירַת מַלְכִין! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: שׇׁלְטָן בְּיָד מַלְכָּא, וְלָא יָאֵי לְמַלְכָּא כָּזָב. מִיָּד אַלְבֵּישׁ יָתְהוֹן לְבוּשִׁין דְּאַרְגְּווֹן, וּשְׁדִי מְנִיכָא דְּדַהֲבָא עַל צַוְּארֵיהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: Consequently,** due to your insolence, **I am executing you by the edict of kings. They said to him:** Certainly, the **authority is in the hand of the king** to execute as you wish; **but falsehood is unbecoming for the king,** and you have pledged not to harm us. **Immediately,** Alexander **dressed them in garments of purple and placed golden chains [*menaykha*] upon their necks.**

Alexander responded by threatening their lives: *hana maktalina yathkon be-gzeirat malkhin* — I will have you put to death by royal decree. The sages replied with calm precision: *shilton be-yad malka* — the king's authority is in his hands, *ve-lo ya'eh le-malka ke-dav* — but it is unbecoming for a king to lie. They invoked whatever prior assurance he had given them that their answers would not endanger them, and held him to it.

### Royal Reward

Alexander reversed himself entirely. *Miyad albishinhu levushin dargavan* — he immediately clothed them in royal purple garments, *ve-shavi manicha de-dahava al tzavreihon* — and placed gold necklaces around their necks. The scene echoes both the honoring of Mordechai — *Mordechai yatza bi-lvush malchut* — and the elevation of Joseph — *ve-ravid ha-zahav al tzavaro*.

### Thematic Analysis

The overarching theme of the entire exchange becomes clear when one considers who Alexander was and what he represented. His tutor was Aristotle. His life's project was the conquest of the entire world — east and west, land and sea. The very questions he asked — heaven or earth, east or west, light or darkness — are the questions of a man whose imagination spans the cosmos. And against all of that, the sages offer a systematic counter-philosophy.

Who is wise? Not the student of Aristotle — but *ha-roeh et ha-nolad*, the one who sees consequences. Who is mighty? Not the conqueror of nations — but *ha-kovesh et yitzro*, the one who masters himself. Who is wealthy? Not the possessor of gold and armies — but *ha-samei'ach be-chelko*, the one satisfied with what he has. How to live? *Yamit atzmo* — self-denial and sacrifice. How to die? *Yechaye et atzmo* — indulge yourself.

And then the question of the sea versus the land comes into focus. It is not merely a geographical curiosity. A man whose desires are boundless will not be content at home — he will want to sail the ocean and claim what lies beyond. But the sages say: you cannot live on the sea. Settle down. Be content. *Eizehu ashir ha-samei'ach be-chelko.*

### Classical Parallels

This entire episode has a remarkable parallel in classical literature. The *Jewish Encyclopedia*, which has an extensive section on Alexander the Great and Talmudic legends, notes that the account of the ten questions — composed in classical Aramaic, which itself suggests it was borrowed from an earlier written record — is closely analogous to an episode described by Plutarch, in which Alexander posed ten questions to ten *Gymnosophists* who had rebelled against him. Several of the questions are identical or closely parallel to those in our *gemara*.

In Plutarch's account, Alexander threatened to put to death whoever gave the worst answer, and to proceed in order through the rest. The questions included: which are more numerous, the living or the dead (answer: the living, since the dead no longer exist); which was older, day or night (answer: day, by one day — to which the sage added that hard questions require strange answers, a remark closely echoing the strategic refusal in our *gemara* to answer about light and darkness); and how could a man best be loved (answer: if he is most powerful yet does not inspire fear — note the contrast with our *gemara*'s answer of *yisne malchut*, which rejects power altogether). The structural parallel to the conclusion is especially striking: in Plutarch, Alexander asks the judge which of them answered worst, and the judge declares them all equally bad — an echo of *kulanu ke-chada shavinan*. Alexander then threatens to kill the judge for the verdict, and is foiled by an argument that he had promised to punish only wrong answers, not all of them.

One significant structural difference: in Plutarch's version each sage gives a different answer, whereas in the Talmudic narrative they answer as one — a point emphasized at the end by *kulanu ke-chada shavinan*, and which may carry a thematic point about unity. Some versions of the story locate the *Gymnosophists* in India and identify them with Brahmin sages, suggesting a further complex history of transmission. But the *Jewish Encyclopedia* notes plainly that this appears to be a fragment of a non-Jewish narrative that has been reworked and absorbed into the Talmudic tradition.

The implicit message of the Jewish reworking is the same as the explicit message of the sages' answers: against Alexander's vaunting ambition and his Aristotelian model of greatness — wisdom as knowledge, strength as conquest, wealth as acquisition — the sages substitute a wholly different value system. The next story, involving the Amazons and the Garden of Eden, continues and deepens precisely that theme.

## Africa & Amazons

### Journey to Africa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהֹן: בָּעֵינָא דְּאֵיזִיל לִמְדִינַת אַפְרִיקֵי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לָא מָצֵית אָזְלַתְּ, דְּפָסְקִי הָרֵי חשֶׁךְ. אָמַר לְהֹן: לָא סַגִּיא דְּלָא אָזֵילְנָא, אַמְּטוּ הָכִי מְשַׁיֵּילְנָא לְכוּ, אֶלָּא מַאי אֶעֱבֵיד?
>
> **Translation:** When Alexander was preparing to part from the Elders of the Negev, **he said to them: I want to go to** wage war against **the country of Africa [*Afriki*];** what do you recommend? **They said to him: You will be unable to go** there, **as the Mountains of Darkness block** the passes. **He said to them:** It is **not possible for me not to go;** and it is **due** to **this** reason that **I ask you** to advise me. **Rather** than refraining from my campaign, **what** might **I do** to cross the Mountains of Darkness?

At the bottom of 32a, Alexander turns from philosophical debate to exploration. *Ba'ina de-azalna le-medinat Afriki* — I want to go to Africa. The sages immediately warn him: *lo matzyat azlat* — you cannot go. *Di-fsikei harei choshech* — the mountains of darkness lie between you and Africa. The geographical imagination of the *gemara* here evokes something out of epic literature: impenetrable mountains of total darkness separating the known world from a hidden continent.

Alexander is unmoved. *Lo sagi de-lo azalna* — it will not suffice not to go. I am going. He is not asking permission; he is asking for practical guidance.

### Crossing Darkness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אַיְיתִי חֲמָרֵי לוּבָאֵי דְּפָרְשִׁי בְּהַבְרָא. וְאַיְיתִי קִיבּוּרֵי דְּמַתְנֵי, וּקְטַר בְּהַאי גִּיסָא, דְּכִי אָתֵית (בְּאוֹרְחָא), נָקְטַתְּ בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ וְאָתֵית לְאַתְרָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: Bring Libyan donkeys that walk** even **in the darkness [*behavra*],** and these animals will guide you through those passes. **And bring coils of rope, and tie** one end of rope **on this** near **side** of the mountains, as you are about to enter there, **so that when you come** to return **by** the same path, **you may take hold of** the ropes left from your initial march, **and,** following them, **you will come back to your place.**

*Aisi chamrei liva'ei le-vahrei* — bring Libyan donkeys, the sages advise, *de-farshi be-chavra* — which can navigate through the darkness (some read: which follow your voice). *Ve-aisi kivorei de-masni* — bring coils of rope; *de-katra be-hai gisa* — tie one end here, so that *ki asis le-asrach* — when you return, *nagdis be-gavaihu* — you can follow the rope back. The Libyan donkeys carry one end of the rope through the mountains, and on the return journey Alexander follows the line back through the darkness. Perhaps the Libyan origin of the donkeys was not incidental — if they are homing animals, like homing pigeons, they will find their way through without needing to be guided.

## Africa & Amazons (II)

### City of Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבַד הָכִי, וַאֲזַל מְטָא לְהָהוּא מָחוֹזָא דְּכוּלֵּיהּ נְשֵׁי. בָּעֵי לְמִיעְבַּד קְרָבָא בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִי קָטְלַתְּ לַן, יֵאמְרוּ: נְשֵׁי קְטַל. אִי (קָטֵילְנָא) [קָטְלִינַן] לָךְ, יֵאמְרוּ: מַלְכָּא דְּקַטְלוּהּ נְשֵׁי. אֲמַר לָהֶן: אַיְיתוֹ לִי נַהֲמָא. אַיְיתוֹ לֵיהּ נַהֲמָא דְּדַהֲבָא אַפָּתוּרָא דְּדַהֲבָא.
>
> **Translation:** Alexander **did this and went** on his campaign. **He came to a certain town whose entire** population was **women,** and **he wanted to wage battle against them.** The women **said to him:** It is not in your interest to fight us. **If you kill us,** people **will say:** Alexander **kills women;** and **if we kill you,** people **will say:** Alexander is the **king whom women killed** in battle. Instead of fighting them, Alexander **said to them: Bring me bread. They brought him bread of gold, upon a table of gold.**

Alexander followed this advice and proceeded. *Ve-azal mato lehu le-machoza de-kulhu neshei* — he arrived at a city entirely populated by women. This is the city of the Amazons. According to various classical myths, the Amazons dwelled in Libya — and the name Libya was used in antiquity to refer to the African continent as a whole, which also accounts for the Libyan donkeys mentioned in the instructions. There are indeed classical myths of Alexander encountering the Amazons.

*Ba'ei le-mevid karva be-hadaihu* — he wanted to wage war against them. The women confronted him with an unanswerable dilemma: *ei katalin lan, yameru neshei katalei* — if you kill us and win, people will say you slaughtered women; *ei katlina lach* — and if we kill you, it is far worse — *yamru malka de-katluhu nashi*, they will say the great king was slain by women. There is no honorable outcome. Alexander recognized the logic and stood down.

### Gold Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִי אָכְלִי אִינָשֵׁי נַהֲמָא דְּדַהֲבָא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֶלָּא אִי נַהֲמָא בָּעֵית, לָא הֲוָה לָךְ בְּאַתְרָךְ נַהֲמָא לְמֵיכַל, דִּשְׁקַלְתְּ וַאֲתֵית לְהָכָא? כִּי נָפֵיק וְאָתֵי, כְּתַב אַבָּבָא דְּמָחוֹזָא: אֲנָא אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרוֹס מוֹקְדוֹן, הֲוֵיתִי שָׁטְיָיא עַד דַּאֲתֵיתִי לִמְדִינָת אַפְרִיקֵי דִּנְשַׁיָּא, וִילֵיפִית עֵצָה מִן נְשַׁיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Alexander **said to** the women: **Do people eat bread of gold? They said to him: But if** all **you wanted** was actual **bread, didn't you have bread to eat in your** own **place?** It was not for bread **that you took** up a campaign and toiled **and came here.** You must have come to increase your wealth. **When** Alexander **left and came** back to his land, he **wrote upon the gate of the town: I, Alexander of Macedon, was a fool until I came to the country of Africa of women, and I learned sense from women.**

*Aisu lei nahama de-dahava a-patura de-dahava* — the women brought him bread made of gold, on a golden table. Alexander protested: *mi achli inshi nahama de-dahava* — who eats golden bread? The women's reply is devastating: *ela ei nahama bayat, lo hava lach be-atrach nahama le-meichal* — if it was simply bread you wanted, you could have eaten bread back home. Why did you travel so far? What did you actually come here looking for?

The *Jewish Encyclopedia* notes that this satire on the ambition of conquerors appears frequently in Jewish legends and has a classical parallel: Plutarch's essay on the virtuous deeds of women describes Pytheas, a wealthy Greek of the time of Xerxes, being served by his wife with golden bread to demonstrate the absurdity of his greed. The moral is identical: *eizehu ashir ha-samei'ach be-chelko*. If what you want is bread to sustain you, you had that at home. The relentless pursuit of gold leads not to satisfaction but to the inability to eat.

### Gate Inscription

When Alexander left the city, *ketiv a-bava de-machoza* — he wrote on the city's gates: *ana Alexandrus Mokdon havaeina shatya* — I, Alexander of Macedon, was a fool, *ad de-asisi li-medinat Afriki de-nashi* — until I came to the land of Africa of women, *ve-yalfit eiza min nashi* — and learned wisdom from women.

This is a striking moment. Through the entire encounter with the ten sages, Alexander never concedes. He pushes back, threatens, counter-argues, and ultimately rewards them while remaining unpersuaded in any deep sense. Here, by contrast, he publicly inscribes his own humiliation on the city gates. The women taught him something the sages, for all their wisdom and wit, apparently could not. Perhaps the sages' exchange was more of an intellectual contest — a battle of wits — while the Amazons made the lesson concrete and unavoidable. One cannot argue with golden bread.

## Garden of Eden

### Fountain Discovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי שָׁקֵיל וְאָתֵי, יָתֵיב אַהָהוּא מַעְיָינָא, קָא אָכֵיל נַהֲמָא. הֲווֹ בִּידֵיהּ גִּלְדָּנֵי דְּמִלְחָא, בַּהֲדֵי דִּמְחַוְּורִי לְהוּ – נְפַל בְּהוּ רֵיחָא. אֲמַר: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַאי עֵינָא מִגַּן עֵדֶן אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to Alexander, the Gemara relates: **When he took** himself **and went** on his way, **he sat at a certain spring** and was **eating bread. He had salted fish [*guldenei*] in his hands,** and **while he cleansed them** of their excessive salt, a particularly pleasant **fragrance fell upon them.** Alexander **said** to himself: I may **conclude from** this event that **this spring comes from** the **Garden of Eden.**

*Ki shakil ve-ati* — as Alexander was traveling through Africa on his various journeys — and he did historically venture into Libya and parts of the Sahara — *yatib a-hahu mayina* — he sat by a well. *Ka-akhal nahama* — he was eating bread. *Havei bideih kavrei de-milkha* — he had some salted fish. *Ba-hadi de-makhvrei lehu* — as he rinsed them in the well water, *nafal behu reikha* — a remarkable scent arose from them. Some versions say *rukha* — that they came back to life, revived.

### Stream to Eden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: שְׁקַל מֵהָנְהוּ מַיָּא, טְרָא בְּאַפֵּיהּ. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: אִידַּלִּי כּוּלֵּיהּ עַד דִּמְטָא לְפִתְחָא דְּגַן עֵדֶן, רְמָא קָלָא: פִּתְחוּ לִי בָּבָא!
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say: He took from those waters** and **washed his face.** And **there are** those **who say: He ascended** along the length of **the entire** spring **until he reached the entrance of the Garden of Eden. He raised** a loud **voice,** calling out: **Open the gate for me!**

*Shema mina hai eina mi-gan eden ati* — from this, Alexander reasoned, this spring must flow from the Garden of Eden. He had found the *makor*, the source, of Eden's waters. One account says he washed his face with the water. Another says *adli kulei* — he followed the entire stream, *ad de-mata le-pitcha de-gan eden* — until he arrived at the very gate of the Garden.

### Eyeball Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ ״זֶה הַשַּׁעַר לַה׳ וְגוֹ׳״. אֲמַר לְהוֹן: אֲנָא נָמֵי מַלְכָּא אֲנָא, מִיחְשָׁב חֲשִׁיבְנָא, הַבוּ לִי מִידֵּי. יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ גּוּלְגֻּלְתָּא חֲדָא, אַתְיַיהּ תַּקְלֵיהּ לְכוּלֵּיהּ דַּהֲבָא וְכַסְפָּא דִּידֵיהּ בַּהֲדַיהּ, לָא הֲוָה (מַתְקָלֵיהּ).
>
> **Translation:** The sentry of the Garden of Eden **said to him: "This is the gate of the Lord;** the righteous shall enter into it" (Psalms 118:20). Since you are not righteous, you may not enter. **He said to them: I too** am worthy, as **I am a king; I am very important.** If I will not be admitted, at least **give me something** from inside. **They gave him one eyeball. He brought it** and **he weighed all the gold and silver that he had against** the eyeball, and yet the riches **did not balance** against the eyeball's greater weight.

*Rama kala: pitchu li bava* — he called out: open the gate for me. The response came immediately: *ze ha-sha'ar la-Hashem, tzadikim yavo'u vo* — this gate is for God, the righteous enter through it. This is the same lesson the ten sages had tried to convey with their answers about wisdom, valor, and wealth. True greatness is righteousness — and Alexander, conqueror of the world, does not qualify.

Alexander did not argue the point of his righteousness. Instead: *ana nami malka ana, chashiv chashivina* — I am at least a king, a person of significance. *Havu li midi* — give me something, anything, from the Garden. *Yahavu leih gulgalta chada* — they gave him one eyeball.

### Eyeball Weighs More

*Taklei le-khulei de-hava ve-kaspa dideih* — he placed all of his gold and silver on one side of the scale, and the eyeball on the other. *Lo hava matkala* — the eyeball outweighed everything. All the wealth of Alexander's conquests could not counterbalance a single human eye.

### Never Satisfied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוֹן לְרַבָּנַן: מַאי הַאי? אָמְרִי גּוּלְגֻּלְתָּא דְּעֵינָא דְּבִישְׂרָא וּדְמָא [הוּא], דְּלָא קָא שָׂבַע. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִמַּאי דְּהָכִי הוּא? שָׁקְלִי קַלִּיל עַפְרָא וְכַסְּיוּהּ, לְאַלְתַּר תְּקַלָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״שְׁאוֹל וַאֲבַדּוֹן לֹא תִשְׁבַּעְנָה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to the Sages: What is this?** Why does this eyeball outweigh everything? **They said: It is the eyeball** of a mortal person **of flesh and blood, which is not satisfied** ever. **He said to them: From where** do you know **that this is** the reason for the unbalanced scale? The Sages answered him: **Take a small amount of dirt and cover** the eye. He did so, and it was **immediately balanced** by its proper counterweight. The eye is never satisfied while it can see, **as it is written: "The netherworld and destruction are never satiated;** so the eyes of man are never satiated" (Proverbs 27:20).

He brought the eyeball to the *rabanan* — understood here as the same Jewish sages he had encountered before — and asked: *mai hai?* What is this? *Gulgalta de-eina de-bisra ve-dama, de-lo kasava* — this is the eyeball of flesh and blood, which is never satisfied. The eye's desire always exceeds what one possesses. You can conquer the entire world and it will not be enough, because desire perpetually outruns acquisition. *Ohev kesef lo yisba kesef* — one who loves money will never have enough.

And then the sages demonstrated: *shakli kalili de-afra ve-chasye* — take a little dirt and cover it. He did so. *Le-altar takla* — immediately it balanced. The eye, covered with earth, no longer outweighed anything. The allusion is immediate: when a person dies, dirt covers the eyes. Only death finally satisfies the eye's desire.

### Sheol Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ: גֵּיהִנָּם לְמַעְלָה מִן הָרָקִיעַ, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים לַאֲחוֹרֵי הָרֵי חשֶׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a statement related to its earlier account of Alexander's journey. **The school of Eliyahu taught: Gehenna is above the heavens, and some say** that it is **beyond the Mountains of Darkness.**

*De-chtiv: she'ol va-avaddon lo tisbe'enah* — as the verse says, Sheol and Abaddon are never satisfied — and the verse concludes, *ve-einei adam lo tisbe'enah*, nor are the eyes of man. The association with *she'ol*, the underworld, drives home the connection between insatiable desire and death.

The *gemara* appends a tradition from the school of Eliyahu: *gehinnom lema'ala min ha-rakia* — Gehinnom is above the heavens. *Ve-yeish omrim: le-achorei harei choshech* — others say it is behind the mountains of darkness — precisely where Alexander traveled. Whether these represent two distinct physical-theological conceptions or two metaphoric types of transgression — the soaring ambition that exceeds the heavens, and the depravity that dwells in the mountains of darkness — is left open. What is clear is that the narrative has come full circle: the mountains of darkness from which Alexander's journey began are now identified as the location of Gehinnom itself.

The sages, in the end, have the last word — though Alexander only openly admitted defeat to the women.

## Torah & Peace

### Night Study

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה בַּלַּיְלָה – שְׁכִינָה כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״קוּמִי רוֹנִּי בַלַּיְלָה לְרֹאשׁ אַשְׁמוּרוֹת שִׁפְכִי כַמַּיִם לִבֵּךְ נֹכַח פְּנֵי ה׳ (וְגוֹ׳)״.
>
> **Translation:** The chapter, as well as the talmudic portion of the tractate, concludes with words of praise for those who study Torah. **Rabbi Ḥiyya taught:** With regard to **anyone who occupies** himself **with Torah at night, the Divine Presence is across from him, as it is stated: "Arise, cry out in the night, at the beginning of the watches; pour out your heart like water before the face of the Lord,** lift up your hands toward Him" (Lamentations 2:19).

With the conclusion of the Alexander narrative, the *gemara* turns to a brief set of teachings that bring *Seder Kodshim* to a close. *Tana Rabbi Chiyya: kol ha-osek ba-Torah ba-laila, shechinah kenegdo* — whoever engages in Torah study at night, the *shechinah* is opposite him. The connection to what preceded may be the motif of *choshech* — darkness — that has run through the entire aggadic section. The verse cited is *kumi roni ba-laila le-rosh ashmurot, shifchi cha-mayim libbech no-ach pnei Hashem* — rise and sing in the night, at the head of the watches, pour out your heart like water before God's presence.

Night study carried particular weight in the ancient world, where artificial light was minimal and the hours after dark were the domain of sleep. To learn at night was to extend oneself beyond natural rhythms in an act of devotion. And why does the *shechinah* attend one who learns at night specifically? Perhaps because night removes the social and communal dimensions of learning — there is no *beit midrash*, no *chavruta* — and one is left alone with the Torah and with God.

### Increase Peace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים מַרְבִּים שָׁלוֹם בָּעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכׇל בָּנַיִךְ לִמּוּדֵי ה׳ וְרַב שְׁלוֹם בָּנָיִךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said: Torah scholars increase peace in the world, as it is stated: "And all your children [*banayikh*] shall be taught of the Lord, and great shall be the peace of your children"** (Isaiah 54:13). This can be read as *bonayikh*, your builders, i.e., scholars build and increase peace for the entire world.

*Amar Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah: talmidei chachamim marbim shalom ba-olam* — disciples of the wise increase peace in the world. The connection here is perhaps to the sages in the Alexander narrative, whose wisdom — even when unheeded by the conqueror himself — represents a countervailing force to war and destruction.

### Peace Verse

The verse cited is *ve-chol banayich limudei Hashem, ve-rav shalom banayich* — all your children are taught of God, and great is the peace of your children. It is worth noting that this citation does not include the well-known homiletic reading *al tikrei banayich ela bonayich* — do not read "your children" but "your builders." That addition, while familiar, is absent here. Perhaps it was appended later; the verse as cited already speaks of those who learn from God, and the connection between Torah study and *shalom* is made without needing the wordplay.

### End of Gemara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ לֹא הָיוּ כּוֹפְתִין
>
> **Translation:**

This is the final *gemara* of *Seder Kodshim*. The tractate of Kinnim, which follows in the standard Vilna *Shas*, consists entirely of *mishnayot* without *gemara*. That means this passage — ending with the value of *talmud Torah* and the peace it brings to the world — is the last piece of *gemara* in the entire order of *Kodshim*. It is characteristic of the *gemara* to conclude a tractate or section with *midrash* and *aggadata* of this kind, and the choice to end here with the pursuit of learning and the increase of peace feels fitting after the long meditation on Alexander's insatiable ambition and the sages' consistent counter-testimony.

*Ha-dran alach hayu kotzim* — with this, the fourth *perek* of Tamid and the *gemara* of *Seder Kodshim* come to a close.

## Temple Service

### One Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** א אָמַר לָהֶם הַמְמוּנֶּה: בָּרְכוּ בְּרָכָה אַחַת! וְהֵם בֵּרְכוּ. קָרְאוּ עֲשֶׂרֶת הַדִּבְּרוֹת, ״שְׁמַע״, ״וְהָיָה אִם שָׁמוֹעַ״, ״וַיֹּאמֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: 5:1 After the priests completed laying the parts of the daily offering on the ramp, they went to the Chamber of Hewn Stone to recite *Shema*. **The appointed** priest who oversaw the lotteries in the Temple **said to** the priests: **Recite a single blessing** of the blessings that accompany *Shema*. **And** the members of the priestly watch **recited a blessing,** and then **they recited the Ten Commandments, *Shema*** (see Deuteronomy 6:4–9), ***VeHaya im Shamoa*** (see Deuteronomy 11:13–21), and ***VaYomer*** (see Numbers 15:37–41), the standard formula of *Shema*.

The *mishnah* continues with the *Avodah* of the morning service. The appointed official — *ha-memuneh*, the overseer of the division of responsibilities — addresses the assembled *kohanim* after the animal has been brought halfway up the ramp: *barchu bracha achat* — make one blessing.

### Shema Recitation

The *kohanim* then proceeded to the *Lishkat ha-Gazit*, the Chamber of Hewn Stone, where they recited *Kriyat Shema*.

### Abbreviated Service

Their *tefillah* was abbreviated, because they needed to return promptly to the *korbanos*. There is extensive discussion in *Berachot* about people whose obligations preclude a full *davvening*, and the *kohanim* exemplify this tension. Specifically, they did not recite the blessing of *Yotzer ha-Me'orot*, because at this point in the morning it was still only *alot ha-shachar* — the first light of dawn — and sunrise (*hanetz ha-chama*) had not yet occurred. The blessing of *Yotzer ha-Me'orot* requires daylight. Accordingly, they said only the blessing of *bocher be-amo Yisrael be-ahava*.

### Ten Commandments

They then read the *aseret ha-dibrot* — the Ten Commandments — before the three *parshiyot* of *Shema*, *Ve-haya im shamoa*, and *Vayomer*. The *gemara* in *Berachot* records that there was initially a movement to incorporate the *aseret ha-dibrot* into the standard daily liturgy for all Jews, precisely because of their centrality and their capacity to encapsulate the *mitzvot*. This was ultimately suppressed out of concern that the *minim* — Jewish sectarians, perhaps including Jewish Christians — would seize upon it as confirmation that the Torah consists solely of the Ten Commandments and nothing more. In the Temple, however, this concern did not apply, and the *aseret ha-dibrot* remained part of the *kohanim*'s service.

### Three Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּרְכוּ אֶת הָעָם שָׁלֹשׁ בְּרָכוֹת: ״אֱמֶת וְיַצִּיב״, וַעֲבוֹדָה וּבִרְכַּת כֹּהֲנִים. וּבַשַּׁבָּת מוֹסִיפִין בְּרָכָה אַחַת לַמִּשְׁמָר הַיּוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, **they blessed with the people three blessings.** These blessings were: **True and Firm,** the blessing of redemption recited after *Shema*; **and** the blessing of the Temple **service,** which is also a blessing recited in the *Amida* prayer; **and the Priestly Benediction,** recited in the form of a prayer, without the lifting of hands that usually accompanies that blessing (*Tosafot*). **And on Shabbat,** when the new priestly watch would begin its service, the priests would **add one blessing** recited **by the outgoing priestly watch,** that love, fraternity, peace, and friendship should exist among the priests of the incoming watch.

Following *Shema*, they recited three blessings. The first was *Emet ve-Yatziv* — the blessing after *Shema*, concluding with *Go'el Yisrael*. The second was *ha-Avodah*, the blessing concerning the Temple service — not the exile-era formulation of *Ha-machazir shechinato le-Tziyyon*, but a blessing in keeping with the active sacrificial service, either a prayer that God accept the sacrifices with favor, or the blessing recited in connection with *birkat kohanim*, ending with *she-otcha levadecha be-yira na'avod*. The third was *birkat kohanim* itself — though in a form distinct from the public *nesiat kapayim*: either the blessing *kohanim* recite before raising their hands, a form of blessing their own role, or a recitation of the biblical verses without the actual spreading of hands over the people.

### Shabbat Blessing

On *Shabbat*, an additional element was added: *u-v'Shabbat mosifim bracha achat le-mishmar ha-yotzei* — the departing *mishmar*, the weekly division of priests completing their service, would add one blessing for the incoming *mishmar*. This blessing was not a standard liturgical *berachah* in the technical sense but a personal blessing of farewell and welcome: *mi she-shachan shemo ba-bayit ha-zeh, hu yitein beineichem ahavah ve-achvah shalom ve-rei'ut* — may He whose name dwells in this house place among you love and brotherhood, peace and friendship. That such a blessing of unity was directed specifically toward the incoming *kohanim* may reflect the natural tensions of rotating communal responsibility and the competition inherent in the lottery system for *korbanos*. The blessing addresses that directly: whatever the outcome of the lots, let there be peace between you.

The relationship between *tefillah* and *korbanos* that emerges from this *mishnah* will be taken up further in the sessions ahead — the *tefillah* framing the *korban*, and the *berachah* consecrating the *avodah*, each illuminating the meaning of the other.
