# Bekhorot 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Estimation & Thought

### Overview & Intro

Today's *daf* is 59, and we pick up at the very top of the page. The subject is the act of counting for *ma'aser behemah*. We have already learned that ideally one uses a staff and paint, processing the animals single-file through an opening; *bedi'avad*, however, the process is valid as long as it is the tenth and there is some form of counting — perhaps even a counting performed entirely in one's head. We will see momentarily how far that latitude extends.

Beyond this, we encountered the even more radical position of *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda*, who holds that the animal need not be the tenth in a sequence at all — it need only represent one-tenth, ten percent. The *Gemara* asks whence he derives this, and the answer is that the Torah's formulation *ha'asiri* — "the tenth" — can be read in connection with *ma'aser* of grain, where the obligation is clearly ten percent rather than the tenth item in a counted sequence. We now pick up with that discussion at the very top of 59a.

### Terumah Link

The *Gemara* had concluded the previous *daf* with the statement: *keshem shehatruma gedola niteles be'omed uv'machshava* — just as *teruma gedola* is taken through estimation and through thought, so too does *trumas ma'aser*. It is worth pausing to appreciate the architecture of this comparison. *Teruma gedola* is the portion of grain separated for the *kohen*; there is no fixed minimum percentage — any small amount qualifies — and the Rabbis provided a rough guideline. *Trumas ma'aser* is what the *Levi'im* separate from their own tithe: ten percent of ten percent, one percent of the original crop, which they in turn give to the *kohanim*. Because *trumas ma'aser* is a precise percentage, one might have assumed it demands rigorous measurement. The *chiddush* of linking it to *teruma gedola* is that even this precise ten percent can be taken through estimation — *be'omed* — and moreover through pure thought — *uv'machshava* — without verbal designation.

### Verbal vs Mental

The *Gemara* on 59a continues: *af trumas ma'aser niteles be'omed uv'machshava* — even *trumas ma'aser*, which is a fixed tenth, may be taken through estimation and through thought. You need not articulate a formal designation; it suffices that you mentally determine that a certain portion constitutes the *ma'aser*. This is a genuine *chiddush*, since with a precise percentage one would expect precision in execution as well.

Now the *Gemara* needs to close the chain from *trumas ma'aser* all the way to ordinary *ma'aser*, since the discussion is ultimately about *ma'aser behemah*. To do so, it notes: *uma'aser karei rachmana truma* — the Torah itself calls *ma'aser* a type of *truma*, as the verse states, *es ma'aser bnei yisrael asher yarimu l'Hashem truma nesativ l'Levi'im l'nachala* — "the tithe of the children of Israel that they will raise up to God as a *truma*, I have given to the *Levi'im* as an inheritance." Since *ma'aser* itself is called a *truma*, the rules of *truma* apply to it: estimation suffices and mental designation is effective. This is a further *chiddush*, because while one might have assumed that *ma'aser* demands precise measurement — there is a *mishna* in *Pirkei Avos* that references the principle of not over-multiplying approximate *ma'aser* estimations — the *Gemara* establishes that estimation and mental designation are nevertheless valid.

### Analogy to Grain

The *Gemara* then completes the argument: *v'eikesh ma'aser behemah l'ma'aser dagan* — *ma'aser behemah* is analogized to *ma'aser dagan*. Just as the grain tithe can be taken through estimation and through thought, so too can *ma'aser behemah*.

At this point it is worth considering precisely what *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda*'s *chiddush* consists of. There appear to be two distinct elements, and it is not entirely clear whether the *Gemara* treats them as equally central. The first element is that one need not verbalize the designation — pure mental intent suffices. The second is that the animal need not be the tenth in a counted sequence; ten percent is sufficient. The analogy to *ma'aser dagan* speaks directly to the second point: by *ma'aser dagan* one does not count to ten, one simply takes any tenth. However, the thrust of the *Gemara*'s discussion, as we will see momentarily, seems to treat the mental-only aspect as a primary *chiddush* in its own right. It is not entirely the same as the issue of *omed*, estimation, which in the context of grain tithe means taking slightly more or slightly less than exactly ten percent without this invalidating the separation. The question for *ma'aser behemah* is rather different: whether counting need occur at all, or whether ten percent can be achieved without a sequence-count. The parallel is thus instructive but not perfectly parallel.

## Auto Sanctification

### Rava's Statement

*Amar Rava*: *asiri me'elav hu kadosh* — the tenth animal becomes sanctified automatically. On its surface, this sounds like a specific articulation of what has been implied: you need not say "I am sanctifying this," because by counting an animal as number ten it becomes *ma'aser* on its own. The *Gemara*, however, understands *Rava* to be making an even more far-reaching claim — that the tenth becomes *ma'aser* even without any counting or designation at all, simply by virtue of objective reality.

This is closely connected to the point raised by *Tosafot* noted previously: when one performs *ma'aser behemah*, one does not actively instill *kedusha* into the animal. Rather, the reality of the animal being the tenth one defines its status. *Rava* is making explicit what that implies in practice.

### Verse Derivation

The *Gemara* asks: *mena lei leRava ha* — what is *Rava*'s source? The first candidate is the *baraita* cited the previous day: *ein li ela she-kara shemo asiri* — I know only that it becomes *ma'aser* when you explicitly call it "the tenth." If you did not call it the tenth, how do I know? The verse says *asiri yihiyeh kodesh* — "the tenth shall be holy" — *mi-kol makom*, regardless of whether it was verbally designated. This implies that even without calling it the tenth, the animal becomes sanctified.

The *Gemara* challenges this derivation: *ve-dilma asiri hu de-la karia kadosh karia* — perhaps it means he did not call it "the tenth" specifically, but he did still say "it is holy." On this reading, the *baraita* does not go as far as *Rava* requires. Perhaps some form of designation is still necessary; only explicit verbal labeling as "the tenth" is dispensable, while a declaration of holiness remains required. This reading preserves a role for designation — either verbal or at minimum some signal of intent.

### Ninth/Tenth Case

The *Gemara* then offers a second candidate for *Rava*'s proof. A *baraita* states: *kara lateshi'i asiri* — you miscounted and called the ninth "the tenth" — *veyatza ha'asiri velo diber* — and the tenth came out and you said nothing. The *halacha* here requires brief background. Both the ninth and the eleventh can acquire a form of *kedushas ma'aser* if miscounted. The ninth, if mistakenly called "the tenth," acquires *kedusha* but cannot be brought as a *korban*; instead, one waits for it to develop a blemish and eats it outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The eleventh, if called "the tenth" when the real tenth has already been designated, acquires full sanctity and can be brought as a *korban* — a distinction we will analyze more fully shortly.

In the case before us: the ninth was mistakenly called the tenth, and the real tenth walked out of the pen without any designation whatsoever. The *baraita*'s ruling is *hateshi'i ne'echal bemumo* — the ninth has *kedushas ma'aser* and is eaten with a blemish — *veha'asiri ma'aser* — and the tenth is *ma'aser*. This appears to vindicate *Rava* precisely: the tenth animal became *ma'aser* despite no designation, solely because it was objectively the tenth.

### Two Interpretations

The *Gemara* challenges this proof as well. *Ve-dilma shani hatam de'ivra lei asiri* — perhaps it is different there because the tenth was already determined. And alternatively, *inami de'achvei lei* — perhaps he did point or signal toward it, performing some non-verbal physical designation. These two answers are quite different in character.

The second answer maintains that some form of designation remains necessary — it just need not be verbal. A physical gesture or signal pointing to the animal suffices. The first answer is more radical in its concession: it effectively grants *Rava*'s principle but argues that the case in the *baraita* is one where the tenth walked out in its proper position, making the reality self-evident, and asks whether *Rava*'s *chiddush* extends even further — to an animal left in the pen rather than having walked out tenth in the line. Notably, the *Gemara* at this stage appears to be operating with the assumption that at minimum some verbal or physical designation is required. This retroactively illuminates *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda*'s position: a major component of his *chiddush* was that purely mental designation suffices, because the main-line *Gemara* discussion seems to assume that verbal or physical designation is the norm.

### Supporting Baraita

The *Gemara* then finds an explicit proof for *Rava*'s position: *ela mehadetanya* — from the following *baraita*. Again the case: *kara lateshi'i asiri* — the ninth was called the tenth — *v'hiniach asiri bedir* — and the tenth remained in the pen without being counted. The ruling: *hateshi'i ne'echal bemumo* — the ninth is eaten with a blemish — *v'ha'asiri ma'aser* — and the tenth is *ma'aser*. Here the *baraita* explicitly states that the tenth, which never walked out, which was never designated — either verbally, physically, or by being counted in sequence — becomes *ma'aser* automatically. Moreover, this happens even in the face of the ninth having been explicitly called *ma'aser*; since the tenth was in objective fact the tenth animal, reality determined its status.

The *Gemara* thus concludes that *Rava*'s position is proven by this *baraita*. The tenth animal is sanctified automatically — *asiri me'elav hu kadosh* — by virtue of being the tenth, without requiring any human act of designation.

## Ninth Sanctity

### Rabbi Shimon's View

Having established the status of the tenth, the *Gemara* turns to the ninth — the animal that was miscounted. We have been assuming throughout that if you call the ninth "the tenth," it automatically acquires *kedushas ma'aser*. But now a *baraita* before *Rav Sheshet* asserts the opposite: the ninth is not sanctified at all. The *Gemara* identifies this as the position of *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon* — that is, *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*.

The *baraita* states: *af al pi she'eino kadosh ela im kein ne'ekar shem asiri heimenu* — the ninth does not become sanctified unless the name "tenth" has been uprooted from number ten. That is, the ninth only acquires *kedusha* if you have also mislabeled number ten.

### Temura Model

To understand *Rabbi Shimon*'s position, one must first understand the *halacha* of number eleven and the model of *temura* that underlies it.

*Temura* is the prohibited attempt to exchange the sanctity of one consecrated animal for another. The Torah forbids this exchange, but rules that if it is done, the second animal also becomes sanctified — and under most circumstances can even be brought as a *korban*. The mechanism is that when one says "this animal shall replace that one," one is attempting to transfer *kedusha* from the original *korban* to the new animal. Despite the prohibition, the *kedusha* takes hold on both.

When one miscounts and calls number eleven "the tenth," there is an element of *temura* in play: number ten already exists, already has *kedusha* by virtue of *Rava*'s principle, and now you are calling eleven by the name that belongs to ten. You are, in effect, attempting to displace ten's *kedusha* and redirect it toward eleven. This is why eleven acquires full sanctity and can be brought as a *korban* — it follows the model of *temura*.

Crucially, however, eleven achieves this elevated status only when you have also mislabeled ten. Specifically, the mechanism requires that when ten walks out you call it nine (or some other number), and when eleven walks out you call it ten. By mislabeling ten, you are actively attempting to "uproot" the sanctity from ten and transfer it to eleven. If you call both ten and eleven by the name "ten," you are not clearly trying to remove ten's sanctity; you might simply be labeling both as ten simultaneously. Only the act of mislabeling ten — *ne'ekar shem asiri heimenu* — constitutes a genuine attempt at transfer, following the *temura* model, and thereby elevates eleven to full *korban* status.

Number nine is different. Nine walked out before ten was counted. There is no ten in the picture yet, no existing *kedusha* to compete with, and no *temura* model to invoke. Nine's sanctity, when it acquires it by being miscalled "the tenth," is thus of a lower grade — it cannot be brought as a *korban*.

*Rabbi Shimon* now argues by analogy: if even eleven, which achieves the full and elevated *kedusha* of a *korban*, requires the uprooting of ten's name in order to attain that status, then nine, which achieves only a lesser *kedusha*, should *a fortiori* require the same. On this reading, the lesser susceptibility to sanctification should mean nine requires the same formal precondition as eleven.

### Gemara Rejects

The *Gemara* rejects this argument cleanly and with finality: *hashta* — quite the opposite reasoning applies. The argument that *Rabbi Shimon* presents actually proves the reverse conclusion.

### Reasoning Explained

The *Gemara* offers two complementary arguments. First: *achad asar shehu oseh kedusha l'hikarev* — eleven achieves the elevated *kedusha* of a *korban*. Because the level of sanctity eleven acquires is so high, it requires a correspondingly demanding condition — you must truly be attempting to transfer the full *kedusha* from ten, which means you must have mislabeled ten. *Im ne'ekar shem asiri heimenu in lo lo* — only if you uproot ten's name does eleven attain that weighty sanctity. But nine, *she'eino oseh kedusha l'hikarev* — which does not attain the *kedusha* sufficient to be brought as a *korban* — requires far less. *Af al gav d'lo ne'ekar shem asiri heimenu* — even without uprooting ten's name, nine becomes sanctified. The lower level of *kedusha* is easier to attain.

The second argument: *achad asar d'havya lei asiri* — by the time eleven walks out, ten already exists as a sanctified entity. Eleven is competing with ten. To achieve sanctification in the face of that competition, you must undercut the competition by mislabeling ten. Nine, by contrast, walks out before ten has been counted: *d'achti lo havya lei asiri* — the tenth has not yet been designated at all. There is no competition, no existing *kedusha* with which nine must contend. It is therefore far easier for nine to become sanctified when miscalled, and it does not require the mislabeling of ten as a precondition.

The *Gemara* concludes: *ve-tu lo midi* — there is nothing more to say. *Rabbi Shimon*'s *kal va-chomer* is not merely unpersuasive; it runs precisely backwards. The very logic he invokes demonstrates why nine should not require the mislabeling of ten.

## Minyan Hara'uy

### Core Principle

*Amar Rava*: *minyan hara'uy poter* — a fit counting exempts the counted animals even if *ma'aser* was never ultimately taken. The principle is this: if animals were counted as part of a process that had the capacity to culminate in *ma'aser* — meaning there were at least ten animals in the pen when counting began — then the animals that passed through the counting process are exempt from *ma'aser*, even if for some reason *ma'aser* was never actually completed. Once animals have been through a legitimate counting process, there is no double jeopardy; they are not counted again.

### Mishna Source

The *Gemara* asks: *mena lei leRava ha* — what is *Rava*'s source? The first attempt is from the *mishna*: *kafatz echad min haminuyin letochan* — if one of the already-counted animals jumped back into the pen, then *kulam peturim* — they are all exempt. The inference seems straightforward: the counted ones are themselves exempt, as the *mishna* states, and since you cannot determine which animal has jumped back, they are all exempt. Is this not exactly *Rava*'s principle — that being counted exempts even without *ma'aser* having been taken?

The *Gemara* deflects: *udilma de'iasurei* — perhaps the *mishna* is discussing a case where one of the animals from whose group *ma'aser* was actually taken jumped back. That is, the pen had thirty animals, ten were counted and number ten walked out as *ma'aser*, and then one of the first nine — from the completed group — jumped back into the remaining twenty. We all agree that if *ma'aser* was actually completed, the first nine are permanently exempt. *Rava*'s *chiddush* concerns animals counted in a process where *ma'aser* was never completed. The *mishna* does not speak to that case.

The *Gemara* then reconsiders the *mishna*'s language. The *mishna* discusses both *min haminuyin* — one of the counted ones jumping back — and *min hameusarim* — one of the *ma'aser*-ed ones jumping back. The *Gemara* initially suggests that these two phrases describe two separate scenarios, thus making the counted-without-*ma'aser* case explicit. But the *Gemara* ultimately rejects this reading on the basis of the *mishna*'s own language: *deika nami*, the inference from context supports reading *meusarim* as referring specifically to the actual *ma'aser* animal. When the *ma'aser* animal jumps back, the ruling is *yiru* — they must all graze until they develop blemishes, since they are all now *safek ma'aser*. This ruling presupposes that the real *ma'aser* animal is back among the group, not merely a member of the counted cohort. Accordingly, the *mishna* cannot serve as proof of *Rava*'s principle.

### Verse Derivation

*Ela amar Rava* — *Rava* then declares: I derive my principle directly from a verse. *Amar kra ya'avor* — the Torah says "shall pass under," *v'lo shekvar avar* — but not that which has already passed. This verse, which established the procedure for *ma'aser behemah*, contains by implication the rule that once an animal has passed through the counting process, it does not pass through again.

Now, one might say this is obvious: of course if *ma'aser* has been taken from a group, you do not tithe the same animals again. But *Rava* argues that if the verse can only be informing us of such an obvious rule, it would not need to be stated at all. The verse must therefore be telling us something that requires actual derivation — *ela lav de'iftaru b'minyan rauy* — that being counted in a fit counting-process exempts even without *ma'aser* having been completed. *Shma mina* — the conclusion stands.

It is worth noting the unusual nature of this source. Generally, *tanna'im* derive laws by direct *derasha* of verses, while *amora'im* interpret the words of *tanna'im*. *Rava* is distinctive in how frequently he engages in direct scriptural derivation, and here he explicitly acknowledges that he has no prior *tannaic* source and instead grounds his principle in his own reading of the *pasuk*.

### Supporting Baraita

The *Gemara* notes that a *baraita* is consistent with *Rava*'s ruling — *tani kevasei d'Rava*. The case: you have ten sheep, put them into a pen, count five of them, and then one of the remaining animals dies. Two scenarios arise. If one of the already-counted five dies, that is of no consequence: when it was counted, the process was a fit one, and the remaining five left in the pen can still be counted to complete *ma'aser*. But if one of the animals left in the pen — one of the uncounted ones — dies, the picture changes: *minuyin peturin* — the five that were already counted are exempt, because when they were counted the process was fit to be completed; *v'she'aino minuyin istarfum l'goren acher* — the four remaining uncounted animals must combine with the next cycle, since they were never part of a completed process and are still subject to *ma'aser*. This is exactly *Rava*'s *chiddush*: the five counted animals are exempt even though *ma'aser* was never actually taken.

## Multiple Doors

### Case One

*Rava* now presents a series of practical cases involving animals that exit through multiple doors simultaneously, complicating the application of *minyan hara'uy*. The *Rambam* explains that in each scenario the owner is counting animals as they pass through each door.

The first case: fourteen sheep are placed in a pen, six exit through one door and four through a second door, leaving four in the pen. Six exit first; at that moment, eight animals remain, so the process could have been completed — this is a *minyan hara'uy*. Then four exit through the other door; at that moment, eight still remain, but this is a separate counting sequence through a separate exit. These four are their own group, and since they total only four, their counting process could not independently be completed.

The four animals left in the pen then determine the outcome. If they exit through the same door as the six, a *ma'aser* is completed: one is taken as *ma'aser*, the remaining counted animals from the six are already exempt, and the four that went through the second door — who were never part of a *minyan hara'uy* — must combine with the next cycle. If the remaining four do not exit through the door of the six (whether they remain in the pen, exit at night when the process lapses, or exit through the door of the other four), then: *shishah peturim* — the six are exempt due to *minyan hara'uy* — but *ve'arba'ah ve'arba'ah mitztarfim legoren acher* — both sets of four are not exempt, and must combine with the next cycle.

### Case Two

The order is reversed: four exit through door one first, then six through door two, with four remaining. When the first four exit, ten animals remain in the pen — the process can be completed; this is a *minyan hara'uy*. When the six then exit, four still remain — again, the process could be completed; this too is a *minyan hara'uy*. Both the four and the six are exempt.

The remaining four then determine whether *ma'aser* is actually completed. If they exit through the same door as the six, ten animals have now passed through that door and *ma'aser* is taken: one animal is designated, and the original first four remain exempt by virtue of *minyan hara'uy*. If the remaining four do not exit through the door of the six — whether staying in the pen or passing through the door of the first four — then *arba'ah veshishah peturim* — both the four and the six are exempt, having each been part of a *minyan hara'uy* — but *ve'arba'ah mitztarfim legoren acher* — the final four, who were never incorporated into a completed process, join the next cycle.

### Case Three

Four exit through door one, four through door two, and six remain. When each group of four exited, ten animals were present — in each case a *minyan hara'uy*. The six remaining animals determine the outcome. If those six exit through a single door — either one — they form a group of ten with the four from that door, and *ma'aser* is taken; the other four are exempt by *minyan hara'uy*. If those six refuse to move and the process lapses — *arba'ah arba'ah peturim* — both sets of four are exempt, having been part of a *minyan hara'uy*; *veshishah mitztarfim legoren acher* — the six, never part of any completed process, join the next cycle.

### Doubtful Counting

The *Gemara* asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what is the *chiddush* of these scenarios? The principle that *minyan hara'uy* exempts was already stated by *Rava*; this is merely straightforward application. *Ve'amar Rava chada zimna* — *Rava* already declared the principle once.

### Theoretical Possibility

The *Gemara* answers: one might have thought that *minyan hara'uy* applies only when the completion of the counting process was certain — *vadai minyan hara'uy amrinan* — but that when it was only possible, *safek minyan hara'uy*, because the animals could have gone either way — *dechazi lehachi vedchazi lehachi* — we do not apply the principle. *Kamashma lan* — the cases teach us otherwise.

*Rashi* explains this with the scenario of the second set of four in the four-four-six case. When those four exit through their door, it is not guaranteed that the remaining six will also pass through that same door. Those six might go through the other door, in which case the second four would never become part of ten. The *minyan* was thus only theoretically possible, not certain. One might have said that such a doubtful *minyan hara'uy* is insufficient. The cases teach that *minyan hara'uy* is effective even when completion was merely theoretically possible, not definitively anticipated.

There is an element here reminiscent of the *yesh breira* / *ein breira* discussion: retroactively, were those four animals part of the relevant process or not? The cases establish that theoretical possibility at the time of counting is sufficient to confer exempt status.

## No Selective

### Fifteen Sheep

*Amar Rava*: if one has fifteen sheep, *lo yomar* — one may not say — "I will tithe ten of them, bring them into the pen, take *ma'aser* from one of them, and exempt the rest, leaving the remaining five entirely aside." This approach is prohibited.

### Proper Procedure

*Ela konsam ledir* — one must put all fifteen into the pen. The rationale is that all of one's animals must be part of the process; one cannot selectively designate only some for *ma'aser* while deliberately leaving others out. *Umotzi asara* — ten then exit — *venotel mehem echad* — one is taken as *ma'aser* — *vehash'ar mitztarfin legoren acher* — and the remaining animals combine with the next cycle.

### Two Innovations

*Rava*'s ruling here contains two distinct *chidushim*. The first is the procedural point itself: all animals must be part of the counting process; one cannot cherry-pick ten and ignore the remaining five. The second is a ruling about the five that are left over. One might have thought that the five not ultimately counted in the current cycle are entirely exempt from the next cycle as well — since they were in some sense associated with the current process, even if left to the side. *Rava* rejects this: as long as *Rosh Hashanah* has not passed, those five that were excluded from the current counting are not exempt. They must combine with whatever animals are born or acquired before the year's end and be counted in the next cycle. The two errors being rejected, then, are: leaving animals out of the current process, and imagining that animals left out are thereby exempt from future obligation.
