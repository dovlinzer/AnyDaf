# Bava Batra 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Toein Vechozer & Migo

### Intro: Toein & Migo

The sugya we now enter touches on two interrelated concepts that may at first appear to be entirely separate discussions but are, in fact, deeply connected: *toein vechozer vetoein* — the question of whether a litigant may revise his claim in the course of litigation — and *migo* — the principle by which a claimant is believed because he could have advanced a stronger or more advantageous claim but chose not to. These two concepts are, in a meaningful sense, the theoretical flip side of one another.

### Migo Definition

*Migo* asks: could the claimant have said something else, something better for his case, and he didn't — and therefore we believe what he actually did say? *Toein vechozer vetoein* asks: did the claimant say something else, and is he now changing what he actually said? The first confers credibility because of restraint; the second raises the question of whether revision undermines credibility. Understanding the tension between them sets the stage for the cases that follow.

---

## David's Field Case

### David's Claim

The *gemara* opens with the formula *zeh omer shelo avotai vezeh omer shelo avotai* — this one says it is his ancestral plot and that one says it is his. In the scenario under discussion, David claims that the field in question has been his ancestral plot — *shel avotai* — and brings witnesses to establish that his father, grandfather, or forebears had been in possession of the land, thereby establishing the ancestral lineage of ownership. The other party — call him Dov — does not have direct witnesses to back up his parallel claim of ancestral ownership. What he does have is testimony that he has occupied and worked the field for the requisite three years of *chazakah*.

### Rava's Migo

Rava introduces the *migo* argument on Dov's behalf. The reasoning runs as follows: if Dov were inclined to lie — and were shrewd about it — he would never have competed with David on the question of whose ancestors owned the field. He would instead have said: "I bought it from you, David, and here are my *edei chazakah* — witnesses to my three years of possession." That claim, combining a purchase claim with *chazakah* testimony, would have neutralized David's ancestral witnesses entirely and won the case outright. The very fact that Dov did not say this, and instead put forward the weaker claim of ancestral ownership, itself testifies to his sincerity. As Rava frames it: *mah lo leshaker* — *ee ba'ei amina leh minach zevintai deachalteih shenei chazakah* — what incentive does Dov have to lie? He could have said, "I bought it from you, David, and I have the *chazakah* years to prove it." Since he did not say that, he should be believed in what he did say.

### Abaye Challenges

Abaye pushes back directly. *Mah lo leshaker bimkom edim lo amrinan* — the principle of *migo*, or *mah lo leshaker*, does not operate in the presence of direct witness testimony. The problem is that Dov is asking the court to accept his claim over the affirmative testimony of David's witnesses. Witnesses carry the highest formal weight in halachic adjudication. The existence of the institution of *edim zomemim* acknowledges theoretically that witnesses can lie, but as a practical matter, witnesses are treated as iron-clad evidence. A *migo* argument — the notion that I could have said something better — cannot override the direct testimony of *edim*. Dov's claim of ancestral ownership therefore cannot stand against David's witnesses.

### Revised Strategy

At this point, having heard the exchange between Abaye and Rava — specifically Abaye's observation that had Dov claimed purchase combined with *chazakah* witnesses, he would have won — Dov pivots. He announces: *in, davatech hi vezavinteh minach* — "You are right, David, it was indeed your father's field, and I bought it from you." And he produces his *chazakah* witnesses accordingly. He now reconceptualizes his earlier claim: when he said the field was *shelo avotai*, he did not mean it literally as an ancestral plot that had always been in his family. Rather, *desmich li aleh kedavatei* — he felt so settled and confident in his possession of the field that he spoke of it as though it were ancestral. He was not being strictly literal.

### Revision Problem

This maneuver immediately raises the central problem: is this legitimate? This looks very much like *toein vechozer vetoein* — making one claim and then turning around and making a different claim. Even granting that Dov can produce witnesses to support his revised story, has he forfeited the right to advance that story by virtue of his initial inconsistent claim? The *gemara* now turns to that question directly.

## Toein Vechozer Rules

### Ulla vs Nahardei

The *gemara* frames the debate between Ulla and the Sages of Nahardea. *Toein vechozer vetoein o ein toein vechozer vetoein?* — can a litigant make a claim and then revise it, or can he not? Ulla holds *toein vechozer vetoein* — revision is permitted, at least in the range of cases that fall short of outright contradiction. The Sages of Nahardea hold *eino toein vechozer vetoein* — when the revision seems to involve a significant contradiction with the original claim, it is not accepted. As is often the case with such disputes, however, neither position is absolute; each side concedes a range of cases to the other.

### Ulla Concedes

Ulla, who is generally permissive about revision, nonetheless concedes a category where even he would reject a revised claim. *Heicha de'amar lei shel avotai velo shel avotecha* — if the litigant said explicitly, "It is my forefather's field and not your forefather's," and now seeks to retract that denial, Ulla agrees that this constitutes a direct contradiction that cannot be reinterpreted away. *Ve'eino toein vechozer vetoien.* Once you have said not only what your claim is but explicitly denied the opposing claim, no subsequent revision will be entertained.

### Revision After Break

Ulla concedes a second category as well. Even where the revision does not involve a direct verbal contradiction of the opposing claim, if the litigant was present in *beis din*, did not advance the revised claim at that time, left, and then returned with the revision, it will not be accepted: *ve'azal me'avrai*, *amar lei ta'an* — he went outside and then came back with a new formulation — *eino chozer vetoein*. The reason is *tanyei agmarei* — he was taught. The gap in the proceedings creates a reasonable suspicion that the litigant received coaching during the break. If, for example, Dov overheard Abaye and Rava discussing the case and learned from their exchange precisely how to reframe his claim, then his subsequent revision — even if it falls short of outright contradiction — looks less like a sincere clarification and more like strategic instruction. Even Ulla, who permits revision, insists that there be at least the possibility of genuine sincerity behind any reinterpretation. Once the appearance of coaching intrudes, that possibility is foreclosed.

### Nahardei Concedes

Nahardea, for its part, also concedes a category of revision that is accepted. *Heicha de'amar lei shel avotai shelakchuha me'avotecha dechozer vetoien* — if the litigant's revised claim is essentially: "When I said it was my father's plot, I meant that my father purchased it from your father," that is a perfectly reasonable reinterpretation rather than a genuine contradiction. Both statements can be simultaneously true. The field was indeed the other party's ancestral land — and it is also my father's land, because my father purchased it from your father. The *chazakah* years further corroborate that version of events. This type of reinterpretation, the Nahardeans concede, is accepted even under the stricter view.

### Tosafos Resolution

Tosafos presses a step further and questions why the *gemara* needed to formulate this as a concession by Nahardea at all. When one party says *shel avotai* and the other says *shel avotai*, who said those two claims are inherently contradictory? There is no logical reason why both cannot be true simultaneously — the field could have passed from one family to another through purchase. The word *avotai* in its literal sense means simply "my father's" or "my ancestors'," and there is nothing in that phrase alone that excludes the possibility that one father purchased from the other father. It is only because we naturally read *avotai* to carry the connotation of an ancestral plot — land that has always been in the family, never alienated — that the two claims seem to conflict. Tosafos resolves that when a litigant insists he meant *avotai* in the more literal, less loaded sense — my father was the owner, not that it was immemorial family property — this is not so radical a revision as to constitute *toein vechozer vetoien* even under Ulla's framework. It is a clarification of what was said, not a contradiction of it.

### Outside vs Inside

The *gemara* then establishes a category on which all opinions agree. *V'heicha d'ashni milei me'ikara v'lo ta'ein* — if outside of court both parties made their competing claims in the way they had been framing them — "my father's" versus "my father's" — and then the litigant arrived in *beis din* and made a different, more specific, and strategically superior claim — *v'ata l'vei dina v'ta'an* — everyone agrees this is not a problem of *toein vechozer vetoien*, and the revised claim in court is accepted. *Avid inish d'la megali ta'anteih ela l'vei dina* — it is entirely normal behavior for a person not to reveal his real legal strategy outside of court. A litigant may speak in general terms in informal settings, even strategically, and reserve the precise formulation of his claim for the formal proceeding. There is no obligation to be consistent between what one says informally outside of court and what one pleads before the *dayanim*. The concern with *toein vechozer vetoien* arises only with respect to official claims made inside *beis din* itself. Even Nahardea agrees to this. The *halacha* is ultimately ruled in accordance with Ameimar, who, though himself a Nahardean, nonetheless concluded: *v'hilchasa toein vechozer vetoien* — a litigant may revise even a significant reinterpretation of his claim, provided it stops short of outright contradiction and there is no reason to suspect external coaching.

## Modern Law Parallel

### David's View

This raises a natural comparison to contemporary litigation practice. In secular courts, a changed story is not automatically rejected; rather, the facts and circumstances surrounding the change are presented to the finder of fact — the jury or the judge — and the change itself becomes evidence to be weighed. The opposing counsel has the opportunity to examine the witness about the inconsistency, challenging the litigant directly: why is it that you are saying this now when before you said something different? The trier of fact then decides which version of events, if either, to credit. The change in position is not per se disqualifying, but it is certainly ammunition for the adversary.

### Gloria's View

There is, however, a more formal constraint that operates in a different way. In early stages of litigation, parties are obligated to provide facts as they understand them — and once those facts are set down on papers, submitted formally, it becomes very difficult to retract them, because in a meaningful sense the litigant has effectively testified to those facts. A motion filed on the papers cannot simply ignore or contradict what was previously submitted. At that point there is a genuine parallel to *toein vechozer vetoien*.

### Pleading Parallel

The parallel becomes especially clear in a specific procedural context: after a complaint has been filed and the opposing party asks for the factual basis of that complaint, the defendant may move for dismissal on the basis of the facts as stated by the plaintiff. The plaintiff cannot then simply say, "Oh, now I'll say something different." There is a point at which the window for revision has closed — and that, as the *gemara* frames it, is precisely the concern of *toein vechozer vetoien*.

## Gloria-Dov Case

### Case Setup

The *gemara* now introduces a new case. Both Dov and Gloria claim that the field in question is their ancestral plot — *zeh omer shel avotai vezeh omer shel avotai*. But this time, the evidentiary picture is more elaborate. Dov brings witnesses who testify both that the field is his ancestral plot and that he has occupied and eaten its produce for the three years of *chazakah*: *aiti sahedi de-avotai ve-achlah shenei chazakah*. Gloria, in turn, brings witnesses who directly contradict the *chazakah* component: *ve-aiti sahedi de-achlah shenei chazakah* — her witnesses say that it is she who has been eating the produce these three years. We therefore have two sets of witnesses in direct conflict with one another on the question of who possessed the field, and the question becomes what to do with Dov's witnesses' testimony about the ancestral nature of the plot, which has not been directly contradicted.

### Rav Nachman

Rav Nachman rules: *okei achilah le-bahadi achilah ve-okei ara be-chezkat avosa* — we place the contradictory *chazakah* testimony against the contradictory *chazakah* testimony, they neutralize one another, and we are left with Dov's uncontradicted ancestral testimony standing. That testimony awards the field to Dov.

### Rava Objects

Rava objects sharply. *Hai edut mukcheshet hi* — the testimony as a whole has been contradicted. When one set of witnesses testifies to two things — ancestral plot and *chazakah* years — and half of that testimony is directly contradicted, the entire testimony must be discarded. We cannot selectively retain the unchallenged portion while setting aside the portion that was contradicted. Moreover, there is a deeper problem: we know that one of these two groups of witnesses is lying. That casts a cloud of dishonesty over both groups. Since Dov's witnesses may be the liars, their testimony about the ancestral plot cannot be credited simply because the other side chose not to contest it directly.

### Nachman Responds

Rav Nachman's response addresses both dimensions of Rava's objection. On the question of the testimony itself — *nehi de-itkachash be-achilata, be-avota mi itkachash?* — yes, they were contradicted about the *chazakah* years, but no one contradicted their testimony about the ancestral ownership. This is a form of *palginan dibura* — we can accept one part of a witness's testimony while setting aside another part. On the question of witness integrity: each group of witnesses retains its *chezkat kashrut* — its presumption of being honest witnesses — because we cannot identify which group is lying. We know one group is dishonest, but since we cannot point the finger at either group specifically, we do not strip either of their presumptive credibility. In the areas where they have not been contradicted, each group is presumed to be testifying truthfully.

## Witnesses Debate

### Setup Scenario

The *gemara* now situates this dispute within a broader *amoraic* debate. The question of how to treat two pairs of witnesses who directly contradict one another — *bei trei uvei trei demachashi ahadadi* — is the subject of an explicit disagreement between Rav Huna and Rav Chisda. It is important to note that this is a different scenario from *edim zomemim*. In the case of *edim zomemim*, the second group does not dispute what happened; they establish that the first group could not have been present to see what they claimed to see. Here, both groups claim to have witnessed the relevant events, and each contradicts the other's account of what occurred.

### Rav Huna Position

Rav Huna rules: *zo ba'ah bifnei atzmah ume'idah vezo ba'ah bifnei atzmah ume'idah* — each group of witnesses retains its presumptive validity. Although for the purposes of the present case we cannot accept either group's testimony — since they directly contradict one another and we cannot adjudicate between them — neither group is stripped of its *chezkat kashrut*. In any future, unrelated proceeding, either group may come before the court and testify, and we will presume them to be reliable.

### Mixed Pair Rejection

There is, however, one critical restriction even within Rav Huna's framework. If the two contradicting groups were, say, Reuven-and-Shimon on one side and Levi-and-Yehuda on the other, then going forward each group may testify separately in other cases. But if Shimon and Levi — one member from each original group — come together as a pair of witnesses in a future case, they cannot be accepted. We know for certain that at least one of these two individuals testified falsely in the prior proceeding. A *kat* of witnesses that includes a definite *ed sheker* cannot function as valid testimony, even if we cannot identify with certainty which of the two it is.

### Rav Chisda Position

Rav Chisda takes a stricter position: *bahadi sahadi shikrei lama li* — why should I have any truck with witnesses whose honesty has been called into question? The cloud of suspicion that hangs over each group cannot simply be ignored on the grounds that we lack the ability to identify who specifically is lying. A court cannot accept witnesses whose credibility has been formally thrown into doubt. Therefore, not only for the immediate case but going forward as well, neither group may testify before the court.

### Connection

The connection to the Gloria-Dov dispute is now made explicit. Rav Nachman, who was willing to accept Dov's witnesses' testimony on the ancestral plot even after they were contradicted on the *chazakah* question, follows Rav Huna's logic: each part of the testimony can be evaluated independently, and the witnesses retain their presumptive credibility where they have not been contradicted. Rava, who rejected the testimony wholesale once contradiction was established, follows Rav Chisda: once the question has been raised, the court cannot proceed to credit anything those witnesses said.

The *gemara* then adds an important qualification. The question of whether Rav Nachman and Rava are simply applying Rav Huna and Rav Chisda respectively requires a closer look. According to Rav Chisda, of course Rav Nachman's approach fails — if the witnesses cannot testify at all going forward, they certainly cannot be credited in the same case on the ancestral question. But the real debate must be situated within the framework of Rav Huna, who does permit the witnesses to testify separately. Even according to Rav Huna, *ad kan lo ka'amar Rav Huna ela b'edut acheret* — when did Rav Huna say the witnesses retain their validity? Only for a different matter, *edut acheret*. *Aval l'oto edut lo!* — but for this same testimony, even Rav Huna would not extend that presumption. Once the contradiction has arisen in the context of testimony supporting Dov's claim to this field, any part of that testimony — whether about *chazakah* or about ancestral ownership — is tainted for purposes of this dispute. This could be either because testimony functions as a single unit and partial contradiction disqualifies the whole (*lo palginan dibura*), or because within the specific context of this litigation, the witnesses' credibility in establishing Dov's claim is compromised. Rava therefore maintains that even within Rav Huna's framework, the ancestral testimony cannot stand in the same proceeding where the contradiction arose.

## Reversal Dilemma

### Initial Award

Working within Rav Nachman's ruling, the field is awarded to Dov on the strength of his uncontradicted ancestral witnesses. Rava's position was overruled and Dov descended into the field — *nacheitnan* — meaning the court conferred possession upon him.

### Gloria's Evidence

After the judgment had been rendered and Dov had taken possession, Gloria produced new witnesses who directly contradicted Dov's ancestral testimony — *hadar aytei sahadai d'vasteihi*. Now both sides have contradictory witnesses on the ancestral claim as well, and the entire evidential picture collapses into mutual neutralization.

### Reversal Question

Rav Nachman declares: *ana nacheitnan ana maskinan* — we brought Dov down to the field, and we will take him back up and out of it. We will reverse our prior ruling. And, he adds: *v'zilusa d'bei dina lo chaishinan* — we are not concerned about the ill repute of the court. The court's obligation is to *emet* — to truth — not to the appearance of consistency. If new evidence has emerged that undermines the basis for the prior ruling, the ruling must be reversed, even if observers will say the court does not know what it is doing.

This raises a larger principle worth pausing over. The question of whether a *beis din* should reverse itself in light of new evidence arises constantly. In secular litigation, new evidence — even very powerful new evidence — is generally not by itself sufficient grounds for appeal; procedural error must typically be shown. The halachic framework, by contrast, makes the question of *ziluta d'bei dina* a live and serious one. Rav Nachman is fully aware of the reputational cost of reversal — especially in a case like this one, where the new ruling does not replace a definite judgment in favor of Dov with a definite judgment in favor of Gloria. It replaces a clear *psak* with a situation of *kol d'alim gvar* — essentially, the Rashbam notes, a throwing up of the hands. When a court's reversal leads not to a new definite ruling but to a legal vacuum, the *ziluta d'bei dina* is especially pronounced: it signals not merely that the court reached the wrong result the first time, but that the court cannot reach any result at all. The argument for leaving the prior judgment in place is correspondingly stronger, since Dov may well still be the rightful owner — we simply do not know — and the alternative is not clarity but confusion.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava — or, as some say, Rav Zeira — challenges Rav Nachman's willingness to reverse with a parallel case. *Ha'im be'emet ein anu chosheshin l'ziluta d'bei dina?* — is it truly the case that we are unconcerned about the ill repute of the court? He cites the case of contradictory witnesses regarding a woman's personal status.

### Woman's Marriage

The scenario: two witnesses testify that a woman's husband has died, and two witnesses testify that he has not died; or similarly, two say she was divorced and two say she was not. *Harei zu lo tinase* — she may not initially remarry, since the contradictions leave her status in doubt and her presumptive status as a married woman (*chezkat eshet ish*) remains operative. *Ve'im niset lo tetze* — but if she went ahead and married, the contradictory witnesses having neutralized one another and the court having in some sense permitted the situation to remain ambiguous, she need not be removed from the new marriage.

### Menachem's Distinction

Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yosi introduces a critical distinction: *eimatai ani omer tetze?* — when do I say she must leave the second marriage? Only *bizman she-bau edim ve-achar kach niset* — when the contradictory witnesses arrived first, the court warned her that she was not permitted to remarry, and she disregarded that ruling and married anyway. In that case she acted in direct defiance of the court and the marriage does not stand. But if the sequence was reversed — if she first received a valid ruling permitting her to marry, she married in full conformity with that ruling, and only afterward did contradictory witnesses arrive — then *harei zu lo tetze*: she does not leave. The parallel to Dov's situation is clear. A valid *psak* was issued; Dov took possession in reliance on it; new contradictory witnesses have now arrived. Just as the woman who married under a valid court ruling need not leave her marriage when contradictory evidence later emerges, so too — the argument goes — the field should remain with Dov.

It is also worth noting, as Tosafos raises, why the woman's actually having gotten married should be necessary — why shouldn't the court's ruling alone have been sufficient? That question is left for another occasion.

## Nachman Concedes

### Public Concession

Rava presses his point: this case of contradictory witnesses to the husband's death is directly analogous to the field case. In both situations, a clear *psak* was issued, the party relied on it, and contradictory witnesses arrived afterward. If we do not compel the woman to leave her new marriage out of concern for *ziluta d'bei dina* — essentially preserving the prior ruling in the face of subsequent uncertainty — then the same logic should apply to Dov's field. Rav Nachman had also been challenged on a parallel basis by Rav Hamnuna in Surya. Faced with both challenges, Rav Nachman concedes: *lo avidina ba'uvda* — I will not reverse the judgment. He announces publicly that the field will remain with Dov.

### Unexpected Action

The *gemara* then records something striking. After making this public announcement, Rav Nachman went out and reversed the judgment anyway — *nafik avad uvda* — taking the field away from Dov in light of the newly arrived contradictory witnesses. An observer who saw this, unaware of Rav Nachman's reasoning, would naturally have concluded *savar taus hu biyadei* — that Rav Nachman had simply forgotten about the challenge that had been raised and acted in error. *Ve'lo hi* — but that is not what happened. *Ela bishum de'talya ba'aflei ravrevei* — rather, he had found support from great sources, like being anchored to mighty oaks, to sustain his original position that a *beis din* may and should reverse itself in the face of new contradictory evidence, notwithstanding the concern for *ziluta d'bei dina*.

### Teaser

What exactly were the sources or arguments that provided Rav Nachman with this renewed confidence? That will be the subject of the *gemara*'s continuation. The daf closes on this open note, with Rav Nachman having publicly conceded, privately reversed, and the reasoning behind that reversal yet to be revealed.
