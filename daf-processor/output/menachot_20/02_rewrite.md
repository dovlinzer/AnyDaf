# Menachot 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav's Principle

### Intro to Rav

The sugya under discussion concerns whether a *pasuk* is repeated in *Tzav*, given that it first appears in *Vayikra*. Rav's general principle is that if a law is repeated, it is *me'akev* — essential and disqualifying if omitted — and if it is not repeated, it is not *me'akev*. Today's daf, daf 20, picks up from 19b, two lines from the bottom, and we now examine whether this principle truly holds.

The previous session addressed *hagashah*, which is not *me'akev* despite being repeated — with the resolution that the repetition serves to specify precisely where the *hagashah* is performed.

### RavHuna's Challenge

The Gemara presents a challenge from Rav Huna: *harei melach* — what about salt? When the Mishnah taught that salt is not *me'akev*, this came as a shock, given that the Torah states *lo tashbit melach berit elokecha me'al minchatecha* — do not allow the salt of your God's covenant to be absent from your *mincha*. The Gemara now interrogates this specifically in light of Rav's principle.

### Salt's Absence in Tzav

The law of salt appears in *Vayikra* but not in *Tzav*, which runs against the general pattern underlying Rav's principle. The standard framework is that *Tzav*, which opens with *Tzav et Aharon*, represents a repetition of *Vayikra* from the perspective of the *kohanim*, while *Vayikra*, which opens with *Daber el bnei yisrael lemor*, addresses the one who brings the *korban*. Yet the entire requirement to salt all *korbanot* appears in *Vayikra* and not in *Tzav*, which does not fit neatly into that pattern.

Be that as it may, the Gemara's question stands: *delo tana bei kra* — the Torah did not repeat the salt law — and yet salt is *me'akev*. This is already a striking point: the Gemara is operating against the plain reading of the Mishnah, which says salt is not *me'akev*.

### Assumption vs Rav

The Gemara begins by assuming that salt is *me'akev*, citing a *baraita*, and it is apparent that the Gemara is intuitively persuaded by this position — presumably because the Torah calls salt a *brit olam*, an eternal covenant. The Gemara then turns this around and presses Rav: you said that a law must be repeated to be *me'akev*, yet salt is not repeated. How does Rav account for this?

Tosafot already flags the underlying assumption here. When Rav articulated his principle that repetition signals *me'akev* status within the laws of *menachot*, the Gemara initially understood him to mean that repetition is the *only* means by which something becomes *me'akev* in this domain. Yet we know from elsewhere that *me'akev* status can derive from other formulations — *chok*, *Torah*, *brit*, and similar expressions. Tosafot explains that the Gemara understood Rav's statement narrowly and specifically in the context of *mincha*, such that within that domain repetition was thought to be the exclusive criterion. The Gemara is about to revise that assumption.

---

## Defending Rav

### First Defense

Rav Yosef offers the first defense: *Rav ketanna didan sevira lei* — Rav holds in accordance with our Mishnah. The Mishnah itself states *lo malach kesheira* — if one did not perform *melicha*, the offering is valid. If so, Rav need not be troubled: even though the Torah calls salt a *brit olam*, it is simply not *me'akev*, consistent with the plain meaning of the Mishnah.

### Abaye's Objection

Abaye pushes back sharply. He argues that one cannot read the Mishnah so straightforwardly. Consider the Mishnah's parallel statement about *yetzika* — if one did not pour the oil, the offering is valid. The Gemara has already established, even without extensive proof texts, that *yetzika* is in fact *me'akev*. To reconcile this, the Gemara reinterpreted *lo yetzak* not to mean that *yetzika* was omitted entirely, but that the pouring was performed by a *zar* — a non-*kohen* — rather than a *kohen*. That is: the Mishnah permits the offering when a non-*kohen* performed the pouring, not when the pouring was skipped altogether.

If that is how we read *lo yetzak* in the Mishnah, Abaye argues, then we should read *lo malach* the same way: not that the *melicha* was omitted, but that a non-*kohen* performed it. The Mishnah would then teach that if a non-*kohen* salted the *kometz*, the offering is still valid — while *melicha* itself remains *me'akev*. This preserves the *brit olam* language without contradicting the Mishnah, and simultaneously undermines Rav Yosef's defense of Rav.

### Non-Kohen Problem

The Gemara objects to this reinterpretation: *ve-chi ta'aleh al da'atcha she-zar kareiv l-gabei mizbeach?* Can one really entertain the notion that a non-*kohen* could approach the *mizbeach*? The *melicha* is performed on the *kometz* immediately before it is burned, meaning one would have to be at the top of the altar. The very idea seems untenable.

The *Shita Mekubetzet* raises the obvious difficulty: even granting that it would be highly improper for a non-*kohen* to ascend the *mizbeach*, that impropriety does not by itself determine whether the offering is valid after the fact. He resolves the difficulty by pointing to the principle established earlier: *mi-kmitza ve-eilach mitzvat kehuna* — from the *kemitza* onward, the service belongs exclusively to the *kohanim*. The Gemara's objection is therefore not merely about decorum or the affront of physical proximity to the *mizbeach*, but about the halakhic impossibility of a post-*kemitza* action being performed by a non-*kohen*. That category has never been opened, even theoretically. While this may not be the most straightforward reading of the Gemara's language, the broader point — that a non-*kohen* performing the *melicha* at this stage is genuinely inconceivable within the halakhic framework — is what forces the Gemara's hand.

### Accept Pshat

The Gemara is compelled to accept the *pshat* of the Mishnah: if one did not perform *melicha*, the offering is valid. This squares with Rav's principle. Yet the Gemara is equally compelled to account for the phrase *brit olam*. It therefore concludes: *ve-iba'it eima keivan di-chtiva beih brit k-man di-tna beih kra dami* — since the *pasuk* uses the word *brit*, it is as though the law were explicitly repeated. The word *brit*, like the words *chok* or *yihiye*, carries the same force as an explicit repetition.

What is striking about this Gemara is the trajectory it traces. It begins by virtually taking for granted that *melicha* is *me'akev* — presumably because of the *brit olam* language — and is reluctant to read the Mishnah according to its plain meaning. Only after the various attempts to reinterpret the Mishnah fail does the Gemara accept the *pshat*. And then it concludes that either salt is not *me'akev* at all, consistent with the Mishnah, or, alternatively, even Rav would acknowledge that *brit* functions as a form of repetition. The Gemara's strong intuition that salt should be *me'akev*, driven by the *pasuk*, is ultimately what shapes the resolution.

What the Gemara has clarified along the way is important: when Rav said that a law must be repeated to be *me'akev*, he did not mean that repetition is the only pathway. *Brit*, *chok*, and similar formulations serve the same function. The Gemara had initially — and incorrectly — read Rav's principle as exhaustive within the domain of *menachot*.

## Salt Repetition

### Where's Repetition?

The Gemara now reconsiders the factual premise of the earlier challenge. The question was: *v'lo tana bei kra* — did the Torah not repeat itself regarding salt? But in fact, is that true? The *pasuk* states *v'chol korban minchatcha b'melach timlach* — every offering of your *mincha* you shall salt with salt. Has salt not been stated more than once?

Until now, repetition has been understood to mean a law stated once in *Vayikra* and again in *Tzav*. Here, however, the text does not appear in *Tzav*. So one might instead argue that the repetition is internal to the *Vayikra* passage itself: the verse *v'chol korban minchatcha b'melach timlach* and the verse *brit melach olam* mentioned at the end of *Korach* could together constitute a repetition.

However, this presents its own difficulty. The phrase *brit melach olam* in *Korach* appears at the conclusion of the discussion of priestly gifts in Bamidbar, where the Torah uses the covenant of salt as a symbol for the eternal promise to the *kohanim*. It states: *kol trumas hakodashim asher yarimu bnei yisrael l'Hashem nasati lcha u'lvanecha u'lvnotecha itcha l'chok olam, bris melach olam hi lifnei Hashem lcha u'lzarcha itcha.* The passage lists everything the *kohen* receives and characterizes it as a *brit melach olam* before God. The *brit* of salt here is invoked as the paradigm for an enduring covenant, applied to the *kehuna*; it is not directly legislating the need for salt on *korbanot*.

It is an interesting inversion. The *baraita* earlier compared salt to *kehuna* — just as *kehuna* is always required, so too is salt always required, since the same *pasuk* applies the *brit melach olam* to both. But the Gemara we just read reversed the analogy: *k'sheim she'efshar l'korbano blo kehuna, kach efshar l'korbano blo melach* — just as a *korban* can, in certain circumstances, be valid without a *kohen*, so too can it be valid without salt. The *kehuna* is taken as the more axiomatic baseline, and salt is the unexpected case. There is something almost playful about this reversal.

### Rabbeinu Tam

Given the difficulty in using the *Korach* verse as the repetition of salt, Rabbeinu Tam emended the *girsa* of the earlier Gemara. He argues that the cited *pasuk* was never meant to be *brit melach olam* from *Korach* at all. Within *Vayikra* itself, the salt requirement is actually stated twice in adjacent verses: first, *v'chol korban minchatcha b'melach timlach*, and second, *v'lo tashbis melach bris elokeicha me'al minchatecha — al kol korbancha takriv melach*. The word *bris* appears in this second verse within *Vayikra* itself — *melach bris elokeicha* — and that is what the Gemara meant when it said the *pasuk* uses *brit*. There was no need to invoke the verse from *Korach* at all.

Whether or not Rabbeinu Tam's emendation is accepted, the more natural understanding of *tana bei kra* is that the very same section in *Vayikra* repeats the salt requirement more than once in its own right. The idea that you must salt the *korbanot* is stated, then restated — that is what constitutes the repetition.

### Baraita's Logic

The Gemara responds to the apparent redundancy by explaining: *ha-hu miba-ye lei l'ched-tanya* — that verse is needed for what is taught in the *baraita*. The verse *v'chol korban minchatcha b'melach timlach* is not simply a reiteration; it is the source for the *baraita*'s detailed derivation of precisely which offerings require salt and which do not. The verse's emphasis on *minchatcha* — your *mincha* — is doing specific halakhic work, and that work cannot be reduced to mere repetition.

This also provides the structural logic of what the Gemara is accomplishing in this section. Under the framework of Rav's principle — explaining what is and is not *me'akev* in the Mishnah — the Gemara is, in the course of asking whether a law was repeated, simultaneously teaching the substantive details of how these laws are performed. Just as the discussion of *hagashah* yielded an explanation of how *hagashah* works, the discussion of salt now yields the full scope of the salt requirement.

## Baraita's Drasha

### Only Mincha?

The *baraita* proceeds as follows. *Ilu ne'emar korban ba-melach* — had the Torah simply said "your *korban* with salt," without mentioning *mincha*, I might have thought that even wood brought to the *mizbeach* and blood itself require salt, since both can be called a *korban*. *Talmud lomar mincha* — therefore the Torah specifically uses *mincha* as the paradigm. Just as *mincha* is distinct in that other things come with it as an obligation — *ma mincha meyuchedet she-acherim ba'im chova lah* — so too, everything else that requires something else to come with it as an obligation requires salt.

What does it mean that other things come with the *mincha* as an obligation? The Gemara explains: it means wood — *etzim*. In order to burn the *kometz* on the altar, one must have wood. The *mincha* paradigm therefore teaches that anything which is placed on the fire and burned on wood requires salt. Blood is sprinkled, not burned; wood is itself the fuel, not something burned on other wood. Both are therefore excluded.

The Gemara, however, immediately presses: why should the relevant characteristic of *mincha* be that things come with it (i.e., that it is burned on wood) rather than that it is a *matir* — the agent that permits the remainder to be consumed? The *kometz* is the *matir* of the *mincha*; the blood is the *matir* of the animal *korban*. If we focus on the *matir* characteristic, we would include blood and exclude most of the things we want to include: the *evarim* of an *olah*, the *imurim* of a *chatat* and *asham*, and so on. So which characteristic of *mincha* is the operative one?

The *baraita* then addresses a further question within its own logic. Having established that we include everything burnt on wood, the *baraita* asks: *ve'ein li ela kometz* — I only know this from the *kometz*; *minayin lerabot et halevona* — how do I know to include the *levona*? The answer given is *sheken ba'ah imah bikli echad* — because the *levona* comes together with the *kometz* in the same vessel and was sanctified along with it. But this immediately prompts a further question: if the *levona* that accompanies the *mincha* is included on the basis of sharing a vessel, how do I know about *levona* that comes by itself — as an independent donation — or the *levona* that sits in the bowls alongside the *lechem hapanim*, or the *ketoret* that is burned on the inner altar? The *baraita* eventually teaches: *talmud lomar al kol korbancha takriv melach* — from the general phrase "on all your *korbanot*," we include all of these.

The Gemara then notes the structural difficulty: the *baraita* appeared to have already settled the matter by establishing the general principle — everything burned on wood requires salt — yet it then doubled back to ask specifically about *levona* as if the earlier derivation had not resolved it. The Gemara explains: *hachi ka'amar* — the *baraita* anticipated a challenge based on *klal u'prat*. One might have argued that *korban* is a *klal*, *mincha* is a *prat*, and by the rule of *klal u'prat*, the *prat* limits the *klal* so that only a *mincha* itself — and nothing else — requires salt. The final clause, *al kol korbancha takriv melach*, then restates a general term, creating a *klal u'prat u'klal* structure: we include everything similar to the *prat*, i.e., everything that shares the relevant characteristic of *mincha*.

This interpretive move actually fits well with the structure of the *pesukim*. The passage emphasizes *mincha*, *mincha*, *mincha* in successive verses — *kol korban minchatcha bamelach timlach*, *velo tashbit melach bris elokeicha me'al minchatecha* — and then concludes with the broad phrase *al kol korbancha takriv melach*. The natural reading of this progression is that *mincha* is exemplary and the final clause extends the requirement broadly to everything relevantly similar. The limitation implied by the *klal u'prat u'klal* structure is not that something must be identical to a *mincha*, but that it must share the characteristic that makes *mincha* the paradigm — namely, that it is burned on wood.

The *baraita* then clarifies the scope of what is included: the *kometz*, the *levona* of the *mincha* together with it in the same vessel, *levona* brought independently, *levona* in the bowls of the *lechem hapanim*, the *ketoret*, the *minchat kohanim* and *minchat kohen mashiach* — both of which are fully burned without *kemitza*, the *minchat nesachim*, the *imurei chatat* and *imurei asham*, the *imurei kodshei kodashim* (which includes the communal *shalmei tzibur* of *Shavuot*), the *imurim* of *kodashim kalim*, the *eivrei olah*, and the *olat ha'of*. All of these require salt, derived from the verse *al kol korbancha takriv melach*. The principle unifying them is that they are offered on the fire.

### Expand to All

The Gemara quotes the *baraita*'s conclusion: *ma mincha meyuchedet she-acherim ba'im chova lah, af kol she-acherim ba'im chova lo* — just as *mincha* is distinct in that other things must come with it as an obligation, so too with everything for which other things must come as an obligation. The Gemara then asks what this phrase means in practice. The answer is: *etzim* — wood. The characteristic that other things must come with it as an obligation is the characteristic of being burned on wood, since wood must be brought to enable the burning. This is the defining feature that connects all salted offerings.

### What's Similar?

The Gemara then raises a sharper challenge to the identification of this characteristic. *Ei ma mincha meyuchedet she-materet af kol she-matir* — one might equally have said that the defining characteristic of *mincha* is that its *kometz* is a *matir*, the agent that permits the remainder to be consumed. By this logic, one would require salt on everything that functions as a *matir* — which means the blood, not the *imurim*. So how do we know which characteristic of *mincha* to select?

The Gemara's resolution is that *minchatecha* appears twice in the relevant verses. The doubling signals: not everything similar to *mincha* in any respect, but only things similar in one specific respect. The question then becomes: which specific characteristic?

One might further suggest that two characteristics must be shared — both that the offering is burned on wood and that it is a *matir* — which would narrow the required salt to the *levona* of the *lechem hapanim* (which is both a *matir* for the *lechem hapanim* and is burned). The Gemara dismisses this: *midei itztarich me'al minchatecha velo me'al damecha* — the very fact that the *pasuk* needed explicitly to exclude blood (*me'al minchatecha velo me'al damecha*) tells us that the category was assumed to be large, requiring narrowing. Had we been limiting inclusion to those things sharing two characteristics, the category would never have been broad enough to include blood in the first place, and no explicit exclusion would have been needed. The explicit exclusion of blood therefore confirms that the category was indeed broad — based on a single characteristic — and requires narrowing by one specific characteristic rather than by adding a second requirement.

The operative characteristic is not the *matir* quality but the quality of being burned. Anything burned on the altar requires salt; blood and wine, which are respectively sprinkled and poured rather than burned, do not.

## Blood Question

### Blood as Matir

The Gemara has established that the principle of *she-acherim ba'im chova lah* — other things come with it as an obligation — is to be understood as referring to wood, meaning that what requires salt is what is burned on wood. The Gemara now asks whether there is any way blood could have entered the category. After all, the initial framing of the *baraita* says: *shome'a ani afilu etzim ve-dam* — I might have thought that even wood and blood require salt, since both can be called a *korban*. The Gemara here probes whether blood could qualify on the basis of being analogous to the *kometz* in its function as a *matir*.

The *kometz* is the *matir* of the *mincha*. Blood is the *matir* of the animal *korban*. If the relevant characteristic of *mincha* that generates the salt requirement were its role as a *matir*, blood would be the natural counterpart in animal *korbanot* and would require salt. The question therefore becomes: is there any grounds on which to read the characteristic of *mincha* as its *matir* function rather than its being burned?

### Nisachim Group

The Gemara introduces a conceptual distinction to rebuff the suggestion that blood is linked to the *nisachim*: *nesachim bahadi imurin hu de'atu* — the *nisachim* are conceptually associated with the *imurim*, not with the blood. The argument is geographic and functional: the *nisachim* are brought to the top of the *mizbeach* and are offered there alongside the *imurim*, which are also burned on the *mizbeach*. The blood, by contrast, is sprinkled at the base or the sides of the altar. If one were to ask where things "come with" the *korban*, the *nisachim* conceptually accompany the *imurim*, not the blood.

### Food & Drink

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason to group *nisachim* with *imurim* rather than with blood? The answer is *achila u-shtiya* — eating and drinking. The *mizbeach* "eats" the *imurim* and "drinks" the wine. This anthropomorphic framing is more honest with the *pesukim* than it may initially seem: the Torah repeatedly refers to the *korbanot* as *lechem isheh* — bread consumed by fire — and the concept of *shulchan gavoah*, the table of the Most High, is well-attested. Within this framework, the wine of the *nisachim* is the drink of the *mizbeach*, and the *imurim* are its food. The blood, by contrast, is not in this category of *achila u-shtiya*; it is sprinkled, not consumed as food or drink.

### Kaparah Link

The Gemara considers a counterargument: *aderaba — kapara v-simcha* — on the contrary, one could link blood and wine through *kapara* and *simcha*. Blood effects atonement; wine brings joy; and atonement is accompanied by joy. Therefore, the wine of the *nisachim* might conceptually belong together with the blood rather than with the *imurim*, since they share the context of atonement and its ensuing joy.

This is a striking argument. We do not usually associate joy with the atonement process itself, but the Gemara draws on the idea that once atonement has been achieved, joy is its natural companion — a concept that surfaces also in the dictum *lo hayu yamim tovim b'Yisrael k'Tu B'Av u-k'Yom HaKippurim*. Rashi explains the connection as the joy of one whose sin has been atoned: *d'mi she-nitkaper chato sameach*.

There is, however, a conceptual weakness in this linkage: the *minchat nesachim* and its accompanying wine are brought specifically with free-will *korbanot* — an *olah* or *shlamim* — and not with a *chatat* or *asham*, which are the paradigmatic *korbanot* of atonement. If the connection between wine and blood were really about the joy of *kapara*, we would expect wine to accompany the *chatat*, which it does not. Furthermore, the argument then pivots: even if we were to say *nisachim* accompany the blood conceptually, the mechanism by which *nisachim* are said to "come with" the *korban* is not the same tight connection that *levona* has to the *kometz*. *Levona* is sanctified together with the *kometz* in the same vessel; *nisachim* have no such intimate structural connection to the blood.

The Gemara makes this explicit: *ella levona ba'ah imah b'kli echad nesachim ein ba'in b'kli echad* — the *levona* comes with the *kometz* in the same vessel; *nisachim* do not come in the same vessel as the blood. Even if we had tried to include blood by arguing that things come with it — namely the *nisachim* — there is no way to get blood included through the back door of the *kometz*-*levona* relationship, because that relationship is structurally unique in its closeness.

The Gemara adds a further point: *aval etzim ki heichi d'mitkashra behu mincha hachi mitkashra b'chulhu korbanot* — when "comes with" is understood as referring to wood, then *mincha* has no closer connection to wood than any other *korban* that is burned. All burned offerings require wood equally, and therefore the characteristic of being burned on wood is genuinely universal among the burned offerings. This allows the category to expand properly outward to everything that is burned, which is exactly what the verses' final clause — *al kol korbancha takriv melach* — seems to require.

It is notable that throughout this discussion, the Gemara never suggested including the *mincha* component of the *minchat nesachim* together with the blood. It focused exclusively on the wine component. This is presumably because the *mincha* component of the *minchat nesachim* — the flour and oil — is naturally grouped with the *imurim* as a burned offering, not with the blood. The Gemara only explored whether the wine could be linked to the blood through either the *achila u-shtiya* or the *kapara v-simcha* framework.

### One Characteristic

The Gemara resolves: *mide'itztarich me'al minchatecha velo me'al damecha — michlal dehanach kulehu atu bechad tzad* — since the *pasuk* explicitly needed to exclude blood, it follows that the entire category is built on a single point of similarity rather than two. Had two points of similarity been required — being burned *and* being a *matir* — blood would never have entered the category to begin with, and no exclusion would have been necessary. The fact that an explicit exclusion of blood was required confirms that the category was constructed on one characteristic, and the *pasuk* is telling us: do not assume any characteristic of *mincha* will suffice; the specific characteristic that generates the salt requirement is being burned, not being a *matir*.

## Ashbanat Tamei

### Six Points

The Gemara now asks: given that both blood and the *evarim* (and *imurim*) have some similarity to the *kometz*, why do we choose to include the *evarim* and *imurim* rather than the blood? The answer is: *shekein Ashnavat Tamei siman* — there is a mnemonic encapsulating the multiple ways in which the *kometz* resembles the *evarim* and *imurim* as opposed to the blood.

The mnemonic — *alef, shin, beit, nun, tet, mem* — stands for six characteristics:

*Alef* — *acherim ba'im chova lah*: the *kometz* and *evarim* are burned on wood, while blood is not.

*Shin* — *ishim kamosa*: both the *kometz* and the *evarim* are offered directly to the fire, the *ishim*, while blood is not.

*Beit* — *bachutz kamosa*: the *kometz* and the *evarim* are offered on the outer *mizbeach*, while blood is sometimes offered on the inner *mizbeach* and thus does not share this characteristic uniformly.

*Nun* — *notar kamosa*: one who leaves over the *kometz* or the *evarim* overnight transgresses *notar* and is liable to *kares*. The laws of *notar* do not apply to blood; although blood that has become old may not be sprinkled, one is not liable for *notar* in connection with blood.

*Tet* — *tuma kamosa*: eating the *kometz* or the *evarim* in a state of *tuma* renders one liable for the transgression of *tum'as ochel kodesh*, carrying a penalty of *kares*. This liability does not apply to blood in the same way.

*Mem* — *me'ila kamosa*: *me'ila* — the prohibition against misusing consecrated property — applies to the *kometz* and to the *evarim*, but blood is excluded from *me'ila*. The fact that blood is excluded from so many of these categories is itself striking; the Gemara has not yet explored these exclusions in depth, but the pattern is consistent.

### Tosafot: Ochel

Tosafot raises the question of the *alef* in the mnemonic. If the mnemonic is *shin, beit, nun, tet, mem* — *Ashnavat* without the *alef* — the *alef* would be superfluous. Tosafot therefore argues that the *alef* is not merely a mnemonic convenience but carries substantive content: *ochel kamosa* — food. The *kometz* and *evarim* are types of food, in the broad sense that they are things one normally salts. This is the point raised earlier: we salt meat and bread; we do not normally salt blood. This practical, almost prosaic observation supports the intuition that salt belongs on the *kometz* and the *evarim*, not on the blood. And since we eat meat to remove the blood from it, the very act of salting in ordinary practice reinforces the halakhic conclusion that blood is not in the category of what is salted.

### More Similarities

The Gemara acknowledges the counterargument: *aderaba dam havi lei leribuyei shekein matir kamosa* — one could equally argue in favor of blood, which resembles the *kometz* in being a *matir*, and also shares the characteristic of *nifsal bishki'as hachama kamosa* — both the blood and the *kometz* become invalid after sunset (*sheki'at ha-chama*). Once the *matir* has been performed — the blood sprinkled, the *kometz* burned — the remaining portions of the *korban* may be burned throughout the night. But the *matir* itself must be performed during the day; blood that has not been sprinkled by nightfall can no longer be used, just as the *kometz* cannot be burned after sunset. Tosafot notes that one could add that both blood and the *kometz*, as *matirim*, are also exempt from *piggul* — improper intent does not disqualify the *matir* itself, only what the *matir* permits.

Nonetheless, the Gemara concludes: *hanach nefishan* — the list of similarities between the *kometz* and the burned offerings is simply much longer than the list of similarities between the *kometz* and the blood. The six (or seven, counting *ochel*) characteristics in the *Ashnavat Tamei* mnemonic all point toward including the burned offerings rather than the blood. The shorter list of *matir*-related similarities to blood is outweighed.

The significance of the phrase *nifsal bishki'as hachama* is not lost on Tosafot, who takes the occasion here to elaborate at length on the position of Rabbeinu Tam regarding the definition of nightfall. The Gemara in Shabbat teaches that *bein hashmashos* — twilight — begins approximately three-quarters of a *mil* after visible sunset, roughly thirteen to fifteen minutes. Rabbeinu Tam, however, based on a Gemara in Pesachim, maintained that nightfall (*layla*) does not truly begin until four *mil* — seventy-two minutes — after visible sunset.

The astronomical model underlying the Gemara's discussion is that the sun, after setting in the west, travels through the thickness of the *rakia*, and some light escapes during this transit, which accounts for the visible light after sunset. The thickness of the *rakia* corresponds to a travel time of four *mil*. Rabbeinu Tam argued that *bein hashmashos* does not begin at the start of this transit but only near its end, when the sun is about to emerge on the other side. Therefore, true nightfall is only at the second sunset — the point at which the sun has fully passed through the *rakia*.

Rabbeinu Tam deployed this view not only stringently — to delay the end of Shabbat — but also leniently, since he held that true *bein hashmashos* does not even begin until close to an hour after visible sunset. In practice, however, virtually no one today rules leniently based on Rabbeinu Tam; the stringency of a late Shabbat conclusion is observed in some communities, but the leniency of ignoring what happens after visible sunset is not.

Rabbeinu Tam's position is influenced partly by geographic realities: in the high latitudes of France, it remains light for a very long time after visible sunset, and the transition to darkness is gradual. In lower latitudes, such as Eretz Yisrael, nightfall comes much more rapidly after sunset — a phenomenon familiar to anyone who has experienced a Jerusalem evening versus a New York one. This latitude effect shaped Rabbeinu Tam's intuition about when true darkness begins.

The reason Tosafot elaborates on all of this here is that the statement *nifsal bishki'as hachama* prompted questions about exactly what *shki'as hachama* means and when it occurs. By *korbanot*, Tosafot in the name of Rabbeinu Tam argues, the invalidation occurs at the first visible sunset, not the second. This required reconciling two *pesukim* about performing the service while it is still day, and Rabbeinu Tam used the distinction between the two sunsets to explain why two separate verses were needed. The detailed treatment of this issue is the substance of the large Tosafot that accounts for much of the material in this section of the daf.

## Baraita's Drasha

### Only Mincha?

Returning to the *baraita*, the Gemara presents the derivation more formally. *Ilu ne'emar korban ba-melach* — had the Torah said only "your *korban* with salt" without specifying *mincha*, I would have included even wood (*etzim*) and blood (*dam*), since both can be called a *korban*. *Talmud lomar mincha* — the Torah therefore specifies *mincha* to provide the paradigm. The *mincha* is distinct in that other things must come with it as an obligation (*she-acherim ba'im chova lah*), meaning it is burned on wood. Everything else for which other things must come as an obligation — i.e., everything burned on wood — likewise requires salt.

The *baraita* then continues with the expansion: *talmud lomar al kol korbancha takriv melach* — the general clause at the end broadens the category back out to include all burned offerings. And then: *talmud lomar me'al minchatcha velo me'al damecha* — the *pasuk* excludes blood, teaching that not every characteristic of *mincha* generates inclusion in the salt requirement.

## Wood as Korban

### Wood Offering

The *baraita* has referred to wood as a *korban*, citing the verse from Nechemiah: *ve-ha-goralot hipallnu al korban ha-etzim* — we cast lots for the *korban* of the wood. This establishes the premise that individuals can voluntarily donate wood to the *mizbeach* as a type of offering. The *baraita* teaches: *melamed she-mitnadvim etzim* — this teaches that one may donate wood as a voluntary offering, the minimum quantity being two logs (*shenei gezerim*).

### Rebbi: Wood Mincha

Rebbi goes further: *etzim korban mincha hen* — wood is not merely a *korban* in the broad sense; it is actually a type of *mincha*. This is an expansive reading of the *mincha* category, but it is consistent with the general tendency of *chazal* to extend the concept of *mincha* to include a range of basic foodstuffs and materials that a person might offer — flour, oil, wine, and, according to Rebbi, even wood. The analogy is apt: the *mincha* exists as an offering accessible to someone who cannot afford an animal, and the willingness to treat even wood as a form of *mincha* reflects the principle that whatever a person is able to give, they may bring before God in a formal way.

### Rebbi's Rules

Because Rebbi categorizes *etzim* as a *mincha*, he derives several halakhic consequences. *Ve'te'unim melach* — wood requires salt, since it is a *mincha*. *Ve'te'unim hagasha* — it requires *hagasha*, the presentation of the offering to the *mizbeach*, just as a *mincha* requires *hagasha*. Rava adds: *etzim te'unim kmitza* — according to Rebbi, wood also requires *kemitza*, the taking of a handful. This last point initially seems strange: *kemitza* is normally the act that separates the part burned on the altar from the remainder that is eaten. If the wood is simply going to be burned entirely, what sense does *kemitza* make? Tosafot explains that there are other cases where *kemitza* is performed even when the remainder is also eventually burned, and Rebbi's position on *etzim* would fit into that pattern. Rav Pappa adds a further consequence: *etzim tzrichim etzim* — wood itself needs to be burned on other wood, since it is treated as a full offering placed on the fire in the regular manner.

### Remove Etzim

Given that Rebbi's position — that *etzim* are a *mincha* — is a minority view against the *Chachamim*, the Gemara says: *sami mikan etzim* — remove wood from the list. The *baraita*'s original suggestion that wood might require salt because it is called a *korban* was premised on Rebbi's classification. Since the *Chachamim* do not treat *etzim* as a *mincha*, and since the *baraita* that calls *etzim* a *korban* is most naturally read in line with Rebbi's view, the broader consensus holds that wood does not require salt. Tosafot clarifies: the emphasis on *etzim* being a *korban* in the *baraita* is the language of Rebbi, who gives it full *korban* status; the *Chachamim* acknowledge that one may donate *etzim*, but they do not give it the halakhic character of a *mincha* and therefore do not require salt on it.

## Final Exclusions

### Me'al Question

Having worked through the full scope of what requires salt, the Gemara returns to the verse *me'al minchatecha velo me'al damecha* — from your *mincha* and not from your blood — which served throughout as the proof text excluding blood from the salt requirement. The question now is: *ela kra leme'utei mai* — at the end of the day, what is this verse excluding? If blood is excluded by *velo me'al damecha*, what else needed to be said? Is blood the only thing explicitly excluded?

### Blood & Wine

The Gemara answers: *afik etzim ve'ayal nesachim* — remove *etzim* from the included list and add *nisachim* to the excluded list. The two things that are not salted, beyond the excluded category of wood (which was never truly in the category under the view of the *Chachamim*), are blood and the wine of the *nisachim*. The *minchat nesachim* — the flour and oil that accompany a free-will *korban* — is salted, since it is burned. But the wine that is poured down the side of the *mizbeach* is not burned; it is poured. Like blood, which is sprinkled rather than burned, wine is not subject to the salt requirement.

Thus the final picture is clean: blood is sprinkled, wine is poured, and neither is burned. Everything that is burned on the *mizbeach* — the *kometz*, the *levona*, the *imurim*, the *evarim*, the *ketoret*, and all the varieties of *mincha* that are wholly burned — requires salt. These two exclusions, blood and wine, represent the offerings that come to the *mizbeach* by means other than fire. With this, the Gemara's analysis of the salt requirement concludes, and the discussion continues in the following session.
