# Nazir 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Single Unknown Burial

### Body in Normal Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹצֵא מֵת בַּתְּחִילָּה, מוּשְׁכָּב כְּדַרְכּוֹ — נוֹטְלוֹ וְאֶת תְּפוּסָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who finds a corpse for the first** time, i.e., he discovers a single corpse in a place that was not previously established as a cemetery, if the corpse is **lying in** the **usual manner** of Jewish burial, **he removes it** from there **and** also **its surrounding** earth. It is assumed that this corpse was buried there alone. There is no concern that this area is a cemetery and therefore the corpse may not be moved, nor does one take into account the possibility that another corpse may be buried in the vicinity.

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 64b opens with a case that has little direct connection to *nazir* per se, but picks up on thematic associations with the opening *mishnah* of the tractate — particularly the principle of *tumat hatehom* and the phrase *raglayim ladavar*. The *mishnah* begins: *hamotzei meit techila* — if someone discovers a dead body that was previously unknown, *mushkav kedarko*, lying in the normal burial position. As the *gemara* will clarify, this specification is not incidental; it comes to exclude certain other cases, as we will see.

The scenario presupposes a body that has been discovered, perhaps in the middle of a road or a field — somewhere it does not belong in the public sphere. In ancient times, if someone died while traveling, the practical exigencies of the moment might have compelled companions to bury the body wherever they happened to be, with the intention perhaps of returning later to relocate it properly. The paradigmatic case from *Tanach* is that of *Rachel*, about whom it is written *vatikaver sham bederech Ephrat* — she was buried right there on the road. Such a burial was understood not to confer a permanent right on that location.

### Removal & Reburial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנַיִם — נוֹטְלָן וְאֶת תְּפוּסָתָן. מָצָא שְׁלֹשָׁה, אִם יֵשׁ בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְעַד שְׁמוֹנֶה — הֲרֵי זוֹ שְׁכוּנַת קְבָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if he found **two** corpses, **he removes them and their surrounding** earth. In a case where he **found three** corpses, **if there is** a space **between this** corpse **and that** corpse **of four to eight cubits,** in a standard design, **this is a graveyard.** There is a concern that this might be an ancient cemetery.

Given that such a burial was understood as temporary and not conferring a permanent claim to the site, the *mishnah* rules: *notlo v'et tfusato* — one removes the body together with its *tfusa*. The word *tfusa* derives from *l'tpos*, to grab or seize, and refers to the surrounding earth that is taken up along with the body. The body is then to be reburied in a proper cemetery. The rationale for the relocation is to ensure that *kohanim*, *nezirim*, and others obligated to avoid *tumat meit* are not prevented from using the road or area.

### Purpose of Dirt

Why is the surrounding dirt taken as well? At first glance this might seem puzzling. After all, bodily fluids in themselves, once the person has died, do not necessarily constitute a primary source of *tumah* — the classic categories of *tumat meit* are flesh, bone, and blood in specific quantities. One might ask, then, what is the concern that drives the requirement to remove the surrounding earth?

The answer seems to be that the concern is not purely about *tumah* in the technical sense but about *kevura* — the dignity and integrity of the burial. One wants all the remains of the *meis* to be reinterred together. There is also a possibility, as the *gemara* will elaborate, that some material in the surrounding earth genuinely could contribute to the halakhic quantities of *rekev* — the decomposed matter of a corpse — that do cause *tumah*. The *mishnah* continues: if two bodies are found, *shnayim notlan v'et tfusatan* — you take both and the surrounding dirt of each and rebury them. But the critical ruling comes next.

## Shechunat Kvarot

### Two Bodies

The discovery of a single body, or even two bodies, does not by itself trigger any assumption of a graveyard. Each is treated individually: you remove it with its surrounding earth and rebury it elsewhere. No special investigation of the surrounding area is required. It is only when three bodies are found that the analysis shifts fundamentally.

### Three Bodies Threshold

*Matza shlosha* — if three bodies are discovered — *im yeish bein zeh l'zeh arba amot ad shmoneh*, if there is between them a spacing within a range of four to eight *amot*, then *harei zeh shechunat kvarot* — this constitutes a *shechunat kvarot*, a neighborhood of graves, that is to say, a graveyard. The logic of the four-to-eight *amot* range is that this corresponds to the spacing one would expect in a crypt or cemetery. If the bodies were too close together, one might suspect a mass burial under some tragic circumstance rather than a planned cemetery. If they were too spread out, they would simply appear to be isolated individual graves with no relation to one another. Within this particular range, the arrangement reflects what one would expect of a deliberately organized burial site.

What precisely the four-to-eight *amot* range means in terms of the layout of the three bodies is itself a matter of detailed discussion. *Tosafos* engages at length with this question in light of a *gemara* in *Bava Basra* concerning how bodies were placed in *kuchim* — burial niches or crypts. *Tosafos* offers the possibility that two of the bodies are within four *amot* of each other, while the full range extends to eight *amot* for all three, though the precise geometric arrangement remains somewhat unclear.

### Twenty-Cubit Radius

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹדֵק הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְהַלָּן עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה, מָצָא אֶחָד בְּסוֹף עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה — בּוֹדֵק הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְהַלָּן עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה. שֶׁרַגְלַיִם לַדָּבָר. שֶׁאִילּוּ תְּחִילָּה מְצָאוֹ — נוֹטְלוֹ וְאֶת תְּפוּסָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** One must therefore **examine from that** spot **outward** for **twenty cubits.** If one **finds** another corpse **at the end of twenty cubits,** he **examines** from that spot **outward twenty cubits, as there is a basis for** anticipating **the matter.** It is likely that he has stumbled upon an ancient gravesite. He is not permitted to relocate the corpses, despite the fact **that if he had found** the single corpse by itself **at first** he could have **removed it and its surrounding** earth.

Once three bodies are found within this range and the location is declared a *shechunat kvarot*, the *mishnah* mandates a further investigation: *bodeik mehemenu u'lehalan esrim ama* — one must check outward from this point twenty *amot*. The presumption is that if this is indeed a graveyard, there may be further burials beyond those three initially discovered, and one must determine the full extent of the graveyard.

If within that twenty-*ama* radius another grave is found, then *bodeik mehemenu u'lehalan esrim ama* — you must check again, another twenty *amot* from that new discovery. The graveyard is assumed to be continuing, and the investigation must keep pace with each new find. The phrase the *mishnah* invokes for this cascading investigation is *raglayim ladavar* — there is good circumstantial evidence warranting continued suspicion that the graveyard extends further.

This is one of the two reasons this *mishnah* appears here rather than simply in a tractate dealing with purity law. First, the subject matter involves buried *tumah* of unknown provenance — *tumat hatehom* — which connects to the opening *mishnah* of the tractate. Second, the phrase *raglayim ladavar* appears here and connects textually to the *mishnah* that preceded it, though in that earlier context the phrase carried a somewhat different nuance: there it referred to a pre-existing basis — the person had already been *tamei*, and that prior status formed the foundation upon which a new *tumah* was assessed. Here, *raglayim ladavar* carries its more common meaning of circumstantial evidence justifying ongoing investigation.

### Context Matters

The *mishnah* concludes with an important interpretive point: *she'ilu techila matza'o notel et tfusato* — had you found that one body alone, in isolation, you would simply have removed it with its surrounding dirt and reburied it elsewhere. You would not have been required to conduct any twenty-*amot* investigation, and even had you proactively done so and found another grave twenty *amot* away, you would not have declared the area a graveyard. You would have treated each grave as a separate isolated burial.

But now, because you began with a cluster of three graves, and this additional grave falls within the twenty *amot* radius of that cluster, the interpretive context is entirely different. The same grave that in isolation would have been treated as a lone burial is now read as part of the graveyard. This is a striking illustration of how the identical physical fact — one grave — can carry entirely different legal significance depending on what has already been established about the surrounding context.

Implicit in the *mishnah*, though not stated explicitly, is a principle the *gemara* spells out: once a location is determined to be a *shechunat kvarot*, the bodies there are not to be disinterred. They have a right to remain where they are. The original permission to disinter a lone grave rests on the assumption that the burial there was incidental — no one plants a permanent cemetery in the middle of a road. But if there is a graveyard, the graveyard predates the road, the road was built over the graveyard, and the bodies have every right to their place. In that case, you do not disinter the graves; you reroute the road.

## Exceptions & Exclusions

### Discovered vs. Known

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מָצָא — פְּרָט לְמָצוּי. מֵת — פְּרָט לְהָרוּג. מוּשְׁכָּב — פְּרָט לְיוֹשֵׁב. כְּדַרְכּוֹ — פְּרָט לְשֶׁרֹאשׁוֹ מוּנָּח בֵּין יַרְכוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda said** the following inferences from the mishna: The phrase: He **found, excludes** a corpse **that** already had **been found.** If it was known that there was one corpse buried in a certain place, the discovery of two previously unknown corpses does not raise the concern that perhaps it is a forgotten graveyard. Similarly, the term **corpse [*met*] excludes a killed [*harug*]** person. Even if there were three corpses found, if there are signs that these people were killed, the area is not assumed to be a graveyard, as they may have been buried where they were found killed. Likewise, the term **lying excludes a sitting** person, as Jews were not generally buried in a seated position. The phrase: **In** the usual **manner, excludes** one **whose head was placed between his thighs,** as that is not the way Jews are buried.

The *gemara* proceeds to interpret each qualifying term in the *mishnah* as excluding a category of case. The method is one of *prat lezeh* — each word of the *mishnah* functions to exclude something, though the *gemara* notes somewhat wryly that this sounds like the style of *derashah* applied to a biblical verse, whereas here it is being applied to a *mishnah*. Nevertheless, the *mishnah* clearly articulates criteria, and those criteria function to exclude cases that do not meet them.

*Matza* — discovered — *prat lematzuy*: this applies specifically to a body whose existence was previously unknown. If a grave was always known to exist in a particular place, that changes the analysis entirely. A known grave is not subject to disinterment; its location was established and has always been recognized. The permission to move a grave depends on the assumption that it was an incidental or emergency burial; a known grave carries with it the presumption that it was intentionally placed there.

### Murdered Bodies

*Meit* — if it is a dead body — *prat le'harug*, as opposed to someone who was murdered. The *gemara* is about to cite the principle that these rules apply only when the body is whole, and a murdered or dismembered body would by definition not be whole. This explains the exclusion, at least in part, though *Tosafos* acknowledges that the rationale for excluding a murdered body is not made fully explicit.

### Position Criteria

*Mushkav kedarko* — lying in the normal way — *prat leyoshev*, as opposed to sitting upright, and *prat leroshon munach bein yarchosav*, as opposed to a fetal position with the head placed between the knees. These exclusions address cases where the burial posture suggests the body may be that of a non-Jew. The normal Jewish burial practice was to lay the body horizontally; sitting or fetal positions were associated with non-Jewish burial customs. Notably, Bronze Age burials in the land were often in a sitting position, so this is not merely a theoretical concern. If the body is non-Jewish, then *tumat ohel* does not apply according to the ruling that a non-Jew does not convey *tumat ohel*, and therefore the entire apparatus of investigation becomes irrelevant.

### Incomplete Bodies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי עוּלָּא בַּר חֲנִינָא: מֵת שֶׁחָסַר, אֵין לוֹ תְּפוּסָה וְלֹא שְׁכוּנַת קְבָרוֹת. וְכׇל הָנֵי מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? אָמְרִינַן דִּילְמָא גּוֹי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla bar Ḥanina taught** a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Oholot* 16:2): **A corpse that is lacking** a part of his body indispensable to life **has no** *halakha* of **surrounding** earth, i.e., there is no need to remove the nearby earth along with the corpse. **Nor** does it have the *halakha* of **a graveyard,** i.e., it does not join with two other corpses to establish this site as a cemetery. The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **all these** listed above, i.e., a corpse that was buried in a sitting position or with its head between its thighs, **what is the reason** that they are **not** considered part of a graveyard? The Gemara answers: **We say** that **perhaps** the deceased **was a gentile,** as Jews are not usually buried in these ways.

*Tani Ulla bar Chanina*: a *braita* teaches that if the body is incomplete — *meit she'chasar ein lo tfisah* — there is no requirement to take the surrounding dirt, *velo shechunat kvarot* — and if three such incomplete bodies are found, one does not establish a *shechunat kvarot* from them.

This ruling raises a genuine question. The consequence in all these exclusion cases is not that one is prohibited from disinterring — it is, if anything, more lenient: one *can* disinter without the additional stringencies. One need not take the surrounding earth, and one need not worry that three such bodies establish a graveyard. The only case where disinterment is clearly prohibited is the case of a *known* grave — *prat lematzuy* — where the presumption is that the grave has a right to remain in place.

But *Tosafos* raises the question: what is the logic for saying that an incomplete body does not establish *shechunat kvarot*? The answer given is *halacha leMoshe miSinai* — which *Tosafos* acknowledges is, formally speaking, something of a last resort in terms of explanations, though it acknowledges that the tractate has had occasion to invoke *halacha leMoshe miSinai* repeatedly.

As for the cases where the exclusion is based on the possibility that the bodies are non-Jewish — there are two possible consequences: either you are still allowed to disinter but without the stringencies of *tfusa* and *shechunat kvarot*, or, if we assume the bodies are non-Jewish, you may simply move on entirely, since a non-Jewish body does not convey *tumat ohel* and there is no *tumah* concern driving the investigation in the first place.

### Head-to-Foot Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא שְׁנַיִם, רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּצַד מַרְגְּלוֹתָיו שֶׁל זֶה, וְרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה בְּצַד מַרְגְּלוֹתָיו שֶׁל זֶה — אֵין לָהֶן תְּפוּסָה וְלֹא שְׁכוּנַת קְבָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further states: If one **found two** corpses, with **the head of this** one **by the feet of that** one **and the head of that** one **by the feet of this** one, **they do not have** the *halakha* of **surrounding earth, nor** do they have the *halakha* of **a graveyard.** This is not the way Jews are buried, as corpses in a Jewish cemetery always face the same direction.

*Matza shnayim, rosho shel zeh b'tzad marglotav shel zeh, v'rosho shel zeh b'tzad marglotav shel zeh* — if two bodies are found buried in a head-to-foot, head-to-foot arrangement, lying parallel to each other in alternating orientation — *ein lahem tfusa v'lo shechunat kvarot*. Here too one does not take the surrounding dirt, and three such burials do not establish a *shechunat kvarot*. This type of arrangement is not consistent with normal Jewish burial practice, suggesting once again that these may be non-Jewish graves, or at least that something atypical is occurring that should not trigger the full apparatus of the graveyard rules.

### Mixed Known/Unknown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא שְׁלֹשָׁה, הָאֶחָד יָדוּעַ וּשְׁנַיִם תְּחִילָּה, אוֹ שְׁנַיִם תְּחִילָּה וּשְׁנַיִם יְדוּעִים — אֵין לָהֶם תְּפוּסָה, וְאֵין לָהֶם שְׁכוּנַת קְבָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** If one **found three** corpses, **one** of which was previously **known, while** the other **two** were found **for the first** time now, **or** if one found **two for the first** time **and two** that were **known, they do not have** the *halakha* of **surrounding** earth, **and they** also **do not have** the *halakha* of **a graveyard.** One does not view these corpses as connected.

*Matza shlosha, echad yadua u'shnayim techila* — if three bodies are found, one of which was always known and two of which are newly discovered — or, switching to a group of four, two known and two newly discovered — *ein lahem tfusa v'ein lahem shechunat kvarot*. Even though three or four bodies are present, if the group is a mixture of known and newly discovered graves, neither *tfusa* nor *shechunat kvarot* applies in the same way.

*Tosafos* explains the logic for the *shechunat kvarot* exclusion, which at least makes a certain amount of sense. The category of *shechunat kvarot* applies either to three bodies that were all previously known — in which case they constitute a recognized graveyard — or to three bodies all newly discovered — in which case we infer that this must be a forgotten graveyard. But a mixture of the two does not fit either category. If it were a recognized graveyard, one would have known about all three. If it were a forgotten graveyard, one would have found all three newly. The mixed case falls between the categories; one can simply assume that the known graves were always there and the newly discovered ones are coincidental additions. There is no basis to declare the entire area a graveyard.

As for why *tfusa* also does not apply in the mixed case, *Tosafos* quotes the *Rashbam* who invokes *halacha leMoshe miSinai*.

## Yeshevav & Akiva

### Yeshevav's Attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי יְשֵׁבָב שֶׁבָּדַק וּמָצָא שְׁנַיִם יְדוּעִין וְאֶחָד תְּחִילָּה, וּבִיקֵּשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹתָן שְׁכוּנַת קְבָרוֹת. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כׇּל שֶׁיָּגַעְתָּ — לָרִיק יָגַעְתָּ. לֹא אָמְרוּ שְׁכוּנַת קְבָרוֹת אֶלָּא לִשְׁלֹשָׁה יְדוּעִין, אוֹ לִשְׁלֹשָׁה תְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* relates: **An incident** occurred **involving Rabbi Yeshevav, who examined and found two known** corpses **and one** corpse discovered **for the first** time, **and he wished to deem** the three corpses **a graveyard. Rabbi Akiva said to him: All your toil is in vain. They said** it is **a graveyard only** in a case **of three known** corpses buried in one spot **or three** corpses found **for the first** time. However, if some were known and others were discovered for the first time, one does not combine them.

The *gemara* records a historical incident: *maaseh b'Rabbi Yeshevav she'badak u'matza shnayim yeduyim v'echad techila* — there was a case involving Rabbi Yeshevav who investigated and found two graves that were previously known and one that was newly discovered. He sought to declare this combination a *shechunat kvarot* and to cordon off the surrounding twenty *amot* for investigation.

### Akiva's Rebuke

Rabbi Akiva said to him: *kol she'yagata l'rik yagata* — all the effort you have put in is for nothing. The phrase is striking and evokes something of the sensibility of *Kohelet* — all our efforts amount to naught. But the legal point is sharp: *lo amru shechunat kvarot ela l'gimel yedua'im o l'shlosha techila* — the category of *shechunat kvarot* was said only when all three are previously known or all three are newly discovered.

### Pure Graveyard Status

The requirement is, in other words, categorical uniformity. Either three known graves — a recognized graveyard — or three newly discovered graves — a forgotten graveyard. Any mixture of the two, and one does not have grounds to declare the area a *shechunat kvarot*. Rabbi Akiva's rebuke also carries a practical dimension: declaring large areas to be graveyards has significant consequences for the public, foreclosing movement through those areas for *kohanim* and others who must avoid *tumat meit*. There is therefore good reason to be precise about when the threshold is met.

## Tfusa & Soil

### Tfusa Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹטְלָן וְאֶת תְּפוּסָתָן. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי תְּפוּסָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אָמַר קְרָא ״וּנְשָׂאתַנִי מִמִּצְרַיִם״ — טוֹל עִמִּי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **he removes them and their surrounding** earth. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **surrounding** earth? **Rav Yehuda said: The verse states** with regard to Jacob's instruction to Joseph to transfer his remains to Eretz Yisrael: **"You shall carry me out from Egypt"** (Genesis 47:30), which indicates: **Take** some earth out from Egypt **with me,** i.e., take the earth that is near the corpse.

The *mishnah* says *notlan v'et tfusatan* — one takes the bodies and their *tfusa*. The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami t'vusa* — what exactly constitutes the *tfusa*?

### Egypt Derivation

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar kra* — Rav Yehuda derives it from a biblical verse: *amitatni miMitzrayim* — "take me up from Egypt," the words of Jacob to Joseph regarding his burial. The *drasha* reads into the phrase the idea that Jacob was asking that some of Egypt — some of the earth — come with him. Rashi explains this as connected to the notion that Jacob did not want to remain in *adama tme'ia*, impure soil, and wished to be brought back to the land of Israel along with some of the surrounding earth.

### Three Fingers Earth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּר תְּפוּסָה? פֵּירֵשׁ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר (בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק): נוֹטֵל עָפָר תִּיחוּחַ, וְחוֹפֵר בַּבְּתוּלָה שָׁלֹשׁ אֶצְבָּעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **And what is the measure of surrounding** earth? **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, explained: One takes loose dirt** from near the corpse, as it is assumed it has been loosened by the blood and moisture from the corpse, **and digs virgin,** uncultivated, ground to a depth of **three fingerbreadths,** in case this earth has absorbed the blood.

Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok specifies: *notel afar tichuach* — one takes the loosened, dug earth, *v'chofer b'vetula* — and one also digs into the virgin, undisturbed earth beneath the body, *gimmel etzba'ot* — three finger-widths deep. The rationale is that bodily fluids are assumed to have seeped even into the undisturbed earth beneath the corpse.

### Coffin & Shards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּר תְּפוּסָה? פֵּירֵשׁ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק: נוֹטֵל אֶת הַקֵּיסָמִין וְאֶת הַקְּסָסוֹת. וְזוֹרֵק אֶת הַוַּודָּאִין וּמַנִּיחַ אֶת הַסְּפֵיקוֹת. וְהַשְּׁאָר מִצְטָרֵף לְרוֹב בִּנְיָנוֹ שֶׁל מֵת, וּלְרוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת, לִמְלֹא תַּרְווֹד רָקָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this ruling from a different *baraita*: **And what is the measure of surrounding** earth? **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, explained: One takes wood chips** found nearby, which might have been part of the coffin, **and lumps** of earth that might have absorbed the blood and moisture from a corpse. **And he discards** that which is **certainly** not from the corpse, e.g., stones. And **he sets aside the** items with regard to which it is **uncertain** if they have a connection with the corpse. **And the rest,** i.e., anything that is apparently from the corpse, **combine to** reach the amount **of the majority of the structure of a corpse, or of a quarter-***kav* **of bones,** or **of a full ladle of dust** from a corpse. The remains of a corpse impart ritual impurity in a tent only if they meet one of those three qualifications. Anything which is apparently the remains of the corpse is considered dust of a corpse with regard to this *halakha*.

The *gemara* then raises a *braita* that appears to give a different account of the *tfusa*: *v'kama hi tfusa — peirash Rabi Elazar b'Rabi Tzadok: notel et hakismin v'et hatzrorot*. Here the *tfusa* is described in terms of taking *kismin* — splinters, presumably from the coffin — and *tzrorot*. *Tosafos* explains *kismin* as material rubbed off from a marble coffin, and as for *tzrorot*, Rashi explains them as clumps of earth that have absorbed blood and brain matter from the body — *tzrorot adama shenit'havu min hadam u'min hamuach* — so that they may contain substance that contributes to the halakhic quantities of the *meis*.

### Sifting Remains

What is significant about this *braita* is that it does not simply say one takes all the surrounding earth indiscriminately. Rather, there is a process of sifting and discrimination: *v'zorek et havada'in* — you discard that which is certainly not part of the *meis*; *u'maniach et hasfeikot* — you set aside in a secluded place that which may possibly contain part of the *meis*, so that people engaged in matters of *tahara* should not come into contact with it; *v'hashar mitztaref l'rov binyano shel meis u'l'rov minyano u'l'mlo tirvad rekav* — and what is clearly identified as part of the body itself combines toward the threshold quantities: the majority of the body's structure (*rov binyano*), the majority of the body's count of bones (*rov minyano*), or the *melo tirvad rekav* — a full ladle's worth of the decomposed matter of the corpse — all of which are the halakhic thresholds for *tumat meis*.

This is notable because it addresses the question raised earlier about *why* the surrounding dirt is removed. It is not a blanket concern about any fluid; it is because specific identifiable material — material that genuinely constitutes part of the *meis* or its decomposition — may have mingled with the earth, and that material contributes to the *shiurim* that convey *tumah*. One does not remove everything; one tries to identify what genuinely matters.

### Rekev Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּר תְּפוּסָה? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם בֶּן עַזַּאי: נוֹטֵל עָפָר תִּיחוּחַ, וְחוֹפֵר בַּבְּתוּלָה שָׁלֹשׁ אֶצְבָּעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: With regard to the first statement of Rabbi Elazar, it was **he who said in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*,** ben Azzai, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **And how much is the measure of surrounding** earth? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of ben Azzai: One takes loose earth and digs virgin** ground to a depth of **three fingerbreadths.**

*Tosafos* raises a difficulty connected to this passage. For material to count as *rekev* — decomposed matter of a corpse — and to contribute to the *shiur* of *melo tirvad rekav*, it must be pure, uncontaminated by foreign matter. But if the body was buried with fragrances, spices, or other materials placed in the *aron*, as was apparently common practice, then any decomposed matter would be contaminated by these foreign substances and would not qualify as *rekev*.

*Tosafos* addresses this by suggesting that the *kismin* in the *braita* refer to ground-off fragments of a *marble* coffin — *aron shel shayish shenichtat* — rather than a wooden coffin. The reason is that a wooden coffin would itself decompose along with the body and potentially disqualify the *rekev*. If the coffin is marble, this problem is avoided. But this solution in turn raises its own difficulty: if the coffin is marble, how would bodily fluids seep out far enough into the surrounding earth to be a concern? The discussion illustrates how intricate these questions become once one tries to reconcile the *braita*'s details with the underlying halakhic requirements for *rekev*.

## Sequential Discovery

### Third Body Found

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בָּדַק וּפִנָּה, בָּדַק וּפִנָּה, בְּדַק וְאַשְׁכַּח — לָא הַאי מְפַנֵּי לֵיהּ גַּבֵּי הָנָךְ תְּרֵי, וְלָא הָנֵי תְּרֵי לְגַבַּי הַאי חַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** With regard to one who **examined,** found a corpse, **and removed** it from its place, and again **examined** nearby, found another corpse, **and removed** it from its place as well, if he **examined** yet again **and discovered** a third corpse, **he does not move this** third one **alongside these two** he has already moved, as he now knows that this was a graveyard and the corpses were buried there intentionally. **And he does not** return **these two alongside this one** either, as he is not obligated to restore the corpses to their prior locations after they have been reburied.

*Amar Rava*: suppose one checks and disinters, then checks again and disinters, and then on a third occasion finds another body — *badak ufina, badak ufina, badak ve'ashkach, mahu* — what is the law? The question is this: if a person had already removed the first two bodies and then discovered a third, the three together would constitute a *shechunat kvarot*. But the two have already been disinterred. What now?

### Retroactive Graveyard

The tension is between two considerations. On one hand, retroactively one can say this was a *shechunat kvarot* all along, and therefore the bodies had a right to remain where they were and should not have been disinterred. On the other hand, the disinterment of the first two had already been completed before the third was found; at that point in time there was no reason to suspect a *shechunat kvarot*.

### Rava's Compromise

Rava rules: *lo hai mafni leh legabi hanach terei, velo hani terei legabi hai chad* — you do not move the third to join the two that were disinterred, since you now know it was part of a graveyard; but you do not need to reinter the two alongside the third either. Each situation is left as it currently stands. The two that were already moved remain where they were reinterred, and the third remains in its original location.

### Continue Disinterring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּתְּנָה רְשׁוּת לִפְנוֹת, מְפַנֶּה לְהוֹן. וְלִישַׁוִּינְהוּ שְׁכוּנַת קְבָרוֹת! אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: עִילָּא, מָצְאוּ וְטִיהֲרוּ אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rava said: Since permission was granted to move** the first and second corpses, one may therefore **move them** all, including the third one. The Gemara asks: **And let us deem them** part of **a graveyard.** Since three corpses were found buried together, there might be others in the vicinity. **Reish Lakish said: They found a pretext and deemed Eretz Yisrael ritually pure.** Since at that point there was just one corpse, the Sages were lenient and were not concerned about any further impurity. The Sages wished to avoid uncertain impurity because people were careful to observe the *halakhot* of ritual purity in Eretz Yisrael, and additional cases of uncertain impurity would be complicated to observe.

*Ika d'amri* — there is an alternative formulation of Rava's ruling: *keivan she'nasna reshut lefanot, mefaneh lehawn* — once permission to disinter was granted, one may continue and disinter the third as well. Since the process was already legitimately begun — two were already moved — and since practically speaking there is a communal interest in having all bodies in proper cemeteries rather than scattered about fields and roads, once one has started one may continue.

### Ilu Matzu Principle

The *gemara* asks: *velishavinhu shechunat kvarot* — why not simply declare this a *shechunat kvarot* and leave all three in place? Reish Lakish answers: *ilu matzu v'tiharu Eretz Yisrael* — they found an excuse and thereby purified *Eretz Yisrael*. The idea is that there is a broader interest in keeping *Eretz Yisrael* accessible and navigable for *kohanim*, for those engaged in *taharos*, and for the public generally. When any excuse presents itself to treat a site as not a *shechunat kvarot* — thus permitting the bodies to be removed to a proper cemetery — one avails oneself of it. In the sequential discovery case, the excuse is that when each body was disinterred, there was no known graveyard; the third body was found only afterward. Therefore one is permitted to use this as the *eila* — the legal basis — to avoid declaring the site a *shechunat kvarot*.

This principle, it should be noted, has a somewhat ironic dimension. The formal halakhic posture is that *Eretz Yisrael* is *tahora* and *Eretz Ha'amim* is *tamei*. Yet here the *gemara* reveals that this presumption of purity for *Eretz Yisrael* was maintained precisely *because* the rabbis used any available excuse to clear areas from *tumah* suspicion. The presumption of purity is not simply a given; it is actively constructed through halakhic creativity.

### Rosh's Reading

The *Rosh* reads the *ilu matzu* principle more broadly. He comments that Reish Lakish's statement reflects a general operating principle: *ba'alila muetes uvisvara katana tiharu Eretz Yisrael mishum kohanim v'osei taharos* — with a small excuse and a modest argument, they purified *Eretz Yisrael* for the sake of *kohanim* and those engaged in *taharos*. This is not merely a rule for the specific sequential-discovery case; it is a general stance that a low threshold of reasoning suffices to establish purity of a place, because practical life in *Eretz Yisrael* requires it.

The *Rosh* adds: *v'lo dami l'kever yadua d'asur lifnos d'ein kan yedia vada'is* — and this is not comparable to a definitively known grave, which cannot be disinterred, because in the case of sequential discovery there is no truly certain knowledge that this was a graveyard. The discovery was piecemeal, and when each disinterment occurred, the graveyard status had not yet been established.

## No Find in Radius

### Twenty-Cubit Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּדַק מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה וְלֹא מָצָא, מַאי? אָמַר רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַב: שְׁכוּנַת קְבָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question: If one **examined twenty cubits** from the location of those corpses **and did not find** anything, **what is** the *halakha*? **Rav Menashya bar Yirmeya says** that **Rav says:** This is **a graveyard.** Those three corpses compose the graveyard and there is no concern for others.

The *gemara* poses a further question: *badak kaf ama v'lo matza mahu* — if one checked twenty *amot* and found nothing, what is the status? The question is somewhat ambiguous. *Tosafos* understands it as asking about checking in one direction and finding nothing — but as *Tosafos* notes, one does not understand why finding nothing in one direction should mean anything, since the graveyard might extend in the other direction. Nevertheless, that is how *Tosafos* reads the question.

### Still a Graveyard

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yirmya amar Rav: shechunat kvaros* — it remains a *shechunat kvarot*. Now, one would expect *shechunat kvarot* to be the stringent ruling — the one that prevents disinterment. But *Tosafos* understands this as a *kula*, a leniency: the answer is that having found nothing, you may assume that you have reached the end of the graveyard and need not continue checking further in that direction. This reading, as *Tosafos* acknowledges, does not sit perfectly with the words of the *gemara*, but that is how *Tosafos* resolves it.

### Intentional Burial

The *Rosh* offers a fundamentally different reading of the question and answer. According to the *Rosh*, the question is: after finding three bodies and checking all twenty *amot* around in all directions — finding nothing — may you now disinter those original three? Perhaps the failure to find anything else in the radius suggests it is not really a *shechunat kvarot* after all, just three coincidental graves; and if not a *shechunat kvarot*, then they can be disinterred.

The answer, says the *Rosh*, is no: *harei zeh shechunat kvarot* — it remains a *shechunat kvarot*. *U'pshita lei keivan d'gimmel hem muchzak lei shechunat kvarot af al pi shelo matza* — once three bodies are found, the presumption of *shechunat kvarot* is established even if the twenty-*amot* investigation yields nothing further. Three bodies in the appropriate configuration is sufficient to establish that this was an intentional burial site. Perhaps it was a small graveyard with only three graves; that is still a graveyard, and those bodies may not be removed. According to this reading, the *ilu matzu* line that appears in some manuscripts is a copyist's error transferred from the preceding discussion, and the *Rosh* removes it from his text.

## Tzaraat Doubts

### Initial Purity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל סְפֵק נְגָעִים בַּתְּחִילָּה, טָהוֹר — עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִזְקַק לְטוּמְאָה. מִשֶּׁנִּזְקַק לְטוּמְאָה — סְפֵקוֹ טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any** case of **uncertainty** with regard to **leprous sores** is **initially** deemed **pure until it is established that** it is a case **of ritual impurity. Once** it **has been determined** to be a case **of impurity, uncertainty** concerning **it is** deemed **impure.**

The next *mishnah* has no connection to *nazirut* at all, except that it employs structural principles parallel to those seen in the earlier *mishnayot* — particularly the principle of *raglayim ladavar* and the concept of *chazaka*. The *mishnah* states: *kol safek negaim bit'chilatah tahor* — all doubts regarding *negaim* before the person has been declared *tamei* are resolved in favor of *tahara*. *Ad shelo nizkak l'tumah* — as long as the person has not yet been formally established as *tamei*, any doubt as to whether a skin lesion qualifies as *nega tzara'at* is resolved leniently.

The underlying principle is *chazaka* — status quo. The person was *tahor* before; absent a definitive determination that the *nega* meets all requirements for *tumah*, the prior status is maintained.

### Established Impurity

*Mishenizkak l'tumah* — once the person has been formally established as *tamei*, *sfeko tamei* — any subsequent doubt is resolved in favor of *tumah*. If there is a question whether the *nega* has cleared sufficiently to render the person *tahor*, the status quo of *tumah* is maintained. This mirrors the principle from the opening *mishnah* of the tractate: a person already *tamei* is not saved by a subsequent *tumat hatehom* being retroactively unknown, because *raglayim ladavar* — there was already a pre-existing basis of *tumah*.

The *mishnah* invokes the phrase *mipnei she'chezkatam tamei* — because the presumptive status is *tamei*. The mechanism is pure *chazaka*: whatever the prior status was, it is maintained in the face of doubt.

### Two People Case

The *Rosh* provides a vivid illustration of how this *mishnah* operates in practice, drawing on cases from tractate *Negaim*. Two people come before the *kohen* at the same time. One has a lesion of a certain smaller size; the other has a lesion the size of a *sela* — a coin, larger than a *gris*. Neither is yet declared *tamei*; both are examined and locked up for a week of assessment. When they return after the week, both lesions are now the size of a *sela*. One of them has clearly spread — having started smaller, it grew — and is therefore *tamei*. The other, which started at *sela* size and remained at *sela* size, did not spread and was never even the minimum size, so it is *tahor*. But the *kohen* has forgotten which person had which initial lesion size, and neither person remembers either. The ruling: both are *tahor*. Even though one of them is objectively *tamei*, since neither can be identified as the *tamei* one, both retain their initial *chezkat tahara*.

### Lesion Progression

The *Rosh* then presents the reverse scenario. Two people come, one with a lesion smaller than a *gris*, one with a lesion the size of a *sela*. Both are examined and locked up. They return and both have lesions the size of *sela va'od* — larger than a *sela*. Both have spread; both are declared *tamei*. They are locked up again. They return once more, and now both lesions have diminished, returning toward a *sela* in size. The question is: when does each of them become *tahor*?

### Baseline Return

The answer is: each person must return to the size they had *initially*, before any spread. For the person who began with a *sela*-sized lesion, returning to a *sela* suffices to establish *tahara* — that is the original baseline. For the person who began with a smaller lesion, a return to *sela* size is not enough; the lesion must return all the way to its original smaller size. Now if both return to *sela* size simultaneously and the *kohen* cannot recall who started with which size, both remain *tamei*. The one who started smaller has not yet returned to baseline; the one who started at *sela* has. But since one cannot determine which is which, both retain their *chezkat tumah* until the matter can be clarified.

## Nega Appearance Order

### Verse on Purity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אָמַר קְרָא ״לְטַהֲרוֹ אוֹ לְטַמְּאוֹ״, הוֹאִיל וּפָתַח [בּוֹ] הַכָּתוּב בְּטׇהֳרָה תְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The verse states:** "This is the law of the plague of leprosy…**to pronounce it pure or to pronounce it impure"** (Leviticus 13:59). **Since the verse opened with purity first,** this teaches that any case of uncertainty concerning leprous sores is deemed pure.

The *gemara* asks: *mena hani milei* — what is the source for the principle of initial *tahara* in cases of doubt? *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav amar kra: letaharo o letame'o* — the verse says "to purify or to make *tamei*." The fact that the verse mentions *tahara* first — *ho'il ufatah bo hakatuv betahara techila* — since the Torah opened with *tahara*, this indicates a disposition toward *tahara* as the starting point.

### Purity Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ מִשֶּׁנִּזְקַק לְטוּמְאָה נָמֵי סְפֵקוֹ טָהוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** If so, **then even once** it **has been determined** to be a case **of impurity, uncertainty** concerning **it** should be deemed **pure as well,** as this interpretation of the verse should apply to all cases of uncertainty with regard to leprosy.

The *gemara* immediately objects: *ei hachi, afilu mishenizkak l'tumah nami sfeko tahor* — if the verse teaches a general inclination toward *tahara*, then even once a person has been declared *tamei*, any subsequent doubt should also be resolved in favor of *tahara*. That would contradict the second half of the *mishnah*. So the verse cannot be the source of the principle stated in the *mishnah*.

### Hair Whitening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כִּי אִיתְּמַר דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, אַהָא אִיתְּמַר: אִם בַּהֶרֶת קָדְמָה לְשֵׂעָר לָבָן — טָמֵא, וְאִם שֵׂעָר לָבָן קוֹדֵם לַבַּהֶרֶת — טָהוֹר. סָפֵק — טָמֵא. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: כִּיהָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, when** this statement, **that Rav Yehuda** said that **Rav said, was stated, it was stated with regard to the following** dispute in a mishna concerning an uncertain leprous sore (*Nega'im* 4:11): **If** the **snow-white leprous sore [*baheret*],** which is one sign of leprosy, **preceded** the **white hair,** which is another sign, **he is impure.** This *halakha* is stated in the Torah (see Leviticus 13:3). **And if** the **white hair preceded** the ***baheret*** he is **pure,** as this is not considered a sign of impurity. If there is **uncertainty** as to which came first, he is **impure. And Rabbi Yehoshua said: *Keiha*.**

The *gemara* resolves this by relocating the verse's teaching: *ela ki itmar deRav Yehuda amar Rav aha itmar* — this verse was cited by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav for a different *halacha* entirely. The verse is not the source for the status quo principle; that principle rests on the general rule of *chazaka*, which operates independently. The verse instead addresses a specific case: *im beheret kadma lese'ar lavan tamei, ve'im se'ar lavan kodem labeheret tahor* — if the *baheret* lesion appeared first and then the hair within it turned white, the person is *tamei*; but if the hair was always white before the lesion appeared, the person is *tahor*, since the *baheret* did not cause the hair to whiten. *Safek* — if one does not know which occurred first — the person is *tamei*.

This case is structurally different from the ordinary doubt: it is not a question of two separate individuals whose identities are confused. It is a question about the order in which two events occurred in the same person. The general principle of *chazaka* does not straightforwardly resolve this, since one knows the person previously had naturally white hair but also knows the lesion is present. It is in this specific case that the verse supplies a rule.

### Unknown Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״כִּיהָה״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כִּיהָה וְטָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of ***keiha*? Rav Yehuda said:** Rabbi Yehoshua **pronounced** the matter **unsolvable and** ruled it is **pure.**

Rabbi Yehoshua comments *kehe* — which Rav Yehuda explains as meaning *tahor*. The *Rosh* understands Rabbi Yehoshua as providing the rationale for why the order-of-events doubt resolves to *tahara*: the verse opens with *letaharo*, establishing that when the order is unknown, one presumes *tahara* based on the textual priority given to purity. This is the unique contribution of the verse in this specific context, as distinct from the *chazaka*-based principle governing the general cases in the *mishnah*.

## Zav Checks

### Seven Factors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּשִׁבְעָה דְּרָכִים בּוֹדְקִין אֶת הַזָּב עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִזְקַק לְזִיבָה: בְּמַאֲכָל וּבְמִשְׁתֶּה, בְּמַשָּׂא וּבִקְפִיצָה, וּבְחוֹלִי, וּבְמַרְאֶה, וּבְהִירְהוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna discusses another case that includes the statement: There is a basis to anticipate the matter. **One examines a man who experienced a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*] in seven ways, as long as he has not been confirmed as** having **a gonorrhea-like discharge [*ziva*].** With regard to an individual's second such discharge, before he has been established as a greater *zav*, one examines to see whether there may have been a particular trigger of his discharge. One examines him **with regard to food and with regard to drink,** in case the discharge might have been the result of overeating or excess drinking; **with regard to a burden,** as it might have been caused by the weight of a heavy burden; **and with regard to jumping,** in case he jumped and this led to the discharge; **and with regard to sickness; and with regard to** an arousing **sight; and with regard to** the **thought** of a woman.

The penultimate *mishnah* — again unrelated to *nazirut* but brought here for the phrase *raglayim ladavar* — deals with the law of *zav*. *Beshiv'ah derachim bodkim et hazav* — in seven ways one examines the *zav*, the man with an abnormal penile discharge. The context requires some clarification. A man can have two types of emission: seminal emission (*keri*), which conveys a lighter degree of *tumah*, or *zivah*, an abnormal non-seminal discharge, which conveys a heavier degree. The law of *zav* applies to the latter. A man becomes a *zav* after two such discharges; a third requires him to bring a *korban* upon purification. The seven-way check applies *ad shelo nizkak le'zivah* — before he has yet been established as a *zav* — meaning before he has seen two such discharges.

The seven factors are potential external causes that, if present, would explain the discharge as something other than *zivah*, thus exempting the emission from counting toward *zav* status.

### External Causes

The seven factors are: *bemachal uv'mishteh* — something he ate or drank that may have caused the discharge; *bemassa* — carrying a heavy load, physical exertion; *uv'kfitzah* — jumping, again exertion; *uv'choli* — illness. If any of these factors were present, and the man has not yet seen two discharges to establish *zav* status, the emission is attributed to the external cause and does not count.

### Sight & Thoughts

The remaining factors are: *uv'mareh uv'hirhur* — something he saw and thoughts that arose from it, referencing visual stimulation and sexual thoughts that might have prompted the discharge through a pathway analogous to arousal. Though counted separately, these two are understood as connected — something seen leading to *hirhur*, mental dwelling, which in turn might cause the emission through something akin to seminal stimulation even if the discharge itself is not semen.

### Pre-Zav Leniency

Before *zav* status is established — *ad shelo nizkak le'zivah* — if any of these seven factors is present, the emission is attributed to the external cause rather than to *zivah*, and the man does not advance toward *zav* status. The logic is that until the pattern is established, any available explanation for the emission is taken seriously and used leniently.

## Zav Status

### No Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁנִּזְקַק לְזִיבָה — אֵין בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ. אוֹנְסוֹ וּסְפֵיקוֹ וְשִׁכְבַת זַרְעוֹ — טְמֵאִים. שֶׁרַגְלַיִם לַדָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Once he has been confirmed as** having **a *ziva*,** after two definite discharges of *ziva*, **one no** longer **examines him** in this way, as any discharge is deemed impure. If one experiences three discharges of *ziva*, he is obligated to bring an offering following his purification. Accordingly, **his** discharge that was due to **circumstances beyond his control,** i.e., for one of the seven reasons listed above, **and his** discharge about which it is **uncertain** if it is *ziva*, **and** even **his semen,** which is not usually considered the discharge of a *zav*, **are** all **impure.** Why is this so? It is **because there is a basis for** anticipating **the matter.** Once he has the status of a *zav*, it can be assumed that subsequent discharges are of *ziva* as well.

*Mishenizkak le'ziva* — once the man has been established as a *zav* by having two qualifying emissions, the calculus changes entirely. *Ein bodkin oto* — one does not investigate these seven factors anymore. Even if exertion, illness, or other factors are present, a subsequent discharge is treated as *zivah* regardless. The established condition is presumed to be the cause.

### Ones & Semen

*Onso vesfeko veshichvat zaro tme'im* — once he is a *zav*, discharges that would otherwise be attributed to *ones* — external compulsion — are *tamei*; discharges that are in doubt as to their nature are *tamei*; even an emission of semen from a *zav* is treated as *tamei*, on the possibility that it contains *zivah* mixed within it. All categories of discharge, once *zav* status is established, are presumed to be manifestations of the underlying condition.

### Attributed to Condition

The reason given is *sheraglayim ladavar* — because there is now a pre-existing basis, a *raglayim ladavar*, that all emissions are attributable to his condition. Once the condition is established, there is no need to search for alternative explanations; everything is attributed to the *zivah*. This is the third application of *raglayim ladavar* in these *mishnayot*, and here it carries its clearest meaning: a pre-established factual basis that governs the interpretation of all subsequent ambiguous events.

## Fatal Wound

### Mortal Wound

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּכֶּה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ וַאֲמָדוּהוּ לְמִיתָה, וְהֵקֵל מִמַּה שֶּׁהָיָה, לְאַחַר מִכָּאן הִכְבִּיד וָמֵת — חַיָּיב. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: פָּטוּר, שֶׁרַגְלַיִם לַדָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages similarly taught: With regard to **one who strikes another** with heavy blows, **and** doctors **assessed** that **he would die** as a result of the beating, **but his** health **improved from what it was,** so that they then determined that he would not die from his injuries, and **afterward** his condition **worsened and he died,** the one who struck him is **liable** to receive court-imposed capital punishment, as it is assumed that the victim's death was caused by the assault. **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** He is **exempt, because there is a basis for** anticipating **the matter.** Since the victim began to recover during his illness, it is reasonable to assume that his death was caused by a factor other than the assault.

The final *mishnah* brought in this cluster — again for *raglayim ladavar* — addresses a capital case: *hamakeh chavero* — one who strikes another, *ve'amduhu lemitah* — and the victim is assessed as likely to die from the wound. The striker faces potential execution for murder if the victim dies.

### Recovery & Relapse

*Vehekel mimah shehaya* — but then the victim improves. The wound appeared mortal, the prognosis seemed clear, but the person begins to recover. Does this improvement change the legal analysis? Then: *v'le'achar mikan hichbid umet* — after the initial improvement, the victim deteriorates and ultimately dies. Does the death still trace back to the original wound?

### Death Attributed

The first opinion: *chayav* — the striker is liable. The fact that the victim temporarily improved does not break the causal chain. The injury was declared mortal at the outset, and the victim ultimately died. The improvement is simply part of the natural and unpredictable course of a fatal wound, and the death is attributed to the original act.

### Different Cause

*Rebbi Nechemiah omer patur* — Rabbi Nechemiah says he is exempt. The improvement, in Rabbi Nechemiah's view, is itself legally significant evidence. It is *raglayim ladavar* — circumstantial evidence — that the original wound was not in fact fatal; something else intervened to cause the ultimate death, and one cannot attribute the death to the original striker.

### Circumstantial Evidence

This use of *raglayim ladavar* is distinct from the earlier applications. In the graveyard case, *raglayim ladavar* referred to positive evidence justifying ongoing suspicion. In the *zav* case, it referred to an established condition that governed interpretation of subsequent ambiguous events. Here, Rabbi Nechemiah uses *raglayim ladavar* to mean that the circumstantial evidence — the fact of improvement — points away from the original wound as the cause of death. The improvement gives *raglayim* — legs, grounds — to the inference that the original wound was survivable, and therefore the striker is exempt. The *tanna kamma*, by contrast, reads the circumstances as confirming the original assessment: the victim was fatally wounded and ultimately died, with the improvement being an interlude that does not change the fundamental trajectory.
