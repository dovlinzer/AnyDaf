# Sanhedrin 46b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Burial & Cemeteries

### Burial Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הָיוּ קוֹבְרִין אוֹתוֹ בְּקִבְרוֹת אֲבוֹתָיו, אֶלָּא שְׁתֵּי בָּתֵּי קְבָרוֹת הָיוּ מְתוּקָּנִין לְבֵית דִּין: אַחַת לַנֶּהֱרָגִין וְלַנֶּחְנָקִין, וְאַחַת לַנִּסְקָלִין וְלַנִּשְׂרָפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **They would not bury him in his ancestral** burial **plot. Rather, two graveyards were established for** the burial of those executed by **the court: One for those who were killed** by decapitation **or strangled, and one for those who were stoned or burned.**

The *mishna* at the close of 46a addresses the post-execution treatment of the condemned. After carrying out the death penalty, the court would not bury the executed person in the family cemetery. Instead, two separate graveyards were maintained: one for those executed by the more stringent forms of execution, and one for those executed by the more lenient forms. This distinction ensures that even in death, the gradations of punishment are preserved, and that those who violated the law in the most severe ways are not interred alongside family members.

### Bone Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְעַכֵּל הַבָּשָׂר, מְלַקְּטִין אֶת הָעֲצָמוֹת וְקוֹבְרִין אוֹתָן בִּמְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** Once **the flesh** of the deceased **had decomposed, they would gather his bones and bury them in their** proper **place** in his ancestral burial plot.

The *mishna* further states: *nis'achal habasar, m'laktin et ha'atzamot v'kovrin otan bimkoman* — once the flesh had decomposed, they would gather the bones and rebury them in the family plot. This practice of *likut atzamot* — the secondary collection and reburial of bones after decomposition — was common in that era more broadly, and the *mishna* here specifies that it was available even for the executed criminal, allowing eventual return to the family burial ground once the flesh had run its course.

### Relatives & Mourning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַקְּרוֹבִים בָּאִים וְשׁוֹאֲלִים בִּשְׁלוֹם הַדַּיָּינִין וּבִשְׁלוֹם הָעֵדִים, כְּלוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין בְּלִבֵּנוּ עֲלֵיכֶם שֶׁדִּין אֱמֶת דַּנְתֶּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** soon after the execution, **the relatives** of the executed transgressor **would come and inquire about the welfare of the judges and about the welfare of the witnesses, as if to say: We hold no** grudges **against you, as you judged a true judgment.**

The *mishna* then describes a striking social ritual: *v'hakerovim ba'im v'sho'alim bishlom hadayanim uvishlom ha'edim* — the relatives of the executed person would come and greet the judges and witnesses, as if to say, "We hold no grudge; we believe this judgment was just." This practice served to protect the honor of the court and to prevent lingering communal strife and resentment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הָיוּ מִתְאַבְּלִין, אֲבָל אוֹנְנִין, שֶׁאֵין אֲנִינוּת אֶלָּא בַּלֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the relatives of the executed man **would not mourn** him with the observance of the usual mourning rites, so that his unmourned death would atone for his transgression; **but they would grieve** over his passing, **since grief is** felt **only in the heart.**

Regarding mourning, the *mishna* rules that the relatives would not observe *aveilus* for the executed person, but they would be *onenim*. The distinction is important. *Aninut* — the acute state of inward devastation — is a feeling, and as the *mishna* concludes: *she'ein aninut ela balev*, *aninut* is only in the heart. No one can legislate emotion, and the family may naturally feel the weight of the loss. But *aveilus* — the formal, public practice of mourning — is an expression of honor to the deceased, and that the family may not perform. Rashi frames this positively: the relatives refrain from *aveilus* not because the executed person is beneath its dignity, but because the absence of *aveilus* itself constitutes an additional disgrace, and that disgrace functions as *kapara* — atonement — for the condemned.

## Execution Procedures

### Hanging Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר "חֵטְא וְתָלִיתָ", הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר תּוֹלִין אוֹתוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהַמַּלְכוּת עוֹשָׂה. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְהוּמָת וְתָלִיתָ" – מְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ תּוֹלִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Were it stated:** And if a man has committed **a sin** worthy of death **you shall hang** him on a tree, **I would have said** that first **they hang him and** only **afterward they put him to death, the way the** gentile **government does,** executing the transgressor by hanging. Therefore, **the verse states:** "And if a man has committed a sin worthy of death, **and he is put to death, and you shall hang** him on a tree" (Deuteronomy 21:22), teaching that first **they put him to death, and** only **afterward they hang him.**

A *baraisa* clarifies what the Torah means when it speaks of hanging. One might read the verse *v'talita v'humat* as mandating crucifixion in the Roman manner — hang the person and the hanging itself kills him. The *baraisa* corrects this: *talmud lomar v'humat v'talita* — first you put the person to death by the court's prescribed method, *and then* you hang the body. The hanging is a post-mortem act, not the execution itself.

### Sunset Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? מְשַׁהִין אוֹתוֹ עַד סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה, וְגוֹמְרִין אֶת דִּינוֹ, וּמְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ תּוֹלִין אוֹתוֹ. אֶחָד קוֹשֵׁר וְאֶחָד מַתִּיר, כְּדֵי לְקַיֵּים מִצְוַת תְּלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **How so? They delay** the verdict **until** it is **near** to **sunset, and** then **they conclude his judgment, and they put him to death, and** immediately **afterward hang him. One ties** him to the hanging post, **and another** immediately **unties** him, **in order to fulfill the mitzva of hanging** the corpse of the executed transgressor.

The *baraisa* continues: *mashhinin oto ad samuch lishki'at hachama* — you wait until close to nightfall before completing and announcing the final judgment. This seems puzzling at first glance, since *halanat hadin* — postponing the verdict — is itself forbidden. The resolution is that the prohibition of *halanat hadin* applies after the judgment has been formally declared; what is being delayed here is the moment of public announcement and execution, not the deliberative process. And the reason for timing it so close to sunset becomes clear from Rashi: if you hang the body in the morning, there is every reason to be complacent about taking it down — hours remain before nightfall, and, like the woman in the parable who had but one task and never accomplished it precisely because she had all day, the duty to remove the body might be forgotten or procrastinated until it becomes nightfall without having acted. *Shema yitrashlu bikvuraso veyavo lidei shikcha* — perhaps they will be negligent about burial and it will be forgotten. By timing the execution to occur just before nightfall, you guarantee urgency: the body must come down immediately.

### Body Removal

The urgency is codified directly: *echad koveish v'echad matir* — one person ties and another unties simultaneously, or in immediate succession. The hanging becomes the most perfunctory of acts, done purely to fulfill the technical *mitzvat teliyah*, with none of the punitive public display that ordinarily accompanies hanging. The Rabbis are quite openly reshaping what the broader purpose of hanging might be. Just as the Rabbis found ways to make capital punishment nearly impossible to carry out, here too the logic of *kilalat Elokim taluy* — that it is a disgrace before God to leave a body hanging — drives the entire procedure toward minimizing the duration of exposure as much as possible. The timing close to sunset is designed not merely to avoid transgressing the prohibition of leaving the body overnight but to reduce the period of hanging to the absolute minimum, even if that creates complications around *inuy hadin* and the possibility of a last-minute reversal while taking the condemned out for execution.

## Source & Ground

### Tree vs. Post

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "עֵץ" – שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי בֵּין בְּתָלוּשׁ בֵּין בִּמְחוּבָּר. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "כִּי קָבוֹר" – מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּסָּר אֶלָּא קְבוּרָה, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁמְחוּסָּר קְצִיצָה וּקְבוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** From the verse: "And you shall hang him on **a tree," I** would **derive** that the body may be hung **either** on a tree that has been **detached** from the ground **or** on one that is still **attached** to the ground. Therefore, **the verse states:** "His body shall not remain all night upon the tree, **but you shall bury** him [***kavor*** *tikberennu*] that day" (Deuteronomy 21:23). Based on the doubled verb, it is derived that not only must the transgressor's body be buried, but the tree on which it is hung must also be buried. As the verse employs the same term to instruct that both must be buried, the verse teaches that the corpse must be hung on a tree **that** has already been detached from the ground and **is lacking only burial,** just as the corpse is lacking only burial. This serves to **exclude** hanging the corpse on a tree **that** is still attached to the ground and **is lacking** both **cutting down and burial.**

A second *baraisa* addresses the biblical text's use of the word *etz* — wood or tree. One might think that the verse permits hanging on either a detached beam or a tree still connected to the ground. The *baraisa* derives from the juxtaposition of *ki kavor tikberenu* — you shall surely bury him — that the instrument of hanging must be such that burial can follow immediately afterward. This means: *mi she'eino mechusar ela kevurah* — something that requires nothing further before burial can occur. A tree connected to the ground requires cutting it down first; that intermediate act disqualifies it. The Sages hold that simply uprooting a stake from the ground — *tlishah* — is trivial and does not count as an intervening step, so a post embedded in the ground would still be permitted. Rabbi Yosi goes further, maintaining that even pulling a stake from the ground is a meaningful act, which would seem to leave one uncertain about what method he would endorse, as the *gemara* notes without resolution.

### Twin Brothers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אוֹמֵר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מָשְׁלוּ מָשָׁל, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לִשְׁנֵי אַחִים תְּאוֹמִים בְּעִיר אַחַת. אֶחָד מִינּוּהוּ מֶלֶךְ, וְאֶחָד יָצָא לְלִיסְטִיּוּת. צִוָּה הַמֶּלֶךְ וּתְלָאוּהוּ. כׇּל הָרוֹאֶה אוֹתוֹ אוֹמֵר: הַמֶּלֶךְ תָּלוּי. צִוָּה הַמֶּלֶךְ וְהוֹרִידוּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir says:** The Sages **told a parable: To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to two brothers** who were **twins** and lived **in the same city. One was appointed king, while the other went out to** engage in **banditry. The king commanded** that his brother be punished, **and they hanged** his twin brother for his crimes. **Anyone who saw** the bandit hanging **would say: The king** was **hanged. The king,** therefore, **commanded** that his brother be taken down, **and they took** the bandit **down.**

The *gemara* then cites a *tanya* presenting a parable from Rabbi Meir that expands and deepens the concept of *kilalat Elokim taluy*. Until this point, the phrase might have been read narrowly — the one hanged is the blasphemer, who is executed *because* he cursed God. Rabbi Meir offers a broader reading: *mashal l'mah hadavar domeh l'shnei achim te'omim b'ir achat* — consider two twin brothers in the same city. One becomes king; the other becomes a bandit. The king orders his twin brother hanged. Anyone who passes and sees the body hanging remarks: the king is hanged. The visual identification is so complete that the dignified twin is implicated in the indignity of the condemned one. So too, human beings are created in the image of God. When a human body hangs in disgrace, it is as though the divine image itself is on display in humiliation. This is what *kilalat Elokim taluy* means in its fullest sense. God, as it were, commanded that the condemned be executed — just as the king commanded his brother's death — but the ongoing presence of the hanging body is a disgrace to the image in which all human beings are formed.

### God's Anguish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלוֹמַר: מִפְּנֵי מָה זֶה תָּלוּי? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבֵּירַךְ אֶת הַשֵּׁם, וְנִמְצָא שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם מִתְחַלֵּל. אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָדָם מִצְטַעֵר, שְׁכִינָה מָה לָשׁוֹן אוֹמֶרֶת? "קַלַּנִי מֵרֹאשִׁי, קַלַּנִי מִזְּרוֹעִי".
>
> **Translation:** **That is to say:** Were the dead man's corpse to remain hanging, reminding everyone of his transgression, people would ask: **For what** reason **was this one hung?** They would be answered: **Because he blessed** God, a euphemism for blasphemy, and the name of Heaven would be desecrated. **Rabbi Meir said:** The phrase "for he that is hung is a curse [*kilelat*] of God" should be understood as follows: **When a man suffers** in the wake of his sin, **what expression does the Divine Presence use? I am distressed [*kallani*] about My head, I am distressed about My arm,** meaning, I, too, suffer when the wicked are punished.

The *mishna* itself contains the *derasha*: *kalani me-roshi, kalani mi-zero'i* — God, as it were, says: my head is heavy upon me, my arm is heavy upon me. This anthropomorphizing language — God speaking of his own distress — intensifies the identification between the divine and the human being made in the divine image. When the hanged body remains on display, God experiences anguish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר "קַל לֵית". אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִם כֵּן, "כָּבֵד עָלַי רֹאשִׁי" "כָּבֵד עָלַי זְרוֹעִי" מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר "קִיל לִי עָלְמָא".
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is this **inferred?** How does Rabbi Meir understand the word *kilelat*? **Abaye says:** When a man is hung after he is put to death, God is **like one who said: I am not light [*kal leit*],** meaning: My head is heavy for Me, My arm is heavy for Me. God is in distress when He has to administer punishment. **Rava said to him: If so, he should have** said explicitly: **My head is heavy for Me, My arm is heavy for Me. Rather, Rava said:** When a man is hung after he is put to death, God is **like one who said: The world is light for me [*kil li alma*],** meaning: I am light, and therefore the world is heavy for Me, and I am in distress.

The *gemara* then parses the precise wordplay underlying *kilalat Elokim*. Abaye reads *kilalat* as suggesting that things are light — *kales kales* — which is taken to mean that things that should feel weighty now feel insubstantial, conveying heaviness through irony. Rava objects: if that is the intent, the verse should have said *kaveda alai roshi*, "my head is heavy" — why speak of lightness? Rava instead reads it as *ki alima* — the world feels light, which is likewise an expression of existential heaviness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵהּ? אִם כֵּן, נֵימָא קְרָא "מְקַלֵּל". מַאי "קִלְלַת"? וְאֵימָא: כּוּלֵּיהּ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? אִם כֵּן, נֵימָא קְרָא "קַלַּת". מַאי "קִלְלַת"? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** word "*kilelat*" **is needed for** what it **itself** teaches, namely that a blasphemer is hung after he has been stoned. How, then, can it be interpreted as alluding to God's distress at the death of a transgressor? The Gemara answers: **If so, the verse should have stated: One who curses [*mekallel* ]. What** is the meaning of ***kilelat*?** It serves to teach the statement taught by Rabbi Meir. The Gemara asks: If so, **say** perhaps that **the entire** verse **comes for this** purpose, to underscore the dignity of the transgressor, who was created in God's image, and not to teach the *halakha* governing a blasphemer. The Gemara responds: **If so, the verse should have stated: Lightness [*kilat*]. What** is the meaning of ***kilelat*? Conclude two** conclusions **from it:** Conclude that the blasphemer is hung after he has been stoned, and conclude that God is distressed at the death of a transgressor.

The *gemara* then raises the question of how much work this play on *kilalat* is doing: the verse needs at minimum to establish that a blasphemer is the paradigm of the one who is hanged. Perhaps the entire verse is consumed by that teaching alone. The *gemara* replies that if that were the sole purpose, the verse would have said simply *mekallel*; the double *lamed* of *kilalat* yields two teachings simultaneously — the blasphemer, and God's anguish. Throughout this passage, Chazal give pointed expression to their profound discomfort with the entire practice of hanging.

## Universal Burial

### Biblical Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא זוֹ בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא כָּל הַמֵּלִין אֶת מֵתוֹ עוֹבֵר בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. הֱלִינוֹ לִכְבוֹדוֹ, לְהָבִיא לוֹ אָרוֹן וְתַכְרִיכִים – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מִנַּיִן לַמֵּלִין אֶת מֵתוֹ שֶׁעוֹבֵר עָלָיו בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "כִּי קָבוֹר תִּקְבְּרֶנּוּ".
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Sages **said not only this,** that an executed transgressor must be buried on the same day that he is killed, **but** they said that **anyone who leaves his deceased** relative **overnight** without burying him **transgresses a prohibition.** But if **he left** the deceased **overnight for the sake of** the deceased's **honor,** e.g., **to bring a coffin or shrouds** for his burial, **he does not transgress** the prohibition against leaving him unburied overnight. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yoḥai: From where** is it derived **that one who leaves his deceased** relative **overnight** without burying him **transgresses a prohibition? The verse states: "But you shall bury him [*kavor tikberennu*]"** (Deuteronomy 21:23), doubling the verb for emphasis.

The *gemara* broadens the discussion: *v'lo zu bilvad* — not only the executed criminal must be buried promptly; the prohibition extends to any corpse. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rashbi derives this from the verse *ki kavor tikberenu bayom hahu* — you shall surely bury him on that day — reading *bayom hahu* not as limited to the executed criminal but as establishing a general norm: leaving any body unburied overnight constitutes a violation of a *lo ta'aseh*. The *gemara* then pivots from the prohibition against leaving the body overnight to a more fundamental question: where does the obligation to bury at all come from?

### Shapur's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ שַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא לְרַב חָמָא: קְבוּרָה מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִין? אִישְׁתִּיק וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי. אֲמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: אִימְּסַר עָלְמָא בִּידָא דְּטַפְשָׁאֵי, דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר "כִּי קָבוֹר".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **King Shapur,** the monarch of Persia, once **said to Rav Ḥama: From where in the Torah is there a hint to** the mitzva of **burial?** What proof is there that the dead must be buried and not treated in some other manner? Rav Ḥama **was silent and said nothing to him,** as he could not find a suitable source. **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: The world has been handed over to the foolish, as** Rav Ḥama **should have said** to King Shapur that the mitzva of burial is derived from the verse: **"But you shall bury** him" (Deuteronomy 21:23).

The *gemara* reports that the Persian king Shapur posed this challenge to Rav Chama: *kvurah min haTorah minayin* — where does the Torah require burial? In Zoroastrian practice, bodies were left exposed in the open. Rav Chama was silent — he had no ready answer. Rav Acha bar Yaakov expresses astonishment: *imsar alma b'yadei d'tifshai* — has the world been handed over to fools? Could Rav Chama not cite *ki kavor tikberenu*? The *gemara* answers that Shapur could have deflected even that: perhaps *kavor tikberenu* only specifies *how* you handle a body — by placing it in a box — rather than mandating burial in the ground. The doubling of the root could mean burial *properly*, not burial *in earth*, and Shapur would not have been persuaded by the double language alone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּלֶיעֱבֵד לֵיהּ אָרוֹן. "תִּקְבְּרֶנּוּ"! לָא מַשְׁמַע לֵיהּ. וְנֵימָא: מִדְּאִיקְּבוּר צַדִּיקֵי? מִנְהֲגָא בְּעָלְמָא! מִדְּקַבְרֵיהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה? דְּלָא לִישְׁתַּנֵּי מִמִּנְהֲגָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In that case, King Shapur could have replied that the verse merely proves **that a coffin should be made for** the deceased so that he can be placed in it, not that the deceased should be buried in the ground, as the verse could be understood as instructing that the corpse be placed in some sort of receptacle, not in the ground. The Gemara challenges: Rav Ḥama could still have claimed that the mitzva of burial is derived from the doubled verb "you shall **bury him** [*kavor **tikberennu*****]."** The Gemara answers: In that case, King Shapur could have replied that **he does not learn** anything from a doubled verb, which seems to be merely a stylistic choice and not the source of a new *halakha*. The Gemara asks: But let Rav Ḥama **say** that the mitzva to bury the dead is derived **from** the fact **that the righteous** forefathers, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, **were** all **buried.** The Gemara answers: King Shapur could have said that this was **merely a custom** of the time, but not a mitzva. The Gemara asks: Rav Ḥama could have derived the mitzva **from** the fact **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, buried Moses,** which proves that this is the proper way to handle the dead. The Gemara answers: King Shapur could still have said that God acted in this manner **in order not to deviate from the** general **custom,** but this does not prove that burying the dead is a mitzva.

Attempts to derive the obligation from the practice of the *tzaddikim* — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Sarah, Rebecca, Rachel and Leah — all buried in the ground — are dismissed: that merely reflects *minhaga be'alma*, ancient custom, not religious obligation. Even the fact that God Himself buried Moshe proves only that God did not want Moshe to be treated differently from common practice. None of these demonstrations establishes a *chiyuv*.

### Doubling of Bury

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי, רֶמֶז לִקְבוּרָה מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "כִּי קָבוֹר תִּקְבְּרֶנּוּ". מִכָּאן רֶמֶז לִקְבוּרָה מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yoḥai: From where in the Torah is there a hint to** the mitzva of **burial? The verse states: "But you shall bury him [*kavor tikberennu*],"** doubling the verb for emphasis. **From here there is a hint to** the mitzva of **burial in the Torah.**

The result is that the *gemara* can only offer *remez l'kvurah min haTorah* — a *hint* in the Torah — rather than an explicit commandment. The word *remez* here is not merely rhetorical modesty; it is a halachically significant categorization.

### Rabbinic Obligation

This brings us to a striking conclusion noted by Rabbeinu Chananel in his commentary to 47a. After canvassing all of the attempted proofs and finding them inadequate to convince even Shapur, Rabbeinu Chananel states plainly: *mitzvah zo miderabbanan hi* — this *mitzvah* is rabbinic in origin, not biblical. The general assumption of halachic discourse is that there is a *de'oraita* obligation of *kevurah* and a *lo ta'aseh de'oraita* of leaving a body unburied overnight. Rabbeinu Chananel appears to read the *gemara* as concluding otherwise. One might add that the *remez* language could reflect a different limitation as well: perhaps the verse in context is speaking specifically about the executed criminal and the need to prevent the ongoing *bizayon* of hanging, rather than articulating a free-standing universal obligation of burial. What precisely constitutes a *de'oraita* obligation of *kevurah*, and whether burial specifically in the earth — as opposed to some other form — is itself biblically mandated, remains a genuinely open question that this *sugya* does not resolve with finality.

## Purpose of Burial

### Disgrace vs. Atonement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: קְבוּרָה מִשּׁוּם בִּזְיוֹנָא הוּא, אוֹ מִשּׁוּם כַּפָּרָה הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Is burial** obligatory **on account of disgrace,** i.e., so that the deceased should not suffer the disgrace of being left exposed as his body begins to decompose, **or is it on account of atonement,** i.e., so that the deceased will achieve atonement by being returned to the ground from which he was formed?

The *gemara* now poses a question that, as the analysis unfolds, reveals considerable halachic depth: *kevurah mishum bizyona hu o mishum kapara?* — is the purpose of burial to protect the body from disgrace, or to provide atonement for the deceased? Rashi notes that the *bizayon* at stake is not merely relative — not simply that burial is the norm and its absence therefore a deviation. It is also that a body left unburied decomposes visibly and becomes repulsive, producing an experience of degradation for all who witness it.

### Refusal Implications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? דְּאָמַר: לָא בָּעֵינָא דְּלִיקְבְּרוּהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא. אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם בִּזְיוֹנָא הוּא – לָא כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם כַּפָּרָה הוּא – הָא אָמַר: לָא בָּעֵינָא כַּפָּרָה. מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **difference** that arises from knowing the reason that burial is necessary? The Gemara answers: There is a difference in a case **where** one **said** before he died: **I do not want them to bury that man,** i.e., myself. **If you say** that burial **is** required **on account of disgrace, it is not in his** power to waive his own burial, as his family shares in the disgrace. **But if you say** that burial **is** required **on account of atonement, didn't he** effectively **say: I do not want atonement,** and with regard to himself one should be able to do as he wishes? **What,** then, is the *halakha*?

The practical difference between the two rationales is sharpest when the deceased has expressed a preference not to be buried: *de'amar lo be'ina likvura le'hahu gavra*. If burial is to prevent *bizayon*, then the deceased has no standing to waive it — *lav kol kimeineih* — because the concern is not his alone. It implicates the family who survives him, and more broadly, it implicates the dignity of the divine image instantiated in every human body. One cannot be *mochel* on a *bizayon* that is not entirely one's own to waive. But if the reason is *kapara* — atonement for the soul — and the person declares *lo be'ina kapara*, I do not want atonement, does he then have the ability to waive it?

This is a striking methodological problem in its own right. If *kevurah* is *de'oraita*, the standard principle of *lo darshinan ta'ama de'kra* — we do not allow the rationale behind a commandment to exempt individuals from its application — would seem to foreclose any *nafka mina* based on motive. The *poskim* address this tension directly. Some commentators see in this very analysis a further indication supporting Rabbeinu Chananel's view that the obligation may be rabbinic, since a *de'oraita* obligation would not typically be undone by identifying its purpose and finding it inapplicable to a particular case.

### Righteous Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מִדְּאִיקְּבוּר צַדִּיקֵי, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם כַּפָּרָה – צַדִּיקֵי לְכַפָּרָה צְרִיכִי? אִין, דִּכְתִיב: "אָדָם אֵין צַדִּיק בָּאָרֶץ אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂה טּוֹב וְלֹא יֶחֱטָא".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof **from** the fact **that the righteous** patriarchs, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, **were** all **buried. And if you say** that burial is required **on account of atonement, do the righteous need atonement?** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Yes,** even the righteous are in need of atonement, **as it is written:** "For **there is no righteous person on earth who does good and never sins"** (Ecclesiastes 7:20), and so even the righteous need atonement for the few sins that they committed over the course of their lifetimes.

One might ask: if burial is for *kapara*, why are *tzaddikim* buried? Do the righteous require atonement? The *gemara* answers with the verse: *adam ein tzaddik ba'aretz asher ya'aseh tov v'lo yecheta* — there is no one so righteous on earth who does only good and never sins. Even the greatest *tzaddik* has some need of *kapara*, and burial provides it.

## Eulogies

### Hesped Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הֶסְפֵּידָא, יְקָרָא דְּחָיֵי הָוֵי אוֹ יְקָרָא דְּשָׁכְבֵי הָוֵי? לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? דְּאָמַר: לָא תִּסְפְּדוּהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא. אִי נָמֵי, לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִיּוֹרְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Is the eulogy** delivered for **the honor of the living** relatives of the deceased, **or is it** delivered for **the honor of the dead?** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **difference** between the two possible reasons? The Gemara answers: There is a difference in a case **where** one **said** before he died: **Do not eulogize that man,** i.e., myself. If the eulogy is delivered to honor the deceased, he is able to forgo this honor, but if it is delivered to honor the living, he is not, as it is not in the power of one individual to forgo the honor of others. **Alternately,** the difference is with regard to whether it is possible **to collect** the eulogist's fee **from the heirs.** If the eulogy is to honor the dead, it is possible to collect this fee from the heirs, even against their will, but if it is to honor the living, they are able to forgo this honor.

The *gemara* raises a parallel question regarding the *hesped*: *yikara de'chayei havei o yikara de'shichvei havei?* — is the eulogy for the honor of the living, or the honor of the dead? It is worth noting at the outset that *hesped* in the Talmudic context is not simply a formal oration praising the deceased. It was performative — mourners would wail, *mekonenot* would compose elegies — and it was understood to be a powerful social ritual. The question of whose honor it serves is thus not merely theoretical.

### Abraham & Sarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: "וַיָּבֹא אַבְרָהָם לִסְפֹּד לְשָׂרָה וְלִבְכֹּתָהּ". וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם יְקָרָא דְּחַיֵּי הוּא, מִשּׁוּם יְקָרָא דְּאַבְרָהָם מְשַׁהוּ לַהּ לְשָׂרָה? שָׂרָה גּוּפַהּ נִיחָא לָהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּמִיַּיקַּר בַּהּ אַבְרָהָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the verse that states: **"And Abraham came to eulogize Sarah and weep over her"** (Genesis 23:2), indicating that Sarah's funeral was delayed until Abraham returned from Beersheba to Hebron to eulogize her. **And if you say** that a eulogy is delivered **due to the honor of the living, would they have** unduly **delayed** burying **Sarah due to Abraham's honor?** The Gemara rejects this argument: **It was satisfactory to Sarah herself** that her funeral was delayed **so that Abraham could be honored by** eulogizing **her.** Since Sarah herself would prefer that Abraham eulogize her, there was no disgrace in waiting for Abraham to arrive.

The *gemara* brings its first proof: *va-yavo Avraham lispod le-Sarah v'livkotah* — Abraham came to eulogize Sarah and to weep for her. If the *hesped* is for the honor of the living, why was the burial of Sarah postponed until Abraham arrived? Delaying burial — which creates its own concerns of *bizayon* to the body — can only be justified for the sake of the deceased. The *gemara* deflects this proof: *Sarah gufa nicha lah ki heichi de'miyaker be'Avraham* — Sarah herself would have wanted this, because she would have wished Abraham to receive the honor that comes to those whose loved ones are properly eulogized. The *hesped* is still framed as being in service of the living, but the justification for postponing the burial is that the *meis* herself desired this honor for her survivors.

### Yerovam's Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: "וְסָפְדוּ לוֹ כׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל וְקָבְרוּ אֹתוֹ". וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם יְקָרָא דְּחַיֵּי הוּא, הָנָךְ בְּנֵי יְקָרָא נִינְהוּ? נִיחָא לְהוּ לְצַדִּיקַיָּא דְּמִיַּיקְּרִי בְּהוּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a different resolution of this dilemma from the verse referring to Abijah, son of Jeroboam: **"And all Israel shall eulogize him and bury him"** (I Kings 14:13). **And if you say** that a eulogy **is** delivered **due to the honor of the living, are these** people, Jeroboam's surviving family, **worthy of** this **honor?** The Gemara answers: **It is satisfactory to the righteous when** other **people are honored through them.** Since that is their wish, they are eulogized even if their wicked relatives are honored as a result.

The *gemara* next cites the case of Aviyah ben Yerovam: only this one among the descendants of Yerovam would be properly buried and eulogized, the others being left unburied as punishment for the sins of their lineage. If *hesped* is for the honor of the living, why would the surviving family — themselves *resha'im* — be denied that honor? They need the *hesped* as much as anyone. The *gemara* answers: *nicha leh le'tzaddika de'miyakru beih inshi* — the righteous young man himself would have wanted to be properly honored, and for his sake the *hesped* is done, even though his surviving relatives are unworthy. The honor flows toward the living — that is what *hesped* does — but the motivation for giving it can derive from what the deceased would have wanted.

### Chizkiyahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר לָהֶן: אַל תִּסְפְּדוּנִי בָּעֲיָירוֹת. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ יְקָרָא דְחַיֵּי, מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינַּהּ? קָסָבַר: לִיתְיַיקְּרוּ בֵּיהּ יִשְׂרָאֵל טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: Before he died, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** his disciples: **Do not eulogize me in the** small **towns** that you pass as you take my body out for burial, but eulogize me only in the larger cities. **And if you say** that a eulogy is delivered **in honor of the living, what difference does it make to him** if he is eulogized also in the smaller towns? The Gemara answers: **He thought** that the people of **Israel would be more** greatly **honored through him** if they gathered together for the eulogies in the larger cities.

The discussion expands beyond *hesped* to encompass all funeral proceedings. The case of Chizkiyahu is invoked: *shegirer atzmos aviv al mita shel chavalim* — Chizkiyahu dragged the bones of his father Menashe on a bed of ropes when Menashe died, thereby actively disgracing the body, in order to bring *kapara* for his wicked father. If funeral proceedings are for the honor of the living, why would Chizkiyahu do something that reflects so badly on himself and on the Jewish people? The *gemara* answers that *Yisrael gufaihu neicha lehu d'michli yeikara'ihu legabei* — Israel themselves were willing to forgo their own honor for the sake of the *kapara* that their king would thereby achieve.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, in his final instructions, directed that his bier not be stopped in the small villages as it was carried from Tzippori to Beit She'arim. If *hesped* is for the honor of the living, the people of those small towns might benefit from the opportunity to eulogize Rabbi. Why would Rabbi deny them that? The *gemara* answers: *nefisha behu Yisrael tfei* — the aggregate honor of all of Israel is greater when large crowds gather in major cities than when dispersed efforts are made village by village. Even according to the view that *hesped* honors the living, Rabbi's instruction was an expression of that same principle — more honor to more people through concentration.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הֱלִינוֹ לִכְבוֹדוֹ, לְשַׁמֵּעַ עָלָיו עֲיָירוֹת, לְהָבִיא לוֹ מְקוֹנְנוֹת, לְהָבִיא לוֹ אָרוֹן וְתַכְרִיכִין – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו, שֶׁכׇּל הָעוֹשֶׂה אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לִכְבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל מֵת. הָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל הָעוֹשֶׂה לִכְבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל חַי, אֵין בּוֹ בִּזָּיוֹן לַמֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If **one left** his deceased relative unburied **overnight for the sake of his honor,** for example, in order **to assemble** the people from **the** neighboring **towns for** the funeral, or **to bring him** professional **lamenters,** or **to bring him a coffin or shrouds, he does not transgress** the prohibition of "his body shall not remain all night," **as anyone who acts** in such a manner does so **only for** the sake of **honoring the dead.** This indicates that the eulogy and other funeral rites are performed to honor the deceased. The Gemara rejects this argument: **This** is what the *baraita* **is saying: Anyone who acts** in such a manner **for** the sake of **honoring the living** does not transgress the prohibition, as **there is no degradation of the dead.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: סִימָן יָפֶה לַמֵּת שֶׁנִּפְרָעִין מִמֶּנּוּ לְאַחַר מִיתָה. מֵת שֶׁלֹּא נִסְפַּד וְלֹא נִקְבַּר, אוֹ שֶׁחַיָּה גּוֹרַרְתּוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ גְּשָׁמִים מְזַלְּפִין עַל מִטָּתוֹ – זֶהוּ סִימָן יָפֶה לַמֵּת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: יְקָרָא דְשָׁכְבֵי הוּא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan says:** It is **a good sign for the deceased when he is punished after** his **death** and does not receive an honorable burial or eulogy, as his lack of honor brings him atonement for his sins. For example, if **the deceased was not eulogized, or** if **he was not buried, or** if **a wild animal dragged his** corpse, **or** if **rain fell on his bier, this is a good sign for the deceased. Learn from** the *baraita* that a eulogy **is** delivered for **the honor of the dead,** so that when he is deprived of this honor, he achieves atonement for his sins. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from** the *baraita* that this is so.

After all of these exchanges, the *gemara* arrives at a definitive conclusion from a *baraisa*: *kol ha-oseh eino ela lichvodo shel meis* — everything done in funeral proceedings is for the honor of the dead. The *gemara* then challenges this from the example where burial was postponed for a legitimate reason connected to the living: how can postponement be justified if everything is for the dead? The *gemara* responds: *kol ha-oseh lichvodo shel chai ein bo bizayon la-meis* — anything done for the honor of the living does not constitute *bizayon* to the dead. And a final proof arrives: *siman yafeh la-meis she-nifrain mimenu le-achar misah* — it is actually a good sign for the deceased when bad things happen to the body after death, such as no eulogy, no burial, or animals dragging the body, because these disgraceful occurrences bring *kapara*. Since these things are "good" by virtue of what they do for the *meis*, it is clear that everything surrounding the body is understood in terms of honor and disgrace to the dead, not to the living. *Shma minah yikara de-shachvei hu, shma minah* — this is the conclusion.

It is worth pausing to note that the *mishna*'s ruling — that relatives do not observe *aveilus* for the executed — now raises a related and unresolved question: whose honor is *aveilus* for? If, as we have now concluded, funeral proceedings honor the dead, then the prohibition of *aveilus* for the executed must be because *aveilus* too is an expression of honor for the deceased, honor that the executed does not yet deserve. This would also imply that a person who instructs his children not to sit *shiva* for him could in principle be obeyed, since *aveilus* is primarily in service of the dead. This question is discussed by the *poskim* and remains live.

## Postponement

### Disgrace Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל חַי מֵבִית לֵיהּ לְמֵת? אִין, כִּי אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: "לֹא תָלִין נִבְלָתוֹ עַל הָעֵץ", דּוּמְיָא דְּתָלוּי דְּאִית בֵּיהּ בִּזָּיוֹן. אֲבָל הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ בִּזָּיוֹן – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** can it be that **due to the honor of the living, they allow the deceased to remain** unburied **overnight?** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** as **when the Merciful One states: "His body shall not remain all night upon the tree,** but you shall bury him that day" (Deuteronomy 21:23), it teaches that the prohibition applies only to cases **similar to** that **of** a person whose body is **hung** after his death, **who suffers degradation** when his corpse is left hanging overnight. **But here, since** the deceased **does not suffer degradation** when the funeral is delayed, as the delay is in order that the burial will be performed with greater dignity, there is **no** violation of the prohibition, and he may be left unburied overnight.

The *gemara* now draws together the threads of the preceding analysis. Postponing burial is prohibited when it constitutes *bizayon* to the body. But *bizayon* is not an absolute category — it is contextual. The prohibition against leaving a body overnight, derived from the verse *lo talin nivlato al ha-etz*, applies *dumya de'talui d'it bei bizayon* — in circumstances resembling those of the hanged body, where there is genuine disgrace. Where burial is delayed for a legitimate purpose, and that purpose removes the character of neglect or contempt from the delay, there is no *bizayon* and therefore no prohibition.

### Legitimate Delays

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הֱלִינוֹ לִכְבוֹדוֹ, לְשַׁמֵּעַ עָלָיו עֲיָירוֹת, לְהָבִיא לוֹ מְקוֹנְנוֹת, לְהָבִיא לוֹ אָרוֹן וְתַכְרִיכִין – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: If **one left** his deceased relative unburied **overnight for the sake of his honor,** for example, in order **to assemble** the people from **the** neighboring **towns for** the funeral, or **to bring him** professional **lamenters,** or **to bring him a coffin or shrouds, he does not transgress** the prohibition of "his body shall not remain all night."

The *gemara* identifies specific categories of permissible delay: *lehavi lo aron v'tachrichin* — waiting to procure a coffin or burial shrouds; *lishmoa alav ayarot* — waiting so that surrounding towns can come to participate in the burial; *lehavi lo mekonenot* — waiting to bring wailers or eulogizers. In all of these cases, *eino over alav* — one does not violate the prohibition. The *gemara* makes explicit that the key principle is that any act done *lichvodo shel chai* — for the honor of the living — does not constitute *bizayon la-meis*, and thus any such purpose can justify postponement, without needing to be framed as what the deceased would have wanted.

This gives somewhat broader latitude than the framework articulated in the conclusion about *hesped*. There, the ultimate principle was that all funeral proceedings honor the dead. Here, the *gemara* justifies delay even for the living's honor, on the grounds that purposeful, dignified delay is not *bizayon*. The practical consequence is significant: one does not have to demonstrate that this is what the deceased would have wanted, or that it is directly for the deceased's honor. As long as there is a genuine reason and the delay is not mere neglect, it falls outside the prohibition.

## Atonement

### Abaye vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי סָבְרַתְּ מֵת מִתּוֹךְ רִשְׁעוֹ הָוְיָא לֵיהּ כַּפָּרָה? מֵת מִתּוֹךְ רִשְׁעוֹ לָא הָוְיָא לֵיהּ כַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **Do you maintain** that **one who dies in his** state of **wickedness** without repenting **achieves atonement?** This is not the case, as **one who dies in his** state of **wickedness** without repenting **does not achieve atonement.**

Abaye challenges the assumption: *mi savrat mes mitoch risho havei lei kapara?* — do you think dying in one's state of wickedness produces atonement? Abaye holds that *mes mitoch risho lo havei lei kapara* — death occurring while the person is still in their unrepentant, unatoned state does not bring atonement.

### Death Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: מִי קָא מְדַמֵּית נֶהֱרָג מִתּוֹךְ רִשְׁעוֹ לְמֵת מִתּוֹךְ רִשְׁעוֹ? מֵת מִתּוֹךְ רִשְׁעוֹ, כֵּיוָן דְּכִי אוֹרְחֵיהּ קָמָיֵית – לָא הָוְיָא לֵיהּ כַּפָּרָה. נֶהֱרָג מִתּוֹךְ רִשְׁעוֹ, כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו כִּי אוֹרְחֵיהּ מָיֵית – הָוְיָא לֵיהּ כַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Abaye: **Are you comparing one who was killed in his** state of **wickedness to one who died in his** state of **wickedness?** An unrepentant sinner **who died in his** state of **wickedness does not achieve atonement, since he died a natural death** and there was nothing to bring about his atonement. But a transgressor **who was killed in his** state of **wickedness achieves atonement,** even without repentance, **since he did not die a natural death,** but rather he was executed.

Rava responds with a crucial distinction: one must not conflate dying naturally in a state of wickedness with being executed. *Mes mitoch risho ki orchei ka mayes, lo havei lei kapara* — natural death occurring in the ordinary course of things does not confer atonement; the mere biological fact of dying is not redemptive. But *neherag mitoch risho, keivan d'lav ki orchei mayes, havei lei kapara* — execution, which is not the natural course of events, does constitute *kapara*, because the violent, imposed quality of the death itself is the redemptive mechanism. Notably, the *gemara* does not at this point require *viduy* or *teshuvah* as prerequisites; the act of being executed by itself appears sufficient. Rava also observes that the category of *neherag* would appear to include violent death more broadly — not only judicial execution.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּדַּע, דִּכְתִיב: מִזְמוֹר לְאָסָף אֱלֹהִים בָּאוּ גוֹיִם בְּנַחֲלָתֶךָ טִמְּאוּ אֶת הֵיכַל קׇדְשֶׁךָ [וְגוֹ׳] נָתְנוּ אֶת נִבְלַת עֲבָדֶיךָ מַאֲכָל לְעוֹף הַשָּׁמָיִם בְּשַׂר חֲסִידֶיךָ לְחַיְתוֹ אָרֶץ. מַאי "עֲבָדֶיךָ" וּמַאי "חֲסִידֶיךָ"? לָאו "חֲסִידֶיךָ" – חֲסִידֶיךָ מַמָּשׁ, "עֲבָדֶיךָ" – הָנָךְ דִּמְחַיְּיבִי דִּינָא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא, וְכֵיוָן דְּאִיקְּטוּל קָרֵי לְהוּ "עֲבָדֶיךָ".
>
> **Translation:** **Know** that this is so, **as it is written: "A song to Asaf. O God, nations are come into Your inheritance; they have defiled your Holy Temple;** they have laid Jerusalem in heaps. **The dead bodies of Your servants they have given to be food to the birds of the sky, the flesh of Your pious ones to the beasts of the earth"** (Psalms 79:1–2). **What** is the meaning of **"Your servants" and what** is the meaning of **"Your pious ones"? Is it not** so that the term **"Your pious ones"** is referring to **literally, Your pious ones,** those who had always feared God; whereas **"Your servants"** is referring to **those who had initially been liable to** receive **punishment** for their sins, **but once they were killed, they are called "Your servants"?** This indicates that a transgressor who was executed achieves atonement even without repentance.

This is borne out by a proof from *Mizmor L'Asaph* (Tehillim 79): the verse speaks of *nivlas avadecha* and *basar chasidecha* — the bodies of God's servants and the flesh of His pious ones — given over to the birds and beasts by foreign conquerors. These people were not the truly righteous; the verse distinguishes *chasidecha* from *avadecha*, and the latter are people who *mechayvei dina de'ikra* — who were deserving of death. Yet once they were killed, they are called God's servants. Violent, unjust death achieves *kapara* and removes the designation of *rasha*.

### Court Execution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי קָא מְדַמֵּית הָרוּגֵי מַלְכוּת לַהֲרוּגֵי בֵּית דִּין?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rava: Are you comparing**

Abaye now introduces a further distinction: *mi kamidames harugei malchus l'harugei Beis Din?* — can you really compare those killed by a foreign government to those executed by the court? *Harugei malchus, keivan d'shelo bedin kamikteli, haveu lehu kapara* — those killed unjustly, outside any legal process, achieve *kapara* precisely because the injustice inflicted upon them is such a violent rupture of the moral order that it itself becomes redemptive. But *harugei Beis Din, keivan devedin kamikteli, lo havei lehu kapara* — those executed by the court, through an ordered and legitimate judicial process, do not achieve *kapara* through the execution itself.

This is a striking and counterintuitive conclusion. One would have supposed that judicial execution, carried out as the sanction for a specific sin after due process — accompanied by confession and formal procedure — would be the paradigmatic case of atonement through punishment. Yet the *gemara* holds that it is precisely the orderly, lawful character of the execution that prevents it from functioning as *kapara*. The violent, unjust death of an enemy's victim has more redemptive power than the lawful execution of a convicted criminal. The proof that *harugei Beis Din* do not achieve *kapara* through execution alone comes from the *mishna* itself: *lo hayu kovrin oso bekivrot avosav* — they were not buried in the family cemetery. If execution alone had conferred *kapara*, there would be no reason to continue treating the deceased as a *rasha* through separate burial.

### Decomposition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ בִּנְידוֹן אַחֲרִינָא. קַשִּׁיּוּ עָלוּהּ: מִשָׁה וּקְבוּרָה בַּיְנֵיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Adda bar Ahava challenges this from the *mishna*: if burial produces *kapara*, why can the family not observe *aveilus* after the burial?

The *gemara* then considers the response that perhaps *kapara* requires both death *and* burial: *misah ukevurah bainan*. The execution alone is insufficient; burial is also necessary. On this view, a person executed by the court will eventually achieve *kapara*, but only after burial — which is why separate burial is still required at the moment of interment (since *kapara* has not yet occurred), but in principle, once buried, the person's status changes.

The *gemara* answers: *bainan nami ikul basar* — you need not only death and burial but the decomposition of the flesh. *Kapara* does not arrive at the moment of burial; it arrives only as the body begins to break down underground.

### Time Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַשִּׁי: תְּחִלַּת אֲנִינוּת מִסְּתִימַת הַגּוֹלֵל. תְּחִלַּת כַּפָּרָה, מֵי־חַזְּזָא צָרָה דִּקְבוּרָה פּוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi offers a more refined formulation. *Aveilus* begins *mistimas hagolel* — from the sealing of the grave. *Kapara* begins *mikeichazu tzara dekivra purta* — when the first traces of the pain of the grave begin, a little while after burial, when the first stages of decomposition commence.

Rav Ashi offers a more refined formulation. *Aveilus* begins *mistimas hagolel* — from the sealing of the grave. *Kapara* begins *mikeichazu tzara dekivra purta* — when the first traces of the pain of the grave begin, a little while after burial, when the first stages of decomposition commence. The difficulty is that there is an irreducible time gap: at the moment *aveilus* would normally begin, *kapara* has not yet occurred, and the deceased is still classified as a *rasha*. Therefore the family is not obligated to observe *aveilus*. Once *kapara* arrives, shortly thereafter, the *chiyuv* of *aveilus* has already been displaced — *ho'il v'idchu yidchu*, just as the disqualified *korbanos* of the *ir hanidachat* remain permanently disqualified once they have been pushed off. The parallel to *dichuy* thus turns out to have been the very key to the discussion from the beginning.

The result is two competing explanations for why *aveilus* is not observed: either *kapara* arrives only after full decomposition — a year or more later — making *aveilus* permanently inapplicable; or *kapara* arrives an hour or so after burial, but it comes too late for *aveilus* to begin, and the principle of *dichuy* forever bars it. A further implication: if a relative was not aware of the execution until after the burial had taken place, it is possible that according to the second view, *aveilus* could be observed at that point — the *chiyuv* beginning upon hearing the news, by which time *kapara* may already have been achieved.

## Forbidden Benefit

### Grave Soil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: קַרְקַע הָעוֹלָם עֲשִׂירָה בַּאִסּוּר הֲנָאָה אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The *gemara* now turns to the question of *issurei hana'ah* — prohibitions on deriving benefit — as they apply to things associated with a *meis*. **Is the earth of a grave** forbidden **to derive benefit from, or not?**

The *gemara* now turns to the question of *issurei hana'ah* — prohibitions on deriving benefit — as they apply to things associated with a *meis*. The background case is that people would take dirt from Rav's grave as a *segula* for treating a day-old fever. When Shmuel was told of this practice, he expressed no concern. The *gemara* frames the question: is the soil of a grave *assur behana'ah*?

### Ground Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, קַרְקַע עוֹלָם, מִדִּכְתִיב: ״עַל הֶהָרִים אֱלֹהֵיהֶם״ – אֱלֹהֵיהֶם, וְלֹא הֶהָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The answer is that *karka olam* — the earth itself — does not become forbidden. **The principle is derived by analogy to** *avodat kochavim*: the verse states **"Their gods are on the mountains, but the mountains are not their gods."** If one worships a mountain, the mountain itself does not become a forbidden idol, because ground cannot be made *assur*.

The answer is that *karka olam* — the earth itself — does not become forbidden. The principle is derived by analogy to *avodat kochavim*: the verse states *al he-harim eloheihem v'lo he-harim eloheihem* — their gods are on the mountains, but the mountains are not their gods. If one worships a mountain, the mountain itself does not become a forbidden idol, because ground cannot be made *assur*. The *gemara* extends this: just as *avodat kochavim bemachubar lo mitzra* — an idol attached to the earth cannot become forbidden — so too burial in the earth does not render the surrounding earth *assur behana'ah*.

### Mausoleums

However, things change when we move from bare ground to constructed structures. A mausoleum or built grave — *kever binyan* — stands in a different category. Something that was originally detached and subsequently attached to the ground retains enough independence from the earth that it can acquire the halachic status of *issurei hana'ah*. Several challenges from the *gemara* — a grave dug for a father but used elsewhere; a new grave into which a *nefel* was placed — are all resolved by the *gemara* as referring to constructed graves, not bare earth. The distinction is somewhat strained in *pshat*, since a mausoleum that is "found" unexpectedly or whose provenance is unknown seems implausible. Nevertheless, the *gemara* expends considerable effort sustaining the principle that earth itself cannot be made *assur behana'ah*, and that only constructed elements qualify.

### Grave Types

The *gemara* closes with a *baraisa* enumerating three categories of graves with respect to moving a body. A *kever hanimtza* — a body discovered buried in one's field without the owner's knowledge — may be moved, and once moved, the location is *tahor* and *mutar behana'ah*, because one cannot render another's property *assur* without his consent. A *kever hayadua* — a grave intentionally placed in a location — may not be moved, and even if moved, the location remains *tamei* rabbinically and *assur behana'ah*, since the deceased had a legitimate claim to that space. A *kever hamazik et harabbim* — a grave whose location causes harm to the public, because of *tum'ah* or otherwise — may be moved in deference to communal welfare. In that case the location becomes *tahor*, since the rabbinic extension of *tum'ah* does not apply after the body is gone, but the space remains *assur behana'ah*, since the original burial was with permission and the body had legitimately belonged there. The *gemara* applies the *kever binyan* reading to all three cases, maintaining throughout that bare earth cannot become *assur*.
