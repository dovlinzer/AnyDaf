# Moed Katan 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rebbi's Maidservant

### Maidservant Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁפְחָה שֶׁל בֵּית רַבִּי מַאי הִיא? דְּאַמְּתָא דְבֵי רַבִּי חֲזֵיתֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה מָחֵי לִבְנוֹ גָּדוֹל, אֲמַרָה: לֶיהֱוֵי הָהוּא גַּבְרָא בְּשַׁמְתָּא, דְּקָעָבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״וְלִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשׁוֹל״. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְלִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשׁוֹל״ — בְּמַכֶּה לִבְנוֹ גָּדוֹל הַכָּתוּק מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** It was related that **the maidservant in the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **saw a certain man who was striking his adult son. She said: Let that man be excommunicated, due to** the fact that **he has transgressed** the injunction: **"You shall not place a stumbling block before the blind"** (Leviticus 19:14), **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states: **"You shall not place a stumbling block before the blind,"** and **the verse speaks** here **of one who strikes his adult son,** as the son is likely to become angry and strike his father back, thereby transgressing the severe prohibition against hitting one's parent.

The Gemara has been occupied with various narratives concerning *niddui*, including the troubling and sadly perennial story of a rabbi with a reputation for impropriety, and whether and how to respond to such a situation. In that context, the Gemara had already alluded to a *niddui* imposed by Rebbi's maidservant, and now it circles back to ask: what exactly was that story?

The Gemara relates: *amsah d'vei Rebbi chazeisa l'hahu gavra d'hava machi livno hagadol* — the maidservant of Rebbi's household observed a certain man striking his adult son. Of course, corporal punishment was a regular feature of child-rearing in that era. But here the problem was that the child was no longer a child. She responded: *l'havei hahu gavra b'shamta* — that person should be in a state of *shamta* — because he is transgressing the prohibition of *lifnei iveir lo sitein michshol*, placing a stumbling block before the blind. A *baraita* supports this: the verse *v'lifnei iveir lo sitein michshol* applies in this context to one who strikes his adult son, because such a son is no longer in a position simply to absorb corporal punishment without consequence. He may rebel against it, strike back, or be further driven down a destructive path he may already be on.

### Lifnei Iveir

This ruling raises a fascinating question in the *poskim*: how literally is this *Gemara* to be taken? In normative halachic discourse, the prohibition of *lifnei iveir lo sitein michshol* is understood in quite narrow terms. A basic criterion, for example, is the principle the Gemara elsewhere calls *trei avrei d'nahara* — that the prohibition applies only where the other person could not have sinned without your assistance, as if you were on opposite banks of a river and only your active intervention places the forbidden object in their reach. In a case like the one here, the father is not handing anything to anyone; he is engaged in his own conduct which might, at some remove, place his son on a wrong path. The argument seems to favor viewing this not as a technical violation of the biblical prohibition of *lifnei iveir*, but rather as a violation of its spirit and underlying value — the concern about leading another person to stumble — without necessarily crossing the precise halachic threshold of the *aveira*. This question does receive treatment in the halachic literature, and it remains genuinely open whether the technical prohibition applies here.

What is in any case striking is the nature of the maidservant's intervention. We all know how little one appreciates being told by someone else how to parent one's children — and yet here she is, observing a stranger's conduct, concluding it will lead to disaster, and imposing a *niddui* upon him. And the Gemara affirms that this *niddui* was meaningful and binding.

### Authority for Niddui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַיְּילוּהּ לִמְעָרְתָּא דַּחֲסִידֵי וְלָא קַיבְּלוּהּ, עַיְּילוּהּ לִמְעָרְתָּא דְּדַיָּינֵי וְקַיבְּלוּהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? דַּעֲבַד כְּרַבִּי אִילְעַאי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אִילְעַאי אוֹמֵר: אִם רוֹאֶה אָדָם שֶׁיִּצְרוֹ מִתְגַּבֵּר עָלָיו — יֵלֵךְ לְמָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִין אוֹתוֹ, וְיִלְבַּשׁ שְׁחוֹרִים וְיִתְעַטֵּף שְׁחוֹרִים, וְיַעֲשֶׂה מַה שֶּׁלִּבּוֹ חָפֵץ, וְאַל יְחַלֵּל שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Because he was a great Torah scholar, **they took him into the caves in which the pious** are interred in order to bury him there, **but** the caves **did not accept him.** **They** then **took him into the caves of the judges, and they accepted him.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that he was accepted there? The Gemara answers: Even though he sinned, **he** still **acted in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ilai, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Ilai says: If a person sees that his** evil **inclination is gaining control over him** and he cannot overcome it, then **he should go to a place where he is not known. He should wear black, and he should wrap his** head **in black,** as if he were a mourner. Perhaps these changes will influence him, so that he not sin. Even if these actions do not help, **he should** at least **do as his heart desires** in private **and not desecrate the name of Heaven in public.**

This brings us to what is perhaps the most interesting dimension of the story: the question of who has the authority to impose *niddui*. Until this point in the sugya, we have seen *niddui* imposed by *chachamim* — rabbinic figures. That context even gave rise to the category of *menadeh likhvodo*, a kind of subjective *niddui* declared on account of personal affront rather than objective wrongdoing. Such a *niddui* does not bind universally; it reflects that individual's honor and, perhaps, extends to his students, but a person's own *rebbi* would not be bound by it. It is a *niddui* grounded in subjectivity.

Here, however, we encounter a qualitatively different category. Rebbi's maidservant — who holds no formal rabbinic title — observes a man doing something she regards as objectively harmful, and declares a *niddui*. This is the more general, objective form of *niddui*: not "my honor was offended" but "something objectively wrong is happening." And the remarkable finding is that such a *niddui* can be imposed by any person who witnesses such conduct. One might have assumed that even an objective *niddui* would require some form of rabbinic sanction or institutional authority. What the Gemara reveals is otherwise: anyone observing genuinely objectionable behavior can give voice to a *niddui*, and that declaration carries binding force for everyone.

This creates a striking and, in certain ways, unsettling picture of communal dynamics — a society in which any observer of misconduct can impose a state of ostracism. One might push back: is not any objectionable behavior grounds for *niddui* then? I don't like what you're doing, so *niddui*? The *Rambam*'s famous list of twenty-four grounds for *niddui*, compiled from various *Gemara* passages, offers some clarification. Much of what he enumerates involves conduct characterized by *bizayon* — contempt or disregard — for *halacha*, for halachic authority, or for the honor of rabbinic figures. A simple transgression, one that does not carry this quality of contempt, is generally not a basis for *niddui*.

One might also suggest that Rebbi's *shifcha* occupied a unique position. We have other traditions about her possessing considerable knowledge of rabbinic usage and obscure halachic terminology, and she was deeply embedded in Rebbi's household. Perhaps by virtue of her own wisdom, or by virtue of her situation, she possessed a kind of standing to impose *niddui* that a random bystander might not. That remains an open question.

The nature of the *niddui* also raises the question of enforcement. The obligations of *niddui* fall primarily upon the person placed in that status — they must observe certain practices that set them apart from ordinary social life. If a person has not changed their behavior after being placed in *niddui*, the escalation is to *cherem*, which carries far broader social consequences: no one conducts business with the person; the entire community participates in the exclusion. The *niddui* itself functions more like a form of probation or a warning — an opportunity for the person to demonstrate contrition and change before more severe measures are taken. And once behavior has genuinely changed, the *niddui* can be released before its formal period expires.

The mechanism of self-enforcement is admittedly puzzling. Why would someone placed in *niddui* willingly observe its practices if they are already disposed toward contempt? The answer implicit in the Gemara seems to be that most people, even those who have crossed a particular line, remain fundamentally invested in the community and its authority structures. They stepped over a boundary; they did not step out of the society. For such a person, the *niddui* and its practices provide a structured path back to good standing — a ritualized demonstration of contrition — and they will avail themselves of it. For those who are genuinely defiant, the answer is escalation to *cherem*.

## Reish Lakish & Fig-Eater

### Fig-Eater Niddui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֲוָה מְנַטַּר פַּרְדֵּיסָא. אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא וְקָאָכֵיל תְּאֵינֵי, רְמָא בֵּיהּ קָלָא וְלָא אַשְׁגַּח בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר: לֶיהֱוֵי הָהוּא גַּבְרָא בְּשַׁמְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, it was related that **Reish Lakish was guarding an orchard** for payment when **a certain man came and ate** some **figs** that were growing there. Reish Lakish **raised his voice** and yelled at him, **but** this man **paid no attention to him** and kept eating. Reish Lakish **said: Let that man be** in a state of **excommunication.**

The Gemara now turns to another story of *niddui*, this one involving competing declarations and the need for adjudication. Reish Lakish was watching over an orchard. A certain man came along and began eating figs from the trees. Reish Lakish called out to him to stop; the man ignored him entirely. Reish Lakish then declared: *l'havei hahu gavra b'shamta* — that man eating my figs should be in a state of *shamta*. Here again, as in the story of Rebbi's maidservant, the *niddui* is imposed not by a formal institutional process but by a person who witnesses conduct he regards as wrongful.

### Counter-Niddui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּרַבָּה, לֶיהֱוֵי הָהוּא גַּבְרָא בְּשַׁמְתָּא, אִם מָמוֹן נִתְחַיַּיבְתִּי לְךָ, נִידּוּי מִי נִתְחַיַּיבְתִּי לָךְ?
>
> **Translation:** The man eating the figs **said to him: On the contrary, let that man,** i.e., Reish Lakish, **be** in a state of **excommunication,** for even **if I have become liable to you for payment,** as I have eaten of the figs without permission, **have I become liable to you for excommunication?** With that statement, the man left.

The man who had been eating the figs was not without a response. He turned to Reish Lakish and declared his own counter-*niddui*: *l'havei hahu gavra b'shamta* — you should be in *shamta*! His argument: *ei mamona nitchayavti lach, niddui mi nitchayavti lach* — just because I may owe you money, does that obligate me in *niddui*? The implication is that even if he acted wrongly, the appropriate remedy for such conduct is not *niddui*.

### Beit Medrash Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: שֶׁלּוֹ — נִידּוּי, שֶׁלְּךָ — אֵינוֹ נִידּוּי. וּמַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? זִיל לְגַבֵּיהּ דְּלִישְׁרֵי לָךְ. לָא יָדַעְנָא לֵיהּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: זִיל לְגַבֵּי נְשִׂיאָה דְּלִישְׁרֵי לָךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: נִידּוּהוּ וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ מִי נִידָּהוּ, יֵלֵךְ אֵצֶל נָשִׂיא וְיַתִּיר לוֹ נִדּוּיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **went to the study hall** to inquire about the *halakha* with regard to this man. The other Sages **said to him: His** decree of **ostracism is** valid, but **your** decree of **ostracism is not.** In other words, that man was correct and Reish Lakish should not have ostracized him in response to his actions. Reish Lakish then asked: If so, **what is the remedy** for this decree of ostracism? The Sages answered him: **Go to him so that he may release you** from it. Reish Lakish replied: **I do not know him. They said to him: Go** then **to the *Nasi*, so that he may release you** from the ban, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **was ostracized, but he does not know who ostracized him, he should go to the *Nasi*, and** the *Nasi* **may release** him from **his** decree of **ostracism.**

The parties came before the *beit medrash* for a determination. The ruling: *shelo niddui, shelcha eino niddui* — the fig-eater's *niddui* against Reish Lakish is binding, while Reish Lakish's *niddui* against the fig-eater is not. Remarkably, the rabbi's *niddui* is illegitimate, and this thief's *niddui* stands.

Tosafot is deeply troubled by this result. If we grant that *niddui* can be imposed in connection with monetary wrongs — and indeed courts do impose *niddui* on someone who refuses to appear for a monetary dispute — what did Reish Lakish do wrong? Tosafot explains: *af al pi she'mshamtinan ba'avur mamon* — even though *niddui* can follow in monetary contexts — *it lomar dehaya lo likro oto l'veit din techila* — in such a case the proper course is to summon the person before a court first. To unilaterally impose *niddui* over a monetary claim — bypassing the court system entirely — is itself an offense. It is a form of *avid inish dina l'nafshei*, taking the law into one's own hands, and it undermines the very institutional authority that *niddui* is meant to protect. This explains the result: not every wrong justifies immediate *niddui*, and in a monetary case, the court must be given the opportunity to act first. The fig-eater, meanwhile, had a genuine grievance about having been wrongfully placed in *niddui*, and his declaration against Reish Lakish was legitimate.

Tosafot adds a further consideration: even where *niddui* would ultimately be appropriate, it is not imposed immediately upon detecting a violation — there is a process and a waiting period. And Rabbeinu Tam, cited in the final lines of Tosafot, suggests an additional *okimta*: perhaps in that locale it was customary for people to eat from trees in an orchard and pay the owner afterward, meaning no actual theft had occurred. On that reading, Reish Lakish had simply overreacted. But the more substantive answer — that bypassing the court is itself a wrong that warrants *niddui* — remains the more significant ruling.

### Release & Nasi

The *beit medrash* ruling leaves Reish Lakish himself in a state of *niddui*. How is he to extricate himself? *Zil legabeih delishrei lach* — go to the person who declared it and have him release you. As we saw, the *niddui* carries a personal dimension: the one who imposed it is in a sense a party, and it is that person — not some third-party authority — who has the standing to release the declarant. Reish Lakish responds that he does not know the man's identity; it was some stranger on the street. The Gemara's answer: *zil legabeih nasi delishrei lach* — go to the *nasi*, and he will release you from the *niddui*.

The Gemara cites a *baraita* in support: if someone was placed in *niddui* and does not know who placed it upon him — *niduho v'eino yodeia mi niduho* — he may go to the *nasi*, who will release him. The *nasi* serves here as a representative of broader rabbinic and political authority, a kind of default releasing agent when the original party is unavailable.

This is worth reflecting on. We have been operating within the framework of *menadeh likhvodo*, where the personal stake of the declaring party is central to both the imposition and release of *niddui*. The fig-eater's *niddui* against Reish Lakish was of this type — rooted in a personal grievance over the affront to his *kavod*. But the role of the *nasi* here suggests that even in such personal *niddui* cases, when the original party cannot be located, communal and institutional authority can step in. The *nasi* embodies both dimensions of authority — the rabbinic-scholarly and the political-communal — and the Gemara does not specify which dimension is being invoked here, which itself is suggestive.

## Leaders & Niddui

### Av Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, בְּאוּשָׁא הִתְקִינוּ: אַב בֵּית דִּין שֶׁסָּרַח — אֵין מְנַדִּין אוֹתוֹ, אֶלָּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הִכָּבֵד וְשֵׁב בְּבֵיתֶךָ״. חָזַר וְסָרַח — מְנַדִּין אוֹתוֹ, מִפְּנֵי חִילּוּל הַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Rav Huna said** that **in Usha it was enacted:** If **the president of the court sinned, he is not ostracized.** Although this would be the appropriate punishment, he is not ostracized, so as not to cause a desecration of God's name. **Rather,** they **say to him** the words of the verse: **"Keep your honor and stay at home"** (II Kings 14:10). That is to say, to preserve your dignity, it would be best if you were to stay at home, resign your position, and refrain from further public appearances. If **he sins again, he is ostracized, due to the desecration of God's name** that would ensue were people to think that he was spared his rightful punishment due to his high position.

The Gemara continues with a tradition transmitted in the name of Rav Huna: *be'Usha hitkinu, av beit din shesarach ein menadin oto* — they instituted at Usha that an *av beit din* who behaves badly is not placed in *niddui*. The *av beit din*, second in standing only to the *nasi*, is to be handled differently when he goes off the proper path. Rather than imposing *niddui*, one says to him: *hikaved v'shev b'veitecha* — respect your honor and sit quietly at home. The impulse is to handle the matter privately: approach him, have a confidential conversation, encourage him to step back from public life and correct his behavior without bringing the matter into the open. This approach has an obvious resonance with recurring situations in communal life, where rabbinic misconduct is addressed first through private channels rather than public censure, in the hope of resolving matters quietly. *Vechazor v'sarach, menadin oto mipnei chillul Hashem* — but if he repeats the misconduct, then he is placed in *niddui*, because at that point the greater danger is the *chillul Hashem* that results from appearing to condone such conduct from a figure of rabbinic authority.

### Private vs. Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁסָּרַח — אֵין מְנַדִּין אוֹתוֹ בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכָשַׁלְתָּ הַיּוֹם וְכָשַׁל גַּם נָבִיא עִמְּךָ לָיְלָה״ — כַּסֵּהוּ כַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** this opinion **disagrees with** that of **Reish Lakish,** for **Reish Lakish said:** If **a Torah scholar sins, he is not ostracized** at all **in public, as it is stated: "Therefore, shall you fall in the day, and the prophet also shall fall with you in the night"** (Hosea 4:5). This is explained to mean: If a prophet or any other important person sins, his offense **should be concealed like the night** and not punished in public.

This two-stage approach is then contrasted with the view of Reish Lakish, who holds a more categorical position: *talmid chacham shesarach ein menadin oto befarhesya* — a *talmid chacham* who acts improperly is never publicly placed in *niddui*, not even at the second stage. The verse he invokes is *v'kashalta hayom v'kashal gam navi imcha layla* — if you stumble today, the prophet will stumble with you as well at night — and he interprets this to mean that a religious authority must be covered like the night, whatever one does. The concern is that public shaming of a Torah figure causes people to think worse of Torah itself, compounding the original harm. Whether this concern ultimately outweighs the alternative — the danger of complicity in a cover-up when the misconduct is likely to become known in any case — is precisely the tension that makes this passage so timelessly fraught.

### Mar Zutra Self-Shamta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר זוּטְרָא חֲסִידָא, כִּי מִיחַיַּיב צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן שַׁמְתָּא, בְּרֵישָׁא מְשַׁמֵּית נַפְשֵׁיהּ, וַהֲדַר מְשַׁמֵּית לְדִידֵיהּ. כִּי הֲוָה עָיֵיל בְּאוּשְׁפִּיזֵיהּ שָׁרֵי לֵיהּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, וַהֲדַר שָׁרֵי לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this issue, it was related that **when a Torah scholar would become liable** to be punished with **excommunication before Mar Zutra the Pious,** Mar Zutra **would first excommunicate himself** as a mark of respect for the Torah scholar, **and afterward he would ostracize** the Torah scholar. This self-imposed excommunication was meant only as a show of honor to the other Torah scholar, and therefore **when** Mar Zutra **would enter his house** of lodging, **he would release his** own excommunication, **and afterward he would release** the other's excommunication.

The Gemara relates the practice of Mar Zutra Chasida: *ki michayav tzurba merabbanan shamta, bereisha mashmis nafshei v'hadar mashmis ledideh* — when a member of the rabbinic college became obligated in *shamta*, Mar Zutra would first put himself into *cherem* and then put the other person in. The analogy is apt: like a host who spills wine at the table so that his guest who has spilled feels less conspicuous, Mar Zutra's self-*shamta* served to remove the sense of disgrace from the imposition.

Tosafot addresses the underlying rationale. One explanation: *mashmis nafshei mishum tza'ar shetzarich lenadot talmid chacham* — he placed himself in *shamta* because of the anguish he felt at being required to impose *niddui* on a fellow rabbinic colleague. This is an illuminating observation, because it introduces the element of *tza'ar* — personal distress and suffering — into the *niddui* framework. We have previously considered the parallel between *niddui* and *avelus*, particularly the practices they share and whether those practices in the context of *niddui* are fundamentally acts of social exclusion or expressions of inner suffering. Tosafot here appears to do something of the reverse: he finds in *niddui* an element that could be understood as an expression of *tza'ar*, such that Mar Zutra's voluntary adoption of *niddui* practices was his way of giving form to how terrible he felt. What makes this especially striking is that Mar Zutra did not merely adopt the *practices* of *niddui* — he placed himself in the *state* of *niddui*, which has always carried with it an element of censure, not merely grief.

Tosafot's second explanation is more pragmatic: *inami sheyehei zachur lehatir* — he placed himself in the same status so that he would not forget to release the other person. If both were in *niddui* together, he would remember to take care of the matter when his own *niddui* needed resolving.

The story then confirms this reading. *Ki havei ayil b'ushpizeih, sharei lei l'nafshei, v'hadar sharei lei l'didei* — when Mar Zutra reached his inn that evening, he released himself from his *niddui*, and then he released the other person as well. The sequence was the mnemonic.

One might also suggest — and this connection was raised in discussion — that Mar Zutra's practice echoes the institution of the city of refuge and the responsibility of the *Kohen Gadol*: a leader bearing collective responsibility for what those within his sphere do. Mar Zutra may have been embracing a degree of personal accountability for the misconduct of his colleague, sharing in the consequence as a gesture of solidarity and shared responsibility.

### Corporal Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: תַּלְמִיד חָכָם מְנַדֶּה לְעַצְמוֹ, וּמֵיפֵר לְעַצְמוֹ. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: תֵּיתֵי לִי, דְּלָא שַׁמֵּיתִי צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן מֵעוֹלָם. אֶלָּא כִּי קָא מִיחַיַּיב צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן שַׁמְתָּא, הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? כִּי הָא דִּבְמַעְרְבָא מִימְּנוּ אַנְּגָדָא דְּצוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן וְלָא מִימְּנוּ אַשַּׁמְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: A Torah scholar may ostracize himself, and he may** similarly **release himself** from self-imposed ostracism. **Rav Pappa said: I have** good **coming to me,** for I am praiseworthy, **as I have never ostracized a Torah scholar.** The Gemara asks about this: **If so, when a Torah scholar was liable to be ostracized, what would he do?** The Gemara answers: He did **as they do in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, where **they appoint** a court **to give a Torah scholar lashes, but they do not appoint** a court **to ostracize him.** That is to say, lashes were preferred over ostracism.

Rav Pappa offers a related but distinct statement: *taysi li d'lo shemisi tzurba merabbanan me'olam* — he counts it as a virtue that he never had to place a member of the rabbinic college in *niddui*. When such a person's conduct did warrant *shamta*, what was his practice? *Ki ha d'ma'arava: minu angida d'tzurba merabbanan, v'lo minu ashamta* — as they do in the west, in Eretz Yisrael: they would convene a *beit din* to administer lashes, but not *shamta*. Corporal punishment, Rav Pappa maintains, is actually preferable to *shamta* for a *talmid chacham*.

This may seem counterintuitive, but there is a genuine logic to it. A state of societal censure lasting thirty days carries enormous social and spiritual weight — it is, as we will see shortly, understood to penetrate to a person's very core. A brief period of physical punishment, by contrast, is finite, localized, and over. It may in fact be far less injurious to a person's standing and dignity than a prolonged public state of exclusion. What appears on the surface to be more severe is, from this perspective, more respectful of the *talmid chacham's* honor.

## Metaphysics of Shamta

### Shamta Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שַׁמְתָּא? אָמַר רַב: שָׁם מִיתָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שְׁמָמָה יִהְיֶה, וּמַהְנְיָא בֵּיהּ כִּי טִיחְיָא בְּתַנּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds with a discussion that explains the severity of the punishment of excommunication: **What** is the meaning of the word **excommunication [*shamta*]? Rav said:** This word is a contraction of the expression **there is death [*sham mita*],** alluding to the deathly aspect of excommunication. **And Shmuel said:** *Shamta* means that **he will be a desolation [*shemama yiheyeh*], and it is effective upon him like fat** smeared **on an oven.** Just as some of the fat will always remain absorbed in the walls of the oven, so too some aspect of the curse contained in the excommunication will continue to adhere to him even after the excommunication has been nullified.

The Gemara now pauses to ask: *mai shamta* — what is the meaning of the word *shamta*? Two Amoraic opinions are offered. Rav says: *sham mita* — there is death there. Shmuel says: *shmama yiheyeh* — he will be in a state of desolation. These etymologies are not merely literary; they begin to gesture toward the metaphysical significance of *shamta* that the Gemara is about to develop more fully.

### 248 Limbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁנִּכְנֶסֶת בְּמָאתַיִם וְאַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה אֵיבָרִים, כָּךְ כְּשֶׁהִיא יוֹצְאָה — יוֹצְאָה מִמָּאתַיִם וְאַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה אֵיבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** this opinion **disagrees with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish,** for **Reish Lakish said: Just as** ostracism **enters the two hundred and forty-eight organs** of one's body when it is first pronounced, **so too when it leaves, it leaves from** his **two hundred and forty-eight organs.**

A further statement deepens this picture: *umahani beih keticha betanura* — the state of *shamta* affixes itself to a person like the greasing of an oven, which locks in heat and flavor. The *shamta* does not simply surround a person from without — it enters in and stays.

This sets up the teaching of Reish Lakish, which stands in contrast: *keshem shenichneset b'matayim v'arba'im u'shmonah evarim, kach kshehi yotza, yotza mimatayim v'arba'im u'shmonah evarim* — just as *shamta* enters the two hundred and forty-eight limbs of the body, so too when it departs, it departs entirely from all two hundred and forty-eight limbs. The entry of *shamta* and its exit are total, bodily, and complete.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהִיא נִכְנֶסֶת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיְתָה הָעִיר חֵרֶם״ — ״חֵרֶם״ בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא מָאתַיִם וְאַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה הָווּ. כְּשֶׁהִיא יוֹצְאָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּרוֹגֶז רַחֵם תִּזְכּוֹר״, ״רַחֵם״ בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא הָכִי הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** The following allusion is offered in support of the opinion of Reish Lakish: **When it enters,** it enters all the organs, **as it is written: "And the city shall be a curse [*ḥerem*]"** (Joshua 6:17), **and the numerical value [*gimatriyya*]** of the word ***ḥerem*,** a concept similar to ostracism, **is two hundred and forty-eight.** Therefore, the verse alludes to the fact that a decree of ostracism penetrates one's two hundred and forty-eight organs. **When it leaves,** it leaves all the organs, **as it is written: "In wrath remember mercy [*raḥem*]"** (Habakkuk 3:2), **and the numerical value** of the word ***raḥem* is also** two hundred and forty-eight, thereby teaching that when the decree of ostracism is revoked, it disappears entirely.

The Gemara supports this from the numerical value of *cherem* — the stricter form of ostracism — which in *gematria* equals two hundred and forty-eight, precisely the number of limbs that *Chazal* enumerate in the human body. *V'hayeta ha'ir cherem* — the city shall be under *cherem*; the word *cherem* itself encodes the full extent of the human person. And for the release from *cherem*, the Gemara invokes *berogez racheim tizkor* — in anger, remember compassion; the word *racheim*, which shares its letters with *cherem*, carries the same numerical value, indicating that the release is equally total. What went in entirely comes out entirely — the same penetrating force, now reversed. The identity of the letters of *cherem* and *racheim* seems to be more than a numerical coincidence; one wonders whether something deeper is being pointed to about the relationship between the decree and its reversal.

### Dog Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: שְׁדִי שַׁמְתָּא אַגְּנוּבְתָּא דְכַלְבָּא וְאִיהִי דִּידַהּ עָבְדָה. דְּהָהוּא כַּלְבָּא דַּהֲוָה אָכֵיל מְסָאנֵי דְרַבָּנַן וְלָא הֲווֹ קָא יָדְעִי מַנּוּ, וְשַׁמִּתוּ לֵיהּ. אִיתְּלַי בֵּיהּ נוּרָא בִּגְנוּבְתֵּיהּ וַאֲכַלְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues discussing the power of a ban. **Rav Yosef said: Cast an excommunication on the tail of a dog and it,** the excommunication, **will do its work** and harm the dog. It was related that there was **a certain dog that would eat the shoes of the Sages, and they did not know who it was** causing this damage. They thought that it was a person, **and** so **they excommunicated** whoever was doing it. Soon thereafter, the dog's **tail caught fire and got burnt.** This shows that excommunication can have a harmful effect even on a dog.

The Gemara then turns to a story that illustrates *niddui*'s metaphysical power in striking fashion. Rav Yosef relates: *shadi shamta agunavta d'kalba, v'ihu d'idei avda* — he cast a *niddui* on the tail of a dog, and it acted on its own. The backstory: a dog had been chewing on the shoes of the rabbis — disrespecting rabbinic honor in the most basic way — and since the owner was unknown, they could not address the matter through normal channels. They placed the dog in *niddui*, and *itlibei nura d'gunavtei v'achaltei* — a fire caught hold of the dog's tail and consumed it.

One may well note the absence of any concern for *tzaar baalei chayim* in this account. But the Gemara's purpose here is unmistakable: *niddui* carries a real, operative metaphysical force, independent of social mechanism.

### Vessel & Shofar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא אַלָּמָא דַּהֲוָה קָא מְצַעַר לֵיהּ לְהָהוּא צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שַׁמְתֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִסְתְּפֵינָא מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁקֵיל פְּתִיחָא עֲלֵיהּ. כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּמִסְתְּפֵינָא מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שִׁקְלֵיהּ, אַחֲתֵיהּ בְּכַדָּא, וְאַחֲתֵיהּ בֵּי קִבְרֵי, וּקְרִי בֵּיהּ אַלְפָּא שִׁפּוּרֵי בְּאַרְבְּעִין יוֹמִין. אָזֵיל עָבֵיד הָכִי, פְּקַע כַּדָּא וּמִית אַלָּמָא. מַאי שִׁפּוּרֵי — שֶׁנִּפְרָעִין מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** It was further related that there was **a violent person who** caused **suffering to a certain Torah scholar.** This Torah scholar **came before Rav Yosef** to ask what he should do. Rav Yosef **said to him: Go and ostracize him.** This Torah scholar **said to him: I am afraid of him,** that he will harass me even more. Rav Yosef **said to him: Take** out, i.e., publish **a written** ostracism **against him.** The Torah scholar said to him: **All the more so I am fearful of him,** for if I publicize the matter he will certainly come after me. Rav Yosef **said to him** to do as follows: **Take** the written ostracism **and place it in a jug, and set it down in a cemetery,** where nobody is found, **and sound a thousand,** i.e., many, ***shofar* blasts over the course of forty days.** That man **went and did this. The jug burst and the violent man died.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the reason that ***shofarot*** are sounded when a decree of ostracism is pronounced? The *shofarot* allude to the fact **that they extract punishment [*shenifra'in*] from** the excommunicated person.

The Gemara continues with the story of someone who came to Rav Yosef for help against a powerful man who was causing anguish to a member of the rabbinic college. Rav Yosef instructed him: *zil shamtei* — go and put him in *niddui*. The man replied: *mistafina minei* — I am afraid of him. If I impose *niddui* on this powerful figure, what will he do to me in retaliation? He has all the social power; the *niddui* will mean nothing to him and may invite real danger.

Rav Yosef then prescribed a procedure. *Shkilei pticha* — take a piece of parchment with the *niddui* written on it. *Achsei b'kada* — place it in an earthenware vessel. *Achsei b'kivrei* — deposit it in a cemetery. *V'karei bei alfa shipurei d'arba'im yomin* — and blow a thousand *shofar* blasts into it over forty days. This is clearly a ritualized, metaphysical procedure rather than a social declaration. *Avid hachi* — he did this. *Paka kada* — the vessel burst. *U'meis alma* — and the powerful man died.

### Person's Death

This story marks a transformation in how the power of *niddui* is being conceived. What began as a tool of social censure — a mechanism by which communal pressure is applied to bring a person back into alignment with halachic norms and communal expectations — has here become something altogether different. The shift is not incidental. As one participant in the shiur observed, and the point is well taken: both the dog story and this story feature scenarios in which the normal social machinery of *niddui* cannot function. You cannot persuade a dog; you cannot bring communal pressure to bear on a man who holds all the social power and before whom others will not dare to refuse business. When the societal impact of *niddui* is not available, the Gemara shifts its emphasis to the metaphysical dimension — a power that operates regardless of social dynamics. What appears on the surface to be a demonstration of *niddui*'s power is equally a testimony to its social impotence in certain contexts.

### Shofar & Evil Eye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תְּבָרָא? אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: תָּבְרִי בָּתֵּי רָמֵי. דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁ״נָּתְנוּ חֲכָמִים עֵינֵיהֶם״, אוֹ מִיתָה אוֹ עוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **What** is the reason that **broken** blasts are sounded on the *shofar* when the excommunication is pronounced? **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, said: It breaks tall buildings,** i.e., a decree of ostracism can harm and break even the high and mighty, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: Wherever** it says that **the Sages set their eyes** in anger upon a particular person, it causes **either death or poverty.**

The Gemara then asks: *mai shipuri* — what is the meaning of the *shofar* blasts? The answer given: they signify that *she-nifra'in mimenu* — punishment will ultimately be exacted from this person. And the word *tavra* — the broken blast — is interpreted to mean: *tavri batei ramei* — it will cause high houses to fall. Those who have erected themselves above the community will be brought low.

The Gemara then cites a *baraita* in the name of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *kol makom she-natnu chachamim eineihem, o mita o oni* — wherever the rabbis turned their gaze against someone, the result was either death or poverty. This too is part of the same pattern. One might observe that *drashos* are made about the symbolism of words — *sham mita*, *shmama*, *shipuri*, *tavra* — precisely when the literal, social force of the institution is losing its teeth. When someone is simply thrown in jail, there is no need to expound on the symbolism of the word jail. The proliferation of hermeneutic significance here is, at least in part, a symptom of an institution straining to assert its authority in a world where its practical power is not always available.

We know from later history that *cherem* and *niddui* continued to serve as instruments of communal governance through the medieval period, yet the same limitations and the same evasions recurred. The passage we have been studying is unusually candid about the gap between the institution's intended force and its real-world reach.

## Nazir & Metzora

### Advance Notice Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַנָּזִיר וְהַמְּצוֹרָע מִטּוּמְאָתוֹ לְטׇהֳרָתוֹ. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מֵרַבִּי זֵירָא: בְּשֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לָהֶם פְּנַאי, אוֹ דִלְמָא אַף בְּשֶׁהָיָה לָהֶם פְּנַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna: **And the nazirite** whose term of naziriteship ended on the intermediate days of a Festival, **and the leper** who needs to purify himself on the intermediate days and must shave his entire body in order to leave **his** state of ritual **impurity** and regain **his** ritual **purity,** these people are permitted to cut their hair on the intermediate days of the Festival because they were not able to do so on the eve of the Festival. **Rabbi Yirmeya raised a question before Rabbi Zeira:** Is this allowance limited to the case **where they did not have the time** to cut their hair before the Festival, as it was prohibited for them to do so beforehand? **Or perhaps** they may cut their hair **even** in a case **where they had the time** to do so beforehand.

The *Mishnah* teaches that among those permitted to take a haircut on *Chol HaMoed* are the *nazir* who is becoming *tahor* and the *metzora* who is becoming *tahor*. Since their condition prevented them from taking the haircut earlier, they are permitted to do so on *Chol HaMoed*. The Gemara raises a question: is this permission limited to a case where they had no advance opportunity — for example, where they became *tahor* only once the *regel* had already begun — or does it apply even where they had an opportunity beforehand and simply did not act on it?

This question is the flip side of another the Gemara has already addressed: a person who had no formal category of exception but in practice had no opportunity to shave before the *regel* — the Gemara's answer there was that we require formal categories, and an informal inability to act does not by itself create a dispensation. Here, we have a person who does fall into a formal category of exception, but who arguably had the opportunity to act beforehand and was negligent. Should the exception still apply?

### Korbanot Postponement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: כׇּל אֵלּוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ מוּתָּרִין לְגַלֵּחַ בַּמּוֹעֵד — בְּשֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לָהֶם פְּנַאי, אֲבָל הָיָה לָהֶם פְּנַאי — אֲסוּרִים. נָזִיר וּמְצוֹרָע, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶם פְּנַאי — מוּתָּרִים, שֶׁלֹּא יְשַׁהוּ קׇרְבְּנוֹתֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: We** already **learned** this in a *baraita*: With regard to **all those** about whom **the** Sages **said** that they **are permitted to cut their hair on** the intermediate days of **a Festival,** they may do so only **if they did not have time** to cut their hair before the Festival. **But** if **they had time** before the Festival began, then **they are prohibited** from doing so. However, **a nazirite and a leper, even if they had the time** to do so before the Festival, **they are permitted** to cut their hair. Why are they granted this special allowance? So **that they will not delay** bringing **their offerings.** Both a nazirite and a leper must cut their hair before sacrificing their offerings in completion of their purification process. Therefore, if they are not permitted to cut their hair, they will not be able to sacrifice their offerings at the proper time.

Rav Zeira answers Rav Yirmiya: we have already taught this — *kol elu she-amru mutarin legaleach ba-moed, beshelo haya lahem pnai* — all those said to be permitted to take a haircut on *Chol HaMoed*, the permission is premised on their not having had the opportunity beforehand. *Aval haya lahem pnai — asurin* — if they had a prior opportunity, they are forbidden. This is the general rule — as in the case of one released from prison: if he was released a day before *Yom Tov*, he should have taken care of it then.

But the *nazir* and *metzora* are different. *Af al pi she-haya lahem pnai, mutarin* — even if they had a prior opportunity, they are permitted. Why? *Shelo yish'hu korbanoseihem* — so as not to delay their *korbanot*. The argument here operates in a direction opposite to the usual one. Ordinarily, a concern that motivates restricting activity on *Chol HaMoed* is that permitting it will cause people to defer taking care of matters beforehand, waiting until the *regel* when they will be going up to Yerushalayim anyway. Here, the Gemara reasons the other way: if we tell the *nazir* or *metzora* who did not shave beforehand that they may not shave on *Chol HaMoed*, we will create a further problem — the shaving is part of the process that precedes bringing the *korbanot*, and forbidding it will in turn delay the *korbanot* themselves.

Tosafot notes that technically the *korban* does not strictly require the shaving to precede it — it could theoretically be brought first. But in practice, the prescribed sequence is: first the shaving, then the *korban*, then the hair is gathered and burned beneath the *shelamim* cooking in the pot, as the Torah describes. Disrupting that sequence creates a real halachic and practical problem. The Gemara therefore concludes that for the *nazir* and *metzora*, even where there was a prior opportunity, we absorb the cost of creating some incentive to defer, in order to avoid delaying the *korbanot*.

One might object: is this not precisely the argument made in other contexts by the *avel* or by any person who failed to shave before the *regel* — that now they are disheveled during the *regel* itself and this is disrespectful of *kavod ha-regel*? And the answer offered there is that yes, that is the cost of maintaining the rule, the cost of ensuring the incentive to act beforehand remains in place. For the *nazir* and *metzora*, the Gemara is saying there is an additional cost beyond *kavod ha-regel*: the postponement of *korbanot*. And that additional cost tips the balance toward leniency even where there was a prior opportunity.

### Kohen & Avel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הַכֹּהֵן וְהָאָבֵל מוּתָּרִין בְּגִילּוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** In was **taught** in a *baraita*: **A priest and a mourner are permitted to cut their hair** during the intermediate days of a Festival.

The Gemara then notes a *baraita* that lists two additional categories not mentioned in our *Mishnah* — the *kohen* and the *avel* — who are permitted to take a haircut when the time comes. The *avel* is easier to understand: we have seen throughout the sugya how a *regel* can terminate *shloshim*, thereby freeing the *avel* from the prohibition on shaving and laundering. The *kohen* refers, as the Gemara will clarify, to a *kohen* whose *mishmar* — his rotation of Temple service — extended up to or into the *regel*. During the *mishmar*, *kohanim* are not permitted to shave or launder (so that they take care of these matters before their rotation begins). Where this *mishmar* prevented them from attending to these matters before the *regel*, the *baraita* allows them to do so on *Chol HaMoed*.

### Avel Eighth Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי אָבֵל הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא שֶׁחָל שְׁמִינִי שֶׁלּוֹ בְּעֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל — אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְגַלּוֹחֵי בָּעֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל! אֶלָּא, שֶׁחָל שְׁמִינִי שֶׁלּוֹ לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל. אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְגַלּוֹחֵי עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רָבִינָא בַּר שֵׁילָא: הֲלָכָה כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, וּמוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְאַבָּא שָׁאוּל בְּשֶׁחָל שְׁמִינִי שֶׁלּוֹ לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל שֶׁמּוּתָּר לְגַלֵּחַ בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** when the **mourner** is permitted to do so? **If we say that the eighth** day of **his** mourning **occurred on the eve of the Festival,** then **he should have cut his hair on the eve of the Festival,** as the more stringent restrictions of his mourning no longer applied. **Rather,** it must be that we are dealing with a case **where the eighth** day of **his** mourning **occurred on a Shabbat that was the eve of the Festival,** and so he could not have cut his hair on the Festival eve. But if this is the case, **he should have cut his hair on Friday, for Rav Ḥisda said** that **Ravina bar Sheila said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul.** If the deceased was buried seven days before the Festival, then not only has the mourner completed the seven-day mourning period, but he is even regarded as having begun the thirty-day mourning period, and so the Festival cancels this thirty-day period of mourning. **And the Rabbis concede** to the opinion of **Abba Shaul when the eighth** day of **his** mourning **occurs on a Shabbat that is the eve of the Festival,** and they maintain **that he is permitted to cut his hair on Friday.**

The Gemara asks: what exactly is the case of the *avel* in the *baraita*? It proceeds by elimination. One might think the case is simply that day eight fell on *Erev ha-Regel*, meaning *shloshim* began on *Erev ha-Regel*, and the *regel* then terminates *shloshim*, permitting the haircut. But the objection is immediate: if day eight was *Erev ha-Regel*, you should have taken care of the haircut then — it was already permitted. The halacha is that when the *regel* will be *mevatel shloshim*, the *avel* may already bathe, launder, and attend to grooming the day before the *regel*, *likhvod ha-regel*. So what prevented you?

The answer must be that day eight fell on *Shabbat Erev ha-Regel* — the *regel* began on Sunday, *shloshim* started on the preceding *Shabbat*, and one obviously cannot take a haircut on *Shabbat*. But then the Gemara raises a further challenge: why not take care of it on *Erev Shabbat*, day seven? And here the ruling of Rav Chisda in the name of Rav Ina bar Shila becomes relevant: the *halacha* follows Abba Shaul.

### Abba Shaul Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, שֶׁחָל שְׁבִיעִי שֶׁלּוֹ לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** the ruling of the *baraita* is **necessary** in the case **where the seventh** day of **his** mourning **occurs on a Shabbat that is the eve of the Festival.** In that case, he certainly cannot cut his hair on Friday because it is only the sixth day of his mourning, and so he is permitted to cut his hair on the intermediate days of the Festival.

Abba Shaul holds that day seven of *aveilus* counts simultaneously toward the end of *shiva* and the beginning of *shloshim* — the principle of *miktzat ha-yom ke-kulo* allows the morning of day seven to serve as the final portion of *shiva*, after which *shloshim* has technically begun. The *chachamim* dispute this, holding that you cannot claim double credit for a single day — *shiva* and *shloshim* cannot overlap on the same calendar day. Rav Chisda rules that the *halacha* follows Abba Shaul.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא בָּרָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, דְּאָמַר: מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ, וְיוֹם שְׁבִיעִי עוֹלֶה לוֹ לְכָאן וּלְכָאן. וְכֵיוָן דְּשַׁבָּת הָוֵי — אָנוּס הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The *tanna* of the *baraita*,** who permits a mourner to cut his hair during the intermediate days of the Festival, **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul, who said:** The legal status of **part of the day is like** that of **an entire** day, **and** therefore **the seventh** day **counts as both** the final day of the seven-day mourning period and the first day of the thirty-day mourning period. From the perspective of the *halakhot* of mourning, one would be permitted to cut his hair; however **since** this day **is Shabbat, he is unavoidably prevented** from doing so, and therefore he is permitted to cut his hair during the intermediate days of the Festival.

Moreover, the *chachamim* agree with Abba Shaul in at least one specific case: where day eight falls on *Shabbat Erev ha-Regel*. In that scenario, they concede that the person may take care of the haircut on *Erev Shabbat*, day seven. Why? Because day eight — which is *Shabbat* — is *Erev ha-Regel*, meaning the *regel* will begin on Sunday and will be *mevatel shloshim*. There is therefore a genuine and concrete reason to permit grooming on day seven: the *regel* is imminent and will terminate *shloshim*, the *avel* simply cannot act on the *Shabbat* that intervenes, and so the *chachamim* allow it on *Erev Shabbat* in light of the exceptional circumstances.

If, however, day seven was *Shabbat Erev ha-Regel*, the situation is more complex. On Abba Shaul's view, day seven is simultaneously the final day of *shiva* and the first of *shloshim*, and the *regel* beginning on Sunday would be *mevatel shloshim*. The *avel* could theoretically have shaved on day six. But no authority permits shaving during the *shiva* period itself — day six is unambiguously within *shiva* — and no one will allow it there. The only possible opportunity was on day seven, but that was *Shabbat*, and no haircut can be taken on *Shabbat*. This is the case where the *avel* truly had no opportunity beforehand, and it is for this case that the *baraita* permits the haircut on *Chol HaMoed*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא דִידַן סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ, וְאַכַּתִּי לָא שְׁלִים אֲבֵילוּת דְּשִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The *tanna*** of **our** mishna, on the other hand, who does not mention that a mourner is permitted to cut his hair on the intermediate days of a Festival, **holds in accordance with** the opinion of the **Rabbis, who say: We do not say** that the legal status of **part of the day is like** that of **an entire** day for a mourner. Therefore, **he has not yet completed** his **seven-**day period of **mourning,** and consequently he will not be permitted to cut his hair even after the Festival, until the completion of the thirty-day mourning period.

The *Mishnah*'s *tanna* does not include this case, because he does not hold by Abba Shaul's principle that day seven counts simultaneously for *shiva* and *shloshim*. Without *miktzat ha-yom ke-kulo*, day seven is purely a *shiva* day, *shloshim* has not yet begun when *Shabbat* arrives, and the *regel* therefore cannot be *mevatel shloshim*, since *shloshim* has not commenced. The *avel* accordingly remains in *shloshim* throughout the *regel* and cannot shave. Only the *baraita's* *tanna*, who follows Abba Shaul, provides the leniency.

### Kohen Mishmar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי כֹּהֵן הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דִּשְׁלִים מִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל — אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְגַלּוֹחֵי עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל! לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּשְׁלִים מִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues and asks: The case of **this priest,** who is permitted to cut his hair during the intermediate days of a Festival, **what are the circumstances? If we say that he completed his watch on the eve of the Festival,** then **he should have cut his hair on the eve of the Festival.** The Gemara answers: **No,** the ruling of the *baraita* is **necessary** in the case **where one completed his watch during the Festival** week itself.

The Gemara then addresses the *kohen* case. If his *mishmar* ended on *Shabbat Erev ha-Regel*, the immediate objection is: take your haircut on Thursday or Friday. The *halacha* is that a *kohen* may not take a haircut during his *mishmar*, and this restriction extends even to Thursday of that week, *likhvod Shabbat*, so that he attends to these things before the rotation begins. So if the *mishmar* ended on *Shabbat* before the *regel*, he had Thursday and Friday available.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא דִידַן סָבַר, כֵּיוָן דִּתְנַן: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה פְּרָקִים בַּשָּׁנָה הָיוּ כׇּל הַמִּשְׁמָרוֹת שָׁווֹת, בְּאֵימוּרֵי הָרְגָלִים, וּבְחִילּוּק לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים — כְּמַאן דְּלָא שְׁלִים מִשְׁמַרְתּוֹ בָּרֶגֶל דָּמֵי. וְתַנָּא בָּרָא סָבַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּשָׁיֵיךְ בְּהָנָךְ מִשְׁמָרוֹת, מִשְׁמַרְתֵּיהּ מִיהָא שְׁלִימָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The *tanna*** of **our** mishna **holds** that **since we learned** in a mishna (*Sukka* 55b): **At the year,** i.e., the three pilgrimage Festivals, **all of the priestly watches share equally in the Festival offerings and in the division of the shewbread** among the priests on Shabbat that occurs on the Festival, therefore, **he is** considered **like one whose watch was not completed during the Festival,** and he may not cut his hair until after the Festival. **And the *tanna* of the *baraita* holds that although** he **belongs** also **to those** other **watches** serving during the Festival, **nevertheless, his own watch was completed** before the Festival, and so he is permitted to cut his hair.

The Gemara's answer: the case is one where his *mishmar* ended during the *regel* itself — he was on rotation through *Chol HaMoed*, and was only free from service in the middle of the *regel*. He genuinely had no opportunity beforehand.

Our *Mishnah*'s *tanna* does not include this case, for the following reason. The *Mishnah* elsewhere teaches that during the three *regalim*, all the *mishmarot* had equal rights to serve in the *Beit HaMikdash* and equal portions in the communal *korbanot* and in the distribution of the *lechem ha-panim*. Since during the *regel* all the *mishmarot* participate equally, the *tanna* reasons that even after a *kohen's* specific rotation has technically ended, the *regel* itself is treated as an extension of his *mishmar* — *k'man delo shalim mishmarto ba-regel dami*, it is as though his *mishmar* has not yet ended. And since his *mishmar* has not ended, the prohibition on taking a haircut during one's *mishmar* continues to apply, and the rationale for the *Chol HaMoed* exception — that he had no earlier opportunity — is undermined, because the exception would be trumped by the ongoing *mishmar* restriction.

The *baraita*'s *tanna* disagrees: *af al gav d'shayaich b'hanech mishmaros* — even though he shares in the communal service of the *regel* — *mishmartei mi-ashlima* — his specific rotation has concluded. He is no longer under the obligation specific to his *mishmar*, and since he had no opportunity to shave before the *regel* began, he may do so on *Chol HaMoed*.

## Multiple Aveilus

### Haircuts During Aveilus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל אֵלּוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ מוּתָּרִין לְגַלֵּחַ בַּמּוֹעֵד — מוּתָּרִין לְגַלֵּחַ בִּימֵי אֶבְלָן. וְהָתַנְיָא: אֲסוּרִים! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב שֵׁילָא: כִּי תַּנְיָא הָכָא מוּתָּרִין — בְּשֶׁתְּכָפוּהוּ אֲבָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: **All those about whom it was said** that they **are permitted to cut their hair on** the intermediate days of **a Festival** because they were unable to do so beforehand, **they may** similarly **cut their hair during the period of their mourning** if they had been unable to do so beforehand. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: **They are** nevertheless **prohibited** from cutting their hair during the period of their mourning? **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Rav Sheila said: When it is taught** in the *baraita* that **they are permitted** to cut their hair, it is referring to one **for whom** two periods of **mourning applied in succession.** In other words, this person was required to practice one period of mourning immediately following another period of mourning, and he is unable to endure the prescribed restrictions for such an extended period.

The Gemara continues: *kol elu she-amru mutarin legaleach ba-moed, mutarin legaleach bimei evlam* — all those granted a dispensation to take a haircut on *Chol HaMoed* are similarly granted a dispensation to take a haircut during their *aveilus*. This is a somewhat surprising category. The logic of the *Chol HaMoed* dispensation is built on the incentive to prepare in advance, and those who were genuinely unable to prepare receive an exception. But *aveilus* is not a festival; its haircut prohibition is not designed to create an advance incentive. It is an expression of mourning itself. Why would the inability to prepare beforehand change whether one may shave during *aveilus*?

The *baraita* then appears to say these people are *asur* — forbidden. The contradiction is resolved by Rav Chisda in the name of Rav Shila: *ki tanya hacha mutarin b'shetakfu avelav* — the dispensation here applies only where the *aveilus* has piled up on the person, where multiple periods of mourning have come one after another and the cumulative burden has become genuinely oppressive.

### Overlapping Aveilus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּשֶׁתְּכָפוּהוּ אֲבָלָיו, מַאי אִירְיָא כׇּל אֵלּוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ, אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא נָמֵי? דְּתַנְיָא: תְּכָפוּהוּ אֲבָלָיו זֶה אַחַר זֶה, הִכְבִּיד שְׂעָרוֹ — מֵיקֵל בְּתַעַר, וּמְכַבֵּס כְּסוּתוֹ בְּמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** this is referring to a case **where his mourning applied in succession,** then **why specifically** does the *baraita* discuss **all those about whom it was said** that they may cut their hair? The allowance should apply **even to everyone,** not only to those who had been unavoidably prevented from cutting their hair in the preceding period, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one's mourning** periods **applied in succession, one after the other,** and **his hair** grew long and **became heavy, he may lighten** it by cutting his hair **with a razor, and he may wash his garment in water,** so that he does not suffer for such a long time without cutting his hair or washing his clothes.

If that is the rationale — cumulative burden — the Gemara asks: why limit this to special categories like one released from prison? A *baraita* already states that if one's *aveilus* *takfu avelo, zeh achar zeh* — comes pressing one after another — *hichbid se'aro, meikal b'ta'ar* — his hair has become heavy, he may lighten it with a razor — *u'michabes k'suto b'mayim* — and he may launder his garments with water. This general leniency already exists for any person facing overlapping *aveilus*.

### Razor vs. Scissors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּתַעַר וְלֹא בְּמִסְפָּרַיִם, בְּמַיִם וְלֹא בְּנֶתֶר וְלֹא בְּאָהָל. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת אָבֵל אָסוּר בְּתִכְבּוֹסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **But wasn't it said with regard to that** *baraita*: **Rav Ḥisda said:** When it states that one may cut his hair, it means that he may do so **with a razor, but not with scissors?** Similarly, when it states that one may wash his clothes, it means that he may launder them **with water, but not with *neter* or soap.** Therefore, the *baraita* teaches that if one had been unavoidably prevented from performing these actions beforehand and then had to observe a double mourning period, he may cut his hair in an ordinary manner, without performing these actions in an altered way. **Rav Ḥisda said: That is to say** that, generally speaking, **a mourner is prohibited from laundering** his clothes.

The Gemara answers that the general leniency is not unlimited. Rav Chisda specifies: *b'ta'ar v'lo b'misparayim* — with a razor, not with scissors; a full haircut is still not permitted, only a lightening of the hair. *B'mayim v'lo b'neter* — with water, but not with detergent. Where the circumstances are more extreme — the overlapping *aveilus* plus, say, an extended period in prison — the leniency expands to permit scissors and normal laundering. The ordinary case of sequential *aveilus* receives only partial relief.

### Laundry Prohibition

The Gemara closes with an inference drawn by Rav Chisda: *Rav Chisda zos omeret, avel asur b'tichboset* — this entire discussion demonstrates that an *avel* is generally forbidden from laundering garments. Since it was necessary to create a specific leniency — limited in scope, permitting only water without detergent — to allow any laundering at all under the press of multiple *aveilus* periods, this proves that in the normal case, even laundering garments without detergent is forbidden to an *avel*. The leniency is only possible against the background of a baseline prohibition; without such a prohibition, there would have been nothing to relax.
