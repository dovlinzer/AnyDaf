# Bava Kamma 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nechasim Hamiyuchadim

### Mishnah Introduction

The *mishnah* introduces the concept of *nechasim hamiyuchadim* — designated or assigned property — as one of the conditions for liability in *nezikin*. The first order of business is to determine what this phrase excludes. Picking up approximately twelve lines down on 13b, the *gemara* asks: *lemautei mai* — what does it come to exclude?

### Two Oxen Case

*Amar Rav Yehuda lemautei zeh omer shorcha hezik vezeh omer shorcha hezik* — Rav Yehuda explains that *nechasim hamiyuchadim* comes to exclude the case where it is not clear whose ox caused the damage. Two oxen were running after a third ox, the third was gored, and now Reuven and Shimon are each pointing the finger at the other. The *gemara* immediately challenges this reading: that case is already taught explicitly later in the *mishnah* — *hayu shenayim rodfim achar echad*, two oxen chasing one, *zeh omer shorcha hezik vezeh omer shorcha hezik*, and the ruling is *shenayim peturin*, both are exempt, by the classic principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*. The *gemara* resolves this by saying the *mishnah* first states the abstract principle and then later makes it explicit — *tani ve'ader mefaresh*. That is one reading of the *mishnah*.

### Hefker Ox Analysis

A *baraita* offers a second interpretation: *prat lenichsei hefker* — *nechasim hamiyuchadim* excludes ownerless property. The *gemara* then probes what case this refers to.

If our ox gored a *hefker* ox — *denagach tora didan letora dehefker* — obviously one is exempt. There is no plaintiff; *man tavei lei*, who would even bring the matter to *beis din*? That is trivial.

The more interesting case is the reverse: an ownerless ox gored one of ours. One might think the solution is simple — just go ahead and take possession of the ox, since it is ownerless. Whatever damage it did to you, you can simply recoup by claiming the *hefker* animal. But the *gemara* refines the case: *beshekadam vezacha bo acher* — what if someone else, say Reuven, got there first and took possession of the *hefker* ox before you could? Now, one might reason that since the *halacha* in a case of a *tam* ox (one that has not gored before) is that payment comes from the body of the ox itself, perhaps you automatically acquired a partial ownership in that ox from the moment it did the damage — a lien, as it were, of one hundred dollars' worth of an ox that caused you a hundred dollars of damage.

The *chiddush* is that this is not so. Without an owner, no liability is triggered at all. You do not acquire any partial ownership in an ox that gored yours if that ox had no owner at the time of the goring. This is not merely an arbitrary exemption. The entire framework of *nezikin* presupposes a person who is liable — either because of negligence or because their property caused the damage. It does not operate as some metaphysical mechanism by which the damaging animal itself becomes partially owned by the victim. Without an owner, the process never begins.

### Ravina's Reading

Ravina offers an even more striking *chiddush* as a third reading of *nechasim hamiyuchadim*: *nagach ve'achar kach hikdish, nagach ve'achar kach hefkir* — my ox gored, and after it gored and I am already liable, I sanctify the ox to the *beis hamikdash* (*hekdesh*), or I renounce ownership (*mafkir*), and thereby escape payment. The idea that one can exit an existing liability through a post-damage transfer of ownership is quite a remarkable claim.

A *baraita* confirms this position: *af illu nagach ve'achar kach hikdish nagach ve'achar kach hefkir patur* — even if after the goring you sanctify or renounce ownership, you are exempt.

## Kofer & Ownership Transf…

### Rav Yehuda on Kofer

The *baraita* cited by Ravina deals specifically with the *kofer* payment — the atonement payment owed when a *shor ha-mu'ad* kills a person. Rav Yehuda teaches that if after the ox kills someone the owner sanctifies or renounces the ox, he is exempt from paying *kofer*. The *pasuk* the *gemara* invokes is *vehu'ad beva'alav vehameis ish* — the ox was warned against the owner and it killed a man or a woman — followed by *ha-shor yisakeil*, the ox shall be stoned.

### Same Status Requirement

The *derasha* is *ad she-tehei misa ve-ha'amada ba-din shavin* — the killing and the adjudication must occur in the same legal context, with the same status of ownership. More precisely, the *gemara* clarifies: from the moment of the killing through the *gmar din* — the issuance of the final verdict — the ox must remain in the same ownership status. *Shavin ke-echad*: the two events must be aligned. If I transfer the ox to *hefker* or *hekdesh* before the *ha'amada ba-din*, I have transferred it into a status that would have exempted me from liability had the ox been in that status at the time of the goring. And so I am off the hook.

The *gemara* raises an obvious question: does it suffice to transfer to *hefker* or *hekdesh* and then reclaim? That is left as an open question. The exemption appears to apply when the ox is in that exempt status at the moment of *ha'amada ba-din*, but whether reclaiming it afterwards reimposes liability is not resolved.

### Extension Beyond Kofer

What is striking is that the *gemara* does not explicitly derive from any *pasuk* how the *kofer* rule — with its requirement that ownership status remain consistent from goring through adjudication — extends to ordinary cases of property damage. There is no textual bridge. And the extension produces a profound injustice: my ox causes a thousand dollars of damage; the ox is worth a hundred dollars; I simply *mafkir* it and escape paying a thousand dollars in damages.

Some *acharonim* suggest, and this resolution is compelling even if the *gemara* does not make it explicit, that the *gemara* may intend to limit this rule to *keren tam* — the case of an unhabituated ox goring for the first three times, where only half-damages are paid.

### Knas vs. Monetary Obliga…

Two reasons converge to make this rule work specifically for *keren tam*. First, there is a debate — to be addressed later — as to whether half-damages for *tam* is a *knas* (a fine) rather than genuine compensation (*mamon*). If it is a *knas*, then the liability does not arise automatically at the moment of the event. In ordinary cases of damage — where my ox tramples your produce or falls into your pit — I owe you money from the instant the damage occurs; *beis din* merely clarifies what already exists. A *knas*, by contrast, is imposed by *beis din*; I do not owe anything until the court imposes it. Accordingly, if the ox is in a legally exempt status — *hefker* or *hekdesh* — at the time *beis din* adjudicates, *beis din* cannot impose the fine because the ox's status has changed.

Second, and just as importantly, this limitation dissolves the injustice problem. When my ox gores for the first three times, my liability is capped at half-damages and is further limited by the value of the ox itself. I never pay more than what the ox is worth. So *mafkir*-ing the ox does not save me any money — indeed, I may lose by renouncing it. The perverse incentive to escape a large damage obligation by abandoning a small ox does not arise if my liability was already capped at the ox's value.

By contrast, applying this rule to full compensatory damages — where an ox worth a hundred dollars could shield me from a thousand-dollar judgment — would be both technically incoherent and deeply unjust. The *gemara* leaves this gap unexplained, and this is one of those moments where the reasoning works compellingly only when we confine the principle to its natural domain. As noted, *ein danin dinei kenasos be-Vavel* — courts in Babylonia do not adjudicate *kenas* matters at all — which adds another dimension to why the *beis din* must be involved for the fine to take effect.

## Own Domain Exemption

### Tosafot's Principle

The *mishnah* states *chutz me-reshut ha-meyuchedet la-mazik* — the damager is exempt when the damage occurs in his own domain. Tosafot articulates this as a broad principle: there is no liability for damage that occurs within the damager's own property when the victim's animal or produce entered without permission.

### Risk & Freedom Logic

The logic is straightforward. If your ox wanders into my property without permission, I can say to you: *torcha bi-reshuti mai ba'ei* — what is your ox doing on my property? You came here of your own accord. I have the right to do whatever I wish on my own property. I am not obligated to police my own animals in my own space to protect a trespasser. You assumed the risk by entering.

This is worth pausing over. The exemption is not merely technical — it reflects a deep principle that a property owner retains freedom of action within his domain, and that a victim who enters without authorization cannot impose liability on the property owner for events that occur there. The *rishonim* make explicit what the *gemara* does not spell out: this applies only when the victim entered without permission. If the damager invited the victim in, the calculus shifts entirely — the invitation removes the predicate for the exemption.

## Shared Domain Debate

### Mishnah Reading

The *mishnah* then presents what turns out to be a genuinely ambiguous line. After establishing the exemption for the damager's own domain, the *mishnah* says *ve-reshut ha-nizak ve-ha-mazik* — the domain of both the damagee and the damager — and then concludes *ke-she-hizik chav ha-mazik le-shalem* — when damage occurs, the damager is obligated to pay.

The question is how to parse this. Is *ve-reshut ha-nizak ve-ha-mazik* part of the *chutz* clause — meaning one is also exempt in a shared domain — and the final phrase is simply restating the general principle of liability? Or does the sentence structure mean that in a shared domain, unlike in the damager's own domain, one is liable to pay?

This ambiguity generates a significant *machloket* among the *amora'im*.

### Rav Chisda's View

Rav Chisda citing Avimi says: *chatzar hashtufin chayav bah al ha-shen ve-al ha-regel* — in a shared domain, one is liable for *shen* and *regel*. It is worth noting why the debate about a shared domain is confined to *shen* and *regel* and does not extend to *keren*. By *keren*, one is liable in *reshut ha-rabim*; the only exemption is when the victim's animal entered the damager's exclusive domain without permission. A shared domain by definition is one where the victim has a right to be — so there is no question one is liable for *keren* in a shared domain. The interesting question is only *shen* and *regel*.

For *shen* and *regel*, the Torah establishes liability in *sadeh acher* — the other person's field — and exemption in *reshut ha-rabim*. A shared domain sits between these poles. From one angle: the exemption in *reshut ha-rabim* is a special case driven by the need to preserve freedom of use of public thoroughfares and by *pshiat ha-nizak* (the victim's own negligence in placing property in a high-traffic area); anything that is not a *reshut ha-rabim* should generate liability, including a shared domain. From the other angle: liability in *shen* and *regel* is specifically tethered to *sadeh acher*; without the quality of "another's domain," there is no liability, and a shared domain where both parties have rights is not *sadeh acher*.

Rav Chisda adopts the first reading: a shared domain is not a *reshut ha-rabim*, so one is liable. The *mishnah*'s final phrase — *ke-she-hizik chav ha-mazik le-shalem* — according to Rav Chisda is the *mishnah*'s affirmative ruling about a shared domain. According to this reading, *ve-reshut ha-nizak ve-ha-mazik* establishes that in a shared domain the damager pays. And the general principle at the end — *ke-she-hizik chav ha-mazik* — is read, according to Shmuel, as coming to include *keren*, while according to Rav, who understands *shor* in the first *mishnah* to already include *keren*, it comes to include the case of a *shomer* — a watchman — whose animal causes damage, a topic the *gemara* develops in a substantial digression.

The *gemara* addresses the *shomer* case at length. The *baraita* states that when an animal under a *shomer*'s care does damage in his domain, a *tam* pays half and a *mu'ad* pays full; but if the animal broke out at night or bandits breached the fence — cases of *onnes* — the *shomer* is exempt. The *chiddush* is not merely that a *shomer* bears responsibility for damage the animal he is watching causes to third parties, which is intuitive enough. The *gemara* pushes into harder cases.

If the ox the *shomer* was watching gored the *shomer*'s own ox, why should the original owner pay? The *shomer* would ordinarily pay for damage the ox causes to third parties, so why should the original owner compensate the *shomer* for damage his own watched ox inflicted on the *shomer*? The reverse case is equally puzzling: if the *shomer*'s own ox gored the ox he was watching, one might think he owes full compensation as a *shomer* — since a *shomer* bears full liability for damage to the item he is guarding, regardless of the cause — not merely half as a *mazik*. The *gemara* resolves these difficulties by introducing a case of stipulation: the *shomer* accepted responsibility for guarding the animal from harm (*shmiras gufo*) but explicitly did not accept responsibility for damage the animal causes (*shmiras nizko*). In such a case, if the watched ox — with no liability resting on the *shomer* for its actions — gores the *shomer*'s own ox, the original owner remains liable. And conversely, the *shomer*'s ox goring the watched ox is addressed by distinguishing between *shomer* liability and *mazik* liability based on what was stipulated.

### Rabbi Elazar's View

Rabbi Elazar takes the opposite position: *patur al ha-shen ve-al ha-regel* — in a shared domain, one is exempt from *shen* and *regel*. According to Rabbi Elazar's reading, *chutz me-reshut ha-meyuchedet la-mazik ve-reshut ha-nizak ve-ha-mazik nami patur* — the exemption clause encompasses both the damager's exclusive domain and a shared domain. The only context in which one is liable for *shen* and *regel* is *sadeh acher* — the victim's domain exclusively. The final phrase *ke-she-hizik chav ha-mazik* then, according to Rabbi Elazar, does not refer to a shared domain at all but is a separate clause — and the *gemara* explains it comes to include *keren*, on the theory that one is liable for *keren* even in a shared domain.

### Baraitot Contradiction

The *gemara* now confronts a direct conflict between two *baraitot*. Rav Yosef cites a *baraita*: *chatzar hashtufin ve-hapundak chayav bahen al ha-shen ve-al ha-regel* — a shared domain and an inn, one is liable for *shen* and *regel*. This appears to be a flat refutation of Rabbi Elazar.

Rabbi Elazar responds that there is an equal and opposite *baraita*: the four categories articulated by Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar in *nezikin*. First category: it is exclusively the damagee's domain (*reshut le-nizak ve-lo le-mazik*) — liable for everything, *chayav bakol*, including *keren*. Second: exclusively the damager's domain (*le-mazik ve-lo le-nizak*) — fully exempt, *patur mikol*, because *torcha bi-reshusi mai ba'ei*. Third: a shared domain (*laze u-laze*), exemplified by *chatzar hashtufin* or a *bika* (valley) — *patur bahen al ha-shen ve-al ha-regel*, exempt from *shen* and *regel*, but liable for *keren* (half-damages for *tam*, full for *mu'ad*). Fourth: neither party's domain (*lo laze ve-lo laze*), described as a domain belonging to neither of them — *chayav al ha-shen ve-al ha-regel*, liable for *shen* and *regel*.

This *baraita* says precisely the opposite of Rav Yosef's *baraita* regarding a shared domain: one is liable according to Rav Yosef's source, exempt according to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. We have an outright contradiction between *baraitot*.

## Reconciling Baraitot

### Rav Yosef's Distinction

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction by introducing a crucial distinction: *ki tanya hi be-chatzer meyuchedet laze u-laze bein la-peiros bein li-shvarim* — the *baraita* that exempts applies to a shared domain in which both parties have full rights: the right to bring their oxen *and* the right to keep their fruit there. *Hahi de-Rav Yosef be-chatzer meyuchedet la-peiros ve-eina meyuchedet li-shvarim* — the *baraita* that creates liability applies to a domain where one has the right to bring fruit but not to bring oxen.

This is the key: what does it mean to have a "right" to be in a given domain? The *gemara* is drawing a distinction between the right to deposit fruit and the right to bring cattle. In the case of Rav Yosef, the victim has a right to have their fruit in this shared courtyard, but the damager has no right to bring their ox there. From the perspective of the ox, it has entered a space it has no right to occupy — and that is functionally equivalent to *sadeh acher*. In the case of the *baraita* that exempts, both parties have the right to bring their oxen, so the ox's presence is entirely legitimate, and there is no basis for liability in *shen* and *regel*.

The *gemara* observes that this reading is confirmed by the language of the two sources themselves: the *baraita* that rules liability spoke of a case comparable to an inn (*pundak*) — one brings luggage to an inn, not oxen; the inn grants rights over fruit and belongings, not over animals. The *baraita* that rules exempt spoke of a *bika*, a valley — a space where one has an unquestioned right to bring one's animal to graze.

### Zevid-Abaye Exchange

Rav Zevid challenges this reconciliation: if the victim has rights to the domain for fruit, can one really say it is *sadeh acher*? The *pasuk* requires *u-vi'er bi-sdei acher*, and here the victim has some rights. Abaye responds: *keivan de-eina meyuchedet li-shvarim, sdeh acher karinan beih* — since the domain is not designated for oxen, we call it *sadeh acher* from the ox's perspective. The victim may have a right to bring fruit there; the ox had no right to be there at all. The ox's right of entry — or lack thereof — is the operative question.

### Mixed Rights Scenario

With the *baraitot* reconciled, the *gemara* asks whether the *amora'im* — Rav Chisda and Rabbi Elazar — were also just talking about different scenarios and do not in fact disagree. The *gemara* answers: possibly. If we are generous, Rav Chisda was speaking about a case where only fruit rights exist (no ox rights), which is functionally *sadeh acher*, and Rabbi Elazar was speaking about a case where both ox rights and fruit rights exist, which is not *sadeh acher* — and both would agree on both cases.

Or, says the *gemara*, if we say they do genuinely disagree, their disagreement is confined to the *kushya* of Rav Zevid and the resolution of Abaye: the case where one has rights for oxen but not for fruit. One position says this is *sadeh acher* because the ox had no right there; the other says you cannot call it *sadeh acher* when the victim has some right in the domain, even if it is only a fruit right.

### Core Principle Extraction

What the *gemara* is doing conceptually throughout this entire discussion is decisive and important. The operative question is never whether a domain resembles *reshut ha-rabim* closely enough to generate an exemption. The *pasuk* does not say: exempt in *reshut ha-rabim*, liable elsewhere. The *pasuk* says *u-vi'er bi-sdei acher* — liable specifically in *sadeh acher*. Accordingly, you are liable in *shen* and *regel* only when you can define the location as *sadeh acher*. And *sadeh acher* requires, at minimum, that the ox had no right to be in that domain. Any time the ox had a right to be there, we cannot call it *sadeh acher*, and liability in *shen* and *regel* is off the table.

The *bika* is in the exempt category precisely because, even if it is technically ownerless, one's ox has a right to be there — it is the kind of space where animals graze freely. The *pundak* is in the liable category because, even though one may have rights to store property there, one does not have a right to bring an ox into an inn.

## Four Categories Analysis

### Damagee's Domain Only

Returning to the *baraita* of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, the *gemara* now unpacks each of the four categories in detail. The first category — *kol she-reshut le-nizak ve-lo le-mazik*, the domain is exclusively the damagee's — generates liability for everything: *chayav bakol*. The *gemara* emphasizes that the *baraita* does not say *al ha-kol* (for all the types of damages) but *bakol* (fully, completely). This reads as: one pays full damages, even for *keren*. This follows Rabbi Tarfon's position: *meshuneh keren be-chatzer ha-nizak nezek shalem meshalem* — when an unusual act of *keren* occurs in the damagee's domain, full damages are owed even if the ox is a *tam*. When the ox invaded a space it had absolutely no right to enter and committed an act of damage there, the elevated circumstance justifies full payment.

### Damager's Domain Only

The second category — *le-mazik ve-lo le-nizak*, exclusively the damager's domain — is *patur mikol*, fully exempt. The victim's property entered without right; *torcha bi-reshusi mai ba'ei*, the damager is under no duty of care.

### Shared Domain Breakdown

The third category — *laze u-laze*, shared domain, exemplified by *chatzar hashtufin* and a *bika* — is *patur* for *shen* and *regel* but liable for *keren*: *tam* pays half, *mu'ad* pays full. As established above, in a shared domain where both parties have rights to bring their oxen, the ox's presence is legitimate and *sadeh acher* cannot be invoked. But *keren* — which is liable even in *reshut ha-rabim* — applies in any domain that is not exclusively the damager's, so liability for *keren* persists.

### Neither's Domain

The fourth category — *lo laze ve-lo laze*, neither party's domain — initially appears to refer to a third party's domain: my ox enters Reuven's yard and eats Shimon's fruit. But the *gemara* rejects this reading: the *pasuk* of *u-vi'er bi-sdei acher* would not be satisfied, because the fruit consumed must belong to the person whose domain was entered. If my ox is in Reuven's yard eating Shimon's fruit, Shimon's property is not in Shimon's domain.

The *gemara* therefore reinterprets *lo laze ve-lo laze* not as "neither party," but as "not both" — meaning it is one person's domain, namely the damagee's. It is *lo laze* (not the damager's) *ve-lo laze* (and not both of theirs) — it is only the damagee's. And therefore one is liable for *shen* and *regel* as in the first category.

This creates an apparent redundancy: the first and fourth categories seem identical. The *gemara* resolves this by noting that the final category carries a different ruling for *keren* — *tam* pays half and *mu'ad* pays full — which follows *rabbanan*, who hold that even in *chatzer ha-nizak* a *tam* pays only half for *keren*. The beginning of the *baraita* followed Rabbi Tarfon (who rules full payment for *keren* in *chatzer ha-nizak*), and the end follows *rabbanan*. Shmuel advised Rav Yehuda: *shevok matnitin*, do not try to reconcile the *baraita* as a unified position — the *reisha* is Rabbi Tarfon and the *seifa* is *rabbanan*.

Ravina citing Rav offers an alternative: the entire *baraita* follows Rabbi Tarfon. The fourth category is not the damagee's domain but rather a domain that is *lo laze ve-lo laze la-peiros ela de-chad, lazeh ve-lazeh li-shvarim* — neither party has exclusive rights to the fruit (only one of them does), but both have rights to bring their oxen. According to this reading, both are permitted to bring oxen, so from the perspective of *keren* it resembles *reshut ha-rabim* (the ox had every right to be there), and the *tam* pays only half. But since only one party has the right to keep fruit there, from the perspective of *shen* it constitutes *chatzer ha-nizak*, and the damager is fully liable.

## Fruit vs. Ox Rights

### Mixed Rights Setup

This brings us to the final and most complex scenario — the one the *gemara* did not explicitly adjudicate but which generates a *machloket* between Rashi and Tosafot. The case, as read by Rashi from the *girsa* of Ravina citing Rav: both parties have rights to bring their oxen to this domain, but only the damagee (not the damager) has the right to keep fruit there. My ox therefore enters a space where it has every right to be — but it eats your fruit, which is in a space where you have a right to keep it and I do not.

### Rashi's Approach

According to Rashi, this scenario splits the liability. With respect to *shen* — the eating of the fruit — it is considered *chatzer ha-nizak*, the damagee's domain. Even though my ox had a right to be there, the absence of my right to keep fruit there means it is, to that degree, *sadeh acher*: the domain is "another's" with respect to what was consumed. Rashi thus holds that *sadeh acher* is satisfied whenever any element of the domain is exclusively the other person's — it need not be that the ox had no right to be there; it is enough that I had no right to the fruit that was consumed. Accordingly, the damager pays fully for *shen* and *regel*.

With respect to *keren*, however — since both parties have the right to bring their oxen — it resembles *reshut ha-rabim*, and a *tam* pays only half.

### Tosafot's Objection

Tosafot rejects this. Rabbeinu Tam's reading reverses the critical variable: the relevant question for *sadeh acher* is always and only whether the ox had a right to be in the domain. If my ox had a right to be there, no element of "another's field" is present, and *shen* and *regel* cannot generate liability. For Tosafot, *sadeh acher* requires that the ox itself had no right of entry — the fruit rights of either party are secondary. Accordingly, Rabbeinu Tam reads *lo laze ve-lo laze* as: neither party has rights to bring their ox there — and then, with respect to *shen*, it is *chatzer ha-nizak*; with respect to *keren*, because both ox rights are absent, it resembles *reshut ha-rabim*.

The divide is thus fundamental. For Rashi, *sadeh acher* is a function of the rights to the property consumed: as long as the victim has a right to the fruit and the damager does not, it is sufficiently "another's domain" to generate liability in *shen*. For Tosafot, *sadeh acher* is solely a function of the ox's right of entry: if my ox had a right to be there at all, the domain cannot be called *sadeh acher*, whatever my rights to the fruit. This sharp disagreement about the precise definition of *sadeh acher* will carry forward into subsequent discussions.
