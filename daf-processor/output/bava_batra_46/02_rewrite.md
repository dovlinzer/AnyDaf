# Bava Batra 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava vs. Abaye

### Two Axes

The Gemara on Daf 46 opens in the middle of a debate between Rava and Abaye concerning the rule stated in the Mishnah: *uman ein lo chazakah* — a craftsman does not acquire a *chazakah* (presumption of ownership) over items in his possession. To understand the structure of this debate, two independent axes must be held in mind simultaneously.

The first axis concerns *eidim* — witnesses. Were there witnesses present when the owner gave the item to the craftsman, or not? The second axis concerns *ra'ah* — visual observation. Do we currently see the item in the craftsman's possession, or not? The obvious case in which the craftsman clearly has no *chazakah* is when witnesses saw the owner give it to the craftsman, and the craftsman is currently seen holding it. Conversely, the obvious case in which the craftsman would have a *chazakah* is when there were no witnesses and nobody currently sees the item in his hands — he could plausibly claim he never received it, or that he returned it long ago.

The dispute between Rava and Abaye concerns which of these two axes is the dominant criterion.

### Rava: Witnesses

Rava's position is that *eidim* are the controlling criterion. Once it is established through witnesses that the owner gave the item to the craftsman for a professional purpose — say, for dry cleaning — the craftsman is never believed to claim ownership of it, even if nobody currently sees the item in his hands. This follows from the principle *hamapkid etzel chaveiro be'eidim tzarich lehachziro be'eidim*: one who deposits an item with another in the presence of witnesses must return it in the presence of witnesses. Since the craftsman cannot demonstrate that he returned the item via witnesses, he is not believed to say he returned it, and by extension, not believed to say he purchased it.

A striking implication immediately surfaces: this principle has nothing specifically to do with the craftsman's professional status. The same rule would apply to any person who received any item as a *pikadon* (deposit) in the presence of witnesses. If I deposit something with a neighbor in front of witnesses and the neighbor later claims he bought it from me, he too would be disbelieved. Rava's position, then, does not actually explain why the Mishnah singles out the *uman*. The Gemara will press this point at length, and *Tosafot* addresses how it might nonetheless remain connected to the *uman* context — but the tension is real and acknowledged.

### Abaye: Possession

Abaye's position is far more directly tied to the *uman*'s unique professional role. For Abaye, the controlling criterion is *ra'ah* — whether we currently observe the item in the craftsman's possession. The logic is as follows: a dry cleaner is the type of person to whom one naturally gives one's suit. Therefore, when we see a dry cleaner holding a suit we knew to be someone else's, that very combination — craftsman plus item of the type he handles — strips away his *chazakah*, even without any witnesses to the original handover. Conversely, if we do not see the item in his hands, Abaye holds that the craftsman is believed to claim ownership, because there is no current visual evidence contradicting his claim.

This approach maps cleanly onto the concept of *uman ein lo chazakah*. A normal person seen holding an item we knew was yours would be believed to say he purchased it — *chazakah mah she-tachat yad adam harei hu shelo*, what a person holds is presumed to be his. But a dry cleaner holding your suit, or a leather worker holding your leather goods, cannot avail himself of that presumption. The *uman*'s professional identity transforms the meaning of possession. If a dry cleaner were holding your shoes, however, he would be believed — shoes are not the type of item one gives a dry cleaner. The professional relationship between the craftsman's specialty and the item in question is what triggers *ain lo chazakah*.

### Rava Retracts

A *baraita* was introduced that appeared to demonstrate that the critical variable is indeed *ra'ah* — visual observation — rather than *eidim*. Confronted with this evidence, Rava retracted his broader position and limited himself to a much narrower claim: *ein lo chazakah* applies only when both criteria are simultaneously present — both witnesses to the original transfer and current visible possession. That, of course, is the uncontested case everyone agrees upon from the outset. Rava's retraction thus left him in agreement with the obvious case and added nothing beyond it. This is where the discussion stood at the close of the previous session.

## Craft Fee Dispute

### Fee Dispute

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita* as part of Rava's attempt to support the position of *Rabba* — the view that *eidim* are the dominant criterion. The case is as follows: a person gives his *tallis* to a craftsman (*noten taliso le'uman*). The craftsman says they agreed on a fee of two *zuz*, while the owner says they agreed on one. Who is believed? The Mishnah rules: *kol zeman she-tallis beyad uman* — as long as the garment remains in the craftsman's hands, the burden of proof falls on the owner (*al habaal habayis l'havi re'iyah*). The craftsman is believed, because of *migo* — since he could have claimed outright that he purchased the garment, he is believed on the lesser claim of the disputed fee amount.

This is a striking *migo*: the craftsman's credibility on the fee dispute derives from his latent ability to claim ownership of the garment entirely.

### Payment Timing

The Gemara continues with the next clause: if the craftsman has already returned the garment and payment is still owed, the rule changes depending on timing. *Bizmano nishba venotel* — within the normal period of payment, the craftsman may take an oath and collect what he claims is owed him. This is a *takanas chachamim*, a rabbinic enactment, based on the reality that an employer is often too distracted and occupied with paying multiple workers simultaneously to keep careful track; therefore the worker is trusted under oath during this period. *Avar zemano* — once the normal payment window has passed, the craftsman who allowed the deadline to lapse without demanding payment is viewed with greater suspicion; perhaps he was not paid precisely because he was already paid and forgot, or perhaps he waited because he was being cavalier. In that case, *hamotzi mechavero alav hare'iyah* — the one seeking to collect bears the burden of proof.

*Tosafot* raises a pointed difficulty with this entire temporal framework: the classic case in which *bizmano nishba venotel* makes sense is when the parties dispute *whether* payment occurred at all. There, the logic is clean — the employer is distracted at the moment of payment, and if the worker truly had not been paid, he would have pressed his claim immediately. But the case here is a dispute over *what was originally agreed upon* — a conversation that took place at the time of the original commission, not at the time of payment. Neither the distraction of the payment moment nor the significance of delayed demand maps naturally onto this scenario. *Tosafot* raises this problem but does not fully resolve it.

### Scenario Analysis

The Gemara now probes what scenario the Mishnah is actually describing — specifically, which combination of *eidim* and *ra'ah* is at play. The question: in what situation is it interesting and non-trivial to say that the craftsman is believed to claim he could assert ownership of the garment (*lekucha hi beyadi*)?

One possibility: there are witnesses to the original transfer, but nobody currently sees the garment in his hands. This would favor Abaye's opponents — even with *eidim*, and without current *ra'ah*, the craftsman is believed. The Gemara dismisses this: if witnesses were present at the original handover, we could simply ask them what fee was stipulated, and there would be no dispute. So witnesses cannot be present in the Mishnah's case.

The Gemara then reasons: *ela lav deleika edim* — it must be that there are no witnesses. The interesting scenario, then, must be one where there are no witnesses but the garment is seen in the craftsman's possession — and even so, he is believed. This would support the view that the determinative axis is *ra'ah*, not *eidim*: even without witnesses, when we see the item in his hands, the craftsman is still believed because of *migo*.

The Gemara responds: *le'olam deleika edim, vehu delo ra'ah* — in truth the scenario could simply be one without witnesses *and* without current visible possession. That is the uninteresting, uncontested case where of course the craftsman is believed. The Mishnah does not tell us which scenario it is, so it cannot serve as proof for either position. The *migo* argument from the craftsman's ability to claim ownership tells us nothing about which axis dominates.

### Nachman Challenge

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak now mounts the definitive challenge against Rava. The Mishnah states *uman ain lo chazakah* — *the craftsman* does not have a *chazakah*. The implicit contrast is: *uman hu de'ein lo chazakah, ha'acher yesh lo chazakah* — it is specifically the craftsman who lacks *chazakah*, while a non-craftsman in an otherwise similar situation would have one.

Now, on Rava's account, the determinative factor is *eidim* — witnesses to the original deposit. But the principle *hamapkid etzel chaveiro be'eidim tzarich lehachziro be'eidim* applies universally, to any person who received any item via witnessed deposit. If a non-craftsman received an item from me with witnesses and is now holding it without having returned it via witnesses, he too is disbelieved when he claims he returned or purchased it. There would be nothing special about the *uman* at all — the rule would be *hamapkid etzel chaveiro be'eidim ein lo chazakah*, not *uman ein lo chazakah*.

The Gemara presses: if there *are* witnesses, why would a non-craftsman have a *chazakah*? He wouldn't. So the case must be without witnesses. *Ela lav dileika edim, de-tani uman ein lo chazakah* — it must be a case without witnesses, and yet the Mishnah still says the *uman* lacks *chazakah*. This can only be explained by Abaye's position: what distinguishes the *uman* from any other person is that we *see* the item in his hands (*ra'ah*), combined with his professional identity. A normal person seen holding something I once owned would be believed — *chazakah mah she-tachat yad adam harei hu shelo*. But a craftsman holding an item of the type he works on cannot avail himself of that presumption. We assume the item is there because it was given to him for work, not because he purchased it.

*Tiyuvta de-Rava tiyuvta* — this constitutes a decisive refutation of Rava. The *ikar* is *ra'ah*: professional identity combined with visual possession.

## Mixed Garments

### Baraita: Accidental Mix-…

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita* from Bava Kamma that, while tangentially related, raises a vivid practical question. *Nitchelfu lo keilim be-keilim be-veit ha'uman* — if garments got mixed up in the craftsman's shop and you came to collect yours but were handed someone else's by mistake, the *baraita* rules: *harei zeh yishtamesh bahen ad she-yavo halaz ve-yitol et shelo* — you may use the other person's garment until the person who accidentally took yours returns it, and then you exchange.

The *baraita* immediately contrasts this with a case of garments mixed up at a gathering — a *shiva* house, a wedding, the coat-room at a synagogue. In that scenario, *lo yishtamesh bahen* — you may not use the other person's garment even while waiting for the mix-up to be untangled. You cannot wear a coat you took home by mistake from the synagogue coat-room even while the other person has yours. The logic is unambiguous: two wrongs do not make a right, and the absence of one wrong does not license another. You have no permission to use something that belongs to another person simply because that person is inadvertently holding yours.

The problem, then, is the first case. Why may you use a garment mistakenly received from the craftsman's shop?

### Craftsman Permission

Rav recounts that he was sitting before his beloved uncle, Rav Chiyya, who offered him the following explanation: *vechi ein adam asui lomar le'uman mechor li taliti?* — is it not entirely normal for a person to say to a craftsman, "sell my garment for me, give me the proceeds"? People leave items with craftsmen on a kind of consignment arrangement.

The reasoning proceeds as follows. Suppose Michael left his coat with the dry cleaner and said "sell it for me." Then David comes to pick up his own coat. The dry cleaner, in confusion, gives David Michael's coat. Even though the dry cleaner did not consciously hand David a loaner or a consignment item, the fact is that Michael's coat *was* legitimately within the dry cleaner's authority to transfer — Michael had given him that right. So David's receipt of Michael's coat, while accidental, falls within the category of a legitimate transfer. David is therefore permitted to use the coat until the situation is clarified and exchanges can be made.

In the synagogue coat-room case, by contrast, no one has given anyone permission to distribute their coat. Whoever holds the garments in that context has no authority to transfer them. So when you take the wrong coat home, there is no possible framework under which you received it legitimately, and you may not use it.

The logic requires that we *hypothesize* a plausible permissive scenario — the craftsman may have had the right to give this item out — and that hypothesis provides enough of a foundation to allow use pending resolution. This is not pure license: as soon as it becomes clear that no such permission existed, the item must be returned.

### Craftsman Only

Rav Papia the son of Rav Nachman adds a limitation: *lo shanu ela hu, aval ishto u'vanav lo* — this permissive rule applies only when the craftsman himself handed you the garment, not when his wife or children did so.

The reason lies in the nature of the authority being invoked. The craftsman has discretion over the items in his shop — he is the one who received consignment rights, who agreed to sell garments for customers, who makes the professional decisions about what to give to whom. His wife and children, working behind the counter, are employees in the relevant sense: they lack the independent discretion to make consignment transfers or loaner arrangements. When they hand out the wrong garment, there is no basis to hypothesize that a legitimate transfer has occurred. We can only assume they made a mistake, and therefore you may not use the item.

This has broader application. When a waiter tells you "it's on the house," or a cashier waives a charge, their authority to do so is not self-evident. Only someone with actual discretionary authority over the item or transaction can make such a determination.

### Grammar Matters

A further limitation is introduced: *hu nami lo amrinan ela d'amar leh tallit stam, aval tallit-cha lo* — even when the craftsman himself hands it over, the permissive rule applies only if he says "here is *a* garment" (*tallit stam*), not "here is *your* garment" (*tallit-cha*).

If the craftsman says "here is your garment" and it turns out not to be yours, it is clear he made a mistake — he thought he was returning yours and was wrong. The intent was a specific return, not a consignment transfer or a loaner arrangement. In that case you must give it back. But if he says "here is *a* garment," the phrasing is open enough to be interpreted as a deliberate offer of a different item — a loaner, a consignment piece, something he had authority to transfer. That interpretive latitude is what permits you to keep using it.

The practical application is somewhat subtle. If you are standing at the counter and know immediately that the garment is not yours, the question of what you are obligated to say on the spot is a genuine *she'eila*. If the craftsman has said *tallit stam* and you have already left, the assumption of a permissive transfer is what you rely on. These distinctions in real-time obligation are worth careful attention.

## Pumbedita Scoundrel

### Real-Life Scenario

Abaye, having established the theoretical primacy of *ra'ah*, now turns to a vivid practical illustration of the difficulties that arise when *ra'ah* cannot be cleanly established. He invites Rava: *ta achvei lach rama'ei d'Pumbedita* — come, let me show you the scoundrels of Pumbedita, and how they exploit legal technicalities to cheat people. The Gemara then dramatizes what one such dishonest dry cleaner would do.

A person comes to retrieve his garment. The dry cleaner says: *lav itam me'olam* — I never had it, I don't know what you're talking about.

### Proving Possession

The owner responds: *ha ika sahadei d'chazya gabach* — I have witnesses who saw my garment in your possession. This is not the same as *eidim* who witnessed the original handover. These are witnesses who observed the item — a specific black coat, a distinctive suit — in the craftsman's hands at some point. That constitutes a case of *ra'ah*. And since we have established that *ra'ah* is the operative criterion, this should be sufficient to strip the craftsman of his *chazakah*.

The dry cleaner's counter-move: *ha achreina havai* — that was a different one. You think there is only one coat like yours? The coat my witnesses saw was not yours; it was another customer's, which merely resembled yours. You cannot prove that what they saw was specifically your garment.

The owner says: produce it, let us examine it — *afkinah ve-nechzinah*. The witnesses, if they can see it, may be able to identify it. The dry cleaner refuses: *ibra la mafkinah lah* — I will not produce it. The *Rashbam* explains the dry cleaner's principled-sounding position: in truth (*be'emet*), he is not even claiming that coat as his own — he is saying it belongs to another customer, and he has an obligation to protect the privacy of his clients. He is not going to rummage through his customers' property on the strength of a frivolous claim.

### Rava Concedes

Rava, to the apparent surprise of those listening, sides with the dry cleaner: *shapir ka'amar lei* — he has said correctly. *Ra'ah tanyah* — it is all about *seeing* the specific item. If you cannot establish that what the witnesses saw was definitively *your* garment — if the craftsman can plausibly claim it was a similar garment belonging to a different customer — there is no legally actionable *ra'ah*. Witnesses who saw *a* coat in his possession but cannot identify it as *your specific* coat have provided suspicion, not proof. Suspicion does not override the craftsman's right to withhold. The scoundrel is technically within his legal rights.

This is, as Rava acknowledges, a genuinely frustrating result. Rights are sometimes used to protect wrongdoing. But without the ability to positively identify the specific item, there is no sufficient *ra'ah*, and the claim fails.

### Practical Advice

Rav Ashi offers a clever workaround. If you are dealing with such a craftsman, you can attempt to engineer a *ra'ah* situation that compels him to produce the item. Approach him and say: I understand why you are holding on to my coat — it must be because I owe you back fees. Let us be fair about this. I acknowledge I might owe you something. But you are holding on to a coat worth ten thousand dollars over a debt of perhaps one thousand. Let us bring the item out, have it assessed, you take what is yours and I will take the remainder — *v'shaiminu shkol at didach v'shkol ana didi*. You are not claiming to be a thief; let us resolve this equitably.

If the craftsman agrees and brings out the coat, witnesses will now observe it in his possession and can identify it — a proper *ra'ah* is created, and at that moment the craftsman's *chazakah* collapses.

Rav Acha the son of Rav Avya notes the counter-move available to a sufficiently savvy scoundrel: *lo tzrichna l'shuma didach* — I don't need your assessment. I've already had others appraise it, and I know its value covers your debt. He refuses to produce it and declines the arrangement entirely. One cannot outfox the fox. The practical advice of Rav Ashi works only against a scoundrel who is not quite clever enough.

The episode as a whole reinforces the theoretical conclusion: *uman ein lo chazakah bimkom re'iyah* — but that *re'iyah* must be a positive, specific identification of the item in question. A general sighting that cannot definitively establish identity is legally insufficient.

## Sharecropper Cases

### Sharecropper Rule

The Gemara now transitions to the Mishnah's parallel rule: *aris ein lo chazakah* — a sharecropper (*aris*) does not acquire a *chazakah* over the land he works. At first glance this seems puzzling. A *chazakah* of land normally requires three years of uncontested use. If the *aris* worked the land for three years and kept all the produce for himself — whereas previously he gave half to the landowner — that shift should constitute strong prima facie evidence that he is no longer a sharecropper but a legitimate owner. Why then does the Mishnah say he has no *chazakah*?

### Permanent Arrangement

Rabbi Yochanan qualifies the Mishnah: *be'arisei batei avos* — we are dealing with a situation of *batei avos*, a permanent, multigenerational sharecropping arrangement. This family has been working this land for generations as *arisim*. The *ikar* of the difficulty is not merely long duration of possession, but the nature of the institutional relationship.

In a standard yearly arrangement, a landowner would never allow a worker who turns over annually to keep all the produce for three years running and then reverse the arrangement; there is no relationship of trust deep enough to support that kind of flexibility. But in a multigenerational *batei avos* arrangement, the trust is substantial. It is entirely plausible that the landowner and the *aris* family agreed on a rotating arrangement: the family keeps everything for three years, then gives everything for three years. Because that kind of extended trust arrangement is conceivable within a *batei avos* context, the three years of keeping all produce does not constitute anomalous behavior that would signal a change in ownership status. Hence no *chazakah*.

Outside the *batei avos* context, a standard *aris* who harvests everything for himself for three years would indeed have a *chazakah*. This is a significant limitation of the Mishnah's ruling.

### Sub-contractors

Rav Nachman introduces a further refinement: *aris she-horid arisin tachtav yesh lo chazakah* — an *aris* who brought in sub-sharecroppers beneath him, replacing himself with others who do the actual work while he collects his percentage off the top, *does* have a *chazakah* — even within the *batei avos* context.

The reason: *delo avid inish dinachtit arisei le'ar'veih ve-shatik* — a person does not simply bring strangers onto another's land without permission and remain silent about it. The landowner never agreed to have unknown third parties working his land. He trusted the *aris* family precisely because of their long relationship. If the landowner observed strangers working his land and said nothing, the only plausible explanation is that the *aris* had already purchased the land and now had full authority to contract with whomever he chose. Three years of such adverse possession — sub-contractors working the land without the landowner's permission, with the landowner silent — is sufficient to establish *chazakah*.

### Active vs. Passive

Rabbi Yochanan draws a crucial distinction within this scenario. *Aris she-chilek la'arisei ein lo chazakah* — but if instead of removing himself and replacing himself with others, the *aris* actively participates alongside additional workers — dividing up tasks, working the land together with them as a working foreman — he does *not* have a *chazakah*.

The distinction is between *horid tachtav* (he put others in his stead — passive substitution) and *chilek* (he divided the work among others while actively participating himself). In the case of *chilek*, the landowner's silence does not signal anything unusual, because the *aris* is still present and working. The landowner trusts the *aris* personally, and as long as the *aris* is actively supervising and participating, the landowner would naturally be comfortable with additional helpers. This is not adverse possession — it is ordinary *aris* behavior with assistance. The *aris* simply received *harmana* — permission to bring in helpers — either explicitly or by implication. Since the landowner's silence is explained by ordinary permission, it cannot generate *chazakah*.

*Harmana b'alma shavya* — it is mere permission the landowner extended, nothing more.

A brief but notable *Tosafot* digression: whenever the Gemara refers to *Rav Nachman* without further specification, it means *Rav Nachman bar Yaakov*. *Tosafot* proves this by observing that throughout all of *Shas*, the full name *Rav Nachman bar Yaakov* never appears unless there is another *Rav Nachman* in the same sugya — at which point disambiguation is necessary. This implies that *stam Rav Nachman* is always *Rav Nachman bar Yaakov*, making the fuller designation redundant except when contrast is required.

## Sharecropper Testimony

### Interest Question

Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda sent a question to Rav Nachman bar Yaakov: *yelamdeinu rabbeinu, aris me'id o eino me'id?* — may a sharecropper testify regarding the ownership of the land he works, or not?

The question seems to answer itself. An *aris* is clearly a *nogea badavar* — a party with a direct interest in the outcome. Who owns the land he works directly affects his terms of employment, his relationship with the landowner, the security of his arrangement, and even the share of produce he is entitled to. If he testifies that the land belongs to Michael rather than Eli, he is simultaneously affirming that Michael is his current employer. The parallel is almost uncomfortably direct: could an employee testify in a lawsuit that determines who his employer is? The potential for bias is obvious.

Rav Yosef, sitting in the room, reported in the name of Shmuel: *aris me'id* — the *aris* can testify. The *Gemara* then challenges this from a *baraita*: *eino me'id* — he cannot. How do we reconcile these?

### No Present Fruit

*Lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. *Ha de'ika peira be'ar'a, ha delika peira be'ar'a* — one ruling applies when there is currently fruit on the land, and the other when there is not.

When fruit is currently growing and ready to be harvested, the *aris* has an immediate, tangible financial stake in the question of ownership. If the land belongs to Michael and the *aris* has an *aris* agreement with Michael, he will collect his full contractual share — half, a third, a quarter, depending on the arrangement. If the land turns out to belong to Eli, and the *aris* had no agreement with Eli, he was working the land without authorization (*yored letoch sdei chaveiro shelo birshut*) and would be entitled only to lower, non-contractual compensation. This is a direct and immediate financial interest, and therefore he cannot testify. He is a *nogea badavar*.

When there is no fruit currently on the land, the question of ownership has no immediate financial consequences for the *aris*. Whatever happened in the past has been resolved; the only question is the future. And for the future, the *aris* has no guaranteed interest: he might work for Michael going forward, or he might work for someone else.

### Future Benefit

*Tosafot* states the reasoning explicitly: *aval leit peira be'ar'a, lo chaishinan shema yesalkennu, dekamah sdot yimtza le'arisut* — when there is no current fruit, we are not concerned that the new owner might dismiss him, because there are plenty of other fields he could find sharecropping work in.

This reasoning is acknowledged to be challenging. The assumption of perfect competition — that the *aris* could find equivalent work with any other landowner without loss — does not obviously hold in reality. The Gemara elsewhere recognizes (*harishon noach li, ha-sheni kasheh heimennu*) that different employers are not fungible, and that a worker may genuinely prefer one employer over another. Why, then, do we dismiss the *aris*'s future interest so readily?

One possibility: when the benefit at issue is entirely prospective, contingent, and non-concrete — not a present entitlement being actively threatened, but a speculative future arrangement — it does not rise to the level of disqualifying *nogea badavar*. The distinction may be between a present interest (fruit currently on the land, money directly at stake) and a future interest (employment that may or may not continue, terms that may or may not be favorable). Future benefit of sufficient uncertainty is not considered enough to disqualify testimony.

The *Rashbam* touches on this distinction, and it provides at least a conceptual framework, even if the practical line is not perfectly sharp.

## Other Interested Parties

### Guarantor

The Gemara now catalogs several analogous cases in which a party with a potential financial interest in the outcome is nonetheless permitted to testify, all operating on the same basic logic of attenuated future benefit.

*Areiv me'id l'loveh* — a guarantor (*areiv*) may testify on behalf of the borrower whose loan he guaranteed. *Ve-hu d'is lei ara achriti* — provided the borrower has at least one other plot of land besides the one under dispute.

The relevant dynamic: I am Gloria's guarantor on a loan from Charlie. David now claims that a certain plot of Gloria's land is actually his. I wish to testify that the land belongs to Gloria. My interest in doing so is transparent — the more land Gloria has, the more buffer there is between me and Charlie's collection. If Gloria has no land at all, Charlie comes to me.

Why am I nonetheless believed to testify? Because for my testimony to actually benefit me, an extended chain of circumstances must first materialize. Gloria must have no cash reserves. Gloria's remaining land — the other plot she does retain — must be seized by a different, competing creditor before Charlie can collect it. Only then, once Gloria's assets are fully exhausted and Charlie cannot collect from her directly, does Charlie approach me. Since so many independent and contingent events must occur before I actually benefit from this testimony, the benefit is considered too attenuated to constitute a disqualifying interest.

A subsidiary point arises regarding the mechanics of creditor collection from land: when creditors compete, there is an established priority order, and a creditor who has collected from land through a lien cannot have that land re-taken by a subsequent competing creditor, lest the system dissolve into circularity. The *areiv* is most exposed when Gloria has sold land (in which case Charlie can still follow the lien) but when another creditor has seized remaining land through a competing claim — that is what fully exposes the guarantor. The point stands: the pathway to the guarantor's liability is long, contingent, and uncertain enough to not constitute present *nogea badavar*.

### Lender

A parallel case: *malveh me'id l'loveh, ve-hu d'is lei ara achriti* — a lender may testify on behalf of his borrower, again provided the borrower retains sufficient other land.

The logic is more straightforward. I lent Michael money. Eli is now claiming one of Michael's plots of land is his. I wish to testify that the land is Michael's. My interest: the more land Michael has, the more I can collect from when the time comes to recover my loan. I naturally prefer a wealthier borrower.

But again, the pathway to actual benefit for me is long. For me to collect from Michael's land at all, Michael must have no cash or other liquid assets. And for the one remaining plot to matter, all of Michael's other assets must be exhausted. Since so many preconditions stand between my testimony and any actual gain, the future benefit is not considered enough to disqualify me. *Ve-hu d'is lei ara achriti* — as long as Michael has at least one other plot, my testimony about the disputed plot is not treated as self-interested in the legally disqualifying sense.

The remaining cases in this series — *lokeach* and *kablan* — will be addressed in the following session.
