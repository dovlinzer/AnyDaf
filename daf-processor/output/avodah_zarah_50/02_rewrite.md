# Avodah Zarah 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Markolis Overview

### Perek IV Opening

Daf 50 opens a new *perek* — the fourth *perek*, which begins with Rabbi Ishmael — and continues the broad thematic concerns of the preceding chapter: identifying objects of idolatrous worship and the rules governing *bittul* of *avodah zarah*. The previous *perek* dealt largely with when one must assume something was worshipped and whether it is consequently forbidden in benefit. This new *perek* shifts toward cases where the object is unambiguously situated within a context of worship, while also continuing to develop the laws of *bittul*. In that sense, it is a natural continuation rather than a sharp break.

### Markolis Identity

The first *mishna* of the *perek* opens with a discussion of *Markolis*. The classic *Markolis* in the *Gemara* appears to be a roadside idol — a deity associated with travelers, identified by some in the shiur with Mercury of Roman mythology (Hermes in the Greek), the god with winged sandals. The mode of worship was distinctive: worshippers would throw stones at a pile, and so the *Markolis* shrine was naturally constituted by an accumulation of stones. It need not have been exclusively by the side of the road, though that appears to have been the typical setting.

## Markolis Overview…

### Three-Stone Config

The characteristic configuration of *Markolis* was a triangular arrangement of three stones — two at the base and one on top, forming a small pyramid. This structural feature is central to the *mishna*'s discussion, since stones could become dislodged or scattered, and the question becomes under what circumstances scattered stones retain the forbidden status of the original shrine.

## Mishnah Debate

### R. Ishmael Position

Rabbi Ishmael's position in the *mishna* is that *shalosh avanim zo betzad zo Markolis* — three stones situated next to one another constitute a *Markolis* and are forbidden — even if they are not configured in the proper pyramidal arrangement. The mere presence of three stones side by side is sufficient for Rabbi Ishmael to treat them as forbidden. Two stones, however, are permissible: *shtayim mutarot*.

### Sages' Position

The Sages disagree. *Vechachamim omrim: shenirot imo asurot, vshe'eini nirot imo mutarot* — stones that are seen together with a *Markolis* shrine are forbidden; stones that are not seen together with it are permissible. The number of stones — whether two or three — is not the operative criterion for the Sages. What matters is whether the stones appear connected to an existing *Markolis* shrine. The *Gemara* now probes what precisely each position entails and how the two views can be made coherent.

The *Gemara* opens by noting that the Sages' position is internally consistent: *bishlama rabanan kasavri ovdin lishvarim* — the Sages apparently hold that worshippers continue to venerate a *Markolis* even after it has collapsed, since the very nature of this idol is that it is constituted by stones and it is normal for it to be disrupted. Therefore, stones found immediately near the shrine — *nirot imo de'ika lemeimar minei nafal asurot* — are presumed to have fallen from it and retain their forbidden status. Stones found beyond that range — *vshe'eini nirot imo mutarot* — are just ordinary stones on the road.

The difficulty is with Rabbi Ishmael's position. If he holds that worshippers continue to venerate the idol even after it collapses (*ovdin lishvarim*), then even two stones found nearby should be forbidden. If he holds the opposite (*ein ovdin lishvarim*), then even three stones should be permissible. Either way, his criterion of "three" seems arbitrary.

The *Gemara* resolves this by citing a ruling of Rav Yosef bar Yosef in the name of Rav Yochanan: all agree that if stones definitively fell from the *Markolis*, they remain forbidden — even according to the opinion that normally one does not continue to worship a broken idol, because here collapse is the natural mode of the shrine and the idol's function continues regardless. The debate between Rabbi Ishmael and the Sages therefore concerns only the ambiguous case.

## Fell vs. Independent

### Fallen Stones Rule

Rav Yosef bar Yosef's ruling establishes that *bidua she-nasru mimenu divrei hakol asurot* — where it is certain that the stones fell from the *Markolis*, all agree they remain forbidden. The rationale is that *Markolis* is different from other idols. A standard idol that breaks loses its sacred status because the god is understood to have departed; a *Markolis*, however, is by its very nature an accumulation of thrown stones, so its collapse is not a sign of abandonment. Thus *hainu orcheih* — this is simply how it works — and everyone would concede the stones remain forbidden.

## Mishnah Debate…

### Proximity Principle

Where the dispute actually operates is in the ambiguous case — *ki peligi bistema*, where the circumstances are unclear. Both Rabbi Ishmael and the Sages agree that stones found close enough to the shrine that one may reasonably presume they fell from it — *bimkoravo nami de'ika lemeimar minei nafal, divrei hakol asurot* — are forbidden. The *chiddush* of Rabbi Ishmael is that he extends concern even to stones found somewhat further away — *bimruchakot* — on the grounds that small subsidiary *Markolises* were constructed near large ones. Even if the stones did not fall from the main shrine, three stones in the vicinity could constitute an independent mini-*Markolis*.

The *mishna*'s language — *batzad Markolis* — is then quantified by the *Gemara*: *mai batzad? batzad arba amot dideih* — "beside" means within four *amot* of the shrine. Stones within four *amot* are treated as *batzad*; those within a much smaller, immediate range are treated as *nirot imo*. The diagram, as it were, has three concentric zones: a very close range where all agree the stone is presumed fallen and forbidden, an intermediate zone (within four *amot*) where Rabbi Ishmael is concerned and the Sages are not, and everything beyond four *amot* where all agree there is no problem.

The debate thus reduces to an empirical disagreement: Rabbi Ishmael holds that worshippers did construct small mini-*Markolises* near the main shrine (*osin Markolis katan batzad Markolis gadol*), and therefore three stones within the intermediate zone — even those that did not fall from the main shrine — are presumed to have constituted such a mini-shrine and are forbidden. *Shalosh asurot, shtayim mutarot*. The Sages disagree: *ein osin Markolis katan batzad Markolis gadol* — no such mini-shrines were made. Therefore, once a stone is far enough away that it cannot be presumed to have fallen from the main shrine, it is irrelevant whether one finds two or three; the only operative criterion remains whether the stone is *nirot imo* — seen together with the shrine — and thus presumed to have fallen from it.

## Fell vs. Independent…

### Rava Reinterprets

A *braita* is cited that appears to complicate matters: *avanim she-nashru min ha-Markolis* — stones that fell from the *Markolis* — *nirot imo asurot, she'ein nirot imo mutarot*. This seems to posit a case where the stones definitively fell, yet even then only those seen together with the shrine are forbidden. This contradicts the ruling just established that definitively fallen stones are universally forbidden. Rashi's reading of this *braita* suggests that even fallen stones are only worshipped if they remain in the vicinity — once they roll out of range, worshippers lose interest, consistent with treating the *Markolis* as a configuration that loses its sanctity when dispersed.

Rava, however, rejects the plain reading. *Lo tima she-nashru* — do not read the *braita* as speaking of stones that definitely fell. *Ela eima she-nimtzu* — rather, it means stones that are found nearby, such that one suspects they fell. This is the more natural reading of *nashru* in context, and it returns us to the same framework as the *mishna*: the dispute is about presumed, not confirmed, origins.

### Tfisa Achat Concept

With Rava's reinterpretation in place, the *Gemara* introduces a further refinement from a different *braita* in the name of Rabbi Ishmael: *shtayim bitfisa lo asurot* — two stones in the same *tfisa* as the shrine are forbidden — *shalosh, afilu meruchakot, asurot* — and three are forbidden even if more distant. This introduces a third spatial concept alongside *nirot imo* and *betzad*: *tfisa achat*, a single unified unit. The *Gemara* notes the apparent contradiction — Rabbi Ishmael's *mishna* says two are permissible, yet this *braita* says two in the same *tfisa* are forbidden — and resolves it: *kan bitfisa achat, kan bishte tfisot* — in one case the stones share the same unit with the shrine, in the other they do not.

## Parameters & Barriers

### Tfisa Achat Defined

*Veheichi dami? De'ika govah beini uveini* — what constitutes separate *tfisot*? If there is a mound of dirt between the stones and the shrine, they are considered separate units. A physical barrier, even a modest earthen mound, creates separation sufficient to preclude the assumption that the stones belonged to the shrine. Without such a barrier, even two stones may be considered part of the same *tfisa* and thus forbidden.

### Meat-Milk Parallel

The concept of *tfisa achat* is borrowed by the *Rishonim* for a parallel discussion in Masechet Chullin concerning the prohibition on eating meat and milk at the same table. One may not eat meat at a table where someone else is eating dairy, because of the risk of inadvertent mixing; but this concern applies only when the two are at the same table in a single *tfisa*. If there is a physical barrier — or according to some authorities, even a *heker*, a visual marker — between the two settings, they may be considered separate *tfisot* and the concern is lifted. The *Rishonim* thus apply the spatial logic of the *Markolis* stones directly to the laws of *basar b'chalav*.

### Tosafot Reading

Tosafot's reading of the full set of distinctions clarifies that all the relevant cases occur within four *amot*. More than four *amot* from the shrine is never a problem. Within four *amot*, the presence or absence of a barrier becomes determinative. If there is no barrier (*delika govah*) and the stones are within four *amot*, then even two stones are problematic, since Rabbi Ishmael holds one must assume they either fell from the main shrine or constitute a mini-*Markolis*. If there is a barrier within those four *amot*, only three stones give rise to concern. The overall structure, then, maintains the basic contours of the debate: the Sages require the stones to be in the immediate *nirot imo* range; Rabbi Ishmael extends concern within the four-*amot* radius, with or without a barrier, varying only the threshold number.

### Mini-Markolis Evidence

One might ask whether mini-*Markolises* were actually constructed near major shrines. The *Gemara* raises exactly this challenge — *UMarkolis kehai gavna mi havah?* — and answers it from a *braita* describing the canonical *Markolis* structure: *achat mikan, ve'achat mikan, ve'achat al gabeihen* — one stone here, one stone here, one on top. This is the primary configuration. Rava explains that while three is the minimum for a primary *Markolis*, a subsidiary mini-shrine might be constituted by as few as two stones — which is why, under certain conditions, Rabbi Ishmael is concerned even with two.

## Yannai Palace Story

### Palace Paving

The *Gemara* now relates a historical incident. King Yannai's palace was eventually destroyed. Non-Jews came and used the rubble to erect a *Markolis* — *atu ovdei kochavim ve'okimu bei Markolis*. Subsequently, other non-Jews who did not worship *Markolis* came, took the stones, and used them to pave roads and thoroughfares: *shaklinhu vechipu bahen drachim ve'sitaot*.

### Road Usage Dispute

The question is whether Jews may travel on those roads. On its face, a non-Jew using the stones to pave roads would seem to constitute a clear act of *bittul* — since non-Jews may be *mevattel* the *avodah zarah* of other non-Jews. Yet some rabbis chose to avoid walking on those roads, while others did not.

### R. Yochanan Ruling

Rabbi Yochanan settled the matter: *bnan shel kedoshim mahalekh aleihen* — even the son of the holy ones walked on those roads, implying that those who abstained were being excessively stringent. The figure identified as "the son of the holy ones" is Rabbi Menachem son of Rabbi Simai — a man so scrupulous about images that he would not look at the image on a coin, since such images were treated as divine by others. Even he had no reservation about these roads. The implication is clear: if such a person saw no problem, neither should anyone else.

## Tikrovet Concept

### No Bittul for Tikrovet

The reason some rabbis abstained is now explained. The principle is stated in the name of Rav Gidel citing Rabbi Chiyya bar Yosef citing Rav: *minayin le-tikrovet avodah zarah she'ein lah bittul le'olamim* — from where do we know that an offering brought to an idol can never be nullified? The verse states: *vayitzmadu le-va'al pe'or vayochlu zivchei meitim* — and they joined themselves to Ba'al Pe'or and ate offerings of the dead. Just as a corpse can never change its *tamei* status — *mah meis ein lo bittul le'olamim* — so too an offering to *avodah zarah* can never be nullified.

This raises a broader question worth pausing over. The law treats *tikrovet avodah zarah* more severely than *avodah zarah* itself in two respects: it conveys *tum'ah* like a corpse (under the same roof), whereas the idol itself conveys *tum'ah* only like a *sheretz*; and it cannot be nullified, whereas the idol itself can. One possible explanation is that the idol was understood by the pagans themselves to be a temporary habitation of the god — a vessel that the deity could enter and leave, and there existed rituals for desanctifying the statue by inducing the god to depart. If the pagans themselves conceived of the statue's sanctity as impermanent, the *halacha* may reflect that the idol's status is correspondingly less fixed. The offering, by contrast, was permanently dedicated and could not be "recalled." Whatever the underlying logic, the *halacha* is clear: *tikrovet* is weightier and admits no *bittul*.

### Stone Status Concern

This principle creates an acute problem for the *Markolis* stones. The *Markolis* was worshipped by throwing stones at it. Those thrown stones might thus qualify as *tikrovet* — offerings brought to the idol. If so, not only can they not be nullified; the fact that non-Jews used them to pave roads makes no difference. *Tikrovet* admits no *bittul* at all, not even by a non-Jew. Those who refrained from walking on the roads were operating from this concern.

The complication, as the *Gemara* makes clear, is that the stones occupy an ambiguous dual status. The pyramid structure itself — the base configuration of the shrine — is the *avodah zarah* proper. The stones thrown at it in worship might be *tikrovet*. But after they have been thrown and become part of the pile, they may themselves become objects of veneration as part of the shrine — making them *avodah zarah* as well. The question is whether the *tikrovet* dimension forecloses the *bittul* that would otherwise be available for the *avodah zarah* dimension.

Those who permitted use of the roads answer: *be'ina ke'ein panim ve'leika* — for a thrown stone to qualify as *tikrovet*, it must resemble the manner in which offerings are made before God, *ke'ein panim*, and it does not. Rashi understands this as a matter of the object: stones are not a food item, and the normal *tikrovet* offered in the *Beit HaMikdash* is an edible substance. Tosafot understands it as a matter of the act: one presents offerings; one does not throw them. Either way, since the stones do not qualify as genuine *tikrovet*, they do not acquire the irrevocable status of *tikrovet*, and the *avodah zarah* dimension can be nullified by a non-Jew in the ordinary way.

Tosafot draws a remarkable practical application from this discussion on 50a. He raises the question of church candles — wax candles brought as donations and lit before statues. A priest extinguishes such candles and sells or gives them to Jews. May Jews benefit from repurposed church candles? Tosafot reasons: *mimah nafshach* — either way it is permissible. If the candles are classified as *tikrovet*, they do not qualify as such because they are not a food-type offering and thus do not meet the threshold of *ke'ein panim* — and therefore they never acquired the status of *tikrovet* and are not forbidden to begin with. If instead they are classified as *noy* — adornments of the idol — then when the priest extinguishes them, *ein lach bittul gadol mizeh* — there is no greater act of *bittul* than that, since the priest has declared by his act that they are no longer intended as adornments of the idol but for ordinary use. Tosafot extends this analysis to various other items traded between Jews and the Church, working through which qualify as *noy*, which as *tikrovet*, and how *bittul* operates in each case.

Tosafot also notes, in connection with the term *beit Kolis* (the *Markolis* of the *mishna* is sometimes called *avnei beit Kolis*), a linguistic observation from Rabbeinu Tam: the word *kilus* in Hebrew connotes praise (*lekales*), and the Sages altered the term to *kolis*, giving it a mocking resonance — consistent with the broader rabbinic practice, discussed in a recent *Gemara*, of modifying the names of idols so as to render them derogatory rather than honorific.

## Non-Jew vs. Jew

### Braita Teaching

Rav Yosef bar Abba reports that Rava bar Yirmiyah visited their locale and brought with him the following *braita*: a non-Jew who took stones from a *Markolis* and used them to pave roads and thoroughfares — *mutarot*, the roads are permissible. A Jew who did the same — *asurot*, forbidden.

### Initial Difficulty

Rava bar Yirmiyah found this *braita* difficult to explain — *veleit nagar velo bar nagar de'yafarkina* — there is no carpenter nor son of a carpenter who can unpack it. The difficulty, on reflection, is not the elementary distinction between a Jew and non-Jew in *bittul*, which is well established. Rather, the difficulty is structural: make up your mind. If these stones are *tikrovet*, no one — not even a non-Jew — can make them permissible, since *tikrovet* admits no *bittul*. If they are not *tikrovet*, they are merely *avodah zarah* proper, and even before any non-Jew acts, they should potentially be subject to *bittul*. What is this intermediate category that requires a non-Jew to unlock the permissibility?

## Non-Jew vs. Jew…

### Rav Sheshet Solution

Rav Sheshet responds: *ana lo nagar ana velo bar nagar ana, ufarkina la* — I am neither a carpenter nor the son of a carpenter, but I can explain it. What is the difficulty? It is the principle of Rav Gidel — that *tikrovet* cannot be nullified. The concern was that the stones qualify as *tikrovet*, which would foreclose *bittul* entirely. The answer is that they do not qualify as *tikrovet*, for the reason already stated: *be'ina ke'ein panim ve'leika*.

## Tikrovet Concept…

### Not Ke'ein Panim

Rav Sheshet's resolution is that the stones thrown at *Markolis* are not genuine *tikrovet* because they do not satisfy the criterion of *ke'ein panim* — resemblance to Temple offerings. Whether one follows Rashi's understanding (the object must be a food-type substance) or Tosafot's (the act of offering must resemble the presentational mode of the Temple service), throwing a stone fails on both counts. The stone is not food, and throwing is not presenting. Accordingly, the stones never acquired *tikrovet* status in the first place.

They do, however, acquire the status of *avodah zarah* itself — for once thrown at the shrine and incorporated into the pile, they become part of the object of veneration. As *avodah zarah*, they are subject to the ordinary rules: a non-Jew can be *mevattel* them; a Jew cannot. That is precisely what the *braita* rules. The apparent paradox dissolves: the stones are neither pure *tikrovet* (which cannot be nullified by anyone) nor mere unworshipped objects (which require no *bittul* at all), but *avodah zarah* proper — which can be nullified, but only by a non-Jew.

## Shimita Digression

### Zizhum Teaching

Because the phrase *veleit nagar velo bar nagar de'yafarkina* — there is no carpenter or son of a carpenter who can explain it — became a recognizable idiom for a halakhic puzzle, the *Gemara* records a second instance of it. Rav Yosef bar Abba reports that Rava bar Yirmiyah brought another *braita* on a different subject entirely: *matlin u'masimim bi'shvi'it* — one may de-worm trees and attach bark to them on *shemitah*. *Ve'ein matlin u'masimim bamo'ed* — but one may not do so on *chol hamoed*. *Kan ve'kan ein megazmin* — in neither case may one prune the tree, neither on *shemitah* nor on *chol hamoed*. *Ve'sachin shemen le'gazum, bein bamo'ed bein bi'shvi'it* — but one may apply oil to the site of a pruned branch, whether on *chol hamoed* or on *shemitah*. And again, the *braita* was followed by the complaint that there is no carpenter or son of a carpenter to explain it.

### Zizhum vs. Gizum

The first potential difficulty is the distinction between *shemitah* and *chol hamoed*: why is de-worming (*zizhum*) permitted on *shemitah* but forbidden on *chol hamoed*? The *Gemara* dismisses this as straightforward. *Shvi'it melachah assar Rachmana, tircha sharei* — on *shemitah*, the Torah forbade only *melachah*, meaning active agricultural labor such as plowing, planting, and irrigating. Mere effort (*tircha*) that does not rise to the level of *melachah* is permitted. *Mo'ed afilu tircha nami assur* — on *chol hamoed*, by contrast, even mere exertion is restricted. The two regimes operate on different prohibitory principles and are therefore not comparable.

### Protection vs. Growth

The second potential difficulty is the distinction between *zizhum* (attaching bark, de-worming) and *gizum* (pruning), both on *shemitah*. Why is the former permitted and the latter forbidden? Again the *Gemara* sees no real difficulty. *Zizhum ukme ilana* — attaching bark and de-worming serves to maintain and protect the tree from deterioration. *Gizum avruei ilana* — pruning serves to enhance and encourage growth. The *shemitah* prohibition targets agricultural improvement; protection against loss is permitted. The distinction is clean and conceptually transparent.

### Oil Applications

A third apparent difficulty concerns the oil applications. The *braita* permits applying oil to a pruned branch (*gazum*) on both *shemitah* and *chol hamoed*. But another *mishna* forbids applying oil to unripe fruit (*pagin*) on *shemitah*. Why should one oil application be permitted and the other forbidden?

### Branch vs. Fruit Oil

The *Gemara* resolves this as well. *Hacha ukme ilana ve'shari* — oil on the pruned branch merely protects the exposed wound from damage; it does nothing to enhance the tree's growth. *Hatam pitumei peirot, ve'assur* — oil rubbed onto unripe fruit actively fattens and enlarges the fruit, which is a form of agricultural improvement forbidden on *shemitah*. Once again, the protection-versus-enhancement distinction carries the analysis.

### Consistency Question

Rav Sama son of Rav Ashi puts the genuine difficulty to Ravina. If all of these distinctions work so cleanly, what is it that the original teacher could not explain? Rav Sama identifies the real puzzle: within *chol hamoed* itself, the *braita* forbids the attaching of bark (*zizhum*) but permits the application of oil to a pruned branch. Yet both acts seem to be about protecting the tree — *michdi hai ukme ve'hai ukme* — so why does one remain forbidden on *chol hamoed* while the other is permitted? There may be distinctions of degree — the level of effort involved, the severity of the loss risked, the extent of the intervention — but no single clean principle accounts for the asymmetry. That, says the *Gemara*, is the genuine *kushya* to which there is no carpenter or son of a carpenter to provide a resolution.

## Stick Worship Acts

### Stick as Substitute

Returning to the main line of the chapter's analysis, the *Gemara* now takes up a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav. The background is the general principle — developed elsewhere in the *Gemara* in Sanhedrin — that two independent categories of act render one liable for *avodah zarah*: performing one of the four classic Temple services (*shechita*, *zerikah*, *haktarah*, *nisuch*) before an idol, even if that is not how the idol is normally worshipped; or performing whatever act is the recognized mode of worship for that particular idol, even if it has no parallel in the Temple service. If *Markolis* is normally worshipped by throwing stones, throwing a stone at it makes one liable; and if one instead performed *shechita* before it, one would also be liable under the first category.

Now suppose the idol in question is normally worshipped with a stick — say, by twirling it as a baton. The stick thus functions as the instrument of worship, effectively substituting for the animal in Temple terms. Rav Yehuda rules: *shavar makkel befaneyha — chayav*. If one broke the stick before the idol rather than twirling it, one is liable. *Zarak makkel befaneyha — patur*. If one threw the stick, one is exempt.

### Break vs. Throw

The distinction rests on the analogy to Temple service. Once the stick is identified as the object used in worship, it is assimilated to the role of the animal in Temple service. An act performed upon it that resembles a classical Temple service generates liability. Breaking the stick resembles *shechita* — the slaughter of an animal — and therefore creates liability. Throwing the stick, however, does not sufficiently resemble *zerikah* — the sprinkling of blood — and therefore does not.

### Resemblance Logic

The logic, as Rashi explains it, is structural. *Shechita* involves cutting into and severing the subject — and breaking a stick does the same. The resemblance is functional: both acts destroy the integrity of a physical object by severing it. *Zerikah*, by contrast, is not merely the act of projection; it is specifically the act of throwing blood such that it splatters.

### Abaye Challenge

Abaye challenges this distinction. Once we have established that the stick stands in for the animal, why should throwing the stick not be equivalent to *zerikah*? If breaking = *shechita*, then throwing should = *zerikah*.

### Splatter Distinction

Rava answers: *be'ina zerikah mishtaberet ve'leika* — the act of *zerikah* is not merely throwing; it is a throwing that causes dispersal, that causes the blood to splatter and break apart upon the surface. When a stick is thrown, it simply falls to the ground. It does not break apart; it does not disperse. The act lacks the defining characteristic of *zerikah*, which is precisely the scattering of the blood upon the altar. The resemblance is therefore insufficient, and one who throws the stick before the idol is *patur*.

The underlying principle governing this entire discussion — that the resemblance must be sufficiently precise, not merely loosely analogous — applies throughout the laws of *avodah zarah* wherever one attempts to map an unconventional act onto the framework of Temple service. Here, breaking achieves sufficient resemblance to *shechita*; throwing does not achieve sufficient resemblance to *zerikah*. The details of what constitutes adequate resemblance, and how those distinctions play out in further cases, carry over into the continuation of the *Gemara*.
