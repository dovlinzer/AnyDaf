# Shabbat 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Deer in Doorway

### First Person

The *mishna* at the bottom of 106b, which we pick up today, continues the discussion of *tzeidah* — the *melacha* of capturing — in the context of a deer that has entered a house. The case presented is: if one person sits by a doorway (*yoshav echad al hapetach*) but does not fill the entire opening (*velo mil'uhu*), the doorway remains partially unblocked and the deer is not yet fully captured.

### Second Person Liable

When a second person then sits beside the first and together they block the opening, the *mishna* rules that the second person is liable (*hasheni chayav*). This case is explicitly distinguished from the earlier *mishna*'s discussion of *shnayim she'asuhu* — two people acting simultaneously — where the debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon turned on the principle of *zeh yachol vezeh lo yachol*. Here, the two acts were sequential: when the first person sat down, nothing was accomplished. It was the second person's act alone that completed the closure of the doorway, and therefore the entire obligation falls on him.

### First Fills Door

The *mishna* then presents the reverse scenario: the first person (*harishon*) sat at the doorway and himself filled the entire opening. A second person then came and sat beside him. Even if the first person subsequently got up and left, the first person is liable (*harishon chayav*) and the second person is exempt (*hasheni patur*). The rationale is clear: the first person alone performed the complete act of capture. When the second person sat down, the deer was already captured — nothing new was accomplished by that act.

### Static vs. Active

The *mishna* makes a further point: the second person's continued sitting after the first departs is not considered an ongoing act of capture. The deer was already captured when the second sat down; sitting statically does not constitute *re-capturing* an already captured animal. This raises an interesting question, however: what if the situation were not purely static? For instance, if a door were structurally unstable and a person had to actively apply continuous pressure to keep it closed — akin to what engineers call a dead man's switch — would that ongoing pressure be considered a continuation of the *melacha* of *tzeidah*? Similarly, if someone else comes to sit in the same spot specifically to relieve the first person and maintain the blockade, and consciously does so knowing the deer is inside, does the intentional nature of that act change anything? These are not resolved questions here, but the *gemara* seems to be moving in the direction that as long as the animal is already captured at the moment you act, the act itself — even if consciously directed toward maintaining that captivity — may be permissible. The key distinction appears to be: are you capturing an uncaptured animal, or acting upon an animal that is already captured?

### Locking House Analogy

The *mishna* concludes with an analogy: this is comparable to a person who locks his house to guard it (*leno'el et beito leshomro*) and discovers after the fact that a deer was inside (*venimtza tzvi shamur betocho*). He is not required to reverse course and release the deer. His original act was one of *mit'asek* — he was entirely unaware that a deer was present — and now that the deer happens to be captured, there is no ongoing *melacha* being performed. Notably, there is no *melacha* of releasing a captured animal, so no concern arises on that side. But equally, leaving the static situation in place, once you have done nothing wrong in creating it, is entirely permissible.

## Bird Under Cloak

### Rav's Teaching

The *gemara* opens with a ruling transmitted in a chain: *amar Rabbi Abba amar Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi amar Rav* — if a bird flew under one's cloak (*nichnesa tzippor tachat knafav*), one may sit and guard it until nightfall (*yeshev umishamro ad shetecheshach*). One need not open the cloak and release the bird. This seems intuitively consistent with the *mishna*'s conclusion about the locked house, but the language of *yeshev umishamro* — "sit and guard it" — introduces some complexity.

### Guarding Question

The question becomes: is the act of guarding an animal that is currently trapped under your cloak a form of active capture? The bird is already caught. But by sitting and watching it, are you doing something — some ongoing act — that enables it to remain caught? Consider an extension: suppose you walk into your house with the bird caught under your cloak and lock the door. At the moment of locking, you are not capturing an already-free bird — it is already caught under the cloak. You then remove the cloak, and the bird is now captured inside the house. Is that permissible, because at no point did you capture an uncaptured animal? The language of Rav's ruling and the *gemara*'s engagement with it suggest that such an approach may well be permitted. The principle seems to be: as long as each discrete act is performed upon an already-captured animal, you are not performing the *melacha* of *tzeidah*.

### Nachman Challenge

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* then challenges Rav's ruling from the *mishna* itself: the *mishna* states that when the first person filled the doorway and the second sat beside him, *af al pi shensistalek harishon vehalach lo*, even after the first person leaves, the first is liable and the second is exempt (*harishon chayav vehasheni patur*). The challenge is: does *patur* here mean merely exempt from a biblical violation but still rabbinically forbidden (*patur aval assur*)? If so, then Rav's ruling that one may *sit and guard* the bird would also be at best *patur aval assur*, not affirmatively permitted.

### Exempt = Permitted

The *gemara* responds: *lo patur muttar* — no, when the *mishna* says exempt, it means fully permissible, not merely exempt from liability. The *gemara* offers a clarification: the word *patur* is used in contrast to the first person who is *chayav*; it is not intended to suggest that the second person's act was somehow improper.

### House Comparison

The proof comes from the *mishna*'s own closing analogy. The *mishna* compares this to one who locks his house not knowing a deer is inside — *lema zeh domeh leno'el et beito leshomro venimtza tzvi shamur betocho*. In that case, the ruling cannot be *patur aval assur*, because the person was completely oblivious. He is in a state of *mit'asek*; of course it is *muttar* for him to lock his house. Since both cases receive the same ruling of *patur*, and the locking-the-house case is clearly *muttar*, it follows that *patur* throughout this passage means *muttar* — *mikh'lal depatur muttar*. *Shema mina.*

The *gemara* notes that there is a variant formulation (*ika d'amri*) in which Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak presents this not as a challenge but as a corroboration of Rav's ruling: the *mishna* itself demonstrates that *patur* means *muttar*, consistent with the permissibility of guarding the bird under the cloak.

## Exempt Principle

### Three Exceptions

Because the passage has raised the distinction between *patur aval assur* and *patur muttar*, the *gemara* invokes Shmuel's famous general principle — which brings the discussion back to the very opening of the *masechet*. Shmuel states: *kol paturei d'Shabbat patur aval assur* — every time the *mishna* uses the word *patur* in the context of Shabbat, it means exempt from biblical liability but still rabbinically forbidden. However, there are three exceptions (*levar mehani telat*) where *patur* in a *mishna* means fully permissible (*muttar lechatchila*). Tosafot notes that Shmuel specifically limits his observation to *mishnayot*; there are many *beraitot* where *patur* means *muttar*, but in a *mishna* such usage is remarkable, and Shmuel identifies only three such instances.

### Deer Exception

The first exception is the case we have just been discussing: the deer in the house. The proof that *patur* means *muttar* — as demonstrated through the comparison to the man who locked his house unaware — has already been established above.

### Abscess Exception

The second exception is the case of *hamafis mursa b'Shabbat* — one who squeezes an abscess on Shabbat to drain it. The *mishna* distinguishes between two intents. If one does so *la'asot lah peh* — to create a lasting opening so the wound remains open and heals — one is *chayav*. The exact *melacha* involved is difficult to identify precisely: Rashi suggests it may be a form of *boneh* or *makeh b'patish* — perhaps the act of creating a deliberate opening in a surface, analogous to a hole pierced in a structure to allow airflow, which the *gemara* elsewhere treats as a form of *boneh*. But if one does so *lehotzi mimena leicha* — merely to extract the pus, with the opening being purely incidental — one is *patur*. This is because extracting pus is not a *melacha*, and the hole produced is not desired for its own sake. This makes it a classic case of *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa*: the *melacha* (making the opening) is performed, but not for the intrinsic purpose of that *melacha*.

### Indirect Melacha

This concept of *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* is what underlies this case and is the basis for the ruling of *patur*. To understand why the *mishna* says it is not only exempt but actually *muttar lechatchila* — which would normally not be said about even a *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa*, since that category is at minimum rabbinically prohibited — one needs a proof. The principle here is directly applicable to many practical questions about Shabbat, such as opening packages. If one opens a bag by tearing it in a way that renders it completely unusable — ripping the side of a bag of potato chips, for instance — the intent is solely to access the contents, and the package itself is destroyed in the process; this is more straightforwardly permitted. More complex cases arise when the opening produces a functional result: piercing a milk carton to create a spout, or opening a bag neatly along its pre-scored top edge, raises the question of whether one is creating a useful vessel (*boneh*) or merely gaining access to the contents. The line between *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* and a fully intended *melacha* is precisely what must be analyzed in such cases.

### Needle & Splinter

The proof that this *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* is *muttar lechatchila* rather than merely *patur aval assur* comes from another *mishna*: *machat shel yad litol bah et hakotz* — one may use a regular sewing needle to remove a splinter. The *mishna*'s permissive ruling implies that this is allowed *lechatchila*, even though in the process of removing the splinter, one may make a small wound and blood may be drawn. This should constitute *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* and warrant at least a rabbinic prohibition. Yet the *mishna* permits it outright, establishing that it is fully *muttar*. The reason is the principle of *mitzta'er*: a person experiencing pain or physical discomfort is permitted to perform an *issur d'rabbanan* on Shabbat for relief. Thus, even a *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa* — which ordinarily carries rabbinic prohibition — is waived when done to alleviate suffering. This is an important and widely applied principle, though its precise contours depend heavily on context: how significant must the discomfort be? When does a trivial annoyance (*michush b'alma*) fall short of the threshold, and when does a serious complaint justify even a *single d'rabbanan*? These gradations constitute, practically speaking, a large portion of the halachic analysis in Shabbat responsa.

### Snake Exception

The third exception is *hatzad nachash b'Shabbat* — one who captures a snake on Shabbat, *im mitasek bo shelo yishachenu* — when one's focus is not on capturing the snake but solely on preventing it from biting. The word *mitasek* here does not carry its usual technical meaning of a completely unaware act, but rather conveys that the person's attention and purpose are directed entirely at the danger, not at the act of capture. One may try to ward it off with a stick and eventually cover it with a vessel — the capturing is incidental; the objective is preventing injury. This is therefore another instance of *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa*: the capturing occurs, but not for the purpose of having a captured snake. One is *patur*. If, however, one captures the snake *l'refuah* — for medicinal use, such as extracting its venom — one is *chayav*, because the capture is now the purpose.

### Vessel Sources

The proof that this *patur* means *muttar* comes from yet another *mishna*: *kofin k'ara al haner bishvil shelo te'echoz bakora* — one may invert a vessel over a candle not to extinguish it but to prevent the flame from spreading to the beams of a house. Similarly, one may place a vessel over an infant's excrement, and over a scorpion (*v'al akrav*) — *shelo tishach*, so that it does not bite. This last case is directly analogous: one is effectively capturing a scorpion, yet the *mishna* permits it explicitly and without qualification. The justification here is slightly different from the *mitzta'er* principle of the splinter case: there one was alleviating existing pain; here one is preventing imminent and non-trivial suffering before it occurs. Both situations — whether relieving current discomfort or preemptively averting serious harm — are sufficient grounds to permit what would otherwise be at minimum a rabbinic prohibition.

## Eight Sheratzim

### Torah List

The *mishna* now turns to a new topic that will also intersect with *tzeidah*: *shemonah sheratzim ha'amurim baTorah* — the eight creeping creatures enumerated in the Torah. The relevant passage in Vayikra states: *v'zeh lachem hatameh basheretz hashoretz al ha'aretz* — these are the *tamei* creatures that creep upon the earth. The Torah then lists: *hacholed v'ha'achbar v'hatzav l'minehu* — the weasel (or mole), the mouse, and the *tzav* (some say a frog or lizard), and then five more: *ha'anakah v'hakoach v'haleta'ah v'hachomet v'hatinshemet* — rendered variously as hedgehog, viper, lizard, snail, and a species sometimes identified as a mole. These eight creatures, the Torah states, cause *tumah* upon contact after their death: *kol hanogea bahem bemotam yitma ad ha'arev*.

### Skin Distinction

The *mishna* states that if one captures (*hatzad*) or wounds (*hachovel*) any of these eight, one is *chayav*. The question immediately arises: why should these eight be subject to the *melacha* of *tzeidah* more than any other creature? The answer connects to the principle of *bimino nitzod* introduced in the preceding *mishna* — that one is only biblically liable for capturing a creature that is *normally* captured. The assumption is that these eight are normally captured, and Rashi explicitly states this: *chayav mipnei shebimino nitzod*. Rashi further suggests that the reason they are normally captured is that they possess a distinct hide (*or*) — a skin layer distinguishable from the flesh beneath — and people capture them for their hides.

### Intent in Capture

The *mishna* then contrasts the *shemonah sheratzim* with the broader category of *shekatzim uremashim* — other creeping creatures. For these others: *hachovel bahen patur* — wounding them carries no liability — and *hatzadan letzorech chayav, shelo letzorech patur* — capturing them is liable only if done with purpose (*letzorech*), and exempt if done without specific intent (*shelo letzorech*). The *shelo letzorech* exemption is clearly grounded in *melacha she'eina tzricha l'gufa*: if you capture a creature not because you want it but to prevent it from running through the house or potentially biting you, you are not performing the *melacha* of *tzeidah* for its own sake. But why are the eight *sheratzim* different — why does even unintentional capture of these eight carry a presumption of liability, while other creatures require active *kavanah*?

Tosafot raises a pointed difficulty here: Rashi says the *shekatzim uremashim* outside the list of eight are categorized as *shelo bimino nitzod* — not normally captured. But if that is so, how can capturing them *letzorech* make one *chayav* at all, if they are not in the category of things classically captured? Tosafot therefore concludes that either this *mishna* does not require *bimino nitzod* as a condition for liability, or that all creatures in question are in fact considered *bimino nitzod*. What distinguishes the two groups is rather the level of intent required: the eight *sheratzim* are so commonly captured — because of their hides and other uses — that *min hastam*, even without articulated intent, capture is presumed purposeful. For the other creatures, since they are less commonly captured for their own sake and there are various other reasons one might cover or confine them (such as preventing bites), active and specific *kavanah* is required before the act is classified as intentional capture.

### Wounding Rules

The distinction regarding *chavalah* — wounding — follows from the hide distinction. The *gemara* (which we will see explicitly below) assumes that the eight *sheratzim* possess a distinct outer hide analogous to the hide of larger animals. When one strikes such a creature and blood is released, even if the blood does not emerge into the open air but rather coagulates beneath the skin — producing something analogous to a bruise or black-and-blue mark — this constitutes *chavalah* and renders one *chayav*. Tosafot explains the underlying principle: a bruise represents blood that has been broken from its vessels and trapped beneath the skin. Since there is a distinct outer layer capable of containing that blood, the act of causing that internal hemorrhage is itself the *chavalah*. The blood has, in effect, been "released," even if it has not emerged externally.

For creatures that lack this distinct outer hide — where the outer surface is continuous with the flesh beneath — the same logic does not apply. If no distinct skin layer captures the blood beneath it, then only when blood actually exits the body into the air has *chavalah* been performed. Without that external release of blood, one has not accomplished the act of wounding for purposes of Shabbat liability.

### Domestic Animals

The *mishna* concludes this section with a ruling regarding *chaya v'of shebireshuto* — a domesticated animal or bird under one's control, such as one's household animals. Because such creatures are already effectively under human dominion and do not require the act of capture to be contained, *hatzadan patur* — capturing them carries no liability. However, *hachovel bahen chayav* — wounding them does carry liability — because any animal of a higher order is presumed to have a distinct skin layer, and *chavalah* is therefore operative.

## Skin Debate

### Skin Derivation

The *gemara* now zooms in on the liability for *chavalah* in the eight *sheratzim*. It notes immediately: since the *mishna* says *hachovel bahen chayav* — one who wounds them is liable — this implies, *mikh'lal d'it lehu or*, that they must have a skin (*or*) that is physiologically distinguishable from their flesh. Only then can blood coagulate beneath the skin and constitute *chavalah*.

### Ben Nuri View

The *gemara* asks: who is the *tanna* who holds that all eight *sheratzim* have such a hide? *Amar Shmuel: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri hi.* The source is a *mishna* in *Chullin* regarding *tumah*, where Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri states: *shemonah sheratzim yesh lahem orot* — the eight *sheratzim* have hides. His point there is that their hides are halachically distinct from their flesh for purposes of *tumah*: only the flesh of these creatures causes *tumah*, not the hide. This presupposes that they have a physiologically distinct outer hide.

### Tumah vs Shabbat

The *gemara* then considers the position of *Rabbah bar Rav Huna amar Rav*: even the *rabbanan* who dispute Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri in the *tumah* context would agree for Shabbat purposes. The *rabbanan* in *Chullin* argue that the hides of (at least five of) the eight *sheratzim* cause *tumah* just as their flesh does — they do not distinguish between the hide and the flesh for purposes of *tum'ah*. Does that mean they would also deny the existence of a physiologically distinct hide, and hence deny *chavalah* liability for Shabbat? The *gemara* suggests that this need not follow.

### Pasuk Basis

The key distinction is introduced here: *ad kan lo pligi rabbanan alei d'Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri ella l'inyan tumah*. The *rabbanan* only dispute Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri for *tumah*, and the basis of their dispute is a *pasuk*. After listing the last five creatures — *ha'anakah v'hakoach v'haleta'ah v'hachomet v'hatinshemet* — the Torah adds: *eleh hatmei'im lachem b'chol hasheretz*, which the *rabbanan* read as coming *l'rabot oroteihen k'visran* — to include their hides as equivalent to their flesh for *tumah* purposes. This is a *chiddush* derived from a *pasuk*, not a statement about physiological reality. The *rabbanan* are not claiming that these five creatures have no distinct hide in a physical sense; they are saying that a *pasuk* instructs us to treat the hide as equivalent to the flesh for *tumah*. Physiologically, they would concede, a distinct hide exists. And since the Shabbat question of *chavalah* depends on physiological reality — can blood coagulate beneath a distinct skin layer? — and not on the *tumah* derivation, the *rabbanan* would agree with Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri that these creatures have hides, and therefore *chavalah* applies on Shabbat. The *tumah* debate is thus narrowly cabined to *tumah*.

The *gemara* then presses further: *ul'inyan Shabbos lo pligi?* — and for Shabbat, do they truly not dispute? A *braita* complicates this picture. The *braita* states that if one captures one of the eight *sheratzim* listed in the Torah and wounds them, one is *chayav*: *divrei Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*. And the Sages (*chachamim*) respond: *ein or ela lema shemanu chachamim* — there is a hide only for those that the Sages listed. This clearly frames the dispute as one about Shabbat, not *tumah*. Before drawing halachic conclusions from this, however, the *gemara* must first determine whether the text of the *braita* is internally coherent.

The difficulty is this: the Sages' list in *tumah* consisted of creatures whose hides *cause tumah* — i.e., their hides are *like* their flesh for *tumah*. That is precisely the list of creatures that do *not* have a distinctly separate hide for *tumah* purposes. So when the *braita* says only creatures on the Sages' list have a hide, it seems to say precisely the opposite of what one would expect. Abaye attempts to resolve this by adding the word "not" — only things *not* on the Sages' list have a hide. Rava objects that this is too free a textual emendation, and instead reads the *braita* differently: the Sages are saying the only things that *lack* a distinct hide are those on their list. But that too is problematic, because it would imply Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds that *none* of the eight have a hide — the opposite of his stated position. Rav Adda bar Matna then offers the resolution: the Sages' response must be understood as addressing *tumah* only, not Shabbat. For Shabbat, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri may well be correct that all eight have a hide; for *tumah*, however, only a subset is treated as having a distinct hide, based on how the Sages read the *pesukim*. This reading allows the *braita*'s internal logic to cohere and preserves the earlier conclusion that the *tumah* dispute does not automatically generate a parallel Shabbat dispute.

However, a further *braita* then makes clear that there *is* in fact a Shabbat dispute: *hatzad echad mishmona sheratzim ha'amurim baTorah, hachovel bahen chayav basheratzim sheyesh lahem orot* — one who wounds them is liable specifically for those among the eight that have a hide — again attributed to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, with the Sages disputing. This *braita* is explicitly discussing Shabbat liability, and the phrase *basheratzim sheyesh lahem orot* makes clear that not everyone agrees all eight have hides. The dispute therefore demonstrably applies to Shabbat as well.

### Yehuda Physiology

*Rav Ashi* then introduces yet another layer: *man tanna kamma — Rabbi Yehuda de'azil batar gisha*. The first (majority) opinion in the *braita* is that of Rabbi Yehuda, who bases his position not on a *pasuk* but on physical reality. Rabbi Yehuda holds that some of the creatures in the second *pasuk* have a thick skin (*gisha*) and some do not, based on actual physiological differences. The source for this is a *mishna* in which *Rabbi Yehuda omer: halta'a kichulda* — the lizard (*halta'a*) is like a weasel (*chulda*). By this Rabbi Yehuda means that unlike the other members of the last five, the *halta'a* has a thick, distinct hide comparable to the weasel — and thus its hide is physiologically distinct. Because Rabbi Yehuda derives his distinctions from physiology rather than from a *pasuk*, his position about *tumah* and his position about Shabbat are intertwined: the same physiological reality that determines whether a hide exists governs both domains.

### Both Apply

This leads to a striking observation. If the *rabbanan* who dispute Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri for *tumah* do so only because of a *pasuk* — and not because of a physiological claim — then for Shabbat purposes they would agree with Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri that all eight have hides. The *braita*'s ruling that for Shabbat, *chavalah* applies to all eight should thus not be attributed only to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri but to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri *and those who dispute him*: *tani divrei Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri u'machlokto*. The *gemara* accepts this and amends the formulation accordingly. The dispute that remains — the dispute of Rabbi Yehuda — is a genuine physiological one, applicable to both *tumah* and Shabbat. The dispute of the *rabbanan*, by contrast, is narrowly about *tumah* because it is rooted in a *pasuk*, not in a claim about the physical nature of the creatures. For Shabbat, they concede.

### Chavala Definition

Having analyzed which creatures are subject to *chavalah*, the *gemara* turns to defining what constitutes *chavalah* itself. The question is posed: *menayin l'chaburah she'eina chozeret* — what is the source for the concept that a wound (*chaburah*) is one that "does not return," i.e., does not heal or reverse itself? The answer comes from a *pasuk* in Yirmiyahu: *hayahafoch kushi oro v'namer chaburburotav* — "can an Ethiopian change his skin, or a leopard his spots?" The word *chaburburotav* is read as deriving from the root *chaburah*. But the *gemara* notes: if the verse means the leopard's colored patches, it should have said *gvanav* — "his colors." Rather, the *namer* is read here not as leopard but as a verbal form from the root *l'hamir*, to change — "can a person's wounds change?" The Ethiopian's skin color is permanent; similarly, a wound (*chaburah*) is permanent in the sense that the blood released does not return. Just as the skin of a Cushite does not revert, so too a wound does not "go back" — *af chaburah eina chozeret*. The precise meaning of *eina chozeret* is worth attention. Tosafot's position, as noted earlier, emphasizes that *chavalah* consists in the release of blood — whether into the open air or captured beneath the skin. Rashi, in at least one passage, suggests that the *chiyuv* may rest on the creation of a permanent wound. If so, one must ask: in what sense is a wound permanent, given that wounds heal? The *gemara*'s reasoning seems to be that even when a wound visibly heals, some internal damage has been done — blood was lost, vessels were broken — and that is the irreversible element. The wound leaves a mark that cannot be "returned."

## Other Sheratzim

### Wounding/Killing

The *mishna* turns next to the broader category of *shaar sheratzim* — the other creeping creatures beyond the eight. For these: *hachovel bahen patur* — wounding them carries no liability (as explained above, in the absence of a distinct hide, blood coagulating beneath the surface is not considered *chavalah*). However, *hatzadan* — capturing them — varies by intent, as discussed. And *hahorgam* — killing them — is *chayav*.

### Eliezer Small Life

The *gemara* asks: who is the *tanna* that rules that killing these creatures makes one liable? *Amar Rabbi Yirmiah: Rabbi Eliezer hi* — it is the position of Rabbi Eliezer. The source: a *braita* in which *Rabbi Eliezer omer: hahoreg kinah b'Shabbat k'horeg gamal b'Shabbat* — one who kills a louse on Shabbat is like one who kills a camel on Shabbat. The implication is that the Rabbis would disagree and hold that killing small creatures — or at least the louse — does not constitute the *melacha* of killing.

### Louse Special

*Rav Yosef* corrects a potential misreading: the dispute is not about the size of the creature. *Ad kan lo pligi Rabbanan alei d'Rabbi Eliezer ella b'kinah she'eina parah v'rabah* — the Rabbis dispute Rabbi Eliezer only regarding the louse, because they believed that a louse does not procreate — that it is produced by spontaneous generation. The debate is therefore quite specific: for a creature believed to arise spontaneously, does its destruction constitute the *melacha* of *nefilat nefesh* — taking a life? For all other creatures that do procreate, the Rabbis would agree with Rabbi Eliezer that killing them is *chayav*.

### Ram Paradigm

Both sides derive their positions from the same paradigm: the rams (*eilim*) used in the construction of the *Mishkan*, whose hides were tanned for the coverings. Killing those rams for the *Mishkan* was clearly a *melacha* and defines the category of killing on Shabbat. Rabbi Eliezer reads this paradigm broadly — *mah eilim sheyesh bahen netilat neshamah, af kol sheyesh bo netilat neshamah*: anything that involves taking a life, even a louse, is included. The Rabbis read the paradigm more narrowly — *mah eilim d'pari v'rabi*: only creatures that procreate. A creature that does not procreate lacks, in some sense, the full quality of life that generates *melacha* liability upon its destruction — or at minimum, does not fit within the paradigm of *eilim* upon which the prohibition is grounded.

### Lice Procreate

Abaye challenges the empirical premise underlying this entire debate. *Amar Abaye: v'kinah ein parah v'rabah?* — are you truly saying that a louse does not procreate? He cites a well-known dictum: *yoshev Hakadosh Baruch Hu vezan mikarnei re'emim v'ad beizei kinim* — God provides sustenance for all creatures, from the horns of the wild ox (*re'emim*) to the eggs of lice (*beizei kinim*). If lice have eggs, they procreate. The *gemara* deflects: *mina hu demikrei beizei kinim* — there is a species of creature called *beizei kinim*; this is a proper name, not a description of lice eggs. Abaye presses further: *v'hatanya: tfuyei uveizei kinim* — another *braita* lists *tfuyei* (some other small creature) and *beizei kinim* together, suggesting they are indeed two distinct creatures. The *gemara* again maintains that *beizei kinim* is simply the name of a species. The underlying belief in spontaneous generation of lice thus remains the operative assumption for the purposes of this *gemara's* halachic analysis, even if Abaye's challenge raises obvious difficulties.

### Flea Capture

The *gemara* then addresses the flea (*parosh*), which clearly does procreate. The *braita* states: *hatzad parosh b'Shabbos, Rabbi Eliezer mechayev v'Rabbi Yehoshua poter* — Rabbi Eliezer holds one liable for capturing a flea on Shabbat; Rabbi Yehoshua exempts. One might think this debate parallels the louse debate and reflects a general disagreement about the severity of killing or capturing small creatures. Rav Ashi corrects this: *tzeida ariika karamit* — one cannot import conclusions about killing from a case about capture, or vice versa; these are distinct *melachot* with distinct conditions. The debate about the flea is specifically about *tzeidah* and turns on the principle of *bimino nitzod*: *ad kan lo pligi Rabbi Eliezer v'Rabbi Yehoshua ella d'mar savar davar she'ein bemino nitzod chayav, umar savar patur* — one holds that even a creature not normally captured (*she'ein bemino nitzod*) makes one liable when captured intentionally, while the other holds that only creatures normally captured generate *chiyuv*. The flea debate is thus an instantiation of the general question about *bimino nitzod*, not a debate about the significance of small creatures.

### All Creatures

When it comes to killing (*hariga*), however, there is no analogous debate: *aval hariga, afilu Rabbi Yehoshua modeh* — even Rabbi Yehoshua agrees that killing is *chayav* for all procreating creatures. The debate about the louse was very precisely circumscribed: it applied only because of the belief in spontaneous generation. Remove that assumption — as applies to all other creatures — and there is universal agreement that taking a life on Shabbat, regardless of the size of the creature, is *chayav*. Size is simply not the relevant variable.
