# Niddah 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah: Shapir & Gender

### Shapir Definition

The twenty-fifth daf opens with a new *mishnah* at the bottom of 24b, continuing the broader discussion of which types of births and miscarriages render a mother *tme'ah leida* — impure with the impurity of childbirth. The previous *mishnah* had addressed the appearance of the fetus and, as the *gemara* developed, also the question of whether it was in the process of natural development, since even a miscarriage confers *tumah* only if it represents the kind of development that could have led to a viable child.

The key term introduced here is *shapir*. While it is sometimes translated as the embryonic sac, the more precise referent appears to be the developing fetus itself — not the *shilya*, which is the placenta and its membranes, but the thing that is presumably the fetus in formation. The difficulty is that it does not yet have a recognizably human appearance; it is something like a flesh blob, a skin-encased mass of indeterminate status. The very question the *mishnah* is exploring is whether we are prepared to call it a *vlad* — a halachically significant fetus — at all. And as we saw over the preceding days, this breaks down into two related but distinct inquiries: is it a question of appearances, or is it a question of what the thing actually is? Is this a genuine fetus in some stage of development, or is it something else entirely?

### Water/Blood Case

The *mishnah* states: *hamapeles shapir malei mayim, malei dam, malei gnunim, eina chosheshes levlad* — if a woman miscarries a *shapir* filled with water, or with blood, or with various mixed colors, she need not be concerned that it is a *vlad*.

This ruling requires clarification. Does it mean that the *shapir* is not a fetus at all — simply an unidentifiable mass that emerged from her body? Or does it mean that it was once a fetus but never developed along a natural trajectory? After all, a fetus in natural development does contain blood in its system. The distinction being implied here is not that blood is inherently foreign to a fetus, but that a properly developing fetus would not present simply as a container filled with blood or water. Something else must be going on — either it is not a fetus at all, or it developed unnaturally. This is precisely what the *gemara* will explore, but the *mishnah* itself leaves it open.

### Merukam: Stricter Rule

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-im haya merukam, teshev le-zachar u-le-nekevah* — if however the *shapir* is formed, if it actually exhibits some shape of a developing fetus, which the *gemara* will describe in considerable detail later in the *daf*, then she must observe the stricter requirements of both male and female.

What does this mean practically? If the child is male, the mother is *tme'ah* for seven days; if female, for fourteen days — so we assume fourteen days of *tumah*, taking the stricter position. On the other side, if male, she is *tehorah* from day eight through day forty; if female, only from day fifteen through day eighty. The combined stringency therefore means she is *tme'ah* from day one through day fourteen, because it might be female, and *tehorah* from day fifteen through day forty, because it cannot be *tehorah* beyond day forty since it might have been male. That is the practical meaning of *teshev le-zachar u-le-nekevah*.

### Sandal & Placenta

The *mishnah* then addresses two additional cases: *hamapeles sandal* — if the miscarriage is a *sandal*, a flattened fetus — or *shilya*, a placenta. In both cases, the ruling is likewise *teshev le-zachar u-le-nekevah*.

The *shilya* case is more straightforward. As the *gemara* states frequently: *lo she-hashilya vlad, ela she-ein shilya belo vlad* — we are not claiming the placenta is itself the fetus, but a placenta without a fetus is impossible. If there is a placenta, there must have been a fetus. And if there was a fetus, even if it subsequently dissolved and cannot be identified, it may be assumed to have been developing naturally. So the *shapir* discussion concerns cases where we cannot even establish that there was ever a fetus; here, with the *shilya*, that uncertainty is resolved.

The *sandal* — the flattened fetus — appears at first glance to be a simpler case: a fetus that was clearly once developing naturally but became compressed. However, as the *gemara* will clarify, the rabbinic understanding is that a baby becomes a *sandal* by being compressed by another baby in the womb. There is an interesting dispute among the *rishonim* as to whether the *gemara* means to suggest that a woman could become pregnant after she was already pregnant — with the second pregnancy causing the first baby to be flattened — or whether it is simply a matter of twins. But in any event, the *sandal* is understood to imply the likely presence of a second fetus. This is why the ruling of *teshev le-zachar u-le-nekevah* by the *sandal* carries an added dimension beyond mere gender uncertainty: one must also account for the possibility of a second fetus whose presence must be recognized. The *gemara* will address why this matters when the second fetus is not visible and may have dissolved.

## Shapir Contents Analysis

### Blood/Water Proof

The *gemara* now analyzes the opening case of the *mishnah*. *Bishlama dam u-mayim, lo khlum hu* — it is understandable that when the *shapir* is filled with water or blood, we say it is nothing, not a *vlad*, because while a fetus does have water and blood in its system, a genuine fetus would not present simply as a container filled with these fluids. That distinction is clear enough.

But the harder case is *gnunim* — the mixed colors, the goopy multicolored contents. Why should that not raise the concern that this was a fetus that dissolved? *Nichush she-vlad hu ve-nimokh* — perhaps we should be concerned that it was indeed a fetus and it dissolved, which would explain the mixture of colors and textures within the sac?

### Abaye: Dissolved Fetus

*Amar Abaye: kama yayin chai shattata immo shel zeh she-nimokh u-vara be-tokh me'eha* — how much raw wine do you think the mother drank that caused the fetus to dissolve inside her womb? This is a striking statement. Abaye is acknowledging that the phenomenon of a dissolved fetus is real — that *yayin chai*, undiluted raw wine, could in extreme cases cause a fetus to dissolve and abort. But he is saying that this is such a rare circumstance, requiring extreme alcohol abuse, that we do not build our default *halacha* around it. Under ordinary circumstances a miscarried fetus does not look like this. So while it is theoretically possible that the mixed colors indicate a dissolved fetus, it is not a realistic concern in the typical case.

### Rava: Not Dissolved

*Rava amar malei tnan* — Rava argues from the language of the *mishnah* itself: it says the *shapir* is *filled* with these mixed contents. *Ve-i ita de-itmuchei itmakh, mikhasar khasar* — if it had truly been a fetus that dissolved on the inside, there would be empty space within the cavity. When a formed thing dissolves, it leaves vacuums, hollows, gaps. The fact that the sac is completely filled is itself proof that it was never a fetus that dissolved; it must have been something else to begin with.

### Rav Adda: Multiple Colors

*Rav Adda bar Abba amar gevanim tnan* — Rav Adda bar Abba focuses on a different aspect of the *mishnah's* language. It says *multiple* colors, not just a single hue. *Ve-i ita de-itmuchei itmakh, kulla be-chad gavna hava ka'ei* — if it were a dissolved fetus, the contents, even if not identifiable as water or blood, would present with a more uniform, consistent color. The wide array of colors is evidence that what is inside is not the product of fetal dissolution.

## Abba Shaul: Wine & Bones

### Raw Wine Effects

Since the discussion touched on raw wine, the *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *Abba Shaul omer kover meitim hayiti* — Abba Shaul testified that he was a grave digger by profession. This made sense in the context of the era, when the practice of *likkut atzamot* — the gathering of bones for secondary burial in a niche or cave — meant that grave diggers would regularly encounter skeletal remains after the flesh had decomposed. Abba Shaul therefore had ample opportunity to observe differences in bone condition across many individuals.

His findings: *hashoteh yayin chai atzmotav srufim* — those who had consumed large quantities of raw, undiluted wine had bones that were burnt, consumed, eaten away. This is not merely a rhetorical claim; it reflects a systematic observation from his work.

### Mixed Wine Dosage

By contrast, someone who drank *mazog* — wine that had been properly diluted, as was the convention — fared differently. The *gemara* here reports Rashi's two explanations for the resulting bone condition, *atzmosav shchuyim*: either the bones were blackened, or they were somehow deficient in marrow. This, while not as severe as the damage from raw wine, was still a negative outcome when the drinking was disproportionate. However, if the person drank the proper amount — *karauy*, the right proportion — then *atzmosav meshuchin*, the bones were like anointed with oil, moist and healthy and in genuinely good condition. The *gemara* adds a parallel set of observations: *ve-chol mi she-shtiyaso meruba me-achilaso, atzmosav srufin* — anyone whose drinking exceeds their eating will have burnt bones regardless of the wine's potency. *Achilaso meruba mi-shtiyaso, atzmosav shchuyim* — if eating exceeds drinking, the bones are blackened. And *karauy, atzmosav meshuchin* — with proper balance, the bones are healthy. One must drink neither too much nor too potent a wine, and always in proper proportion to one's food.

### Og's Giant Bones

A second *baraita* is cited, again attributed to Abba Shaul — though the *gemara* notes the alternative attribution *ve-i teima Rabbi Yochanan*, meaning that some attribute this to Rabbi Yochanan, though the identification as a grave digger points more naturally to Abba Shaul. He recounts: *pa'am achas ratzti achar tzvi, ve-nichnasnti be-kulit shel meis* — once while running after a deer, he entered the thigh bone of a corpse. The bone was so vast that the deer could enter it and he could pursue for *shalosh parsa'os* — three parasangs — entirely within the bone, without catching the deer and without reaching the end of the bone. He turned back. *Amru li: shel Og Melech ha-Bashan haysa* — people told him those were the bones of Og, King of Bashan, the biblical giant. The story is embedded here in the text because of the shared context of grave digging and bone examination.

### Absalom's Eye; Stature

Yet another account: *pa'am achas niftacha me'ara tachtai* — a cave opened beneath him, and he found himself standing inside the eye socket of a buried corpse up to his nose. The eye socket was large enough to contain his entire body. When he emerged, *amru li: ayin shel Avshalom haysa* — people told him it was the eye of Absalom. This identification carries its own interest, given Absalom's renowned physical stature and the biblical account of his extraordinary hair.

The *gemara* then addresses a potential objection: *ve-shema tomar Abba Shaul nanos haya* — perhaps one would say that Abba Shaul was himself a small man, a dwarf, and therefore these observations prove nothing about the size of the bones. The *gemara* counters: *Abba Shaul aroch be-dora hava* — Abba Shaul was the tallest of his generation. Indeed, *Rabbi Tarfon magi'ah le-ketefo* — Rabbi Tarfon, himself a tall figure, only reached his shoulders.

### Yeridat Hadorot

This observation about relative stature launches the *gemara* into a striking generational catalog. *Rabbi Tarfon aroch be-dora hava, veRabbi Meir magi'ah le-ketefo* — Rabbi Tarfon was the tallest of his generation, and Rabbi Meir only reached his shoulders. *Rabbi Meir aroch be-dora hava, veRabbi magi'ah le-ketefo* — Rabbi Meir was the tallest of his generation, and Rabbi (Yehudah HaNasi) only reached his shoulders. And so it continues: *Rabbi aroch be-dora hava, veRabbi Chiyya magi'ah le-ketefo*; *Rabbi Chiyya aroch be-dora hava, veRav magi'ah le-ketefo*; *Rav aroch be-dora hava, veRav Yehuda magi'ah le-ketefo*. Note that the progression moves from *tanna'im* through the transitional figure of Rabbi Chiyya into the *amora'im*. *Rav Yehuda aroch be-dora hava, ve-Adda Dayyala magi'ah le-ketefo* — and Adda Dayyala, Rashi explains, was the rabbinic assistant (*shamsha de-rabbanan*), already a step removed from the scholarly hierarchy.

At the top of 68a the progression continues further: *Parshavna de-Pumbedita* — an office or a man's name, with Tosafot suggesting it was the governor of Pumbedita — *kailei lei le-Adda Dayyala ad palgei*, only reached half his height. And finally, *ve-chulei alma kailei lei le-Parshavna de-Pumbedita ad chartzei* — the general population only reached the loins of that figure. The subtext is unmistakably *yeridat hadorot* — the decline of generations in stature, greatness, and distinction. Each subsequent generation's tallest figure reaches only the shoulders of the previous one — a pattern that seems to describe far more than physical height, tracking as it does the succession from the greatest *tanna'im* through the *amora'im* and then into mere rabbinic functionaries and the general populace.

One might ask why Rabbi Meir is chosen as the tallest of his generation given that the *halacha* almost never follows his opinion. The *gemara* itself frequently notes that Rabbi Meir was the most brilliant of his generation — so much so that others could not always follow his reasoning. Stature in wisdom and positional authority in *pesak halacha* are not always the same thing. The choice of Rabbi Meir, Rabbi (Yehudah HaNasi), Rabbi Chiyya, and Rav is fairly clear; Rav Yehuda is a major *amora* and defensible. The choices of Abba Shaul, Rabbi Tarfon, and Adda Dayyala are less immediately obvious from the standard hierarchy.

## Shapir Meat: R. Yehoshua

### Shapir All Flesh

The *gemara* now takes up a question that was posed before Rabbi (Yehudah HaNasi): *hamapel shapir malei basar mahu* — what is the law if the *shapir* is completely filled with flesh? Not water, not blood, not a mixture of colors, but solid flesh. This, intuitively, seems like it ought to be a fetus — what is a developing fetus if not flesh? Yet it has not yet achieved the state of *merukam*, meaning it has not yet begun to take on identifiable features of a formed fetus. It is a solid flesh blob, but unshapen. Rabbi responded: *lo shamati* — I have no tradition on this matter.

### R. Yishmael: Flesh Case

At that point, *Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose* spoke up: *kach amar Abba* — here is what my father, Rabbi Yose, said. *Malei dam, tme'ah niddah* — if the *shapir* is filled with blood, she is *tme'ah* as a *niddah*, recalling the earlier discussions about blood that issues in a contained form. *Malei basar, tme'ah leida* — if it is filled with flesh, it is considered a *vlad* and she is *tme'ah* with the impurity of childbirth. So Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose provided a clear, two-part ruling from his father.

### Rabbi Challenges

Rabbi's response is striking. *Im malei davar chadash amarta lanu mishum avicha, shma'nucha* — had you simply cited your father's ruling as a novel position we had never heard, we would have accepted it without question; your father's authority is unimpeachable. *Achshav, me-ha kamaisa ke-yachida ka'amar* — but now you have revealed too much. Because the first part of the ruling — that the *shapir* filled with blood makes her a *niddah* — is in fact a minority opinion, associated with Rabbi Meir, not the majority view. The majority position, as the *gemara* understands it, is that blood contained entirely within a sac, without ever reaching the vaginal wall, does not confer *niddah* status. So since the first ruling of Rabbi Yose follows a minority view, Rabbi now suspects the second ruling might similarly track a minority position — *shema amar mishum Rabbi Yehoshua* — perhaps he was following Rabbi Yehoshua, who is about to be quoted, and whose ruling is likewise a minority opinion. *Ve-ein halacha ke-Rabbi Yehoshua* — and we would not rule like Rabbi Yehoshua, at least as a *yachid* against the *rabbim*.

### Braita: Formed/Unformed

The *gemara* now cites the relevant *baraita* directly: *hamapel shapir she-eino merukam* — if a woman miscarries a *shapir* that is not yet formed. *Rabbi Yehoshua omer vlad* — Rabbi Yehoshua says it is considered a *vlad*. *Ve-Chachamim omrim eino vlad* — the *Chachamim* say it is not. This is the center of the debate: the *mishnah* spoke of the formed fetus (*merukam*) and the unformed contents (water, blood, colors); the *baraita* now adds the middle case — the unformed flesh blob — and presents the disagreement explicitly.

### RS ben Lakish Debate

Two *amora'im* dispute what exactly is the scope of this disagreement. *Amar Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish mishum Rabbi Yehoshua: machloket be-achur* — Reish Lakish says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua that the dispute between Rabbi Yehoshua and the *Chachamim* applies only when the flesh is *achur*, opaque or dark. Rashi understands this as referring to a flesh that contains cloudy liquid; Tosafot argues the distinction is between flesh that is visually opaque — normal looking flesh with thickness and density — versus flesh that is more translucent. *Aval be-tzalul, divrei ha-kol eino vlad* — but if the flesh is transparent or translucent, everyone agrees it is not a *vlad*.

By contrast, *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi omer: be-tzalul machloket* — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says the opposite, that the debate applies precisely when it is translucent; the dispute is there, not in the opaque case. The *gemara* then asks whether according to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, the *machloket* applies only to the translucent case with the opaque being universally a *vlad*, or whether the dispute applies to both cases. *Teiku* — the *gemara* leaves this question unresolved.

## Skin Derasha

### Genesis Verse

The *gemara* now introduces a *derasha* from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya — who is, it should be noted, *stam* Rabbi Yehoshua throughout the *mishnah*, the same Rabbi Yehoshua whose ruling was quoted in the *baraita* above — and presents here his exegetical basis for his position. *Vaya'as Hashem Elokim le-Adam u-le-ishto kasnot or va-yalbishem* — "And God made for Adam and his wife garments of skin and dressed them." The *derasha* reads *or* not as leather but as skin in the literal sense: God clothed Adam and Chava in actual flesh and skin. The verse is then applied to a principle about human development: *melamed she-ein HaKadosh Baruch Hu oseh or la-adam ela im ken nutzar* — God does not form skin for a human being unless that human has been created, meaning unless it was a developing fetus. The implication is that the presence of skin on a *shapir* is proof that it was once a genuine fetus in development.

### Skin Proof Logic

The logic of the *derasha* runs as follows: skin is something that God fashions specifically for a formed human being. Therefore, anything exhibiting skin must, by the verse's implication, have been a developing human. Applied to the case of the *shapir*: if this flesh blob has skin on it — a visible epidermis distinct from mere undifferentiated tissue — that is sufficient proof that it was a *vlad*, regardless of what is found on its interior. The concern about water or blood on the inside would presumably mean that such a *shapir* lacked skin, or that the question about skin was never raised in those cases; here the argument is that once skin is present, the identity as a fetus is established.

### Opaque vs. Transparent

The *gemara* now uses this *derasha* to weigh in on the dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. *Lo shna achur ve-lo shna tzalul* — since the ruling is derived from a verse about skin, it does not matter whether the skin is opaque or transparent; the verse does not differentiate. Now, *ei amarta bishlama be-tzalul machloket* — if you accept the position of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that the dispute applies to the translucent case, then there is a good reason Rabbi Yehoshua needed to cite a *pasuk*. One would have thought that transparent skin is insufficient evidence of fetal development — the verse comes to teach us otherwise, that even transparent skin establishes fetal status.

*Ela ei amarta be-achur machloket, lama li kra, svara be-alma hu* — but if you say, as Reish Lakish does, that the dispute is only about opaque skin, then what is the verse accomplishing? Opaque skin as a proof of fetal development is a straightforward logical inference; one does not need a scriptural derivation for it. The very fact that Rabbi Yehoshua found it necessary to cite a *pasuk* suggests he was ruling in a case where logic alone would not have led to that conclusion — namely, the case of transparent skin. *Shema mina be-tzalul machloket. Shema mina.* This supports Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's position.

The observation that *lama li kra, svara be-alma hu* deserves a brief note. The phrase reflects the *gemara's* recognition of the independent force of logical reasoning (*svara*). Indeed, in more dramatic instances elsewhere, the *gemara* will go so far as to override the plain meaning of a verse because the *svara* compels a different reading — *shavik leih le-kra de-dachik ve-uki anafsheih*, setting aside a verse that strains under the weight of logic and interpreting it in accordance with reason. Such cases illustrate the remarkable authority accorded to *svara* within the *gemara's* halachic methodology. As a related matter, it should be noted that proof from *pesukim* carries relatively little independent weight in *halachic* adjudication; the *gemara* is the operative code for *halacha*, not the *Chumash*, and proofs from *pesukim* function more typically as *asmachta* — support and reinforcement — than as primary *halachic* sources.

## Rav Nachman Position

### RN: Opaque Only

Notwithstanding the seemingly strong inference just drawn in favor of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's position, the *gemara* immediately reports: *ve-chein amar Rav Nachman, amar Rabbah bar Avuha: machloket be-achur* — Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha states that the dispute between Rabbi Yehoshua and the *Chachamim* applies only in the case of opaque skin. *Aval be-tzalul, divrei ha-kol eino vlad* — but if the skin is transparent, all agree it is not a *vlad*. So despite the *gemara's* inference from the *derasha*, Rav Nachman holds with Reish Lakish. The question remains unresolved as a matter of final ruling.

### Rava: Bechoros Challenge

Rava now challenges Rav Nachman with a *mishnah* from *Bechoros*. The issue there is determining whether a birth counted as a first birth, which affects whether the next offspring is a *bechor*. The *mishnah* states: *siman bi-vehemah dakah, tinuf* — for a small animal, a malodorous discharge is the sign that a fetus had previously passed; *be-gasah, shilya* — for a large animal like a cow, a placenta; *be-ishah, shapir ve-shilya* — for a woman, either a *shapir* or a *shilya*. The question Rava presses is: why does the *mishnah* cite the *shapir* specifically for a woman and not for an animal?

### Shapir for Animals

*De-ilu shapir bi-vehemah lo patar* — the *shapir* does not exempt from *bechor* status in the case of an animal. If we are simply speaking of an opaque, unformed but plausible fetus, the logic that it is a *vlad* should apply equally to animals and humans. Why then is the *shapir* cited only for the human case?

*Ei amarta bishlama be-tzalul machloket* — if we say the dispute (and Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling that it is a *vlad*) applies to the case of transparent skin, then we have a ready explanation: the verse cited by Rabbi Yehoshua — *kasnot or*, God clothing human beings in skin — creates a specific derivation for humans. Transparent skin would be considered a *vlad* for a woman because of that *pasuk*; for an animal, where no such verse applies, it would not be. The asymmetry between human and animal is accounted for by the scriptural basis. *Mishum hachi be-ishah, deribi beih kra, patar bah; be-vehemah, de-lo ribi beih kra, lo patar bah*.

*Ela ei amarta be-achur machloket* — but if you say the debate concerns only opaque skin, the argument rests entirely on *svara*, on logical inference. And if the logic holds, *mai shna ishah u-mai shna behemah* — what distinguishes a woman from an animal? Why should an opaque, unformed but plausible flesh mass count as a *vlad* for a woman and not for an animal? No adequate answer suggests itself.

The *gemara* responds by challenging the premise: *mi savrat Rabbi Yehoshua vadai peshita lei* — do you really think Rabbi Yehoshua held with certainty that these unformed fetuses are definitively a *vlad*? Not necessarily. *Rabbi Yehoshua sfuki me-safeka lei* — perhaps Rabbi Yehoshua was himself in doubt about how to classify these borderline cases. When he said they are a *vlad*, he meant he was ruling stringently out of uncertainty, not that he had a definitive tradition. *Mesafeka lei ve-azil hacha le-chumra ve-hacha le-chumra* — he goes stringently in each direction, and the directions happen to be opposite depending on the context.

*Gabi ishah de-mamona hu* — in the case of a woman and the *bechor* question, the practical consequence for a human *bechor* is monetary: whether the father must give five *shekel* to the *kohen* for *pidyon haben*. Under doubt, *safek mamona le-kula* — monetary uncertainty is resolved leniently. Since the *kohen* is trying to extract money, the doubt favors the father. Therefore, treating the *shapir* as a *vlad* means the next child is not required to undergo *pidyon haben* — a lenient outcome for the father. This is the appropriate way to handle the doubt.

*Gabi behemah de-isura hu* — but for an animal *bechor*, the practical consequence is a prohibition: working the animal, shearing it. *Hatam de-ika gabi giza ve-avoda, safek isura le-chumra* — there the doubt is in the domain of prohibition, and prohibitory doubt is resolved stringently. We must assume the animal might be a *bechor* and treat it accordingly. So the *shapir* does not release the animal from *bechor* status, not because it is definitely not a *vlad*, but because under doubt we are strict about prohibitions. The very same unformed *shapir* generates opposite legal consequences in the two domains, not because its status differs, but because the rules governing doubt differ.

This resolution then also explains Rabbi Yehoshua's position in the context of *tumah* and *tahara* for the woman herself: *hacha nami gabi ishah safek tumah le-chumra* — here too, where we are dealing with the woman's *tumah*, we are stringent, and she is treated as *tme'ah*. As for the clean days of *tahara*, Rabbi Yehoshua would not grant them under this doubt, because that too would be a leniency. Rashi in fact explains the *mishnah* in a way that requires this understanding — that saying it is a *vlad* here means only that she receives the days of *tumah*, not that she receives any days of *tahara*.

### RH: Multiple Views

*Amar lei Rav Chanina bar Shelmia le-Rav* — Rav Chanina bar Shelmia posed the situation to Rav: we now have multiple positions before us. Rabbi (Yehudah HaNasi) said he had no tradition about the unformed flesh *shapir*. Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yose ruled it is *tme'ah leida*. Rabbi Oshaya and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi debated whether that ruling applied to opaque or transparent skin. *Mar ke-man sevira lei* — where does Rav himself stand?

### Rav vs. Shmuel

*Amar lei: ani omer echad zeh ve-echad zeh eino chosheshes* — Rav answered that whether the *shapir* is opaque or transparent, if it is unformed it is not a *vlad* and there is no concern. This aligns with the *Chachamim* who argued against Rabbi Yehoshua. *U-Shmuel amar echad zeh ve-echad zeh chosheshes* — Shmuel by contrast says that in either case one must be concerned; it may be a *vlad* and one must be stringent in all directions. Shmuel, then, is the more stringent voice, treating every case of unformed *shapir* — whether opaque or translucent — as a doubtful *vlad* requiring caution.

## Shmuel: Nechardea Rule

### Never Tahor

*Ve-azal Shmuel le-ta'amei* — Shmuel consistently applied his own reasoning. *De-chi ata Rav Dimi amar: me'olam lo ishta'kechu shapir be-Nehardea* — when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael to Bavel he reported that in Nehardea, Shmuel's city, they never treated an unformed *shapir* as *tahor*. Even if it was wholly unformed, even if it was translucent, Shmuel was always concerned that it might have the status of a *vlad*, and the woman was never declared *tahor* on that basis.

### Transparent Exception

There was, however, one exception. *Lebar me-ahu shapira de-asai le-kamei de-Shmuel* — except for a specific case of a *shapir* brought before Shmuel himself. *De-manach lei chuta de-sa'ara me-hai gisa ve-chazei lei me-hai gisa* — he placed a single strand of hair on one side and could see it through from the other side. This was genuinely transparent skin, not merely light in color or thin in texture, but so clear that a hair placed behind it was visible through it. *Amar: im ita de-vlad hava, lo hava zichi kulei hai* — Shmuel declared that if this were truly a fetus, it would not be this transparent. That extreme degree of translucence was itself definitive evidence that the object was not a *vlad*. In that singular case alone Shmuel was prepared to say it was *tahor*.

## Fetal Formation Signs

### Eyes Formation

The *mishnah* having established the category of *merukam* — the formed fetus — the *gemara* now turns to the detailed description of what that means in practice. *Tanu rabanan eizehu shapir merukam* — the rabbis taught: what constitutes a formed *shapir*? *Abba Shaul omer techilas briyaso* — Abba Shaul says it is when the fetus is at the beginning of its formation. Some manuscripts read *me-rosho*, meaning formation begins from the head, though other traditions in the *gemara* indicate development begins from the navel; Tosafot notes that some texts read *ke-rashum*, meaning lizard-like, and explains that this refers not to the shape but to the scale — the external limbs are at the size of a lizard's. *U-shtei einav ki-shtei tipin shel zvuv* — its two eyes look like two drops, the two eyes of a fly: extremely small, like dots.

### RChiyya Details

Rabbi Chiyya taught a more detailed *baraita* on the same theme, expanding on the description of fetal formation signs. The two eyes, *meruchakim ze mi-ze* — are spread apart from one another, positioned toward the sides of the head rather than close together as in a fully formed human face. *Shnei chotmin ki-shtei tipin shel zvuv* — the two nostrils are likewise like two tiny drops, each like the eye of a fly in scale. *U-mekuravim ze la-ze* — but unlike the eyes, the nostrils are close to each other. *U-fiv patuach ke-chut ha-sa'ara* — the mouth appears as just a hairline opening, a thin line. *U-geviyato ka-adasha* — and the genital area looks like a lentil bean; this is described specifically for the male, a small round protrusion.

*Ve-im hayta nekevah, nidonah (or nikeret) ki-se'orah le-orkah* — and if it was female, the appearance is like a barley bean lengthwise, and the *gemara* will shortly specify that it appears as a barley bean split down the middle, which is how the beginning of female genital development presents.

### No Digits Yet

*Ve-chituch yadayim ve-raglayim ein lo* — at this stage the fetus does not yet have defined hands and feet. Whether this means there are no limbs at all or that the digits are not yet separated is not entirely certain; it may be that what is being described is the absence of distinct fingers and toes rather than the absence of limb buds entirely. *Ve-al ze meforash ba-kabbala* — on this a verse from *Iyov* is cited: *halo ke-chalav tatchini* — did You not pour me out like milk; *u-ki-gevina takpiani* — You congealed me like cheese; *or u-vasar talbisheni* — You clothed me with skin and flesh; *u-ve-atzamot u-gidim te-sochecheni* — and with bones and sinews You wove me. The verse is read as describing the sequential stages of fetal development, with skin and flesh preceding the full formation of bones and limbs.

### Inspection Methods

*Ve-ein bodkin oto be-mayim* — one does not inspect this fetus in water. *She-ha-mayim azim ve-tordin oto* — water is harsh and will cause the delicate tissue to dissolve or disintegrate. *Ela bodkin oto be-shemen* — instead, one uses oil for inspection, *she-ha-shemen rach u-metzachtzecho* — because oil is gentle and clarifies the surface, allowing one to see the features clearly. *Ve-ein ro'im ela ba-chamma* — and the inspection should be done only in sunlight, not in artificial light or shade. The precision required to identify these minute features — dots for eyes, a hairline for the mouth — makes adequate illumination essential.

### Gender Detection

*Keitzad bodkin oto* — and what specifically are they inspecting for? *Le-da'at im zachar hu im nekevah hi* — to determine whether it is male or female. This is of direct *halachic* consequence: the gender of the fetus determines whether the mother's period of *tum'at leida* is forty days or eighty days. If the *shapir* is confirmed to be *merukam*, she is *tme'ah leida*, but the length of the subsequent *taharah* period depends on the gender. Therefore one wants to inspect the genital area as carefully as possible without disrupting the tissue — hence the use of oil and sunlight.

## Stick Test Debate

### Toothpick Method

*Abba Shaul omer* — and some say *Abba Shaul bar Ramash* — describes a practical testing method. *Mevi kisam she-rosho chalak* — one brings a toothpick with a smooth tip, free of splinters. *U-mena'ne'o be-oto makom* — one moves it around in the genital area of the fetus. *Im sachsech, be-yadu'a she-zachar hu* — if the toothpick meets an obstruction, it is known to be male; the resistance comes from a protruding structure, identifiable as the beginning of the male genital. *Ve-im lav, be-yadu'a she-nekevah hi* — and if there is no such obstruction, it is known to be female.

### Vertical Movement Only

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha: lo shanu ela mi-lemata le-ma'ala* — Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha specifies that this method is valid only when the toothpick is moved vertically, from below upward.

### Side Movement Risk

*Aval min ha-tzedadim, eima kotlei beit ha-rachem ninhu* — if one moves the toothpick from side to side, there is a concern that any resistance encountered might not be from the male genital at all, but from the walls of the uterus (or, as the context suggests, possibly the labia). A false-positive reading of obstruction in the lateral direction could therefore lead to an erroneous gender determination. Only vertical movement provides a reliable result.

## Female Genitalia Signs

### Barley Bean Split

*Amar Rabbah bar Abba, tana: im hayta nekevah nidonit ki-se'oret sedukah* — Rabbah bar Abba cited a *baraita*: if it was female, the genital area is judged by its resemblance to a barley bean that is split down the middle. This describes the visible external anatomy of early female fetal development — a small double protrusion divided by a central groove.

### Scrotum Alternative

*Masiv lei Rav Nachman: u-dilma chuta de-veitzim ninhu* — Rav Nachman raised an objection: how do we know that this split protrusion represents female genitalia? Perhaps it is the scrotum of a male fetus, with the central line being the *raphe* — the dividing seam between the two scrotal compartments, visible on the exterior.

### Scrotum Absent

*Amar Abaye: hashta beitzim gufaihu lo yedi'ei* — Abaye responded: at the developmental stage we are discussing, the scrotum itself has not yet descended or developed externally, so it cannot be seen at all. *Chuta de-veitzim yedi'ei* — and you think the dividing line of the scrotum would already be visible? Clearly not. If there is a visible split protrusion at this stage, it must be female genitalia.

## Limb Thread Appearance

### RAmram Thickness

*Amar Rav Amram, tana: shetei yerecho teha ki-shnei chutin shel zehorit* — Rav Amram cited a *baraita*: the two thighs of the fetus at this stage of development resemble two strands of red thread. *Ve-amar Rav Amram alah: ke-shel erev* — and Rav Amram added by way of clarification: they are as thick as the *erev*, which is the thicker element of the weave (the woof rather than the warp). *U-shnei zero'oteha ki-shnei chutin shel zehorit* — similarly the two arms look like two threads of the same material. *Ve-amar Rav Amram: lo ke-shel sheti* — and the arms are the thickness of the *sheti*, which is the thinner element of the weave. The arms at this stage are therefore thinner than the thighs.

## Hair Test Requirement

### Hair Required

*Amar lei Shmuel le-Rav Yehuda: Shinena* — Shmuel addressed his student Rav Yehuda with the affectionate epithet meaning "sharp one." *Lo ta'avid uvda ad she-yash'ir* — do not render a definitive ruling treating something as a *vlad* until you see hair on it. This is a notably stringent requirement, since fetal hair development occurs relatively late and is not easily visible on a tiny miscarried fetus.

Shmuel's overall approach now becomes apparent in its full scope: from the opposite direction, he had already stated that even an unformed, translucent *shapir* requires concern and must be treated stringently as a possible *vlad*. Now he adds that at no point should one treat it as a *definite* and fully established *vlad* until hair is present. So throughout the entire intermediate range — from formless to clearly formed but before hair — Shmuel holds the status in suspense: *tme'ah* on the *tumah* side, but no *taharah* days.

### Contradiction Noted

*U-mi amar Shmuel hachi? Ve-ha-amar Shmuel echad zeh ve-echad zeh chosheshes* — this raises an apparent contradiction. Shmuel said in the previous discussion that in both cases — whether opaque or translucent — one must be concerned that it is a *vlad*. If he is concerned about even an unformed *shapir*, that seems to indicate he treats it as a *vlad*. But here he says do not call it a *vlad* until there is hair. These two positions seem to pull in opposite directions: one says treat even the formless as a *vlad*, the other says do not call even the formed a *vlad* until it has hair.

### RAmi Resolution

*Amar Rav Ami bar Shmuel* — Rav Ami bar Shmuel resolved this from his own transmission of Shmuel's teaching. *Lei-chosh chosheshes* — yes, one is concerned and treats it as a *vlad* in the stringent direction: she receives the days of *tumah*. *Yemei taharah lo yahavinan lah ad she-yash'ir* — but we do not grant her days of *taharah* until hair is present, meaning we do not treat the *vlad* as fully established in the lenient direction either. From the very beginning, when the *shapir* is still unformed and even translucent, through the point when it begins to take on recognizable features but before hair develops, the entire period is treated as a doubtful *vlad*: the days of *tumah* are observed, but no days of *taharah* are granted. Only once hair is visible does the status shift to a definitively established *vlad* with full accompanying *taharah* days.

## Fetus Dating Story

### 41-Day Fetus

The *gemara* concludes with a remarkable story illustrating Shmuel's concern about uncertainty in these judgments — and paradoxically, his extraordinary personal ability to transcend it. *De-hahu shapira de-atai le-kamei de-Mar Shmuel* — a miscarried fetus was brought before Shmuel. He examined it and declared: *arbai bar arbayin ve-chad yoma* — this fetus is 41 days in development.

### 40 Days Calc

*U-vachashi mi-yoma de-azla le-tvila ad hahu yoma, ve-lo havei ela arbayin yoman* — when the relevant party calculated backward from the day the fetus was miscarried to the most recent day the woman had immersed in the *mikveh*, the interval came to only 40 days. Shmuel's count of 41 days of development thus implied that conception had occurred before the immersion, meaning the husband had cohabited with his wife while she was still a *niddah*. *Ve-amar lehu: hai bi-niddah ba'al* — Shmuel declared: this man had relations with his wife while she was a *niddah*. *Ve-odi ve-aktafei* — the husband confessed and Shmuel administered lashes.

### Shmuel Exceptional

*Shani Shmuel de-rav govria* — Shmuel was exceptional; his ability was sui generis. He could look at a fetus and determine its developmental age to the day with a precision that others simply could not match. It is precisely because he understood how difficult this assessment is for ordinary people — and how catastrophic an error in judgment could be — that his rules for everyone else are so stringent. His own extraordinary capacity led him to appreciate rather than underestimate the risks of error. And therefore while he himself could function with confidence, he issued rules for his students and for posterity that demanded caution at every stage.

One final observation is worth noting. The story introduces the 40-to-41-day threshold, which raises a question about the nature of all the preceding discussions. Throughout this *daf*, when the *gemara* discusses unformed or partially formed fetuses, the question has been framed in terms of identification: is this *shapir* really a *vlad* or is it something else? Yet the story of Shmuel's dating suggests an alternative framing: perhaps the concern is not purely one of identification, but also of sufficiency of development — whether the fetus has reached 40 days and thus attained a threshold of *halachic* significance. Both framings may be simultaneously operative. And for those familiar with the first chapter of *Ketubot*, the phrase *shani Shmuel de-rav govria* carries an additional resonance there that provides its own illuminating counterpoint to this passage.
