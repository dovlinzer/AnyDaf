# Megillah 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nikra u'Metargem

### Mishnah Overview

The previous *daf* concluded with a discussion of inappropriate ways to render the *targum* of certain verses, and the present *mishnah* continues directly on the theme of reading and translation in public practice. The governing framework is the institution of *targum* — the Aramaic rendering of the biblical text that would follow each verse or passage as it was read aloud in the *beit knesset*. Not every passage that was read was necessarily accompanied by its *targum*, and not every passage could even be read at all in a public setting. The *mishnah* sets out a series of cases that illustrate these distinctions.

### Reuven Story

*Maaseh Reuven nikra v'lo metargem* — the story of Reuven's act with Bilhah, Yaakov's concubine, is read but not translated. As will be elaborated in the *gemara*, the concern is for Reuven's honor, and the *targum* would make the account too accessible to the broader, less scholarly audience assembled in the *shul*.

### Tamar & Others

*Maaseh Tamar v'Yehudah nikra umetargem* — the story of Tamar and Yehudah is read and translated. Despite the morally uncomfortable circumstances that frame the narrative, Yehudah ultimately acknowledges his wrongdoing and the episode comes to serve as a model of *teshuvah*. That redemptive quality justifies full public translation.

### Eigel Stories

*Maaseh eigel harishon nikra umetargem* — the first story of the golden calf is read and translated — a statement that implies, somewhat surprisingly, that there is also a *second* story of the *eigel*. *V'hasheini nikra v'lo metargem* — the second is read but not translated. The content and rationale for this distinction will be unpacked in the *gemara*.

### Kohanim & David

*Birchas Kohanim* and *maaseh Dovid v'Amnon* — the priestly blessing and the incident of Amnon and Tamar — are grouped together in the *mishnah* as cases that are read but not translated. It is striking that the *mishnah* joins one passage from the *Torah* and one from *Nevi'im* under a single ruling. The grouping of *Birchas Kohanim* here is particularly puzzling; its content is entirely positive and seemingly uncontroversial. The rationale will emerge in the *gemara*.

### Prohibited Haftarot

The *mishnah* then turns to *haftarot* that are entirely prohibited. *Ein maftirin b'maaseh Merkava* — one may not read as a *haftarah* the passage from the beginning of Yechezkel that deals with divine mysteries. *Rabbi Yehuda matir* — Rabbi Yehuda permits it. As *Tosafot* notes, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda, which is why on *Shavuot* morning we do in fact read *maaseh Merkava* as the *haftarah*. *Rabbi Eliezer omer ein maftirin b'hoda'at Yerushalayim* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that the passage in which the prophet publicly catalogues Jerusalem's sins may not be read as a *haftarah*. As the *gemara* will clarify, the concern is not simply that the content is harsh, but that the specific phrasing places the reader in the posture of one standing over the community and pronouncing judgment upon it.

The very existence of these prohibitions reflects an important historical reality: the selection of *haftarot* was not yet fully fixed. There was apparently considerable latitude in different communities, and the *mishnah*'s rulings function precisely because that choice existed and needed to be constrained.

## Gemara Categories

### Three Categories

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita* that explicitly organizes the material into three tiers: *yesh nikrain umitargemin* — some passages are read and translated; *yesh nikrain v'lo mitargemin* — some are read but not translated; and *yesh lo nikrain v'lo mitargemin* — some are neither read nor translated in public. What follows is a detailed examination of each category, focusing on passages where one might have assumed the practice would be otherwise.

### Read & Translate

The *gemara* proceeds to enumerate passages that are read and translated, noting in each case that one might have thought they should be suppressed or at least not rendered into Aramaic for the full community.

*Maaseh Bereishis* — the creation narrative. *Peshita* — obviously it is read, so why state it? The *hava amina* is that one might fear that making the account fully accessible will prompt people to probe forbidden metaphysical questions: *ma l'mala ma l'mata*, what is above and below, *ma l'fnim ma l'achor*, what preceded creation and what follows it. Nevertheless, the *gemara* concludes we are not sufficiently concerned — such curiosity will not lead anywhere spiritually dangerous.

*Maaseh Lot v'shtei bnotav* — the story of Lot and his daughters, which involves incest. Again, one might object. The *hava amina* is not concern for Lot's honor — he was not Jewish — but rather for the honor of Avraham, whose relative this was, and whose prayers had saved him. The *gemara* concludes that this consideration does not override the obligation to transmit the story.

*Maaseh Tamar v'Yehudah* — one might have thought we should protect Yehudah's honor. The *gemara* concludes *shvachei* — it is ultimately to his credit — *d'odei*, because he confessed.

*Maaseh eigel nikra umetargem rishon* — the first eigel story is read and translated. The *hava amina*: perhaps we should protect the honor of the Jewish people, *neichush lichvodan shel Yisrael*, given the gravity of what they did. The *gemara* responds: *kol she-kein denicha lehu d'havei lehu kaparah* — it is actually beneficial that they suffer a degree of embarrassment, since that embarrassment constitutes something of an *atonement*. This is a striking argument: the people being discussed are not literally alive to experience the shame, yet the *gemara* suggests that the collective representation of their disgrace serves an atoning function. The logic seems particularly applicable to so weighty a sin, one whose effects ripple through generations.

There is something theologically striking about this entire enterprise. The *Torah* itself contains these stories, and if the *Torah* is divine, then God chose to make them part of the received text. Yet the Rabbis are effectively deliberating about which portions of it are appropriate for which audience to hear. One is reminded of contemporary decisions about which *teshuvot* to publish or withhold from a collected volume — the parallel raises the same tension.

### Klalot u'Brachot

*Klalos u-brachos nikrain umitargemin* — the curses and blessings are read and translated. The *gemara* asks: *peshita*, obviously — why should we imagine otherwise? The *hava amina*: perhaps we worry that hearing the terrible curses will *chalsha da'atayhu de-tzibbura*, leave the community despondent and disheartened. The *gemara* concludes we are not concerned — indeed, some argue it is beneficial, keeping people away from *aveiros* out of awareness of consequences. *Rashi* records a variant reading (*girsa*) suggesting the concern was the opposite: that people might perform *mitzvot* exclusively out of fear of punishment rather than from *ahavah v'yirah*. Either way, the *gemara* rules that the full text with translation is appropriate.

*Azharos v'onshin nikrain umitargemin* — warnings and their penalties are also read and translated. The *hava amina*: one might think to suppress the punishments so that people are not motivated only by fear. The *gemara* rejects this: we do not editorially trim God's *Torah*. And indeed, as is noted, so much of the *Torah* — especially *Devarim* — is built around the structure of command and consequence.

### Tanach Stories

*Ma'aseh Amnon v'Tamar nikra u-mitargem* — the story of Amnon's violation of his sister Tamar is read and translated. *Ma'aseh Avshalom* — the story of Avshalom's rebellion — is similarly read and translated. The *hava amina* in the latter case: *leichush likraya de-David*, perhaps we should be concerned for David's honor, since a son's disgrace reflects on the father. The *gemara* concludes we need not be concerned, apparently because the narrative is not directly about David himself.

*Ma'aseh pilegesh be-Giva* — the incident of the concubine at Gibeah — is read and translated. The *hava amina*: perhaps we should protect the honor of *shevet Binyamin*, since the perpetrators were Benjaminites. The *gemara* concludes that this reflects on the particular individuals, not on the tribe as a whole.

This raises a broader question that the *gemara* does not fully address: why are some figures protected — Reuven, Aharon — while others, like those implicated in *maaseh pilegesh be-Giva* or *mechirat Yosef*, are not? The *Torah*'s willingness to present human beings in their full complexity is part of its extraordinary power, even as the Rabbis are here making choices about what to amplify and what to muffle in the public reading.

### Hodaat Yerushalayim

*Hoda et Yerushalayim et to'avoteha* — the verse in which the prophet declares Jerusalem's sins — is read and translated. *Peshita*, obviously, since it is simply one of many prophetic passages castigating the Jewish people. The *gemara* clarifies: *le-apuki mi-de-Rabbi Eliezer* — this ruling comes specifically to exclude the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that this passage may not even be used as a *haftarah* at all.

A *baraita* elaborates. There was once a man who stood at the reader's platform (*yored lifnei ha-teiva*) and began reciting *Hoda et Yerushalayim et to'avoteha*. Rabbi Eliezer said to him: before you go about inspecting the sins of Jerusalem, *tzei u-vdok be-to'avot imecha* — go examine your own mother's sins. The rebuke carries a particular force: by reading that verse in that mode, the reader positions himself as the one pronouncing judgment over the community, standing above them as an inspector of their failings. Subsequently, *badku acharav u-matzau bo shemetz pasul* — when they investigated the man's background, they found a genealogical blemish. Whether Rabbi Eliezer knew this when he spoke or whether his sharp retort happened to be vindicated by subsequent investigation, the essential objection stands: the particular phrasing of *hoda* — instruct, inform — makes the reader the one who is cataloguing Jerusalem's sins, and that posture is inappropriate. This is distinct from other prophetic passages that speak *about* the people's sins in a more descriptive or communal frame. The *gemara* accordingly rules that the mainstream position is to read and translate it, excluding Rabbi Eliezer's stringency.

## Read, Not Translate

### Reuven Case

Turning to the second category — *nikrin v'lo mitargemin* — the *gemara* brings a *maaseh* involving Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel. He was visiting a place called Chavul, and the *chazan* was reading from the Torah. The *chazan* read the verses describing Reuven's act: *vayiskhav et Bilhah pilegesh aviv*, and continued into the following verse, *vayihyu bnei Yaakov shneim asar*. Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel immediately instructed the *meturgeman* — not the reader, who is permitted to read — to translate only the latter part, *vayihyu bnei Yaakov shneim asar*, and to skip the translation of the verse identifying Reuven's act. *V'shibchuhu chachamim* — the Sages praised him for doing so.

This account is illuminating in more than one way. It seems that the practice of not translating *maaseh Reuven* was not yet universally established or enforced; otherwise there would be no need for Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel's intervention and no cause for the *chachamim* to praise it. It also suggests that the visiting rabbinic scholar was not always the automatic authority in local *batei knesset*, and that these figures were often working to establish or extend their influence over local practice.

### Eigel HaSheni

The *gemara* now addresses the second *eigel* story. *Maaseh eigel hasheni nikra v'lo mitargem* — it is read but not translated. What does this mean? *Eizo maaseh eigel hasheni?* — what constitutes the second story? The *gemara* identifies it as the passage from *vayomer Moshe* through *vayar Moshe*, that is, the retelling of the events in which Moshe confronts Aharon and Aharon responds. *Rashi* explains that the problematic element is Aharon's reply: *va'ashlichehu ba'esh vayetzei haeigel hazeh* — "I threw it into the fire and out came this calf." The phrasing implies that the calf emerged with its own independent force, suggesting some genuine power or divine energy behind the idol. Translating this openly might give the impression that there was genuine substance — *mamashut* — to the *eigel*, and that is what we suppress.

### Tosafot Girsa

*Tosafot* offers a different explanation, citing the *Yerushalmi*. According to *Tosafot*, the passage left untranslated is not the one containing Aharon's problematic statement but rather the one in which Moshe publicly berates Aharon — *mah asa lecha ha'am hazeh ki hiveita alav chata'ah gdolah*. The reasoning rests on a principle stated in the *Yerushalmi*: *ein domeh gnuto shel yachid b'yachid o shel tzibur b'tzibur l'gnuto shel yachid b'tzibur* — the shame of an individual embarrassed privately, or a community embarrassed before a community, is qualitatively different from the shame of an individual whose disgrace is exposed before the broader public. That last situation is the most severe. *Mishum kvodo shel Aharon lo ratza l'targem* — out of concern for Aharon's honor, one does not translate the passage in which Moshe publicly castigates him before all of Israel.

*Tosafot*'s explanation faces an obvious challenge: we earlier established that *maaseh Amnon v'Tamar nikra u-mitargem*, and that story similarly involves an individual's disgrace being made public. The answer offered is that Amnon and Tamar are not figures whose honor we are especially concerned to protect in the same way as Aharon. This leaves some questions open, including why *mechirat Yosef* or other equally troubling individual shaming narratives are not similarly restricted — a question that receives no clear answer here.

The *gemara* adds a *baraita* from Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: *l'olam yehei adam zahir b'teshuvato* — a person should always be careful in how he responds, particularly when under pressure or attack. Because of the way Aharon responded to Moshe — *va'ashlichehu ba'esh vayetzei haeigel hazeh* — *porku hame'orerim*, the skeptics and questioners found license to cast off the divine yoke. That response gave apparent credibility to the notion that the *eigel* had genuine independent power. This supports *Rashi*'s reading that it is specifically Aharon's statement that is the problem, and it is therefore that statement and its surrounding passage that is not translated.

### Birchas Kohanim

*Birchas Kohanim nikra v'lo metargem* — the priestly blessing is read but not translated. This remains the most puzzling case. *Mai ta'ama?* — *mishum dichitv yissa* — because it is written *yissa Hashem panav eilecha*, "may God lift His face toward you." The concern is *nesiat panim*, the showing of special favor. The *Torah* explicitly prohibits a judge from being *nose panim*, from giving preferential treatment. If God is declared to show special favor to Israel, this seems theologically uncomfortable — it implies that God does not play by the same rules He requires of human judges. By leaving the verse in the original Hebrew without a translation that would make it fully accessible, the concern is that this apparent inconsistency will be raised too openly.

One might also suggest a simpler concern: perhaps the audience who required a *targum* was more marginal in their commitment, and this particularist declaration, fully spelled out in vernacular, would be too jarring for them. The precise sociological identity of those who understood Aramaic but not biblical Hebrew is itself a fascinating historical question — Hellenized Jews would have known Greek rather than Aramaic — and the answer is not obvious.

### David & Amnon

*Ma'aseh David v'Amnon lo nikrin v'lo metargemin* — the story of David and Amnon is neither read nor translated. But the *gemara* immediately challenges this: the *mishnah* itself already stated that *maaseh Amnon v'Tamar nikra u-mitargem*! Is this not the same story?

*Lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. *Ha de'chtiv Amnon ben David* — the verses that explicitly identify him as "Amnon the son of David" are neither read nor translated, out of concern for David's honor. *Ha de'chtiv Amnon stama* — the verses that refer simply to "Amnon," without making the familial connection explicit, may be read and translated. The story is thus effectively told, but the passages that directly link the incident to David's household are omitted from public reading and translation entirely.

## Euphemisms & Language

### Offensive Verses

*Kol ha-mikra'ot ha-ktuvim ba-Torah l'gnai, koreh otan l'shavach* — biblical verses written in crude or offensive language are read aloud in more refined, euphemistic terms. This is a striking principle. The previous *daf* established that one may not offer a *targum* that softens or sanitizes the meaning of a verse — that is a violation of *halacha*. Yet here we find that the spoken reading itself is adjusted, replacing crude terminology with more refined equivalents. The distinction is that this is not a matter of *halacha* and more precisely it is not a *targum* at all, but a reading practice — and the substitution is one of register rather than meaning, a toning down of language without distortion of content.

Two examples appear in the *Torah* itself. The word *yishgalena* — a very crude term for sexual intercourse — is read as *yishkavena*, the more neutral "to lie with." The word *afolim* — referring crudely to hemorrhoids, with an implied anatomical crudeness — is read as *techorim*, the more clinical and delicate term. These are the actual substitutions made in public reading of those *psukim*.

Several additional examples come from the *nevi'im*. *Chiryonim* — excrement, stated crudely — is read as *dovyonim*, the flow of birds, which describes the same substance with much less offense. The phrase *le'echol et chareihem* — to eat their excrement — is read as *le'echol et tzo'atam*, again shifting to less crude terminology. *Ve'lishtot et meimei shineihem* — literally "to drink the water of their teeth," understood as referring to loose stools or intestinal fluid — is read as *ve'lishtot et meimei ragleihem*, their "leg water," a transparent euphemism but a more delicate one. Finally, *lamachra'ot*, another crude term for excrement, is read as *lemotza'ot*, places of excretion.

### Mockery Rules

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha takes issue with one case. He says *lamachra'ot kishman* — you should actually read that crude word as it appears, without substitution. *Mipnei she'gnai le'avodat kochavim* — because it is referring to idols, and it is entirely appropriate to speak of idols in degrading terms.

This opens into a broader principle, stated by Rav Nachman: *kol leitzanuta assura bar mi-leitzanuta de-avodat kochavim deshareya* — all mockery is forbidden except for mockery directed at idolatry, which is permitted. Several proofs are adduced. *Kara Bel karas Nevo* — Bel bows, Nevo splashes — these are described in the verse in deliberately undignified terms, including imagery suggesting the idols cannot even manage basic bodily functions. Rabbi Yannai brings a further proof from *Hoshea*, where the word *kaved* — honor — is read as *kaveid* — its weight, perhaps implying its excrement — *al tikrei kvodo ela kveido*, do not read "its honor" but "its burden," again a deliberate degradation of the idol's status.

Rav Huna bar Manoach in the name of Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika goes further still: it is permitted for a Jew to say to an idolator *shakli la'avodat kochavim ve'anchei beshin-tav* — take your idol and insert it in *shin-tav*, an unmistakably crude expression. Rav Ashi adds: *hai man de-sanei shamanei*, someone with a bad reputation — *sharei lei levazuyei be-gimel-shin* — may be spoken of in disparaging terms, while *hai man de-shafir shamanei*, one with a good reputation, *sharei lei le-shabuchei* — may be praised, and furthermore *yanuchu lo brachot al rosho* — the one who praises him will receive blessings upon his head.

### Responsibility

These rulings collectively fill in a broader picture of responsibility in speech and transmission. The discussion of *nikra u-metargem* throughout this chapter is ultimately about the choices we make in passing information along: what frame we put around it, what we amplify, what we suppress, who the audience is, and what effect the transmission will have. That framework extends beyond the synagogue reading and into everyday life — questions of *lashon hara*, public embarrassment, and the ethics of praise and blame. The *gemara* raises these themes without fully developing them here, but the connection between the laws of public reading and the broader responsibilities of speech is unmistakable.

*Hadran alach Hakoreh et ha-Megilla Omed.* This concludes the third chapter, which began with the manner of reading the *Megillah* but ranged widely across *Kriyat HaTorah*, *haftarah*, *targum*, and the ethical dimensions of public transmission.

## Kedusha Beit Knesset

### Sale of Assets

The final chapter of the tractate now begins, and it is devoted to *kedushat beit ha-knesset* — the sanctity of the synagogue. Some *Rishonim* — the *Rif*, the *Rambam*, and others — placed this chapter third rather than fourth; as organized in our text, however, the flow from the reading practices of the *beit knesset* to the sanctity of the *beit knesset* itself is coherent.

The *mishnah* opens: *benei ir she-machru rechovah shel ir* — if the people of a city sold the city square. The *rechov* — the public square — occasionally served as the gathering place for communal fast-day prayers, as we know from *Taanit*, and this occasional religious use is understood to confer upon it a degree of *kedusha*. The proceeds of its sale may be used to purchase a *beit knesset* — a synagogue proper, which has a higher level of dedicated sacred purpose. If a *beit knesset* is sold, one may use the proceeds to purchase a *teivah*, the platform or ark where the *Sefer Torah* is placed. If a *teivah* is sold, one may buy *mitpachot* — the coverings used to wrap the *Sefer Torah*. If those are sold, one may purchase *sefarim* — the other books of *Tanach*, not a *Sefer Torah* itself. And if those are sold, one may use the money to purchase a *Sefer Torah*.

The direction of movement is always upward: each step represents a greater degree of proximity to the *Sefer Torah*, and each purchase must represent an elevation in *kedusha*, not a descent. The obvious practical question — what about selling one *shul* to buy another *shul*, or one *teivah* to replace another *teivah* — is left unaddressed both in the *mishnah* and, as will be seen, largely in the *gemara* as well. This lacuna is taken up extensively by the *Rishonim*.

### Hierarchy

The hierarchy implicit in the *mishnah* runs: public square, *beit knesset*, *teivah*, *mitpachot*, *sifrei Tanach*, *Sefer Torah*. One reading of this structure is that all of the *kedusha* involved is ultimately a function of the *Sefer Torah* itself — expanding circles of sanctity radiating outward from the *Sefer Torah* to its coverings, to the platform that holds it, to the building that houses it, and finally to the public space where the *Sefer Torah* was occasionally brought in times of communal distress. On this reading, the *mishnah* does not require any independent theory of synagogue sanctity — it all derives from the *Sefer Torah*'s intrinsic *kedusha*.

### Sanctity Models

The *Rishonim* offer at least three distinct frameworks for understanding *kedushat beit ha-knesset*, and the differences have significant practical implications.

The *Ramban* understands the *kedusha* of a *beit knesset* not through the *Sefer Torah* but as a *tashmishei mitzvah* — an object used in the performance of a *mitzvah*, specifically the *mitzvah* of *davening*. Just as a *lulav* is used for the *mitzvah* of *lulav* and a *shofar* for the *mitzvah* of *shofar*, the *beit knesset* is the instrument through which the *mitzvah* of communal prayer is performed. The consequence of this position is significant: what does one do with a *lulav* after one has finished using it? One throws it away. The *Ramban*'s view thus renders the *kedusha* of a *beit knesset* minimal in a certain sense — once the community has genuinely ceased using it, it loses its sacred status. The remaining question is merely who has the authority to determine when it is no longer in use. One student's observation is apt here: it is a difficult view, and one that runs against the more intuitive sense of the *gemara*'s discussion, which seems to presuppose an intrinsic and abiding *kedusha*.

The *Ran* offers a different explanation. He too connects the *kedusha* to *tefillah* rather than to the *Sefer Torah*, but his reasoning is distinct: since *devarim shebikdusha* are recited in the *beit knesset* — the *kedushah*, the *kaddish*, *kadosh kadosh kadosh* — it is the recitation of those holy declarations, requiring a *minyan*, that invests the place with *kedusha*. Importantly, on the *Ran*'s view, a private home in which an individual prays alone would not attain the same status, since *devarim shebikdusha* require a *minyan* and cannot be said *beyachidus*.

The third view understands the *kedusha* of a *beit knesset* as patterned after the *Beit HaMikdash*. The Rabbis understood the *beit knesset* to function as a *mikdash me'at*, a miniature sanctuary, and granted it a rabbinic *kedusha* that parallels, in diminished form, the sanctity of the *Beit HaMikdash* itself. One student raises a perceptive point: while much of the *gemara*'s tendency is to ground rabbinic institutions in the *Torah* rather than in the *Mikdash*, the third model here points back toward the *Mikdash* as the source. The observation that both impulses are present in the *gemara* — and that they function as a counterweight to each other — is worth noting.

### No Downgrade

*Aval im machru Torah lo yikchu sefarim* — if they sold a *Sefer Torah*, they may not use the proceeds to purchase *sifrei Nach*. Descending the hierarchy in any direction is prohibited. Each line in the concluding section of the *mishnah* forbids using the proceeds of a higher-sanctity sale to acquire something of lower sanctity: from *Sefer Torah* downward through *sefarim*, *mitpachot*, *teivah*, *beit knesset*, and finally the *rechov*.

*Vechen bemotreihen* — any leftover funds from a transaction are also subject to the same restrictions. If one sold a *teivah* and purchased *mitpachot* with the proceeds and a sum remained, that remainder must also be applied upward, not diverted for other purposes.

One student raises a fair point: these restrictions presuppose a degree of communal stability that may not always have obtained. Over the centuries, Jewish communities have faced forced relocations, demographic shifts, and the need to liquidate sacred property under duress. The *gemara* itself, it turns out, does introduce *beraitot* suggesting that under certain conditions — with sufficient communal authority behind the decision — there is more latitude than the *mishnah* alone implies. But that discussion comes later.

## Public vs. Private

### Village vs. City

The *gemara* now introduces a crucial qualification. *Amar Rabbah bar bar Channah amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the *mishnah*'s ruling — that the sale of communal property may only be directed toward higher-sanctity acquisitions — is attributed to Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yosi as the *setam* author. The *Tosefta* explicitly records that attribution. *V'chachamim omrim harechov ein bo mishum kedusha* — the Rabbis hold, by contrast, that the public square has no *kedusha* at all. Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yosi's reasoning is *hoil umitpallelin bo b'ta'aniyot* — since people pray there on fast days, it acquires some measure of sanctity. The Rabbis counter that this is *hu kara b'alma* — that is merely an incidental designation, not a genuine identity. Using a space occasionally for prayer does not transform it into a sacred place.

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: the rules governing what may be done with the proceeds of a sale apply *lo shanu ela Beit Knesset shel kfarim* — only to a synagogue of a village. *Aval Beit Knesset shel krachim* — but a synagogue in a large city — *kevan d'mei'alma asu lei* — since people come from everywhere to pray there, *lo matzei mazvinu lei* — the local people cannot sell it. *D'havei lei d'rabbim* — it belongs to the broader public.

The question of what constitutes this public ownership is treated at length in *Tosafot*, who identifies two possible understandings. One is that since the majority of those who pray there have contributed to it financially — as we know from ancient synagogue inscriptions where prominent individuals and outside donors are recorded — they are considered part-owners. The other explanation is that even without monetary contribution, a synagogue built to serve the broader public is in some sense *made for them*, and this intent alone renders them part-owners in a meaningful sense. *Kevan d'ledat otan rabbim na'aseh chamur kedushato v'ein yacholim l'mochro* — since it was created with those many in mind, its sanctity is more stringent and it cannot be sold. As *Tosafot* also notes, one could alternatively explain that since many do in fact contribute to its construction and upkeep, they are owners in a more conventional sense.

### Rav Ashi Case

An exception is introduced through a statement of Rav Ashi himself. *Hai bei knishta d'Mata Mechasya* — the synagogue in Mata Mechasya — *af al gav d'mei'alma asu lei* — even though people come from all over to pray there — *kevan d'adaata d'didi ka'atu* — they come on account of me. Since the people are drawn to that *shul* because of Rav Ashi's presence and stature, not because of the institution itself, *i ba'ina mazvinna lah* — if I want to, I can sell it.

This is a remarkable claim. It implies that the "ownership" or authority over a *beit knesset* is not necessarily a function of who financed it, but of whose *shul* it functionally is — for whom it was made, or in whose presence it operates. Just as a city synagogue may be considered owned by the *rabbim* because it was made to serve them, so Rav Ashi's synagogue may be considered his because it was made — in practical terms — to serve his community gathered around him. The concept here is better understood as *authority* than as monetary ownership, in the same way that a communal board does not own the synagogue in the proprietary sense but is empowered to make decisions about it.

### Coppersmiths

A *meisive* challenges the rule that synagogues in cities cannot be sold. *Maaseh b'veit ha-knesset shel torsiyim* — there is a story of the synagogue of the Coppersmiths' guild in Jerusalem, *shemacharu le-Rabbi Eliezer* — which they sold to Rabbi Eliezer, who proceeded to use it for his own purposes. This was in Jerusalem, certainly a large city — *dekrachim havei* — and yet the sale apparently went through. How?

The *gemara* answers: *hahi beiknishta zuta hava* — that was a small synagogue. *Deinhu avduah* — they themselves had built it and maintained it as a private or guild institution. A small, privately constituted *beit knesset*, even within a large city, retains the character of a private institution rather than a public one, and its members retain the authority to dispose of it. This raises the question of what determines a *shul*'s identity as public or private: is it the number of contributors, the intended scope of service, the subjective sense of those who use it, or some combination of all three? The *gemara* does not resolve this cleanly.

### Jerusalem Division

The *gemara* then turns to the broader claim that synagogues in cities are treated as public property because they are considered to belong to the *rabbim*. A new question arises: does this logic apply specifically to *batei knesset*, or does it reflect something more fundamental about property in large cities — and specifically in Jerusalem?

The *mishnah* in *Nega'im* teaches that houses can become *tamei* with *tzara'at* only in *aretz achuzatchem* — inherited land — and Jerusalem is excluded because it was never distributed as a *nachala* to any particular tribe. *Tanna Kamma savar lo nischalka Yerushalayim l'shvatim* — the first opinion holds that Jerusalem was never divided among the tribes at all. *Rabbi Yehuda savar nischalka Yerushalayim l'shvatim* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that it was divided.

A *baraita* records what fell in each portion. *Mah haya b'chelko shel Yehudah* — Yehudah received the Har HaBayit, the *lishkot*, and the *azarot* — the *Ezrat Yisrael*, *Ezrat Nashim*, and *Ezrat Kohanim*. *U'mah haya b'chelko shel Binyamin* — Binyamin received the *ulam*, the *heichal*, and the *beit kodesh ha-kodashim*. The division thus ran through the center of the *Beit HaMikdash* compound itself, with Yehudah's portion covering the outer precincts and Binyamin's portion covering the inner sanctuary.

The *mizbeach* fell on a strip — *retzuah* — that protruded from Yehudah's territory into Binyamin's portion, with the altar itself built upon it. Other sources suggest that Yehudah received only a narrow eastern edge of the *mizbeach*, which is why there was no *yesod* — the projecting base — on the eastern side: the ideal was for the entire altar to rest within Binyamin's *chelek*. *V'haya Binyamin hatzaddik mitztayer aleha be'chol yom* — Binyamin grieved over this every day, longing to absorb that strip fully into his portion. *Zacha Binyamin v'na'asah ushpizchin la-shechina* — because of this yearning, Binyamin merited to become the host of the *Shechina*, with the *Kodesh HaKodashim* situated in his portion.

The *gemara* adjusts the reading of Rabbi Yehuda's position as well. The ruling attributed to him — *ani lo shamati ela limkom mekudash bilvad* — is emended slightly: not merely *makom mikdash* (the Temple site specifically) but *makom mekudash* (sanctified places), encompassing synagogues and *batei midrash* as well. These too, according to Rabbi Yehuda, are exempt from *nega'im*, because they share in the quality of public sacred space that places them outside the ordinary category of private inherited property.

### No Private Property

*Tanna Kamma savar lo nischalka Yerushalayim l'shvatim* — the first opinion, that Jerusalem was never apportioned to any tribe, carries a further implication that goes well beyond the question of *nega'im*. *Ein maskirin batim b'Yerushalayim mipnei she'einam shelahem* — one may not rent houses in Jerusalem to visiting pilgrims, because those houses are not truly the owners' to rent. The city belongs, in some fundamental sense, to all of *Klal Yisrael*, and those who happen to reside there hold their dwellings not as private property in the full sense but as something closer to a perpetual stewardship. When *Klal Yisrael* comes up for the *regel*, the houses of Jerusalem are in some meaningful sense everyone's houses, and therefore one cannot charge for access to them.

This is a striking legal and conceptual claim. It is not simply that *lo nischalka Yerushalayim l'shvatim* means Jerusalem was not assigned to a particular tribe while still allowing for individual private ownership within it. Rather, the *gemara* takes the logic further: no private property exists in Jerusalem in the ordinary sense at all. Everything belongs collectively to *Klal Yisrael*, and the residents' rights are more in the nature of usufruct than true ownership.

### Bed Rental

*Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok omer: af lo mitot* — Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok adds that one may not even charge for the beds. A resident might reason: I cannot charge for the room, since the ground beneath the house belongs to all of Israel — but the bed is my chattel, my movable property, and I may charge for that. Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok rejects this: the bed sits on this ground, and this ground belongs to *Klal Yisrael*, and therefore you cannot separate the bed from the ground on which it rests and charge me for it.

*U'lfichach* — the corollary of this arrangement is that when pilgrims offer the owners the skins of their *korbanot* — valuable leather, which the pilgrim has no way to carry home — *ba'alei ushpizin notlin otan b'zroa*, the hosts may take those hides and indeed may claim them by force. This serves as a de facto form of compensation: since you cannot charge the pilgrims, you receive something of value in exchange for the hospitality you are obligated to provide.

*Amar Abaye: sh'ma mina, orech ar'a l'mishbak inish gufa u-mashcha b'ushpizeih* — from this we derive that courtesy demands that a guest leave behind goods — a hide, a jug — at the home where he stayed, rather than stripping the place bare upon departure. The contrast with contemporary practice is noted without further elaboration.
