# Yevamot 95 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wife Dead Report

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲזוּל אִשְׁתּוֹ וְגִיסוֹ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, דְּאַהֲנִי הָנֵי נִשּׂוּאִים דְּקָמִיתַּסְרָא אֵשֶׁת גִּיסוֹ אַגִּיסוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי: אֵשֶׁת גִּיסוֹ אֲסִירָא, אִשְׁתּוֹ שַׁרְיָא,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the case of a man who married his wife's sister after he was informed that his wife was dead, the Gemara comments: **And even if his wife and his brother-in-law** both **went overseas** and he was told that they had died, the *halakha* is **that this marriage** he performed **is effective** only to the extent **that his brother-in-law's wife is forbidden to his brother-in-law.** The reason for this prohibition is that he performed a marriage ceremony with a married woman by mistake, and one who erroneously weds a married woman has thereby rendered her forbidden to her husband. The Gemara adds: **Even so,** it is only **his brother-in-law's wife** who **is forbidden** to her husband, whereas **his** own **wife** remains **permitted** to him.

The daf begins toward the bottom of 94b. The case under discussion is not the more familiar scenario of a woman whose husband is reported dead and who then remarries — that case occupied much of the preceding material — but rather its mirror image: a husband whose wife is reported dead, who then marries her sister, and whose wife subsequently returns alive. The Gemara had already addressed certain preliminary complications arising from this scenario, particularly whether the marriage to the sister creates ongoing obligations. Specifically, it considered whether, according to Rabbi Akiva, the sister would require a *get*, which would in turn render the original wife forbidden to him as the sister of his divorcée (*achos grushaso*). That possibility was ultimately rejected, and even according to Rabbi Akiva, it is assumed that the husband may return to his first wife.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא — הָוְיָא לַהּ אֲחוֹת גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva. The reason is that if the mishna follows the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, his wife is now considered, with regard to him, the sister of his divorcée.

At this point, Rav Ashi poses a question to Rav Kahana, beginning with the word *Akiva* approximately twelve lines from the bottom of 94b: if the *mishnah* can even be in accordance with Rabbi Akiva — since the man is not required to give a *get* to the sister — then why does the *mishnah* present only the case of the sister as the scenario in which the report of the wife's death would be relevant to his marrying someone new?

For most forbidden relationships (*arayot*), the prohibition does not dissolve upon the death of the relevant party. The sister, however, is the paradigmatic exception: the prohibition of *achos isha* is tied explicitly to the wife's lifetime, as the verse states that it is forbidden to take a woman alongside her sister *in her lifetime* (*bechayeha*). That is presumably why the sister is the only such case addressed in the *mishnah* — she is the only *ervah* who could become permitted once the wife dies. But one might ask: what about the mother-in-law (*chamuto*)? The general assumption is that the prohibition of marrying a woman and her mother applies even after the wife has died. Yet according to Rabbi Akiva, perhaps it does not. If so, then the *mishnah* should also have presented the case where the wife is reported dead and the husband marries the mother-in-law.

### Woman & Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: כׇּל עֲרָיוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה — אֵין צְרִיכוֹת הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט, חוּץ מֵאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת עַל פִּי בֵּית דִּין, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מוֹסִיף אַף אֵשֶׁת אָח וַאֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה. וּדְתַנְיָא: ״בָּאֵשׁ יִשְׂרְפוּ אוֹתוֹ וְאֶתְהֶן״ — אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת אַחַת מֵהֶן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת שְׁתֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** As it is taught in a *baraita*: None of those with whom relations are forbidden by Torah law require a bill of divorce from him, even if he married them in a proper manner, apart from a married woman who married by mistake by permission of the court. And Rabbi Akiva adds: Also a brother's wife and a wife's sister. As it is taught in a *baraita*: The Torah states, with regard to one who takes a woman and her daughter: "They shall be burned in fire, he and they [*et'hen*]"(Leviticus 20:14). Now this cannot literally mean that both women are burned, as the first woman he took did not sin at all. The Sages therefore explained that the word *et'hen* means he and one of them [*mehen*]. This is the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says: He and both of them.

To understand why this is relevant, the Gemara cites a *baraisa* on the verse *ki yikach ish es isha ve'imah* — "if a man takes a woman and her mother, you shall burn him and them (*oso ves-hen*)." The question arises: what did the wife do that she too should be executed? The debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva turns on the reading of *ves-hen*. Rabbi Yishmael reads it as referring to only one of them — the mother — *oso ves achas meihen*. Rabbi Akiva reads it as both — *oso ves shteihen* — meaning both the mother and the grandmother, or some other combination involving multiple generations, not the original wife.

### Abaye vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְאַבָּיֵי דְּאָמַר מַשְׁמָעוּת דּוֹרְשִׁין אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: חֲדָא כְּתִיב, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: תַּרְתֵּי כְּתִיב — שַׁפִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** Granted, this makes sense according to the explanation of this dispute suggested by Abaye, who said that the interpretation of the meaning of the verse is the difference between them. In other words, Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva did not argue over the *halakha* itself, but merely over the manner in which the *halakha* is derived from the Torah. That is, Rabbi Yishmael holds that it states: One woman, and the plain meaning of the verse is: He and one of them. And Rabbi Akiva holds that it states: Two, e.g., if he took two women who were both forbidden to him, such as his mother-in-law and her mother, they are both liable to be executed by burning. If this is the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael, it is fine, as there is no proof from here that the prohibition of a mother-in-law lapses upon his wife's death.

Abaye and Rava disagree about how to interpret this tannaitic dispute. According to Abaye, Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva are not debating any practical *halakha* at all; they are debating only how to read the verse (*mashma'us dorshin ika beina'ihu*). Rabbi Yishmael understands the verse as describing one configuration of the prohibited relationship; Rabbi Akiva understands it as describing two distinct configurations. No one debates the underlying *din*. This reading, as Abaye acknowledges, is fine (*bishlama le-Abaye*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר: חֲמוֹתוֹ לְאַחַר מִיתָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ — לִיתְנֵי נָמֵי חֲמוֹתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** However, there is a difficulty according to the explanation of Rava, who said that the practical difference between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva's opinion concerns one's mother-in-law after his wife's death. Rabbi Yishmael maintains that even after the death of one's wife he is liable for marrying his mother-in-law. Rabbi Akiva maintains that one is liable only if both women are alive, as the verse mentions two women, but if the first one has already died his relations with the second woman are no longer punishable by Torah law. If so, let the *tanna* of the mishna, according to Rabbi Akiva, also teach that one must give a bill of divorce to his mother-in-law whom he married by mistake, as she too will be permitted to him after his wife's death.

According to Rava, however, there is a genuine halakhic dispute — namely, whether the prohibition of the mother-in-law persists after the wife's death (*chamosto le'achar misah ika beina'ihu*). On Rava's reading, Rabbi Akiva's phrase *oso ves shteihen* is interpreted to mean that the death penalty applies only when both women — the wife and the mother — are still alive. If the wife had already died, the man who then marries the mother would not be executed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: נְהִי דְּמַיעֲטַהּ קְרָא מִשְּׂרֵפָה — מֵאִיסּוּרָא מִי מַיעֲטַהּ קְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana said to Rav Ashi: Granted that the verse excluded her from the punishment of burning, did the verse also exclude her from a prohibition? Even Rabbi Akiva agrees that the Torah prohibits a man from marrying his mother-in-law after his wife's death. Consequently, he cannot marry her in a permitted manner, despite the fact that according to Rava's explanation Rabbi Akiva maintains that they are not executed by burning.

This reading creates the logical opening for Rav Ashi's question: if the *mishnah* can accord with Rabbi Akiva, and if Rabbi Akiva holds that the mother-in-law prohibition dissolves after the wife's death, then the *mishnah* should include the case of the mother-in-law as a second scenario in which hearing of the wife's death opens up a new permitted marriage. The Gemara's response, formulated by Abaye, is that even if Rabbi Akiva exempts from the *sreifah* (burning), one cannot conclude that he removes the prohibition entirely. As Tosafos makes explicit: the phrase *me'issura mi mietei rachmana* — "did the Torah exempt it from prohibition?" — refers not to some minor rabbinic *issur* but to the full weight of a negative commandment and *kares*. The man simply would not be executed; the *issur* remains. Therefore, it would not be relevant to include this scenario in the *mishnah*.

## Sleeping Sister

### Hava Amina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתֵאָסֵר בִּשְׁכִיבָה דַּאֲחוֹתָהּ, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם! לָא דָּמֵי: אִשְׁתּוֹ, דִּבְמֵזִיד אֲסִירָא מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, בְּשׁוֹגֵג גְּזַרוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question, from a different perspective: And let his wife be forbidden by his sexual relations with her sister, just as it is in the case of a woman whose husband went overseas, who is forbidden to her husband if she had relations with another man by mistake. The Gemara answers: This is not comparable. With regard to his wife, who is forbidden to him by Torah law if she committed adultery intentionally, the Sages decreed concerning her that she is forbidden to him even if she did so unwittingly.

Returning to the case at hand — Reuven is married to Rachel, she travels to *medinat hayam*, word arrives that she has died, and Reuven marries her sister Leah — the Gemara now raises the following question: granted that we do not treat this as a genuine marriage, and Leah requires no *get*, should we not at least say that the very act of Reuven sleeping with Leah ought to render Rachel forbidden to him?

The intuition behind this suggestion is a kind of symmetry with the laws of adultery: if a woman sleeps with another man, that act renders her forbidden to her husband, even if she acted entirely unknowingly (*shogeg*). The Rabbis extended the biblical prohibition to cases of *shogeg* as a rabbinic decree (*gazru bah rabanan*). Should the same not apply here in reverse?

### No Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה, דִּבְמֵזִיד לָא אֲסִירָא מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, בְּשׁוֹגֵג לָא גְּזַרוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן. וּמְנָלַן דְּלָא אֲסִירָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״אוֹתָהּ״, אוֹתָהּ — שְׁכִיבָתָהּ אוֹסַרְתָּהּ, וְאֵין שְׁכִיבַת אֲחוֹתָהּ אוֹסַרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** However, with regard to a wife's sister, where even if the sister sins intentionally the wife is not forbidden to him by Torah law, if he did so unwittingly the Sages did not decree with regard to him. And from where do we derive that she is not forbidden? As it is taught in a *baraita* that in the verse: "A man, when his wife goes aside…and a man lies **with her"** (Numbers 5:12–13), the emphasis of "her" teaches: It is **her intercourse** with another man that **renders her forbidden** to her husband, **but the intercourse of her sister does not render her forbidden.**

The Gemara rejects the comparison — *lo damya*. When a wife commits adultery, even inadvertently, the Rabbis decreed she becomes forbidden because the underlying biblical prohibition is severe: intentional adultery (*bemezid*) renders her forbidden *mid'Oraisa*. The rabbinic extension to *shogeg* is therefore grounded in a pre-existing biblical framework. But in the case of *achos isha*: sleeping with the wife's sister, even intentionally (*bemezid*), does not render the wife *Rachel* forbidden to Reuven under any biblical provision. There is no *issur d'Oraisa* establishing that intercourse with the sister causes the wife to become forbidden. Consequently, there is no basis for a rabbinic decree extending such a consequence to cases of *shogeg* either. (*Bishogeg lo gazru bah rabanan.*)

### Otah Drasha

The Gemara then cites a *baraisa* that grounds this principle in a textual source. From the verse in the *parsha* of *sotah* — *ve'shachav ish osah shichvas zera* — the Rabbis derive: *otah* — specifically *her* act of sleeping with another man renders her forbidden (*shichvasah osartah*), but the act of her sister sleeping with her husband does not render her forbidden (*ve'ein shichvas achosah osartah*).

But why did the Torah need to state this? Surely this is self-evident. The *baraisa* therefore presents the logical argument that might have led us astray — the *hava amina* requiring refutation.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיָּכוֹל, וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁבָּא עַל אִיסּוּר קַל — נֶאֱסַר הָאוֹסֵר, מְקוֹם שֶׁבָּא עַל אִיסּוּר חָמוּר — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנֶּאֱסַר הָאוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** As, were it not for this verse, one **might** have thought: Could this **not** be derived through **an** *a fortiori* **inference: And if in** a case **where he has relations subject to a light prohibition, the one causing** her to be rendered **prohibited is forbidden,** then **in** a situation **where he has intercourse subject to a severe prohibition, is it not right that the one causing** her to be rendered **prohibited** should be **forbidden?** This *a fortiori* inference will be explained later in the Gemara.

The *hava amina* is a *kal vachomer*. In a case where a man has intercourse with a woman who is an *issur kal* — adultery is here called a "light prohibition" — the one who defines her status (*ha'oser*) becomes forbidden. Specifically: Reuven sleeps with Dvora while she is married to Barak. Reuven has committed adultery, the *issur kal*. The consequence: Barak — who is the *oser*, the one whose existence creates her forbidden status as a married woman — now finds his wife forbidden to him. That is the effect of *ba al issur kal ne'esar ha'oser*. By *kal vachomer*: if Reuven were instead to sleep with Leah, his own wife's sister, which is called *issur chamur*, then Rachel — the one who defines Leah's forbidden status as her sister — should certainly become forbidden as well.

The reasoning here is admittedly strained. The *baraisa* itself acknowledges that we need a verse to correct the *kal vachomer*, and the verse (*otah*) does so: only *her* act — the wife's own act of infidelity — can render her forbidden. Not the act of her sister.

## Beit Shammai Debate

### Rav Yehuda Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בְּבָא עַל חֲמוֹתוֹ שֶׁפָּסַל אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — בְּבָא עַל אֲחוֹת אִשְׁתּוֹ, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: פָּסַל, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: לֹא פָּסַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree with regard to one who has relations with his mother-in-law, that he renders his wife disqualified** from remaining married to him. **With regard to what** case **did they disagree? With regard to one who has relations with his wife's sister, as Beit Shammai say** that **he renders** his wife **disqualified, and Beit Hillel say he does not render** her **disqualified.**

Rav Yehuda now introduces a statement clarifying what Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel actually debate. According to Rav Yehuda, Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree about the case where a man has intercourse with his wife's mother: in that case, all agree that the wife becomes forbidden (*posel*) as a result. The source for this position is the very verse we handled earlier — *ves-hen* — which, even if not interpreted to mandate execution of the wife, establishes that the wife's status is affected when a man sleeps with her mother. The debate of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, according to Rav Yehuda, concerns only the case of *boel achos ishto* — intercourse with the wife's sister. There, Beit Shammai says that the wife does become forbidden (*posel*), while Beit Hillel says she does not (*lo posel*).

### Rabbi Yosi Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בְּבָא עַל אֲחוֹת אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַל אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — בְּבָא עַל חֲמוֹתוֹ, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: פָּסַל, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: לֹא פָּסַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said:** Not so, as **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree with regard to one who has relations with his wife's sister, that he does not render his wife disqualified** from remaining married to him. **With regard to what did they disagree? With regard to one who has relations with his mother-in-law, as Beit Shammai say he renders** his wife **disqualified and Beit Hillel say he does not render** her **disqualified.**

Rabbi Yosi disagrees with this framing entirely. He states that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not debate the case of the wife's sister at all — everyone agrees that sleeping with the sister does not render the wife forbidden. This follows directly from the *drasha* on *otah*: *otah* — *her* sleeping with another man makes her forbidden; the sleeping of her sister does not.

### Mother Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. הָהוּא דַּעֲבַד אִיסּוּרָא בַּחֲמָתֵיהּ. אַתְיֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה, נַגְּדֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי לָאו דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — אַסְרִיתַהּ עֲלָךְ אִיסּוּרָא דְעָלַם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara relates: **A certain** individual **performed a transgression** by having relations **with his mother-in-law. Rav Yehuda had him brought** for judgment and ordered that he **be flogged. He said to him: If** it were **not** for the fact that **Shmuel said the *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, I would render** your wife **forbidden to you permanently.**

What then is the debate of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel? According to Rabbi Yosi, the debate is only in the case of *boel chamoso* — intercourse with the mother-in-law. Beit Shammai holds that this does render the wife forbidden (*posel*); Beit Hillel holds that it does not (*lo posel*). The ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel is stated explicitly: *ein halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — we do not rule like Rabbi Yehuda's position, which extended the prohibition to the mother-in-law even according to Beit Hillel. The law follows Beit Hillel, and thus sleeping with the mother-in-law does not render the wife forbidden. This ruling is illustrated with a practical case: a man who committed the transgression of sleeping with his mother-in-law was brought before Rav Yehuda and given lashes, but Rav Yehuda explicitly told him: had Shmuel not ruled otherwise, I would have declared you permanently forbidden to your wife — but since Shmuel ruled *ein halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda*, you may return to your wife.

## Beit Shammai Debate (continued)

### Asymmetry Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁבַּתְּחִלָּה, הוּא מוּתָּר בְּכׇל הַנָּשִׁים שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם, וְהִיא מוּתֶּרֶת בְּכׇל הָאֲנָשִׁים שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם. קִדְּשָׁהּ — הוּא אֲסָרָהּ, וְהִיא אֲסָרַתּוּ. מְרוּבֶּה אִיסּוּר שֶׁאֲסָרָהּ, מֵאִיסּוּר שֶׁאֲסָרַתְהוּ. שֶׁהוּא אֲסָרָהּ בְּכׇל אֲנָשִׁים שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם, וְהִיא לָא אֲסָרַתְהוּ אֶלָּא בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei explains why Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree with regard to the case of one who has relations with his wife's sister. This is **because at first,** before the marriage, **he is permitted to all the women in the world and she is permitted to all the men in the world.** After **he** has **betrothed** her as his wife, **he renders her forbidden** to all men, **and she renders him forbidden** to her relatives. Consequently, the **prohibition by which** he **renders her forbidden** is **greater than** the **prohibition by which** she **renders him forbidden, as he renders her forbidden to all** the **men in the world and she renders him forbidden** by their betrothal **only to her relatives.**

The *baraisa* cited in this context articulates the logical basis for the *hava amina* that had suggested the wife's sister ought to render the wife forbidden. The argument proceeds by observing a fundamental asymmetry between the husband's and wife's respective prohibitions upon marriage.

Before marriage, he was permitted to any woman in the world and she was permitted to any man in the world (*hu mutar bechol hanashim shebaolam vehi muteret bechol ha-anashim shebaolam*). Upon marriage, he makes her forbidden to all other men, and she makes him forbidden only to her relatives. The asymmetry is therefore striking: his prohibition upon her is total — every other man in the world is now off-limits — while her prohibition upon him is narrow — only her immediate relatives (her mother, her sister, etc.) are now forbidden.

## Sleeping Sister (continued)

### Wife vs. Husband

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה הוּא, שֶׁאֲסָרָהּ בְּכׇל אֲנָשִׁים שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם, שָׁגְגָה בָּאָסוּר לָהּ — אֵינָהּ נֶאֱסֶרֶת בַּמּוּתָּר לָהּ, הִיא, שֶׁלֹּא אֲסָרַתְהוּ אֶלָּא בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ, שָׁגַג בָּאָסוּר לוֹ — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱסַר (לוֹ) בַּמּוּתָּר לוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei's explanation continues. Could this *halakha* of a wife's sister **not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference: And if he prohibited her** through their betrothal **to all men in the world,** and yet she was **unwitting with one forbidden to her,** i.e., she had relations with another man by mistake, **she is not forbidden to** he who is **permitted to her,** her husband; **she, who prohibited him only to her relatives,** if **he was unwitting with one forbidden to him,** her sister, **is it not right that we should not render him forbidden to** her, she who is **permitted to him,** namely his wife?

This asymmetry generates a *kal vachomer*. If we consider the wife's situation: he makes her forbidden to the entire world, and yet if she unintentionally (*shogeg*) sleeps with one of those men, that does not render her additionally forbidden to the one man she is permitted to, namely her husband — since as established, *shogeg* adultery renders her forbidden only by rabbinic decree in view of the underlying biblical prohibition. How much more so should the husband, whose prohibition created by her is far narrower, limited only to her relatives, not find that his act of *shogeg* intercourse with one of those relatives renders his wife forbidden to him?

The *kal vachomer*, properly formulated: the wife, whose prohibition is vastly more extensive and whose status is far more globally redefined by marriage, does not lose her permitted status vis-à-vis her husband when she unintentionally transgresses one of the men she is forbidden to. Certainly then the husband, whose prohibitions are far more limited — only her relatives — should not find that his unintentional intercourse with one of those relatives renders his wife forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶה הַדִּין לְשׁוֹגֵג. לְמֵזִיד מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹתָהּ״, אוֹתָהּ — שְׁכִיבָתָהּ אוֹסַרְתָּהּ, וְאֵין שְׁכִיבַת אֲחוֹתָהּ אוֹסַרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is the** *a fortiori* **inference** and the reason for the *halakha* of **an unwitting** sinner, i.e., that if he had unwitting relations with one of his wife's relations the wife is not thereby rendered forbidden to him. **With regard to** one whose act was **intentional, from where** is the *halakha* derived? **The verse states: "Her,"** meaning that it is **her intercourse** with another man that **renders her forbidden** to her husband, **but the intercourse of her** husband with **her sister does not render her forbidden** to him.

This disposes of the *shogeg* case. But what of *bemezid*? After all, intentional adultery does render the wife forbidden to her husband. Perhaps intentional intercourse with the sister should similarly render the wife forbidden. The *baraisa* then addresses this: *v'zo adim l'shogeg* — the above reasoning is effective for *shogeg*, but *lamezid minayin*? How do we know that even intentional intercourse with the sister does not render the wife forbidden? The answer: *talmud lomar otah* — only her own act of forbidden intercourse renders her forbidden; the sexual act of her sister does not, under any circumstances, render her forbidden.

## Issur Kal

### Rav Chisda Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אִיסּוּר קַל״? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַחְזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ מִשֶּׁנִּשֵּׂאת.
>
> **Translation:** § At the start of the *baraita* the *tanna* stated an *a fortiori* inference that is not entirely clear: If in a place where he has relations subject to a light prohibition, the one causing her to be rendered prohibited is forbidden. The Gemara asks: **What is** this **light prohibition? Rav Ḥisda said:** It is referring to one who **remarries his divorcée after she married** another man.

The Gemara now returns to examine the *kal vachomer* that drove the *hava amina* — the argument that sleeping with the wife's sister (*issur chamur*) should render the wife forbidden, since sleeping with a married woman (*issur kal*) renders her husband forbidden to her. The phrase *issur kal* in this context requires clarification. What precisely counts as an *issur kal*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא עָלֶיהָ הַאי — אַסְרַהּ עֲלֵיהּ דְּהַאי. בָּא עָלֶיהָ אִידַּךְ — אַסְרַהּ עֲלֵיהּ דְּהַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that according to this interpretation the *a fortiori* inference should be understood as follows: If **this one,** the second husband, **has relations with her, he has rendered her forbidden to that one,** the first husband. And if the second man divorced her and then **the other one,** the first husband, **had relations with her, he has** likewise **rendered her forbidden to this one.** This demonstrates that even with regard to a light prohibition the man who renders her forbidden is also forbidden by this intercourse.

Rav Chisda proposes: *machzir grushato misheniset* — the case of a man who remarries his divorced wife after she had married another man. The structure of the case runs as follows. Reuven marries Rachel; he divorces her; she marries Barak. Now Barak divorces Rachel and Reuven remarries her. Reuven has committed the prohibition of *machzir grushato misheniset*. But what has happened to Barak's status vis-à-vis Rachel? Because Rachel remarried Reuven, she is now forbidden to Barak — he cannot take her back. So: Barak's prior marriage to Rachel is what made her forbidden to Reuven in stage two, making Barak the *oser*; and then Reuven's act of remarriage — the *issur kal* — renders the *oser* (Barak) forbidden to the woman. *Ba la-hai asra a-hai, ba la-idach asrah l'hai.* This, says Rav Chisda, is the paradigm of *bemakom she'ba issur kal ne'esar ha'oser*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְמַחְזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ מִשֶּׁנִּשֵּׂאת, שֶׁכֵּן נִטְמָא הַגּוּף, וְאִיסּוּרָהּ בְּרוֹב, וְאִיסּוּרָהּ אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this interpretation: **What** about the fact **that one who remarries his divorcée after she married** another man cannot be considered to have violated a light prohibition, as the prohibition is stringent in several regards: **As the body is defiled** by this intercourse, for the Torah states "after she has been defiled" (Deuteronomy 24:4); **and her prohibition** applies **to the majority** of the Jewish people, not to select groups; **and her prohibition** is **an irrevocable prohibition,** as she is no longer permitted to her first husband after having relations with her second husband. This last stringency is not true of his wife's sister, who is permitted to him after the death of his wife.

The Gemara rejects this as a basis for comparison, on two grounds. First: *nitma'ah haguf* — in that case the woman's own body was directly involved in the act that changed her status; she herself married Reuven. Second: *ve'issur olam* — the prohibition that results is permanent; she will always be forbidden to Barak. By contrast, the prohibition of *achos isha* dissolves if the wife dies. Therefore this is not a genuine *issur kal* — in the relevant respects, it is in fact more severe than the sister prohibition.

### Yevama Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: יְבָמָה. יְבָמָה לְמַאן? אִילֵּימָא לְאַחֵר, וְכִדְרַב הַמְנוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁזִּינְּתָה אֲסוּרָה לִיבָמָהּ —
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara rejects this explanation in favor of the following one that **Reish Lakish said:** The *baraita* is referring to relations with a ***yevama*,** which is called a light prohibition, as the man who has relations with her is forbidden to her. The Gemara clarifies: With regard to this ***yevama*, with whom** did she engage in intercourse? **If we say** that she had relations **with another** man, not her *yavam*, this would mean that the ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Hamnuna. As Rav Hamnuna said: A widow waiting for her *yavam* who engaged in licentious sexual relations** with another man is **forbidden to her *yavam**.** The argument would be as follows: Although the *yavam* renders the *yevama* forbidden to every other man, if she has relations with another she becomes forbidden to him as well.

Reish Lakish proposes the case of a *yevama* — a woman awaiting *yibum*. The *issur kal* would be intercourse with another man, violating the prohibition of *lo tihyeh eishes hameis hachutza l'ish zar*, a simple *lav*. Per the ruling of Rav Hamnuna, a *shomeres yavam* who commits *zenus* becomes forbidden to the brother-in-law (*yavam*), who is the one who made her status as a *yevama* defined. This appears to be a cleaner parallel: a simple *lav* is violated, and the result is that the *oser* — the *yavam* who defined her status — becomes forbidden to her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לִיבָמָה שֶׁכֵּן נִטְמָא הַגּוּף, וְאִיסּוּרָהּ בְּרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** However, this argument can also be challenged: **What** about the fact **that** in the case of **a *yevama*** who engages in this forbidden relationship, **the body is defiled and her prohibition** applies equally **to the majority** of the people. Therefore, one cannot derive the prohibitions of one's wife's relatives from this *halakha*.

The Gemara again objects: *nitma'ah haguf* — it was her own person, her own act, that generated the consequence. And additionally, *issura berov* — the prohibition she now carries extends broadly: she is forbidden to the entire world, not just to one person, as would be the case for the wife whose husband sleeps with her sister. The parallel therefore fails on the same grounds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא יְבָמָה לָאַחִין. עֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר הַאי — אַסְרַהּ עֲלֵיהּ דְּהַאי. בָּא עָלֶיהָ אִידַּךְ — אַסְרַהּ עֲלֵיהּ דְּהַאי. מַאי אִירְיָא שֶׁבָּא עָלֶיהָ שֵׁנִי? אֲפִילּוּ עֲבַד בָּהּ נָמֵי מַאֲמָר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *baraita* must be referring to a case of **a *yevama* to the brothers,** as follows: If **this** brother **performed levirate betrothal [*ma'amar*] with her he has rendered her forbidden to that one,** the other brothers, as she is effectively betrothed to him. If one of the **other** brothers, who had not performed levirate betrothal with her, subsequently **has relations with her, he has rendered her forbidden to this one** who had performed levirate betrothal with her. The Gemara asks: If that is the meaning of the *baraita*, **why specifically** state **that** the **second had relations with her? Even** if **he performed levirate betrothal with her too,** he thereby renders her forbidden to the first brother, which proves that it is not the act of intercourse itself that causes the prohibition.

The Gemara then considers whether the *yevama* case can be reformulated as one involving the *brothers* rather than a stranger — specifically, a case where one brother performed *ma'amar* with the *yevama* and a second brother then violated that by engaging in *yibum*, thereby rendering her forbidden to the first brother. The Gemara notes that it is not the sexual act per se that generates the prohibition; even a *get* or *chalitza* by the second brother would produce the same effect, making the act of intercourse irrelevant as the operative cause. This proposed parallel thus fails as well.

### Sotah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: סוֹטָה. סוֹטָה לְמַאן? אִילֵימָא לַבַּעַל: בָּא עָלֶיהָ בַּעַל — אַסְרַהּ עֲלֵיהּ דְּבוֹעֵל. מַאי אִירְיָא בָּא עָלֶיהָ? אֲפִילּוּ נָתַן לָהּ גֵּט — וַאֲפִילּוּ אָמַר ״אֵין אֲנִי מַשְׁקֶה״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The light prohibition is that of **a *sota*.** The Gemara asks: This ***sota*, to whom** is she forbidden? **If we say** that she is forbidden **to the husband,** the explanation would be as follows: If her **husband has relations with her,** despite the fact that she is forbidden to him after she disobeyed his warning not to seclude herself with a certain man, he **has rendered her forbidden to the fornicator,** as she is barred from marrying him even if her husband divorces her. However, **why** is this true **specifically** in a case in which he **had relations with her? Even** if her husband only **gave her a bill of divorce** and did not have relations with her after her seclusion, **or even** if **he said: I** will not force her **to drink** the waters of a *sota*, she is likewise forbidden to the other man.

Rabbi Yochanan proposes *sotah* as the *issur kal*. The idea requires clarification, because the *sotah* case has two possible readings. One reading: the husband has intercourse with the *sotah*, and this makes her forbidden to the adulterer — *ba al ha-baal asar lah le-boel*. But the Gemara objects: the wife becomes forbidden to the adulterer from the moment she commits adultery, regardless of whether the husband subsequently has intercourse with her; even a *get* from the husband would seal her prohibition from the adulterer. The husband's intercourse is therefore not the operative cause.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא סוֹטָה לַבּוֹעֵל. הַאי אִיסּוּר קַל הוּא? אִיסּוּר חָמוּר הוּא, דְּהַיְינוּ אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *baraita* is referring to **a *sota*** who had relations **with the fornicator,** thereby rendering herself permanently forbidden to her husband, as she was a married woman at the time. Yet this too is puzzling: **Is this a light prohibition? It is a severe prohibition, as this is** the prohibition proscribing **a married woman,** one of the most serious of all prohibitions.

The alternative reading, therefore, must be: the *sotah* as a paradigm for the prohibition of a married woman (*eshet ish*) sleeping with any man other than her husband — the fundamental prohibition of adultery itself. That act renders her forbidden to her husband. This would be the *issur kal*.

### Rava Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ. וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ. וּמַאי קָרֵי לַהּ ״אִיסּוּר קַל״ — שֶׁאֵין הָאוֹסְרָהּ אוֹסְרָהּ כׇּל יָמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that the light prohibition is actually that of **a married woman. And similarly, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael, **he said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that the *baraita* is referring to **a married woman. And** for **what** reason does the *tanna* **call** this **a light prohibition?** The reason is that it differs from and is more lenient than other prohibitions in **that** her husband, **who renders her forbidden, does not render her forbidden for his whole lifetime,** as he can negate the prohibition by giving her a bill of divorce.

But can adultery really be called an *issur kal*? The Gemara itself raises the objection: *issur chamur hu, dehainu eshet ish* — it is a grave prohibition, carrying the death penalty. Rava's response, and simultaneously that of Rabin citing Rabbi Yochanan, is to simply own the comparison directly: yes, the *issur kal* we are discussing is *eshet ish* — adultery. Why does it merit the label *issur kal*? Because the prohibition is not permanent. A married woman could in principle become permitted to any given man — including the adulterer — if she were to receive a *get*. By contrast, the wife's sister is forbidden so long as the wife is alive, with no mechanism for permissibility. *She-eina ossra kol yamav* — she does not always remain forbidden to him. That is the tenuous distinction that allows us to call adultery *issur kal* relative to the prohibition of *achos isha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אַבָּא חָנָן אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ. וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם הַבָּא עַל אִיסּוּר קַל, שֶׁאֵין הָאוֹסְרָהּ אוֹסְרָהּ כׇּל יָמָיו — נֶאֱסַר הָאוֹסֵר, הַבָּא עַל אִיסּוּר חָמוּר, שֶׁהָאוֹסְרָהּ אוֹסְרָהּ כׇּל יָמָיו — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנֶּאֱסַר הָאוֹסְרָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** This is also taught in a *baraita*: **Abba Ḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Elazar:** The *baraita* is referring to **a married woman. And if in** a case **where he has relations with** a married woman, which is **a light prohibition,** due to the fact **that he who renders her forbidden does not render her forbidden for his whole lifetime,** and yet **the one who renders her forbidden is forbidden,** as the husband of a woman who committed adultery is permanently barred from having relations with her, then the following conclusion is correct: **One who has relations subject to a severe transgression,** e.g., a wife's sister, which is severe because **the one who renders** his wife **forbidden renders her forbidden for her whole lifetime,** since as long as his wife remains alive he is forbidden to her sister, **is it not right that the one who renders her forbidden should be forbidden?**

The *baraisa* cited by Abba Chanan in the name of Abba Elazar confirms this reading. *U-mah bimkom ha-baal issur kal she-eina ossra kol yamav ne'esar ha-oseh* — when a man comes upon a married woman (*issur kal*, since she could become permitted via divorce), the act makes her forbidden to the one who defined her status. *Ha-ba al issur chamur she-ossra kol yamav* — one who sleeps with the wife's sister, the more severe prohibition, applicable for the wife's entire lifetime — should render the one who defined her status (*ha-oseh*) forbidden as well, by a *kal vachomer*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹתָהּ״, אוֹתָהּ — שְׁכִיבָתָהּ אוֹסַרְתָּהּ, וְאֵין שְׁכִיבַת אֲחוֹתָהּ אוֹסַרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse** therefore **states: "Her,"** from which it is derived: It is **her intercourse** that **renders her forbidden, but the intercourse of her sister does not render her forbidden.**

The answer: *talmud lomar otah* — only her own act of forbidden intercourse renders her forbidden. *Otah shchivasah poseltah, ve-ein shchivat achotah poseltah.*

Tosafos notes a residual difficulty. Even granting that adultery is called *issur kal*, the earlier objection remains: in the adultery case, the woman's own body was the locus of the violation (*nitma'ah haguf*). That objection still applies and was never fully answered. Tosafos remarks with understated reserve — *aval kzat kashya* — that it remains somewhat difficult. The honest assessment is that the *hava amina* that sleeping with the sister should render the wife forbidden is not a particularly strong one; the *otah* verse is still needed, but the logical underpinnings of the argument being refuted are acknowledged to be strained.

## Rabbi Yosi Principle

### Tanna Kamma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״מֵתָה אִשְׁתְּךָ״, וְנָשָׂא אֲחוֹתָהּ מֵאָבִיהָ. מֵתָה, וְנָשָׂא אֲחוֹתָהּ מֵאִמָּהּ. מֵתָה, וְנָשָׂא אֲחוֹתָהּ מֵאָבִיהָ. מֵתָה, וְנָשָׂא אֲחוֹתָהּ מֵאִמָּהּ, וְנִמְצְאוּ כּוּלָּן קַיָּימוֹת — מוּתָּר בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה וּבַשְּׁלִישִׁית וּבַחֲמִישִׁית, וּפוֹטְרוֹת צָרוֹתֵיהֶן. וְאָסוּר בַּשְּׁנִיָּה וּבָרְבִיעִית, וְאֵין בִּיאַת אַחַת מֵהֶן פּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁפּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים — פּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין פּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים — אֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Witnesses said to a husband: Your wife is dead, and he married her paternal sister, and witnesses subsequently told him that his second wife was dead and he married her maternal sister; afterward witnesses said that this one too was dead and he married her paternal sister; finally they told him that she was dead and he married the last woman's maternal sister, and then they were all discovered to be alive. In this case he is permitted to his first wife, and to the third and to the fifth. Since these women are not sisters, his betrothal to them is effective. Consequently, if he died and one of them entered into levirate marriage, they exempt their rival wives. But he is forbidden to the second and fourth wife, each of whom is the sister of his original wife. Therefore, if he passed away and the *yavam* had relations with one of them, his relations with any one of them does not exempt her rival wife, as she was forbidden to his brother, which means there was no mitzva of levirate marriage here at all. Rabbi Yosei says: Whoever disqualifies others also disqualifies himself, and whoever does not disqualify others does not disqualify himself either.

The *mishnah* contains the following enigmatic ruling attributed to Rabbi Yosi: *kol sheposel al yedei acherim posel al yedei atzmo* — whoever invalidates others also invalidates himself; *v'chol she'eino posel al yedei acherim eino posel al yedei atzmo* — and whoever does not invalidate others does not invalidate himself. The Gemara asks: *mai ka'amar Rabbi Yosi* — what exactly is Rabbi Yosi saying?

The Gemara first considers whether Rabbi Yosi is responding to the case described by the *Tanna Kamma*, which the Gemara (following Tosafos) reads as the background scenario for this debate. That case is: both Reuven's wife Rachel and his brother-in-law Barak travel to a distant country (*medinat hayam*). Word arrives that both died. Reuven, hearing that Rachel is dead and Barak is dead, marries Leah (Barak's wife). It then turns out that both Rachel and Barak are alive.

### Lack of Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲזוּל אִשְׁתּוֹ וְגִיסוֹ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, דְּאַהֲנִי הָנֵי נִשּׂוּאִים דְּקָמִיתַּסְרָא אֵשֶׁת גִּיסוֹ אַגִּיסוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי: אֵשֶׁת גִּיסוֹ אֲסִירָא, אִשְׁתּוֹ שַׁרְיָא,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the case of a man who married his wife's sister after he was informed that his wife was dead, the Gemara comments: And even if his wife and his brother-in-law both went overseas and he was told that they had died, the *halakha* is that this marriage he performed is effective only to the extent that his brother-in-law's wife is forbidden to his brother-in-law. The reason for this prohibition is that he performed a marriage ceremony with a married woman by mistake, and one who erroneously weds a married woman has thereby rendered her forbidden to her husband. The Gemara adds: Even so, it is only his brother-in-law's wife who is forbidden to her husband, whereas his own wife remains permitted to him.

What is Leah's status? She believed her husband Barak was dead, remarried Reuven, and now it turns out Barak is alive. She suffers all the negative consequences: *asura mizeh umizeh*, forbidden to both men and required to leave. What is Rachel's status? Rachel's husband Reuven married her sister Leah — as we have established, this does not constitute a genuine marriage, Leah requires no *get*, and Reuven may return to Rachel.

The striking asymmetry: Leah, the one who triggered the question about whether she was "really" married to Reuven, bears all the consequences of that quasi-marriage. Yet Reuven, who was on the other side of that same quasi-marriage, bears none of them and may freely return to Rachel. The *Tanna Kamma* accepts this asymmetry. Rabbi Yosi apparently does not.

### Consistency Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַמֵּי אַרֵישָׁא: נִיסֵּת עַל פִּי בֵּית דִּין — תֵּצֵא, וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן. עַל פִּי עֵדִים — תֵּצֵא, וְחַיֶּיבֶת בְּקׇרְבָּן. יִפָּה כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל בֵּית דִּין, שֶׁפֹּטְרָהּ מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן. וְקָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא: לָא שְׁנָא עַל פִּי עֵדִים, דְּאֵשֶׁת גִּיסוֹ שַׁרְיָא. וְלָא שְׁנָא עַל פִּי בֵּית דִּין, דְּאֵשֶׁת גִּיסוֹ אֲסִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said:** Rabbi Yosei's statement does not refer to this *halakha*, but **to the first** mishna of the chapter, which taught that if a woman whose husband went overseas was informed that he was dead and she **married by** permission of **the court she must leave** her new husband **and is exempt from** bringing **an offering.** If she married **based on** the testimony of **witnesses,** without the authorization of the court, **she must leave and is liable to** bring **an offering.** In this regard, the **power of the court is enhanced, as she is exempt from an offering. And** accordingly, if his wife and brother-in-law went overseas and witnesses came and testified that they were both dead, **the first *tanna* says** that it **is no different** whether the wife of his brother-in-law married him **based on** the testimony of **witnesses** alone, in **which** case the **wife of his brother-in-law is permitted** to her husband, as she is considered as having acted under duress, having heard testimony that her husband was dead, **and** it **is no different** if she married him **by** permission of **the court, as** although the **wife of his brother-in-law is prohibited,** his own wife remains permitted to him.

The Gemara explores how Rabbi Yosi would formulate his consistency principle. One possibility is that he argues *le-kula*: since Reuven is permitted to return to Rachel, showing that his marriage to Leah was not genuine, then by the same logic Leah should be permitted to return to Barak. If this is Rabbi Yosi's intent, then the formulation *kol she'eino posel al yedei acherim eino posel al yedei atzmo* — if you don't invalidate others, you don't invalidate yourself — fits this reading, starting with the *kula* and deriving leniency in both directions.

The alternative reading is that Rabbi Yosi argues *le-chumra*: just as Leah is forbidden to Barak, so Rachel should be forbidden to Reuven. This fits the other half of the formulation: *kol sheposel al yedei acherim posel al yedei atzmo* — whoever invalidates others also invalidates himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: עַל פִּי בֵּית דִּין, דְּפוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים — פּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ. עַל פִּי עֵדִים, דְּאֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים — אֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ami continues his explanation. **And Rabbi Yosei says to** the first *tanna*: One who married **by** permission of **the court, who disqualifies others,** he also **disqualified himself** to his own wife; however, in the case of one who married **based on** the testimony of **witnesses** who **does not disqualify others,** I agree **that he does not disqualify himself,** and his wife is permitted to him.

To properly situate Rabbi Yosi's position, the Gemara introduces the distinction between cases involving *eid echad* (one witness) and *shenei eidim* (two witnesses). The rule established earlier in the tractate is that all the negative consequences of a mistaken remarriage — the requirement to leave both men, the loss of the *ketubah*, etc. — apply only when the remarriage was based on a single witness (*al pi eid echad*), because only then does the Rabbinic leniency (*mishum iguna hikilu bah rabanan*) apply and the woman is expected to act on insufficient testimony. When two witnesses testified to the husband's death, the remarriage was fully permissible, and if the husband returns alive, she may return to him with no negative consequences.

Armed with this distinction, the *Tanna Kamma*'s position becomes: it does not matter whether the remarriage was based on one witness (such that Leah cannot return to Barak) or two witnesses (such that she can). Either way, Reuven may return to Rachel. Rabbi Yosi's response, as now understood, is: you cannot split the ruling this way. When it was *al pi beis din* (i.e., a single witness — *eid echad* — which is what relying on *beis din*'s authorization means in this context), and Leah is therefore forbidden to Barak, Rachel is also forbidden to Reuven — *poseil al yedei acherim, posel al yedei atzmo*. When it was *al pi shenei eidim*, such that Leah is not forbidden to Barak, Rachel is also not forbidden to Reuven — *eino posel al yedei acherim, eino posel al yedei atzmo*. You must be consistent.

A related question arises from this discussion: what standard of testimony does Reuven himself need in order to remarry in the first place — to marry Leah — upon hearing that Rachel has died? May he rely on a single witness? Tosafos on 94b (*af al gav*) addresses this directly, concluding that for Reuven to marry the sister specifically, two witnesses are required. The rationale for the general leniency of *eid echad* is *mishum iguna* — to spare a woman the suffering of remaining an *agunah*. A man does not face that predicament; he can always marry another woman. Therefore the same leniency does not apply, and he would need two witnesses to the wife's death before marrying her sister.

### Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם אַסֵּיפָא (הָא — דִּנְסֵיב אֵשֶׁת גִּיסוֹ, וְהָא — דִּנְסֵיב אֲרוּסַת גִּיסוֹ). הָא — דְּאָזְלִי אֲרוּסָתוֹ וְגִיסוֹ, הָא — דְּאָזְלִי אִשְׁתּוֹ וְגִיסוֹ, וְקָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא: לָא שְׁנָא אִשְׁתּוֹ וְגִיסוֹ, וְלָא שְׁנָא אֲרוּסָתוֹ וְגִיסוֹ — אֵשֶׁת גִּיסוֹ אֲסִירָא, וְאִשְׁתּוֹ שַׁרְיָא,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa said: Actually,** Rabbi Yosei is referring **to the latter clause** of the mishna, and the explanation is as follows: **This** is referring to one **who married his brother-in-law's wife, and that** case is referring to one **who married his brother-in-law's betrothed.** Alternatively, **this** involves a situation **where his betrothed and his brother-in-law went** overseas, whereas **that** concerns a situation **when his wife and his brother-in-law went** abroad. **And the first *tanna* said:** It **is no different** whether the ones who left were **his wife and his brother-in-law, and** it **is no different** whether they were **his betrothed and his brother-in-law.** Either way **his brother-in-law's wife is forbidden** to her husband **and his** own **wife** or betrothed **is permitted** to him.

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha offers an alternative framing of the scenario. Rather than the one-witness versus two-witness distinction, he focuses on whether Rachel was fully married to Reuven (*nisuin*) or only betrothed (*eirusin*/*arusin*) at the time she traveled away with Barak.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אִשְׁתּוֹ וְגִיסוֹ, דְּלֵיכָּא לְמֵימַר תְּנָאָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ בְּנִשּׂוּאִין, דְּאֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי אַחֵר — אֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ. אֲרוּסָתוֹ וְגִיסוֹ, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר תְּנָאָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ בְּקִידּוּשִׁין, וּפוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים — אַף פּוֹסֵל עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa continues his interpretation. **And Rabbi Yosei said to** the first *tanna*: If **his wife and his brother-in-law** left, in **which** case **it cannot be said** that **he had a condition with regard to his marriage** to his wife, i.e., that the finalization of the marriage was pending on the fulfillment of some condition, everyone would realize that his marriage to her sister was a mistake and she is therefore permitted to his brother-in-law. Consequently, **as he does not disqualify another, he does not disqualify himself** either. If, however, it was **his betrothed and his brother-in-law** who left, **when it can be** mistakenly **said** that **he had a condition with regard to his betrothal** and the condition was unfulfilled, his brother-in-law's wife is therefore prohibited from returning to his brother-in-law. **And** therefore, as **he disqualifies others he also disqualifies himself,** and his betrothed is forbidden to him.

This distinction matters for the following reason. The reason Reuven is generally permitted to return to Rachel — and the reason Leah is not viewed as his genuine wife — is that everyone understands that Leah's sister was alive and that Reuven was married to her. With *nisuin*, no one entertains the possibility that the marriage to Rachel somehow never materialized. With *eirusin* only, however, people might speculate that perhaps a condition (*tenai*) in the betrothal was never fulfilled, or that the *nisuin* simply never occurred, and therefore Reuven's marriage to Leah might be seen as more plausible. That perception matters enormously for how the community understands whether this was a genuine marriage or a complete mistake.

### Rav Yosef Reading

Rav Yosef's reading of Rabbi Yosi takes this even further, introducing a scenario where Rabbi Yosi is actually *lenient* — going even further than the *Tanna Kamma* in one respect. The *Tanna Kamma* says that in the case of *ishto ve-giso* (Rachel and Barak both went away), *eshet giso asura ve-ishto sharya* — Leah is forbidden to Barak and Rachel is permitted to Reuven. Rav Yosef's reading of Rabbi Yosi is: when Rachel was fully married to Reuven (*nisuin*), everyone in hindsight knows that it was simply impossible for him to have been genuinely married to Leah. Since everyone will understand this retroactively, the marriage to Leah will not even carry the appearance of a marriage — it will be seen universally as a mistake, indeed as *shogeg* if at all, but certainly not a genuine *kinyan*. In that case, not only can Reuven return to Rachel — Leah can return to Barak as well. There is no semblance of a real marriage to Reuven, and therefore Leah's status is not compromised. Rabbi Yosi, in the case of *nisuin*, is *meikel* in both directions.

In the case of *eirusin*, however — where Rachel was only betrothed to Reuven — people might genuinely wonder whether the first marriage ever fully took effect. In that case, the marriage to Leah does carry some semblance of a real marriage. Leah is forbidden to Barak (*posel al yedei acherim*), and therefore Rachel is also forbidden to Reuven (*posel al yedei atzmo*). Rabbi Yosi is *machmir* in both directions.

The elegance of Rav Yosef's reading is that Rabbi Yosi is entirely consistent: never splitting the ruling, going *le-kula* in both directions when *nisuin* makes the mistake transparent, and *le-chumra* in both directions when *eirusin* creates ambiguity.

### Final Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וּמִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. Rav Yosef strongly objects to this: And did Shmuel** actually **say this?**

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* — the *halakha* follows Rabbi Yosi, with his principle of consistency between the treatment of the two couples. The daf concludes here with Rav Yosef raising a challenge to this ruling — *metiv Rav Yosef, u-mi amar Shmuel hachi* — which will be taken up in the next session.
