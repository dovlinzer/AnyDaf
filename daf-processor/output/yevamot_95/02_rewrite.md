# Yevamot 95 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wife Dead Report

### Case Setup

The daf begins toward the bottom of 94b. The case under discussion is not the more familiar scenario of a woman whose husband is reported dead and who then remarries — that case occupied much of the preceding material — but rather its mirror image: a husband whose wife is reported dead, who then marries her sister, and whose wife subsequently returns alive. The Gemara had already addressed certain preliminary complications arising from this scenario, particularly whether the marriage to the sister creates ongoing obligations. Specifically, it considered whether, according to Rabbi Akiva, the sister would require a *get*, which would in turn render the original wife forbidden to him as the sister of his divorcée (*achos grushaso*). That possibility was ultimately rejected, and even according to Rabbi Akiva, it is assumed that the husband may return to his first wife.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

At this point, Rav Ashi poses a question to Rav Kahana, beginning with the word *Akiva* approximately twelve lines from the bottom of 94b: if the *mishnah* can even be in accordance with Rabbi Akiva — since the man is not required to give a *get* to the sister — then why does the *mishnah* present only the case of the sister as the scenario in which the report of the wife's death would be relevant to his marrying someone new?

For most forbidden relationships (*arayot*), the prohibition does not dissolve upon the death of the relevant party. The sister, however, is the paradigmatic exception: the prohibition of *achos isha* is tied explicitly to the wife's lifetime, as the verse states that it is forbidden to take a woman alongside her sister *in her lifetime* (*bechayeha*). That is presumably why the sister is the only such case addressed in the *mishnah* — she is the only *ervah* who could become permitted once the wife dies. But one might ask: what about the mother-in-law (*chamuto*)? The general assumption is that the prohibition of marrying a woman and her mother applies even after the wife has died. Yet according to Rabbi Akiva, perhaps it does not. If so, then the *mishnah* should also have presented the case where the wife is reported dead and the husband marries the mother-in-law.

### Woman & Mother

To understand why this is relevant, the Gemara cites a *baraisa* on the verse *ki yikach ish es isha ve'imah* — "if a man takes a woman and her mother, you shall burn him and them (*oso ves-hen*)." The question arises: what did the wife do that she too should be executed? The debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva turns on the reading of *ves-hen*. Rabbi Yishmael reads it as referring to only one of them — the mother — *oso ves achas meihen*. Rabbi Akiva reads it as both — *oso ves shteihen* — meaning both the mother and the grandmother, or some other combination involving multiple generations, not the original wife.

### Abaye vs. Rava

Abaye and Rava disagree about how to interpret this tannaitic dispute. According to Abaye, Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva are not debating any practical *halakha* at all; they are debating only how to read the verse (*mashma'us dorshin ika beina'ihu*). Rabbi Yishmael understands the verse as describing one configuration of the prohibited relationship; Rabbi Akiva understands it as describing two distinct configurations. No one debates the underlying *din*. This reading, as Abaye acknowledges, is fine (*bishlama le-Abaye*).

According to Rava, however, there is a genuine halakhic dispute — namely, whether the prohibition of the mother-in-law persists after the wife's death (*chamosto le'achar misah ika beina'ihu*). On Rava's reading, Rabbi Akiva's phrase *oso ves shteihen* is interpreted to mean that the death penalty applies only when both women — the wife and the mother — are still alive. If the wife had already died, the man who then marries the mother would not be executed.

This reading creates the logical opening for Rav Ashi's question: if the *mishnah* can accord with Rabbi Akiva, and if Rabbi Akiva holds that the mother-in-law prohibition dissolves after the wife's death, then the *mishnah* should include the case of the mother-in-law as a second scenario in which hearing of the wife's death opens up a new permitted marriage. The Gemara's response, formulated by Abaye, is that even if Rabbi Akiva exempts from the *sreifah* (burning), one cannot conclude that he removes the prohibition entirely. As Tosafos makes explicit: the phrase *me'issura mi mietei rachmana* — "did the Torah exempt it from prohibition?" — refers not to some minor rabbinic *issur* but to the full weight of a negative commandment and *kares*. The man simply would not be executed; the *issur* remains. Therefore, it would not be relevant to include this scenario in the *mishnah*.

## Sleeping Sister

### Hava Amina

Returning to the case at hand — Reuven is married to Rachel, she travels to *medinat hayam*, word arrives that she has died, and Reuven marries her sister Leah — the Gemara now raises the following question: granted that we do not treat this as a genuine marriage, and Leah requires no *get*, should we not at least say that the very act of Reuven sleeping with Leah ought to render Rachel forbidden to him?

The intuition behind this suggestion is a kind of symmetry with the laws of adultery: if a woman sleeps with another man, that act renders her forbidden to her husband, even if she acted entirely unknowingly (*shogeg*). The Rabbis extended the biblical prohibition to cases of *shogeg* as a rabbinic decree (*gazru bah rabanan*). Should the same not apply here in reverse?

### No Prohibition

The Gemara rejects the comparison — *lo damya*. When a wife commits adultery, even inadvertently, the Rabbis decreed she becomes forbidden because the underlying biblical prohibition is severe: intentional adultery (*bemezid*) renders her forbidden *mid'Oraisa*. The rabbinic extension to *shogeg* is therefore grounded in a pre-existing biblical framework. But in the case of *achos isha*: sleeping with the wife's sister, even intentionally (*bemezid*), does not render the wife *Rachel* forbidden to Reuven under any biblical provision. There is no *issur d'Oraisa* establishing that intercourse with the sister causes the wife to become forbidden. Consequently, there is no basis for a rabbinic decree extending such a consequence to cases of *shogeg* either. (*Bishogeg lo gazru bah rabanan.*)

### Otah Drasha

The Gemara then cites a *baraisa* that grounds this principle in a textual source. From the verse in the *parsha* of *sotah* — *ve'shachav ish osah shichvas zera* — the Rabbis derive: *otah* — specifically *her* act of sleeping with another man renders her forbidden (*shichvasah osartah*), but the act of her sister sleeping with her husband does not render her forbidden (*ve'ein shichvas achosah osartah*).

But why did the Torah need to state this? Surely this is self-evident. The *baraisa* therefore presents the logical argument that might have led us astray — the *hava amina* requiring refutation.

### Kal Vachomer

The *hava amina* is a *kal vachomer*. In a case where a man has intercourse with a woman who is an *issur kal* — adultery is here called a "light prohibition" — the one who defines her status (*ha'oser*) becomes forbidden. Specifically: Reuven sleeps with Dvora while she is married to Barak. Reuven has committed adultery, the *issur kal*. The consequence: Barak — who is the *oser*, the one whose existence creates her forbidden status as a married woman — now finds his wife forbidden to him. That is the effect of *ba al issur kal ne'esar ha'oser*. By *kal vachomer*: if Reuven were instead to sleep with Leah, his own wife's sister, which is called *issur chamur*, then Rachel — the one who defines Leah's forbidden status as her sister — should certainly become forbidden as well.

The reasoning here is admittedly strained. The *baraisa* itself acknowledges that we need a verse to correct the *kal vachomer*, and the verse (*otah*) does so: only *her* act — the wife's own act of infidelity — can render her forbidden. Not the act of her sister.

## Beit Shammai Debate

### Rav Yehuda Opinion

Rav Yehuda now introduces a statement clarifying what Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel actually debate. According to Rav Yehuda, Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree about the case where a man has intercourse with his wife's mother: in that case, all agree that the wife becomes forbidden (*posel*) as a result. The source for this position is the very verse we handled earlier — *ves-hen* — which, even if not interpreted to mandate execution of the wife, establishes that the wife's status is affected when a man sleeps with her mother. The debate of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, according to Rav Yehuda, concerns only the case of *boel achos ishto* — intercourse with the wife's sister. There, Beit Shammai says that the wife does become forbidden (*posel*), while Beit Hillel says she does not (*lo posel*).

### Rabbi Yosi Challenge

Rabbi Yosi disagrees with this framing entirely. He states that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not debate the case of the wife's sister at all — everyone agrees that sleeping with the sister does not render the wife forbidden. This follows directly from the *drasha* on *otah*: *otah* — *her* sleeping with another man makes her forbidden; the sleeping of her sister does not.

### Mother Debate

What then is the debate of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel? According to Rabbi Yosi, the debate is only in the case of *boel chamoso* — intercourse with the mother-in-law. Beit Shammai holds that this does render the wife forbidden (*posel*); Beit Hillel holds that it does not (*lo posel*). The ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel is stated explicitly: *ein halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — we do not rule like Rabbi Yehuda's position, which extended the prohibition to the mother-in-law even according to Beit Hillel. The law follows Beit Hillel, and thus sleeping with the mother-in-law does not render the wife forbidden. This ruling is illustrated with a practical case: a man who committed the transgression of sleeping with his mother-in-law was brought before Rav Yehuda and given lashes, but Rav Yehuda explicitly told him: had Shmuel not ruled otherwise, I would have declared you permanently forbidden to your wife — but since Shmuel ruled *ein halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda*, you may return to your wife.

## Beit Shammai Debate (II)

### Asymmetry Logic

The *baraisa* cited in this context articulates the logical basis for the *hava amina* that had suggested the wife's sister ought to render the wife forbidden. The argument proceeds by observing a fundamental asymmetry between the husband's and wife's respective prohibitions upon marriage.

Before marriage, he was permitted to any woman in the world and she was permitted to any man in the world (*hu mutar bechol hanashim shebaolam vehi muteret bechol ha-anashim shebaolam*). Upon marriage, he makes her forbidden to all other men, and she makes him forbidden only to her relatives. The asymmetry is therefore striking: his prohibition upon her is total — every other man in the world is now off-limits — while her prohibition upon him is narrow — only her immediate relatives (her mother, her sister, etc.) are now forbidden.

## Sleeping Sister (II)

### Wife vs. Husband

This asymmetry generates a *kal vachomer*. If we consider the wife's situation: he makes her forbidden to the entire world, and yet if she unintentionally (*shogeg*) sleeps with one of those men, that does not render her additionally forbidden to the one man she is permitted to, namely her husband — since as established, *shogeg* adultery renders her forbidden only by rabbinic decree in view of the underlying biblical prohibition. How much more so should the husband, whose prohibition created by her is far narrower, limited only to her relatives, not find that his act of *shogeg* intercourse with one of those relatives renders his wife forbidden to him?

The *kal vachomer*, properly formulated: the wife, whose prohibition is vastly more extensive and whose status is far more globally redefined by marriage, does not lose her permitted status vis-à-vis her husband when she unintentionally transgresses one of the men she is forbidden to. Certainly then the husband, whose prohibitions are far more limited — only her relatives — should not find that his unintentional intercourse with one of those relatives renders his wife forbidden.

This disposes of the *shogeg* case. But what of *bemezid*? After all, intentional adultery does render the wife forbidden to her husband. Perhaps intentional intercourse with the sister should similarly render the wife forbidden. The *baraisa* then addresses this: *v'zo adim l'shogeg* — the above reasoning is effective for *shogeg*, but *lamezid minayin*? How do we know that even intentional intercourse with the sister does not render the wife forbidden? The answer: *talmud lomar otah* — the verse says *otah*. Her own sexual act renders her forbidden; the sexual act of her sister does not, under any circumstances, render her forbidden.

## Issur Kal

### Rav Chisda Case

The Gemara now returns to examine the *kal vachomer* that drove the *hava amina* — the argument that sleeping with the wife's sister (*issur chamur*) should render the wife forbidden, since sleeping with a married woman (*issur kal*) renders her husband forbidden to her. The phrase *issur kal* in this context requires clarification. What precisely counts as an *issur kal*?

Rav Chisda proposes: *machzir grushato misheniset* — the case of a man who remarries his divorced wife after she had married another man. The structure of the case runs as follows. Reuven marries Rachel; he divorces her; she marries Barak. Now Barak divorces Rachel and Reuven remarries her. Reuven has committed the prohibition of *machzir grushato misheniset*. But what has happened to Barak's status vis-à-vis Rachel? Because Rachel remarried Reuven, she is now forbidden to Barak — he cannot take her back. So: Barak's prior marriage to Rachel is what made her forbidden to Reuven in stage two, making Barak the *oser*; and then Reuven's act of remarriage — the *issur kal* — renders the *oser* (Barak) forbidden to the woman. *Ba la-hai asra a-hai, ba la-idach asrah l'hai.* This, says Rav Chisda, is the paradigm of *bemakom she'ba issur kal ne'esar ha'oser*.

The Gemara rejects this as a basis for comparison, on two grounds. First: *nitma'ah haguf* — in that case the woman's own body was directly involved in the act that changed her status; she herself married Reuven. Second: *ve'issur olam* — the prohibition that results is permanent; she will always be forbidden to Barak. By contrast, the prohibition of *achos isha* dissolves if the wife dies. Therefore this is not a genuine *issur kal* — in the relevant respects, it is in fact more severe than the sister prohibition.

### Yevama Case

Reish Lakish proposes the case of a *yevama* — a woman awaiting *yibum*. The *issur kal* would be intercourse with another man, violating the prohibition of *lo tihyeh eishes hameis hachutza l'ish zar*, a simple *lav*. Per the ruling of Rav Hamnuna, a *shomeres yavam* who commits *zenus* becomes forbidden to the brother-in-law (*yavam*), who is the one who made her status as a *yevama* defined. This appears to be a cleaner parallel: a simple *lav* is violated, and the result is that the *oser* — the *yavam* who defined her status — becomes forbidden to her.

The Gemara again objects: *nitma'ah haguf* — it was her own person, her own act, that generated the consequence. And additionally, *issura berov* — the prohibition she now carries extends broadly: she is forbidden to the entire world, not just to one person, as would be the case for the wife whose husband sleeps with her sister. The parallel therefore fails on the same grounds.

The Gemara then considers whether the *yevama* case can be reformulated as one involving the *brothers* rather than a stranger — specifically, a case where one brother performed *ma'amar* with the *yevama* and a second brother then violated that by engaging in *yibum*, thereby rendering her forbidden to the first brother. The Gemara notes that it is not the sexual act per se that generates the prohibition; even a *get* or *chalitza* by the second brother would produce the same effect, making the act of intercourse irrelevant as the operative cause. This proposed parallel thus fails as well.

### Sotah Case

Rabbi Yochanan proposes *sotah* as the *issur kal*. The idea requires clarification, because the *sotah* case has two possible readings. One reading: the husband has intercourse with the *sotah*, and this makes her forbidden to the adulterer — *ba al ha-baal asar lah le-boel*. But the Gemara objects: the wife becomes forbidden to the adulterer from the moment she commits adultery, regardless of whether the husband subsequently has intercourse with her; even a *get* from the husband would seal her prohibition from the adulterer. The husband's intercourse is therefore not the operative cause.

The alternative reading, therefore, must be: the *sotah* as a paradigm for the prohibition of a married woman (*eshet ish*) sleeping with any man other than her husband — the fundamental prohibition of adultery itself. That act renders her forbidden to her husband. This would be the *issur kal*.

### Rava Conclusion

But can adultery really be called an *issur kal*? The Gemara itself raises the objection: *issur chamur hu, dehainu eshet ish* — it is a grave prohibition, carrying the death penalty. Rava's response, and simultaneously that of Ravin citing Rabbi Yochanan, is to simply own the comparison directly: yes, the *issur kal* we are discussing is *eshet ish* — adultery. Why does it merit the label *issur kal*? Because the prohibition is not permanent. A married woman could in principle become permitted to any given man — including the adulterer — if she were to receive a *get*. By contrast, the wife's sister is forbidden so long as the wife is alive, with no mechanism for permissibility. *She-eina ossra kol yamav* — she does not always remain forbidden to him. That is the tenuous distinction that allows us to call adultery *issur kal* relative to the prohibition of *achos isha*.

The *baraisa* cited by Abba Chanan in the name of Abba Elazar confirms this reading. *U-mah bimkom ha-baal issur kal she-eina ossra kol yamav ne'esar ha-oseh* — when a man comes upon a married woman (*issur kal*, since she could become permitted via divorce), the act makes her forbidden to the one who defined her status. *Ha-ba al issur chamur she-ossra kol yamav* — one who sleeps with the wife's sister, the more severe prohibition, applicable for the wife's entire lifetime — should render the one who defined her status (*ha-oseh*) forbidden as well, by a *kal vachomer*. The answer: *talmud lomar otah* — only her own act of forbidden intercourse renders her forbidden. *Otah shchivasah poseltah, ve-ein shchivat achotah poseltah.*

Tosafos notes a residual difficulty. Even granting that adultery is called *issur kal*, the earlier objection remains: in the adultery case, the woman's own body was the locus of the violation (*nitma'ah haguf*). That objection still applies and was never fully answered. Tosafos remarks with understated reserve — *aval kzat kashya* — that it remains somewhat difficult. The honest assessment is that the *hava amina* that sleeping with the sister should render the wife forbidden is not a particularly strong one; the *otah* verse is still needed, but the logical underpinnings of the argument being refuted are acknowledged to be strained.

## Rabbi Yosi Principle

### Tanna Kamma

The *mishnah* contains the following enigmatic ruling attributed to Rabbi Yosi: *kol sheposel al yedei acherim posel al yedei atzmo* — whoever invalidates others also invalidates himself; *v'chol she'eino posel al yedei acherim eino posel al yedei atzmo* — and whoever does not invalidate others does not invalidate himself. The Gemara asks: *mai ka'amar Rabbi Yosi* — what exactly is Rabbi Yosi saying?

The Gemara first considers whether Rabbi Yosi is responding to the case described by the *Tanna Kamma*, which the Gemara (following Tosafos) reads as the background scenario for this debate. That case is: both Reuven's wife Rachel and his brother-in-law Barak travel to a distant country (*medinat hayam*). Word arrives that both died. Reuven, hearing that Rachel is dead and Barak is dead, marries Leah (Barak's wife). It then turns out that both Rachel and Barak are alive.

### Lack of Parallel

What is Leah's status? She believed her husband Barak was dead, remarried Reuven, and now it turns out Barak is alive. She suffers all the negative consequences: *asura mizeh umizeh*, forbidden to both men and required to leave. What is Rachel's status? Rachel's husband Reuven married her sister Leah — as we have established, this does not constitute a genuine marriage, Leah requires no *get*, and Reuven may return to Rachel.

The striking asymmetry: Leah, the one who triggered the question about whether she was "really" married to Reuven, bears all the consequences of that quasi-marriage. Yet Reuven, who was on the other side of that same quasi-marriage, bears none of them and may freely return to Rachel. The *Tanna Kamma* accepts this asymmetry. Rabbi Yosi apparently does not.

### Consistency Principle

The Gemara explores how Rabbi Yosi would formulate his consistency principle. One possibility is that he argues *le-kula*: since Reuven is permitted to return to Rachel, showing that his marriage to Leah was not genuine, then by the same logic Leah should be permitted to return to Barak. If this is Rabbi Yosi's intent, then the formulation *kol she'eino posel al yedei acherim eino posel al yedei atzmo* — if you don't invalidate others, you don't invalidate yourself — fits this reading, starting with the *kula* and deriving leniency in both directions.

The alternative reading is that Rabbi Yosi argues *le-chumra*: just as Leah is forbidden to Barak, so Rachel should be forbidden to Reuven. This fits the other half of the formulation: *kol sheposel al yedei acherim posel al yedei atzmo* — whoever invalidates others also invalidates himself.

To properly situate Rabbi Yosi's position, the Gemara introduces the distinction between cases involving *eid echad* (one witness) and *shenei eidim* (two witnesses). The rule established earlier in the tractate is that all the negative consequences of a mistaken remarriage — the requirement to leave both men, the loss of the *ketubah*, etc. — apply only when the remarriage was based on a single witness (*al pi eid echad*), because only then does the Rabbinic leniency (*mishum iguna hikilu bah rabanan*) apply and the woman is expected to act on insufficient testimony. When two witnesses testified to the husband's death, the remarriage was fully permissible, and if the husband returns alive, she may return to him with no negative consequences.

Armed with this distinction, the *Tanna Kamma*'s position becomes: it does not matter whether the remarriage was based on one witness (such that Leah cannot return to Barak) or two witnesses (such that she can). Either way, Reuven may return to Rachel. Rabbi Yosi's response, as now understood, is: you cannot split the ruling this way. When it was *al pi beis din* (i.e., a single witness — *eid echad* — which is what relying on *beis din*'s authorization means in this context), and Leah is therefore forbidden to Barak, Rachel is also forbidden to Reuven — *poseil al yedei acherim, posel al yedei atzmo*. When it was *al pi shenei eidim*, such that Leah is not forbidden to Barak, Rachel is also not forbidden to Reuven — *eino posel al yedei acherim, eino posel al yedei atzmo*. You must be consistent.

A related question arises from this discussion: what standard of testimony does Reuven himself need in order to remarry in the first place — to marry Leah — upon hearing that Rachel has died? May he rely on a single witness? Tosafos on 94b (*af al gav*) addresses this directly, concluding that for Reuven to marry the sister specifically, two witnesses are required. The rationale for the general leniency of *eid echad* is *mishum iguna* — to spare a woman the suffering of remaining an *agunah*. A man does not face that predicament; he can always marry another woman. Therefore the same leniency does not apply, and he would need two witnesses to the wife's death before marrying her sister.

### Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha offers an alternative framing of the scenario. Rather than the one-witness versus two-witness distinction, he focuses on whether Rachel was fully married to Reuven (*nisuin*) or only betrothed (*eirusin*/*arusin*) at the time she traveled away with Barak.

This distinction matters for the following reason. The reason Reuven is generally permitted to return to Rachel — and the reason Leah is not viewed as his genuine wife — is that everyone understands that Leah's sister was alive and that Reuven was married to her. With *nisuin*, no one entertains the possibility that the marriage to Rachel somehow never materialized. With *eirusin* only, however, people might speculate that perhaps a condition (*tenai*) in the betrothal was never fulfilled, or that the *nisuin* simply never occurred, and therefore Reuven's marriage to Leah might be seen as more plausible. That perception matters enormously for how the community understands whether this was a genuine marriage or a complete mistake.

### Rav Yosef Reading

Rav Yosef's reading of Rabbi Yosi takes this even further, introducing a scenario where Rabbi Yosi is actually *lenient* — going even further than the *Tanna Kamma* in one respect. The *Tanna Kamma* says that in the case of *ishto ve-giso* (Rachel and Barak both went away), *eshet giso asura ve-ishto sharya* — Leah is forbidden to Barak and Rachel is permitted to Reuven. Rav Yosef's reading of Rabbi Yosi is: when Rachel was fully married to Reuven (*nisuin*), everyone in hindsight knows that it was simply impossible for him to have been genuinely married to Leah. Since everyone will understand this retroactively, the marriage to Leah will not even carry the appearance of a marriage — it will be seen universally as a mistake, indeed as *shogeg* if at all, but certainly not a genuine *kinyan*. In that case, not only can Reuven return to Rachel — Leah can return to Barak as well. There is no semblance of a real marriage to Reuven, and therefore Leah's status is not compromised. Rabbi Yosi, in the case of *nisuin*, is *meikel* in both directions.

In the case of *eirusin*, however — where Rachel was only betrothed to Reuven — people might genuinely wonder whether the first marriage ever fully took effect. In that case, the marriage to Leah does carry some semblance of a real marriage. Leah is forbidden to Barak (*posel al yedei acherim*), and therefore Rachel is also forbidden to Reuven (*posel al yedei atzmo*). Rabbi Yosi is *machmir* in both directions.

The elegance of Rav Yosef's reading is that Rabbi Yosi is entirely consistent: never splitting the ruling, going *le-kula* in both directions when *nisuin* makes the mistake transparent, and *le-chumra* in both directions when *eirusin* creates ambiguity.

### Final Ruling

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* — the *halakha* follows Rabbi Yosi, with his principle of consistency between the treatment of the two couples. The daf concludes here with Rav Yosef raising a challenge to this ruling — *metiv Rav Yosef, u-mi amar Shmuel hachi* — which will be taken up in the next session.
