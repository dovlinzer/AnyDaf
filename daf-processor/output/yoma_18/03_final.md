# Yoma 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Preparation

### Beis Din's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָסְרוּ לוֹ זְקֵנִים מִזִּקְנֵי בֵּית דִּין, וְקוֹרִין לְפָנָיו בְּסֵדֶר הַיּוֹם, וְאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: אִישִׁי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל! קְרָא אַתָּה בְּפִיךְ, שֶׁמָּא שָׁכַחְתָּ אוֹ שֶׁמָּא לֹא לָמַדְתָּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Sages **provided** the High Priest **with Elders** selected **from the Elders of the court, and they** would **read before him the order** of the service **of the day** of Yom Kippur. **And they** would **say to him: My Master, High Priest. Read** the order of the service **with your own mouth,** as **perhaps you forgot** this reading **or perhaps you did not learn** to read.

The *mishnah* of daf 18 brings us to the transition from the *kohen gadol*'s weeklong preparation into the specific protocols of *Erev Yom Kippur* itself. Having concluded the discussions of the regular *avodot* — the *nerot* and *ketores* and the structural details drawn from Middot and Tamid — we now arrive at a more action-oriented section of the tractate, focused on the *kohen gadol* as *Yom Kippur* approaches.

The *mishnah* opens: *masru lo zekeinim mizekinei Beis Din* — elders from the elders of *Beis Din* were assigned to him. The subject of *masru* is left anonymous; the *zekeinim* are the grammatical object, not the subject. What this establishes is that at this stage in the process, the *kohen gadol* came under the formal auspices of *Beis Din*. This is consistent with a broader assumption found throughout the *Gemara* — one that emerged repeatedly in the discussions in Shekalim — that everything that transpired in the *Beis HaMikdash*, even the conduct of the *kohanim* and the leadership of the *kohen gadol* himself, ultimately operated under the authority of *Beis Din*.

### Beis Din Representation

What is worth pausing over is the precise nature of *Beis Din*'s role here. Soloveitchik observes that *Beis Din* does not function solely as an adjudicating body or even merely as the authoritative interpreter of *Torah* law. In many contexts, *Beis Din* acts as the representative of *Klal Yisrael*. The parallel to the *eglah arufah* is instructive: when *veyatzu zekanecha ve'shoftecha* — the elders and judges go forth — they are acting not as a court rendering legal decisions but as the representatives of the people. Similarly here, *Beis Din*'s presence with the *kohen gadol* embodies the fact that the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* is performed on behalf of and for the sake of the entire Jewish people.

At the same time, *Beis Din* in this context may simultaneously represent *Torah* authority — and those are genuinely distinct roles. When the elders oversee the *kohen gadol* before *Yom Kippur*, there is both a representational dimension (they stand for *Klal Yisrael*) and a supervisory one (they stand for *Torah* law and its proper execution). It is worth holding both of these in mind as we proceed.

### Reading the Service

The *mishnah* continues: *vekorin lefanav b'seder hayom* — they would read before him the order of the day's service. The word *korin* here is significant. This appears not to be a how-to manual or step-by-step instructional guide, but a reading from the *Torah* itself — the *pesukim* of *parshas Acharei Mos*. This is a striking detail, because not everything about the *avodas Yom HaKippurim* is made fully explicit in the plain meaning of those *pesukim*; there are procedural elements that require tradition beyond the text.

The *mishnah* then records that they would say to him: *Ishi Kohen Gadol, kra attah be'ficha* — Mr. *kohen gadol*, read the passages yourself, *shemma shachachta*, in case you have forgotten, *o shemma lo lamad'ta*, or perhaps you never learned it at all. That second possibility — that a *kohen gadol* might never have learned the *avodah* — obviously calls for explanation, and the *Gemara* will address it shortly.

## Animal Parade & Desp (II)

### Animal Parade

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶרֶב יוֹם כִּפּוּרִים שַׁחֲרִית מַעֲמִידִין אוֹתוֹ בְּשַׁעַר מִזְרָח, וּמַעֲבִירִין לְפָנָיו פָּרִים וְאֵילִים וּכְבָשִׂים כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא מַכִּיר וְרָגִיל בַּעֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **On Yom Kippur eve** in the **morning,** the Elders **stand him at** the **eastern gate** of the courtyard **and pass before him bulls and rams and sheep so that he will be familiar** with the animals **and** grow **accustomed to the service,** as these were the animals sacrificed on Yom Kippur.

On *Erev Yom HaKippurim Shacharis*, the *mishnah* describes the following procedure: *ma'amidim oto be-Sha'ar HaMizrach* — they would station him at the eastern gate, at *Sha'ar Nicanor*, the entrance to the *Azarah* — *uma'avirin lefanav parim ve-eilim u-kevasim*, and they would parade before him oxen, rams, and sheep. The purpose is stated explicitly: *kedei sheyehei makhir ve-ragil ba'avodah*, so that he should recognize the animals and be fluent in the *avodah*.

### Roleplay Explanation

This raises an obvious question: what is the point of parading these animals before him? Even an ignorant person knows what a cow looks like. The Maharsha explains — and this accords with an intuition one might independently arrive at — that this was not a zoological identification exercise. What they were actually doing was a full roleplay. They would say: here is the ox — now walk toward it, now perform the *shechitah*, now take this and move it here. The point was not intellectual knowledge but physical, embodied familiarity, so that on *Yom Kippur* itself the sequence would flow as second nature rather than requiring conscious recollection at each step. Acting it out with the actual animals makes the choreography intuitive in a way that reading the *pesukim* cannot.

One might wonder why this roleplay was not performed every day of the preparatory week leading up to *Yom Kippur*, which would seem to offer an even better opportunity to ingrain the sequence. That question remains. What the *mishnah* does record is that this particular parade took place on the morning of *Erev Yom Kippur*.

Some modern interpreters have drawn a connection between this scene and *Ma'aseh Bereishit* — Adam in the garden with the animals brought before him — noting that the *avodah* liturgy recited on *Yom Kippur* itself begins precisely with the creation narrative. The textual basis for this parallel is admittedly thin, since there is no mention of naming the animals here, but the structural resonance is worth noting.

## Seminal Emission Con (II)

### Food Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים לֹא הָיוּ מוֹנְעִין מִמֶּנּוּ מַאֲכָל וּמִשְׁתֶּה, עֶרֶב יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה לֹא הָיוּ מַנִּיחִין אוֹתוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל הַרְבֵּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַמַּאֲכָל מֵבִיא אֶת הַשֵּׁינָה.
>
> **Translation:** Throughout **all the seven days** that the High Priest was in the *Parhedrin* chamber, **they would not withhold from him** any **food or drink** that he desired. However, **on Yom Kippur eve at nightfall, they would not allow him to eat a great deal because food induces sleep** and they did not allow him to sleep, as will be explained.

The *mishnah* records that throughout the seven days of preparation, *lo hayu mon'in mimenu ma'achal u-mishteh* — they did not withhold food and drink from him; he could eat what he wished. But *Erev Yom Kippur im chasheichah* — as nightfall of *Erev Yom Kippur* approached — *lo hayu manichin oto leechol harbeh mipnei shehama'achal meivi et hasheinah*: they would not allow him to eat a large meal, because eating heavily leads to sleep. This is somewhat paradoxical: at the very moment when everyone else is eating their *se'udah hamafseket* in preparation for the fast, the *kohen gadol* was actually limiting his intake. The reason is that he needed to remain awake the entire night preceding *Yom Kippur*, out of concern that if he fell asleep, he would have a seminal emission, become *tamei*, and be unfit for the *avodah* — necessitating recourse to the backup *kohen gadol*.

## Ignorant Kohen Gadol

### Gemara's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא שֶׁמָּא שָׁכַח — לְחַיֵּי. אֶלָּא שֶׁמָּא לֹא לָמַד — מִי מוֹקְמִינַן כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara wonders about the depiction in the mishna of the Elders questioning the High Priest as to whether he forgot this reading or perhaps did not learn to read. **Granted, perhaps he forgot,** that is **fine,** as it is conceivable that he is not accustomed to reading the Torah and might have forgotten this portion. **However,** is it conceivable that **perhaps** the High Priest **did not learn** to read? **Do we appoint** a High Priest **of that sort** who never learned the Bible?

The *Gemara* now confronts the difficulty embedded in the *mishnah*'s language. The phrase *shemma shachachta* — perhaps you forgot — is at least comprehensible, if a little surprising. But the phrase *o shemma lo lamad'ta* — or perhaps you never learned it — is genuinely puzzling. *Mi mokminan ki hai gavna?* — would we ever actually appoint a *kohen gadol* who had never learned the *avodah*? He has never read *parshas Acharei Mos*?

One might add: even *shachachta* deserves scrutiny. If a *kohen gadol* is truly *gadol bechochma*, how does one forget what is written in *Acharei Mos*? And if this is not his first year serving as *kohen gadol*, then presumably he performed the entire *avodah* just twelve months prior — how is forgetting even a possibility?

### Kohen Gadol Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: "וְהַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל מֵאֶחָיו", שֶׁיְּהֵא גָּדוֹל מֵאֶחָיו בְּכֹחַ, בְּנוֹי, בְּחָכְמָה, וּבְעוֹשֶׁר. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם אֵין לוֹ, שֶׁאֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים מְגַדְּלִין אוֹתוֹ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְהַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל מֵאֶחָיו" — גַּדְּלֵהוּ מִשֶּׁל אֶחָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that it is stated: **"And the priest who is greater than his brethren"** (Leviticus 21:10); this teaches **that he** must **be greater than his** priestly **brethren in strength, in beauty, in wisdom, and in wealth. *Aḥerim* say:** Wealth is not a prerequisite for selecting a High Priest, but **from where** is it derived **that if he does not have** property of his own **that his brethren the priests elevate him** and render him wealthy from their own property? **The verse states: "And the priest who is greater [*haggadol*] than his brethren"; elevate him [*gaddelehu*] from** the property **of his brethren.** In any event, there is a consensus that wisdom is a prerequisite for his selection.

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in response. The Torah's phrase *vehakohen hagadol me'echav* — the *kohen* who is greater than his brethren — is read to require that the *kohen gadol* be genuinely superior to the other *kohanim* in concrete respects. The *baraita* lists these: *bekoach*, physical strength; *benoy*, impressive appearance; *bechochma*, wisdom and intelligence; *ube'osher*, wealth. These criteria are not arbitrary. Physical impressiveness — height, strength, distinguished appearance — projects the authority and gravitas that leadership requires. Empirically, of course, there is no correlation between these qualities and actual capability, yet they generate the impression of authority in the eyes of the people, and that impression itself has social and institutional value.

*Acherim omrim* — others add a further ruling: if the ideal candidate lacks wealth, the other *kohanim* are obligated to contribute from their own resources to endow him, based on the reading *gadlehu mishel achav* — make him great from what belongs to his brethren. The point for our discussion is clear: given that a *kohen gadol* must be *gadol bechochma*, how could it ever occur that he failed to learn — or forgot — the *avodas Yom HaKippurim*?

### Bayis Rishon/Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּמִקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן, כָּאן בְּמִקְדָּשׁ שֵׁנִי. דְּאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: תַּרְקַבָּא דְּדִינָרֵי עַיִּילָא לֵיהּ מָרְתָּא בַּת בַּיְיתּוֹס לְיַנַּאי מַלְכָּא עַל דְּאוֹקְמֵיהּ לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן גַּמְלָא בְּכָהֲנֵי רַבְרְבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This is **not difficult. There,** the *baraita* that lists wisdom among the attributes of the High Priest is referring to **the First Temple,** where this *halakha* was observed and the High Priests possessed those attributes listed. **Here,** the mishna is referring to **the Second Temple,** where this *halakha* was not observed, so a situation where the High Priest was not well-versed in the Bible was conceivable. **As Rav Asi said:** The wealthy **Marta, daughter of Baitos, brought a half-*se'a* of dinars in to King Yannai for** the fact **that he appointed Yehoshua ben Gamla as High Priest.** This is an example of the appointment of High Priests by means of bribery and gifts. Since that was the practice, a totally ignorant High Priest could have been appointed.

Rav Yosef resolves the difficulty with a single distinction: *lo kashya kan be'Mikdash Rishon kan be'Mikdash Sheni* — there is no difficulty; the two cases refer to different historical contexts. In the *Beis HaMikdash Rishon*, *kohanim gedolim* were appointed in accordance with the proper criteria — *gadol bechochma, be'osher, bekoach* — and the concern that a *kohen gadol* had never learned the *avodah* was simply inapplicable. In the *Beis HaMikdash Sheni*, however, the position became susceptible to purchase and political manipulation, and under those conditions an ignorant *kohen gadol* was a genuine possibility.

The *Gemara* illustrates this with the case cited by Rav Assi: *tarkava de'dinari* — a basketful of gold dinars — *aylah lah Martha bas Baittos le'Yannai malka*, Martha the daughter of Baittos brought to King Yannai, *al de'ukmei le'Yehoshua ben Gamla be'khanei ravrevi* — to have Yehoshua ben Gamla appointed to the high priesthood. Baittos was a name associated with great wealth elsewhere in the *Gemara* (one recalls the famous case of Baittos ben Zonin and the question of *matzos* made with molds), and his daughter apparently inherited substantially from that estate.

A striking question arises from Rashi's comment on this passage. Rashi notes that Martha was an *almanah* — a widow — which raises an immediate difficulty: a *kohen gadol* is prohibited from marrying a widow. How could Yehoshua ben Gamla, once appointed *kohen gadol*, remain married to her? The *Rambam*'s ruling in *hilchos issurei biah* is relevant here: he holds that the prohibition for a *kohen gadol* regarding an *almanah* applies specifically to the act of *bi'ah* in that relationship, and uniquely — unlike the parallel prohibitions applicable to ordinary *kohanim* regarding *zonah*, *chalulah*, and *grushah* — he receives *malkos* for relations with an *almanah* even without prior formal *kiddushin*: *lo yichalel, keivan sheba'ala chillela*. This makes the scenario in which a *kohen gadol* was already married to a widow at the time of his appointment genuinely complex from a halachic standpoint — and indeed raises the further question of whether *vichaper ba'ado uvead beito* could be fulfilled when *beito* is *beito ha'asura lo*.

Tosafot adds a separate observation: the *Yannai HaMelech* referenced in this story cannot be the well-known Yannai HaMelech, since other *Gemaros* indicate that he himself served as *kohen gadol* and would not have sold the position to another. Tosafot therefore maintains that this refers to a different king named Yannai.

There is also a tradition in the *Yerushalmi* relevant to the question of seminal emission and the *kohen gadol*: one of the *nissim* of the *Beis HaMikdash* was that the *kohen gadol* never experienced a seminal emission. This yields two possible responses to the concern raised by the *mishnah*. The first is the principle of *ein somchin al hanes* — one does not rely on miracles, and practical precautions must be taken regardless. The second aligns with Rav Yosef's distinction: that particular *nes* belonged to the era of the *Bayis Rishon*, and in the diminished circumstances of the *Bayis Sheni*, one could not assume its continuation. The very need for the precautions described in our *mishnah* reflects that *yeridah*.

## Animal Parade & Desp (II)

### Goats Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אַף הַשְּׂעִירִים. וְתַנָּא דִּידַן מַאי טַעְמָא לָא תְּנָא שְׂעִירִים? כֵּיוָן דְּעַל חֵטְא קָא אָתוּ — חָלְשָׁא דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* **taught** in the *Tosefta*: **Even goats** were brought before him. The Gemara asks: **And the *tanna*** of **our** mishna, **what is the reason** that **he did not teach** that **goats** were among the animals that passed before the High Priest? The Gemara answers: **Since** goats **come** as atonement **for sins,** passing them before the High Priest will evoke transgressions and he will **become distraught.**

A *baraita* notes that the goats were also paraded before him during the week — *af hase'irim*. Yet our *mishnah* makes no mention of goats. The *Gemara* asks: *tana didan mai ta'ama lo tani se'irim?* — why does our *mishnah* omit them? The answer: *keivan d'al cheit ka'asi* — since the goats come for the sin of *Klal Yisrael* (one is the goat sent over the cliff, the other is brought as a *korban*), the sight of them would cause *chalsha da'atei* — a sinking of the spirit, a despair — as the *kohen gadol* contemplates the weight of the nation's sins that he is being called to atone for.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, פַּר נָמֵי עַל חֵטְא הוּא דְּאָתֵי! פַּר, כֵּיוָן דְּעָלָיו וְעַל אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים הוּא דְּאָתֵי, בְּאֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים, אִי אִיכָּא אִינִישׁ דְּאִית בֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא — מִידָּע יָדַע לֵיהּ וּמַהְדַּר לֵיהּ בִּתְשׁוּבָה. בְּכוּלְּהוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל — לָא יָדַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, a bull** should not be passed before him, **as it too comes** to atone **for sin.** The Gemara answers that there is a difference in the case of **a bull, since** it is to atone **for his** sins **and for** the sins of **his brethren the priests that it comes; among his brethren the priests, if there is a person who has a** sinful **matter,** the High Priest **would know** about it **and** lead **him back to** the path of righteousness **through repentance.** Therefore, passing a bull before the High Priest will not render him distraught, as it will merely remind him of his responsibility toward his priestly brethren. On the other hand, **with regard to the entire Jewish people, he does not know** of their sinful matters and is unable to facilitate their repentance. Passing goats before the High Priest will evoke their sins as well as his inability to correct the situation, leaving him distraught.

The *Gemara* then asks: *ee hachi, par nami* — if so, what about his own *par*, his ox? That too comes for sin. The *Gemara* answers: *keivan d'alav v'al echav hakoanim hu d'asi* — the ox comes for him and for his fellow *kohanim*. Regarding the *kohanim*, *meida yada lei* — he is personally acquainted with them; he knows who among them may be transgressing. *U'mahader lei b'teshuva* — he can bring a fellow *kohen* into his confidence, address him directly, and guide him toward *teshuva* before *Yom Kippur*. He has some degree of agency and influence.

### Influence Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: אִי בַּר אֲחָתָיךְ דַּיָּילָא הָוֵי חֲזִי, בְּשׁוּקָא קַמֵּיהּ לָא תַּחְלֵיף.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the High Priest being privy to the sinful behavior of his fellow priests, **Ravina said** that **this** explains the folk saying **that people say: If** the beloved **son of your** beloved **sister becomes a policeman [*dayyala*], see** to it that **in the marketplace you do not pass before him.** Be wary of him because he knows your sins.

With *Klal Yisrael* at large, there is no such agency. *U'vechulu Yisrael lo yada* — he does not know who the sinners are among the broader people, and he cannot influence them. That helplessness is what generates the deeper despair when confronting the goat. *Amar Ravina*: this is the meaning of the folk saying, *ee bar achasach daila, havi chazi b'shuka kamei lo sachloof* — if your nephew is a tax collector, don't walk in front of him in the marketplace. The implication is that with one's own family and close circle, one is exposed, known, and subject to accountability. The *kohen gadol* feels that accountability with the *kohanim* — if one of them is sinning, he would know about it, and he could act. With the rest of Israel, he has no such knowledge or leverage, and the despair is correspondingly greater.

The result is that the roleplay with animals during the week is conducted with the oxen, rams, and sheep — but conspicuously without the goats. This is no small omission: a substantial portion of the entire *Yom Kippur avodah* centers on the goats — the *shechitas hase'ir*, the *lot* drawing, and the dispatch of the goat over the cliff. To conduct the roleplay without any of that is notable.

The *Gemara*'s conclusion here also opens a related question that will receive fuller treatment in the eighth *perek*: to what extent is *teshuva* a necessary component of *kaparah* when *korbanos* are present? Without *korbanos*, the entire focus of *kaparah* falls on *teshuva*, but whether *teshuva* was strictly required in order to achieve atonement when the *korbanos* themselves were being brought is a significant *machlokes* that will be addressed later.

## Seminal Emission Con (II)

### Foods Causing Emission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן נָקוֹסָא אוֹמֵר: מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ סְלָתוֹת וּבֵיצִים כְּדֵי לְמַסְמְסוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁאַתָּה מְבִיאוֹ לִידֵי חִימּוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Nekosa says:** On Yom Kippur eve **they feed him fine flour and eggs in order to loosen his** bowels, so that he will not need to relieve himself on Yom Kippur. **They said to** Rabbi Yehuda ben Nekosa: In feeding him those foods, **all the more so that you bring him to a state of arousal.** Feeding him those foods is antithetical to the efforts to prevent the High Priest from becoming impure, as they are liable to cause him to experience a seminal emission.

The *mishnah* stated that on *Erev Yom Kippur* as darkness fell, they would not allow him to eat heavily in order to prevent sleep and the resulting risk of seminal emission. A *baraita* elaborates on what Rabbi Yehuda ben Nakusa proposed: *maachilin oto solet uveitzim kedei lehamssu* — they would feed him fine flour products and eggs, as a kind of purgative to clear his digestive system, ensuring he would not be interrupted during the *avodah*. But this proposal was rejected: *kol sheken sheata mevio lidei chimum* — on the contrary, these are precisely the foods that generate bodily heat, meaning they stimulate sexual arousal and are liable to produce the seminal emission one is trying to avoid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא סוֹמְכוֹס אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אֵין מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ לֹא "אב״י", וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: לֹא "אבב״י", וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף לֹא יַיִן לָבָן. לֹא "אב״י" — לֹא אֶתְרוֹג, וְלֹא בֵּיצִים, וְלֹא יַיִן יָשָׁן. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לֹא "אבב״י" — לֹא אֶתְרוֹג, וְלֹא בֵּיצִים, וְלֹא בָּשָׂר שָׁמֵן, וְלֹא יַיִן יָשָׁן. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף לֹא יַיִן לָבָן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַיַּיִן לָבָן מֵבִיא אֶת הָאָדָם לִידֵי טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Sumakhos said in the name of Rabbi Meir: One does not feed him** foods represented by the acrostic: ***Alef*, *beit*, *yod*; and some say** that one does **not** feed him foods represented by the acrostic: ***Alef*, *beit*, *beit*, *yod*; and some say neither** does one feed him **white wine.** The Gemara elaborates: **Not *alef*, *beit*, *yod*** means **neither *etrog*, nor eggs [*beitzim*], nor old wine [*yayin*]. And some say: Not *alef*, *beit*, *beit*, *yod*** means **neither *etrog*, nor eggs [*beitzim*], nor fatty meat [*basar*], nor old wine [*yayin*]. And some say neither** does one feed him **white wine because white wine brings** a **man to** the **impurity** of a seminal emission.

A further *baraita* cites Sumchus in the name of Rabbi Meir: *ein maachilin oto lo avi* on *Erev Yom Kippur* — that is, no *alef*, no *vav*, no *yud*: no *etrog*, no *yayin yashan* (old wine), no *beitzim* (eggs). An alternative version expands the acronym to *avavi*: *etrog*, *beitzim*, *basar shamen* (fatty meat), and old wine. Some add white wine as well — *mipnei shehayayin lavan meivi et ha'adam lidei tumah* — because white wine brings a person to *tumah*, meaning seminal emission. Notably, the concern about the night of *Erev Yom Kippur* is particularly acute: any *tumah* incurred on a previous day would allow time to immerse in a *mikveh* and recover before *Yom Kippur*, but a seminal emission on *Erev Yom Kippur* night would leave the *kohen gadol* unfit for the *avodah* with no opportunity to rectify it.

### Zav vs. Baal Keri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: זָב תּוֹלִין לוֹ בְּמַאֲכָל, וְכׇל מִינֵי מַאֲכָל.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **the Sages taught:** If a man experienced an emission that could render him **a *zav*, one attributes** the emission not to his being a *zav* but perhaps to a different cause, e.g., **to food, or to all kinds of food,** i.e., he may have eaten too much food, which could have caused the emission.

The *Gemara* now broadens the discussion into the underlying *halachic* framework concerning these foods. A *baal keri* — a man who has had a seminal emission — becomes *tamei* regardless of what caused it: whether sexual thoughts, a nocturnal emission with no identifiable cause, or certain foods. The cause is irrelevant to the *tumah*.

A *zav* is categorically different. *Zivah* is a non-seminal penile flow (associated by some with gonorrhea), and the *Gemara* understands that *zivah* cannot be attributed to an external cause — *machmat ones*. If a man had this type of emission following sexual arousal, or after consuming foods that can precipitate such a physiological response, it is considered externally caused and he does not acquire the status of *zav*. What is striking is that although the *Gemara* is entirely clear that *zivah* is not semen, it nonetheless assumes that the same types of triggers — sexual thoughts and certain foods — can produce this flow as well. The physiological parallel is drawn even where the substances differ entirely.

The *Gemara* states: *zav tolin lo bema'achal uvechol minei ma'achal* — for a *zav*, one may attribute the emission to food, even to overeating generally, regardless of specific food categories.

### Foods for Zav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פִּנְחָס אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא: אֵין מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ לֹא "חגב״י" וְלֹא "גב״ם", וְלֹא כׇּל דְּבָרִים הַמְּבִיאִים לִידֵי טוּמְאָה. לֹא "חגב״י" — לֹא חָלָב, וְלֹא גְּבִינָה, וְלֹא בֵּיצָה, וְלֹא יַיִן. וְלֹא "גב״ם" — מֵי גְרִיסִין שֶׁל פּוֹל, וּבָשָׂר שָׁמֵן, וּמֻרְיָיס.
>
> **Translation:** **Elazar ben Pineḥas says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira:** During the days that a *zav* is examining himself to determine whether or not he is impure, **one feeds him neither** foods represented by the acrostic: ***Ḥet*, *gimmel*, *beit*, *yod*, nor** foods represented by the acrostic: ***Gimmel*, *beit*, *mem*, nor any** food **items that** might **bring him to impurity** caused by an emission. The Gemara explains: **Not *ḥet*, *gimmel*, *beit*, *yod*** means **neither milk [*ḥalav*], nor cheese [*gevina*], nor egg [*beitza*], nor wine [*yayin*]. And not *gimmel*, *beit*, *mem*** means **neither soup of pounded beans [*mei gerisin*], nor fatty meat [*basar*], nor** small **fish** pickled **in brine [*muryas*].**

Elazar ben Pinchas then cites Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira with a more specific ruling: *ein maachilin oto lo chagavi velo gabam* — you should not feed a *zav* the following foods when he is counting his seven clean days. The acronym *chagavi* yields: *chalav* (milk), *gevina* (cheese), *beitzah* (egg), *yayin* (wine). The acronym *gabam* yields: *mei gerisin shel pol* (a pounded-bean soup), *basar shamena* (fatty meat), and *muryaas* (a type of fish preparation). The overlap with the foods mentioned earlier in the context of the *kohen gadol* — particularly eggs, wine, and fatty meat — is evident, though the list for the *zav* is more expansive and includes dairy products not mentioned in the earlier context.

### Five Things Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא כׇּל דְּבָרִים הַמְּבִיאִים לִידֵי טוּמְאָה, לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי הָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים מְבִיאִים אֶת הָאָדָם לִידֵי טוּמְאָה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הַשּׁוּם וְהַשַּׁחֲלַיִם וַחֲלֹגְלוֹגוֹת וְהַבֵּיצִים וְהַגַּרְגִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about the phrase: **Nor any** food **items that** might **bring him to impurity; what does it** come **to include? It** comes **to include that which the Sages taught: Five** food **items bring** a **man to** a state of **impurity** due to emission. **And these are: Garlic, cress, purslane, eggs, and arugula.**

The *baraita* concludes with the phrase *velo kol devarim hamevi'in lidei tumah* — and not any foods that bring to *tumah*. The *Gemara* asks: *le'atuyei mai* — what does that phrase add? The answer is that it comes to include what the rabbis taught elsewhere: *chamisha devarim mevi'im et ha'adam lidei tumah* — five things that lead to seminal emission. They are: *hashum* (garlic), *hashachalim* (which Rashi identifies as cress), *hachalglogot* (purslane), *habeitzim* (eggs), and *hagargir* (which the Mesorah identifies as garden rocket, or arugula).

### Arugula Digression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיֵּצֵא אֶחָד אֶל הַשָּׂדֶה לְלַקֵּט אוֹרוֹת", תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר: זֶה גַּרְגִּיר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָן "אוֹרוֹת" — שֶׁמְּאִירוֹת אֶת הָעֵינַיִם. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַמּוֹצֵיא גַּרְגִּיר, אִם יָכוֹל לְאׇכְלוֹ אוֹכְלוֹ, וְאִם לָאו — מַעֲבִירוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי עֵינָיו. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּגַרְגִּירָא מַצְרָנְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the arugula plant, the Gemara cites a verse: **"And one of them went out into the fields to collect *orot*"** (II Kings 4:39). It was **taught in the name of Rabbi Meir** with regard to *orot* in this verse: **This is** the plant called **arugula. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Why are** these arugula plants **called *orot*?** It is because **they enlighten [*me'irot*] the eyes. Rav Huna said:** With regard to **one who finds arugula, if he can eat it, he eats it, and if not, he passes it over his eyes,** as that too is beneficial. **Rav Pappa said: Arugula** is most effective when it grows **on the border** of the field, where it is unadulterated by other plants.

The mention of *gargir* prompts a short *aggadic* digression. A *tanna* in the name of Rabbi Meir identifies the *orot* gathered in the field (mentioned in a Biblical passage) as *gargir*. Rabbi Yochanan asks: *lama nikra sheman orot?* — why are they called *orot* (literally, lights)? The answer: *sheme'irot et ha'einayim* — because arugula illuminates the eyes, meaning it is beneficial for eyesight.

Rav Huna adds a practical instruction: *hamotzeh gargir* — if one comes across wild arugula growing in a field, *im yachol le'achlo ochlo* — if you can tolerate eating it (arugula being notoriously bitter), then eat it for the health benefit. *Ve'im lo, ma'aviro al gabei einav* — and if you cannot stomach it, simply pass it across your eyes, and even that will benefit the eyesight.

Rav Papa qualifies this: this is specifically true of *gargira dematzra* — arugula that grows at the edge of the field, where it receives no nutrients from neighboring plants, leaving it in a purer, more concentrated form. This is Rashi's explanation of the distinction.

### Guest Etiquette

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: אַכְסְנַאי לֹא יֹאכַל בֵּיצִים, וְלֹא יִישַׁן בְּטַלִּיתוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: A guest** should **neither eat eggs,** because they lead to a seminal emission, **nor sleep in a garment belonging to the homeowner,** his host, because if he experiences a seminal emission and it gets on the garment, he will be diminished in the estimation of his host.

Rav Gidal cites Rav with a practical ruling that connects the concern about seminal emission to the context of a guest staying in someone else's home: *achsanai lo yochel beitzim* — a guest should not eat eggs while at someone's house. The reason, given what the *Gemara* has established about eggs and seminal emission, is that a nocturnal emission on someone else's sheets and in someone else's bed would be a source of embarrassment. Relatedly: *velo yishan betalito shel ba'al habayit* — a guest should not sleep using the host's cloak as a blanket. If he were to have a seminal emission under the host's garment, it would be obvious and shameful. Rashi suggests an alternative reading of this second concern: the embarrassment might run in the other direction — the guest, by sleeping with the host's cloak, might discover evidence of the host's own emission. Either way, the practical counsel is clear.

## Traveling Marriages

### Rav's Announcement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כִּי מִקְּלַע לְדַרְשִׁישׁ, מַכְרֵיז: מַאן הָוְיָא לְיוֹמָא. רַב נַחְמָן כַּד מִקְּלַע לְשַׁכְנְצִיב, מַכְרֵיז: מַאן הָוְיָא לְיוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav would happen** to come **to Darshish he would declare: Who will be** married to me **for the day** that I am here so that I will not be unwed in this place, after which I will divorce her? Similarly, **when Rav Naḥman would come to Shekhantziv he would declare: Who will be** married to me **for the day** that I am here?

The *Gemara* now introduces a closely related discussion, arising naturally from the concern about men away from home and their management of sexual desire. Rav, when he would visit Darshish, *makhriz* — he would make a public announcement: *man havya leyoma?* — who would be with me for today? Rav Nachman would do likewise when he traveled to Shachnatziv.

### Bigamy Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַתַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִשָּׂא אָדָם אִשָּׁה בִּמְדִינָה זוֹ, וְיֵלֵךְ וְיִשָּׂא אִשָּׁה בִּמְדִינָה אַחֶרֶת, שֶׁמָּא יִזְדַּוְּוגוּ זֶה אֵצֶל זֶה וְנִמְצָא אָח נוֹשֵׂא אֲחוֹתוֹ וּמְמַלֵּא כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ מַמְזֵרוּת, וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: "וּמָלְאָה הָאָרֶץ זִמָּה". אָמְרִי: רַבָּנַן — קָלָא אִית לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: A man should not marry a woman in one state and go and marry** another **woman in a different state, lest** a **match be arranged** between the child of **this** wife **with** the child of **that** wife who are unaware of their relationship. This would **lead to a brother marrying his sister,** filling the whole world in its entirety with *mamzerim*. **And concerning this it is stated: "And the land became filled with lewdness"** (Leviticus 19:29). The Sages **say** in response: **The Sages generate publicity.** Since they were well-known, the identity of their children was also undoubtedly known. Therefore, there was no concern that errors of this kind would befall their children.

The *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty from a *baraita*: Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov rules that a man may not marry a woman in one country and then go and marry another woman in a different country, because their children may eventually encounter one another without knowing they share a father, and a brother and sister may end up marrying — thereby filling the land with *mamzerut*. How, then, could Rav and Rav Nachman take wives in different places they visited?

The *Gemara* answers: *Rabbanan kali it lehu* — the rabbis had a recognized reputation. Their names were known throughout the Jewish world. Unlike an ordinary person named Shmuel whose children in two different cities might not identify each other as half-siblings, the children of Rav or Rav Nachman would identify their father by name and standing. When two people were considering marriage and discovered they both had the same famous father, they would know immediately. The concern about inadvertent incest therefore does not apply.

Rashi also notes that Rav would divorce these women afterwards — *veteitze le'achar mikan* — both for the woman's sake (she should not remain bound to him between infrequent visits) and to explain why he would need to make the announcement anew each time he arrived.

### Dam Chimud Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: תְּבָעוּהָ לְהִנָּשֵׂא, וְנִתְפַּיְּיסָה — צְרִיכָה לֵישֵׁב שִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a different problem with the practice of Rav and Rav Naḥman. **But didn't Rava say:** With regard to **one who proposed marriage to** a woman **and she agreed,** she **is required to sit seven clean days,** as perhaps due to the anticipatory desire she might not notice that she experienced menstrual bleeding and she is therefore impure. How, then, could these *amora'im* marry a woman on the day that they proposed?

But the *Gemara* presses further. Rava ruled: *tav'uha lehinasay* — if a groom pressed his betrothed to advance the wedding date, and she agreed (*venitpaisah*), she requires *shiva neki'im* — seven clean days — because the suddenness of the demand likely caused excitement that may have led to spotting. This phenomenon is called *dam chimud* — the blood of desire or excitement. If so, how could Rav or Rav Nachman marry a woman and proceed to relations the same day, given that the very announcement of the intent to marry would itself trigger the concern of *dam chimud*?

This principle of *dam chimud* connects to a number of further *halachic* applications worth noting. It underlies the practice that a *kallah* does not go to the *mikveh* more than four days before the wedding; the approach of the wedding creates ongoing concern about excitement-related spotting. There is a division among the *Rishonim* on the scope of this concern: some hold that a woman must check herself daily after the *mikveh* as the wedding draws closer, since proximity to the wedding continuously intensifies the concern. The majority position, however, holds that the concern is specifically tied to *suddenness* — the shock of an unexpected demand — rather than to the general anticipation of a wedding one has been planning and preparing for.

### Yichud Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן אוֹדוֹעֵי הֲווֹ מוֹדְעוּ לְהוּ, מִקְדָּם הֲווֹ קָדְמִי וּמְשַׁדְּרִי שְׁלוּחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages would inform them by sending messengers before** their arrival. The messenger would announce that the *amora* sought to marry a local woman. The woman who agreed would in fact wait seven clean days before marrying him.

The *Gemara* offers two answers. The first: *Rabbanan adoyi havei mode'i lehu, mikdam havei makdimi umashadrei shlucha* — the rabbis would announce their arrival ahead of time by sending a messenger. Rather than making the announcement upon arrival, they would send someone in advance to find a suitable woman and to allow her time to go to the *mikveh* and count the seven clean days before he arrived. This fundamentally changes the picture: it is no longer a sudden announcement but a structured, advance arrangement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא יַחוֹדֵי הֲווֹ מְיַחֲדִי לְהוּ, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ פַּת בְּסַלּוֹ לְמִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ פַּת בְּסַלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that these Sages were not actually proposing marriage; rather, they proposed so that they could **be in seclusion with** the women, without consummating the relationship. Since the women knew that the marriage would not be consummated, they did not experience anticipatory desire. **There is no similarity between one who has bread in his basket and one who does not have bread in his basket.** One who does not have access to bread experiences hunger more acutely than one for whom bread is available and can eat whenever he chooses. Similarly, an unmarried man experiences a more acute desire. In order to mitigate that desire, these Sages made certain that women would be designated for them.

The second answer: *yichudi havei miyachadi lehu* — they would have *yichud* with these women, and the principle of *pas besalo* applies: *lefi she-eino domeh mi she-yesh lo pas besalo le-mi she-ein lo pas besalo* — it is not the same for one who has bread in his basket as for one who does not. Rashi understands this to mean that they would not actually have relations; the knowledge that the woman was available to him was sufficient to reduce the urgency of the desire, and so no actual intercourse occurred.

Tosafot objects to this conclusion on two grounds. First, *pas besalo* — if you have bread in your basket, the natural thing is to eat it. Second, even on its own terms, the woman would not be accessible for relations during the week the *Gemara* presupposes them to be together, precisely because of the *dam chimud* concern — so in what sense is it *pas besalo* at all?

The *Ri* proposes an alternative reading: the announcement was never about marriage or sex explicitly. Rav and Rav Nachman announced only that they were seeking *yichud* — someone to share a room with them. Because sex was never mentioned in the announcement, the *dam chimud* concern does not arise; it was the suddenness of a sexual proposal that triggers that concern, not the mere prospect of *yichud*. If sexual relations ultimately occurred during the stay, it would not be precipitated by a shocking announcement.

## Oath & Instructions

### Transfer Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מְסָרוּהוּ זִקְנֵי בֵית דִּין לְזִקְנֵי כְהוּנָּה וְהֶעֱלוּהוּ בֵּית אַבְטִינָס, וְהִשְׁבִּיעוּהוּ, וְנִפְטְרוּ וְהָלְכוּ לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The Elders of the court** who read the order of the service of the day before the High Priest **passed him to the Elders of the priesthood, and they took him up to the House of Avtinas. And they administered him an oath and took leave** of him **and went on their way.**

The *mishnah* now describes the final transfer of the *kohen gadol* as *Yom Kippur* eve concludes: *masru ziknei Beis Din le-ziknei kehunah* — the elders of *Beis Din* handed him over to the elders of the *kehunah*. The symbolic structure here is clear: he passes from the oversight of those who represent *Torah* authority and *Klal Yisrael* into the hands of his own priestly colleagues. They bring him to *Beis Avtinas*, where the *ketores* was prepared, since what follows centers on the *avodas haketores* — the most technical and contested element of the *Yom Kippur* service.

### Ketores Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: אִישִׁי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל! אָנוּ שְׁלוּחֵי בֵּית דִּין, וְאַתָּה שְׁלוּחֵנוּ וּשְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין. מַשְׁבִּיעִין אָנוּ עָלֶיךָ בְּמִי שֶׁשִּׁכֵּן שְׁמוֹ בַּבַּיִת הַזֶּה שֶׁלֹּא תְּשַׁנֶּה דָּבָר מִכׇּל מַה שֶּׁאָמַרְנוּ לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** When they administered this oath **they said to him: My Master, High Priest. We are agents of the court, and you are our agent and the agent of the court. We administer an oath to you in** the name of **Him who housed His name in this House, that you will not change** even one **matter from all that we have said to you** with regard to the burning of the incense or any other service that you will perform when alone.

The *mishnah* records that *v'hishbi'uhu* — they administered an oath to him. The substance of the oath is declared: *Ishi Kohen Gadol* — the assembled elders address him — *anu shluchei Beis Din, v'ata shlucheinu u-shliach Beis Din* — we are emissaries of *Beis Din*, and you are our emissary and the emissary of *Beis Din*. The Maharsha notes an allusion in the oath formula — *mi she-shachan shemo babayis hazeh* (the One whose name dwells in this house) — to the *pasuk* in the *parsha* of *Yom Kippur* describing God as *hashochen itam betoch tumasam*.

The content of the oath: *shelo teshaneh davar mikol mah she-amarnu lach* — that he will not deviate from anything they have taught him. The specific concern is the dispute between the *Perushim* and the *Tzadukim* regarding the *ketores*: the *Tzadukim* held that the incense was placed on the coals before entering the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, while the *Perushim* held that he entered first and only then placed the incense on the fire. Given that a large proportion of the *kohanim* in the *Bayis Sheni* period were *Tzadukim*, and the *kohen gadol* himself may well have been one, this oath was not ceremonial — it was the rabbinic establishment's means of ensuring that the *avodah* was performed according to the *halacha* as they understood it.

### Emotional Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא פּוֹרֵשׁ וּבוֹכֶה, וְהֵן פּוֹרְשִׁין וּבוֹכִין.
>
> **Translation:** **he would leave** them **and cry, and they would leave** him **and cry** in sorrow that the oath was necessary.

*Hu poresh v'bocheh v'hem porshim v'bochin* — he would go apart and weep, and they would go apart and weep. He wept at being suspected; they wept at being compelled to suspect him. The emotional valence of this moment is striking, and the *mishnah* preserves it without further comment.

### Staying Awake Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם (הוּא) הָיָה חָכָם — דּוֹרֵשׁ, וְאִם לָאו — תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים דּוֹרְשִׁים לְפָנָיו. וְאִם רָגִיל לִקְרוֹת — קוֹרֵא, וְאִם לָאו — קוֹרִין לְפָנָיו. וּבַמֶּה קוֹרִין לְפָנָיו: בְּאִיּוֹב וּבְעֶזְרָא וּבְדִבְרֵי הַיָּמִים. זְכַרְיָה בֶּן קְבוּטָל אוֹמֵר: פְּעָמִים הַרְבֵּה קָרִיתִי לְפָנָיו בְּדָנִיֵּאל.
>
> **Translation:** They kept him occupied throughout the night to prevent him from sleeping. **If he was a scholar, he** would **teach** Torah. **If** he was **not** a scholar, **Torah scholars** would **teach** Torah **before him. And if** he was **accustomed to read** the Bible, **he** would **read; and if** he was **not, they** would **read** the Bible **before him. And what** books would **they read before him** to pique his interest so that he would not fall asleep? They would read **from Job, and from Ezra, and from Chronicles. Zekharya, son of Kevutal, says: Many times I read before him from** the book of **Daniel.**

The *mishnah* then describes how the *kohen gadol* was kept awake throughout the night. If he was a scholar, *doreish* — he would deliver a *shiur* himself. *Ve'im lav* — if he lacked the capacity to deliver but could comprehend, *talmidei chachamim darshin lefanav* — Torah sages would lecture before him. *Ve'im ragil likrot* — if he was literate and could at least read fluently, he would read from *Tanakh* on his own. *Ve'im lav, korin lefanav* — and if even that was beyond his capacity, others would read to him.

The *mishnah* specifies which books were read to him in that final case: *be'Iyov uve'Ezra uve'Divrei HaYamim* — Iyov, Ezra, and Divrei HaYamim. Ezra, written largely in Aramaic (the spoken vernacular), would be accessible and engaging, with narrative directly relevant to the *Beis HaMikdash* and its restoration. Iyov offers rich philosophical substance. Divrei HaYamim contains sections of dense genealogy alongside narrative — potentially less immediately gripping, though some portions are highly relevant. Zecharya ben Kevutal adds his testimony: *pa'amim harbeh karisi lefanav be'Daniel* — I read to him from Sefer Daniel on many occasions, with its compelling narrative sections well-suited to holding a listener's attention through the night.

### Oath Authority Debate

The *mishnah*'s account of the oath contains an ambiguity that divides the *Rishonim*. After recording that the *ziknei Beis Din* handed the *kohen gadol* over to the *ziknei kehunah*, the text says *ve'halchu lahem ve'amru lo* — they departed and said to him. The question is whether the *amru lo* refers to the *ziknei Beis Din* — meaning they administered the oath before departing — or whether it refers to the *ziknei kehunah* — meaning the *Beis Din* elders left, and it was the priestly elders who then administered the oath.

The Ramban maintains that it was the *ziknei Beis Din* who administered the *shevuah*, not the *ziknei kehunah*. This reading carries a coherent institutional logic: the rabbinic establishment, representing *Torah* authority and standing guard against *Tzaduki* practice, retains direct supervisory involvement in this critical moment. The alternative reading — that *Beis Din* departed and the responsibility passed entirely to the *ziknei kehunah* — frames the scene quite differently, leaving the priests themselves as the guarantors of correct practice. The ambiguity in the *mishnah*'s language, and the debate it generates, reflects the broader question of exactly how the relationship between rabbinic and priestly authority was structured at this climactic moment before the *avodas Yom HaKippurim*.
