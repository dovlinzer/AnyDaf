# Megillah 16b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yosef's Weeping

### Plural Necks

The *daf* opens with a verse from the story of Yosef's reunion with his brothers: *vayipol al tzavarei Binyamin achiv* — Yosef fell on Binyamin's neck and wept, *u'Binyamin bacha al tzavarav*. The Gemara asks: *kama tzavarin havu leh* — how many necks does he have? The word *tzavarei* is plural. As Rashi notes, this is simply how the language refers to the neck; nonetheless, the Gemara uses it as an occasion for *drasha*.

### Two Temples

Rabbi Elazar teaches: *bacha al shnei mikdashim she'atidin lihyot b'chelko shel Binyamin v'atidin l'yecharev* — Yosef wept over the two *mikdashim* that would stand in Binyamin's portion of the Land of Israel and would eventually be destroyed. This is striking in light of the broader context: the *Megillah* itself is situated, according to *Chazal*, between these two *mikdashim* — so that the symbolism of Mordechai's *levush malchus* and Yosef's gifts intersects with the destruction of the *batei mikdash* in Binyamin's territory.

### Mishkan Shiloh

*U'Binyamin bacha al tzavarav* — and Binyamin wept on Yosef's necks. Here the Gemara passes over the plural without comment, and instead explains that Binyamin wept *al mishkan Shiloh she'atid lihyot b'chelko shel Yosef v'atid l'yecharev* — over the destruction of the *mishkan* at Shiloh, which would stand in Yosef's portion and likewise be destroyed. Thus the mutual weeping was not merely an expression of joy at reunion, but carried the weight of future destruction.

### Yosef's Sincerity

The verse continues: *v'hinei eyneichem ra'ot v'eynei achi Binyamin ki pi hamedaber aleichem* — behold, your eyes see and the eyes of my brother Binyamin that it is my mouth speaking to you. Rabbi Elazar explains: why single out *eynei achi Binyamin* separately? Yosef is making a point. Just as he clearly harbors nothing against Binyamin — who had no part in the plot to sell him — so too, *kach ein li b'libi aleichem*, he holds no grudge against the brothers either. *Ki pi hamedaber aleichem — k'fi ken libi*: what his mouth says is precisely what is in his heart. He is being entirely sincere.

### Gifts to Father

The verse states that Yosef sent his father ten donkeys laden with *mi'tuv Mitzrayim* — the good of Egypt. The Gemara asks: what is the good of Egypt? Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet said in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *shalach lo yayin yashan she'da'at zekenim nocha heymenu* — he sent him old wine, which elderly people truly appreciate and which puts them in a settled, contented state of mind.

## Prostration & Power

### Brothers Prostrate

After Yaakov dies, the brothers fear that Yosef will now take his revenge, and they prostrate themselves before him. Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet said in the name of Rabbi Elazar — and one notes that many of the teachings in this passage are transmitted through this same chain — *hainu d'amri inshi, ta'ala b'idneih sagid leh*: this is what people say: a fox in his time, you must bow down to him. They prostrated themselves before Yosef because Yosef held the power.

### Fox Metaphor

The Gemara immediately questions this application: *mai betzirosieh me'achvoh* — in what way was Yosef inferior to his brothers, like a fox? He was their equal by birth; the fox metaphor implies someone at a lower station who happens to have power. Therefore, the Gemara says, *ela ei ismar hachi ismar* — if this saying was transmitted, it was transmitted in a different context. The better context is *vayishtachu Yisrael al rosh hamitah* — when Yaakov bowed down on his bed as Yosef came to receive his blessing and bring Ephraim and Menashe. There Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet in the name of Rabbi Elazar applies the saying: *ta'ala b'idneih sagid leh* — even a fox has his day, and since Yosef held temporal power, even Yaakov, his father, acknowledged that.

### Yaakov Bows

The phrase *vayishtachu Yisrael al rosh hamitah* thus receives its proper context. Yaakov bowing before Yosef was not mere deference to a son; it reflected the recognition that Yosef's position of authority warranted acknowledgment — even from his father. The proverb is not demeaning to Yosef but descriptive of the reality of worldly power.

### Candles Metaphor

Returning to the brothers who have prostrated themselves, the verse says *vaynachim otam vayidaber al libam* — he consoled them and spoke to their hearts. Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet in the name of Rabbi Elazar explains: *vayidaber al libam* means he spoke *devarim she'miskablim al halev*, words that could actually be received by the heart. What did he say? *Umah asarah nerot lo yakhlu lekhabot ner echad, ner echad heikh yakhol lekhabot asarah nerot* — if ten candles could not extinguish one candle, how could one candle extinguish ten? By framing his reassurance in terms of *nerot*, Yosef elevated the conversation to the spiritual plane. Not only would he not harm them — he could not, even were he to try. The metaphor suggests that on the level of the soul, such a thing is impossible.

## Four Themes

### Four Purim Themes

The *Megillah* states: *layehudim hayetah orah vesimkhah vesason vikar* — to the Jews there was light, joy, rejoicing, and honor. The Gemara interprets each of these four terms as referring to a specific religious institution.

### Light = Torah

Rav Yehudah teaches: *orah zo Torah* — the light is *Torah*. The proof text is *ki ner mitzvah ve-Torah or* — a *mitzvah* is a candle and *Torah* is light. This verse, with its reference to the *ner*, connects back as well to the earlier *nerot* imagery.

### Joy, Rejoicing, Honor

*Simkhah zeh Yom Tov* — joy refers to *Yom Tov*, as the verse says *vesamakhta be-khagekha*. *Sason zo milah* — rejoicing refers to *milah*, derived from the verse *sas anokhi al imratekha kemotze shalal rav*, which is recited at a *bris milah*. Rashi adds that when God commands *bris milah* to Avraham, the Torah uses the word *vayomer* — *amiraha* — which connects to *imratekha*. *Vikar elu tefillin* — honor refers to *tefillin*, from the verse *verau kol amei ha-aretz ki shem Hashem nikra alekha ve-yar'u mimmeka* — all the peoples of the earth will see and be in awe of you.

### Haman's Decrees

What does all of this mean in context? Why would the *Megillah*'s celebration be expressed specifically through *Torah*, *Yom Tov*, *milah*, and *tefillin*? Rashi explains that Haman did not merely decree to destroy the Jews physically; he also, as in the period of the *Chanukah* story, decreed against Jewish religious practice. Thus: *orah zo Torah shegazar aleihem Haman shelo yaasku ba-Torah* — Haman decreed they should not engage in *Torah* study. Similarly he forbade observance of *Yamim Tovim*, the practice of *milah*, and the wearing of *tefillin*. The Gemara is thus, to some degree, overlapping the themes of *Chanukah* and *Purim* — both involved decrees not only against Jewish life but against Jewish religious identity.

## Ten Sons Names

### One Breath

The Gemara turns to the public reading of the names of the ten sons of Haman: *ve'et Parshandasa* — *aseret benei Haman*. Rav Adda of Yafo teaches: *aseret benei Haman ve-aseret, tzarikh lemimreinhu beneshimah akhat* — the ten sons of Haman and the word *aseret* must all be said in a single breath. *Mai taama* — what is the reason? *Kulu behadi hadadi nafka nishmatayhu* — because their souls all departed simultaneously. They were all hanged at the same time, and saying the names in one breath symbolizes that simultaneity. There is something significant, apparently, in the fact that they all died in a single instant.

### Tosafot Opinion

Tosafot notes that saying the names in one breath is *le-khatchilah*; *aval be-diavad yatza* — if one did not manage it, the obligation is nonetheless fulfilled after the fact. This raises a practical question: in many communities the *ba'al korei* pauses and the congregation recites the ten names themselves. This practice may reflect the principle of *shomea k'oneh* — hearing is like saying — but holding one's breath is not the same as the listener holding their breath. Therefore, many hold that one should recite the names oneself in a single breath, or at minimum hold one's breath while the *chazzan* recites them.

### Stretched Vav

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *vav shel Vaizasa tzarikh lemimtechah bezikifa kemordya dilvros* — the *vav* of *Vaizasa* must be stretched out, written elongated like the pole used on the river called Livros. *Mai taama* — *kulu bechad zikifa izdakaifu* — they were all hanged on the same pole. Therefore the *vav* is written to visually symbolize the gallows. This is a striking tradition, for it implies a change in the actual manner of writing the letter based on symbolic considerations — something unusual, since ordinarily the way letters are written derives from an independent *masorah*, not from interpretive associations.

### Brick Pattern

Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa taught in the name of a scholar from the village of Timra: *kol hashirot kulan nichtavot ariach al gabei levina, ulevina al gabei ariach* — all the poems and songs in *Tanach* are written in a staggered pattern, like bricks: a half-brick over a full brick, and a full brick over a half-brick. This is the familiar pattern of *shirat hayam* (Az Yashir) and other *shirot*, where the text alternates in a staggered arrangement.

### Staggered Exception

The exceptions are *aseret benei Haman* and the listing of the kings of Kena'an — *she'ariach al gabei ariach ulevina al gabei levina* — where the text is arranged in straight columns, column directly over column, without staggering. One might also note that *Ha'azinu* presents a similar straight-column format with a central space, though the Gemara does not incorporate it into this particular point.

### No Rising

*Mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for this departure from the standard pattern of *shirot*? *Shelo tehe tekuma lemapaltan* — so that there should be no rising up from their fall. The instability of a staggered pattern is precisely why bricks are normally laid that way — it creates structural strength. Here, by placing column directly over column, the text visually enacts complete, irreversible collapse. There should be nothing left standing; the arrangement signals total destruction with no possibility of recovery.

A further scribal tradition, not discussed in the Gemara itself but relevant to the practice, holds that the names of the ten sons must fill an entire column. Most *soferim* accomplish this by writing the letters of *aseret benei Haman* in a larger size. The Vilna Gaon, however, strongly objected to this practice, arguing that we have no *masorah* to write these names in a font larger than the surrounding text — the only attested scribal anomaly is the elongated *vav* of *Vaizasa*. A *megillah* written with eleven lines per column allows the ten names plus the word *va-aseret* to fill a column in standard-size script, thus satisfying the columnar requirement without departing from the accepted *masorah* of letter size.

## Achashverosh's Shift

### Tone Shift

The verse reads: *vayomer hamelech l'Esther ha-malka b'Shushan ha-bira hargu hayehudim ve'ibed chamesh meot ish...u-mah she'elatekh vetinatein lakh* — the king informed Esther of the number slain in Shushan and throughout the provinces, and then in the very same verse offered her whatever she wished. The shift in tone is dramatic and jarring: the verse begins in what appears to be an expression of alarm or even accusation, and ends with a generous open-ended offer. This is a textually strange *pasuk*.

### Angel's Slap

Rabbi Abbahu teaches: *melamed sheba malach v'staho al piv* — an angel came and struck Achashverosh on his mouth, changing the words he was about to say. Just as an angel had guided Esther's approach at a critical moment, so here an angel intervened mid-sentence to alter Achashverosh's speech, producing the abrupt shift in tone visible in the verse itself.

### Read From Scroll

The verse later states: *uvevo'ah lifnei hamelech amar im hasefer yashuv machshavto hara'ah asher chashav al hayehudim al rosho.* Rabbi Yochanan interprets *amar im hasefer* as: *yomar bepeh mah shekatuv basefer* — what is written in the scroll should be declared aloud. Rashi's reading (*haichi garsinan*) is that this refers to Mordechai's role: the text is tracing how the events of Purim were to be recited and recorded annually, with the instruction that what is written in the *sefer* — the *Megillah* — should be proclaimed orally. In this reading, *amar im hasefer* becomes a foundational reference to the annual reading of the *Megillah* itself.

## Scroll Writing

### Scribal Lines

The verse *divrei shalom ve'emet* prompts a *halachic* teaching. Rabbi Tanchum — and some say Rabbi Assi — teaches: *melamed shetzricha sirtut ka'amittah shel Torah* — the *Megillah* requires *sirtut*, the scored lines that a scribe makes on the parchment before writing, just as is required by *amittah shel Torah*, the truth of the *Torah*.

### Sirtut Debate

Rashi understands *amittah shel Torah* to mean a *Sefer Torah* itself, meaning the *Megillah* must have *sirtut* just as a *Sefer Torah* does. Tosafot disagrees, holding that technically a *Sefer Torah* does not require *sirtut*, and that *amittah shel Torah* here refers to a *mezuzah*. Either way, the teaching situates the *Megillah* alongside the most formally demanding categories of *sifrei kodesh*. This connects to a broader theme: just as the Gemara will shortly establish that the *Megillah* must be written in Hebrew — despite the *mishnah*'s more permissive stance toward other *sefarim* — so too here the *Megillah* is given weight comparable to, or even exceeding, other portions of the *Torah*. Tosafot reads the same stringency regarding *sirtut* in the same direction: the *Megillah* shares this demanding standard with the *mezuzah* rather than with the more lenient category of *Sefer Torah*.

## Acceptance of Purim

### Esther's Ordinance

The verse reads: *um'amar Esther kiyam divrei ha-Purim ha-eleh*. The Gemara immediately questions: *um'amar Esther in divrei ha-tzomot lo?* — and Esther's word established the Purim days, but what of the reference to *divrei ha-tzomot ve-za'akatam* earlier in the same passage?

### Fasts & Cries

The full verse runs: *lekayem et divrei ha-Purim ha-eleh bizmaneihem ka'asher kiyam aleihem Mordechai ha-Yehudi ve-Esther ha-malka ve-ka'asher kiyemu al nafsham ve-al zar'am divrei ha-tzomot u-za'akatam* — to establish these Purim days in their times, just as Mordechai and Esther had established them, and just as they had accepted upon themselves and their descendants the matter of the fasts and their crying out.

### Validation

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the apparent difficulty by reading *divrei ha-tzomot ve-za'akatam u-ma'amar Esther* as a continuous phrase rather than placing the break at *ve-za'akatam*. The meaning then becomes: it was through *divrei ha-tzomot ve-za'akatam u-ma'amar Esther* — through the fasting, the crying out, and Esther's proclamation together — that *kiyam divrei ha-Purim ha-eleh*, these days of Purim were validated and brought about. The Gemara is thus reading the verse as a historical reference to what produced the Purim miracle in the first place, not as an institution of a future fast day. (The later practice of *Taanit Esther* is post-Talmudic, and Rashi's reading does not understand the verse as establishing such a fast.)

## Mordechai's Status

### Mishneh Status

The *Megillah* concludes: *ki Mordechai ha-Yehudi mishneh la-melech Achashverosh ve-gadol la-Yehudim ve-ratzui le-rov echav* — second to the king and great among the Jews and accepted by most of his brothers. *Le-rov echav ve-lo le-chol echav* — most but not all. The famous *midrash*: *melamed she-pirshu mimenu miktzat Sanhedrin* — some members of the *Sanhedrin* distanced themselves from him, because he had departed from the *beit midrash*.

### Torah Priority

Rav Yosef draws the principle: *gadol talmud Torah yoter me-hatzalat nefashot* — *talmud Torah* is greater than saving lives. *De-me'ikara chashev leh le-Mordechai batar arba'ah* — originally, in Ezra, Mordechai is listed after four others, making him fifth on the list. *U-levasof batar chamishah* — later in the text, after the Purim story, he appears only after five others, falling to sixth place. He descended in standing precisely because his involvement in political affairs and saving lives drew him away from *Torah* study.

### Rank Change

The *pessukim* bear this out. Initially it is written: *asher ba'u im Zerubavel Yeshua Nechemiah Serayah Re'elayah Mordechai Bilshan* — Mordechai is fifth. Later: *ha-ba'im im Zerubavel Yeshua Nechemiah Azaryah Ra'amyah Nachamani Mordechai Bilshan* — Mordechai is sixth, having dropped one position. This is a remarkable statement in context, because the *Gemara* opened this tractate by saying that one suspends *talmud Torah* in favor of *mikra megillah* — thus seemingly elevating *mikra megillah* over *Torah* study — and yet here the Gemara is moving in the opposite direction, suggesting that Mordechai's very heroism in saving his people came at a cost to his standing in *Torah*.

## Torah Primacy

### Torah > Temple

Rava (and some say Rav Shmuel bar Marta) states: *gadol talmud Torah yoter mi-binyan Beit ha-Mikdash* — *talmud Torah* is greater than building the *Beit ha-Mikdash*.

### Ezra's Choice

The proof: *she-kol zman she-Baruch ben Neriah kayam lo hinicho Ezra ve'ala* — as long as Baruch ben Neriah was alive in Bavel, Ezra did not leave to go up to the Land of Israel. Ezra, the paradigmatic figure of *Torah* transmission — *Ezra Hasofer*, the proto-rabbinic figure — only came in the second wave of returnees. The Gemara's explanation: his *rebbe* was still alive in Bavel, and he was not yet free to leave. The construction of the *Beit ha-Mikdash* was underway, and yet Ezra remained in *yeshiva*.

### Halakha/Aggada

This creates a striking tension that the *Gemara* leaves unresolved. The *halacha* is clear: one suspends *talmud Torah* for *mikra megillah*, and equally one suspends *talmud Torah* for *binyan Beit ha-Mikdash*. And yet the closing *sugyot* of this *perek* — centered on the very themes of *mikra megillah* and *Beit ha-Mikdash* — insist that *talmud Torah* surpasses them all. The *aggadeta* functions here as a counter-voice to the *halacha*, shaping attitudes and maintaining *Torah* study's primacy in the hierarchy of values even where the formal legal ruling goes the other way. This kind of juxtaposition — where *aggadeta* is placed deliberately alongside a *halacha* that points in the opposite direction — is a recognizable pattern in the *Gemara*, similar to the tension in *Berachot* around *tefilla* and *talmud Torah*, or the famous tension around *ad de-lo yada* and the story of Rava and Rabbi Zeira.

### Torah > Parents

Rava bar bar Rav Yitzchak states: *gadol talmud Torah yoter mi-kibbud av va-em* — *talmud Torah* is greater than *kibbud av va-em*. The proof: *she-kol otan shanim she-haya Yaakov Avinu b'veit Ever lo ne'enash* — during all the years that Yaakov our father was in the *yeshiva* of Ever, he was not punished.

### Yaakov's Years

This teaching presupposes that Yaakov spent fourteen hidden years learning in the *yeshiva* of Ever before his journey to Lavan in Aram Naharayim — a tradition found in Rashi on the *Torah*. The claim is that those fourteen years, spent away from his parents in order to study, were not counted against him.

### Age Math

The Gemara establishes this through a careful reconstruction of Yaakov's age. The Torah records that Yishmael died at 137. Yishmael was 14 years older than Yitzchak, since Avraham was 86 at Yishmael's birth and 100 at Yitzchak's birth. Yitzchak was 60 when Yaakov and Esav were born, making Yishmael 74 at that point. Since Yishmael died at 137, he had 63 more years to live from the time of Yaakov's birth. A *baraita* teaches that Yaakov was 63 when he received his father's blessing and departed — and at that very time, Yishmael died.

The Gemara establishes this through the verse that describes Eisav going to marry Machalat, the daughter of Yishmael, sister of Nevayot. Since she is Yishmael's daughter, why specify that she is the sister of Nevayot? *Melamed shekiddshah Yishmael umet* — Yishmael had betrothed her to Eisav and then died, so that at the actual *nisuin* it was her brother Nevayot who gave her away. This establishes that Yishmael died at precisely the time these events occurred — when Yaakov was 63.

From age 63, fourteen more years until Yosef was born (*arba esreh ad demitayallid Yosef*), making Yaakov 77 at Yosef's birth. Yosef was 30 when he stood before Paroh, making Yaakov 107. Seven years of plenty and two years of famine add nine more years, bringing Yaakov to 116. But when Paroh asks Yaakov his age, Yaakov says he is 130. The discrepancy is 14 years — precisely the fourteen years of *Beit Ever*. *Shema mina arba esreh dehavah b'veit Ever lo chashiv lehu* — those fourteen years in the *yeshiva* of Ever were not counted in the tally, which is itself the proof that he was not punished for them.

### Parallel Years

The Gemara then connects this to Yosef's absence from Yaakov. A *baraita* teaches: *nimtza Yosef sheperash me'aviv esrim ushtayim shanah* — Yosef was separated from his father for twenty-two years. He was 17 when sold, and we have established through the calculations above that he was 39 when reunited with Yaakov (30 before Paroh, plus seven years of plenty and two of famine) — indeed twenty-two years. *Keshem shepeirash Yaakov Avinu me'aviv* — this parallels the twenty-two years Yaakov was not with his own father. But wait: the math for Yaakov yields 36 years away (14 for Rachel and Leah, 6 for the flocks, plus the 14 of *Beit Ever*, plus 2 additional years of travel). Subtracting the 14 of *Beit Ever* leaves 22 — the parallel is exact. *Ella arbisar d'havavei bei Ever lo chashiv lehu* — the fourteen of *Beit Ever* are not counted, and the remaining twenty-two correspond precisely to the twenty-two years Yosef was absent. The symmetry itself demonstrates that the fourteen years of *Torah* study were not reckoned as a period of negligence toward his father.

The remaining discrepancy — the 20 years in Lavan's house, not yet 22 — is resolved by adding the two years Yaakov spent traveling home: eighteen months at Succoth (*vayiven lo bayit ulemiknehu asah Succoth*, implying a summer, winter, and summer — eighteen months), and six months at *Beit El* where he brought offerings. Together these account for the additional two years, bringing the total to twenty-two.

One should note a nuance in how to understand this teaching. Yaakov was told by both parents to go to Lavan; his departure was at their direction. The question is therefore not simply whether he should have been home but whether the specific circumstances — the fourteen years in *Beit Ever*, the additional six years working for flocks, the two years of travel — represent a failure of *kibbud av va-em*. The Gemara's point is subtler: even when one has good reason to be away, even when the *halacha* permits or even mandates the departure, the absence from parents carries a real-world cost. The *halacha* regarding one who moves away for *Torah* study or livelihood or even *aliyah* may well permit it, but the *aggadata* here preserves the awareness that something of *kibbud av va-em* is nonetheless not being fulfilled in the fullest sense.

## Reading Rules

### Forward Only

The *mishnah* of the next *perek* opens: *hakorei et ha-Megillah lemafrea lo yatza* — one who reads the *Megillah* out of order, reading later passages before earlier ones, has not fulfilled the obligation.

### Language Rules

*Kara al peh* — if one read it from memory without looking at a scroll, *kara Targum* — if one read it in Aramaic translation, *bechol lashon* — or in any other language, *lo yatza* — one has not fulfilled the obligation.

### Language Exception

*Aval korin osa liloazos bilaaz* — however, one may read the *Megillah* to a non-Hebrew speaker in their own foreign language. And conversely: *vehaloez sheshama Ashuris yatza* — a non-Hebrew speaker who hears the *Megillah* read in Hebrew has fulfilled the obligation, even without understanding. The Gemara will explore what distinguishes these cases.

### Breaks Allowed

*Karaah seirugin* — if one read with pauses and interruptions — *umisnamnem* — or while dozing off — *yatza*, one has nonetheless fulfilled the obligation.

### Intent Required

*Haya koshva dorsha umagiha* — if one was in the process of writing the *Megillah*, or expounding on it (*darshan*-style), or proofreading it, and in doing so verbalized the words — *im kiven libo yatza* — if one directed one's mind toward the act of reading as a *mitzvah*, one has fulfilled the obligation. *Ve-im lav lo yatza* — if not, one has not. At minimum, one must be aware that one is performing an act of reading; a question remains whether one must additionally have conscious intent to fulfill the *mitzvah*.

### Material Rules

*Haysa kesuva besam uvesikra uvekumus uvekankantum* — if the *Megillah* was written in various types of dye or pigment other than *dyo*, *al hanyar* — on paper, which in antiquity was a cloth-based material — *ve-al hadiphtera* — or on processed leather that is not *klaf* — *lo yatza*, one has not fulfilled the obligation.

### Script & Ink

*Ad shetehei kesuva Ashuris al hasefer u-vidyo* — until it is written in the Hebrew script, on proper parchment (*klaf*), and with *dyo*, the recognized *halachic* ink. This requirement — Hebrew script, *klaf*, and *dyo* — connects back directly to the earlier discussion: the *Megillah* is treated with the same formality as the most demanding categories of *sifrei kodesh*.

## Backward Prohibition

### Verse Source

The Gemara asks: *manah hanei milei* — from where is the prohibition against reading the *Megillah* out of order derived? Rabba cites the verse: *keichsavim u-chezmanim* — as written and in their times.

### Time Logic

The analogy: just as time does not go backward — one does not observe the 15th celebration before the 14th — so too *af ksavam lemafrea lo* — what is written about them is not read backward.

### Action Not Reading

The Gemara immediately objects: *midi keria kesiv hacha? Asiya kesiv hacha* — is it reading that the verse refers to? The verse says *lihyot osim* — to perform these days *keichsavim u-chezmanim*. It is speaking about the act of observance, not about reading. The verse does not address *keria* at all.

### Memory & Action

The Gemara therefore redirects: *ela mehacha* — rather, derive it from the verse *vehayamim ha-eleh nizkarim venaasim* — these days are remembered and performed. *Iskash zchira la-asiya* — the verse links *zechira* (memory/reading) to *asiya* (observance). Just as *asiya lemafrea lo* — one does not observe Purim out of order — so too *zchira lemafrea lo* — one does not read it out of order.

### Other Mitzvot

A *baraita* teaches that the same principle applies to *Hallel*, *krias Shema*, and *tefilla* — all must be performed in their proper order. The Gemara then asks for the source in each case. Rabba cites *mimizrach shemesh ad mevo'o* — from sunrise to sunset — which describes a natural sequence and applies to the *psukim* of *Hallel*. Rav Yosef cites *zeh hayom asah Hashem* — a day unfolds in sequence. Rav Avya cites *yehi shem Hashem mevorach*, which Rashi reads as implying natural sequential order. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak and some say Rav Acha bar Yaakov cite *me'atah ve'ad olam* — from now to forever — again implying an irreversible sequence.

## Shema Language

### Language Dispute

A *baraita* states: *Krias Shema ke'chsavah* — *Krias Shema* must be recited as it is written, meaning in Hebrew. This is the position of Rabbi. The Rabbis (*Rabbanan*) disagree: *bechol lashon* — it may be recited in any language.

### Rabbi's Source

Rabbi's source is the verse *ve'hayu ve'hayu ha-devarim ha-eleh* — *ve'hayu* implies that the words must remain exactly as they are, in their original Hebrew form.

### Rabbanan Source

The Rabbis derive from the word *Shema* itself: *bechol lashon she'atah shome'a* — however you are able to hear and understand. The obligation is to hear and comprehend, which can be accomplished in any language.

### Hearing Dispute

Rabbi further derives from *Shema* that one must verbalize the words audibly enough to hear them oneself: *hashmi'a le'oznecha mah she'atah motzi mipicha* — make your ears hear what your mouth utters. The Rabbis, by contrast, hold with the view that *hakorei Shema velo hishmia le'ozno yatza* — even if one did not hear one's own words, the obligation is fulfilled.

### Ritual vs Meaning

There is a coherent conceptual link between Rabbi's two positions. Rabbi focuses on the ritual, objective dimensions of the act: the specific Hebrew text must be used, and the words must be verbalized audibly. The Rabbis, by contrast, orient toward meaning and comprehension: what matters is that one is communicating the content to oneself, whether in Hebrew or another language, and whether or not one can physically hear one's own voice. Rabbi demands more of the formal ritual act; the Rabbis are more attuned to the act of understanding and inner address.

### Resolution

The Gemara then asks: given that *Rabbanan* allow any language, why does the verse say *ve'hayu* at all? And given that Rabbi requires Hebrew, why does the verse say *Shema*? The Gemara resolves both questions. For Rabbanan: even if the default were any language, *ve'hayu* was still necessary — otherwise one might have concluded from *Shema* that Hebrew was required, following Rabbi's reasoning. So *kasav rachmana ve'hayu* to insist that any language suffices. For Rabbi: even if the default were Hebrew, *Shema* was still necessary — because without it one might have misread *ve'hayu* to require an additional condition beyond Hebrew. Each verse addresses a potential misreading, not the baseline assumption.

The Gemara also uses this discussion to raise a broader question: does Rabbi hold that the entire *Torah* was given to be recited in any language (*bechol lashon ne'emrah*), making the default permissive, with *ve'hayu* functioning as a specific restriction? Or do the Rabbis hold that the default for all *Torah* recitations is Hebrew (*bilshon hakodesh ne'emrah*), with *Shema* functioning as a specific permission? The Gemara works through both possibilities and concludes that neither position can be simply identified with one side of the broader question about default language for *Torah* recitations generally, since each verse serves a necessary corrective function regardless of what the underlying default might be.

## Tefilla Order

### Shimon Hapakuli

A *baraita* states: *Shimon Hapakuli hesdir shemoneh esrei berachot lifnei Rabban Gamliel al ha-seder be-Yavneh* — Shimon Hapakuli (apparently a tradesman, possibly a cotton merchant) organized the 18 *berachot* in their order before Rabban Gamliel at Yavneh. He did not author them; he arranged the existing *berachot* into a fixed order and fixed text, presenting this arrangement before Rabban Gamliel for ratification.

### Elder Tradition

Rabbi Yochanan teaches an alternative tradition: *me'ah ve-esrim zekenim uvahem kama nevi'im tiknu shmoneh esrei berachot al ha-seder* — 120 elders, including among them several prophets, instituted the 18 *berachot* in their order. The Gemara will work to reconcile these two traditions, but in either case the tradition points back to the prophetic and elder-led institution of communal prayer in the early Second Temple period.

### Opening Praises

The *baraita* proceeds to ground the three opening *berachot* — *avot*, *gevurot*, and *kedushot* — in Scripture. *Minayin she'omrim avot* — from where do we derive the blessing of *avot*? *Havu la-Hashem bnei eilim* — give to God, sons of the mighty, i.e., the forebears: Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov. *Minayin she'omrim gevurot* — from where *gevurot*? *Havu la-Hashem kavod va-oz* — give to God honor and strength. *Minayin she'omrim kedushot* — from where *kedushot*? *Havu la-Hashem kvod shemo hishtachavu la-Hashem behadrat kodesh* — give to God the honor of His name, bow before God in holy splendor.

### Avot's Role

The structure of these opening *berachot* addresses a fundamental challenge of prayer: how can a finite, corporeal human being presume to approach an infinite God? The *berachah* of *avot* provides the entry point — we are not coming on our own authority but as inheritors of a relationship already established by the *avot*, who have already, in some sense, legitimated our standing before God. Then *gevurot* and *kedushot* acknowledge the immense gap: God's power is beyond measure, and God's *kedushah* marks the absolute difference between Creator and creature. These three *berachot* together constitute both an acknowledgment of that gap and the inherited claim that allows one nonetheless to stand in prayer.
