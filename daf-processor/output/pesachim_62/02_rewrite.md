# Pesachim 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Machshevet Ochlim BiZrika

### Rabba vs. Rav Chisda

The daf opens in the middle of a debate between Rabba and Rav Chisda concerning *machshevet ochlim bizrika* — a form of invalid intent that is unique to the *korban pesach*. As discussed on the preceding daf, the Mishna identifies a *psul* specific to the *korban pesach*: if at the moment of *shechita* one has in mind only those who are unable to eat the *korban* — an *arel*, a *tamei* person, or someone otherwise physically incapable — the *korban* is invalidated. This *psul* derives from the verse *ish lefi ochlo tachossu al haseh*, and it is unique to the *korban pesach* on two grounds: first, the *korban pesach* is fundamentally about the eating; and second, the eating is made possible only through the act of *shechita*. Accordingly, the *psul* applies only to the *shechita*, and only when every person one has in mind is invalid. A mixed intent — some valid, some not — does not invalidate.

The specific debate between Rabba and Rav Chisda concerns the following case: at the moment of *shechita*, one intends not that the *shechita* is being performed for invalid persons, but that the subsequent *zerika* — the sprinkling of the blood — will be performed for their sake. The thought thus occurs at the moment of *shechita*, but it pertains to the act of *zerika*. Rav Chisda holds that this invalidates the *korban*; Rabba holds that it does not. An attempt to resolve this dispute from a *baraita* proved inconclusive, and the Gemara now turns to a different approach.

### Rav Ashi's Resolution

*Ela amar Rav Ashi* — Rather, says Rav Ashi: Rav Chisda and Rabba in fact disagree about the interpretation of a *pasuk*. It is striking that a later *amora* frames what appears to be a dispute between *amoraim* as rooted in a disagreement over a scriptural verse — a mode of argumentation more typical of *tannaim*. The verse in question is *venirtza lo lechapper alav*, from the opening of Vayikra, which concerns the *olah* and teaches that the *korban* achieves *ritzui* — divine acceptance — for the owner through the *zerikat hadam*.

### Shelo Limnuiav Concept

According to the way Rashi explains Rav Ashi's resolution, the debate between Rabba and Rav Chisda is not actually about *shelo le'ochlav* — the *psul* unique to the *korban pesach* — but rather about *shelo limnuiav*: having in mind a person who is not the owner of the *korban*. This transforms the question from a *korban pesach*-specific problem into a general one that applies to all *korbanot*. The verse *venirtza lo lechapper alav* teaches that *zerikat hadam* must be performed for the sake of the owner — *alav velo chaveiro* — and not for a non-owner. This is a universal requirement at the moment of *zerika*.

The shift is subtle but important. The phrase *shelo limnuiav* is terminology native to the *korban pesach*, since *minuy* — being designated as a participant in a *chaburah* — is a concept specific to the *pesach*. However, the underlying reality of *shelo limnuiav* is equivalent to *shelo lishmo be'alim*, the general prohibition on performing *zerika* with the intent to benefit the wrong owner. Rav Ashi's claim is that the debate between Rabba and Rav Chisda is not about the unique eating-based *psul* of the *korban pesach*, but about this general requirement that *zerika* be performed for the sake of the legitimate owners. The *korban pesach* provides an interesting context for exploring what happens when the alternative owner one has in mind is completely irrelevant to the *korban* — as in the case of an *arel*.

### Rabba's View on Arelim

The Gemara now presents the positions in their reframed form. *Alav velo chaveiro* teaches that *zerika* must be for the owner and not for another person. Rabba holds that the invalidity of intending a different person — *chaveiro dumya didei* — applies only to someone who is analogous to the owner, meaning someone for whom the *korban* is in principle relevant: *mah hu de-bar kapparah af chaveiro de-bar kapparah*. Just as the owner is someone for whom atonement through this *korban* is possible, the problematic "other person" must also be someone for whom this *korban* is relevant. 

A useful illustration: if one were performing the *zerika* for a *chatat* of a *yoledet* and had in mind a man — someone for whom this particular *korban* has no applicability — that would not invalidate, since the person is entirely outside the *parsha* of this *korban*. Applying this to the case of the *arel*: the *arel* is not an owner who cannot eat but a non-owner who cannot eat. Since he is *de-lav bar kapparah hu* — not in the *parsha* of the *korban pesach* — having him in mind does not create an invalidity. According to Rabba, only an intent for someone who is genuinely situated within the framework of this *korban* can generate the *psul* of *shelo lishmo be'alim*.

### Rav Chisda's View on Are…

Rav Chisda disagrees. He holds that even this *arel* — *hai arel nami* — is considered to be within the *parsha* of the *korban pesach*. Since he is a Jew and is in principle obligated in *korban pesach*, he is *bar kapparah hu*. The reason he currently cannot bring the *korban* is incidental: *ho'il de-i ba'ei matkan nafshei* — since, if he wished, he could rectify his situation by undergoing *brit milah*. He is not categorically excluded from the *parsha* of the *korban*; there is merely a circumstantial barrier. Therefore, having the *arel* in mind when performing the *zerika* does constitute an intent for an alternate "owner" who is within the relevant framework, and this invalidates.

One might ask: what if the *arel* in question is a hemophiliac, for whom *brit milah* would involve *sakanat nefashot* and is thus practically impossible? The principle of *ho'il* as Rav Chisda employs it need not be understood in purely practical terms. The point may be that the problem is incidental rather than categorical — that *brit milah* as a concept applies to this person, and the *korban pesach* applies to him, with circumstances having conspired to make it temporarily or even permanently impossible. Even a hemophiliac, on this reading, would not be categorically excluded from the *parsha* in the sense relevant to this *psul*.

### Hoil Principle Examined

The Gemara now asks: does Rav Chisda consistently apply the principle of *ho'il*? The Gemara raises a case discussed earlier: if someone bakes or cooks on *Yom Tov* for a weekday purpose, does he receive lashes? Rav Chisda said *loka* — he does receive lashes — while Rava said *eino loka*, because we say *ho'il*: since guests might drop by, this food would theoretically be fit for consumption on *Yom Tov* itself. Rav Chisda rejects this application of *ho'il*: since there was no immediate need, no actual eaters present, the license to cook does not apply.

One might immediately wonder whether these two uses of *ho'il* are genuinely comparable. In the case of the *arel*, the question is whether someone is categorically in or out of the *parsha* of the *korban*, given that his exclusion is circumstantial. In the *Yom Tov* case, the question is whether the theoretical possibility of guests arriving is sufficient to license an otherwise prohibited act of *melacha*. These seem like fundamentally different questions that happen to share the same terminology. Each belongs to its own *halachic* framework, and there seems to be no obvious reason why one's position on one should determine one's position on the other.

Nevertheless, the Gemara treats this as a genuine tension. It notes that Rava's two positions are not in conflict, because in the case of the *arel* he holds that *ho'il* does not apply — since becoming circumcised requires a significant act (*mechusar ma'aseh*) — while in the *Yom Tov* case he does allow the *ho'il* argument, since no such barrier exists: guests need only knock on the door. Thus Rava, fundamentally a proponent of *ho'il* where the barrier is low, consistently rejects it where the barrier is substantial. But the Gemara concludes that Rav Chisda's positions are in tension with one another — *de-Rav Chisda ad de-Rav Chisda kashya* — and provides an answer: *ki leis lei le-Rav Chisda ho'il le-kula*, Rav Chisda rejects *ho'il* when it would produce a leniency, but *le-chumra* — when *ho'il* would produce a stringency — *is lei*, he does hold it. Since the *arel* being theoretically in the *parsha* is a stringency, Rav Chisda accepts the *ho'il*; since guests theoretically arriving would be a leniency permitting cooking, he rejects it.

### Tosafot on Hoil

Tosafot takes issue with this resolution. The distinction between *le-kula* and *le-chumra* seems to be a characteristically rabbinic consideration — the kind of asymmetric treatment that applies at the *de-rabbanan* level. If *ho'il* is a *de-oraita* principle, one should either hold it or not hold it, regardless of whether the consequence is a leniency or a stringency. Tosafot therefore argues that Rav Chisda in principle does *not* hold of *ho'il*, which is precisely why he rules *loka* in the *Yom Tov* case. In the case of the *arel*, Rav Chisda would likewise, strictly speaking, agree that the *arel* is outside the *parsha* and the *korban* is not invalidated. However, rabbinically, he treats the *arel* as being within the *parsha* to produce the stringent result of invalidating the *korban*.

This interpretation raises an immediate question: if the *korban pesach* is only rabbinically invalidated, how has the owner fulfilled his biblical obligation? He presumably will not bring a *pesach sheni*, since biblically the first *korban* was valid. Tosafot answers that the invalidity is imposed at the moment of *shechita*, when the intent regarding *zerika* was already formed. The rabbis instruct that the *zerikat hadam* not be performed in such a case. Since the *zerika* is never executed, the owner never becomes *yotzei* in any event, and the question of what he is to do for his biblical obligation is resolved: he was simply never fully *yotzei* because the *zerika* did not occur.

It is worth noting, however, that one need not necessarily frame the *kula/chumra* distinction as a rabbinic matter. The underlying logic may be that *ho'il* situates something in a genuinely gray category — neither fully in nor fully out. How one responds to gray categories may legitimately differ depending on whether one is determining whether a license exists or whether a problem exists. A gray category may not be sufficient to license a prohibited act, yet may be entirely sufficient to generate a problem. The asymmetry, on this reading, reflects something real about the structure of *halachic* analysis rather than a mere rabbinic convention.

## Arelim-Tumah Analogy

### Baraita Comparison

The Gemara now returns to the *baraita* that had been cited in the preceding discussion, which attempted to establish that partial *arelim* — some members of the *chaburah* being *arelim* and others not — does not invalidate the *korban*, by analogy to the case of *tumah*. The *baraita* reasoned: just as partial *tumah* does not invalidate — in the same way that *kol tumah* is needed to invalidate — so too, partial *orlah* should not invalidate.

Mar Zutra son of Rav Mari posed a question to Ravina: *ho'il ve'orla poseles ve'tuma poseles* — both *orlah* and *tumah* invalidate the *korban*. *Mah tumah lo asah bah miktzat tumah k'chol tumah* — just as with *tumah*, partial *tumah* is not equivalent to total *tumah*, so too *af orlah lo asah miktzat orlah k'chol orlah* — by *orlah* as well, only a situation in which all those one has in mind are *arelim* would produce a *psul*.

### Analogy Problem

The Gemara immediately challenges this. *Hai tumah heichi dami?* — What is this case of *tumah* that the *baraita* takes for granted? If the analogy refers to *tumat gavra* — some of the people in the *chaburah* being *tamei* and others *tahor* — then the *halacha* in both cases is obviously identical, and there is no reason the author of the *baraita* would have known the rule about *tumah* while remaining uncertain about *orlah*, or vice versa. If four or five people are *tamei* and four or five are *tahor*, the *tamei'im* do not invalidate the *tehorim*; but by the same token, a mix of *arelim* and *mulim* is already established to be *kasher*. The cases are perfectly parallel. The analogy teaches nothing. *U'mai shena tumah depeshita lei u'mai shena orlah demistapka lei* — why would the *halacha* of *tumah* be obvious to the author while the *halacha* of *orlah* remained in doubt?

### Tumat Basar Solution

*Ela tumah betumat basar* — The Gemara resolves this by reframing the analogy. The *baraita* is not comparing *arelim* to *tamei* people, but to *tamei meat* — the meat of the *korban* itself becoming *tamei*. What does it mean, in that context, that partial *tumah* does not equal total *tumah*? The Gemara explains: *ve'illu itma chad me'eivarim, hai de'itma sarfinan lei, ve'idach achlinan lei* — if one limb of the *korban* becomes *tamei* after *shechita*, that limb is burned, and the remaining portions are eaten. The *tumah* of a portion does not contaminate the whole. By analogy, the *arel* status of some members of the *chaburah* does not affect the rest. The analogy is admittedly loose — meat becoming partially *tamei* is not the same category as people being partially *arelim* — but that is the structure the *baraita* is using.

The Gemara then presses further. If we say the *baraita* is discussing *tumat basar*, how does the end of the *baraita* work? The *baraita* argues that *tumah* and *orlah* are preferable comparands because both are phenomena that do not apply universally to all *korbanot* — *davar she'eino noheg bechol sevachim* — whereas *pigul* (wrong time intent) does apply universally and should therefore not be the model. But if we are talking about *tumat basar*, that is a *halacha* that applies to all *korbanot*. It is not unique to *korban pesach*. Only *tumat gavra* — people being *tamei* — is uniquely consequential by *korban pesach*, since *arelim* and *tamei* people can send their *korbanot* through agents for other types of *korbanot*, while they cannot participate in the *korban pesach*.

The Gemara's answer: *reisha betumat basar ve'seifa betumat gavrei* — the beginning of the *baraita*, where it makes the specific partial-versus-total analogy, is speaking of *tumat basar*; the end, where it argues that *tumah* and *orlah* are uniquely *korban pesach* phenomena, is speaking of *tumat gavra*. The two halves of the *baraita* use *tumah* in different senses. The response: *shem tumah kachada* — they are all subsumed under the general category of *tumah*, and the *baraita* is entitled to move between these related senses.

### Pesach Meat Tamei Rule

This discussion draws out a striking and distinctive *halacha* of the *korban pesach*. By other *korbanot*, one can perform *zerikat hadam* as long as either the meat or the *chelev* remains available — since the *zerika* functions as a *matir*, enabling either the eating of the meat or the burning of the *eimurim* on the altar. There is a tannaitic opinion that if neither the meat nor the *chelev* is available, *zerikat hadam* cannot be performed, since there is nothing left for it to be *matir*; but if either the meat or the fat remains, *zerika* is valid. By the *korban pesach*, however, this rule does not apply symmetrically. The Gemara states: *nitma chelev uvasar kayam, zorek et hadam* — if the fat is *tamei* and the meat remains, one performs *zerikat hadam*. But *nitma basar vechelev kayam, eino zorek et hadam* — if the meat is *tamei* and only the fat remains, one does not perform *zerikat hadam*. Why? Because the *korban pesach* is entirely about eating. If *zerikat hadam* cannot be *matir achila* — because the meat is *tamei* and cannot be eaten — there is no purpose to performing the *zerika* at all.

This is a rule unique to the *korban pesach*. Other *korbanot* permit *zerika* as long as either the meat or the *chelev* is present. By the *korban pesach*, *zerika* is only meaningful if the meat itself can be eaten. *Tumat basar* thus has a uniquely consequential role by *korban pesach* that it does not have by other *korbanot* — which, as the Gemara notes, provides another sense in which *tumah* is *eino noheg bechol sevachim* as far as the *korban pesach* is concerned.

### Pesach Be'tumah Exception

The Gemara then introduces a second answer — *ve'iba'it eima kula betumat basar* — proposing that the *baraita* consistently refers to *tumat basar* throughout, including where it says *tumah* is sometimes permitted. This requires explaining in what sense *tumat basar* is ever permitted, since we typically allow *tamei* people to bring *korbanot*, not *tamei* meat to be eaten.

The answer: by a *korban pesach* brought *be'tumah*, the *tamei* meat is in fact permitted to be eaten. By other *korbanot* brought *be'tumah* — that is, when the entire community is *tamei* — the *korban* is brought, but *tamei* people bringing a *korban* do not eat it; only the *zerika* is permitted, not the *achila*. But the *korban pesach* is fundamentally about eating, and therefore *pesach haba betumah ne'echal betumah* — a *pesach* brought in a state of *tumah* is eaten even in that state of *tumah*: *shelo ba mitchilato ela l'achilah* — from its very inception it comes only for the purpose of eating. Thus, even *tumat basar* is overridden by a *korban pesach* brought *be'tumah*, and in that sense *tumat basar* has a *korban pesach*-specific dimension that can be said to be unique to *pesach*.

The Gemara thus uncovers two powerful and distinctive principles of the *korban pesach*, both flowing from the fundamental axiom that the *korban pesach* is entirely oriented toward eating. First: if the meat is *tamei*, there is no *zerikat hadam*, unlike all other *korbanot*. Second: when the *pesach* is brought *be'tumah*, not only is the *zerika* permitted, as would be the case with other *korbanot* brought *be'tumah*, but the *tamei* meat is itself permitted to be eaten.

## Pesach Aged Out Query

### Aged Animal Case

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua raises a challenge based on a *baraita*. The case involves a *pesach she'avar shenato* — a *pesach* animal that has exceeded its permitted age, now more than a year old, and thus cannot serve as a *korban pesach*. There are two scenarios in which a *pesach* effectively becomes a *shelamim*: when it is not the fourteenth of Nisan — in which case the status must be adjusted if it is too early, or passes automatically if it is too late — and when, even on the fourteenth of Nisan, the animal has aged out and can no longer function as a *korban pesach*, automatically taking on the status of a *shelamim*. Such an animal on the fourteenth is effectively a *shelamim* — though with a *pesach* substrate — and the question concerns what happens when it is *shechted* with the intent appropriate to a *korban pesach*.

The *baraita* presents two cases. The first: *haPesach she'avar shenato shechato bizmano lishmo* — a *pesach* that has aged out, *shechted* on the fourteenth *lishmo*, for the sake of a *korban pesach*. The second: *vechen hashochet acherim l'shem Pesach bizmano* — a straightforward *shelamim* that is *shechted* on the fourteenth with the intent of making it a *korban pesach*. In the ordinary case of *shelo lishmo* by a *shelamim* — for instance, *shechting* a *shelamim* with the intent of an *olah* — the first *mishna* of Zevachim teaches that *kol hazevachim shenizbechu shelo lishman kesherin ela shelo alu l'baalim l'shem chova*: all *korbanot shelo lishman* are valid, except they do not count for the owner. Only a *pesach* and a *chatat* are categorically invalidated by *shelo lishmo*. A *shelamim* done *lishmo* of another *korban* should thus be valid. But the question here is whether the reverse dynamic — *shechting* a *shelamim l'shem pesach* on the fourteenth — creates an invalidity by mirror image, since that very day a *pesach shechted l'shem shelamim* would be invalidated.

### Rabbi Eliezer Debate

*Rabbi Eliezer posel* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that this *korban* is invalidated. *Ve'Rabbi Yehoshua machshir* — Rabbi Yehoshua holds it is valid.

### Hoil and Timing

The Gemara observes that the dispute arises specifically because the *shechita* occurs *bizmano* — on the fourteenth. *Ha shelo bizmano kasher* — had this occurred on some other day, such as on Purim, even Rabbi Eliezer would agree it is *kasher*, because it is a straightforward case of *shelo lishmo* by a *shelamim*, which is universally valid. Why, then, is it *kasher shelo bizmano*? The Gemara asks: *neima ho'il u'bizmano posel shelo bizmano nami posel* — let us apply *ho'il*: since *bizmano* it would be invalidating, even *shelo bizmano* should be invalidating. This invokes the same *ho'il* reasoning that was used in the discussion of the *arel* — the theoretical possibility of a different outcome is used to argue for a stringency in the present case. The parallel seems strained: in the *arel* case, *ho'il* was used to argue that the person could theoretically enter the *parsha* of the *korban*; here, *ho'il* is used to argue that since *shechting lishmo de-pesach* on the fourteenth would invalidate, the same intent on a different day should equally invalidate. The settings are quite different, and the comparison of *ho'il* arguments across such disparate categories is itself striking.

### Rav Papa Resolution

*Amar Rav Papa: shani hatam* — Rav Papa answers that the case of *shelo bizmano* is different, because the *pasuk* explicitly limits the relevant context to the fourteenth. *V'amartem zevach pesach hu* — "And you shall say: it is the *zevach pesach*." From *hu b'davka v'lo hu l'shem acherim*, we learn that a *pesach shechted lishmo* of another *korban* on the fourteenth is invalid — this is the source for the *psul* of a *pesach shelo lishmo* established in the first *mishna*. But the same verse also implies *v'lo acherim lishmo*: the reverse *shelo lishmo* — *shechting* another *korban l'shem pesach* — likewise invalidates on the fourteenth, which is the basis of Rabbi Eliezer's position.

Crucially, this relationship is reciprocal and confined to the fourteenth. *Bizmano she'hu pasul l'shem acherim, acherim psulin lishmo* — on the fourteenth, when a *pesach* becomes invalid if done for the sake of another *korban*, other *korbanot* also become invalid when done for the sake of the *pesach*. *Shelo bizmano she'hu kasher l'shem acherim, acherim ksherim lishmo* — on any other day, when a *pesach* done for the sake of another *korban* is *kasher* (becoming a *shelamim*), other *korbanot* done *l'shem pesach* are equally *kasher*. The *halacha* is thus anchored entirely in the fourteenth and flows directly from the *pasuk*. There is no occasion for *ho'il* to apply, because the Torah itself defines the temporal frame within which the *psul* operates and explicitly excludes other days.

## Sefer Yuchsin Anecdote

### Learning Request

Rav Simlai came before Rabbi Yochanan. He said to him: *nitni li mar sefer yuchsin* — let the master teach me the *sefer yuchsin*. Rashi explains that *sefer yuchsin* may refer to one of two things: either a record of family lineages and genealogical statuses — closely related to the concerns of the fourth chapter of Kiddushin, dealing with who is *meyuchas* and who may have a question of *mamzerut* — or, alternatively, a form of *midrashei tannaim* on the book of Divrei Hayamim, which is itself structured almost entirely around genealogical lists.

### Refusal and Brurya

Rabbi Yochanan responded: *me'heichan at?* — Where do you come from? Rav Simlai replied that he was from Lod, and that he now resided in Neharde'a. Rabbi Yochanan said: *ein donin* (or according to some textual variants, *ein shonin*) *lo l'Lodim v'lo l'Neharde'im* — one does not teach the *sefer yuchsin* to people from Lod or to people from Neharde'a, and *kol shekein d'at mi-Lod u-movtach b'Neharde'a* — all the more so to someone who comes from Lod and resides in Neharde'a. Rashi suggests that either this was an excuse, and Rabbi Yochanan simply did not wish to teach this material to Rav Simlai, or that there were actual genealogical concerns associated with those communities, and this material should not be exposed in those circles — consistent with the broader sensitivity around *mamzerut* and the practice of not publicizing such information. Indeed, the concern with *sefer yuchsin* as a guarded text is directly analogous to certain contemporary practices in which genetic information relevant to communal *shidduchim* is kept confidential and accessible only in coded form, so as not to stigmatize individuals.

Having been rebuffed, Rav Simlai nonetheless continued to press. He was eventually persuaded, and Rabbi Yochanan agreed — but with a caveat. Rav Simlai said he wished to learn the material in three months. At this, Rabbi Yochanan took a clod of earth and threw it at him. He said: *u'mah Brurya, deveiteih d'Rabbi Meir, barteih d'Rabbi Chananya ben Teradyon, de-tanya tlas mei shmaiteta b'yoma mi-gimal mei ravasa* — consider Brurya, wife of Rabbi Meir and daughter of Rabbi Chananya ben Teradyon, who was able in one day to learn 300 *shmaatot* from 300 different sages, and even so *lo yaksa yedei chovasa bi-tlas shnin* — she was not able to master this material in three years. *Ve'at amarta bi-tlasa yarchei* — and you think to do it in three months?

The invocation of Brurya in this context is noteworthy on multiple levels. A discussion that began as a question about genealogies and *yichus* now uses Brurya as its exemplar of prodigious learning — and in the very same breath, Rabbi Yochanan establishes her identity through her *yichus*: the wife of Rabbi Meir, the daughter of Rabbi Chananya ben Teradyon. There may also be an implicit contrast with Rav Simlai: Brurya had impeccable *yichus*, and even she could not master this material in three years. Rav Simlai comes from Lod and lives in Neharde'a, and imagines he can do it in three months.

### Lishmо vs. Le'ochlav

With the discussion of *sefer yuchsin* effectively closed, Rav Simlai attempted to salvage something from the encounter. As they were getting up to leave, he addressed Rabbi Yochanan with a *lomdus* question: *mah bein lishmo v'shelo lishmo, u-le'ochlav v'shelo le'ochlav* — what is the difference between the invalidity of *shelo lishmo* and the invalidity of *shelo le'ochlav*? The question compares the two *mishnayot* we have been examining. By *shelo lishmo*, even a partial invalid intent — *shechting* with some wrong-name intent — suffices to invalidate the *korban*. By *shelo le'ochlav*, a mixed intent — some valid eaters, some invalid — does not invalidate. Why is *shelo lishmo* a more potent *psul* than *shelo le'ochlav*? Rabbi Yochanan, apparently satisfied that this was a genuine talmid chacham, replied: *ho v'tzurba m'rabanan at* — I see that you are at least somewhat of a *talmid chacham*. And he proceeded to answer.

### Four Distinctions

Rabbi Yochanan articulates several reasons why *shelo lishmo* is a stronger and more consequential *psul* than *shelo le'ochlav*.

First: *lishmo v'shelo lishmo psulo b'gufo* — the invalidity of *shelo lishmo* is intrinsic to the identity of the *korban* itself. *Lo'ochlav v'shelo lo'ochlav ein psulo b'gufo* — the invalidity of *shelo le'ochlav* is more external; it concerns the owners, the people associated with the *korban*, not the *korban* in its own identity.

Second: *lishmo v'shelo lishmo i efshar levareir issurah* — by *shelo lishmo*, one cannot distinguish or isolate the invalid element. If the intent at *shechita* was both *lishmo* and *shelo lishmo*, which part of the *korban* carries the wrong intent? The invalidity is pervasive and undifferentiated throughout the act. *Lo'ochlav v'shelo lo'ochlav efshar levareir issurah* — by *shelo le'ochlav*, the invalidity can be distinguished. The specific individuals who are valid eaters and those who are invalid are identifiable, and at the time of eating it will be clear who is eating properly and who is not. The *psul* is bounded and specific, not pervasive.

Third: *lishmo v'shelo lishmo yeshno b'arba avodot* — the *psul* of *shelo lishmo* applies across all four of the *avodot*: *shechita*, *kabbalat hadam*, *holachat hadam*, and *zerika*. *Lo'ochlav v'shelo lo'ochlav eino b'arba avodot* — the *psul* of *shelo le'ochlav* applies only at *shechita*.

Fourth: *lishmo v'shelo lishmo yeshno b'tzibur k'v'yachid* — the *psul* of *shelo lishmo* applies equally to a *korban tzibur* and to a *korban yachid*. *Lo'ochlav v'shelo lo'ochlav eino b'tzibur k'v'yachid* — the *psul* of *shelo le'ochlav* does not apply to a *korban tzibur*. This is because *shelo le'ochlav* is a *psul* unique to the *korban pesach*, and there is no communal *korban pesach* with a *chaburah* structure in the same sense as a *korban yachid*.

### Rav Ashi Unifies

*Rav Ashi amar: psulo b'gufo v'i efshar levareir issuro chada milta hi* — Rav Ashi says that the first two distinctions — that the *psul* is intrinsic to the *korban*'s identity, and that the invalid element cannot be differentiated or isolated — are in fact one and the same point. *D'mai ta'am amar psulo b'gufo? Mishum d'i efshar levareir issuro* — why do we say the *psul* is intrinsic to the *gufo* of the *korban*? Because it cannot be distinguished or extracted. The non-differentiability is the reason the *psul* is intrinsic. It would have been possible to argue that these are distinct ideas — that *psulo b'gufo* refers to the *korban*'s identity being affected by the wrong intent, while *efshar levareir* refers to the practical question of whether the invalid element can be isolated — but Rav Ashi collapses these into a single conceptual point.

### Sefer Yuchsin Hidden

The Gemara closes with a statement of Rami bar Rav Yehuda citing Rav: *miyom shenignaz Sefer Yuchsin, tashash kochan shel chachamim, v'cheiha me'or eineihem* — from the day the *Sefer Yuchsin* was hidden, the strength of the *chachamim* was sapped and the light of their eyes dimmed.

If *sefer yuchsin* refers to a genealogical register of family statuses, the word *nignaz* — hidden — would be literal: the material was deliberately kept from public knowledge, consistent with the general principle of not publicizing matters of *mamzerut*. If it refers to the *midrashei tannaim* on Divrei Hayamim, then Rashi understands *nignaz* as lost. The magnitude of what was lost in either case is illustrated by the closing remark of Mar Zutra: *bein Etzla l'Atzel* — between one mention of the name "Atzel" and the next, spanning an entire chapter of Divrei Hayamim, *tanu arba mei'a gamlei d'drasha* — four hundred camel-loads of *drashot* could be expounded. That this material could be derived from what is perhaps the most apparently arid and genealogical section of the entire Tanach — a chapter consisting almost entirely of "the son of so-and-so was so-and-so" — speaks to the extraordinary creative and interpretive richness of the tradition that was lost. And with that sobering reflection, the daf concludes.
