# Bekhorot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abaye: Animal Model

### Mishna Setup

The new *mishna* that opens our daf picks up immediately after the discussion of partnership with a non-Jew, whose important practical conclusion was that since even partial ownership of an ear exempts an animal from *bechor* status, it has become a *mitzvah* to sell the ear of the mother animal to a non-Jew—thereby avoiding all the complications of *kedushah* and *bechor*—and this is indeed the common practice today. The new *mishna* now turns to a different exemption: *kohanim u'levi'im peturin mi'kal vachomer*—Kohanim and Levi'im are exempt from redeeming the firstborn donkey, by means of a *kal vachomer*. The *mishna* states: *im piteru et shel Yisrael ba'midbar, din hu she'yifteru et shel atzman*—if they exempted those of the Israelites in the wilderness, it certainly follows that they should exempt their own.

### Kal Vachomer

Before we can accept this reasoning, however, a fundamental question must be confronted. The Torah states *behemot ha'Levi'im tachat behemotam*—the animals of the Levi'im in exchange for their animals. What exactly was the mechanism of exemption? Perhaps a Levi who happened to possess a sheep would thereby be exempt from the *peter chamor* obligation, since the sheep would stand in place of the firstborn donkey. But that is a very different claim from saying that by virtue of being a *kohen* or a *levi*, one's firstborn donkey is categorically exempt. The *gemara* therefore presses the point: is the exemption a function of personal status, or does it require the presence of a substitute animal?

### Abaye's Reading

The *gemara* opens with a sharp observation: *inu patrei*—did the Kohanim and Levi'im actually exempt the animals of the Israelites? No. *Adam, adam patar*—it was person opposite person. The *levi* as a person stood opposite the *bechor* of the Israelite. *Behemah, behemah patrah*—and the animals of the Levi'im stood opposite the firstborn animals of the Israelites. The verse makes this explicit: *kach et ha'Levi'im tachat bechor bi'vnei Yisrael ve'et behemot ha'Levi'im tachat behemotam*—take the Levi'im in place of the firstborn of the Israelites, and the animals of the Levi'im in place of their animals. If so, what entitles us to exempt the *levi*'s own *peter chamor* simply by virtue of his being a *levi*?

*Amar Abaye*—Abaye proposes a reading of the *mishna* that addresses this challenge directly. The *mishna*, he says, must be understood as: *kohanim u'levi'im peturin behemotam mi'kal vachomer*—it is their animals that are exempt, by means of a *kal vachomer*. The *kal vachomer* is: if in the wilderness the animals of the Levi'im exempted the firstborn animals of the Israelites, certainly they should exempt their own firstborn donkeys. In other words, Abaye insists that the exemption is mediated through an animal substitute. The Levi who possesses a sheep thereby exempts his *peter chamor*, on the model of what occurred in the *midbar*. The personal status of the *levi* does not, by itself, discharge the obligation.

### Raba Objects

Raba responds to Abaye with two objections. The first is textual: *ve'ha piteru inu katani*—the *mishna* uses the language "they exempt," referring to the *kohanim* and *levi'im* themselves, not to their animals. Abaye's reading requires inserting "their animals" into a text that does not say it. More importantly, there is an immediate practical difference: if the *kohen* has no animal other than the firstborn donkey itself, is he obligated or not? Abaye's model requires a substitute animal; Raba maintains the exemption is personal.

### Raba's Kosher Animal Que…

Raba's second objection cuts deeper. *Ve'od, im itta*—if it is truly the case that we derive the exemption of donkeys directly from the narrative model of the *midbar*, where animals of the Levi'im stood in place of animals of the Israelites, then why are Kohanim and Levi'im not also exempt from *bechor behemah tehorah*—the firstborn of kosher animals? This requires brief context: every listener knows that a *kohen* or *levi* is exempt from *pidyon haben*, but fewer may have paused to ask whether they are obligated in *peter chamor* or *bechor behemah*. Our *mishna* teaches they are exempt from *peter chamor*—but they are in fact obligated in *bechor behemah tehorah*. If a Kohen's sheep gives birth to a firstborn, he must still bring it as a *korban*; the exemption does not extend there. Yet Abaye's model, which reads the *midbar* exchange directly as a template for all animal firstborns, should logically extend the exemption to sheep and goats as well—since in the wilderness, the animals of the Levi'im stood opposite the firstborn animals of the Israelites, which certainly included kosher animals. The *mishna* itself, Raba notes, is explicit: *lo niftaru mi'bechor behemah tehorah ella mi'pidyon haben u'peter chamor*—they are only exempt from *pidyon haben* and *peter chamor*, not from the firstborn of a kosher animal. How does Abaye account for this?

## Raba: Status Model

### Raba's Reading

*Ella amar Rava*—rather, Raba proposes an entirely different reading of the *mishna*. The correct interpretation is: *kohanim u'levi'im pataru et shelahem atzmam mi'kal vachomer*—the Kohanim and Levi'im exempt *themselves*—that is, their firstborn donkeys are exempt by virtue of their own personal status—and the *kal vachomer* is built on a different foundation entirely.

### Levi Status & Substituti…

Raba's *kal vachomer* proceeds as follows: *im hifkia kedushatan shel Levi'im kedushat Yisrael ba'midbar*—if the sanctity of the Levi'im was able to substitute for the sanctity of the firstborn Israelites in the wilderness, then certainly it should uproot and replace their own *bechor* status. The model is now the person, not the animal. In the wilderness, the *levi* as a person, with his *kedushat Levi*, stood opposite and replaced the *kedushat ha'bechor* of the Israelite firstborn. We are not learning from what the animals did; we are learning from what the people did.

### Firstborn Levi'im

This leads to an important observation, implicit in the *gemara* but made explicit by Rashi in his commentary on the Torah. The Torah records that there were approximately 22,000 Levi'im counted against 22,273 firstborn Israelites, leaving 273 unredeemed who required five *sela'im* each. But when one adds up the Levi'im more carefully, the number exceeds 22,000—it comes to something like 22,300, meaning there were enough Levi'im that a genuine one-to-one substitution would have left no remainder at all. Rashi explains that those Levi'im who were themselves firstborns could not substitute for the firstborn of Israel, because their *kedushat Levi* was already serving as a substitute for their own *kedushat ha'bechor*. One *levi* who is himself a *bechor* cannot redeem another's *bechor*; his personal substitution is already "used" on himself.

This is precisely the conceptual engine behind why a *levi* does not require *pidyon ha'bechor* today. It is not merely a historical dispensation but a continuing structural reality: the *levi*'s very status as a *levi* substitutes for and replaces his status as a *bechor*. The *avodah* that was once carried out by the *bechorim* has been transferred to the *levi'im*, and so the *levi* who is a firstborn is, as it were, in a state of self-substitution.

### Gezerah Shavah

Having established that the personal status of the *levi* exempts him from *pidyon bechor* of humans, Raba then asks: *behemah teme'ah minalan*—how do we know the exemption extends to firstborn donkeys? The *gemara* answers by invoking a verse: *ach padoh tifdeh et bechor ha'adam ve'et bechor ha'behemah ha'teme'ah tifdeh*—you shall surely redeem the firstborn human and the firstborn of the non-kosher animal. The Torah places both in a single verse, linking the *mitzvah* of redeeming firstborn humans to the *mitzvah* of redeeming firstborn donkeys. This constitutes a *gezerah shavah*—a verbal-structural analogy—connecting the two obligations. Since the *levi* is exempt from the former, he is equally exempt from the latter.

### Kosher Animals Obligatory

This same reasoning explains why the exemption does not extend to *bechor behemah tehorah*. The *mitzvah* of *bechor behemah tehorah* is categorically different: it is not about redemption at all. When a kosher animal gives birth to a firstborn, the obligation is to retain its *kedushah* and bring it as a *korban* in the *Beit HaMikdash*. There is no payment, no exchange, no redemption in the ordinary sense. By contrast, both *pidyon ha'bechor* of a human and *peter chamor* of a donkey share the structure of redemption—*pidyon*, an act of paying to release a sanctified object from its obligatory status. The verse *padoh tifdeh* connects precisely these two, and it is within this shared category of redemption that the Levi's status creates an exemption.

### Final Raba Framework

The full framework that emerges from Raba is therefore: Levi'im—and Kohanim, who are counted among the Levi'im—are exempt from *pidyon bechor* of humans because their *kedushat Levi* structurally substitutes for *kedushat ha'bechor*. They are exempt from *peter chamor* by extension, because the Torah links the redemption of firstborn humans and firstborn donkeys in a single verse. They remain fully obligated in *bechor behemah tehorah*, because that *mitzvah* is not about redemption but about *korban*, and the structural logic of substitution does not reach there.

Tosafot adds an important clarification of why Abaye's model also does not extend to *bechor behemah tehorah*, even though he is learning from the animal substitution in the *midbar*. Even according to Abaye, he would agree that there is a fundamental distinction: the obligation of *bechor behemah tehorah* is not about transferring or neutralizing *kedushah*—it is about the inherent sanctity of the firstborn animal and the requirement to bring it as a *korban*. Since neither the sheep nor the donkey has, in any meaningful sense, the kind of *kedushah* that is "transferred" the way human firstborn sanctity was transferred in the *midbar*, and since the entire thrust of *bechor behemah tehorah* is to maintain rather than dissolve that status, Abaye also would not apply the *midbar* animal model to exempt a Levi from bringing his firstborn sheep or goat as a *korban*. In the end, then, Abaye and Raba reach the same practical *halachah*—the exemption covers *peter chamor* and *pidyon bechor*, but not *bechor behemah tehorah*—though they arrive there by different routes.

## Rav Pappa Challenges

### No Sheep Problem

*Amar lei Rav Pappa le'Abaye*—Rav Pappa now raises a series of sharp challenges to Abaye's model. The first follows directly from Abaye's reading: since, according to Abaye, the exemption of the *levi*'s donkeys is grounded in the animal substitution of the *midbar*—where the sheep of the Levi'im stood in exchange for the firstborn donkeys of the Israelites—it would follow that this model only applies when the *levi* actually possesses a sheep. *Ben Levi de'havi lei seh de'afka lifka, de'lav seh delifka lo lifka*—a son of a *levi* who has a sheep, fine, that sheep exempts his *peter chamor*; but a son of a *levi* who has no sheep would not be exempt. Yet the *mishna* states categorically that Levi'im are exempt, without qualification. Are we really to say that a *levi* in the wilderness who had no sheep was obligated to redeem his firstborn donkey? That has never been heard of, and if it were true in the wilderness it should be true today—yet the *mishna* says nothing of the sort.

### Age/Counting Problem

The second challenge applies *bein ledidach bein le'Rava*—whether we follow Abaye or Raba, and concerns the age requirement built into the *midbar* narrative. The counting of the Levi'im who substituted for the firstborn Israelites included only those who were *miben chodesh*—one month and older. A baby *levi* less than one month old was not part of the exchange. If we are truly grounding the exemption in what happened in the *midbar*, a *levi* who was less than a month old at the time of that exchange never participated in it, and by the logic of the narrative, his firstborn status was never substituted—meaning he should theoretically still be obligated.

It must be admitted that this question is somewhat difficult to understand on its own terms. From a purely *halachic* standpoint, the obligation of *pidyon bechor* does not arise until the child is a month old in any case—so by the time the obligation applies, the child is already in the category of those who were counted. The question seems to be pressing not at the *halachic* level but at the narrative level: at the actual historical moment of the wilderness exchange, those baby Levi'im under a month old were simply not present as participants in the substitution. If we are learning out the exemption from that one historical event, how can those infants be covered? This is the thrust of Rav Pappa's challenge, and it underscores the broader methodological difficulty of grounding law in narrative.

### Mother Status Problem

The third challenge is the most penetrating. *Leviya lo tifka*—what about the case where the mother is a *leviya* but the father is not a *levi*? In the *midbar*, a son follows the tribe of his father. So if a *leviya* married a man from *Shevet Reuven*, their son would be classified as belonging to *Shevet Reuven*—he would not have been counted among the Levi'im, would not have participated in the exchange, and would have had no role in the substitution. Yet the accepted *halacha* is that a *leviya's* son is exempt from *pidyon haben*. This is taught explicitly as an *amora'ic* ruling: *leviya sheyalda bena patur mechamesh sela'im*—if a *leviya* gives birth, her son is exempt from the five *sela'im* of redemption. If the entire exemption flows from what took place in the *midbar*, and the son of a *leviya* married to a non-*levi* had no place in that event, how can he be exempt?

This challenge cuts to the heart of the *gemara*'s entire framework. Normally, *halacha* is not derived from narrative. Here the *gemara* has been operating on the premise that the wilderness exchange provides the basis for the laws of exemption. Rav Pappa is pressing: the narrative simply does not hold together as a source of law with sufficient rigor to generate the rules we actually follow.

## Shift to Halacha

### Womb-Breaching Answer

The *gemara* begins its response to the *leviya* question: *ha lo kashya*, that is not difficult, citing the teaching of the son of Rav Yosef in the name of Raba—*peter rechem be'peter rechem tala Rachmana*—the Torah tied the obligation to the breaching of the womb. Since the definition of the firstborn obligation is formulated in terms of *peter rechem*, the opening of the womb, women are structurally relevant. The mother matters because the *mitzvah* is defined as the one who breaks open the mother's womb. This grounds the exemption of a *leviya*'s son not in the narrative but in the *halachic* definition of the *mitzvah* itself.

### Paradigm & Expansion

What emerges is a more nuanced picture of how the *midbar* narrative functions in the *gemara*'s reasoning. The narrative is not being used as a direct source of law in the sense that every *halacha* must correspond to something that actually occurred in the wilderness. Rather, the wilderness exchange serves as a paradigm—a foundational model—from which initial principles are derived, and those principles are then extended and developed according to their own internal *halachic* logic. From the narrative we learn that the personal status of a *levi* creates an exemption in the context of firstborn obligations. From the *halachic* structure of *peter rechem*, we then derive that the mother's status also matters—and therefore a *leviya*'s son is exempt even though that configuration had no direct parallel in the wilderness.

### Narrative Limits

This distinction—between using the narrative as a paradigm and being strictly limited to it—resolves the apparent inconsistency. We are not bound to say: only what literally occurred in the *midbar* generates exemption. Rather, the narrative establishes a conceptual framework, and *halacha* then fills in the details according to its own principles. The case of the *leviya* is not excluded because it did not happen in the wilderness; it is included because once we know that Levi status generates exemption, and once we know that *peter rechem* makes the mother relevant, the combined *halachic* logic yields exemption for the *leviya*'s son as a second-order derivation.

## Universal Exemption

### Aharon Exception

Having navigated the *leviya* question, the *gemara* turns to another figure who creates a structural problem for any narrative-based exemption: Aharon. The *gemara* raises the point—still pressing the narrative model—that Aharon was not counted among those whose persons or animals participated in the substitution. He was one of the counters, not the countees. *Ve'Aharon shelo hayah be'oto minyan lo lifka*—since Aharon was not part of that counting, how was he, or his animals, exempt from *peter chamor*? A *baraita* confirms: *lama nakud al Aharon shebe'chumash ha'pekudim*—there is a dot over Aharon's name in the Book of Numbers to indicate *shelo hayah be'oto minyan*, that he was not part of the counting. This is a direct challenge to Abaye's model: if the exemption requires having participated in the narrative exchange, Aharon simply was not there.

### HaLevi'im Verse

The *gemara* answers: *amar Kra ha'Levi'im*. The verse refers to "the Levi'im"—in the definite plural—and from this we derive *hukshu kol ha'Levi'im zeh lazeh*, all Levi'im are linked to one another. This means that once we know that some Levi'im were exempt—those who had sheep, whose animals participated in the substitution in the *midbar*—the term *ha'Levi'im* extends that exemption to all Levi'im collectively. The exemption is not limited to precisely those individuals or animals that were literally part of the one-time wilderness event. The Torah's use of the definite plural encompasses the entire tribe, including Aharon, including those without sheep, including all subsequent generations.

This is a significant structural move. Abaye begins with the animal substitution as the basis for the exemption, but through the verse of *ha'Levi'im*, he ends up in a position practically indistinguishable from Raba: the exemption is categorical and applies to all Levi'im by virtue of their tribal identity, without requiring each individual to possess a substitute animal. The *halachic* conclusion converges even as the underlying reasoning diverges.

### Kohanim as Levi'im

The *gemara* then asks: *Kohanim minalan*—how do we know that Kohanim are also categorically exempt, given that Aharon, who was the paradigmatic *kohen*, was not even counted? The answer comes from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *b'esrim v'arba'ah mekomot nikre'u ha'Kohanim Levi'im*—in twenty-four places in Scripture the Kohanim are referred to as Levi'im, because they are in fact members of the tribe of Levi. *V'zeh echad mehem*—and one particularly explicit example is the verse *ve'ha'kohanim ha'levi'im bnei Tzadok*—the Kohanim the Levi'im, the sons of Tzadok. Since we know that Tzadok was a *kohen*, the verse explicitly calls Kohanim Levi'im. Once the term *ha'Levi'im* extends the exemption to all Levi'im, and since Kohanim are repeatedly designated as Levi'im throughout Scripture, Kohanim are included in the universal exemption as well.

### Perpetuity Verse

Finally, having established that all Levi'im and Kohanim are exempt, the *gemara* asks: *ve'ledorot minalan*—what gives us the right to transition from a one-time narrative to a perpetual *halachah*? Perhaps the exemption applied only to that moment in the wilderness. The answer is the verse *ve'hayu li ha'Levi'im*—they shall be for Me. The *levi'im* retain their status permanently, and this retention of status carries with it the ongoing role they occupy within the *mitzvah* of *pidyon bechor*. *Havaya be'havaya sanyuha*—the language of "they shall be" implies continuity into the future. Thus the exemption from *pidyon bechor* of humans and *peter chamor* extends to all generations, while the obligation in *bechor behemah tehorah*—which operates on an entirely different basis—remains.

## Narrative-Halacha Relati…

### Reading Halacha into Nar…

At this point the *gemara* turns to a question that is striking in its frankness: having established that we read from narrative into *halacha*, are we also permitted to read *halacha* back into the narrative? Specifically, the *gemara* asks: we assumed, throughout this discussion, that when the Torah describes the animals of the Levi'im standing in exchange for the animals of Israel, the instrument of exchange was sheep for donkeys—*seh* for *peter chamor*. But the Torah itself never says this. The Torah says broadly *behemot ha'Levi'im tachat behemotam*—the animals of the Levi'im in exchange for their animals. How do we know that what was happening in that narrative paralleled the *halakhic* rule of *seh* for *peter chamor*? We are, in effect, retroactively projecting the *halachah* back onto the story.

### Money-Sheep Parallel

Rav Chisda addresses this: *ne'emar kesef le'dorot ve'ne'emar seh le'dorot*. For future generations, the Torah specifies that money redeems the firstborn human and that a sheep redeems the firstborn donkey. Now, *ma kesef amar le'dorot bo pidu le'oso sha'ah*—the same way the Torah tells us for future generations that money is used in redemption of firstborn humans, and we know that in the wilderness the extra 273 firstborn Israelites were in fact redeemed with five *shekel*—that is, the narrative already reflects the future *halachah* in the case of money. We can verify the parallel: what was done in the wilderness for the 273 unredeemed firstborns matched precisely what the *halachah* would require in all future generations. Therefore, by analogy, *af seh amar le'dorot bo pidu le'oso sha'ah*—the same parallel should hold for sheep and donkeys. Since the *halachah* for future generations is sheep for donkey, we can infer that this was also what occurred in the narrative.

### Money in Redemption

The *gemara* presses back, however: *mah lekesef sheken podin bo hekdeshot uma'aser sheni*—perhaps money is unique. Money has broad redemptive power throughout *halacha*: it redeems *hekdesh*, it redeems *ma'aser sheni*, it operates across multiple domains of sacred-object redemption. It makes sense, therefore, that money would appear both in the narrative and in the *halachah* going forward—its repeated use is a function of its general utility as a redemptive instrument. But sheep have no such general role in redemption. So perhaps the parallel for money does not carry over to sheep; perhaps sheep were used for donkeys in the *halachah* but we cannot assume from this that sheep were what was happening in the narrative.

### Padoh Tifdeh Link

The *gemara* therefore turns to a direct verse: *ach padoh tifdeh et bechor ha'adam ve'et bechor ha'behemah ha'teme'ah tifdeh*—you shall surely redeem the firstborn human and you shall redeem the firstborn of the non-kosher animal. This verse places humans and donkeys under a single *mitzvah* structure. Just as the Torah draws no distinction between the narrative event and the future *halachah* when it comes to the firstborn human—what occurred in the wilderness was the same as what the *halachah* would mandate forever after—so too the verse creates a structural equation between humans and donkeys, forbidding us from drawing a distinction: *af behemah teme'ah lo tachlok bo bein le'dorot bein le'oto sha'ah*—by the firstborn donkey as well, do not distinguish between what was done then and what is required for future generations. Since the *halachah* going forward uses a sheep for donkey, we now have a textual basis to conclude that this is also what was happening in the narrative.

### One-to-Many Redemption

Rav Chanina now raises a further point about the structure of the *midbar* exchange: *secho shel ben Levi patar kama pitrei chamorei mi'Yisrael*—the sheep of a son of a *levi* would exempt many firstborn donkeys of Israel. In the wilderness, the exchange was not one-to-one. The Torah counts with great precision the numbers of firstborn Israelites and the number of Levi'im, carefully accounting for the 273 excess. But it performs no such parallel accounting for the animals. There is no verse telling us that there were so many firstborn donkeys of Israel, so many animals of the Levi'im, and so many left over requiring monetary redemption. This absence suggests the exchange was entirely symbolic: one animal of a *levi* stood opposite many firstborn donkeys of Israel.

### No Animal Counting

Abaye supports this conclusion: *she'harei manah ha'katuv otfim ba'adam ve'lo manah otfim ba'behemah*—the Torah took care to enumerate the excess firstborn humans, but made no mention of excess animals. If it were a one-to-one substitution, the Torah's meticulous accounting for the human case would lead us to expect the same accounting for the animal case. The absence of animal counting implies that exact numbers were irrelevant—one animal sufficed for many.

The *gemara* attempts to deflect this inference: *dilma lo havu nefishei le'vehemos tuvei*—perhaps there simply were not many excess firstborn animals; perhaps the Levi'im had more sheep than there were firstborn donkeys among the Israelites, and so there was no surplus to account for. The *gemara* counters by pointing to the verse recording that the children of Reuven and Gad had *mikne rav*—abundant livestock—suggesting that the Israelites generally had very large animal holdings, making it implausible that the Levi'im's animals exceeded the Israelites' firstborn animals in number. Yet the *gemara* pushes further: even if the Israelites had many cattle overall, perhaps only a small fraction were *bechorim*, and the Levi'im's ordinary (*peshutim*) sheep still outnumbered them. Thus a one-to-one substitution remains possible, and the absence of counting cannot by itself prove the symbolic one-to-many model.

The *gemara* therefore returns to the text: *amar Kra u'vehemat ha'Levi'im tachat behematam*—the verse reads *behemat* (singular construct) of the Levi'im in exchange for *behematam* (their animals, in the plural possessive). The juxtaposition of singular *behemat* against the plural *behematam* indicates *behemah achat tachat behemot harbeh*—one animal in exchange for many. It must be admitted that the grammatical point here is difficult: *behematam* reflects the plural of the owners (the Israelites) rather than a plurality of animals, and the *gemara*'s inference is noted as challenging by later commentators. Nevertheless, the conclusion stands: in the *midbar*, one animal of a *levi* redeemed many firstborn donkeys of Israel.

### Halachic Application

Raba then closes the sugya by applying this insight *le-halachah*: *af anan nami tanina—u'podeh bo pe'amim harbeh*—we too have learned in a *mishna* that one may redeem with the same sheep multiple times. This parallel to the *midbar*'s one-to-many model is real but not exact. According to Rashi, the case is: one redeems a donkey with a sheep, gives the sheep to the *kohen*, then buys it back, and uses it again to redeem the next donkey. The sheep can cycle through multiple redemptions, but not simultaneously—each use must be completed and the sheep recovered before it can serve again. Tosafot offers what may be a more genuinely parallel scenario: if one has multiple firstborn donkeys about which there are *doubts*—perhaps the mother's birth order is uncertain, or there are multiple questionable cases—one may use a single sheep to redeem all the doubtful cases at once. This more closely mirrors the *midbar*'s symbolic one-to-many exchange, where the exact correspondence of animals did not need to be tracked. The narrative and the *halachah* thus remain in ongoing conversation: the wilderness event illuminates the law, the law illuminates the narrative, and both converge in a framework that is richer than either alone.
