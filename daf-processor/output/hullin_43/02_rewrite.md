# Hullin 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tannaitic Treifos & 18

### Desagar Overview

The *daf* we have been studying is 43, and we resume from 42b, beginning with the word *gluda* — found roughly midway down the page, marked by an asterisk. By way of orientation: we have been engaged with a *braisa* that lists eighteen *treifos* in the name of Rabbi Yishmael. Our *mishnah* also contains eighteen, but various other Tannaitic and Amoraic sources expand that number — so the central challenge has been determining how to accommodate these additional cases within the fixed count of eighteen.

The four additional Tannaitic teachings were grouped under the mnemonic *desagar* (dalet-samech-gimel-reish). The *dalet* referred to an animal whose leg was cut from the knee upward — which we concluded was a matter of dispute, and thus the *tanna d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* need not accept it. The *samech* referred to *chasra*, an animal missing one or two vertebrae of the spine — which we determined is unambiguously a *treifa*, and which we accommodated by consolidating two inner stomachs (the *hamasess* and the *beis hakosos*), treating them as a single entry so that the missing vertebra could take the freed spot. We now turn to the remaining two items of the *desagar* group: *gimmel* for *gluda* and *reish* for *charusa*.

### Gluda Debate

*Va'ika gluda* — what of the *gluda*, the animal that has been skinned alive? This condition is indeed mentioned in a *mishnah* as a *treifa*, but it is the subject of a Tannaitic dispute: *savar lei Rabbi Meir demachshir* — Rabbi Meir holds it does not render the animal a *treifa*. Since the *tanna d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* would side with Rabbi Meir's dissenting view, this case does not require a place on the list of eighteen.

### Charusa & Gallbladder

*Va'ika charusa* — what of the *charusa*? The term *charusa* is borrowed from the description of a *lulav* that has dried out and hardened. It refers here to a lung that has lost its moisture and become hardened. If this happens naturally it is not a *treifa*, but if it results from some form of human intervention — *b'dei shamayim* in the sense of an external cause — it is. By all accounts this qualifies as a *treifa*, so we must find a way to make room for it on the list.

The *gemara* asks: *mara man tanni lah?* — who is it that teaches that a hole in the gallbladder renders an animal a *treifa*, as our *mishnah* implies? The answer is: that position belongs exclusively to Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda, and it is not universally accepted. Therefore, the *gemara* resolves the problem simply: *apik mara v'ayeil charusa* — remove the gallbladder from the count, since its status as a *treifa* is a matter of dispute, and bring in the *charusa*, the hardened lung, in its place.

### Tannaitic Summary

To summarize where we stand with the *desagar* group: of the four additional Tannaitic teachings, two require accommodation within the eighteen. The animal with a missing vertebra is accommodated by consolidating the two inner stomachs into a single entry. The *charusa* — the hardened lung — is accommodated by removing the gallbladder, whose *treifa* status is debated. The *gluda* requires no accommodation because it is itself disputed. These two techniques — consolidation and removal of contested items — are the tools we will continue to employ as the *gemara* now confronts a much larger challenge: seven Amoraic additions to the list.

The significance of this challenge should be appreciated. When dealing with other Tannaitic teachings, one can at least argue that the earlier *tanna* who listed eighteen was working with the same underlying material and simply arranged it differently. The *amoraim*, however, are introducing cases not found in the *mishnah* at all. If one holds with the principle *treifa einah chaya* — that what defines a *treifa* is an injury from which the animal cannot survive — then there is room to say the *amoraim* were simply working out the practical implications of that principle over time. But Rabbi Yishmael holds that *treifos* can survive, and that the list of eighteen is *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — a fixed, divinely transmitted enumeration. On that view, the *amoraim* cannot simply have added to it; whatever they articulated must have always been part of the list, even if not previously spelled out. This is, admittedly, a difficult position — and it is one more reason why the simpler reading may be to accept the principle *treifa einah chaya* and allow the list to develop over time.

## Seven Amoraic Treifos

### Rav Matna: Dislocated Hip

*Ve-eika shev shmaitata* — there are seven Amoraic teachings about additional *treifos* not mentioned in the *mishnah*. We count them in turn.

First: *de-amar Rav Matna* — Rav Matna teaches that *hai buka de-itma de-shaf mi-duchtei*, the upper thigh bone — the ball-and-socket joint at the hip — if it becomes dislocated, renders the animal a *treifa*. Anatomically, this refers to the joint at the very top of the hip where the femoral head sits. One might note that in antiquity a dislocated hip would leave the animal permanently unable to stand; today it can be surgically corrected — though of course inability to stand does not by itself imply the animal will die.

### Diseased Kidney

Second: *de-amar Rav Chana bar Pappa mishmeih de-Rav* — Rav Chana bar Pappa in the name of Rav teaches *laksa b'kulyah achat treifa*: if even one of an animal's kidneys is diseased — the *gemara* will later explain this as a pus-filled sac within the kidney — the animal is a *treifa*. This applies a fortiori if both kidneys are affected, but even one suffices.

### Spleen: Rupture vs. Remo…

Third: *u-detnan nital ha-techol kesheira* — our *mishnah* states that if the spleen is removed, the animal is kosher. But *ve-amar Rav Avya mishmeih de-Rava* — Rav Avya in the name of Rava qualifies this: *lo shanu ela nital* — it is only if the spleen is entirely removed that the animal is kosher; *aval nikav treifa* — if the spleen is present but has a hole in it, it is a *treifa*. The intuition here, though not explicitly stated by the *gemara*, may be that a ruptured spleen can leak dangerous fluids, whereas a completely absent spleen poses no comparable risk — a parallel, as one might note, to the case of the appendix, where a burst appendix is considerably more dangerous than a missing one.

### Detached Trachea/Esophag…

Fourth: *ve-amar Rabba bar bar Chana amar Shmuel* — if the *simanim*, the trachea and esophagus, are *nishladu be-ruban* — mostly pulled away from the surrounding tissue of the neck, though still attached in some places — that too renders the animal a *treifa*.

### Uprooted Rib

Fifth: *ve-amar Rabba bar Shila amar Rav Masana amar Shmuel* — *ne-ekra tsela me-ikara treifa*: if a single rib is uprooted from its point of origin at the vertebra — not merely broken, but fully torn away from its base — that is a *treifa*. Note that this is distinct from the earlier rule about most ribs being broken; here even one rib, if completely dislodged from its origin, suffices.

### Skull & Abdominal Muscles

Sixth: *ve-gulgoles she-nechbesa be-rubah* — a skull that has been beaten in across most of its surface. This is not a case of a hole in the skull but of the bone being crushed and pushed inward over the majority of its area.

Seventh: *u-vasar ha-chofeh es rov ha-keres be-rubo treifa* — if the muscles covering the underside of the *keres*, the rumen, are torn across the majority of their width. The *keres* is the enormous first stomach of the ruminant, and the abdominal muscles run beneath it for much of its length. Importantly, the *mishnah* specifies *rov ha-keres* because the muscles do not extend beneath the entire *keres* — portions of the stomach lie too deep within the body to be covered by them — and the ruling refers specifically to the section of muscle that runs directly underneath the stomach. If the majority of that section is torn, the animal is a *treifa*.

### Seventh Teaching

With these seven Amoraic additions enumerated, the question now becomes acute: how can all seven be fitted within a list of eighteen that was ostensibly already full? The *gemara* must find a way to open enough space — which, as we are about to see, requires some fairly aggressive consolidation of the existing entries.

## Holes Consolidation

### Eight Holes as One

The *gemara* proposes a bold consolidation: *nikvei tamanya havei* — count all the organs mentioned in the *mishnah* for which a hole renders the animal a *treifa*, and you will find there are eight of them. Rather than treating each holed organ as a separate entry, Rabbi Yishmael may have counted all cases of holes as a single category. That single category would represent one entry on the list, thereby freeing up seven spots — precisely enough to insert the seven Amoraic additions. *havei chashvinu bechad, afik sheva, ve-ayiti hanei sheva* — count them all as one, remove seven from the count, and bring in these seven.

### Further Consolidation

The *gemara* raises an objection. If we are consolidating at this level, then *psukei nami terei havei* — there are also two organs in the *mishnah* for which being cut through renders them a *treifa*: the trachea and the spinal cord. If we apply the same logic and count all cases of cutting as one category, we free up another spot — bringing us to seventeen rather than eighteen. *chashvinu bechad, uvatzru lehu acharina* — count them as one and we have one fewer than eighteen.

Furthermore, *ve-od de-Rav Avya mishmeih de-Rava nami nikva hu* — Rav Avya's teaching about a hole in the spleen is also a case of a hole, and must be absorbed into the holes category. That reduces our count by yet another, leaving us at sixteen rather than eighteen — meaning we have opened up nine spaces when we only needed seven.

### Balance to 18

The *gemara* resolves this on 43a: *ella hanech tartei de-afkinhu lo teipok* — the two items we removed from the list earlier, in the course of making room for the Tannaitic additions, should not have been removed after all. We used those two vacancies to accommodate the missing vertebra and the hardened lung; those two cases count as the items that fill the extra spots we inadvertently opened. With that adjustment, the arithmetic returns to eighteen.

## Ulla's 8 Types

### Eight Injury Types

With so much consolidation already underway — grouping cases not by the organ affected but by the type of injury sustained — Ulla draws the natural conclusion and proposes a fundamental reclassification: *amar Ulla shemonah minei treifos neemru le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — there were really eight *types* of *treifos* transmitted to Moses at Sinai, where the unit of classification is the nature of the injury rather than the organ involved.

The eight types are: *nekuva* — a hole; *psuka* — something cut through; *netula* — something entirely removed (as with the liver); *chasura* — something lacking, meaning a portion has been excised without creating a hole that goes all the way through the wall; *kru'ah* — torn, as with the abdominal muscles; *drusa* — clawed by a predator, which carries a concern of poison or infection; *nefula* — having fallen from a height, producing internal displacement even without specific visible injury; and *shvura* — literally broken, as when most of the ribs are fractured. These eight categories encompass the entire range of *treifa* cases, and the elegance of Ulla's framework is that it organizes them by the mechanism of harm rather than by anatomy.

### Diseased Excluded

Notably, Ulla does not include *laksa* — a diseased organ — as one of the eight types. The *gemara* makes this explicit: *le-apokei lakuta de-Rav Chisda bar Pappa* — this is precisely to exclude the teaching of Rav Chisda bar Pappa about the diseased kidney.

This exclusion raises a striking conceptual question. One of the persistent difficulties throughout this *masechta* is defining the boundary between a *treifa* — an animal with a specific qualifying injury — and an animal that is simply dying. Once "diseased" is introduced as a category, the line becomes genuinely blurred. It is true that the diseased-kidney case is not about a general systemic illness but about a specific organ with a pus-filled lesion, which makes it closer in character to the other injury-based *treifos*. Nevertheless, Ulla declines to treat it as a separate type.

Rashi's explanation of why it is excluded is particularly illuminating. One might have argued that a diseased kidney should be assimilated into the *nekuva* category — the hole category — on the theory that the pus-filled sac will eventually rupture and create a hole. Rashi points out that this argument fails because a hole in the kidney is actually *not* a *treifa*. A diseased kidney is a *treifa* on its own terms, not as a harbinger of a hole. That is precisely why Rav Chisda bar Pappa needed to introduce it as an independent ruling — and precisely why Ulla rejects it as a type that does not fit within the established framework. The question of where disease ends and *treifa* begins remains genuinely unresolved, and the fact that the diseased-kidney case occupies a category of its own is itself a reflection of that conceptual tension.

### Practical Checking

A natural question arises from all of this: with so extensive a list of possible *treifos*, how do *shochtim* and those who inspect animals after *shechita* actually operate in practice? The answer is that they do not check for most of these conditions at all. The underlying principle is the distinction between *miut ha-matzui* — a common minority occurrence — and *miut she-eino matzui* — an uncommon minority occurrence. If a problem arises in fewer than approximately five percent of cases, there is no halachic obligation to check for it, and we rely instead on the animal's presumptive status of being permissible.

In practice, the only organ that is routinely checked is the lung, because pulmonary problems are sufficiently common to constitute a *miut ha-matzui*. All the other conditions discussed throughout this *masechta*, however anatomically significant, arise rarely enough that no inspection is required. Of course, if a problem is actually discovered — either upon examination or because circumstances make it known, as occurred when certain growth hormones were injected into cattle in a manner that punctured internal organs — the discovery must be addressed. But the default is to rely on *chazaka* and the rarity of these injuries.

## Gallbladder Debate

### Yosei & Eight Holes

Chiya bar Rav now observes: *cheit treifos yesh bi-nekuva* — there are eight cases in the *mishnah* of organs for which a hole renders the animal a *treifa*. *im tomar tes* — if you were to suggest the number is nine, because of the gallbladder (*mara*) mentioned in our *mishnah*, the answer is: *rabbi yosei bar rabbi yehuda katani lah* — that entry was taught only by Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda and does not represent the majority view.

The source is a *braisa*: *nikvah ha-keivah* — if the final stomach has a hole; *nikvu ha-dakin* — or the small intestines have a hole — *treifa*. *rabbi yosei bar rabbi yehuda omer af nikvah ha-marah* — Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda adds: even a hole in the gallbladder. Our *mishnah*, in including the gallbladder, follows Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda's individual position. Those who do not accept his view count only eight cases of holes, not nine.

By this accounting, the gallbladder entry is removed from the list, and the hole in the spleen — introduced by Rav Avya in the name of Rava — likewise represents a contested addition. The base count of *nekuva* cases, absent these disputed entries, stands at eight.

### Gallbladder Ruling

Immediately following this discussion, *amar Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef amar Rabbi Yochanan halacha ke-Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda* — we rule in accordance with Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda that a hole in the gallbladder does indeed render the animal a *treifa*. This ruling is the first in a series of traditions transmitted by Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, which the *gemara* now strings together.

### Iyov Proof

The *gemara* then reports: *va-amar Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef amar Rabbi Yochanan mah ahadru leh chavrei le-Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda* — what did the colleagues of Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda say in response to his position? They apparently held that an injury to the gallbladder is not a *treifa* at all, and they derived this from a verse in *iyov*: *yishpoch la-aretz merarati* — "My bile pours out onto the earth." Reading this literally, they understood *iyov* to be describing the loss of his gallbladder as one of his afflictions — *ve-adayin Iyov kayam* — and yet *iyov* survived. From this they concluded that the loss of a gallbladder, even in the most extreme case, is not fatal.

### Miracles Dismissed

Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda responds: *ein mazkirin maaseh nissim* — one may not cite miracles as halachic proof. And lest the colleagues insist on a literal reading, he preempts them with another verse from *iyov*: *yefalach kilyotai ve-lo yachmol* — "He will pierce my kidneys and show no compassion." By the same logic, *iyov* apparently had injured kidneys — and since a diseased kidney is a *treifa*, how did he survive? *ella nisa shani* — it was all miraculous. And the license for all those miracles is given explicitly at the opening of the book: *rak et nafsho shmor* — God instructs the *satan* to bring every affliction upon *iyov* while preserving his life. The entire narrative of *iyov*'s physical suffering is thus a sustained miracle, and no *halachic* inference can be drawn from it.

What is worth noting here is the implicit consensus this discussion reveals. The entire argument — on both sides — turns on whether the animal could have lived. The assumption that *treifa einah chaya*, that what distinguishes a *treifa* is the inability to survive, is not argued for but simply presupposed. And similarly, the *gemara* here treats the physiology of humans and animals as presumptively parallel — an assumption that *tosafot* examines carefully elsewhere, concluding that the comparison holds when the relevant organs are physiologically similar but may not hold when they differ, as in the case of the membrane covering the brain, which *tosafot* notes is thicker in humans than in animals.

## Yitzchak/Yochanan Series

### Liver Remainder

*Va-amar Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef amar Rabbi Yochanan* continues with another ruling. The question concerns how much of the liver must remain for the animal not to be a *treifa*. Our *mishnah* states *nitla ha-kaved ve-lo nishtayer mimenu klum* — if the liver is removed and nothing remains, it is a *treifa* — implying by contrast that if anything at all remains, however small, the animal is kosher. A later *mishnah*, however, stipulates that a *ke-zayit* — an olive-sized portion — must remain. *halacha ke-zayit ve-omer ke-zayit* — the ruling of Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef in the name of Rabbi Yochanan is that we require a *ke-zayit*.

### Stam Mishna Authority

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *umi amar Rabbi Yochanan hachi?* — but doesn't Rabbi Yochanan follow the rule *halacha ke-stam Mishnah* — we rule in accordance with an anonymous *mishnah*? And our first *mishnah* on the liver is a *stam Mishnah* that implies any remainder suffices. How then can Rabbi Yochanan rule against it?

Rashi explains the difficulty precisely: when two anonymous *mishnahs* contradict each other, a judge who holds by the authority of a *stam Mishnah* as a general rule cannot simply choose one over the other without justification — the result would be an unresolved *safek*. So if Rabbi Yochanan consistently defers to *stam Mishnahs*, he ought to be unable to issue a definitive ruling here.

The *gemara*'s resolution is *amora'ei ninhu ve-aliba de-Rabbi Yochanan* — the question of whether Rabbi Yochanan holds by the authority of a *stam Mishnah* is itself a dispute among *amoraim*. According to those who hold that he does not adopt this as a general rule, he is free to state his own considered opinion regardless of how anonymous *mishnahs* align, and no tension arises.

### Sealed Gallbladder

A further ruling in the same chain: *va-amar Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef amar Rabbi Yochanan marah she-nikvah* — concerning the gallbladder with a hole, which we have ruled is a *treifa* — *ha-kaved sotmah* — if the liver overlies the gallbladder and the hole is precisely at the point of contact between the two organs, such that the liver seals the hole from the very moment of injury, then *kesherah* — it is kosher. Rashi is careful to emphasize the temporal point: this is not a case of scar tissue forming over a hole after the fact; from the very instant the injury occurred, it was covered by the liver and therefore never constituted an open hole in the halachically relevant sense.

## Double-Layer Organs

### Gizzard Layers

*Va-amar Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef amar Rabbi Yochanan nikav ha-korkevan ve-ha-kis shelo kayam kesherah* — if the gizzard has a hole in its outer wall, but the inner membrane (*kis*) remains intact, the animal is kosher. The *korkevan* — gizzard — is the muscular grinding organ found in birds; it operates through a combination of thick outer wall muscles and inner folds of membrane that assist in breaking down food. These two structures — the outer wall and the inner *kis* — are treated as distinct layers for the purpose of this *halacha*.

### Inner Membrane Hole

*Ibaia lehu* — a question is then raised: *nikav ha-kis ve-ha-korkevan kayam mahu* — what if it is the reverse, if the inner membrane has a hole but the outer wall is intact? Is that also permissible, or does it matter which layer is breached?

### Both Layers Required

*Ta shema de-amar Rav Nachman nikav zeh belo zeh kesherah* — come and hear: Rav Nachman teaches that a hole in one layer without the other is not a problem in either direction. Only when the hole penetrates through both layers does the animal become a *treifa*.

### Esophagus Layers

*Amar Rava shnei orot yesh lo la-veshet* — Rava states that the esophagus similarly has two layers of skin: *chizon adom u-fnim lavan* — the outer layer is red and the inner layer is white. And by the same principle: *nikav zeh belo zeh kesherah* — a hole in only one of the two layers is not a *treifa*; the hole must penetrate both.

### Color Change Issue

One might ask why Rava finds it necessary to specify that the outer layer is red and the inner white. The answer is: *de-i chalif treifa* — because if the colors reverse or change — if what was white becomes red or vice versa — the animal is a *treifa*, even without any hole, cut, or tear. This is a striking ruling, because a color change does not fit neatly into any of Ulla's eight categories of injury. Rashi suggests it may be connected to the concern of *drusa* — that the color change results from some form of poison or toxin — which ties it back to the case in the *mishnah* about an animal that may have been clawed by a predator.

## Non-Parallel Holes

### Parallel Question

*Ibaia lehu* — a further question is raised that applies to both the gizzard and the esophagus: *nikvu shneihem zeh she-lo keneged zeh mahu* — what if both layers have holes, but the holes are not parallel? That is, the outer hole is in one location and the inner hole is in a different location, so that there is no continuous perforation from exterior to interior. Is such a configuration a *treifa* or not?

### Mar Zutra Ruling

*Amar Mar Zutra mishmeih de-Rav Papa* — Mar Zutra in the name of Rav Papa rules: *be-veshet kasher* — by the esophagus, non-parallel holes are not a problem; *be-korkevan pasul* — by the gizzard, they are. Rashi explains the reasoning underlying the gizzard ruling: because food collects and sits in the gizzard for a period of time, there is an opportunity for material to exit through one hole and find its way to the other, effectively creating a continuous pathway. In the esophagus, by contrast, food and liquid pass through rapidly and continuously, so the two non-parallel holes would never functionally combine.

### Rav Ashi Objection

*Mateif lah Rav Ashi* — Rav Ashi objects and argues the opposite. By the esophagus, *de-achla bei u-fa'aya bei* — the animal eats through it and vocalizes through it (or at minimum the esophagus moves in conjunction with vocalization, as Rashi notes) — *revacha gamdei lah u-fashta lah* — it expands and contracts with considerable movement, *ve-zimnin de-mahandzin be-hadi hadadei* — and sometimes, as the membranes shift around, the two holes chance to align. Therefore non-parallel holes in the esophagus are actually more worrisome, not less. By contrast, *korkevana de-minach nayeich* — the gizzard just sits there quietly; *ke-dekai kai* — it stays as it is, and non-parallel holes in the gizzard will never line up.

### Reconciliation

*Amar lei Rav Acha breih de-Rav Yosef le-Rav Ashi* — Rav Acha the son of Rav Yosef said to Rav Ashi: *hachi amrinan mishmeih de-Mar Zutra de-amar mishmeih de-Rav Papa ke-vachach* — that is precisely what we transmit in the name of Mar Zutra in the name of Rav Papa: in agreement with your position, that non-parallel holes are more problematic in the esophagus than in the gizzard. The apparent contradiction between Mar Zutra's ruling and Rav Ashi's objection is thus resolved: the transmission attributed to Mar Zutra actually aligns with Rav Ashi's view, and the initial formulation was simply imprecise.

## Scar Tissue Issue

### Scar Doesn't Save

*Ve-amar Rava krum she-ala machamat makah ba-veshet eino krum* — Rava rules that scar tissue which grows over a wound in the esophagus is halachically disregarded. It does not constitute a valid membrane and does not rescue the animal from the status of *treifa*.

### Scar Deterioration

Rashi explains the basis of this ruling: we assume that scar tissue of this kind will not endure — it will eventually reopen. The healing is therefore presumed to be temporary and insufficient to restore the animal's status.

### Status Irreversible

Rashi goes further and generalizes this principle: any scar tissue that develops over an injury, anywhere in the animal, does not reverse the *treifa* status once it has been established. The one exception we noted earlier — a hole in the gallbladder that is covered by the overlying liver — is not analogous, because there the hole was sealed from the very first moment of injury and never had the status of an open hole. This is categorically different from a hole that existed, rendered the animal a *treifa*, and subsequently closed over with scar tissue.

One might ask whether the ruling rests on the conceptual principle that *treifa* status, once acquired, is irreversible — or whether it rests on the empirical assumption that scar tissue will reopen. The question has practical implications: if it is purely conceptual, the rule applies universally; if it is empirical, one could imagine cases where the evidence warrants a different assessment. The *gemara* does not resolve this explicitly, but Rashi's formulation leans toward the empirical explanation, at least as the primary rationale for this particular case.

This question is also central to the laws of *glatt* meat. The word *glatt* means smooth — referring to lungs that are free of adhesions. When adhesions are present, the question arises whether they are covering a hole in the lung. If they are, and if a hole once existed, the animal would be a *treifa* despite the adhesion — consistent with the principle that scar tissue does not reverse the status.

## Thorn Concern

### Ulla's Thorn Rule

*Amar Ulla yashav lo kotz ba-veshet ein chosheshin shema hivri* — Ulla teaches: if a thorn is found lodged in the wall of an animal's esophagus, we are not concerned that it may have pierced all the way through the wall and that the outer layer subsequently healed over. Rashi gives two possible readings of the word *hivri*: either it means "healed," so that we are not concerned that the thorn pierced through and the wound then closed; or, following the *aruch*, it means "pierced," so that we are simply not concerned that the partial penetration we observe extended all the way through. In either reading, the ruling is that the visible partial penetration does not obligate us to assume the worst.

### Safek Principles

The *gemara* immediately presses: *u-le-Ulla mai shena mi-safek dirusah* — why is this different from the case of doubtful *drusa*, where we do check for possible hidden damage? Ulla's apparent position is that in cases of doubt we are not concerned — *ein chosheshin le-safek dirusah* — and the *gemara* now tests this against a series of parallels.

### Comparison Cases

*U-mai shena mi-shtei chatichot achat shel chelev ve-achat shel shuman* — what about two pieces of food, one forbidden fat and one permitted fat? Surely there we are strict even though we cannot identify which is which. The *gemara* answers: *hatam itchazek issura* — there, the existence of a forbidden item is already established; one of the two pieces is definitively forbidden, and we must take it seriously. Here, by contrast, we have never established that any problem exists at all; there is merely a thorn that may or may not have penetrated fully.

*U-mai shena me-ha-shochet be-sakin ve-nimtzet peguma* — what about the case of a *shochet* who slaughtered with a knife that was later found to be nicked? There we are concerned that the nick may have existed at the time of *shechita* and invalidated it. The *gemara* answers: *hatam ityelid rei'uta be-sakin* — there, we see that a defect actually materialized in the knife, which gives us concrete reason to wonder whether it existed earlier. Here, no defect has materialized; we merely observe a thorn partially embedded.

*U-mai shena mi-safek tumah bi-reshut ha-yachid de-sfeiko tamei* — what about doubtful *tumah* in a private domain, where the rule is that we rule strictly? The *gemara* responds: *u-le-ta'ameich de-amrinan safek tumah bi-reshut ha-rabim sfeiko tahor* — but by the same token, in a public domain doubtful *tumah* results in a ruling of *tahor*. Why? *ella hatam hilcheta gemiri lah mi-sotah* — the rules of doubtful *tumah* are derived from the case of *sotah* and are sui generis; they cannot simply be exported to govern doubts in the laws of *treifos*.

The *gemara* here cycles through multiple cases of doubt — some where we are strict, some where we are lenient — without settling on a unified principle. *tosafot* observes that what the *gemara* conspicuously omits is the concept of *chezkat heter* — the presumption that an animal, once validly slaughtered, is permissible. That presumption would provide a clean and general basis for leniency in all doubtful cases. The *gemara*, however, does not invoke it here.

### Rav Kahana's Answer

A resolution is finally offered by Rav Kahana. *yativ hahu me-rabbanan kamei de-rav kahana ve-yativ ve-ka'amar* — a scholar was sitting before Rav Kahana and stated: Ulla's ruling that we are not concerned applies only when the thorn is found simply loose inside the hollow of the esophagus — but if it is found lodged in the wall, we *are* concerned that it may have penetrated all the way through. *amar lei rav kahana: lo ttzitu lei* — Rav Kahana rejected this. *yashav itamar* — Ulla's teaching was specifically about a thorn lodged in the wall, and even in that case we are not concerned.

As for a thorn found merely loose in the hollow of the esophagus — that case would never have needed to be stated. *de-hulchei chivya baraita kotzei achli* — animals of the field eat thorns all the time; of course one might be found in the esophagus. Ulla's *chiddush* is precisely the more stringent-seeming case: even when the thorn is embedded in the wall of the esophagus, we do not assume it pierced all the way through.
