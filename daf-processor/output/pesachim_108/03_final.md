# Pesachim 108 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pre-Seder Eating

### Opening & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָתֵי לְאִימְּנוֹעֵי מִלְּמִיעְבַּד פִּסְחָא. אוֹ דִילְמָא, סָמוּךְ לְמִנְחָה קְטַנָּה תְּנַן — וּמִשּׁוּם מַצָּה, דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְמֵיכְלַהּ לְמַצָּה אֲכִילָה גַּסָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **and he will end up refraining from performing the Paschal lamb? Or perhaps we learned** this *halakha* in the mishna as pertaining to the time **adjacent to the lesser *minḥa*, and** the reason for the prohibition is **due to *matza*.** If one eats shortly before nightfall, **perhaps he will come to eat the *matza*** as an act of **excessive eating,** when one forces himself to eat despite the fact that he has no desire to do so.

The daf opens at the very bottom of 107b, marking a transition in the tractate: we have moved from the laws of *kiddush* and *havdalah* into the practical concerns of *erev haseder* itself.

### Mincha Ketana Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי (יוֹסֵי): אֲבָל מְטַבֵּיל הוּא בְּמִינֵי תַּרְגִּימָא. רַבִּי יִצְחָק מְטַבֵּיל בְּיַרְקֵי. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַשַּׁמָּשׁ מְטַבֵּיל בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִין וְנוֹתְנָן לִפְנֵי הָאוֹרְחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said:** It is prohibited to eat a proper meal from *minḥa* time onward; **however, one** may **dip** and eat **types of refreshments,** e.g., fruit or meat that do not constitute a full meal and will not fill one's stomach. The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Yitzḥak** would **dip** and eat **vegetables. That** opinion, that it is permitted to snack after *minḥa* time on Passover eve, **was also taught** in a *baraita*: During the afternoon of Passover eve, **the waiter** may **dip in the intestines** of the animals that had been slaughtered in preparation for the Festival meals **and place them before the guests** who had registered for the Paschal lamb. This was done to whet their appetites, so they would eat the Paschal lamb and *matza* that evening with greater relish.

The *mishnah* states that one may not eat before nightfall, beginning from *mincha* onward. The *gemara*, as it has already reiterated at the opening of the *perek*, specifies that this refers to *mincha ketana* — that is, from approximately 3:30 in the afternoon, meaning the prohibition effectively begins at 3:00 p.m. The concern is straightforward: one must not arrive at the *seder* already full, since the appetite for *matzah* must be intact.

### Permitted Snacks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִירוּ לָכֶם נִיר וְאַל תִּזְרְעוּ אֶל קוֹצִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And although there is no** absolute **proof for** this **matter,** there **is an allusion to** this **matter,** as **it is stated: "Break up for yourselves a fallow ground, and do not sow among thorns"** (Jeremiah 4:3). This verse teaches that one must undertake preparations to achieve positive results. Similarly, one should eat a small amount in the afternoon to enable him to consume more in the evening.

Certain snacks remain permitted even during this restricted period. The *gemara* refers to *minei targima* — light foods such as fruits and vegetables. According to Tosafot, even small amounts of *mezonot* that are *kosher l'pesach* might be permitted. The *gemara* also describes people who would partake of *b'nei me'ayim* — bits of the intestines that were to be used for the meat eaten that night — as a kind of pre-meal appetizer. This is not merely tolerated but may even be preferable, since it whets the appetite. The *gemara* supports this notion through a *pasuk* cited as a *zecher ladavar*: *niru lachem nir v'al tizre'u el kotzim* — "plow for yourselves a plowed field and do not plant among the thorns." Just as one must prepare the field before planting, one should prepare the stomach before eating, and a small amount of food accomplishes precisely that.

### Rava's Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא הֲוָה שָׁתֵי חַמְרָא כּוּלֵּי מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא דְפִיסְחָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנִיגְרְרֵיהּ לְלִיבֵּיהּ דְּנֵיכוֹל מַצָּה טְפֵי לְאוּרְתָּא. אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ דְּחַמְרָא מִיגְרָר גָּרֵיר, דִּתְנַן:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rava would drink wine the entire day of Passover eve, so** as to **whet his appetite** to enable him **to eat more *matza* at night. Rava said: From where do I say it, that wine whets** the appetite? **As we learned** in a mishna:

The *gemara* continues: Rava would make a point of drinking wine every *erev pesach* — *rava hava shati chamra kol ma'ale yoma d'pischa* — specifically in order to whet his appetite so that he would eat *matzah* with greater desire that evening. As Rava himself explains: *minan amina lei d'chamra migrar garir* — from where do I know that wine draws the appetite? From the *mishnah*, as we will see.

### Extra Cups Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין הַכּוֹסוֹת הַלָּלוּ אִם רָצָה לִשְׁתּוֹת — יִשְׁתֶּה, בֵּין שְׁלִישִׁי לִרְבִיעִי — לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִסְעָד סָעֵיד, אַמַּאי יִשְׁתֶּה? הָא קָא אָכֵיל לְמַצָּה אֲכִילָה גַּסָּה. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מִגְרָר גָּרֵיר.
>
> **Translation:** During the Passover seder, **between these cups** that one is obligated to drink, e.g., between the first two of the four cups of wine, **if one wants to drink he** may **drink.** However, **between the third and fourth** cups, which are consumed after the meal, **one** may **not drink. And if you say that** wine **satisfies** a person, **why** may **one drink** extra cups? **He will** later **eat *matza*** when he is already satiated, which will constitute **an excessive eating. Rather, learn from** this **that** wine **whets** the appetite.

Rava derives his principle from the *mishnah*'s ruling that between the prescribed cups — between the first and second and between the second and third — one is permitted to drink additional wine: *bein hakosot halalu im ratza lishtot yishte*. The question Rava presses is this: if wine were actually satiating, why would additional cups be permitted before the eating of *matzah*? Drinking between the first and second cup could fill one up before the *matzah* of *hamotzi*, and drinking between the second and third cup could impair one's appetite for the *matzah* of *afikoman*. The fact that the *mishnah* permits this extra drinking demonstrates that wine does not satiate but rather stimulates the appetite — *migrar garir*.

### 3rd–4th Cup Ban

This raises an obvious question: if wine whets the appetite, why is drinking between the third and fourth cups prohibited — *bein shlishi l'revi'i lo yishte*? Here the *gemara* shifts to a different concern entirely. The Rashbam explains that once *birkat hamazon* has been recited over the third cup, there is no further need to stimulate the appetite, since the primary *matzah* obligations have already been fulfilled. Accordingly, the *gemara* offers two reasons for the prohibition on drinking between the third and fourth cups. First, the Rashbam: *lo shrana lei dimichzei k'mosif al hakosot* — at that point, additional drinking merely looks like adding to the four cups with no justification, since the appetite-whetting purpose is no longer operative. Second, from the Yerushalmi: *kedei shelo yishtaker* — so that one should not become drunk and thereby be incapable of completing *hallel* at the end of the *seder*. The Yerushalmi itself then raises a counterpoint: has one not already become drunk from the wine consumed during the meal? The answer is that drinking during a meal, when the stomach is occupied with food, does not produce the same intoxicating effect as drinking after a meal on an empty stomach.

It is worth noting the character of this entire discussion. The arguments about whether wine whets or satiates the appetite are treated as logical and *halachic* in nature rather than empirical — a reminder that in the *gemara*'s framework, legal analysis and conceptual reasoning operate independently of observed physiological fact.

### Rav Sheshet Fast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת הֲוָה יָתֵיב בְּתַעֲנִיתָא כׇּל מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא דְפִסְחָא. נֵימָא קָא סָבַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: סָמוּךְ לְמִנְחָה גְּדוֹלָה תְּנַן — וּמִשּׁוּם פִּסְחָא הוּא, דִּילְמָא מִימְּשַׁךְ וְאָתֵי לְאִימְּנוֹעֵי מִלְּמֶעְבַּד פִּיסְחָא הוּא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Sheshet would fast the entire eve of Passover.** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that **Rav Sheshet maintains** that this practice was necessary because of two factors? First, when the mishna states that one may not eat adjacent to *minḥa* time, **we learned** this ruling with regard to the period of time **adjacent to the greater *minḥa*, and** the reason for the prohibition **is due to the Paschal lamb, lest one be drawn** after one's meal **and come to refrain from performing** the sacrifice of **the Paschal lamb.**

The *gemara* records that Rav Sheshet would fast every *erev pesach*: *rav sheshet havai yativ b'tanisa kol ma'ale yoma d'pischa*. On first reading, one might assume this reflects an extreme stringency regarding the concern about appetite — that he did not believe that a small amount of eating was beneficial and simply refrained entirely.

### Korban Distraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר לַהּ כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַכְשִׁיר הָיָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא בְּפֶסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שַׁחֲרִית בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר לִשְׁמוֹ, וּמִצַּפְרָא זְמַן פִּסְחָא הוּא, דְּכוּלֵּי יוֹמָא חֲזֵי לְפִסְחָא,
>
> **Translation:** **And** second, **Rav Sheshet maintains in accordance with that** statement **that Rabbi Oshaya said** that **Rabbi Elazar said: Ben Beteira would deem valid a Paschal lamb that was slaughtered in the morning on the fourteenth** of Nisan **for its own purpose,** as **from the morning it is** already the **time** during which **a Paschal lamb** may be sacrificed, **as the whole day is fit for the Paschal lamb.**

The *gemara* however entertains a different explanation. Perhaps Rav Sheshet held that the *mishnah*'s prohibition begins from *mincha gedola* — noon — and that the reason is not hunger but rather distraction from bringing the *korban pesach*: one must not be preoccupied with eating when the time for the offering has arrived. This would explain fasting from midday onward, though it leaves unexplained why he fasted the entire day. The *gemara* resolves this by citing the opinion of Ben Betira, who held that the *korban pesach* slaughtered in the morning of the fourteenth — *b'arba'asa lishmo* — is valid *bedieved*, because the entire day is in some sense fit for the *korban pesach*. Rav Sheshet would adopt the interpretive tradition that *bein ha'arbayim* refers not to the afternoon hours but to the period between the night of the thirteenth and the night of the fourteenth — meaning the entire day of the fourteenth — and therefore the concern about distraction from the *korban pesach* applies from the earliest hours of the day. This is a striking reading, and the *gemara* itself notes its strangeness on multiple levels: that the prohibition would apply even nowadays after the *churban*, that it would extend to the hours that are only *bedieved* valid for the *korban pesach*, and that even the smallest amount of eating would constitute distraction sufficient to trigger the prohibition.

### Later Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּסָבַר ״בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם״ — בֵּין עֶרֶב דְּאֶתְמוֹל לְעֶרֶב דְּהָאִידָּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **As** ben Beteira **maintained** that when the Torah says the Paschal lamb must be sacrificed **"*bein ha'arbayim*"** (Exodus 12:6), which literally means: Between the evenings, but is often rendered: In the afternoon, the term refers to any time **between the evening of yesterday and the current evening** of the fourteenth. In other words, as Rav Sheshet maintained that the reason one may not eat on Passover eve is to prevent him from being distracted from preparing the Paschal lamb, and he also maintained that the Paschal offering may be sacrificed during the entire day of the fourteenth of Nisan, therefore, he would not eat that entire day.

Ultimately the *gemara* rejects this whole line of reasoning:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא, שָׁאנֵי רַב שֵׁשֶׁת דְּאִיסְתְּנִיס הֲוָה, דְּאִי טָעֵים בְּצַפְרָא מִידֵּי, לְאוּרְתָּא לָא הֲוָה מַהְנֵי לֵיהּ מֵיכְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They say** in response to this suggested interpretation of Rav Sheshet's practice: **No,** it is by no means clear that this was his reasoning. **Rav Sheshet was different, as he was delicate [*istenis*], for if he would taste some** food **in the morning,** the **food** he ate **at night would not be effective for him.** He would therefore fast the whole day so that he could eat *matza* at night with a hearty appetite.

*amri lo* — no, that was not Rav Sheshet's motivation at all. The answer is simply: *shani rav sheshet d'istnis havai* — Rav Sheshet was different because he was exceptionally finicky. For him, *de'ei ta'im b'zafra midi l'orsa lo havai mahani lei mikhla* — if he tasted even the smallest amount of food in the morning, he would have no interest in eating even come evening time. It was purely a matter of individual temperament, not a general ruling.

### Tosafot Nuance

Tosafot adds an important clarification regarding the nature of Rav Sheshet's practice. The phrase *havai yativ b'tanisa* — that he would sit in a fast — need not be taken literally to mean that he formally accepted a *ta'anis* upon himself. It may simply mean *shome'a atzmo mil'echol* — that he was careful not to eat. Alternatively, Tosafot suggests, he may have formally accepted a fast upon himself as a practical aid to memory, *kedei sheyisga v'yizaker mil'echol*, to help ensure he would not inadvertently eat. It is in this context that Tosafot introduces, almost as an aside, the famous custom of the firstborn fasting on *erev pesach*: *habechoros nahagu litanos erev pesach*. He cites this from *masechet Sofrim* — one of the minor tractates edited during the Geonic period, though likely containing earlier Tannaitic and Amoraic material — which records *she'ein misanin benissan ela bechoros be'erev pesach*. This custom is not the subject of the *gemara* here, but Tosafot takes the occasion to note its existence.

In summary, the prohibition against eating before the *seder* begins at *mincha ketana* — around 3:00 p.m. — and applies to substantial meals. It does not prohibit all food; small amounts of *minei targima* are permitted and may even be desirable for whetting the appetite. The prohibition extends to *matzah* the entire day, and according to Tosafot, arguably to *matzah ashirah* made with fruit juices, which might constitute a substantial enough eating to be prohibited from 3:00 p.m. onward.

## Haseiba

### Poor Person Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִירוּ לָכֶם נִיר וְאַל תִּזְרְעוּ אֶל קוֹצִים״. וַאֲפִילּוּ עָנִי שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא יֹאכַל עַד שֶׁיָּסֵב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And although there is no** absolute **proof for** this **matter,** there **is an allusion to** this **matter,** as **it is stated: "Break up for yourselves a fallow ground, and do not sow among thorns"** (Jeremiah 4:3). This verse teaches that one must undertake preparations to achieve positive results. Similarly, one should eat a small amount in the afternoon to enable him to consume more in the evening. We learned in the mishna that **even the poorest of Jews** should **not eat until he reclines.**

The *mishnah* establishes that *v'afilu ani shebe'yisrael lo yochal ad she'yeisav* — even the poorest person in Israel may not eat on *pesach* until he reclines. This statement will bear closer examination shortly; for now, the emphasis is on the universality of the requirement. No one, regardless of economic station, is exempt from *haseibah*.

### Matzah Maror

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: מַצָּה צָרִיךְ הֲסִיבָּה, מָרוֹר אֵין צָרִיךְ הֲסִיבָּה. יַיִן, אִיתְּמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: צָרִיךְ הֲסִיבָּה. וְאִיתְּמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: אֵין צָרִיךְ הֲסִיבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated that *amora'im* discussed the requirement to recline. Everyone agrees that ***matza* requires reclining,** i.e., one must recline when eating *matza*, and **bitter herbs do not require reclining.** With regard to **wine, it was stated in the name of Rav Naḥman** that wine **requires reclining, and it was** also **stated in the name of Rav Naḥman** that wine **does not require reclining.**

The *gemara* records: *matzah tzarich haseibah* — eating *matzah* requires reclining, since *matzah* is a symbol of freedom and *haseibah* is a symbol of freedom. *Maror ein tzarich haseibah* — *maror* does not require reclining. Indeed, it would be something of a mixed message to recline while eating *maror*, whose entire significance is bound up with the experience of enslavement.

### Wine Cups

When it comes to wine, the *gemara* records what appears to be a contradiction within the teachings attributed to Rav Nachman himself: *itmar mishmeih d'Rav Nachman tzarich haseibah*, and also *itmar mishmeih d'Rav Nachman ein tzarich haseibah*. One tradition requires reclining for wine; another does not. This is surprising, since one might have expected wine — the quintessential symbol of aristocratic freedom — to require *haseibah* unambiguously.

## Haseiba (II)

### All Cups Safe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא בְּתַרְתֵּי כָּסֵי קַמָּאֵי, הָא בְּתַרְתֵּי כָּסֵי בָּתְרָאֵי. אָמְרִי לַהּ לְהַאי גִּיסָא וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְהַאי גִּיסָא. אָמְרִי לַהּ לְהַאי גִּיסָא: תְּרֵי כָּסֵי קַמָּאֵי — בָּעוּ הֲסִיבָּה, דְּהַשְׁתָּה הוּא דְּקָא מַתְחֲלָא לַהּ חֵירוּת. תְּרֵי כָּסֵי בָּתְרָאֵי — לָא בָּעוּ הֲסִיבָּה, מַאי דַּהֲוָה הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And** these two statements **do not disagree** with each other: **This** statement is referring **to the first two cups, and that** statement is referring **to the last two cups.** However, it was not clear which two cups require reclining according to Rav Naḥman. **Some say** the explanation **in this manner and some say it in that manner.** The Gemara elaborates: **Some say it in this manner,** that **the first two cups require reclining, as it is now that freedom begins.** Since reclining is a sign of freedom, while discussing the exodus from Egypt it is appropriate to drink while reclining. By contrast, the **last two cups do not require reclining,** because **what was** already **was.** In other words, by this point one has completed the discussion of the Exodus and has reached the latter stages of the seder.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *v'lo pligi* — these two traditions do not actually dispute one another. One applies to the first two cups and the other to the last two. But the *gemara* then notes that this distinction is itself disputed — *amri lah l'hai gisa v'amri lah l'hai gisa* — with one view holding that the first two cups require *haseibah* because it is during the recitation of the *haggadah* that the movement from *shibud l'geulah* takes place, and another view holding that only the last two cups require *haseibah* because it is only after the *geulah* has been fully articulated that one stands in a genuine experience of freedom. Since neither resolution commands consensus, the *gemara* concludes: *idi v'idi ba'ei haseibah* — one should recline for all four cups. The Ran addresses the question of why we do not apply the general principle of *sfeika d'rabanan l'kula*. His answer is that *sfeika d'rabanan l'kula* operates when the scope of an established institution is uncertain in particular cases; it does not operate where applying it would entirely undermine the institution. If we were to apply leniency here, the result would be that *haseibah* is never required for wine — which would hollow out the institution entirely. Hence we require *haseibah* for all four.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְהַאי גִּיסָא: אַדְּרַבָּה, תְּרֵי כָּסֵי בָּתְרָאֵי — בָּעוּ הֲסִיבָּה, הָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא דְּקָא הָוְיָא חֵירוּת. תְּרֵי כָּסֵי קַמָּאֵי — לָא בָּעוּ הֲסִיבָּה, דְּאַכַּתִּי ״עֲבָדִים הָיִינוּ״ קָאָמַר. הַשְׁתָּא דְּאִיתְּמַר הָכִי וְאִיתְּמַר הָכִי, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בָּעוּ הֲסִיבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say it in that manner** and claim that **on the contrary,** the **last two cups require reclining,** as it is **at that time that there is freedom.** However, the **first two cups do not require reclining, as one still says: We were slaves.** The Gemara concludes: **Now that it was stated so, and it was stated so,** i.e., there are two conflicting opinions and it cannot be proven which two cups require reclining, both **these** sets of cups **and those require reclining.**

It is worth noting in passing that cups two and three seem, on the merits, to be the strongest candidates for *haseibah*: by the end of the second cup, *asher ge'alanu* has been recited and the story of redemption has been told, and throughout the third cup the meal of *matzah* — the symbol of *cheirus* — has been eaten. The first cup stands prior to the telling, and the fourth cup follows the conclusion of the formal narrative. Yet despite this intuition, the *gemara* frames the choice as first-two versus last-two rather than cups two and three.

### Tosafot Meal Focus

Tosafot engages this question with nuance. He addresses why *matzah* continues to require *haseibah* even according to the view that limits wine's *haseibah* to the first two cups — since *matzah* comes after the second cup, after *birkat hage'ulah* has already been recited. He offers two answers, and they point in different directions conceptually.

The first answer is that *matzah* is the *ikar seudah* — the heart of the meal itself — and *haseibah* is fundamentally a feature of how one eats a meal, not merely of how one performs symbolic ritual eating. This is an important observation that reflects the original character of *haseibah* as described in the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* states simply that a person may not eat until he reclines — *lo yochal ad she'yeisav* — without connecting *haseibah* to specific ritual acts at all. *Haseibah* was the posture of the wealthy Roman citizen at his meal; it was the meal itself that was symbolic. What the *gemara* does is reframe *haseibah* as an accompaniment to specific ritual eating acts — *matzah*, *yayin*, *maror* — and that is indeed how most people think about it today. But Tosafot here pulls back toward the original conception: the reason *matzah* requires *haseibah* regardless of where it falls in the narrative arc is that *matzah* is the meal, and *haseibah* belongs to the meal.

The second answer is that *matzah* is intrinsically inscribed as a symbol of *cheirus*, as the Torah itself records — *shelo hispik bitzekam lehachmitz ad shenigalu* — and therefore whenever *matzah* is eaten, it requires *haseibah*, whereas wine's symbolic significance is more dependent on its position within the structure of the *seder*. According to this second answer, *haseibah* for *matzah* is limited to the ritual *kezayit* at *hamotzi* and the *afikoman*, not to all *matzah* consumed during the meal.

Tosafot thus presents two distinct conceptual frameworks. According to the first, *haseibah* is fundamentally about the meal — and therefore applies to all *matzah* eaten during the meal. According to the second, *haseibah* is about symbolic ritual acts of *geulah* — and therefore applies only to the two primary *kezayit* obligations. The practical implications for eating *matzah* throughout the course of the meal differ accordingly, and many *rishonim* follow the more restrictive approach, limiting *haseibah* to the formal first and last *kezayit*. Tosafot, however, invites us to consider the broader conception embedded in the *mishnah*'s original formulation.

## Haseiba Positions

### Back Flat Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּרַקְדָּן לָא שְׁמֵיהּ הֲסִיבָּה. הֲסִיבַּת יָמִין לָא שְׁמַהּ הֲסִיבָּה. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁמָּא יַקְדִּים קָנֶה לְוֶושֶׁט, וְיָבֹא לִידֵי סַכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the *halakha* of reclining. **Lying on one's back is not called reclining. Reclining to the right is not called reclining,** as free men do not recline in this manner. People prefer to recline on their left and use their right hand to eat, whereas they find it more difficult to eat the other way. **And not only that, but** if one reclines to the right, **perhaps the windpipe will precede the esophagus.** The food will enter the windpipe, **and one will come into danger** of choking.

The *gemara* now turns to the physical mechanics of *haseibah*. Two positions are disqualified. First, *pirkadan lo shme haseibah* — lying flat on one's back does not count as *haseibah*. *Haseibah* requires reclining to the side; flat on the back is neither a normal eating position nor one that carries the requisite symbolic significance. Second, Rava rules: *haseibah yemin lo shme haseibah* — reclining on the right side does not constitute valid *haseibah*.

### Trachea Risk

The *gemara* adds a further concern regarding right-side reclining: *v'lo od ella shema yakdim kaneh l'veshet* — one risks having food enter the trachea before the esophagus, creating a danger of choking. This raises the question of why reclining on the right would produce this anatomical risk.

### Rashi Rashbam

Rashi and the Rashbam disagree on how to read the *gemara*'s medical concern. Rashi holds that the trachea and esophagus are oriented front to back rather than left to right. On his reading, the danger of food entering the trachea is not specifically associated with right-side reclining but with lying flat on one's back: in that position, the flap over the esophagus functions improperly, leaving the trachea exposed. The Rashbam objects to Rashi's reading on literary grounds — the *gemara* introduces the medical concern immediately after discussing right-side reclining, not after discussing lying flat on one's back, which suggests the concern is specifically linked to right-side reclining. The Rashbam therefore maintains that the danger is indeed specific to reclining on the right side.

### Medieval Anatomy

The Rashbam's reading generates its own difficulty: if the trachea and esophagus are positioned front to back, how would reclining to the right or left affect the risk of food entering the trachea? A number of *rishonim* resolve this by reading the *gemara* as implicitly assuming that the trachea and esophagus are positioned left to right rather than front to back. On that assumption, if the trachea is located to the left, reclining on the right would tilt the trachea downward, creating the risk. This anatomical assumption is, of course, at variance with what is known from dissection and from the *gemara*'s own discussions of *shechita*, where the trachea and esophagus are consistently treated as front to back. The impulse to salvage a coherent anatomical explanation from the *gemara*'s language is understandable, but the assumption it requires is difficult to sustain.

## Haseiba Positions (II)

### Lefty Problem

This anatomical question has direct *halachic* consequences for left-handed individuals. If left-side reclining is required because that is the natural position for a right-handed person to eat comfortably — freeing the right hand to handle food — then a left-handed person would naturally prefer to recline on the right. But if the prohibition on right-side reclining is grounded in the anatomical premise that the trachea sits to the left and is therefore endangered when one leans right, then even a left-handed person may not lean on the right, since the anatomical risk would apply equally regardless of handedness. The *Shulchan Aruch* follows this latter position and rules that even left-handed persons must recline on the left.

### Unusual Position

A different explanation, attributed to the *Yere'im* and others among the *rishonim*, avoids the anatomical difficulty entirely. On this view, the concern that food will enter the trachea has nothing to do with the positioning of the organs relative to one another. Rather, it is a function of eating in an awkward or unnatural position: when a person eats in a way that strains or distorts the normal act of swallowing, food is more likely to go down the wrong passage. The reason reclining on the right is problematic for a right-handed person is precisely that it is an uncomfortable and unusual way to eat — and eating in an unusual way creates risk. Accordingly, the rule becomes: lean on whichever side allows you to eat naturally and comfortably. A right-handed person reclines on the left; a left-handed person reclines on the right. This reading has significant *halachic* appeal and resolves the conflict with the anatomy known from *shechita*. The *Shulchan Aruch*'s ruling notwithstanding, this remains the practice of many left-handed individuals today.

## Exemptions

### Woman & Husband

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשָּׁה אֵצֶל בַּעְלָהּ לָא בָּעֲיָא הֲסִיבָּה, וְאִם אִשָּׁה חֲשׁוּבָה הִיא — צְרִיכָה הֲסִיבָּה. בֵּן אֵצֶל אָבִיו בָּעֵי הֲסִיבָּה. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: תַּלְמִיד אֵצֶל רַבּוֹ מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **A woman** who is **with her husband is not required to recline, but if she is an important woman, she is required to recline. A son** who is **with his father is required to recline. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **a student** who is **with his teacher?** Perhaps he is not obligated to recline, as he is in awe of his rabbi, and reclining is a sign of complete freedom and independence.

The *gemara* rules: *isha etzel ba'alah lo ba'ei haseibah* — a woman in the presence of her husband is not required to recline, because the social reality of her subservient status renders the gesture incongruous or potentially offensive to the husband. However, *v'im isha chashuva hi* — if she is a distinguished woman, one who carries a recognized degree of independence in her social standing, then *tzricha haseibah* — she is required to recline.

This ruling is striking in its irony. The *mishnah* insisted that even the *ani* — the most economically marginal person — must recline and demonstrate freedom. Yet the *gemara* here acknowledges that for some people, the very social realities of their lives cannot be set aside even on *seder* night. The *gemara*'s reasoning appears to operate on one of two tracks: either that reclining in this context would constitute an affront to the husband's honor, or that a gesture performed in a context that renders it incongruous loses its symbolic force and becomes meaningless. The tension with the *mishnah*'s universal declaration is real and palpable.

### Ashkenazi Women

Later *rishonim* of Ashkenaz, quoted by the Rema, made a sweeping declaration: *kol hanashim shelanu chashuvot hen* — all our women are *chashuvot*. This statement was rooted in a sociological reality: in medieval Ashkenaz, women functioned as independent economic agents, businesswomen who operated publicly and were not perceived as simply subservient to their husbands in the way that may have characterized the social environment reflected in the *gemara*. The *rishonim* were not apologizing for or reinterpreting the *gemara*; they were applying its logic — which always linked the exemption to perceived social subservience — to a reality in which that subservience no longer applied. The *gemara* clearly creates a class distinction among women based on social status; what the Ashkenazic *rishonim* did was effectively collapse that distinction for their community. This broader recognition of women's social emancipation in Ashkenaz surfaces in other areas of Tosafot's discussions as well, including complex questions around *yichud* and the reality that women engaged in commercial dealings requiring them to be in private with men, including non-Jewish men.

### Son Student

The *gemara* rules that a son in his father's presence is *required* to recline — *ben etzel aviv ba'a haseibah* — the filial relationship does not create a degree of awe that overrides the obligation. The more fraught question is that of a student before his teacher.

### Abaye Account

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, (אֲמַר) אַבָּיֵי: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי מָר זְגֵינַן אַבִּירְכֵי דַהֲדָדֵי, כִּי אָתֵינַן לְבֵי רַב יוֹסֵף, אָמַר לַן: לָא צְרִיכִתוּ, מוֹרָא רַבָּךְ כְּמוֹרָא שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof that **Abaye said: When we were in the house of** my **Master,** Rabba, there was not enough room for everyone to recline on Passover, so **we reclined on each other's knees,** to fulfill the obligation to recline. **When we came to the house of Rav Yosef,** he **said to us: You need not** recline, as **the fear of your teacher is like the fear of Heaven.** A student is subject to the authority of his teacher and may not display freedom in his presence.

Abaye provides the empirical evidence: *ki havinan bei mar Rabba zageinan a-birchei de-hadadei* — when we were at the house of our master Rabba, we would recline across one another's laps, approximating *haseibah* in the absence of proper couches. Thus at Rabba's table, the students did in fact recline. But *ki atinan l'vei Rav Yosef* — when Abaye and the students came to the academy of Rav Yosef, who became the leading master after Rabba's death, the situation was different.

### Rav Yosef

Rav Yosef told the students: *lo tzarichitu* — you do not need to recline. His reasoning: *mora rabach k'mora shamayim* — the awe of your master should be like the awe of Heaven. In this context, reclining would be inappropriate, either because it would be disrespectful to the master or because the gesture of freedom cannot carry its symbolic weight in a setting defined by that level of awe and subservience. The *gemara*'s phrasing, *lo tzarichitu* rather than *asur lachem*, is notable — it says "you do not need to" rather than "you are prohibited from doing so," leaving open the precise nature of the exemption.

### Rav Talmid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: עִם הַכֹּל אָדָם מֵיסֵב, וַאֲפִילּוּ תַּלְמִיד אֵצֶל רַבּוֹ! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — בִּשְׁוַלְיָא דְנַגָּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection: A person must recline** in the presence **of anyone, and even a student** who is **with his teacher** must do so. This *baraita* directly contradicts the statement of Rav Yosef. The Gemara answers: **When that** *baraita* **was taught,** it was **with regard to a craftsman's apprentice,** not a student of Torah in the company of his rabbi. One who is in the presence of a person teaching him a trade is not in awe of his instructor, and he is therefore obligated to recline.

When a *baraita* appears to contradict Rav Yosef — stating *im kol adam meisav va'afilu talmid etzel rabo*, that a person reclines in the presence of anyone, even a student before his *rebbe* — the *gemara* resolves the tension by distinguishing between two types of master-subordinate relationship. *Ki tanya hahi b'shulya de'nagrei* — that *baraita* refers to an apprentice of a craftsman (*shulya de'nagrei*: an apprentice carpenter). A craft apprentice is in a relationship of economic and practical dependency with his master, but that relationship does not carry the same weight of *mora* — the deep, almost religious awe — that characterizes the relationship between a *talmid* and his *rabbinic* teacher. Hence the craft apprentice reclines, while the *talmid chacham* before his *rav* may not.

This distinction also raises the question of whether the *gemara*'s concern throughout is the preservation of honor and hierarchy — ensuring that the student does not appear presumptuous before his master — or whether it is the deeper claim that in a context of genuine subservience, *haseibah* simply lacks the symbolic resonance it requires to be meaningful. Both readings are plausible, and the choice between them matters: if it is about protecting honor, one would more readily excuse deference to a *rav* than to a craft-master; if it is about symbolic meaning, neither relationship would seem to disqualify *haseibah*, yet the entire point of the *seder* is that even the *ani* must enact freedom tonight.

### Shamash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שַׁמָּשׁ מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הַשַּׁמָּשׁ שֶׁאָכַל כְּזַיִת מַצָּה כְּשֶׁהוּא מֵיסֵב — יָצָא. מֵיסֵב — אִין, לֹא מֵיסֵב — לָא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בָּעֵי הֲסִיבָּה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **a waiter?** Is a waiter obligated to recline? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a solution, **as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: A waiter who ate an olive-bulk of *matza* while reclining has fulfilled** his obligation. The Gemara infers: If he ate *matza* while **reclining, yes,** he has fulfilled his obligation; if he was **not reclining, no,** he has not fulfilled the obligation. **Learn from** this that a waiter **requires reclining.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is the case.

The *gemara* poses a further question: *shamash mahu* — what is the status of an attendant at the meal? He is serving in an explicitly subservient capacity even at the *seder* itself. The *gemara* resolves the question with a ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *ha'shamash she'achal kezayit matzah keshehu meisav yatza* — an attendant who eats a *kezayit* of *matzah* while reclining has fulfilled his obligation. The formulation *meisav in, lo meisav lo* — only when reclining yes, not reclining no — makes the requirement explicit: the *shamash* must recline when eating *matzah*. The word *yatza* in this context further implies that failing to recline would mean the obligation has *not* been fulfilled, requiring the act to be repeated.

### Freedom Tension

What emerges from this entire discussion is a striking and somewhat troubling pattern. The *mishnah* insists on *haseibah* for everyone — *afilu ani shebe'yisrael*. The *gemara* then carves out a series of exceptions for people whose social reality involves ongoing subservience: a woman before her husband, a *talmid* before his *rebbe*. Yet the *shamash* — who is actively serving at this very meal — is required to recline. The distinction seems to operate not on the basis of one's current role but on the basis of one's ongoing social status: those whose lives are structurally defined by subservience to another cannot fully set that aside even on the night when freedom is supposed to be universally enacted. It is, as one must acknowledge, a profound tension at the heart of the *seder*'s symbolism.

## Women Cups

### Miracle Salvation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת בְּאַרְבָּעָה כּוֹסוֹת הַלָּלוּ, שֶׁאַף הֵן הָיוּ בְּאוֹתוֹ הַנֵּס.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: Women are obligated in these four cups** of wine at the Passover seder. **As they too were** included **in that miracle** of the Exodus, they are therefore obligated to participate in the celebration.

Having addressed *haseibah* for women, the *gemara* now turns to the question of women's obligation in the *arba kosot* altogether. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi rules: *nashim chayavot b'arba kosot halalu* — women are obligated in the four cups. The reason given is *she'af hem hayu b'oto hanes* — for they too were in that miracle, they too were redeemed from Egypt.

### Rashbam Agents

The Rashbam reads the word *af* — "even" or "also" — as pointing to something more active than mere inclusion in the redemption. He draws a parallel to the *gemara*'s identical formulation in connection with *ner chanukah* and *megillah* — in all three cases, the phrase *she'af hem hayu b'oto hanes* is used to explain women's obligation. The Rashbam's interpretation is that in all three cases, women were not merely beneficiaries of the miracle but active agents of the redemption. By the exodus from Egypt, the *midrash* identifies *nashim tzidkaniyot* as those who sustained Jewish continuity against Pharaoh's decrees, seducing their husbands to have children even when infants were being cast into the Nile. By Purim, Esther was the central agent of salvation. By Chanukah, the tradition of Yehudit is part of the relevant narrative.

### Tosafot Alt

Tosafot objects to the Rashbam's reading on the grounds that the word *af* does not naturally convey agency, and that on his reading the women would be the primary rather than additional agents — which is not quite what the language implies. Tosafot instead cites the Yerushalmi's more straightforward reading: women were included in the miracle of redemption along with everyone else, and that is sufficient to obligate them. But this raises a separate difficulty: if women were included in the miracle of the exodus, why are they not obligated in *sukkah*, which also commemorates the exodus? The answer Tosafot offers is that *sukkah* is a Biblical obligation, and the Biblical exemption of women from time-bound positive commandments — *mitzvot asei she'hazman grama* — cannot be overridden by the rabbinic reasoning that they participated in the miracle. These three obligations — the four cups, *megillah*, and *ner chanukah* — are all rabbinic enactments, and when the rabbis created these obligations from scratch, they built them in a manner that encompasses all who participated in the relevant miracle.

### Soloveitchik

Rav Soloveitchik offered a further explanation that ties these three cases together more tightly. He observed that what distinguishes *megillah*, *ner chanukah*, and the *arba kosot* from other commemorations of redemption is that all three are characterized by *pirsum hanes* — the public proclamation and demonstration of the miracle. *Megillah* and *ner chanukah* explicitly carry this rubric; the recitation of the *haggadah* and the drinking of the four cups are similarly outward, performative, communicative acts, aimed at broadcasting the experience of redemption to as wide an audience as possible. It is precisely this quality of publicizing and broadcasting the miracle that demands the participation of everyone — women and men alike — because the effectiveness of *pirsum hanes* is maximized through maximal participation. This explains why women are included in these three specific *mitzvot* in a way that does not extend to other commemorations of the exodus.

## Cup Measures

### Revi'is Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַרְבָּעָה כּוֹסוֹת הַלָּלוּ צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהֶן כְּדֵי מְזִיגַת כּוֹס יָפֶה. שְׁתָאָן חַי — יָצָא. שְׁתָאָן בְּבַת אַחַת — יָצָא. הִשְׁקָה מֵהֶן לְבָנָיו וְלִבְנֵי בֵיתוֹ — יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: These four cups must contain** enough undiluted wine to allow **for diluting a significant cup.** In talmudic times, people would not drink pure wine. They would dilute it with water, generally adding three times as much water as wine. If **one drank them undiluted,** he has **fulfilled** his obligation. If **one drank them all at once,** i.e., he poured all four cups of wine into one large cup and drank it, he has **fulfilled** the obligation. If **one gave his sons or the members of his household to drink from them,** he has nevertheless **fulfilled** the obligation.

The *gemara* now turns to the technical requirements governing the four cups themselves. Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: *arba kosot halalu tzarich she'yehei bahen kedei mezigas kos yafeh* — each of the four cups must contain sufficient wine to constitute the mixing of a proper cup. A *kos yafeh* — a proper, fitting cup — is defined as a *revi'is*, roughly three and a half ounces. In the era of the *gemara*, wine was drunk mixed with water in a ratio of three parts water to one part wine; accordingly, a *revi'is* of raw wine, divided into the four cups and then mixed, would yield four cups each containing a full *revi'is* of mixed wine.

### Raw Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתָאָן חַי יָצָא, אָמַר רָבָא: יְדֵי יַיִן — יָצָא, יְדֵי חֵירוּת — לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now addresses each of these rulings of Shmuel in turn. Shmuel said that if **one drank them undiluted** he has **fulfilled** his obligation. **Rava said:** He has **fulfilled** the obligation to drink the four cups of **wine,** but he has **not fulfilled** the obligation to drink in a way that expresses **freedom,** which is the preferable way to fulfill the mitzva, as aristocrats do not drink undiluted wine.

The *gemara* then introduces a series of conditional cases. *Shasan chai yatza* — if one drank the four cups with raw, unmixed wine, he has fulfilled his obligation. Raw wine was not the normal mode of drinking in the ancient world; nonetheless, it seems to count. However, as the Rashbam notes, this connects to the earlier discussion about *chamar medinah*: even when drinking something that requires a *revi'is* as a minimum measure, one must drink a full *revi'is* of that substance. So the ruling here is that drinking a *revi'is* of raw wine counts — not that drinking the smaller quantity that would otherwise be mixed into a *revi'is* of diluted wine counts.

### Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתָאָן בְּבַת אַחַת, רַב אָמַר: יְדֵי יַיִן — יָצָא, יְדֵי אַרְבָּעָה כּוֹסוֹת — לֹא יָצָא. הִשְׁקָה מֵהֶן לְבָנָיו וְלִבְנֵי בֵיתוֹ — יָצָא, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: וְהוּא דְּאִשְׁתִּי רוּבָּא דְכָסָא.
>
> **Translation:** If **one drank** the four cups **all at once, Rav said** that he has **fulfilled** the obligation to drink **wine** as an expression of rejoicing on the Festival, but he has **not fulfilled** the obligation to drink **four cups,** which requires four distinct cups, each drunk separately. Shmuel also stated that if **one gave his sons or the members of his household to drink from them,** he has nevertheless **fulfilled** his obligation. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: And this is** the case only **if he** himself **drank the majority of the cup.**

The *mishnah* also records two further conditional rulings. *Sheshasam b'vat achat yatza* — if one drank all four cups in rapid sequence rather than at their designated points in the *seder*, one has fulfilled the obligation. *Hishkan l'vanav ul'vnei beito yatza* — if one did not drink the cups personally but gave them to one's children or household members to drink, one has fulfilled the obligation. These rulings seem to significantly relax the requirements, and the *gemara* will proceed to substantially reinterpret them.

### Rava Clarify

Rava revisits each of these rulings and reframes them. Regarding raw wine: *yedei yayin yatza, yedei cheirus lo yatza* — one may have fulfilled the basic obligation of drinking wine, which suffices perhaps for the *bracha* of *borei pri hagafen* or for the dimension of *simchat yom tov*, but one has not fulfilled the obligation of the *arba kosot* as a demonstration of *cheirus*. The *arba kosot* require wine consumed in the socially normative, dignified manner — drinking raw, undiluted wine does not carry that connotation. Similarly, regarding sequential drinking: *yedei yayin yatza, yedei arba kosot lo yatza* — drinking all four cups at once may count as wine consumption but not as the structured, ceremonially differentiated four cups of the *seder*. Each cup must be drunk at its proper point in the *seder* proceedings.

### Taste Appear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אַרְבָּעָה כּוֹסוֹת הַלָּלוּ צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהֶן כְּדֵי רְבִיעִית, אֶחָד חַי וְאֶחָד מָזוּג, אֶחָד חָדָשׁ וְאֶחָד יָשָׁן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא בּוֹ טַעַם וּמַרְאֵה יַיִן. קָתָנֵי מִיהַת כְּדֵי רְבִיעִית, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ כּוֹס יָפֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the above rulings from a *baraita*: **These four cups must contain** one **quarter**-*log*, **whether** the wine **is undiluted or diluted, whether** it is **new or aged. Rabbi Yehuda says: It must have the taste and appearance of wine. In any event,** this *baraita* **is teaching** that each cup must contain at least the amount **of** one **quarter-***log*, **and** yet **you said** that each must contain enough for diluting **a significant cup.**

A *baraita* is cited that states the four cups must each contain a *revi'is*, and that this applies whether the wine is raw or mixed, new or old. Rabbi Yehuda adds a further requirement: *tzarich she'yehei bo ta'am u'mareh yayin* — the wine must have both the taste and the appearance of wine. Not every liquid that technically bears the name *yayin* qualifies; it must present itself recognizably as wine both to the palate and to the eye.

### Same Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי חַד שִׁיעוּרָא הוּא, מַאי ״כְּדֵי מְזִיגַת כּוֹס יָפֶה״ דְּקָאָמַר — לְכׇל חַד וְחַד, דְּהָוֵי לְהוּ כּוּלְּהוּ רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** **They say** in response that **this and that are one** and the same **measure.** The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of the expression: **Enough for diluting a significant cup, which** Shmuel **said?** He meant that there must be enough undiluted wine **for each and every one** of the cups, i.e., one quarter-*log* of diluted wine. **This** amounts to one **quarter**-*log* of undiluted wine **for all of them** combined. A significant cup contains one quarter-*log*. This quarter-*log* is comprised of one quarter undiluted wine and three quarters water. Therefore, each cup must contain at least one quarter of one quarter-*log* of undiluted wine, so that one consumes a full quarter-*log* of liquid from each cup.

The *gemara* then notes an apparent inconsistency: the *baraita* specifies a *revi'is* while Shmuel spoke of a *kos yafeh*. Are these the same measure or different? The *gemara* resolves this simply: *idi v'idi chad shi'ura hu* — they are one and the same measure; a *kos yafeh* and a *revi'is* are identical.

### Mixing Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא בּוֹ טַעַם וּמַרְאֶה. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״אַל תֵּרֶא יַיִן כִּי יִתְאַדָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* stated that **Rabbi Yehuda says** the cup from which one drinks **must have** the **taste and appearance** of wine. **Rava said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda? As it is written: "Look not upon wine when it is red"** (Proverbs 23:31). This verse proves that the appearance of wine and not only its taste is important.

The *gemara* then inquires into the precise meaning of Shmuel's formulation — *kedei mezigas kos yafeh*, enough to produce a proper mixed cup. One possible interpretation is that one needs a *revi'is* of raw wine which, when divided across four cups and then each portion mixed with water in the appropriate ratio, yields four cups each containing a full *revi'is* of mixed wine. This interpretation integrates Shmuel's language about mixing with the quantitative requirement of a *revi'is* per cup, and sets up the further discussion — to be continued on the following daf — regarding the taste and appearance requirements articulated by Rabbi Yehuda and how those interact with the measure of the cups.
