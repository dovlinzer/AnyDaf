# Zevachim 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kinnim & Olah/Chatas

### Kinnim Overview

Today's daf is 68, a relatively compact page whose complexity derives from its engagement with *kinnim* — the subject of an entire tractate at the end of Zevachim that consists exclusively of *mishnah* with no *gemara*, devoted entirely to the laws of mixed bird sacrifices. The classic case involves a *chatas* and an *olah* brought by a woman after childbirth, and the intricate questions that arise when those birds become mixed up and offered in the wrong manner. An additional layer of indeterminacy arises from the fact that the woman may hand the birds to the *kohen* without designating which is which, leaving the *kohen* to determine the assignment himself.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Position

The *gemara* brings this material into view because of a position stated by Rabbi Yehoshua: if a bird *olah* is performed below — in the manner of a *chatas* — it does not incur *me'ilah*. The *gemara* had initially assumed that this meant the *olah* actually transforms into a *chatas*. It then sought to bring evidence from *kinnim* that no such transformation occurs.

### Gemara Evidence

The evidence from *kinnim* establishes that an *olah* done in the manner of a *chatas* does not genuinely become a *chatas*. The *gemara* proceeds to demonstrate why that evidence is dispositive: because the author of the tractate *Kinnim* is none other than Rabbi Yehoshua himself.

### Authorial Voice

The *gemara*'s point is that if Rabbi Yehoshua authored *Kinnim*, and *Kinnim* demonstrates that performing an *olah* below does not convert it into a *chatas*, then Rabbi Yehoshua himself never intended to say that such a conversion occurs. His original statement was more limited: performing an *olah* in the manner of a *chatas* is sufficiently *chatas*-like that *me'ilah* no longer applies. Whether the *kohanim* would then be permitted to eat the meat is a separate question, but the fundamental claim was simply that *me'ilah* is removed — not that the bird's identity changes. The *kinnim* discussion will ultimately arrive at a closing statement whose authorial voice unmistakably sounds like Rabbi Yehoshua, and it is that closing statement which anchors the *gemara*'s identification. The purpose of citing this *mishnah* in full is not to analyze it in the manner of conventional *gemara* discussion, but to reach that punchline. What follows is an explanation of the *mishnah* itself, with the help of Rashi, so that the punchline can be appreciated in context.

## Kinnim Mishnah

### Woman's Vow

The *mishnah* opens: a woman declares, "I hereby take upon myself to bring two birds if I give birth to a male." She gives birth to a male and is now obligated to bring two pairs of birds — one pair for her *neder* (her personal vow, both birds of which are *olahs*) and one pair for her *chova* (her post-childbirth obligation, consisting of a *chatas* and an *olah*). She hands both pairs to the *kohen*.

### Same Type Birds

In the first case, all four birds are of the same species — either all *torim* (turtledoves) or all *bnei yonah* (young pigeons). What the *kohen* is required to do is perform three birds above (as *olahs*) and one below (as a *chatas*). An important rule, noted by Rashi, is that the first pair is always defined as the *chova* — the obligatory *chatas* and *olah* pair.

### Kohen's Error

The *kohen*, however, does not ask. He assumes that since a woman brought him two sets of birds, they must be for two separate childbirth obligations — one pair for each — and so he treats each pair symmetrically, offering one bird from each pair above and one from each pair below. He performs two above and two below rather than three and one. He does this without consulting the woman.

### Same Type Obligation

The first pair was handled correctly: one *chatas* and one *olah*, which is exactly what the *chova* required. But the second pair — which should have consisted of two *olahs* for the *neder* — was split, with only one offered above. What must now be done? She must bring one additional bird (*preidah achat*) and offer it above as an *olah*. This is straightforward when all the birds are of the same species: since we know the type, one more *tor* (or *ben yonah*, as the case may be) suffices to complete the missing *olah*.

### Two Types Birds

Now consider the case where the two pairs consisted of birds of two different species — one pair of *torim* and one pair of *bnei yonah* — and the *kohen* again split them two above and two below without consulting the woman. Rashi notes an additional complication: the *kohen* does not remember which pair he processed first. Since the first pair is always defined as the *chova*, and since the second pair that functions as the *neder* must be matched in species to the first pair of the *neder*, the uncertainty about ordering creates uncertainty about which species must be added.

### Two Types Obligation

If the *tor* was the first pair, then the *ben yonah* is the second (the *neder*), and one must add one *ben yonah* to complete the missing *olah* of the *neder*. If the *ben yonah* was first, then the *tor* is the *neder*, and one must add one *tor*. Since neither possibility can be ruled out, she must bring one *tor* and one *ben yonah* above — two birds total. That is the ruling for this case: *u-meshnei minim tavi shtayim* — when two species are involved, she brings two additional birds.

### Pirshah Nidrah

The third case is *pirshah nidrah* — the woman specified her vow. At the time she took the *neder*, she declared not only that she would bring two birds as an *olah*, but also that they would be a particular species — say, *bnei yonah*. However, by the time she comes to the *Beit HaMikdash*, she has forgotten which species she specified. Rashi's contribution here is essential; without it, the *mishnah* is nearly impossible to follow.

### Forgot Specification

Even before any *kohen* error enters the picture, her forgetting already creates an obligation. Her *chova* can be satisfied with any species — she was never required to specify that. But for the *neder*, she knows she promised a specific species and cannot recall which one. She must therefore bring enough birds to cover both possibilities. Even without the *kohen* mixing anything up, she arrives at the *Beit HaMikdash* with three pairs: one for the *chova* (whatever species she chooses) and two more for the *neder* (one of each species, since she does not know which she vowed). Had the *kohen* then acted correctly — performing the first pair as *chatas* and *olah*, and the next two pairs entirely above — everything would have been discharged. But the *kohen*, again acting without asking, assumed all the birds fell into the standard two-pair structure and split everything two above and two below.

### Pirshah Obligation

Now we must calculate what remains to be done. She was supposed to have three *olahs* for the *neder* (one of each species, and then a second of whichever species she specified — but she doesn't know which). She actually brought two pairs, and the *kohen* offered only one from each pair above. Compare what was needed with what was done: she is missing one additional *tor* and two additional *bnei yonah* (or vice versa, depending on which she specified) — a total of three additional *preidim* above. That is the ruling for *pirshah nidrah* when all birds are the same species: *tzricha she-tavi od gimmel peradim mimin echad*. When two species are involved, the uncertainty about ordering (which was first, which was second) compounds the uncertainty about her specification, and the total comes to four additional birds.

### Kava'ah Nidrah

The fourth and most complex case is *kava'ah nidrah* — the woman not only specified her vow in terms of species, but declared that she would bring her *neder* together with her *chova* as a single unified offering. The word *kava'ah* signals that she fixed them together as one integrated unit. Once again, Rashi is indispensable for understanding what this means in practice.

### Kava'ah Constraint

The key constraint introduced by *kava'ah* is that all the birds — the *neder* and the *chova* together — must be brought simultaneously as one combined *korban gadol*. They cannot be completed piecemeal. This creates a novel complication: if she brings some of the birds and not all of them at once, the *neder* portion is not satisfied, even if something was technically offered. However, the *chova* itself is not blocked — as soon as the *chatas* and *olah* of the *chova* are performed, she has fulfilled her post-childbirth obligation regardless. What the *kava'ah* means is that the *neder* cannot be discharged independently; it must come as part of one complete unit alongside the corresponding *olah* that corresponds to the *chova*.

This creates an unusual *halacha*: if she brought some birds and left others undone, the *neder* is entirely unresolved. To now satisfy the *neder*, she must bring all of the *neder*'s birds together — including an *olah* that corresponds in species to the *olah* of the *chova*, even though she has already technically fulfilled her *chova* obligation. That corresponding *olah* is not a second fulfillment of the *chova*; it is a requirement of the *neder* she bound to the *chova*. The effect is that one of the new *olahs* she brings will formally correspond to the *olah* of the obligatory pair, even though the obligation was already discharged when the original pair was offered.

### Kava'ah Obligation

She must now bring five additional *preidim* above. Why five? Because she was obligated to bring all of the *neder* birds simultaneously with the *chova*, and since she did not do so, none of the *neder* birds she brought previously receive credit. She must redo all of the *neder* — which consists of two possible *olahs* (for the two possible species she might have specified) plus additional coverage for not knowing which species she vowed — and she must include the *olah* corresponding to the *chova* pair. Since none of the previously offered *neder* birds count, she needs to bring all five from scratch.

When two species are involved, the uncertainty about which pair was first — and therefore which species must correspond to the *chova*'s *olah* — adds one more bird to the requirement, bringing the total to six.

### Woman Unclear

The final scenario in this *mishnah* is a state of complete compounded confusion. The woman handed the birds to the *kohen* without any clear indication of what she was giving him: she herself does not know whether she gave him all *torim*, all *bnei yonah*, or a mixture — *ve-eina yoda'as mah nasana*.

### Kohen Confused

The *kohen*, for his part, also has no idea what he did. He does not know whether he performed some above and some below, all above, or all below — *ve-eino yode'a mah asa*. Both parties are in a state of complete uncertainty.

### Both Unclear

What does she now owe? One might think she is obligated to cover every conceivable scenario from scratch, but there are limits. She must bring four *preidim* for the *neder* and two for the *chova*. The rationale for requiring two for the *chova* is that if the *kohen* brought everything above, then no *chatas* was performed for the *chova*, and we do not know what species the *olah* of the *chova* would need to correspond to, given that we do not know what the *chatas* was. She must therefore ensure coverage for both possibilities.

### One Chatas

The first opinion in the *mishnah* holds that she brings only one *chatas* in addition to the other birds. The reasoning is that the *chatas* determines the *olah* — not the reverse. If everything was brought above as *olahs*, she now brings a *chatas* of whichever species she chooses. That *chatas* then retroactively "selects" from among the *olahs* already brought which one corresponds to it as the matching pair. There is no need to bring two *chatatot* because the *chatas* is not determined by anything that was previously brought; she can choose freely, and whatever she chooses will align with one of the *olahs* above.

### Chatas vs Ola

The underlying question this debate turns on is directional: does the *chatas* determine which *olah* it pairs with, or does the first act performed determine what the second must be? If the *chatas* governs, it can be freely chosen and it will define the set. If the first act performed is always the *chova* and that first act defines the *matzav*, then the first *olah* brought above defines what species the *chatas* must be — and since we do not know what that first *olah* was, we do not know what species the *chatas* must be.

### Ben Azzai

Ben Azzai holds the latter view: *shtei chatatot* — two *chatatot* are required. For Ben Azzai, it is the first thing brought that determines the second. The first item offered is always defined as part of the *chova*, and therefore the first *olah* brought above defines the species of the *chatas*. Since we do not know what species was brought first, we cannot know what species the *chatas* must be, and we must bring one of each to be certain. For the *chachamim*, the *chatas* is open-ended and freely determined; for Ben Azzai, it is fixed by the first *olah* brought — and therefore, under conditions of total uncertainty, two *chatatot* are necessary.

## RY Connection

### Closing Statement

Having worked through the *mishnah*, the *gemara* returns to its original purpose. The closing statement of the *mishnah* — *amar Rabbi Yehoshua zehu she'amru* — is identified as the authorial voice of Rabbi Yehoshua. The phrase *zehu she'amru* carries the quality of a narrator stepping back and reflecting on what has just been discussed: not quoting a prior ruling so much as summarizing an insight. The *gemara* hears in that formulation the unmistakable voice of the *mishnah*'s author, and identifies that author as Rabbi Yehoshua.

---

## Ram Parable

### Ram Sound

What does Rabbi Yehoshua say in that closing statement? He offers a parable: when a ram is alive, it produces one sound — a single *beh*. When the ram dies and its parts are put to use, it produces seven sounds. The horns become musical instruments, the intestines become strings for a lyre, the hide becomes a drum. One living creature with one voice becomes seven different voices in its death.

### Expansion Theme

The application is clear from the *mishnah* just studied. Had the woman known exactly what she had vowed, remembered it clearly, and the *kohen* had acted correctly, she would have needed to bring four birds. She forgot her specification — the number grew to six. The *kohen* then compounded the confusion — the number grew to eight. Each additional error, each additional layer of forgetting or mishandling, does not simply add a fixed increment; it can trigger an expansion that multiplies the original obligation in ways that seem disproportionate to the initial mistake. This is the moral that Rabbi Yehoshua draws: things start one way and, through mishandling, expand beyond what anyone initially anticipated. As one student aptly noted, this is the spirit of *me'uvat lo yuchal litkon* — what has been made crooked cannot be straightened. You cannot simply start over; the errors already made shape what remains to be done, and sometimes the obligation to remedy the situation exceeds what would have been required had one acted correctly from the start.

## RY Connection (II)

### Clarification

Having now read the *mishnah* in full and identified its closing voice as Rabbi Yehoshua, the *gemara* delivers its conclusion. *Eima de-amar Rabbi Yehoshua la-apokei midei me'ilah* — when Rabbi Yehoshua said that an *olah* done below in the manner of a *chatas* is treated like a *chatas*, he meant only that it removes *me'ilah*. *Le-maskei lei le-chovah mi amar* — he never said it would actually fulfill the woman's *chova* obligation. The *kinnim* evidence confirms this: an *olah* performed in the manner of a *chatas* does not genuinely become a *chatas*, and Rabbi Yehoshua, as the author of *Kinnim*, could never have intended otherwise.

## Invalid Melikah

### Generic Opening

The *gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*. There is a question among the commentators whether its opening line belongs to the *mishnah* proper, though Rashi includes it. The line reads: *kol ha-psulim she-malku, melikatam psulah ve-einah metamei be-veit ha-beli'ah* — all the invalid ones who performed *melikah*, their *melikah* is invalid, and the bird does not convey *tum'ah* if swallowed.

### Baseline Halacha

To appreciate this, one must first understand the baseline *halacha*. Ordinarily, *melikah* performed on a non-consecrated bird — wringing the neck in the manner used for bird *korbanot* — renders the bird a *neveilah*. The marker of *neveilah* status that the *mishnah* employs is whether swallowing the bird conveys *tum'ah* to the one who swallows it. This formulation is used rather than simply saying the bird is forbidden to eat, because a bird *olah* is also forbidden to eat — for entirely different reasons — and we need a marker that specifically indicates *neveilah* status rather than mere *issur*. When *melikah* is performed properly in the context of a *korban*, however, it does not render the bird a *neveilah*, even though the same act on an ordinary bird would.

### Left Hand & Night

The *mishnah* then gives concrete examples of invalid *melikah* that nonetheless do not produce *neveilah* status. A *kohen* who performed *melikah* with his left hand — *malak b'smol* — has performed it invalidly, since *melikah* requires the right hand. A *kohen* who performed it at night — *o ba-lailah* — has also acted invalidly, since *korbanot* are offered during the day. Yet in both cases, the *melikah*, though *psulah*, does not render the bird a *neveilah*.

### Shechita Cases

Two additional cases involve *shechita* rather than *melikah*. If one performed *shechita* on an unconsecrated bird inside the *Beit HaMikdash* — *shachat chullin bifnim* — or on a consecrated bird outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — *ve-kodshim bachutz* — these are also not cases of *neveilah*. The *chiddush* here is that even though *shechita* is not the proper mode of slaughter for bird *korbanot* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, and even though *shechita* of consecrated birds outside is a grave violation, since *shechita* was performed the bird retains the status of a properly slaughtered animal and is not a *neveilah*.

### Neveila Cases

By contrast, the following acts do render the bird a *neveilah*: performing *melikah* with a knife (*malak be-sakin*), performing *melikah* on unconsecrated birds inside the *Beit HaMikdash* (*melikat chullin bifnim*), and performing *melikah* on consecrated birds outside the *Beit HaMikdash* (*kodshim bachutz*). These cases are marked as producing *neveilah* status.

### Key Principle

The underlying principle can be formulated as follows: if the *melikah* was performed with the right act (*melikah* rather than *sakin*), on the right object (consecrated birds, *kodshim*), and in the right place (inside the *Beit HaMikdash*), then even if the performer was a *psul* — a non-*kohen*, someone who used the wrong hand, or acted at night — the act remains within the bounds of *melikah* and does not produce *neveilah*. The bird's invalidity is a *psul ba-kodesh*, an invalidity that occurred within the sacred context. But if any one of those three elements is wrong — wrong act, wrong object, or wrong place — the *melikah* is totally out of bounds and the bird is a *neveilah*. One might attempt a more abstract formulation — whether the *psul* occurred within the *kodesh* context or outside it — but as will become apparent, that formulation does not map cleanly onto every case.

## Zar Debate

### Rav Position

The *gemara* now introduces a dispute between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan. Rav states: *smol ve-lailah eino metamei be-veit ha-beli'ah* — the left hand and nighttime do not render the bird a *neveilah*, confirming our *mishnah*. But *zar ve-sakin metamei be-veit ha-beli'ah* — a non-*kohen* and a knife both render the bird a *neveilah*. Rav thus adds to the *mishnah*'s explicit cases the position that a *zar* who performed *melikah* falls into the same category as someone who used a knife.

### Rav Reasoning

The reasoning emerges from the nature of *melikah* itself. The instrument of *melikah* is the *kohen*'s thumbnail — not a knife, and not anyone else's thumbnail, but specifically the *kohen*'s own. A non-*kohen*'s thumbnail is not merely a disqualified performer; it is, in effect, the wrong object. Just as using a knife is not *melikah* at all — it is merely tearing off the head with an alien implement — so too a non-*kohen*'s thumbnail is structurally equivalent to a knife. The act performed by a *zar* is not *melikah* in any meaningful sense; it is the wrong instrument entirely, regardless of the fact that it is anatomically a thumbnail.

### Bama Challenge

The *gemara* raises a challenge: we know that a *zar* can perform *shechita* validly, at least in some contexts. If a *zar*'s involvement is sometimes valid, perhaps that argues that a *zar* is not so categorically excluded as to render the *melikah* a *neveilah*. In general, the *gemara*'s framework for distinguishing valid-but-*psul* *melikah* from invalid-*neveilah* *melikah* has relied on whether the mode in question is ever valid. Left hand: valid on *Yom Kippur* for certain services. Nighttime: valid for the burning of fats and limbs (*evarim u-fedarim*). By that logic, a *zar* — valid for *shechita* — should also not be totally out of bounds.

### Para Aduma

The *gemara* responds: *shechita lav avodah hi* — *shechita* is not an *avodah*. The reasoning runs as follows: a *zar* is always valid for *shechita* of *korbanot*. If *shechita* were an *avodah*, then the *zar*'s validity would demonstrate that a *zar* can sometimes perform an *avodah* — which would be the relevant precedent. But since *shechita* is never an *avodah* for *korbanot*, the *zar*'s capacity there does not establish that a *zar* can ever validly perform an *avodah*. One might object: the *shechita* of *para aduma* requires a *kohen* — does that not show that *shechita* belongs in the category of *avodah*? The *gemara* responds that *para aduma* is sui generis — it is not a *korban* at all, its sanctity is *kedushat bedek ha-bayit* rather than *kedushat korban*, and it is not brought inside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The requirement of a *kohen* for *para aduma* proves nothing about *shechita* in general, since there are non-*avodah* functions that require *kohanim* — such as examining *tzara'at* — and *para aduma* may simply be another such case.

### Im Alu Lo Yered

The *gemara* further explores whether one can learn from the laws of *bamah* — the private altar used before the *Beit HaMikdash* was established — where *kohanim* are not required and a *zar* may act. If the *bamah* allows a *zar* to perform sacrificial rites, might that not indicate that a *zar* is not so absolutely excluded? The *gemara* rejects this: what applies on a *bamah* is categorically irrelevant to the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *gemara* further notes that even the rule that *yotzei* — meat taken outside the bounds of the *Beit HaMikdash* — remains on the altar if placed there (*im alu lo yered*) does not derive from the *bamah*; it derives from the verse *zot torat ha-olah*, which establishes a general principle that once something is on the altar, the burden of proof lies with those who would remove it, not those who would leave it. There is no need to learn from *bamah*.

### Conclusion

The conclusion of Rav's position: since *shechita* is not an *avodah* for *korbanot*, and since *bamah* is irrelevant, there is no scenario in which a *zar* is validly performing an *avodah*. *Melikah* is an *avodah*, and an *avodah* requires a *kohen*. A *zar* who performs *melikah* is therefore completely out of bounds — as out of bounds as someone using a knife — and the bird is a *neveilah*.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *zar ein metamei beit ha-beli'ah, sakin metamei beit ha-beli'ah* — the non-*kohen* does not render the bird a *neveilah*, but the knife does. For Rabbi Yochanan, the *zar*'s disqualification is merely a personal *psul* — he is the wrong performer — but the act itself, the object used, and the place are all correct. That is sufficient for the *melikah* to fall within the valid-but-*psul* category, producing an invalid *korban* but not a *neveilah*.

### Mishnah Analysis

The *gemara* attempts to adjudicate between these views based on the generic opening line of the *mishnah* — *kol ha-psulim she-malku* ("all the invalid ones who performed *melikah*"). For Rabbi Yochanan, this generic umbrella is what includes a *zar*, since the *mishnah* goes on to specify only left hand and night explicitly. The umbrella must be adding something beyond the explicit cases — and the *zar* is that addition. For Rav, however, the umbrella cannot be including a *zar*, since a *zar*'s *melikah* would not be in the valid-but-*psul* category at all. Rav reads the *mishnah* as making a generic statement followed by a specification of precisely what it means — *kol ha-psulim* is simply the introduction, and *smol ve-lailah* are the full content. The *gemara* similarly examines the closing *klal* of the *mishnah* — *kol she-haya psulo ba-kodesh* — and concludes that for both Rav and Rabbi Yochanan, these general formulations are adding relatively narrow information that was not already stated explicitly: the opening *klal* adds the case of *shechita* of consecrated birds inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, and the closing *klal* adds the case of *melikah* of unconsecrated birds outside. The debate between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan about the *zar* is not resolved by these general formulations.

### Person vs Object

The conceptual heart of the dispute is this: is the problem with a *zar* a *person* problem or an *object* problem? For Rabbi Yochanan, it is purely a person problem — the wrong individual performed a valid act with the correct instrument in the correct place. That is a *psul ba-kodesh* and does not produce *neveilah*. For Rav, the *kohen*'s thumbnail is itself the legally defined instrument of *melikah* — it is not merely the right performer but the right object. A non-*kohen*'s thumbnail is therefore structurally equivalent to a knife: it is the wrong object, and the act is not *melikah* at all. That is why, for Rav, *melikat zar* falls into the same category as *melikat sakin* and renders the bird a *neveilah*.
