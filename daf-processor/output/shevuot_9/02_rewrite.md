# Shevuot 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## YK Korbanos

### YK Inner Overview

The discussion opens by reviewing what has already been established regarding the *korbanot* of *Yom Kippur*. The inner service performed in the *Kodesh HaKodashim* atones for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* — the sin of one who entered the *mikdash* or ate *kodashim* while *tamei* — based on the verse *v'chiper al ha-kodesh mi-tumot b'nei Yisrael*. The specific configuration of *yedi'ah* (knowledge) associated with this atonement is that there was awareness at the beginning — the person knew they were *tamei* at some point — but no awareness at the end, meaning the sin is not presently known. This is derived from the word *l'khol chatatam*: the sin is not currently obligating a personal *chatat*, yet it could theoretically become one if the person were to regain awareness, since *lchol chatatam* implies the possibility of a future individual *chatat*.

### YK Musaf Linkage

The *Yom Kippur musaf* — the outer goat — is treated as the counterpart. The Torah links the two through the phrase *milvad chatat ha-kippurim*, "besides the *chatat* of *Yom Kippur*," yoking the outer to the inner service. From this linkage we learn that both atone for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*, while each addresses a distinct *yedi'ah* configuration. The inner service covers cases where knowledge existed at the beginning but not the end; the outer *musaf* covers the reverse — where there was no knowledge at the beginning but knowledge at the end.

### R. Yishmael Position

A complication arises from the position of Rabbi Yishmael, whose view has not yet been fully engaged. Rabbi Yishmael holds that even if there was no *yedi'ah ba-tchilah* — no awareness at the time of the sin — the person is still obligated to bring a personal *korban* once they become aware afterward. His reasoning is grounded in the verse *v'ne'elam mimenu v'hu yada v'ashem*: a later awareness suffices, regardless of whether one knew at the moment of transgression. This means that for Rabbi Yishmael, the case of no knowledge at the beginning but knowledge at the end is already addressed by the individual's own *korban*. Consequently, the *musaf* of *Yom Kippur* must be assigned to something else — namely, the case of no *yedi'ah* at either the beginning or the end, which is exactly what the *Gemara* concludes: *al she-ein bo yedi'ah lo ba-tchilah v'lo ba-sof*.

### Linking Question

This raises an immediate question: if Rabbi Yishmael holds that all the *musafim* — of *Yom Kippur*, of *regalim*, and of *Rosh Chodesh* — atone for the same category (no knowledge at beginning or end), then what is the purpose of the Torah's linkage? Why does the verse connect the outer *Yom Kippur musaf* to the inner service via *milvad chatat ha-kippurim*? The two have entirely different *yedi'ah* profiles: the inner has knowledge at the beginning, the outer — for Rabbi Yishmael — has none at all. What is being achieved by drawing them together?

### Atonement Function

The *Gemara* resolves the question: *l'mai hilkheta itkash chitzon l'pnimay? mah pnimay eino m'chapper b'sha'ar aveirot, af chitzon eino m'chapper b'sha'ar aveirot*. The linkage is not about *yedi'ah* at all. Its purpose is to establish that just as the inner service atones exclusively for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* and nothing beyond that, so too the outer *musaf* atones exclusively for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*. The Torah needed to make this explicit because the outer *musaf*, like the *musafim* of *regalim* and *Rosh Chodesh*, is described only generically — *se'ir l'chapper*, a goat for atonement — with no specification of what sin is being addressed. The link to the inner service, which is explicitly tied to *tumat mikdash*, communicates that the outer *musaf* operates in the same domain. For Rabbi Yishmael, then, every *musaf* offering — whether of *Yom Kippur*, *regalim*, or *Rosh Chodesh* — atones for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* when there was no knowledge at either stage, and the linkage merely clarifies the category of sin being addressed.

## Musaf Scope

### Why So Many

At this point a broader and genuinely pressing question demands attention: why are so many *korbanot* — every *musaf* of the year — devoted to variations of a single sin, *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*? The communal *korbanot chatat* that are brought throughout the year, the outer goat of *Yom Kippur*, the goats of every *Yom Tov* and every *Rosh Chodesh* — all of them are apparently channeled toward this one category of transgression. What about every other sin that an individual or community might commit without awareness? A person who violated *Shabbat* unknowingly, or ate *chelev* with partial knowledge — who is bringing communal atonement for them throughout the year?

### Theological Sig.

One response is that *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* is not merely a technical infraction but represents something of profound theological weight. The *pasuk* says *hashochein itam b'toch tumotam* — God dwells with Israel in the midst of their *tumah*. That divine indwelling, the very presence of *kedushah* in a physical place, is precisely what is violated when someone enters the *mikdash* while *tamei* or consumes *kodashim* in a state of *tumah*. Even if the broader notion of *tumah* encompasses the defilement wrought by sin in general, the most concrete and tangible violation of God's presence among Israel is this failure of sensitivity to the sanctity of the *Makom HaMikdash* and its *kodashim*. It is, in other words, the sin that most directly challenges the premise of the *mikdash* itself — the idea that God can dwell among a people in this world. This is why, perhaps, the central communal *korbanot* of atonement in the *Beit HaMikdash* are focused on precisely this category: not because other sins are unimportant, but because this sin strikes at the foundation of everything the *mikdash* represents.

### Central Focus

Still, the question remains pointed: it is not merely that many *korbanot* address this sin, but that all the communal *korbanot chatat* — not one, not two, but the entire apparatus of *musaf* goats across the year — address nothing else. The analogy that presents itself is to a pharmacy stocked with twenty remedies for coughs and nothing for headaches or sore throats. The question is not why there are so many medicines, but why this one ailment receives exclusive attention from the entire stock. Other sins — indeed, every category of inadvertent communal transgression that falls outside *tumat mikdash* — seem to receive no comparable ongoing atonement mechanism from the *musaf* system, except for the single annual dispatch of the *se'ir la-azazel*, the goat sent to the desert on *Yom Kippur*, which does atone broadly for all sins once a year. The emphasis on *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* across the entire calendar of *musaf korbanot* remains, in some sense, striking, and perhaps its very singularity is the point: the health of the *mikdash* and the nation's relationship to its *kedushah* is the central axis around which all communal atonement turns.

### Priestly Role

This also connects to the central responsibility assigned to the *kohanim* — *veshamru et mishmarti* — to guard the *mikdash* from the *tumah* of *Bnei Yisrael*. The preservation of the *kedushah* of the place is their defining role, and the *musaf* system of atonement correspondingly focuses on the ways in which that sanctity is violated, even inadvertently. This includes not only entering the *mikdash* while *tamei*, but also eating *korbanot* in a state of *tumah* while in *Yerushalayim* — a violation of the *korban's* sanctity even outside the *Beit HaMikdash* proper.

## R. Yehudah

### Knowledge Source

The *Mishnah* presents the opinion of Rabbi Yehudah: the goats of the *regalim* and the goats of *Rosh Chodesh* atone for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* when the sin was committed without any *yedi'ah* — neither at the beginning nor at the end. The *Gemara* now asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Yehuda?* — what is the source that allows us to assign this specific function to the *musaf korbanot* of *Rosh Chodesh* and the *regalim*? After all, the Torah simply says *se'ir izim echad l'chapper* — a goat for atonement — without specifying which sins are addressed. One might suppose these goats atone for any number of transgressions; what limits them to *tumat mikdash*?

### L'Chatat L'Hashem

The *Gemara* points to the unusual formulation by *Rosh Chodesh*: *ve-se'ir izim echad le-chatat la-Hashem* — a goat for a *chatat* to God. The phrase *la-Hashem* is distinctive. From it the *Gemara* derives: *cheit she-ein makir bo ela Hashem* — it is a sin that only God knows about. The sinner has no awareness, neither at the time of transgression nor afterward; only God is cognizant of what occurred. This establishes that we are dealing with the category of no *yedi'ah* at the beginning and no *yedi'ah* at the end. However, this does not yet tell us that the sin in question is *tumat mikdash*. The *pasuk* merely tells us about the state of knowledge — that the sin is unknown to anyone but God. A separate step is required to establish that the category of sin is *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* specifically.

### Moon Midrash

Before proceeding to that question, the *Gemara* notes that the word *la-Hashem* is also the source for one of the most theologically arresting *midrashim* in the entire *Gemara*. The *Gemara* cites Reish Lakish: *mah nishtanah se'ir shel Rosh Chodesh she-ne'emar bo la-Hashem?* — why is the goat of *Rosh Chodesh* singled out with the phrase "to God"? Rather than reading *le-chatat la-Hashem* as a sin offering offered *to* God, Reish Lakish reads it as a sin offering brought *on account of* God, as though God Himself requires atonement. *Amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu: se'ir zeh yehei kaparah al she-mi'ateti et ha-yare'ach* — God says, let this goat be an atonement for Me, because I diminished the moon. The Torah initially describes *shenei ha-me'orot ha-gedolim*, two great luminaries, and then immediately speaks of a great luminary and a small one. The *Gemara* elsewhere relates that the moon approached God with the complaint that two kings cannot share a single crown. God responded by diminishing the moon — and then, in a sense, owes atonement for what was done.

### Rashi Interp.

This is, as the *Gemara* itself acknowledges through the marginal notation that this is one of the *sodot* of *kabbalah* not to be taken literally, a profoundly radical statement. Rashi addresses the theological difficulty directly. His reading of *hevi'u kaparah* is not that God acknowledges wrongdoing in any conventional sense, but rather that this is an act of *lehafis da'atah shel levanah* — appeasing the moon, making amends to restore its sense of dignity. God is not confessing to a sin but is doing something to address the hurt caused, even if the original decision was not wrong.

### Necessary Harm

This points to a broader principle worth dwelling on. A *chatat* can sometimes be brought even in the absence of culpable wrongdoing — as in the case of a person who was raised entirely unaware of their Jewish identity, committed transgressions unknowingly, and upon learning of their heritage must bring a *chatat* despite bearing no moral blame. Something went wrong in the world; something must be done to address it. The same logic applies here. Even if a decision was the right one — even if there was no alternative, no option that could have avoided the outcome — if harm resulted, if someone or something was diminished in the process, there remains a responsibility to address that harm. The necessity of an action does not dissolve the reality of the wound it created.

This is a powerful moral and theological principle in its own right. We sometimes ask difficult questions about suffering in the world — why disease, why death, why does this have to be someone's fate? The theoretical answer that this was the best of all possible worlds, or that divine necessity demanded it, does not extinguish the reality that harm occurred and that something must be done in response to it. The *Gemara* here suggests that even God, so to speak, acknowledges this — that necessity is not the same as innocence when it comes to the hurt that follows.

### Jewish Fate

The *Maharsha*, in his commentary on the parallel passage in Chullin, extends this reading to the Jewish people themselves. The nations of the world count by the solar calendar; Israel counts by the lunar calendar. The moon waxes and wanes, and so too the fortunes of Israel across history have waxed and waned against the seemingly steady dominion of the great powers. The sun represents the nations; the moon, Israel. God says, in effect: it is necessary that Israel suffers in this world, that its time of full dominion belongs to the world to come — but the suffering is real, and God acknowledges a responsibility to address it. The goat of *Rosh Chodesh*, offered each month as the moon begins its renewed cycle, carries this weight: an acknowledgment that the diminishment was real, that the hurt demands a response, whatever the ultimate justification may have been.

## Musaf Derivation

### Fixed Times

Having established from *la-Hashem* that the *Rosh Chodesh* goat addresses sins unknown to anyone but God, the *Gemara* returns to the question of how we know it is specifically *tumat mikdash* that is being addressed. For this the *Gemara* invokes the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *hoil ve-ze ba bi-zman kavua ve-ze ba bi-zman kavua* — just as the *Yom Kippur musaf* comes at a fixed time and the *Rosh Chodesh musaf* comes at a fixed time, and just as the *Yom Kippur musaf* atones for nothing other than *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*, so too the *Rosh Chodesh* goat atones for nothing other than *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*. The reasoning is that the category of goats brought as communal *chatat* at fixed times in the *Beit HaMikdash* is consistently associated with *tumat mikdash*, and a parallel case is assimilated into that pattern.

### Regalim Question

This derivation establishes the function of the *Rosh Chodesh* goats. But a further step is needed: *se'irei regalim minalan?* — how do we know the same is true of the *regalim* goats? Unlike *Rosh Chodesh*, which occurs with great regularity every month, the *regalim* occur less frequently. And unlike *Yom Kippur*, which is a day saturated with atonement, the *regalim* carry no such special status. So one cannot simply invoke the same *gezeirah shavah* that linked *Rosh Chodesh* to *Yom Kippur* and assume it extends further to the *regalim*.

### RCh Scope Limit

The *Gemara* explains why the analogy works for *Rosh Chodesh* but cannot straightforwardly be extended to the *regalim*. By *Rosh Chodesh*, the Torah says *le-chatat la-Hashem* — a formulation that, taken at face value, would suggest the *Rosh Chodesh* goat atones for every sin in the world that only God knows about, not merely *tumat mikdash*. The *Yom Kippur* comparison does not expand *Rosh Chodesh*'s scope; it restricts it. We already know that *Yom Kippur*, which carries enormous atoning power, is nonetheless limited to *tumat mikdash* — so the *Rosh Chodesh* goat, which is less powerful, cannot be broader. This is *gilluy milta be-alma hu* — *Yom Kippur* is not generating a new rule for *Rosh Chodesh* but revealing the actual scope of what *Rosh Chodesh* already does.

By contrast, the *regalim* goats are described simply as *le-chatat*, with no qualifying phrase like *la-Hashem*. Their scope is entirely undefined. If we attempt to learn their function from *Yom Kippur* in order to show they too address *tumat mikdash*, this would be an expansion, not a restriction — we would be arguing that a less powerful *musaf* can nonetheless handle a serious sin. That requires independent justification. And one cannot learn the *regalim* directly from *Rosh Chodesh* either, since *Rosh Chodesh* occurs with far greater frequency — *sheken tadir* — which itself might be the source of its atoning potency, making it an inappropriate model for the *regalim*.

### Vav Link

The resolution comes from the formulation *ve-sa'ir echad le-chatat* — the *vav* that begins the phrase describing the *regalim* goat. Rabbi Chama bar Chanina observes that the *vav* — *se'ir ve-se'ir* — creates a linkage between the goat of the *regalim* and the goat of *Rosh Chodesh*. Since we have established that the *Rosh Chodesh* goat addresses *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*, the *vav* yoking the *regalim* goat to it means the *regalim* goat operates in the same domain. This is not the power of an independent derivation but of a connective particle that assimilates the *regalim* into the established framework of *Rosh Chodesh*.

### Final Chart

With this, the complete picture emerges. The *Yom Kippur* inner service — from *v'chiper al ha-kodesh mi-tumot bnei Yisrael* — addresses *tumat mikdash* with *yedi'ah* at the beginning and no *yedi'ah* at the end. The *Yom Kippur musaf* — linked to the inner service through *milvad chatat ha-kippurim* — addresses *tumat mikdash* with no *yedi'ah* at the beginning and *yedi'ah* at the end. The goats of *Rosh Chodesh* and the *regalim* — from *le-chatat la-Hashem* and the *vav* linkage — address *tumat mikdash* with no *yedi'ah* at the beginning or the end. Each slot on the chart is filled; each level of *yedi'ah* has its corresponding *korban*.

## Unknown Sin

### Ibayu Lehu

A question now arises regarding the precise scope of the *Rosh Chodesh* and *regalim* category. The formulation is *she-ein bo yedi'ah lo ba-tchilah v'lo ba-sof* — no knowledge at the beginning and no knowledge at the end. But does this require that the sin will *never* come to light, or does it also cover sins that, while presently unknown, could eventually be discovered? What if someone witnessed the transgression and could in principle inform the sinner? Right now the sinner has no awareness; only God knows. But it is conceivable that, at some future point, the sinner will find out — in which case there would ultimately be *yedi'ah ba-sof*, knowledge at the end. Does that potential future awareness disqualify the case from the *Rosh Chodesh* and *regalim* atonement and place it instead in the *Yom Kippur musaf* category, which handles the case of no knowledge at the beginning but knowledge at the end?

### Baraita

A *baraita* resolves the question. Rabbi Yehudah's ruling explicitly includes *bechet she-sofo lehivada* — a sin that will eventually become known — in the category covered by the goats of *Rosh Chodesh* and the *regalim*. If right now there is no *yedi'ah*, the atonement of these *musaf korbanot* applies, regardless of whether knowledge might emerge later.

### No Retroactive

The reasoning is clear upon reflection. The distinguishing feature of the *Yom Kippur musaf* — the outer goat — is that it covers cases where there was *yedi'ah ba-sof*, knowledge at the end, combined with no *yedi'ah ba-tchilah*. This describes a person who finds out after the fact that they committed the sin. But such a person will never retroactively have possessed knowledge *before* sinning. The *yedi'ah ba-tchilah* that would place someone in a different category — the inner *Yom Kippur* service — is something that can never be acquired retroactively. What is possible is acquiring *yedi'ah ba-sof*: learning what one did after the fact. If and when that happens, the person moves into the *Yom Kippur musaf* category. But as long as the sin remains presently unknown, the sinner is where they are — in the domain of *Rosh Chodesh* and *regalim* — and those *korbanot* atone for them. The possibility of future discovery does not preemptively reclassify the sin.

## R. Shimon

### Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon disputes Rabbi Yehudah's assignment. His position is that the goats of the *regalim* function as Rabbi Yehudah describes — they atone for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* with no *yedi'ah* at either stage. But *Rosh Chodesh*, for Rabbi Shimon, operates in a different domain entirely. While it remains connected to the world of the *mikdash* and *kodashim*, it does not atone for the sin of entering the *mikdash* in a state of *tumah* or eating *kodashim* while *tamei*. Instead, Rabbi Shimon assigns it a distinct function.

### Eating Tamei

For Rabbi Shimon, the *Rosh Chodesh* goat atones for eating a *korban tamei* — not a case where the *person* was *tamei* and ate a *korban*, but a case where the *korban itself* became *tamei* and the person consumed it. This is a qualitatively different transgression. When a *korban* becomes *tamei* and one eats it, the sin is not a *chiyuv chatat* for the individual — it is not punishable by *karet* — because the violation is not of the *korban's* status as such but of the *korban* qua *kodesh* that has been compromised. Since there is no *chiyuv karet* attached to eating a *korban tamei* (as distinct from eating *kodashim* while one is *tamei*), there is no personal *chatat*. No individual would ever bring a *korban* for this. And yet something has gone wrong — the sacred has been treated carelessly — and the *Rosh Chodesh* goat addresses that wrong.

### Avon HaEidah

The source for Rabbi Shimon's position is the incident recorded in Tanach where Moshe confronts Aharon and his sons for burning the *chatat* on the day of the inauguration of the *Mishkan* — the *Rosh Chodesh Nisan* on which the *Mishkan* began its service. Moshe declares: *ve-osah nasan lachem laseis et avon ha-eidah* — this was given to you to carry the sin of the congregation. The *chatat* in question, according to the *Gemara*, was the *Rosh Chodesh* goat. And the sin it carries is described as *avon ha-eidah*, the sin of the congregation — not *avon ha-kodashim*, the sin related to the status of the *korban*. A *gezeirah shavah* on the word *avon* then links this to the function of the *tzitz*, the priestly forehead plate, which is described in the Torah as bearing *avon ha-kodashim*. The connection between these two *avonot* will now be drawn carefully.

## Two Avonot

### Tzitz Avon

The *Gemara* establishes a *gezeirah shavah* connecting *avon ha-kodashim* (by the *tzitz*) with *avon ha-eidah* (by the *Rosh Chodesh* goat). Both passages use the word *avon*, and this shared language links them interpretively. The question is what each is doing and how they relate to each other.

### Kodashim Valid

The *tzitz* — the golden forehead plate inscribed with *kodesh la-Hashem* that the *kohen gadol* wore — bears, as the Torah says, *avon ha-kodashim*. What this means in practice is that if a *korban*'s blood, fats, or meat became *tamei*, the presence of the *tzitz* renders the *korban* valid nonetheless — *kasher l'haktir* and *kasher l'achilah* — in a way it would not otherwise be. This is a matter of the *korban's* status. The *avon*, the "sin," in question is the *tumah* that has affected the *kodashim* themselves, and the *tzitz* carries or neutralizes that *avon* to preserve the offering's validity.

### Eida Sin

The *avon ha-eidah* by *Rosh Chodesh* is a different matter entirely. Here too the scenario involves a *korban* that has become *tamei* — the same underlying factual premise. But the concern is not the validity of the *korban* as an offering; it is the fact that people *ate* it. That eating is the sin of the congregation — *avon ha-eidah* — and the *Rosh Chodesh* goat atones for that. So both the *tzitz* and the *Rosh Chodesh* goat operate in the same general domain — a *korban* that has become *tamei* — but they address entirely different consequences: the *tzitz* addresses the *korban's* validity as an offering; the *Rosh Chodesh* goat addresses the sin of having consumed it.

### Single Function

One might ask: since the *tzitz* and the *Rosh Chodesh* goat are linked through the shared word *avon* and both deal with *tamei korbanot*, why not allow each to do double duty? Let the *Rosh Chodesh* goat both atone for the eating of *tamei korbanot* and also validate a *korban* that became *tamei* — useful, for example, if the *tzitz* were broken and unavailable. The *Gemara* rejects this. The Torah says *ve-osah nasan lachem laseis et avon ha-eidah* — *it* was given to carry *avon ha-eidah*, with the singular pronoun *otah* implying exclusivity: this *korban* does this one job, and nothing else. *Avon echad hu nosei ve-eino nosei shenei avonot* — it carries one *avon*, not two. The same logic applies in the other direction: the *tzitz* too does its own exclusive job. *Ha-eidah velo kodashim* — the *Rosh Chodesh* goat is for the congregation, not the *kodashim*; *kodashim velo eidah* — the *tzitz* is for the *kodashim*, not the congregation. Each has its own reserved domain.

## Regalim Function

### Regalim Problem

With *Rosh Chodesh* now assigned to the atonement for eating *korbanot tamei*, the question of the *regalim* goats becomes acute. Earlier, in Rabbi Yehudah's framework, the *regalim* were linked to *Rosh Chodesh* via the *vav* — *se'ir ve-se'ir* — and thereby assigned to *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* with no *yedi'ah* at either stage. But that derivation assumed *Rosh Chodesh* was itself in the *tumat mikdash* category. For Rabbi Shimon, *Rosh Chodesh* has been reassigned. So what does the *vav* linkage now accomplish, and how do we establish the function of the *regalim* goats?

### Vav Linkage

The *vav* still operates, but what it now tells us is more limited. Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina's principle — *se'ir ve-se'ir*, the connection created by the *vav* — tells us that the *regalim* goat is *like* the *Rosh Chodesh* goat in the sense that it too addresses some matter relating to *kodesh*. Since the *Rosh Chodesh* goat is about *tamei korbanot* — a sin in the domain of *kodashim* — the *regalim* goat is similarly situated in the world of *kedushah* and *mikdash*. The *vav* establishes the domain without specifying the exact function.

### No Double Duty

Could the *regalim* goats then do the same thing as *Rosh Chodesh* — also atone for eating *korbanot tamei*? No. The principle established above from *otah* — that the *Rosh Chodesh* goat exclusively bears *avon ha-eidah* and nothing else bears that *avon* — cuts in both directions. Just as the *Rosh Chodesh* goat does not do the *tzitz's* job, the *regalim* goats do not do the *Rosh Chodesh* goat's job. This is the *Ayelet HaShachar* principle: each atonement service has an exclusive domain. Could the *regalim* goats then share the function of the *Yom Kippur musaf*? No — *achas ba-shanah*, "once a year," specifies that the *Yom Kippur* atonements are annual and exclusive. Nothing else replicates them.

### Reserved Rows

The picture that emerges is one of reserved rows, each occupied by a single *korban* doing its exclusive work. The *tzitz* handles the validity of *tamei korbanot* as offerings. The *Rosh Chodesh* goat handles the atonement for eating *tamei korbanot*. The inner *Yom Kippur* service handles *tumat mikdash* with *yedi'ah* at the beginning and none at the end. The outer *Yom Kippur musaf* handles *tumat mikdash* with no *yedi'ah* at the beginning and *yedi'ah* at the end. These rows are occupied and cannot be doubled.

### Conclusion

What remains? The *regalim* goats are linked to the *kodesh* domain by the *vav*, and every other role in that domain is filled. The only open slot is *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* — entering the *mikdash* or eating *kodashim* while *tamei* — with no *yedi'ah* at the beginning and no *yedi'ah* at the end. All other *yedi'ah* configurations for *tumat mikdash* are already addressed: the individual brings a personal *korban* if there was knowledge at both stages; the inner *Yom Kippur* service covers knowledge at the beginning but not the end; the outer *Yom Kippur musaf* covers knowledge at the end but not the beginning. The *regalim* therefore fill the final row: *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* when neither beginning nor end brings any awareness to the sinner. This is Rabbi Shimon's framework — *Rosh Chodesh* doing its own separate work on *tamei korbanot*, and the *regalim* occupying the final slot in the *tumat mikdash* system.
