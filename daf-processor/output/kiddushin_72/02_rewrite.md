# Kiddushin 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chalazone Nihavnad

### Rav Ika's Claim

The sugya opens with a statement transmitted through a chain of authorities: *amar Rav Ika bar Avin amar Rav Chananel amar Rav* — Chalazone Nihavnad, a city located in Iran that exists to this day, has the status of *gola* with respect to *yuchsin*. That is, it enjoys the same presumption of genealogical purity as Pumbedita, the paradigmatic *meyuchas* community of Babylonian Jewry.

Before proceeding, it is worth recalling the broader context in which this statement appears. The preceding dappim engaged at length with the question of *yuchsin* — genealogical fitness for marriage — examining which lands could be relied upon, which families could not, and how far the concern for *yichus* could be pressed in practice. Bavel was identified as superior to *chutz la'aretz*, while Eretz Yisrael occupied an intermediate position. Yet the *Gemara* simultaneously pushed back against the entire enterprise, arguing that once a family has become intermingled with *psulim*, there is no way to identify it, and one should neither ask nor tell. Even the role of Eliyahu HaNavi at the end of days was reinterpreted: rather than exposing families of problematic lineage that have already been absorbed into the community, he will address only those cases that remain conspicuously and publicly unresolved. In other words, the *Gemara* has repeatedly and powerfully challenged the practical relevance of *yichus* as a halakhic category — even while nominally maintaining the structure of the *Mishnah's* concern. Against that backdrop, the claim that a specific Iranian city qualifies as *meyuchas* like *gola* is itself notable, and the *Gemara* immediately scrutinizes it.

### Abaye's Challenge

*Amar lei Abaye: lo tetzitzu lei* — do not rely on what Rav Ika bar Avin said. The reason: *yevama hi d'nafla lei hatam* — he had a *yevama*, a sister-in-law whom he was obligated to marry, living in that very city. His claim that Chalazone Nihavnad is *meyuchas* is therefore self-interested and cannot be trusted.

This picks up a theme the *Gemara* developed previously, in which Rav Pappa made a negative claim about a certain place — asserting it had Samaritans intermingled within it — and the *Gemara* suggested this was motivated by the fact that he had been refused permission to marry a woman from there, making it look more like slander born of resentment than an objective report. *Tosafot* notes that it was not actually Rav Pappa himself but someone else, yet the pattern is clear. In that earlier case, one could not trust a negative claim about a place's *yichus* because the claimant had a personal grievance. Here, conversely, one cannot trust a positive claim because the claimant stands to benefit from it. Taken together, these cases leave the reliability of any testimony about a community's *yichus* deeply in question — whether the claim is favorable or unfavorable, some personal motive may be driving it.

One might ask: even if Rav Ika had a personal interest, he was transmitting in the name of Rav Chananel, who transmitted in the name of Rav — surely those earlier authorities had no such interest? The *Gemara* addresses this directly. Rav Ika responded: *atu didi?* — are you accusing me of fabricating this? I said it in the name of Rav Chananel, and Rav Chananel has no *yevama* there; he was not *nogeia ba-davar*. So they went and asked Rav Chananel directly. Rav Chananel replied: *hachi amar Rav* — this is indeed what Rav said, that Chalazone Nihavnad is like *gola* for *yuchsin*. The chain thus traces back to Rav himself — but it now emerges that Rav was the one who had the *yevama* there. The very source of the tradition is compromised by a personal interest.

The episode is a striking illustration of how the *Gemara* refuses to allow *yichus* claims to pass unchallenged. Every positive or negative assessment of a community's genealogical standing is potentially tainted by the claimant's circumstances.

### Ten Tribes Connection

*U-pligi d'Rav Abba bar Kahana* — this disputes the position of Rav Abba bar Kahana, who independently addressed the question of Chalazone Nihavnad by grounding it in a biblical text. Rav Abba bar Kahana cited the verse describing where the Ten Tribes were settled after the Assyrian exile under Sancherev: *va-yanchim ba-Chalach u-va-Chavor nehar Gozan ve-arei Madai* — and he settled them in Chalach, Chavor, the river of Gozan, and the cities of Madai. He then identified each toponym: *Chalach zo Chalazone* — Chalach is Chalazone; *Chavor zo Hadyav* — Chavor is Hadyav; *nehar Gozan ze Ginzak* — the river Gozan is Ginzak; and *arei Madai ze Chemdan ve-chaveroteha* — the cities of Madai are Chemdan and its neighboring cities. An alternative tradition identifies the last location not as Chemdan but as *Nahavnad ve-chaveroteha* — Nihavnad and its neighbors.

The implication is significant. Chalazone and Nihavnad — the very city whose *meyuchas* status was being claimed — are identified as places where the Ten Tribes were exiled and settled. Since the Ten Tribes are understood to have intermingled with the surrounding populations and lost their distinct Jewish identity, these locations cannot be presumed genealogically pure. On the contrary, according to Rav Abba bar Kahana, they are precisely the kind of places one should view with suspicion regarding *yuchsin*.

The *Gemara* then asks about the neighboring cities of Nihavnad mentioned by Shmuel: *krach Moshchi, Chutzki, ve-Rumki*. Rabbi Yochanan adds: *ve-kulam lipsol* — all of these are cities problematic for *yuchsin*.

### Border Cities

The *Gemara* now introduces a further complication from the book of Daniel. The verse *ve-tlat alin be-pumeih bein shinaiha* — three leaves were in its mouth, between its teeth — is expounded by Rabbi Yochanan: *zu Chalazone, Hadyav, ve-Natzivin* — these are the three cities, Chalazone, Hadyav, and Natzivin. *She-pe'amim bole'an u-fe'amim poltetan* — sometimes they are swallowed up by the Persian Empire and sometimes they are expelled from it.

The image is vivid: these cities sit on the border, sometimes absorbed within the empire's control and sometimes outside it, perpetually shifting between jurisdictions, like territory that alternates between two powers. The analogy to border regions that have historically moved between empires — absorbed one year, expelled the next — makes the point concrete. Their unstable political status is directly connected to their uncertain *yuchsin* status: when a community's identity and boundaries are permanently in flux, its genealogical records cannot be relied upon.

The *Gemara* then proceeds with the vision of the bear-like beast, identified by a *baraita* as referring to the Persians — *she-ochlim ve-shotim ke-dov, u-muse'arim ke-dov, ve-ein lahem menuchah ke-dov* — who eat and drink like a bear, are as hairy as a bear, and have no rest like a bear. Rabbi Ami, when he would see a Persian riding, would say: there goes a traveling bear. It is worth noting — as the scholar Yaakov Elman has observed — that this particular set of pejoratives (hairy, fat, bear-like) was itself a Persian slur directed against others; the *Gemara* here applies it back against the Persians themselves, an ironic inversion captured by the principle *kol ha-posel b'mumo posel*.

Rav asked Levi to describe the Persians through a *mashal*. Levi said they resemble the troops of the house of David — meaning they are valorous and powerful. The Chaverin, a nation that came to dominate parts of the region after the Persians and proved far more oppressive toward Jews, are like *malachei chabalah* — angels of destruction. The Ishmaelites are compared to *se'irim shel beit ha-kisei* — the demons of the outhouse. And the Torah scholars of Bavel? They are like the ministering angels — *malachei ha-shareit* — dressed in white, wrapped in their garments, their appearance distinguished and luminous. Rashi elaborates, citing other *Gemarot* that compare the *talmidei chachamim* of Bavel to the vision in Yechezkel of the angel *ish lavush badim*, clothed in linen — because they too would sit wrapped in *sedinim ha-metzuyatzin*, fringed linen garments, like heavenly figures.

## Rebbi's Prophecy

### Humanya

*Ki hava nicha nafshia d'Rabbi amar* — as Rebbi lay dying, he was moved to prophetic utterance, and this is what he declared. The first statement: *Humanya ika b'Bavel kulah Amonai* — there is a place in Bavel called Humanya, and it is entirely populated by Ammonites. The halakhic relevance is implicit: the prohibition of *lo yavo Amoni* applies to the male — *Ammoni v'lo Ammonit* — so the concern, while not all-encompassing, is nonetheless a real *yuchsin* issue.

That Rebbi chose to devote his final moments to enumerating these genealogical problems is itself striking. Rebbi was the great champion of Babylonian Jewry's honor — it was he who resisted the claim that Bavel was inferior to Eretz Yisrael in terms of *yichus*. And yet here, on his deathbed, he is cataloguing problems specifically in Bavel. The answer may be that precisely because he otherwise defended Bavel's honor, this transmission had special weight — he was not slandering a community out of bias, but passing down information he felt obligated to disclose at the end of his life, information that might otherwise be lost.

### Misgarya

*Misgarya ika b'Bavel kulah d'mamzeira hi* — there is a place in Bavel called Misgarya that is entirely populated by *mamzerim*. Again, the focus is on Bavel specifically — the very land whose genealogical standing Rebbi had defended throughout his life.

### Birka

*Birka ika b'Bavel, shenei achim yesh sham ha-machlifim neshotehem zeh la-zeh* — there is a place called Birka in Bavel, where two brothers live who swap their wives with one another. The consequence is that all children born of these unions are *mamzerim*, since a married woman who cohabits with another man produces illegitimate offspring. Rebbi identifies Birka as a community bearing this taint.

### Birta deSatya

*Birta deSatya ika b'Bavel, hayom saru me-acharei ha-Makom* — there is a place in Bavel called Birta deSatya, meaning something like the city that strays, and Rebbi declares: today they strayed from God. What occurred? *De-akfei peira be-khifrei be-Shabta* — fish rose to the surface of the pond on *Shabbat*, and the townspeople could not resist the opportunity: *va-azlu ve-tzadu lehu be-Shabta* — they went and caught the fish on *Shabbat*.

The obvious question arises: why does Rebbi include this? The other items in his deathbed disclosure concern permanent *yuchsin* problems — communities with *mamzerim* or *Ammonites* intermingled. A single *Shabbat* violation, whatever its gravity, would not seem to leave a lasting genealogical stain. What is the relevance?

The *Gemara* continues: *ve-shamteinun Rav Akhi berei de-Rabbi Yoshiya* — Rav Akhi the son of Rabbi Yoshiya put them in *cherem* on account of what they had done. But the consequence of this severe communal excommunication was *ve-ishtamad* — they all abandoned the faith. The lesson embedded here is itself a warning: a *cherem* applied to an entire city, based on what may have been prophetic knowledge rather than direct halakhic adjudication, can be counterproductive to the point of catastrophe. The *Gemara* records the outcome in a single phrase, without further editorial comment, leaving the weight of the result to speak for itself.

### Akra de-Agma

*Akra de-Agma ika b'Bavel, Adda bar Ahava yesh bah* — there is a place in Bavel called Akra de-Agma, and in it lives Adda bar Ahava. *Hayom yoshev be-cheiko shel Avraham* — today he sits in the lap of Avraham. Rashi suggests this might mean he has died and ascended to the world of the righteous, but notes the difficulty that Rav Adda bar Ahava was a much later figure than Rebbi. An alternative reading offered by Rashi is that today was his *bris milah* — he was just born, and is thus literally in the embrace of Avraham, as the sandek holds the infant. *Tosafot* proposes that there may have been two figures with the same name. The precise resolution remains uncertain, but the significance of the mention is clear: Rebbi wanted to note that in this place, a figure of great spiritual stature was present.

### Succession of Tzadikim

*Hayom nolad Rav Yehuda b'Bavel* — today, Rav Yehuda was born in Bavel. This statement becomes the pivot for a teaching about the continuity of *tzadikim*: *keshe-mes Rabbi Akiva nolad Rebbe* — when Rabbi Akiva died, Rebbi was born; *keshe-mes Rebbe nolad Rav Yehuda* — when Rebbi died, Rav Yehuda was born; *keshe-mes Rav Yehuda nolad Rava* — when Rav Yehuda died, Rava was born; *keshe-mes Rava nolad Rav Ashi* — when Rava died, Rav Ashi was born. The *Rambam's* text had the formulation *ad she-lo mes Rava nolad Rav Ashi* — before Rava died, Rav Ashi was already born — suggesting the succession is not merely sequential but overlapping.

*Le-lamedakh she-ein tzadik niftar min ha-olam ad she-nivra tzadik kamosohu* — to teach you that a righteous person does not depart from this world until another like him has already been created to take his place. The prooftext is *ve-zarach ha-shemesh u-va ha-shemesh* — the sun rises and the sun sets — read as a statement that before one sun sets, the next has already risen. This is supported by the story of Eli and Shmuel: *ad she-lo kavsah shimsho shel Eli zarchah shimsho shel Shmuel ha-Ramati* — before Eli's light was extinguished, Shmuel's had already begun to shine, as indicated by the verse *ve-ner Elokim terem yichbeh u-Shmuel shochev* — the lamp of God had not yet gone out when Shmuel was lying in the sanctuary, already receiving his prophetic calling.

One might pause over the choice of the word *tzadik*. The chain being traced is not merely a chain of righteous individuals in a general sense, but of generational halakhic leadership — great *rabbinic* figures whose absence would leave a community without guidance. The proof from Eli and Shmuel is instructive in this regard, as Shmuel was Eli's own student. The verse speaks to an overlap, not merely a succession, and the concern is that no generation be left without its guiding light.

The *Gemara* then cites: *tziva Hashem l'Yaakov sevivo tzarav* — God has commanded that Yaakov's oppressors always surround him. Rabbi Yehuda explains: *kegon Humanya l'pum Nahara* — just as Humanya surrounds Pum Nahara, a place inhabited by Jews, closing the circle back to Rebbi's opening statement about Humanya and its Ammonite population.

## Pelatya ben Benaya

### Good Version

The *Gemara* cites a verse from Yechezkel: *va-yehi k'hinavi u-Pelatya ben Benaya met* — and it was as I prophesied that Pelatya ben Benaya died, and I fell upon my face and cried out with a great voice, saying, "Ah, Lord God!" This is interpreted as a dispute between Rav and Shmuel. One says the prophet's outcry was *l'tova* — mourning the death of a righteous man. The other says it was *l'ra'ah* — distress that an evil man died too peacefully.

The positive interpretation unfolds as follows. The governor of Mishan — and it is worth noting that Mishan is one of the places previously identified as having problematic *yuchsin* — was the son-in-law of Nevuchadnetzar. After Nevuchadnetzar destroyed the *Beis HaMikdash* and took the Jews captive, the governor sent a message demanding his share of the captives: *mikulei hai shivya d'ayeisis lach reisha d'varta lan* — from all those captives you brought, give us the leadership of the settlement, our portion. Nevuchadnetzar wanted to comply and send Jewish captives to Mishan. Pelatya ben Benaya intervened and said to Nevuchadnetzar: *anan d'chashvinan neiku mikameich hacha* — we Israelites, who are important, should remain here with you, the great king; *v'avdin neizlu l'hasam* — let our slaves go there. By this maneuver, he ensured that the Jewish captives stayed in Bavel rather than being sent to Mishan.

Why was this a kindness? Rashi explains it on two grounds. First, Bavel afforded the Jews far better conditions than Mishan would have. Second, it is generally worse to be a servant of a servant — *avadim l'avadim* — subject to someone who is himself subordinate to a greater power, than to be in the service of the ultimate sovereign. The prophet's anguished cry was therefore a lament: *mi she-asa tova b'Yisrael yamut b'chatzi yamav?* — someone who did such good for Israel should die in the prime of his life?

### Bad Version

The negative interpretation draws on a different passage in Yechezkel. The prophet is brought to the eastern gate of the *Beis HaMikdash*, where he sees twenty-five men, and among them he identifies *Yaazanya ben Azur ve-Pelatya ben Benaya, sarei ha-am* — officers of the nation. He is then brought to the inner courtyard, where again there are approximately twenty-five men — *achoreihem el heichal Hashem u-fneihem kedma* — their backs were toward the sanctuary and their faces toward the east.

The *Gemara* asks: when the verse says their faces were toward the east, is it not obvious that their backs were toward the west? What is the need to specify *achoreihem el heichal Hashem*? *Melamed she-hayu mafri'in atzman u-mitrizin atzman klapei ma'alah* — this teaches that they would expose and relieve themselves in a manner directed toward God, as an act of deliberate desecration and contempt toward the *Heichal*. The prophet's outcry of anguish was therefore not a lament but rather an expression of outrage: *mi she-asa ra'ah zot b'Yisrael yamut al mitato?* — someone who committed such desecration against Israel should die peacefully in his bed?

### Shmuel's Position

*Tistayem b'Shmuel d'amar l'ra'ah* — the *Gemara* proposes to identify Shmuel as the one who holds the negative version. The reasoning: *d'amar Rabbi Chiya bar Avin amar Shmuel, Moshchani harei hi k'gola l'yuchsin* — Shmuel says Moshchani is like *gola* for *yuchsin*. And separately: *Mishan lo chashu lah, lo mishum avdut v'lo mishum mamzerut, ela kohanim she-hayu bah lo hikpidu al gerushot* — regarding Mishan, there is no concern for slave status or *mamzerut*; the only issue is that the *kohanim* there did not guard themselves from marrying divorced women, creating *chalalim*. This is exclusively a *kehuna* concern, not a general *yuchsin* problem.

Now, if according to the positive version, what was sent to Mishan were slaves — not Jews — and Shmuel holds that there are no slave-status concerns in Mishan, this would seem to be no problem at all. But the *Gemara* notes that Shmuel has a principle: *ha-mafkir avdo yatza l'cheirut v'eino tzarich get shichrur* — one who renounces ownership of his slave, the slave goes free without requiring a formal writ of manumission. If the Jewish masters were taken captive and their slaves along with them, the masters' despair of ever regaining their slaves constitutes an implicit renunciation of ownership. *She-ne'emar kol eved ish miknat kasef* — the verse says "slave of a man," meaning only when the *ish* maintains his ownership is the person considered a slave; once effective ownership lapses, the slave is freed.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *l'olam eima lach Shmuel amar l'tova* — you can maintain that Shmuel holds the positive version after all. Even according to Shmuel, slaves were sent to Mishan; and even according to Shmuel, those individuals ceased to be slaves once ownership was effectively abandoned. Therefore Shmuel's statement that Mishan has no slave-status concern is entirely consistent with the positive reading of Pelatya's intervention.

This resolution carries an implicit but striking observation about the scope of the *yuchsin* concern. The *Gemara* is essentially saying that even freed slaves — individuals who, in the previous chapter of the *Mishnah*, are ranked lower than *yisrael* in the hierarchy of marriageability — are treated as having no meaningful *yuchsin* issue once they are technically free. The concern for *yichus* is thus narrowly halakhic, not a broader social or cultural judgment about genealogical quality.

## Rabbi Meir vs. Chachamim

### Rabbi Meir's Position

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: zu divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: the entire framework we have been working with — the graduated hierarchy of Bavel as superior to *chutz la'aretz* for *yuchsin*, with all its distinctions and concerns — represents only the position of Rabbi Meir. It is not the consensus view.

This is a remarkable statement. Everything the *Gemara* has developed across the preceding dappim about the superiority of Bavel, the problems of other lands, and the careful distinctions between communities — all of it, according to this formulation, reflects the singular opinion of one Tanna. And this statement comes, pointedly, from Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel — the very authority who elsewhere was responsible for many of the most pointed warnings about *yuchsin* problems in specific places.

### Chachamim's View

*Ve-chachamim omrim kol ha'aratzot b'chezkas kashrus hein omdot* — the Chachamim say: all lands stand under a presumption of genealogical fitness. This is a sweeping counter-position. Rather than elaborate hierarchies of *yuchsin* quality between different lands, the Chachamim apply the standard principle of *chazakah* — the ordinary legal presumption that things are as they appear and as they have been — to the question of lineage. Why should a person's fitness for marriage be treated differently from any other matter of personal status, where we simply follow the *chazakah*?

### Amemar vs. Rav Ashi

*Amemar sharei lei l'Rav Huna bar Nasan l'minsav itesa mechoza'ah* — Ameimar permitted Rav Huna bar Nasan to marry a woman from Chozaya, a place outside of Bavel and therefore in *chutz la'aretz*. Rav Ashi challenged him: *mah da'atach?* — on what basis? You are relying on the statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel that this whole framework is only Rabbi Meir's view. But the houses of Rav Kahana, Rav Pappa, and Rav Zevid did not transmit the tradition in these terms — they did not frame it as a dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim, but as a settled and unanimous concern.

### Version Disputes

Despite Rav Ashi's objection, Ameimar did not accept it. The *Gemara* records: *afilu hachi lo kibel minei* — even so, he did not accept from him, *mishum d'shmi'a lei me-Rav Zevid d'Naharda'ah* — because he had heard it from Rav Zevid of Nehardea, a different Rav Zevid from the one cited earlier, that this framework of *yuchsin* concerns was indeed only Rabbi Meir's position.

The competing attributions here are themselves revealing. The same essential dispute — whether concern for *yuchsin* across lands is a universal position or merely Rabbi Meir's minority view — is being transmitted differently by different *batei midrash*. The houses of Rav Kahana, Rav Pappa, and Rav Zevid (one version) did not transmit the framing as a *machloket*; Rav Zevid of Nehardea (another version) did. This mirrors a broader pattern in the *Gemara's* treatment of *yuchsin* throughout these pages: even on the question of how much to worry about *yuchsin*, there is irresolvable uncertainty about what anyone actually held.

It is also worth noting the internal tension in attributing the limiting formulation — "this is only Rabbi Meir" — to Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel. Throughout the preceding discussion, it has consistently been Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel who transmitted the most pointed statements about *yuchsin* problems: this place is filled with *mamzerim*, that place is problematic, and so on. Yet here it is the same authority who provides the most powerful tool for dismissing the entire apparatus — it is only Rabbi Meir. One possible resolution is that *la matni hachi* — the tradition that this is a dispute of Rabbi Meir versus Chachamim — was itself contested, and different *amoraim* disputed whether Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel actually said it.

One further observation the *Gemara* invites: is Rabbi Meir's concern for *yuchsin* consistent with his general halakhic disposition of *chaish l'miyuta* — being attentive to small minorities and minority possibilities? If so, the Chachamim's counter-position — that everyone has a *chezkas kashrus* — represents simply the standard application of *chazakah*, without any heightened vigilance toward the remote possibility of genealogical disqualification.

## Future Purification

### Rabbi Yosi's View

*Tanu Rabbanan: mamzerei ve-netinei tehorim le'atid lavo, divrei Rabbi Yosi* — our Rabbis taught: *mamzerim* and *netinim* will become pure in the future, in the words of Rabbi Yosi. This is a significant claim about the eschatological resolution of personal status issues. *Netinim* — descendants of the Gibeonites who were made Temple servants by David — are disqualified from full membership in the Jewish community, their status a product of circumstances rather than of any sin. And *mamzerim*, however they came to be, bear their status indefinitely. Rabbi Yosi maintains that in the future, both will be purified.

The prooftext is Yechezkel's vision of the messianic era: *ve-zarakti aleichem mayim tehorim u-tehartem* — I will sprinkle pure water upon you and you shall become pure. If God is going to purify Israel from all impurity in the future, that purification must include the removal of these personal status stigmas as well.

### Rabbi Meir's Response

Rabbi Meir disagrees. *Keshe-hu omer mi-kol tumoteichem u-mi-kol gilluleichem* — when the verse says "from all your impurities and from all your idolatries," that specifies the source of the purification: sin and ritual defilement. *Ve-lo min ha-mamzerut* — but not from personal status issues. The word *tumah* refers to moral and ritual contamination; the status of *mamzer* is a halakhic category of personal identity, not a species of ritual impurity, and the verse does not promise its erasure.

Rabbi Yosi responds: *keshe-hu omer ateher etchem* — but then the verse continues with the separate phrase "I will purify you," which is a second, broader statement of purification. *Hevei omer af min ha-mamzerut* — this additional formulation includes even the removal of *mamzerut*.

*Bishlama le-Rabbi Meir*, the verse *ve-yashav mamzer be-Ashdod* is well-understood — in the future, *mamzerim* will be settled in Ashdod, meaning they will be identified and separated, retaining their distinct status even in messianic times. But according to Rabbi Yosi, *mai ve-yashav mamzer be-Ashdod?* Rav Yosef offers a *targum*: *ve-yateivun beit Yisrael le-ruchatzan be-arahon, de-havu damu beh le-nuchrain* — the house of Israel will dwell in security in their own land, they who had been like strangers. The *mamzer* here is a metaphor for Israel's own experience of exile and marginalization — the nation that was itself like a rejected outsider, dwelling in a land not its own, will now return and settle securely. The implication is that just as Israel's status as a stranger is reversed in the messianic era, so too is the *mamzer's* status of exclusion reversed.

There is something poignant in this reading. The *Gemara* is drawing on the profound parallel between the *mamzer's* condition — excluded, marginalized, unable to marry fully into the community — and the Jewish people's condition in exile. Both are described by the same word, and both find their resolution in the same future moment.

## Future Purification…

### Yechezkel's Vision

The previous discussion of Yechezkel's prophecy continues. Rabbi Yosi's reading of *ve-yashav mamzer be-Ashdod*, rendered by Rav Yosef's *targum* as speaking of Israel's homecoming and security after generations of displacement, frames the entire messianic purification as encompassing the reversal of every form of exclusion — including the status of *mamzer*.

### Halacha K'Rabbi Yosi

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* — Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi. Again this is Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel — the same voice that previously said the entire *yuchsin* framework is only Rabbi Meir's view, and the same voice behind so much of the *yuchsin* discussion across these pages. The package is consistent: we are not particularly concerned about *yuchsin* in the present, and in the future it will all be set right.

The ruling is *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* even though the subject matter is eschatological — a statement about what will be true *le'atid lavo*. The use of the term *halacha* in this context is itself striking.

### Rav Yosef's Statement

*Amar Rav Yosef: ilmale d'amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel halacha k'Rabbi Yosi, hava ati Eliyahu u-mafik minnan tzvarne tzvarne kolarin* — Rav Yosef said: were it not for the fact that Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel ruled that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi, Eliyahu would have come and extracted from us, group by group, great numbers of people — identifying the *psulim* among us and expelling them from the community.

This is a breathtaking statement. The ruling *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* is understood not merely as a practical legal decision about the present, but as a decision that effectively reshapes what Eliyahu will do when he comes. Before this ruling, the plan — as one reading of the verses suggested — was that Eliyahu would come and expose all the hidden *psulim*, performing a massive rectification through exclusion. By ruling *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi*, the tradition has changed the trajectory: the future rectification will be one of inclusion and purification, not identification and expulsion.

This is reminiscent of the principle *lo bashamayim hi* — the Torah is not in heaven — but even more radical, because here the *halacha* is not merely overriding a heavenly voice in a present dispute, but actively determining what the heavenly plan will look like in the future. As one participant noted, this is an extraordinary use of the word *halacha* in the context of a question about messianic times — and the audacity of it is clearly the point.

## Marriage Status Rules

### Ger and Mamzeret

Having engaged in an extended multi-layered dismantling of the practical concern for *yuchsin* — through arguments about unknowability, through limiting it to Rabbi Meir's minority position, through appeal to future purification — the *Gemara* now pivots decisively to concrete, specific *halachot* about marriage between identified individuals of known status. The previous discussion has effectively cleared the ground; now we address what the rules actually are when the relevant facts are known.

*Tanu Rabbanan: ger nosei mamzeret, divrei Rabbi Yosi* — a *ger* may marry a *mamzeret*, in the words of Rabbi Yosi. *Rabbi Yehuda omer: ger lo yisa mamzeret* — Rabbi Yehuda says a *ger* may not marry a *mamzeret*. The pivot of the dispute is the verse *lo yavo mamzer bi-khal Hashem* — a *mamzer* may not enter the assembly of God. Does that prohibition encompass *gerim* as well? Is a *ger* included in the *kahal* that a *mamzer* cannot enter?

### Chamisha Kahale

The *Gemara* grounds the dispute in a *derashah* on the word *kahal* and its multiple occurrences in the Torah. *Chamisha kahale ketivi* — the word *kahal* appears five times in relevant contexts. Rashi enumerates: *lo yavo mamzer bi-khal Hashem* (once); *lo yavo lo bi-khal Hashem* (again, emphasizing the double exclusion); *lo yavo Amoni u-Moavi bi-khal Hashem* (concerning Ammonites and Moabites); and further instances. Each use of *kahal* is taken to include an additional category of Jews within the prohibited assembly: one *kahal* for *kohanim*, one for *leviyim*, and one for *yisraelim* — establishing that the prohibition on a *mamzer* marrying into the *kahal* applies not only to the inner priestly circles but to the full range of Jewish lineage.

### Vadai vs. Safek

The remaining two uses of the word *kahal* teach that: *v'chad l'mishri mamzer b'shtuki* — one use permits a *mamzer* to marry a *shtuki* (a person of unknown paternity, a doubtful *mamzer*); *v'chad l'mishri shtuki b'yisrael* — and one use permits a *shtuki* to marry into the full Jewish community. The reasoning is that *bi-khal Hashem* means *kahal vadai* — a *definite* assembly, a person of *definite* Jewish fitness. *Kahal safek* — a person whose status is uncertain — is not encompassed by the prohibition. Therefore *lo yavo mamzer bi-khal Hashem* means only a *definite mamzer* may not enter; a person of *doubtful mamzer* status (*shtuki*) may marry a *yisrael*. Correspondingly, the prohibition bars a *mamzer* only from a *definite kahal*; he may marry a *shtuki*, who is only a *safek kahal*.

The practical significance is substantial: *mid'oraita*, a *shtuki* may marry into a *kohen*, *levi*, or *yisrael* family. The *mishnah's* stricter treatment of *shtuki* is *mid'rabbanan*.

### Gerim Outside Kahal

*Kahal gerim lo ikra kahal* — converts were never referred to as *kahal* in the Torah. Therefore, the prohibition of *lo yavo mamzer bi-khal Hashem* does not apply to them as the protected community. A *mamzer* may marry a *giyoret*, and a *ger* may marry a *mamzeret*, because *gerim* were not included in the Torah's designation of *kahal Hashem*. This is Rabbi Yosi's position.

### One Law Argument

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and sources his position in a different verse: *ha-kahal, chuka achat lachem v'la-ger ha-gar* — the assembly: one law for you and for the *ger* who dwells among you. This verse explicitly places the *ger* within the *kahal*, meaning that when the Torah says a *mamzer* cannot enter *kahal Hashem*, the *ger* is included in that *kahal*. Accordingly, a *mamzer* cannot marry a *giyoret*, and a *ger* cannot marry a *mamzeret*.

Rabbi Yosi responds: *chuka achat hefsik ha-inyan* — the phrase *chuka achat* ("one law") interrupts the passage and is a separate statement. The verse says "one law for you and for the *ger*" — meaning certain laws apply equally to both — but it does not mean the *ger* is identified as part of the *kahal* in the genealogical sense.

### Marriage to Kohenet

*Echad ger v'echad eved meshuchrar v'chalal mutarin b'kohenet* — a *ger*, a freed slave, and a *chalal* are all permitted to marry a *kohenet* (the daughter of a *kohen*). This is uncontested. The Torah's marriage restrictions on *kehuna* apply to *kohanim* as men — they may not marry a *giyoret*, a *chalalah*, a divorced woman, or a *zonah*. There is no corresponding restriction on which men a *kohenet* may marry. Since a *ger*, freed slave, and *chalal* each have the same *yisrael* status for purposes of marriage eligibility, they may marry any woman a *yisrael* may marry, including a *bat kohen*.

### Women's Status

*Mesayei lei l'Rav, d'amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: hitir kesherot l'hinasei l'psulim* — this supports Rav's position that *kesherot* — women of fit status — are not restricted from marrying men of *pasul* categories. The entire structure of marriage prohibitions in *kehuna* is directed at the *kohanic* male. A woman, regardless of her father's lineage, bears no independent restriction based on whom she marries, as long as the marriage itself is permitted from his side.

### Zeira Stoned

*Darash Zeira b'Mechuza: ger mutar b'mamzeret* — Rabbi Zeira stood up and publicly taught in Mechuza that a *ger* is permitted to marry a *mamzeret*, in accordance with Rabbi Yosi. *Ragmuhu kulei alma b'etrogaihu* — everyone pelted him with their *etrogim*. The incident apparently occurred during *Sukkot*. Rashi explains that the audience was not actually offended by the permissive ruling itself — being told that something is *mutar* is hardly an insult. What offended them was the underlying *derashah*: Rabbi Zeira's teaching implied that *gerim* were never called *kahal*, that they stand outside the primary designation of the Jewish assembly. For a community with a large *ger* population, this was experienced as a statement of exclusion and diminishment of identity, not as halakhic permission.

### Rava's Strategy

*Amar Rava: mi ika d'darash milta ki hai b'duchta d'shchichei giyorei?* — who would be so careless as to teach this in a place full of converts? Rava himself then taught in Mechuza. He began with: *ger mutar b'kohenet* — a *ger* may marry a *kohenet*. The community was delighted — *tnu b'shirai*, they clothed him in silk garments — for this ruling affirmed the *ger's* full status and his access to the most distinguished women of the Jewish community. Then Rava added: *ger mutar b'mamzeret* — a *ger* may also marry a *mamzeret*. The community objected that he had undermined his first teaching.

Rava replied: *lo d'tava l'chu avdi l'chu* — no, I am doing you good. *Ei bei meihacha nasiv v'ei bei meihacha nasiv* — if he wishes, he may marry from here (a *kohenet*), and if he wishes, from there (a *mamzeret*). The point Rava was making was not that the second ruling is offensive, but that it should be understood as an expansion of opportunity, not as a statement of diminishment. The pedagogical lesson is clear: the same halakhic content, presented in a different sequence and framing, can be received as affirming rather than insulting. One begins by establishing the *ger's* full dignity and standing, and then presents the broader range of his legal options.

### Final Halacha

*Hilkhata: ger mutar b'kohenet u-mutar b'mamzeret* — the practical ruling is that a *ger* is permitted with a *kohenet* and permitted with a *mamzeret*. *Mutar b'kohenet d'huzharu kesherot l'hinasei l'psulim* — the permission with a *kohenet* reflects the rule that women of fit status are not restricted from marrying men of *pasul* category; the restriction of *kehuna* runs only to the *kohanic* male. *U-mutar b'mamzeret k'Rabbi Yosi* — the permission with a *mamzeret* follows Rabbi Yosi's ruling, which is the *halacha l'maaseh*.
