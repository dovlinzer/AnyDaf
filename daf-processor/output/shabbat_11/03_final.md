# Shabbat 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## City Settlement

### Recently Settled Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא בַּר מַחְסֵיָא אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם יְחַזֵּר אָדָם וְיֵשֵׁב בְּעִיר שֶׁיְּשִׁיבָתָהּ קְרוֹבָה, שֶׁמִּתּוֹךְ שֶׁיְּשִׁיבָתָהּ קְרוֹבָה עֲוֹנוֹתֶיהָ מוּעָטִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava bar Meḥasseya said that Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said Rav said: A person should always seek and dwell in a city whose settling took place in the recent past, meaning that it was recently established, as due to the fact that its settling is recent its sins are few,** as its residents have not yet had the opportunity to commit many sins there.

The daf begins with a series of statements transmitted by Rabba bar Mechasya in the name of Rav Chama bar Guria in the name of Rav. The first of these concerns where a person ought to choose to live: *le'olam yachzor adam veyeishev be'ir sheyeshivatah kerovah* — a person should always seek to dwell in a city that has been recently settled, *shemitokh sheyeshivatah kerovah avonoteha mu'atin*, for since it has been settled only recently, its sins are few.

This is a striking and somewhat pessimistic observation. The implicit assumption is that the longer a city has existed, the greater its accumulated history of sin, so that its transgressions outweigh its merits. One might ask: what does this say about Yerushalayim, a city thousands of years old? The question answers itself.

### Lot & Sodom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִנֵּה נָא הָעִיר הַזֹּאת קְרוֹבָה לָנוּס שָׁמָּה וְהִיא מִצְעָר״. מַאי ״קְרוֹבָה״? אִילֵימָא קְרוֹבָה דְּמִיקָּרְבָא וְזוּטָא — וְהָא קָא חָזוּ לַהּ! אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁיְּשִׁיבָתָהּ קְרוֹבָה, עֲוֹנוֹתֶיהָ מוּצְעָרִין. אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין: מַאי קְרָא? — דִּכְתִיב: ״אִמָּלְטָה נָּא שָׁמָּה״. ״נָא״ בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא — חַמְשִׁין וְחַד הָוֵי, וְשֶׁל סְדוֹם חֲמִישִּׁים וּשְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **as it is stated that Lot said to the angel: "Behold, here is this city that is close to run away to and it is small"** (Genesis 19:20). **What is the meaning of the word close? If you say: That it is close in distance and truly small, why did he need to say that to the angel? Didn't they see it? Rather, because its settling was close,** that it had been recently settled, and therefore **its sins were few. Rabbi Avin said: What is the verse that teaches us that Zoar was newer than the other cities? As it is written: "I will escape there please [*na*]"** (Genesis 19:20); the **numerological value of *nun alef*, the letters of the word *na*, is fifty-one, while Sodom was fifty-two** years old.

The source for this principle is derived from Lot's plea to the angels saving him from Sedom. The verse reads: *Hineh na ha'ir hazot kerovah lanus shamah vehi mitzar* — behold, this city nearby is small; let me flee there. The Gemara raises an obvious difficulty: since the angels could see for themselves that the city was nearby and small, why would Lot have to mention it? The answer is that *kerovah* does not mean geographically close, but recently settled: *mitokh sheyeshivatah kerovah avonoteha mutz'ari mi-Sedom* — because it has been settled only recently, its sins are fewer than those of Sedom and the surrounding cities, and it is therefore worthy of being spared.

Rav Avi then asks: where is this embedded in the text? He answers through *gematriya*: the word *na* — as in *imaltah na shamah*, "let me please flee there" — has a numerical value of 51. Sedom, it turns out, had existed for 52 years, and its period of peace was half that, namely 26 years. Rashi works out the 52-year figure from the genealogical calculations surrounding Peleg, whose name commemorates the division at the time of the flood, such that tracing the chronology yields Sedom's age at the time of its destruction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶשְׂרִים וָשֵׁשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: ״שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה עָבְדוּ אֶת כְּדׇרְלָעוֹמֶר וּשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה מָרָדוּ. וּבְאַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְגוֹ׳״
>
> **Translation:** during which they committed their sins was altogether **twenty-six** years, as it is written: **"Twelve years they served Chedorlaomer and thirteen years they rebelled, and in the fourteenth year** Chedorlaomer came" (Genesis 14:4–5). The twelve years plus the fourteen years during which they were enslaved were not years of tranquility, leaving only twenty-six tranquil years when they were sinful.

The 26 years of peace is derived from the verse in *Lech Lecha*: *shteim-esreh shanah avadu et Kedorlaomer ushlosh-esreh shanah maradu* — twelve years they served Kedorlaomer, and thirteen years they rebelled. The Gemara and Rashi read this not as "in the thirteenth year they rebelled" but as "for a period of thirteen years they were in rebellion," and in the fourteenth year the great war of the four kings against the five took place — the war in which Avraham ultimately interceded. Thus: twelve years of servitude, thirteen of rebellion, one year of war — twenty-six years of upheaval. After Avraham saved the kings of Sedom and Amora, there followed twenty-six years of peace, after which Sedom was destroyed. The small city to which Lot fled had been settled for only 51 years — one year less — and so its sins were fewer.

### Synagogue Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא בַּר מַחְסֵיָא אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: כׇּל עִיר שֶׁגַּגּוֹתֶיהָ גְּבוֹהִין מִבֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת לְסוֹף חֲרֵבָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְרוֹמֵם אֶת בֵּית אֱלֹהֵינוּ וּלְהַעֲמִיד אֶת חׇרְבוֹתָיו״. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּבָתִּים, אֲבָל בְּקִשְׁקוּשֵׁי וַאֲבַרְוָרֵי לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava bar Meḥasseya said that Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said Rav said: Any city whose roofs are higher than the synagogue will ultimately be destroyed** because of the contempt shown the synagogue. Allusion to this is from that **which is stated: "To uplift the house of our God and restore its ruins"** (Ezra 9:9). The house that is devoted to God needs to be elevated above the other houses of the city. The Gemara adds: **And this applies only to the height of the houses themselves. However, if the poles [*kashkushei*] and the towers [*abrurei*]** that extend from the house are higher than the synagogue, **we have no** problem **with it.**

A further statement in the same tradition: *kol ir shegagoteha gevohin mibeit haknesset lesof charevah* — any city whose rooftops are higher than the synagogue will ultimately be destroyed. The proof-text is from Ezra, speaking of the return and the rebuilding of the Beit HaMikdash: *leromem et beit eloheinu uleha'amid et charvotav* — to raise up the house of our God and to erect its ruins. If you elevate the house of God, the ruins will be rebuilt; if not, the reverse will follow.

A qualification is offered: *vehani mili bebatim* — this concern applies only to houses. *Aval bekaskusei ve'avruyei leit lan bah* — for monuments, towers, or city walls (the latter being the interpretation of the *Aruch*), there is no problem if they are taller. It is specifically residential buildings that must not overtop the *shul*.

### Mata Mechasya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲנָא עֲבַדִי לְמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא דְּלָא חֲרַבָה. וְהָא חֲרַבָה! מֵאוֹתוֹ עָוֹן לֹא חָרְבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I caused the city of Mata Meḥasseya to not be destroyed** by building the synagogue higher than the other houses. The Gemara asks: **Wasn't** Mata Meḥasseya ultimately **destroyed?** The Gemara answers: **It was not destroyed because of that sin;** other sins caused its destruction.

Rav Ashi states: *ana avdi lemata Mechasya delo charva* — I have ensured that Mata Mechasya will not be destroyed, having taken care that the *beit haknesset* stands taller than the other buildings. The Gemara immediately objects: *veha charva* — but Mata Mechasya was in fact destroyed! This must be a later editorial comment, since it would have been rather pointed for Rav Ashi to have said this after the destruction had already occurred. The Gemara resolves it: *me'oto avon lo charva* — it was not destroyed on account of that particular sin. One might observe that this resolution, while technically satisfying, renders the principle somewhat unfalsifiable: a city may be destroyed, but it can always be said that the cause was some other transgression.

## Governance

### Gov't Complexity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא בַּר מַחְסֵיָא אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: אִם יִהְיוּ כׇּל הַיָּמִים דְּיוֹ, וַאֲגַמִּים קוּלְמוֹסִים, וְשָׁמַיִם יְרִיעוֹת, וְכׇל בְּנֵי אָדָם לַבְלָרִין — אֵין מַסְפִּיקִים לִכְתּוֹב חֲלָלָהּ שֶׁל רְשׁוּת. מַאי קְרָאָה אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: ״שָׁמַיִם לָרוּם וָאָרֶץ לָעוֹמֶק וְלֵב מְלָכִים אֵין חֵקֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava bar Meḥasseya said that Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said Rav said: Even if all the seas would be ink, and the reeds that grow near swamps would be quills, and the heavens would be parchment upon which the words would be written, and all the people would be scribes; all of these are insufficient to write the unquantifiable space of governmental authority,** i.e., all the considerations with which a government must concern itself and deal. **Rav Mesharshiya said: What is the verse that alludes to this? "The Heavens on High and the land to the depth and the heart of kings are unsearchable"** (Proverbs 25:3).

Another statement in the same tradition: *im yiheyu kol hayamim deyo va'agamim kumsim veshamayim yeri'ot vechol benei adam lavlarin, ein maspikin lichtov chalalah shel rashut* — if all the seas were ink and all the swamps were reeds, and the heavens were parchment and all people were scribes, it would still not suffice to write out the full depth of what is required to govern well.

The proof-text is cited by Rav Mesharshiya from Proverbs: *shamayim larom ve'eretz la'omek velev melachim ein cheker* — the heavens above, the earth below, and the hearts of kings — no one can fully fathom them. Rashi unpacks what this means practically: the ruler must hold in mind the affairs of many provinces, the conduct of wars, and the administration of judgments across countless departments. The sheer volume of information required — the data, the decisions, the management of military and civil affairs simultaneously — defies complete description. This is a remarkable statement; while the Talmud elsewhere speaks of gratitude for government and the need to pray for its welfare, a passage so attentive to the genuine difficulty and intellectual demands of ruling well is rare.

## Political Authority

### Ishmael/Nations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא בַּר מַחְסֵיָא אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: תַּחַת יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְלֹא תַּחַת נׇכְרִי. תַּחַת נׇכְרִי וְלֹא תַּחַת חַבָּר. תַּחַת חַבָּר וְלֹא תַּחַת תַּלְמִיד חָכָם. תַּחַת תַּלְמִיד חָכָם וְלֹא תַּחַת יָתוֹם וְאַלְמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava bar Meḥasseya said that Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said Rav said: It is preferable to be under the yoke of Ishmael and not under the yoke of a stranger,** the Romans; **under a stranger and not under a *Ḥabar,*** a Persian Zoroastrian fire priest; **under a *Ḥabar* and not under a Torah scholar,** as if one offends a Torah scholar who is greater than he, the scholar will be exacting with him and he will be punished at the hand of Heaven; **under a Torah scholar and not under an orphan or a widow,** as they are easily insulted and God promised to hear their cries and punish those who offend them.

The same tradition continues: *tachat Yishmael velo tachat nochri* — better to live under Yishmael than under other nations. Some textual variants read *Edomi* rather than *nochri*; if the reading is *Edom*, this refers to Rome, since by the time of the Gemara, *Edom* — identified with Esav — had become the standing designation for Rome, and later, as Rome became Christian, for Christianity. The censoring of *Edom* in medieval manuscripts becomes understandable in that context. It is worth noting, however, as a student correctly pointed out, that at the time of the Gemara's composition Islam had not yet arisen — Muhammad belongs to the seventh century, while the Gemara's redaction is conventionally placed in the fifth or sixth century. *Yishmael* in the Gemara therefore means Arabs, not Muslims, and the statement should be read accordingly.

According to the Steinsaltz commentary, the *chaveirim* referred to in the next clause were Persian Zoroastrian fire priests — a group known from several other passages in the Talmud to have been particularly oppressive toward Jews in the Babylonian period.

### Chaveirim/Scholars

Better under *Yishmael* than under other nations; *tachat nochri velo tachat chaveir* — better under other nations than under the *chaveirim*. And then the statement that is genuinely arresting: *tachat chaveir velo tachat talmid chacham* — better even under the *chaveirim* than under a Torah scholar. As several *gemarot* from earlier in the tractate make clear, a Torah sage who is offended or insulted can visit severe consequences upon the one who wronged him. Being in a position of dependence upon or subordination to such a figure carries real risk.

### Scholars/Vulnerable

And yet the chain continues further still: *tachat talmid chacham velo tachat yasom ve'almanah* — better even under a Torah scholar than under an orphan and a widow. This is a striking reversal. The *Torah* consistently places the orphan and widow among the most vulnerable members of society, the ones most in need of protection. Here the argument is that when positions are reversed and the formerly vulnerable are in a position of power or authority, the results can be most dangerous of all.

Rashi explains: *mipnei shedamatam metzuyah* — because their tears come readily, meaning that it is very easy to cause them harm, and therefore — whether because they have internalized their own suffering and react with heightened sensitivity, or because God responds swiftly to their cry — one under their authority is in a particularly precarious position. This raises important questions. The *Torah* explicitly invokes the experience of oppression in Egypt as the basis for the commandment to protect the vulnerable: you know what it was like to be oppressed. Yet the Gemara implicitly acknowledges that the logic does not always operate that way. When positions are reversed, those who have suffered may not always extend the compassion they were shown.

## Afflictions

### Sicknesses/Pains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא בַּר מַחְסֵיָא אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: כׇּל חוֹלִי, וְלֹא חוֹלִי מֵעַיִם. כׇּל כְּאֵב, וְלֹא כְּאֵב לֵב. כׇּל מֵיחוֹשׁ, וְלֹא מֵיחוֹשׁ רֹאשׁ. כׇּל רָעָה, וְלֹא אִשָּׁה רָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava bar Meḥasseya said that Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said Rav said: It is preferable to suffer from any extended illness and not from an intestinal illness. Similarly, it is preferable to suffer any pain,** even if it is sharp and excruciating, **and not heart pain; any slight ache and not a headache; any evil and not an evil wife.**

The same chain of transmission continues with a parallel series of comparative rankings, this time concerning physical suffering. *Kol choli velo choli me'ayim* — any illness is preferable to intestinal illness, which is the worst of all localized ailments. *Kol ke'ev velo ke'ev lev* — any pain is preferable to cardiac pain. *Kol michush velo michush rosh* — any discomfort or ache is preferable to a headache. The medical logic, as participants observed, is not without force: death from infectious diarrhea remains a leading cause of mortality globally even today; cardiac pain can indicate myocardial infarction; and a patient who presents describing the worst headache of their life must be presumed to have a hemorrhagic stroke until proven otherwise.

### Evil Wife

*Kol ra'ah velo ishah ra'ah* — any misfortune is preferable to a bad wife. The Gemara will return to this in the continuation: *zacha ezer lo zacha kenegdo* — if one merits it, she is a help; if not, she is against him.

## Governance…

### Kings' Hearts

The proof-text cited by Rav Mesharshiya for the incomprehensibility of royal governance — *shamayim larom ve'eretz la'omek velev melachim ein cheker*, the heavens above, the earth below, and the hearts of kings which no one can fully fathom — anchors the earlier statement about governance in scripture. Rashi elaborates: *omek libam shehu tzarich lihyot lev lechama medinot u'lemilchamot u'lechama mishpatim* — the depth of the royal heart is that it must hold simultaneously the affairs of many provinces, the conduct of multiple wars, and the adjudication of countless legal matters. The administrative complexity is simply beyond full articulation.

## Fasting Dreams

### Same Day Fast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא בַּר מַחְסֵיָא אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: יָפָה תַּעֲנִית לַחֲלוֹם כְּאֵשׁ לִנְעוֹרֶת. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וּבוֹ בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava bar Meḥasseya said that Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said Rav said: A fast is effective to neutralize a bad dream like fire burns chaff. Rav Ḥisda said: And a fast is effective specifically on that day** that he dreamed.

A further statement in the same tradition: *yafeh ta'anit lachalom ke'esh lino'eret* — a fast is beneficial for a bad dream just as fire is for flax. Fire consumes flax almost instantly; fasting, performed at once, likewise addresses the evil portent of the dream rapidly and effectively. Thematically, this echoes discussions that arose near the very end of Berachot, where the relationship between dreams and spiritual danger was treated at length.

Rav Chisda qualifies the statement: *u'vo bayom* — it must be on the very same day as the dream. The fast is effective precisely because it is immediate.

### Shabbat Fast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef said: One suffering from a bad dream that he dreamed is permitted to fast even on Shabbat.**

Rav Yosef goes further: *afilu beShabbos* — even on Shabbat. Although fasting on Shabbat is ordinarily prohibited, when one has experienced a bad dream, the fast is permitted even on Shabbat, such is the immediacy required.

### Borrowed Fasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידֵּי אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַב אָשֵׁי. עָבְדִי לֵיהּ עִיגְלָא תִּילְתָּא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לִטְעוֹם מָר מִידֵּי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: בְּתַעֲנִית יָתֵיבְנָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְלָא סָבַר לֵיהּ מָר לְהָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה? דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לֹוֶה אָדָם תַּעֲנִיתוֹ וּפוֹרֵעַ! אֲמַר לְהוּ: תַּעֲנִית חֲלוֹם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Yehoshua, son of Rav Idi, happened to come to the house of Rav Ashi. They prepared a third-born calf,** whose meat is high quality, **for him. They said to him: Let the Master taste something. He said to them: I am sitting in the midst of a fast. They said to him: And does the Master not hold in accordance with this *halakha* of Rav Yehuda, as Rav Yehuda said: A person can borrow his fast and not fast on the day that he originally designated, and repay it by fasting on another day? You can postpone your fast to another day. He said to them: It is a fast for a dream.**

Rav Yehoshua the son of Rav Idi came to the house of Rav Ashi, where they had prepared for him a choice calf — the most distinguished of dishes, explained by Rashi as either the third-born or the most mature and tender of calves — and invited him to eat. He declined, saying *beta'anisa yasivna* — I am in the middle of a fast. They pressed him: does the master not hold of the ruling of Rav Yehuda? For Rav Yehuda said: *loveh adam ta'aniso uforea* — a person may "borrow" against an obligatory fast, eat today, and repay the fast on another day. The notion that a fast can be borrowed and paid back on a different occasion raises interesting questions about the circumstances under which this applies — whether it is limited to a series of fasts undertaken by vow, where interchangeability may be more readily assumed, or whether it extends even to a single obligatory fast. Rav Yehoshua, however, replied: *ta'anis chalom hu* — this is a fast for a dream. The logic of borrowing does not apply here, because the entire efficacy of the fast depends on its being performed that very day. To defer it would be to forfeit its purpose entirely.

## Interrupting Activities

### Before Mincha

With the completion of this series of statements in the name of Rav, the Gemara returns to the *mishnayot* of tractate Shabbat. The *Mishnah* had established that certain major activities may not be commenced before *Mincha* — before one has davened. The *Rama* rules leniently, holding that the prohibition applies only to genuinely extended undertakings, such as a wedding feast or a lengthy session at the bathhouse, and even then only from the time of *Mincha ketana* — roughly three-thirty in the afternoon — onward. Shorter activities or minor matters remain permitted even according to the *Mishnah*'s concern, though this is admittedly difficult to read into any position in the *Gemara* straightforwardly. The *Rama* supports his leniency on the grounds that in our times there are adequate mechanisms — announcements, alarms, communal reminders — for ensuring one does not miss *davening*, and this consideration eases many of the concerns underlying these *halachot*.

### Interruption Rules

If one has already begun an activity before the time of *davening*, the *Mishnah* rules that one need not interrupt it for *tefilla*, since *tefilla* is *derabanan* and one who has begun may continue. One does, however, interrupt for *Krias Shema*, which is *de'oraisa*. The *Mishnah* appears to restate this at its conclusion — first saying one does not interrupt, then adding that one does interrupt for *Krias Shema* but not for *tefilla* — a repetition that invites explanation.

### Torah Scholars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הִתְחִילוּ אֵין מַפְסִיקִין, מַפְסִיקִין לִקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא אֵין מַפְסִיקִין? סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לְדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה. דְּתַנְיָא: חֲבֵרִים שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹסְקִין בַּתּוֹרָה — מַפְסִיקִין לִקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, וְאֵין מַפְסִיקִין לִתְפִלָּה. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי וַחֲבֵירָיו שֶׁתּוֹרָתָן אוּמָּנוּתָן. אֲבָל כְּגוֹן אָנוּ, מַפְסִיקִין לִקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְלִתְפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **if they already began any one of the activities mentioned in the mishna they need not stop to recite the *Amida* prayer; however, they stop to recite *Shema*.** The Gemara asks: **Didn't the first clause of the mishna already teach that they need not stop? Why does the mishna repeat it?** The Gemara answers: In **the latter clause of the mishna, we came to discuss matters of Torah.** With regard to those engaged in Torah study, they need not stop for prayer, but they are required to stop to recite *Shema*. **As it was taught in a *baraita*: Torah scholars, who were engaged in the study of Torah, stop their Torah study for *Shema*, and they do not stop for prayer. Rabbi Yoḥanan said a caveat to this statement: They only taught that they need not stop for prayer with regard to the likes of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai and his colleagues, whose Torah is their vocation** and they never interrupt their Torah study. **However, for the likes of us, who also engage in other activities, we stop both for *Shema* and for prayer.**

The Gemara resolves the apparent duplication: the second clause of the *Mishnah* is addressing an entirely different case. *Osek b'divrei Torah* — one who is engaged in Torah study — operates under a different *halacha* altogether. A *braita* teaches: *chaverim shehayu oskim baTorah mafsikin l'Krias Shema ve'ein mafsikin l'tefilla* — colleagues engaged in Torah learning must interrupt for *Krias Shema* but need not interrupt for *tefilla*. And this principle, as the subsequent discussion makes clear, applies even if interrupting for *tefilla* would mean missing it entirely for that day — a striking consequence.

Rabbi Yochanan limits this ruling: *lo shanu ela ke'gon Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai ve'chaverav* — it applies only to figures like Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai and his circle, *she'torasan umnasan*, for whom Torah is their sole occupation, who never take time for other pursuits. *Aval ke'gon anu* — but for the rest of us, who do interrupt learning for mundane matters, we also interrupt for *tefilla*. One cannot claim exemption from *davening* on the basis of Torah study if one's Torah study coexists with other activities.

### Learning/Shema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין מַפְסִיקִין לִתְפִלָּה, כָּךְ אֵין מַפְסִיקִין לִקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע! כִּי תָּנֵי הַהִיא — בְּעִיבּוּר שָׁנָה. דְּאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, וְכֵן תָּנוּ סָבֵי דְהַגְרוֹנְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר צָדוֹק: כְּשֶׁהָיִינוּ עוֹסְקִין בְּעִיבּוּר הַשָּׁנָה בְּיַבְנֶה לֹא הָיִינוּ מַפְסִיקִין לֹא לִקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְלֹא לִתְפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the essence of the statement the Gemara asks: **Didn't we learn in a different *baraita*: Just as they do not stop for prayer, they do not stop for *Shema*?** The Gemara answers: **When that *baraita* was taught,** it was taught with regard to those engaged **in the intercalation of the year.** Since their activity is crucial and all the Festivals of the year are determined through that activity, the Sages allowed them to continue and not stop to recite *Shema*. **As Rav Adda bar Ahava said, and the Elders of the city of Hagronya also taught that Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, said: When we were engaged in the intercalation of the year in Yavne, we would stop neither for *Shema* nor for prayer.**

The Gemara then raises a *braita* presenting an even more extreme position: *keshem she'ein mafsikim le'tefilla kach ein mafsikim le'Krias Shema* — just as one does not interrupt for *tefilla*, one does not interrupt even for *Krias Shema*. This appears to contradict the prior ruling. The resolution: *ki tanya hahi be'ibbur shana* — that *braita* speaks of the intercalation of the year. When the court was assembled to determine whether to add a month to the calendar — a matter of profound consequence for the entire Jewish people's religious calendar, requiring intensive and time-pressured investigation — nothing was permitted to interrupt. As Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok testified: *keshe'hayinu oskim be'ibbur shana be'Yavne lo hayinu mafsikim lo le'Krias Shema ve'lo le'tefilla* — when we were engaged in intercalating the year in Yavne, we interrupted for nothing.

### Learning vs. Doing

This discussion prompts a broader reflection on the underlying principles. The general rule is *osek bemitzvah patur min hamitzvah* — one engaged in performing a *mitzvah* is exempt from another *mitzvah*. The *chiddush* of this principle is that it operates even when the exemption will result in the second *mitzvah* being missed entirely — it is not merely a question of sequencing when both can eventually be fulfilled. As the Gemara in Berachot illustrated, the *chosson* who does not recite *Krias Shema* on his wedding night operates within this framework.

The question that naturally arises, then, is not the initial one — how can Torah study exempt one from *davening*? — but rather the reverse question posed by the *Rishonim*: if one is *osek bemitzvah* while learning, why must one interrupt even for *Krias Shema*? The *Rishonim* and *Acharonim* address this through a broader discussion about the relationship between *talmud* and *ma'aseh*, between learning and doing. A *mishnah* in Pirkei Avot states *lo hatalmud hi ha'ikar ella ha'ma'aseh*, though some *Rishonim* read this not as a claim that doing is more important than learning, but as a pedagogical statement: teaching through example and action is more effective than teaching through speech.

The question of why one interrupts learning for *Krias Shema* — a biblical *mitzvah* of doing — even though one is *osek bemitzvah*, leads some *Rishonim* to conclude that the very purpose of learning is ultimately to produce action, and therefore when an act of doing presents itself, the learning is interrupted. Others formulate it differently: learning need not be merely a means to an end, and may indeed be *talmud Torah keneged kulam*; but learning that entirely ignores opportunities for action becomes hollow. It is not that doing is superior to learning, but that learning which never translates into action undermines itself. This does not require us to say that learning is merely instrumental; it requires only that learning not be entirely sealed off from the world of doing.

A further consideration also emerges from other *gemarot*: *efshar la'asuto al yedei acherim* — if someone else can fulfill the obligation, one may remain engaged in one's *mitzvah* and let the other person fulfill it. But for personal obligations — like *Krias Shema* — no delegation is possible, and one must interrupt. This nuanced picture means that, contrary to the initial impression, Torah scholars are not being given an unlimited exemption from all religious obligation; they are held to interrupt for biblical personal duties, and the broader principle of *osek bemitzvah* is not a blanket pass for neglecting the world of *ma'aseh*.

## Shabbat Prohibitions

### Craftsman Tools

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יֵצֵא הַחַיָּיט בְּמַחֲטוֹ סָמוּךְ לַחֲשֵׁכָה, שֶׁמָּא יִשְׁכַּח וְיֵצֵא. וְלֹא הַלַּבְלָר בְּקוּלְמוֹסוֹ. וְלֹא יְפַלֶּה אֶת כֵּלָיו, וְלֹא יִקְרָא לְאוֹר הַנֵּר. בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ, הַחַזָּן רוֹאֶה הֵיכָן תִּינוֹקוֹת קוֹרְאִין, אֲבָל הוּא לֹא יִקְרָא. כַּיּוֹצֵא בוֹ: לֹא יֹאכַל הַזָּב עִם הַזָּבָה, מִפְּנֵי הֶרְגֵּל עֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna deals with various decrees, especially with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat, which were issued in order to distance a person from transgressions that he is liable to commit through habit and routine. The mishna said: **The tailor may not go out with his needle adjacent to nightfall on Shabbat eve, lest he forget that he is carrying the needle and go out with it to the public domain even after Shabbat begins. And, similarly, the scribe may not go out with his quill for the same reason. And one may not shake his clothes to rid them of lice; and one may not read a book by candlelight, so that he will not come to adjust the wick of the lamp. However, in truth they said an established *halakha*: The attendant sees where in the book the children under his supervision are reading in the Torah, even by candlelight on Shabbat. However, he himself may not read. Similarly, the Sages issued a similar decree with regard to other *halakhot*, as they said: The *zav* may not eat even with his wife the *zava*, despite the fact that they are both ritually impure, because, by eating together, they will come to excessive intimacy and become accustomed to sin.**

The daf begins with a series of statements transmitted by Rabba bar Mechasya in the name of Rav Chama bar Guria in the name of Rav. The first of these concerns where a person ought to choose to live: *le'olam yachzor adam veyeishev be'ir sheyeshivatah kerovah* — a person should always seek to dwell in a city that has been recently settled, *shemitokh sheyeshivatah kerovah avonoteha mu'atin*, for since it has been settled only recently, its sins are few.

The Gemara now returns to the *Mishnah*. The first *mishnah* of tractate Shabbat concerned the laws of *hotza'ah* — carrying between domains. The second concerned commencing activities on *Erev Shabbat* that might lead one to forget *Shabbat*. The present *Mishnah* combines both themes: activities forbidden on *Erev Shabbat* because they may result in inadvertent carrying.

*Lo yetzei hachayat bemachato samuch lachasheicha shema yishkach veyetzei* — a tailor must not go out carrying his needle close to dark on *Erev Shabbat*, lest he forget and carry it out into the public domain on *Shabbat*. Similarly, a scribe must not go out with his quill, for the same reason. These are not purely *Shabbat* prohibitions; they are protective *gezeirot* against inadvertent carrying.

### Delousing

*Velo yifleh es keilav* — one should not delouse one's garments on *Shabbat*, as the Gemara will explain, because one may come to kill a louse — an act that falls within the category of forbidden *melacha* — or for related concerns.

### Candlelight Reading

*Velo yikra le'or haner* — one should not read by candlelight on *Shabbat*, lest one become so engrossed in reading that one tilts the candle to see better, which would constitute a forbidden act. An exception exists: *hachazzan ro'eh heichan tinokos korin* — the teacher may use the candlelight to find the place in the text where the children were reading, *aval hu lo yikra* — but he may not himself read by it. Finding one's place is a momentary act that does not risk the same level of engrossment; reading does.

### Zavim Separated

The *Mishnah* extends its principle of *gezeira* beyond *Shabbat* altogether: *keyotzei bo lo yochal hazav im hazava mipnei hergel aveira* — similarly, a *zav* — a man who has experienced an unusual penile discharge and is therefore *tamei* — should not eat together with a *zava* — a woman who bleeds outside her menstrual cycle — lest it lead to sin. The practical context is a setting where those who are *tamei* would be separated from those who are *tahor* during meals. If the only two individuals consigned to the *tamei* room are a man and a woman who are not married to one another, this creates an impermissible situation of *yichud*. As one participant raised, Rashi notes that the severity here connects to *kareis*: even if the couple were married, relations with a *zava* are forbidden under penalty of *kareis*, analogous to the prohibition against relations with a *nidda*.

The question of why specifically this example appears at the end of the *Mishnah* — rather than any of countless other possible illustrations of *gezeira* — is genuinely puzzling. One suggestion raised in the discussion is precisely the severity of the consequence: these are not merely rabbinic prohibitions but matters potentially carrying *kareis*, placing them in a category alongside the *chilul Shabbat* examples that precede them.

This discussion naturally raises the broader question of how prevalent *gezeirot* of this type — "don't do X because it will lead to Y" — actually were in early rabbinic thought. There is a position, drawn from comparison of the Bavli and Yerushalmi, that the Bavli is far more inclined to frame prohibitions as *gezeirot*, whereas the Yerushalmi often either does not have the same *gezeirot* or frames the same prohibitions differently, as extensions of the underlying law rather than as protective safeguards. These may have been some of the earliest identified cases of clearly dangerous activity — matters that were explicitly flagged as "do not do this because it leads somewhere else" — before the full proliferation of *gezeirot* as a category.

## Domain Transfer

### Leaning Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנַן הָתָם: לֹא יַעֲמוֹד אָדָם בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וְיִשְׁתֶּה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְיִשְׁתֶּה בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד. אֲבָל אִם הִכְנִיס רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ לִמְקוֹם שֶׁהוּא שׁוֹתֶה — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Among the *halakhot* concerning decrees that were issued lest one come to commit a transgression, **we learned in a mishna there: A person may not stand in the private domain and drink water located in the public domain,** or vice versa, stand **in the public domain and drink water located in the private domain,** lest he transfer the vessel from which he is drinking the water to the place where he is standing and become liable to bring a sin-offering. **However, if he introduced his head and most of his body into the place where the water that he is drinking is located, there is no longer room for concern, and it is permitted.**

The Gemara now cites a *Mishnah* from tractate Eruvin: *lo yaamod adam bi'reshus hayachid ve'yishte bi'reshus harabim* — a person may not stand in a *reshus hayachid* and drink from a vessel located in the *reshus harabim*, and vice versa. Tosafot raises a subtle question: why is this prohibited at all? If the person picks up the cup in the *reshus harabim* and drinks while standing in the *reshus harabim*, with only the mouth and the swallowing occurring in that domain, has anything been carried? Tosafot demonstrates from multiple passages that once liquid is swallowed, the Gemara ceases to track its location — it comes to rest at the moment of swallowing, while the person is still in the public domain. So technically no carrying has occurred.

Nevertheless, the *Mishnah* prohibits it, because one might end up pulling the cup back to oneself in the other domain, completing an act of carrying. An exception applies: *aval im hichnis rosho ve'rubo li-makom she'hu shote* — if not only the head but most of the body has entered the other domain, it is permitted, because then one is effectively in the domain from which one is drinking, and the concern about inadvertently carrying back disappears.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בַּגַּת.
>
> **Translation:** **and the same is true in the wine press.**

The *Mishnah* also mentions *ve-chen be-gas* — and similarly regarding a wine vat or pressing vat — the significance of which becomes a subject of debate.

### Gezeirah Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כַּרְמְלִית מַאי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הִיא הִיא. רָבָא אָמַר: הִיא גוּפָהּ גְּזֵירָה, וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּם וְנִגְזוֹר גְּזֵירָה לִגְזֵירָה?
>
> **Translation:** In light of the *halakha* that was taught in this mishna **a dilemma was raised before the Sages: What is the legal status of a *karmelit* in this matter? Is it permissible to stand in one domain and drink from a *karmelit* or not? Abaye said: That case is equal to that case, i.e., the same way that the Sages prohibited drinking from the private domain to the public domain and vice versa, so too, they prohibited drinking from the *karmelit* to another domain. Rava said:** It is not prohibited. **It, the prohibition to carry between a *karmelit* and another domain, itself is merely a rabbinic decree. And will we arise and issue one decree to prevent violation of another decree?** Although the Sages prohibited doing so in one of the domains by Torah law, i.e., the public and the private domains, a similar decree was not issued in a *karmelit,* which is a domain by rabbinic law.

The Gemara raises a question: *karmelis mai* — what is the *halacha* when the domain in question is a *karmelis*, an intermediate domain that is neither a full *reshus hayachid* nor a full *reshus harabim*? The significance of the question is that carrying between a *karmelis* and another domain is itself only a rabbinic prohibition. Would we extend the prohibition on leaning-and-drinking to a *karmelis* as well — thereby making a *gezeira* on something that is itself only *derabanan*?

Abaye rules: *hi hi* — the same prohibition applies; we do make the *gezeira* even here. Rava disagrees: *hi gufa gezeira* — the *karmelis* itself is a *gezeira*; *ve'ana meikum ve'nigzar gezeirah l'gezeirah?* — are we going to add a *gezeira* on top of a *gezeira*?

The general principle is that we do not make a *gezeira l'gezeira*, and there are two ways to understand why. One understanding is that since the underlying act is only rabbinic, it is not serious enough to warrant an additional safeguard — this would argue against any *gezeira* on any *derabanan* prohibition. The other understanding is that if something is already functioning as a protective barrier for the biblical prohibition, adding yet another layer of protection around the protective barrier is excessive — one fence should be sufficient. Both framings capture something real, and the tension between them explains why the principle is not absolute: there are areas of *halacha* where we clearly do make layered *gezeirot*, as with the complex rules surrounding *basar b'chalav*.

Tosafot is quick to note that Abaye does not reject the general principle of *gezeira l'gezeira* — he agrees with it in other contexts. His position is that for the specific case of carrying between domains, we do apply a double *gezeira*. This makes the dispute between Abaye and Rava a narrow one about whether *hotza'ah* is exceptional in this regard.

### Wine Vat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ — דְּקָתָנֵי: ״וְכֵן בַּגַּת״. מַאי גַּת? אִי רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — תְּנֵינָא! אִי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — תְּנֵינָא. אֶלָּא לָאו כַּרְמְלִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: From where do I say that *halakha*, i.e., that the decree applies to a *karmelit*? From that which we learned at the end of the mishna in tractate *Eiruvin*: And the same is true in the wine press. The question arises: What is the status of the wine press in terms of the domains of Shabbat? If you say that it is the private domain, we already learned that in the mishna. If it is the public domain, we already learned that as well. Rather, isn't this press a *karmelit*?** Apparently, a *karmelit* was also prohibited in the mishna.

Abaye seeks proof that we make a *gezeira* even where the underlying act would only be rabbinic, from the clause *ve-chen be-gas* in the *Mishnah*. He argues: what type of vat is being discussed? If the vat has walls ten *tefachim* high and is four by four *tefachim* in area, it is a *reshus hayachid*, and leaning over to drink from it was already covered by the earlier ruling. If the vat is configured such that it has the status of a *reshus harabim*, again that is already covered. The fact that the *Mishnah* mentions the vat separately must therefore mean it has the status of a *karmelis*, and yet the same prohibition is extended to it — demonstrating that we do make a *gezeira* even on something that is itself *derabanan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: ״וְכֵן בַּגַּת״, לְעִנְיַן מַעֲשֵׂר. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: ״וְכֵן בַּגַּת״ — לְעִנְיַן מַעֲשֵׂר. דִּתְנַן: שׁוֹתִין עַל הַגַּת, בֵּין עַל הַחַמִּין, בֵּין עַל הַצּוֹנֵן, וּפָטוּר. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק מְחַיֵּיב. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַל הַחַמִּין, חַיָּיב. עַל הַצּוֹנֵן — פָּטוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַחֲזִיר אֶת הַמּוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** That which we learned in the mishna: **And the same is true in the wine press, is not relevant to the *halakhot* of Shabbat. It refers to the matter of the *halakhot* of tithes. And Rav Sheshet also said:** That which we learned in the mishna: **And the same is true in the wine press, refers to the matter of tithes, as we learned in a mishna: One may *ab initio* drink grape juice directly on the press without tithing, whether the juice was diluted with hot water,** even though he will then be unable to return the leftover wine to the press, as it would ruin all the wine in the press, **or whether the juice was diluted with cold water, in which case he could return the leftover wine without ruining the rest, and he is exempt.** Drinking that way is considered incidental drinking, and anything that is not a fixed meal is exempt from tithing. That is the **statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, obligates one to separate the tithe in both cases. And the Rabbis say:** There is a distinction between these two cases; when the wine was diluted **with hot water, since he cannot return what is left of the wine to the press, he is obligated to tithe, as it is like fixed drinking for which one is obligated to tithe. However, when the wine was diluted with cold water, he is exempt, because he returns the leftover wine to the press,** and it is incidental drinking, which is exempt from tithing. Our mishna, which says: And the same is true in the press, means that only if his head and most of his body was in the press is he permitted to drink without separating the tithe, and that *halakha* is not at all related to matters of Shabbat.

Rava rejects this proof: *ve-chen be-gas l'inyan ma'aser* — the mention of the wine vat has nothing to do with the laws of domain transfer at all; it refers rather to a parallel *gezeira* in the laws of *trumos u'ma'asros*. Rav Sheshes concurs with Rava.

The relevant *halacha* of *ma'asros* is as follows. Wine becomes subject to *ma'asrot* — obligatory tithing — once the *gmar melacha* is complete, when the wine has run from the pressing vat into the collection vat. While the wine is still in the pressing vat and the process is not yet finished, one may drink from it on a *shtiyas arai* basis — a temporary, incidental drink, merely a taste. The question is what constitutes *shtiyas arai* versus *shtiyas keva*, a settled or permanent drink. The *Mishnah* in *ma'asros* records a dispute: Rabbi Meir holds that drinking over the vat always qualifies as *shtiyas arai*, whether one has mixed the wine with hot water or cold water. Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok holds that any mixing renders it a *shtiyas keva*. The Sages distinguish: mixing with hot water — since one cannot pour it back into the vat, it would contaminate the rest — renders the drinking permanent, while mixing with cold water, since one can pour the remainder back, remains *shtiyas arai*.

On this reading, *ve-chen be-gas* means: just as there is a concern about leaning over domains and drinking, so too with the wine vat, there is a comparable concern — that once one removes the wine from the vat itself, even temporarily, it transitions from *shtiyas arai* to something more permanent, with *ma'aser* implications.

### Craftspeople Tools

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: לֹא יֵצֵא הַחַיָּיט בְּמַחֲטוֹ סָמוּךְ לַחֲשֵׁיכָה שֶׁמָּא יִשְׁכַּח וְיֵצֵא. מַאי לָאו דִּתְחוּבָה לוֹ בְּבִגְדוֹ? לָא, דְּנָקֵיט לֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** As proof for Abaye's opinion, the Gemara states that which **we learned in our mishna: The tailor may not go out with his needle adjacent to nightfall on Shabbat eve, lest he forget that he is carrying the needle and go out with it to the public domain even after Shabbat begins. Is it not speaking here in a case where the needle was stuck in his clothing?** In that case, even if he was to go out into the public domain with the needle, he would not be liable by Torah law, since that is not the typical manner of carrying out; carrying out an object in that manner is prohibited only by rabbinic decree [*shevut*]. Nevertheless, not only did the Rabbis issue a decree to prohibit going out with the needle on Shabbat, they issued a decree to prevent violation of another decree and prohibited the tailor from going out with his needle adjacent to nightfall. Apparently, the Sages institute a decree to prevent violation of another decree with regard to the *halakhot* of carrying out on Shabbat (*Tosafot*). Consequently, with regard to the *halakhot* of *karmelit*, the Sages issued a decree as well, and this is proof for Abaye's opinion. The Gemara rejects this: **No, the mishna is referring to a case where he is holding the needle in his hand,** which constitutes performance of the full-fledged prohibited labor of carrying out.

The *Mishnah* is cited again: a tailor must not go out with his needle on *Erev Shabbat*. The Gemara explores what form of carrying this describes. Does the *Mishnah* mean the tailor is holding the needle in his hand — the normal way anyone would carry a needle — or that it is pinned (*techuna*) into his garment?

If pinned, the carrying is unusual (*she'lo k'darkah*) for the general population, and therefore only a rabbinic violation. The question then becomes whether the *Mishnah* is prohibiting a *gezeira* on a *derabanan* act of carrying. A *braita* makes explicit that the prohibition refers to the needle being *techuna*, pinned into the garment. But the Gemara deflects: perhaps this prohibition is just for *Shabbat* itself, not necessarily *Erev Shabbat* — which is obvious in any case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לֹא יֵצֵא הַחַיָּיט בְּמַחֲטוֹ הַתְּחוּבָה לוֹ בְּבִגְדוֹ. מַאי לָאו בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת? לָא, כִּי תַּנְיָא הַהִיא בְּשַׁבָּת. וְהָתַנְיָא: לֹא יֵצֵא הַחַיָּיט בְּמַחֲטוֹ הַתְּחוּבָה בְּבִגְדוֹ בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה. הָא מַנִּי? — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר אוּמָּן דֶּרֶךְ אוּמָּנֻתוֹ, חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Come and hear another proof from that which was taught explicitly in the *baraita*: The tailor may not go out with his needle stuck in his clothing. Is it not speaking of a case where he goes out on Shabbat eve,** and the Sages issued a decree to prevent violation of another decree, just as Abaye said? The Gemara rejects this: **No, when that was taught in the *baraita*, it was only with regard to carrying out on Shabbat itself.** The Gemara asks further: **Wasn't it taught explicitly in a *baraita*: The tailor may not go out with his needle stuck in his clothing on Shabbat eve at nightfall,** and the Sages issued a decree to prevent violation of another decree, just as Abaye said? The Gemara rejects this: **Whose opinion is cited in this *baraita*? It is the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who said: A craftsman who carries out an object in the manner common to his craft,** even if others do not generally carry it out in that manner, the craftsman **is liable,** because he carried the object out in a manner standard for him.

A further *braita* is cited, stating explicitly: *lo yetzei hachayat bemachato tchuva lo bivigdo be'erev Shabbat im chasheicha* — the tailor may not go out with the needle pinned into his garment on *Erev Shabbat* as dark falls. The Gemara responds: *ha manni Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this *braita* represents the position of Rabbi Yehuda, *de'amar uman derech umanuto chayav* — who holds that a craftsperson who carries in the distinctive manner associated with his craft is biblically liable, because for the craftsperson this *is* the normal mode of carrying. A tailor who wears a needle pinned in his garment is doing what tailors do; it functions as a professional marker. For Rabbi Yehuda, therefore, the carrying is biblical, and no *gezeira l'gezeira* is at issue.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא יֵצֵא הַחַיָּיט בְּמַחֲטוֹ הַתְּחוּבָה לוֹ בְּבִגְדוֹ, וְלֹא נַגָּר בְּקֵיסָם שֶׁבְּאׇזְנוֹ, וְלֹא סוֹרֵק בִּמְשִׁיחָה שֶׁבְּאׇזְנוֹ וְלֹא גַּרְדִּי בְּאִירָא שֶׁבְּאׇזְנוֹ, וְלֹא צַבָּע בְּדוּגְמָא שֶׁבְּצַוָּארוֹ, וְלֹא שׁוּלְחָנִי בְּדִינָר שֶׁבְּאׇזְנוֹ. וְאִם יָצָא — פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אוּמָּן דֶּרֶךְ אוּמָּנֻתוֹ — חַיָּיב, וּשְׁאָר כׇּל אָדָם — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught in a *baraita*: The tailor may not go out with his needle that is stuck in his clothing, and a carpenter may not go out with the wood chip that is behind his ear** for use as a measuring stick, **and a comber of wool may not go out with a cord** with which he ties bundles of wool and which is usually placed that is **on his ear, and a weaver may not go out with a bit of wool that is on his ear** which he uses for the purpose of his work, **and the painter may not go out with the sample of dyed wool that is on his neck, and a money changer may not go out with the dinar that is in his ear.** In all of these cases the *halakha* is that **if he went out, he is exempt by Torah law, but it is prohibited for him to do so by rabbinic decree. This is the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: A craftsman who carries out an object in the manner common to his craft on Shabbat is liable by Torah law; any other person who carries it out in that manner is exempt, but it is prohibited for him to do so.**

The *braita* that follows spells out this principle across multiple trades: the tailor with a needle in his garment, a carpenter with a wood chip in his ear, a wool-comber with a thread in his ear, a weaver with fibers in his ear, a dyer with colored swatches around his neck, a moneychanger with a coin in his ear. Rabbi Meir holds that in all these cases, *patur aval asur* — one is exempt from biblical liability but the act is rabbinically forbidden, since it is not the manner in which the general population carries. Rabbi Yehuda holds that for the craftsperson, *uman derech umanuto chayav* — this manner of carrying is biblically prohibited because it is the craftsperson's own normal mode.

### Zav Pad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: לֹא יֵצֵא הַזָּב בְּכִיסוֹ, וְאִם יָצָא — פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: לֹא יֵצֵא, וְאִם יָצָא — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** Since the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda with regard to the legal status of one who carries out an object in an atypical manner was mentioned, the Gemara discusses a contradiction between two related *baraitot*. **It was taught in one *baraita*: The *zav* may not go out on Shabbat with his pouch** that he ties to his organ in order to absorb his emission. **And if he went out, he is exempt by Torah law but it is prohibited for him to do so by rabbinic law. And it was taught in another *baraita*: The *zav* may not go out on Shabbat with his pouch. And if he went out unwittingly, he is liable to bring a sin-offering.**

A final set of *beraitot* addresses a *zav* who goes out wearing a pad to absorb his discharge. One *braita* states: *lo yetzei hazav bekiso ve'im yatza patur* — the *zav* may not go out with the pad, but if he did, he is only rabbinically liable. A second *braita* states: *ve'im yatza chayav chatat* — if he went out, he is biblically liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This is **not difficult.** There is no contradiction between the *baraitot*, as **this *baraita*, which deems him exempt, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir; that**, the other *baraita*, which deems him liable, **is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.**

Rav Yosef resolves the contradiction in the manner now familiar: *lo kashya ha Rabbi Meir veha Rabbi Yehuda* — one *braita* reflects Rabbi Meir's position, the other Rabbi Yehuda's. Wearing a pad is what a *zav* normally does; for Rabbi Yehuda this constitutes normal carrying and is therefore biblical, while for Rabbi Meir the comparison class is the general population, for whom this is not a normal mode of carrying, making it only rabbinic.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמִידֵּי דְּלָאו הַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ, בְּמִידֵּי דְּהַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁחָקַק קַב בִּבְקַעַת בְּשַׁבָּת לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא מְחַיַּיב?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Say that you heard that Rabbi Meir deems him exempt with regard to an object that is not carried out in its typical manner. However, with regard to a matter that is carried out in its typical manner, did you hear that he deems him exempt?** In general, one carries out a needle in his hand. Rabbi Meir exempts one who carries it out in his clothing, even if he is a craftsman. However, this pouch of a *zav*, even though it is not held in his hand, is always carried out in that manner, and, even according to Rabbi Meir, that constitutes a bona fide act of carrying out. **As, if you do not say so,** that the specifics of various prohibited labors can be performed in different manners, in the case of **a layman, who carved out a vessel the size of a ***kav*** in a piece of wood on Shabbat, would you say that Rabbi Meir also does not deem him liable** for performing a prohibited labor on Shabbat because he is not a craftsman and he did not craft the vessel according to the standards of a craftsman? Certainly, the layman performed a full-fledged labor to the best of his ability and he is liable.

Abaye challenges this resolution. He asks: when did Rabbi Meir say that a person is *patur* for carrying in an unusual manner? In cases where the mode of carrying is genuinely unusual — *bemidi delav hainu orchaya*. But here, any person who has a flow and needs to manage it would carry a pad in precisely this way. It is not an unusual mode; it is the ordinary and necessary mode for anyone in this condition. It should therefore be considered normal carrying and be biblical even according to Rabbi Meir.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּזָב בַּעַל שְׁתֵּי רְאִיּוֹת, כָּאן בְּזָב בַּעַל שָׁלֹשׁ רְאִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Hamnuna said: This is not difficult,** as the two *baraitot* are referring to two different cases. **Here,** in the *baraita* that deemed him liable by Torah law, it is referring **to a *zav* who experienced two sightings** of an emission. Liability to bring an offering as part of the purification process is only after he sees three emissions. Therefore, the *zav* requires the pouch in order to ascertain whether or not he experienced a third emission. However, **there,** in the *baraita* that deems him exempt, it is referring **to a *zav* who already experienced three sightings.** For him there is no significance whether or not he experiences an additional emission. Therefore, the pouch is insignificant and he has no interest in carrying it out.

Rav Hamnuna offers an alternative resolution: *kan bezav ba'al shtei reiyot, kan bezav ba'al gimel reiyot* — the two *beraitot* discuss different cases. One speaks of a *zav* who has had two flows; the other of a *zav* who has had three. The *zav* with two flows wears the pad to inspect it — to determine whether he has had a third flow, which would fully establish his *zav* status and obligate him to bring a *korban*. Since he is wearing the pad for a specific, purposeful reason, his carrying is intentional and purposeful — and therefore biblical. The *zav* with three flows, one might think, has no such diagnostic purpose and therefore no purposeful carrying. But the Gemara objects: *zav ba'al gimel nami miba'ei lei lisfira* — even someone who has already had three flows needs the seven clean days confirmed by inspecting the pad. So the purpose remains. The Gemara responds: *lo nitzrecha ela bo bayom* — the distinction is relevant only to the day on which he experiences his third flow itself. On that very day, an additional flow has no further *halachic* consequence — his status is already established, and another flow does not change his obligations for that day. Therefore on that day specifically, if he wears the pad, the carrying lacks the same purposeful intent, and that may render it only rabbinic.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא זָב בַּעַל שְׁתֵּי רְאִיּוֹת דְּחַיָּיב, דְּמִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִבְדִיקָה, זָב בַּעַל שָׁלֹשׁ נָמֵי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִסְפִירָה? לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְבוֹ בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different about a *zav* who had two sightings, who is liable, as he requires the pouch for the purpose of examination** to ascertain whether or not he experienced a third sighting, and **a *zav* who already experienced three sightings and requires the pouch for the purpose of counting** clean days? In order to become ritually pure, he must count seven clean days without experiencing an emission. If so, even a *zav* who had three sightings requires the pouch, in order to ascertain whether or not he experienced another emission. The Gemara answers: That *baraita* **was only needed for that day when he already saw his third emission. In any case, that day will not be a clean day.**

This question of the *zav*'s pad is not merely theoretical. Rav Moshe Feinstein addresses a direct *shaila* arising from it: whether a woman in a place without an *eruv* may go out on *Shabbat* wearing a sanitary pad. The case is structurally identical to the *Gemara*'s discussion, and the continuation tomorrow will resolve whether the *zav*'s carrying is biblical or rabbinic — a resolution with direct practical implications.
