# Menachot 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kli Metzaref

### Rav Kahana's Teaching

The daf opens at the end of 23b, picking up from a lengthy discussion concerning the mixing of *menachot* and questions of *bittul* and *min b'mino* according to Rabbi Yehuda. We now turn to a distinct topic introduced by the phrase *ki salik Rav Kahana* — when Rav Kahana went up to Eretz Yisrael, he found the sons of Rabbi Chiya (*bnei Rabbi Chiya*) sitting and discussing the following *halacha*.

### Two Piles Combining

The case: an *isaron* — the flour designated for a *mincha* — has been divided into two separate piles, not touching one another, and placed together in a single vessel. A *tvul yom* then touches one of the piles. The *tvul yom* is someone who has immersed in a *mikvah* that day and will be fully *tahor* that evening, but for now retains the status of a *sheni l'tumah*, sufficient to render *terumah* or *kodesh* items *tamei*. This serves as an example of someone with a minimal level of *tumah* who can still effect contamination of sacred items. The question — *mahu?* — is whether his touching one pile renders the other pile *tamei* as well.

The basis for thinking it might is a well-known *mishna* in Chagiga: *kli metzaref mah sheb'tocho l'kodesh* — a vessel combines whatever is inside it when dealing with *kodesh* items for the purposes of *tumah v'tahara*. But perhaps that principle only applies when the items inside the vessel are actually touching one another. Maybe when the two piles are entirely separate, the *kli* cannot bridge the gap between them. Or perhaps, as the other possibility runs, it makes no difference — even completely separate piles within the same vessel are considered one unit. One might further ask: if they are touching one another, why would the *kli* need to do any combining at all? Even if each pile retains its distinct shape — perhaps because oil has already been mixed into each — two piles merely touching are not yet a single unit, and without the *kli*, a *tvul yom* touching pile A would need pile A to independently transfer *tumah* to pile B, which it might not be able to do in the ordinary workings of *hilchot tumah*.

### Metzaref vs Mechaber

Rav Kahana responded to the *bnei Rabbi Chiya* with a careful linguistic observation: *mi tnan kli mechaber?* — the *mishna* does not say the vessel *joins* the contents. It says *kli metzaref* — it *combines* them. The word *mechaber* would imply the vessel fuses them into a single physical unit, which would presuppose actual contact. *Metzaref*, by contrast, denotes a less tight form of connection — the items remain distinct, yet the vessel draws them together. This language itself signals that even in the absence of direct contact, the vessel connects all its contents. Therefore, even across an air space, if one pile is touched it renders the other *tamei*.

### Extraneous Flour

The discussion continues: *hoshit echad beineihen mahu?* — what if one places an additional half-*isaron* between the two proper halves? This third pile does not belong to the *mincha*; it is extraneous. Rav Kahana replied: *tzarich l'kli kli metzarfo, ein tzarich l'kli ein kli metzarfo* — something that requires the *kli shares* to constitute a *mincha* is combined by the vessel; something that does not require it, that has no proper claim on the vessel, is not combined by it. The extraneous flour is therefore not drawn into the *tziruf* and does not participate in the connection.

### Air Space Transfer

A further question: *hoshit tvul yom et etzba'o beineihem mahu?* — what if the *tvul yom* did not touch either pile directly but inserted his finger into the air space between pile A and pile B? If the two piles are considered a single combined unit, perhaps the air space between them is also suffused with the status of *mincha*, and touching that air space should be enough to transfer *tumah* to everything. Rav Kahana rejected this: *ein lecha davar shemitamei me'avir ela kli cheres bilvad* — nothing transmits *tumah* through air space except a *kli cheres*. The principle of *kli metzaref* combines the discrete physical items that need the vessel and are appropriate to it; it does not convert the air inside the vessel into an extension of those items. *Tumah* still travels by direct contact. The *kli* simply treats all such items as one, so that touching any one of them affects all — but only if an item is actually touched.

### Kli Cheres Exception

This ruling clarifies the scope of *kli metzaref*: it is a principle of combination for *kodesh* items that belong within the vessel, not a principle that fills the vessel's interior with a continuous field of *tumah*. The transmission of *tumah* through air — *tumah me'avir* — is a separate and exceptional concept, confined to *kli cheres* by explicit *halacha*. The two principles must not be conflated.

## Tziruf Kli & Kometz

### De'Oraita Question

Having taught the *bnei Rabbi Chiya*, Rav Kahana now turned the tables and posed his own question to them: *mahu likmotz mizeh al zeh, tziruf de'oraita o derabbanan?* Can one take a *kemitza* from one of the two separate piles in the *kli shares* and have it count as a valid *kemitza* for the entire *mincha*? The deeper question is whether *tziruf kli* — the combining effected by the vessel — operates at the *de'oraita* level or only *derabbanan*. The potential *de'oraita* basis would be the verse *kaf achat asara zahav*, from which one might derive that everything in a *kaf* is considered a single unit. If *tziruf kli* is *de'oraita*, then the two piles genuinely constitute one *mincha* and a *kemitza* taken from one serves the whole. If it is only *derabbanan*, the combining may be no more than a stringency for *tumah*, and *de'oraita* the piles remain distinct — so that even in the same vessel they would not constitute a valid *mincha* and the *kemitza* would not be properly taken.

### Two Menachot Proof

The *bnei Rabbi Chiya* acknowledged they had no direct answer — *zu lo shamanu* — but they had heard something in a related context from which they believed a proof could be drawn. The *mishna* teaches: *shtei menachot shelo nikmetzu venitarvu* — two *menachot* that have not yet had the *kemitza* taken from them became mixed together. In such a case, if one can take the *kemitza* from the A-section alone and from the B-section alone — meaning each *mincha* still has a portion that remains unmixed with the other — both are valid, because a pure A *kemitza* and a pure B *kemitza* were each taken. But if the mixing is so thorough that the overlap area is required for either *kemitza*, both are invalid. The Gemara reads this *mishna* as implying that the A-section, the AB-overlap, and the B-section are three separate piles, not touching. Yet the *kemitza* from A can combine with the flour in the mixed AB pile, which only makes sense if they are all in the same *kli shares* and *tziruf kli* is working to make them one — even for purposes of validating the *kemitza*. This would suggest *tziruf kli* operates at the *de'oraita* level.

### Comb Analogy

Rava challenged this proof: *dilma b'gushim hamachulakim asuyim k'masreik* — perhaps the *mishna* is not describing three separate non-touching piles at all. Rather, the configuration resembles a comb (*masreik*): the base of the comb is the mixed AB section, and the separate teeth of the comb are the pure A portion and the pure B portion. Everything is in fact touching; there are simply sub-regions that are purely A and purely B emerging from a shared base that contains both. On this reading the *mishna* requires no appeal to *tziruf kli* bridging a gap, and the question of whether *tziruf kli* is *de'oraita* remains unanswered.

### Mimenu Braita

Rava himself then offered a different source: *ta shma, detanya, v'herim mimenu — mimenu min hamachubar*. The *pasuk* governing the *kometz* requires that the taking of the handful be from a single connected unit. The *braita* derives from *mimenu* that one may not divide the *isaron* into two separate vessels and take the *kemitza* from one of them. The implicit inference is: *ha b'kli echad dumya d'shnei kelim* — but if it were in one vessel, even if it resembled two vessels in being two separate piles, it sounds as though the *kometz* would be valid. This seems to be a solid proof that *tziruf kli* works at the *de'oraita* level to make a *kemitza* valid.

### Compartmented Vessels

Abaye qualified the proof: *dilma shnei kelim heichi dami k'gon kviza b'kava* — perhaps what the *braita* is ruling out when it disqualifies "two vessels" is a specific configuration, namely a measuring vessel (*kfiza*) carved within a larger vessel (*kava*), where the dividing wall between the compartments runs the full height of the vessel. In that case, even if the flour in the two compartments happens to touch above the rim, the division below makes it as though there are two separate vessels — and that is what the *braita* rejects. The scenario the *braita* considers valid, on this reading, is one in which the dividing wall ends before the top of the vessel — like a trough for chickens (*arivata shel tarnegolim*), as Rashi explains — so that the contents of the two compartments touch one another within the walls of the vessel itself. That is what the *braita* means by "one vessel similar to two." But fully separate, non-touching piles without even that upper connection are not addressed by the *braita* at all. The question — *heicha dela nagei klal tiba'ei lach* — therefore remains open.

## External Connections

### Water Connection

Rami bar Yirmiyah posed a sequence of thought experiments pushing the logic of *tziruf kli* further. The first: *tziruf kli v'chibur mayim mahu* — what happens when the principle of *kli metzaref* intersects with a connection by water? As Rashi explains the scenario: pile A and pile B are inside the vessel, and pile C is outside the vessel, connected to pile B through a flowing liquid — perhaps the oil from B is running outward and making contact with C. Normally a connection through liquid between two separate food items might be sufficient to treat them as joined. The question is whether, when A touches B via *tziruf kli* inside the vessel, the *tumah* then continues outward to C via the liquid connection. Do we say *kli metzaref mah sheb'tocho l'kodesh* applies only to items on the inside of the vessel — *hanei milei d'gavai, aval d'barai lo* — or do we say that since there is a connection, the connection simply continues — *keivan d'mchaber, mchaber* — and C is pulled along as well? It should be noted that Tosafot struggles with this Gemara, since it is not at all obvious why a liquid connection between B and C would ordinarily combine them for purposes of *tumah*; the Gemara seems to take it as a given that liquid does connect them and confines the question to whether *tziruf kli* extends across that connection.

### Extending Tumah

The next step is the reverse direction: *chibur mayim v'tziruf kli, v'naga tvul yom mi'bachutz mahu* — what if a *tvul yom* touched C, which is outside the vessel? Because C and B are connected by liquid, B becomes *tamei*. Now, since B and A are inside the same vessel, would *tziruf kli* transfer the *tumah* from B to A? The argument against it: when does the principle of *kli metzaref* operate? Perhaps only when the *tumah* originates within the vessel — *ki tnan kli metzaref hanei milei d'naga mi'gavai* — so that if one touches A inside the vessel, the *tumah* jumps to B, and vice versa. But if the original touch occurred outside the vessel on C, and C contaminated B, the initial *tumah* event occurred outside the vessel. Perhaps in that case *tziruf kli* does not carry it further to A. Or perhaps it makes no difference — *o dilma lo shna*.

### Tumah Origin (Teiku)

The Gemara leaves both questions unresolved — *teiku*. The underlying difficulty, as noted, is the very premise that liquid connection ordinarily would combine B and C; but leaving that aside, whether *tziruf kli* can extend *tumah* outward to an externally connected item, and whether the *origin* of the *tumah* — inside or outside the vessel — affects the principle's operation, remain open questions.

## Sava Lei Tumah

### Tvul Yom on Zav

Rava raised a new question: *isaron shechilko, v'nitma echad mehen, v'hiniach b'visa, v'chazar tvul yom v'naga b'oso tamei mahu*. You have an *isaron* divided into two piles, A and B, not yet in a vessel. Pile A is touched by a *zav* and becomes a *rishon l'tumah*. Then both A and B are placed together in the same vessel. Since A was already *tamei* before being placed in the vessel, it does not transfer its existing *tumah* to B through *tziruf kli* — the combining principle only operates when *tumah* occurs within the vessel, not when something already *tamei* is introduced into it. So B remains entirely *tahor*. A *tvul yom* then comes and touches A while it is in the vessel. The *tumah* event now occurs within the vessel: the *tvul yom* touches A, and this should cause A's *tumah* to transfer to B via *tziruf kli*. The complication is that the *tvul yom* is himself a *sheni l'tumah*, and touching him would make A a *shlishi* — a far weaker level of *tumah* than the *rishon* A already is. Does the act of touching A matter at all? *Mi amrinan sava lei tumah* — do we say A is already "sated" with *tumah* at a high level and cannot receive an additional weaker level? Or do we not say this — *o lo* — and A does indeed become a *shlishi* as well, and that *tumah* event within the vessel then activates *tziruf kli* to make B *tamei*?

### Sheet & Midras

Abaye challenged the premise that we would ever say *sava lei tumah*. He cited a *mishna* in Kelim: *sadin tamei midras* — a sheet has contracted *tumah* as a *midras*. The concept of *tumah* through *midras* derives from the explicit verses — *kol hamishkav asher yishkav alav hazav yitma, v'chol hamerkav asher yirkav alav hazav yitma* — that anything serving as a sitting or lying surface for a *zav* or *zava* becomes an *av hatumah*, even without direct physical contact. So the sheet used as bedding by a *zav* is an *av hatumah*. Now the owner declares he is no longer using it as a sheet and hangs it as a curtain (*vilon*) dividing two rooms. Since it no longer functions as a surface for lying or sitting, it can no longer carry *tumah* as a *midras*; the *tumah* of *midras* requires that the object continue to function as something upon which one sits or lies. Yet the *mishna* rules *aval tamei maga midras* — it retains the *tumah* of having touched a *midras*. This is a striking ruling: the sheet touched no external *midras*; it *was* a *midras*. The answer is that while it was a *midras* it was also simultaneously touching itself, and so when the *midras* status departs, the status of having touched a *midras* remains, leaving it as a *rishon l'tumah*. The sheet held two levels simultaneously — *av* and *rishon* — and the departure of the higher level leaves the lower one behind, which implies that something can indeed hold a weaker level of *tumah* alongside a stronger one.

### Rabbi Yosi Dispute

Rabbi Yosi in the *mishna* disputed this reasoning: *v'chi b'eizeh midras naga zeh?* — this sheet never touched a *midras*; it *was* a *midras* and that is a different matter entirely. Merely being a *midras* does not confer the status of having *touched* a *midras*. However, Rabbi Yosi conceded: *ela ela shennaga bo hazav, tamei maga hazav* — if a *zav* actually touched the sheet directly, then when the *midras* status departs, the sheet retains the *tumah* of having been touched by a *zav*, leaving it as a *rishon*. And if the sheet was a *midras* by virtue of the *zav* sitting on something above it — so that the *zav* never touched the sheet directly — then when it becomes a curtain it is entirely *tahor* according to Rabbi Yosi.

The Gemara now draws an inference from Rabbi Yosi's position: when a *zav* did touch the sheet, *ki naga bo hazav mi itamei* — it is indeed *tamei*. And presumably, Rava argues, this includes a case where the *zav* touched the sheet *after* it was already a *midras* — meaning the sheet was already an *av hatumah*, and a *zav's* touch can ordinarily only make something a *rishon*, yet Rabbi Yosi would accept that it acquired that *rishon* status in addition. If so, this would prove that an object that already has a high level of *tumah* can still acquire a weaker level on top of it — *sava lei tumah* does not apply.

### Rabbi Yosi Dispute

Rava replied: *u'mi mayiti d'hai shennaga bo hazav l'vatar midras?* — what grounds are there to assume that Rabbi Yosi was describing the *zav* touching the sheet after it was already a *midras*? Perhaps the reverse sequence is meant: first the *zav* touched the sheet, making it a *rishon*, and then the *zav* sat on it, making it a *midras* and an *av*. In that case, the lighter *tumah* came first and then the heavier *tumah* was added — *tumah chamurah al tumah kalah* — and of course something can accumulate levels when they arrive in ascending order. That is entirely unremarkable. But in our case — *aval hacha d'eidi v'eidi tumah kalah* — whether both levels of *tumah* are similarly light, or a lighter one comes after a heavier one, there may well be a reason to say that the earlier, stronger *tumah* prevents the later, weaker one from registering. So the argument from Rabbi Yosi's case remains inconclusive, because we do not know the sequence Rabbi Yosi had in mind.

### Multiple Tumah Levels

The Gemara then tries to prove from the *seifa* of that same *mishna*: *modeh Rabbi Yosi* — Rabbi Yosi concedes the following. If two blankets are folded one atop the other, and a *zav* sits on the top one, both blankets are *tamei midras* — both are cushions of the *zav* — but the *zav's* body only physically contacts the upper one. Now the upper blanket (A) is a *midras* directly by the *zav*, and the lower blanket (B) is also a *midras* because it too is a surface the *zav* makes use of, but additionally, B was touched by A, which is a *midras*, giving B also the status of *maga midras*. The *mishna* rules that the upper blanket is *tamei midras* and the lower is *tamei midras* and *maga midras*. But B was already an *av* by virtue of being a *midras* — why does it also receive the lighter status of *maga midras*? Does Rabbi Yosi not say *sava lei tumah*?

### Simultaneous Levels

The Gemara resolves this: *hatam b'vat achat, hacha b'zeh achar zeh* — there it happened simultaneously; here it is sequential. At the very moment B became a *midras*, it also became a *maga midras*, because both events occurred in the same instant. There is no objection to something holding multiple levels of *tumah* if they arise at the same time — it is only the question of adding a weaker level *after* the fact of a higher level that is in dispute. The case of the two blankets therefore does not resolve whether *sava lei tumah* applies when a lighter *tumah* arrives subsequent to a heavier one already present. The question accordingly remains open.

## Lost & Replaced

### Three Halves Setup

Rava raised another question, one that the lecturer aptly characterizes as a "Schrödinger's *mincha*." An *isaron* is divided into two halves, A and B. B is then lost. A replacement half is set aside to take B's place — call it B-prime — so the *mincha* is now to consist of A and B-prime. Then B, the original lost half, is found. All three — A, B, and B-prime — are now sitting together in the same vessel.

### Connection Logic

The crux of the problem is that the *kli* is *metzaref*, it combines — but which piles does it combine? Either B or B-prime is the true second half of the *mincha* together with A, but both are in the vessel. The *tziruf* accordingly operates in ambiguous directions.

### Lost Tamei Transfer

*Nitma avud* — if B, the originally lost half, becomes *tamei*: then *avud v'rishon mitztarfin* — B and A are connected, because B's only legitimate partner is A, not B-prime. *Mufrash ein mitztaref* — B-prime does not become *tamei* through B, since there is no connection between B and B-prime; they are both competing, as it were, for A's position, but they have no relationship to one another directly.

### Replacement Tamei

*Nitma mufrash* — if B-prime becomes *tamei*: then *mufrash v'rishon mitztarfin* — B-prime and A are connected. *Avud ein mitztaref* — B does not become *tamei* through B-prime, for the same reason: B and B-prime have no point of connection to one another.

### Original Tamei

*Nitma rishon* — if A becomes *tamei*: then *shneihem mitztarfin* — both B and B-prime become *tamei*, because A is potentially connected to either of them, so *tziruf kli* carries the *tumah* in both directions.

### Household Unity

Abaye went further: *afilu nitma echad mehen nami shneihem mitztarfin* — even if any one of the three becomes *tamei*, all three become *tamei*. His reasoning: *kulhu bnei bikta dehadadi ninhu* — they are all members of the same household. Even though B and B-prime have no direct connection to each other, they are both connected through A, and all three share the same vessel. Abaye uses the analogy of a household with three siblings: A and B are half-brothers through their mother, A and B-prime are half-brothers through their father. B and B-prime share no direct relation — yet they all live under one roof, and what affects one affects all. Abaye's rule is a stringency (*lechumra*): even *tumah* originating in B or B-prime flows to all three.

### Kometz Consumed

The same logic governs the *kemitza*. *Kamatz min ha'avud* — if the *kometz* was taken from B: then *shirayu v'rishon ne'echalin, mufrash eino ne'echal* — what remains of B after the *kemitza*, together with A, may be eaten; the *mincha* has been defined by the act of taking from B as consisting of B and A. B-prime may not be eaten, since B-prime is not the same *mincha* as the one defined by B and A.

### Indeterminate Kometz

*Kamatz min hamufrash* — if the *kometz* was taken from B-prime: then what remains of B-prime and A may be eaten; B may not. *Kamatz min harishon* — if the *kometz* was taken from A: then *shneihem einam ne'echalim* — neither B nor B-prime may be eaten. The reason is that a *mincha* consists of precisely one *isaron*, and when the *kometz* is taken from A, it is entirely indeterminate whether B or B-prime is the second half of this *mincha*. Since the one that is *not* part of the *mincha* cannot be eaten — it is *kodesh* but not designated for this offering — and since it cannot be determined which is which, neither B nor B-prime may be consumed. Abaye added his characteristic stringency here as well: *afilu kamatz me'echad meihem shneihem einam ne'echalim* — even when the *kometz* is taken from B or from B-prime, neither B nor B-prime may be eaten, because *kulhu bnei bikta dehadadi ninhu* — all three are members of one household, and B-prime retains its indeterminate status regardless of whether the *kometz* was taken from B.

### Deficient Kometz

Rav Papa raised a difficulty: if A and B-prime and B are all treated as part of the same *mincha* due to their household unity — with B and B-prime in an indeterminate relationship to the *mincha* — then the *kometz* taken from A effectively represents three piles. The *kometz* ought to work for A and whichever of B or B-prime is the true second half, but it cannot simultaneously represent three halves when the *mincha* only consists of two. One could think of it as follows: one-third of the *kometz* is working for A, one-third for B, and one-third for B-prime — but only two of those thirds are legitimate, since only two halves constitute the actual *mincha*. That means a third of the *kometz* is not legitimately accounted for, which represents a *kometz* that is effectively only two-thirds of what it should be. Expressed differently: a sixth of B and a sixth of B-prime are not properly covered by this *kometz*, so there is a portion of the offering — approximately a sixth — that cannot validly be offered up. Rav Papa son of Rav Masharshiya sharpened the question further: it is not merely the *shirayim* that are problematic — *kometz gufeh heichi karev?* — how can the *kometz* itself be offered at all? *Ha'ika tilta d'chulin* — one-third of it is *chulin*, representing whichever of B or B-prime is not actually part of the *mincha*.

### Kohen's Intent

Rav Ashi resolved both difficulties: *kometz b'da'atei d'kahana talya milta* — the *kometz* is determined by the intent of the *kohen*, and *ki kamatz isaron ka kamatz* — when the *kohen* takes the handful, he is taking it as a *kometz* from a single *isaron*. It is true that the identity of the second half of the *isaron* cannot be objectively determined — is it B or B-prime? — but the *kohen* is not taking a *kometz* from an *isaron* and a half. His intent is to serve whichever *isaron* this actually is, however indeterminate that may appear from the outside. We do not say the *kometz* is working for three piles, thereby invalidating a third of itself. Rather, because the *kohen's* intent governs the *kometz*, and his intent is squarely for one *isaron*, the *kometz* is valid — and the question of which half is B and which is B-prime does not fractionate the *kometz* or render part of it *chulin*.
