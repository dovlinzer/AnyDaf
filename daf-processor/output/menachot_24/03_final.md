# Menachot 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kli Metzaref

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיק רַב כָּהֲנָא, אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ לִבְנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: עִשָּׂרוֹן שֶׁחִלְּקוֹ וְהִנִּיחוֹ בְּבִיסָא, וְנָגַע טְבוּל יוֹם בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן, מַהוּ? כִּי תְּנַן: כְּלִי מְצָרֵף מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ לַקֹּדֶשׁ, הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּנָגְעִי בַּהֲדָדֵי, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא נָגְעִי בַּהֲדָדֵי – לָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא?
>
> **Translation:** When Rav Kahana ascended from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, he found the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya, who were sitting and saying the following: In the case of **a tenth** of an ephah of a meal offering **that one divided** and **placed in a receptacle** such that the flour of the measure was in two places, not in contact with each other, **and one** who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed **touched one of** the portions of the meal offering, **what is** the *halakha*? Does he disqualify only the part of the meal offering that he touched, or the other part as well? **When we learned** in a mishna (*Ḥagiga* 20b) that **a vessel joins** all the food **that is in it with regard to sacrificial** food, meaning that if some of the contents become impure all the contents become impure as well, does **this matter** apply only **where** the contents **are touching each other, but where** the contents **are not touching each other** the ritual impurity is **not** imparted to the other contents? **Or perhaps** there is **no difference.**

### Rav Kahana's Teaching

The daf opens at the end of 23b, picking up from a lengthy discussion concerning the mixing of *menachot* and questions of *bittul* and *min b'mino* according to Rabbi Yehuda. We now turn to a distinct topic introduced by the phrase *ki salik Rav Kahana* — when Rav Kahana went up to Eretz Yisrael, he found the sons of Rabbi Chiya (*bnei Rabbi Chiya*) sitting and discussing the following *halacha*.

### Two Piles Combining

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ אִיהוּ: מִי תְּנַן "כְּלִי מְחַבֵּר"? "כְּלִי מְצָרֵף" תְּנַן, כֹּל דְּהוּ. הוֹשִׁיט אֶחָד לְבֵינֵיהֶן – מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana **said to** the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya: **Did we learn** in the mishna that **a vessel connects** the contents within it? **We learned** that **a vessel joins** the contents within it, indicating that it does so in **any** case, whether or not the contents are in contact with one another. The sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya then asked Rav Kahana: If one **inserted** another **one** half-tenth of an ephah **between them,** and one who was ritually impure who immersed that day touched it, **what is** the *halakha*? Are the first two half-tenths rendered impure?

The case: an *isaron* — the flour designated for a *mincha* — has been divided into two separate piles, not touching one another, and placed together in a single vessel. A *tvul yom* then touches one of the piles. The *tvul yom* is someone who has immersed in a *mikvah* that day and will be fully *tahor* that evening, but for now retains the status of a *sheni l'tumah*, sufficient to render *terumah* or *kodesh* items *tamei*. This serves as an example of someone with a minimal level of *tumah* who can still effect contamination of sacred items. The question — *mahu?* — is whether his touching one pile renders the other pile *tamei* as well.

The basis for thinking it might is a well-known *mishna* in Chagiga: *kli metzaref mah sheb'tocho l'kodesh* — a vessel combines whatever is inside it when dealing with *kodesh* items for the purposes of *tumah v'tahara*. But perhaps that principle only applies when the items inside the vessel are actually touching one another. Maybe when the two piles are entirely separate, the *kli* cannot bridge the gap between them. Or perhaps, as the other possibility runs, it makes no difference — even completely separate piles within the same vessel are considered one unit. One might further ask: if they are touching one another, why would the *kli* need to do any combining at all? Even if each pile retains its distinct shape — perhaps because oil has already been mixed into each — two piles merely touching are not yet a single unit, and without the *kli*, a *tvul yom* touching pile A would need pile A to independently transfer *tumah* to pile B, which it might not be able to do in the ordinary workings of *hilchot tumah*.

### Metzaref vs Mechaber

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: צָרִיךְ לִכְלִי – כְּלִי מְצָרְפוֹ, אֵין צָרִיךְ לִכְלִי – אֵין כְּלִי מְצָרְפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana **said to them:** Only when an item **requires a vessel** in order for it to be sanctified, e.g., in the case of the two half-tenths of an ephah of a meal offering, does the **vessel join it** together. In the case of an item that **does not require a vessel,** such as this half-tenth that was placed between them, the **vessel does not join it.**

Rav Kahana responded to the *bnei Rabbi Chiya* with a careful linguistic observation: *mi tnan kli mechaber?* — the *mishna* does not say the vessel *joins* the contents. It says *kli metzaref* — it *combines* them. The word *mechaber* would imply the vessel fuses them into a single physical unit, which would presuppose actual contact. *Metzaref*, by contrast, denotes a less tight form of connection — the items remain distinct, yet the vessel draws them together. This language itself signals that even in the absence of direct contact, the vessel connects all its contents. Therefore, even across an air space, if one pile is touched it renders the other *tamei*.

### Extraneous Flour

The discussion continues: the sons of Rabbi Chiya ask — *hoshit echad beineihem mahu?* — what if one places an additional half-*isaron* between the two proper halves? This third pile does not belong to the *mincha*; it is extraneous. Rav Kahana replied: *tzarich l'kli kli metzarfo, ein tzarich l'kli ein kli metzarfo* — something that requires the *kli* to be part of a *mincha* is combined by the vessel; something that does not require it, that has no proper claim on the vessel, is not combined by it. The extraneous flour is therefore not drawn into the *tziruf* and does not participate in the connection.

### Air Space Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹשִׁיט טְבוּל יוֹם אֶת אֶצְבָּעוֹ בֵּינֵיהֶן, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֵין לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא מֵאֲוִירוֹ אֶלָּא כְּלִי חֶרֶס בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya then asked: If **one who immersed that day inserted his finger between** the two half-tenths of the ephah that were placed in the receptacle, without touching either one, **what is** the *halakha*? Are the two half-tenths rendered impure? Rav Kahana **said to them** in response: The **only item you have that transmits impurity through its airspace** is **an earthenware vessel alone.**

A further question: *hoshit tvul yom et etzba'o beineihem mahu?* — what if the *tvul yom* did not touch either pile directly but inserted his finger into the air space between pile A and pile B? If the two piles are considered a single combined unit, perhaps the air space between them is also suffused with the status of *mincha*, and touching that air space should be enough to transfer *tumah* to everything. Rav Kahana rejected this: *ein lecha davar shemitamei me'avir ela kli cheres bilvad* — nothing transmits *tumah* through air space except a *kli cheres*. The principle of *kli metzaref* combines the discrete physical items that need the vessel and are appropriate to it; it does not convert the air inside the vessel into an extension of those items. *Tumah* still travels by direct contact. The *kli* simply treats all such items as one, so that touching any one of them affects all — but only if an item is actually touched.

### Kli Cheres Exception

This ruling clarifies the scope of *kli metzaref*: it is a principle of combination for *kodesh* items that belong within the vessel, not a principle that fills the vessel's interior with a continuous field of *tumah*. The transmission of *tumah* through air — *tumah me'avir* — is a separate and exceptional concept, confined to *kli cheres* by explicit *halacha*. The two principles must not be conflated.

## Tziruf Kli & Kometz

### De'Oraita Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אִיהוּ בְּעָא מִינַּיְיהוּ: מַהוּ לִקְמוֹץ מִזֶּה עַל זֶה? צֵירוּף דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אוֹ דְּרַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana **himself then asked** the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya: If two half-tenths of an ephah of a meal offering are placed in one vessel but are not in contact with each other, **what is** the *halakha*? Can one **remove a handful from this** half-tenth of an ephah **on** behalf of **that** half-tenth of an ephah? Is the **joining** of the contents of the vessel effective **by Torah law or by rabbinic law?** If it is effective by Torah law, then the removal of the handful is valid. If it is effective by rabbinic law, then the removal of the handful was not performed correctly, since it was not taken from the entire tenth of an ephah of the meal offering.

Having taught the *bnei Rabbi Chiya*, Rav Kahana now turned the tables and posed his own question to them: *mahu likmotz mizeh al zeh, tziruf de'oraita o derabbanan?* Can one take a *kemitza* from one of the two separate piles in the *kli shares* and have it count as a valid *kemitza* for the entire *mincha*? The deeper question is whether *tziruf kli* — the combining effected by the vessel — operates at the *de'oraita* level or only *derabbanan*. The potential *de'oraita* basis would be the verse *kaf achat asara zahav*, from which one might derive that everything in a *kaf* is considered a single unit. If *tziruf kli* is *de'oraita*, then the two piles genuinely constitute one *mincha* and a *kemitza* taken from one serves the whole. If it is only *derabbanan*, the combining may be no more than a stringency for *tumah*, and *de'oraita* the piles remain distinct — so that even in the same vessel they would not constitute a valid *mincha* and the *kemitza* would not be properly taken.

### Two Menachot Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: זוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ, כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ שָׁמַעְנוּ, דִּתְנַן: שְׁתֵּי מְנָחוֹת שֶׁלֹּא נִקְמְצוּ וְנִתְעָרְבוּ זוֹ בְּזוֹ, אִם יָכוֹל לִקְמוֹץ מִזּוֹ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ וּמִזּוֹ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ – כְּשֵׁירוֹת, וְאִם לָאו – פְּסוּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to** Rav Kahana: **We did not hear** the *halakha* with regard to **this** case explicitly, but **we heard** the *halakha* with regard to a case **similar to this. As we learned** in the mishna: In the case of **two meal offerings from which a handful was not removed and** which **were intermingled with each other, if** the priest **can remove a handful from this** meal offering **by itself and from that** meal offering **by itself, they are fit** meal offerings, **but if not, they are unfit,** as the handful of each meal offering must be taken from its original source.

The *bnei Rabbi Chiya* acknowledged they had no direct answer — *zu lo shamanu* — but they had heard something in a related context from which they believed a proof could be drawn. The *mishna* teaches: *shtei menachot shelo nikmetzu venitarvu* — two *menachot* that have not yet had the *kemitza* taken from them became mixed together. In such a case, if one can take the *kemitza* from the A-section alone and from the B-section alone — meaning each *mincha* still has a portion that remains unmixed with the other — both are valid, because a pure A *kemitza* and a pure B *kemitza* were each taken. But if the mixing is so thorough that the overlap area is required for either *kemitza*, both are invalid. The Gemara reads this *mishna* as implying that the A-section, the AB-overlap, and the B-section are three separate piles, not touching. Yet the *kemitza* from A can combine with the flour in the mixed AB pile, which only makes sense if they are all in the same *kli shares* and *tziruf kli* is working to make them one — even for purposes of validating the *kemitza*. This would suggest *tziruf kli* operates at the *de'oraita* level.

### Comb Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דִּלְמָא בְּגוּשִּׁים הַמְחוּלָּקִין הָעֲשׂוּיִין כְּמַסְרֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This cannot be inferred from the mishna, as **perhaps** the ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to** a case where the **clumps** of the meal offering **are divided like** the teeth of **a comb,** so that although the handful is removed from a clump of the meal offering that is separate from the clump that is intermingled with the other offering, all parts of the meal offering are still in contact with one another. It may still be that in the case presented by Rav Kahana, where the parts are truly separated from one another, it is possible that one cannot remove the handful from one part on behalf of the other.

Rava challenged this proof: *dilma b'gushim hamachulakim asuyim k'masreik* — perhaps the *mishna* is not describing three separate non-touching piles at all. Rather, the configuration resembles a comb (*masreik*): the base of the comb is the mixed AB section, and the separate teeth of the comb are the pure A portion and the pure B portion. Everything is in fact touching; there are simply sub-regions that are purely A and purely B emerging from a shared base that contains both. On this reading the *mishna* requires no appeal to *tziruf kli* bridging a gap, and the question of whether *tziruf kli* is *de'oraita* remains unanswered.

### Mimenu Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: "וְהֵרִים מִמֶּנּוּ" – מִן הַמְחוּבָּר, שֶׁלֹּא יָבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן בִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים וְיִקְמוֹץ. הָא בִּכְלִי אֶחָד דּוּמְיָא דִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים – קָמֵיץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Since this question was not resolved, **what** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter? **Rava said: Come** and **hear** a proof, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And he shall take up from it** his handful" (Leviticus 6:8), meaning that he shall take the handful **from** the meal offering **that is connected.** This teaches **that one shall not bring a tenth** of an ephah divided **in two vessels and remove the handful** from one on behalf of the other. It can therefore be inferred that **in** the case of **one vessel** that is **similar to two vessels,** as the entirety of the meal offering is brought in one vessel although the different parts are not touching, one may **remove the handful** from one part on behalf of the other part.

Rava himself then offered a different source: *ta shma, detanya, v'herim mimenu — mimenu min hamachubar*. The *pasuk* governing the *kometz* requires that the taking of the handful be from a single connected unit. The *braita* derives from *mimenu* that one may not divide the *isaron* into two separate vessels and take the *kemitza* from one of them. The implicit inference is: *ha b'kli echad dumya d'shnei kelim* — but if it were in one vessel, even if it resembled two vessels in being two separate piles, it sounds as though the *kometz* would be valid. This seems to be a solid proof that *tziruf kli* works at the *de'oraita* level to make a *kemitza* valid.

### Compartmented Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: דִּילְמָא שְׁנֵי כֵּלִים הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן קְפִיזָא בְּקַבָּא, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דַּעֲרִיבִי מֵעִילַּאי, כֵּיוָן דְּמִיפַּסְקָן מְחִיצָתָא דִּקְפִיזָא מִתַּתָּא. הָא כְּלִי אֶחָד דּוּמְיָא דִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן עֲרֵיבַת תַּרְנְגוֹלִין, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּמִיפַּסְקָן מְחִיצָתָא הָא נְגִיעַ, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָא נְגִיעַ כְּלָל – תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **Perhaps** one could say: **What are the circumstances** when the *baraita* states that one may not bring a tenth of an ephah in **two vessels?** The circumstances are, **for example,** if one hollowed out the area of a smaller ***kefiza*** measure **within** the area of a larger ***kav*** measure, so that within the one receptacle there were two cavities divided by a partition that did not reach the top of the receptacle. In this case, **even though** the two **are intermingled on top,** above the partition, **since the partition of the *kefiza*** measure **divides** them **below,** they are still separated and not joined together. Abaye continues: **What are the circumstances** of **one vessel** that is **similar to two vessels,** with regard to which you inferred that one may remove the handful from one part on behalf of the other part? The circumstances are, **for example, a hen trough** that is filled with water or fodder, **and even though a partition divides** the top of the trough, the contents are **touching** below. **But here,** in the case of two half-tenths of an ephah that are placed in a receptacle **that are not touching** each other **at all, you should raise the dilemma** as to whether the handful may be removed from one part on behalf of the other.

Abaye qualified the proof: *dilma shnei kelim heichi dami k'gon kviza b'kava* — perhaps what the *braita* is ruling out when it disqualifies "two vessels" is a specific configuration, namely a measuring vessel (*kfiza*) carved within a larger vessel (*kava*), where the dividing wall between the compartments runs the full height of the vessel. In that case, even if the flour in the two compartments happens to touch above the rim, the division below makes it as though there are two separate vessels — and that is what the *braita* rejects. The scenario the *braita* considers valid, on this reading, is one in which the dividing wall ends before the top of the vessel — like a trough for chickens (*arivata shel tarnegolim*), as Rashi explains — so that the contents of the two compartments touch one another within the walls of the vessel itself. That is what the *braita* means by "one vessel similar to two." But fully separate, non-touching piles without even that upper connection are not addressed by the *braita* at all. The question — *heicha dela nagei klal tiba'ei lach* — therefore remains open.

## External Connections

### Water Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: צֵירוּף כְּלִי וְחִיבּוּר מַיִם, מַהוּ? כִּי תְּנַן כְּלִי מְצָרֵף מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ לַקֹּדֶשׁ, הָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּגַוַּאי, אֲבָל דְּבָרַאי – לָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא, כֵּיוָן דִּמְחַבַּר – מְחַבַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** In a scenario where **a vessel joins** the two half-tenths of an ephah that are inside the vessel but not touching, **and** there is **a connection** by means of **water** between one of the half-tenths of the ephah inside the vessel and another half-tenth of an ephah that is outside the vessel, and one who immersed that day touched the other half-tenth of an ephah that is inside the vessel, **what is** the *halakha*? Does he also disqualify the half-tenth of an ephah that is outside the vessel? **When we learned** in a mishna (*Ḥagiga* 20b) that **a vessel joins** all the food **that is in it with regard to sacrificial** food, meaning that if some of the contents become impure all the contents become impure as well, does **this matter** apply only to **that** which is **inside** the vessel, **but not** to **that** which is **outside** of it, despite the fact that the outer item is connected to an item inside the vessel? **Or perhaps, since** the half-tenth of an ephah found outside the vessel **is connected** to an impure item, it **is connected** and becomes impure.

Rami bar Yirmiyah posed a sequence of thought experiments pushing the logic of *tziruf kli* further. The first: *tziruf kli v'chibur mayim mahu* — what happens when the principle of *kli metzaref* intersects with a connection by water? As Rashi explains the scenario: pile A and pile B are inside the vessel, and pile C is outside the vessel, connected to pile B through a flowing liquid — perhaps the oil from B is running outward and making contact with C. Normally a connection through liquid between two separate food items might be sufficient to treat them as joined. The question is whether, when A touches B via *tziruf kli* inside the vessel, the *tumah* then continues outward to C via the liquid connection. Do we say *kli metzaref mah sheb'tocho l'kodesh* applies only to items on the inside of the vessel — *hanei milei d'gavai, aval d'barai lo* — or do we say that since there is a connection, the connection simply continues — *keivan d'mchaber, mchaber* — and C is pulled along as well? It should be noted that Tosafot struggles with this Gemara, since it is not at all obvious why a liquid connection between B and C would ordinarily combine them for purposes of *tumah*; the Gemara seems to take it as a given that liquid does connect them and confines the question to whether *tziruf kli* extends across that connection.

### Extending Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר: כֵּיוָן דִּמְחַבַּר מְחַבַּר, חִיבּוּר מַיִם וְצֵירוּף כְּלִי, וְנָגַע טְבוּל יוֹם מִבַּחוּץ – מַהוּ? כִּי תְּנַן "כְּלִי מְצָרֵף", הָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּנָגַע מִגַּוַּאי, אֲבָל מִבָּרַאי – לָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya continues: **And if you say** that **since** the half-tenth of an ephah found outside the vessel **is connected** to an impure item, it **is connected** and becomes impure, one can raise another dilemma. In a case where there is **a connection** by means of **water** between a half-tenth of an ephah outside a vessel and another half-tenth of an ephah that is inside the vessel, **and** the **vessel joins** two half-tenths of an ephah that are inside the vessel, **and one who immersed that day touched** the half-tenth of an ephah **that is outside** the vessel, **what is** the *halakha*? **When we learned** in a mishna that **a vessel joins** all the food that is in it with regard to sacrificial food, meaning that if some of the contents become impure all the contents become impure as well, does **this matter** apply only in a case **where** the one who immersed that day **touched** that **which is inside** the vessel, thereby transmitting impurity to all of the contents of the vessel, and due to the connection by means of water the impurity is then transmitted to that which is outside the vessel, **but** it does **not** apply in a case **where** the one who immersed that day touched that **which is outside** of the vessel, and only the half-tenth of an ephah that is connected to the outer item becomes impure? **Or perhaps** this case **is no different,** and the vessel joins all of its contents with regard to ritual impurity. The Gemara comments: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The next step is the reverse direction: *chibur mayim v'tziruf kli, v'naga tvul yom mi'bachutz mahu* — what if a *tvul yom* touched C, which is outside the vessel? Because C and B are connected by liquid, B becomes *tamei*. Now, since B and A are inside the same vessel, would *tziruf kli* transfer the *tumah* from B to A? The argument against it: when does the principle of *kli metzaref* operate? Perhaps only when the *tumah* originates within the vessel — *ki tnan kli metzaref hanei milei d'naga mi'gavai* — so that if one touches A inside the vessel, the *tumah* jumps to B, and vice versa. But if the original touch occurred outside the vessel on C, and C contaminated B, the initial *tumah* event occurred outside the vessel. Perhaps in that case *tziruf kli* does not carry it further to A. Or perhaps it makes no difference — *o dilma lo shna*.

### Tumah Origin (Teiku)

The Gemara leaves both questions unresolved — *teiku*. The underlying difficulty, as noted, is the very premise that liquid connection ordinarily would combine B and C; but leaving that aside, whether *tziruf kli* can extend *tumah* outward to an externally connected item, and whether the *origin* of the *tumah* — inside or outside the vessel — affects the principle's operation, remain open questions.

## Sava Lei Tumah

### Tvul Yom on Zav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: עִשָּׂרוֹן שֶׁחִלְּקוֹ, וְנִטְמָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְהִנִּיחוֹ בְּבִיסָא, וְחָזַר טְבוּל יוֹם וְנָגַע בְּאוֹתוֹ טָמֵא – מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן שְׂבַע לוֹ טוּמְאָה, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** § It has been demonstrated that a vessel joins the contents that are found in it, even if they are not touching one another, with regard to ritual impurity, such that if some of the contents become impure, all of the contents are rendered impure. **Rava raised a dilemma:** With regard to **a tenth** of an ephah of a meal offering **that one divided** and then placed the two halves in different vessels, **and one of them became impure and** afterward **he placed it in a receptacle** along with the second half-tenth of an ephah, **and then one who immersed that day touched that one** that was already rendered **impure, what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that the item is already **saturated with impurity** and cannot be rendered impure a second time, and therefore the second half-tenth of the ephah is not rendered impure even though it is joined in the same receptacle, **or not?**

Rava raised a new question, one that the lecturer aptly characterizes as a "Schrödinger's *mincha*." An *isaron* is divided into two halves, A and B. B is then lost. A replacement half is set aside to take B's place — call it B-prime — so the *mincha* is now to consist of A and B-prime. Then B, the original lost half, is found. All three — A, B, and B-prime — are now sitting together in the same vessel.

Actually, I must correct the structure here. The question Rava asked is different. Let me re-read it: an *isaron* divided into two halves, one half becomes impure and is placed in a vessel with the other half (which is still tahor). A *tvul yom* then touches the half that was already impure. The question is: does the *tvul yom* render the other half impure as well, or do we say that the already-impure half is "saturated" with impurity and cannot receive additional impurity? The reason this matters: if it can receive additional impurity from the *tvul yom*, then this impurity event happens within the vessel, activating *tziruf kli* to make the other half impure too. But if not, nothing happens.

### Sheet & Midras

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּמִי אָמְרִינַן שְׂבַע לֵיהּ טוּמְאָה? וְהָתְנַן: סָדִין טָמֵא מִדְרָס וַעֲשָׂאוֹ וִילוֹן – טָהוֹר מִן הַמִּדְרָס, אֲבָל טָמֵא מַגַּע מִדְרָס.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: And do we say** that an item that is already **saturated with impurity** cannot be rendered impure a second time? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Kelim* 27:9): With regard to **a sheet** that is impure due to **ritual impurity** imparted by **treading,** e.g., if a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*] lay down on it and transferred to it this severe impurity, **and** afterward **one made a curtain [*villon*] of it,** it is **pure** with regard to ritual impurity **imparted by treading,** as it is no longer fit for sitting or lying down. **But** it is **impure** due to having been in **contact** with an item that became ritually impure with impurity imparted by **treading,** as it is viewed as having been in contact with itself, and therefore it can impart impurity to food and drink.

Abaye challenged the premise that we would ever say *sava lei tumah*. He cited a *mishna* in Kelim: *sadin tamei midras* — a sheet has contracted *tumah* as a *midras*. The concept of *tumah* through *midras* derives from the explicit verses — *kol hamishkav asher yishkav alav hazav yitma, v'chol hamerkav asher yirkav alav hazav yitma* — that anything serving as a sitting or lying surface for a *zav* or *zava* becomes an *av hatumah*, even without direct physical contact. So the sheet used as bedding by a *zav* is an *av hatumah*. Now the owner declares he is no longer using it as a sheet and hangs it as a curtain (*vilon*) dividing two rooms. Since it no longer functions as a surface for lying or sitting, it can no longer carry *tumah* as a *midras*; the *tumah* of *midras* requires that the object continue to function as something upon which one sits or lies. Yet the *mishna* rules *aval tamei maga midras* — it retains the *tumah* of having touched a *midras*. This is a striking ruling: the sheet touched no external *midras*; it *was* a *midras*. The answer is that while it was a *midras* it was also simultaneously touching itself, and so when the *midras* status departs, the status of having touched a *midras* remains, leaving it as a *rishon l'tumah*. The sheet held two levels simultaneously — *av* and *rishon* — and the departure of the higher level leaves the lower one behind, which implies that something can indeed hold a weaker level of *tumah* alongside a stronger one.

### Rabbi Yosi Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: בְּאֵיזֶה מִדְרָס נָגַע זֶה? אֶלָּא שֶׁאִם נָגַע בּוֹ הַזָּב, טָמֵא מַגַּע הַזָּב.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Yosei said: What** source of impurity imparted by **treading did this** curtain **touch? Rather,** the *halakha* is **that if a *zav* touched** the sheet itself before it was made into a curtain, and did not only lie on it without touching it directly, then although the curtain is pure with regard to ritual impurity imparted by treading, it is nevertheless **impure** due to **contact** with **a *zav*.** This is because the impurity transmitted by contact with a *zav* applies in the case of a curtain, which is not the *halakha* with regard to impurity imparted by treading.

Rabbi Yosi in the *mishna* disputed this reasoning: *v'chi b'eizeh midras naga zeh?* — this sheet never touched a *midras*; it *was* a *midras* and that is a different matter entirely. Merely being a *midras* does not confer the status of having *touched* a *midras*. However, Rabbi Yosi conceded: *ela ela shennaga bo hazav, tamei maga hazav* — if a *zav* actually touched the sheet directly, then when the *midras* status departs, the sheet retains the *tumah* of having been touched by a *zav*, leaving it as a *rishon*. And if the sheet was a *midras* by virtue of the *zav* sitting on something above it — so that the *zav* never touched the sheet directly — then when it becomes a curtain it is entirely *tahor* according to Rabbi Yosi.

### Rebuttal to Abaye's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי נָגַע בּוֹ הַזָּב מִיהָא טָמֵא, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְבַסּוֹף כְּטָמֵא מִדְרָס, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַגַּע הַזָּב, אַמַּאי? לֵימָא: שְׂבַע לֵיהּ טוּמְאָה!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye comments: **When a *zav* touched** the sheet, **in any event** it was rendered **impure, even** if he touched it **after** he lay on the sheet, thereby rendering it impure with impurity imparted by treading. In this manner, it was rendered impure with the **ritual impurity** imparted by **treading, and afterward** it was again rendered impure due to **contact** with **a *zav*.** According to the statement of Rava, **why** would this be the *halakha*? **Let us say** that the item is already **saturated with impurity** and cannot be rendered impure a second time.

Abaye went further by pointing out that if we indeed accept Rabbi Yosi's reasoning, the sheet becomes a *rishon* due to being touched by a *zav*, and this status would coexist with the prior status as a *midras* (which rendered it an *av*). Thus the sheet simultaneously holds two levels of *tumah*, with the lighter one registering alongside the heavier one, proving that *sava lei tumah* does not apply.

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמִמַּאי דְּהַאי "שֶׁאִם נָגַע בּוֹ הַזָּב" לְבָתַר מִדְרָס? דִּילְמָא מִקַּמֵּי מִדְרָס, דְּהָוְיָא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה עַל טוּמְאָה קַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him** in response: **And from where** do you know **that this** statement of Rabbi Yosei: **That if a *zav* touched** the sheet it is nevertheless impure due to contact with a *zav*, is referring **to** a case where a *zav* touched the sheet **after** it was rendered impure with the ritual impurity imparted by **treading? Perhaps** he was referring to a case where a *zav* touched the sheet, rendering it impure due to contact with a *zav*, **before** he lay on it and rendered it impure with the ritual impurity imparted by **treading.** In that case, the **severe** form of ritual **impurity** imparted by the treading of the *zav*, which is a primary source of ritual impurity that imparts impurity to all people and items, **takes effect in addition to** the **lesser** form of **impurity** imparted by contact with a *zav*, which imparts impurity only to food and drink.

Rava replied: *u'mi mayiti d'hai shennaga bo hazav l'vatar midras?* — what grounds are there to assume that Rabbi Yosi was describing the *zav* touching the sheet after it was already a *midras*? Perhaps the reverse sequence is meant: first the *zav* touched the sheet, making it a *rishon*, and then the *zav* sat on it, making it a *midras* and an *av*. In that case, the lighter *tumah* came first and then the heavier *tumah* was added — *tumah chamurah al tumah kalah* — and of course something can accumulate levels when they arrive in ascending order. That is entirely unremarkable. But in our case — *aval hacha d'eidi v'eidi tumah kalah* — whether both levels of *tumah* are similarly light, or a lighter one comes after a heavier one, there may well be a reason to say that the earlier, stronger *tumah* prevents the later, weaker one from registering. So the argument from Rabbi Yosi's case remains inconclusive, because we do not know the sequence Rabbi Yosi had in mind.

### Additional Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי טוּמְאָה קַלָּה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But here,** in the case of part of a meal offering that was touched by one who immersed that day after having already become impure due to the touch of one who immersed that day, **where** both **this and that** are **lesser** forms of **impurity,** perhaps the impurity does **not** take effect a second time, as it is already impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִסֵּיפָא, מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בִּשְׁנֵי סְדִינִין הַמְקוּפָּלִין וּמוּנָּחִין זֶה עַל זֶה וְיָשַׁב זָב עֲלֵיהֶן, שֶׁהָעֶלְיוֹן טָמֵא מִדְרָס, וְהַתַּחְתּוֹן טָמֵא מִדְרָס וּמַגַּע מִדְרָס. וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא: שְׂבַע לֵיהּ טוּמְאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather,** the proof against the existence of a principle that an item can be saturated with impurity and not susceptible to becoming impure a second time is **from the last clause** of a *baraita* that corresponds to the mishna: **Rabbi Yosei concedes that in** a case of **two sheets that are folded and placed on** top of **one another, and a *zav* sat upon them, the top** sheet is rendered impure with the **ritual impurity** imparted by **treading, and the bottom** sheet is rendered impure with the **ritual impurity** imparted by **treading and** due to **contact** with the top sheet that has become ritually impure with impurity imparted by **treading.** The Gemara explains: **But** according to the opinion advanced in Rava's dilemma, **why** would this be the case? **Let us say** that the bottom sheet is already **saturated with impurity** and cannot be rendered impure a second time.

The Gemara then tries to prove from the *seifa* of that same *mishna*: *modeh Rabbi Yosi* — Rabbi Yosi concedes the following. If two blankets are folded one atop the other, and a *zav* sits on the top one, both blankets are *tamei midras* — both are cushions of the *zav* — but the *zav's* body only physically contacts the upper one. Now the upper blanket (A) is a *midras* directly by the *zav*, and the lower blanket (B) is also a *midras* because it too is a surface the *zav* makes use of, but additionally, B was touched by A, which is a *midras*, giving B also the status of *maga midras*. The *mishna* rules that the upper blanket is *tamei midras* and the lower is *tamei midras* and *maga midras*. But B was already an *av* by virtue of being a *midras* — why does it also receive the lighter status of *maga midras*? Does Rabbi Yosi not say *sava lei tumah*?

### Simultaneous Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם בְּבַת אַחַת, הָכָא בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **There,** with regard to the bottom sheet, the two types of impurity take effect **simultaneously,** whereas **here,** with regard to the impure meal offering, the two forms of impurity take effect **one after the other.** It is only in the latter case that Rava suggests that the second type of impurity does not take effect. Therefore, there is no conclusive proof, and the question raised by Rava remains unresolved.

The Gemara resolves this: *hatam b'vat achat, hacha b'zeh achar zeh* — there it happened simultaneously; here it is sequential. At the very moment B became a *midras*, it also became a *maga midras*, because both events occurred in the same instant. There is no objection to something holding multiple levels of *tumah* if they arise at the same time — it is only the question of adding a weaker level *after* the fact of a higher level that is in dispute. The case of the two blankets therefore does not resolve whether *sava lei tumah* applies when a lighter *tumah* arrives subsequent to a heavier one already present. The question accordingly remains open.

## Lost & Replaced

### Three Halves Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: עִשָּׂרוֹן שֶׁחִלְּקוֹ, וְאָבַד אֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו, וְנִמְצָא הָרִאשׁוֹן, וַהֲרֵי שְׁלָשְׁתָּן מוּנָּחִין בְּבִיסָא – נִטְמָא אָבוּד, אָבוּד וְרִאשׁוֹן מִצְטָרְפִין, מוּפְרָשׁ אֵין מִצְטָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** In a case where one **divided a tenth** of an ephah of a meal offering into two halves, **and one** half was **lost and he separated another** half **in its stead, and** afterward **the first** lost half **was found, and all three are placed in a receptacle** together, if the one that had been **lost became impure, the** previously **lost** half-tenth of an ephah **and** the **first** half-tenth of an ephah **join together** and become impure, in accordance with the mishna cited earlier (*Ḥagiga* 20b) that a vessel joins the two together with regard to ritual impurity. But the half-tenth of an ephah that was **separated** to replace the lost half-tenth **does not join together** with the other half-tenths, and it remains pure.

Rava raised another question, one that the lecturer aptly characterizes as a "Schrödinger's *mincha*." An *isaron* is divided into two halves, A and B. B is then lost. A replacement half is set aside to take B's place — call it B-prime — so the *mincha* is now to consist of A and B-prime. Then B, the original lost half, is found. All three — A, B, and B-prime — are now sitting together in the same vessel.

### Connection Logic

The crux of the problem is that the *kli* is *metzaref*, it combines — but which piles does it combine? Either B or B-prime is the true second half of the *mincha* together with A, but both are in the vessel. The *tziruf* accordingly operates in ambiguous directions.

### Lost Tamei Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְמָא מוּפְרָשׁ – מוּפְרָשׁ וְרִאשׁוֹן מִצְטָרְפִין, אָבוּד אֵין מִצְטָרֵף; נִטְמָא רִאשׁוֹן – שְׁנֵיהֶם מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** If the one that had been **separated** to replace the lost half-tenth **became impure,** then the **separated** half-tenth **and** the **first** half-tenth **join together** and become impure, since the former was separated in order to complete the tenth together with the first half-tenth, while the previously **lost** half-tenth **does not join together** with them. If the **first** half-tenth **became impure,** then **both** the previously lost half-tenth as well as the half-tenth that was separated as its replacement **join together** and become ritually impure, as each of them had at one point been part of the same tenth as the first half-tenth.

*Nitma avud* — if B, the originally lost half, becomes *tamei*: then *avud v'rishon mitztarfin* — B and A are connected, because B's only legitimate partner is A, not B-prime. *Mufrash ein mitztaref* — B-prime does not become *tamei* through B, since there is no connection between B and B-prime; they are both competing, as it were, for A's position, but they have no relationship to one another directly.

*Nitma mufrash* — if B-prime becomes *tamei*: then *mufrash v'rishon mitztarfin* — B-prime and A are connected. *Avud ein mitztaref* — B does not become *tamei* through B-prime, for the same reason: B and B-prime have no point of connection to one another.

*Nitma rishon* — if A becomes *tamei*: then *shneihem mitztarfin* — both B and B-prime become *tamei*, because A is potentially connected to either of them, so *tziruf kli* carries the *tumah* in both directions.

### Household Unity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ נִטְמָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן נָמֵי – שְׁנֵיהֶם מִצְטָרְפִין, מַאי טַעְמָא? כּוּלְּהוּ בְּנֵי בִיקְתָּא דַּהֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Even if** any **one of** the half-tenths **became impure, both** remaining half-tenths **join together** and become impure as well. **What is the reason? They are all residents of one cabin,** i.e., they were meant to be part of the same meal offering.

Abaye went further: *afilu nitma echad mehen nami shneihem mitztarfin* — even if any one of the three becomes *tamei*, all three become *tamei*. His reasoning: *kulhu bnei bikta dehadadi ninhu* — they are all members of the same household. Even though B and B-prime have no direct connection to each other, they are both connected through A, and all three share the same vessel. Abaye uses the analogy of a household with three siblings: A and B are half-brothers through their mother, A and B-prime are half-brothers through their father. B and B-prime share no direct relation — yet they all live under one roof, and what affects one affects all. Abaye's rule is a stringency (*lechumra*): even *tumah* originating in B or B-prime flows to all three.

### Kometz Consumed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן קְמִיצָה, קָמַץ מִן הָאָבוּד – שְׁיָרָיו וְרִאשׁוֹן נֶאֱכָלִין, מוּפְרָשׁ אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל; קָמַץ מִן הַמּוּפְרָשׁ – שְׁיָרָיו וְרִאשׁוֹן נֶאֱכָלִין, אָבוּד אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly,** according to Rava, **with regard to the removal of the handful,** if one **removed the handful from** the previously **lost** half-tenth, **its remainder and** the remainder of the **first** half-tenth of an ephah **are eaten** by the priests, while the half-tenth of an ephah that was **separated** in its stead **is not eaten.** Since it was not meant to join together with this other half-tenth, the removal of the handful does not permit its consumption. If one **removed the handful from** the one that had been **separated** in place of the lost half-tenth, then **its remainder and** the **first** half-tenth of an ephah **are eaten,** while the previously **lost** half-tenth **is not eaten.**

The same logic governs the *kemitza*. *Kamatz min ha'avud* — if the *kometz* was taken from B: then *shirayu v'rishon ne'echalin, mufrash eino ne'echal* — what remains of B after the *kemitza*, together with A, may be eaten; the *mincha* has been defined by the act of taking from B as consisting of B and A. B-prime may not be eaten, since B-prime is not the same *mincha* as the one defined by B and A.

*Kamatz min hamufrash* — if the *kometz* was taken from B-prime: then what remains of B-prime and A may be eaten; B may not. 

### Indeterminate Kometz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָמַץ מִן רִאשׁוֹן – שְׁנֵיהֶם אֵינָן נֶאֱכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** If one **removed the handful from** the **first** half-tenth, then **both** the previously lost half-tenth as well as the half-tenth that had been separated in its stead **are not eaten.** This is because the removal of the handful allows the remainder of only one tenth to be eaten, and it is not known whether the consumption of the previously lost half-tenth or the replacement half-tenth has now been permitted.

*Kamatz min harishon* — if the *kometz* was taken from A: then *shneihem einam ne'echalim* — neither B nor B-prime may be eaten. The reason is that a *mincha* consists of precisely one *isaron*, and when the *kometz* is taken from A, it is entirely indeterminate whether B or B-prime is the second half of this *mincha*. Since the one that is *not* part of the *mincha* cannot be eaten — it is *kodesh* but not designated for this offering — and since it cannot be determined which is which, neither B nor B-prime may be consumed. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ קָמַץ מֵאֶחָד מֵהֶן, שְׁנֵיהֶן אֵינָן נֶאֱכָלִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? כּוּלְּהוּ נָמֵי בְּנֵי בִיקְתָּא דַּהֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Even if** one **removed the handful from** any **one of them, both** remaining half-tenths **are not eaten. What is the reason? They are all residents of one cabin,** and it is not possible to know whether the consumption of one or of the other has been permitted.

Abaye added his characteristic stringency here as well: *afilu kamatz me'echad meihem shneihem einam ne'echalim* — even when the *kometz* is taken from B or from B-prime, neither B nor B-prime may be eaten, because *kulhu bnei bikta dehadadi ninhu* — all three are members of one household, and B-prime retains its indeterminate status regardless of whether the *kometz* was taken from B.

### Deficient Kometz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: וְשִׁירַיִם דִּידֵיהּ מִיהָא נֶאֱכָלִין? הָא אִיכָּא דַּנְקָא דְּקוֹמֶץ דְּלָא קָרֵיב! מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: וְקוֹמֶץ גּוּפֵיהּ הֵיכִי קָרֵיב? הָא אִיכָּא תְּלָתָא חוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this** ruling of Abaye: **And** is that to say that **in any event** the **remainder of** the half-tenth itself from which the handful was taken **is eaten? But one-sixth [*danka*] of** the **handful** that was removed **was not sacrificed** to permit this remainder. The handful was removed to permit the consumption of the remainders of all three half-tenths of an ephah in the receptacle. Since the handful included one-third that was removed to account for the half-tenth that is not needed, it turns out that each of the two actual half-tenths should have had an additional one-sixth removed to render them permitted. **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Mesharshiyya,** also **objects to this** ruling of Abaye: **And** with regard to **the handful itself, how can it be sacrificed? But one-third** of it, i.e., the portion separated to permit the extraneous half-tenth of an ephah, **is non-sacred.**

Rav Papa raised a difficulty: if A and B-prime and B are all treated as part of the same *mincha* due to their household unity — with B and B-prime in an indeterminate relationship to the *mincha* — then the *kometz* taken from A effectively represents three piles. The *kometz* ought to work for A and whichever of B or B-prime is the true second half, but it cannot simultaneously represent three halves when the *mincha* only consists of two. One could think of it as follows: one-third of the *kometz* is working for A, one-third for B, and one-third for B-prime — but only two of those thirds are legitimate, since only two halves constitute the actual *mincha*. That means a third of the *kometz* is not legitimately accounted for, which represents a *kometz* that is effectively only two-thirds of what it should be. Expressed differently: a sixth of B and a sixth of B-prime are not properly covered by this *kometz*, so there is a portion of the offering — approximately a sixth — that cannot validly be offered up. Rav Papa son of Rav Masharshiya sharpened the question further: it is not merely the *shirayim* that are problematic — *kometz gufeh heichi karev?* — how can the *kometz* itself be offered at all? *Ha'ika tilta d'chulin* — one-third of it is *chulin*, representing whichever of B or B-prime is not actually part of the *mincha*.

### Kohen's Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: קוֹמֶץ בְּדַעְתָּא דְּכֹהֵן תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא, וְכֹהֵן כִּי קָמֵיץ – אַעִשָּׂרוֹן קָא קָמֵיץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** These questions present no difficulty, since with regard to the removal of the **handful, the matter is dependent on the intention of** the **priest. And when** the **priest removes the handful, he removes it to** permit the remainder of **the tenth** of an ephah, and not the remainder of the extraneous half-tenth. Still, the other two halves may not be eaten because it is not possible to know whether the consumption of one or of the other has been permitted.

Rav Ashi resolved both difficulties: *kometz b'da'atei d'kahana talya milta* — the *kometz* is determined by the intent of the *kohen*, and *ki kamatz isaron ka kamatz* — when the *kohen* takes the handful, he is taking it as a *kometz* from a single *isaron*. It is true that the identity of the second half of the *isaron* cannot be objectively determined — is it B or B-prime? — but the *kohen* is not taking a *kometz* from an *isaron* and a half. His intent is to serve whichever *isaron* this actually is, however indeterminate that may appear from the outside. We do not say the *kometz* is working for three piles, thereby invalidating a third of itself. Rather, because the *kohen's* intent governs the *kometz*, and his intent is squarely for one *isaron*, the *kometz* is valid — and the question of which half is B and which is B-prime does not fractionate the *kometz* or render part of it *chulin*.
