# Bava Kamma 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meitav & Negligence

### Opening Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהִזִּיק חָב הַמַּזִּיק לְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי נֶזֶק בְּמֵיטַב הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **When** an animal or item one is responsible to safeguard **causes damage, the one liable for the damage** caused by insufficiently safeguarding it **is obligated to pay payments** of restitution **for damage with** his best-quality **land.**

We arrive at Daf 10 having just completed several pages devoted to the concept of *meitav* — the form of payment required when one owes compensation for damages — and we now turn to what will occupy the bulk of the *masecheta*: the direct question of *mamon hamazik*, when one's property or one's own actions cause damage and under what circumstances liability follows. The previous *mishna* was notably cryptic, its language archaic and seemingly quite old, and it concluded by repeating the refrain of the very first *mishna* of the tractate: *kshe-hizik chav hamazik leshalem tashlumei nezek mimeitav ha'aretz* — when animals or property cause damage, one pays with the best of the land.

### Negligence Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁחַבְתִּי בִּשְׁמִירָתוֹ – הִכְשַׁרְתִּי אֶת נִזְקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **anything** for **which I became responsible for safeguarding it** to prevent it from causing damage, if it in fact causes damage, it is considered as if **I** actively **facilitated that damage,** and accordingly I must pay for it.

The *mishna* framed this principle with a striking formulation: *chavti bishmirato hichsharti nizko* — when I was obligated to watch and I was negligent and did not watch, it is as though I laid the groundwork for the damage that would occur. This framing bears on a fundamental conceptual question that runs throughout the *masecheta*: is one liable because one's animal or property did the damage, or is one liable because one's negligence brought it about? The *mishna* clearly seems to emphasize the latter — that one has ultimately prepared and enabled the damage.

### Fire Exemption Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹר וּבוֹר שֶׁמְּסָרָן לְחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, וְהִזִּיקוּ – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּם. מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּאֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **an ox or a pit that one transferred to** the care of **a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor,** whose presumed limited intellectual capacity means they are deemed incapable of sufficiently safeguarding them from causing damage, **and** the ox or pit **caused damage,** the *halakha* is that since the owner of the ox or pit did not fulfill his duty to safeguard them, he is **liable to pay** for the damage, **which is not so in** a corresponding case where the damage is caused by **a fire.**

The *Gemara* used this emphasis on negligence as an occasion to examine a related question: the case in which one gives over an ox or a well, or a fire, to a *cheresh shoteh ve-katan* — a deaf-mute, a mentally incompetent person, or a minor — as custodians. In such a case, one is more liable for the ox and the well than for the fire. This was the subject of a debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, which we will encounter more fully in approximately two weeks in a dedicated *sugya*. For now, the brief summary is that Reish Lakish holds the *cheresh shoteh ve-katan* is adequate custodianship for fire because if given only an ember, it will die out on its own — the inadequate custodian neither improves nor worsens the situation, and the fire extinguishes itself. Rabbi Yochanan, however, holds that even where the fire is a live flame, one would be exempt, a position we will explore in greater detail later.

## Avot Nezikin Comparison

### Shor vs Bor Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּשׁוֹר מִבְּבוֹר, חוֹמֶר בְּבוֹר מִבְּשׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** There is **a stringency that applies to** the category of **Ox as opposed to** the category of **Pit,** and conversely, there is **a stringency that applies to Pit as opposed to Ox.**

With that background in place — and keeping in mind the distinction that fire carries a more lenient rule in the *cheresh shoteh ve-katan* case — the *Gemara* proceeds to a *braita* that compares and contrasts the different *avot nezikin*, much as we have done earlier in the tractate. The *braita* opens: *chomer bashor mibabor ve-chomer babor mibashor* — there are respects in which *shor* (the ox, specifically in the context of goring) is more stringent than *bor* (the pit), and respects in which *bor* is more stringent than *shor*.

### Shor Weightier Points

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּשׁוֹר מִבְּבוֹר – שֶׁהַשּׁוֹר מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַכּוֹפֶר, וְחַיָּיב בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים שֶׁל עֶבֶד, נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ – אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וְדַרְכּוֹ לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַזִּיק; מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The **stringency that applies to Ox as opposed to Pit** is **that** if **the ox** kills a Jew, the owner is liable to **pay a ransom** to the victim's heirs. **And for** killing **a slave** the owner of the ox **is liable** to pay **thirty** *sela* to the slave's master. Furthermore, in such a case, once the court hears the evidence and the **verdict of** the ox **is complete** and the court rules that the ox must be killed, it is **prohibited to derive** any **benefit** from the ox. **And** from then, it is considered that **the** typical **manner** of the ox is **to proceed and cause damage.** All of **this is not so in** the case of **a pit** that caused damage.

The ways in which *shor* is weightier than *bor*: first, *she-shor meshalem et hakofer* — if a *mu'ad* ox gores a person and that person dies, the owner pays *kofer*, an atonement payment. By contrast, if a person falls into a well and dies, there is no *kofer* payment; the *kofer* applies only when an ox or similar animal kills a person, not when a person dies in a pit. Second, *ve-chayav bishloshim shel eved* — if the ox kills a slave, the owner pays the fixed penalty of thirty *shekel* of silver. Again, this does not apply when a well causes a slave's death. Third, *nigmar dino asur behana'ah* — there is the category of *shor haniskal*: if an ox has been judged by a court to have killed someone, it is forbidden in benefit even before it is stoned, from the very moment its judgment is rendered. This status of *shor haniskal* does not apply to any other category.

### Shor Characteristics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדַרְכּוֹ לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַזִּיק
>
> **Translation:** **And** from then, it is considered that **the** typical **manner** of the ox is **to proceed and cause damage.**

In terms of its character, *shor* is also weightier because *darko leilech ulehazik* — it goes out and creates damage; damage does not merely come to it, as is the case with the stationary pit.

### Bor Weightier Points

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּבוֹר מִבְּשׁוֹר – שֶׁהַבּוֹר תְּחִילַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתוֹ לְנֵזֶק, וּמוּעָד מִתְּחִילָּתוֹ; מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּשׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** And the **stringency,** i.e., the reason to be stringent, **that applies to Pit as opposed to Ox** is **that** with regard to **the** primary category of **Pit, its initial formation,** e.g., its digging, **is** done in a manner that can **result in damage, and** the one responsible for it **is** considered **forewarned from its inception. This is not so with regard to Ox.**

The ways in which *bor* is weightier than *shor*: *shehabor techilat asiyato lenezek* — the well, from the very moment it was created, is a hazard. An ox is born as a harmless calf; its entire identity is not from the outset that of a danger. Furthermore, *umuad mitechilato* — in contrast specifically to the goring category, where a *tam* ox does not have an established propensity to gore and therefore pays only half-damages because the harm is unanticipated, the well from its very beginning has the propensity to cause damage, and the harm it causes is to be expected. *Techilat asiyato lenezek* thus captures both that the well's identity is inherently hazardous and that the damage it causes is foreseeable. None of these things are true of *shor*.

### Shor vs Fire Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּשׁוֹר מִבְּאֵשׁ, חוֹמֶר בְּאֵשׁ מִבְּשׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** There is **a stringency that applies to** the category of **Ox as opposed to Fire,** and conversely, there is **a stringency that applies to Fire as opposed to Ox.**

The *braita* now turns to comparing *shor* to *esh* (fire), examining both directions: *chomer bashor mibe'esh* and *chomer be'esh mibashor*.

### Shor Fire Penalties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּשׁוֹר מִבְּאֵשׁ – שֶׁהַשּׁוֹר מְשַׁלֵּם כּוֹפֶר, וְחַיָּיב בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים שֶׁל עֶבֶד, נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ – אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, מְסָרוֹ לְחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן – חַיָּיב; מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּאֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The **stringency that applies to the** category of **Ox as opposed to** the category of **Fire** is **that** if **an ox** kills a Jew the owner is liable to **pay a ransom, and for** killing **a slave** the owner is **liable** to pay **thirty** *sela*. Furthermore, in such a case, once the court hears the evidence and the **verdict of** the ox **is complete** and the court rules that the ox must be killed, it is **prohibited to derive** any **benefit** from the ox. And if **one transfers** his ox **to** the care of **a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor,** and it causes damage, he is **liable.** All of **this is not so with regard to a fire.**

The ways in which *shor* is weightier than *esh*: as established, these will always be the monetary payments triggered by death — *kofer* for a free person, the thirty-*shekel* payment for a slave, and the status of *shor haniskal*. All of these apply only to the *keren* category and not to fire. In addition — and here the *braita* introduces something new that ties back to our earlier discussion — *mesaro lacheresh shoteh vekatan chayav*: if one gave an ox to a *cheresh shoteh ve-katan* to watch, one remains liable, because even if the ox is bound, it is likely to struggle free, and such custodianship is therefore insufficient. This is not so for fire — *ma she'ein ken be'esh* — where the *cheresh shoteh ve-katan*'s custody is deemed adequate. The characteristic *darko leilech ulehazik* was not listed as a distinction here because fire too has the character of *darko leilech ulehazik* — it spreads and creates damage — and so that cannot serve as a point of contrast.

### Fire Weightier Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּאֵשׁ מִבְּשׁוֹר – שֶׁהָאֵשׁ מוּעֶדֶת מִתְּחִילָּתָהּ, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּשׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** And the **stringency that applies to Fire as opposed to Ox is that** the one responsible for **the fire is** considered **forewarned from its inception, which is not so with regard to an ox.**

The ways in which *esh* is weightier than *shor*: *she'esh mu'edet mitechilatah* — fire has a propensity to cause damage from its very inception, *ma she'ein ken beshur*, which is not true of *shor* in the goring category. An ox is not presumed from the outset to have the propensity to gore; the first time it gores it pays only half-damages. Fire, by contrast, is considered *mu'ad* from its very first occurrence.

### Bor vs Fire Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּאֵשׁ מִבְּבוֹר, וְחוֹמֶר בְּבוֹר מִבְּאֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** There is **a stringency that applies to** the category of **Fire as opposed to** the category of **Pit,** and conversely, there is **a stringency that applies to Pit as opposed to Fire.**

Having compared *shor* to *bor* and *shor* to *esh*, the *braita* now addresses the one remaining pair: *bor* versus *esh*.

### Bor Fire Inherent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּבוֹר מִבְּאֵשׁ – שֶׁתְּחִילַּת עֲשִׂיָּיתוֹ לְנֵזֶק, מְסָרוֹ לְחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן – חַיָּיב; מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּאֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The **stringency that applies to** the category of **Pit as opposed to** the category of **Fire** is **that its initial formation,** e.g., its digging, **is** done in a manner that can **result in damage,** and if **one transfers it to** the care of **a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor** and it causes damage, one is **liable. This is not so with regard to a fire.**

The ways in which *bor* is weightier than *esh*: *shetechilat asiyato lenezek* — the pit, from its inception, is a hazard. Lighting a fire in one's own property is not analogous to placing a pit in the public domain; the pit's very placement constitutes the danger. And again, *mesaro lacheresh shoteh vekatan chayav* — one remains liable when handing over custodianship of a well to a *cheresh shoteh ve-katan*, because even a covered well requires responsible oversight to ensure the cover does not break. By contrast, for fire — *ma she'ein ken be'esh* — one is exempt when giving a *gachelet* (ember) to a *cheresh shoteh ve-katan*.

### Fire Unfit Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹמֶר בְּאֵשׁ מִבְּבוֹר – שֶׁהָאֵשׁ דַּרְכָּהּ לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַזִּיק, וּמוּעֶדֶת לֶאֱכוֹל בֵּין דָּבָר הָרָאוּי לָהּ וּבֵין דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לָהּ; מָה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The **stringency that applies to Fire as opposed to Pit** is **that the** typical **manner of a fire** is **to proceed and cause damage. And** the one responsible for **it is** considered **forewarned** with regard **to** its **consuming both something that is fitting for it and something that is not fitting for it,** i.e., both flammable and non-flammable items. **This is not so with regard to a pit.**

The ways in which *esh* is weightier than *bor*: *she'esh darchah leilech ulehazik* — fire goes out and creates damage, unlike the pit to which things must come. And further, *umu'edet le'echol bein davar hara'ui lah bein davar she'eino ra'ui lah* — fire has a propensity to consume both things that are fit for it and things that are not fit for it, which is *ma she'ein ken bebor*, not the case by the pit. The meaning of *davar she'eino ra'ui lah* will require unpacking, and the *Gemara* will return to it shortly.

## Keilim Exemptions

### Keilim Omission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי חוֹמֶר בְּשׁוֹר מִבְּבוֹר – שֶׁהַשּׁוֹר חִיֵּיב בּוֹ אֶת הַכֵּלִים, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּבוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** the *baraita* also **teach** the following additional stringency: **A stringency that applies to Ox as opposed to Pit** is **that** if **the ox** damages vessels, the ox's owner **is liable** to pay **for the vessels, which is not so with regard to a pit,** which incurs liability for its owner only for damage it causes to people and to animals but not to vessels.

At this point the *Gemara* raises a challenge: why did the *braita* omit a significant distinction between *shor* and *bor*? The *Gemara* asks: *u'litnei chomer beshor mibabor she-shor chayav bo es hakeilim ma she'ein ken bebor* — an ox is liable for damages to *keilim* (vessels), whereas a well is exempt from liability for *keilim*. This exemption derives from the famous *derasha* on the verse *venafal shamah shor o chamor*: *shor velo adam, chamor velo keilim* — the Torah's specification of "ox or donkey" teaches that one is liable for animals but not for persons (in the death context), and for donkeys but not for vessels. The *shor velo adam* is less intuitive — if a person is injured by falling into a well, one would of course be liable for injury — and that passage concerns the exemption from *kofer* in the death case. But the more commonly discussed exclusion is *chamor velo keilim*: if vessels are damaged in a pit, the owner of the pit is exempt. Since *shor* is liable for *keilim* and *bor* is exempt, why was this not listed as a way in which *shor* is weightier?

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דִּמְחַיֵּיב עַל נִזְקֵי כֵלִים בְּבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita* taught? **It is** taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who deems** one **liable** also **for damage** caused **to vessels by** his **pit.**

The *Gemara* answers: *ha mani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this *braita* reflects the view of Rabbi Yehuda, *de-michayev al nizkei keilim bebor*, who holds that one is in fact liable for vessel damage caused by a pit and rejects the *derasha* that would exempt *keilim*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, there is no exemption for *keilim* in *bor*, and so the distinction cannot be listed.

### Unfit Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דָּבָר הָרָאוּי לָהּ״ מַאי נִינְהוּ – עֵצִים; ״דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לָהּ״ מַאי נִינְהוּ – כֵּלִים; ״מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּבוֹר״. אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הָא אָמְרַתְּ מְחַיֵּיב הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה עַל נִזְקֵי כֵלִים בְּבוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Something that is fitting for it, what are these?** Pieces of **wood. Something that is not fitting for it, what are these? Vessels.** And yet the *baraita* concludes: **This is not so with regard to a pit,** which indicates that liability is not incurred for damage done to vessels by one's pit. The Gemara presents the difficulty: **If** the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, didn't you say** that **Rabbi Yehuda deems** one **liable for damage** caused **to vessels by** his **pit?**

But the *Gemara* now uses this to probe the meaning of *davar she'eino ra'ui lah*. The line in the *braita* says fire is weightier than *bor* because it consumes things unfit for it, *ma she'ein ken bebor*. The *Gemara* asks: *davar hara'ui lah mai nihu* — what is normally fit for fire? *Eitzim* — wood and ordinary fuel. *Davar she'eino ra'ui lah mai nihu* — what is unfit? *Keilim* — vessels. Fire does not normally burn vessels in the way it burns wood, yet if it does, one is liable. And this is in contrast to *bor*, where *keilim* are exempt. So this line is understood to be making the same *keilim* distinction: one is liable for vessel damage caused by fire, exempt for vessel damage by a pit. If the *braita* is going according to Rabbi Yehuda, however, and Rabbi Yehuda holds that even *bor* is liable for *keilim*, then that distinction disappears and this line no longer makes sense.

### Incomplete List Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם רַבָּנַן הִיא, וּתְנָא וְשַׁיַּיר. מַאי שַׁיַּיר דְּהַאי שַׁיַּיר? שַׁיַּיר טָמוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, actually,** the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and** the additional stringency mentioned is true but was not mentioned because the *tanna* **taught** certain cases **and omitted** others. The Gemara asks: **What** else **did he omit** that makes it reasonable to assume **that he omitted this?** As a *tanna* would never omit just a single case, perforce there must be more. The Gemara explains: He also **omitted** the case of damage done to **a concealed** item. Liability is incurred for damage done to a concealed item only when it was caused through a category of damage other than fire. If it is damaged by a fire that one lit, he is exempt.

The *Gemara* therefore offers an alternative: *ella lerabbanan vetannei veshayar* — the *braita* follows the Rabbanan, and *davar she'eino ra'ui lah* does refer to *keilim*, but the list is simply incomplete. It omitted the *keilim* distinction in the *shor-bor* comparison even though it existed. The *Gemara* then immediately asks, as it always does when told a list is incomplete: *mai shayar de-hai shayar* — what else did it leave off? Because one cannot posit an incomplete list that omitted only a single item.

The *Gemara* answers: *shayer tamun* — it also omitted *tamun*, buried or hidden items. This is a useful occasion for a brief review: each category of *avot nezikin* has its own special exemption. *Shen veregel* is exempt in the public domain. *Bor* is exempt for *keilim*. *Esh* is exempt for *devarim hamitmanim* — things that are buried or hidden. If a fire burns something concealed in a haystack, the owner of the fire is exempt, whereas if an ox walks on something buried in a haystack, the owner is liable. So *shor* is weightier than *esh* in the *tamun* case as well, and this too was omitted from the list. The list thus left off at minimum both *keilim* and *tamun*, which justifies characterizing it as incomplete.

### Tosafot Unlikely Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְ״דָבָר שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לָהּ״ לָאו לְאֵתוֹיֵי כֵּלִים, אֶלָּא לְאֵתוֹיֵי לִיחֲכָה נִירוֹ וְסִכְסְכָה אֲבָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another explanation of the *baraita*: **If you wish, say** that **actually** the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and** explain that the term: **Something that is not fitting for it,** in the *baraita*, **does not** serve **to include vessels;** with regard to vessels there is no distinction between Fire and Pit. **Rather,** it serves **to include** a case where a fire **scorched** another person's **plowed field or singed [*sikhsekha*] his stones,** which are ways of causing damage that cannot be caused by a pit.

The *Gemara* also — in a second answer, *iba'it eima leolam Rabbi Yehuda* — returns to the possibility that the *braita* follows Rabbi Yehuda. In that case, since Rabbi Yehuda holds one is liable for *keilim* even in *bor*, *davar she'eino ra'ui lah* cannot refer to *keilim*. What then does it mean? The *Gemara* explains: *lav le'atuyei keilim ella le'atuyei likhukha d'niro vesikhsukha d'avnav* — it comes to include a case where fire scorches someone's fallow field or singes their rocks. This is *she'eino ra'ui lah* — unfit for it — in the sense that it is an unlikely thing for fire to damage significantly.

This raises two questions, both of which Tosafot addresses. First, why is damage to a fallow field or rocks considered *she'eino ra'ui lah*? Second, what is the contrast with *bor* — what is the *ma she'ein ken bebor*?

On the first question: Tosafot explains that it is not that fire cannot spread to a fallow field, but that it is very unlikely for fire to cause meaningful damage to rocks or to a field that lacks standing produce or significant fuel. A fallow field may contain incidental growth from dropped seeds or other random vegetation, but there is little for a fire to catch onto in a serious way, and rocks by their nature absorb little damage. Yet even in such a case, if fire does cause damage, one is liable.

On the second question, Tosafot addresses the contrast with *bor*. Rashi appears to suggest the comparison is that a fallow field cannot fall into a well — but Tosafot dismisses this as an absurd basis for comparison. One could say the same of countless things that cannot fall into a well; you do not compare liability in possible cases to liability in impossible ones. Instead, Tosafot explains the contrast as follows: if a well is only nine *tefachim* deep, it is not categorized as capable of causing death, only damage. If an animal nonetheless dies in a nine-*tefach* well, one is exempt — because one is not liable for damage that was not normally to be anticipated from that particular pit. By contrast, fire is liable even for unlikely damage — damage to rocks and fallow fields that fire would not normally affect. Thus *ma she'ein ken bebor*: the pit exempts for unanticipated damage, whereas fire does not. This resolves the difficulty without needing to invoke *keilim* at all, and it allows the *braita* to work even according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *bor* is liable for *keilim*.

### Psulei Mukdashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִיתְנֵי חוֹמֶר בְּשׁוֹר מִבְּבוֹר – שֶׁהַשּׁוֹר חִיֵּיב בּוֹ שׁוֹר פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין, מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּבוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** If so, **let** the *baraita* also **teach** the following additional stringency: **A stringency that applies to Ox as opposed to Pit** is **that in the** primary category of **Ox one is liable** if his ox damages another's **ox** that is in the category of **disqualified consecrated** animals, i.e., an animal set aside to be an offering that was disqualified from use and then redeemed. He is liable despite the fact that even after being redeemed it retains a degree of sanctity. **This is not so with regard to** damage caused by **a pit** to a disqualified consecrated animal, as in that case he is not liable for the damage caused.

Rav Ashi raises a further challenge. He notes an additional distinction that was omitted: *she-hashor chayav bo shor psulei hamukdashim ma she'ein ken babor* — an ox is liable if it gores an ox that was once consecrated as a *korban* but became disqualified and was redeemed, known as *shor psulei hamukdashim*. Such an animal remains one's property — one may slaughter and eat it, though with certain restrictions — and if another ox gores it, the damaging owner is liable. Yet if this same animal falls into a pit, the owner of the pit is exempt. Why was this not listed as another way in which *shor* is weightier than *bor*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבָּנַן הִיא, אַיְּידֵי דְּשַׁיַּיר הָךְ – שַׁיַּיר נָמֵי הָךְ. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מַאי שַׁיַּיר דְּהַאי שַׁיַּיר?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi explains his objection: **Granted, if you say** that the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** then **since** the *tanna* **omitted that** case of damage done to vessels, **he also omitted this** case of damage done to disqualified consecrated animals. **But if you say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with **Rabbi Yehuda, what** else **did he omit** that makes it reasonable to assume **that he omitted this?**

The *Gemara* responds that if the *braita* follows the Rabbanan — as in the earlier answer — this presents no difficulty: *aidi de-shayer hacha shayer nami hacha*, since we have already established the list is incomplete, having omitted *keilim*, *tamun*, and now *psulei hamukdashim* as well. The list is simply abbreviated and there is no need to account for every omission. But if the *braita* follows Rabbi Yehuda, this becomes problematic, because Rabbi Yehuda not only accepts liability for *keilim* in *bor* but also — as the *Gemara* notes — rejects the exemption for *tamun* in *esh*. Under Rabbi Yehuda's view, the only thing actually omitted is *psulei hamukdashim*, and one cannot posit an incomplete list that left off only a single item.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁיַּיר דָּשׁ בְּנִירוֹ. אִי מִשּׁוּם דָּשׁ בְּנִירוֹ – לָאו שִׁיּוּרָא הוּא, דְּהָ[קָ]תָנֵא: שֶׁכֵּן דַּרְכּוֹ לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַזִּיק!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He omitted** the case of an ox that intentionally **tramples on a plowed field of** another person in order to cause damage. Since the damage was intentional it is included in the primary category of Goring and so one is liable. This manner of causing damage cannot be done by a pit. The Gemara rejects this: **If** one claims that the *tanna* omitted the case of damage done to disqualified consecrated animals only **due to** the fact that he also omitted the case of an ox that intentionally **tramples on a plowed field of** another person, that is not a sufficient justification. The latter case **is not an** additional independent **omission, as it is** included in that which is **taught** in that *baraita*: The stringency that applies to Fire as opposed to Pit is **that the** typical **manner of a fire** is **to proceed and cause damage.** The fact that there is no case in the category of Pit corresponding to an ox that intentionally trampled on a plowed field is addressed by this clause.

The *Gemara* considers whether *dash binira* — an ox that inadvertently plows a fallow field — might serve as another omitted item to justify an incomplete list under Rabbi Yehuda. Rashi characterizes this as *keren*, describing it as intentional-type damage because the ox is habituated to plowing. However, the *Gemara* rejects this answer: *de-tana she-ken darko leilech ulehazik* — the distinction that an ox goes out and creates damage, while the pit can only damage things that come to it, already encompasses the idea that an ox damages things incapable of moving to it, such as a field. The *dash binira* case is already subsumed within *darko leilech ulehazik* and adds nothing new. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the *braita* does not work according to Rabbi Yehuda; the *maskana* is that it follows the Rabbanan.

## Partial Negligence

### Partial Preparation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִכְשַׁרְתִּי בְּמִקְצָת נִזְקוֹ – חַבְתִּי בְּתַשְׁלוּמֵי נִזְקוֹ כְּהֶכְשֵׁר כׇּל נִזְקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In any case in which **I facilitated part of the damage it** caused, **I am liable for payments** of restitution **for damage it** caused, **as** if I were the **one who facilitated the entire damage it** caused.

The *Gemara* now turns to the second cryptic phrase in the *mishna*: *hichsharti bemiktzat nizko chavti betashlumei nizko ke-hachshar kol nizko* — even if I prepared only part of the damage, I am liable for all of it, as though I had prepared the entire damage. This is the principle of partial preparation carrying full liability.

### Two Diggers Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִכְשַׁרְתִּי מִקְצָת נִזְקוֹ – חַבְתִּי בְּתַשְׁלוּמֵי נִזְקוֹ כְּהֶכְשֵׁר כׇּל נִזְקוֹ. כֵּיצַד? הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר תִּשְׁעָה, וּבָא אַחֵר וְהִשְׁלִימוֹ לַעֲשָׂרָה – הָאַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a *baraita* that elucidates the mishna's ruling: In any case in which **I facilitated part of the damage it** caused, **I am liable for payments** of restitution **for damage it** caused, **as** if I were the **one who facilitated the entire damage it** caused. **How so?** In the case of **one who digs a pit** to a depth of **nine** handbreadths, **and another** person **comes** along **and completes** the digging **to** a depth of **ten** handbreadths, the depth at which a pit, according to *halakha*, can cause death, only **the latter** individual is **liable** for injuries and death caused by the pit. Although the pit was already able to cause injury before the second individual deepened it, since by deepening it he increased its capacity to cause damage, he becomes liable for any damage it causes.

The *braita* that fleshes out this principle is as follows: *ha-chofer bor tisha* — Reuven digs a pit nine *tefachim* deep. A nine-*tefach* pit is liable for damage but not for death, as we have established. *U-va acher ve-hishlimo le-asara* — Shimon then comes and digs the tenth *tefach*, completing the pit. If an animal dies in the well, it is obviously only Shimon's responsibility, since Reuven's nine-*tefach* pit was not capable of causing death. But the *chiddush* concerns damage: one might reasonably assume Reuven and Shimon should split the liability for *nezek*, since Reuven already created something capable of causing damage. The *braita* teaches otherwise: *ha'acharon chayav* — the last one is fully liable, even for *nezek*. This ruling is *v'd'lo k'Rabbi* — not in accordance with Rabbi.

### Rabbi vs Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי – דְּתַנְיָא: הַחוֹפֵר בּוֹר תִּשְׁעָה, וּבָא אַחֵר וְהִשְׁלִימוֹ לַעֲשָׂרָה – אַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אַחַר אַחֲרוֹן לְמִיתָה, אַחַר שְׁנֵיהֶם לִנְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And this is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who digs a pit** to a depth of **nine** handbreadths, **and another** person **comes** along **and completes** the digging **to** a depth of **ten** handbreadths, only **the latter** individual is **liable** for both injuries and death caused by the pit. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: With regard to death** caused by the pit, the responsibility is **ascribed to the latter** individual. **With regard to damage** caused by the pit, the responsibility is **ascribed to the two of them.**

A separate *braita* records Rabbi's position: *achar acharon l'misa, v'achar shneihem l'nezikin* — the last one is indeed fully liable for death, but for damage they are both liable. The *tanna kamma*, by contrast, holds that the last one is fully liable even for *nezek*. The *Gemara* cites Tosafot's reference to a *pasuk*: *ki yichre ish bor* — one person digs the pit — *echad ve-lo shnayim*, one and not two, meaning that even when two people dig, there are cases in which only one bears the full liability.

### Transformation Principle

Why should the last person bear full liability for *nezek*, rather than sharing it proportionally with Reuven? The key, as Tosafot explains, is the concept of *transformation*. The liability falls entirely on Shimon not simply because he did the last act, but because his act qualitatively transformed the nature of the pit. Reuven created a *bor* of *nezek* — a pit categorized as capable of causing damage. Shimon transformed it into a *bor* of *misa* — a pit categorized as capable of causing death. Because Shimon changed its fundamental identity, the pit is now entirely identified as his, and he is liable for everything it does, including *nezek*.

This contrasts with a case where two people each dig equal amounts that remain within the same category. If one person digs four *tefachim* and another adds four more, both contributing to a *bor* of *nezek* without either transforming it into a different category, they would both be liable. The *chiddush* applies only when the last person changes the pit's identity from one legal category to another. This is the only scenario in which the principle *ki yichre ish bor — echad ve-lo shnayim* operates to assign sole liability to the last actor.

One might ask about intermediate cases — say, one person digs eight *tefachim* and another adds one. The answer to this connects to the deeper conceptual models underlying the entire discussion. If one understands liability as rooted in *ownership* of the *bor*, then once both parties participate in digging, they are like joint owners and would split equally regardless of proportion, just as joint owners of an ox each bear liability for what that ox does. If, however, one understands liability as rooted in *negligence and fault*, then proportional contribution becomes relevant, and one who dug eight *tefachim* might bear greater liability than one who dug only one. These models — ownership versus negligence — are precisely the two frameworks the *mishna* has been gesturing toward throughout, and they carry real practical consequences for how liability is distributed in joint-causation scenarios.

### Liability Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: לְמִיתָה, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** The *baraita* refers only to a digger's liability **for death** caused by a pit, **and** then the ruling of the *baraita* is **unanimous,** i.e., it is in accordance with the opinions of both the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

Rav Pappa offers a clarification: *lemisa divrei hakol* — with respect to death, all agree that the last person who deepened the pit to ten *tefachim* is fully liable, even though Reuven's nine *tefachim* laid the groundwork. This is less of a *chiddush* since Shimon is the one who created the death-capable pit, though it is still noteworthy that even the partial groundwork Reuven laid does not make him share in the death liability at all. For *nezikin*, that is where the debate lies: the *tanna kamma* holds the last one is fully liable, while Rabbi holds they split it.

## Contributory Cases

### Zeira Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָא אִיכָּא: מָסַר שׁוֹרוֹ לַחֲמִישָּׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם, וּפָשַׁע בּוֹ אֶחָד מֵהֶן – וְהִזִּיק, חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira objects to** the explanation of the *baraita*, that the mishna is referring only to one specific case: **But are there no more** cases? **But isn't there** the case of one who **transferred his ox to five individuals** in order for them to safeguard it, **and one of them was negligent** in his duties **and** the ox **caused damage?** Isn't this individual **liable** for all the damage? This seems to be an additional example of the principle in the mishna that if one facilitated part of the damage caused, he is liable for payments of restitution for the damage caused, as if he were the one who facilitated the entire damage, so the *baraita* should have mentioned it.

Rabbi Zeira challenges: *v'tu leka* — are there really no other cases where one does only a little and is nevertheless fully liable for everything? He proposes cases that should also fit the *mishna*'s principle, and then subjects them to scrutiny.

### Five Watchers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דִּבְלָאו אִיהוּ לָא הֲוָה מִינְּטַר, פְּשִׁיטָא דְּאִיהוּ קָעָבֵיד! אֶלָּא דִּבְלָאו אִיהוּ נָמֵי מִינְּטַר? מַאי קָעָבֵיד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the possibility that the mishna could be referring to this case: **What are the circumstances** in which the negligent watchman bears full liability? **If we say that without him** the ox **would not have been** properly **safeguarded** because the ox was particularly strong and it took all five individuals to safeguard it, it **is obvious** that the negligent individual is liable for all the damage. The reason is **that** he alone, through his negligence, **caused** all the damage, not just part of it. **Rather,** the case must be **where** even **without him** the ox **would still have been** sufficiently **safeguarded.** The Gemara asks: But if that is the case, **what did he do** by not safeguarding it? It was still safeguarded without him, so he should not be liable for even part of the damage. It is apparent, then, that the mishna is not referring to this case.

The first case: *masar shor le-chamisha bnei adam* — one entrusts an ox to five people to guard together. *U-pasha bo echad mehen* — one of the five is negligent, and because of his negligence the ox escapes and causes damage. Is the one negligent party fully liable? The *Gemara* deconstructs this into two scenarios. If *bilav ihu lo havi mintar* — without him, the remaining four would not have been sufficient to guard the ox — then *peshita*, of course he is liable; his negligence was entirely responsible for the lapse in guarding. That is not a novel case. If, on the other hand, *bilav ihu nami mintar* — actually one person would have been sufficient and the five were simply redundant — then *ma ka'avid*, what has the negligent one actually done? His departure did not genuinely expose the ox to danger, since four capable people remained. In that case, the departure cannot be characterized as *peshi'ah* at all, because the effective level of guarding was not diminished by his absence. Either scenario thus fails to generate the interesting case the *mishna* contemplates — it is either obvious or it is simply not a genuine case of liability.

### Fire Addition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לָהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: וְהָא אִיכָּא מַרְבֶּה בַּחֲבִילָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet objects to** the explanation of the *baraita*, that the mishna is referring only to one specific case: **But isn't there** also the case of a fire that was left unattended by its owner and someone else **augmented** the fire **by** adding **a bundle** of wood to it, thereby increasing the capacity of the fire to cause damage to another's field? Even though he only increased the fire's capacity to damage, he is liable for any damage it causes. Seemingly, this is an additional example of the mishna's principle that if one facilitated part of the damage caused, he is liable for payments of restitution for the damage caused, as if he were one who facilitated the entire damage, so the *baraita* should have mentioned it.

Rav Sheshet offers a second case: *ha'ika marbe be-chavila* — what about one who adds a bundle of wood to someone else's fire, and the fire then spreads and causes damage? The same analysis applies. If *bilav ihu lo azla* — without the additional wood the fire would not have spread — *peshita*, it is obviously his fire now. But if *bilav ihu azla* — the fire would have spread regardless — *mai ka'avid*, what did he actually contribute?

### Tosafot Fire Analysis

Tosafot pushes back on the simple reading of *mai ka'avid* as meaning the additional party is entirely exempt. Tosafot holds that one cannot simply say: the fire was going to spread anyway, so I bear no responsibility for having thrown more wood on it. Tosafot cites the case from the next *perek* where one person digs a pit to ten *tefachim*, a second deepens it to twenty, and a third deepens it to thirty — all are liable, even though the animal would have died at ten. So too, one who adds fuel to a spreading fire should bear some liability.

Yet Tosafot is genuinely uncertain about the threshold. He asks: *v'chi bishvil she-hishlich ish etz be-toch esh gedola yitchayev* — if Michael has a huge bonfire and I throw my toothpick into it, does it become equally my fire? That is clearly not the intent. Tosafot observes that this is analogous to one person digging a pit to ten *tefachim* and another deepening it to ten thousand; the trivial contribution cannot generate equal liability. But Tosafot does not resolve where the threshold lies — at what point does one's addition of fuel make it sufficiently "one's fire" to generate full or proportional liability? These questions are left open. The underlying tension, as noted above, is between an ownership model — who owns this fire? — and a negligence model — whose act actually brought about the damage? By *bor*, the ownership model is more tractable because a pit stays in place and can be quantified; by fire, which spreads dynamically, the negligence or causation model may be more appropriate, and causation analysis is sensitive to whether the damage would have occurred regardless of one's contribution.

## Bench Weight Case

### Six on Bench

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לָהּ רַב פָּפָּא, וְהָא אִיכָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁיָּשְׁבוּ עַל סַפְסָל אֶחָד וְלֹא שְׁבָרוּהוּ, וּבָא אֶחָד וְיָשַׁב עָלָיו וּשְׁבָרוֹ – הָאַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב. וְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּגוֹן פָּפָּא בַּר אַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to** the claim of the *baraita* that the mishna refers only to one specific case: **But isn't there** also **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a case in which **five** people **were sitting on one bench [*safsal*] and it did not break, and** then **one** additional person **came and sat upon it and broke it** with his added weight, **the latter** individual is **liable** for all the damage. **And Rav Pappa said** by way of clarification that this applies in a case **where** the last individual to sit down was as heavy as **Pappa bar Abba.** Since he could have potentially broken it even on his own, he had no right to use it. In this case, even though the weight of the first five individuals was presumably a contributing factor in causing the damage, since the damage was ultimately caused by the additional weight of the last individual, he is liable for all of the damage. Seemingly, this is an additional example of the mishna's principle, and the *baraita* should have mentioned it.

Rav Papa introduces a *braita* rather than a thought exercise: *chamisha she-yasvu al safsal echad ve-lo shivruhu, u-va echad ve-yashav alav ve-shivruhu* — five people sat on a bench and it did not break; a sixth person sat on it and it broke. *Ha'acharon chayav* — the last one is fully liable. Rav Papa notes the case is like *Papa bar Abba*, evidently a notably weighty individual, whose additional mass caused the bench to collapse.

### Bench Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דִּבְלָאו אִיהוּ לָא אִיתְּבַר – פְּשִׁיטָא. אֶלָּא דִּבְלָאו אִיהוּ נָמֵי אִיתְּבַר – מַאי קָעָבֵיד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **What are the circumstances? If we say that without him** the bench **would never have broken** under the weight of the first five people, then it **is obvious** that the last individual is liable for all the damage, as ultimately it was his action alone that caused the damage. **Rather,** it must be that **even without him** the bench **would have broken** under the weight of the first five people, and the last individual sat down just as it was about to break. The Gemara asks: But if that is the case, **what did he do,** i.e., why should he be liable at all? It is apparent, then, that the mishna is not referring to this case.

The *Gemara* poses the familiar challenge: what exactly is the scenario? If *bilav ihu lo itbar* — without him the bench would not have broken — then *peshita*, obviously the sixth person is liable, he exceeded the weight limit. If *bilav ihu nami itbar* — the bench was already going to break with just five people — then *ma ka'avid*, what did the sixth person do?

### Acceleration Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא; דִּבְלָאו אִיהוּ הָוֵי מִיתְּבַר בִּתְרֵי שָׁעֵי, וְהַשְׁתָּא אִיתְּבַר בַּחֲדָא שָׁעָה; דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אִי לָאו אַתְּ – הָוֵי יָתְבִינַן טְפֵי פּוּרְתָּא, וְקָיְימִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where without him it would have broken in two hours, and now it broke in one hour.** The *baraita* teaches that the last individual alone is liable and not the first five, **as they can say to** the last individual: **Were it not for you, we would have sat a little bit more and** then **stood up;** consequently, the bench would never have broken. Therefore, it was ultimately you who caused the bench to break, and therefore only you are liable.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *de-bilav ihu hava itbar bi-trei sha'ei ve-hashta itbar be-chada sha'a* — without the sixth person the bench would have broken in two hours, but with him it broke in one hour. He accelerated the damage. Now this is a genuine *chiddush*: one might argue that the first five people, by sitting on an overloaded bench, were already doing something wrong and bear responsibility. The sixth person sat down in circumstances where damage was already foreseeable. Why should he bear full liability? The *Gemara* explains that the five could argue: *ilu lav at haveinan yasvinan tutei purta ve-kaiman* — had you not arrived, we would have gotten up before the bench broke. During that first hour, nothing would have happened; only because you came did the break occur within this hour. So it is your *nezek*, not ours.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לְהוּ: אִי לָאו אַתּוּן – בְּדִידִי לָא הֲוָה מִיתְּבַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion, because in that situation the last individual would have a valid counterclaim: **But let him say to them: Were it not for you** continuing to sit on the bench after I sat down, the bench would not have broken, as **under my** weight alone **it would not have broken.** Accordingly, we should share the liability for damaging it.

The sixth person might counter: *ilu lav atun bididi lo hava itbar* — if not for you being here with me, it would not have broken. The *Gemara* accepts this counter-argument as valid, and reverses itself: since the first five were already in a situation where the bench would eventually break, and they were not getting up, they too bear responsibility. The *Gemara* understands their continued sitting — even in the first hour where nothing would yet break — as participatory in the eventual damage, because they are sitting in circumstances under which breakage could occur. The bench is, in a sense, being damaged incrementally with every moment of excess weight. All parties are therefore liable.

### Holding Down

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּבַהֲדֵי דְּסָמֵיךְ בְּהוּ, תְּבַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers a different suggestion: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where** instantaneously, **as he was leaning upon** the other five people, **the bench broke.**

The *Gemara* then presents a distinct case: *de-samach behu tavar* — what if one does not sit on the bench but instead leans on or holds down one of the five people, preventing them from rising, so that the bench breaks under the weight of the five? Here the principle invoked is *kocho kegufoy dami* — one's force or energy is equivalent to one's body. The person who prevented another from rising is liable, even though it was not his own body weight that broke the bench, but rather the weight of the person he restrained. The *chiddush* is that preventing someone from getting up — rather than adding one's own weight — is treated as an exercise of force legally equivalent to one's own bodily action. There is genuine uncertainty in the *rishonim* about whether Rashi means the person was actively pushing down or simply blocking the other from standing; if the latter, the case is a more dramatic *chiddush* because the restraining person added no weight at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, כֹּחוֹ – לָאו כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּכֹחוֹ כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי, דְּכֹל הֵיכָא דְּגוּפוֹ תָּבַר – כֹּחוֹ נָמֵי תָּבַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The ruling is necessary **lest you say** that when one causes damage with **one's direct force** it **is not equivalent to** a situation where one causes damage with **one's body.** If he broke the bench by actually sitting down upon it, his action would be considered a direct act of damage completed with his body and he alone would be liable even though the other peoples' weight was a contributing factor. In this case, since he broke the bench by merely leaning upon the others sitting there, it is his force that led to the damage, not his body, and one might have thought that since the weight of the others certainly contributed to the breakage they should share liability. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us that** causing damage with **one's direct force is equivalent to** causing damage with **one's body.** And it teaches that the *halakha* is **that anywhere that** one would be liable if **his body broke** something, one is **also** liable if **his force broke** something.

### Kocho Principle

The principle is articulated as follows: *de-heikha de-gufey tavar, kocho nami tavar* — wherever one's body would have broken it, one's *koach* — one's force or energy — also breaks it. One is liable through *kocho* in the same way one is liable through direct bodily action.

## Multiple Beatings

### Ten Beatings Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָא אִיכָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: הִכּוּהוּ עֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם בְּעֶשֶׂר מַקְלוֹת, בֵּין בְּבַת אַחַת בֵּין בָּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, וָמֵת – כּוּלָּן פְּטוּרִין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא אוֹמֵר: בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – הָאַחֲרוֹן חַיָּיב, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקֵּירַב אֶת מִיתָתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to consider the possibility that there are additional cases covered by the mishna's ruling aside from the one listed in the *baraita*: **And are there not more** cases? **But isn't there** also the case of **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **was beaten by ten people with ten sticks, whether** they beat him **simultaneously or one after the other, and he died, they are all exempt** from liability for killing him. **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says:** Where they beat him **sequentially, the last** individual to beat him alone **is liable, because he hastened his death.** In this case, the other individuals contributed to the man's death, but the last one alone is liable. Why didn't the *baraita* also mention this case?

The *braita* presenting the final case: *hiku'u asarah b'nei adam be-asarah maklot, bein be-vas achas bein be-zeh achar zeh, u-met, kulam peturim* — ten people beat a victim with sticks, either simultaneously or sequentially, and the victim dies. All are exempt. Since each person contributed only a partial, non-lethal blow, no individual can be held fully liable for the death.

### Sequential Causation

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira dissents: *be-zeh achar zeh ha'acharon chayav mipnei she-keirev misaso* — if the beatings were sequential, the last person is liable because he brought about the death. Everyone else had beaten the victim to within an inch of his life; the last blow closed that final gap. The last person is therefore the proximate cause of death. This is the principle of *keirev misaso* — bringing about death — as a basis for sole liability even when others contributed.

### Gemara Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּקְטָלָא לָא קָמַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The *baraita* **is not speaking of** one's liability to receive **the death penalty,** only of one's liability to pay damages.

The *Gemara* clarifies why the *mishna*'s earlier case — the tenth *tefach* of the pit — need not be seen as reflecting Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira's minority view. The *mishna* there concerns *nezikin*, financial liability, not capital punishment; the *masechet* is not *b'ktala ka mairy*, not dealing with the death penalty context in which Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira's ruling would be relevant. Furthermore, *d'plugta lo ka mairy* — the *Gemara* will not establish a *mishna* as following a minority position. The earlier ruling that the one who dug the tenth *tefach* is liable even for *nezek* follows the Rabbanan against Rabbi; establishing that ruling requires invoking a majority position. But to have the *mishna* follow Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira against the Rabbanan — *lo mukminan* — that we will not do.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בִּפְלוּגְתָּא לָא קָמַיְירֵי. וְלָא?! וְהָא אוֹקֵימְנַן דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי! דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי – וּכְרַבָּנַן, מוֹקְמִינַן; כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא – וְלָא כְּרַבָּנַן, לָא מוֹקְמִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the *baraita* **is not speaking of** an issue that is the subject of **a dispute.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't** it? **But didn't we uphold that** the case stated in the *baraita* concerning a pit is subject to a dispute, and **it is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, but of the Rabbis? The Gemara explains: **We will interpret** the *baraita* to be **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis and not** to be **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, but **we will not interpret** it to be **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and not in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.** In other words, although we will interpret the *baraita* as referring to a case that is subject to a dispute, that applies only if it is in accordance with the majority opinion in that dispute.

### Contributory Summary

These final *sugyot* bring to a close a recurring theme throughout the first *perek*: the problem of contributory negligence and last-actor liability. When are all parties equally exempt, as in the ten-beatings case? When is the last party alone fully liable, as in the tenth-*tefach* case? When should liability be distributed proportionally, and when equally? When does a trivial contribution trigger no liability at all, and when does even a small act — the sixth person on the bench, the one who deepens a pit by a single *tefach* — carry full responsibility? These questions recur in various forms throughout the *masecheta* and will receive much fuller treatment in the *sugyot* to come.
