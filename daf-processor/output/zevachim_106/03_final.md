# Zevachim 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Parim HaNisrafim

### Three Camps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר – חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת, כָּאן – לְמַחֲנֶה אַחַת; לוֹמַר לָךְ: כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּצָא חוּץ לְמַחֲנֶה אַחַת – מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** As the Sages taught in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the bull and goat that are burned on Yom Kippur: "They shall be carried forth outside the camp" (Leviticus 16:27). **There,** elsewhere, the verse **states** that such bulls and goats are burned **outside three camps,** those of the Tabernacle, the Levites, and the Israelites, whereas **here,** the verse states only that they are taken outside **one camp,** i.e., the Tabernacle. This serves **to tell you: Once** the offering **emerges beyond one camp,** one who carries it **renders** his **garments impure.**

The *daf* opens by returning to the *mishnah* treating the *parim hanisrafim* and *se'irim hanisrafim* — the *korbanot* burned outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The Torah establishes that those who perform the burning, along with their garments, contract *tumah*. The *mishnah* taught that this *tumah* takes effect upon exiting the *azarah*: those carrying the front of the animal became *tamei* upon leaving, while those carrying the back became *tamei* only once they too had fully exited. Rabbi Shimon dissented, holding that *tumah* is generated not by the exit from the *Mikdash* but by actual participation in the burning itself.

The *Gemara* now asks: *mina hani mili* — what is the source for the ruling that *tumah* commences upon departure from the *azarah*? A *braita* provides the answer through a comparison: *lehalan*, in the earlier passages in *Vayikra* dealing with the *par kohen gadol* and the *par helam davar shel tzibur*, the Torah specifies that the burning takes place outside of three camps. Yet *kan*, in the passage treating the *par v'se'ir* of *Yom Kippur*, the Torah says only *el michutz lamachaneh* — outside of one camp. The purpose of this juxtaposition is to teach that while the actual burning must occur outside all three camps — *machaneh shechinah* (the *Beit HaMikdash*), *machaneh leviyah* (the *Har HaBayit*), and *machaneh yisrael* (Yerushalayim) — the *tumah* is incurred as soon as one exits even the first camp, the *azarah* itself.

### Redundant Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִיא גּוּפַהּ מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהוֹצִיא אֶת כׇּל הַפָּר אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ – חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מַחֲנֶה אַחַת? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר בְּפַר הָעֵדָה: ״מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ – שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר שָׂרַף אֵת הַפָּר הָרִאשׁוֹן״; לִיתֵּן לוֹ מַחֲנֶה שְׁנִיָּה. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ בַּדֶּשֶׁן – שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״עַל שֶׁפֶךְ הַדֶּשֶׁן יִשָּׂרֵף״; לִיתֵּן לוֹ מַחֲנֶה שְׁלִישִׁית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains further: **And from where do we** derive **that** *halakha* **itself,** that the bulls and goats are burned outside the three camps? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It is stated about the bull brought as a sin offering of the High Priest: **"Even the whole bull shall he carry outside the camp** unto a pure place, where the ashes are poured out, and burn it" (Leviticus 4:12), meaning that he should take it **outside** the **three camps. Do you say** that he takes it **outside** the **three camps, or** is he required to take it outside **only one camp?** **When** the verse **states with regard to the bull** brought as **a communal** sin offering: "He shall carry the bull **outside the camp,** and burn it as he burned the first bull" (Leviticus 4:21), it requires explanation, **as there is no need** for the verse **to state** "outside the camp," **since it is already stated** at the end of that same verse: **"And burn it as he burned the first bull,"** which indicates that all the *halakhot* of the bull brought as a sin offering of a High Priest apply to the bull brought as a communal sin offering. What then does the verse mean when it states "outside the camp"? **To give it a second camp,** i.e., it indicates that it must be removed not only from the camp of the Divine Presence, corresponding to the Temple, but also from the Levite camp, corresponding to the Temple Mount. And **when** another verse **states with regard to** the removal of **the ash:** "And he shall put off his garments, and put on other garments, and carry forth the ashes **outside the camp** to a pure place" (Leviticus 6:4), that verse also requires an explanation, **as there is no need** for the verse **to state** this, **since it is already stated** with regard to the bull brought as a sin offering of a High Priest: "Even the whole bull shall he carry outside the camp to a pure place, where the ashes are poured out, and burn it on wood with fire; **where the ashes are poured out shall it be burned"** (Leviticus 4:12). The repetition of "outside the camp" indicates that he is required **to give it a third camp,** i.e., teaching that it is burned when outside the Israelite camp, corresponding to the land outside the walls of Jerusalem.

This raises an immediate question: where is the source in the earlier *Vayikra* passages establishing that the burning is required outside all three camps? The *Gemara* addresses this through a careful analysis of redundant language. The *pasuk* reads: *v'hotzi et kol hapar el michutz lamachaneh el makom tahor el shefech hadeshen v'saraf oto al etzim ba'esh al shefech hadeshen yisaref.* One might have thought that *michutz lamachaneh* here refers to only one camp. But three separate instances of *michutz lamachaneh* appear in different contexts, each one adding an additional *machaneh*.

The first instance appears in the passage of the *par kohen gadol* — *el michutz lamachaneh* — indicating one camp. A second *michutz lamachaneh* appears in the passage of the *par helam davar shel tzibur*, which explicitly connects its burning to the *par kohen gadol* with the phrase *ka'asher saraf et hapar harishon*. Since the connection was already established, the repetition of *el michutz lamachaneh* is superfluous unless it is to add a second camp. A third *michutz lamachaneh* appears in the *tzav* section in the context of *terumat hadeshen* — the removal of ashes. Since the *parim* are already known to be burned *al shefech hadeshen*, the *pasuk* need not have repeated *michutz lamachaneh*; its repetition therefore adds a third camp. The combined three instances thus establish that the burning must occur outside all three camps. The single *michutz lamachaneh* of *Yom Kippur* cannot add a fourth camp — it therefore teaches instead that *tumah* attaches even upon exiting the first camp.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – הַאי ״מִחוּץ לְמַחֲנֶה״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״חוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״חוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״; מָה לְהַלָּן – חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת, אַף כָּאן – חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת. וּמָה לְהַלָּן – לְמִזְרָחָהּ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what does Rabbi Shimon do with this** phrase, stated with regard to the bull and goat of Yom Kippur: **"Outside the camp"** (Leviticus 16:27), given that he holds that the garments do not become impure until the offering is burning? The Gemara answers: **He requires it for** that **which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: It is stated here: "Outside the camp," and it is stated there,** with regard to the red heifer: "He shall bring it **outside the camp"** (Numbers 19:3). **Just as here,** the bull and goat of Yom Kippur are burned **outside three camps, so too there,** the red heifer is burned **outside three camps. And just as there,** the red heifer is burned **east of Jerusalem,** since it must be burned "toward the front of the Tent of Meeting" (Numbers 19:4), opposite the entrance of the Temple, which is to its east,

For Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *tumah* is created at the moment of burning rather than upon exit, the repeated *michutz lamachaneh* of *Yom Kippur* cannot serve the function the *chachamim* assign to it. So what does Rabbi Shimon do with this language? The *Gemara* answers by citing a *braita*: Rabbi Eliezer teaches that the *michutz lamachaneh* by the *par v'se'ir* of *Yom Kippur* is meant to be linked via a *gezeirah shavah* to the *michutz lamachaneh* found in the passage of the *parah adumah* — *v'hotzi otah el michutz lamachaneh v'shachat otah lefanav*. The purpose of this linkage is to connect the place of burning of the *parim hanisrafim* to the place where the *parah adumah* ritual is performed. Just as the *parah adumah* is performed outside three camps, so too must the burning of the *parim hanisrafim* occur outside three camps.

### Location Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף כָּאן – לְמִזְרָחָהּ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן הֵיכָא שָׂרֵיף לְהוּ? כִּדְתַנְיָא: הֵיכָן נִשְׂרָפִין? לִצְפוֹן יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **so too here,** the bull and goat of Yom Kippur are burned **east of Jerusalem. But according to the Rabbis,** then, **where** outside Jerusalem **do they burn them?** The Gemara responds: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Where are** the bulls and goats **burned?** They are burned **north of Jerusalem, outside of** the **three camps.**

This linkage to the *parah adumah* has a further consequence: it establishes the geographic location of the burning. The *parah adumah* is performed to the *east* of Yerushalayim, because the *kohen* must sprinkle the blood toward *petach ohel moed*, and since that opening faces east, the *parah adumah* site must lie further to the east. Rabbi Eliezer's *gezeirah shavah* would therefore place the burning of the *parim hanisrafim* to the east of Yerushalayim as well, as stated explicitly at the top of 106a.

The *chachamim*, however, who use *michutz lamachaneh* to derive the timing of *tumah* and do not apply the *gezeirah shavah* to the *parah adumah*, maintain a different location. A *braita* teaches on their view: the burning takes place to the *north* of Yerushalayim, outside the three camps. Rashi explains — though the *Gemara* itself does not state it explicitly — that since these *korbanot* are *chataot*, and *chataot* are associated with the north side of the *mizbe'ach*, the natural location for their burning outside the city is also to the north.

### Ashes Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: אַבֵּית הַדֶּשֶׁן נִשְׂרָפִין. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאן תַּנָּא דִּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״עַל שֶׁפֶךְ הַדֶּשֶׁן יִשָּׂרֵף״ – שֶׁיְּהֵא שָׁם דֶּשֶׁן (שֶׁיַּקְדִּים לְשָׁם דֶּשֶׁן). רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: שֶׁיְּהֵא מְקוֹמוֹ מְשׁוּפָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: They are burned on the place of the ashes,** where the ashes from the altar were poured. **Rava said: Who is the *tanna* who disagrees with Rabbi Yosei HaGelili? It is Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to bulls that are burned: **"Where the ashes are poured out [*shefekh hadeshen*] shall it be burned"** (Leviticus 4:12). This teaches **that ashes must be there** already when the bulls are burned. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says:** This verse teaches **that its place should slope [*meshupakh*]** downward so that ashes from the burning will slide downhill.

Within this locational discussion, the *Gemara* considers the opinion of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, who states that the *parim hanisrafim* are burned *el beit hadeshen* — at the place where ashes are poured. This seems to merely restate the *pasuk*, which already says *al shefech hadeshen yisaref*. Rava therefore frames the question as: who disagrees with Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, and in doing so clarifies what the *chiddush* must be.

The answer is Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. A *braita* cites two positions. The first holds that *al shefech hadeshen* means ashes must *literally* be present at the site — the ashes removed from the *mizbe'ach* must already be there when the *parim* are burned. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov disagrees and holds that what is required is not actual ashes but an inclined terrain, such that ashes *could* be poured there. Abaye suggests a third possibility: perhaps the two opinions do not actually contradict each other, and Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov means that in *addition* to requiring ashes, the location must also feature a slope. The *Gemara* does not formally resolve the question, leaving open whether the opinions are mutually exclusive or complementary.

### Tumah at Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשּׂוֹרֵף מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים, וְלֹא הַמַּצִּית אֶת הָאוּר מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים, וְלֹא הַמְסַדֵּר אֶת הַמַּעֲרָכָה מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים. וְאֵיזֶהוּ הַשּׂוֹרֵף? הַמְסַיֵּיעַ בִּשְׁעַת שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the bull and goat of Yom Kippur: "And he who burns them shall wash his garments" (Leviticus 16:28). This teaches that only **the one who burns** them **renders** his **garments impure, but the one who kindles the fire does not render** his **garments impure, and the one who sets up the arrangement** of wood **does not render** his **garments impure. And who is** considered **the one who burns? One who assists at the** actual **time of burning.**

The *Gemara* now presents a *braita* that, in the manner of Rabbi Shimon, specifies when precisely *tumah* is generated for those involved in the burning. *Hasoref metamei begadim velo hamatzit et ha'or* — the one who burns contracts *tumah begadim*, but not the one who merely ignites the fire. And certainly not *hamasader hama'arachah* — the one who lays out the wood. Only active participation during the burning itself renders a person *tamei*: *hamisayei bish'at sreifah* — one who assists while the burning is in progress, whether by placing the animal on the fire or stoking the flames.

### Charcoal vs. Ash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אַף מִשֶּׁנַּעֲשׂוּ אֵפֶר מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹתָם״ – אוֹתָם מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים, וּמִשֶּׁנַּעֲשִׂין אֵפֶר אֵין מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אוֹתָם מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים, נִיתַּךְ הַבָּשָׂר אֵין מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רָבָא: אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּשַׁוְּיֵיהּ חָרוֹכָא.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that this priest **renders** his **garments impure even after** the bull and goat **become ash.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And he who burns **them"** (Leviticus 16:28), teaching that **they,** the whole bull and goat, **render garments impure, but they do not render garments impure once they become ash. Rabbi Shimon says:** The word "them" teaches that **they render garments impure,** but once **the flesh is incinerated they do not render garments impure.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the opinion of the first *tanna* and the opinion of Rabbi Shimon? **Rava said:** The difference **between them is when he turned it into a charred** mass, and the form of the animal has become distorted, but has not actually become ash.

The *braita* continues: one might think that even after the animal has fully turned to ash, there would still be *tumah begadim* for those involved. The Torah says *hasoref otam* — it is still *otam*, them, the original thing. Once the animal has become ash, it is no longer *otam*, no longer the same entity, and therefore *tumah begadim* no longer applies.

Rabbi Shimon adds a nuance: *otam metameim begadim nitach habasar ein metameim begadim* — once the flesh has congealed and hardened into a charred mass, it is no longer *metamei*. The *Gemara* asks: *mai beineihu* — what is the practical difference between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon? Rava answers: *ika beineihu deshavyei charucha* — the case of charcoal. If the flesh has become charcoal — no longer the original flesh, but not yet ash — the *tanna kamma*, who requires full reduction to ash before *tumah* ends, would still count this as *metamei begadim*, while Rabbi Shimon, who requires only that the flesh lose its character as *otam*, would already exempt the participants from *tumah begadim*.

The *hadran* of *Parim HaNisrafim* is recited at this point, marking the conclusion of the chapter.

## Shechutei Chutz

### Two Prohibitions

The *perek* opens an entirely new *halacha* — a new *perek* — addressing the *issurim* of bringing *korbanot* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. This *perek* is called *hashocheit v'hama'aleh bachutz*, and it deals with two distinct prohibitions: *shechutei chutz*, the act of slaughtering a sanctified animal outside the *Mikdash*, and *ha'alat chutz*, the act of burning or offering it up on an outside altar.

### Torah Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט וְהַמַּעֲלֶה בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב עַל הַשְּׁחִיטָה, וְחַיָּיב עַל הַעֲלָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who slaughters** an offering outside the Temple courtyard **and one who offers** it **up outside** the Temple courtyard is **liable for the slaughter and liable for** the **offering up,** as each act involves an independent prohibition. If done intentionally, he is liable to receive excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*] for each act, and if done unwittingly, he is liable to bring a sin offering for each act.

The *pesukim* underlying these laws come from *Vayikra* 17. Verse 3 reads: *ish ish m'beit Yisrael asher yishchat shor o chesev o ez bamachaneh o asher yishchat michutz lamachaneh v'el petach ohel mo'ed lo hevi'o l'hakriv korban la-Hashem lifnei mishkan Hashem dam yeichashev la'ish hahu dam shafach v'nichras ha'ish hahu mikerev amo.* The case described is one where an animal was sanctified as a *korban* but was slaughtered outside, without being brought to the *ohel mo'ed*. This constitutes *shechutei chutz* and carries the punishment of *kares*.

Verse 8 then addresses *ha'alat chutz*: *ish ish m'beit Yisrael u'min hager asher yagur b'socham asher ya'aleh olah o zavach v'el petach ohel mo'ed lo yavi'ennu la'asos oso la-Hashem v'nichras ha'ish hahu me'ammav.* Here, the operative word is *ya'aleh* — he who *offers up* outside — making clear that burning or placing the sacrifice on an outside altar, as distinct from the *shechita*, is also independently punishable with *kares*.

### Two Aveirot

The *mishnah* rules: *hashocheit v'hama'aleh bachutz chayav al hashechita v'chayav al haha'ala* — one who slaughters and offers outside is liable separately for each act. Since *shechita* and *ha'ala* are two independent prohibitions, even if both were performed within a single lapse of awareness (*b'shogeig*), two separate *korbanot* must be brought.

### Rabbi Yosi HaGlili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: שָׁחַט בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ. שָׁחַט בַּחוּץ וְהֶעֱלָה בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר, שֶׁלֹּא הֶעֱלָה אֶלָּא דָּבָר פָּסוּל. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף הַשּׁוֹחֵט בִּפְנִים וּמַעֲלֶה בַּחוּץ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהוֹצִיאוֹ – פְּסָלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says:** If **he slaughtered** an offering **inside** the courtyard **and** then **offered** it **up outside** the courtyard, he is liable. But if **he slaughtered** it **outside,** thereby rendering it unfit, **and** then **he offered** it **up outside,** he is **exempt** for the offering up, **as he offered up only an item** that is **unfit,** and one is liable only for offering up an item that is fit to be offered up inside the Temple. The Rabbis **said to him:** According to your reasoning, **even** in a case where **he slaughters** it **inside and offers** it **up outside,** he should be exempt, **since** the moment **that he took it outside** the courtyard, he thereby **rendered it unfit.** Yet, in such a case, he is certainly liable for offering it up. So too, one who slaughters an offering outside and then offers it up outside is liable.

Rabbi Yosei HaGlili introduces a qualification: *shachat bifnim v'he'ela bachutz chayav* — one who slaughters *inside* the *Mikdash* and burns *outside* is liable for *ha'alat chutz*, because the meat was fit to be offered on the *mizbe'ach* at the time of *shechita*. However, *shachat bachutz v'he'ela bachutz patur* — if one slaughters *outside* and then burns *outside*, he is liable for *shechutei chutz* but *exempt* from *ha'alat chutz*. His reasoning: once the *shechita* took place outside, the meat was never fit to be placed on the *mizbe'ach* in the first instance. The prohibition of *ha'alat chutz* presupposes meat that *could have* been legitimately offered inside; meat from an outside *shechita* is *pasul* from the outset and therefore cannot generate liability for the *ha'ala*.

A logical consequence of Rabbi Yosei HaGlili's position is that one can never be liable for both *shechita* and *ha'ala* of the same animal: liability for the *shechita* precludes liability for the *ha'ala* of that same animal, and vice versa.

### Chachamim Refute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף הַשּׁוֹחֵט בִּפְנִים וּמַעֲלֶה בַּחוּץ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהוֹצִיאוֹ – פְּסָלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him:** According to your reasoning, **even** in a case where **he slaughters** it **inside and offers** it **up outside,** he should be exempt, **since** the moment **that he took it outside** the courtyard, he thereby **rendered it unfit.** Yet, in such a case, he is certainly liable for offering it up. So too, one who slaughters an offering outside and then offers it up outside is liable.

The *chachamim* respond: *af hashocheit bifnim u'ma'aleh bachutz* — even in your own favored scenario, where one slaughters inside and burns outside, there is a problem. *Keivan she'hotzi'o pasul* — the moment the meat was taken out of the *Mikdash*, it became *pasul*. So in *every* case of *ha'alat chutz*, the meat burned outside is already unfit for the *mizbe'ach*. Since even the case Rabbi Yosei HaGlili himself considers liable involves *pasul* meat, the distinction he draws between slaughtering inside versus outside becomes untenable. The liability for *ha'alat chutz* does not depend on the meat's current fitness; it depends on whether the animal began as a sanctified *korban*.

### Eating Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַטָּמֵא שֶׁאָכַל בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ טָמֵא בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ טָהוֹר – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל טָהוֹר – חַיָּיב, וְטָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל טָמֵא – פָּטוּר, שֶׁלֹּא אָכַל אֶלָּא דָּבָר טָמֵא. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל אֶת הַטָּהוֹר, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּגַע בּוֹ – טִמְּאוֹהוּ. וְטָהוֹר שֶׁאָכַל טָמֵא – פָּטוּר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל טוּמְאַת הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **One who is ritually impure who ate** sacrificial food, **whether** it was **ritually impure sacrificial** food **or ritually pure sacrificial** food, is **liable** to receive *karet* if he did so intentionally and to bring a sliding-scale offering if he did so unwittingly. **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: An impure** person **who ate pure** sacrificial food is **liable. But an impure** person **who ate impure** sacrificial food is **exempt, as he merely ate an impure item,** and the prohibition against eating sacrificial food while one is impure applies only to pure sacrificial food. The Rabbis **said to him:** According to your logic, this *halakha* would apply **even** in a case of **an impure** person **who ate** what had been **pure** sacrificial food, because **once he touched it, he** thereby **rendered it ritually impure.** Yet, in such a case, he is certainly liable for eating it. So too, an impure person who ate impure sacrificial food is liable. **And a pure** person **who ate impure** sacrificial food is **exempt, as** one is **liable** for eating sacrificial food in impurity **only due to** the **impurity of** one's **body,** but not due to the impurity of the food.

A parallel dispute illuminates the same underlying logic. The Torah states that a *tamei* person who eats *kodashim* is liable for *kares*. However, eating a *tamei korban* carries a *lav* but not *kares*. The *tanna kamma* rules: *ha-tamei she'achal bein kodesh tamei bein kodesh tahor chayav* — if the *person* is *tamei*, he is liable for *kares* regardless of whether the *korban* itself is *tahor* or *tamei*.

Rabbi Yosei HaGlili disagrees: *tamei she'achal tahor chayav, v'tamei she'achal tamei patur* — only if the person is *tamei* and eats a *tahor korban* is there *kares*; if the *korban* was also *tamei*, there is no *kares*, because *shelo achal ela davar tamei* — he ate something that was already defiled. The parallel to the *ha'ala* debate is clear: in both cases, Rabbi Yosei HaGlili holds that liability requires the object of the prohibited act to have been in its pristine, fit state.

The *chachamim* again respond: *af tamei she'achal et hatahor — keivan shenaga bo tim'o* — even in the classic case of a *tamei* person eating a *tahor* meat, the moment he touches it he renders it *tamei*. So in every scenario that generates *kares*, the person ends up eating something that has by that point become *tamei*. Since even the case generating liability necessarily involves *tamei* meat, the distinction Rabbi Yosei HaGlili draws cannot be sustained. All agree that *tahor she'achal tamei patur* — a *tahor* person who eats *tamei* meat is exempt from *kares*, since the liability is solely tied to *tumas haguf*, the impurity of the *person*.

### Kares Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא הֶעֱלָה – כְּתִיב עוֹנֶשׁ וּכְתִיב אַזְהָרָה. עוֹנֶשׁ – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֹא הֱבִיאוֹ״. אַזְהָרָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״הִשָּׁמֶר לְךָ פֶּן תַּעֲלֶה עוֹלוֹתֶיךָ״; וְכִי הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הִשָּׁמֶר״, ״פֶּן״ וְ״אַל״ – אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if one both slaughters and offers up an offering outside the Temple courtyard, he is liable for each act, as they are independent prohibitions. The Gemara asks: **Granted** that one is liable for the **offering up,** as the **punishment** for this act **is written** in the Torah **and** the **prohibition** concerning this act **is** also **written** in the Torah. The **punishment** is **as it is written:** "Any man…that offers up a burnt offering or sacrifice, **and will not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting,** to sacrifice it to the Lord, and that man shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 17:8–9). The **prohibition** is **as it is written: "Take heed to yourself lest you offer up your burnt offerings** in every place that you see" (Deuteronomy 12:13). **And** this is **in accordance with that which Rabbi Avin says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: Wherever it is stated** in the Torah: **Observe,** or: **Lest, or: Do not, it is nothing other than a prohibition.** Accordingly, the verse in Deuteronomy is understood as issuing a prohibition.

The *Gemara* now turns to the sourcing of both the punishment and the prohibition for each of the two *aveirot*. Regarding *ha'alat chutz*, both the *onesh* and the *azhara* are readily available. The *onesh* — the punishment of *kares* — is explicit in the *pesukim* of *Vayikra* 17 cited above. The *azhara* — the prohibitory warning — is found in *Devarim*: *hishamer lecha pen ta'aleh olotecha bechol makom asher tireh* — guard yourself lest you offer up your *olot* wherever you choose. In line with the principle stated by Rabbi Avin in the name of Rabbi Elazar — *kol makom shene'emar hishamer pen v'al eino ela lo sa'aseh* — whenever the Torah uses the language of *hishamer*, *pen*, or *al*, it constitutes a negative prohibition. Thus for *ha'alat chutz*, both the *kares* and the *lo sa'aseh* are clearly established.

### Shechita Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁחִיטָה – בִּשְׁלָמָא עוֹנֶשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֹא הֱבִיאוֹ״; אֶלָּא אַזְהָרָה מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְלֹא יִזְבְּחוּ עוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** **But** for the **slaughtering,** why is one liable? **Granted** that the **punishment** is stated in the Torah, **as it is written:** "Any man…that slaughters it outside the camp, **and he did not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting,** to sacrifice an offering to the Lord, before the Tabernacle of the Lord…that man shall be cut off from among his people" (Leviticus 17:3–4). **But from where do we** derive its **prohibition?** One is liable only in a case where the Torah specifies both the prohibition and the punishment. The Gemara answers: **The verse states** in the continuation of that passage: **"And they shall not slaughter anymore** their offerings to the *se'irim* after whom they go astray" (Leviticus 17:7).

For *shechutei chutz*, the *onesh* is similarly explicit — the same *pasuk* of *v'nichras ha'ish hahu* applies. However, the *azhara*, the prohibitory warning from which liability derives, is not obvious. The *Gemara* proposes: *amar kra v'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem* — the *pasuk* states "and they shall no longer slaughter their sacrifices," which appears to function as the negative prohibition for *shechutei chutz*.

### Two Uses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר! דְּאָמַר: מִנַּיִן לַזּוֹבֵחַ בְּהֵמָה לְמַרְקוּלִיס שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא יִזְבְּחוּ עוֹד אֶת זִבְחֵיהֶם״ – אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לִכְדַרְכָּהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֵיכָה יַעַבְדוּ״; תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְשֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the use of this verse as a source: But **this** verse is **necessary for** the purpose of expounding **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Elazar, as he says: From where** is it derived with regard to **one who slaughters an animal** as an offering **to Mercury,** a pagan deity, **that he is liable** even though this is not the established manner in which that deity is worshipped? **As it is written:** "And **they shall not slaughter anymore their offerings** to the *se'irim*." **If** the verse **is not** needed to teach the **matter of** worshipping a deity **in accordance with its established manner,** as it is already taught that one is liable for this, **as it is written:** "Take heed to yourself…lest you inquire after their gods, saying: **How do these** nations **serve** their gods, so too will I do likewise" (Deuteronomy 12:30), then **apply it** to the **matter of** worshipping a deity in a way **that** is **not in accordance with its established manner.**

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: that same phrase, *v'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem*, is needed by Rabbi Elazar for a different teaching entirely — regarding *avodah zarah*. The question is how one becomes liable for *avodah zarah* when worshipping an idol in a manner that is *not* the standard form of worship for that deity. The *pasuk ikah ya'avdu hagoyim ha'eileh et eloheihem v'e'eseh chein gam ani* already prohibits performing the *classic* worship of foreign gods. The superfluous *v'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem* is therefore applied *im eino inyan l'chedarkah* — since it is unnecessary for the standard mode of idol worship — *tenehu inyan l'shelo chedarkah* — to teach that even a *shechita* offered to an idol in a non-standard manner still generates *chiyuv* of *avodah zarah*.

If the entire phrase is thus consumed by the *avodah zarah* derivation, it cannot simultaneously serve as the *azhara* for *shechutei chutz*. Rabba resolves this by proposing a double reading: *krei beih v'lo yizbechu, u'krei beih v'lo od* — one should read the phrase in two stages. *V'lo yizbechu* — do not slaughter — serves as the *azhara* for *shechutei chutz*. *V'lo od et zivcheihem l'se'irim* — and no longer offer your sacrifices to the goat-gods — addresses the *avodah zarah* prohibition. The two *dinim* are derived from the same phrase read in two overlapping ways.

### Era 1: Mikdash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: קְרִי בֵּיהּ ״וְלֹא יִזְבְּחוּ״, וּקְרִי בֵּיהּ ״וְלֹא עוֹד״. אַכַּתִּי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: עַד כָּאן הוּא מְדַבֵּר בְּקָדָשִׁים שֶׁהִקְדִּישָׁן בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת וְהִקְרִיבָן בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת – שֶׁהֲרֵי עוֹנְשָׁן אָמוּר: ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״, אַזְהָרָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״הִשָּׁמֶר לְךָ פֶּן תַּעֲלֶה עֹלֹתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** Both *halakhot* can be derived from the same verse. **Read into** the verse as though it stops after the phrase: **"And they shall not slaughter"** (Leviticus 17:7), and relates to the prohibition against slaughtering outside the Temple courtyard, which was mentioned in the previous verses. **And** also **read into** the verse as relating to the verse's continuation: **And not anymore** their offerings to the *se'irim*, which serves as the source for the prohibition against sacrificing offerings to false deities. The Gemara challenges: But the verse is **still necessary for** the purpose of expounding **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "Any man…that slaughters it outside the camp, and he did not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, to sacrifice an offering to the Lord" (Leviticus 17:3–4). **Until this point,** the verse is **speaking about sacrificial** animals **that one consecrated during a period when the prohibition against** sacrificing on private **altars** was in effect, i.e., after the Tabernacle was erected, **and** then he also **sacrificed them during a period when the prohibition against** sacrificing on private **altars** was in effect. This is apparent, **as the punishment for** sacrificing **them is stated** in this verse: **"And** he did not bring it **to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting,** to sacrifice an offering to the Lord, before the Tabernacle of the Lord…that man shall be cut off from among his people." The **prohibition** is also explicit, **as it is written: "Take heed to yourself lest you offer up your burnt offerings** in every place that you see" (Deuteronomy 12:13).

The *Gemara* then raises yet another challenge: that phrase is needed for a different *braita* altogether. The *braita* begins by noting: *ad kan hu m'daber b'kodashim she'hikdisham b'sha'at issur bamot v'hikrivam b'sha'at issur bamot* — the initial *pesukim* of *Vayikra* 17 address the paradigmatic case: animals sanctified and slaughtered *during the era of the Mikdash*, when *bamot* were already forbidden. The sin consists in not bringing them *el petach ohel mo'ed*. That case generates full *kares*, as stated explicitly: *v'nichras ha'ish hahu*.

### Era 2: Bamot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – בִּזְבָחִים שֶׁהִקְדִּישָׁן בִּשְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר הַבָּמוֹת וְהִקְרִיבָן בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְמַעַן אֲשֶׁר יָבִיאוּ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת זִבְחֵיהֶם אֲשֶׁר הֵם זֹבְחִים״ – זְבָחִים שֶׁהִתַּרְתִּי לְךָ כְּבָר. ״עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה״ – לוֹמַר לָךְ: הַזּוֹבֵחַ בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ הִקְרִיב עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה. ״וֶהֱבִיאֻם לַה׳״ – זוֹ מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה. לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלֹא יִזְבְּחוּ עוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** **From that** point **onward,** the verse is speaking **about sacrificial** animals **that one consecrated during a period when** there was **permission** to sacrifice offerings on private **altars,** i.e., before the Tabernacle was erected, **and** then **he sacrificed them** outside the Tabernacle **during a period when the prohibition against** sacrificing on private **altars** was in effect. This is apparent, **as it is stated: "In order that the children of Israel shall bring their sacrifices, which they slaughter** upon the open field, that they shall bring them to the Lord, to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 17:5). The phrase "their sacrifices, which they slaughter" is interpreted as referring to **offerings that I have previously permitted for you** to slaughter on private altars. This verse teaches that those offerings may now be sacrificed only inside the Tabernacle. The phrase **"upon the open field" tells you** that in the case of **one who slaughters** an offering on a private altar **during a period when the prohibition against** sacrificing on private **altars** is in effect, even if he sacrifices the offering to God, **the verse ascribes him** blame **as if he sacrificed** it **upon the open field** in idolatrous worship. The verse continues: **"That they shall bring them to the Lord." This** is **a positive mitzva** to sacrifice in the Tabernacle even offerings that were consecrated before the Tabernacle was erected. **From where** is it derived that there is **a prohibition** against sacrificing them outside the Tabernacle? **The verse states: "And they shall not slaughter anymore** their offerings to the *se'irim* after whom they go astray; this shall be to them an eternal statute, throughout their generations" (Leviticus 17:7).

The *braita* then identifies a second scenario embedded in the subsequent *pesukim*: *v'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem asher heim zovchim al pnei hasadeh* — and they shall no longer slaughter their sacrifices that they slaughter in the field. The phrase *asher heim zovchim al pnei hasadeh* — while they are currently slaughtering in the field — describes a different historical situation: animals that were sanctified *during the era when bamot were permitted*, when bringing *korbanot* outside the central *Mikdash* was acceptable. The *braita* reads the shift in *pesukim* as addressing a case where the *Mikdash* was subsequently built. Even though the animal was sanctified *b'sha'at heter bamot*, once the *Beit HaMikdash* was erected it could no longer be offered outside. One does not get "grandfathered in." The obligation is to bring it inside: *v'hevi'um la-Hashem* — and they shall bring them to God.

### Velo Yizbechu Od

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְהֵא עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״חֻקַּת עוֹלָם תִּהְיֶה זֹּאת לָהֶם לְדֹרֹתָם״ – זֹאת לָהֶם וְלֹא אַחֶרֶת לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that sacrificing these offerings outside the Tabernacle **would be punishable** by ***karet*,** as is the *halakha* with regard to offerings consecrated after the Tabernacle was erected. Therefore, **the verse states: "This shall be to them an eternal statute, throughout their generations"** (Leviticus 17:7). One can infer from this verse that **this,** the punishment for transgressing a positive mitzva and a prohibition, applies **to them, but no other** punishment applies **to them.** It is clear from this *baraita* that the verse "And they shall not slaughter anymore" is used to teach about the prohibition against sacrificing outside the Tabernacle those offerings that were consecrated while there was permission to sacrifice on private altars, and not those offerings that were consecrated while it was prohibited to sacrifice on private altars.

The prohibition in this second scenario — that one may no longer slaughter such an animal on an outside *bamah* now that the *Mikdash* has been built — is derived from *v'lo yizbechu od*: they shall no longer continue doing so. The word *od* — "any longer" — fits this scenario with precision. There was a time when this was permitted; now that the *Mikdash* stands, it is forbidden. The *lo saaseh* of this second scenario is thus derived from *v'lo yizbechu od*, and this is the reading the *braita* requires.

### Punishment Limited

In this second scenario, if one violates the prohibition and slaughters outside or burns on an external *bamah*, what is the penalty? The *Gemara* establishes: there is a *mitzvas aseh* to bring the animal to God, and a *lo saaseh* against slaughtering it outside — derived from *v'lo yizbechu od*. But one might think *kares* applies here too. The *Gemara* rejects this: *talmud lomar chukas olam tihyeh zos lahem l'dorosim* — this shall be an eternal statute for all generations. *Zos lahem v'lo acheres lahem* — this, and not something else, applies to them. The *aseh* and the *lo saaseh* apply to this second scenario; *kares* does not.

The consequence is that *v'lo yizbechu od* is fully occupied by this second scenario — sanctified *b'sha'at heter bamot*, slaughtered *b'sha'at issur bamot* — and cannot simultaneously function as the *azhara* for the primary case of *shechutei chutz*. The *Gemara* thus leaves open the question of where the *lo saaseh* for the paradigmatic case of *shechutei chutz* — when the animal was sanctified and slaughtered entirely during the *sha'at issur bamot* — is actually derived from, carrying this question forward to the next *daf*.
