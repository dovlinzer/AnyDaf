# Zevachim 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Parim HaNisrafim

### Three Camps

The *daf* opens by returning to the *mishnah* treating the *parim hanisrafim* and *se'irim hanisrafim* — the *korbanot* burned outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The Torah establishes that those who perform the burning, along with their garments, contract *tumah*. The *mishnah* taught that this *tumah* takes effect upon exiting the *azarah*: those carrying the front of the animal became *tamei* upon leaving, while those carrying the back became *tamei* only once they too had fully exited. Rabbi Shimon dissented, holding that *tumah* is generated not by the exit from the *Mikdash* but by actual participation in the burning itself.

The *Gemara* now asks: *mina hani mili* — what is the source for the ruling that *tumah* commences upon departure from the *azarah*? A *braita* provides the answer through a comparison: *lehalan*, in the earlier passages in *Vayikra* dealing with the *par kohen gadol* and the *par helam davar shel tzibur*, the Torah specifies that the burning takes place outside of three camps. Yet *kan*, in the passage treating the *par v'se'ir* of *Yom Kippur*, the Torah says only *el michutz lamachaneh* — outside of one camp. The purpose of this juxtaposition is to teach that while the actual burning must occur outside all three camps — *machaneh shechinah* (the *Beit HaMikdash*), *machaneh leviyah* (the *Har HaBayit*), and *machaneh yisrael* (Yerushalayim) — the *tumah* is incurred as soon as one exits even the first camp, the *azarah* itself.

### Redundant Language

This raises an immediate question: where is the source in the earlier *Vayikra* passages establishing that the burning is required outside all three camps? The *Gemara* addresses this through a careful analysis of redundant language. The *pasuk* reads: *v'hotzi et kol hapar el michutz lamachaneh el makom tahor el shefech hadeshen v'saraf oto al etzim ba'esh al shefech hadeshen yisaref.* One might have thought that *michutz lamachaneh* here refers to only one camp. But three separate instances of *michutz lamachaneh* appear in different contexts, each one adding an additional *machaneh*.

The first instance appears in the passage of the *par kohen gadol* — *el michutz lamachaneh* — indicating one camp. A second *michutz lamachaneh* appears in the passage of the *par helam davar shel tzibur*, which explicitly connects its burning to the *par kohen gadol* with the phrase *ka'asher saraf et hapar harishon*. Since the connection was already established, the repetition of *el michutz lamachaneh* is superfluous unless it is to add a second camp. A third *michutz lamachaneh* appears in the *tzav* section in the context of *terumat hadeshen* — the removal of ashes. Since the *parim* are already known to be burned *al shefech hadeshen*, the *pasuk* need not have repeated *michutz lamachaneh*; its repetition therefore adds a third camp. The combined three instances thus establish that the burning must occur outside all three camps. The single *michutz lamachaneh* of *Yom Kippur* cannot add a fourth camp — it therefore teaches instead that *tumah* attaches even upon exiting the first camp.

### Rabbi Eliezer

For Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *tumah* is created at the moment of burning rather than upon exit, the repeated *michutz lamachaneh* of *Yom Kippur* cannot serve the function the *chachamim* assign to it. So what does Rabbi Shimon do with this language? The *Gemara* answers by citing a *braita*: Rabbi Eliezer teaches that the *michutz lamachaneh* by the *par v'se'ir* of *Yom Kippur* is meant to be linked via a *gezeirah shavah* to the *michutz lamachaneh* found in the passage of the *parah adumah* — *v'hotzi otah el michutz lamachaneh v'shachat otah lefanav*. The purpose of this linkage is to connect the place of burning of the *parim hanisrafim* to the place where the *parah adumah* ritual is performed. Just as the *parah adumah* is performed outside three camps, so too must the burning of the *parim hanisrafim* occur outside three camps.

### Location Debate

This linkage to the *parah adumah* has a further consequence: it establishes the geographic location of the burning. The *parah adumah* is performed to the *east* of Yerushalayim, because the *kohen* must sprinkle the blood toward *petach ohel moed*, and since that opening faces east, the *parah adumah* site must lie further to the east. Rabbi Eliezer's *gezeirah shavah* would therefore place the burning of the *parim hanisrafim* to the east of Yerushalayim as well, as stated explicitly at the top of 106a.

The *chachamim*, however, who use *michutz lamachaneh* to derive the timing of *tumah* and do not apply the *gezeirah shavah* to the *parah adumah*, maintain a different location. A *braita* teaches on their view: the burning takes place to the *north* of Yerushalayim, outside the three camps. Rashi explains — though the *Gemara* itself does not state it explicitly — that since these *korbanot* are *chataot*, and *chataot* are associated with the north side of the *mizbe'ach*, the natural location for their burning outside the city is also to the north.

### Ashes Requirement

Within this locational discussion, the *Gemara* considers the opinion of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, who states that the *parim hanisrafim* are burned *el beit hadeshen* — at the place where ashes are poured. This seems to merely restate the *pasuk*, which already says *al shefech hadeshen yisaref*. Rava therefore frames the question as: who disagrees with Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, and in doing so clarifies what the *chiddush* must be.

The answer is Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. A *braita* cites two positions. The first holds that *al shefech hadeshen* means ashes must *literally* be present at the site — the ashes removed from the *mizbe'ach* must already be there when the *parim* are burned. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov disagrees and holds that what is required is not actual ashes but an inclined terrain, such that ashes *could* be poured there. Abaye suggests a third possibility: perhaps the two opinions do not actually contradict each other, and Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov means that in *addition* to requiring ashes, the location must also feature a slope. The *Gemara* does not formally resolve the question, leaving open whether the opinions are mutually exclusive or complementary.

### Tumah at Burning

The *Gemara* now presents a *braita* that, in the manner of Rabbi Shimon, specifies when precisely *tumah* is generated for those involved in the burning. *Hasoref metamei begadim velo hamatzit et ha'or* — the one who burns contracts *tumah begadim*, but not the one who merely ignites the fire. And certainly not *hamasader hama'arachah* — the one who lays out the wood. Only active participation during the burning itself renders a person *tamei*: *hamisayei bish'at sreifah* — one who assists while the burning is in progress, whether by placing the animal on the fire or stoking the flames.

### Charcoal vs. Ash

The *braita* continues: one might think that even after the animal has fully turned to ash, there would still be *tumah begadim* for those involved. The Torah says *hasoref otam* — it is still *otam*, them, the original thing. Once the animal has become ash, it is no longer *otam*, no longer the same entity, and therefore *tumah begadim* no longer applies.

Rabbi Shimon adds a nuance: *otam metameim begadim nitach habasar ein metameim begadim* — once the flesh has congealed and hardened into a charred mass, it is no longer *metamei*. The *Gemara* asks: *mai beineihu* — what is the practical difference between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon? Rava answers: *ika beineihu deshavyei charucha* — the case of charcoal. If the flesh has become charcoal — no longer the original flesh, but not yet ash — the *tanna kamma*, who requires full reduction to ash before *tumah* ends, would still count this as *metamei begadim*, while Rabbi Shimon, who requires only that the flesh lose its character as *otam*, would already exempt the participants from *tumah begadim*.

The *hadran* of *Parim HaNisrafim* is recited at this point, marking the conclusion of the chapter.

## Shechutei Chutz

### Two Prohibitions

With the conclusion of *parim hanisrafim*, the *Gemara* opens an entirely new *halacha* — a new *perek* — addressing the *issurim* of bringing *korbanot* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. This *perek* is called *hashocheit v'hama'aleh bachutz*, and it deals with two distinct prohibitions: *shechutei chutz*, the act of slaughtering a sanctified animal outside the *Mikdash*, and *ha'alat chutz*, the act of burning or offering it up on an outside altar.

### Torah Sources

The *pesukim* underlying these laws come from *Vayikra* 17. Verse 3 reads: *ish ish m'beit Yisrael asher yishchat shor o chesev o ez bamachaneh o asher yishchat michutz lamachaneh v'el petach ohel mo'ed lo hevi'o l'hakriv korban la-Hashem lifnei mishkan Hashem dam yeichashev la'ish hahu dam shafach v'nichras ha'ish hahu mikerev amo.* The case described is one where an animal was sanctified as a *korban* but was slaughtered outside, without being brought to the *ohel mo'ed*. This constitutes *shechutei chutz* and carries the punishment of *kares*.

Verse 8 then addresses *ha'alat chutz*: *ish ish m'beit Yisrael u'min hager asher yagur b'socham asher ya'aleh olah o zavach v'el petach ohel mo'ed lo yavi'ennu la'asos oso la-Hashem v'nichras ha'ish hahu me'ammav.* Here, the operative word is *ya'aleh* — he who *offers up* outside — making clear that burning or placing the sacrifice on an outside altar, as distinct from the *shechita*, is also independently punishable with *kares*.

### Two Aveirot

The *mishnah* rules: *hashocheit v'hama'aleh bachutz chayav al hashechita v'chayav al haha'ala* — one who slaughters and offers outside is liable separately for each act. Since *shechita* and *ha'ala* are two independent prohibitions, even if both were performed within a single lapse of awareness (*b'shogeig*), two separate *korbanot* must be brought.

### Rabbi Yosi HaGlili

Rabbi Yosi HaGlili introduces a qualification: *shachat bifnim v'he'ela bachutz chayav* — one who slaughters *inside* the *Mikdash* and burns *outside* is liable for *ha'alat chutz*, because the meat was fit to be offered on the *mizbe'ach* at the time of *shechita*. However, *shachat bachutz v'he'ela bachutz patur* — if one slaughters *outside* and then burns *outside*, he is liable for *shechutei chutz* but *exempt* from *ha'alat chutz*. His reasoning: once the *shechita* took place outside, the meat was never fit to be placed on the *mizbe'ach* in the first instance. The prohibition of *ha'alat chutz* presupposes meat that *could have* been legitimately offered inside; meat from an outside *shechita* is *pasul* from the outset and therefore cannot generate liability for the *ha'ala*.

A logical consequence of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's position is that one can never be liable for both *shechita* and *ha'ala* of the same animal: liability for the *shechita* precludes liability for the *ha'ala* of that same animal, and vice versa.

### Chachamim Refute

The *chachamim* respond: *af hashocheit bifnim u'ma'aleh bachutz* — even in your own favored scenario, where one slaughters inside and burns outside, there is a problem. *Keivan she'hotzi'o pasul* — the moment the meat was taken out of the *Mikdash*, it became *pasul*. So in *every* case of *ha'alat chutz*, the meat burned outside is already unfit for the *mizbe'ach*. Since even the case Rabbi Yosi HaGlili himself considers liable involves *pasul* meat, the distinction he draws between slaughtering inside versus outside becomes untenable. The liability for *ha'alat chutz* does not depend on the meat's current fitness; it depends on whether the animal began as a sanctified *korban*.

### Eating Korban

A parallel dispute illuminates the same underlying logic. The Torah states that a *tamei* person who eats *kodashim* is liable for *kares*. However, eating a *tamei korban* carries a *lav* but not *kares*. The *tanna kamma* rules: *ha-tamei she'achal bein kodesh tamei bein kodesh tahor chayav* — if the *person* is *tamei*, he is liable for *kares* regardless of whether the *korban* itself is *tahor* or *tamei*.

Rabbi Yosi HaGlili disagrees: *tamei she'achal tahor chayav, v'tamei she'achal tamei patur* — only if the person is *tamei* and eats a *tahor korban* is there *kares*; if the *korban* was also *tamei*, there is no *kares*, because *shelo achal ela davar tamei* — he ate something that was already defiled. The parallel to the *ha'ala* debate is clear: in both cases, Rabbi Yosi HaGlili holds that liability requires the object of the prohibited act to have been in its pristine, fit state.

The *chachamim* again respond: *af tamei she'achal et hatahor — keivan shenaga bo tim'o* — even in the classic case of a *tamei* person eating a *tahor* meat, the moment he touches it he renders it *tamei*. So in every scenario that generates *kares*, the person ends up eating something that has by that point become *tamei*. Since even the case generating liability necessarily involves *tamei* meat, the distinction Rabbi Yosi HaGlili draws cannot be sustained. All agree that *tahor she'achal tamei patur* — a *tahor* person who eats *tamei* meat is exempt from *kares*, since the liability is solely tied to *tumas haguf*, the impurity of the *person*.

### Kares Source

The *Gemara* now turns to the sourcing of both the punishment and the prohibition for each of the two *aveirot*. Regarding *ha'alat chutz*, both the *onesh* and the *azhara* are readily available. The *onesh* — the punishment of *kares* — is explicit in the *pesukim* of *Vayikra* 17 cited above. The *azhara* — the prohibitory warning — is found in *Devarim*: *hishamer lecha pen ta'aleh olotecha bechol makom asher tireh* — guard yourself lest you offer up your *olot* wherever you choose. In line with the principle stated by Rabbi Avin in the name of Rabbi Elazar — *kol makom shene'emar hishamer pen v'al eino ela lo sa'aseh* — whenever the Torah uses the language of *hishamer*, *pen*, or *al*, it constitutes a negative prohibition. Thus for *ha'alat chutz*, both the *kares* and the *lo sa'aseh* are clearly established.

### Shechita Warning

For *shechutei chutz*, the *onesh* is similarly explicit — the same *pasuk* of *v'nichras ha'ish hahu* applies. However, the *azhara*, the prohibitory warning from which liability derives, is not obvious. The *Gemara* proposes: *amar kra v'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem* — the *pasuk* states "and they shall no longer slaughter their sacrifices," which appears to function as the negative prohibition for *shechutei chutz*.

### Two Uses

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: that same phrase, *v'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem*, is needed by Rabbi Elazar for a different teaching entirely — regarding *avodah zarah*. The question is how one becomes liable for *avodah zarah* when worshipping an idol in a manner that is *not* the standard form of worship for that deity. The *pasuk ikah ya'avdu hagoyim ha'eileh et eloheihem v'e'eseh chein gam ani* already prohibits performing the *classic* worship of foreign gods. The superfluous *v'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem* is therefore applied *im eino inyan l'chedarkah* — since it is unnecessary for the standard mode of idol worship — *tenehu inyan l'shelo chedarkah* — to teach that even a *shechita* offered to an idol in a non-standard manner still generates *chiyuv* of *avodah zarah*.

If the entire phrase is thus consumed by the *avodah zarah* derivation, it cannot simultaneously serve as the *azhara* for *shechutei chutz*. Rabba resolves this by proposing a double reading: *krei beih v'lo yizbechu, u'krei beih v'lo od* — one should read the phrase in two stages. *V'lo yizbechu* — do not slaughter — serves as the *azhara* for *shechutei chutz*. *V'lo od et zivcheihem l'se'irim* — and no longer offer your sacrifices to the goat-gods — addresses the *avodah zarah* prohibition. The two *dinim* are derived from the same phrase read in two overlapping ways.

### Era 1: Mikdash

The *Gemara* then raises yet another challenge: that phrase is needed for a different *braita* altogether. The *braita* begins by noting: *ad kan hu m'daber b'kodashim she'hikdisham b'sha'at issur bamot v'hikrivam b'sha'at issur bamot* — the initial *pesukim* of *Vayikra* 17 address the paradigmatic case: animals sanctified and slaughtered *during the era of the Mikdash*, when *bamot* were already forbidden. The sin consists in not bringing them *el petach ohel mo'ed*. That case generates full *kares*, as stated explicitly: *v'nichras ha'ish hahu*.

### Era 2: Bamot

The *braita* then identifies a second scenario embedded in the subsequent *pesukim*: *v'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem asher heim zovchim al pnei hasadeh* — and they shall no longer slaughter their sacrifices that they slaughter in the field. The phrase *asher heim zovchim al pnei hasadeh* — while they are currently slaughtering in the field — describes a different historical situation: animals that were sanctified *during the era when bamot were permitted*, when bringing *korbanot* outside the central *Mikdash* was acceptable. The *braita* reads the shift in *pesukim* as addressing a case where the *Mikdash* was subsequently built. Even though the animal was sanctified *b'sha'at heter bamot*, once the *Beit HaMikdash* was erected it could no longer be offered outside. One does not get "grandfathered in." The obligation is to bring it inside: *v'hevi'um la-Hashem* — and they shall bring them to God.

### Velo Yizbechu Od

The prohibition in this second scenario — that one may no longer slaughter such an animal on an outside *bamah* now that the *Mikdash* has been built — is derived from *v'lo yizbechu od*: they shall no longer continue doing so. The word *od* — "any longer" — fits this scenario with precision. There was a time when this was permitted; now that the *Mikdash* stands, it is forbidden. The *lo saaseh* of this second scenario is thus derived from *v'lo yizbechu od*, and this is the reading the *braita* requires.

### Punishment Limited

In this second scenario, if one violates the prohibition and slaughters outside or burns on an external *bamah*, what is the penalty? The *Gemara* establishes: there is a *mitzvas aseh* to bring the animal to God, and a *lo saaseh* against slaughtering it outside — derived from *v'lo yizbechu od*. But one might think *kares* applies here too. The *Gemara* rejects this: *talmud lomar chukas olam tihyeh zos lahem l'dorosam* — this shall be an eternal statute for all generations. *Zos lahem v'lo acheres lahem* — this, and not something else, applies to them. The *aseh* and the *lo saaseh* apply to this second scenario; *kares* does not.

The consequence is that *v'lo yizbechu od* is fully occupied by this second scenario — sanctified *b'sha'at heter bamot*, slaughtered *b'sha'at issur bamot* — and cannot simultaneously function as the *azhara* for the primary case of *shechutei chutz*. The *Gemara* thus leaves open the question of where the *lo saaseh* for the paradigmatic case of *shechutei chutz* — when the animal was sanctified and slaughtered entirely during the *sha'at issur bamot* — is actually derived from, carrying this question forward to the next *daf*.
