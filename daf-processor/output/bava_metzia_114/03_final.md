# Bava Metzia 114 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mesadrim L'Baal Chov

### Intro to Mesadrim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבְרָא עֲלֵיהּ קָרְמֵי, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Indeed,** the debtor's needs are **cast upon him, because it is stated** in connection with this same issue of returning the collateral: **"And it shall be righteousness to you"** (Deuteronomy 24:13), which indicates that there is an obligation for the creditor to act toward the debtor with righteousness.

The daf picks up at the bottom of 113b, in the midst of the *mitzvos* related to seizing collateral — the prohibition against entering a debtor's home, the obligation to return a poor person's collateral on a regular basis, and related rules. The Gemara then introduces the central topic that will occupy much of today's daf: *mesadrim l'baal chov* — the obligation to make arrangements, or as Rashi and Tosafot suggest, to leave things over for the debtor. The question is: when the time comes to collect a debt, must the creditor leave the debtor some basic necessities to live on, or may he take everything?

*Mesadrim l'baal chov* answers that he must leave a basic minimum. The source is *erchin* — the specific class of vow to the *Beis HaMikdash* in which the Torah fixes the amount owed based on the age and gender of the person valued. The Torah also provides that if the person who made such a vow cannot afford the fixed amount, the *kohen* assesses his means and determines how much he can afford, and that adjusted figure becomes his obligation. From this law of *erchin* we learn the concept of assessing a basic subsistence floor that must be left to the person. The application to ordinary loans is importantly different from bankruptcy: if a debtor owes ten thousand dollars and we determine he can afford to pay five thousand, he still owes the remaining five thousand. We are not canceling the debt — we are simply not stripping him of the bare minimum needed to live.

### Kol Yisrael Bnei Melachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּכִי קָאָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל זַבּוֹנֵי – לִגְמָרֵי קָאָמַר? דִּלְמָא הָכִי קָאָמַר: עַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם [מַ]הְדַּר לֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ מַיְהְדַּר לֵיהּ לְמַאי דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ, וּמְזַבְּנִינַן מַאי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where** is it known **that when Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said** that he **sells** the collateral, he **was saying** that there may be **a complete** sale? **Perhaps this** is what he **is saying: Until thirty days,** the creditor **returns** it **to** the debtor **as is; from that** point **onward,** the creditor **returns to him that which is fit for him, and we sell what is not fit for him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַאי סְבָרָא, לֵיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ. דְּאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ: כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּנֵי מְלָכִים הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **If it enters your mind** that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel maintains this reasoning, there is nothing that is unfit for him. As Abaye said: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva all hold** that **all Israel are the children of kings.** In other words, a Jew is never deemed unfit to use a certain item, even if it is a luxury item.

The Gemara raises a challenge from Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who ruled in the Mishnah that if a creditor has taken a poor person's cloak as collateral, he must return it each day — but after thirty days he may sell it and apply the proceeds to the debt. This seems to imply that one need not leave the debtor anything at all: *ein mesadrim l'baal chov*.

The Gemara attempts to reconcile this by suggesting that under the framework of *mesadrim*, one would sell an expensive garment and replace it with a simpler one. But this attempt runs into a serious obstacle. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, along with other *Tannaim*, subscribes to the principle of *kol Yisrael bnei melachim hein* — all Jews carry a dignity befitting royalty. Under that principle, if a person has been going about in a silver-embroidered cloak, that garment is considered appropriate for him. You cannot tell him to make do with a plainer one, any more than you could take away nine of his ten cars and insist he is adequately served by the remaining vehicle, even if that remaining vehicle happens to be a Rolls Royce.

To see this principle expressed with full clarity, the Gemara cites a *baraita* — seven lines from the bottom of 113b — that reads: if someone had creditors holding a thousand-*zuz* debt against him and he was wearing a gorgeous garment worth a hundred *maneh*, *mafshitin oso mimenu* — we strip it from him — *u-malbishin oso istila ha-re'uya lo* — and clothe him with a garment appropriate to his circumstances. But then the Gemara cites two contradicting *baraisos*, one in the name of Rabbi Yishmael and one in the name of Rabbi Akiva, both of which declare: *kol Yisrael re'uyin l'osa istila* — all Jews are fit for that elegant garment. Under their view, if this is what the person was wearing, he is entitled to keep it. The *kol Yisrael bnei melachim* position thus forecloses the possibility of selling a fancy item in exchange for a basic one. If one were to apply *mesadrim* consistently under this view, one would be unable to make any such substitution at all — which raises its own problems.

### Fancy vs. Basic Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַחְרֵישָׁה לְמַאי חַזְיָא? אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַבָּה: מַחְרֵישָׁה דְּכַסְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But for what is a plow fit?** In other words, how can there be a difference in price in this case? **Rava bar Rabba said:** This is referring to **a silver plow,** which is an ornament and not used for work.

The Gemara asks: according to the initial approach that we leave the person what is appropriate for him and sell off the excess, this makes sense for a pillow or blanket — one can imagine the difference between a fine down blanket and a basic polyester one. But what about a *makhresha*? How does one distinguish between an expensive and an inexpensive version of a plow? They all serve the same function.

Rava bar Rava answers: *makhresha d'chaspa* — say the person owns a silver plow. Unusual as that may be, it creates a meaningful distinction between the luxury object and a functional replacement.

### Tosafot on Plows

At this point, Tosafot raises a significant concern. The whole discussion of returning collateral on a daily basis is grounded in the verse *ki hi k'suto levada hi simlato le'oro* — it speaks of lying down in one's garment, of clothing and physical covering. Tosafot argues that this *mitzvah* of daily return applies only to items that relate directly to a person's body — clothing, a pillow, a blanket. A plow, which is a means of livelihood rather than a garment for the body, would not fall under this obligation.

This leads Tosafot to a further point about the category of *keilim she'oseh ochel nefesh* — implements used to produce food. Some *Rishonim* include a plow in this category, since plowing is how one grows food. Tosafot reads the category more narrowly, limiting it to implements that come into direct contact with food in its preparation — a grinder, a mill, a mixing bowl — what the Talmud calls *rehayim varechev*. Plowing the ground is too indirect. Under Tosafot's view, a creditor may in principle take a person's plow even if it is his only means of livelihood, and he would not be obligated to return it daily.

Given this, Tosafot offers an alternative reading of *makhresha*: not a plow at all, but a type of body comb — he cites another Gemara in support, and indeed the resemblance is plausible given the prong-like shape of a plow. Under this reading, the *makhresha* is a personal grooming implement, making it squarely within the category of body-related items that must be returned daily, and one can easily imagine the contrast between a silver comb and a plain one.

### Rav Chana's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב חַגָּא: וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ, לָאו עֲלַי קָרְמֵית? אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥagga objects to this** entire opinion concerning the arrangement made for a debtor: **Let** the creditor **say to** the debtor: **Your** needs **are not cast upon me.** In other words, why should I, who lent you money, make allowances for your livelihood? **Abaye said to him:**

The Gemara now records an objection from Rav Chana: *u-leima lei lav alai ramya* — why should this be my responsibility? What is the basis for requiring the creditor to ensure the debtor's dignity and daily comfort?

Tosafot explains that the challenge is directed specifically at the obligation to return collateral daily — and under the *bnei melachim* principle, even to return a beautiful, expensive garment on a daily basis. The creditor finds himself asking: if the man is poor, why does the Torah impose on me this ongoing burden of returning such a costly object? What is the rationale?

### Abaye's Response

Abaye answers: *ivra aleika rami* — in truth it is your responsibility. *She-mishum she-ne'emar v'lacha tihyeh tzedakah* — because the Torah says it will be counted as righteousness for you. The Torah frames the daily return of the garment as an act of *tzedakah*, of upright conduct, of maintaining the debtor's dignity. Even if the garment is expensive and the arrangement feels burdensome, that is precisely what the Torah demands, and the reward accrues to the creditor who upholds it.

## Gezerah Shavah Debate

### Question Raised

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ שֶׁיְּסַדְּרוּ בְּבַעַל חוֹב? מִי גָמַר ״מִיכָה״ ״מִיכָה״ מֵעֲרָכִין, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **making arrangements for the debtor** so that he will retain some of his possessions so that he may continue living as before, albeit at a slightly lower standard? The issue on which this is based is **whether or not** a verbal analogy is **derived from** the usage of the term **"poor"** written in the context of a debtor (Leviticus 25:35) and the term **"poor"** written in the context of **valuations** (Leviticus 27:8), as the Gemara will discuss further at the end of the *amud*.

Having discussed *mesadrim l'baal chov* and having seen a *baraita* that essentially asserts the practice, the Gemara now introduces a parallel formulation of the debate. The question is posed: *mahu she-yesadru b'baal chov* — does one make arrangements for the debtor? Can the law of *mesadrim* be derived for ordinary debts by means of a *gezerah shavah* from *erchin*?

The linguistic parallel is as follows: regarding a loan the Torah writes *v'chi yamuach achicha* — if your brother becomes poor; regarding *erchin* the Torah writes *v'im mach hu me'erkecha* — if he is too poor to meet the valuation. The same root *m-ch* appears in both contexts, and one might argue that whatever accommodation is provided in *erchin* should likewise be extended to the ordinary debtor.

### Rabin's Letter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דִּשְׁלַח רָבִין בְּאִגַּרְתֵּיהּ: דָּבָר זֶה שָׁאַלְתִּי לְכׇל רַבּוֹתַי, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לִי דָּבָר. בְּרַם כָּךְ הָיְתָה שְׁאֵלָה: הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מָנֶה לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת״, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּסַדְּרוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof, **as Rabin sent** a message **in his letter** from Eretz Yisrael: **I asked all my teachers** concerning **this matter, but they did not tell me anything. But there was this question** concerning a similar matter that I heard them discuss: With regard to **one who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **one hundred dinars for the Temple maintenance, what is** the *halakha* as to whether **they make arrangements** for him? Although an arrangement is explicitly taught only with regard to the specific type of donation of valuations, is it applicable here as well?

Ulla, or perhaps a different authority, introduces a teaching that arrived in a letter sent by Rabin: *davar zeh sha'alti l'chol rabosai* — I posed this question to all of my Rebbes, and none of them was able to give me a decisive answer. However, Rabin continues, a related question did arise in the *Beis Medrash*, and from the assumptions embedded in that question we may be able to infer an answer to ours.

### Non-Erech Donation Case

The question that arose in the *Beis Medrash* was this: *ha-omer harei alai maneh l'vedek ha-bayis* — what if someone pledged a hundred *maneh* to the *Beis HaMikdash* not through the *erchin* mechanism (where the Torah sets fixed values by age and gender) but simply as an ordinary *neder*, a voluntary donation? If that person cannot pay, do we apply *mesadrim* — do we leave him a basic amount and allow *hekdesh* to take the rest? Or may *hekdesh* strip him completely bare?

This question is structurally parallel to ours. Just as one might try to extend *mesadrim* from *erchin* to ordinary debts, the question asks whether *mesadrim* extends from *erchin* to other cases of *hekdesh*.

### Kal V'Chomer Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּבַר פַּדָּא, וְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּאִילְפָא אָמְרִי: קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִבַּעַל חוֹב. וּמָה בַּעַל חוֹב שֶׁמַּחְזִירִין – אֵין מְסַדְּרִין, הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁאֵין מַחְזִירִין – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֵין מְסַדְּרִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov in the name of bar Padda, and Rabbi Yirmeya in the name of Ilfa,** each **say:** It is an ***a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakhot* of **a debtor: And if** for **a debtor, to** whom one **returns** his collateral, they do **not make arrangements** for the payment of his debt, then in the case of **consecration, where** they do **not return** his collateral, **is it not logical that** they should **not make arrangements** for the payment of his debt?

Rabbi Yaakov in the name of Bar Pada and Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Ilfi offer a *kal v'chomer*: if by an ordinary *baal chov* — where the creditor has an obligation to return collateral to the debtor — we nonetheless say *ein mesadrim*, we do not leave the debtor a basic amount, then surely by *hekdesh* — which has no obligation to return any collateral it has taken — how much more certainly would *ein mesadrim* apply.

The significant point embedded in this argument is the assumption it takes for granted: *ein mesadrim l'baal chov*. Even though the creditor must return collateral, he is not required to leave the debtor a basic subsistence amount when collecting the loan. That is the assumption upon which the entire *kal v'chomer* is built.

### Nidon B'Kvodo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״נֶדֶר בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״ כְּתִיב, מָה עֲרָכִין מְסַדְּרִין – אַף הֶקְדֵּשׁ מְסַדְּרִין. וְאִידַּךְ: הַהוּא לְנִידּוֹן בִּכְבוֹדוֹ הוּא דַּאֲתָא. מָה עֲרָכִין נִידּוֹן בִּכְבוֹדוֹ – אַף הֶקְדֵּשׁ נִידּוֹן בִּכְבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It **is written:** "When a man shall clearly utter **a vow according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:2). In this verse, all vows of consecrated property are juxtaposed to valuations, teaching that **just as** they **make arrangements** for the payment of a debt with regard to **valuations, so too** they **make arrangements** for the payment of a debt with regard to any vow of **consecration. And** what do **the other** Sages, i.e., Rabbi Ya'akov and Rabbi Yirmeya, derive from this juxtaposition between vows and valuations? The Gemara replies: They maintain **that** this juxtaposition **comes to** teach the *halakha* that a vow of consecration is **judged by its significance.** If one stated a vow of valuation concerning a vital part of his body, e.g., that he will donate the value of his heart, he is obligated to pay not only the value of that organ, but the valuation of his entire self. Consequently, the phrase "a vow according to your valuation" indicates that **just as valuations** are **judged by their significance, so too consecrated** property is **judged by its significance.**

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees — not with the assumption about *baal chov*, but with the ruling about *hekdesh* and non-*erchin* donations. He points to the phrase *neder b'erkecha* — a vow with a valuation — which linguistically connects *erchin* to other types of vows. Just as by *erchin* we apply *mesadrim*, so too by other forms of *hekdesh* we apply *mesadrim*.

The Gemara then asks: and what do the other *Amoraim* — those who do not extend *mesadrim* to non-*erchin hekdesh* — do with this same phrase *neder b'erkecha*? They derive from it a different law altogether: *nidon b'khvodo* — something is assessed according to the significance of the item being valued.

The practical application is as follows. If someone says *erech ploni alai*, the valuation of a specific person is upon me, and a standard *erchin* obligation results. But if someone says he accepts the *erech* of a particular limb — say, the heart of an ox, or the heart of a person — since the heart is an organ whose vitality is inseparable from the life of the whole body, it is assessed as if he said *d'mei* of the entire creature. Similarly, if he said *d'mei regel shor alai* — the value of the leg of an ox — one calculates the leg's market price. But if he said *d'mei lev shor alai* — the value of the heart of an ox — it is assessed at the value of the whole ox. That is *nidon b'khvodo*: assessed by the significance of the limb, with limbs representing the entire body evaluated at the full price. This *halacha* is learned from *neder b'erkecha*, leaving that verse unavailable to teach the extension of *mesadrim* from *erchin* to other *hekdesh* cases.

### Verse Limits to Erchin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיסַדְּרוּ בְּבַעַל חוֹב קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵעֲרָכִין: וּמָה עֲרָכִין שֶׁאֵין מַחְזִירִין – מְסַדְּרִין, בַּעַל חוֹב שֶׁמַּחְזִירִין – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמְּסַדְּרִין? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִם מָךְ הוּא מֵעֶרְכֶּךָ״ – ״הוּא״ וְלֹא בַּעַל חוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But they should make arrangements for a debtor** based on an ***a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakhot* of **valuations,** as follows: **And if** in the case of **valuations** the *halakha* is **that** they do **not return** his collateral and yet they do **make arrangements** for the payment of his debt, then with regard to **a debtor,** where the *halakha* is **that** one does **return** his collateral, **is it not logical that** they should **make arrangements** for the payment of his debt? The Gemara responds: **The verse states: "But if he is too poor for your valuation**…and the priest shall value him, according to the means of the one that vowed shall the priest value him" (Leviticus 27:8). The Torah emphasizes that this *halakha* is applicable only to **"he"** who makes a valuation, **but not** to **a debtor.**

The Gemara now runs the *kal v'chomer* in the opposite direction: perhaps we should say *mesadrim l'baal chov* on the basis of a *kal v'chomer* from *erchin*. The argument would run: *u-mah erchin she-ein machzirin mesadrin* — *erchin* and *hekdesh* generally do not return collateral, yet they do apply *mesadrim*. Then *baal chov she-machzirin* — an ordinary creditor who must return the collateral — how much more so should *mesadrim* apply!

The Gemara answers: *amar kra v'im mach hu me'erkecha* — the verse comes and limits the accommodation. *Hu v'lo baal chov* — it applies to the vow to *hekdesh*, not to an ordinary debt. The *kal v'chomer* was a cogent argument, but the verse cuts it off.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ: הַאי, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּמָכוּתוֹ מִתְּחִילָּתוֹ וְעַד סוֹפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** opinion, which maintains that they do make arrangements for a debtor, how is the word "he" interpreted? The Gemara answers: This word teaches that the *halakha* does not apply **unless** he **remains in his** state of **poverty from the beginning to the end.** If he was rich at the outset, or grew wealthy at some later stage, arrangements are not made for him.

The Gemara then asks: and what does the authority who would extend *mesadrim* to *hekdesh* beyond *erchin* do with this limiting word *hu*? The answer is that *hu* carries a different limitation: *ad she-yehei b'makhuto mitchilato v'ad sofo* — the accommodation of *erchin* applies only when the person was consistently poor from the moment of the vow through the moment of assessment. If he was wealthy when he vowed and poor at assessment, or poor when he vowed and wealthy at assessment, the accommodation does not apply. The word *hu* limits *erchin* temporally, not categorically, and is therefore still available to allow an extension to other *hekdesh* cases.

### Reverse Kal V'Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיַחְזִירוּ בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִבַּעַל חוֹב: וּמָה בַּעַל חוֹב שֶׁאֵין מְסַדְּרִין – מַחְזִירִין, הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁמְּסַדְּרִין – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמַּחְזִירִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks an additional question: **And** once it is established that arrangements are not made for a debtor, **they should return** the collateral **in** the case of **consecration** based on **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakhot* of **a debtor: And if** in the case of **a debtor, where** they do **not make arrangements** for him, the creditor nevertheless **returns** his collateral, with regard to **consecration, where** they do **make arrangements** for the payment of his debt, **is it not logical that** they should **return** his collateral to him?

The Gemara now runs yet another *kal v'chomer*, this time to ask why *hekdesh* should not be required to return collateral. The argument: *u-mah baal chov she-ein mesadrin machzirin* — by an ordinary creditor, who does not apply *mesadrim*, you nonetheless must return collateral. By *hekdesh*, which does apply *mesadrim*, how much more so should there be an obligation to return collateral!

### Hekdesh Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְשָׁכַב בְּשַׂלְמָתוֹ וּבֵרְכֶךָּ״, יָצָא הֶקְדֵּשׁ, שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ בְּרָכָה. וְלָא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ וְשָׂבָעְתָּ וּבֵרַכְתָּ וְגוֹ׳״! אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה״, מִי שֶׁצָּרִיךְ צְדָקָה, יָצָא הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ צְדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to a regular loan: "You shall restore to him the collateral…**and he will sleep in his garment and he will bless you"** (Deuteronomy 24:13), **excluding consecration, where** there is **no need** for **a blessing,** and therefore it is not included in the *halakha* of returning the collateral. The Gemara is puzzled by this claim: **And** is consecrated property **not** in need of a blessing? **But isn't it written: "And you shall eat and be satisfied, and bless** the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 8:10), indicating that consecrated property also requires a blessing? **Rather,** the reason is that **the verse states** with regard to the restoration of collateral: **"And it shall be righteousness [*tzedaka*] for you"** (Deuteronomy 24:13), which is referring to caring for **one who requires charity [*tzedaka*], excluding consecrated** property, **which** does **not require charity.**

The Gemara answers with a verse: *v'shachav b'salmato u-veiracheka* — he will lie down in his cloak and give you a blessing. The creditor must return the garment so that the debtor can bless him. *Yatza hekdesh she-ein tzarich berachah* — *hekdesh* is exempt because it has no need of the debtor's blessing.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: does God not need blessings? The verse *v'achalta v'savata u-veirachta et Hashem elokecha* — you shall eat, be satisfied, and bless the Lord your God — suggests He does. The Gemara therefore turns to another phrase in the verse: *u-lecha tihyeh tzedakah lifnei Hashem elokecha* — and it shall be *tzedakah* for you before God. *Mi she-tzarich tzedakah* — this applies to one who needs charity. *Yatza hekdesh she-ein tzarich tzedakah* — *hekdesh* is excluded because it has no need of charity.

A brief but pointed observation deserves mention here. The word *tzedakah* derives from *tzedek* — righteousness — and carries a sense of obligation rooted in justice, quite the opposite of the Christian concept of charity as a voluntary act of grace toward the undeserving. Whether the Gemara intends *tzedakah* here in its rabbinic sense of almsgiving or in the broader sense of righteous conduct affects how one reads the exclusion, but the presumed reading is the former: *hekdesh* does not require your charitable support.

The striking irony the Gemara has thus established is this: *hekdesh* does not return collateral, but it does apply *mesadrim* — it cannot strip the person bare when collecting. An ordinary *baal chov*, by contrast, must return the collateral daily, but when collection time comes, he may strip the debtor bare — *ein mesadrim l'baal chov*. The two regimes are, in a sense, mirror images of each other.

## Rabbah bar Avuha Story

### Cemetery Encounter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ לְאֵלִיָּהוּ דְּקָאֵי בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַהוּ שֶׁיְּסַדְּרוּ בְּבַעַל חוֹב?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rabba bar Avuh found Elijah standing in a graveyard of gentiles.** Rabba bar Avuh **said to him: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **making arrangements for the debtor** so that he will retain some of his possessions so that he may continue living as before, albeit at a slightly lower standard?

The Gemara now relates a remarkable story. Rabbah bar Avuha encountered Eliyahu HaNavi — and found him standing in a non-Jewish cemetery. Rabbah bar Avuha asked him: *mahu she-yesadru b'baal chov* — what is the *halacha*? Do we make arrangements for the debtor?

### Eliyahu's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גָּמַר ״מִיכָה״ ״מִיכָה״ מֵעֲרָכִין. גַּבֵּי עֲרָכִין כְּתִיב ״וְאִם מָךְ הוּא מֵעֶרְכֶּךָ״, גַּבֵּי בַּעַל חוֹב כְּתִיב ״וְכִי יָמוּךְ אָחִיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Elijah **said to him:** A verbal analogy is **derived from** the usage of the term **"poor"** written in the context of a debtor and the term **"poor"** written in the context of **valuations.** With regard to **valuations,** it **is written: "But if he is too poor [*makh*] for your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:8), and **with regard to a creditor,** it **is written: "But if your brother be poor [*yamukh*]"** (Leviticus 25:35).

Eliyahu replied: *gemar micha micha me-erchin* — we derive it by *gezerah shavah*, the verbal analogy from the word *mach* appearing in both the context of loans and the context of *erchin*. This, at least in Eliyahu's telling, is the basis for *mesadrim l'baal chov* — an answer that stands in notable tension with everything the earlier *sugya* established.

### Naked Person Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן לְעָרוֹם שֶׁלֹּא יִתְרוֹם? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא יִרְאֶה בְךָ עֶרְוַת דָּבָר״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Avuh now asks Elijah another question: **From where** is it derived with regard **to a naked person that he may not separate *teruma*?** He replied: **As it is written: "And He see no unseemly thing in you"** (Deuteronomy 23:15). This verse indicates that one may not recite any words of sanctity, including the blessing upon separating *teruma*, in front of one who is naked.

Rabbah bar Avuha then posed a second question: *minyan l'arum shelo yitrom* — from where do we know that a naked person may not take *terumah*? Rashi points out that the real question concerns the *berachah* that precedes separating *terumah*, not the act itself — a person without clothing cannot recite a blessing, since the verse says *v'lo yireh becha ervas davar v'shav me'acharecha*, implying that nakedness and divine presence are incompatible. Rabbah bar Avuha's question is what is the source for the *Mishnah* that prohibits separating *terumah* while unclothed, which is really a question about the underlying principle governing blessings and bodily exposure.

### Kohen in Cemetery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו כֹּהֵן הוּא מָר? מַאי טַעְמָא קָאֵי מָר בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא מַתְנֵי מָר טְהָרוֹת? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: קִבְרֵיהֶן שֶׁל נׇכְרִים אֵין מְטַמְּאִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתֵּן צֹאנִי צֹאן מַרְעִיתִי אָדָם אַתֶּם״. אַתֶּם קְרוּיִין ״אָדָם״, וְאֵין נׇכְרִים קְרוּיִין ״אָדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The *amora* proceeded to ask Elijah a different question and **said to him: Is not the Master a priest? What is the reason** that **the Master** is **standing in a cemetery?** Elijah **said to him: Has the Master not studied** the mishnaic order of ***Teharot*? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says** that **the graves of gentiles do not render** one **impure, as it is stated: "And you, My sheep, the sheep of My pasture, are man"** (Ezekiel 34:31), which teaches that **you,** i.e., the Jewish people, **are called "man," but gentiles are not called "man."** Since the Torah states with regard to ritual impurity imparted in a tent: "If a man dies in a tent" (Numbers 19:14), evidently impurity imparted by a tent does not apply to gentiles.

After answering, Rabbah bar Avuha turned to Eliyahu and said: *lav kohen hu mar* — are you not a *kohen*? *Mai ta'ama kai mar b'veis ha-kevaros* — what are you doing in a cemetery?

Rashi notes that there is a dispute in the tradition about Eliyahu's identity: some say he was Pinchas, which would make him a *kohen*, while others disagree. The questioner was evidently proceeding on the assumption that Eliyahu was indeed a *kohen* and should not be in a place of *tumah*.

Eliyahu replied: *lo masni mar Taharos* — have you never learned *Taharos*? And he cited a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *kivrehem shel ovdei kochavim ein metam'in* — the graves of non-Jews do not convey *tumah*. The proof text is *v'aten tzoni tzon mar'isi adam atem* — you are my flock, the flock of my pasturing, you are called *adam*. *Atem keruyim adam v'ein ovdei kochavim keruyim adam* — Jews are designated by the term *adam*, whereas non-Jews are not.

This is a striking and, frankly, very harsh Gemara. It is not merely a hermeneutic point about *tumah*; it makes a claim about the designation of humanity itself that sits uncomfortably alongside other Gemaras that use *ha-adam* to include non-Jews. Tosafot grapples with this extensively, distinguishing between *adam* and *ha-adam*, and suggesting that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's position may not represent the full spectrum of *Tannaitic* opinion. The matter is part of a much larger discussion.

On the practical *halacha* for *kohanim*: the question raised by Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's position is specifically about *tumas ohel* — whether being under the same roof as, or directly over, a non-Jewish corpse or grave conveys *tumah* even without direct contact. The general *halachic* assumption follows Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai that non-Jewish graves do not convey *tumas ohel*, though some are *choshesh* for the possibility that they do. This has real *halacha l'maaseh* implications for *kohanim* in hospitals, museums that house ancient remains, and similar settings.

### Four vs. Six Sedarim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּאַרְבְּעָה לָא מָצֵינָא, בְּשִׁיתָּא מָצֵינָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Avuh **said to him:** How could I be familiar with that *baraita*? If **I cannot** be proficient **in** the more commonly studied **four** orders of the Mishna, **can I** be knowledgeable **in** all **six?**

After Eliyahu explained why the non-Jewish cemetery posed no problem, he asked Rabbah bar Avuha: why have you not learned *Taharos*? Rabbah bar Avuha replied: *b'arba lo masina, b'shisa masina* — I cannot even master the four *sedarim* that are *halacha l'maaseh*; how should I be expected to know all six?

The four *sedarim* he considers practically relevant are *Moed*, *Nashim*, *Nezikin*, and *Kodashim*. Rashi explains the inclusion of *Kodashim* on the grounds that studying it is considered like bringing *korbanos*, and there is also *Chullin* within it, with its laws of *shechitah* and *tereifos*. *Taharos* and *Zeraim* fall lower on the priority list — *Zeraim* because most of it is not applicable in the *beis medrash* context of *chutz la-aretz*, and *Taharos* because, though it does have some ongoing relevance through *kehunah* and *niddah*, it is treated as secondary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאַמַּאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּחִיקָא לִי מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Elijah **said to him: Why** are you not learned in them all? Rabba bar Avuh **said to him: The matter** of a livelihood is **pressing for me,** and I am therefore unable to study properly.

The reason Rabbah bar Avuha cannot master even the four practical *sedarim* is that he is desperately poor. *D'dichek li milsa* — I have a very hard time making a living; I must spend my time working just to put food on the table.

### Gan Eden Leaves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּבְרֵיהּ וְעַיְּילֵיהּ לְגַן עֵדֶן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּשׁוֹט גְּלִימָךְ, סְפִי שְׁקוֹל מֵהָנֵי טַרְפֵי. סְפָא שְׁקַל.
>
> **Translation:** Elijah **led him and brought him into the Garden of Eden** and **said to him: Remove your cloak, gather up** and **take** some **of these leaves** lying around. Rabba Bar Avuh **gathered** them **up** and **took** them.

Eliyahu then took him somewhere — the Gemara says *dabri v'ayeilei l'Gan Eden* — he led him into *Gan Eden*. This is understood not as the celestial afterlife but as the Garden of Eden that still exists somewhere on this earth, hidden and inaccessible under normal circumstances.

There, Eliyahu told him: *pashut glimach* — take off your cloak — *safi shkole mei-hani tarfei* — and gather some of these leaves. The implication is that these leaves possess extraordinary value and would make Rabbah bar Avuha wealthy enough to sit and learn.

### Olam HaBa Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲוָה נָפֵיק, שְׁמַע דְּקָאָמַר: מַאן קָא אָכֵיל לְעָלְמֵיהּ כְּרַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ. נְפַץ שְׁדָנְהוּ. אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי אַתְיֵיהּ לִגְלִימֵיהּ, סְחַט גְּלִימָא רֵיחָא. זַבְּנֵיהּ בִּתְרֵיסַר אַלְפֵי דִּינָרֵי, פַּלְגִינְהוּ לְחַתְנָווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When** he **was exiting,** he **heard** a voice **that declared: Who** else **consumes his World**-to-Come **like Rabba bar Avuh,** who takes his merit of the next world for his use in the present one? **He spread** out his cloak and **threw away** the leaves. **Even so,** when he **brought his cloak** back, he discovered that **the cloak** had **absorbed** such a good **scent** from those leaves that he **sold it for twelve thousand dinars.** Since he knew that this was taken from his portion in the World-to-Come, he did not want to benefit from it himself, and he therefore **divided** the sum **among his sons-in-law.**

Upon hearing this, he scattered the leaves and threw them back. Nonetheless, *sachit glima reicha* — his cloak had absorbed the fragrance of the *Gan Eden* leaves. He sold that cloak for twelve thousand *dinarei*, which would have been more than enough to free him from material want and allow him to devote himself to learning.

### Distribution & Parallels

The story ends with Rabbah bar Avuha distributing the money — *palginu l'chasansanei* — to his sons-in-law. Which means he still did not gain the personal financial relief that would allow him to sit and learn.

The story is a fascinating one, and it is worth pausing to notice how it resonates with the surrounding *halachic* discussion. The two questions Rabbah bar Avuha posed to Eliyahu — *mesadrim l'baal chov* and the question about the naked person who cannot recite a blessing — both reflect his own lived condition of poverty: the question of being stripped bare of everything, and the question of nakedness. Then Eliyahu leads him into *Gan Eden*, where he enters a domain he is not normally permitted to enter, takes something, and is compelled to return it — a structural echo of the creditor entering the house and being made to return the collateral. And when he does return it, the consequence, precisely as the verse promises — *u-veiracheka*, he will bless you — is that he emerges with a blessing of sorts, albeit embedded in the fragrance that absorbed into his cloak. Even the substitution motif is present: he went in with a fine cloak, he came out with something more modest, but what the garment retained was more valuable than the leaves he had tried to take.

## Halachic Rulings

### Tosafot's Position

The *sugyot* on *mesadrim l'baal chov* have moved back and forth, but Eliyahu's teaching at least appears to say *mesadrim l'baal chov* and anchors it in a *gezerah shavah*. How do we *pasken*?

Looking at Tosafot on 114a, *dibur hamaschil* *mahu she-yesadru b'baal chov*, Tosafot rules: *pasken Rabbeinu Tam b'Sefer HaYashar d'ein mesadrin b'baal chov*. We rule like the earlier *sugya* — *ein mesadrim*. The basis is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the Mishnah, whose position implies throughout that *ein mesadrim l'baal chov*.

As for Eliyahu's teaching, Tosafot has a different *girsa* of the text. In Tosafot's version, Rabbah bar Avuha did not ask Eliyahu *mahu she-yesadru b'baal chov* — whether we do it at all — but rather *minayin she-mesadrin l'baal chov* — for those who hold that we do, what is the *halachic* source? This reading allows Tosafot to treat Eliyahu's answer as reporting a minority position's derivation rather than a ruling, and hence as not determinative of the *halacha*. The reading is strained, Tosafot acknowledges, but it is his way of maintaining that *ein mesadrim l'baal chov*.

### Mashkon vs. Geviyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאִם אִישׁ עָנִי הוּא לֹא תִשְׁכַּב בַּעֲבֹטוֹ״, הָא עָשִׁיר – שְׁכַיב. מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״וְאִם אִישׁ עָנִי הוּא״ – לֹא תִשְׁכַּב וַעֲבוֹטוֹ אֶצְלְךָ. הָא עָשִׁיר – שְׁכַב וַעֲבוֹטוֹ אֶצְלְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: **"If he be a poor man, you shall not sleep with his collateral"** (Deuteronomy 24:12), **but** if he is **wealthy,** one may **lie down.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *tanna* **saying? Rav Sheshet said** that **this** is what he **is saying: And if he be a poor man, you shall not sleep while his collateral is with you;** rather, you must restore it to him before the sun sets. **But** if he is **a wealthy** man, you may **lie down while his collateral is with you.**

Tosafot then makes a critical distinction that clarifies the entire discussion. At the bottom of 114a, the last few words of the line read: *v'rabbanan d'matnitin nami matu savri d'ein mesadrin* — the Rabbis of the Mishnah who require the creditor to return the cloak daily may nonetheless agree that *ein mesadrim* when it comes time to collect.

How can both be true simultaneously? Tosafot draws a sharp line between two periods: the *mashkon* period, when the creditor is holding collateral and the loan has not yet been fully called in; and the *geviyah* period, the moment of actual collection.

During the *mashkon* period, Tosafot explains — *u-mairi b'vo litol b'torat mashkon* — the Rabbis and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel are debating how long you must continue returning the collateral: the Rabbis say indefinitely, *machzirin lo kol yemei chayav*, while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says you must return it for thirty days and may then sell it. But both positions are operating within the *mashkon* mode, in which the creditor is still allowing the situation to be extended and is not yet demanding full repayment.

Once the creditor shifts to *torat gviyah* — the actual collection mode — *yachul l'machro miyad v'lo yeshader klal*. He may sell everything immediately and is not required even to replace an expensive garment with a cheaper one. *Ein mesadrim* applies without qualification. He need not leave the debtor anything.

This distinction — between the protections that apply during the *mashkon* stage and the full right of collection that applies during *geviyah* — is foundational to the *halachic* structure.

### Rambam's Ruling

The Rambam in *Perek* 1, *Halacha* 4 of *Hilchos Malveh V'Loveh* rules differently. He says: *kesheyitba hamalveh hovato* — when the time comes to collect the loan — even if the creditor is wealthy and the debtor is struggling to put food on the table — *ein merahamin badin*. There is no room for mercy in the legal proceedings. One collects to the last penny, *ad pruta achrona*, from all available *mitaltelin*, and if those do not suffice, from real property.

But then he adds: *achar kach mesadrin* — after all that, we apply *mesadrim*. *Mesadrin l'baal chov kederech she-mesadrin b'erchin* — we arrange for the debtor just as we arrange in the case of *erchin*. And the procedure is: *omrin laloveh havei kol hamitaltelin sheyesh lach* — we tell the debtor, bring every item of *mitaltelin* you possess — *lo taniah afilu machat achat* — do not leave out so much as a single pin. A full inventory is required. Then, from that complete inventory, we allocate him *mazon shloshim yom* — food for thirty days — and *kesus shneim asar chodesh* — clothing for twelve months — and the creditor takes the rest.

So the Rambam's ruling is a both-and position. *Ein merahamin* is real: the creditor has the right to a complete accounting and collection to the last cent. But *mesadrim l'baal chov* is also real: once that accounting is done, a basic subsistence floor is carved out. The Rambam thus rules against Tosafot, who holds *ein mesadrim* entirely.

### Ein Merahamin

What is striking about the Rambam is how he holds these two principles in tension. The sense of *ein merahamin badin* — no mercy in law — is genuinely meant: you are entitled to demand a full, comprehensive inventory and you need not be moved by the debtor's poverty when the legal right to collect has arrived. And yet woven into that same legal structure is an obligation to leave the person with enough to live on. The *ein merahamin* is not a license to be cruel; it is a statement about legal entitlement. The *mesadrim* then operates as a kind of mandatory minimum, baked into the collection process itself.

## Taking & Returning Collateral

### Baraita on Poor/Rich

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאִם אִישׁ עָנִי הוּא לֹא תִשְׁכַּב בַּעֲבֹטוֹ״, הָא עָשִׁיר – שְׁכַיב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** One **who lends** money to **another is not permitted to take collateral from him, and is not obligated to return** it **to him, and transgresses all of these labels [*shemot*]** of prohibitions. The meaning of this *baraita* is unclear, and the Gemara asks: **What is** the *tanna* **saying?**

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *v'im ish ani hu lo tishkav ba'avoto* — if he is a poor person, do not lie down while his collateral is still with you. The implication is: *ha ashir shachiv* — if the debtor is wealthy, you may. The Gemara takes this to mean literally sleeping in his garment, and asks what that means — *mai ka'amar*? Rashi notes that sleeping in the debtor's garment would constitute a kind of *ribbis*.

### Friend Lending Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר לוֹ, וְעוֹבֵר בְּכׇל הַשֵּׁמוֹת הַלָּלוּ. מַאי קָאָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** One **who lends** money to **another is not permitted to take collateral from him, and is not obligated to return** it **to him, and transgresses all of these labels [*shemot*]** of prohibitions. The meaning of this *baraita* is unclear, and the Gemara asks: **What is** the *tanna* **saying?**

A *baraita* is then cited regarding lending to a friend: *hamalveh es haveiro eino rashai lemashkeno* — one who lends to his friend is not permitted to take a *mashkon*, presumably by entering into his home as the earlier prohibitions describe. The *baraita* then says he is not obligated to return it — and then says that if he fails to return it, he transgresses all these prohibitions. The Gemara asks how this can be internally consistent — he is not allowed, he has no obligation to return, yet he transgresses? *Mai ka'amar* — what is this saying?

### Rav Sheshet Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָכִי קָאָמַר, הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ, וְאִם מִשְׁכְּנוֹ – חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר לוֹ. וְעוֹבֵר בְּכׇל הַשֵּׁמוֹת הַלָּלוּ – אַסֵּיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: This** is what he **is saying,** One **who lends** money to **another is not permitted to take collateral from him, and if** he did **take collateral from him,** he is **obligated to return** it **to him.** As for the clause: **And** he **transgresses all of these labels** of prohibitions, this is referring **to the latter clause,** i.e., the case implicit in the *baraita*, where the creditor took collateral from the debtor and did not return it, and the *baraita* explains that such a person violates all of the Torah prohibitions that apply to this situation.

Rav Sheshet resolves this. *Lo tishkav va'avoto etzlacha* — do not go to bed while the collateral is still with you. The *baraita* is not saying he may literally sleep in the garment; it is saying do not retire for the night without having returned it. And *ha ashir shachiv va'avoto etzlacha* — if the debtor is rich, then yes, you may keep it through the night without returning it. The obligation of daily return applies only where the debtor is poor.

As for the internal contradiction in the *baraita*, Rav Sheshet re-reads it: *v'im mashkeno chayav lehachazir lo* — if he did take the *mashkon*, whether in violation or through *beis din*, he is obligated to return it. The word *eino* was an error in the text. And the phrase *v'over bechol hashemos halalu* refers to the person who, having taken the *mashkon*, fails to return it — he transgresses the positive and negative commandments of return: *hashev tashiv es ha'avos*, *lo tishkav ba'avoto*, *kevo hashemesh teshivenu lo*.

### Rava's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ, וְאִם מִשְׁכְּנוֹ – חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר לוֹ. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁמִּשְׁכְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ, אֲבָל מִשְׁכְּנוֹ בִּשְׁעַת הַלְוָאָתוֹ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר לוֹ. וְעוֹבֵר בְּכׇל הַשֵּׁמוֹת הַלָּלוּ – אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: This** is what the *tanna* **is saying:** One **who lends** money to **another is not permitted to take collateral from him, and if** he did **take collateral from him,** he is **obligated to return** it **to him. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is referring to **where he took collateral from him when** it was **not at the time of the loan,** but rather as a means of ensuring payment. **But** if he **took collateral from him at the time of the loan,** in which case the collateral serves as a guarantee of the loan, **he is not obligated to return** it **to him.** According to this interpretation, the statement: **And** he **transgresses of all these labels** of prohibitions, is referring **to the first clause** of the *baraita*, concerning the prohibition against taking collateral.

Rava reads the *baraita* differently. He maintains the word *eino* — it is correct — and organizes the *baraita* around a key temporal distinction. *Bameh devarim amurim she-mashkeno shelo be-shas halva'aso* — when do all these rules apply? When the creditor took the *mashkon* after the loan was made, not at the moment of the loan itself. *Aval mashkeno be-shas halva'aso, eino chayav lehachazir lo* — if the collateral was arranged at the very time of the loan, as part of the original transaction, the creditor is not required to return it daily.

When collateral is seized after the loan — particularly when it is overdue — the creditor acts from a position of legal power over the debtor, and the Torah's protections are triggered: he cannot enter the house, he must return it if he took it, and he must return it daily if the debtor is poor. But at the time of the loan itself, both parties are more or less equally situated and are freely negotiating the terms of the transaction. Collateral taken then is part of the deal and is not viewed through the same lens of coercive power. The prohibitions and the daily-return obligation therefore do not apply to collateral taken *be-shas halva'aso*.

The closing phrase of the *baraita* — *v'over bechol hashemos halalu* — belongs, on Rava's reading, to the opening clause: it refers to the creditor who takes *mashkon shelo be-shas halva'aso* and fails to return it, who thereby transgresses multiple prohibitions.

## Night & Day Garments

### Rav Shizi's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב שֵׁיזְבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״עַד בֹּא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ תְּשִׁיבֶנּוּ לוֹ״ – זוֹ כְּסוּת לַיְלָה, ״הָשֵׁב תָּשִׁיב לוֹ אֶת הַעֲבוֹט כְּבוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״ – זוֹ כְּסוּת יוֹם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּימָמָא בְּלֵילְיָא לְמָה לִי, וּדְלֵילְיָא בִּימָמָא לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi taught** the following *baraita* **before Rava:** With regard to the verse: "And if you take as collateral your neighbor's garment, **you shall restore it to him until the sun goes down"** (Exodus 22:25), **this** is referring to **a garment** worn at **night** and teaches that the garment is returned during the day; and with regard to the verse: **"You shall restore to him the collateral when the sun goes down"** (Deuteronomy 24:13), **this** is referring to **a garment** worn during the **day.** Rava **said to him:** This statement is puzzling, as with regard to a garment worn **in the day, why do I** need it **at night, and** as for a garment worn **at night, why do I** need it **in the day?** What purpose is served by giving back the garments at such times?

Rav Shizi taught before Rava a *baraita* reconciling two verses. One verse in *Shemos* seems to speak of returning the garment by sunrise; one verse in *Devarim* speaks of returning it by sunset. *Kevo hashemesh teshivenu lo* — Rashi reads this as: *veyehi beyado ad bo hashemesh*, let it remain with him until sunset. So one verse says keep the garment with the debtor through the night and return it at sunrise; the other says keep it with the debtor through the day and return it at sunset.

*Zu kesut lailah* — one verse addresses a night garment: the debtor keeps it through the night and returns it in the morning. *Zu kesut yom* — the other addresses a day garment: the debtor keeps it through the day and the creditor takes it back at nightfall.

### Day/Night Problem

But this creates an immediate problem. What good does it do the *ani* to receive his night garment back at sunrise, or his day garment at sunset? Having one's pajamas returned at dawn is of no practical benefit.

### Solution Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיסְמְיַיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״עַד בֹּא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ תְּשִׁיבֶנּוּ לוֹ״ – זוֹ כְּסוּת יוֹם שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה לַחֲבוֹל בַּלַּיְלָה, ״הָשֵׁב תָּשִׁיב לוֹ אֶת הַעֲבוֹט כְּבוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״ – זוֹ כְּסוּת לַיְלָה שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה לַחֲבוֹל בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheizevi **said to him:** Do you think this *baraita* is so corrupt that **I** should **erase it,** i.e., no longer teach it? Rava **said to him: No,** do not erase it, because **this** is what it **is saying:** With regard to the verse **"You shall restore it to him until when the sun goes down," this** is referring to **a garment** worn during the **day, which may be taken as collateral by night** but must be returned to the debtor for the day. With regard to the verse **"You shall restore to him the collateral when the sun goes down," this** is referring to **a garment** worn at **night, which may be taken as collateral by day.**

The Gemara resolves this by reframing what "night garment" and "day garment" mean from the creditor's perspective. *Ad bo hashemesh teshivenu lo*, let him keep it until sunset — that is a day garment: the debtor holds it during the day for use, and the creditor may reclaim it at nightfall. *Kevo hashemesh teshivenu lo*, return it to him at sunset — that is a night garment: the creditor holds it during the day and returns it at nightfall so the debtor can sleep in it. In short: day garments remain with the debtor during the day; night garments are returned to the debtor each night. The back-and-forth is not random — each item is with the appropriate party at the time it is needed.

## Benefits of Mashkon

### Death & Reclaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִשְׁכְּנוֹ וּמֵת, שׁוֹמְטוֹ מֵעַל גַּבֵּי בָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If he **took collateral from him,** returned it, **and** then the debtor **died,** the creditor may **take** the collateral **from** the debtor's **children** and is under no obligation to leave it with them.

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *mashkon umet shomto me-al gabei banav* — if the creditor took a *mashkon* and the debtor died, the creditor may reclaim that collateral from the heirs. Ordinarily, when a debtor dies, the creditor has liens only on real property (*karka*) and cannot collect from *mitaltelin* — movable goods — held by the heirs. But because this item has the status of a *mashkon*, the creditor retains rights over it even though he has been returning it daily and even though it is physically back in the debtor's household at the moment of death.

### Rabbi Meir's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁמְּמַשְׁכְּנִין לָמָּה מַחְזִירִין? לָמָּה מַחְזִירִין?! רַחֲמָנָא אָמַר אַהְדַּר! אֶלָּא: מֵאַחַר שֶׁמַּחְזִירִין,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir said: But since** one **takes collateral, why does** he **return** it? The Gemara expresses surprise at this question: **Why does** he **return** it? **The Merciful One states** to **return** it. **Rather,** the question is as follows: **Since** he must **return** it,

Rabbi Meir asks: *vechi me'achar she-machzirin lama machzirin* — given that the creditor must return the *mashkon*, why does he bother returning it? If he has to give it back every night anyway, what is the point? The Gemara initially reads this as: once you take collateral, why do you return it at all? But then the Gemara says the answer is obvious — *Rachmana amar hadar*, the Torah commands it. So the Gemara re-reads Rabbi Meir's question as: given that the Torah requires you to return it, why do you bother taking it back again the next day? It seems like a pointless exercise.

### Shmita & Movables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמָּה חוֹזְרִין וּמְמַשְׁכְּנִין? שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא שְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתּוֹ, וְלֹא יֵעָשֶׂה מִטַּלְטְלִין אֵצֶל בָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **why** does one **go back and take the collateral** again, as the creditor must anyway restore it to the debtor the following day? The Gemara replies: Any loan that is secured by collateral is not canceled by the Sabbatical Year, in contrast to other debts, which are canceled. Therefore, this ensures **that the Sabbatical** Year **should not cancel it. And** an additional reason is so that the collateral **should not become movable property** in the possession **of his children,** as one generally cannot claim such items from orphans to pay for their father's debt.

The Gemara answers: *shelo tehei shevi'it meshamtetto* — taking and maintaining the *mashkon* prevents the loan from being annulled by *shmita*. Since the loan is in some sense already "seized" and embodied in the collateral arrangement, *shmita* does not wipe it out. *V'lo yehei metaltelin etzel banav* — and it allows the creditor to collect from movable goods even after the debtor's death, which he could not otherwise do.

The creditor therefore perpetuates the daily exercise of taking and returning not out of formalism but for these concrete legal benefits.

### Rav Adda's Refinement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דַּהֲדַר וּמַשְׁכְּנֵיהּ, הָא לָא הֲדַר וּמַשְׁכְּנֵיהּ – לָא! אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָא: וְלָאו תָּרוֹצֵי קָא מְתָרְצַתְּ לַהּ? תָּרֵיץ הָכִי: וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁמַּחְזִירִין לָמָּה מְמַשְׁכְּנִין מֵעִיקָּרָא – שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא שְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתּוֹ, וְלֹא יֵעָשֶׂה מִטַּלְטְלִין אֵצֶל בָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: The **reason** these exceptions apply is **that** he **goes back and takes it as collateral; but** if he did **not go back and take it as collateral,** these exceptions do **not** apply. This *baraita* therefore contradicts the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, who held that the initial seizing of collateral is sufficient to grant the creditor full rights to it. **Rav Adda bar Mattana said: And did you not resolve** this *baraita* once already by adjusting its wording slightly? **Answer** it by changing its formulation again in **this** manner: **And since** one must **return** the collateral, **why** does one **take collateral at the outset?** It is done so **that the Sabbatical** Year **should not cancel** the debt, **and** so that it **should not become movable property** in the possession **of his children.** This version is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as it teaches that one who has taken collateral even on a single occasion may confiscate it from the debtor's heirs.

The Gemara then raises a subtle objection. If the benefit against *shmita* and the right to collect from *mitaltelin* after death depend on the creditor's currently being in possession of the *mashkon*, then when the debtor dies at a moment when the *mashkon* has been returned — when it is physically in the debtor's home — has the creditor lost those rights?

Rav Adda bar Matna resolves this by amending the reading of the question: *vechi me'achar she-machzirin lama memashkenin me'ikara* — given that you are going to have to return it, why bother taking it as collateral in the first place? The answer — *shelo tehei shevi'it meshamtetto velo yehei metaltelin etzel banav* — is the same, but the framing is different. The benefits attach not to the creditor's physical possession at any given moment but to the *status* established by the original act of taking the item as collateral. Even when the *mashkon* is currently back in the debtor's home, the rights persist because the collateral relationship was established. Whether *shmita* arrives or the debtor dies while the item is physically with the debtor or with the creditor makes no difference — the *mashkon* relationship provides the protection regardless.

### Incentive to Repay

Tosafot, in his concluding remarks on this topic, adds a further practical dimension. Rav Chananel had asked: given that you must return it, why do you bother taking it back again day after day? Tosafot's answer goes beyond the legal benefits: *kedei shelo yuchal haloveh lichfor* — so that the debtor cannot later deny the debt, since the ongoing exercise of the creditor coming to take and return the *mashkon* is itself ongoing acknowledgment of the debt's existence. And further: *vegam yemaher lifroa hachov, u-livakesh ma'os, ki yitbayesh bechol yom va-yom yachziru mashkono* — the daily ritual is genuinely demeaning for the debtor. Each day someone comes to his door and takes away his belongings, even if they are returned that same evening. This ongoing indignity creates powerful pressure on the debtor to find the money and pay off the loan as quickly as possible. The *mashkon* arrangement thus serves not only as legal security for the creditor but as a socially effective mechanism for encouraging prompt repayment.
