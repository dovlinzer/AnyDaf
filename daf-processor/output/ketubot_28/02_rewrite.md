# Ketubot 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zechariah ben HaKatzav

### Non-Jews Enter City

The daf opens by returning to the *mishna* at the bottom of 27b, which deals with the question of whether women's marital status is compromised when enemy troops enter a city. Although the *mishna* appears to treat the concern broadly — that any woman married to a *kohen* may be suspected of having been violated — the *gemara* effectively narrows the circumstances to the point where the rule would rarely apply. The *mishna* already establishes that a single witness is believed in such a case, though there is debate about a maidservant or the husband himself. Working within the framework of the *mishna*, which does not credit the husband's testimony, we arrive at the case of Zechariah ben HaKatzav.

### Zechariah Testimony

The *mishna* records: *Amar Zechariah ben HaKatzav: hamaon hazeh* — by this Temple, he swore, *lo zaza yada mitoch yadi mishesha nichnesu ovdei kochavim li'Yerushalayim v'ad sheyatzu* — from the time the non-Jews entered Jerusalem until they left, her hand never left my hand. Whether this refers to the destruction of the Temple or a later episode such as the time of Bar Kochba is uncertain. The claim is clear: he was with her the entire time, so she could not have been violated. Yet the Rabbis respond: *ein adam me'id al atzmo* — a person cannot testify on his own behalf. Because his testimony bears directly on his wife's permissibility to him, it is considered self-interested and therefore inadmissible. What is striking here is that both he and she presumably know the true facts — and yet the rabbinic posture is that they cannot simply continue their lives together on the basis of their own knowledge.

### Living Arrangements

A *tanna* adds: *v'af al pi chen yiched lah bayit bechatzeiro* — even so, he set aside a house for her within his courtyard. They did not — or could not — simply resume cohabitation, yet she remained within the family compound. The arrangement was carefully managed: *uch'she'hi yotza yotza berosh b'nei uch'she'hi nichneset nichneset besof b'nei* — when she left she went at the head of the children, and when she entered she came in at the rear of the group, so that she would never be left alone with him in the courtyard. Whether they divorced formally or simply ceased living as husband and wife is not entirely clear from the text, but the living arrangement itself was structured to prevent *yichud*.

## Gerusha Restrictions

### Abaye's Question

This case prompts Abaye to pose a question: *mahu la'asot begerusha kein?* — what about arranging a similar arrangement for a *gerusha*, a divorced woman? That is, if a *kohen* divorces his wife and she wishes to continue living within the same courtyard — perhaps to continue raising the children — can that arrangement be structured so as to prevent *yichud* and thereby be permissible?

### Shvuyah vs. Gerusha

The *gemara* distinguishes the cases. In the earlier scenario, the leniency derives from the fact that we are dealing with a *shvuyah* — or a woman in analogous circumstances — where there is doubt about what actually occurred. *Hatam hu debishvuyah hikilu* — there they were lenient because it was not established that she was actually forbidden. *Aval hacha lo* — but here, in the case of a *gerusha*, there is no doubt: she is definitively forbidden to her former husband who is a *kohen*. The question remains whether this distinction is determinative: *o dilma lo shna* — or perhaps it makes no difference?

### Baraita Shchunah

*Ta shema, ditanya* — come and hear what was taught in a *baraita*: *Hamgaresh ishto lo tinnasei bishchunutah* — if a man divorces his wife, she should not remarry and settle within the same *shchunah*, the same neighborhood. Rashi explains the concern: this previously married couple, who now belong to others, know each other's body language and habits well, and proximity will make it dangerously easy for them to fall into an illicit relationship. *V'im haya kohen lo tadur immo bemavui* — and if the divorcing husband was a *kohen*, she cannot even live with him in the same *mavui*, even if she has not remarried at all.

### Shchunah/Mavui Defined

It is worth clarifying the spatial terminology. A *shchunah* refers to a neighborhood in the general sense — Tosafot defines it as within three houses. A *mavui* is an alley or passage into which multiple courtyards open, making it a larger area than the *chatzer*, the courtyard, where individual homes face each other. The earlier case of Zechariah ben HaKatzav involved housing within a single *chatzer* — *bayit bechatzeiro*. The *baraita* here speaks of a *kohen* and his *gerusha* needing to be separated by an even greater distance, namely the entire *mavui*, precisely because when she is not remarried the concern for renewed intimacy is even more acute.

Tosafot notes that the distinction maps onto the level of concern: when she has remarried another man, keeping them out of the same *shchunah* suffices. But when she is unmarried and forbidden to him as his *gerusha*, they must be entirely out of the same *mavui*. If the town is a small village, the *baraita* treats it as equivalent to a *shchunah*, though the Bach emends this to read *kemavui*.

### Who Must Move

This raises a practical question: *Mi nidche mipnei mi?* — who must actually leave? The language of the *baraita* — *lo tadur*, *lo tinnasei* — places the obligation on her. A further *baraita* confirms: *hi nidcheit mipanav, veino nidche mipaneha* — she is the one displaced by his presence; he need not move on account of her. The underlying logic is that upon divorce she leaves the household — *veyatza mibeito* — and so the question of where she resettles is hers to manage, not his.

However, this changes if she owns the property: *veim hayta chatzer shelah, hu nidche mipaneha* — if the courtyard was hers, he is the one who must move.

### Joint Ownership

A question is then raised: *hayta chatzer shel shneihem mahu?* — what if the courtyard was jointly owned? Both have a legal claim to remain. The *gemara* attempts to resolve this by examining the source of the rule that she is displaced: *hi nidcheit mipanav* — the initial statement assumed his courtyard. If it were hers, he moves. The joint case is left ambiguous, and the *gemara* considers the possibility that the ruling applies only when one party is a renter with no real ownership stake — *dilma de'oger miger* — in which case she, as the departing party, would be the one to go. But when there is true joint title, who prevails is genuinely unclear.

### Rav's Statement

The *gemara* resolves the case of joint ownership by appeal to a verse and a ruling of Rav. The verse, *Hinei Hashem metaltelcha taltela gever*, speaks of divine exile as a punishment — *taltela gever*, the exile of a man. From this Rav derives: *taltulei de'gavra kashi mede'itata* — it is harder for a man to be uprooted than for a woman. Since this was framed as a curse, the inference is that displacement is more burdensome for a man. Therefore, even in a case of joint ownership, she is the one required to move, as her uprooting causes relatively less hardship.

One might note the irony: the woman is called the *bayit*, the home; she is the one who raises the children and provides domestic stability. And yet the societal structure — the assumption that the household belongs to the man — leads to this conclusion. The practical reality in many divorce situations today, of course, runs in the opposite direction, precisely because the woman's stability is recognized as essential for the children.

## Legal Proceedings

### Loan Repayment

The *gemara* continues in this vein, addressing further contact between a *kohen* and his divorced wife. *Tanu Rabbanan*: if when they were married he took out a loan from her — Rashi explains this refers to property she brought into the marriage, the *nichsei milog* — and now they are divorced, *ela einah nifraat ela al yedei acher* — she may only collect repayment through an intermediary. The concern is that having them meet privately for the purpose of a financial transaction will lead to intimacy. Similarly, she should not personally press the claim; she too should send a representative.

### Rav Sheshet

Rav Sheshet goes considerably further: *de'atu lekaman ledina, lo mizdakkinnan lehu* — if they come before us for a court case, we refuse to hear it. This is a striking and puzzling position. Any two people involved in a legal dispute must be able to bring their matter before a *beit din*. The concern, as Rav Pappa explains, is that litigation between a *kohen* and his *gerusha* will involve repeated meetings — at the courthouse, over discussions of the case — and familiarity will breed opportunity. One might ask: is the alternative really better? Refusing to adjudicate hardly eliminates the tension; if anything, it forces them to deal with their dispute without any structure at all.

### Shamta/Lashes

The alternatives offered are even more puzzling. Rav Pappa says *shamutei mishamttinan lehu* — we place them under *shamta*, a minor form of excommunication. Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua adds *nagudei nami menagdinan lehu* — we even administer lashes. What this is meant to accomplish in practical terms is difficult to understand. It does not resolve their dispute or give either party a remedy.

### Rav Nachman Source

Rav Nachman cites a Tannaitic source: *Tana d'vei Abba Rabbati*, meaning the tractate known as *Masechet Semachot* (also called *Avel Rabbati*), a collection of Tannaitic and Amoraic statements. Though associated with mourning law, the passage here is not about *aveilut* at all.

### Nisuin vs. Eirussin

The source clarifies: *bamei devarim amurim* — when do these restrictions apply? *Shenitgarsha min hanisuinn* — when they had actually lived together as husband and wife, having had full *nisuinn*. *Aval nitgarsha min haeirussin* — but if they had only been *mekudeshet* and the marriage was dissolved before *nisuinn*, with no prior sexual relationship, then *nifraat al yedei atzmah* — she may herself be present to collect the loan, *she'ein libo gas bah* — because he does not feel so comfortable with her that contact will lead to intimacy.

### Rava's Intermediary

A case then comes before Rava: a man and woman who had been betrothed — *eirusin* without *nisuinn* — came to him for a court case. Rav Adda bar Matna was sitting before him. Rava placed a third party between them — *oki Rava shlucha beinaihu* — so that they could each present their positions through a representative rather than address each other directly. This is the practical solution the *gemara* has been moving toward.

Rav Adda bar Matna challenges Rava: we have just learned from Rav Nachman that in the case of *eirusin* alone, no such precaution is necessary. Rava responds: *k'chazina d'kagaissi b'hadadi* — I can see from their body language that they are very comfortable with one another, and so I am being appropriately cautious.

### Implementation Dispute

An alternative version of the exchange presents the case in reverse. According to *ika d'amri* — some say — Rava did *not* initially put an intermediary in place. Rav Adda bar Matna suggested he should, citing Rav Nachman's leniency for *eirusin* cases. Rava replied that in this particular instance the couple's ease with one another was evident — *k'chazina d'ka gaissi b'hadadi* — and therefore caution was warranted. The two versions converge on the same outcome: the intermediary is required when the couple demonstrates a level of comfort that creates genuine risk, regardless of whether they had experienced *nisuinn*.

One student's observation sheds light on the earlier puzzle of refusing to hear cases or imposing *shamta* and lashes: perhaps those rulings apply only where no intermediary arrangement has been established. Once the *gemara* arrives at Rava's solution, it retroactively supplies the practical mechanism that makes the other restrictions coherent.

## Childhood Testimony

### Mishna Categories

The *mishna* — the final *mishna* of the chapter — introduces a new set of rules: *v'eilu ne'emanim l'ha'id b'godlan mah she'ra'u b'kotnan* — the following are believed to testify as adults about what they saw when they were children. The underlying principle requires explanation. The *gemara* will establish that for standard testimony, a person must be a *kosher eid* — a valid witness — not only at the moment of testifying but at the moment of witnessing. A child, like a slave or non-Jew, is not a valid witness. Therefore, observations made in childhood would ordinarily be inadmissible. The *mishna* lists the exceptions, and the *gemara* will explain each one.

This *mishna* serves as a fitting conclusion to the chapter. The chapter began with the question of whether a woman was a *besulah* at the time of her first marriage — relevant to her *kesubah* of 200. That led to discussions of testimony about personal status, about weddings and witnesses, about women suspected of violation. Now the chapter closes by addressing how certain kinds of testimony about such matters — observed in childhood — may nonetheless be accepted.

### Signature Recognition

The first category: *ne'eman adam lomar zeh ktav yado shel abba, v'zeh ktav yado shel rebbi, v'zeh ktav yado shel achi* — a person may testify that he recognizes the signature of his father, his teacher, or his brother, having seen it as a child. This is relevant to *kiyum shtaros*, the rabbinic requirement to validate the signatures on a document before it may be enforced in court.

The second category: *zachur hayiti b'ploni sheyatza behinuma verosha parua* — I remember that so-and-so went out in that bridal procession with her hair uncovered, meaning it was a first wedding, with all the ceremony and display that that entails. This ties directly back to the first *mishna* of the chapter, about whether a woman was a virgin at the time of her marriage, which determines whether her *kesubah* is 200 or 100.

### Establishing Kohen

The third and fourth categories involve testimony establishing someone as a *kohen*. One may testify: *v'shayah ish ploni yotzei mibeit hasefer litvol leechol bitrumah* — I remember that a certain man would leave our school, go to the *mikvah*, and eat *terumah*. Or: *v'shayah cholek immanu al hagoren* — he would come with us and receive a portion at the granary. Both are forms of childhood observation that can establish someone's priestly status.

### Beit Hapras

One may also testify: *vehamakom hazeh beit hapras* — this area is a *beit hapras*, a field in which a grave has been plowed over and is therefore off-limits to *kohanim* due to *tumah* concerns. At first glance this seems puzzling, since such testimony restricts rather than permits. Tosafot explains that the leniency runs in the opposite direction: when a *beit hapras* cannot be precisely located within a large plot of land, *kohanim* are barred from the entire area. A childhood witness who can pinpoint the location of the grave thereby *permits* access to the rest of the field. The testimony, properly understood, is a *kulah*.

### Techumim

A further category: *vead kan hayinu ba'im b'shabbat* — we would come only this far on *Shabbat*, or we would go as far as this point, establishing the *techum*, the Sabbath boundary for the town. Since it is laborious to verify the precise location of the last inhabited structure and measure 2,000 *amot* from it, the testimony of someone who recalls the boundary from childhood carries practical weight.

### Not Believed Cases

The *mishna* then draws a sharp contrast. One is *not* believed to say *derech hayah l'ploni bemakom hazeh* — that a certain person had a right-of-way through this field — or *ma'amad umisped hayah l'ploni bemakom hazeh* — that a funeral procession with its attendant gathering of mourners would take place on this patch of land belonging to another. Both of these claims would effectively transfer property rights from one party to another. These are strict *dinei mamonot* cases, requiring formal *edut*. Childhood observation cannot supply it.

The contrast is instructive: matters of personal status, priestly standing, Sabbath boundaries, and document validation are all areas where the *mishna* makes exceptions. But the moment the testimony would result in taking property from one person and assigning rights to another, the full rigor of evidentiary law applies without exception.

## Gemara Analysis

### Kiyum Shtarot

The *gemara* now works through each category in the *mishna* to explain why the exception is warranted. Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua opens with a general qualification: *vehu sheyesh gadol immo* — the child is believed only when testifying together with an adult witness. This qualification applies at minimum to the first case of signature recognition, where two witnesses are always required to validate a document. Whether it applies to some of the other cases as well is a matter of debate among the *rishonim*.

The *gemara* also notes that all three examples of signature recognition — father, brother, and teacher — are necessary and cannot be derived from any two. If only the father's signature were mentioned, one might say the child was believed *mishum d'shchiach gabei* — because he spent so much time with his father and saw the signature frequently. But one might not extend this to the teacher. Conversely, if only the teacher's signature were mentioned, one would say the child was believed *mishum d'isa emteh d'rabbo* — because fear of the teacher made the child pay particularly careful attention, and he would never claim to recognize the signature unless he was certain. But one might exclude the father on that basis. And neither consideration applies to a brother, who is neither so constantly present as the father nor so awe-inspiring as the teacher — *d'lais lei lo hai velo hai*. Hence all three must be stated.

The underlying reason the *mishna* accepts this testimony at all: *keivan d'kiyum shtaros m'drabbanan, heimnu rabbanan b'drabbanan* — since the validation of documents is itself only a rabbinic requirement, the rabbis were willing to be lenient in accepting this testimony for a rabbinic matter.

### First Wedding

For the case of testimony that a woman was married with the full ceremony of a first wedding — *gilluy milsa b'alma hu* — the *gemara* explains that this is not really testimony in the formal sense at all. *Keivan d'rov nashim besulos nisas* — since most women who marry do so as virgins, in a first wedding, we are already operating from a strong presumption. The testimony of the childhood witness merely confirms what we would assume in any event. Rashi explains: *ein hadavar tzarich edus ela l'farseim sheyeitzei hadin l'or belo gimgum* — we do not truly need the testimony; it is only required so that the ruling can be issued with clarity and without hesitation. Since it is not substantive testimony so much as confirmation of an existing presumption, we are more lenient about its source.

### Slave Distinction

For the testimony establishing someone as a *kohen* — that he would leave school, go to the *mikvah*, and eat *terumah* — the *gemara* raises an objection: *v'dilma eved kohen hu?* — perhaps he was a slave belonging to a *kohen* rather than a *kohen* himself? Slaves of *kohanim* are permitted to eat *terumah*, so the mere fact of eating *terumah* does not prove priestly lineage.

The *gemara* answers by invoking the principle of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *asur l'adam sheyelameid avdo Torah* — it is forbidden to give one's slave a formal Torah education. If the person in question was attending school — *beit hasefer* — he cannot have been a slave, since slaves were not sent for formal Jewish schooling. One might ask: what kind of slave is this? It is an *eved Kena'ani*, a non-Jewish slave, who is on a trajectory toward partial observance but is not sent to *cheder*. The Talmud notes that such a slave might acquire some self-taught knowledge and might even read *pesukim* on his own — and a *baraita* confirms that even one who reads three *pesukim* in the synagogue, wears *tefillin* before his master, or serves as an *apitropos* over his master's estate does not thereby achieve freedom (*harei zeh lo yatza l'cheirus*). The *gemara* distinguishes between self-acquired knowledge and formal schooling: *d'kanoi bei minhag banim* — it is specifically sending a slave to school in the manner of sons that is forbidden. Since the person in question was being formally educated, he was clearly not a slave.

### Terumah D'rabbanan

Having established that the person was a *kohen* and not a slave, the *gemara* turns to why we believe the childhood witness on this point. The answer: *b'terumah d'rabbanan* — we believe him only to the extent of permitting the person to eat *terumah d'rabbanan*, rabbinic *terumah*. We are not establishing his priestly status for more consequential matters — such as questions of lineage or marriage — on the basis of childhood observation alone. Again, the pattern holds: childhood testimony is accepted for rabbinic matters or for things where formal testimony is not strictly required, but not for biblical-level determinations or for the transfer of property.

### Terumah Distribution

The *gemara* then asks about the second proof of *kehuna* — that the person would come to the granary and receive a portion — and raises the same concern: *v'dilma eved kohen hu?* — perhaps he was a slave of a *kohen*? Here the answer is different. A *baraita* is cited: *ein cholkin terumah l'eved ela im ken rabbo immo* — according to Rabbi Yehuda, you do not distribute *terumah* to a slave unless his master is present. The reason is that in a community where people draw an inference from receiving *terumah* to priestly status — *maalin mi-terumah le-yichusin* — giving *terumah* to a slave in the absence of his master would cause observers to wrongly conclude that the slave is himself a *kohen*. Therefore, if someone is receiving *terumah* independently, we may assume he is actually a *kohen*.

Rabbi Yose disagrees, permitting a slave to go on his own and state: *im kohen ani tnu li bishvil atzmi, v'im eved kohen ani tnu li bishvil rabi* — if I am the *kohen*, give it to me for myself; if I am the slave of a *kohen*, give it to me on behalf of my master. This arises in a case where a free woman and a slave woman both gave birth simultaneously and the infants were mixed up, so that neither boy knows whether he is the master or the slave. Rabbi Yose permits one to go independently in this fashion.

The *gemara* notes that the positions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose are perfectly correlated: in the place of Rabbi Yehuda, where receiving *terumah* serves as an indicator of priestly status for purposes of *yichusin*, one does not give to a slave without the master present. In the place of Rabbi Yose, where no such inference is drawn, one may give to a slave independently. The two rulings are logically interdependent.

### Rabbi Elazar Tale

A *baraita* concludes with a cautionary tale from Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yose. He states: *mi-yamai lo he'edati* — throughout my life I was scrupulous about not giving testimony, out of fear of causing harm through error. But *paam achas he'edati* — once I did testify — *he'alu eved le-kehuna al pi* — and based on my testimony they nearly elevated a slave to priestly status.

The *gemara* immediately asks: can we really say that happened? *Ve-hashta ma-behemtan shel tzadikim ein Hakadosh Baruch Hu mevi takala al yadam* — if God does not bring about stumbling even through the animals of the righteous (alluding to the famous story of the donkey of Pinchas ben Yair, which refused to eat food that was *tevel*), how much more so would God not allow the righteous themselves to sin? Therefore the *gemara* amends the account: *ela bikshu le-haalos eved le-kehuna al pi* — they were on the verge of doing so, and the mistake was caught at the last moment.

What happened? Rabbi Elazar had seen someone receive *terumah* independently in the locale of Rabbi Yose — where such practice is permitted even for a slave. He then went and testified about it in the locale of Rabbi Yehuda — where independent receipt of *terumah* is taken as proof of priestly status. The observation was accurate; the inference drawn from it was wrong, because the evidentiary standard was different in the two communities.

Tosafot, in several places, notes that the principle *ein Hakadosh Baruch Hu mevi takala* — that God does not cause the righteous to stumble — applies specifically to matters of forbidden food. The connection here is not exact, since it is about priestly status rather than directly about eating. But the *gemara* invokes the principle nonetheless. Whatever its precise scope, the tale is a vivid illustration of why testimony must be evaluated not only for its factual accuracy but for the interpretive framework within which it is being offered.

## Additional Categories

### Family Status

The *gemara* turns to the remaining categories of the *mishna* and the *baraita* that supplements it. The *baraita* states that a child is believed to testify: *kach amar li abba, mishpachah zu tehorah, mishpachah zu tmeiah* — my father told me that this family is *tehorah* and this family is *tmeiah*. The *gemara* immediately flags the language: *tehorah u'tmeiah salka da'atach?* — can you really have families classified as ritually pure or impure in the relevant sense? The *gemara* clarifies that this is a euphemism — or more precisely, a substitution — and the actual content is: *mishpachah zu ksheirah, mishpachah zu psulah* — this family is of fit lineage (*ksheira*), this family is of disqualified lineage (*psulah*), meaning perhaps there are *challalim* or *mamzerim* among them.

This is a considerably more sweeping claim than the other categories. Here the child is being believed not merely for the purpose of *terumah d'rabbanan* but apparently for personal status and lineage — *yichusin* — itself. The *gemara* does not dwell on this tension, moving on to the *ketzatzah* case.

### Akum/Eved

The *baraita* then addresses cases analogous to childhood: *im hayah akum v'nisgayer, eved v'nishtachrer, ein ne'emanim* — if a person was a non-Jew when he witnessed something and has since converted, or was a slave when he witnessed it and has since been freed, he is not believed. This is the default position. The question is whether such a person is in a position structurally parallel to a child — someone not yet a *kosher eid* who has now attained that status — or whether the gap is more fundamental.

The *baraita* also reiterates that in neither the case of a convert nor a freed slave is the person believed to testify about rights of way or funeral procession rights — those remain strictly in the category of *dinei mamonot* requiring formal testimony.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka dissents: *ne'emanim* — they are believed. His position is that a former non-Jew or slave who observed certain matters is believed to testify about them now.

### Attention Debate

The *gemara* analyzes the debate between the anonymous first opinion and Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka in psychological terms. *Tanna Kama savar kivan d'akum hu lo hava dayik* — the first *tanna* holds that since the person was a non-Jew or slave at the time of witnessing, he was not paying careful attention. The events of the Jewish world were not his concern; he lacked the context to understand what he was seeing. *Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka savar kivan d'daiteih l'igiyurei mah daik yifei* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka holds that since the person intended to convert, he was paying close attention. The debate is thus not formal but psychological: was this person in a position to register what he witnessed with the care and comprehension that valid testimony requires?

One might note the difficulty with this framing: the *tanna kama*'s position is understandable for someone with no connection to conversion at all, but Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka's leniency seems to assume the person was already on the path to conversion at the moment of witnessing — which may not always have been the case. For a freed slave, the question of whether he "intended" freedom at the time of witnessing is even more complex. The Talmud also suggests that this could be a case of *masiach l'fi tumo* — offhand, unselfconscious testimony — which carries its own evidentiary weight.

### Ketzatzah Case

The *gemara* finally turns to the *ketzatzah* case: *mai ketzatzah?* — what is this *ketzatzah* ceremony? The Rabbis taught: *Keitzad ketzatzah? Echad min ha'achim shenasa isha she'eina hogenes lo* — what is *ketzatzah*? If one of the brothers married a woman beneath his family's standing, *ba'in bnei mishpacha u'mevi'in chavit m'leia peiros, v'shovrin osa b'emtza rechova* — members of the family would come, bring a barrel filled with fruit, and smash it in the middle of the street. *V'omerim: acheinu beit Yisrael, shim'u — ploni nasa isha she'eina hogenes lo* — they would declare publicly: our brothers, the Jewish people, hear us — this man has married a woman unbefitting to him. *U'misyar'im anu shema yisarev zaro b'zareinu* — and we fear lest his offspring intermarry with ours, since by this marriage his children's *yichus* has been diminished. *V'kachu lachem dugma l'doros shelo yisarev zaro b'zareinu* — take this as a lesson for generations: his seed shall not mix with ours.

This is the ceremony that a child may testify he witnessed. What he learns from it — and what he transmits — is *yichus* information: this family's status, and by extension the standing of those who marry into it or out of it. The weight placed on lineage throughout this chapter is nowhere more dramatically illustrated than here, where a family stages a public shaming of one of its own members rather than allow the perception that his descendants carry their name and their status.

The *gemara* closes the chapter by noting the symbolic resonance of the barrel. At a first wedding, the barrel of wine — associated with the imagery of the bride — was a sign of joy and wholeness. Here, the barrel is filled with fruit, and it is smashed. If wine in the wedding context evokes the bride herself, and children are the fruit of the union, then the smashing of a barrel of fruit in the public square is a declaration that the fruit of *this* union will not be grafted onto the family tree. The imagery with which the *perek* began — the status of a bride, the question of her first wedding, the *kesubah* she is owed — closes with this public reckoning with the consequences of a marriage made without regard for *yichus*.
