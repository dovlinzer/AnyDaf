# Zevachim 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Akiva & Blemishes

### Akiva's Leniency

The daf opens with an explanation of Rabbi Akiva's position regarding the principle of *alu lo yerdu* — that items placed upon the *mizbeiach* need not be removed. The *gemara* on 85b had included animals with a *baal mum* among the cases where *im alu lo yerdu* applies, which is initially difficult. A *baal mum* is a classic example of *lo hayu psulan bakodesh* — the disqualification did not arise from a defect in a ritual performed within the context of sanctification. The question, then, is how Rabbi Akiva could extend *alu lo yerdu* to such a case at all.

### Yochanan's Limitation

The *gemara* resolves this by significantly narrowing Rabbi Akiva's position. Rabbi Yochanan states: *lo hichshir Rabbi Akiva ela bidukin sheba'ayin* — Rabbi Akiva only permitted the case of a minor blemish resembling a cataract of the eye. The reasoning is *ho'il vekasher be'ofos*: since this type of blemish has an analogue in the laws of bird offerings, where a bird is disqualified only if an entire limb is missing, there is a foothold for permitting the animal to remain on the *mizbeiach*. Without such an analogue, the animal would fall squarely into the category of *lo hayu psulan bakodesh* and would have to be removed.

One might note that many blemishes more severe than a cataract are equally inapplicable to birds, yet Rashi explains that more severe blemishes are *megunneh* — unseemly — and therefore inappropriate to present as an offering to God. The *gemara* thus limits Rabbi Akiva to something nearly invisible, even as the bird analogy might logically support a broader category. The tension is acknowledged but the *gemara* holds firm to the narrow reading.

### Blemish Before Kedushá

A further limitation is added: *vehu shekadam hekdeshan es mumam* — Rabbi Akiva's leniency applies only when the sanctification preceded the blemish, such that the *psul* arose after the animal was already consecrated. This brings the case closer to the category of *psulo bakodesh*, since the disqualification occurred within the context of sanctification. If, however, the blemish preceded the sanctification, then from the very outset the animal never acquired a genuine *kedushat mizbeiach*, and even according to Rabbi Akiva it would have to be removed if placed on the *mizbeiach*.

### Female Ola Case

On this basis, Rabbi Akiva would also concede the case of a female animal consecrated as an *olah*. Since an *olah* may only be male, a female animal designated as an *olah* is, at the moment of consecration, already unfit. The *gemara* analogizes this to a case where the *mum* preceded the *hekdesh*, since the disqualification was present from the moment the *kedushah* was conferred, and therefore no genuine *kedushat olah* was ever established. Even Rabbi Akiva would agree that such an animal, if placed on the *mizbeiach*, must be brought down. The *gemara* itself notes that being the wrong sex is not technically a *mum* — it acknowledges the category is being used loosely — but the operative principle is the same: when the problem antedated the consecration, *alu yerdu*.

## Nirva Be'ofot

### Yirmiyah's Question

The *gemara* transitions to a question posed by Rabbi Yirmiyah: *yesh nirva be'ofos o ein nirva be'ofos?* — does the disqualification of *nirva* (an animal that was the object of a sexual act by a human) apply to birds? The general rule, derived from the word *min habeheimah*, is that one may bring an offering from domesticated animals but not from all animals — and among the excluded categories are a *rovei'ah* (a male animal that penetrated a human) and a *nirva* (an animal that was penetrated by a human male). Rabbi Yirmiyah's question is whether these disqualifications extend to birds.

### Two Approaches

Two interpretive frameworks are proposed. The first holds that *nirva* and *rovei'ah* are a package deal: *kol heicha de'isa beroveia isa benireva, kol heicha delisa beroveia lisa benireva* — wherever the disqualification of *rovei'ah* applies, so does that of *nirva*, and wherever *rovei'ah* cannot apply, *nirva* does not apply either. Since it is anatomically impossible for a bird to perform an act of penetration upon a human, it cannot be a *rovei'ah*, and therefore the disqualification of *nirva* would not apply to it either.

The second framework rejects the package deal approach entirely and instead invokes an abstract category: *harei ne'evda ba aveira* — regardless of the specific mechanism, any animal used in an act of transgression is thereby disqualified. Under this reading, the inquiry into *rovei'ah* and *nirva* as paired concepts is beside the point; the mere fact of having been used in sin would suffice to disqualify the bird.

### Rava's Proof

Rava adduces a proof from Rabbi Akiva's position. The *mishnah* lists *rovei'ah* and *nirva* among the cases where *im alu yerdu* — where the animal must be removed from the *mizbeiach*. Rabbi Akiva disagrees only regarding *ba'alei mumim* and does not extend his leniency to *rovei'ah* and *nirva*. Rava reasons: *ve'im isa* — if birds were in fact permitted even in a case of *nirva*, Rabbi Akiva should have applied the same logic he used for the cataract blemish. Just as the cataract has an analogue in the laws of birds and thereby qualifies for *im alu lo yerdu* even in a large animal, so too, if birds were unaffected by *nirva*, Rabbi Akiva should have argued that the animal-*nirva* case has a bird analogue and warranted the same treatment. Since Rabbi Akiva says nothing of the kind, it must be that he concedes birds are also disqualified by *nirva*. The *gemara* accepts this: *shema minah*.

It should be noted, however, that the proof is not entirely airtight. Rabbi Akiva's position on the cataract has already been limited to an almost imperceptible blemish on the grounds that more severe blemishes are *megunneh*. One could argue that *nirva* falls into the same category of something unseemly and would not be permitted regardless of the bird analogy. The *gemara* acknowledges the logic but accepts Rava's inference nonetheless.

### Bechoros Evidence

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a stronger and more direct proof from the *mishnah* in Bechoros. That *mishnah* lists the categories of disqualified birds whose *melikah* renders them *neveilah* rather than a valid *korban* offering. *Melikah* — the incision made with the thumbnail at the back of the neck — is the proper mode of slaughter for bird offerings; if performed on an invalid bird, it does not constitute a valid act of slaughter, and the bird is classified as *neveilah*, which conveys *tum'ah* to one who swallows it. Among the invalid birds listed is *hanireva* — a bird that was penetrated. The *mishnah* thus explicitly places a bird-*nirva* in the category of disqualified offerings. *Shema minah* — this is a far clearer proof than the inference from Rabbi Akiva's silence.

## Chanina & Practice

### Temple Practice

The *mishnah* records that Rabbi Chanina Segan Hakohanim testified that his father, during the period of the *Beis Hamikdash*, would actually push *ba'alei mumim* off the *mizbeiach* — in direct disagreement with Rabbi Akiva. Since the *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah* had already ruled that such animals must be removed, the question is what additional information Rabbi Chanina is conveying. One possibility is that he is simply providing eyewitness confirmation: the halachic debate notwithstanding, this is how it was actually practiced, and such historical testimony carries significant weight.

### K'Achar Yad Method

A second possibility is that Rabbi Chanina is specifying *how* the animals were removed: *k'achar yad* — in a subtle, incidental manner. Rashi explains that this means it was done privately and unobtrusively, without making a conspicuous display of removing something from the *mizbeiach*. To ostentatiously remove an animal from the altar in full public view would constitute a denigration of it. The *k'achar yad* method thus reflects a sensitivity to the dignity of the *mizbeiach* even in the act of correcting an error.

## Yerdu Lo Ya'alu

### Non-Restoration

The *mishnah* continues with the reciprocal principle: *k'sheim she'im alu lo yerdu, kach im yardu lo ya'alu* — just as certain invalid items that were placed on the *mizbeiach* need not be removed, so too if those items were actually brought down, they may not be restored to the *mizbeiach*. The leniency of *im alu lo yerdu* does not confer upon those items a positive status that would entitle them to go back up once removed.

### Mashla Bahen Ha'ur

Ulla qualifies this principle. He states: *lo shanu ela shelo mashla bahen ha'ur* — the rule that removed items may not be restored applies only if the fire had not yet taken hold of them. Once the fire has seized the item — *mashla bahen ha'ur* — it is transformed in status. The item is now described as *na'asa lachmo shel mizbeiach*, having become as it were the bread of the *mizbeiach*, the *lechem isheh* consumed by the fire. At that point, even if the item falls off the *mizbeiach*, it may be restored — because it is no longer regarded as something *pasul* that happens to be on the altar, but rather as something that properly belongs to the consuming fire. This principle applies, at minimum, to items in the category of *im alu lo yerdu*; its extension to other cases will be developed further.

### Bones Dispute

There is a dispute between Rav Mari and Rav Chanina Misura regarding which portion of the *mishnah* Ulla's ruling addresses. Rav Mari applies it to the *reisha* — the earlier portion of the *mishnah* dealing with invalid *korbanos* — while Rav Chanina Misura applies it to the *seifa*, which concerns the bones, sinews, horns, and hooves of an animal.

The *seifa* of the *mishnah* states that these components, when still attached (*mechubarim*) to the animal, are offered on the *mizbeiach* together with the *olah*, but if they have become separated (*pirshu*), they are not offered. Ulla's principle, according to Rav Chanina Misura, means that even these separated items — which in their separated state should not be placed on the *mizbeiach* at all — if they were placed on the *mizbeiach* and the fire seized them and they then fell off, may be restored.

The *gemara* then draws out the logical relationship between the two applications. If one holds that Ulla's principle applies to the *seifa* (the bones and hooves), it certainly applies to the *reisha* (the invalid *korbanos*), since the latter are at least items that have some claim to being offered, having achieved the status of *im alu lo yerdu*. But if one holds that it applies only to the *reisha*, it does not necessarily follow that it applies to the *seifa*. The bones and hooves, being items that would never intrinsically belong on the *mizbeiach* by themselves — unlike the limbs of a *pasul olah*, which are classically the type of thing offered — represent a more fundamental inapplicability. The *gemara* notes that, counterintuitively, the *pasul* limb of an *olah* is in one sense less problematic than a hoof, because the limb at least represents the classical content of a *mizbeiach* offering, whereas the hoof standing alone is fundamentally alien to it.

### Fire Seizing Detail

The discussion of *mashla bahen ha'ur* also admits of two scenarios. In the first, the hoof was still attached when the leg was placed on the *mizbeiach*, and as the fire burned the leg the hoof separated and flew off. In that case Ulla's principle would clearly apply: the hoof was legitimately on the *mizbeiach* and was seized by the fire before it separated. In the second scenario, the hoof had already separated before being placed on the *mizbeiach*, was placed up improperly (with the status of *im alu lo yerdu*), and then, after the fire had seized it, fell off. In that case as well, Ulla's principle would permit restoration. The difficulty of tracking all the permutations is acknowledged; the bottom line is that once the fire has genuinely taken hold of an item, its relationship to the *mizbeiach* is qualitatively different, and a subsequent fall does not preclude restoration — at minimum in terms of *mutar*, even if the question of *chayav* remains to be developed on the following *daf*.

## Bones & Hooves

### Attached vs. Separated

The next *mishnah* enumerates items that, if placed on the *mizbeiach*, must be removed: meat of *kodshei kodashim* that is not an *olah* (such as a *chatas*), meat of *kodashim kalim* (such as a *shlamim*), the remainder of the *omer*, the *shtei halechem*, the *lechem hapanim*, the remainder of a *mincha*, and the *ketoret* — all of which are consumed by the *kohanim* or have their designated mode of offering on the inner *mizbeiach*, and therefore do not belong on the outer *mizbeiach*. If placed there, *im alu yerdu*.

The *mishnah* then introduces the case of the wool on the heads of sheep and the hair from the beards of goats, and then returns to the bones, sinews, horns, and hooves. The rule is: *bizman shehem mechubarim ya'alu* — when attached, they are offered, as the verse states *v'hiktir hakohen et hakol*, the *kohen* shall cause the entire thing to be burned. The basis is *v'asita olotecha habasar v'hadam* — the *olah* is defined by the meat and the blood. When the bones and hooves are still integrated into the limb, they are part of *et hakol* and go up. But *pirshu lo ya'alu* — if they have separated, they do not go up separately, for the verse has established that the essence of the *olah* is the meat and the blood.

### Baraita Position

A *braita* takes the analysis further. Citing the same verse *v'hiktir hakohen et hakol*, the *braita* expands its scope to include the bones, sinews, horns, and hooves. The *braita* then considers whether perhaps even after separation they must be offered — *yachol afilu pirshu*. The tension with *v'asita olotecha habasar v'hadam* is resolved through the distinction: if attached, bring them up; if separated — but the *braita* then adds a further clause: *afilu hein berosh hamizbeach yerdu* — even if they are already atop the *mizbeiach*, they must be brought down. This goes beyond the *mishnah*, which had stated only *lo ya'alu* (they should not be brought up) without ruling that they must be removed if already placed. The *braita* also specifies that the obligation to restore pertains to the *basar v'dam* — the actual flesh — if it flies off the fire, but not to the bones and sinews, since those are not *basar v'dam*.

### Rabbi's View

The *gemara* identifies this ruling as that of Rabbi (Rebbi). A second *braita* is cited in which Rebbi reconciles the two verses: *et hakol* — when the parts are still joined — *ya'alu*; once they are *pirshu* — *afilu hein berosh hamizbeach yerdu* — even if they are already atop the *mizbeiach*, they are brought down.

This allows for up to three positions. The *mishnah*'s view: *lo ya'alu* if separated below, but if placed up, perhaps no obligation to remove. The *tanna kamma* of the *braita*: even if separated below, there is an obligation to bring them up (the separation does not eliminate the duty to offer), and the only distinction is that if they fly off the fire there is no obligation to restore them. Rebbi: *pirshu yerdu* — both that they should not be placed up and that if they were placed up they must be removed.

Rashi and Tosafot each consolidate these three into two positions but in opposite ways. Rashi holds that Rebbi is the *tanna* of our *mishnah* as well — the *mishnah*'s *lo ya'alu* implies *yerdu* if they were placed up, and the more radical position is the *tanna kamma* who says even separated below they are to be offered. Tosafot holds that Rebbi represents the most stringent position; the *braita*'s statement that *pirshu ya'alu* refers not to separation at the floor of the *Mikdash* but to separation that occurs atop the *mizbeiach* itself — in which case the fire may continue to consume them — and the *mishnah*'s *lo ya'alu* reflects the intermediate view that they should not be brought up but if they were, they need not be removed.

### Zeira's Direction

Rabbi Zeira adds a further refinement, addressing Rebbi's ruling that *pirshu yerdu*. He limits this to cases where the hoof separates *klapei mata* — in the direction away from the fire, moving downward. But if it separates *klapei ma'ala* — moving toward the fire — then *kruvei hu d'akrivei l'ochla*: it is still in the process of being offered and consumed, and is not considered truly separated. In that case it would not be subject to the requirement of removal even according to Rebbi.

### Zerikat Hadam

Rava then refines the entire framework. Setting aside the question of directional movement, Rava establishes: *lo shanu ela shepirshu l'achar zerika* — Rebbi's ruling that separated parts must be removed applies only when the separation occurs after *zerikat hadam*. Whether the separation occurred below at the floor of the *Mikdash* or at the top of the *mizbeiach*, if it happened after the blood-sprinkling, removal is required.

But *pirshu kodem zerika* — if the hoof was already separated before *zerikat hadam* — then the *zerikah* applied only to the attached portions of the *korban*. The hoof was never part of the *korban* to which the *zerikah* pertained, and therefore it is not sanctified in any binding sense. In such a case — *afilu l'mevad mineihu katatei d'sakina* — the *kohen* may even use the bone to fashion a knife handle. It has no *kedushas* that would preclude personal use.

This is a striking distinction: the status of the bones and hooves turns on whether they were physically integrated with the sacrificial animal at the moment of *zerikat hadam*. If they were, they become bound up in the *kedushah* of the *korban* and their separation creates a problem requiring proper disposal. If they were not, they never entered the sphere of *kodshim* at all.

## Bones Status

### Bones Permissible

The *gemara* now addresses the status of bones with respect to personal use, deriving from a *gezeirah shavah*. The verse by the *olah* states *lo yihiyeh* (regarding the skin going to the *kohen*), and the verse by the *asham* uses the same language regarding the meat of the *asham* going to the *kohen*. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yishmael derives: just as by the *asham* the bones are permitted to the *kohen* for personal use (*mah asham atzmosav mutarin*), so too by an *olah* the bones are permitted (*af olah atzmos mutarin*). Despite the *olah* being entirely consumed, the *lo yihiyeh* applies analogously to items like the skin, bones, horns, and the like — permitting their use for any personal purpose, including fashioning a knife handle.

The *gemara* notes that these *lo yihiyeh* formulations are *mufni* — superfluous in their respective contexts and therefore available to form the basis of a *gezeirah shavah* that is impervious to logical challenge. If they were not *mufni*, one could *mifrach* — one could object: the *asham*'s bones are permitted because the *asham*'s meat is permitted; you cannot extrapolate from there to the *olah*, whose meat is not permitted. But since the words are superfluous to begin with, the *gezeirah shavah* stands even in the face of that logical distinction.

The framework underlying all of this, consistent with Rava's ruling above, is that these bones are permissible specifically when they were separated before *zerikat hadam* — when the *zerikah* never applied to them.

### Tosefta Challenge

Rav Adda bar Ahava raises a challenge from a *Tosefta*. The *Tosefta* teaches: *atzmos kodshim lifnei zerika mo'alin bahen* — bones of *kodshim* (other than an *olah*) are subject to *me'ilah* before *zerikat hadam*, since the *zerikah* is what releases the meat for permitted use; before the *zerikah*, everything remains in the domain of *me'ilah*. After *zerikah*, *ein mo'alin bahen* — the *zerikah* releases the bones as well, consistent with the *asham* ruling. But then the *Tosefta* states regarding an *olah*: *mo'alin bahen le'olam* — there is always *me'ilah* in the bones of an *olah*, both before and after *zerikah*, since the *olah* is entirely burned and nothing is ever released. This appears to directly contradict the ruling that bones separated before *zerikah* are entirely free of *kedushas* and may be used for any purpose.

### Before/After Zrika

The *gemara* resolves the challenge by reading a distinction into the *Tosefta*. The *Tosefta*'s statement *mol'alin bahen le'olam* is reread: *pirshu lifnei zerika — ein mo'alin bahen*, if the bones were separated before *zerikah*, there is no *me'ilah*, consistent with Rava. *Le'achar zerikah — mo'alin bahem*: if they separated after *zerikah*, they are subject to *me'ilah*, for they were part of the *korban* when the *zerikah* applied, and their subsequent separation leaves them as *kodshim psulim* — sanctified items that no longer have a valid mode of offering. They must be disposed of properly, and misappropriating them would constitute *me'ilah*.

### Rabbi Eleazar

Rabbi Eleazar takes the opposite view. According to Rabbi Eleazar, *pirshu lifnei zerikah — mo'alin bahem*: if the bones separated before *zerikah*, there is *me'ilah*. His reasoning, as explained by Rashi, is that in that case the bones were part of the *korban* but were never fit for offering — the *zerikah* never applied to them, so they had no *sh'at heter*, no moment of release. Something that is *kodesh* and never had a *sh'at heter* remains permanently in the domain of *me'ilah*. Conversely, *le'achar zerikah — lo nehenin v'lo mo'alin*: if they separated after the *zerikah*, the blood-sprinkling released them from *me'ilah* even though they never ascended the *mizbeiach*; on the other hand, one is still rabbinically prohibited from deriving benefit from them. This is the precise inverse of Rava's position.

## Falling Limbs

### Falling Items

The *mishnah* now states a general rule: *v'kulan she'paku me'al gabi hamizbe'ach lo yachzir* — all of the aforementioned items, meaning those in the category of *alu lo yerdu*, if they flew off the *mizbeiach*, are not returned. This is the correlative of *yerdu lo ya'alu*: the leniency of allowing them to remain does not translate into an obligation or even a right to restore them once they have left the *mizbeiach*. (The prior discussion of *mashla bahen ha'ur* qualifies this, as established by Ulla.) Similarly, a piece of charcoal (*gachelet*) that flies off the *mizbeiach* is not returned. Rashi reads *lo yachzir* regarding the *gachelet* as indicating no obligation to restore rather than a prohibition — the charcoal is not inherently problematic, and the *kohen* simply has no duty to retrieve it.

### Before Midnight

The *mishnah* then addresses the case of limbs of a genuine, valid *olah* burning on the *mizbeiach* that flew off. Here the *mishnah* draws a clear temporal distinction. *Kodem chatzot — yachzir*: before midnight, the limbs must be returned to the *mizbeiach*. In this case *yachzir* clearly means an obligation, since there remains a *mitzvah* of burning. Moreover, *u'mo'alin bahen* — there is still *me'ilah* in these limbs, since the *mitzvah* of *haktarah* has not been completed.

### After Midnight

*Le'achar chatzot — lo yachzir*: after midnight, there is no obligation to restore the limbs. Furthermore, *v'ein mo'alin bahen* — there is no *me'ilah*. The implications of *chatzot* are therefore twofold: it marks the point after which the limbs are considered halachically as though they have been consumed, eliminating both the *mitzvah* of restoration and the status of *me'ilah*. Why midnight should have this effect is not immediately obvious and will be explored in the *gemara*.

## Shariri & Night

### Meat Remains

The *gemara* opens with a direct question: *heichi dami* — what is the actual case in which *chatzot* makes a difference? *Im de'is behu mamash* — if there is still actual meat remaining that has not been burned, then *afilu achar chatzot nami*, even after midnight there remains a *mitzvah* to burn it and *me'ilah* should still apply. *Im de'las behu mamash* — if the meat has been entirely consumed and reduced to ash, then *afilu kodem chatzot nami lo*, even before midnight the *mitzvah* is done and there should be no *me'ilah*. How does midnight function as a determinative point in either scenario?

### Shariri Definition

The *gemara* answers at the top of 86b: *lo tzricha — b'shariri*. The ruling is needed for the case of *shariri* — meat that has been hardened by fire to the point where all moisture has been burned away, yet has not yet been fully reduced to charcoal and ash. Such material occupies an intermediate state: it is neither raw meat nor ash. For this ambiguous category, the *gemara* rules that *chatzot* is determinative.

### Two Phases

The *gemara* then asks: *minani mili* — from where is this derived? Rav answers by citing the relevant verses in the *parasha* of *tzav*. One verse states *kol halayla*, establishing that the *olah* burns on the *mizbeiach* the entire night. Another verse juxtaposes *kol halayla* with the act of *terumas hadeshen* — the removal of the ash — implying that the ash removal is also a nighttime activity. The apparent contradiction is resolved: *chalkeyu* — the night is divided. *Chetzyo l'haktara v'chetzyo l'harama* — the first half of the night is characterized by *haktarah* (burning), and the second half by *haramah* (ash removal). For *shariri*, the ambiguous case, this division is operative: during the first half, the emphasis is on burning, and *shariri* is treated as still requiring *haktarah*; during the second half, the emphasis is on ash and completion, and *shariri* is treated as already consumed. This ruling is clearly *d'oraisa*, since the question of *me'ilah* is at stake.

### Kahana Challenge

Rav Kahana challenges this framework from a *mishnah* in Yoma. The practice of *terumas hadeshen* varied according to circumstance: on a regular day it was performed from the cock-crowing (*mikrias hagever*); on Yom Kippur, from midnight (*mechatzot*), to accommodate the *kohen gadol*'s grueling schedule; and on the *regalim*, from the first watch of the night (*me'ashmores harishona*), because the volume of *korbanos* was so large that the *Mikdash* filled with worshippers before the cock crowed and the ashes needed to be cleared earlier. If the second half of the night is the designated time for *terumas hadeshen*, how could it be performed in the first third of the night on the *regalim*?

### Yochanan's Solution

Rabbi Yochanan addresses this by attending closely to the verse. The verse says *kol halayla* — the whole night — and also *ad haboker* — until the morning. Since *kol halayla* already implies *ad haboker*, the additional phrase is superfluous. Rabbi Yochanan applies the principle: *ten boker l'bokro* — give a morning to the morning of the first watch. The phrase *ad haboker* indicates that there is a "morning" earlier in the night, a point before dawn at which the transition to ash-removal emphasis may already take effect. This allows the practice of *terumas hadeshen* to be moved earlier when circumstances require it.

### Rashi's Reading

Rashi reads Rabbi Yochanan's ruling as a refinement rather than a replacement of the *chatzot* framework. The two phases of the night are not exclusive in their respective activities. The first half emphasizes *haktarah*, but *haktarah* continues throughout the night whenever there is meat that genuinely requires burning. The second half emphasizes *terumas hadeshen*, but the *ten boker l'bokro* principle allows *terumas hadeshen* to be moved earlier when the volume of offerings demands it. The practical rule, therefore, is: on a regular day, *terumas hadeshen* is performed from cock-crowing; on Yom Kippur, from midnight; on the *regalim*, from the first watch.

The *chatzot* division — first half for *haktarah*, second half for *haramah* — governs the intermediate case of *shariri* alone. For *shariri* in the first half of the night, the meat is treated as not yet consumed, *me'ilah* applies, and there is an obligation to restore it to the *mizbeiach* if it falls. For *shariri* in the second half of the night, it is treated as already consumed, there is no *me'ilah*, and there is no obligation to restore. But for fully unconsumed meat, the *mitzvah* of *haktarah* remains in force throughout the night regardless of *chatzot*, and for meat already fully reduced to ash, the *mitzvah* is complete regardless of when that occurred.
