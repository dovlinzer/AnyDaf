# Hullin 130 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gifts to Kohanim

### Priestly gifts intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַזְּרוֹעַ וְהַלְּחָיַיִם וְהַקֵּבָה נוֹהֲגִין בָּאָרֶץ וּבְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת, בַּחוּלִּין אֲבָל לֹא בַּמּוּקְדָּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mitzva to give **the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw** of slaughtered animals to the priests, known as the gifts of the priesthood, **applies** both **in Eretz** Yisrael **and outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **in the presence of the Temple and not in the presence of the Temple,** and it applies **to non-sacred** animals, **but not to sacrificial** animals.

Today's *daf* is 130, the beginning of a new *perek*. The *perek* we just finished was, without question, the most challenging in *Chullin* — anatomical difficulties aside. We now turn to the last three chapters, which continue to address matters relating to animals but shift focus to the gifts derived from them: beginning with the shearing of sheep and the portions taken at *shechita* of cattle and sheep, and concluding with the commandment of sending away the mother bird. It is a welcome change of pace.

This *perek* opens with *hazroa vehalechayayim veakeiva* — the arm, the jaw, and the stomach. The *Torah* commands that when a person slaughters a cow or a sheep, he gives the arm, the jaw, and one of the stomachs to the *kohanim*. The relevant *pesukim* are in *Devarim* 18. The *Torah* first notes that the *kohanim* have no tribal portion and that they eat the fire-offerings of God: *lo yihyeh lakohanim halevi'im kol shevet levi chelek venachala im yisrael ishei Hashem venachalato yocheilun* — they have no inheritance; they live from the *korbanot*. But the *kohanim* were only present in the *Beit Hamikdash* roughly two weeks out of the year. What sustained them the rest of the time?

### Three gifts explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיָה בַּדִּין, וּמָה אִם הַחוּלִּין, שֶׁאֵינָן חַיָּיבִים בְּחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק – חַיָּיבִים בְּמַתָּנוֹת, קָדָשִׁים, שֶׁחַיָּיבִים בְּחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁחַיָּיבִים בְּמַתָּנוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** It is necessary to emphasize that it does not apply to sacrificial animals, **as by right it should be** inferred *a fortiori*: **If non-sacred** animals, **which are not obligated to** have the **breast and thigh** taken from them and given to the priest, **are obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood given from them, then with regard to **sacrificial** animals, **which are obligated to** have the **breast and thigh** given from them, **is it not right that** they should be **obligated to** have **gifts** of the priesthood given from them?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וָאֶתֵּן אֹתָם לְאַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן וּלְבָנָיו לְחׇק עוֹלָם״ – אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁאָמוּר בָּעִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states:** "For the breast of waving and the thigh of giving I have taken of the children of Israel from the sacrifice of the peace offerings, **and have given them to Aaron the priest and to his sons as a due forever** from the children of Israel" (Leviticus 7:34), from which it is derived that the priest **has only that which is stated with regard to** that **matter,** i.e., the breast and the thigh, and not the foreleg, the jaw and the maw.

The very next *pasuk* answers directly: *vezeh yihyeh mishpat hakohanim me'eit ha'am me'eit zovechei hazevach im shor im seh venatan lakohen hazroa vehalechayayim vehakeiva* — this shall be the law of the *kohanim* from the people, from those who slaughter an ox or a sheep: one gives the *kohen* the arm, the cheeks, and the maw. The *pasuk* continues: *reishit degancha tiroshcha veyitzharecha vereishit geiz tzoncha titein lo* — the first of the grain, the wine, the oil, and the first of the shearing of the flock shall be given to him.

There is a famous *Midrash* connecting these specific portions to *Pinchas*: he used his *zeroa* — his arm — to thrust the spear, and the spear passed through the stomach, *el hakuba* — *hakeiva*. Since *Pinchas* was a *kohen*, the *Midrash* sees these gifts as symbolically linked to his zealotry on God's behalf.

### Kohanim sustenance

The placement of these *pesukim* is deliberate. The *Torah* juxtaposes the gifts of the arm, jaw, and stomach, the *terumah* of grain, wine, and oil, and the first of the shearing, precisely because it is explaining how the *kohanim* sustain themselves. Grain is covered by *terumah*; clothing by *reishit hagez*; and meat by *hazroa vehalechayayim veakeiva*. The *kohanim* serve in a role of *avodat hakodesh* and are understood to function not only within the *Beit Hamikdash* but as teachers and religious leaders for the broader community — *yoru mishpatecha l'Yaakov*. The *Torah* accordingly provides for the full range of their material needs.

It bears noting that these gifts are not *kadosh* in the manner of *terumah*; they can be used and sold. The obligation to give them, however, applies in all times and in all places, as the *Mishnah* makes clear.

## Non-sanctified vs sacrificial animals

### Application scope

The *Mishnah* states: *noheg ba'aretz uvachutz la'aretz* — this obligation applies both within the Land of Israel and outside it — *bifnei habayit veshelo bifnei habayit* — whether the Temple stands or not. The gifts of *hazroa vehalechayayim veakeiva* are thus a perpetual obligation, independent of the *Beit Hamikdash*.

### Exclusion from korbanot

The *Mishnah* continues: *bechullin aval lo bemukdashin* — in non-sanctified animals but not in *korbanot*. When one brings an animal as a sacrifice, he does not separately give these portions as gifts to the *kohanim*.

### Kal vachomer setup

The *Mishnah* itself raises the question of why the gifts do not apply to *korbanot*. The argument that might have led us to think they should runs as follows: by *chullin*, ordinary non-sanctified animals, one does not give the *chazeh* and the *shok* — the breast and the thigh — as one does by a *shelamim*, and yet one does give *hazroa vehalechayayim veakeiva*. By *kodshim*, where one already gives the breast and thigh, one might reason *kal vachomer* — how much more so should these additional gifts apply. If a lesser obligation includes the gifts, surely a greater obligation should include them as well.

### Verse excludes korbanot

Against this *kal vachomer*, the *Torah* provides an explicit exclusion. The verse concerning the *shelamim* states: *ve'asain osam l'Aharon hakohen ulvanav l'chok olam* — I give these to Aaron and his sons as an eternal statute. The word *osam* — "them" — limits the priestly entitlement from *korbanot* to the *chazeh* and *shok* alone. The gifts of *hazroa vehalechayayim veakeiva* are not given from a *korban*; *ein lo ela mah shene'emar binyan* — one receives from the *korban* only what the verse explicitly designates.

## Blemish before sanctity

### Permanent blemish ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים שֶׁקָּדַם מוּם קָבוּעַ לְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן, וְנִפְדּוּ – חַיָּיבִין בַּבְּכוֹרָה וּבַמַּתָּנוֹת, וְיוֹצְאִין לְחוּלִּין לְהִגָּזֵז וּלְהֵעָבֵד, וּוְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן מוּתָּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **All sacrificial** animals in **which a permanent blemish preceded their consecration** do not assume inherent sanctity, and only their value is consecrated. **And** once **they were redeemed,** they **are obligated in** the mitzva of **a firstborn,** i.e., their offspring are subject to being counted a firstborn, **and in the gifts** of the priesthood, **and they can emerge** from their sacred status and assume **non-sacred** status **with regard to being shorn and with regard to being utilized for labor,** as it is prohibited to shear animals with sacred status or utilize them for labor. **And their offspring and their milk are permitted after their redemption.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשּׁוֹחֲטָן בַּחוּץ פָּטוּר, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וְאִם מֵתוּ – יִפָּדוּ, חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וּמִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **And one who slaughters** these animals **outside** the Temple courtyard is **exempt** from *karet*, **and** those animals **do not render** an animal that was **a substitute** for them consecrated. **And if** these animals **died** before they were redeemed, **they may be redeemed** and fed to dogs. Although typically sacrificial animals that were redeemed may not be fed to the dogs, in this case it is permitted. This is the *halakha* with regard to all animals **except for the firstborn** animal **and the** animal **tithe,** whose sanctity is inherent, even when a permanent blemish preceded their consecration.

The *Mishnah* now turns to a question that, while only tangentially related to *hazroa vehalechayayim veakeiva*, is highly relevant to the larger category of when an animal is considered to have had the status of a *korban*. This passage, in fact, appears verbatim in *Bechoros* as well, because it defines the precise conditions under which an animal enters the *korban* category — which in turn determines whether it is excluded from *bechorah* and from the priestly gifts alike.

The principle: *kol hakodashim shekadam mum kavua lehekdesham* — if a permanent blemish preceded the sanctification of the animal. A *mum kavua* is a blemish that will not heal. If such a blemish already existed at the moment of sanctification, the animal could never have been designated as a *korban* in the first place, since it was already unfit. Consequently, whatever sanctity the animal received cannot be *kedushat haguf* — the sanctity of an object that is itself to be used as a sacrifice. Instead, it receives only *kedushat bedek habayit* — the sanctity of an object donated to the Temple treasury for its monetary value. The *Beit Hamikdash* owns its worth, not its identity.

The distinction is significant. *Kedushat bedek habayit* is, in essence, *kedushat damim* — the treasury cares only about the object's monetary value, not about what it is intrinsically. Just as one might donate a car to the *Beit Hamikdash* and the treasury simply assesses and sells it, so too this blemished animal: its identity as a cow or a sheep was never sanctified. It was merely property owned by the Temple for the sake of its price.

### Treasury property effects

Since this animal never possessed the sanctity of a *korban*, the *Mishnah* rules: *venifdu chayavim bivichorah uvematanot* — once redeemed, it is subject to the laws of *bechorah* and the priestly gifts. *Veyotzim lechulin lehiggazez ulehe'aved* — after redemption, it fully returns to the status of *chullin*; it may be shorn and worked.

This last point requires explanation. The *Torah* prohibits working or shearing an animal that was once a *korban* and was subsequently redeemed after acquiring a blemish. The principle is that even after redemption, the animal retains a residual *kedushah* of a former *korban*, and therefore may only be slaughtered and eaten — it cannot be put to secular use. But that residual sanctity applies only when the animal once genuinely had *kedushat korban*. Here, where the animal's sanctity was always only *kedushat bedek habayit*, redemption severs the relationship completely. There is no residual sanctity, because there was never *kedushat haguf* to begin with. The transaction is simply: the Temple owned it; now it does not. Accordingly, *vladan vechalban mutar* — its offspring and milk are permitted after redemption — and *lachtar pidyonam* — once redeemed.

Furthermore: *vehashochtan bachutz patur* — if one slaughters such an animal outside the *Beit Hamikdash*, one does not transgress the prohibition of offering a *korban* outside the Temple. That prohibition applies specifically to animals that possess *kedushat korban*. This animal may have been owned by the *Beit Hamikdash*, but it was never a *korban*, so no transgression is incurred — just as slaughtering an animal that was donated to the Temple treasury but was never a sacrifice does not constitute *shechita* of a *korban* outside the *Beit Hamikdash*.

*Ve'ein osin temurah* — the laws of *temura* do not apply. The *Torah* states that if one attempts to substitute a sanctified *korban* with another animal, both become *kadosh*: *vehaya hu utmurato yihye kodesh*. This principle of *temura* applies only to animals that possess genuine *kedushat korban*. An animal that was always only in the category of *bedek habayit* cannot generate *temura*.

*Ve'im mesu yifdu* — if such an animal dies, it may still be redeemed. By contrast, an animal that possessed genuine *kedushat korban* may not be redeemed after death. Two reasons underlie this: first, the principle of *ein podin et hakodashim l'hachilam l'klavim* — one does not redeem *korbanot* merely to use them as dog food. If the animal is dead, redemption would serve no licit purpose. Second, the *Torah*'s language for redeeming a blemished *korban* — *vehe'emido lifnei Hashem vehe'ericho lifnei hakohen* — requires that the animal stand and be assessed. Both *ha'amadah v'ha'arachah* — standing and valuation — are prerequisites that cannot be fulfilled by a dead animal. Neither consideration applies to an animal whose sanctity was only *bedek habayit*, and thus it may be redeemed even after death, if only to recover whatever monetary value remains.

### Bechor & maaser exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וּמִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** except for the firstborn and the tithe animal.

The *Mishnah* records one exception to the rule that a pre-existing *mum kavua* prevents an animal from acquiring *kedushat korban*: *chutz min habechor umin hamaser* — except for the firstborn and the tithe animal.

Consider an animal born with a congenital blemish. Generally, if the blemish precedes the sanctification, the animal never achieves *kedushat korban*. Yet if this animal is a *bechor* — a firstborn male — the rule is different. The sanctity of *bechor* is not something actively conferred by a person through speech; it arises automatically and intrinsically from the act of birth. Because *kedushah* is imposed upon the animal by the *Torah* itself by virtue of being born firstborn, that identity as a *korban bechor* attaches regardless of any pre-existing blemish. The animal cannot be brought to the *mizbe'ach* because of its *mum*, but it nonetheless possesses the full *kedushah* of a *korban bechor*.

Similarly with *ma'aser behemah*: one gathers all newly born animals of the year, drives them into a pen, and counts them — *ha'asiri yihyeh kodesh*, the tenth is holy. If the tenth animal to emerge happens to have a blemish, it does not receive merely *kedushat bedek habayit*. Its sanctity as *ma'aser* is conferred automatically by the counting process, not by an act of verbal dedication on the owner's part. The *pesukim* teach that *ha'asiri yihyeh kodesh* regardless of a blemish. Accordingly, both *bechor* and *ma'aser behemah* acquire full *kedushat korban* even when the blemish preceded what would otherwise be considered their sanctification.

## Sanctity before blemish

### Sanctified then blemished

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁקָּדַם הֶקְדֵּשָׁן אֶת מוּמָן, אוֹ מוּם עוֹבֵר קוֹדֵם לְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן, וּלְאַחַר מִכָּאן נוֹלַד לָהֶם מוּם קָבוּעַ, וְנִפְדּוּ – פְּטוּרִין מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה וּמִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, וְאֵינָן יוֹצְאִין לְחוּלִּין לְהִגָּזֵז וּלְהֵעָבֵד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **all** sacrificial animals **whose consecration preceded their blemish, or** who had **a temporary blemish prior to their consecration and afterward developed a permanent blemish and they were redeemed, they are exempt from** the mitzva of **a firstborn, and from the gifts** of the priestood, **and they do not emerge** from their sacred status and assume **non-sacred** status **with regard to being shorn and with regard to being utilized for labor.**

The *Mishnah* now presents the contrasting case: *kol shekadam hekdeshan es mumam* — where the sanctification preceded the blemish — or *mum over kodem hekdeshan* — where a *mum over*, a transient blemish, preceded sanctification. A transient blemish does not define the animal's identity, since it will heal; it does not bar the animal from *kedushat korban*, and it does not prevent the sanctity from taking hold. If the animal was then sanctified and only later developed a permanent blemish — *ve'achar kach nolad lahem mum kavua* — the permanent blemish occurred after the animal already possessed the full sanctity of a *korban*. As a result, all of the rules of *kedushat korban* apply.

### Post-redemption restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּוְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן אָסוּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן, וְהַשּׁוֹחֲטָן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב, וְעוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וְאִם מֵתוּ – יִקָּבֵרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And their offspring,** which were conceived prior to redemption, **and their milk, are prohibited after their redemption. And one who slaughters them outside** the Temple courtyard is **liable** to receive *karet*, **and** those animals **render** an animal that was **a substitute** for them consecrated. **And if** these animals **died** before they were redeemed, they may not be redeemed and fed to dogs; rather, **they must be buried.**

For such an animal, once it acquires its permanent blemish and is redeemed, the following consequences obtain: *peturim min habechorahumin hamatnot* — it is exempt from the laws of *bechorah*, and one does not give *zeroa lechayayim vekeivah* from it after *shechita*. *Ve'einam yotzim lechulin lehiggazez ulehe'aved* — it does not fully return to *chullin*; it may not be shorn and may not be worked. *Uveladan vechalban assur le'achar pidyonam* — even after redemption, its offspring and milk are forbidden for benefit.

Furthermore: *vehashochtan bachutz chayav* — one who slaughters such an animal outside the *Beit Hamikdash* incurs liability, because the animal possesses *kedushat korban*. One might ask: if it has a permanent blemish, it cannot be brought as a *korban* in any event — how can one be liable for slaughtering it outside? The *Gemara* addresses this by confining the ruling to a specific type of blemish, such as a cataract of the eye, which is technically a *mum* sufficient to permit redemption and preclude normal offering, but which, if the animal were actually placed upon the *mizbe'ach*, would not be removed *bedi'eved*. Such a blemish allows the animal to be redeemed and bars it from ideal offering, but since *bedi'eved* it would be accepted, one is still liable for slaughtering it outside. *Ve'osim temurah* — the laws of *temura* apply, since the animal possesses genuine *kedushat korban*. *Ve'im mesu yikkaveru* — if such an animal dies before redemption, it must be buried; it cannot be redeemed, because of the requirements of *ha'amadah v'ha'arachah* and the prohibition of redeeming *korbanot* for use as dog food.

### Why restrictions apply

The underlying principle is that when an animal's identity was defined by *kedushat korban* — when the *kedushah* came first — that identity does not fully dissolve upon redemption. Even after the formal *kedushah* has been removed through *pidyon*, the animal retains a residual dimension of its former sanctity. The practical expression of this residual *kedushah* is that one may do nothing with the animal except slaughter it and eat it: one may not shear it, may not work it, may not benefit from its milk or its offspring. All the exclusions flow from this.

Contrast this with the first case: if the blemish preceded sanctification, the animal's identity was never defined by *kedushat korban* at all. The *Beit Hamikdash* owned it as property; redemption simply transfers that ownership back. No residual sanctity remains, because there was no *kedushat haguf* to leave a trace.

## 'Ze' and ownership

### Rav Chisda ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״זֶה״ לְמָה לִי? לְכִדְרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַמַּזִּיק מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה אוֹ שֶׁאֲכָלָן – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if that is so, **why do I** need the verse to state: **"This** shall be the priests' due" (Deuteronomy 18:3)? The Gemara answers: The verse is necessary **for that which Rav Ḥisda** taught, **as Rav Ḥisda said: One who causes damage** to **gifts of the priesthood, or who consumed them** before they were given to the priests, is **exempt from** having **to pay** to the priest, as the verse states: "This shall be the priests' due," which indicates that only the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw themselves, not their replacements, are given to the priests.

With the *Mishnah*'s framework now established, the *Gemara* turns to a remarkable ruling of Rav Chisda. The *Gemara* has already noted that when the *Torah* introduces the gifts of *hazroa vehalechayayim veakeiva*, it uses the word *ze* — *vezeh yihyeh mishpat hakohanim* — "this is the law of the *kohanim*." After establishing that this word *ze* excluded the *chazeh veshok* from non-*korban* animals (since the *chazeh veshok* is a *korban* ritual that cannot be replicated outside the *Beit Hamikdash* and cannot be performed inside with *chullin*), the *Gemara* asks: what further teaching does *ze* come to convey?

The answer is: *lichdirav Chisda* — it comes to support Rav Chisda's ruling. *Amar Rav Chisda: hamazik matnot kehuna o she'achalan patur milishalem* — one who damages gifts owed to a *kohen*, or who eats them, is exempt from payment. This applies to *hazroa vehalechayayim veakeiva*, to *reishit hagez*, and to analogous priestly gifts.

This is a striking ruling. One might assume that since the gifts are owed to the *kohanim* by *Torah* law, destroying them would obligate compensation. Rav Chisda says otherwise. The *Gemara* presents two possible rationales, each reflecting a fundamentally different understanding of the ownership of these gifts before they are handed over.

### Two explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַמַּזִּיק מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה, אוֹ שֶׁאֲכָלָן – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא דִּכְתִיב ״זֶה״, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ מָמוֹן שֶׁאֵין לוֹ תּוֹבְעִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes **the** matter **itself: Rav Ḥisda said** that **one who causes damage** to **gifts of the priesthood or who consumed them** is **exempt from** having **to pay** a substitute to the priest. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason? If you wish, say that** it is because **"this" is written** in the verse, indicating that only the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw themselves are given to the priests, as explained above. **And if you wish, say** instead that it is **because it is money that has no claimants.** Since one may give the gifts to any priest, no single priest can issue a claim on them.

*I ba'it eima mishum dehavei lei mamon she'ein lo tovin* — one may explain that the gifts, once separated, belong collectively to the *kohanim* as a class, but since there is no specific individual *kohen* who has a claim, no one can bring the damager to court. This is the principle of *mamon she'ein lo tovin* — property with no particular claimant. The damager has in fact injured the *kohanim*'s collective property, but since the injury cannot be attributed to any identifiable individual, there is no mechanism for legal recovery.

*Ve'i ba'it eima midikativ ze* — alternatively, the word *ze* teaches that the obligation to give the gifts exists only when they are physically present. If the gift has been destroyed, the obligation simply does not apply — not because the *kohanim* owned it and cannot collect, but because the *kohanim* never owned it to begin with. On this reading, the gift remains in the owner's possession until actually transferred. The owner has a *mitzvah* to give it; if he destroys it, he has subverted that *mitzvah*, but he has not damaged anyone else's property. He damaged his own.

These are two very different conceptual frameworks. The *mamon she'ein lo tovin* explanation acknowledges that the property already belongs to the *kohanim* as a collective; the obstacle is procedural. The *ze* explanation holds that the property never transferred — it belongs to the giver until the moment of actual delivery.

### 'Ze' means present

The word *ze* thus functions as a limiting principle: the obligation attaches only to that which is physically present, *ze* — this thing before you. If it is not here, there is no obligation to give, and consequently no liability for its destruction. The verb form of giving applies only to something that can actually be given.

## Verse & courts

### Court enforcement query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״וְזֶה יִהְיֶה מִשְׁפַּט הַכֹּהֲנִים״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַמַּתָּנוֹת דִּין. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? לָאו לְהוֹצִיאָן בְּדַיָּינִין? לָא, לְחוֹלְקָן בְּדַיָּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*. The verse states: **"And this shall be the priests' due [*mishpat*]"** (Deuteronomy 18:3), which **teaches that the gifts** given to the priests are considered **a judgment,** as *mishpat* can mean judgment. The Gemara continues: **With regard to what *halakha*** are the gifts a judgment? Is it **not with regard to extracting** them **through judges,** i.e., a priest may claim them in court and the court will order the individual to give the gifts to that priest? If so, the gifts are considered to be money that has claimants. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** they are a judgment **with regard to distributing them through judges,** i.e., the court dictates to the owner to which type of priests he should give the gifts.

The *Gemara* now examines a *baraita* that appears to challenge Rav Chisda's ruling. The *baraita* states: *vezeh yihyeh mishpat hakohanim — melamed shehamatnot din* — "this is the law of the *kohanim*" teaches that the gifts have the status of *din*, meaning they are subject to the law of the courts. At first reading, this sounds as though the courts can compel payment or adjudicate claims relating to these gifts — which would seem to contradict Rav Chisda's ruling that one who damages them is exempt.

The *Gemara* asks: *lema'i hilchata? lav lehotzi'o bedayanim?* — For what practical purpose does the *baraita* say the gifts are a *din*? Is it not to say that one can collect compensation through the courts? Rashi notes that this might mean even the first *kohen* to file suit could collect. If so, the principle of *mamon she'ein lo tovin* would not apply here — even as a collective, the *kohanim* could bring a claim.

### Worthy kohanim rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי, דְּאָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין נוֹתְנִין מַתָּנָה לְכֹהֵן עַם הָאָרֶץ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לָעָם לְיוֹשְׁבֵי יְרוּשָׁלִַם לָתֵת מְנָת לַכֹּהֲנִים וְלַלְוִיִּם לְמַעַן יֶחֶזְקוּ בְּתוֹרַת ה׳״, כׇּל הַמַּחְזִיק בְּתוֹרַת ה׳ – יֵשׁ לוֹ מְנָת, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מַחֲזִיק בְּתוֹרַת ה׳ – אֵין לוֹ מְנָת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **in accordance with that which Rav Shmuel bar Naḥmani** taught, **as Rav Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: From where** is it derived **that one does not give a gift** of the priesthood **to a priest** who is **an *am ha'aretz*?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And he commanded the people who dwelled in Jerusalem to give the portion of the priests and of the Levites, so that they may firmly adhere to the Torah of the Lord"** (II Chronicles 31:4). This indicates that **anyone who firmly adheres to the Torah of the Lord has a portion, and one who does not firmly adhere to the Torah of the Lord does not have a portion.**

The *Gemara* rejects this reading. *Lo, lechulkan bedayanim* — the *baraita* does not mean courts can collect compensation for damaged gifts. Rather, it means that courts oversee the proper distribution of gifts. The *Gemara* invokes the teaching of Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani: one does not give the gifts of the *kohanim* — whether *terumah*, *zeroa lechayayim vekeivah*, or any other priestly gift — to a *kohen* who is an *am ha'aretz*, one who is not careful in his religious observance.

The source is a verse in *Divrei Hayamim*, cited in the context of the reign of Yehoshaphat. Yehoshaphat said to the people of Jerusalem: *latet menat hakohanim vehaleviyim lema'an yechezku betorat Hashem* — give the gifts to the *kohanim* and *leviyim* so that they will be strengthened in the Torah of God. The *Gemara* derives: *kol hamachzik beTorat Hashem yesh lo menat* — one who upholds God's Torah is entitled to receive; *veshe'eino machzik beTorat Hashem ein lo menat* — one who does not is not.

This is a *lekatchila* standard. Tosafot notes that *bedi'avad*, or where no other *kohen* is available, one may give to an *am ha'aretz kohen*. But when there is a choice, the obligation to give priestly gifts is bound up with the *kohen*'s role as representative and teacher of Torah. The *Beit Hamikdash* role and the communal religious-leadership role are intertwined; the gifts are meant to support the latter as much as the former. The court's oversight, then, is directed at ensuring that gifts flow to those *kohanim* who genuinely fulfill that role.

### Baraita ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא אוֹמֵר: ״מִשְׁפָּט״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַמַּתָּנוֹת דִּין. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק דִּין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״זֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof with regard to the statement of Rav Ḥisda from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says** that the phrase "And this shall be the priests' **due" teaches that the gifts** of the priesthood are **a judgment.** One **might** have thought that the gifts of the **breast and thigh** are **also a judgment.** Therefore, **the verse states: "This,"** to teach that only the gifts of the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw are called a judgment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְחוֹלְקוֹ בְּדַיָּינִין – אַטּוּ חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק לָאו בְּדַיָּינִין מִיחַלְּקוּ? אֶלָּא לָאו (להוציא) [לְהוֹצִיאוֹ] בְּדַיָּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **With regard to what** matter is this *halakha* stated? **If we say** that it is **with regard to distributing** them **through judges, is that to say** that the **breast and thigh are not distributed through judges?** These are also called a "portion" (II Chronicles 31:4), and therefore the court determines to which priests they should be given, as stated above. **Rather, is it not** stated **with regard to extracting** them **through judges?** If so, one can infer from the *baraita* that gifts of the priesthood can be extracted in court by a priest, which contradicts the statement of Rav Ḥisda.

The *Gemara* then examines a second *baraita* in which Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: *mishpat — melamed shematanot din* — the word *mishpat* teaches that the gifts are subject to court law. *Yachol afilu chazeh veshok* — one might have thought this applies even to the *chazeh* and *shok* of a *korban*. *Talmud lomar ze* — the verse says *ze*, "this," excluding the *chazeh veshok* and restricting the principle to the gifts of *zeroa lechayayim vekeivah*.

The question then is: *l'mai* — for what purpose? *Ileima l'chulkan bedayanim* — if it means the courts oversee distribution to worthy *kohanim*, then why would the *chazeh veshok* be excluded? Presumably the same principle of giving only to deserving *kohanim* should apply to *korban* portions as well. The *Gemara* does not easily resolve this, and one might note that the *chazeh veshok* is in any case divided equally among the *kohanim* serving that week. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* here suggests another reading: the reference to courts is about the right to collect compensation — *lo tzivu bedayanim* — and it is that right that is specific to the gifts of *zeroa lechayayim vekeivah*.

### Possession requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דַּאֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ. אִי דַּאֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? דַּאֲתוֹ לִידֵיהּ בְּטִבְלַיְיהוּ, וְקָסָבַר הַאי תַּנָּא: מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ – כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Here, we are dealing with** a case **where** the gifts already **came into** the priest's **possession,** and the owner stole them from him. The *baraita* teaches that the priest may claim them in court and the court will order that they be returned to him. The Gemara asks: **If** this is a case **where they came into his possession, what** is the purpose **of stating** that the priest may claim them in court? That is obvious, as they already belong to him. The Gemara responds: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where they came into his possession while they** were still **untithed,** i.e., the priest received the entire animal before the gifts were separated, **and this *tanna* holds** that **gifts that have not been separated are considered as though they have been separated.** Accordingly, the gifts belong to the priest, and if the owner takes them from him against his will it is considered theft.

To harmonize this with Rav Chisda's ruling that one who damages these gifts is exempt, the *Gemara* clarifies: *hacha bema'i askinan — de'asu liyadei* — the *baraita* is discussing a case where the gifts actually came into the *kohen*'s possession. *Le'asu liyadei betivlaihu* — specifically, a case where a *kohen* came and physically seized the whole animal — before the gifts had been formally separated — and claimed the *zeroa lechayayim vekeivah*. One might have thought he is not entitled to it since it was not yet separated and he was not given it. The *baraita* teaches, based on the principle that *matnot shelo hurmu kemi shehurmu damyan* — gifts not yet separated are treated as if already separated — that even in this case his possession is valid, and if someone subsequently damages those portions, the court can compel payment.

Why does this not apply to the *chazeh veshok*? Because the *chazeh veshok* is distributed to the *kohanim* serving that week's rotation. A *kohen* who was not on duty that week and burst in to seize the *chazeh veshok* would have no entitlement, and his seizure would not grant him ownership. The distinction, then, is that these priestly gifts — unlike *korban* portions — can be acquired through physical seizure even before formal separation, and it is that mode of acquisition that creates court-enforceable ownership.

Before the *kohen* takes possession, Rav Chisda's rule holds: one who damages the gifts is exempt, either because of *mamon she'ein lo tovin* or because of the *ze* principle. Once the *kohen* has validly taken possession, the ordinary laws of property damage apply.

## Poor gifts analogy

### Traveler taking poor gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁהָיָה עוֹבֵר מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, וְצָרִיךְ לִיטּוֹל לֶקֶט, שִׁכְחָה, וּפֵאָה, וּמַעְשַׂר עָנִי – נוֹטֵל, וְלִכְשֶׁיַּחְזוֹר יְשַׁלֵּם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof with regard to the statement of Rav Ḥisda from a mishna (*Pe'a* 5:4): In the case of **a homeowner who was passing from place to place,** and he ran out of money while traveling **and needs to take gleanings, forgotten** sheaves, ***pe'a*, or the poor man's tithe** in order to sustain himself, he may **take** them, **and when he returns** to his house **he will pay** a poor person for whatever he took; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.** The mishna teaches that a poor person may extract payment for that which the homeowner took, despite the fact that he never had possession of the gifts. This ruling apparently contradicts the statement of Rav Ḥisda.

The *Gemara* introduces a *baraita* from a related domain to test Rav Chisda's ruling. The *baraita* discusses a *ba'al habayit* — a person of means — who is traveling from place to place and finds himself with no funds or food. Being temporarily without resources, he is permitted to take *leket*, *shikcha*, *pe'ah*, and *ma'aser ani* — the agricultural gifts designated for the poor — even though he is not ordinarily eligible.

Rabbi Eliezer says: *notel hu vechesheyachzor yeshalem* — he takes them, and when he returns home, he repays. The implication is that there is some form of obligation to pay back on account of his having taken gifts he was not permanently entitled to.

### Repayment dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מִדַּת חֲסִידוּת שָׁנוּ כָּאן. אָמַר רָבָא: תַּנָּא תָּנֵי ״יְשַׁלֵּם״, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מִדַּת חֲסִידוּת שָׁנוּ כָּאן?! וְעוֹד, מִדְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לֵיקוּם וְלֹיתֵוב?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** The mishna **taught an attribute of piety here,** i.e., strictly speaking a poor person has no right to claim any amount from the homeowner for what he took. **Rava said** in bewilderment: **The *tanna* taught** explicitly that **he will pay, and you say** that he **taught an attribute of piety here? And furthermore, should** one **arise and raise an objection from** the statement **of Rabbi Eliezer?** The *halakha* is not in accordance with his opinion.

This seems to challenge Rav Chisda's ruling: if destroying or consuming gifts meant for the poor creates an obligation to repay, it would suggest that similar gifts — those of the *kohanim* — should also generate an obligation when consumed improperly. The *Gemara* raises this challenge and then considers how Rav Chisda might have responded.

The initial suggestion is that Rav Chisda would say *midat chasidut shanu kan* — what Rabbi Eliezer taught here is merely a matter of pious conduct, not a legal obligation. One is not compelled by the courts to repay; it is simply the right thing to do.

### Poverty timing test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִסֵּיפָא, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עָנִי הָיָה בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה, טַעְמָא דְּעָנִי, הָא עָשִׁיר – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the objection is **from the latter clause** of the mishna: **And the Rabbis say** that the homeowner is not required to pay for what he took during his travels, as **he was** considered **poor at that time.** It may be inferred that the **reason** he is exempt from payment is only **that** he was considered **poor** at the time, **but** if he were considered **wealthy,** he would have to **pay** the poor people who issue a claim against him in court, as he was not entitled to those gifts.

Rava, however, objects to this framing. He argues: the word used is *yeshalem* — he shall pay — not a recommendation for piety. Rabbi Eliezer cannot be reduced to a counsel of *chasidut*. Moreover, Rava argues, the challenge to Rav Chisda should not have been drawn from Rabbi Eliezer's opinion at all — Rabbi Eliezer is a single *tanna* whose view is not authoritative.

Rava reframes the entire discussion. The real challenge comes from the *seifa* of the *Mishnah*, from the ruling of the *Chachamim*: *ani haya be'oto sha'ah* — he was poor at that moment. The *Chachamim* hold that the wealthy traveler, being temporarily without resources, was genuinely in a state of poverty at that time, and therefore was fully entitled to take the gifts. He owes nothing upon return.

The implication of the *Chachamim*'s reasoning is important: *ta'ama de'ani*, the reason he need not repay is that he was legally poor at the time. But if he were not poor — *ha'ashir meshalem* — if he had money available and simply helped himself to *leket shikcha pe'ah* and *ma'aser ani*, he would be obligated to repay. Why? *Lehavi kemazik matnot kehuna* — why should this be different from damaging gifts of the *kohanim*, where Rav Chisda says one need not pay?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? לֶיהֱוֵי כְּמַזִּיק מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה אוֹ שֶׁאֲכָלָן! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מִדַּת חֲסִידוּת שָׁנוּ כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: But **why** must he pay? **Let it be** considered **like** a case where one **causes damage to gifts of the priesthood or consumes them,** with regard to which Rav Ḥisda states that he is not required to pay the priest. The opinion of the Rabbis apparently contradicts the statement of Rav Ḥisda. **Rav Ḥisda says:** Even if the homeowner was considered wealthy at the time he is not required to pay, and the Rabbis who obligated him to pay **taught an attribute of piety here.**

It is to this question that Rav Chisda applies *midat chasidut shanu kan*. The *Chachamim*'s implication that the wealthy person who takes gifts of the poor must repay is not a court-enforceable obligation — after all, which *ani* will file suit? It is *mamon she'ein lo tovin*. But morally, since one took what was not rightfully his, it is more than *midat chasidut* — it is strongly the right thing to do to donate an equivalent amount to the poor.

### Final conclusion

The *Gemara* thus concludes with Rav Chisda's ruling intact. One who damages gifts of the *kohanim* — or priestly-analogous gifts such as those designated for the poor — before they are transferred is exempt from payment in court. Two explanations remain on the table, reflecting different understandings of ownership:

The first is *mamon she'ein lo tovin*: the property already belongs to the collective of *kohanim* or the collective of *aniyim*, but since no individual can assert a claim, there is no legal mechanism for recovery. The damage is real; the remedy is absent.

The second is the *ze* principle: the gift has not yet transferred. It remains the owner's property, and in destroying it he has deprived himself of the ability to fulfill his *mitzvah*, but he has not damaged anyone else's property. The obligation to give exists only when *ze* — this thing — is actually present.

These two explanations are not merely technical alternatives. They reflect fundamentally different understandings of the moment at which ownership of priestly and other obligatory gifts transfers from the giver to the recipient — a question the *Gemara* leaves instructively open.
