# Ketubot 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kim Lei Review

### Kim Lei Summary

Today's daf is 36, and we pick up roughly two-thirds of the way down the page, at the words *ilanit ein lah knas*. We have, for the time being, finished with the extraordinary seven-*daf* *sugya* on *kim lei b'deraba minei*. By way of brief review: the *gemara* ultimately phrased the range of opinions as follows. Where the transgression carries a penalty of *misa beis din*, a biblical death sentence, the *gemara* at minimum implies — though *Tosfos* questions whether this is truly universal — that everyone would agree the death penalty overrides any monetary obligation, even in a case of *shogeg*. Where the penalty is *kares*, that was the position of Rabbi Nechunya ben HaKanah and was subject to debate. Where the penalty is *malkus*, the debate was widest: Rabbi Meir held that both punishments could be administered simultaneously; Ulla held that the offender pays rather than receives *malkus* (or, according to *Tosfos*, this may have applied only to the specific *knas* of *ones umefate*); and the standard position was that *malkus* is administered and no monetary payment follows — within which there was a further dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish, Rabbi Yochanan maintaining that this principle applies only when *malkus* was actually administered, and Resh Lakish holding it applies even in cases of *shogeg*, without *hatara*, even when *malkus* was never carried out.

### Ilanit & Age Status

The *gemara* now turns back to the categories of women who do or do not receive a *knas*, not from the perspective of *kim lei* but from the perspective of their personal status. The *mishna* had established that even women in certain *arayos* relationships — as long as the offense is not in the category of *chayav misa* — can still give rise to a *knas*, though a *beraisa* reflecting the positions of either Rabbi Shimon Hatimani or Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya restricted the *knas* to cases where marriage to the woman is at least theoretically binding, or even more restrictively, where marriage is actually permitted.

The *gemara* now returns to the question of whether the *knas* applies only to a *na'arah* or even to a *ketana*. Rabbi Meir holds it is limited to a *na'arah*; the *chachamim* extend it to a *ketana* as well. One practical application of this dispute involves the *ilanit* — a woman who never achieves physical maturity and therefore transitions directly from the status of *ketana* at age twenty to *bogeret*, never passing through the stage of *na'arah*. A *beraisa* stated that she receives no *knas*, presumably because she is never a *na'arah*.

### Besula Status

Beyond the *na'arah* question and the *arayos* question, a third axis of analysis concerns whether the woman can be presumed to be a *besula*. This issue was touched upon in connection with the *mema'enet* — a girl who is rabbinically but not biblically married and has been living with another man, and who therefore cannot be presumed a *besula*. The *gemara* now proceeds to examine other cases where the *besula* dimension of the definition is at stake.

## Ilanit Ein Lah Knas

### Ilanit Case

The *gemara* cites a *beraisa* stating: *ilanit ein lah knas* — an *ilanit* has no *knas* — and likewise no *knas* in the case of seduction involving her. This is consistent with what was established previously: she is never a *na'arah*.

### Ilanit Contradiction

The *gemara* immediately introduces a contradiction: *u-remihu* — and we raise a difficulty — for another *beraisa* lists the *chereshet*, the *shota*, and the *ilanit* together and declares *yesh lahem knas* — they do have a *knas*. Moreover, that same *beraisa* adds *ve-yesh lahem ta'anat betulim*: the husband has grounds to raise a claim of non-virginity the morning after the wedding night. This *ta'anat betulim* is, of course, no advantage to the wife; it means the husband has a legitimate basis to challenge her presumed *besula* status and seek to reduce her *kesuvah* from two hundred to one hundred or eliminate it entirely. The *chereshet* and the *shota* are new information here and will be addressed in a moment. As for the *ilanit*: one *beraisa* says she has a *knas* and the other says she does not — an apparent contradiction.

### Meir vs Rabbanan

The *gemara* immediately resolves this: *ve-hai mai rumiya?* — what kind of contradiction is this at all? *Ha Rabbi Meir, ha Rabanan.* We already established that the entire debate about the *ilanit* traces directly to the *tanna'im* dispute about whether a *ketana* has a *knas*. The *beraisa* that denies her a *knas* reflects Rabbi Meir's position that only a *na'arah* is entitled, while the *beraisa* that grants her one reflects the *Rabanan*'s view that even a *ketana* qualifies. There is no contradiction at all. The *gemara* therefore asks: *ve-ka'arei lei mai ka'arei lei?* — why did you even think to raise this as a difficulty, given that this is transparently a *machlokes* between *tanna'im*? The *gemara* answers: *mishum de-is lei de-mir me-achrisi aloyei* — he knew full well the *ilanit* question was answerable; he introduced it only to lay the groundwork for bringing a second *beraisa* that poses a genuine difficulty, one he truly wanted to examine.

## Chereshet & Shotah

### Have Knas & Betulim

The more serious challenge involves the *chereshet* and *shota*. The *beraisa* just cited declares that a deaf-mute woman and a mentally ill woman — neither of whom is considered a *bar da'at* — are presumed virgins, have a *knas*, and are subject to *ta'anat betulim*. This is itself a striking *chiddush*: these are women that people might readily take advantage of, and yet the *beraisa* presumes they remain virgins, entitling them to the full protections of a *besula*.

### No Ta'anat Betulim

A contradictory *beraisa*, however, lists *ha-chereshet ve-ha-shota ve-ha-bogeret u-mukat eitz ein lahem ta'anat betulim* — a deaf-mute, a mentally ill woman, a *bogeret*, and a *mukat eitz* are not subject to *ta'anat betulim*. The *mukat eitz* is a woman who has sustained a physical injury that has ruptured the hymen; she obviously cannot be presumed a *besula* in the physical sense. The *bogeret* will be addressed separately. But the *chereshet* and *shota* are the live contradiction: one *beraisa* says the husband can raise a *ta'anat betulim*, the other says he cannot. One might have assumed this reflects a dispute about whether we presume these women are virgins — and if we do not, then there would similarly be a debate about whether they are entitled to a *knas*. But the *gemara* rejects this framing.

### Gamliel vs Yehoshua

Rav Sheshet resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — ha Rabban Gamliel, ha Rabbi Yehoshua.* This answer is elegant. The question of *ta'anat betulim* is not simply a function of whether we presume the woman to be a virgin. Even if we universally presume she is a virgin — and the *gemara* maintains that everyone agrees a *chereshet* and *shota* are presumed virgins, so that everyone agrees they are entitled to a *knas* — the husband's *ta'anat betulim* claim may or may not succeed for entirely separate reasons. Recall the dispute in the first *perek* between Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua regarding the defenses available to a wife when her husband raises a claim of non-virginity. Rabban Gamliel accepts certain counter-claims — for instance, that she was violated after the *kiddushin*, or that she is a *mukat eitz* — and if she makes such a claim, the husband's assertion does not prevail. Rabbi Yehoshua, by contrast, holds *lo mipi anu chayim*: no defense she can offer will save her. Accordingly, the *beraisa* that says the husband has a valid *ta'anat betulim* follows Rabbi Yehoshua — since she cannot rebut him successfully, his claim stands. The *beraisa* that says there is no *ta'anat betulim* follows Rabban Gamliel — not because we presume her not to be a virgin, but because there is always a counter-claim available that will defeat the husband's assertion.

### Beis Din Representation

The *gemara* presses further: granted that Rabban Gamliel validates such defenses — but Rabban Gamliel's validation was predicated on *her* making the claim. What if she cannot speak for herself? Does Rabban Gamliel's leniency apply when the wife never personally lodges any particular defense? The *gemara* answers affirmatively: *kivan de-amar Rabban Gamliel mehemana, she-kegon zeh ptach picha l'ilem hu* — since Rabban Gamliel established that a wife is believed when she offers such explanations, the principle of "open your mouth for the mute" applies, and *beis din* steps in to present the most advantageous position on her behalf. These women, as non-*bnei da'at*, cannot represent themselves, and so *beis din* assumes the role, arguing for them — whether the defense is that she was violated after the *kiddushin*, as *Rashi* suggests, or that she became a *mukat eitz*, as *Tosfos* prefers.

It is worth noting the underlying tension here: some of Rabban Gamliel's defenses derive their persuasive force from the woman's *nes* — the brazenness required to lie in her husband's presence — or from the definite (*bari*) character of her own claim. When *beis din* makes the argument on her behalf, those considerations weaken. Nevertheless, the principle of *beis din* representing those without legal competence extends to cases of *ketanim* and others lacking *da'at*, and the *gemara* accepts that this representation can suffice to defeat the husband's claim.

The upshot is significant: the contradiction in the *baraitot* about *chereshet* and *shota* has nothing to do with whether they are presumed virgins. Universally, we assume they are virgins, and universally they are entitled to a *knas*. The contradiction concerns only whether the husband's *ta'anat betulim* succeeds — and that turns on the Rabban Gamliel–Rabbi Yehoshua debate about available defenses.

## Bogeret & Suma Cases

### Bogeret Layla Rishona

The *gemara* now turns to the *bogeret*, who the *beraisa* listed as one who has no *ta'anat betulim*. What is the basis for this? The *gemara* cites Rav: *bogeret nosnin lah layla rishona* — for a *bogeret*, all hymenal bleeding during the first night of marriage is attributed to the hymen rather than to *dam niddah*. This refers back to the *niddah* discussion in the earlier *perekm* where the question was how long after the wedding night a woman's bleeding could be attributed to the hymen and therefore not treated as *niddah*. For a younger woman or one who has not yet established a regular cycle, more time is granted; for a *bogeret*, who has been regularly menstruating, only the first night qualifies. The critical point is that this ruling presupposes that a *bogeret* does have a hymen that bleeds. We assume the hymen is intact. So if the husband maintains the hymen was not intact on the wedding night, why can he not make a *ta'anat betulim*?

### Blood vs Petach

The *gemara* clarifies the distinction between two possible claims. The discussion is at the top of 36b. If the husband claims *ta'anat damim* — that there was no blood on the wedding night — then *hacha nami*, he would have a valid claim, because we assume a *bogeret* does have an intact hymen and there will be blood; the absence of blood is meaningful evidence. But the case of *bogeret ein lah ta'anat betulim* is where he makes not a claim about blood but a claim of *petach patuach* — that physically the experience did not feel as it would with a virgin. This claim fails for a *bogeret*, because the *gemara* says her more developed physiology means the situation will feel different regardless, and no inference can be drawn from that sensation. As *Rashi* explains, *bo'el kama be'ilos* — the first night includes multiple acts, and all the bleeding throughout that night is attributed to hymenal rupture, which is why the analysis focuses on the night as a whole. But once we shift to the *petach patuach* claim rather than the blood claim, the *bogeret*'s physical maturity renders that claim meaningless, and the husband loses.

### Suma Accident Theory

The *beraisa* cited earlier included a position from Sumchos, in the name of Rabbi Meir: *suma ein lah ta'anat betulim* — a blind woman cannot be subject to a *ta'anat betulim*, meaning the husband has no valid claim even if he finds evidence of non-virginity. The reasoning offered by Rav Zeira on behalf of Sumchos is that a blind woman is prone to stumbling and falling — *mipnei she-nichbetes al gabei karka* — and such a fall might cause a physical injury that ruptures the hymen, effectively making her a *mukat eitz*. Since one cannot rule out such an accident, the husband should not have assumed that an intact hymen was guaranteed, and his claim therefore carries no weight.

### All Women Have Accidents

The *gemara* challenges this immediately: *kulan nami chabutei michabtan* — all women trip and have accidents, not only blind women. Sighted women sustain physical injuries too. Notably, the *gemara* does not respond by arguing that blind women are statistically more accident-prone. That is not the *gemara*'s answer.

### Blind Woman Unawareness

Instead, the *gemara* offers a distinct principle: *kulan ro'ot u-mar'ot l'iman* — a sighted girl who has an accident and notices blood or some other indication will tell her mother what happened, and her mother will explain that the hymen has been affected. *Zo eina ro'a ve-eina mar'a l'ima* — this woman does not see what happened, and therefore she does not inform her mother, and she remains unaware that she has effectively become a *mukat eitz*. Accordingly, when she marries, she does not disclose anything to her husband — not out of concealment, but out of genuine ignorance. He, having had no reason to suspect an injury, enters the marriage assuming she is a *besula* in the full physical sense. According to Sumchos and Rabbi Meir, this is sufficient to deny the husband a valid *ta'anat betulim*: since she might have sustained such an injury without knowing it, he cannot now claim he was deceived.

One might note — and this was acknowledged — that this reasoning sits uncomfortably with what a woman's own physical senses would detect. The *gemara*'s formulation rests on the visual dimension of the experience, and the logic is somewhat strained when applied to a woman without sight. Nevertheless, the operative ruling according to Sumchos is that we place the blind woman in the category of one who might unknowingly be a *mukat eitz*, and the husband's claim is therefore not actionable.

## Shem Ra & Promiscuity

### Shem Ra Divorce

The *gemara* now returns to the first *beraisa* with which this section began, which listed another category of women who do not receive a *knas*: *ha-yotzet mishum shem ra ein lah knas v'lo pitu'i* — a woman who has been sent away on account of *shem ra* has neither a *knas* nor a claim arising from seduction. The *shem ra* referenced by the *Torah* in the case of *ve-hotzi aleha shem ra* is the husband's allegation of adultery that, if substantiated, results in the wife's execution. That is a woman who has already been married; what does this have to do with the laws of a *na'arah betulah*?

The *gemara* raises the obvious question: *b'skila hi* — she is subject to stoning. *Tosfos* addresses this with two lines of analysis. First, if she committed adultery she is definitively a *be'ulah* and self-evidently not a candidate for *knas*, which is limited to a *besula*. Second, one might have thought the issue is *kim lei b'deraba minei* — that the perpetrator's capital liability overrides the monetary *knas* — but *Tosfos* points out this is not the right framing here, because it is not the same individual facing both punishments in the usual sense of *kim lei*.

### Rumor vs Proof

Rav Sheshet therefore reinterprets the case entirely: *mi she-yatza lah shem ra be-yaldutah ein lah knas* — we are not speaking of the biblical *shem ra* case of proven adultery. Rather, *yotzet mishum shem ra* means that a rumor has circulated in the community that this woman is promiscuous — not proven adultery, not judicial procedure, but public gossip suggesting she has been sexually active. On that basis, she is presumed to be a *be'ulah* and loses her entitlement to a *knas*.

### Rumor Insufficient

Rav Pappa then draws an inference: *shma mina* — we can learn from this case that if there is only an unsubstantiated rumor, that alone is not legally sufficient. He analogizes to a *shtar* with a disputed validity: if people are merely gossiping that a document is a forgery — *nefik kala* — we do not refuse to enforce the document on that basis alone. The parallel would suggest that mere gossip about a woman's promiscuity is similarly insufficient to deny her a *knas*. And indeed, Rava states explicitly: *yatza lah shem mezana ba-ir ein chosheshin lah* — if a rumor circulates in a town that a woman is promiscuous, we give it no halachic weight whatsoever.

### Witnesses & Behavior

If so, what level of evidence does suffice? *De-atu bei terei ve-amri* — two people come and give firsthand testimony. But what exactly do they testify to? Not that they personally witnessed her engage in intercourse — that would not be mere *shem ra* at all. Rather, in the parallel case of the *shtar*: two witnesses come and testify that the claimant personally approached them and asked them to forge a document on his behalf. They refused, but the fact that he solicited forgery creates concrete suspicion about the document now before the court.

Analogously, by the woman: two men come and testify that she approached them and solicited intercourse. They declined, but the act of solicitation itself — firsthand knowledge of actual conduct, not mere rumor — creates a presumption that she has been *ba'alas* with someone, since such overtures, if repeated, will eventually find a willing recipient.

### Document vs Woman

The *gemara* pauses to question whether these two cases are truly analogous. *Bishlama hasam shechichi prutzim* — in the case of the woman, it is entirely credible to assume that if she repeatedly propositioning men, eventually someone will say yes, because promiscuous men are not uncommon. *Ela hacha* — but in the document case, if someone is looking for a forger and has not found one yet, should we not assume that people generally have a *chezkas kashrus* and will refuse? Perhaps even with money offered, no one agreed to forge.

The *gemara* rejects this distinction: *kevan de-mahader a-ziyufa, eimar zeiyuphi zeiyeiph* — since he was actively searching for someone to forge the document, even if he found no partner, he likely forged it himself. So in both cases — the woman who propositioned witnesses, and the man who sought a forger — concrete evidence of actual behavior (not mere rumor) creates a sufficient presumption to change the legal outcome. In neither case does bare gossip suffice; in both, firsthand testimony about specific conduct does. The *gemara* notes, with some irony, that we are apparently more willing to assume a man will ultimately say yes to a sexual proposition than to assume he will agree to forge a document, even when financial inducement is involved.

## Mishna: No Knas

### Convert/Captive/Slave

The *gemara* now arrives at the next *mishna*, which mirrors the first and makes explicit what the preceding week's discussion had been assuming. *Ve-eilu she-ein lahem knas* — the following women are not entitled to a *knas*. The first group consists of women presumed to be a *be'ulah* by virtue of their prior status. *Ha-ba al ha-giyores ve-al ha-shvuya ve-al ha-shifcha* — a woman who is a convert, a woman who was taken captive, or a woman who is a non-Jewish slave — *she-nifdU ve-she-nitgeiyeru ve-she-nishtachreru* — and who have since been redeemed, converted, or freed — are presumed *be'ulot* if their transition out of that status occurred when they were older than three years of age. The reasoning is that in their prior condition — as non-Jews, slaves, or captives — we assume they engaged in or were subject to intercourse, and since this occurred after age three, it constitutes a legally cognizable act that ruptures the hymen permanently.

### Yehuda: Captive Virgin

Rabbi Yehuda dissents regarding the captive woman: *shvuya she-nifdas harei hi be-kedushata* — a redeemed captive retains her sanctity, and we presume she was not raped, *af al pi she-gedolah* — even if she was an adult when captured. Accordingly, even an adult captive woman receives the *knas* and retains the full presumption of virginity. This immediately raises the question that will dominate the subsequent *gemara* discussion: if Rabbi Yehuda assumes she was not raped, why was this not stated earlier in the context of whether she may return to her *kohen* husband? The *gemara* will address this directly.

### Capital Crimes

The *mishna* then addresses the second category of women without a *knas*: those whose cases involve capital offenses. *Ha-ba al bito, al bas bito, al bas bno, al bas ishto, al bas bnah, al bas bitah* — intercourse with one's daughter, granddaughter, stepdaughter, or the children or grandchildren of a wife — *ein lahem knas* — carries no *knas*. The reason is distinct from the *be'ulah* issue: here it is because the offense is *chayav misa*, a capital crime.

### Ason Pasuk

The *mishna* provides the textual basis: *mipnei she-mischayev be-nafsho* — because the perpetrator is liable with his life. *Ve-chol ha-mischayev be-nafsho ein meshalem mamon* — anyone who has committed a capital offense does not pay a monetary penalty. The scriptural source is the *pasuk* in Exodus: *ve-im lo yihyeh ason anosh ye'anesh* — if there is no tragedy, then he shall pay the financial penalty. This implies that where there is a fatal consequence — where a *misa* attaches — the monetary penalty is set aside. This *pasuk* and the concept of *ason* are precisely what had been invoked throughout the preceding seven-*daf* *sugya* on *kim lei b'deraba minei*, and the *mishna* here provides the direct textual anchor for that entire discussion. One might ask whether the *pasuk* refers specifically to actual execution or more broadly to any capital-level offense even without *eidim* and *hasra'ah*; this connects to the *gemara*'s earlier treatment of *tana devei Chizkiya* and the distinction between *ason* and *din ason*, which the *gemara* discusses but which the *mishna* treats as settled for present purposes.

## Captive Woman Status

### Dosa's Assumption

Rabbi Yochanan observes that Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Dosa, though addressing different areas of *halacha*, effectively articulate the same underlying assumption. Rabbi Yehuda, as stated in our *mishna*, holds that a redeemed captive retains her presumed virginity. Rabbi Dosa, as taught in a *beraisa*, holds that a captive woman may continue to eat *terumah* — *shvuya ocheles bi-terumah divrei Rabbi Dosa* — which implies that her captivity does not invalidate her priestly status. Had she had intercourse with a non-Jew, even involuntarily, she would be disqualified from eating *terumah*. Rabbi Dosa's reasoning is stated directly: *vechi mah asa lah Aravi halaz* — what did this captor do to her? *Vechi mipnei she-mishmesh lah bein dadeha pasla min ha-kehuna* — merely because he may have fondled her, does that disqualify her from *kehuna*? Rabbi Dosa's position is that captors do not actually rape their captives; whatever contact may occur does not rise to the level of intercourse, and therefore her status remains intact.

### Rava's Doubt

Rava raises the question of whether these two positions — Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Dosa — necessarily cohere across all areas of *halacha*. Even if both assume the captive was not raped, perhaps each applied that assumption only within his particular domain. *Ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Yehuda hacha* — Rabbi Yehuda may have made this assumption specifically in the context of *knas*, and would not extend it to *terumah*. Similarly, Rabbi Dosa may have been speaking specifically about *terumah* and would not apply the same assumption to *knas*. Each area of *halacha* may carry its own logic that leads to leniency or stringency independent of what another area demands.

### Chotei Nishkar

Abaye then highlights the policy dimension of Rabbi Yehuda's position. *Shelo yihyeh chote nishkar* — the principle of ensuring that a sinner does not benefit from his transgression. The rapist is obligated to pay fifty *shekel*. If a technicality — the prior captive status of the victim — is used to exempt him from payment, then the sinner derives benefit: he commits a terrible act and escapes the full legal consequences. To prevent that outcome, Rabbi Yehuda is prepared to assume that the woman was not raped during captivity, so that a subsequent *ones* or *pituy* will not be exempted from its *knas*. This is not necessarily a categorical metaphysical claim about what captors do or do not do; it may be a policy-driven willingness to make the more protective assumption within the *knas* context specifically.

### Universal vs Contextual

The *gemara* tests whether Rabbi Yehuda's position is purely policy-driven or truly universal. A *beraisa* states: *shvuya she-nishbis harei hi bi-kedushata, afilu bas eser shanim kesubatah masayim* — a captive woman retains her presumed sanctity, and even at a substantial age her *kesuvah* remains two hundred. This suggests that Rabbi Yehuda's assumption is applied even in the *kesuvah* context — not merely in the *knas* context. If it were only a policy response to *chote nishkar*, why extend it to *kesuvah*? The *gemara* responds that the *kesuvah* context also has its own policy driver: *dilma minsi ve-lo nasvi lah* — if we declare that a formerly captive woman is presumed to have been raped, people will simply avoid marrying her. Branding her as a *be'ulah* by assumption makes her effectively unmarriageable. Society's interest in facilitating her remarriage therefore provides an independent policy reason to maintain the presumption of virginity in the *kesuvah* context as well, entirely separate from *chote nishkar*.

### Kesuvah Protection

The *gemara*'s conclusion on this point is that the assumptions made by Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Dosa may well be context-specific rather than categorical. When it comes to *knas*, there is a policy reason — *chote nishkar* — to presume the woman is a virgin. When it comes to *kesuvah*, there is a different but equally compelling policy reason — the desire to ensure she remains marriageable. When it comes to *terumah*, the analysis may point in a different direction: since *terumah* is nowadays rabbinic in force, there is room for leniency on that front; but since *knas* is biblical and involves extracting money from a defendant, one might be more cautious about imposing it on the basis of a contested assumption. Each domain has its own considerations, and the *gemara* leaves open that Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Dosa may not have intended their assumptions to operate uniformly across all areas.

## Kohein & Captive

### Redeem vs Testify

The *gemara* now examines a *beraisa* that addresses the *kohen* who wishes to marry a redeemed captive. The *beraisa* states: if a *kohen* redeems a captive woman, he may marry her. If he merely testifies about her status — that she was not violated — he may not marry her. This is initially puzzling: the more he knows about her fitness, the less he is permitted to act on that knowledge?

### Both Required

The *gemara* clarifies that the *beraisa* must be read conjunctively rather than disjunctively: *ha-podeh et ha-shvuya u-me'id bah yissanah* — the *kohen* who both redeems her and testifies about her may marry her; *me'id bah ke-dei lo yissanah* — testimony alone, without the act of redemption, is insufficient.

### Money as Proof

The logic is that testimony by itself, when offered by a man who wishes to marry the woman, is suspect: perhaps his *yetzer hara* has gotten the better of him and he is fabricating or embellishing. But if he also pays money to redeem her — *lo shadei inish zuzei bi-khdi* — people do not throw their money away for nothing. A man who is prepared to lay out real funds to redeem her is demonstrating through his actions that he is serious and truthful, and his accompanying testimony is therefore credible. The irony is apparent: one would think that a man willing to commit his life to marrying this woman would be all the more motivated to lie, but the financial outlay is treated as the threshold indicator of sincerity.

### Yehuda: Always Marry

Rabbi Yehuda's position in the *beraisa* is that *bein kach u-vein kach lo yissanah* — in either case, whether he redeems her or merely testifies, he may not marry her. This appears to contradict Rabbi Yehuda's general assumption that captive women are presumed not to have been raped. If he genuinely holds that assumption, why should the *kohen* be unable to marry her regardless?

### Yehuda vs Rabbanan

Rav Pappa suggests emending Rabbi Yehuda's position: read it as *bein kach u-vein kach yissanah* — either way he *may* marry her, since Rabbi Yehuda universally presumes the captive was not violated. Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua, however, defends the text as it stands and offers a different reading: *Rabbi Yehuda le-divrehem de-Rabbanan ka'amar* — Rabbi Yehuda is not stating his own view but is arguing *ad hominem* against the *Rabanan*. From his own perspective, *l'didi, bein kach u-vein kach yissanah* — either way he can marry her, because he holds categorically that captive women are presumed to be virgins. But he says to the *Rabanan*: *ela le-didchu, bein kach u-vein kach lo yissanah* — but according to you, who generally presume that captive women have been violated, you should logically prohibit the marriage in either case, because even a *kohen*'s own testimony should be viewed with suspicion when he stands to benefit from it through marriage. The *Rabanan* respond that when redemption accompanies testimony, *lo shadei inish zuzei bi-khdi* — the financial commitment renders the testimony reliable — and they therefore permit marriage in that combined case. Testimony without redemption remains insufficient.

The *gemara* thus concludes this section with Rabbi Yehuda's position understood not as mere policy calculation — whether about *chote nishkar* or *kesuvah* protection — but, as *Tosfos* emphasizes, as a genuine categorical principle: a captive woman is presumed not to have been raped for all purposes, including the question of whether a *kohen* may marry her.
