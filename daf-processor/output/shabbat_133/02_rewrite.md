# Shabbat 133 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Milah & Tzara'at

### Baraita: Tzara'at

The *daf* opens by returning to the *baraita* that derives the laws of *brit milah* from the phrase *basar orlato*, and the *gemara* now extends this analysis to the interaction between *milah* and *tzara'at*. The *baraita* states: even if there is a *baheret* — a *tzara'at* lesion — on the foreskin, one cuts it away together with the foreskin. *Baheret imo* — the lesion is removed along with it. This is the position of Rabbi Yehoshua.

The *gemara* immediately asks: why do you need a *pasuk* for this? The act of removing the *tzara'at* is a *davar she'eino mitkaven* — an unintended consequence. The mohel intends only to perform the *brit milah*; the removal of the *tzara'at* is incidental. And a *davar she'eino mitkaven* is permissible. The *gemara* even gestures at the obvious rejoinder — that this may be a case of *psik reisha* — before proceeding to examine the matter more carefully.

### Abaye vs. Rava

Abaye answers that the *pasuk* is necessary only according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that a *davar she'eino mitkaven* is forbidden even when unintended. The *pasuk* comes to override that prohibition and permit the incidental removal of *tzara'at* in the course of *milah*. Rava, however, argues that the *pasuk* is necessary even according to Rabbi Shimon — who generally permits unintended consequences — because *modeh Rabbi Shimon psik reisha velo yamut*: Rabbi Shimon concedes that when a forbidden result is the certain and inevitable consequence of one's action, it cannot be called truly unintended. Since cutting away the foreskin will necessarily remove the *tzara'at* along with it, this is a *psik reisha*, and Rabbi Shimon would ordinarily prohibit it. The *pasuk* therefore comes to permit it specifically in the context of *milah*.

The *gemara* then asks: does Abaye himself not hold that Rabbi Shimon agrees in a case of *psik reisha*? After all, both Abaye and Rava are cited together as stating *modeh Rabbi Shimon psik reisha velo yamut*. The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that Abaye came around to this position only after hearing it from Rava. In an earlier stage of his thinking, Abaye held that even a *psik reisha* would be permissible according to Rabbi Shimon, and therefore the *pasuk* was needed only for Rabbi Yehuda. Once persuaded by Rava's formulation, he accepted that Rabbi Shimon too would require the *pasuk* in a case of *psik reisha*.

### Indirect Removal

The *gemara* then notes that some authorities cite this same dispute — between Abaye and Rava regarding whether the *pasuk* is needed for Rabbi Yehuda alone or also for Rabbi Shimon — in connection with a different *baraita* about *tzara'at*, one not specifically involving *brit milah* but rather the general prohibition against removing a *tzara'at* lesion. The *pasuk* says *hishtamer b'nega hatzara'at lishmor vela'asot* — take great care regarding the *tzara'at* lesion, to observe and to act properly. The *baraita* derives from *la'asot* that one may not actively remove the lesion. However, one may wrap a bandage around one's leg to protect it from friction, or carry a pole on one's shoulder over the site of the lesion, and *im avrah avrah* — if the lesion happens to be worn away as a result, so it is. The prohibition is against active intentional removal; one need not go out of one's way to avoid circumstances that might incidentally cause the lesion to fade.

Here too the *gemara* asks why a *pasuk* is needed, since this is a *davar she'eino mitkaven*. And here too the same dispute plays out: Abaye says the *pasuk* is needed for Rabbi Yehuda, while Rava says it is needed even for Rabbi Shimon, since in a case where the rubbing away is a certain consequence, it would constitute a *psik reisha* and Rabbi Shimon would ordinarily prohibit it.

The *gemara* thus identifies two categories of permissible *tzara'at* removal that require scriptural authorization. The first is active removal during *brit milah*, which is explicitly sanctioned by the *pasuk* derived from *basar orlato*. The second is indirect removal through ordinary activity — wearing garments, carrying objects — where the removal happens incidentally; even here, where the removal may be a necessary consequence, the *pasuk* tells us it is permissible.

### Rav Avin: Intent

The *gemara* then returns to Abaye's earlier position and asks: according to the view that even Rabbi Shimon would have permitted *psik reisha* across the board, what purpose does the word *basar* serve in the derivation? If everything short of active intention were already permitted under Rabbi Shimon's general principle, the specific *pasuk* by *tzara'at* and *milah* would be redundant.

Rav Avin answers: the *pasuk* addresses a case where the person performing the *brit milah* explicitly declares that he intends to cut away the *tzara'at* as well. In such a case one might have thought that since there is now active intent to violate the prohibition of *tzara'at* removal, the act should be forbidden. The *pasuk* teaches that so long as he is performing the *brit milah* and not altering his actions because of the *tzara'at*, it does not matter that he harbors the intent to remove the lesion — the *milah* permits the removal regardless. This is a significant *chiddush*: even explicit intent does not disqualify the act, provided the *milah* itself proceeds normally.

### Minor & Intent

The *gemara* then asks: this explanation works well for an adult performing his own *brit milah*, since one can speak of the adult having such intent. But what about a minor? A minor cannot form the relevant intention. The *gemara* answers by way of Rav Mesharshiya: the case for the minor is that *avi haben* — the father — declares that he intends to remove the *tzara'at* as part of the *milah* being performed on his son. The *chiddush* is the same: even though the father actively intends to remove the *tzara'at*, the *milah* permits this, provided nothing changes about how the procedure is performed.

### Alternative Methods

The *gemara* immediately adds a qualification: even if the *pasuk* permits acting with such intent, one should nonetheless seek to minimize direct conflict where possible. Citing Resh Lakish, the *gemara* invokes the principle that wherever one encounters a positive commandment in conflict with a negative commandment, if it is possible to fulfill both, that is the preferred course; only when it is impossible does the *aseh* override the *lo ta'aseh*. Applied here: if there is another mohel available who does not harbor the explicit intent to remove the *tzara'at*, one should use him instead, so as to avoid the direct confrontation between the *aseh* of *milah* and the prohibition against removing *tzara'at*. It is only when no such alternative is available — *d'leika acher* — that the current mohel, despite his stated intent, may proceed.

It is worth noting that the standard rule of *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh* is not what is operating here in the straightforward sense, since the prohibition against *tzara'at* removal is not merely a *lo ta'aseh* but also a *mitzvat aseh* — making it more weighty than a simple negative commandment. The permission here derives from the specific *pasuk* regarding *brit milah*, not from the general principle. The *gemara*'s invocation of the principle is therefore to press the point that even where a *pasuk* permits the conflict, the preference is always to find a way that avoids it.

One might ask whether, at least in the case of an adult who is not yet circumcised, the solution is simply to wait until the *tzara'at* clears. This is an excellent question, and indeed the *chiddush* of the *pasuk* seems to go beyond mere urgency: even where one could theoretically wait for the lesion to resolve, the *milah* nonetheless licenses the removal. This is not a matter of overriding the *tzara'at* prohibition due to time pressure — it is a more fundamental redefinition of what the act of *milah* permits. That is also precisely why the *gemara* becomes more demanding when the mohel actively intends the removal: if the act itself is what provides the license, that license holds even without urgency, but it should still be conducted in the least confrontational manner possible.

## Milah & Yom Tov

### Late Milah

The *gemara* now turns back to the earlier *baraita* and addresses the principle that *yom tov eino docheh* — a *brit milah* that is not performed on its eighth day does not override *Yom Tov*. The contrast with *Shabbat* is worth noting: one might have thought that since *Yom Tov* is less weighty than *Shabbat*, the *aseh* of *milah* could override the *lo ta'aseh* of *Yom Tov* through the standard principle of *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh*. The *chiddush* is therefore more pronounced here — even *Yom Tov* is not overridden by a *milah shelo bizmanah*.

### Chizkiyah Drasha

The *gemara* asks: from where do we know this? Chizkiyah, and the school of Chizkiyah, derive it from the double mention of *ad boker* in the laws of the *korban pesach*. The *pasuk* first states *lo sosiru mimenu ad boker*, do not leave over from the *korban pesach* until the morning of the fifteenth of Nisan, and then says *v'hanosar mimenu ad boker ba'eish tisrofu*, what remains, burn it in fire in the morning. The sages read the second *ad boker* as referring not to the morning of the fifteenth but to the morning of the sixteenth, thereby establishing that the burning of leftover *pesach* meat is performed on the sixteenth rather than the fifteenth. The implication is that burning *nosar* — itself a *mitzvah* — is pushed off from the fifteenth because the fifteenth is *Yom Tov*, even though the *mitzvah* of burning could theoretically be done that day. Since the burning is not specifically a *mitzvah* of the fifteenth — it can wait until the sixteenth — it does not override *Yom Tov*. By the same logic, a *milah shelo bizmanah*, which has no specific urgency of today, does not override *Yom Tov*.

### Pesach Offerings

Abaye derives the same principle from a different *pasuk*: *olas Shabbos b'shabbaso* — the Shabbat *olah* is brought on its Shabbat, and not the *olah* of a weekday on Shabbat, nor the *olah* of *erev Yom Tov* on *Yom Tov*. Although burning leftover fat is a *mitzvah*, since it is not specifically the *mitzvah* of that day, it cannot override *Yom Tov*. The same logic extends to *milah shelo bizmanah*.

Rava derives it from yet another *pasuk*: *hu levado yei'aseh lachem* — it, alone, may be done for you on *Yom Tov*. The word *hu* emphasizes that only what is done for the sake of eating on *Yom Tov* is permitted, and not even *machshirin* — preparatory acts — are included. The derivation *levado v'lo milah shelo bizmanah* excludes a non-eighth-day *milah* from the category of what overrides *Yom Tov*. This is all the more significant because one might have argued the contrary by way of *kal vachomer*: *tzara'at* overrides the Temple service, the Temple service overrides Shabbat, and *milah* overrides *tzara'at* — perhaps *milah* should override Shabbat or *Yom Tov* even when not in its proper time. The specific *pasuk* forecloses that reasoning.

### Yom Tov Nature

Rav Ashi takes a different approach entirely. In his view, no special *pasuk* is needed for *milah shelo bizmanah* vis-à-vis *Yom Tov*, because *Yom Tov* itself carries both a positive and a negative dimension: *shabbaton asei hu* — the requirement that *Yom Tov* be a day of rest is itself a positive commandment, in addition to the *lo ta'aseh* of *lo ta'aseh melachah*. Since *Yom Tov* comprises both an *aseh* and a *lo ta'aseh*, the straightforward rule of *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh* cannot apply — a single *aseh* does not override a *lo ta'aseh v'asei*.

The specific *psukim* that permit *milah bizmanah* to override Shabbat were directed precisely at eighth-day *milah*, as derived from *u'vayom hashemini yimol basar arlato* — "on the eighth day." Those *psukim*, and the special weight they confer, apply only to *milah bizmanah*. Once a *milah* is not performed on the eighth day, it loses that exceptional license and becomes like any other *mitzvah*. Any ordinary *mitzvah* — a single *aseh* — cannot override the *aseh v'lo ta'aseh* of *Yom Tov*, and so *milah shelo bizmanah* does not override it either.

It is worth noting, in connection with this discussion, one important and practically relevant exception to the principle that eighth-day *milah* overrides Shabbat. The *gemara* elsewhere derives this override from the *pasuk* in *Parshat Tazria*: *ishah ki tazria v'yaldah zachar u'vayom hashemini yimol basar arlato*. The *drash* understands *ki tazria* as referring specifically to birth through the natural birth canal — *b'makom shemazra'at*. A birth by cesarean section therefore falls outside this *parasha*, and the *asei* of *milah* in such a case does not override Shabbat. This is a significant and practically important *halacha* that is not always known: a baby born by cesarean section whose eighth day falls on Shabbat does not have his *brit milah* performed on Shabbat. The *halacha* is ruled accordingly.

The *gemara* concludes this section by affirming *halacha k'Rabbi Akiva*, who articulates the general rule that anything that could have been done before Shabbat does not override Shabbat, and that this ruling applies both in the context of *milah* and in the context of *korban pesach*. The *gemara* notes that both rulings must be stated explicitly — one cannot be inferred from the other. If we knew only the ruling by *milah*, we might think that by *korban pesach*, where there is *kares* for failing to bring it, even the preparatory acts should be permitted on Shabbat. And if we knew only the ruling by *korban pesach*, we might think that by *milah*, where the covenant was mentioned thirteen times in the *parasha* of Avraham, even the *machshirin* should override Shabbat. Each ruling is independently necessary.

## Milah on Shabbat

### Tzorchei Milah

The *mishna* states: *osin kol tzorchei milah b'Shabbat* — all the needs of *milah* are performed on Shabbat. This encompasses two distinct categories. The first includes acts that are part of the *mitzvah* of *brit milah* itself, which overrides Shabbat. The second includes health-related procedures performed after the *milah* is complete, which are permitted on the basis of *pikuach nefesh*.

### Preparation Issues

The *mishna* enumerates the acts that are permitted: *molin u'porin u'motzatzin* — the cutting of the foreskin, the *priah* (tearing away the thin membrane beneath the foreskin), and *metzitzah* (drawing out the blood from the wound). One may then apply a bandage. Cumin, which appears to have been used to treat the wound, may be applied; if it was not ground before Shabbat, one may chew it with one's teeth and apply it from the mouth. Similarly, if wine and oil were not mixed together before Shabbat, one does not mix them now but rather applies each one separately to the wound. The emphasis in these last cases is that where advance preparation was not done, the procedure should be carried out with a *shinui* — an alteration from the normal manner of performing the act.

### Wrapping & Shinui

The *mishna* further states that one may not fashion a specially cut wrap for the wound *lechatchilah* on Shabbat, but one may take a cloth and wrap it around. If no cloth was prepared at the site of the *brit milah* before Shabbat, one may wrap it around one's finger and carry it there, even from another courtyard. Rashi explains that this wrap serves not merely a medical purpose but also to prevent the skin from moving back over the site of the incision — and so it is more properly understood as part of the *milah* itself rather than purely a health measure.

### Shinui Debate

The requirement to perform these acts with a *shinui* when not prepared in advance raises a broader question. Tosafot note: even though after the *brit milah* the baby has the status of a *choleh she'yesh bo sakanah* — a patient whose life may be at risk — we nonetheless require a *shinui* wherever possible: *kama d'efshar l'shannuyei meshanninan*. This is a striking position, because the standard rule is that for a genuine *choleh she'yesh bo sakanah*, one acts without hesitation and without modification.

What emerges from Tosafot is that the categories of a woman in childbirth and a baby after *brit milah* represent a distinct class: they are treated presumptively as *choleh she'yesh bo sakanah* even without specific indicators that the situation is life-threatening, yet precisely because this is a kind of default classification rather than a case of manifest acute danger, there remains room to require a *shinui* when one can be managed. This is different from the approach taken to an unambiguous medical emergency — a building collapse, a cardiac event — where one acts completely without modification. The two cases we have encountered so far in which a *shinui* is expected, namely childbirth and post-*milah*, form a category of their own, and that distinction is worth bearing in mind.

## Continuity of Act

### Kol Tzorchei

At the top of 133b, the *gemara* asks: the *mishna* listed many specific permitted acts, and then said *kol tzorchei milah* — all the needs of *milah*. What does the inclusive phrase add that the list did not already cover?

### Engagement Status

The *gemara* answers by citing a *baraita*: as long as the mohel is actively engaged in the *brit milah* — *kol zman she'hu osek b'milah* — he may return to cut away remaining strands of skin (*tzitzin*), whether those strands are ones that *me'akvin et ha-milah* — that prevent the *milah* from being halachically valid — or those that *ein me'akvin* — that do not prevent validity but leave the *milah* aesthetically incomplete. In other words, even after enough has been done that the *milah* is technically valid and the person is *yotzei*, the mohel may continue cutting, since he is still in the midst of the act.

However, once *peireish* — once the mohel has disengaged, setting down his instrument and stepping back from the procedure — the situation changes. If he then notices that something was left incomplete, he may return only to remove strands that *me'akvin et ha-milah*, that is, strands whose presence renders the *milah* invalid. He may not return to remove strands that are merely cosmetically incomplete. The *chiddush* of *kol tzorchei milah* is thus that continued engagement permits more than the bare minimum required for the *mitzvah* — the mohel may finish the aesthetic work so long as he has not disengaged.

This raises the question: who holds that once one disengages, one cannot return even for *tzitzin she'ein me'akvin*? Perhaps there is a position that even after *peireish* one may return, since the entire act was licensed from the outset.

### Pesach Analogy

Rabba bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan answers: it is Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka. The *baraita* there discusses the case where the fourteenth of Nisan falls on Shabbat. One may flay the *korban pesach* on Shabbat up to the chest — *mafshit et ha-Pesach ad ha-chaze* — in order to reach the *eimurim* (the portions to be burned on the altar), which must be burned that day. This flaying is permitted because it is necessary for the *korban* that must be done on Shabbat itself. Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka holds that once the *eimurim* have been removed, one may not continue flaying the entire animal, since the remaining flaying serves only to preserve the meat for the evening meal — a purpose that belongs to after Shabbat. The rabbis disagree and permit flaying the whole animal, since that too aids the proper use of the *korban*.

According to Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, once the *eimurim* have been removed, the licensed act is complete, and re-engaging with the remaining flaying is considered a new act rather than a continuation of the same process. The *gemara* finds this parallel to the case of *milah*: once the mohel disengages after completing a technically valid *milah*, returning to remove cosmetically incomplete strands is likewise treated as a new act rather than a continuation.

### Aesthetic Doctrine

The *gemara* immediately challenges this parallel: perhaps by *milah* one should be permitted to return even after *peireish*, because of *Zeh Eli v'anvehu* — the principle of beautifying the performance of *mitzvot*. The *baraita* expounds: *hitna'eh lefanav b'mitzvot* — adorn yourself before God through the *mitzvot*. This means making a beautiful *sukkah*, a beautiful *lulav*, a beautiful *shofar*, beautiful *tzitzit*, a beautiful *Sefer Torah* — with beautiful ink, a fine quill, an expert scribe, wrapped in fine silks. All of this constitutes the *mitzvah* of *Zeh Eli v'anvehu*: to beautify not only the *mitzvah* object itself but all that accompanies it.

Applied to *brit milah*: by going back and finishing the aesthetic work after *peireish*, one is beautifying the *milah* itself. Perhaps, then, the principle of *Zeh Eli v'anvehu* extends the license to re-engage, and one should be permitted to return even after separating. This would distinguish *milah* from the *korban pesach* case, where completing the flaying does not beautify any aspect of the sacrificial act itself and thus cannot be grounded in this principle.

## Milah & Yom Tov…

### Beautification

The *baraita* on *Zeh Eli v'anvehu* concludes with Abba Shaul's interpretation: *ve'anvehu hevei domeh lo* — glorify Him means become like Him. Abba Shaul reads *ve'anvehu* as *ani va'hu* — I and He — and understands the verse as an obligation of *imitatio dei*. Just as He is compassionate and gracious, so too you should be compassionate and gracious: *mah hu chanun v'rachum af atah heyei chanun v'rachum*.

It is worth pausing here to note that the tradition contains two distinct formulations of this idea of walking in God's ways. One formulation focuses on character traits — *chanun v'rachum*, compassion and graciousness — cultivated internally. The other formulation focuses on actions: just as God clothed the naked and visited the sick, so too should you clothe the naked and visit the sick. These represent two recognizably different approaches to the moral life: one grounded in virtue and the cultivation of character, the other grounded in the performance of specific beneficial acts.

The Rambam was a particularly strong proponent of the character-trait approach. He explains, for instance, the teaching in *Pirkei Avot* that *hakol lefi hamaaseh* — it is all according to the action — as meaning that giving a small amount of *tzedakah* repeatedly is preferable to giving a large amount at once, precisely because frequent giving more effectively cultivates the internal disposition of generosity. This emphasis on character formation, rather than on the magnitude of the act, reflects the Rambam's broader moral philosophy. The Rambam's approach also ties into his theological position: since God cannot truly be described in positive terms, the reason the Torah uses attributes like *chanun* and *rachum* to describe God is not to tell us what God actually is, but to indicate what character traits we should emulate. The *Torah* chose those particular attributes as the model for human striving — rather than, say, God's role in bringing natural calamity — in order to orient human moral development toward *ve'halachta bidrachav*.

The immediate point of the *gemara*, however, is that *Zeh Eli v'anvehu* as applied to *milah* might supply grounds for permitting the mohel to re-engage after *peireish* in order to aesthetically complete the *milah*. The *gemara* takes this suggestion seriously and must look elsewhere for the authority that prevents such re-engagement.

## Continuity of Act…

### Lechem Panim

Rav Ashi offers an alternative resolution. He identifies the *tanna* who holds that one may not return after *peireish* as the *rabbanan* who dispute Rabbi Yose in a different context.

The *mishna* in the context of *Lechem haPanim* describes the procedure for exchanging the showbread on Shabbat. Four *kohanim* enter: two carrying the two new stacks of bread and two carrying the two *bazichin* (spoons of incense). Four others enter ahead of them: two to remove the existing stacks and two to remove the existing spoons. The ones carrying the new bread stand on the north side, facing south; those removing the old bread stand on the south side, facing north. *Elu moshchin v'elu manichim* — as some draw out, others set down, simultaneously, so that the *shulchan* is never without bread for even a moment. *Tefach shel zeh b'tzad tefach shel zeh* — one person's *tefach* of bread is immediately adjacent to the other's, the old being drawn out as the new is placed in, because the *pasuk* says *lechem panim lefanai tamid* — the showbread must be constantly before Me.

Rabbi Yose holds that even if the old bread is removed first and there is a gap before the new bread is placed, that too is considered *tamid* — continuous — since the bread was there in the morning and will be there again shortly. The *rabbanan* insist that *tamid* means there can be no interruption whatsoever; a single moment without bread on the *shulchan* disqualifies the arrangement from being called continuous.

Rav Ashi draws the analogy: the *rabbanan* here hold that any break in a state or process renders it non-continuous. Applied to *brit milah*: once the mohel has disengaged — *peireish* — there is a break in the continuity of the act. The *rabbanan* would therefore say that returning after this break is not a continuation but a new act, and a new act is not licensed by the original permission to perform *milah* on Shabbat.

This is a striking conceptual move. The *gemara* is comparing the continuity of a static state — the permanent presence of the *Lechem haPanim* — with the continuity of an active process, namely the performance of *milah*. One might object that these are fundamentally different: if we ask whether a static state was uninterrupted, any gap in the state defeats the claim; but if we ask whether an action was continuous, a pause in the middle of the same process by the same actor might be treated differently. The *gemara* nonetheless draws the parallel, and it is from this comparison that the ruling emerges that a mohel who disengages may not return to remove *tzitzin she'ein me'akvin*.

The overall conclusion of this section is therefore twofold: first, so long as the mohel remains actively engaged, he may continue to cut even what is not strictly necessary for the *milah*'s validity — this is the *chiddush* of *kol tzorchei milah*; and second, once he disengages, he may only return to complete what is genuinely indispensable.

## Incomplete Milah

### Final Strands

The *baraita* states: *mechatkhin et ha-milah* — one may trim the final strands of the *milah* on Shabbat; and if one did not do so, one is liable to *kares*.

### Who Gets Kares

The *gemara* asks: who is liable to *kares* here, and for what? *Kares* could theoretically be for failing to complete the *brit milah*, or it could be for violating Shabbat by performing the act without proper authorization. Rav Kahana answers: it is the *umman* — the mohel — who is liable, meaning the *kares* here is for violating Shabbat.

### Rav Pappa

Rav Pappa objects: why should the mohel be liable? He could have said to those present, "I did half the *mitzvah* — you do the other half." *Ana avid palga d'mitzvah, atun avid palga d'mitzvah.* What he did was permitted on Shabbat; another person could have completed the remainder. The mohel's partial performance was authorized, so how can he be considered to have violated Shabbat?

Rav Pappa therefore reinterprets the *baraita*: it refers not to the mohel but to the *gadol* — the adult who failed to complete his own *brit milah* — who receives *kares* for remaining uncircumcised. Rav Ashi objects to this as well: the *kares* for remaining uncircumcised is explicit in the *pasuk* — *v'arel zachar asher lo yimmol* — and requires no *baraita* to establish it. The *baraita* must therefore be coming to teach something beyond the obvious.

### Nightfall Case

Rav Ashi accordingly returns to the original interpretation: it is the *umman* — the mohel — who is liable, and the *kares* is for violating Shabbat. The case is one where the mohel arrived at *bein hashmashot* — at the very onset of twilight, the transition into Shabbat — and was told by those present that he would not have enough time to complete the *brit milah* before Shabbat began. *Lo mesafket*, they said. He replied that he would have enough time, *mesafkina*, proceeded to begin the *brit milah*, and was unable to finish before Shabbat arrived. Having begun without sufficient time and having failed to complete the procedure, he is found to have performed a *chabbura* — an act of wounding — on Shabbat without the authorization that completing the *milah* would have conferred. Because he was warned and chose to proceed recklessly, he is held responsible: *mechalel Shabbat* and liable accordingly.

This raises an interesting question that the *gemara* leaves unanswered: what if a mohel began with full authorization — where there was ample time and no warning — and then through some misfortune ruined the procedure without being able to complete it? Would he be considered to have violated Shabbat? The case the *gemara* constructed is specifically one in which the mohel should never have begun, having been warned that he lacked sufficient time. The conclusion is therefore that he acted without proper license from the outset, and his failure to complete the *milah* confirms that the Shabbat violation was not covered by the *mitzvah*. The broader question — whether a mohel who began with genuine license but somehow could not complete the procedure would be held liable — remains open.
