# Pesachim 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bal Yera'eh Bal Yimatze

### Extension outside house

We begin at the point on 5b where the lines widen. Yesterday's discussion established the scope of the *issur* of *bal yera'eh bal yimatzei*: it applies to one's *chametz* whether or not it is in one's house, because one *pasuk* speaks of *bevateichem* and another of *bigvulecha*, and both are required. Each *pasuk* independently is insufficient — from one alone, one might have thought it permissible to bury the *chametz*, or that while it cannot be found in one's house, it may remain in a field, cave, or cistern. The two *pesukim* together close off those possibilities entirely.

### Hiding & deposits issue

The *Gemara* now turns to a seeming contradiction embedded in a lengthy passage reviewed at the close of the previous session. The position was advanced there that one might have thought it permissible to hide one's *chametz* or to accept a *pikdon* from a *nochri* — reasoning that the *chametz* deposited by the non-Jew is not one's own, and therefore not subject to the prohibition. Yet the *Gemara* responds: *talmud lomar lo yimatzei* — the *pasuk* says it cannot be found, and that suffices to forbid it.

But this creates an immediate tension: did we not just say from *lo yera'eh lecha* that one is prohibited from seeing only one's *own* chametz, but that *chametz* belonging to others — even *hekdesh chametz* — is permitted? If so, why should accepting a deposit of non-Jewish *chametz* be a problem at all? The *nochri* is asking you to watch his Oreo cookies, so to speak. Either that is a problem or it is not.

### Responsibility principle

The *Gemara* resolves this by drawing a distinction: *ha de-kibbel alei achrayut ha de-lo kibbel alei achrayut* — it depends entirely on whether one has accepted responsibility for the deposit. If something happens to the non-Jew's *chametz* while it is in your possession and you are obligated to compensate him, it is as if the *chametz* is yours. The logic of *shomer chinam* and *shomer sachar* applies: once you accept financial responsibility, the object acquires the legal character of your own property. If you did not accept such responsibility, it remains the non-Jew's *chametz* — the kind one is permitted to "see" because it is not *shelcha*.

### Mechuza soldiers case

This principle finds expression in a parallel ruling: *ha de-amar lehu Rava livnei Mechuza* — just as Rava instructed the residents of Mechuza regarding the soldiers quartered in their homes. The objection to housing soldiers is not a new one — colonial-era America had similar grievances — but the *halachic* point Rava makes is that the residents of Mechuza were obligated to remove the *chametz* of the soldiers billeted with them. Why? *Keivan di-illu migna ve-illu mitabed birshuteichun kai ve-bayitu lishalumei* — because if that *chametz* were stolen or lost, it would be within their domain and they would be required to pay for it. Therefore, *kedilchon dami ve-assur* — it is treated as theirs and is forbidden.

It is worth noting the interesting asymmetry in this case: the residents are not voluntarily accepting responsibility — they are being *held* responsible by the soldiers' presence. Yet the *halachic* consequence is identical.

### Svara vs. pasuk

The *Gemara* now questions the very premise of this analysis. *Hanicha leman de-amar davar hagorem lemamon kemamon dami* — this entire construction works smoothly according to the view that something which will cause a person to pay money is itself treated as their property. But what of the view that holds *lav kemamon dami* — that such indirect financial exposure does not render an object legally one's own?

According to that view, *shani hacha de-amar lo yimatzei* — in the case of *chametz*, it is different, because the Torah itself says *lo yimatzei*. The *pasuk* functions as an independent *chumra*, rendering the *chametz* forbidden even where general principles would not do so.

A second formulation reverses the emphasis. *Ika de-amri*: for the view that *davar hagorem lemamon lav kemamon dami*, the *pasuk* of *lo yimatzei* is precisely what is needed — *hainu de-itztrich lo yimatzei* — that is why the special *pasuk* is required. For the view that *kemamon dami*, however — that indirect financial liability does render the object one's own — the *pasuk* appears superfluous. Why would I need the *pasuk* if a *svara* already establishes the same conclusion?

This raises a broader point, and a striking one. *Svarot* are apparently as powerful as *pesukim* — yet the *Gemara*'s treatment of when a *pasuk* is rendered unnecessary by a *svara* is not entirely consistent. Sometimes they invoke *lavo alav bishnei lavin*, deriving two separate prohibitions from what might seem redundant. Other times, when a *svara* exists, the *pasuk* is redirected to serve a different *inyan* entirely. The tension is real, and both possibilities are alive here.

### Returned chametz case

The *Gemara* concludes that even for the view that *kemamon dami*, the *pasuk* is still needed, and for a specific reason: *salka da'atcha amina ho'il vechi ityei hadar be'einei lav birshutei kai kamashma lan*. One might have thought that in a case where the *chametz* will ultimately be returned to the non-Jew — where, for example, the soldiers pack up and leave and one simply hands back their *chametz* — it is never truly in one's possession. After all, it was given to you, you never wanted it, it is his, and when he leaves you give it back. One might reason: *beinei* — it is intact, it remains his throughout, and it was never mine at any moment. Therefore, comes the *pasuk* to teach that even then, one transgresses *bal yera'eh uval yimatzei*.

This is quite a stringent ruling. The *pasuk lo yimatzei* extends the prohibition even to *chametz* one never intended to acquire, never exercised dominion over, and that will be returned whole to its rightful owner.

## Non-Jewish ownership

### Bechor ownership issue

Having discussed the concept of non-Jewish ownership over *chametz*, the *Gemara* transitions to a related category: cases where a non-Jew holds partial ownership in an animal that would otherwise be subject to *bechorah*. As we know from *Kodashim*, an animal must be entirely in Jewish ownership to be subject to the obligation of *bechor* — partial non-Jewish ownership exempts it.

The question now becomes: what if the non-Jewish government has not a proprietary share in a specific animal, but rather a general tax claim against one's livestock? The government, after all, demands a tenth of one's cattle — but it does not have a *tefisah* in this particular animal. One can give them a different animal, not the *bechor*. Does the government's general tax lien nonetheless constitute enough of an ownership interest to exempt the *bechor*?

### Arnona tax case

This is the question posed in *ba'ei Rava behemot arnona* — the case of the *arnona* tax. Is such an animal *chayevet bivchorah* or not? The *Gemara* specifies: *kol heicha de-matzi mesalek lei b'zuzi lo kamiba'aya lan de-chayav* — where you can pay off the government in money, there is no question. If the government is content with cash and has no specific interest in taking animals, then there is clearly no partial ownership in any particular beast, and the animal is certainly subject to the *bechor* obligation.

The question arises only *heicha de-lo matzi mesalek lei b'zuzi* — where you cannot pay them off, where they specifically insist on a tenth of your actual herd. Does that specific demand create a lien sufficient to be considered an ownership stake in the *bechor* and thereby exempt it? Or is it still more abstract — a general claim against the herd that does not attach to any individual animal, leaving the *bechor* subject to its own separate obligation?

### Rava's exemption

Rava was asked, and he responded: *peturah* — exempt. Where you cannot pay them off and they specifically demand animals, the tax lien is considered substantial enough to constitute a degree of non-Jewish ownership that exempts the animal from *bechorah*.

### Behemah vs. isah

*Ika de-amri* introduces an alternative formulation. Here Rav Pappa states that there is a *matnita* that actually declares the animal *patur* from *bechorah* — but then draws a distinction with the case of *isat ha'arnona*, dough subject to the same tax: *chayevet be-challah*, it is still subject to the *challah* obligation, *ve-af al gav de-lo matzi mesalek lei* — even where one cannot pay them off. If we are saying the animal is exempt, why is the bread subject?

### Public knowledge

*Behemah it lei kala, isah leit lei kala* — for livestock, the matter is well known; for bread, it is not. The reasoning is as follows: tax collectors coming to take cattle is a matter of public knowledge — everyone in a community is aware that the government has an interest in the herd, and that this claim effectively constitutes a partial ownership stake. The *bechor* is therefore understood by all to be exempt. Bread, by contrast, is not subject to the same public awareness. The government's interest in the baker's output may not be widely known, and people who observe the baker not taking *challah* will not understand the reason. The result is a concern of *marit ayin* — it will appear that *challah* is simply not being taken, giving rise to a problematic appearance of neglecting a Torah obligation. Therefore, even where the government technically has a claim over the dough, the obligation of *challah* remains in force.

The precise mechanics of who would observe whether *challah* was taken is admittedly puzzling — how public can the baker's practice actually be? One possible resolution: in a town with a single baker, the government's tax policy on cattle is general knowledge, but the specific implications for bread may not be. In any event, that is the principle: *it lei kala* versus *leit lei kala* drives the distinction between the two cases.

## Non-Jew in house

### Non-Jew's chametz entry

The *Gemara* now turns to a *beraita* addressing the non-Jew who enters a Jewish home with *chametz* in hand: *nochri shenichnas lechatzeiro shel yisrael ve-chamtzo beyado* — a non-Jew who enters the courtyard of a Jew with his *chametz*. The ruling: *ein ze zakuk leva'er* — one is not obligated to have it removed. It is not your *chametz*; you have no obligation to tell him to leave or to dispose of it immediately.

### Designated space

However, *hifkido etzlo zakuk leva'er* — if he deposits it with you, you are obligated to remove it. This, as established, follows from the principle of *achrayut*: accepting the deposit makes it legally yours.

The *beraita* then introduces a third case: *yicheid lo bayit* — if one designates a space for the non-Jew's *chametz*, *ein ze zakuk leva'er* — one is again not obligated. Rashi explains that by designating a space and saying, essentially, put it wherever you like, one is not thereby accepting *achrayut*. That is the entire point: the obligation to remove flows from acceptance of responsibility, and by designating a space without formally accepting a deposit, one makes clear that the *chametz* remains entirely the non-Jew's.

### Tosafot view

Tosafot disagrees with Rashi's framing. According to Tosafot, even if one *has* accepted *achrayut*, if one nonetheless designates a specific space for the non-Jew, that suffices to exempt from the obligation. The two positions — Rashi's, that designating a space signals *non-acceptance* of responsibility, and Tosafot's, that it operates as an independent factor even alongside accepted responsibility — lead to meaningfully different frameworks.

### Lo yimatze scope

The *beraita* concludes with the phrase *shene'emar lo yimatze*. The question then arises: to which case does this *pasuk* attach? Rav Pappa says it refers back to the first case — *areisha ka'i* — specifically to the case of the formal deposit, *hifkido etzlo zakuk leva'er*. The *pasuk lo yimatzei* is the reason one must remove it in that case: by accepting the deposit and its attendant responsibility, the *chametz* becomes one's own and falls under *lo yimatzei*. According to Rashi's view, the second case — designating a space without accepting responsibility — would not implicate *lo yimatzei* at all, since the *chametz* never became one's own.

### Rav Ashi's view

Rav Ashi reads it differently: *l'olam aseifa ka'i* — the *pasuk* actually refers to the second case, the designated space. How does that work? *Ke-shene'emar lo yimatze b'vateichem* — because the Torah says it cannot be found *in your homes*, and this *chametz* is not considered to be in your home at all. *V'hai lav dideih hu* — it is not yours. And why is it not yours? Because *nochri ki ka ayil l'veitei d'nafshei ka ayil* — when the non-Jew enters and retrieves his *chametz*, he is entering what is, in the relevant sense, *his* space. He has rights to that designated area such that when he goes into it, he goes into his own domain.

The *pasuk* thus comes to *permit* the second case, not to *prohibit* it. *Lo yimatzei* teaches that only *chametz* which is genuinely *matsui beyadcha* — readily accessible to you — is prohibited. This designated space, which the non-Jew enters as if it were his own, does not meet that threshold.

### Sekhirus kanya

This, however, raises a sharp question: *lemeimra de-shkhirut kanya* — does this mean that designating a space for the non-Jew effectively constitutes a rental? Has the non-Jew thereby acquired the area as a *shkhirut*? If so, one needs to account for the broader *halachic* principle regarding renting to non-Jews.

### Idolatry concern

*Ve-tenan af bemakom she-amru lehaskhir lo leveit dira amru mipnei she-makhnisin le-tocho avodat gilulim* — the *Mishnah* teaches that even in cases where it is permitted to rent property to non-Jews, it was said not for a *beit dira* — a dwelling — because they will bring their idols into it. If designating a chametz-storage space within a Jewish home operates as a *shkhirut* and thereby renders it the non-Jew's domain, we have effectively created a *beit dira* arrangement — precisely what is prohibited because of the concern about idolatry being brought into what might be understood as one's own home.

So the question presses: if *shkhirut kanya* and the non-Jew has effectively acquired this space within the Jewish house, how can we permit designating such a space?

### Torah exclusion

The resolution is: *shani hacha de-afkei rahmana bilshon lo yimatzei* — it is different here because the Torah expressed the prohibition in the language of *lo yimatzei*. The *pasuk* itself defines the scope of what is forbidden.

### Matsui beyadecha

*Mi she-matsui beyadekha, yatsei zeh she-eino matsui beyadekha* — only that which is readily accessible to you, in your control and at your disposal, falls under the prohibition. That which is not *matsui beyadekha* is excluded. The designated space given to the non-Jew for his *chametz* is, in the relevant sense, not readily accessible to the Jew: he did not accept responsibility for it, he does not exercise dominion over it, and when the non-Jew enters to retrieve it, he enters what has been assigned to the non-Jew's use. Therefore *lo yimatzei* does not apply.

The bottom line, at least according to Rashi, is that the exemption does not rest on the non-Jew having formally *acquired* the space — which would raise the *beit dira* problem — but rather that the Jew has not accepted responsibility for the *chametz*, and consequently it is not *matsui beyado*. The designation of a space signals and effects this non-acceptance of responsibility. The particulars of whether the non-Jew has some right of use short of ownership, or whether the space technically remains in the Jew's domain while still not being *matsui*, are somewhat difficult to work out precisely — but the operative principle is clear: the absence of *achrayut* means the absence of the *matsui beyadcha* relationship that *lo yimatzei* requires.

This ruling has real practical implications. A non-Jew who simply walks into a Jewish home with *chametz* on *Pesach* — without a deposit arrangement and without a designated space — raises no obligation on the Jew's part. One need not order him out or demand he remove his food. And if a space is designated for non-Jewish workers or maintenance staff in a building over *Pesach*, the *chametz* they bring to their designated area is not the Jew's concern under this *issur*. This is what the *Gemara* is saying — though practical *peskening* on such matters belongs to one's own *posek*.

## Covering on Yom Tov

### Muktzeh covering

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: if one finds *chametz* in one's home on *yom tov* — having searched and thought everything was gone, yet opening a cabinet and discovering something — what does one do? *Chametz* on *yom tov* is *muktzeh*: one has no permitted use for it, and one cannot burn it either, since lighting a fire for this purpose is not permitted on *yom tov*. The ruling is *kofeh alav keli* — one covers it with a vessel and deals with it after *yom tov*.

### Hekdesh exception

*Amar Rava: im she-hekdesho eino tzarich* — if one declares the *chametz* to be *hekdesh*, one need not cover it at all. *Mivdal bedili minei* — one knows to stay well away from *hekdesh*. It is off-limits in a serious sense, and one will naturally maintain distance from it without the need for a physical cover to serve as a reminder or barrier.

### Non-Jewish separation

*Ve-amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: non-Jewish *chametz* found in one's home requires a *mechitzah* of ten *tefachim* to separate it — *mishum heker*, for purposes of recognition and distinction. It must be clearly set apart so that it is evident that this *chametz* is not the Jew's. *Ve-shel hekdesh eino tzarich* — *hekdesh chametz*, by contrast, does not require a physical partition, for the same reason as before: *mivdal bedil inish minei* — people know to keep away from *hekdesh* and will not approach it carelessly.

## Timing of bi'ur

### 30-day rule

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: one who sets out on a sea voyage or joins a caravan more than thirty days before *Pesach* — *ein zakuk leva'er*. No obligation to perform *bi'ur* before departing. This operates on the understanding that *min ha-Torah*, *bitul* alone suffices; and if one has already performed *bitul* and is not returning, the need for physical *bi'ur* does not arise. One is not here for *Pesach*, one is not going to eat the *chametz*, and one has already nullified it in one's heart.

Within thirty days of *Pesach*, however — *toch sheloshim yom zakuk leva'er* — one is obligated to perform *bi'ur* before departing.

### Abaye's intent

Abaye qualifies this: even the ruling that within thirty days one is obligated to perform *bi'ur* — *lo amaran ella sheda'ato lachzor* — applies only when one intends to return for *Pesach*. But if one does not intend to return, *ein zakuk leva'er* — even within thirty days one is not obligated. One might wonder whether "return" means returning before *Pesach* ends, and the answer is yes — the question is whether one will be home during *Pesach* itself.

### Rava's principle

Rava sharpens the formulation: if you are going to return, then *afilu meRosh HaShanah nami* — even if you are leaving six months before *Pesach*, you must perform *bi'ur*. The controlling factor is not temporal proximity to *Pesach* but rather intent. *Da'ato lachzor* means you will come back to your *chametz* — and therefore the obligation applies regardless of when you leave. Conversely, the exemption before thirty days applies only *ella she-ein da'ato lachzor* — where one truly has no intention of returning for *Pesach*. Within thirty days, the obligation applies in any case.

### Storehouse case

The same principle appears in a related ruling: *de-amar Rava oseh beito otzar* — if one turns one's house into a storehouse with both *chametz* and other items packed together. *Kodem sheloshim yom ein zakuk leva'er, toch sheloshim yom zakuk leva'er* — outside thirty days, no obligation; within thirty days, obligated. And even before thirty days, the exemption applies only *ella she-ein da'ato lefanoto* — if one has no intention of clearing out the storehouse before *Pesach*. If one does intend to access and clear it, then *afilu kodem sheloshim yom nami zakuk leva'er*. The principle is consistent: intent to engage with the *chametz* over *Pesach* triggers the obligation; genuine separation from it removes it.

### Issur hana'ah

Rashi makes a striking observation. Why is it that if one intends to return, one is obligated even from *Rosh HaShanah*? *Deki hadar bei b'yemei haPesach avar alei* — because when one returns during *Pesach*, one transgresses *bal yera'eh uval yimatzei*. And once *Pesach* has arrived, *sheharei issur hana'ah hu ve-eino shelo* — the *chametz* is *issur hana'ah* and is no longer legally his. One cannot perform *bitul* at that point because the Torah has removed it from one's *reshut* in the relevant sense. And so one will find oneself faced with *chametz* one cannot nullify, and the moment one sees it and does not immediately remove it, one transgresses. Hence the obligation to perform *bi'ur* before leaving — so that one does not return to find *chametz* that one is powerless to address.

## Bitul after bedika…

### Issur hana'ah paradox

This raises what is truly a striking conceptual difficulty. On *Pesach* itself, *chametz* is *issur hana'ah* and, in a meaningful legal sense, is out of one's possession — the Torah removes it from one's *reshut*. Yet one remains liable for *bal yera'eh uval yimatzei* as if it were one's own. The Torah simultaneously removes the *chametz* from one's possession (in the sense that one cannot benefit from it or perform *bitul* over it) and retains it in one's possession (in the sense that one remains *over* on the prohibition). This paradox is essential for understanding the structure of the obligation and will continue to bear on the discussion that follows.

## Timing of bi'ur…

### Lav hanitak la'aseh

Tosafot on a different *daf* offers an intriguing position: *lav hanitak la'aseh*. Even if one keeps *chametz* in one's house for the entire duration of *Pesach* without removing it — even for several days — one does not violate the *lav* as long as one burns it before *Pesach* ends. The reasoning is that the *lav* of possessing *chametz* is *nitak la'aseh*, linked to the positive commandment of *tashbitu*: *ach bayom harishon tashbitu*. Since *tashbitu* is an *aseh* that can redeem the situation, the *lav* does not fully crystallize until the *aseh* becomes impossible to perform — that is, until *Pesach* is over.

One might ask: does this mean that *lav hanitak la'aseh* operates not merely to exempt from *malkut* but to prevent violation of the *lav* altogether? Apparently so, at least according to Tosafot's position. The *lav* has not been transgressed until the window for performing *tashbitu* closes.

If so, one might further ask: given that burning suffices, why must one perform *bitul* before *Pesach*? Tosafot would agree that *bitul* cannot be performed on *Pesach* itself — the Torah removes the *chametz* from one's *reshut* for purposes of *bitul* — but the concern is practical: to burn *chametz* one needs wood and fire, and the process takes time. The *Rabbanan* feared that one would procrastinate, delay gathering what is needed, and ultimately fail to burn the *chametz* before *Pesach* ends altogether. *Bitul* before *Pesach*, therefore, is a safeguard against that failure — ensuring that even if the physical *bi'ur* is not performed, the *chametz* has already been addressed.

## 30-day origin

### Pesach Sheini source

Why thirty days? *Ke-detanya sho'alin ve-dorshin be-hilchot Pesach kodem la-Pesach shloshim yom* — as the *beraita* teaches, one inquires into and expounds the laws of *Pesach* within thirty days of *Pesach*. Within that window, one is already in the ambit of *Pesach* and its concerns. The thirty-day period itself derives from the interval between *Pesach Rishon* and *Pesach Sheini*: Moshe stood at *Pesach Rishon* and warned regarding *Pesach Sheini* — *shene'emar ve-ya'asu venei Yisrael et ha-Pesach*. Those who were *tamei* came to Moshe at *Pesach Rishon* and were then told about the *Pesach Sheini* that would take place a month later.

### Pesach context

The principle is that within thirty days of *Pesach*, a person is already engaged in *Pesach* matters. The halakhic world of *Pesach* has begun to bear on him, and so the obligations associated with *bi'ur* properly take hold from that point.

### Two-week option

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: *shtei shabbatot* — two weeks suffice. The appearance of thirty days in the story of *Pesach Sheini*, he argues, is incidental. The people came to Moshe at *Pesach Rishon* asking about their situation, and since they were already discussing *Pesach*, Moshe addressed all *Pesach*-related matters, including the institution of *Pesach Sheini*. But that is a function of the specific circumstance — the *Pesach Sheini* teaching did not require thirty days of lead time; Moshe could have waited and told them two weeks later, just before *Pesach Sheini*. The thirty-day figure is therefore not normative. For everyone else, two weeks is the appropriate threshold.

Where does Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel get two weeks? *Sheharei Moshe omed be-Rosh Chodesh u-mazhir al ha-Pesach* — because Moshe stood on *Rosh Chodesh Nisan* and warned about *Pesach*, which falls fourteen days later. The *pasuk* in *Parshat Bo*: *ha-chodesh hazeh lachem rosh chodashim*, and then, *dabru el kol adat benei Yisrael lemor be-asor la-chodesh hazeh veyikechu lahem ish seh* — Hashem tells Moshe on *Rosh Chodesh* to instruct the people about the *korban Pesach* that begins on the tenth and is brought on the fourteenth. Two weeks thus anchors the obligation.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *mimmay de-vereish yarcha ka'i* — how do we know that Moshe actually taught the people on *Rosh Chodesh*? Perhaps Hashem spoke to Moshe on *Rosh Chodesh*, but Moshe transmitted the instruction to the people on the fourth or fifth of the month — which would give less than two weeks. *Dilma be-arba'ah be-yarcha o be-chamishah be-yarcha ka'i*? The two-week calculation depends on Moshe telling the people on *Rosh Chodesh* itself.

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak mishmeih de-Ravina*: another *pasuk* is brought. *Vayedabber Hashem el Moshe be-midbar Sinai ba-shanah ha-sheniyah ba-chodesh ha-rishon* — Hashem spoke to Moshe in *Midbar Sinai* in the second year, in the first month, and then *ve-ya'asu venei Yisrael et ha-Pesach bemo'ado*. Here too the question: how do we know this was *Rosh Chodesh*? It says only "in the first month."

The answer comes through *gezeirah shavah*: *atya midbar mimmidbar* — the word *be-midbar* appears here in the context of the *Pesach* instruction, and it also appears at the opening of *Sefer Ba-Midbar*: *vayedabber Hashem el Moshe be-midbar Sinai be-Ohel Moed be-echad la-chodesh ha-sheni* — on the first of the second month, which is *Rosh Chodesh*. Just as the *midbar* statement in *Sefer Bamidbar* was delivered on *Rosh Chodesh*, so too the *midbar* statement regarding *Pesach* was delivered on *Rosh Chodesh* — specifically *Rosh Chodesh Nisan*, two weeks before *Pesach*. That is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's source.

### Chronological order

The *Gemara* then notes that the opening of *Sefer Ba-Midbar* — which took place in the second month — appears *before* the *Pesach* passage — which took place in the first month. Why is the Torah presenting events in the second month before events in the first month? *Amar Rav Menashia bar Tachlipa mishmeih de-Rav*: *zot omeret ein mukdam u-meuchar ba-Torah* — from here we learn that the Torah is not bound by chronological order.

### Single topic order

Rav Pappa immediately qualifies: *lo amran ela be-trei inyanei* — we say this only when two separate *inyanim* are involved. The beginning of *Bamidbar* concerns the census of the Jewish people; the *Pesach* passage concerns the *korban Pesach*. When two distinct topics are in play, the Torah may present them in any order. *Aval be-chad inyana mai de-mukdam mukdam ve-mai de-meuchar meuchar* — but within a single *inyan*, what comes first is first and what comes later is later. There is chronological integrity within a unified topic.

### Hermeneutics

And if one does not accept this limitation, the entire system of hermeneutical derivation would collapse. *Ve-ee lo tema hachi, klal u-frat ein bi-chlal ela mah she-bi-frat* — consider: the rule of *klal u-frat* depends on identifying which phrase is the *klal* and which is the *prat*, and this identification rests on order of appearance. The *klal* comes first, the *prat* second, and the *klal* is then understood to be limited to what the *prat* specifies. But if there is no chronological integrity within an *inyan*, then for all we know the *prat* came first and the *klal* came second — in which case the rule of *prat u-chlal* applies instead, and the *klal* is understood as *expanding* beyond the *prat* rather than being limited by it. The entire basis for choosing between these hermeneutical rules would evaporate. *Dilma prat u-chlal hu*?

The same problem applies in reverse: *ve-tu prat u-chlal na'asa klal mosif al ha-prat* — a *prat* followed by a *klal* means the *klal* adds onto the *prat*. But without order, how do you know which is which?

The *Gemara* then presses: could one not say the same thing even about *trei inyanei* — two separate topics? If we invoke *ein mukdam u-meuchar* for two separate topics, why doesn't that also disrupt the *klal u-frat* rules that appear across different passages? The answer: *hanicha leman de-amar klal u-frat meruchakin zeh mi-zeh ein danin oto be-klal u-frat* — according to the view that a *klal* and *prat* widely separated from one another do not generate *klal u-frat* derivation at all, the question does not arise. *Ela le-man de-amar danin* — but according to the view that even widely separated *klal* and *prat* do generate the derivation, the response is: *afilu le-man de-amar danin, hanei milei be-chad inyana, aval be-trei inyanei ein danin* — even that view limits the derivation to a single *inyan*. Across two separate topics, *klal u-frat* simply does not operate regardless.

What runs beneath this entire discussion is a striking intellectual honesty about the nature of textual interpretation. The *Gemara* is implicitly acknowledging that the Torah's text could in principle be read in many ways — that the assignment of *klal* and *prat*, the determination of chronological order, the identification of which passage governs which — all of these could theoretically go differently. The *Rabbanan* know the text admits multiple readings, and they are here establishing the rules of engagement: within a single *inyan*, order is binding. Across *inyanim*, it is not. This is not presented as though the rules derive self-evidently from the text — it is presented as a set of disciplined hermeneutical commitments, and the intellectual tension underneath that commitment is very much alive in the *sugya*.

## Bitul after bedika…

### Bitul necessity

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: *ha-bodek tzarich she-yevatel* — one who searches for *chametz* must nonetheless still perform *bitul*. Having done a thorough *bedika*, one might think the *bitul* is superfluous — after all, one has found and removed everything. Why is *bitul* still required?

The first suggested answer is: perhaps there are still crumbs (*perurin*) remaining. But this is immediately rejected — *ha ninhu lo chashivi*, crumbs are *batel* and do not constitute a violation of *bal yera'eh*. A few crumbs are simply not significant enough to trigger the prohibition. Nor does the general significance of one's house impart significance to the crumbs — *keivan de-minter lehu agav beiteih chashivi* is proposed and then set aside. The *beraita* demonstrates: when a field-owner watches his land during one harvest season while the previous season's remnants sit untended, those remnants are not considered significant. If the owner is *makpid* on them, they become significant; if not, they are *hefker* and *patur* from *ma'aser*. Since one certainly does not care about a few crumbs after a thorough *bedika*, they achieve no significance.

### Gluska yafa concern

*Amar Rava*: the true reason one who performs *bedika* still requires *bitul* is a *gezeirah* — a rabbinic precaution. *Gezeirah shema yimtza gluska yafa* — lest one find a beautiful, choice roll (*gluska yafa*) that one did not discover during the search. If there is no prior *bitul*, one's attention becomes arrested by this discovery — *da'ateih ilaveih* — one's mind is drawn to it. And then? One might hesitate to destroy it.

The further difficulty is that if one finds this *gluska yafa* on *Pesach* itself, one cannot simply perform *bitul* at that point: *dilma mashkachat lah batar issura* — perhaps one discovers it after the *issur* has taken effect. And *lav birshuteih kayma, de-lo matzi levatlei* — it is no longer in one's legal *reshut* in the sense required for *bitul*; one cannot nullify it.

### Rav Elazar

*Amar Rav Elazar*: there are two things not in a person's *reshut*, yet the Torah treats them as if they were: *bor birshut ha-rabim* — a pit dug in the public domain, and *chametz mi-shesh sha'ot ul-maalah* — *chametz* from the sixth hour onward. In both cases, the person does not own the relevant domain or object in the ordinary sense, yet the Torah holds them liable as owners. Regarding *chametz*: once the sixth hour arrives and the *issur* of *chametz* takes effect, the *chametz* is *issur hana'ah* and has in a meaningful sense left one's possession — yet *ve-chaschan ha-katuv k'ilu birshutu*, the Torah treats it as if it remained in one's *reshut*, and one transgresses *bal yera'eh uval yimatzei*. One cannot perform *bitul* at this stage, and one is liable for what one cannot legally exercise dominion over. This is why the *bitul* must be performed *before* that threshold is crossed.

## Bitul mechanics

### Rashi's ke'afra

How precisely does *bitul chametz* work? Rashi's formulation of the *bitul* text is instructive: *ha-bodek tzarich she-yevatel be-libo samuch la-bedika miyad*, and what one says is *kol chamira de-ika be-veita hadein livatel*. Notably, Rashi makes no mention of *hefker*. He says *livatel* — that it be nullified. And the language associated with *bitul* is *ke-afra de-ara* — like the dust of the earth. This is not a declaration of ownerlessness; it is a declaration of insignificance, of non-existence as an object of value.

### Tosafot hefker

Tosafot, on the *mishnah*, reads *bitul* as an act of *hefker* — rendering the *chametz* ownerless. If it is ownerless, it is no longer one's own, and the prohibition of *bal yera'eh* cannot apply. The mechanism, on this view, is divestiture of ownership.

### Ran's objection

The Ran objects strongly to this position. His critique appears at the opening of his commentary on the first *daf*. First, the textual objection: the formulation of *bitul* is *ke-afra de-ara*, with no mention of *hefker* — the language does not support reading *bitul* as *hefker*. Second, and more fundamentally, *hefker* is ineffective in this context. *Hefker* means that one has relinquished ownership and anyone may now acquire the object. But ownership under *hefker* passes fully to another party only when that party takes possession. Until someone else acquires the *chametz*, it remains, in a relevant sense, the original owner's. And on *Pesach*, no one is permitted to acquire *chametz* — it is *issur hana'ah* for everyone — meaning no one will ever take it. If *hefker* is only fully effective upon acquisition by another, and no acquisition can occur, the *hefker* of *chametz* on *Pesach* is a legal nullity. Furthermore, *bitul* is performed *be-libo*, in one's heart — and *devarim she-ba-lev einan devarim*, unexpressed mental intentions have no legal force. *Hefker* performed purely mentally, without verbal declaration, would be ineffective on those grounds as well.

### Ran's alternative

The Ran's own understanding of *bitul* is more subtle. When *Pesach* arrives, as established, the Torah's law of *issur hana'ah* effectively displaces the *chametz* from one's *reshut* — it is removed from one's possession in the sense that one can derive no benefit from it. Yet the Torah simultaneously holds one liable for *bal yera'eh uval yimatzei* as if it were fully in one's possession. The Torah, in effect, *returns* the *chametz* to one's possession for purposes of this prohibition. What *bitul* accomplishes is not making the *chametz* ownerless but rather *preventing it from returning to one's possession* through the Torah's mechanism. By performing *bitul* — by mentally treating the *chametz* as destroyed, as non-existent, as mere dust — one preempts the Torah's act of re-attribution. Since *bitul* does not need to be effective as an act of acquisition or transfer to another party, it can operate purely *be-libo*. One is not divesting ownership; one is foreclosing the re-establishment of a connection that would otherwise be imposed by the Torah's own framework.

### Both positions

Given the range of positions — Rashi and the Ran understanding *bitul* as mental negation or destruction rather than *hefker*, and Tosafot understanding it as *hefker* — the practical formulation adopted is inclusive: both *yibatil* and *lihyot hefker ke-afra de-ara*. By including both elements — the declaration of nullification and the language of *hefker* cast as dust-like insignificance — one is covered according to all the major views. If *bitul* works as *hefker*, the declaration accomplishes that. If it works as mental destruction and non-attribution, the *ke-afra de-ara* language accomplishes that. The formulation is thus structured to satisfy the requirements of the full range of *rishonim* on the question.
