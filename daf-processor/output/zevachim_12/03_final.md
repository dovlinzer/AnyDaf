# Zevachim 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pesach Morning 14th

### Mishna Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שַׁחֲרִית בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ – רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מַכְשִׁיר, כְּאִילּוּ נִשְׁחַט בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר. בֶּן בְּתִירָא פּוֹסֵל, כְּאִילּוּ נִשְׁחַט בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the Paschal offering that one slaughtered** on the **morning of** the **fourteenth** of Nisan, the day when the Paschal offering should be slaughtered in the afternoon, if he did so **not for its sake, Rabbi Yehoshua deems it fit as though it were slaughtered on the thirteenth** of Nisan. An animal consecrated as a Paschal offering that was slaughtered not at its designated time for the sake of a different offering is fit for sacrifice as a peace offering. **Ben Beteira deems it unfit as though it were slaughtered** in the **afternoon** of the fourteenth.

The Mishna on 11b turns from the general laws of *shelo lishmo* to zoom in on a specific and puzzling case involving the *korban pesach*. The *pesach* has a designated time — *bein ha'arbayim*, generally understood as the afternoon beginning from *chatzot* — and if slaughtered *shelo lishmo* during that window, it is *pasul*. But what happens if the *pesach* is slaughtered in the morning of the 14th of Nisan, *shelo lishmo*?

The background rule is clear: a *pesach* slaughtered when it is *eino zmano* — not its time — and *shelo lishmo* is *kasher* as a *shlamim*. The question is whether the morning of the 14th counts as *zmano* or as *eino zmano*. If it is *eino zmano*, then *shelo lishmo* should render it *kasher* as a *shlamim*. If it is considered *zmano*, then *shelo lishmo* would *pasul* it, just as the Mishna already taught regarding a *pesach bizmano shelo lishmo*.

### Dispute Setup

The Mishna records a dispute between Rabbi Yehoshua and Ben Beteira. Rabbi Yehoshua rules *kasher* — *ke'ilu nishchat bishlosha-asar*, as if it had been slaughtered on the 13th. In his view, the morning of the 14th is *eino zmano* for the *pesach*, and since it was slaughtered *shelo lishmo* outside its time, it is valid as a *shlamim*. Ben Beteira rules *pasul* — *ke'ilu nishchat bein ha'arbayim*, as if it had been slaughtered in the afternoon. In his view, the afternoon of the 14th is close enough — or determinative enough — that the morning already carries the status of *zmano*, and therefore a *pesach* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* during this time is *pasul*.

A striking consequence of Ben Beteira's position, as was noted during the discussion, is that on the 14th in the morning, the *pesach* is invalid no matter what one does: *lishmo* it is *pasul* because the true *zman* has not yet arrived (*pesach kodem zmano*), and *shelo lishmo* it is *pasul* because the day is already considered *bizmano* and a *pesach bizmano shelo lishmo* is *pasul*. The morning of the 14th is, in effect, a complete blackout period.

### Ben Beteira Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מַכְשִׁיר הָיָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא בְּפֶסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שַׁחֲרִית בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר לִשְׁמוֹ, דְּכוּלֵּיהּ יוֹמָא זִימְנֵיהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Oshaya says: With regard to a Paschal offering that one slaughtered** on the **morning of** the **fourteenth** of Nisan **for its sake, ben Beteira would deem** it **fit, as** he held that **the entire day,** not only the afternoon, **is its** designated **time.**

The Gemara then cites Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya, who offers a more radical reading of Ben Beteira's position: *machshir hayah Ben Beteira b'pesach sheshchato shacharit bearba'asar lishmo* — Ben Beteira actually holds that a *pesach* slaughtered in the morning of the 14th *lishmo* is *kasher*. In other words, Ben Beteira does not merely say that the afternoon *zman* is close enough to color the morning; he genuinely believes that the *zman* of the *korban pesach* begins in the morning. The entire day of the 14th is *zmano*. That is why *shelo lishmo* is *pasul* — not because the afternoon is approaching, but because the morning itself is a valid time. His phrase *ke'ilu nishchat bein ha'arbayim* was formulated in response to Rabbi Yehoshua's phrasing (*ke'ilu nishchat bishlosha-asar*), matching the structure of the debate — but the underlying position is that the morning is itself fully valid.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַדְּמִיפַּלְגִי בְּשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ – לִיפַּלְגוּ בְּלִשְׁמוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, rather than disagreeing** in the mishna **with regard to** a Paschal offering that was slaughtered on the morning of the fourteenth **not for its sake, let them disagree with regard to** one slaughtered **for its sake;** ben Beteira holds that it is fit, and Rabbi Yehoshua holds that it is unfit.

The Gemara immediately presses this reading: *ei demapligi be'shelo lishmah liflagi be'lishmah* — if this is the real dispute, why are they arguing about the *shelo lishmo* case, which is derivative? They should be debating the more fundamental question: can a *korban pesach* be brought *lishmo* in the morning?

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אִיפְּלִגוּ בְּלִשְׁמוֹ, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֲבָל בְּשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ – מוֹדֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְבֶן בְּתִירָא, הוֹאִיל וּמִקְצָתוֹ רָאוּי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If** the mishna were to write only that **they disagree with regard to** a Paschal offering slaughtered **for its sake, I would say: But in** a case where it was slaughtered **not for its sake, Rabbi Yehoshua concedes to ben Beteira** that it is disqualified, even though it was slaughtered in the morning, **since** at least **part of** the day, the afternoon, is **fit** for its sacrifice as a Paschal offering. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that they disagree with regard to this case as well.

The Gemara answers: had they debated *lishmo* directly, one might have concluded that even Rabbi Yehoshua — who holds the *pesach* is only valid in the afternoon — would nonetheless agree that *shelo lishmo* in the morning is *pasul*, on the grounds that *ho'il u'miktzat ra'uy*, since part of the day is its *zman*. The reasoning: even if the morning is not itself the true *zman*, perhaps the fact that the afternoon will be *zmano* is enough to make the whole day *bizmano* for purposes of *shelo lishmo*. By formulating their dispute specifically over *shelo lishmo*, the Mishna teaches us that Rabbi Yehoshua holds two things: first, the *pesach* itself is only valid in the afternoon; and second, even though the afternoon *zman* is coming later that day, the morning is still sufficiently *eino zmano* that *shelo lishmo* there is *kasher* as a *shlamim*.

## Bein Arbayim

### Bein Arbayim Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״! אָמַר עוּלָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב עִילַּאי: בֵּין שְׁנֵי עֲרָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can ben Beteira claim that a Paschal offering may be slaughtered in the morning? **But isn't it written:** "And the whole assembly of the congregation of Israel shall slaughter it **in the afternoon [*bein ha'arbayim*]"** (Exodus 12:6)? **Ulla, son of Rav Ilai, says:** Ben Beteira interprets the phrase *bein ha'arbayim* literally: **Between two evenings,** i.e., between the eve of the fourteenth and the night after the fourteenth. Accordingly, the Paschal offering may be sacrificed all day long.

The Gemara now interrogates the premise that Ben Beteira holds the entire day to be *zmano*. Doesn't the Torah say the *pesach* is brought *bein ha'arbayim* — between the evenings — which is universally understood to mean the afternoon? The Gemara raises an alternative reading offered in the name of the son of Rabbi Ilai: *bein shnei arvayim* — between two different "evenings." Sunrise, understood as the end of the prior night, is the first *erev*; sunset is the second. Between these two lies the entire day. On this reading, *bein ha'arbayim* means the whole day.

### Tamid & Menorah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּמִיד, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״ – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּכוּלֵּי יוֹמָא כָּשֵׁר?! הָתָם, מִדִּכְתִיב: ״אֶת הַכֶּבֶשׂ אֶחָד תַּעֲשֶׂה בַבֹּקֶר״, מִכְּלָל דְּ״בֵין הָעַרְבָּיִם״ – בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, with regard to the **daily offering, concerning which it is written:** "The one lamb you shall offer in the morning, and the other lamb you shall offer ***bein ha'arbayim*"** (Numbers 28:4), is **the entire day fit** for sacrifice **also?** The Gemara answers: **There, since it is written: "The one lamb you shall offer in the morning," by inference,** the term ***bein ha'arbayim*** is referring to the **actual afternoon.**

The Gemara challenges this extended reading with a series of parallels. The *korban tamid* is also described as brought *bein ha'arbayim* — does that mean it could be offered at any point during the day? Clearly not. The Gemara answers that for the *tamid*, the Torah itself provides the corrective: *es hakeves echad ta'aseh baboker* — one lamb in the morning. The explicit morning-and-afternoon pairing of the two *tamid* offerings establishes that *bein ha'arbayim* for the afternoon *tamid* means the afternoon specifically, not the entire day. Even if one would otherwise read *bein ha'arbayim* expansively, the verse forces the narrow reading here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵרוֹת, דִּכְתִיב ״בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם״ – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּכוּלֵּי יוֹמָא כָּשֵׁר?! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵעֶרֶב וְעַד בֹּקֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the **lamps** of the Candelabrum, one should say the same, **as it is written:** "And when Aaron lights the lamps ***bein ha'arbayim*"** (Exodus 30:8). Is **the entire day fit** for lighting **also,** according to the opinion of ben Beteira? The Gemara answers: **There it is different, as it is written:** "To burn **from evening to morning"** (Exodus 27:21).

The Gemara similarly asks about the *menorah*: it too is described as lit *bein ha'arbayim* — does that mean it could be lit anytime during the day? No, because the Torah says *me-erev ad boker*, from evening until morning — which establishes that the lighting is specifically in the late afternoon or evening, not throughout the day.

### Ketores Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתַנְיָא: ״מֵעֶרֶב וְעַד בֹּקֶר״ – תֵּן לָהּ מִדָּתָהּ שֶׁתְּהֵא דּוֹלֶקֶת וְהוֹלֶכֶת מֵעֶרֶב וְעַד בֹּקֶר. דָּבָר אַחֵר: אֵין לְךָ עֲבוֹדָה כְּשֵׁרָה מֵעֶרֶב וְעַד בֹּקֶר, אֶלָּא זוֹ בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: The phrase **"from evening to morning"** indicates that you must **allocate** the Candelabrum **its measure** of oil so **that it will burn from evening until morning. Alternatively,** the same verse can be interpreted as follows: **Only this rite is valid from evening until morning,** since all other rites must be performed during the day. Therefore, the Candelabrum is lit only at the end of the day.

A related *braita* is cited: *me-erev ad boker, tein lah midasah shetehei dolekes ve-holeches me-erev ad boker* — the lamp must be given enough oil to burn through the night. The *braita* also adds: this is the only *avodah* in the *Beis Hamikdash* that begins during the day and continues through the night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְטוֹרֶת, דִּכְתִיב בָּהּ: ״בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״ – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּכוּלֵּי יוֹמָא כָּשֵׁר?! שָׁאנֵי קְטֹרֶת, דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ לְנֵרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the **incense,** one should say likewise, **as it is written with regard to it: "*Bein ha'arbayim*** he shall burn it" (Exodus 30:8). Is **the entire day also fit** for burning the incense according to the opinion of ben Beteira? The Gemara answers: The **incense is different,** **as it is juxtaposed with** the lighting of the **lamps** (see Exodus 30:8). Evidently, it is burned in the afternoon as well.

Turning to the *ketores* — also described as burned *bein ha'arbayim* — the Gemara similarly asks whether that could mean any time of day. No, because the Torah links it explicitly to the lighting of the *menorah* by Aharon: *u'vha'alos Aharon es haneros bein ha'arbayim yaktirenah* — when Aharon lights the lamps. Since the lighting of the *menorah* has been established to be in the afternoon, the *ketores* is similarly fixed to that time.

### Pesach Erev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״שָׁם תִּזְבַּח אֶת הַפֶּסַח בָּעָרֶב״! הָהוּא לִ״יאוּחַר דָּבָר״ הוּא דַּאֲתָא, דְּתַנְיָא: יְאוּחַר דָּבָר שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ ״בָּעָרֶב״ וּ״בֵין הָעַרְבָּיִם״, לְדָבָר שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ אֶלָּא ״בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״ בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Just as it is written explicitly that the Candelabrum must be lit in the evening, **there too,** with regard to a Paschal offering, **it is written: "There you shall sacrifice the Passover offering at evening"** (Deuteronomy 16:6). How can ben Beteira claim that it may be sacrificed all day long? The Gemara answers: **That** verse **comes to** teach a different *halakha*, that a certain **item should be** sacrificed **after** another item. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the order of precedence between the Paschal offering and the daily afternoon offering: **An item,** i.e., the Paschal offering, **with regard to which** both the expressions: **"In the evening," and: "*Bein ha'arbayim*,"** are **stated, should be** sacrificed **after an item,** the daily afternoon offering, **with regard to which only "*bein ha'arbayim*" is stated.**

Returning to the *pesach*, the Gemara notes that by *pesach* it also says *ba'erev*, in the evening — which should likewise restrict the *pesach* to the afternoon rather than the entire day. But the Gemara responds: *hahu l'ichar davar hu d'asa* — that word *erev* by *pesach* serves a different purpose: it teaches that the *pesach* must be the last sacrifice brought in the afternoon.

### Ordering: After Tamid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּאִילּוּ שָׁחֵיט לֵיהּ מִצַּפְרָא – אָמְרַתְּ זִימְנֵיהּ הוּא, וְכִי מָטֵי בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם – אָמְרַתְּ ״יְאוּחַר דָּבָר״? אִין; דְּהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה – מִתְפַּלֵּל שֶׁל מִנְחָה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מִתְפַּלֵּל שֶׁל מוּסָפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is there anything** comparable to ben Beteira's suggestion, **that if one slaughters it in the morning, you say** that **it is its** designated **time** and it is fit; **but when afternoon comes, you say** that the **item should be** sacrificed only **after** the daily afternoon offering? The Gemara answers: **Yes,** there is a precedent; **as Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one did not recite the additional prayers of Shabbat, the New Moon, or a Festival, which should be recited in the morning, until it is time for the afternoon prayer, the ***halakha*** is that he should first **recite the afternoon prayer, and recite the additional prayers thereafter.**

This produces an initially striking result: if one holds that the *pesach* is valid in the morning, then it could be brought anytime during the morning. But once the afternoon arrives, it must come *after* the *tamid*. The Gemara validates this asymmetry by analogy to Rabbi Yochanan's ruling on *tefillah*: if one forgot to daven *musaf* and realizes this in the afternoon, one should daven *mincha* first and then *musaf* — because *tadir v'she'eino tadir, tadir kodem*, the more frequent takes precedence. Even though *musaf* would normally precede *mincha* in terms of the day's sequence, in the afternoon the more frequent *mincha* goes first. Similarly, the *pesach* which in the morning can be brought at any point, must in the afternoon defer to the *tamid* which is *tadir*.

## Gemara Reinterpretation

### Rabbi's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד הֵשִׁיב רַבִּי – תַּחַת בֶּן בְּתִירָא – לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לֹא; אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר – שֶׁאֵין מִקְצָתוֹ רָאוּי, תֹּאמַר בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר – שֶׁמִּקְצָתוֹ רָאוּי?!
>
> **Translation:** **Furthermore,** this interpretation of the opinion of ben Beteira is difficult, as **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **responded on behalf of ben Beteira to the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua** that a Paschal offering slaughtered not for its sake on the morning of the fourteenth of Nisan is fit, as though it were slaughtered on the thirteenth: **No, if you said** that this is the *halakha* **with regard to** the **thirteenth, as no part of** the day is **fit** for sacrificing the Paschal offering, **shall you** also **say** that this is the *halakha* **with regard to** the **fourteenth, part of which** is **fit** for sacrificing the Paschal offering? Since it can be sacrificed in the afternoon of the fourteenth, if it is slaughtered in the morning not for its sake, it is disqualified.

Having developed all of the above, the Gemara introduces a *tosefta* in which *Rabbi* — standing in for Ben Beteira, as the Gemara puts it, *heishiv Rabbi tachas Ben Beteira l'divrei Rabbi Yehoshua* — challenges Rabbi Yehoshua's position. Rabbi argues: *im amarta bishloshah asar* — on the 13th, no part of the day is a valid time for the *pesach*, so one might accept that *shelo lishmo* there is *kasher*. *Tomar b'arba'ah asar* — but on the 14th, *shemiktzaso ra'uy*, part of the day — the afternoon — is indeed the *zman* of the *pesach*. Therefore, even the morning should be considered *bizmano* enough to *pasul* a *pesach* slaughtered *shelo lishmo*.

### Everyone Agrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פּוֹסֵל הָיָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא בְּפֶסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר שַׁחֲרִית, בֵּין לִשְׁמוֹ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, הוֹאִיל וּמִקְצָתוֹ רָאוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Oshaya's interpretation of ben Beteira's opinion is rejected. Instead, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: With regard to a Paschal offering that one slaughtered on the fourteenth in the morning, ben Beteira would deem** it **unfit, whether** it was slaughtered **for its sake or not for its sake.** It is unfit even if it was slaughtered not for its sake, **since part of** the day is **fit** for its sacrifice as a Paschal offering.

The Gemara therefore revises its understanding of the dispute entirely. Rabbi Yochanan states: *posel hayah Ben Beteira b'pesach sheshchato b'arba'ah asar shachris bein lishmo bein shelo lishmo* — Ben Beteira invalidates the *pesach* slaughtered on the morning of the 14th regardless of intent. Everyone agrees — both Rabbi Yehoshua and Ben Beteira — that the *pesach* is only truly valid *lishmo* in the afternoon. The question is never whether the morning is an independent valid time for the *pesach*.

### Shelo Lishmo Debate

The real and only dispute is: does *shelo lishmo* in the morning count as *bizmano* because the afternoon *zman* is coming later that day? Ben Beteira says yes — the halachic unit of time is the day, not the hour. Since the 14th is the *zman* of the *pesach*, the entire day carries that status, and a *pesach* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* anywhere on the 14th is *pasul bizmano*. Rabbi Yehoshua says no — the morning is genuinely *eino zmano*, and even though the afternoon *zman* is approaching, that is insufficient to make the morning *bizmano* for purposes of invalidating a *shelo lishmo* slaughter.

## Dichui Concept

### Abahu's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְגַדֵּף בַּהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אִם כֵּן, פֶּסַח כָּשֵׁר לְבֶן בְּתִירָא – הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? אִי דְּאַפְרְשֵׁיהּ הָאִידָּנָא – דָּחוּי מֵעִיקָּרוֹ הוּא, וְאִי דְּאַפְרְשִׁינְהוּ מֵאֶתְמוֹל – נִרְאֶה וְנִדְחֶה הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu ridiculed [*megaddef* ]** this interpretation: **If so, how can you find a fit Paschal offering according to ben Beteira? If** its owner **designated** an animal **now,** on the morning of the fourteenth, as a Paschal offering, **it is disqualified from the outset,** as it cannot be sacrificed at all, either for its sake or not for its sake. **And** even **if** its owner **designated it the previous day,** when it was fit to be sacrificed not for its sake, **it was fit and** then **disqualified** the next morning, when it was no longer fit to be sacrificed until the afternoon. Once an offering is disqualified, it can no longer become fit.

Rabbi Abbahu now raises a sharp objection. Given Ben Beteira's position, how can there ever be a *kasher korban pesach* at all? The morning of the 14th is entirely invalid in both directions — *lishmo* it is *pasul* because the true *zman* has not arrived, and *shelo lishmo* it is *pasul* because it is already considered *bizmano*. This makes the morning a complete blackout. Any *korban pesach* that passes through this blackout period should be considered *dachui* — pushed off and permanently stigmatized.

### Dichui Mei'ikara

Rabbi Abbahu elaborates: *ha de-afrishei ba-tzafra, dachui mei-ikara hu* — if you sanctified the animal during the morning of the 14th, it was *dachui mei-ikara*, never valid from the moment of sanctification, since at that moment it could neither be brought *lishmo* nor *shelo lishmo*. And *ha de-afrishinhu me-etmol, nira ve-nidcha* — if you sanctified it on the 13th, that is even worse, because *nira ve-nidcha* — something that was once fit and then became unfit — is subject to an even stronger form of rejection. A candidate who was hired and then fired is in a worse position than one who never got the job. Either way, Ben Beteira seems to leave no path to a *kasher pesach*. Rabbi Abbahu therefore concludes: *tehei l-achar chatzot* — according to Ben Beteira, the only way to have a valid *korban pesach* is to sanctify it after *chatzot*, once the afternoon has already begun and the *pesach* can immediately be brought.

### Abaye's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא מִצַּפְרָא – אֵין מְחוּסַּר זְמַן לְבוֹ בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: You** may **even say** that it is fit if designated **in the morning,** as an offering is **not** disqualified due to the fact that it is an offering **whose time has not yet** arrived if its time will arrive **on that same day.**

Abaye rejects this constraint: *afilu teima mi-tzafra* — even if sanctified in the morning, it is fine. The principle is *ein mechusar zman l-vo bayom* — something that can be offered later today is not considered to be lacking in time. We do not measure halachic validity by the current moment; we measure in whole-day units. The same logic that makes Ben Beteira say the morning of the 14th is *bizmano* — because the *pesach* can be brought later today — equally prevents us from calling the morning a *dichui* period. Since the animal can be brought later today, it is not halachically entering an unfit time when it passes through the morning.

### Rav Papa's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא מֵאוּרְתָּא – לַיְלָה אֵין מְחוּסָּר זְמַן; דְּתָנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: לֵיל שְׁמִינִי נִכְנָס לַדִּיר לְהִתְעַשֵּׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: You** may **even say** that it is fit if designated **from** the previous **night,** as an offering that was designated at **night** and may be sacrificed the following day is **not** considered an offering **whose time has not yet** arrived, **as it is taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael:** Although an animal is unfit for sacrifice until its eighth day, on the **night** before its **eighth** day it may already **enter the den to be tithed.**

Rav Papa goes further: *afilu teima mei-orta* — one could even sanctify the *pesach* the night before and it would not be *nidcha*. The principle is *layla ein mechusar zman* — the nighttime is not considered a period in which the *korban* is lacking its time. Night and day together constitute a single halachic unit, and since the *pesach* can be brought the following afternoon, the night before is already considered part of its time. This is not unlike any other *korban* that is sanctified at night — one never says such an animal is *nidcha* simply because *korbanot* are not brought at night; since it will be brought in the morning, the sanctification is entirely valid.

## Mechusar Zman

### Eighth Day Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרַבִּי אַפְטוֹרִיקִי, דְּרַבִּי אַפְטוֹרִיקִי רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״וְהָיָה שִׁבְעַת יָמִים תַּחַת אִמּוֹ״ – הָא לַיְלָה חֲזֵי; וּכְתִיב ״וּמִיּוֹם הַשְּׁמִינִי וָהָלְאָה יֵרָצֶה״ – הָא לַיְלָה לָא חֲזֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Appetoriki, as Rabbi Appetoriki raises a contradiction: It is written:** "When a bull or a sheep or a goat is born, **then it shall be seven days with its mother"** (Leviticus 22:27); **consequently,** on the following **night,** i.e., the night before the eighth day, **it is fit** for sacrifice. **And it is written** subsequently in that verse: **"But from the eighth day and on it may be accepted** for an offering made by fire unto the Lord"; **consequently,** on the previous **night,** i.e., the night before the eighth day, **it is not** yet **fit.**

The Gemara now points out that the phrase *mechusar zman* has a more specific technical meaning beyond "can be done later today." It refers to an animal that has not yet reached its eighth day — the minimum age for a *korban*. An animal born seven days ago cannot yet be brought as a *korban*, even though tomorrow morning it will be eligible.

### Maaser Behema

The application of *lailah ein mechusar zman* in this more technical sense comes from a *braita* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *leil shemini nichnas ledir lehit'aser* — on the night of the eighth day, such an animal may enter the pen to be counted for *maaser behema*. When performing *maaser behema*, one counts the animals one by one and designates every tenth; this is done only with animals old enough to be brought as *korbanot*, i.e., at least eight days old. Yet the *halacha* permits including such an animal on the night of the eighth day, even though it is not yet morning and cannot actually be brought as a *korban* until then. Since in the morning it will be eight days old and fit, the nighttime of the eighth day is already treated as if it is fit.

### Lailah Ain Mechusar

This is the source for the principle that night is not considered *mechusar zman* — the night does not render an animal lacking in time when that time will arrive in the morning. We count in whole-day units, and the eighth day begins at nightfall.

### Rabbi Aptorikí

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? לַיְלָה לִקְדוּשָּׁה, וְיוֹם לְהַרְצָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? At **night** it is fit **for consecration, and** the following **day** it is fit **for effecting acceptance,** i.e., for sacrifice. Apparently, an offering that may be sacrificed the following day can be consecrated the previous night, even though it may not be sacrificed at night, and it is not considered an offering whose time has not yet arrived.

Rabbi Aptorikí notes an apparent contradiction. One verse says *vehaya shivas yamim tachas imo* — the animal must be seven days with its mother. Once seven days have passed, the night of the eighth arrives and the animal seems immediately eligible. But another verse says *miyom hashemini vahalah yeratze* — only from the eighth day onward is it fit to be brought. These two verses seem to conflict: the night of the eighth is after seven days have passed, yet it is not yet the eighth day itself.

The resolution: *laila lekeddusha, yom lehertza'ah* — the night of the eighth is fit for sanctification, while the actual bringing of the *korban* takes place only during the day. An animal sanctified on the night of the eighth is not *nidcha* and not considered to have been sanctified prematurely — it has reached the threshold of eligibility for sanctification even though the day has not yet begun. But it may only be physically offered once the day arrives. This framework — night for sanctification, day for offering — maintains internal consistency: we measure fitness for *keddusha* in whole-day units, while the actual *avodah* is restricted to daytime.

## Live Animals Dichui

### Zeira's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לֵימָא קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים נִדְחִין?!
>
> **Translation:** § Rabbi Abbahu presumes that an animal consecrated before it may be sacrificed is disqualified. With regard to this, **Rabbi Zeira said to Rabbi Abbahu: Shall we say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan holds** that consecrated **animals** that cannot be sacrificed at any given moment **are disqualified** from ever being sacrificed?

Rabbi Zeira raises a pointed question to Rabbi Abba: *leima kasvar Rabbi Yochanan baalei chayim nidchin?* The entire analysis just conducted concerning the *pesach* passing through an invalid period presupposed that it matters whether the living animal is considered *nidcha* or not. The very fact that we had to find a reason why such an animal is *not* pushed off implies that in principle live animals *can* be pushed off. Does Rabbi Yochanan hold that *baalei chayim nidchin* — that living animals are subject to *dichui*?

This question is theoretically significant. There is reason to think that a living being, precisely because it is dynamic and not static, cannot be permanently stigmatized the way an already-slaughtered sacrifice can. A living creature has, so to speak, the capacity for renewal. But the analysis of the *pesach* case seemed to take seriously the possibility that a live animal could become *nidcha*.

### Partnership Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין; דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין – הִקְדִּישׁ חֶצְיָהּ, וְחָזַר וְלָקַח חֶצְיָהּ וְהִקְדִּישָׁהּ – קְדוֹשָׁה, וְאֵינָהּ קְרֵיבָה. וְעוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה, וּתְמוּרָתָהּ כְּיוֹצֵא בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu **said to him: Yes, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **an animal belonging to two partners,** if one of them **consecrated** the **half of it** that belongs to him, **and then purchased** the other **half of it** from his partner **and consecrated it, it is consecrated,** despite the fact that it was consecrated piecemeal. **But it may not be sacrificed,** since when he first consecrated it, the consecration did not extend to his partner's half. Since the animal was not fit to be sacrificed then, it is disqualified permanently. **But** as it is consecrated now, it can **render** consecrated as **a substitute** a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for it. If the owner substitutes a non-sacred animal for this one, the second animal becomes consecrated as well. **And** yet **its substitute is like it;** it too is consecrated but cannot be sacrificed.

Rabbi Abba confirms that yes, Rabbi Yochanan holds *baalei chayim nidchin*, and cites a case Rabbi Yochanan himself discussed. Two partners own an animal together. One partner sanctifies his half. The *keddusha* does not spread throughout the animal because the other half belongs to someone else, so the animal is half-sanctified and cannot be brought as a *korban*. The partner then purchases the other half from his co-owner and sanctifies it as well. The animal is now fully sanctified. Nonetheless, says Rabbi Yochanan, the animal is *kedusha ve-eina kereiva* — it is holy but cannot be offered. Why? Because there was a period when it had *keddusha* but could not be brought — the half-sanctified state. That passing through a sanctified-but-unfit period is enough to render it permanently *nidcha*.

### Three Conclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים נִדְחִין, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דָּחוּי מֵעִיקָּרָא הָוֵה דָּחוּי, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ יֵשׁ דִּחוּי בְּדָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one can **conclude three** points **from** this statement: **Conclude from it** that **animals** that cannot be sacrificed **are** permanently **disqualified; and conclude from it** that **disqualification from the outset,** i.e., from the time the offering is consecrated, **is** permanent **disqualification; and conclude from it** that **there is** permanent **disqualification** even **in** a case where the animal possesses sanctity that inheres in its **monetary** value, rather than inherent sanctity.

The Gemara notes that three distinct principles can be derived from this case. First, *shma mina baalei chayim nidchin* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that live animals can indeed be *nidcha*. Second, *dichui mei-ikara havei dichui* — even if the animal was pushed off from the very beginning, having never been fully fit, that still constitutes *dichui*. Some might have argued that *dichui* only applies when something was once fit and then became unfit (*nira ve-nidcha*), but not when it was never fit from the start; this case demonstrates otherwise. Third, *yesh dichui be-damim* — even a sanctity that is merely *kedushat damim* (monetary sanctification of the animal's value rather than full bodily *kedushat mizbeach*) is sufficient to create *dichui* if the animal passed through a period when it had that sanctity but could not be offered.

## Dichui Cases

### Mumar Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָכַל חֵלֶב וְהִפְרִישׁ קׇרְבָּן, וְנִשְׁתַּמֵּד, וְחָזַר בּוֹ – הוֹאִיל וְנִדְחָה, יִדָּחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **ate** forbidden **fat and designated an offering** to atone for the transgression, **and** then **apostatized,** thereby disqualifying himself from bringing an offering, **and** later **recanted** his apostasy, **since** the offering was already **disqualified, it shall be disqualified** permanently.

Ulla in the name of Rabbi Yochanan presents a case that further illustrates *dichui* with living beings. Reuven ate *chelev* in error and then designated a *korban chatas* for that sin. He then became a *mumar* — rejecting that *mitzvah* or the Torah entirely — and in that state was unfit to bring a *korban*. In the language of the Gemara, a sacrifice brought by such a person is a *zevach resha'im*, unfit. He subsequently did *teshuvah*. Nevertheless — *ho'il ve-nidcha yidache* — since the *korban* passed through a period when it could not be brought, it remains *nidcha* and he must designate a new *korban*.

### Shoteh Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָכַל חֵלֶב וְהִפְרִישׁ קׇרְבָּן, וְנִשְׁתַּטָּה, וְחָזַר וְנִשְׁתַּפָּה – הוֹאִיל וְנִדְחָה, יִדָּחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated** that **Rabbi Yirmeya says** that **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **ate** forbidden **fat and designated an offering** to atone for his transgression, **and** then **became an imbecile,** who is unfit to bring an offering, **and** then **again became** halakhically **competent, since** the offering was already **disqualified, it shall be disqualified** permanently.

A parallel case is reported by Rabbi Yirmiyah in the name of Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: Reuven ate *chelev* in error, designated a *korban*, and then suffered a period of mental illness (*nishtateh*). He subsequently recovered (*nishtafa*). Here too — *ho'il ve-nidcha yidache* — the *korban* is *nidcha* because it passed through a period when the owner could not bring it.

### Why Both Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן קַמַּיְיתָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּהוּא דָּחֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּיָדַיִם; אֲבָל הָכָא דְּמִמֵּילָא אִידְּחִי – כְּיָשֵׁן דָּמֵי. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָכָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין בְּיָדוֹ לַחֲזוֹר; אֲבָל הָכָא דְּיֵשׁ בְּיָדוֹ לַחֲזוֹר – אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** both statements **are necessary. As, if** Rabbi Yoḥanan **had taught us** only **the first** statement, concerning an apostate, one might have reasoned that the offering is permanently disqualified **because he disqualified himself by** his own **action, but here,** in the case of one who became an imbecile, **where he was disqualified** through a process that occurs **by itself,** when he becomes competent again he may bring his sacrifice, as it is **considered as** though he were **asleep.** If one designated an offering and fell asleep, this certainly does not disqualify it. **And if** Rabbi Yoḥanan **had taught us** only the statement **here,** with regard to one who became an imbecile, one might have reasoned that the offering is permanently disqualified **because it is not in his power to return** to competence, **but here,** in the case of an apostate, **since it is in his power to recant** his apostasy, **I would say** that the offering is **not** permanently disqualified. Therefore, both statements are **necessary.**

The Gemara explains why both cases must be taught. If only the *mumar* case were given, one might think: *mishum de-hu dachi nafshei be-yadayim* — he actively and willfully pushed himself out of eligibility, so *dichui* applies. But perhaps the *shoteh*, who did not choose his state, is different — *ki yashen dami*, he is like someone sleeping, in a temporary and natural state, and perhaps the *korban* is not *nidcha*. Conversely, if only the *shoteh* case were given, one might say: *mishum de-ein be-yado lachazor* — since he cannot will himself out of his illness, there is full *dichui*. But perhaps a *mumar*, who can choose to do *teshuvah* at any moment, is not considered to have pushed the *korban* away fully — *aval hacha de-yesh be-yado lachazor, eima lo*. Both cases are needed to establish that in either situation — voluntary rejection or involuntary incapacity — the *korban* remains *nidcha*.

This discussion also raises an interesting related question about a person in a coma. Unlike sleep, a coma is not a natural cycle one can expect to emerge from, and unlike the *mumar*, the person has no capacity to will himself out of the state. Such a person's status is at least as strongly subject to *dichui* as the *shoteh* case, perhaps more so.

### Horayos Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אָכַל חֵלֶב וְהִפְרִישׁ קׇרְבָּן, וְהוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁחֵלֶב מוּתָּר, וְחָזְרוּ בָּהֶן, מַהוּ? מִי הָוֵי דָּחוּי, אוֹ לָא הָוֵי דָּחוּי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If one **ate** forbidden **fat and designated an offering** for the transgression, **and** then the **court ruled that** the type of **fat** he ate is **permitted,** thereby rendering the offering unnecessary, **and** the court subsequently **retracted** its ruling, in this case, **what is** the *halakha*? **Is** the offering permanently **disqualified, or is it not** permanently **disqualified?**

Rabbi Yirmiyah then poses a more difficult question: someone ate *chelev* in error, designated a *korban*, and then *Beis Din* issued an erroneous ruling that *chelev* is in fact permitted. While this ruling stands, the *Beis Din* itself would have rejected this *korban* — the act is not considered a sin, so there is nothing to bring a *korban* for. *Beis Din* subsequently retracted its ruling. Is the *korban* now *nidcha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: כִּי פָּתַח רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בִּדְחוּיִין – מֵהָא פָּתַח. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָתָם – גַּבְרָא אִידְּחִי, קׇרְבָּן לָא אִידְּחִי; הָכָא – קׇרְבָּן נָמֵי אִידְּחִי.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain elder [*hahu sava*] said to** Rabbi Yirmeya: **When Rabbi Yoḥanan introduced** the topic of permanently **disqualified** offerings, **he introduced** it **with this** case. **What is the reason? There,** in the case of one who apostatized or became an imbecile, although **the person was disqualified,** the **offering** itself **was not disqualified.** Consequently, it is less evident that the offering will be disqualified permanently. But **here,** in a case where the court ruled that the fat is permitted, the **offering** itself **was also disqualified,** as it was rendered unnecessary. Therefore, this is a more obvious example.

A *sava* — a certain elder — reports that when Rabbi Yochanan would begin teaching the laws of *dichui*, this was precisely the case he would open with, as his paradigmatic example.

### Horayos Explanation

This is surprising — one might think the *Horayos* case is *less* compelling than the *mumar* or *shoteh* cases. After all, *Beis Din*'s ruling was simply an error; retroactively, the act always was a sin. Why should the *korban* be pushed off?

The Gemara explains: *mai ta'ama — hasam gavra idchi, korban lo idchi* — in the *mumar* and *shoteh* cases, it is the *person* who became temporarily unfit to bring the *korban*, but there was never any period in which the concept of a *korban* for that sin was itself meaningless. The *korban chatas* always had a purpose; it was just the *gavra*, the individual, who was temporarily disqualified. Here, however, *hacha korban nami idchi* — during the period of the erroneous ruling, the very idea of a *korban* for this act was erased. The act was declared permissible; no *korban* was called for. The *korban* itself — not just the person — was pushed away. That is why Rabbi Yochanan found this to be the clearest and most forceful illustration of *dichui*.

## Ben Azzai Tradition

### Tradition Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן עַזַּאי: מְקוּבָּל אֲנִי מִפִּי שִׁבְעִים וּשְׁנַיִם זָקֵן בַּיּוֹם שֶׁהוֹשִׁיבוּ אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בִּישִׁיבָה, שֶׁכׇּל הַזְּבָחִים הַנֶּאֱכָלִין שֶׁנִּזְבְּחוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – כְּשֵׁרִין, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים מִשּׁוּם חוֹבָה; חוּץ מִן הַפֶּסַח וּמִן הַחַטָּאת. וְלֹא הוֹסִיף בֶּן עַזַּאי אֶלָּא הָעוֹלָה, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Shimon ben Azzai said: I received** a tradition **from seventy-two elders [*zaken*]** that all slaughtered offerings that are eaten, if slaughtered not for their sake, are fit. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need to **teach** the phrase **seventy-two** elders using the singular form: ***Zaken*,** rather than the plural form: *Zekenim*? The Gemara answers: **Because they all maintained one opinion,** i.e., they all agreed with this *halakha*. The mishna continues: **Ben Azzai added only the burnt offering** to the sin offering and the Paschal offering, which are mentioned in the first mishna as disqualified when sacrificed not for their sake. Based on that version, **Ben Azzai added** to the *halakha* cited in the first mishna **only the burnt offering,** which is not eaten, **and the Rabbis** disagreed and **did not concede to him.**

The Mishna records Shimon ben Azzai's statement: *mekubal ani mipi shivim u-shnayim zekenim* — I have received a tradition from seventy-two elders, on the day that Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah was appointed head of the academy. The tradition was that all edible sacrifices slaughtered *shelo lishmo* are *kasher*, though the owners do not fulfill their obligation, with the exception of the *pesach* and the *chatat*. The significant word in Ben Azzai's formulation is *hane'echalim* — the edible sacrifices. The only sacrifice not included in that category (given that *chatat* is explicitly excepted) is the *olah*, which is entirely burnt.

### Olah Addition

The Mishna therefore concludes: *v'lo hosif Ben Azzai ela ha-olah* — the only thing Ben Azzai added beyond what was already known was the *olah*. The tradition as he received it implicitly included the *olah* among those for which *shelo lishmo* is *pasul*. However, *v'lo hodu lo chachamim* — the *chachamim* did not agree to this addition. In their view, the list is limited to *chatat* and *pesach*.

### Rav Huna Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֶן עַזַּאי? ״עוֹלָה הוּא אִשֵּׁה רֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ לַה׳״ – הִיא לִשְׁמָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה, שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of ben Azzai?** The verse states: **"It is a burnt offering, an offering made by fire, of a pleasing aroma unto the Lord"** (Leviticus 1:13). The word **"it"** teaches that if it is sacrificed **for its sake,** it is **fit;** if sacrificed **not for its sake,** it is **unfit.**

What is the reasoning behind Ben Azzai's position? Rav Huna offers a *kal va'chomer*: if a *chatat*, which is not entirely burnt (*eina kalil*) and retains portions for human consumption, is nevertheless *pasul shelo lishmo*, then an *olah*, which is entirely consumed on the *mizbeach* (*shehi kalil*) and thus embodies the highest level of dedication to God, should certainly be *pasul shelo lishmo*.

## Kal Vachomer Challenge

### Atonement Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְחַטָּאת, שֶׁכֵּן מְכַפֶּרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this inference: **What** is notable **about a sin offering?** It is notable **in that it atones** for sin, in contrast to a burnt offering, which is not brought for atonement. Therefore, only a sin offering is disqualified when sacrificed not for its sake.

The Gemara immediately challenges this *kal va'chomer*. A *chatat* has the special quality of *mechaperet* — it atones — and perhaps it is this quality that makes it especially sensitive to intent, requiring *lishmo*. One cannot necessarily extend that requirement to the *olah*, which does not perform the same atoning function.

### Pesach Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֶּסַח יוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **A Paschal offering can prove** the point, as it is not brought for atonement, yet it is disqualified if sacrificed not for its sake.

The response: *Pesach yochiach* — the *pesach* proves that *kaparah* is not the critical factor. The *pesach* does not atone for sins and yet *shelo lishmo* is *pasul* for it as well. The common denominator of *pesach* and *chatat* shows that the *lishmo* requirement is not specifically tied to *kaparah*.

### Fixed Time Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְפֶסַח, שֶׁכֵּן זְמַנּוֹ קָבוּעַ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this as well: **What** is notable **about a Paschal offering?** It is notable **in that its time is set** at Passover eve, in contrast to a burnt offering, which does not have a designated time.

But then one could argue: *mah le-pesach sheken zemano kavua* — what is special about *pesach* is its fixed time. The *pesach* has a rigid, once-a-year *zman*, and perhaps that rigidity is what generates the heightened requirement of *lishmo*. One cannot extend from *pesach* to *olah* because *olah* has no fixed time.

### Common Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטָּאת תּוֹכִיחַ. וְחָזַר הַדִּין; לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה; הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁהֵן קֳדָשִׁים, וּשְׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן פָּסוּל; אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא עוֹלָה – שֶׁהִיא קָדָשִׁים, וּשְׁחָטָהּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: If so, **a sin offering can prove** the point, since it has no set time. **And the inference has reverted** to its starting point. The *halakha* is derived from the common element of the two sources: **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case. **Their common element is that they are offerings, and** if one **slaughtered them not for their sake,** they are **disqualified. So too, I shall include a burnt offering** in this *halakha*, **as it is an offering, and** therefore if one **slaughtered it not for its sake,** it is **disqualified.**

The response: *chatat tochiach* — the *chatat* will prove that a fixed time is not the key factor, since *chatat* has no fixed time yet *shelo lishmo* is *pasul* for it. Then the *kal va'chomer* is reformulated as a *tzad ha-shaveh*, a common denominator: *shechein kodashim veshachtan shelo lishman pasul* — they are both *kodashim* for which *shelo lishmo* is *pasul*. Therefore, the *olah*, which is also *kodashim* and whose *shechita shelo lishmah* should likewise be *pasul*, is included.

### Denominator Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן צַד כָּרֵת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this as well: **What** is notable **about their common element?** It is notable in **that they** both **have an element of *karet*.** A sin offering is brought for a transgression punishable by *karet* when committed intentionally, and one who refrains intentionally from bringing a Paschal offering is liable to be punished with *karet*. A burnt offering does not have an element of *karet*.

However, this common denominator proves too much. Once the *tzad ha-shaveh* is stated in these terms — they are *kodashim* for which *shelo lishmo* is *pasul* — there is no principled reason to stop at *olah*. All *korbanot* would appear to be *kodashim*, and all would then be subject to invalidity *shelo lishmo*. But the *chachamim* hold that the general rule is precisely the opposite — *shelo lishmo* is *kasher* for all *korbanot* except *pesach* and *chatat*. The *kal va'chomer* seems to be proving too broad a conclusion.

### Karet Objection

A further challenge: *mah lehatzad hashaveh shebahen, shechein yesh bahen tzad karet* — both *pesach* and *chatat* are connected to *karet*. Not bringing a *pesach* carries *karet*; a *chatat* atones for sins that involve *karet*. The *olah* has no such connection to *karet*, and therefore one cannot extend the rule to it.

### Tzibur Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עַזַּאי, צַד כָּרֵת לָא פָּרֵיךְ. אָשָׁם נָמֵי לַיְיתֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Ben Azzai** **does not refute** the inference by citing a common **element of *karet*,** as the elements of *karet* in a sin offering and a Paschal offering are not parallel. The Gemara asks: If so, **let it be derived** from this inference that **a guilt offering** is **also** disqualified if sacrificed not for its sake.

The Gemara reports that Ben Azzai is not persuaded by the *karet* challenge. He responds with a different limiting principle: *shechein yeshnan betzibbur keveyachid* — both *pesach* and *chatat* are brought in a communal context as well as individually. A *chatat* is brought as a communal offering (as in the *Horayos* scenario or on *Rosh Chodesh*), and the *pesach*, though individually designated, is brought by the entire community together. The *olah* similarly has communal dimensions, whereas an *asham* does not — there is no communal *asham*. This connection to *tzibbur*, rather than the *karet* connection, is what Ben Azzai sees as the shared characteristic justifying the extension to *olah* but not to *asham*.

### Tradition Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָשָׁם – פָּרֵיךְ: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בְּצִבּוּר כִּבְיָחִיד. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: צַד כָּרֵת נָמֵי פָּרֵיךְ, וּבֶן עַזַּאי גְּמָרָא גְּמִיר לַהּ. וְהָא דְּקָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא קַל וָחוֹמֶר – לֹא אֲמָרָהּ אֶלָּא לְחַדֵּד בָּהּ תַּלְמִידָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: With regard to **a guilt offering,** ben Azzai **refutes** the inference as follows: **What** is notable **about the common element of** a sin offering and a Paschal offering? It is notable **in that there are communal** offerings **as well as individual** offerings of these types, as well as communal burnt offerings, whereas there is no communal guilt offering. **And if you wish, say** instead that actually, ben Azzai **also refutes** the entire inference, because a sin offering and a Paschal offering uniquely share **an element of *karet*. And** the reason **ben Azzai** maintains that a burnt offering sacrificed not for its sake is disqualified is that **he learned it** as **a tradition. And** as for the fact **that Rav Huna cites an *a fortiori*** inference, he does not believe it to be the true source for ben Azzai's *halakha*; **he said it only to sharpen** the minds of **his students with it,** challenging them to refute it.

The Gemara ultimately steps back from the entire enterprise of constructing a *kal va'chomer*. As the objections multiply and interlock, it concludes: *u-ben Azzai gemara gmir lah* — Ben Azzai's position is not derived from a *kal va'chomer* at all. It rests on his received tradition from the seventy-two elders. That is exactly what he said at the outset: *mekubal ani*. Why, then, did Rav Huna present the *kal va'chomer*? *Lechadudei letalmidiah* — to sharpen his students. The *kal va'chomer* was offered as an exercise: can you construct a valid logical argument? Where does it break down? What objections can be raised? The entire elaborate back-and-forth was pedagogical — a workout for the analytic mind — not a genuine attempt to derive Ben Azzai's ruling from first principles, since that ruling ultimately rests on tradition alone.
