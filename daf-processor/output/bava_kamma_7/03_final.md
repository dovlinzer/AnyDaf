# Bava Kamma 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Akiva vs. Yishmael

### Meitav Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם״ – מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ שֶׁל נִיזָּק, וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ שֶׁל נִיזָּק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לִגְבּוֹת לַנִּזָּקִין מִן הָעִידִּית. וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The Torah states with regard to paying damages: **"Of the best of his field, and of the best of his vineyard he shall pay"** (Exodus 22:4). What is the meaning of "his field" and "his vineyard"? It refers to that property of the injured party; the one liable for the damage pays from land of a quality that is commensurate to **the best**-quality **field of** the **injured** party **and** to **the best**-quality **vineyard of** the **injured** party, even if the property he damaged was of a lesser quality; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says** that **the verse comes only to** allow **injured** parties **to collect** compensation **from superior-quality** land belonging to the one liable for the damage, in the event that he has no money or movable property. **And** by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference one can derive that the Temple treasury of **consecrated** property collects from superior-quality land.

We pick up at the very bottom of 6b, where the Gemara begins unpacking the final clause of the *braita* cited at the close of the Mishnah's discussion — the rule of *meitav*, payment from the best of the land. The *pasuk* that serves as the locus of this discussion, treating the case of *shein v'regel*, states *meitav sadeihu u'meitav karmo yishalem* — he shall pay from the best of his field and the best of his vineyard. The question is: whose best land provides the standard of measurement?

The *Gemara* frames the dispute as follows: Rabbi Akiva holds *meitav d'mazik* — the payment must come from the finest land belonging to the *mazik*, the party who caused the damage. Rabbi Yishmael holds *meitav d'nizak* — one must find something comparable to the finest land of the *nizak*, the injured party. Everyone agrees that the *mazik* does not pay *more* than the value of the damage; the question is purely about the *quality* of the property used for payment. People much prefer receiving one hundred dollars' worth of grade-A land over one hundred dollars' worth of grade-C land, since better land is far easier to sell. Therefore, Rabbi Akiva holds that the *mazik* must pay from his own grade-A land, while Rabbi Yishmael holds that you identify the grade-A land of the *nizak* and match it — if the *mazik* lacks comparable land, the *nizak* receives an upgrade, but the baseline is set by what the *nizak* himself owns, not by what is the *mazik*'s personal best.

### All Damages Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לִגְבּוֹת לַנִּזָּקִין מִן הָעִידִּית, וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse comes only to teach that all categories of damage collection are taken from the best. And certainly when the obligation runs toward *hekdesh*, consecrated Temple property.

Rabbi Akiva's principle does not stop at *shein v'regel*. The *Gemara* had established through a *gezeirah shavah* — connecting the language of *tachas nisinah yishaleim kesef* to the context of *shein v'regel* — that all four primary categories of damage (*arba'ah avos nezikin*), and indeed all subsidiary forms of liability including theft, robbery, unpaid guardians, and bodily harm (*sheves*, *ripuy*, *boshes*), are governed by the same *meitav* standard. Rabbi Akiva therefore opens: *lo ba ha-katuv ella ligvos l'nezikin min ha'iddit* — the Torah comes only to teach that all categories of damage collection are taken from the best. He then adds a second principle: *v'kal vachomer l'hekdesh* — and certainly when the obligation runs toward *hekdesh*, consecrated Temple property.

## Hekdesh Kal Vachomer

### Initial KV Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ״?
>
> **Translation:** What precisely is the case in which one might owe damages to *hekdesh* and thereby be required to pay from *meitav*?

The *Gemara* immediately interrogates this *kal vachomer*. What precisely is the case in which one might owe damages to *hekdesh* and thereby be required to pay from *meitav*? The most natural reading of *v'kal vachomer l'hekdesh* is that if we pay the best to a lay person injured by our ox, we should certainly pay the best to *hekdesh* when our ox gores a *hekdesh* ox.

### Torah Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא דִּנְגַח תּוֹרָא דִידַן לְתוֹרָא דְהֶקְדֵּשׁ, ״שׁוֹר רֵעֵהוּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – וְלֹא שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that **our ox,** i.e., an ox belonging to a Jew, **gored an ox that is consecrated** property, there is a difficulty, as **the Merciful One states:** "And if one man's ox hurts the **ox of another"** (Exodus 21:35). This teaches that the *halakha* applies only if the ox hurt an ox belonging to another Jew, **but not** if it hurt **an ox that is consecrated** property. In the latter case no damages are collected.

The *Gemara* objects sharply: *shor re'eihu amar rachmana v'lo shor hekdesh* — the Torah explicitly says "his neighbor's ox," meaning a lay person's ox, not an ox belonging to *hekdesh*. If one's ox gores the ox of *hekdesh*, one is entirely exempt from payment. So how can Rabbi Akiva build a *kal vachomer* on a case that never generates liability in the first place?

### Tosafot Extension

Tosafot, citing the *Yerushalmi*, takes this further. The exemption is not limited to the goring-ox case. From the *pasuk* concerning *shor* and a parallel formulation in the *bor* case, and through a chain of derivations, the *halacha* emerges that one is technically exempt from all forms of damage to *hekdesh* property. If you burn *hekdesh*'s field, destroy their property through any of the standard categories of *nezikin*, you bear no Toraitic liability. Tosafot does note that if a person actively and intentionally destroys *hekdesh* property — as in a direct act of *kochav* or *yadayim* — there is at least a rabbinic obligation to pay. But as a matter of Torah law, *hekdesh* does not collect tort damages. This is, to put it plainly, a striking result.

### KV Difficulty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יְהֵא אֶלָּא בַּעַל חוֹב, וּבַעַל חוֹב בְּבֵינוֹנִית!
>
> **Translation:** The Temple treasurer **should not** be treated any better than an ordinary **creditor,** **and** the *halakha* of **a creditor** is to collect only **from intermediate-quality** land, not from superior-quality land.

Given that one never pays damages to *hekdesh*, the *Gemara* presses: how can Rabbi Akiva construct any *kal vachomer* at all? If *hekdesh* never collects damages, what case is he envisioning where *hekdesh* would collect from *meitav*?

### Debt vs. Damages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מָנֶה לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת״, דְּאָתֵי גִּזְבָּר וְשָׁקֵיל מֵעִידִּית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** we say that the *a fortiori* inference is referring not to a case of damage but **to one who** vows and **says: It is** incumbent **upon me** to donate **one hundred dinars for Temple maintenance.** And the inference teaches that if the one who took the vow has no money, then the Temple **treasurer comes and collects** the hundred dinars **from** his **superior-quality** land.

The *Gemara* proposes: perhaps the case is not damages at all, but a voluntary pledge. *Ve'omer harei alai maneh libedek habayis* — someone declares an obligation to give one hundred *zuz* to the Temple treasury (*Beit HaMikdash*). The *gizbar* (Temple treasurer) comes to collect, and he collects from the *eidis*, the best land, if the person has not paid in cash.

But the *Gemara* rejects this as well. *Lo yehe ela ba'al chov* — in that scenario, *hekdesh* is simply a creditor, and creditors collect from *beinonit*, middle-quality land, not from *eidis*. Rabbi Akiva said the *kal vachomer* demonstrates that *hekdesh* collects the *best*, not merely what any creditor would collect. Moreover, the entire logic of a *kal vachomer* from lay person to *hekdesh* collapses once we acknowledge that *hekdesh* is actually *weaker* in the domain of *nezikin* — they collect nothing in damages. *Ikka lemifrach: mah l'va'al chov sheken yafe kocho b'nezikin* — one can refute the *kal vachomer* entirely: the lay person's right to collect payment at its best quality may be grounded in the fact that he possesses strong powers in tort law. *Ta'omar b'hekdesh shehoru kocho b'nezikin* — can we say the same of *hekdesh*, whose position in *nezikin* is utterly impoverished?

## Shimon ben Menasya

### Immunity Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דִּנְגַח תּוֹרָא דִידַן לְתוֹרָא דְהֶקְדֵּשׁ. וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ: ״שׁוֹר רֵעֵהוּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – וְלֹא שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ; רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Actually,** we can explain that we are dealing here with a case **where our ox gored an ox that is consecrated** property. **And** with regard to that **which** posed **a difficulty for you** about this explanation, i.e., that **the Merciful One states: "**The **ox of another"** (Exodus 21:35), which indicates that liability is incurred only for damaging another person's ox **but not** for **an ox consecrated** to the Temple, **Rabbi Akiva** disagrees with that interpretation and **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya.**

The *Gemara* resolves the entire difficulty by attributing to Rabbi Akiva a specific reading of the *pasuk* — namely, that he holds like Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya. A *braita* records: *shor shel hekdesh shenagach shor shel hedyot — patur*. If *hekdesh*'s ox gores a lay person's ox, *hekdesh* is exempt, *hekdesh* has complete immunity in the role of the *mazik*. But *shor shel hedyot shenagach shor shel hekdesh — bein tam bein mu'ad meshalem nezek shalem*. If a lay person's ox gores *hekdesh*'s ox, one is liable not for half-damages (*chatzi nezek*, the normal rule for a *tam* ox) but for full damages (*nezek shalem*) — even on the first offense.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט – פָּטוּר; שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ, בֵּין תָּם בֵּין מוּעָד – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says:** If **an ox that is consecrated** property **gored an ox belonging to a common person [*hedyot*],** the Temple treasury is **exempt** from liability. By contrast, in the case of **an ox belonging to a common person that gored an ox that is consecrated** property, **whether** the ox that gored the other ox was **an innocuous** ox, i.e., an ox with no consistent history of causing damage with the intent to injure, **or** it was **a forewarned** ox, i.e., an ox whose owner was forewarned because his ox had already gored another ox three times, the ox's owner **pays** the **full** cost of the **damage.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya derives this from the very *pasuk* that seemed to exempt liability to *hekdesh*: *ki yigach shor ish et shor re'eihu* — when a man's ox gores his neighbor's ox. The formulation *shor ish* and *shor re'eihu* defines a case of lay person versus lay person, yielding the standard half-damages regime. But when *hekdesh* is involved, that formula no longer applies — and the deviation cuts both ways. When *hekdesh* is the *gorer*, it pays nothing; when *hekdesh* is the *goree*, we pay in full.

### Supports Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְכִי תֵּימָא סָבַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: בַּעַל חוֹב נָמֵי בְּעִידִּית; אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְבַעַל חוֹב – שֶׁכֵּן יִפָּה כֹּחוֹ בְּנִזָּקִין, תֹּאמַר בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ – שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ בְּנִזָּקִין!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **Rabbi Akiva holds** that **a creditor** can **collect** the money owed him **from superior-quality** land **as well,** there is still a difficulty; the *a fortiori* inference **can** still **be refuted** in the following manner: **What** is notable **about** an ordinary **creditor?** He is notable **in that** the Torah **enhanced his power with regard to payment of damages,** as an ordinary person can collect payment of damages for loss or injury caused by another's ox. But **can you say** the same about the Temple treasury of **consecrated** property, with regard to which the Torah **weakened its power with regard to payment of damages,** not allowing it to collect such compensation? Accordingly, even if a creditor collects from superior-quality land, it does not necessarily follow that a Temple treasurer would also do so. It is therefore apparent that Rabbi Akiva's *a fortiori* inference is not referring to the case of one who donates money to the Temple.

This reading directly supports Rabbi Akiva's *kal vachomer*. According to Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, one is in fact liable for damages to *hekdesh* property — and the liability is, if anything, *stricter* than standard tort liability. Once you accept that a lay person owes *hekdesh* damages, the *kal vachomer* is fully coherent: if a lay person collects from *eidis*, then *hekdesh*, which enjoys an even stronger claim in the *nezikin* framework, certainly collects from *eidis*.

One might note the apparent anachronism: Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya is a later *Tanna* than Rabbi Akiva. But the *Gemara*'s formulation — *Rabbi Akiva savar lah ke-Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya* — does not mean Rabbi Akiva was *following* Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya. Rather, it means Rabbi Akiva was *anticipating* the same interpretive position that Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya would later articulate explicitly.

### Debate Scope Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, מַאי ״לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב״?
>
> **Translation:** If it is so that this is the disagreement, then **what** is the meaning of Rabbi Akiva's statement: **The verse comes only** to allow injured parties to collect compensation from superior-quality land? This indicates that Rabbi Akiva disagrees about how to understand this verse.

Having established that Rabbi Akiva's *kal vachomer* requires liability to *hekdesh*, the *Gemara* raises a new question: perhaps the entire dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael is not about *meitav d'mazik* versus *meitav d'nizak* at all. Perhaps both Tannaim agree that assessment follows the *nizak*, and their real dispute is about the *hekdesh* question — whether or not one owes damages to *hekdesh*, tracking the debate between Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya and the Rabbis.

### Primary vs. Secondary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, מַאי ״קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ״?
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** if the subject is the Temple treasury, **what** is the meaning of: **And** by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference one can derive that the Temple treasury of **consecrated** property collects from superior-quality land. If, as suggested, Rabbi Akiva holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, then he maintains that damage to consecrated property is dealt with more stringently than damage to another person's property, in that if one's ox damages consecrated property one is liable for the full cost of the damage even if the ox was considered innocuous. If damage to consecrated property is dealt with more stringently, there is no basis to derive an *a fortiori* inference.

The *Gemara* dismisses this reading. *Im kein mai lav ba ha-katuv* — if so, what is the force of Rabbi Akiva's primary statement that "the Torah comes to teach that damages are collected from *eidis*"? And *ve-od mai kal vachomer l'hekdesh* — the *hekdesh* point is introduced as a secondary corollary, a *kal vachomer* built upon the primary ruling. The structure of Rabbi Akiva's statement makes unmistakably clear that his central point — and the core of his dispute with Rabbi Yishmael — is the quality of land used for payment. The *hekdesh* observation is a further implication, not the heart of the disagreement.

## Rav Ashi Braita

### Explicit Assignment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, תַּנְיָא בְּהֶדְיָא: ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם״ – מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ שֶׁל נִיזָּק, וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ שֶׁל נִיזָּק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ שֶׁל מַזִּיק, וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ שֶׁל מַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, didn't Rav Ashi say:** It is **taught explicitly** in a *baraita*: The verse: **"The best of his field and the best of his vineyard he shall pay"** (Exodus 22:4), teaches that the appraisal is of **the best of the field of the injured** party, **and** of **the best of the vineyard of the injured** party. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says:** The appraisal is of **the best of the field of the one liable for the damage, and** of **the best of the vineyard of the one liable for the damage.** This clearly indicates that according to Rabbi Akiva compensation is collected from the superior-quality land belonging to the one liable for the damage.

Rav Ashi then adds: *tanya b'hedya* — there is an explicit *braita* that settles the matter definitively. The *braita* states: *meitav sadehu u'meitav karmo yeshaleim — meitav sadehu shel nizak u'meitav karmo shel nizak, divrei Rabbi Yishmael; Rabbi Akiva omer: meitav sadehu shel mazik u'meitav karmo shel mazik.* Rabbi Yishmael's position is that "his best field" refers to the *nizak*'s best field; Rabbi Akiva holds it refers to the *mazik*'s best field.

### Resolution

The reason the *Gemara* had entertained any ambiguity is that when one looks at the *braita* from the previous passage alone, Rabbi Akiva's formulation — *lo ba ha-katuv ella ligvos l'nezikin min ha'iddit* — does not explicitly say "the *mazik*'s land." Rabbi Yishmael also says one collects from *iddit*; he simply defines it differently. One might therefore have suspected that Rabbi Akiva's primary *chiddush* was the second clause (liability to *hekdesh*), not the first. Rav Ashi's *braita*, however, assigns positions explicitly: Rabbi Yishmael says *meitav shel nizak*, Rabbi Akiva says *meitav shel mazik*. The dispute over the quality and identity of the land paid is real and primary. The *hekdesh* issue is a secondary point that Rabbi Akiva makes in addition — and according to the *Gemara*, Rabbi Akiva does indeed hold that one owes damages to *hekdesh* and pays them in the same manner as to a lay person.

## Meitav vs. Equivalency

### Contradiction Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרָבָא, כְּתִיב: ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם״ – מֵיטַב אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא; וְהָתַנְיָא: ״יָשִׁיב״ – לְרַבּוֹת שָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף, וַאֲפִילּוּ סוּבִּין!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raises a contradiction** and addresses it **to Rava: It is written: "The best of his field and of the best of his vineyard he shall pay"** (Exodus 22:4), which indicates that from his **best**-quality land, **yes,** he shall pay, but from **something else, no,** he shall not pay. **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And if a man shall open a pit, or if a man shall dig a pit and not cover it, and an ox or a donkey fall therein, the owner of the pit shall pay; **he shall recompense** money to its owners" (Exodus 21:33–34)? Since the verse states: "The owner of the pit shall pay," the additional term "he shall recompense" is superfluous. It therefore serves **to include** any item **worth money, and even bran,** a relatively inferior commodity, as valid forms of restitution.

The *Gemara* records that Abaye challenged Rava with a *rami* — a juxtaposition of two apparently contradictory texts. On one hand, *k'tiv*: the *pasuk* says *meitav sadehu u'meitav karmo yishalem* — implying that only the best will do, nothing less. On the other hand, *v'atanya*: a *braita* teaches regarding the *bor* case that *kesef yeshiv l'va'alav* — "money he shall return to the owners" — and expounds *l'rabos shava kesef*, to include anything of equivalent monetary value. The *braita* explicitly includes even *subin* — bran — as a valid form of payment. How can both be true? The *pasuk* demands the best, yet the *braita* permits even the most modest chattel.

### Land vs. Chattel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן מִדַּעְתּוֹ, כָּאן בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava resolves the contradiction: This is **not difficult. Here,** the term "he shall recompense" is referring to a case where he pays **of his own volition,** without being taken to court, and he may therefore use any form of payment. **There,** the phrase "of the best of his field" is referring to a case where he pays **against his will,** after the injured party has sued him in court. Since he caused the injured party additional trouble, he must pay using his best-quality land.

The resolution — which in retrospect seems transparent once stated, though the *Gemara* will work toward it through several stages — is that *meitav* applies exclusively to land, while chattel of any kind is treated as equivalent to cash. Land is illiquid. It is difficult to sell, restricted to a small pool of potential buyers, and its value is hard to realize quickly. When the *Torah* insists on *meitav* for land payment, it is ensuring the *nizak* receives property he can actually convert to value. Chattel, by contrast, is mobile and marketable — if you cannot find a buyer locally, you can transport it elsewhere. Therefore any chattel, regardless of quality, satisfies the obligation.

### Willing vs. Forced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב עִילַּאי: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״יְשַׁלֵּם״ – בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla, son of Rav Ilai, said:** According to this explanation, the language of the verse **is also precise, as it is written: "He shall pay,"** a term that, as opposed to: "He shall recompense," connotes that he is forced to pay **against his will.**

The *Gemara* initially offers a different resolution: *kan mida'ato kan b'al korcho* — the distinction is not between land and chattel but between voluntary payment and court-enforced seizure. If the *mazik* pays willingly, of his own initiative, the *nizak* must accept whatever is tendered. If the *nizak* was forced to go to court and have the property seized against the *mazik*'s will, he may then demand the best quality. Ulla son of Rav Ilai suggests this reading may even be embedded in the *pesukim*: *yeshalem* — "he shall pay" — connotes compelled payment, justifying the demand for *meitav*, while *yeshiv* — "he shall return" — connotes voluntary restitution, permitting any form of equivalent value.

### Pesuk Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי כְּתִיב ״יְשׁוּלַּם״?! ״יְשַׁלֵּם״ כְּתִיב – מִדַּעְתּוֹ מַשְׁמַע!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him** that the precise meaning of the verse does not indicate that he is paying against his will: **Is it written: He shall be paid,** in the passive form, which would clearly indicate that he did not voluntarily initiate the payment himself but rather is paying against his will? No; **"he shall pay" is written,** which can also **indicate** that he pays **of his own volition.**

Abaye rejects this on linguistic grounds: *mi k'tiv yushalam?* The *pasuk* writes *yeshalem*, not *yushalam*. The form *yushalem* (passive: "it shall be paid up") would clearly connote compulsion. But *yeshalem* (active: "he will pay") can just as naturally denote voluntary payment — *mida'ato*. The textual distinction cannot bear the weight of the proposed legal difference. The answer, though reasonable in principle, cannot be extracted from the *pasuk* with sufficient precision, and Abaye is not satisfied.

## Ma'aser Ani Digression

### Bad Market Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּדְמָר – דְּתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ בָּתִּים שָׂדוֹת וּכְרָמִים, וְאֵינוֹ מוֹצֵא לְמוֹכְרָן – מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, עַד מֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** an alternative resolution, **similar to that** with which the **Master,** i.e., Abaye's teacher, Rabba, employed in order to resolve a different difficulty. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: A wealthy person is not entitled to take a poor man's tithe. He is instead expected to sell his assets and purchase food with the proceeds. But in the case of an individual **who owns houses, fields, and vineyards, but** at the present time **cannot find** buyers **to whom he can sell them,** one **may feed him** with food from the **poor man's tithe up until half** the value of his assets.

Abaye then invokes a principle that Rabba derives from a *braita* — not directly about *nezikin* at all, but about *ma'aser ani*, the tithe for the poor distributed in the third and sixth years of the sabbatical cycle. The *braita* describes the following scenario: someone owns houses, fields, and vineyards — substantial real property — but *eino motzei limochran*, he cannot sell them at their normal market price. Is he considered an *ani* (poor person) entitled to receive *ma'aser ani* and the other agricultural gifts for the poor (*leket*, *shikcha*, *peah*)? The *braita* rules: *ma'achilo oso ma'aser ani ad mechtza* — one may give him *ma'aser ani* up to half the normal assessment.

The stakes of the question: to be considered an *ani* entitled to such gifts, one must possess less than two hundred *zuz*. If you are asset-rich but cash-poor, does your net worth count at its theoretical market value or at what you could actually realize today?

### Rashi's Reading

There are two ways to read this case. One approach — mentioned and then rejected by Rashi — frames the *ma'aser ani* as a form of *rachmanus*, a charitable accommodation to prevent the person from being forced into a ruinous sale. On this reading, even if his assets are objectively worth more than two hundred *zuz*, we give him *ma'aser ani* to tide him over.

Rashi prefers a different reading: the question is purely about how to assess the person's *net worth* under current market conditions. He may possess property that *would* fetch two hundred *zuz* under normal conditions, but today, at current market prices, it is only worth one hundred *zuz*. The legal question is whether we evaluate his assets at their general, long-term market value or at what they would actually fetch right now, at the moment of assessment.

### Half-Value Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵי בָּהּ מָר: הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי (הוּזַל) [דְּזוּל] אַרְעָתָא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, וְדִידֵיהּ נָמֵי זָל בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ – אֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא נָמֵי לִיסְפֵּי לֵיהּ, דְּהָא זוּל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Master **discussed it** and asked: **What are the circumstances** that led to him being unable to sell his land? **If everyone's land depreciated** in value, **and his** land **also depreciated along with theirs, let us provide him** with **even more** than half the value of his assets, **as,** since **everyone's land also depreciated,** the market value of his property is low and he is genuinely classified as a poor person, who may take as much poor man's tithe as is given to him.

The *braita* rules *ad mechtza* — we will assess the property at no less than half its normal value. Even if the current market is depressed and no buyers can be found, we do not reduce the assessed value below fifty percent of the recognized normal price. If, at that reduced valuation, the person's total assets still exceed two hundred *zuz*, he is not an *ani*. If the half-value assessment brings him below two hundred *zuz*, he is entitled to receive *ma'aser ani* — but only up to the amount that would bring his total back to two hundred *zuz*.

The *Gemara* asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the scenario? If the land has genuinely depreciated across the board — everyone's land is worth less right now — then by current market prices he is objectively an *ani*, and one should be able to give him as much as desired in a single gift, the way one always may for an unambiguous *ani*. Why cap it at half?

### Seasonal Rates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּאוֹקִיר אַרְעָתָא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, וְדִידֵיהּ – אַיְּידֵי דְּעָיֵיל וְנָפֵיק אַזּוּזֵי, זָל אַרְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be **that everyone** else's **land appreciated** in value, **and** with regard to **his land, since** people see **that he goes in and goes out for money,** i.e., he is in need of money, they reason that he will be forced to accept a lower offer for his property. Consequently, **his land depreciated** in value.

Rabba explains: *lo tzricha* — the *braita* is needed for a very specific case. *D'veyomei Nissan yakra ara u'veyomei Tishrei zal ara* — in the spring (*Nissan*), agricultural land commands a premium, because farmers want to acquire land before the plowing season so they can prepare and plant for the coming agricultural cycle. In the fall (*Tishrei*), land is cheap, because the optimal moment for new ownership has passed. Normal sellers simply wait: *natri ad Nissan* — they hold out until the spring to sell at peak value. Tosafot notes that this same seasonal dynamic applies to houses as well — there is a recognized peak season for rental and purchase, and prices vary accordingly throughout the year.

The person in the *braita* cannot afford to wait. *Ho'il v'itztareichu lei zuzei — zavin kedehashta* — since he urgently needs cash, he must sell at today's lower price. Now the key distinction emerges: this is not merely a case of someone being taken advantage of because creditors know he is desperate (a purely subjective disadvantage that would not change his objective net worth). Rather, there is a genuine, objective difference in market price between *Nissan* and *Tishrei*. His land is objectively worth less right now, at *Tishrei* rates, than it would be worth in *Nissan*. The normal person is not penalized for this because the normal person waits and sells in the spring. This person, however, is compelled to sell now.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר מָר עֲלַהּ: לָא צְרִיכָא; דִּבְיוֹמֵי נִיסָן יָקְרָא אַרְעָתָא, וּבְיוֹמֵי תִּשְׁרֵי זָל אַרְעָתָא; דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – נָטְרִי עַד נִיסָן, וּמְזַבְּנִי; וְהַאי, הוֹאִיל וְאִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ זוּזֵי – זָבֵין כִּדְהַשְׁתָּא. עַד פַּלְגָא – אוֹרְחֵיהּ לְמֵיזַל, טְפֵי – לָאו אוֹרְחֵיהּ לְמֵיזַל.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Master,** Rabba, **said concerning this: No,** the *halakha* that he is provided with only up to half the value of his assets is **necessary** in a case where he cannot sell due to seasonal fluctuations in price. This is **because in the days** of the month **of Nisan,** which is the beginning of the harvest season, the market value of **land appreciates, and in the days** of the month **of Tishrei,** a time when the harvest had already been reaped and it is too late to prepare the land for the coming year, the market value of **land depreciates. Consequently, everyone** generally **waits until Nisan and sells** only at that point. **But this** person, **since he is in need of money, sells** his land in Tishrei **according to the current,** lower, market price. Accordingly, based on the current market prices, his assets have a low value and there is justification in regarding him as a poor person. Yet, since if he waited until Nisan, as everyone else does, his land would have a high value, he cannot truly be classified as a poor person. Therefore, he is permitted to take only as much poor man's tithe as will cover his losses. The Gemara explains why he is permitted to take only up to half the value of his land: Since **it is the nature of** land **to depreciate up to half** its value, and it is **not its nature to depreciate more** than that, he is entitled to take poor man's tithe that is worth up to half the value of his land and the rest he can supplement by selling his land at its current, lower price.

Therefore: *ad palga orchei lemizal* — a depreciation of up to fifty percent between *Nissan* and *Tishrei* rates is recognized as a normal seasonal fluctuation. More than fifty percent, *lav orchei lemizal* — that exceeds what is considered a normal seasonal swing. The *braita*'s rule of *ad mechtza* thus reflects the outer limit of recognized seasonal price variation. We assess his assets at *Tishrei* prices, which may be as low as half the *Nissan* rate, and on that basis determine his status. Since he is an *ani* only relative to current prices — but in a few months will not be an *ani* — he occupies a borderline category, and the amount of *ma'aser ani* given to him is capped at what would bring his assessed net worth up to two hundred *zuz*, rather than being unlimited.

## Seasonal Rates Applied

### Normal Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא נָמֵי גַּבֵּי נִזָּקִין – דִּינֵיהּ בְּעִידִּית;
>
> **Translation:** Abaye uses a similar distinction to resolve the contradiction he raises: **And here also, with regard to damages,** if one considers a case where the injured party comes to collect his damages during Tishrei, **his legal right** is to collect payment **from superior-quality** land. This will be a relatively large amount of land, owing to the current seasonal depreciation in its value.

Returning now to *nezikin*, the *Gemara* draws on the seasonal-rates framework. The normal rule is that damages are collected from *eidis*, the best-quality land, assessed at its current market rate.

### Lower Grade Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ אִיהוּ: הַב לִי בֵּינוֹנִית טְפֵי פּוּרְתָּא; אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי שָׁקְלַתְּ כְּדִינָךְ – שְׁקוֹל כִּדְהַשְׁתָּא, וְאִי לָא – שְׁקֹיל כִּי יוּקְרָא דִּלְקַמֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But if** the injured party **says to** the one liable for the damage: **Give me slightly more land of intermediate quality** instead, then the one liable for the damage **can say to him: If you** choose to **take** superior-quality land, **as is your legal right, take** a large amount **in accordance with the current** market value; **but if not,** and you wish to take intermediate-quality land, **take according to the future** value in Nisan, after it will have **appreciated,** which will mean you will be entitled to a smaller amount of land. This can be used to resolve the contradiction: The phrase "of the best of his field," which indicates that payment is made only from superior-quality land, defines the injured party's basic rights. The term "he shall recompense," which indicates that payment may be made in any form, is referring to a case where the injured party forgoes his rights. In such a case the value of the land or item he agrees to receive is appraised based on its appreciated value at the season when it is generally sold.

Suppose, however, that the *nizak* would prefer to receive a larger quantity of lower-quality land (*beinonit*) rather than a smaller amount of *eidis*, such that the total value is equivalent. Perhaps the *beinonit* borders his existing property and is therefore more valuable to him personally. The *mazik* can respond: *ei shaklat ke-dinach shakul* — if you want to exercise your legal right, take *eidis* at its current (*Tishrei*) market rate. *Ve'i lo* — but if you want me to give you *beinonit* instead, *be-shuka ki-yokra de-lakamei* — we will assess the *beinonit* at the higher (*Nissan*) seasonal rate.

### Disadvantage Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: אִם כֵּן, הוֹרַעְתָּ כֹּחָן שֶׁל נִזָּקִין אֵצֶל בֵּינוֹנִית וְזִיבּוּרִית – דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר מִמֵּיטַב, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מִבֵּינוֹנִית וְזִיבּוּרִית נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov objects to this** suggestion: **If so, you have weakened the power of injured** parties **with regard to intermediate-quality and inferior-quality** land, as, by agreeing to receive land of a lower quality than that to which their rights entitle them, they will actually receive less land, since the land is then appraised based on the appreciated market values in Nisan. Such an opinion is untenable, **as the Merciful One states** that injured parties collect **from the best**-quality land, clearly intending to enhance their rights, **and** yet **you said** he **cannot also** collect **from intermediate-quality and inferior-quality** land unless he agrees that the land should be appraised based on the appreciated Nisan values, thereby lowering the value of his payment.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov objects sharply: *im kein hora'ta kochan shel nizakin etzel beinonit ve-ziburit* — this arrangement actively harms the *nizak* in any case involving *beinonit* or *ziburit*. The *Torah* gave the *nizak* the advantage of *meitav* precisely to protect him. Yet under this proposal, the *nizak* cannot even collect from lower-quality land without being forced to accept an unfavorable valuation. *Rachmana amar mi-meitav* — the Torah intended this as a benefit, yet the result disadvantages him when he moves to any grade below the best. This cannot be the correct *halacha*.

### Ketuva Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: אִי אִיכָּא לְדַמּוֹיֵי – לְבַעַל חוֹב מְדַמֵּינַן לֵיהּ; בַּעַל חוֹב – דִּינֵיהּ בְּבֵינוֹנִית. וְאִי אָמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי זִיבּוּרִית טְפֵי פּוּרְתָּא, אָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי שָׁקְלַתְּ כְּדִינָךְ – שְׁקוֹל כִּדְהַשְׁתָּא, וְאִי לָא – שְׁקֹיל כִּי יוּקְרָא דִּלְקַמֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: If there is** a case **to compare to** Rabba's distinction, it is not compared to a case of payment of damages, but **we can compare it to** a case of **a creditor** who comes to collect the loan during Tishrei. **His legal right is** to collect **from** the borrower's **intermediate-quality** land, which will be a relatively large amount owing to the current seasonal depreciation in the value of land. **But if** the creditor **says to** the borrower: **Give me slightly more land of inferior quality** instead, the borrower **can say to him: If you** choose to **take** the intermediate-quality land, **as is your legal right, take** a large amount **in accordance with the current** market value, **but if not,** and you wish to take land of inferior quality, **take** it **in accordance with the future, appreciated,** market value in the month of Nisan. This would mean that the creditor would be entitled to a smaller amount of land.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov therefore recasts the framework. Rather than applying the seasonal-rate model to *nezikin* at all, he suggests it may be relevant to the case of a woman collecting her *ketuva*. A woman collecting her marriage settlement is entitled only to *ziburit* — the lowest-grade land. If she asks to receive *beinonit* instead — less of it in quantity, but higher in quality — she is asking for an accommodation beyond her strict entitlement. In that situation, the husband may respond: *ei shaklat ke-dinach, shakalt ke-de-hashta* — if you want what you are legally owed, take *ziburit* at current market rates. *Ve'i lo, shakalt ke-ikra de-lakamei* — if you want me to give you *beinonit* as a favor, we will assess it at the higher seasonal rate.

Tosafot observes that the same logic could apply to a *ba'al chov* (creditor) who asks to collect from *eidis* rather than the *beinonit* he is entitled to. The debtor could respond: fine, you may take *eidis*, but we will use *Nissan* rates rather than current *Tishrei* rates. The principle is: when a party requests property of *higher* quality than their legal entitlement — as an accommodation or upgrade — the counterparty may insist on assessing it at the higher seasonal rate. But when a party is moving *down* from their entitlement, as in the *nezikin* case, the model cannot apply, for the reasons Rav Acha bar Yaakov articulated.

## Chattels Always Meitav

### Rabba's First Try

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ – מִמֵּיטַב לִיתֵּיב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Whatever he gives** the injured party as payment **he must give him of the best** of that type. For example, even if he pays in bran he must pay with his best-quality bran, and this is the meaning of the phrase "of the best of his field." Accordingly, the contradiction is resolved. The term "he shall recompense" indicates that payment can be given in any form, but the phrase "of the best of his field" indicates only that whichever form of payment is used, it must be of a superior quality, and not, as Abaye initially understood it, as limiting the form of payment specifically to land of superior quality.

Rava proposes: *kol d'yavlei mi-meitav leis-vei lei* — whatever category of property the *mazik* chooses to pay with, he must pay the best of that category. You may pay in bran — but it must be the finest bran. You may pay in paperclips — but give the best paperclips.

### Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ״ כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't "the best of his field" written,** which suggests that the requirement to pay with one's best land applies only when one pays with land?

The *Gemara* immediately rejects this: *u'meitav sadehu kesiv* — the verse says "best *field*." There is no textual basis for a principle of "best of whatever genre you choose." The Torah's *meitav* is specifically tied to land, not to a general principle of quality-maximization within any medium of payment.

### Pappa Huna Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי אֲתָא רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מִבֵּי רַב, פָּרְשׁוּהָ: כֹּל מִילֵּי – מֵיטַב הוּא, דְּאִי לָא מִזְדַּבַּן הָכָּא – מִזְדַּבַּן בְּמָתָא אַחֲרִיתִי; לְבַר מֵאַרְעָא – דְּלִיתֵּיב לֵיהּ מִמֵּיטַב, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִקְפּוֹץ עֲלַהּ זָבוֹנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, when Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, came from Rav's academy, they explained it** as follows: With regard to payment, **all items are** classified as property of the **best** quality, **as, if** an item **cannot be sold here, it can be sold in another city.** Since movable items are easily liquidated, they are always considered an acceptable form of payment. This is **with the exception of land,** which is not always easily sold. Therefore, the *halakha* is that the one liable for the damage **must give** the injured party payment **from his best**-quality land, which is easier to sell. This is **in order** to ensure the possibility **that a buyer will jump at** the opportunity to purchase **it,** thereby providing the injured party with the possibility of liquidating it. This resolves the contradiction. The phrase "of the best of his field" indicates that if payment is made with land it must be with superior-quality land, and the term "he shall recompense" indicates that if payment is made from movable property, anything worth money may be used.

When Rav Pappa and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua returned from the academy (*bei Rav*), they provided the definitive resolution: *perushuha — kol mili meitav hu* — regarding all chattel, anything is considered *meitav*. There is no contradiction. *Meitav* is demanded for land; for chattel, any item of equivalent value is inherently considered *meitav*.

### Why Land Differs

The reasoning: *d'ilu lo mizdaban hacha mizdabin b'masa achrisei* — if you cannot sell chattel here, you can transport it to another town and sell it there. Chattel will always find a market somewhere; the *nizak* need not accept any disadvantage. Therefore there is no need to insist on higher quality — any chattel, regardless of grade, is by definition *meitav* in the sense that it is realizable. *L'var mi-ara* — this is not true of land. Land has no portability. Its pool of potential buyers is small and geographically fixed. *D'leis lei mi-meitav* — that is why for land, only high-quality land satisfies the obligation. *Ke-hichi d'likfots alei zavina* — the entire point is to give the *nizak* something that buyers will jump to acquire. Only premium land guarantees that. Any chattel, by contrast, can always find its market.

### Loan Exception

This resolution raises a further question regarding loans. Tosafot discusses the case in several places: if someone borrowed one hundred *zuz* in cash, may the borrower repay in chattel — say, paperclips of equivalent value — and instruct the lender to go find a buyer? The position of Tosafot is that by a standard cash loan, the lender gave cash and is entitled to cash back. The *nizak* in tort cases accepted the risk of an unusual form of restitution, but a lender who extended coin does not forfeit his right to receive coin in return. The principle that *kol mili meitav hu* — that any chattel is treated as the equivalent of *meitav* — applies to debts arising from damages and similar obligations, not to a loan of money, where the original transaction was denominated in cash.

## Objective vs. Subjective

### The Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַבָּא מֵאַקְרוֹנְיָא – מֵרַבִּי אַבָּא: כְּשֶׁהֵן שָׁמִין – בְּשֶׁלּוֹ הֵן שָׁמִין, אוֹ בְּשֶׁל עוֹלָם הֵן שָׁמִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Abba from Akronya asked Rabbi Abba: When** the court **appraises** land to determine if it is to be classified as being of superior-quality, does **it appraise** land **based on** the quality of a property owner's other land **or** does **it appraise** it **based on** the quality of the land in **the world** at large?

Rav Shmuel bar Abba from Akrunya posed a question to Rabbi Abba: *k'shehen shamin, beshelahen shamin o beshel olam shamin?* When we assess *meitav* — when we say the *mazik* must pay from his best land — do we measure "best" against the *mazik*'s own holdings, or do we invoke a universal, objective standard?

The question has practical force only in one direction. If the *mazik* owns only grade-B and grade-C land, he obviously cannot be compelled to produce grade-A land he does not have. The question arises when the *mazik* owns land that *exceeds* the universal standard for grade A — say, he holds prime Manhattan real estate while the rest of the world considers wheat farmland in Kansas to be "grade A." Would we demand that he pay from his prime Manhattan holdings (subjective best), or would we assess the universal grade A and he pays land of that quality (objective standard)?

### Yishmael's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל לָא תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּאָמַר בִּדְנִיזָּק שָׁיְימִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Do not raise the dilemma in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, as he says** that **we appraise** land **based on** the quality of land owned by the **injured** party, and therefore payment is made with land that is of a similar quality to the best-quality land of the injured party. Accordingly, it is obvious that the classification is based only on the land of the injured party.

According to Rabbi Yishmael, there is no question. He holds *meitav d'nizak* — the assessment is based on the *nizak*'s best land, so obviously one looks at the *nizak*. The *mazik*'s personal holdings are irrelevant except insofar as he must match the *nizak*'s grade.

### Akiva's Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – דְּאָמַר בִּדְמַזִּיק שָׁיְימִינַן, מַאי? ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּנִיזָּק; אוֹ דִלְמָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּעָלְמָא נָמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **When you raise this dilemma,** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that **we appraise** land **based on** the quality of land owned by **the one liable for the damage,** and he pays with his superior-quality land even if its quality exceeds that of any of the injured party's land. Consequently, according to Rabbi Akiva it is appropriate to ask whether the appraisal is based only on his other land or based on the land in the world at large. **What** is the answer to the dilemma? When **the Merciful One states: "The best of his field"** (Exodus 22:4), does the stress in the verse on "his field" serve **to exclude** only the possibility of appraising land based solely on the value of the land **of the injured** party, but land is appraised based on the quality of land in the world at large? **Or perhaps,** does it serve **to exclude** the possibility of appraising land based on the quality of land **of the world** at large **as well,** and it is sufficient if the land he pays with is his best-quality land?

The question is posed specifically according to Rabbi Akiva, who holds *meitav d'mazik*. When the *Torah* says "his best field," does "his" serve to exclude the *nizak*'s land (directing us to the *mazik*'s holdings generally, at the universal grade-A standard), or does "his" mean specifically *his personal* best — the finest land he actually owns, whatever its absolute quality?

### Pesuk Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, רַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ״, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ בְּשֶׁל עוֹלָם הֵן שָׁמִין?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba **said to him: The Merciful One states: "The best of his field," and you say** the court **appraises** land **based on** the quality of land in **the world** at large? Certainly the term "his field" teaches that it is sufficient that the land be the best of the land of the one liable for the damage.

Rabbi Abba's response is straightforward: *Rachmana amar meitav sadehu* — the Torah says "his best field." *Ve'at amrat beshel olam shamin?* If you want to apply some universal, impersonal standard, how do you account for the possessive "his"? The text is explicit: it is *his* best field — the subjective best of the *mazik* himself. The *mazik*'s finest real estate, whatever its absolute standing in the world's market, is what must be tendered. We will continue from here.
