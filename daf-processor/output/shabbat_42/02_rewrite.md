# Shabbat 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Metzaref & Unintended Ac…

### Tempering Problem Intro

The discussion opens on *daf* 42a, beginning with the second word, *lemaymra*. The background is a *mishnah* that addresses placing cold water into a hot pot or kettle that has been removed from the fire: if the quantity of cold water is large, it will merely warm up, which is permissible; if the quantity is small, the water may reach the point of *bishul*, which is forbidden. That is the *mishnah*'s concern. The *gemara*, however, introduces an additional concern: why isn't placing a large quantity of cold water into an empty, hot pot considered *metzaref* — tempering — the kind of hardening of metal that occurs when heated metal is plunged into cold water? That process constitutes a forbidden act on *Shabbos*, whether biblical or rabbinic, because it is tantamount to fashioning the vessel.

### Two Answers on Tempering

The *gemara* offers two answers. The first answer is that in order to achieve tempering, one would have to fill the vessel completely with cold water, which is more than the case described. The answer the *gemara* primarily focuses on, however, turns on whether we follow *Rabbi Yehuda* or *Rabbi Shimon*. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, the principle of *davar she'eino mitkaven* applies: if one does not intend for a particular outcome, and that outcome is not an inevitable consequence of the action, the act is permissible. Tempering is not one's intention, and it is not a *psik reisha*, so according to *Rabbi Shimon* there is no prohibition. The other answer — applicable even according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, who is stringent regarding *davar she'eino mitkaven* — is that the scenario of the *mishnah* does not actually involve immersing a dry hot vessel into cold water. Rather, the pot that was removed from the fire still contains hot water; one is simply adding cold water to a vessel already full of hot liquid. Under those circumstances, *metzaref* would not apply regardless of intent.

### Shmuel's Metal/Wood Dist…

Having established that *Shmuel*'s answer invoked *Rabbi Shimon*'s principle of *davar she'eino mitkaven*, the *gemara* now asks whether *Shmuel* consistently holds like *Rabbi Shimon*. This is one of the central questions in *Masechet Shabbat*: how do we rule regarding an activity that might give rise to a *melacha* even though that outcome is unintended? If we follow *Rabbi Yehuda*, a great many ordinary activities on *Shabbos* would be prohibited. The *halacha* follows *Rabbi Shimon* that *davar she'eino mitkaven* is permitted — and that is *Shmuel*'s stated position.

The *gemara* raises a tension. The text at the top of 42a reads: *lemaymra d'Shmuel k'Rabbi Shimon svira leh* — this implies *Shmuel* holds like *Rabbi Shimon*. But does he not? — *v'ha amar Shmuel*: *Shmuel* himself ruled that one may extinguish (*mechabin*) a burning coal of metal (*gachelet shel matechet*) in the public domain (*reshut harabbim*) in order that the masses not be injured by it, but one may not do the same for a burning coal of wood (*gachelet shel etz*). This ruling introduces a significant principle: that certain *derabbanan* prohibitions may be violated on *Shabbos* when there is an overriding concern for the welfare of the public. There are various categories of such overriding concerns — pain (*mitzta'er*), even short of risk to life; danger to the public (*hezek d'rabbim*); or the need of a *mitzvah* (*tzorech mitzvah*). In each case, the question is: what threshold of need is sufficient to override a *derabbanan*, and to what degree of prohibition does the dispensation extend? These questions do not always admit of rigorous, categorical answers.

### Charcoal & Kibuy

The case at hand is a burning coal in the *reshut harabbim*. There is no risk to life, but someone who steps on it will suffer a painful burn. *Shmuel* permits extinguishing it to protect the public — but only if it is metal, not wood. What is the reason for this distinction?

For *kibuy* — extinguishing fire — to constitute a biblical *melacha*, the act must be constructive in some positive sense. Merely removing a fire accomplishes nothing creative. The one thing that extinguishing can create is charcoal. Therefore, *kibuy* is a *melacha de'oraita* only when the act of extinguishing produces charcoal. If one extinguishes a burning piece of wood, one has produced charcoal — that is a biblical transgression, which cannot be overridden simply to prevent injury to the public when there is no risk to life. Perhaps with a *shinuy* it could be permitted, but directly and intentionally, no. If, however, one extinguishes a burning hot piece of metal, no charcoal is produced. Whether the metal is actually on fire or merely glowing with heat makes no difference — one is not creating charcoal, and therefore the act is not a *melacha de'oraita*. Since it is only a *derabbanan*, it may be set aside to protect the public from harm.

### Electric Lights & Esh

This distinction has direct implications for the question of turning off an electric light on *Shabbos*. Electric lights — at least incandescent ones — are generally assumed to constitute *esh* (fire), since they generate both heat and light and gradually consume the metal filament. The assumption holds that something qualifies as fire only when it produces both heat and light together. But whether turning off an electric light constitutes *kibuy de'oraita* is a separate question. According to *Shmuel*'s logic regarding *gachelet shel matechet*, since no charcoal is created when an electric light is extinguished, it would appear that turning one off would not be a *de'oraita* violation. This does not mean it is permitted — it remains forbidden — but the nature of the prohibition would appear to be rabbinic rather than biblical. There is in fact some discussion in the *poskim* about precisely this question, though in practice we treat it as if it were a *de'oraita*. Nevertheless, *Shmuel*'s principle does seem to point in that direction.

One might ask whether the distinction between metal and wood is even practically meaningful. After all, a burning hot piece of glowing metal in the *reshut harabbim* is an unusual scenario, perhaps even more so than a burning coal of wood. The sense one gets, however, is that the *gemara* is describing a genuinely possible situation — one might be near a blacksmith's forge, for example, where metal could be heated to a glowing red — even if it is not the most common circumstance.

### Hezek Rabbim Principle

The *gemara* now asks: if *Shmuel* holds like *Rabbi Shimon* that *davar she'eino mitkaven* is permitted, then even in the case of the burning wood, since one is not intending to create charcoal, one should be permitted to extinguish it. How, then, does *Shmuel* forbid extinguishing burning wood?

### Two Debates on Intent

The *gemara*'s answer resolves this by clarifying that *Shmuel* does not treat the two debates as linked. The text reads: *uv'davar she'eino mitkaven savar lah keRabbi Shimon, bimelachah she'einah tzerichah legufah savar lah keRabbi Yehuda* — regarding *davar she'eino mitkaven* he holds like *Rabbi Shimon*; regarding *melacha she'eina tzerichah legufah* he holds like *Rabbi Yehuda*.

These are two distinct debates that should not be conflated. The first debate is: may I perform an action that might — but need not — result in a *melacha*, when that outcome is not my intention? That is *davar she'eino mitkaven*. *Rabbi Shimon* says yes; *Rabbi Yehuda* says no. The second debate concerns a categorically different situation: I am intentionally performing an act, and the act is definitely occurring, but I am not doing it for the defined, canonical purpose of that *melacha*. The paradigmatic example is digging a hole not to create a pit but to extract the dirt. The hole is created; there is no question of an unintended outcome. But the *melacha* of digging is defined as creating a hole, and that is not my purpose. That is *melacha she'eina tzerichah legufah*. Here too, *Rabbi Shimon* is lenient — one is exempt, though it is not outright permitted — and *Rabbi Yehuda* considers it a full violation.

The case of extinguishing burning wood belongs to the second category, not the first. One is intentionally extinguishing the fire. The question is only whether doing so constitutes the *melacha* of *kibuy* as properly defined, since one's purpose is to protect people rather than to produce charcoal. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, since one is not acting for the purpose of charcoal-making, one would be exempt. But *Shmuel* holds like *Rabbi Yehuda* on this point, and therefore considers the act a full violation — which cannot be overridden merely by the concern for public safety when life is not at risk.

This split between the two debates is also the position of the *Rambam*, who rules that *davar she'eino mitkaven* is permitted like *Rabbi Shimon*, but that *melacha she'eina tzerichah legufah* constitutes a full violation like *Rabbi Yehuda*. In general *halacha*, we rule like *Rabbi Shimon* across both debates. Occasionally a *posek* will invoke the *Rambam*'s stricter position on *melacha she'eina tzerichah legufah* as grounds for additional caution, but the operative rule follows *Rabbi Shimon* in both areas.

### Ravina's Broken Glass

*Ravina* now applies the principle established by *Shmuel* — that one may violate a *derabbanan* to protect the public from injury — to additional cases. *Ravina* says: *hilchach* — therefore — if there is a sharp thorn (*kotz*), or more practically, broken glass (*shever*) in the *reshut harabbim*, which poses a genuine risk of injury to the public even without risk to life, one may remove it. Since one would be carrying in the *reshut harabbim*, what may one do? One moves it in increments of less than *arba amos* at a time, so that each individual movement does not constitute a biblical violation of carrying. The cumulative effect is that the glass is cleared, but each act individually is only a *derabbanan*, and that is permitted to protect the public. *U've-charmelis* — if the glass is in a *karmelis*, the situation is even simpler, since carrying in a *karmelis* is entirely a *derabbanan*, and removing the glass in one motion is straightforwardly permitted.

This establishes a vital principle across *hilchos Shabbos*: one may violate a *derabbanan* to prevent *hezek rabbim*, injury to the public. The two examples — extinguishing a metal coal, and clearing broken glass — illustrate both sides of the principle. In the metal coal case, the act of extinguishing is itself not a *de'oraita* (no charcoal is created), so the *derabbanan* is waived. In the case of wood, if one accepts that *melacha she'eina tzerichah legufah* is only rabbinic (as *Rabbi Shimon* holds), then even extinguishing burning wood would be permitted to protect the public — since once the act is reduced to a *derabbanan*, the dispensation for *hezek rabbim* applies.

## Hot/Cold Water Mixing

### Spices in Vessels

The *mishnah* now turns to a related case, though the connection will become clear. The *mishnah* states that one may take cold water and place it into a hot vessel or cup in order to warm it, provided the quantity of cold water is large enough that it will not reach the point of *bishul*. The *gemara* now moves to examine a *baraita* that addresses the mixing of hot and cold water more directly.

### Beit Shammai's Restricti…

The *baraita* reads: *noten adam chamin letoch hatzonen velo hatzonen letoch hachamin — divrei Beit Shammai* — one may pour hot water into cold, but not cold water into hot; that is the position of *Beit Shammai*. *Uveit Hillel omrim* — *Beit Hillel* permits both: whether hot into cold or cold into hot.

### Rashi: Element Hierarchy

Why should the direction of mixing matter at all? *Rashi* and *Tosafot* offer two different explanations. *Rashi* invokes a discussion familiar from *Pesachim* and other tractates — primarily relevant in the context of *kashrut* — about whether we say *ila'ah gavar* (the upper element wins out) or *tata'ah gavar* (the lower element wins out). When one substance is poured into another, the question is which one dominates. *Rashi* applies this principle here: when cold water is poured into hot water, the hot water on the bottom wins out — *tata'ah gavar* — and heats up the cold water, potentially bringing it to the point of *bishul*. When hot water is poured into cold, the cold water on the bottom wins out, cooling off the hot water rather than being heated by it, so there is no concern of cooking. That is why *Beit Shammai* permits hot into cold but forbids cold into hot.

### Tosafot: Majority Princi…

*Tosafot* rejects this explanation entirely and offers a different reading. According to *Tosafot*, the relevant factor is not which element is on top or bottom as a matter of abstract principle, but rather which element constitutes the majority. When one pours liquid into a vessel, the assumption is that the vessel already contains the greater quantity; what one is adding is the smaller amount. Therefore, if one pours a small quantity of cold water into a large amount of hot water, the hot water — being the majority — will heat up the cold water and may cook it. Conversely, if one pours a small amount of hot water into a large amount of cold water, the cold water dominates and cools everything down. There is no general metaphysical principle that the bottom always wins; rather, the bottom wins because it is assumed to be the greater quantity into which the other substance is being poured. This difference between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* has practical implications — the case of tea, for instance, may come out differently depending on which reading one adopts.

### Beit Hillel on Kos

If *Beit Shammai* forbids cold into hot, why does *Beit Hillel* permit it? The *mishnah* answers: *bameh devarim amurim — bekos* — when is that prohibition relevant? Only if the vessel in question is something more serious. In an ordinary cup (*kos*), *Beit Hillel* permits both directions freely. The reason is that a cup is a *kli sheni* — a second vessel, not the vessel in which the water was heated on the fire. And as a general principle that the *gemara* has established and will continue to develop, a *kli sheni* does not cook. Even if the water in the cup is scalding hot and the addition of cold water might theoretically raise its temperature further, since we are not dealing with a *kli rishon*, there is no concern of *bishul*. One may therefore drop cold water, or even an ice cube, into one's scalding hot cup of tea without concern — it is a *kli sheni*, and *kli sheni* does not cook.

### Bath Exception

However, *aval be-ambati* — if one is dealing with a bath (*ambati*), the situation changes. The bath, *Rashi* explains, is a *kli rishon*: water was heated directly in it, over a fire. In that case, *Beit Hillel* concedes to *Beit Shammai*: one may put hot water into the cold bath, but not cold water into the hot bath. Cold water poured into a *kli rishon* containing boiling hot water would be cooked by the surrounding liquid — that is a genuine concern of *bishul* from which even *Beit Hillel* does not exempt one.

*Tosafot*, it should be noted, reads the distinction differently: even if the bath were technically a *kli sheni*, it would still be treated more stringently because bath water is typically heated to a far higher temperature than drinking water, making the risk of cooking more significant in appearance if not in strict law. According to *Rashi*, however, the reason is purely structural — the bath is a *kli rishon*.

### R. Shimon ben Menasya St…

*V'Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya oser* — *Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya* goes further and forbids even placing hot water into the cold bath. *Amar Rav Nachman halacha k'Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya* — *Rav Nachman* rules that the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya*. In the bath, even hot into cold is forbidden.

### Basin Problem

*Rav Yosef* then proposed to extend this ruling: *safal harei hu k'ambati* — a *safal*, a basin used for washing one's hands and face, should be governed by the same *halacha* as the bath. Even though a basin is a *kli sheni* (water is poured into it from a kettle rather than heated directly in it), since it is used for bathing purposes, one might treat it identically to the bath.

*Abaye* immediately objected: *tani Rabbi Chiyya safal eino k'ambati* — *Rabbi Chiyya* has a *baraita* that explicitly states the basin is *not* like the bath. The bath is a *kli rishon*; the basin is a *kli sheni*. One may be strict about the *kli rishon* without extending that stringency to the *kli sheni*.

The *gemara* then doubles back to consider the logical implications of *Rav Yosef*'s initial proposal. Had one accepted both that the basin is like the bath *and* that we rule like *Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya* who forbids even hot into cold in the bath, it would follow that one could never add hot water to cold or cold to hot even in a basin. But then the *gemara* asks pointedly: *ela be-Shabbos v'rechitza be-chamim leika* — how would one ever wash oneself in warm water on *Shabbos* at all? Warm water is, by definition, a mixture of hot and cold. If one cannot add cold to hot or hot to cold in any vessel used for bathing purposes, then bathing with comfortably warm water becomes practically impossible.

This question connects to a broader contemporary issue. In many homes and apartment buildings, the use of hot water on *Shabbos* raises its own concerns. When hot water is drawn from a water heater, cold water flows in to replace it. If the water at the bottom of the tank is at *yad soledet bo* or above, the cold water entering is effectively being cooked — cold water is entering a vessel of *yad soledet bo* hot water, which constitutes *bishul*. In an apartment building where many non-observant residents are simultaneously using hot water on *Shabbos*, one might argue that there is reasonable doubt as to whether the bottom layer of the tank remains at *yad soledet bo* at any given moment, and therefore the concern may not rise to the level of a *psik reisha*. In a private house, however, the analysis is more complicated, since if no one else is using the hot water, the tank remains fully heated and turning on the tap would bring cold water into *yad soledet bo* water, cooking it. Independently, one should note that setting a mixing faucet to the "warm" position does not resolve the problem if using hot water is forbidden — warm water from a single-lever tap is simply a mix of hot and cold drawn from the same pipes.

### Resolution: Kos vs. Bath

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by reinterpreting the ruling of *Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya*. The assumption had been that when *Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya* says *oser*, he is commenting on the latter clause (*seifa*) of the *baraita* — forbidding both hot-into-cold and cold-into-hot in the bath. The *gemara* says: *mi savrat Rabbi Shimon aseifa kai?* — why should you assume that? *Areisha kai* — he is commenting on the first clause (*reisha*), specifically on the position of *Beit Hillel* that in a *kos*, a second vessel, both directions are permitted. *Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya* says *oser*: even in a *kos*, one may not pour cold into hot. His stringency is not about the bath — it is about the cup. He is, in effect, agreeing with *Beit Shammai* regarding the *kos*.

*Leima Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya d'amar k'Beit Shammai* — the *gemara* notes this is exactly what *Beit Shammai* said: even in a second vessel, cold into hot is forbidden. Once this is understood, the practical picture becomes clear and workable. A *kli rishon* presents a genuine concern for *bishul* — one may not add cold water to the hot bath. A *kli sheni*, whether a cup or a basin, does not cook, and one may mix freely in either direction. The bath (*ambati*), being a *kli rishon*, follows *Beit Hillel*'s concession to *Beit Shammai*: hot into cold is permitted, cold into hot is not. But the basin (*safal*), being a *kli sheni*, is not like the bath, and one may use it freely.

### Rav Huna on Rava

*Amar Rav Huna brei de'Rav Yehoshua*: I observed *Rava*, *d'lo kapid amana* — he was not concerned about the vessel, meaning he was not particular about which direction he mixed. The *gemara* proves this from *Rabbi Chiyya*'s *baraita*: *noten adam kiton shel mayim l'toch safal shel mayim bein chamin l'toch tzonen bein tzonen l'toch chamin* — one may pour a flask of water into a basin of water, whether hot into cold or cold into hot. *Rav Huna* then questioned whether *Rava*'s leniency could be proven from there: *dilma shani hatam d'mifsek kli* — perhaps the case is different because the flask containing cold water was merely placed into the hot water in the basin, with the vessel itself acting as a barrier preventing direct mixing; the likelihood of heating would be much less than direct pouring. But *Rav Huna* was told: *me'areh itmar* — the *baraita* in fact speaks of *pouring*, not of submerging: *me'areh adam kiton shel mayim l'toch safal shel mayim bein chamin l'toch tzonen bein tzonen l'toch chamin* — one may pour a flask into a basin in either direction. This reaffirms without qualification that as long as one is not dealing with a *kli rishon*, mixing hot and cold in either direction is entirely permissible.

### Kli Sheni Principle

The conclusion is crisp and fundamental: a *kli sheni* does not cook. Whether it is a cup, a bowl, or a basin; whether one pours hot into cold or cold into hot — as long as the vessel is not the *kli rishon* that was on the fire, there is no concern of *bishul*, even if the liquid in question reaches *yad soledet bo*. If the vessel in question is a *kli rishon* — such as the bath directly heated over fire — then the directional concern of *Beit Shammai* (and ultimately *Beit Hillel*'s concession) applies, and one may not pour cold water into hot. This *kli sheni* principle will now serve as the foundation for the discussion that follows.

## Spices & Bishul

### Spices Mishnah

The *mishnah* states: *alpas u'kedeira she'he'eviran merotchin* — a pan or a pot (*alpas* and *kedeira*, both vessel types that sit directly on a fire) that one has removed from the flame while the contents were still boiling. *Lo yiten letochan tavlin* — one may not add spices to them at that point. Even though the pot is no longer on the fire, the liquid inside is a *kli rishon* and is boiling hot (*rotei'ach*), and adding spices would constitute *bishul*. *Aval noten hu letoch haka'arah o letoch hatamchui* — however, one may add spices once the soup has been poured into a bowl (*ka'arah*) or a serving tureen (*tamchui*). These are *kli sheni* vessels, and as the principle established above dictates, a *kli sheni* does not cook.

Two clarifications are in order. First, what is the status of adding spices directly to the pot even though it has been removed from the fire? Almost all *rishonim* hold, based on earlier *gemara* discussions (including the case of the egg hard-boiled by proximity to a kettle even without direct flame), that a *kli rishon* — even off the fire — retains sufficient heat to cook things placed into it, and doing so would constitute a biblical transgression. The *Ramban* (Nachmanides) is a notable exception, arguing that actual *bishul de'oraita* requires placing something directly on the fire; a *kli rishon* removed from the fire would be only a *derabbanan* violation. The overwhelming consensus of *rishonim* rejects this position and holds that a *kli rishon* off the fire remains capable of biblical cooking.

Second, the *mishnah* describes the liquid as *rotei'ach* — boiling — which is a significantly higher temperature than *yad soledet bo*. *Rashi* on this *mishnah* (at the bottom of 42a) makes this explicit: *mi-shete'shach de-kli rishon kol zeman she-rote'ach mevashel* — a *kli rishon* cooks other things for as long as its contents are boiling. This might suggest that a *kli rishon* only cooks other things when the liquid is *rotei'ach*, not merely *yad soledet bo*. In practice, however, the *halacha* assumes that a *kli rishon* at *yad soledet bo* — even off the fire — can cook whatever is placed into it. The *mishnah*'s description of the liquid as *rotei'ach* simply reflects the common scenario; it does not restrict the prohibition to that threshold alone.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

*Rabbi Yehuda* in the *mishnah* takes a more lenient position, at least regarding a *kli rishon* off the fire: *lekhol hu noten chutz mi-davar she-yesh bo chometz ve-tzi'ir* — one may add spices to anything, except something that contains vinegar (*chometz*) or fish brine (*tzi'ir*), which somehow accelerates cooking. Before analyzing *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position, a brief methodological note: when the *mishnah* and *gemara* speak of *tavlin* (spices), they typically mean larger, more substantial items — onions, garlic, and similar ingredients — not simply powdered condiments. This is worth noting because the question of what constitutes *bishul* is itself subtle. When one places onions or garlic into hot soup, one is genuinely transforming those ingredients through heat. But when one adds spice powders or salt, the situation is less clear — one may be extracting flavor or causing dissolution rather than inducing a fundamental chemical transformation. This distinction matters, and it will resurface shortly in the discussion of tea.

### Debate on R. Yehuda

The *gemara* raises the question: when *Rabbi Yehuda* says one may add spices to *any* pan or pot — is he being lenient regarding the *reisha* (the *kli rishon* scenario), permitting one to add spices even to a boiling pot that is a *kli rishon* as long as it is off the fire and contains no vinegar or brine? Or is he being stringent on the *seifa* (the *kli sheni* scenario), agreeing with the *tanna kamma* that the *kli rishon* cooks, but adding that even a *kli sheni* will cook if it contains vinegar or brine?

The *gemara* resolves this with a *baraita*: *Rabbi Yehuda omer: le-chol ilfsin hu noten, le-chol kederot hu noten, chutz mi-davar she-yesh bo chomets ve-tsir* — *Rabbi Yehuda* says one may add to any pan, to any pot, except something with vinegar or brine. The fact that he refers to *ilfsin* and *kederot* — the *kli rishon* pots themselves — indicates that he is commenting on the *kli rishon* scenario and being lenient: a *kli rishon* off the fire, according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, will not cook spices unless the liquid also contains vinegar or brine. *Tosafot* notes that one might question whether *Rabbi Yehuda* truly means that nothing cooks in a *kli rishon* off the fire except in the presence of vinegar or brine, or whether he intends this ruling specifically for spices (which perhaps require an activating agent to cook in such conditions). Either way, *Rabbi Yehuda* is significantly more lenient than the *tanna kamma* regarding *kli rishon* off the fire.

### Salt Cooks in Kli Sheni

*Amar leh Abaye: tani Rabbi Chiyya — melach eina ke-tavlin*. Here the *gemara* introduces a distinction that will have sweeping consequences: salt (*melach*) is not like other spices (*tavlin*). *De-kli sheni nami mevashla* — salt cooks even in a *kli sheni*. The ordinary principle that a *kli sheni* does not cook, according to *Rabbi Chiyya*'s teaching as cited by *Abaye*, does not apply to salt, which is said to dissolve and cook even outside the primary vessel.

*U-pliga de-Rav Nachman* — this directly contradicts the position of *Rav Nachman*. *De-amar Rav Nachman: tsricha milcha bishula ke-bisra de-tura* — *Rav Nachman* holds that salt requires as much cooking as the meat of an ox. Just as a substantial piece of beef requires prolonged, intense heat to cook through — direct fire for an extended period — so too does salt require exceptional conditions to be considered "cooked." *Rav Nachman* thus rules not that salt is hard to cook in a *kli sheni*, but that salt is hard to cook at all.

### Salt Needs Much Heat

The practical reality underlying *Rav Nachman*'s position is worth appreciating. The salt available in ancient times likely consisted of large chunks of rock salt or minimally processed sea salt. Whether or not such salt undergoes a meaningful chemical transformation when placed in hot liquid — comparable to the transformation of protein bonds in meat — is itself a question. Salt dissolving into water is not the same kind of reaction as meat cooking; it is dissociation, not a fundamental alteration of state. Understood in this light, *Rav Nachman*'s statement that salt requires cooking equivalent to a side of beef may be understood as expressing that salt does not cook in any ordinary sense at all.

### Salt Requires Fire

*Abaye* then presents the second version of *Rabbi Chiyya*'s teaching, which resolves the apparent conflict: *melach eina k'tavlin — d'bi kli rishon nami lo beshala* — salt is not like spices precisely because it does not cook even in a *kli rishon*. This is why *Rav Nachman* says it requires cooking like a side of beef — not to say that salt cooks given sufficient time in a *kli rishon*, but rather that salt requires even more than a *kli rishon* can provide; it needs to be directly over the fire for a sustained period, just as tough meat does. According to this reading, both *Rabbi Chiyya* and *Rav Nachman* are saying the same thing: salt does not cook in a *kli rishon* off the fire, and it certainly does not cook in a *kli sheni*.

### Kalei Habishul Category

This *gemara* is of extraordinary significance in *hilchos Shabbos*, and it is worth pausing to appreciate both of its main positions and their implications.

The second version — *Rav Nachman*'s position, which the *gemara* ultimately affirms — maintains a clean, formal structure: *kli rishon* cooks; *kli sheni* does not. Some items, like a side of beef or (in a different sense) salt, require even more than a *kli rishon* can provide off the fire. The line is firm.

But the first version — the initial statement of *Abaye* citing *Rabbi Chiyya* — opened the door to something quite different: the possibility that salt cooks in a *kli sheni*, because it cooks more readily than ordinary spices. If salt can cook in a *kli sheni*, what else might? The answer is: potentially many things. From this emerges the category of *kallei habishul* — items that cook easily — and the attendant question of whether the *kli sheni* principle can be relied upon when dealing with such items. Even though the *gemara* apparently rejects the first version, the fact that it was raised as a possibility means that the door was opened, however slightly, and it was not simply dismissed out of hand.

### Modern Halachic Approach…

Contemporary *halacha* offers essentially three approaches to the *kli sheni* question, rooted in the *gemara*'s discussion.

The first approach holds that a *kli sheni* never cooks, period. The *gemara* teaches this as an absolute rule, and the salt discussion at most introduced a rejected possibility that should not alter settled law. This is the most lenient view and reflects a straightforward reading of the *gemara*'s conclusion.

The second approach — associated with the *Chazon Ish* — acknowledges the *gemara*'s *kli sheni* principle as the general rule but insists that items demonstrably in the category of *kallei habishul* (things that evidently cook even with minimal heat) must be treated more stringently, even in a *kli sheni*. The presumption remains that *kli sheni* does not cook; the exception is reserved for cases where it is quite clear that a particular item cooks very easily.

The third and most stringent approach — reflected in the *Mishnah Berurah* and *Magen Avraham* and widely followed in practice — holds that one must treat every item as a potential *kallei habishul* unless the *gemara* explicitly classifies it as one of the items that do not cook in a *kli sheni*. The *gemara* gives us, effectively, three clear exemptions: water (or any liquid that was the subject of the earlier discussion about *kli sheni*), *tavlin* (spices, as the *mishnah* states), and very tough meat (which requires direct fire). Everything else must be regarded with suspicion, and one cannot rely on the *kli sheni* exemption. On this view, so much of what we do on *Shabbos* with food and beverages becomes subject to scrutiny.

### Tea Question

The tea question illustrates the stakes of these positions with particular clarity. May one steep tea — whether loose leaf or a tea bag — in a *kli sheni* or *kli shlishi*? Some argue that tea should be classified as *tavlin* (spices), since the *mishnah* permits adding spices to a *kli sheni* freely. Others argue that tea is clearly in the category of *kallei habishul*, since one can observe that it "cooks" — or at least produces its characteristic color and flavor — even in vessels far removed from the original heat source.

*Rav Soloveitchik* addressed this directly and argued that what happens with tea is not *bishul* at all. *Bishul* involves the application of heat to transform an object — to change its chemical or physical state, to render it edible or alter its taste through that transformation. When one steeps tea, one is not cooking the tea leaves; one is extracting flavor from them. The tea leaf itself is not transformed into something new; the flavor compounds are drawn out into the hot water. Critically, this extraction occurs in cold water as well — one can make cold-brew tea without any heat at all. The fact that flavor is extracted in cold water demonstrates that what is happening is not a heat-dependent transformation (which is the essence of *bishul*) but rather a dissolution or extraction process. Therefore, even if tea is placed in a *kli sheni* or *kli shlishi*, there is no *bishul*.

This argument also prompts a broader reflection about what *bishul* of spices means in general. When one adds spices to hot soup, one is — depending on the spice — either causing them to dissolve, extracting their flavor compounds, or, in the case of something like onions or garlic, genuinely cooking and transforming the vegetable. The *gemara*'s use of *tavlin* primarily in the sense of substantial ingredients like onions and garlic is therefore not incidental. Whether one is truly cooking a spice powder or merely dissolving it or extracting it is a question the *gemara* does not fully unpack, though it is latent in the discussion.

*Rav Moshe Feinstein* was asked whether the degree of heat in the *kli sheni* or *kli shlishi* matters: if the water is at full boiling (*rotei'ach*) as opposed to merely *yad soledet bo*, should one be more stringent even in a *kli shlishi*? *Rav Moshe* rejected this distinction. The *gemara* established formal categories — *kli rishon* and *kli sheni* — and the degree of heat within the *kli sheni* does not change those categories. Nevertheless, the opening created by the first version of the salt *gemara* — however apparently rejected — continues to generate what the *gemara* itself might call a field day of questions: what counts as *tavlin*? Does the number of vessels (*kli sheni*, *kli shlishi*, *kli revii*) matter? Does the temperature within the *kli sheni* affect anything? None of these questions has an indisputable answer rooted in an explicit *gemara*, and so the discussion remains open — all of it traceable back to this one passage about salt.

## Vessels & Muktzah

### Oil Dripping Rule

The final *mishnah* of the *daf* shifts from *bishul* concerns to *muktzah*. The *mishnah* states: *ein nosnin kli tachat haner lekabel bo et hashemen* — one may not place a vessel underneath a lamp in order to catch the oil dripping from it.

### Two Explanations

Two reasons are offered for this prohibition. The first is the principle of *ein mivattlin kli mehikhano*: one may not render a functional vessel unusable on *Shabbos*. The vessel in question is perfectly usable; by positioning it to catch *muktzah* oil — oil that has been designated for burning in the lamp and is therefore set aside — one effectively makes the vessel into a receptacle for *muktzah* and thereby renders it unusable on *Shabbos*. The second explanation invokes a different principle: one may not move a non-*muktzah* object for the sake of a *muktzah* object — *ein kli nital ela litzorekh davar hanital*. Since the oil is *muktzah*, placing the vessel to catch it amounts to moving a permissible item for the sake of a forbidden one.

### Pre-Shabbat Exception

*Ve'im nesanu mibe'od yom mutar* — if the vessel was already placed beneath the lamp before *Shabbos* began, it is permitted. The vessel was designated for this purpose before *Shabbos*; it is therefore not being newly rendered unusable on *Shabbos* itself, and the act of catching the oil occurs passively, without any active placement on *Shabbos*.

### Egg Catching

*Amar Rav Chisda*: although one may not place a vessel beneath a lamp to catch the oil, one may not place a vessel under a hen (*tarnigolet*) to catch its egg either. A freshly laid egg is *nolad* — it came into existence on *Shabbos* itself — and is therefore *muktzah*. Catching it in a vessel would render the vessel a receptacle for *muktzah*.

### On Top vs. Under

However, *aval kofeh aleha kli shelo tishaver* — one may place the vessel *on top of* the egg rather than beneath it. The reason is precise: when a vessel sits on top of an egg, it is not "holding" the egg; the egg is not resting in or on the vessel. An object that holds *muktzah* is called a *basis* and is itself forbidden to move. But a vessel merely placed over an egg — covering and protecting it — does not bear the egg in any meaningful sense, and therefore does not acquire the status of *basis*. The vessel remains movable; only the egg is *muktzah*.

### Common vs. Rare Acts

*Amar Rava*: what is the underlying reasoning of *Rav Chisda*? *Kasavar: tarnigolet asuya lehapil beitsatah be'ashpah* — a hen typically drops its egg on the ground, in a random location, not in a carefully arranged spot. People may walk over such an egg and step on it; covering it with a vessel to protect it from being crushed is the normal, expected protective measure in a common circumstance. *Ve'eina asuya lehapil beitsatah bimkom midron* — but it is not typical for a hen to lay her egg on a slope or incline such that the egg would roll away and fall. Placing a vessel *beneath* the egg to catch it as it rolls would address that rare circumstance.

*Rava* thus explains that the distinction between placing a vessel on top of the egg (permitted) and beneath it (forbidden) is not simply about *basis* status, though that consideration is present. More fundamentally, the *gemara* establishes a principle: *hatzalah metziuyah hitiru, hatzalah she'einah metziuyah lo hitiru* — the Sages permitted one to take protective action in common circumstances, but not for rare ones. Saving the egg from being stepped on is a common concern; saving it from rolling down an incline is not. It is only for the former that one is permitted to handle the vessel in a way that might otherwise be restricted.
