# Pesachim 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oven & Forbidden Wood

### Setup: Orlah/Kilayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תַּנּוּר שֶׁהִסִּיקוֹ בִּקְלִיפֵּי עׇרְלָה אוֹ בְּקַשִּׁין שֶׁל כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם, חָדָשׁ — יוּתַּץ, יָשָׁן — יוּצַן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an oven that one lit with the peels of *orla*** fruit, **or with straw** of grain that was planted in a prohibited mixture **of diverse kinds in a vineyard,** if it was a **new** oven, and by lighting it he hardened the oven and made it stronger for use in the future, then **it** must **be shattered.** Since prohibited items were used in the process of forming the oven, one may not derive benefit from the use of the prohibited items. However, if it was an **old** oven, **it** may **be cooled,** and it is prohibited to use the oven only while it is still hot.

The previous daf concluded with a rich discussion of *hana'ah* — specifically the question of whether benefit that comes upon a person passively, without any deliberate action, constitutes forbidden *hana'ah*, and the related question of what categories of sensory experience even qualify. Auditory pleasure (*kol*) and visual pleasure (*mareh*) are not considered biblically significant forms of *hana'ah*, though they may be rabbinically restricted; olfactory pleasure (*rei'ach*), by contrast, is treated as more substantive and physical, and is indeed considered a genuine concern.

We continue now on 27a with a *sugya* that is closely related to these *hana'ah* themes. The *mishna* had stated that one may not use *chametz* to heat up an oven — burning *chametz* to bake bread or cook food constitutes a forbidden form of benefit. The *Gemara* broadens this discussion by citing a *Tosefta* (*tanu rabbanan*) that applies analogous reasoning to other items that are *assur be-hana'ah*.

The case is: *tanur she-hisiko bi-klipei orlah* — an oven that was fired using shells or wood from *orlah* (fruit from a tree's first three years, which is forbidden in benefit), *o be-kashim shel kilei ha-kerem* — or with stalks from *kilayei ha-kerem* (a forbidden mixture of grain sown in a vineyard). Both *orlah* and *kilayim* are classic categories of *issurei hana'ah*, and the *Tosefta* applies to them the same concerns raised by the *mishna* regarding *chametz*.

### New Oven Hardening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָדָשׁ — יוּתַּץ
>
> **Translation:** if it was a **new** oven, **it** must **be shattered.**

The *Tosefta* distinguishes between a new oven (*chadash*) and an old oven (*yashan*). If the oven is new, the initial firing serves as a kind of kiln process: it hardens the pottery and permanently transforms the vessel. By using wood of *issurei hana'ah* for this purpose, one has derived a concrete and lasting benefit — the oven itself has been fashioned and completed through the forbidden material. The *Tosefta* therefore rules *chadash yutzat* — the new oven must be destroyed.

One might have argued otherwise: the prohibited act of firing occurred once and is over; going forward, one is merely using the oven, not the *issurei hana'ah* themselves. But this position rejects that reasoning. The continued use of the oven is viewed as a continued, indirect derivation of benefit from those *issurei hana'ah* — one would never be cooking in this oven today were it not for the fact that the forbidden wood hardened it in the first place. The wood accomplished a *gmar ma'aseh*, a completed constructive act, and every subsequent use remains connected to that initial benefit.

### Old Oven Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָשָׁן — יוּצַן.
>
> **Translation:** if it was an **old** oven, **it** may **be cooled.**

If the oven is old (*yashan*), the analysis differs. An old oven does not require that initial kiln-firing; it is already functional. The current fire, however, is itself derived from *issurei hana'ah*, and one may not benefit from that active fire. The ruling is therefore *yutzan* — let it cool. Once the fire has gone out completely, the oven may be used again, provided that any subsequent fire comes from permitted wood. The oven itself, having not been fundamentally altered or constituted by the forbidden fire, does not require destruction.

## Bread Dispute

### Rebbi: Bread Assur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָפָה בּוֹ אֶת הַפַּת, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: הַפַּת אֲסוּרָה
>
> **Translation:** If **one baked bread** with *asheira* wood as the fuel, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: It is prohibited** to eat or derive **benefit from it.**

The *Tosefta* continues: *afah bo et ha-pat* — suppose one went ahead and baked bread in this oven while the forbidden fire was still active. *Rebbi omer: ha-pat asurah* — Rebbi holds that the bread is forbidden. This seems intuitive: if one cannot derive benefit from an oven hardened by *issurei hana'ah*, one certainly cannot eat bread baked through them.

### Chachamim: Bread Mutar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַפַּת מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: It is permitted** to eat and derive benefit from **the bread.**

*Ve-Chachamim omrim: ha-pat muteret* — the Sages, however, rule that the bread is permitted. Their position is that although an act of transgression may have occurred in the baking, once the bread has been baked, eating it does not constitute a new or ongoing act of *hana'ah* from the *issurei hana'ah*. The forbidden benefit has already been derived; continuing to eat the bread is not a further violation.

### Continued Benefit Logic

The underlying conceptual question is whether eating the bread constitutes a *continued* deriving of benefit from the *issurei hana'ah*, or whether the moment of baking was the singular act of *hana'ah* and what follows is simply using bread. Logically, one might assume that the Sages would apply the same reasoning to the oven — if the benefit was exhausted in the act of hardening, then continued use of the oven should similarly be permissible. The *Gemara* will indeed draw this connection.

## Oven & Forbidden Wood…

### Coals Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּישְּׁלָהּ עַל גַּבֵּי גֶּחָלִים — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** If **he cooked** the bread **over the coals** that remained from prohibited wood, **everyone agrees** that **it is permitted.**

The *Tosefta* contains a further provision: *bishra agavi gechali* — if the wood has already turned to coals before the bread is baked, then *divrei hakol mutar* — everyone agrees the bread is permissible. Once the wood has fully combusted into charcoal, it is no longer considered *orlah* or *kilayei ha-kerem*; the forbidden material has been destroyed and no longer exists as such. At that stage, it may even be permitted *le-chatchilah* to bake the bread.

### Nisrafim Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַנִּשְׂרָפִין — אֶפְרָן מוּתָּר, חוּץ מֵעֲצֵי אֲשֵׁירָה. וְאֵפֶר הֶקְדֵּשׁ — לְעוֹלָם אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **all** prohibited items **that must be burned, their ashes are permitted** after the burning, **except for wood from an *asheira*? And consecrated ash is prohibited forever.**

This ruling is not as self-evident as it might appear. The *Ran* notes that the question of when *issurei hana'ah* lose their status is a substantive one, and the *Gemara* articulates the governing principle at the close of today's *daf*: *kol ha-nisrafim afaran mutar* — anything that the *halacha* prescribes to be burned, its ashes are permissible; *kol ha-nikbarim afaran assur* — anything for which burial is the designated mode of disposal, its ashes remain forbidden even after burning.

The logic is tied to the concept of *na'aseit mitzvato* — once the prescribed *mitzvah* has been performed upon an object, it loses its forbidden status. For *kilayei ha-kerem*, the *Gemara* derives from the verse *pen tikdash* (Devarim 22:9) the requirement to burn it — *tukad aish* — and therefore, once burned, the *mitzvah* has been fulfilled and the object becomes permissible. The same principle applies to *orlah*.

By contrast, a *shor ha-niskal* (an ox condemned to be stoned) has no prescribed act of burning; even if one chooses to burn it, the act does not trigger *na'aseit mitzvato*, and the ashes remain forbidden. The *Ran* explicitly limits the ruling about coals to *kilayim* and *orlah*, where burning is itself the commanded act. Other *issurei hana'ah* — including *chametz be-Pesach*, whose method of destruction is a separate discussion — may not share this leniency.

It should be noted that *na'aseit mitzvato* can operate even without a complete physical transformation. By analogy: *zerikat ha-dam* at the *mizbe'ach* does not visibly alter the blood, yet it renders the offering permissible because the *mitzvah* has been fulfilled. In principle, even if the prescribed act were accomplished before the substance became fully unrecognizable, *na'aseit mitzvato* would be sufficient to permit it.

The distinction between *kilayei ha-kerem* specifically — and not *kilayei zera'im* — should be noted; it is the former to which the *drasha* of *pen tikdash* / *tukad aish* applies.

## Zeh V'Zeh Gorem

### Oven & Fire Join

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹ דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מִשּׁוּם דְּיֵשׁ שֶׁבַח עֵצִים בַּפַּת. זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **when did we actually hear Rebbi say the bread is forbidden? because the benefit of the wood remains present in the bread. The wood baked the bread, and that contribution (*shevach etzim*) is still considered to inhere in the bread; eating it is therefore still a form of deriving benefit from the *issurei hana'ah*. But that reasoning applies cleanly to the bread, where the only contributor is the wood. The oven case is more complex: did we ever hear that Rebbi holds that *zeh va-zeh gorem* — two things jointly contributing — constitutes a problem?**

The *Gemara* now considers whether the two positions in the *Tosefta* regarding the oven — one requiring the destruction of a new oven, the other permitting continued use — map onto the dispute between Rebbi and the *Chachamim* regarding the bread. The *Gemara* suggests: *ha Rebbi, ha Rabbanan* — one position reflects Rebbi's view and the other the Sages'.

But the *Gemara* resists this too quickly. *Eima de-shamat lei le-Rabbi* — when did we actually hear Rebbi say the bread is forbidden? *Mishum de-yesh shevach eitzim be-pat* — because the benefit of the wood remains present in the bread. The wood baked the bread, and that contribution (*shevach etzim*) is still considered to inhere in the bread; eating it is therefore still a form of deriving benefit from the *issurei hana'ah*.

But that reasoning applies cleanly to the bread, where the only contributor is the wood. The oven case is more complex: *zeh va-zeh gorem mi-shamat lei* — did we ever hear that Rebbi holds that *zeh va-zeh gorem* — two things jointly contributing — constitutes a problem? When one bakes bread in an oven, two things are contributing to the baking: the oven and the fire. The benefit being derived is not traceable exclusively to the *issurei hana'ah*; it is a joint product. Perhaps even Rebbi would not say that a dual-contributor scenario creates a prohibition.

This introduces the central conceptual question of *zeh va-zeh gorem*: when two things jointly bring about a result and one of them is forbidden, does the forbidden element's participation make the outcome prohibited? Or does the presence of the permitted contributor sufficiently dilute the problem?

### Sourdough: Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דַּעֲצֵי אֲשֵׁירָה, דִּתְנַן: נָטַל הֵימֶנָּה עֵצִים — אֲסוּרִין בַּהֲנָאָה. הִסִּיק בָּהֶן אֶת הַתַּנּוּר, חָדָשׁ — יוּתַּץ, יָשָׁן — יוּצַן. אָפָה בּוֹ אֶת הַפַּת — אֲסוּרִין בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the reference is to the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer** with regard to **wood from an *asheira*. As we learned** in a mishna: If **one took wood from** an *asheira*, **it is prohibited to** derive **benefit from it.** With regard to **one** who **lit an oven with** the wood, if it was a **new** oven, **it** must **be broken.** If it was an **old** oven, **it** may **be cooled.** If **one baked bread** with *asheira* wood as the fuel, **it is prohibited to** derive **benefit from it.**

The *Gemara* therefore looks for a *Tanna* who explicitly rules that *zeh va-zeh gorem* is forbidden. *Ela lo kashya: ha Rabbi Eliezer, ha Rabbanan* — let us say the debate about the oven reflects the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages.

*D'tnan* — the *mishna* presents the following case: *seor shel chullin ve-shel terumah she-naflu le-toch issah* — sourdough starter of ordinary produce (*chullin*) and sourdough starter of *terumah* both fell into a batch of dough. *Ve-ein be-zeh ke-dei le-hachmitz ve-ein be-zeh ke-dei le-hachmitz* — neither on its own contains enough to cause the dough to ferment. *Ve-nitztarfu ve-chimtzu* — but together they caused fermentation. The analogy to *davar ha-mamid* (a substance that gives form or structure) is instructive: even a small quantity that has a transformative effect may retain its forbidden status regardless of its proportional insignificance.

*Rabbi Eliezer omer: achar acharon ani ba* — Rabbi Eliezer says one follows the last element added. His reasoning is sequential: whatever fell in first had no effect on its own; the dough was non-fermenting until the final element was added, and therefore that final element is the operative cause. If the last element added was *chullin*, the *terumah* is not considered to have caused the fermentation, and the bread is permissible. *Ve-Chachamim omrim* — the Sages say it makes no difference whether the *issur* fell in first or last; in neither case did the forbidden element cause the fermentation alone, and therefore *zeh va-zeh gorem* is permissible.

### Abaye: Issur Present

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁקָּדַם וְסִילֵּק אֶת הָאִיסּוּר, אֲבָל לֹא קָדַם וְסִילֵּק אֶת הָאִיסּוּר — אָסוּר, אַלְמָא זֶה וְזֶה גּוֹרֵם אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer** taught that when the permitted leaven fell in last, the mixture is permitted **only** when **he first removed the prohibited** leaven before the permitted leaven fell into the dough and made it rise. **However, if he did not first remove the prohibited leaven,** the dough **is prohibited** even if the permitted leaven fell in last. **Apparently,** when **both this and that cause** the dough to become leavened bread, **it is prohibited.**

The *Gemara* notes, however, that Rabbi Eliezer's position is ambiguous. His formulation of "following the last" could mean something other than a ruling on *zeh va-zeh gorem*. Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer would agree that simultaneous contribution (*bat achat*) is permissible, and his concern is only about sequential contribution — specifically, whichever element was last is considered the operative cause, and if that last element was the *issur*, it is problematic. But if the *issur* acted simultaneously with the *heter*, or if the *issur* came first and the *heter* came last, it might be fine — not because of *zeh va-zeh gorem*, but simply because of the "last one" principle.

Abaye therefore proposes a reinterpretation: *lo shanu ela she-kadam ve-silek et ha-issur* — Rabbi Eliezer is lenient only where the *issur* fell in first and was then removed before the *heter* fell in. In that case, the *issur* was no longer present when fermentation occurred, and one can say it contributed nothing to the outcome. But if the *issur* was not removed — if it was still present — then even if it was not the last element added, it is still forbidden, because it is present while the fermentation occurs. On this reading, Rabbi Eliezer's position is that whenever the *issur* remains present, it is a problem — which would mean he holds that *zeh va-zeh gorem* is *assur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּטַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כְּאַבָּיֵי? דִּילְמָא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מִשּׁוּם דְּאַחַר אַחֲרוֹן אֲנִי בָּא — לָא שְׁנָא קָדַם וְסִילֵּק אֶת הָאִיסּוּר, לָא שְׁנָא לֹא קָדַם וְסִילֵּק אֶת הָאִיסּוּר. אֲבָל בְּבַת אַחַת, הָכִי נָמֵי דִּשְׁרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this statement: And **from where** is it apparent **that the reason for Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion **is in accordance with Abaye's** explanation? **Perhaps the reason for Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion **is due to** the following, which Rabbi Eliezer said explicitly: **I follow the final** element. **And it is no different** if **he first removed the prohibited item and it is no different** if **he did not first remove the prohibited item. However,** if they both fell in **at once it should be permitted,** because where both this and that cause the dough to become leavened bread Rabbi Eliezer rules that the mixture is permitted.

*U-mimai de-ta'amei de-Rabbi Eliezer ke-d'Abaye* — but the *Gemara* immediately pushes back: what makes us confident that Abaye's interpretation is correct? The *Gemara* notes, strikingly, that it is prepared to question whether an *Amora*'s reading of a *Tanna* is accurate — a move that is itself unusual. Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer means exactly what he says: follow the last element, regardless of whether the *issur* is still present. *Dilma ta'amei de-Rabbi Eliezer mishum de-achar acharon ani ba* — maybe his entire principle is simply "the last one counts," and if the last one was *heter*, there is no problem even if the *issur* is still present in the dough. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that Rabbi Eliezer in the sourdough case cannot definitively establish that *zeh va-zeh gorem* is forbidden.

## Asherah Wood Case

### Avodah Zarah Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: נָטַל הֵימֶנָּה עֵצִים — אֲסוּרִין בַּהֲנָאָה. הִסִּיק בָּהֶן אֶת הַתַּנּוּר, חָדָשׁ — יוּתַּץ, יָשָׁן — יוּצַן. אָפָה בּוֹ אֶת הַפַּת — אֲסוּרִין בַּהֲנָאָה. נִתְעָרְבָה בְּאַחֲרוֹת וַאֲחֵרוֹת בַּאֲחֵרוֹת — כּוּלָּן אֲסוּרִין בַּהֲנָאָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יוֹלִיךְ הֲנָאָה לְיָם הַמֶּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna: If **one took wood from** an *asheira*, **it is prohibited to** derive **benefit from it.** With regard to **one** who **lit an oven with** the wood, if it was a **new** oven, **it** must **be broken.** If it was an **old** oven, **it** may **be cooled.** If **one baked bread** with *asheira* wood as the fuel, **it is prohibited to** derive **benefit from it.** If this bread **was mixed together with other** bread, **and** that **other** bread **was mixed with other** bread, **it is prohibited to derive benefit from all** of this bread. **Rabbi Eliezer says: He casts the benefit into the Dead Sea [*Yam HaMelaḥ*].** In other words, one is not required to destroy the entire mixture when the prohibited bread is mixed with a large quantity of other bread. Instead one should designate money equal in value to the value of the original wood from the *asheira*, and he should destroy this money to offset the benefit he derived from the prohibited wood.

The *mishna* in Avodah Zarah teaches: *natal himenah etzim* — if one took wood from an *Asherah* tree (a tree worshipped as part of idolatry), *assurin be-hana'ah* — that wood is forbidden in benefit. *Hisik ba-hen et ha-tannur* — if one used those sticks to fire up an oven: *chadash yutatz, yashan yutzan* — a new oven must be destroyed; an old oven must simply be allowed to cool. *Afa bo et ha-pat* — if one baked bread in the oven, whether new or old, *assurin be-hana'ah* — the bread is forbidden in benefit.

The *mishna* then addresses a case of commingling: *nitarva be-acherot ve-acherot be-acherot* — the forbidden loaf became mixed with other loaves, and those then mixed with still others. Despite the possibility of invoking *rov*, *bittul*, or *sfeik sfeika*, by *Avodah Zarah* the rule is one of complete stringency: *kullan assurin be-hana'ah* — all the loaves are forbidden in benefit.

Rabbi Eliezer offers a more lenient option in the commingled case: *yolichet hana'ah le-Yam Ha-Melach* — one may cast into the sea a coin equivalent in value to the benefit one would have derived from the bread, and this symbolic renunciation of profit renders the bread permissible. The Sages respond: *ein pidyon le-Avodah Zarah* — there is no redemption for *Avodah Zarah*. (The full discussion of this position belongs to tractate Avodah Zarah; what is relevant here is the underlying *mishna* and its ruling about the oven.)

### Attributing Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — דַּחֲמִיר אִיסּוּרַהּ, בִּשְׁאָר אִיסּוּרִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Say that you heard that Rabbi Eliezer** and the first *tanna* are stringent in this matter **with regard to idolatry, whose prohibition is stringent.** However, **with regard to other prohibitions in the Torah,** which are less stringent, **did you hear him** express this opinion?

What is relevant to the current *sugya* is that this *mishna* in Avodah Zarah — bracketing the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Tanna Kamma* regarding the coin — reflects a unanimous position that a new oven must be destroyed and the bread is forbidden. This is precisely the strict position we needed to identify for the oven case in our *Tosefta*. Since Rabbi Eliezer's name appears in this *mishna*, the *Gemara* attributes the strict position to him — and to the *Tanna Kamma* alongside him.

The *Gemara* immediately raises an objection: *eimur de-shamat lei le-Rabbi Eliezer be-Avodah Zarah* — we heard this stringency in the context of *Avodah Zarah*, which is *d'chamir issura* — known for its exceptional strictness. By *Avodah Zarah*, commingled bread is never *batel*, and a host of additional stringencies apply. *Aval be-sha'ar issurin she-ba-Torah mi-shamat lei* — but regarding other *issurin* of the Torah, did we ever hear Rabbi Eliezer apply this position?

This point has direct practical relevance. The question of *zeh va-zeh gorem* as it might apply to *Pesach* — for instance, the concern raised by some about purchasing milk and eggs produced from animals that consumed *chametz* feed — is addressed explicitly by Rav Moshe Feinstein. Rav Moshe notes that the stringencies motivating this *chumra* derive from discussions rooted in the laws of *Avodah Zarah*, where the *Gemara* is peculiarly strict. One cannot assume that stringencies applicable to *Avodah Zarah* carry over to *chametz*; in principle, the distinction the *Gemara* is drawing here precludes that transfer, and Rav Moshe rules that the concern is not applicable to *chametz*.

### Braita Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָא תַּנְיָא בְּהֶדְיָא: וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹסֵר בְּכׇל אִיסּוּרִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, wasn't** it **taught explicitly** in a *baraita*: **And, similarly, Rabbi Eliezer would prohibit** these types of mixtures **with regard to all prohibitions in the Torah.**

The *Gemara* responds with two points. First, as a practical matter: if we do not use this *mishna* in Avodah Zarah to identify the *Tanna* who holds the strict oven position by *orlah*, we are left without any named *Tanna* to attribute it to. We therefore must associate it with Rabbi Eliezer and the *Tanna Kamma* of Avodah Zarah, and assume that the distinction between *Avodah Zarah* and other *issurin* does not hold.

Second: *ha tanya be-hedya* — there is a *Braita* that states it explicitly. The *Braita* records that Rabbi Eliezer (together with the *Tanna Kamma*) applies the strict position *be-chol issurin she-ba-Torah* — to all categories of things forbidden in benefit, not only *Avodah Zarah*. The *Gemara* acknowledges an interesting textual wrinkle: in the *mishna* in Avodah Zarah, it is actually Rabbi Eliezer who is *matir* relative to the *Tanna Kamma* — he is the more lenient one, allowing the coin-throwing ritual — while the *Tanna Kamma* is stricter. So the attribution of the strict oven position to "Rabbi Eliezer" requires a nuanced reading: it is really the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer together on the oven question, even as they diverge on the commingling question.

## Zeh V'Zeh Gorem…

### Pots vs. Ovens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר ״זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם אָסוּר״ — רַבִּי הַיְינוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר ״זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם מוּתָּר״, וְהָכָא מִשּׁוּם דְּיֵשׁ שֶׁבַח עֵצִים בְּפַת הוּא — הָנֵי קְעָרוֹת וְכוֹסוֹת וּצְלוֹחִיּוֹת אֲסִירִי.
>
> **Translation:** **If you say,** based on the previously stated opinions, that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that when **both this and that cause, it is prohibited,** then the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **is** identical to the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** as both state that it is prohibited for this same reason. **And if you say** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that when **both this and that cause, it is permitted, and here,** where Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi rules that it is prohibited, **it is because there is improvement from the wood in the bread** itself, then in that case, deriving benefit from any of **these** earthenware **bowls, cups, and flasks** that were made in such an oven should also **be prohibited,** since the improvement from the wood is in them as well. If one were to use such utensils he would be deriving benefit from a prohibited item.

Abaye now articulates the logical structure of the dispute more precisely. *Im timtzei lomar zeh va-zeh gorem assur* — if we accept that *zeh va-zeh gorem* is forbidden, then Rebbi, who rules the bread forbidden because *yesh shevach eitzim be-fat*, would also rule that the oven is forbidden, since the oven-plus-fire scenario is simply a *zeh va-zeh gorem* on top of the already problematic *shevach etzim* concern. *Ve-im timtzei lomar zeh va-zeh gorem mutar* — and if *zeh va-zeh gorem* is permissible, then even Rebbi would say that the oven is permissible, because the oven's contribution is jointly made with the fire, and Rebbi's concern (*yesh shevach etzim be-fat*) would only apply to the bread, not to the dual-contributor scenario of the oven.

This framing opens a further question about intermediate cases. *Hani kderot ve-khosot ve-tzluchiyot assuri* — what about plates, cups, and bowls? If forbidden wood was used to fire pottery in a kiln, and those ceramic vessels are now used, are they like the bread or like the oven?

The answer is that plates and cups are treated like the bread: using them is a single-contributor *hana'ah*. The vessel derived its form from the forbidden fire, and when one uses it, one is deriving benefit from that vessel alone, with no other contributor at that moment. The oven, by contrast, is only ever used *together with* a new fire; the benefit of baking bread comes from both the oven and the fire jointly, making it a *zeh va-zeh gorem* scenario.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּתַנּוּר וּקְדֵירָה: לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם אָסוּר״ — אָסוּר. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם מוּתָּר״ — שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **When** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Sages **disagree is** in a case **where an oven and a pot** were formed using prohibited wood. **According to the one who says** that when **both this and that cause it is prohibited, it is prohibited** to derive benefit from these as well, since the prohibited item was a contributing factor in the initial formation of the object. However, **according to the one who says** that when **both this and that cause, it is permitted, it is permitted** to derive benefit from them. This is because one derives benefit from the prohibited oven and pot only once they have been subsequently heated by permitted wood. Therefore, the influence on the pot of the prohibited item is only one component in the preparation of this food.

The case of a pot (*kedeirah*) is subtler and is the subject of a dispute within the *Gemara*. *Ki pligi be-tannur u-ve-kedeirah* — the debate between Rebbi and Rabbi Eliezer, or between those who permit and those who forbid in the *zeh va-zeh gorem* scenario, concerns specifically the oven and the pot. *Le-man de-amar zeh va-zeh gorem assur* — if *zeh va-zeh gorem* is a problem, both the oven and the pot are forbidden. *Le-man de-amar zeh va-zeh gorem mutar* — if it is permissible, both are allowed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״זֶה וָזֶה גּוֹרֵם מוּתָּר״, קְדֵירָה — אֲסוּרָה, דְּהָא קַבְּלָה בִּישּׁוּלָא מִקַּמֵּי דְּנִיתֵּן עֵצִים דְּהֶיתֵּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **even according to the one who says** that when **both this and that cause, it is permitted, the pot** made through the use of prohibited wood **is prohibited, since it holds the food** inside it **before the permitted wood is placed** in the oven. Therefore, one derives benefit from the prohibited vessel itself without any contribution from a permitted source.

But *ika de-amrei* — an alternative formulation holds that even according to the opinion that *zeh va-zeh gorem* is permissible, the pot may still be forbidden: *de-kablah bishulah mi-kamei de-nitan etzim de-heteira* — because one places the food into the pot before lighting the fire. At the moment the food enters the pot and begins to be affected, the pot is acting alone, without any other contributor. Only an oven receives the food *after* the fire is already burning, so that from the first moment of baking, two things are contributing. A pot that is placed empty onto the fire first and only then filled, everyone would agree, is permissible — since the food is never placed into the pot without the fire already being present as a joint contributor.

## Shmuel's Girsa

### Two Text Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תַּנּוּר שֶׁהִסִּיקוֹ בִּקְלִיפֵּי עׇרְלָה, אוֹ בְּקַשִּׁין שֶׁל כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם, חָדָשׁ — יוּתַּץ, יָשָׁן — יוּצַן. אָפָה בּוֹ אֶת הַפַּת, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: הַפַּת מוּתֶּרֶת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַפַּת אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **an oven that one lit with peels of *orla*** fruit, **or with straw** of grain that was planted in a prohibited mixture of **diverse kinds in a vineyard,** if it is a **new** oven, **it** must **be shattered.** If it is an **old** oven, **it** may **be cooled.** If **one baked bread in it, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: The bread is permitted, and the Rabbis say: The bread is prohibited.**

*Amar Rav Yosef amar Rav Yehudah amar Shmuel*: The *Braita* as Shmuel transmitted it reads: *tanur she-hisiko be-klifei orlah o be-kashim shel kilei ha-kerem: chadash yutzat, yashan yutzan. Afah bo et ha-pat: Rebbi omer ha-pat muteret; ve-Chachamim omrim ha-pat asurah*. According to this *girsa*, it is Rebbi who permits the bread and the *Chachamim* who forbid it — the reverse of our *Tosefta*, in which Rebbi forbids and the *Chachamim* permit.

### Shmuel's Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא אִיפְּכָא! שְׁמוּאֵל אִיפְּכָא תָּנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Wasn't the reverse taught** in the *baraita*? The Gemara answers: **Shmuel teaches the reverse,** that it is Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi who permits one to derive benefit from this bread even in the previously mentioned *baraita*.

*Ve-hatanya ipcha* — but our *Tosefta* has the positions reversed: Rebbi forbids and the *Chachamim* permit. The *Gemara* acknowledges: *Shmuel ipcha tani* — Shmuel taught the *Braita* in the inverted form.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּעָלְמָא קָסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְלֹא מֵחֲבֵירָיו, וּבְהָא — אֲפִילּוּ מֵחֲבֵירָיו. וְסָבַר אַתְנְיַיהּ אִיפְּכָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנֵיקוּם רַבָּנַן לְאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say:** Shmuel accepts the original text of the *baraita* that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is the one who prohibits deriving benefit from the bread. And **generally, Shmuel holds** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **over his** individual **colleague** who disagrees; however, the *halakha* does **not** follow him **over** several of **his colleagues** who disagree. **And in this** particular case, the *halakha* follows Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **even over his colleagues. And** Shmuel **holds: I will reverse** the two sides presented here, **in order to establish the Rabbis'** opinion **as a prohibition.** Therefore, the conclusion will be to rule that it is prohibited, in accordance with the majority opinion. Although in Shmuel's version the attributions of the opinions are technically inaccurate, the benefit is that when people see that the Rabbis rule that it is prohibited in this case, they will be inclined to accept their majority opinion, which is the correct *halakha*.

*Ve-iba'it eima: be-alma kasavar Shmuel halacha ke-Rabbi me-chaveiro* — Shmuel generally holds that the *halacha* follows Rebbi when he argues with a single colleague, but not when he argues against many colleagues (*me-haveirav*). In our case, Rebbi is arguing against the *Chachamim* — a majority — and so Shmuel's ordinary rule would lead him to rule like the *Chachamim*, i.e., that the bread is permissible. But Shmuel in fact holds that the bread is *forbidden*, agreeing with Rebbi on the merits.

This creates a problem of presentation. If Shmuel were to rule openly that the bread is forbidden — citing a *Braita* in which Rebbi is the minority — his colleagues would object: according to Shmuel's own principle (*halacha ke-Rabbi me-chaveiro, ve-lo me-haveirav*), he should be ruling leniently. So Shmuel *ashniye ipcha* — he deliberately inverted the *girsa* of the *Braita* so that in his version, the *Chachamim* (the majority) are the ones who forbid. This way, *d'nigmaru Rabbanan le-issura* — the majority position in the text aligns with the stringent ruling, and Shmuel can rule stringently without appearing to contradict his own principle.

This is, as the *Gemara* presents it, a striking and genuinely troubling passage. Whatever happened to intellectual honesty? Is this not a misrepresentation of *Torah*? It is worth noting a partial parallel: the *Gemara* elsewhere records that Rebbi himself would sometimes take a *da'at yachid* and incorporate it as the *stam* (anonymous) position in the *Mishna*, thereby giving it greater weight. But that is a question of editorial presentation, not falsification — the minority view is being elevated, not reversed. Here the *Gemara* says plainly that Shmuel would actually reverse the *girsa* so that people would accept his *psak*. The *Gemara* itself does not soften this; it presents it without apology.

## Coals vs. Flames

### Whispering Coals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, חַד אָמַר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא גֶּחָלִים עוֹמְמוֹת, אֲבָל גֶּחָלִים לוֹחֲשׁוֹת — אֲסוּרִין, וְחַד אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ גֶּחָלִים לוֹחֲשׁוֹת נָמֵי מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records a dispute: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said** one opinion, **and Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** another opinion. **One** of them **said: They taught** this leniency **only** when one cooks with **dim coals,** whose heat is merely a remnant of the earlier lighting; **however,** when one cooks with **glowing coals,** the bread is **prohibited. And one** of them **said: Even** when **the coals are glowing,** the bread is **also permitted.**

We return now to the provision in the *Tosefta* that when bread is baked over *gechalim* — coals — rather than active flames, *divrei hakol* the bread is permitted. The *Gemara* cites a dispute between *Rav Yehudah amar Shmuel* and *Rav Chiya bar Ashi amar Rabbi Yochanan* about the exact scope of this leniency.

*Chad amar: lo shanu ela be-gechalim omemot* — one opinion holds that the unanimous leniency applies only to coals that are *omemot*, essentially extinct — fully black charcoal with no remaining combustion. *Aval gechalim lochashot* — but if they are *lochashot*, "whispering" coals — still glowing, still smoldering — *assurin*: still forbidden, because there remains active burning of the wood, and the bread is still being baked with wood of *issurei hana'ah*.

*Ve-chad amar: afilu gechalim lochashot nami mutarot* — the other opinion holds that even glowing, whispering coals are permissible, and that even Rebbi would agree at this stage that the bread is permitted.

### Raging Flame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לוֹחֲשׁוֹת אֲסוּרִין, מִשּׁוּם דְּיֵשׁ שֶׁבַח עֵצִים בַּפַּת. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֲפִילּוּ לוֹחֲשׁוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת, פַּת דְּאָסַר דְּיֵשׁ שֶׁבַח עֵצִים בַּפַּת לְרַבִּי הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לֵיהּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּשֶׁאֲבוּקָה כְּנֶגְדּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who said** that baking with **glowing** coals renders the bread **prohibited,** this is **because there is improvement from the** prohibited **wood in the bread. However, according to the one** who **said** that **even** when baking with **glowing** coals the bread is **permitted,** since they are no longer considered to be wood, **where do you find the case** where **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems bread to be prohibited because there is improvement from** the prohibited **wood in it?** Why should there be a difference between glowing coals and actual burning wood? **Rav Pappa said:** The case is **when a flame is** directly **opposite** the bread. When he cooks the bread directly in front of the wood, it is improved directly by the wood. When the coals are merely glowing, there is no direct benefit from the wood.

This second opinion creates a puzzle. *Bishlama le-man de-amar lochashot assurin* — the position that whispering coals are forbidden is coherent: there is still some unburned wood contributing to the baking, and therefore *yesh shevach eitzim be-fat* applies. *Ela le-man de-amar afilu lochashot mutarot* — but if even whispering coals are permissible, what case remains where Rebbi would say the bread is forbidden? If the threshold for leniency is that low, when is there ever a case of *yesh shevach eitzim be-fat*?

*Amar Rav Pappa: ke-she'avuka ke-negdo* — Rav Pappa answers: the case where Rebbi forbids is when there is a full, raging flame directly opposite the bread. That is when the wood is actively burning and visibly contributing to the baking; at that stage, the *shevach etzim* is present and operative.

The conceptual distinction being drawn here is somewhat elusive. If whispering coals are permissible because the wood is essentially charred and no longer contributes as wood, then the question is whether *lochashot* still contains some unburned wood or not. If there is still some wood burning in *lochashot*, then the distinction from *avuka ke-negdo* is one of degree — 2% unburned wood versus 100% — and it is not obvious why the former is permissible and the latter forbidden, unless one invokes *zeh va-zeh gorem*: in the *lochashot* case, most of the contribution comes from the charcoal (which is already transformed and permissible), and only a minimal share from the remaining wood.

### Chachamim Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּרַבָּנַן דִּפְלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ — שָׁרוּ אֲפִילּוּ כְּשֶׁאֲבוּקָה כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, אֶלָּא עֵצִים דְּאִיסּוּרָא לְרַבָּנַן הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ? אָמַר רַב אַמֵּי בַּר חָמָא: בְּשַׁרְשִׁיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does this prove **by inference that the Rabbis who disagree with** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **permit** one to eat this bread **even when the flame is opposite it? But** if this is the case, **where do you find the case** where **it is prohibited according to the Rabbis** to derive benefit from **wood? Rav Ami bar Ḥama said:** It is found **in** the case of **a stool** made from the wood. Although they hold that it is permitted to derive indirect benefit from the wood, even the Rabbis agree that one may not derive benefit from a stool that is made from the wood itself.

*Michlal* — from this one can infer the *Chachamim*'s reasoning. *De-Rabbanan de-pligi alei: shari afilu ke-she'avuka ke-negdo* — the Sages who disagree with Rebbi would permit even when there is a full raging flame opposite the bread. Their reasoning, as the *Gemara* understands it, is more radical than a simple *bedi'avad* leniency: they hold that fire is itself already-destroyed wood. When wood burns, what is produced — the flame itself — is generated from the consumption and destruction of the wood. The flame you see is not the wood; it is the product of the wood's annihilation. Therefore, from the moment the fire exists, it is already a product of destroyed material, and cooking over it is never in the category of deriving benefit from *issurei hana'ah*.

If this is so, the *Chachamim* would say not merely that the bread baked with forbidden fire is permissible *bedi'avad*, but that one may *le-chatchilah* cook using such fire. This seems a far-reaching conclusion, and the *Gemara* itself acknowledges the tension with the *stam mishna* that states *ve-lo yasik bo tannur ve-khirayim* — one may not fire an oven with *chametz*. That ruling appears to assume that everyone agrees one may not use forbidden wood to fire an oven, which does not fit easily with the *Chachamim*'s position as the *Gemara* has now framed it. The *Gemara* is aware of this difficulty.

*Ela etzim de-issura Rabbanan heichi mashkachat lehu* — so how, according to the *Chachamim*, could one ever transgress with forbidden wood? If fire already represents destroyed wood, making a fire with forbidden wood is never a transgression. *Amar Rami bar Chama: be-shashifa* — Rami bar Chama answers with a single word: with a stool. One transgresses with forbidden wood not by burning it, but by fashioning an object from it — a chair, a table, some artifact — and deriving use from that object directly. Only then is one benefiting from the *issurei hana'ah* as such. Burning it, by the *Chachamim*'s logic, always transforms it into something other than itself.

## Hekdesh Wood

### Hekdesh Non-Batel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: תַּנּוּר שֶׁהִסִּיקוֹ בַּעֲצֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְאָפָה בּוֹ הַפַּת, לְרַבָּנַן דְּשָׁרוּ בְּקַמַּיְיתָא, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַפַּת אֲסוּרָה. וּמָה בֵּין זוֹ לְעׇרְלָה?! אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! עׇרְלָה בְּטֵילָה בְּמָאתַיִם, הֶקְדֵּשׁ אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶלֶף לֹא בָּטֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda:** With regard to **an oven that one kindled with consecrated wood and baked bread in it, according to the Rabbis, who permitted** the bread **in the first** case where it was baked with *orla* wood, **what is the *halakha*? He said to him: The bread is prohibited.** He responded: **What is the difference between this** bread **and** bread baked with ***orla*** peels? **Rava said: How can** these cases **be compared? *Orla* is nullified** in a mixture of one part in **two hundred;** it is possible that less than this amount of *orla* was absorbed by the bread. However, **consecrated** wood **is not nullified even in** a mixture of one part in **one thousand.** Therefore, even when there is only a miniscule amount of the consecrated matter in the bread it is still prohibited.

The *Gemara* now introduces a new question: *tanur she-hisiku be-atzei hekdesh* — what if the oven was fired with consecrated wood (*hekdesh*), and then bread was baked in it? *De-Rabbanan de-sharu bi-kamaita* — the Sages who normally permit the bread in the *orlah*/*kilayim* cases: what would they say when the wood is *hekdesh*?

*Amar lei: ha-pat asurah* — the bread is forbidden even according to the Sages, in the case of *hekdesh*. Why should *hekdesh* differ? *Hachi hashta* — the comparison is not apt, answers Rava. *Orlah beteilah be-matayim* — *orlah* becomes *batel* at a ratio of one in two hundred. *Hekdesh afilu be-elef lo batil* — *hekdesh* is never *batel* at any ratio. Since *hekdesh* is subject to uniquely severe rules of non-*bittul*, we apply correspondingly greater stringency across the board, including to the question of whether the bread retains forbidden status. The logic parallels what the *Gemara* said earlier about *Avodah Zarah*: where a category is inherently more severe, we are more strict even in cases where the direct reasoning might not compel it.

### Me'ilah Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אִי קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ — הָא קַשְׁיָא: וַהֲלֹא מָעַל הַמַּסִּיק, וְכׇל הֵיכָא דְּמָעַל הַמַּסִּיק נָפְקוּ לְהוּ לְחוּלִּין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: If it was difficult for** Rami bar Ḥama, **this is** what he found **difficult: Didn't the kindler** of the fire transgress the prohibition of **misuse** of **consecrated property,** as anyone who unwittingly uses consecrated property for a non-sacred use violates this prohibition? **And any** case **where the kindler** of an oven **misuses consecrated property** by doing so, the wood **is transferred to non-sacred** status. The wood loses its sanctity when misused, and the one who misused it must donate other wood to the Temple in its place. In that case, the wood used to heat the oven is non-sacred wood and the bread should be permitted.

*Ela amar Rava: ei kashya li ha kashya* — Rava says the more pressing question is not whether the bread is forbidden but how it could be forbidden at all given the laws of *me'ilah*. *Ve-halo maal ha-masik* — the moment one takes consecrated firewood and places it in the oven, one has committed *me'ilah* — unauthorized benefit from sacred property. And the operative rule is: *kol heicha de-maal ha-masik, nafku lehu le-chullin* — wherever the one who lit the fire is guilty of *me'ilah*, the object loses its consecrated status and becomes *chullin*. So by the time the bread is baked, the wood has already ceased to be *hekdesh*, and the bread should be entirely permissible.

The *Gemara* is careful to specify: this applies to objects sanctified for their monetary value (*kedushat damim*), which lose their *hekdesh* status through *me'ilah*. Objects whose sanctity is intrinsic — *korbanos*, *keilei shareit* — retain their identity. But firewood sanctified for the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* treasury is the former category and should, on this analysis, become *chullin* the moment it is misappropriated.

### Shelamim Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָכָא בַּעֲצֵי שְׁלָמִים עָסְקִינַן, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּשׁוֹגֵג מִתְחַלֵּל, בְּמֵזִיד אֵינוֹ מִתְחַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Here, we are dealing with wood** which had been set aside **for** purchasing **peace-offerings.** This wood, while sanctified, has a lesser status of sanctity and does not become fully consecrated until the blood of the offering has been sprinkled. **And** this dilemma was raised **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who **said:** If one **unwittingly** misused **consecrated property, it becomes desecrated** and loses its elevated status. However, if one used the object **intentionally, it is not desecrated** and remains consecrated. Since the act here is intentional, the consecrated wood does not lose its status.

*Amar Rav Pappa: hacha be-atzei shelamim askinan* — Rav Pappa answers: we are discussing wood that has been consecrated with the sanctity of a *shelamim* offering — specifically, wood that one declared: with the proceeds of the sale of this wood, I will purchase a *shelamim*.

The reason this matters is that *shelamim* is unique among *korbanos*: there is no *me'ilah* on a *shelamim*, because a *shelamim* is considered essentially the property of the owner who brings it. The *imurin* (fatty portions) are God's portion, and those become off-limits after *zerikat ha-dam*, but the bulk of the *shelamim* is eaten by the owner and his household. The *shelamim* is therefore not considered fully *mish-ulchan gavoah* — from the table of the Most High — in the same sense that a *chatat* is. A *chatat*, by contrast, is described as *mi-shulchan gavoah ka zachu*: the *kohanim* who eat it are eating from God's meal; they are considered part of the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* apparatus, not independent recipients.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמֵזִיד מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו בַּר מְעִילָה — הוּא לָא נָפֵיק לְחוּלִּין. שְׁלָמִים נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָאו בַּר מְעִילָה נִינְהוּ — לָא נָפְקָא לְחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that when one **intentionally** uses this object it does **not** lose its status? **Since** it is **not subject to** the *halakha* of **misuse of consecrated property,** as one is liable to bring an offering only for unwitting misuse of consecrated property, **it is not transferred to non-sacred** status. The same *halakha* applies to the wood set aside for **peace-offerings as well. Since** at that stage **it is not subject to** the *halakha* of **misuse of consecrated property,** as that applies only after the animal's blood has been sprinkled, then according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, even if one unwittingly uses this wood, **it is not transferred to non-sacred** status; rather, it remains prohibited.

Since there is no *me'ilah* on a *shelamim*, misusing wood consecrated with the sanctity of a *shelamim* does not trigger *me'ilah*, and therefore the wood does not become *chullin*. It remains *hekdesh* even after misuse.

The *Gemara* adds: *ve-aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda* — this follows the position of Rabbi Yehuda. *De-amar hekdesh be-shogeg mitchalel* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that *hekdesh* is desecrated (becomes *chullin*) through inadvertent *me'ilah* (*be-shogeg*). *Be-mezid eino mitchalel* — but deliberate misuse (*be-mezid*) does not desecrate it. The reason, counterintuitive as it may seem, is that *me'ilah* as a legal category applies only where one can bring a *korban* of *me'ilah*, and a *korban* of *me'ilah* is only brought for inadvertent (*shogeg*) violations. *Mezid* misuse, however serious as a prohibition, does not carry the *korban* and therefore lacks the full legal trigger that would render the object *chullin*. *Lo nafik le-chullin* — it does not exit its sanctified status.

By analogy: *shelamim nami* — *shelamim* likewise does not qualify for *me'ilah* as a legal category, because one does not bring a *korban me'ilah* for misusing a *shelamim*. *Lo nafik le-chullin* — therefore, even when misused, it retains its *hekdesh* status. It is not the act of misuse that discharges *hekdesh*; it is the specific legal mechanism of *me'ilah*. Where that mechanism is absent — whether because the transgressor acted *be-mezid*, or because the object is *shelamim* — the *hekdesh* endures.

### Ashes Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הֵיכָא דְּמָעַל הַמַּסִּיק נָפְקִי לְחוּלִּין? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: כׇּל הַנִּשְׂרָפִין — אֶפְרָן מוּתָּר, חוּץ מֵעֲצֵי אֲשֵׁירָה. וְאֵפֶר הֶקְדֵּשׁ — לְעוֹלָם אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And anywhere the kindler** of an oven transgresses the prohibition of **misuse of consecrated property** by using consecrated wood, **is it transferred to non-sacred** status? **Wasn't** it **taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **all** prohibited items **that must be burned, their ashes are permitted** after the burning, **except for wood from an *asheira*? And consecrated ash is prohibited forever.** Therefore, it is possible that when one kindles an oven with this consecrated wood, although he misuses consecrated property, the ash remains prohibited.

The *Gemara* closes with the *Braita* mentioned at the outset: *kol ha-nisrafim afaran mutar* — all objects that are prescribed to be burned, their ashes are permissible — *chutz me-atzei Asherah ve-afar hekdesh* — with the exception of *Asherah* wood and the ashes of *hekdesh*.

*D'afar hekdesh le-olam assur* — the ashes of *hekdesh* wood remain forbidden permanently. But this raises the same question again: once the wood is burned, hasn't *me'ilah* been committed, rendering the ashes *chullin*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּפְלָה דְּלֵיקָה מֵאֵילֶיהָ בַּעֲצֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ, דְּלֵיכָּא אִינָשׁ דְּנִמְעוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said:** That *baraita* is discussing a case **where a fire began on its own among consecrated wood and there is no one who misused consecrated property.** Since this is the case, even the ash that is left from this wood remains consecrated property and one may not benefit from it.

*Amar Rami bar Chama: ke-gon she-nafal deleka me'eleha be-atzei hekdesh* — who said any *me'ilah* was committed? Perhaps a fire broke out on its own, spontaneously consuming the consecrated wood. *De-leka inish de-nimal* — no human committed *me'ilah*, and yet the ashes remain. This establishes that the ashes of *hekdesh* are forbidden even in the complete absence of *me'ilah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שְׁמַעְיָה אָמַר: בְּאוֹתָן שֶׁטְּעוּנִין גְּנִיזָה. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשָׂמוֹ״ בְּנַחַת, ״וְשָׂמוֹ״ כּוּלּוֹ, ״וְשָׂמוֹ״ שֶׁלֹּא יְפַזֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shemaya said:** This *baraita* is dealing **with those** types of consecrated ash **that require burial,** such as the ash removed from the altar. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: "And he shall take up the ash from where the fire has consumed the burnt-offering on the altar, and he shall put it beside the altar" (Leviticus 6:3). The phrase **"And he shall put it"** indicates that he must do so **gently; "and he shall put it"** also indicates that he must place **all of it; "and he shall put it"** also indicates **that he** may **not scatter** the ashes. Apparently, even after the offering has been burned it remains sacred, and one may not derive benefit from it. However, when it was burned it was not subject to misuse of consecrated property, as its burning is a necessary step in the process of sacrificing the offerings.

*Ve-Rav Ashi amar: be-osam she-te'unim gniza* — Rav Ashi offers a different case: ashes that require *gniza* (burial/preservation), specifically the *terumas ha-deshen* — the ashes removed from the *mizbe'ach* each morning, which the *Torah* requires to be placed carefully beside the *mizbe'ach* and not scattered. *D'tanya: ve-samo benachas, ve-samo kulo, ve-samo she-lo yefazer* — these ashes were never the product of any *me'ilah*; they were created through the legitimate service of the *mizbe'ach* itself. They therefore retain their sacred status permanently.

The *Gemara*'s conclusion is that *hekdesh* and *Avodah Zarah* represent the two categories subject to heightened stringency throughout this entire *sugya*. As a general rule, burning destroys forbidden status (*nisrafim afaran mutar*), and benefiting from the product of the burning — whether in the form of baked bread or coal — follows from that leniency. But in the exceptional categories of *Avodah Zarah* and *hekdesh*, the ashes remain forbidden, the bread remains problematic, and the principles of *zeh va-zeh gorem* that would otherwise relax the analysis are held to a stricter standard.
