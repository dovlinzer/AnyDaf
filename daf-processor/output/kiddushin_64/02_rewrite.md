# Kiddushin 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father's Believability

### Capital Punishment Query

The Gemara opens with a question — *ibaya lehu* — that naturally extends from the *mishnah* concerning a father's authority to marry off his minor daughter. The *mishnah* had established, grounded in the *pasuk* from the *motzi shem ra* case — *et biti natati la-ish hazeh*, "I gave my daughter to this man" — that a father is believed when he declares he has married off his daughter. This generates immediate practical consequences: if the father says he married her to Reuven and cannot remember, she may not freely marry another without a *get*. But the Gemara now presses further: *mahu liskol al yado* — does the father's testimony carry sufficient weight that a third party who commits adultery with this daughter could be executed on the basis of that testimony alone? That is, if the father declares she is married and Shimon, with witnesses to the act itself but with no independent witnesses to the marriage, has relations with her, would Shimon receive *skilah*?

### Rav: No Execution

Rav rules: *ein soklin* — no, one does not execute on the basis of the father's testimony. The Torah believed the father *l'issura*, to create a prohibition, to treat the daughter as married for purposes of restricting her behavior and requiring a *get* before she may remarry. But *l'ketala lo heimneih* — the Torah did not extend that credibility to the point of executing another person on the father's word.

### Rav Assi: Yes Execution

Rav Assi disagrees and rules: *soklin* — one does execute. When the Torah granted the father believability through the verse *et biti natati la-ish hazeh*, it did so comprehensively: *l'chol milei heimneih rachmana l'av*, the Torah trusted the father in all matters, without reservation.

### Woman vs. Father

Rav Assi then offers a remarkable self-reflective comment. He acknowledges that his two rulings — the one taught previously and the one just stated — *me'afsan igra*, they shatter the rooftops, they sound internally contradictory. The teaching from the previous day's discussion was that if a woman says "I was married to someone but I cannot remember who," and a man steps forward claiming to be that husband, we do not believe him — because we suspect the woman might collude with an unsuitable suitor who cannot be trusted to fear contradiction. By contrast, when the *father* says he married off his daughter to someone and cannot remember who, and a man steps forward claiming it was him, we *do* believe that man, because he fears being contradicted by the father. The father, after all, might well say: "I don't know who it was, but it certainly was not you."

Now Rav Assi articulates the tension: *makom sheba lichnos koneis* — in the scenario involving the father, even though we are willing to let the claimant live with the woman as her husband, implying that we treat the father's testimony as something less than absolute fact (since we readily accept the claimant's word), Rav Assi nevertheless rules that we would give *skilah* to some other man who commits adultery with her. And yet in the case where the woman herself testifies she was married — where we treat her testimony as weighty enough to refuse belief in any claimant who says he is the husband — we would *not* give *skilah* if someone were to commit adultery with her. This seems backwards: the woman's testimony about her own marriage is treated as more binding than the father's when it comes to refusing the claimant, yet less binding when it comes to capital consequences.

### Resolving Contradiction

Rav Assi himself resolves the apparent contradiction: *v'lo hee* — in truth, this is not a real problem. The Torah simply treated these two sources of testimony differently, each in its own domain. *L'av heimneih rachmana, l'didah lo heimneih* — the Torah trusted the father; it did not trust the woman. The father's declaration about his minor daughter is treated as fact for purposes of capital punishment. The woman's declaration about herself is sufficient to prohibit her remarriage without a *get*, but it is not treated as juridical fact sufficient for execution. Meanwhile, the rule about whether to believe a man who comes forward claiming to be the husband operates on entirely separate logic — the father's declaration creates a dynamic where the claimant fears contradiction, the woman's does not. These are independent considerations that do not produce a true contradiction.

### Rav Chisda Alternative

Rav Chisda rules: *echad zeh v'echad zeh ein soklin* — neither the father's testimony about his daughter's marriage nor the woman's testimony about herself is sufficient to impose capital punishment. And Rav Chisda here follows his broader principle, articulated elsewhere, that when the Torah grants an individual *ne'eman* status regarding personal matters of status, that believability is not equivalent to formal testimony by *edim*. It is a limited form of credibility.

### Age & Sacrifice

This principle is illustrated by a case Rav Chisda himself stated: if a father declares "my son is nine years and a day old" or "my daughter is three years and a day old," these ages are halachically significant — a boy of nine years and a day who engages in intercourse with a married woman has effected a halachically cognizable act of intercourse, making the woman liable, though not the minor himself; and similarly for a girl of three years and a day. Rav Chisda ruled that the father is believed to establish these ages *lekorban* — to obligate an adult party in an unintentional act to bring a *korban* — but not *lemalkos* and not *le'onashim*, not for lashes and not for execution. A *baraita* is then cited in support of this position: it teaches the same formula regarding a father's declaration that his son is thirteen years and a day or his daughter is twelve years and a day, making them halachic adults. The father is believed *lenedarim ulcharamim ulehakdashot ule'erchin* — for all matters of vows, consecrations, and monetary obligations — but not for physical punishments. This confirms the approach of Rav Chisda.

### Adolescence Status

The *baraita* thus closes this opening sugya. The father is *ne'eman* to establish the majority status of his children for purposes of vows and sanctification, and *ne'eman* to establish the married status of his daughter for purposes of creating a prohibition and requiring a *get* — but in neither case does the Torah's grant of believability rise to the level of imposing capital punishment or corporal punishment on others.

## Marriage & Divorce

### Past Marriage Claim

The Gemara now arrives at the official *daf* — 64 — and opens a new *mishnah*. The *mishnah* presents a series of cases in which a father makes declarations about his daughter, and the question in each case is whether he is *ne'eman*. The first case: *kidashtiha vegerashtiha keshehi katana veharei hi katana* — the father says he married off his daughter and subsequently accepted a *get* on her behalf, all of this occurring while she was a minor, and she is currently still a minor. In such a case he is *ne'eman*. The practical consequence is that she is now a divorced woman and therefore forbidden to marry a *kohen*. The second variation: the father makes the same claim — he married her and divorced her while she was a minor — but she is now an adult at the time of his declaration. In this case he is *eino ne'eman*, not believed, and therefore she is treated as never having been married and is fully permitted to a *kohen*.

### Captivity Claim

The next case: *nishbis uphdisiha* — the father says his daughter was taken captive and he redeemed her. This claim, if accepted, would render her forbidden to a *kohen* due to the presumption of rape during captivity — a concern whose painful contemporary resonance the teacher pauses to note. The *mishnah* rules: *bein ketana bein gedola eino ne'eman*, whether she is a minor or an adult, the father is not believed. She therefore remains fully permitted to a *kohen*.

### Present Capacity

The Gemara asks: *mai shna reisha umai shna seifa* — what distinguishes the opening case, where he is believed, from the closing case, where he is not? The Gemara answers: *reisha biyado, seifa lav biyado* — in the first case, the matter is within his control; in the second, it is not. When his daughter is still a minor, it is within his power to marry her off and to receive a *get* on her behalf. The Torah believed him precisely because the act was something he could perform. By contrast, a daughter being taken captive is entirely beyond his control; he cannot cause that to happen. Therefore he has no *biyado* and he is not believed.

### Control Analysis

This principle — that *biyado* is the criterion for *ne'eman* status — is understood, at least by Rashi, as a form of *mah li leshaker*: since he could have done the thing he claims, there is no motive to fabricate. Some *rishonim* frame it differently, as a structural principle that authority over a matter generates credibility about it, rather than as a psychological inference. Either way, *biyado* in the *reisha* is the basis for belief, and *lav biyado* in the *seifa* is the basis for disbelief.

### Chalal Marriage

The Gemara immediately challenges this framework: is it truly the case that the father has no capacity to affect his daughter's status vis-à-vis a *kohen*? *Veharei biyado lehasi'ah lechalal* — he could marry her off to a *chalal*, a man born of a forbidden union involving a *kohen* (for example, the son of a *kohen* who married a divorcée). Such a marriage is itself permitted, but the woman who marries a *chalal* becomes a *chalalah* and is thereby forbidden to *kohanim*. Since the father has the capacity to effect this result, he should — by the *biyado* logic — be believed about her captivity status, which concerns the same issue of *pesul* to *kehunah*.

### Chalal Resolution

The Gemara answers: *ha lo kashya, k'Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda* — this is not difficult, because our *mishnah* follows the opinion of Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda. Rabbi Dostai holds that *benos Yisrael mikveh tahara lechalalim* — a Jewish woman who is not herself a *chalalah* serves as a source of purification for the *chalal* she marries, meaning that neither the woman nor their children take on the *chalal* status. Therefore, according to Rabbi Dostai, marrying the daughter to a *chalal* does not make her a *chalalah* and does not affect her status for *kehunah* at all. The father thus has no ability to render her *pesulah* to a *kohen* through that route, and the *biyado* reasoning does not apply.

### Mamzer Marriage

The Gemara presses further: *veharei biyado lehasi'ah l'mamzer* — but he could marry her off to a *mamzer*. A *bat Yisrael* is forbidden to marry a *mamzer*, but the *kiddushin* in such a case would nevertheless be binding, since a *mamzer*'s forbiddenness is only a *lav* — a simple negative prohibition — and *kiddushin* takes effect even in such cases. The consequence of such a marriage would render her forbidden to a *kohen*. Even though the father would be transgressing in arranging this, the fact that the *kiddushin* is binding means it remains within his power — and thus he should be believed about her captivity status.

The Gemara responds: *k'Rabbi Akiva* — our *mishnah* follows Rabbi Akiva, who holds *ein kiddushin tofsin b'chiyuvei lavin*, that *kiddushin* does not take effect in any forbidden union, including those prohibited by a mere *lav*. Therefore, according to Rabbi Akiva, an attempt to marry the daughter to a *mamzer* would produce no valid *kiddushin*, the child of such a union would be a *mamzer*, and the father has effectively no power to change her status. *Biyado* is absent.

The Gemara continues probing. Could the father arrange a marriage of an *almanah* to a *Kohen Gadol*? Such a union is forbidden, and the child is a *chalal* but — according to Rabbi Simai's understanding of Rabbi Akiva — not a *mamzer*. Since it is not a *mamzer*-producing union, Rabbi Akiva would agree that the *kiddushin* is binding. This would mean the father *does* have a mechanism, at least in theory, to render his daughter forbidden to a *kohen* even under Rabbi Akiva's framework, and the *biyado* argument would re-emerge.

The Gemara resolves this by appealing to the view of Rabbi Yeshovav. Rabbi Yeshovav declared, *bavo venitzvach al Akiva ben Yosef* — let us come and cry out against Rabbi Akiva — arguing that Rabbi Akiva's position is that *kol she'ein lo biah b'Yisrael havlad mamzer*, any forbidden intercourse whatsoever, including the *issurei kehunah* cases, produces a *mamzer*. Under this view, Rabbi Akiva would hold that even the union of an *almanah* with a *Kohen Gadol* produces a *mamzer* and the *kiddushin* is void. Thus, the father has no avenue by which to render his daughter *pesulah* to a *kohen*, and his lack of *biyado* is complete.

However, the Gemara notes a further complication: if Rabbi Yeshovav was specifically responding to Rabbi Simai's formulation — rather than making a free-standing claim about Rabbi Akiva's view — then Rabbi Yeshovav only disagrees with Rabbi Simai's example of *almanah* to *Kohen Gadol*, while perhaps conceding that an *issur asei* case (a prohibition derived from a positive commandment, such as a third-generation *Mitzri*) would still produce binding *kiddushin* even under Rabbi Akiva. In that event, even under the most restrictive reading, the father could theoretically marry his daughter to such a man and thereby render her forbidden to a *kohen*, and the *biyado* logic would resurface. The Gemara has thus reached a point where the *biyado* framework is under severe pressure.

### Rav Ashi Reframe

At this juncture, Rav Ashi intervenes to reject the entire *biyado* framework as an explanation for the *mishnah*. *V'tisbra* — is this really the correct reading? Do we truly believe that the reason the father is *ne'eman* in the *reisha* is because the matter is within his control? The objection is decisive on two fronts. First, *nehi d'biyado le-kadsha, biyado le-garsha*? — granted that he could initiate the marriage, but the divorce must be initiated by the husband, not the father. The father cannot compel a *get*. How then can *biyado* explain his believability about the divorce? Second, *ilu amar hai d'lo nicha begava mi matzi mekadesh leih lah* — even for the marriage itself, if the prospective groom were to say he is unwilling, the father could not force it through. The father is not in full control of either the marriage or the divorce. The *biyado* explanation therefore cannot be the true foundation of the *mishnah*.

### Torah Source

Rav Ashi therefore offers an entirely different explanation: *reisha rachmana heimneih* — in the *reisha* of the *mishnah*, the father is believed because the Torah directly granted him that believability. This is demonstrated by the *pasuk* cited by Rav Huna in the name of Rav: the verse *et biti natati la-ish hazeh* — the father's declaration in the *motzi shem ra* narrative is treated by the Torah as possessing halachic weight in court. From here we derive that a father is *ne'eman* to establish that he married off his daughter. Furthermore, parsing the verse: *la-ish asrah* — by saying "to a man," he creates a prohibition; *la-ish hazeh hitirah* — by saying "to *this* man," he identifies the husband and thereby makes her permissible to him specifically. From that same verse, then, we derive both his believability to declare the marriage and his believability to delimit its scope — and by extension, to confirm the divorce that released her.

### Limited Scope

This *ne'emanut* is therefore inherent and textually grounded, not derived from any logic of control or *mah li leshaker*. And precisely because it is textually grounded in the context of marriage, it is also textually limited: *be-nisuin heimneih rachmana l'av, be-shvuyah lo heimneih* — the Torah believed the father with respect to matters of marriage and *kiddushin*; it did not extend that believability to matters of captivity. The father's declaration that his daughter was taken captive is thus simply outside the scope of the Torah's grant, regardless of any question of *biyado*. She therefore retains her full permitted status vis-à-vis a *kohen*.

## Yibum Status

### Sons Claim

The *mishnah* now turns to a different application of the *ne'eman* principle, this time concerning a dying man's deathbed declarations about his family structure, which have direct bearing on whether his widow will be subject to *yibum*. If a man says on his deathbed *yesh li banim* — I have sons — he is believed, and his widow is thereby exempt from *yibum*. Even if no one knew of these sons and the presumed status would otherwise obligate her in *yibum*, the husband's declaration on his deathbed establishes their existence.

### Brothers Claim

Conversely, if a man says on his deathbed *yesh li achim* — I have brothers — when no brothers were previously known, he is *eino ne'eman*; he is not believed. His widow therefore remains in her prior status and does not become obligated in *yibum* or *chalitza* on the basis of his declaration.

### Permit Not Forbid

The pattern that emerges is: the husband is believed to permit — to free his wife from *yibum* — but not to forbid, not to impose upon her an obligation of *yibum* or *chalitza* that did not previously exist. The *mishnah* does not make explicit what the presumed status was before the husband spoke, nor does it explain the logical basis for this asymmetry, though one possible explanation involves *biyado*: he could have given her a *get* to free her from *yibum* entirely, and therefore when he speaks to that effect, he is believed.

### Not Rabbi Nathan

The Gemara notes: *neima matnitin d'lo k'Rabbi Nathan* — let us say our *mishnah* does not follow Rabbi Nathan's position.

### Contradictions

A *baraita* is cited presenting a more complex scenario. At the time of *kiddushin*, the husband said "I have children" — she need not worry about *yibum*. At the time of death he said "I have no children." Alternatively, at *kiddushin* he said "I have no brothers," and at death he said "I have brothers." In other words, his deathbed statement directly contradicts what he said at the time of marriage. This is a sharper case than our *mishnah*, where the husband simply makes a single declaration.

### Rabbi's Rule

Rabbi holds: *ne'eman lehatir ve'eino ne'eman le'asor* — even in this case of self-contradiction, one accepts the statement that permits and disregards the statement that forbids. The earlier statement at the time of marriage that exempted her from *yibum* is accepted; the later statement that would reimpose the obligation is rejected. This is more expansive than our *mishnah*, because here Rabbi maintains his position even in the face of explicit contradiction, accepting the permitting statement over the forbidding one regardless of which came first or last.

### Rabbi Nathan

Rabbi Nathan rules: *af ne'eman le'asor* — the husband is believed even to forbid, even to impose *yibum* obligation. The last statement he makes is credited with establishing the reality. This position appears incompatible with our *mishnah* at first glance, since our *mishnah* refuses to believe the husband when he says things that obligate *yibum*.

### Rava Harmony

Rava attempts to show that Rabbi Nathan could still agree with our *mishnah*. His argument is based on the specific logic of the *baraita* case: *keivan debishat mita kahadar bei, eima kushta ka'amar* — since the husband reverses himself on his deathbed, one might reasonably infer that he is now telling the truth. He deceived his wife at the time of marriage, telling her what she wanted to hear in order to ensure she would agree to marry him. Now, lying on his deathbed, laden with guilt, he recants and speaks the truth. Rabbi Nathan, Rava argues, believes the deathbed statement precisely because this narrative of guilt-driven truth-telling is plausible. In our *mishnah*, by contrast, the husband simply makes a statement without any prior contradiction. The issue of *yibum* never arose at the time of marriage. There is no backdrop of guilt and no prior false assurance. Indeed, one might suspect the opposite — that the dying man wants to make his wife's life difficult, as it were a final act of spite. In that context, Rava suggests, even Rabbi Nathan might concede that the husband is not believed to impose the obligation.

### Abaye Objection

Abaye rejects Rava's harmonization. His objection runs in the opposite direction: *hashta, mah hatam de'kamarei lei le'diburei, amarta kushta ka'amar* — in the *baraita*'s case, the husband contradicts his own prior statement, yet even there Rabbi Nathan accepts the deathbed declaration and imposes *yibum* obligation. Now in our *mishnah*, where the husband never made any prior statement and thus does not contradict himself, should we not *certainly* — *lo kol sheken* — accept his deathbed statement and worry about the *yibum* obligation? The fact that in the *baraita* there is a contradiction, which might otherwise undermine credibility, and Rabbi Nathan still credits the deathbed statement, should make it all the more compelling that in our *mishnah*, where there is no undermining contradiction, we credit his statement. Abaye concludes that the logic favors reading our *mishnah* as Rabbi's position, not Rabbi Nathan's, though the reasoning cuts sharply in either direction.

### Unresolved

As the *daf* closes, a number of questions remain unresolved. What exactly was the presumed status of the widow before the husband spoke in each case of the *mishnah*? What is the precise logical basis — whether *biyado*, presumption of status, or some other principle — for the rule that the husband is believed to permit but not to forbid? How do Rabbi Nathan's various scenarios cohere into a single principled position? These questions will be taken up when Abaye rereads the *mishnah* in the continuation of the sugya, though, as the teacher notes, not all the difficulties will be fully resolved even then.
