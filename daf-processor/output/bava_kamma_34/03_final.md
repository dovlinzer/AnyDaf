# Bava Kamma 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Akiva vs. Ishmael

### Ownership vs. Lien

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דִּמְשַׁעְבַּד לֵיהּ לְנִיזָּק – לָאו כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ מָכוּר.
>
> **Translation:** does he say that **since** the ox **is liened** to the debt **to the injured** party, who will collect it should the ox's owner not have sufficient funds, **it is not in his** power to sell it? Rav Naḥman **said to him: It is not sold.**

The sugya on 33b opens with the fundamental dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Ishmael concerning the nature of the *nizak*'s claim against the *tam* ox that gored. According to Rabbi Akiva, once a *tam* gores, the *nizak* immediately becomes a co-owner of the ox itself — the ox passes directly into the *nizak*'s possession. According to Rabbi Ishmael, by contrast, the *nizak* is owed money, but his claim is limited in recovery to the value of the ox: he may collect from the ox itself, but he remains fundamentally a creditor rather than an owner. The Gemara describes this as an *apoteiki* — a focused lien — on the ox. The *mazik* still owns the animal; the *nizak* simply holds an unusually powerful security interest in it.

### Machro/Hekdesho

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר מַעְרְבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מְכָרוֹ – אֵין מָכוּר, הִקְדִּישׁוֹ – מוּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Taḥalifa from the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **taught** the following *baraita* with regard to the belligerent ox **before Rabbi Abbahu:** If **he sold it, it is not sold,** but if **he consecrated it, it is consecrated.**

The Gemara records that Rav Tachlifa bar Marava taught the following *baraita* before Rabbi Abahu: *machro ein machur, hekdesho mukdash* — if he sold the ox, the sale does not hold; but if he sanctified it, it takes effect as *hekdesh*. The immediate question is: who is the subject of these actions — the *mazik* or the *nizak*? And why the apparent inconsistency between the two rulings?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם מַזִּיק, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל; ״מְכָרוֹ אֵינוֹ מָכוּר״ – אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּהָא מְשַׁעְבְּדָא לֵיהּ לְנִיזָּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Actually,** it is referring to **the one liable for the damage, and everyone agrees** with its ruling. The ruling that if **he sold it, it is not sold** is the *halakha* **even** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, as** the ox **is liened to the injured** party, precluding the owner from selling it.

The Gemara first considers whether we are speaking of the *mazik* who sells or sanctifies the ox. If the *mazik* sells it and the sale is invalid, that fits Rabbi Akiva's position that the *nizak* already owns the ox outright — but then why would the *mazik*'s sanctification take effect? Conversely, if we say the subject is the *nizak*, then the rule that his sale is ineffective fits Rabbi Ishmael — the *nizak* does not own the ox and therefore cannot sell it — but then why does his sanctification work, since under Rabbi Ishmael he has no ownership?

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: *le-olam mazik* — we are speaking throughout of the *mazik*, and the ruling operates according to both positions simultaneously.

### Lien Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מְשַׁעְבְּדָא לֵיהּ לְנִיזָּק.
>
> **Translation:** as the ox **is liened to the injured** party

The Gemara explains *machro ein machur* as follows: even according to Rabbi Ishmael, who holds that the *mazik* retains ownership of his ox, the sale is not fully valid — not in the sense that it is wholly void, but in the sense that it is not permanent. The *nizak* retains his lien and can retrieve the ox from the purchaser. The sale allows the buyer to use the ox for plowing (*le-radya*) until the *nizak* exercises his lien, but the *nizak*'s right to recover it remains intact. *Machro ein machur* thus does not mean the sale is entirely without effect; it means the sale cannot permanently defeat the *nizak*'s claim.

That said, the plain sense of the *baraita* — as the Gemara acknowledges — is considerably more permissive: it seems to say that before the court case, the *mazik* can fully remove the ox from the *nizak*'s reach. The Gemara is deliberately strengthening the *nizak*'s lien beyond what the surface reading of the *baraita* would suggest.

### Bedek Habayis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִקְדִּישׁוֹ מוּקְדָּשׁ״ – אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ; דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ הֶקְדֵּשׁ יוֹצֵא בְּלֹא פִּדְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The statement that if **he consecrated it,** it is **consecrated,** is the *halakha* **even** according **to Rabbi Akiva,** since it is not actually consecrated but is considered so only **due to** the statement **of Rabbi Abbahu. As Rabbi Abbahu says** that if one consecrates liened property, although the consecration does not take effect, nevertheless he is required to redeem it, due to a rabbinic **decree lest** people **say** that **consecrated** property can be **removed** from the ownership of the Temple treasury **without redemption.** Therefore, the ineffectiveness of the ox's consecration notwithstanding, he is still required to redeem it, by means of minimal payment, so as not to cause the denigration of Temple property.

As for *hekdesho mukdash* — that sanctification takes effect — the Gemara explains this as well. Even according to Rabbi Akiva, who says the *nizak* already owns the ox, the *mazik*'s act of sanctification has a degree of legal force, because of the principle taught by Rabbi Abahu.

Rabbi Abahu rules that there is a rabbinic concern that *hekdesh* not appear to dissolve without a proper act of *pidyon* — lest people say that *hekdesh yotzei belo pidyon*, that consecrated property can be removed from its sacred status without redemption. Now, when property subject to a creditor's lien is sanctified to *bedek habayis* — to the Temple maintenance fund, as opposed to being consecrated as a *korban* — the sanctification is treated like a sale to a third party. The *hekdesh* of *bedek habayis* represents the Temple's proprietary ownership, and therefore, just as a *nizak* can retrieve an ox from a third-party purchaser by exercising his lien, so too he can retrieve it from *hekdesh*. His lien trumps even the Temple's claim.

Nevertheless, because the ox was genuinely held as *hekdesh* in the interim, the *nizak* must perform some nominal act of redemption — he gives a small amount to *hekdesh* — so that it does not appear as though sacred property simply loses its *kedusha* automatically. Under Rabbi Akiva, the *nizak* truly owned the ox from the moment of the goring, and the sanctification never really took hold; yet even so, the Gemara applies Rabbi Abahu's principle, requiring a token *pidyon* so as not to create the appearance of *hekdesh* dissolving without redemption. Under Rabbi Ishmael, the *nizak*'s lien genuinely yields to the *hekdesh* during the interim period, making the *pidyon* all the more clearly necessary before the *nizak* retrieves it.

## Mishnah: Tam Rules

### Amad Ba-Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שׁוֹר תָּם שֶׁהִזִּיק; עַד שֶׁלֹּא עָמַד בַּדִּין – מְכָרוֹ מָכוּר, הִקְדִּישׁוֹ מוּקְדָּשׁ, שְׁחָטוֹ וּנְתָנוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה – מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי. מִשֶּׁעָמַד בַּדִּין – מְכָרוֹ אֵינוֹ מָכוּר, הִקְדִּישׁוֹ אֵינוֹ מוּקְדָּשׁ, שְׁחָטוֹ וּנְתָנוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה – לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an innocuous ox that caused damage,** if, **before** its owner **stood trial, he sold it,** it is **sold.** If **he consecrated it,** it is **consecrated.** If **he slaughtered it or gave it as a gift, what he did is done,** i.e., takes effect. By contrast, **once he stood trial** and is now obligated to pay the injured party, if **he sold it, it is not sold;** if **he consecrated it, it is not consecrated;** if **he slaughtered it or gave it is a gift, he has done nothing.**

The *baraita* restates the governing framework. In the case of a *tam* that caused damage, the rules turn critically on whether the matter has come before *beit din* — *amad ba-din*, literally "stood in judgment." The *baraita* distinguishes sharply between what the *mazik* may do before and after that moment.

### Pre-Court Actions

Before *amad ba-din*, the *baraita* rules: *makhar machur, hekdesho mukdash* — if the *mazik* sells the ox, the sale is effective; if he sanctifies it, the sanctification holds; if he slaughters it and gives it away as a gift, what he has done is done. As noted above, the Gemara significantly limits the plain reading of these rulings. The sale is "effective" only in the interim sense that the buyer may use the ox for plowing until the *nizak* exercises his lien. The sanctification works, but the *nizak* may still retrieve the ox from *hekdesh* after performing a nominal act of *pidyon* in keeping with Rabbi Abahu's principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא נְתָנוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה, מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי – לְרִדְיָא; אֶלָּא שְׁחָטוֹ – לֵיתֵי וְלִשְׁתַּלַּם מִבִּשְׂרֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the statement that if **he slaughtered it or gave it as a present, what he did is done,** the Gemara asks: **Granted,** if **he gave it as a present, what he did is done** with regard to the recipient's permission to use it **for plowing. But** if **he slaughtered it,** how does that affect the injured party's rights? **Let him come and receive payment from** the slaughtered ox's **meat.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״חַי״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא חַי, שְׁחָטוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּמָכְרוּ אֶת הַשּׁוֹר״ מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: It is stated in the Torah: "Then they shall sell the **live** ox" (Exodus 21:35). **I have** derived **only** that the injured party receives a share of ownership if the belligerent ox is **alive. From where** do I derive that this applies even if the ox's owner **slaughtered it? The verse states: "Then they shall sell** the live **ox,"** indicating that **in any case,** whatever the circumstances, the injured party is paid from proceeds of the sale of the belligerent ox.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לִפְחַת שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi said:** This statement **is necessary only** with regard **to the diminished** value of the ox due to its **slaughter.** Although the value of the ox may no longer cover the damage, its owner is not liable to compensate the injured party beyond the ox's current value.

What of slaughter? The *baraita* states that if the *mazik* slaughtered the ox and gave it away, *mah she-asa asui* — what he did stands. The Gemara asks: why should this be final? Let the *nizak* come and collect from the meat. A *baraita* supports this: the verse says *umachru et hashor hachai* — sell the *living* ox — which might suggest the *nizak*'s rights expire upon slaughter. But the continuation, *umachru et hashor*, without the word *hachai*, teaches that the *nizak* still has rights even to a slaughtered animal.

Rav Shizbi therefore explains that *mah she-asa asui* in the case of slaughter does not mean the *nizak* loses his claim entirely. Rather, it refers to *pachat shechita* — the depreciation caused by the act of slaughter itself. If after slaughter the carcass is worth only eighty of an original hundred-dollar value, the *nizak* may collect eighty but not the full hundred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, הַמַּזִּיק שִׁעְבּוּדוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: That is to say** that **one who causes damage to another's liened property is exempt** from paying compensation, since the property does not actually belong to the one who holds the lien.

This leads to the principle stated by Rav Avuya, son of Rav Yehoshua: *ha-mazik shibudo shel chaveiro patur* — one who damages property upon which another holds a lien is exempt. I owe you a hundred dollars; you hold a lien on my plot of land; I go and destroy that land — I am not liable for the indirect loss to your security interest. The land was mine when I damaged it. Similarly, when the *mazik* slaughters the ox, it is still legally his at that moment, so the *nizak*'s resulting loss is treated as an indirect consequence, not a direct act of damage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי רַבָּה אַמְרַהּ – דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: הַשּׂוֹרֵף שְׁטָרוֹתָיו שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: **Rabba stated this** principle, **as well,** and there would be no need for Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, to state it. **As Rabba says: One who burns another's documents,** in which other people's debts to him are recorded, **is exempt,** although the owner of the documents can no longer collect payment from liened property.

The Gemara notes that this same principle was already derivable from a ruling of Rava — *ha-soref shtaro shel chaveiro patur*: one who burns another's debt document is exempt. He destroyed a piece of paper; the underlying debt still exists; the loss of the ability to prove it in court is indirect. The Gemara works through why neither of these prior cases would have been sufficient to generate the full principle, and why the case of slaughter adds something: slaughtering an animal and reducing it to a carcass is, in terms of real destruction, equivalent to digging ditches into a field held under lien — and yet even in that case one is exempt. These acts fall under the broader principle of *grama* and *dinei de-garmi* — indirect causation — a topic the Gemara will address more fully later in the tractate.

### Post-Court Control

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָדְמוּ בַּעֲלֵי חוֹבוֹת (וְהִגְבִּיהוּ) [וְגָבוּהוּ]; בֵּין חָב עַד שֶׁלֹּא הִזִּיק, בֵּין הִזִּיק עַד שֶׁלֹּא חָב – לֹא עָשָׂוּ וְלֹא כְּלוּם, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מִשְׁתַּלֵּם אֶלָּא מִגּוּפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **creditors collected** the innocuous ox **first, whether** its owner **owed** them **before** his ox **caused damage or whether it caused damage before he owed** them, they **have done nothing.** Their collection is void, **because** restitution is **paid only from the body of** the ox, as it was innocuous, and it is therefore designated exclusively for this compensation.

Once there has been *amad ba-din*, the *baraita* rules differently: *makhar eino machur, hekdesho eino mukdash* — a sale by the *mazik* is invalid, and a sanctification does not take effect. At this point the *nizak*'s rights have been fully established by the court, and the *mazik* can no longer act unilaterally to undercut them.

It is worth pausing here to note a point raised by *Tosafot* and debated extensively among the *rishonim* and *acharonim*. According to the normative ruling that *chatzi nezek knasa* — that the half-damages paid by a *tam* are a fine rather than ordinary compensation — a fine does not take on legal reality until there is *ha'amadah be-din*. Normally, the moment I borrow money from you or break your property, I owe you money as an objective fact; the court merely clarifies and enforces a pre-existing reality. A *knas*, however, is different: the court does not merely identify the obligation, it creates and imposes it. Before the court acts, I owe you nothing.

Given this, one might have expected that even according to Rabbi Akiva — who says the *nizak* becomes an immediate co-owner — the ownership should not arise until *ha'amadah be-din*, since the underlying debt is a *knas*. Nevertheless, the Gemara understands that according to Rabbi Akiva the *nizak* becomes a co-owner automatically, without any court action. This may be because the verse *umachru et hashor hachai* frames the arrangement as an immediate operative reality. The *baraita* before us, which draws a distinction based on *amad ba-din*, is accordingly read by the Gemara as functioning within the framework of Rabbi Ishmael, who attributes greater rights to the *mazik* before the court acts.

### Creditors vs. Nizak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: מַה שֶּׁגָּבָה גָּבָה; וְשָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ גַּבָּךְ הֲוָה – לָא מִינָּךְ (הֲוָה) גְּבַי לֵיהּ? דְּהַאי תּוֹרָא דְּאַזְּקַן, מִינֵּיהּ מִשְׁתַּלְּמְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No; actually, I** could **say to you** that **the collection of** the later creditor **is valid, and there,** in the case where a creditor seized the belligerent ox, **it is different, as** the injured party **can say to** the creditor who seized the ox: **If** the ox **was in your possession, would I not have collected it from you?** The reason I would take it is **that I am paid from this ox that caused me damage.** Therefore, the creditor cannot collect the ox, even if the debt preceded the damage.

The *baraita* then addresses the claim of the *mazik*'s general creditors. *Kidmu ba'alei chovot ve-higbiu* — if the creditors came and collected the ox — *bein chav ad she-lo hizik bein hizik ad she-lo chav*, regardless of whether the debt to the creditors arose before or after the damage to the *nizak*'s ox, *lo asu velo klum* — what they did has no effect — *lefi she-ein meshalem ela migufo* — because the *nizak*'s collection is from the body of the ox itself.

This is striking, and the Gemara presses the point. If the *mazik* owed the creditors money before the damage occurred, the creditors held an earlier lien — *ba'alei chov kodem* — so why should the *nizak* be able to displace them? And even if the damage preceded the loan, perhaps the creditors, as the first to actually seize the ox, should be entitled to keep it. The Gemara raises the general question of whether a later creditor who seizes property first is allowed to retain it — and in ordinary circumstances, the answer may well be yes.

The Gemara responds: *le-olam eima lach mah she-gava gava* — ordinarily, what a creditor seizes he may keep. But this case is different, because the *nizak* can say to the creditor: *ilu gabakh havei lav minach havah gavina lei* — had the ox always been in your possession, I would still have had the right to collect from it, since it is the very ox that damaged me. *De-hai tora de-azkin minei mishtalamna* — it is from this ox, the ox that gored mine, that I collect.

*Tosafot* notes that this argument is ultimately just a sophisticated articulation of the *baraita*'s own conclusion — *lefi she-ein meshalem ela migufo*. The *nizak*'s lien is focused specifically on the body of the ox, not on the *mazik*'s assets generally. The creditor's lien, by contrast, attaches to all the *mazik*'s property indiscriminately. Because the *nizak* has this uniquely focused claim on the specific ox, he has a superior right to that animal over any general creditor, regardless of the order in which the debts arose or the property was seized. The creditor is not left without recourse — he may turn to the *mazik*'s other assets — but he cannot hold this particular ox against the *nizak*'s focused claim.

## Damage Assessment

### Shevach Mazik

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם, וְחָבַל בּוֹ בַּחֲמִשִּׁים זוּז, וְשָׁבַח וְעָמַד עַל אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זוּז – שֶׁאִלְמָלֵא לֹא הִזִּיקוֹ הָיָה עוֹמֵד עַל שְׁמֹנֶה מֵאוֹת זוּז; נוֹתֵן כִּשְׁעַת הַנֶּזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** With regard to an innocuous **ox worth two hundred** dinars **that gored** another **ox** worth **two hundred** dinars **and injured it,** reducing its value **by fifty dinars [*zuz*], and** the injured ox subsequently **appreciated** in value, **and** its value **stood at four hundred dinars, while if** the belligerent ox **had not injured it, its** value **would have** now **stood at eight hundred dinars,** in this case the owner of the belligerent ox **gives him** only twenty-five dinars, which is half the value of the damage **according to** its value at **the time the injury** occurred.

The Gemara turns to a *baraita* that explores the principles governing how damage is assessed when the value of the animals changes between the moment of the goring and the time the case reaches court.

The case: an ox worth 200 gores an ox worth 200, causing 50 worth of damage. Afterward, the *nizak*'s injured ox appreciates — it is now worth 400, and had it not been damaged it would have been worth 800. How much does the *mazik* pay? The rule is *noten ke-sha'at ha-nezek* — payment is assessed at the moment of the damage. The damage was 50 at the time of the goring, so the *mazik* pays 25 (half of 50, as this is a *tam*). One cannot claim that because the ox would have been worth 800 but is now only worth 400, the damage amounts to 400 — that increased loss is treated as indirect, a loss of anticipated profit that does not enter into the assessment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁבַח מַזִּיק – נוֹתֵן לוֹ כִּשְׁעַת הַנֶּזֶק. כָּחַשׁ – כִּשְׁעַת הַעֲמָדָה בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** If the ox **that caused the injury,** from which the owner of the injured ox collects damages, **appreciated** in value, its owner **gives** the injured party a share of the ox **according to** its value **at the time of the injury.** If **it depreciated** in value, the owner gives him a share **according to** its value at **the time of standing trial.**

### Pachat at Amad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּחַשׁ – כִּשְׁעַת הַעֲמָדָה בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** If the injured ox **depreciated** in value and its worth is now less than at the time it was injured, the damage is evaluated **according to** the ox's worth at **the time of standing trial.** In other words, the owner of the belligerent ox must pay based on the lower value, as though the continued decline is part of the original injury.

The *baraita* then addresses the reverse: *kachash ke-sha'at ha-amadah ba-din* — if the *nizak*'s ox depreciated by the time of the court case. Suppose it was worth 200 at the time of the goring, the damage was 50, but by the time of *amad ba-din* it had fallen further in value to 100. In that case, the *mazik* must pay based on the depreciated value — effectively 50 now constitutes proportionally greater damage, and payment is calculated accordingly.

This asymmetry is notable. When the ox goes up in value, the *nizak* gains nothing from the increase — but when it goes down, the *mazik* must pay based on the lower value, as though the continued decline is part of the original injury. This seems inconsistent: if the *nizak* holds a percentage of the animal (à la Rabbi Akiva), he should gain when it rises and lose when it falls. If he is only owed a fixed sum (à la Rabbi Ishmael), he should always recover that fixed sum regardless of subsequent fluctuations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: שָׁבַח מַזִּיק – נוֹתֵן כִּשְׁעַת הַנֶּזֶק. מַנִּי – רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בַּעַל חוֹב הוּא, וְזוּזֵי הוּא דְּמַסֵּיק לֵיהּ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **The Master said** in the first clause of the second half of the *baraita* that if the ox **that caused the damage appreciated** in value, its owner **gives** the injured party a share **according to** the ox's value at **the time of the injury.** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is** this ruling? **It is** apparently in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who says** that the injured party **is** considered **a creditor, and it is money that he is claiming from him,** as he has no share of ownership in the belligerent ox.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא; וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּשֶׁפִּיטְּמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No, it is all** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** that they are partners, **and here,** in the case where the belligerent ox appreciated in value, **we are dealing with** a case **where** the reason it appreciated is that its owner **fattened it.** Though the injured party has a share of ownership in the ox, he has no share in the appreciation in value, and his share is therefore calculated according to its value at the time of the injury.

The Gemara asks of the *seifa* — the depreciation case — that it seems to operate like Rabbi Akiva (the *nizak* bears a percentage interest), while the *reisha* — the appreciation case — seems to operate like Rabbi Ishmael. Does the *baraita* split between the two opinions? The Gemara answers: *lo kula Rabbi Akiva hi* — the entire *baraita* is according to Rabbi Akiva. Both rules operate on the premise that the *nizak* is a co-owner.

### Continued Injury

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּחַשׁ מֵחֲמַת מַאי? אִילֵימָא דְּכָחֲשָׁה מֵחֲמַת מְלָאכָה, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: אַתְּ מַכְחֲשַׁתְּ, וַאֲנָא יָהֵיבְנָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Due to what did it depreciate** in value? **If we say that** the ox **depreciated** in value **due to labor** for which it was used, **let** the liable party **say to him: You reduced** its **value and I should** therefore **give** you extra compensation?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: דִּכְחַשׁ מֵחֲמַת מַכָּה, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: קַרְנָא דְּתוֹרָךְ קְבִירָא בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** It is a case **where it depreciated** in value **due to the** continuous effect of the **wound, as** the injured party **can say to him: The horn of your ox is buried in it,** i.e., it is still losing value due to the wound.

The reason for the asymmetry lies in the specific circumstances of each case. The *baraita*'s statement that the *mazik* pays only the original 50 even when the ox appreciated to 400 refers to a case where the *mazik* fattened the animal — *ke-she-pitmu*. The increase in value resulted from the *mazik*'s own investment and effort. Even if the *nizak* is a co-owner, he cannot fully claim the benefit of appreciation that was generated by the *mazik*'s expenditure. The Gemara adds via Rav Pappa that the *reisha* also teaches a separate *chiddush* for cases of natural appreciation: even if the ox simply became worth more on its own, the *mazik* is not excused from paying the original 50 on the theory that the animal is now worth more than it was originally — because the gap between 400 and 800 demonstrates that real damage was done. *Tosafot* notes, however, that this resolution is not entirely clean: even partners in a field, when one improves it unilaterally, generally cannot exclude the other partner from all benefit, and the precise allocation remains a subject of discussion among the *rishonim*.

As for the depreciation case: the Gemara asks, *kachash machmat mai* — what caused the ox to decline in value? If the *nizak* himself worked the animal into decline, the *mazik* could reasonably say: you diminished it yourself. Rav Ashi answers that the case in view is *kachash machmat makah* — the ox declined because of the original wound. The injury kept worsening. In such a case, the *nizak* can say *kurna de-torach kevira beh* — the horn of your ox is still embedded in my ox, meaning the original injury is the ongoing cause of every subsequent decline. This is not *grama* — it is not treated as an indirect consequence that falls outside the *mazik*'s liability. Rather, it is how the full extent of the original injury is properly assessed. The damage is not capped at the 50 measured at the moment of the goring; it is measured by how much worse the injury ultimately became.

The overall principle that emerges: appreciation of the *mazik*'s ox generates no windfall for the *nizak* — that is an indirect matter of lost profit. But continued depreciation of the *nizak*'s ox as a direct result of the original wound is part of the damage itself, assessed at the time of *amad ba-din*. Under Rabbi Akiva, the *nizak* as co-owner loses when the animal loses value, but gains from the original owner's investment only to a limited degree. These are the practical *nafka minas* between the two positions — and the Gemara has significantly strengthened the *nizak*'s lien even under Rabbi Ishmael, against what the plain reading of the *baraita* would suggest.

## Torah Case Debate

### R. Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם, וְאֵין הַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה כְּלוּם – אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר, עַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״וּמָכְרוּ אֶת הַשּׁוֹר הַחַי וְחָצוּ אֶת כַּסְפּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to an innocuous **ox worth two hundred** dinars **that gored** another **ox worth two hundred, and the carcass is worth nothing, Rabbi Meir said:** It is **about this** type of case that **it is stated: "Then they shall sell the live ox, and divide its monetary value"** (Exodus 21:35).

The Gemara now turns to the *baraita* that parses the precise scriptural case in the Torah. The verse (*Shemot* 21:35) reads: *umachru et hashor hachai vechatzu et kaspo vegam et ha-met yechetzun* — they shall sell the live ox and split its proceeds, and they shall also split the dead one. The question is: what scenario does the Torah envision?

Rabbi Meir says: *al zeh ne'emar* — the Torah is speaking to the case of an ox worth 200 that gores an ox worth 200, where the *neveilah* — the carcass — is worth nothing at all. In that case, the debt is 100 (the full value of the dead animal), and since it is a *tam*, chatzi nezek is 50. The verse *umachru et hashor hachai vechatzu et kaspo* addresses this case directly. But then how does Rabbi Meir read *vegam et ha-met yechetzun*? He explains: this does not mean physically divide the carcass. Rather, *pachat she-pichas'o mitah machatzin be-chai* — the depreciation caused by the death is split within the context of the live ox. *Vegam et ha-met yechetzun* refers to splitting the loss, not splitting the carcass.

### R. Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וְכֵן הֲלָכָה; קִיַּימְתָּ ״וּמָכְרוּ אֶת הַשּׁוֹר הַחַי וְחָצוּ אֶת כַּסְפּוֹ״, וְלֹא קִיַּימְתָּ ״וְגַם הַמֵּת יֶחֱצוּן״! וְאֵיזֶה? זֶה שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם, וְהַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז; שֶׁזֶּה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said to him: And that is** the ***halakha*,** yet your interpretation of the verse is incorrect. **You have upheld** the clause: **"Then they shall sell the live ox and divide its monetary value,"** which fits your interpretation of the case. But **you have not upheld** the latter clause of the verse: **"And the dead they shall also divide,"** since in the case you mentioned the carcass is worthless. Rather, to **which** case is the verse referring? **It is** the case of **an ox worth two hundred** dinars **that gored** another **ox worth two hundred** dinars, **and the carcass is worth fifty dinars.** In this case, **this** party **takes half** the value of **the living** ox, one hundred dinars, **and half the** value of the **dead** ox, twenty-five dinars; **and that** party also **takes half** the value of **the living** ox **and half** the value of **the dead** ox.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees. He says: yes, the ruling you describe is correct, and yes, that is the *halacha* — but that is not the case the Torah is addressing. The Torah speaks of an ox worth 200 goring an ox worth 200 where the *neveilah* is worth 50. In that situation, the *mazik* and *nizak* each take half of the live ox and half of the carcass, walking away with 125 each. Each lost 75 from an original holding of 200. The total damage was 150, and chatzi nezek is 75 — perfectly consistent with half-damages. The verse *vegam et ha-met yechetzun* means exactly what it says: they divide the carcass.

Rabbi Yehuda's objection to Rabbi Meir's case is pointed: if the *neveilah* is worth nothing, why does the Torah bother saying *vegam et ha-met yechetzun* — why legislate a division of a worthless object? The scriptural instruction to split the dead animal implies the dead animal has value. Hence the Torah must be speaking to the case where the carcass is worth 50.

### Pachat Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם, וְהַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז – זֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת, וְזֶהוּ שׁוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּתּוֹרָה; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** With regard to **an ox worth two hundred** dinars **that gored an ox worth two hundred** dinars, **and the carcass is worth fifty dinars, this** party **takes half** the value of **the living** ox **and half** the value of **the dead** ox, **and that** party also **takes half** the value of **the living** ox **and half** the value of **the dead** ox, **and this is the** case of the belligerent **ox stated in the Torah.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵין זֶהוּ שׁוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּתּוֹרָה, אֶלָּא שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם שֶׁנָּגַח לְשׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם, וְאֵין הַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה כְּלוּם, עַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״וּמָכְרוּ אֶת הַשּׁוֹר הַחַי וְחָצוּ אֶת כַּסְפּוֹ״. אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְגַם אֶת הַמֵּת יֶחֱצוּן״? פְּחָת שֶׁפְּחָתַתּוּ מִיתָה – מַחֲצִין בַּחַי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says: This is not** the case of the **ox stated in the Torah. Rather,** it is the case of **an ox worth two hundred** dinars **that gored an ox worth two hundred** dinars, **and the carcass is worth nothing.** It is **about this** case that **it is stated** in the verse: **"Then they shall sell the live ox, and divide its monetary value." Rather, how do I realize** the meaning of **"And the dead they shall also divide"?** This clause means that the **diminished** value of the dead ox **that was diminished by** its **death** is compensated for by **dividing** the value of **the live** ox between the two parties, while its owner does not receive a share in the carcass of the dead ox.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי בֵּין רַבִּי מֵאִיר בֵּין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הַאי מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה שָׁקֵיל, וְהַאי מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה שָׁקֵיל; מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now, both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda** hold that in the case described by Rabbi Yehuda, where the carcass is worth fifty, **this** party **takes one hundred and twenty-five, and that** party **takes one hundred and twenty-five.** Rabbi Meir holds that the injured party keeps the carcass, worth fifty, and receives another seventy-five dinars, half the value of the damage, from the proceeds of the sale of the belligerent ox. Its owner is thereby left with one hundred and twenty-five dinars out of the two hundred that it is worth. Rabbi Yehuda maintains that the value of both oxen is split between the two parties, leaving each with one hundred and twenty-five. If so, **what is** the difference **between** the two *tanna'im*? Why does it matter in which manner the compensation is calculated?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּחַת נְבֵילָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: פְּחַת נְבֵילָה – דְּנִיזָּק הָוֵי, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: פְּחַת נְבֵילָה – דְּמַזִּיק הָוֵי פַּלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to **the diminishing** value **of the carcass.** If the carcass depreciates in value between the ox's death and the time it is sold, **Rabbi Meir holds** that **the diminishing** value **of the carcass** is sustained **by the injured** party. The one liable for the damage is not responsible for any depreciation in value that occurs after the loss caused by the attack has been evaluated. **And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **half of the diminishing** value **of the carcass** is sustained **by the one liable for damage,** since he receives half of the dead ox.

What is actually at stake between them? The Gemara notes that in the case Rabbi Yehuda describes — the *neveilah* worth 50 — both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree on the arithmetic: each party walks away with 125 and absorbs a loss of 75. *Mai beinaihu* — what do they actually dispute?

Rava answers: *pachat neveilah ika beinaihu* — the question of who bears the depreciation of the carcass. If after the goring the carcass continues to lose value, who absorbs that loss — the *nizak* or the *mazik*? Rabbi Meir holds *pachat neveilah de-nizak havei* — the depreciation of the carcass falls entirely on the *nizak*. The *mazik* pays for the loss at the time of death, but the carcass itself and any further decline in its value belong to the *nizak*. Rabbi Yehuda, insisting that the Torah says *vegam et ha-met yechetzun*, holds that the carcass is actually split between them, making the *mazik* a part-owner in the carcass — and therefore *pachat neveilah de-mazik havei palga*: the *mazik* absorbs half of any subsequent depreciation.

### Tam vs. Muad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִם כֵּן, מָצִינוּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה תָּם חָמוּר מִמּוּעָד!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **If so, we have found, according to Rabbi Yehuda, a case where the *halakha* with regard to an innocuous** ox that causes damage **is more stringent than** with regard to **a forewarned** ox, since according to all opinions, in a case where a forewarned ox kills another ox, the carcass belongs exclusively to the injured party, and he sustains any subsequent decrease in its value.

Abaye immediately raises an objection: *matzinu le-Rabbi Yehuda tam chamur mi-muad* — according to Rabbi Yehuda, a *tam* would be stricter in this respect than a *muad*. In every other category of damage — *bor*, *muad*, and so forth — the *nizak* takes the carcass and the *mazik* pays for the loss. The *mazik* never bears the depreciation of the carcass. A *tam*, which is universally the most lenient category of payment, would according to Rabbi Yehuda uniquely impose on the *mazik* a share of the carcass's ongoing decline.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי – כְּדִתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: תָּם חַיָּיב, וּמוּעָד פָּטוּר; אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – לְעִנְיַן שְׁמִירָה, דִּכְתִיבִי קְרָאֵי; לְעִנְיַן תַּשְׁלוּמִין מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **indeed,** Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *halakha* with regard to an innocuous ox is more stringent than with regard to a forewarned ox, **as we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If a bailee did not safeguard an ox properly, and it escaped and caused damage, if it was **an innocuous** ox he **is liable, and** if it was **a forewarned** ox he **is exempt** (45b), that mishna cannot serve as proof for the issue under discussion. **Say that you heard Rabbi Yehuda** express this opinion, that the *halakha* with regard to an innocuous ox is more stringent, **with regard to** the *halakhot* of **safeguarding, as verses are written** in the Torah from which this *halakha* is derived. But **with regard to compensation, did you** ever **hear him** express this opinion?

One might respond: perhaps Rabbi Yehuda holds that a *tam* is indeed stricter in certain respects. After all, elsewhere Rabbi Yehuda rules — as will appear later in the tractate — that the minimum level of *shemira* required to be exempt from liability is actually higher for a *tam* than for a *muad*. Perhaps Rabbi Yehuda accepts that a *tam* can impose greater obligations.

The Gemara rejects this parallel. Rabbi Yehuda's stringency regarding *shemira* for a *tam* is derived from specific scriptural verses — *dikesivei kra*. But one has never heard Rabbi Yehuda apply greater stringency to a *tam* in the domain of *tashlumin*, of the actual payments. Payment by a *tam* is always more lenient. The *baraita* itself confirms this: Rabbi Yehuda says there — *tam ha-kal kol she-ken* — if a *muad* pays only the actual loss without any windfall from the split, then a *tam*, which is the more lenient category, certainly cannot produce a result that benefits the *nizak* beyond his actual loss. Rabbi Yehuda explicitly rejects the possibility that a *tam* would ever be more burdensome for the *mazik* than a *muad* in terms of what is owed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָנֶה שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים, וְהַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה סֶלַע – זֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת?
>
> **Translation:** On the contrary; **but isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One **might** have thought that in the case of **an ox worth one hundred dinars that gored an ox worth five *sela*,** i.e., twenty dinars, **and the carcass is worth one *sela*,** i.e., four dinars, the *halakha* is that **this** party **takes half the living** ox **and half the dead** ox, **and that** party **takes half the living** ox **and half the dead** ox. Accordingly, the injured party receives fifty-two dinars in value, which is much more than his ox was worth before it was killed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: וְכִי מוּעָד לָמָּה יוֹצֵא – לְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו אוֹ לְהָקֵל עָלָיו? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר לְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו; וּמָה מוּעָד אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיק, תָּם הַקַּל לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** Instead, continues Rabbi Yehuda, you should **say: For what** purpose **is** the case of **a forewarned** ox **singled out** by the *halakha*? Is it **to render it** more **stringent** than the case of an innocuous ox, or **to render it** more **lenient?** Clearly, **you must say** that it is **to render it** more **stringent.** And therefore, **if** in the case of **a forewarned** ox, its owner **pays only** the value of **what he damaged** and no more, in the case of **an innocuous** ox, **which is** more **lenient, all the more so** is it **not** clear that the owner is not liable to pay more than the value of the damage? Evidently, Rabbi Yehuda does not allow for liability in the case of an innocuous ox to be more stringent than in the case of a forewarned ox.

### Shevach Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁבַח נְבֵילָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, דְּמָר סָבַר: דְּנִיזָּק הָוֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: פַּלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rava's explanation, that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda disagree with regard to a case where the carcass depreciated in value, should be rejected. Instead, their dispute should be explained according to what **Rabbi Yoḥanan said,** i.e., that the practical difference **between them** is with regard to **the appreciation of the carcass** in value after the ox's death; **as** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that the entire increased value of the carcass **belongs to the injured** party, since the owner of the belligerent ox has no share in the carcass, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **half** of the increased value belongs to the injured party and half goes to the one liable for the damage.

Rabbi Yochanan therefore proposes a different resolution of the dispute. The argument between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda is not about *pachat neveilah* — both agree the depreciation of the carcass falls on the *nizak* alone. Rather, the dispute concerns *shevach neveilah* — appreciation of the carcass.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּקָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ חָס רַחֲמָנָא עִילָּוֵיהּ דְּמַזִּיק, דְּשָׁקֵיל בִּשְׁבָחָא; יָכוֹל שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָנֶה, וְהַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז – זֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת?
>
> **Translation:** **And this** explains **what was difficult for Rabbi Yehuda** in another *baraita*. **Now that you say** that **the Merciful One has mercy on the one liable for damage, as he takes** a share **of the increased value** of the carcass, **can** it be that in the case of **an ox worth five *sela*,** i.e., twenty dinars, **that gored an ox worth one hundred dinars, and the carcass is worth fifty dinars, this** party **takes half the live** ox **and half the dead** ox, **and that** party **takes half the live** ox **and half the dead** ox? Accordingly, the owner of the belligerent ox receives thirty-five dinars in value, which is more than his ox was worth.

If after the goring the carcass increases in value — perhaps hides became more valuable, or the meat fetched a better price — who benefits from that increase? Rabbi Meir holds that *shevach neveilah de-nizak havei* — the appreciation belongs entirely to the *nizak*, who holds the carcass. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *shevach neveilah de-mazik havei palga* — because the *mazik* is a part-owner in the carcass (per the verse *vegam et ha-met yechetzun*), he shares in any increase in value. Crucially, this means the *mazik* benefits from the appreciation: his effective payment is reduced, since the *nizak* must now credit the *mazik* with his share of the increased carcass value. This is a leniency for the *mazik* rather than a stringency, which is precisely why Rabbi Yehuda embraces it — it is consistent with the principle that a *tam* never imposes greater payment than a *muad*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: הֵיכָן מָצִינוּ מַזִּיק נִשְׂכָּר, שֶׁזֶּה נִשְׂכָּר? וְאוֹמֵר: ״שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם״ – בְּעָלִים מְשַׁלְּמִין, וְאֵין בְּעָלִים נוֹטְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **you** should **say: Where do we find** a case where **the one liable for damage gains** from the damage, comparable to this case, **where this** owner of the belligerent ox **gains,** receiving more than the value of his ox? Clearly there is no such case, as one who is liable for damage does not gain from it. **And** in addition, **it says: "He shall pay"** (Exodus 21:36), indicating that **the owner** of the belligerent ox **pays, and the owner does not take** more than the amount his animal was worth at the time of the damage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא פְּסֵידָא לְנִיזָּק; אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא פְּסֵידָא לְנִיזָּק – כְּגוֹן שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים, וְהַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה שְׁלֹשִׁים זוּז – שָׁקֵיל נָמֵי מַזִּיק בִּשְׁבָחָא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: For **what** reason was it necessary to add the claim: **And it says:** "He shall pay"? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **this matter,** that the one liable for damage does not gain, **applies** specifically **where there is a loss sustained by the injured** party, as in this case, where the decrease in the ox's value is fifty dinars, and if the carcass is shared by the liable party, the injured party receives only thirty-five dinars, thereby sustaining a loss. **But** in a case **where there is no loss sustained by the injured** party, **for example,** the case of **an ox worth five *sela*,** i.e., twenty dinars, **that gored** another **ox worth five *sela*, and the carcass** appreciated in value and is now **worth thirty dinars,** more than the ox was worth when it was alive, in this case **the one liable for damage also takes** a share **of the increased value,** since the injured party ends up not having sustained any financial loss, as he receives a share of ten dinars in the belligerent ox and another fifteen in the carcass, gaining five dinars over the value of his ox.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם״ – בְּעָלִים מְשַׁלְּמִין, וְאֵין בְּעָלִים נוֹטְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** It is to counter this claim that Rabbi Yehuda adds: **And it says: "He shall pay,"** indicating that **the owner** of the belligerent ox **pays, and the owner does not take** more than the amount his animal was worth at the time of the damage.

*De-mar savar de-nizak havei u-mar savar palga* — one holds the appreciation belongs entirely to the *nizak*, the other holds it is split. This is what prompted Rabbi Yehuda's analysis: the Torah goes easy on the *mazik* in the case of a *tam*, making him a part-owner of the carcass not to burden him with its depreciation but to allow him to benefit from its appreciation — thereby reducing what he effectively pays. The verse *vegam et ha-met yechetzun*, on this reading, extends a leniency: it gives the *mazik* a share in the upside of the carcass, ensuring that chatzi nezek in a *tam* is never more burdensome than full payment in a *muad*.
