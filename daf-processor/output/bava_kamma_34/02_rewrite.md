# Bava Kamma 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Akiva vs. Ishmael

### Ownership vs. Lien

The sugya on 33b opens with the fundamental dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Ishmael concerning the nature of the *nizak*'s claim against the *tam* ox that gored. According to Rabbi Akiva, once a *tam* gores, the *nizak* immediately becomes a co-owner of the ox itself — the ox passes directly into the *nizak*'s possession. According to Rabbi Ishmael, by contrast, the *nizak* is owed money, but his claim is limited in recovery to the value of the ox: he may collect from the ox itself, but he remains fundamentally a creditor rather than an owner. The Gemara describes this as an *apoteiki* — a focused lien — on the ox. The *mazik* still owns the animal; the *nizak* simply holds an unusually powerful security interest in it.

### Machro/Hekdesho

The Gemara records that Rav Tachlifa bar Marava taught the following *baraita* before Rabbi Abahu: *machro ein machur, hekdesho mukdash* — if he sold the ox, the sale does not hold; but if he sanctified it, it takes effect as *hekdesh*. The immediate question is: who is the subject of these actions — the *mazik* or the *nizak*? And why the apparent inconsistency between the two rulings?

The Gemara first considers whether we are speaking of the *mazik* who sells or sanctifies the ox. If the *mazik* sells it and the sale is invalid, that fits Rabbi Akiva's position that the *nizak* already owns the ox outright — but then why would the *mazik*'s sanctification take effect? Conversely, if we say the subject is the *nizak*, then the rule that his sale is ineffective fits Rabbi Ishmael — the *nizak* does not own the ox and therefore cannot sell it — but then why does his sanctification work, since under Rabbi Ishmael he has no ownership?

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: *le-olam mazik* — we are speaking throughout of the *mazik*, and the ruling operates according to both positions simultaneously.

### Lien Interpretation

The Gemara explains *machro ein machur* as follows: even according to Rabbi Ishmael, who holds that the *mazik* retains ownership of his ox, the sale is not fully valid — not in the sense that it is wholly void, but in the sense that it is not permanent. The *nizak* retains his lien and can retrieve the ox from the purchaser. The sale allows the buyer to use the ox for plowing (*le-radya*) until the *nizak* exercises his lien, but the *nizak*'s right to recover it remains intact. *Machro ein machur* thus does not mean the sale is entirely without effect; it means the sale cannot permanently defeat the *nizak*'s claim.

That said, the plain sense of the *baraita* — as the Gemara acknowledges — is considerably more permissive: it seems to say that before the court case, the *mazik* can fully remove the ox from the *nizak*'s reach. The Gemara is deliberately strengthening the *nizak*'s lien beyond what the surface reading of the *baraita* would suggest.

### Bedek Habayis

As for *hekdesho mukdash* — that sanctification takes effect — the Gemara explains this as well. Even according to Rabbi Akiva, who says the *nizak* already owns the ox, the *mazik*'s act of sanctification has a degree of legal force, because of the principle taught by Rabbi Abahu.

Rabbi Abahu rules that there is a rabbinic concern that *hekdesh* not appear to dissolve without a proper act of *pidyon* — lest people say that *hekdesh yotzei belo pidyon*, that consecrated property can be removed from its sacred status without redemption. Now, when property subject to a creditor's lien is sanctified to *bedek habayis* — to the Temple maintenance fund, as opposed to being consecrated as a *korban* — the sanctification is treated like a sale to a third party. The *hekdesh* of *bedek habayis* represents the Temple's proprietary ownership, and therefore, just as a *nizak* can retrieve an ox from a third-party purchaser by exercising his lien, so too he can retrieve it from *hekdesh*. His lien trumps even the Temple's claim.

Nevertheless, because the ox was genuinely held as *hekdesh* in the interim, the *nizak* must perform some nominal act of redemption — he gives a small amount to *hekdesh* — so that it does not appear as though sacred property simply loses its *kedusha* automatically. Under Rabbi Akiva, the *nizak* truly owned the ox from the moment of the goring, and the sanctification never really took hold; yet even so, the Gemara applies Rabbi Abahu's principle, requiring a token *pidyon* so as not to create the appearance of *hekdesh* dissolving without redemption. Under Rabbi Ishmael, the *nizak*'s lien genuinely yields to the *hekdesh* during the interim period, making the *pidyon* all the more clearly necessary before the *nizak* retrieves it.

## Mishnah: Tam Rules

### Amad Ba-Din

The *baraita* restates the governing framework. In the case of a *tam* that caused damage, the rules turn critically on whether the matter has come before *beit din* — *amad ba-din*, literally "stood in judgment." The *baraita* distinguishes sharply between what the *mazik* may do before and after that moment.

### Pre-Court Actions

Before *amad ba-din*, the *baraita* rules: *makhar machur, hekdesho mukdash* — if the *mazik* sells the ox, the sale is effective; if he sanctifies it, the sanctification holds; if he slaughters it and gives it away as a gift, what he has done is done. As noted above, the Gemara significantly limits the plain reading of these rulings. The sale is "effective" only in the interim sense that the buyer may use the ox for plowing until the *nizak* exercises his lien. The sanctification works, but the *nizak* may still retrieve the ox from *hekdesh* after performing a nominal act of *pidyon* in keeping with Rabbi Abahu's principle.

What of slaughter? The *baraita* states that if the *mazik* slaughtered the ox and gave it away, *mah she-asa asui* — what he did stands. The Gemara asks: why should this be final? Let the *nizak* come and collect from the meat. A *baraita* supports this: the verse says *umachru et hashor hachai* — sell the *living* ox — which might suggest the *nizak*'s rights expire upon slaughter. But the continuation, *umachru et hashor*, without the word *hachai*, teaches that the *nizak* still has rights even to a slaughtered animal.

Rav Shizbi therefore explains that *mah she-asa asui* in the case of slaughter does not mean the *nizak* loses his claim entirely. Rather, it refers to *pachat shechita* — the depreciation caused by the act of slaughter itself. If after slaughter the carcass is worth only eighty of an original hundred-dollar value, the *nizak* may collect eighty but not the full hundred.

This leads to the principle stated by Rav Avuya, son of Rav Yehoshua: *ha-mazik shibudo shel chaveiro patur* — one who damages property upon which another holds a lien is exempt. I owe you a hundred dollars; you hold a lien on my plot of land; I go and destroy that land — I am not liable for the indirect loss to your security interest. The land was mine when I damaged it. Similarly, when the *mazik* slaughters the ox, it is still legally his at that moment, so the *nizak*'s resulting loss is treated as an indirect consequence, not a direct act of damage.

The Gemara notes that this same principle was already derivable from a ruling of Rava — *ha-soref shtaro shel chaveiro patur*: one who burns another's debt document is exempt. He destroyed a piece of paper; the underlying debt still exists; the loss of the ability to prove it in court is indirect. The Gemara works through why neither of these prior cases would have been sufficient to generate the full principle, and why the case of slaughter adds something: slaughtering an animal and reducing it to a carcass is, in terms of real destruction, equivalent to digging ditches into a field held under lien — and yet even in that case one is exempt. These acts fall under the broader principle of *grama* and *dinei de-garmi* — indirect causation — a topic the Gemara will address more fully later in the tractate.

### Post-Court Control

Once there has been *amad ba-din*, the *baraita* rules differently: *makhar eino machur, hekdesho eino mukdash* — a sale by the *mazik* is invalid, and a sanctification does not take effect. At this point the *nizak*'s rights have been fully established by the court, and the *mazik* can no longer act unilaterally to undercut them.

It is worth pausing here to note a point raised by *Tosafot* and debated extensively among the *rishonim* and *acharonim*. According to the normative ruling that *chatzi nezek knasa* — that the half-damages paid by a *tam* are a fine rather than ordinary compensation — a fine does not take on legal reality until there is *ha'amadah be-din*. Normally, the moment I borrow money from you or break your property, I owe you money as an objective fact; the court merely clarifies and enforces a pre-existing reality. A *knas*, however, is different: the court does not merely identify the obligation, it creates and imposes it. Before the court acts, I owe you nothing.

Given this, one might have expected that even according to Rabbi Akiva — who says the *nizak* becomes an immediate co-owner — the ownership should not arise until *ha'amadah be-din*, since the underlying debt is a *knas*. Nevertheless, the Gemara understands that according to Rabbi Akiva the *nizak* becomes a co-owner automatically, without any court action. This may be because the verse *umachru et hashor hachai* frames the arrangement as an immediate operative reality. The *baraita* before us, which draws a distinction based on *amad ba-din*, is accordingly read by the Gemara as functioning within the framework of Rabbi Ishmael, who attributes greater rights to the *mazik* before the court acts.

### Creditors vs. Nizak

The *baraita* then addresses the claim of the *mazik*'s general creditors. *Kidmu ba'alei chovot ve-higbiu* — if the creditors came and collected the ox — *bein chav ad she-lo hizik bein hizik ad she-lo chav*, regardless of whether the debt to the creditors arose before or after the damage to the *nizak*'s ox, *lo asu velo klum* — what they did has no effect — *lefi she-ein meshalem ela migufo* — because the *nizak*'s collection is from the body of the ox itself.

This is striking, and the Gemara presses the point. If the *mazik* owed the creditors money before the damage occurred, the creditors held an earlier lien — *ba'alei chov kodem* — so why should the *nizak* be able to displace them? And even if the damage preceded the loan, perhaps the creditors, as the first to actually seize the ox, should be entitled to keep it. The Gemara raises the general question of whether a later creditor who seizes property first is allowed to retain it — and in ordinary circumstances, the answer may well be yes.

The Gemara responds: *le-olam eima lach mah she-gava gava* — ordinarily, what a creditor seizes he may keep. But this case is different, because the *nizak* can say to the creditor: *ilu gabakh havei lav minach havah gavina lei* — had the ox always been in your possession, I would still have had the right to collect from it, since it is the very ox that damaged me. *De-hai tora de-azkin minei mishtalamna* — it is from this ox, the ox that gored mine, that I collect.

*Tosafot* notes that this argument is ultimately just a sophisticated articulation of the *baraita*'s own conclusion — *lefi she-ein meshalem ela migufo*. The *nizak*'s lien is focused specifically on the body of the ox, not on the *mazik*'s assets generally. The creditor's lien, by contrast, attaches to all the *mazik*'s property indiscriminately. Because the *nizak* has this uniquely focused claim on the specific ox, he has a superior right to that animal over any general creditor, regardless of the order in which the debts arose or the property was seized. The creditor is not left without recourse — he may turn to the *mazik*'s other assets — but he cannot hold this particular ox against the *nizak*'s focused claim.

## Damage Assessment

### Shevach Mazik

The Gemara turns to a *baraita* that explores the principles governing how damage is assessed when the value of the animals changes between the moment of the goring and the time the case reaches court.

The case: an ox worth 200 gores an ox worth 200, causing 50 worth of damage. Afterward, the *nizak*'s injured ox appreciates — it is now worth 400, and had it not been damaged it would have been worth 800. How much does the *mazik* pay? The rule is *noten ke-sha'at ha-nezek* — payment is assessed at the moment of the damage. The damage was 50 at the time of the goring, so the *mazik* pays 25 (half of 50, as this is a *tam*). One cannot claim that because the ox would have been worth 800 but is now only worth 400, the damage amounts to 400 — that increased loss is treated as indirect, a loss of anticipated profit that does not enter into the assessment.

The Gemara identifies this ruling as consistent with Rabbi Ishmael's position that the *nizak* holds only a lien, not a share of ownership. Were the *nizak* a co-owner according to Rabbi Akiva, one might expect him to share in the ox's appreciation — his percentage stake would grow along with the animal's value. But the rule here is that he receives only the fixed amount assessed at the moment of the goring, suggesting he is a creditor owed a sum, not a partner in the animal.

### Pachat at Amad

The *baraita* then addresses the reverse: *kachash ke-sha'at ha-amadah ba-din* — if the *nizak*'s ox depreciated by the time of the court case. Suppose it was worth 200 at the time of the goring, the damage was 50, but by the time of *amad ba-din* it had fallen further in value to 100. In that case, the *mazik* pays based on the depreciated value — effectively 50 now constitutes proportionally greater damage, and payment is calculated accordingly.

This asymmetry is notable. When the ox goes up in value, the *nizak* gains nothing from the increase — but when it goes down, the *mazik* must pay based on the lower value, as though the continued decline is part of the original injury. This seems inconsistent: if the *nizak* holds a percentage of the animal (à la Rabbi Akiva), he should gain when it rises and lose when it falls. If he is only owed a fixed sum (à la Rabbi Ishmael), he should always recover that fixed sum regardless of subsequent fluctuations.

The Gemara asks of the *seifa* — the depreciation case — that it seems to operate like Rabbi Akiva (the *nizak* bears a percentage interest), while the *reisha* — the appreciation case — seems to operate like Rabbi Ishmael. Does the *baraita* split between the two opinions? The Gemara answers: *lo kula Rabbi Akiva hi* — the entire *baraita* is according to Rabbi Akiva. Both rules operate on the premise that the *nizak* is a co-owner.

### Continued Injury

The reason for the asymmetry lies in the specific circumstances of each case. The *baraita*'s statement that the *mazik* pays only the original 50 even when the ox appreciated to 400 refers to a case where the *mazik* fattened the animal — *ke-she-pitmu*. The increase in value resulted from the *mazik*'s own investment and effort. Even if the *nizak* is a co-owner, he cannot fully claim the benefit of appreciation that was generated by the *mazik*'s expenditure. The Gemara adds via Rav Pappa that the *reisha* also teaches a separate *chiddush* for cases of natural appreciation: even if the ox simply became worth more on its own, the *mazik* is not excused from paying the original 50 on the theory that the animal is now worth more than it was originally — because the gap between 400 and 800 demonstrates that real damage was done. *Tosafot* notes, however, that this resolution is not entirely clean: even partners in a field, when one improves it unilaterally, generally cannot exclude the other partner from all benefit, and the precise allocation remains a subject of discussion among the *rishonim*.

As for the depreciation case: the Gemara asks, *kachash machmat mai* — what caused the ox to decline in value? If the *nizak* himself worked the animal into decline, the *mazik* could reasonably say: you diminished it yourself. Rav Ashi answers that the case in view is *kachash machmat makah* — the ox declined because of the original wound. The injury kept worsening. In such a case, the *nizak* can say *kurna de-torach kevira beh* — the horn of your ox is still embedded in my ox, meaning the original injury is the ongoing cause of every subsequent decline. This is not *grama* — it is not treated as an indirect consequence that falls outside the *mazik*'s liability. Rather, it is how the full extent of the original injury is properly assessed. The damage is not capped at the 50 measured at the moment of the goring; it is measured by how much worse the injury ultimately became.

The overall principle that emerges: appreciation of the *mazik*'s ox generates no windfall for the *nizak* — that is an indirect matter of lost profit. But continued depreciation of the *nizak*'s ox as a direct result of the original wound is part of the damage itself, assessed at the time of *amad ba-din*. Under Rabbi Akiva, the *nizak* as co-owner loses when the animal loses value, but gains from the original owner's investment only to a limited degree. These are the practical *nafka minas* between the two positions — and the Gemara has significantly strengthened the *nizak*'s lien even under Rabbi Ishmael, against what the plain reading of the *baraita* would suggest.

## Torah Case Debate

### R. Meir Position

The Gemara now turns to the *baraita* that parses the precise scriptural case in the Torah. The verse (*Shemot* 21:35) reads: *umachru et hashor hachai vechatzu et kaspo vegam et ha-met yechetzun* — they shall sell the live ox and split its proceeds, and they shall also split the dead one. The question is: what scenario does the Torah envision?

Rabbi Meir says: *al zeh ne'emar* — the Torah is speaking to the case of an ox worth 200 that gores an ox worth 200, where the *neveilah* — the carcass — is worth nothing at all. In that case, the debt is 100 (the full value of the dead animal), and since it is a *tam*, chatzi nezek is 50. The verse *umachru et hashor hachai vechatzu et kaspo* addresses this case directly. But then how does Rabbi Meir read *vegam et ha-met yechetzun*? He explains: this does not mean physically divide the carcass. Rather, *pachat she-pichas'o mitah machatzin be-chai* — the depreciation caused by the death is split within the context of the live ox. *Vegam et ha-met yechetzun* refers to splitting the loss, not splitting the carcass.

### R. Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees. He says: yes, the ruling you describe is correct, and yes, that is the *halacha* — but that is not the case the Torah is addressing. The Torah speaks of an ox worth 200 goring an ox worth 200 where the *neveilah* is worth 50. In that situation, the *mazik* and *nizak* each take half of the live ox and half of the carcass, walking away with 125 each. Each lost 75 from an original holding of 200. The total damage was 150, and chatzi nezek is 75 — perfectly consistent with half-damages. The verse *vegam et ha-met yechetzun* means exactly what it says: they divide the carcass.

Rabbi Yehuda's objection to Rabbi Meir's case is pointed: if the *neveilah* is worth nothing, why does the Torah bother saying *vegam et ha-met yechetzun* — why legislate a division of a worthless object? The scriptural instruction to split the dead animal implies the dead animal has value. Hence the Torah must be speaking to the case where the carcass is worth 50.

### Pachat Neveilah

What is actually at stake between them? The Gemara notes that in the case Rabbi Yehuda describes — the *neveilah* worth 50 — both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree on the arithmetic: each party walks away with 125 and absorbs a loss of 75. *Mai beinaihu* — what do they actually dispute?

Rava answers: *pachat neveilah ika beinaihu* — the question of who bears the depreciation of the carcass. If after the goring the carcass continues to lose value, who absorbs that loss — the *nizak* or the *mazik*? Rabbi Meir holds *pachat neveilah de-nizak havei* — the depreciation of the carcass falls entirely on the *nizak*. The *mazik* pays for the loss at the time of death, but the carcass itself and any further decline in its value belong to the *nizak*. Rabbi Yehuda, insisting that the Torah says *vegam et ha-met yechetzun*, holds that the carcass is actually split between them, making the *mazik* a part-owner in the carcass — and therefore *pachat neveilah de-mazik havei palga*: the *mazik* absorbs half of any subsequent depreciation.

### Tam vs. Muad

Abaye immediately raises an objection: *matzinu le-Rabbi Yehuda tam chamur mi-muad* — according to Rabbi Yehuda, a *tam* would be stricter in this respect than a *muad*. In every other category of damage — *bor*, *muad*, and so forth — the *nizak* takes the carcass and the *mazik* pays for the loss. The *mazik* never bears the depreciation of the carcass. A *tam*, which is universally the most lenient category of payment, would according to Rabbi Yehuda uniquely impose on the *mazik* a share of the carcass's ongoing decline.

One might respond: perhaps Rabbi Yehuda holds that a *tam* is indeed stricter in certain respects. After all, elsewhere Rabbi Yehuda rules — as will appear later in the tractate — that the minimum level of *shemira* required to be exempt from liability is actually higher for a *tam* than for a *muad*. Perhaps Rabbi Yehuda accepts that a *tam* can impose greater obligations.

The Gemara rejects this parallel. Rabbi Yehuda's stringency regarding *shemira* for a *tam* is derived from specific scriptural verses — *dikesivei kra*. But one has never heard Rabbi Yehuda apply greater stringency to a *tam* in the domain of *tashlumin*, of the actual payments. Payment by a *tam* is always more lenient. The *baraita* itself confirms this: Rabbi Yehuda says there — *tam ha-kal kol she-ken* — if a *muad* pays only the actual loss without any windfall from the split, then a *tam*, which is the more lenient category, certainly cannot produce a result that benefits the *nizak* beyond his actual loss. Rabbi Yehuda explicitly rejects the possibility that a *tam* would ever be more burdensome for the *mazik* than a *muad* in terms of what is owed.

### Shevach Neveilah

Rabbi Yochanan therefore proposes a different resolution of the dispute. The argument between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda is not about *pachat neveilah* — both agree the depreciation of the carcass falls on the *nizak* alone. Rather, the dispute concerns *shevach neveilah* — appreciation of the carcass.

If after the goring the carcass increases in value — perhaps hides became more valuable, or the meat fetched a better price — who benefits from that increase? Rabbi Meir holds that *shevach neveilah de-nizak havei* — the appreciation belongs entirely to the *nizak*, who holds the carcass. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *shevach neveilah de-mazik havei palga* — because the *mazik* is a part-owner in the carcass (per the verse *vegam et ha-met yechetzun*), he shares in any increase in value. Crucially, this means the *mazik* benefits from the appreciation: his effective payment is reduced, since the *nizak* must now credit the *mazik* with his share of the increased carcass value. This is a leniency for the *mazik* rather than a stringency, which is precisely why Rabbi Yehuda embraces it — it is consistent with the principle that a *tam* never imposes greater payment than a *muad*.

*De-mar savar de-nizak havei u-mar savar palga* — one holds the appreciation belongs entirely to the *nizak*, the other holds it is split. This is what prompted Rabbi Yehuda's analysis: the Torah goes easy on the *mazik* in the case of a *tam*, making him a part-owner of the carcass not to burden him with its depreciation but to allow him to benefit from its appreciation — thereby reducing what he effectively pays. The verse *vegam et ha-met yechetzun*, on this reading, extends a leniency: it gives the *mazik* a share in the upside of the carcass, ensuring that chatzi nezek in a *tam* is never more burdensome than full payment in a *muad*.
